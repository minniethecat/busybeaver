## Overview

Boris Anchoukoff is the [[herzoslovakia|Herzoslovakia]]n valet who accompanied [[prince-michael-obolovitch|Prince Michael Obolovitch]] to [[chimneys|Chimneys]]. Described by [[bill-eversleigh|Bill Eversleigh]] as "an unmitigated ruffian" [018-110], Boris is in fact a man of fierce loyalty and few words. After his master's murder, he transfers his devotion to [[anthony-cade|Anthony Cade]], whom he addresses simply as "Master" and serves as an informal agent in the investigation.

## Character Profile

### Appearance and Manner

Boris is a [[herzoslovakia|Herzoslovakia]]n of few words whose features "contracted painfully" when he speaks of his dead master, his voice coming "harsh with emotion" [014-115]. He moves with military precision — "rigidly at attention" [022-086], saluting and turning "sharply on his heel" [022-101]. His English is limited, and he communicates largely through simple declarations and gestures. Anthony treats him with a mixture of amusement and respect, translating his brief utterances for the benefit of others.

> "I am Boris Anchoukoff." [016-177]

### Background

Boris served as Prince Michael's trusted valet and was the only member of the [[herzoslovakia|Herzoslovakia]]n party who accompanied the Prince to [[chimneys|Chimneys]] on the evening of the murder — [[captain-andrassy|Captain Andrassy]], the equerry, having remained in [[london|London]] [011-037]. His position as the Prince's personal servant gave him intimate access to his master's affairs.

## Key Actions

Boris is interviewed by [[superintendent-battle|Superintendent Battle]] and [[george-lomax|George Lomax]] following the murder [014-104]. When questioned, his emotional response — features contracting, swallowing twice before speaking — reveals the depth of his loyalty to his dead master [014-115].

He later encounters [[anthony-cade|Anthony Cade]] alone and declares his allegiance: "I am Boris Anchoukoff" [016-177]. When Anthony questions his purpose, Boris responds with a touch of scorn, dismissing the idea that anyone else could serve him now [016-183].

> Boris Anchoukoff looked at him with a touch of scorn. [016-183]

In one of the novel's more comic passages, Boris serves as an informal detective. He retrieves a scrap of paper dropped by a "foreign gentleman," delivers it to Anthony with a military bow, and when asked where he found it, replies simply: "He dropped it. I bring it to the Master" [022-095]. When Anthony repeats "to the Master?" Boris merely looks at him reproachfully [022-099].

Anthony later uses Boris's account of Prince Michael's last moments — specifically the Prince's dying reference to a "cipher" — to unlock a crucial clue in the secret passage [023-073].

## Relationships

- **[[anthony-cade|Anthony Cade]]** — Boris transfers his loyalty to Anthony, calling him "Master" and serving as his informal agent [016-177], [022-090]
- **[[captain-andrassy|Captain Andrassy]]** — his countryman and the Prince's equerry, though the two operate independently [011-037]
- **[[baron-lolopretjzyl|Baron Lolopretjzyl]]** — the [[herzoslovakia|Herzoslovakia]]n representative in [[london|London]], another royalist ally [014-104]

## Significance

Boris Anchoukoff is a minor but memorable character whose devotion provides both comic relief and narrative utility. His transfer of loyalty from the dead Prince Michael to [[anthony-cade|Anthony Cade]] creates an informal intelligence network that helps Anthony piece together the mystery. His retrieval of the dropped paper — a classic Christie device — advances the plot at a critical moment. Boris also represents the human cost of the political intrigue: a loyal servant whose entire existence was defined by service to a murdered master.
