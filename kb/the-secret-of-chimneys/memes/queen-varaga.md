## Overview

Queen Varaga of Herzoslovakia — born [[mademoiselle-brun|Angèle Mory]] — is one of the novel's most complex and significant off-stage characters. A Parisian actress who rose to become Queen of a Balkan kingdom, she was simultaneously a professional [[king-victor|jewel thief]] and confederate of the master criminal [[king-victor|King Victor]]. Dead before the story begins, her legacy of stolen jewels and political chaos drives the central mystery of [[the-secret-of-chimneys|The Secret of Chimneys]].

## Character Profile

### Background: From Actress to Queen

[[mademoiselle-brun|Angèle Mory]] was a music hall performer in Paris, described by [[anthony-cade|Anthony Cade]] as "some little guttersnipe of a music hall artiste" [001-118]. According to the Sûreté's intelligence, she was approached by the revolutionary [[red-hand|Comrades of the Red Hand]], who offered her "a huge sum if she would aid them in their plans" — her role being "to infatuate the young king, and decoy him to some spot agreed upon with them" [019-0[[TOC|31]]].

> "She succeeded in captivating the king who fell desperately in love with her and loaded her with jewels. It was then that she conceived the idea of being — not a king's mistress, but a queen!" [019-032]

Rather than delivering [[nicholas-iv|Nicholas IV]] to the revolutionaries, [[mademoiselle-brun|Angèle Mory]] pursued a bolder ambition: she married him. Introduced into [[herzoslovakia|Herzoslovakia]] as "the [[mademoiselle-brun|Countess Varaga Popoleffsky]], an offshoot of the Romanovs," she became Queen Varaga [019-032]. Her triumph was short-lived, however, as the betrayed [[red-hand|Comrades of the Red Hand]] "twice attempted her life" and "finally worked up the country to such a pitch that a revolution broke out in which both the king and queen perished" [019-032].

### Criminal Career

[[superintendent-battle|Superintendent Battle]] reveals that Queen Varaga was not merely an opportunistic actress but a professional thief who "still kept in with her confederate, [[king-victor|King Victor]]" and "continued to correspond with him, in a secret code, from the Court of [[herzoslovakia|Herzoslovakia]]" [019-033]. Her letters to [[king-victor|Victor]] were "written in English, and signed with the name of an English lady then at the Embassy" — [[virginia-revel|Virginia Revel]]'s name was used [019-033].

## Key Actions

Queen Varaga's most significant crime occurred during her state visit to England with [[nicholas-iv|Nicholas IV]], when she was received as a guest at [[chimneys|Chimneys]] by the late Marquis of [[lord-caterham|Caterham]]. [[superintendent-battle|Battle]] lays out the logic: "there we have a royal personage and at the same time an expert thief" [019-037]. During this visit, the Koh-i-Noor diamond was stolen and replaced with an expert forgery — "the substitute which was so wonderful as to deceive anyone but an expert could only have been fashioned by [[king-victor|King Victor]]" [019-037].

The [[red-hand|Comrades of the Red Hand]], furious at her betrayal — "she sold them, betrayed them, and rose high, but they never forgot" — finally exacted their revenge in the revolution [019-032]. Both Nicholas and Varaga died, their bodies "horribly mutilated and hardly recognizable" [019-032].

## Relationships

- **[[nicholas-iv|King Nicholas IV]]** — her husband, who married her despite her unsuitability and was killed alongside her [001-118]
- **[[king-victor|King Victor]]** — her criminal confederate, with whom she maintained a secret coded correspondence [019-033]
- **[[virginia-revel|Virginia Revel]]** — unknowing victim whose name Varaga used as a cover for her letters to [[king-victor|King Victor]] [019-033], [019-037]
- **The [[red-hand|Comrades of the Red Hand]]** — the revolutionary group she betrayed, leading to her death [019-032]
- **[[count-stylptitch|Count Stylptitch]]** — his memoirs and letters, entrusted to [[james-mcgrath|Jimmy McGrath]] and then [[anthony-cade|Anthony Cade]], contained damaging information about Varaga's activities [001-004]

## Significance

Queen Varaga is the novel's hidden engine. Her theft of the Koh-i-Noor, her secret correspondence with [[king-victor|King Victor]], and the compromising letters she wrote using [[virginia-revel|Virginia Revel]]'s name connect every thread of the plot: the [[herzoslovakia|Herzoslovakia]]n political crisis, the jewel hunt at [[chimneys|Chimneys]], the blackmail of [[virginia-revel|Virginia]], and the pursuit of [[king-victor|King Victor]]. She embodies Christie's recurring theme of the danger posed by those who cross class boundaries — her fatal flaw was not her criminality but her ambition to be queen.
