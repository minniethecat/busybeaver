# Koh-i-Noor Diamond

The mysterious gem whose disappearance from [[chimneys]] during a diplomatic function seven years before the novel's events triggers the intersecting conspiracies of jewel thieves, revolutionaries, and royal claimants — a classic Christie MacGuffin whose hiding place is encoded in [[count-stylptitch-memoirs]] and whose recovery determines the fate of the [[herzoslovakia|Herzoslovakian]] throne.

## Description

The diamond is referred to only indirectly throughout the novel. [[george-lomax]] insists on secrecy:

> "Hush, Battle!" George glanced suspiciously round him. "I beg of you, mention no names. Much better not. If you must speak of it, call it the K." [014-087]

[[superintendent-battle]] describes it as "something" that "disappeared in a way that seemed incredible unless you admitted two things — that the thief was a royal personage and that at the same time it was the work of a high-class professional" (4541-PN). The jewel is one of the historic gems of the [[herzoslovakia|Herzoslovakia]]n crown. After the revolution, "many of the Crown Jewels which had fallen, of course, into the hands of the mob, found their way to [[paris|Paris]], and it was discovered that in nine cases out of ten the principal stones had been replaced by paste" (4554-PN). The Koh-i-Noor — the most famous stone — vanished entirely.

[[king-victor]] is believed to have created the paste substitute that replaced the original stone, and the coded letters between him and [[queen-varaga]] are thought to contain "the instructions for finding the diamond" (4818-PN). The jewel is ultimately concealed within one of the hollow suits of armour in [[the-council-chamber-at-chimneys|the Council Chamber]] at [[chimneys|Chimneys]], undisturbed for years until the events of the novel bring every interested party to the estate simultaneously. [[lady-eileen-bundle-brent|Bundle Brent]] treats the whole affair with characteristic irreverence:

> "The thingummybob," said Bundle. "The historic diamond of purple princes that was pinched in the dark ages before I grew to years of discretion." [023-008]

## Role in the Novel

The Koh-i-Noor functions as the novel's central MacGuffin — the object everyone is searching for, whose possession carries political as well as monetary value. [[anthony-cade]] learns of it from [[james-mcgrath]] in [[bulawayo|Bulawayo]]: "He said he knew where the Koh-i-Noor was" (353-PN), referring to [[count-stylptitch]]. The memoirs contain the secret of its hiding place, and their scheduled publication on October 13th gives every faction a deadline. [[king-victor]] infiltrates [[chimneys]] to find the jewel; [[red-hand]] want it to fund their revolutionary activities; the [[herzoslovakian-royalists|Herzoslovakian Royalists]] need it as a legitimizing symbol for [[prince-michael-obolovitch]]'s claim to the throne. The discovery of its hiding place — baked into the hollow armour — is the novel's climactic revelation.

The search is riddled with false leads and misdirection, and the true hiding place is revealed only at the end:

> "I guess you haven't tumbled to the little idea, Mr. Lomax," said the drawling voice of Mr. Fish. "That historic diamond was never behind the books in the library." [029-007]

## Significance in the Plot

- **Political token**: Possession of the Koh-i-Noor could validate or undermine a claim to the [[herzoslovakia|Herzoslovakia]]n throne, making it a prize for both royalists and revolutionaries
- **Criminal target**: [[king-victor]]'s entire criminal career has been building toward this theft — "[[king-victor|King Victor]] never stole the Crown Jewels of [[herzoslovakia|Herzoslovakia]] as far as I've heard" (401-PN), suggesting this job has been years in planning
- **The count's secret**: [[count-stylptitch]] knew the diamond's location and encoded it in his memoirs
- **The murder motive**: The search for the diamond brings the killer to [[chimneys]] and ultimately leads to [[prince-michael-obolovitch|Prince Michael]]'s death
