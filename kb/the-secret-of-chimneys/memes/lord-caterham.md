# Lord Caterham

Clement Edward Alistair Brent, ninth Marquis of Caterham, is the owner of the [[chimneys]] estate and one of the novel's most distinctive supporting characters — a small, shabbily-dressed gentleman who would rather be anywhere else than hosting a diplomatic house party for the British government.

## Role in the Novel

Lord Caterham serves as the reluctant host of the weekend party at [[chimneys]] that forms the novel's central setting. His role is largely that of a comic foil to the pompous [[george-lomax]], and he provides a note of weary, aristocratic detachment that contrasts with the scheming of the politicians, the intensity of the detectives, and the romantic entanglements of the younger characters. Despite his protestations, he is drawn into the [[herzoslovakia]] affair and the events surrounding the [[theft-of-the-koh-i-noor]] and the murder at his own house.

## Character Profile

### Appearance and Manner

Lord Caterham is described as "a small gentleman, shabbily dressed, and entirely unlike the popular conception of a Marquis. He had faded blue eyes, a thin melancholy nose, and a vague but courteous manner" (003-PN). His physical presence is notably unassuming for a man of his station — he is far more interested in escaping political conversations than in asserting his title.

### Background and Inheritance

The principal misfortune of Lord Caterham's life was to have succeeded his brother, the eighth Marquis, four years before the novel opens. His predecessor had been "a man of mark, a household word all over England" — at one time Secretary of State for Foreign Affairs, and his country seat [[chimneys]] was famous for its hospitality and informal weekend parties at which "history had been made and unmade" (003-PN). The ninth Marquis, by contrast, "had the utmost respect and esteem for the memory of his brother," but strongly objected to "the assumption that he was bound to follow in his brother's footsteps, and that [[chimneys|Chimneys]] was a national possession rather than a private country house" (003-PN).

### Attitude to Politics

The narrator flatly states: "There was nothing that bored Lord Caterham more than politics — unless it was politicians" (003-PN). This disposition makes him an unwilling participant in the affairs of state that continuously intrude upon his life through [[chimneys]].

## Key Actions

### Hosting the House Party

When pressed by [[george-lomax]] to host a diplomatic house party for [[prince-michael-obolovitch]] under the guise of a shooting party at [[chimneys|Chimneys]], Lord Caterham's first instinct is to flee: "I was thinking of going abroad this week," he protests. When that fails, he pleads health: "My doctor seems to think I'm in rather a bad way" (003-PN), eyeing a passing taxi "with longing eyes."

### Approving Virginia Revel's Involvement

Lord Caterham's mood shifts markedly when Lomax suggests including his cousin [[virginia-revel]] in the party. He "brightened up" immediately, declaring her "the most charming woman in [[london|London]]" and agreeing that her presence might make him "possibly enjoy the party after all" (003-PN).

### Reaction to the Scandal

Throughout the discussion of [[count-stylptitch]]'s memoirs and the potential political scandal they could cause, Lord Caterham displays an unhelpful relish for the dramatic possibilities. When Lomax warns of the far-reaching consequences should [[count-stylptitch-memoirs|the memoirs]] reveal a certain disappearance, Lord Caterham predicts with "evident enjoyment": "Of course they would" — meaning the public would demand to know why it was hushed up (003-PN).

### Life at Chimneys During the Investigation

Throughout the murder investigation at [[chimneys]], Lord Caterham maintains his characteristic air of detached bemusement. He is content to leave the detecting to [[superintendent-battle]] and the politicking to [[george-lomax|George Lomax]], while he himself contemplates escape to more congenial surroundings.

## Relationships

- **[[george-lomax]]** — Lord Caterham's political tormentor, "an upright Christian gentleman" whom Lord Caterham thoroughly dislikes. He finds Lomax's "puffy red face, his heavy breathing, and his prominent blue eyes" particularly irksome (003-PN)
- **[[virginia-revel]]** — His cousin-in-law, whom he considers "the most charming woman in [[london|London]]" and whose presence at [[chimneys|Chimneys]] promises to make the party bearable
- **[[lady-eileen-bundle-brent]]** ([[lady-eileen-bundle-brent|Lady Eileen Brent]]) — His spirited daughter, who takes after her late mother rather than her retiring father, and is "a red-hot socialist" according to her father, who correctly predicts she would be useless for diplomatic handling of sensitive matters
- **[[superintendent-battle]]** — The [[scotland-yard|Scotland Yard]] detective called to investigate [[murder-at-chimneys|the murder at Chimneys]]; Lord Caterham is content to leave the investigating entirely to [[superintendent-battle|Battle]]
- **[[anthony-cade]]** — One of the guests at [[chimneys|Chimneys]], whose involvement in the affair Lord Caterham observes with detached interest

### Family

Lord Caterham is the father of two daughters: [[lady-eileen-bundle-brent]] ([[lady-eileen-bundle-brent|Lady Eileen Brent]]) and her sister Daisy. His late wife — formerly married to the brother of [[virginia-revel]]'s late husband — was a woman of considerable political skill. His sister-in-law, Marcia, the widow of the eighth Marquis (the Duke of Perth's daughter), is a "redoubtable" political hostess whom Lord Caterham fears.
