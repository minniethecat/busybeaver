## Overview

Bulawayo is the southern African city where [[the-secret-of-chimneys|The Secret of Chimneys]] begins. It is the setting for the novel's opening chapter, in which [[anthony-cade|Anthony Cade]] — working as a tour guide — encounters his old friend [[james-mcgrath|Jimmy McGrath]] and agrees to deliver [[count-stylptitch|Count Stylptitch]]'s memoirs and a packet of letters to [[england|England]]. The city serves as the remote starting point from which the novel's tangled European intrigue is launched.

## Description

Christie's Bulawayo is depicted through the eyes of the English tourists Anthony escorts: "The sun was unbearably hot, the hotel was uncomfortable, there seemed to be nowhere particular to go until the moment should arrive to motor to the Matoppos" [001-003]. The town functions as a colonial waystation — a place where English adventurers like Anthony and Jimmy pass through between more significant destinations.

Anthony and Jimmy met years earlier "in the interior of Africa," but Bulawayo itself represents civilization's edge — close enough to the wild for men like them but still connected to the imperial networks that allow [[james-mcgrath|McGrath]]'s story to reach [[england|England]].

## Role in the Novel

Bulawayo is the novel's narrative launch point. It is here that [[james-mcgrath|Jimmy McGrath]], "more or less in funds at the time of his encounter with Anthony at Bulawayo" [007-081], hands over [[count-stylptitch-memoirs|the manuscript]] and letters that will drive the entire plot. The city's remoteness from Europe underscores the improbability of the chain of events: a dying [[herzoslovakia|Herzoslovakia]]n agent in Africa, a Canadian journalist, and an English adventurer-turned-tour-guide combine to send explosive political documents halfway across the world.

The authenticity of Anthony's Bulawayo connection is later verified by [[superintendent-battle|Battle]], who confirms "he was in Bulawayo with Mr. [[james-mcgrath|McGrath]] at the time he stated" [022-013] — establishing Anthony's bona fides and validating his account of how he came to possess the documents.

## Significance

Bulawayo represents the global reach of the novel's intrigue. That a dying [[herzoslovakia|Herzoslovakia]]n agent should entrust state secrets to a Canadian journalist in the interior of Africa, who then passes them to an English adventurer in Bulawayo — this chain of happenstance establishes the novel's keynote of improbable coincidence that Christie manages to make feel entirely natural. Bulawayo is the farthest point from [[chimneys|Chimneys]] in the novel's geography, and the fact that the story must travel such a distance to begin emphasises the extraordinary convergence of forces at the English country house.
