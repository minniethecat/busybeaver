# Chimneys

The country estate owned by [[lord-caterham|Lord Caterham]], where much of the novel's action takes place. A grand English country house, it becomes the gathering point for diplomats, detectives, revolutionaries, and royalty — all drawn by the secret that gives the novel its name [469-473]. Anthony Cade recognizes it immediately as "one of the stately homes of England" [477-477].

## Historic Significance

Under the eighth Marquess of Caterham, Chimneys achieved national prominence as a center of political and diplomatic activity. The eighth Marquess, at one time Secretary of State for Foreign Affairs, made Chimneys famous for its hospitality and informal diplomacy [531-531]. History was made and unmade at weekend parties at Chimneys, and there was hardly anyone of note in England — or indeed in Europe — who had not, at one time or another, stayed there [531-531].

The house's political significance was such that its name became synonymous with international negotiation. George Lomax describes it as "quite apart from its historic associations, ideally situated" for hosting Prince Michael Obolovitch's delicate diplomatic mission [563-563].

> The principal misfortune of Lord Caterham's life was to have succeeded his brother, the eighth Marquis, four years ago. For the previous Lord Caterham had been a man of mark, a household word all over England. At one time Secretary of State for Foreign Affairs, he had always bulked largely in the counsels of the Empire, and his country seat, Chimneys, was famous for its hospitality. [531-531]

## Description

Chimneys is a large, historic country house with extensive grounds including a rose garden. It features an Elizabethan wing and numerous rooms, including the [[council-chamber|Council Chamber]] that was once used for international diplomatic meetings. The house maintains a full staff including [[tredwell|Tredwell]] the butler [3353-3353].

The ninth Marquess, the current Lord Caterham, views Chimneys less as a national institution and more as a private country house — a perspective that conflicts with George Lomax's expectations. Caterham resents "the assumption that he was bound to follow in his brother's footsteps, and that Chimneys was a national possession rather than a private country house" [533-533].

## Role in the Novel

Chimneys serves as the physical nexus where all plot threads converge. Lord Caterham reluctantly hosts a house party under the guise of a shooting party, intended to provide cover for sensitive diplomatic negotiations [547-547]. The gathering includes:

- [[foreign-office|British Foreign Office]] representatives seeking to resolve the Herzoslovakian succession
- Secret agents and revolutionaries from the [[red-hand|Red Hand]] organization
- [[scotland-yard|Scotland Yard]] detectives investigating the murder
- International figures including [[herman-isaacstein|Herman Isaacstein]] and a French detective

> "Prince Michael Obolovitch arrives the end of the week, and the whole thing can be carried through at Chimneys under the guise of a shooting party." [547-547]

## Key Events

- The diplomatic house party hosted by [[lord-caterham|Lord Caterham]]
- Discovery of a body and the subsequent murder investigation in the [[council-chamber|Council Chamber]]
- Midnight adventures in the house and gardens
- The confrontation that reveals the true identity of Prince Michael
- Recovery of [[koh-i-noor-diamond|the Koh-i-Noor diamond]]

## The Chimneys Mystery

The novel's title — [[the-secret-of-chimneys|The Secret of Chimneys]] — refers to an incident that occurred during the eighth Marquess's tenure. A disappearance took place while distinguished guests were staying at Chimneys, an event that "almost ruined his career" [615-615]. This historic secret intertwines with the present-day Herzoslovakian conspiracy, making the house itself a character in the unfolding drama.
