# George Lomax

The Honourable George Lomax was a senior British politician and [[foreign-office|Foreign Office]] official who played a central role in organizing the diplomatic conference at [[chimneys|Chimneys]], aimed at restoring [[prince-michael-obolovitch|Prince Michael Obolovitch]] to the [[herzoslovakia|Herzoslovakia]]n throne. A robust, self-important man, he served as the chief political architect of the British Government's Balkan policy in the affair.

## Appearance and Manner

George Lomax was described as "a robust man, George Lomax, inclined to embonpoint, with a red face and protuberant eyes, and an immense sense of his own importance" [003-037]. His manner of engaging others was forceful — he "had the unpleasant habit of retaining a hold upon a person with whom he was engaged in serious conversation — doubtless the result of long experience" [003-042]. In conversation with [[lord-caterham|Lord Caterham]] at their club, "he had a firm grip of the lapel of [[lord-caterham|Lord Caterham]]'s coat," preventing escape from the political monologue [003-042].

[[lord-caterham|Lord Caterham]]'s opinion of Lomax was unambiguous: "As far as it is possible for one upright Christian gentleman to dislike another upright Christian gentleman, [[lord-caterham|Lord Caterham]] disliked the Hon. George Lomax. He disliked his puffy red face, his heavy breathing, and his prominent blue eyes" [003-086].

## Role in the Chimneys Conference

Lomax was the driving force behind the [[chimneys|Chimneys]] house party, which served as cover for sensitive diplomatic negotiations. He persuaded [[lord-caterham|Lord Caterham]] to host the gathering by explaining that [[prince-michael-obolovitch|Prince Michael Obolovitch]]'s arrival required a discreet venue for political discussions [003-014].

The plan involved managing multiple politically sensitive elements: [[count-stylptitch-memoirs|the memoirs]] of the late [[count-stylptitch|Count Stylptitch]], which contained potentially damaging revelations about the Obolovitch dynasty [003-040], and the presence of various international figures with competing interests in [[herzoslovakia|Herzoslovakia]]'s future.

Lomax pressed [[lord-caterham|Lord Caterham]] for discretion: "[[lord-caterham|Caterham]], [[lord-caterham|Caterham]], implored Lomax in an agonized whisper. 'Discretion, I beg of you. Above all things, discretion'" [003-043].

## Organizational Methods and Weaknesses

Despite his self-importance, Lomax's organizational abilities had notable gaps. A critical error involved sending [[bill-eversleigh|Bill Eversleigh]] to determine the arrival of [[james-mcgrath|McGrath]]'s ship:

> George Lomax had made one mistake — there was a weak spot in his preparations. The weak spot was Bill. [005-044]

Bill's poor enunciation led to confusion over the ship name "Granarth Castle," which the clerk at the Union Castle offices misheard. Lomax accepted the incorrect information, leading to a delay in [[james-mcgrath|McGrath]]'s arrival and complications in the carefully planned schedule.

## Relationship with Virginia Revel

Lomax recognized the value of a skilled political hostess, suggesting that [[virginia-revel|Virginia Revel]] be enlisted to help manage the delicate diplomatic situation. He told [[lord-caterham|Lord Caterham]]: "A woman could handle the whole thing delicately and with tact — put the position before him, as it were, without getting his back up" [003-065]. Lomax was clearly in awe of [[virginia-revel|Virginia]], and she in turn found him useful but unimpressive, noting dryly: "My cousin George Lomax does" when asked who might benefit from the murder [009-086].
