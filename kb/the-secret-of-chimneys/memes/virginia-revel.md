# Virginia Revel

Virginia Revel was a young, charming, and capable widow who became central to the events at [[chimneys|Chimneys]]. Recruited by her cousin [[george-lomax|George Lomax]] to serve as a political hostess for the diplomatic conference, she proved herself far more than a decorative presence -- she became a key participant in the unfolding mystery, and ultimately [[anthony-cade|Anthony Cade]]'s romantic interest and partner.

## Character and Appearance

Virginia was widely regarded as "the most charming woman in [[london|London]]" [003-075]. [[lord-caterham|Lord Caterham]], a man generally bored by social obligations, brightened at the prospect of her attendance at the [[chimneys|Chimneys]] house party [003-075]. She combined social grace with sharp intelligence and a capacity for decisive action, unafraid to challenge the conventions expected of a woman in her position.

> Virginia Revel was just twenty-seven. She was tall and of an exquisite slimness -- indeed, a poem might have been written to her slimness, it was so exquisitely proportioned. Her hair was of real bronze, with the greenish tint in its gold; she had a determined little chin, a lovely nose, slanting blue eyes that showed a gleam of deepest cornflower between the half-closed lids, and a delicious and quite indescribable mouth that tilted ever so slightly at one corner in what is known as "the signature of Venus." It was a wonderfully expressive face, and there was a sort of radiant vitality about her that always challenged attention. It would have been quite impossible ever to ignore Virginia Revel. [004-029]

Her personal style and attitude toward romance were unconventional for her time. When pressed by [[bill-eversleigh|Bill Eversleigh]] to marry him, she demurred with characteristic frankness: "I haven't got to marry anyone. I love being a wicked widow" [004-071]. She had no intention of rushing into matrimony, telling Bill that she was "the kind of person who marries enthusiastically if they marry at all" [004-070]. Her independent spirit was reflected in her playful declaration that she suspected herself of being "polyandrous" [004-082], a remark that disarmed and delighted in equal measure.

When [[george-lomax|George Lomax]] arrived at her house for tea, Virginia "flung open both windows, and [[george-lomax|George Lomax]] came into the room to find her standing erect with dancing eyes and windblown hair" -- a deliberate display of independence in the face of Lomax's suffocating formality [007-009].

## Recruitment to the Conference

[[george-lomax|George Lomax]] identified Virginia as the ideal political hostess, reasoning: "A woman could handle the whole thing delicately and with tact -- put the position before him, as it were, without getting his back up. Not that I approve of women in politics -- St. Stephen's is ruined, absolutely ruined, nowadays. But woman in her own sphere can do wonders" [003-065].

[[lord-caterham|Lord Caterham]] concurred enthusiastically: "A very good suggestion of yours, Lomax. The most charming woman in [[london|London]]" [003-075]. Virginia's late husband, Tim Revel, had been attached to the British Embassy in [[herzoslovakia|Herzoslovakia]], giving her firsthand knowledge of the Balkan political situation that Lomax sought to exploit [003-075].

Lomax's original plan was for Virginia to charm a Canadian journalist, Jimmy McGrath, into deferring publication of Count Stylptitch's memoirs. Virginia accepted the assignment with characteristic good humour and an undercurrent of scepticism about the Foreign Office's priorities.

## The Blackmail Incident

Before her departure for [[chimneys|Chimneys]], Virginia was confronted at her Pont Street home by a foreign stranger who attempted to blackmail her with a packet of love letters, supposedly written by her to a Captain O'Neill in Paris [006-001]--[006-005]. Virginia immediately recognised that the letters were forgeries -- the signature on them was her name, but the handwriting was not hers [006-041]--[006-044]. Rather than calling the police, she chose a more unorthodox response. When [[george-lomax|George Lomax]] arrived for tea moments after the blackmailer's departure and expressed horror at her course of action, she explained:

> "Several reasons. To begin with he was doing it so beautifully -- blackmailing me, I mean -- I hate to interrupt an artist when he's doing his job really well. And then, you see, I'd never been blackmailed -- And I wanted to see what it felt like." [006-100]--[006-102]

She greeted Lomax's arrival with breezy insouciance: "I'll shut them in a minute, George, but I felt the room ought to be aired. Did you fall over the blackmailer in the hall?" [006-076] When Lomax sputtered "The what?", Virginia spelled it out for him: "Blackmailer, George. B.l.a.c.k.m.a.i.l.e.r? Blackmailer. One who blackmails" [006-078]. The entire exchange epitomised her refusal to be intimidated -- whether by criminals, or by the ponderous self-importance of her cousin.

The forged letters, it later emerged, had been written from [[chimneys|Chimneys]] during an earlier house party [015-063]. The existence of a "second Virginia Revel" became one of the story's central mysteries, and it was a mark of [[anthony-cade|Anthony Cade]]'s perceptiveness that he immediately dismissed any suspicion that the letters were genuine. As he told her:

> "I don't know anything about your moral character. You might have a lover, and you might write to him. But you'd never lie down to be blackmailed. The Virginia Revel of those letters was scared stiff. You'd have fought." [015-060]

This assessment -- recognising Virginia's fundamental courage and refusal to be victimised -- was the foundation of the trust and partnership that grew between them.

## Role at Chimneys

At [[chimneys|Chimneys]], Virginia's role extended far beyond social hosting. She became a confidante to [[anthony-cade|Anthony Cade]], assisted in the investigation of Prince Michael's murder, and demonstrated coolness under pressure. She was unafraid to speak her mind about the political machinations around her, noting drily when asked who might have benefited from the murder: "My cousin [[george-lomax|George Lomax]] does" [009-073].

She participated actively in the investigation, joining the confidential discussions in the Council Chamber alongside [[lady-eileen-bundle-brent|Bundle Brent]], [[superintendent-battle|Superintendent Battle]], [[monsieur-lemoine|M. Lemoine]], and [[anthony-cade|Anthony Cade]] [023-001]. When Bundle's explanations of secret passages grew tangled, Virginia's encouraging "You can when you try" [023-007] revealed her gift for drawing out the best in those around her.

Her relationship with [[anthony-cade|Anthony Cade]] developed from amused acquaintance to genuine partnership. Their first substantive conversation, walking by the lake at [[chimneys|Chimneys]], ranged from the murder investigation to the double mystery of the false Virginia Revel [015-009]--[015-082]. Anthony found in her a kindred spirit -- someone who, like himself, was willing to bend rules in pursuit of a just outcome.

### The Midnight Adventure

Virginia played an active part in the nighttime investigation at [[chimneys|Chimneys]]. When Bill Eversleigh awoke from a vivid dream of rescuing her from a burning building [017-005], it was Virginia herself who was shaking him awake, enlisting his aid to investigate suspicious activity in the Council Chamber. Her imperious instruction that Bill remove his boots before proceeding -- "You'll have to take them off" [017-042] -- and her teasing about his pyjamas: "I like your pyjamas, Bill. Brightness without vulgarity" [017-022], showed her characteristic blend of practical competence and irreverent wit even in dangerous circumstances.

## Marriage and Aftermath

The final revelation of the mystery brought with it a personal one: [[anthony-cade|Anthony Cade]] was in truth Prince Nicholas Obolovitch of [[herzoslovakia|Herzoslovakia]]. Virginia, far from being dismayed to discover she had unwittingly married royalty, declared it "perfectly screaming" and announced she would "love it" [030-063]--[030-064]. She looked forward to their new life with characteristic gusto:

> "We'll have a lot of fun. Teaching the brigands not to be brigands, and the assassins not to assassinate, and generally improving the moral tone of the country." [030-071]

When Anthony asked if she loved him, her answer -- "Not a bit!" [030-081] -- was delivered "very low, in a sweet shaken voice" with her head resting against his shoulder [030-080], prompting Anthony to declare: "You little devil. Now I know for certain that I shall love you until I die" [030-082]. The exchange captured everything essential about Virginia Revel: her refusal to be predictable, her delight in confounding expectations, and the depth of feeling beneath her gleaming, mischievous surface.
