# Foreign Office

The British Foreign Office, represented in the novel by the Hon. [[george-lomax]], is the government department that transforms [[chimneys]] from a private country estate into the stage for international diplomacy. It is the engine behind the [[herzoslovakia]] royalist restoration plan — and the force whose political imperatives collide with criminal conspiracy at [[lord-caterham]]'s house party.

## Role in the Novel

The Foreign Office embodies the official, bureaucratic interest in [[herzoslovakia|Herzoslovakia]]: securing oil concessions for British companies, supporting a friendly monarch ([[prince-michael-obolovitch]]), and preventing diplomatic scandal. Its methods — secrecy, manipulation, and the use of informal settings for formal negotiations — reflect the early-twentieth-century tradition of British "clubland diplomacy."

## Key Operations

### The Herzoslovakian Restoration

The Foreign Office's primary objective is to restore the [[obolovitch-dynasty]] in [[herzoslovakia]] with British backing. This involves:

- Supporting [[prince-michael-obolovitch]] as the preferred candidate for the throne
- Brokering financial arrangements between the prince and [[herman-isaacstein]]'s syndicate
- Ensuring oil concessions are granted to British companies, not American competitors
- Using [[chimneys]] as the informal venue for these sensitive negotiations

As [[george-lomax]] explains: "One slip over this [[herzoslovakia|Herzoslovakia]]n business and we're done. It is most important that the Oil concessions should be granted to a British company" (003-PN).

### The Memoirs Crisis

The Foreign Office is deeply alarmed by [[count-stylptitch]]'s memoirs, which threaten to expose sensitive diplomatic secrets from the late statesman's time in power. Lomax arranges for the suppression or controlled release of these documents, dispatching agents to [[paris|Paris]] and working to intercept [[count-stylptitch-memoirs|the memoirs]] before they reach public circulation.

### The House Party

The shooting party at [[chimneys]] is the Foreign Office's creation — a cover for the diplomatic negotiations between Prince Michael, [[herman-isaacstein|Isaacstein]], and interested parties. Lomax orchestrates the guest list, choosing [[virginia-revel]] for her diplomatic knowledge, [[bill-eversleigh]] for his utility, and excluding himself ("it wouldn't do... for me to be actually a member of the house party") while remaining nearby at the Abbey (003-PN).

## Personnel

### George Lomax

[[george-lomax]] is the novel's embodiment of the Foreign Office — "a robust man, inclined to embonpoint, with a red face and protuberant eyes, and an immense sense of his own importance" (003-PN). His manner is pompous, his methods secretive, and his ability to bore [[lord-caterham]] is seemingly limitless. He represents the bureaucratic formality that the novel's more adventurous characters consistently subvert.

### Bill Eversleigh

[[bill-eversleigh]] is Lomax's junior aide, deployed to [[chimneys]] as a messenger and facilitator. Unlike his superior, Bill is easygoing and personally popular with the other guests, though his diplomatic subtlety is limited.

### Virginia Revel

[[virginia-revel]] is co-opted by Lomax as an informal diplomatic asset — her charm, intelligence, and knowledge of [[herzoslovakia|Herzoslovakia]] (her husband served at the embassy there) make her an ideal intermediary for handling the delicate matter of [[james-mcgrath]] and [[count-stylptitch-memoirs|the memoirs]].

## Political Context

The Foreign Office's involvement in [[herzoslovakia|Herzoslovakia]] reflects the real-world British diplomatic approach to the [[balkans|Balkans]] in the early twentieth century: supporting friendly regimes, securing commercial advantages (particularly oil rights, as the age of petroleum dawned), and managing the fallout of the collapsed Ottoman and Austro-Hungarian empires. Christie's portrayal is both affectionate and gently satirical — the Foreign Office's earnest diplomatic machinery is repeatedly tripped up by murder, romance, and the sheer unwillingness of [[lord-caterham]] to cooperate.
