## Overview

Prince Nicholas Obolovitch is a member of the [[herzoslovakia|Herzoslovakia]]n royal family, the first cousin of [[prince-michael-obolovitch|Prince Michael Obolovitch]]. After Prince Michael's murder, Prince Nicholas becomes the next heir to the [[herzoslovakia|Herzoslovakia]]n throne. However, his identity becomes the subject of a sophisticated criminal scheme — [[king-victor|King Victor]], the master international thief, impersonates Prince Nicholas to extract money from American investors interested in [[herzoslovakia|Herzoslovakia]]n oil concessions.

## Royal Lineage

Prince Nicholas belongs to the House of Obolovitch, the ruling dynasty of [[herzoslovakia|Herzoslovakia]] until the assassination of [[nicholas-iv|King Nicholas IV]] approximately seven years before the events of the novel [001-027]. The Obolovitch dynasty had been in abeyance since the "martyrdom of [[nicholas-iv|His Most Gracious Majesty King Nicholas IV]] of blessed memory" [007-022].

When [[prince-michael-obolovitch|Prince Michael Obolovitch]] is murdered at [[chimneys|Chimneys]], Prince Nicholas becomes the next heir. As [[superintendent-battle|Superintendent Battle]] confirms: "in all probability Prince Nicholas would be the next heir" [014-041]. [[george-lomax|George Lomax]] describes him as Prince Michael's "first cousin" [014-043].

> The Obolovitch dynasty is extinct. There are probably shoals of young ones knocking about, cousins and second cousins and third cousins once removed. [001-048]

## The Impersonation

The most significant aspect of Prince Nicholas's role in the novel is not his direct presence but the exploitation of his identity. Prince Nicholas was rumoured to have died in the Congo years before, and [[king-victor|King Victor]] seized upon this uncertainty to resurrect him [029-045].

As [[monsieur-lemoine|Monsieur Lemoine]] of the Sûreté explains, [[king-victor|King Victor]] "resurrects Prince Nicholas, and plays him to such purpose that he gets away with a tremendous haul of American dollars — all on account of the supposed oil concessions" [029-045]. The impersonation was so convincing that it took in American investors and nearly succeeded before [[king-victor|King Victor]] was "unmasked, and had to leave the country hurriedly" [029-045].

> What part do you think he played over there? The part of Prince Nicholas of Herzoslovakia. [029-043]

## Role in the Political Intrigue

The question of Prince Nicholas's whereabouts becomes a significant concern for the British Government. [[superintendent-battle|Battle]] asks pointedly about the Prince's location — "where he is at present" [014-043] — because whoever controls the heir to the throne potentially controls [[herzoslovakia|Herzoslovakia]]'s political future.

The uncertainty surrounding Prince Nicholas's fate (whether he truly died in the Congo or is still alive) creates the "colossal bluff" [029-045] that [[king-victor|King Victor]] exploits. This ambiguity is central to the novel's resolution, as it allows the criminal conspiracy to function and creates additional suspects and motives for Prince Michael's murder.

- **[[king-victor|King Victor]]**: The master criminal who impersonates Prince Nicholas to defraud American investors [029-043]
- **[[superintendent-battle|Superintendent Battle]]**: [[superintendent-battle|Battle]] is keenly interested in Prince Nicholas's whereabouts [014-043]

## Significance

Prince Nicholas Obolovitch is one of the novel's most important off-stage characters — a man whose very existence (or non-existence) shapes the actions of every major player. The question of whether he is alive or dead, and who might be impersonating him, is central to the mystery's resolution. His case illustrates the novel's theme of identity and impersonation, which runs through multiple character arcs.
