## Role in the Novel

Constable Johnson is present at Chimneys from the discovery of Prince Michael's body
through to the novel's epilogue. As Inspector Badgworthy's attendant, he provides a
local perspective on the extraordinary events.

Johnson is described as "very new to the Force, with a downy unfledged look about him,
like a human chicken" [010-001]. Despite his inexperience, he proves observant and
useful: his brother is the local railway porter, giving Johnson detailed knowledge
of all arrivals and departures at the station [010-010].

In the novel's final chapter, Johnson reappears digging in the garden at Chimneys,
searching for the missing Koh-i-Noor diamond [031-001]. It is during this excavation
that the diamond is recovered — Johnson's digging uncovers the jewel that has been
the object of so much pursuit [031-002].

## Description

Johnson is young and "downy unfledged" [010-001]. He is suitably impressed by the
gravity of a murder case, echoing "Murder" when Inspector Badgworthy tells him
the news [010-004]. He has a local's pragmatic worldview: he is "sorry it were a
foreigner" because "Foreigners, Johnson felt, were liable to be shot" — a murder
of a foreign gentleman seems less real to him than one of a local person [010-007].

Johnson's defining professional advantage is his family connection — his brother
the railway porter keeps him "always well up in arrivals and departures" at the
local station [010-010]. He can recite the complete passenger manifest for the
previous day's trains [010-012].

## Key Relationships

- **Inspector Badgworthy**: Johnson's superior officer [010-001]
- **Dr. Cartwright**: Fellow local official, shares excitement at the murder case [010-002]
- **Lord Caterham**: The owner of Chimneys, to whose summons they respond [010-004]
- **Superintendent Battle**: The Scotland Yard detective who takes over the case

## Key Events

- Accompanies Inspector Badgworthy to Chimneys upon receiving Lord Caterham's
  telephone call about the murder [010-001]
- Provides crucial information about train arrivals, helping establish that the
  dead man did not arrive by rail [010-010]
- Takes notes on Inspector Badgworthy's instructions to investigate at the Cricketers
  inn [010-014]
- In the epilogue (Chapter 31), digs in the Chimneys garden for the missing
  Koh-i-Noor diamond [031-001]
- Uncovers the diamond during the excavation — one of the novel's final revelations
  [031-002]

## Significance

Constable Johnson serves as the "everyman" perspective on the extraordinary events
at Chimneys — a young local constable thrust into a case involving Balkan politics,
international jewel thieves, and Scotland Yard. His railway intelligence provides
a crucial piece of the investigation, and his final act of digging up the diamond
brings the novel's central mystery to a satisfying conclusion.
