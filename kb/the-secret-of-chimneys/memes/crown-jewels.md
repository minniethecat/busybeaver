## Role in the Novel

The Herzoslovakian Crown Jewels serve as a testament to the criminal nature of Queen Varaga (Angèle Mory). After the assassination of the king and queen during the revolution, many of the Crown Jewels fell into the hands of the mob and found their way to Paris [4561-4561]. Upon examination, it was discovered that in nine cases out of ten, the principal stones had been replaced by paste.

## The Paste Substitution

The jewel substitution reveals that Angèle Mory, even after becoming Queen Varaga of Herzoslovakia, continued to practice her former criminal activities [4561-4561]. The jewels included some very famous stones among the Herzoslovakian collection. The systematic replacement of genuine gems with paste demonstrates both her skill and her ongoing connection to the Parisian underworld.

## Connection to the Broader Plot

The Crown Jewels affair ties together several threads of the novel's backstory: Angèle Mory's rise from Parisian actress to queen, her betrayal of the Comrades of the Red Hand, and the revolution that ultimately claimed both her life and the king's. The jewels also connect to the novel's central theme of authenticity versus deception.

> "After the assassination of the king and queen, many of the Crown Jewels which had fallen, of course, into the hands of the mob, found their way to Paris, and it was discovered that in nine cases out of ten the principal stones had been replaced by paste—and mind you, there were some very famous stones among the jewels of Herzoslovakia. So as a queen, Angèle Mory still practised her former activities." [4561-4561]
