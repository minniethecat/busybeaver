## Summary

A lunch at [[chimneys|Chimneys]] brings together an awkwardly heterogeneous assembly of guests during the investigation. The meal is described as "somewhat of an ordeal" as diplomats, detectives, children, and suspects gather around the same table [1832-1832].

## Context

The lunch party includes [[lord-caterham|Lord Caterham]], who is "lethargic and depressed"; [[bill-eversleigh|Bill Eversleigh]], staring "longingly at [[virginia-revel|Virginia]]"; [[george-lomax|George Lomax]], conversing "weightily with [[baron-lolopretjzyl|the Baron]] and [[herman-isaacstein|Mr. Isaacstein]]"; and the children Guggle and Winkle, "completely beside themselves with joy of having a murder in the house" [1832-1832]. [[hiram-fish|Mr. Hiram Fish]] "slowly masticated his food, and drawled out dry remarks in his own peculiar idiom," while [[superintendent-battle|Superintendent Battle]] "had considerately vanished, and nobody knew what had become of him" [1832-1832].

The Baron and [[captain-andrassy|Captain Andrassy]] are "correct, formal, full of etiquette," having "the air of attending a meal in a mausoleum" [1832-1832]. The juxtaposition of formal diplomatic etiquette with the chaotic presence of excited children and the underlying tension of an active murder investigation creates the distinctly awkward atmosphere.

[[lord-caterham|Lord Caterham]] brightens only at the prospect of the meal itself, having previously remarked on finding "talking to foreigners particularly" tiring [1819-1819].
