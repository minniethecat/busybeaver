# Rebellion

Rebellion appears in Chesterton's essays in three distinct keys: as a political category he explicitly distinguishes from the relation between the sexes, as a half-remembered romantic inheritance in Scottish music, and as a word misapplied — with characteristic perversity — to those whose real offence is not defiance but submission. The thread connecting these instances is Chesterton's persistent interest in whether a word names a reality or obscures one.

## Rebellion Distinguished from the Sexes

In the Front Matter, Chesterton makes a sharp conceptual distinction: "It is ultimately idiotic to compare this prehistoric participation with any royalties or rebellions. Genuine royalties wish to crush rebellions. Genuine rebels wish to destroy kings. The sexes cannot wish to abolish each other" (P01-088). The argument is structural, not sentimental. Rebellion belongs to the logic of political opposition — one party wishes to displace another. But marriage, in Chesterton's view, is "rooted in an aboriginal unity of instincts" and therefore cannot be modelled on the pattern of rebellion and suppression. The distinction reveals his deeper conviction that political categories misdescribe domestic realities.

## The Romantic Air of Scottish Rebellion

In "The Mystagogue," Chesterton encounters a band playing Jacobite tunes — "Some of the real airs of the old Scottish rebellion were played, such as 'Charlie is My Darling,' or 'What's a' the steer, kimmer?'" (014-167). He notes with delight the paradox: "songs that men had sung while marching to destroy and drive out the monarchy under which we live," songs in which "the actual words 'King George' occurred as a curse and a derision" — yet played without political consequence, absorbed into the atmosphere of a modern gathering. Rebellion here has been aestheticised, its political charge drained away into melody. Chesterton does not condemn this; he is too interested in the fact that it can happen at all.

## The Misapplied Accusation

"The New Theologian" offers the sharpest instance of Chesterton's nominalism about rebellion: "They must accuse [[edward-carson|Sir Edward Carson]] of outrageous rebellion, when his offence has really been a sleek submission to the powers that be" (017-087). The sentence turns on the word "really" — the public language of political accusation has exactly reversed the truth. Carson's offence is not defiance but conformity; the charge of "rebellion" is a rhetorical convenience, not a description. Chesterton sees in this misfit between word and deed a pattern that extends to Lloyd George, to [[dean-inge|Dean Inge]], and beyond — "a mysterious misfit of names" that pervades modern public discourse. Rebellion, in this usage, is what you call behaviour you wish to condemn, regardless of whether the behaviour is in fact rebellious.
