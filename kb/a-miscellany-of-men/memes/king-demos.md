## Dean Inge's Irony

The figure of King Demos appears in Chesterton's essay on [[dean-inge|Dean Inge]], the "Gloomy Dean" of St. Paul's Cathedral.

Chesterton reports that Inge — whom he regards as a "New Theologian, liberal in theology and nothing else" — had dismissed Christian Socialists as "court chaplains of King Demos" (018-013). This deployed the classical personification of the people as a monarch to suggest that reformers were merely flattering the masses.

Chesterton immediately identifies the irony: Inge considered himself "highly progressive and advanced," yet his rhetoric deployed the oldest of anti-democratic tropes (018-019–018-020).

## Demos Has Never Been King

Chesterton's rebuttal is direct and historically grounded. "We should not expect a mere old-fashioned country clergyman to know that Demos has never been king in [[england|England]] and precious seldom anywhere else" (018-015–018-016).

He adds: "we should not expect him to realise that if King Demos had any chaplains they would be uncommonly poorly paid" (018-017–018-018). The personification is thus doubly false: democracy has not ruled, and its supposed priests have not profited. Chesterton exposes the rhetorical trick of treating "the people" as a sovereign when actual power tells a different story.

## Chesterton's Democratic Vision

King Demos serves as a vehicle for Chesterton's own democratic convictions. Throughout "A Miscellany of Men," he argues that genuine popular rule has rarely been tried — what passes for democracy is often oligarchy wearing a popular mask.

The image of King Demos as a monarch without power or treasury encapsulates this critique: the people are rhetorically enthroned but practically dispossessed. Chesterton's alternative is not the abolition of Demos but his actual coronation — a distributist democracy in which ordinary people exercise real economic and political sovereignty.
