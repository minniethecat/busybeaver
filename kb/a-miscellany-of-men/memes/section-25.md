# The Contented Man

[024-001]THE CONTENTED MAN

[024-002]The word content is not inspiring nowadays; rather it is irritating

[024-003]because it is dull. It prepares the mind for a little sermon in the

[024-004]style of the Vicar of Wakefield about how you and I should be satisfied

[024-005]with our countrified innocence and our simple village sports. The word,

[024-006]however, has two meanings, somewhat singularly connected; the “sweet

[024-007]content” of the poet and the “cubic content” of the mathematician. Some

[024-008]distinguish these by stressing the different syllables. Thus, it might

[024-009]happen to any of us, at some social juncture, to remark gaily, “Of the

[024-010]content of the King of the Cannibal Islands' Stewpot I am content to be

[024-011]ignorant”; or “Not content with measuring the cubic content of my safe,

[024-012]you are stealing the spoons.” And there really is an analogy between the

[024-013]mathematical and the moral use of the term, for lack of the observation

[024-014]of which the latter has been much weakened and misused.

[024-015]The preaching of contentment is in disrepute, well deserved in so far

[024-016]that the moral is really quite inapplicable to the anarchy and insane

[024-017]peril of our tall and toppling cities. Content suggests some kind of

[024-018]security; and it is not strange that our workers should often think

[024-019]about rising above their position, since they have so continually to

[024-020]think about sinking below it. The philanthropist who urges the poor to

[024-021]saving and simple pleasures deserves all the derision that he gets. To

[024-022]advise people to be content with what they have got may or may not be

[024-023]sound moral philosophy.

[024-024]But to urge people to be content with what they haven't got is a piece

[024-025]of impudence hard for even the English poor to pardon. But though the

[024-026]creed of content is unsuited to certain special riddles and wrongs,

[024-027]it remains true for the normal of mortal life. We speak of divine

[024-028]discontent; discontent may sometimes be a divine thing, but content must

[024-029]always be the human thing. It may be true that a particular man, in his

[024-030]relation to his master or his neighbour, to his country or his enemies,

[024-031]will do well to be fiercely unsatisfied or thirsting for an angry

[024-032]justice. But it is not true, no sane person can call it true, that man

[024-033]as a whole in his general attitude towards the world, in his posture

[024-034]towards death or green fields, towards the weather or the baby, will be

[024-035]wise to cultivate dissatisfaction. In a broad estimate of our earthly

[024-036]experience, the great truism on the tablet remains: he must not covet

[024-037]his neighbour's ox nor his ass nor anything that is his. In highly

[024-038]complex and scientific civilisations he may sometimes find himself

[024-039]forced into an exceptional vigilance. But, then, in highly complex and

[024-040]scientific civilisations, nine times out of ten, he only wants his own

[024-041]ass back.

[024-042]But I wish to urge the case for cubic content; in which (even more than

[024-043]in moral content) I take a personal interest. Now, moral content has

[024-044]been undervalued and neglected because of its separation from the other

[024-045]meaning. It has become a negative rather than a positive thing. In some

[024-046]accounts of contentment it seems to be little more than a meek despair.

[024-047]But this is not the true meaning of the term; it should stand for the

[024-048]idea of a positive and thorough appreciation of the content of anything;

[024-049]for feeling the substance and not merely the surface of experience.

[024-050]“Content” ought to mean in English, as it does in French, being pleased;

[024-051]placidly, perhaps, but still positively pleased. Being contented with

[024-052]bread and cheese ought not to mean not caring what you eat. It ought

[024-053]to mean caring for bread and cheese; handling and enjoying the cubic

[024-054]content of the bread and cheese and adding it to your own. Being

[024-055]content with an attic ought not to mean being unable to move from it and

[024-056]resigned to living in it. It ought to mean appreciating what there is

[024-057]to appreciate in such a position; such as the quaint and elvish slope of

[024-058]the ceiling or the sublime aerial view of the opposite chimney-pots. And

[024-059]in this sense contentment is a real and even an active virtue; it is not

[024-060]only affirmative, but creative. The poet in the attic does not forget

[024-061]the attic in poetic musings; he remembers whatever the attic has of

[024-062]poetry; he realises how high, how starry, how cool, how unadorned and

[024-063]simple--in short, how Attic is the attic.

[024-064]True contentment is a thing as active as agriculture. It is the power of

[024-065]getting out of any situation all that there is in it. It is arduous and

[024-066]it is rare. The absence of this digestive talent is what makes so

[024-067]cold and incredible the tales of so many people who say they have been

[024-068]“through” things; when it is evident that they have come out on the

[024-069]other side quite unchanged. A man might have gone “through” a plum

[024-070]pudding as a bullet might go through a plum pudding; it depends on the

[024-071]size of the pudding--and the man. But the awful and sacred question

[024-072]is “Has the pudding been through him?” Has he tasted, appreciated, and

[024-073]absorbed the solid pudding, with its three dimensions and its three

[024-074]thousand tastes and smells? Can he offer himself to the eyes of men as

[024-075]one who has cubically conquered and contained a pudding?

[024-076]In the same way we may ask of those who profess to have passed through

[024-077]trivial or tragic experiences whether they have absorbed the content

[024-078]of them; whether they licked up such living water as there was. It is a

[024-079]pertinent question in connection with many modern problems.

[024-080]Thus the young genius says, “I have lived in my dreary and squalid

[024-081]village before I found success in Paris or Vienna.” The sound

[024-082]philosopher will answer, “You have never lived in your village, or you

[024-083]would not call it dreary and squalid.”

[024-084]Thus the Imperialist, the Colonial idealist (who commonly speaks and

[024-085]always thinks with a Yankee accent) will say, “I've been right away from

[024-086]these little muddy islands, and seen God's great seas and prairies.” The

[024-087]sound philosopher will reply, “You have never been in these islands; you

[024-088]have never seen the weald of Sussex or the plain of Salisbury; otherwise

[024-089]you could never have called them either muddy or little.”

[024-090]Thus the Suffragette will say, “I have passed through the paltry duties

[024-091]of pots and pans, the drudgery of the vulgar kitchen; but I have come

[024-092]out to intellectual liberty.” The sound philosopher will answer, “You

[024-093]have never passed through the kitchen, or you never would call it

[024-094]vulgar. Wiser and stronger women than you have really seen a poetry

[024-095]in pots and pans; naturally, because there is a poetry in them.” It is

[024-096]right for the village violinist to climb into fame in Paris or Vienna;

[024-097]it is right for the stray Englishman to climb across the high shoulder

[024-098]of the world; it is right for the woman to climb into whatever cathedrae

[024-099]or high places she can allow to her sexual dignity. But it is wrong that

[024-100]any of these climbers should kick the ladder by which they have climbed.

[024-101]But indeed these bitter people who record their experiences really

[024-102]record their lack of experiences. It is the countryman who has not

[024-103]succeeded in being a countryman who comes up to London. It is the

[024-104]clerk who has not succeeded in being a clerk who tries (on vegetarian

[024-105]principles) to be a countryman. And the woman with a past is generally a

[024-106]woman angry about the past she never had.

[024-107]When you have really exhausted an experience you always reverence and

[024-108]love it. The two things that nearly all of us have thoroughly and really

[024-109]been through are childhood and youth. And though we would not have them

[024-110]back again on any account, we feel that they are both beautiful, because

[024-111]we have drunk them dry.

[024-112]THE ANGRY AUTHOR: HIS FAREWELL

[024-113]I have republished all these old articles of mine because they cover

[024-114]a very controversial period, in which I was in nearly all the

[024-115]controversies, whether I was visible there or no. And I wish to gather

[024-116]up into this last article a valedictory violence about all such things;

[024-117]and then pass to where, beyond these voices, there is peace--or in

[024-118]other words, to the writing of Penny Dreadfuls; a noble and much-needed

[024-119]work. But before I finally desert the illusions of rationalism for

[024-120]the actualities of romance, I should very much like to write one last

[024-121]roaring, raging book telling all the rationalists not to be so utterly

[024-122]irrational. The book would be simply a string of violent vetoes, like

[024-123]the Ten Commandments. I would call it “Don'ts for Dogmatists; or Things

[024-124]I am Tired Of.”

[024-125]This book of intellectual etiquette, like most books of etiquette, would

[024-126]begin with superficial things; but there would be, I fancy, a wailing

[024-127]imprecation in the words that could not be called artificial; it might

[024-128]begin thus:--

[024-129](1) Don't use a noun and then an adjective that crosses out the noun.

[024-130]An adjective qualifies, it cannot contradict. Don't say, “Give me a

[024-131]patriotism that is free from all boundaries.” It is like saying, “Give

[024-132]me a pork pie with no pork in it.” Don't say, “I look forward to that

[024-133]larger religion that shall have no special dogmas.” It is like saying,

[024-134]“I look forward to that larger quadruped who shall have no feet.” A

[024-135]quadruped means something with four feet; and a religion means something

[024-136]that commits a man to some doctrine about the universe. Don't let

[024-137]the meek substantive be absolutely murdered by the joyful, exuberant

[024-138]adjective.

[024-139](2) Don't say you are not going to say a thing, and then say it. This

[024-140]practice is very flourishing and successful with public speakers. The

[024-141]trick consists of first repudiating a certain view in unfavourable

[024-142]terms, and then repeating the same view in favourable terms. Perhaps the

[024-143]simplest form of it may be found in a landlord of my neighbourhood, who

[024-144]said to his tenants in an election speech, “Of course I'm not going to

[024-145]threaten you, but if this Budget passes the rents will go up.” The thing

[024-146]can be done in many forms besides this. “I am the last man to

[024-147]mention party politics; but when I see the Empire rent in pieces by

[024-148]irresponsible Radicals,” etc. “In this hall we welcome all creeds. We

[024-149]have no hostility against any honest belief; but only against that black

[024-150]priestcraft and superstition which can accept such a doctrine as,” etc.

[024-151]“I would not say one word that could ruffle our relations with Germany.

[024-152]But this I will say; that when I see ceaseless and unscrupulous

[024-153]armament,” etc. Please don't do it. Decide to make a remark or not to

[024-154]make a remark. But don't fancy that you have somehow softened the saying

[024-155]of a thing by having just promised not to say it.

[024-156](3) Don't use secondary words as primary words. “Happiness” (let us say)

[024-157]is a primary word. You know when you have the thing, and you jolly well

[024-158]know when you haven't. “Progress” is a secondary word; it means the

[024-159]degree of one's approach to happiness, or to some such solid ideal. But

[024-160]modern controversies constantly turn on asking, “Does Happiness help

[024-161]Progress?” Thus, I see in the New Age this week a letter from Mr.

[024-162]Egerton Swann, in which he warns the world against me and my friend Mr.

[024-163]Belloc, on the ground that our democracy is “spasmodic” (whatever that

[024-164]means); while our “reactionism is settled and permanent.” It never

[024-165]strikes Mr. Swann that democracy means something in itself; while

[024-166]“reactionism” means nothing--except in connection with democracy.

[024-167]You cannot react except from something. If Mr. Swann thinks I have ever

[024-168]reacted from the doctrine that the people should rule, I wish he would

[024-169]give me the reference.

[024-170](4) Don't say, “There is no true creed; for each creed believes itself

[024-171]right and the others wrong.” Probably one of the creeds is right and

[024-172]the others are wrong. Diversity does show that most of the views must

[024-173]be wrong. It does not by the faintest logic show that they all must be

[024-174]wrong. I suppose there is no subject on which opinions differ with more

[024-175]desperate sincerity than about which horse will win the Derby. These are

[024-176]certainly solemn convictions; men risk ruin for them. The man who puts

[024-177]his shirt on Potosi must believe in that animal, and each of the other

[024-178]men putting their last garments upon other quadrupeds must believe in

[024-179]them quite as sincerely. They are all serious, and most of them are

[024-180]wrong. But one of them is right. One of the faiths is justified; one of

[024-181]the horses does win; not always even the dark horse which might stand

[024-182]for Agnosticism, but often the obvious and popular horse of Orthodoxy.

[024-183]Democracy has its occasional victories; and even the Favourite has been

[024-184]known to come in first. But the point here is that something comes in

[024-185]first. That there were many beliefs does not destroy the fact that there

[024-186]was one well-founded belief. I believe (merely upon authority) that the

[024-187]world is round. That there may be tribes who believe it to be triangular

[024-188]or oblong does not alter the fact that it is certainly some shape, and

[024-189]therefore not any other shape. Therefore I repeat, with the wail of

[024-190]imprecation, don't say that the variety of creeds prevents you from

[024-191]accepting any creed. It is an unintelligent remark.
