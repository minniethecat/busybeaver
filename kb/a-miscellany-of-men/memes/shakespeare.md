William Shakespeare (1564–1616) appears in Chesterton's "A Miscellany of Men" not as a subject of literary analysis but as the unwitting participant in a journalistic anecdote that illustrates Chesterton's broader argument about haste, misunderstanding, and the public's readiness to assume the worst of the press.

## The Gray's Elegy Anecdote

In the essay "The Man Who Thinks Backwards" (section-10), Chesterton recounts an episode from his own journalistic career. Writing a denunciation of a theological opponent, he deliberately misattributed the line "that wreathes its old fantastic roots so high" to Shakespeare, when in fact it belonged to Thomas Gray's "Elegy in a Country Churchyard" (010-031–010-035). The misattribution was itself part of the argument — a deliberate error meant to be caught. But what followed was not recognition of the joke: Chesterton received some twenty-five letters from correspondents, none of whom conceived it could be a mistake (010-036–010-038).

The responses revealed a peculiar social dynamic. One reader professed to have read through "all Shakespeare" and failed to find the line (010-042). Another asked in moral distress whether Gray was really a plagiarist (010-043–010-044). The editor, publishing Chesterton's correction, titled it "'Mr. Chesterton Explains'" with sarcastic quotation marks, implying that Chesterton was a man "who doesn't know Gray from Shakespeare" (010-055–010-056).

## Chesterton's Point About Haste

The anecdote serves a larger purpose in Chesterton's essay. He argues that the public imagines the journalist's life as one of leisured exactitude, when in reality it is governed by deadline pressure and haste. The misattribution was not the result of ignorance but of the mechanical conditions of journalism — "the pencil staggers along, making the world a present of fifteen hundred unimportant words, and making Shakespeare a present of a portion of Gray's Elegy" (010-101–010-102). The journalist, Chesterton suggests, turns his attention next to "the enigma of whether a brother should commandeer a sister's necklace" — domestic trivia arriving mid-composition (010-104–010-105).

## A Brief Early Reference

Shakespeare also appears in an earlier essay, "The Thing" (section-4), where Chesterton describes a monomaniac who studies "cryptograms in Shakespeare through monstrously magnifying spectacles" (004-167). This passing reference uses Shakespeare as a symbol of the kind of canonical text that attracts obsessive, conspiratorial reading — a warning about the hermeneutics of paranoia.
