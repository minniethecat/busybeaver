## A Youthful Creation

G.K. Chesterton recounts how, as a young man, he encountered an empty house on a country road. Standing before its blank walls, he gave it an imaginary inhabitant.

Chesterton "gave, with all the sky-deep gravity of youth, a benediction upon the man who was going to live there," and "for the convenience of meditation" named him James Harrogate (008-167). This act of naming reflects his conviction that houses exist for human dwelling and that imagination fills the void left by absence.

## The Red Chalk Poem

Chesterton did not merely name his creation — he inscribed verses upon the walls. In what he "supposed to be the dining-room," he wrote a graceless but earnest grace (009-006):

> James Harrogate, thank God for meat, (009-006)
> Then eat and eat and eat and eat, (009-006)

He also scrawled "some longer lyric" upon a bedroom wall, beginning: "When laying what you call your head, O Harrogate, upon your bed" (009-011–009-013). The poems, written in red chalk, were Chesterton's youthful blessing upon domestic life — a benediction disguised as doggerel that celebrated the ordinary sacraments of eating and sleeping.

## The Return After Forty Years

Some four decades later, Chesterton returned to the same road and found the house transformed. Its windows "glowed with lights of many artificial sorts," and from within came "a stream of lamplight and a stream of singing" (009-019–009-021).

A girl stood at a piano, filling the house with "healthy sentimentalism" where once his blessing had "died on the wind and my poems been covered up by the wallpaper" (009-023–009-024).

In a moment of mad impulse, Chesterton rang the bell. He asked the suburban maid: "Does Mr. James Harrogate live here?" She replied that he did not, but offered to inquire in the neighbourhood (009-029–009-032). Chesterton declined the search, recognising that the imaginary tenant he had conjured in youth belonged to a vanished world of red chalk and sky-deep gravity.
