# The Man On Top

[020-001]I did not know they were furniture-vans; at the first glance and in the

[020-002]smoky distance I thought they were a row of cottages. A low stone wall

[020-003]cut off the wheels, and the vans were somewhat of the same colour as the

[020-004]yellowish clay or stone of the buildings around them. I had come across

[020-005]that interminable Eastern plain which is like the open sea, and all the

[020-006]more so because the one small hill and tower of Lincoln stands up in it

[020-007]like a light-house. I had climbed the sharp, crooked streets up to this

[020-008]ecclesiastical citadel; just in front of me was a flourishing and richly

[020-009]coloured kitchen garden; beyond that was the low stone wall; beyond

[020-010]that the row of vans that looked like houses; and beyond and above that,

[020-011]straight and swift and dark, light as a flight of birds, and terrible as

[020-012]the Tower of Babel, Lincoln Cathedral seemed to rise out of human sight.

[020-013]As I looked at it I asked myself the questions that I have asked here;

[020-014]what was the soul in all those stones? They were varied, but it was not

[020-015]variety; they were solemn, but it was not solemnity; they were farcical,

[020-016]but it was not farce. What is it in them that thrills and soothes a man

[020-017]of our blood and history, that is not there in an Egyptian pyramid or

[020-018]an Indian temple or a Chinese pagoda? All of a sudden the vans I had

[020-019]mistaken for cottages began to move away to the left. In the start this

[020-020]gave to my eye and mind I really fancied that the Cathedral was moving

[020-021]towards the right. The two huge towers seemed to start striding across

[020-022]the plain like the two legs of some giant whose body was covered with

[020-023]the clouds. Then I saw what it was.

[020-024]The truth about Gothic is, first, that it is alive, and second, that

[020-025]it is on the march. It is the Church Militant; it is the only fighting

[020-026]architecture. All its spires are spears at rest; and all its stones are

[020-027]stones asleep in a catapult. In that instant of illusion, I could hear

[020-028]the arches clash like swords as they crossed each other. The mighty

[020-029]and numberless columns seemed to go swinging by like the huge feet of

[020-030]imperial elephants. The graven foliage wreathed and blew like banners

[020-031]going into battle; the silence was deafening with all the mingled noises

[020-032]of a military march; the great bell shook down, as the organ shook up

[020-033]its thunder. The thirsty-throated gargoyles shouted like trumpets from

[020-034]all the roofs and pinnacles as they passed; and from the lectern in

[020-035]the core of the cathedral the eagle of the awful evangelist clashed his

[020-036]wings of brass.

[020-037]And amid all the noises I seemed to hear the voice of a man shouting in

[020-038]the midst like one ordering regiments hither and thither in the fight;

[020-039]the voice of the great half-military master-builder; the architect of

[020-040]spears. I could almost fancy he wore armour while he made that church;

[020-041]and I knew indeed that, under a scriptural figure, he had borne in

[020-042]either hand the trowel and the sword.

[020-043]I could imagine for the moment that the whole of that house of life had

[020-044]marched out of the sacred East, alive and interlocked, like an army.

[020-045]Some Eastern nomad had found it solid and silent in the red circle of

[020-046]the desert. He had slept by it as by a world-forgotten pyramid; and been

[020-047]woke at midnight by the wings of stone and brass, the tramping of the

[020-048]tall pillars, the trumpets of the waterspouts. On such a night every

[020-049]snake or sea-beast must have turned and twisted in every crypt or corner

[020-050]of the architecture. And the fiercely coloured saints marching eternally

[020-051]in the flamboyant windows would have carried their glorioles like

[020-052]torches across dark lands and distant seas; till the whole mountain of

[020-053]music and darkness and lights descended roaring on the lonely Lincoln

[020-054]hill. So for some hundred and sixty seconds I saw the battle-beauty of

[020-055]the Gothic; then the last furniture-van shifted itself away; and I saw

[020-056]only a church tower in a quiet English town, round which the English

[020-057]birds were floating.

[020-058]THE MAN ON TOP

[020-059]There is a fact at the root of all realities to-day which cannot be

[020-060]stated too simply. It is that the powers of this world are now not

[020-061]trusted simply because they are not trustworthy. This can be quite

[020-062]clearly seen and said without any reference to our several passions or

[020-063]partisanships. It does not follow that we think such a distrust a wise

[020-064]sentiment to express; it does not even follow that we think it a good

[020-065]sentiment to entertain. But such is the sentiment, simply because such

[020-066]is the fact. The distinction can be quite easily defined in an example.

[020-067]I do not think that private workers owe an indefinite loyalty to their

[020-068]employer. But I do think that patriotic soldiers owe a more or less

[020-069]indefinite loyalty to their leader in battle. But even if they ought to

[020-070]trust their captain, the fact remains that they often do not trust him;

[020-071]and the fact remains that he often is not fit to be trusted.

[020-072]Most of the employers and many of the Socialists seem to have got a very

[020-073]muddled ethic about the basis of such loyalty; and perpetually try to

[020-074]put employers and officers upon the same disciplinary plane. I should

[020-075]have thought myself that the difference was alphabetical enough. It has

[020-076]nothing to do with the idealising of war or the materialising of trade;

[020-077]it is a distinction in the primary purpose. There might be much more

[020-078]elegance and poetry in a shop under William Morris than in a regiment

[020-079]under Lord Kitchener. But the difference is not in the persons or the

[020-080]atmosphere, but in the aim. The British Army does not exist in order

[020-081]to pay Lord Kitchener. William Morris's shop, however artistic and

[020-082]philanthropic, did exist to pay William Morris. If it did not pay the

[020-083]shopkeeper it failed as a shop; but Lord Kitchener does not fail if he

[020-084]is underpaid, but only if he is defeated. The object of the Army is the

[020-085]safety of the nation from one particular class of perils; therefore,

[020-086]since all citizens owe loyalty to the nation, all citizens who are

[020-087]soldiers owe loyalty to the Army. But nobody has any obligation to make

[020-088]some particular rich man richer. A man is bound, of course, to consider

[020-089]the indirect results of his action in a strike; but he is bound to

[020-090]consider that in a swing, or a giddy-go-round, or a smoking concert;

[020-091]in his wildest holiday or his most private conversation. But direct

[020-092]responsibility like that of a soldier he has none. He need not aim

[020-093]solely and directly at the good of the shop; for the simple reason that

[020-094]the shop is not aiming solely and directly at the good of the nation.

[020-095]The shopman is, under decent restraints, let us hope, trying to get what

[020-096]he can out of the nation; the shop assistant may, under the same decent

[020-097]restraints, get what he can out of the shopkeeper. All this distinction

[020-098]is very obvious. At least I should have thought so.

[020-099]But the primary point which I mean is this. That even if we do take the

[020-100]military view of mercantile service, even if we do call the rebellious

[020-101]shop assistant “disloyal”--that leaves exactly where it was the

[020-102]question of whether he is, in point of fact, in a good or bad shop.

[020-103]Granted that all Mr. Poole's employees are bound to follow for ever the

[020-104]cloven pennon of the Perfect Pair of Trousers, it is all the more true

[020-105]that the pennon may, in point of fact, become imperfect. Granted that

[020-106]all Barney Barnato's workers ought to have followed him to death or

[020-107]glory, it is still a Perfectly legitimate question to ask which he was

[020-108]likely to lead them to. Granted that Dr. Sawyer's boy ought to die for

[020-109]his master's medicines, we may still hold an inquest to find out if he

[020-110]died of them. While we forbid the soldier to shoot the general, we may

[020-111]still wish the general were shot.

[020-112]The fundamental fact of our time is the failure of the successful man.

[020-113]Somehow we have so arranged the rules of the game that the winners are

[020-114]worthless for other purposes; they can secure nothing except the prize.

[020-115]The very rich are neither aristocrats nor self-made men; they are

[020-116]accidents--or rather calamities. All revolutionary language is

[020-117]a generation behind the times in talking of their futility. A

[020-118]revolutionist would say (with perfect truth) that coal-owners know next

[020-119]to nothing about coal-mining. But we are past that point. Coal-owners

[020-120]know next to nothing about coal-owning. They do not develop and defend

[020-121]the nature of their own monopoly with any consistent and courageous

[020-122]policy, however wicked, as did the old aristocrats with the monopoly of

[020-123]land. They have not the virtues nor even the vices of tyrants; they have

[020-124]only their powers. It is the same with all the powerful of to-day; it is

[020-125]the same, for instance, with the high-placed and high-paid official. Not

[020-126]only is the judge not judicial, but the arbiter is not even arbitrary.

[020-127]The arbiter decides, not by some gust of justice or injustice in his

[020-128]soul like the old despot dooming men under a tree, but by the permanent

[020-129]climate of the class to which he happens to belong. The ancient wig of

[020-130]the judge is often indistinguishable from the old wig of the flunkey.

[020-131]To judge about success or failure one must see things very simply; one

[020-132]must see them in masses, as the artist, half closing his eyes against

[020-133]details, sees light and shade. That is the only way in which a just

[020-134]judgment can be formed as to whether any departure or development, such

[020-135]as Islam or the American Republic, has been a benefit upon the whole.

[020-136]Seen close, such great erections always abound in ingenious detail and

[020-137]impressive solidity; it is only by seeing them afar off that one can

[020-138]tell if the Tower leans.

[020-139]Now if we thus take in the whole tilt or posture of our modern state,

[020-140]we shall simply see this fact: that those classes who have on the whole

[020-141]governed, have on the whole failed. If you go to a factory you will

[020-142]see some very wonderful wheels going round; you will be told that the

[020-143]employer often comes there early in the morning; that he has great

[020-144]organising power; that if he works over the colossal accumulation of

[020-145]wealth he also works over its wise distribution. All this may be true of

[020-146]many employers, and it is practically said of all.

[020-147]But if we shade our eyes from all this dazzle of detail; if we simply

[020-148]ask what has been the main feature, the upshot, the final fruit of the

[020-149]capitalist system, there is no doubt about the answer. The special and

[020-150]solid result of the reign of the employers has been--unemployment.

[020-151]Unemployment not only increasing, but becoming at last the very pivot

[020-152]upon which the whole process turns.

[020-153]Or, again, if you visit the villages that depend on one of the great

[020-154]squires, you will hear praises, often just, of the landlord's good sense

[020-155]or good nature; you will hear of whole systems of pensions or of care

[020-156]for the sick, like those of a small and separate nation; you will see

[020-157]much cleanliness, order, and business habits in the offices and accounts

[020-158]of the estate. But if you ask again what has been the upshot, what has

[020-159]been the actual result of the reign of landlords, again the answer is

[020-160]plain. At the end of the reign of landlords men will not live on the

[020-161]land. The practical effect of having landlords is not having tenants.

[020-162]The practical effect of having employers is that men are not employed.

[020-163]The unrest of the populace is therefore more than a murmur against

[020-164]tyranny; it is against a sort of treason. It is the suspicion that

[020-165]even at the top of the tree, even in the seats of the mighty, our very

[020-166]success is unsuccessful.
