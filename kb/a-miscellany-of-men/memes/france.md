# France

France serves as [[england|England]]'s essential counterpoint throughout G.K. Chesterton's "A Miscellany of Men" (1912). Where [[england|England]] is a paradox of personalities, France is a republic of impersonality. Where [[england|England]] muddles through without a revolution, France had cut cleanly with its past — and Chesterton finds virtues in both arrangements.

## The Impersonal Republic

In "The Poet and the Cheese," Chesterton draws a structural contrast between the two nations. In France and America, he writes, government politics are more personal in substance but impersonal in form. "But in the republics, in France and America, therefore names are suppressed." (002-126–002-128) The French republican system, unlike the English aristocratic anarchy, subordinates the person to the office.

This is not, for Chesterton, a simple criticism. The French approach has its own logic: by making government impersonal, it prevents the accumulation of personal power that had corrupted English institutions. The French have chosen clarity over texture — and there is something to be said for clarity.

## The Gift of Revolution

In "The Separatist and Sacred Things," Chesterton offers his most considered judgment on the [[french-revolution|French Revolution]]. "Now the great gift of a revolution (as in France) is that it makes men free to choose the past." (015-018) The paradox is characteristic: revolution, which appears to destroy tradition, actually liberates people to select which traditions they wish to keep.

The French, having overthrown the Bourbons, could look back across their entire history and choose what to preserve. The English, having had no such rupture, remained burdened with everything. "Frenchmen have all the ages behind them, and can pick and choose." (015-061) The absence of an English revolution was not, in this analysis, an unmixed blessing.

## Cathedrals and Conscripts

Chesterton's France is not merely a political abstraction. In "The Free Man," he describes walking through a small French town and finding its cathedral — "one of those gray, craggy, crumbling churches, which look as if they had grown out of the rock." (012-138) The French landscape carries its history visibly, from the medieval church to the Napoleonic barracks.

He tells the story of a French conscript — "sent to serve in the French Army; sent (as it happened) at an awful moment, when the French Army was encamped at a parting of the ways." (012-149–012-150) The individual Frenchman, like the individual Englishman, is caught in the machinery of his nation's political arrangements. But the machinery is different — and the difference illuminates both nations.
