King George V (1865–1936), who reigned from 1910 to 1936, appears in Chesterton's "A Miscellany of Men" not as a political figure but as the accidental protagonist of a cultural paradox. In "The Chartered Libertine" (section-14), Chesterton recounts his experience of the King's Coronation celebrations and discovers, in the music of the occasion, a lesson about English liberty.

## A Coronation of Contradictions

Chesterton describes "helping King George V to get crowned, by walking round a local bonfire and listening to a local band" (014-160–014-161). The deliberately casual framing — the Coronation as a neighbourhood bonfire — is characteristically Chestertonian: the grand political event experienced through the ordinary.

The band, by Chesterton's theory of music, could not be too loud, "and this band was so loud, emphatic, and obvious, that I actually recognised one or two of the tunes" (014-162–014-164). What he recognised, however, was remarkable.

## The Jacobite Irony

"A formidable proportion of them were Jacobite tunes; that is, tunes that had been primarily meant to keep George V out of his throne for ever" (014-165–014-166). The band played "Charlie is My Darling" and "What's a' the steer, kimmer?" — "songs that men had sung while marching to destroy and drive out the monarchy under which we live" (014-168–014-170).

These were songs in which the "very kinsmen of the present King were swept aside as usurpers" and the words "King George" occurred "as a curse and a derision" (014-171–014-172). Yet no one objected to them being played at the Coronation.

The songs were "played to celebrate his very Coronation; played as promptly and innocently as if they had been 'Grandfather's Clock' or 'Rule Britannia' or 'The Honeysuckle and the Bee'" (014-173–014-175). Chesterton treats this cultural forgetfulness not as hypocrisy but as evidence of a people who do not treat art as political weapon.

## The Preceding Anecdote

Chesterton introduces the King George anecdote after describing a friend who sang old French royalist songs. This friend was (and is) a Radical, but "it never occurred to him that there could be any harm in singing archaic lyrics out of remote centuries; that one had to be a Catholic to enjoy the 'Dies Irae,' or a Protestant to remember 'Lillibullero'" (014-155–014-157).

Yet he was "stopped and gravely warned that things so politically provocative might get him at least into temporary trouble" (014-158–014-159). The King George Coronation story follows as proof of the friend's instinct — the English absorb and neutralise political songs without effort, a Jacobite anthem becoming Coronation music without anyone noticing.
