# The Other Kind Of Man

[021-001]THE OTHER KIND OF MAN

[021-002]There are some who are conciliated by Conciliation Boards. There are

[021-003]some who, when they hear of Royal Commissions, breathe again--or

[021-004]snore again. There are those who look forward to Compulsory Arbitration

[021-005]Courts as to the islands of the blest. These men do not understand the

[021-006]day that they look upon or the sights that their eyes have seen.

[021-007]The almost sacramental idea of representation, by which the few may

[021-008]incarnate the many, arose in the Middle Ages, and has done great things

[021-009]for justice and liberty. It has had its real hours of triumph, as when

[021-010]the States General met to renew France's youth like the eagle's; or

[021-011]when all the virtues of the Republic fought and ruled in the figure of

[021-012]Washington. It is not having one of its hours of triumph now. The

[021-013]real democratic unrest at this moment is not an extension of the

[021-014]representative process, but rather a revolt against it. It is no good

[021-015]giving those now in revolt more boards and committees and compulsory

[021-016]regulations. It is against these very things that they are revolting.

[021-017]Men are not only rising against their oppressors, but against their

[021-018]representatives or, as they would say, their misrepresentatives.

[021-019]The inner and actual spirit of workaday England is coming out not in

[021-020]applause, but in anger, as a god who should come out of his tabernacle

[021-021]to rebuke and confound his priests.

[021-022]There is a certain kind of man whom we see many times in a day, but whom

[021-023]we do not, in general, bother very much about. He is the kind of man of

[021-024]whom his wife says that a better husband when he's sober you couldn't

[021-025]have. She sometimes adds that he never is sober; but this is in anger

[021-026]and exaggeration. Really he drinks much less and works much more than

[021-027]the modern legend supposes. But it is quite true that he has not the

[021-028]horror of bodily outbreak, natural to the classes that contain ladies;

[021-029]and it is quite true that he never has that alert and inventive sort

[021-030]of industry natural to the classes from which men can climb into great

[021-031]wealth. He has grown, partly by necessity, but partly also by temper,

[021-032]accustomed to have dirty clothes and dirty hands normally and without

[021-033]discomfort. He regards cleanliness as a kind of separate and special

[021-034]costume; to be put on for great festivals. He has several really curious

[021-035]characteristics, which would attract the eyes of sociologists, if they

[021-036]had any eyes. For instance, his vocabulary is coarse and abusive, in

[021-037]marked contrast to his actual spirit, which is generally patient and

[021-038]civil. He has an odd way of using certain words of really horrible

[021-039]meaning, but using them quite innocently and without the most distant

[021-040]taint of the evils to which they allude. He is rather sentimental; and,

[021-041]like most sentimental people, not devoid of snobbishness. At the

[021-042]same time, he believes the ordinary manly commonplaces of freedom and

[021-043]fraternity as he believes most of the decent traditions of Christian

[021-044]men: he finds it very difficult to act according to them, but this

[021-045]difficulty is not confined to him. He has a strong and individual sense

[021-046]of humour, and not much power of corporate or militant action. He is not

[021-047]a Socialist. Finally, he bears no more resemblance to a Labour Member

[021-048]than he does to a City Alderman or a Die-Hard Duke. This is the Common

[021-049]Labourer of England; and it is he who is on the march at last.

[021-050]See this man in your mind as you see him in the street, realise that it

[021-051]is his open mind we wish to influence or his empty stomach we wish to

[021-052]cure, and then consider seriously (if you can) the five men, including

[021-053]two of his own alleged oppressors, who were summoned as a Royal

[021-054]Commission to consider his claims when he or his sort went out on strike

[021-055]upon the railways. I knew nothing against, indeed I knew nothing about,

[021-056]any of the gentlemen then summoned, beyond a bare introduction to

[021-057]Mr. Henderson, whom I liked, but whose identity I was in no danger of

[021-058]confusing with that of a railway-porter. I do not think that any old

[021-059]gentleman, however absent-minded, would be likely on arriving at Euston,

[021-060]let us say, to hand his Gladstone-bag to Mr. Henderson or to attempt to

[021-061]reward that politician with twopence. Of the others I can only judge

[021-062]by the facts about their status as set forth in the public Press. The

[021-063]Chairman, Sir David Harrell, appeared to be an ex-official distinguished

[021-064]in (of all things in the world) the Irish Constabulary. I have no

[021-065]earthly reason to doubt that the Chairman meant to be fair; but I am not

[021-066]talking about what men mean to be, but about what they are. The police

[021-067]in Ireland are practically an army of occupation; a man serving in them

[021-068]or directing them is practically a soldier; and, of course, he must

[021-069]do his duty as such. But it seems truly extraordinary to select as one

[021-070]likely to sympathise with the democracy of England a man whose whole

[021-071]business in life it has been to govern against its will the democracy

[021-072]of Ireland. What should we say if Russian strikers were offered the

[021-073]sympathetic arbitration of the head of the Russian Police in Finland

[021-074]or Poland? And if we do not know that the whole civilised world sees

[021-075]Ireland with Poland as a typical oppressed nation, it is time we did.

[021-076]The Chairman, whatever his personal virtues, must be by instinct

[021-077]and habit akin to the capitalists in the dispute. Two more of the

[021-078]Commissioners actually were the capitalists in the dispute. Then

[021-079]came Mr. Henderson (pushing his trolley and cheerily crying, “By your

[021-080]leave.”), and then another less known gentleman who had “corresponded”

[021-081]with the Board of Trade, and had thus gained some strange claim to

[021-082]represent the very poor.

[021-083]Now people like this might quite possibly produce a rational enough

[021-084]report, and in this or that respect even improve things. Men of

[021-085]that kind are tolerably kind, tolerably patriotic, and tolerably

[021-086]business-like. But if any one supposes that men of that kind can

[021-087]conceivably quiet any real quarrel with the Man of the Other Kind, the

[021-088]man whom I first described, it is frantic. The common worker is angry

[021-089]exactly because he has found out that all these boards consist of the

[021-090]same well-dressed Kind of Man, whether they are called Governmental or

[021-091]Capitalist. If any one hopes that he will reconcile the poor, I say, as

[021-092]I said at the beginning, that such a one has not looked on the light of

[021-093]day or dwelt in the land of the living.

[021-094]But I do not criticise such a Commission except for one most practical

[021-095]and urgent purpose. It will be answered to me that the first Kind of Man

[021-096]of whom I spoke could not really be on boards and committees, as modern

[021-097]England is managed. His dirt, though necessary and honourable, would

[021-098]be offensive: his speech, though rich and figurative, would be almost

[021-099]incomprehensible. Let us grant, for the moment, that this is so. This

[021-100]Kind of Man, with his sooty hair or sanguinary adjectives, cannot be

[021-101]represented at our committees of arbitration. Therefore, the other Kind

[021-102]of Man, fairly prosperous, fairly plausible, at home at least with the

[021-103]middle class, capable at least of reaching and touching the upper class,

[021-104]he must remain the only Kind of Man for such councils.

[021-105]Very well. If then, you give at any future time any kind of compulsory

[021-106]powers to such councils to prevent strikes, you will be driving the

[021-107]first Kind of Man to work for a particular master as much as if you

[021-108]drove him with a whip.

[021-109]THE MEDIÆVAL VILLAIN

[021-110]I see that there have been more attempts at the whitewashing of King

[021-111]John.

[021-112]But the gentleman who wrote has a further interest in the matter; for he

[021-113]believes that King John was innocent, not only on this point, but as a

[021-114]whole. He thinks King John has been very badly treated; though I am not

[021-115]sure whether he would attribute to that Plantagenet a saintly merit or

[021-116]merely a humdrum respectability.

[021-117]I sympathise with the whitewashing of King John, merely because it is

[021-118]a protest against our waxwork style of history. Everybody is in a

[021-119]particular attitude, with particular moral attributes; Rufus is always

[021-120]hunting and Coeur-de-Lion always crusading; Henry VIII always marrying,

[021-121]and Charles I always having his head cut off; Alfred rapidly and in

[021-122]rotation making his people's clocks and spoiling their cakes; and

[021-123]King John pulling out Jews' teeth with the celerity and industry of

[021-124]an American dentist. Anything is good that shakes all this stiff

[021-125]simplification, and makes us remember that these men were once alive;

[021-126]that is, mixed, free, flippant, and inconsistent. It gives the mind

[021-127]a healthy kick to know that Alfred had fits, that Charles I prevented

[021-128]enclosures, that Rufus was really interested in architecture, that Henry

[021-129]VIII was really interested in theology.

[021-130]And as these scraps of reality can startle us into more solid

[021-131]imagination of events, so can even errors and exaggerations if they are

[021-132]on the right side. It does some good to call Alfred a prig, Charles I a

[021-133]Puritan, and John a jolly good fellow; if this makes us feel that they

[021-134]were people whom we might have liked or disliked. I do not myself think

[021-135]that John was a nice gentleman; but for all that the popular picture of

[021-136]him is all wrong. Whether he had any generous qualities or not, he had

[021-137]what commonly makes them possible, dare-devil courage, for instance, and

[021-138]hotheaded decision. But, above all, he had a morality which he broke,

[021-139]but which we misunderstand.

[021-140]The mediæval mind turned centrally upon the pivot of Free Will. In

[021-141]their social system the mediævals were too much PARTI-PER-PALE, as

[021-142]their heralds would say, too rigidly cut up by fences and quarterings

[021-143]of guild or degree. But in their moral philosophy they always thought of

[021-144]man as standing free and doubtful at the cross-roads in a forest. While

[021-145]they clad and bound the body and (to some extent) the mind too stiffly

[021-146]and quaintly for our taste, they had a much stronger sense than we have

[021-147]of the freedom of the soul. For them the soul always hung poised like an

[021-148]eagle in the heavens of liberty. Many of the things that strike a modern

[021-149]as most fantastic came from their keen sense of the power of choice.

[021-150]For instance, the greatest of the Schoolmen devotes folios to the minute

[021-151]description of what the world would have been like if Adam had refused

[021-152]the apple; what kings, laws, babies, animals, planets would have been

[021-153]in an unfallen world. So intensely does he feel that Adam might have

[021-154]decided the other way that he sees a complete and complex vision of

[021-155]another world, a world that now can never be.

[021-156]This sense of the stream of life in a man that may turn either way

[021-157]can be felt through all their popular ethics in legend, chronicle, and

[021-158]ballad. It is a feeling which has been weakened among us by two heavy

[021-159]intellectual forces. The Calvinism of the seventeenth century and the

[021-160]physical science of the nineteenth, whatever other truths they may have

[021-161]taught, have darkened this liberty with a sense of doom. We think of

[021-162]bad men as something like black men, a separate and incurable kind of

[021-163]people. The Byronic spirit was really a sort of operatic Calvinism. It

[021-164]brought the villain upon the stage; the lost soul; the modern version

[021-165]of King John. But the contemporaries of King John did not feel like that

[021-166]about him, even when they detested him. They instinctively felt him to

[021-167]be a man of mixed passions like themselves, who was allowing his evil

[021-168]passions to have much too good a time of it. They might have spoken of

[021-169]him as a man in considerable danger of going to hell; but they would

[021-170]have not talked of him as if he had come from there. In the ballads of

[021-171]Percy or Robin Hood it frequently happens that the King comes upon the

[021-172]scene, and his ultimate decision makes the climax of the tale. But we

[021-173]do not feel, as we do in the Byronic or modern romance, that there is

[021-174]a definite stage direction “Enter Tyrant.” Nor do we behold a deus ex

[021-175]machina who is certain to do all that is mild and just. The King in the

[021-176]ballad is in a state of virile indecision. Sometimes he will pass from

[021-177]a towering passion to the most sweeping magnanimity and friendliness;

[021-178]sometimes he will begin an act of vengeance and be turned from it by

[021-179]a jest. Yet this august levity is not moral indifference; it is moral

[021-180]freedom. It is the strong sense in the writer that the King, being

[021-181]the type of man with power, will probably sometimes use it badly and

[021-182]sometimes well. In this sense John is certainly misrepresented, for he

[021-183]is pictured as something that none of his own friends or enemies saw. In

[021-184]that sense he was certainly not so black as he is painted, for he lived

[021-185]in a world where every one was piebald.
