# Saint George

Saint George is the patron saint of [[england|England]], a Christian martyr of the third century and legendary dragon-slayer. In [[section-17|The Aristocratic 'Arry]], G.K. Chesterton uses St. George as a central emblem for understanding Western Christianity.

## The Dragon and the Saint

Chesterton argues that the nursery tale of St. George and the Dragon best expresses the relation between West and East. (016-003–016-004)

The essential difference is fundamental: the Dragon wanted to eat St. George, whereas St. George would have felt a strong distaste for eating the Dragon. (016-007–016-008) In most stories George killed the Dragon; in many he spared and baptised it. But in neither case did the Christian have any appetite for cold dragon. (016-009–016-011)

## Resistance to Absorption

For Chesterton, St. George represents the Western principle of refusing to be absorbed. The Eastern saint, by contrast, wanted to be swallowed up — drawn toward Pantheism and Cosmic Unity. (016-015–016-017)

"The Western saint, like St. George, was sainted by the Western Church precisely because he refused to be swallowed." (016-018–016-019) This same impulse prevented nationalities from disappearing into empires and kept Pantheism from fully taking root in Christendom. (016-020–016-024)

## The Mummers' Play

Chesterton also invokes St. George in English folk tradition: the Christmas Mummers' Play of St. George, the [[turkish-knight|Turkish Knight]], and the Very Old Man. (016-037)

He reflects that even a humble Ratcatcher knows St. George of [[england|England]], and imagines the saint entering through the garden door on Christmas Eve. (016-089, 016-096) For Chesterton, this popular memory — embedded in custom rather than [[dogma|creed]] — testifies to the durability of the Western symbols George represents.
