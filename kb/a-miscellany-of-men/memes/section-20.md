# The Architect Of Spears

[019-001]But in this scientific picture of him twenty causes, accidental and

[019-002]conventional, had combined to obliterate him altogether. The limits

[019-003]of photography forbade the strong and almost melodramatic colouring

[019-004]of cheek and eyebrow. The accident of the lighting took nearly all the

[019-005]darkness out of the hair and made him look almost like a fair man. The

[019-006]framing and limitation of the shoulders made him look like a big man;

[019-007]and the devastating bore of being photographed when you want to write

[019-008]poetry made him look like a lazy man. Holding his head back, as people

[019-009]do when they are being photographed (or shot), but as he certainly never

[019-010]held it normally, accidentally concealed the bald dome that dominated

[019-011]his slight figure. Here we have a clockwork picture, begun and finished

[019-012]by a button and a box of chemicals, from which every projecting feature

[019-013]has been more delicately and dexterously omitted than they could

[019-014]have been by the most namby-pamby flatterer, painting in the weakest

[019-015]water-colours, on the smoothest ivory.

[019-016]I happen to possess a book of Mr. Max Beerbohm's caricatures, one of

[019-017]which depicts the unfortunate poet in question. To say it represents

[019-018]an utterly incredible hobgoblin is to express in faint and inadequate

[019-019]language the license of its sprawling lines. The authorities thought it

[019-020]strictly safe and scientific to circulate the poet's photograph. They

[019-021]would have clapped me in an asylum if I had asked them to circulate

[019-022]Max's caricature. But the caricature would have been far more likely to

[019-023]find the man.

[019-024]This is a small but exact symbol of the failure of scientific

[019-025]civilisation. It is so satisfied in knowing it has a photograph of a man

[019-026]that it never asks whether it has a likeness of him. Thus declarations,

[019-027]seemingly most detailed, have flashed along the wires of the world ever

[019-028]since I was a boy. We were told that in some row Boer policemen had

[019-029]shot an Englishman, a British subject, an English citizen. A long time

[019-030]afterwards we were quite casually informed that the English citizen was

[019-031]quite black. Well, it makes no difference to the moral question; black

[019-032]men should be shot on the same ethical principles as white men. But

[019-033]it makes one distrust scientific communications which permitted so

[019-034]startling an alteration of the photograph. I am sorry we got hold of a

[019-035]photographic negative in which a black man came out white. Later we were

[019-036]told that an Englishman had fought for the Boers against his own flag,

[019-037]which would have been a disgusting thing to do. Later, it was admitted

[019-038]that he was an Irishman; which is exactly as different as if he had been

[019-039]a Pole. Common sense, with all the facts before it, does see that black

[019-040]is not white, and that a nation that has never submitted has a right to

[019-041]moral independence. But why does it so seldom have all the facts before

[019-042]it? Why are the big aggressive features, such as blackness or the Celtic

[019-043]wrath, always left out in such official communications, as they were

[019-044]left out in the photograph? My friend the poet had hair as black as an

[019-045]African and eyes as fierce as an Irishman; why does our civilisation

[019-046]drop all four of the facts? Its error is to omit the arresting

[019-047]thing--which might really arrest the criminal. It strikes first the

[019-048]chilling note of science, demanding a man “above the middle height, chin

[019-049]shaven, with gray moustache,” etc., which might mean Mr. Balfour or Sir

[019-050]Redvers Buller. It does not seize the first fact of impression, as that

[019-051]a man is obviously a sailor or a Jew or a drunkard or a gentleman or a

[019-052]nigger or an albino or a prize-fighter or an imbecile or an American.

[019-053]These are the realities by which the people really recognise each other.

[019-054]They are almost always left out of the inquiry.

[019-055]THE SULTAN

[019-056]There is one deep defect in our extension of cosmopolitan and Imperial

[019-057]cultures. That is, that in most human things if you spread your butter

[019-058]far you spread it thin. But there is an odder fact yet: rooted in

[019-059]something dark and irrational in human nature. That is, that when you

[019-060]find your butter thin, you begin to spread it. And it is just when you

[019-061]find your ideas wearing thin in your own mind that you begin to spread

[019-062]them among your fellow-creatures. It is a paradox; but not my paradox.

[019-063]There are numerous cases in history; but I think the strongest case is

[019-064]this. That we have Imperialism in all our clubs at the very time when we

[019-065]have Orientalism in all our drawing-rooms.

[019-066]I mean that the colonial ideal of such men as Cecil Rhodes did not arise

[019-067]out of any fresh creative idea of the Western genius, it was a fad,

[019-068]and like most fads an imitation. For what was wrong with Rhodes was not

[019-069]that, like Cromwell or Hildebrand, he made huge mistakes, nor even that

[019-070]he committed great crimes. It was that he committed these crimes and

[019-071]errors in order to spread certain ideas. And when one asked for the

[019-072]ideas they could not be found. Cromwell stood for Calvinism, Hildebrand

[019-073]for Catholicism: but Rhodes had no principles whatever to give to the

[019-074]world. He had only a hasty but elaborate machinery for spreading the

[019-075]principles that he hadn't got. What he called his ideals were the dregs

[019-076]of a Darwinism which had already grown not only stagnant, but poisonous.

[019-077]That the fittest must survive, and that any one like himself must be the

[019-078]fittest; that the weakest must go to the wall, and that any one he could

[019-079]not understand must be the weakest; that was the philosophy which

[019-080]he lumberingly believed through life, like many another agnostic old

[019-081]bachelor of the Victorian era. All his views on religion (reverently

[019-082]quoted in the Review of Reviews) were simply the stalest ideas of his

[019-083]time. It was not his fault, poor fellow, that he called a high hill

[019-084]somewhere in South Africa “his church.” It was not his fault, I mean,

[019-085]that he could not see that a church all to oneself is not a church at

[019-086]all. It is a madman's cell. It was not his fault that he “figured out

[019-087]that God meant as much of the planet to be Anglo-Saxon as possible.”

[019-088]Many evolutionists much wiser had “figured out” things even more

[019-089]babyish. He was an honest and humble recipient of the plodding popular

[019-090]science of his time; he spread no ideas that any cockney clerk in

[019-091]Streatham could not have spread for him. But it was exactly because he

[019-092]had no ideas to spread that he invoked slaughter, violated justice, and

[019-093]ruined republics to spread them.

[019-094]But the case is even stronger and stranger. Fashionable Imperialism not

[019-095]only has no ideas of its own to extend; but such ideas as it has are

[019-096]actually borrowed from the brown and black peoples to whom it seeks to

[019-097]extend them. The Crusading kings and knights might be represented

[019-098]as seeking to spread Western ideas in the East. But all that our

[019-099]Imperialist aristocrats could do would be to spread Eastern ideas in the

[019-100]East. For that very governing class which urges Occidental Imperialism

[019-101]has been deeply discoloured with Oriental mysticism and Cosmology.

[019-102]The same society lady who expects the Hindoos to accept her view of

[019-103]politics has herself accepted their view of religion. She wants first

[019-104]to steal their earth, and then to share their heaven. The same Imperial

[019-105]cynic who wishes the Turks to submit to English science has himself

[019-106]submitted to Turkish philosophy, to a wholly Turkish view of despotism

[019-107]and destiny.

[019-108]There is an obvious and amusing proof of this in a recent life of

[019-109]Rhodes. The writer admits with proper Imperial gloom the fact that

[019-110]Africa is still chiefly inhabited by Africans. He suggests Rhodes in

[019-111]the South confronting savages and Kitchener in the North facing Turks,

[019-112]Arabs, and Soudanese, and then he quotes this remark of Cecil Rhodes:

[019-113]“It is inevitable fate that all this should be changed; and I should

[019-114]like to be the agent of fate.” That was Cecil Rhodes's one small genuine

[019-115]idea; and it is an Oriental idea.

[019-116]Here we have evident all the ultimate idiocy of the present Imperial

[019-117]position. Rhodes and Kitchener are to conquer Moslem bedouins and

[019-118]barbarians, in order to teach them to believe only in inevitable fate.

[019-119]We are to wreck provinces and pour blood like Niagara, all in order to

[019-120]teach a Turk to say “Kismet”; which he has said since his cradle. We

[019-121]are to deny Christian justice and destroy international equality, all in

[019-122]order to teach an Arab to believe he is “an agent of fate,” when he has

[019-123]never believed anything else. If Cecil Rhodes's vision could come true

[019-124](which fortunately is increasingly improbable), such countries as Persia

[019-125]or Arabia would simply be filled with ugly and vulgar fatalists in

[019-126]billycocks, instead of with graceful and dignified fatalists in turbans.

[019-127]The best Western idea, the idea of spiritual liberty and danger, of a

[019-128]doubtful and romantic future in which all things may happen--this

[019-129]essential Western idea Cecil Rhodes could not spread, because (as he

[019-130]says himself) he did not believe in it.

[019-131]It was an Oriental who gave to Queen Victoria the crown of an Empress

[019-132]in addition to that of a Queen. He did not understand that the title of

[019-133]King is higher than that of Emperor. For in the East titles are meant

[019-134]to be vast and wild; to be extravagant poems: the Brother of the Sun and

[019-135]Moon, the Caliph who lives for ever. But a King of England (at least in

[019-136]the days of real kings) did not bear a merely poetical title; but rather

[019-137]a religious one. He belonged to his people and not merely they to him.

[019-138]He was not merely a conqueror, but a father--yes, even when he was

[019-139]a bad father. But this sort of solid sanctity always goes with local

[019-140]affections and limits: and the Cecil Rhodes Imperialism set up not the

[019-141]King, but the Sultan; with all the typically Eastern ideas of the magic

[019-142]of money, of luxury without uproar; of prostrate provinces and a chosen

[019-143]race. Indeed Cecil Rhodes illustrated almost every quality essential to

[019-144]the Sultan, from the love of diamonds to the scorn of woman.

[019-145]THE ARCHITECT OF SPEARS

[019-146]The other day, in the town of Lincoln, I suffered an optical illusion

[019-147]which accidentally revealed to me the strange greatness of the Gothic

[019-148]architecture. Its secret is not, I think, satisfactorily explained

[019-149]in most of the discussions on the subject. It is said that the Gothic

[019-150]eclipses the classical by a certain richness and complexity, at once

[019-151]lively and mysterious. This is true; but Oriental decoration is equally

[019-152]rich and complex, yet it awakens a widely different sentiment. No

[019-153]man ever got out of a Turkey carpet the emotions that he got from a

[019-154]cathedral tower. Over all the exquisite ornament of Arabia and India

[019-155]there is the presence of something stiff and heartless, of something

[019-156]tortured and silent. Dwarfed trees and crooked serpents, heavy flowers

[019-157]and hunchbacked birds accentuate by the very splendour and contrast of

[019-158]their colour the servility and monotony of their shapes. It is like the

[019-159]vision of a sneering sage, who sees the whole universe as a pattern.

[019-160]Certainly no one ever felt like this about Gothic, even if he happens

[019-161]to dislike it. Or, again, some will say that it is the liberty of the

[019-162]Middle Ages in the use of the comic or even the coarse that makes the

[019-163]Gothic more interesting than the Greek. There is more truth in this;

[019-164]indeed, there is real truth in it. Few of the old Christian cathedrals

[019-165]would have passed the Censor of Plays. We talk of the inimitable

[019-166]grandeur of the old cathedrals; but indeed it is rather their gaiety

[019-167]that we do not dare to imitate. We should be rather surprised if a

[019-168]chorister suddenly began singing “Bill Bailey” in church. Yet that would

[019-169]be only doing in music what the mediævals did in sculpture. They put

[019-170]into a Miserere seat the very scenes that we put into a music hall

[019-171]song: comic domestic scenes similar to the spilling of the beer and the

[019-172]hanging out of the washing. But though the gaiety of Gothic is one of

[019-173]its features, it also is not the secret of its unique effect. We see

[019-174]a domestic topsy-turvydom in many Japanese sketches. But delightful

[019-175]as these are, with their fairy tree-tops, paper houses, and toddling,

[019-176]infantile inhabitants, the pleasure they give is of a kind quite

[019-177]different from the joy and energy of the gargoyles. Some have even been

[019-178]so shallow and illiterate as to maintain that our pleasure in medieval

[019-179]building is a mere pleasure in what is barbaric, in what is rough,

[019-180]shapeless, or crumbling like the rocks. This can be dismissed after the

[019-181]same fashion; South Sea idols, with painted eyes and radiating bristles,

[019-182]are a delight to the eye; but they do not affect it in at all the

[019-183]same way as Westminster Abbey. Some again (going to another and almost

[019-184]equally foolish extreme) ignore the coarse and comic in mediævalism;

[019-185]and praise the pointed arch only for its utter purity and simplicity, as

[019-186]of a saint with his hands joined in prayer. Here, again, the uniqueness

[019-187]is missed. There are Renaissance things (such as the ethereal silvery

[019-188]drawings of Raphael), there are even pagan things (such as the

[019-189]Praying Boy) which express as fresh and austere a piety. None of these

[019-190]explanations explain. And I never saw what was the real point about

[019-191]Gothic till I came into the town of Lincoln, and saw it behind a row of

[019-192]furniture-vans.
