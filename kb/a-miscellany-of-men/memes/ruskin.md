John Ruskin (1819–1900), the [[queen-victoria|Victoria]]n art critic, social thinker, and polemicist, appears twice in Chesterton's "A Miscellany of Men" — once as a foil in a discussion of liberty, and once as an exemplar of intelligible art criticism. Chesterton's treatment of Ruskin reveals a characteristic ambivalence: respect for Ruskin's critical seriousness, combined with resistance to his conclusions when they touch political liberty.

## Liberty Against Regularity

In "The Free Man" (section-7), Chesterton invokes Ruskin to define his own position on freedom by opposition. Ruskin had rebuked [[samuel-taylor-coleridge|Coleridge]] for praising freedom, arguing that "no man would wish the sun to be free" (007-155–007-157). For Chesterton, this is precisely the error: "no man would wish to be the sun" (007-158). The human condition is not astronomical regularity but the capacity for choice — "man has to save his soul by choice" (007-155).

Chesterton sharpens the contrast with biblical rhetoric. He declares more sympathy with "Joshua stopping the sun in heaven than with the idea of Ruskin trotting his daily round in imitation of its regularity" (007-159–007-160). The point is political and theological: "Joshua was a Radical, and his astronomical act was distinctly revolutionary. For all revolution is the mastering of matter by the spirit of man" (007-161–007-162). Ruskin's preference for cosmic order over human freedom becomes, in Chesterton's reading, a kind of anti-democratic impulse disguised as aesthetic philosophy.

## The Intelligible Critic

Chesterton's second reference, in "The Chartered Libertine" (section-14), is more generous. Discussing the possibility of translating visual art into prose, he names Ruskin alongside [[walter-pater|Walter Pater]] as a critic who "does suggest an intelligent motive" for artistic choices (014-098). Ruskin's account of Turner "destroying forests and falsifying landscapes" is cited as an example of criticism that finds "the exact adjectives which might parallel in English prose what has been done in Italian painting" (014-099–014-104).

Yet even here Chesterton qualifies his admiration. Ruskin and Pater were "far too fastidious for my taste; they urged to excess the idea that a sense of art was a sort of secret; to be patiently taught and slowly learnt" (014-100–014-101). The qualification is significant: Chesterton's democratic instincts resist any claim that meaning is an exclusive possession. Still, he grants that Ruskin and Pater "thought it could be taught: they thought it could be learnt" (014-102) — and this pedagogical commitment, in Chesterton's view, is what separates the genuine critic from the mere aesthete.
