# Romance

Romance, for Chesterton, is not primarily a literary genre but a way of seeing. It is the conviction that the world is dramatic — that things have beginnings that matter and ends worth reaching. When he writes of romance, he is describing a certain quality of attention: the capacity to be struck by the significance of what is.

## Romance as Beginning and End

Chesterton's most compact definition of romance appears in his remark that "the best parts of every proper romance are the first chapter and the last" — the beginning that sets everything in motion and the end that brings everything home. (008-100)

He sharpens the point further: "the beginning and end of all romance" is the commitment of two people to one another. (008-102) This is a remarkable claim — that the whole architecture of romance literature, with its quests and dragons and shipwrecks, is an elaboration of a single human choice. Romance, at its root, is the story of a promise kept.

## Romance Against Modern Disenchantment

Chesterton contrasts the romantic sensibility with what he sees as the flattened affect of modern literature: "we do not feel, as we do in the Byronic or modern romance, that there is" anything at stake beyond the mood of the protagonist. (021-173)

The Byronic hero is the enemy of romance proper because he treats the world as a backdrop for his own emotions. True romance treats the world as a character in its own right — mysterious, resistant, worthy of quest.

Against this self-absorption, Chesterton sets a different tradition: "a tempestuous flame of Celtic romance spring[ing] up" in certain sensibilities, a romance that is about the encounter between the self and something larger. (018-093)

## Romance and the Ordinary

Chesterton argues that romance is most at home in the ordinary. The "romance, which colours the West Country" is not exotic; it is local, rooted in particular places and particular people. (003-035) Romance is not about escaping the ordinary but about discovering the extraordinary within it.

This connects to Chesterton's philosophy of "the actualities of romance," a phrase that captures his resistance to romance as escapism. (024-120) He "should very much like to write" about romance as it actually operates in human life — not as fantasy but as a realism of the highest order.

Romance is the literary form of hope — a way of taking seriously the human need for meaning, beauty, and narrative coherence.
