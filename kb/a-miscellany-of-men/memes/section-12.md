# The Wrong Incendiary

[011-001]I was coming the other day along a great valley road that strikes out of

[011-002]the westland counties about Glasgow, more or less towards the east and

[011-003]the widening of the Forth. It may, for all I know (I amused myself with

[011-004]the fancy), be the way along which Wallace came with his crude army,

[011-005]when he gave battle before Stirling Brig; and, in the midst of mediæval

[011-006]diplomacies, made a new nation possible. Anyhow, the romantic quality of

[011-007]Scotland rolled all about me, as much in the last reek of Glasgow as in

[011-008]the first rain upon the hills. The tall factory chimneys seemed trying

[011-009]to be taller than the mountain peaks; as if this landscape were full

[011-010](as its history has been full) of the very madness of ambition. The

[011-011]wageslavery we live in is a wicked thing. But there is nothing in which

[011-012]the Scotch are more piercing and poetical, I might say more perfect,

[011-013]than in their Scotch wickedness. It is what makes the Master of

[011-014]Ballantrae the most thrilling of all fictitious villains. It is what

[011-015]makes the Master of Lovat the most thrilling of all historical villains.

[011-016]It is poetry. It is an intensity which is on the edge of madness or

[011-017](what is worse) magic. Well, the Scotch have managed to apply something

[011-018]of this fierce romanticism even to the lowest of all lordships and

[011-019]serfdoms; the proletarian inequality of today. You do meet now and then,

[011-020]in Scotland, the man you never meet anywhere else but in novels; I mean

[011-021]the self-made man; the hard, insatiable man, merciless to himself as

[011-022]well as to others. It is not “enterprise”; it is kleptomania. He is

[011-023]quite mad, and a much more obvious public pest than any other kind of

[011-024]kleptomaniac; but though he is a cheat, he is not an illusion. He does

[011-025]exist; I have met quite two of him. Him alone among modern merchants

[011-026]we do not weakly flatter when we call him a bandit. Something of the

[011-027]irresponsibility of the true dark ages really clings about him. Our

[011-028]scientific civilisation is not a civilisation; it is a smoke nuisance.

[011-029]Like smoke it is choking us; like smoke it will pass away. Only of one

[011-030]or two Scotsmen, in my experience, was it true that where there is smoke

[011-031]there is fire.

[011-032]But there are other kinds of fire; and better. The one great advantage

[011-033]of this strange national temper is that, from the beginning of all

[011-034]chronicles, it has provided resistance as well as cruelty. In Scotland

[011-035]nearly everything has always been in revolt--especially loyalty.

[011-036]If these people are capable of making Glasgow, they are also capable of

[011-037]wrecking it; and the thought of my many good friends in that city makes

[011-038]me really doubtful about which would figure in human memories as the

[011-039]more huge calamity of the two. In Scotland there are many rich men so

[011-040]weak as to call themselves strong. But there are not so many poor men

[011-041]weak enough to believe them.

[011-042]As I came out of Glasgow I saw men standing about the road. They had

[011-043]little lanterns tied to the fronts of their caps, like the fairies

[011-044]who used to dance in the old fairy pantomimes. They were not, however,

[011-045]strictly speaking, fairies. They might have been called gnomes, since

[011-046]they worked in the chasms of those purple and chaotic hills. They worked

[011-047]in the mines from whence comes the fuel of our fires. Just at the moment

[011-048]when I saw them, moreover, they were not dancing; nor were they working.

[011-049]They were doing nothing. Which, in my opinion (and I trust yours), was

[011-050]the finest thing they could do.

[011-051]THE SECTARIAN OF SOCIETY

[011-052]A fixed creed is absolutely indispensable to freedom. For while men are

[011-053]and should be various, there must be some communication between them if

[011-054]they are to get any pleasure out of their variety. And an intellectual

[011-055]formula is the only thing that can create a communication that does not

[011-056]depend on mere blood, class, or capricious sympathy. If we all start

[011-057]with the agreement that the sun and moon exist, we can talk about our

[011-058]different visions of them. The strong-eyed man can boast that he sees

[011-059]the sun as a perfect circle. The shortsighted man may say (or if he is

[011-060]an impressionist, boast) that he sees the moon as a silver blur. The

[011-061]colour-blind man may rejoice in the fairy-trick which enables him to

[011-062]live under a green sun and a blue moon. But if once it be held that

[011-063]there is nothing but a silver blur in one man's eye or a bright circle

[011-064](like a monocle) in the other man's, then neither is free, for each is

[011-065]shut up in the cell of a separate universe.

[011-066]But, indeed, an even worse fate, practically considered, follows from

[011-067]the denim of the original intellectual formula. Not only does the

[011-068]individual become narrow, but he spreads narrowness across the world

[011-069]like a cloud; he causes narrowness to increase and multiply like a weed.

[011-070]For what happens is this: that all the shortsighted people come together

[011-071]and build a city called Myopia, where they take short-sightedness for

[011-072]granted and paint short-sighted pictures and pursue very short-sighted

[011-073]policies. Meanwhile all the men who can stare at the sun get together on

[011-074]Salisbury Plain and do nothing but stare at the sun; and all the men who

[011-075]see a blue moon band themselves together and assert the blue moon, not

[011-076]once in a blue moon, but incessantly. So that instead of a small and

[011-077]varied group, you have enormous monotonous groups. Instead of the

[011-078]liberty of dogma, you have the tyranny of taste.

[011-079]Allegory apart, instances of what I mean will occur to every one;

[011-080]perhaps the most obvious is Socialism. Socialism means the ownership

[011-081]by the organ of government (whatever it is) of all things necessary to

[011-082]production. If a man claims to be a Socialist in that sense he can be

[011-083]any kind of man he likes in any other sense--a bookie, a Mahatma,

[011-084]a man about town, an archbishop, a Margate nigger. Without recalling

[011-085]at the moment clear-headed Socialists in all of these capacities, it

[011-086]is obvious that a clear-headed Socialist (that is, a Socialist with a

[011-087]creed) can be a soldier, like Mr. Blatchford, or a Don, like Mr. Ball,

[011-088]or a Bathchairman like Mr. Meeke, or a clergyman like Mr. Conrad Noel,

[011-089]or an artistic tradesman like the late Mr. William Morris.

[011-090]But some people call themselves Socialists, and will not be bound by

[011-091]what they call a narrow dogma; they say that Socialism means far, far

[011-092]more than this; all that is high, all that is free, all that is, etc.,

[011-093]etc. Now mark their dreadful fate; for they become totally unfit to be

[011-094]tradesmen, or soldiers, or clergymen, or any other stricken human thing,

[011-095]but become a particular sort of person who is always the same. When once

[011-096]it has been discovered that Socialism does not mean a narrow economic

[011-097]formula, it is also discovered that Socialism does mean wearing one

[011-098]particular kind of clothes, reading one particular kind of books,

[011-099]hanging up one particular kind of pictures, and in the majority of cases

[011-100]even eating one particular kind of food. For men must recognise each

[011-101]other somehow. These men will not know each other by a principle, like

[011-102]fellow citizens. They cannot know each other by a smell, like dogs. So

[011-103]they have to fall back on general colouring; on the fact that a man of

[011-104]their sort will have a wife in pale green and Walter Crane's “Triumph of

[011-105]Labour” hanging in the hall.

[011-106]There are, of course, many other instances; for modern society is almost

[011-107]made up of these large monochrome patches. Thus I, for one, regret

[011-108]the supersession of the old Puritan unity, founded on theology, but

[011-109]embracing all types from Milton to the grocer, by that newer Puritan

[011-110]unity which is founded rather on certain social habits, certain common

[011-111]notions, both permissive and prohibitive, in connection with Particular

[011-112]social pleasures.

[011-113]Thus I, for one, regret that (if you are going to have an aristocracy)

[011-114]it did not remain a logical one founded on the science of heraldry; a

[011-115]thing asserting and defending the quite defensible theory that physical

[011-116]genealogy is the test; instead of being, as it is now, a mere machine of

[011-117]Eton and Oxford for varnishing anybody rich enough with one monotonous

[011-118]varnish.

[011-119]And it is supremely so in the case of religion. As long as you have a

[011-120]creed, which every one in a certain group believes or is supposed to

[011-121]believe, then that group will consist of the old recurring figures of

[011-122]religious history, who can be appealed to by the creed and judged by it;

[011-123]the saint, the hypocrite, the brawler, the weak brother. These people

[011-124]do each other good; or they all join together to do the hypocrite good,

[011-125]with heavy and repeated blows. But once break the bond of doctrine which

[011-126]alone holds these people together and each will gravitate to his own

[011-127]kind outside the group. The hypocrites will all get together and

[011-128]call each other saints; the saints will get lost in a desert and call

[011-129]themselves weak brethren; the weak brethren will get weaker and weaker

[011-130]in a general atmosphere of imbecility; and the brawler will go off

[011-131]looking for somebody else with whom to brawl.

[011-132]This has very largely happened to modern English religion; I have been

[011-133]in many churches, chapels, and halls where a confident pride in having

[011-134]got beyond creeds was coupled with quite a paralysed incapacity to

[011-135]get beyond catchwords. But wherever the falsity appears it comes from

[011-136]neglect of the same truth: that men should agree on a principle, that

[011-137]they may differ on everything else; that God gave men a law that they

[011-138]might turn it into liberties.

[011-139]There was hugely more sense in the old people who said that a wife

[011-140]and husband ought to have the same religion than there is in all the

[011-141]contemporary gushing about sister souls and kindred spirits and auras of

[011-142]identical colour. As a matter of fact, the more the sexes are in violent

[011-143]contrast the less likely they are to be in violent collision. The more

[011-144]incompatible their tempers are the better. Obviously a wife's soul

[011-145]cannot possibly be a sister soul. It is very seldom so much as a first

[011-146]cousin. There are very few marriages of identical taste and temperament;

[011-147]they are generally unhappy. But to have the same fundamental theory, to

[011-148]think the same thing a virtue, whether you practise or neglect it, to

[011-149]think the same thing a sin, whether you punish or pardon or laugh at

[011-150]it, in the last extremity to call the same thing duty and the same thing

[011-151]disgrace--this really is necessary to a tolerably happy marriage;

[011-152]and it is much better represented by a common religion than it is by

[011-153]affinities and auras. And what applies to the family applies to the

[011-154]nation. A nation with a root religion will be tolerant. A nation with no

[011-155]religion will be bigoted. Lastly, the worst effect of all is this:

[011-156]that when men come together to profess a creed, they come courageously,

[011-157]though it is to hide in catacombs and caves. But when they come together

[011-158]in a clique they come sneakishly, eschewing all change or disagreement,

[011-159]though it is to dine to a brass band in a big London hotel. For birds of

[011-160]a feather flock together, but birds of the white feather most of all.

[011-161]THE FOOL

[011-162]For many years I had sought him, and at last I found him in a club. I

[011-163]had been told that he was everywhere; but I had almost begun to think

[011-164]that he was nowhere. I had been assured that there were millions of him;

[011-165]but before my late discovery I inclined to think that there were none of

[011-166]him. After my late discovery I am sure that there is one; and I incline

[011-167]to think that there are several, say, a few hundreds; but unfortunately

[011-168]most of them occupying important positions. When I say “him,” I mean the

[011-169]entire idiot.
