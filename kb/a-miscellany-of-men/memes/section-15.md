# The Mystagogue

[014-001]There are two other odd and rather important things to be said about

[014-002]them. The first is this: that with this aristocracy we do not have the

[014-003]chance of a lucky variety in types which belongs to larger and looser

[014-004]aristocracies. The moderately rich include all kinds of people even

[014-005]good people. Even priests are sometimes saints; and even soldiers are

[014-006]sometimes heroes. Some doctors have really grown wealthy by curing their

[014-007]patients and not by flattering them; some brewers have been known to

[014-008]sell beer. But among the Very Rich you will never find a really generous

[014-009]man, even by accident. They may give their money away, but they will

[014-010]never give themselves away; they are egoistic, secretive, dry as old

[014-011]bones. To be smart enough to get all that money you must be dull enough

[014-012]to want it.

[014-013]Lastly, the most serious point about them is this: that the new miser

[014-014]is flattered for his meanness and the old one never was. It was never

[014-015]called self-denial in the old miser that he lived on bones. It is called

[014-016]self-denial in the new millionaire if he lives on beans. A man like

[014-017]Dancer was never praised as a Christian saint for going in rags. A

[014-018]man like Rockefeller is praised as a sort of pagan stoic for his

[014-019]early rising or his unassuming dress. His “simple” meals, his “simple”

[014-020]clothes, his “simple” funeral, are all extolled as if they were

[014-021]creditable to him. They are disgraceful to him: exactly as disgraceful

[014-022]as the tatters and vermin of the old miser were disgraceful to him. To

[014-023]be in rags for charity would be the condition of a saint; to be in rags

[014-024]for money was that of a filthy old fool. Precisely in the same way,

[014-025]to be “simple” for charity is the state of a saint; to be “simple” for

[014-026]money is that of a filthy old fool. Of the two I have more respect for

[014-027]the old miser, gnawing bones in an attic: if he was not nearer to God,

[014-028]he was at least a little nearer to men. His simple life was a little

[014-029]more like the life of the real poor.

[014-030]THE MYSTAGOGUE

[014-031]Whenever you hear much of things being unutterable and indefinable and

[014-032]impalpable and unnamable and subtly indescribable, then elevate your

[014-033]aristocratic nose towards heaven and snuff up the smell of decay. It is

[014-034]perfectly true that there is something in all good things that is beyond

[014-035]all speech or figure of speech. But it is also true that there is in all

[014-036]good things a perpetual desire for expression and concrete embodiment;

[014-037]and though the attempt to embody it is always inadequate, the attempt is

[014-038]always made. If the idea does not seek to be the word, the chances are

[014-039]that it is an evil idea. If the word is not made flesh it is a bad word.

[014-040]Thus Giotto or Fra Angelico would have at once admitted theologically

[014-041]that God was too good to be painted; but they would always try to paint

[014-042]Him. And they felt (very rightly) that representing Him as a rather

[014-043]quaint old man with a gold crown and a white beard, like a king of the

[014-044]elves, was less profane than resisting the sacred impulse to express Him

[014-045]in some way. That is why the Christian world is full of gaudy

[014-046]pictures and twisted statues which seem, to many refined persons, more

[014-047]blasphemous than the secret volumes of an atheist. The trend of good

[014-048]is always towards Incarnation. But, on the other hand, those refined

[014-049]thinkers who worship the Devil, whether in the swamps of Jamaica or the

[014-050]salons of Paris, always insist upon the shapelessness, the wordlessness,

[014-051]the unutterable character of the abomination. They call him “horror

[014-052]of emptiness,” as did the black witch in Stevenson's Dynamiter; they

[014-053]worship him as the unspeakable name; as the unbearable silence. They

[014-054]think of him as the void in the heart of the whirlwind; the cloud on

[014-055]the brain of the maniac; the toppling turrets of vertigo or the endless

[014-056]corridors of nightmare. It was the Christians who gave the Devil a

[014-057]grotesque and energetic outline, with sharp horns and spiked tail. It

[014-058]was the saints who drew Satan as comic and even lively. The Satanists

[014-059]never drew him at all.

[014-060]And as it is with moral good and evil, so it is also with mental clarity

[014-061]and mental confusion. There is one very valid test by which we may

[014-062]separate genuine, if perverse and unbalanced, originality and revolt

[014-063]from mere impudent innovation and bluff. The man who really thinks he

[014-064]has an idea will always try to explain that idea. The charlatan who has

[014-065]no idea will always confine himself to explaining that it is much too

[014-066]subtle to be explained. The first idea may really be very outree or

[014-067]specialist; it may really be very difficult to express to ordinary

[014-068]people. But because the man is trying to express it, it is most probable

[014-069]that there is something in it, after all. The honest man is he who is

[014-070]always trying to utter the unutterable, to describe the indescribable;

[014-071]but the quack lives not by plunging into mystery, but by refusing to

[014-072]come out of it.

[014-073]Perhaps this distinction is most comically plain in the case of the

[014-074]thing called Art, and the people called Art Critics. It is obvious that

[014-075]an attractive landscape or a living face can only half express the holy

[014-076]cunning that has made them what they are. It is equally obvious that

[014-077]a landscape painter expresses only half of the landscape; a portrait

[014-078]painter only half of the person; they are lucky if they express so much.

[014-079]And again it is yet more obvious that any literary description of the

[014-080]pictures can only express half of them, and that the less important

[014-081]half. Still, it does express something; the thread is not broken that

[014-082]connects God With Nature, or Nature with men, or men with critics. The

[014-083]“Mona Lisa” was in some respects (not all, I fancy) what God meant her

[014-084]to be. Leonardo's picture was, in some respects, like the lady. And

[014-085]Walter Pater's rich description was, in some respects, like the picture.

[014-086]Thus we come to the consoling reflection that even literature, in the

[014-087]last resort, can express something other than its own unhappy self.

[014-088]Now the modern critic is a humbug, because he professes to be entirely

[014-089]inarticulate. Speech is his whole business; and he boasts of being

[014-090]speechless. Before Botticelli he is mute. But if there is any good in

[014-091]Botticelli (there is much good, and much evil too) it is emphatically

[014-092]the critic's business to explain it: to translate it from terms of

[014-093]painting into terms of diction. Of course, the rendering will be

[014-094]inadequate--but so is Botticelli. It is a fact he would be the

[014-095]first to admit. But anything which has been intelligently received can

[014-096]at least be intelligently suggested. Pater does suggest an intelligent

[014-097]cause for the cadaverous colour of Botticelli's “Venus Rising from the

[014-098]Sea.” Ruskin does suggest an intelligent motive for Turner destroying

[014-099]forests and falsifying landscapes. These two great critics were far too

[014-100]fastidious for my taste; they urged to excess the idea that a sense

[014-101]of art was a sort of secret; to be patiently taught and slowly learnt.

[014-102]Still, they thought it could be taught: they thought it could be learnt.

[014-103]They constrained themselves, with considerable creative fatigue, to find

[014-104]the exact adjectives which might parallel in English prose what has been

[014-105]clone in Italian painting. The same is true of Whistler and R. A. M.

[014-106]Stevenson and many others in the exposition of Velasquez. They had

[014-107]something to say about the pictures; they knew it was unworthy of the

[014-108]pictures, but they said it.

[014-109]Now the eulogists of the latest artistic insanities (Cubism and Post

[014-110]Impressionism and Mr. Picasso) are eulogists and nothing else. They

[014-111]are not critics; least of all creative critics. They do not attempt

[014-112]to translate beauty into language; they merely tell you that it is

[014-113]untranslatable--that is, unutterable, indefinable, indescribable,

[014-114]impalpable, ineffable, and all the rest of it. The cloud is their

[014-115]banner; they cry to chaos and old night. They circulate a piece of paper

[014-116]on which Mr. Picasso has had the misfortune to upset the ink and tried

[014-117]to dry it with his boots, and they seek to terrify democracy by the good

[014-118]old anti-democratic muddlements: that “the public” does not understand

[014-119]these things; that “the likes of us” cannot dare to question the dark

[014-120]decisions of our lords.

[014-121]I venture to suggest that we resist all this rubbish by the very simple

[014-122]test mentioned above. If there were anything intelligent in such art,

[014-123]something of it at least could be made intelligible in literature. Man

[014-124]is made with one head, not with two or three. No criticism of Rembrandt

[014-125]is as good as Rembrandt; but it can be so written as to make a man go

[014-126]back and look at his pictures. If there is a curious and fantastic art,

[014-127]it is the business of the art critics to create a curious and fantastic

[014-128]literary expression for it; inferior to it, doubtless, but still akin to

[014-129]it. If they cannot do this, as they cannot; if there is nothing in their

[014-130]eulogies, as there is nothing except eulogy--then they are quacks

[014-131]or the high-priests of the unutterable. If the art critics can say

[014-132]nothing about the artists except that they are good it is because

[014-133]the artists are bad. They can explain nothing because they have found

[014-134]nothing; and they have found nothing because there is nothing to be

[014-135]found.

[014-136]THE RED REACTIONARY

[014-137]The one case for Revolution is that it is the only quite clean and

[014-138]complete road to anything--even to restoration. Revolution alone

[014-139]can be not merely a revolt of the living, but also a resurrection of the

[014-140]dead.

[014-141]A friend of mine (one, in fact, who writes prominently on this paper)

[014-142]was once walking down the street in a town of Western France, situated

[014-143]in that area that used to be called La Vendee; which in that great

[014-144]creative crisis about 1790 formed a separate and mystical soul of its

[014-145]own, and made a revolution against a revolution. As my friend went down

[014-146]this street he whistled an old French air which he had found, like Mr.

[014-147]Gandish, “in his researches into 'istry,” and which had somehow taken

[014-148]his fancy; the song to which those last sincere loyalists went into

[014-149]battle. I think the words ran:

[014-150]Monsieur de Charette.

[014-151]Dit au gens d'ici.

[014-152]Le roi va remettre.

[014-153]Le fleur de lys.

[014-154]My friend was (and is) a Radical, but he was (and is) an Englishman, and

[014-155]it never occurred to him that there could be any harm in singing archaic

[014-156]lyrics out of remote centuries; that one had to be a Catholic to enjoy

[014-157]the “Dies Irae,” or a Protestant to remember “Lillibullero.” Yet he was

[014-158]stopped and gravely warned that things so politically provocative might

[014-159]get him at least into temporary trouble.

[014-160]A little time after I was helping King George V to get crowned, by

[014-161]walking round a local bonfire and listening to a local band. Just as a

[014-162]bonfire cannot be too big, so (by my theory of music) a band cannot

[014-163]be too loud, and this band was so loud, emphatic, and obvious, that I

[014-164]actually recognised one or two of the tunes. And I noticed that quite a

[014-165]formidable proportion of them were Jacobite tunes; that is, tunes that

[014-166]had been primarily meant to keep George V out of his throne for ever.

[014-167]Some of the real airs of the old Scottish rebellion were played, such

[014-168]as “Charlie is My Darling,” or “What's a' the steer, kimmer?” songs that

[014-169]men had sung while marching to destroy and drive out the monarchy under

[014-170]which we live. They were songs in which the very kinsmen of the present

[014-171]King were swept aside as usurpers. They were songs in which the actual

[014-172]words “King George” occurred as a curse and a derision. Yet they

[014-173]were played to celebrate his very Coronation; played as promptly and

[014-174]innocently as if they had been “Grandfather's Clock” or “Rule Britannia”

[014-175]or “The Honeysuckle and the Bee.”
