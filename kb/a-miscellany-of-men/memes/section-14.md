# The Miser And His Friends

[013-001]There are many reasons for becoming a soldier. It may be a matter of

[013-002]hereditary luck or abject hunger or heroic virtue or fugitive vice; it

[013-003]may be an interest in the work or a lack of interest in any other work.

[013-004]But there would always be two or three kinds of people who would never

[013-005]tend to soldiering; all those kinds of people were there. A lad with red

[013-006]hair, large ears, and very careful clothing, somehow conveyed across

[013-007]the church that he had always taken care of his health, not even from

[013-008]thinking about it, but simply because he was told, and that he was one

[013-009]of those who pass from childhood to manhood without any shock of being a

[013-010]man. In the row in front of him there was a very slight and vivid little

[013-011]Jew, of the sort that is a tailor and a Socialist. By one of those

[013-012]accidents that make real life so unlike anything else, he was the one

[013-013]of the company who seemed especially devout. Behind these stiff or

[013-014]sensitive boys were ranged the ranks of their mothers and fathers, with

[013-015]knots and bunches of their little brothers and sisters.

[013-016]The children kicked their little legs, wriggled about the seats, and

[013-017]gaped at the arched roof while their mothers were on their knees praying

[013-018]their own prayers, and here and there crying. The gray clouds of

[013-019]rain outside gathered, I suppose, more and more; for the deep church

[013-020]continuously darkened. The lads in front began to sing a military hymn

[013-021]in odd, rather strained voices; I could not disentangle the words, but

[013-022]only one perpetual refrain; so that it sounded like

[013-023]Sacrarterumbrrar pour la patrie,

[013-024]Valdarkararump pour la patrie.

[013-025]Then this ceased; and silence continued, the coloured windows growing

[013-026]gloomier and gloomier with the clouds. In the dead stillness a child

[013-027]started crying suddenly and incoherently. In a city far to the north a

[013-028]French diplomatist and a German aristocrat were talking.

[013-029]I will not make any commentary on the thing that could blur the outline

[013-030]of its almost cruel actuality. I will not talk nor allow any one else to

[013-031]talk about “clericalism” and “militarism.” Those who talk like that are

[013-032]made of the same mud as those who call all the angers of the unfortunate

[013-033]“Socialism.” The women who were calling in the gloom around me on God

[013-034]and the Mother of God were not “clericalists”; or, if they were,

[013-035]they had forgotten it. And I will bet my boots the young men were not

[013-036]“militarists”--quite the other way just then. The priest made a

[013-037]short speech; he did not utter any priestly dogmas (whatever they are),

[013-038]he uttered platitudes. In such circumstances platitudes are the only

[013-039]possible things to say; because they are true. He began by saying that

[013-040]he supposed a large number of them would be uncommonly glad not to go.

[013-041]They seemed to assent to this particular priestly dogma with even

[013-042]more than their alleged superstitious credulity. He said that war was

[013-043]hateful, and that we all hated it; but that “in all things reasonable”

[013-044]the law of one's own commonwealth was the voice of God. He spoke about

[013-045]Joan of Arc; and how she had managed to be a bold and successful soldier

[013-046]while still preserving her virtue and practising her religion; then he

[013-047]gave them each a little paper book. To which they replied (after a brief

[013-048]interval for reflection):

[013-049]Pongprongperesklang pour la patrie,

[013-050]Tambraugtararronc pour la patrie.

[013-051]which I feel sure was the best and most pointed reply.

[013-052]While all this was happening feelings quite indescribable crowded

[013-053]about my own darkening brain, as the clouds crowded above the darkening

[013-054]church. They were so entirely of the elements and the passions that I

[013-055]cannot utter them in an idea, but only in an image. It seemed to me

[013-056]that we were barricaded in this church, but we could not tell what was

[013-057]happening outside the church. The monstrous and terrible jewels of the

[013-058]windows darkened or glistened under moving shadow or light, but the

[013-059]nature of that light and the shapes of those shadows we did not know and

[013-060]hardly dared to guess. The dream began, I think, with a dim fancy that

[013-061]enemies were already in the town, and that the enormous oaken doors were

[013-062]groaning under their hammers. Then I seemed to suppose that the town

[013-063]itself had been destroyed by fire, and effaced, as it may be thousands

[013-064]of years hence, and that if I opened the door I should come out on a

[013-065]wilderness as flat and sterile as the sea. Then the vision behind the

[013-066]veil of stone and slate grew wilder with earthquakes. I seemed to

[013-067]see chasms cloven to the foundations of all things, and letting up an

[013-068]infernal dawn. Huge things happily hidden from us had climbed out of

[013-069]the abyss, and were striding about taller than the clouds. And when the

[013-070]darkness crept from the sapphires of Mary to the sanguine garments of

[013-071]St. John I fancied that some hideous giant was walking round the church

[013-072]and looking in at each window in turn.

[013-073]Sometimes, again, I thought of that church with coloured windows as a

[013-074]ship carrying many lanterns struggling in a high sea at night. Sometimes

[013-075]I thought of it as a great coloured lantern itself, hung on an iron

[013-076]chain out of heaven and tossed and swung to and fro by strong wings, the

[013-077]wings of the princes of the air. But I never thought of it or the young

[013-078]men inside it save as something precious and in peril, or of the things

[013-079]outside but as something barbaric and enormous.

[013-080]I know there are some who cannot sympathise with such sentiments of

[013-081]limitation; I know there are some who would feel no touch of the heroic

[013-082]tenderness if some day a young man, with red hair, large ears, and

[013-083]his mother's lozenges in his pocket, were found dead in uniform in the

[013-084]passes of the Vosges. But on this subject I have heard many philosophies

[013-085]and thought a good deal for myself; and the conclusion I have come to is

[013-086]Sacrarterumbrrar pour la Pattie, and it is not likely that I shall alter

[013-087]it now.

[013-088]But when I came out of the church there were none of these things,

[013-089]but only a lot of Shops, including a paper-shop, on which the posters

[013-090]announced that the negotiations were proceeding satisfactorily.

[013-091]THE MISER AND HIS FRIENDS

[013-092]It is a sign of sharp sickness in a society when it is actually led by

[013-093]some special sort of lunatic. A mild touch of madness may even keep a

[013-094]man sane; for it may keep him modest. So some exaggerations in the State

[013-095]may remind it of its own normal. But it is bad when the head is cracked;

[013-096]when the roof of the commonwealth has a tile loose.

[013-097]The two or three cases of this that occur in history have always been

[013-098]gibbeted gigantically. Thus Nero has become a black proverb, not

[013-099]merely because he was an oppressor, but because he was also an

[013-100]aesthete--that is, an erotomaniac. He not only tortured other

[013-101]people's bodies; he tortured his own soul into the same red revolting

[013-102]shapes. Though he came quite early in Roman Imperial history and was

[013-103]followed by many austere and noble emperors, yet for us the Roman

[013-104]Empire was never quite cleansed of that memory of the sexual madman. The

[013-105]populace or barbarians from whom we come could not forget the hour when

[013-106]they came to the highest place of the earth, saw the huge pedestal of

[013-107]the earthly omnipotence, read on it Divus Caesar, and looked up and saw

[013-108]a statue without a head.

[013-109]It is the same with that ugly entanglement before the Renaissance, from

[013-110]which, alas, most memories of the Middle Ages are derived. Louis XI

[013-111]was a very patient and practical man of the world; but (like many

[013-112]good business men) he was mad. The morbidity of the intriguer and the

[013-113]torturer clung about everything he did, even when it was right. And just

[013-114]as the great Empire of Antoninus and Aurelius never wiped out Nero, so

[013-115]even the silver splendour of the latter saints, such as Vincent de Paul,

[013-116]has never painted out for the British public the crooked shadow of Louis

[013-117]XI. Whenever the unhealthy man has been on top, he has left a horrible

[013-118]savour that humanity finds still in its nostrils. Now in our time the

[013-119]unhealthy man is on top; but he is not the man mad on sex, like Nero; or

[013-120]mad on statecraft, like Louis XI; he is simply the man mad on money. Our

[013-121]tyrant is not the satyr or the torturer; but the miser.

[013-122]The modern miser has changed much from the miser of legend and anecdote;

[013-123]but only because he has grown yet more insane. The old miser had

[013-124]some touch of the human artist about him in so far that he collected

[013-125]gold--a substance that can really be admired for itself, like ivory

[013-126]or old oak. An old man who picked up yellow pieces had something of the

[013-127]simple ardour, something of the mystical materialism, of a child who

[013-128]picks out yellow flowers. Gold is but one kind of coloured clay, but

[013-129]coloured clay can be very beautiful. The modern idolater of riches is

[013-130]content with far less genuine things. The glitter of guineas is like

[013-131]the glitter of buttercups, the chink of pelf is like the chime of bells,

[013-132]compared with the dreary papers and dead calculations which make the

[013-133]hobby of the modern miser.

[013-134]The modern millionaire loves nothing so lovable as a coin. He is content

[013-135]sometimes with the dead crackle of notes; but far more often with the

[013-136]mere repetition of noughts in a ledger, all as like each other as eggs

[013-137]to eggs. And as for comfort, the old miser could be comfortable, as many

[013-138]tramps and savages are, when he was once used to being unclean. A man

[013-139]could find some comfort in an unswept attic or an unwashed shirt. But

[013-140]the Yankee millionaire can find no comfort with five telephones at his

[013-141]bed-head and ten minutes for his lunch. The round coins in the miser's

[013-142]stocking were safe in some sense. The round noughts in the millionaire's

[013-143]ledger are safe in no sense; the same fluctuation which excites him with

[013-144]their increase depresses him with their diminution. The miser at least

[013-145]collects coins; his hobby is numismatics. The man who collects noughts

[013-146]collects nothings.

[013-147]It may be admitted that the man amassing millions is a bit of an idiot;

[013-148]but it may be asked in what sense does he rule the modern world. The

[013-149]answer to this is very important and rather curious. The evil enigma

[013-150]for us here is not the rich, but the Very Rich. The distinction is

[013-151]important; because this special problem is separate from the old general

[013-152]quarrel about rich and poor that runs through the Bible and all strong

[013-153]books, old and new. The special problem to-day is that certain powers

[013-154]and privileges have grown so world-wide and unwieldy that they are out

[013-155]of the power of the moderately rich as well as of the moderately

[013-156]poor. They are out of the power of everybody except a few

[013-157]millionaires--that is, misers. In the old normal friction of normal

[013-158]wealth and poverty I am myself on the Radical side. I think that a

[013-159]Berkshire squire has too much power over his tenants; that a Brompton

[013-160]builder has too much power over his workmen; that a West London doctor

[013-161]has too much power over the poor patients in the West London Hospital.

[013-162]But a Berkshire squire has no power over cosmopolitan finance, for

[013-163]instance. A Brompton builder has not money enough to run a Newspaper

[013-164]Trust. A West End doctor could not make a corner in quinine and freeze

[013-165]everybody out. The merely rich are not rich enough to rule the modern

[013-166]market. The things that change modern history, the big national and

[013-167]international loans, the big educational and philanthropic foundations,

[013-168]the purchase of numberless newspapers, the big prices paid for peerages,

[013-169]the big expenses often incurred in elections--these are getting too

[013-170]big for everybody except the misers; the men with the largest of earthly

[013-171]fortunes and the smallest of earthly aims.
