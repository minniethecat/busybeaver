# The Aristocratic 'Arry

[016-001]For the first injustice, of which we have spoken, still recurs; and if

[016-002]we grant that the East has a right to its difference, it is not realised

[016-003]in what we differ. That nursery tale from nowhere about St. George and

[016-004]the Dragon really expresses best the relation between the West and the

[016-005]East. There were many other differences, calculated to arrest even

[016-006]the superficial eye, between a saint and a dragon. But the essential

[016-007]difference was simply this: that the Dragon did want to eat St. George;

[016-008]whereas St. George would have felt a strong distaste for eating the

[016-009]Dragon. In most of the stories he killed the Dragon. In many of the

[016-010]stories he not only spared, but baptised it. But in neither case did the

[016-011]Christian have any appetite for cold dragon. The Dragon, however,

[016-012]really has an appetite for cold Christian--and especially for cold

[016-013]Christianity. This blind intention to absorb, to change the shape of

[016-014]everything and digest it in the darkness of a dragon's stomach; this is

[016-015]what is really meant by the Pantheism and Cosmic Unity of the East. The

[016-016]Cosmos as such is cannibal; as old Time ate his children. The Eastern

[016-017]saints were saints because they wanted to be swallowed up. The Western

[016-018]saint, like St. George, was sainted by the Western Church precisely

[016-019]because he refused to be swallowed. The same process of thought that has

[016-020]prevented nationalities disappearing in Christendom has prevented the

[016-021]complete appearance of Pantheism. All Christian men instinctively resist

[016-022]the idea of being absorbed into an Empire; an Austrian, a Spanish, a

[016-023]British, or a Turkish Empire. But there is one empire, much larger and

[016-024]much more tyrannical, which free men will resist with even stronger

[016-025]passion. The free man violently resists being absorbed into the empire

[016-026]which is called the Universe. He demands Home Rule for his nationality,

[016-027]but still more Home Rule for his home. Most of all he demands Home

[016-028]Rule for himself. He claims the right to be saved, in spite of Moslem

[016-029]fatalism. He claims the right to be damned in spite of theosophical

[016-030]optimism. He refuses to be the Cosmos; because he refuses to forget it.

[016-031]THE MUMMER

[016-032]The night before Christmas Eve I heard a burst of musical voices so

[016-033]close that they might as well have been inside the house instead of

[016-034]just outside; so I asked them inside, hoping that they might then seem

[016-035]farther away. Then I realised that they were the Christmas Mummers, who

[016-036]come every year in country parts to enact the rather rigid fragments of

[016-037]the old Christmas play of St. George, the Turkish Knight, and the Very

[016-038]Venal Doctor. I will not describe it; it is indescribable; but I will

[016-039]describe my parallel sentiments as it passed.

[016-040]One could see something of that half-failure that haunts our artistic

[016-041]revivals of mediæval dances, carols, or Bethlehem Plays. There are

[016-042]elements in all that has come to us from the more morally simple

[016-043]society of the Middle Ages: elements which moderns, even when they are

[016-044]mediævalists, find it hard to understand and harder to imitate. The

[016-045]first is the primary idea of Mummery itself. If you will observe a child

[016-046]just able to walk, you will see that his first idea is not to dress up

[016-047]as anybody--but to dress up. Afterwards, of course, the idea

[016-048]of being the King or Uncle William will leap to his lips. But it is

[016-049]generally suggested by the hat he has already let fall over his nose,

[016-050]from far deeper motives. Tommy does not assume the hat primarily because

[016-051]it is Uncle William's hat, but because it is not Tommy's hat. It is a

[016-052]ritual investiture; and is akin to those Gorgon masks that stiffened the

[016-053]dances of Greece or those towering mitres that came from the mysteries

[016-054]of Persia. For the essence of such ritual is a profound paradox: the

[016-055]concealment of the personality combined with the exaggeration of the

[016-056]person. The man performing a rite seeks to be at once invisible and

[016-057]conspicuous. It is part of that divine madness which all other creatures

[016-058]wonder at in Man, that he alone parades this pomp of obliteration and

[016-059]anonymity. Man is not, perhaps, the only creature who dresses himself,

[016-060]but he is the only creature who disguises himself. Beasts and birds do

[016-061]indeed take the colours of their environment; but that is not in order

[016-062]to be watched, but in order not to be watched; it is not the formalism

[016-063]of rejoicing, but the formlessness of fear. It is not so with men, whose

[016-064]nature is the unnatural. Ancient Britons did not stain themselves blue

[016-065]because they lived in blue forests; nor did Georgian beaux and belles

[016-066]powder their hair to match an Arctic landscape; the Britons were not

[016-067]dressing up as kingfishers nor the beaux pretending to be polar bears.

[016-068]Nay, even when modern ladies paint their faces a bright mauve, it is

[016-069]doubted by some naturalists whether they do it with the idea of escaping

[016-070]notice. So merry-makers (or Mummers) adopt their costume to heighten

[016-071]and exaggerate their own bodily presence and identity; not to sink it,

[016-072]primarily speaking, in another identity. It is not Acting--that

[016-073]comparatively low profession—comparatively I mean. It is Mummery;

[016-074]and, as Mr. Kensit would truly say, all elaborate religious ritual is

[016-075]Mummery. That is, it is the noble conception of making Man something

[016-076]other and more than himself when he stands at the limit of human things.

[016-077]It is only careful faddists and feeble German philosophers who want to

[016-078]wear no clothes; and be “natural” in their Dionysian revels. Natural

[016-079]men, really vigorous and exultant men, want to wear more and more

[016-080]clothes when they are revelling. They want worlds of waistcoats and

[016-081]forests of trousers and pagodas of tall hats toppling up to the stars.

[016-082]Thus it is with the lingering Mummers at Christmas in the country. If

[016-083]our more refined revivers of Miracle Plays or Morrice Dances tried to

[016-084]reconstruct the old Mummers' Play of St. George and the Turkish Knight

[016-085](I do not know why they do not) they would think at once of picturesque

[016-086]and appropriate dresses. St. George's panoply would be pictured from

[016-087]the best books of armour and blazonry: the Turkish Knight's arms and

[016-088]ornaments would be traced from the finest Saracenic arabesques. When my

[016-089]garden door opened on Christmas Eve and St. George of England entered,

[016-090]the appearance of that champion was slightly different. His face was

[016-091]energetically blacked all over with soot, above which he wore an

[016-092]aged and very tall top hat; he wore his shirt outside his coat like a

[016-093]surplice, and he flourished a thick umbrella. Now do not, I beg you,

[016-094]talk about “ignorance”; or suppose that the Mummer in question (he is a

[016-095]very pleasant Ratcatcher, with a tenor voice) did this because he knew

[016-096]no better. Try to realise that even a Ratcatcher knows St. George of

[016-097]England was not black, and did not kill the Dragon with an umbrella.

[016-098]The Rat-catcher is not under this delusion; any more than Paul Veronese

[016-099]thought that very good men have luminous rings round their heads; any

[016-100]more than the Pope thinks that Christ washed the feet of the twelve in

[016-101]a Cathedral; any more than the Duke of Norfolk thinks the lions on a

[016-102]tabard are like the lions at the Zoo. These things are denaturalised

[016-103]because they are symbols; because the extraordinary occasion must hide

[016-104]or even disfigure the ordinary people. Black faces were to mediæval

[016-105]mummeries what carved masks were to Greek plays: it was called being

[016-106]“vizarded.” My Rat-catcher is not sufficiently arrogant to suppose for

[016-107]a moment that he looks like St. George. But he is sufficiently humble to

[016-108]be convinced that if he looks as little like himself as he can, he will

[016-109]be on the right road.

[016-110]This is the soul of Mumming; the ostentatious secrecy of men in

[016-111]disguise. There are, of course, other mediæval elements in it which

[016-112]are also difficult to explain to the fastidious mediævalists of to-day.

[016-113]There is, for instance, a certain output of violence into the void. It

[016-114]can best be defined as a raging thirst to knock men down without the

[016-115]faintest desire to hurt them. All the rhymes with the old ring have the

[016-116]trick of turning on everything in which the rhymsters most sincerely

[016-117]believed, merely for the pleasure of blowing off steam in startling

[016-118]yet careless phrases. When Tennyson says that King Arthur “drew all the

[016-119]petty princedoms under him,” and “made a realm and ruled,” his grave

[016-120]Royalism is quite modern. Many mediævals, outside the mediæval

[016-121]republics, believed in monarchy as solemnly as Tennyson. But that older

[016-122]verse

[016-123]When good King Arthur ruled this land

[016-124]He was a goodly King--

[016-125]He stole three pecks of barley-meal

[016-126]To make a bag-pudding.

[016-127]is far more Arthurian than anything in The Idylls of the King. There are

[016-128]other elements; especially that sacred thing that can perhaps be called

[016-129]Anachronism. All that to us is Anachronism was to mediævals merely

[016-130]Eternity. But the main excellence of the Mumming Play lies still,

[016-131]I think, in its uproarious secrecy. If we cannot hide our hearts in

[016-132]healthy darkness, at least we can hide our faces in healthy blacking.

[016-133]If you cannot escape like a philosopher into a forest, at least you can

[016-134]carry the forest with you, like a Jack-in-the-Green. It is well to walk

[016-135]under universal ensigns; and there is an old tale of a tyrant to whom

[016-136]a walking forest was the witness of doom. That, indeed, is the very

[016-137]intensity of the notion: a masked man is ominous; but who shall face a

[016-138]mob of masks?

[016-139]THE ARISTOCRATIC 'ARRY

[016-140]The Cheap Tripper, pursued by the curses of the aesthetes and the

[016-141]antiquaries, really is, I suppose, a symptom of the strange and almost

[016-142]unearthly ugliness of our diseased society. The costumes and customs

[016-143]of a hundred peasantries are there to prove that such ugliness does

[016-144]not necessarily follow from mere poverty, or mere democracy, or mere

[016-145]unlettered simplicity of mind.

[016-146]But though the tripper, artistically considered, is a sign of our

[016-147]decadence, he is not one of its worst signs, but relatively one of its

[016-148]best; one of its most innocent and most sincere. Compared with many

[016-149]of the philosophers and artists who denounce him; he looks like a God

[016-150]fearing fisher or a noble mountaineer. His antics with donkeys and

[016-151]concertinas, crowded charabancs, and exchanged hats, though clumsy, are

[016-152]not so vicious or even so fundamentally vulgar as many of the amusements

[016-153]of the overeducated. People are not more crowded on a char-a-banc than

[016-154]they are at a political “At Home,” or even an artistic soiree; and if

[016-155]the female trippers are overdressed, at least they are not overdressed

[016-156]and underdressed at the same time. It is better to ride a donkey than to

[016-157]be a donkey. It is better to deal with the Cockney festival which asks

[016-158]men and women to change hats, rather than with the modern Utopia that

[016-159]wants them to change heads.

[016-160]But the truth is that such small, but real, element of vulgarity as

[016-161]there is indeed in the tripper, is part of a certain folly and falsity

[016-162]which is characteristic of much modernity, and especially of the very

[016-163]people who persecute the poor tripper most. There is something in the

[016-164]whole society, and even especially in the cultured part of it, that does

[016-165]things in a clumsy and unbeautiful way.

[016-166]A case occurs to me in the matter of Stonehenge, which I happened to

[016-167]visit yesterday. Now to a person really capable of feeling the poetry of

[016-168]Stonehenge it is almost a secondary matter whether he sees Stonehenge

[016-169]at all. The vast void roll of the empty land towards Salisbury, the gray

[016-170]tablelands like primeval altars, the trailing rain-clouds, the vapour

[016-171]of primeval sacrifices, would all tell him of a very ancient and

[016-172]very lonely Britain. It would not spoil his Druidic mood if he

[016-173]missed Stonehenge. But it does spoil his mood to find

[016-174]Stonehenge--surrounded by a brand-new fence of barbed wire, with a

[016-175]policeman and a little shop selling picture post-cards.
