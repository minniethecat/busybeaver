# The Chartered Libertine

[023-001]To me, unfortunately perhaps (for I speak merely of individual taste), a

[023-002]cat is a wild animal. A cat is Nature personified. Like Nature, it is

[023-003]so mysterious that one cannot quite repose even in its beauty. But like

[023-004]Nature again, it is so beautiful that one cannot believe that it is

[023-005]really cruel. Perhaps it isn't; and there again it is like Nature. Men

[023-006]of old time worshipped cats as they worshipped crocodiles; and those

[023-007]magnificent old mystics knew what they were about. The moment in which

[023-008]one really loves cats is the same as that in which one (moderately and

[023-009]within reason) loves crocodiles. It is that divine instant when a man

[023-010]feels himself--no, not absorbed into the unity of all things (a

[023-011]loathsome fancy)--but delighting in the difference of all things.

[023-012]At the moment when a man really knows he is a man he will feel, however

[023-013]faintly, a kind of fairy-tale pleasure in the fact that a crocodile is

[023-014]a crocodile. All the more will he exult in the things that are more

[023-015]evidently beautiful than crocodiles, such as flowers and birds and

[023-016]cats--which are more beautiful than either. But it does not follow

[023-017]that he will wish to pick all the flowers or to cage all the birds or to

[023-018]own all the cats.

[023-019]No one who still believes in democracy and the rights of man will admit

[023-020]that any division between men and men can be anything but a fanciful

[023-021]analogy to the division between men and animals. But in the sphere of

[023-022]such fanciful analogy there are even human beings whom I feel to be like

[023-023]cats in this respect: that I can love them without liking them. I

[023-024]feel it about certain quaint and alien societies, especially about the

[023-025]Japanese. The exquisite old Japanese draughtsmanship (of which we shall

[023-026]see no more, now Japan has gone in for Progress and Imperialism) had a

[023-027]quality that was infinitely attractive and intangible. Japanese pictures

[023-028]were really rather like pictures made by cats. They were full of

[023-029]feathery softness and of sudden and spirited scratches. If any one will

[023-030]wander in some gallery fortunate enough to have a fine collection of

[023-031]those slight water-colour sketches on rice paper which come from the

[023-032]remote East, he will observe many elements in them which a fanciful

[023-033]person might consider feline. There is, for instance, that odd enjoyment

[023-034]of the tops of trees; those airy traceries of forks and fading twigs, up

[023-035]to which certainly no artist, but only a cat could climb. There is that

[023-036]elvish love of the full moon, as large and lucid as a Chinese lantern,

[023-037]hung in these tenuous branches. That moon is so large and luminous

[023-038]that one can imagine a hundred cats howling under it. Then there is the

[023-039]exhaustive treatment of the anatomy of birds and fish; subjects in which

[023-040]cats are said to be interested. Then there is the slanting cat-like eye

[023-041]of all these Eastern gods and men--but this is getting altogether

[023-042]too coincident. We shall have another racial theory in no time

[023-043](beginning “Are the Japs Cats?”), and though I shall not believe in

[023-044]my theory, somebody else might. There are people among my esteemed

[023-045]correspondents who might believe anything. It is enough for me to say

[023-046]here that in this small respect Japs affect me like cats. I mean that I

[023-047]love them. I love their quaint and native poetry, their instinct of easy

[023-048]civilisation, their unique unreplaceable art, the testimony they bear

[023-049]to the bustling, irrepressible activities of nature and man. If I were

[023-050]a real mystic looking down on them from a real mountain, I am sure I

[023-051]should love them more even than the strong winged and unwearied birds

[023-052]or the fruitful, ever multiplying fish. But, as for liking them, as one

[023-053]likes a dog--that is quite another matter. That would mean trusting

[023-054]them.

[023-055]In the old English and Scotch ballads the fairies are regarded very much

[023-056]in the way that I feel inclined to regard Japs and cats. They are not

[023-057]specially spoken of as evil; they are enjoyed as witching and wonderful;

[023-058]but they are not trusted as good. You do not say the wrong words or give

[023-059]the wrong gifts to them; and there is a curious silence about what would

[023-060]happen to you if you did. Now to me, Japan, the Japan of Art, was always

[023-061]a fairyland. What trees as gay as flowers and peaks as white as

[023-062]wedding cakes; what lanterns as large as houses and houses as frail as

[023-063]lanterns!... but... but... the missionary explained (I read in the

[023-064]paper) that the assertion and denial about the Japanese use of torture

[023-065]was a mere matter of verbal translation. “The Japanese would not call

[023-066]twisting the thumbs back 'torture.'”

[023-067]THE CHARTERED LIBERTINE

[023-068]I find myself in agreement with Mr. Robert Lynd for his most just remark

[023-069]in connection with the Malatesta case, that the police are becoming

[023-070]a peril to society. I have no attraction to that sort of atheist

[023-071]asceticism to which the purer types of Anarchism tend; but both an

[023-072]atheist and an ascetic are better men than a spy; and it is ignominious

[023-073]to see one's country thus losing her special point of honour about

[023-074]asylum and liberty. It will be quite a new departure if we begin to

[023-075]protect and whitewash foreign policemen. I always understood it was

[023-076]only English policemen who were absolutely spotless. A good many of us,

[023-077]however, have begun to feel with Mr. Lynd, and on all sides authorities

[023-078]and officials are being questioned. But there is one most graphic and

[023-079]extraordinary fact, which it did not lie in Mr. Lynd's way to touch

[023-080]upon, but which somebody really must seize and emphasise. It is

[023-081]this: that at the very time when we are all beginning to doubt these

[023-082]authorities, we are letting laws pass to increase their most capricious

[023-083]powers. All our commissions, petitions, and letters to the papers

[023-084]are asking whether these authorities can give an account of their

[023-085]stewardship. And at the same moment all our laws are decreeing that they

[023-086]shall not give any account of their stewardship, but shall become yet

[023-087]more irresponsible stewards. Bills like the Feeble-Minded Bill and

[023-088]the Inebriate Bill (very appropriate names for them) actually arm with

[023-089]scorpions the hand that has chastised the Malatestas and Maleckas with

[023-090]whips. The inspector, the doctor, the police sergeant, the well-paid

[023-091]person who writes certificates and “passes” this, that, or the other;

[023-092]this sort of man is being trusted with more authority, apparently

[023-093]because he is being doubted with more reason. In one room we are asking

[023-094]why the Government and the great experts between them cannot sail a

[023-095]ship. In another room we are deciding that the Government and experts

[023-096]shall be allowed, without trial or discussion, to immure any one's body,

[023-097]damn any one's soul, and dispose of unborn generations with the levity

[023-098]of a pagan god. We are putting the official on the throne while he is

[023-099]still in the dock.

[023-100]The mere meaning of words is now strangely forgotten and falsified; as

[023-101]when people talk of an author's “message,” without thinking whom it

[023-102]is from; and I have noted in these connections the strange misuse of

[023-103]another word. It is the excellent mediæval word “charter.” I remember

[023-104]the Act that sought to save gutter-boys from cigarettes was called

[023-105]“The Children's Charter.” Similarly the Act which seeks to lock up as

[023-106]lunatics people who are not lunatics was actually called a “charter” of

[023-107]the feeble-minded. Now this terminology is insanely wrong, even if the

[023-108]Bills are right. Even were they right in theory they would be applied

[023-109]only to the poor, like many better rules about education and cruelty.

[023-110]A woman was lately punished for cruelty because her children were not

[023-111]washed when it was proved that she had no water. From that it will be an

[023-112]easy step in Advanced Thought to punishing a man for wine-bibbing when

[023-113]it is proved that he had no wine. Rifts in right reason widen down the

[023-114]ages. And when we have begun by shutting up a confessedly kind

[023-115]person for cruelty, we may yet come to shutting up Mr. Tom Mann for

[023-116]feeblemindedness.

[023-117]But even if such laws do good to children or idiots, it is wrong to use

[023-118]the word “charter.” A charter does not mean a thing that does good to

[023-119]people. It means a thing that grants people more rights and liberties.

[023-120]It may be a good thing for gutter-boys to be deprived of their

[023-121]cigarettes: it might be a good thing for aldermen to be deprived of

[023-122]their cigars. But I think the Goldsmiths' Company would be very much

[023-123]surprised if the King granted them a new charter (in place of their

[023-124]mediæval charter), and it only meant that policemen might pull the

[023-125]cigars out of their mouths. It may be a good thing that all drunkards

[023-126]should be locked up: and many acute statesmen (King John, for instance)

[023-127]would certainly have thought it a good thing if all aristocrats could

[023-128]be locked up. But even that somewhat cynical prince would scarcely have

[023-129]granted to the barons a thing called “the Great Charter” and then locked

[023-130]them all up on the strength of it. If he had, this interpretation of the

[023-131]word “charter” would have struck the barons with considerable surprise.

[023-132]I doubt if their narrow mediæval minds could have taken it in.

[023-133]The roots of the real England are in the early Middle Ages, and no

[023-134]Englishman will ever understand his own language (or even his own

[023-135]conscience) till he understands them. And he will never understand them

[023-136]till he understands this word “charter.” I will attempt in a moment

[023-137]to state in older, more suitable terms, what a charter was. In modern,

[023-138]practical, and political terms, it is quite easy to state what a charter

[023-139]was. A charter was the thing that the railway workers wanted last

[023-140]Christmas and did not get; and apparently will never get. It is called

[023-141]in the current jargon “recognition”; the acknowledgment in so many words

[023-142]by society of the immunities or freedoms of a certain set of men. If

[023-143]there had been railways in the Middle Ages there would probably have

[023-144]been a railwaymen's guild; and it would have had a charter from the

[023-145]King, defining their rights. A charter is the expression of an idea

[023-146]still true and then almost universal: that authority is necessary

[023-147]for nothing so much as for the granting of liberties. Like everything

[023-148]mediæval, it ramified back to a root in religion; and was a sort of

[023-149]small copy of the Christian idea of man's creation. Man was free, not

[023-150]because there was no God, but because it needed a God to set him free.

[023-151]By authority he was free. By authority the craftsmen of the guilds were

[023-152]free. Many other great philosophers took and take the other view:

[023-153]the Lucretian pagans, the Moslem fatalists, the modern monists and

[023-154]determinists, all roughly confine themselves to saying that God gave

[023-155]man a law. The mediæval Christian insisted that God gave man a charter.

[023-156]Modern feeling may not sympathise with its list of liberties, which

[023-157]included the liberty to be damned; but that has nothing to do with the

[023-158]fact that it was a gift of liberties and not of laws. This was mirrored,

[023-159]however dimly, in the whole system. There was a great deal of gross

[023-160]inequality; and in other aspects absolute equality was taken

[023-161]for granted. But the point is that equality and inequality were

[023-162]ranks--or rights. There were not only things one was forbidden

[023-163]to do; but things one was forbidden to forbid. A man was not only

[023-164]definitely responsible, but definitely irresponsible. The holidays of

[023-165]his soul were immovable feasts. All a charter really meant lingers alive

[023-166]in that poetic phrase that calls the wind a “chartered” libertine.

[023-167]Lie awake at night and hear the wind blowing; hear it knock at every

[023-168]man's door and shout down every man's chimney. Feel how it takes

[023-169]liberties with everything, having taken primary liberty for itself; feel

[023-170]that the wind is always a vagabond and sometimes almost a housebreaker.

[023-171]But remember that in the days when free men had charters, they held that

[023-172]the wind itself was wild by authority; and was only free because it had

[023-173]a father.
