# The Suffragist

[001-001]I was conscious of some atmosphere, still and yet bracing, that I had

[001-002]met somewhere in literature. There was poetry in it as well as piety;

[001-003]and yet it was not poetry after my particular taste. It was somehow at

[001-004]once solid and airy. Then I remembered that it was the atmosphere in

[001-005]some of Wordsworth's rural poems; which are full of genuine freshness

[001-006]and wonder, and yet are in some incurable way commonplace. This was

[001-007]curious; for Wordsworth's men were of the rocks and fells, and not of

[001-008]the fenlands or flats. But perhaps it is the clearness of still water

[001-009]and the mirrored skies of meres and pools that produces this crystalline

[001-010]virtue. Perhaps that is why Wordsworth is called a Lake Poet instead

[001-011]of a mountain poet. Perhaps it is the water that does it. Certainly the

[001-012]whole of that town was like a cup of water given at morning.

[001-013]After a few sentences exchanged at long intervals in the manner of

[001-014]rustic courtesy, I inquired casually what was the name of the town. The

[001-015]old lady answered that its name was Stilton, and composedly continued

[001-016]her needlework. But I had paused with my mug in air, and was gazing at

[001-017]her with a suddenly arrested concern. “I suppose,” I said, “that it has

[001-018]nothing to do with the cheese of that name.” “Oh, yes,” she answered,

[001-019]with a staggering indifference, “they used to make it here.”

[001-020]I put down my mug with a gravity far greater than her own. “But this

[001-021]place is a Shrine!” I said. “Pilgrims should be pouring into it from

[001-022]wherever the English legend has endured alive. There ought to be a

[001-023]colossal statue in the market-place of the man who invented Stilton

[001-024]cheese. There ought to be another colossal statue of the first cow who

[001-025]provided the foundations of it. There should be a burnished tablet let

[001-026]into the ground on the spot where some courageous man first ate Stilton

[001-027]cheese, and survived. On the top of a neighbouring hill (if there

[001-028]are any neighbouring hills) there should be a huge model of a Stilton

[001-029]cheese, made of some rich green marble and engraven with some haughty

[001-030]motto: I suggest something like 'Ver non semper viret; sed Stiltonia

[001-031]semper virescit.'” The old lady said, “Yes, sir,” and continued her

[001-032]domestic occupations.

[001-033]After a strained and emotional silence, I said, “If I take a meal here

[001-034]tonight can you give me any Stilton?”

[001-035]“No, sir; I'm afraid we haven't got any Stilton,” said the immovable

[001-036]one, speaking as if it were something thousands of miles away.

[001-037]“This is awful,” I said: for it seemed to me a strange allegory of

[001-038]England as she is now; this little town that had lost its glory; and

[001-039]forgotten, so to speak, the meaning of its own name. And I thought it

[001-040]yet more symbolic because from all that old and full and virile life,

[001-041]the great cheese was gone; and only the beer remained. And even that

[001-042]will be stolen by the Liberals or adulterated by the Conservatives.

[001-043]Politely disengaging myself, I made my way as quickly as possible to

[001-044]the nearest large, noisy, and nasty town in that neighbourhood, where I

[001-045]sought out the nearest vulgar, tawdry, and avaricious restaurant.

[001-046]There (after trifling with beef, mutton, puddings, pies, and so on) I

[001-047]got a Stilton cheese. I was so much moved by my memories that I wrote

[001-048]a sonnet to the cheese. Some critical friends have hinted to me that my

[001-049]sonnet is not strictly new; that it contains “echoes” (as they express

[001-050]it) of some other poem that they have read somewhere. Here, at least,

[001-051]are the lines I wrote:

[001-052]SONNET TO A STILTON CHEESE

[001-053]Stilton, thou shouldst be living at this hour

[001-054]And so thou art.  Nor losest grace thereby;

[001-055]England has need of thee, and so have I--

[001-056]She is a Fen.  Far as the eye can scour,

[001-057]League after grassy league from Lincoln tower

[001-058]To Stilton in the fields, she is a Fen.

[001-059]Yet this high cheese, by choice of fenland men,

[001-060]Like a tall green volcano rose in power.

[001-061]Plain living and long drinking are no more,

[001-062]And pure religion reading 'Household Words',

[001-063]And sturdy manhood sitting still all day

[001-064]Shrink, like this cheese that crumbles to its core;

[001-065]While my digestion, like the House of Lords,

[001-066]The heaviest burdens on herself doth lay.

[001-067]I confess I feel myself as if some literary influence, something that

[001-068]has haunted me, were present in this otherwise original poem; but it is

[001-069]hopeless to disentangle it now.

[001-070]THE THING

[001-071]The wind awoke last night with so noble a violence that it was like

[001-072]the war in heaven; and I thought for a moment that the Thing had broken

[001-073]free. For wind never seems like empty air. Wind always sounds full and

[001-074]physical, like the big body of something; and I fancied that the Thing

[001-075]itself was walking gigantic along the great roads between the forests of

[001-076]beech.

[001-077]Let me explain. The vitality and recurrent victory of Christendom have

[001-078]been due to the power of the Thing to break out from time to time from

[001-079]its enveloping words and symbols. Without this power all civilisations

[001-080]tend to perish under a load of language and ritual. One instance of this

[001-081]we hear much in modern discussion: the separation of the form from the

[001-082]spirit of religion. But we hear too little of numberless other cases of

[001-083]the same stiffening and falsification; we are far too seldom reminded

[001-084]that just as church-going is not religion, so reading and writing are

[001-085]not knowledge, and voting is not self-government. It would be easy to

[001-086]find people in the big cities who can read and write quickly enough to

[001-087]be clerks, but who are actually ignorant of the daily movements of the

[001-088]sun and moon.

[001-089]The case of self-government is even more curious, especially as one

[001-090]watches it for the first time in a country district. Self-government

[001-091]arose among men (probably among the primitive men, certainly among the

[001-092]ancients) out of an idea which seems now too simple to be understood.

[001-093]The notion of self-government was not (as many modern friends and foes

[001-094]of it seem to think) the notion that the ordinary citizen is to be

[001-095]consulted as one consults an Encyclopaedia. He is not there to be asked

[001-096]a lot of fancy questions, to see how he answers them. He and his fellows

[001-097]are to be, within reasonable human limits, masters of their own lives.

[001-098]They shall decide whether they shall be men of the oar or the wheel, of

[001-099]the spade or the spear. The men of the valley shall settle whether the

[001-100]valley shall be devastated for coal or covered with corn and vines; the

[001-101]men of the town shall decide whether it shall be hoary with thatches or

[001-102]splendid with spires. Of their own nature and instinct they shall gather

[001-103]under a patriarchal chief or debate in a political market-place. And in

[001-104]case the word “man” be misunderstood, I may remark that in this moral

[001-105]atmosphere, this original soul of self-government, the women always have

[001-106]quite as much influence as the men. But in modern England neither the

[001-107]men nor the women have any influence at all. In this primary matter, the

[001-108]moulding of the landscape, the creation of a mode of life, the people

[001-109]are utterly impotent. They stand and stare at imperial and economic

[001-110]processes going on, as they might stare at the Lord Mayor's Show.

[001-111]Round about where I live, for instance, two changes are taking place

[001-112]which really affect the land and all things that live on it, whether for

[001-113]good or evil. The first is that the urban civilisation (or whatever

[001-114]it is) is advancing; that the clerks come out in black swarms and the

[001-115]villas advance in red battalions. The other is that the vast estates

[001-116]into which England has long been divided are passing out of the hands

[001-117]of the English gentry into the hands of men who are always upstarts and

[001-118]often actually foreigners.

[001-119]Now, these are just the sort of things with which self-government was

[001-120]really supposed to grapple. People were supposed to be able to indicate

[001-121]whether they wished to live in town or country, to be represented by a

[001-122]gentleman or a cad. I do not presume to prejudge their decision; perhaps

[001-123]they would prefer the cad; perhaps he is really preferable. I say that

[001-124]the filling of a man's native sky with smoke or the selling of his roof

[001-125]over his head illustrate the sort of things he ought to have some say

[001-126]in, if he is supposed to be governing himself. But owing to the strange

[001-127]trend of recent society, these enormous earthquakes he has to pass over

[001-128]and treat as private trivialities. In theory the building of a villa

[001-129]is as incidental as the buying of a hat. In reality it is as if all

[001-130]Lancashire were laid waste for deer forests; or as if all Belgium

[001-131]were flooded by the sea. In theory the sale of a squire's land to a

[001-132]moneylender is a minor and exceptional necessity. In reality it is a

[001-133]thing like a German invasion. Sometimes it is a German invasion.

[001-134]Upon this helpless populace, gazing at these prodigies and fates, comes

[001-135]round about every five years a thing called a General Election. It

[001-136]is believed by antiquarians to be the remains of some system of

[001-137]self-government; but it consists solely in asking the citizen questions

[001-138]about everything except what he understands. The examination paper of

[001-139]the Election generally consists of some such queries as these: “I. Are

[001-140]the green biscuits eaten by the peasants of Eastern Lithuania in your

[001-141]opinion fit for human food? II. Are the religious professions of the

[001-142]President of the Orange Free State hypocritical or sincere? III. Do you

[001-143]think that the savages in Prusso-Portuguese East Bunyipland are as happy

[001-144]and hygienic as the fortunate savages in Franco-British West Bunyipland?

[001-145]IV. Did the lost Latin Charter said to have been exacted from Henry III

[001-146]reserve the right of the Crown to create peers? V. What do you think of

[001-147]what America thinks of what Mr. Roosevelt thinks of what Sir Eldon Gorst

[001-148]thinks of the state of the Nile? VI. Detect some difference between the

[001-149]two persons in frock-coats placed before you at this election.”

[001-150]Now, it never was supposed in any natural theory of self-government that

[001-151]the ordinary man in my neighbourhood need answer fantastic questions

[001-152]like these. He is a citizen of South Bucks, not an editor of 'Notes and

[001-153]Queries'. He would be, I seriously believe, the best judge of whether

[001-154]farmsteads or factory chimneys should adorn his own sky-line, of whether

[001-155]stupid squires or clever usurers should govern his own village. But

[001-156]these are precisely the things which the oligarchs will not allow him to

[001-157]touch with his finger. Instead, they allow him an Imperial destiny and

[001-158]divine mission to alter, under their guidance, all the things that he

[001-159]knows nothing about. The name of self-government is noisy everywhere:

[001-160]the Thing is throttled.

[001-161]The wind sang and split the sky like thunder all the night through;

[001-162]in scraps of sleep it filled my dreams with the divine discordances

[001-163]of martyrdom and revolt; I heard the horn of Roland and the drums of

[001-164]Napoleon and all the tongues of terror with which the Thing has gone

[001-165]forth: the spirit of our race alive. But when I came down in the morning

[001-166]only a branch or two was broken off the tree in my garden; and none of

[001-167]the great country houses in the neighbourhood were blown down, as would

[001-168]have happened if the Thing had really been abroad.

[001-169]THE MAN WHO THINKS BACKWARDS

[001-170]The man who thinks backwards is a very powerful person to-day: indeed,

[001-171]if he is not omnipotent, he is at least omnipresent. It is he who writes

[001-172]nearly all the learned books and articles, especially of the scientific

[001-173]or skeptical sort; all the articles on Eugenics and Social Evolution

[001-174]and Prison Reform and the Higher Criticism and all the rest of it. But

[001-175]especially it is this strange and tortuous being who does most of the

[001-176]writing about female emancipation and the reconsidering of marriage. For

[001-177]the man who thinks backwards is very frequently a woman.
