# The Divine Detective

[022-001]King John would be represented in a modern play or novel as a kind

[022-002]of degenerate; a shifty-eyed moral maniac with a twist in his soul's

[022-003]backbone and green blood in his veins. The mediævals were quite capable

[022-004]of boiling him in melted lead, but they would have been quite incapable

[022-005]of despairing of his soul in the modern fashion. A striking a fortiori

[022-006]case is that of the strange mediæval legend of Robert the Devil.

[022-007]Robert was represented as a monstrous birth sent to an embittered woman

[022-008]actually in answer to prayers to Satan, and his earlier actions are

[022-009]simply those of the infernal fire let loose upon earth. Yet though he

[022-010]can be called almost literally a child of hell, yet the climax of the

[022-011]story is his repentance at Rome and his great reparation. That is the

[022-012]paradox of mediæval morals: as it must appear to the moderns. We must

[022-013]try to conceive a race of men who hated John, and sought his blood, and

[022-014]believed every abomination about him, who would have been quite capable

[022-015]of assassinating or torturing him in the extremity of their anger. And

[022-016]yet we must admit that they would not really have been fundamentally

[022-017]surprised if he had shaved his head in humiliation, given all his goods

[022-018]to the poor, embraced the lepers in a lazar-house, and been canonised

[022-019]as a saint in heaven. So strongly did they hold that the pivot of Will

[022-020]should turn freely, which now is rusted, and sticks.

[022-021]For we, whatever our political opinions, certainly never think of our

[022-022]public men like that. If we hold the opinion that Mr. Lloyd George is a

[022-023]noble tribune of the populace and protector of the poor, we do not admit

[022-024]that he can ever have paltered with the truth or bargained with the

[022-025]powerful. If we hold the equally idiotic opinion that he is a red and

[022-026]rabid Socialist, maddening mobs into mutiny and theft, then we expect

[022-027]him to go on maddening them--and us. We do not expect him, let

[022-028]us say, suddenly to go into a monastery. We have lost the idea of

[022-029]repentance; especially in public things; that is why we cannot

[022-030]really get rid of our great national abuses of economic tyranny and

[022-031]aristocratic avarice. Progress in the modern sense is a very dismal

[022-032]drudge; and mostly consists of being moved on by the police. We move on

[022-033]because we are not allowed to move back. But the really ragged prophets,

[022-034]the real revolutionists who held high language in the palaces of kings,

[022-035]they did not confine themselves to saying, “Onward, Christian soldiers,”

[022-036]still less, “Onward, Futurist soldiers”; what they said to high emperors

[022-037]and to whole empires was, “Turn ye, turn ye, why will ye die?”

[022-038]THE DIVINE DETECTIVE

[022-039]Every person of sound education enjoys detective stories, and there

[022-040]are even several points on which they have a hearty superiority to

[022-041]most modern books. A detective story generally describes six living

[022-042]men discussing how it is that a man is dead. A modern philosophic story

[022-043]generally describes six dead men discussing how any man can possibly be

[022-044]alive. But those who have enjoyed the roman policier must have noted

[022-045]one thing, that when the murderer is caught he is hardly ever hanged.

[022-046]“That,” says Sherlock Holmes, “is the advantage of being a private

[022-047]detective”; after he has caught he can set free. The Christian Church

[022-048]can best be defined as an enormous private detective, correcting

[022-049]that official detective--the State. This, indeed, is one of the

[022-050]injustices done to historic Christianity; injustices which arise from

[022-051]looking at complex exceptions and not at the large and simple fact. We

[022-052]are constantly being told that theologians used racks and thumbscrews,

[022-053]and so they did. Theologians used racks and thumbscrews just as they

[022-054]used thimbles and three-legged stools, because everybody else used them.

[022-055]Christianity no more created the mediæval tortures than it did the

[022-056]Chinese tortures; it inherited them from any empire as heathen as the

[022-057]Chinese.

[022-058]The Church did, in an evil hour, consent to imitate the commonwealth and

[022-059]employ cruelty. But if we open our eyes and take in the whole picture,

[022-060]if we look at the general shape and colour of the thing, the real

[022-061]difference between the Church and the State is huge and plain. The

[022-062]State, in all lands and ages, has created a machinery of punishment,

[022-063]more bloody and brutal in some places than others, but bloody and brutal

[022-064]everywhere. The Church is the only institution that ever attempted to

[022-065]create a machinery of pardon. The Church is the only thing that ever

[022-066]attempted by system to pursue and discover crimes, not in order to

[022-067]avenge, but in order to forgive them. The stake and rack were merely the

[022-068]weaknesses of the religion; its snobberies, its surrenders to the

[022-069]world. Its speciality--or, if you like, its oddity--was this

[022-070]merciless mercy; the unrelenting sleuthhound who seeks to save and not

[022-071]slay.

[022-072]I can best illustrate what I mean by referring to two popular plays

[022-073]on somewhat parallel topics, which have been successful here and in

[022-074]America. The Passing of the Third Floor Back is a humane and reverent

[022-075]experiment, dealing with the influence of one unknown but divine figure

[022-076]as he passes through a group of Squalid characters. I have no desire to

[022-077]make cheap fun of the extremely abrupt conversions of all these people;

[022-078]that is a point of art, not of morals; and, after all, many conversions

[022-079]have been abrupt. This saviour's method of making people good is to tell

[022-080]them how good they are already; and in the case of suicidal outcasts,

[022-081]whose moral backs are broken, and who are soaked with sincere

[022-082]self-contempt, I can imagine that this might be quite the right way.

[022-083]I should not deliver this message to authors or members of Parliament,

[022-084]because they would so heartily agree with it.

[022-085]Still, it is not altogether here that I differ from the moral of Mr.

[022-086]Jerome's play. I differ vitally from his story because it is not a

[022-087]detective story. There is in it none of this great Christian idea of

[022-088]tearing their evil out of men; it lacks the realism of the saints.

[022-089]Redemption should bring truth as well as peace; and truth is a fine

[022-090]thing, though the materialists did go mad about it. Things must be

[022-091]faced, even in order to be forgiven; the great objection to “letting

[022-092]sleeping dogs lie” is that they lie in more senses than one. But in Mr.

[022-093]Jerome's Passing of the Third Floor Back the redeemer is not a divine

[022-094]detective, pitiless in his resolve to know and pardon. Rather he is a

[022-095]sort of divine dupe, who does not pardon at all, because he does not

[022-096]see anything that is going on. It may, or may not, be true to say, “Tout

[022-097]comprendre est tout pardonner.” But it is much more evidently true to

[022-098]say, “Rien comprendre est rien Pardonner,” and the “Third Floor Back”

[022-099]does not seem to comprehend anything. He might, after all, be a quite

[022-100]selfish sentimentalist, who found it comforting to think well of his

[022-101]neighbours. There is nothing very heroic in loving after you have been

[022-102]deceived. The heroic business is to love after you have been undeceived.

[022-103]When I saw this play it was natural to compare it with another play

[022-104]which I had not seen, but which I have read in its printed version.

[022-105]I mean Mr. Rann Kennedy's Servant in the House, the success of which

[022-106]sprawls over so many of the American newspapers. This also is concerned

[022-107]with a dim, yet evidently divine, figure changing the destinies of a

[022-108]whole group of persons. It is a better play structurally than the other;

[022-109]in fact, it is a very fine play indeed; but there is nothing

[022-110]aesthetic or fastidious about it. It is as much or more than the other

[022-111]sensational, democratic, and (I use the word in a sound and good sense)

[022-112]Salvationist.

[022-113]But the difference lies precisely in this--that the Christ of Mr.

[022-114]Kennedy's play insists on really knowing all the souls that he loves;

[022-115]he declines to conquer by a kind of supernatural stupidity. He pardons

[022-116]evil, but he will not ignore it. In other words, he is a Christian, and

[022-117]not a Christian Scientist. The distinction doubtless is partly explained

[022-118]by the problems severally selected. Mr. Jerome practically supposes

[022-119]Christ to be trying to save disreputable people; and that, of course,

[022-120]is naturally a simple business. Mr. Kennedy supposes Him to be trying

[022-121]to save the reputable people, which is a much larger affair. The chief

[022-122]characters in The Servant in the House are a popular and strenuous

[022-123]vicar, universally respected, and his fashionable and forcible wife.

[022-124]It would have been no good to tell these people they had some good in

[022-125]them--for that was what they were telling themselves all day long.

[022-126]They had to be reminded that they had some bad in them--instinctive

[022-127]idolatries and silent treasons which they always tried to forget. It is

[022-128]in connection with these crimes of wealth and culture that we face the

[022-129]real problem of positive evil. The whole of Mr. Blatchford's controversy

[022-130]about sin was vitiated throughout by one's consciousness that whenever

[022-131]he wrote the word “sinner” he thought of a man in rags. But here, again,

[022-132]we can find truth merely by referring to vulgar literature--its

[022-133]unfailing fountain. Whoever read a detective story about poor people?

[022-134]The poor have crimes; but the poor have no secrets. And it is because

[022-135]the proud have secrets that they need to be detected before they are

[022-136]forgiven.

[022-137]THE ELF OF JAPAN

[022-138]There are things in this world of which I can say seriously that I

[022-139]love them but I do not like them. The point is not merely verbal, but

[022-140]psychologically quite valid. Cats are the first things that occur to me

[022-141]as examples of the principle. Cats are so beautiful that a creature from

[022-142]another star might fall in love with them, and so incalculable that he

[022-143]might kill them. Some of my friends take quite a high moral line about

[022-144]cats. Some, like Mr. Titterton, I think, admire a cat for its moral

[022-145]independence and readiness to scratch anybody “if he does not behave

[022-146]himself.” Others, like Mr. Belloc, regard the cat as cruel and secret, a

[022-147]fit friend for witches; one who will devour everything, except, indeed,

[022-148]poisoned food, “so utterly lacking is it in Christian simplicity and

[022-149]humility.” For my part, I have neither of these feelings. I admire cats

[022-150]as I admire catkins; those little fluffy things that hang on trees. They

[022-151]are both pretty and both furry, and both declare the glory of God. And

[022-152]this abstract exultation in all living things is truly to be called

[022-153]Love; for it is a higher feeling than mere affectional convenience; it

[022-154]is a vision. It is heroic, and even saintly, in this: that it asks for

[022-155]nothing in return. I love all the cats in the street as St. Francis of

[022-156]Assisi loved all the birds in the wood or all the fishes in the sea; not

[022-157]so much, of course, but then I am not a saint. But he did not wish to

[022-158]bridle a bird and ride on its back, as one bridles and rides on a horse.

[022-159]He did not wish to put a collar round a fish's neck, marked with the

[022-160]name “Francis,” and the address “Assisi”--as one does with a dog.

[022-161]He did not wish them to belong to him or himself to belong to them;

[022-162]in fact, it would be a very awkward experience to belong to a lot of

[022-163]fishes. But a man does belong to his dog, in another but an equally

[022-164]real sense with that in which the dog belongs to him. The two bonds of

[022-165]obedience and responsibility vary very much with the dogs and the men;

[022-166]but they are both bonds. In other words, a man does not merely love a

[022-167]dog; as he might (in a mystical moment) love any sparrow that perched

[022-168]on his windowsill or any rabbit that ran across his path. A man likes a

[022-169]dog; and that is a serious matter.
