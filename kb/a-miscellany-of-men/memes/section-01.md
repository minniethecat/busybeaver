[P01-042]THE SUFFRAGIST

[P01-043]Rightly or wrongly, it is certain that a man both liberal and chivalric,

[P01-044]can and very often does feel a dis-ease and distrust touching

[P01-045]those political women we call Suffragettes. Like most other popular

[P01-046]sentiments, it is generally wrongly stated even when it is rightly felt.

[P01-047]One part of it can be put most shortly thus: that when a woman puts up

[P01-048]her fists to a man she is putting herself in the only posture in which

[P01-049]he is not afraid of her. He can be afraid of her speech and still more

[P01-050]of her silence; but force reminds him of a rusted but very real weapon

[P01-051]of which he has grown ashamed. But these crude summaries are never quite

[P01-052]accurate in any matter of the instincts. For the things which are the

[P01-053]simplest so long as they are undisputed invariably become the subtlest

[P01-054]when once they are disputed: which was what Joubert meant, I suppose,

[P01-055]when he said, “It is not hard to believe in God if one does not define

[P01-056]Him.” When the evil instincts of old Foulon made him say of the poor,

[P01-057]“Let them eat grass,” the good and Christian instincts of the poor

[P01-058]made them hang him on a lamppost with his mouth stuffed full of that

[P01-059]vegetation. But if a modern vegetarian aristocrat were to say to the

[P01-060]poor, “But why don't you like grass?” their intelligences would be much

[P01-061]more taxed to find such an appropriate repartee. And this matter of the

[P01-062]functions of the sexes is primarily a matter of the instincts; sex and

[P01-063]breathing are about the only two things that generally work best

[P01-064]when they are least worried about. That, I suppose, is why the same

[P01-065]sophisticated age that has poisoned the world with Feminism is also

[P01-066]polluting it with Breathing Exercises. We plunge at once into a forest

[P01-067]of false analogies and bad blundering history; while almost any man or

[P01-068]woman left to themselves would know at least that sex is quite different

[P01-069]from anything else in the world.

[P01-070]There is no kind of comparison possible between a quarrel of man and

[P01-071]woman (however right the woman may be) and the other quarrels of slave

[P01-072]and master, of rich and poor, or of patriot and invader, with which the

[P01-073]Suffragists deluge us every day. The difference is as plain as noon;

[P01-074]these other alien groups never came into contact until they came into

[P01-075]collision. Races and ranks began with battle, even if they afterwards

[P01-076]melted into amity. But the very first fact about the sexes is that they

[P01-077]like each other. They seek each other: and awful as are the sins and

[P01-078]sorrows that often come of their mating, it was not such things that

[P01-079]made them meet. It is utterly astounding to note the way in which modern

[P01-080]writers and talkers miss this plain, wide, and overwhelming fact: one

[P01-081]would suppose woman a victim and nothing else. By this account ideal,

[P01-082]emancipated woman has, age after age, been knocked silly with a stone

[P01-083]axe. But really there is no fact to show that ideal, emancipated woman

[P01-084]was ever knocked silly; except the fact that she is silly. And that

[P01-085]might have arisen in so many other ways. Real responsible woman has

[P01-086]never been silly; and any one wishing to knock her would be wise (like

[P01-087]the streetboys) to knock and run away. It is ultimately idiotic to

[P01-088]compare this prehistoric participation with any royalties or rebellions.

[P01-089]Genuine royalties wish to crush rebellions. Genuine rebels wish to

[P01-090]destroy kings. The sexes cannot wish to abolish each other; and if we

[P01-091]allow them any sort of permanent opposition it will sink into something

[P01-092]as base as a party system.

[P01-093]As marriage, therefore, is rooted in an aboriginal unity of instincts,

[P01-094]you cannot compare it, even in its quarrels, with any of the mere

[P01-095]collisions of separate institutions. You could compare it with the

[P01-096]emancipation of negroes from planters--if it were true that a white

[P01-097]man in early youth always dreamed of the abstract beauty of a black

[P01-098]man. You could compare it with the revolt of tenants against a

[P01-099]landlord--if it were true that young landlords wrote sonnets to

[P01-100]invisible tenants. You could compare it to the fighting policy of the

[P01-101]Fenians--if it were true that every normal Irishman wanted an

[P01-102]Englishman to come and live with him. But as we know there are no

[P01-103]instincts in any of these directions, these analogies are not only

[P01-104]false but false on the cardinal fact. I do not speak of the comparative

[P01-105]comfort or merit of these different things: I say they are different. It

[P01-106]may be that love turned to hate is terribly common in sexual matters: it

[P01-107]may be that hate turned to love is not uncommon in the rivalries of race

[P01-108]or class. But any philosophy about the sexes that begins with anything

[P01-109]but the mutual attraction of the sexes, begins with a fallacy; and all

[P01-110]its historical comparisons are as irrelevant and impertinent as puns.

[P01-111]But to expose such cold negation of the instincts is easy: to express

[P01-112]or even half express the instincts is very hard. The instincts are very

[P01-113]much concerned with what literary people call “style” in letters or more

[P01-114]vulgar people call “style” in dress. They are much concerned with how

[P01-115]a thing is done, as well as whether one may do it: and the deepest

[P01-116]elements in their attraction or aversion can often only be conveyed

[P01-117]by stray examples or sudden images. When Danton was defending himself

[P01-118]before the Jacobin tribunal he spoke so loud that his voice was heard

[P01-119]across the Seine, in quite remote streets on the other side of the

[P01-120]river. He must have bellowed like a bull of Bashan. Yet none of us would

[P01-121]think of that prodigy except as something poetical and appropriate. None

[P01-122]of us would instinctively feel that Danton was less of a man or even

[P01-123]less of a gentleman, for speaking so in such an hour. But suppose we

[P01-124]heard that Marie Antoinette, when tried before the same tribunal,

[P01-125]had howled so that she could be heard in the Faubourg St.

[P01-126]Germain--well, I leave it to the instincts, if there are any left.

[P01-127]It is not wrong to howl. Neither is it right. It is simply a question

[P01-128]of the instant impression on the artistic and even animal parts of

[P01-129]humanity, if the noise were heard suddenly like a gun.

[P01-130]Perhaps the nearest verbal analysis of the instinct may be found in

[P01-131]the gestures of the orator addressing a crowd. For the true orator must

[P01-132]always be a demagogue: even if the mob be a small mob, like the French

[P01-133]committee or the English House of Lords. And “demagogue,” in the good

[P01-134]Greek meaning, does not mean one who pleases the populace, but one who

[P01-135]leads it: and if you will notice, you will see that all the instinctive

[P01-136]gestures of oratory are gestures of military leadership; pointing the

[P01-137]people to a path or waving them on to an advance. Notice that long

[P01-138]sweep of the arm across the body and outward, which great orators use

[P01-139]naturally and cheap orators artificially. It is almost the exact gesture

[P01-140]of the drawing of a sword.

[P01-141]The point is not that women are unworthy of votes; it is not even that

[P01-142]votes are unworthy of women. It is that votes are unworthy of men, so

[P01-143]long as they are merely votes; and have nothing in them of this ancient

[P01-144]militarism of democracy. The only crowd worth talking to is the crowd

[P01-145]that is ready to go somewhere and do something; the only demagogue worth

[P01-146]hearing is he who can point at something to be done: and, if he points

[P01-147]with a sword, will only feel it familiar and useful like an elongated

[P01-148]finger. Now, except in some mystical exceptions which prove the rule,

[P01-149]these are not the gestures, and therefore not the instincts, of women.

[P01-150]No honest man dislikes the public woman. He can only dislike the

[P01-151]political woman; an entirely different thing. The instinct has nothing

[P01-152]to do with any desire to keep women curtained or captive: if such a

[P01-153]desire exists. A husband would be pleased if his wife wore a gold crown

[P01-154]and proclaimed laws from a throne of marble; or if she uttered oracles

[P01-155]from the tripod of a priestess; or if she could walk in mystical

[P01-156]motherhood before the procession of some great religious order. But that

[P01-157]she should stand on a platform in the exact altitude in which he stands;

[P01-158]leaning forward a little more than is graceful and holding her mouth

[P01-159]open a little longer and wider than is dignified--well, I only

[P01-160]write here of the facts of natural history; and the fact is that it is

[P01-161]this, and not publicity or importance, that hurts. It is for the modern

[P01-162]world to judge whether such instincts are indeed danger signals; and

[P01-163]whether the hurting of moral as of material nerves is a tocsin and a

[P01-164]warning of nature.

[P01-165]THE POET AND THE CHEESE

[P01-166]There is something creepy in the flat Eastern Counties; a brush of the

[P01-167]white feather. There is a stillness, which is rather of the mind than of

[P01-168]the bodily senses. Rapid changes and sudden revelations of scenery, even

[P01-169]when they are soundless, have something in them analogous to a movement

[P01-170]of music, to a crash or a cry. Mountain hamlets spring out on us with

[P01-171]a shout like mountain brigands. Comfortable valleys accept us with open

[P01-172]arms and warm words, like comfortable innkeepers. But travelling in the

[P01-173]great level lands has a curiously still and lonely quality; lonely even

[P01-174]when there are plenty of people on the road and in the market-place.

[P01-175]One's voice seems to break an almost elvish silence, and something

[P01-176]unreasonably weird in the phrase of the nursery tales, “And he went a

[P01-177]little farther and came to another place,” comes back into the mind.

[P01-178]In some such mood I came along a lean, pale road south of the fens, and

[P01-179]found myself in a large, quiet, and seemingly forgotten village. It was

[P01-180]one of those places that instantly produce a frame of mind which, it may

[P01-181]be, one afterwards decks out with unreal details. I dare say that grass

[P01-182]did not really grow in the streets, but I came away with a curious

[P01-183]impression that it did. I dare say the marketplace was not literally

[P01-184]lonely and without sign of life, but it left the vague impression of

[P01-185]being so. The place was large and even loose in design, yet it had the

[P01-186]air of something hidden away and always overlooked. It seemed shy, like

[P01-187]a big yokel; the low roofs seemed to be ducking behind the hedges and

[P01-188]railings; and the chimneys holding their breath. I came into it in that

[P01-189]dead hour of the afternoon which is neither after lunch nor before tea,

[P01-190]nor anything else even on a half-holiday; and I had a fantastic feeling

[P01-191]that I had strayed into a lost and extra hour that is not numbered in

[P01-192]the twenty-four.

[P01-193]I entered an inn which stood openly in the market-place yet was almost

[P01-194]as private as a private house. Those who talk of “public-houses” as if

[P01-195]they were all one problem would have been both puzzled and pleased with

[P01-196]such a place. In the front window a stout old lady in black with an

[P01-197]elaborate cap sat doing a large piece of needlework. She had a kind of

[P01-198]comfortable Puritanism about her; and might have been (perhaps she was)

[P01-199]the original Mrs. Grundy. A little more withdrawn into the parlour sat

[P01-200]a tall, strong, and serious girl, with a face of beautiful honesty and

[P01-201]a pair of scissors stuck in her belt, doing a small piece of needlework.

[P01-202]Two feet behind them sat a hulking labourer with a humorous face like

[P01-203]wood painted scarlet, with a huge mug of mild beer which he had not

[P01-204]touched, and probably would not touch for hours. On the hearthrug there

[P01-205]was an equally motionless cat; and on the table a copy of 'Household

[P01-206]Words'.
