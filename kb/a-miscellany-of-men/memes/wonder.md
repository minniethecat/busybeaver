# Wonder

Wonder, for Chesterton, was not a decorative emotion — a pleasant frill added to the serious business of thinking. It was the foundation of thinking itself. To lose the capacity for wonder was, in his view, to lose the capacity to see the world as it actually is: strange, surprising, and saturated with meaning that no theory can exhaust.

## The Freshness of the Commonplace

Chesterton locates the paradox of wonder in his opening essay, where he describes the atmosphere of [[william-wordsworth|Wordsworth]]'s rural poems as "full of genuine freshness and wonder, and yet are in some incurable way commonplace." (001-006) The freshness and the commonplaceness are not opposed; they are the same thing seen from different angles. What is common is also fresh, if you have eyes to see it — and what is fresh is also common, because the world renews itself every morning.

This is the Chestertonian epistemology in miniature. The world does not need to be exotic to be wonderful; it needs to be seen. The poet's task is not to transport us to strange lands but to make our own land strange to us — to restore the wonder that familiarity has worn away.

## The Divine Madness of Man

In one of his most striking passages, Chesterton meditates on the peculiar human practice of ritual disguise. "It is part of that divine madness which all other creatures wonder at in Man," he writes, "that he alone parades this pomp of obliteration and anonymity." (016-058) Man is the only creature who dresses himself, and the only creature who disguises himself — who takes pleasure in becoming, for a ceremonial moment, someone or something other than what he is.

The phrase "divine madness" is carefully chosen. Wonder, for Chesterton, is not a mild or comfortable emotion; it is a kind of madness, a willingness to be astonished, a capacity for being overwhelmed. The man who cannot be astonished is not rational; he is dead.

## The Wonderful Wheels of Industry

Chesterton extends his analysis of wonder into the economic sphere with characteristic unpredictability. "If you go to a factory," he writes, "you will see some very wonderful wheels going round." (020-142) The wheels are genuinely wonderful — marvels of engineering, testimony to human ingenuity. But the wonder of the wheels must not be allowed to obscure the question of whether the men who tend them are justly treated.

This is wonder as a moral discipline. To see the wheels as wonderful is right and proper; to see only the wheels, and not the men, is a failure of vision. Wonder, properly directed, leads toward justice; wonder, misdirected, becomes a tool of oppression, a dazzle that prevents scrutiny.

## Fairies, Cats, and the Untrusted Wonderful

Chesterton turns to folklore to make a subtle point about the relationship between wonder and trust. In the old ballads, fairies "are not specially spoken of as evil; they are enjoyed as witching and wonderful; but they are not trusted as good." (023-057) Wonder and trust are distinct responses, and Chesterton insists on keeping them separate.

This is a political as well as an aesthetic point. The wonderful is not automatically the good, and the capacity to inspire wonder is not the same as the capacity to deserve trust. A political movement, a social class, or a foreign culture may be genuinely wonderful without being trustworthy — and the person who confuses the two has committed an error that is both aesthetic and moral.

## The Unexpected Spring

Chesterton closes one of his essays with a meditation on Spring itself — "one of those wonderful evenings in which the sky was warm and radiant while the earth was still comparatively cold and wet." (003-108) The wonder of Spring, he observes, lies in its unexpectedness: "it is of the essence of Spring to be unexpected; as in that heroic and hackneyed line about coming 'before the swallow dares.'" (003-110)

The line is hackneyed because it is true, and Chesterton refuses to be embarrassed by truths merely because they are familiar. Wonder, in the end, is the willingness to be surprised by what you already know — to find the hackneyed heroic, and the commonplace fresh, and the ordinary world more astonishing than any fantasy.
