# The Thing

[003-001]A title does not now even serve as a distinction: it does not

[003-002]distinguish. A coronet is not merely an extinguisher: it is a

[003-003]hiding-place.

[003-004]But the really odd thing is this. This false quality in titles does not

[003-005]merely apply to the new and vulgar titles, but to the old and historic

[003-006]titles also. For hundreds of years titles in England have been

[003-007]essentially unmeaning; void of that very weak and very human instinct in

[003-008]which titles originated. In essential nonsense of application there is

[003-009]nothing to choose between Northcliffe and Norfolk. The Duke of Norfolk

[003-010]means (as my exquisite and laborious knowledge of Latin informs me) the

[003-011]Leader of Norfolk. It is idle to talk against representative government

[003-012]or for it. All government is representative government until it begins

[003-013]to decay. Unfortunately (as is also evident) all government begins to

[003-014]decay the instant it begins to govern. All aristocrats were first meant

[003-015]as envoys of democracy; and most envoys of democracy lose no time in

[003-016]becoming aristocrats. By the old essential human notion, the Duke of

[003-017]Norfolk ought simply to be the first or most manifest of Norfolk men.

[003-018]I see growing and filling out before me the image of an actual Duke of

[003-019]Norfolk. For instance, Norfolk men all make their voices run up very

[003-020]high at the end of a sentence. The Duke of Norfolk's voice, therefore,

[003-021]ought to end in a perfect shriek. They often (I am told) end sentences

[003-022]with the word “together”; entirely irrespective of its meaning. Thus

[003-023]I shall expect the Duke of Norfolk to say: “I beg to second the motion

[003-024]together”; or “This is a great constitutional question together.” I

[003-025]shall expect him to know much about the Broads and the sluggish rivers

[003-026]above them; to know about the shooting of water-fowl, and not to

[003-027]know too much about anything else. Of mountains he must be wildly and

[003-028]ludicrously ignorant. He must have the freshness of Norfolk; nay, even

[003-029]the flatness of Norfolk. He must remind me of the watery expanses, the

[003-030]great square church towers and the long level sunsets of East England.

[003-031]If he does not do this, I decline to know him.

[003-032]I need not multiply such cases; the principle applies everywhere. Thus I

[003-033]lose all interest in the Duke of Devonshire unless he can assure me that

[003-034]his soul is filled with that strange warm Puritanism, Puritanism shot

[003-035]with romance, which colours the West Country. He must eat nothing but

[003-036]clotted cream, drink nothing but cider, reading nothing but 'Lorna

[003-037]Doone', and be unacquainted with any town larger than Plymouth, which he

[003-038]must regard with some awe, as the Central Babylon of the world. Again, I

[003-039]should expect the Prince of Wales always to be full of the mysticism and

[003-040]dreamy ardour of the Celtic fringe.

[003-041]Perhaps it may be thought that these demands are a little extreme; and

[003-042]that our fancy is running away with us. Nevertheless, it is not my Duke

[003-043]of Devonshire who is funny; but the real Duke of Devonshire. The point

[003-044]is that the scheme of titles is a misfit throughout: hardly anywhere do

[003-045]we find a modern man whose name and rank represent in any way his type,

[003-046]his locality, or his mode of life. As a mere matter of social comedy,

[003-047]the thing is worth noticing. You will meet a man whose name suggests a

[003-048]gouty admiral, and you will find him exactly like a timid organist:

[003-049]you will hear announced the name of a haughty and almost heathen grande

[003-050]dame, and behold the entrance of a nice, smiling Christian cook. These

[003-051]are light complications of the central fact of the falsification of all

[003-052]names and ranks. Our peers are like a party of mediæval knights who

[003-053]should have exchanged shields, crests, and pennons. For the present rule

[003-054]seems to be that the Duke of Sussex may lawfully own the whole of Essex;

[003-055]and that the Marquis of Cornwall may own all the hills and valleys so

[003-056]long as they are not Cornish.

[003-057]The clue to all this tangle is as simple as it is terrible. If England

[003-058]is an aristocracy, England is dying. If this system IS the country,

[003-059]as some say, the country is stiffening into more than the pomp and

[003-060]paralysis of China. It is the final sign of imbecility in a people that

[003-061]it calls cats dogs and describes the sun as the moon--and is very

[003-062]particular about the preciseness of these pseudonyms. To be wrong, and

[003-063]to be carefully wrong, that is the definition of decadence. The disease

[003-064]called aphasia, in which people begin by saying tea when they mean

[003-065]coffee, commonly ends in their silence. Silence of this stiff sort is

[003-066]the chief mark of the powerful parts of modern society. They all seem

[003-067]straining to keep things in rather than to let things out. For the kings

[003-068]of finance speechlessness is counted a way of being strong, though it

[003-069]should rather be counted a way of being sly. By this time the Parliament

[003-070]does not parley any more than the Speaker speaks. Even the newspaper

[003-071]editors and proprietors are more despotic and dangerous by what they do

[003-072]not utter than by what they do. We have all heard the expression “golden

[003-073]silence.” The expression “brazen silence” is the only adequate phrase

[003-074]for our editors. If we wake out of this throttled, gaping, and wordless

[003-075]nightmare, we must awake with a yell. The Revolution that releases

[003-076]England from the fixed falsity of its present position will be not less

[003-077]noisy than other revolutions. It will contain, I fear, a great deal of

[003-078]that rude accomplishment described among little boys as “calling names”;

[003-079]but that will not matter much so long as they are the right names.

[003-080]THE GARDENER AND THE GUINEA

[003-081]Strictly speaking, there is no such thing as an English Peasant.

[003-082]Indeed, the type can only exist in community, so much does it depend on

[003-083]cooperation and common laws. One must not think primarily of a French

[003-084]Peasant; any more than of a German Measle. The plural of the word is its

[003-085]proper form; you cannot have a Peasant till you have a peasantry. The

[003-086]essence of the Peasant ideal is equality; and you cannot be equal all by

[003-087]yourself.

[003-088]Nevertheless, because human nature always craves and half creates

[003-089]the things necessary to its happiness, there are approximations and

[003-090]suggestions of the possibility of such a race even here. The nearest

[003-091]approach I know to the temper of a Peasant in England is that of the

[003-092]country gardener; not, of course, the great scientific gardener attached

[003-093]to the great houses; he is a rich man's servant like any other. I mean

[003-094]the small jobbing gardener who works for two or three moderate-sized

[003-095]gardens; who works on his own; who sometimes even owns his house; and

[003-096]who frequently owns his tools. This kind of man has really some of the

[003-097]characteristics of the true Peasant--especially the characteristics

[003-098]that people don't like. He has none of that irresponsible mirth which

[003-099]is the consolation of most poor men in England. The gardener is even

[003-100]disliked sometimes by the owners of the shrubs and flowers; because

[003-101](like Micaiah) he prophesies not good concerning them, but evil. The

[003-102]English gardener is grim, critical, self-respecting; sometimes even

[003-103]economical. Nor is this (as the reader's lightning wit will flash back

[003-104]at me) merely because the English gardener is always a Scotch gardener.

[003-105]The type does exist in pure South England blood and speech; I have

[003-106]spoken to the type. I was speaking to the type only the other evening,

[003-107]when a rather odd little incident occurred.

[003-108]It was one of those wonderful evenings in which the sky was warm and

[003-109]radiant while the earth was still comparatively cold and wet. But it

[003-110]is of the essence of Spring to be unexpected; as in that heroic and

[003-111]hackneyed line about coming “before the swallow dares.” Spring never is

[003-112]Spring unless it comes too soon. And on a day like that one might pray,

[003-113]without any profanity, that Spring might come on earth as it was in

[003-114]heaven. The gardener was gardening. I was not gardening. It is needless

[003-115]to explain the causes of this difference; it would be to tell the

[003-116]tremendous history of two souls. It is needless because there is a more

[003-117]immediate explanation of the case: the gardener and I, if not equal in

[003-118]agreement, were at least equal in difference. It is quite certain that

[003-119]he would not have allowed me to touch the garden if I had gone down

[003-120]on my knees to him. And it is by no means certain that I should have

[003-121]consented to touch the garden if he had gone down on his knees to me.

[003-122]His activity and my idleness, therefore, went on steadily side by side

[003-123]through the long sunset hours.

[003-124]And all the time I was thinking what a shame it was that he was not

[003-125]sticking his spade into his own garden, instead of mine: he knew about

[003-126]the earth and the underworld of seeds, the resurrection of Spring and

[003-127]the flowers that appear in order like a procession marshalled by a

[003-128]herald. He possessed the garden intellectually and spiritually, while

[003-129]I only possessed it politically. I know more about flowers than

[003-130]coal-owners know about coal; for at least I pay them honour when they

[003-131]are brought above the surface of the earth. I know more about gardens

[003-132]than railway shareholders seem to know about railways: for at least I

[003-133]know that it needs a man to make a garden; a man whose name is Adam. But

[003-134]as I walked on that grass my ignorance overwhelmed me--and yet that

[003-135]phrase is false, because it suggests something like a storm from the sky

[003-136]above. It is truer to say that my ignorance exploded underneath me, like

[003-137]a mine dug long before; and indeed it was dug before the beginning of

[003-138]the ages. Green bombs of bulbs and seeds were bursting underneath me

[003-139]everywhere; and, so far as my knowledge went, they had been laid by

[003-140]a conspirator. I trod quite uneasily on this uprush of the earth; the

[003-141]Spring is always only a fruitful earthquake. With the land all alive

[003-142]under me I began to wonder more and more why this man, who had made the

[003-143]garden, did not own the garden. If I stuck a spade into the ground, I

[003-144]should be astonished at what I found there...and just as I thought this

[003-145]I saw that the gardener was astonished too.

[003-146]Just as I was wondering why the man who used the spade did not profit by

[003-147]the spade, he brought me something he had found actually in my soil. It

[003-148]was a thin worn gold piece of the Georges, of the sort which are called,

[003-149]I believe, Spade Guineas. Anyhow, a piece of gold.

[003-150]If you do not see the parable as I saw it just then, I doubt if I can

[003-151]explain it just now. He could make a hundred other round yellow fruits:

[003-152]and this flat yellow one is the only sort that I can make. How it came

[003-153]there I have not a notion--unless Edmund Burke dropped it in his

[003-154]hurry to get back to Butler's Court. But there it was: this is a cold

[003-155]recital of facts. There may be a whole pirate's treasure lying under

[003-156]the earth there, for all I know or care; for there is no interest in a

[003-157]treasure without a Treasure Island to sail to. If there is a treasure it

[003-158]will never be found, for I am not interested in wealth beyond the dreams

[003-159]of avarice since I know that avarice has no dreams, but only insomnia.

[003-160]And, for the other party, my gardener would never consent to dig up the

[003-161]garden.

[003-162]Nevertheless, I was overwhelmed with intellectual emotions when I saw

[003-163]that answer to my question; the question of why the garden did not

[003-164]belong to the gardener. No better epigram could be put in reply than

[003-165]simply putting the Spade Guinea beside the Spade. This was the only

[003-166]underground seed that I could understand. Only by having a little more

[003-167]of that dull, battered yellow substance could I manage to be idle while

[003-168]he was active. I am not altogether idle myself; but the fact remains

[003-169]that the power is in the thin slip of metal we call the Spade Guinea,

[003-170]not in the strong square and curve of metal which we call the Spade.

[003-171]And then I suddenly remembered that as I had found gold on my ground by

[003-172]accident, so richer men in the north and west counties had found coal in

[003-173]their ground, also by accident.
