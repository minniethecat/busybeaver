# The Modern

"The modern" in Chesterton's vocabulary is not a neutral temporal designation but a term of critique. It names a set of assumptions — about progress, about the superiority of the present, about the obsolescence of the past — that Chesterton subjects to sustained scrutiny throughout "A Miscellany of Men."

## The Typical Modern Movement

Chesterton approaches modernity through its movements. "Our modern discussions about everything," he writes, tend to proceed from a set of unexamined premises that beg the very questions at issue. (002-055) The typical modern movement, he argues, does not argue for its assumptions; it merely assumes them, and treats anyone who does not share them as not merely wrong but unintelligible.

He is particularly attentive to "the way in which modern" thought proceeds backwards — "a tail-foremost way." (002-080) The modern thinker, in Chesterton's portrait, begins with conclusions and works backward to premises, mistaking preference for proof. The typical modern movement is not, he insists, typical because it is right but because it is fashionable. (002-082)

## Modern England

Chesterton applies his critique specifically to his own country. "In modern [[england|England]] neither the..." (001-106) — he observes a gap between English ideals and English realities, a gap that the rhetoric of progress serves to conceal rather than reveal. Modern [[england|England]] congratulates itself on its advancement while the substance of English liberty erodes.

"We hear much in modern discussion: the separation of..." (001-081) — Chesterton implies that what is discussed is not what matters. The modern discourse, with its vocabulary of rights and reforms, often misses the underlying realities of power, community, and belief that actually shape human life.

## The Modern and the Ancient

Crucially, Chesterton does not oppose "the modern" in the name of a golden age. He opposes it in the name of reality. "Self-government was not (as many modern friends and foes..." think) invented yesterday. (001-093) The modern claim to novelty is, for Chesterton, usually a claim to ignorance — a refusal to recognise that previous generations faced similar problems and may have solved them better.

"The powerful parts of modern society," he observes, "all seem" to operate on principles that contradict their stated ideals. (003-066) Modern society, for Chesterton, is "almost" entirely built on this contradiction between profession and practice. (011-106) The cure is not to go backward but to stop assuming that going forward is always going right.
