# The Romantic In The Rain

[018-001]This is the spirit which makes the Christian poor begin their terrible

[018-002]murmur whenever there is a turn of prices or a deadlock of toil

[018-003]that threatens them with vagabondage or pauperisation; and we cannot

[018-004]encourage the Dean with any hope that this spirit can be cast out.

[018-005]Christendom will continue to suffer all the disadvantages of being

[018-006]Christian: it is the Dean who must be gently but firmly altered. He had

[018-007]absent-mindedly strayed into the wrong continent and the wrong creed. I

[018-008]advise him to chuck it.

[018-009]But the case is more curious still. To connect the Dean with Confucian

[018-010]temples or traditions may have appeared fantastic; but it is not. Dr.

[018-011]Inge is not a stupid old Tory Rector, strict both on Church and State.

[018-012]Such a man might talk nonsense about the Christian Socialists being

[018-013]“court chaplains of King Demos” or about his own superb valour in

[018-014]defying the democracy that rages in the front pews of Anglican churches.

[018-015]We should not expect a mere old-fashioned country clergyman to know that

[018-016]Demos has never been king in England and precious seldom anywhere else;

[018-017]we should not expect him to realise that if King Demos had any chaplains

[018-018]they would be uncommonly poorly paid. But Dr. Inge is not old-fashioned;

[018-019]he considers himself highly progressive and advanced. He is a New

[018-020]Theologian; that is, he is liberal in theology--and nothing else.

[018-021]He is apparently in sober fact, and not as in any fantasy, in sympathy

[018-022]with those who would soften the superior claim of our creed by urging

[018-023]the rival creeds of the East; with those who would absorb the virtues of

[018-024]Buddhism or of Islam. He holds a high seat in that modern Parliament of

[018-025]Religions where all believers respect each other's unbelief.

[018-026]Now this has a very sharp moral for modern religious reformers. When

[018-027]next you hear the “liberal” Christian say that we should take what is

[018-028]best in Oriental faiths, make quite sure what are the things that people

[018-029]like Dr. Inge call best; what are the things that people like Dr. Inge

[018-030]propose to take. You will not find them imitating the military valour of

[018-031]the Moslem. You will not find them imitating the miraculous ecstasy of

[018-032]the Hindoo. The more you study the “broad” movement of today, the more

[018-033]you will find that these people want something much less like Chinese

[018-034]metaphysics, and something much more like Chinese Labour. You will find

[018-035]the levelling of creeds quite unexpectedly close to the lowering of

[018-036]wages. Dr. Inge is the typical latitudinarian of to-day; and was never

[018-037]more so than when he appeared not as the apostle of the blacks, but as

[018-038]the apostle of the blacklegs. Preached, as it is, almost entirely

[018-039]among the prosperous and polite, our brotherhood with Buddhism or

[018-040]Mohammedanism practically means this--that the poor must be as meek

[018-041]as Buddhists, while the rich may be as ruthless as Mohammedans. That is

[018-042]what they call the reunion of all religions.

[018-043]THE ROMANTIC IN THE RAIN

[018-044]The middle classes of modern England are quite fanatically fond of

[018-045]washing; and are often enthusiastic for teetotalism. I cannot therefore

[018-046]comprehend why it is that they exhibit a mysterious dislike of rain.

[018-047]Rain, that inspiring and delightful thing, surely combines the qualities

[018-048]of these two ideals with quite a curious perfection. Our philanthropists

[018-049]are eager to establish public baths everywhere. Rain surely is a public

[018-050]bath; it might almost be called mixed bathing. The appearance of persons

[018-051]coming fresh from this great natural lustration is not perhaps polished

[018-052]or dignified; but for the matter of that, few people are dignified when

[018-053]coming out of a bath. But the scheme of rain in itself is one of an

[018-054]enormous purification. It realises the dream of some insane hygienist:

[018-055]it scrubs the sky. Its giant brooms and mops seem to reach the starry

[018-056]rafters and Starless corners of the cosmos; it is a cosmic spring

[018-057]cleaning.

[018-058]If the Englishman is really fond of cold baths, he ought not to grumble

[018-059]at the English climate for being a cold bath. In these days we are

[018-060]constantly told that we should leave our little special possessions and

[018-061]join in the enjoyment of common social institutions and a common social

[018-062]machinery. I offer the rain as a thoroughly Socialistic institution. It

[018-063]disregards that degraded delicacy which has hitherto led each gentleman

[018-064]to take his shower-bath in private. It is a better shower-bath, because

[018-065]it is public and communal; and, best of all, because somebody else pulls

[018-066]the string.

[018-067]As for the fascination of rain for the water drinker, it is a fact the

[018-068]neglect of which I simply cannot comprehend. The enthusiastic water

[018-069]drinker must regard a rainstorm as a sort of universal banquet and

[018-070]debauch of his own favourite beverage. Think of the imaginative

[018-071]intoxication of the wine drinker if the crimson clouds sent down claret

[018-072]or the golden clouds hock. Paint upon primitive darkness some such

[018-073]scenes of apocalypse, towering and gorgeous skyscapes in which champagne

[018-074]falls like fire from heaven or the dark skies grow purple and tawny with

[018-075]the terrible colours of port. All this must the wild abstainer feel, as

[018-076]he rolls in the long soaking grass, kicks his ecstatic heels to heaven,

[018-077]and listens to the roaring rain. It is he, the water drinker, who

[018-078]ought to be the true bacchanal of the forests; for all the forests are

[018-079]drinking water. Moreover, the forests are apparently enjoying it: the

[018-080]trees rave and reel to and fro like drunken giants; they clash boughs

[018-081]as revellers clash cups; they roar undying thirst and howl the health of

[018-082]the world.

[018-083]All around me as I write is a noise of Nature drinking: and Nature makes

[018-084]a noise when she is drinking, being by no means refined. If I count

[018-085]it Christian mercy to give a cup of cold water to a sufferer, shall I

[018-086]complain of these multitudinous cups of cold water handed round to all

[018-087]living things; a cup of water for every shrub; a cup of water for every

[018-088]weed? I would be ashamed to grumble at it. As Sir Philip Sidney said,

[018-089]their need is greater than mine--especially for water.

[018-090]There is a wild garment that still carries nobly the name of a wild

[018-091]Highland clan: a elan come from those hills where rain is not so much an

[018-092]incident as an atmosphere. Surely every man of imagination must feel

[018-093]a tempestuous flame of Celtic romance spring up within him whenever he

[018-094]puts on a mackintosh. I could never reconcile myself to carrying an

[018-095]umbrella; it is a pompous Eastern business, carried over the heads of

[018-096]despots in the dry, hot lands. Shut up, an umbrella is an unmanageable

[018-097]walking stick; open, it is an inadequate tent. For my part, I have no

[018-098]taste for pretending to be a walking pavilion; I think nothing of my

[018-099]hat, and precious little of my head. If I am to be protected against

[018-100]wet, it must be by some closer and more careless protection, something

[018-101]that I can forget altogether. It might be a Highland plaid. It might be

[018-102]that yet more Highland thing, a mackintosh.

[018-103]And there is really something in the mackintosh of the military

[018-104]qualities of the Highlander. The proper cheap mackintosh has a blue and

[018-105]white sheen as of steel or iron; it gleams like armour. I like to think

[018-106]of it as the uniform of that ancient clan in some of its old and misty

[018-107]raids. I like to think of all the Macintoshes, in their mackintoshes,

[018-108]descending on some doomed Lowland village, their wet waterproofs

[018-109]flashing in the sun or moon. For indeed this is one of the real beauties

[018-110]of rainy weather, that while the amount of original and direct light

[018-111]is commonly lessened, the number of things that reflect light is

[018-112]unquestionably increased. There is less sunshine; but there are more

[018-113]shiny things; such beautifully shiny things as pools and puddles and

[018-114]mackintoshes. It is like moving in a world of mirrors.

[018-115]And indeed this is the last and not the least gracious of the casual

[018-116]works of magic wrought by rain: that while it decreases light, yet it

[018-117]doubles it. If it dims the sky, it brightens the earth. It gives the

[018-118]roads (to the sympathetic eye) something of the beauty of Venice.

[018-119]Shallow lakes of water reiterate every detail of earth and sky; we

[018-120]dwell in a double universe. Sometimes walking upon bare and lustrous

[018-121]pavements, wet under numerous lamps, a man seems a black blot on all

[018-122]that golden looking-glass, and could fancy he was flying in a yellow

[018-123]sky. But wherever trees and towns hang head downwards in a pigmy puddle,

[018-124]the sense of Celestial topsy-turvydom is the same. This bright, wet,

[018-125]dazzling confusion of shape and shadow, of reality and reflection, will

[018-126]appeal strongly to any one with the transcendental instinct about this

[018-127]dreamy and dual life of ours. It will always give a man the strange

[018-128]sense of looking down at the skies.

[018-129]THE FALSE PHOTOGRAPHER

[018-130]When, as lately, events have happened that seem (to the fancy, at least)

[018-131]to test if not stagger the force of official government, it is

[018-132]amusing to ask oneself what is the real weakness of civilisation, ours

[018-133]especially, when it contends with the one lawless man. I was reminded of

[018-134]one weakness this morning in turning over an old drawerful of pictures.

[018-135]This weakness in civilisation is best expressed by saying that it cares

[018-136]more for science than for truth. It prides itself on its “methods”

[018-137]more than its results; it is satisfied with precision, discipline, good

[018-138]communications, rather than with the sense of reality. But there are

[018-139]precise falsehoods as well as precise facts. Discipline may only mean

[018-140]a hundred men making the same mistake at the same minute. And good

[018-141]communications may in practice be very like those evil communications

[018-142]which are said to corrupt good manners. Broadly, we have reached a

[018-143]“scientific age,” which wants to know whether the train is in the

[018-144]timetable, but not whether the train is in the station. I take one

[018-145]instance in our police inquiries that I happen to have come across: the

[018-146]case of photography.

[018-147]Some years ago a poet of considerable genius tragically disappeared,

[018-148]and the authorities or the newspapers circulated a photograph of him, so

[018-149]that he might be identified. The photograph, as I remember it, depicted

[018-150]or suggested a handsome, haughty, and somewhat pallid man with his head

[018-151]thrown back, with long distinguished features, colourless thin hair and

[018-152]slight moustache, and though conveyed merely by the head and shoulders,

[018-153]a definite impression of height. If I had gone by that photograph I

[018-154]should have gone about looking for a long soldierly but listless man,

[018-155]with a profile rather like the Duke of Connaught's.

[018-156]Only, as it happened, I knew the poet personally; I had seen him a great

[018-157]many times, and he had an appearance that nobody could possibly forget,

[018-158]if seen only once. He had the mark of those dark and passionate Westland

[018-159]Scotch, who before Burns and after have given many such dark eyes and

[018-160]dark emotions to the world. But in him the unmistakable strain, Gaelic

[018-161]or whatever it is, was accentuated almost to oddity; and he looked

[018-162]like some swarthy elf. He was small, with a big head and a crescent of

[018-163]coal-black hair round the back of a vast dome of baldness. Immediately

[018-164]under his eyes his cheekbones had so high a colour that they might have

[018-165]been painted scarlet; three black tufts, two on the upper lip and one

[018-166]under the lower, seemed to touch up the face with the fierce moustaches

[018-167]of Mephistopheles. His eyes had that “dancing madness” in them which

[018-168]Stevenson saw in the Gaelic eyes of Alan Breck; but he sometimes

[018-169]distorted the expression by screwing a monstrous monocle into one of

[018-170]them. A man more unmistakable would have been hard to find. You could

[018-171]have picked him out in any crowd--so long as you had not seen his

[018-172]photograph.
