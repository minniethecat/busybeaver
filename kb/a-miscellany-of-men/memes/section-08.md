# The Voter and the Two Voices

[007-001]Well, what would happen then, over the larger parts of the planet, parts

[007-002]inhabited by savages? Savages, of course, would hunt and fish. That

[007-003]retreat for the English poor was perceived; and that retreat was cut

[007-004]off. Game laws were made to extend over districts like the Arctic snows

[007-005]or the Sahara. The rich man had property over animals he had no more

[007-006]dreamed of than a governor of Roman Africa had dreamed of a giraffe.

[007-007]He owned all the birds that passed over his land: he might as well have

[007-008]owned all the clouds that passed over it. If a rabbit ran from Smith's

[007-009]land to Brown's land, it belonged to Brown, as if it were his pet dog.

[007-010]The logical answer to this would be simple: Any one stung on Brown's

[007-011]land ought to be able to prosecute Brown for keeping a dangerous wasp

[007-012]without a muzzle.

[007-013]Thus the poor man was forced to be a tramp along the roads and to sleep

[007-014]in the open. That retreat was perceived; and that retreat was cut off.

[007-015]A landless man in England can be punished for behaving in the only way

[007-016]that a landless man can behave: for sleeping under a hedge in Surrey or

[007-017]on a seat on the Embankment. His sin is described (with a hideous sense

[007-018]of fun) as that of having no visible means of subsistence.

[007-019]The last possibility, of course, is that upon which all human beings

[007-020]would fall back if they were sinking in a swamp or impaled on a spike

[007-021]or deserted on an island. It is that of calling out for pity to the

[007-022]passerby. That retreat was perceived; and that retreat was cut off. A

[007-023]man in England can be sent to prison for asking another man for help in

[007-024]the name of God.

[007-025]You have done all these things, and by so doing you have forced the poor

[007-026]to serve the rich, and to serve them on the terms of the rich. They have

[007-027]still one weapon left against the extremes of insult and unfairness:

[007-028]that weapon is their numbers and the necessity of those numbers to the

[007-029]working of that vast and slavish machine. And because they still had

[007-030]this last retreat (which we call the Strike), because this retreat

[007-031]was also perceived, there was talk of this retreat being also cut off.

[007-032]Whereupon the workmen became suddenly and violently angry; and struck at

[007-033]your Boards and Committees here, there, and wherever they could. And you

[007-034]opened on them the eyes of owls, and said, “It must be the sunshine.”

[007-035]You could only go on saying, “The sun, the sun.” That was what the man

[007-036]in Ibsen said, when he had lost his wits.

[007-037]THE WRONG INCENDIARY

[007-038]I stood looking at the Coronation Procession--I mean the one

[007-039]in Beaconsfield; not the rather elephantine imitation of it which, I

[007-040]believe, had some success in London--and I was seriously impressed.

[007-041]Most of my life is passed in discovering with a deathly surprise that

[007-042]I was quite right. Never before have I realised how right I was in

[007-043]maintaining that the small area expresses the real patriotism: the

[007-044]smaller the field the taller the tower. There were things in our local

[007-045]procession that did not (one might even reverently say, could not) occur

[007-046]in the London procession. One of the most prominent citizens in our

[007-047]procession (for instance) had his face blacked. Another rode on a pony

[007-048]which wore pink and blue trousers. I was not present at the Metropolitan

[007-049]affair, and therefore my assertion is subject to such correction as the

[007-050]eyewitness may always offer to the absentee. But I believe with some

[007-051]firmness that no such features occurred in the London pageant.

[007-052]But it is not of the local celebration that I would speak, but of

[007-053]something that occurred before it. In the field beyond the end of my

[007-054]garden the materials for a bonfire had been heaped; a hill of every kind

[007-055]of rubbish and refuse and things that nobody wants; broken chairs, dead

[007-056]trees, rags, shavings, newspapers, new religions, in pamphlet form,

[007-057]reports of the Eugenic Congress, and so on. All this refuse, material

[007-058]and mental, it was our purpose to purify and change to holy flame on the

[007-059]day when the King was crowned. The following is an account of the rather

[007-060]strange thing that really happened. I do not know whether it was any

[007-061]sort of symbol; but I narrate it just as it befell.

[007-062]In the middle of the night I woke up slowly and listened to what I

[007-063]supposed to be the heavy crunching of a cart-wheel along a road of loose

[007-064]stones. Then it grew louder, and I thought somebody was shooting out

[007-065]cartloads of stones; then it seemed as if the shock was breaking big

[007-066]stones into pieces. Then I realised that under this sound there was also

[007-067]a strange, sleepy, almost inaudible roar; and that on top of it every

[007-068]now and then came pigmy pops like a battle of penny pistols. Then I knew

[007-069]what it was. I went to the window; and a great firelight flung across

[007-070]two meadows smote me where I stood. “Oh, my holy aunt,” I thought,

[007-071]“they've mistaken the Coronation Day.”

[007-072]And yet when I eyed the transfigured scene it did not seem exactly like

[007-073]a bonfire or any ritual illumination. It was too chaotic, and too close

[007-074]to the houses of the town. All one side of a cottage was painted pink

[007-075]with the giant brush of flame; the next side, by contrast, was painted

[007-076]as black as tar. Along the front of this ran a blackening rim or rampart

[007-077]edged with a restless red ribbon that danced and doubled and devoured

[007-078]like a scarlet snake; and beyond it was nothing but a deathly fulness of

[007-079]light.

[007-080]I put on some clothes and went down the road; all the dull or startling

[007-081]noises in that din of burning growing louder and louder as I walked. The

[007-082]heaviest sound was that of an incessant cracking and crunching, as if

[007-083]some giant with teeth of stone was breaking up the bones of the world. I

[007-084]had not yet come within sight of the real heart and habitat of the fire;

[007-085]but the strong red light, like an unnatural midnight sunset, powdered

[007-086]the grayest grass with gold and flushed the few tall trees up to the

[007-087]last fingers of their foliage. Behind them the night was black and

[007-088]cavernous; and one could only trace faintly the ashen horizon beyond the

[007-089]dark and magic Wilton Woods. As I went, a workman on a bicycle shot a

[007-090]rood past me; then staggered from his machine and shouted to me to tell

[007-091]him where the fire was. I answered that I was going to see, but thought

[007-092]it was the cottages by the wood-yard. He said, “My God!” and vanished.

[007-093]A little farther on I found grass and pavement soaking and flooded, and

[007-094]the red and yellow flames repainted in pools and puddles. Beyond were

[007-095]dim huddles of people and a small distant voice shouting out orders. The

[007-096]fire-engines were at work. I went on among the red reflections, which

[007-097]seemed like subterranean fires; I had a singular sensation of being in a

[007-098]very important dream. Oddly enough, this was increased when I found that

[007-099]most of my friends and neighbours were entangled in the crowd. Only in

[007-100]dreams do we see familiar faces so vividly against a black background of

[007-101]midnight. I was glad to find (for the workman cyclist's sake) that

[007-102]the fire was not in the houses by the wood-yard, but in the wood-yard

[007-103]itself. There was no fear for human life, and the thing was seemingly

[007-104]accidental; though there were the usual ugly whispers about rivalry and

[007-105]revenge. But for all that I could not shake off my dream-drugged soul a

[007-106]swollen, tragic, portentous sort of sensation, that it all had something

[007-107]to do with the crowning of the English King, and the glory or the end

[007-108]of England. It was not till I saw the puddles and the ashes in broad

[007-109]daylight next morning that I was fundamentally certain that my midnight

[007-110]adventure had not happened outside this world.

[007-111]But I was more arrogant than the ancient Emperors Pharaoh or

[007-112]Nebuchadnezzar; for I attempted to interpret my own dream. The fire was

[007-113]feeding upon solid stacks of unused beech or pine, gray and white piles

[007-114]of virgin wood. It was an orgy of mere waste; thousands of good

[007-115]things were being killed before they had ever existed. Doors, tables,

[007-116]walking-sticks, wheelbarrows, wooden swords for boys, Dutch dolls for

[007-117]girls I could hear the cry of each uncreated thing as it expired in the

[007-118]flames. And then I thought of that other noble tower of needless things

[007-119]that stood in the field beyond my garden; the bonfire, the mountain of

[007-120]vanities, that is meant for burning; and how it stood dark and lonely in

[007-121]the meadow, and the birds hopped on its corners and the dew touched and

[007-122]spangled its twigs. And I remembered that there are two kinds of fires,

[007-123]the Bad Fire and the Good Fire the last must surely be the meaning of

[007-124]Bonfire. And the paradox is that the Good Fire is made of bad things, of

[007-125]things that we do not want; but the Bad Fire is made of good things,

[007-126]of things that we do want; like all that wealth of wood that might have

[007-127]made dolls and chairs and tables, but was only making a hueless ash.

[007-128]And then I saw, in my vision, that just as there are two fires, so there

[007-129]are two revolutions. And I saw that the whole mad modern world is a race

[007-130]between them. Which will happen first--the revolution in which

[007-131]bad things shall perish, or that other revolution, in which good things

[007-132]shall perish also? One is the riot that all good men, even the most

[007-133]conservative, really dream of, when the sneer shall be struck from the

[007-134]face of the well-fed; when the wine of honour shall be poured down the

[007-135]throat of despair; when we shall, so far as to the sons of flesh is

[007-136]possible, take tyranny and usury and public treason and bind them into

[007-137]bundles and burn them. And the other is the disruption that may come

[007-138]prematurely, negatively, and suddenly in the night; like the fire in my

[007-139]little town.

[007-140]It may come because the mere strain of modern life is unbearable; and in

[007-141]it even the things that men do desire may break down; marriage and

[007-142]fair ownership and worship and the mysterious worth of man. The two

[007-143]revolutions, white and black, are racing each other like two railway

[007-144]trains; I cannot guess the issue...but even as I thought of it, the

[007-145]tallest turret of the timber stooped and faltered and came down in a

[007-146]cataract of noises. And the fire, finding passage, went up with a spout

[007-147]like a fountain. It stood far up among the stars for an instant, a

[007-148]blazing pillar of brass fit for a pagan conqueror, so high that one

[007-149]could fancy it visible away among the goblin trees of Burnham or along

[007-150]the terraces of the Chiltern Hills.

[007-151]THE FREE MAN

[007-152]The idea of liberty has ultimately a religious root; that is why men

[007-153]find it so easy to die for and so difficult to define. It refers finally

[007-154]to the fact that, while the oyster and the palm tree have to save

[007-155]their lives by law, man has to save his soul by choice. Ruskin rebuked

[007-156]Coleridge for praising freedom, and said that no man would wish the sun

[007-157]to be free. It seems enough to answer that no man would wish to be the

[007-158]sun. Speaking as a Liberal, I have much more sympathy with the idea of

[007-159]Joshua stopping the sun in heaven than with the idea of Ruskin trotting

[007-160]his daily round in imitation of its regularity. Joshua was a Radical,

[007-161]and his astronomical act was distinctly revolutionary. For all

[007-162]revolution is the mastering of matter by the spirit of man, the

[007-163]emergence of that human authority within us which, in the noble words of

[007-164]Sir Thomas Browne, “owes no homage unto the sun.”
