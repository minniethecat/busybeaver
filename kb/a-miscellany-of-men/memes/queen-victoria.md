# Queen Victoria

Queen Victoria reigned over the United Kingdom from 1837 to 1901, the longest reign of any British monarch until Elizabeth II. In "The Architect Of Spears," G.K. Chesterton references her as part of a broader argument about the meaning of Western monarchy and the confusion introduced by Eastern imperial titles.

## Empress and Queen

Chesterton's only direct reference to Victoria occurs in a discussion of titles and their cultural meaning. He notes that it was an Oriental — Benjamin Disraeli — who gave Queen Victoria the crown of an Empress in addition to that of a Queen. (019-131)

The distinction mattered to Chesterton because, as he saw it, Eastern titles aim at vastness and poetic extravagance — "the Brother of the Sun and Moon, the Caliph who lives for ever" — whereas a King of [[england|England]] bore "not a merely poetical title; but rather a religious one." (019-132–019-137) The king belonged to his people, not merely they to him. The addition of "Empress" was, in Chesterton's implicit view, a misunderstanding of what Western kingship meant.

## The Victorian Era as Background

Chesterton uses "Victorian" more broadly to describe a type — the "agnostic old bachelor of the Victorian era" whose views on religion were "simply the stalest ideas of his time." (019-080–019-083)

This Victorian type, for Chesterton, represented a generation that had lost faith without gaining freedom — men who called a high hill in South Africa their church because they could no longer call any church their own. (019-084) Victoria's reign gave its name to an age of scientific certainty and imperial confidence that Chesterton spent much of his career challenging, though he does not blame the Queen herself for these developments.

## Western Kingship and Eastern Empire

The reference to Victoria sits within one of Chesterton's recurring themes: the difference between Western and Eastern conceptions of authority. For Chesterton, Western rule was personal, limited, and rooted in mutual belonging; Eastern rule was impersonal, unlimited, and rooted in cosmic assertion.

Victoria receiving the title of Empress represented, in his view, an Eastern idea grafted onto a Western institution — the beginning of an imperial confusion that would culminate in the "Orientalism in all our drawing-rooms" he observed in his own time. (019-065) The monarch who had once embodied a national and even domestic ideal became, under the spell of empire, something more remote and abstract.
