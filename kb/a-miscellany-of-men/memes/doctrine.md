# Doctrine

Doctrine, in Chesterton's hands, is not a dry theological abstraction but the defining commitment that makes coherent thought and action possible. A religion, he insists, means "something that commits a man to some [[dogma|doctrine]] about the universe" — without which it ceases to be a religion at all (024-136). Doctrine is the bond that holds communities together (011-125), the principle that gives meaning to [[dogma|creed]]s, and the mark of intellectual courage in an age that prefers vagueness. Across his essays, Chesterton traces the idea through the materialist theories of [[karl-marx|Marx]]ists (006-042), the social cohesion of religious groups (011-125), and the democrat's conviction that the people should rule (024-168), arguing in each case that the willingness to hold a definite [[dogma|doctrine]] is a sign of intellectual health rather than narrowness.

## Doctrine as Intellectual Commitment

Chesterton's most compressed statement on [[dogma|doctrine]] occurs in his attack on the modern habit of using adjectives that cancel their nouns. "Don't say, 'I look forward to that larger religion that shall have no special dogmas,'" he warns — for a quadruped means something with four feet, and "a religion means something that commits a man to some [[dogma|doctrine]] about the universe" (024-136). To speak of a religion without [[dogma|doctrine]] is to speak of a pork pie with no pork in it: the phrase sounds progressive but is actually nonsense.

This is not mere word-play. For Chesterton, [[dogma|doctrine]] is what gives a [[dogma|creed]] its substance and its power. To abandon [[dogma|doctrine]] is not to become more tolerant but to become incapable of coherent belief. The man who refuses to commit himself to a [[dogma|doctrine]] about the universe has not thereby become wiser; he has simply declined to think. Chesterton returns to this point in his final admonitions, where he warns that the refusal to hold a [[dogma|creed]] is not a mark of sophistication but of intellectual cowardice. "Don't (if any one calls your [[dogma|doctrine]] mad, which is likely enough), don't answer that madmen are only the minority" — the proper response to the charge of madness is not to deny the [[dogma|doctrine]] but to defend it (025-001). A conviction worth holding is worth holding even when it is unpopular.

## The Social Bond of Doctrine

Doctrine, for Chesterton, is not merely individual conviction; it is the social fabric that holds groups together. In "The Wrong Incendiary," he observes that as long as a religious group shares a [[dogma|creed]], it will contain all the old recurring figures of religious history — the saint, the hypocrite, the brawler, the weak brother — and these figures can be "appealed to by the [[dogma|creed]] and judged by it" (011-125). The [[dogma|creed]] provides a common standard; the hypocrite can be exposed, the brawler rebuked, the saint recognised.

"But once break the bond of [[dogma|doctrine]] which alone holds these people together," Chesterton warns, "and each will gravitate to his own kind outside the group" (011-125). The hypocrites will call each other saints, the saints will lose themselves in a desert, and the weak brethren will grow weaker still. Without the bond of [[dogma|doctrine]], the community dissolves into its component parts, each fragment seeking its own level. Chesterton sees this process already at work in modern English religion, where a "confident pride in having got beyond [[dogma|creed]]s" is coupled with "quite a paralysed incapacity to get beyond catchwords." The loss of [[dogma|doctrine]], far from liberating the mind, leaves it defenceless against vagueness and fashion.

## Doctrine in Political Life

Chesterton extends his analysis into the political realm, where he observes that every serious political position rests on a doctrinal foundation — and that those who deny this are often the most doctrinaire of all. He examines the [[karl-marx|Marx]]ist "materialist theory of history," the [[dogma|doctrine]] "taught, I believe, by most followers of [[karl-marx|Karl Marx]]," that all historical events are rooted in economic motives (006-042). Chesterton finds this [[dogma|doctrine]] not merely untrue but self-defeating. If the materialist argument is accepted, it can be turned against the Socialist who uses it, for "the capitalist can get much more fun out of the [[dogma|doctrine]]" (006-098). The same argument that explains working-class revolt by economic pressure can just as easily explain it by the weather — and the capitalist's version is more flattering to the capitalist.

But Chesterton does not reject political [[dogma|doctrine]] as such. On the contrary, he insists that the democrat must hold fast to the [[dogma|doctrine]] that the people should rule. When a critic accuses him of having "reacted from the [[dogma|doctrine]] that the people should rule," Chesterton responds sharply: "If Mr. Swann thinks I have ever reacted from the [[dogma|doctrine]] that the people should rule, I wish he would give me the reference" (024-168). The [[dogma|doctrine]] of democracy is not a temporary preference but a settled conviction, and Chesterton defends it as such.

Similarly, he upholds "the [[dogma|doctrine]] of human equality" — which "reposes upon this: that there is no man really clever who has not found that he is stupid" (025-019). Political [[dogma|doctrine]], for Chesterton, is the articulated form of a society's deepest commitments, and those commitments must be stated clearly or they will be abandoned without a fight. The rhetorical trick of claiming to welcome all [[dogma|creed]]s while denouncing "such a [[dogma|doctrine]] as" priestcraft and superstition (024-150) is, for Chesterton, a betrayal of intellectual honesty — a way of having convictions without taking responsibility for them.
