# The Mad Official

[008-001]Generally, the moral substance of liberty is this: that man is not meant

[008-002]merely to receive good laws, good food or good conditions, like a

[008-003]tree in a garden, but is meant to take a certain princely pleasure in

[008-004]selecting and shaping like the gardener. Perhaps that is the meaning

[008-005]of the trade of Adam. And the best popular words for rendering the real

[008-006]idea of liberty are those which speak of man as a creator. We use the

[008-007]word “make” about most of the things in which freedom is essential, as

[008-008]a country walk or a friendship or a love affair. When a man “makes his

[008-009]way” through a wood he has really created, he has built a road, like the

[008-010]Romans. When a man “makes a friend,” he makes a man. And in the third

[008-011]case we talk of a man “making love,” as if he were (as, indeed, he is)

[008-012]creating new masses and colours of that flaming material an awful form

[008-013]of manufacture. In its primary spiritual sense, liberty is the god in

[008-014]man, or, if you like the word, the artist.

[008-015]In its secondary political sense liberty is the living influence of the

[008-016]citizen on the State in the direction of moulding or deflecting it. Men

[008-017]are the only creatures that evidently possess it. On the one hand, the

[008-018]eagle has no liberty; he only has loneliness. On the other hand, ants,

[008-019]bees, and beavers exhibit the highest miracle of the State influencing

[008-020]the citizen; but no perceptible trace of the citizen influencing the

[008-021]State. You may, if you like, call the ants a democracy as you may

[008-022]call the bees a despotism. But I fancy that the architectural ant who

[008-023]attempted to introduce an art nouveau style of ant-hill would have a

[008-024]career as curt and fruitless as the celebrated bee who wanted to swarm

[008-025]alone. The isolation of this idea in humanity is akin to its religious

[008-026]character; but it is not even in humanity by any means equally

[008-027]distributed. The idea that the State should not only be supported by

[008-028]its children, like the ant-hill, but should be constantly criticised and

[008-029]reconstructed by them, is an idea stronger in Christendom than any

[008-030]other part of the planet; stronger in Western than Eastern Europe. And

[008-031]touching the pure idea of the individual being free to speak and act

[008-032]within limits, the assertion of this idea, we may fairly say, has been

[008-033]the peculiar honour of our own country. For my part I greatly prefer the

[008-034]Jingoism of Rule Britannia to the Imperialism of The Recessional. I have

[008-035]no objection to Britannia ruling the waves. I draw the line when

[008-036]she begins to rule the dry land--and such damnably dry land

[008-037]too--as in Africa. And there was a real old English sincerity

[008-038]in the vulgar chorus that “Britons never shall be slaves.” We had no

[008-039]equality and hardly any justice; but freedom we were really fond of.

[008-040]And I think just now it is worth while to draw attention to the old

[008-041]optimistic prophecy that “Britons never shall be slaves.”

[008-042]The mere love of liberty has never been at a lower ebb in England than

[008-043]it has been for the last twenty years. Never before has it been so easy

[008-044]to slip small Bills through Parliament for the purpose of locking people

[008-045]up. Never was it so easy to silence awkward questions, or to protect

[008-046]high-placed officials. Two hundred years ago we turned out the Stuarts

[008-047]rather than endanger the Habeas Corpus Act. Two years ago we abolished

[008-048]the Habeas Corpus Act rather than turn out the Home Secretary. We passed

[008-049]a law (which is now in force) that an Englishman's punishment shall not

[008-050]depend upon judge and jury, but upon the governors and jailers who have

[008-051]got hold of him. But this is not the only case. The scorn of liberty

[008-052]is in the air. A newspaper is seized by the police in Trafalgar Square

[008-053]without a word of accusation or explanation. The Home Secretary says

[008-054]that in his opinion the police are very nice people, and there is an end

[008-055]of the matter. A Member of Parliament attempts to criticise a peerage.

[008-056]The Speaker says he must not criticise a peerage, and there the matter

[008-057]drops.

[008-058]Political liberty, let us repeat, consists in the power of

[008-059]criticising those flexible parts of the State which constantly require

[008-060]reconsideration, not the basis, but the machinery. In plainer words,

[008-061]it means the power of saying the sort of things that a decent but

[008-062]discontented citizen wants to say. He does not want to spit on the

[008-063]Bible, or to run about without clothes, or to read the worst page in

[008-064]Zola from the pulpit of St. Paul's. Therefore the forbidding of these

[008-065]things (whether just or not) is only tyranny in a secondary and special

[008-066]sense. It restrains the abnormal, not the normal man. But the normal

[008-067]man, the decent discontented citizen, does want to protest against

[008-068]unfair law courts. He does want to expose brutalities of the police.

[008-069]He does want to make game of a vulgar pawnbroker who is made a Peer. He

[008-070]does want publicly to warn people against unscrupulous capitalists and

[008-071]suspicious finance. If he is run in for doing this (as he will be)

[008-072]he does want to proclaim the character or known prejudices of the

[008-073]magistrate who tries him. If he is sent to prison (as he will be) he

[008-074]does want to have a clear and civilised sentence, telling him when he

[008-075]will come out. And these are literally and exactly the things that

[008-076]he now cannot get. That is the almost cloying humour of the present

[008-077]situation. I can say abnormal things in modern magazines. It is the

[008-078]normal things that I am not allowed to say. I can write in some solemn

[008-079]quarterly an elaborate article explaining that God is the devil; I can

[008-080]write in some cultured weekly an aesthetic fancy describing how I

[008-081]should like to eat boiled baby. The thing I must not write is rational

[008-082]criticism of the men and institutions of my country.

[008-083]The present condition of England is briefly this: That no Englishman can

[008-084]say in public a twentieth part of what he says in private. One cannot

[008-085]say, for instance, that--But I am afraid I must leave out

[008-086]that instance, because one cannot say it. I cannot prove my

[008-087]case--because it is so true.

[008-088]THE HYPOTHETICAL HOUSEHOLDER

[008-089]We have read of some celebrated philosopher who was so absent-minded

[008-090]that he paid a call at his own house. My own absent-mindedness is

[008-091]extreme, and my philosophy, of course, is the marvel of men and angels.

[008-092]But I never quite managed to be so absent-minded as that. Some yards at

[008-093]least from my own door, something vaguely familiar has always caught

[008-094]my eye; and thus the joke has been spoiled. Of course I have quite

[008-095]constantly walked into another man's house, thinking it was my own

[008-096]house; my visits became almost monotonous. But walking into my own house

[008-097]and thinking it was another man's house is a flight of poetic detachment

[008-098]still beyond me. Something of the sensations that such an absent-minded

[008-099]man must feel I really felt the other day; and very pleasant sensations

[008-100]they were. The best parts of every proper romance are the first chapter

[008-101]and the last chapter; and to knock at a strange door and find a nice

[008-102]wife would be to concentrate the beginning and end of all romance.

[008-103]Mine was a milder and slighter experience, but its thrill was of the

[008-104]same kind. For I strolled through a place I had imagined quite virgin

[008-105]and unvisited (as far as I was concerned), and I suddenly found I was

[008-106]treading in my own footprints, and the footprints were nearly twenty

[008-107]years old.

[008-108]It was one of those stretches of country which always suggests an almost

[008-109]unnatural decay; thickets and heaths that have grown out of what were

[008-110]once great gardens. Garden flowers still grow there as wild flowers,

[008-111]as it says in some good poetic couplet which I forget; and there is

[008-112]something singularly romantic and disastrous about seeing things that

[008-113]were so long a human property and care fighting for their own hand in

[008-114]the thicket. One almost expects to find a decayed dog-kennel; with the

[008-115]dog evolved into a wolf.

[008-116]This desolate garden-land had been even in my youth scrappily planned

[008-117]out for building. The half-built or empty houses had appeared quite

[008-118]threateningly on the edge of this heath even when I walked over it years

[008-119]ago and almost as a boy. I was astonished that the building had gone

[008-120]no farther; I suppose somebody went bankrupt and somebody else disliked

[008-121]building. But I remember, especially along one side of this tangle or

[008-122]coppice, that there had once been a row of half-built houses. The brick

[008-123]of which they were built was a sort of plain pink; everything else was a

[008-124]blinding white; the houses smoked with white dust and white sawdust;

[008-125]and on many of the windows were rubbed those round rough disks of white

[008-126]which always delighted me as a child. They looked like the white eyes of

[008-127]some blind giant.

[008-128]I could see the crude, parched pink-and-white villas still; though I had

[008-129]not thought at all of them for a quarter of my life; and had not thought

[008-130]much of them even when I saw them. Then I was an idle, but eager youth

[008-131]walking out from London; now I was a most reluctantly busy middle-aged

[008-132]person, coming in from the country. Youth, I think, seems farther off

[008-133]than childhood, for it made itself more of a secret. Like a prenatal

[008-134]picture, distant, tiny, and quite distinct, I saw this heath on which I

[008-135]stood; and I looked around for the string of bright, half-baked villas.

[008-136]They still stood there; but they were quite russet and weather-stained,

[008-137]as if they had stood for centuries.

[008-138]I remembered exactly what I had done on that day long ago. I had half

[008-139]slid on a miry descent; it was still there; a little lower I had knocked

[008-140]off the top of a thistle; the thistles had not been discouraged, but

[008-141]were still growing. I recalled it because I had wondered why one knocks

[008-142]off the tops of thistles; and then I had thought of Tarquin; and then I

[008-143]had recited most of Macaulay's VIRGINIA to myself, for I was young. And

[008-144]then I came to a tattered edge where the very tuft had whitened with

[008-145]the sawdust and brick-dust from the new row of houses; and two or three

[008-146]green stars of dock and thistle grew spasmodically about the blinding

[008-147]road.

[008-148]I remembered how I had walked up this new one-sided street all those

[008-149]years ago; and I remembered what I had thought. I thought that this

[008-150]red and white glaring terrace at noon was really more creepy and more

[008-151]lonesome than a glimmering churchyard at midnight. The churchyard could

[008-152]only be full of the ghosts of the dead; but these houses were full of

[008-153]the ghosts of the unborn. And a man can never find a home in the future

[008-154]as he can find it in the past. I was always fascinated by that mediæval

[008-155]notion of erecting a rudely carpentered stage in the street, and acting

[008-156]on it a miracle play of the Holy Family or the Last Judgment. And I

[008-157]thought to myself that each of these glaring, gaping, new jerry-built

[008-158]boxes was indeed a rickety stage erected for the acting of a real

[008-159]miracle play; that human family that is almost the holy one, and that

[008-160]human death that is near to the last judgment.

[008-161]For some foolish reason the last house but one in that imperfect row

[008-162]especially haunted me with its hollow grin and empty window-eyes.

[008-163]Something in the shape of this brick-and-mortar skeleton was attractive;

[008-164]and there being no workmen about, I strolled into it for curiosity and

[008-165]solitude. I gave, with all the sky-deep gravity of youth, a benediction

[008-166]upon the man who was going to live there. I even remember that for the

[008-167]convenience of meditation I called him James Harrogate.
