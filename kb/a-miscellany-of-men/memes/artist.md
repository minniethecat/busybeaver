# Artist

For Chesterton, the artist is not a professional identity but a human possibility. The artist is anyone who sees the world freshly — the child who builds a kingdom out of furniture, the craftsman who takes pride in his work, the ordinary person who suddenly perceives that a streetlamp is beautiful. The tragedy of modernity, in his account, is that it has turned the artist into a specialist when he ought to be the universal man.

## The Child as Original Artist

Chesterton locates the origin of the artistic impulse in childhood. "The instant impression on the artistic and even animal parts of" a person, he writes, is the raw material from which all art is made. (P01-128) The child who is struck by the colour of a leaf or the shape of a cloud is already an artist — not because he can paint the leaf or describe the cloud, but because he has been struck. Art begins in perception, not in production.

This is why Chesterton insists that the artistic faculty is "in all children and young artists; it springs up" naturally and is only later educated out of them. (009-070) The school that teaches a child to draw accurately often teaches him to see dully. The real artistic education would be the opposite: teaching the adult to recover the child's capacity for being astonished.

## The Artist and the Aesthete

Chesterton draws a sharp line between the artist and the aesthete. The artist makes; the aesthete merely appreciates. The artist is "attracted to an artistic attenuation" of experience — he wants to thin life down to a manageable aesthetic surface — whereas the true artist wants life in its full thickness. (009-133) The aesthete's refinement is actually a form of cowardice: he fears the mess of real existence and retreats into the clean space of the gallery.

This distinction becomes explicit when Chesterton contrasts "an artistic tradesman like the late Mr." William Morris with the purely contemplative connoisseur. (011-089) Morris was an artist precisely because he was a tradesman — a maker of things, a worker in materials, someone whose hands were dirty. The connoisseur who only looks at art is not an artist at all; he is a consumer, and his consumption is no more creative than eating dinner.

## The Human Touch

One of Chesterton's most suggestive observations about the artist concerns the quality he calls "some touch of the human artist about him." (013-124) He is describing a creator who leaves the marks of his making visible — the brushstroke, the chisel mark, the personal signature that says "a human being made this." The divine Creator, in Chesterton's theology, does the same: the world bears the marks of its Maker, and it is precisely the imperfect, uneven, personal quality of creation that testifies to its origin in love rather than mechanism.

This "human touch" is what the machine cannot replicate. The factory product is perfect in the way a corpse is perfect — smooth, uniform, dead. The artist's work is alive precisely because it is imperfect, because you can see the hand that made it trembling slightly as it worked.

## The Artist Who Flouts Convention

Chesterton's final word on the artist comes in a paradoxical observation about artistic freedom. It is "curiously assumed that the artist must be at his best if he flouts" all convention and strikes out entirely on his own. (015-097) But Chesterton questions this assumption. The artist who knows nothing of tradition has nothing to rebel against — and rebellion without an opponent is mere flailing.

The great artists, in his reading, are not those who ignored tradition but those who mastered it and then transformed it from within. Shakespeare did not invent the sonnet; he perfected it. The artist needs limits — the limits of form, of tradition, of a living community — because creativity is not the absence of constraint but the transcendence of it.
