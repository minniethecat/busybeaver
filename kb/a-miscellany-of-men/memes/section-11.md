# The Sun Worshipper

[010-001]THE REAL JOURNALIST

[010-002]Our age which has boasted of realism will fail chiefly through lack of

[010-003]reality. Never, I fancy, has there been so grave and startling a divorce

[010-004]between the real way a thing is done and the look of it when it is

[010-005]done. I take the nearest and most topical instance to hand a newspaper.

[010-006]Nothing looks more neat and regular than a newspaper, with its parallel

[010-007]columns, its mechanical printing, its detailed facts and figures, its

[010-008]responsible, polysyllabic leading articles. Nothing, as a matter

[010-009]of fact, goes every night through more agonies of adventure, more

[010-010]hairbreadth escapes, desperate expedients, crucial councils, random

[010-011]compromises, or barely averted catastrophes. Seen from the outside, it

[010-012]seems to come round as automatically as the clock and as silently as the

[010-013]dawn. Seen from the inside, it gives all its organisers a gasp of relief

[010-014]every morning to see that it has come out at all; that it has come out

[010-015]without the leading article upside down or the Pope congratulated on

[010-016]discovering the North Pole.

[010-017]I will give an instance (merely to illustrate my thesis of unreality)

[010-018]from the paper that I know best. Here is a simple story, a little

[010-019]episode in the life of a journalist, which may be amusing and

[010-020]instructive: the tale of how I made a great mistake in quotation. There

[010-021]are really two stories: the story as seen from the outside, by a

[010-022]man reading the paper; and the story seen from the inside, by the

[010-023]journalists shouting and telephoning and taking notes in shorthand

[010-024]through the night.

[010-025]This is the outside story; and it reads like a dreadful quarrel. The

[010-026]notorious G. K. Chesterton, a reactionary Torquemada whose one gloomy

[010-027]pleasure was in the defence of orthodoxy and the pursuit of heretics,

[010-028]long calculated and at last launched a denunciation of a brilliant

[010-029]leader of the New Theology which he hated with all the furnace of his

[010-030]fanatic soul. In this document Chesterton darkly, deliberately, and not

[010-031]having the fear of God before his eyes, asserted that Shakespeare wrote

[010-032]the line “that wreathes its old fantastic roots so high.” This he said

[010-033]because he had been kept in ignorance by Priests; or, perhaps, because

[010-034]he thought craftily that none of his dupes could discover a curious and

[010-035]forgotten rhyme called 'Elegy in a Country Churchyard'. Anyhow, that

[010-036]orthodox gentleman made a howling error; and received some twenty-five

[010-037]letters and post-cards from kind correspondents who pointed out the

[010-038]mistake.

[010-039]But the odd thing is that scarcely any of them could conceive that it

[010-040]was a mistake. The first wrote in the tone of one wearied of epigrams,

[010-041]and cried, “What is the joke NOW?” Another professed (and practised, for

[010-042]all I know, God help him) that he had read through all Shakespeare and

[010-043]failed to find the line. A third wrote in a sort of moral distress,

[010-044]asking, as in confidence, if Gray was really a plagiarist. They were a

[010-045]noble collection; but they all subtly assumed an element of leisure and

[010-046]exactitude in the recipient's profession and character which is far from

[010-047]the truth. Let us pass on to the next act of the external tragedy.

[010-048]In Monday's issue of the same paper appeared a letter from the same

[010-049]culprit. He ingenuously confessed that the line did not belong to

[010-050]Shakespeare, but to a poet whom he called Grey. Which was another

[010-051]cropper--or whopper. This strange and illiterate outbreak was

[010-052]printed by the editor with the justly scornful title, “Mr. Chesterton

[010-053]'Explains'?” Any man reading the paper at breakfast saw at once the

[010-054]meaning of the sarcastic quotation marks. They meant, of course, “Here

[010-055]is a man who doesn't know Gray from Shakespeare; he tries to patch it up

[010-056]and he can't even spell Gray. And that is what he calls an Explanation.”

[010-057]That is the perfectly natural inference of the reader from the letter,

[010-058]the mistake, and the headline--as seen from the outside. The

[010-059]falsehood was serious; the editorial rebuke was serious. The stern

[010-060]editor and the sombre, baffled contributor confront each other as the

[010-061]curtain falls.

[010-062]And now I will tell you exactly what really happened. It is honestly

[010-063]rather amusing; it is a story of what journals and journalists really

[010-064]are. A monstrously lazy man lives in South Bucks partly by writing a

[010-065]column in the Saturday Daily News. At the time he usually writes it

[010-066](which is always at the last moment) his house is unexpectedly invaded

[010-067]by infants of all shapes and sizes. His Secretary is called away; and

[010-068]he has to cope with the invading pigmies. Playing with children is a

[010-069]glorious thing; but the journalist in question has never understood

[010-070]why it was considered a soothing or idyllic one. It reminds him, not

[010-071]of watering little budding flowers, but of wrestling for hours with

[010-072]gigantic angels and devils. Moral problems of the most monstrous

[010-073]complexity besiege him incessantly. He has to decide before the awful

[010-074]eyes of innocence, whether, when a sister has knocked down a brother's

[010-075]bricks, in revenge for the brother having taken two sweets out of his

[010-076]turn, it is endurable that the brother should retaliate by scribbling on

[010-077]the sister's picture book, and whether such conduct does not justify the

[010-078]sister in blowing out the brother's unlawfully lighted match.

[010-079]Just as he is solving this problem upon principles of the highest

[010-080]morality, it occurs to him suddenly that he has not written his Saturday

[010-081]article; and that there is only about an hour to do it in. He wildly

[010-082]calls to somebody (probably the gardener) to telephone to somewhere for

[010-083]a messenger; he barricades himself in another room and tears his hair,

[010-084]wondering what on earth he shall write about. A drumming of fists on

[010-085]the door outside and a cheerful bellowing encourage and clarify his

[010-086]thoughts; and he is able to observe some newspapers and circulars in

[010-087]wrappers lying on the table. One is a dingy book catalogue; the second

[010-088]is a shiny pamphlet about petrol; the third is a paper called The

[010-089]Christian Commonwealth. He opens it anyhow, and sees in the middle of a

[010-090]page a sentence with which he honestly disagrees. It says that the sense

[010-091]of beauty in Nature is a new thing, hardly felt before Wordsworth. A

[010-092]stream of images and pictures pour through his head, like skies chasing

[010-093]each other or forests running by. “Not felt before Wordsworth!” he

[010-094]thinks. “Oh, but this won't do... bare ruined choirs where late the

[010-095]sweet birds sang... night's candles are burnt out... glowed with living

[010-096]sapphires... leaving their moon-loved maze... antique roots fantastic...

[010-097]antique roots wreathed high... what is it in As You Like It?”

[010-098]He sits down desperately; the messenger rings at the bell; the children

[010-099]drum on the door; the servants run up from time to time to say the

[010-100]messenger is getting bored; and the pencil staggers along, making

[010-101]the world a present of fifteen hundred unimportant words, and making

[010-102]Shakespeare a present of a portion of Gray's Elegy; putting “fantastic

[010-103]roots wreathed high” instead of “antique roots peep out.” Then the

[010-104]journalist sends off his copy and turns his attention to the enigma

[010-105]of whether a brother should commandeer a sister's necklace because the

[010-106]sister pinched him at Littlehampton. That is the first scene; that is

[010-107]how an article is really written.

[010-108]The scene now changes to the newspaper office. The writer of the article

[010-109]has discovered his mistake and wants to correct it by the next day:

[010-110]but the next day is Sunday. He cannot post a letter, so he rings up the

[010-111]paper and dictates a letter by telephone. He leaves the title to his

[010-112]friends at the other end; he knows that they can spell “Gray,” as no

[010-113]doubt they can: but the letter is put down by journalistic custom in a

[010-114]pencil scribble and the vowel may well be doubtful. The friend writes

[010-115]at the top of the letter “'G. K. C.' Explains,” putting the initials in

[010-116]quotation marks. The next man passing it for press is bored with these

[010-117]initials (I am with him there) and crosses them out, substituting with

[010-118]austere civility, “Mr. Chesterton Explains.” But and now he hears

[010-119]the iron laughter of the Fates, for the blind bolt is about to

[010-120]fall--but he neglects to cross out the second “quote” (as we call

[010-121]it) and it goes up to press with a “quote” between the last words.

[010-122]Another quotation mark at the end of “explains” was the work of one

[010-123]merry moment for the printers upstairs. So the inverted commas were

[010-124]lifted entirely off one word on to the other and a totally innocent

[010-125]title suddenly turned into a blasting sneer. But that would have

[010-126]mattered nothing so far, for there was nothing to sneer at. In the same

[010-127]dark hour, however, there was a printer who was (I suppose) so devoted

[010-128]to this Government that he could think of no Gray but Sir Edward Grey.

[010-129]He spelt it “Grey” by a mere misprint, and the whole tale was complete:

[010-130]first blunder, second blunder, and final condemnation.

[010-131]That is a little tale of journalism as it is; if you call it egotistic

[010-132]and ask what is the use of it I think I could tell you. You might

[010-133]remember it when next some ordinary young workman is going to be hanged

[010-134]by the neck on circumstantial evidence.

[010-135]THE SENTIMENTAL SCOT

[010-136]Of all the great nations of Christendom, the Scotch are by far the most

[010-137]romantic. I have just enough Scotch experience and just enough Scotch

[010-138]blood to know this in the only way in which a thing can really be known;

[010-139]that is, when the outer world and the inner world are at one. I know it

[010-140]is always said that the Scotch are practical, prosaic, and puritan; that

[010-141]they have an eye to business. I like that phrase “an eye” to business.

[010-142]Polyphemus had an eye for business; it was in the middle of his

[010-143]forehead. It served him admirably for the only two duties which are

[010-144]demanded in a modern financier and captain of industry: the two duties

[010-145]of counting sheep and of eating men. But when that one eye was put out

[010-146]he was done for. But the Scotch are not one-eyed practical men, though

[010-147]their best friends must admit that they are occasionally business-like.

[010-148]They are, quite fundamentally, romantic and sentimental, and this

[010-149]is proved by the very economic argument that is used to prove their

[010-150]harshness and hunger for the material. The mass of Scots have accepted

[010-151]the industrial civilisation, with its factory chimneys and its famine

[010-152]prices, with its steam and smoke and steel--and strikes. The mass

[010-153]of the Irish have not accepted it. The mass of the Irish have clung to

[010-154]agriculture with claws of iron; and have succeeded in keeping it. That

[010-155]is because the Irish, though far inferior to the Scotch in art and

[010-156]literature, are hugely superior to them in practical politics. You do

[010-157]need to be very romantic to accept the industrial civilisation. It does

[010-158]really require all the old Gaelic glamour to make men think that Glasgow

[010-159]is a grand place. Yet the miracle is achieved; and while I was in

[010-160]Glasgow I shared the illusion. I have never had the faintest illusion

[010-161]about Leeds or Birmingham. The industrial dream suited the Scots. Here

[010-162]was a really romantic vista, suited to a romantic people; a vision of

[010-163]higher and higher chimneys taking hold upon the heavens, of fiercer

[010-164]and fiercer fires in which adamant could evaporate like dew. Here were

[010-165]taller and taller engines that began already to shriek and gesticulate

[010-166]like giants. Here were thunderbolts of communication which already

[010-167]flashed to and fro like thoughts. It was unreasonable to expect the

[010-168]rapt, dreamy, romantic Scot to stand still in such a whirl of wizardry

[010-169]to ask whether he, the ordinary Scot, would be any the richer.

[010-170]He, the ordinary Scot, is very much the poorer. Glasgow is not a rich

[010-171]city. It is a particularly poor city ruled by a few particularly rich

[010-172]men. It is not, perhaps, quite so poor a city as Liverpool, London,

[010-173]Manchester, Birmingham, or Bolton. It is vastly poorer than Rome, Rouen,

[010-174]Munich, or Cologne. A certain civic vitality notable in Glasgow may,

[010-175]perhaps, be due to the fact that the high poetic patriotism of the Scots

[010-176]has there been reinforced by the cutting common sense and independence

[010-177]of the Irish. In any case, I think there can be no doubt of the main

[010-178]historical fact. The Scotch were tempted by the enormous but unequal

[010-179]opportunities of industrialism, because the Scotch are romantic. The

[010-180]Irish refused those enormous and unequal opportunities, because the

[010-181]Irish are clear-sighted. They would not need very clear sight by this

[010-182]time to see that in England and Scotland the temptation has been a

[010-183]betrayal. The industrial system has failed.
