# Instinct

For Chesterton, instinct is not the enemy of reason but its prerequisite. Across the Preface and the essays that follow, he returns to the idea that the deepest human certainties — about justice, about beauty, about the difference between good and evil — are matters of instinct before they are matters of argument. Civilisation, in his view, is built on instincts that it has largely forgotten how to name.

## The Primacy of Instinct

Chesterton opens his case for instinct in the Preface, where he declares that the "cold negation of the instincts is easy" but that to express or even "half express the instincts is very hard." (P01-111) (P01-112) The formulation is characteristically paradoxical: denying our instincts requires no effort, because instincts are easily ignored in favour of fashionable abstractions; but to articulate what our instincts actually tell us demands the hardest kind of intellectual honesty.

He sharpens this point by observing that our judgments about character and conduct are "primarily a matter of the instincts" — rooted in intuitions of sex, kinship, and shared humanity that no theory can supply. (P01-062) When we recoil from cruelty or recognise courage, we are not applying a rule we learned; we are responding from a depth that rules only attempt to codify.

## Instinct Against Theory

Chesterton's defence of instinct is, at bottom, a defence of the ordinary person against the expert. He notes that when Foulon said "Let them eat grass," the "good and Christian instincts of the poor" instantly recognised the remark as wicked — not because they had studied ethics but because their instincts had not been corrupted by power. (P01-057) Instinct here functions as a democratic faculty: it belongs to everyone, and it is most reliable in those who have not been trained out of it.

This theme recurs in his treatment of political economy. He suspects that the "forces that were not" instinctive — the abstractions of economists and planners — have displaced the instincts that once guided human beings in their dealings with one another. (006-090) The "economic instinct," he suggests, has been twisted by ideologies that treat human beings as abstractions rather than as persons. (006-090) When instinct is suppressed, the result is not superior rationality but a deranged version of reason cut off from its human source.

## Instinct and the Senses

Chesterton connects instinct to the bodily senses in a striking passage from the Preface, arguing that the body itself is a repository of wisdom. "Rapid changes and sudden revelations of sc" — the broken quotation gestures toward the way instinct operates through the senses, bypassing the slower machinery of deliberation. (P01-168) The instincts, he writes elsewhere, are like "danger signals" — flashes of perception that alert us to what formal reasoning might take pages to demonstrate. (P01-162)

This is not anti-intellectualism. Chesterton is not arguing that we should stop thinking and trust our gut. He is arguing that thinking without instinct is like a compass without a magnetised needle — it spins freely but points nowhere. The instincts give reason its orientation; they tell us what is worth reasoning about.
