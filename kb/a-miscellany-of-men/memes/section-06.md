# The Nameless Man

[005-001]This slow and awful self-hypnotism of error is a process that can occur

[005-002]not only with individuals, but also with whole societies. It is hard

[005-003]to pick out and prove; that is why it is hard to cure. But this mental

[005-004]degeneration may be brought to one test, which I truly believe to be a

[005-005]real test. A nation is not going mad when it does extravagant things,

[005-006]so long as it does them in an extravagant spirit. Crusaders not cutting

[005-007]their beards till they found Jerusalem, Jacobins calling each other

[005-008]Harmodius and Epaminondas when their names were Jacques and Jules, these

[005-009]are wild things, but they were done in wild spirits at a wild moment.

[005-010]But whenever we see things done wildly, but taken tamely, then the State

[005-011]is growing insane. For instance, I have a gun license. For all I know,

[005-012]this would logically allow me to fire off fifty-nine enormous field-guns

[005-013]day and night in my back garden. I should not be surprised at a man

[005-014]doing it; for it would be great fun. But I should be surprised at the

[005-015]neighbours putting up with it, and regarding it as an ordinary thing

[005-016]merely because it might happen to fulfill the letter of my license.

[005-017]Or, again, I have a dog license; and I may have the right (for all I

[005-018]know) to turn ten thousand wild dogs loose in Buckinghamshire. I should

[005-019]not be surprised if the law were like that; because in modern England

[005-020]there is practically no law to be surprised at. I should not be

[005-021]surprised even at the man who did it; for a certain kind of man, if he

[005-022]lived long under the English landlord system, might do anything. But I

[005-023]should be surprised at the people who consented to stand it. I should,

[005-024]in other words, think the world a little mad if the incident, were

[005-025]received in silence.

[005-026]Now things every bit as wild as this are being received in silence every

[005-027]day. All strokes slip on the smoothness of a polished wall. All blows

[005-028]fall soundless on the softness of a padded cell. For madness is a

[005-029]passive as well as an active state: it is a paralysis, a refusal of

[005-030]the nerves to respond to the normal stimuli, as well as an unnatural

[005-031]stimulation. There are commonwealths, plainly to be distinguished here

[005-032]and there in history, which pass from prosperity to squalor, or from

[005-033]glory to insignificance, or from freedom to slavery, not only in

[005-034]silence, but with serenity. The face still smiles while the limbs,

[005-035]literally and loathsomely, are dropping from the body. These are peoples

[005-036]that have lost the power of astonishment at their own actions. When they

[005-037]give birth to a fantastic fashion or a foolish law, they do not start

[005-038]or stare at the monster they have brought forth. They have grown used

[005-039]to their own unreason; chaos is their cosmos; and the whirlwind is the

[005-040]breath of their nostrils. These nations are really in danger of going

[005-041]off their heads en masse; of becoming one vast vision of imbecility,

[005-042]with toppling cities and crazy country-sides, all dotted with

[005-043]industrious lunatics. One of these countries is modern England.

[005-044]Now here is an actual instance, a small case of how our social

[005-045]conscience really works: tame in spirit, wild in result, blank in

[005-046]realisation; a thing without the light of mind in it. I take this

[005-047]paragraph from a daily paper:--“At Epping, yesterday, Thomas

[005-048]Woolbourne, a Lambourne labourer, and his wife were summoned for

[005-049]neglecting their five children. Dr. Alpin said he was invited by the

[005-050]inspector of the N.S.P.C.C. to visit defendants' cottage. Both the

[005-051]cottage and the children were dirty. The children looked exceedingly

[005-052]well in health, but the conditions would be serious in case of illness.

[005-053]Defendants were stated to be sober. The man was discharged. The woman,

[005-054]who said she was hampered by the cottage having no water supply and

[005-055]that she was ill, was sentenced to six weeks' imprisonment. The sentence

[005-056]caused surprise, and the woman was removed crying, 'Lord save me!'”

[005-057]I know no name for this but Chinese. It calls up the mental picture of

[005-058]some archaic and changeless Eastern Court, in which men with dried faces

[005-059]and stiff ceremonial costumes perform some atrocious cruelty to the

[005-060]accompaniment of formal proverbs and sentences of which the very meaning

[005-061]has been forgotten. In both cases the only thing in the whole farrago

[005-062]that can be called real is the wrong. If we apply the lightest touch of

[005-063]reason to the whole Epping prosecution it dissolves into nothing.

[005-064]I here challenge any person in his five wits to tell me what that woman

[005-065]was sent to prison for. Either it was for being poor, or it was for

[005-066]being ill. Nobody could suggest, nobody will suggest, nobody, as a

[005-067]matter of fact, did suggest, that she had committed any other crime.

[005-068]The doctor was called in by a Society for the Prevention of Cruelty

[005-069]to Children. Was this woman guilty of cruelty to children? Not in the

[005-070]least. Did the doctor say she was guilty of cruelty to children? Not in

[005-071]the least. Was these any evidence even remotely bearing on the sin of

[005-072]cruelty? Not a rap. The worse that the doctor could work himself up

[005-073]to saying was that though the children were “exceedingly” well, the

[005-074]conditions would be serious in case of illness. If the doctor will tell

[005-075]me any conditions that would be comic in case of illness, I shall attach

[005-076]more weight to his argument.

[005-077]Now this is the worst effect of modern worry. The mad doctor has

[005-078]gone mad. He is literally and practically mad; and still he is quite

[005-079]literally and practically a doctor. The only question is the old one,

[005-080]Quis docebit ipsum doctorem? Now cruelty to children is an utterly

[005-081]unnatural thing; instinctively accursed of earth and heaven. But neglect

[005-082]of children is a natural thing; like neglect of any other duty, it is

[005-083]a mere difference of degree that divides extending arms and legs in

[005-084]calisthenics and extending them on the rack. It is a mere difference of

[005-085]degree that separates any operation from any torture. The thumb-screw

[005-086]can easily be called Manicure. Being pulled about by wild horses can

[005-087]easily be called Massage. The modern problem is not so much what people

[005-088]will endure as what they will not endure. But I fear I interrupt.... The

[005-089]boiling oil is boiling; and the Tenth Mandarin is already reciting the

[005-090]“Seventeen Serious Principles and the Fifty-three Virtues of the Sacred

[005-091]Emperor.”

[005-092]THE ENCHANTED MAN

[005-093]When I arrived to see the performance of the Buckinghamshire Players,

[005-094]who acted Miss Gertrude Robins's POT LUCK at Naphill a short time ago,

[005-095]it is the distressing, if scarcely surprising, truth that I entered very

[005-096]late. This would have mattered little, I hope, to any one, but that late

[005-097]comers had to be forced into front seats. For a real popular English

[005-098]audience always insists on crowding in the back part of the hall; and

[005-099](as I have found in many an election) will endure the most unendurable

[005-100]taunts rather than come forward. The English are a modest people; that

[005-101]is why they are entirely ruled and run by the few of them that happen to

[005-102]be immodest. In theatrical affairs the fact is strangely notable; and in

[005-103]most playhouses we find the bored people in front and the eager people

[005-104]behind.

[005-105]As far as the performance went I was quite the reverse of a bored

[005-106]person; but I may have been a boring person, especially as I was thus

[005-107]required to sit in the seats of the scornful. It will be a happy day in

[005-108]the dramatic world when all ladies have to take off their hats and all

[005-109]critics have to take off their heads. The people behind will have a

[005-110]chance then. And as it happens, in this case, I had not so much taken

[005-111]off my head as lost it. I had lost it on the road; on that strange

[005-112]journey that was the cause of my coming in late. I have a troubled

[005-113]recollection of having seen a very good play and made a very bad speech;

[005-114]I have a cloudy recollection of talking to all sorts of nice people

[005-115]afterwards, but talking to them jerkily and with half a head, as a man

[005-116]talks when he has one eye on a clock.

[005-117]And the truth is that I had one eye on an ancient and timeless clock,

[005-118]hung uselessly in heaven; whose very name has passed into a figure

[005-119]for such bemused folly. In the true sense of an ancient phrase, I

[005-120]was moonstruck. A lunar landscape a scene of winter moonlight had

[005-121]inexplicably got in between me and all other scenes. If any one had

[005-122]asked me I could not have said what it was; I cannot say now. Nothing

[005-123]had occurred to me; except the breakdown of a hired motor on the ridge

[005-124]of a hill. It was not an adventure; it was a vision.

[005-125]I had started in wintry twilight from my own door; and hired a small

[005-126]car that found its way across the hills towards Naphill. But as

[005-127]night blackened and frost brightened and hardened it I found the way

[005-128]increasingly difficult; especially as the way was an incessant ascent.

[005-129]Whenever we topped a road like a staircase it was only to turn into a

[005-130]yet steeper road like a ladder.

[005-131]At last, when I began to fancy that I was spirally climbing the Tower

[005-132]of Babel in a dream, I was brought to fact by alarming noises, stoppage,

[005-133]and the driver saying that “it couldn't be done.” I got out of the car

[005-134]and suddenly forgot that I had ever been in it.

[005-135]From the edge of that abrupt steep I saw something indescribable, which

[005-136]I am now going to describe. When Mr. Joseph Chamberlain delivered his

[005-137]great patriotic speech on the inferiority of England to the Dutch parts

[005-138]of South Africa, he made use of the expression “the illimitable veldt.”

[005-139]The word “veldt” is Dutch, and the word “illimitable” is Double Dutch.

[005-140]But the meditative statesman probably meant that the new plains gave him

[005-141]a sense of largeness and dreariness which he had never found in England.

[005-142]Well, if he never found it in England it was because he never looked for

[005-143]it in England. In England there is an illimitable number of illimitable

[005-144]veldts. I saw six or seven separate eternities in cresting as many

[005-145]different hills. One cannot find anything more infinite than a finite

[005-146]horizon, free and lonely and innocent. The Dutch veldt may be a little

[005-147]more desolate than Birmingham. But I am sure it is not so desolate as

[005-148]that English hill was, almost within a cannon-shot of High Wycombe.

[005-149]I looked across a vast and voiceless valley straight at the moon, as if

[005-150]at a round mirror. It may have been the blue moon of the proverb; for on

[005-151]that freezing night the very moon seemed blue with cold. A deathly frost

[005-152]fastened every branch and blade to its place. The sinking and softening

[005-153]forests, powdered with a gray frost, fell away underneath me into an

[005-154]abyss which seemed unfathomable. One fancied the world was soundless

[005-155]only because it was bottomless: it seemed as if all songs and cries

[005-156]had been swallowed in some unresisting stillness under the roots of the

[005-157]hills. I could fancy that if I shouted there would be no echo; that if

[005-158]I hurled huge stones there would be no noise of reply. A dumb devil had

[005-159]bewitched the landscape: but that again does not express the best or

[005-160]worst of it. All those hoary and frosted forests expressed something so

[005-161]inhuman that it has no human name. A horror of unconsciousness lay on

[005-162]them; that is the nearest phrase I know. It was as if one were looking

[005-163]at the back of the world; and the world did not know it. I had taken the

[005-164]universe in the rear. I was behind the scenes. I was eavesdropping upon

[005-165]an unconscious creation.
