Oliver Cromwell (1599–1658), the Lord Protector of [[england|England]] following the Civil [[war|War]] and execution of Charles I, appears in Chesterton's "A Miscellany of Men" in a single concentrated passage within the essay "The Sentimental Scot" (section-19). His role in Chesterton's argument is precise and comparative: Cromwell is the man who had principles.

## Principles Against Machinery

Chesterton's reference to Cromwell comes in the context of a critique of [[cecil-rhodes|Cecil Rhodes]] and the colonial ideal. The essay argues that Rhodes's imperialism was not a genuine product of Western creative genius but a "fad, and like most fads an imitation" (019-067–019-068). The charge Chesterton levels is not that Rhodes committed crimes — it is that he committed them without purpose.

Cromwell and Hildebrand (Pope [[hildebrand|Gregory VII]]) are invoked as the counter-examples. "What was wrong with Rhodes was not that, like Cromwell or Hildebrand, he made huge mistakes, nor even that he committed great crimes. It was that he committed these crimes and errors in order to spread certain ideas. And when one asked for the ideas they could not be found" (019-069–019-072).

The contrast is drawn with aphoristic force: "Cromwell stood for [[john-calvin|Calvin]]ism, Hildebrand for Catholicism: but Rhodes had no principles whatever to give to the world. He had only a hasty but elaborate machinery for spreading the principles that he hadn't got" (019-072–019-075). The phrase is devastating — Rhodes as a distributor of empty packages, an imperialist without an empire of ideas.

## The Principle of Having Principles

Chesterton's use of Cromwell illuminates a recurring theme across the essays: that actions require intellectual justification. The colonial administrator who cannot answer the question "spread what?" is worse than the conqueror who can. Cromwell may have been wrong — Chesterton, a Catholic sympathiser, would hardly endorse [[john-calvin|Calvin]]ism — but he was wrong about something. He had a theological vision that gave shape to his political action. Rhodes, in Chesterton's telling, merely had "the dregs of a Darwinism which had already grown not only stagnant, but poisonous" (019-075–019-076).

The passage thus belongs to Chesterton's broader critique of late-[[queen-victoria|Victoria]]n materialism, in which evolutionary thinking is emptied of moral content and reduced to a mechanical rule: "that the fittest must survive, and that any one like himself must be the fittest" (019-077–019-078).
