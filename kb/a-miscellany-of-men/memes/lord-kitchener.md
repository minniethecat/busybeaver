# Lord Kitchener

Lord Kitchener (1850–1916) was a British Field Marshal and Secretary of State for [[war|War]] during the First World [[war|War]]. In [[section-21|The Man On Top]], G.K. Chesterton uses Kitchener to illustrate a distinction between public service and commercial enterprise.

## Service vs Commerce

Chesterton contrasts Kitchener's military role with [[william-morris|William Morris]]'s artistic shop. The [[british-army|British Army]], he argues, does not exist "in order to pay Lord Kitchener" — its object is the safety of the nation. (020-079–020-081)

[[william-morris|Morris]]'s shop, however artistic, existed to pay [[william-morris|William Morris]]. "If it did not pay the shopkeeper it failed as a shop; but Lord Kitchener does not fail if he is underpaid, but only if he is defeated." (020-082–020-084)

## The Aim of the Army

Chesterton elaborates that the distinction lies not in persons or atmosphere but in purpose. "The object of the Army is the safety of the nation from one particular class of perils." (020-084–020-085)

All citizens owe loyalty to the nation, and therefore soldiers owe loyalty to the Army. But nobody has any obligation to make a shop profitable — commerce answers to private interest, not public duty. (020-086–020-087)

## Public Duty and Private Enterprise

Chesterton's point is not about the merits of Kitchener or [[william-morris|Morris]] as individuals. He uses the comparison to argue that certain institutions exist for collective purposes that transcend market logic. A general is not a failed businessman when underpaid; a businessman is not a failed general when profitable. The two roles answer to fundamentally different standards.
