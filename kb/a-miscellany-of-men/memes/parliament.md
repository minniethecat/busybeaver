# Parliament

The British Parliament — the Mother of Parliaments — receives a sharp and skeptical treatment in G.K. Chesterton's "A Miscellany of Men" (1912). Writing at the height of parliamentary prestige, Chesterton saw not the august deliberative body of patriotic imagination but an institution that had, in crucial respects, ceased to perform its founding function.

## The Parliament That Does Not Parley

Chesterton's most pointed criticism appears in "The Thing." There he observes that "by this time the Parliament does not parley any more than the Speaker speaks." (003-069) The pun is deliberate and devastating: an institution named for speech had become an institution of silence.

The great debates that were supposed to shape the nation's course had been replaced by procedural machinery. The House of Lords, far from being a check on the Commons, represented the same aristocratic principle in more concentrated form. The system had become a way of "being sly" rather than a way of being strong. (003-068–003-069)

## Small Bills and Locked Doors

In "The Mad Official," Chesterton identifies a specific parliamentary pathology: legislation that expands state power while evading public scrutiny. "Never before has it been so easy to slip small Bills through Parliament for the purpose of locking people up." (008-044)

A Member of Parliament who "attempts to criticise a peerage" is silenced by the Speaker on procedural grounds. (008-055) The rules that ensured orderly debate now ensure certain subjects cannot be debated at all. Parliament, in Chesterton's account, has become an instrument of closure rather than opening.

## The Accidental Parliamentarian

In "The Free Man," Chesterton encounters a man who is simultaneously "a county magistrate, a Member of Parliament, and a director of several important companies." (012-097) The accumulation of roles is the point: the same class of men occupies every position of authority.

The candidate for Parliament is also "Colonel of the Yeomanry, old family doctor, Poor [[law|Law]] guardian, coroner." (012-109) The political class is not a separate profession but the gentry wearing different hats. Parliament, far from being the people's house, is the gentry's club.
