# Imperialism

Imperialism — the extension of British rule and influence over foreign peoples — was at the height of its prestige when G.K. Chesterton wrote "A Miscellany of Men" in 1912. In "The Architect Of Spears," Chesterton mounts one of his most concentrated critiques of the imperial project, arguing that fashionable Imperialism suffered not from excessive Western confidence but from a secret intellectual bankruptcy.

## The Empty Evangelism

Chesterton opens his critique with a paradox: "We have Imperialism in all our clubs at the very time when we have Orientalism in all our drawing-rooms." (019-064–019-065) The colonial ideal of [[cecil-rhodes|Cecil Rhodes]] did not arise from "any fresh creative idea of the Western genius." (019-066–019-067) It was a fad — and like most fads, an imitation.

The charge is devastating because it inverts the imperial self-image. Imperialists presented themselves as bearers of civilization; Chesterton argues they had no civilization to bear. Rhodes made "huge mistakes" and violated justice not because he was a fanatic with too many ideas, but because he "had no ideas to spread." (019-068–019-093) The violence of empire was not the excess of conviction but the substitute for it.

## Borrowed from the Brown and Black

Chesterton sharpens the argument: "Fashionable Imperialism not only has no ideas of its own to extend; but such ideas as it has are actually borrowed from the brown and black peoples to whom it seeks to extend them." (019-094–019-097)

While Crusading kings could at least claim to spread Western ideas in the East, the modern imperial aristocrat could only "spread Eastern ideas in the East." (019-098–019-100) The governing class urging "Occidental Imperialism has been deeply discoloured with Oriental mysticism and Cosmology." (019-100–019-101) Imperialism, in Chesterton's telling, is not Western arrogance but Western emptiness dressed in borrowed robes.

## The Empress and the Empire

Chesterton ties his critique of imperialism to his broader argument about Western kingship. He notes that "an Oriental" — Disraeli — gave [[queen-victoria|Queen Victoria]] the crown of Empress in addition to Queen. (019-131) The Eastern title was a poetic extravagance incompatible with Western kingship, which was "not a merely poetical title; but rather a religious one." (019-132–019-137)

The imperial project, for Chesterton, was Eastern in form and spirit — vast, abstract, impersonal — grafted onto a Western political tradition that had always been local, personal, and mutual. The empire did not extend English liberty; it corrupted it.
