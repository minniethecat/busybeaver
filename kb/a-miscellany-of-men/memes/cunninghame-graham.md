R. B. Cunninghame Graham (1852–1936) enters Chesterton's "[[section-16|The Separatist And Sacred Things]]" in a single, memorable sentence. Chesterton is surveying the various past eras that public figures of his day would choose to restore, and he pits Graham against [[rudyard-kipling|Rudyard Kipling]] in a debate over the Dark Ages — with a verdict that is as brief as it is confident.

## The Debate That Graham Would Win

The passage unfolds as a catalogue of political nostalgias. Dr. Clifford would restore Puritan idealism; Dr. Horton would restore the anti-Roman passion of the Reformation; [[hilaire-belloc|Belloc]] would restore the Middle Ages; [[lord-rosebery|Lord Rosebery]] would restore the eighteenth-century Erastian oligarchy (015-048–015-053). Each man is sincere, and each is attached to a different past.

Then Chesterton introduces his final pairing. "The Dark Ages would probably be disputed (from widely different motives) by Mr. [[rudyard-kipling|Rudyard Kipling]] and Mr. Cunninghame Graham. But Mr. Cunninghame Graham would win" (015-054–015-056). The sentence is remarkable for what it does not do: Chesterton provides no argument, no evidence, no reasoning. He simply declares the outcome.

The confidence of the verdict reflects Chesterton's assessment of both men. Kipling, the laureate of empire, would presumably argue against the Dark Ages as a period of barbarism and disorder. Graham, the Scottish socialist and adventurer who had ridden with gauchos in South America and sat in [[parliament|Parliament]] as a founding member of the Scottish Labour Party, would presumably argue for the Dark Ages as a period of organic community and authentic life before the corruptions of modern capitalism and imperialism.

## The Larger Point

Chesterton's immediate point, however, is not about either Kipling or Graham individually. It is that "the black case against Conservative (or Evolutionary) politics is that none of these sincere men can win" (015-057). Dr. Clifford cannot get back his Puritan commonwealth; Belloc cannot get back his medieval Christendom; [[lord-rosebery|Rosebery]] cannot get back his Whig oligarchy. The political system Chesterton describes is one in which every genuine restorationist is structurally impotent. Graham may win the hypothetical debate with Kipling, but in the actual political order, nobody wins — because the system is designed to absorb and neutralise all sincere conviction.
