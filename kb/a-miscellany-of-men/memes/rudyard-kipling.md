Rudyard Kipling (1865–1936) appears twice in Chesterton's essays, in strikingly different registers. In "[[section-07|The Gardener and the Guinea]]" he is invoked as an authority on the psychology of heat-induced delusion. In "[[section-16|The Separatist And Sacred Things]]" he enters as a contestant in Chesterton's survey of political nostalgia — the desire to restore some lost historical era — where Chesterton dispatches him with characteristic brevity.

## The Heat and the Hebrews

Chesterton's first reference to Kipling occurs in a passage about the Israelites in Egypt. He argues that the Exodus was not caused by theology or politics but by climate. "There can be no doubt that the real cause was the warm weather. Such a climate notoriously also produces delusions and horrible fancies, such as Mr. Kipling describes" (006-115–006-117). The reference is to Kipling's stories of tropical madness — the unhinging effect of heat on the European mind in India and other colonial settings.

Chesterton wields Kipling as a witness for the prosecution in his argument that the Israelites' belief in their own divine mission was a fever-dream. "It was while their brains were disordered by the heat that the Jews fancied that they were founding a nation, that they were led by a prophet, and, in short, that they were going to be of some importance in the affairs of the world" (006-118–006-120). The irony is Chestertonian through and through: he uses Kipling's authority to deflate the very kind of imperial self-importance with which Kipling is often associated.

## The Dark Ages Disputed

In "The Separatist And Sacred Things", Chesterton catalogues the various past eras that different public figures would prefer to restore. "Dr. Clifford would probably be glad to see again the great Puritan idealism... Dr. Horton probably really regrets the old passion that excommunicated Rome. In the same way Mr. Belloc would really prefer the Middle Ages; as [[lord-rosebery|Lord Rosebery]] would prefer the Erastian oligarchy of the eighteenth century" (015-048–015-053).

Kipling enters the catalogue in a pairing Chesterton clearly enjoys. "The Dark Ages would probably be disputed (from widely different motives) by Mr. Rudyard Kipling and Mr. Cunninghame Graham. But Mr. Cunninghame Graham would win" (015-054–015-056). The verdict is delivered without argument — Chesterton simply asserts that Graham would carry the debate. The point, however, is not who would win but that "none of these sincere men can win" (015-057). The Conservative or Evolutionary politics that Chesterton attacks is a system in which every genuine restorationist is impotent: the past cannot be retrieved by political means, however eloquently one argues for it.
