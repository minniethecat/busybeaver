Sir Philip Sidney (1554–1586) appears in Chesterton's "[[section-18|The New Theologian]]" as the voice of an older, more generous way of seeing the world. Reflecting on the rain that falls on every shrub and every weed alike, Chesterton writes: "I would be ashamed to grumble at it. As Sir Philip Sidney said, their need is greater than mine — especially for water" (018-088–018-089). The allusion is to the story of the wounded Sidney at Zutphen, passing his water-bottle to a dying soldier with those exact words.

## The Flower of Knighthood

Sidney was the Renaissance ideal made flesh. Nephew to Robert Dudley, the Earl of Leicester, he moved at the centre of Elizabethan power but was drawn always to the life of letters and action. His *Defence of Poesy* (c. 1579) is the first major work of English literary criticism, arguing that the poet "comes to you with words set in delightful proportion" and that poetry surpasses both history and philosophy in its power to move men to virtue.

His *Arcadia*, a prose romance interspersed with verse, was written for the amusement of his sister, the Countess of Pembroke. It became one of the most widely read books of the following century — [[king-charles-i]] reportedly carried a copy to the scaffold. His sonnet sequence *Astrophil and Stella* launched the English sonnet vogue that would culminate in [[shakespeare]].

Sidney's death at the Battle of Zutphen in 1586, aged thirty-one, sealed his legend. Wounded in the thigh, he gave his water to a common soldier. The story was told throughout Europe, and it made Sidney not merely a poet and a soldier but an exemplar — the man who embodied the chivalric code he had written about.

## "Their Need Is Greater Than Mine"

Chesterton's use of the Sidney story in "The New Theologian" is both deft and characteristic. The essay is a critique of modern theology's tendency to abstract God into a distant principle. Chesterton's counter-move is to point to the particular, the local, the creaturely — the rain that falls on weeds as well as roses.

Sidney's words at Zutphen serve Chesterton's argument perfectly. The dying courtier does not calculate whether the soldier is worth saving. He does not theorise about the distribution of resources. He gives the water because the other man needs it more. That is the aristocratic principle Chesterton praises everywhere: not the pride of rank but the generosity of the noble, who acts because action is called for, not because it is efficient.

## Sidney's England and Chesterton's

The Elizabethan age held a particular fascination for Chesterton. It was, for him, the last moment before [[england|England]] broke with the medieval synthesis — Reformation but not yet Puritan, Renaissance but still chivalric. Sidney was its perfect representative: Protestant but not iconoclastic, learned but not pedantic, a man of action who wrote poetry and a poet who died a soldier's death.

Chesterton's own distributist politics, with its emphasis on the smallholder and the local community, owed something to this nostalgic vision of Elizabethan [[england|England]] as a land of rooted liberties. Sidney, the country gentleman who wrote *Arcadia* at Wilton, embodied a kind of Englishness that industrialism and imperialism had, in Chesterton's view, all but destroyed.
