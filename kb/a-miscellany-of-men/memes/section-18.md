# The New Theologian

[017-001]Now if you protest against this, educated people will instantly answer

[017-002]you, “Oh, it was done to prevent the vulgar trippers who chip stones and

[017-003]carve names and spoil the look of Stonehenge.” It does not seem to

[017-004]occur to them that barbed wire and a policeman rather spoil the look of

[017-005]Stonehenge. The scratching of a name, particularly when performed

[017-006]with blunt penknife or pencil by a person of imperfect School Board

[017-007]education, can be trusted in a little while to be indistinguishable from

[017-008]the grayest hieroglyphic by the grandest Druid of old. But nobody could

[017-009]get a modern policeman into the same picture with a Druid. This really

[017-010]vital piece of vandalism was done by the educated, not the uneducated;

[017-011]it was done by the influence of the artists or antiquaries who wanted

[017-012]to preserve the antique beauty of Stonehenge. It seems to me curious to

[017-013]preserve your lady's beauty from freckles by blacking her face all over;

[017-014]or to protect the pure whiteness of your wedding garment by dyeing it

[017-015]green.

[017-016]And if you ask, “But what else could any one have done, what could the

[017-017]most artistic age have done to save the monument?” I reply, “There are

[017-018]hundreds of things that Greeks or Mediævals might have done; and I have

[017-019]no notion what they would have chosen; but I say that by an instinct in

[017-020]their whole society they would have done something that was decent and

[017-021]serious and suitable to the place. Perhaps some family of knights or

[017-022]warriors would have the hereditary duty of guarding such a place. If so

[017-023]their armour would be appropriate; their tents would be appropriate;

[017-024]not deliberately--they would grow like that. Perhaps some religious

[017-025]order such as normally employ nocturnal watches and the relieving of

[017-026]guard would protect such a place. Perhaps it would be protected by all

[017-027]sorts of rituals, consecrations, or curses, which would seem to you

[017-028]mere raving superstition and silliness. But they do not seem to me one

[017-029]twentieth part so silly, from a purely rationalist point of view, as

[017-030]calmly making a spot hideous in order to keep it beautiful.”

[017-031]The thing that is really vulgar, the thing that is really vile, is to

[017-032]live in a good place Without living by its life. Any one who settles

[017-033]down in a place without becoming part of it is (barring peculiar

[017-034]personal cases, of course) a tripper or wandering cad. For instance,

[017-035]the Jew is a genuine peculiar case. The Wandering Jew is not a wandering

[017-036]cad. He is a highly civilised man in a highly difficult position; the

[017-037]world being divided, and his own nation being divided, about whether he

[017-038]can do anything else except wander.

[017-039]The best example of the cultured, but common, tripper is the educated

[017-040]Englishman on the Continent. We can no longer explain the quarrel by

[017-041]calling Englishmen rude and foreigners polite. Hundreds of Englishmen

[017-042]are extremely polite, and thousands of foreigners are extremely rude.

[017-043]The truth of the matter is that foreigners do not resent the rude

[017-044]Englishman. What they do resent, what they do most justly resent, is

[017-045]the polite Englishman. He visits Italy for Botticellis or Flanders

[017-046]for Rembrandts, and he treats the great nations that made these things

[017-047]courteously--as he would treat the custodians of any museum. It

[017-048]does not seem to strike him that the Italian is not the custodian of the

[017-049]pictures, but the creator of them. He can afford to look down on such

[017-050]nations--when he can paint such pictures.

[017-051]That is, in matters of art and travel, the psychology of the cad.

[017-052]If, living in Italy, you admire Italian art while distrusting Italian

[017-053]character, you are a tourist, or cad. If, living in Italy, you admire

[017-054]Italian art while despising Italian religion, you are a tourist, or cad.

[017-055]It does not matter how many years you have lived there. Tourists will

[017-056]often live a long time in hotels without discovering the nationality

[017-057]of the waiters. Englishmen will often live a long time in Italy without

[017-058]discovering the nationality of the Italians. But the test is simple. If

[017-059]you admire what Italians did without admiring Italians--you are a

[017-060]cheap tripper.

[017-061]The same, of course, applies much nearer home. I have remarked elsewhere

[017-062]that country shopkeepers are justly offended by London people, who,

[017-063]coming among them, continue to order all their goods from London. It is

[017-064]caddish to wink and squint at the colour of a man's wine, like a wine

[017-065]taster; and then refuse to drink it. It is equally caddish to wink and

[017-066]squint at the colour of a man's orchard, like a landscape painter; and

[017-067]then refuse to buy the apples. It is always an insult to admire a thing

[017-068]and not use it. But the main point is that one has no right to see

[017-069]Stonehenge without Salisbury Plain and Salisbury. One has no right to

[017-070]respect the dead Italians without respecting the live ones. One has no

[017-071]right to visit a Christian society like a diver visiting the deep-sea

[017-072]fishes--fed along a lengthy tube by another atmosphere, and seeing

[017-073]the sights without breathing the air. It is very real bad manners.

[017-074]THE NEW THEOLOGIAN

[017-075]It is an old story that names do not fit things; it is an old story

[017-076]that the oldest forest is called the New Forest, and that Irish stew is

[017-077]almost peculiar to England. But these are traditional titles that tend,

[017-078]of their nature, to stiffen; it is the tragedy of to-day that even

[017-079]phrases invented for to-day do not fit it. The forest has remained new

[017-080]while it is nearly a thousand years old; but our fashions have grown old

[017-081]while they were still new.

[017-082]The extreme example of this is that when modern wrongs are attacked,

[017-083]they are almost always attacked wrongly. People seem to have a positive

[017-084]inspiration for finding the inappropriate phrase to apply to an

[017-085]offender; they are always accusing a man of theft when he has been

[017-086]convicted of murder. They must accuse Sir Edward Carson of outrageous

[017-087]rebellion, when his offence has really been a sleek submission to

[017-088]the powers that be. They must describe Mr. Lloyd George as using his

[017-089]eloquence to rouse the mob, whereas he has really shown considerable

[017-090]cleverness in damping it down. It was probably under the same impulse

[017-091]towards a mysterious misfit of names that people denounced Dr. Inge as

[017-092]“the Gloomy Dean.”

[017-093]Now there is nothing whatever wrong about being a Dean; nor is there

[017-094]anything wrong about being gloomy. The only question is what dark but

[017-095]sincere motives have made you gloomy. What dark but sincere motives

[017-096]have made you a Dean. Now the address of Dr. Inge which gained him

[017-097]this erroneous title was mostly concerned with a defence of the modern

[017-098]capitalists against the modern strikers, from whose protest he appeared

[017-099]to anticipate appalling results. Now if we look at the facts about that

[017-100]gentleman's depression and also about his Deanery, we shall find a very

[017-101]curious state of things.

[017-102]When Dr. Inge was called “the Gloomy Dean” a great injustice was done

[017-103]him. He had appeared as the champion of our capitalist community against

[017-104]the forces of revolt; and any one who does that exceeds in optimism

[017-105]rather than pessimism. A man who really thinks that strikers have

[017-106]suffered no wrong, or that employers have done no wrong--such a man

[017-107]is not a Gloomy Dean, but a quite wildly and dangerously happy Dean. A

[017-108]man who can feel satisfied with modern industrialism must be a man with

[017-109]a mysterious fountain of high spirits. And the actual occasion is not

[017-110]less curious; because, as far as I can make out, his title to gloom

[017-111]reposes on his having said that our worker's demand high wages, while

[017-112]the placid people of the Far East will quite cheerfully work for less.

[017-113]This is true enough, of course, and there does not seem to be much

[017-114]difficulty about the matter. Men of the Far East will submit to very low

[017-115]wages for the same reason that they will submit to “the punishment known

[017-116]as Li, or Slicing”; for the same reason that they will praise polygamy

[017-117]and suicide; for the same reason that they subject the wife utterly to

[017-118]the husband or his parents; for the same reason that they serve their

[017-119]temples with prostitutes for priests; for the same reason that they

[017-120]sometimes seem to make no distinction between sexual passion and sexual

[017-121]perversion. They do it, that is, because they are Heathens; men with

[017-122]traditions different from ours about the limits of endurance and the

[017-123]gestures of self-respect. They may be very much better than we are in

[017-124]hundreds of other ways; and I can quite understand a man (though

[017-125]hardly a Dean) really preferring their historic virtues to those of

[017-126]Christendom. A man may perhaps feel more comfortable among his Asiatic

[017-127]coolies than among his European comrades: and as we are to allow the

[017-128]Broadest Thought in the Church, Dr. Inge has as much right to his heresy

[017-129]as anybody else. It is true that, as Dr. Inge says, there are numberless

[017-130]Orientals who will do a great deal of work for very little money; and

[017-131]it is most undoubtedly true that there are several high-placed and

[017-132]prosperous Europeans who like to get work done and pay as little as

[017-133]possible for it.

[017-134]But I cannot make out why, with his enthusiasm for heathen habits and

[017-135]traditions, the Dean should wish to spread in the East the ideas which

[017-136]he has found so dreadfully unsettling in the West. If some thousands of

[017-137]years of paganism have produced the patience and industry that Dean Inge

[017-138]admires, and if some thousand years of Christianity have produced

[017-139]the sentimentality and sensationalism which he regrets, the obvious

[017-140]deduction is that Dean Inge would be much happier if he were a heathen

[017-141]Chinese. Instead of supporting Christian missions to Korea or Japan, he

[017-142]ought to be at the head of a great mission in London for converting the

[017-143]English to Taoism or Buddhism. There his passion for the moral beauties

[017-144]of paganism would have free and natural play; his style would improve;

[017-145]his mind would begin slowly to clear; and he would be free from all

[017-146]sorts of little irritating scrupulosities which must hamper even the

[017-147]most Conservative Christian in his full praise of sweating and the sack.

[017-148]In Christendom he will never find rest. The perpetual public criticism

[017-149]and public change which is the note of all our history springs from a

[017-150]certain spirit far too deep to be defined. It is deeper than democracy;

[017-151]nay, it may often appear to be non-democratic; for it may often be the

[017-152]special defence of a minority or an individual. It will often leave the

[017-153]ninety-and-nine in the wilderness and go after that which is lost. It

[017-154]will often risk the State itself to right a single wrong; and do justice

[017-155]though the heavens fall. Its highest expression is not even in the

[017-156]formula of the great gentlemen of the French Revolution who said that

[017-157]all men were free and equal. Its highest expression is rather in the

[017-158]formula of the peasant who said that a man's a man for a' that. If there

[017-159]were but one slave in England, and he did all the work while the rest

[017-160]of us made merry, this spirit that is in us would still cry aloud to

[017-161]God night and day. Whether or no this spirit was produced by, it clearly

[017-162]works with, a creed which postulates a humanised God and a vividly

[017-163]personal immortality. Men must not be busy merely like a swarm, or even

[017-164]happy merely like a herd; for it is not a question of men, but of a

[017-165]man. A man's meals may be poor, but they must not be bestial; there must

[017-166]always be that about the meal which permits of its comparison to

[017-167]the sacrament. A man's bed may be hard, but it must not be abject or

[017-168]unclean: there must always be about the bed something of the decency of

[017-169]the death-bed.
