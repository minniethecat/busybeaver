# William Wordsworth

William Wordsworth (1770–1850) was a major English Romantic poet and Poet Laureate from 1843 until his death. He is best known for "Lyrical Ballads" (1798, with [[samuel-taylor-coleridge|Samuel Taylor Coleridge]]) and "The Prelude." In [[section-02|The Suffragist]], G.K. Chesterton reflects on the peculiar quality of Wordsworth's poetry.

## Crystalline Virtue

Chesterton describes the atmosphere of Wordsworth's rural poems as "at once solid and airy" — full of genuine freshness and wonder, yet inescapably commonplace. (001-004–001-006)

He notes the apparent paradox: Wordsworth's men were of the rocks and fells, not of the fenlands. Yet Chesterton suggests it may be "the clearness of still water and the mirrored skies of meres and pools that produces this crystalline virtue." (001-008–001-009)

## Lake Poet or Mountain Poet?

Chesterton proposes that Wordsworth is rightly called a Lake Poet rather than a mountain poet. "Perhaps it is the water that does it," he muses. "Certainly the whole of that town was like a cup of water given at morning." (001-011–001-012)

This observation connects Wordsworth's poetic quality — clarity, reflection, stillness — to the physical landscape of the Lake District, suggesting that environment shapes artistic temperament in ways deeper than subject matter alone.

## The Romantic Context

Wordsworth appears in Chesterton's essay as part of a broader reflection on atmosphere and place. Chesterton compares the air of Stilton to "some of Wordsworth's rural poems; which are full of genuine freshness and wonder, and yet are in some incurable way commonplace." (001-005–001-006)

This ambiguous praise — genuine wonder paired with the commonplace — reflects Chesterton's complex relationship with Romanticism: appreciative of its attention to nature, skeptical of its tendency toward sentimentality.
