# Revolution

Revolution, for Chesterton, is never a single thing. Across "A Miscellany of Men," he treats it as a double phenomenon: there is the revolution that liberates thought, and there is the revolution that merely inverts power. The distinction is central to his political thinking, and it appears with remarkable consistency across essays that otherwise range from astronomy to the [[french-revolution|French Revolution]] to the language of modern radicalism.

## The Two Revolutions

Chesterton's clearest statement on revolution comes in "The Mystagogue," where he distinguishes between two kinds of revolutionary change that are racing toward collision. "There are two revolutions," he writes, and the question is "which will happen first — the revolution in which" good things shall perish, "or that other revolution, in which good things" shall be restored. (007-129) (007-130) (007-131)

The white revolution, in Chesterton's scheme, is the mastering of matter by spirit — the astronomical revolution of Copernicus, which he calls "distinctly revolutionary" precisely because it reordered the human imagination rather than the human hierarchy. (007-161) (007-162) The black revolution, by contrast, is the mastering of men by other men, using the tools and language of liberation as camouflage. These "two revolutions, white and black, are racing" through modern history, and Chesterton sees the contest between them as the central drama of his age. (007-143) The distinction is not between radical and conservative but between two kinds of radicalism: one that enlarges human freedom and one that contracts it.

## The Gift of Revolution

In "The Sick Man," Chesterton identifies what he considers the great gift of revolution — and here he has the [[french-revolution|French Revolution]] specifically in mind. "Now the great gift of a revolution (as in [[france|France]]) is that it makes everybody think" freshly about things that custom had rendered invisible. (015-018) The revolutionary moment breaks the spell of the ordinary, forcing people to reconsider arrangements they had mistaken for necessities.

This is not a defence of revolutionary violence. Chesterton elsewhere calls himself "a bit of a revolutionist" but adds that he "cannot quite go" all the way with those who treat revolution as an end in itself. (012-077) The value of revolution, in his view, is instrumental: it clears the ground for thought. The danger is that the clearing becomes the whole project, and the revolution consumes its own children — or, worse, installs a new orthodoxy more rigid than the old one. Chesterton notes that revolutionary language is inherently unstable: "All revolutionary language is" prone to exaggeration, and the revolutionary "would say (with perfect truth)" things that, taken literally, become monstrous. (020-116) (020-118)

## Revolution Against Revolution

Chesterton's most paradoxical move is his insistence that every genuine revolution must itself be revolutionised against. In "The Mystagogue," he describes a figure who "made a revolution against a revolution" — that is, who used the spirit of revolutionary questioning to question the revolution itself. (014-145) This is not reaction but radicalism turned inward, the refusal to let even one's own convictions harden into dogma.

The real revolutionists, Chesterton observes, are not those who merely repeat the slogans of the previous generation's upheaval but those "who held high language in" the service of permanent principles. (022-034) There is a distinction here between the professional revolutionary, who administers dissent, and the genuine insurgent, who risks everything — including the approval of other insurgents — for what he believes. Chesterton's sympathies lie with the latter, and his critique of the former anticipates a great deal of twentieth-century disillusionment with revolutionary politics that had become bureaucratic.
