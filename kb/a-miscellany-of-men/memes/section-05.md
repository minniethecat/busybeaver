# The Man Who Thinks Backwards

[004-001]I told the gardener that as he had found the thing he ought to keep it,

[004-002]but that if he cared to sell it to me it could be valued properly, and

[004-003]then sold. He said at first, with characteristic independence, that he

[004-004]would like to keep it. He said it would make a brooch for his wife. But

[004-005]a little later he brought it back to me without explanation. I could not

[004-006]get a ray of light on the reason of his refusal; but he looked lowering

[004-007]and unhappy. Had he some mystical instinct that it is just such

[004-008]accidental and irrational wealth that is the doom of all peasantries?

[004-009]Perhaps he dimly felt that the boy's pirate tales are true; and that

[004-010]buried treasure is a thing for robbers and not for producers. Perhaps

[004-011]he thought there was a curse on such capital: on the coal of the

[004-012]coal-owners, on the gold of the gold-seekers. Perhaps there is.

[004-013]THE VOTER AND THE TWO VOICES

[004-014]The real evil of our Party System is commonly stated wrong. It was

[004-015]stated wrong by Lord Rosebery, when he said that it prevented the best

[004-016]men from devoting themselves to politics, and that it encouraged a

[004-017]fanatical conflict. I doubt whether the best men ever would devote

[004-018]themselves to politics. The best men devote themselves to pigs and

[004-019]babies and things like that. And as for the fanatical conflict in

[004-020]party politics, I wish there was more of it. The real danger of the two

[004-021]parties with their two policies is that they unduly limit the outlook of

[004-022]the ordinary citizen. They make him barren instead of creative, because

[004-023]he is never allowed to do anything except prefer one existing policy to

[004-024]another. We have not got real Democracy when the decision depends upon

[004-025]the people. We shall have real Democracy when the problem depends upon

[004-026]the people. The ordinary man will decide not only how he will vote, but

[004-027]what he is going to vote about.

[004-028]It is this which involves some weakness in many current aspirations

[004-029]towards the extension of the suffrage; I mean that, apart from all

[004-030]questions of abstract justice, it is not the smallness or largeness of

[004-031]the suffrage that is at present the difficulty of Democracy. It is not

[004-032]the quantity of voters, but the quality of the thing they are voting

[004-033]about. A certain alternative is put before them by the powerful houses

[004-034]and the highest political class. Two roads are opened to them; but they

[004-035]must go down one or the other. They cannot have what they choose, but

[004-036]only which they choose. To follow the process in practice we may put it

[004-037]thus. The Suffragettes--if one may judge by their frequent ringing

[004-038]of his bell--want to do something to Mr. Asquith. I have no notion

[004-039]what it is. Let us say (for the sake of argument) that they want to

[004-040]paint him green. We will suppose that it is entirely for that simple

[004-041]purpose that they are always seeking to have private interviews with

[004-042]him; it seems as profitable as any other end that I can imagine to such

[004-043]an interview. Now, it is possible that the Government of the day might

[004-044]go in for a positive policy of painting Mr. Asquith green; might give

[004-045]that reform a prominent place in their programme. Then the party in

[004-046]opposition would adopt another policy, not a policy of leaving Mr.

[004-047]Asquith alone (which would be considered dangerously revolutionary), but

[004-048]some alternative course of action, as, for instance, painting him red.

[004-049]Then both sides would fling themselves on the people, they would both

[004-050]cry that the appeal was now to the Caesar of Democracy. A dark and

[004-051]dramatic air of conflict and real crisis would arise on both sides;

[004-052]arrows of satire would fly and swords of eloquence flame. The Greens

[004-053]would say that Socialists and free lovers might well want to paint Mr.

[004-054]Asquith red; they wanted to paint the whole town red. Socialists would

[004-055]indignantly reply that Socialism was the reverse of disorder, and that

[004-056]they only wanted to paint Mr. Asquith red so that he might resemble

[004-057]the red pillar-boxes which typified State control. The Greens would

[004-058]passionately deny the charge so often brought against them by the Reds;

[004-059]they would deny that they wished Mr. Asquith green in order that he

[004-060]might be invisible on the green benches of the Commons, as certain

[004-061]terrified animals take the colour of their environment.

[004-062]There would be fights in the street perhaps, and abundance of ribbons,

[004-063]flags, and badges, of the two colours. One crowd would sing, “Keep the

[004-064]Red Flag Flying,” and the other, “The Wearing of the Green.” But when

[004-065]the last effort had been made and the last moment come, when two

[004-066]crowds were waiting in the dark outside the public building to hear the

[004-067]declaration of the poll, then both sides alike would say that it was now

[004-068]for democracy to do exactly what it chose. England herself, lifting her

[004-069]head in awful loneliness and liberty, must speak and pronounce judgment.

[004-070]Yet this might not be exactly true. England herself, lifting her head in

[004-071]awful loneliness and liberty, might really wish Mr. Asquith to be pale

[004-072]blue. The democracy of England in the abstract, if it had been allowed

[004-073]to make up a policy for itself, might have desired him to be black

[004-074]with pink spots. It might even have liked him as he is now. But a huge

[004-075]apparatus of wealth, power, and printed matter has made it practically

[004-076]impossible for them to bring home these other proposals, even if they

[004-077]would really prefer them. No candidates will stand in the spotted

[004-078]interest; for candidates commonly have to produce money either from

[004-079]their own pockets or the party's; and in such circles spots are not

[004-080]worn. No man in the social position of a Cabinet Minister, perhaps,

[004-081]will commit himself to the pale-blue theory of Mr. Asquith; therefore it

[004-082]cannot be a Government measure, therefore it cannot pass.

[004-083]Nearly all the great newspapers, both pompous and frivolous, will

[004-084]declare dogmatically day after day, until every one half believes

[004-085]it, that red and green are the only two colours in the paint-box. THE

[004-086]OBSERVER will say: “No one who knows the solid framework of politics or

[004-087]the emphatic first principles of an Imperial people can suppose for

[004-088]a moment that there is any possible compromise to be made in such a

[004-089]matter; we must either fulfil our manifest racial destiny and crown the

[004-090]edifice of ages with the august figure of a Green Premier, or we must

[004-091]abandon our heritage, break our promise to the Empire, fling ourselves

[004-092]into final anarchy, and allow the flaming and demoniac image of a Red

[004-093]Premier to hover over our dissolution and our doom.” The DAILY MAIL

[004-094]would say: “There is no halfway house in this matter; it must be green

[004-095]or red. We wish to see every honest Englishman one colour or the other.”

[004-096]And then some funny man in the popular Press would star the sentence

[004-097]with a pun, and say that the DAILY MAIL liked its readers to be green

[004-098]and its paper to be read. But no one would even dare to whisper that

[004-099]there is such a thing as yellow.

[004-100]For the purposes of pure logic it is clearer to argue with silly

[004-101]examples than with sensible ones: because silly examples are simple. But

[004-102]I could give many grave and concrete cases of the kind of thing to which

[004-103]I refer. In the later part of the Boer War both parties perpetually

[004-104]insisted in every speech and pamphlet that annexation was inevitable and

[004-105]that it was only a question whether Liberals or Tories should do it. It

[004-106]was not inevitable in the least; it would have been perfectly easy to

[004-107]make peace with the Boers as Christian nations commonly make peace with

[004-108]their conquered enemies. Personally I think that it would have been

[004-109]better for us in the most selfish sense, better for our pocket and

[004-110]prestige, if we had never effected the annexation at all; but that is a

[004-111]matter of opinion. What is plain is that it was not inevitable; it was

[004-112]not, as was said, the only possible course; there were plenty of other

[004-113]courses; there were plenty of other colours in the box. Again, in the

[004-114]discussion about Socialism, it is repeatedly rubbed into the public mind

[004-115]that we must choose between Socialism and some horrible thing that they

[004-116]call Individualism. I don't know what it means, but it seems to mean

[004-117]that anybody who happens to pull out a plum is to adopt the moral

[004-118]philosophy of the young Horner--and say what a good boy he is for

[004-119]helping himself.

[004-120]It is calmly assumed that the only two possible types of society are a

[004-121]Collectivist type of society and the present society that exists at this

[004-122]moment and is rather like an animated muck-heap. It is quite unnecessary

[004-123]to say that I should prefer Socialism to the present state of things. I

[004-124]should prefer anarchism to the present state of things. But it is simply

[004-125]not the fact that Collectivism is the only other scheme for a more equal

[004-126]order. A Collectivist has a perfect right to think it the only sound

[004-127]scheme; but it is not the only plausible or possible scheme. We might

[004-128]have peasant proprietorship; we might have the compromise of Henry

[004-129]George; we might have a number of tiny communes; we might have

[004-130]co-operation; we might have Anarchist Communism; we might have a hundred

[004-131]things. I am not saying that any of these are right, though I cannot

[004-132]imagine that any of them could be worse than the present social

[004-133]madhouse, with its top-heavy rich and its tortured poor; but I say that

[004-134]it is an evidence of the stiff and narrow alternative offered to the

[004-135]civic mind, that the civic mind is not, generally speaking, conscious of

[004-136]these other possibilities. The civic mind is not free or alert enough

[004-137]to feel how much it has the world before it. There are at least ten

[004-138]solutions of the Education question, and no one knows which Englishmen

[004-139]really want. For Englishmen are only allowed to vote about the two

[004-140]which are at that moment offered by the Premier and the Leader of the

[004-141]Opposition. There are ten solutions of the drink question; and no one

[004-142]knows which the democracy wants; for the democracy is only allowed to

[004-143]fight about one Licensing Bill at a time.

[004-144]So that the situation comes to this: The democracy has a right to answer

[004-145]questions, but it has no right to ask them. It is still the political

[004-146]aristocracy that asks the questions. And we shall not be unreasonably

[004-147]cynical if we suppose that the political aristocracy will always be

[004-148]rather careful what questions it asks. And if the dangerous comfort and

[004-149]self-flattery of modern England continues much longer there will be less

[004-150]democratic value in an English election than in a Roman saturnalia of

[004-151]slaves. For the powerful class will choose two courses of action, both

[004-152]of them safe for itself, and then give the democracy the gratification

[004-153]of taking one course or the other. The lord will take two things so much

[004-154]alike that he would not mind choosing from them blindfold--and then

[004-155]for a great jest he will allow the slaves to choose.

[004-156]THE MAD OFFICIAL

[004-157]Going mad is the slowest and dullest business in the world. I have very

[004-158]nearly done it more than once in my boyhood, and so have nearly all

[004-159]my friends, born under the general doom of mortals, but especially of

[004-160]moderns; I mean the doom that makes a man come almost to the end of

[004-161]thinking before he comes to the first chance of living.

[004-162]But the process of going mad is dull, for the simple reason that a man

[004-163]does not know that it is going on. Routine and literalism and a

[004-164]certain dry-throated earnestness and mental thirst, these are the very

[004-165]atmosphere of morbidity. If once the man could become conscious of his

[004-166]madness, he would cease to be man. He studies certain texts in Daniel

[004-167]or cryptograms in Shakespeare through monstrously magnifying spectacles,

[004-168]which are on his nose night and day. If once he could take off the

[004-169]spectacles he would smash them. He deduces all his fantasies about the

[004-170]Sixth Seal or the Anglo-Saxon Race from one unexamined and invisible

[004-171]first principle. If he could once see the first principle, he would see

[004-172]that it is not there.
