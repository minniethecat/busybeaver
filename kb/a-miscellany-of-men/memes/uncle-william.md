## The Hat and the Identity

In his essay on the Christmas Mummers, Chesterton pauses to observe the psychology of childhood dress-up. "If you will observe a child just able to walk," he writes, "you will see that his first idea is not to dress up as anybody — but to dress up" (016-045–016-047).

The identity comes second, suggested by the costume already assumed. "Afterwards, of course, the idea of being the King or Uncle William will leap to his lips. But it is generally suggested by the hat he has already let fall over his nose, from far deeper motives" (016-048–016-050).

Uncle William thus enters Chesterton's argument not as a particular person but as an archetype — the familiar adult whose identity a child borrows when ritual demands a name.

## Tommy's Hat and Uncle William's

Chesterton sharpens the point with a distinction: "Tommy does not assume the hat primarily because it is Uncle William's hat, but because it is not Tommy's hat. It is a ritual investiture; and is akin to those Gorgon masks that stiffened the dances of Greece or those towering mitres that came from the mysteries of Persia" (016-051–016-053).

The child's impulse is not imitation but transformation — the desire to become something other, more, and greater than oneself. Uncle William is the convenient label attached to this deeper ritual urge, not its origin.

## The Ritual Investiture

By elevating a child's game of wearing Uncle William's hat to the level of Greek masks and Persian mitres, Chesterton makes a characteristic move: he finds in the ordinary the trace of the cosmic.

The impulse to dress up as Uncle William is, in Chesterton's reading, the same impulse that drives all human ritual — the "profound paradox" of simultaneously concealing and exaggerating the self (016-055–016-056).

Uncle William, though himself unnamed and unknown beyond this passage, becomes a symbol of the universal human drive toward mummery and the "divine madness" of ritual transformation (016-057–016-058).
