William Ralph Inge (1860–1954), the Dean of St Paul's popularly known as "the Gloomy Dean," appears in Chesterton's "[[section-17|The Aristocratic 'Arry]]" as the subject of a characteristically Chestertonian inversion. Inge is not attacked for gloominess — Chesterton considers gloom a perfectly respectable quality in a Dean — but for a logical inconsistency in his cultural pessimism. The passage is brief but precisely aimed: Chesterton uses Inge's own stated preferences to expose what he sees as the self-contradiction of a certain kind of Christian anti-modernism.

## The Gloomy Dean

Chesterton first notes Inge in passing, as part of a catalogue of misapplied labels. "It was probably under the same impulse towards a mysterious misfit of names that people denounced Dr. Inge as 'the Gloomy Dean'" (017-091–017-092). His immediate qualification is telling: "Now there is nothing whatever wrong about being a Dean; nor is there anything wrong about being gloomy" (017-093–017-094). Chesterton's quarrel is never with the label but with whether the label captures anything real.

Dean Inge was a public intellectual of considerable reach — a Christian Platonist, a cultural critic, and a prolific writer whose pessimistic assessments of modern civilization earned him his nickname. Chesterton's engagement with him is brief but characteristic: he does not dispute Inge's premises so much as he turns them inside out.

## The Pagan Irony

The substantive passage is concentrated in a few sentences of section-017, but its logic is sharp. "If some thousands of years of paganism have produced the patience and industry that Dean Inge admires, and if some thousand years of Christianity have produced the sentimentality and sensationalism which he regrets, the obvious deduction is that Dean Inge would be much happier if he were a heathen Chinese" (017-136–017-140).

The argument is a neatly constructed trap. Inge, as a Christian clergyman, laments the defects of Christian civilization while praising pagan virtues. Chesterton's response is not to defend Christianity against the charge — he lets the charge stand for the sake of argument — but to point out where Inge's own logic leads. If paganism produces the virtues and Christianity the vices, then the consistent position is to become a pagan. "Instead of supporting Christian missions to Korea or Japan, he ought to be profoundly and reverently grateful to Korea and Japan for supporting pagan missions to [[england|England]]" (017-141).

The wit is Chestertonian to the bone: it takes the opponent's argument more seriously than the opponent does, and follows it to a conclusion the opponent would never accept. Inge's cultural pessimism, in Chesterton's reading, is not too Christian but not Christian enough — it fails to see that the virtues it admires might themselves be Christian in origin, or at least compatible with Christian civilization.
