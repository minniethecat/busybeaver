# King John

King John (1166–1216) reigned as King of [[england|England]] from 1199 until his death. He is best known for signing Magna Carta in 1215 and for his portrayal as a villain in the Robin Hood legend. In [[section-22|The Other Kind of Man]], G.K. Chesterton offers a characteristically contrarian defense of John.

## Against the Waxwork History

Chesterton sympathises with attempts to "whitewash" King John, not because he believes John was virtuous, but as a protest against the "waxwork style of history." (021-117–021-118)

In this style, every historical figure is frozen in a single pose. Rufus is always hunting, Henry VIII always marrying, Charles I always losing his head. (021-119–021-121)

King John is reduced to pulling out Jews' teeth "with the celerity and industry of an American dentist." (021-123–021-124) Anything that shakes this stiff simplification is valuable, because it reminds us that "these men were once alive; that is, mixed, free, flippant, and inconsistent." (021-125–021-126)

## Medieval Free Will

Chesterton explains that the medieval mind turned centrally upon Free Will. While their social system was rigidly structured, "in their moral philosophy they always thought of man as standing free and doubtful at the cross-roads in a forest." (021-140–021-144)

The greatest Schoolmen devoted folios to describing what the world would have been like if Adam had refused the apple. (021-150–021-155) This sense of liberty has been weakened, Chesterton argues, by [[john-calvin|Calvin]]ism and nineteenth-century physical science. (021-158–021-161)

## A Man of Mixed Passions

The contemporaries of King John did not view him as an irredeemable villain. "They instinctively felt him to be a man of mixed passions like themselves, who was allowing his evil passions to have much too good a time of it." (021-165–021-168)

Chesterton does not claim John was a nice gentleman. But he insists that the popular picture is wrong. John had daredevil courage and hotheaded decision, and he "had a morality which he broke, but which we misunderstand." (021-135–021-139)
