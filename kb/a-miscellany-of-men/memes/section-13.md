# The Free Man

[012-001]I have never been able to discover that “stupid public” of which so many

[012-002]literary men complain. The people one actually meets in trains or at tea

[012-003]parties seem to me quite bright and interesting; certainly quite enough

[012-004]so to call for the full exertion of one's own wits. And even when I have

[012-005]heard brilliant “conversationalists” conversing with other people, the

[012-006]conversation had much more equality and give and take than this age of

[012-007]intellectual snobs will admit. I have sometimes felt tired, like other

[012-008]people; but rather tired with men's talk and variety than with their

[012-009]stolidity or sameness; therefore it was that I sometimes longed to find

[012-010]the refreshment of a single fool.

[012-011]But it was denied me. Turn where I would I found this monotonous

[012-012]brilliancy of the general intelligence, this ruthless, ceaseless sparkle

[012-013]of humour and good sense. The “mostly fools” theory has been used in an

[012-014]anti-democratic sense; but when I found at last my priceless ass, I

[012-015]did not find him in what is commonly called the democracy; nor in the

[012-016]aristocracy either. The man of the democracy generally talks quite

[012-017]rationally, sometimes on the anti-democratic side, but always with an

[012-018]idea of giving reasons for what he says and referring to the realities

[012-019]of his experience. Nor is it the aristocracy that is stupid; at least,

[012-020]not that section of the aristocracy which represents it in politics.

[012-021]They are often cynical, especially about money, but even their

[012-022]boredom tends to make them a little eager for any real information or

[012-023]originality. If a man like Mr. Winston Churchill or Mr. Wyndham made up

[012-024]his mind for any reason to attack Syndicalism he would find out what it

[012-025]was first. Not so the man I found in the club.

[012-026]He was very well dressed; he had a heavy but handsome face; his black

[012-027]clothes suggested the City and his gray moustaches the Army; but the

[012-028]whole suggested that he did not really belong to either, but was one

[012-029]of those who dabble in shares and who play at soldiers. There was some

[012-030]third element about him that was neither mercantile nor military. His

[012-031]manners were a shade too gentlemanly to be quite those of a gentleman.

[012-032]They involved an unction and over-emphasis of the club-man: then I

[012-033]suddenly remembered feeling the same thing in some old actors or old

[012-034]playgoers who had modelled themselves on actors. As I came in he said,

[012-035]“If I was the Government,” and then put a cigar in his mouth which he

[012-036]lit carefully with long intakes of breath. Then he took the cigar out

[012-037]of his mouth again and said, “I'd give it 'em,” as if it were quite a

[012-038]separate sentence. But even while his mouth was stopped with the cigar

[012-039]his companion or interlocutor leaped to his feet and said with great

[012-040]heartiness, snatching up a hat, “Well, I must be off. Tuesday!”. I

[012-041]dislike these dark suspicions, but I certainly fancied I recognised the

[012-042]sudden geniality with which one takes leave of a bore.

[012-043]When, therefore, he removed the narcotic stopper from his mouth it was

[012-044]to me that he addressed the belated epigram. “I'd give it 'em.”

[012-045]“What would you give them,” I asked, “the minimum wage?”

[012-046]“I'd give them beans,” he said. “I'd shoot 'em down shoot 'em down,

[012-047]every man Jack of them. I lost my best train yesterday, and here's

[012-048]the whole country paralysed, and here's a handful of obstinate fellows

[012-049]standing between the country and coal. I'd shoot 'em down!”

[012-050]“That would surely be a little harsh,” I pleaded. “After all, they

[012-051]are not under martial law, though I suppose two or three of them have

[012-052]commissions in the Yeomanry.”

[012-053]“Commissions in the Yeomanry!” he repeated, and his eyes and face, which

[012-054]became startling and separate, like those of a boiled lobster, made me

[012-055]feel sure that he had something of the kind himself.

[012-056]“Besides,” I continued, “wouldn't it be quite enough to confiscate their

[012-057]money?”

[012-058]“Well, I'd send them all to penal servitude, anyhow,” he said, “and I'd

[012-059]confiscate their funds as well.”

[012-060]“The policy is daring and full of difficulty,” I replied, “but I do not

[012-061]say that it is wholly outside the extreme rights of the republic. But

[012-062]you must remember that though the facts of property have become

[012-063]quite fantastic, yet the sentiment of property still exists. These

[012-064]coal-owners, though they have not earned the mines, though they could

[012-065]not work the mines, do quite honestly feel that they own the mines.

[012-066]Hence your suggestion of shooting them down, or even of confiscating

[012-067]their property, raises very--”

[012-068]“What do you mean?” asked the man with the cigar, with a bullying eye.

[012-069]“Who yer talking about?”

[012-070]“I'm talking about what you were talking about,” I replied; “as you put

[012-071]it so perfectly, about the handful of obstinate fellows who are standing

[012-072]between the country and the coal. I mean the men who are selling their

[012-073]own coal for fancy prices, and who, as long as they can get those

[012-074]prices, care as little for national starvation as most merchant princes

[012-075]and pirates have cared for the provinces that were wasted or the peoples

[012-076]that were enslaved just before their ships came home. But though I am a

[012-077]bit of a revolutionist myself, I cannot quite go with you in the extreme

[012-078]violence you suggest. You say--”

[012-079]“I say,” he cried, bursting through my speech with a really splendid

[012-080]energy like that of some noble beast, “I say I'd take all these blasted

[012-081]miners and--”

[012-082]I had risen slowly to my feet, for I was profoundly moved; and I stood

[012-083]staring at that mental monster.

[012-084]“Oh,” I said, “so it is the miners who are all to be sent to penal

[012-085]servitude, so that we may get more coal. It is the miners who are to be

[012-086]shot dead, every man Jack of them; for if once they are all shot dead

[012-087]they will start mining again...You must forgive me, sir; I know I seem

[012-088]somewhat moved. The fact is, I have just found something. Something I

[012-089]have been looking for for years.”

[012-090]“Well,” he asked, with no unfriendly stare, “and what have you found?”

[012-091]“No,” I answered, shaking my head sadly, “I do not think it would be

[012-092]quite kind to tell you what I have found.”

[012-093]He had a hundred virtues, including the capital virtue of good humour,

[012-094]and we had no difficulty in changing the subject and forgetting the

[012-095]disagreement. He talked about society, his town friends and his country

[012-096]sports, and I discovered in the course of it that he was a county

[012-097]magistrate, a Member of Parliament, and a director of several important

[012-098]companies. He was also that other thing, which I did not tell him.

[012-099]The moral is that a certain sort of person does exist, to whose glory

[012-100]this article is dedicated. He is not the ordinary man. He is not the

[012-101]miner, who is sharp enough to ask for the necessities of existence. He

[012-102]is not the mine-owner, who is sharp enough to get a great deal more, by

[012-103]selling his coal at the best possible moment. He is not the aristocratic

[012-104]politician, who has a cynical but a fair sympathy with both economic

[012-105]opportunities. But he is the man who appears in scores of public places

[012-106]open to the upper middle class or (that less known but more powerful

[012-107]section) the lower upper class. Men like this all over the country are

[012-108]really saying whatever comes into their heads in their capacities of

[012-109]justice of the peace, candidate for Parliament, Colonel of the Yeomanry,

[012-110]old family doctor, Poor Law guardian, coroner, or above all, arbiter in

[012-111]trade disputes. He suffers, in the literal sense, from softening of the

[012-112]brain; he has softened it by always taking the view of everything most

[012-113]comfortable for his country, his class, and his private personality.

[012-114]He is a deadly public danger. But as I have given him his name at the

[012-115]beginning of this article there is no need for me to repeat it at the

[012-116]end.

[012-117]THE CONSCRIPT AND THE CRISIS

[012-118]Very few of us ever see the history of our own time happening. And

[012-119]I think the best service a modern journalist can do to society is to

[012-120]record as plainly as ever he can exactly what impression was produced on

[012-121]his mind by anything he has actually seen and heard on the outskirts of

[012-122]any modern problem or campaign. Though all he saw of a railway strike

[012-123]was a flat meadow in Essex in which a train was becalmed for an hour or

[012-124]two, he will probably throw more light on the strike by describing this

[012-125]which he has seen than by describing the steely kings of commerce and

[012-126]the bloody leaders of the mob whom he has never seen--nor any one

[012-127]else either. If he comes a day too late for the battle of Waterloo (as

[012-128]happened to a friend of my grandfather) he should still remember that a

[012-129]true account of the day after Waterloo would be a most valuable thing to

[012-130]have. Though he was on the wrong side of the door when Rizzio was being

[012-131]murdered, we should still like to have the wrong side described in

[012-132]the right way. Upon this principle I, who know nothing of diplomacy or

[012-133]military arrangements, and have only held my breath like the rest of

[012-134]the world while France and Germany were bargaining, will tell quite

[012-135]truthfully of a small scene I saw, one of the thousand scenes that were,

[012-136]so to speak, the anterooms of that inmost chamber of debate.

[012-137]In the course of a certain morning I came into one of the quiet squares

[012-138]of a small French town and found its cathedral. It was one of those gray

[012-139]and rainy days which rather suit the Gothic. The clouds were leaden,

[012-140]like the solid blue-gray lead of the spires and the jewelled windows;

[012-141]the sloping roofs and high-shouldered arches looked like cloaks drooping

[012-142]with damp; and the stiff gargoyles that stood out round the walls were

[012-143]scoured with old rains and new. I went into the round, deep porch with

[012-144]many doors and found two grubby children playing there out of the rain.

[012-145]I also found a notice of services, etc., and among these I found the

[012-146]announcement that at 11.30 (that is about half an hour later) there

[012-147]would be a special service for the Conscripts, that is to say, the draft

[012-148]of young men who were being taken from their homes in that little town

[012-149]and sent to serve in the French Army; sent (as it happened) at an awful

[012-150]moment, when the French Army was encamped at a parting of the ways.

[012-151]There were already a great many people there when I entered, not only of

[012-152]all kinds, but in all attitudes, kneeling, sitting, or standing

[012-153]about. And there was that general sense that strikes every man from a

[012-154]Protestant country, whether he dislikes the Catholic atmosphere or

[012-155]likes it; I mean, the general sense that the thing was “going on all the

[012-156]time”; that it was not an occasion, but a perpetual process, as if it

[012-157]were a sort of mystical inn.

[012-158]Several tricolours were hung quite near to the altar, and the young men,

[012-159]when they came in, filed up the church and sat right at the front.

[012-160]They were, of course, of every imaginable social grade; for the French

[012-161]conscription is really strict and universal. Some looked like young

[012-162]criminals, some like young priests, some like both. Some were so

[012-163]obviously prosperous and polished that a barrack-room must seem to

[012-164]them like hell; others (by the look of them) had hardly ever been in so

[012-165]decent a place. But it was not so much the mere class variety that most

[012-166]sharply caught an Englishman's eye. It was the presence of just those

[012-167]one or two kinds of men who would never have become soldiers in any

[012-168]other way.
