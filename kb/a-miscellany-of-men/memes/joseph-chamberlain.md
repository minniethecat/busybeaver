Joseph Chamberlain (1836–1914) enters Chesterton's "[[section-05|The Man Who Thinks Backwards]]" as the perfect specimen of the essay's central diagnosis. Chesterton recalls "Mr. Joseph Chamberlain" delivering "his great patriotic speech on the inferiority of [[england|England]] to the Dutch parts of South Africa," in which he deployed the phrase "the illimitable veldt" (005-136–005-137). The logic, as Chesterton unpacks it, is backward: a man calls himself a patriot while rhapsodising about a foreign landscape and disparaging his own.

## The Radical Who Became Imperialist

Chamberlain's political trajectory was one of the most dramatic in [[queen-victoria|Victoria]]n Britain. He rose as a radical Liberal, the "gas-and-water socialist" who municipalised Birmingham's utilities and championed social reform. But his break with Gladstone over Irish [[home|Home]] Rule in 1886 sent him into alliance with the Conservatives as a Liberal Unionist, and his appointment as Colonial Secretary in 1895 completed the transformation: the radical became the empire-builder.

It was Chamberlain who declared that "the British Empire is the greatest of God's instruments for the welfare of mankind." His tariff reform campaign of the early 1900s — imperial preference, a customs union of the Empire against the world — split the Conservative Party and dominated British politics until his stroke in 1906.

## "The Illimitable Veldt"

Chesterton's chapter "The Man Who Thinks Backwards" diagnoses a peculiar modern habit: taking an idea that points in one direction and drawing from it the opposite conclusion. Chamberlain's speech on South Africa is the opening example.

The illimitable veldt was, for Chamberlain, a reason to think less of [[england|England]]. But as Chesterton points out, the logical inference runs the other way: "If a man says he loves [[england|England]] and then proceeds to describe the illimitable veldt, he is thinking backwards. The proper inference from the veldt is that South Africa is a good place for South Africans, not that Englishmen should go there" (005-137–005-139). Chamberlain's patriotism, in this reading, was a strange form of self-deprecation dressed in imperial language.

## The Boer War and Its Discontents

Chamberlain's Colonial Secretaryship coincided with the Boer [[war|War]] (1899–1902), a conflict Chesterton opposed and which became a touchstone for his anti-imperialism. The war pitted British forces against the Dutch-descended Boer republics of the Transvaal and Orange Free State. Chamberlain was one of its principal architects.

Chesterton's critique of Chamberlain is not that he was insincere — quite the opposite. He was sincere, but his sincerity was directed toward a contradiction: loving [[england|England]] by disparaging it, spreading English liberty by conquering people who did not want it. This is the same structure Chesterton would later identify in [[cecil-rhodes]]: an imperialism without ideas, all machinery and no content.
