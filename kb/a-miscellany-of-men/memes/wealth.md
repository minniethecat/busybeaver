# Wealth

Wealth, in Chesterton's moral economy, was not a sin — but it was a danger, and a danger made worse by the refusal of the wealthy to acknowledge it as one. Across "A Miscellany of Men," he returns repeatedly to the question of what wealth does to those who possess it, and what the concentration of wealth does to the commonwealth that tolerates it.

## Accidental and Irrational Wealth

Chesterton begins his analysis with an observation that cuts against the complacent assumption that wealth is always earned. There is, he suggests, something fundamentally disordered about "accidental and irrational wealth" — the kind of wealth that comes not from production but from inheritance, speculation, or the accidents of property. (004-008) It is, he darkly hints, "the doom of all peasantries" — the force that disconnects labour from reward and turns honest producers into dependents.

The buried treasure of pirate tales, Chesterton observes, "is a thing for robbers and not for producers." (004-008) (004-009) The boy's adventure story contains a deeper truth than the economist's treatise: wealth without work is not prosperity but plunder, however legal its form.

## The Apparatus of Wealth and Power

Wealth, for Chesterton, is never merely economic. It is always also political, because it is always allied with "a huge apparatus of wealth, power, and printed matter" that shapes what the public is allowed to think and what candidates are allowed to propose. (004-075) The wealthy do not simply have more money than others; they have more capacity to shape the terms of debate, to define what is realistic and what is utopian, to ensure that certain questions are never asked.

This is the structural dimension of Chesterton's critique. He is not interested in denouncing individual rich men as wicked; he is interested in showing that the concentration of wealth inevitably produces a concentration of power, and that a democracy that tolerates the second while regulating only the first has misunderstood its own predicament.

## The Normal Friction of Wealth and Poverty

Chesterton is careful to distinguish his position from both the revolutionary and the defender of the status quo. "In the old normal friction of normal wealth and poverty I am myself on the Radical side," he declares. (013-158) He thinks a Berkshire squire has too much power over his tenants and a Brompton builder too much over his workmen — not because squires and builders are wicked, but because the normal friction of inequality is itself an injustice.

The phrase "normal friction" is carefully chosen. Chesterton does not imagine a world without inequality; he imagines a world in which inequality does not entail domination. The squire may be richer than the tenant, but he should not have "too much power" over him. (013-158) The distinction between wealth and power is, for Chesterton, the distinction between a tolerable inequality and an intolerable one.

## The Crimes of Wealth and Culture

Chesterton's most severe language is reserved for the moral blindness that wealth produces. He speaks of "these crimes of wealth and culture" as the setting in which "we face the real problem of positive evil" — not the evil of poverty and desperation, but the evil of comfort and self-satisfaction. (022-128) The rich man, in Chesterton's analysis, is tempted not toward obvious vices but toward a subtler corruption: the belief that his prosperity is a sign of his virtue.

The employer who rises early and works hard at the "wise distribution" of his wealth may be genuinely admirable — but Chesterton warns against mistaking the "wonderful wheels going round" in the factory for evidence that the system as a whole is just. (020-142) (020-145) The wheels are wonderful; the question is whether the men who turn them are fairly treated.
