Walter Pater (1839–1894) appears in Chesterton's "[[section-15|The Mystagogue]]" as an exemplar of what art criticism ought to be.

Chesterton invokes Pater alongside [[ruskin|John Ruskin]] as one of the "two great critics" who believed that art could be taught and that the critic had a duty to find words for what the eye receives (014-099–014-104).

## The Chain from God to Critic

Chesterton locates Pater within a chain from divine intention through nature and art to criticism.

"The 'Mona Lisa' was in some respects (not all, I fancy) what God meant her to be. Leonardo's picture was, in some respects, like the lady. And Walter Pater's rich description was, in some respects, like the picture" (014-083–014-085).

The chain is imperfect at every link, but imperfection does not mean failure. "Thus we come to the consoling reflection that even literature, in the last resort, can express something other than its own unhappy self" (014-086–014-087).

Pater's prose is not a substitute for the painting but a parallel act of creation. The adequacy of the translation is always partial, but the attempt itself is valid.

## Pater Against the Mute Critics

Chesterton's argument targets the modern critic who "professes to be entirely inarticulate" — who boasts of being speechless before art (014-088–014-090).

Against this posture, Chesterton sets Pater and Ruskin as critics who did the work. "Pater does suggest an intelligent cause for the cadaverous colour of Botticelli's 'Venus Rising from the Sea'" (014-097).

Chesterton acknowledges that Pater and Ruskin "were far too fastidious for my taste; they urged to excess the idea that a sense of art was a sort of secret; to be patiently taught and slowly learnt" (014-099–014-101).

Yet this fastidiousness rested on a sound conviction. "Still, they thought it could be taught: they thought it could be learnt. They constrained themselves, with considerable creative fatigue, to find the exact adjectives which might parallel in English prose what has been done in Italian painting" (014-102–014-105).

The contrast is with the champions of Cubism, Post-Impressionism, and Picasso.

They "do not attempt to translate beauty into language; they merely tell you that it is untranslatable — that is, unutterable, indefinable, indescribable, impalpable, ineffable, and all the rest of it" (014-109–014-114).

For Chesterton, Pater's effortful translation, however imperfect, is intellectually and morally superior to the modern critic's lazy appeal to the ineffable.
