# Dogma

Dogma, for Chesterton, is not the enemy of liberty but its precondition. Again and again across his essays, he returns to the paradox that fixed principles are what make free and varied life possible, while the rejection of dogma produces not liberation but monotony and intellectual tyranny. He treats the word with deliberate provocativeness, reclaiming it from those who use "dogma" as a term of abuse and showing that the most dogmatic people are often those who pride themselves on having no dogmas at all.

## The Bond of Freedom

Chesterton's most sustained argument for dogma occurs in the essay on "The Sectarian of [[society|Society]]," where he opens with a blunt assertion: "A fixed creed is absolutely indispensable to freedom." (011-052) The argument is deceptively simple. If men are to enjoy their variety -- to disagree fruitfully, to have genuine differences of opinion -- they must share a common ground from which disagreement can begin. An intellectual formula, a dogma, is "the only thing that can create a communication that does not depend on mere blood, class, or capricious sympathy." (011-052) Without it, each man is locked in his own universe, unable even to recognise that he differs from his neighbour.

Chesterton illustrates this with an allegory of vision. If all men agree that the sun and moon exist, then the strong-eyed man, the shortsighted man, and the colour-blind man can all describe what they see and learn from one another. But if that common reference point is denied, "neither is free, for each is shut up in the cell of a separate universe." (011-078) The consequence of abandoning dogma is not the broad-minded tolerance that its opponents promise, but the opposite: "Instead of the liberty of dogma, you have the tyranny of taste." (011-078) Men sort themselves into monotonous camps of the like-minded -- all the shortsighted together, all the long-sighted together -- and variety collapses into faction.

## Dogma and the Diversity of Persons

The same principle applies to human communities. Chesterton argues that a binding creed produces a rich variety of human types within a single group -- "the saint, the hypocrite, the brawler, the weak brother" -- each of whom can be appealed to by the creed and judged by it. (011-125) The creed holds these diverse personalities together in a living tension; they do each other good, or even "join together to do the hypocrite good, with heavy and repeated blows." (011-125) But "once break the bond of doctrine which alone holds these people together," each type gravitates to its own kind, and the community dissolves into isolated factions of the selfsame. (011-125)

Chesterton applies this test to Socialism specifically. A clear definition of Socialism -- ownership by government of the means of production -- allows a Socialist to be "any kind of man he likes in any other sense": soldier, tradesman, clergyman, or artist. (011-091) But those who refuse to be bound by "what they call a narrow dogma" and insist that Socialism means "far, far more" end up becoming a single monotonous type, wearing the same clothes and reading the same books. (011-091) The rejection of dogma, far from liberating them, narrows them.

## The Unacknowledged Dogma

Chesterton is equally alert to the dogmas that hide themselves as common sense. The modern intellectual who will not "begin with any dogmas about the nature of man" merely substitutes an unexamined dogma about the primacy of observable fact -- a positivism that is itself a creed, though it denies the name. (002-059) The newspapers that "declare dogmatically day after day, until every one half believes" that only two colours exist in the paint-box are practising a dogma all the more tyrannical for being unacknowledged. (004-084)

The English, Chesterton observes, have "a huge fundamental dogma, which the French have not" -- the belief that "the past is past." (015-015) This dogma is so deeply embedded that it is held unconsciously; the English act on it without knowing they hold it. And the man who demands "that larger religion that shall have no special dogmas" is asking for something logically impossible: "A quadruped means something with four feet; and a religion means something that commits a man to some doctrine about the universe." (024-133) To be without dogma is not to be free but to be without meaning.
