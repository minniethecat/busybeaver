# The Gardener and the Guinea

[006-001]I shall not express what the place expressed. I am not even sure that it

[006-002]is a thing that ought to be expressed. There was something heathen about

[006-003]its union of beauty and death; sorrow seemed to glitter, as it does in

[006-004]some of the great pagan poems. I understood one of the thousand poetical

[006-005]phrases of the populace, “a God-forsaken place.” Yet something was

[006-006]present there; and I could not yet find the key to my fixed impression.

[006-007]Then suddenly I remembered the right word. It was an enchanted place. It

[006-008]had been put to sleep. In a flash I remembered all the fairy-tales about

[006-009]princes turned to marble and princesses changed to snow. We were in a

[006-010]land where none could strive or cry out; a white nightmare. The moon

[006-011]looked at me across the valley like the enormous eye of a hypnotist; the

[006-012]one white eye of the world.

[006-013]There was never a better play than POT LUCK; for it tells a tale with a

[006-014]point and a tale that might happen any day among English peasants. There

[006-015]were never better actors than the local Buckinghamshire Players: for

[006-016]they were acting their own life with just that rise into exaggeration

[006-017]which is the transition from life to art. But all the time I was

[006-018]mesmerised by the moon; I saw all these men and women as enchanted

[006-019]things. The poacher shot pheasants; the policeman tracked pheasants; the

[006-020]wife hid pheasants; they were all (especially the policeman) as true

[006-021]as death. But there was something more true to death than true to life

[006-022]about it all: the figures were frozen with a magic frost of sleep or

[006-023]fear or custom such as does not cramp the movements of the poor men of

[006-024]other lands. I looked at the poacher and the policeman and the gun; then

[006-025]at the gun and the policeman and the poacher; and I could find no name

[006-026]for the fancy that haunted and escaped me. The poacher believed in the

[006-027]Game Laws as much as the policeman. The poacher's wife not only believed

[006-028]in the Game Laws, but protected them as well as him. She got a promise

[006-029]from her husband that he would never shoot another pheasant. Whether he

[006-030]kept it I doubt; I fancy he sometimes shot a pheasant even after that.

[006-031]But I am sure he never shot a policeman. For we live in an enchanted

[006-032]land.

[006-033]THE SUN WORSHIPPER

[006-034]There is a shrewd warning to be given to all people who are in revolt.

[006-035]And in the present state of things, I think all men are revolting in

[006-036]that sense; except a few who are revolting in the other sense. But the

[006-037]warning to Socialists and other revolutionaries is this: that as sure as

[006-038]fate, if they use any argument which is atheist or materialistic, that

[006-039]argument will always be turned against them at last by the tyrant and

[006-040]the slave. To-day I saw one too common Socialist argument turned Tory,

[006-041]so to speak, in a manner quite startling and insane. I mean that modern

[006-042]doctrine, taught, I believe, by most followers of Karl Marx, which is

[006-043]called the materialist theory of history. The theory is, roughly, this:

[006-044]that all the important things in history are rooted in an economic

[006-045]motive. In short, history is a science; a science of the search for

[006-046]food.

[006-047]Now I desire, in passing only, to point out that this is not merely

[006-048]untrue, but actually the reverse of the truth. It is putting it too

[006-049]feebly to say that the history of man is not only economic. Man would

[006-050]not have any history if he were only economic. The need for food is

[006-051]certainly universal, so universal that it is not even human. Cows

[006-052]have an economic motive, and apparently (I dare not say what ethereal

[006-053]delicacies may be in a cow) only an economic motive. The cow eats grass

[006-054]anywhere and never eats anything else. In short, the cow does fulfill

[006-055]the materialist theory of history: that is why the cow has no history.

[006-056]“A History of Cows” would be one of the simplest and briefest of

[006-057]standard works. But if some cows thought it wicked to eat long grass

[006-058]and persecuted all who did so; if the cow with the crumpled horn were

[006-059]worshipped by some cows and gored to death by others; if cows began to

[006-060]have obvious moral preferences over and above a desire for grass, then

[006-061]cows would begin to have a history. They would also begin to have a

[006-062]highly unpleasant time, which is perhaps the same thing.

[006-063]The economic motive is not merely not inside all history; it is actually

[006-064]outside all history. It belongs to Biology or the Science of Life; that

[006-065]is, it concerns things like cows, that are not so very much alive. Men

[006-066]are far too much alive to get into the science of anything; for them we

[006-067]have made the art of history. To say that human actions have depended

[006-068]on economic support is like saying that they have depended on having two

[006-069]legs. It accounts for action, but not for such varied action; it is a

[006-070]condition, but not a motive; it is too universal to be useful. Certainly

[006-071]a soldier wins the Victoria Cross on two legs; he also runs away on two

[006-072]legs. But if our object is to discover whether he will become a V.C. or

[006-073]a coward the most careful inspection of his legs will yield us little or

[006-074]no information. In the same way a man will want food if he is a dreamy

[006-075]romantic tramp, and will want food if he is a toiling and sweating

[006-076]millionaire. A man must be supported on food as he must be supported

[006-077]on legs. But cows (who have no history) are not only furnished more

[006-078]generously in the matter of legs, but can see their food on a much

[006-079]grander and more imaginative scale. A cow can lift up her eyes to the

[006-080]hills and see uplands and peaks of pure food. Yet we never see the

[006-081]horizon broken by crags of cake or happy hills of cheese.

[006-082]So far the cow (who has no history) seems to have every other advantage.

[006-083]But history--the whole point of history--precisely is that

[006-084]some two legged soldiers ran away while others, of similar anatomical

[006-085]structure, did not. The whole point of history precisely is: some people

[006-086](like poets and tramps) chance getting money by disregarding it, while

[006-087]others (such as millionaires) will absolutely lose money for the fun

[006-088]of bothering about it. There would be no history if there were only

[006-089]economic history. All the historical events have been due to the

[006-090]twists and turns given to the economic instinct by forces that were not

[006-091]economic. For instance, this theory traces the French war of Edward

[006-092]III to a quarrel about the French wines. Any one who has even smelt the

[006-093]Middle Ages must feel fifty answers spring to his lips; but in this case

[006-094]one will suffice. There would have been no such war, then, if we all

[006-095]drank water like cows. But when one is a man one enters the world

[006-096]of historic choice. The act of drinking wine is one that requires

[006-097]explanation. So is the act of not drinking wine.

[006-098]But the capitalist can get much more fun out of the doctrine.

[006-099]When strikes were splitting England right and left a little while ago,

[006-100]an ingenious writer, humorously describing himself as a Liberal, said

[006-101]that they were entirely due to the hot weather. The suggestion was

[006-102]eagerly taken up by other creatures of the same kind, and I really do

[006-103]not see why it was not carried farther and applied to other lamentable

[006-104]uprisings in history. Thus, it is a remarkable fact that the weather is

[006-105]generally rather warm in Egypt; and this cannot but throw a light on

[006-106]the sudden and mysterious impulse of the Israelites to escape from

[006-107]captivity. The English strikers used some barren republican formula

[006-108](arid as the definitions of the medieval schoolmen), some academic

[006-109]shibboleth about being free men and not being forced to work except for

[006-110]a wage accepted by them. Just in the same way the Israelites in Egypt

[006-111]employed some dry scholastic quibble about the extreme difficulty of

[006-112]making bricks with nothing to make them of. But whatever fantastic

[006-113]intellectual excuses they may have put forward for their strange and

[006-114]unnatural conduct in walking out when the prison door was open, there

[006-115]can be no doubt that the real cause was the warm weather. Such a climate

[006-116]notoriously also produces delusions and horrible fancies, such as Mr.

[006-117]Kipling describes. And it was while their brains were disordered by the

[006-118]heat that the Jews fancied that they were founding a nation, that they

[006-119]were led by a prophet, and, in short, that they were going to be of some

[006-120]importance in the affairs of the world.

[006-121]Nor can the historical student fail to note that the French monarchy was

[006-122]pulled down in August; and that August is a month in summer.

[006-123]In spite of all this, however, I have some little difficulty myself

[006-124]in accepting so simple a form of the Materialist Theory of History (at

[006-125]these words all Marxian Socialists will please bow their heads three

[006-126]times), and I rather think that exceptions might be found to the

[006-127]principle. Yet it is not chiefly such exceptions that embarrass my

[006-128]belief in it.

[006-129]No; my difficulty is rather in accounting for the strange coincidence by

[006-130]which the shafts of Apollo split us exclusively along certain lines of

[006-131]class and of economics. I cannot understand why all solicitors did not

[006-132]leave off soliciting, all doctors leave off doctoring, all judges leave

[006-133]off judging, all benevolent bankers leave off lending money at high

[006-134]interest, and all rising politicians leave off having nothing to add to

[006-135]what their right honourable friend told the House about eight years

[006-136]ago. The quaint theoretic plea of the workers, that they were striking

[006-137]because they were ill paid, seems to receive a sort of wild and hazy

[006-138]confirmation from the fact that, throughout the hottest weather, judges

[006-139]and other persons who are particularly well paid showed no disposition

[006-140]to strike. I have to fall back therefore on metaphysical fancies of my

[006-141]own; and I continue to believe that the anger of the English poor (to

[006-142]steal a phrase from Sir Thomas Browne) came from something in man that

[006-143]is other than the elements and that owes no homage unto the sun.

[006-144]When comfortable people come to talking stuff of that sort, it is really

[006-145]time that the comfortable classes made a short summary and confession

[006-146]of what they have really done with the very poor Englishman. The dawn of

[006-147]the mediæval civilisation found him a serf; which is a different thing

[006-148]from a slave. He had security; although the man belonged to the land

[006-149]rather than the land to the man. He could not be evicted; his rent could

[006-150]not be raised. In practice, it came to something like this: that if the

[006-151]lord rode down his cabbages he had not much chance of redress; but he

[006-152]had the chance of growing more cabbages. He had direct access to the

[006-153]means of production.

[006-154]Since then the centuries in England have achieved something different;

[006-155]and something which, fortunately, is perfectly easy to state. There is

[006-156]no doubt about what we have done. We have kept the inequality, but we

[006-157]have destroyed the security. The man is not tied to the land, as in

[006-158]serfdom; nor is the land tied to the man, as in a peasantry. The rich

[006-159]man has entered into an absolute ownership of farms and fields; and (in

[006-160]the modern industrial phrase) he has locked out the English people. They

[006-161]can only find an acre to dig or a house to sleep in by accepting such

[006-162]competitive and cruel terms as he chooses to impose.
