# Habit

Habit appears in Chesterton's essays as a double-edged phenomenon: it is the glue that holds society together and the chain that binds men to unexamined prejudice. He treats habit as a social and moral force — sometimes the ground of civic order, sometimes the mask of injustice, and sometimes the very texture of uncritical thought.

## Social Habits as the Foundation of Unity

In "The Wrong Incendiary," Chesterton reflects on the transformation of social cohesion. He regrets the passing of the old Puritan unity, founded on theology but embracing all types from Milton to the grocer, and its replacement by a "newer Puritan unity which is founded rather on certain social habits, certain common notions, both permissive and prohibitive, in connection with Particular social pleasures" (011-110). Where once men recognised each other by a shared principle, they now recognise each other by shared tastes — the kind of clothes one wears, the books one reads, the pictures one hangs on the wall. Modern society, he observes, is "almost made up of these large monochrome patches" (011-108) — uniformities of habit that supply the coherence that theology once provided, but on a thinner, more conventional basis. Habit, in this sense, has become the substitute for [[dogma|creed]].

## Habit as Intellectual Reflex

In "The Separatist And Sacred Things," Chesterton identifies a different kind of habit — the intellectual reflex of uncritical admiration. English magazines, he notes, "fall into a habit of wondering praise of Bushido, the Japanese chivalry, as if no Western knights had ever vowed noble vows" (015-082). This habit is a form of intellectual laziness: it is easier to praise what is distant and exotic than to examine what is near and familiar. The same inclination that romanticises the East also blinds the Englishman to the virtues of his own tradition. Chesterton's point is that habit, in this context, is not a neutral repetition but an active distortion — a trained inclination of the mind that prevents genuine seeing.

## The Ambiguity of Heathen Habits

In "The New Theologian," Chesterton turns the question of habit into a paradox. He cannot understand why [[dean-inge|Dean Inge]], "with his enthusiasm for heathen habits and traditions, should wish to spread in the East the ideas which he has found so dreadfully unsettling in the West" (017-134). The Dean admires Oriental patience and industry — the fruit of "some thousands of years of paganism" — yet wants to export Western Christianity, which has produced the "sentimentality and sensationalism" he regrets. Chesterton's irony exposes a contradiction: if heathen habits have produced virtues worth admiring, why dismantle the very system that produced them? Habit here is neither good nor bad in itself; its moral weight depends entirely on whether one is willing to live with its consequences.

## Business Habits and the Mask of Order

In "The Man On Top," Chesterton examines habit in its most practical form. On the great estates, one sees "much cleanliness, order, and business habits in the offices and accounts of the estate" (020-157). These habits are real and, in their own terms, admirable. Yet they are also a mask: when one steps back and asks what these habits have actually produced, the answer is unemployment and rural depopulation. The business habits of the landlord class create a surface of efficiency that conceals a deeper failure. Habit here is the virtue that becomes a vice by omission — it allows men to admire the machinery while ignoring the outcome.

## Habit and Class Instinct

In "The Other Kind Of Man," Chesterton deepens the critique. The Chairman of a labour dispute commission, whatever his "personal virtues, must be by instinct and habit akin to the capitalists in the dispute" (021-077). Habit here is not merely personal routine but social conditioning — an unearned alignment of outlook that predetermines judgment. Two more of the Commissioners, Chesterton notes, were themselves capitalists. The habits of the governing class, however benign in intention, inevitably tilt the scales of justice. Habit, in this final sense, is the invisible architecture of inequality — the set of ingrained responses that make injustice feel natural.
