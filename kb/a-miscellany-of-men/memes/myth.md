# Myth

Myth, for Chesterton, is not what the nineteenth-century comparative anthropologists supposed it to be. It is not a primitive attempt at science, nor a coded description of the seasons, nor a hieroglyph for the sun. He reverses the standard account: myths arise from man's irrepressible instinct to see personality in everything, not from his effort to explain nature.

The key to understanding any myth, he argues with characteristic paradox, is to discover the one myth that is not mythical at all.

## The Solar Myth as Counterfeit

Chesterton's most sustained attack on modern mythography comes in his demolition of the "Solar Myth" theory — the notion that gods and heroes are really allegorical representations of the sun. He satirises the idea with savage humour: primitive man, on this account, observed the sun, decided to keep it secret, and invented "a picture of a man killing a snake" as an elaborate code. (009-119)

> One does not need to know much mythology to know that this is a myth. It is commonly called the Solar Myth. (009-120)

The satire exposes what Chesterton sees as the fundamental error of modern myth-studies. They attribute to primitive man a calculating, abstract, almost scientific mentality, whereas in reality primitive thought was poetic, personal, and dramatic. "The god was never a symbol or hieroglyph representing the sun. The sun was a hieroglyph representing the god." (009-122)

The scientist's account of mythology, Chesterton concludes, "is more mythical than the myth itself" (009-059) — a fiction about fiction, but one that lacks the imagination of the original.

## The Priority of Personality

Chesterton's positive account of myth is rooted in his conviction that human beings are irreducibly personal. "We human beings have never worshipped [[nature|Nature]]; and indeed, the reason is very simple. It is that all human beings are superhuman beings." (009-135-136)

We have "printed our own image upon [[nature|Nature]], as God has printed His image upon us" (009-137) — a statement that is at once theological and anthropological. This is why myths take the form they do.

Jupiter does not mean thunder; thunder means the march and victory of Jupiter. Neptune does not mean the sea; the sea is his, and he made it. (009-141-143) The mythological imagination begins with personal agents — gods, heroes, spirits — and sees their actions reflected in nature.

In this, Chesterton argues, myth is closer to [[poetry|Poetry]] than to science, continuous with the instinct that makes a lover say "my love is like a red, red rose" without any intention of praising roses under an allegory. (009-156)

## The Myth That Is Not a Myth

The most distinctive claim Chesterton makes about myth is his conclusion:

> I say you cannot really understand any myths till you have found that one of them is not a myth. Turnip ghosts mean nothing if there are no real ghosts. (009-148-149)

The argument is a classic Chestertonian reversal. Forged bank-notes mean nothing if there are no real bank-notes; "Heathen gods mean nothing, and must always mean nothing, to those of us that deny the Christian God" (009-151-152). False gods, false myths, false religions are only intelligible against the background of a true one.

To understand why men make myths, one must recognise that mythology distorts something real — the human encounter with the divine.

This is not a repudiation of mythology but its vindication. Chesterton reads pagan myths as fragments of a broken truth, anticipations and parodies of real revelation. "When once it is the real God the Cosmos falls down before Him, offering flowers in spring as flames in winter" (009-155) — the myths of dying gods find their reality in the [[religion|Christian religion]].

"Christ is the Sun of Easter" is not a piece of solar allegory but the proclamation of a historical event. (009-161) The true myth fulfils what the false ones grope toward.
