# The Enchanted Man

[009-001]As I reflected it crawled back into my memory that I had mildly played

[009-002]the fool in that house on that distant day. I had some red chalk in

[009-003]my pocket, I think, and I wrote things on the unpapered plaster walls;

[009-004]things addressed to Mr. Harrogate. A dim memory told me that I had

[009-005]written up in what I supposed to be the dining-room:

[009-006]James Harrogate, thank God for meat,

[009-007]Then eat and eat and eat and eat,

[009-008]or something of that kind. I faintly feel that some longer lyric

[009-009]was scrawled on the walls of what looked like a bedroom, something

[009-010]beginning:

[009-011]When laying what you call your head,

[009-012]O Harrogate, upon your bed,

[009-013]and there all my memory dislimns and decays. But I could still see quite

[009-014]vividly the plain plastered walls and the rude, irregular writing,

[009-015]and the places where the red chalk broke. I could see them, I mean, in

[009-016]memory; for when I came down that road again after a sixth of a century

[009-017]the house was very different.

[009-018]I had seen it before at noon, and now I found it in the dusk. But its

[009-019]windows glowed with lights of many artificial sorts; one of its low

[009-020]square windows stood open; from this there escaped up the road a stream

[009-021]of lamplight and a stream of singing. Some sort of girl, at least,

[009-022]was standing at some sort of piano, and singing a song of healthy

[009-023]sentimentalism in that house where long ago my blessing had died on the

[009-024]wind and my poems been covered up by the wallpaper. I stood outside that

[009-025]lamplit house at dusk full of those thoughts that I shall never express

[009-026]if I live to be a million any better than I expressed them in red

[009-027]chalk upon the wall. But after I had hovered a little, and was about to

[009-028]withdraw, a mad impulse seized me. I rang the bell. I said in distinct

[009-029]accents to a very smart suburban maid, “Does Mr. James Harrogate live

[009-030]here?”

[009-031]She said he didn't; but that she would inquire, in case I was looking

[009-032]for him in the neighbourhood; but I excused her from such exertion. I

[009-033]had one moment's impulse to look for him all over the world; and then

[009-034]decided not to look for him at all.

[009-035]THE PRIEST OF SPRING

[009-036]The sun has strengthened and the air softened just before Easter Day.

[009-037]But it is a troubled brightness which has a breath not only of novelty

[009-038]but of revolution, There are two great armies of the human intellect

[009-039]who will fight till the end on this vital point, whether Easter is to

[009-040]be congratulated on fitting in with the Spring--or the Spring on

[009-041]fitting in with Easter.

[009-042]The only two things that can satisfy the soul are a person and a

[009-043]story; and even a story must be about a person. There are indeed

[009-044]very voluptuous appetites and enjoyments in mere abstractions like

[009-045]mathematics, logic, or chess. But these mere pleasures of the mind

[009-046]are like mere pleasures of the body. That is, they are mere pleasures,

[009-047]though they may be gigantic pleasures; they can never by a mere increase

[009-048]of themselves amount to happiness. A man just about to be hanged may

[009-049]enjoy his breakfast; especially if it be his favourite breakfast; and

[009-050]in the same way he may enjoy an argument with the chaplain about heresy,

[009-051]especially if it is his favourite heresy. But whether he can enjoy

[009-052]either of them does not depend on either of them; it depends upon his

[009-053]spiritual attitude towards a subsequent event. And that event is really

[009-054]interesting to the soul; because it is the end of a story and (as some

[009-055]hold) the end of a person.

[009-056]Now it is this simple truth which, like many others, is too simple for

[009-057]our scientists to see. This is where they go wrong, not only about

[009-058]true religion, but about false religions too; so that their account of

[009-059]mythology is more mythical than the myth itself. I do not confine myself

[009-060]to saying that they are quite incorrect when they state (for instance)

[009-061]that Christ was a legend of dying and reviving vegetation, like Adonis

[009-062]or Persephone. I say that even if Adonis was a god of vegetation,

[009-063]they have got the whole notion of him wrong. Nobody, to begin with, is

[009-064]sufficiently interested in decaying vegetables, as such, to make any

[009-065]particular mystery or disguise about them; and certainly not enough to

[009-066]disguise them under the image of a very handsome young man, which is a

[009-067]vastly more interesting thing. If Adonis was connected with the fall

[009-068]of leaves in autumn and the return of flowers in spring, the process of

[009-069]thought was quite different. It is a process of thought which springs

[009-070]up spontaneously in all children and young artists; it springs up

[009-071]spontaneously in all healthy societies. It is very difficult to explain

[009-072]in a diseased society.

[009-073]The brain of man is subject to short and strange snatches of sleep. A

[009-074]cloud seals the city of reason or rests upon the sea of imagination; a

[009-075]dream that darkens as much, whether it is a nightmare of atheism or a

[009-076]daydream of idolatry. And just as we have all sprung from sleep with a

[009-077]start and found ourselves saying some sentence that has no meaning, save

[009-078]in the mad tongues of the midnight; so the human mind starts from its

[009-079]trances of stupidity with some complete phrase upon its lips; a complete

[009-080]phrase which is a complete folly. Unfortunately it is not like the dream

[009-081]sentence, generally forgotten in the putting on of boots or the putting

[009-082]in of breakfast. This senseless aphorism, invented when man's mind was

[009-083]asleep, still hangs on his tongue and entangles all his relations to

[009-084]rational and daylight things. All our controversies are confused by

[009-085]certain kinds of phrases which are not merely untrue, but were

[009-086]always unmeaning; which are not merely inapplicable, but were always

[009-087]intrinsically useless. We recognise them wherever a man talks of “the

[009-088]survival of the fittest,” meaning only the survival of the survivors; or

[009-089]wherever a man says that the rich “have a stake in the country,” as

[009-090]if the poor could not suffer from misgovernment or military defeat; or

[009-091]where a man talks about “going on towards Progress,” which only means

[009-092]going on towards going on; or when a man talks about “government by the

[009-093]wise few,” as if they could be picked out by their pantaloons. “The wise

[009-094]few” must mean either the few whom the foolish think wise or the very

[009-095]foolish who think themselves wise.

[009-096]There is one piece of nonsense that modern people still find themselves

[009-097]saying, even after they are more or less awake, by which I am

[009-098]particularly irritated. It arose in the popularised science of the

[009-099]nineteenth century, especially in connection with the study of myths and

[009-100]religions. The fragment of gibberish to which I refer generally takes

[009-101]the form of saying “This god or hero really represents the sun.” Or

[009-102]“Apollo killing the Python MEANS that the summer drives out the winter.”

[009-103]Or “The King dying in a western battle is a SYMBOL of the sun setting

[009-104]in the west.” Now I should really have thought that even the skeptical

[009-105]professors, whose skulls are as shallow as frying-pans, might have

[009-106]reflected that human beings never think or feel like this. Consider what

[009-107]is involved in this supposition. It presumes that primitive man went out

[009-108]for a walk and saw with great interest a big burning spot on the sky. He

[009-109]then said to primitive woman, “My dear, we had better keep this quiet.

[009-110]We mustn't let it get about. The children and the slaves are so very

[009-111]sharp. They might discover the sun any day, unless we are very careful.

[009-112]So we won't call it 'the sun,' but I will draw a picture of a man

[009-113]killing a snake; and whenever I do that you will know what I mean.

[009-114]The sun doesn't look at all like a man killing a snake; so nobody can

[009-115]possibly know. It will be a little secret between us; and while the

[009-116]slaves and the children fancy I am quite excited with a grand tale of

[009-117]a writhing dragon and a wrestling demigod, I shall really MEAN this

[009-118]delicious little discovery, that there is a round yellow disc up in the

[009-119]air.” One does not need to know much mythology to know that this is a

[009-120]myth. It is commonly called the Solar Myth.

[009-121]Quite plainly, of course, the case was just the other way. The god

[009-122]was never a symbol or hieroglyph representing the sun. The sun was a

[009-123]hieroglyph representing the god. Primitive man (with whom my friend

[009-124]Dombey is no doubt well acquainted) went out with his head full of gods

[009-125]and heroes, because that is the chief use of having a head. Then he saw

[009-126]the sun in some glorious crisis of the dominance of noon on the distress

[009-127]of nightfall, and he said, “That is how the face of the god would shine

[009-128]when he had slain the dragon,” or “That is how the whole world would

[009-129]bleed to westward, if the god were slain at last.”

[009-130]No human being was ever really so unnatural as to worship Nature. No

[009-131]man, however indulgent (as I am) to corpulency, ever worshipped a man

[009-132]as round as the sun or a woman as round as the moon. No man, however

[009-133]attracted to an artistic attenuation, ever really believed that the

[009-134]Dryad was as lean and stiff as the tree. We human beings have never

[009-135]worshipped Nature; and indeed, the reason is very simple. It is that all

[009-136]human beings are superhuman beings. We have printed our own image upon

[009-137]Nature, as God has printed His image upon us. We have told the enormous

[009-138]sun to stand still; we have fixed him on our shields, caring no more

[009-139]for a star than for a starfish. And when there were powers of Nature we

[009-140]could not for the time control, we have conceived great beings in human

[009-141]shape controlling them. Jupiter does not mean thunder. Thunder means the

[009-142]march and victory of Jupiter. Neptune does not mean the sea; the sea is

[009-143]his, and he made it. In other words, what the savage really said about

[009-144]the sea was, “Only my fetish Mumbo could raise such mountains out of

[009-145]mere water.” What the savage really said about the sun was, “Only my

[009-146]great great-grandfather Jumbo could deserve such a blazing crown.”

[009-147]About all these myths my own position is utterly and even sadly simple.

[009-148]I say you cannot really understand any myths till you have found that

[009-149]one of them is not a myth. Turnip ghosts mean nothing if there are

[009-150]no real ghosts. Forged bank-notes mean nothing if there are no real

[009-151]bank-notes. Heathen gods mean nothing, and must always mean nothing, to

[009-152]those of us that deny the Christian God. When once a god is admitted,

[009-153]even a false god, the Cosmos begins to know its place: which is the

[009-154]second place. When once it is the real God the Cosmos falls down before

[009-155]Him, offering flowers in spring as flames in winter. “My love is like a

[009-156]red, red rose” does not mean that the poet is praising roses under the

[009-157]allegory of a young lady. “My love is an arbutus” does not mean that the

[009-158]author was a botanist so pleased with a particular arbutus tree that he

[009-159]said he loved it. “Who art the moon and regent of my sky” does not mean

[009-160]that Juliet invented Romeo to account for the roundness of the moon.

[009-161]“Christ is the Sun of Easter” does not mean that the worshipper is

[009-162]praising the sun under the emblem of Christ. Goddess or god can clothe

[009-163]themselves with the spring or summer; but the body is more than raiment.

[009-164]Religion takes almost disdainfully the dress of Nature; and indeed

[009-165]Christianity has done as well with the snows of Christmas as with the

[009-166]snow-drops of spring. And when I look across the sun-struck fields, I

[009-167]know in my inmost bones that my joy is not solely in the spring, for

[009-168]spring alone, being always returning, would be always sad. There is

[009-169]somebody or something walking there, to be crowned with flowers: and my

[009-170]pleasure is in some promise yet possible and in the resurrection of the

[009-171]dead.
