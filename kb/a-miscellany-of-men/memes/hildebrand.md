Pope Gregory VII, born Hildebrand of Sovana, appears in Chesterton's "A Miscellany of Men" in a striking passage within his critique of [[cecil-rhodes|Cecil Rhodes]] and British imperialism (019-069). Chesterton invokes Hildebrand not to praise the medieval pope, but to draw a pointed contrast: unlike Rhodes, Hildebrand at least "stood for" something identifiable — the cause of Catholicism and papal supremacy.

## The Gregorian Reform and Principles

Chesterton's reference to Hildebrand appears in a passage dissecting the ideological emptiness of Rhodes's imperialism. The argument is devastating in its simplicity: "Cromwell stood for [[john-calvin|Calvin]]ism, Hildebrand for Catholicism: but Rhodes had no principles whatever to give to the world" (019-072). Hildebrand thus serves as a benchmark of principled action — however one judges his aims, they were at least coherent and articulable.

The historical Hildebrand was the architect of the Gregorian Reform, a sweeping program that asserted papal authority over secular rulers, enforced clerical celibacy, and combated simony. His clash with Emperor Henry IV at Canossa in 1077 became one of the defining confrontations between church and state in medieval Europe. Chesterton, a Catholic convert, would have known this history intimately.

## Cromwell and Hildebrand: A Parallel

The pairing of Cromwell and Hildebrand in the same sentence is revealing. Chesterton groups them as men who "made huge mistakes" and "committed great crimes" (019-069) — yet they possessed something Rhodes lacked: a definite [[dogma|creed]]. [[cromwell|Oliver Cromwell]] represented [[john-calvin|Calvin]]ism's militant, iconoclastic strain; Hildebrand represented the Catholic Church's claim to universal spiritual authority. Both were, in Chesterton's framing, men of conviction, however flawed.

This parallel also reflects Chesterton's broader philosophical habit of respecting ideological opponents who fought for a clear idea, while reserving his sharpest contempt for those who wield power without any idea at all. Rhodes, with his vague talk of "the fittest" and "survival," represented the latter category in Chesterton's taxonomy of power.

## Hildebrand in Chesterton's Anti-Imperialist Argument

The passage in which Hildebrand appears is part of a sustained attack on the colonial ideal of men like Rhodes. Chesterton argues that Rhodes's imperialism was "a fad, and like most fads an imitation" (019-067), lacking any genuine creative principle derived from Western civilization. The contrast with Hildebrand — a man who, whatever his faults, acted from a coherent theological and institutional vision — sharpens the indictment.

For Chesterton, the tragedy of modern empire was not its cruelty so much as its emptiness. The medieval pope and the Puritan revolutionary, for all their errors, belonged to traditions that could explain themselves. Rhodes belonged to a tradition — Darwinian materialism — that Chesterton considered not only stagnant but "poisonous" (019-076).
