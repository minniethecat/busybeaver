Cecil Rhodes (1853–1902) appears in Chesterton's "[[section-19|The Romantic In The Rain]]" as the central exhibit in a sustained critique of British [[imperialism|Imperialism]]. Chesterton's argument is not that Rhodes committed crimes or errors — many historical figures did — but that he committed them in service of ideas he did not possess. Rhodes becomes, in Chesterton's hands, the archetype of a peculiarly modern emptiness: a man who built an empire without ever articulating what it stood for (019-066–019-075).

## The Imperialist Without Ideas

Chesterton's opening charge against Rhodes is startlingly direct. What was wrong with Rhodes, he writes, was not "that, like Cromwell or Hildebrand, he made huge mistakes, nor even that he committed great crimes" (019-068–019-069). It was something more fundamental: "he committed these crimes and errors in order to spread certain ideas. And when one asked for the ideas they could not be found" (019-071–019-072).

The contrast with historical figures is deliberate and devastating. "Cromwell stood for [[john-calvin|Calvin]]ism, Hildebrand for Catholicism: but Rhodes had no principles whatever to give to the world. He had only a hasty but elaborate machinery for spreading the principles that he hadn't got" (019-072–019-075). Rhodes, in Chesterton's reading, was the ultimate imperial bureaucrat — all infrastructure, no content.

What Rhodes called his ideals were, according to Chesterton, "the dregs of a Darwinism which had already grown not only stagnant, but poisonous" (019-075–019-076). The survival of the fittest, the weakest to the wall — "that was the philosophy which he lumberingly believed through life, like many another agnostic old bachelor of the [[queen-victoria|Victoria]]n era" (019-079–019-081). Chesterton's tone here is almost pitying: "It was not his fault, poor fellow, that he called a high hill somewhere in South Africa 'his church'" (019-083–019-084).

## The Oriental Irony

The critique deepens into a paradox that Chesterton finds almost comically revealing. Not only did Rhodes have no Western ideas to spread — but "such ideas as it has are actually borrowed from the brown and black peoples to whom it seeks to extend them" (019-095–019-097).

The evidence Chesterton cites is Rhodes's own reported remark: "'It is inevitable fate that all this should be changed; and I should like to be the agent of fate'" (019-113–019-114). Chesterton pounces: "That was Cecil Rhodes's one small genuine idea; and it is an Oriental idea" (019-114–019-115).

The absurdity, as Chesterton frames it, is total. "Rhodes and Kitchener are to conquer Moslem bedouins and barbarians, in order to teach them to believe only in inevitable fate. We are to wreck provinces and pour blood like Niagara, all in order to teach a Turk to say 'Kismet'; which he has said since his cradle" (019-117–019-120). The Western idea — "the idea of spiritual liberty and danger, of a doubtful and romantic future in which all things may happen — this essential Western idea Cecil Rhodes could not spread, because (as he says himself) he did not believe in it" (019-127–019-130).

## The Sultan Archetype

Chesterton's final move is to recast Rhodes not as a Western empire-builder but as an Eastern despot. The Rhodes [[imperialism|Imperialism]] "set up not the King, but the Sultan; with all the typically Eastern ideas of the magic of money, of luxury without uproar; of prostrate provinces and a chosen race" (019-140–019-142).

The catalog of Sultan-like qualities is precise and cutting: "Indeed Cecil Rhodes illustrated almost every quality essential to the Sultan, from the love of diamonds to the scorn of woman" (019-143–019-144). Chesterton had earlier argued that a true King belongs to his people — "he was not merely a conqueror, but a father" (019-138). The Sultan, by contrast, merely owns them. Rhodes, in Chesterton's anatomy, was the Sultan dressed in a billycock hat.
