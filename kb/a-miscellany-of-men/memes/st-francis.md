# Saint Francis of Assisi

Saint Francis of Assisi (1181–1226) was an Italian Catholic friar and founder of the Franciscan [[order|Order]]. G.K. Chesterton references St. Francis across multiple essays to illustrate the distinctive character of Christian spirituality.

## Christian Mysticism

In [[section-16|The Separatist And Sacred Things]], Chesterton contrasts Christian mysticism with Eastern mysticism. The Christian mystic says, like St. Francis, "My brother fire and my sister water" — an ecstasy of creation, separation, and mutual surprise. (015-145–015-147)

The Eastern mystic, by contrast, says "Myself fire and myself water" — an ecstasy of unity that extends the self into everything or contracts everything into nothing. (015-148–015-150)

## Selfless Love

In [[section-23|The Divine Detective]], Chesterton invokes St. Francis as a model of saintly love that asks nothing in return. "I love all the cats in the street as St. Francis of Assisi loved all the birds in the wood or all the fishes in the sea." (022-155–022-156)

This love is heroic because it does not seek to possess or control. St. Francis did not wish to bridle a bird and ride on its back — he loved freely, without expectation of return. (022-157–022-159)

## Chesterton and Franciscan Spirituality

Chesterton's admiration for St. Francis ran deep — he later wrote a full biography of the saint. In these essays, Francis appears as Chesterton's touchstone for a distinctively Christian way of being in the world: neither absorbing creation into the self (Eastern Pantheism) nor dominating it (modern utilitarianism), but loving it as brother and sister.

This Franciscan vision of kinship with creation stands as an alternative both to the "Cosmic Unity" Chesterton critiques and to the commercial relationships that reduce everything to payment and profit.
