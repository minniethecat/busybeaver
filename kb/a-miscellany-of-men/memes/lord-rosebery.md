# Lord Rosebery

Archibald Primrose, 5th Earl of Rosebery (1847–1929), served briefly as British Prime Minister and remained an influential figure in Liberal politics. In Chesterton's political taxonomy, Rosebery represents the limit-case of [[democracy|democratic]] Whig statesmanship — a figure whose commitment to [[liberty|liberty]] was genuine but whose vision was bound to an [[section-15|aristocratic order]] that could not survive the democratic century. and remained an influential figure in Liberal politics. G.K. Chesterton references him twice in "A Miscellany of Men," each time using Rosebery's position as a foil for his own political arguments.

## The Party System Critique

In "The Man Who Thinks Backwards," Chesterton takes up the conventional critique of the British party system and turns it on its head. The "real evil of our Party System" had been "stated wrong by Lord Rosebery, when he said that it prevented the best men from devoting themselves to politics, and that it encouraged a fanatical conflict." (004-014–004-017)

Chesterton disagrees on both counts. He doubts "whether the best men ever would devote themselves to politics" — the best men, he suggests, devote themselves to "pigs and babies and things like that." (004-017–004-018) And as for fanatical conflict, Chesterton wishes "there was more of it." (004-020) For Chesterton, Rosebery's complaint revealed a deeper misunderstanding: the problem with the two-party system was not that it fostered too much passion but that it "unduly limit[ed] the outlook of the ordinary citizen." (004-021)

## The Eighteenth-Century Preference

In "The Separatist And Sacred Things," Chesterton returns to Rosebery in a sweeping survey of competing nostalgias. Just as Dr. Horton "probably really regrets the old passion that excommunicated Rome" and [[hilaire-belloc|Hilaire Belloc]] "would really prefer the Middle Ages," so "Lord Rosebery would prefer the Erastian oligarchy of the eighteenth century." (015-050–015-053)

Rosebery thus stands in Chesterton's taxonomy for a particular kind of Whig nostalgia — a preference for the measured, aristocratic, latitudinarian governance of the Georgian era, in which the state managed the church and gentlemen managed the state. Chesterton groups Rosebery with other sincere men whose political visions, however genuine, cannot be realized — "none of these sincere men can win." (015-057–015-058)

## The Limits of Whig Statesmanship

Rosebery embodied a style of Liberal statesmanship that was already passing in Chesterton's time. [[wealth|Wealth]]y, cultured, and intellectually restless, Rosebery won the Derby twice while serving in high office — a combination unimaginable in later British politics. But his brief, unhappy premiership demonstrated the limits of the aristocratic Liberal tradition.

Chesterton's treatment of Rosebery is characteristic of his method: he treats political figures not as individual actors but as representatives of philosophical positions. Rosebery stands for the reasonable, moderate, Whig view of politics — and Chesterton's point is that reasonableness and moderation, however admirable, are not enough.
