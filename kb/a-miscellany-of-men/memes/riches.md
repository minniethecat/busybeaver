# Riches

Chesterton's treatment of riches is governed by a single sharp distinction: between the old love of money as a beautiful thing and the modern worship of wealth as an abstraction. His quarrel is not with riches as such — he is too Catholic for that — but with the peculiar thinness of the modern relation to them.

## The Idolater of Riches and the Old Miser

In "The Miser And His Friends," Chesterton draws a surprising contrast between the traditional miser and the modern millionaire. The old miser, he argues, at least loved something tangible: "Gold is but one kind of coloured clay, but coloured clay can be very beautiful. The modern idolater of riches is content with far less genuine things" (013-129). The miser who hoarded coins had a sensory relation to his wealth — "the glitter of guineas is like the glitter of buttercups, the chink of pelf is like the chime of bells." The modern rich man, by contrast, deals in "dreary papers and dead calculations," in "the mere repetition of noughts in a ledger, all as like each other as eggs to eggs." Chesterton's point is paradoxical but characteristic: the miser's sin was at least a human sin, attached to colour and sound and touch. The modern capitalist's relation to wealth is an abstraction twice removed — not even paper money, but ledger entries — and therefore less human, less vivid, less capable of being loved or renounced.

## Riches Measured Against the East

In "The Separatist And Sacred Things," "riches" appears in a different register — as a figure for spiritual depth rather than material accumulation. Chesterton writes that "all that is richest, deepest, and subtlest in the East is rooted in Pantheism; but all that is richest, deepest, and subtlest in us is concerned with denying passionately that Pantheism is either the highest or the purest religion" (015-091). Here "richest" is a measure of intellectual and spiritual substance, not coin. Chesterton is willing to concede the depth of Eastern thought — the adjective "richest" grants real value — while insisting that the West's deepest achievement is its rejection of the pantheist dissolution. The word functions in two economies at once: the material economy of gold and ledgers, and the spiritual economy of depth and thinness. Chesterton's charge against modernity is that it has impoverished both.
