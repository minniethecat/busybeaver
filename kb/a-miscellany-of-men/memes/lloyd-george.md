David Lloyd George (1863–1945) appears in two essays in "A Miscellany of Men," each time as an example in an argument about something other than Lloyd George himself. In "[[section-17|The Aristocratic 'Arry]]", Chesterton uses him to illustrate a pattern of misdirected accusation. In "[[section-22|The Other Kind Of Man]]", he becomes the test case for a claim about how modern societies have lost the idea of repentance in public life. In both instances, Lloyd George functions not as a subject of analysis but as a familiar public figure whose career illuminates a larger cultural condition.

## The Wrong Accusation

Chesterton's argument in "The Aristocratic 'Arry" is that modern public discourse consistently misfires. "People seem to have a positive inspiration for finding the inappropriate phrase to apply to an offender; they are always accusing a man of theft when he has been convicted of murder" (017-083–017-085). The examples accumulate: [[edward-carson|Sir Edward Carson]] is accused of rebellion when his sin was submission (017-086–017-087).

Lloyd George enters as the second exhibit. "They must describe Mr. Lloyd George as using his eloquence to rouse the mob, whereas he has really shown considerable cleverness in damping it down" (017-088–017-090). The observation is characteristically Chestertonian: the charge against Lloyd George is not that it is false but that it is the *wrong* charge — it misses what is actually happening in favour of a more dramatic but less accurate narrative. The same impulse, Chesterton notes, "denounced Dr. Inge as 'the Gloomy Dean'" (017-091–017-092) — another mislabel, though Chesterton grants that being a Dean and being gloomy are each in themselves unobjectionable (017-093–017-094).

## The Man Who Cannot Repent

In "[[section-22|The Other Kind Of Man]]", Chesterton constructs a more ambitious argument about modernity and repentance. He contrasts the medieval attitude — in which a public man might suddenly give all his goods to the poor, embrace lepers, and be canonised — with the modern attitude, in which such transformations are literally unthinkable (022-015–022-020).

Lloyd George becomes the demonstration. "For we, whatever our political opinions, certainly never think of our public men like that. If we hold the opinion that Mr. Lloyd George is a noble tribune of the populace and protector of the poor, we do not admit that he can ever have paltered with the truth or bargained with the powerful. If we hold the equally idiotic opinion that he is a red and rabid Socialist, maddening mobs into mutiny and theft, then we expect him to go on maddening them — and us" (022-021–022-027).

The crucial line follows: "We do not expect him, let us say, suddenly to go into a monastery" (022-027–022-028). The loss Chesterton diagnoses is the loss of the idea of repentance — "especially in public things; that is why we cannot really get rid of our great national abuses of economic tyranny and aristocratic avarice" (022-029–022-031). Lloyd George, whatever one thinks of his politics, is trapped in a frame that permits no moral drama — only the dreary continuity of being "moved on by the police" (022-032–022-033).
