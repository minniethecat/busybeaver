# The Separatist And Sacred Things

[015-001]That contrast is the measure, not only between two nations, but between

[015-002]two modes of historical construction and development. For there is not

[015-003]really very much difference, as European history goes, in the time that

[015-004]has elapsed between us and the Jacobite and between us and the Jacobin.

[015-005]When George III was crowned the gauntlet of the King's Champion was

[015-006]picked up by a partisan of the Stuarts. When George III was still on the

[015-007]throne the Bourbons were driven out of France as the Stuarts had been

[015-008]driven out of England. Yet the French are just sufficiently aware that

[015-009]the Bourbons might possibly return that they will take a little trouble

[015-010]to discourage it; whereas we are so certain that the Stuarts will never

[015-011]return that we actually play their most passionate tunes as a compliment

[015-012]to their rivals. And we do not even do it tauntingly. I examined the

[015-013]faces of all the bandsmen; and I am sure they were devoid of irony:

[015-014]indeed, it is difficult to blow a wind instrument ironically. We do it

[015-015]quite unconsciously; because we have a huge fundamental dogma, which the

[015-016]French have not. We really believe that the past is past. It is a very

[015-017]doubtful point.

[015-018]Now the great gift of a revolution (as in France) is that it makes men

[015-019]free in the past as well as free in the future. Those who have cleared

[015-020]away everything could, if they liked, put back everything. But we who

[015-021]have preserved everything--we cannot restore anything. Take,

[015-022]for the sake of argument, the complex and many coloured ritual of the

[015-023]Coronation recently completed. That rite is stratified with the separate

[015-024]centuries; from the first rude need of discipline to the last fine shade

[015-025]of culture or corruption, there is nothing that cannot be detected or

[015-026]even dated. The fierce and childish vow of the lords to serve their lord

[015-027]“against all manner of folk” obviously comes from the real Dark Ages;

[015-028]no longer confused, even by the ignorant, with the Middle Ages. It comes

[015-029]from some chaos of Europe, when there was one old Roman road across four

[015-030]of our counties; and when hostile “folk” might live in the next village.

[015-031]The sacramental separation of one man to be the friend of the fatherless

[015-032]and the nameless belongs to the true Middle Ages; with their great

[015-033]attempt to make a moral and invisible Roman Empire; or (as the

[015-034]Coronation Service says) to set the cross for ever above the ball.

[015-035]Elaborate local tomfooleries, such as that by which the Lord of the

[015-036]Manor of Work-sop is alone allowed to do something or other, these

[015-037]probably belong to the decay of the Middle Ages, when that great

[015-038]civilisation died out in grotesque literalism and entangled heraldry.

[015-039]Things like the presentation of the Bible bear witness to the

[015-040]intellectual outburst at the Reformation; things like the Declaration

[015-041]against the Mass bear witness to the great wars of the Puritans; and

[015-042]things like the allegiance of the Bishops bear witness to the wordy and

[015-043]parenthetical political compromises which (to my deep regret) ended the

[015-044]wars of religion.

[015-045]But my purpose here is only to point out one particular thing. In all

[015-046]that long list of variations there must be, and there are, things

[015-047]which energetic modern minds would really wish, with the reasonable

[015-048]modification, to restore. Dr. Clifford would probably be glad to see

[015-049]again the great Puritan idealism that forced the Bible into an antique

[015-050]and almost frozen formality. Dr. Horton probably really regrets the

[015-051]old passion that excommunicated Rome. In the same way Mr. Belloc

[015-052]would really prefer the Middle Ages; as Lord Rosebery would prefer

[015-053]the Erastian oligarchy of the eighteenth century. The Dark Ages would

[015-054]probably be disputed (from widely different motives) by Mr. Rudyard

[015-055]Kipling and Mr. Cunninghame Graham. But Mr. Cunninghame Graham would

[015-056]win.

[015-057]But the black case against Conservative (or Evolutionary) politics is

[015-058]that none of these sincere men can win. Dr. Clifford cannot get back

[015-059]to the Puritans; Mr. Belloc cannot get back to the mediævals; because

[015-060](alas) there has been no Revolution to leave them a clear space for

[015-061]building or rebuilding. Frenchmen have all the ages behind them, and can

[015-062]wander back and pick and choose. But Englishmen have all the ages on top

[015-063]of them, and can only lie groaning under that imposing tower, without

[015-064]being able to take so much as a brick out of it. If the French decide

[015-065]that their Republic is bad they can get rid of it; but if we decide that

[015-066]a Republic was good, we should have much more difficulty. If the French

[015-067]democracy actually desired every detail of the mediæval monarchy, they

[015-068]could have it. I do not think they will or should, but they could. If

[015-069]another Dauphin were actually crowned at Rheims; if another Joan of Arc

[015-070]actually bore a miraculous banner before him; if mediæval swords shook

[015-071]and blazed in every gauntlet; if the golden lilies glowed from every

[015-072]tapestry; if this were really proved to be the will of France and the

[015-073]purpose of Providence--such a scene would still be the lasting and

[015-074]final justification of the French Revolution.

[015-075]For no such scene could conceivably have happened under Louis XVI.

[015-076]THE SEPARATIST AND SACRED THINGS

[015-077]In the very laudable and fascinating extensions of our interest in

[015-078]Asiatic arts or faiths, there are two incidental injustices which we

[015-079]tend nowadays to do to our own records and our own religion. The first

[015-080]is a tendency to talk as if certain things were not only present in the

[015-081]higher Orientals, but were peculiar to them. Thus our magazines will

[015-082]fall into a habit of wondering praise of Bushido, the Japanese chivalry,

[015-083]as if no Western knights had ever vowed noble vows, or as if no Eastern

[015-084]knights had ever broken them. Or again, our drawing-rooms will be full

[015-085]of the praises of Indian renunciation and Indian unworldliness, as if no

[015-086]Christians had been saints, or as if all Buddhists had been. But if the

[015-087]first injustice is to think of human virtues as peculiarly Eastern, the

[015-088]other injustice is a failure to appreciate what really is peculiarly

[015-089]Eastern. It is too much taken for granted that the Eastern sort of

[015-090]idealism is certainly superior and convincing; whereas in truth it is

[015-091]only separate and peculiar. All that is richest, deepest, and subtlest

[015-092]in the East is rooted in Pantheism; but all that is richest, deepest,

[015-093]and subtlest in us is concerned with denying passionately that Pantheism

[015-094]is either the highest or the purest religion.

[015-095]Thus, in turning over some excellent books recently written on the

[015-096]spirit of Indian or Chinese art and decoration, I found it quietly and

[015-097]curiously assumed that the artist must be at his best if he flows with

[015-098]the full stream of Nature; and identifies himself with all things; so

[015-099]that the stars are his sleepless eyes and the forests his far-flung

[015-100]arms. Now in this way of talking both the two injustices will be found.

[015-101]In so far as what is claimed is a strong sense of the divine in all

[015-102]things, the Eastern artists have no more monopoly of it than they have

[015-103]of hunger and thirst.

[015-104]I have no doubt that the painters and poets of the Far East do exhibit

[015-105]this; but I rebel at being asked to admit that we must go to the Far

[015-106]East to find it. Traces of such sentiments can be found, I fancy, even

[015-107]in other painters and poets. I do not question that the poet Wo Wo (that

[015-108]ornament of the eighth dynasty) may have written the words: “Even the

[015-109]most undignified vegetable is for this person capable of producing

[015-110]meditations not to be exhibited by much weeping.” But, I do not

[015-111]therefore admit that a Western gentleman named Wordsworth (who made

[015-112]a somewhat similar remark) had plagiarised from Wo Wo, or was a mere

[015-113]Occidental fable and travesty of that celebrated figure. I do not deny

[015-114]that Tinishona wrote that exquisite example of the short Japanese poem

[015-115]entitled “Honourable Chrysanthemum in Honourable Hole in Wall.” But I do

[015-116]not therefore admit that Tennyson's little verse about the flower in the

[015-117]cranny was not original and even sincere.

[015-118]It is recorded (for all I know) of the philanthropic Emperor Bo, that

[015-119]when engaged in cutting his garden lawn with a mower made of alabaster

[015-120]and chrysoberyl, he chanced to cut down a small flower; whereupon, being

[015-121]much affected, he commanded his wise men immediately to take down upon

[015-122]tablets of ivory the lines beginning: “Small and unobtrusive blossom

[015-123]with ruby extremities.” But this incident, touching as it is, does not

[015-124]shake my belief in the incident of Robert Burns and the daisy; and I am

[015-125]left with an impression that poets are pretty much the same everywhere

[015-126]in their poetry--and in their prose.

[015-127]I have tried to convey my sympathy and admiration for Eastern art and

[015-128]its admirers, and if I have not conveyed them I must give it up and go

[015-129]on to more general considerations. I therefore proceed to say--with

[015-130]the utmost respect, that it is Cheek, a rarefied and etherealised form

[015-131]of Cheek, for this school to speak in this way about the mother that

[015-132]bore them, the great civilisation of the West. The West also has its

[015-133]magic landscapes, only through our incurable materialism they look

[015-134]like landscapes as well as like magic. The West also has its symbolic

[015-135]figures, only they look like men as well as symbols. It will be answered

[015-136](and most justly) that Oriental art ought to be free to follow its own

[015-137]instinct and tradition; that its artists are concerned to suggest one

[015-138]thing and our artists another; that both should be admired in their

[015-139]difference. Profoundly true; but what is the difference? It is certainly

[015-140]not as the Orientalisers assert, that we must go to the Far East for a

[015-141]sympathetic and transcendental interpretation of Nature. We have paid

[015-142]a long enough toll of mystics and even of madmen to be quit of that

[015-143]disability.

[015-144]Yet there is a difference, and it is just what I suggested. The Eastern

[015-145]mysticism is an ecstasy of unity; the Christian mysticism is an ecstasy

[015-146]of creation, that is of separation and mutual surprise. The latter says,

[015-147]like St. Francis, “My brother fire and my sister water”; the former

[015-148]says, “Myself fire and myself water.” Whether you call the Eastern

[015-149]attitude an extension of oneself into everything or a contraction of

[015-150]oneself into nothing is a matter of metaphysical definition. The

[015-151]effect is the same, an effect which lives and throbs throughout all the

[015-152]exquisite arts of the East. This effect is the Sing called rhythm, a

[015-153]pulsation of pattern, or of ritual, or of colours, or of cosmic theory,

[015-154]but always suggesting the unification of the individual with the world.

[015-155]But there is quite another kind of sympathy the sympathy with a

[015-156]thing because it is different. No one will say that Rembrandt did not

[015-157]sympathise with an old woman; but no one will say that Rembrandt painted

[015-158]like an old woman. No one will say that Reynolds did not appreciate

[015-159]children; but no one will say he did it childishly. The supreme instance

[015-160]of this divine division is sex, and that explains (what I could never

[015-161]understand in my youth) why Christendom called the soul the bride of

[015-162]God. For real love is an intense realisation of the “separateness” of

[015-163]all our souls. The most heroic and human love-poetry of the world is

[015-164]never mere passion; precisely because mere passion really is a melting

[015-165]back into Nature, a meeting of the waters. And water is plunging

[015-166]and powerful; but it is only powerful downhill. The high and human

[015-167]love-poetry is all about division rather than identity; and in the great

[015-168]love-poems even the man as he embraces the woman sees her, in the same

[015-169]instant, afar off; a virgin and a stranger.
