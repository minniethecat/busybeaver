# King Arthur

King Arthur is the legendary king of Britain, central to the Arthurian literary tradition. In [[section-17|The Aristocratic 'Arry]], G.K. Chesterton contrasts the solemn royalism of Tennyson with the irreverent vitality of older folk ballads.

## Tennyson vs the Folk Ballad

Chesterton notes that Tennyson's depiction of Arthur — who "drew all the petty princedoms under him" and "made a realm and ruled" — reflects a quite modern, serious Royalism. (016-118–016-120)

By contrast, the older folk ballad runs: "When good King Arthur ruled this land / He was a goodly King — / He stole three pecks of barley-meal / To make a bag-pudding." (016-123–016-126)

## The Living King

For Chesterton, this irreverent ballad is "far more Arthurian than anything in The Idylls of the King." (016-127) The folk tradition captures a living, human king — capable of mischief and absurdity — rather than a solemn icon of perfect rule. The ballad's Arthur is real in a way that Tennyson's idealised monarch is not.

## Medieval Monarchy and Folk Culture

Chesterton's comparison reflects his broader argument about medieval culture. Many medieval people "believed in monarchy as solemnly as Tennyson," but their folk culture retained space for irreverence toward authority figures. (016-120–016-121)

This combination of reverence and irreverence, Chesterton suggests, is more authentically medieval — and more human — than the uniform solemnity of [[queen-victoria|Victoria]]n Arthurianism.
