# The Poet and the Cheese

[002-001]Thinking backwards is not quite easy to define abstractedly; and,

[002-002]perhaps, the simplest method is to take some object, as plain as

[002-003]possible, and from it illustrate the two modes of thought: the right

[002-004]mode in which all real results have been rooted; the wrong mode, which

[002-005]is confusing all our current discussions, especially our discussions

[002-006]about the relations of the sexes. Casting my eye round the room, I

[002-007]notice an object which is often mentioned in the higher and subtler of

[002-008]these debates about the sexes: I mean a poker. I will take a poker and

[002-009]think about it; first forwards and then backwards; and so, perhaps, show

[002-010]what I mean.

[002-011]The sage desiring to think well and wisely about a poker will begin

[002-012]somewhat as follows: Among the live creatures that crawl about this star

[002-013]the queerest is the thing called Man. This plucked and plumeless bird,

[002-014]comic and forlorn, is the butt of all the philosophies. He is the only

[002-015]naked animal; and this quality, once, it is said, his glory, is now his

[002-016]shame. He has to go outside himself for everything that he wants. He

[002-017]might almost be considered as an absent-minded person who had gone

[002-018]bathing and left his clothes everywhere, so that he has hung his hat

[002-019]upon the beaver and his coat upon the sheep. The rabbit has white warmth

[002-020]for a waistcoat, and the glow-worm has a lantern for a head. But man has

[002-021]no heat in his hide, and the light in his body is darkness; and he must

[002-022]look for light and warmth in the wild, cold universe in which he is

[002-023]cast. This is equally true of his soul and of his body; he is the one

[002-024]creature that has lost his heart as much as he has lost his hide. In a

[002-025]spiritual sense he has taken leave of his senses; and even in a literal

[002-026]sense he has been unable to keep his hair on. And just as this external

[002-027]need of his has lit in his dark brain the dreadful star called religion,

[002-028]so it has lit in his hand the only adequate symbol of it: I mean the red

[002-029]flower called Fire. Fire, the most magic and startling of all material

[002-030]things, is a thing known only to man and the expression of his sublime

[002-031]externalism. It embodies all that is human in his hearths and all that

[002-032]is divine on his altars. It is the most human thing in the world; seen

[002-033]across wastes of marsh or medleys of forest, it is veritably the purple

[002-034]and golden flag of the sons of Eve. But there is about this generous and

[002-035]rejoicing thing an alien and awful quality: the quality of torture. Its

[002-036]presence is life; its touch is death. Therefore, it is always necessary

[002-037]to have an intermediary between ourselves and this dreadful deity; to

[002-038]have a priest to intercede for us with the god of life and death; to

[002-039]send an ambassador to the fire. That priest is the poker. Made of

[002-040]a material more merciless and warlike than the other instruments of

[002-041]domesticity, hammered on the anvil and born itself in the flame, the

[002-042]poker is strong enough to enter the burning fiery furnace, and, like

[002-043]the holy children, not be consumed. In this heroic service it is often

[002-044]battered and twisted, but is the more honourable for it, like any other

[002-045]soldier who has been under fire.

[002-046]Now all this may sound very fanciful and mystical, but it is the right

[002-047]view of pokers, and no one who takes it will ever go in for any wrong

[002-048]view of pokers, such as using them to beat one's wife or torture one's

[002-049]children, or even (though that is more excusable) to make a policeman

[002-050]jump, as the clown does in the pantomime. He who has thus gone back to

[002-051]the beginning, and seen everything as quaint and new, will always see

[002-052]things in their right order, the one depending on the other in degree of

[002-053]purpose and importance: the poker for the fire and the fire for the man

[002-054]and the man for the glory of God.

[002-055]This is thinking forwards. Now our modern discussions about everything,

[002-056]Imperialism, Socialism, or Votes for Women, are all entangled in

[002-057]an opposite train of thought, which runs as follows:--A modern

[002-058]intellectual comes in and sees a poker. He is a positivist; he will not

[002-059]begin with any dogmas about the nature of man, or any day-dreams about

[002-060]the mystery of fire. He will begin with what he can see, the poker; and

[002-061]the first thing he sees about the poker is that it is crooked. He says,

[002-062]“Poor poker; it's crooked.” Then he asks how it came to be crooked; and

[002-063]is told that there is a thing in the world (with which his temperament

[002-064]has hitherto left him unacquainted)--a thing called fire. He points

[002-065]out, very kindly and clearly, how silly it is of people, if they want

[002-066]a straight poker, to put it into a chemical combustion which will very

[002-067]probably heat and warp it. “Let us abolish fire,” he says, “and then

[002-068]we shall have perfectly straight pokers. Why should you want a fire

[002-069]at all?” They explain to him that a creature called Man wants a fire,

[002-070]because he has no fur or feathers. He gazes dreamily at the embers for

[002-071]a few seconds, and then shakes his head. “I doubt if such an animal is

[002-072]worth preserving,” he says. “He must eventually go under in the cosmic

[002-073]struggle when pitted against well-armoured and warmly protected species,

[002-074]who have wings and trunks and spires and scales and horns and shaggy

[002-075]hair. If Man cannot live without these luxuries, you had better abolish

[002-076]Man.” At this point, as a rule, the crowd is convinced; it heaves up all

[002-077]its clubs and axes, and abolishes him. At least, one of him.

[002-078]Before we begin discussing our various new plans for the people's

[002-079]welfare, let us make a kind of agreement that we will argue in a

[002-080]straightforward way, and not in a tail-foremost way. The typical modern

[002-081]movements may be right; but let them be defended because they are right,

[002-082]not because they are typical modern movements. Let us begin with the

[002-083]actual woman or man in the street, who is cold; like mankind before the

[002-084]finding of fire. Do not let us begin with the end of the last red-hot

[002-085]discussion--like the end of a red hot poker. Imperialism may be

[002-086]right. But if it is right, it is right because England has some divine

[002-087]authority like Israel, or some human authority like Rome; not because we

[002-088]have saddled ourselves with South Africa, and don't know how to get rid

[002-089]of it. Socialism may be true. But if it is true, it is true because the

[002-090]tribe or the city can really declare all land to be common land, not

[002-091]because Harrod's Stores exist and the commonwealth must copy them.

[002-092]Female suffrage may be just. But if it is just, it is just because women

[002-093]are women, not because women are sweated workers and white slaves and

[002-094]all sorts of things that they ought never to have been. Let not the

[002-095]Imperialist accept a colony because it is there, nor the Suffragist

[002-096]seize a vote because it is lying about, nor the Socialist buy up an

[002-097]industry merely because it is for sale.

[002-098]Let us ask ourselves first what we really do want, not what recent legal

[002-099]decisions have told us to want, or recent logical philosophies proved

[002-100]that we must want, or recent social prophecies predicted that we shall

[002-101]some day want. If there must be a British Empire, let it be British, and

[002-102]not, in mere panic, American or Prussian. If there ought to be female

[002-103]suffrage, let it be female, and not a mere imitation as coarse as

[002-104]the male blackguard or as dull as the male clerk. If there is to be

[002-105]Socialism, let it be social; that is, as different as possible from all

[002-106]the big commercial departments of to-day. The really good journeyman

[002-107]tailor does not cut his coat according to his cloth; he asks for more

[002-108]cloth. The really practical statesman does not fit himself to existing

[002-109]conditions, he denounces the conditions as unfit. History is like some

[002-110]deeply planted tree which, though gigantic in girth, tapers away at

[002-111]last into tiny twigs; and we are in the topmost branches. Each of us is

[002-112]trying to bend the tree by a twig: to alter England through a distant

[002-113]colony, or to capture the State through a small State department, or to

[002-114]destroy all voting through a vote. In all such bewilderment he is wise

[002-115]who resists this temptation of trivial triumph or surrender, and happy

[002-116](in an echo of the Roman poet) who remembers the roots of things.

[002-117]THE NAMELESS MAN

[002-118]There are only two forms of government—the monarchy or personal

[002-119]government, and the republic or impersonal government. England is not a

[002-120]government; England is an anarchy, because there are so many kings.

[002-121]But there is one real advantage (among many real disadvantages) in the

[002-122]method of abstract democracy, and that is this: that under impersonal

[002-123]government politics are so much more personal. In France and America,

[002-124]where the State is an abstraction, political argument is quite full

[002-125]of human details--some might even say of inhuman details. But in

[002-126]England, precisely because we are ruled by personages, these personages

[002-127]do not permit personalities. In England names are honoured, and

[002-128]therefore names are suppressed. But in the republics, in France

[002-129]especially, a man can put his enemies' names into his article and his

[002-130]own name at the end of it.

[002-131]This is the essential condition of such candour. If we merely made our

[002-132]anonymous articles more violent, we should be baser than we are now. We

[002-133]should only be arming masked men with daggers instead of cudgels. And I,

[002-134]for one, have always believed in the more general signing of articles,

[002-135]and have signed my own articles on many occasions when, heaven knows,

[002-136]I had little reason to be vain of them. I have heard many arguments for

[002-137]anonymity; but they all seem to amount to the statement that anonymity

[002-138]is safe, which is just what I complain of. In matters of truth the fact

[002-139]that you don't want to publish something is, nine times out of ten, a

[002-140]proof that you ought to publish it.

[002-141]But there is one answer to my perpetual plea for a man putting his name

[002-142]to his writing. There is one answer, and there is only one answer, and

[002-143]it is never given. It is that in the modern complexity very often a

[002-144]man's name is almost as false as his pseudonym. The prominent person

[002-145]today is eternally trying to lose a name, and to get a title. For

[002-146]instance, we all read with earnestness and patience the pages of the

[002-147]'Daily Mail', and there are times when we feel moved to cry, “Bring to

[002-148]us the man who thought these strange thoughts! Pursue him, capture

[002-149]him, take great care of him. Bring him back to us tenderly, like some

[002-150]precious bale of silk, that we may look upon the face of the man who

[002-151]desires such things to be printed. Let us know his name; his social

[002-152]and medical pedigree.” But in the modern muddle (it might be said)

[002-153]how little should we gain if those frankly fatuous sheets were indeed

[002-154]subscribed by the man who had inspired them. Suppose that after every

[002-155]article stating that the Premier is a piratical Socialist there were

[002-156]printed the simple word “Northcliffe.” What does that simple word

[002-157]suggest to the simple soul? To my simple soul (uninstructed otherwise)

[002-158]it suggests a lofty and lonely crag somewhere in the wintry seas towards

[002-159]the Orkheys or Norway; and barely clinging to the top of this crag the

[002-160]fortress of some forgotten chieftain. As it happens, of course, I

[002-161]know that the word does not mean this; it means another Fleet Street

[002-162]journalist like myself or only different from myself in so far as he has

[002-163]sought to secure money while I have sought to secure a jolly time.
