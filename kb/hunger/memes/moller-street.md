# Möller Street

## Overview

Möller Street (Norwegian: Möllergaden or Møllergaten) is a street in [[christiania|Christiania]] that appears in Part II of *[[hunger|Hunger]]* as the site of an eating-house whose roasting meat arrests [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] mid-stride — a moment of acute sensory temptation that crystallizes his exclusion from the city's ordinary commerce of sustenance. The street is also referenced as the location where he once applied for work as a wood-chopper.

## In the Narrative

The narrator encounters Möller Street at a moment of particular physical vulnerability. After being rebuffed by a woman who has "nothing for me to-day" (002-095), his hunger sharpens to the point where his senses become preternaturally acute:

> I halted outside an eating-house in Möller Street, and sniffed the fresh smell of meat roasting inside; my hand was already upon the door-handle, and I was on the point of entering without any fixed purpose, when I bethought myself in time, and left the spot. (002-097)

The image is powerful in its precision — his hand already on the handle, the smell of roasting meat, the "without any fixed purpose" that acknowledges the irrationality of entering a place he cannot afford. His self-restraint in turning away is one of the novel's quiet heroisms: a victory of dignity over desperation that costs him physically.

Later, in a moment of bitter self-examination, the narrator recalls: "Had I not, in sooth, even applied for a place as wood-chopper in Möllergaden in order to earn my daily bread?" (002-107). The street thus represents both temptation (the eating-house) and the narrator's sporadic, unsuccessful attempts at conventional employment. That he applies to chop wood — manual labor wholly unsuited to his temperament and physical condition — underscores the desperation that drives him even to Möller Street's more honest possibilities.

## Symbolic Significance

Möller Street functions as a threshold — a liminal space between the narrator's hunger and the city's abundance. The eating-house door, with his hand upon the handle, is the novel in miniature: the narrator perpetually on the verge of entering, perpetually withdrawing. The street's name — Möller, meaning miller — carries an ironic resonance: the miller grinds grain into flour, transforms raw material into sustenance, yet the narrator can access none of this transformation. He is as close to food as it is possible to be — his hand on the handle — and as far from it as starvation.
