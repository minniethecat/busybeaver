Artistic creation — the process of making literary art — is both a theme and a formal principle in Knut Hamsun's *Hunger*.

## Writing as Survival

The narrator is a writer by identity if not by income. His attempts to sell feuilletons and articles to Kristiania newspapers provide the novel's economic subplot. Writing is literally a survival strategy: the sale of a piece means food and lodging; its rejection means continued hunger. The marketplace for literary production is rendered with bitter specificity.

## Writing as Self-Creation

Beyond economic necessity, writing is the narrator's mode of self-creation. He invents words ("Kuboaa"), elaborates philosophical systems, and crafts narratives — including, crucially, the narrative of his own life. The novel blurs the line between the narrator as experiencer and the narrator as writer, suggesting that consciousness itself is a kind of ongoing composition.

## The Fragment as Form

The narrator's creative output is characteristically fragmentary: a feuilleton here, an article idea there, a philosophical sketch abandoned halfway. This fragmentation mirrors both his psychological state and the novel's own modernist form — the rejection of completed, polished artifact in favor of process and incompletion.

## PN References
Throughout the novel; the narrator's identity as a writer and his creative efforts are continuous themes.
