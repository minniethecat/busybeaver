# Pride

## Overview

**Pride** is one of the most defining psychological traits of the unnamed narrator in [[knut-hamsun|Knut Hamsun]]'s *Hunger* (1890). It functions as both a defense mechanism against the degradation of poverty and a self-destructive force that prevents [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] from accepting help. Throughout the novel, pride operates in tension with shame, hunger, and despair, creating the volatile psychological state that drives the narrative.

## In the Narrative

Pride first appears as a barrier to [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s basic survival. When he is unable to pay his rent, his "pride forbade my returning to my lodging" (001-214), compelling him to wander the streets rather than face his [[the-landlady|landlady]]'s judgment. This decision, while preserving a fragile sense of dignity, exposes him to greater physical suffering.

The narrator's pride is frequently self-aware — he recognizes its irrationality but cannot overcome it. During a confrontation with his [[the-landlady|landlady]], he admonishes himself: "No untimely pride! Brave it out!" (004-064). Yet the very act of giving himself this instruction reveals how deeply pride shapes his behavior. Even his creative efforts are marked by the desire to produce words that "swell with pride and intolerance" (004-081), suggesting that pride is not merely a social defense but an integral part of his artistic identity.

A fleeting moment in Part II captures [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s ambivalence toward pride: when a stranger offers a kind interpretation of something he has written, he "suddenly felt a little spark of pride" (002-215). This spark is rare — a glimpse of positive self-regard amidst the relentless self-laceration that otherwise characterizes his inner life.

By Part IV, [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s relationship with pride has become explicitly mortal. He reflects that "one might die of too much pride" (004-118), recognizing that the very quality that sustains his sense of self is also capable of destroying him. This paradox — pride as both life-preserving and life-threatening — is central to [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s psychological portrait.

## Literary Context

[[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s treatment of pride reflects the Nietzschean and individualistic currents of late nineteenth-century Scandinavian thought. The narrator's pride is not the aristocratic self-assurance of a traditional hero but a desperate, almost pathological assertion of selfhood in conditions that systematically deny it. The preface describes [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]] as "an individualistic romanticist" whose heroes display "violent, defiant deviation from everything average and ordinary" (P04-003). The narrator's pride is precisely this: a rebellion against the degrading sameness that poverty imposes.
