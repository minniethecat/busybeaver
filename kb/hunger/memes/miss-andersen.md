Miss Andersen is a minor but symbolically significant figure in the novel. Her advertisement for "winding-sheets" (burial shrouds) is visible from the narrator's attic window and becomes a recurring visual fixture in his impoverished surroundings.

## The Winding-Sheet Advertisement

The narrator first notices the advertisement on waking: "I could even make out the grinning lean letters of 'winding-sheets to be had at Miss Andersen's' on the right of it. That occupied me for a long while" [001-003]. The skeletal lettering of the advertisement mirrors the narrator's own emaciated condition — just as he wastes away from hunger, the lettering of the advertisement seems to take on the character of a death's-head, grinning at him from the wall.

The winding-sheet advertisement appears again when the narrator's hunger and desperation intensify. The "skeleton-like letters of Miss Andersen's winding-sheet advertisement" serve as a constant reminder of mortality [001-005], reinforcing the novel's themes of bodily decay and the proximity of death. The narrator cannot escape the advertisement's morbid presence — it is among the first things he sees each morning and accompanies his daily struggle for survival.

## Symbolic Function

Miss Andersen herself never appears directly in the narrative. She exists only through her advertisement, functioning as an absent presence whose commercial message about burial shrouds casts a funereal shadow over the narrator's daily struggle for survival. This technique — a character evoked solely through a material trace — is characteristic of Hamsun's narrative method, where objects and signs often carry more weight than the people who produce them.

The advertisement's content is particularly pointed: winding-sheets are the cloths used to wrap the dead for burial. That this commercial message should be the narrator's constant visual companion in his attic room suggests the proximity of death to his existence. It also introduces an ironic counterpoint: while the narrator struggles to stay alive through finding food, the advertisement above him quietly offers the implements of death.

## Narrative Technique

The use of Miss Andersen exemplifies an important aspect of Hamsun's literary method in *Hunger*: the deployment of minor characters not as developed figures but as fragments of the city's commercial and social life, glimpsed through the narrator's highly subjective and often distorted perception. These fragmentary presences — the landlady, the pawnbroker, the editor, Miss Andersen — collectively construct the texture of Christiania as a city of fleeting encounters and incomplete knowledge.
