Mark Twain (1835–1910), born Samuel Langhorne Clemens, was an American writer, humorist, and lecturer. His novels *The Adventures of Tom Sawyer* (1876) and *Adventures of Huckleberry Finn* (1884) are landmarks of American literature.

## Twain and Hamsun

In his introduction to *Hunger*, Edwin Björkman quotes an unnamed critic's assessment of Hamsun's second novel, *Mysteries* (1892): "in its abrupt swingings from laughter to tears, from irreverence to awe, from the ridiculous to the sublime, one finds the spirits of Dostoyevski and Mark Twain blended." This apparently incongruous pairing captures the distinctive tonal volatility of Hamsun's early work.

The comparison to Twain highlights Hamsun's capacity for abrupt shifts between comedy and pathos, irreverent humor and existential seriousness. Like Twain, Hamsun deployed irony and whimsy to undercut pretension while maintaining an underlying gravity. The pairing with Dostoyevski adds the dimension of psychological depth and moral seriousness that distinguishes Hamsun from mere humorists.
