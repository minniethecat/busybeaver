The Harbour of Kristiania (Oslo) is the waterfront area where ships from across the Baltic and North Sea trade routes docked. In the late 19th century, it was the city's primary connection to international commerce and travel.

## In Hunger

The harbour forms part of the liminal geography of *Hunger*'s conclusion. The narrator's departure from Kristiania by ship — joining the crew of the Russian barque *Copegoro* — takes place in this zone where the city meets the sea. The harbour represents escape, transition, and the ambiguous promise of elsewhere.

Like the docks, the harbour is a space of labor and commerce, where the narrator briefly considers manual work. This association with physical labor connects the waterfront to questions of class and dignity.
