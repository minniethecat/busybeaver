# Sunset Glow

## Overview

**Sunset Glow** (*Aftenrøde*) is a play by [[knut-hamsun|Knut Hamsun]], published in 1898. It is the third and final installment of the dramatic trilogy that began with *At the Gates of the Kingdom* (1895) and continued with *[[the-game-of-life|The Game of Life]]* (1896). The English-language preface to *Hunger* describes the trilogy's arc: "The first play is laid in Christiania, the second in the Northland, and the third in Christiania again. The hero of all three is Ivar Kareno, a student and thinker who is first presented to us at the age of 29, then at 39, and finally at 50" (P04-013). The play represents the final installment of [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s most concentrated period of theatrical writing, after which he returned primarily to the novel form.

## The Trilogy Arc

The preface provides the fullest account of the trilogy's thematic movement. [[ivar-kareno|Ivar Kareno]], the central figure, appears at three stages of life: "the irreconcilable rebel of 'At the Gates of the Kingdom,' the heaven-storming truth-seeker of 'The Game of Life,' and the acclaimed radical leader in the first acts of 'Sunset Glow'" (P04-013). In the play's climactic turn, Kareno "surrenders at last to the powers that be in order to gain a safe and sheltered harbor for his declining years" — only for "another man of 29" to rise and "denounce him and to take up the rebel cry of youth to which he has become a traitor" (P04-013). The preface characterizes the trilogy's lesson as that "every one is a rebel at 30 and a renegade at 50," though it cautions that "Hamsun's ironical humor and whimsical manner of expression do more than the plot itself to knit the plays into an organic unit" (P04-013).

The title — evoking the fading light of day — carries the elegiac sensibility characteristic of [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s prose. After *Sunset Glow*, [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]] would not return to the stage for five years, his next play being *Vendt the Monk* in 1903 (P04-014).

## Relationship to Hunger

*Sunset Glow* and *Hunger* share deeper thematic affinities than their different genres might suggest. Both works center on a figure who struggles against the constraints of his society — the starving writer in *Hunger* who refuses to compromise his artistic principles, and [[ivar-kareno|Ivar Kareno]] in *Sunset Glow* who begins as "the acclaimed radical leader" (P04-013) before capitulating to the establishment. The trilogy's cyclical pattern — the rebel of 30 who becomes the renegade of 50, only to be replaced by a new rebel — echoes the unnamed narrator of *Hunger*'s oscillation between defiant pride and abject desperation. Where *Hunger* tracks this conflict in the compressed, feverish space of a single autumn in [[christiania|Christiania]], *Sunset Glow* stretches it across a lifetime, giving the struggle between individual integrity and social accommodation a more explicitly philosophical treatment. Both works draw on [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s characteristic irony — the preface notes that "several of the characters are delightfully drawn, particularly the two women who play the greatest part in Kareno's life: his wife Eline, and Teresita, who is one more of his many feminine embodiments of the passionate and changeable Northland nature" (P04-013) — a descriptive mode that echoes the narrator of *Hunger*'s own volatile and self-lacerating tone.
