# Queen Tamara

## Overview

**Queen Tamara** (*Dronning Tamara*) is a play by [[knut-hamsun|Knut Hamsun]], published and first performed in 1903. It was one of "three subsequent plays" (P04-014) that followed the opening drama of the [[the-kareno-trilogy|Kareno trilogy]], alongside *Vendt the Monk* (1903) and *[[at-the-mercy-of-life|At the Mercy of Life]]* (1910). The play represents [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s sustained engagement with theatrical form during the first decade of the twentieth century — a period in which he produced nearly as many plays as novels.

## Description

*Queen Tamara* takes its title and subject from Tamar, the legendary queen of Georgia who ruled in the late twelfth and early thirteenth centuries — a figure associated with Georgia's golden age of cultural and military achievement. [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s choice of a medieval Georgian queen as dramatic subject is characteristic of his restlessness with Norwegian settings and his attraction to figures of exceptional power and singularity.

The play belongs to [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s middle period, when he was moving between forms — novels, plays, poetry — and experimenting with different modes of literary expression. *Queen Tamara* shares with [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s novels of this period a fascination with will, power, and the psychology of exceptional individuals. The historical setting allowed [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]] to explore these themes at a remove from contemporary Norwegian society, in a register more symbolic than realistic.

## Relationship to Hunger

*Queen Tamara* could hardly be more different from *Hunger* in subject, setting, and form. Where *Hunger* is contemporary, urban, and psychologically interior, *Queen Tamara* is historical, exotic, and dramatically external. Yet both works reflect [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s preoccupation with the exceptional individual — the figure who stands apart from the ordinary, driven by forces that the ordinary cannot comprehend. The hungry narrator and the Georgian queen are, at different registers and in different genres, expressions of the same [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]ian archetype.

## Critical Reception

*Queen Tamara* was part of [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s sustained dramatic output, though his plays have generally been less celebrated than his novels. The play's historical subject and symbolic mode reflect the broader currents of European drama at the turn of the century, when historical and verse drama coexisted with the emerging naturalist and symbolist movements.
