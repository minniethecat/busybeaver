Religious language and biblical allusion appear throughout Knut Hamsun's *Hunger*, but the novel's relationship to faith is characteristically ambivalent and modernist.

## Biblical Allusion

The narrator uses biblical language and imagery — sometimes sincerely, sometimes ironically. His suffering evokes Christological echoes without claiming redemption. The Bible is present in the novel as a cultural and literary resource rather than a source of living faith.

## The Absence of Consolation

What is most striking about religion in *Hunger* is its failure to provide consolation. The narrator visits churches and cemeteries, but these spaces offer no spiritual relief. His suffering is existential rather than redemptive — it leads nowhere, teaches nothing, and is ultimately simply endured. This is a distinctly modern religious sensibility, in which the forms of faith persist but their content has been hollowed out.
