# The Narrator

## Overview

The **narrator** of *[[hunger|Hunger]]* — never named in the text — is a young writer living in [[christiania|Christiania]] whose desperate poverty and psychological volatility constitute the novel's entire substance. He is the sole consciousness through which the narrative is filtered; no event occurs outside his perception, and no character exists except as refracted through his hungry, brilliant, and unreliable mind. He is widely understood to be a semi-autobiographical figure, drawing on [[knut-hamsun|Knut Hamsun]]'s own experiences of artistic struggle in Christiania during the late 1880s.

> **Synonym note**: This page covers the same character as [[the-unnamed-narrator]] — the narrator is also referred to as "the unnamed narrator," "the protagonist," or "the starving writer."

## In the Narrative

The narrator is introduced in a state of crisis — hungry, unable to pay his rent, and struggling to produce writing that will sustain him. Across four Parts, he navigates the streets of Christiania in an increasingly desperate condition, oscillating between manic creative energy and abject despair. One moment he is "calmed and in good spirit" (001-129); the next, "more and more miserable in spirit" (001-221).

His psychological volatility is the novel's true subject. He refuses work that would compromise his artistic identity — applying instead for a book-keeping position with [[christy-the-grocer|Christy the grocer]] that he never follows up on (002-076). He clings to his pride even as it destroys him: passing an eating-house in [[moller-street|Möller Street]], he "was on the point of entering without any fixed purpose, when I bethought myself in time" (002-097). He constructs elaborate fantasies — a three-act medieval drama, an "Allegory of a Conflagration in a Bookshop" — that blur the boundary between creativity and delusion.

The narrator's unreliability is a defining feature. He lies to strangers about his identity, exaggerates his circumstances, and invents characters like Joachim [[kierulf|Kierulf]], the wool-dealer, whom he searches for methodically before concluding Kierulf is merely "a reminiscence — a phantom" (003-043).

## Relationship to Ylajali

[[ylajali|Ylajali]] is the narrator's most significant relationship in the novel. He names her himself — "I named you Ylajali. How do you like that? It has a gliding sound" (003-159) — and she becomes both the object of his romantic idealization and the catalyst for his most intense self-doubt. She is simultaneously "a fairy, a princess" (001-116) in the story he tells about her, and a real woman whose escort by [[the-duke|The Duke]] devastates him in Part IV (004-027).

## Critical Context

The narrator has been read as a prototype of the modernist anti-hero — isolated, alienated, incapable of integrating into society, yet possessed of an acute, almost painful sensitivity. His psychological fragmentation anticipates the stream-of-consciousness narrators of Joyce and Woolf, while his urban wandering prefigures the flâneur figures of twentieth-century literature. The radical subjectivity Hamsun achieved — the novel as a direct transcription of a single consciousness — was revolutionary in 1890.
