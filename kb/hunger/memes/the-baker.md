# The Baker

## Overview

**The baker** in Knut Hamsun's *Hunger* (1890) is less a developed character than a presence — the person behind the counter of the bakery whose goods the unnamed narrator can see, smell, and desire but cannot purchase. Bakers and their shops recur throughout the novel as sites of temptation and humiliation, where the narrator's hunger is made most acute by the proximity of food.

## In the Narrative

The narrator first encounters the motif of bread early in Part I, noting "an inflated advertisement of Fabian Olsens' new-baked bread" on the wall of his room P01-001. Bakehouses and food shops reappear with a sensual intensity that borders on the hallucinatory. In Part II, the narrator spots "a marvellously large penny loaf at a baker's — the largest I could possibly get for the price" P02-083 and later passes the same baker's shop again, observing that "the loaf is still in its place" P02-086. He lingers outside, gazing at bread and pastries, his hunger sharpened by the sight of what he cannot have. In a moment of desperate temptation, he even considers begging for bread but immediately recoils: "Fie! I whispered to myself, and shook my head, and held on my way" P02-172. In Part III, the memory of the baker returns: "Fabian Olsen, the baker's new-baked bread" surfaces in his thoughts as he contrasts his present misery with a slightly better past P03-047.

## Relationship to the Narrator

The baker represents the fundamental transaction that the narrator cannot complete: money for food. In a city full of food, the narrator starves because he lacks the means to participate in this most basic exchange. The baker's presence makes this paradox visible and unbearable. Each pass by the baker's shop is a test of the narrator's pride — he refuses to beg, even as his body wastes away P02-172.
