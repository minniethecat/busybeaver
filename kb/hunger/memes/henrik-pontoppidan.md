Henrik Pontoppidan (1857–1943) was a Danish realist writer who shared the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1917 with Karl Gjellerup. His novels provide a sweeping panorama of Danish society from the late 19th to early 20th centuries.

## Major Works

His masterwork is the trilogy *Lykke-Per* (1898–1904, "Lucky Per"), which traces a young man's rebellion against his religious upbringing and his ultimately failed pursuit of engineering and worldly ambition. *De dødes rige* (1912–1916, "The Kingdom of the Dead") is another major cycle examining social and political transformation.

## Context in Björkman's Introduction

Edwin Björkman's introduction to *Hunger* names Pontoppidan alongside Selma Lagerlöf as writers approaching Hamsun's stature but lacking his full "width of outlook, validity of interpretation and authority of tone." This ranking reflects the critical consensus that Hamsun represented a more forceful, original voice. Pontoppidan's naturalism and social realism stand in contrast to Hamsun's psychological subjectivism.
