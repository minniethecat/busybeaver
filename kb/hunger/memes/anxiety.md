## Overview

Anxiety in Knut Hamsun's *Hunger* pervades the narrator's consciousness as a diffuse, free-floating unease that colors all his perceptions and interactions. Unlike fear, which has specific objects, the narrator's anxiety is generalized — a nervous irritability that transforms mundane encounters into sources of acute distress and drives his increasingly erratic behavior.

## In Hunger

The narrator acknowledges his anxious disposition early in the novel, confessing, "I had grown nervous and irritable" (001-002). This admission frames his mental state not as a temporary condition but as a progressive deterioration linked to his physical circumstances. His nervous system, deprived of sustenance, becomes hypersensitive to stimuli that a well-fed person would scarcely notice.

This heightened sensitivity manifests in disproportionate reactions to trivial events, as when a woman's face "made a loathsome impression upon me. The long yellow snag looked like a little finger pointing out of her gum" (001-011). The narrator himself recognizes the irrationality of such responses but is powerless to moderate them.

When facing professional situations, his anxiety intensifies. Approaching an editor, he asks anxiously, "Have I done anything wrong?" (001-304), revealing a baseline expectation of rejection and censure. His anxiety about his appearance — the state of his clothes, his visible poverty — compounds his social unease. The novel thus portrays anxiety as both a symptom of starvation and a self-perpetuating state that accelerates the narrator's social and psychological decline.
