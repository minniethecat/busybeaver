# George Egerton

**George Egerton** was the translator responsible for the English edition of [[knut-hamsun|Knut Hamsun]]'s *Hunger*, published in 1899 (P03-002). The translation — based on the 1890 Norwegian original *Sult* — brought Hamsun's breakthrough novel to English-speaking readers and is the source text from which this wiki's corpus is derived. The Gutenberg front matter credits Egerton as the translator (P02-006), and the edition was based on "the eighth printing, published in September 1921 by Alfred A. Knopf, Inc." (P03-001), reproducing a text that had already been in English circulation for over two decades. While [[edwin-bjorkman|Edwin Björkman]] contributed the introduction, Egerton's translational choices shaped every sentence of the English *Hunger*, mediating between Hamsun's distinctive Norwegian prose and its Anglophone reception.

## Role as Translator

Egerton does not appear as a character in *Hunger* but is the invisible mediator through whom the entire English text is accessed. Every sentence — from the novel's opening image of newspaper-covered walls to the narrator's final departure from Christiania — is filtered through Egerton's selection of vocabulary, his handling of Hamsun's prose rhythms, and his rendering of the Norwegian original into English.

> Translator: George Egerton (P02-006)

This brief credit in the front matter (P02-006) conceals a complex act of literary transmission. The narrator's distinctive voice — fragmented, associative, oscillating between lyricism and brutality — is in part Egerton's achievement as well as Hamsun's. Translation studies increasingly recognize the translator as co-creator rather than mere conduit.

## The Translation's Significance

Egerton's translation matters for several intersecting reasons:

| Aspect | Significance |
|--------|-------------|
| Historical timing | Published just 9 years after the Norwegian original, during Hamsun's rise to international fame (P03-002) |
| Literary influence | Shaped how English-language modernists encountered Hamsun's proto-modernist style |
| Textual authority | Serves as the base text for all English-language scholarship until newer translations appeared |
| Corpus foundation | Every PN-cited passage in this wiki is Egerton's English rendering |
| Editorial history | The PG edition reproduces a 1921 Knopf printing, noted as "a bowdlerised version of the original 1899 translation" (P03-002) |

## Relationship to the Narrator

As translator, Egerton occupies a unique position: he is the channel through which the [[the-unnamed-narrator|unnamed narrator]]'s voice reaches English-speaking readers. The narrator's distinctive style — its abrupt shifts in tone, its mixture of self-laceration and grandiosity, its physiological precision — emerges through Egerton's lexical choices: "listlessly," "frenzy of hunger," "delirium of weakness." Each word is a decision.

## Critical Context

The Egerton translation has been both praised and critiqued. Some scholars note that certain Norwegian idioms are domesticated; others argue that Egerton's Victorian prose smooths Hamsun's rougher edges. The PG edition itself notes that "this edition is a bowdlerised version of the original 1899 translation, also by George Egerton" (P03-002), acknowledging the editorial modifications between 1899 and 1921. Björkman's preface mentions that *Hunger*, "Fictoria" and "Shallow Soil" were the only Hamsun works then available in English (P04-018), underscoring the scarcity of translations in the early twentieth century. Yet Egerton's translation's historical importance is undeniable — it was the English *Hunger* for over a century, read by generations of writers and readers who encountered Hamsun through Egerton's eyes.
