# The Landlady

## Overview

**The Landlady** is a minor but symbolically significant figure in [[knut-hamsun|Knut Hamsun]]'s *[[hunger|Hunger]]* (1890). She owns [[the-attic|the attic]] room where [[the-unnamed-narrator|the unnamed narrator]] lives and appears primarily as a source of pressure — her questioning looks remind [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] of his unpaid rent and his precarious social position. Like many secondary characters in the novel, she is rendered through the narrator's subjective perception rather than objective description.

## In the Narrative

The landlady's presence is felt most acutely in the narrator's [[shame|shame]]. He describes being "obliged to hang my head and slink past her with a shamefaced air" (001-141), physically shrinking from her gaze. She does not need to speak — her eyes alone communicate the demand that the narrator cannot meet.

In Part IV, the landlady becomes the focus of an extended confrontation. She "talked with pauses, gave me these thrusts at short intervals, and spun it out to make it clearer and clearer that it was me she meant" (004-064). Her indirectness — never explicitly asking the narrator to leave, but making it unmistakably clear that he is unwelcome — is a form of social violence that the narrator experiences as almost physical.

The narrator's response to the landlady reveals his characteristic psychological defenses: "Quiet," he tells himself, "only keep quiet! Just no putting on side on my part — no untimely [[pride|pride]]! Brave it out!" (004-064). The interior monologue captures the narrator's desperate attempt to manage a social situation that is, from his perspective, unwinnable.

## Relationship to the Narrator

The landlady represents the ordinary social world that the narrator cannot participate in — the world of contracts, obligations, and reciprocal exchanges. Her silent demand for rent is fundamentally reasonable, which makes the narrator's inability to meet it all the more humiliating. She is not cruel; she is simply the embodiment of an order that has no place for him.
