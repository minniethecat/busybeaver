# The Maid

## Overview

**The Maid** is not a single named character but a recurring social type in Knut Hamsun's *Hunger* (1890). Servants and working-class women appear throughout the novel as figures whom the narrator alternately idealizes, resents, and identifies with. Their presence underscores the narrator's ambiguous social position — he is not a servant, but he is closer to servitude than to the middle-class existence he aspires to.

## In the Narrative

Maids appear at thresholds — doorways, staircases, entrances to buildings — where the narrator must negotiate access. In an early encounter, "a servant-maid leant out of it, with her sleeves turned up, and began to clean the panes on the outside" P01-051, a figure of mundane domesticity observed by the restless narrator. They are gatekeepers, and the narrator's interactions with them reveal his uncertain status. When he attempts to see the pastor, he tells the maid, "I want to see the pastor" P02-146, and she asks, "Was it anything particular?" P02-149 — a brief exchange that captures the power differential between servant and supplicant. Later, "a maid comes out and opens the door" P03-020, a recurring image of the servant as the first face encountered at any threshold.

The narrator also encounters nursemaids in public spaces: "down near the Queen's arbour two nursemaids were trundling their perambulators" P01-038 and later "the nursemaids who sat in groups" as the day declines P01-129. His own landlady — part of the servant economy — torments him with "her inquiring look in the morning when I met her on the stairs" due to his unpaid rent P01-039.

The narrator's attitude toward maids is characteristically volatile. He can be gallant, contemptuous, or pleading — his behavior determined less by the maid's actual character than by his own momentary psychological state. In this, maids function as blank screens onto which the narrator projects his shifting self-conceptions.

## Relationship to the Narrator

Maids occupy a liminal social space that mirrors the narrator's own. They are not the poor (they have employment, however menial) but neither are they the bourgeoisie. The narrator's identification with servants — his sense that he is, like them, at the mercy of those with money and status — coexists with his need to distinguish himself from them. He is not a servant; he is an artist. The distinction matters enormously to him, even when it is invisible to everyone else.
