# Henrik Ibsen

## Overview

**Henrik Ibsen** (1828–1906) was a Norwegian playwright and one of the most influential figures in modern drama. In the preface to [[knut-hamsun|Knut Hamsun]]'s *[[hunger|Hunger]]* (1890), Ibsen is invoked as the benchmark against which Hamsun's literary significance is measured: "Since the [[death|death]] of Ibsen and [[august-strindberg|Strindberg]], [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]] is undoubtedly the foremost creative writer of the Scandinavian countries" (P04-001). The preface frames this judgment within a broader portrait of Hamsun as an "individualistic romanticist and a highly subjective aristocrat, whose foremost passion in life is violent, defiant deviation from everything average and ordinary" (P04-003).

## In the Narrative

Ibsen does not appear in the novel itself but is mentioned in the preface as part of the literary-historical framework that contextualizes Hamsun's achievement. The preface notes that even Ibsen, like Bjornson and Strindberg, was influenced by "the neo-romantic movement" (P04-010) that Hamsun helped inaugurate with *Hunger*. This movement represented a generational shift: Hamsun had worked through years of obscurity, producing a long poem and a short novel at age eighteen before his "first real artistic achievement with 'Hunger'" (P04-007).

Ibsen's *Peer Gynt* is referenced in connection with Hamsun's verse drama *[[vendt-the-monk|Vendt the Monk]]*, whose protagonist "has much in common with Peer Gynt without being in any way an imitation or a duplicate" (P04-014). The preface further situates Hamsun's dramatic ambitions by surveying his theatrical output, including [[the-kareno-trilogy|the Kareno trilogy]] — "[[at-the-gates-of-the-kingdom|At the Gates of the Kingdom]]," "[[the-game-of-life|The Game of Life]]," and "[[sunset-glow|Sunset Glow]]" (P04-013) — works that, like Ibsen's plays, grapple with the conflict between individual conviction and social conformity.

## Relationship to the Narrator

Ibsen's relationship to Hamsun is that of predecessor to successor — the established master whose death (along with Strindberg's) cleared space for a new literary voice. This Oedipal dynamic, in which the younger writer must both acknowledge and surpass the elder, is characteristic of literary modernism.

## Critical Context

Ibsen's significance to *Hunger* is structural rather than thematic: he represents the literary tradition that Hamsun was both extending and transforming. Hamsun's psychological realism, with its focus on subjective consciousness rather than social drama, marked a departure from Ibsenite realism while acknowledging its foundation.
