In Knut Hamsun's *Hunger* (1890), hunger operates far beyond its literal meaning of food deprivation. It functions as the novel's central metaphor, organizing meaning on multiple levels simultaneously.

## Literal Hunger

The narrator's physical starvation is the novel's immediate subject. He goes days without food, pawns his belongings, and experiences the physiological and psychological effects of malnutrition: vertigo, hallucinations, mood swings, and cognitive deterioration. This literal hunger is rendered with harrowing specificity.

## Creative Hunger

Hunger also represents the artist's appetite for experience, recognition, and expression. The narrator is a writer who cannot afford to write; his creative drive persists despite (and sometimes because of) his physical deprivation. The novel suggests a connection between material want and creative fertility — hunger as the condition of artistic production.

## Spiritual and Existential Hunger

At its deepest level, hunger in the novel signifies an existential condition: the human craving for meaning, dignity, and connection in an indifferent universe. The narrator's physical hunger is an extreme form of a universal human condition. His oscillations between pride and self-abasement, between creative ecstasy and despair, map the terrain of modern consciousness itself.

## The Title's Ambiguity

The Norwegian title *Sult* carries the same range of meanings as English "hunger": from physical need to spiritual longing. Hamsun's choice of this single powerful word for his title announces the novel's ambition to treat a physical condition as a metaphysical metaphor.
