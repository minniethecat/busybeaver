## Overview

*Vendt the Monk* (*Munken Vendt*) is a verse drama in eight acts by Knut Hamsun, published in 1903. It is widely considered the most remarkable of Hamsun's three plays from this period, the others being *Queen Tamara* (1903) and *At the Mercy of Life* (1910). The play centres on one of Hamsun's most typical vagabond heroes, the monk Vendt (P04-014).

## Description

The monk Vendt is a dreamer in revolt against the world's alleged injustice, a rebel against the very powers that invisibly move the universe, and a passionate lover of life who ultimately accepts existence as a joyful battle before dreaming of the long peace to come. The character bears comparison to Ibsen's Peer Gynt without being in any way an imitation or a duplicate. The vigor and charm of the verse surprised critics when the play was published, as Hamsun had previously given no proof of poetic gift in the narrower sense (P04-014).

## Relationship to Hunger

While *Hunger* (1890) established Hamsun as a prose stylist of psychological intensity, *Vendt the Monk* revealed an entirely different dimension of his talent: the ability to write sustained dramatic verse. Published thirteen years after *Hunger*, the play demonstrates Hamsun's continued fascination with outsider figures who rebel against society and the cosmic order—a theme that runs from the starving narrator of *Hunger* through the vagabond heroes of his later works (P04-014).
