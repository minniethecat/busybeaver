The Lame Old Man is a significant minor character in Knut Hamsun's *Hunger*, encountered by the narrator in one of the novel's most revealing episodes.

## The Gift

In a characteristic gesture of self-destructive generosity, the narrator, having just earned a small sum from a feuilleton sale, gives money to the lame old man — a figure even more pitiable than himself. This act crystallizes the novel's central paradox: the narrator's pride expresses itself through acts that deepen his material suffering.

By giving to someone worse off, the narrator momentarily reverses the hierarchy of pity and reclaims the position of benefactor rather than beggar. The lame old man thus serves as a crucial psychological mirror, allowing the narrator to perform the generosity his own pride prevents him from accepting.
