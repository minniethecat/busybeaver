# The Kareno Trilogy

## Overview

The **Kareno Trilogy** is a sequence of three plays by [[knut-hamsun|Knut Hamsun]], with Ivar Kareno as the central figure — "a student and thinker who is endowed with a pronounced tendency to mysticism" (P04-013). The trilogy marks [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s decisive turn from the novel to the drama after the line of narrative works culminating in *[[pan|Pan]]* (1894), and represents his most sustained engagement with theatrical form.

## Description

The trilogy centers on Ivar Kareno, a figure who shares with [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s earlier protagonists — the hungry narrator, Nagel, Glahn — a condition of radical singularity. The preface describes him as "a student and thinker" whose mysticism sets him apart from the ordinary characters surrounding him. The trilogy extends [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s psychological method into dramatic form, using dialogue rather than interior monologue to render the [[soul|consciousness]] of an exceptional individual in tension with society.

The plays trace Kareno's development across a span of years, following him from youth through maturity — a temporal scope that distinguishes the trilogy from the compressed timescale of [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s novels. The dramatic form imposes a different discipline on [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s characteristic intensity: where the novels can dissolve into pure interiority, the plays force [[soul|consciousness]] to articulate itself in speech.

## Relationship to Hunger

The Kareno Trilogy represents a significant departure from the world of *Hunger*. Where the novel is set in the streets and rooms of [[christiania|Christiania]], rendered with documentary precision, the trilogy moves toward symbolic and mystical terrain. Yet Kareno, like *Hunger*'s narrator, embodies the [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]ian type: the isolated [[soul|consciousness]] that "is endowed with a pronounced tendency to mysticism" (P04-013), the individual whose inner life is too intense to be contained by conventional social forms.

The preface positions the trilogy as the pivot in [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s career — the point after the "line of novels culminating in *[[pan|Pan]]*, he turned suddenly to the drama" (P04-013). This turn is important for understanding *Hunger*'s place in [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s development: the novel represents the peak of one phase, the trilogy the beginning of another.

## Critical Reception

[[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s plays have generally received less critical attention than his novels, though they were an important part of his literary output during the middle period of his career. The trilogy's mysticism and dramatic form reflect [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s ongoing experimentation with genre and his resistance to being confined to any single mode of literary expression.
