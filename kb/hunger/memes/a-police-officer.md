# A Police Officer

The **police officer** appears at several crucial moments in Knut Hamsun's *Hunger*, embodying the social order that the [[the-unnamed-narrator|unnamed narrator]] inhabits precariously. Unlike other authority figures — the [[the-landlady|landlady]] with her personal claim on rent, [[the-uncle]] with his familial authority — the policeman represents the impersonal power of the state. His judgment is institutional, not personal, and the narrator's vulnerability to it reflects his broader exclusion from the protections of citizenship and respectability.

## In the Narrative

The narrator's encounters with police are marked by anxiety and performance. He must appear respectable — a citizen, not a vagrant — even when his circumstances make respectability impossible:

> He called a policeman, and I desired nothing better than to fall into the clutches of the law. (001-264)

This moment captures the narrator's paradoxical relationship to authority: he simultaneously fears the policeman's power and longs for the order it represents. To be arrested would be, in its way, to be acknowledged — to matter enough to be constrained.

At the [[the-guard-house|Guard-House]], the narrator's identity becomes an official matter. The police represent the boundary between belonging and exclusion, a threshold the narrator perpetually straddles. He is not a criminal, but his poverty places him outside the protections that citizenship normally affords.

## Symbolic Significance

The police officer functions across three registers in *Hunger*:

| Register | Function |
|----------|----------|
| **Social** | Embodiment of institutional authority and the state's monopoly on legitimate force |
| **Psychological** | Mirror of the narrator's anxiety about his own respectability and social standing |
| **Narrative** | Catalyst for key plot moments — encounters with police mark thresholds in the narrator's descent |

The policeman is the figure of the threshold — the line between citizen and outcast that the narrator perpetually crosses. Unlike the landlady, whose power is economic and personal, the policeman's authority is abstract, legal, and final. His presence on the streets of [[christiania|Christiania]] reminds the narrator — and the reader — that the city is not merely a setting but a regulated space, a grid of rules and enforcement.

## The Guard-House Encounter

The most significant police encounter occurs at the Guard-House, where the narrator goes seeking shelter under a false name. This episode reveals the narrator's complex relationship with institutional authority: he is simultaneously deceiving the police and dependent on their procedural obligations. The false identity he invents is both a shield against exposure and a confession of his own illegibility within the social order.
