## Overview

The "Allegory of a Conflagration in a Bookshop" is a fictional newspaper piece that the narrator labours over during Part II of the novel. Conceived as an allegory about a fire, it deepens into a dark vision in which it is not books that burn but human brains — a metaphor the narrator considers his most profound thought. He intends to sell the completed piece to the Commandor to pay his debts.

## Description

The narrator first mentions the allegory early in Part II, describing it as "an allegory about a fire — a profound thought upon which I intended to expend all my energy, and bring it to the 'Commandor' in payment" (004-004). He works on it from his room on the second floor of his lodging house, buoyed by the belief that the Commandor would see he "had helped a talent this time" (004-004).

The work progresses in fits and starts. After a moment of high spirits, he seats himself and finds that the allegory "progressed swimmingly, better than it had done for a long time" — working for an hour without tiring (004-004). At what he considers its most crucial point, he articulates the central conceit: "It was not books that were burning, it was brains, human brains; and I intended to make a perfect Bartholomew's night of these burning brains" (004-004).

The allegory is ultimately left unfinished. A series of interruptions — the landlady bursting in with an accounting question, the arrival of a strange sailor who takes over his room — derails the narrator's concentration. Retreating to the ante-room, he abandons the allegory entirely for a new idea: a one-act drama titled "The Sign of the Cross" (004-004).

## The "Burning Brains" Metaphor

The shift from burning books to burning brains is the allegory's most significant imaginative leap — and perhaps the novel's most concentrated image of intellectual self-destruction. Where a conventional allegory about a bookshop fire might lament the loss of knowledge or culture, the narrator's version turns inward, transforming the external calamity into a vision of mass psychological annihilation. The phrase "a perfect Bartholomew's night" (004-004) invokes the St. Bartholomew's Day massacre of 1572, grafting the historical weight of religious slaughter onto a metaphor for mental collapse.

This substitution — brains for books — also collapses the distance between the writer and his subject. The narrator is himself a brain on fire: feverish, starving, hallucinating. What he writes about is already happening to him. The allegory is thus doubly autobiographical: it narrates the narrator's own condition under the thinnest veil of fiction. As with other embedded works in the novel — the newspaper article, "The Sign of the Cross" — the allegory functions as a mirror held up to the consciousness that produces it, revealing more about the authorial mind than about its ostensible subject.

## Relationship to Hunger

The allegory serves a dual function in the novel. On the surface, it represents the narrator's desperate hope of earning money through his writing — a concrete goal that sustains him through hunger. On a deeper level, the image of burning brains externalises the narrator's own mental state: his starving, feverish, deteriorating mind is itself undergoing a conflagration. The allegory's abandonment mirrors the narrator's broader pattern of creative starts that cannot be sustained, each new project representing a fresh hope that circumstances eventually extinguish.

The allegory's compositional arc — initial inspiration, productive flow, interruption, abandonment — is itself a miniature of the novel's larger narrative rhythm. The narrator repeatedly experiences moments of creative and emotional elevation, only to be pulled back down by material reality: hunger, landlords, the demands of survival. The allegory's fate is the fate of every project the narrator undertakes.
