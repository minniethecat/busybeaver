## Overview

Begging represents one of the most acute sources of moral anguish for the unnamed narrator in Knut Hamsun's *Hunger*. Though driven to the brink of starvation, the narrator repeatedly resists the act of directly asking for charity, experiencing each encounter with the possibility of begging as a crisis of identity and self-respect. Begging in the novel is less an economic transaction than a site where pride, shame, and the social self collide.

## In Hunger

The narrator's first significant encounter with begging occurs when an old lame man asks him for "a halfpenny for milk" (001-016). The narrator, himself penniless, nevertheless promises to "go and get a little money for you; just a few pence" (001-023), inverting the roles of beggar and benefactor. This pattern of giving when he has nothing recurs throughout the novel as a means of preserving his sense of superiority over those he perceives as truly destitute.

The fear of being mistaken for a beggar haunts the narrator. When he approaches a woman on the street, she "took me without further ado for a beggar" (001-271), and this misidentification wounds him: "So it had gone so far with me that any one might point at me and say, 'There goes a beggar, one of those people who get their food at the back-doors of the Vartov'" (001-279). His entire self-image is threatened by the external perception of mendicancy.

Despite his desperate hunger, the narrator's pride often prevents him from asking directly for money. When he attempts to borrow five shillings from a friend, his companion preempts him by requesting the same amount first, and the narrator laughs with relief: "How confoundedly lucky it was that he had forestalled me; my self-respect was saved" (002-060). The narrator would rather starve than be seen as a supplicant. This paradox — begging to survive while being unable to beg without destroying his self-worth — drives much of the novel's psychological tension.
