## Overview

The Bible is the sacred text of Christianity, comprising the Old and New Testaments. In Knut Hamsun's *Hunger*, biblical references and the figure of God form a persistent subtext to the protagonist's internal monologue, recurring most intensely during his moments of greatest physical and psychological distress.

## Role in Hunger

In [[hunger]], the protagonist's relationship with God is ambivalent, oscillating between desperate appeal and bitter accusation. He experiences his suffering alternately as divine punishment and cosmic indifference. When his hunger is at its worst, he addresses God directly:

> God knows, thought I, if looking for employment will ever again avail me aught (001-006).

As his condition deteriorates, his thoughts turn darker: "Supposing God Almighty simply intended to annihilate me?" (001-068). He grows "more and more bitter against God for His prolonged inflictions" (001-072), and imagines "a gaping hole after the finger, which was God's finger, and a wound in my brain in the track of His finger" (001-073).

This biblical register serves a complex function in the novel. It elevates the protagonist's mundane suffering to cosmic scale, simultaneously inviting and undermining a religious reading of his ordeal. The biblical allusions frame his hunger as a modern, secularized passion — a trial without redemption.

## Prayer and Desperation

The narrator's religious invocations intensify as his physical condition worsens. In the grip of pain, he cries out with the language of faith:

> In my pain I cried: "Lord, my God and Father!" (001-139)

His prayer is not abstract piety but visceral need: "I prayed to God in silence for this place" (001-145). At moments of reprieve, his gratitude takes on a devotional quality: the narrator feels "grateful towards God and all creation, and I knelt" (001-163). His cry "It was God! It was God!" (001-165) registers a sudden, overwhelming sense of deliverance that is as credulous as his accusations of divine persecution.

Yet the pattern reveals a deeper dynamic. The narrator's faith is wholly contingent on his physical state — gratitude when fed, accusation when starving, prayer when desperate. The biblical language becomes a vocabulary for extremity rather than a stable theological framework, making God a character in the narrator's internal drama whose role shifts with each swing of fortune.

## Theological Dimensions

Hamsun's deployment of biblical imagery does more than render the narrator's suffering in religious terms — it enacts a critique of providential narrative. The narrator imagines "up in Heaven God Almighty sat and kept a watch" (001-266), yet this surveillance brings no comfort: it is the stare of a cosmic antagonist, not a protector. The thought of divine observation leads directly to thoughts of "the unpardonable offence for which God in His justice must cast me" (001-267).

> The thought of God began to occupy me. It seemed to me an unpardonable arrogance on my part, my miserable doings notwithstanding, to summon the attention of the Ruler of the Universe (001-070).

This passage illustrates the central theological tension of *Hunger*: the narrator simultaneously insists on his insignificance — too trivial to merit God's attention — and demands that same attention as the measure of his suffering's meaning. The biblical register is thus self-undermining, invoking divine significance only to expose the absence of any transcendent order willing to supply it.
