# The Duke

## Overview

"The Duke" is a well-known lady's doctor in Christiania whose nickname derives from his reputation and manner. He appears in [[knut-hamsun|Knut Hamsun]]'s *[[hunger|Hunger]]* at two key moments: first as a passing commentator on [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s mental state in Part II, and later as the man seen escorting [[ylajali|Ylajali]] in Part IV — a sighting that triggers one of the narrator's most intense spirals of jealousy and wounded pride.

## In the Narrative

The Duke first enters the story in Part II, when the narrator — talking to himself on the pavement, asking "What shall I do?" — is overheard by a passing gentleman who remarks with a smile: "You ought to go and ask to be locked up" (002-175). The narrator recognizes him as "one of our well-known lady's doctors, nicknamed 'The Duke'" — a man he knows and has shaken hands with. The casual diagnosis that the narrator is mad strikes deeply, and he spends the following pages wrestling with and resisting the idea of being locked up (002-175–002-176). The Duke does not pause to hear his effect; he delivers his verdict and walks on — a miniature of the city's relationship to its suffering inhabitants.

The Duke reappears in Part IV in a more consequential role. As the narrator stands talking with [[missy|Missy]] on Carl Johann Street, he spots Ylajali approaching in a red dress, accompanied by a man. Missy identifies him: "The Duke, didn't you see? The so-called 'Duke'" (004-027). The sighting devastates the narrator. He falls silent, then launches into a long internal monologue of jealousy and defensive pride: "Yes, of course 'the Duke' would make the pace with her. Well, what odds? How did it concern me?" (004-038). He consoles himself by imagining Ylajali degraded, telling himself she has "fallen off" and is "soiled" — a transparent attempt to mask his wounded feelings with contempt (004-038).

## Relationship to the Narrator

The Duke functions as a dual threat to the narrator. In Part II, he represents the clinical, dismissive gaze of respectable society — diagnosing madness with a smile and walking on, his authority as a doctor making his casual cruelty official. In Part IV, he becomes a romantic rival, embodying everything the narrator lacks: social standing, professional identity, and the means to escort a woman like Ylajali in public on Carl Johann Street. The narrator's reaction to seeing Ylajali with the Duke is one of the novel's most vivid portrayals of his fragile ego: he cycles through jealousy, self-consolation, fantasies of revenge ("before eight days had flown, I would have brought this young woman to her knees"), and finally a forced indifference that barely conceals his hurt (004-038).

## Critical Context

The Duke illustrates [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s skill at minor characterization through social titles. "The Duke" is not the man's name but his public identity — a nickname that captures both his social elevation and, ironically, the absurdity of such elevation in the context of a starving narrator's perspective. The title "Duke" in a city with no aristocracy is itself a satire of Christiania's social pretensions.
