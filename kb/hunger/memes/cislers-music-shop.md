## Overview

Cisler's music shop is a commercial establishment in Christiania that appears at two strategically distant points in the novel — first as the setting of an early sighting of a woman resembling Ylajali in Part I, and later as a name the narrator drops while trying to prove his sanity in Part II. The shop functions as a narrative landmark connecting the narrator's romantic obsession with his psychological deterioration.

## In the Narrative

### Part I: The First Sighting

The shop first appears in Part I, shortly after the narrator's initial encounter with the woman who will later be revealed as Ylajali. Following two women through the streets, he watches them turn and stop "at Cisler's music-shop" and speak together (001-052). The narrator halts on the opposite pavement to observe. One of the women looks back at him — a glance that inflames his imagination — and they then proceed onwards, turning "the corner of University Street and up towards St. Olav's place" (001-052). The narrator remains "as close at their heels as I dared to be," and when they turn round again they send him "a half-fearful, half-questioning look" with no resentment in it (001-052).

This moment at Cisler's shop is significant because it marks the transition from a chance street encounter to the narrator's active pursuit. The shop serves as a fixed point in the geography of his obsession — a place he can later revisit in memory, associated with the first stirrings of his fixation on the woman who becomes Ylajali.

### Part II: The Sanity Test

Cisler himself is invoked in Part II under entirely different circumstances. After the Duke casually suggests the narrator should be "locked up" (002-175), the narrator frantically tries to prove his lucidity to himself by recalling acquaintances: "For example, there was Cisler the music-seller; I hadn't been in his shop since the beginning of the summer" (002-176). He wonders whether the name had come to him as "a hint from on high" (002-176) and resolves to visit Cisler to ask for money.

When he arrives at the shop, he is shown into Cisler's office where the proprietor sits — "handsome, well-dressed in the latest style — running down some accounts" (002-176). The narrator stammeringly asks for a shilling, but even as he speaks Cisler "turned about to his desk, and resumed his work" (002-176). When the narrator finishes, Cisler "glanced sideways at me, shook his handsome head, and said, 'No'; simply 'no' — no explanation — not another word" (002-176).

## The Double Function of the Shop

Cisler's music shop operates at two narrative extremes: in Part I it is a site of romantic possibility, the backdrop for the narrator's enchanted pursuit of Ylajali; in Part II it becomes a site of humiliation, where the well-dressed, prosperous Cisler dismisses the narrator without explanation. The contrast between these two appearances maps the narrator's own decline: from someone who watches beautiful women from across the street to someone who begs shopkeepers for shillings and is refused with a single word.

Cisler's silence — "'No'; simply 'no'" — is one of the novel's most economical cruelties. Unlike the Commandor, who engages with the narrator at length, Cisler offers no explanation, no softening courtesy. His refusal is as clean and final as the closing of a ledger. That the narrator once bought music from this man "often, when times were good" (002-176) only deepens the fall: Cisler's shop is a measure of the distance between the narrator's former respectability and his present degradation.
