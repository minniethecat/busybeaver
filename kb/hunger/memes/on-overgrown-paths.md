## Overview

*On Overgrown Paths* (*Paa gjengrodde Stier*) is Knut Hamsun's final book, published in 1949 when he was 90 years old. Written during his confinement at a psychiatric clinic and later under house arrest following his support for the Nazi regime during World War II, it is his literary testament and self-defense.

## Description

The memoir recounts Hamsun's experiences during the legal proceedings against him after the war, including his stay at the psychiatric clinic in Oslo and his interrogation. Written in a fragmented, associative style reminiscent of his early modernist works, the book moves between the present moment, memories of his life, and reflections on aging and mortality. Despite his failing eyesight and advanced age, Hamsun produced a work of remarkable literary vitality.

## Relationship to Hunger

*On Overgrown Paths* closes the arc that *Hunger* opened. Both books are written in a first-person, subjective mode that blurs the boundary between confession, fiction, and self-mythologizing. The narrator of *Hunger* is a young man starving for recognition in Christiania; the narrator of *On Overgrown Paths* is an old man, nearly blind and deaf, fighting for his legacy. The two works form startling bookends to one of modern literature's most extraordinary careers.
