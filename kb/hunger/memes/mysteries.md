# Mysteries

## Overview

**Mysteries** (*Mysterier*) is a novel by [[knut-hamsun|Knut Hamsun]], published in 1892, two years after *Hunger*. The preface to *Hunger* describes it as a work that "pretends to be a novel" (P04-011) — a phrase that captures both its experimental form and its defiance of genre expectations. Set in a small Norwegian coastal town, *Mysteries* centers on Johan Nilsen Nagel, a stranger whose arrival disrupts the community and whose behavior grows increasingly erratic.

## Description

*Mysteries* extends *Hunger*'s psychological method into new territory. Where *Hunger* follows a single [[soul|consciousness]] through the streets of [[christiania|Christiania]], *Mysteries* places its protagonist in a social microcosm — a small town where Nagel's strangeness is amplified by the conventionality that surrounds him. Nagel arrives with money and mysterious origins; he falls in love, philosophizes, performs acts of inexplicable generosity and cruelty. The novel's refusal to explain Nagel — his motivations remain opaque, his actions contradictory — makes it one of the most challenging works of early modernism.

The novel's psychological focus is on abnormal states: obsession, self-contradiction, the compulsion to perform identities that one does not fully inhabit. Nagel is both more social than *Hunger*'s narrator and more opaque — a figure whose interior life is rendered from within yet remains fundamentally unknowable.

## Relationship to Hunger

*Mysteries* is the direct successor to *Hunger*, published just two years later. The preface places it in the sequence of early [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]] works that established his reputation, following *Hunger* (1890) and preceding *[[pan|Pan]]* (1894). Where *Hunger* explores [[soul|consciousness]] under the pressure of physical deprivation, *Mysteries* explores [[soul|consciousness]] under the pressure of social observation — Nagel is constantly watched, judged, and misunderstood by the townspeople, just as the hungry narrator is watched and judged by the citizens of [[christiania|Christiania]].

## Critical Reception

*Mysteries* has proven to be one of [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s most enduring and influential works, particularly for its anticipation of existentialist themes. Its influence can be traced through twentieth-century literature — from Kafka's alienated protagonists to the existential novels of the mid-century. The preface's description of it as a work that "pretends to be a novel" (P04-011) acknowledges its formal radicalism, which has been increasingly appreciated by later critics.
