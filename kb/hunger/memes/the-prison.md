The prison — the Kristiania police station with holding cells — appears in *Hunger* as the site of one of the novel's most significant episodes. Unable to afford lodging, the narrator deliberately gets himself arrested to secure a night's shelter.

## The Prison Episode

In a moment of desperation, the narrator presents himself at the police station and gives a false name, claiming to be a journalist who has missed his lodging. The police, skeptical of his story, place him in a cell for the night. This episode crystallizes the novel's central irony: the police station, an institution of social control and punishment, becomes for the narrator a refuge — providing the bed and shelter that respectable society denies him.

## Social and Psychological Dimensions

The prison episode dramatizes the narrator's ambiguous social position. He is neither a criminal nor fully a vagrant, yet he occupies the same institutional space as both. The false name he gives the police represents another layer of his fragmented identity. The morning after, his release back onto the streets — back into hunger and homelessness — makes freedom itself feel like punishment.

This episode exemplifies Hamsun's method of using institutional spaces to reveal the contradictions in social arrangements and the precariousness of identity under conditions of extreme deprivation.
