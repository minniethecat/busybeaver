The human condition refers to the universal and inescapable features of human existence: birth and death, suffering and joy, isolation and connection, the search for meaning in a universe that offers no guarantees.

## Hunger as Case Study

Knut Hamsun's *Hunger* explores the human condition by intensifying it to an extreme. The narrator's physical starvation strips away the buffers — comfort, routine, social connection — that normally obscure existential realities. In his condition, basic questions of dignity, purpose, and the value of existence become inescapably urgent.

## The Particular and the Universal

Björkman's introduction hints at this universalizing dimension when he writes that Hamsun's 'fantastic figures ... relate themselves to ordinary humanity as the microscopic enlargement of a cross section to the living tissues. What we see is true in everything but proportion.' The narrator's extremity reveals structures present in ordinary experience but normally invisible.
