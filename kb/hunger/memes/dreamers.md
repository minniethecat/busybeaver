# Dreamers

## Overview

**Dreamers** (*Sværmere*) is a novel by [[knut-hamsun|Knut Hamsun]], published in 1904 — a year of remarkable productivity that also saw the publication of his poetry collection *[[the-wild-choir|The Wild Choir]]*. The preface to *Hunger* lists it alongside "[[struggling-life|Struggling Life]]" (1905) in the catalogue of [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s works (P04-015). *Dreamers* belongs to the middle period of [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s career, after the early psychological novels (*Hunger*, *[[mysteries|Mysteries]]*, *[[pan|Pan]]*) and before the epic works of his maturity (*[[the-growth-of-the-soil|The Growth of the Soil]]*).

## Description

*Dreamers* takes place in a small Norwegian coastal town — a setting that links it to *[[mysteries|Mysteries]]* (1892) and anticipates the rural communities of [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s later fiction. The novel's protagonist displays the characteristic [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]ian traits of singularity and intensity, though the tone is less [[fever|feverish]] than in the early works. The middle-period novels are marked by a growing interest in community and social interaction — a move away from the radical solitude of *Hunger*'s narrator toward a more complex engagement with the social world.

The novel was published in a period of intense literary output for [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]], appearing between the short story collection *Kratskog* (1903) and the novel *Under the Autumn Star* (1906). This productivity reflects [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s growing stature in Scandinavian and European letters during the first decade of the twentieth century.

## Relationship to Hunger

*Dreamers* represents a different phase of [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s career from *Hunger* — less radically experimental in form, more engaged with social comedy and community dynamics. Yet the novel shares with *Hunger* a fascination with the psychology of exceptional individuals and the friction between inner life and social expectation. The title itself — "Dreamers" — suggests a concern with the interior life that links the novel to [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s earliest and most celebrated literary achievements.

## Critical Reception

*Dreamers* has received less critical attention than [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s major novels, but it occupies an important place in the arc of his career — a transitional work that bridges the psychological intensity of the early work with the broader social canvas of the later fiction.
