## Overview

In 1877, the Norwegian capital city of Christiania was officially renamed Kristiania, reverting to a more Norwegian spelling of the name given in 1624 after King Christian IV. The city would later be renamed Oslo in 1925, restoring its medieval name.

## Details

The city was originally named Oslo when founded around 1049. After a devastating fire in 1624, King Christian IV of Denmark-Norway ordered the city rebuilt and renamed it Christiania in his own honor. The 1877 spelling change to Kristiania reflected growing Norwegian nationalist sentiment during the period leading toward full independence from Sweden (achieved in 1905). The final renaming to Oslo in 1925 completed this symbolic reclamation of Norwegian identity.

## Linguistic Nationalism

The shift from the Latinate "Christiania" to the Norwegian-spelled "Kristiania" was part of a broader linguistic nationalism that characterized late nineteenth-century Norway. During the same period, Ivar Aasen was developing *landsmål* (later Nynorsk), a written standard based on Norwegian dialects, as an alternative to the Danish-influenced *riksmål* (later Bokmål). The renaming of the capital participated in this same movement — the assertion that Norway's institutions, including its capital city, should bear Norwegian names rather than Danish ones.

By 1890, when Knut Hamsun published *Hunger*, the spelling "Kristiania" had been official for thirteen years, though both forms were still in circulation. The transitional nature of the city's name during this period mirrors the broader transitional state of Norwegian society — caught between Danish cultural influence and the assertion of an independent national identity.

## Significance for Hunger

Knut Hamsun's *Hunger* (1890) is set in Kristiania, though the city is never explicitly named in the novel — its streets and landmarks are described in detail sufficient for any reader familiar with the capital. The city's public spaces — Carl Johann Street, University Street, the wharves, the cemetery — form the geography of the protagonist's wanderings.

The novel captures a city in transition, caught between its provincial past and its aspirations as a modern European capital, much as the name changes themselves reflect Norway's evolving national self-conception. The narrator's rootless movement through unnamed streets mirrors the city's own unsettled identity in this period.

Hamsun's choice not to name the city is itself significant. By omitting the name "Kristiania" (or "Christiania"), he universalizes the urban setting — the narrator's experience of hunger and alienation could take place in any modern city. Yet the detailed topography ensures that for Norwegian readers, the setting is unmistakable. This tension between the specific and the universal is characteristic of Hamsun's modernist technique.

## Context

The late 19th century was a period of intense nation-building in Norway, marked by the growth of a distinct Norwegian literary language (*landsmål*/*nynorsk*), the flourishing of national romanticism in the arts, and the gradual dissolution of the union with Sweden. The renaming of the capital should be understood within this broader cultural movement. That *Hunger* was published in 1890, just thirteen years after the name change, places the novel squarely in this period of evolving national identity. The narrator's psychological disintegration unfolds against the backdrop of a city — and a nation — in the process of becoming itself.
