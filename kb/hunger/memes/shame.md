# Shame

**Shame** is a pervasive psychological emotion in [[knut-hamsun|Knut Hamsun]]'s *Hunger*, experienced by the [[the-unnamed-narrator|unnamed narrator]] as a constant companion to his physical starvation. Unlike mere embarrassment, [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s shame is deeply entwined with his sense of [[pride|dignity]], his creative identity, and his precarious position in [[christiania|Christiania]]'s social order. The emotion appears in at least fifteen distinct passages, forming one of the novel's most consistent psychological threads and linking [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s physical [[hunger]] to his tortured self-image.

## In the Narrative

In Part I, [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s shame is primarily social — he is acutely aware of his shabby appearance and fears judgment. When his [[the-landlady|landlady]] looks at him with questioning eyes:

> I was obliged to hang my head and slink past her with a shamefaced air. (001-141)

This physical manifestation — the lowered head, avoided eye contact — recurs as a bodily expression of diminished social status. After a trivial social misstep, he is "ashamed and afraid that any one might" (001-212) — shame and fear merging into a single paralyzing affect.

The narrator's shame is also self-directed, a form of moral self-reproach. After accepting [[grace|kindness]] while starving:

> "You ought to be ashamed of yourself," I said aloud. (002-134)

Yet this shame coexists paradoxically with [[pride]] — he would "rather die of hunger" than debase himself further. The tension between shame and pride drives much of [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s erratic behavior. The narrator even contemplates a deliberate rejection of shame:

> If I could only knock all shame on the head and apply to him. (001-284)

The hypothetical — "if I could only" — reveals shame as the insurmountable barrier between [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] and the practical relief of his suffering.

## Psychological Structure

Shame operates across three interlocking registers in *Hunger*:

| Register | Manifestation | Key Passages |
|----------|--------------|-------------|
| **Social** | [[fear|Fear]] of judgment, avoidance of eye contact, performance of respectability | 001-141, 001-212 |
| **Moral** | Self-reproach, guilt over accepting help, internalized judgment | 002-134, 001-045 |
| **Somatic** | Shame as physical sensation, "shameless appetite," the body as shameful | 003-072, 003-077 |

By Part III, shame becomes almost physical. The narrator describes a "shameless appetite" (003-072) that gnaws at him — the language of shame transferred from the social to the somatic. Hunger itself becomes shameful, reducing [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] to writhing animality. Throughout, shame also attaches to creative failure — the inability to write, to sell articles, to sustain himself as an artist.

## Literary Significance

[[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s treatment of shame marks a departure from naturalist conventions. Where a naturalist might depict shame as a straightforward consequence of poverty, [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]] renders it as a complex psychological state — simultaneously self-lacerating and self-aggrandizing. The narrator's shame feeds his sense of specialness, his conviction of being a misunderstood genius, aligning with the preface's description of [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]] as an "individualistic romanticist" (P04-003).

The novel's stream-of-[[soul|consciousness]] form makes shame not merely a theme but a structural principle. The reader experiences humiliation with an immediacy unprecedented in 1890, the prose veering between grandiosity and self-loathing in ways that mirror [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s unstable [[soul|psyche]].
