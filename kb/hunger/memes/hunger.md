## Overview

Hunger is both the literal physical condition and the central metaphorical force driving the unnamed narrator in Knut Hamsun's novel *Hunger*. It functions not merely as a bodily sensation but as a transformative psychological state that dissolves the boundaries between reality and delusion, pride and degradation, creativity and madness.

## In Hunger

The narrator describes the physical experience of prolonged hunger in visceral terms. He observes that "whenever I had been hungry for any length of time, it was just as if my brains ran quite gently out of my head and left me with a vacuum" (001-071). This experience of cognitive dissolution intensifies throughout the novel: by afternoon, "hunger began to assail me downright in earnest. I was faint, and now and again I had to retch furtively" (001-236).

Hunger also produces paradoxical states of euphoria. The narrator enters what he calls "the joyous frenzy of hunger. I was empty and free from pain, and I gave free rein to every thought" (002-024). At other moments, starvation distorts perception entirely: "what strange freaks one's thoughts are guilty of when one is starving. I feel myself lifted up by these notes, dissolved in tones, and I float out" (002-105).

The social dimension of hunger appears when the narrator must beg. He tells a stranger, "I am really nearly dead with hunger; it is now many days since I have eaten anything" (002-187). Yet his pride continually clashes with his bodily need, and he often refuses food when offered, as when a companion exclaims that "it will never do for you to go and starve yourself to death" (003-109) and offers him half-a-sovereign.

In the final part of the novel, the narrator's relationship with hunger shifts. He notes, "now I no longer felt hungry, only the sweet stuff I had eaten began to cause me discomfort" (004-142), suggesting that by this point, the psychological turbulence has overtaken the purely physical drive.
