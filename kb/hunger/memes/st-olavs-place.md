# St. Olav's Place

## Overview

**St. Olav's Place** (St. Olavs plass) is a public square in [[christiania|Christiania]], located at the intersection of University Street and several other thoroughfares. In [[knut-hamsun|Knut Hamsun]]'s *Hunger*, it serves as a recurring landmark in [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s urban geography — the place where he first encounters the address of [[ylajali|Ylajali]], and where he repeatedly returns to observe and imagine her life.

## In the Narrative

St. Olav's Place enters the novel early in Part I as [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] walks "up towards St. Olav's place" (001-052) along University Street. His attention is caught by two women ahead of him, and he follows their movements with a mixture of curiosity and self-[[soul|consciousness]].

The location acquires narrative weight when [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] learns [[ylajali|Ylajali]]'s address: "St. Olav's Place, No. 2" (001-093). This single line transforms the square from a generic urban waypoint into a charged site of desire and [[dreams|imagination]]. The old man who gives him the address claims he "knew every stone in St. Olav's Place. There was a fountain, some lamp-posts, a few trees" (001-094) — the square is rendered with documentary specificity even as it becomes the locus of [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s [[dreams|fantasy]].

The square returns in Part III when [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] takes "a turn up St. Olav's Place and took a furtive look at certain lighted windows" (003-120). His surveillance of [[ylajali|Ylajali]]'s residence is furtive and compulsive — he watches her windows, imagines her life within, constructs an entire emotional world around a glimpse. Later that night, he "walked once more towards St. Olav's Place" (003-121), drawn back again despite himself, his eyes fixed on University Street.

## Symbolic Significance

St. Olav's Place represents the tension between public space and private obsession. It is a public square — open, civic, traversed by strangers — yet for [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] it becomes intensely private, the container of his fixation on [[ylajali|Ylajali]]. The square's physical features (fountain, lamp-posts, trees) are catalogued with apparent detachment, but beneath that cataloguing runs the current of [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s emotional investment.

The square also embodies [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s relationship to visibility and invisibility in the city. He is simultaneously the anonymous pedestrian and the obsessive watcher — present in public space but alienated from its ordinary social uses. St. Olav's Place is where [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s interior life most dramatically collides with the exterior world of [[christiania|Christiania]]'s streets.

## Real-World Context

St. Olavs plass remains a recognizable square in central [[christiania|Oslo]] today, located near the National Gallery and the University of [[christiania|Oslo]]. In the late nineteenth century, the area was part of [[christiania|Christiania]]'s expanding urban core, with new residential buildings housing the city's growing professional class. The specificity of "No. 2" — an actual address — grounds the novel's psychological intensity in real urban geography, a technique characteristic of [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s literary realism.
