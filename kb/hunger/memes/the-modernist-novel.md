The modernist novel refers to the radical transformation of prose fiction in the late 19th and early 20th centuries, characterized by a turn inward to subjective consciousness, formal experimentation, fragmentation of narrative, and alienation from traditional social and moral frameworks.

## Hunger as Proto-Modernist Novel

Knut Hamsun's *Hunger* (1890) is widely recognized as a foundational text of the modernist novel, appearing decades before the high modernist works of Joyce, Woolf, Kafka, and Proust. Its innovations include:

- **Subjective Narration:** The entire novel is filtered through a single, unstable consciousness whose perceptions are shaped by physical and psychological states.
- **Anti-Realism:** Hamsun rejects the documentary realism of the 19th-century novel in favor of psychological truth rendered through distortion and intensification.
- **Urban Alienation:** The novel situates its psychological drama in the modern city, where traditional community has been replaced by anonymous crowds and market relations.
- **Formal Fragmentation:** The narrative proceeds episodically, driven by associative logic rather than causally linked plot.

## Historical Position

Björkman's introduction notes that *Hunger* was immediately recognized as something new: "a new kind of realism that had nothing to do with photographic reproduction of details." The novel's publication in 1890 places it at the threshold of literary modernism, anticipating the formal and thematic concerns that would define the next half-century of European fiction.
