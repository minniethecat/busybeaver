# The Attic

## Overview

The **attic** at Bernt Akers Street No. 10 is [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s second residence in [[knut-hamsun|Knut Hamsun]]'s *Hunger*. After losing his first rented room, [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] moves to this attic — a space physically elevated yet socially degraded, marking his descent into deeper poverty and isolation. The attic appears in Part II as a defining location for [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s relationships with the women who briefly enter his life.

## In the Narrative

The attic is introduced through [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s connection to a grocer's son, "a student, living in the attic of a five-storeyed house" (002-077). The mention of "five-storeyed" is significant — the attic is at the top of the building, the farthest point from the street, requiring the greatest physical effort to reach. It is a space of marginal habitation, borrowed or rented cheaply, appropriate for someone with no fixed place in the social order.

When [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] asks a girl about the former occupant, she confirms: "Was it he who had the attic?" and adds simply, "He had moved" (002-080). The brevity of this exchange underscores the transience of attic life — people come and go, their residences as precarious as their social standing.

The full address is given with documentary precision: "Bernt Akers Street, No. 10, in the attic" (002-085). The narrator considers whether to go there and "mount all the weary stairs to the attic" (002-086) — the word "weary" conveying both physical exhaustion and [[soul|spirit]]ual fatigue. The stairs are an ordeal, a vertical journey that parallels [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s downward social trajectory: the higher he climbs, the lower he falls.

## Symbolic Significance

The attic is a spatial metaphor for social marginality. It is at the top of the house but at the bottom of the social hierarchy — a paradox that mirrors [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s own condition as an educated man reduced to [[poverty|destitution]]. The attic's elevation offers no prestige, only distance from the world below.

The attic also represents the novel's concern with thresholds and boundaries. The narrator is always on the verge of being ejected — from rooms, from social circles, from the city itself. The attic, at the extreme margin of domestic space, is the last interior refuge before the street. Even here, [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] is not secure; the student "had moved," and [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]], too, will eventually move on.
