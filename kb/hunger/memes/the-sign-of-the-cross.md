"The Sign of the Cross" is a fictional one-act medieval drama that the unnamed narrator of Knut Hamsun's *Hunger* claims to be writing. It exists only as a fiction within the fiction — a story the narrator tells, but whose actual existence remains entirely uncertain.

## The Claim

The narrator mentions the play during a tense encounter with "[[missy|Missy]]" in Part IV. When Missy asks what the narrator does all day now, the narrator replies: "I write, naturally. What else should I do? Is it not that I live by? For the moment, I am working at a great drama, 'The Sign of the Cross.' Theme taken from the Middle Ages" [004-041].

Missy's response — "By Jove!" [004-042] — suggests a mix of surprise and skepticism. The exchange carries dramatic irony: the reader knows the narrator is near destitution and has likely not been writing anything, making the claim of a "great drama" poignant.

## The Title as Self-Portrait

The title itself invites interpretation. "The Sign of the Cross" evokes medieval Christian drama, suggesting a world of moral certainties that stands in stark contrast to the narrator's own fragmented modern existence. The cross — an instrument of suffering transformed into a symbol of redemption — mirrors the narrator's own attempt to convert his suffering into art. In this reading, the fictional play is not merely a lie told to Missy but an idealized projection of the narrator's creative aspirations: the medieval drama he wishes he could write, the coherent work of art his own psychological state cannot produce.

The medieval setting also distances the work from the narrator's present circumstances. Just as the narrator seeks escape from Christiania through the *Copégoro* at the novel's end, his imagination seeks escape from modernity through a medieval creative project. The temporal distance between the medieval theme and the 1890s setting of the novel parallels the spatial distance between Christiania and the open sea.

## Narrative Function

Whether the drama ever existed beyond the narrator's boast is left deliberately ambiguous. Like many of the narrator's self-representations — his tales of his supposed landlord Happolati, his imagined inventions, his fabricated encounters — "The Sign of the Cross" may be a product of his desperate need to maintain a social identity as a writer, even when his actual productivity has collapsed.

The play functions as a marker of the gap between the narrator's self-image as a literary figure and the harsh reality of his situation. It is mentioned precisely once in the novel, in this single exchange with Missy, and never referred to again — a fact that strongly suggests it was invented for that specific social performance. The narrator's assertion that he is working on it "For the moment" [004-041] is particularly telling: the present continuous masks the absence of any actual work.

In the broader pattern of the narrator's inventions throughout *Hunger*, "The Sign of the Cross" belongs to a category of fictions told not for material gain (unlike his lies to obtain money) but for identity maintenance — the stories he tells to preserve the fiction that he remains a functioning writer, even as starvation and psychological deterioration make writing impossible.
