Bookseller Pascha runs a bookshop in [[christiania|Christiania]] that serves as a reference point in the narrator's urban geography. The shop is one of several commercial establishments the narrator passes during his perambulations through the city.

## In the Narrative

The narrator observes Pascha's shop during a street encounter: "A few minutes later, they reached Pascha's bookshop. I had already stopped at the first window, and as they go by I step forward" [001-046]. The bookshop functions as both a physical landmark and a reminder of the literary world the narrator aspires to join.

Pascha's shop reappears later in the novel as a practical resource, when the narrator enters it to consult a directory: "I entered Pascha's book-shop, found Pastor Levion's address in the directory, and started for it" [001-068]. This moment reveals the bookshop's dual nature — a commercial establishment that nevertheless provides access to information the narrator needs. The fact that he can enter freely and consult a directory without purchasing anything suggests a space of conditional public access.

## Symbolic Function

The narrator later invokes Pascha's name in a moment of sardonic reflection: "Why not select just as well Bookseller Pascha, or Hennechen the steam agent?" [001-067]. This offhand pairing of the bookseller with a steam agent suggests the commercial, rather than intellectual, character of Pascha's trade — bookselling as business rather than literary pursuit. The narrator's rhetorical question frames Pascha as a representative of the mundane commercial world against which his own suffering is measured. If God's arbitrary selection of a victim could have fallen on anyone, why not on the comfortably employed Pascha?

The question recurs later in the same Part, when the narrator again compares his plight to those of ordinary working men: "Was I not just as much entitled to live as any one else? for example, as Bookseller Pascha or Steam Agent Hennechen?" [001-072]. The repetition of this pairing cements Pascha's symbolic role — he is the stable, employed, respectable citizen whose unexamined life contrasts with the narrator's acute awareness of his own degradation.

Pascha, like many minor characters in the novel, never interacts directly with the narrator. He exists at the periphery of the narrative, one of the many figures who populate the commercial landscape of late nineteenth-century Christiania and whose presence defines the urban environment through which the hungry protagonist moves. His bookshop, with its directories and printed matter, represents a world of ordered knowledge that stands in ironic counterpoint to the narrator's disintegrating mental state.

## Place in the Urban Geography

Pascha's bookshop is one of several fixed commercial points that anchor the narrator's otherwise disorienting wanderings through Christiania. Alongside Semb the draper and other shopkeepers, Pascha contributes to the novel's detailed mapping of the city's retail geography. The bookshop's location on a street the narrator frequents makes it part of his habitual circuit — a static presence in a life marked by constant motion and instability.
