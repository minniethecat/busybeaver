## Overview

Reality in Knut Hamsun's *Hunger* serves as an intrusive counterforce to the narrator's elaborate inner life. The narrator continuously constructs fantasies, rationalizations, and self-justifications, but objective reality — his poverty, his hunger, his social degradation — repeatedly breaks through, forcing moments of painful recognition. The word "reality" itself appears with unusual frequency in his monologue, functioning as a signal that a corrective is about to be applied to his self-deceptions.

## The Rhetoric of "In Reality"

The narrator frequently deploys the phrase "in reality" as a self-correcting mechanism. When describing a broken chair, he notes that "the chair over in the corner was in reality a mere attempt at a rocking-chair" (001-157). This parenthetical correction reveals a characteristic movement of his mind: first the embellishment, then the deflation. The chair is not merely a chair; it aspires to be a rocking-chair, but fails. The narrator's syntax enacts the same pattern as his broader psychology — reach upward, then correct downward.

When lying in bed, he admits: "I try and fall asleep, but in reality to wrestle again with the dark" (002-023). Here "in reality" does not so much correct a falsehood as it deepens one. The struggle with darkness is presented as the truth beneath the pretense of rest. Similarly, reflecting on language, he notes that "in reality the word is fitted to signify the darkness of the soul" (002-026), using the concept to ground linguistic abstraction in emotional truth. Language is not arbitrary for this narrator; words carry real, dark weight.

## Reality as Intrusion

External reality intrudes most jarringly through encounters with other people. When the landlady appears, "I am recalled at once to reality. I recognize 'Scissors'" (002-119) — the arrival of another person shatters his reverie and returns him to his impoverished material circumstances. The nickname "Scissors" anchors the landlady in the narrator's private taxonomy even as her physical presence forces him back into the shared world. Other people in *Hunger* function as agents of reality, their mere presence sufficient to puncture the narrator's self-enclosed mental theater.

The social dimension of reality becomes especially acute around questions of appearance. The narrator becomes preoccupied with how others perceive him, asking, "How did I look in reality?" (002-172). This question reveals an anxious awareness that his self-image may not match objective perception — that the person he experiences himself to be is not the person others see. The gap between self-conception and social reality is one of the novel's deepest preoccupations.

## The Cost of Truth

Even positive developments carry a hidden cost in the narrator's relationship with reality. When he briefly comes into money, he reflects that "this money had in reality cost me much secret anguish" (003-016), acknowledging that his brief financial relief was purchased through deception — a lie told to a concerned editor. The phrase "in reality" here serves a retrospective moral function: it admits the true price of the transaction, which the surface narrative of good fortune obscures.

This pattern — reality as the unwelcome truth that must eventually be acknowledged — defines the narrator's entire trajectory through the novel. His fantasies, his lies, his grandiose self-projections are not escapes from reality but deferrals of its reckoning. Each intrusion of the real — a landlady's knock, a policeman's glance, the physical collapse of hunger — is not an interruption but a return. Reality is what the narrator keeps trying to leave, and what keeps finding him.
