## Overview

*Brushwood* (*Kratskog*) is a short story collection by Knut Hamsun, published in 1903. It belongs to the middle period of Hamsun's career, during which he produced a steady stream of fiction, travel writing, and poetry (P04-015).

## Description

The collection appeared as part of a prolific decade for Hamsun, which also saw the publication of *Victoria* (1898), *In Wonderland* (1903, travel sketches from the Caucasus), *The Wild Choir* (1904, poetry), and several novels. *Brushwood* represents Hamsun's continuing engagement with the short story form alongside his longer fictional works (P04-015).

## Place in Hamsun's Career

By 1903 Hamsun's reputation was firmly established — *Hunger* (1890) had announced his arrival, *Pan* (1894) had confirmed his talent, and *Victoria* (1898) had demonstrated his range. *Brushwood* appeared at a moment when Hamsun was diversifying into multiple genres simultaneously: fiction, travel sketches, poetry, and drama. This period of generic experimentation preceded the broader social canvases of his later novels, such as *Children of the Time* (1913) and *Segelfoss Town* (1915). The short story form, with its compression and intensity, suited a writer whose primary gift was for psychological concentration rather than elaborate plotting.

## The Short Story Form

Hamsun's turn to the short story in *Brushwood* can be understood as an extension of the episodic structure that characterized *Hunger* itself. Where *Hunger* strings together loosely connected episodes unified by a single consciousness, the short story collection allows each episode to stand as an independent work while still benefiting from the cumulative effect of shared themes and sensibility. The form permits Hamsun to explore a range of characters and situations — urban and rural, comic and tragic — that a single novel could not accommodate, while maintaining the intensity of focus that was his signature contribution to modern narrative technique.

## Relationship to Hunger

By 1903, Hamsun had moved well beyond the psychological intensity of *Hunger* (1890) into a broader range of genres and tones. The short story form allowed him to experiment with narrative techniques that would inform his later novels, including the panoramic social observation found in *Children of the Time* and *Segelfoss Town* (P04-015).
