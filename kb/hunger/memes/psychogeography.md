Psychogeography — the study of the effects of geographical environment on emotions and behavior — provides a useful framework for understanding the urban space of Knut Hamsun's *Hunger*. Though the term was coined by the Situationist International in the 1950s, the practice of mapping psychological experience onto urban geography has a longer literary history.

## Kristiania as Psychological Landscape

In *Hunger*, Kristiania is not a neutral backdrop but an active force shaping the narrator's consciousness. Different districts encode different emotional valences: the impoverished Vaterland district, the bourgeois promenade of Karl Johans Gate, the liminal harbour, the cemetery with its intimations of mortality. The narrator's wanderings trace a psychogeographic map of the city as a terrain of anxiety, desire, humiliation, and fleeting transcendence.

## The Wandering Subject

The narrator's characteristic activity is walking — aimless, compulsive, driven by hunger and nervous energy. This wandering is not the purposeful movement of the flâneur but a more desperate and involuntary mapping of urban space through the distressed body. Each street corner, each shop window, each public square becomes a node in a network of remembered encounters, failures, and fantasies.
