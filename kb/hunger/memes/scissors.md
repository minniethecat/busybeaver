"Scissors" is the narrator's nickname for a newspaper office clerk who serves as gatekeeper between the narrator and the editor in Knut Hamsun's *Hunger*. The name derives from his characteristic activity — "probing for minor items of news amongst the provincial papers" with scissors [002-070].

Scissors first appears at the newspaper office when the narrator arrives to submit a manuscript. He "is running through a lot of old papers" and informs the narrator that the editor has not yet arrived [001-174]. When the narrator impresses upon him the importance of the manuscript, Scissors replies with laconic indifference: "'All right,' replied 'Scissors,' and busied himself again with his papers" [001-176].

On a later visit the narrator "inquires timorously for the editor" and receives no answer — Scissors "sits and probes for minor items of news" [002-070]. After a long pause, he finally speaks without looking up: "The editor has not come yet!" [002-072]. The narrator realizes with humiliation that Scissors has not once looked at him during the entire exchange, having recognized him by voice alone. The narrator interprets this as a sign of his low standing: "You are in such bad odour here" [002-076].

In another encounter, Scissors attempts to pass the narrator on the street without acknowledgment — "doesn't even acknowledge my nod; is suddenly intently busied with his nails" [002-119]. The narrator must assert himself to be led to the editor's private office [002-123].

Scissors embodies the impersonal bureaucracy of the newspaper world and the minor humiliations the narrator endures. His mechanical work with scissors — cutting, sorting, discarding — mirrors the newspaper's power to accept or reject the narrator's literary output.

## The Newspaper as Gatekeeping Machine

Scissors functions as the first tier in a layered system of access that the narrator must navigate. Before he can reach the editor — who holds the power of publication and payment — he must pass through Scissors, a figure whose authority is entirely procedural yet absolute within its narrow domain. The narrator's literary ambition is subject to a clerk whose primary qualification is physical proximity to the editor's door.

The newspaper's architecture mirrors this hierarchy. The outer room, where Scissors works, is a space of waiting and uncertainty. The inner office, where the editor sits, is the site of judgment and reward. Between them stands Scissors, whose indifference is more wounding than overt hostility would be — he does not reject the narrator so much as fail to register his existence. The narrator is "forced to leave" because "Scissors had not once looked up at me" [002-076], a dismissal accomplished not through words but through the refusal to grant eye contact.

> The man sits and probes for minor items of news amongst the provincial papers with scissors. I inquire timorously for the editor. No answer (002-070).

This scene captures the essential dynamic: the narrator's "timorous" approach meets a silence that is not hostile but merely occupied — Scissors is busy with work that, from his perspective, matters more than the supplicant before him. The scissors themselves are the instrument of a value judgment: what is worth clipping and what is discarded. The narrator's presence, like his manuscripts, falls into the latter category.

## Narrative Function

Beyond his role as gatekeeper, Scissors serves as a barometer of the narrator's social standing across the arc of the novel. His behavior toward the narrator — from curt acknowledgment to studied avoidance — registers the narrator's declining status with more precision than the narrator's own self-assessments. When Scissors refuses to acknowledge a greeting on the street [002-119], the slight is objective evidence that the narrator's position in the social world has deteriorated, confirming what his internal monologue only suspects.

The nickname "Scissors" is itself an act of narrative power — the one domain where the narrator retains authority. By naming the clerk after his tool, the narrator transforms a figure of institutional indifference into a character he controls. The nickname reduces a man to his function, a gesture of ironic mastery that mirrors the dehumanization the narrator himself experiences. In a novel where the protagonist is named by no one, his ability to name others — "Scissors," "Missy," "the Commandor" — is one of his few remaining prerogatives.
