The dialectic of boredom and nervous overstimulation is a structuring rhythm of Knut Hamsun's *Hunger*, reflecting the psychological conditions of modern urban existence.

## The Empty Room

The narrator's attic room is a space of enforced idleness and sensory deprivation. He lies awake listening to clocks strike, reads old newspaper wallpaper, and dozes in his red rocking-chair. These scenes of inactivity and boredom are the negative pole of the novel's psychological rhythm.

## The Overstimulated Street

Counterposed to the empty room is the overstimulating street, where the narrator's senses are assaulted by crowds, noises, sights, and the constant pressure of social encounter. His wanderings through Kristiania alternate between hyper-alert observation and exhausted withdrawal — a pattern that anticipates later analyses of urban sensory overload.

## Modernist Significance

The boredom-stimulation oscillation is central to modernist literature's mapping of urban consciousness. From Baudelaire's Paris to Joyce's Dublin to Eliot's London, the modern city is experienced as a field of excessive stimuli that paradoxically produces states of numbness and alienation. Hamsun's *Hunger* captures this dynamic with pioneering intensity.
