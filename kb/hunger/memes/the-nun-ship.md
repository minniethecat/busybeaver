# The Nun

The *Nun* is a ship that appears in the novel as a recurring symbol of escape. Moored alongside the wharf in Christiania, it represents the narrator's last-resort fantasy of fleeing his desperate circumstances by working his way to Archangel or wherever else the vessel might be bound.[001-1274]

## The Ship as Escape

During one of his lowest periods, the narrator consoles himself with the thought that even if his writing career fails entirely, he still has the option of taking to the ships. The *Nun* lies at the wharf ready to sail, and he imagines working his passage out to Archangel in northern Russia. The narrator frames this as a practical fallback — a recognition that "there was no lack of openings on many sides" — even as his physical condition deteriorates dramatically.[001-1274]

> Supposing that writing were to fail, and the worst were to come to the worst, I still had the ships to take to. The *Nun* lay alongside the wharf, ready to sail, and I might, perhaps, work my way out to Archangel, or wherever else she might be bound; there was no lack of openings on many sides.[001-1274]

## The Inquiry at the Wharf

Later in the novel, the narrator finds himself wandering down to the railway wharf in a state of extreme physical and emotional exhaustion. He encounters a lame man and instinctively asks whether the *Nun* has sailed. The question surprises even the narrator himself — he snaps his fingers and exclaims "Ay, by Jove, the *Nun*; yes, the *Nun*!" — realizing that "the thought of her had been smouldering in me. I had carried it about unconsciously."[001-1858]

The lame man confirms that the *Nun* has indeed sailed, but cannot say where she was bound or what cargo she had taken on. By this point, however, the narrator has already lost interest in the ship, his attention drifting instead to questions about the distance to Holmestrand.[001-1858]

> In this manner, I reached the railway wharf. There was no traffic, no noise — only here and there a person to be seen, a labourer or sailor slinking round with their hands in their pockets. I took notice of a lame man, who looked sharply at me as we passed one another. I stopped him instinctively, touched my hat, and inquired if he knew if the Nun had sailed. Someway, I couldn't help snapping my fingers right under the man's nose, and saying, "Ay, by Jove, the *Nun*; yes, the *Nun*!" which I had totally forgotten. All the same, the thought of her had been smouldering in me. I had carried it about unconsciously.[001-1858]

## Symbolic Significance

The *Nun* functions as an escape fantasy that the narrator carries with him as psychological insurance. When his writing is rejected and his body fails him, the idea of simply boarding a ship and sailing away offers a form of mental relief. Yet the ship's actual departure — it has already sailed when he finally goes to the wharf — mirrors the narrator's inability to act on his own escape fantasies. The vessel that might have carried him away has gone without him, and even the knowledge of its destination is lost.[001-1860]

The *Nun* is one of several Christiania landmarks that anchor the novel's geography to the narrator's inner life, alongside [[our-saviours-church]] and [[the-wharf]].
