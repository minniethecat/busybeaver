The Grocer Christy keeps a shop at No. 13 Groenlandsleret in Christiania. He is one of the few named commercial figures in the novel and represents a possible escape from the narrator's economic desperation — a hope that remains perpetually unrealized.

## The Advertisement and Application

The narrator discovers the grocer's advertisement while scanning newspaper placards for employment opportunities. The notice seeks a man "for a couple of hours' book-keeping" with remuneration to be negotiated. The narrator resolves to demand less than anyone else for the work — "sixpence was ample, or perhaps fivepence" — and prays silently for the position [001-120]. He writes an application and posts it without visiting in person, a decision that reflects both his social anxiety and his tendency to defer direct confrontation with potential employers.

## The Unfulfilled Approach

Days later, driven by hunger and the failure of all other options, the narrator recalls the grocer. He reflects that he has not even applied personally or sought an answer, reasoning that his previous lack of follow-through does not guarantee failure [001-153]. He sets out for Groenlandsleret, telling himself that the grocer might be a generous soul who would pay him a shilling in advance — "People of that sort had sometimes the most capital ideas" [001-153].

## The Open-Ended Possibility

The novel never reveals whether the narrator ultimately meets Christy or secures the position. Like many of the narrator's hopes, the grocer remains a possibility that hovers at the edge of the narrative, never confirmed or denied. This structural indeterminacy is characteristic of Hamsun's method in *Hunger*: the narrative follows the rhythms of the narrator's consciousness, and when his attention wanders — as it inevitably does — the grocer is simply left behind.

The grocer's function in the novel is thus not as a developed character but as the embodiment of a specific kind of hope: the hope for regular, modest, respectable employment that would rescue the narrator from his downward spiral. That this hope remains unrealized — indeed, that the narrator cannot even bring himself to pursue it consistently — speaks to the profound dislocation between his self-image as an intellectual and the practical demands of economic survival in the modern city.
