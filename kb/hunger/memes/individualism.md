Individualism, in the context of Knut Hamsun's work, refers to the philosophical and aesthetic commitment to the sovereignty of individual consciousness, values, and will over and against social convention, collective morality, and democratic leveling.

## Hamsun's Individualism

Edwin Björkman's introduction to *Hunger* identifies Hamsun as "an individualistic romanticist and a highly subjective aristocrat, whose foremost passion in life is violent, defiant deviation from everything average and ordinary." This characterization captures the aggressive, aristocratic quality of Hamsun's individualism — it is not the liberal individualism of rights and tolerance but a Nietzschean assertion of exceptional being against the "dominance of the many."

Björkman notes that Hamsun "fears and flouts the dominance of the many, and his heroes, who are nothing but slightly varied images of himself, are invariably marked by an originality of speech and action that brings them close to, if not across, the borderline of the eccentric."

## Individualism in Hunger

The narrator of *Hunger* embodies individualism as both strength and pathology. His refusal to accept charity, his insistence on maintaining appearances, his creative ambitions — all express a fierce commitment to individual dignity. Yet this same individualism isolates him, drives him to self-destructive acts of pride, and ultimately proves unsustainable within the social world of Kristiania.

This tension — individualism as the highest value and the deepest source of suffering — is central to Hamsun's tragic vision. His heroes are condemned to authenticity in a world that rewards conformity.
