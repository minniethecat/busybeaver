## Overview

Isolation in Knut Hamsun's *Hunger* operates on multiple levels: physical (the narrator's attic room, his wandering through indifferent streets), social (his inability to maintain relationships or secure employment), and existential (his detachment from the norms and rhythms of bourgeois life). This layered isolation is both a consequence of his poverty and a condition he paradoxically cultivates to protect his fragile sense of exceptionality.

## In Hunger

The narrator's physical isolation is most starkly represented by his bare attic room — a space without proper fastening, without a stove, where the floor "rocked up and down with every step I took across it" (001-004). This room is both refuge and prison, offering privacy but also sealing him off from the world of warmth and human connection.

His social isolation manifests in fleeting, failed encounters. After leaving an editor's office, he reflects, "If I only had some place in which to take refuge for the night! I consider where I can stow myself away, and am so absorbed in this query that I come to a standstill in the middle of the street" (001-209). Homelessness literalizes his social displacement — he has no place in the city's geography of belonging.

The narrator's isolation is also self-imposed. He resists offers of help, lies about his circumstances, and cultivates a persona of artistic superiority that distances him from ordinary people. His isolation thus functions simultaneously as a mark of his degradation and a badge of his exceptional sensibility, reflecting the modernist trope of the alienated artist that Hamsun helped pioneer.
