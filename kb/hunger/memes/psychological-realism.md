Psychological realism is a literary mode that foregrounds the interior life of characters — their thoughts, feelings, perceptions, and psychological processes — over external plot events or social documentation. It emerged in the 19th century as novelists increasingly turned inward, exploring the complexities and contradictions of individual consciousness.

## From Dostoyevski to Hamsun

Fyodor Dostoyevski is often credited as a pioneer of psychological realism, and the comparison between Hamsun and Dostoyevski is made repeatedly in Björkman's introduction to *Hunger*. However, Hamsun radicalized psychological realism in a distinctly modernist direction.

Traditional psychological realism (as in Dostoyevski or Henry James) still operates within a framework of coherent character and causal motivation. Hamsun's innovation in *Hunger* is to present consciousness as fragmentary, contradictory, and driven by bodily states rather than rational deliberation. The narrator's hunger is not merely a condition he endures but the very medium of his consciousness — his perceptions, decisions, and self-narratives are functions of his physiological state.

## Anticipating Stream of Consciousness

*Hunger* (1890) predates the major stream-of-consciousness novels — Dorothy Richardson's *Pointed Roofs* (1915), Joyce's *Ulysses* (1922), Woolf's *Mrs Dalloway* (1925) — by decades. While Hamsun does not fully abandon narrative structure for associative flow, the novel's intense focus on the narrator's moment-to-moment subjective experience, its temporal fragmentation, and its willingness to render irrational or contradictory mental states make it a crucial precursor to high modernist psychological fiction.
