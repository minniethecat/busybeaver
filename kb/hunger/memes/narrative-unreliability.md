Narrative unreliability describes a narrative situation in which the reader has reason to doubt the accuracy, completeness, or honesty of the narrator's account. While the concept was formalized by Wayne C. Booth in *The Rhetoric of Fiction* (1961), modernist writers including Hamsun had been practicing unreliability for decades.

## Unreliability in Hunger

The narrator of *Hunger* is unreliable on multiple levels:

**Physiological Unreliability:** Hunger distorts perception. The narrator experiences hallucinations, vertigo, and cognitive impairment. His sensory reports cannot be trusted as objective descriptions of the world.

**Psychological Unreliability:** Pride and self-deception shape the narrator's account of his own motivations. He constructs elaborate rationalizations for self-destructive behavior, narrating himself as a romantic hero when his actions suggest something closer to pathology.

**Creative Unreliability:** The narrator is a writer, and his account of events is inevitably shaped by literary convention and creative imagination. The novel blurs the line between what happened and what the narrator has invented — most notably in the Ylajali episodes, where fantasy and reality become indistinguishable.

## Modernist Significance

Hamsun's use of unreliability is historically significant because it emerges not from a postmodern skepticism about truth but from a modernist commitment to psychological authenticity. The narrator is unreliable not because truth is impossible but because consciousness itself — shaped by bodily states, emotions, and desires — is inherently distorting. This is unreliability as psychological realism, not as epistemological critique.
