# Knut Hamsun

## Overview

**Knut Hamsun** (born Knud Pedersen, August 4, 1859 – February 19, 1952) was a Norwegian writer and Nobel Prize laureate whose novel *[[hunger|Hunger]]* (*Sult*, 1890) is widely regarded as one of the first works of literary modernism. The preface to the English edition of *Hunger*, written by [[edwin-bjorkman|Edwin Björkman]], provides a detailed account of Hamsun's life, literary career, and significance within Scandinavian and European letters (P04-001).

## In the Narrative

Hamsun does not appear as a character in *Hunger*, but the preface (P04) constitutes a substantial biographical and critical essay. Björkman describes Hamsun's birth "on Aug. 4, 1860, in one of the sunny valleys of central [[norway|Norway]]" (P04-005), his family's move to [[lofoten|Lofoten]] at age four, and his early literary ambitions. Apprenticed to a shoemaker, Hamsun "used his scant savings to arrange for the private printing of a long poem and a short novel produced at the age of eighteen" (P04-007), signing himself Knud Pedersen Hamsund.

Before achieving literary success, Hamsun "had tried life as coal-heaver and school teacher, as road-mender and surveyor's attendant, as farm hand and streetcar conductor, as lecturer and free-lance journalist, as tourist and emigrant" (P04-008). He twice visited America, "working chiefly on the plains of North Dakota and in the streets of Chicago" (P04-008), an experience that produced a critical volume, *[[the-spiritual-life-of-modern-america|The Spiritual Life of Modern America]]*.

The preface positions Hamsun's literary achievement as emerging from this restless, self-invented life. The Northland, "with its glaring lights and black shadows, its unearthly joys and abysmal despairs, is present and dominant in every line that Hamsun ever wrote" (P04-006). The first sketch of *Hunger* appeared in 1888 in the Danish periodical *[[new-earth-periodical|New Earth]]*, and the completed novel in 1890 "was greeted as one of the chief heralds of the neo-romantic movement" (P04-010). Subsequent works — *[[mysteries|Mysteries]]* (1892), *[[pan|Pan]]* (1894), [[the-kareno-trilogy|the Kareno trilogy]], *[[the-growth-of-the-soil|The Growth of the Soil]]* (1917) — established Hamsun as "undoubtedly the foremost creative writer of the Scandinavian countries" (P04-001) after the deaths of [[henrik-ibsen|Ibsen]] and [[august-strindberg|Strindberg]].

## Relationship to the Narrator

Hamsun's relationship to [[the-unnamed-narrator|the unnamed narrator]] of *Hunger* is complex and deliberately ambiguous. Björkman notes that "practically every one of Hamsun's heroes is of the same age as he was then" (P04-007) — [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] is a version of Hamsun, filtered through the lens of fiction. "In all the literature known to me, there is no writer who appears more ruthlessly and fearlessly himself" (P04-004), Björkman writes, confirming that the narrator's voice is drawn directly from Hamsun's own character. The novel draws on Hamsun's own experiences of [[poverty|poverty]] and artistic [[struggle|struggle]] in [[christiania|Christiania]], making it a work of autobiographical psychological realism.

## Critical Context

Hamsun's literary significance was immediate and enduring. The preface describes him as "an individualistic romanticist and a highly subjective aristocrat, whose foremost passion in life is violent, defiant deviation from everything average and ordinary" (P04-003). His psychological method — rendering consciousness from within rather than observing it from without — was a "new kind of realism" that "had nothing to do with photographic reproduction of details" (P04-010), influencing the development of modernist narrative across Europe. The preface notes that *Hunger* was "life, but presented in the Impressionistic temper of a Gauguin or Cezanne" (P04-010).

After the novels of the 1890s — including the pastoral lyricism of *Pan*, "into which he poured the ecstatic [[dreams|dreams]] of the little boy from the south" (P04-012) — Hamsun "turned suddenly to the drama" (P04-013), producing the Kareno trilogy (1895–1898) and later the verse drama *[[vendt-the-monk|Vendt the Monk]]* (1903). The period from 1897 to 1912 saw a rich output of novels, short stories, and poetry (P04-015).

His Nobel Prize in 1920 for *The Growth of the Soil* — "[[the-earths-increase|the Earth's Increase]]," as Hamsun preferred (P04-017) — confirmed his status as a major figure in world literature, though his later political sympathies during World War II have complicated his legacy.
