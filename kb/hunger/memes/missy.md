"Missy" is a recurring acquaintance of the unnamed narrator in Knut Hamsun's *Hunger*, appearing primarily in Parts I and IV. The narrator explicitly states from the beginning that he "never cared particularly for 'Missy'" [001-179], establishing an ambivalent relationship that persists throughout the novel.

Missy first appears in Part I, approaching the narrator at the market and greeting him. His inquisitive nature is immediately evident as he peers at the narrator's parcel and asks questions [001-179]. The encounter is brief but establishes Missy as a figure who inserts himself into the narrator's life without genuine warmth.

In Part IV, Missy reappears in [[carl-johann-street|Carl Johann Street]], greeting the narrator outside a shoemaker's shop [004-008]. This encounter marks a pivotal moment, as Missy spots [[ylajali|Ylajali]] walking with her companion and identifies the man as "The Duke" — the "so-called 'Duke'" [004-027]. Missy's gossiping reveals key information about Ylajali's social world: "if she is in contact with that fellow; well, then, I wouldn't like to answer for her" [004-037].

Missy also inquires intrusively about the narrator's work, asking "But what do you do all day now?" [004-040]. The narrator tells him he is writing "a great drama, 'The Sign of the Cross,' theme taken from the Middle Ages" [004-041], which elicits Missy's skeptical but impressed "By Jove!" [004-042]. The encounter reveals Missy's function as a character who surfaces social information the narrator would otherwise lack, while also serving as a minor irritant — a figure of gossip and superficial concern.

## The Informant Function

Missy's narrative role extends beyond that of a minor acquaintance. He functions as an unwitting informant, delivering to the narrator — and thus to the reader — crucial intelligence about the social world from which the protagonist is excluded. The narrator, isolated by poverty and pride, has no access to the circles in which Ylajali moves. Missy, by contrast, occupies the permeable social space of the street — he knows who walks with whom, who is connected to whom, and what those connections imply.

The information Missy provides about Ylajali's companion is not solicited; it is volunteered, almost compulsively. His "gossipy interest" in the narrator's affairs [004-040] is paired with an equally intense interest in the affairs of others. This makes him a conduit for social intelligence that operates independently of the narrator's needs or desires — a leak in the wall that separates the protagonist from the society he observes but cannot join.

The narrator's relationship to this information is characteristically ambivalent. He needs what Missy knows but resents the manner of its delivery. The information about Ylajali and "the Duke" wounds him even as it enlightens him, confirming suspicions he has not wanted to examine. Missy's gossip, like his nickname, is a diminutive form that carries disproportionate weight.

## Gossip as Social Currency

Missy's mode of interaction — the casual inquiry, the knowing glance, the insinuating remark — operates within an economy of gossip in which information is exchanged for social standing. By demonstrating what he knows about the narrator's activities and Ylajali's companions, Missy asserts a position of social competence that the narrator cannot match.

When Missy asks "what do you do all day now?" [004-040], the question is not merely conversational. It probes the narrator's status, and the narrator's response — the inflated claim of writing "a great drama" [004-041] — is a defensive performance designed to counter the implied inquiry. The exchange maps the asymmetry of their positions: Missy asks, the narrator explains; Missy knows, the narrator pretends.

Yet the relationship is not one of simple dominance. Missy's "By Jove!" [004-042] registers genuine — if fleeting — respect for the narrator's artistic ambition. And the narrator's continued engagement with Missy, despite his stated antipathy, suggests a reluctant dependence on this channel of social information. In a novel structured by the protagonist's radical isolation, Missy is one of the few figures who consistently crosses the boundary between the narrator's private world and the public life of the city.
