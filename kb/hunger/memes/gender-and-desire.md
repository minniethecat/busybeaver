Gender and desire play a complex role in Knut Hamsun's *Hunger*, most prominently in the narrator's encounter with the woman he names Ylajali. This episode introduces erotic longing into a novel otherwise dominated by material deprivation and psychological interiority.

## The Ylajali Episode

The narrator's pursuit of and fantasized relationship with Ylajali occupies a significant portion of the novel. The encounter is marked by the same ambivalence that characterizes all the narrator's social interactions: desperate longing combined with proud self-sabotage. He invents the name "Ylajali" for her — a creative act that transforms the real woman into a projection of his imagination.

## Femininity as Salvation and Threat

Hamsun's female characters — Ylajali in *Hunger*, Edvarda in *Pan*, Eline and Teresita in the trilogy — often embody the dual nature Björkman attributes to Teresita: the "passionate and changeable Northland nature." Women in Hamsun represent both the possibility of connection (and thus escape from solipsistic isolation) and the threat of surrender (and thus loss of the proud self that defines the Hamsun hero).
