# At the Mercy of Life

## Overview

**At the Mercy of Life** is a play by [[knut-hamsun|Knut Hamsun]], published in 1910, listed among [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s dramatic works in the preface to *Hunger*. It represents one part of [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s remarkably diverse literary output during his most productive decades.

## Description

The preface to *Hunger* groups At the Mercy of Life with two other Hamsun plays from the same period: "Of three subsequent plays, 'Vendt the Monk,' (1903), 'Queen Tamara' (1903) and 'At the Mercy of Life' (1910), the first mentioned is by far the most remarkable" [P04-014]. The work belongs to the period of [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s career when he was moving restlessly between forms — novels, plays, poetry, travel sketches, short stories — testing the capacities of each genre while building the body of work that would culminate in the Nobel Prize for *[[the-growth-of-the-soil|The Growth of the Soil]]* in 1920.

## Relationship to Hunger

At the Mercy of Life is connected to *Hunger* primarily through the arc of [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s career — it represents a stage in the development of the writer whose first major novel established the psychological method that would influence European modernism. The preface's inclusion of At the Mercy of Life in its bibliographic catalogue [P04-014] attests to its place in [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s complete works, even if its relationship to *Hunger* specifically is one of chronology rather than direct thematic connection.

## Critical Reception

As a work from [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s middle period, At the Mercy of Life has received less critical attention than his major novels. Its significance lies primarily in its place within the arc of [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s career — a career remarkable for its generic range and its sustained exploration of the psychology of exceptional individuals.
