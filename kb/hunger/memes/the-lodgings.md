# The Lodgings

## Overview

The **lodgings** at Tomtegaden No. 11 in [[christiania|Christiania]] represent [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s third and most stable residence in [[knut-hamsun|Knut Hamsun]]'s *Hunger*. Advertised with the sign "Entertainment and lodgings for travellers" (003-117), this establishment offers [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] a temporary reprieve from the cycle of eviction that defines his earlier dwellings. It is here that he lives through much of Part III, conducting his surveillance of [[ylajali|Ylajali]] and his sporadic attempts at writing.

## In the Narrative

The lodgings first appear as the cab driver's destination: "Drive to [[tomtegaden|Tomtegaden]], No. 11" (003-026). The narrator arrives at the building whose sign reads "Refreshment and lodgings for travellers" (003-042) — a commercial promise of rest and sustenance. The building's identity as a travellers' lodging is significant: it is designed for transients, people passing through. The narrator, too, is a transient — but his transience is chronic, not circumstantial.

The narrator's return to the lodgings after his wanderings is rendered with a striking image: "I found myself, singularly enough, outside No. 11 [[tomtegaden|Tomtegaden]]. After I had stood and collected my thoughts for a moment, I went into the 'Entertainment and lodgings for travellers.' Here I asked for shelter and" (003-117). The phrase "singularly enough" suggests [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s detachment from his own movements — he arrives at the lodgings not by conscious decision but by a kind of bodily autopilot. The sentence breaks off before revealing what he asked for beyond shelter, as if even the narration cannot complete the acknowledgment of his need.

The lodgings are "fairly comfortable" (003-120) — [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s own judgment, measured against his previous accommodations. From the newspaper-walled room to the five-storeyed attic to Tomtegaden No. 11, [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s residences trace a trajectory not of improvement but of oscillation. The lodgings are better than the attic but not permanent; they are "for travellers," and [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] will, inevitably, travel on.

## Symbolic Significance

The lodgings embody [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s condition of permanent transience. The sign "for travellers" announces what [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] cannot admit: that he belongs nowhere, that every dwelling is temporary, that his true address is the street. The lodgings offer "entertainment" as well as shelter — a commodity [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] can no more afford than the roof over his head. He is a traveller without a destination, a lodger without a home.

The narrator's unconscious navigation back to the lodgings ("singularly enough") reveals the depth of his estrangement from his own agency. He does not decide to return; his body brings him there while his mind is elsewhere. The lodgings are the space [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] inhabits when he is not fully present to his own life.
