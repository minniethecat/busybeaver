# Segelfoss Town

## Overview

**Segelfoss Town** (*Segelfoss by*) is a novel by [[knut-hamsun|Knut Hamsun]], published in 1915. The English-language preface to *Hunger* references it in the chronological listing of [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s major works: "'Segelfoss Town' in 1915 and '[[the-growth-of-the-soil|The Growth of the Soil]]' in 1917" (P04-017). The novel belongs to [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s later mature period, appearing just two years before the work that would win him the Nobel Prize in Literature. It continues the social panorama first opened in *[[children-of-the-age|Children of the Age]]* (1913), forming the middle volume of what might be called the Segelfoss cycle (P04-016).

## Description

*Segelfoss Town* forms part of [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s exploration of small-town Norwegian life in the 1910s, a theme that reaches its fullest expression in *[[the-growth-of-the-soil|The Growth of the Soil]]* (1917). The Segelfoss setting — a fictional Norwegian coastal town — allowed [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]] to develop the social and economic themes that distinguish his later novels from the psychological intensity of his early works like *Hunger*. The novel continues [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s shift away from the first-person urban narratives of the 1890s toward a broader, more panoramic vision of Norwegian society.

The preface describes how [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s vision expanded in this period: "The life pictured is the life of a certain spot of ground — Segelfoss manor, and later the town of Segelfoss — rather than that of one or two isolated individuals. One might almost say that Hamsun's vision has become social at last" (P04-016). The novel is set in [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s beloved Northland, but captures a moment of transition: "the old primitive life is going — going even in the outlying districts, where the pioneers are already breaking ground for new permanent settlements. Business of a modern type has arrived" (P04-017). The preface notes that "much of the quiet humor displayed in these the latest and maturest of Hamsun's works springs from the spectacle of its influence on the natives, whose hands used always to be in their pockets, and whose credulity in face of the improbable was only surpassed by their unwillingness to believe anything reasonable" (P04-017).

## Relationship to Hunger

*Segelfoss Town* could hardly be more different from *Hunger* in form, setting, and narrative strategy. Where *Hunger* is a claustrophobic first-person account of a single starving writer's disintegration through the streets of [[christiania|Christiania]], *Segelfoss Town* adopts a broad social canvas, tracking the life of an entire community across time. The preface itself marks this as a conscious departure: the shift from "one or two isolated individuals" to "the life of a certain spot of ground" (P04-016) represents the polar opposite of *Hunger*'s relentless interiority. Yet beneath this surface divergence lies a continuity of theme: both works examine the pressures of modernity on individual dignity — in *Hunger* through the lens of urban poverty and psychological disintegration, in *Segelfoss Town* through the comic incongruity between traditional Northland life and the arrival of modern business. The "quiet humor" (P04-017) that the preface finds in the Segelfoss books is notably absent from *Hunger*, but the underlying concern with how economic forces shape human experience runs through both works.
