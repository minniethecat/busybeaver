# Christiania

**Christiania** (now Oslo) is the setting of [[knut-hamsun|Knut Hamsun]]'s *Hunger* (1890). The [[the-unnamed-narrator|unnamed narrator]] wanders its streets, parks, and squares in a state of near-starvation, and the city becomes an active presence — a theater of temptation, [[shame|humiliation]], and fleeting beauty. [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]] lived in Christiania during the period fictionalized in the novel; the preface notes his early works included "social studies of Christiania's Bohemia" (P04-012). The city's specific geography — [[st-olavs-place|St. Olav's Place]], [[graendsen|Grændsen]], [[the-railway-square|the Railway Square]] — anchors the novel's psychological intensity in documentary precision.

## In the Narrative

The narrator's relationship with Christiania is fundamentally ambivalent. The city represents both literary possibility and the crushing weight of social judgment. Its streets are where he walks "listlessly, without troubling about anything" (001-012), where the "ever-increasing noise" lures him from his rented room (001-004). Christiania's public spaces constantly remind him of what he lacks.

The city is rendered through named locations that are real points in late-nineteenth-century Christiania. The narrator's wandering is geographically specific, not abstract. He is drawn toward the marketplace, the shops, the newspaper boards — places where consumption is visible and his own exclusion from it most painful. [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s first play would also be "laid in Christiania" (P04-013), confirming the city's centrality to his imaginative world.

The narrator's ambivalence crystallizes in a late passage:

> I must for the present to the town — to Christiania, where the windows gleamed so. (004-163)

Even the decision to depart is tinged with longing. Christiania is simultaneously the place that starves him and the place whose gleaming windows promise something more.

## Symbolic Significance

Christiania functions as a social panopticon across four key patterns:

| Pattern | Manifestation | Narrative Effect |
|----------|--------------|------------------|
| Surveillance | Windows, passersby, shop displays | Constant exposure to judgment |
| Exclusion | Shops, cafés, rented rooms | Visible poverty vs visible consumption |
| Conformity | "Conformity, right in the town of Christiania" (002-172) | Social expectation as suffocation |
| Redemption | Parks, gardens, the fjord glimpsed between streets | Fleeting beauty within the grid |

The city embodies "conformity, right in the town of Christiania" (002-172) — capturing [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s sense of being crushed by social expectation. Yet Christiania also offers anonymity: the freedom to be nobody, to disappear into crowds. This doubleness — prison and promise — is essential to [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s modernist project. The "Garden in Christiania" (004-169) offers nature within the urban grid, just as creative inspiration offers relief from hunger.

## Real-World Context

Christiania was the official name of Oslo from 1624 to 1925. In the late nineteenth century, when [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]] lived there, it was a city of roughly 150,000 undergoing rapid modernization. The "Bohemia" of Christiania that [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]] chronicled (P04-012) was a real community of artists, writers, and radicals centered around the city's cafés. [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s experiences of poverty in Christiania — pawning possessions, sleeping in cheap rooms, struggling to publish — directly informed *Hunger*'s texture.
