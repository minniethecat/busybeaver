Mortality and bodily decay are pervasive themes in Knut Hamsun's *Hunger*. The narrator's physical deterioration — from malnutrition, exposure, and psychological stress — makes death an ever-present reality rather than an abstract concern.

## Bodily Decay

The novel renders the narrator's physical decline with clinical precision: vertigo, trembling, hair loss, dental problems, and episodes of near-collapse. This attention to the body aligns *Hunger* with naturalist fiction while transcending it through the intensity of subjective rendering. The body is not merely a biological machine breaking down but the medium of consciousness itself — as the body deteriorates, so does the coherence of selfhood.

## The Cemetery

The cemetery scenes are the novel's most concentrated explorations of mortality. The narrator retreats to the graveyard not for religious consolation but for a space where his condition — hovering between life and death, respectability and abjection — finds its symbolic landscape. Among the dead, the living narrator's suffering achieves a kind of dignity through proximity.
