# Bernt Akers Street

## Overview

Bernt Akers Street (Norwegian: Bernt Ankers gate) is a street in [[christiania|Christiania]] where the narrator's acquaintance, the theological student [[hans-pauli-pettersen|Hans Pauli Pettersen]], lived in an attic room at number 10. It appears in Part II as the destination of an exhausting journey that ends in crushing disappointment — one of the novel's starkest depictions of hope's collapse into numb despair.

## In the Narrative

The narrator learns of the address when he visits a shop to inquire about Student Pettersen and is told: "Bernt Akers Street, No. 10, in the attic" (002-085). Asked to deliver two letters that had come for Pettersen, he sets out — trudging up through the city, past the joiners eating their dinner from [[the-dampkokken|the Dampkökken]] and past the baker's shop where a loaf sits tantalizingly on display. He reaches Bernt Akers Street "half dead with fatigue" (002-086).

Climbing the weary stairs to the attic, he rehearses how Pettersen will help him — Pettersen with his "big heart," the friend who will surely extend a hand to a fellow in need. But on the door he finds only a card: "H. P. Pettersen, Theological Student, 'gone home'" (002-087). [[the-unnamed-narrator|The narrator]] sits on the bare floor, "dulled with fatigue, fairly beaten with exhaustion," mechanically repeating the words "gone home" (002-088). He remains there for ten, perhaps twenty minutes in a state of numb apathy — one of the novel's most devastating images of despair unchanneled into action.

Later in Part II, the narrator uses the address as a pretext: when a pawnbroker urges him to enter a shop, he protests, "I can't; I promised to be in Bernt Akers Street at half-past seven" (002-216) — though it is already past eight and the claim is false. The address that once held the promise of help has become a convenient lie, a phantom obligation deployed to escape a social situation.

## Symbolic Significance

Bernt Akers Street represents the exhaustion of hope. The narrator's journey to the attic is a pilgrimage toward a promised refuge — Pettersen's helping hand — that proves to be a closed door. The physical exhaustion of climbing to the attic mirrors the spiritual exhaustion of finding yet another avenue of survival blocked. The card reading "gone home" is a cruel pun: the theological student has returned to his family's home, a place of comfort and belonging, while the narrator has no home to return to and no family to receive him.

The image of the narrator sitting on the bare attic floor, repeating "gone home" (002-088), is one of the novel's most Beckett-like moments — a figure reduced to near-immobility, muttering words that have ceased to carry meaning, in a space that offers no shelter beyond its bare floorboards.
