Lieutenant Thomas Glahn is the protagonist of Knut Hamsun's novel *Pan* (1894). A former military officer, Glahn lives alone in a hut in the Northland forest, hunting with his dog Aesop and experiencing an intense, lyrical relationship with the natural world.

## Glahn in Björkman's Assessment

Edwin Björkman's introduction to *Hunger* singles out *Pan* as the novel in which "the real Hamsun" was "finally revealed." He describes the book as having "its chief interest in a primitively spontaneous man's reactions to a nature so overwhelming that it makes mere purposeless existence seem a sufficient end in itself."

Björkman compares the later novel *Benoni and Rosa* to *Pan* and notes that the later work's central figure is not "the central figure to the same extent as Lieutenant Glahn in 'Pan.'" This assessment establishes Glahn as the paradigmatic Hamsun hero — solitary, nature-immersed, ruled by impulse and emotion rather than social convention.

## The Glahn Archetype

Glahn represents a significant evolution from the Hunger narrator. While both are alienated from society, Glahn's alienation is chosen rather than inflicted, and his relationship to nature is ecstatic rather than hostile. He embodies Hamsun's turn from the urban psychological novel to the nature-mystical mode that would culminate in *Growth of the Soil*.
