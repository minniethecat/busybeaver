The unnamed newspaper editor works at a newspaper office in [[christiania|Christiania]]. He is one of the few characters in the novel who treats the narrator with a measure of professional respect, ultimately accepting his story for publication.

## Encounters with the Editor

The narrator first visits the editor's office hoping to sell a manuscript. The office clerk, nicknamed "Scissors," tells him the editor has not yet arrived [001-174]. When the narrator returns later, he finds the editor at his table, facing the window with pen in hand [001-203]. This initial image — the editor absorbed in his work, barely acknowledging the narrator's presence — establishes the power dynamic that governs their relationship throughout the novel.

The editor's acceptance of the narrator's story marks a turning point in the narrative. The narrator receives a letter informing him that his story has been accepted and set in type immediately [001-310]. This success brings a brief moment of joy and relief to the otherwise destitute protagonist, providing one of the novel's rare instances of genuine achievement.

On a later visit, the narrator encounters difficulty getting past "Scissors" to see the editor. When he finally enters the private office, the editor greets him kindly: "Good-day! Is it you? Sit down" [002-123] [002-124]. The editor's courteous treatment contrasts with the narrator's experiences of rejection and humiliation elsewhere in the city.

## The Gatekeeper Dynamic

The editor's office operates through a two-tier structure: the fearsome gatekeeper "Scissors" guards access, while the editor himself remains remote and inaccessible. The narrator must navigate this bureaucratic obstacle course each time he hopes to sell his work. The tension between the editor's abstract benevolence and the practical difficulties of reaching him mirrors the broader economic structures of the city — systems of value that the narrator knows exist but cannot reliably access.

## A Figure of Contrast

The editor represents one of the few professional relationships in the novel where the protagonist's literary ambitions are taken seriously. Yet his authority remains impersonal: he is never named, never described in physical detail, and exists for the narrator primarily as the source of payment and validation. This depersonalization of the economic relationship between artist and publisher reflects the broader anonymity of modern urban life that Hamsun's novel so acutely registers.
