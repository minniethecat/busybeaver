Stream of consciousness is a narrative technique that attempts to render the continuous, associative flow of thoughts, sensations, memories, and perceptions as they pass through a character's mind. The term was coined by the philosopher William James in *The Principles of Psychology* (1890) — the same year *Hunger* was published.

## Hunger as Proto-Stream-of-Consciousness

While *Hunger* does not employ stream of consciousness in the fully developed form found in Joyce's *Ulysses* or Woolf's *Mrs Dalloway*, it represents a decisive step toward the technique. The novel's narration adheres closely to the narrator's immediate experience, moving associatively rather than according to conventional plot logic.

Key anticipatory features include: temporal fragmentation (the narrator's sense of time distorts with hunger), free indirect discourse blending narrator and character, the rendering of half-formed thoughts and irrational impulses, and the subordination of external events to internal responses.

## Historical Position

Edwin Björkman's 1921 introduction to *Hunger* recognizes the novel's psychological novelty: it was "a new kind of realism that had nothing to do with photographic reproduction of details" but was instead "a professedly psychological study that had about as much in common with the old-fashioned conceptions of man's mental activities as the delirious utterances of a fever patient." This characterization captures precisely what would later be theorized as stream of consciousness.
