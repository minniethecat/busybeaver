The dynamic tension between pride and poverty is the central social-psychological mechanism driving the narrative of Knut Hamsun's *Hunger*. The narrator's poverty is not simply an external condition but is actively maintained and intensified by his pride — a paradox that generates the novel's distinctive tragic-comic tone.

## The Mechanism

Throughout the novel, the narrator repeatedly refuses or sabotages opportunities for material relief because accepting them would compromise his sense of dignity and self-worth. He gives away money he has just earned to a beggar more pitiable than himself; he refuses food offered by concerned strangers; he insists on maintaining the fiction of being a gentleman even when his clothing and body betray his destitution.

This is not simple irrationality. The narrator's pride is his last possession — the one thing poverty cannot take from him unless he surrenders it. Yet by protecting his pride at the cost of his physical well-being, he deepens his suffering and accelerates his decline.

## Social Dimension

The pride-poverty dynamic also has a class dimension. The narrator is not working-class by origin; his poverty is a fall from bourgeois respectability, not an inherited condition. His pride is class pride — the maintenance of status distinctions even when the material basis for those distinctions has vanished. This makes his suffering specifically modern: it is the suffering not of traditional poverty but of downward social mobility in an urban capitalist economy.
