Friedrich Nietzsche (1844–1900) was a German philosopher, cultural critic, and philologist whose ideas profoundly influenced modern intellectual history. His concepts of the will to power, the Übermensch, the death of God, and the critique of traditional morality shaped 20th-century thought across philosophy, literature, and the arts.

## Nietzsche and Hamsun

Edwin Björkman's introduction to *Hunger* explicitly links Hamsun's dramatic hero Ivar Kareno to Nietzsche: "Characteristically, Kareno is a sort of Nietzschean rebel against the victorious majority." This connection is not incidental: Hamsun's protagonists — from the narrator of *Hunger* to Kareno to Lieutenant Glahn — embody a Nietzschean individualism that rejects herd morality and asserts the primacy of personal will and aristocratic values.

The Nietzschean strain in Hamsun's work manifests in his heroes' defiance of convention, their embrace of suffering as ennobling, and their conviction that genuine greatness stands necessarily opposed to mass values. This philosophical alignment would later contribute to the controversy surrounding Hamsun's political sympathies.
