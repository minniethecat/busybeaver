## Overview

The Fortress (Akershus Fortress) is a medieval castle and military fortification overlooking the Oslo Fjord in Christiania. Dating from the late 13th century, it has served as a royal residence, military stronghold, and prison. In *Hunger*, the narrator gravitates towards the fortress and the sea beyond it during moments when he seeks solitude and distance from the city center, making it a geographical and psychological boundary in his wandering.

## In the Narrative

The fortress appears in two key passages, each marking a moment of physical and spiritual extremity. In Part I, deep in distress, the narrator walks towards the sea near the fortress and finds a seat where he can rest, describing his pain as so acute that he feels he could die from it:

> "I go towards the sea, near the fortress. I find a seat once more, and my pain was so acute that I could have died from it." [001-212]

The movement towards the fortress — and past it, towards the water — marks his impulse to escape the urban center and its social demands. The sea beyond the fortress represents a boundary where the city ends and something vast and indifferent begins.

In Part IV, the narrator is walking in the garden and the road leading down towards the fortress, cold and still sleepy, physically weakened after another night of deprivation [004-077]. The fortress here serves as a directional anchor, a fixed point in his disoriented geography of Christiania. In both passages, the narrator does not enter the fortress — he walks towards it, past it, or around it, never inside. The fortress remains an unattainable object, like the social order it symbolizes, visible but inaccessible.

## Symbolic Significance

The fortress, with its associations of military strength, endurance, and impenetrability, stands in stark contrast to the narrator's bodily weakness and psychological vulnerability. His movement towards the fortress and then beyond it to the sea suggests a desire to pass through the symbols of order and power into something more elemental and unbound. The fortress also marks the edge of the city — walking that far takes him to the boundary between civilization (Christiania's streets) and nature (the fjord). The fact that the narrator never enters the fortress, only approaches and passes it, reinforces his status as an outsider to every institution: he cannot enter the newspaper office, he cannot enter the Stiftsgaarden, and he cannot enter the fortress.

## Real-World Context

Akershus Fortress (Akershus festning) was constructed around 1299 under King Haakon V and is one of Norway's most historically significant buildings. By Hamsun's time, it had long ceased to function as a royal residence but remained an active military installation and symbol of Norwegian sovereignty. Its position on a promontory overlooking the Oslofjord made it the most prominent landmark on Christiania's waterfront, visible from much of the city. Hamsun's use of the fortress as a psychological marker — a place the narrator walks towards when he seeks solitude — reflects its actual geography at the edge of the 19th-century city center.
