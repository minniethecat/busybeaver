## Overview

The body in Knut Hamsun's *Hunger* is not merely a biological vessel but a source of acute consciousness. The narrator's starving body becomes an object of scrutiny, shame, and alienation — he observes his own physical deterioration with a mixture of clinical detachment and visceral horror, transforming bodily experience into both the subject and the engine of his psychological disintegration.

## In Hunger

The narrator exerts his body with desperate intensity, described early in the novel as he "exerted his whole body, using all his strength in his endeavours to get along" (001-013). Physical effort is his primary strategy for coping with hunger, and the body becomes an instrument to be pressed into service against its own deterioration.

Yet this same body is also a source of disgust and metaphysical anxiety. He imagines it as "food for worms" and asks, "Hath not my Heavenly Father created this earthly body?" (001-073), revealing a spiritual crisis mapped onto physical decay. His bodily awareness extends to competitive comparison: "I knocked against cars, left everybody behind me, competed with the body" (001-201), where the body is both vehicle and adversary.

The narrator's physical state is described in visceral terms. At one point, he feels "it through every pore in my body" (002-032), experiencing sensation with unbearable intensity. Later, "I was trembling over my whole body" (002-134) and "a cold feeling over my whole body" (002-187) map psychological distress directly onto physical symptoms. In a moment of striking self-examination, he recounts: "I had examined my body a few days ago, one noon up in my room" (003-045), inspecting "the whole of my gaunt, shrunken body" (003-045) with the same detached curiosity one might bring to a scientific specimen, yet shrinking from what he sees.

## The Fevered Body

As the narrator's starvation progresses, his body becomes a site of feverish disintegration. He describes himself "moist all over, my whole body bathed in dampness" (003-068), the physical boundary between self and world dissolving in sweat. The experience intensifies into a totalizing collapse: "all my life, every cell in my body, every power of my soul, gasped for breath" (003-087), a moment where physical and spiritual desperation become indistinguishable. Even after the immediate crisis, he continues "trembling over my whole body, and hiccoughed now and then" (003-088), the body retaining the memory of its ordeal.

This fevered state alters perception itself. The narrator describes seeing another person "as if I saw him with my entire body instead of only with my eyes" (003-100), suggesting a synesthetic collapse of the senses under the pressure of starvation. The body is no longer an instrument of perception but has become perception itself — a total sensory organ overwhelmed by its own condition.

## The Body as Social Interface

The narrator's body mediates his encounters with others, often in moments of degradation or unwanted intimacy. When a woman "pressed her body greedily against my breast" (003-184), the physical contact carries an ambiguity between desire and revulsion characteristic of the novel's treatment of bodily experience.

In confrontations with other men, the body becomes a marker of vulnerability. When a companion "raised the upper part of his body a little and spat in the face" of another (004-054), the narrator's own reaction — trembling "over my whole body with irritation" (004-057) — confirms that witnessing violence to another's body is experienced physically in one's own. The body in *Hunger* is never a private domain; it is continuously exposed to, and shaped by, the gaze and actions of others.

## The Body and Hamsun's Oeuvre

The intense bodily consciousness of *Hunger* is consistent with the broader preoccupations of Hamsun's work. The body of his writing — what one critic calls "the body of his work" (P04-018) — consistently returns to the physical experience of consciousness, making the body not merely a theme but the fundamental medium through which his characters encounter the world.
