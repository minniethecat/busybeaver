# Nature

## Overview

**Nature** in [[knut-hamsun|Knut Hamsun]]'s literary universe, as described in the preface to *Hunger* (1890), is not merely a setting but a determining force. The novel itself is set in urban [[christiania|Christiania]], yet the preface frames [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s entire oeuvre through the lens of his relationship to the Northland -- a nature so extreme that it "makes mere purposeless existence seem a sufficient end in itself" (P04-012). This tension between the urban novel and the natural sensibility that produced it is central to understanding [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s vision.

## In the Narrative

Although *Hunger* takes place almost entirely within the city, nature enters the novel through the preface's elaborate framework. [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s "nature" -- his innate character -- is described as "poetic and picturesque" (P04-004), and this personality is traced to the Northland's "glaring lights and black shadows" (P04-006). The implication is that nature shapes character not through direct contact but through inheritance -- [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]] carries the Northland within him even in [[christiania|Christiania]]'s streets.

Yet nature is not absent from the novel's urban world. The narrator describes sitting "all through the summer, up in the churchyards or parks, where I used to sit and write my articles" (001-007). These green spaces -- the churchyards, the parks, the cemetery benches -- are the novel's substitute for wilderness, offering the narrator temporary refuge from the city's social pressures. The cemetery at Our Saviour's becomes a recurring destination, a place where he finds "a quiet seat on a bench near the chapel" (001-193). Among the dead, the narrator escapes the living gaze.

The novel's weather mirrors the narrator's psychological states. On a good morning, "this air was clear and bright and my mind too was without a shadow" (001-012). But as his condition deteriorates, "the wind freshened, the clouds chased madly across the heavens, and it grew cooler and cooler as it got darker" (001-295). Nature in the city is atmospheric rather than geographical -- weather, not landscape, is the medium through which the natural world registers. When it "snowed still more the next day, a heavy snow mingled with rain; great wet flakes that fell to earth and were turned to mud" (003-001), the raw, icy air becomes a physical correlative for the narrator's inner confusion.

The sea appears at moments of crisis. The narrator goes "towards the sea, near the fortress" where he finds "a seat once more" (001-212). The sea is a limit -- the edge of the city, the edge of the land -- and the narrator gravitates toward it when the urban interior becomes unbearable. It is the closest the novel comes to the Northland nature that [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s preface celebrates.

The preface's most lyrical passage describes [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s novel *[[pan|Pan]]*, in which a "primitively spontaneous man" responds to "a nature so overwhelming that it makes mere purposeless existence seem a sufficient end in itself" (P04-012). This vision of nature -- as a force that strips away social pretense and reveals the essential self -- informs the psychological realism of *Hunger* itself.

The later [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]] novels are described as set in a Northland where "nature as man's chief antagonist" (P04-017) replaces social conflict. This framework retroactively illuminates *Hunger*: [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s struggle against hunger, shame, and social degradation can be read as an urban displacement of the natural struggle that [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s later characters experience directly.

The preface also personifies nature through the figure of Teresita, "one more of his many feminine embodiments of the passionate and changeable Northland nature" (P04-013). Nature is not passive landscape but active force -- gendered, willful, beyond human control.

## Literary Context

[[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s treatment of nature marks a significant departure from both Romantic nature-worship and Naturalist environmental determinism. Nature is not a source of moral lessons or a mechanism of social causation -- it is a presence whose sheer scale relativizes human concerns. This post-Romantic, proto-modernist vision of nature as sublime indifference would influence writers from [[knut-hamsun|Knut Hamsun]]'s own later work to the environmental literature of the twentieth century.
