Bjørnstjerne Bjørnson (1832–1910) was a Norwegian writer, poet, dramatist, and public figure who received the 1903 Nobel Prize in Literature. He is considered one of the "Four Greats" of Norwegian literature alongside Henrik Ibsen, Jonas Lie, and Alexander Kielland.

## Literary Career

Bjørnson wrote poetry, novels, and plays. His peasant novels *Synnøve Solbakken* (1857) and *A Happy Boy* (1860) established his reputation. His dramatic works include the historical trilogy *Sigurd the Crusader* and social dramas like *The Editor* and *A Bankruptcy*.

## Relationship to Hamsun

Bjørnson was an established literary figure when Knut Hamsun emerged. In Björkman's introduction to *Hunger*, Bjørnson is cited among the Scandinavian masters whose "changed moods" reflected the neo-romantic movement that Hamsun helped herald. Unlike Ibsen and Strindberg, whose influence on Hamsun is more directly documented, Bjørnson represents the broader literary establishment against which Hamsun's individualism reacted.
