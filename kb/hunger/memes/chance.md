## Overview

Chance in Knut Hamsun's *Hunger* is the unpredictable, often cruel mechanism that governs the protagonist's life — random encounters, lucky breaks, and unfortunate coincidences that alternately rescue and torment him. In a novel where the narrator has no stable income, no social safety net, and no reliable relationships, chance becomes the only force that can alter his trajectory. The protagonist's life is not shaped by planning or merit but by arbitrary events, reflecting both the chaos of urban poverty and a deeper philosophical view that human fate is governed by forces beyond individual control.

In the modern city of Christiania, the protagonist is buffeted by currents he cannot predict: landlords who may or may not evict him, strangers who may or may not offer charity, editors who may or may not accept his work. Hamsun's treatment of chance aligns with the modernist rejection of deterministic, plot-driven narrative — rather than a chain of cause and effect, the protagonist's life unfolds as a series of random encounters, a literary technique that mirrors the psychological fragmentation of the starving mind.

## In Hunger

The protagonist repeatedly treats chance encounters as potential salvation. When he learns of a possible contact, he reflects: "Pauli's address; being my last chance, I must turn it to account" [002-082]. Each new lead is treated with desperate urgency precisely because he has no reliable means of improving his situation — his only strategy is to seize whatever chance offers. This creates a paradox: by treating every opportunity as a "last chance," the protagonist invests each encounter with impossible stakes, guaranteeing disappointment.

Sometimes chance takes on a quasi-religious quality. In one passage, the name "Cisler" suddenly appears in his thoughts, and he wonders: "Was that perchance a hint from on high?" [002-176]. The protagonist interprets random mental associations as signs or providence, illustrating how desperation transforms coincidence into meaning. In the absence of any genuine religious framework, the starving mind creates its own system of signs and portents — a private superstition born of hunger and helplessness.

The protagonist is acutely aware of how little control he has. In conversation with a stranger, he admits: "there is naturally not an atom of a chance for me" [003-157], a moment of bleak self-awareness that contrasts with his usual manic hopefulness. This rare honesty — in a narrative dominated by self-deception and bluster — reveals that beneath his elaborate fabrications, the narrator understands his own desperation.

Small opportunities — "for half-pence whenever the chance offered" [004-105] — become matters of survival. The protagonist learns to exploit every random opening, no matter how small. Even in casual conversation, the language of chance pervades: a character responds "unless it chanced to be a young fellow" [004-150], reflecting how thoroughly the vocabulary of randomness permeates the novel's world.

The cumulative effect of chance in the novel is not liberating but exhausting. The protagonist cannot plan, cannot save, cannot build — he can only react to whatever the next moment brings. This passivity, enforced by poverty, is one of *Hunger*'s most devastating insights into the psychology of deprivation.

## Literary Context

Hamsun's use of chance as a narrative device distinguishes *Hunger* from the plotted novels of nineteenth-century realism. Where a Dickens protagonist might encounter chance events that ultimately serve a moral or providential design, the narrator of *Hunger* encounters randomness that resists interpretation. This reflects the influence of Dostoevsky — whom Hamsun admired — particularly the Dostoevskian insight that extreme psychological states produce irrational, unpredictable behavior.

The novel's episodic structure, driven by chance meetings and arbitrary misfortunes, anticipates the stream-of-consciousness technique that would later be developed by Joyce and Woolf. *Hunger* does not tell a story shaped by cause and effect but rather immerses the reader in a consciousness battered by random stimuli. Chance, in this sense, is not merely a theme but a structural principle.

## Related Concepts

Chance is closely tied to **hope** — hope is what transforms a random opportunity into a perceived lifeline. **Delusion** results when the protagonist reads too much meaning into chance events. **Fate** (or its absence) is the larger philosophical question that chance raises: is there any order to the protagonist's suffering, or is it all random? **Despair** sets in when chance consistently fails to deliver.
