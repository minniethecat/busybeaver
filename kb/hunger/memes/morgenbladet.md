# Morgenbladet

**Morgenbladet** (The Morning Paper), founded in 1819, appears in *Hunger*'s opening sentence not as a source of news but as wallpaper — old issues pasted to the walls of [[the-room|the narrator's rented room]]. Its material presence is the novel's first image:

> By the door where the wall of the room was papered with old numbers of the Morgenbladet, I could distinguish clearly a notice from the Director of Lighthouses, and a little to the left of it an advertisement of Fabian Olsens' new-baked bread. (001-001)

This newspaper-as-wallpaper introduces the novel's central tension: [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] lives surrounded by the world of print, yet stands excluded from it. The *Morgenbladet* "near the door was rent in strips" (001-004) — its physical degradation mirroring [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s own.

## The Newspaper as Credential

In Part II, when detained by police, [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] invokes *Morgenbladet* as a claim to professional identity:

> "Morgenbladet!" said I. "I have been out a" — (002-009)

The claim is hollow, and [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] immediately regrets having "dragged in the Morgenbladet" (002-012), knowing the police will verify it. Yet he doubles down:

> "Morgen! if you please! and then Morgenbladet!" (002-016)

The fragmented, exclamatory quotation captures the [[despair|desperation]] of his performance. *Morgenbladet* is simultaneously a real institution and an empty signifier — a name [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] deploys to construct a social identity he does not possess.

## Symbolic Functions

| Level | Function | Significance |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| Material | Wallpaper in [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s room | [[poverty|Poverty]] as lived environment |
| Professional | Claimed journalistic affiliation | Precarious identity construction |
| Symbolic | The world of print and publication | The literary economy [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] seeks to enter |

The newspaper's institutional authority contrasts with [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s marginality. Between the solidity of the institution — *Morgenbladet* was and remains one of Norway's oldest newspapers — and the precarity of the individual, the novel's opening image captures the economy of literary production and exclusion that defines [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s condition.

## Historical Context

*Morgenbladet* was in the late nineteenth century one of [[christiania|Christiania]]'s leading daily newspapers, known for coverage of politics, culture, and international affairs. Its appearance in *Hunger* documents the material conditions of [[christiania|Christiania]]'s struggling writers: rooms papered with old newsprint, the detritus of print culture serving as insulation. The newspaper continues to publish today, making it one of Norway's oldest surviving periodicals.
