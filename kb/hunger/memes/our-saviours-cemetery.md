# Our Saviour's Cemetery

## Overview

**Our Saviour's Cemetery** (Vår Frelsers gravlund) is the churchyard adjacent to Our Saviour's Church in [[christiania|Christiania]], appearing in [[knut-hamsun|Knut Hamsun]]'s *Hunger* as a space of paradoxical refuge. Among the graves, [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] finds momentary peace — a quiet that contrasts with the noise of the streets and the clamor of his own hunger. [[death|Death]], in the cemetery, becomes a condition more restful than [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s living starvation.

## In the Narrative

The cemetery enters the novel during one of [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s periods of urban wandering. After spending time between the marketplace and [[graendsen|Grændsen]], he "went straight on to Our Saviour's Cemetery, where I found a quiet seat on a bench near the chapel" (001-193). The cemetery offers what the city cannot: stillness, solitude, freedom from the social gaze. Among the dead, [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] is no longer on display.

The cemetery's function as a refuge from the living city is reinforced by its contrast with the urban environment. The narrator has been walking through commercial streets, reading newspapers he cannot buy, surrounded by the commerce from which his poverty excludes him. The cemetery cancels all of this: there is nothing to buy, nothing to pawn, nothing to hunger for among the graves.

In Part I, [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] returns: "In this manner I came up to Our Saviour's Cemetery" (001-281). The phrase "in this manner" suggests a wandering without destination — the cemetery appears not as a goal but as the terminus of a drift. The cemetery is where one ends up when the city has offered nothing else.

The narrator also passes time in "the churchyards or parks, where I used to sit" (001-007) — a general reference to the graveyards that serve as his open-air refuges. The cemetery is part of a broader pattern in [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s spatial life: when [[the-room|the rented room]] fails, when the park is too crowded, when the street is too exposing, the churchyard offers its ambivalent shelter.

## Symbolic Significance

The cemetery is the novel's most literal image of death as a condition preferable to [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s life. The graves promise an end to hunger, to shame, to the endless internal monologue that constitutes [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s [[soul|consciousness]]. Yet the cemetery is not morbid in [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s rendering — it is peaceful, a green space within the city where [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] can sit on a bench and, for a moment, cease to struggle.

The cemetery's association with writing is significant. The narrator sits on the bench "near the chapel" (001-193) in the posture of contemplation — the same posture he assumes in the park, in his room, wherever he attempts to compose. The cemetery is another writing space, and its silence is more conducive to thought than the park's crowds or the room's oppressive bareness.
