# Memory

## Overview

**Memory** in [[knut-hamsun|Knut Hamsun]]'s *Hunger* (1890) is an ambivalent faculty — at once a lifeline to [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s past identity and a torment that heightens his awareness of how far he has fallen. The narrator's memories operate unpredictably, surfacing in moments of crisis and dissolving when he most needs them. Memory is thus one of the novel's key psychological structures, mediating between [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s former self and his present degradation.

## In the Narrative

Memory first appears as a source of comfort. The "rhythmical sound of Biblical language" runs through [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s memory (001-073), connecting him to childhood and a world of order and meaning. This religious memory provides a counterpoint to his present chaos — a reminder that he was once someone else.

Yet memory is also unreliable. A person he expected to meet "had vanished from my memory; was wiped out with many other things" (003-043). The narrator's cognitive deterioration under the pressure of hunger means that memory itself becomes a casualty. What he can and cannot remember maps his psychological decline.

The narrator's relationship to memory is actively managed. He tries to "impress upon his memory gravely" (001-174) a message for someone, treating his own mind as an unreliable instrument that must be consciously directed. And he "ransacks his memory to find a man good for a penny" (002-052), turning memory into a survival tool — a database of potential benefactors to be systematically searched.

There is also a wish to forget: certain things he hopes will "be quietly effaced from my memory" (001-082). Memory is thus not merely passive but actively shaped by [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s desires — what he wishes to remember and what he wishes to forget reveal his deepest anxieties.

## Literary Context

[[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s treatment of memory as fragmented, unreliable, and emotionally charged anticipates the modernist stream-of-[[soul|consciousness]] technique that would be developed by Proust, Joyce, and Woolf. The narrator's memories do not form a coherent autobiographical narrative but surface unpredictably, triggered by sensory stimuli and emotional states. This represents a significant departure from the orderly flashbacks of nineteenth-century realism.
