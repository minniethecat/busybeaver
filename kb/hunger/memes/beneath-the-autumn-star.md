# Beneath the Autumn Star

## Overview

**Beneath the Autumn Star** (*Under Høststjernen*) is a novel by [[knut-hamsun|Knut Hamsun]], published in 1906. The English-language preface to *Hunger* references it in the context of [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s later bibliography: "…travelling sketches, 1905; 'Beneath the Autumn Star' a novel, 1906; '[[benoni-and-rosa|Benoni]],' and '[[benoni-and-rosa|Rosa]],' 1908" (P04-015). The novel marks [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s return to fiction after a period of travel writing and drama.

## Description

*Beneath the Autumn Star* was written during [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s mature period, when he was an established literary figure in Scandinavia and increasingly known internationally. The novel's title evokes the melancholic, nature-infused sensibility characteristic of [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s prose — a quality already present in *Hunger*'s lyrical descriptions of [[christiania|Christiania]]'s streets and seasons. The novel was preceded by *Stridende Liv* (1905), a collection of travel sketches, suggesting that [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]] used the travel form to gather material and reset his approach before returning to sustained narrative fiction.

The novel is part of a sequence with *[[benoni-and-rosa|Benoni]]* and *[[benoni-and-rosa|Rosa]]* (both 1908), forming a loose thematic grouping in [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s bibliography. This pattern — works appearing in pairs or clusters — is characteristic of [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s creative rhythm, visible also in the "[[the-kareno-trilogy|Kareno trilogy]]" of plays and the later [[the-growth-of-the-soil|Growth of the Soil]] period.

## Relationship to Hunger

*Beneath the Autumn Star* was published sixteen years after *Hunger*, and it belongs to a very different phase of [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s career. While *Hunger* was the work of a struggling, unknown writer, *Beneath the Autumn Star* was the product of an established author at the height of his powers. Nevertheless, the novel shares *Hunger*'s fundamental concern with the individual's relationship to nature and society, and its title — with its autumnal, elegiac quality — extends the lyrical sensibility that was already one of *Hunger*'s most remarked-upon features.
