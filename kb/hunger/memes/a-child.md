# A Child

## Overview

**Children** appear periodically in Knut Hamsun's *Hunger* (1890), serving as mirrors that reflect the unnamed narrator's own vulnerability and his distance from ordinary social life. The narrator's interactions with children reveal his isolation — he is neither a child (innocent) nor a parent (responsible), but something in between that society has no category for.

## In the Narrative

Children in *Hunger* are often observers — they watch the narrator with curiosity or suspicion, seeing him without the filters that adults apply. A child's stare can be more unsettling than an adult's because children do not disguise their reactions. The narrator, conscious of his shabby appearance, feels particularly exposed under the gaze of children.

At other moments, the narrator identifies with children, seeing in their vulnerability a reflection of his own. He is, in some sense, childlike — helpless before forces he cannot control, dependent on a world that has no obligation to care for him.

## Relationship to the Narrator

Children represent the ordinary human connections that the narrator has lost or never had. He has no family, no dependents, no role in the generational chain that structures most lives. His encounters with children remind him of what he lacks.
