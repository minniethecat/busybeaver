# Guilt

**Guilt** in Knut Hamsun's *Hunger* is a pervasive psychological presence — a combination of genuine moral self-reproach and the irrational self-accusation that starvation produces in a sensitive mind. The protagonist's guilt is unusual in literature of the period: he feels guilt not only for what he does but for what he almost does, and even for who he is.

## Definition

Guilt in *Hunger* operates on two levels. At the surface, the protagonist experiences flashes of remorse for specific actions — lying, taking advantage of others' kindness, petty dishonesty. At a deeper level, guilt is existential: he feels fundamentally unworthy, as if his very existence in a state of poverty and failure is a moral failing. This guilt is both cause and symptom of his psychological deterioration.

What makes the protagonist's guilt distinctive is its anticipatory quality. He reproaches himself for actions contemplated but not committed, for thoughts that hardly rise to the level of intention. This hypersensitivity — the inability to distinguish between temptation and transgression — suggests a conscience that has been sharpened rather than dulled by hunger. Starvation does not brutalize the narrator's moral sense; it makes it excruciatingly acute.

## In Hunger

The protagonist's guilt is most acute when hunger has weakened his self-control. He reflects on "the strange freaks one's thoughts are guilty of when one is starving" [002-105], recognizing that his mental state is not fully under his command. This insight is remarkable — he observes his own deterioration with clinical detachment, yet cannot stop it. The word "freaks" is telling: these are not the natural thoughts of a healthy mind but aberrations produced by a body in crisis.

Guilt can attach to near-misses as powerfully as to actual transgressions. He is tormented by "the memory of which I had almost been guilty" [002-134], and swears "That is, by God! the shabbiest thing I have ever done" about a contemplated action. The guilt of almost-doing is indistinguishable from the guilt of doing — a mark of his hypersensitive moral awareness. This passage illustrates a central psychological dynamic in *Hunger*: the narrator's interior life is as punishing as his external circumstances.

The protagonist's guilt also surfaces in moments of self-awareness about his own dishonesty: he is appalled "that I could for a moment be guilty of such a mean trick as that" [004-020]. Each recognition of his own moral failure deepens his self-loathing. Yet paradoxically, this capacity for guilt also distinguishes him — it proves he retains a moral sensibility that poverty has not obliterated. A truly degraded person would not care; the protagonist cares too much.

The progression of guilt episodes through the novel tracks the narrator's decline. Early guilts are about specific dishonest acts; later guilts become existential — he feels guilty simply for existing, for consuming resources, for taking up space in a world that has no place for him.

## Literary Context

Hamsun's treatment of guilt reflects the influence of Dostoevsky, particularly *Crime and Punishment*, which Hamsun had read and admired. Like Raskolnikov, the narrator of *Hunger* is tormented by a conscience that seems to operate independently of any external moral authority. But where Dostoevsky's guilt is ultimately religious — leading toward redemption — Hamsun's guilt leads nowhere. It is a closed loop: self-accusation produces suffering, which produces more occasions for guilt, without resolution.

This secularization of guilt marks *Hunger* as a definitively modern work. In a world without God, the protagonist must serve as his own judge, jury, and executioner — and the sentence is always guilty.

## Related Concepts

Guilt is closely linked to **shame** — while shame is social (fear of others' judgment), guilt is internal (self-judgment). **Conscience** is the faculty that produces guilt, and the protagonist's conscience remains active even as his circumstances deteriorate. **Pride** amplifies guilt: the protagonist's high self-regard makes his moral failures more painful. Finally, **despair** is the cumulative effect of guilt — each guilty moment contributes to the protagonist's sense that he deserves his suffering.
