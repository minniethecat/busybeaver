Youngstorget is a public square in central Oslo, Norway. Originally called Nytorvet ("New Square"), it was renamed in 1951 after the merchant and politician Jørgen Young, who owned much of the surrounding land in the mid-19th century.

## Historical Context

In the late 19th century, Youngstorget served as an important market square and public gathering space. It was surrounded by commercial buildings, including the Folketeateret (People's Theatre). Its location in the city center made it a natural crossroads for foot traffic and commerce.

## In Hunger

Youngstorget and its environs form part of the urban landscape through which Hamsun's narrator moves during his aimless, hungry wanderings. Like other Kristiania locations in the novel, it serves less as a distinct named site than as part of the continuous psychogeographic map that structures the narrator's consciousness. Market squares like Youngstorget represent public spaces where the narrator's poverty and social exclusion are most painfully visible — surrounded by food, commerce, and communal life, yet utterly excluded from participation.
