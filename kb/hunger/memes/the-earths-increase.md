## Overview

*The Earth's Increase* — the title Knut Hamsun himself preferred for what was published in English as *Growth of the Soil* — is a 1917 novel (*Markens Grøde* in the original Norwegian). The novel was the culmination of Hamsun's middle period and won him the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1920. It continues the narrative path Hamsun had entered with *Children of the Time* (1913) and *Segelfoss Town* (1915), shifting the scene to the Norwegian Northland where modern business begins to encroach on primitive rural life (P04-017).

## The Title

Hamsun's preference for "The Earth's Increase" over "The Growth of the Soil" is revealing. The King James phrasing — "the earth's increase" echoes biblical language of fruitfulness and God's blessing on the land — captures the novel's quasi-religious reverence for agrarian life. "The Growth of the Soil," by contrast, sounds agronomic, as though the novel were a treatise on crop rotation. Hamsun's preferred title insists on the moral and spiritual dimension of cultivation: the earth does not merely produce; it *increases*, in the biblical sense of multiplying and filling the earth.

## Setting and Themes

The novel is set in Hamsun's beloved Northland, depicting a world in transition. The old primitive life is fading even in the outlying districts, where pioneers break ground for new permanent settlements. Modern business arrives, and much of the novel's quiet humor springs from its influence on the natives, "whose hands used always to be in their pockets, and whose credulity in face of the improbable was only surpassed by their unwillingness to believe anything reasonable" (P04-017).

The central character, Isak Sellanraa, embodies the values Hamsun had come to champion by this stage of his career: rootedness, silence, patient labor, and an almost mystical connection to the soil. Isak is in every respect the antithesis of *Hunger*'s narrator — where the narrator is restless, voluble, urban, and self-destructive, Isak is settled, taciturn, rural, and generative. The two protagonists mark the poles of Hamsun's imaginative world.

## From Hunger to The Earth's Increase

The distance between *Hunger* (1890) and *The Earth's Increase* (1917) measures not only Hamsun's artistic development but a fundamental reorientation of his values. Where *Hunger* focused on the internal psychological landscape of a single starving writer in Christiania — a consciousness turned inward to the point of dissolution — *The Earth's Increase* turns outward to the land itself and the communal life of settlers. Nature, which barely appears in the urban claustrophobia of *Hunger*, becomes in the later novel "man's chief antagonist" (P04-017), a force at once hostile and redemptive.

This shift from city to soil, from psychology to husbandry, from solitary consciousness to communal ritual, reflects a broader trajectory in Hamsun's thought. The young Hamsun who wrote *Hunger* was an urban bohemian, steeped in the nervous energy of modernism. The mature Hamsun of *The Earth's Increase* had become an agrarian conservative, suspicious of cities, industry, and the rootlessness they foster. The novel that won him the Nobel Prize is, in a sense, the novel that repudiates the world of his breakthrough.

## The Nobel Prize

The 1920 Nobel Prize in Literature was awarded to Hamsun "for his monumental work, *Growth of the Soil*." The prize citation acknowledged the novel's "epic" quality and its portrayal of "the life of the soil." The award cemented Hamsun's international reputation and marked the high point of his public career. It also represented a validation of the very values — agrarianism, tradition, resistance to modernity — that Hamsun had come to espouse, values that would take a darker political turn in the decades to follow.
