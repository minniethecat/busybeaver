Ivar Kareno is the central character of Knut Hamsun's dramatic trilogy, consisting of "At the Gates of the Kingdom" (1895), "The Game of Life" (1896), and "Sunset Glow" (1898). He is a student and thinker whose life is depicted at three stages: age 29, 39, and 50. The trilogy traces his transformation from an irreconcilable rebel to a man who ultimately surrenders to the established order.

The first play, set in Christiania, introduces Kareno at 29 as a rebel and truth-seeker. He is described as "the irreconcilable rebel of 'At the Gates of the Kingdom,' the heaven-storming truth-seeker of 'The Game of Life'" (P04-075). The second play moves to the Northland, and the third returns to Christiania. Across the trilogy, the lesson emerges that "every one is a rebel at 30 and a renegade at 50" (P04-075).

Two women play significant roles in Kareno's life: his wife Eline and Teresita, described as "one more of his many feminine embodiments of the passionate and changeable Northland nature" (P04-075). When Kareno finally surrenders to the powers that be for security in his later years, a younger man of 29 stands ready to take up the rebel cause, mirroring Kareno's own youthful convictions.

Kareno has been characterized as "a sort of Nietzschean rebel against the victorious majority" (P04-075). Hamsun's treatment of the character stresses capacity for action over the purposes toward which that capacity may be directed. As a literary figure, Kareno represents Hamsun's exploration of the conflict between individual conviction and social accommodation that runs through much of his work.

## The Trilogy Structure

The three plays that constitute Kareno's story form a carefully constructed dramatic arc. "At the Gates of the Kingdom" (1895) stages the rebellion of youth — Kareno at 29, irreconcilable and uncompromising, refusing the terms offered by the established order. "The Game of Life" (1896) shifts the setting from Christiania to the Northland, placing Kareno at 39 in a world where the ideals of his twenties meet the practical demands of middle age. "Sunset Glow" (1898) returns to Christiania and finds Kareno at 50, having made his peace with the very powers he once opposed.

The trilogy's structure reflects a cyclical understanding of generational conflict. Hamsun described Ivar Kareno as "a student and thinker" (P04-013) whose trajectory is not unique but representative — the trilogy suggests that rebellion and capitulation are not individual choices but phases in a recurring social pattern, each generation producing its own rebels and renegades.

## The Nietzschean Dimension

Kareno's characterization as a Nietzschean figure deserves closer examination. His rebellion is directed "against the victorious majority" (P04-075), not against specific injustices or institutions. This distinguishes Kareno from political revolutionaries — his revolt is philosophical, a matter of spirit rather than program. Hamsun's interest lies in the energy of rebellion itself, the "capacity for action" abstracted from any particular goal, which aligns Kareno with a vitalist tradition in which the will to act precedes and transcends the ends toward which action is directed.
