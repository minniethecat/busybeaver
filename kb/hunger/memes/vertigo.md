## Overview

Vertigo in Knut Hamsun's *Hunger* is the physical manifestation of the narrator's prolonged starvation — a recurring symptom that bridges the bodily experience of hunger and its psychological effects. The dizziness, lightheadedness, and loss of equilibrium serve as somatic markers of the narrator's deteriorating physical condition and contribute to his increasingly unstable perception of reality.

## In Hunger

The narrator first mentions vertigo early in the novel, noting that "a few times I had kept my bed for the day with vertigo" (001-002). This admission establishes the symptom as a chronic condition, not a momentary affliction. The vertigo is directly linked to his lack of food and the precariousness of his existence in the attic room.

The sensation intensifies as the novel progresses. The narrator describes how "my head grew light and far off, I no longer felt its weight on my shoulders" (001-071), capturing the dissociative quality of hunger-induced dizziness. This physical lightness parallels his psychological state — unmoored, floating, detached from the solid world of well-fed citizens.

In a later episode, the narrator experiences a more acute episode: "I grew giddy as I stood. I staggered forward to the counter, and gave the watch to the assistant" (002-215). Here, vertigo directly accompanies the act of pawning his last valuable possession, intertwining physical collapse with social degradation. The body's failure mirrors the self's dissolution.
