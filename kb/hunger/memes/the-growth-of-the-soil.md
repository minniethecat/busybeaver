# The Growth of the Soil

## Overview

**The Growth of the Soil** (*Markens Grøde*) is [[knut-hamsun|Knut Hamsun]]'s 1917 novel, for which he was awarded the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1920. It is a monumental epic of Norwegian rural life, following the settler Isak as he clears land, builds a farm, and raises a family in the northern [[nature|wilderness]]. The preface to *Hunger* traces [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s "increasing authority" through the works leading up to "The Growth of the Soil," in which his mature vision "stood finally revealed" (P04-012).

## Description

*The Growth of the Soil* departs radically from the urban, psychological focus of [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s early novels. It is an agrarian epic, celebrating the [[pride|dignity]] of labor, the bond between humans and the land, and the slow, cumulative rhythms of rural life. Isak, the novel's protagonist, is a figure of almost mythic simplicity — strong, silent, attuned to the natural world in a way that [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s earlier urban heroes could only yearn for.

The novel's style is correspondingly different: broad, unhurried, epic in scope. Where *Hunger* is claustrophobic and intense, *The Growth of the Soil* is expansive and patient. The preface notes that "the title ought to be 'The Earth's Increase'" (P04-017), suggesting a biblical resonance — the novel as a modern Genesis, a story of creation and cultivation.

## Relationship to Hunger

The relationship between *The Growth of the Soil* and *Hunger* is one of radical contrast. The starving urban artist of *Hunger* and the rooted peasant farmer of *The Growth of the Soil* represent opposite poles of [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s imaginative world. Yet both figures embody [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s preoccupation with the solitary [[soul|consciousness]] in tension with its environment — whether that environment is the indifferent streets of [[christiania|Christiania]] or the demanding soil of the northern [[nature|wilderness]].

The preface traces a coherent arc from *Hunger*'s urban psychology to *The Growth of the Soil*'s rural epic, following [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]] through the intermediate works — [[editor-lynge|Editor Lynge]], [[new-earth-periodical|New Earth]], [[pan|Pan]], the [[the-kareno-trilogy|Kareno trilogy]] — that bridge these two poles.

## Critical Reception

*The Growth of the Soil* was [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s greatest international success, winning him the Nobel Prize in 1920. The Nobel committee cited the novel's "monumental epic" quality and its celebration of the "simple life of the soil." The novel remains widely read in Norway and internationally, though its political and ideological dimensions have been subject to critical re-evaluation, particularly in light of [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s later political affiliations.
