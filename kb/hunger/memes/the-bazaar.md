# The Bazaar

## Overview

The **bazaar** in [[christiania|Christiania]] is a covered market arcade that appears repeatedly in [[knut-hamsun|Knut Hamsun]]'s *Hunger* as a site of commercial transaction, economic [[despair|desperation]], and [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s fraught relationship with material objects. It is where he pawns his waistcoat, contemplates begging, and observes the ordinary commerce from which his poverty excludes him.

## In the Narrative

The bazaar enters the novel as a backdrop to [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s physical decline. Passing "the fountain at the corner of the bazaar," he "swallowed a mouthful of water" (001-281) — the bazaar's periphery serving as a site of minimal, animal survival. The fountain, the corner, the bazaar: these are [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s landmarks, mapped not by civic pride but by the geography of hunger.

In Part II, the bazaar becomes a place of deliberate avoidance: "I was a long distance away from the bazaar, and I would not ask at a private house" (002-197). The narrator's poverty makes the bazaar both a necessity and a site of shame — a place he must approach to survive but whose proximity reminds him of his degradation.

The bazaar's most significant appearance comes in Part III. The narrator goes "off to the clothes-shop in the bazaar. It occurred to me that I might" (003-002) — the unfinished thought suggesting both hope and its immediate foreclosure. He climbs "up the steps to the bazaar and took hold of one and began" (003-003), the physical act of ascending the steps marking the threshold between the street's anonymity and the market's exposed commercial space. Here he pawns his waistcoat — the transaction that temporarily funds his cab ride to [[tomtegaden|Tomtegaden]] and sets the events of Part III in motion.

Later, reduced further, he thinks "to go to one of the meat bazaars underneath me, and beg a piece of raw meat" (003-078) — the bazaar now the site of the most abject form of transaction: begging for food to eat raw.

The bazaar is also a liminal space, located "between the church and the bazaar" (001-296) — placed geographically and symbolically between the sacred and the commercial, [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] occupies neither domain.

## Symbolic Significance

The bazaar is the novel's primary space of economic exchange — and therefore of [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s exclusion from the normal economy. Other people buy and sell; [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] pawns and begs. The bazaar's arcades, filled with clothing, meat, and goods, constitute a material world [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] can only observe or briefly touch before surrendering his possessions to it.

The architecture of the bazaar — its steps, its corners, its fountain — creates a spatial rhythm of approach and retreat. The narrator circles the bazaar without ever fully entering its commercial life. He is a figure at the margin of exchange, always on the steps, never at the counter.

## Real-World Context

Covered market arcades were common features of late-nineteenth-century European cities, offering protected spaces for butchers, clothiers, and other vendors. [[christiania|Christiania]]'s bazaars would have been part of the city's modern commercial infrastructure — a space of orderly transaction that [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s narrator can access only as an outsider.
