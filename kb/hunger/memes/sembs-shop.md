## Overview

Semb's shop is a cloth-seller's establishment in Christiania that the narrator visits repeatedly during Part I, primarily as a place where he can have his bread wrapped in paper to avoid the shame of carrying it openly through the streets.

## In the Narrative

Semb's first appears when the narrator, having received money for an article, buys bread and cheese but becomes self-conscious about carrying them. He hits upon a solution: "it occurred to me that I might go into Semb's and get it wrapped up in paper" [001-167]. The shop serves as a respectable cover — entering a cloth-seller's gives him a reason to carry a paper-wrapped parcel without revealing its humble contents.

Later the same day, when a policeman questions why he is standing still on the pavement, the narrator invents an excuse: "Oh, I have been down to Semb and got some cloth for a suit" [001-182]. The lie gives him enough confidence to maintain his composure. But the policeman's suspicion drives him back to the shop: "I look up and start; I am outside Semb's shop again" [001-212] — he has circled back to it unconsciously, as if the shop has become a landmark of safety in his mental geography.

After pawning his waistcoat, the narrator walks through the city with "the paper from Semb on top" of his parcel [001-230], the shop's wrapping now a small but potent shield against public scrutiny.
