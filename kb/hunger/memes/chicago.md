Chicago was, during the 1880s, one of the fastest-growing cities in the world, a symbol of American industrial capitalism and urban modernity. Knut Hamsun lived and worked there during his second visit to the United States (1886–1888), employed as a streetcar conductor.

## Hamsun in Chicago

Edwin Björkman records that Hamsun "worked chiefly on the plains of North Dakota and in the streets of Chicago" during his American years. The contrast between these two settings — the agricultural frontier and the industrial metropolis — gave Hamsun a comprehensive view of American life at its extremes.

Chicago in the 1880s was defined by explosive growth, mass immigration, labor conflict (the Haymarket affair occurred in 1886), and the consolidation of industrial wealth. For Hamsun, the city represented everything he found alienating about American civilization: its materialism, its leveling democracy, and its lack of the spiritual and aristocratic values he prized.

## Literary Impact

The Chicago experience, combined with his North Dakota years, produced *The Spiritual Life of Modern America* (1889). More importantly, the experience of urban alienation in Chicago — a city far larger and more anonymous than Kristiania — may have informed the psychological landscape of *Hunger*, where the city becomes not merely a setting but an antagonist, a force that both oppresses and defines the narrator's consciousness.
