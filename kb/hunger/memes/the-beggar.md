The beggar appears as a recurring figure of social degradation in the novel, embodying the fate the narrator desperately seeks to avoid. The protagonist's encounters with or observations of beggars serve to measure his own precarious social standing.

## Social Anxiety and Mistaken Identity

The narrator is acutely aware that others perceive him as belonging to the same class as the beggar. When he approaches a woman on the street, "She took me without further ado for a beggar" [001-272]. This moment of mistaken identity forces the narrator to confront how his ragged appearance has erased the distinction between himself and those he considers beneath him.

The narrator imagines a passerby pointing at him and saying to himself, "There goes a beggar — one of those people who get their food handed out to them at folk's back-doors" [001-273]. This internalized shame reveals the narrator's terror of being subsumed into the anonymous mass of the urban poor. The beggar thus functions as both a literal presence on the streets of Christiania and a psychological mirror for the narrator's deepest anxieties.

Despite his attempts to maintain the appearance of a struggling writer rather than a beggar, the narrator's physical deterioration — his worn clothing, his hunger, his lack of fixed address — increasingly aligns him with the very figure he dreads becoming.

## The Beggar as Social Mirror

Hamsun uses the figure of the beggar to explore the fluid and precarious nature of social identity in the modern city. The narrator, who considers himself an intellectual and an artist, is horrified to discover that the city's other inhabitants cannot see the distinction he feels so keenly. This mismatch between internal self-conception and external perception is one of the novel's central preoccupations.

The beggar also serves a structural function in the novel's episodic architecture. Each encounter with poverty on the street — the beggar, the man selling rolls, the various figures who populate the narrator's wandering route — contributes to a cumulative portrait of urban marginality in which the narrator himself is an uneasy participant.

## Narrative Context

The beggar episodes occur within the longer arc of the narrator's downward trajectory through Christiania. The protagonist's repeated confrontations with those even lower on the social scale than himself serve a dual function: they remind him of what he might become, and they allow him — however briefly — to differentiate himself from those he perceives as truly destitute. This precarious distinction, maintained through tortured acts of pride and charity, is one of the novel's most psychologically acute observations.
