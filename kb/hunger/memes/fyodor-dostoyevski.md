# Fyodor Dostoyevski

## Overview

**Fyodor Dostoyevski** (1821–1881) was a Russian novelist whose psychological depth and exploration of extreme mental states made him a crucial influence on [[knut-hamsun|Knut Hamsun]]. The preface to *[[hunger|Hunger]]* (1890) names Dostoyevski multiple times, positioning him as both a predecessor and a point of comparison for Hamsun's literary method.

## In the Narrative

Dostoyevski is invoked in the preface as a measure of Hamsun's significance. "He is spoken of as the equal of Tolstoy and Dostoyevski" (P04-002) in Russia, where Hamsun's collected works had appeared in several editions. This comparison is strategic: by linking Hamsun to the greatest Russian psychological novelists, the preface asserts his place in the European literary canon.

The preface also notes that Hamsun's *[[mysteries|Mysteries]]* blends "the spirits of Dostoyevski and Mark Twain" (P04-011), an unusual pairing that captures the novel's oscillation between psychological darkness and ironic humor. This blending of influences — the Russian psychological tradition and American humor — is presented as characteristic of Hamsun's originality.

The preface's description of Hamsun's temperament illuminates the Dostoyevskian dimension of his work. Hamsun is characterized as a writer who "must be classed as an individualistic romanticist and a highly subjective aristocrat, whose foremost passion in life is violent, defiant deviation from everything average and ordinary" (P04-003). This radical subjectivity — the refusal of social norm and the embrace of extreme consciousness — is precisely the Dostoyevskian inheritance. The preface deepens this portrait by observing that "in all the literature known to me, there is no writer who appears more ruthlessly and fearlessly himself" (P04-004), a description that recalls Dostoyevski's own confessional intensity.

*Hunger* itself is described as "a professedly psychological study that had about as much in common with the old-fashioned conceptions of man's mental activities as the delirious utterances of a [[fever|fever]] patient" (P04-010). This conception of psychology — as something febrile, subjective, and radically interior — descends directly from Dostoyevski's narrative innovations.

## Relationship to the Narrator

Dostoyevski's influence on Hamsun is most visible in the psychological method of *Hunger*. Like Dostoyevski's underground man, Hamsun's narrator is a figure of radical subjectivity — isolated, self-contradictory, and rendered through a first-person narration that dissolves the boundary between consciousness and [[reality|reality]].
