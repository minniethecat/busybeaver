# Norway

## Overview

**Norway** (Norwegian: *Norge*) is a Nordic country in Northern Europe, occupying the western portion of the Scandinavian Peninsula. It is the setting of Knut Hamsun's *Hunger* and the author's native country, whose social conditions, literary culture, and physical landscape profoundly shaped the novel.

## In Hamsun's Life

Knut Hamsun was born on August 4, 1859 (or 1860 — sources differ), "in one of the sunny valleys of central Norway" (P04-005). His family moved to Lofoten, in northern Norway, when he was four years old. These early experiences of Norway's dramatic geography — the northern mountains "bathing their feet in the ocean and their crowns in the light of a never-setting sun" (P04-012) — infused Hamsun's writing with a deep sense of place.

Hamsun's restless youth took him across Norway in various occupations: "coal-heaver and school teacher, as road-mender and surveyor's attendant, as farm hand and streetcar conductor, as lecturer and free-lance journalist" (P04-008). This intimate knowledge of Norwegian society across classes informed his fiction, particularly the economic precarity depicted in *Hunger*.

## In the Novel

*Hunger* takes place in Christiania (now Oslo), the capital of Norway, during the 1880s. The novel's social world — its newspaper economy, class distinctions, boarding houses, and street life — is specifically Norwegian, though the narrator's psychological experience transcends any single national context. The novel's Norwegian setting is essential to understanding the specific economic and social pressures the narrator faces, as well as the literary marketplace within which Hamsun himself operated.

## Norway in the 1880s

At the time of *Hunger*'s setting, Norway was in a period of significant cultural and political development. The country was in a personal union with Sweden (dissolved 1905), and Christiania was experiencing rapid urbanization. Norwegian literature was entering its golden age, with figures like Henrik Ibsen, Bjørnstjerne Bjørnson, and the young Knut Hamsun establishing an internationally recognized national literature. Björkman's preface notes Hamsun's rapid rise to become "undoubtedly the foremost creative writer of the Scandinavian countries" (P04-001).
