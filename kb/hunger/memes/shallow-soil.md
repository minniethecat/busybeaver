# Shallow Soil

## Overview

**Shallow Soil** is a novel by [[knut-hamsun|Knut Hamsun]], published in 1893. The English-language preface to *Hunger* lists it among [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s notable early works: "books: 'Hunger,' 'Fictoria' and 'Shallow Soil' (rendered in the list above…)" (P04-018). The grouping with *Hunger* and *Fictoria* (later translated as *Victoria*) places *Shallow Soil* among the foundational works of [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s early career.

## Description

*Shallow Soil* was published three years after *Hunger* and belongs to the period of [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s most rapid artistic development. Coming after the sensation of *Hunger* (1890) and *[[mysteries|Mysteries]]* (1892), the novel arrived when [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]] was consolidating his position as a leading voice of the neo-romantic movement in Scandinavian literature. The original Norwegian title, *[[new-earth-periodical|Ny Jord]]* (literally "[[new-earth-periodical|New Earth]]" or "New Soil"), connects thematically to growth and renewal — motifs that would recur throughout [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s later works, most famously in *[[the-growth-of-the-soil|Growth of the Soil]]* (1917), which won him the Nobel Prize.

The preface's translator notes that the novel's title should not be confused with the Danish literary periodical *[[new-earth-periodical|New Earth]]* (*[[new-earth-periodical|Ny Jord]]*) in which the first sketch of *Hunger* appeared in 1888. The shared name is coincidental, though it suggests the resonance of agricultural and geological metaphors in [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s imaginative world.

## Relationship to Hunger

*Shallow Soil* extends the artistic project that *Hunger* initiated. While *Hunger* explored the psychology of extreme deprivation through a first-person narrator, *Shallow Soil* represents [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s turn toward broader social themes — the title itself suggesting a critique of superficiality in modern life. The novel belongs to the early 1890s, when [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]] was transforming the psychological intensity of *Hunger* into a sustained literary career, testing his voice across different subjects and forms.
