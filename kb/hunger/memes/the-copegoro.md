The *Copégoro* is a Russian-flagged barque moored in the harbor of Christiania, which appears in the climactic final section of Knut Hamsun's *Hunger*. The ship serves as the vehicle of the narrator's escape from the city at the novel's end.

## Appearance in the Novel

The narrator first notices the *Copégoro* during a moment of extreme confusion and exhaustion. Sitting on a packing-case at the wharf, he describes his mental state as "growing more and more confused" [004-143]. He does not resist the collapse: "I do not stir; I simply make no effort whatever to keep up any longer. I just sit there and stare at the *Copégoro*, the barque flying the Russian flag" [004-143].

The wild thoughts that precede this moment — "Supposing I were in all secretness to cut the hawser mooring one of those ships? Supposing I were to suddenly yell out 'Fire'?" [004-143] — reveal a mind on the edge of dissolution. The *Copégoro*, with its foreign flag, represents a portal out of the city that has consumed the narrator.

The ship's physical details anchor the scene in sensory reality: "A large barque with the Russian flag lay and discharged coal. I read her name, *Copégoro*, on her side" [004-143]. The narrator notes the ship's draft: "She lay with IX foot visible on her side, in spite of all the ballast she had already taken in, and there was a hollow boom through the whole ship whenever the coal-heavers' tubs bumped against the side" [004-143]. This detailed observation — the foot markers, the booming sound — suggests a mind momentarily anchored by the physical world, even as it drifts toward dissolution.

## The Departure

A man at the rail catches the narrator's attention — "the red lantern slung at the port shines down upon his head" [004-144] — and the narrator approaches, though "I had no object in talking, as I did not expect to get a reply" [004-144]. Despite this indifference, he asks: "Do you sail tonight, Captain?" [004-144]. The reply comes unexpectedly: "Yes; in a short time" [004-144]. This exchange, brief and oddly formal, sets in motion the narrator's departure.

The narrator's approach to the ship is characteristically oblique. He does not explicitly decide to leave — rather, events arrange themselves around his passivity. A ten-krone note given to him moments earlier by a stranger becomes the means of his passage. The *Copégoro* thus serves as the passive vehicle of an escape the narrator could not — or would not — actively engineer for himself.

## Symbolic Significance

The *Copégoro* functions as both a literal escape vehicle and a symbolic counterpoint to the land-bound suffering that dominates the novel. Its Russian origins suggest the vastness beyond the narrator's narrow world of hunger and humiliation in Christiania. The ship's foreignness — its flag, its name, its crew — embodies the possibility of a world elsewhere, beyond the city's familiar geography of shame.

Critics have noted that the *Copégoro* ending resists closure. The narrator does not arrive anywhere; the novel ends with the ship sailing away from Christiania, the protagonist's fate left entirely open. The ship's name, appearing only once in the text, acquires retrospective weight as the last named object in the novel — the final physical thing the narrator sees before the narrative breaks off. It is mentioned only once, yet its function as the exit point from the novel's world grants it disproportionate significance in the architecture of *Hunger*.
