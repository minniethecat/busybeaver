# Pan

**Pan** is a novel by [[knut-hamsun|Knut Hamsun]], published in 1894, two years after [[mysteries|Mysteries]] and four years after *Hunger*. The preface to *Hunger* identifies *Pan* as the work in which "the real [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]" emerged -- a writer of lyrical intensity and psychological penetration. Named for the Greek god of nature, the novel departs from the urban settings of *Hunger* to locate its drama in the Norwegian Northland, following Lieutenant Glahn's solitary existence as hunter and wanderer. The preface describes [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s own nature as "poetic and picturesque" (P04-004), a character formed by the Northland's "glaring lights and black shadows" (P04-006) -- qualities that find their fullest expression in *Pan*. The novel represents the peak of [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s early novelistic phase and a decisive turn in his literary career.

## Description

*Pan* is set in the remote forests of northern Norway, where Lieutenant Thomas Glahn lives a semi-feral existence. His [[soul|consciousness]] merges with the natural world in passages of extraordinary lyricism, while his relationship with Edvarda, daughter of a local trader, provides the novel's emotional core. The preface places *Pan* in [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s trajectory with a celebrated description:

> Then came "Pan" in 1894, and the real Hamsun stood revealed. (P04-012)

The preface elaborates on *Pan*'s singular achievement: it is a novel "almost without a plot, and having its chief interest in a primitively spontaneous man's reactions to a nature so overwhelming that it makes mere purposeless existence seem a sufficient end in itself" (P04-012). Into it, Hamsun poured "the ecstatic dreams of the little boy from the south as, for the first time, he saw the forestclad northern mountains bathing their feet in the ocean and their crowns in the light of a never-setting sun" (P04-012).

The novel's prose represents a significant development from *Hunger*. Where the earlier work is [[fever|feverish]] and urban, *Pan* is lyrical and pastoral -- though no less psychologically acute. Glahn's [[soul|consciousness]], like [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s in *Hunger*, is rendered from within, blurring the boundary between perception and hallucination.

## Relationship to Hunger

*Pan* marks a turning point in [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s career across several dimensions:

| Aspect | Hunger (1890) | Pan (1894) |
|--------|--------------|------------|
| Setting | Urban ([[christiania|Christiania]] streets) | Rural (Northland forest) |
| Protagonist | Unnamed, starving writer | Named officer, hunter |
| Isolation | Involuntary, social | Voluntary, pastoral |
| Prose style | [[fever|Fever]]ish, fragmented | Lyrical, flowing |
| Relation to nature | City parks as refuge | Full immersion in [[nature|wilderness]] |

After the "line of novels culminating in *Pan*, he turned suddenly to the drama" (P04-013) -- *Pan* thus stands as the culmination of [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s first creative period and the gateway to his later work. The sequels to *Pan* include "Benoni, and Rosa, two novels forming to some extent sequels to Pan" (P04-015), and "A Wanderer Plays with Muted Strings" (P04-015), which would explore Glahn's story further.

## Critical Reception

*Pan* was immediately recognized as a major work and remains one of [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s most widely read novels. The preface describes it as revealing [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s "increasing authority" (P04-012). Its literary stature is underscored by the preface's later comparison: when discussing the later novel *Children of the Time*, Bjorkman notes that the central figure is not "the central figure to the same extent as Lieutenant Glahn in 'Pan' or Kareno in the trilogy" (P04-016) -- placing Glahn as the benchmark against which all Hamsun's subsequent protagonists are measured. Its influence extends across European modernism -- the novel's lyrical rendering of [[soul|consciousness]] anticipates the psychological novels of the twentieth century. Lieutenant Glahn, a figure of "violent, defiant deviation from everything average and ordinary" (P04-003), embodies the [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]ian hero at his most compelling: solitary, in tune with nature, fundamentally incompatible with social existence.
