Karl Johans Gate is the main thoroughfare of central Oslo, named after King Karl III Johan (Charles XIV John of Sweden). It stretches from the Central Station to the Royal Palace, passing the Parliament building, the National Theatre, and the university.

## Historical Significance

Laid out in the 1840s and named in 1852, Karl Johans Gate became the symbolic heart of the Norwegian capital. By the 1880s–1890s — the period of Hamsun's *Hunger* — it was the city's premier promenade, lined with shops, cafés, and institutions. It represented the public face of Kristiania, where different social classes intersected visibly.

## In Hunger

The narrator of *Hunger* frequently traverses Karl Johans Gate during his wanderings. The street functions as a stage for social observation: here he encounters editors, ladies with parasols, fellow writers, and the anonymous crowds of the city. The contrast between the narrator's deteriorating physical condition and the prosperous thoroughfare amplifies the novel's themes of alienation and social exclusion.

The street's association with publishing and journalism (newspaper offices were concentrated nearby) connects it directly to the narrator's professional aspirations and failures.

## PN References

The narrator's perambulations along Karl Johans Gate recur throughout Parts I–IV of the novel.
