## Overview

Struggle is the fundamental condition of the protagonist in Knut Hamsun's *Hunger* — a relentless, often irrational striving against hunger, poverty, and his own psychological deterioration. To read the novel is to inhabit this struggle from within: the first-person narration makes the reader experience the narrator's effort not as a theme to be observed but as a sensation to be endured. What distinguishes the narrator's struggle is its lack of strategic direction: he does not formulate a plan, seek steady employment, or systematically pursue opportunities. Instead, he struggles reactively — lurching from crisis to crisis with intelligence but without strategy, a quality that captures how extreme deprivation impairs the capacity for long-term thinking.

## In Hunger

The novel's introduction frames struggle as central to Hamsun's literary project. The author wrote during "those days of feverish literary struggle" before rising "to heights where he could look down upon his own early endeavors" [P04-007]. This biographical context suggests that the novel's depiction of struggle draws from Hamsun's own experience. The phrase "feverish literary struggle" links creative effort to physical illness, as if struggle were a sickness from which the artist might eventually recover.

The broader philosophical framing in the introduction notes how we are often "moved to sympathy by the spectacle of their struggle" [P04-005], suggesting that struggle — not achievement — is what generates human connection and narrative interest.

Within the narrative, the protagonist's struggle takes on almost absurd proportions. In one scene, he "competed with the very horses, struggled like a madman to arrive there first" [001-201], describing a frantic race through Christiania's streets driven by an irrational impulse. The comparison to horses — beasts of burden — reduces the narrator to a creature governed by instinct rather than reason.

His struggle is not always dignified — it can be petty and desperate. He admits to having "struggled with the meanest thoughts" about poor people and their rent [001-265], revealing how his own suffering corrodes his empathy. Hunger makes him small, envious, and cruel in his thoughts — an important counterpoint to the more romantic depictions of creative struggle.

The psychological dimension of struggle is most vivid when the narrator describes his attempt to endure: "to deaden the stings a little, I struggled on, keeping my eyes fastened on a point far ahead" [002-195]. The struggle is not to achieve anything specific but simply to continue existing. The image of "keeping my eyes fastened on a point far ahead" suggests a technique of endurance — focus on an arbitrary distant goal to avoid confronting the impossibility of the present.

A female character in the novel also embodies this theme, making "a despairing struggle" and giving "vent at last to a little shriek" [003-206], suggesting that struggle is a universal human condition, not unique to the protagonist. This brief episode — a woman whose circumstances are left unexplained — generalizes the experience of struggle beyond the narrator's particular case.

## Literary Context

The theme of existential struggle in *Hunger* places Hamsun in a lineage that runs from Kierkegaard's philosophy of the solitary individual to the twentieth-century existentialists. The narrator's struggle is not for a specific outcome but for the maintenance of selfhood itself. Unlike the social-problem novels of the nineteenth century, which located struggle in economic conditions that might be reformed, *Hunger* suggests that struggle is irreducible — a permanent feature of the human condition, intensified but not created by poverty.

This view of struggle as intrinsic rather than circumstantial would influence later modernist and existentialist writers. The narrator's experience anticipates Camus's Sisyphus — the struggle itself, not its outcome, defines the person.

## Related Concepts

Struggle in *Hunger* is tightly bound to **hope** — it is hope that gives struggle its direction. **Pride** complicates the protagonist's struggle by preventing him from accepting help. **Suffering** is both the cause and consequence of his struggle. **Hunger** is the most immediate form of struggle, the bodily demand that drives all other conflicts.
