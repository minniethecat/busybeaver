# August Strindberg

## Overview

**August Strindberg** (1849–1912) was a Swedish playwright, novelist, and painter, and one of the most significant figures in Scandinavian literature. The preface to [[knut-hamsun|Knut Hamsun]]'s *[[hunger|Hunger]]* (1890) names Strindberg alongside [[henrik-ibsen|Ibsen]] as the measure of literary greatness: "Since the [[death|death]] of Ibsen and Strindberg, [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]] is undoubtedly the foremost creative writer of the Scandinavian countries" (P04-001). The preface portrays Hamsun as an heir to this tradition — an "individualistic romanticist and a highly subjective aristocrat" (P04-003) whose originality places him in the lineage of the Scandinavian masters.

## In the Narrative

Strindberg does not appear in the novel but is invoked in the preface's literary-historical framework. His works are implicitly referenced in the description of the neo-romantic movement, which found "typical expressions not only in the works of theretofore unknown writers, but in the changed moods of masters like Ibsen and Bjornson and Strindberg" (P04-010).

The preface's literary method — positioning Hamsun through comparisons with international figures — extends beyond Scandinavia. The same paragraph that describes the neo-romantic sensibility also notes *Hunger*'s affinities with "the Impressionistic temper of a Gauguin or Cezanne" (P04-010). And in discussing *[[mysteries|Mysteries]]*, the preface identifies a surprising synthesis: "one finds the spirits of [[fyodor-dostoyevski|Dostoyevski]] and Mark Twain blended" (P04-011). This cosmopolitan frame of reference, in which Strindberg stands as the Swedish pillar, establishes the breadth of the tradition Hamsun was entering.

## Relationship to the Narrator

Strindberg, like Ibsen, represents the literary establishment that Hamsun was simultaneously inheriting and challenging. Both writers were pioneers of psychological drama and prose, and their influence on Hamsun's narrative technique — particularly the focus on individual consciousness and the rejection of conventional realism — was significant. The preface's comparison of Hamsun's *[[vendt-the-monk|Vendt the Monk]]* to Ibsen's *Peer Gynt* — noting that Vendt "has much in common with Peer Gynt without being in any way an imitation or a duplicate" (P04-014) — applies equally to Hamsun's relationship with Strindberg: influence absorbed and transformed rather than copied.

## Critical Context

The pairing of Ibsen and Strindberg in the preface is strategic: it positions Hamsun as the rightful heir to a Scandinavian literary tradition that, with their deaths, had reached a generational turning point. Hamsun's *Hunger* is presented as the work that inaugurates the new era.
