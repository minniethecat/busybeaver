# Children of the Age

## Overview

**Children of the Age** (*Børn av Tiden*) is a novel by [[knut-hamsun|Knut Hamsun]], published in 1913. The English-language preface to *Hunger* presents it as a pivotal work that "in many ways struck a new note" after a period when critics feared [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s best work lay behind him (P04-016). At the time of its publication, some observers doubted whether Hamsun could still produce work of the first rank, making the novel's appearance a significant moment of vindication (P04-016).

## Description

The novel represents a significant turning point in [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s career. Where his earlier works — *Hunger*, [[mysteries|Mysteries]], [[pan|Pan]] — centered on a single isolated protagonist, *Children of the Age* widened the frame. The preface observes that while "there is still a central figure, and still he possesses many of the old [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]] traits," the protagonist "has crossed the meridian at last and become an observer rather than a fighter and doer" (P04-016). This shift from active participant to observing consciousness mirrors Hamsun's own artistic evolution from the subjective intensity of the 1890s to the more detached, panoramic mode of his mature work.

The life depicted is "the life of a certain spot of ground — Segelfoss manor, and later the town of Segelfoss — rather than that of one or two isolated individuals" (P04-016). The preface goes so far as to suggest that "one might almost say that [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s vision has become social at last" (P04-016). The novel inaugurated the Segelfoss cycle, which continued with *[[segelfoss-town|Segelfoss Town]]* (1915) and culminated in *[[the-growth-of-the-soil|The Growth of the Soil]]* (1917), the work for which Hamsun would receive the Nobel Prize in Literature (P04-017).

## Relationship to Hunger

*Children of the Age* is separated from *Hunger* by twenty-three years and an entire artistic transformation. Where *Hunger* is a novel of interiority — every event filtered through the starving narrator's consciousness — *Children of the Age* turns outward, making a community rather than a single psyche its subject. The preface's observation that the protagonist has "become an observer rather than a fighter and doer" (P04-016) could equally describe Hamsun's own evolution as a novelist. *Hunger*'s [[christiania|Christiania]] is a city seen through the distorting lens of starvation; *Children of the Age*'s Segelfoss is a community rendered with the broad, steady gaze of social observation. Yet the two works share a fundamental concern with the relationship between the individual and their environment, and both display Hamsun's characteristic attention to the textures of everyday life — a quality already present in *Hunger*'s minute descriptions of streets, rooms, and the physical sensations of poverty.
