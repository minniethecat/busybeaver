# Ylajali

## Overview

**Ylajali** is the most significant female figure in [[knut-hamsun|Knut Hamsun]]'s *[[hunger|Hunger]]* (1890), a mysterious young woman whom [[the-unnamed-narrator|the unnamed narrator]] encounters in the streets of [[christiania|Christiania]] and whose name — invented by [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] himself — becomes a symbol of his romantic and artistic aspirations. She appears across all four Parts of the novel, though her physical presence is fleeting; it is in the narrator's [[imagination|imagination]] that she achieves her full significance.

## In the Narrative

Ylajali first appears in Part I, introduced by "a gliding, nervous sound — Ylajali!" (001-042). The name is presented not as something she tells him but as something he perceives — an auditory sensation that slides into a proper name. When "she is quite close to me" (001-042), the narrator is already in the grip of a fantasy that the encounter itself cannot sustain.

The narrator names her himself: "I named you Ylajali. How do you like that? It has a gliding sound" (003-159). This act of naming is crucial — Ylajali is in part the narrator's creation, a character he writes into being. She is "a fairy, a princess" (001-116) in the story he tells about her, and her [[reality|reality]] is always mediated through his narrative framing.

In Part II, Ylajali becomes a vision of impossible beauty: "you see everything is of ruby — Ylajali, Ylajali! that swelling crimson" (002-064). The repetition of her name is incantatory — Ylajali is less a person than a spell, a word that conjures a world of color and sensuality that the narrator's austerity cannot provide.

By Part III, the relationship has become more complex. The narrator wonders: "Had I, too, not polluted her in my thoughts?" (003-014), acknowledging that his fantasy has a dark underside. Yet he also experiences "the joy of being able to meet Ylajali cleanly and honourably" (003-017) — the possibility of a real relationship, however fleeting.

## Relationship to the Narrator

Ylajali functions as a mirror for the narrator's desires and insecurities. She is simultaneously the object of his romantic idealization and the catalyst for his most intense self-doubt. The narrator cannot separate his feelings for Ylajali from his anxieties about his [[poverty|poverty]], his appearance, and his worthiness — she becomes the test that he knows he will fail.
