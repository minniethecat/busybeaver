# Joachim Kierulf

## Overview

Joachim Kierulf is a wool-dealer in [[christiania|Christiania]] whom [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] claims to be seeking during a feverish, delusional episode in Part III of *[[hunger|Hunger]]*. Kierulf does not actually appear in the novel — he exists only as a name the narrator invents and repeats while wandering the streets in a state of mental and physical exhaustion. The episode is one of the novel's most clinically precise depictions of starvation-induced confabulation and the collapse of the boundary between [[memory|memory]] and [[imagination|imagination]].

## In the Narrative

Kierulf is mentioned exclusively in Part III (003-021–003-043). The narrator, wandering aimlessly and nearing the limits of starvation, stops a girl on the street and asks for "Kierulf — Joachim Kierulf, if I might add further — a wool-dealer; in short, not a man one could make a mistake about" (003-021). The precision of the name and profession is striking — Joachim Kierulf, wool-dealer — as if specificity itself could conjure the man into existence. The girl shakes her head and replies that no Kierulf lives there (003-022). Undeterred, the narrator persists, repeating the name insistently: "Kierulf, a dealer in wool — Kierulf" (003-028). He later asks a policeman the same question, who responds with a mixture of puzzlement and professional courtesy: "What was it you said he was called? — Kierulf?" (003-032).

The episode culminates as the narrator reflects that Kierulf has "vanished from my memory; was wiped out with many other mad whims which came and went in turns" and that he now recalls him "no longer, except as a reminiscence — a phantom" (003-043). The word "phantom" is the narrator's own — a rare moment of metacognitive honesty in which he acknowledges that what he pursued through the streets was not a person but a projection.

## Psychological Significance

The Kierulf episode is one of the novel's most clinically precise depictions of starvation-induced confabulation. The narrator does not merely forget a real person; he invents one with sufficient detail — full name, profession, implied business dealings — to sustain a prolonged search through the streets of Christiania. The episode's structure mirrors the architecture of delusion: a fixed idea, impervious to contrary evidence (the girl's denial, the policeman's bafflement), pursued with methodical persistence until it simply dissolves, replaced by another "mad whim" (003-043).

The narrator's choice of a wool-dealer as the object of his search is psychologically suggestive. Wool — a commodity of warmth, comfort, and material security — is precisely what the starving, coatless narrator lacks. The phantom Kierulf may thus represent an unconscious projection: the narrator is not merely looking for a person but for the material relief that person's name metonymically evokes. That Kierulf is a "dealer" — someone who transacts, who exchanges goods for money — further links him to the economic desperation that drives the narrator's mental dissolution.

The episode's narrative placement in Part III, after the narrator's imprisonment and release, reinforces the pattern of accelerating psychological fragmentation. As physical [[hunger|hunger]] deepens, the boundary between memory and invention blurs. The narrator's concluding admission — that Kierulf is now merely "a reminiscence — a phantom" (003-043) — is arguably the novel's most honest self-assessment: a recognition that the mind under extreme duress generates its own reality, populating the world with figures as substantial and as insubstantial as itself.

## Relationship to the Narrator

Kierulf has no actual relationship to the narrator because Kierulf almost certainly does not exist. The narrator's repeated inquiries about a "wool-dealer named Kierulf" represent one of the novel's clearest depictions of starvation-induced psychosis. The narrator himself becomes uncertain whether Kierulf is a real person he once knew or a complete invention — a moment of meta-cognitive awareness that the boundaries between his inner world and external reality have collapsed entirely.

## Critical Context

The Kierulf episode anticipates modernist techniques of unreliable narration that would become central to twentieth-century literature. [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s innovation is not merely to present an unreliable narrator but to make the narrator partially aware of his own unreliability — to show him catching himself in the act of fabrication and acknowledging it without fully correcting it. The "phantom" that Kierulf becomes (003-043) is also, in a sense, what all fictional characters are: names invested with reality by the act of narration, capable of vanishing when the narration withdraws its belief.
