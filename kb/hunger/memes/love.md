## Overview

Love in Knut Hamsun's *Hunger* appears as both a romantic ideal and a source of torment for the protagonist, who oscillates between passionate yearning and self-loathing detachment. What makes the treatment of love distinctive in this novel is that it is almost entirely imaginary — the narrator loves from a distance, loves in fantasy, loves in language, but never loves in reality.

## In Hunger

The protagonist experiences love not as a stable bond but as fleeting, intense encounters — often imagined or projected onto strangers — that highlight his profound isolation. He is a lover without a beloved, a romantic without a romance. This pattern illustrates a broader aspect of the narrator's psychology: his interior life is rich and intense, but it has almost no correspondence with external reality. He constructs elaborate romantic scenarios around women he barely knows, transforming casual glances into passionate encounters in his imagination. Love becomes another form of the self-deception that sustains him.

The narrator fixates on a woman he calls "Ylajali," observing her from afar and constructing elaborate romantic scenarios without ever truly engaging with her. In one passage, he notes a woman's appearance: "She was the loveliest creature I had ever seen" [001-116], yet his admiration quickly becomes a source of self-conscious anguish rather than connection. An old fellow's knowing response — "So--o; was she so lovely?" [001-117] — underscores the gap between the narrator's romantic intensity and the mundane reality others perceive.

The narrator's romantic imagination sometimes finds expression in quasi-poetic outbursts: "Passionately she breathes. Kiss me — loved one — more — more! Your arms are soft" [002-064]. This private ecstasy, addressed to no real person, reveals the solipsistic quality of his love. He is not reaching toward another person but toward an idealized image that exists only in his mind.

The physical landscape of Christiania also becomes a canvas for the protagonist's romantic sensibilities. He notes "a pair of lovers stood in a doorway and talked together in low tones" in Rebslager [002-172], observing others' intimacy from the outside. In his moments of heightened sensitivity — often driven by hunger — even the weather becomes romantic: "Well, it was a lovely day, anyway! Sunlight bright..." [002-053]. A morning walk can inspire the exclamation "What caresses me with soothing loveliness" [003-066] — love transferred from absent human beings onto the natural world.

A darker side of the narrator's romanticism emerges in his confession: "I am so desperately in love with you, and it is so preposterous" [003-157]. This line — part of an imaginary dialogue — captures the self-awareness that accompanies his delusions. He knows his love is preposterous; he cannot stop feeling it. The romantic ideal is also fragile: a woman who once seemed beautiful can appear the next day "not in the very slightest degree lovely to me; she had fallen off" [004-038]. The narrator's romantic perceptions are as unstable as his physical condition.

## Art and Love

Edwin Björkman's introduction describes Hamsun himself as "a passionate lover of life who in the end accepts his fate serenely" [P04-014], and notes that *Victoria* contains "a charming love story that embodies the tender, wistful, and passionate elements of his early work" [P04-015]. This framing suggests that love in Hamsun's universe is inextricably linked to the artistic temperament — intense, consuming, and often unfulfilled. The narrator expresses love through artistic passion as much as through romantic attachment, and his creative identity and capacity for love are portrayed as aspects of a single temperament.

## Literary Context

Hamsun's treatment of love in *Hunger* reflects the influence of Dostoevsky's underground man — a protagonist whose romantic impulses are inseparable from self-loathing and social alienation. But where Dostoevsky's anti-hero is paralyzed by excessive consciousness, Hamsun's narrator is driven by a hunger that transforms consciousness itself into a kind of intoxication.

The novel also anticipates elements of the surrealist treatment of love — the obsessive idealization of the beloved, the collapse of the boundary between fantasy and reality. The narrator's "Ylajali" is less a character than a projection screen for his desires, a forerunner of the surrealist *femme-enfant*.

## Related Concepts

Love in *Hunger* intersects with several other central themes. **Shame** prevents the protagonist from pursuing genuine romantic connection, as his poverty makes him feel unworthy. **Hope** fuels his romantic fantasies — the belief that love might redeem his suffering. **Isolation** defines his actual condition, as his experience of love remains entirely internal. **Despair** follows each failed romantic impulse, deepening his psychological deterioration.
