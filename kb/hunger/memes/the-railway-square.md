# The Railway Square

## Overview

The **Railway Square** (Jernbanetorget) is the public plaza outside [[christiania|Christiania]]'s central railway station, appearing at several pivotal moments in [[knut-hamsun|Knut Hamsun]]'s *Hunger*. It is a space of transit and threshold — where the city meets its transport infrastructure, where [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] contemplates departure, and where the impulse to leave [[christiania|Christiania]] crystallizes into action or dissipates into continued wandering.

## In the Narrative

The railway square enters the novel in Part II as a destination [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] reaches without conscious intent: "Before I realized it, I was at the railway square. The dock on Our Saviour's pointed to half-past one" (002-097). The square is defined by its clock and its spatial relationship — near the wharf, the railway bridge, the church — a junction of urban landmarks that mirrors [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s psychological condition at a crossroads.

In Part III, the square becomes the site of pursuit and paranoia. The narrator "jogged along again to the railway square" (003-100), followed by a man whose silence he interprets as menace. The square's openness, meant for the free movement of travelers, becomes a theater of projected [[fear|anxiety]]: "The man does not utter a syllable" (003-100). At the corner of "the railway square and Harbour Street" (003-102), [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s pursuer suddenly reveals himself — not as a threat but as an acquaintance, collapsing [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s paranoid narrative into anticlimax.

The railway square reappears in Part IV, with [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] standing "at Johann Street, almost at the railway square. God knows why I stood just there" (004-006). The self-[[soul|consciousness]] of the aside is characteristic: [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] does not know why he is drawn to the railway square, only that he is. The square exerts a gravitational pull — the promise of trains, of departure, of escape from [[christiania|Christiania]] — that [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] can never fully act on.

## Symbolic Significance

The railway square is the novel's primary spatial symbol of the possibility of departure. Every railway station is a threshold between where one is and where one might go, and [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s repeated gravitation toward the square expresses a desire for escape that he can never quite execute. He approaches the trains but never boards them; he stands at the brink of departure but remains.

The square's association with precise time — "the dock on Our Saviour's pointed to half-past one" (002-097) — contrasts with [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s own temporal drift. The railway runs on schedules; [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] lives outside them. The clock towers that mark the square's perimeter measure a time [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] does not inhabit.
