Class and status distinctions permeate Knut Hamsun's *Hunger*, structuring the narrator's experience of Kristiania and his own identity. The narrator occupies an ambiguous class position: he is educated and aspires to literary recognition, yet lives in conditions of extreme material deprivation.

## Downward Mobility

The narrator's poverty is not inherited working-class life but a fall from bourgeois respectability. He has the cultural capital of education and literary ambition without the economic capital to sustain it. This makes his suffering specifically modern — it is the precariousness of the intellectual proletariat in a market economy that values only what can be sold.

## Status Performance

Throughout the novel, the narrator performs status he does not possess. He dresses carefully, speaks formally to strangers, and insists on being treated as a gentleman. These performances are both protective (warding off the stigma of vagrancy) and self-destructive (preventing him from accepting the charity that might save him).
