# Growth of the Soil

## Overview

*Growth of the Soil* (Norwegian: *Markens Grøde*) is [[knut-hamsun|Knut Hamsun]]'s 1917 novel for which he was awarded the [[hamsun-nobel-prize|Nobel Prize in Literature]] in 1920. The novel represents the culmination of Hamsun's "Nordland" period — novels set in northern Norway that celebrate the primitive relationship between humans and the land. The preface to *[[hunger|Hunger]]* (P04) traces Hamsun's literary development from the urban psychological intensity of his breakthrough novel to the epic, pastoral vision of *Growth of the Soil*.

## Description

Set in the Norwegian wilderness, *Growth of the Soil* follows Isak, a pioneer who clears land, builds a homestead, and establishes a family in the Nordland region. The novel spans generations, chronicling the slow, patient work of cultivation against the backdrop of an indifferent but majestic natural world. Unlike the febrile, urban consciousness of *Hunger*'s narrator, Isak is a man of few words and instinctive wisdom — "a primitively spontaneous man" whose reactions to "a nature so overwhelming that it makes mere purposeless existence seem a sufficient end in itself" (P04-019) represent Hamsun's mature vision of human fulfillment.

The novel also traces the arrival of modern commerce and industry in the north — the tension between the old agrarian life and the new forces of capital and urbanization that threaten it. Hamsun's characteristic irony and humor pervade the depiction of "the natives, whose hands used always to be in their pockets, and whose credulity in face of the improbable was only surpassed by their unwillingness to believe anything reasonable" (P04-020).

## Relationship to Hunger

The preface to *Hunger* positions *Growth of the Soil* as the endpoint of Hamsun's artistic trajectory — "the real Hamsun, the Hamsun who ever since has moved logically and with increasing authority to 'The Growth of the Soil,' stood finally revealed" with the publication of *Pan* in 1894 (P04-012). Where *Hunger* depicts consciousness in a state of urban fragmentation, *Growth of the Soil* depicts life in a state of organic wholeness. The two novels are complementary poles of Hamsun's vision: the city and the wilderness, the mind and the soil, disintegration and integration.

Edwin Björkman, in his introduction, notes that the English title ought properly to be *The Earth's Increase* — a translation that better captures the biblical resonance of the Norwegian *Markens Grøde* (P04-020). The novel was published in English translation in 1918, one year after the Norwegian original.

## Critical Reception

*Growth of the Soil* was the principal work cited by the Nobel Committee in awarding Hamsun the 1920 Nobel Prize in Literature. It has been read variously as a celebration of agrarian values, a critique of modernity, and — problematically, in light of Hamsun's later political sympathies — as an expression of the *Blut und Boden* ideology that would later be appropriated by fascism. Contemporary scholarship tends to separate the novel's literary achievement from Hamsun's biography while acknowledging the complexity of this separation.
