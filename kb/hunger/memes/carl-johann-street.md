## Overview

Carl Johann Street (Karl Johans gate) is the principal boulevard of Christiania (present-day Oslo), named after King Charles III John of Sweden and Norway. Stretching from the Central Railway Station to the Royal Palace, it was and remains the city's most prominent thoroughfare. In *Hunger*, it serves as the central artery of the city through which the narrator repeatedly passes — a stage where he observes the bustling life of the city from which he is increasingly alienated [001-064].

## In the Narrative

The narrator traverses Carl Johann Street throughout the novel, often in states of heightened physical and emotional distress. Early in Part I, he walks among the "laughing folk" who move up and down the street, acutely conscious of his own shabby appearance and exclusion from their world:

> "Laughing folk walked up and down Carl Johann Street. I stuck my elbows closely to my sides and tried to make myself look small and inconspicuous." [001-064]

After a failed attempt to sell an article to a newspaper, he takes a turn down Carl Johann in agitation, wheeling round before continuing [001-193]. In a moment of particular desperation, he "flew up Carl Johann" and began to swear aloud, utterly miserable in spirit [001-221]. The street appears again in Part III, where the narrator wanders it early in the morning, keeping watch for the woman in the red dress [003-121]. In Part IV, he notes a shoemaker's shop located far down Carl Johann Street, near the railway square [004-006], and later watches the red dress glide up the street and disappear [004-025].

The street thus traces the arc of the narrator's obsession: from the anonymous crowd to the singular figure of the woman in red, from public humiliation to private fantasy.

## Symbolic Significance

Carl Johann Street represents the public, respectable Christiania from which the narrator is excluded. It is the space of "laughing folk" — the well-fed, well-dressed citizens whose normalcy the narrator can observe but not join. His repeated walks along the street map his oscillation between attempted integration and defiant estrangement: he walks there to feel part of the city, yet each visit sharpens his awareness of his own degradation. The street's linear axis — from the railway station at one end to the palace at the other — also mirrors the social hierarchy of the city, with the narrator trapped somewhere in the middle, going nowhere.

## Real-World Context

Karl Johans gate was laid out in the 1840s as part of Christiania's expansion and named after King Charles III John (Karl Johan in Swedish/Norwegian), the monarch of the United Kingdoms of Sweden and Norway from 1818 to 1844. By the 1880s, when *Hunger* is set, it had become the city's main promenade, lined with shops, cafes, and the Parliament building (Stortinget). Hamsun himself walked these streets during his own impoverished years in Christiania, lending the fictional narrator's geography an autobiographical precision. The street remains Oslo's central boulevard today.
