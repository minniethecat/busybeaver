# Fear

## Overview

**Fear** is a constant undercurrent in [[knut-hamsun|Knut Hamsun]]'s *Hunger* (1890), manifesting as social anxiety, fear of exposure, and a deeper existential dread. Unlike [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s pride, which he consciously wrestles with, fear often operates below the level of explicit reflection — it is a condition he inhabits rather than a feeling he examines. The preface notes that [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]] "fears and flouts the dominance of the many" (P04-003), identifying fear of the collective as a defining trait of both author and narrator.

## In the Narrative

The narrator's fear is most acute in social situations. When a stranger "sent me a half-fearful, half-questioning look" (001-052), [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] projects his own anxiety onto others, reading fear in their faces. This projective quality recurs throughout the novel — [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s internal state colors his perception of everyone he encounters.

Fear of exposure is a recurring motif. The narrator hides his poverty from others, terrified of being seen as what he is. A shopkeeper "sought for words as if he feared to offend" (001-125), and this delicate social dance — where both parties fear the other's judgment — captures [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s precarious position in [[christiania|Christiania]]'s social order.

In Part I, fear and anger merge: [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] "fought and wrestled with anger and fear until nearly dawn" (001-233). This combination of fear and rage is characteristic of [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s psychological portrait — fear does not produce passivity but a volatile oscillation between withdrawal and aggression.

The narrator's fear also has a physical dimension. He wakes "in a sweat of fear" (001-285), his body registering what his conscious mind cannot fully process. Fear thus becomes somatic, indistinguishable from the fever and hunger that wrack his body. In this, [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]] anticipates the twentieth-century understanding of anxiety as a psychosomatic condition.

Despite [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s claims of fearlessness — "I had no fear whatever for the future" (001-029) — these assertions ring hollow against the accumulating evidence of his terror. His bravado is itself a defense against fear, a performative courage that masks deeper vulnerability.

## Literary Context

[[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s treatment of fear as a diffuse, somatic, and socially mediated condition represents a significant departure from the more externalized fears of earlier literature. Where a Gothic novel might present fear as a response to a specific threat, [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s fear is atmospheric — it permeates [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s [[soul|consciousness]] without a single identifiable source. This anticipates the generalized anxiety that would become a central theme in modernist and existentialist literature.
