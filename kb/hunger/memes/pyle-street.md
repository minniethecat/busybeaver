# Pyle Street

## Overview

Pyle Street (Norwegian: Pilestredet) is a street in [[christiania|Christiania]] that features in *[[hunger|Hunger]]* primarily for its pawnbroker's shop — one of the few economic resources [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] can access during his financial desperation. The street represents the narrator's last, humiliating recourse: the conversion of personal belongings into survival money, a transaction that strips him not only of objects but of the identity and dignity those objects represent.

## In the Narrative

The narrator first hurries down Pyle Street early in Part I when he decides to pawn his waistcoat: "I hurried as fast as I could down Pyle Street, where I knew of a pawnbroker's shop where I had raised money on things before" (001-024). The phrase "where I had raised money on things before" is quietly devastating — the pawnbroker is not a discovery but a familiar destination, a place the narrator has visited often enough to know its location by heart.

After the transaction, he turns down Pyle Street again, stopping before a grocer's shop whose "whole window was filled with eatables" (001-032) — the pawnbroker's money, barely acquired, already insufficient to bridge the gap between desire and survival. Later, realizing he has left his pencil at the pawnbroker's — wrapped in the paper with his waistcoat — he returns to Pyle Street to retrieve it (001-038, 001-056). The pencil, a writer's essential tool, is worth nothing to the pawnbroker but everything to the narrator; its loss and recovery on Pyle Street encapsulates the street's dual role as a site of both material degradation and the stubborn preservation of creative identity.

In Part II, the narrator walks "a long way up Pyle Street" before standing still, exhausted (002-134). This aimless wandering along Pyle Street is part of his hunger-driven circuit through Christiania — a pattern of movement that connects the street to other recurring destinations in the narrator's geography, particularly [[the-dampkokken]] and [[our-saviours-church]].

## Symbolic Significance

Pyle Street embodies the economics of desperation. The pawnbroker's shop transforms the personal into the anonymous — a waistcoat becomes collateral, a pencil becomes trash. Each visit to Pyle Street marks a step in the narrator's material disintegration: first the waistcoat, then other possessions, each object carrying a memory or an identity that the transaction erases. The street's very name — "Pyle," from the Norwegian *pil* suggesting an arrow or a pile — carries a faint suggestion of accumulation and passage, of things gathered and things lost.

The street's proximity to a grocer's shop (001-032) creates a spatial irony: the narrator passes from the pawnbroker (where he acquires money) to the grocer (where he could acquire food) but cannot complete the circuit. He has enough for money, or enough for food, but never enough for both at once. Pyle Street is where the narrator's economic logic breaks down — where exchange value and use value, money and sustenance, refuse to align.

## Real-World Context

Pilestredet is a real street in Oslo (formerly Christiania), running through the central and western parts of the city. In the late 19th century when *Hunger* is set, the area would have been a mixed residential and commercial district, with pawnbrokers, grocers, and small shops typical of working-class neighborhoods. The street's name derives from a row of willow trees (*pile* in older Norwegian) that once lined it — a pastoral echo entirely absent from the narrator's experience of the street as a site of urban deprivation.
