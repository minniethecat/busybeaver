# Crimes of Futurity

## Overview

"**Crimes of Futurity**" is a fictional article and thesis that the unnamed narrator of [[knut-hamsun|Knut Hamsun]]'s *Hunger* attempts to write while wandering the streets of [[christiania|Christiania]] in a state of starvation. The work exists entirely within the novel's fictional universe — it is [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s intellectual project, never completed, and functions as a window into his restless, self-deceiving mind. The piece is first mentioned alongside another possible topic, "Freedom of Will," as [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] casts about for a subject that might earn him money and restore his [[pride|dignity]] (001-014).

## Description

The article on the "Crimes of Futurity" is conceived as a philosophical investigation — a treatise on the moral transgressions of future ages. The narrator returns to the idea repeatedly throughout Part One of the novel, each time with renewed conviction that the piece will be his breakthrough. "Before evening my thesis on the 'Crimes of Futurity' would be ready," he tells himself at one point, beginning to formulate the argument in his mind (001-027).

Yet the work remains perpetually unfinished. The narrator's physical deterioration — his hunger, his sleeplessness, his inability to afford paper or a quiet room — conspires against his intellectual ambition. In a moment of bitter self-awareness, he reflects that the subject is "so simple and straight-ahead as the 'Crimes of Futurity,' that any ass might arrive at the result" (001-037). This self-lacerating comparison reveals [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s deeper fear: that his intellectual pretensions are hollow, that the work he pursues may be worthless even if completed.

The "Crimes of Futurity" serves as a counterpoint to another of [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s writing projects, the treatise on "[[philosophical-cognition|Philosophical Cognition]]" — the former an article, the latter a three-volume dissertation. Together they map [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s oscillating self-image: sometimes a journalist turning out a quick piece, sometimes a serious philosopher producing a magnum opus.

## Relationship to Hunger

The "Crimes of Futurity" is one of several writing projects [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] pursues and abandons throughout *Hunger*. These fictional works — never quoted directly in the novel — function as dramatic irony. The reader never sees a word of [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s writing; instead, [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]] shows only the gap between [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s lofty intellectual claims and his degrading physical circumstances. The article's grandiose title — "Crimes of Futurity" — contrasts sharply with [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s immediate reality of pawned waistcoats, eviction notices, and gnawing hunger.

The work also illuminates [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s relationship to the intellectual culture of [[christiania|Christiania]]. He imagines himself part of the city's literary world, a contributor to its newspapers and periodicals, even as he sleeps in doorways and begs for bread. The "Crimes of Futurity" is his ticket back into that world — or so he believes.
