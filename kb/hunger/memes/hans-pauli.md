# Hans Pauli

## Overview

**Hans Pauli** (full name Hans Pauli Pettersen) is a recurring acquaintance of the unnamed narrator in Knut Hamsun's *Hunger* (1890). A "peasant-farmer" (002-077) by background, Hans Pauli occupies a social position slightly above the narrator's — he has a blanket to lend, an address that can be sought, and the stability that the narrator lacks. His appearances in the novel mark the narrator's sporadic attempts to maintain social connections amid accelerating personal disintegration.

## In the Narrative

Hans Pauli first appears in Part I as a passing figure: "Up in Graendsen I met Hans Pauli, who nodded and hurried past" (001-013). This brief encounter establishes the pattern — Hans Pauli is someone the narrator knows but cannot rely on, a node in a social network that is fraying.

In Part II, Hans Pauli becomes central to the narrator's survival strategy. The narrator still possesses "the bed [that] was lent to me by Hans Pauli some months ago" (002-067), and Hans Pauli's blanket becomes one of the narrator's few remaining possessions — a material link to a more stable past. The narrator resolves "to put Hans Pauli into a good humour" (002-086) in hopes of extracting further assistance, revealing the transactional nature of their relationship.

The narrator's description of Hans Pauli is notably generous: "Hans Pauli had such a big heart" (002-087). This attribution of generosity may or may not be accurate — the narrator's judgment is unreliable, and his descriptions of others often reflect his needs rather than their qualities. But the characterization matters: Hans Pauli represents the possibility of human kindness in a world that otherwise offers the narrator only indifference.

By the end of Part II, the narrator is reduced to rolling up "Hans Pauli's blanket" (002-155) — a strange comfort object that is both a practical possession and a symbolic link to the only person who has consistently helped him.

## Relationship to the Narrator

Hans Pauli functions as a baseline of normalcy against which the narrator's deterioration can be measured. He is not a close friend — their encounters are brief, transactional — but he is a consistent presence, someone the narrator can seek out when all other options have been exhausted. The asymmetry of their relationship (Hans Pauli lends; the narrator borrows) mirrors the narrator's broader dependency on a society that has no place for him.
