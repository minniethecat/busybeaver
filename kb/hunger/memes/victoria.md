# Victoria

## Overview

**Victoria** is a novel by [[knut-hamsun|Knut Hamsun]], published in 1898, during the prolific decade that followed his breakthrough with *Hunger* (1890). The English-language preface to *Hunger* describes it as "a novel with a charming love story that embodies the tenderest note in his production" (P04-015). It was published the same year as *[[sunset-glow|Sunset Glow]]*, Hamsun's second play (P04-013), marking a year in which Hamsun worked simultaneously in dramatic and novelistic forms.

## Description

*Victoria* stands apart from the [[fever|feverish]] intensity of *Hunger* and the lyrical pastoral of [[pan|Pan]] (1894). Where *Hunger* tracks the disintegration of a starving writer in [[christiania|Christiania]] and [[pan|Pan]] follows Lieutenant Glahn's solitary life in the forest, *Victoria* centers on a romantic relationship rendered with a delicacy unusual in [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s early work. The novel belongs to the period between 1897 and 1912, during which [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]] "produced a series of volumes that simply marked a further development of the tendencies shown in his first novels" (P04-015). It sits between the short story collection *Siesta* (1897) and the travel sketches *[[in-wonderland|In Wonderland]]* (1903) in [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s bibliography (P04-015).

The novel appeared during a phase when [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]] was experimenting across genres. The decade that opened with *Victoria* saw Hamsun produce short stories, travel sketches, dramas, and verse — a breadth of output that the preface characterizes as "a further development of the tendencies shown in his first novels" (P04-015). *Victoria* represents the lyrical, romantic strain in Hamsun's writing that would later find echoes in the pastoral mode of [[pan|Pan]] and the Northland novels.

## Relationship to Hunger

*Victoria* stands in marked contrast to *Hunger* in both tone and subject matter. Where *Hunger* is a work of raw psychological intensity — tracking a starving writer's disintegration through the streets of [[christiania|Christiania]] — *Victoria* offers a tender love story. The preface's description of the novel as embodying "the tenderest note in his production" (P04-015) suggests that even Hamsun's earliest readers recognized *Victoria* as a demonstration of the author's emotional range. Published only eight years after *Hunger*, the novel showed that Hamsun was not confined to the fevered, hallucinatory mode of his debut. The two works together established the poles of Hamsun's artistic temperament: the tormented and the tender. This duality — the capacity to render both psychological extremity and delicate emotion — became a hallmark of Hamsun's career, visible in the contrast between later works like the intense *[[mysteries|Mysteries]]* (1892) and the pastoral *[[pan|Pan]]* (1894).
