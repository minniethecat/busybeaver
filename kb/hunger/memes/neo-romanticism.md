Neo-Romanticism (Nyromantik) was a literary and artistic movement that emerged in Scandinavia during the 1890s as a reaction against the social realism and naturalism of the Modern Breakthrough. It emphasized subjectivity, mysticism, nature worship, and the irrational dimensions of human experience.

## Origins and Characteristics

The movement was partly a generational reaction: younger writers found the Breakthrough's demand that literature "put problems under debate" aesthetically limiting and philosophically reductive. Neo-romanticism reclaimed the centrality of individual consciousness, emotional intensity, and the mystery of nature — not as escapism but as a deeper engagement with human reality.

Key characteristics included: focus on psychological interiority rather than social documentation; celebration of nature as a living, mystical presence; interest in the irrational, the ecstatic, and the dreamlike; and a revival of lyrical and symbolic modes of expression.

## Hamsun's Role

Edwin Björkman's introduction to *Hunger* explicitly identifies Hamsun as "one of the chief heralds of the neo-romantic movement then spreading rapidly through the Scandinavian north." The completed *Hunger* (1890) stood at the movement's threshold, and Hamsun's subsequent work — particularly *Mysteries* (1892) and *Pan* (1894) — fully exemplified neo-romantic aesthetics.

Björkman notes that the movement found "typical expressions not only in the works of theretofore unknown writers, but in the changed moods of masters like Ibsen and Bjornson and Strindberg" — suggesting that neo-romanticism was not merely an avant-garde insurgency but a broad transformation of Scandinavian literary culture.
