Scandinavian literature refers to the literary traditions of Norway, Sweden, and Denmark (and, in broader definitions, Iceland, Finland, and the Faroe Islands). In the late 19th century, this relatively small region produced a remarkable concentration of literary innovation that transformed European literature.

## The Scandinavian Golden Age

The period from roughly 1870 to 1920 saw an extraordinary flowering of Scandinavian writing. Henrik Ibsen revolutionized drama; August Strindberg pushed naturalism and expressionism to their limits; Bjørnstjerne Bjørnson became a Nobel laureate and national icon; Selma Lagerlöf became the first woman to win the Nobel Prize in Literature; and Knut Hamsun pioneered the psychological novel that would become literary modernism.

## Björkman's Hierarchical Frame

Edwin Björkman's introduction to *Hunger* provides a revealing map of this literary landscape. He ranks Hamsun above Lagerlöf and Pontoppidan, placing him immediately after the deceased masters Ibsen and Strindberg. This hierarchy reflects the critical consensus of 1921 while also revealing the gendered and national dynamics of Scandinavian literary reputation.

## The Small-Language Problem

Scandinavian literature's international reception has always been mediated by translation. Björkman notes that Hamsun had been "made known" to English-speaking readers "only through the casual publication at long intervals of a few of his books," and hopes for wider availability. This pattern — of major Scandinavian writers reaching world audiences fitfully and late — has been a persistent feature of the region's literary history.
