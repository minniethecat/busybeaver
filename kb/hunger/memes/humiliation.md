## Overview

Humiliation in Knut Hamsun's *Hunger* (1890) is not merely an occasional feeling but a structuring condition of the narrator's existence. His poverty exposes him to constant small degradations — from the pitying glances of strangers to the shame of being unable to pay his rent — and his heightened self-consciousness magnifies each indignity into existential crisis.

## In Hunger

The narrator's susceptibility to humiliation is established early. Walking the streets of Christiania, he feels the judgment of passersby: "I had a feeling that he particularly examined the knees of my trousers, and his shameless effrontery bored me" (001-029). His poverty makes his body a spectacle, transforming everyday urban encounters into trials of self-worth.

The motif of hiding and avoidance recurs throughout the novel as a response to anticipated humiliation. When unable to pay his rent, the narrator slinks past his landlady: "Twice my landlady had asked me with her eyes for payment, and I was obliged to hang my head and slink past her with a shamefaced air" (001-141). The physical gesture — hanging the head, slinking — becomes a recurring bodily expression of his social degradation. Later, he wraps his vest in paper to avoid the shame of carrying it visibly to the pawnshop: "not only would it look better, but I need no longer be ashamed of carrying it" (001-167).

Humiliation is tightly linked to the narrator's sense of professional identity. As a writer — or aspiring writer — he occupies an ambiguous social position, claiming the dignity of intellectual labor while enduring the material conditions of a beggar. The gap between his self-conception and his social reality is the space where humiliation breeds. After being followed by a woman he believes is laughing at him, he feels his annoyance turn to shame: "This forbearance with my annoyance shamed me thoroughly and made me lower my eyes" (001-053) — a pattern where even his attempts at dignity collapse into fresh humiliation.

The narrator's humiliation is rarely passive; it generates elaborate psychological defenses — fantasies of superiority, sudden acts of generosity with his last coins, aggressive outbursts — that only deepen his predicament. Hamsun presents humiliation not as a simple emotion but as a complex engine of the novel's psychological drama.

## Literary Context

Hamsun's treatment of humiliation situates *Hunger* within the tradition of Dostoevsky's *Notes from Underground*, whose anti-hero is similarly driven by the tension between self-loathing and grandiose self-regard. But where Dostoevsky's underground man is paralyzed by excessive consciousness, Hamsun's narrator is propelled into the streets — his humiliation compels movement, action, and the continuous performance of dignity in the face of its absence.
