# The Guard-House

## Overview

The **guard-house** is a municipal overnight jail in [[christiania|Christiania]] where the unnamed narrator of [[knut-hamsun|Knut Hamsun]]'s *Hunger* is detained in Part II. Having no roof over his head and unwilling to return to his rented room, [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] contrives to be arrested — turning the guard-house into a perverse refuge, a space of last resort that is simultaneously punishment and shelter.

## In the Narrative

The guard-house episode begins with a darkly comic premise. The narrator, unable to pay for lodging and unwilling to sleep on the street, decides to get himself arrested. He announces to a policeman that he is a journalist without a fixed address — a calculated confession designed to secure a night's shelter. The policeman is incredulous: "see a journalist in the night guard-house without a roof over his head?" (002-007). The irony is that [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] is exactly what he claims to be — a journalist, a writer, a man of letters — reduced to gaming the penal system for a place to sleep.

Inside the guard-house, [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] occupies a cell described with the precision of sensory deprivation: "a greyish square in the wall, a suggestion of light" (002-032). The cell becomes a space of existential meditation. Deprived of external stimuli, [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] turns inward: "towards the lighter, ever lighter square in the wall, I amused myself" (002-035) — the minimal feature of the cell becoming the entire theater of his [[soul|consciousness]].

The narrator's self-conception within the guard-house is characteristically grandiose and self-mocking. He imagines himself "high above all the prisoners. A homeless Cabinet Minister" (002-034) — a phrase that captures the novel's central paradox: his poverty is absolute yet his sense of distinction is undiminished.

When morning comes, [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] is released: "I stride out of the guard-house" (002-050). The verb "stride" is important — he exits not with the shuffle of a released prisoner but with the gait of a man who has chosen his fate, however absurdly.

Later in Part II, the threat of the guard-house recurs: [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] is warned that he will end up there again "unless I wished to lie in the guard-house again" (002-194). The guard-house has become a reference point in the geography of his degradation — a place one falls into, or chooses, but never entirely escapes.

## Symbolic Significance

The guard-house is the novel's most extreme interior space — a place of pure confinement where [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] has no possessions, no autonomy, no identity beyond what the state assigns him. His strategy of voluntary arrest transforms punishment into refuge, revealing the absurdity of a social order that can provide shelter only as a form of incarceration.

The cell's "greyish square" — a window or vent admitting minimal light — functions as the novel's most abstract image of [[soul|consciousness]] itself. In the darkness, [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s mind generates its own content: fantasies of distinction, self-mocking narratives, the endless internal monologue that constitutes the novel's true subject. The guard-house strips away all external props and leaves only [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s [[soul|consciousness]] — hungry, active, unstoppable.
