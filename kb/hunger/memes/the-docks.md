The docks and harbour area of Kristiania form a significant spatial zone in Knut Hamsun's *Hunger*, particularly in the novel's concluding passages. The waterfront represents the liminal boundary between the city and the wider world — between entrapment and escape.

## The Docks as Liminal Space

Harbour areas in 19th-century literature frequently function as zones of transition and possibility. In *Hunger*, the docks embody the tension between the narrator's rootedness in Kristiania (despite his suffering) and the possibility of departure. The ships that come and go — including the Russian barque *Copegoro* — symbolize alternative futures unavailable to the protagonist.

The docks are also sites of manual labor, where the narrator briefly considers working as a coal-heaver. This association with physical work connects the waterfront to questions of dignity, class, and the narrator's ambivalent relationship to his own body.

## The Novel's Conclusion

At the end of *Hunger*, the narrator leaves Kristiania by ship from the docks, accepting a position aboard a vessel bound for England. This departure — an escape from the city that has "marked" him — represents neither triumph nor defeat but a kind of exhausted release. The docks thus frame the novel's narrative arc: from arrival and immersion in the city to departure and uncertain futures.
