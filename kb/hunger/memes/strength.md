## Overview

Strength in Knut Hamsun's *Hunger* operates as a finite, depletable resource that the narrator measures, marshals, and mourns. Both physical stamina and force of will are subject to the same inexorable decline as his body wastes from starvation, yet he repeatedly summons reserves of strength through sheer determination, treating the will as a muscle that can override physical limitation.

## In Hunger

The narrator's display of strength is performative from the outset. Waiting to enlist in the Fire Brigade, he describes men "thrusting our chests out to give an idea of strength and bravery" (001-006), establishing strength as something projected socially as much as possessed. He himself is rejected on account of his eyesight, but the phrase "give an idea of" is telling: even before hunger takes hold, strength is a performance.

Walking the morning streets, the narrator asserts: "I was strong as a giant; could stop a dray with my shoulders" (001-009). This declaration, arriving immediately after the Fire Brigade rejection, reads as compensatory fantasy — the narrator invents a hyperbolic physical self to counter the one the inspector dismissed. Shortly afterward, reality intrudes: he watches an old lame man who "exerted his whole body, using all his strength in his endeavours to get along speedily" (001-013), and the old man's struggle prefigures his own.

At moments of crisis, the narrator experiences surges of "brutal strength" (001-262) — an aggressive, almost animal vitality that erupts paradoxically from his weakened state. After the police cell episode, fury "welled up in me, blazing with brutal strength. I fetched my parcel from the entry, set my teeth together, jostled against the peaceful folk on the footpath, and never once asked their pardon" (001-262). Yet depletion follows swiftly: "I had not strength enough to go" (001-297). The oscillation between sudden power and collapse is one of the novel's defining rhythms.

Willpower becomes the narrator's substitute for physical stamina. At a critical moment, he resolves: "Lie in that cell again I would not" (002-195), and the text reports simply: "This gave me strength." The determination itself becomes an energy source. Similarly, he "gulped it down by sheer strength of will" (003-084), forcing his body to comply through mental force when physical ability fails. In his most desperate moments, he attempts "to try and regain my strength: I clenched my teeth, wrinkled my brow" (004-103), treating the body as something that can be commanded back to vigor through grim determination and physical tension.

## Physical vs. Mental Strength

*Hunger* draws a sharp and continually tested distinction between bodily capacity and volitional force. Physical strength is measurable, depletable, and governed by the simple arithmetic of caloric intake — the narrator's body weakens inexorably as his fast continues, and he reports its failures with clinical precision. Mental strength, by contrast, operates on an economy of pride and self-assertion: it can be summoned by an act of will, sustained by humiliation avoided, and amplified by the imperative not to submit.

The tension between these two registers becomes most acute in moments where the narrator must perform tasks his body cannot accomplish.

> I used my last strength to tidy up my bed a little (003-065).

This small act of domestic order — smoothing a bed — requires the narrator's *last* strength, yet he spends it on dignity rather than survival. It is a transaction that illustrates the priority system of the narrator's psychology: appearances and self-respect consume resources that physical survival could claim.

The narrator's discovery that "sheer strength of will" (003-084) can override bodily refusal is both his greatest resource and his most dangerous delusion. It enables him to continue when a purely physical creature would collapse, but it also prevents him from accurately assessing his own condition. The will, in Hamsun's rendering, becomes a kind of counterfeit currency — it purchases continued consciousness, but does not forestall the body's ultimate claim.

## Performative Strength

Strength in *Hunger* is never a private possession; it is always staged for an audience, real or imagined. The narrator's obsessive awareness of how he appears to others means that strength is something he must *demonstrate* — to passersby, to editors, to policemen — even when he possesses none.

This performative dimension is signaled early in the novel, when the narrator observes men who puff "their chests out to give an idea of strength and bravery" (001-006). The phrase "give an idea" is crucial: strength is a representation, a signal broadcast for social consumption. The narrator's own exertions follow the same logic. When he "exerted his whole body, using all his strength in his endeavours to get along" (001-013), the act of walking down a street becomes a theatrical performance of normalcy — an expenditure of energy whose purpose is to conceal the expenditure of energy.

The tragedy of performative strength is that it consumes the very resource it displays. Every act of appearing strong diminishes the actual strength available for survival. This paradox lies at the heart of the narrator's predicament and gives *Hunger* its distinctive quality of exhaustion: the protagonist is not merely starving, but starving *while acting fed*.

## Strength and Honour

The narrator's sense of honour is consistently described in the language of physical strength. He reflects: "It is simply my sense of honour that keeps me afloat now. But it is strong enough too; at least, it has kept me up to date" (004-022). Here the moral and the physical converge — honour is a "strong" force, one that "keeps him afloat" as if he were swimming. This metaphor is not incidental. Throughout the novel, the narrator treats his ethical self as a body that must be exercised and maintained, subject to the same laws of fatigue and recovery as his physical frame.

When he resists the "strong temptation" (001-239) to pawn Hans Pauli's blanket dishonestly, the victory produces not relief but exaltation: "The consciousness that I was yet pure and honourable rose to my head, filled me with a splendid sense of having principle, character, of being a shining white beacon in a muddy, human sea amidst floating wreck" (001-239). Moral strength, unlike physical strength, actually increases through expenditure — each resisted temptation strengthens the will for the next. This asymmetry between the physical (depleted by use) and the moral (fortified by use) is one of the novel's most subtle psychological observations.
