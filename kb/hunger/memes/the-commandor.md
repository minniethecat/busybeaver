"The Commandor" is the narrator's respectful nickname for a newspaper editor in Christiania who plays a significant role in Parts III and IV of *Hunger*. Unlike the earlier editor, this man treats the narrator with notable kindness and generosity, representing one of the few figures of genuine benevolence in the novel.

## In the Narrative

The narrator first visits the Commandor's office and is asked to sit down while the editor finishes writing. The office is described with fine detail — "busts, prints, cuttings, and an enormous paper-basket, that looked as if it might swallow a man, bones and all" [003-098]. The narrator feels sad at the sight of this "monstrous chasm, this dragon's mouth, that always stood open, always ready to receive rejected work, newly crushed hopes" [003-098].

When the Commandor greets him, he makes "a half-serious, half-playful motion with his hand" and points to a chair [003-098]. The narrator leaves resolved not to return until he has something that would "astonish the 'Commandor' a bit, and make him order me to be paid half-a-sovereign without a moment's hesitation" [003-116].

In a later encounter on the street, the Commandor notices the narrator's distress, slackens his pace, and kindly asks "Have you anything to live on in the meantime?" [003-107]. He then gives the narrator money without requiring any accounting — "It isn't worth while feeling put out about it. I know you can write for it" [003-114]. The narrator is "dumb with joy" and cannot even bring himself to thank him properly [003-113].

The Commandor's perceptiveness is itself a form of kindness. As the narrator notes: "Perhaps the 'Commandor' guesses that something is amiss. I try to regain my composure. The 'Commandor' tweaks his nose and looks at me" [003-103–003-106]. He "guesses" rather than demands explanation, allowing the narrator to preserve the fiction of professional normalcy that is essential to his self-respect.

## An Economy of Dignity

The Commandor's gift of money operates within the novel's complex moral economy of giving and receiving. Unlike the pawnbroker's transactional exchanges or the landlady's contractual demands, the Commandor's payment carries no explicit obligation. He gives the narrator money not for a specific article but on the promise of future work — an act of credit that is simultaneously financial and personal.

This distinction matters profoundly to the narrator, whose [[pride]] makes every other form of assistance unbearable. The Commandor navigates this sensitivity with unusual skill. His half-serious, half-playful gesture [003-098] defuses the solemnity that would make charity humiliating. His casual inquiry — "Have you anything to live on in the meantime?" [003-107] — frames the gift as a practical question rather than a moral judgment. And his final reassurance — "I know you can write for it" [003-114] — converts the transaction from alms into an advance, restoring the narrator to the status of a professional rather than a beggar.

## Contrast with Other Authority Figures

The Commandor's treatment of the narrator gains its full significance when set against the novel's other figures of institutional power. The first newspaper editor the narrator encounters (in Parts I–II) is a study in impersonality: he accepts a manuscript without engagement, his communication mediated through "Scissors" the clerk, his approval conveyed indirectly through a letter that reaches the narrator long after the encounter. This editor represents the cold machinery of the literary marketplace — a system that processes manuscripts without processing the human being who wrote them.

The policeman offers another contrast. Where the Commandor extends protection by giving money, the policeman extends protection by threatening imprisonment — a form of care that the narrator experiences as violation. The Commandor's gift preserves freedom; the policeman's intervention restricts it.

Even [[ylajali]]'s relationship with the narrator, for all its emotional intensity, is marked by a fundamental asymmetry — she observes him, fascinates him, but ultimately remains inaccessible. The Commandor, by contrast, offers something simpler and perhaps more valuable within the novel's value system: a moment of uncomplicated recognition. He sees the narrator as a writer who can "write for it" [003-114], affirming an identity the narrator has struggled to maintain through every indignity of starvation.
