# Despair

## Overview

**Despair** is one of the most intense emotional states depicted in [[knut-hamsun|Knut Hamsun]]'s *Hunger* (1890). Unlike [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s pride or shame, which have specific social triggers, despair is a more diffuse and existential condition — a response not merely to hunger but to the apparent meaninglessness of his suffering. It appears throughout the novel in sudden, overwhelming waves, often followed by equally sudden reversals into creative mania or defiant self-assertion.

## In the Narrative

Despair in *Hunger* is intimately connected to [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s creative struggles. He describes "the despairing efforts to find a word" (002-032), linking despair to the writer's fundamental dilemma: the gap between vision and expression. This creative despair is not merely frustration but an existential crisis — a doubt about whether his suffering has any purpose or whether his artistic aspirations are delusional.

The narrator's despair also has a spatial dimension. Crying out "with despair because this little street" (002-110) was so beautiful, he experiences the cruel contrast between aesthetic sensitivity and physical degradation. The world's beauty becomes a source of pain rather than comfort, reminding him of everything his poverty excludes him from.

In his "restless brain," [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] has "in despair often chosen the most remote themes" (001-007), suggesting that despair drives the very extremity of his creative [[dreams|imagination]]. His intellectual ambitions are themselves a flight from — and a product of — his desperate circumstances.

Part III depicts despair in its most dramatic form. The narrator describes a woman who "made a despairing struggle" (003-206), projecting his own emotional state onto those he observes. By Part IV, despair has become almost mechanical: during "the despairing moment when my [[the-landlady|landlady]]" (004-064) confronts him, the emotion is both acute and familiar, a recurring feature of his existence.

The preface places this despair in a broader context: [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s Northland is a [[nature|landscape]] of "abysmal despairs" (P04-006), suggesting that [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s psychological states are rooted in a geographical and cultural sensibility — the extremes of light and darkness, summer and winter, that characterize northern Norway.

## Literary Context

[[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s depiction of despair anticipates the existentialist themes that would dominate twentieth-century literature. Unlike the moralized despair of earlier Christian writers, [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s despair has no theological framework — it is purely immanent, a function of the individual's relationship to his circumstances and his own [[soul|consciousness]]. The oscillation between despair and ecstatic creativity prefigures the absurdist sensibility of Camus and the psychological explorations of Dostoyevsky, whom the preface identifies as a key influence.
