# University Street

## Overview

University Street (Norwegian: Universitetsgaten) is a street in [[christiania|Christiania]] that serves as a recurring pathway in the narrator's urban geography — most significantly as the route along which he escorts [[ylajali|Ylajali]] through the city, a walk charged with longing, embarrassment, and the fragile possibility of connection.

## In the Narrative

University Street first appears in Part I as part of the narrator's tracking of two women through the city. After they stop at Cisler's music-shop on Carl Johann Street, the pair "came back, went the same road as they had come, passed me again, and turned the corner of University Street and up towards St. Olav's place" (001-019). The narrator, shamefully following them, notes their forbearance with his intrusion — a moment of social awkwardness that establishes University Street as a boundary between the public promenade of Carl Johann and the more intimate terrain of St. Olav's Place.

In Part II, the street marks the narrator's exclusion from fashionable life: "Bowing and laughing folk walked up and down Carl Johann Street. I stuck my elbows closely to my sides, tried to make myself look small, and slipped unperceived past some acquaintances who had taken up their stand at the corner of University Street to gaze at the passers-by" (002-074). The corner of University Street becomes a vantage point for the city's spectators — the confident, the well-fed, the "happy souls" from whom the narrator must hide.

The street's most significant appearance comes in Part III, during the narrator's evening walk with Ylajali. She leads him through the city, and "we turned up University Street, and could already see the lights in St. Olav's Place" (003-132). When they reach Carl Johann, she decides to turn back, and "we returned through University Street" (003-133). The street becomes the physical path of their brief, charged intimacy — a corridor between the commercial glow of Carl Johann and the private darkness of St. Olav's Place where their encounter began and will end.

In Part IV, the narrator keeps "my eyes on University Street" as he waits for Ylajali near the appointed hour, "tremulous, delicious dread" filling him as the clocks strike eight (004-028). The street is the axis of his anticipation — the line along which she will either appear or not appear.

## Symbolic Significance

University Street functions as a corridor of transition — between public and private, between hope and disappointment, between the narrator's fantasies of connection and the reality of his isolation. Its name carries an ironic charge: the narrator, a writer and intellectual, has no institutional affiliation with any university; the street that bears the name of learning leads him not to knowledge but to the painful education of hunger and rejection.
