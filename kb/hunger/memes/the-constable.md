The Constable appears repeatedly throughout "Hunger" as an embodiment of the city's impersonal authority. Unlike the narrator's other encounters with officialdom, his interactions with the constable are marked by a strange blend of fear, defiance, and dark comedy.

The narrator's first significant encounter with a constable occurs at the watch-house. Standing before the building, he contemplates slipping into a passage unnoticed. When he ascends the steps to speak to the watchman, "the uplifted ax, with its edge turned against me, darts like a cold slash through my nerves" (002-023). The armed authority figure reduces the narrator to mute terror, and he slinks away feeling as though he has escaped a great peril.

Later, unable to enter his locked lodging, the narrator seeks help at the Town Hall. A constable suggests he report himself as homeless — an idea the narrator embraces with desperate enthusiasm (002-038). At the police station, he finds himself among thirty or forty other homeless people, all waiting for the registering clerk to issue breakfast tickets.

The constable also functions as a disruptive force in the narrator's creative efforts. While the narrator stands beneath a street lamp, laboring to complete his article for the newspaper, a constable plants himself nearby, spoiling the narrator's concentration entirely (002-114). When the narrator later acts erratically in the street, another constable orders him to move on with the curt command: "No disturbance in the street; so, march" (002-114).

## Authority and the City

The constable in *Hunger* is less a character than a function of urban space. Christiania's streets are policed by figures who are interchangeable — the narrator never distinguishes one constable from another, and their commands follow an identical formula. "No disturbance in the street; so, march" (002-114) is the constable's refrain, reducing the complexities of the narrator's interior crisis to a simple spatial infraction.

This interchangeability is thematically significant. In a novel otherwise saturated with subjective consciousness, the constable represents the external world's refusal to acknowledge the narrator's inner turmoil. The constable sees only a vagrant causing a disturbance, never a man in the grip of existential desperation. Each encounter reinforces the narrator's isolation: authority in *Hunger* is not tyrannical but indifferent, more frightening in its blankness than any active persecution could be.

## The Constable as Narrative Device

Structurally, the constable encounters function as punctuation marks in the novel's episodic narrative. They occur at moments when the narrator's physical or psychological state is most precarious — locked out of his lodging, attempting to write, acting erratically in public — and each encounter forces a confrontation between the narrator's inner world and the outer world's demand for orderly behavior. The constable does not advance the plot so much as he measures the widening gap between the narrator's subjective reality and the social reality of Christiania.
