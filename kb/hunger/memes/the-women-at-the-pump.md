## Overview

*The Women at the Pump* (*Konerne ved Vandposten*) is a novel by Knut Hamsun published in 1920. Set in a small Norwegian coastal town, it is a darkly comic portrait of gossip, petty intrigue, and the hidden lives behind respectable facades.

## Description

The novel centers on the town pump, where the local women gather to draw water and exchange news. Through their conversations, Hamsun exposes the secret affairs, financial schemes, and private tragedies of the community. The narrative technique — fragmented, polyphonic, and oblique — allows the reader to piece together events that the characters themselves only partially understand. Oliver Andersen, a disabled former sailor, and his family serve as a focal point for the town's collective gaze and judgment.

## Relationship to Hunger

*The Women at the Pump* extends Hamsun's interest in marginalized figures and the psychology of communities. While *Hunger* is an intense study of a single consciousness in isolation, *The Women at the Pump* inverts this perspective: it examines how a community constructs its shared understanding of individuals through fragmentary observation and gossip. Both novels share a concern with the gap between inner experience and external perception.
