The Pawnbroker operates a shop on a second floor on Pyle Street in Christiania. He is one of the narrator's most frequent points of contact in the commercial underworld of the city, serving as a reluctant creditor throughout the protagonist's descent into poverty.

The narrator first visits this pawnbroker, "one to whom I had never been before," bringing his waistcoat as collateral [001-024]. The transaction foreshadows the series of increasingly desperate exchanges that define the narrator's relationship with the city's economic margins. When the narrator later needs his pencil to continue writing, he must return to retrieve it from the pawnbroker's shop [001-040].

The pawnbroker's most memorable interaction with the narrator occurs during a later visit. When the narrator, in a state of desperate excitement, attempts an unusual transaction, "the old pawnbroker burst out laughing, and returned to his desk without saying a word" [002-199]. This laughter functions as a kind of verdict on the narrator's condition — a wordless acknowledgment of how far he has fallen that cuts deeper than any verbal refusal could.

Despite the narrator's poverty and erratic behavior, the pawnbroker maintains a degree of professional civility. On an earlier occasion, after the narrator retrieved his pencil, "the honest pawnbroker bowed twice profoundly" as the narrator departed [001-062]. The narrator, in turn, imagines himself in a position of power over the pawnbroker, capable of granting him a position — a fantasy that reveals the narrator's desperate need to invert his actual powerlessness.
