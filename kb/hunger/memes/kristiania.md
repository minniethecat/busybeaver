Kristiania (also spelled Christiania) was the name of Norway's capital from 1624 to 1925, when it was renamed Oslo. The city serves as the primary setting for Knut Hamsun's novel *Hunger* (1890), and its streets, parks, and social geography are integral to the narrative's texture.

## Historical Context

Founded around 1040, the city was originally called Oslo. After a fire in 1624, King Christian IV of Denmark-Norway rebuilt the city and renamed it Christiania in his own honor. From 1877, the official spelling shifted to "Kristiania." The name reverted to Oslo in 1925, a decision driven by Norwegian nationalist sentiment.

## Kristiania in the 1880s–1890s

During the period depicted in *Hunger*, Kristiania was a rapidly modernizing city of approximately 150,000 inhabitants. The late 19th century brought industrialization, class stratification, and a growing literary and artistic bohemia. The city's geography — from the impoverished Vaterland district to the more prosperous Karl Johans Gate — maps the narrator's social descent.

## In Hunger

Hamsun's novel opens with the famous line: "It was during the time I wandered about and starved in Christiania: Christiania, this singular city, from which no man departs without carrying away the traces of his sojourn there." This characterization establishes the city as more than background — it is an active presence that marks its inhabitants permanently.

The narrator's wanderings trace a psychogeography of the city: the attic room with *Morgenbladet* wallpaper, the cemetery where he seeks refuge, the docks, the prison where he spends a night, the various streets and squares where he encounters editors, pawnbrokers, and strangers. Kristiania's physical space becomes an externalization of the narrator's psychological states.
