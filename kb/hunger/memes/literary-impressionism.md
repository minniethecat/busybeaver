Literary Impressionism is a mode of writing that prioritizes the rendering of sensory experience, subjective perception, and momentary impressions over objective narration and linear plot development. Emerging in the late 19th century alongside Impressionism in painting, it represented a fundamental shift in how literature could represent reality.

## Characteristics

Literary Impressionism is characterized by: fragmented rather than continuous narration; emphasis on sensory detail (light, color, sound, smell) over abstract analysis; narration filtered through a single, often unstable consciousness; temporal discontinuity that mirrors psychological rather than chronological time; and a preference for showing consciousness in process rather than reporting completed thoughts.

## Hamsun's Impressionism

Edwin Björkman's introduction to *Hunger* makes explicit the connection between Hamsun's literary method and visual Impressionism: Hamsun's prose was "life, but presented in the Impressionistic temper of a Gauguin or Cezanne." This was "a new kind of realism that had nothing to do with photographic reproduction of details" but instead captured psychological truth through intensified subjective rendering.

The comparison to Post-Impressionism (Gauguin, Cézanne) rather than Impressionism proper (Monet, Renoir) is significant: Hamsun's technique goes beyond recording sensory surfaces to distort and intensify them in the service of psychological expression. The narrator's consciousness in *Hunger* does not passively receive impressions but actively transforms them through the lens of hunger, pride, and creative mania.
