# The Dampkökken

The Dampkökken (literally "Steam Kitchen") was a famous cheap eating-house in Christiania, providing hot meals to working people at low prices. It appears in three passages of the novel as a symbol of ordinary sustenance from which the narrator is excluded.[001-570]

## The Bill of Fare

At three o'clock, as hunger assaults him in earnest, the narrator makes a point of swinging by the Dampkökken and reading the bill of fare posted outside. He shrugs his shoulders theatrically, "as if corned beef or salt port was not meet food for me" — a performance of indifference that masks his desperate inability to afford even the cheapest meal.[001-570]

> It was three o'clock. Hunger began to assail me downright in earnest. I was faint, and now and again I had to retch furtively. I swung round by the Dampkökken, read the bill of fare, and shrugged my shoulders in a way to attract attention, as if corned beef or salt port was not meet food for me.[001-570]

## A Measure of Wealth

The Dampkökken becomes a metaphorical unit of value in the novel. When the narrator is given five shillings by the "Commandor," he reflects with ironic relief that he might just as well have been asked for "five shares in the Dampkökken, or an estate out in Aker" — placing the eating-house alongside real estate as a measure of the unattainable.[002-097]

## Working-Class Sustenance

The Dampkökken serves the working poor of Christiania. The narrator passes joiners sitting with their food cans between their knees, "eating their good warm dinner from the Dampkökken," while he himself trudges on toward Bernt Akers Street, "half dead with fatigue." The image of laborers enjoying hot meals that the narrator cannot afford intensifies his physical and social isolation.[002-1000]

> I trudge up town again, along the same road, pass by the joiners — who are sitting with their cans between their knees, eating their good warm dinner from the Dampkökken — pass the bakers, where the loaf is still in its place, and at length reach Bernt Akers Street, half dead with fatigue.[002-1000]

The Dampkökken is described in the novel's notes as a "steam cooking-kitchen and famous cheap eating-house," a real Christiania institution that served as a precursor to the modern cafeteria.[004-158]
