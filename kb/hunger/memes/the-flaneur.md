The flâneur — the urban stroller who observes the city with leisured detachment — was theorized by Charles Baudelaire in the 1860s and later analyzed by Walter Benjamin in his studies of 19th-century Paris. The flâneur is at home in the crowd, an anonymous observer who derives aesthetic pleasure from the spectacle of urban life.

## The Hunger Narrator as Anti-Flâneur

The narrator of Knut Hamsun's *Hunger* shares the flâneur's characteristic activity — walking the city, observing its inhabitants — but from a radically different position. Where the classic flâneur is a gentleman of leisure, the Hunger narrator walks because he has nowhere to go; where the flâneur observes from a position of detachment, the narrator observes from a condition of desperate need.

This inversion of the flâneur figure is characteristically modernist. The leisured observer of 19th-century Paris becomes the hungry, anxious wanderer of fin-de-siècle Kristiania. The flâneur's aesthetic pleasure in urban spectacle is replaced by the narrator's existential distress — the city is not a source of delight but a theater of humiliation.
