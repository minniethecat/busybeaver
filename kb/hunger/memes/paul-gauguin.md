Paul Gauguin (1848–1903) was a French Post-Impressionist painter known for his bold use of color, synthetist style, and depictions of Tahitian life. His work moved beyond Impressionism toward Symbolism and primitivism.

## Gauguin as Hamsun's Artistic Analogue

In his introduction to *Hunger*, Edwin Björkman makes a striking cross-media comparison: Hamsun's prose in *Hunger* was "life, but presented in the Impressionistic temper of a Gauguin or Cezanne." This comparison is illuminating: Gauguin rejected photographic realism in favor of subjective color and simplified forms, just as Hamsun rejected realist conventions in favor of a feverish, subjective narrative consciousness.

The comparison captures the modernist break from 19th-century realism in both literature and painting. Björkman positions *Hunger* as a literary parallel to the revolution in visual art represented by Post-Impressionism — not documented surface reality but psychological and emotional truth rendered through distortion and intensification.
