# Tomtegaden

## Overview

**Tomtegaden** is a street in [[christiania|Christiania]] where [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] of [[knut-hamsun|Knut Hamsun]]'s *Hunger* finds lodgings during Part III. The street appears at a transitional moment in the narrative — after the eviction from the first room and the attic episode, Tomtegaden offers [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] a temporary base from which he continues his wandering, his writing, and his surveillance of [[ylajali|Ylajali]]. The street's name enters the novel as a cab driver's destination: "Drive to Tomtegaden, No. 11" (003-026).

## In the Narrative

Tomtegaden appears first as an address spoken in a moment of disorientation. The narrator, having come into a small sum of money, hires a cab and gives the driver the address: "No. Drive to Tomtegaden, No. 11." He is "in a state of" (003-026) — the sentence breaks off, leaving his emotional condition suspended between elation and collapse. The cab ride itself is rendered with [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s characteristic blend of external observation and internal chaos.

The arrival at Tomtegaden is a moment of grounding: "At length we reach Tomtegaden. The driver pulls up" (003-040). The street represents stability after movement, a fixed point in [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s urban drift. The building at No. 11 bears the sign "Refreshment and lodgings for travellers" (003-042) — a commercial promise of rest and sustenance that [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]], perpetually hungry and homeless, cannot fully access.

The narrator lives in Tomtegaden through much of Part III. "At noon I left my lodgings in Tomtegaden, where I still lived and found fairly comfortable quarters" (003-120) — the phrase "fairly comfortable" is a relative judgment, measured against the room with old newspaper walls and the five-storeyed attic. Tomtegaden represents a midpoint on the scale of [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s accommodations: not the worst, not permanent, merely adequate for the present.

In Part IV, [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s path crosses Tomtegaden once more: "When I got to the corner of Tomtegaden and the railway place, the street was all a-swelter" (004-103). The railway place intersection marks Tomtegaden's proximity to the city's transport infrastructure — a borderland between residential stability and the impulse to depart.

## Symbolic Significance

Tomtegaden represents [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s condition of temporary stability — the street where he pauses between crises, never quite settling. Its name, "Tomte" suggesting a plot or lot of land, carries connotations of tenancy and impermanence: a place one occupies but does not own. The narrator's rooms on Tomtegaden are "fairly comfortable" but never home; he is always already moving toward the next departure.

The street's association with the cab ride is significant. The narrator arrives at Tomtegaden not by walking — his usual mode — but by being driven, a passenger in his own life. This passivity mirrors his broader condition throughout the novel: he is moved by forces he does not control, deposited at addresses he has not chosen.
