# Christy the Grocer

## Overview

Christy the Grocer is a minor, offstage character in [[knut-hamsun|Knut Hamsun]]'s *[[hunger|Hunger]]* — the proprietor of a shop at No. 13 [[groenlandsleret|Groenlandsleret]] in [[christiania|Christiania]] who advertised for a man to do "a couple of hours' book-keeping" each week. The narrator applies for the position in writing but never presents himself in person, and Christy never appears directly in the narrative. He exists only as a name on an envelope and an opportunity the narrator cannot bring himself to pursue.

## In the Narrative

Christy enters the story in Part II when the narrator, in his habitual scan of newspaper placards for situations vacant, finds the following:

> A grocer in Groenlandsleret wanted a man every week for a couple of hours' book-keeping; remuneration according to agreement. I noted my man's address, and prayed to God in silence for this place. I would demand less than any one else for my work; sixpence was ample, or perhaps fivepence. (002-076)

The narrator writes an application to "Christy the grocer, No. 13 Groenlandsleret" (002-077) and posts it. But he never follows up. Later, during a moment of renewed desperation, he rationalizes his inaction:

> There would surely be some way or another of getting help when the right time came! Now, there was the grocer in Groenlandsleret. Had I importuned him every hour in the day since I sent in my application? Had I rung the bell early and late, and been turned away? Why, I had not even applied personally to him or sought an answer! It did not follow, surely, that it must needs be an absolutely vain attempt. (002-155)

This rationalization is characteristic of [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s psychology: he transforms his failure to act into a preserved possibility, a door he has deliberately left unknocked rather than one that might be closed. The grocer becomes a symbol of every opportunity the narrator's pride and passivity prevent him from seizing.

## Psychological Significance

Christy the Grocer represents the narrator's paradoxical relationship to ordinary employment. The position — book-keeping for a few hours a week — is modest, eminently suitable for a writer who needs only enough to sustain himself between creative bursts. Yet the narrator never applies in person. The pattern recurs throughout the novel: he applies for a wood-chopping position in [[moller-street|Möllergaden]] (002-107) but similarly fails to follow through. The grocer is thus one instance of a larger pattern — the narrator's inability to cross the threshold between his fantasies of self-sufficiency and the mundane reality of earning a living. Each unfollowed application preserves the possibility that he *could* have succeeded, had he tried — a comfort his pride requires.
