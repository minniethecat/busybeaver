# The Artist

## Overview

The figure of **the artist** is central to [[knut-hamsun|Knut Hamsun]]'s *Hunger* (1890), not merely as a biographical fact ([[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] is a writer) but as a conceptual category — a way of understanding [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s psychology, his social position, and his relationship to suffering. The preface describes [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s sense that "the artist and [[the-unnamed-narrator|the vagabond]] seem equally to have been in the blood" (P04-007), linking artistic identity to rootlessness and marginality.

## In the Narrative

The narrator's identity as an artist is both a source of pride and a burden. He defines himself by his writing, yet his writing brings him no income and little recognition. He clings to his artistic identity as the one thing that distinguishes him from the merely poor — he is not a beggar but an artist temporarily without means. This distinction, however fragile, is what allows him to maintain his sense of self.

The preface's description of [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s family background adds a deterministic dimension: "artistic preoccupations" that "turned one or two of them into skilled craftsmen" (P04-004) suggest that artistry is inherited, a matter of blood rather than choice. The narrator is an artist not because he has chosen to be one but because he cannot be anything else.

In Part I, [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] seeks out a "young artist I knew, a stripling I had once" (001-221) helped, attempting to find community among fellow artists. This gesture toward artistic solidarity is characteristic — [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] imagines artists as a tribe, bound by shared sensibility rather than shared circumstance.

## Literary Context

[[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s portrayal of the artist anticipates the twentieth-century figure of the "starving artist" — the creative genius whose suffering is both the price and the proof of authenticity. Unlike the bohemian artists of nineteenth-century Paris (depicted by Murger and Puccini), [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s artist is not romanticized. His suffering is not picturesque but degrading; his art is not celebrated but barely noticed. This unflinching realism about artistic poverty was new in 1890 and remains powerful today.
