# The Wild Choir

## Overview

**The Wild Choir** (*Det vilde Kor*) is a collection of poems by [[knut-hamsun|Knut Hamsun]], published in 1904. The preface to *Hunger* lists it among [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s works in a dense catalogue of his output: "The Wild Choir, a collection of poems, 1904" (P04-015). It represents [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s only published volume of verse — a brief foray into poetry from a writer whose reputation rests on his novels and, to a lesser extent, his plays.

## Description

*The Wild Choir* appeared in a period of intense productivity for [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]], sandwiched between the short story collection *Kratskog* (1903) and the novel *[[dreamers|Dreamers]]* (1904). The volume suggests [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s restlessness with genre boundaries — a writer who moved between forms, testing the capacities of each. The title evokes a wild, untamed chorus — an image that resonates with [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s characteristic themes of nature, instinct, and the defiance of convention.

The poems in *The Wild Choir* have received relatively little critical attention compared to [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s narrative works, and the volume remains one of the lesser-known items in his bibliography. Its inclusion in the preface's catalogue, however, attests to its place in the arc of [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s career — one stop in a journey that moved restlessly between forms and genres.

## Relationship to Hunger

*The Wild Choir* has no direct connection to the content of *Hunger*, but it illuminates [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s broader literary identity. The fact that the author of *Hunger* — a novel of urban starvation and psychological intensity — also wrote a volume of lyric poetry suggests the range of his literary ambition. The poetry represents a different register of [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s sensibility: not the [[fever|feverish]] interiority of the hungry narrator but the lyrical evocation of nature and instinct suggested by the collection's title.

## Critical Reception

As [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s sole published volume of poetry, *The Wild Choir* occupies a unique place in his bibliography. It has been studied primarily as a footnote to his novels — a detour that confirms rather than challenges the dominance of prose in his career. The collection's title and themes, however, connect it to the nature-worship and primitivism that characterize [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s later epic fiction, particularly *[[the-growth-of-the-soil|The Growth of the Soil]]*.
