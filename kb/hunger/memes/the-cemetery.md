The cemetery in Knut Hamsun's *Hunger* serves as a recurring locus of refuge, contemplation, and symbolic significance. It is almost certainly based on Vår Frelsers gravlund (Our Savior's Cemetery) in Kristiania, established in 1808 and the burial place of notable Norwegian figures.

## Function in the Novel

The cemetery provides the homeless, hungry narrator with a paradoxical sanctuary. It is a public space that offers privacy, a place of death that affirms life, and a site of social respectability that accommodates the outcast. The narrator retreats to the cemetery when his physical and psychological distress becomes unbearable.

The cemetery scenes are among the most atmospheric in the novel, blending Gothic and modernist elements. The narrator's meditations among the graves connect his personal suffering to universal themes of mortality, memory, and the relationship between the living and the dead.

## Symbolic Dimension

The cemetery functions symbolically on multiple levels: as a memento mori echoing the narrator's physical deterioration, as a space of social inversion (the outcast among the respectable dead), and as a liminal zone where the boundaries between self and world, sanity and madness, become permeable.

The narrator visits the cemetery at multiple points throughout Parts I–IV.
