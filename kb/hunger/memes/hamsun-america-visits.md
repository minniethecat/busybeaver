Knut Hamsun visited the United States twice in the 1880s, experiences that profoundly influenced his development as a writer and his critical perspective on modernity.

## First Visit (1882–1884)

Hamsun's first American sojourn took him to the Midwestern plains, where he worked as a farmhand in North Dakota and in various manual labor jobs. This immersion in the raw, sprawling agricultural frontier gave him firsthand experience of American life that would later inform his critique of American civilization.

## Second Visit (1886–1888)

His second visit brought him to Chicago, where he worked as a streetcar conductor and observed urban American life. The experience was disillusioning; Hamsun found American culture materialistic and spiritually impoverished. Upon returning to Norway in 1888, he published *The Spiritual Life of Modern America* (*Fra det moderne Amerikas Aandsliv*, 1889), a scathing critique that a Norwegian critic later called "a masterpiece of distorted criticism."

## Literary Consequences

As Edwin Björkman notes in his introduction to *Hunger*, Hamsun "failed utterly to establish any sympathetic contact between himself and the new world" during his American years. This failure proved generative: the experience of alienation in America prefigured the psychological themes of *Hunger*. Hamsun's return to Norway in 1888 coincided with his literary breakthrough — the first sketch of *Hunger* appeared that year in the Danish periodical *New Earth*.

Hamsun later distanced himself from his anti-American book, inscribing a copy in 1903: "A youthful work. It has ceased to represent my opinion of America."
