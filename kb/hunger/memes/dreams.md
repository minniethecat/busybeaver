# Dreams

## Overview

**Dreams** in [[knut-hamsun|Knut Hamsun]]'s *Hunger* (1890) encompass both [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s literal fever-dreams and his waking fantasies of escape, recognition, and [[grace|redemption]]. [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]] uses dreams to blur the boundary between inner and outer reality, making [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s psychological states the true subject of the novel. The preface speaks of "the ecstatic dreams of [[a-child|the little boy]] from the south" (P04-012), linking dreams to both creative vision and geographical [[memory|nostalgia]].

## In the Narrative

Dreams in the novel are inseparable from [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s physical condition. He falls asleep "with my brain full of fever and dreams" (001-196), the two states merging into a single altered [[soul|consciousness]]. [[fever|Fever]] produces dreams, and dreams intensify fever — a vicious circle that mirrors [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s broader predicament.

The narrator is aware of the danger that dreams represent. He dismisses his visions as "senseless dreams" (001-215), recognizing that they are symptoms of his deterioration rather than genuine inspiration. Yet he cannot escape them — the dreams are not voluntary but imposed by his starving brain.

Beyond literal dreams, [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s waking life is dominated by fantasies. He imagines encounters, conversations, and triumphs — elaborate daydreams that provide temporary relief from his circumstances. These waking dreams serve a double function: they sustain his sense of himself as special, but they also prevent him from taking the practical actions that might actually improve his condition.

The preface frames dreams as central to [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s artistic vision. The "ecstatic dreams of [[a-child|the little boy]]" (P04-012) are the source of *[[pan|Pan]]*, [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s lyrical novel of the Northland, and the trilogy hero "dreams of the long peace to come" (P04-014). Dreams are thus both a theme within the fiction and a description of the creative process itself.

## Literary Context

The prominence of dreams in *Hunger* places [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]] at the threshold of modernism. Where earlier realist writers might use dreams as plot devices or moral allegories, [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]] uses them to dissolve the boundary between objective and subjective reality. The narrator's dreams are not episodes within a realistic narrative — they are the narrative. This anticipates the dream-logic of Kafka, the stream of [[soul|consciousness]] of Joyce, and the surrealist explorations of the unconscious.
