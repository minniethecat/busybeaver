## Overview

The Tivoli (Christiania Tivoli) was an amusement park and pleasure garden in Christiania, opened in 1843 and located near the city center. Modeled after the famous Tivoli Gardens in Copenhagen, it offered music, illuminations, and entertainments to the city's residents. In *Hunger*, Tivoli serves as a landmark along the narrator's wandering routes and as the site of a significant episode that blurs the line between memory and fantasy.

## In the Narrative

Tivoli first appears in Part I when the narrator follows a man down towards the amusement park, noting with his characteristic obsessive attention to dress the man's narrow collar and his awkward, thin-legged walk [001-051]. This seemingly casual observation establishes Tivoli as part of the narrator's mental map of Christiania — a destination associated with leisure that he can only observe from the outside.

Later in Part I, Tivoli becomes central to one of the narrator's most elaborate self-deceptions. He recalls having saved a young woman from an assault at Tivoli — an event that becomes entangled with his fantasies and delusions about a romantic connection with her:

> "I had saved her from an assault in the Tivoli, and upon whom I had called later on." [001-221]

The ambiguity of this recollection — is it a real memory or a fabrication born of hunger? — is characteristic of the novel's unreliable narration. The narrator inflates this single act of intervention into a full romantic narrative, only to have it collapse when reality intrudes.

In Part II, Tivoli resurfaces in a moment of absurdist confusion. A character tells the narrator a story about a man who asked what a word meant and was told "it meant either God or the Tivoli" [002-025] — a throwaway line that captures the narrator's own blurring of the sacred and the worldly in his hunger-driven state. The juxtaposition of God and Tivoli — the ultimate spiritual authority and a pleasure garden — mirrors the narrator's own oscillation between lofty artistic ambition and degrading physical need.

## Symbolic Significance

Tivoli functions in the novel as a liminal space where the boundaries between reality and fantasy dissolve. It is a place of public entertainment — music, lights, crowds — yet for the narrator it becomes the setting of an imagined chivalric rescue and an obsessive romantic fixation. The Tivoli episode exemplifies how the narrator seizes upon any scrap of social contact and inflates it into a sustaining fiction, only to be crushed when reality reasserts itself. The park's association with pleasure and leisure also makes it doubly inaccessible to the narrator: he cannot afford admission, and he cannot afford the social ease that the park's patrons enjoy.

## Real-World Context

Christiania Tivoli opened in 1843 at the corner of Stortingsgata and Rosenkrantz' gate, inspired by the success of Tivoli Gardens in Copenhagen (opened the same year). It featured a concert hall, restaurant, and landscaped gardens with illuminations — a precursor to the modern amusement park. The park operated until 1937, when it was demolished to make way for office buildings. Hamsun's reference to Tivoli places the novel firmly in the Christiania of his own youth, when the park was a fixture of the city's social life.
