# Conscience

## Overview

**Conscience** in [[knut-hamsun|Knut Hamsun]]'s *Hunger* (1890) is not a stable moral compass but a faculty that [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] alternately invokes and suppresses. At times he acts "without remorse or prick of conscience" (001-265); at others, conscience becomes the voice that judges his every action. The narrator's relationship to conscience reveals the novel's underlying moral psychology: in conditions of extreme deprivation, conscience is a luxury that the hungry cannot afford.

## In the Narrative

The most explicit statement on conscience comes in Part II, when [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] reflects: "You are too poor to support a conscience. You are hungry" (002-144). This is a moment of bitter clarity — the recognition that moral scruples are contingent on material security. Conscience is not an innate faculty but a function of social position; the destitute, [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]] suggests, are denied even the right to moral self-judgment.

Yet conscience never fully disappears. In Part IV, [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] attempts to reassert his moral autonomy: "Thus dictate I and my own conscience" (004-086) (004-087). This declaration is both defiant and desperate — an assertion of moral agency at the very moment when hunger has reduced him to near-animal existence. The repetition of the phrase across two paragraphs underscores its importance as a psychological anchor.

The narrator's conscience operates primarily as self-accusation. He is his own harshest judge, and his internal monologue is filled with moral self-reproach. Yet this conscience is also performative — a way of asserting his humanity against the dehumanizing conditions of poverty.

## Literary Context

[[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s treatment of conscience reflects the Nietzschean skepticism toward conventional morality that permeated late nineteenth-century European thought. The idea that conscience is socially constructed rather than innate — that the poor cannot afford one — would have been a provocative claim in 1890. It aligns with [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s broader rejection of social convention and his celebration of the individualistic rebel.
