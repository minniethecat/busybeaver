# Hope

**Hope** in Knut Hamsun's *Hunger* functions as a double-edged psychological mechanism — it sustains the protagonist through his worst deprivations, yet its repeated disappointment deepens his descent into despair. The novel traces the cycle of hope and collapse with an almost physiological precision, as if hope were a bodily function disrupted by starvation.

## Definition

In the context of *Hunger*, hope is not the calm, rational optimism of a well-fed person. It is a desperate, almost manic state of expectation that flares up whenever the protagonist glimpses a possible escape from his circumstances — a sale, a charitable encounter, a moment of inspiration. Each surge of hope propels him forward; each collapse leaves him more broken than before.

The oscillation between hope and despair in the novel mirrors the cycle of hunger and satiation. Just as the protagonist's body lurches between starvation and brief nourishment, his psyche swings between elation and collapse. Hope is the psychological equivalent of a meal — temporary, intense, and followed inevitably by emptiness.

## In Hunger

The novel opens with hope as the narrator's animating force. In an early passage, he reflects on "the cherished, deluded hopes, and fresh endeavours that arise, only to be shattered" [001-006], establishing the cyclical pattern that defines the entire narrative. This opening confession is remarkable for its self-awareness: the protagonist knows his hopes are deluded, yet he cannot stop hoping. Knowledge does not protect him from the psychological machinery of expectation.

Hope in *Hunger* is often tied to concrete, small-scale expectations rather than grand ambitions. When the narrator attempts to sell his vest buttons, he is animated by "the hope of being able to sell these few poor trifles" [002-117]. Similarly, after securing a potential address, he walks "full of trust and hope, all the way to Tolbod-gaden" [002-082], treating it as his "last chance." Each small success — or even the prospect of success — temporarily lifts his spirits. The modesty of these hopes makes their disappointment more poignant: he is not hoping for wealth or fame, merely for the price of a meal.

Buoyed by these moments of possibility, the narrator experiences a physical lightness: "Buoyed up by hope and satisfaction, constantly buoyed up" [001-158]. This almost giddy state is the psychological signature of hope in *Hunger* — a temporary elevation that makes the subsequent fall more painful.

The protagonist himself recognizes the terminal nature of hopelessness: "If all hope is over, why there is an end to everything" [002-196]. This admission reveals that hope, however fragile, is what separates him from complete self-destruction. Even near the novel's end, in a moment of creative frenzy, he "hoped so surely to be able to write something really great" [004-045]. The adverb "surely" is characteristically excessive — a hope that presents itself as certainty, revealing the delusional structure of the narrator's optimism.

The narrator describes waiting as a ritual of hope: "each time I still hoped for a possible succour I whispered to myself" [002-154]. Hope has become almost a private incantation repeated against the evidence. This ritualization suggests its psychological function: it is less a rational assessment of probabilities than a survival mechanism that must be performed regardless of circumstances.

## Literary Context

Hamsun's treatment of hope places *Hunger* in dialogue with the philosophical pessimism of Schopenhauer, who argued that hope is merely the temporary postponement of suffering. The narrator's experience bears this out: hope does not lead to fulfillment but to renewed disappointment. Yet Hamsun's treatment is more psychologically nuanced than philosophical pessimism — the protagonist's hope is not merely futile but constitutive of his identity. Without hope, he would cease to be the person he is.

This treatment also reflects Hamsun's broader critique of Enlightenment rationalism. The protagonist's hopes are not based on evidence or probability — they are irrational outbursts of feeling. In this sense, hope in *Hunger* is an assertion of the irrational, emotional self against a world that demands calculation and prudence.

## Related Concepts

Hope in *Hunger* is inextricably linked to **delusion** — the protagonist's hopes are often unfounded, yet they sustain him. **Despair** is hope's shadow, always waiting to reclaim him. **Dreams** represent hope's imaginative dimension, the fantasy of a better life. Finally, **shame** complicates hope: the protagonist often sabotages his own chances because his pride cannot tolerate the vulnerability that hope requires.
