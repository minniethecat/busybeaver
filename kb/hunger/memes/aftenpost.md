# Aftenpost

## Overview

**Aftenpost** (Aftenposten, "The Evening Post") is a Norwegian newspaper, founded in 1860, that appears in [[knut-hamsun|Knut Hamsun]]'s *Hunger* as a public text [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] reads but cannot afford to buy. Unlike *[[morgenbladet|Morgenbladet]]*, which functions as wallpaper in his room, *Aftenpost* is encountered in the social space of the street — a newspaper "newly posted up" (001-193) for public reading.

## Description

*Aftenposten* was, and remains, one of Norway's leading daily newspapers. In the [[christiania|Christiania]] of [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s novel, newspapers were posted publicly on boards for passersby to read — a practice that allowed those without means to participate in the city's information life. The narrator takes advantage of this, spending "a good deal of time between the marketplace and the [[graendsen|Grændsen]]" reading the *Aftenpost* (001-193).

## Relationship to Hunger

*Aftenpost* represents [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s participation in the public sphere at its most minimal. He reads the newspaper posted on the street because he cannot buy it; he participates in the city's information economy as a consumer of free public text. The *Aftenpost*, like the newspaper office he visits to submit his own writing, embodies the world of print — authoritative, institutional, permanent — that [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] can only approach from the margins.

The contrast with *[[morgenbladet|Morgenbladet]]* is instructive: *[[morgenbladet|Morgenbladet]]* is the newspaper [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] claims to write for (a credential he invokes at the guard-house), while *Aftenpost* is the newspaper he merely reads — a consumer rather than a producer of news. Together, the two newspapers define the poles of [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s relationship to the world of print.

## Critical Reception

*Aftenposten* continues to publish today as one of Norway's most widely read newspapers. Its appearance in *Hunger* documents the material conditions of urban information culture in late-nineteenth-century [[christiania|Christiania]].
