The Hack-Driver appears in a single extended scene late in the novel, when the intoxicated narrator impulsively hires a drosky for a frenzied journey across Christiania. His brief but vivid role illuminates the narrator's precarious mental state and the social distance between the protagonist and ordinary working citizens.

The narrator, drunk on beer and the euphoria of having money, arrives at Parliament Place and suddenly decides to hire a vehicle. He approaches the first drosky and calls out "Ullevoldsveien, No. 37" — Ylajali's address [002-177]. The driver sets off, but during the journey he repeatedly looks back into the carriage, peering at the narrator sheltered beneath the hood. The narrator's ragged appearance has attracted his suspicion.

When they reach Ullevoldsveien and the narrator realizes no one is home, he redirects the driver to Tomtegaden No. 11 with escalating urgency [002-177]. The driver, now convinced that the matter is one of life and death, whips his horse sharply and races through the streets. He calls out to warn pedestrians: "Get out of the way there, man!--Here's a man who's dying!" [002-180].

The scene ends with the narrator's realization that the driver has noted him as someone distinctive — "a man one would not be likely to make any mistake about" [002-180]. In his paranoid state, the narrator interprets this as a threat, fearing that the driver could identify him to the authorities should the narrator's theft of money be discovered.
