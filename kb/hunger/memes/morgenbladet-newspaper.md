*Morgenbladet* (The Morning Paper) is a Norwegian daily newspaper founded in 1819, making it one of the country's oldest periodicals. In the 19th century, it was one of Kristiania's leading newspapers, covering politics, culture, and commerce.

## In Hunger

*Morgenbladet* appears memorably in the opening passages of Knut Hamsun's *Hunger*. The narrator's attic room is wallpapered with old copies of the newspaper:

"I could distinguish clearly a notice from the Director of Lighthouses, and a little to the left of that an inflated advertisement of Fabian Olsens' new-baked bread." (001-001)

This detail operates on multiple levels. The newspaper-as-wallpaper literalizes the narrator's immersion in the world of print — he is a writer, after all. It also signals his poverty: proper wallpaper is beyond his means. The fragmented notices and advertisements create a textual collage that mirrors the narrator's own fragmented consciousness.

The detail of the *Morgenbladet* wallpaper recurs later: "the *Morgenbladet* near the door was rent in strips a span long" by the wind, linking the deterioration of the room to the narrator's own physical and mental decline.

## Cultural Context

*Morgenbladet* was associated with the cultural and political establishment of 19th-century Kristiania. Its presence in the novel — reduced to peeling wallpaper — can be read as a modernist gesture: the authority of print culture, like the authority of objective realism, is here reduced to mere surface, a material from which the narrator's subjective experience can be constructed.
