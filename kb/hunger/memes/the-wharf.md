# The Wharf

## Overview

The **wharf** at [[christiania|Christiania]]'s harbor represents the literal and symbolic edge of the city in [[knut-hamsun|Knut Hamsun]]'s *Hunger*. It is the place [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] walks toward when his suffering becomes unbearable — a liminal zone between land and water, city and sea, life and the possibility of its end. The wharf is mentioned across Parts II, III, and IV, recurring at moments of existential crisis.

## In the Narrative

The wharf first appears in Part II as a point on [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s spatial map: the railway bridge is located "near the wharf" (002-097), linking the transport infrastructure of rail and sea. This early mention establishes the wharf as part of the city's margin — a place of movement and transit rather than settlement.

In Part III, [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] makes a deliberate journey toward the water: "I made my way down town, down to the wharf, farther and farther away from" (003-088). The sentence breaks off — "away from" what? The city? The self? The unfinished thought mirrors [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s own inability to complete the gesture of departure. He reaches the railway wharf and finds it empty: "There was no traffic, no noise" (003-089). The wharf's desolation matches [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s interior emptiness — a space stripped of commerce and crowds, reduced to pure geography.

The wharf's most significant appearance comes in Part IV, when [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s crisis reaches its extreme: "'Tire'? I walk farther down the wharf, find a packing-case and sit down" (004-143). The word "Tire" — possibly "tired," interrupted — suggests the exhaustion that has brought [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] to the city's edge. The packing-case, a piece of commercial detritus, becomes a seat from which he contemplates the water. The wharf is the space of last resort, the place one goes when there is nowhere further to go.

## Symbolic Significance

The wharf embodies the novel's preoccupation with boundaries and thresholds. It is where the city meets the sea, where solid ground gives way to water, where the possibility of departure becomes tangible. The narrator's gravitation toward the wharf at moments of crisis expresses a desire for dissolution — a longing to dissolve into the element, to cease being a self with a body that hungers and a mind that cannot rest.

But the wharf is also a space of inaction. The narrator sits on the packing-case; he does not jump. The wharf is the stage for contemplating the ultimate departure without ever taking it. Like the railway square, the wharf represents a threshold [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] approaches but never crosses — the geography of a life lived in permanent suspension.
