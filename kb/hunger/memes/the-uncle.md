# The Uncle

## Overview

**"The Uncle"** is [[the-unnamed-narrator|the unnamed narrator]]'s ironic name for a [[christiania|Christiania]] pawnbroker in [[knut-hamsun|Knut Hamsun]]'s *[[hunger|Hunger]]* (1890). The nickname — always placed in quotation marks in the text — encapsulates [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s ambivalent relationship to this figure: he is simultaneously a source of desperately needed cash and an object of resentment and mockery.

## In the Narrative

The Uncle first appears indirectly. The narrator notes that his "belongings had been taken to my 'Uncle'" (001-002), establishing [[the-pawnbroker|the pawnbroker]] as the repository of his dwindling possessions. By Part II, "everything had been brought to my 'Uncle's,' and consumed" (002-067) — a bleak summary of the narrator's progressive dispossession.

The most extended interaction occurs in Part II. The narrator arrives "with my buttons ready in my hand; 'Uncle' is sitting at his desk" (002-197), attempting to pawn the buttons from his coat. The Uncle's response is devastating in its casualness: "'For the buttons?' — and 'Uncle' stares astonishedly at me" (002-198). The buttons are worthless, and the narrator's desperation is made visible.

The Uncle's mockery is quiet but cutting: "'Nothing on your glasses,' said 'Uncle'; I told you that once before" (002-200). This reference to the narrator's repeated failures — he has been here before, with equally worthless items — captures the relationship's dynamic: the narrator, perpetually hoping; the Uncle, perpetually unimpressed.

## Relationship to the Narrator

The Uncle represents the economic [[reality|reality]] that the narrator cannot escape. Unlike [[the-landlady|the landlady]], whose demands are social and moral, the Uncle's transactions are purely commercial — and it is precisely this impersonality that makes them so humiliating. The narrator's nickname is a defense: calling him "Uncle" domesticates a relationship that is, in [[truth|truth]], one of pure economic power.
