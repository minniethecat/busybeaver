## Overview

The Stiftsgaarden (Stiftsgården) was a notable building in Christiania associated with official functions and the diocesan administration. In *Hunger*, it functions as a symbol of the social and bureaucratic order from which the narrator is excluded — a place where important people conduct important business while he scavenges on the streets outside.

## In the Narrative

The Stiftsgaarden appears in Part II entirely through the voice of other characters rather than through the narrator's direct experience. A man tells the narrator an absurd story about sitting in state at the Stiftsgaarden until two o'clock, having forgotten the door-key — a boast that attempts to convey social importance through association with an official building:

> "Sat in state in the Stiftsgaarden till two o'clock; forgot door-key." [002-016]

The man's casual mention of the Stiftsgaarden, dropped into conversation as a marker of status, is characteristic of the minor characters who populate the novel's taverns and streets — figures who inflate their own importance through name-dropping and fabricated connections to power.

Later in the same part, a character claims to receive communications from the Stiftsgaarden, again invoking the building as a signifier of connection to institutional authority [002-121]. The narrator himself never enters the Stiftsgaarden, nor does he claim any association with it. He encounters it only as a word in others' mouths — a ghost of respectability that belongs to a world he cannot access.

The fact that the narrator only hears about the Stiftsgaarden through others' boasts reinforces his exclusion from institutional Christiania. While men with keys and communications busy themselves inside, he drifts through the streets outside, locked out of every door.

## Symbolic Significance

The Stiftsgaarden represents the world of officialdom: positions, salaries, keys, communications — everything the narrator lacks. It is invoked by minor characters as social currency, a name to drop that signals one's proximity to power. For the narrator, who cannot even afford a room key, the Stiftsgaarden embodies the stable, bureaucratic existence he has either rejected or been rejected by. Its appearance in the novel only through hearsay and boasting makes it a phantom of respectability, visible to the narrator only through the claims of others. The irony is sharp: the people who claim connection to the Stiftsgaarden are themselves marginal figures in a tavern, their boasts as hollow as the narrator's own fantasies about literary success.

## Real-World Context

Stiftsgården in Christiania was the administrative seat of the Diocese of Oslo (Christiania Stift), housing the offices of the diocesan governor (stiftsamtmann). Located in the city center, it was a significant building in the bureaucratic landscape of 19th-century Christiania. The building's association with church administration also carries a subtle religious overtone that would have been legible to Hamsun's contemporary readers — the narrator, who wrestles with God throughout the novel, is doubly excluded from this seat of both earthly and ecclesiastical power.
