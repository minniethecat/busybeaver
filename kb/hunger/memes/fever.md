# Fever

## Overview

**Fever** in [[knut-hamsun|Knut Hamsun]]'s *Hunger* (1890) is both a physical symptom of [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s deteriorating body and a metaphor for his psychological state. The narrator's "feverish literary struggle" (P04-007) — a phrase used to describe [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s own creative period — blurs the line between creative inspiration and pathological obsession. Fever appears throughout the novel as a bridge between the physical and the mental, the real and the hallucinatory.

## In the Narrative

The narrator's fever is closely tied to his hunger. In Part I, he falls asleep "with my brain full of fever and dreams" (001-196), and anticipates that "fever would fill my brain" (001-215) as his condition worsens. Fever is not merely a symptom but an altered state of [[soul|consciousness]] — one that enables the creative [[dreams|visions]] that make him a writer while simultaneously threatening his sanity.

The fever intensifies as the narrative progresses. In Part II, [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]] acknowledges his condition with ironic detachment: "There's too much fever" (002-128) in his writing, a moment of self-awareness that is itself a product of the fevered state. He considers explaining his condition to a potential benefactor, reasoning that "he would be sure to aid me" (002-129) — yet pride prevents him.

By Part III, the fever has become a full bodily crisis. The narrator describes being "bathed in dampness. The fever had laid violent hands on me" (003-068). The language is that of assault — fever as an external force that possesses and overwhelms the self. In Part IV, he drags himself up "wet with fever and exhaustion" (004-163), the fever now indistinguishable from the starvation that produced it.

The preface provides an important framing: [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s literary style itself is described through the metaphor of fever. The novel's psychological realism "had about as much in common with the old-fashioned conceptions of man's mental activities as the delirious utterances of a fever patient" (P04-010). Fever is thus not merely a theme within the novel but a description of its narrative method — the fevered, associative, stream-of-[[soul|consciousness]] style that was revolutionary in 1890.

## Literary Context

The use of fever as a literary metaphor has a long history, from the Romantic association of fever with poetic genius to the Naturalist use of fever as a clinical symptom of social decay. [[knut-hamsun|Hamsun]]'s innovation is to make fever the very mode of narration. The reader experiences [[the-unnamed-narrator|the narrator]]'s fever not as a described condition but as the texture of the prose itself — fragmentary, associative, oscillating between lucidity and delirium. This technique would influence later modernist writers, from Joyce to Kafka.
