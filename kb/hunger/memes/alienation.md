Alienation is a concept describing the condition of estrangement or separation — from society, from one's own labor, from authentic selfhood, or from meaning itself. The concept has roots in Hegelian and Marxist philosophy but became a central preoccupation of modernist literature in the late 19th and early 20th centuries.

## Alienation in Hunger

Knut Hamsun's *Hunger* presents alienation with extraordinary intensity. The unnamed narrator is alienated on multiple levels:

**Social Alienation:** He is excluded from the economic life of Kristiania — unable to afford food, lodging, or even proper clothing. This material exclusion is compounded by social stigma; he is mistaken for a vagrant, treated with contempt, and rendered invisible by his poverty.

**Self-Alienation:** The narrator's hunger produces a fragmentation of identity. He observes his own behavior with ironic detachment, gives false names, and experiences his body and mind as alien to himself. His pride leads him to refuse help while his desperation drives him to degrading acts.

**Creative Alienation:** As a writer who cannot afford to write, the narrator is alienated from his own creative labor. The articles he sells for a few shillings are commodities disconnected from his artistic identity.

## Pre-Existentialist Alienation

Written decades before Sartre and Camus, *Hunger* anticipates existentialist treatments of alienation. The narrator's condition is not merely social (as in Marxist analysis) but ontological — a fundamental separation from the conditions of meaningful existence. This makes *Hunger* a precursor to the 20th-century literature of absurdity and estrangement.
