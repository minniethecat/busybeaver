## Overview

Prison in Knut Hamsun's *Hunger* (1890) functions on multiple levels: as a literal institution (the guard-house where the narrator spends a night), as a metaphor for the narrator's entrapment in poverty, and as a psychological state of self-imposed confinement. The prison motif connects the novel's spatial poetics — the room, the cell, the city streets — to its existential themes.

## In Hunger

The narrator's most direct encounter with imprisonment occurs in Part II, when, lacking a roof over his head, he voluntarily presents himself at the night guard-house. He frames his situation with characteristic self-consciousness: "What did it look like to see a journalist in the night guard-house without a roof over his head?" (002-007). The guard-house becomes both shelter and stigma — a space that provides physical safety while marking his social degradation.

Within the cell, the narrator's experience is ambivalent. He initially finds comfort: "The bright cell had a friendly air; I felt comfortably and well sheltered; and listened with pleasure to the rain outside" (002-016). But this sense of refuge is fleeting. His physical weakness transforms the cell into a site of hallucination and collapse: "I had fallen to the floor, incapable of stumbling about the cell any longer" (002-031). The cell becomes a container for his unraveling consciousness.

Even in confinement, the narrator's pride reasserts itself through fantasy: he imagines himself "high above all the prisoners. A homeless Cabinet Minister" (002-034) — a phrase that encapsulates the novel's central paradox, where absolute social degradation coexists with an undiminished sense of distinction. Upon release the next morning, he exits not with the shame of a discharged prisoner but "with elevated head, the carriage of a millionaire" (002-050).

The prison motif extends beyond the guard-house. The narrator's room, the city of Christiania itself, and his own starving body are all figured as confining spaces. In Part II's darkest moments, he anticipates returning to "the guard-house again, imprisoned in a dark cell which had not a spark of light in it" (002-176) — the literal threat of incarceration merging with existential dread.

## Literary Context

Hamsun's use of the prison motif in *Hunger* draws on a tradition stretching from Dostoevsky's prison writings to the Symbolist fascination with enclosed spaces. However, Hamsun's treatment is distinctive in its psychological interiority — the prison is less a physical place than a state of consciousness, an extension of the narrator's own mind. This anticipates the existential prison of Kafka's later works and the modernist treatment of urban space as a carceral landscape.
