The union between Norway and Sweden lasted from 1814 to 1905, a period during which Norway was constitutionally distinct but shared a monarch and foreign policy with Sweden. This political arrangement formed the backdrop for the Norwegian literary and cultural renaissance that included Ibsen, Bjørnson, and Hamsun.

## Historical Background

After the Napoleonic Wars, the Treaty of Kiel (1814) transferred Norway from Denmark to Sweden. Norwegian resistance led to the adoption of a separate Norwegian constitution at Eidsvoll in May 1814 and a brief war before Norway accepted a looser union. Throughout the 19th century, Norwegian nationalism increasingly demanded full sovereignty.

## Cultural Impact

The union period coincided with Norway's most significant literary and artistic flowering. The struggle for cultural and linguistic independence fueled the work of writers who sought to define a distinctly Norwegian identity. Bjørnson and Ibsen were openly engaged in national politics; Hamsun, born in 1860, grew up during the height of this nationalist ferment.

The union dissolved peacefully in 1905, with Norway becoming a fully independent kingdom under King Haakon VII.

## Relevance to Hunger

While *Hunger* (1890) does not directly address the union, the novel's setting in Kristiania — a city whose very name reflected Danish (and then Swedish) influence — implicitly engages with questions of national identity. The novel's psychological individualism can be read partly as a reaction against the nationalist collectivism that dominated Norwegian culture.
