The doppelgänger motif — the double, the shadow self, the uncanny duplicate — is a recurring feature of 19th and early 20th-century literature, from Poe and Dostoyevski to Stevenson and Wilde. In Hamsun's *Hunger*, the motif operates not through a literal double but through the narrator's radical self-division.

## The Split Narrator

The narrator of *Hunger* constantly observes himself as if from outside. He comments on his own behavior with ironic detachment, analyzes his motivations as if they belonged to someone else, and experiences his body as alien to his will. This splitting of consciousness — the self that acts and the self that watches — is the doppelgänger motif internalized as psychological condition.

## Modernist Transformation

Where the traditional doppelgänger (as in *Dr. Jekyll and Mr. Hyde* or Poe's "William Wilson") externalizes moral conflict as a literal double, Hamsun internalizes it as a feature of consciousness itself. The narrator does not need a supernatural duplicate because his ordinary consciousness is already split — into the hungry body and the observing mind, the proud gentleman and the desperate pauper, the creative artist and the abject failure.
