# Section 47

[047-001]Nobody was left in the lower part of the house. Not many minutes passed

[047-002]when Grace heard a knock at the door—a single knock, not loud enough to

[047-003]reach the ears of those in the bedroom. She went to the top of the

[047-004]stairs and said, faintly, “Come up,” knowing that the door stood, as

[047-005]usual in such houses, wide open.

[047-006]Retreating into the gloom of the broad landing she saw rise up the

[047-007]stairs a woman whom at first she did not recognize, till her voice

[047-008]revealed her to be [[suke-damson|Suke Damson]], in great fright and sorrow. A streak of

[047-009]light from the partially closed door of Grace’s room fell upon her face

[047-010]as she came forward, and it was drawn and pale.

[047-011]“Oh, [[grace-melbury|Miss Melbury]]—I would say Mrs. [[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Fitzpiers]],” she said, wringing her

[047-012]hands. “This terrible news. Is he dead? Is he hurted very bad? Tell me;

[047-013]I couldn’t help coming; please forgive me, Miss [[george-melbury|Melbury]]—Mrs. Fitzpiers

[047-014]I would say!”

[047-015]Grace sank down on the oak chest which stood on the landing, and put

[047-016]her hands to her now flushed face and head. Could she order Suke Damson

[047-017]down-stairs and out of the house? Her husband might be brought in at

[047-018]any moment, and what would happen? But could she order this genuinely

[047-019]grieved woman away?

[047-020]There was a dead silence of half a minute or so, till Suke said, “Why

[047-021]don’t ye speak? Is he here? Is he dead? If so, why can’t I see

[047-022]him—would it be so very wrong?”

[047-023]Before Grace had answered somebody else came to the door below—a

[047-024]foot-fall light as a roe’s. There was a hurried tapping upon the panel,

[047-025]as if with the impatient tips of fingers whose owner thought not

[047-026]whether a knocker were there or no. Without a pause, and possibly

[047-027]guided by the stray beam of light on the landing, the newcomer ascended

[047-028]the staircase as the first had done. Grace was sufficiently visible,

[047-029]and the lady, for a lady it was, came to her side.

[047-030]“I could make nobody hear down-stairs,” said [[felice-charmond|Felice Charmond]], with lips

[047-031]whose dryness could almost be heard, and panting, as she stood like one

[047-032]ready to sink on the floor with distress. “What is—the matter—tell me

[047-033]the worst! Can he live?” She looked at Grace imploringly, without

[047-034]perceiving poor Suke, who, dismayed at such a presence, had shrunk away

[047-035]into the shade.

[047-036]Mrs. Charmond’s little feet were covered with mud; she was quite

[047-037]unconscious of her appearance now. “I have heard such a dreadful

[047-038]report,” she went on; “I came to ascertain the truth of it. Is

[047-039]he—killed?”

[047-040]“She won’t tell us—he’s dying—he’s in that room!” burst out Suke,

[047-041]regardless of consequences, as she heard the distant movements of Mrs.

[047-042]Melbury and Grammer in the bedroom at the end of the passage.

[047-043]“Where?” said Mrs. Charmond; and on Suke pointing out the direction,

[047-044]she made as if to go thither.

[047-045]Grace barred the way. “He is not there,” she said. “I have not seen him

[047-046]any more than you. I have heard a report only—not so bad as you think.

[047-047]It must have been exaggerated to you.”

[047-048]“Please do not conceal anything—let me know all!” said Felice,

[047-049]doubtingly.

[047-050]“You shall know all I know—you have a perfect right to know—who can

[047-051]have a better than either of you?” said Grace, with a delicate sting

[047-052]which was lost upon Felice Charmond now. “I repeat, I have only heard a

[047-053]less alarming account than you have heard; how much it means, and how

[047-054]little, I cannot say. I pray God that it means not much—in common

[047-055]humanity. You probably pray the same—_for other reasons_.”

[047-056]She regarded them both there in the dim light a while.

[047-057]They stood dumb in their trouble, not stinging back at her; not heeding

[047-058]her mood. A tenderness spread over Grace like a dew. It was well, very

[047-059]well, conventionally, to address either one of them in the wife’s

[047-060]regulation terms of virtuous sarcasm, as woman, creature, or thing, for

[047-061]losing their hearts to her husband. But life, what was it, and who was

[047-062]she? She had, like the singer of the psalm of Asaph, been plagued and

[047-063]chastened all the day long; but could she, by retributive words, in

[047-064]order to please herself—the individual—“offend against the generation,”

[047-065]as he would not?

[047-066]“He is dying, perhaps,” blubbered Suke Damson, putting her apron to her

[047-067]eyes.

[047-068]In their gestures and faces there were anxieties, [[love-and-desire|affection]], agony of

[047-069]heart, all for a man who had wronged them—had never really behaved

[047-070]towards either of them anyhow but selfishly. Neither one but would have

[047-071]wellnigh sacrificed half her life to him, even now. The tears which his

[047-072]possibly critical situation could not bring to her eyes surged over at

[047-073]the contemplation of these fellow-[[gender-roles|women]]. She turned to the balustrade,

[047-074]bent herself upon it, and wept.

[047-075]Thereupon Felice began to cry also, without using her handkerchief, and

[047-076]letting the tears run down silently. While these three poor women stood

[047-077]together thus, pitying another though most to be pitied themselves, the

[047-078]pacing of a horse or horses became audible in the court, and in a

[047-079]moment Melbury’s voice was heard calling to his stableman. Grace at

[047-080]once started up, ran down the stairs and out into the quadrangle as her

[047-081]father crossed it towards the door. “Father, what is the matter with

[047-082]him?” she cried.

[047-083]“Who—Edgar?” said Melbury, abruptly. “Matter? Nothing. What, my dear,

[047-084]and have you got home safe? Why, you are better already! But you ought

[047-085]not to be out in the air like this.”

[047-086]“But he has been thrown off his horse!”

[047-087]“I know; I know. I saw it. He got up again, and walked off as well as

[047-088]ever. A fall on the leaves didn’t hurt a spry fellow like him. He did

[047-089]not come this way,” he added, significantly. “I suppose he went to look

[047-090]for his horse. I tried to find him, but could not. But after seeing him

[047-091]go away under the [[nature-in-the-woodlanders|trees]] I found the horse, and have led it home for

[047-092]safety. So he must walk. Now, don’t you stay out here in this night

[047-093]air.”

[047-094]She returned to the house with her father. When she had again ascended

[047-095]to the landing and to her own rooms beyond it was a great relief to her

[047-096]to find that both Petticoat the First and Petticoat the Second of her

[047-097]_Bien-aimé_ had silently disappeared. They had, in all probability,

[047-098]heard the words of her father, and departed with their anxieties

[047-099]relieved.

[047-100]Presently her parents came up to Grace, and busied themselves to see

[047-101]that she was comfortable. Perceiving soon that she would prefer to be

[047-102]left alone they went away.

[047-103]Grace waited on. The clock raised its voice now and then, but her

[047-104]husband did not return. At her father’s usual hour for retiring he

[047-105]again came in to see her. “Do not stay up,” she said, as soon as he

[047-106]entered. “I am not at all tired. I will sit up for him.”

[047-107]“I think it will be useless, Grace,” said Melbury, slowly.

[047-108]“Why?”

[047-109]“I have had a bitter quarrel with him; and on that account I hardly

[047-110]think he will return to-night.”

[047-111]“A quarrel? Was that after the fall seen by the boy?”

[047-112]Melbury nodded an affirmative, without taking his eyes off the candle.

[047-113]“Yes; it was as we were coming home together,” he said.

[047-114]Something had been swelling up in Grace while her father was speaking.

[047-115]“How could you want to quarrel with him?” she cried, suddenly. “Why

[047-116]could you not let him come home quietly if he were inclined to? He is

[047-117]my husband; and now you have married me to him surely you need not

[047-118]provoke him unnecessarily. First you induce me to accept him, and then

[047-119]you do things that divide us more than we should naturally be divided!”

[047-120]“How can you speak so unjustly to me, Grace?” said Melbury, with

[047-121]indignant sorrow. “_I_ divide you from your husband, indeed! You little

[047-122]think—”

[047-123]He was inclined to say more—to tell her the whole story of the

[047-124]encounter, and that the provocation he had received had lain entirely

[047-125]in hearing her despised. But it would have greatly distressed her, and

[047-126]he forbore. “You had better lie down. You are tired,” he said,

[047-127]soothingly. “Good-night.”

[047-128]The household went to bed, and a silence fell upon the dwelling, broken

[047-129]only by the occasional skirr of a halter in Melbury’s stables. Despite

[047-130]her father’s advice Grace still waited up. But nobody came.

[047-131]It was a critical time in Grace’s emotional life that night. She

[047-132]thought of her husband a good deal, and for the nonce forgot

[047-133][[giles-winterborne|Winterborne]].

[047-134]“How these unhappy women must have admired Edgar!” she said to herself.

[047-135]“How attractive he must be to everybody; and, indeed, he is

[047-136]attractive.” The possibility is that, piqued by [[jealousy-and-rivalry|rivalry]], these ideas

[047-137]might have been transformed into their corresponding emotions by a show

[047-138]of the least reciprocity in Fitzpiers. There was, in truth, a love-bird

[047-139]yearning to fly from her heart; and it wanted a lodging badly.

[047-140]But no husband came. The fact was that Melbury had been much mistaken

[047-141]about the condition of Fitzpiers. People do not fall headlong on stumps

[047-142]of underwood with impunity. Had the old man been able to watch

[047-143]Fitzpiers narrowly enough, he would have observed that on rising and

[047-144]walking into the thicket he dropped blood as he went; that he had not

[047-145]proceeded fifty yards before he showed signs of being dizzy, and,

[047-146]raising his hands to his head, reeled and fell down.

[047-147]CHAPTER XXXVI.

[047-148]Grace was not the only one who watched and meditated in [[little-hintock|Hintock]] that

[047-149]night. Felice Charmond was in no mood to retire to rest at a customary

[047-150]hour; and over her drawing-room fire at the Manor House she sat as

[047-151]motionless and in as deep a reverie as Grace in her little apartment at

[047-152]the homestead.

[047-153]Having caught ear of Melbury’s intelligence while she stood on the

[047-154]landing at his house, and been eased of much of her mental distress,

[047-155]her sense of personal decorum returned upon her with a rush. She

[047-156]descended the stairs and left the door like a ghost, keeping close to

[047-157]the walls of the building till she got round to the gate of the

[047-158]quadrangle, through which she noiselessly passed almost before Grace

[047-159]and her father had finished their discourse. Suke Damson had thought it

[047-160]well to imitate her superior in this respect, and, descending the back

[047-161]stairs as Felice descended the front, went out at the side door and

[047-162]home to her cottage.

[047-163]Once outside Melbury’s gates Mrs. Charmond ran with all her speed to

[047-164]the Manor House, without stopping or turning her head, and splitting

[047-165]her thin boots in her haste. She entered her own dwelling, as she had

[047-166]emerged from it, by the drawing-room window. In other circumstances she

[047-167]would have felt some timidity at undertaking such an unpremeditated

[047-168]excursion alone; but her anxiety for another had cast out her fear for

[047-169]herself.

[047-170]Everything in her drawing-room was just as she had left it—the candles

[047-171]still burning, the casement closed, and the shutters gently pulled to,

[047-172]so as to hide the state of the window from the cursory glance of a

[047-173]servant entering the apartment. She had been gone about three-quarters

[047-174]of an hour by the clock, and nobody seemed to have discovered her

[047-175]absence. Tired in body but tense in mind, she sat down, palpitating,

[047-176]round-eyed, bewildered at what she had done.

[047-177]She had been betrayed by affrighted love into a visit which, now that

[047-178]the emotion instigating it had calmed down under her belief that

[047-179]Fitzpiers was in no danger, was the saddest surprise to her. This was

[047-180]how she had set about doing her best to escape her passionate bondage

[047-181]to him! Somehow, in declaring to Grace and to herself the unseemliness

[047-182]of her infatuation, she had grown a convert to its irresistibility. If

[047-183]Heaven would only give her strength; but Heaven never did! One thing

[047-184]was indispensable; she must go away from Hintock if she meant to

[047-185]withstand further temptation. The struggle was too wearying, too

[047-186]hopeless, while she remained. It was but a continual capitulation of

[047-187]conscience to what she dared not name.
