# Section 12

[012-001]He thought and thought, and at last decided that as good a way as any

[012-002]would be to give a Christmas party, and ask Grace and her parents to

[012-003]come as chief guests.

[012-004]These ruminations were occupying him when there became audible a slight

[012-005]knocking at his front door. He descended the path and looked out, and

[012-006]beheld [[marty-south|Marty South]], dressed for out-door work.

[012-007]“Why didn’t you come, Mr. [[giles-winterborne|Winterborne]]?” she said. “I’ve been waiting

[012-008]there hours and hours, and at last I thought I must try to find you.”

[012-009]“Bless my soul, I’d quite forgot,” said Giles.

[012-010]What he had forgotten was that there was a thousand young fir-[[nature-in-the-woodlanders|trees]] to

[012-011]be planted in a neighboring spot which had been cleared by the

[012-012]wood-cutters, and that he had arranged to plant them with his own

[012-013]hands. He had a marvellous power of making trees grow. Although he

[012-014]would seem to shovel in the earth quite carelessly, there was a sort of

[012-015]sympathy between himself and the fir, oak, or beech that he was

[012-016]operating on, so that the roots took hold of the soil in a few days.

[012-017]When, on the other hand, any of the journeymen planted, although they

[012-018]seemed to go through an identically similar process, one quarter of the

[012-019]trees would die away during the ensuing August.

[012-020]Hence Winterborne found delight in the work even when, as at present,

[012-021]he contracted to do it on portions of the woodland in which he had no

[012-022]personal interest. Marty, who turned her hand to anything, was usually

[012-023]the one who performed the part of keeping the trees in a perpendicular

[012-024]position while he threw in the mould.

[012-025]He accompanied her towards the spot, being stimulated yet further to

[012-026]proceed with the work by the knowledge that the ground was close to the

[012-027]way-side along which Grace must pass on her return from [[hintock-house|Hintock House]].

[012-028]“You’ve a cold in the head, Marty,” he said, as they walked. “That

[012-029]comes of cutting off your hair.”

[012-030]“I suppose it do. Yes; I’ve three headaches going on in my head at the

[012-031]same time.”

[012-032]“Three headaches!”

[012-033]“Yes, a rheumatic headache in my poll, a sick headache over my eyes,

[012-034]and a misery headache in the middle of my brain. However, I came out,

[012-035]for I thought you might be waiting and grumbling like anything if I was

[012-036]not there.”

[012-037]The holes were already dug, and they set to work. Winterborne’s fingers

[012-038]were endowed with a gentle conjuror’s touch in spreading the roots of

[012-039]each little tree, resulting in a sort of caress, under which the

[012-040]delicate fibres all laid themselves out in their proper directions for

[012-041]growth. He put most of these roots towards the south-west; for, he

[012-042]said, in forty years’ time, when some great gale is blowing from that

[012-043]quarter, the trees will require the strongest holdfast on that side to

[012-044]stand against it and not fall.

[012-045]“How they sigh directly we put ’em upright, though while they are lying

[012-046]down they don’t sigh at all,” said Marty.

[012-047]“Do they?” said Giles. “I’ve never noticed it.”

[012-048]She erected one of the young pines into its hole, and held up her

[012-049]finger; the soft musical breathing instantly set in, which was not to

[012-050]cease night or day till the grown tree should be felled—probably long

[012-051]after the two planters should be felled themselves.

[012-052]“It seems to me,” the girl continued, “as if they sigh because they are

[012-053]very sorry to begin life in earnest—just as we be.”

[012-054]“Just as we be?” He looked critically at her. “You ought not to feel

[012-055]like that, Marty.”

[012-056]Her only reply was turning to take up the next tree; and they planted

[012-057]on through a great part of the day, almost without another word.

[012-058]Winterborne’s mind ran on his contemplated evening-party, his

[012-059]abstraction being such that he hardly was conscious of Marty’s presence

[012-060]beside him. From the nature of their employment, in which he handled

[012-061]the spade and she merely held the tree, it followed that he got good

[012-062]exercise and she got none. But she was an heroic girl, and though her

[012-063]out-stretched hand was chill as a stone, and her cheeks blue, and her

[012-064]cold worse than ever, she would not complain while he was disposed to

[012-065]continue work. But when he paused she said, “Mr. Winterborne, can I run

[012-066]down the lane and back to warm my feet?”

[012-067]“Why, yes, of course,” he said, awakening anew to her existence.

[012-068]“Though I was just thinking what a mild day it is for the season. Now I

[012-069]warrant that cold of yours is twice as bad as it was. You had no

[012-070]business to chop that hair off, Marty; it serves you almost right. Look

[012-071]here, cut off home at once.”

[012-072]“A run down the lane will be quite enough.”

[012-073]“No, it won’t. You ought not to have come out to-day at all.”

[012-074]“But I should like to finish the—”

[012-075]“Marty, I tell you to go home,” said he, peremptorily. “I can manage to

[012-076]keep the rest of them upright with a stick or something.”

[012-077]She went away without saying any more. When she had gone down the

[012-078][[orchard-culture|orchard]] a little distance she looked back. Giles suddenly went after

[012-079]her.

[012-080]“Marty, it was for your good that I was rough, you know. But warm

[012-081]yourself in your own way, I don’t care.”

[012-082]When she had run off he fancied he discerned a woman’s dress through

[012-083]the holly-bushes which divided the coppice from the road. It was Grace

[012-084]at last, on her way back from the interview with [[felice-charmond|Mrs. Charmond]]. He

[012-085]threw down the tree he was planting, and was about to break through the

[012-086]belt of holly when he suddenly became aware of the presence of another

[012-087]man, who was looking over the hedge on the opposite side of the way

[012-088]upon the figure of the unconscious Grace. He appeared as a handsome and

[012-089]gentlemanly personage of six or eight and twenty, and was quizzing her

[012-090]through an eye-glass. Seeing that Winterborne was noticing him, he let

[012-091]his glass drop with a click upon the rail which protected the hedge,

[012-092]and walked away in the opposite direction. Giles knew in a moment that

[012-093]this must be [[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Mr. Fitzpiers]]. When he was gone, Winterborne pushed

[012-094]through the hollies, and emerged close beside the interesting object of

[012-095]their contemplation.

[012-097]“I heard the bushes move long before I saw you,” she began. “I said

[012-098]first, ‘it is some terrible beast;’ next, ‘it is a poacher;’ next, ‘it

[012-099]is a friend!’”

[012-100]He regarded her with a slight smile, weighing, not her speech, but the

[012-101]question whether he should tell her that she had been watched. He

[012-102]decided in the negative.

[012-103]“You have been to the house?” he said. “But I need not ask.” The fact

[012-104]was that there shone upon [[grace-melbury|Miss Melbury]]’s face a species of exaltation,

[012-105]which saw no environing details nor his own occupation; nothing more

[012-106]than his bare presence.

[012-107]“Why need you not ask?”

[012-108]“Your face is like the face of Moses when he came down from the Mount.”

[012-109]She reddened a little and said, “How can you be so profane, Giles

[012-110]Winterborne?”

[012-111]“How can you think so much of that class of people? Well, I beg pardon;

[012-112]I didn’t mean to speak so freely. How do you like her house and her?”

[012-113]“Exceedingly. I had not been inside the walls since I was a child, when

[012-114]it used to be let to strangers, before Mrs. Charmond’s late husband

[012-115]bought the property. She is _so_ nice!” And Grace fell into such an

[012-116]abstracted gaze at the imaginary image of Mrs. Charmond and her

[012-117]niceness that it almost conjured up a vision of that lady in mid-air

[012-118]before them.

[012-119]“She has only been here a month or two, it seems, and cannot stay much

[012-120]longer, because she finds it so lonely and damp in winter. She is going

[012-121]abroad. Only think, she would like me to go with her.”

[012-122]Giles’s features stiffened a little at the news. “Indeed; what for? But

[012-123]I won’t keep you standing here. Hoi, Robert!” he cried to a swaying

[012-124]collection of clothes in the distance, which was the figure of [[robert-creedle|Creedle]]

[012-125]his man. “Go on filling in there till I come back.”

[012-126]“I’m a-coming, sir; I’m a-coming.”

[012-127]“Well, the reason is this,” continued she, as they went on

[012-128]together—“Mrs. Charmond has a delightful side to her character—a [[love-and-desire|desire]]

[012-129]to record her impressions of travel, like Alexandre Dumas, and Méry,

[012-130]and Sterne, and others. But she cannot find energy enough to do it

[012-131]herself.” And Grace proceeded to explain Mrs. Charmond’s proposal at

[012-132]large. “My notion is that Méry’s style will suit her best, because he

[012-133]writes in that soft, emotional, luxurious way she has,” Grace said,

[012-134]musingly.

[012-135]“Indeed!” said Winterborne, with mock awe. “Suppose you talk over my

[012-136]head a little longer, Miss Grace [[george-melbury|Melbury]]?”

[012-137]“Oh, I didn’t mean it!” she said, repentantly, looking into his eyes.

[012-138]“And as for myself, I hate French books. And I love dear old [[little-hintock|Hintock]],

[012-139]_and the people in it_, fifty times better than all the Continent. But

[012-140]the scheme; I think it an enchanting notion, don’t you, Giles?”

[012-141]“It is well enough in one sense, but it will take you away,” said he,

[012-142]mollified.

[012-143]“Only for a short time. We should return in May.”

[012-144]“Well, Miss Melbury, it is a question for your father.”

[012-145]Winterborne walked with her nearly to her house. He had awaited her

[012-146]coming, mainly with the view of mentioning to her his proposal to have

[012-147]a Christmas party; but homely Christmas gatherings in the venerable and

[012-148]jovial Hintock style seemed so primitive and uncouth beside the lofty

[012-149]matters of her converse and thought that he refrained.

[012-150]As soon as she was gone he turned back towards the scene of his

[012-151]planting, and could not help saying to himself as he walked, that this

[012-152]engagement of his was a very unpromising business. Her outing to-day

[012-153]had not improved it. A woman who could go to Hintock House and be

[012-154]friendly with its mistress, enter into the views of its mistress, talk

[012-155]like her, and dress not much unlike her, why, she would hardly be

[012-156]contented with him, a yeoman, now immersed in tree-planting, even

[012-157]though he planted them well. “And yet she’s a true-hearted girl,” he

[012-158]said, thinking of her words about Hintock. “I must bring matters to a

[012-159]point, and there’s an end of it.”

[012-160]When he reached the plantation he found that Marty had come back, and

[012-161]dismissing Creedle, he went on planting silently with the girl as

[012-162]before.

[012-163]“Suppose, Marty,” he said, after a while, looking at her extended arm,

[012-164]upon which old scratches from briers showed themselves purple in the

[012-165]cold wind—“suppose you know a person, and want to bring that person to

[012-166]a good understanding with you, do you think a Christmas party of some

[012-167]sort is a warming-up thing, and likely to be useful in hastening on the

[012-168]matter?”

[012-169]“Is there to be dancing?”

[012-170]“There might be, certainly.”

[012-171]“Will He dance with She?”

[012-172]“Well, yes.”

[012-173]“Then it might bring things to a head, one way or the other; I won’t be

[012-174]the one to say which.”

[012-175]“It shall be done,” said Winterborne, not to her, though he spoke the

[012-176]words quite loudly. And as the day was nearly ended, he added, “Here,

[012-177]Marty, I’ll send up a man to plant the rest to-morrow. I’ve other

[012-178]things to think of just now.”

[012-179]She did not inquire what other things, for she had seen him walking

[012-180]with Grace Melbury. She looked towards the western sky, which was now

[012-181]aglow like some vast foundery wherein new worlds were being cast.

[012-182]Across it the bare bough of a tree stretched horizontally, revealing

[012-183]every twig against the red, and showing in dark profile every beck and

[012-184]movement of three pheasants that were settling themselves down on it in

[012-185]a row to roost.

[012-186]“It will be fine to-morrow,” said Marty, observing them with the

[012-187]vermilion light of the sun in the pupils of her eyes, “for they are

[012-188]a-croupied down nearly at the end of the bough. If it were going to be

[012-189]stormy they’d squeeze close to the trunk. The weather is almost all

[012-190]they have to think of, isn’t it, Mr. Winterborne? and so they must be

[012-191]lighter-hearted than we.”
