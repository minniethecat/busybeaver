# Section 42

[042-001]“Do sit down, [[george-melbury|Mr. Melbury]]. You have felled all the [[nature-in-the-woodlanders|trees]] that were to

[042-002]be purchased by you this season, except the oaks, I believe.”

[042-003]“Yes,” said Melbury.

[042-004]“How very nice! It must be so charming to work in the woods just now!”

[042-005]She was too careless to affect an interest in an extraneous person’s

[042-006]affairs so consummately as to deceive in the manner of the perfect

[042-007]social machine. Hence her words “very nice,” “so charming,” were

[042-008]uttered with a perfunctoriness that made them sound absurdly unreal.

[042-009]“Yes, yes,” said Melbury, in a reverie. He did not take a chair, and

[042-010]she also remained standing. Resting upon his stick, he began: “Mrs.

[042-011]Charmond, I have called upon a more serious matter—at least to me—than

[042-012]tree-throwing. And whatever mistakes I make in my manner of speaking

[042-013]upon it to you, madam, do me the justice to set ’em down to my want of

[042-014]practice, and not to my want of care.”

[042-015][[felice-charmond|Mrs. Charmond]] looked ill at ease. She might have begun to guess his

[042-016]meaning; but apart from that, she had such dread of contact with

[042-017]anything painful, harsh, or even earnest, that his preliminaries alone

[042-018]were enough to distress her. “Yes, what is it?” she said.

[042-019]“I am an old man,” said Melbury, “whom, somewhat late in life, God

[042-020]thought fit to bless with one child, and she a daughter. Her mother was

[042-021]a very dear wife to me, but she was taken away from us when the child

[042-022]was young, and the child became precious as the apple of my eye to me,

[042-023]for she was all I had left to [[love-and-desire|love]]. For her sake entirely I married as

[042-024]second wife a homespun woman who had been kind as a mother to her. In

[042-025]due time the question of her [[education-and-class-mobility|education]] came on, and I said, ‘I will

[042-026]educate the maid well, if I live upon bread to do it.’ Of her possible

[042-027][[marriage-in-the-woodlanders|marriage]] I could not bear to think, for it seemed like a death that she

[042-028]should cleave to another man, and grow to think his house her home

[042-029]rather than mine. But I saw it was the law of nature that this should

[042-030]be, and that it was for the maid’s happiness that she should have a

[042-031]home when I was gone; and I made up my mind without a murmur to help it

[042-032]on for her sake. In my youth I had wronged my dead friend, and to make

[042-033]amends I determined to give her, my most precious possession, to my

[042-034]friend’s son, seeing that they liked each other well. Things came about

[042-035]which made me doubt if it would be for my daughter’s happiness to do

[042-036]this, inasmuch as the young man was poor, and she was delicately

[042-037]reared. Another man came and paid court to her—one her equal in

[042-038]breeding and accomplishments; in every way it seemed to me that he only

[042-039]could give her the home which her training had made a necessity almost.

[042-040]I urged her on, and she married him. But, ma’am, a fatal mistake was at

[042-041]the root of my reckoning. I found that this well-born gentleman I had

[042-042]calculated on so surely was not stanch of heart, and that therein lay a

[042-043]danger of great sorrow for my daughter. Madam, he saw you, and you know

[042-044]the rest....I have come to make no demands—to utter no threats; I have

[042-045]come simply as a father in great grief about this only child, and I

[042-046]beseech you to deal kindly with my daughter, and to do nothing which

[042-047]can turn her husband’s heart away from her forever. Forbid him your

[042-048]presence, ma’am, and speak to him on his duty as one with your power

[042-049]over him well can do, and I am hopeful that the rent between them may

[042-050]be patched up. For it is not as if you would lose by so doing; your

[042-051]course is far higher than the courses of a simple professional man, and

[042-052]the gratitude you would win from me and mine by your kindness is more

[042-053]than I can say.”

[042-054]Mrs. Charmond had first rushed into a mood of indignation on

[042-055]comprehending Melbury’s story; hot and cold by turns, she had murmured,

[042-056]“Leave me, leave me!” But as he seemed to take no notice of this, his

[042-057]words began to influence her, and when he ceased speaking she said,

[042-058]with hurried, hot breath, “What has led you to think this of me? Who

[042-059]says I have won your daughter’s husband away from her? Some monstrous

[042-060]calumnies are afloat—of which I have known nothing until now!”

[042-061]Melbury started, and looked at her simply. “But surely, ma’am, you know

[042-062]the truth better than I?”

[042-063]Her features became a little pinched, and the touches of powder on her

[042-064]handsome face for the first time showed themselves as an extrinsic

[042-065]film. “Will you leave me to myself?” she said, with a faintness which

[042-066]suggested a guilty conscience. “This is so utterly unexpected—you

[042-067]obtain admission to my presence by misrepresentation—”

[042-068]“As God’s in heaven, ma’am, that’s not true. I made no pretence; and I

[042-069]thought in reason you would know why I had come. This gossip—”

[042-070]“I have heard nothing of it. Tell me of it, I say.”

[042-071]“Tell you, ma’am—not I. What the gossip is, no matter. What really is,

[042-072]you know. Set facts right, and the scandal will right of itself. But

[042-073]pardon me—I speak roughly; and I came to speak gently, to coax you, beg

[042-074]you to be my daughter’s friend. She loved you once, ma’am; you began by

[042-075]liking her. Then you dropped her without a reason, and it hurt her warm

[042-076]heart more than I can tell ye. But you were within your right as the

[042-077]superior, no doubt. But if you would consider her position now—surely,

[042-078]surely, you would do her no harm!”

[042-079]“Certainly I would do her no harm—I—” Melbury’s eye met hers. It was

[042-080]curious, but the allusion to Grace’s former love for her seemed to

[042-081]touch her more than all Melbury’s other arguments. “Oh, Melbury,” she

[042-082]burst out, “you have made me so unhappy! How could you come to me like

[042-083]this! It is too dreadful! Now go away—go, go!”

[042-084]“I will,” he said, in a husky tone.

[042-085]As soon as he was out of the room she went to a corner and there sat

[042-086]and writhed under an emotion in which hurt [[pride-and-humiliation|pride]] and vexation mingled

[042-087]with better sentiments.

[042-088]Mrs. Charmond’s mobile spirit was subject to these fierce periods of

[042-089]stress and storm. She had never so clearly perceived till now that her

[042-090]soul was being slowly invaded by a delirium which had brought about all

[042-091]this; that she was losing judgment and dignity under it, becoming an

[042-092]animated impulse only, a passion incarnate. A fascination had led her

[042-093]on; it was as if she had been seized by a hand of velvet; and this was

[042-094]where she found herself—overshadowed with sudden night, as if a tornado

[042-095]had passed by.

[042-096]While she sat, or rather crouched, unhinged by the interview,

[042-097]lunch-time came, and then the early afternoon, almost without her

[042-098]consciousness. Then “a strange gentleman who says it is not necessary

[042-099]to give his name,” was suddenly announced.

[042-100]“I cannot see him, whoever he may be. I am not at home to anybody.”

[042-101]She heard no more of her visitor; and shortly after, in an attempt to

[042-102]recover some mental serenity by violent physical exercise, she put on

[042-103]her hat and cloak and went out-of-doors, taking a path which led her up

[042-104]the slopes to the nearest spur of the wood. She disliked the woods, but

[042-105]they had the advantage of being a place in which she could walk

[042-106]comparatively unobserved.

[042-107]CHAPTER XXXIII.

[042-108]There was agitation to-day in the lives of all whom these matters

[042-109]concerned. It was not till the [[little-hintock|Hintock]] dinner-time—one o’clock—that

[042-110]Grace discovered her father’s absence from the house after a departure

[042-111]in the morning under somewhat unusual conditions. By a little reasoning

[042-112]and inquiry she was able to come to a conclusion on his destination,

[042-113]and to divine his errand.

[042-114]Her husband was absent, and her father did not return. He had, in

[042-115]truth, gone on to [[sherton-abbas|Sherton]] after the interview, but this Grace did not

[042-116]know. In an indefinite dread that something serious would arise out of

[042-117]Melbury’s visit by reason of the inequalities of temper and nervous

[042-118]irritation to which he was subject, something possibly that would bring

[042-119]her much more misery than accompanied her present negative state of

[042-120]mind, she left the house about three o’clock, and took a loitering walk

[042-121]in the woodland track by which she imagined he would come home. This

[042-122]track under the bare trees and over the cracking sticks, screened and

[042-123]roofed in from the outer world of wind and cloud by a net-work of

[042-124]boughs, led her slowly on till in time she had left the larger trees

[042-125]behind her and swept round into the coppice where [[giles-winterborne|Winterborne]] and his

[042-126]men were clearing the undergrowth.

[042-127]Had Giles’s attention been concentrated on his hurdles he would not

[042-128]have seen her; but ever since Melbury’s passage across the opposite

[042-129]glade in the morning he had been as uneasy and unsettled as Grace

[042-130]herself; and her advent now was the one appearance which, since her

[042-131]father’s avowal, could arrest him more than Melbury’s return with his

[042-132]tidings. Fearing that something might be the matter, he hastened up to

[042-133]her.

[042-134]She had not seen her old lover for a long time, and, too conscious of

[042-135]the late pranks of her heart, she could not behold him calmly. “I am

[042-136]only looking for my father,” she said, in an unnecessarily apologetic

[042-137]intonation.

[042-138]“I was looking for him too,” said Giles. “I think he may perhaps have

[042-139]gone on farther.”

[042-140]“Then you knew he was going to the House, Giles?” she said, turning her

[042-141]large tender eyes anxiously upon him. “Did he tell you what for?”

[042-142]Winterborne glanced doubtingly at her, and then softly hinted that her

[042-143]father had visited him the evening before, and that their old

[042-144]friendship was quite restored, on which she guessed the rest.

[042-145]“Oh, I am glad, indeed, that you two are friends again!” she cried. And

[042-146]then they stood facing each other, fearing each other, troubling each

[042-147]other’s souls. Grace experienced acute misery at the sight of these

[042-148]wood-cutting scenes, because she had estranged herself from them,

[042-149]craving, even to its defects and inconveniences, that homely sylvan

[042-150]life of her father which in the best probable succession of events

[042-151]would shortly be denied her.

[042-152]At a little distance, on the edge of the clearing, [[marty-south|Marty South]] was

[042-153]shaping spar-gads to take home for manufacture during the evenings.

[042-154]While Winterborne and [[grace-melbury|Mrs. Fitzpiers]] stood looking at her in their

[042-155]mutual embarrassment at each other’s presence, they beheld approaching

[042-156]the girl a lady in a dark fur mantle and a black hat, having a white

[042-157]veil tied picturesquely round it. She spoke to Marty, who turned and

[042-158]courtesied, and the lady fell into conversation with her. It was Mrs.

[042-159]Charmond.

[042-160]On leaving her house, Mrs. Charmond had walked on and onward under the

[042-161]fret and fever of her mind with more vigor than she was accustomed to

[042-162]show in her normal moods—a fever which the solace of a cigarette did

[042-163]not entirely allay. Reaching the coppice, she listlessly observed Marty

[042-164]at work, threw away her cigarette, and came near. Chop, chop, chop,

[042-165]went Marty’s little billhook with never more assiduity, till Mrs.

[042-166]Charmond spoke.

[042-167]“Who is that young lady I see talking to the woodman yonder?” she

[042-168]asked.

[042-169]“Mrs. [[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Fitzpiers]], ma’am,” said Marty.

[042-170]“Oh,” said Mrs. Charmond, with something like a start; for she had not

[042-171]recognized Grace at that distance. “And the man she is talking to?”

[042-172]“That’s Mr. Winterborne.”

[042-173]A redness stole into Marty’s face as she mentioned Giles’s name, which

[042-174]Mrs. Charmond did not fail to notice informed her of the state of the

[042-175]girl’s heart. “Are you engaged to him?” she asked, softly.
