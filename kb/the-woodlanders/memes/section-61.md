# Section 61

[061-001]There the practical part ended; he then went on—

[061-002]“My last year of experience has added ten years to my age, dear Grace

[061-003]and dearest wife that ever erring man undervalued. You may be

[061-004]absolutely indifferent to what I say, but let me say it: I have never

[061-005]loved any woman alive or dead as I [[love-and-desire|love]], respect, and honor you at this

[061-006]present moment. What you told me in the [[pride-and-humiliation|pride]] and haughtiness of your

[061-007]heart I never believed [this, by the way, was not strictly true]; but

[061-008]even if I had believed it, it could never have estranged me from you.

[061-009]Is there any use in telling you—no, there is not—that I dream of your

[061-010]ripe lips more frequently than I say my prayers; that the old familiar

[061-011]rustle of your dress often returns upon my mind till it distracts me?

[061-012]If you could condescend even only to see me again you would be

[061-013]breathing life into a corpse. My pure, pure Grace, modest as a

[061-014]turtledove, how came I ever to possess you? For the sake of being

[061-015]present in your mind on this lovers’ day, I think I would almost rather

[061-016]have you hate me a little than not think of me at all. You may call my

[061-017]fancies whimsical; but remember, sweet, lost one, that ‘[[nature-in-the-woodlanders|nature]] is one

[061-018]in love, and where ’tis fine it sends some instance of itself.’ I will

[061-019]not intrude upon you further now. Make me a little bit happy by sending

[061-020]back one line to say that you will consent, at any rate, to a short

[061-021]interview. I will meet you and leave you as a mere acquaintance, if you

[061-022]will only afford me this slight means of making a few explanations, and

[061-023]of putting my position before you. Believe me, in spite of all you may

[061-024]do or feel,

[061-025]Your lover always (once your husband),

[061-026]“E.F.”

[061-027]It was, oddly enough, the first occasion, or nearly the first on which

[061-028]Grace had ever received a love-letter from him, his courtship having

[061-029]taken place under conditions which rendered letter-writing unnecessary.

[061-030]Its perusal, therefore, had a certain novelty for her. She thought

[061-031]that, upon the whole, he wrote love-letters very well. But the chief

[061-032]rational interest of the letter to the reflective Grace lay in the

[061-033][[fate-and-free-will|chance]] that such a meeting as he proposed would afford her of setting

[061-034]her doubts at rest, one way or the other, on her actual share in

[061-035][[giles-winterborne|Winterborne]]’s death. The relief of consulting a skilled mind, the one

[061-036]professional man who had seen Giles at that time, would be immense. As

[061-037]for that statement that she had uttered in her disdainful grief, which

[061-038]at the time she had regarded as her triumph, she was quite prepared to

[061-039]admit to him that his belief was the true one; for in wronging herself

[061-040]as she did when she made it, she had done what to her was a far more

[061-041]serious thing, wronged Winterborne’s memory.

[061-042]Without consulting her father, or any one in the house or out of it,

[061-043]Grace replied to the letter. She agreed to meet [[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Fitzpiers]] on two

[061-044]conditions, of which the first was that the place of meeting should be

[061-045]the top of [[rubdown-hill|Rubdown Hill]], the second that he would not object to Marty

[061-046]South accompanying her.

[061-047]Whatever part, much or little, there may have been in Fitzpiers’s

[061-048]so-called valentine to his wife, he felt a delight as of the bursting

[061-049]of spring when her brief reply came. It was one of the few pleasures

[061-050]that he had experienced of late years at all resembling those of his

[061-051]early youth. He promptly replied that he accepted the conditions, and

[061-052]named the day and hour at which he would be on the spot she mentioned.

[061-053]A few minutes before three on the appointed day found him climbing the

[061-054]well-known hill, which had been the axis of so many critical movements

[061-055]in their lives during his residence at [[little-hintock|Hintock]].

[061-056]The sight of each homely and well-remembered object swelled the regret

[061-057]that seldom left him now. Whatever paths might lie open to his future,

[061-058]the soothing shades of Hintock were forbidden him forever as a

[061-059]permanent dwelling-place.

[061-060]He longed for the society of Grace. But to lay offerings on her

[061-061]slighted altar was his first aim, and until her propitiation was

[061-062]complete he would constrain her in no way to return to him. The least

[061-063]reparation that he could make, in a case where he would gladly have

[061-064]made much, would be to let her feel herself absolutely free to choose

[061-065]between living with him and without him.

[061-066]Moreover, a subtlist in emotions, he cultivated as under glasses

[061-067]strange and mournful pleasures that he would not willingly let die just

[061-068]at present. To show any forwardness in suggesting a _modus vivendi_ to

[061-069]Grace would be to put an end to these exotics. To be the vassal of her

[061-070]sweet will for a time, he demanded no more, and found solace in the

[061-071]contemplation of the soft miseries she caused him.

[061-072]Approaching the hill-top with a mind strung to these notions, Fitzpiers

[061-073]discerned a gay procession of people coming over the crest, and was not

[061-074]long in perceiving it to be a [[marriage-in-the-woodlanders|wedding]]-party.

[061-075]Though the wind was keen the [[gender-roles|women]] were in light attire, and the

[061-076]flowered waistcoats of the men had a pleasing vividness of pattern.

[061-077]Each of the gentler ones clung to the arm of her partner so tightly as

[061-078]to have with him one step, rise, swing, gait, almost one centre of

[061-079]gravity. In the buxom bride Fitzpiers recognized no other than [[suke-damson|Suke]]

[061-080]Damson, who in her light gown looked a giantess; the small husband

[061-081]beside her he saw to be Tim Tangs.

[061-082]Fitzpiers could not escape, for they had seen him; though of all the

[061-083]beauties of the world whom he did not wish to meet Suke was the chief.

[061-084]But he put the best face on the matter that he could and came on, the

[061-085]approaching company evidently discussing him and his separation from

[061-086][[grace-melbury|Mrs. Fitzpiers]]. As the couples closed upon him he expressed his

[061-087]congratulations.

[061-088]“We be just walking round the parishes to show ourselves a bit,” said

[061-089]Tim. “First we het across to Delborough, then athwart to here, and from

[061-090]here we go to Rubdown and Millshot, and then round by the cross-roads

[061-091]home. Home says I, but it won’t be that long! We be off next month.”

[061-092]“Indeed. Where to?”

[061-093]Tim informed him that they were going to New Zealand. Not but that he

[061-094]would have been contented with Hintock, but his wife was ambitious and

[061-095]wanted to leave, so he had given way.

[061-096]“Then good-by,” said Fitzpiers; “I may not see you again.” He shook

[061-097]hands with Tim and turned to the bride. “Good-by, Suke,” he said,

[061-098]taking her hand also. “I wish you and your husband prosperity in the

[061-099]country you have chosen.” With this he left them, and hastened on to

[061-100]his appointment.

[061-101]The wedding-party re-formed and resumed march likewise. But in

[061-102]restoring his arm to Suke, Tim noticed that her full and blooming

[061-103]countenance had undergone a change. “Holloa! me dear—what’s the

[061-104]matter?” said Tim.

[061-105]“Nothing to speak o’,” said she. But to give the lie to her assertion

[061-106]she was seized with lachrymose twitches, that soon produced a dribbling

[061-107]face.

[061-108]“How—what the devil’s this about!” exclaimed the bridegroom.

[061-109]“She’s a little wee bit overcome, poor dear!” said the first

[061-110]bridesmaid, unfolding her handkerchief and wiping Suke’s eyes.

[061-111]“I never did like parting from people!” said Suke, as soon as she could

[061-112]speak.

[061-113]“Why him in particular?”

[061-114]“Well—he’s such a clever doctor, that ’tis a thousand pities we sha’n’t

[061-115]see him any more! There’ll be no such clever doctor as he in New

[061-116]Zealand, if I should require one; and the thought o’t got the better of

[061-117]my feelings!”

[061-118]They walked on, but Tim’s face had grown rigid and pale, for he

[061-119]recalled slight circumstances, disregarded at the time of their

[061-120]occurrence. The former boisterous laughter of [[the-wedding-of-grace-and-fitzpiers|the wedding]]-party at the

[061-121]groomsman’s jokes was heard ringing through the woods no more.

[061-122]By this time Fitzpiers had advanced on his way to the top of the hill,

[061-123]where he saw two figures emerging from the bank on the right hand.

[061-124]These were the expected ones, Grace and [[marty-south|Marty South]], who had evidently

[061-125]come there by a short and [[secrecy-and-concealment|secret]] path through the wood. Grace was

[061-126]muffled up in her winter dress, and he thought that she had never

[061-127]looked so seductive as at this moment, in the noontide bright but

[061-128]heatless sun, and the keen wind, and the purplish-gray masses of

[061-129]brushwood around.

[061-130]Fitzpiers continued to regard the nearing picture, till at length their

[061-131]glances met for a moment, when she demurely sent off hers at a tangent

[061-132]and gave him the benefit of her three-quarter face, while with

[061-133]courteous completeness of conduct he lifted his hat in a large arc.

[061-134]Marty dropped behind; and when Fitzpiers held out his hand, Grace

[061-135]touched it with her fingers.

[061-136]“I have agreed to be here mostly because I wanted to ask you something

[061-137]important,” said Mrs. Fitzpiers, her intonation modulating in a

[061-138]direction that she had not quite wished it to take.

[061-139]“I am most attentive,” said her husband. “Shall we take to the wood for

[061-140]privacy?”

[061-141]Grace demurred, and Fitzpiers gave in, and they kept the public road.

[061-142]At any rate she would take his arm? This also was gravely negatived,

[061-143]the refusal being audible to Marty.

[061-144]“Why not?” he inquired.

[061-145]“Oh, Mr. Fitzpiers—how can you ask?”

[061-146]“Right, right,” said he, his effusiveness shrivelled up.

[061-147]As they walked on she returned to her inquiry. “It is about a matter

[061-148]that may perhaps be unpleasant to you. But I think I need not consider

[061-149]that too carefully.”

[061-150]“Not at all,” said Fitzpiers, heroically.

[061-151]She then took him back to the time of poor Winterborne’s death, and

[061-152]related the precise circumstances amid which his fatal illness had come

[061-153]upon him, particularizing the dampness of the shelter to which he had

[061-154]betaken himself, his concealment from her of the hardships that he was

[061-155]undergoing, all that he had put up with, all that he had done for her

[061-156]in his scrupulous considerateness. The retrospect brought her to tears

[061-157]as she asked him if he thought that the sin of having driven him to his

[061-158]death was upon her.

[061-159]Fitzpiers could hardly help showing his satisfaction at what her

[061-160]narrative indirectly revealed, the actual harmlessness of an escapade

[061-161]with her lover, which had at first, by her own showing, looked so

[061-162]grave, and he did not care to inquire whether that harmlessness had

[061-163]been the result of aim or of accident. With regard to her question, he

[061-164]declared that in his judgment no human being could answer it. He

[061-165]thought that upon the whole the balance of probabilities turned in her

[061-166]favor. Winterborne’s apparent strength, during the last months of his

[061-167]life, must have been delusive. It had often occurred that after a first

[061-168]attack of that insidious disease a person’s apparent recovery was a

[061-169]physiological mendacity.

[061-170]The relief which came to Grace lay almost as much in sharing her

[061-171]knowledge of the particulars with an intelligent mind as in the

[061-172]assurances Fitzpiers gave her. “Well, then, to put this case before

[061-173]you, and obtain your professional opinion, was chiefly why I consented

[061-174]to come here to-day,” said she, when he had reached the aforesaid

[061-175]conclusion.

[061-176]“For no other reason at all?” he asked, ruefully.

[061-177]“It was nearly the whole.”

[061-178]They stood and looked over a gate at twenty or thirty starlings feeding

[061-179]in the grass, and he started the talk again by saying, in a low voice,

[061-180]“And yet I love you more than ever I loved you in my life.”

[061-181]Grace did not move her eyes from the birds, and folded her delicate

[061-182]lips as if to keep them in subjection.

[061-183]“It is a different kind of love altogether,” said he. “Less passionate;

[061-184]more profound. It has nothing to do with the material conditions of the

[061-185]object at all; much to do with her character and goodness, as revealed

[061-186]by closer observation. ‘Love talks with better knowledge, and knowledge

[061-187]with dearer love.’”
