# Grammer Oliver

Grammer Oliver is the aged servant of the Melbury household in Little Hintock. Her illness in the early chapters serves as the catalyst for introducing Dr. Edred Fitzpiers to the Melbury family, and Fitzpiers's extraordinary compact with her — proposing to exchange the rest of his own life for her recovery — sets in motion one of the novel's most unsettling meditations on the commodification of life and the physician's power over mortality.

## 身份与背景

Grammer Oliver — "Grammer" being a West Country dialectal variant of "Grandmother," denoting her age and status rather than a literal familial relation — serves as a domestic in the household of [[George Melbury|George Melbury]] at Little Hintock [005-071]. Her name and role evoke the deep-rooted tradition of lifelong retainers in rural English households, where servants became quasi-family members over decades of service. She is of an age that commands respect from both the Melbury family and the wider Hintock community.

## 性格与特征

Oliver embodies the canny, plain-speaking character of the old Wessex servant class. She is not awed by Fitzpiers's medical pretensions — when he first arrives, she greets him with the matter-of-fact observation that it "do do... that's late" [005-071] [005-075]. Despite her age and infirmity, she retains a sharp judgement and a decisiveness that contrasts with the wavering and emotionally driven decisions of her social superiors. When Fitzpiers offers his compact, her response is firm: "I will not have him" [022-076], demonstrating a moral clarity that the educated characters in the novel often lack.

## 关键关系

- **[[George Melbury|George Melbury]]**：Her employer and the master of Melbury House. Oliver has served the family for decades and occupies a position of trust and affection within the household [016-123].
- **[[Grace Melbury|Grace Melbury]]**：The daughter of the household whom Oliver has known since childhood. Grace is deeply affected by Oliver's illness and the compact with Fitzpiers, which disturbs her sense of moral order [022-142].
- **[[Dr. Edred Fitzpiers]]**：The physician who attends Oliver during her illness. Fitzpiers's proposal — a "compact" by which he would exchange the remainder of his own life for hers — is the novel's first major revelation of his character as both brilliant and morally transgressive [022-155] [022-179].

## 叙事线索

Oliver first appears at the Melbury household when Fitzpiers arrives for breakfast, serving as the household's domestic anchor [006-035]. Her presence is a constant throughout the early chapters — winding the clock, bringing wood for the fire, announcing visitors [008-133] [044-141]. She falls ill, and her condition deteriorates to a point where Fitzpiers is summoned [022-056]. It is during this medical crisis that Fitzpiers reveals to Grace the nature of the compact he had once proposed to Oliver: that he would shorten his own life to prolong hers, an arrangement Oliver had refused [022-179] [024-018].

The compact episode becomes a critical turning point in Grace's perception of Fitzpiers — she is simultaneously fascinated and horrified by the implications. The idea that a life can be bartered, measured, and exchanged like a commodity introduces a thread of moral unease that will persist through the novel's treatment of love, medicine, and social transaction.

## 意义与解读

Grammer Oliver functions as a moral touchstone in the novel. Her plain refusal of Fitzpiers's compact — "I will not have him" — represents the instinctive moral wisdom of the rural poor, who recognize a transaction too grotesque to contemplate even when it benefits them directly. Hardy juxtaposes this against the educated classes' capacity for rationalizing morally dubious arrangements. Oliver's name itself — "Grammer," a dialect word for grandmother — anchors her in a pre-modern world of folk wisdom and oral tradition, standing in contrast to Fitzpiers's imported Continental rationalism and the novel's persistent theme of the old rural order giving way to new professional classes.
