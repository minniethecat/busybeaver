# Section 30

[030-001][[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Fitzpiers]] did not stay more than an hour, but that time had apparently

[030-002]advanced his sentiments towards Grace, once and for all, from a vaguely

[030-003]liquescent to an organic shape. She would not have accompanied him to

[030-004]the door in response to his whispered “Come!” if her mother had not

[030-005]said in a matter-of-fact way, “Of course, Grace; go to the door with

[030-006]Mr. Fitzpiers.” Accordingly Grace went, both her parents remaining in

[030-007]the room. When the young pair were in the great brick-floored hall the

[030-008]lover took the girl’s hand in his, drew it under his arm, and thus led

[030-009]her on to the door, where he stealthily kissed her.

[030-010]She broke from him trembling, blushed and turned aside, hardly knowing

[030-011]how things had advanced to this. Fitzpiers drove off, kissing his hand

[030-012]to her, and waving it to [[george-melbury|Melbury]] who was visible through the window.

[030-013]Her father returned the surgeon’s action with a great flourish of his

[030-014]own hand and a satisfied smile.

[030-015]The intoxication that Fitzpiers had, as usual, produced in Grace’s

[030-016]brain during the visit passed off somewhat with his withdrawal. She

[030-017]felt like a woman who did not know what she had been doing for the

[030-018]previous hour, but supposed with trepidation that the afternoon’s

[030-019]proceedings, though vague, had amounted to an engagement between

[030-020]herself and the handsome, coercive, irresistible Fitzpiers.

[030-021]This visit was a type of many which followed it during the long summer

[030-022]days of that year. Grace was borne along upon a stream of reasonings,

[030-023]arguments, and persuasions, supplemented, it must be added, by

[030-024]inclinations of her own at times. No woman is without aspirations,

[030-025]which may be innocent enough within certain limits; and Grace had been

[030-026]so trained socially, and educated intellectually, as to see clearly

[030-027]enough a pleasure in the position of wife to such a man as Fitzpiers.

[030-028]His material standing of itself, either present or future, had little

[030-029]in it to give her ambition, but the possibilities of a refined and

[030-030]cultivated inner life, of subtle psychological intercourse, had their

[030-031]charm. It was this rather than any vulgar idea of marrying well which

[030-032]caused her to float with the current, and to yield to the immense

[030-033]influence which Fitzpiers exercised over her whenever she shared his

[030-034]society.

[030-035]Any observer would shrewdly have prophesied that whether or not she

[030-036]loved him as yet in the ordinary sense, she was pretty sure to do so in

[030-037]time.

[030-038]One evening just before dusk they had taken a rather long walk

[030-039]together, and for a short cut homeward passed through the shrubberies

[030-040]of [[hintock-house|Hintock House]]—still deserted, and still blankly confronting with its

[030-041]sightless shuttered windows the surrounding foliage and slopes. Grace

[030-042]was tired, and they approached the wall, and sat together on one of the

[030-043]stone sills—still warm with the sun that had been pouring its rays upon

[030-044]them all the afternoon.

[030-045]“This place would just do for us, would it not, dearest,” said her

[030-046]betrothed, as they sat, turning and looking idly at the old facade.

[030-047]“Oh yes,” said Grace, plainly showing that no such fancy had ever

[030-048]crossed her mind. “She is away from home still,” Grace added in a

[030-049]minute, rather sadly, for she could not forget that she had somehow

[030-050]lost the valuable friendship of the lady of this bower.

[030-051]“Who is?—oh, you mean [[felice-charmond|Mrs. Charmond]]. Do you know, dear, that at one

[030-052]time I thought you lived here.”

[030-053]“Indeed!” said Grace. “How was that?”

[030-054]He explained, as far as he could do so without mentioning his

[030-055]disappointment at finding it was otherwise; and then went on: “Well,

[030-056]never mind that. Now I want to ask you something. There is one detail

[030-057]of our [[marriage-in-the-woodlanders|wedding]] which I am sure you will leave to me. My inclination is

[030-058]not to be married at the horrid little church here, with all the yokels

[030-059]staring round at us, and a droning parson reading.”

[030-060]“Where, then, can it be? At a church in town?”

[030-061]“No. Not at a church at all. At a registry office. It is a quieter,

[030-062]snugger, and more convenient place in every way.”

[030-063]“Oh,” said she, with real distress. “How can I be married except at

[030-064]church, and with all my dear friends round me?”

[030-065]“Yeoman [[giles-winterborne|Winterborne]] among them.”

[030-066]“Yes—why not? You know there was nothing serious between him and me.”

[030-067]“You see, dear, a noisy bell-ringing marriage at church has this

[030-068]objection in our case: it would be a thing of report a long way round.

[030-069]Now I would gently, as gently as possible, indicate to you how

[030-070]inadvisable such publicity would be if we leave [[little-hintock|Hintock]], and I purchase

[030-071]the practice that I contemplate purchasing at [[budmouth|Budmouth]]—hardly more than

[030-072]twenty miles off. Forgive my saying that it will be far better if

[030-073]nobody there knows where you come from, nor anything about your

[030-074]parents. Your beauty and knowledge and manners will carry you anywhere

[030-075]if you are not hampered by such retrospective criticism.”

[030-076]“But could it not be a quiet ceremony, even at church?” she pleaded.

[030-077]“I don’t see the necessity of going there!” he said, a trifle

[030-078]impatiently. “Marriage is a civil contract, and the shorter and simpler

[030-079]it is made the better. People don’t go to church when they take a

[030-080]house, or even when they make a will.”

[030-081]“Oh, Edgar—I don’t like to hear you speak like that.”

[030-082]“Well, well—I didn’t mean to. But I have mentioned as much to your

[030-083]father, who has made no objection; and why should you?”

[030-084]She gave way, deeming the point one on which she ought to allow

[030-085]sentiment to give way to policy—if there were indeed policy in his

[030-086]plan. But she was indefinably depressed as they walked homeward.

[030-087]CHAPTER XXIV.

[030-088]He left her at the door of her father’s house. As he receded, and was

[030-089]clasped out of sight by the filmy shades, he impressed Grace as a man

[030-090]who hardly appertained to her existence at all. Cleverer, greater than

[030-091]herself, one outside her mental orbit, as she considered him, he seemed

[030-092]to be her ruler rather than her equal, protector, and dear familiar

[030-093]friend.

[030-094]The disappointment she had experienced at his wish, the shock given to

[030-095]her girlish sensibilities by his irreverent views of marriage, together

[030-096]with the sure and near approach of the day fixed for committing her

[030-097]future to his keeping, made her so restless that she could scarcely

[030-098]sleep at all that night. She rose when the sparrows began to walk out

[030-099]of the roof-holes, sat on the floor of her room in the dim light, and

[030-100]by-and-by peeped out behind the window-curtains. It was even now day

[030-101]out-of-doors, though the tones of morning were feeble and wan, and it

[030-102]was long before the sun would be perceptible in this overshadowed vale.

[030-103]Not a sound came from any of the out-houses as yet. The tree-trunks,

[030-104]the road, the out-buildings, the garden, every object wore that aspect

[030-105]of mesmeric fixity which the suspensive quietude of daybreak lends to

[030-106]such scenes. Outside her window helpless immobility seemed to be

[030-107]combined with intense consciousness; a meditative inertness possessed

[030-108]all things, oppressively contrasting with her own active emotions.

[030-109]Beyond the road were some cottage roofs and orchards; over these roofs

[030-110]and over the apple-[[nature-in-the-woodlanders|trees]] behind, high up the slope, and backed by the

[030-111]plantation on the crest, was the house yet occupied by her future

[030-112]husband, the rough-cast front showing whitely through its creepers. The

[030-113]window-shutters were closed, the bedroom curtains closely drawn, and

[030-114]not the thinnest coil of smoke rose from the rugged chimneys.

[030-115]Something broke the stillness. The front door of the house she was

[030-116]gazing at opened softly, and there came out into the porch a female

[030-117]figure, wrapped in a large shawl, beneath which was visible the white

[030-118]skirt of a long loose garment. A gray arm, stretching from within the

[030-119]porch, adjusted the shawl over the woman’s shoulders; it was withdrawn

[030-120]and disappeared, the door closing behind her.

[030-121]The woman went quickly down the box-edged path between the raspberries

[030-122]and currants, and as she walked her well-developed form and gait

[030-123]betrayed her individuality. It was [[suke-damson|Suke Damson]], the affianced one of

[030-124]simple young Tim Tangs. At the bottom of the garden she entered the

[030-125]shelter of the tall hedge, and only the top of her head could be seen

[030-126]hastening in the direction of her own dwelling.

[030-127]Grace had recognized, or thought she recognized, in the gray arm

[030-128]stretching from the porch, the sleeve of a dressing-gown which Mr.

[030-129]Fitzpiers had been wearing on her own memorable visit to him. Her face

[030-130]fired red. She had just before thought of dressing herself and taking a

[030-131]lonely walk under the trees, so coolly green this early morning; but

[030-132]she now sat down on her bed and fell into reverie. It seemed as if

[030-133]hardly any time had passed when she heard the household moving briskly

[030-134]about, and breakfast preparing down-stairs; though, on rousing herself

[030-135]to robe and descend, she found that the sun was throwing his rays

[030-136]completely over the tree-tops, a progress of natural phenomena denoting

[030-137]that at least three hours had elapsed since she last looked out of the

[030-138]window.

[030-139]When attired she searched about the house for her father; she found him

[030-140]at last in the garden, stooping to examine the potatoes for signs of

[030-141]disease. Hearing her rustle, he stood up and stretched his back and

[030-142]arms, saying, “Morning t’ye, Gracie. I congratulate ye. It is only a

[030-143]month to-day to the time!”

[030-144]She did not answer, but, without lifting her dress, waded between the

[030-145]dewy rows of tall potato-green into the middle of the plot where he

[030-146]was.

[030-147]“I have been thinking very much about my position this morning—ever

[030-148]since it was light,” she began, excitedly, and trembling so that she

[030-149]could hardly stand. “And I feel it is a false one. I wish not to marry

[030-150]Mr. Fitzpiers. I wish not to marry anybody; but I’ll marry Giles

[030-151]Winterborne if you say I must as an alternative.”

[030-152]Her father’s face settled into rigidity, he turned pale, and came

[030-153]deliberately out of the plot before he answered her. She had never seen

[030-154]him look so incensed before.

[030-155]“Now, hearken to me,” he said. “There’s a time for a woman to alter her

[030-156]mind; and there’s a time when she can no longer alter it, if she has

[030-157]any right eye to her parents’ honor and the seemliness of things. That

[030-158]time has come. I won’t say to ye, you _shall_ marry him. But I will say

[030-159]that if you refuse, I shall forever be ashamed and a-weary of ye as a

[030-160]daughter, and shall look upon you as the hope of my life no more. What

[030-161]do you know about life and what it can bring forth, and how you ought

[030-162]to act to lead up to best ends? Oh, you are an ungrateful maid, Grace;

[030-163]you’ve seen that fellow Giles, and he has got over ye; that’s where the

[030-164][[secrecy-and-concealment|secret]] lies, I’ll warrant me!”

[030-165]“No, father, no! It is not Giles—it is something I cannot tell you of—”

[030-166]“Well, make fools of us all; make us laughing-stocks; break it off;

[030-167]have your own way.”

[030-168]“But who knows of the engagement as yet? how can breaking it disgrace

[030-169]you?”

[030-170]Melbury then by degrees admitted that he had mentioned the engagement

[030-171]to this acquaintance and to that, till she perceived that in his

[030-172]restlessness and [[pride-and-humiliation|pride]] he had published it everywhere. She went

[030-173]dismally away to a bower of laurel at the top of the garden. Her father

[030-174]followed her.

[030-175]“It is that Giles Winterborne!” he said, with an upbraiding gaze at

[030-176]her.

[030-177]“No, it is not; though for that matter you encouraged him once,” she

[030-178]said, troubled to the verge of despair. “It is not Giles, it is Mr.

[030-179]Fitzpiers.”

[030-180]“You’ve had a tiff—a lovers’ tiff—that’s all, I suppose!”

[030-181]“It is some woman—”

[030-182]“Ay, ay; you are jealous. The old story. Don’t tell me. Now do you bide

[030-183]here. I’ll send Fitzpiers to you. I saw him smoking in front of his

[030-184]house but a minute by-gone.”
