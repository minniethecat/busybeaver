# Section 36

[036-001]“What does it all mean?” he asked.

[036-002]She sat in an easy-chair, her face being turned away. “Oh,” she

[036-003]murmured, “it is because the world is so dreary outside. Sorrow and

[036-004]bitterness in the sky, and floods of agonized tears beating against the

[036-005]panes. I lay awake last night, and I could hear the scrape of snails

[036-006]creeping up the window-glass; it was so sad! My eyes were so heavy this

[036-007]morning that I could have wept my life away. I cannot bear you to see

[036-008]my face; I keep it away from you purposely. Oh! why were we given

[036-009]hungry hearts and wild desires if we have to live in a world like this?

[036-010]Why should Death only lend what Life is compelled to borrow—rest?

[036-011]Answer that, Dr. [[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Fitzpiers]].”

[036-012]“You must eat of a second tree of knowledge before _you_ can do it,

[036-013][[felice-charmond|Felice Charmond]].”

[036-014]“Then, when my emotions have exhausted themselves, I become full of

[036-015]fears, till I think I shall die for very fear. The terrible

[036-016]insistencies of society—how severe they are, and cold and

[036-017]inexorable—ghastly towards those who are made of wax and not of stone.

[036-018]Oh, I am afraid of them; a stab for this error, and a stab for

[036-019]that—correctives and regulations framed that society may tend to

[036-020]perfection—an end which I don’t care for in the least. Yet for this,

[036-021]all I do care for has to be stunted and starved.”

[036-022]Fitzpiers had seated himself near her. “What sets you in this mournful

[036-023]mood?” he asked, gently. (In reality he knew that it was the result of

[036-024]a loss of tone from staying in-doors so much, but he did not say so.)

[036-025]“My reflections. Doctor, you must not come here any more. They begin to

[036-026]think it a farce already. I say you must come no more. There—don’t be

[036-027]angry with me;” and she jumped up, pressed his hand, and looked

[036-028]anxiously at him. “It is necessary. It is best for both you and me.”

[036-029]“But,” said Fitzpiers, gloomily, “what have we done?”

[036-030]“Done—we have done nothing. Perhaps we have thought the more. However,

[036-031]it is all vexation. I am going away to [[middleton-abbey|Middleton Abbey]], near

[036-032][[shottsford-forum|Shottsford]], where a relative of my late husband lives, who is confined

[036-033]to her bed. The engagement was made in [[london-in-the-woodlanders|London]], and I can’t get out of

[036-034]it. Perhaps it is for the best that I go there till all this is past.

[036-035]When are you going to enter on your new practice, and leave [[little-hintock|Hintock]]

[036-036]behind forever, with your pretty wife on your arm?”

[036-037]“I have refused the opportunity. I [[love-and-desire|love]] this place too well to depart.”

[036-038]“You _have?_” she said, regarding him with wild uncertainty. “Why do

[036-039]you ruin yourself in that way? Great Heaven, what have I done!”

[036-040]“Nothing. Besides, you are going away.”

[036-041]“Oh yes; but only to Middleton Abbey for a month or two. Yet perhaps I

[036-042]shall gain strength there—particularly strength of mind—I require it.

[036-043]And when I come back I shall be a new woman; and you can come and see

[036-044]me safely then, and bring your wife with you, and we’ll be friends—she

[036-045]and I. Oh, how this shutting up of one’s self does lead to indulgence

[036-046]in idle sentiments. I shall not wish you to give your attendance to me

[036-047]after to-day. But I am glad that you are not going away—if your

[036-048]remaining does not injure your prospects at all.”

[036-049]As soon as he had left the room the mild friendliness she had preserved

[036-050]in her tone at parting, the playful sadness with which she had

[036-051]conversed with him, equally departed from her. She became as heavy as

[036-052]lead—just as she had been before he arrived. Her whole being seemed to

[036-053]dissolve in a sad powerlessness to do anything, and the sense of it

[036-054]made her lips tremulous and her closed eyes wet. His footsteps again

[036-055]startled her, and she turned round.

[036-056]“I returned for a moment to tell you that the evening is going to be

[036-057]fine. The sun is shining; so do open your curtains and put out those

[036-058]lights. Shall I do it for you?”

[036-059]“Please—if you don’t mind.”

[036-060]He drew back the window-curtains, whereupon the red glow of the lamp

[036-061]and the two candle-flames became almost invisible with the flood of

[036-062]late autumn sunlight that poured in. “Shall I come round to you?” he

[036-063]asked, her back being towards him.

[036-064]“No,” she replied.

[036-065]“Why not?”

[036-066]“Because I am crying, and I don’t want to see you.”

[036-067]He stood a moment irresolute, and regretted that he had killed the

[036-068]rosy, passionate lamplight by opening the curtains and letting in

[036-069]garish day.

[036-070]“Then I am going,” he said.

[036-071]“Very well,” she answered, stretching one hand round to him, and

[036-072]patting her eyes with a handkerchief held in the other.

[036-073]“Shall I write a line to you at—”

[036-074]“No, no.” A gentle reasonableness came into her tone as she added, “It

[036-075]must not be, you know. It won’t do.”

[036-076]“Very well. Good-by.” The next moment he was gone.

[036-077]In the evening, with listless adroitness, she encouraged the maid who

[036-078]dressed her for dinner to speak of Dr. Fitzpiers’s [[marriage-in-the-woodlanders|marriage]].

[036-079]“[[grace-melbury|Mrs. Fitzpiers]] was once supposed to favor Mr. [[giles-winterborne|Winterborne]],” said the

[036-080]young woman.

[036-081]“And why didn’t she marry him?” said Mrs. Charmond.

[036-082]“Because, you see, ma’am, he lost his houses.”

[036-083]“Lost his houses? How came he to do that?”

[036-084]“The houses were held on lives, and the lives dropped, and your agent

[036-085]wouldn’t renew them, though it is said that Mr. Winterborne had a very

[036-086]good claim. That’s as I’ve heard it, ma’am, and it was through it that

[036-087]the match was broke off.”

[036-088]Being just then distracted by a dozen emotions, Mrs. Charmond sunk into

[036-089]a mood of dismal self-reproach. “In refusing that poor man his

[036-090]reasonable request,” she said to herself, “I foredoomed my rejuvenated

[036-091]girlhood’s romance. Who would have thought such a business matter could

[036-092]have nettled my own heart like this? Now for a winter of regrets and

[036-093]agonies and useless wishes, till I forget him in the spring. Oh! I am

[036-094]glad I am going away.”

[036-095]She left her chamber and went down to dine with a sigh. On the stairs

[036-096]she stood opposite the large window for a moment, and looked out upon

[036-097]the lawn. It was not yet quite dark. Half-way up the steep green slope

[036-098]confronting her stood [[timothy-tangs|old Timothy Tangs]], who was shortening his way

[036-099]homeward by clambering here where there was no road, and in opposition

[036-100]to express orders that no path was to be made there. Tangs had

[036-101]momentarily stopped to take a pinch of snuff; but observing Mrs.

[036-102]Charmond gazing at him, he hastened to get over the top out of hail.

[036-103]His precipitancy made him miss his footing, and he rolled like a barrel

[036-104]to the bottom, his snuffbox rolling in front of him.

[036-105]Her indefinite, idle, impossible passion for Fitzpiers; her

[036-106]constitutional cloud of misery; the sorrowful drops that still hung

[036-107]upon her eyelashes, all made way for the incursive mood started by the

[036-108]spectacle. She burst into an immoderate fit of laughter, her very gloom

[036-109]of the previous hour seeming to render it the more uncontrollable. It

[036-110]had not died out of her when she reached the dining-room; and even

[036-111]here, before the servants, her shoulders suddenly shook as the scene

[036-112]returned upon her; and the tears of her hilarity mingled with the

[036-113]remnants of those engendered by her grief.

[036-114]She resolved to be sad no more. She drank two glasses of champagne, and

[036-115]a little more still after those, and amused herself in the evening with

[036-116]singing little amatory songs.

[036-117]“I must do something for that poor man Winterborne, however,” she said.

[036-118]CHAPTER XXVIII.

[036-119]A week had passed, and Mrs. Charmond had left [[hintock-house|Hintock House]]. Middleton

[036-120]Abbey, the place of her sojourn, was about twenty miles distant by

[036-121]road, eighteen by bridle-paths and footways.

[036-122]Grace observed, for the first time, that her husband was restless, that

[036-123]at moments he even was disposed to avoid her. The scrupulous civility

[036-124]of mere acquaintanceship crept into his manner; yet, when sitting at

[036-125]meals, he seemed hardly to hear her remarks. Her little doings

[036-126]interested him no longer, while towards her father his bearing was not

[036-127]far from supercilious. It was plain that his mind was entirely outside

[036-128]her life, whereabouts outside it she could not tell; in some region of

[036-129]science, possibly, or of psychological literature. But her hope that he

[036-130]was again immersing himself in those lucubrations which before her

[036-131]marriage had made his light a landmark in Hintock, was founded simply

[036-132]on the slender fact that he often sat up late.

[036-133]One evening she discovered him leaning over a gate on Rub-Down Hill,

[036-134]the gate at which Winterborne had once been standing, and which opened

[036-135]on the brink of a steep, slanting down directly into [[blackmoor|Blackmoor Vale]], or

[036-136]the [[white-hart-vale|Vale of the White Hart]], extending beneath the eye at this point to

[036-137]a distance of many miles. His attention was fixed on the landscape far

[036-138]away, and Grace’s approach was so noiseless that he did not hear her.

[036-139]When she came close she could see his lips moving unconsciously, as to

[036-140]some impassioned visionary theme.

[036-141]She spoke, and Fitzpiers started. “What are you looking at?” she asked.

[036-142]“Oh! I was contemplating our old place of Buckbury, in my idle way,” he

[036-143]said.

[036-144]It had seemed to her that he was looking much to the right of that

[036-145]cradle and tomb of his ancestral [[pride-and-humiliation|dignity]]; but she made no further

[036-146]observation, and taking his arm walked home beside him almost in

[036-147]silence. She did not know that Middleton Abbey lay in the direction of

[036-148]his gaze. “Are you going to have out Darling this afternoon?” she

[036-149]asked, presently. Darling being the light-gray mare which Winterborne

[036-150]had bought for Grace, and which Fitzpiers now constantly used, the

[036-151]animal having turned out a wonderful bargain, in combining a perfect

[036-152]docility with an almost human intelligence; moreover, she was not too

[036-153]young. Fitzpiers was unfamiliar with horses, and he valued these

[036-154]qualities.

[036-155]“Yes,” he replied, “but not to drive. I am riding her. I practise

[036-156]crossing a horse as often as I can now, for I find that I can take much

[036-157]shorter cuts on horseback.”

[036-158]He had, in fact, taken these riding exercises for about a week, only

[036-159]since Mrs. Charmond’s absence, his universal practice hitherto having

[036-160]been to drive.

[036-161]Some few days later, Fitzpiers started on the back of this horse to see

[036-162]a patient in the aforesaid Vale. It was about five o’clock in the

[036-163]evening when he went away, and at bedtime he had not reached home.

[036-164]There was nothing very singular in this, though she was not aware that

[036-165]he had any patient more than five or six miles distant in that

[036-166]direction. The clock had struck one before Fitzpiers entered the house,

[036-167]and he came to his room softly, as if anxious not to disturb her.

[036-168]The next morning she was stirring considerably earlier than he.

[036-169]In the yard there was a conversation going on about the mare; the man

[036-170]who attended to the horses, Darling included, insisted that the latter

[036-171]was “hag-rid;” for when he had arrived at the stable that morning she

[036-172]was in such a state as no horse could be in by honest riding. It was

[036-173]true that the doctor had stabled her himself when he got home, so that

[036-174]she was not looked after as she would have been if he had groomed and

[036-175]fed her; but that did not account for the appearance she presented, if

[036-176]Mr. Fitzpiers’s journey had been only where he had stated. The

[036-177]phenomenal exhaustion of Darling, as thus related, was sufficient to

[036-178]develop a whole series of tales about riding witches and demons, the

[036-179][[narrative-style|narration]] of which occupied a considerable time.

[036-180]Grace returned in-doors. In passing through the outer room she picked

[036-181]up her husband’s overcoat which he had carelessly flung down across a

[036-182]chair. A turnpike ticket fell out of the breast-pocket, and she saw

[036-183]that it had been issued at Middleton Gate. He had therefore visited

[036-184]Middleton the previous night, a distance of at least five-and-thirty

[036-185]miles on horseback, there and back.
