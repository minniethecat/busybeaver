# Section 21

[021-001]Opposite the garden door and visible from the parlor window was a

[021-002]swing-gate leading into a field, across which there ran a footpath. The

[021-003]swing-gate had just been repainted, and on one fine afternoon, before

[021-004]the paint was dry, and while gnats were still dying thereon, the

[021-005]surgeon was standing in his sitting-room abstractedly looking out at

[021-006]the different pedestrians who passed and repassed along that route.

[021-007]Being of a philosophical stamp, he perceived that the character of each

[021-008]of these travellers exhibited itself in a somewhat amusing manner by

[021-009]his or her method of handling the gate.

[021-010]As regarded the men, there was not much variety: they gave the gate a

[021-011]kick and passed through. The [[gender-roles|women]] were more contrasting. To them the

[021-012]sticky wood-work was a barricade, a disgust, a menace, a treachery, as

[021-013]the case might be.

[021-014]The first that he noticed was a bouncing woman with her skirts tucked

[021-015]up and her hair uncombed. She grasped the gate without looking, giving

[021-016]it a supplementary push with her shoulder, when the white imprint drew

[021-017]from her an exclamation in language not too refined. She went to the

[021-018]green bank, sat down and rubbed herself in the grass, cursing the

[021-019]while.

[021-020]“Ha! ha! ha!” laughed the doctor.

[021-021]The next was a girl, with her hair cropped short, in whom the surgeon

[021-022]recognized the daughter of his late patient, [[john-south|the woodman South]].

[021-023]Moreover, a black bonnet that she wore by way of mourning unpleasantly

[021-024]reminded him that he had ordered the felling of a tree which had caused

[021-025]her parent’s death and [[giles-winterborne|Winterborne]]’s losses. She walked and thought,

[021-026]and not recklessly; but her preoccupation led her to grasp

[021-027]unsuspectingly the bar of the gate, and touch it with her arm.

[021-028][[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Fitzpiers]] felt sorry that she should have soiled that new black frock,

[021-029]poor as it was, for it was probably her only one. She looked at her

[021-030]hand and arm, seemed but little surprised, wiped off the disfigurement

[021-031]with an almost unmoved face, and as if without abandoning her original

[021-032]thoughts. Thus she went on her way.

[021-033]Then there came over the green quite a different sort of personage. She

[021-034]walked as delicately as if she had been bred in town, and as firmly as

[021-035]if she had been bred in the country; she seemed one who dimly knew her

[021-036]appearance to be attractive, but who retained some of the charm of

[021-037]being ignorant of that fact by forgetting it in a general pensiveness.

[021-038]She approached the gate. To let such a creature touch it even with a

[021-039]tip of her glove was to Fitzpiers almost like letting her proceed to

[021-040]tragical self-destruction. He jumped up and looked for his hat, but was

[021-041]unable to find the right one; glancing again out of the window he saw

[021-042]that he was too late. Having come up, she stopped, looked at the gate,

[021-043]picked up a little stick, and using it as a bayonet, pushed open the

[021-044]obstacle without touching it at all.

[021-045]He steadily watched her till she had passed out of sight, recognizing

[021-046]her as the very young lady whom he had seen once before and been unable

[021-047]to identify. Whose could that emotional face be? All the others he had

[021-048]seen in [[little-hintock|Hintock]] as yet oppressed him with their crude rusticity; the

[021-049]contrast offered by this suggested that she hailed from elsewhere.

[021-050]Precisely these thoughts had occurred to him at the first time of

[021-051]seeing her; but he now went a little further with them, and considered

[021-052]that as there had been no carriage seen or heard lately in that spot

[021-053]she could not have come a very long distance. She must be somebody

[021-054]staying at [[hintock-house|Hintock House]]? Possibly [[felice-charmond|Mrs. Charmond]], of whom he had heard

[021-055]so much—at any rate an inmate, and this probability was sufficient to

[021-056]set a mild radiance in the surgeon’s somewhat dull sky.

[021-057]Fitzpiers sat down to the book he had been perusing. It happened to be

[021-058]that of a German metaphysician, for the doctor was not a practical man,

[021-059]except by fits, and much preferred the ideal world to the real, and the

[021-060]discovery of principles to their application. The young lady remained

[021-061]in his thoughts. He might have followed her; but he was not

[021-062]constitutionally active, and preferred a conjectural pursuit. However,

[021-063]when he went out for a ramble just before dusk he insensibly took the

[021-064]direction of Hintock House, which was the way that Grace had been

[021-065]walking, it having happened that her mind had run on Mrs. Charmond that

[021-066]day, and she had walked to the brow of a hill whence the house could be

[021-067]seen, returning by another route.

[021-068]Fitzpiers in his turn reached the edge of the glen, overlooking the

[021-069]manor-house. The shutters were shut, and only one chimney smoked. The

[021-070]mere aspect of the place was enough to inform him that Mrs. Charmond

[021-071]had gone away and that nobody else was staying there. Fitzpiers felt a

[021-072]vague disappointment that the young lady was not Mrs. Charmond, of whom

[021-073]he had heard so much; and without pausing longer to gaze at a carcass

[021-074]from which the spirit had flown, he bent his steps homeward.

[021-075]Later in the evening Fitzpiers was summoned to visit a cottage patient

[021-076]about two miles distant. Like the majority of young practitioners in

[021-077]his position he was far from having assumed the [[pride-and-humiliation|dignity]] of being driven

[021-078]his rounds by a servant in a brougham that flashed the sunlight like a

[021-079]mirror; his way of getting about was by means of a gig which he drove

[021-080]himself, hitching the rein of the horse to the gate post, shutter hook,

[021-081]or garden paling of the domicile under visitation, or giving pennies to

[021-082]little boys to hold the animal during his stay—pennies which were well

[021-083]earned when the cases to be attended were of a certain cheerful kind

[021-084]that wore out the patience of the little boys.

[021-085]On this account of travelling alone, the night journeys which Fitzpiers

[021-086]had frequently to take were dismal enough, a serious apparent

[021-087]perversity in [[nature-in-the-woodlanders|nature]] ruling that whenever there was to be a birth in a

[021-088]particularly inaccessible and lonely place, that event should occur in

[021-089]the night. The surgeon, having been of late years a town man, hated the

[021-090]solitary midnight woodland. He was not altogether skilful with the

[021-091]reins, and it often occurred to his mind that if in some remote depths

[021-092]of the trees an accident were to happen, the fact of his being alone

[021-093]might be the death of him. Hence he made a practice of picking up any

[021-094]countryman or lad whom he chanced to pass by, and under the disguise of

[021-095]treating him to a nice drive, obtained his companionship on the

[021-096]journey, and his convenient assistance in opening gates.

[021-097]The doctor had started on his way out of the village on the night in

[021-098]question when the light of his lamps fell upon the musing form of

[021-099]Winterborne, walking leisurely along, as if he had no object in life.

[021-100]Winterborne was a better class of companion than the doctor usually

[021-101]could get, and he at once pulled up and asked him if he would like a

[021-102]drive through the wood that fine night.

[021-103]Giles seemed rather surprised at the doctor’s friendliness, but said

[021-104]that he had no objection, and accordingly mounted beside Mr. Fitzpiers.

[021-105]They drove along under the black boughs which formed a network upon the

[021-106]stars, all the trees of a species alike in one respect, and no two of

[021-107]them alike in another. Looking up as they passed under a horizontal

[021-108]bough they sometimes saw objects like large tadpoles lodged

[021-109]diametrically across it, which Giles explained to be pheasants there at

[021-110]roost; and they sometimes heard the report of a gun, which reminded him

[021-111]that others knew what those tadpole shapes represented as well as he.

[021-112]Presently the doctor said what he had been going to say for some time:

[021-113]“Is there a young lady staying in this neighborhood—a very attractive

[021-114]girl—with a little white boa round her neck, and white fur round her

[021-115]gloves?”

[021-116]Winterborne of course knew in a moment that Grace, whom he had caught

[021-117]the doctor peering at, was represented by these accessories. With a

[021-118]wary grimness, partly in his character, partly induced by the

[021-119]circumstances, he evaded an answer by saying, “I saw a young lady

[021-120]talking to Mrs. Charmond the other day; perhaps it was she.”

[021-121]Fitzpiers concluded from this that Winterborne had not seen him looking

[021-122]over the hedge. “It might have been,” he said. “She is quite a

[021-123]gentlewoman—the one I mean. She cannot be a permanent resident in

[021-124]Hintock or I should have seen her before. Nor does she look like one.”

[021-125]“She is not staying at Hintock House?”

[021-126]“No; it is closed.”

[021-127]“Then perhaps she is staying at one of the cottages, or farmhouses?”

[021-128]“Oh no—you mistake. She was a different sort of girl altogether.” As

[021-129]Giles was nobody, Fitzpiers treated him accordingly, and apostrophized

[021-130]the night in continuation:

[021-131]“She moved upon this earth a shape of brightness,

[021-132]A power, that from its objects scarcely drew

[021-133]One impulse of her being—in her lightness

[021-134]Most like some radiant cloud of morning dew,

[021-135]Which wanders through the waste air’s pathless blue,

[021-136]To nourish some far desert: she did seem

[021-137]Beside me, gathering beauty as she grew,

[021-138]Like the bright shade of some immortal dream

[021-139]Which walks, when tempests sleep, the wave of life’s dark stream.”

[021-140]The consummate charm of the lines seemed to Winterborne, though he

[021-141]divined that they were a quotation, to be somehow the result of his

[021-142]lost [[love-and-desire|love]]’s charms upon Fitzpiers.

[021-143]“You seem to be mightily in love with her, sir,” he said, with a

[021-144]sensation of heart-sickness, and more than ever resolved not to mention

[021-145]Grace by name.

[021-146]“Oh no—I am not that, Winterborne; people living insulated, as I do by

[021-147]the solitude of this place, get charged with emotive fluid like a

[021-148]Leyden-jar with electric, for want of some conductor at hand to

[021-149]disperse it. Human love is a subjective thing—the essence itself of

[021-150]man, as that great thinker Spinoza the philosopher says—_ipsa hominis

[021-151]essentia_—it is joy accompanied by an idea which we project against any

[021-152]suitable object in the line of our vision, just as the rainbow iris is

[021-153]projected against an oak, ash, or elm tree indifferently. So that if

[021-154]any other young lady had appeared instead of the one who did appear, I

[021-155]should have felt just the same interest in her, and have quoted

[021-156]precisely the same lines from Shelley about her, as about this one I

[021-157]saw. Such miserable creatures of circumstance are we all!”

[021-158]“Well, it is what we call being in love down in these parts, whether or

[021-159]no,” said Winterborne.

[021-160]“You are right enough if you admit that I am in love with something in

[021-161]my own head, and no thing in itself outside it at all.”

[021-162]“Is it part of a country doctor’s duties to learn that view of things,

[021-163]may I ask, sir?” said Winterborne, adopting the Socratic εἰρωνεία with

[021-164]such well-assumed simplicity that Fitzpiers answered, readily,

[021-165]“Oh no. The real truth is, Winterborne, that [[rural-medicine-in-the-woodlanders|medical practice]] in places

[021-166]like this is a very rule-of-thumb matter; a bottle of bitter stuff for

[021-167]this and that old woman—the bitterer the better—compounded from a few

[021-168]simple stereotyped prescriptions; occasional attendance at births,

[021-169]where mere presence is almost sufficient, so healthy and strong are the

[021-170]people; and a lance for an abscess now and then. Investigation and

[021-171]experiment cannot be carried on without more appliances than one has

[021-172]here—though I have attempted it a little.”

[021-173]Giles did not enter into this view of the case; what he had been struck

[021-174]with was the curious parallelism between Mr. Fitzpiers’s manner and

[021-175]Grace’s, as shown by the fact of both of them straying into a subject

[021-176]of discourse so engrossing to themselves that it made them forget it

[021-177]was foreign to him.
