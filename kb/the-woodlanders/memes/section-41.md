# Section 41

[041-001]The hazel-tree did not belie its name to-day. The whole of the

[041-002]copse-wood where the mist had cleared returned purest tints of that

[041-003]hue, amid which [[giles-winterborne|Winterborne]] himself was in the act of making a hurdle,

[041-004]the stakes being driven firmly into the ground in a row, over which he

[041-005]bent and wove the twigs. Beside him was a square, compact pile like the

[041-006]altar of Cain, formed of hurdles already finished, which bristled on

[041-007]all sides with the sharp points of their stakes. At a little distance

[041-008]the men in his employ were assisting him to carry out his contract.

[041-009]Rows of copse-wood lay on the ground as it had fallen under the axe;

[041-010]and a shelter had been constructed near at hand, in front of which

[041-011]burned the fire whose smoke had attracted him. The air was so dank that

[041-012]the smoke hung heavy, and crept away amid the bushes without rising

[041-013]from the ground.

[041-014]After wistfully regarding Winterborne a while, [[george-melbury|Melbury]] drew nearer, and

[041-015]briefly inquired of Giles how he came to be so busily engaged, with an

[041-016]undertone of slight surprise that Winterborne could seem so thriving

[041-017]after being deprived of Grace. Melbury was not without emotion at the

[041-018]meeting; for Grace’s affairs had divided them, and ended their intimacy

[041-019]of old times.

[041-020]Winterborne explained just as briefly, without raising his eyes from

[041-021]his occupation of chopping a bough that he held in front of him.

[041-022]“’Twill be up in April before you get it all cleared,” said Melbury.

[041-023]“Yes, there or thereabouts,” said Winterborne, a chop of the billhook

[041-024]jerking the last word into two pieces.

[041-025]There was another interval; Melbury still looked on, a chip from

[041-026]Winterborne’s hook occasionally flying against the waistcoat and legs

[041-027]of his visitor, who took no heed.

[041-028]“Ah, Giles—you should have been my partner. You should have been my

[041-029]son-in-law,” the old man said at last. “It would have been far better

[041-030]for her and for me.”

[041-031]Winterborne saw that something had gone wrong with his former friend,

[041-032]and throwing down the switch he was about to interweave, he responded

[041-033]only too readily to the mood of the [[timber-economy|timber]]-dealer. “Is she ill?” he

[041-034]said, hurriedly.

[041-035]“No, no.” Melbury stood without speaking for some minutes, and then, as

[041-036]though he could not bring himself to proceed, turned to go away.

[041-037]Winterborne told one of his men to pack up the tools for the night and

[041-038]walked after Melbury.

[041-039]“Heaven forbid that I should seem too inquisitive, sir,” he said,

[041-040]“especially since we don’t stand as we used to stand to one another;

[041-041]but I hope it is well with them all over your way?”

[041-042]“No,” said Melbury—“no.” He stopped, and struck the smooth trunk of a

[041-043]young ash-tree with the flat of his hand. “I would that his ear had

[041-044]been where that rind is!” he exclaimed; “I should have treated him to

[041-045]little compared wi’ what he deserves.”

[041-046]“Now,” said Winterborne, “don’t be in a hurry to go home. I’ve put some

[041-047]cider down to warm in my shelter here, and we’ll sit and drink it and

[041-048]talk this over.”

[041-049]Melbury turned unresistingly as Giles took his arm, and they went back

[041-050]to where the fire was, and sat down under the screen, the other woodmen

[041-051]having gone. He drew out the cider-mug from the ashes and they drank

[041-052]together.

[041-053]“Giles, you ought to have had her, as I said just now,” repeated

[041-054]Melbury. “I’ll tell you why for the first time.”

[041-055]He thereupon told Winterborne, as with great relief, the story of how

[041-056]he won away Giles’s father’s chosen one—by nothing worse than a lover’s

[041-057]cajoleries, it is true, but by means which, except in [[love-and-desire|love]], would

[041-058]certainly have been pronounced cruel and unfair. He explained how he

[041-059]had always intended to make reparation to Winterborne the father by

[041-060]giving Grace to Winterborne the son, till the devil tempted him in the

[041-061]person of [[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Fitzpiers]], and he broke his virtuous vow.

[041-062]“How highly I thought of that man, to be sure! Who’d have supposed he’d

[041-063]have been so weak and wrong-headed as this! You ought to have had her,

[041-064]Giles, and there’s an end on’t.”

[041-065]Winterborne knew how to preserve his calm under this unconsciously

[041-066]cruel tearing of a healing wound to which Melbury’s concentration on

[041-067]the more vital subject had blinded him. The young man endeavored to

[041-068]make the best of the case for Grace’s sake.

[041-069]“She would hardly have been happy with me,” he said, in the dry,

[041-070]unimpassioned voice under which he hid his feelings. “I was not well

[041-071]enough educated: too rough, in short. I couldn’t have surrounded her

[041-072]with the refinements she looked for, anyhow, at all.”

[041-073]“Nonsense—you are quite wrong there,” said the unwise old man,

[041-074]doggedly. “She told me only this day that she hates refinements and

[041-075]such like. All that my trouble and money bought for her in that way is

[041-076]thrown away upon her quite. She’d fain be like [[marty-south|Marty South]]—think o’

[041-077]that! That’s the top of her ambition! Perhaps she’s right. Giles, she

[041-078]loved you—under the rind; and, what’s more, she loves ye still—worse

[041-079]luck for the poor maid!”

[041-080]If Melbury only had known what fires he was recklessly stirring up he

[041-081]might have held his peace. Winterborne was silent a long time. The

[041-082]darkness had closed in round them, and the monotonous drip of the fog

[041-083]from the branches quickened as it turned to fine rain.

[041-084]“Oh, she never cared much for me,” Giles managed to say, as he stirred

[041-085]the embers with a brand.

[041-086]“She did, and does, I tell ye,” said the other, obstinately. “However,

[041-087]all that’s vain talking now. What I come to ask you about is a more

[041-088]practical matter—how to make the best of things as they are. I am

[041-089]thinking of a desperate step—of calling on the woman Charmond. I am

[041-090]going to appeal to her, since Grace will not. ’Tis she who holds the

[041-091]balance in her hands—not he. While she’s got the will to lead him

[041-092]astray he will follow—poor, unpractical, lofty-notioned dreamer—and how

[041-093]long she’ll do it depends upon her whim. Did ye ever hear anything

[041-094]about her character before she came to [[little-hintock|Hintock]]?”

[041-095]“She’s been a bit of a charmer in her time, I believe,” replied Giles,

[041-096]with the same level quietude, as he regarded the red coals. “One who

[041-097]has smiled where she has not loved and loved where she has not married.

[041-098]Before Mr. Charmond made her his wife she was a play-actress.”

[041-099]“Hey? But how close you have kept all this, Giles! What besides?”

[041-100]“Mr. Charmond was a rich man, engaged in the iron trade in the north,

[041-101]twenty or thirty years older than she. He married her and retired, and

[041-102]came down here and bought this property, as they do nowadays.”

[041-103]“Yes, yes—I know all about that; but the other I did not know. I fear

[041-104]it bodes no good. For how can I go and appeal to the forbearance of a

[041-105]woman in this matter who has made cross-loves and crooked entanglements

[041-106]her trade for years? I thank ye, Giles, for finding it out; but it

[041-107]makes my plan the harder that she should have belonged to that unstable

[041-108]tribe.”

[041-109]Another pause ensued, and they looked gloomily at the smoke that beat

[041-110]about the hurdles which sheltered them, through whose weavings a large

[041-111]drop of rain fell at intervals and spat smartly into the fire. Mrs.

[041-112]Charmond had been no friend to Winterborne, but he was manly, and it

[041-113]was not in his heart to let her be condemned without a trial.

[041-114]“She is said to be generous,” he answered. “You might not appeal to her

[041-115]in vain.”

[041-116]“It shall be done,” said Melbury, rising. “For good or for evil, to

[041-117][[felice-charmond|Mrs. Charmond]] I’ll go.”

[041-118]CHAPTER XXXII.

[041-119]At nine o’clock the next morning Melbury dressed himself up in shining

[041-120]broadcloth, creased with folding and smelling of camphor, and started

[041-121]for [[hintock-house|Hintock House]]. He was the more impelled to go at once by the

[041-122]absence of his son-in-law in [[london-in-the-woodlanders|London]] for a few days, to attend, really

[041-123]or ostensibly, some professional meetings. He said nothing of his

[041-124]destination either to his wife or to Grace, fearing that they might

[041-125]entreat him to abandon so risky a project, and went out unobserved. He

[041-126]had chosen his time with a view, as he supposed, of conveniently

[041-127]catching Mrs. Charmond when she had just finished her breakfast, before

[041-128]any other business people should be about, if any came. Plodding

[041-129]thoughtfully onward, he crossed a glade lying between Little Hintock

[041-130]Woods and the plantation which abutted on the park; and the spot being

[041-131]open, he was discerned there by Winterborne from the copse on the next

[041-132]hill, where he and his men were working. Knowing his mission, the

[041-133]younger man hastened down from the copse and managed to intercept the

[041-134]timber-merchant.

[041-135]“I have been thinking of this, sir,” he said, “and I am of opinion that

[041-136]it would be best to put off your visit for the present.”

[041-137]But Melbury would not even stop to hear him. His mind was made up, the

[041-138]appeal was to be made; and Winterborne stood and watched him sadly till

[041-139]he entered the second plantation and disappeared.

[041-140]Melbury rang at the tradesmen’s door of the manor-house, and was at

[041-141]once informed that the lady was not yet visible, as indeed he might

[041-142]have guessed had he been anybody but the man he was. Melbury said he

[041-143]would wait, whereupon the young man informed him in a neighborly way

[041-144]that, between themselves, she was in bed and asleep.

[041-145]“Never mind,” said Melbury, retreating into the court, “I’ll stand

[041-146]about here.” Charged so fully with his mission, he shrank from contact

[041-147]with anybody.

[041-148]But he walked about the paved court till he was tired, and still nobody

[041-149]came to him. At last he entered the house and sat down in a small

[041-150]waiting-room, from which he got glimpses of the kitchen corridor, and

[041-151]of the white-capped maids flitting jauntily hither and thither. They

[041-152]had heard of his arrival, but had not seen him enter, and, imagining

[041-153]him still in the court, discussed freely the possible reason of his

[041-154]calling. They marvelled at his temerity; for though most of the tongues

[041-155]which had been let loose attributed the chief blame-worthiness to

[041-156]Fitzpiers, these of her household preferred to regard their mistress as

[041-157]the deeper sinner.

[041-158]Melbury sat with his hands resting on the familiar knobbed thorn

[041-159]walking-stick, whose growing he had seen before he enjoyed its use. The

[041-160]scene to him was not the material environment of his person, but a

[041-161]tragic vision that travelled with him like an envelope. Through this

[041-162]vision the incidents of the moment but gleamed confusedly here and

[041-163]there, as an outer landscape through the high-colored scenes of a

[041-164]stained window. He waited thus an hour, an hour and a half, two hours.

[041-165]He began to look pale and ill, whereupon the butler, who came in, asked

[041-166]him to have a glass of wine. Melbury roused himself and said, “No, no.

[041-167]Is she almost ready?”

[041-168]“She is just finishing breakfast,” said the butler. “She will soon see

[041-169]you now. I am just going up to tell her you are here.”

[041-170]“What! haven’t you told her before?” said Melbury.

[041-171]“Oh no,” said the other. “You see you came so very early.”

[041-172]At last the bell rang: Mrs. Charmond could see him. She was not in her

[041-173]private sitting-room when he reached it, but in a minute he heard her

[041-174]coming from the front staircase, and she entered where he stood.

[041-175]At this time of the morning Mrs. Charmond looked her full age and more.

[041-176]She might almost have been taken for the typical _femme de trente ans_,

[041-177]though she was really not more than seven or eight and twenty. There

[041-178]being no fire in the room, she came in with a shawl thrown loosely

[041-179]round her shoulders, and obviously without the least suspicion that

[041-180]Melbury had called upon any other errand than timber. Felice was,

[041-181]indeed, the only woman in [[the-hintock-parish|the parish]] who had not heard the rumor of her

[041-182]own weaknesses; she was at this moment living in a fool’s paradise in

[041-183]respect of that rumor, though not in respect of the weaknesses

[041-184]themselves, which, if the truth be told, caused her grave misgivings.
