# Blackmoor Vale

Blackmoor Vale (also referred to as Blackmoor Valley or, interchangeably, the Great White Hart) is a broad valley in the Wessex landscape of Thomas Hardy's *The Woodlanders*. Visible from the elevated vantage points of the Hintock region, it represents the wider world beyond the enclosed woodlands, serving as both a geographical and symbolic horizon.

## 地理位置与景观

Blackmoor Vale lies below the heights of Hintock, visible from Rub-Down Hill. The narrator describes how "a steep" slope slants "down directly into Blackmoor Vale, or the Vale of the White Hart, extending beneath the eye at this point to a distance of many miles" [036-135]. From this vantage, the vale presents as an expansive prospect — a vast tract of countryside stretching to the horizon, contrasting with the enclosed and intimate scale of the Hintock woodlands.

The gate on Rub-Down Hill — the same gate where Giles Winterborne was once seen standing — opens directly onto this vista [036-134]. The spot becomes significant when Grace Melbury discovers her husband Fitzpiers leaning there, absorbed in contemplation of the distant landscape [036-133].

## 双重命名

The narrator uses "Blackmoor Vale" and "the Vale of the White Hart" (or "Great White Hart") interchangeably [036-135], [038-086]. This dual naming reflects the layered identity of the Wessex landscape — one name evoking the dark, peaty soil of the lowland moors ("Blackmoor"), the other the more poetic and heraldic association with the white hart of folklore and heraldry. Both names describe the same geographical feature, viewed through different lenses.

## 叙事中的存在

Blackmoor Vale appears in the narrative primarily as a distant prospect — something gazed upon rather than entered. When Melbury and Grace climb the hill at dawn searching for the missing Fitzpiers, they halt "beneath a half-dead oak" and look out across "the Great White Hart or Blackmoor Valley" [038-086]. The vale at dawn is described as "wrapped in a dim atmosphere of unnaturalness," with the eastern sky "like a livid curtain edged with pink" [038-092].

Fitzpiers himself is twice associated with this view: once when Grace discovers him lost in contemplation of it [036-133], and again through his dawn return through the vale after his mysterious night ride [038-085]. In both instances, the vale serves as a visual correlative for the doctor's detachment — his gaze fixed on distant, intangible horizons rather than on the immediate world of Hintock and his marriage.

## 象征意义

As a landscape feature, Blackmoor Vale embodies the tension between enclosure and expanse that runs through *The Woodlanders*. The woodlands of Hintock enclose and shelter; the vale opens outward, offering a vista that is both alluring and unsettling. Fitzpiers's fixation on the distant view signals his rootlessness and his unfitness for the rooted, arboreal world that Giles Winterborne embodies.

## 文学渊源

The dual naming of the vale — Blackmoor and White Hart — echoes a broader pattern in Hardy's Wessex fiction, where landscape features bear both prosaic local names and more evocative, sometimes heraldic alternatives. The "White Hart" is a traditional English pub and heraldic symbol, but in the context of the novel it suggests something wilder and more elusive: the white hart of medieval legend, a creature that could never be captured. The name hints at the unattainable horizons that draw Fitzpiers's gaze away from his domestic world.
