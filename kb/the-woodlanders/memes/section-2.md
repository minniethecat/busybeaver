# Section 2

[002-001]CHAPTER II.

[002-002]In the room from which this cheerful blaze proceeded, he beheld a girl

[002-003]seated on a willow chair, and busily occupied by the light of the fire,

[002-004]which was ample and of wood. With a bill-hook in one hand and a leather

[002-005]glove, much too large for her, on the other, she was making spars, such

[002-006]as are used by thatchers, with great rapidity. She wore a leather apron

[002-007]for this purpose, which was also much too large for her figure. On her

[002-008]left hand lay a bundle of the straight, smooth sticks called

[002-009]spar-gads—the raw material of her manufacture; on her right, a heap of

[002-010]chips and ends—the refuse—with which the fire was maintained; in front,

[002-011]a pile of the finished articles. To produce them she took up each gad,

[002-012]looked critically at it from end to end, cut it to length, split it

[002-013]into four, and sharpened each of the quarters with dexterous blows,

[002-014]which brought it to a triangular point precisely resembling that of a

[002-015]bayonet.

[002-016]Beside her, in case she might require more light, a brass candlestick

[002-017]stood on a little round table, curiously formed of an old coffin-stool,

[002-018]with a deal top nailed on, the white surface of the latter contrasting

[002-019]oddly with the black carved oak of the substructure. The social

[002-020]position of the household in the past was almost as definitively shown

[002-021]by the presence of this article as that of an esquire or nobleman by

[002-022]his old helmets or shields. It had been customary for every well-to-do

[002-023]villager, whose tenure was by [[lifehold-tenure|copy of court-roll]], or in any way more

[002-024]permanent than that of the mere cotter, to keep a pair of these stools

[002-025]for the use of his own dead; but for the last generation or two a

[002-026]feeling of cui bono had led to the discontinuance of the custom, and

[002-027]the stools were frequently made use of in the manner described.

[002-028]The young woman laid down the bill-hook for a moment and examined the

[002-029]palm of her right hand, which, unlike the other, was ungloved, and

[002-030]showed little hardness or roughness about it. The palm was red and

[002-031]blistering, as if this present occupation were not frequent enough with

[002-032]her to subdue it to what it worked in. As with so many right hands born

[002-033]to manual labor, there was nothing in its fundamental shape to bear out

[002-034]the physiological conventionalism that gradations of birth, gentle or

[002-035]mean, show themselves primarily in the form of this member. Nothing but

[002-036]a cast of the die of [[fate-and-free-will|destiny]] had decided that the girl should handle

[002-037]the tool; and the fingers which clasped the heavy ash haft might have

[002-038]skilfully guided the pencil or swept the string, had they only been set

[002-039]to do it in good time.

[002-040]Her face had the usual fulness of expression which is developed by a

[002-041]life of solitude. Where the eyes of a multitude beat like waves upon a

[002-042]countenance they seem to wear away its individuality; but in the still

[002-043]water of privacy every tentacle of feeling and sentiment shoots out in

[002-044]visible luxuriance, to be interpreted as readily as a child’s look by

[002-045]an intruder. In years she was no more than nineteen or twenty, but the

[002-046]necessity of taking thought at a too early period of life had forced

[002-047]the provisional curves of her childhood’s face to a premature finality.

[002-048]Thus she had but little pretension to beauty, save in one prominent

[002-049]particular—her hair. Its abundance made it almost unmanageable; its

[002-050]color was, roughly speaking, and as seen here by firelight, brown, but

[002-051]careful notice, or an observation by day, would have revealed that its

[002-052]true shade was a rare and beautiful approximation to chestnut.

[002-053]On this one bright gift of Time to the particular victim of his now

[002-054]before us the new-comer’s eyes were fixed; meanwhile the fingers of his

[002-055]right hand mechanically played over something sticking up from his

[002-056]waistcoat-pocket—the bows of a pair of scissors, whose polish made them

[002-057]feebly responsive to the light within. In her present beholder’s mind

[002-058]the scene formed by the girlish spar-maker composed itself into a

[002-059]post-Raffaelite picture of extremest quality, wherein the girl’s hair

[002-060]alone, as the focus of observation, was depicted with intensity and

[002-061]distinctness, and her face, shoulders, hands, and figure in general,

[002-062]being a blurred mass of unimportant detail lost in haze and obscurity.

[002-063]He hesitated no longer, but tapped at the door and entered. The young

[002-064]woman turned at the crunch of his boots on the sanded floor, and

[002-065]exclaiming, “Oh, [[percombe|Mr. Percombe]], how you frightened me!” quite lost her

[002-066]color for a moment.

[002-067]He replied, “You should shut your door—then you’d hear folk open it.”

[002-068]“I can’t,” she said; “the chimney smokes so. Mr. Percombe, you look as

[002-069]unnatural out of your shop as a canary in a thorn-hedge. Surely you

[002-070]have not come out here on my account—for—”

[002-071]“Yes—to have your answer about this.” He touched her head with his

[002-072]cane, and she winced. “Do you agree?” he continued. “It is necessary

[002-073]that I should know at once, as the lady is soon going away, and it

[002-074]takes time to make up.”

[002-075]“Don’t press me—it worries me. I was in hopes you had thought no more

[002-076]of it. I can _not_ part with it—so there!”

[002-077]“Now, look here, Marty,” said the barber, sitting down on the

[002-078]coffin-stool table. “How much do you get for making these spars?”

[002-079]“Hush—father’s up-stairs awake, and he don’t know that I am doing his

[002-080]work.”

[002-081]“Well, now tell me,” said the man, more softly. “How much do you get?”

[002-082]“Eighteenpence a thousand,” she said, reluctantly.

[002-083]“Who are you making them for?”

[002-084]“[[george-melbury|Mr. Melbury]], the [[timber-economy|timber]]-dealer, just below here.”

[002-085]“And how many can you make in a day?”

[002-086]“In a day and half the night, three bundles—that’s a thousand and a

[002-087]half.”

[002-088]“Two and threepence.” The barber paused. “Well, look here,” he

[002-089]continued, with the remains of a calculation in his tone, which

[002-090]calculation had been the reduction to figures of the probable monetary

[002-091]magnetism necessary to overpower the resistant force of her present

[002-092]purse and the woman’s [[love-and-desire|love]] of comeliness, “here’s a sovereign—a gold

[002-093]sovereign, almost new.” He held it out between his finger and thumb.

[002-094]“That’s as much as you’d earn in a week and a half at that rough man’s

[002-095]work, and it’s yours for just letting me snip off what you’ve got too

[002-096]much of.”

[002-097]The girl’s bosom moved a very little. “Why can’t the lady send to some

[002-098]other girl who don’t value her hair—not to me?” she exclaimed.

[002-099]“Why, simpleton, because yours is the exact shade of her own, and ’tis

[002-100]a shade you can’t match by dyeing. But you are not going to refuse me

[002-101]now I’ve come all the way from [[sherton-abbas|Sherton]] o’ purpose?”

[002-102]“I say I won’t sell it—to you or anybody.”

[002-103]“Now listen,” and he drew up a little closer beside her. “The lady is

[002-104]very rich, and won’t be particular to a few shillings; so I will

[002-105]advance to this on my own responsibility—I’ll make the one sovereign

[002-106]two, rather than go back empty-handed.”

[002-107]“No, no, no!” she cried, beginning to be much agitated. “You are

[002-108]a-tempting me, Mr. Percombe. You go on like the Devil to Dr. Faustus in

[002-109]the penny book. But I don’t want your money, and won’t agree. Why did

[002-110]you come? I said when you got me into your shop and urged me so much,

[002-111]that I didn’t mean to sell my hair!” The speaker was hot and stern.

[002-112]“Marty, now hearken. The lady that wants it wants it badly. And,

[002-113]between you and me, you’d better let her have it. ’Twill be bad for you

[002-114]if you don’t.”

[002-115]“Bad for me? Who is she, then?”

[002-116]The barber held his tongue, and the girl repeated the question.

[002-117]“I am not at liberty to tell you. And as she is going abroad soon it

[002-118]makes no difference who she is at all.”

[002-119]“She wants it to go abroad wi’?”

[002-120]Percombe assented by a nod. The girl regarded him reflectively. “Barber

[002-121]Percombe,” she said, “I know who ’tis. ’Tis she at the House—Mrs.

[002-122]Charmond!”

[002-123]“That’s my [[secrecy-and-concealment|secret]]. However, if you agree to let me have it, I’ll tell

[002-124]you in confidence.”

[002-125]“I’ll certainly not let you have it unless you tell me the truth. It is

[002-126][[felice-charmond|Mrs. Charmond]].”

[002-127]The barber dropped his voice. “Well—it is. You sat in front of her in

[002-128]church the other day, and she noticed how exactly your hair matched her

[002-129]own. Ever since then she’s been hankering for it, and at last decided

[002-130]to get it. As she won’t wear it till she goes off abroad, she knows

[002-131]nobody will recognize the change. I’m commissioned to get it for her,

[002-132]and then it is to be made up. I shouldn’t have vamped all these miles

[002-133]for any less important employer. Now, mind—’tis as much as my business

[002-134]with her is worth if it should be known that I’ve let out her name; but

[002-135]honor between us two, Marty, and you’ll say nothing that would injure

[002-136]me?”

[002-137]“I don’t wish to tell upon her,” said Marty, coolly. “But my hair is my

[002-138]own, and I’m going to keep it.”

[002-139]“Now, that’s not fair, after what I’ve told you,” said the nettled

[002-140]barber. “You see, Marty, as you are in the same parish, and in one of

[002-141]her cottages, and your father is ill, and wouldn’t like to turn out, it

[002-142]would be as well to oblige her. I say that as a friend. But I won’t

[002-143]press you to make up your mind to-night. You’ll be coming to market

[002-144]to-morrow, I dare say, and you can call then. If you think it over

[002-145]you’ll be inclined to bring what I want, I know.”

[002-146]“I’ve nothing more to say,” she answered.

[002-147]Her companion saw from her manner that it was useless to urge her

[002-148]further by speech. “As you are a trusty young woman,” he said, “I’ll

[002-149]put these sovereigns up here for ornament, that you may see how

[002-150]handsome they are. Bring the hair to-morrow, or return the sovereigns.”

[002-151]He stuck them edgewise into the frame of a small mantle looking-glass.

[002-152]“I hope you’ll bring it, for your sake and mine. I should have thought

[002-153]she could have suited herself elsewhere; but as it’s her fancy it must

[002-154]be indulged if possible. If you cut it off yourself, mind how you do it

[002-155]so as to keep all the locks one way.” He showed her how this was to be

[002-156]done.

[002-157]“But I sha’nt,” she replied, with laconic indifference. “I value my

[002-158]looks too much to spoil ’em. She wants my hair to get another lover

[002-159]with; though if stories are true she’s broke the heart of many a noble

[002-160]gentleman already.”

[002-161]“Lord, it’s wonderful how you guess things, Marty,” said the barber.

[002-162]“I’ve had it from them that know that there certainly is some foreign

[002-163]gentleman in her eye. However, mind what I ask.”

[002-164]“She’s not going to get him through me.”

[002-165]Percombe had retired as far as the door; he came back, planted his cane

[002-166]on the coffin-stool, and looked her in the face. “[[marty-south|Marty South]],” he

[002-167]said, with deliberate emphasis, “_you’ve got a lover yourself_, and

[002-168]that’s why you won’t let it go!”

[002-169]She reddened so intensely as to pass the mild blush that suffices to

[002-170]heighten beauty; she put the yellow leather glove on one hand, took up

[002-171]the hook with the other, and sat down doggedly to her work without

[002-172]turning her face to him again. He regarded her head for a moment, went

[002-173]to the door, and with one look back at her, departed on his way

[002-174]homeward.

[002-175]Marty pursued her occupation for a few minutes, then suddenly laying

[002-176]down the bill-hook, she jumped up and went to the back of the room,

[002-177]where she opened a door which disclosed a staircase so whitely scrubbed

[002-178]that the grain of the wood was wellnigh sodden away by such cleansing.

[002-179]At the top she gently approached a bedroom, and without entering, said,

[002-180]“Father, do you want anything?”
