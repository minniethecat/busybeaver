# Section 56

[056-001]“Yes, Grace, yes. I do not at all mean that the question between us has

[056-002]not been settled by the fact of your [[marriage-in-the-woodlanders|marriage]] turning out hopelessly

[056-003]unalterable. I merely meant—well, a feeling no more.”

[056-004]“In a week, at the outside, I should be discovered if I stayed here:

[056-005]and I think that by law he could compel me to return to him.”

[056-006]“Yes; perhaps you are right. Go when you wish, dear Grace.”

[056-007]His last words that evening were a hopeful remark that all might be

[056-008]well with her yet; that [[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Mr. Fitzpiers]] would not intrude upon her life,

[056-009]if he found that his presence cost her so much pain. Then the window

[056-010]was closed, the shutters folded, and the rustle of his footsteps died

[056-011]away.

[056-012]No sooner had she retired to rest that night than the wind began to

[056-013]rise, and, after a few prefatory blasts, to be accompanied by rain. The

[056-014]wind grew more violent, and as the storm went on, it was difficult to

[056-015]believe that no opaque body, but only an invisible colorless thing, was

[056-016]trampling and climbing over the roof, making branches creak, springing

[056-017]out of the [[nature-in-the-woodlanders|trees]] upon the chimney, popping its head into the flue, and

[056-018]shrieking and blaspheming at every corner of the walls. As in the old

[056-019]story, the assailant was a spectre which could be felt but not seen.

[056-020]She had never before been so struck with the devilry of a gusty night

[056-021]in a wood, because she had never been so entirely alone in spirit as

[056-022]she was now. She seemed almost to be apart from herself—a vacuous

[056-023]duplicate only. The recent self of physical animation and clear

[056-024]intentions was not there.

[056-025]Sometimes a bough from an adjoining tree was swayed so low as to smite

[056-026]the roof in the manner of a gigantic hand smiting the mouth of an

[056-027]adversary, to be followed by a trickle of rain, as blood from the

[056-028]wound. To all this weather [[giles-winterborne|Giles]] must be more or less exposed; how

[056-029]much, she did not know.

[056-030]At last Grace could hardly endure the idea of such a hardship in

[056-031]relation to him. Whatever he was suffering, it was she who had caused

[056-032]it; he vacated his house on account of her. She was not worth such

[056-033]self-sacrifice; she should not have accepted it of him. And then, as

[056-034]her anxiety increased with increasing thought, there returned upon her

[056-035]mind some incidents of her late intercourse with him, which she had

[056-036]heeded but little at the time. The look of his face—what had there been

[056-037]about his face which seemed different from its appearance as of yore?

[056-038]Was it not thinner, less rich in hue, less like that of ripe autumn’s

[056-039]brother to whom she had formerly compared him? And his voice; she had

[056-040]distinctly noticed a change in tone. And his gait; surely it had been

[056-041]feebler, stiffer, more like the gait of a weary man. That slight

[056-042]occasional noise she had heard in the day, and attributed to squirrels,

[056-043]it might have been his cough after all.

[056-044]Thus conviction took root in her perturbed mind that Winterborne was

[056-045]ill, or had been so, and that he had carefully concealed his condition

[056-046]from her that she might have no scruples about accepting a hospitality

[056-047]which by the nature of the case expelled her entertainer.

[056-048]“My own, own, true l——, my dear kind friend!” she cried to herself.

[056-049]“Oh, it shall not be—it shall not be!”

[056-050]She hastily wrapped herself up, and obtained a light, with which she

[056-051]entered the adjoining room, the cot possessing only one floor. Setting

[056-052]down the candle on the table here, she went to the door with the key in

[056-053]her hand, and placed it in the lock. Before turning it she paused, her

[056-054]fingers still clutching it; and pressing her other hand to her

[056-055]forehead, she fell into agitating thought.

[056-056]A tattoo on the window, caused by the tree-droppings blowing against

[056-057]it, brought her indecision to a close. She turned the key and opened

[056-058]the door.

[056-059]The darkness was intense, seeming to touch her pupils like a substance.

[056-060]She only now became aware how heavy the rainfall had been and was; the

[056-061]dripping of the eaves splashed like a fountain. She stood listening

[056-062]with parted lips, and holding the door in one hand, till her eyes,

[056-063]growing accustomed to the obscurity, discerned the wild brandishing of

[056-064]their boughs by the adjoining trees. At last she cried loudly with an

[056-065]effort, “Giles! you may come in!”

[056-066]There was no immediate answer to her cry, and overpowered by her own

[056-067]temerity, Grace retreated quickly, shut the door, and stood looking on

[056-068]the floor. But it was not for long. She again lifted the latch, and

[056-069]with far more determination than at first.

[056-070]“Giles, Giles!” she cried, with the full strength of her voice, and

[056-071]without any of the shamefacedness that had characterized her first cry.

[056-072]“Oh, come in—come in! Where are you? I have been wicked. I have thought

[056-073]too much of myself! Do you hear? I don’t want to keep you out any

[056-074]longer. I cannot bear that you should suffer so. Gi-i-iles!”

[056-075]A reply! It was a reply! Through the darkness and wind a voice reached

[056-076]her, floating upon the weather as though a part of it.

[056-077]“Here I am—all right. Don’t trouble about me.”

[056-078]“Don’t you want to come in? Are you not wet? _Come to me! I don’t mind

[056-079]what they say, or what they think any more._”

[056-080]“I am all right,” he repeated. “It is not necessary for me to come.

[056-081]Good-night! good-night!”

[056-082]Grace sighed, turned and shut the door slowly. Could she have been

[056-083]mistaken about his health? Perhaps, after all, she had perceived a

[056-084]change in him because she had not seen him for so long. Time sometimes

[056-085]did his ageing work in jerks, as she knew. Well, she had done all she

[056-086]could. He would not come in. She retired to rest again.

[056-087]CHAPTER XLII.

[056-088]The next morning Grace was at the window early. She felt determined to

[056-089]see him somehow that day, and prepared his breakfast eagerly. Eight

[056-090]o’clock struck, and she had remembered that he had not come to arouse

[056-091]her by a knocking, as usual, her own anxiety having caused her to stir.

[056-092]The breakfast was set in its place without. But he did not arrive to

[056-093]take it; and she waited on. Nine o’clock arrived, and the breakfast was

[056-094]cold; and still there was no Giles. A thrush, that had been repeating

[056-095]itself a good deal on an opposite bush for some time, came and took a

[056-096]morsel from the plate and bolted it, waited, looked around, and took

[056-097]another. At ten o’clock she drew in the tray, and sat down to her own

[056-098]solitary meal. He must have been called away on business early, the

[056-099]rain having cleared off.

[056-100]Yet she would have liked to assure herself, by thoroughly exploring the

[056-101]precincts of the hut, that he was nowhere in its vicinity; but as the

[056-102]day was comparatively fine, the dread lest some stray passenger or

[056-103]woodman should encounter her in such a reconnoitre paralyzed her wish.

[056-104]The solitude was further accentuated to-day by the stopping of the

[056-105]clock for want of winding, and the fall into the chimney-corner of

[056-106]flakes of soot loosened by the rains. At noon she heard a slight

[056-107]rustling outside the window, and found that it was caused by an eft

[056-108]which had crept out of the leaves to bask in the last sun-rays that

[056-109]would be worth having till the following May.

[056-110]She continually peeped out through the lattice, but could see little.

[056-111]In front lay the brown leaves of last year, and upon them some

[056-112]yellowish-green ones of this season that had been prematurely blown

[056-113]down by the gale. Above stretched an old beech, with vast armpits, and

[056-114]great pocket-holes in its sides where branches had been amputated in

[056-115]past times; a black slug was trying to climb it. Dead boughs were

[056-116]scattered about like ichthyosauri in a museum, and beyond them were

[056-117]perishing woodbine stems resembling old ropes.

[056-118]From the other window all she could see were more trees, jacketed with

[056-119]lichen and stockinged with moss. At their roots were stemless yellow

[056-120]fungi like lemons and apricots, and tall fungi with more stem than

[056-121]stool. Next were more trees close together, wrestling for existence,

[056-122]their branches disfigured with wounds resulting from their mutual

[056-123]rubbings and blows. It was the struggle between these neighbors that

[056-124]she had heard in the night. Beneath them were the rotting stumps of

[056-125]those of the group that had been vanquished long ago, rising from their

[056-126]mossy setting like decayed teeth from green gums. Farther on were other

[056-127]tufts of moss in islands divided by the shed leaves—variety upon

[056-128]variety, dark green and pale green; moss-like little fir-trees, like

[056-129]plush, like malachite stars, like nothing on earth except moss.

[056-130]The strain upon Grace’s mind in various ways was so great on this the

[056-131]most desolate day she had passed there that she felt it would be

[056-132]well-nigh impossible to spend another in such circumstances. The

[056-133]evening came at last; the sun, when its chin was on the earth, found an

[056-134]opening through which to pierce the shade, and stretched irradiated

[056-135]gauzes across the damp atmosphere, making the wet trunks shine, and

[056-136]throwing splotches of such ruddiness on the leaves beneath the beech

[056-137]that they were turned to gory hues. When night at last arrived, and

[056-138]with it the time for his return, she was nearly broken down with

[056-139]suspense.

[056-140]The simple evening meal, partly tea, partly supper, which Grace had

[056-141]prepared, stood waiting upon the hearth; and yet Giles did not come. It

[056-142]was now nearly twenty-four hours since she had seen him. As the room

[056-143]grew darker, and only the firelight broke against the gloom of the

[056-144]walls, she was convinced that it would be beyond her staying power to

[056-145]pass the night without hearing from him or from somebody. Yet eight

[056-146]o’clock drew on, and his form at the window did not appear.

[056-147]The meal remained untasted. Suddenly rising from before the hearth of

[056-148]smouldering embers, where she had been crouching with her hands clasped

[056-149]over her knees, she crossed the room, unlocked the door, and listened.

[056-150]Every breath of wind had ceased with the decline of day, but the rain

[056-151]had resumed the steady dripping of the night before. Grace might have

[056-152]stood there five minutes when she fancied she heard that old sound, a

[056-153]cough, at no great distance; and it was presently repeated. If it were

[056-154]Winterborne’s, he must be near her; why, then, had he not visited her?

[056-155]A horrid misgiving that he could not visit her took possession of

[056-156]Grace, and she looked up anxiously for the lantern, which was hanging

[056-157]above her head. To light it and go in the direction of the sound would

[056-158]be the obvious way to solve the dread problem; but the conditions made

[056-159]her hesitate, and in a moment a cold sweat pervaded her at further

[056-160]sounds from the same quarter.

[056-161]They were low mutterings; at first like persons in conversation, but

[056-162]gradually resolving themselves into varieties of one voice. It was an

[056-163]endless monologue, like that we sometimes hear from inanimate nature in

[056-164]deep [[secrecy-and-concealment|secret]] places where water flows, or where ivy leaves flap against

[056-165]stones; but by degrees she was convinced that the voice was

[056-166]Winterborne’s. Yet who could be his listener, so mute and patient; for

[056-167]though he argued so rapidly and persistently, nobody replied.

[056-168]A dreadful enlightenment spread through the mind of Grace. “Oh,” she

[056-169]cried, in her anguish, as she hastily prepared herself to go out, “how

[056-170]selfishly correct I am always—too, too correct! Cruel propriety is

[056-171]killing the dearest heart that ever woman clasped to her own.”

[056-172]While speaking thus to herself she had lit the lantern, and hastening

[056-173]out without further thought, took the direction whence the mutterings

[056-174]had proceeded. The course was marked by a little path, which ended at a

[056-175]distance of about forty yards in a small erection of hurdles, not much

[056-176]larger than a shock of corn, such as were frequent in the woods and

[056-177]copses when the cutting season was going on. It was too slight even to

[056-178]be called a hovel, and was not high enough to stand upright in;

[056-179]appearing, in short, to be erected for the temporary shelter of fuel.

[056-180]The side towards Grace was open, and turning the light upon the

[056-181]interior, she beheld what her prescient fear had pictured in snatches

[056-182]all the way thither.
