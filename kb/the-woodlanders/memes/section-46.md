# Section 46

[046-001]CHAPTER XXXV.

[046-002]The mare paced along with firm and cautious tread through the copse

[046-003]where [[giles-winterborne|Winterborne]] had worked, and into the heavier soil where the oaks

[046-004]grew; past Great Willy, the largest oak in the wood, and thence towards

[046-005][[nellcombe-bottom|Nellcombe Bottom]], intensely dark now with overgrowth, and popularly

[046-006]supposed to be haunted by the spirits of the fratricides exorcised from

[046-007][[hintock-house|Hintock House]].

[046-008]By this time [[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Fitzpiers]] was quite recovered as to physical strength. But

[046-009]he had eaten nothing since making a hasty breakfast in [[london-in-the-woodlanders|London]] that

[046-010]morning, his anxiety about [[felice-charmond|Felice]] having hurried him away from home

[046-011]before dining; as a consequence, the old rum administered by his

[046-012]father-in-law flew to the young man’s head and loosened his tongue,

[046-013]without his ever having recognized who it was that had lent him a

[046-014]kindly hand. He began to speak in desultory sentences, Melbury still

[046-015]supporting him.

[046-016]“I’ve come all the way from London to-day,” said Fitzpiers. “Ah, that’s

[046-017]the place to meet your equals. I live at [[little-hintock|Hintock]]—worse, at Little

[046-018]Hintock—and I am quite lost there. There’s not a man within ten miles

[046-019]of Hintock who can comprehend me. I tell you, Farmer What’s-your-name,

[046-020]that I’m a man of [[education-and-class-mobility|education]]. I know several languages; the poets and I

[046-021]are familiar friends; I used to read more in metaphysics than anybody

[046-022]within fifty miles; and since I gave that up there’s nobody can match

[046-023]me in the whole county of [[wessex-in-the-woodlanders|Wessex]] as a scientist. Yet I an doomed to

[046-024]live with tradespeople in a miserable little hole like Hintock!”

[046-025]“Indeed!” muttered Melbury.

[046-026]Fitzpiers, increasingly energized by the alcohol, here reared himself

[046-027]up suddenly from the bowed posture he had hitherto held, thrusting his

[046-028]shoulders so violently against Melbury’s breast as to make it difficult

[046-029]for the old man to keep a hold on the reins. “People don’t appreciate

[046-030]me here!” the surgeon exclaimed; lowering his voice, he added, softly

[046-031]and slowly, “except one—except one!...A passionate soul, as warm as she

[046-032]is clever, as beautiful as she is warm, and as rich as she is

[046-033]beautiful. I say, old fellow, those claws of yours clutch me rather

[046-034]tight—rather like the eagle’s, you know, that ate out the liver of

[046-035]Pro—Pre—the man on Mount Caucasus. People don’t appreciate me, I say,

[046-036]except _her!_ Ah, gods, I am an unlucky man! She would have been mine,

[046-037]she would have taken my name; but unfortunately it cannot be so. I

[046-038]stooped to mate beneath me, and now I rue it.”

[046-039]The position was becoming a very trying one for Melbury, corporeally

[046-040]and mentally. He was obliged to steady Fitzpiers with his left arm, and

[046-041]he began to hate the contact. He hardly knew what to do. It was useless

[046-042]to remonstrate with Fitzpiers, in his intellectual confusion from the

[046-043]rum and from the fall. He remained silent, his hold upon his companion,

[046-044]however, being stern rather than compassionate.

[046-045]“You hurt me a little, farmer—though I am much obliged to you for your

[046-046]kindness. People don’t appreciate me, I say. Between ourselves, I am

[046-047]losing my practice here; and why? Because I see matchless attraction

[046-048]where matchless attraction is, both in person and position. I mention

[046-049]no names, so nobody will be the wiser. But I have lost her, in a

[046-050]legitimate sense, that is. If I were a free man now, things have come

[046-051]to such a pass that she could not refuse me; while with her fortune

[046-052](which I don’t covet for itself) I should have a [[fate-and-free-will|chance]] of satisfying

[046-053]an honorable ambition—a chance I have never had yet, and now never,

[046-054]never shall have, probably!”

[046-055]Melbury, his heart throbbing against the other’s backbone, and his

[046-056]brain on fire with indignation, ventured to mutter huskily, “Why?”

[046-057]The horse ambled on some steps before Fitzpiers replied, “Because I am

[046-058]tied and bound to another by law, as tightly as I am to you by your

[046-059]arm—not that I complain of your arm—I thank you for helping me. Well,

[046-060]where are we? Not nearly home yet?...Home, say I. It _is_ a home! When

[046-061]I might have been at the other house over there.” In a stupefied way he

[046-062]flung his hand in the direction of the park. “I was just two months too

[046-063]early in committing myself. Had I only seen the other first—”

[046-064]Here the old man’s arm gave Fitzpiers a convulsive shake. “What are you

[046-065]doing?” continued the latter. “Keep still, please, or put me down. I

[046-066]was saying that I lost her by a mere little two months! There is no

[046-067]chance for me now in this world, and it makes me reckless—reckless!

[046-068]Unless, indeed, anything should happen to the other one. She is amiable

[046-069]enough; but if anything should happen to her—and I hear she is

[046-070]ill—well, if it _should_, I should be free—and my fame, my happiness,

[046-071]would be insured.”

[046-072]These were the last words that Fitzpiers uttered in his seat in front

[046-073]of the [[timber-economy|timber]]-merchant. Unable longer to master himself, Melbury, the

[046-074]skin of his face compressed, whipped away his spare arm from

[046-075]Fitzpiers’s waist, and seized him by the collar.

[046-076]“You heartless villain—after all that we have done for ye!” he cried,

[046-077]with a quivering lip. “And the money of hers that you’ve had, and the

[046-078]roof we’ve provided to shelter ye! It is to me, [[george-melbury|George Melbury]], that

[046-079]you dare to talk like that!” The exclamation was accompanied by a

[046-080]powerful swing from the shoulder, which flung the young man head-long

[046-081]into the road, Fitzpiers fell with a heavy thud upon the stumps of some

[046-082]undergrowth which had been cut during the winter preceding. Darling

[046-083]continued her walk for a few paces farther and stopped.

[046-084]“God forgive me!” Melbury murmured, repenting of what he had done. “He

[046-085]tried me too sorely; and now perhaps I’ve murdered him!”

[046-086]He turned round in the saddle and looked towards the spot on which

[046-087]Fitzpiers had fallen. To his great surprise he beheld the surgeon rise

[046-088]to his feet with a bound, as if unhurt, and walk away rapidly under the

[046-089][[nature-in-the-woodlanders|trees]].

[046-090]Melbury listened till the rustle of Fitzpiers’s footsteps died away.

[046-091]“It might have been a crime, but for the mercy of Providence in

[046-092]providing leaves for his fall,” he said to himself. And then his mind

[046-093]reverted to the words of Fitzpiers, and his indignation so mounted

[046-094]within him that he almost wished the fall had put an end to the young

[046-095]man there and then.

[046-096]He had not ridden far when he discerned his own gray mare standing

[046-097]under some bushes. Leaving Darling for a moment, Melbury went forward

[046-098]and easily caught the younger animal, now disheartened at its freak. He

[046-099]then made the pair of them fast to a tree, and turning back, endeavored

[046-100]to find some trace of Fitzpiers, feeling pitifully that, after all, he

[046-101]had gone further than he intended with the offender.

[046-102]But though he threaded the wood hither and thither, his toes ploughing

[046-103]layer after layer of the little horny scrolls that had once been

[046-104]leaves, he could not find him. He stood still listening and looking

[046-105]round. The breeze was oozing through the network of boughs as through a

[046-106]strainer; the trunks and larger branches stood against the light of the

[046-107]sky in the forms of writhing men, gigantic candelabra, pikes, halberds,

[046-108]lances, and whatever besides the fancy chose to make of them. Giving up

[046-109]the search, Melbury came back to the horses, and walked slowly

[046-110]homeward, leading one in each hand.

[046-111]It happened that on this self-same evening a boy had been returning

[046-112]from Great to Little Hintock about the time of Fitzpiers’s and

[046-113]Melbury’s passage home along that route. A horse-collar that had been

[046-114]left at the harness-mender’s to be repaired was required for use at

[046-115]five o’clock next morning, and in consequence the boy had to fetch it

[046-116]overnight. He put his head through the collar, and accompanied his walk

[046-117]by whistling the one tune he knew, as an antidote to fear.

[046-118]The boy suddenly became aware of a horse trotting rather friskily along

[046-119]the track behind him, and not knowing whether to expect friend or foe,

[046-120]prudence suggested that he should cease his whistling and retreat among

[046-121]the trees till the horse and his rider had gone by; a course to which

[046-122]he was still more inclined when he found how noiselessly they

[046-123]approached, and saw that the horse looked pale, and remembered what he

[046-124]had read about Death in the Revelation. He therefore deposited the

[046-125]collar by a tree, and hid himself behind it. The horseman came on, and

[046-126]the youth, whose eyes were as keen as telescopes, to his great relief

[046-127]recognized the doctor.

[046-128]As Melbury surmised, Fitzpiers had in the darkness taken Blossom for

[046-129]Darling, and he had not discovered his mistake when he came up opposite

[046-130]the boy, though he was somewhat surprised at the liveliness of his

[046-131]usually placid mare. The only other pair of eyes on the spot whose

[046-132]vision was keen as the young carter’s were those of the horse; and,

[046-133]with that strongly conservative objection to the unusual which animals

[046-134]show, Blossom, on eying the collar under the tree—quite invisible to

[046-135]Fitzpiers—exercised none of the patience of the older horse, but shied

[046-136]sufficiently to unseat so second-rate an equestrian as the surgeon.

[046-137]He fell, and did not move, lying as Melbury afterwards found him. The

[046-138]boy ran away, salving his conscience for the desertion by thinking how

[046-139]vigorously he would spread the alarm of the accident when he got to

[046-140]Hintock—which he uncompromisingly did, incrusting the skeleton event

[046-141]with a load of dramatic horrors.

[046-142]Grace had returned, and the fly hired on her account, though not by her

[046-143]husband, at the Crown Hotel, [[shottsford-forum|Shottsford-Forum]], had been paid for and

[046-144]dismissed. The long drive had somewhat revived her, her illness being a

[046-145]feverish intermittent nervousness which had more to do with mind than

[046-146]body, and she walked about her sitting-room in something of a hopeful

[046-147]mood. [[mrs-melbury|Mrs. Melbury]] had told her as soon as she arrived that her husband

[046-148]had returned from London. He had gone out, she said, to see a patient,

[046-149]as she supposed, and he must soon be back, since he had had no dinner

[046-150]or tea. Grace would not allow her mind to harbor any suspicion of his

[046-151]whereabouts, and her step-mother said nothing of Mrs. Charmond’s

[046-152]rumored sorrows and plans of departure.

[046-153]So the young wife sat by the fire, waiting silently. She had left

[046-154]Hintock in a turmoil of feeling after the revelation of Mrs. Charmond,

[046-155]and had intended not to be at home when her husband returned. But she

[046-156]had thought the matter over, and had allowed her father’s influence to

[046-157]prevail and bring her back; and now somewhat regretted that Edgar’s

[046-158]arrival had preceded hers.

[046-159]By-and-by Mrs. Melbury came up-stairs with a slight air of flurry and

[046-160]abruptness.

[046-161]“I have something to tell—some bad news,” she said. “But you must not

[046-162]be alarmed, as it is not so bad as it might have been. Edgar has been

[046-163]thrown off his horse. We don’t think he is hurt much. It happened in

[046-164]the wood the other side of Nellcombe Bottom, where ’tis said the ghosts

[046-165]of the brothers walk.”

[046-166]She went on to give a few of the particulars, but none of the invented

[046-167]horrors that had been communicated by the boy. “I thought it better to

[046-168]tell you at once,” she added, “in case he should not be very well able

[046-169]to walk home, and somebody should bring him.”

[046-170]Mrs. Melbury really thought matters much worse than she represented,

[046-171]and Grace knew that she thought so. She sat down dazed for a few

[046-172]minutes, returning a negative to her step-mother’s inquiry if she could

[046-173]do anything for her. “But please go into the bedroom,” Grace said, on

[046-174]second thoughts, “and see if all is ready there—in case it is serious.”

[046-175]Mrs. Melbury thereupon called Grammer, and they did as directed,

[046-176]supplying the room with everything they could think of for the

[046-177]accommodation of an injured man.
