# Section 60

[060-001]As soon as she had quite recovered from this foiled attack upon her

[060-002]life, Grace went to [[marty-south|Marty South]]’s cottage. The current of her being had

[060-003]again set towards the lost [[giles-winterborne|Giles Winterborne]].

[060-004]“Marty,” she said, “we both loved him. We will go to his grave

[060-005]together.”

[060-006]Great [[little-hintock|Hintock]] church stood at the upper part of the village, and could

[060-007]be reached without passing through the street. In the dusk of the late

[060-008]September day they went thither by [[secrecy-and-concealment|secret]] ways, walking mostly in

[060-009]silence side by side, each busied with her own thoughts. Grace had a

[060-010]trouble exceeding Marty’s—that haunting sense of having put out the

[060-011]light of his life by her own hasty doings. She had tried to persuade

[060-012]herself that he might have died of his illness, even if she had not

[060-013]taken possession of his house. Sometimes she succeeded in her attempt;

[060-014]sometimes she did not.

[060-015]They stood by the grave together, and though the sun had gone down,

[060-016]they could see over the [[nature-in-the-woodlanders|woodland]] for miles, and down to the vale in

[060-017]which he had been accustomed to descend every year, with his portable

[060-018]mill and press, to make cider about this time.

[060-019]Perhaps Grace’s first grief, the discovery that if he had lived he

[060-020]could never have claimed her, had some power in softening this, the

[060-021]second. On Marty’s part there was the same consideration; never would

[060-022]she have been his. As no anticipation of gratified [[love-and-desire|affection]] had been

[060-023]in existence while he was with them, there was none to be disappointed

[060-024]now that he had gone.

[060-025]Grace was abased when, by degrees, she found that she had never

[060-026]understood Giles as Marty had done. Marty South alone, of all the [[gender-roles|women]]

[060-027]in Hintock and the world, had approximated to Winterborne’s level of

[060-028]intelligent intercourse with nature. In that respect she had formed the

[060-029]complement to him in the other sex, had lived as his counterpart, had

[060-030]subjoined her thought to his as a corollary.

[060-031]The casual glimpses which the ordinary population bestowed upon that

[060-032]wondrous world of sap and leaves called the [[the-woodland-tracts|Hintock woods]] had been with

[060-033]these two, Giles and Marty, a clear gaze. They had been possessed of

[060-034]its finer mysteries as of commonplace knowledge; had been able to read

[060-035]its hieroglyphs as ordinary writing; to them the sights and sounds of

[060-036]night, winter, wind, storm, amid those dense boughs, which had to Grace

[060-037]a touch of the uncanny, and even the supernatural, were simple

[060-038]occurrences whose origin, continuance, and laws they foreknew. They had

[060-039]planted together, and together they had felled; together they had, with

[060-040]the run of the years, mentally collected those remoter signs and

[060-041]symbols which, seen in few, were of runic obscurity, but all together

[060-042]made an alphabet. From the light lashing of the twigs upon their faces,

[060-043]when brushing through them in the dark, they could pronounce upon the

[060-044]species of the tree whence they stretched; from the quality of the

[060-045]wind’s murmur through a bough they could in like manner name its sort

[060-046]afar off. They knew by a glance at a trunk if its heart were sound, or

[060-047]tainted with incipient decay, and by the state of its upper twigs, the

[060-048]stratum that had been reached by its roots. The artifices of the

[060-049]seasons were seen by them from the conjuror’s own point of view, and

[060-050]not from that of the spectator’s.

[060-051]“He ought to have married _you_, Marty, and nobody else in the world!”

[060-052]said Grace, with conviction, after thinking somewhat in the above

[060-053]strain.

[060-054]Marty shook her head. “In all our out-door days and years together,

[060-055]ma’am,” she replied, “the one thing he never spoke of to me was love;

[060-056]nor I to him.”

[060-057]“Yet you and he could speak in a tongue that nobody else knew—not even

[060-058]my father, though he came nearest knowing—the tongue of the trees and

[060-059]fruits and flowers themselves.”

[060-060]She could indulge in mournful fancies like this to Marty; but the hard

[060-061]core to her grief—which Marty’s had not—remained. Had she been sure

[060-062]that Giles’s death resulted entirely from his exposure, it would have

[060-063]driven her well-nigh to insanity; but there was always that bare

[060-064]possibility that his exposure had only precipitated what was

[060-065]inevitable. She longed to believe that it had not done even this.

[060-066]There was only one man whose opinion on the circumstances she would be

[060-067]at all disposed to trust. Her husband was that man. Yet to ask him it

[060-068]would be necessary to detail the true conditions in which she and

[060-069]Winterborne had lived during these three or four critical days that

[060-070]followed her flight; and in withdrawing her original defiant

[060-071]announcement on that point, there seemed a weakness she did not care to

[060-072]show. She never doubted that [[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Fitzpiers]] would believe her if she made a

[060-073]clean confession of the actual situation; but to volunteer the

[060-074]correction would seem like signalling for a truce, and that, in her

[060-075]present frame of mind, was what she did not feel the need of.

[060-076]It will probably not appear a surprising statement, after what has been

[060-077]already declared of Fitzpiers, that the man whom Grace’s fidelity could

[060-078]not keep faithful was stung into passionate throbs of interest

[060-079]concerning her by her avowal of the contrary.

[060-080]He declared to himself that he had never known her dangerously full

[060-081]compass if she were capable of such a reprisal; and, melancholy as it

[060-082]may be to admit the fact, his own [[pride-and-humiliation|humiliation]] and regret engendered a

[060-083]smouldering admiration of her.

[060-084]He passed a month or two of great misery at [[exbury|Exbury]], the place to which

[060-085]he had retired—quite as much misery indeed as Grace, could she have

[060-086]known of it, would have been inclined to inflict upon any living

[060-087]creature, how much soever he might have wronged her. Then a sudden hope

[060-088]dawned upon him; he wondered if her affirmation were true. He asked

[060-089]himself whether it were not the act of a woman whose natural purity and

[060-090]innocence had blinded her to the contingencies of such an announcement.

[060-091]His wide experience of the sex had taught him that, in many cases,

[060-092]women who ventured on hazardous matters did so because they lacked an

[060-093]imagination sensuous enough to feel their full force. In this light

[060-094]Grace’s bold avowal might merely have denoted the desperation of one

[060-095]who was a child to the realities of obliquity.

[060-096]Fitzpiers’s mental sufferings and suspense led him at last to take a

[060-097]melancholy journey to the neighborhood of Little Hintock; and here he

[060-098]hovered for hours around the scene of the purest emotional experiences

[060-099]that he had ever known in his life. He walked about the woods that

[060-100]surrounded [[george-melbury|Melbury]]’s house, keeping out of sight like a criminal. It

[060-101]was a fine evening, and on his way homeward he passed near Marty

[060-102]South’s cottage. As usual she had lighted her candle without closing

[060-103]her shutters; he saw her within as he had seen her many times before.

[060-104]She was polishing tools, and though he had not wished to show himself,

[060-105]he could not resist speaking in to her through the half-open door.

[060-106]“What are you doing that for, Marty?”

[060-107]“Because I want to clean them. They are not mine.” He could see,

[060-108]indeed, that they were not hers, for one was a spade, large and heavy,

[060-109]and another was a bill-hook which she could only have used with both

[060-110]hands. The spade, though not a new one, had been so completely

[060-111]burnished that it was bright as silver.

[060-112]Fitzpiers somehow divined that they were Giles Winterborne’s, and he

[060-113]put the question to her.

[060-114]She replied in the affirmative. “I am going to keep ’em,” she said,

[060-115]“but I can’t get his apple-mill and press. I wish could; it is going to

[060-116]be sold, they say.”

[060-117]“Then I will buy it for you,” said Fitzpiers. “That will be making you

[060-118]a return for a kindness you did me.” His glance fell upon the girl’s

[060-119]rare-colored hair, which had grown again. “Oh, Marty, those locks of

[060-120]yours—and that letter! But it was a kindness to send it, nevertheless,”

[060-121]he added, musingly.

[060-122]After this there was confidence between them—such confidence as there

[060-123]had never been before. Marty was shy, indeed, of speaking about the

[060-124]letter, and her motives in writing it; but she thanked him warmly for

[060-125]his promise of the cider-press. She would travel with it in the autumn

[060-126]season, as he had done, she said. She would be quite strong enough,

[060-127]with old [[robert-creedle|Creedle]] as an assistant.

[060-128]“Ah! there was one nearer to him than you,” said Fitzpiers, referring

[060-129]to Winterborne. “One who lived where he lived, and was with him when he

[060-130]died.”

[060-131]Then Marty, suspecting that he did not know the true circumstances,

[060-132]from the fact that [[grace-melbury|Mrs. Fitzpiers]] and himself were living apart, told

[060-133]him of Giles’s generosity to Grace in giving up his house to her at the

[060-134]risk, and possibly the sacrifice, of his own life. When the surgeon

[060-135]heard it he almost envied Giles his chivalrous character. He expressed

[060-136]a wish to Marty that his visit to her should be kept secret, and went

[060-137]home thoughtful, feeling that in more that one sense his journey to

[060-138]Hintock had not been in vain.

[060-139]He would have given much to win Grace’s forgiveness then. But whatever

[060-140]he dared hope for in that kind from the future, there was nothing to be

[060-141]done yet, while Giles Winterborne’s memory was green. To wait was

[060-142]imperative. A little time might melt her frozen thoughts, and lead her

[060-143]to look on him with toleration, if not with love.

[060-144]CHAPTER XLV.

[060-145]Weeks and months of mourning for Winterborne had been passed by Grace

[060-146]in the soothing monotony of the memorial act to which she and Marty had

[060-147]devoted themselves. Twice a week the pair went in the dusk to Great

[060-148]Hintock, and, like the two mourners in _Cymbeline_, sweetened his sad

[060-149]grave with their flowers and their tears. Sometimes Grace thought that

[060-150]it was a pity neither one of them had been his wife for a little while,

[060-151]and given the world a copy of him who was so valuable in their eyes.

[060-152]Nothing ever had brought home to her with such force as this death how

[060-153]little acquirements and culture weigh beside sterling personal

[060-154]character. While her simple sorrow for his loss took a softer edge with

[060-155]the lapse of the autumn and winter seasons, her self-reproach at having

[060-156]had a possible hand in causing it knew little abatement.

[060-157]Little occurred at Hintock during these months of the fall and decay of

[060-158]the leaf. Discussion of the almost contemporaneous death of Mrs.

[060-159]Charmond abroad had waxed and waned. Fitzpiers had had a marvellous

[060-160]escape from being dragged into the inquiry which followed it, through

[060-161]the accident of their having parted just before under the influence of

[060-162]Marty South’s letter—the tiny instrument of a cause deep in nature.

[060-163]Her body was not brought home. It seemed to accord well with the fitful

[060-164]fever of that impassioned woman’s life that she should not have found a

[060-165]native grave. She had enjoyed but a life-interest in the estate, which,

[060-166]after her death, passed to a relative of her husband’s—one who knew not

[060-167][[felice-charmond|Felice]], one whose purpose seemed to be to blot out every vestige of

[060-168]her.

[060-169]On a certain day in February—the cheerful day of St. Valentine, in

[060-170]fact—a letter reached Mrs. Fitzpiers, which had been mentally promised

[060-171]her for that particular day a long time before.

[060-172]It announced that Fitzpiers was living at some midland town, where he

[060-173]had obtained a temporary practice as assistant to some local medical

[060-174]man, whose curative principles were all wrong, though he dared not set

[060-175]them right. He had thought fit to communicate with her on that day of

[060-176]tender traditions to inquire if, in the event of his obtaining a

[060-177]substantial practice that he had in view elsewhere, she could forget

[060-178]the past and bring herself to join him.
