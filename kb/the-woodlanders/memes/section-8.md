# Section 8

[008-001]As he drove off with her out of the town the glances of people fell

[008-002]upon them, the younger thinking that Mr. [[giles-winterborne|Winterborne]] was in a pleasant

[008-003]place, and wondering in what relation he stood towards her. Winterborne

[008-004]himself was unconscious of this. Occupied solely with the idea of

[008-005]having her in charge, he did not notice much with outward eye, neither

[008-006]observing how she was dressed, nor the effect of the picture they

[008-007]together composed in the landscape.

[008-008]Their conversation was in briefest phrase for some time, Grace being

[008-009]somewhat disconcerted, through not having understood till they were

[008-010]about to start that Giles was to be her sole conductor in place of her

[008-011]father. When they were in the open country he spoke.

[008-012]“Don’t Brownley’s farm-buildings look strange to you, now they have

[008-013]been moved bodily from the hollow where the old ones stood to the top

[008-014]of the hill?”

[008-015]She admitted that they did, though she should not have seen any

[008-016]difference in them if he had not pointed it out.

[008-017]“They had a good crop of bitter-sweets; they couldn’t grind them all”

[008-018](nodding towards an [[orchard-culture|orchard]] where some heaps of apples had been left

[008-019]lying ever since the ingathering).

[008-020]She said “Yes,” but looking at another orchard.

[008-021]“Why, you are looking at John-apple-[[nature-in-the-woodlanders|trees]]! You know bitter-sweets—you

[008-022]used to well enough!”

[008-023]“I am afraid I have forgotten, and it is getting too dark to

[008-024]distinguish.”

[008-025]Winterborne did not continue. It seemed as if the knowledge and

[008-026]interest which had formerly moved Grace’s mind had quite died away from

[008-027]her. He wondered whether the special attributes of his image in the

[008-028]past had evaporated like these other things.

[008-029]However that might be, the fact at present was merely this, that where

[008-030]he was seeing John-apples and farm-buildings she was beholding a far

[008-031]remoter scene—a scene no less innocent and simple, indeed, but much

[008-032]contrasting—a broad lawn in the fashionable suburb of a fast city, the

[008-033]evergreen leaves shining in the evening sun, amid which bounding girls,

[008-034]gracefully clad in artistic arrangements of blue, brown, red, black,

[008-035]and white, were playing at games, with laughter and chat, in all the

[008-036][[pride-and-humiliation|pride]] of life, the notes of piano and harp trembling in the air from

[008-037]the open windows adjoining. Moreover, they were girls—and this was a

[008-038]fact which [[grace-melbury|Grace Melbury]]’s delicate [[gender-roles|femininity]] could not lose sight

[008-039]of—whose parents Giles would have addressed with a deferential Sir or

[008-040]Madam. Beside this visioned scene the homely farmsteads did not quite

[008-041]hold their own from her present twenty-year point of survey. For all

[008-042]his woodland sequestration, Giles knew the primitive simplicity of the

[008-043]subject he had started, and now sounded a deeper note.

[008-044]“’Twas very odd what we said to each other years ago; I often think of

[008-045]it. I mean our saying that if we still liked each other when you were

[008-046]twenty and I twenty-five, we’d—”

[008-047]“It was child’s tattle.”

[008-048]“H’m!” said Giles, suddenly.

[008-049]“I mean we were young,” said she, more considerately. That gruff manner

[008-050]of his in making inquiries reminded her that he was unaltered in much.

[008-051]“Yes....I beg your pardon, Miss [[george-melbury|Melbury]]; your father _sent_ me to meet

[008-052]you to-day.”

[008-053]“I know it, and I am glad of it.”

[008-054]He seemed satisfied with her tone and went on: “At that time you were

[008-055]sitting beside me at the back of your father’s covered car, when we

[008-056]were coming home from gypsying, all the party being squeezed in

[008-057]together as tight as sheep in an auction-pen. It got darker and darker,

[008-058]and I said—I forget the exact words—but I put my arm round your waist

[008-059]and there you let it stay till your father, sitting in front suddenly

[008-060]stopped telling his story to Farmer Bollen, to light his pipe. The

[008-061]flash shone into the car, and showed us all up distinctly; my arm flew

[008-062]from your waist like lightning; yet not so quickly but that some of ’em

[008-063]had seen, and laughed at us. Yet your father, to our amazement, instead

[008-064]of being angry, was mild as milk, and seemed quite pleased. Have you

[008-065]forgot all that, or haven’t you?”

[008-066]She owned that she remembered it very well, now that he mentioned the

[008-067]circumstances. “But, goodness! I must have been in short frocks,” she

[008-068]said.

[008-069]“Come now, Miss Melbury, that won’t do! Short frocks, indeed! You know

[008-070]better, as well as I.”

[008-071]Grace thereupon declared that she would not argue with an old friend

[008-072]she valued so highly as she valued him, saying the words with the easy

[008-073]elusiveness that will be polite at all costs. It might possibly be

[008-074]true, she added, that she was getting on in girlhood when that event

[008-075]took place; but if it were so, then she was virtually no less than an

[008-076]old woman now, so far did the time seem removed from her present. “Do

[008-077]you ever look at things philosophically instead of personally?” she

[008-078]asked.

[008-079]“I can’t say that I do,” answered Giles, his eyes lingering far ahead

[008-080]upon a dark spot, which proved to be a brougham.

[008-081]“I think you may, sometimes, with advantage,” said she. “Look at

[008-082]yourself as a pitcher drifting on the stream with other pitchers, and

[008-083]consider what contrivances are most desirable for avoiding cracks in

[008-084]general, and not only for saving your poor one. Shall I tell you all

[008-085]about Bath or Cheltenham, or places on the Continent that I visited

[008-086]last summer?”

[008-087]“With all my heart.”

[008-088]She then described places and persons in such terms as might have been

[008-089]used for that purpose by any woman to any man within the four seas, so

[008-090]entirely absent from that description was everything specially

[008-091]appertaining to her own existence. When she had done she said, gayly,

[008-092]“Now do you tell me in return what has happened in [[little-hintock|Hintock]] since I have

[008-093]been away.”

[008-094]“Anything to keep the conversation away from her and me,” said Giles

[008-095]within him.

[008-096]It was true cultivation had so far advanced in the soil of Miss

[008-097]Melbury’s mind as to lead her to talk by rote of anything save of that

[008-098]she knew well, and had the greatest interest in developing—that is to

[008-099]say, herself.

[008-100]He had not proceeded far with his somewhat bald [[narrative-style|narration]] when they

[008-101]drew near the carriage that had been preceding them for some time. Miss

[008-102]Melbury inquired if he knew whose carriage it was.

[008-103]Winterborne, although he had seen it, had not taken it into account. On

[008-104]examination, he said it was [[felice-charmond|Mrs. Charmond]]’s.

[008-105]Grace watched the vehicle and its easy roll, and seemed to feel more

[008-106]nearly akin to it than to the one she was in.

[008-107]“Pooh! We can polish off the mileage as well as they, come to that,”

[008-108]said Winterborne, reading her mind; and rising to emulation at what it

[008-109]bespoke, he whipped on the horse. This it was which had brought the

[008-110]nose of Mr. Melbury’s old gray close to the back of Mrs. Charmond’s

[008-111]much-eclipsing vehicle.

[008-112]“There’s [[marty-south|Marty South]] sitting up with the coachman,” said he, discerning

[008-113]her by her dress.

[008-114]“Ah, poor Marty! I must ask her to come to see me this very evening.

[008-115]How does she happen to be riding there?”

[008-116]“I don’t know. It is very singular.”

[008-117]Thus these people with converging destinies went along the road

[008-118]together, till Winterborne, leaving the track of the carriage, turned

[008-119]into Little Hintock, where almost the first house was the

[008-120][[timber-economy|timber]]-merchant’s. Pencils of dancing light streamed out of the windows

[008-121]sufficiently to show the white laurestinus flowers, and glance over the

[008-122]polished leaves of laurel. The interior of the rooms could be seen

[008-123]distinctly, warmed up by the fire-flames, which in the parlor were

[008-124]reflected from the glass of the pictures and bookcase, and in the

[008-125]kitchen from the utensils and ware.

[008-126]“Let us look at the dear place for a moment before we call them,” she

[008-127]said.

[008-128]In the kitchen dinner was preparing; for though Melbury dined at one

[008-129]o’clock at other times, to-day the meal had been kept back for Grace. A

[008-130]rickety old spit was in motion, its end being fixed in the fire-dog,

[008-131]and the whole kept going by means of a cord conveyed over pulleys along

[008-132]the ceiling to a large stone suspended in a corner of the room. Old

[008-133][[grammer-oliver|Grammer Oliver]] came and wound it up with a rattle like that of a mill.

[008-134]In the parlor a large shade of [[mrs-melbury|Mrs. Melbury]]’s head fell on the wall and

[008-135]ceiling; but before the girl had regarded this room many moments their

[008-136]presence was discovered, and her father and stepmother came out to

[008-137]welcome her.

[008-138]The character of the Melbury family was of that kind which evinces some

[008-139]shyness in showing strong emotion among each other: a trait frequent in

[008-140]rural households, and one which stands in curiously inverse relation to

[008-141]most of the peculiarities distinguishing villagers from the people of

[008-142]towns. Thus hiding their warmer feelings under commonplace talk all

[008-143]round, Grace’s reception produced no extraordinary demonstrations. But

[008-144]that more was felt than was enacted appeared from the fact that her

[008-145]father, in taking her in-doors, quite forgot the presence of Giles

[008-146]without, as did also Grace herself. He said nothing, but took the gig

[008-147]round to the yard and called out from the spar-house the man who

[008-148]particularly attended to these matters when there was no conversation

[008-149]to draw him off among the copse-workers inside. Winterborne then

[008-150]returned to the door with the intention of entering the house.

[008-151]The family had gone into the parlor, and were still absorbed in

[008-152]themselves. The fire was, as before, the only light, and it irradiated

[008-153]Grace’s face and hands so as to make them look wondrously smooth and

[008-154]fair beside those of the two elders; shining also through the loose

[008-155]hair about her temples as sunlight through a brake. Her father was

[008-156]surveying her in a dazed conjecture, so much had she developed and

[008-157]progressed in manner and stature since he last had set eyes on her.

[008-158]Observing these things, Winterborne remained dubious by the door,

[008-159]mechanically tracing with his fingers certain time-worn letters carved

[008-160]in the jambs—initials of by-gone generations of householders who had

[008-161]lived and died there.

[008-162]No, he declared to himself, he would not enter and join the family;

[008-163]they had forgotten him, and it was enough for to-day that he had

[008-164]brought her home. Still, he was a little surprised that her father’s

[008-165]eagerness to send him for Grace should have resulted in such an

[008-166]anticlimax as this.

[008-167]He walked softly away into the lane towards his own house, looking back

[008-168]when he reached the turning, from which he could get a last glimpse of

[008-169]the timber-merchant’s roof. He hazarded guesses as to what Grace was

[008-170]saying just at that moment, and murmured, with some self-derision,

[008-171]“nothing about me!” He looked also in the other direction, and saw

[008-172]against the sky the thatched hip and solitary chimney of Marty’s

[008-173]cottage, and thought of her too, struggling bravely along under that

[008-174]humble shelter, among her spar-gads and pots and skimmers.

[008-175]At the timber-merchant’s, in the mean time, the conversation flowed;

[008-176]and, as Giles Winterborne had rightly enough deemed, on subjects in

[008-177]which he had no share. Among the excluding matters there was, for one,

[008-178]the effect upon Mr. Melbury of the womanly mien and manners of his

[008-179]daughter, which took him so much unawares that, though it did not make

[008-180]him absolutely forget the existence of her conductor homeward, thrust

[008-181]Giles’s image back into quite the obscurest cellarage of his brain.

[008-182]Another was his interview with Mrs. Charmond’s agent that morning, at

[008-183]which the lady herself had been present for a few minutes. Melbury had

[008-184]purchased some standing timber from her a long time before, and now

[008-185]that the date had come for felling it he was left to pursue almost his

[008-186]own course. This was what the household were actually talking of during

[008-187]Giles’s cogitation without; and Melbury’s satisfaction with the clear

[008-188]atmosphere that had arisen between himself and the deity of the groves

[008-189]which enclosed his residence was the cause of a counterbalancing

[008-190]mistiness on the side towards Winterborne.
