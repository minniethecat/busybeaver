[001-052]CHAPTER I.

[001-053]The rambler who, for old association or other reasons, should trace the

[001-054]forsaken coach-road running almost in a meridional line from Bristol to

[001-055]the south shore of England, would find himself during the latter half

[001-056]of his journey in the vicinity of some extensive woodlands,

[001-057]interspersed with apple-orchards. Here the [[nature-in-the-woodlanders|trees]], [[timber-economy|timber]] or

[001-058]fruit-bearing, as the case may be, make the wayside hedges ragged by

[001-059]their drip and shade, stretching over the road with easeful

[001-060]horizontality, as if they found the unsubstantial air an adequate

[001-061]support for their limbs. At one place, where a hill is crossed, the

[001-062]largest of the woods shows itself bisected by the high-way, as the head

[001-063]of thick hair is bisected by the white line of its parting. The spot is

[001-064]lonely.

[001-065]The physiognomy of a deserted highway expresses solitude to a degree

[001-066]that is not reached by mere dales or downs, and bespeaks a tomb-like

[001-067]stillness more emphatic than that of glades and pools. The contrast of

[001-068]what is with what might be probably accounts for this. To step, for

[001-069]instance, at the place under notice, from the hedge of the plantation

[001-070]into the adjoining pale thoroughfare, and pause amid its emptiness for

[001-071]a moment, was to exchange by the act of a single stride the simple

[001-072]absence of human companionship for an incubus of the forlorn.

[001-073]At this spot, on the lowering evening of a by-gone winter’s day, there

[001-074]stood a man who had entered upon the scene much in the aforesaid

[001-075]manner. Alighting into the road from a stile hard by, he, though by no

[001-076]means a “chosen vessel” for impressions, was temporarily influenced by

[001-077]some such feeling of being suddenly more alone than before he had

[001-078]emerged upon the highway.

[001-079]It could be seen by a glance at his rather finical style of dress that

[001-080]he did not belong to the country proper; and from his air, after a

[001-081]while, that though there might be a sombre beauty in the scenery, music

[001-082]in the breeze, and a wan procession of coaching ghosts in the sentiment

[001-083]of this old turnpike-road, he was mainly puzzled about the way. The

[001-084]dead men’s work that had been expended in climbing that hill, the

[001-085]blistered soles that had trodden it, and the tears that had wetted it,

[001-086]were not his concern; for [[fate-and-free-will|fate]] had given him no time for any but

[001-087]practical things.

[001-088]He looked north and south, and mechanically prodded the ground with his

[001-089]walking-stick. A closer glance at his face corroborated the testimony

[001-090]of his clothes. It was self-complacent, yet there was small apparent

[001-091]ground for such complacence. Nothing irradiated it; to the eye of the

[001-092]magician in character, if not to the ordinary observer, the expression

[001-093]enthroned there was absolute submission to and belief in a little

[001-094]assortment of forms and habitudes.

[001-095]At first not a soul appeared who could enlighten him as he desired, or

[001-096]seemed likely to appear that night. But presently a slight noise of

[001-097]laboring wheels and the steady dig of a horse’s shoe-tips became

[001-098]audible; and there loomed in the notch of the hill and plantation that

[001-099]the road formed here at the summit a carrier’s van drawn by a single

[001-100]horse. When it got nearer, he said, with some relief to himself, “’Tis

[001-101][[mrs-dollery|Mrs. Dollery]]’s—this will help me.”

[001-102]The vehicle was half full of passengers, mostly [[gender-roles|women]]. He held up his

[001-103]stick at its approach, and the woman who was driving drew rein.

[001-104]“I’ve been trying to find a short way to [[little-hintock|Little Hintock]] this last

[001-105]half-hour, Mrs. Dollery,” he said. “But though I’ve been to Great

[001-106]Hintock and [[hintock-house|Hintock House]] half a dozen times I am at fault about the

[001-107]small village. You can help me, I dare say?”

[001-108]She assured him that she could—that as she went to [[great-hintock|Great Hintock]] her

[001-109]van passed near it—that it was only up the lane that branched out of

[001-110]the lane into which she was about to turn—just ahead. “Though,”

[001-111]continued Mrs. Dollery, “’tis such a little small place that, as a town

[001-112]gentleman, you’d need have a candle and lantern to find it if ye don’t

[001-113]know where ’tis. Bedad! I wouldn’t live there if they’d pay me to. Now

[001-114]at Great Hintock you do see the world a bit.”

[001-115]He mounted and sat beside her, with his feet outside, where they were

[001-116]ever and anon brushed over by the horse’s tail.

[001-117]This van, driven and owned by Mrs. Dollery, was rather a movable

[001-118]attachment of the roadway than an extraneous object, to those who knew

[001-119]it well. The old horse, whose hair was of the roughness and color of

[001-120]heather, whose leg-joints, shoulders, and hoofs were distorted by

[001-121]harness and drudgery from colthood—though if all had their rights, he

[001-122]ought, symmetrical in outline, to have been picking the herbage of some

[001-123]Eastern plain instead of tugging here—had trodden this road almost

[001-124]daily for twenty years. Even his subjection was not made congruous

[001-125]throughout, for the harness being too short, his tail was not drawn

[001-126]through the crupper, so that the breeching slipped awkwardly to one

[001-127]side. He knew every subtle incline of the seven or eight miles of

[001-128]ground between Hintock and [[sherton-abbas|Sherton Abbas]]—the market-town to which he

[001-129]journeyed—as accurately as any surveyor could have learned it by a

[001-130]Dumpy level.

[001-131]The vehicle had a square black tilt which nodded with the motion of the

[001-132]wheels, and at a point in it over the driver’s head was a hook to which

[001-133]the reins were hitched at times, when they formed a catenary curve from

[001-134]the horse’s shoulders. Somewhere about the axles was a loose chain,

[001-135]whose only known purpose was to clink as it went. Mrs. Dollery, having

[001-136]to hop up and down many times in the service of her passengers, wore,

[001-137]especially in windy weather, short leggings under her gown for

[001-138]modesty’s sake, and instead of a bonnet a felt hat tied down with a

[001-139]handkerchief, to guard against an earache to which she was frequently

[001-140]subject. In the rear of the van was a glass window, which she cleaned

[001-141]with her pocket-handkerchief every market-day before starting. Looking

[001-142]at the van from the back, the spectator could thus see through its

[001-143]interior a square piece of the same sky and landscape that he saw

[001-144]without, but intruded on by the profiles of the seated passengers, who,

[001-145]as they rumbled onward, their lips moving and heads nodding in animated

[001-146]private converse, remained in happy unconsciousness that their

[001-147]mannerisms and facial peculiarities were sharply defined to the public

[001-148]eye.

[001-149]This hour of coming home from market was the happy one, if not the

[001-150]happiest, of the week for them. Snugly ensconced under the tilt, they

[001-151]could forget the sorrows of the world without, and survey life and

[001-152]recapitulate the incidents of the day with placid smiles.

[001-153]The passengers in the back part formed a group to themselves, and while

[001-154]the new-comer spoke to the proprietress, they indulged in a

[001-155]confidential chat about him as about other people, which the noise of

[001-156]the van rendered inaudible to himself and Mrs. Dollery, sitting

[001-157]forward.

[001-158]“’Tis [[percombe|Barber Percombe]]—he that’s got the waxen woman in his window at

[001-159]the top of Abbey Street,” said one. “What business can bring him from

[001-160]his shop out here at this time and not a journeyman hair-cutter, but a

[001-161]master-barber that’s left off his pole because ’tis not genteel!”

[001-162]They listened to his conversation, but Mr. Percombe, though he had

[001-163]nodded and spoken genially, seemed indisposed to gratify the curiosity

[001-164]which he had aroused; and the unrestrained flow of ideas which had

[001-165]animated the inside of the van before his arrival was checked

[001-166]thenceforward.

[001-167]Thus they rode on till they turned into a half-invisible little lane,

[001-168]whence, as it reached the verge of an eminence, could be discerned in

[001-169]the dusk, about half a mile to the right, gardens and orchards sunk in

[001-170]a concave, and, as it were, snipped out of the woodland. From this

[001-171]self-contained place rose in stealthy silence tall stems of smoke,

[001-172]which the eye of imagination could trace downward to their root on

[001-173]quiet hearth-stones festooned overhead with hams and flitches. It was

[001-174]one of those sequestered spots outside the gates of the world where may

[001-175]usually be found more meditation than action, and more passivity than

[001-176]meditation; where reasoning proceeds on narrow premises, and results in

[001-177]inferences wildly imaginative; yet where, from time to time, no less

[001-178]than in other places, dramas of a grandeur and unity truly Sophoclean

[001-179]are enacted in the real, by virtue of the concentrated passions and

[001-180]closely knit interdependence of the lives therein.

[001-181]This place was the Little Hintock of the master-barber’s search. The

[001-182]coming night gradually obscured the smoke of the chimneys, but the

[001-183]position of the sequestered little world could still be distinguished

[001-184]by a few faint lights, winking more or less ineffectually through the

[001-185]leafless boughs, and the undiscerned songsters they bore, in the form

[001-186]of balls of feathers, at roost among them.

[001-187]Out of the lane followed by the van branched a yet smaller lane, at the

[001-188]corner of which the barber alighted, Mrs. Dollery’s van going on to the

[001-189]larger village, whose superiority to the despised smaller one as an

[001-190]exemplar of the world’s movements was not particularly apparent in its

[001-191]means of approach.

[001-192]“A very clever and learned young doctor, who, they say, is in league

[001-193]with the devil, lives in the place you be going to—not because there’s

[001-194]anybody for’n to cure there, but because ’tis the middle of his

[001-195]district.”

[001-196]The observation was flung at the barber by one of the women at parting,

[001-197]as a last attempt to get at his errand that way.

[001-198]But he made no reply, and without further pause the pedestrian plunged

[001-199]towards the umbrageous nook, and paced cautiously over the dead leaves

[001-200]which nearly buried the road or street of the hamlet. As very few

[001-201]people except themselves passed this way after dark, a majority of the

[001-202]denizens of Little Hintock deemed window-curtains unnecessary; and on

[001-203]this account Mr. Percombe made it his business to stop opposite the

[001-204]casements of each cottage that he came to, with a demeanor which showed

[001-205]that he was endeavoring to conjecture, from the persons and things he

[001-206]observed within, the whereabouts of somebody or other who resided here.

[001-207]Only the smaller dwellings interested him; one or two houses, whose

[001-208]size, antiquity, and rambling appurtenances signified that

[001-209]notwithstanding their [[rural-isolation|remoteness]] they must formerly have been, if they

[001-210]were not still, inhabited by people of a certain social standing, being

[001-211]neglected by him entirely. Smells of pomace, and the hiss of fermenting

[001-212]cider, which reached him from the back quarters of other tenements,

[001-213]revealed the recent occupation of some of the inhabitants, and joined

[001-214]with the scent of decay from the perishing leaves underfoot.

[001-215]Half a dozen dwellings were passed without result. The next, which

[001-216]stood opposite a tall tree, was in an exceptional state of radiance,

[001-217]the flickering brightness from the inside shining up the chimney and

[001-218]making a luminous mist of the emerging smoke. The interior, as seen

[001-219]through the window, caused him to draw up with a terminative air and

[001-220]watch. The house was rather large for a cottage, and the door, which

[001-221]opened immediately into the living-room, stood ajar, so that a ribbon

[001-222]of light fell through the opening into the dark atmosphere without.

[001-223]Every now and then a moth, decrepit from the late season, would flit

[001-224]for a moment across the out-coming rays and disappear again into the

[001-225]night.
