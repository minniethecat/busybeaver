# Mrs. Dollery

Mrs. Dollery is the carrier who drives the van between Little Hintock and the market town of Sherton-Abbas, serving as one of the novel's links between the secluded woodland community and the wider world. She appears in the opening chapter and at key transitional moments, her van functioning as a narrative device that brings outsiders into Hintock and carries news outward.

## 身份与背景

Mrs. Dollery's van is described as "rather a movable attachment of the roadway than an extraneous object" [001-117], suggesting her long-established place in the local transport network. The van itself is intimately connected to her identity — it "was to clink as it went" [001-135], and she moves through the landscape with the familiarity of someone who has traversed the same route countless times [001-105].

## 性格与特征

Mrs. Dollery is pragmatic and straightforward, aware of her town's limitations. She tells the barber — who is in fact Dr. Fitzpiers in disguise — that Hintock "'tis such a little small place that, as a town clerk, you'd have nothing to do" [001-111]. Her assessment reveals the local perspective on Hintock's insignificance while also demonstrating her willingness to engage with strangers. She is observant and communicative, serving as an informal conduit of information between the rural hamlet and the market town.

## 叙事线索

Mrs. Dollery first appears in the novel's opening pages, driving her van along the road from Sherton-Abbas and picking up the barber (Fitzpiers) along the way [001-101] [001-105]. She delivers a frank assessment of Hintock to her passenger [001-111]. The van and its driver reappear at significant transitions: when Grace returns to Hintock, Mrs. Dollery's van delivers a package that draws the attention of the villagers [031-103] [031-109]. She stays at Melbury's house for some time on that occasion [031-117]. Later, a Sherton man waits for Mrs. Dollery's van to bring news [044-066].

## 意义与解读

Mrs. Dollery and her van embody the thin thread connecting Little Hintock to the outside world. Her recurring presence at moments of arrival and departure — Fitzpiers's entrance, Grace's return, the transmission of news — makes her a structural marker of the boundary between the woodland community and the rest of Wessex. Her van is a liminal space where outsiders begin their transition into the Hintock world, and where news from the town reaches the isolated hamlet.
