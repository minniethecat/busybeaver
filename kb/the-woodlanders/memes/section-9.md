# Section 9

[009-001]“So thoroughly does she trust me,” said [[george-melbury|Melbury]], “that I might fell,

[009-002]top, or lop, on my own judgment, any stick o’ [[timber-economy|timber]] whatever in her

[009-003]wood, and fix the price o’t, and settle the matter. But, name it all! I

[009-004]wouldn’t do such a thing. However, it may be useful to have this good

[009-005]understanding with her....I wish she took more interest in the place,

[009-006]and stayed here all the year round.”

[009-007]“I am afraid ’tis not her regard for you, but her dislike of [[little-hintock|Hintock]],

[009-008]that makes her so easy about the [[nature-in-the-woodlanders|trees]],” said [[mrs-melbury|Mrs. Melbury]].

[009-009]When dinner was over, Grace took a candle and began to ramble

[009-010]pleasurably through the rooms of her old home, from which she had

[009-011]latterly become wellnigh an alien. Each nook and each object revived a

[009-012]memory, and simultaneously modified it. The chambers seemed lower than

[009-013]they had appeared on any previous occasion of her return, the surfaces

[009-014]of both walls and ceilings standing in such relations to the eye that

[009-015]it could not avoid taking microscopic note of their irregularities and

[009-016]old fashion. Her own bedroom wore at once a look more familiar than

[009-017]when she had left it, and yet a face estranged. The world of little

[009-018]things therein gazed at her in helpless stationariness, as though they

[009-019]had tried and been unable to make any progress without her presence.

[009-020]Over the place where her candle had been accustomed to stand, when she

[009-021]had used to read in bed till the midnight hour, there was still the

[009-022]brown spot of smoke. She did not know that her father had taken

[009-023]especial care to keep it from being cleaned off.

[009-024]Having concluded her perambulation of this now uselessly commodious

[009-025]edifice, Grace began to feel that she had come a long journey since the

[009-026]morning; and when her father had been up himself, as well as his wife,

[009-027]to see that her room was comfortable and the fire burning, she prepared

[009-028]to retire for the night. No sooner, however, was she in bed than her

[009-029]momentary sleepiness took itself off, and she wished she had stayed up

[009-030]longer. She amused herself by listening to the old familiar noises that

[009-031]she could hear to be still going on down-stairs, and by looking towards

[009-032]the window as she lay. The blind had been drawn up, as she used to have

[009-033]it when a girl, and she could just discern the dim tree-tops against

[009-034]the sky on the neighboring hill. Beneath this meeting-line of light and

[009-035]shade nothing was visible save one solitary point of light, which

[009-036]blinked as the tree-twigs waved to and fro before its beams. From its

[009-037]position it seemed to radiate from the window of a house on the

[009-038]hill-side. The house had been empty when she was last at home, and she

[009-039]wondered who inhabited the place now.

[009-040]Her conjectures, however, were not intently carried on, and she was

[009-041]watching the light quite idly, when it gradually changed color, and at

[009-042]length shone blue as sapphire. Thus it remained several minutes, and

[009-043]then it passed through violet to red.

[009-044]Her curiosity was so widely awakened by the phenomenon that she sat up

[009-045]in bed, and stared steadily at the shine. An appearance of this sort,

[009-046]sufficient to excite attention anywhere, was no less than a marvel in

[009-047]Hintock, as Grace had known the hamlet. Almost every diurnal and

[009-048]nocturnal effect in that woodland place had hitherto been the direct

[009-049]result of the regular terrestrial roll which produced the season’s

[009-050]changes; but here was something dissociated from these normal

[009-051]sequences, and foreign to local habit and knowledge.

[009-052]It was about this moment that Grace heard the household below preparing

[009-053]to retire, the most emphatic noise in the proceeding being that of her

[009-054]father bolting the doors. Then the stairs creaked, and her father and

[009-055]mother passed her chamber. The last to come was [[grammer-oliver|Grammer Oliver]].

[009-056]Grace slid out of bed, ran across the room, and lifting the latch,

[009-057]said, “I am not asleep, Grammer. Come in and talk to me.”

[009-058]Before the old woman had entered, Grace was again under the bedclothes.

[009-059]Grammer set down her candlestick, and seated herself on the edge of

[009-060][[grace-melbury|Miss Melbury]]’s coverlet.

[009-061]“I want you to tell me what light that is I see on the hill-side,” said

[009-062]Grace.

[009-063]Mrs. Oliver looked across. “Oh, that,” she said, “is from the doctor’s.

[009-064]He’s often doing things of that sort. Perhaps you don’t know that we’ve

[009-065]a doctor living here now—[[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Mr. Fitzpiers]] by name?”

[009-066]Grace admitted that she had not heard of him.

[009-067]“Well, then, miss, he’s come here to get up a practice. I know him very

[009-068]well, through going there to help ’em scrub sometimes, which your

[009-069]father said I might do, if I wanted to, in my spare time. Being a

[009-070]bachelor-man, he’ve only a lad in the house. Oh yes, I know him very

[009-071]well. Sometimes he’ll talk to me as if I were his own mother.”

[009-072]“Indeed.”

[009-073]“Yes. ‘Grammer,’ he said one day, when I asked him why he came here

[009-074]where there’s hardly anybody living, ‘I’ll tell you why I came here. I

[009-075]took a map, and I marked on it where [[old-jones|Dr. Jones]]’s practice ends to the

[009-076]north of this district, and where Mr. Taylor’s ends on the south, and

[009-077]little Jimmy Green’s on the east, and somebody else’s to the west. Then

[009-078]I took a pair of compasses, and found the exact middle of the country

[009-079]that was left between these bounds, and that middle was Little Hintock;

[009-080]so here I am....’ But, Lord, there: poor young man!”

[009-081]“Why?”

[009-082]“He said, ‘Grammer Oliver, I’ve been here three months, and although

[009-083]there are a good many people in the Hintocks and the villages round,

[009-084]and a scattered practice is often a very good one, I don’t seem to get

[009-085]many patients. And there’s no society at all; and I’m pretty near

[009-086]melancholy mad,’ he said, with a great yawn. ‘I should be quite if it

[009-087]were not for my books, and my lab—laboratory, and what not. Grammer, I

[009-088]was made for higher things.’ And then he’d yawn and yawn again.”

[009-089]“Was he really made for higher things, do you think? I mean, is he

[009-090]clever?”

[009-091]“Well, no. How can he be clever? He may be able to jine up a broken man

[009-092]or woman after a fashion, and put his finger upon an ache if you tell

[009-093]him nearly where ’tis; but these young men—they should live to my time

[009-094]of life, and then they’d see how clever they were at five-and-twenty!

[009-095]And yet he’s a projick, a real projick, and says the oddest of rozums.

[009-096]‘Ah, Grammer,’ he said, at another time, ‘let me tell you that

[009-097]Everything is Nothing. There’s only Me and not Me in the whole world.’

[009-098]And he told me that no man’s hands could help what they did, any more

[009-099]than the hands of a clock....Yes, he’s a man of strange meditations,

[009-100]and his eyes seem to see as far as the north star.”

[009-101]“He will soon go away, no doubt.”

[009-102]“I don’t think so.” Grace did not say “Why?” and Grammer hesitated. At

[009-103]last she went on: “Don’t tell your father or mother, miss, if I let you

[009-104]know a [[secrecy-and-concealment|secret]].”

[009-105]Grace gave the required promise.

[009-106]“Well, he talks of buying me; so he won’t go away just yet.”

[009-107]“Buying you!—how?”

[009-108]“Not my soul—my body, when I’m dead. One day when I was there cleaning,

[009-109]he said, ‘Grammer, you’ve a large brain—a very large organ of brain,’

[009-110]he said. ‘A woman’s is usually four ounces less than a man’s; but yours

[009-111]is man’s size.’ Well, then—hee, hee!—after he’d flattered me a bit like

[009-112]that, he said he’d give me ten pounds to have me as a natomy after my

[009-113]death. Well, knowing I’d no chick nor chiel left, and nobody with any

[009-114]interest in me, I thought, faith, if I can be of any use to my

[009-115]fellow-creatures after I’m gone they are welcome to my services; so I

[009-116]said I’d think it over, and would most likely agree and take the ten

[009-117]pounds. Now this is a secret, miss, between us two. The money would be

[009-118]very useful to me; and I see no harm in it.”

[009-119]“Of course there’s no harm. But oh, Grammer, how can you think to do

[009-120]it? I wish you hadn’t told me.”

[009-121]“I wish I hadn’t—if you don’t like to know it, miss. But you needn’t

[009-122]mind. Lord—hee, hee!—I shall keep him waiting many a year yet, bless

[009-123]ye!”

[009-124]“I hope you will, I am sure.”

[009-125]The girl thereupon fell into such deep reflection that conversation

[009-126]languished, and Grammer Oliver, taking her candle, wished Miss Melbury

[009-127]good-night. The latter’s eyes rested on the distant glimmer, around

[009-128]which she allowed her reasoning fancy to play in vague eddies that

[009-129]shaped the doings of the philosopher behind that light on the lines of

[009-130]intelligence just received. It was strange to her to come back from the

[009-131]world to Little Hintock and find in one of its nooks, like a tropical

[009-132]plant in a hedgerow, a nucleus of advanced ideas and practices which

[009-133]had nothing in common with the life around. Chemical experiments,

[009-134]anatomical projects, and metaphysical conceptions had found a strange

[009-135]home here.

[009-136]Thus she remained thinking, the imagined pursuits of the man behind the

[009-137]light intermingling with conjectural sketches of his personality, till

[009-138]her eyes fell together with their own heaviness, and she slept.

[009-139]CHAPTER VII.

[009-140]Kaleidoscopic dreams of a weird alchemist-surgeon, Grammer Oliver’s

[009-141]skeleton, and the face of [[giles-winterborne|Giles Winterborne]], brought Grace Melbury to

[009-142]the morning of the next day. It was fine. A north wind was blowing—that

[009-143]not unacceptable compromise between the atmospheric cutlery of the

[009-144]eastern blast and the spongy gales of the west quarter. She looked from

[009-145]her window in the direction of the light of the previous evening, and

[009-146]could just discern through the trees the shape of the surgeon’s house.

[009-147]Somehow, in the broad, practical daylight, that unknown and lonely

[009-148]gentleman seemed to be shorn of much of the interest which had invested

[009-149]his personality and pursuits in the hours of darkness, and as Grace’s

[009-150]dressing proceeded he faded from her mind.

[009-151]Meanwhile, Winterborne, though half assured of her father’s favor, was

[009-152]rendered a little restless by Miss Melbury’s behavior. Despite his dry

[009-153]self-control, he could not help looking continually from his own door

[009-154]towards the timber-merchant’s, in the probability of somebody’s

[009-155]emergence therefrom. His attention was at length justified by the

[009-156]appearance of two figures, that of Mr. Melbury himself, and Grace

[009-157]beside him. They stepped out in a direction towards the densest quarter

[009-158]of the wood, and Winterborne walked contemplatively behind them, till

[009-159]all three were soon under the trees.

[009-160]Although the time of bare boughs had now set in, there were sheltered

[009-161]hollows amid the Hintock plantations and copses in which a more tardy

[009-162]leave-taking than on windy summits was the rule with the foliage. This

[009-163]caused here and there an apparent mixture of the seasons; so that in

[009-164]some of the dells that they passed by holly-berries in full red were

[009-165]found growing beside oak and hazel whose leaves were as yet not far

[009-166]removed from green, and brambles whose verdure was rich and deep as in

[009-167]the month of August. To Grace these well-known peculiarities were as an

[009-168]old painting restored.

[009-169]Now could be beheld that change from the handsome to the curious which

[009-170]the features of a wood undergo at the ingress of the winter months.

[009-171]Angles were taking the place of curves, and reticulations of surfaces—a

[009-172]change constituting a sudden lapse from the ornate to the primitive on

[009-173]Nature’s canvas, and comparable to a retrogressive step from the art of

[009-174]an advanced school of painting to that of the Pacific Islander.

[009-175]Winterborne followed, and kept his eye upon the two figures as they

[009-176]threaded their way through these sylvan phenomena. Mr. Melbury’s long

[009-177]legs, and gaiters drawn in to the bone at the ankles, his slight stoop,

[009-178]his habit of getting lost in thought and arousing himself with an

[009-179]exclamation of “Hah!” accompanied with an upward jerk of the head,

[009-180]composed a personage recognizable by his neighbors as far as he could

[009-181]be seen. It seemed as if the squirrels and birds knew him. One of the

[009-182]former would occasionally run from the path to hide behind the arm of

[009-183]some tree, which the little animal carefully edged round _pari passu_

[009-184]with Melbury and his daughters movement onward, assuming a mock manner,

[009-185]as though he were saying, “Ho, ho; you are only a timber-merchant, and

[009-186]carry no gun!”
