# Section 16

[016-001]“Then why the d—— didn’t you, or get the old buffer to do it for you?”

[016-002]said the man, as he cantered away.

[016-003]She looked rather disconcerted at this reply, and observing her

[016-004]father’s face, saw that it was quite red.

[016-005]“He ought not to have spoken to ye like that!” said the old man, in the

[016-006]tone of one whose heart was bruised, though it was not by the epithet

[016-007]applied to himself. “And he wouldn’t if he had been a gentleman. ’Twas

[016-008]not the language to use to a woman of any niceness. You, so well read

[016-009]and cultivated—how could he expect ye to know what tom-boy field-folk

[016-010]are in the habit of doing? If so be you had just come from trimming

[016-011]swedes or mangolds—joking with the rough work-folk and all that—I could

[016-012]have stood it. But hasn’t it cost me near a hundred a year to lift you

[016-013]out of all that, so as to show an example to the neighborhood of what a

[016-014]woman can be? Grace, shall I tell you the [[secrecy-and-concealment|secret]] of it? ’Twas because I

[016-015]was in your company. If a black-coated squire or pa’son had been

[016-016]walking with you instead of me he wouldn’t have spoken so.”

[016-017]“No, no, father; there’s nothing in you rough or ill-mannered!”

[016-018]“I tell you it is that! I’ve noticed, and I’ve noticed it many times,

[016-019]that a woman takes her color from the man she’s walking with. The woman

[016-020]who looks an unquestionable lady when she’s with a polished-up fellow,

[016-021]looks a mere tawdry imitation article when she’s hobbing and nobbing

[016-022]with a homely blade. You sha’n’t be treated like that for long, or at

[016-023]least your children sha’n’t. You shall have somebody to walk with you

[016-024]who looks more of a dandy than I—please God you shall!”

[016-025]“But, my dear father,” she said, much distressed, “I don’t mind at all.

[016-026]I don’t wish for more honor than I already have!”

[016-027]“A perplexing and ticklish possession is a daughter,” according to

[016-028]Menander or some old Greek poet, and to nobody was one ever more so

[016-029]than to [[george-melbury|Melbury]], by reason of her very dearness to him. As for Grace,

[016-030]she began to feel troubled; she did not perhaps wish there and then to

[016-031]unambitiously devote her life to [[giles-winterborne|Giles Winterborne]], but she was

[016-032]conscious of more and more uneasiness at the possibility of being the

[016-033]social hope of the family.

[016-034]“You would like to have more honor, if it pleases me?” asked her

[016-035]father, in continuation of the subject.

[016-036]Despite her feeling she assented to this. His reasoning had not been

[016-037]without its weight upon her.

[016-038]“Grace,” he said, just before they had reached the house, “if it costs

[016-039]me my life you shall marry well! To-day has shown me that whatever a

[016-040]young woman’s niceness, she stands for nothing alone. You shall marry

[016-041]well.”

[016-042]He breathed heavily, and his breathing was caught up by the breeze,

[016-043]which seemed to sigh a soft remonstrance.

[016-044]She looked calmly at him. “And how about Mr. Winterborne?” she asked.

[016-045]“I mention it, father, not as a matter of sentiment, but as a question

[016-046]of keeping faith.”

[016-047]The [[timber-economy|timber]]-merchant’s eyes fell for a moment. “I don’t know—I don’t

[016-048]know,” he said. “’Tis a trying strait. Well, well; there’s no hurry.

[016-049]We’ll wait and see how he gets on.”

[016-050]That evening he called her into his room, a snug little apartment

[016-051]behind the large parlor. It had at one time been part of the bakehouse,

[016-052]with the ordinary oval brick oven in the wall; but Mr. Melbury, in

[016-053]turning it into an office, had built into the cavity an iron safe,

[016-054]which he used for holding his private papers. The door of the safe was

[016-055]now open, and his keys were hanging from it.

[016-056]“Sit down, Grace, and keep me company,” he said. “You may amuse

[016-057]yourself by looking over these.” He threw out a heap of papers before

[016-058]her.

[016-059]“What are they?” she asked.

[016-060]“Securities of various sorts.” He unfolded them one by one. “Papers

[016-061]worth so much money each. Now here’s a lot of turnpike bonds for one

[016-062]thing. Would you think that each of these pieces of paper is worth two

[016-063]hundred pounds?”

[016-064]“No, indeed, if you didn’t say so.”

[016-065]“’Tis so, then. Now here are papers of another sort. They are for

[016-066]different sums in the three-per-cents. Now these are Port Breedy Harbor

[016-067]bonds. We have a great stake in that harbor, you know, because I send

[016-068]off timber there. Open the rest at your pleasure. They’ll interest ye.”

[016-069]“Yes, I will, some day,” said she, rising.

[016-070]“Nonsense, open them now. You ought to learn a little of such matters.

[016-071]A young lady of [[education-and-class-mobility|education]] should not be ignorant of money affairs

[016-072]altogether. Suppose you should be left a widow some day, with your

[016-073]husband’s title-deeds and investments thrown upon your hands—”

[016-074]“Don’t say that, father—title-deeds; it sounds so vain!”

[016-075]“It does not. Come to that, I have title-deeds myself. There, that

[016-076]piece of parchment represents houses in [[sherton-abbas|Sherton Abbas]].”

[016-077]“Yes, but—” She hesitated, looked at the fire, and went on in a low

[016-078]voice: “If what has been arranged about me should come to anything, my

[016-079]sphere will be quite a middling one.”

[016-080]“Your sphere ought not to be middling,” he exclaimed, not in [[love-and-desire|passion]],

[016-081]but in earnest conviction. “You said you never felt more at home, more

[016-082]in your element, anywhere than you did that afternoon with Mrs.

[016-083]Charmond, when she showed you her house and all her knick-knacks, and

[016-084]made you stay to tea so nicely in her drawing-room—surely you did!”

[016-085]“Yes, I did say so,” admitted Grace.

[016-086]“Was it true?”

[016-087]“Yes, I felt so at the time. The feeling is less strong now, perhaps.”

[016-088]“Ah! Now, though you don’t see it, your feeling at the time was the

[016-089]right one, because your mind and body were just in full and fresh

[016-090]cultivation, so that going there with her was like meeting like. Since

[016-091]then you’ve been biding with us, and have fallen back a little, and so

[016-092]you don’t feel your place so strongly. Now, do as I tell ye, and look

[016-093]over these papers and see what you’ll be worth some day. For they’ll

[016-094]all be yours, you know; who have I got to leave ’em to but you? Perhaps

[016-095]when your education is backed up by what these papers represent, and

[016-096]that backed up by another such a set and their owner, men such as that

[016-097]fellow was this morning may think you a little more than a buffer’s

[016-098]girl.”

[016-099]So she did as commanded, and opened each of the folded representatives

[016-100]of hard cash that her father put before her. To sow in her heart

[016-101]cravings for social position was obviously his strong desire, though in

[016-102]direct antagonism to a better feeling which had hitherto prevailed with

[016-103]him, and had, indeed, only succumbed that morning during the ramble.

[016-104]She wished that she was not his worldly hope; the responsibility of

[016-105]such a position was too great. She had made it for herself mainly by

[016-106]her appearance and attractive behavior to him since her return. “If I

[016-107]had only come home in a shabby dress, and tried to speak roughly, this

[016-108]might not have happened,” she thought. She deplored less the fact than

[016-109]the sad possibilities that might lie hidden therein.

[016-110]Her father then insisted upon her looking over his checkbook and

[016-111]reading the counterfoils. This, also, she obediently did, and at last

[016-112]came to two or three which had been drawn to defray some of the late

[016-113]expenses of her clothes, board, and education.

[016-114]“I, too, cost a good deal, like the horses and wagons and corn,” she

[016-115]said, looking up sorrily.

[016-116]“I didn’t want you to look at those; I merely meant to give you an idea

[016-117]of my investment transactions. But if you do cost as much as they,

[016-118]never mind. You’ll yield a better return.”

[016-119]“Don’t think of me like that!” she begged. “A mere chattel.”

[016-120]“A what? Oh, a dictionary word. Well, as that’s in your line I don’t

[016-121]forbid it, even if it tells against me,” he said, good-humoredly. And

[016-122]he looked her proudly up and down.

[016-123]A few minutes later [[grammer-oliver|Grammer Oliver]] came to tell them that supper was

[016-124]ready, and in giving the information she added, incidentally, “So we

[016-125]shall soon lose the mistress of [[hintock-house|Hintock House]] for some time, I hear,

[016-126]Maister Melbury. Yes, she’s going off to foreign parts to-morrow, for

[016-127]the rest of the winter months; and be-chok’d if I don’t wish I could do

[016-128]the same, for my wynd-pipe is furred like a flue.”

[016-129]When the old woman had left the room, Melbury turned to his daughter

[016-130]and said, “So, Grace, you’ve lost your new friend, and your [[fate-and-free-will|chance]] of

[016-131]keeping her company and writing her travels is quite gone from ye!”

[016-132]Grace said nothing.

[016-133]“Now,” he went on, emphatically, “’tis Winterborne’s affair has done

[016-134]this. Oh yes, ’tis. So let me say one word. Promise me that you will

[016-135]not meet him again without my knowledge.”

[016-136]“I never do meet him, father, either without your knowledge or with

[016-137]it.”

[016-138]“So much the better. I don’t like the look of this at all. And I say it

[016-139]not out of harshness to him, poor fellow, but out of tenderness to you.

[016-140]For how could a woman, brought up delicately as you have been, bear the

[016-141]roughness of a life with him?”

[016-142]She sighed; it was a sigh of sympathy with Giles, complicated by a

[016-143]sense of the intractability of circumstances.

[016-144]At that same hour, and almost at that same minute, there was a

[016-145]conversation about Winterborne in progress in the village street,

[016-146]opposite Mr. Melbury’s gates, where [[timothy-tangs|Timothy Tangs]] the elder and Robert

[016-147][[robert-creedle|Creedle]] had accidentally met.

[016-148]The sawyer was asking Creedle if he had heard what was all over the

[016-149]parish, the skin of his face being drawn two ways on the matter—towards

[016-150]brightness in respect of it as news, and towards concern in respect of

[016-151]it as circumstance.

[016-152]“Why, that poor little lonesome thing, [[marty-south|Marty South]], is likely to lose

[016-153]her father. He was almost well, but is much worse again. A man all skin

[016-154]and grief he ever were, and if he leave [[little-hintock|Little Hintock]] for a better

[016-155]land, won’t it make some difference to your Maister Winterborne,

[016-156][[community-and-kinship|neighbor]] Creedle?”

[016-157]“Can I be a prophet in Israel?” said Creedle. “Won’t it! I was only

[016-158]shaping of such a thing yesterday in my poor, long-seeing way, and all

[016-159]the work of the house upon my one shoulders! You know what it means? It

[016-160]is upon [[john-south|John South]]’s life that all Mr. Winterborne’s houses hang. If so

[016-161]be South die, and so make his decease, thereupon the law is that the

[016-162]houses fall without the least chance of absolution into HER hands at

[016-163]the House. I told him so; but the words of the faithful be only as

[016-164]wind!”

[016-165]CHAPTER XIII.

[016-166]The news was true. The life—the one fragile life—that had been used as

[016-167]a measuring-tape of time by law, was in danger of being frayed away. It

[016-168]was the last of a group of lives which had served this purpose, at the

[016-169]end of whose breathings the small homestead occupied by South himself,

[016-170]the larger one of Giles Winterborne, and half a dozen others that had

[016-171]been in the possession of various Hintock village families for the

[016-172]previous hundred years, and were now Winterborne’s, would fall in and

[016-173]become part of the encompassing estate.

[016-174]Yet a short two months earlier Marty’s father, aged fifty-five years,

[016-175]though something of a fidgety, anxious being, would have been looked on

[016-176]as a man whose existence was so far removed from hazardous as any in

[016-177][[the-hintock-parish|the parish]], and as bidding fair to be prolonged for another quarter of

[016-178]a century.

[016-179]Winterborne walked up and down his garden next day thinking of the

[016-180]contingency. The sense that the paths he was pacing, the cabbage-plots,

[016-181]the apple-[[nature-in-the-woodlanders|trees]], his dwelling, cider-cellar, wring-house, stables, and

[016-182]weathercock, were all slipping away over his head and beneath his feet,

[016-183]as if they were painted on a magic-lantern slide, was curious. In spite

[016-184]of John South’s late indisposition he had not anticipated danger. To

[016-185]inquire concerning his health had been to show less sympathy than to

[016-186]remain silent, considering the material interest he possessed in the

[016-187]woodman’s life, and he had, accordingly, made a point of avoiding

[016-188]Marty’s house.
