## Overview

The man-trap incident is a dramatic turning point in *The Woodlanders*, occurring in the woods near Little Hintock. Timothy Tangs, driven by jealousy over his wife Suke's involvement with Fitzpiers, sets a brutal man-trap in the forest path [063-159]. The trap does not catch its intended victim but instead ensnares Grace Melbury, creating a crisis that reverberates through the novel's final chapters.

## The Setting of the Trap

Timothy Tangs, a woodman and husband of Suke Damson, discovers a man-trap in a keeper's out-house [063-166]. His jealousy over Fitzpiers's affair with Suke drives him to set the trap on a woodland path frequented by the doctor. Tangs carefully positions the trap behind a bush, concealing it "behind a bush where the only course for a foot-passenger" lay [064-107]. The trap is an archaic and brutal device — earlier described as having lamed a man for life [064-029].

## The Entrapment

On the night in question, the man-trap is thrown — but the jaws close not on Fitzpiers, but on part of a woman's dress [064-303]. Grace Melbury, walking through the woods, becomes the victim. The scene is one of sudden violence: "my clothes were caught in a trap" [065-045]. The discovery of the trap adds "an item of fact" [065-259] to the tangled suspicions already circulating in Hintock, though its full implications remain unclear. Fitzpiers later recounts: "I was nearly caught in a man-trap, and that's how" [066-043], connecting himself to the event that was meant for him.

## Aftermath

The man-trap incident deepens the atmosphere of danger and moral darkness in the woodland. Timothy Tangs's act of vengeance, born of personal grievance, nearly destroys an innocent party. The incident reinforces Hardy's theme of unintended consequences — where human passions, like the trap itself, catch the wrong victims.
