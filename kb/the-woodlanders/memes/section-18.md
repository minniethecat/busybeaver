# Section 18

[018-001]The fog of the previous evening still lingered so heavily over the

[018-002]woods that the morning could not penetrate the [[nature-in-the-woodlanders|trees]] till long after

[018-003]its time. The load being a ponderous one, the lane crooked, and the air

[018-004]so thick, [[giles-winterborne|Winterborne]] set out, as he often did, to accompany the team

[018-005]as far as the corner, where it would turn into a wider road.

[018-006]So they rumbled on, shaking the foundations of the roadside cottages by

[018-007]the weight of their progress, the sixteen bells chiming harmoniously

[018-008]over all, till they had risen out of the valley and were descending

[018-009]towards the more open route, the sparks rising from their creaking skid

[018-010]and nearly setting fire to the dead leaves alongside.

[018-011]Then occurred one of the very incidents against which the bells were an

[018-012]endeavor to guard. Suddenly there beamed into their eyes, quite close

[018-013]to them, the two lamps of a carriage, shorn of rays by the fog. Its

[018-014]approach had been quite unheard, by reason of their own noise. The

[018-015]carriage was a covered one, while behind it could be discerned another

[018-016]vehicle laden with luggage.

[018-017]Winterborne went to the head of the team, and heard the coachman

[018-018]telling the carter that he must turn back. The carter declared that

[018-019]this was impossible.

[018-020]“You can turn if you unhitch your string-horses,” said the coachman.

[018-021]“It is much easier for you to turn than for us,” said Winterborne.

[018-022]“We’ve five tons of [[timber-economy|timber]] on these wheels if we’ve an ounce.”

[018-023]“But I’ve another carriage with luggage at my back.”

[018-024]Winterborne admitted the strength of the argument. “But even with

[018-025]that,” he said, “you can back better than we. And you ought to, for you

[018-026]could hear our bells half a mile off.”

[018-027]“And you could see our lights.”

[018-028]“We couldn’t, because of the fog.”

[018-029]“Well, our time’s precious,” said the coachman, haughtily. “You are

[018-030]only going to some trumpery little village or other in the

[018-031]neighborhood, while we are going straight to Italy.”

[018-032]“Driving all the way, I suppose,” said Winterborne, sarcastically.

[018-033]The argument continued in these terms till a voice from the interior of

[018-034]the carriage inquired what was the matter. It was a lady’s.

[018-035]She was briefly informed of the timber people’s obstinacy; and then

[018-036]Giles could hear her telling the footman to direct the timber people to

[018-037]turn their horses’ heads.

[018-038]The message was brought, and Winterborne sent the bearer back to say

[018-039]that he begged the lady’s pardon, but that he could not do as she

[018-040]requested; that though he would not assert it to be impossible, it was

[018-041]impossible by comparison with the slight difficulty to her party to

[018-042]back their light carriages. As [[fate-and-free-will|fate]] would have it, the incident with

[018-043][[grace-melbury|Grace Melbury]] on the previous day made Giles less gentle than he might

[018-044]otherwise have shown himself, his confidence in the sex being rudely

[018-045]shaken.

[018-046]In fine, nothing could move him, and the carriages were compelled to

[018-047]back till they reached one of the sidings or turnouts constructed in

[018-048]the bank for the purpose. Then the team came on ponderously, and the

[018-049]clanging of its sixteen bells as it passed the discomfited carriages,

[018-050]tilted up against the bank, lent a particularly triumphant tone to the

[018-051]team’s progress—a tone which, in point of fact, did not at all attach

[018-052]to its conductor’s feelings.

[018-053]Giles walked behind the timber, and just as he had got past the yet

[018-054]stationary carriages he heard a soft voice say, “Who is that rude man?

[018-055]Not [[george-melbury|Melbury]]?” The sex of the speaker was so prominent in the voice that

[018-056]Winterborne felt a pang of regret.

[018-057]“No, ma’am. A younger man, in a smaller way of business in Little

[018-058][[little-hintock|Hintock]]. Winterborne is his name.”

[018-059]Thus they parted company. “Why, Mr. Winterborne,” said the wagoner,

[018-060]when they were out of hearing, “that was She—[[felice-charmond|Mrs. Charmond]]! Who’d ha’

[018-061]thought it? What in the world can a woman that does nothing be

[018-062]cock-watching out here at this time o’ day for? Oh, going to Italy—yes

[018-063]to be sure, I heard she was going abroad, she can’t endure the winter

[018-064]here.”

[018-065]Winterborne was vexed at the incident; the more so that he knew Mr.

[018-066]Melbury, in his adoration of [[hintock-house|Hintock House]], would be the first to blame

[018-067]him if it became known. But saying no more, he accompanied the load to

[018-068]the end of the lane, and then turned back with an intention to call at

[018-069]South’s to learn the result of the experiment of the preceding evening.

[018-070]It chanced that a few minutes before this time Grace Melbury, who now

[018-071]rose soon enough to breakfast with her father, in spite of the

[018-072]unwontedness of the hour, had been commissioned by him to make the same

[018-073]inquiry at South’s. [[marty-south|Marty]] had been standing at the door when Miss

[018-074]Melbury arrived. Almost before the latter had spoken, Mrs. Charmond’s

[018-075]carriages, released from the obstruction up the lane, came bowling

[018-076]along, and the two girls turned to regard the spectacle.

[018-077]Mrs. Charmond did not see them, but there was sufficient light for them

[018-078]to discern her outline between the carriage windows. A noticeable

[018-079]feature in her _tournure_ was a magnificent mass of braided locks.

[018-080]“How well she looks this morning!” said Grace, forgetting Mrs.

[018-081]Charmond’s slight in her generous admiration. “Her hair so becomes her

[018-082]worn that way. I have never seen any more beautiful!”

[018-083]“Nor have I, miss,” said Marty, dryly, unconsciously stroking her

[018-084]crown.

[018-085]Grace watched the carriages with lingering regret till they were out of

[018-086]sight. She then learned of Marty that South was no better. Before she

[018-087]had come away Winterborne approached the house, but seeing that one of

[018-088]the two girls standing on the door-step was Grace, he suddenly turned

[018-089]back again and sought the shelter of his own home till she should have

[018-090]gone away.

[018-091]CHAPTER XIV.

[018-092]The encounter with the carriages having sprung upon Winterborne’s mind

[018-093]the image of Mrs. Charmond, his thoughts by a natural channel went from

[018-094]her to the fact that several cottages and other houses in the two

[018-095]Hintocks, now his own, would fall into her possession in the event of

[018-096]South’s death. He marvelled what people could have been thinking about

[018-097]in the past to invent such precarious tenures as these; still more,

[018-098]what could have induced his ancestors at Hintock, and other village

[018-099]people, to exchange their old copyholds for life-leases. But having

[018-100]naturally succeeded to these properties through his father, he had done

[018-101]his best to keep them in order, though he was much struck with his

[018-102]father’s negligence in not insuring South’s life.

[018-103]After breakfast, still musing on the circumstances, he went upstairs,

[018-104]turned over his bed, and drew out a flat canvas bag which lay between

[018-105]the mattress and the sacking. In this he kept his leases, which had

[018-106]remained there unopened ever since his father’s death. It was the usual

[018-107]hiding-place among rural lifeholders for such documents. Winterborne

[018-108]sat down on the bed and looked them over. They were ordinary leases for

[018-109]three lives, which a member of the South family, some fifty years

[018-110]before this time, had accepted of the lord of the manor in lieu of

[018-111]certain copyholds and other rights, in consideration of having the

[018-112]dilapidated houses rebuilt by said lord. They had come into his

[018-113]father’s possession chiefly through his mother, who was a South.

[018-114]Pinned to the parchment of one of the indentures was a letter, which

[018-115]Winterborne had never seen before. It bore a remote date, the

[018-116]handwriting being that of some solicitor or agent, and the signature

[018-117]the landholder’s. It was to the effect that at any time before the last

[018-118]of the stated lives should drop, Mr. Giles Winterborne, senior, or his

[018-119]representative, should have the privilege of adding his own and his

[018-120]son’s life to the life remaining on payment of a merely nominal sum;

[018-121]the concession being in consequence of the elder Winterborne’s consent

[018-122]to demolish one of the houses and relinquish its site, which stood at

[018-123]an awkward corner of the lane and impeded the way.

[018-124]The house had been pulled down years before. Why Giles’s father had not

[018-125]taken advantage of his privilege to insert his own and his son’s lives

[018-126]it was impossible to say. The likelihood was that death alone had

[018-127]hindered him in the execution of his project, as it surely was, the

[018-128]elder Winterborne having been a man who took much pleasure in dealing

[018-129]with house property in his small way.

[018-130]Since one of the Souths still survived, there was not much doubt that

[018-131]Giles could do what his father had left undone, as far as his own life

[018-132]was concerned. This possibility cheered him much, for by those houses

[018-133]hung many things. Melbury’s doubt of the young man’s fitness to be the

[018-134]husband of Grace had been based not a little on the precariousness of

[018-135]his holdings in Little and [[great-hintock|Great Hintock]]. He resolved to attend to the

[018-136]business at once, the fine for renewal being a sum that he could easily

[018-137]muster. His scheme, however, could not be carried out in a day; and

[018-138]meanwhile he would run up to South’s, as he had intended to do, to

[018-139]learn the result of the experiment with the tree.

[018-140]Marty met him at the door. “Well, Marty,” he said; and was surprised to

[018-141]read in her face that the case was not so hopeful as he had imagined.

[018-142]“I am sorry for your labor,” she said. “It is all lost. He says the

[018-143]tree seems taller than ever.”

[018-144]Winterborne looked round at it. Taller the tree certainly did seem, the

[018-145]gauntness of its now naked stem being more marked than before.

[018-146]“It quite terrified him when he first saw what you had done to it this

[018-147]morning,” she added. “He declares it will come down upon us and cleave

[018-148]us, like ‘the sword of the Lord and of Gideon.’”

[018-149]“Well; can I do anything else?” asked he.

[018-150]“The doctor says the tree ought to be cut down.”

[018-151]“Oh—you’ve had the doctor?”

[018-152]“I didn’t send for him. Mrs. Charmond, before she left, heard that

[018-153]father was ill, and told him to attend him at her expense.”

[018-154]“That was very good of her. And he says it ought to be cut down. We

[018-155]mustn’t cut it down without her knowledge, I suppose.”

[018-156]He went up-stairs. There the old man sat, staring at the now gaunt tree

[018-157]as if his gaze were frozen on to its trunk. Unluckily the tree waved

[018-158]afresh by this time, a wind having sprung up and blown the fog away,

[018-159]and his eyes turned with its wavings.

[018-160]They heard footsteps—a man’s, but of a lighter type than usual. “There

[018-161]is [[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Doctor Fitzpiers]] again,” she said, and descended. Presently his

[018-162]tread was heard on the naked stairs.

[018-163]Mr. Fitzpiers entered the sick-chamber just as a doctor is more or less

[018-164]wont to do on such occasions, and pre-eminently when the room is that

[018-165]of a humble cottager, looking round towards the patient with that

[018-166]preoccupied gaze which so plainly reveals that he has wellnigh

[018-167]forgotten all about the case and the whole circumstances since he

[018-168]dismissed them from his mind at his last exit from the same apartment.

[018-169]He nodded to Winterborne, with whom he was already a little acquainted,

[018-170]recalled the case to his thoughts, and went leisurely on to where South

[018-171]sat.

[018-172]Fitzpiers was, on the whole, a finely formed, handsome man. His eyes

[018-173]were dark and impressive, and beamed with the light either of energy or

[018-174]of susceptivity—it was difficult to say which; it might have been a

[018-175]little of both. That quick, glittering, practical eye, sharp for the

[018-176]surface of things and for nothing beneath it, he had not. But whether

[018-177]his apparent depth of vision was real, or only an artistic accident of

[018-178]his corporeal moulding, nothing but his deeds could reveal.

[018-179]His face was rather soft than stern, charming than grand, pale than

[018-180]flushed; his nose—if a sketch of his features be _de rigueur_ for a

[018-181]person of his pretensions—was artistically beautiful enough to have

[018-182]been worth doing in marble by any sculptor not over-busy, and was hence

[018-183]devoid of those knotty irregularities which often mean power; while the

[018-184]double-cyma or classical curve of his mouth was not without a looseness

[018-185]in its close. Nevertheless, either from his readily appreciative mien,

[018-186]or his reflective manner, or the instinct towards profound things which

[018-187]was said to possess him, his presence bespoke the philosopher rather

[018-188]than the dandy or macaroni—an effect which was helped by the absence of

[018-189]trinkets or other trivialities from his attire, though this was more

[018-190]finished and up to date than is usually the case among rural

[018-191]practitioners.
