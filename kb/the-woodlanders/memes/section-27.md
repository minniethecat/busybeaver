# Section 27

[027-001]Soon the listeners could hear nothing of their proceedings beyond the

[027-002]faintest occasional rustle of leaves. Grammer whispered again to Marty:

[027-003]“Why didn’t ye go and try your luck with the rest of the maids?”

[027-004]“I don’t believe in it,” said Marty, shortly.

[027-005]“Why, half [[the-hintock-parish|the parish]] is here—the silly hussies should have kept it

[027-006]quiet. I see Mr. [[giles-winterborne|Winterborne]] through the leaves, just come up with

[027-007][[robert-creedle|Robert Creedle]]. Marty, we ought to act the part o’ Providence

[027-008]sometimes. Do go and tell him that if he stands just behind the bush at

[027-009]the bottom of the slope, Miss Grace must pass down it when she comes

[027-010]back, and she will most likely rush into his arms; for as soon as the

[027-011]clock strikes, they’ll bundle back home—along like hares. I’ve seen

[027-012]such larries before.”

[027-013]“Do you think I’d better?” said Marty, reluctantly.

[027-014]“Oh yes, he’ll bless ye for it.”

[027-015]“I don’t want that kind of blessing.” But after a moment’s thought she

[027-016]went and delivered the information; and Grammer had the satisfaction of

[027-017]seeing Giles walk slowly to the bend in the leafy defile along which

[027-018]Grace would have to return.

[027-019]Meanwhile [[mrs-melbury|Mrs. Melbury]], deserted by Grace, had perceived [[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Fitzpiers]] and

[027-020]Winterborne, and also the move of the latter. An improvement on

[027-021]Grammer’s idea entered the mind of Mrs. [[george-melbury|Melbury]], for she had lately

[027-022]discerned what her husband had not—that Grace was rapidly fascinating

[027-023]the surgeon. She therefore drew near to Fitzpiers.

[027-024]“You should be where Mr. Winterborne is standing,” she said to him,

[027-025]significantly. “She will run down through that opening much faster than

[027-026]she went up it, if she is like the rest of the girls.”

[027-027]Fitzpiers did not require to be told twice. He went across to

[027-028]Winterborne and stood beside him. Each knew the probable purpose of the

[027-029]other in standing there, and neither spoke, Fitzpiers scorning to look

[027-030]upon Winterborne as a rival, and Winterborne adhering to the off-hand

[027-031]manner of indifference which had grown upon him since his dismissal.

[027-032]Neither Grammer nor [[marty-south|Marty South]] had seen the surgeon’s manoeuvre, and,

[027-033]still to help Winterborne, as she supposed, the old woman suggested to

[027-034]the wood-girl that she should walk forward at the heels of Grace, and

[027-035]“tole” her down the required way if she showed a tendency to run in

[027-036]another direction. Poor Marty, always doomed to sacrifice [[love-and-desire|desire]] to

[027-037]obligation, walked forward accordingly, and waited as a beacon, still

[027-038]and silent, for the retreat of Grace and her giddy companions, now

[027-039]quite out of hearing.

[027-040]The first sound to break the silence was the distant note of Great

[027-041][[little-hintock|Hintock]] clock striking the significant hour. [[About]] a minute later that

[027-042]quarter of the wood to which the girls had wandered resounded with the

[027-043]flapping of disturbed birds; then two or three hares and rabbits

[027-044]bounded down the glade from the same direction, and after these the

[027-045]rustling and crackling of leaves and dead twigs denoted the hurried

[027-046]approach of the adventurers, whose fluttering gowns soon became

[027-047]visible. [[grace-melbury|Miss Melbury]], having gone forward quite in the rear of the

[027-048]rest, was one of the first to return, and the excitement being

[027-049]contagious, she ran laughing towards Marty, who still stood as a

[027-050]hand-post to guide her; then, passing on, she flew round the fatal bush

[027-051]where the undergrowth narrowed to a gorge. Marty arrived at her heels

[027-052]just in time to see the result. Fitzpiers had quickly stepped forward

[027-053]in front of Winterborne, who, disdaining to shift his position, had

[027-054]turned on his heel, and then the surgeon did what he would not have

[027-055]thought of doing but for Mrs. Melbury’s encouragement and the sentiment

[027-056]of an eve which effaced conventionality. Stretching out his arms as the

[027-057]white figure burst upon him, he captured her in a moment, as if she had

[027-058]been a bird.

[027-059]“Oh!” cried Grace, in her fright.

[027-060]“You are in my arms, dearest,” said Fitzpiers, “and I am going to claim

[027-061]you, and keep you there all our two lives!”

[027-062]She rested on him like one utterly mastered, and it was several seconds

[027-063]before she recovered from this helplessness. Subdued screams and

[027-064]struggles, audible from neighboring brakes, revealed that there had

[027-065]been other lurkers thereabout for a similar purpose. Grace, unlike most

[027-066]of these companions of hers, instead of gasping and writhing, said in a

[027-067]trembling voice, “Mr. Fitzpiers, will you let me go?”

[027-068]“Certainly,” he said, laughing; “as soon as you have recovered.”

[027-069]She waited another few moments, then quietly and firmly pushed him

[027-070]aside, and glided on her path, the moon whitening her hot blush away.

[027-071]But it had been enough—new relations between them had begun.

[027-072]The case of the other girls was different, as has been said. They

[027-073]wrestled and tittered, only escaping after a desperate struggle.

[027-074]Fitzpiers could hear these enactments still going on after Grace had

[027-075]left him, and he remained on the spot where he had caught her,

[027-076]Winterborne having gone away. On a sudden another girl came bounding

[027-077]down the same descent that had been followed by Grace—a fine-framed

[027-078]young woman with naked arms. Seeing Fitzpiers standing there, she said,

[027-079]with playful effrontery, “May’st kiss me if ‘canst catch me, Tim!”

[027-080]Fitzpiers recognized her as [[suke-damson|Suke Damson]], a hoydenish damsel of the

[027-081]hamlet, who was plainly mistaking him for her lover. He was impulsively

[027-082]disposed to profit by her error, and as soon as she began racing away

[027-083]he started in pursuit.

[027-084]On she went under the boughs, now in light, now in shade, looking over

[027-085]her shoulder at him every few moments and kissing her hand; but so

[027-086]cunningly dodging about among the [[nature-in-the-woodlanders|trees]] and moon-shades that she never

[027-087]allowed him to get dangerously near her. Thus they ran and doubled,

[027-088]Fitzpiers warming with the chase, till the sound of their companions

[027-089]had quite died away. He began to lose hope of ever overtaking her, when

[027-090]all at once, by way of encouragement, she turned to a fence in which

[027-091]there was a stile and leaped over it. Outside the scene was a changed

[027-092]one—a meadow, where the half-made hay lay about in heaps, in the

[027-093]uninterrupted shine of the now high moon.

[027-094]Fitzpiers saw in a moment that, having taken to open ground, she had

[027-095]placed herself at his mercy, and he promptly vaulted over after her.

[027-096]She flitted a little way down the mead, when all at once her light form

[027-097]disappeared as if it had sunk into the earth. She had buried herself in

[027-098]one of the hay-cocks.

[027-099]Fitzpiers, now thoroughly excited, was not going to let her escape him

[027-100]thus. He approached, and set about turning over the heaps one by one.

[027-101]As soon as he paused, tantalized and puzzled, he was directed anew by

[027-102]an imitative kiss which came from her hiding-place, and by snatches of

[027-103]a local ballad in the smallest voice she could assume:

[027-104]“O come in from the foggy, foggy dew.”

[027-105]In a minute or two he uncovered her.

[027-106]“Oh, ’tis not Tim!” said she, burying her face.

[027-107]Fitzpiers, however, disregarded her resistance by reason of its

[027-108]mildness, stooped and imprinted the purposed kiss, then sunk down on

[027-109]the next hay-cock, panting with his race.

[027-110]“Whom do you mean by Tim?” he asked, presently.

[027-111]“My young man, Tim Tangs,” said she.

[027-112]“Now, honor bright, did you really think it was he?”

[027-113]“I did at first.”

[027-114]“But you didn’t at last?”

[027-115]“I didn’t at last.”

[027-116]“Do you much mind that it was not?”

[027-117]“No,” she answered, slyly.

[027-118]Fitzpiers did not pursue his questioning. In the moonlight Suke looked

[027-119]very beautiful, the scratches and blemishes incidental to her out-door

[027-120]occupation being invisible under these pale rays. While they remain

[027-121]silent the coarse whir of the eternal night-jar burst sarcastically

[027-122]from the top of a tree at the nearest corner of the wood. Besides this

[027-123]not a sound of any kind reached their ears, the time of nightingales

[027-124]being now past, and Hintock lying at a distance of two miles at least.

[027-125]In the opposite direction the hay-field stretched away into [[rural-isolation|remoteness]]

[027-126]till it was lost to the eye in a soft mist.

[027-128]When the general stampede occurred Winterborne had also been looking

[027-129]on, and encountering one of the girls, had asked her what caused them

[027-130]all to fly.

[027-131]She said with solemn breathlessness that they had seen something very

[027-132]different from what they had hoped to see, and that she for one would

[027-133]never attempt such unholy ceremonies again. “We saw Satan pursuing us

[027-134]with his hour-glass. It was terrible!”

[027-135]This account being a little incoherent, Giles went forward towards the

[027-136]spot from which the girls had retreated. After listening there a few

[027-137]minutes he heard slow footsteps rustling over the leaves, and looking

[027-138]through a tangled screen of honeysuckle which hung from a bough, he saw

[027-139]in the open space beyond a short stout man in evening-dress, carrying

[027-140]on one arm a light overcoat and also his hat, so awkwardly arranged as

[027-141]possibly to have suggested the “hour-glass” to his timid observers—if

[027-142]this were the person whom the girls had seen. With the other hand he

[027-143]silently gesticulated and the moonlight falling upon his bare brow

[027-144]showed him to have dark hair and a high forehead of the shape seen

[027-145]oftener in old prints and paintings than in real life. His curious and

[027-146]altogether alien aspect, his strange gestures, like those of one who is

[027-147]rehearsing a scene to himself, and the unusual place and hour, were

[027-148]sufficient to account for any trepidation among the Hintock daughters

[027-149]at encountering him.

[027-150]He paused, and looked round, as if he had forgotten where he was; not

[027-151]observing Giles, who was of the color of his environment. The latter

[027-152]advanced into the light. The gentleman held up his hand and came

[027-153]towards Giles, the two meeting half-way.

[027-154]“I have lost my way,” said the stranger. “Perhaps you can put me in the

[027-155]path again.” He wiped his forehead with the air of one suffering under

[027-156]an agitation more than that of simple fatigue.

[027-157]“The turnpike-road is over there,” said Giles

[027-158]“I don’t want the turnpike-road,” said the gentleman, impatiently. “I

[027-159]came from that. I want [[hintock-house|Hintock House]]. Is there not a path to it across

[027-160]here?”

[027-161]“Well, yes, a sort of path. But it is hard to find from this point.

[027-162]I’ll show you the way, sir, with great pleasure.”

[027-163]“Thanks, my good friend. The truth is that I decided to walk across the

[027-164]country after dinner from the hotel at [[sherton-abbas|Sherton]], where I am staying for

[027-165]a day or two. But I did not know it was so far.”

[027-166]“It is about a mile to the house from here.”

[027-167]They walked on together. As there was no path, Giles occasionally

[027-168]stepped in front and bent aside the underboughs of the trees to give

[027-169]his companion a passage, saying every now and then when the twigs, on

[027-170]being released, flew back like whips, “Mind your eyes, sir.” To which

[027-171]the stranger replied, “Yes, yes,” in a preoccupied tone.

[027-172]So they went on, the leaf-shadows running in their usual quick

[027-173]succession over the forms of the pedestrians, till the stranger said,

[027-174]“Is it far?”

[027-175]“Not much farther,” said Winterborne. “The plantation runs up into a

[027-176]corner here, close behind the house.” He added with hesitation, “You

[027-177]know, I suppose, sir, that [[felice-charmond|Mrs. Charmond]] is not at home?”

[027-178]“You mistake,” said the other, quickly. “Mrs. Charmond has been away

[027-179]for some time, but she’s at home now.”

[027-180]Giles did not contradict him, though he felt sure that the gentleman

[027-181]was wrong.

[027-182]“You are a native of this place?” the stranger said.

[027-183]“Yes.”

[027-184]“Well, you are happy in having a home. It is what I don’t possess.”

[027-185]“You come from far, seemingly?”

[027-186]“I come now from the south of Europe.”

[027-187]“Oh, indeed, sir. You are an Italian, or Spanish, or French gentleman,

[027-188]perhaps?”

[027-189]“I am not either.”

[027-190]Giles did not fill the pause which ensued, and the gentleman, who

[027-191]seemed of an emotional nature, unable to resist friendship, at length

[027-192]answered the question.
