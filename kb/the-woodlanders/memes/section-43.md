# Section 43

[043-001]“No, ma’am,” said [[marty-south|Marty]]. “_She_ was once; and I think—”

[043-002]But Marty could not possibly explain the complications of her thoughts

[043-003]on this matter—which were nothing less than one of extraordinary

[043-004]acuteness for a girl so young and inexperienced—namely, that she saw

[043-005]danger to two hearts naturally honest in Grace being thrown back into

[043-006][[giles-winterborne|Winterborne]]’s society by the neglect of her husband. Mrs. Charmond,

[043-007]however, with the almost supersensory means to knowledge which [[gender-roles|women]]

[043-008]have on such occasions, quite understood what Marty had intended to

[043-009]convey, and the picture thus exhibited to her of lives drifting away,

[043-010]involving the wreck of poor Marty’s hopes, prompted her to more

[043-011]generous resolves than all [[george-melbury|Melbury]]’s remonstrances had been able to

[043-012]stimulate.

[043-013]Full of the new feeling, she bade the girl good-afternoon, and went on

[043-014]over the stumps of hazel to where Grace and Winterborne were standing.

[043-015]They saw her approach, and Winterborne said, “She is coming to you; it

[043-016]is a good omen. She dislikes me, so I’ll go away.” He accordingly

[043-017]retreated to where he had been working before Grace came, and Grace’s

[043-018]formidable rival approached her, each woman taking the other’s measure

[043-019]as she came near.

[043-020]“Dear—[[grace-melbury|Mrs. Fitzpiers]],” said [[felice-charmond|Felice Charmond]], with some inward turmoil

[043-021]which stopped her speech. “I have not seen you for a long time.”

[043-022]She held out her hand tentatively, while Grace stood like a wild animal

[043-023]on first confronting a mirror or other puzzling product of

[043-024]civilization. Was it really Mrs. Charmond speaking to her thus? If it

[043-025]was, she could no longer form any guess as to what it signified.

[043-026]“I want to talk with you,” said Mrs. Charmond, imploringly, for the

[043-027]gaze of the young woman had chilled her through. “Can you walk on with

[043-028]me till we are quite alone?”

[043-029]Sick with distaste, Grace nevertheless complied, as by clockwork and

[043-030]they moved evenly side by side into the deeper recesses of the woods.

[043-031]They went farther, much farther than Mrs. Charmond had meant to go; but

[043-032]she could not begin her conversation, and in default of it kept

[043-033]walking.

[043-034]“I have seen your father,” she at length resumed. “And—I am much

[043-035]troubled by what he told me.”

[043-036]“What did he tell you? I have not been admitted to his confidence on

[043-037]anything he may have said to you.”

[043-038]“Nevertheless, why should I repeat to you what you can easily divine?”

[043-039]“True—true,” returned Grace, mournfully. “Why should you repeat what we

[043-040]both know to be in our minds already?”

[043-041]“Mrs. [[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Fitzpiers]], your husband—” The moment that the speaker’s tongue

[043-042]touched the dangerous subject a vivid look of self-consciousness

[043-043]flashed over her, in which her heart revealed, as by a lightning gleam,

[043-044]what filled it to overflowing. So transitory was the expression that

[043-045]none but a sensitive woman, and she in Grace’s position, would have had

[043-046]the power to catch its meaning. Upon her the phase was not lost.

[043-047]“Then you _do_ [[love-and-desire|love]] him!” she exclaimed, in a tone of much surprise.

[043-048]“What do you mean, my young friend?”

[043-049]“Why,” cried Grace, “I thought till now that you had only been cruelly

[043-050]flirting with my husband, to amuse your idle moments—a rich lady with a

[043-051]poor professional gentleman whom in her heart she despised not much

[043-052]less than her who belongs to him. But I guess from your manner that you

[043-053]love him desperately, and I don’t hate you as I did before.”

[043-054]“Yes, indeed,” continued Mrs. Fitzpiers, with a trembling tongue,

[043-055]“since it is not playing in your case at all, but _real_. Oh, I do pity

[043-056]you, more than I despise you, for _you_ will s-s-suffer most!”

[043-057]Mrs. Charmond was now as much agitated as Grace. “I ought not to allow

[043-058]myself to argue with you,” she exclaimed. “I demean myself by doing it.

[043-059]But I liked you once, and for the sake of that time I try to tell you

[043-060]how mistaken you are!” Much of her confusion resulted from her wonder

[043-061]and alarm at finding herself in a sense dominated mentally and

[043-062]emotionally by this simple school-girl. “I do not love him,” she went

[043-063]on, with desperate untruth. “It was a kindness—my making somewhat more

[043-064]of him than one usually does of one’s doctor. I was lonely; I

[043-065]talked—well, I trifled with him. I am very sorry if such child’s

[043-066]playing out of pure friendship has been a serious matter to you. Who

[043-067]could have expected it? But the world is so simple here.”

[043-068]“Oh, that’s affectation,” said Grace, shaking her head. “It is no

[043-069]use—you _love_ him. I can see in your face that in this matter of my

[043-070]husband you have not let your acts belie your feelings. During these

[043-071]last four or six months you have been terribly indiscreet; but you have

[043-072]not been insincere, and that almost disarms me.”

[043-073]“I _have_ been insincere—if you will have the word—I mean I _have_

[043-074]coquetted, and do _not_ love him!”

[043-075]But Grace clung to her position like a limpet. “You may have trifled

[043-076]with others, but him you love as you never loved another man.”

[043-077]“Oh, well—I won’t argue,” said Mrs. Charmond, laughing faintly. “And

[043-078]you come to reproach me for it, child.”

[043-079]“No,” said Grace, magnanimously. “You may go on loving him if you

[043-080]like—I don’t mind at all. You’ll find it, let me tell you, a bitterer

[043-081]business for yourself than for me in the end. He’ll get tired of you

[043-082]soon, as tired as can be—you don’t know him so well as I—and then you

[043-083]may wish you had never seen him!”

[043-084]Mrs. Charmond had grown quite pale and weak under this prophecy. It was

[043-085]extraordinary that Grace, whom almost every one would have

[043-086]characterized as a gentle girl, should be of stronger fibre than her

[043-087]interlocutor. “You exaggerate—cruel, silly young woman,” she

[043-088]reiterated, writhing with little agonies. “It is nothing but playful

[043-089]friendship—nothing! It will be proved by my future conduct. I shall at

[043-090]once refuse to see him more—since it will make no difference to my

[043-091]heart, and much to my name.”

[043-092]“I question if you will refuse to see him again,” said Grace, dryly, as

[043-093]with eyes askance she bent a sapling down. “But I am not incensed

[043-094]against you as you are against me,” she added, abandoning the tree to

[043-095]its natural perpendicular. “Before I came I had been despising you for

[043-096]wanton cruelty; now I only pity you for misplaced affection. When Edgar

[043-097]has gone out of the house in hope of seeing you, at seasonable hours

[043-098]and unseasonable; when I have found him riding miles and miles across

[043-099]the country at midnight, and risking his life, and getting covered with

[043-100]mud, to get a glimpse of you, I have called him a foolish man—the

[043-101]plaything of a finished coquette. I thought that what was getting to be

[043-102]a tragedy to me was a comedy to you. But now I see that tragedy lies on

[043-103]_your_ side of the situation no less than on mine, and more; that if I

[043-104]have felt trouble at my position, you have felt anguish at yours; that

[043-105]if I have had disappointments, you have had despairs. Heaven may

[043-106]fortify _me_—God help _you!_”

[043-107]“I cannot attempt to reply to your raving eloquence,” returned the

[043-108]other, struggling to restore a [[pride-and-humiliation|dignity]] which had completely collapsed.

[043-109]“My acts will be my proofs. In the world which you have seen nothing

[043-110]of, friendships between men and women are not unknown, and it would

[043-111]have been better both for you and your father if you had each judged me

[043-112]more respectfully, and left me alone. As it is I wish never to see or

[043-113]speak to you, madam, any more.”

[043-114]Grace bowed, and Mrs. Charmond turned away. The two went apart in

[043-115]directly opposite courses, and were soon [[secrecy-and-concealment|hidden]] from each other by

[043-116]their umbrageous surroundings and by the shadows of eve.

[043-117]In the excitement of their long argument they had walked onward and

[043-118]zigzagged about without regarding direction or distance. All sound of

[043-119]the woodcutters had long since faded into [[rural-isolation|remoteness]], and even had not

[043-120]the interval been too great for hearing them they would have been

[043-121]silent and homeward bound at this twilight hour. But Grace went on her

[043-122]course without any misgiving, though there was much underwood here,

[043-123]with only the narrowest passages for walking, across which brambles

[043-124]hung. She had not, however, traversed this the wildest part of the wood

[043-125]since her childhood, and the transformation of outlines had been great;

[043-126]old [[nature-in-the-woodlanders|trees]] which once were landmarks had been felled or blown down, and

[043-127]the bushes which then had been small and scrubby were now large and

[043-128]overhanging. She soon found that her ideas as to direction were

[043-129]vague—that she had indeed no ideas as to direction at all. If the

[043-130]evening had not been growing so dark, and the wind had not put on its

[043-131]night moan so distinctly, Grace would not have minded; but she was

[043-132]rather frightened now, and began to strike across hither and thither in

[043-133]random courses.

[043-134]Denser grew the darkness, more developed the wind-voices, and still no

[043-135]recognizable spot or outlet of any kind appeared, nor any sound of the

[043-136]Hintocks floated near, though she had wandered probably between one and

[043-137]two hours, and began to be weary. She was vexed at her foolishness,

[043-138]since the ground she had covered, if in a straight line, must

[043-139]inevitably have taken her out of the wood to some remote village or

[043-140]other; but she had wasted her forces in countermarches; and now, in

[043-141]much alarm, wondered if she would have to pass the night here. She

[043-142]stood still to meditate, and fancied that between the soughing of the

[043-143]wind she heard shuffling footsteps on the leaves heavier than those of

[043-144]rabbits or hares. Though fearing at first to meet anybody on the [[fate-and-free-will|chance]]

[043-145]of his being a friend, she decided that the fellow night-rambler, even

[043-146]if a poacher, would not injure her, and that he might possibly be some

[043-147]one sent to search for her. She accordingly shouted a rather timid

[043-148]“Hoi!”

[043-149]The cry was immediately returned by the other person; and Grace running

[043-150]at once in the direction whence it came beheld an indistinct figure

[043-151]hastening up to her as rapidly. They were almost in each other’s arms

[043-152]when she recognized in her vis-a-vis the outline and white veil of her

[043-153]whom she had parted from an hour and a half before—Mrs. Charmond.

[043-154]“I have lost my way, I have lost my way,” cried that lady. “Oh—is it

[043-155]indeed you? I am so glad to meet you or anybody. I have been wandering

[043-156]up and down ever since we parted, and am nearly dead with terror and

[043-157]misery and fatigue!”

[043-158]“So am I,” said Grace. “What _shall_ we, _shall_ we do?”

[043-159]“You won’t go away from me?” asked her companion, anxiously.

[043-160]“No, indeed. Are you very tired?”

[043-161]“I can scarcely move, and I am scratched dreadfully about the ankles.”

[043-162]Grace reflected. “Perhaps, as it is dry under foot, the best thing for

[043-163]us to do would be to sit down for half an hour, and then start again

[043-164]when we have thoroughly rested. By walking straight we must come to a

[043-165]track leading somewhere before the morning.”

[043-166]They found a clump of bushy hollies which afforded a shelter from the

[043-167]wind, and sat down under it, some tufts of dead fern, crisp and dry,

[043-168]that remained from the previous season forming a sort of nest for them.

[043-169]But it was cold, nevertheless, on this March night, particularly for

[043-170]Grace, who with the sanguine prematureness of youth in matters of

[043-171]dress, had considered it spring-time, and hence was not so warmly clad

[043-172]as Mrs. Charmond, who still wore her winter fur. But after sitting a

[043-173]while the latter lady shivered no less than Grace as the warmth

[043-174]imparted by her hasty walking began to go off, and they felt the cold

[043-175]air drawing through the holly leaves which scratched their backs and

[043-176]shoulders. Moreover, they could hear some drops of rain falling on the

[043-177]trees, though none reached the nook in which they had ensconced

[043-178]themselves.
