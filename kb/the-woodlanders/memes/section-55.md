# Section 55

[055-001]“You are locked in,” he said; “and your own mistress.”

[055-002]Even in her trouble she could not refrain from a faint smile at his

[055-003]scrupulousness, as she took the door-key.

[055-004]“Do you feel better?” he went on. “If so, and you wish to give me some

[055-005]of your supper, please do. If not, it is of no importance. I can get

[055-006]some elsewhere.”

[055-007]The grateful sense of his kindness stirred her to action, though she

[055-008]only knew half what that kindness really was. At the end of some ten

[055-009]minutes she again came to the window, pushed it open, and said in a

[055-010]whisper, “[[giles-winterborne|Giles]]!” He at once emerged from the shade, and saw that she

[055-011]was preparing to hand him his share of the meal upon a plate.

[055-012]“I don’t like to treat you so hardly,” she murmured, with deep regret

[055-013]in her words as she heard the rain pattering on the leaves. “But—I

[055-014]suppose it is best to arrange like this?”

[055-015]“Oh yes,” he said, quickly.

[055-016]“I feel that I could never have reached [[sherton-abbas|Sherton]].”

[055-017]“It was impossible.”

[055-018]“Are you sure you have a snug place out there?” (With renewed

[055-019]misgiving.)

[055-020]“Quite. Have you found everything you want? I am afraid it is rather

[055-021]rough accommodation.”

[055-022]“Can I notice defects? I have long passed that stage, and you know it,

[055-023]Giles, or you ought to.”

[055-024]His eyes sadly contemplated her face as its pale responsiveness

[055-025]modulated through a crowd of expressions that showed only too clearly

[055-026]to what a pitch she was strung. If ever Winterborne’s heart fretted his

[055-027]bosom it was at this sight of a perfectly defenceless creature

[055-028]conditioned by such circumstances. He forgot his own agony in the

[055-029]satisfaction of having at least found her a shelter. He took his plate

[055-030]and cup from her hands, saying, “Now I’ll push the shutter to, and you

[055-031]will find an iron pin on the inside, which you must fix into the bolt.

[055-032]Do not stir in the morning till I come and call you.”

[055-033]She expressed an alarmed hope that he would not go very far away.

[055-034]“Oh no—I shall be quite within hail,” said Winterborne.

[055-035]She bolted the window as directed, and he retreated. His snug place

[055-036]proved to be a wretched little shelter of the roughest kind, formed of

[055-037]four hurdles thatched with brake-fern. Underneath were dry sticks, hay,

[055-038]and other litter of the sort, upon which he sat down; and there in the

[055-039]dark tried to eat his meal. But his appetite was quite gone. He pushed

[055-040]the plate aside, and shook up the hay and sacks, so as to form a rude

[055-041]couch, on which he flung himself down to sleep, for it was getting

[055-042]late.

[055-043]But sleep he could not, for many reasons, of which not the least was

[055-044]thought of his charge. He sat up, and looked towards the cot through

[055-045]the damp obscurity. With all its external features the same as usual,

[055-046]he could scarcely believe that it contained the dear friend—he would

[055-047]not use a warmer name—who had come to him so unexpectedly, and, he

[055-048]could not help admitting, so rashly.

[055-049]He had not ventured to ask her any particulars; but the position was

[055-050]pretty clear without them. Though social law had negatived forever

[055-051]their opening paradise of the previous June, it was not without stoical

[055-052][[pride-and-humiliation|pride]] that he accepted the present trying conjuncture. There was one

[055-053]man on earth in whom she believed absolutely, and he was that man. That

[055-054]this crisis could end in nothing but sorrow was a view for a moment

[055-055]effaced by this triumphant thought of her trust in him; and the purity

[055-056]of the [[love-and-desire|affection]] with which he responded to that trust rendered him

[055-057]more than proof against any frailty that besieged him in relation to

[055-058]her.

[055-059]The rain, which had never ceased, now drew his attention by beginning

[055-060]to drop through the meagre screen that covered him. He rose to attempt

[055-061]some remedy for this discomfort, but the trembling of his knees and the

[055-062]throbbing of his pulse told him that in his weakness he was unable to

[055-063]fence against the storm, and he lay down to bear it as best he might.

[055-064]He was angry with himself for his feebleness—he who had been so strong.

[055-065]It was imperative that she should know nothing of his present state,

[055-066]and to do that she must not see his face by daylight, for its color

[055-067]would inevitably betray him.

[055-068]The next morning, accordingly, when it was hardly light, he rose and

[055-069]dragged his stiff limbs about the precincts, preparing for her

[055-070]everything she could require for getting breakfast within. On the bench

[055-071]outside the window-sill he placed water, wood, and other necessaries,

[055-072]writing with a piece of chalk beside them, “It is best that I should

[055-073]not see you. Put my breakfast on the bench.”

[055-074]At seven o’clock he tapped at her window, as he had promised,

[055-075]retreating at once, that she might not catch sight of him. But from his

[055-076]shelter under the boughs he could see her very well, when, in response

[055-077]to his signal, she opened the window and the light fell upon her face.

[055-078]The languid largeness of her eyes showed that her sleep had been little

[055-079]more than his own, and the pinkness of their lids, that her waking

[055-080]hours had not been free from tears.

[055-081]She read the writing, seemed, he thought, disappointed, but took up the

[055-082]materials he had provided, evidently thinking him some way off. Giles

[055-083]waited on, assured that a girl who, in spite of her culture, knew what

[055-084]country life was, would find no difficulty in the simple preparation of

[055-085]their food.

[055-086]Within the cot it was all very much as he conjectured, though Grace had

[055-087]slept much longer than he. After the loneliness of the night, she would

[055-088]have been glad to see him; but appreciating his feeling when she read

[055-089]the writing, she made no attempt to recall him. She found abundance of

[055-090]provisions laid in, his plan being to replenish his buttery weekly, and

[055-091]this being the day after the victualling van had called from Sherton.

[055-092]When the meal was ready, she put what he required outside, as she had

[055-093]done with the supper; and, notwithstanding her longing to see him,

[055-094]withdrew from the window promptly, and left him to himself.

[055-095]It had been a leaden dawn, and the rain now steadily renewed its fall.

[055-096]As she heard no more of Winterborne, she concluded that he had gone

[055-097]away to his daily work, and forgotten that he had promised to accompany

[055-098]her to Sherton; an erroneous conclusion, for he remained all day, by

[055-099]force of his condition, within fifty yards of where she was. The

[055-100]morning wore on; and in her doubt when to start, and how to travel, she

[055-101]lingered yet, keeping the door carefully bolted, lest an intruder

[055-102]should discover her. Locked in this place, she was comparatively safe,

[055-103]at any rate, and doubted if she would be safe elsewhere.

[055-104]The humid gloom of an ordinary wet day was doubled by the shade and

[055-105]drip of the leafage. Autumn, this year, was coming in with rains.

[055-106]Gazing, in her enforced idleness, from the one window of the

[055-107]living-room, she could see various small members of the animal

[055-108][[community-and-kinship|community]] that lived unmolested there—creatures of hair, fluff, and

[055-109]scale, the toothed kind and the billed kind; underground creatures,

[055-110]jointed and ringed—circumambulating the hut, under the impression that,

[055-111]Giles having gone away, nobody was there; and eying it inquisitively

[055-112]with a view to winter-quarters. Watching these neighbors, who knew

[055-113]neither law nor sin, distracted her a little from her trouble; and she

[055-114]managed to while away some portion of the afternoon by putting Giles’s

[055-115]home in order and making little improvements which she deemed that he

[055-116]would value when she was gone.

[055-117]Once or twice she fancied that she heard a faint noise amid the [[nature-in-the-woodlanders|trees]],

[055-118]resembling a cough; but as it never came any nearer she concluded that

[055-119]it was a squirrel or a bird.

[055-120]At last the daylight lessened, and she made up a larger fire for the

[055-121]evenings were chilly. As soon as it was too dark—which was

[055-122]comparatively early—to discern the human countenance in this place of

[055-123]shadows, there came to the window to her great delight, a tapping which

[055-124]she knew from its method to be Giles’s.

[055-125]She opened the casement instantly, and put out her hand to him, though

[055-126]she could only just perceive his outline. He clasped her fingers, and

[055-127]she noticed the heat of his palm and its shakiness.

[055-128]“He has been walking fast, in order to get here quickly,” she thought.

[055-129]How could she know that he had just crawled out from the straw of the

[055-130]shelter hard by; and that the heat of his hand was feverishness?

[055-131]“My dear, good Giles!” she burst out, impulsively.

[055-132]“Anybody would have done it for you,” replied Winterborne, with as much

[055-133]matter-of-fact as he could summon.

[055-134]“[[About]] my getting to [[exbury|Exbury]]?” she said.

[055-135]“I have been thinking,” responded Giles, with tender deference, “that

[055-136]you had better stay where you are for the present, if you wish not to

[055-137]be caught. I need not tell you that the place is yours as long as you

[055-138]like; and perhaps in a day or two, finding you absent, he will go away.

[055-139]At any rate, in two or three days I could do anything to assist—such as

[055-140]make inquiries, or go a great way towards Sherton-Abbas with you; for

[055-141]the cider season will soon be coming on, and I want to run down to the

[055-142]Vale to see how the crops are, and I shall go by the Sherton road. But

[055-143]for a day or two I am busy here.” He was hoping that by the time

[055-144]mentioned he would be strong enough to engage himself actively on her

[055-145]behalf. “I hope you do not feel over-much melancholy in being a

[055-146]prisoner?”

[055-147]She declared that she did not mind it; but she sighed.

[055-148]From long acquaintance they could read each other’s heart-symptoms like

[055-149]books of large type. “I fear you are sorry you came,” said Giles, “and

[055-150]that you think I should have advised you more firmly than I did not to

[055-151]stay.”

[055-152]“Oh no, dear, dear friend,” answered Grace, with a heaving bosom.

[055-153]“Don’t think that that is what I regret. What I regret is my enforced

[055-154]treatment of you—dislodging you, excluding you from your own house. Why

[055-155]should I not speak out? You know what I feel for you—what I have felt

[055-156]for no other living man, what I shall never feel for a man again! But

[055-157]as I have vowed myself to somebody else than you, and cannot be

[055-158]released, I must behave as I do behave, and keep that vow. I am not

[055-159]bound to him by any divine law, after what he has done; but I have

[055-160]promised, and I will pay.”

[055-161]The rest of the evening was passed in his handing her such things as

[055-162]she would require the next day, and casual remarks thereupon, an

[055-163]occupation which diverted her mind to some degree from pathetic views

[055-164]of her attitude towards him, and of her life in general. The only

[055-165]infringement—if infringement it could be called—of his predetermined

[055-166]bearing towards her was an involuntary pressing of her hand to his lips

[055-167]when she put it through the casement to bid him good-night. He knew she

[055-168]was weeping, though he could not see her tears.

[055-169]She again entreated his forgiveness for so selfishly appropriating the

[055-170]cottage. But it would only be for a day or two more, she thought, since

[055-171]go she must.

[055-172]He replied, yearningly, “I—I don’t like you to go away.”

[055-173]“Oh, Giles,” said she, “I know—I know! But—I am a woman, and you are a

[055-174]man. I cannot speak more plainly. ‘Whatsoever things are pure,

[055-175]whatsoever things are of good report’—you know what is in my mind,

[055-176]because you know me so well.”
