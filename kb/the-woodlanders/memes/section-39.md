# Section 39

[039-001]That [[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Fitzpiers]] could allow himself to look on any other creature for a

[039-002]moment than Grace filled [[george-melbury|Melbury]] with grief and astonishment. In the

[039-003]pure and simple life he had led it had scarcely occurred to him that

[039-004]after [[marriage-in-the-woodlanders|marriage]] a man might be faithless. That he could sweep to the

[039-005]heights of [[felice-charmond|Mrs. Charmond]]’s position, lift the veil of Isis, so to

[039-006]speak, would have amazed Melbury by its audacity if he had not

[039-007]suspected encouragement from that quarter. What could he and his simple

[039-008]Grace do to countervail the passions of such as those two sophisticated

[039-009]beings—versed in the world’s ways, armed with every apparatus for

[039-010]victory? In such an encounter the homely [[timber-economy|timber]]-dealer felt as inferior

[039-011]as a bow-and-arrow savage before the precise weapons of modern warfare.

[039-012]Grace came out of the house as the morning drew on. The village was

[039-013]silent, most of the folk having gone to the fair. Fitzpiers had retired

[039-014]to bed, and was sleeping off his fatigue. She went to the stable and

[039-015]looked at poor Darling: in all probability [[giles-winterborne|Giles Winterborne]], by

[039-016]obtaining for her a horse of such intelligence and docility, had been

[039-017]the means of saving her husband’s life. She paused over the strange

[039-018]thought; and then there appeared her father behind her. She saw that he

[039-019]knew things were not as they ought to be, from the troubled dulness of

[039-020]his eye, and from his face, different points of which had independent

[039-021]motions, twitchings, and tremblings, unknown to himself, and

[039-022]involuntary.

[039-023]“He was detained, I suppose, last night?” said Melbury.

[039-024]“Oh yes; a bad case in the vale,” she replied, calmly.

[039-025]“Nevertheless, he should have stayed at home.”

[039-026]“But he couldn’t, father.”

[039-027]Her father turned away. He could hardly bear to see his whilom truthful

[039-028]girl brought to the [[pride-and-humiliation|humiliation]] of having to talk like that.

[039-029]That night carking care sat beside Melbury’s pillow, and his stiff

[039-030]limbs tossed at its presence. “I can’t lie here any longer,” he

[039-031]muttered. Striking a light, he wandered about the room. “What have I

[039-032]done—what have I done for her?” he said to his wife, who had anxiously

[039-033]awakened. “I had long planned that she should marry the son of the man

[039-034]I wanted to make amends to; do ye mind how I told you all about it,

[039-035]Lucy, the night before she came home? Ah! but I was not content with

[039-036]doing right, I wanted to do more!”

[039-037]“Don’t raft yourself without good need, George,” she replied. “I won’t

[039-038]quite believe that things are so much amiss. I won’t believe that Mrs.

[039-039]Charmond has encouraged him. Even supposing she has encouraged a great

[039-040]many, she can have no motive to do it now. What so likely as that she

[039-041]is not yet quite well, and doesn’t care to let another doctor come near

[039-042]her?”

[039-043]He did not heed. “Grace used to be so busy every day, with fixing a

[039-044]curtain here and driving a tin-tack there; but she cares for no

[039-045]employment now!”

[039-046]“Do you know anything of Mrs. Charmond’s past history? Perhaps that

[039-047]would throw some light upon things. Before she came here as the wife of

[039-048]old Charmond four or five years ago, not a soul seems to have heard

[039-049]aught of her. Why not make inquiries? And then do ye wait and see more;

[039-050]there’ll be plenty of opportunity. Time enough to cry when you know

[039-051]’tis a crying matter; and ’tis bad to meet troubles half-way.”

[039-052]There was some good-sense in the notion of seeing further. Melbury

[039-053]resolved to inquire and wait, hoping still, but oppressed

[039-054]between-whiles with much fear.

[039-055]CHAPTER XXX.

[039-056]Examine Grace as her father might, she would admit nothing. For the

[039-057]present, therefore, he simply watched.

[039-058]The suspicion that his darling child was being slighted wrought almost

[039-059]a miraculous change in Melbury’s [[nature-in-the-woodlanders|nature]]. No man so furtive for the time

[039-060]as the ingenuous countryman who finds that his ingenuousness has been

[039-061]abused. Melbury’s heretofore confidential candor towards his

[039-062]gentlemanly son-in-law was displaced by a feline stealth that did

[039-063]injury to his every action, thought, and mood. He knew that a woman

[039-064]once given to a man for life took, as a rule, her lot as it came and

[039-065]made the best of it, without external interference; but for the first

[039-066]time he asked himself why this so generally should be so. Moreover,

[039-067]this case was not, he argued, like ordinary cases. Leaving out the

[039-068]question of Grace being anything but an ordinary woman, her peculiar

[039-069]situation, as it were in mid-air between two planes of society,

[039-070]together with the loneliness of [[little-hintock|Hintock]], made a husband’s neglect a far

[039-071]more tragical matter to her than it would be to one who had a large

[039-072]circle of friends to fall back upon. Wisely or unwisely, and whatever

[039-073]other fathers did, he resolved to fight his daughter’s battle still.

[039-074]Mrs. Charmond had returned. But [[hintock-house|Hintock House]] scarcely gave forth signs

[039-075]of life, so quietly had she reentered it. He went to church at Great

[039-076]Hintock one afternoon as usual, there being no service at the smaller

[039-077]village. A few minutes before his departure, he had casually heard

[039-078]Fitzpiers, who was no church-goer, tell his wife that he was going to

[039-079]walk in the wood. Melbury entered the building and sat down in his pew;

[039-080]the parson came in, then Mrs. Charmond, then Mr. Fitzpiers.

[039-081]The service proceeded, and the jealous father was quite sure that a

[039-082]mutual consciousness was uninterruptedly maintained between those two;

[039-083]he fancied that more than once their eyes met. At the end, Fitzpiers so

[039-084]timed his movement into the aisle that it exactly coincided with Felice

[039-085]Charmond’s from the opposite side, and they walked out with their

[039-086]garments in contact, the surgeon being just that two or three inches in

[039-087]her rear which made it convenient for his eyes to rest upon her cheek.

[039-088]The cheek warmed up to a richer tone.

[039-089]This was a worse feature in the flirtation than he had expected. If she

[039-090]had been playing with him in an idle freak the game might soon have

[039-091]wearied her; but the smallest germ of [[love-and-desire|passion]]—and [[gender-roles|women]] of the world do

[039-092]not change color for nothing—was a threatening development. The mere

[039-093]presence of Fitzpiers in the building, after his statement, was

[039-094]wellnigh conclusive as far as he was concerned; but Melbury resolved

[039-095]yet to watch.

[039-096]He had to wait long. Autumn drew shiveringly to its end. One day

[039-097]something seemed to be gone from the gardens; the tenderer leaves of

[039-098]vegetables had shrunk under the first smart frost, and hung like faded

[039-099]linen rags; then [[the-woodland-tracts|the forest]] leaves, which had been descending at

[039-100]leisure, descended in haste and in multitudes, and all the golden

[039-101]colors that had hung overhead were now crowded together in a degraded

[039-102]mass underfoot, where the fallen myriads got redder and hornier, and

[039-103]curled themselves up to rot. The only suspicious features in Mrs.

[039-104]Charmond’s existence at this season were two: the first, that she lived

[039-105]with no companion or relative about her, which, considering her age and

[039-106]attractions, was somewhat unusual conduct for a young widow in a lonely

[039-107]country-house; the other, that she did not, as in previous years, start

[039-108]from Hintock to winter abroad. In Fitzpiers, the only change from his

[039-109]last autumn’s habits lay in his abandonment of night study—his lamp

[039-110]never shone from his new dwelling as from his old.

[039-111]If the suspected ones met, it was by such adroit contrivances that even

[039-112]Melbury’s vigilance could not encounter them together. A simple call at

[039-113]her house by the doctor had nothing irregular about it, and that he had

[039-114]paid two or three such calls was certain. What had passed at those

[039-115]interviews was known only to the parties themselves; but that Felice

[039-116]Charmond was under some one’s influence Melbury soon had opportunity of

[039-117]perceiving.

[039-118]Winter had come on. Owls began to be noisy in the mornings and

[039-119]evenings, and flocks of wood-pigeons made themselves prominent again.

[039-120]One day in February, about six months after the marriage of Fitzpiers,

[039-121]Melbury was returning from [[great-hintock|Great Hintock]] on foot through the lane, when

[039-122]he saw before him the surgeon also walking. Melbury would have

[039-123]overtaken him, but at that moment Fitzpiers turned in through a gate to

[039-124]one of the rambling drives among the trees at this side of the wood,

[039-125]which led to nowhere in particular, and the beauty of whose serpentine

[039-126]curves was the only justification of their existence. Felice almost

[039-127]simultaneously trotted down the lane towards the timber-dealer, in a

[039-128]little basket-carriage which she sometimes drove about the estate,

[039-129]unaccompanied by a servant. She turned in at the same place without

[039-130]having seen either Melbury or apparently Fitzpiers. Melbury was soon at

[039-131]the spot, despite his aches and his sixty years. Mrs. Charmond had come

[039-132]up with the doctor, who was standing immediately behind the carriage.

[039-133]She had turned to him, her arm being thrown carelessly over the back of

[039-134]the seat. They looked in each other’s faces without uttering a word, an

[039-135]arch yet gloomy smile wreathing her lips. Fitzpiers clasped her hanging

[039-136]hand, and, while she still remained in the same listless attitude,

[039-137]looking volumes into his eyes, he stealthily unbuttoned her glove, and

[039-138]stripped her hand of it by rolling back the gauntlet over the fingers,

[039-139]so that it came off inside out. He then raised her hand to his month,

[039-140]she still reclining passively, watching him as she might have watched a

[039-141]fly upon her dress. At last she said, “Well, sir, what excuse for this

[039-142]disobedience?”

[039-143]“I make none.”

[039-144]“Then go your way, and let me go mine.” She snatched away her hand,

[039-145]touched the pony with the whip, and left him standing there, holding

[039-146]the reversed glove.

[039-147]Melbury’s first impulse was to reveal his presence to Fitzpiers, and

[039-148]upbraid him bitterly. But a moment’s thought was sufficient to show him

[039-149]the futility of any such simple proceeding. There was not, after all,

[039-150]so much in what he had witnessed as in what that scene might be the

[039-151]surface and froth of—probably a state of mind on which censure operates

[039-152]as an aggravation rather than as a cure. Moreover, he said to himself

[039-153]that the point of attack should be the woman, if either. He therefore

[039-154]kept out of sight, and musing sadly, even tearfully—for he was meek as

[039-155]a child in matters concerning his daughter—continued his way towards

[039-156]Hintock.

[039-157]The insight which is bred of deep sympathy was never more finely

[039-158]exemplified than in this instance. Through her guarded manner, her

[039-159]dignified speech, her placid countenance, he discerned the interior of

[039-160]Grace’s life only too truly, [[secrecy-and-concealment|hidden]] as were its incidents from every

[039-161]outer eye.

[039-162]These incidents had become painful enough. Fitzpiers had latterly

[039-163]developed an irritable discontent which vented itself in monologues

[039-164]when Grace was present to hear them. The early morning of this day had

[039-165]been dull, after a night of wind, and on looking out of the window

[039-166]Fitzpiers had observed some of Melbury’s men dragging away a large limb

[039-167]which had been snapped off a beech-tree. Everything was cold and

[039-168]colorless.

[039-169]“My good Heaven!” he said, as he stood in his dressing-gown. “This is

[039-170]life!” He did not know whether Grace was awake or not, and he would not

[039-171]turn his head to ascertain. “Ah, fool,” he went on to himself, “to clip

[039-172]your own wings when you were free to soar!...But I could not rest till

[039-173]I had done it. Why do I never recognize an opportunity till I have

[039-174]missed it, nor the good or ill of a step till it is irrevocable!...I

[039-175]fell in love....Love, indeed!—

[039-176]“‘Love’s but the frailty of the mind

[039-177]When ’tis not with ambition joined;

[039-178]A sickly flame which if not fed, expires,

[039-179]And feeding, wastes in self-consuming fires!’

[039-180]Ah, old author of ‘The Way of the World,’ you knew—you knew!” Grace

[039-181]moved. He thought she had heard some part of his soliloquy. He was

[039-182]sorry—though he had not taken any precaution to prevent her.
