# Section 31

[031-001]He went off hastily out of the garden-gate and down the lane. But she

[031-002]would not stay where she was; and edging through a slit in the

[031-003]garden-fence, walked away into the wood. Just about here the [[nature-in-the-woodlanders|trees]] were

[031-004]large and wide apart, and there was no undergrowth, so that she could

[031-005]be seen to some distance; a sylph-like, greenish-white creature, as

[031-006]toned by the sunlight and leafage. She heard a foot-fall crushing dead

[031-007]leaves behind her, and found herself reconnoitered by [[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Fitzpiers]]

[031-008]himself, approaching gay and fresh as the morning around them.

[031-009]His remote gaze at her had been one of mild interest rather than of

[031-010]rapture. But she looked so lovely in the green world about her, her

[031-011]pink cheeks, her simple light dress, and the delicate flexibility of

[031-012]her movement acquired such rarity from their wild-wood setting, that

[031-013]his eyes kindled as he drew near.

[031-014]“My darling, what is it? Your father says you are in the pouts, and

[031-015]jealous, and I don’t know what. Ha! ha! ha! as if there were any rival

[031-016]to you, except vegetable nature, in this home of recluses! We know

[031-017]better.”

[031-018]“Jealous; oh no, it is not so,” said she, gravely. “That’s a mistake of

[031-019]his and yours, sir. I spoke to him so closely about the question of

[031-020][[marriage-in-the-woodlanders|marriage]] with you that he did not apprehend my state of mind.”

[031-021]“But there’s something wrong—eh?” he asked, eying her narrowly, and

[031-022]bending to kiss her. She shrank away, and his purposed kiss miscarried.

[031-023]“What is it?” he said, more seriously for this little defeat.

[031-024]She made no answer beyond, “Mr. Fitzpiers, I have had no breakfast, I

[031-025]must go in.”

[031-026]“Come,” he insisted, fixing his eyes upon her. “Tell me at once, I

[031-027]say.”

[031-028]It was the greater strength against the smaller; but she was mastered

[031-029]less by his manner than by her own sense of the unfairness of silence.

[031-030]“I looked out of the window,” she said, with hesitation. “I’ll tell you

[031-031]by-and-by. I must go in-doors. I have had no breakfast.”

[031-032]By a sort of divination his conjecture went straight to the fact. “Nor

[031-033]I,” said he, lightly. “Indeed, I rose late to-day. I have had a broken

[031-034]night, or rather morning. A girl of the village—I don’t know her

[031-035]name—came and rang at my bell as soon as it was light—between four and

[031-036]five, I should think it was—perfectly maddened with an aching tooth. As

[031-037]no-body heard her ring, she threw some gravel at my window, till at

[031-038]last I heard her and slipped on my dressing-gown and went down. The

[031-039]poor thing begged me with tears in her eyes to take out her tormentor,

[031-040]if I dragged her head off. Down she sat and out it came—a lovely molar,

[031-041]not a speck upon it; and off she went with it in her handkerchief, much

[031-042]contented, though it would have done good work for her for fifty years

[031-043]to come.”

[031-044]It was all so plausible—so completely explained. Knowing nothing of the

[031-045]incident in the wood on old Midsummer-eve, Grace felt that her

[031-046]suspicions were unworthy and absurd, and with the readiness of an

[031-047]honest heart she jumped at the opportunity of honoring his word. At the

[031-048]moment of her mental liberation the bushes about the garden had moved,

[031-049]and her father emerged into the shady glade. “Well, I hope it is made

[031-050]up?” he said, cheerily.

[031-051]“Oh yes,” said Fitzpiers, with his eyes fixed on Grace, whose eyes were

[031-052]shyly bent downward.

[031-053]“Now,” said her father, “tell me, the pair of ye, that you still mean

[031-054]to take one another for good and all; and on the strength o’t you shall

[031-055]have another couple of hundred paid down. I swear it by the name.”

[031-056]Fitzpiers took her hand. “We declare it, do we not, my dear Grace?”

[031-057]said he.

[031-058]Relieved of her doubt, somewhat overawed, and ever anxious to please,

[031-059]she was disposed to settle the matter; yet, womanlike, she would not

[031-060]relinquish her opportunity of asking a concession of some sort. “If our

[031-061]wedding can be at church, I say yes,” she answered, in a measured

[031-062]voice. “If not, I say no.”

[031-063]Fitzpiers was generous in his turn. “It shall be so,” he rejoined,

[031-064]gracefully. “To holy church we’ll go, and much good may it do us.”

[031-065]They returned through the bushes indoors, Grace walking, full of

[031-066]thought between the other two, somewhat comforted, both by Fitzpiers’s

[031-067]ingenious explanation and by the sense that she was not to be deprived

[031-068]of a religious ceremony. “So let it be,” she said to herself. “Pray God

[031-069]it is for the best.”

[031-070]From this hour there was no serious attempt at recalcitration on her

[031-071]part. Fitzpiers kept himself continually near her, dominating any

[031-072]rebellious impulse, and shaping her will into passive concurrence with

[031-073]all his desires. Apart from his lover-like anxiety to possess her, the

[031-074]few golden hundreds of the [[timber-economy|timber]]-dealer, ready to hand, formed a warm

[031-075]background to Grace’s lovely face, and went some way to remove his

[031-076]uneasiness at the prospect of endangering his professional and social

[031-077]chances by an alliance with the family of a simple countryman.

[031-078]The interim closed up its perspective surely and silently. Whenever

[031-079]Grace had any doubts of her position, the sense of contracting time was

[031-080]like a shortening chamber: at other moments she was comparatively

[031-081]blithe. Day after day waxed and waned; the one or two woodmen who

[031-082]sawed, shaped, spokeshaved on her father’s premises at this inactive

[031-083]season of the year, regularly came and unlocked the doors in the

[031-084]morning, locked them in the evening, supped, leaned over their

[031-085]garden-gates for a whiff of evening air, and to catch any last and

[031-086]farthest throb of news from the outer world, which entered and expired

[031-087]at [[little-hintock|Little Hintock]] like the exhausted swell of a wave in some innermost

[031-088]cavern of some innermost creek of an embayed sea; yet no news

[031-089]interfered with the nuptial purpose at their [[community-and-kinship|neighbor]]’s house. The

[031-090]sappy green twig-tips of the season’s growth would not, she thought, be

[031-091]appreciably woodier on the day she became a wife, so near was the time;

[031-092]the tints of the foliage would hardly have changed. Everything was so

[031-093]much as usual that no itinerant stranger would have supposed a woman’s

[031-094][[fate-and-free-will|fate]] to be hanging in the balance at that summer’s decline.

[031-095]But there were preparations, imaginable readily enough by those who had

[031-096]special knowledge. In the remote and fashionable town of Sandbourne

[031-097]something was growing up under the hands of several persons who had

[031-098]never seen [[grace-melbury|Grace Melbury]], never would see her, or care anything about

[031-099]her at all, though their creation had such interesting relation to her

[031-100]life that it would enclose her very heart at a moment when that heart

[031-101]would beat, if not with more emotional ardor, at least with more

[031-102]emotional turbulence than at any previous time.

[031-103]Why did [[mrs-dollery|Mrs. Dollery]]’s van, instead of passing along at the end of the

[031-104]smaller village to [[great-hintock|Great Hintock]] direct, turn one Saturday night into

[031-105]Little Hintock Lane, and never pull up till it reached [[george-melbury|Mr. Melbury]]’s

[031-106]gates? The gilding shine of evening fell upon a large, flat box not

[031-107]less than a yard square, and safely tied with cord, as it was handed

[031-108]out from under the tilt with a great deal of care. But it was not heavy

[031-109]for its size; Mrs. Dollery herself carried it into the house. Tim

[031-110]Tangs, the hollow-turner, Bawtree, [[suke-damson|Suke Damson]], and others, looked

[031-111]knowing, and made remarks to each other as they watched its entrance.

[031-112]Melbury stood at the door of the timber-shed in the attitude of a man

[031-113]to whom such an arrival was a trifling domestic detail with which he

[031-114]did not condescend to be concerned. Yet he well divined the contents of

[031-115]that box, and was in truth all the while in a pleasant exaltation at

[031-116]the proof that thus far, at any rate, no disappointment had supervened.

[031-117]While Mrs. Dollery remained—which was rather long, from her sense of

[031-118]the importance of her errand—he went into the out-house; but as soon as

[031-119]she had had her say, been paid, and had rumbled away, he entered the

[031-120]dwelling, to find there what he knew he should find—his wife and

[031-121]daughter in a flutter of excitement over [[the-wedding-of-grace-and-fitzpiers|the wedding]]-gown, just arrived

[031-122]from the leading dress-maker of Sandbourne watering-place aforesaid.

[031-123]During these weeks [[giles-winterborne|Giles Winterborne]] was nowhere to be seen or heard

[031-124]of. At the close of his tenure in Hintock he had sold some of his

[031-125]furniture, packed up the rest—a few pieces endeared by associations, or

[031-126]necessary to his occupation—in the house of a friendly neighbor, and

[031-127]gone away. People said that a certain laxity had crept into his life;

[031-128]that he had never gone near a church latterly, and had been sometimes

[031-129]seen on Sundays with unblacked boots, lying on his elbow under a tree,

[031-130]with a cynical gaze at surrounding objects. He was likely to return to

[031-131]Hintock when the [[orchard-culture|cider-making]] season came round, his apparatus being

[031-132]stored there, and travel with his mill and press from village to

[031-133]village.

[031-134]The narrow interval that stood before the day diminished yet. There was

[031-135]in Grace’s mind sometimes a certain anticipative satisfaction, the

[031-136]satisfaction of feeling that she would be the heroine of an hour;

[031-137]moreover, she was proud, as a cultivated woman, to be the wife of a

[031-138]cultivated man. It was an opportunity denied very frequently to young

[031-139][[gender-roles|women]] in her position, nowadays not a few; those in whom parental

[031-140]discovery of the value of [[education-and-class-mobility|education]] has implanted tastes which parental

[031-141]circles fail to gratify. But what an attenuation was this cold [[pride-and-humiliation|pride]] of

[031-142]the dream of her youth, in which she had pictured herself walking in

[031-143]state towards the altar, flushed by the purple light and bloom of her

[031-144]own [[love-and-desire|passion]], without a single misgiving as to the sealing of the bond,

[031-145]and fervently receiving as her due

[031-146]“The homage of a thousand hearts; the fond, deep love of one.”

[031-147]Everything had been clear then, in imagination; now something was

[031-148]undefined. She had little carking anxieties; a curious fatefulness

[031-149]seemed to rule her, and she experienced a mournful want of some one to

[031-150]confide in.

[031-151]The day loomed so big and nigh that her prophetic ear could, in fancy,

[031-152]catch the noise of it, hear the murmur of the villagers as she came out

[031-153]of church, imagine the jangle of the three thin-toned Hintock bells.

[031-154]The dialogues seemed to grow louder, and the ding-ding-dong of those

[031-155]three crazed bells more persistent. She awoke: the morning had come.

[031-156]Five hours later she was the wife of Fitzpiers.

[031-157]CHAPTER XXV.

[031-158]The chief hotel at [[sherton-abbas|Sherton]]-Abbas was an old stone-fronted inn with a

[031-159]yawning arch, under which vehicles were driven by stooping coachmen to

[031-160]back premises of wonderful commodiousness. The windows to the street

[031-161]were mullioned into narrow lights, and only commanded a view of the

[031-162]opposite houses; hence, perhaps, it arose that the best and most

[031-163]luxurious private sitting-room that the inn could afford over-looked

[031-164]the nether parts of the establishment, where beyond the yard were to be

[031-165]seen gardens and orchards, now bossed, nay incrusted, with scarlet and

[031-166]gold fruit, stretching to infinite distance under a luminous lavender

[031-167]mist. The time was early autumn,

[031-168]“When the fair apples, red as evening sky,

[031-169]Do bend the tree unto the fruitful ground,

[031-170]When juicy pears, and berries of black dye,

[031-171]Do dance in air, and call the eyes around.”

[031-172]The landscape confronting the window might, indeed, have been part of

[031-173]the identical stretch of country which the youthful Chatterton had in

[031-174]his mind.

[031-175]In this room sat she who had been the maiden Grace Melbury till the

[031-176]finger of fate touched her and turned her to a wife. It was two months

[031-177]after the wedding, and she was alone. Fitzpiers had walked out to see

[031-178]the abbey by the light of sunset, but she had been too fatigued to

[031-179]accompany him. They had reached the last stage of a long eight-weeks’

[031-180]tour, and were going on to Hintock that night.
