# Section 66

[066-001]The waiter did not.

[066-002]“Do you know the lady’s name?”

[066-003]Of this, too, the household was ignorant, the hotel having been taken

[066-004]by brand-new people from a distance. They knew the gentleman very well

[066-005]by sight, and had not thought it necessary to ask him to enter his

[066-006]name.

[066-007]“Oh, the gentleman appears again now,” said [[george-melbury|Melbury]] to himself. “Well,

[066-008]I want to see the lady,” he declared.

[066-009]A message was taken up, and after some delay the shape of Grace

[066-010]appeared descending round the bend of the stair-case, looking as if she

[066-011]lived there, but in other respects rather guilty and frightened.

[066-012]“Why—what the name—” began her father. “I thought you went out to get

[066-013]parsley!”

[066-014]“Oh, yes—I did—but it is all right,” said Grace, in a flurried whisper.

[066-015]“I am not alone here. I am here with Edgar. It is entirely owing to an

[066-016]accident, father.”

[066-017]“Edgar! An accident! How does he come here? I thought he was two

[066-018]hundred mile off.”

[066-019]“Yes, so he is—I mean he has got a beautiful practice two hundred miles

[066-020]off; he has bought it with his own money, some that came to him. But he

[066-021]travelled here, and I was nearly caught in a [[the-man-trap-incident|man-trap]], and that’s how

[066-022]it is I am here. We were just thinking of sending a messenger to let

[066-023]you know.”

[066-024]Melbury did not seem to be particularly enlightened by this

[066-025]explanation.

[066-026]“You were caught in a man-trap?”

[066-027]“Yes; my dress was. That’s how it arose. Edgar is up-stairs in his own

[066-028]sitting-room,” she went on. “He would not mind seeing you, I am sure.”

[066-029]“Oh, faith, I don’t want to see him! I have seen him too often a’ready.

[066-030]I’ll see him another time, perhaps, if ’tis to oblige ’ee.”

[066-031]“He came to see me; he wanted to consult me about this large

[066-032]partnership I speak of, as it is very promising.”

[066-033]“Oh, I am glad to hear it,” said Melbury, dryly.

[066-034]A pause ensued, during which the inquiring faces and whity-brown

[066-035]clothes of Melbury’s companions appeared in the door-way.

[066-036]“Then bain’t you coming home with us?” he asked.

[066-037]“I—I think not,” said Grace, blushing.

[066-038]“H’m—very well—you are your own mistress,” he returned, in tones which

[066-039]seemed to assert otherwise. “Good-night;” and Melbury retreated towards

[066-040]the door.

[066-041]“Don’t be angry, father,” she said, following him a few steps. “I have

[066-042]done it for the best.”

[066-043]“I am not angry, though it is true I have been a little misled in this.

[066-044]However, good-night. I must get home along.”

[066-045]He left the hotel, not without relief, for to be under the eyes of

[066-046]strangers while he conversed with his lost child had embarrassed him

[066-047]much. His search-party, too, had looked awkward there, having rushed to

[066-048]the task of investigation—some in their shirt sleeves, others in their

[066-049]leather aprons, and all much stained—just as they had come from their

[066-050]work of [[timber-economy|barking]], and not in their [[sherton-abbas|Sherton]] marketing attire; while

[066-051][[robert-creedle|Creedle]], with his ropes and grapnels and air of impending tragedy, had

[066-052]added melancholy to gawkiness.

[066-053]“Now, neighbors,” said Melbury, on joining them, “as it is getting

[066-054]late, we’ll leg it home again as fast as we can. I ought to tell you

[066-055]that there has been some mistake—some arrangement entered into between

[066-056]Mr. and [[grace-melbury|Mrs. Fitzpiers]] which I didn’t quite understand—an important

[066-057]practice in the Midland counties has come to him, which made it

[066-058]necessary for her to join him to-night—so she says. That’s all it

[066-059]was—and I’m sorry I dragged you out.”

[066-060]“Well,” said the hollow-turner, “here be we six mile from home, and

[066-061]night-time, and not a hoss or four-footed creeping thing to our name. I

[066-062]say, we’ll have a mossel and a drop o’ summat to strengthen our nerves

[066-063]afore we vamp all the way back again? My throat’s as dry as a kex. What

[066-064]d’ye say so’s?”

[066-065]They all concurred in the need for this course, and proceeded to the

[066-066]antique and lampless back street, in which the red curtain of the Three

[066-067]Tuns was the only radiant object. As soon as they had stumbled down

[066-068]into the room Melbury ordered them to be served, when they made

[066-069]themselves comfortable by the long table, and stretched out their legs

[066-070]upon the herring-boned sand of the floor. Melbury himself, restless as

[066-071]usual, walked to the door while he waited for them, and looked up and

[066-072]down the street.

[066-073]“I’d gie her a good shaking if she were my maid; pretending to go out

[066-074]in the garden, and leading folk a twelve-mile traipse that have got to

[066-075]get up at five o’clock to morrow,” said a bark-ripper; who, not working

[066-076]regularly for Melbury, could afford to indulge in strong opinions.

[066-077]“I don’t speak so warm as that,” said the hollow-turner, “but if ’tis

[066-078]right for couples to make a country talk about their separating, and

[066-079]excite the neighbors, and then make fools of ’em like this, why, I

[066-080]haven’t stood upon one leg for five-and-twenty year.”

[066-081]All his listeners knew that when he alluded to his foot-lathe in these

[066-082]enigmatic terms, the speaker meant to be impressive; and Creedle chimed

[066-083]in with, “Ah, young [[gender-roles|women]] do wax wanton in these days! Why couldn’t she

[066-084]ha’ bode with her father, and been faithful?” Poor Creedle was thinking

[066-085]of his old employer.

[066-086]“But this deceiving of folks is nothing unusual in [[marriage-in-the-woodlanders|matrimony]],” said

[066-087][[farmer-bawtree|Farmer Bawtree]]. “I knowed a man and wife—faith, I don’t mind owning, as

[066-088]there’s no strangers here, that the pair were my own relations—they’d

[066-089]be at it that hot one hour that you’d hear the poker and the tongs and

[066-090]the bellows and the warming-pan flee across the house with the

[066-091]movements of their vengeance; and the next hour you’d hear ’em singing

[066-092]‘The Spotted Cow’ together as peaceable as two holy twins; yes—and very

[066-093]good voices they had, and would strike in like professional

[066-094]ballet-singers to one another’s support in the high notes.”

[066-095]“And I knowed a woman, and the husband o’ her went away for

[066-096]four-and-twenty year,” said the bark-ripper. “And one night he came

[066-097]home when she was sitting by the fire, and thereupon he sat down

[066-098]himself on the other side of the chimney-corner. ‘Well,’ says she,

[066-099]‘have ye got any news?’ ‘Don’t know as I have,’ says he; ‘have you?’

[066-100]‘No,’ says she, ‘except that my daughter by my second husband was

[066-101]married last month, which was a year after I was made a widow by him.’

[066-102]‘Oh! Anything else?’ he says. ‘No,’ says she. And there they sat, one

[066-103]on each side of that chimney-corner, and were found by their neighbors

[066-104]sound asleep in their chairs, not having known what to talk about at

[066-105]all.”

[066-106]“Well, I don’t care who the man is,” said Creedle, “they required a

[066-107]good deal to talk about, and that’s true. It won’t be the same with

[066-108]these.”

[066-109]“No. He is such a projick, you see. And she is a wonderful scholar

[066-110]too!”

[066-111]“What women do know nowadays!” observed the hollow-turner. “You can’t

[066-112]deceive ’em as you could in my time.”

[066-113]“What they knowed then was not small,” said [[john-upjohn|John Upjohn]]. “Always a good

[066-114]deal more than the men! Why, when I went courting my wife that is now,

[066-115]the skilfulness that she would show in keeping me on her pretty side as

[066-116]she walked was beyond all belief. Perhaps you’ve noticed that she’s got

[066-117]a pretty side to her face as well as a plain one?”

[066-118]“I can’t say I’ve noticed it particular much,” said the hollow-turner,

[066-119]blandly.

[066-120]“Well,” continued Upjohn, not disconcerted, “she has. All women under

[066-121]the sun be prettier one side than t’other. And, as I was saying, the

[066-122]pains she would take to make me walk on the pretty side were unending!

[066-123]I warrant that whether we were going with the sun or against the sun,

[066-124]uphill or downhill, in wind or in lewth, that wart of hers was always

[066-125]towards the hedge, and that dimple towards me. There was I, too simple

[066-126]to see her wheelings and turnings; and she so artful, though two years

[066-127]younger, that she could lead me with a cotton thread, like a blind ram;

[066-128]for that was in the third climate of our courtship. No; I don’t think

[066-129]the women have got cleverer, for they was never otherwise.”

[066-130]“How many climates may there be in courtship, Mr. Upjohn?” inquired a

[066-131]youth—the same who had assisted at [[giles-winterborne|Winterborne]]’s Christmas party.

[066-132]“Five—from the coolest to the hottest—leastwise there was five in

[066-133]mine.”

[066-134]“Can ye give us the chronicle of ’em, Mr. Upjohn?”

[066-135]“Yes—I could. I could certainly. But ’tis quite unnecessary. They’ll

[066-136]come to ye by nater, young man, too soon for your good.”

[066-137]“At present Mrs. [[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Fitzpiers]] can lead the doctor as your mis’ess could

[066-138]lead you,” the hollow-turner remarked. “She’s got him quite tame. But

[066-139]how long ’twill last I can’t say. I happened to be setting a wire on

[066-140]the top of my garden one night when he met her on the other side of the

[066-141]hedge; and the way she queened it, and fenced, and kept that poor

[066-142]feller at a distance, was enough to freeze yer blood. I should never

[066-143]have supposed it of such a girl.”

[066-144]Melbury now returned to the room, and the men having declared

[066-145]themselves refreshed, they all started on the homeward journey, which

[066-146]was by no means cheerless under the rays of the high moon. Having to

[066-147]walk the whole distance they came by a foot-path rather shorter than

[066-148]the highway, though difficult except to those who knew the country

[066-149]well. This brought them by way of Great [[little-hintock|Hintock]]; and passing the

[066-150]church-yard they observed, as they talked, a motionless figure standing

[066-151]by the gate.

[066-152]“I think it was [[marty-south|Marty South]],” said the hollow-turner, parenthetically.

[066-153]“I think ’twas; ’a was always a lonely maid,” said Upjohn. And they

[066-154]passed on homeward, and thought of the matter no more.

[066-155]It was Marty, as they had supposed. That evening had been the

[066-156]particular one of the week upon which Grace and herself had been

[066-157]accustomed to privately deposit flowers on Giles’s grave, and this was

[066-158]the first occasion since his death, eight months earlier, on which

[066-159]Grace had failed to keep her appointment. Marty had waited in the road

[066-160]just outside Little Hintock, where her fellow-pilgrim had been wont to

[066-161]join her, till she was weary; and at last, thinking that Grace had

[066-162]missed her and gone on alone, she followed the way to [[great-hintock|Great Hintock]],

[066-163]but saw no Grace in front of her. It got later, and Marty continued her

[066-164]walk till she reached the church-yard gate; but still no Grace. Yet her

[066-165]sense of comradeship would not allow her to go on to the grave alone,

[066-166]and still thinking the delay had been unavoidable, she stood there with

[066-167]her little basket of flowers in her clasped hands, and her feet chilled

[066-168]by the damp ground, till more than two hours had passed.

[066-169]She then heard the footsteps of Melbury’s men, who presently passed on

[066-170]their return from the search. In the silence of the night Marty could

[066-171]not help hearing fragments of their conversation, from which she

[066-172]acquired a general idea of what had occurred, and where Mrs. Fitzpiers

[066-173]then was.

[066-174]Immediately they had dropped down the hill she entered the church-yard,

[066-175]going to a secluded corner behind the bushes, where rose the unadorned

[066-176]stone that marked the last bed of Giles Winterborne. As this solitary

[066-177]and silent girl stood there in the moonlight, a straight slim figure,

[066-178]clothed in a plaitless gown, the contours of womanhood so undeveloped

[066-179]as to be scarcely perceptible, the marks of poverty and toil effaced by

[066-180]the misty hour, she touched sublimity at points, and looked almost like

[066-181]a being who had rejected with indifference the attribute of sex for the

[066-182]loftier quality of abstract humanism. She stooped down and cleared away

[066-183]the withered flowers that Grace and herself had laid there the previous

[066-184]week, and put her fresh ones in their place.
