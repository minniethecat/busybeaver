## Overview

Great Hintock is the larger of the two Hintock villages and serves as the ecclesiastical and administrative center of the Hintock parish. While Little Hintock is the primary setting of *The Woodlanders*, Great Hintock provides essential context — its church, its blacksmith, and its band appear as points of connection between the secluded woodland hamlet and the wider world.

## Geography

Great Hintock lies within walking distance of Little Hintock, the two villages connected by lanes and woodland paths [018-135]. The journey between them is a familiar one for the novel's characters: Melbury walks from Great Hintock on multiple occasions [039-121], and Grace passes through Great Hintock on her way to and from Little Hintock [065-167].

Unlike Little Hintock, which sits nestled deep within the woods, Great Hintock has more connection to the broader region. It is described as being on the route to more distant destinations, functioning as a waypoint between isolation and the market towns beyond.

## Landmarks and Features

### The Parish Church

Great Hintock church stands at the upper part of the parish [060-006], a position that reinforces its role as the spiritual center of the community. The church serves both Little and Great Hintock, binding the scattered woodland population into a single ecclesiastical unit.

### The Blacksmith

The master-blacksmith of Great Hintock [033-055] is a figure of practical importance in the rural economy, serving the needs of farmers, woodlanders, and travelers alike.

### The Band

The village band of Great Hintock [013-092] provides music for local dances and gatherings. Their presence at events like the Midsummer dance [014-066] marks a rare moment of festivity and social gathering in the novel's otherwise austere rural life.

## Social Role

Great Hintock represents a slight step up the social ladder from Little Hintock. Marty South describes it as a place where "you do see the world a bit" [001-114], suggesting that even this modest village offers more exposure to external life than the deeply secluded woodland hamlet. The presence of tradesmen [026-176] and the church establishes Great Hintock as a modest but functioning village center.

When Grace Melbury observes that "Great Hintock is bad enough—but Little Hintock" [065-167], she articulates the novel's social geography: Great Hintock, though hardly cosmopolitan, stands above Little Hintock in the rural hierarchy.
