# Buckbury Fitzpiers

Buckbury Fitzpiers is the ancestral village of the Fitzpiers family, situated in a sheltered valley within driving distance of [[little-hintock|Little Hintock]]. Though it appears only briefly in *The Woodlanders*, its significance lies in what it represents: the faded gentility and historical prestige of the family to which [[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Edred Fitzpiers]] belongs.

## Location and Description

The village is described as lying in "a particularly green sheltered bottom" [029-079] behind a hill, its location marked by "a plantation reaching over the hill like a great slug" [029-078]. [[george-melbury|George Melbury]] points it out to his daughter [[grace-melbury|Grace]] with evident admiration, noting that the Fitzpiers family "were lords of the manor for I don't know how many hundred years" [029-080] — a claim that underlines the family's long-established status in the region.

## The Castle Ruins

Near the village stand the ruins of a castle, which Grace visits when she drives out to see the place for herself. She has "no doubt that it represented the ancient stronghold of the Fitzpiers family" [029-109], though the remains are described as "few, and consisted mostly of remnants of the lower" [029-110] portions of the structure. The ruined castle serves as a visual emblem of the family's decline — once powerful lords of the manor, the Fitzpiers family is now represented only by the doctor and "a maiden lady living I don't know where" [029-085].

## Symbolic Significance

Buckbury Fitzpiers functions in the novel as a marker of inherited social status. Melbury's eagerness to point out the village to Grace is part of his campaign to persuade her that marrying Fitzpiers represents a step up in the world — to "feel as if you've stepped into history" [029-087]. The irony, carefully developed by Hardy, is that this grand heritage has dwindled to a country doctor with a modest practice and dubious morals.
