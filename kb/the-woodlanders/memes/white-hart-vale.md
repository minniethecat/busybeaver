# White Hart Vale

White Hart Vale is an expansive valley in the Wessex landscape of Thomas Hardy's *The Woodlanders*, visible from the elevated vantage points around Hintock. Known variously as the Vale of the White Hart, the Great White Hart, and Blackmoor Valley (or Blackmoor Vale), it represents the wider agricultural world beyond the enclosed woodlands. The vale serves both as a pastoral backdrop to the cider-making economy and as the geographical setting for one of the novel's pivotal episodes — Fitzpiers's mysterious nocturnal ride and the gradual assembly of evidence pointing to his affair with Felice Charmond.

## 地理位置与多重命名

The vale lies at the foot of the steep slopes below Hintock, accessible via Rub-Down Hill. The narrator describes how "a steep" incline slants "down directly into Blackmoor Vale, or the Vale of the White Hart, extending beneath the eye at this point to a distance of many miles" [036-135]. This double naming — Blackmoor and White Hart — is a notable feature of Hardy's treatment of the landscape: the names are used interchangeably, as when Melbury and Grace look out across "the Great White Hart or Blackmoor Valley" [038-086] during their dawn search for Fitzpiers.

The duality of the name reflects two ways of seeing the same land: "Blackmoor" evokes the dark, peaty soil of the lowland terrain, while "White Hart" carries heraldic and folkloric associations. The white hart was a symbol of purity and pursuit in medieval legend, and its application to this working agricultural valley introduces a note of ironic elevation — this is cider country, not chivalric romance, yet the name endures.

## 景观与农业

The autumn landscape of White Hart Vale presents an image of agricultural richness: it is "surrounded by orchards lustrous with the ripeness of the season" [037-054]. The vale is the centre of local cider production — a cider-maker works there with "his vats and presses beside him" [050-095] — and the orchards and pressing sheds form part of the rural economy that sustains the Hintock community. Cider itself recurs throughout the novel as a social medium and a marker of rural identity, and White Hart Vale is its place of origin.

The lower reaches of the vale are notably damp. After Fitzpiers's night ride, the mud spattered on his mare could only have come from "the clammy hollows of the vale beyond Owlscombe," where "the stiff soil retained moisture for weeks after the uplands were dry" [038-141]. This detail — the distinctive, lingering mud of the vale's hollows — becomes an inadvertent geographical witness to Fitzpiers's movements, a material clue that Melbury reads alongside the name "Felice" murmured in his son-in-law's half-conscious state [038-144].

## 叙事中的角色

### Fitzpiers 的夜骑

White Hart Vale is the terrain through which Fitzpiers rides on the night that unravels his marriage. Having left his wife Grace alone, Fitzpiers travels homeward through the vale in a state of extreme exhaustion. He stops at Holywell spring to let his mare drink, and it is there that "the clock of Owlscombe Church struck twelve" — the last conscious memory Fitzpiers retains before falling into a deep sleep in the saddle [038-120]. He awakens only when Grace finds him at dawn, slumped on his horse at the edge of the woodlands.

The vale thus becomes the geographical correlative of Fitzpiers's moral darkness: it is through this landscape that he travels between his wife at Hintock and his mistress Mrs. Charmond, and it is the mud of the vale's hollows — "the foreign quality of the mud" [038-144] — that provides the first physical evidence of his duplicity.

### 远景与疏离

Grace discovers Fitzpiers leaning on the gate at Rub-Down Hill, looking out over the vale — "his attention fixed on the landscape far away" [036-137], his "lips moving unconsciously, as to some impassioned visionary theme" [036-139]. This image of the doctor gazing abstractedly at the distant vale recurs as a motif: his eyes are always on the horizon, never on the immediate world of Hintock and his marriage. The vale, in its vastness, becomes the visual emblem of his rootlessness and his unfitness for the enclosed, arboreal existence that Giles Winterborne embodies.

### 黎明搜索

When Grace and her father climb the hill at dawn to search for the missing Fitzpiers, the vale is described with a preternatural, almost Gothic atmosphere: "wrapped in a dim atmosphere of unnaturalness," the eastern sky "like a livid curtain edged with pink" [038-092]. This uncanny dawn light, spreading over the vale below them, marks the moment when the crisis of the Fitzpiers marriage becomes visible — no longer a private suspicion but a public emergency, witnessed by Melbury, the workmen, and the landscape itself.

## Owlscombe and Human Settlement

The village of Owlscombe lies within White Hart Vale [038-050]. It is the nominal destination that Grace gives for Fitzpiers's absence — he is supposedly "detained by a case at Owlscombe" — but this proves to be a fiction [038-050]. The real Owlscombe, with its church clock striking twelve [038-120], is a landmark of truth in an episode thick with deception: the clock tells the hour plainly, even as Fitzpiers's story does not.

## 象征意义

White Hart Vale functions in the novel as a threshold space between the known and the unknown, the domestic and the hidden. The woodlands of Hintock enclose and shelter; the vale opens outward, promising both fertility (orchards, cider) and danger (mud, darkness, the secret road to Mrs. Charmond). Its dual name — Blackmoor / White Hart — encodes this doubleness: it is at once a place of earthy agricultural labour and a landscape of romantic and heraldic association, neither of which quite captures the human drama that unfolds across it.
