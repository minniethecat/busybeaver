# Section 54

[054-001]“Oh, father!” said Grace, turning white with dismay.

[054-002]“Why not?” said he, a little of his former doggedness returning. He

[054-003]was, in truth, disposed to somewhat more leniency towards her husband

[054-004]just now than he had shown formerly, from a conviction that he had

[054-005]treated him over-roughly in his anger. “Surely it is the most

[054-006]respectable thing to do?” he continued. “I don’t like this state that

[054-007]you are in—neither married nor single. It hurts me, and it hurts you,

[054-008]and it will always be remembered against us in [[little-hintock|Hintock]]. There has never

[054-009]been any scandal like it in the family before.”

[054-010]“He will be here in less than an hour,” murmured Grace. The twilight of

[054-011]the room prevented her father seeing the despondent misery of her face.

[054-012]The one intolerable condition, the condition she had deprecated above

[054-013]all others, was that of [[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Fitzpiers]]’s reinstatement there. “Oh, I won’t,

[054-014]I won’t see him,” she said, sinking down. She was almost hysterical.

[054-015]“Try if you cannot,” he returned, moodily.

[054-016]“Oh yes, I will, I will,” she went on, inconsequently. “I’ll try;” and

[054-017]jumping up suddenly, she left the room.

[054-018]In the darkness of the apartment to which she flew nothing could have

[054-019]been seen during the next half-hour; but from a corner a quick

[054-020]breathing was audible from this impressible creature, who combined

[054-021]modern nerves with primitive emotions, and was doomed by such

[054-022]coexistence to be numbered among the distressed, and to take her

[054-023]scourgings to their exquisite extremity.

[054-024]The window was open. On this quiet, late summer evening, whatever sound

[054-025]arose in so secluded a district—the chirp of a bird, a call from a

[054-026]voice, the turning of a wheel—extended over bush and tree to unwonted

[054-027]distances. Very few sounds did arise. But as Grace invisibly breathed

[054-028]in the brown glooms of the chamber, the small remote noise of light

[054-029]wheels came in to her, accompanied by the trot of a horse on the

[054-030]turnpike-road. There seemed to be a sudden hitch or pause in the

[054-031]progress of the vehicle, which was what first drew her attention to it.

[054-032]She knew the point whence the sound proceeded—the hill-top over which

[054-033]travellers passed on their way hitherward from [[sherton-abbas|Sherton Abbas]]—the place

[054-034]at which she had emerged from the wood with [[felice-charmond|Mrs. Charmond]]. Grace slid

[054-035]along the floor, and bent her head over the window-sill, listening with

[054-036]open lips. The carriage had stopped, and she heard a man use

[054-037]exclamatory words. Then another said, “What the devil is the matter

[054-038]with the horse?” She recognized the voice as her husband’s.

[054-039]The accident, such as it had been, was soon remedied, and the carriage

[054-040]could be heard descending the hill on the Hintock side, soon to turn

[054-041]into the lane leading out of the highway, and then into the “drong”

[054-042]which led out of the lane to the house where she was.

[054-043]A spasm passed through Grace. The Daphnean instinct, exceptionally

[054-044]strong in her as a girl, had been revived by her widowed [[rural-isolation|seclusion]]; and

[054-045]it was not lessened by her affronted sentiments towards the comer, and

[054-046]her regard for another man. She opened some little ivory tablets that

[054-047]lay on the dressing-table, scribbled in pencil on one of them, “I am

[054-048]gone to visit one of my school-friends,” gathered a few toilet

[054-049]necessaries into a hand-bag, and not three minutes after that voice had

[054-050]been heard, her slim form, hastily wrapped up from observation, might

[054-051]have been seen passing out of the back door of [[george-melbury|Melbury]]’s house. Thence

[054-052]she skimmed up the garden-path, through the gap in the hedge, and into

[054-053]the mossy cart-track under the [[nature-in-the-woodlanders|trees]] which led into the depth of the

[054-054]woods.

[054-055]The leaves overhead were now in their latter green—so opaque, that it

[054-056]was darker at some of the densest spots than in winter-time, scarce a

[054-057]crevice existing by which a ray could get down to the ground. But in

[054-058]open places she could see well enough. Summer was ending: in the

[054-059]daytime singing insects hung in every sunbeam; vegetation was heavy

[054-060]nightly with globes of dew; and after showers creeping damps and

[054-061]twilight chills came up from the hollows. The plantations were always

[054-062]weird at this hour of eve—more spectral far than in the leafless

[054-063]season, when there were fewer masses and more minute lineality. The

[054-064]smooth surfaces of glossy plants came out like weak, lidless eyes;

[054-065]there were strange faces and figures from expiring lights that had

[054-066]somehow wandered into the canopied obscurity; while now and then low

[054-067]peeps of the sky between the trunks were like sheeted shapes, and on

[054-068]the tips of boughs sat faint cloven tongues.

[054-069]But Grace’s fear just now was not imaginative or spiritual, and she

[054-070]heeded these impressions but little. She went on as silently as she

[054-071]could, avoiding the hollows wherein leaves had accumulated, and

[054-072]stepping upon soundless moss and grass-tufts. She paused breathlessly

[054-073]once or twice, and fancied that she could hear, above the beat of her

[054-074]strumming pulse, the vehicle containing Fitzpiers turning in at the

[054-075]gate of her father’s premises. She hastened on again.

[054-076]The [[the-woodland-tracts|Hintock woods]] owned by Mrs. Charmond were presently left behind,

[054-077]and those into which she next plunged were divided from the latter by a

[054-078]bank, from whose top the hedge had long ago perished—starved for want

[054-079]of sun. It was with some caution that Grace now walked, though she was

[054-080]quite free from any of the commonplace timidities of her ordinary

[054-081]pilgrimages to such spots. She feared no lurking harms, but that her

[054-082]effort would be all in vain, and her return to the house rendered

[054-083]imperative.

[054-084]She had walked between three and four miles when that prescriptive

[054-085]comfort and relief to wanderers in woods—a distant light—broke at last

[054-086]upon her searching eyes. It was so very small as to be almost sinister

[054-087]to a stranger, but to her it was what she sought. She pushed forward,

[054-088]and the dim outline of a dwelling was disclosed.

[054-089]The house was a square cot of one story only, sloping up on all sides

[054-090]to a chimney in the midst. It had formerly been the home of a

[054-091]charcoal-burner, in times when that fuel was still used in the county

[054-092]houses. Its only appurtenance was a paled enclosure, there being no

[054-093]garden, the shade of the trees preventing the growth of vegetables. She

[054-094]advanced to the window whence the rays of light proceeded, and the

[054-095]shutters being as yet unclosed, she could survey the whole interior

[054-096]through the panes.

[054-097]The room within was kitchen, parlor, and scullery all in one; the

[054-098]natural sandstone floor was worn into hills and dales by long treading,

[054-099]so that none of the furniture stood level, and the table slanted like a

[054-100]desk. A fire burned on the hearth, in front of which revolved the

[054-101]skinned carcass of a rabbit, suspended by a string from a nail. Leaning

[054-102]with one arm on the mantle-shelf stood [[giles-winterborne|Winterborne]], his eyes on the

[054-103]roasting animal, his face so rapt that speculation could build nothing

[054-104]on it concerning his thoughts, more than that they were not with the

[054-105]scene before him. She thought his features had changed a little since

[054-106]she saw them last. The fire-light did not enable her to perceive that

[054-107]they were positively haggard.

[054-108]Grace’s throat emitted a gasp of relief at finding the result so nearly

[054-109]as she had hoped. She went to the door and tapped lightly.

[054-110]He seemed to be accustomed to the noises of woodpeckers, squirrels, and

[054-111]such small creatures, for he took no notice of her tiny signal, and she

[054-112]knocked again. This time he came and opened the door. When the light of

[054-113]the room fell upon her face he started, and, hardly knowing what he

[054-114]did, crossed the threshold to her, placing his hands upon her two arms,

[054-115]while surprise, joy, alarm, sadness, chased through him by turns. With

[054-116]Grace it was the same: even in this stress there was the fond fact that

[054-117]they had met again. Thus they stood,

[054-118]“Long tears upon their faces, waxen white

[054-119]With extreme sad delight.”

[054-120]He broke the silence by saying in a whisper, “Come in.”

[054-121]“No, no, Giles!” she answered, hurriedly, stepping yet farther back

[054-122]from the door. “I am passing by—and I have called on you—I won’t enter.

[054-123]Will you help me? I am afraid. I want to get by a roundabout way to

[054-124]Sherton, and so to [[exbury|Exbury]]. I have a school-fellow there—but I cannot

[054-125]get to Sherton alone. Oh, if you will only accompany me a little way!

[054-126]Don’t condemn me, Giles, and be offended! I was obliged to come to you

[054-127]because—I have no other help here. Three months ago you were my lover;

[054-128]now you are only my friend. The law has stepped in, and forbidden what

[054-129]we thought of. It must not be. But we can act honestly, and yet you can

[054-130]be my friend for one little hour? I have no other—”

[054-131]She could get no further. Covering her eyes with one hand, by an effort

[054-132]of repression she wept a silent trickle, without a sigh or sob.

[054-133]Winterborne took her other hand. “What has happened?” he said.

[054-134]“He has come.”

[054-135]There was a stillness as of death, till Winterborne asked, “You mean

[054-136]this, Grace—that I am to help you to get away?”

[054-137]“Yes,” said she. “Appearance is no matter, when the reality is right. I

[054-138]have said to myself I can trust you.”

[054-139]Giles knew from this that she did not suspect his treachery—if it could

[054-140]be called such—earlier in the summer, when they met for the last time

[054-141]as lovers; and in the intensity of his contrition for that tender

[054-142]wrong, he determined to deserve her faith now at least, and so wipe out

[054-143]that reproach from his conscience. “I’ll come at once,” he said. “I’ll

[054-144]light a lantern.”

[054-145]He unhooked a dark-lantern from a nail under the eaves and she did not

[054-146]notice how his hand shook with the slight strain, or dream that in

[054-147]making this offer he was taxing a convalescence which could ill afford

[054-148]such self-sacrifice. The lantern was lit, and they started.

[054-149]CHAPTER XLI.

[054-150]The first hundred yards of their course lay under motionless trees,

[054-151]whose upper foliage began to hiss with falling drops of rain. By the

[054-152]time that they emerged upon a glade it rained heavily.

[054-153]“This is awkward,” said Grace, with an effort to hide her concern.

[054-154]Winterborne stopped. “Grace,” he said, preserving a strictly business

[054-155]manner which belied him, “you cannot go to Sherton to-night.”

[054-156]“But I must!”

[054-157]“Why? It is nine miles from here. It is almost an impossibility in this

[054-158]rain.”

[054-159]“True—_why?_” she replied, mournfully, at the end of a silence. “What

[054-160]is reputation to me?”

[054-161]“Now hearken,” said Giles. “You won’t—go back to your—”

[054-162]“No, no, no! Don’t make me!” she cried, piteously.

[054-163]“Then let us turn.” They slowly retraced their steps, and again stood

[054-164]before his door. “Now, this house from this moment is yours, and not

[054-165]mine,” he said, deliberately. “I have a place near by where I can stay

[054-166]very well.”

[054-167]Her face had drooped. “Oh!” she murmured, as she saw the dilemma. “What

[054-168]have I done!”

[054-169]There was a smell of something burning within, and he looked through

[054-170]the window. The rabbit that he had been cooking to coax a weak appetite

[054-171]was beginning to char. “Please go in and attend to it,” he said. “Do

[054-172]what you like. Now I leave. You will find everything about the hut that

[054-173]is necessary.”

[054-174]“But, Giles—your supper,” she exclaimed. “An out-house would do for

[054-175]me—anything—till to-morrow at day-break!”

[054-176]He signified a negative. “I tell you to go in—you may catch agues out

[054-177]here in your delicate state. You can give me my supper through the

[054-178]window, if you feel well enough. I’ll wait a while.”

[054-179]He gently urged her to pass the door-way, and was relieved when he saw

[054-180]her within the room sitting down. Without so much as crossing the

[054-181]threshold himself, he closed the door upon her, and turned the key in

[054-182]the lock. Tapping at the window, he signified that she should open the

[054-183]casement, and when she had done this he handed in the key to her.
