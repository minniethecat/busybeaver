# Section 23

[023-001]But whether he meditated the Muses or the philosophers, the loneliness

[023-002]of [[little-hintock|Hintock]] life was beginning to tell upon his impressionable [[nature-in-the-woodlanders|nature]].

[023-003]Winter in a solitary house in the country, without society, is

[023-004]tolerable, nay, even enjoyable and delightful, given certain

[023-005]conditions, but these are not the conditions which attach to the life

[023-006]of a professional man who drops down into such a place by mere

[023-007]accident. They were present to the lives of [[giles-winterborne|Winterborne]], [[george-melbury|Melbury]], and

[023-008]Grace; but not to the doctor’s. They are old association—an almost

[023-009]exhaustive biographical or historical acquaintance with every object,

[023-010]animate and inanimate, within the observer’s horizon. He must know all

[023-011]about those invisible ones of the days gone by, whose feet have

[023-012]traversed the fields which look so gray from his windows; recall whose

[023-013]creaking plough has turned those sods from time to time; whose hands

[023-014]planted the trees that form a crest to the opposite hill; whose horses

[023-015]and hounds have torn through that underwood; what birds affect that

[023-016]particular brake; what domestic dramas of [[love-and-desire|love]], [[jealousy-and-rivalry|jealousy]], revenge, or

[023-017]disappointment have been enacted in the cottages, the mansion, the

[023-018]street, or on the green. The spot may have beauty, grandeur, salubrity,

[023-019]convenience; but if it lack memories it will ultimately pall upon him

[023-020]who settles there without opportunity of intercourse with his kind.

[023-021]In such circumstances, maybe, an old man dreams of an ideal friend,

[023-022]till he throws himself into the arms of any impostor who chooses to

[023-023]wear that title on his face. A young man may dream of an ideal friend

[023-024]likewise, but some humor of the blood will probably lead him to think

[023-025]rather of an ideal mistress, and at length the rustle of a woman’s

[023-026]dress, the sound of her voice, or the transit of her form across the

[023-027]field of his vision, will enkindle his soul with a flame that blinds

[023-028]his eyes.

[023-029]The discovery of the attractive Grace’s name and family would have been

[023-030]enough in other circumstances to lead the doctor, if not to put her

[023-031]personality out of his head, to change the character of his interest in

[023-032]her. Instead of treasuring her image as a rarity, he would at most have

[023-033]played with it as a toy. He was that kind of a man. But situated here

[023-034]he could not go so far as amative cruelty. He dismissed all reverential

[023-035]thought about her, but he could not help taking her seriously.

[023-036]He went on to imagine the impossible. So far, indeed, did he go in this

[023-037]futile direction that, as others are wont to do, he constructed

[023-038]dialogues and scenes in which Grace had turned out to be the mistress

[023-039]of Hintock Manor-house, the mysterious [[felice-charmond|Mrs. Charmond]], particularly

[023-040]ready and willing to be wooed by himself and nobody else. “Well, she

[023-041]isn’t that,” he said, finally. “But she’s a very sweet, nice,

[023-042]exceptional girl.”

[023-043]The next morning he breakfasted alone, as usual. It was snowing with a

[023-044]fine-flaked desultoriness just sufficient to make the woodland gray,

[023-045]without ever achieving whiteness. There was not a single letter for

[023-046][[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Fitzpiers]], only a medical circular and a weekly newspaper.

[023-047]To sit before a large fire on such mornings, and read, and gradually

[023-048]acquire energy till the evening came, and then, with lamp alight, and

[023-049]feeling full of vigor, to pursue some engrossing subject or other till

[023-050]the small hours, had hitherto been his practice. But to-day he could

[023-051]not settle into his chair. That self-contained position he had lately

[023-052]occupied, in which the only attention demanded was the concentration of

[023-053]the inner eye, all outer regard being quite gratuitous, seemed to have

[023-054]been taken by insidious stratagem, and for the first time he had an

[023-055]interest outside the house. He walked from one window to another, and

[023-056]became aware that the most irksome of solitudes is not the solitude of

[023-057][[rural-isolation|remoteness]], but that which is just outside desirable company.

[023-058]The breakfast hour went by heavily enough, and the next followed, in

[023-059]the same half-snowy, half-rainy style, the weather now being the

[023-060]inevitable relapse which sooner or later succeeds a time too radiant

[023-061]for the season, such as they had enjoyed in the late midwinter at

[023-062]Hintock. To people at home there these changeful tricks had their

[023-063]interests; the strange mistakes that some of the more sanguine trees

[023-064]had made in budding before their month, to be incontinently glued up by

[023-065]frozen thawings now; the similar sanguine errors of impulsive birds in

[023-066]framing nests that were now swamped by snow-water, and other such

[023-067]incidents, prevented any sense of wearisomeness in the minds of the

[023-068]natives. But these were features of a world not familiar to Fitzpiers,

[023-069]and the inner visions to which he had almost exclusively attended

[023-070]having suddenly failed in their power to absorb him, he felt

[023-071]unutterably dreary.

[023-072]He wondered how long [[grace-melbury|Miss Melbury]] was going to stay in Hintock. The

[023-073]season was unpropitious for accidental encounters with her

[023-074]out-of-doors, and except by accident he saw not how they were to become

[023-075]acquainted. One thing was clear—any acquaintance with her could only,

[023-076]with a due regard to his future, be casual, at most of the nature of a

[023-077]flirtation; for he had high aims, and they would some day lead him into

[023-078]other spheres than this.

[023-079]Thus desultorily thinking he flung himself down upon the couch, which,

[023-080]as in many draughty old country houses, was constructed with a hood,

[023-081]being in fact a legitimate development from the settle. He tried to

[023-082]read as he reclined, but having sat up till three o’clock that morning,

[023-083]the book slipped from his hand and he fell asleep.

[023-084]CHAPTER XVIII.

[023-085]It was at this time that Grace approached the house. Her knock, always

[023-086]soft in virtue of her nature, was softer to-day by reason of her

[023-087]strange errand. However, it was heard by the farmer’s wife who kept the

[023-088]house, and Grace was admitted. Opening the door of the doctor’s room

[023-089]the housewife glanced in, and imagining Fitzpiers absent, asked Miss

[023-090]Melbury to enter and wait a few minutes while she should go and find

[023-091]him, believing him to be somewhere on the premises. Grace acquiesced,

[023-092]went in, and sat down close to the door.

[023-093]As soon as the door was shut upon her she looked round the room, and

[023-094]started at perceiving a handsome man snugly ensconced in the couch,

[023-095]like the recumbent figure within some canopied mural tomb of the

[023-096]fifteenth century, except that his hands were by no means clasped in

[023-097]prayer. She had no doubt that this was the doctor. Awaken him herself

[023-098]she could not, and her immediate impulse was to go and pull the broad

[023-099]ribbon with a brass rosette which hung at one side of the fireplace.

[023-100]But expecting the landlady to re-enter in a moment she abandoned this

[023-101]intention, and stood gazing in great embarrassment at the reclining

[023-102]philosopher.

[023-103]The windows of Fitzpiers’s soul being at present shuttered, he probably

[023-104]appeared less impressive than in his hours of animation; but the light

[023-105]abstracted from his material presence by sleep was more than

[023-106]counterbalanced by the mysterious influence of that state, in a

[023-107]stranger, upon the consciousness of a beholder so sensitive. So far as

[023-108]she could criticise at all, she became aware that she had encountered a

[023-109]specimen of creation altogether unusual in that locality. The occasions

[023-110]on which Grace had observed men of this stamp were when she had been

[023-111]far removed away from Hintock, and even then such examples as had met

[023-112]her eye were at a distance, and mainly of coarser fibre than the one

[023-113]who now confronted her.

[023-114]She nervously wondered why the woman had not discovered her mistake and

[023-115]returned, and went again towards the bell-pull. Approaching the chimney

[023-116]her back was to Fitzpiers, but she could see him in the glass. An

[023-117]indescribable thrill passed through her as she perceived that the eyes

[023-118]of the reflected image were open, gazing wonderingly at her, and under

[023-119]the curious unexpectedness of the sight she became as if spellbound,

[023-120]almost powerless to turn her head and regard the original. However, by

[023-121]an effort she did turn, when there he lay asleep the same as before.

[023-122]Her startled perplexity as to what he could be meaning was sufficient

[023-123]to lead her to precipitately abandon her errand. She crossed quickly to

[023-124]the door, opened and closed it noiselessly, and went out of the house

[023-125]unobserved. By the time that she had gone down the path and through the

[023-126]garden door into the lane she had recovered her equanimity. Here,

[023-127]screened by the hedge, she stood and considered a while.

[023-128]Drip, drip, drip, fell the rain upon her umbrella and around; she had

[023-129]come out on such a morning because of the seriousness of the matter in

[023-130]hand; yet now she had allowed her mission to be stultified by a

[023-131]momentary tremulousness concerning an incident which perhaps had meant

[023-132]nothing after all.

[023-133]In the mean time her departure from the room, stealthy as it had been,

[023-134]had roused Fitzpiers, and he sat up. In the reflection from the mirror

[023-135]which Grace had beheld there was no mystery; he had opened his eyes for

[023-136]a few moments, but had immediately relapsed into unconsciousness, if,

[023-137]indeed, he had ever been positively awake. That somebody had just left

[023-138]the room he was certain, and that the lovely form which seemed to have

[023-139]visited him in a dream was no less than the real presentation of the

[023-140]person departed he could hardly doubt.

[023-141]Looking out of the window a few minutes later, down the box-edged

[023-142]gravel-path which led to the bottom, he saw the garden door gently

[023-143]open, and through it enter the young girl of his thoughts, Grace having

[023-144]just at this juncture determined to return and attempt the interview a

[023-145]second time. That he saw her coming instead of going made him ask

[023-146]himself if his first impression of her were not a dream indeed. She

[023-147]came hesitatingly along, carrying her umbrella so low over her head

[023-148]that he could hardly see her face. When she reached the point where the

[023-149]raspberry bushes ended and the strawberry bed began, she made a little

[023-150]pause.

[023-151]Fitzpiers feared that she might not be coming to him even now, and

[023-152]hastily quitting the room, he ran down the path to meet her. The nature

[023-153]of her errand he could not divine, but he was prepared to give her any

[023-154]amount of encouragement.

[023-155]“I beg pardon, Miss Melbury,” he said. “I saw you from the window, and

[023-156]fancied you might imagine that I was not at home—if it is I you were

[023-157]coming for.”

[023-158]“I was coming to speak one word to you, nothing more,” she replied.

[023-159]“And I can say it here.”

[023-160]“No, no. Please do come in. Well, then, if you will not come into the

[023-161]house, come as far as the porch.”

[023-162]Thus pressed she went on to the porch, and they stood together inside

[023-163]it, Fitzpiers closing her umbrella for her.

[023-164]“I have merely a request or petition to make,” she said. “My father’s

[023-165]servant is ill—a woman you know—and her illness is serious.”

[023-166]“I am sorry to hear it. You wish me to come and see her at once?”

[023-167]“No; I particularly wish you not to come.”

[023-168]“Oh, indeed.”

[023-169]“Yes; and she wishes the same. It would make her seriously worse if you

[023-170]were to come. It would almost kill her....My errand is of a peculiar

[023-171]and awkward nature. It is concerning a subject which weighs on her

[023-172]mind—that unfortunate arrangement she made with you, that you might

[023-173]have her body—after death.”

[023-174]“Oh! [[grammer-oliver|Grammer Oliver]], the old woman with the fine head. Seriously ill,

[023-175]is she!”

[023-176]“And _so_ disturbed by her rash compact! I have brought the money

[023-177]back—will you please return to her the agreement she signed?” Grace

[023-178]held out to him a couple of five-pound notes which she had kept ready

[023-179]tucked in her glove.

[023-180]Without replying or considering the notes, Fitzpiers allowed his

[023-181]thoughts to follow his eyes, and dwell upon Grace’s personality, and

[023-182]the sudden close relation in which he stood to her. The porch was

[023-183]narrow; the rain increased. It ran off the porch and dripped on the

[023-184]creepers, and from the creepers upon the edge of Grace’s cloak and

[023-185]skirts.
