# Section 14

[014-001]Meanwhile, in the adjoining room [[giles-winterborne|Giles]] was presiding in a

[014-002]half-unconscious state. He could not get over the initial failures in

[014-003]his scheme for advancing his suit, and hence he did not know that he

[014-004]was eating mouthfuls of bread and nothing else, and continually

[014-005]snuffing the two candles next him till he had reduced them to mere

[014-006]glimmers drowned in their own grease. [[robert-creedle|Creedle]] now appeared with a

[014-007]specially prepared dish, which he served by elevating the little

[014-008]three-legged pot that contained it, and tilting the contents into a

[014-009]dish, exclaiming, simultaneously, “Draw back, gentlemen and ladies,

[014-010]please!”

[014-011]A splash followed. Grace gave a quick, involuntary nod and blink, and

[014-012]put her handkerchief to her face.

[014-013]“Good heavens! what did you do that for, Creedle?” said Giles, sternly,

[014-014]and jumping up.

[014-015]“’Tis how I do it when they baint here, maister,” mildly expostulated

[014-016]Creedle, in an aside audible to all the company.

[014-017]“Well, yes—but—” replied Giles. He went over to Grace, and hoped none

[014-018]of it had gone into her eye.

[014-019]“Oh no,” she said. “Only a sprinkle on my face. It was nothing.”

[014-020]“Kiss it and make it well,” gallantly observed [[farmer-bawtree|Mr. Bawtree]].

[014-021][[grace-melbury|Miss Melbury]] blushed.

[014-022]The [[timber-economy|timber]]-merchant said, quickly, “Oh, it is nothing! She must bear

[014-023]these little mishaps.” But there could be discerned in his face

[014-024]something which said “I ought to have foreseen this.”

[014-025]Giles himself, since the untoward beginning of the feast, had not quite

[014-026]liked to see Grace present. He wished he had not asked such people as

[014-027]Bawtree and the hollow-turner. He had done it, in dearth of other

[014-028]friends, that the room might not appear empty. In his mind’s eye,

[014-029]before the event, they had been the mere background or padding of the

[014-030]scene, but somehow in reality they were the most prominent personages

[014-031]there.

[014-032]After supper they played cards, Bawtree and the hollow-turner

[014-033]monopolizing the new packs for an interminable game, in which a lump of

[014-034]chalk was incessantly used—a game those two always played wherever they

[014-035]were, taking a solitary candle and going to a private table in a corner

[014-036]with the mien of persons bent on weighty matters. The rest of the

[014-037]company on this account were obliged to put up with old packs for their

[014-038]round game, that had been lying by in a drawer ever since the time that

[014-039]Giles’s grandmother was alive. Each card had a great stain in the

[014-040]middle of its back, produced by the touch of generations of damp and

[014-041]excited thumbs now fleshless in the grave; and the kings and queens

[014-042]wore a decayed expression of feature, as if they were rather an

[014-043]impecunious dethroned race of monarchs hiding in obscure slums than

[014-044]real regal characters. Every now and then the comparatively few remarks

[014-045]of the players at the round game were harshly intruded on by the

[014-046]measured jingle of Farmer Bawtree and the hollow-turner from the back

[014-047]of the room:

[014-048]“And I′ will hold′ a wa′-ger with you′

[014-049]That all′ these marks′ are thirt′-y two!”

[014-050]accompanied by rapping strokes with the chalk on the table; then an

[014-051]exclamation, an argument, a dealing of the cards; then the commencement

[014-052]of the rhymes anew.

[014-053]The timber-merchant showed his feelings by talking with a satisfied

[014-054]sense of weight in his words, and by praising the party in a

[014-055]patronizing tone, when Winterborne expressed his fear that he and his

[014-056]were not enjoying themselves.

[014-057]“Oh yes, yes; pretty much. What handsome glasses those are! I didn’t

[014-058]know you had such glasses in the house. Now, Lucy” (to his wife), “you

[014-059]ought to get some like them for ourselves.” And when they had abandoned

[014-060]cards, and Winterborne was talking to [[george-melbury|Melbury]] by the fire, it was the

[014-061]timber-merchant who stood with his back to the mantle in a proprietary

[014-062]attitude, from which post of vantage he critically regarded Giles’s

[014-063]person, rather as a superficies than as a solid with ideas and feelings

[014-064]inside it, saying, “What a splendid coat that one is you have on,

[014-065]Giles! I can’t get such coats. You dress better than I.”

[014-066]After supper there was a dance, the bandsmen from Great [[little-hintock|Hintock]] having

[014-067]arrived some time before. Grace had been away from home so long that

[014-068]she had forgotten the old figures, and hence did not join in the

[014-069]movement. Then Giles felt that all was over. As for her, she was

[014-070]thinking, as she watched the gyrations, of a very different measure

[014-071]that she had been accustomed to tread with a bevy of sylph-like

[014-072]creatures in muslin, in the music-room of a large house, most of whom

[014-073]were now moving in scenes widely removed from this, both as regarded

[014-074]place and character.

[014-075]A woman she did not know came and offered to tell her fortune with the

[014-076]abandoned cards. Grace assented to the proposal, and the woman told her

[014-077]tale unskilfully, for want of practice, as she declared.

[014-078]Mr. Melbury was standing by, and exclaimed, contemptuously, “Tell her

[014-079]fortune, indeed! Her fortune has been told by men of science—what do

[014-080]you call ’em? Phrenologists. You can’t teach her anything new. She’s

[014-081]been too far among the wise ones to be astonished at anything she can

[014-082]hear among us folks in Hintock.”

[014-083]At last the time came for breaking up, Melbury and his family being the

[014-084]earliest to leave, the two card-players still pursuing their game

[014-085]doggedly in the corner, where they had completely covered Giles’s

[014-086]mahogany table with chalk scratches. The three walked home, the

[014-087]distance being short and the night clear.

[014-088]“Well, Giles is a very good fellow,” said Mr. Melbury, as they struck

[014-089]down the lane under boughs which formed a black filigree in which the

[014-090]stars seemed set.

[014-091]“Certainly he is,” said Grace, quickly, and in such a tone as to show

[014-092]that he stood no lower, if no higher, in her regard than he had stood

[014-093]before.

[014-094]When they were opposite an opening through which, by day, the doctor’s

[014-095]house could be seen, they observed a light in one of his rooms,

[014-096]although it was now about two o’clock.

[014-097]“The doctor is not abed yet,” said [[mrs-melbury|Mrs. Melbury]].

[014-098]“Hard study, no doubt,” said her husband.

[014-099]“One would think that, as he seems to have nothing to do about here by

[014-100]day, he could at least afford to go to bed early at night. ’Tis

[014-101]astonishing how little we see of him.”

[014-102]Melbury’s mind seemed to turn with much relief to the contemplation of

[014-103][[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Mr. Fitzpiers]] after the scenes of the evening. “It is natural enough,”

[014-104]he replied. “What can a man of that sort find to interest him in

[014-105]Hintock? I don’t expect he’ll stay here long.”

[014-106]His mind reverted to Giles’s party, and when they were nearly home he

[014-107]spoke again, his daughter being a few steps in advance: “It is hardly

[014-108]the line of life for a girl like Grace, after what she’s been

[014-109]accustomed to. I didn’t foresee that in sending her to boarding-school

[014-110]and letting her travel, and what not, to make her a good bargain for

[014-111]Giles, I should be really spoiling her for him. Ah, ’tis a thousand

[014-112]pities! But he ought to have her—he ought!”

[014-113]At this moment the two exclusive, chalk-mark men, having at last really

[014-114]finished their play, could be heard coming along in the rear,

[014-115]vociferously singing a song to march-time, and keeping vigorous step to

[014-116]the same in far-reaching strides—

[014-117]“She may go, oh!

[014-118]She may go, oh!

[014-119]She may go to the d—— for me!”

[014-120]The timber-merchant turned indignantly to Mrs. Melbury. “That’s the

[014-121]sort of society we’ve been asked to meet,” he said. “For us old folk it

[014-122]didn’t matter; but for Grace—Giles should have known better!”

[014-123]Meanwhile, in the empty house from which the guests had just cleared

[014-124]out, the subject of their discourse was walking from room to room

[014-125]surveying the general displacement of furniture with no ecstatic

[014-126]feeling; rather the reverse, indeed. At last he entered the bakehouse,

[014-127]and found there Robert Creedle sitting over the embers, also lost in

[014-128]contemplation. Winterborne sat down beside him.

[014-129]“Well, Robert, you must be tired. You’d better get on to bed.”

[014-130]“Ay, ay, Giles—what do I call ye? Maister, I would say. But ’tis well

[014-131]to think the day _is_ done, when ’tis done.”

[014-132]Winterborne had abstractedly taken the poker, and with a wrinkled

[014-133]forehead was ploughing abroad the wood-embers on the broad hearth, till

[014-134]it was like a vast scorching Sahara, with red-hot bowlders lying about

[014-135]everywhere. “Do you think it went off well, Creedle?” he asked.

[014-136]“The victuals did; that I know. And the drink did; that I steadfastly

[014-137]believe, from the holler sound of the barrels. Good, honest drink

[014-138]’twere, the headiest mead I ever brewed; and the best wine that berries

[014-139]could rise to; and the briskest Horner-and-Cleeves cider ever wrung

[014-140]down, leaving out the spice and sperrits I put into it, while that

[014-141]egg-flip would ha’ passed through muslin, so little curdled ’twere.

[014-142]’Twas good enough to make any king’s heart merry—ay, to make his whole

[014-143]carcass smile. Still, I don’t deny I’m afeared some things didn’t go

[014-144]well with He and his.” Creedle nodded in a direction which signified

[014-145]where the Melburys lived.

[014-146]“I’m afraid, too, that it was a failure there!”

[014-147]“If so, ’twere doomed to be so. Not but what that snail might as well

[014-148]have come upon anybody else’s plate as hers.”

[014-149]“What snail?”

[014-150]“Well, maister, there was a little one upon the edge of her plate when

[014-151]I brought it out; and so it must have been in her few leaves of

[014-152]wintergreen.”

[014-153]“How the deuce did a snail get there?”

[014-154]“That I don’t know no more than the dead; but there my gentleman was.”

[014-155]“But, Robert, of all places, that was where he shouldn’t have been!”

[014-156]“Well, ’twas his native home, come to that; and where else could we

[014-157]expect him to be? I don’t care who the man is, snails and caterpillars

[014-158]always will lurk in close to the stump of cabbages in that tantalizing

[014-159]way.”

[014-160]“He wasn’t alive, I suppose?” said Giles, with a shudder on Grace’s

[014-161]account.

[014-162]“Oh no. He was well boiled. I warrant him well boiled. God forbid that

[014-163]a _live_ snail should be seed on any plate of victuals that’s served by

[014-164]Robert Creedle....But Lord, there; I don’t mind ’em myself—them small

[014-165]ones, for they were born on cabbage, and they’ve lived on cabbage, so

[014-166]they must be made of cabbage. But she, the close-mouthed little lady,

[014-167]she didn’t say a word about it; though ’twould have made good small

[014-168]conversation as to the nater of such creatures; especially as wit ran

[014-169]short among us sometimes.”

[014-170]“Oh yes—’tis all over!” murmured Giles to himself, shaking his head

[014-171]over the glooming plain of embers, and lining his forehead more than

[014-172]ever. “Do you know, Robert,” he said, “that she’s been accustomed to

[014-173]servants and everything superfine these many years? How, then, could

[014-174]she stand our ways?”

[014-175]“Well, all I can say is, then, that she ought to hob-and-nob elsewhere.

[014-176]They shouldn’t have schooled her so monstrous high, or else bachelor

[014-177]men shouldn’t give randys, or if they do give ’em, only to their own

[014-178]race.”

[014-179]“Perhaps that’s true,” said Winterborne, rising and yawning a sigh.

[014-180]CHAPTER XI.

[014-181]“’Tis a pity—a thousand pities!” her father kept saying next morning at

[014-182]breakfast, Grace being still in her bedroom.

[014-183]But how could he, with any self-respect, obstruct Winterborne’s suit at

[014-184]this stage, and nullify a scheme he had labored to promote—was, indeed,

[014-185]mechanically promoting at this moment? A crisis was approaching, mainly

[014-186]as a result of his contrivances, and it would have to be met.

[014-187]But here was the fact, which could not be disguised: since seeing what

[014-188]an immense change her last twelve months of absence had produced in his

[014-189]daughter, after the heavy sum per annum that he had been spending for

[014-190]several years upon her [[education-and-class-mobility|education]], he was reluctant to let her marry

[014-191]Giles Winterborne, indefinitely occupied as woodsman, cider-merchant,

[014-192]apple-farmer, and what not, even were she willing to marry him herself.
