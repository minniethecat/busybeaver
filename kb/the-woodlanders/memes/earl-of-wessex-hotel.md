# Earl of Wessex Hotel

The Earl of Wessex Hotel is a hotel in Sherton Abbas that appears in Thomas Hardy's novel *The Woodlanders*. It is described as a "grand new" establishment and serves as a setting for several social encounters between the novel's principal characters.

## Location

The hotel is situated in Sherton Abbas. It possesses a courtyard and a balcony, which are referenced in key scenes involving Grace Melbury and Giles Winterborne. The hotel is located near Sheep Street, one of the thoroughfares through which Grace passes on horseback [050-115].

## Key scenes

The hotel appears in a notable memory recounted by the narrator. Grace Melbury recalls a time when she beheld Giles Winterborne "making cider in the court-yard of the Earl of Wessex Hotel, while she was figuring as a fine lady in the balcony above" [050-121]. This recollection underscores the shift in their social standing — Giles at work in the courtyard below, Grace observing from the balcony.

Later in the narrative, Grace reflects that when she had previously taken a meal in a public place, it had been "with Fitzpiers at the grand new Earl of Wessex Hotel in that town, after a two months' roaming and sojourning at the gigantic hotels of the Continent" [051-140]. The description "grand new" suggests the hotel was a recently built establishment, intended to accommodate travellers of some means.

Giles Winterborne, concerned for Grace's wellbeing, invites her to the hotel for refreshment: "Come round to the Earl of Wessex. I'll order a tea there. I did not remember that what was good enough for me was not good enough for you" [051-163]. This offer carries a note of self-consciousness on Giles's part, acknowledging the social distance between them.

## Significance

The Earl of Wessex Hotel represents the modern, commercial world that contrasts with the ancient woodland setting of the Hintocks. It is a place of public dining and social display — a departure from the private, rural spaces where much of the novel's intimate drama unfolds. The hotel's presence in Sherton Abbas marks the town as connected to broader Victorian travel and commerce, while the woodland hamlets remain apart.
