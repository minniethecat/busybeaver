# Section 57

[057-001]Upon the straw within, [[giles-winterborne|Winterborne]] lay in his clothes, just as she had

[057-002]seen him during the whole of her stay here, except that his hat was

[057-003]off, and his hair matted and wild.

[057-004]Both his clothes and the straw were saturated with rain. His arms were

[057-005]flung over his head; his face was flushed to an unnatural crimson. His

[057-006]eyes had a burning brightness, and though they met her own, she

[057-007]perceived that he did not recognize her.

[057-008]“Oh, my Giles,” she cried, “what have I done to you!”

[057-009]But she stopped no longer even to reproach herself. She saw that the

[057-010]first thing to be thought of was to get him indoors.

[057-011]How Grace performed that labor she never could have exactly explained.

[057-012]But by dint of clasping her arms round him, rearing him into a sitting

[057-013]posture, and straining her strength to the uttermost, she put him on

[057-014]one of the hurdles that was loose alongside, and taking the end of it

[057-015]in both her hands, dragged him along the path to the entrance of the

[057-016]hut, and, after a pause for breath, in at the door-way.

[057-017]It was somewhat singular that Giles in his semi-conscious state

[057-018]acquiesced unresistingly in all that she did. But he never for a moment

[057-019]recognized her—continuing his rapid conversation to himself, and

[057-020]seeming to look upon her as some angel, or other supernatural creature

[057-021]of the visionary world in which he was mentally living. The undertaking

[057-022]occupied her more than ten minutes; but by that time, to her great

[057-023]thankfulness, he was in the inner room, lying on the bed, his damp

[057-024]outer clothing removed.

[057-025]Then the unhappy Grace regarded him by the light of the candle. There

[057-026]was something in his look which agonized her, in the rush of his

[057-027]thoughts, accelerating their speed from minute to minute. He seemed to

[057-028]be passing through the universe of ideas like a comet—erratic,

[057-029]inapprehensible, untraceable.

[057-030]Grace’s distraction was almost as great as his. In a few moments she

[057-031]firmly believed he was dying. Unable to withstand her impulse, she

[057-032]knelt down beside him, kissed his hands and his face and his hair,

[057-033]exclaiming, in a low voice, “How could I? How could I?”

[057-034]Her timid morality had, indeed, underrated his chivalry till now,

[057-035]though she knew him so well. The purity of his [[nature-in-the-woodlanders|nature]], his freedom from

[057-036]the grosser passions, his scrupulous delicacy, had never been fully

[057-037]understood by Grace till this strange self-sacrifice in lonely

[057-038]juxtaposition to her own person was revealed. The perception of it

[057-039]added something that was little short of reverence to the deep

[057-040][[love-and-desire|affection]] for him of a woman who, herself, had more of Artemis than of

[057-041]Aphrodite in her constitution.

[057-042]All that a tender nurse could do, Grace did; and the power to express

[057-043]her solicitude in action, unconscious though the sufferer was, brought

[057-044]her mournful satisfaction. She bathed his hot head, wiped his

[057-045]perspiring hands, moistened his lips, cooled his fiery eyelids, sponged

[057-046]his heated skin, and administered whatever she could find in the house

[057-047]that the imagination could conceive as likely to be in any way

[057-048]alleviating. That she might have been the cause, or partially the

[057-049]cause, of all this, interfused misery with her sorrow.

[057-050]Six months before this date a scene, almost similar in its mechanical

[057-051]parts, had been enacted at [[hintock-house|Hintock House]]. It was between a pair of

[057-052]persons most intimately connected in their lives with these. Outwardly

[057-053]like as it had been, it was yet infinite in spiritual difference,

[057-054]though a woman’s devotion had been common to both.

[057-055]Grace rose from her attitude of affection, and, bracing her energies,

[057-056]saw that something practical must immediately be done. Much as she

[057-057]would have liked, in the emotion of the moment, to keep him entirely to

[057-058]herself, medical assistance was necessary while there remained a

[057-059]possibility of preserving him alive. Such assistance was fatal to her

[057-060]own [[secrecy-and-concealment|concealment]]; but even had the [[fate-and-free-will|chance]] of benefiting him been less

[057-061]than it was, she would have run the hazard for his sake. The question

[057-062]was, where should she get a medical man, competent and near?

[057-063]There was one such man, and only one, within accessible distance; a man

[057-064]who, if it were possible to save Winterborne’s life, had the brain most

[057-065]likely to do it. If human pressure could bring him, that man ought to

[057-066]be brought to the sick Giles’s side. The attempt should be made.

[057-067]Yet she dreaded to leave her patient, and the minutes raced past, and

[057-068]yet she postponed her departure. At last, when it was after eleven

[057-069]o’clock, Winterborne fell into a fitful sleep, and it seemed to afford

[057-070]her an opportunity.

[057-071]She hastily made him as comfortable as she could, put on her things,

[057-072]cut a new candle from the bunch hanging in the cupboard, and having set

[057-073]it up, and placed it so that the light did not fall upon his eyes, she

[057-074]closed the door and started.

[057-075]The spirit of Winterborne seemed to keep her company and banish all

[057-076]sense of darkness from her mind. The rains had imparted a

[057-077]phosphorescence to the pieces of touchwood and rotting leaves that lay

[057-078]about her path, which, as scattered by her feet, spread abroad like

[057-079]spilt milk. She would not run the hazard of losing her way by plunging

[057-080]into any short, unfrequented track through the denser parts of the

[057-081]woodland, but followed a more open course, which eventually brought her

[057-082]to the highway. Once here, she ran along with great speed, animated by

[057-083]a devoted purpose which had much about it that was stoical; and it was

[057-084]with scarcely any faltering of spirit that, after an hour’s progress,

[057-085]she passed over [[rubdown-hill|Rubdown Hill]], and onward towards that same [[little-hintock|Hintock]], and

[057-086]that same house, out of which she had fled a few days before in

[057-087]irresistible alarm. But that had happened which, above all other things

[057-088]of chance and change, could make her deliberately frustrate her plan of

[057-089]flight and sink all regard of personal consequences.

[057-090]One speciality of [[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Fitzpiers]]’s was respected by Grace as much as

[057-091]ever—his professional skill. In this she was right. Had his persistence

[057-092]equalled his insight, instead of being the spasmodic and fitful thing

[057-093]it was, fame and fortune need never have remained a wish with him. His

[057-094]freedom from conventional errors and crusted prejudices had, indeed,

[057-095]been such as to retard rather than accelerate his advance in Hintock

[057-096]and its neighborhood, where people could not believe that nature

[057-097]herself effected cures, and that the doctor’s business was only to

[057-098]smooth the way.

[057-099]It was past midnight when Grace arrived opposite her father’s house,

[057-100]now again temporarily occupied by her husband, unless he had already

[057-101]gone away. Ever since her emergence from the denser plantations about

[057-102]Winterborne’s residence a pervasive lightness had hung in the damp

[057-103]autumn sky, in spite of the vault of cloud, signifying that a moon of

[057-104]some age was shining above its arch. The two white gates were distinct,

[057-105]and the white balls on the pillars, and the puddles and damp ruts left

[057-106]by the recent rain, had a cold, corpse-eyed luminousness. She entered

[057-107]by the lower gate, and crossed the quadrangle to the wing wherein the

[057-108]apartments that had been hers since her [[marriage-in-the-woodlanders|marriage]] were situate, till she

[057-109]stood under a window which, if her husband were in the house, gave

[057-110]light to his bedchamber.

[057-111]She faltered, and paused with her hand on her heart, in spite of

[057-112]herself. Could she call to her presence the very cause of all her

[057-113]foregoing troubles? Alas!—old Jones was seven miles off; Giles was

[057-114]possibly dying—what else could she do?

[057-115]It was in a perspiration, wrought even more by consciousness than by

[057-116]exercise, that she picked up some gravel, threw it at the panes, and

[057-117]waited to see the result. The night-bell which had been fixed when

[057-118]Fitzpiers first took up his residence there still remained; but as it

[057-119]had fallen into disuse with the collapse of his practice, and his

[057-120]elopement, she did not venture to pull it now.

[057-121]Whoever slept in the room had heard her signal, slight as it was. In

[057-122]half a minute the window was opened, and a voice said “Yes?”

[057-123]inquiringly. Grace recognized her husband in the speaker at once. Her

[057-124]effort was now to disguise her own accents.

[057-125]“Doctor,” she said, in as unusual a tone as she could command, “a man

[057-126]is dangerously ill in One-chimney Hut, out towards Delborough, and you

[057-127]must go to him at once—in all mercy!”

[057-128]“I will, readily.”

[057-129]The alacrity, surprise, and pleasure expressed in his reply amazed her

[057-130]for a moment. But, in truth, they denoted the sudden relief of a man

[057-131]who, having got back in a mood of contrition, from erratic abandonment

[057-132]to fearful joys, found the soothing routine of professional practice

[057-133]unexpectedly opening anew to him. The highest desire of his soul just

[057-134]now was for a respectable life of painstaking. If this, his first

[057-135]summons since his return, had been to attend upon a cat or dog, he

[057-136]would scarcely have refused it in the circumstances.

[057-137]“Do you know the way?” she asked.

[057-138]“Yes,” said he.

[057-139]“One-chimney Hut,” she repeated. “And—immediately!”

[057-140]“Yes, yes,” said Fitzpiers.

[057-141]Grace remained no longer. She passed out of the white gate without

[057-142]slamming it, and hastened on her way back. Her husband, then, had

[057-143]re-entered her father’s house. How he had been able to effect a

[057-144]reconciliation with the old man, what were the terms of the treaty

[057-145]between them, she could not so much as conjecture. Some sort of truce

[057-146]must have been entered into, that was all she could say. But close as

[057-147]the question lay to her own life, there was a more urgent one which

[057-148]banished it; and she traced her steps quickly along the meandering

[057-149]track-ways.

[057-150]Meanwhile, Fitzpiers was preparing to leave the house. The state of his

[057-151]mind, over and above his professional zeal, was peculiar. At Grace’s

[057-152]first remark he had not recognized or suspected her presence; but as

[057-153]she went on, he was awakened to the great resemblance of the speaker’s

[057-154]voice to his wife’s. He had taken in such good faith the statement of

[057-155]the household on his arrival, that she had gone on a visit for a time

[057-156]because she could not at once bring her mind to be reconciled to him,

[057-157]that he could not quite actually believe this comer to be she. It was

[057-158]one of the features of Fitzpiers’s repentant humor at this date that,

[057-159]on receiving the explanation of her absence, he had made no attempt to

[057-160]outrage her feelings by following her; though nobody had informed him

[057-161]how very shortly her departure had preceded his entry, and of all that

[057-162]might have been inferred from her precipitancy.

[057-163][[george-melbury|Melbury]], after much alarm and consideration, had decided not to follow

[057-164]her either. He sympathized with her flight, much as he deplored it;

[057-165]moreover, the tragic color of the antecedent events that he had been a

[057-166]great means of creating checked his instinct to interfere. He prayed

[057-167]and trusted that she had got into no danger on her way (as he supposed)

[057-168]to [[sherton-abbas|Sherton]], and thence to [[exbury|Exbury]], if that were the place she had gone

[057-169]to, forbearing all inquiry which the strangeness of her departure would

[057-170]have made natural. A few months before this time a performance by Grace

[057-171]of one-tenth the magnitude of this would have aroused him to unwonted

[057-172]investigation.

[057-173]It was in the same spirit that he had tacitly assented to Fitzpiers’s

[057-174]domicilation there. The two men had not met face to face, but Mrs.

[057-175]Melbury had proposed herself as an intermediary, who made the surgeon’s

[057-176]re-entrance comparatively easy to him. Everything was provisional, and

[057-177]nobody asked questions. Fitzpiers had come in the performance of a plan

[057-178]of penitence, which had originated in circumstances hereafter to be

[057-179]explained; his self-[[pride-and-humiliation|humiliation]] to the very bass-string was deliberate;

[057-180]and as soon as a call reached him from the bedside of a dying man his

[057-181]desire was to set to work and do as much good as he could with the

[057-182]least possible fuss or show. He therefore refrained from calling up a

[057-183]stableman to get ready any horse or gig, and set out for One-chimney

[057-184]Hut on foot, as Grace had done.
