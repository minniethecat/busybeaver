# Section 4

[004-001]“I wish for nothing definite. But there’s a lot of things possible for

[004-002]her. Why, [[felice-charmond|Mrs. Charmond]] is wanting some refined young lady, I hear, to

[004-003]go abroad with her—as companion or something of the kind. She’d jump at

[004-004]Grace.”

[004-005]“That’s all uncertain. Better stick to what’s sure.”

[004-006]“True, true,” said [[george-melbury|Melbury]]; “and I hope it will be for the best. Yes,

[004-007]let me get ’em married up as soon as I can, so as to have it over and

[004-008]done with.” He continued looking at the imprint, while he added,

[004-009]“Suppose she should be dying, and never make a track on this path any

[004-010]more?”

[004-011]“She’ll write soon, depend upon’t. Come, ’tis wrong to stay here and

[004-012]brood so.”

[004-013]He admitted it, but said he could not help it. “Whether she write or

[004-014]no, I shall fetch her in a few days.” And thus speaking, he covered the

[004-015]track, and preceded his wife indoors.

[004-016]Melbury, perhaps, was an unlucky man in having within him the sentiment

[004-017]which could indulge in this foolish fondness about the imprint of a

[004-018]daughter’s footstep. Nature does not carry on her government with a

[004-019]view to such feelings, and when advancing years render the open hearts

[004-020]of those who possess them less dexterous than formerly in shutting

[004-021]against the blast, they must suffer “buffeting at will by rain and

[004-022]storm” no less than Little Celandines.

[004-023]But her own existence, and not Mr. Melbury’s, was the centre of [[marty-south|Marty]]’s

[004-024]consciousness, and it was in relation to this that the matter struck

[004-025]her as she slowly withdrew.

[004-026]“That, then, is the [[secrecy-and-concealment|secret]] of it all,” she said. “And [[giles-winterborne|Giles Winterborne]]

[004-027]is not for me, and the less I think of him the better.”

[004-028]She returned to her cottage. The sovereigns were staring at her from

[004-029]the looking-glass as she had left them. With a preoccupied countenance,

[004-030]and with tears in her eyes, she got a pair of scissors, and began

[004-031]mercilessly cutting off the long locks of her hair, arranging and tying

[004-032]them with their points all one way, as the barber had directed. Upon

[004-033]the pale scrubbed deal of the coffin-stool table they stretched like

[004-034]waving and ropy weeds over the washed gravel-bed of a clear stream.

[004-035]She would not turn again to the little looking-glass, out of humanity

[004-036]to herself, knowing what a deflowered visage would look back at her,

[004-037]and almost break her heart; she dreaded it as much as did her own

[004-038]ancestral goddess Sif the reflection in the pool after the rape of her

[004-039]locks by Loke the malicious. She steadily stuck to business, wrapped

[004-040]the hair in a parcel, and sealed it up, after which she raked out the

[004-041]fire and went to bed, having first set up an alarum made of a candle

[004-042]and piece of thread, with a stone attached.

[004-043]But such a reminder was unnecessary to-night. Having tossed till about

[004-044]five o’clock, Marty heard the sparrows walking down their long holes in

[004-045]the thatch above her sloping ceiling to their orifice at the eaves;

[004-046]whereupon she also arose, and descended to the ground-floor again.

[004-047]It was still dark, but she began moving about the house in those

[004-048]automatic initiatory acts and touches which represent among housewives

[004-049]the installation of another day. While thus engaged she heard the

[004-050]rumbling of Mr. Melbury’s wagons, and knew that there, too, the day’s

[004-051]toil had begun.

[004-052]An armful of gads thrown on the still hot embers caused them to blaze

[004-053]up cheerfully and bring her diminished head-gear into sudden prominence

[004-054]as a shadow. At this a step approached the door.

[004-055]“Are folk astir here yet?” inquired a voice she knew well.

[004-056]“Yes, Mr. Winterborne,” said Marty, throwing on a tilt bonnet, which

[004-057]completely hid the recent ravages of the scissors. “Come in!”

[004-058]The door was flung back, and there stepped in upon the mat a man not

[004-059]particularly young for a lover, nor particularly mature for a person of

[004-060]affairs. There was reserve in his glance, and restraint upon his mouth.

[004-061]He carried a horn lantern which hung upon a swivel, and wheeling as it

[004-062]dangled marked grotesque shapes upon the shadier part of the walls.

[004-063]He said that he had looked in on his way down, to tell her that they

[004-064]did not expect her father to make up his contract if he was not well.

[004-065]Mr. Melbury would give him another week, and they would go their

[004-066]journey with a short load that day.

[004-067]“They are done,” said Marty, “and lying in the cart-house.”

[004-068]“Done!” he repeated. “Your father has not been too ill to work after

[004-069]all, then?”

[004-070]She made some evasive reply. “I’ll show you where they be, if you are

[004-071]going down,” she added.

[004-072]They went out and walked together, the pattern of the air-holes in the

[004-073]top of the lantern being thrown upon the mist overhead, where they

[004-074]appeared of giant size, as if reaching the tent-shaped sky. They had no

[004-075]remarks to make to each other, and they uttered none. Hardly anything

[004-076]could be more isolated or more self-contained than the lives of these

[004-077]two walking here in the lonely antelucan hour, when gray shades,

[004-078]material and mental, are so very gray. And yet, looked at in a certain

[004-079]way, their lonely courses formed no detached design at all, but were

[004-080]part of the pattern in the great web of human doings then weaving in

[004-081]both hemispheres, from the White Sea to Cape Horn.

[004-082]The shed was reached, and she pointed out the spars. Winterborne

[004-083]regarded them silently, then looked at her.

[004-084]“Now, Marty, I believe—” he said, and shook his head.

[004-085]“What?”

[004-086]“That you’ve done the work yourself.”

[004-087]“Don’t you tell anybody, will you, Mr. Winterborne?” she pleaded, by

[004-088]way of answer. “Because I am afraid Mr. Melbury may refuse my work if

[004-089]he knows it is mine.”

[004-090]“But how could you learn to do it? ’Tis a trade.”

[004-091]“Trade!” said she. “I’d be bound to learn it in two hours.”

[004-092]“Oh no, you wouldn’t, Mrs. Marty.” Winterborne held down his lantern,

[004-093]and examined the cleanly split hazels as they lay. “Marty,” he said,

[004-094]with dry admiration, “your father with his forty years of practice

[004-095]never made a spar better than that. They are too good for the thatching

[004-096]of houses—they are good enough for the furniture. But I won’t tell. Let

[004-097]me look at your hands—your poor hands!”

[004-098]He had a kindly manner of a quietly severe tone; and when she seemed

[004-099]reluctant to show her hands, he took hold of one and examined it as if

[004-100]it were his own. Her fingers were blistered.

[004-101]“They’ll get harder in time,” she said. “For if father continues ill, I

[004-102]shall have to go on wi’ it. Now I’ll help put ’em up in wagon.”

[004-103]Winterborne without speaking set down his lantern, lifted her as she

[004-104]was about to stoop over the bundles, placed her behind him, and began

[004-105]throwing up the bundles himself. “Rather than you should do it I will,”

[004-106]he said. “But the men will be here directly. Why, Marty!—whatever has

[004-107]happened to your head? Lord, it has shrunk to nothing—it looks an apple

[004-108]upon a gate-post!”

[004-109]Her heart swelled, and she could not speak. At length she managed to

[004-110]groan, looking on the ground, “I’ve made myself ugly—and hateful—that’s

[004-111]what I’ve done!”

[004-112]“No, no,” he answered. “You’ve only cut your hair—I see now.”

[004-113]“Then why must you needs say that about apples and gate-posts?”

[004-114]“Let me see.”

[004-115]“No, no!” She ran off into the gloom of the sluggish dawn. He did not

[004-116]attempt to follow her. When she reached her father’s door she stood on

[004-117]the step and looked back. Mr. Melbury’s men had arrived, and were

[004-118]loading up the spars, and their lanterns appeared from the distance at

[004-119]which she stood to have wan circles round them, like eyes weary with

[004-120]watching. She observed them for a few seconds as they set about

[004-121]harnessing the horses, and then went indoors.

[004-122]CHAPTER IV.

[004-123]There was now a distinct manifestation of morning in the air, and

[004-124]presently the bleared white visage of a sunless winter day emerged like

[004-125]a dead-born child. The villagers everywhere had already bestirred

[004-126]themselves, rising at this time of the year at the far less dreary hour

[004-127]of absolute darkness. It had been above an hour earlier, before a

[004-128]single bird had untucked his head, that twenty lights were struck in as

[004-129]many bedrooms, twenty pairs of shutters opened, and twenty pairs of

[004-130]eyes stretched to the sky to forecast the weather for the day.

[004-131]Owls that had been catching mice in the out-houses, rabbits that had

[004-132]been eating the wintergreens in the gardens, and stoats that had been

[004-133]sucking the blood of the rabbits, discerning that their human neighbors

[004-134]were on the move, discreetly withdrew from publicity, and were seen and

[004-135]heard no more that day.

[004-136]The daylight revealed the whole of Mr. Melbury’s homestead, of which

[004-137]the wagon-sheds had been an outlying erection. It formed three sides of

[004-138]an open quadrangle, and consisted of all sorts of buildings, the

[004-139]largest and central one being the dwelling itself. The fourth side of

[004-140]the quadrangle was the public road.

[004-141]It was a dwelling-house of respectable, roomy, almost dignified aspect;

[004-142]which, taken with the fact that there were the remains of other such

[004-143]buildings thereabout, indicated that [[little-hintock|Little Hintock]] had at some time or

[004-144]other been of greater importance than now, as its old name of Hintock

[004-145]St. Osmond also testified. The house was of no marked antiquity, yet of

[004-146]well-advanced age; older than a stale novelty, but no canonized

[004-147]antique; faded, not hoary; looking at you from the still distinct

[004-148]middle-distance of the early Georgian time, and awakening on that

[004-149]account the instincts of reminiscence more decidedly than the remoter

[004-150]and far grander memorials which have to speak from the misty reaches of

[004-151]mediaevalism. The faces, dress, passions, gratitudes, and revenues of

[004-152]the great-great-grandfathers and grandmothers who had been the first to

[004-153]gaze from those rectangular windows, and had stood under that

[004-154]key-stoned doorway, could be divined and measured by homely standards

[004-155]of to-day. It was a house in whose reverberations queer old personal

[004-156]tales were yet audible if properly listened for; and not, as with those

[004-157]of the castle and cloister, silent beyond the possibility of echo.

[004-158]The garden-front remained much as it had always been, and there was a

[004-159]porch and entrance that way. But the principal house-door opened on the

[004-160]square yard or quadrangle towards the road, formerly a regular carriage

[004-161]entrance, though the middle of the area was now made use of for

[004-162]stacking [[timber-economy|timber]], fagots, bundles, and other products of the wood. It

[004-163]was divided from the lane by a lichen-coated wall, in which hung a pair

[004-164]of gates, flanked by piers out of the perpendicular, with a round white

[004-165]ball on the top of each.

[004-166]The building on the left of the enclosure was a long-backed erection,

[004-167]now used for [[woodland-crafts|spar-making]], sawing, crib-framing, and copse-ware

[004-168]manufacture in general. Opposite were the wagon-sheds where Marty had

[004-169]deposited her spars.

[004-170]Here Winterborne had remained after the girl’s abrupt departure, to see

[004-171]that the wagon-loads were properly made up. Winterborne was connected

[004-172]with the Melbury family in various ways. In addition to the sentimental

[004-173]relationship which arose from his father having been the first Mrs.

[004-174]Melbury’s lover, Winterborne’s aunt had married and emigrated with the

[004-175]brother of the timber-merchant many years before—an alliance that was

[004-176]sufficient to place Winterborne, though the poorer, on a footing of

[004-177]social intimacy with the Melburys. As in most villages so secluded as

[004-178]this, intermarriages were of Hapsburgian frequency among the

[004-179]inhabitants, and there were hardly two houses in Little Hintock

[004-180]unrelated by some matrimonial tie or other.

[004-181]For this reason a curious kind of partnership existed between Melbury

[004-182]and the younger man—a partnership based upon an unwritten code, by

[004-183]which each acted in the way he thought fair towards the other, on a

[004-184]give-and-take principle. Melbury, with his timber and copse-ware

[004-185]business, found that the weight of his labor came in winter and spring.

[004-186]Winterborne was in the apple and cider trade, and his requirements in

[004-187]cartage and other work came in the autumn of each year. Hence horses,

[004-188]wagons, and in some degree men, were handed over to him when the apples

[004-189]began to fall; he, in return, lending his assistance to Melbury in the

[004-190]busiest wood-cutting season, as now.
