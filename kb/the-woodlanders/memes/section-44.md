# Section 44

[044-001]“If we were to cling close together,” said Mrs. Charmond, “we should

[044-002]keep each other warm. But,” she added, in an uneven voice, “I suppose

[044-003]you won’t come near me for the world!”

[044-004]“Why not?”

[044-005]“Because—well, you know.”

[044-006]“Yes. I will—I don’t hate you at all.”

[044-007]They consequently crept up to one another, and being in the dark,

[044-008]lonely and weary, did what neither had dreamed of doing beforehand,

[044-009]clasped each other closely, Mrs. Charmond’s furs consoling Grace’s cold

[044-010]face, and each one’s body as she breathed alternately heaving against

[044-011]that of her companion.

[044-012]When a few minutes had been spent thus, Mrs. Charmond said, “I am so

[044-013]wretched!” in a heavy, emotional whisper.

[044-014]“You are frightened,” said Grace, kindly. “But there is nothing to

[044-015]fear; I know these woods well.”

[044-016]“I am not at all frightened at the wood, but I am at other things.”

[044-017]Mrs. Charmond embraced Grace more and more tightly, and the younger

[044-018]woman could feel her [[community-and-kinship|neighbor]]’s breathings grow deeper and more

[044-019]spasmodic, as though uncontrollable feelings were germinating.

[044-020]“After I had left you,” she went on, “I regretted something I had said.

[044-021]I have to make a confession—I must make it!” she whispered, brokenly,

[044-022]the instinct to indulge in warmth of sentiment which had led this woman

[044-023]of passions to respond to [[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Fitzpiers]] in the first place leading her now

[044-024]to find luxurious comfort in opening her heart to his wife. “I said to

[044-025]you I could give him up without pain or deprivation—that he had only

[044-026]been my pastime. That was untrue—it was said to deceive you. I could

[044-027]not do it without much pain; and, what is more dreadful, I _cannot_

[044-028]give him up—even if I would—of myself alone.”

[044-029]“Why? Because you [[love-and-desire|love]] him, you mean.”

[044-030][[felice-charmond|Felice Charmond]] denoted assent by a movement.

[044-031]“I knew I was right!” said Grace, exaltedly. “But that should not deter

[044-032]you,” she presently added, in a moral tone. “Oh, do struggle against

[044-033]it, and you will conquer!”

[044-034]“You are so simple, so simple!” cried Felice. “You think, because you

[044-035]guessed my assumed indifference to him to be a sham, that you know the

[044-036]extremes that people are capable of going to! But a good deal more may

[044-037]have been going on than you have fathomed with all your insight. I

[044-038]_cannot_ give him up until he chooses to give up me.”

[044-039]“But surely you are the superior in station and in every way, and the

[044-040]cut must come from you.”

[044-041]“Tchut! Must I tell verbatim, you simple child? Oh, I suppose I must! I

[044-042]shall eat away my heart if I do not let out all, after meeting you like

[044-043]this and finding how guileless you are.” She thereupon whispered a few

[044-044]words in the girl’s ear, and burst into a violent fit of sobbing.

[044-045]Grace started roughly away from the shelter of the fur, and sprang to

[044-046]her feet.

[044-047]“Oh, my God!” she exclaimed, thunderstruck at a revelation transcending

[044-048]her utmost suspicion. “Can it be—can it be!”

[044-049]She turned as if to hasten away. But Felice Charmond’s sobs came to her

[044-050]ear: deep darkness circled her about, the funereal [[nature-in-the-woodlanders|trees]] rocked and

[044-051]chanted their diriges and placebos around her, and she did not know

[044-052]which way to go. After a moment of energy she felt mild again, and

[044-053]turned to the motionless woman at her feet.

[044-054]“Are you rested?” she asked, in what seemed something like her own

[044-055]voice grown ten years older.

[044-056]Without an answer Mrs. Charmond slowly rose.

[044-057]“You mean to betray me!” she said from the bitterest depths of her

[044-058]soul. “Oh fool, fool I!”

[044-059]“No,” said Grace, shortly. “I mean no such thing. But let us be quick

[044-060]now. We have a serious undertaking before us. Think of nothing but

[044-061]going straight on.”

[044-062]They walked on in profound silence, pulling back boughs now growing

[044-063]wet, and treading down woodbine, but still keeping a pretty straight

[044-064]course. Grace began to be thoroughly worn out, and her companion too,

[044-065]when, on a sudden, they broke into the deserted highway at the hill-top

[044-066]on which the [[sherton-abbas|Sherton]] man had waited for [[mrs-dollery|Mrs. Dollery]]’s van. Grace

[044-067]recognized the spot as soon as she looked around her.

[044-068]“How we have got here I cannot tell,” she said, with cold civility. “We

[044-069]have made a complete circuit of [[little-hintock|Little Hintock]]. The hazel copse is

[044-070]quite on the other side. Now we have only to follow the road.”

[044-071]They dragged themselves onward, turned into the lane, passed the track

[044-072]to Little Hintock, and so reached the park.

[044-073]“Here I turn back,” said Grace, in the same passionless voice. “You are

[044-074]quite near home.”

[044-075]Mrs. Charmond stood inert, seeming appalled by her late admission.

[044-076]“I have told you something in a moment of irresistible desire to

[044-077]unburden my soul which all but a fool would have kept silent as the

[044-078]grave,” she said. “I cannot help it now. Is it to be a [[secrecy-and-concealment|secret]]—or do you

[044-079]mean war?”

[044-080]“A secret, certainly,” said Grace, mournfully. “How can you expect war

[044-081]from such a helpless, wretched being as I!”

[044-082]“And I’ll do my best not to see him. I am his slave; but I’ll try.”

[044-083]Grace was naturally kind; but she could not help using a small dagger

[044-084]now.

[044-085]“Pray don’t distress yourself,” she said, with exquisitely fine scorn.

[044-086]“You may keep him—for me.” Had she been wounded instead of mortified

[044-087]she could not have used the words; but Fitzpiers’s hold upon her heart

[044-088]was slight.

[044-089]They parted thus and there, and Grace went moodily homeward. Passing

[044-090]Marty’s cottage she observed through the window that the girl was

[044-091]writing instead of chopping as usual, and wondered what her

[044-092]correspondence could be. Directly afterwards she met people in search

[044-093]of her, and reached the house to find all in serious alarm. She soon

[044-094]explained that she had lost her way, and her general depression was

[044-095]attributed to exhaustion on that account.

[044-096]Could she have known what Marty was writing she would have been

[044-097]surprised.

[044-098]The rumor which agitated the other folk of Hintock had reached the

[044-099]young girl, and she was penning a letter to Fitzpiers, to tell him that

[044-100]Mrs. Charmond wore her hair. It was poor Marty’s only card, and she

[044-101]played it, knowing nothing of fashion, and thinking her revelation a

[044-102]fatal one for a lover.

[044-103]CHAPTER XXXIV.

[044-104]It was at the beginning of April, a few days after the meeting between

[044-105]Grace and Mrs. Charmond in the wood, that Fitzpiers, just returned from

[044-106][[london-in-the-woodlanders|London]], was travelling from Sherton-Abbas to Hintock in a hired

[044-107]carriage. In his eye there was a doubtful light, and the lines of his

[044-108]refined face showed a vague disquietude. He appeared now like one of

[044-109]those who impress the beholder as having suffered wrong in being born.

[044-110]His position was in truth gloomy, and to his appreciative mind it

[044-111]seemed even gloomier than it was. His practice had been slowly

[044-112]dwindling of late, and now threatened to die out altogether, the

[044-113]irrepressible [[old-jones|old Dr. Jones]] capturing patients up to Fitzpiers’s very

[044-114]door. Fitzpiers knew only too well the latest and greatest cause of his

[044-115]unpopularity; and yet, so illogical is man, the second branch of his

[044-116]sadness grew out of a remedial measure proposed for the first—a letter

[044-117]from Felice Charmond imploring him not to see her again. To bring about

[044-118]their severance still more effectually, she added, she had decided

[044-119]during his absence upon almost immediate departure for the Continent.

[044-120]The time was that dull interval in a woodlander’s life which coincides

[044-121]with great activity in the life of the woodland itself—a period

[044-122]following the close of the winter tree-cutting, and preceding the

[044-123][[timber-economy|barking]] season, when the saps are just beginning to heave with the

[044-124]force of hydraulic lifts inside all the trunks of [[the-woodland-tracts|the forest]].

[044-125][[giles-winterborne|Winterborne]]’s contract was completed, and the plantations were

[044-126]deserted. It was dusk; there were no leaves as yet; the nightingales

[044-127]would not begin to sing for a fortnight; and “the Mother of the Months”

[044-128]was in her most attenuated phase—starved and bent to a mere bowed

[044-129]skeleton, which glided along behind the bare twigs in Fitzpiers’s

[044-130]company.

[044-131]When he reached home he went straight up to his wife’s sitting-room. He

[044-132]found it deserted, and without a fire. He had mentioned no day for his

[044-133]return; nevertheless, he wondered why she was not there waiting to

[044-134]receive him. On descending to the other wing of the house and inquiring

[044-135]of [[mrs-melbury|Mrs. Melbury]], he learned with much surprise that Grace had gone on a

[044-136]visit to an acquaintance at [[shottsford-forum|Shottsford-Forum]] three days earlier; that

[044-137]tidings had on this morning reached her father of her being very unwell

[044-138]there, in consequence of which he had ridden over to see her.

[044-139]Fitzpiers went up-stairs again, and the little drawing-room, now

[044-140]lighted by a solitary candle, was not rendered more cheerful by the

[044-141]entrance of [[grammer-oliver|Grammer Oliver]] with an apronful of wood, which she threw on

[044-142]the hearth while she raked out the grate and rattled about the

[044-143]fire-irons, with a view to making things comfortable. Fitzpiers

[044-144]considered that Grace ought to have let him know her plans more

[044-145]accurately before leaving home in a freak like this. He went

[044-146]desultorily to the window, the blind of which had not been pulled down,

[044-147]and looked out at the thin, fast-sinking moon, and at the tall stalk of

[044-148]smoke rising from the top of [[suke-damson|Suke Damson]]’s chimney, signifying that the

[044-149]young woman had just lit her fire to prepare supper.

[044-150]He became conscious of a discussion in progress on the opposite side of

[044-151]the court. Somebody had looked over the wall to talk to the sawyers,

[044-152]and was telling them in a loud voice news in which the name of Mrs.

[044-153]Charmond soon arrested his ears.

[044-154]“Grammer, don’t make so much noise with that grate,” said the surgeon;

[044-155]at which Grammer reared herself upon her knees and held the fuel

[044-156]suspended in her hand, while Fitzpiers half opened the casement.

[044-157]“She is off to foreign lands again at last—hev made up her mind quite

[044-158]sudden-like—and it is thoughted she’ll leave in a day or two. She’s

[044-159]been all as if her mind were low for some days past—with a sort of

[044-160]sorrow in her face, as if she reproached her own soul. She’s the wrong

[044-161]sort of woman for Hintock—hardly knowing a beech from a woak—that I

[044-162]own. But I don’t care who the man is, she’s been a very kind friend to

[044-163]me.

[044-164]“Well, the day after to-morrow is the Sabbath day, and without charity

[044-165]we are but tinkling simples; but this I do say, that her going will be

[044-166]a blessed thing for a certain married couple who remain.”

[044-167]The fire was lighted, and Fitzpiers sat down in front of it, restless

[044-168]as the last leaf upon a tree. “A sort of sorrow in her face, as if she

[044-169]reproached her own soul.” Poor Felice. How Felice’s frame must be

[044-170]pulsing under the conditions of which he had just heard the caricature;

[044-171]how her fair temples must ache; what a mood of wretchedness she must be

[044-172]in! But for the mixing up of his name with hers, and her determination

[044-173]to sunder their too close acquaintance on that account, she would

[044-174]probably have sent for him professionally. She was now sitting alone,

[044-175]suffering, perhaps wishing that she had not forbidden him to come

[044-176]again.

[044-177]Unable to remain in this lonely room any longer, or to wait for the

[044-178]meal which was in course of preparation, he made himself ready for

[044-179]riding, descended to the yard, stood by the stable-door while Darling

[044-180]was being saddled, and rode off down the lane. He would have preferred

[044-181]walking, but was weary with his day’s travel.

[044-182]As he approached the door of [[marty-south|Marty South]]’s cottage, which it was

[044-183]necessary to pass on his way, she came from the porch as if she had

[044-184]been awaiting him, and met him in the middle of the road, holding up a

[044-185]letter. Fitzpiers took it without stopping, and asked over his shoulder

[044-186]from whom it came.
