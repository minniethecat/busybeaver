# Section 65

[065-001]“Don’t be grieved about me—don’t, dear Edgar!” she exclaimed, rushing

[065-002]up and bending over him. “I am not hurt a bit! I was coming on to find

[065-003]you after I had released myself, but I heard footsteps; and I hid away,

[065-004]because I was without some of my clothing, and I did not know who the

[065-005]person might be.”

[065-006][[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Fitzpiers]] had sprung to his feet, and his next act was no less

[065-007]unpremeditated by him than it was irresistible by her, and would have

[065-008]been so by any woman not of Amazonian strength. He clasped his arms

[065-009]completely round, pressed her to his breast, and kissed her

[065-010]passionately.

[065-011]“You are not dead!—you are not hurt! Thank God—thank God!” he said,

[065-012]almost sobbing in his delight and relief from the horror of his

[065-013]apprehension. “Grace, my wife, my [[love-and-desire|love]], how is this—what has happened?”

[065-014]“I was coming on to you,” she said as distinctly as she could in the

[065-015]half-smothered state of her face against his. “I was trying to be as

[065-016]punctual as possible, and as I had started a minute late I ran along

[065-017]the path very swiftly—fortunately for myself. Just when I had passed

[065-018]between these [[nature-in-the-woodlanders|trees]] I felt something clutch at my dress from behind

[065-019]with a noise, and the next moment I was pulled backward by it, and fell

[065-020]to the ground. I screamed with terror, thinking it was a man lying down

[065-021]there to murder me, but the next moment I discovered it was iron, and

[065-022]that my clothes were caught in a trap. I pulled this way and that, but

[065-023]the thing would not let go, drag it as I would, and I did not know what

[065-024]to do. I did not want to alarm my father or anybody, as I wished nobody

[065-025]to know of these meetings with you; so I could think of no other plan

[065-026]than slipping off my skirt, meaning to run on and tell you what a

[065-027]strange accident had happened to me. But when I had just freed myself

[065-028]by leaving the dress behind, I heard steps, and not being sure it was

[065-029]you, I did not like to be seen in such a pickle, so I hid away.”

[065-030]“It was only your speed that saved you! One or both of your legs would

[065-031]have been broken if you had come at ordinary walking pace.”

[065-032]“Or yours, if you had got here first,” said she, beginning to realize

[065-033]the whole ghastliness of the possibility. “Oh, Edgar, there has been an

[065-034]Eye watching over us to-night, and we should be thankful indeed!”

[065-035]He continued to press his face to hers. “You are mine—mine again now.”

[065-036]She gently owned that she supposed she was. “I heard what you said when

[065-037]you thought I was injured,” she went on, shyly, “and I know that a man

[065-038]who could suffer as you were suffering must have a tender regard for

[065-039]me. But how does this awful thing come here?”

[065-040]“I suppose it has something to do with poachers.” Fitzpiers was still

[065-041]so shaken by the sense of her danger that he was obliged to sit awhile,

[065-042]and it was not until Grace said, “If I could only get my skirt out

[065-043]nobody would know anything about it,” that he bestirred himself.

[065-044]By their united efforts, each standing on one of the springs of the

[065-045]trap, they pressed them down sufficiently to insert across the jaws a

[065-046]billet which they dragged from a faggot near at hand; and it was then

[065-047]possible to extract the silk mouthful from the monster’s bite, creased

[065-048]and pierced with many holes, but not torn. Fitzpiers assisted her to

[065-049]put it on again; and when her customary contours were thus restored

[065-050]they walked on together, Grace taking his arm, till he effected an

[065-051]improvement by clasping it round her waist.

[065-052]The ice having been broken in this unexpected manner, she made no

[065-053]further attempt at reserve. “I would ask you to come into the house,”

[065-054]she said, “but my meetings with you have been kept [[secrecy-and-concealment|secret]] from my

[065-055]father, and I should like to prepare him.”

[065-056]“Never mind, dearest. I could not very well have accepted the

[065-057]invitation. I shall never live here again—as much for your sake as for

[065-058]mine. I have news to tell you on this very point, but my alarm had put

[065-059]it out of my head. I have bought a practice, or rather a partnership,

[065-060]in the Midlands, and I must go there in a week to take up permanent

[065-061]residence. My poor old great-aunt died about eight months ago, and left

[065-062]me enough to do this. I have taken a little furnished house for a time,

[065-063]till we can get one of our own.”

[065-064]He described the place, and the surroundings, and the view from the

[065-065]windows, and Grace became much interested. “But why are you not there

[065-066]now?” she said.

[065-067]“Because I cannot tear myself away from here till I have your promise.

[065-068]Now, darling, you will accompany me there—will you not? To-night has

[065-069]settled that.”

[065-070]Grace’s tremblings had gone off, and she did not say nay. They went on

[065-071]together.

[065-072]The adventure, and the emotions consequent upon the reunion which that

[065-073]event had forced on, combined to render Grace oblivious of the

[065-074]direction of their desultory ramble, till she noticed they were in an

[065-075]encircled glade in the densest part of the wood, whereon the moon, that

[065-076]had imperceptibly added its rays to the scene, shone almost vertically.

[065-077]It was an exceptionally soft, balmy evening for the time of year, which

[065-078]was just that transient period in the May month when beech-trees have

[065-079]suddenly unfolded large limp young leaves of the softness of

[065-080]butterflies’ wings. Boughs bearing such leaves hung low around, and

[065-081]completely enclosed them, so that it was as if they were in a great

[065-082]green vase, which had moss for its bottom and leaf sides.

[065-083]The clouds having been packed in the west that evening so as to retain

[065-084]the departing glare a long while, the hour had seemed much earlier than

[065-085]it was. But suddenly the question of time occurred to her.

[065-086]“I must go back,” she said; and without further delay they set their

[065-087]faces towards [[little-hintock|Hintock]]. As they walked he examined his watch by the aid

[065-088]of the now strong moonlight.

[065-089]“By the gods, I think I have lost my train!” said Fitzpiers.

[065-090]“Dear me—whereabouts are we?” said she.

[065-091]“Two miles in the direction of [[sherton-abbas|Sherton]].”

[065-092]“Then do you hasten on, Edgar. I am not in the least afraid. I

[065-093]recognize now the part of the wood we are in and I can find my way back

[065-094]quite easily. I’ll tell my father that we have made it up. I wish I had

[065-095]not kept our meetings so private, for it may vex him a little to know I

[065-096]have been seeing you. He is getting old and irritable, that was why I

[065-097]did not. Good-by.”

[065-098]“But, as I must stay at the Earl of [[wessex-in-the-woodlanders|Wessex]] to-night, for I cannot

[065-099]possibly catch the train, I think it would be safer for you to let me

[065-100]take care of you.”

[065-101]“But what will my father think has become of me? He does not know in

[065-102]the least where I am—he thinks I only went into the garden for a few

[065-103]minutes.”

[065-104]“He will surely guess—somebody has seen me for certain. I’ll go all the

[065-105]way back with you to-morrow.”

[065-106]“But that newly done-up place—the Earl of Wessex!”

[065-107]“If you are so very particular about the publicity I will stay at the

[065-108]Three Tuns.”

[065-109]“Oh no—it is not that I am particular—but I haven’t a brush or comb or

[065-110]anything!”

[065-111]CHAPTER XLVIII.

[065-112]All the evening [[george-melbury|Melbury]] had been coming to his door, saying, “I wonder

[065-113]where in the world that girl is! Never in all my born days did I know

[065-114]her bide out like this! She surely said she was going into the garden

[065-115]to get some parsley.”

[065-116]Melbury searched the garden, the parsley-bed, and the [[orchard-culture|orchard]], but

[065-117]could find no trace of her, and then he made inquiries at the cottages

[065-118]of such of his workmen as had not gone to bed, avoiding Tangs’s because

[065-119]he knew the young people were to rise early to leave. In these

[065-120]inquiries one of the men’s wives somewhat incautiously let out the fact

[065-121]that she had heard a scream in the wood, though from which direction

[065-122]she could not say.

[065-123]This set Melbury’s fears on end. He told the men to light lanterns, and

[065-124]headed by himself they started, [[robert-creedle|Creedle]] following at the last moment

[065-125]with quite a burden of grapnels and ropes, which he could not be

[065-126]persuaded to leave behind, and the company being joined by the

[065-127]hollow-turner and the man who kept the cider-house as they went along.

[065-128]They explored the precincts of the village, and in a short time lighted

[065-129]upon the [[the-man-trap-incident|man-trap]]. Its discovery simply added an item of fact without

[065-130]helping their conjectures; but Melbury’s indefinite alarm was greatly

[065-131]increased when, holding a candle to the ground, he saw in the teeth of

[065-132]the instrument some frayings from Grace’s clothing. No intelligence of

[065-133]any kind was gained till they met a woodman of Delborough, who said

[065-134]that he had seen a lady answering to the description her father gave of

[065-135]Grace, walking through the wood on a gentleman’s arm in the direction

[065-136]of Sherton.

[065-137]“Was he clutching her tight?” said Melbury.

[065-138]“Well—rather,” said the man.

[065-139]“Did she walk lame?”

[065-140]“Well, ’tis true her head hung over towards him a bit.”

[065-141]Creedle groaned tragically.

[065-142]Melbury, not suspecting the presence of Fitzpiers, coupled this account

[065-143]with the man-trap and the scream; he could not understand what it all

[065-144]meant; but the sinister event of the trap made him follow on.

[065-145]Accordingly, they bore away towards the town, shouting as they went,

[065-146]and in due course emerged upon the highway.

[065-147]Nearing Sherton-Abbas, the previous information was confirmed by other

[065-148]strollers, though the gentleman’s supporting arm had disappeared from

[065-149]these later accounts. At last they were so near Sherton that Melbury

[065-150]informed his faithful followers that he did not wish to drag them

[065-151]farther at so late an hour, since he could go on alone and inquire if

[065-152]the woman who had been seen were really Grace. But they would not leave

[065-153]him alone in his anxiety, and trudged onward till the lamplight from

[065-154]the town began to illuminate their fronts. At the entrance to the High

[065-155]Street they got fresh scent of the pursued, but coupled with the new

[065-156]condition that the lady in the costume described had been going up the

[065-157]street alone.

[065-158]“Faith!—I believe she’s mesmerized, or walking in her sleep,” said

[065-159]Melbury.

[065-160]However, the identity of this woman with Grace was by no means certain;

[065-161]but they plodded along the street. Percombe, the hair-dresser, who had

[065-162]despoiled [[marty-south|Marty]] of her tresses, was standing at his door, and they duly

[065-163]put inquiries to him.

[065-164]“Ah—how’s Little Hintock folk by now?” he said, before replying. “Never

[065-165]have I been over there since one winter night some three year ago—and

[065-166]then I lost myself finding it. How can ye live in such a one-eyed

[065-167]place? [[great-hintock|Great Hintock]] is bad enough—hut Little Hintock—the bats and owls

[065-168]would drive me melancholy-mad! It took two days to raise my sperrits to

[065-169]their true pitch again after that night I went there. Mr. Melbury, sir,

[065-170]as a man’s that put by money, why not retire and live here, and see

[065-171]something of the world?”

[065-172]The responses at last given by him to their queries guided them to the

[065-173]building that offered the best accommodation in Sherton—having been

[065-174]enlarged contemporaneously with the construction of the railway—namely,

[065-175]the [[earl-of-wessex-hotel|Earl of Wessex Hotel]].

[065-176]Leaving the others without, Melbury made prompt inquiry here. His alarm

[065-177]was lessened, though his perplexity was increased, when he received a

[065-178]brief reply that such a lady was in the house.

[065-179]“Do you know if it is my daughter?” asked Melbury.
