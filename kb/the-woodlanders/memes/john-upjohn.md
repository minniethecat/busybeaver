# John Upjohn

John Upjohn is a hollow-turner of Little Hintock — a craftsman who shapes wooden vessels on a lathe — and one of the settled old men of the parish whose conversation and memory provide the novel with its texture of local knowledge. He appears at the opening, working alongside the other woodlanders in the winter clearing, and returns memorably in the novel's closing chapters as a living archive of Hintock's romantic history, asked to recount "the chronicle" of local courtships for the edification — and perhaps the instruction — of the assembled company.

## 身份与背景

Like Timothy Tangs, Upjohn is a hollow-turner — a skilled woodland craft requiring a lathe and years of apprenticeship [005-016]. He is of the generation that remembers the old ways of the district, and his name ("Upjohn") has the same Saxon provenance as Tangs and Creedle, placing him among the indigenous population of Little Hintock rather than the incomers who arrive as the novel progresses. His friendship with Grammer Oliver — he greets her with the warm observation that "it do do my heart good to see ye" [005-071] — establishes him early as a figure rooted in the community's affective bonds, not merely its economic ones.

## 性格与特征

Upjohn's defining trait is his role as a talker and rememberer. When Fitzpiers introduces himself with the exotic claim of being "an Italianized American, a South Carolinian by birth," it is Upjohn who provides the dry local commentary on what such a man costs "what she costs" — a cryptic but pointed observation about the expense of novelty [028-149]. He is not disconcerted by sceptical questions about his tales [066-120], and his willingness to hold forth on the romantic history of the district — "Always a good woman... always a lonely maid" [066-113] [066-153] — shows him to be a natural storyteller, the kind of figure on whom oral cultures depend for their continuity.

## 关键关系

- **[[Grammer Oliver|Grammer Oliver]]**：Fellow servant-class neighbour and friend; their exchanges in the early chapters establish the network of familiar voices that constitute the community's baseline against which the principal characters' dramas are played out [005-071].
- **[[Giles Winterborne|Giles Winterborne]]**：Neighbour and, tangentially, part of the circle of working men whose opinions and gossip form the novel's chorus. Upjohn is asked to adjust Giles's horse's girths before a journey [045-061], a small detail that places him in the fabric of everyday Hintock life.

## 叙事线索

Upjohn appears in the novel's opening tableau — "Upjohn, engaged in the hollow-turner's trade" [005-016] — establishing his place in the working world. He reappears in the middle chapters in the context of Fitzpiers's arrival and the curiosity it stirs [028-149], and then most prominently in the novel's final pages (Chapter 48), where he is called upon to recount the history of courtships in the district — "Tell us the chronicle of 'em, Mr. Upjohn" — and delivers a reflective assessment of the women of Hintock: "Always a good woman... always a lonely maid" [066-134] [066-153]. This closing appearance, coming after the deaths of Giles Winterborne and the resolution of Grace's marital crisis, serves as a kind of communal epitaph: the old man, speaking for the old world, remembering what has been lost.

## 意义与解读

Upjohn exemplifies a Hardy character type: the rural elder whose speech preserves the collective memory of the community. His role as "chronicler" in the novel's closing chapter positions him as an alternative to the educated narrator — where the novel's third-person voice offers psychological depth and literary allusion, Upjohn offers the plain truth of local experience. The fact that he is asked to speak at all, and that the assembled company listens, suggests that even in a world increasingly shaped by Fitzpiers's cosmopolitan medicine and Grace's metropolitan education, the authority of the old oral witness retains its pull. His final pronouncement — "always a lonely maid" — carries a melancholy that is the novel's own.
