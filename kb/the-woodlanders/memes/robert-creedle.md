# Robert Creedle

Robert Creedle is an old workman employed by Giles Winterborne in the woodland operations around Little Hintock. A garrulous, weather-beaten figure of the old Wessex labouring class, Creedle appears throughout the novel's middle chapters as a steady presence in Winterborne's enterprise — splitting wood, pressing cider, and offering his unfiltered opinions on all matters. His loyalty to Giles, even after the latter's financial ruin, makes him a representative of the traditional bond between master and man in the rural economy.

## 身份与背景

Creedle is described on his first appearance as "an old man who worked for Winterborne" [005-019]. His weathered appearance suggests a lifetime of outdoor labour — Hardy notes that he carried about him the air of "a weather-beaten sailor," though his sea was the woodlands of Wessex [005-028]. He lives in or near Little Hintock and appears to have no family of his own, his life entirely absorbed into the rhythms of Winterborne's timber enterprise. His skill set encompasses the full range of woodland crafts: spar-making, timber-hauling, and — most notably — the operation of the cider-press [037-085].

## 性格与特征

Creedle is defined by his irrepressible loquacity. He offers opinions freely, grieves openly, and never hesitates to speak his mind — even when it would be wiser to hold his tongue. When scolded by Giles for gossiping about Grace Melbury's father, Creedle throws "grieved remembrance into his tone" [005-057], more hurt by the reprimand than the substance of it. His speech is thick with West Country dialect and folk wisdom, marking him as a character rooted in oral tradition rather than written learning. His loyalty to Giles is absolute: when Giles falls into financial difficulty, Creedle remains with him, his presence a constant marker of the older bonds of personal fealty that the novel contrasts with the contractual relationships of the modern economy.

## 叙事线索

Creedle first appears at the opening of the novel, working alongside Giles and the two Timothys in the woodland clearing [005-019]. His presence in the early chapters establishes the working world of Little Hintock as a community bound by shared labour and oral culture. He features in key scenes of the novel's middle section: warming himself by the embers when Giles returns home [014-127], sharing in the collective response to Fitzpiers's arrival and the disruptions it brings to the local order [014-164], and — perhaps most memorably — conducting the cider-making operation, where his skill transforms the orchard's yield into the drink that fuels the community's social life [037-085].

In the novel's later stages, Creedle remains a fixture of Winterborne's diminished enterprise, his continued presence a testament to the loyalty that survives economic misfortune. When Melbury observes that "Robert Creedle will be nailed up in parish boards," it is a grim prognosis of the old workman's fate in a world where the traditional bonds of patronage are eroding [059-055].

## 意义与解读

Creedle is a chorus figure in the novel — commenting, reacting, and embodying the collective voice of the Hintock working community. His volubility serves a structural purpose: where other characters (Giles, Marty) are taciturn by nature, Creedle speaks the subtext aloud, articulating the community's responses to events. Hardy uses him to anchor the novel's social world in the specific textures of rural labour: the click of the billhook, the creak of the cider-press, the gossip around the evening fire. In an increasingly monetized social order, Creedle represents a vanishing type — the lifelong retainer whose bond to his master is not a contract but a way of life.
