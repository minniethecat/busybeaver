# Section 32

[032-001]In the yard, between Grace and the orchards, there progressed a scene

[032-002]natural to the locality at this time of the year. An apple-mill and

[032-003]press had been erected on the spot, to which some men were bringing

[032-004]fruit from divers points in mawn-baskets, while others were grinding

[032-005]them, and others wringing down the pomace, whose sweet juice gushed

[032-006]forth into tubs and pails. The superintendent of these proceedings, to

[032-007]whom the others spoke as master, was a young yeoman of prepossessing

[032-008]manner and aspect, whose form she recognized in a moment. He had hung

[032-009]his coat to a nail of the out-house wall, and wore his shirt-sleeves

[032-010]rolled up beyond his elbows, to keep them unstained while he rammed the

[032-011]pomace into the bags of horse-hair. Fragments of apple-rind had

[032-012]alighted upon the brim of his hat—probably from the bursting of a

[032-013]bag—while brown pips of the same fruit were sticking among the down

[032-014]upon his fine, round arms.

[032-015]She realized in a moment how he had come there. Down in the heart of

[032-016]the apple country nearly every farmer kept up a [[orchard-culture|cider-making]] apparatus

[032-017]and wring-house for his own use, building up the pomace in great straw

[032-018]“cheeses,” as they were called; but here, on the margin of Pomona’s

[032-019]plain, was a debatable land neither orchard nor sylvan exclusively,

[032-020]where the apple produce was hardly sufficient to warrant each

[032-021]proprietor in keeping a mill of his own. This was the field of the

[032-022]travelling cider-maker. His press and mill were fixed to wheels instead

[032-023]of being set up in a cider-house; and with a couple of horses, buckets,

[032-024]tubs, strainers, and an assistant or two, he wandered from place to

[032-025]place, deriving very satisfactory returns for his trouble in such a

[032-026]prolific season as the present.

[032-027]The back parts of the town were just now abounding with

[032-028]apple-gatherings. They stood in the yards in carts, baskets, and loose

[032-029]heaps; and the blue, stagnant air of autumn which hung over everything

[032-030]was heavy with a sweet cidery smell. Cakes of pomace lay against the

[032-031]walls in the yellow sun, where they were drying to be used as fuel. Yet

[032-032]it was not the great make of the year as yet; before the standard crop

[032-033]came in there accumulated, in abundant times like this, a large

[032-034]superfluity of early apples, and windfalls from the [[nature-in-the-woodlanders|trees]] of later

[032-035]harvest, which would not keep long. Thus, in the baskets, and quivering

[032-036]in the hopper of the mill, she saw specimens of mixed dates, including

[032-037]the mellow countenances of streaked-jacks, codlins, costards,

[032-038]stubbards, ratheripes, and other well-known friends of her ravenous

[032-039]youth.

[032-040]Grace watched the head-man with interest. The slightest sigh escaped

[032-041]her. Perhaps she thought of the day—not so far distant—when that friend

[032-042]of her childhood had met her by her father’s arrangement in this same

[032-043]town, warm with hope, though diffident, and trusting in a promise

[032-044]rather implied than given. Or she might have thought of days earlier

[032-045]yet—days of childhood—when her mouth was somewhat more ready to receive

[032-046]a kiss from his than was his to bestow one. However, all that was over.

[032-047]She had felt superior to him then, and she felt superior to him now.

[032-048]She wondered why he never looked towards her open window. She did not

[032-049]know that in the slight commotion caused by their arrival at the inn

[032-050]that afternoon [[giles-winterborne|Winterborne]] had caught sight of her through the archway,

[032-051]had turned red, and was continuing his work with more concentrated

[032-052]attention on the very account of his discovery. [[robert-creedle|Robert Creedle]], too,

[032-053]who travelled with Giles, had been incidentally informed by the hostler

[032-054]that Dr. [[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Fitzpiers]] and his young wife were in the hotel, after which

[032-055]news Creedle kept shaking his head and saying to himself, “Ah!” very

[032-056]audibly, between his thrusts at the screw of the cider-press.

[032-057]“Why the deuce do you sigh like that, Robert?” asked Winterborne, at

[032-058]last.

[032-059]“Ah, maister—’tis my thoughts—’tis my thoughts!...Yes, ye’ve lost a

[032-060]hundred load o’ [[timber-economy|timber]] well seasoned; ye’ve lost five hundred pound in

[032-061]good money; ye’ve lost the stone-windered house that’s big enough to

[032-062]hold a dozen families; ye’ve lost your share of half a dozen good

[032-063]wagons and their horses—all lost!—through your letting slip she that

[032-064]was once yer own!”

[032-065]“Good God, Creedle, you’ll drive me mad!” said Giles, sternly. “Don’t

[032-066]speak of that any more!”

[032-067]Thus the subject had ended in the yard. Meanwhile, the passive cause of

[032-068]all this loss still regarded the scene. She was beautifully dressed;

[032-069]she was seated in the most comfortable room that the inn afforded; her

[032-070]long journey had been full of variety, and almost luxuriously

[032-071]performed—for Fitzpiers did not study economy where pleasure was in

[032-072]question. Hence it perhaps arose that Giles and all his belongings

[032-073]seemed sorry and common to her for the moment—moving in a plane so far

[032-074]removed from her own of late that she could scarcely believe she had

[032-075]ever found congruity therein. “No—I could never have married him!” she

[032-076]said, gently shaking her head. “Dear father was right. It would have

[032-077]been too coarse a life for me.” And she looked at the rings of sapphire

[032-078]and opal upon her white and slender fingers that had been gifts from

[032-079]Fitzpiers.

[032-080]Seeing that Giles still kept his back turned, and with a little of the

[032-081]above-described [[pride-and-humiliation|pride]] of life—easily to be understood, and possibly

[032-082]excused, in a young, inexperienced woman who thought she had married

[032-083]well—she said at last, with a smile on her lips, “Mr. Winterborne!”

[032-084]He appeared to take no heed, and she said a second time, “Mr.

[032-085]Winterborne!”

[032-086]Even now he seemed not to hear, though a person close enough to him to

[032-087]see the expression of his face might have doubted it; and she said a

[032-088]third time, with a timid loudness, “Mr. Winterborne! What, have you

[032-089]forgotten my voice?” She remained with her lips parted in a welcoming

[032-090]smile.

[032-091]He turned without surprise, and came deliberately towards the window.

[032-092]“Why do you call me?” he said, with a sternness that took her

[032-093]completely unawares, his face being now pale. “Is it not enough that

[032-094]you see me here moiling and muddling for my daily bread while you are

[032-095]sitting there in your success, that you can’t refrain from opening old

[032-096]wounds by calling out my name?”

[032-097]She flushed, and was struck dumb for some moments; but she forgave his

[032-098]unreasoning anger, knowing so well in what it had its root. “I am sorry

[032-099]I offended you by speaking,” she replied, meekly. “Believe me, I did

[032-100]not intend to do that. I could hardly sit here so near you without a

[032-101]word of recognition.”

[032-102]Winterborne’s heart had swollen big, and his eyes grown moist by this

[032-103]time, so much had the gentle answer of that familiar voice moved him.

[032-104]He assured her hurriedly, and without looking at her, that he was not

[032-105]angry. He then managed to ask her, in a clumsy, constrained way, if she

[032-106]had had a pleasant journey, and seen many interesting sights. She spoke

[032-107]of a few places that she had visited, and so the time passed till he

[032-108]withdrew to take his place at one of the levers which pulled round the

[032-109]screw.

[032-110]Forgotten her voice! Indeed, he had not forgotten her voice, as his

[032-111]bitterness showed. But though in the heat of the moment he had

[032-112]reproached her keenly, his second mood was a far more tender one—that

[032-113]which could regard her renunciation of such as he as her glory and her

[032-114]privilege, his own fidelity notwithstanding. He could have declared

[032-115]with a contemporary poet—

[032-116]“If I forget,

[032-117]The salt creek may forget the ocean;

[032-118]If I forget

[032-119]The heart whence flows my heart’s bright motion,

[032-120]May I sink meanlier than the worst

[032-121]Abandoned, outcast, crushed, accurst,

[032-122]If I forget.

[032-123]“Though you forget,

[032-124]No word of mine shall mar your pleasure;

[032-125]Though you forget,

[032-126]You filled my barren life with treasure,

[032-127]You may withdraw the gift you gave;

[032-128]You still are queen, I still am slave,

[032-129]Though you forget.”

[032-130]She had tears in her eyes at the thought that she could not remind him

[032-131]of what he ought to have remembered; that not herself but the pressure

[032-132]of events had dissipated the dreams of their early youth. Grace was

[032-133]thus unexpectedly worsted in her encounter with her old friend. She had

[032-134]opened the window with a faint sense of triumph, but he had turned it

[032-135]into sadness; she did not quite comprehend the reason why. In truth it

[032-136]was because she was not cruel enough in her cruelty. If you have to use

[032-137]the knife, use it, say the great surgeons; and for her own peace Grace

[032-138]should have contemned Winterborne thoroughly or not at all. As it was,

[032-139]on closing the window an indescribable, some might have said dangerous,

[032-140]pity quavered in her bosom for him.

[032-141]Presently her husband entered the room, and told her what a wonderful

[032-142]sunset there was to be seen.

[032-143]“I have not noticed it. But I have seen somebody out there that we

[032-144]know,” she replied, looking into the court.

[032-145]Fitzpiers followed the direction of her eyes, and said he did not

[032-146]recognize anybody.

[032-147]“Why, Mr. Winterborne—there he is, cider-making. He combines that with

[032-148]his other business, you know.”

[032-149]“Oh—that fellow,” said Fitzpiers, his curiosity becoming extinct.

[032-150]She, reproachfully: “What, call Mr. Winterborne a fellow, Edgar? It is

[032-151]true I was just saying to myself that I never could have married him;

[032-152]but I have much regard for him, and always shall.”

[032-153]“Well, do by all means, my dear one. I dare say I am inhuman, and

[032-154]supercilious, and contemptibly proud of my poor old ramshackle family;

[032-155]but I do honestly confess to you that I feel as if I belonged to a

[032-156]different species from the people who are working in that yard.”

[032-157]“And from me too, then. For my blood is no better than theirs.”

[032-158]He looked at her with a droll sort of awakening. It was, indeed, a

[032-159]startling anomaly that this woman of the tribe without should be

[032-160]standing there beside him as his wife, if his sentiments were as he had

[032-161]said. In their travels together she had ranged so unerringly at his

[032-162]level in ideas, tastes, and habits that he had almost forgotten how his

[032-163]heart had played havoc with his principles in taking her to him.

[032-164]“Ah YOU—you are refined and educated into something quite different,”

[032-165]he said, self-assuringly.

[032-166]“I don’t quite like to think that,” she murmured with soft regret. “And

[032-167]I think you underestimate Giles Winterborne. Remember, I was brought up

[032-168]with him till I was sent away to school, so I cannot be radically

[032-169]different. At any rate, I don’t feel so. That is, no doubt, my fault,

[032-170]and a great blemish in me. But I hope you will put up with it, Edgar.”

[032-171]Fitzpiers said that he would endeavor to do so; and as it was now

[032-172]getting on for dusk, they prepared to perform the last stage of their

[032-173]journey, so as to arrive at [[little-hintock|Hintock]] before it grew very late.

[032-174]In less than half an hour they started, the cider-makers in the yard

[032-175]having ceased their labors and gone away, so that the only sounds

[032-176]audible there now were the trickling of the juice from the tightly

[032-177]screwed press, and the buzz of a single wasp, which had drunk itself so

[032-178]tipsy that it was unconscious of nightfall. Grace was very cheerful at

[032-179]the thought of being soon in her sylvan home, but Fitzpiers sat beside

[032-180]her almost silent. An indescribable oppressiveness had overtaken him

[032-181]with the near approach of the journey’s end and the realities of life

[032-182]that lay there.

[032-183]“You don’t say a word, Edgar,” she observed. “Aren’t you glad to get

[032-184]back? I am.”

[032-185]“You have friends here. I have none.”

[032-186]“But my friends are yours.”

[032-187]“Oh yes—in that sense.”

[032-188]The conversation languished, and they drew near the end of Hintock

[032-189]Lane. It had been decided that they should, at least for a time, take

[032-190]up their abode in her father’s roomy house, one wing of which was quite

[032-191]at their service, being almost disused by the Melburys. Workmen had

[032-192]been painting, papering, and whitewashing this set of rooms in the

[032-193]wedded pair’s absence; and so scrupulous had been the timber-dealer

[032-194]that there should occur no hitch or disappointment on their arrival,

[032-195]that not the smallest detail remained undone. To make it all complete a

[032-196]ground-floor room had been fitted up as a surgery, with an independent

[032-197]outer door, to which Fitzpiers’s brass plate was screwed—for mere

[032-198]ornament, such a sign being quite superfluous where everybody knew the

[032-199]latitude and longitude of his neighbors for miles round.
