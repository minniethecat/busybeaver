# Section 51

[051-001][[giles-winterborne|Winterborne]] had advanced, too. He could criticise her. Times had been

[051-002]when to criticise a single trait in [[grace-melbury|Grace Melbury]] would have lain as

[051-003]far beyond his powers as to criticise a deity. This thing was sure: it

[051-004]was a new woman in many ways whom he had come out to see; a creature of

[051-005]more ideas, more [[pride-and-humiliation|dignity]], and, above all, more assurance, than the

[051-006]original Grace had been capable of. He could not at first decide

[051-007]whether he were pleased or displeased at this. But upon the whole the

[051-008]novelty attracted him.

[051-009]She was so sweet and sensitive that she feared his silence betokened

[051-010]something in his brain of the [[nature-in-the-woodlanders|nature]] of an enemy to her. “What are you

[051-011]thinking of that makes those lines come in your forehead?” she asked.

[051-012]“I did not mean to offend you by speaking of the time being premature

[051-013]as yet.”

[051-014]Touched by the genuine loving-kindness which had lain at the foundation

[051-015]of these words, and much moved, Winterborne turned his face aside, as

[051-016]he took her by the hand. He was grieved that he had criticised her.

[051-017]“You are very good, dear Grace,” he said, in a low voice. “You are

[051-018]better, much better, than you used to be.”

[051-019]“How?”

[051-020]He could not very well tell her how, and said, with an evasive smile,

[051-021]“You are prettier;” which was not what he really had meant. He then

[051-022]remained still holding her right hand in his own right, so that they

[051-023]faced in opposite ways; and as he did not let go, she ventured upon a

[051-024]tender remonstrance.

[051-025]“I think we have gone as far as we ought to go at present—and far

[051-026]enough to satisfy my poor father that we are the same as ever. You see,

[051-027]Giles, my case is not settled yet, and if—Oh, suppose I _never_ get

[051-028]free!—there should be any hitch or informality!”

[051-029]She drew a catching breath, and turned pale. The dialogue had been

[051-030]affectionate comedy up to this point. The gloomy atmosphere of the

[051-031]past, and the still gloomy horizon of the present, had been for the

[051-032]interval forgotten. Now the whole environment came back, the due

[051-033]balance of shade among the light was restored.

[051-034]“It is sure to be all right, I trust?” she resumed, in uneasy accents.

[051-035]“What did my father say the solicitor had told him?”

[051-036]“Oh—that all is sure enough. The case is so clear—nothing could be

[051-037]clearer. But the legal part is not yet quite done and finished, as is

[051-038]natural.”

[051-039]“Oh no—of course not,” she said, sunk in meek thought. “But father said

[051-040]it was _almost_—did he not? Do you know anything about the new law that

[051-041]makes these things so easy?”

[051-042]“Nothing—except the general fact that it enables ill-assorted husbands

[051-043]and wives to part in a way they could not formerly do without an Act of

[051-044]Parliament.”

[051-045]“Have you to sign a paper, or swear anything? Is it something like

[051-046]that?”

[051-047]“Yes, I believe so.”

[051-048]“How long has it been introduced?”

[051-049]“[[About]] six months or a year, the lawyer said, I think.”

[051-050]To hear these two poor Arcadian innocents talk of imperial law would

[051-051]have made a humane person weep who should have known what a dangerous

[051-052]structure they were building up on their supposed knowledge. They

[051-053]remained in thought, like children in the presence of the

[051-054]incomprehensible.

[051-055]“Giles,” she said, at last, “it makes me quite weary when I think how

[051-056]serious my situation is, or has been. Shall we not go out from here

[051-057]now, as it may seem rather fast of me—our being so long together, I

[051-058]mean—if anybody were to see us? I am almost sure,” she added,

[051-059]uncertainly, “that I ought not to let you hold my hand yet, knowing

[051-060]that the documents—or whatever it may be—have not been signed; so that

[051-061]I—am still as married as ever—or almost. My dear father has forgotten

[051-062]himself. Not that I feel morally bound to any one else, after what has

[051-063]taken place—no woman of spirit could—now, too, that several months have

[051-064]passed. But I wish to keep the proprieties as well as I can.”

[051-065]“Yes, yes. Still, your father reminds us that life is short. I myself

[051-066]feel that it is; that is why I wished to understand you in this that we

[051-067]have begun. At times, dear Grace, since receiving your father’s letter,

[051-068]I am as uneasy and fearful as a child at what he said. If one of us

[051-069]were to die before the formal signing and sealing that is to release

[051-070]you have been done—if we should drop out of the world and never have

[051-071]made the most of this little, short, but real opportunity, I should

[051-072]think to myself as I sunk down dying, ‘Would to my God that I had

[051-073]spoken out my whole heart—given her one poor little kiss when I had the

[051-074][[fate-and-free-will|chance]] to give it! But I never did, although she had promised to be

[051-075]mine some day; and now I never can.’ That’s what I should think.”

[051-076]She had begun by watching the words from his lips with a mournful

[051-077]regard, as though their passage were visible; but as he went on she

[051-078]dropped her glance. “Yes,” she said, “I have thought that, too. And,

[051-079]because I have thought it, I by no means meant, in speaking of the

[051-080]proprieties, to be reserved and cold to you who loved me so long ago,

[051-081]or to hurt your heart as I used to do at that thoughtless time. Oh, not

[051-082]at all, indeed! But—ought I to allow you?—oh, it is too quick—surely!”

[051-083]Her eyes filled with tears of bewildered, alarmed emotion.

[051-084]Winterborne was too straightforward to influence her further against

[051-085]her better judgment. “Yes—I suppose it is,” he said, repentantly. “I’ll

[051-086]wait till all is settled. What did your father say in that last

[051-087]letter?”

[051-088]He meant about his progress with the petition; but she, mistaking him,

[051-089]frankly spoke of the personal part. “He said—what I have implied.

[051-090]Should I tell more plainly?”

[051-091]“Oh no—don’t, if it is a [[secrecy-and-concealment|secret]].”

[051-092]“Not at all. I will tell every word, straight out, Giles, if you wish.

[051-093]He said I was to encourage you. There. But I cannot obey him further

[051-094]to-day. Come, let us go now.” She gently slid her hand from his, and

[051-095]went in front of him out of the Abbey.

[051-096]“I was thinking of getting some dinner,” said Winterborne, changing to

[051-097]the prosaic, as they walked. “And you, too, must require something. Do

[051-098]let me take you to a place I know.”

[051-099]Grace was almost without a friend in the world outside her father’s

[051-100]house; her life with [[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Fitzpiers]] had brought her no society; had

[051-101]sometimes, indeed, brought her deeper solitude and inconsideration than

[051-102]any she had ever known before. Hence it was a treat to her to find

[051-103]herself again the object of thoughtful care. But she questioned if to

[051-104]go publicly to dine with Giles Winterborne were not a proposal, due

[051-105]rather to his unsophistication than to his discretion. She said gently

[051-106]that she would much prefer his ordering her lunch at some place and

[051-107]then coming to tell her it was ready, while she remained in the Abbey

[051-108]porch. Giles saw her secret reasoning, thought how hopelessly blind to

[051-109]propriety he was beside her, and went to do as she wished.

[051-110]He was not absent more than ten minutes, and found Grace where he had

[051-111]left her. “It will be quite ready by the time you get there,” he said,

[051-112]and told her the name of the inn at which the meal had been ordered,

[051-113]which was one that she had never heard of.

[051-114]“I’ll find it by inquiry,” said Grace, setting out.

[051-115]“And shall I see you again?”

[051-116]“Oh yes—come to me there. It will not be like going together. I shall

[051-117]want you to find my father’s man and the gig for me.”

[051-118]He waited on some ten minutes or a quarter of an hour, till he thought

[051-119]her lunch ended, and that he might fairly take advantage of her

[051-120]invitation to start her on her way home. He went straight to The Three

[051-121]Tuns—a little tavern in a side street, scrupulously clean, but humble

[051-122]and inexpensive. On his way he had an occasional misgiving as to

[051-123]whether the place had been elegant enough for her; and as soon as he

[051-124]entered it, and saw her ensconced there, he perceived that he had

[051-125]blundered.

[051-126]Grace was seated in the only dining-room that the simple old hostelry

[051-127]could boast of, which was also a general parlor on market-days; a long,

[051-128]low apartment, with a sanded floor herring-boned with a broom; a wide,

[051-129]red-curtained window to the street, and another to the garden. Grace

[051-130]had retreated to the end of the room looking out upon the latter, the

[051-131]front part being full of a mixed company which had dropped in since he

[051-132]was there.

[051-133]She was in a mood of the greatest depression. On arriving, and seeing

[051-134]what the tavern was like, she had been taken by surprise; but having

[051-135]gone too far to retreat, she had heroically entered and sat down on the

[051-136]well-scrubbed settle, opposite the narrow table with its knives and

[051-137]steel forks, tin pepper-boxes, blue salt-cellars, and posters

[051-138]advertising the sale of bullocks against the wall. The last time that

[051-139]she had taken any meal in a public place it had been with Fitzpiers at

[051-140]the grand new [[earl-of-wessex-hotel|Earl of Wessex Hotel]] in that town, after a two months’

[051-141]roaming and sojourning at the gigantic hotels of the Continent. How

[051-142]could she have expected any other kind of accommodation in present

[051-143]circumstances than such as Giles had provided? And yet how unprepared

[051-144]she was for this change! The tastes that she had acquired from

[051-145]Fitzpiers had been imbibed so subtly that she hardly knew she possessed

[051-146]them till confronted by this contrast. The elegant Fitzpiers, in fact,

[051-147]at that very moment owed a long bill at the above-mentioned hotel for

[051-148]the luxurious style in which he used to put her up there whenever they

[051-149]drove to [[sherton-abbas|Sherton]]. But such is social sentiment, that she had been quite

[051-150]comfortable under those debt-impending conditions, while she felt

[051-151]humiliated by her present situation, which Winterborne had paid for

[051-152]honestly on the nail.

[051-153]He had noticed in a moment that she shrunk from her position, and all

[051-154]his pleasure was gone. It was the same susceptibility over again which

[051-155]had spoiled his Christmas party long ago.

[051-156]But he did not know that this recrudescence was only the casual result

[051-157]of Grace’s apprenticeship to what she was determined to learn in spite

[051-158]of it—a consequence of one of those sudden surprises which confront

[051-159]everybody bent upon turning over a new leaf. She had finished her

[051-160]lunch, which he saw had been a very mincing performance; and he brought

[051-161]her out of the house as soon as he could.

[051-162]“Now,” he said, with great sad eyes, “you have not finished at all

[051-163]well, I know. Come round to the Earl of [[wessex-in-the-woodlanders|Wessex]]. I’ll order a tea there.

[051-164]I did not remember that what was good enough for me was not good enough

[051-165]for you.”

[051-166]Her face faded into an aspect of deep distress when she saw what had

[051-167]happened. “Oh no, Giles,” she said, with extreme pathos; “certainly

[051-168]not. Why do you—say that when you know better? You _ever_ will

[051-169]misunderstand me.”

[051-170]“Indeed, that’s not so, Mrs. Fitzpiers. Can you deny that you felt out

[051-171]of place at The Three Tuns?”

[051-172]“I don’t know. Well, since you make me speak, I do not deny it.”

[051-173]“And yet I have felt at home there these twenty years. Your husband

[051-174]used always to take you to the Earl of Wessex, did he not?”

[051-175]“Yes,” she reluctantly admitted. How could she explain in the street of

[051-176]a market-town that it was her superficial and transitory taste which

[051-177]had been offended, and not her nature or her [[love-and-desire|affection]]? Fortunately, or

[051-178]unfortunately, at that moment they saw [[george-melbury|Melbury]]’s man driving vacantly

[051-179]along the street in search of her, the hour having passed at which he

[051-180]had been told to take her up. Winterborne hailed him, and she was

[051-181]powerless then to prolong the discourse. She entered the vehicle sadly,

[051-182]and the horse trotted away.
