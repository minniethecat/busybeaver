# Section 24

[024-001]“The rain is wetting your dress; please do come in,” he said. “It

[024-002]really makes my heart ache to let you stay here.”

[024-003]Immediately inside the front door was the door of his sitting-room; he

[024-004]flung it open, and stood in a coaxing attitude. Try how she would,

[024-005]Grace could not resist the supplicatory mandate written in the face and

[024-006]manner of this man, and distressful resignation sat on her as she

[024-007]glided past him into the room—brushing his coat with her elbow by

[024-008]reason of the narrowness.

[024-009]He followed her, shut the door—which she somehow had hoped he would

[024-010]leave open—and placing a chair for her, sat down. The concern which

[024-011]Grace felt at the development of these commonplace incidents was, of

[024-012]course, mainly owing to the strange effect upon her nerves of that view

[024-013]of him in the mirror gazing at her with open eyes when she had thought

[024-014]him sleeping, which made her fancy that his slumber might have been a

[024-015]feint based on inexplicable reasons.

[024-016]She again proffered the notes; he awoke from looking at her as at a

[024-017]piece of live statuary, and listened deferentially as she said, “Will

[024-018]you then reconsider, and cancel the bond which poor [[grammer-oliver|Grammer Oliver]] so

[024-019]foolishly gave?”

[024-020]“I’ll cancel it without reconsideration. Though you will allow me to

[024-021]have my own opinion about her foolishness. Grammer is a very wise

[024-022]woman, and she was as wise in that as in other things. You think there

[024-023]was something very fiendish in the compact, do you not, [[grace-melbury|Miss Melbury]]?

[024-024]But remember that the most eminent of our surgeons in past times have

[024-025]entered into such agreements.”

[024-026]“Not fiendish—strange.”

[024-027]“Yes, that may be, since strangeness is not in the [[nature-in-the-woodlanders|nature]] of a thing,

[024-028]but in its relation to something extrinsic—in this case an unessential

[024-029]observer.”

[024-030]He went to his desk, and searching a while found a paper, which he

[024-031]unfolded and brought to her. A thick cross appeared in ink at the

[024-032]bottom—evidently from the hand of Grammer. Grace put the paper in her

[024-033]pocket with a look of much relief.

[024-034]As [[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Fitzpiers]] did not take up the money (half of which had come from

[024-035]Grace’s own purse), she pushed it a little nearer to him. “No, no. I

[024-036]shall not take it from the old woman,” he said. “It is more strange

[024-037]than the fact of a surgeon arranging to obtain a subject for dissection

[024-038]that our acquaintance should be formed out of it.”

[024-039]“I am afraid you think me uncivil in showing my dislike to the notion.

[024-040]But I did not mean to be.”

[024-041]“Oh no, no.” He looked at her, as he had done before, with puzzled

[024-042]interest. “I cannot think, I cannot think,” he murmured. “Something

[024-043]bewilders me greatly.” He still reflected and hesitated. “Last night I

[024-044]sat up very late,” he at last went on, “and on that account I fell into

[024-045]a little nap on that couch about half an hour ago. And during my few

[024-046]minutes of unconsciousness I dreamed—what do you think?—that you stood

[024-047]in the room.”

[024-048]Should she tell? She merely blushed.

[024-049]“You may imagine,” Fitzpiers continued, now persuaded that it had,

[024-050]indeed, been a dream, “that I should not have dreamed of you without

[024-051]considerable thinking about you first.”

[024-052]He could not be acting; of that she felt assured.

[024-053]“I fancied in my vision that you stood there,” he said, pointing to

[024-054]where she had paused. “I did not see you directly, but reflected in the

[024-055]glass. I thought, what a lovely creature! The design is for once

[024-056]carried out. Nature has at last recovered her lost union with the Idea!

[024-057]My thoughts ran in that direction because I had been reading the work

[024-058]of a transcendental philosopher last night; and I dare say it was the

[024-059]dose of Idealism that I received from it that made me scarcely able to

[024-060]distinguish between reality and fancy. I almost wept when I awoke, and

[024-061]found that you had appeared to me in Time, but not in Space, alas!”

[024-062]At moments there was something theatrical in the delivery of

[024-063]Fitzpiers’s effusion; yet it would have been inexact to say that it was

[024-064]intrinsically theatrical. It often happens that in situations of

[024-065]unrestraint, where there is no thought of the eye of criticism, real

[024-066]feeling glides into a mode of manifestation not easily distinguishable

[024-067]from rodomontade. A veneer of affectation overlies a bulk of truth,

[024-068]with the evil consequence, if perceived, that the substance is

[024-069]estimated by the superficies, and the whole rejected.

[024-070]Grace, however, was no specialist in men’s manners, and she admired the

[024-071]sentiment without thinking of the form. And she was embarrassed:

[024-072]“lovely creature” made explanation awkward to her gentle modesty.

[024-073]“But can it be,” said he, suddenly, “that you really were here?”

[024-074]“I have to confess that I have been in the room once before,” faltered

[024-075]she. “The woman showed me in, and went away to fetch you; but as she

[024-076]did not return, I left.”

[024-077]“And you saw me asleep,” he murmured, with the faintest show of

[024-078][[pride-and-humiliation|humiliation]].

[024-079]“Yes—_if_ you were asleep, and did not deceive me.”

[024-080]“Why do you say if?”

[024-081]“I saw your eyes open in the glass, but as they were closed when I

[024-082]looked round upon you, I thought you were perhaps deceiving me.

[024-083]“Never,” said Fitzpiers, fervently—“never could I deceive you.”

[024-084]Foreknowledge to the distance of a year or so in either of them might

[024-085]have spoiled the effect of that pretty speech. Never deceive her! But

[024-086]they knew nothing, and the phrase had its day.

[024-087]Grace began now to be anxious to terminate the interview, but the

[024-088]compelling power of Fitzpiers’s atmosphere still held her there. She

[024-089]was like an inexperienced actress who, having at last taken up her

[024-090]position on the boards, and spoken her speeches, does not know how to

[024-091]move off. The thought of Grammer occurred to her. “I’ll go at once and

[024-092]tell poor Grammer of your generosity,” she said. “It will relieve her

[024-093]at once.”

[024-094]“Grammer’s a nervous disease, too—how singular!” he answered,

[024-095]accompanying her to the door. “One moment; look at this—it is something

[024-096]which may interest you.”

[024-097]He had thrown open the door on the other side of the passage, and she

[024-098]saw a microscope on the table of the confronting room. “Look into it,

[024-099]please; you’ll be interested,” he repeated.

[024-100]She applied her eye, and saw the usual circle of light patterned all

[024-101]over with a cellular tissue of some indescribable sort. “What do you

[024-102]think that is?” said Fitzpiers.

[024-103]She did not know.

[024-104]“That’s a fragment of [[john-south|old John South]]’s brain, which I am

[024-105]investigating.”

[024-106]She started back, not with aversion, but with wonder as to how it

[024-107]should have got there. Fitzpiers laughed.

[024-108]“Here am I,” he said, “endeavoring to carry on simultaneously the study

[024-109]of physiology and transcendental philosophy, the material world and the

[024-110]ideal, so as to discover if possible a point of contrast between them;

[024-111]and your finer sense is quite offended!”

[024-112]“Oh no, Mr. Fitzpiers,” said Grace, earnestly. “It is not so at all. I

[024-113]know from seeing your light at night how deeply you meditate and work.

[024-114]Instead of condemning you for your studies, I admire you very much!”

[024-115]Her face, upturned from the microscope, was so sweet, sincere, and

[024-116]self-forgetful in its aspect that the susceptible Fitzpiers more than

[024-117]wished to annihilate the lineal yard which separated it from his own.

[024-118]Whether anything of the kind showed in his eyes or not, Grace remained

[024-119]no longer at the microscope, but quickly went her way into the rain.

[024-120]CHAPTER XIX.

[024-121]Instead of resuming his investigation of South’s brain, which perhaps

[024-122]was not so interesting under the microscope as might have been expected

[024-123]from the importance of that organ in life, Fitzpiers reclined and

[024-124]ruminated on the interview. Grace’s curious susceptibility to his

[024-125]presence, though it was as if the currents of her life were disturbed

[024-126]rather than attracted by him, added a special interest to her general

[024-127]charm. Fitzpiers was in a distinct degree scientific, being ready and

[024-128]zealous to interrogate all physical manifestations, but primarily he

[024-129]was an idealist. He believed that behind the imperfect lay the perfect;

[024-130]that rare things were to be discovered amid a bulk of commonplace; that

[024-131]results in a new and untried case might be different from those in

[024-132]other cases where the conditions had been precisely similar. Regarding

[024-133]his own personality as one of unbounded possibilities, because it was

[024-134]his own—notwithstanding that the factors of his life had worked out a

[024-135]sorry product for thousands—he saw nothing but what was regular in his

[024-136]discovery at [[little-hintock|Hintock]] of an altogether exceptional being of the other

[024-137]sex, who for nobody else would have had any existence.

[024-138]One habit of Fitzpiers’s—commoner in dreamers of more advanced age than

[024-139]in men of his years—was that of talking to himself. He paced round his

[024-140]room with a selective tread upon the more prominent blooms of the

[024-141]carpet, and murmured, “This phenomenal girl will be the light of my

[024-142]life while I am at Hintock; and the special beauty of the situation is

[024-143]that our attitude and relations to each other will be purely spiritual.

[024-144]Socially we can never be intimate. Anything like matrimonial intentions

[024-145]towards her, charming as she is, would be absurd. They would spoil the

[024-146]ethereal character of my regard. And, indeed, I have other aims on the

[024-147]practical side of my life.”

[024-148]Fitzpiers bestowed a regulation thought on the advantageous [[marriage-in-the-woodlanders|marriage]] he

[024-149]was bound to make with a woman of family as good as his own, and of

[024-150]purse much longer. But as an object of contemplation for the present,

[024-151]as objective spirit rather than corporeal presence, Grace [[george-melbury|Melbury]] would

[024-152]serve to keep his soul alive, and to relieve the monotony of his days.

[024-153]His first notion—acquired from the mere sight of her without

[024-154]converse—that of an idle and vulgar flirtation with a [[timber-economy|timber]]-merchant’s

[024-155]pretty daughter, grated painfully upon him now that he had found what

[024-156]Grace intrinsically was. Personal intercourse with such as she could

[024-157]take no lower form than intellectual communion, and mutual explorations

[024-158]of the world of thought. Since he could not call at her father’s,

[024-159]having no practical views, cursory encounters in the lane, in the wood,

[024-160]coming and going to and from church, or in passing her dwelling, were

[024-161]what the acquaintance would have to feed on.

[024-162]Such anticipated glimpses of her now and then realized themselves in

[024-163]the event. Rencounters of not more than a minute’s duration, frequently

[024-164]repeated, will build up mutual interest, even an intimacy, in a lonely

[024-165]place. Theirs grew as imperceptibly as the tree-twigs budded. There

[024-166]never was a particular moment at which it could be said they became

[024-167]friends; yet a delicate understanding now existed between two who in

[024-168]the winter had been strangers.

[024-169]Spring weather came on rather suddenly, the unsealing of buds that had

[024-170]long been swollen accomplishing itself in the space of one warm night.

[024-171]The rush of sap in the veins of the trees could almost be heard. The

[024-172]flowers of late April took up a position unseen, and looked as if they

[024-173]had been blooming a long while, though there had been no trace of them

[024-174]the day before yesterday; birds began not to mind getting wet. In-door

[024-175]people said they had heard the nightingale, to which out-door people

[024-176]replied contemptuously that they had heard him a fortnight before.

[024-177]The young doctor’s practice being scarcely so large as a [[london-in-the-woodlanders|London]]

[024-178]surgeon’s, he frequently walked in the wood. Indeed such practice as he

[024-179]had he did not follow up with the assiduity that would have been

[024-180]necessary for developing it to exceptional proportions. One day, book

[024-181]in hand, he walked in a part of the wood where the trees were mainly

[024-182]oaks. It was a calm afternoon, and there was everywhere around that

[024-183]sign of great undertakings on the part of vegetable nature which is apt

[024-184]to fill reflective human beings who are not undertaking much themselves

[024-185]with a sudden uneasiness at the contrast. He heard in the distance a

[024-186]curious sound, something like the quack of a duck, which, though it was

[024-187]common enough here about this time, was not common to him.
