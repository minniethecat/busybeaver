# Section 38

[038-001]“Never.”

[038-002]“So much the better for your stomach,” said [[grace-melbury|Mrs. Fitzpiers]], in an

[038-003]altered voice. And turning away quickly, she went on.

[038-004]As her husband’s character thus shaped itself under the touch of time,

[038-005]Grace was almost startled to find how little she suffered from that

[038-006]jealous excitement which is conventionally attributed to all wives in

[038-007]such circumstances. But though possessed by none of that feline

[038-008]wildness which it was her moral duty to experience, she did not fail to

[038-009]know that she had made a frightful mistake in her [[marriage-in-the-woodlanders|marriage]].

[038-010]Acquiescence in her father’s wishes had been degradation to herself.

[038-011]People are not given premonitions for nothing; she should have obeyed

[038-012]her impulse on that early morning, and steadfastly refused her hand.

[038-013]Oh, that plausible tale which her then betrothed had told her about

[038-014]Suke—the dramatic account of her entreaties to him to draw the aching

[038-015]enemy, and the fine artistic touch he had given to the story by

[038-016]explaining that it was a lovely molar without a flaw!

[038-017]She traced the remainder of the [[nature-in-the-woodlanders|woodland]] track dazed by the

[038-018]complications of her position. If his protestations to her before their

[038-019]marriage could be believed, her husband had felt [[love-and-desire|affection]] of some sort

[038-020]for herself and this woman simultaneously; and was now again spreading

[038-021]the same emotion over [[felice-charmond|Mrs. Charmond]] and herself conjointly, his manner

[038-022]being still kind and fond at times. But surely, rather than that, he

[038-023]must have played the hypocrite towards her in each case with elaborate

[038-024]completeness; and the thought of this sickened her, for it involved the

[038-025]conjecture that if he had not loved her, his only motive for making her

[038-026]his wife must have been her little fortune. Yet here Grace made a

[038-027]mistake, for the love of men like [[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Fitzpiers]] is unquestionably of such

[038-028]quality as to bear division and transference. He had indeed, once

[038-029]declared, though not to her, that on one occasion he had noticed

[038-030]himself to be possessed by five distinct infatuations at the same time.

[038-031]Therein it differed from the highest affection as the lower orders of

[038-032]the animal world differ from advanced organisms, partition causing, not

[038-033]death, but a multiplied existence. He had loved her sincerely, and had

[038-034]by no means ceased to love her now. But such double and treble

[038-035]barrelled hearts were naturally beyond her conception.

[038-036]Of poor [[suke-damson|Suke Damson]], Grace thought no more. She had had her day.

[038-037]“If he does not love me I will not love him!” said Grace, proudly. And

[038-038]though these were mere words, it was a somewhat formidable thing for

[038-039]Fitzpiers that her heart was approximating to a state in which it might

[038-040]be possible to carry them out. That very absence of hot [[jealousy-and-rivalry|jealousy]] which

[038-041]made his courses so easy, and on which, indeed, he congratulated

[038-042]himself, meant, unknown to either wife or husband, more mischief than

[038-043]the inconvenient watchfulness of a jaundiced eye.

[038-044]Her sleep that night was nervous. The wing allotted to her and her

[038-045]husband had never seemed so lonely. At last she got up, put on her

[038-046]dressing-gown, and went down-stairs. Her father, who slept lightly,

[038-047]heard her descend, and came to the stair-head.

[038-048]“Is that you, Grace? What’s the matter?” he said.

[038-049]“Nothing more than that I am restless. Edgar is detained by a case at

[038-050][[owlscombe|Owlscombe]] in [[white-hart-vale|White Hart Vale]].”

[038-051]“But how’s that? I saw the woman’s husband at Great [[little-hintock|Hintock]] just afore

[038-052]bedtime; and she was going on well, and the doctor gone then.”

[038-053]“Then he’s detained somewhere else,” said Grace. “Never mind me; he

[038-054]will soon be home. I expect him about one.”

[038-055]She went back to her room, and dozed and woke several times. One

[038-056]o’clock had been the hour of his return on the last occasion; but it

[038-057]passed now by a long way, and Fitzpiers did not come. Just before dawn

[038-058]she heard the men stirring in the yard; and the flashes of their

[038-059]lanterns spread every now and then through her window-blind. She

[038-060]remembered that her father had told her not to be disturbed if she

[038-061]noticed them, as they would be rising early to send off four loads of

[038-062]hurdles to a distant sheep-fair. Peeping out, she saw them bustling

[038-063]about, the hollow-turner among the rest; he was loading his

[038-064]wares—wooden-bowls, dishes, spigots, spoons, cheese-vats, funnels, and

[038-065]so on—upon one of her father’s wagons, who carried them to the fair for

[038-066]him every year out of neighborly kindness.

[038-067]The scene and the occasion would have enlivened her but that her

[038-068]husband was still absent; though it was now five o’clock. She could

[038-069]hardly suppose him, whatever his infatuation, to have prolonged to a

[038-070]later hour than ten an ostensibly professional call on Mrs. Charmond at

[038-071]Middleton; and he could have ridden home in two hours and a half. What,

[038-072]then, had become of him? That he had been out the greater part of the

[038-073]two preceding nights added to her uneasiness.

[038-074]She dressed herself, descended, and went out, the weird twilight of

[038-075]advancing day chilling the rays from the lanterns, and making the men’s

[038-076]faces wan. As soon as [[george-melbury|Melbury]] saw her he came round, showing his alarm.

[038-077]“Edgar is not come,” she said. “And I have reason to know that he’s not

[038-078]attending anybody. He has had no rest for two nights before this. I was

[038-079]going to the top of the hill to look for him.”

[038-080]“I’ll come with you,” said Melbury.

[038-081]She begged him not to hinder himself; but he insisted, for he saw a

[038-082]peculiar and rigid gloom in her face over and above her uneasiness, and

[038-083]did not like the look of it. Telling the men he would be with them

[038-084]again soon, he walked beside her into the turnpike-road, and partly up

[038-085]the hill whence she had watched Fitzpiers the night before across the

[038-086][[blackmoor|Great White Hart]] or Blackmoor Valley. They halted beneath a half-dead

[038-087]oak, hollow, and disfigured with white tumors, its roots spreading out

[038-088]like accipitrine claws grasping the ground. A chilly wind circled round

[038-089]them, upon whose currents the seeds of a neighboring lime-tree,

[038-090]supported parachute-wise by the wing attached, flew out of the boughs

[038-091]downward like fledglings from their nest. The vale was wrapped in a dim

[038-092]atmosphere of unnaturalness, and the east was like a livid curtain

[038-093]edged with pink. There was no sign nor sound of Fitzpiers.

[038-094]“It is no use standing here,” said her father. “He may come home fifty

[038-095]ways...why, look here!—here be Darling’s tracks—turned homeward and

[038-096]nearly blown dry and hard! He must have come in hours ago without your

[038-097]seeing him.”

[038-098]“He has not done that,” said she.

[038-099]They went back hastily. On entering their own gates they perceived that

[038-100]the men had left the wagons, and were standing round the door of the

[038-101]stable which had been appropriated to the doctor’s use. “Is there

[038-102]anything the matter?” cried Grace.

[038-103]“Oh no, ma’am. All’s well that ends well,” said [[timothy-tangs|old Timothy Tangs]].

[038-104]“I’ve heard of such things before—among workfolk, though not among your

[038-105]gentle people—that’s true.”

[038-106]They entered the stable, and saw the pale shape of Darling standing in

[038-107]the middle of her stall, with Fitzpiers on her back, sound asleep.

[038-108]Darling was munching hay as well as she could with the bit in her

[038-109]month, and the reins, which had fallen from Fitzpiers’s hand, hung upon

[038-110]her neck.

[038-111]Grace went and touched his hand; shook it before she could arouse him.

[038-112]He moved, started, opened his eyes, and exclaimed, “Ah, Felice!...Oh,

[038-113]it’s Grace. I could not see in the gloom. What—am I in the saddle?”

[038-114]“Yes,” said she. “How do you come here?”

[038-115]He collected his thoughts, and in a few minutes stammered, “I was

[038-116]riding along homeward through the vale, very, very sleepy, having been

[038-117]up so much of late. When I came opposite Holywell spring the mare

[038-118]turned her head that way, as if she wanted to drink. I let her go in,

[038-119]and she drank; I thought she would never finish. While she was

[038-120]drinking, the clock of Owlscombe Church struck twelve. I distinctly

[038-121]remember counting the strokes. From that moment I positively recollect

[038-122]nothing till I saw you here by my side.”

[038-123]“The name! If it had been any other horse he’d have had a broken neck!”

[038-124]murmured Melbury.

[038-125]“’Tis wonderful, sure, how a quiet hoss will bring a man home at such

[038-126]times!” said [[john-upjohn|John Upjohn]]. “And what’s more wonderful than keeping your

[038-127]seat in a deep, slumbering sleep? I’ve knowed men drowze off walking

[038-128]home from randies where the mead and other liquors have gone round

[038-129]well, and keep walking for more than a mile on end without waking.

[038-130]Well, doctor, I don’t care who the man is, ’tis a mercy you wasn’t a

[038-131]drownded, or a splintered, or a hanged up to a tree like Absalom—also a

[038-132]handsome gentleman like yerself, as the prophets say.”

[038-133]“True,” murmured old Timothy. “From the soul of his foot to the crown

[038-134]of his head there was no blemish in him.”

[038-135]“Or leastwise you might ha’ been a-wownded into tatters a’most, and no

[038-136]doctor to jine your few limbs together within seven mile!”

[038-137]While this grim address was proceeding, Fitzpiers had dismounted, and

[038-138]taking Grace’s arm walked stiffly in-doors with her. Melbury stood

[038-139]staring at the horse, which, in addition to being very weary, was

[038-140]spattered with mud. There was no mud to speak of about the Hintocks

[038-141]just now—only in the clammy hollows of the vale beyond Owlscombe, the

[038-142]stiff soil of which retained moisture for weeks after the uplands were

[038-143]dry. While they were rubbing down the mare, Melbury’s mind coupled with

[038-144]the foreign quality of the mud the name he had heard unconsciously

[038-145]muttered by the surgeon when Grace took his hand—“Felice.” Who was

[038-146]Felice? Why, Mrs. Charmond; and she, as he knew, was staying at

[038-147]Middleton.

[038-148]Melbury had indeed pounced upon the image that filled Fitzpiers’s

[038-149]half-awakened soul—wherein there had been a picture of a recent

[038-150]interview on a lawn with a capriciously passionate woman who had begged

[038-151]him not to come again in tones whose vibration incited him to disobey.

[038-152]“What are you doing here? Why do you pursue me? Another belongs to you.

[038-153]If they were to see you they would seize you as a thief!” And she had

[038-154]turbulently admitted to his wringing questions that her visit to

[038-155]Middleton had been undertaken less because of the invalid relative than

[038-156]in shamefaced fear of her own weakness if she remained near his home. A

[038-157]triumph then it was to Fitzpiers, poor and hampered as he had become,

[038-158]to recognize his real conquest of this beauty, delayed so many years.

[038-159]His was the selfish passion of Congreve’s Millamont, to whom love’s

[038-160]supreme delight lay in “that heart which others bleed for, bleed for

[038-161]me.”

[038-162]When the horse had been attended to Melbury stood uneasily here and

[038-163]there about his premises; he was rudely disturbed in the comfortable

[038-164]views which had lately possessed him on his domestic concerns. It is

[038-165]true that he had for some days discerned that Grace more and more

[038-166]sought his company, preferred supervising his kitchen and bakehouse

[038-167]with her step-mother to occupying herself with the lighter details of

[038-168]her own apartments. She seemed no longer able to find in her own hearth

[038-169]an adequate focus for her life, and hence, like a weak queen-bee after

[038-170]leading off to an independent home, had hovered again into the parent

[038-171]hive. But he had not construed these and other incidents of the kind

[038-172]till now.

[038-173]Something was wrong in the dove-cot. A ghastly sense that he alone

[038-174]would be responsible for whatever unhappiness should be brought upon

[038-175]her for whom he almost solely lived, whom to retain under his roof he

[038-176]had faced the numerous inconveniences involved in giving up the best

[038-177]part of his house to Fitzpiers. There was no room for doubt that, had

[038-178]he allowed events to take their natural course, she would have accepted

[038-179][[giles-winterborne|Winterborne]], and realized his old dream of restitution to that young

[038-180]man’s family.
