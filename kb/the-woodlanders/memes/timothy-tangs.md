# Timothy Tangs

Timothy Tangs the elder is a woodworker of Little Hintock, a hollow-turner by trade, who works alongside Giles Winterborne's crew in the woodland operations. Together with his son — "young Timothy Tangs" — and Robert Creedle, he forms part of the stable core of the Hintock labouring community. His appearances in the novel, though brief, consistently serve to voice the perspective of the settled rural craftsman — observant, opinionated, and unimpressed by the pretensions of outsiders.

## 身份与背景

Tangs is established from the novel's opening pages as one of the workmen in the winter woodland scene: "Timothy Tangs and young Timothy Tangs, top and bottom, were engaged in the hollow-turner's trade" [005-017]. The hollow-turner's craft — making turned wooden vessels such as bowls and cups — was a specialized skill within the broader woodland economy, placing Tangs slightly above the general labourer in the hierarchy of rural trades. He is of an age to be considered "old Timothy Tangs," father to a grown son who works beside him [016-146] [036-098].

## 性格与特征

Tangs possesses the characteristic bluntness of the Wessex labourer. When Fitzpiers's arrival and plans are discussed, Tangs's judgement is direct: "He must," he says — an elliptical phrase that, in context, conveys both the inevitability and the dubiousness of the outsider's ambitions [006-002]. He is observant without being meddlesome: his encounters with other characters — asking Grace if she will join in a gathering [026-197], confronting her unexpectedly on a path [036-098] — are brief but carefully placed interventions that advance the narrative's social texture. His concluding pronouncement on one of the novel's romantic entanglements — "Well, all's well that ends well" [038-103] — carries the weight of a man who has seen enough of life to know that most things do, in fact, end one way or another.

## 叙事线索

Tangs appears in the novel's opening scene, one of the workmen clustered around the fire in the woodland clearing, establishing the working world into which the novel's protagonists will move [005-095]. He participates in the communal labour at Melbury's gates [016-146], encounters Grace in the lanes around Hintock [026-197] [036-098], and offers his characteristic terse wisdom at key narrative junctures. His appearances, though brief and widely spaced, function as a kind of narrative punctuation — the return of the familiar, unchanging voice of the settled community against which the dramas of the principal characters unfold.

## 意义与解读

In a novel structured around the tensions between stability and change, Timothy Tangs represents the former. His craft — hollow-turning — is among the most ancient of woodland trades, and his presence alongside his son suggests a continuity of skill and occupation across generations. Hardy uses Tangs and characters like him to anchor the novel's social world, providing a baseline of ordinariness against which the extraordinary events — Fitzpiers's arrival, Giles's death, Grace's marital crisis — are measured. His name, with its Saxon ring, locates him deep in the Wessex soil, a counterweight to the French-derived Fitzpiers and the class-mobile Melburys.
