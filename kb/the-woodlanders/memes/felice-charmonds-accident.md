## Overview

Mrs. Felice Charmond's carriage accident occurs in the latter part of *The Woodlanders*, serving as a catalyst that reconnects her with Dr. Fitzpiers and accelerates the novel's marital crisis. The accident brings the doctor to her bedside, rekindling their prior attachment at the very moment Grace is attempting to salvage her marriage.

## The Incident

The details of the accident are conveyed through report rather than direct narration, maintaining the novel's characteristic distance from its gentry characters. Mrs. Charmond sustains injuries severe enough to require medical attention, and Fitzpiers — as the local physician with prior acquaintance — is summoned to attend her.

## Fitzpiers's Attendance

Fitzpiers's professional duty to attend Mrs. Charmond places him in an impossible personal position. His history with her — the prior flirtation, the intercepted correspondence, Grace's knowledge of their connection — means that every visit is freighted with suspicion. The accident transforms a latent emotional entanglement into an active crisis, as Fitzpiers's medical visits provide both the opportunity and the pretext for renewed intimacy.

## Consequences

The accident and its aftermath contribute directly to the unraveling of Grace and Fitzpiers's marriage. Grace's awareness of Fitzpiers's attendance on Mrs. Charmond sharpens her sense of betrayal, while Fitzpiers himself is drawn back into the world of the gentry that he prefers to the woodland obscurity of Hintock.
