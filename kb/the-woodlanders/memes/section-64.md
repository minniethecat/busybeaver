# Section 64

[064-001]The sight of one of these gins when set produced a vivid impression

[064-002]that it was endowed with life. It exhibited the combined aspects of a

[064-003]shark, a crocodile, and a scorpion. Each tooth was in the form of a

[064-004]tapering spine, two and a quarter inches long, which, when the jaws

[064-005]were closed, stood in alternation from this side and from that. When

[064-006]they were open, the two halves formed a complete circle between two and

[064-007]three feet in diameter, the plate or treading-place in the midst being

[064-008]about a foot square, while from beneath extended in opposite directions

[064-009]the soul of the apparatus, the pair of springs, each one being of a

[064-010]stiffness to render necessary a lever or the whole weight of the body

[064-011]when forcing it down.

[064-012]There were men at this time still living at [[little-hintock|Hintock]] who remembered when

[064-013]the gin and others like it were in use. Tim Tangs’s great-uncle had

[064-014]endured a night of six hours in this very trap, which lamed him for

[064-015]life. Once a keeper of [[the-woodland-tracts|Hintock woods]] set it on the track of a poacher,

[064-016]and afterwards, coming back that way, forgetful of what he had done,

[064-017]walked into it himself. The wound brought on lockjaw, of which he died.

[064-018]This event occurred during the thirties, and by the year 1840 the use

[064-019]of such implements was well-nigh discontinued in the neighborhood. But

[064-020]being made entirely of iron, they by no means disappeared, and in

[064-021]almost every village one could be found in some nook or corner as

[064-022]readily as this was found by Tim. It had, indeed, been a fearful

[064-023]amusement of Tim and other Hintock lads—especially those who had a dim

[064-024]sense of becoming renowned poachers when they reached their prime—to

[064-025]drag out this trap from its hiding, set it, and throw it with billets

[064-026]of wood, which were penetrated by the teeth to the depth of near an

[064-027]inch.

[064-028]As soon as he had examined the trap, and found that the hinges and

[064-029]springs were still perfect, he shouldered it without more ado, and

[064-030]returned with his burden to his own garden, passing on through the

[064-031]hedge to the path immediately outside the boundary. Here, by the help

[064-032]of a stout stake, he set the trap, and laid it carefully behind a bush

[064-033]while he went forward to reconnoitre. As has been stated, nobody passed

[064-034]this way for days together sometimes; but there was just a possibility

[064-035]that some other pedestrian than the one in request might arrive, and it

[064-036]behooved Tim to be careful as to the identity of his victim.

[064-037]Going about a hundred yards along the rising ground to the right, he

[064-038]reached a ridge whereon a large and thick holly grew. Beyond this for

[064-039]some distance the wood was more open, and the course which [[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Fitzpiers]]

[064-040]must pursue to reach the point, if he came to-night, was visible a long

[064-041]way forward.

[064-042]For some time there was no sign of him or of anybody. Then there shaped

[064-043]itself a spot out of the dim mid-distance, between the masses of

[064-044]brushwood on either hand. And it enlarged, and Tim could hear the

[064-045]brushing of feet over the tufts of sour-grass. The airy gait revealed

[064-046]Fitzpiers even before his exact outline could be seen.

[064-047]Tim Tangs turned about, and ran down the opposite side of the hill,

[064-048]till he was again at the head of his own garden. It was the work of a

[064-049]few moments to drag out the [[the-man-trap-incident|man-trap]], very gently—that the plate might

[064-050]not be disturbed sufficiently to throw it—to a space between a pair of

[064-051]young oaks which, rooted in contiguity, grew apart upward, forming a

[064-052]V-shaped opening between; and, being backed up by bushes, left this as

[064-053]the only course for a foot-passenger. In it he laid the trap with the

[064-054]same gentleness of handling, locked the chain round one of the [[nature-in-the-woodlanders|trees]],

[064-055]and finally slid back the guard which was placed to keep the gin from

[064-056]accidentally catching the arms of him who set it, or, to use the local

[064-057]and better word, “toiled” it.

[064-058]Having completed these arrangements, Tim sprang through the adjoining

[064-059]hedge of his father’s garden, ran down the path, and softly entered the

[064-060]house.

[064-061]Obedient to his order, [[suke-damson|Suke]] had gone to bed; and as soon as he had

[064-062]bolted the door, Tim unlaced and kicked off his boots at the foot of

[064-063]the stairs, and retired likewise, without lighting a candle. His object

[064-064]seemed to be to undress as soon as possible. Before, however, he had

[064-065]completed the operation, a long cry resounded without—penetrating, but

[064-066]indescribable.

[064-067]“What’s that?” said Suke, starting up in bed.

[064-068]“Sounds as if somebody had caught a hare in his gin.”

[064-069]“Oh no,” said she. “It was not a hare, ’twas louder. Hark!”

[064-070]“Do ’ee get to sleep,” said Tim. “How be you going to wake at half-past

[064-071]three else?”

[064-072]She lay down and was silent. Tim stealthily opened the window and

[064-073]listened. Above the low harmonies produced by the instrumentation of

[064-074]the various species of trees around the premises he could hear the

[064-075]twitching of a chain from the spot whereon he had set the man-trap. But

[064-076]further human sound there was none.

[064-077]Tim was puzzled. In the haste of his project he had not calculated upon

[064-078]a cry; but if one, why not more? He soon ceased to essay an answer, for

[064-079]Hintock was dead to him already. In half a dozen hours he would be out

[064-080]of its precincts for life, on his way to the antipodes. He closed the

[064-081]window and lay down.

[064-082]The hour which had brought these movements of Tim to birth had been

[064-083]operating actively elsewhere. Awaiting in her father’s house the minute

[064-084]of her appointment with her husband, Grace Fitzpiers deliberated on

[064-085]many things. Should she inform her father before going out that the

[064-086]estrangement of herself and Edgar was not so complete as he had

[064-087]imagined, and deemed desirable for her happiness? If she did so she

[064-088]must in some measure become the apologist of her husband, and she was

[064-089]not prepared to go so far.

[064-090]As for him, he kept her in a mood of considerate gravity. He certainly

[064-091]had changed. He had at his worst times always been gentle in his manner

[064-092]towards her. Could it be that she might make of him a true and worthy

[064-093]husband yet? She had married him; there was no getting over that; and

[064-094]ought she any longer to keep him at a distance? His suave deference to

[064-095]her lightest whim on the question of his comings and goings, when as

[064-096]her lawful husband he might show a little independence, was a trait in

[064-097]his character as unexpected as it was engaging. If she had been his

[064-098]empress, and he her thrall, he could not have exhibited a more

[064-099]sensitive care to avoid intruding upon her against her will.

[064-100]Impelled by a remembrance she took down a prayer-book and turned to the

[064-101][[marriage-in-the-woodlanders|marriage]]-service. Reading it slowly through, she became quite appalled

[064-102]at her recent off-handedness, when she rediscovered what awfully solemn

[064-103]promises she had made him at those chancel steps not so very long ago.

[064-104]She became lost in long ponderings on how far a person’s conscience

[064-105]might be bound by vows made without at the time a full recognition of

[064-106]their force. That particular sentence, beginning “Whom God hath joined

[064-107]together,” was a staggerer for a gentlewoman of strong devotional

[064-108]sentiment. She wondered whether God really did join them together.

[064-109]Before she had done deliberating the time of her engagement drew near,

[064-110]and she went out of the house almost at the moment that Tim Tangs

[064-111]retired to his own.

[064-112]The position of things at that critical juncture was briefly as

[064-113]follows.

[064-114]Two hundred yards to the right of the upper end of Tangs’s garden

[064-115]Fitzpiers was still advancing, having now nearly reached the summit of

[064-116]the wood-clothed ridge, the path being the actual one which further on

[064-117]passed between the two young oaks. Thus far it was according to Tim’s

[064-118]conjecture. But about two hundred yards to the left, or rather less,

[064-119]was arising a condition which he had not divined, the emergence of

[064-120]Grace as aforesaid from the upper corner of her father’s garden, with

[064-121]the view of meeting Tim’s intended victim. Midway between husband and

[064-122]wife was the diabolical trap, silent, open, ready.

[064-123]Fitzpiers’s walk that night had been cheerful, for he was convinced

[064-124]that the slow and gentle method he had adopted was promising success.

[064-125]The very restraint that he was obliged to exercise upon himself, so as

[064-126]not to kill the delicate bud of returning confidence, fed his flame. He

[064-127]walked so much more rapidly than Grace that, if they continued

[064-128]advancing as they had begun, he would reach the trap a good half-minute

[064-129]before she could reach the same spot.

[064-130]But here a new circumstance came in; to escape the unpleasantness of

[064-131]being watched or listened to by lurkers—naturally curious by reason of

[064-132]their strained relations—they had arranged that their meeting for

[064-133]to-night should be at the holm-tree on the ridge above named. So soon,

[064-134]accordingly, as Fitzpiers reached the tree he stood still to await her.

[064-135]He had not paused under the prickly foliage more than two minutes when

[064-136]he thought he heard a scream from the other side of the ridge.

[064-137]Fitzpiers wondered what it could mean; but such wind as there was just

[064-138]now blew in an adverse direction, and his mood was light. He set down

[064-139]the origin of the sound to one of the superstitious freaks or

[064-140]frolicsome scrimmages between sweethearts that still survived in

[064-141]Hintock from old-English times; and waited on where he stood till ten

[064-142]minutes had passed. Feeling then a little uneasy, his mind reverted to

[064-143]the scream; and he went forward over the summit and down the embowered

[064-144]incline, till he reached the pair of sister oaks with the narrow

[064-145]opening between them.

[064-146]Fitzpiers stumbled and all but fell. Stretching down his hand to

[064-147]ascertain the obstruction, it came in contact with a confused mass of

[064-148]silken drapery and iron-work that conveyed absolutely no explanatory

[064-149]idea to his mind at all. It was but the work of a moment to strike a

[064-150]match; and then he saw a sight which congealed his blood.

[064-151]The man-trap was thrown; and between its jaws was part of a woman’s

[064-152]clothing—a patterned silk skirt—gripped with such violence that the

[064-153]iron teeth had passed through it, skewering its tissue in a score of

[064-154]places. He immediately recognized the skirt as that of one of his

[064-155]wife’s gowns—the gown that she had worn when she met him on the very

[064-156]last occasion.

[064-157]Fitzpiers had often studied the effect of these instruments when

[064-158]examining the collection at [[hintock-house|Hintock House]], and the conception instantly

[064-159]flashed through him that Grace had been caught, taken out mangled by

[064-160]some [[fate-and-free-will|chance]] passer, and carried home, some of her clothes being left

[064-161]behind in the difficulty of getting her free. The shock of this

[064-162]conviction, striking into the very current of high hope, was so great

[064-163]that he cried out like one in corporal agony, and in his misery bowed

[064-164]himself down to the ground.

[064-165]Of all the degrees and qualities of punishment that Fitzpiers had

[064-166]undergone since his sins against Grace first began, not any even

[064-167]approximated in intensity to this.

[064-168]“Oh, my own—my darling! Oh, cruel Heaven—it is too much, this!” he

[064-169]cried, writhing and rocking himself over the sorry accessories of her

[064-170]he deplored.

[064-171]The voice of his distress was sufficiently loud to be audible to any

[064-172]one who might have been there to hear it; and one there was. Right and

[064-173]left of the narrow pass between the oaks were dense bushes; and now

[064-174]from behind these a female figure glided, whose appearance even in the

[064-175]gloom was, though graceful in outline, noticeably strange.

[064-176]She was in white up to the waist, and figured above. She was, in short,

[064-177]Grace, his wife, lacking the portion of her dress which the gin

[064-178]retained.
