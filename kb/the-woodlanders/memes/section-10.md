# Section 10

[010-001]They went noiselessly over mats of starry moss, rustled through

[010-002]interspersed tracts of leaves, skirted trunks with spreading roots,

[010-003]whose mossed rinds made them like hands wearing green gloves; elbowed

[010-004]old elms and ashes with great forks, in which stood pools of water that

[010-005]overflowed on rainy days, and ran down their stems in green cascades.

[010-006]On older [[nature-in-the-woodlanders|trees]] still than these, huge lobes of fungi grew like lungs.

[010-007]Here, as everywhere, the Unfulfilled Intention, which makes life what

[010-008]it is, was as obvious as it could be among the depraved crowds of a

[010-009]city slum. The leaf was deformed, the curve was crippled, the taper was

[010-010]interrupted; the lichen eat the vigor of the stalk, and the ivy slowly

[010-011]strangled to death the promising sapling.

[010-012]They dived amid beeches under which nothing grew, the younger boughs

[010-013]still retaining their hectic leaves, that rustled in the breeze with a

[010-014]sound almost metallic, like the sheet-iron foliage of the fabled

[010-015]Jarnvid wood. Some flecks of white in Grace’s drapery had enabled [[giles-winterborne|Giles]]

[010-016]to keep her and her father in view till this time; but now he lost

[010-017]sight of them, and was obliged to follow by ear—no difficult matter,

[010-018]for on the line of their course every wood-pigeon rose from its perch

[010-019]with a continued clash, dashing its wings against the branches with

[010-020]wellnigh force enough to break every quill. By taking the track of this

[010-021]noise he soon came to a stile.

[010-022]Was it worth while to go farther? He examined the doughy soil at the

[010-023]foot of the stile, and saw among the large sole-and-heel tracks an

[010-024]impression of a slighter kind from a boot that was obviously not local,

[010-025]for Winterborne knew all the cobblers’ patterns in that district,

[010-026]because they were very few to know. The mud-picture was enough to make

[010-027]him swing himself over and proceed.

[010-028]The character of the woodland now changed. The bases of the smaller

[010-029]trees were nibbled bare by rabbits, and at divers points heaps of

[010-030]fresh-made chips, and the newly-cut stool of a tree, stared white

[010-031]through the undergrowth. There had been a large fall of [[timber-economy|timber]] this

[010-032]year, which explained the meaning of some sounds that soon reached him.

[010-033]A voice was shouting intermittently in a sort of human bark, which

[010-034]reminded Giles that there was a sale of trees and fagots that very day.

[010-035][[george-melbury|Melbury]] would naturally be present. Thereupon Winterborne remembered

[010-036]that he himself wanted a few fagots, and entered upon the scene.

[010-037]A large group of buyers stood round the auctioneer, or followed him

[010-038]when, between his pauses, he wandered on from one lot of plantation

[010-039]produce to another, like some philosopher of the Peripatetic school

[010-040]delivering his lectures in the shady groves of the Lyceum. His

[010-041]companions were timber-dealers, yeomen, farmers, villagers, and others;

[010-042]mostly woodland men, who on that account could afford to be curious in

[010-043]their walking-sticks, which consequently exhibited various

[010-044]monstrosities of vegetation, the chief being cork-screw shapes in black

[010-045]and white thorn, brought to that pattern by the slow torture of an

[010-046]encircling woodbine during their growth, as the Chinese have been said

[010-047]to mould human beings into grotesque toys by continued compression in

[010-048]infancy. Two [[gender-roles|women]], wearing men’s jackets on their gowns, conducted in

[010-049]the rear of the halting procession a pony-cart containing a tapped

[010-050]barrel of beer, from which they drew and replenished horns that were

[010-051]handed round, with bread-and-cheese from a basket.

[010-052]The auctioneer adjusted himself to circumstances by using his

[010-053]walking-stick as a hammer, and knocked down the lot on any convenient

[010-054]object that took his fancy, such as the crown of a little boy’s head,

[010-055]or the shoulders of a by-stander who had no business there except to

[010-056]taste the brew; a proceeding which would have been deemed humorous but

[010-057]for the air of stern rigidity which that auctioneer’s face preserved,

[010-058]tending to show that the eccentricity was a result of that absence of

[010-059]mind which is engendered by the press of affairs, and no freak of fancy

[010-060]at all.

[010-061]Mr. Melbury stood slightly apart from the rest of the Peripatetics, and

[010-062]Grace beside him, clinging closely to his arm, her modern attire

[010-063]looking almost odd where everything else was old-fashioned, and

[010-064]throwing over the familiar garniture of the trees a homeliness that

[010-065]seemed to demand improvement by the addition of a few contemporary

[010-066]novelties also. Grace seemed to regard the selling with the interest

[010-067]which attaches to memories revived after an interval of obliviousness.

[010-068]Winterborne went and stood close to them; the timber-merchant spoke,

[010-069]and continued his buying; Grace merely smiled. To justify his presence

[010-070]there Winterborne began bidding for timber and fagots that he did not

[010-071]want, pursuing the occupation in an abstracted mood, in which the

[010-072]auctioneer’s voice seemed to become one of the natural sounds of the

[010-073]woodland. A few flakes of snow descended, at the sight of which a

[010-074]robin, alarmed at these signs of imminent winter, and seeing that no

[010-075]offence was meant by the human invasion, came and perched on the tip of

[010-076]the fagots that were being sold, and looked into the auctioneer’s face,

[010-077]while waiting for some [[fate-and-free-will|chance]] crumb from the bread-basket. Standing a

[010-078]little behind Grace, Winterborne observed how one flake would sail

[010-079]downward and settle on a curl of her hair, and how another would choose

[010-080]her shoulder, and another the edge of her bonnet, which took up so much

[010-081]of his attention that his biddings proceeded incoherently; and when the

[010-082]auctioneer said, every now and then, with a nod towards him, “Yours,

[010-083]Mr. Winterborne,” he had no idea whether he had bought fagots, poles,

[010-084]or logwood.

[010-085]He regretted, with some causticity of humor, that her father should

[010-086]show such inequalities of temperament as to keep Grace tightly on his

[010-087]arm to-day, when he had quite lately seemed anxious to recognize their

[010-088]betrothal as a fact. And thus musing, and joining in no conversation

[010-089]with other buyers except when directly addressed, he followed the

[010-090]assemblage hither and thither till the end of the auction, when Giles

[010-091]for the first time realized what his purchases had been. Hundreds of

[010-092]fagots, and divers lots of timber, had been set down to him, when all

[010-093]he had required had been a few bundles of spray for his odd man Robert

[010-094][[robert-creedle|Creedle]]’s use in baking and lighting fires.

[010-095]Business being over, he turned to speak to the timber merchant. But

[010-096]Melbury’s manner was short and distant; and Grace, too, looked vexed

[010-097]and reproachful. Winterborne then discovered that he had been

[010-098]unwittingly bidding against her father, and picking up his favorite

[010-099]lots in spite of him. With a very few words they left the spot and

[010-100]pursued their way homeward.

[010-101]Giles was extremely sorry at what he had done, and remained standing

[010-102]under the trees, all the other men having strayed silently away. He saw

[010-103]Melbury and his daughter pass down a glade without looking back. While

[010-104]they moved slowly through it a lady appeared on horseback in the middle

[010-105]distance, the line of her progress converging upon that of Melbury’s.

[010-106]They met, Melbury took off his hat, and she reined in her horse. A

[010-107]conversation was evidently in progress between Grace and her father and

[010-108]this equestrian, in whom he was almost sure that he recognized Mrs.

[010-109]Charmond, less by her outline than by the livery of the groom who had

[010-110]halted some yards off.

[010-111]The interlocutors did not part till after a prolonged pause, during

[010-112]which much seemed to be said. When Melbury and Grace resumed their walk

[010-113]it was with something of a lighter tread than before.

[010-114]Winterborne then pursued his own course homeward. He was unwilling to

[010-115]let coldness grow up between himself and the Melburys for any trivial

[010-116]reason, and in the evening he went to their house. On drawing near the

[010-117]gate his attention was attracted by the sight of one of the bedrooms

[010-118]blinking into a state of illumination. In it stood Grace lighting

[010-119]several candles, her right hand elevating the taper, her left hand on

[010-120]her bosom, her face thoughtfully fixed on each wick as it kindled, as

[010-121]if she saw in every flame’s growth the rise of a life to maturity. He

[010-122]wondered what such unusual brilliancy could mean to-night. On getting

[010-123]in-doors he found her father and step-mother in a state of suppressed

[010-124]excitement, which at first he could not comprehend.

[010-125]“I am sorry about my biddings to-day,” said Giles. “I don’t know what I

[010-126]was doing. I have come to say that any of the lots you may require are

[010-127]yours.”

[010-128]“Oh, never mind—never mind,” replied the timber-merchant, with a slight

[010-129]wave of his hand, “I have so much else to think of that I nearly had

[010-130]forgot it. Just now, too, there are matters of a different kind from

[010-131]trade to attend to, so don’t let it concern ye.”

[010-132]As the timber-merchant spoke, as it were, down to him from a higher

[010-133]moral plane than his own, Giles turned to [[mrs-melbury|Mrs. Melbury]].

[010-134]“Grace is going to the House to-morrow,” she said, quietly. “She is

[010-135]looking out her things now. I dare say she is wanting me this minute to

[010-136]assist her.” Thereupon Mrs. Melbury left the room.

[010-137]Nothing is more remarkable than the independent personality of the

[010-138]tongue now and then. Mr. Melbury knew that his words had been a sort of

[010-139]boast. He decried boasting, particularly to Giles; yet whenever the

[010-140]subject was Grace, his judgment resigned the ministry of speech in

[010-141]spite of him.

[010-142]Winterborne felt surprise, pleasure, and also a little apprehension at

[010-143]the news. He repeated Mrs. Melbury’s words.

[010-144]“Yes,” said paternal [[pride-and-humiliation|pride]], not sorry to have dragged out of him what

[010-145]he could not in any circumstances have kept in. “Coming home from the

[010-146]woods this afternoon we met [[felice-charmond|Mrs. Charmond]] out for a ride. She spoke to

[010-147]me on a little matter of business, and then got acquainted with Grace.

[010-148]’Twas wonderful how she took to Grace in a few minutes; that

[010-149]freemasonry of [[education-and-class-mobility|education]] made ’em close at once. Naturally enough she

[010-150]was amazed that such an article—ha, ha!—could come out of my house. At

[010-151]last it led on to Mis’ess Grace being asked to the House. So she’s busy

[010-152]hunting up her frills and furbelows to go in.” As Giles remained in

[010-153]thought without responding, Melbury continued: “But I’ll call her

[010-154]down-stairs.”

[010-155]“No, no; don’t do that, since she’s busy,” said Winterborne.

[010-156]Melbury, feeling from the young man’s manner that his own talk had been

[010-157]too much at Giles and too little to him, repented at once. His face

[010-158]changed, and he said, in lower tones, with an effort, “She’s yours,

[010-159]Giles, as far as I am concerned.”

[010-160]“Thanks—my best thanks....But I think, since it is all right between us

[010-161]about the biddings, that I’ll not interrupt her now. I’ll step

[010-162]homeward, and call another time.”

[010-163]On leaving the house he looked up at the bedroom again. Grace,

[010-164]surrounded by a sufficient number of candles to answer all purposes of

[010-165]self-criticism, was standing before a cheval-glass that her father had

[010-166]lately bought expressly for her use; she was bonneted, cloaked, and

[010-167]gloved, and glanced over her shoulder into the mirror, estimating her

[010-168]aspect. Her face was lit with the natural elation of a young girl

[010-169]hoping to inaugurate on the morrow an intimate acquaintance with a new,

[010-170]interesting, and powerful friend.

[010-171]CHAPTER VIII.

[010-172]The inspiriting appointment which had led [[grace-melbury|Grace Melbury]] to indulge in a

[010-173]six-candle illumination for the arrangement of her attire, carried her

[010-174]over the ground the next morning with a springy tread. Her sense of

[010-175]being properly appreciated on her own native soil seemed to brighten

[010-176]the atmosphere and herbage around her, as the glowworm’s lamp

[010-177]irradiates the grass. Thus she moved along, a vessel of emotion going

[010-178]to empty itself on she knew not what.

[010-179]Twenty minutes’ walking through copses, over a stile, and along an

[010-180]upland lawn brought her to the verge of a deep glen, at the bottom of

[010-181]which [[hintock-house|Hintock House]] appeared immediately beneath her eye. To describe

[010-182]it as standing in a hollow would not express the situation of the

[010-183]manor-house; it stood in a hole, notwithstanding that the hole was full

[010-184]of beauty. From the spot which Grace had reached a stone could easily

[010-185]have been thrown over or into, the birds’-nested chimneys of the

[010-186]mansion. Its walls were surmounted by a battlemented parapet; but the

[010-187]gray lead roofs were quite visible behind it, with their gutters, laps,

[010-188]rolls, and skylights, together with incised letterings and

[010-189]shoe-patterns cut by idlers thereon.
