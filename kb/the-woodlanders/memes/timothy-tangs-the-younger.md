# Timothy Tangs (the younger)

Young Timothy Tangs is the son of old Timothy Tangs the hollow-turner. He works alongside his father as a bottom sawyer in Mr. Melbury's timber pit, representing the generational continuity of woodland trades — a father and son labouring together in the same craft, passing skills and livelihoods down through the family line.

## 身份与背景

Young Timothy Tangs appears as a sawyer working in tandem with his father: they are described together as "Timothy Tangs and young Timothy Tangs, top and bottom sawyers, at work in Mr. Melbury's pit outside" [005-017]. The "top and bottom sawyers" designation indicates the traditional paired arrangement of pit-sawing, where one man stands above the timber and another below, pulling a long two-handled saw. Young Timothy, as the bottom sawyer, would have had the more physically demanding and uncomfortable position, working in the pit beneath the log.

## 性格与特征

The younger Tangs is given one moment of direct speech, when he reflects on the passage of time and Grace Melbury's growth: "It seems no time ago that she was a little playward girl" [005-095]. This remark reveals a character capable of nostalgic reflection — someone who has watched the community change around him and registers the swiftness with which youth passes into adulthood. The comment aligns him with the novel's broader meditation on time, change, and the cycles of rural life.

While his father speaks frequently and participates in discussions about Fitzpiers and the community, young Timothy remains largely silent, defined primarily through his work and his filial relationship.

## 叙事线索

Young Timothy Tangs appears only in the early chapters of the novel, at the opening gathering in Melbury's pit where the Hintock men discuss the arrival of the new doctor [005-017]. His single spoken line — about Grace having been "a little playward girl" — is part of the communal conversation about the Melbury family and the changes they have witnessed [005-095]. He does not reappear in later chapters, his function being primarily to complete the portrait of the Tangs family and the generational structure of woodland labour.

## 意义与解读

Young Timothy Tangs is one of the novel's most minor characters, yet his presence serves an important structural function. The pairing of father and son at the saw-pit embodies the inheritance of trade, skill, and social position that defines the woodlanders' world — a world where sons follow fathers into the same labour, and where the rhythms of work are passed down across generations. His near-silence in the narrative is itself significant: in a novel concerned with class mobility (Grace's education), romantic choice (Grace between Giles and Fitzpiers), and the disruption of traditional patterns, young Timothy represents the unbroken line — the quiet continuity that the novel's central dramas threaten to disrupt.
