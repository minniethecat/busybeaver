# George Melbury

George Melbury is the timber merchant of Little Hintock — "Mr. George Melbury, the timber, bark, and copse-ware merchant" (003-067) — whose ambition for his daughter Grace sets the novel's plot in motion. He is a figure of genuine complexity: a loving father whose love is expressed as social calculation, a successful tradesman whose success breeds aspirations beyond his station, and a man whose good intentions produce catastrophic consequences.

## Biography

### The Timber Merchant of Little Hintock

Melbury's identity is inseparable from his trade. "Mr. Melbury's wagons" (004-050) are a familiar sound in the woodland, their rumbling marking the rhythms of economic life. He is a substantial figure in the local economy, his "timber and copse-ware" business (004-184) employing woodlanders throughout the district and structuring the community's material existence. His homestead — "the whole of Mr. Melbury's homestead" (004-136) — is a physical manifestation of his prosperity and his rootedness in the woodland world.

Melbury's rise to prosperity has not been without cost. Hardy's narrator notes that "Melbury, perhaps, was an unlucky man" (004-016), the qualification suggesting that his success has been achieved despite forces — economic, social, personal — that have worked against him. His first marriage, to Grace's mother, ended with her death; his second, to a woman of "much affection" (006-057), has not produced the emotional fulfillment that his material success might have promised.

### Ambition and Its Consequences

Melbury's defining characteristic is his ambition for Grace. He determines that she "should be well educated" (003-120), sending her to a "boarding-school" (005-143) that will polish her into a gentlewoman. This decision, motivated by love, proves to be the novel's originating error. The education that was meant to secure Grace's future instead alienates her from the woodland world and from Giles Winterborne, the man she was promised to marry.

Melbury's ambition expresses itself in a characteristic pattern of assertion followed by guilt. Having declared "I still mean to do it" (003-155) — referring to his plan to elevate Grace's social position — he finds himself caught between his aspirations for his daughter and his recognition of Giles's worth. The tension between class ambition and personal loyalty defines his character throughout the novel.

### The Marriage to Fitzpiers

Melbury initially endorses Giles as his daughter's suitor, declaring "I want her to marry Giles Winterborne" (003-124). But when Fitzpiers appears — a man of old family and professional status — Melbury's calculations shift. Fitzpiers offers the social elevation that Giles, for all his virtue, cannot provide. Melbury's withdrawal of support from Giles is not malicious but is nonetheless decisive: it removes the economic and social backing that might have enabled Giles to compete with Fitzpiers on equal terms.

When the marriage to Fitzpiers collapses in betrayal, Melbury is consumed by guilt. "It is to me, George Melbury, that you ought to have looked to shelter ye!" (046-078) — his anguished cry registering his recognition that his ambition has harmed the daughter it was meant to help.

## Character and Personality

Melbury is defined by contradiction. He is both shrewd and naive — capable of building a prosperous business yet unable to perceive that Fitzpiers's charm masks moral vacancy. He is loving yet controlling — genuinely devoted to Grace's welfare yet unwilling to trust her judgment. He is a man of his class who aspires beyond it, and the contradiction between his material success and his social insecurity produces a restlessness that proves fatal to everyone he loves.

His relationship with authority is particularly revealing. When he tells Giles, "Nonsense, he's only a doctor" (006-003), he is at once asserting his own status as a substantial citizen and betraying his anxiety about the professional class that Fitzpiers represents. He defers to social status while resenting it, a tension that shapes every decision he makes about Grace's future.

## Role in the Narrative

George Melbury is the novel's prime mover — the character whose decisions generate the plot's central conflicts. It is Melbury who sends Grace to school, Melbury who withdraws support from Giles, Melbury who facilitates the marriage to Fitzpiers, and Melbury who, in the novel's final movement, attempts to undo the damage he has caused. He is, in Hardy's economy, an agent of fate whose agency is always already constrained by the social structures within which he operates.

His function in the novel is not unlike that of King Lear — a father whose love for his daughter is expressed as a determination to control her destiny, with consequences that destroy the very bonds he sought to strengthen. The woodland setting transposes this tragic pattern into the register of Victorian social realism, replacing the kingdom with a timber business and the heath with a copse.

## Relationships

- **[[grace-melbury]]**: His daughter, the object of his ambition and the victim of his miscalculations. His love for her is genuine; his understanding of her is limited.
- **[[giles-winterborne]]**: His business partner and former intended son-in-law. Melbury values Giles's skill and character but ultimately cannot accept him as a match for his educated daughter. His guilt over this betrayal haunts the novel's second half.
- **[[dr-edred-fitzpiers]]**: His son-in-law, whose charm and status initially dazzle Melbury but whose subsequent behavior confirms every anxiety Melbury suppressed during the courtship.

## Critical Analysis

George Melbury embodies the contradictions of the Victorian lower-middle class — prosperous enough to aspire to gentility, but too embedded in trade to achieve it. His ambition for Grace is the engine of the plot, and Hardy's treatment of him is characteristically double: sympathetic to the love that motivates him, unsparing about the damage that love causes.

Melbury represents a specifically Victorian tragedy — the tragedy of social mobility without moral or cultural resources to sustain it. He has the means to send Grace to school but not the wisdom to understand what that education will cost her. He can recognize Fitzpiers's social status but not his moral worthlessness. In this, he is less a villain than a symptom — a representative figure through whom Hardy anatomizes the costs of class aspiration in a society that offers mobility without enlightenment.
