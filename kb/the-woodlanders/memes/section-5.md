# Section 5

[005-001]Before he had left the shed a boy came from the house to ask him to

[005-002]remain till [[george-melbury|Mr. Melbury]] had seen him. [[giles-winterborne|Winterborne]] thereupon crossed

[005-003]over to the spar-house where two or three men were already at work, two

[005-004]of them being travelling spar-makers from White-hart Lane, who, when

[005-005]this kind of work began, made their appearance regularly, and when it

[005-006]was over disappeared in silence till the season came again.

[005-007]Firewood was the one thing abundant in [[little-hintock|Little Hintock]]; and a blaze of

[005-008]gad-cuds made the outhouse gay with its light, which vied with that of

[005-009]the day as yet. In the hollow shades of the roof could be seen dangling

[005-010]etiolated arms of ivy which had crept through the joints of the tiles

[005-011]and were groping in vain for some support, their leaves being dwarfed

[005-012]and sickly for want of sunlight; others were pushing in with such force

[005-013]at the eaves as to lift from their supports the shelves that were fixed

[005-014]there.

[005-015]Besides the itinerant journey-workers there were also present John

[005-016][[john-upjohn|Upjohn]], engaged in the hollow-turnery trade, who lived hard by; old

[005-017][[timothy-tangs|Timothy Tangs]] and [[timothy-tangs-the-younger|young Timothy Tangs]], top and bottom sawyers, at work

[005-018]in Mr. Melbury’s pit outside; [[farmer-bawtree|Farmer Bawtree]], who kept the cider-house,

[005-019]and [[robert-creedle|Robert Creedle]], an old man who worked for Winterborne, and stood

[005-020]warming his hands; these latter being enticed in by the ruddy blaze,

[005-021]though they had no particular business there. None of them call for any

[005-022]remark except, perhaps, Creedle. To have completely described him it

[005-023]would have been necessary to write a military memoir, for he wore under

[005-024]his smock-frock a cast-off soldier’s jacket that had seen hot service,

[005-025]its collar showing just above the flap of the frock; also a hunting

[005-026]memoir, to include the top-boots that he had picked up by [[fate-and-free-will|chance]]; also

[005-027]chronicles of voyaging and shipwreck, for his pocket-knife had been

[005-028]given him by a weather-beaten sailor. But Creedle carried about with

[005-029]him on his uneventful rounds these silent testimonies of war, sport,

[005-030]and adventure, and thought nothing of their associations or their

[005-031]stories.

[005-032]Copse-work, as it was called, being an occupation which the secondary

[005-033]intelligence of the hands and arms could carry on without requiring the

[005-034]sovereign attention of the head, the minds of its professors wandered

[005-035]considerably from the objects before them; hence the tales, chronicles,

[005-036]and ramifications of family history which were recounted here were of a

[005-037]very exhaustive kind, and sometimes so interminable as to defy

[005-038]description.

[005-039]Winterborne, seeing that Melbury had not arrived, stepped back again

[005-040]outside the door; and the conversation interrupted by his momentary

[005-041]presence flowed anew, reaching his ears as an accompaniment to the

[005-042]regular dripping of the fog from the plantation boughs around.

[005-043]The topic at present handled was a highly popular and frequent one—the

[005-044]personal character of [[felice-charmond|Mrs. Charmond]], the owner of the surrounding woods

[005-045]and groves.

[005-046]“My brother-in-law told me, and I have no reason to doubt it,” said

[005-047]Creedle, “that she’d sit down to her dinner with a frock hardly higher

[005-048]than her elbows. ‘Oh, you wicked woman!’ he said to himself when he

[005-049]first see her, ‘you go to your church, and sit, and kneel, as if your

[005-050]knee-jints were greased with very saint’s anointment, and tell off your

[005-051]Hear-us-good-Lords like a business man counting money; and yet you can

[005-052]eat your victuals such a figure as that!’ Whether she’s a reformed

[005-053]character by this time I can’t say; but I don’t care who the man is,

[005-054]that’s how she went on when my brother-in-law lived there.”

[005-055]“Did she do it in her husband’s time?”

[005-056]“That I don’t know—hardly, I should think, considering his temper. Ah!”

[005-057]Here Creedle threw grieved remembrance into physical form by slowly

[005-058]resigning his head to obliquity and letting his eyes water. “That man!

[005-059]‘Not if the angels of heaven come down, Creedle,’ he said, ‘shall you

[005-060]do another day’s work for me!’ Yes—he’d say anything—anything; and

[005-061]would as soon take a winged creature’s name in vain as yours or mine!

[005-062]Well, now I must get these spars home-along, and to-morrow, thank God,

[005-063]I must see about using ’em.”

[005-064]An old woman now entered upon the scene. She was Mr. Melbury’s servant,

[005-065]and passed a great part of her time in crossing the yard between the

[005-066]house-door and the spar-shed, whither she had come now for fuel. She

[005-067]had two facial aspects—one, of a soft and flexible kind, she used

[005-068]indoors when assisting about the parlor or upstairs; the other, with

[005-069]stiff lines and corners, when she was bustling among the men in the

[005-070]spar-house or out-of-doors.

[005-071]“Ah, [[grammer-oliver|Grammer Oliver]],” said John Upjohn, “it do do my heart good to see

[005-072]a old woman like you so dapper and stirring, when I bear in mind that

[005-073]after fifty one year counts as two did afore! But your smoke didn’t

[005-074]rise this morning till twenty minutes past seven by my beater; and

[005-075]that’s late, Grammer Oliver.”

[005-076]“If you was a full-sized man, John, people might take notice of your

[005-077]scornful meanings. But your growing up was such a scrimped and scanty

[005-078]business that really a woman couldn’t feel hurt if you were to spit

[005-079]fire and brimstone itself at her. Here,” she added, holding out a

[005-080]spar-gad to one of the workmen, from which dangled a long

[005-081]black-pudding—“here’s something for thy breakfast, and if you want tea

[005-082]you must fetch it from in-doors.”

[005-083]“Mr. Melbury is late this morning,” said the bottom-sawyer.

[005-084]“Yes. ’Twas a dark dawn,” said Mrs. Oliver. “Even when I opened the

[005-085]door, so late as I was, you couldn’t have told poor men from gentlemen,

[005-086]or John from a reasonable-sized object. And I don’t think maister’s

[005-087]slept at all well to-night. He’s anxious about his daughter; and I know

[005-088]what that is, for I’ve cried bucketfuls for my own.”

[005-089]When the old woman had gone Creedle said,

[005-090]“He’ll fret his gizzard green if he don’t soon hear from that maid of

[005-091]his. Well, learning is better than houses and lands. But to keep a maid

[005-092]at school till she is taller out of pattens than her mother was in

[005-093]’em—’tis tempting Providence.”

[005-094]“It seems no time ago that she was a little playward girl,” said young

[005-095]Timothy Tangs.

[005-096]“I can mind her mother,” said the hollow-turner. “Always a teuny,

[005-097]delicate piece; her touch upon your hand was as soft and cool as wind.

[005-098]She was inoculated for the small-pox and had it beautifully fine, just

[005-099]about the time that I was out of my apprenticeship—ay, and a long

[005-100]apprenticeship ’twas. I served that master of mine six years and three

[005-101]hundred and fourteen days.”

[005-102]The hollow-turner pronounced the days with emphasis, as if, considering

[005-103]their number, they were a rather more remarkable fact than the years.

[005-104]“Mr. Winterborne’s father walked with her at one time,” said old

[005-105]Timothy Tangs. “But Mr. Melbury won her. She was a child of a woman,

[005-106]and would cry like rain if so be he huffed her. Whenever she and her

[005-107]husband came to a puddle in their walks together he’d take her up like

[005-108]a half-penny doll and put her over without dirting her a speck. And if

[005-109]he keeps the daughter so long at boarding-school, he’ll make her as

[005-110]nesh as her mother was. But here he comes.”

[005-111]Just before this moment Winterborne had seen Melbury crossing the court

[005-112]from his door. He was carrying an open letter in his hand, and came

[005-113]straight to Winterborne. His gloom of the preceding night had quite

[005-114]gone.

[005-115]“I’d no sooner made up my mind, Giles, to go and see why Grace didn’t

[005-116]come or write than I get a letter from her—‘Clifton: Wednesday. My dear

[005-117]father,’ says she, ‘I’m coming home to-morrow’ (that’s to-day), ‘but I

[005-118]didn’t think it worth while to write long beforehand.’ The little

[005-119]rascal, and didn’t she! Now, Giles, as you are going to [[sherton-abbas|Sherton]] market

[005-120]to-day with your apple-[[nature-in-the-woodlanders|trees]], why not join me and Grace there, and

[005-121]we’ll drive home all together?”

[005-122]He made the proposal with cheerful energy; he was hardly the same man

[005-123]as the man of the small dark hours. Ever it happens that even among the

[005-124]moodiest the tendency to be cheered is stronger than the tendency to be

[005-125]cast down; and a soul’s specific gravity stands permanently less than

[005-126]that of the sea of troubles into which it is thrown.

[005-127]Winterborne, though not demonstrative, replied to this suggestion with

[005-128]something like alacrity. There was not much doubt that [[marty-south|Marty]]’s grounds

[005-129]for cutting off her hair were substantial enough, if Ambrose’s eyes had

[005-130]been a reason for keeping it on. As for the [[timber-economy|timber]]-merchant, it was

[005-131]plain that his invitation had been given solely in pursuance of his

[005-132]scheme for uniting the pair. He had made up his mind to the course as a

[005-133]duty, and was strenuously bent upon following it out.

[005-134]Accompanied by Winterborne, he now turned towards the door of the

[005-135]spar-house, when his footsteps were heard by the men as aforesaid.

[005-136]“Well, John, and Lot,” he said, nodding as he entered. “A rimy

[005-137]morning.”

[005-138]“’Tis, sir!” said Creedle, energetically; for, not having as yet been

[005-139]able to summon force sufficient to go away and begin work, he felt the

[005-140]necessity of throwing some into his speech. “I don’t care who the man

[005-141]is, ’tis the rimiest morning we’ve had this fall.”

[005-142]“I heard you wondering why I’ve kept my daughter so long at

[005-143]boarding-school,” resumed Mr. Melbury, looking up from the letter which

[005-144]he was reading anew by the fire, and turning to them with the

[005-145]suddenness that was a trait in him. “Hey?” he asked, with affected

[005-146]shrewdness. “But you did, you know. Well, now, though it is my own

[005-147]business more than anybody else’s, I’ll tell ye. When I was a boy,

[005-148]another boy—the pa’son’s son—along with a lot of others, asked me ‘Who

[005-149]dragged Whom round the walls of What?’ and I said, ‘Sam Barrett, who

[005-150]dragged his wife in a chair round the tower corner when she went to be

[005-151]churched.’ They laughed at me with such torrents of scorn that I went

[005-152]home ashamed, and couldn’t sleep for [[pride-and-humiliation|shame]]; and I cried that night till

[005-153]my pillow was wet: till at last I thought to myself there and

[005-154]then—‘They may laugh at me for my ignorance, but that was father’s

[005-155]fault, and none o’ my making, and I must bear it. But they shall never

[005-156]laugh at my children, if I have any: I’ll starve first!’ Thank God,

[005-157]I’ve been able to keep her at school without sacrifice; and her

[005-158]scholarship is such that she stayed on as governess for a time. Let ’em

[005-159]laugh now if they can: Mrs. Charmond herself is not better informed

[005-160]than my girl Grace.”

[005-161]There was something between high indifference and humble emotion in his

[005-162]delivery, which made it difficult for them to reply. Winterborne’s

[005-163]interest was of a kind which did not show itself in words; listening,

[005-164]he stood by the fire, mechanically stirring the embers with a spar-gad.

[005-165]“You’ll be, then, ready, Giles?” Melbury continued, awaking from a

[005-166]reverie. “Well, what was the latest news at [[shottsford-forum|Shottsford]] yesterday, Mr.

[005-167]Bawtree?”

[005-168]“Well, Shottsford is Shottsford still—you can’t victual your carcass

[005-169]there unless you’ve got money; and you can’t buy a cup of genuine

[005-170]there, whether or no....But as the saying is, ‘Go abroad and you’ll

[005-171]hear news of home.’ It seems that our new [[community-and-kinship|neighbor]], this young Dr.

[005-172]What’s-his-name, is a strange, deep, perusing gentleman; and there’s

[005-173]good reason for supposing he has sold his soul to the wicked one.”

[005-174]“’Od name it all,” murmured the timber-merchant, unimpressed by the

[005-175]news, but reminded of other things by the subject of it; “I’ve got to

[005-176]meet a gentleman this very morning, and yet I’ve planned to go to

[005-177]Sherton Abbas for the maid.”

[005-178]“I won’t praise the doctor’s wisdom till I hear what sort of bargain

[005-179]he’s made,” said the top-sawyer.

[005-180]“’Tis only an old woman’s tale,” said Bawtree. “But it seems that he

[005-181]wanted certain books on some mysterious science or black-art, and in

[005-182]order that the people hereabout should not know anything about his dark

[005-183]readings, he ordered ’em direct from [[london-in-the-woodlanders|London]], and not from the Sherton

[005-184]book-seller. The parcel was delivered by mistake at the pa’son’s, and

[005-185]he wasn’t at home; so his wife opened it, and went into hysterics when

[005-186]she read ’em, thinking her husband had turned heathen, and ’twould be

[005-187]the ruin of the children. But when he came he said he knew no more

[005-188]about ’em than she; and found they were this Mr. Fitzpier’s property.

[005-189]So he wrote ‘Beware!’ outside, and sent ’em on by the sexton.”
