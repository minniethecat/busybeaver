# Section 33

[033-001][[george-melbury|Melbury]] and his wife welcomed the twain with [[love-and-desire|affection]], and all the

[033-002]house with deference. They went up to explore their rooms, that opened

[033-003]from a passage on the left hand of the staircase, the entrance to which

[033-004]could be shut off on the landing by a door that Melbury had hung for

[033-005]the purpose. A friendly fire was burning in the grate, although it was

[033-006]not cold. [[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Fitzpiers]] said it was too soon for any sort of meal, they

[033-007]only having dined shortly before leaving [[sherton-abbas|Sherton]]-Abbas. He would walk

[033-008]across to his old lodging, to learn how his locum tenens had got on in

[033-009]his absence.

[033-010]In leaving Melbury’s door he looked back at the house. There was

[033-011]economy in living under that roof, and economy was desirable, but in

[033-012]some way he was dissatisfied with the arrangement; it immersed him so

[033-013]deeply in son-in-lawship to Melbury. He went on to his former

[033-014]residence. His deputy was out, and Fitzpiers fell into conversation

[033-015]with his former landlady.

[033-016]“Well, Mrs. Cox, what’s the best news?” he asked of her, with cheery

[033-017]weariness.

[033-018]She was a little soured at losing by his [[marriage-in-the-woodlanders|marriage]] so profitable a

[033-019]tenant as the surgeon had proved to be during his residence under her

[033-020]roof; and the more so in there being hardly the remotest [[fate-and-free-will|chance]] of her

[033-021]getting such another settler in the [[little-hintock|Hintock]] solitudes. “’Tis what I

[033-022]don’t wish to repeat, sir; least of all to you,” she mumbled.

[033-023]“Never mind me, Mrs. Cox; go ahead.”

[033-024]“It is what people say about your hasty marrying, Dr. Fitzpiers.

[033-025]Whereas they won’t believe you know such clever doctrines in physic as

[033-026]they once supposed of ye, seeing as you could marry into Mr. Melbury’s

[033-027]family, which is only Hintock-born, such as me.”

[033-028]“They are kindly welcome to their opinion,” said Fitzpiers, not

[033-029]allowing himself to recognize that he winced. “Anything else?”

[033-030]“Yes; _she’s_ come home at last.”

[033-031]“Who’s she?”

[033-032]“[[felice-charmond|Mrs. Charmond]].”

[033-033]“Oh, indeed!” said Fitzpiers, with but slight interest. “I’ve never

[033-034]seen her.”

[033-035]“She has seen you, sir, whether or no.”

[033-036]“Never.”

[033-037]“Yes; she saw you in some hotel or street for a minute or two while you

[033-038]were away travelling, and accidentally heard your name; and when she

[033-039]made some remark about you, Miss Ellis—that’s her maid—told her you was

[033-040]on your wedding-tower with Mr. Melbury’s daughter; and she said, ‘He

[033-041]ought to have done better than that. I fear he has spoiled his

[033-042]chances,’ she says.”

[033-043]Fitzpiers did not talk much longer to this cheering housewife, and

[033-044]walked home with no very brisk step. He entered the door quietly, and

[033-045]went straight up-stairs to the drawing-room extemporized for their use

[033-046]by Melbury in his and his bride’s absence, expecting to find her there

[033-047]as he had left her. The fire was burning still, but there were no

[033-048]lights. He looked into the next apartment, fitted up as a little

[033-049]dining-room, but no supper was laid. He went to the top of the stairs,

[033-050]and heard a chorus of voices in the [[timber-economy|timber]]-merchant’s parlor below,

[033-051]Grace’s being occasionally intermingled.

[033-052]Descending, and looking into the room from the door-way, he found quite

[033-053]a large gathering of neighbors and other acquaintances, praising and

[033-054]congratulating [[grace-melbury|Mrs. Fitzpiers]] on her return, among them being the

[033-055]dairyman, [[farmer-bawtree|Farmer Bawtree]], and the master-blacksmith from [[great-hintock|Great Hintock]];

[033-056]also the cooper, the hollow-turner, the exciseman, and some others,

[033-057]with their wives, who lived hard by. Grace, girl that she was, had

[033-058]quite forgotten her new [[pride-and-humiliation|dignity]] and her husband’s; she was in the midst

[033-059]of them, blushing, and receiving their compliments with all the

[033-060]pleasure of old-comradeship.

[033-061]Fitzpiers experienced a profound distaste for the situation. Melbury

[033-062]was nowhere in the room, but Melbury’s wife, perceiving the doctor,

[033-063]came to him. “We thought, Grace and I,” she said, “that as they have

[033-064]called, hearing you were come, we could do no less than ask them to

[033-065]supper; and then Grace proposed that we should all sup together, as it

[033-066]is the first night of your return.”

[033-067]By this time Grace had come round to him. “Is it not good of them to

[033-068]welcome me so warmly?” she exclaimed, with tears of friendship in her

[033-069]eyes. “After so much good feeling I could not think of our shutting

[033-070]ourselves up away from them in our own dining-room.”

[033-071]“Certainly not—certainly not,” said Fitzpiers; and he entered the room

[033-072]with the heroic smile of a martyr.

[033-073]As soon as they sat down to table Melbury came in, and seemed to see at

[033-074]once that Fitzpiers would much rather have received no such

[033-075]demonstrative reception. He thereupon privately chid his wife for her

[033-076]forwardness in the matter. [[mrs-melbury|Mrs. Melbury]] declared that it was as much

[033-077]Grace’s doing as hers, after which there was no more to be said by that

[033-078]young woman’s tender father. By this time Fitzpiers was making the best

[033-079]of his position among the wide-elbowed and genial company who sat

[033-080]eating and drinking and laughing and joking around him; and getting

[033-081]warmed himself by the good cheer, was obliged to admit that, after all,

[033-082]the supper was not the least enjoyable he had ever known.

[033-083]At times, however, the words about his having spoiled his

[033-084]opportunities, repeated to him as those of Mrs. Charmond, haunted him

[033-085]like a handwriting on the wall. Then his manner would become suddenly

[033-086]abstracted. At one moment he would mentally put an indignant query why

[033-087]Mrs. Charmond or any other woman should make it her business to have

[033-088]opinions about his opportunities; at another he thought that he could

[033-089]hardly be angry with her for taking an interest in the doctor of her

[033-090]own parish. Then he would drink a glass of grog and so get rid of the

[033-091]misgiving. These hitches and quaffings were soon perceived by Grace as

[033-092]well as by her father; and hence both of them were much relieved when

[033-093]the first of the guests to discover that the hour was growing late rose

[033-094]and declared that he must think of moving homeward. At the words

[033-095]Melbury rose as alertly as if lifted by a spring, and in ten minutes

[033-096]they were gone.

[033-097]“Now, Grace,” said her husband as soon as he found himself alone with

[033-098]her in their private apartments, “we’ve had a very pleasant evening,

[033-099]and everybody has been very kind. But we must come to an understanding

[033-100]about our way of living here. If we continue in these rooms there must

[033-101]be no mixing in with your people below. I can’t stand it, and that’s

[033-102]the truth.”

[033-103]She had been sadly surprised at the suddenness of his distaste for

[033-104]those old-fashioned [[nature-in-the-woodlanders|woodland]] forms of life which in his courtship he

[033-105]had professed to regard with so much interest. But she assented in a

[033-106]moment.

[033-107]“We must be simply your father’s tenants,” he continued, “and our

[033-108]goings and comings must be as independent as if we lived elsewhere.”

[033-109]“Certainly, Edgar—I quite see that it must be so.”

[033-110]“But you joined in with all those people in my absence, without knowing

[033-111]whether I should approve or disapprove. When I came I couldn’t help

[033-112]myself at all.”

[033-113]She, sighing: “Yes—I see I ought to have waited; though they came

[033-114]unexpectedly, and I thought I had acted for the best.”

[033-115]Thus the discussion ended, and the next day Fitzpiers went on his old

[033-116]rounds as usual. But it was easy for so super-subtle an eye as his to

[033-117]discern, or to think he discerned, that he was no longer regarded as an

[033-118]extrinsic, unfathomed gentleman of limitless potentiality, scientific

[033-119]and social; but as Mr. Melbury’s compeer, and therefore in a degree

[033-120]only one of themselves. The Hintock woodlandlers held with all the

[033-121]strength of inherited conviction to the aristocratic principle, and as

[033-122]soon as they had discovered that Fitzpiers was one of the old Buckbury

[033-123]Fitzpierses they had accorded to him for nothing a touching of

[033-124]hat-brims, promptness of service, and deference of approach, which

[033-125]Melbury had to do without, though he paid for it over and over. But

[033-126]now, having proved a traitor to his own cause by this marriage,

[033-127]Fitzpiers was believed in no more as a superior hedged by his own

[033-128]divinity; while as doctor he began to be rated no higher than old

[033-129]Jones, whom they had so long despised.

[033-130]His few patients seemed in his two months’ absence to have dwindled

[033-131]considerably in number, and no sooner had he returned than there came

[033-132]to him from the Board of Guardians a complaint that a pauper had been

[033-133]neglected by his substitute. In a fit of pride Fitzpiers resigned his

[033-134]appointment as one of the surgeons to the union, which had been the

[033-135]nucleus of his practice here.

[033-136]At the end of a fortnight he came in-doors one evening to Grace more

[033-137]briskly than usual. “They have written to me again about that practice

[033-138]in [[budmouth|Budmouth]] that I once negotiated for,” he said to her. “The premium

[033-139]asked is eight hundred pounds, and I think that between your father and

[033-140]myself it ought to be raised. Then we can get away from this place

[033-141]forever.”

[033-142]The question had been mooted between them before, and she was not

[033-143]unprepared to consider it. They had not proceeded far with the

[033-144]discussion when a knock came to the door, and in a minute Grammer ran

[033-145]up to say that a message had arrived from [[hintock-house|Hintock House]] requesting Dr.

[033-146]Fitzpiers to attend there at once. Mrs. Charmond had met with a slight

[033-147]accident through the overturning of her carriage.

[033-148]“This is something, anyhow,” said Fitzpiers, rising with an interest

[033-149]which he could not have defined. “I have had a presentiment that this

[033-150]mysterious woman and I were to be better acquainted.”

[033-151]The latter words were murmured to himself alone.

[033-152]“Good-night,” said Grace, as soon as he was ready. “I shall be asleep,

[033-153]probably, when you return.”

[033-154]“Good-night,” he replied, inattentively, and went down-stairs. It was

[033-155]the first time since their marriage that he had left her without a

[033-156]kiss.

[033-157]CHAPTER XXVI.

[033-158][[giles-winterborne|Winterborne]]’s house had been pulled down. On this account his face had

[033-159]been seen but fitfully in Hintock; and he would probably have

[033-160]disappeared from the place altogether but for his slight business

[033-161]connection with Melbury, on whose premises Giles kept his [[orchard-culture|cider-making]]

[033-162]apparatus, now that he had no place of his own to stow it in. Coming

[033-163]here one evening on his way to a hut beyond the wood where he now

[033-164]slept, he noticed that the familiar brown-thatched pinion of his

[033-165]paternal roof had vanished from its site, and that the walls were

[033-166]levelled. In present circumstances he had a feeling for the spot that

[033-167]might have been called morbid, and when he had supped in the hut

[033-168]aforesaid he made use of the spare hour before bedtime to return to

[033-169]Little Hintock in the twilight and ramble over the patch of ground on

[033-170]which he had first seen the day.

[033-171]He repeated this evening visit on several like occasions. Even in the

[033-172]gloom he could trace where the different rooms had stood; could mark

[033-173]the shape of the kitchen chimney-corner, in which he had roasted apples

[033-174]and potatoes in his boyhood, cast his bullets, and burned his initials

[033-175]on articles that did and did not belong to him. The apple-trees still

[033-176]remained to show where the garden had been, the oldest of them even now

[033-177]retaining the crippled slant to north-east given them by the great

[033-178]November gale of 1824, which carried a brig bodily over the Chesil

[033-179]Bank. They were at present bent to still greater obliquity by the

[033-180]heaviness of their produce. Apples bobbed against his head, and in the

[033-181]grass beneath he crunched scores of them as he walked. There was nobody

[033-182]to gather them now.

[033-183]It was on the evening under notice that, half sitting, half leaning

[033-184]against one of these inclined trunks, Winterborne had become lost in

[033-185]his thoughts, as usual, till one little star after another had taken up

[033-186]a position in the piece of sky which now confronted him where his walls

[033-187]and chimneys had formerly raised their outlines. The house had jutted

[033-188]awkwardly into the road, and the opening caused by its absence was very

[033-189]distinct.
