# Melbury's House

Melbury's house occupies a distinctive position in Hintock's physical and social geography. It is not a cottage like those of the woodland laborers, nor a manor house like Mrs. Charmond's Hintock House — it is a tradesman's residence, substantial and comfortable, that announces its owner's prosperity while also marking the limits of his social standing.

## Architecture and Position

The house sits adjacent to Melbury's timber yard — one could "enter the latter from the timber-merchant's back door" (003-085). This adjacency of domestic space and commercial operation is the defining feature of Melbury's house. Unlike the gentry, who separate their residences from the sources of their wealth, the timber merchant lives in and among his business. The house is comfortable — Melbury's prosperity is real — but it can never be genteel, because the smell of timber and the sounds of the yard are always present.

## The House as Social Marker

Melbury's house is the site of the novel's most consequential social transactions. It is here that Fitzpiers first encounters Grace; here that the courtship unfolds; here that Grace returns after the failure of her marriage. The house witnesses the entire arc of the novel's central plot — from hope through catastrophe to the ambiguous resolution of the ending. Its rooms contain the aspirations, the deceptions, and the grief that constitute the novel's emotional core.

## Domestic Life and Trade

Hardy uses Melbury's house to explore the relationship between domesticity and commerce in rural life. The house is well-appointed — Melbury has "spent heavy sums" on its furnishings as well as on Grace's education — but its comfort is the product of trade, not inheritance. This fact haunts the novel's treatment of the Melbury family: their prosperity is earned, and therefore fragile, and therefore anxious.
