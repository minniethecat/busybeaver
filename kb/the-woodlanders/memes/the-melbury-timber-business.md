# The Melbury Timber Business

George Melbury's timber enterprise is the economic engine of Little Hintock. As "the timber, bark, and copse-ware merchant" (003-067), Melbury employs much of the village's male population, shapes its seasonal rhythms, and generates the wealth that funds Grace's education — the investment that drives the novel's central plot.

## The Business

Melbury's operation encompasses the full cycle of woodland commerce: the felling of mature timber for construction, the stripping of oak bark for the tanning trade, and the cutting of copse-wood for hurdles, thatching spars, and fuel. The timber yard adjacent to his house — one could "enter the latter from the timber-merchant's back door" (003-085) — is the physical center of the enterprise, where "stacking timber, fagots, bundles, and other products" (004-162) occupy the yard.

The business operates on a scale that makes Melbury the most substantial employer in the district while still falling short of gentry status. His wealth is real — sufficient to spend "several years upon her education" (014-190) — but it is the wealth of trade, not land, and therefore carries no social elevation.

## Seasonal Rhythms

The timber business follows the seasonal calendar of the woodland. Spring brings the barking season, when oak bark is stripped for the tanneries; winter is the time for felling, when the sap is down and the timber will season properly. The novel's plot is keyed to these rhythms — the opening action occurs during the barking season, establishing immediately the inseparability of human drama from economic cycle.

## Labor and Hierarchy

The business creates a hierarchy of labor: at the top, Melbury as proprietor and merchant; below him, skilled workers like Giles Winterborne who understand timber and orchard work; and at the base, the laborers who do the heaviest work of felling and hauling. This hierarchy maps onto the novel's class structure with precision.

## Economic Precarity

Hardy is attentive to the precarity of the timber trade. Weather, market prices, and the health of the woodland itself all affect Melbury's fortunes. When his economic position weakens, the effects ripple through the entire community — a reminder that in Hintock, the fate of the woodland and the fate of its people are one.
