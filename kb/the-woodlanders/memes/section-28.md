# Section 28

[028-001]“I am an Italianized American, a South Carolinian by birth,” he said.

[028-002]“I left my native country on the failure of the Southern cause, and

[028-003]have never returned to it since.”

[028-004]He spoke no more about himself, and they came to the verge of the wood.

[028-005]Here, striding over the fence out upon the upland sward, they could at

[028-006]once see the chimneys of the house in the gorge immediately beneath

[028-007]their position, silent, still, and pale.

[028-008]“Can you tell me the time?” the gentleman asked. “My watch has

[028-009]stopped.”

[028-010]“It is between twelve and one,” said [[giles-winterborne|Giles]].

[028-011]His companion expressed his astonishment. “I thought it between nine

[028-012]and ten at latest! Dear me—dear me!”

[028-013]He now begged Giles to return, and offered him a gold coin, which

[028-014]looked like a sovereign, for the assistance rendered. Giles declined to

[028-015]accept anything, to the surprise of the stranger, who, on putting the

[028-016]money back into his pocket, said, awkwardly, “I offered it because I

[028-017]want you to utter no word about this meeting with me. Will you

[028-018]promise?”

[028-019]Winterborne promised readily. He thereupon stood still while the other

[028-020]ascended the slope. At the bottom he looked back dubiously. Giles would

[028-021]no longer remain when he was so evidently desired to leave, and

[028-022]returned through the boughs to [[little-hintock|Hintock]].

[028-023]He suspected that this man, who seemed so distressed and melancholy,

[028-024]might be that lover and persistent wooer of [[felice-charmond|Mrs. Charmond]] whom he had

[028-025]heard so frequently spoken of, and whom it was said she had treated

[028-026]cavalierly. But he received no confirmation of his suspicion beyond a

[028-027]report which reached him a few days later that a gentleman had called

[028-028]up the servants who were taking care of [[hintock-house|Hintock House]] at an hour past

[028-029]midnight; and on learning that Mrs. Charmond, though returned from

[028-030]abroad, was as yet in [[london-in-the-woodlanders|London]], he had sworn bitterly, and gone away

[028-031]without leaving a card or any trace of himself.

[028-032]The girls who related the story added that he sighed three times before

[028-033]he swore, but this part of the narrative was not corroborated. Anyhow,

[028-034]such a gentleman had driven away from the hotel at [[sherton-abbas|Sherton]] next day in

[028-035]a carriage hired at that inn.

[028-036]CHAPTER XXII.

[028-037]The sunny, leafy week which followed the tender doings of Midsummer Eve

[028-038]brought a visitor to [[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Fitzpiers]]’s door; a voice that he knew sounded in

[028-039]the passage. [[george-melbury|Mr. Melbury]] had called. At first he had a particular

[028-040]objection to enter the parlor, because his boots were dusty, but as the

[028-041]surgeon insisted he waived the point and came in.

[028-042]Looking neither to the right nor to the left, hardly at Fitzpiers

[028-043]himself, he put his hat under his chair, and with a preoccupied gaze at

[028-044]the floor, he said, “I’ve called to ask you, doctor, quite privately, a

[028-045]question that troubles me. I’ve a daughter, Grace, an only daughter, as

[028-046]you may have heard. Well, she’s been out in the dew—on Midsummer Eve in

[028-047]particular she went out in thin slippers to watch some vagary of the

[028-048]Hintock maids—and she’s got a cough, a distinct hemming and hacking,

[028-049]that makes me uneasy. Now, I have decided to send her away to some

[028-050]seaside place for a change—”

[028-051]“Send her away!” Fitzpiers’s countenance had fallen.

[028-052]“Yes. And the question is, where would you advise me to send her?”

[028-053]The [[timber-economy|timber]]-merchant had happened to call at a moment when Fitzpiers was

[028-054]at the spring-tide of a sentiment that Grace was a necessity of his

[028-055]existence. The sudden pressure of her form upon his breast as she came

[028-056]headlong round the bush had never ceased to linger with him, ever since

[028-057]he adopted the manoeuvre for which the hour and the moonlight and the

[028-058]occasion had been the only excuse. Now she was to be sent away.

[028-059]Ambition? it could be postponed. Family? culture and reciprocity of

[028-060]tastes had taken the place of family nowadays. He allowed himself to be

[028-061]carried forward on the wave of his [[love-and-desire|desire]].

[028-062]“How strange, how very strange it is,” he said, “that you should have

[028-063]come to me about her just now. I have been thinking every day of coming

[028-064]to you on the very same errand.”

[028-065]“Ah!—you have noticed, too, that her health——”

[028-066]“I have noticed nothing the matter with her health, because there is

[028-067]nothing. But, Mr. Melbury, I have seen your daughter several times by

[028-068]accident. I have admired her infinitely, and I was coming to ask you if

[028-069]I may become better acquainted with her—pay my addresses to her?”

[028-070]Melbury was looking down as he listened, and did not see the air of

[028-071]half-misgiving at his own rashness that spread over Fitzpiers’s face as

[028-072]he made this declaration.

[028-073]“You have—got to know her?” said Melbury, a spell of dead silence

[028-074]having preceded his utterance, during which his emotion rose with

[028-075]almost visible effect.

[028-076]“Yes,” said Fitzpiers.

[028-077]“And you wish to become better acquainted with her? You mean with a

[028-078]view to [[marriage-in-the-woodlanders|marriage]]—of course that is what you mean?”

[028-079]“Yes,” said the young man. “I mean, get acquainted with her, with a

[028-080]view to being her accepted lover; and if we suited each other, what

[028-081]would naturally follow.”

[028-082]The timber-merchant was much surprised, and fairly agitated; his hand

[028-083]trembled as he laid by his walking-stick. “This takes me unawares,”

[028-084]said he, his voice wellnigh breaking down. “I don’t mean that there is

[028-085]anything unexpected in a gentleman being attracted by her; but it did

[028-086]not occur to me that it would be you. I always said,” continued he,

[028-087]with a lump in his throat, “that my Grace would make a mark at her own

[028-088]level some day. That was why I educated her. I said to myself, ‘I’ll do

[028-089]it, cost what it may;’ though her mother-law was pretty frightened at

[028-090]my paying out so much money year after year. I knew it would tell in

[028-091]the end. ‘Where you’ve not good material to work on, such doings would

[028-092]be waste and vanity,’ I said. ‘But where you have that material it is

[028-093]sure to be worth while.’”

[028-094]“I am glad you don’t object,” said Fitzpiers, almost wishing that Grace

[028-095]had not been quite so cheap for him.

[028-096]“If she is willing I don’t object, certainly. Indeed,” added the honest

[028-097]man, “it would be deceit if I were to pretend to feel anything else

[028-098]than highly honored personally; and it is a great credit to her to have

[028-099]drawn to her a man of such good professional station and venerable old

[028-100]family. That huntsman-fellow little thought how wrong he was about her!

[028-101]Take her and welcome, sir.”

[028-102]“I’ll endeavor to ascertain her mind.”

[028-103]“Yes, yes. But she will be agreeable, I should think. She ought to be.”

[028-104]“I hope she may. Well, now you’ll expect to see me frequently.”

[028-105]“Oh yes. But, name it all—about her cough, and her going away. I had

[028-106]quite forgot that that was what I came about.”

[028-107]“I assure you,” said the surgeon, “that her cough can only be the

[028-108]result of a slight cold, and it is not necessary to banish her to any

[028-109]seaside place at all.”

[028-110]Melbury looked unconvinced, doubting whether he ought to take

[028-111]Fitzpiers’s professional opinion in circumstances which naturally led

[028-112]him to wish to keep her there. The doctor saw this, and honestly

[028-113]dreading to lose sight of her, he said, eagerly, “Between ourselves, if

[028-114]I am successful with her I will take her away myself for a month or

[028-115]two, as soon as we are married, which I hope will be before the chilly

[028-116]weather comes on. This will be so very much better than letting her go

[028-117]now.”

[028-118]The proposal pleased Melbury much. There could be hardly any danger in

[028-119]postponing any desirable change of air as long as the warm weather

[028-120]lasted, and for such a reason. Suddenly recollecting himself, he said,

[028-121]“Your time must be precious, doctor. I’ll get home-along. I am much

[028-122]obliged to ye. As you will see her often, you’ll discover for yourself

[028-123]if anything serious is the matter.”

[028-124]“I can assure you it is nothing,” said Fitzpiers, who had seen Grace

[028-125]much oftener already than her father knew of.

[028-126]When he was gone Fitzpiers paused, silent, registering his sensations,

[028-127]like a man who has made a plunge for a pearl into a medium of which he

[028-128]knows not the density or temperature. But he had done it, and Grace was

[028-129]the sweetest girl alive.

[028-130]As for the departed visitor, his own last words lingered in Melbury’s

[028-131]ears as he walked homeward; he felt that what he had said in the

[028-132]emotion of the moment was very stupid, ungenteel, and unsuited to a

[028-133]dialogue with an educated gentleman, the smallness of whose practice

[028-134]was more than compensated by the former greatness of his family. He had

[028-135]uttered thoughts before they were weighed, and almost before they were

[028-136]shaped. They had expressed in a certain sense his feeling at

[028-137]Fitzpiers’s news, but yet they were not right. Looking on the ground,

[028-138]and planting his stick at each tread as if it were a flag-staff, he

[028-139]reached his own precincts, where, as he passed through the court, he

[028-140]automatically stopped to look at the men working in the shed and

[028-141]around. One of them asked him a question about wagon-spokes.

[028-142]“Hey?” said Melbury, looking hard at him. The man repeated the words.

[028-143]Melbury stood; then turning suddenly away without answering, he went up

[028-144]the court and entered the house. As time was no object with the

[028-145]journeymen, except as a thing to get past, they leisurely surveyed the

[028-146]door through which he had disappeared.

[028-147]“What maggot has the gaffer got in his head now?” said Tangs the elder.

[028-148]“Sommit to do with that chiel of his! When you’ve got a maid of yer

[028-149]own, [[john-upjohn|John Upjohn]], that costs ye what she costs him, that will take the

[028-150]squeak out of your Sunday shoes, John! But you’ll never be tall enough

[028-151]to accomplish such as she; and ’tis a lucky thing for ye, John, as

[028-152]things be. Well, he ought to have a dozen—that would bring him to

[028-153]reason. I see ’em walking together last Sunday, and when they came to a

[028-154]puddle he lifted her over like a halfpenny doll. He ought to have a

[028-155]dozen; he’d let ’em walk through puddles for themselves then.”

[028-156]Meanwhile Melbury had entered the house with the look of a man who sees

[028-157]a vision before him. His wife was in the room. Without taking off his

[028-158]hat he sat down at random.

[028-159]“Luce—we’ve done it!” he said. “Yes—the thing is as I expected. The

[028-160]spell, that I foresaw might be worked, has worked. She’s done it, and

[028-161]done it well. Where is she—Grace, I mean?”

[028-162]“Up in her room—what has happened!”

[028-163]Mr. Melbury explained the circumstances as coherently as he could. “I

[028-164]told you so,” he said. “A maid like her couldn’t stay hid long, even in

[028-165]a place like this. But where is Grace? Let’s have her down.

[028-166]Here—Gra-a-ace!”

[028-167]She appeared after a reasonable interval, for she was sufficiently

[028-168]spoiled by this father of hers not to put herself in a hurry, however

[028-169]impatient his tones. “What is it, father?” said she, with a smile.

[028-170]“Why, you scamp, what’s this you’ve been doing? Not home here more than

[028-171]six months, yet, instead of confining yourself to your father’s rank,

[028-172]making havoc in the educated classes.”

[028-173]Though accustomed to show herself instantly appreciative of her

[028-174]father’s meanings, Grace was fairly unable to look anyhow but at a loss

[028-175]now.

[028-176]“No, no—of course you don’t know what I mean, or you pretend you don’t;

[028-177]though, for my part, I believe [[gender-roles|women]] can see these things through a

[028-178]double hedge. But I suppose I must tell ye. Why, you’ve flung your

[028-179]grapnel over the doctor, and he’s coming courting forthwith.”

[028-180]“Only think of that, my dear! Don’t you feel it a triumph?” said Mrs.

[028-181]Melbury.

[028-182]“Coming courting! I’ve done nothing to make him,” Grace exclaimed.
