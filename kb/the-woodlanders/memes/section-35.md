# Section 35

[035-001]On entering [[little-hintock|Little Hintock]] he found himself regarding it in a new

[035-002]way—from the [[hintock-house|Hintock House]] point of view rather than from his own and

[035-003]the Melburys’. The household had all gone to bed, and as he went

[035-004]up-stairs he heard the snore of the [[timber-economy|timber]]-merchant from his quarter of

[035-005]the building, and turned into the passage communicating with his own

[035-006]rooms in a strange access of sadness. A light was burning for him in

[035-007]the chamber; but Grace, though in bed, was not asleep. In a moment her

[035-008]sympathetic voice came from behind the curtains.

[035-009]“Edgar, is she very seriously hurt?”

[035-010][[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Fitzpiers]] had so entirely lost sight of [[felice-charmond|Mrs. Charmond]] as a patient that

[035-011]he was not on the instant ready with a reply.

[035-012]“Oh no,” he said. “There are no bones broken, but she is shaken. I am

[035-013]going again to-morrow.”

[035-014]Another inquiry or two, and Grace said,

[035-015]“Did she ask for me?”

[035-016]“Well—I think she did—I don’t quite remember; but I am under the

[035-017]impression that she spoke of you.”

[035-018]“Cannot you recollect at all what she said?”

[035-019]“I cannot, just this minute.”

[035-020]“At any rate she did not talk much about me?” said Grace with

[035-021]disappointment.

[035-022]“Oh no.”

[035-023]“But you did, perhaps,” she added, innocently fishing for a compliment.

[035-024]“Oh yes—you may depend upon that!” replied he, warmly, though scarcely

[035-025]thinking of what he was saying, so vividly was there present to his

[035-026]mind the personality of Mrs. Charmond.

[035-027]CHAPTER XXVII.

[035-028]The doctor’s professional visit to Hintock House was promptly repeated

[035-029]the next day and the next. He always found Mrs. Charmond reclining on a

[035-030]sofa, and behaving generally as became a patient who was in no great

[035-031]hurry to lose that title. On each occasion he looked gravely at the

[035-032]little scratch on her arm, as if it had been a serious wound.

[035-033]He had also, to his further satisfaction, found a slight scar on her

[035-034]temple, and it was very convenient to put a piece of black plaster on

[035-035]this conspicuous part of her person in preference to gold-beater’s

[035-036]skin, so that it might catch the eyes of the servants, and make his

[035-037]presence appear decidedly necessary, in case there should be any doubt

[035-038]of the fact.

[035-039]“Oh—you hurt me!” she exclaimed one day.

[035-040]He was peeling off the bit of plaster on her arm, under which the

[035-041]scrape had turned the color of an unripe blackberry previous to

[035-042]vanishing altogether. “Wait a moment, then—I’ll damp it,” said

[035-043]Fitzpiers. He put his lips to the place and kept them there till the

[035-044]plaster came off easily. “It was at your request I put it on,” said he.

[035-045]“I know it,” she replied. “Is that blue vein still in my temple that

[035-046]used to show there? The scar must be just upon it. If the cut had been

[035-047]a little deeper it would have spilt my hot blood indeed!” Fitzpiers

[035-048]examined so closely that his breath touched her tenderly, at which

[035-049]their eyes rose to an encounter—hers showing themselves as deep and

[035-050]mysterious as interstellar space. She turned her face away suddenly.

[035-051]“Ah! none of that! none of that—I cannot coquet with you!” she cried.

[035-052]“Don’t suppose I consent to for one moment. Our poor, brief, youthful

[035-053]hour of [[love-and-desire|love]]-making was too long ago to bear continuing now. It is as

[035-054]well that we should understand each other on that point before we go

[035-055]further.”

[035-056]“Coquet! Nor I with you. As it was when I found the historic gloves, so

[035-057]it is now. I might have been and may be foolish; but I am no trifler. I

[035-058]naturally cannot forget that little space in which I flitted across the

[035-059]field of your vision in those days of the past, and the recollection

[035-060]opens up all sorts of imaginings.”

[035-061]“Suppose my mother had not taken me away?” she murmured, her dreamy

[035-062]eyes resting on the swaying tip of a distant tree.

[035-063]“I should have seen you again.”

[035-064]“And then?”

[035-065]“Then the fire would have burned higher and higher. What would have

[035-066]immediately followed I know not; but sorrow and sickness of heart at

[035-067]last.”

[035-068]“Why?”

[035-069]“Well—that’s the end of all love, according to Nature’s law. I can give

[035-070]no other reason.”

[035-071]“Oh, don’t speak like that,” she exclaimed. “Since we are only

[035-072]picturing the possibilities of that time, don’t, for pity’s sake, spoil

[035-073]the picture.” Her voice sank almost to a whisper as she added, with an

[035-074]incipient pout upon her full lips, “Let me think at least that if you

[035-075]had really loved me at all seriously, you would have loved me for ever

[035-076]and ever!”

[035-077]“You are right—think it with all your heart,” said he. “It is a

[035-078]pleasant thought, and costs nothing.”

[035-079]She weighed that remark in silence a while. “Did you ever hear anything

[035-080]of me from then till now?” she inquired.

[035-081]“Not a word.”

[035-082]“So much the better. I had to fight the battle of life as well as you.

[035-083]I may tell you about it some day. But don’t ever ask me to do it, and

[035-084]particularly do not press me to tell you now.”

[035-085]Thus the two or three days that they had spent in tender acquaintance

[035-086]on the romantic slopes above the Neckar were stretched out in

[035-087]retrospect to the length and importance of years; made to form a canvas

[035-088]for infinite fancies, idle dreams, luxurious melancholies, and sweet,

[035-089]alluring assertions which could neither be proved nor disproved. Grace

[035-090]was never mentioned between them, but a rumor of his proposed domestic

[035-091]changes somehow reached her ears.

[035-092]“Doctor, you are going away,” she exclaimed, confronting him with

[035-093]accusatory reproach in her large dark eyes no less than in her rich

[035-094]cooing voice. “Oh yes, you are,” she went on, springing to her feet

[035-095]with an air which might almost have been called passionate. “It is no

[035-096]use denying it. You have bought a practice at [[budmouth|Budmouth]]. I don’t blame

[035-097]you. Nobody can live at Hintock—least of all a professional man who

[035-098]wants to keep abreast of recent discovery. And there is nobody here to

[035-099]induce such a one to stay for other reasons. That’s right, that’s

[035-100]right—go away!”

[035-101]“But no, I have not actually bought the practice as yet, though I am

[035-102]indeed in treaty for it. And, my dear friend, if I continue to feel

[035-103]about the business as I feel at this moment—perhaps I may conclude

[035-104]never to go at all.”

[035-105]“But you hate Hintock, and everybody and everything in it that you

[035-106]don’t mean to take away with you?”

[035-107]Fitzpiers contradicted this idea in his most vibratory tones, and she

[035-108]lapsed into the frivolous archness under which she hid passions of no

[035-109]mean strength—strange, smouldering, erratic passions, kept down like a

[035-110]stifled conflagration, but bursting out now here, now there—the only

[035-111]certain element in their direction being its unexpectedness. If one

[035-112]word could have expressed her it would have been Inconsequence. She was

[035-113]a woman of perversities, delighting in frequent contrasts. She liked

[035-114]mystery, in her life, in her love, in her history. To be fair to her,

[035-115]there was nothing in the latter which she had any great reason to be

[035-116]ashamed of, and many things of which she might have been proud; but it

[035-117]had never been fathomed by the honest minds of Hintock, and she rarely

[035-118]volunteered her experiences. As for her capricious [[nature-in-the-woodlanders|nature]], the people

[035-119]on her estates grew accustomed to it, and with that marvellous subtlety

[035-120]of contrivance in steering round odd tempers, that is found in sons of

[035-121]the soil and dependants generally, they managed to get along under her

[035-122]government rather better than they would have done beneath a more

[035-123]equable rule.

[035-124]Now, with regard to the doctor’s notion of leaving Hintock, he had

[035-125]advanced further towards completing the purchase of the Budmouth

[035-126]surgeon’s good-will than he had admitted to Mrs. Charmond. The whole

[035-127]matter hung upon what he might do in the ensuing twenty-four hours. The

[035-128]evening after leaving her he went out into the lane, and walked and

[035-129]pondered between the high hedges, now greenish-white with wild

[035-130]clematis—here called “old-man’s beard,” from its aspect later in the

[035-131]year.

[035-132]The letter of acceptance was to be written that night, after which his

[035-133]departure from Hintock would be irrevocable. But could he go away,

[035-134]remembering what had just passed? The trees, the hills, the leaves, the

[035-135]grass—each had been endowed and quickened with a subtle charm since he

[035-136]had discovered the person and history, and, above all, mood of their

[035-137]owner. There was every temporal reason for leaving; it would be

[035-138]entering again into a world which he had only quitted in a passion for

[035-139][[rural-isolation|isolation]], induced by a fit of Achillean moodiness after an imagined

[035-140]slight. His wife herself saw the awkwardness of their position here,

[035-141]and cheerfully welcomed the purposed change, towards which every step

[035-142]had been taken but the last. But could he find it in his heart—as he

[035-143]found it clearly enough in his conscience—to go away?

[035-144]He drew a troubled breath, and went in-doors. Here he rapidly penned a

[035-145]letter, wherein he withdrew once for all from the treaty for the

[035-146]Budmouth practice. As the postman had already left Little Hintock for

[035-147]that night, he sent one of [[george-melbury|Melbury]]’s men to intercept a mail-cart on

[035-148]another turnpike-road, and so got the letter off.

[035-149]The man returned, met Fitzpiers in the lane, and told him the thing was

[035-150]done. Fitzpiers went back to his house musing. Why had he carried out

[035-151]this impulse—taken such wild trouble to effect a probable injury to his

[035-152]own and his young wife’s prospects? His motive was fantastic, glowing,

[035-153]shapeless as the fiery scenery about the western sky. Mrs. Charmond

[035-154]could overtly be nothing more to him than a patient now, and to his

[035-155]wife, at the outside, a patron. In the unattached bachelor days of his

[035-156]first sojourning here how highly proper an emotional reason for

[035-157]lingering on would have appeared to troublesome dubiousness.

[035-158]Matrimonial ambition is such an honorable thing.

[035-159]“My father has told me that you have sent off one of the men with a

[035-160]late letter to Budmouth,” cried Grace, coming out vivaciously to meet

[035-161]him under the declining light of the sky, wherein hung, solitary, the

[035-162]folding star. “I said at once that you had finally agreed to pay the

[035-163]premium they ask, and that the tedious question had been settled. When

[035-164]do we go, Edgar?”

[035-165]“I have altered my mind,” said he. “They want too much—seven hundred

[035-166]and fifty is too large a sum—and in short, I have declined to go

[035-167]further. We must wait for another opportunity. I fear I am not a good

[035-168]business-man.” He spoke the last words with a momentary faltering at

[035-169]the great foolishness of his act; for, as he looked in her fair and

[035-170]honorable face, his heart reproached him for what he had done.

[035-171]Her manner that evening showed her disappointment. Personally she liked

[035-172]the home of her childhood much, and she was not ambitious. But her

[035-173]husband had seemed so dissatisfied with the circumstances hereabout

[035-174]since their [[marriage-in-the-woodlanders|marriage]] that she had sincerely hoped to go for his sake.

[035-175]It was two or three days before he visited Mrs. Charmond again. The

[035-176]morning had been windy, and little showers had sowed themselves like

[035-177]grain against the walls and window-panes of the Hintock cottages. He

[035-178]went on foot across the wilder recesses of the park, where slimy

[035-179]streams of green moisture, exuding from decayed holes caused by old

[035-180]amputations, ran down the bark of the oaks and elms, the rind below

[035-181]being coated with a lichenous wash as green as emerald. They were

[035-182]stout-trunked trees, that never rocked their stems in the fiercest

[035-183]gale, responding to it entirely by crooking their limbs. Wrinkled like

[035-184]an old crone’s face, and antlered with dead branches that rose above

[035-185]the foliage of their summits, they were nevertheless still green—though

[035-186]yellow had invaded the leaves of other trees.

[035-187]She was in a little boudoir or writing-room on the first floor, and

[035-188]Fitzpiers was much surprised to find that the window-curtains were

[035-189]closed and a red-shaded lamp and candles burning, though out-of-doors

[035-190]it was broad daylight. Moreover, a large fire was burning in the grate,

[035-191]though it was not cold.
