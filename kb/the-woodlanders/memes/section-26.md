# Section 26

[026-001]Yes, she was looking for something; and she came round by the prostrate

[026-002][[nature-in-the-woodlanders|trees]] that would have been invisible but for the white nakedness which

[026-003]enabled her to avoid them easily. Thus she approached the heap of

[026-004]ashes, and acting upon what was suggested by a still shining ember or

[026-005]two, she took a stick and stirred the heap, which thereupon burst into

[026-006]a flame. On looking around by the light thus obtained she for the first

[026-007]time saw the illumined face of [[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Fitzpiers]], precisely in the spot where

[026-008]she had left him.

[026-009]Grace gave a start and a scream: the place had been associated with him

[026-010]in her thoughts, but she had not expected to find him there still.

[026-011]Fitzpiers lost not a moment in rising and going to her side.

[026-012]“I frightened you dreadfully, I know,” he said. “I ought to have

[026-013]spoken; but I did not at first expect it to be you. I have been sitting

[026-014]here ever since.”

[026-015]He was actually supporting her with his arm, as though under the

[026-016]impression that she was quite overcome, and in danger of falling. As

[026-017]soon as she could collect her ideas she gently withdrew from his grasp,

[026-018]and explained what she had returned for: in getting up or down from the

[026-019]gig, or when sitting by the hut fire, she had dropped her purse.

[026-020]“Now we will find it,” said Fitzpiers.

[026-021]He threw an armful of last year’s leaves on to the fire, which made the

[026-022]flame leap higher, and the encompassing shades to weave themselves into

[026-023]a denser contrast, turning eve into night in a moment. By this radiance

[026-024]they groped about on their hands and knees, till Fitzpiers rested on

[026-025]his elbow, and looked at Grace. “We must always meet in odd

[026-026]circumstances,” he said; “and this is one of the oddest. I wonder if it

[026-027]means anything?”

[026-028]“Oh no, I am sure it doesn’t,” said Grace in haste, quickly assuming an

[026-029]erect posture. “Pray don’t say it any more.”

[026-030]“I hope there was not much money in the purse,” said Fitzpiers, rising

[026-031]to his feet more slowly, and brushing the leaves from his trousers.

[026-032]“Scarcely any. I cared most about the purse itself, because it was

[026-033]given me. Indeed, money is of little more use at [[little-hintock|Hintock]] than on

[026-034]Crusoe’s island; there’s hardly any way of spending it.”

[026-035]They had given up the search when Fitzpiers discerned something by his

[026-036]foot. “Here it is,” he said, “so that your father, mother, friend, or

[026-037]_admirer_ will not have his or her feelings hurt by a sense of your

[026-038]negligence after all.”

[026-039]“Oh, he knows nothing of what I do now.”

[026-040]“The admirer?” said Fitzpiers, slyly.

[026-041]“I don’t know if you would call him that,” said Grace, with simplicity.

[026-042]“The admirer is a superficial, conditional creature, and this person is

[026-043]quite different.”

[026-044]“He has all the cardinal virtues.”

[026-045]“Perhaps—though I don’t know them precisely.”

[026-046]“You unconsciously practise them, [[grace-melbury|Miss Melbury]], which is better.

[026-047]According to Schleiermacher they are Self-control, Perseverance,

[026-048]Wisdom, and Love; and his is the best list that I know.”

[026-049]“I am afraid poor—” She was going to say that she feared

[026-050][[giles-winterborne|Winterborne]]—the giver of the purse years before—had not much

[026-051]perseverance, though he had all the other three; but she determined to

[026-052]go no further in this direction, and was silent.

[026-053]These half-revelations made a perceptible difference in Fitzpiers. His

[026-054]sense of personal superiority wasted away, and Grace assumed in his

[026-055]eyes the true aspect of a mistress in her lover’s regard.

[026-056]“Miss [[george-melbury|Melbury]],” he said, suddenly, “I divine that this virtuous man you

[026-057]mention has been refused by you?”

[026-058]She could do no otherwise than admit it.

[026-059]“I do not inquire without good reason. God forbid that I should kneel

[026-060]in another’s place at any shrine unfairly. But, my dear Miss Melbury,

[026-061]now that he is gone, may I draw near?”

[026-062]“I—I can’t say anything about that!” she cried, quickly. “Because when

[026-063]a man has been refused you feel pity for him, and like him more than

[026-064]you did before.”

[026-065]This increasing complication added still more value to Grace in the

[026-066]surgeon’s eyes: it rendered her adorable. “But cannot you say?” he

[026-067]pleaded, distractedly.

[026-068]“I’d rather not—I think I must go home at once.”

[026-069]“Oh yes,” said Fitzpiers. But as he did not move she felt it awkward to

[026-070]walk straight away from him; and so they stood silently together. A

[026-071]diversion was created by the accident of two birds, that had either

[026-072]been roosting above their heads or nesting there, tumbling one over the

[026-073]other into the hot ashes at their feet, apparently engrossed in a

[026-074]desperate quarrel that prevented the use of their wings. They speedily

[026-075]parted, however, and flew up, and were seen no more.

[026-076]“That’s the end of what is called [[love-and-desire|love]]!” said some one.

[026-077]The speaker was neither Grace nor Fitzpiers, but [[marty-south|Marty South]], who

[026-078]approached with her face turned up to the sky in her endeavor to trace

[026-079]the birds. Suddenly perceiving Grace, she exclaimed, “Oh, Miss Melbury!

[026-080]I have been following they pigeons, and didn’t see you. And here’s Mr.

[026-081]Winterborne!” she continued, shyly, as she looked towards Fitzpiers,

[026-082]who stood in the background.

[026-083]“Marty,” Grace interrupted. “I want you to walk home with me—will you?

[026-084]Come along.” And without lingering longer she took hold of Marty’s arm

[026-085]and led her away.

[026-086]They went between the spectral arms of the peeled trees as they lay,

[026-087]and onward among the growing trees, by a path where there were no oaks,

[026-088]and no [[timber-economy|barking]], and no Fitzpiers—nothing but copse-wood, between which

[026-089]the primroses could be discerned in pale bunches. “I didn’t know Mr.

[026-090]Winterborne was there,” said Marty, breaking the silence when they had

[026-091]nearly reached Grace’s door.

[026-092]“Nor was he,” said Grace.

[026-093]“But, Miss Melbury, I saw him.”

[026-094]“No,” said Grace. “It was somebody else. Giles Winterborne is nothing

[026-095]to me.”

[026-096]CHAPTER XX.

[026-097]The leaves over Hintock grew denser in their substance, and the

[026-098]woodland seemed to change from an open filigree to a solid opaque body

[026-099]of infinitely larger shape and importance. The boughs cast green

[026-100]shades, which hurt the complexion of the girls who walked there; and a

[026-101]fringe of them which overhung Mr. Melbury’s garden dripped on his

[026-102]seed-plots when it rained, pitting their surface all over as with

[026-103]pock-marks, till Melbury declared that gardens in such a place were no

[026-104]good at all. The two trees that had creaked all the winter left off

[026-105]creaking, the whir of the night-jar, however, forming a very

[026-106]satisfactory continuation of uncanny music from that quarter. Except at

[026-107]mid-day the sun was not seen complete by the Hintock people, but rather

[026-108]in the form of numerous little stars staring through the leaves.

[026-109]Such an appearance it had on Midsummer Eve of this year, and as the

[026-110]hour grew later, and nine o’clock drew on, the irradiation of the

[026-111]daytime became broken up by weird shadows and ghostly nooks of

[026-112]indistinctness. Imagination could trace upon the trunks and boughs

[026-113]strange faces and figures shaped by the dying lights; the surfaces of

[026-114]the holly-leaves would here and there shine like peeping eyes, while

[026-115]such fragments of the sky as were visible between the trunks assumed

[026-116]the aspect of sheeted forms and cloven tongues. This was before the

[026-117]moonrise. Later on, when that planet was getting command of the upper

[026-118]heaven, and consequently shining with an unbroken face into such open

[026-119]glades as there were in the neighborhood of the hamlet, it became

[026-120]apparent that the margin of the wood which approached the

[026-121]timber-merchant’s premises was not to be left to the customary

[026-122]stillness of that reposeful time.

[026-123]Fitzpiers having heard a voice or voices, was looking over his garden

[026-124]gate—where he now looked more frequently than into his books—fancying

[026-125]that Grace might be abroad with some friends. He was now irretrievably

[026-126]committed in heart to Grace Melbury, though he was by no means sure

[026-127]that she was so far committed to him. That the Idea had for once

[026-128]completely fulfilled itself in the objective substance—which he had

[026-129]hitherto deemed an impossibility—he was enchanted enough to fancy must

[026-130]be the case at last. It was not Grace who had passed, however, but

[026-131]several of the ordinary village girls in a group—some steadily walking,

[026-132]some in a mood of wild gayety. He quietly asked his landlady, who was

[026-133]also in the garden, what these girls were intending, and she informed

[026-134]him that it being [[the-midsummer-eve|Old Midsummer Eve]], they were about to attempt some

[026-135]spell or enchantment which would afford them a glimpse of their future

[026-136]partners for life. She declared it to be an ungodly performance, and

[026-137]one which she for her part would never countenance; saying which, she

[026-138]entered her house and retired to bed.

[026-139]The young man lit a cigar and followed the bevy of maidens slowly up

[026-140]the road. They had turned into the wood at an opening between Melbury’s

[026-141]and Marty South’s; but Fitzpiers could easily track them by their

[026-142]voices, low as they endeavored to keep their tones.

[026-143]In the mean time other inhabitants of Little Hintock had become aware

[026-144]of the nocturnal experiment about to be tried, and were also sauntering

[026-145]stealthily after the frisky maidens. Miss Melbury had been informed by

[026-146]Marty South during the day of the proposed peep into futurity, and,

[026-147]being only a girl like the rest, she was sufficiently interested to

[026-148]wish to see the issue. The moon was so bright and the night so calm

[026-149]that she had no difficulty in persuading [[mrs-melbury|Mrs. Melbury]] to accompany her;

[026-150]and thus, joined by Marty, these went onward in the same direction.

[026-151]Passing Winterborne’s house, they heard a noise of hammering. Marty

[026-152]explained it. This was the last night on which his paternal roof would

[026-153]shelter him, the days of grace since it fell into hand having expired;

[026-154]and Giles was taking down his cupboards and bedsteads with a view to an

[026-155]early exit next morning. His encounter with [[felice-charmond|Mrs. Charmond]] had cost him

[026-156]dearly.

[026-157]When they had proceeded a little farther Marty was joined by Grammer

[026-158]Oliver (who was as young as the youngest in such matters), and Grace

[026-159]and Mrs. Melbury went on by themselves till they had arrived at the

[026-160]spot chosen by the village daughters, whose primary intention of

[026-161]keeping their expedition a [[secrecy-and-concealment|secret]] had been quite defeated. Grace and

[026-162]her step-mother paused by a holly-tree; and at a little distance stood

[026-163]Fitzpiers under the shade of a young oak, intently observing Grace, who

[026-164]was in the full rays of the moon.

[026-165]He watched her without speaking, and unperceived by any but Marty and

[026-166]Grammer, who had drawn up on the dark side of the same holly which

[026-167]sheltered Mrs. and Miss Melbury on its bright side. The two former

[026-168]conversed in low tones.

[026-169]“If they two come up in Wood next Midsummer Night they’ll come as one,”

[026-170]said Grammer, signifying Fitzpiers and Grace. “Instead of my

[026-171]skellington he’ll carry home her living carcass before long. But though

[026-172]she’s a lady in herself, and worthy of any such as he, it do seem to me

[026-173]that he ought to marry somebody more of the sort of Mrs. Charmond, and

[026-174]that Miss Grace should make the best of Winterborne.”

[026-175]Marty returned no comment; and at that minute the girls, some of whom

[026-176]were from [[great-hintock|Great Hintock]], were seen advancing to work the incantation,

[026-177]it being now about midnight.

[026-178]“Directly we see anything we’ll run home as fast as we can,” said one,

[026-179]whose courage had begun to fail her. To this the rest assented, not

[026-180]knowing that a dozen neighbors lurked in the bushes around.

[026-181]“I wish we had not thought of trying this,” said another, “but had

[026-182]contented ourselves with the hole-digging to-morrow at twelve, and

[026-183]hearing our husbands’ trades. It is too much like having dealings with

[026-184]the Evil One to try to raise their forms.”

[026-185]However, they had gone too far to recede, and slowly began to march

[026-186]forward in a skirmishing line through the trees towards the deeper

[026-187]recesses of the wood. As far as the listeners could gather, the

[026-188]particular form of black-art to be practised on this occasion was one

[026-189]connected with the sowing of hemp-seed, a handful of which was carried

[026-190]by each girl. At the moment of their advance they looked back, and

[026-191]discerned the figure of Miss Melbury, who, alone of all the observers,

[026-192]stood in the full face of the moonlight, deeply engrossed in the

[026-193]proceedings. By contrast with her life of late years they made her feel

[026-194]as if she had receded a couple of centuries in the world’s history. She

[026-195]was rendered doubly conspicuous by her light dress, and after a few

[026-196]whispered words, one of the girls—a bouncing maiden, plighted to young

[026-197][[timothy-tangs|Timothy Tangs]]—asked her if she would join in. Grace, with some

[026-198]excitement, said that she would, and moved on a little in the rear of

[026-199]the rest.
