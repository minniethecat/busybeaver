# Section 15

[015-001]“She will be his wife if you don’t upset her notion that she’s bound to

[015-002]accept him as an understood thing,” said [[mrs-melbury|Mrs. Melbury]]. “Bless ye,

[015-003]she’ll soon shake down here in [[little-hintock|Hintock]], and be content with [[giles-winterborne|Giles]]’s way

[015-004]of living, which he’ll improve with what money she’ll have from you.

[015-005]’Tis the strangeness after her genteel life that makes her feel

[015-006]uncomfortable at first. Why, when _I_ saw Hintock the first time I

[015-007]thought I never could like it. But things gradually get familiar, and

[015-008]stone floors seem not so very cold and hard, and the hooting of the

[015-009]owls not so very dreadful, and loneliness not so very lonely, after a

[015-010]while.”

[015-011]“Yes, I believe ye. That’s just it. I _know_ Grace will gradually sink

[015-012]down to our level again, and catch our manners and way of speaking, and

[015-013]feel a drowsy content in being Giles’s wife. But I can’t bear the

[015-014]thought of dragging down to that old level as promising a piece of

[015-015]maidenhood as ever lived—fit to ornament a palace wi’—that I’ve taken

[015-016]so much trouble to lift up. Fancy her white hands getting redder every

[015-017]day, and her tongue losing its pretty up-country curl in talking, and

[015-018]her bounding walk becoming the regular Hintock shail and wamble!”

[015-019]“She may shail, but she’ll never wamble,” replied his wife, decisively.

[015-020]When Grace came down-stairs he complained of her lying in bed so late;

[015-021]not so much moved by a particular objection to that form of indulgence

[015-022]as discomposed by these other reflections.

[015-023]The corners of her pretty mouth dropped a little down. “You used to

[015-024]complain with justice when I was a girl,” she said. “But I am a woman

[015-025]now, and can judge for myself....But it is not that; it is something

[015-026]else!” Instead of sitting down she went outside the door.

[015-027]He was sorry. The petulance that relatives show towards each other is

[015-028]in truth directed against that intangible Causality which has shaped

[015-029]the situation no less for the offenders than the offended, but is too

[015-030]elusive to be discerned and cornered by poor humanity in irritated

[015-031]mood. [[george-melbury|Melbury]] followed her. She had rambled on to the paddock, where

[015-032]the white frost lay, and where starlings in flocks of twenties and

[015-033]thirties were walking about, watched by a comfortable family of

[015-034]sparrows perched in a line along the string-course of the chimney,

[015-035]preening themselves in the rays of the sun.

[015-036]“Come in to breakfast, my girl,” he said. “And as to Giles, use your

[015-037]own mind. Whatever pleases you will please me.”

[015-038]“I am promised to him, father; and I cannot help thinking that in honor

[015-039]I ought to marry him, whenever I do marry.”

[015-040]He had a strong suspicion that somewhere in the bottom of her heart

[015-041]there pulsed an old simple indigenous feeling favorable to Giles,

[015-042]though it had become overlaid with implanted tastes. But he would not

[015-043]distinctly express his views on the promise. “Very well,” he said. “But

[015-044]I hope I sha’n’t lose you yet. Come in to breakfast. What did you think

[015-045]of the inside of [[hintock-house|Hintock House]] the other day?”

[015-046]“I liked it much.”

[015-047]“Different from friend Winterborne’s?”

[015-048]She said nothing; but he who knew her was aware that she meant by her

[015-049]silence to reproach him with drawing cruel comparisons.

[015-050]“[[felice-charmond|Mrs. Charmond]] has asked you to come again—when, did you say?”

[015-051]“She thought Tuesday, but would send the day before to let me know if

[015-052]it suited her.” And with this subject upon their lips they entered to

[015-053]breakfast.

[015-054]Tuesday came, but no message from Mrs. Charmond. Nor was there any on

[015-055]Wednesday. In brief, a fortnight slipped by without a sign, and it

[015-056]looked suspiciously as if Mrs. Charmond were not going further in the

[015-057]direction of “taking up” Grace at present.

[015-058]Her father reasoned thereon. Immediately after his daughter’s two

[015-059]indubitable successes with Mrs. Charmond—the interview in the wood and

[015-060]a visit to the House—she had attended Winterborne’s party. No doubt the

[015-061]out-and-out joviality of that gathering had made it a topic in the

[015-062]neighborhood, and that every one present as guests had been widely

[015-063]spoken of—Grace, with her exceptional qualities, above all. What, then,

[015-064]so natural as that Mrs. Charmond should have heard the village news,

[015-065]and become quite disappointed in her expectations of Grace at finding

[015-066]she kept such company?

[015-067]Full of this _post hoc_ argument, Mr. Melbury overlooked the infinite

[015-068]throng of other possible reasons and unreasons for a woman changing her

[015-069]mind. For instance, while knowing that his Grace was attractive, he

[015-070]quite forgot that Mrs. Charmond had also great pretensions to beauty.

[015-071]In his simple estimate, an attractive woman attracted all around.

[015-072]So it was settled in his mind that her sudden mingling with the

[015-073]villagers at the unlucky Winterborne’s was the cause of her most

[015-074]grievous loss, as he deemed it, in the direction of Hintock House.

[015-075]“’Tis a thousand pities!” he would repeat to himself. “I am ruining her

[015-076]for conscience’ sake!”

[015-077]It was one morning later on, while these things were agitating his

[015-078]mind, that, curiously enough, something darkened the window just as

[015-079]they finished breakfast. Looking up, they saw Giles in person mounted

[015-080]on horseback, and straining his neck forward, as he had been doing for

[015-081]some time, to catch their attention through the window. Grace had been

[015-082]the first to see him, and involuntarily exclaimed, “There he is—and a

[015-083]new horse!”

[015-084]On their faces as they regarded Giles were written their suspended

[015-085]thoughts and compound feelings concerning him, could he have read them

[015-086]through those old panes. But he saw nothing: his features just now

[015-087]were, for a wonder, lit up with a red smile at some other idea. So they

[015-088]rose from breakfast and went to the door, Grace with an anxious,

[015-089]wistful manner, her father in a reverie, Mrs. Melbury placid and

[015-090]inquiring. “We have come out to look at your horse,” she said.

[015-091]It could be seen that he was pleased at their attention, and explained

[015-092]that he had ridden a mile or two to try the animal’s paces. “I bought

[015-093]her,” he added, with warmth so severely repressed as to seem

[015-094]indifference, “because she has been used to carry a lady.”

[015-095]Still Mr. Melbury did not brighten. Mrs. Melbury said, “And is she

[015-096]quiet?”

[015-097]Winterborne assured her that there was no doubt of it. “I took care of

[015-098]that. She’s five-and-twenty, and very clever for her age.”

[015-099]“Well, get off and come in,” said Melbury, brusquely; and Giles

[015-100]dismounted accordingly.

[015-101]This event was the concrete result of Winterborne’s thoughts during the

[015-102]past week or two. The want of success with his evening party he had

[015-103]accepted in as philosophic a mood as he was capable of; but there had

[015-104]been enthusiasm enough left in him one day at [[sherton-abbas|Sherton Abbas]] market to

[015-105]purchase this old mare, which had belonged to a neighboring parson with

[015-106]several daughters, and was offered him to carry either a gentleman or a

[015-107]lady, and to do odd jobs of carting and agriculture at a pinch. This

[015-108]obliging quadruped seemed to furnish Giles with a means of reinstating

[015-109]himself in Melbury’s good opinion as a man of considerateness by

[015-110]throwing out future possibilities to Grace.

[015-111]The latter looked at him with intensified interest this morning, in the

[015-112]mood which is altogether peculiar to woman’s [[nature-in-the-woodlanders|nature]], and which, when

[015-113]reduced into plain words, seems as impossible as the penetrability of

[015-114]matter—that of entertaining a tender pity for the object of her own

[015-115]unnecessary coldness. The imperturbable poise which marked Winterborne

[015-116]in general was enlivened now by a freshness and animation that set a

[015-117]brightness in his eye and on his cheek. Mrs. Melbury asked him to have

[015-118]some breakfast, and he pleasurably replied that he would join them,

[015-119]with his usual lack of tactical observation, not perceiving that they

[015-120]had all finished the meal, that the hour was inconveniently late, and

[015-121]that the note piped by the kettle denoted it to be nearly empty; so

[015-122]that fresh water had to be brought in, trouble taken to make it boil,

[015-123]and a general renovation of the table carried out. Neither did he know,

[015-124]so full was he of his tender ulterior object in buying that horse, how

[015-125]many cups of tea he was gulping down one after another, nor how the

[015-126]morning was slipping, nor how he was keeping the family from dispersing

[015-127]about their duties.

[015-128]Then he told throughout the humorous story of the horse’s purchase,

[015-129]looking particularly grim at some fixed object in the room, a way he

[015-130]always looked when he narrated anything that amused him. While he was

[015-131]still thinking of the scene he had described, Grace rose and said, “I

[015-132]have to go and help my mother now, Mr. Winterborne.”

[015-133]“H’m!” he ejaculated, turning his eyes suddenly upon her.

[015-134]She repeated her words with a slight blush of awkwardness; whereupon

[015-135]Giles, becoming suddenly conscious, too conscious, jumped up, saying,

[015-136]“To be sure, to be sure!” wished them quickly good-morning, and bolted

[015-137]out of the house.

[015-138]Nevertheless he had, upon the whole, strengthened his position, with

[015-139]her at least. Time, too, was on his side, for (as her father saw with

[015-140]some regret) already the homeliness of Hintock life was fast becoming

[015-141]effaced from her observation as a singularity; just as the first

[015-142]strangeness of a face from which we have for years been separated

[015-143]insensibly passes off with renewed intercourse, and tones itself down

[015-144]into simple identity with the lineaments of the past.

[015-145]Thus Mr. Melbury went out of the house still unreconciled to the

[015-146]sacrifice of the gem he had been at such pains in mounting. He fain

[015-147]could hope, in the [[secrecy-and-concealment|secret]] nether chamber of his mind, that something

[015-148]would happen, before the balance of her feeling had quite turned in

[015-149]Winterborne’s favor, to relieve his conscience and preserve her on her

[015-150]elevated plane.

[015-151]He could not forget that Mrs. Charmond had apparently abandoned all

[015-152]interest in his daughter as suddenly as she had conceived it, and was

[015-153]as firmly convinced as ever that the comradeship which Grace had shown

[015-154]with Giles and his crew by attending his party had been the cause.

[015-155]Matters lingered on thus. And then, as a hoop by gentle knocks on this

[015-156]side and on that is made to travel in specific directions, the little

[015-157]touches of circumstance in the life of this young girl shaped the

[015-158]curves of her career.

[015-159]CHAPTER XII.

[015-160]It was a day of rather bright weather for the season. [[grace-melbury|Miss Melbury]] went

[015-161]out for a morning walk, and her ever-regardful father, having an hour’s

[015-162]leisure, offered to walk with her. The breeze was fresh and quite

[015-163]steady, filtering itself through the denuded mass of twigs without

[015-164]swaying them, but making the point of each ivy-leaf on the trunks

[015-165]scratch its underlying [[community-and-kinship|neighbor]] restlessly. Grace’s lips sucked in this

[015-166]native air of hers like milk. They soon reached a place where the wood

[015-167]ran down into a corner, and went outside it towards comparatively open

[015-168]ground. Having looked round about, they were intending to re-enter the

[015-169]copse when a fox quietly emerged with a dragging brush, trotted past

[015-170]them tamely as a domestic cat, and disappeared amid some dead fern.

[015-171]They walked on, her father merely observing, after watching the animal,

[015-172]“They are hunting somewhere near.”

[015-173]Farther up they saw in the mid-distance the hounds running hither and

[015-174]thither, as if there were little or no scent that day. Soon divers

[015-175]members of the hunt appeared on the scene, and it was evident from

[015-176]their movements that the chase had been stultified by general

[015-177]puzzle-headedness as to the whereabouts of the intended victim. In a

[015-178]minute a farmer rode up to the two pedestrians, panting with acteonic

[015-179]excitement, and Grace being a few steps in advance, he addressed her,

[015-180]asking if she had seen the fox.

[015-181]“Yes,” said she. “We saw him some time ago—just out there.”

[015-182]“Did you cry Halloo?”

[015-183]“We said nothing.”
