## Overview

The Midsummer Eve gathering is a significant early event in *The Woodlanders*, occurring in the woodland between Little and Great Hintock. On "Old Midsummer Eve" [026-134], the rural folk gather to observe traditional customs — planting ferns and gathering dew — which are believed to bring luck in love. It is during this gathering that Grace Melbury and Dr. Edred Fitzpiers first experience a meaningful romantic connection.

## The Setting

On Midsummer Eve, the woodland takes on a distinctive character [026-109]. The fading light and the gathering of villagers from both Little and Great Hintock create a liminal atmosphere — between day and night, work and festivity, the ordinary and the magical. The coming together of the two communities for a shared tradition underscores the event's social significance.

## The Folk Traditions

The villagers observe two key folk traditions: fern-planting and dew-gathering. The superstition holds that those who plant ferns together on Midsummer Eve will come together as a couple by the next Midsummer: "next Midsummer Night they'll come as one" [026-169]. The dew of Midsummer Eve is also believed to have magical properties, and Grace's participation in the dew-gathering [028-037] becomes a point of intimate connection with Fitzpiers.

## Grace and Fitzpiers

The Midsummer Eve gathering marks a turning point in Grace and Fitzpiers's relationship. The "tender doings of Midsummer Eve" [028-037] provide a sanctioned space for romantic expression within the rural community's folk framework. Grace's presence in the dew — "she's been out in the dew — on Midsummer Eve" [028-046] — becomes a subject of talk and marks her as participating in the rituals of courtship.

The "incident in the wood on old Midsummer-eve" [031-045] lingers in Grace's memory, representing the moment when her attraction to Fitzpiers began to crystallize. The folk traditions of the evening provide a natural and unforced framework for what will become one of the novel's central romantic entanglements.

## Significance

The Midsummer Eve gathering exemplifies Hardy's use of seasonal customs and folk traditions to structure his narrative. The event bridges the ancient rural calendar with the modern romantic plot, showing how the old ways still shape human connection in the woodland world.
