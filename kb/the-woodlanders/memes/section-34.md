# Section 34

[034-001]In the silence the trot of horses and the spin of carriage-wheels

[034-002]became audible; and the vehicle soon shaped itself against the blank

[034-003]sky, bearing down upon him with the bend in the lane which here

[034-004]occurred, and of which the house had been the cause. He could discern

[034-005]the figure of a woman high up on the driving-seat of a phaeton, a groom

[034-006]being just visible behind. Presently there was a slight scrape, then a

[034-007]scream. [[giles-winterborne|Winterborne]] went across to the spot, and found the phaeton half

[034-008]overturned, its driver sitting on the heap of rubbish which had once

[034-009]been his dwelling, and the man seizing the horses’ heads. The equipage

[034-010]was [[felice-charmond|Mrs. Charmond]]’s, and the unseated charioteer that lady herself.

[034-011]To his inquiry if she were hurt she made some incoherent reply to the

[034-012]effect that she did not know. The damage in other respects was little

[034-013]or none: the phaeton was righted, Mrs. Charmond placed in it, and the

[034-014]reins given to the servant. It appeared that she had been deceived by

[034-015]the removal of the house, imagining the gap caused by the demolition to

[034-016]be the opening of the road, so that she turned in upon the ruins

[034-017]instead of at the bend a few yards farther on.

[034-018]“Drive home—drive home!” cried the lady, impatiently; and they started

[034-019]on their way. They had not, however, gone many paces when, the air

[034-020]being still, Winterborne heard her say “Stop; tell that man to call the

[034-021]doctor—[[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Mr. Fitzpiers]]—and send him on to the House. I find I am hurt

[034-022]more seriously than I thought.”

[034-023]Winterborne took the message from the groom and proceeded to the

[034-024]doctor’s at once. Having delivered it, he stepped back into the

[034-025]darkness, and waited till he had seen Fitzpiers leave the door. He

[034-026]stood for a few minutes looking at the window which by its light

[034-027]revealed the room where Grace was sitting, and went away under the

[034-028]gloomy [[nature-in-the-woodlanders|trees]].

[034-029]Fitzpiers duly arrived at [[hintock-house|Hintock House]], whose doors he now saw open

[034-030]for the first time. Contrary to his expectation there was visible no

[034-031]sign of that confusion or alarm which a serious accident to the

[034-032]mistress of the abode would have occasioned. He was shown into a room

[034-033]at the top of the staircase, cosily and femininely draped, where, by

[034-034]the light of the shaded lamp, he saw a woman of full round figure

[034-035]reclining upon a couch in such a position as not to disturb a pile of

[034-036]magnificent hair on the crown of her head. A deep purple dressing-gown

[034-037]formed an admirable foil to the peculiarly rich brown of her

[034-038]hair-plaits; her left arm, which was naked nearly up to the shoulder,

[034-039]was thrown upward, and between the fingers of her right hand she held a

[034-040]cigarette, while she idly breathed from her plump lips a thin stream of

[034-041]smoke towards the ceiling.

[034-042]The doctor’s first feeling was a sense of his exaggerated prevision in

[034-043]having brought appliances for a serious case; the next, something more

[034-044]curious. While the scene and the moment were new to him and

[034-045]unanticipated, the sentiment and essence of the moment were

[034-046]indescribably familiar. What could be the cause of it? Probably a

[034-047]dream.

[034-048]Mrs. Charmond did not move more than to raise her eyes to him, and he

[034-049]came and stood by her. She glanced up at his face across her brows and

[034-050]forehead, and then he observed a blush creep slowly over her decidedly

[034-051]handsome cheeks. Her eyes, which had lingered upon him with an

[034-052]inquiring, conscious expression, were hastily withdrawn, and she

[034-053]mechanically applied the cigarette again to her lips.

[034-054]For a moment he forgot his errand, till suddenly arousing himself he

[034-055]addressed her, formally condoled with her, and made the usual

[034-056]professional inquiries about what had happened to her, and where she

[034-057]was hurt.

[034-058]“That’s what I want you to tell me,” she murmured, in tones of

[034-059]indefinable reserve. “I quite believe in you, for I know you are very

[034-060]accomplished, because you study so hard.”

[034-061]“I’ll do my best to justify your good opinion,” said the young man,

[034-062]bowing. “And none the less that I am happy to find the accident has not

[034-063]been serious.”

[034-064]“I am very much shaken,” she said.

[034-065]“Oh yes,” he replied; and completed his examination, which convinced

[034-066]him that there was really nothing the matter with her, and more than

[034-067]ever puzzled him as to why he had been fetched, since she did not

[034-068]appear to be a timid woman. “You must rest a while, and I’ll send

[034-069]something,” he said.

[034-070]“Oh, I forgot,” she returned. “Look here.” And she showed him a little

[034-071]scrape on her arm—the full round arm that was exposed. “Put some

[034-072]court-plaster on that, please.”

[034-073]He obeyed. “And now,” she said, “before you go I want to put a question

[034-074]to you. Sit round there in front of me, on that low chair, and bring

[034-075]the candles, or one, to the little table. Do you smoke? Yes? That’s

[034-076]right—I am learning. Take one of these; and here’s a light.” She threw

[034-077]a matchbox across.

[034-078]Fitzpiers caught it, and having lit up, regarded her from his new

[034-079]position, which, with the shifting of the candles, for the first time

[034-080]afforded him a full view of her face. “How many years have passed since

[034-081]first we met!” she resumed, in a voice which she mainly endeavored to

[034-082]maintain at its former pitch of composure, and eying him with daring

[034-083]bashfulness.

[034-084]“_We_ met, do you say?”

[034-085]She nodded. “I saw you recently at an hotel in [[london-in-the-woodlanders|London]], when you were

[034-086]passing through, I suppose, with your bride, and I recognized you as

[034-087]one I had met in my girlhood. Do you remember, when you were studying

[034-088]at Heidelberg, an English family that was staying there, who used to

[034-089]walk—”

[034-090]“And the young lady who wore a long tail of rare-colored hair—ah, I see

[034-091]it before my eyes!—who lost her gloves on the Great Terrace—who was

[034-092]going back in the dusk to find them—to whom I said, ‘I’ll go for them,’

[034-093]and you said, ‘Oh, they are not worth coming all the way up again for.’

[034-094]I _do_ remember, and how very long we stayed talking there! I went next

[034-095]morning while the dew was on the grass: there they lay—the little

[034-096]fingers sticking out damp and thin. I see them now! I picked them up,

[034-097]and then—”

[034-098]“Well?”

[034-099]“I kissed them,” he rejoined, rather shamefacedly.

[034-100]“But you had hardly ever seen me except in the dusk?”

[034-101]“Never mind. I was young then, and I kissed them. I wondered how I

[034-102]could make the most of my _trouvaille_, and decided that I would call

[034-103]at your hotel with them that afternoon. It rained, and I waited till

[034-104]next day. I called, and you were gone.”

[034-105]“Yes,” answered she, with dry melancholy. “My mother, knowing my

[034-106]disposition, said she had no wish for such a chit as me to go falling

[034-107]in [[love-and-desire|love]] with an impecunious student, and spirited me away to Baden. As

[034-108]it is all over and past I’ll tell you one thing: I should have sent you

[034-109]a line passing warm had I known your name. That name I never knew till

[034-110]my maid said, as you passed up the hotel stairs a month ago, ‘There’s

[034-111]Dr. Fitzpiers.’”

[034-112]“Good Heaven!” said Fitzpiers, musingly. “How the time comes back to

[034-113]me! The evening, the morning, the dew, the spot. When I found that you

[034-114]really were gone it was as if a cold iron had been passed down my back.

[034-115]I went up to where you had stood when I last saw you—I flung myself on

[034-116]the grass, and—being not much more than a boy—my eyes were literally

[034-117]blinded with tears. Nameless, unknown to me as you were, I couldn’t

[034-118]forget your voice.”

[034-119]“For how long?”

[034-120]“Oh—ever so long. Days and days.”

[034-121]“Days and days! _Only_ days and days? Oh, the heart of a man! Days and

[034-122]days!”

[034-123]“But, my dear madam, I had not known you more than a day or two. It was

[034-124]not a full-blown love—it was the merest bud—red, fresh, vivid, but

[034-125]small. It was a colossal passion in posse, a giant in embryo. It never

[034-126]matured.”

[034-127]“So much the better, perhaps.”

[034-128]“Perhaps. But see how powerless is the human will against

[034-129]predestination. We were prevented meeting; we have met. One feature of

[034-130]the case remains the same amid many changes. You are still rich, and I

[034-131]am still poor. Better than that, you have (judging by your last remark)

[034-132]outgrown the foolish, impulsive passions of your early girl-hood. I

[034-133]have not outgrown mine.”

[034-134]“I beg your pardon,” said she, with vibrations of strong feeling in her

[034-135]words. “I have been placed in a position which hinders such

[034-136]outgrowings. Besides, I don’t believe that the genuine subjects of

[034-137]emotion do outgrow them; I believe that the older such people get the

[034-138]worse they are. Possibly at ninety or a hundred they may feel they are

[034-139]cured; but a mere threescore and ten won’t do it—at least for me.”

[034-140]He gazed at her in undisguised admiration. Here was a soul of souls!

[034-141]“Mrs. Charmond, you speak truly,” he exclaimed. “But you speak sadly as

[034-142]well. Why is that?”

[034-143]“I always am sad when I come here,” she said, dropping to a low tone

[034-144]with a sense of having been too demonstrative.

[034-145]“Then may I inquire why you came?”

[034-146]“A man brought me. Women are always carried about like corks upon the

[034-147]waves of masculine desires....I hope I have not alarmed you; but

[034-148][[little-hintock|Hintock]] has the curious effect of bottling up the emotions till one can

[034-149]no longer hold them; I am often obliged to fly away and discharge my

[034-150]sentiments somewhere, or I should die outright.”

[034-151]“There is very good society in the county for those who have the

[034-152]privilege of entering it.”

[034-153]“Perhaps so. But the misery of remote country life is that your

[034-154]neighbors have no toleration for difference of opinion and habit. My

[034-155]neighbors think I am an atheist, except those who think I am a Roman

[034-156]Catholic; and when I speak disrespectfully of the weather or the crops

[034-157]they think I am a blasphemer.”

[034-158]She broke into a low musical laugh at the idea.

[034-159]“You don’t wish me to stay any longer?” he inquired, when he found that

[034-160]she remained musing.

[034-161]“No—I think not.”

[034-162]“Then tell me that I am to be gone.”

[034-163]“Why? Cannot you go without?”

[034-164]“I may consult my own feelings only, if left to myself.”

[034-165]“Well, if you do, what then? Do you suppose you’ll be in my way?”

[034-166]“I feared it might be so.”

[034-167]“Then fear no more. But good-night. Come to-morrow and see if I am

[034-168]going on right. This renewal of acquaintance touches me. I have already

[034-169]a friendship for you.”

[034-170]“If it depends upon myself it shall last forever.”

[034-171]“My best hopes that it may. Good-by.”

[034-172]Fitzpiers went down the stairs absolutely unable to decide whether she

[034-173]had sent for him in the natural alarm which might have followed her

[034-174]mishap, or with the single view of making herself known to him as she

[034-175]had done, for which the capsize had afforded excellent opportunity.

[034-176]Outside the house he mused over the spot under the light of the stars.

[034-177]It seemed very strange that he should have come there more than once

[034-178]when its inhabitant was absent, and observed the house with a nameless

[034-179]interest; that he should have assumed off-hand before he knew Grace

[034-180]that it was here she lived; that, in short, at sundry times and seasons

[034-181]the individuality of Hintock House should have forced itself upon him

[034-182]as appertaining to some existence with which he was concerned.

[034-183]The intersection of his temporal orbit with Mrs. Charmond’s for a day

[034-184]or two in the past had created a sentimental interest in her at the

[034-185]time, but it had been so evanescent that in the ordinary onward roll of

[034-186]affairs he would scarce ever have recalled it again. To find her here,

[034-187]however, in these somewhat romantic circumstances, magnified that

[034-188]by-gone and transitory tenderness to indescribable proportions.
