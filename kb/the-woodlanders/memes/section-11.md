# Section 11

[011-001]The front of the house exhibited an ordinary manorial presentation of

[011-002]Elizabethan windows, mullioned and hooded, worked in rich snuff-colored

[011-003]freestone from local quarries. The ashlar of the walls, where not

[011-004]overgrown with ivy and other creepers, was coated with lichen of every

[011-005]shade, intensifying its luxuriance with its nearness to the ground,

[011-006]till, below the plinth, it merged in moss.

[011-007]Above the house to the back was a dense plantation, the roots of whose

[011-008][[nature-in-the-woodlanders|trees]] were above the level of the chimneys. The corresponding high

[011-009]ground on which Grace stood was richly grassed, with only an old tree

[011-010]here and there. A few sheep lay about, which, as they ruminated, looked

[011-011]quietly into the bedroom windows. The situation of the house,

[011-012]prejudicial to humanity, was a stimulus to vegetation, on which account

[011-013]an endless shearing of the heavy-armed ivy was necessary, and a

[011-014]continual lopping of trees and shrubs. It was an edifice built in times

[011-015]when human constitutions were damp-proof, when shelter from the

[011-016]boisterous was all that men thought of in choosing a dwelling-place,

[011-017]the insidious being beneath their notice; and its hollow site was an

[011-018]ocular reminder, by its unfitness for modern lives, of the fragility to

[011-019]which these have declined. The highest architectural cunning could have

[011-020]done nothing to make [[hintock-house|Hintock House]] dry and salubrious; and ruthless

[011-021]ignorance could have done little to make it unpicturesque. It was

[011-022]vegetable nature’s own home; a spot to inspire the painter and poet of

[011-023]still life—if they did not suffer too much from the relaxing

[011-024]atmosphere—and to draw groans from the gregariously disposed. Grace

[011-025]descended the green escarpment by a zigzag path into the drive, which

[011-026]swept round beneath the slope. The exterior of the house had been

[011-027]familiar to her from her childhood, but she had never been inside, and

[011-028]the approach to knowing an old thing in a new way was a lively

[011-029]experience. It was with a little flutter that she was shown in; but she

[011-030]recollected that [[felice-charmond|Mrs. Charmond]] would probably be alone. Up to a few

[011-031]days before this time that lady had been accompanied in her comings,

[011-032]stayings, and goings by a relative believed to be her aunt; latterly,

[011-033]however, these two ladies had separated, owing, it was supposed, to a

[011-034]quarrel, and Mrs. Charmond had been left desolate. Being presumably a

[011-035]woman who did not care for solitude, this deprivation might possibly

[011-036]account for her sudden interest in Grace.

[011-037]Mrs. Charmond was at the end of a gallery opening from the hall when

[011-038][[grace-melbury|Miss Melbury]] was announced, and saw her through the glass doors between

[011-039]them. She came forward with a smile on her face, and told the young

[011-040]girl it was good of her to come.

[011-041]“Ah! you have noticed those,” she said, seeing that Grace’s eyes were

[011-042]attracted by some curious objects against the walls. “They are

[011-043]man-traps. My husband was a connoisseur in man-traps and spring-guns

[011-044]and such articles, collecting them from all his neighbors. He knew the

[011-045]histories of all these—which gin had broken a man’s leg, which gun had

[011-046]killed a man. That one, I remember his saying, had been set by a

[011-047]game-keeper in the track of a notorious poacher; but the keeper,

[011-048]forgetting what he had done, went that way himself, received the charge

[011-049]in the lower part of his body, and died of the wound. I don’t like them

[011-050]here, but I’ve never yet given directions for them to be taken away.”

[011-051]She added, playfully, “Man-traps are of rather ominous significance

[011-052]where a person of our sex lives, are they not?”

[011-053]Grace was bound to smile; but that side of womanliness was one which

[011-054]her inexperience had no great zest in contemplating.

[011-055]“They are interesting, no doubt, as relics of a barbarous time happily

[011-056]past,” she said, looking thoughtfully at the varied designs of these

[011-057]instruments of torture—some with semi-circular jaws, some with

[011-058]rectangular; most of them with long, sharp teeth, but a few with none,

[011-059]so that their jaws looked like the blank gums of old age.

[011-060]“Well, we must not take them too seriously,” said Mrs. Charmond, with

[011-061]an indolent turn of her head, and they moved on inward. When she had

[011-062]shown her visitor different articles in cabinets that she deemed likely

[011-063]to interest her, some tapestries, wood-carvings, ivories, miniatures,

[011-064]and so on—always with a mien of listlessness which might either have

[011-065]been constitutional, or partly owing to the situation of the place—they

[011-066]sat down to an early cup of tea.

[011-067]“Will you pour it out, please? Do,” she said, leaning back in her

[011-068]chair, and placing her hand above her forehead, while her almond

[011-069]eyes—those long eyes so common to the angelic legions of early Italian

[011-070]art—became longer, and her voice more languishing. She showed that

[011-071]oblique-mannered softness which is perhaps most frequent in [[gender-roles|women]] of

[011-072]darker complexion and more lymphatic temperament than Mrs. Charmond’s

[011-073]was; who lingeringly smile their meanings to men rather than speak

[011-074]them, who inveigle rather than prompt, and take advantage of currents

[011-075]rather than steer.

[011-076]“I am the most inactive woman when I am here,” she said. “I think

[011-077]sometimes I was born to live and do nothing, nothing, nothing but float

[011-078]about, as we fancy we do sometimes in dreams. But that cannot be really

[011-079]my [[fate-and-free-will|destiny]], and I must struggle against such fancies.”

[011-080]“I am so sorry you do not enjoy exertion—it is quite sad! I wish I

[011-081]could tend you and make you very happy.”

[011-082]There was something so sympathetic, so appreciative, in the sound of

[011-083]Grace’s voice, that it impelled people to play havoc with their

[011-084]customary reservations in talking to her. “It is tender and kind of you

[011-085]to feel that,” said Mrs. Charmond. “Perhaps I have given you the notion

[011-086]that my languor is more than it really is. But this place oppresses me,

[011-087]and I have a plan of going abroad a good deal. I used to go with a

[011-088]relative, but that arrangement has dropped through.” Regarding Grace

[011-089]with a final glance of criticism, she seemed to make up her mind to

[011-090]consider the young girl satisfactory, and continued: “Now I am often

[011-091]impelled to record my impressions of times and places. I have often

[011-092]thought of writing a ‘New _Sentimental Journey_.’ But I cannot find

[011-093]energy enough to do it alone. When I am at different places in the

[011-094]south of Europe I feel a crowd of ideas and fancies thronging upon me

[011-095]continually, but to unfold writing-materials, take up a cold steel pen,

[011-096]and put these impressions down systematically on cold, smooth

[011-097]paper—that I cannot do. So I have thought that if I always could have

[011-098]somebody at my elbow with whom I am in sympathy, I might dictate any

[011-099]ideas that come into my head. And directly I had made your acquaintance

[011-100]the other day it struck me that you would suit me so well. Would you

[011-101]like to undertake it? You might read to me, too, if desirable. Will you

[011-102]think it over, and ask your parents if they are willing?”

[011-103]“Oh yes,” said Grace. “I am almost sure they would be very glad.”

[011-104]“You are so accomplished, I hear; I should be quite honored by such

[011-105]intellectual company.”

[011-106]Grace, modestly blushing, deprecated any such idea.

[011-107]“Do you keep up your lucubrations at [[little-hintock|Little Hintock]]?”

[011-108]“Oh no. Lucubrations are not unknown at Little Hintock; but they are

[011-109]not carried on by me.”

[011-110]“What—another student in that retreat?”

[011-111]“There is a surgeon lately come, and I have heard that he reads a great

[011-112]deal—I see his light sometimes through the trees late at night.”

[011-113]“Oh yes—a doctor—I believe I was told of him. It is a strange place for

[011-114]him to settle in.”

[011-115]“It is a convenient centre for a practice, they say. But he does not

[011-116]confine his studies to medicine, it seems. He investigates theology and

[011-117]metaphysics and all sorts of subjects.”

[011-118]“What is his name?”

[011-119]“[[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Fitzpiers]]. He represents a very old family, I believe, the Fitzpierses

[011-120]of Buckbury-Fitzpiers—not a great many miles from here.”

[011-121]“I am not sufficiently local to know the history of the family. I was

[011-122]never in the county till my husband brought me here.” Mrs. Charmond did

[011-123]not care to pursue this line of investigation. Whatever mysterious

[011-124]merit might attach to family antiquity, it was one which, though she

[011-125]herself could claim it, her adaptable, wandering _weltbürgerliche_

[011-126]nature had grown tired of caring about—a peculiarity that made her a

[011-127]contrast to her neighbors. “It is of rather more importance to know

[011-128]what the man is himself than what his family is,” she said, “if he is

[011-129]going to practise upon us as a surgeon. Have you seen him?”

[011-130]Grace had not. “I think he is not a very old man,” she added.

[011-131]“Has he a wife?”

[011-132]“I am not aware that he has.”

[011-133]“Well, I hope he will be useful here. I must get to know him when I

[011-134]come back. It will be very convenient to have a medical man—if he is

[011-135]clever—in one’s own parish. I get dreadfully nervous sometimes, living

[011-136]in such an outlandish place; and [[sherton-abbas|Sherton]] is so far to send to. No doubt

[011-137]you feel Hintock to be a great change after watering-place life.”

[011-138]“I do. But it is home. It has its advantages and its disadvantages.”

[011-139]Grace was thinking less of the solitude than of the attendant

[011-140]circumstances.

[011-141]They chatted on for some time, Grace being set quite at her ease by her

[011-142]entertainer. Mrs. Charmond was far too well-practised a woman not to

[011-143]know that to show a marked patronage to a sensitive young girl who

[011-144]would probably be very quick to discern it, was to demolish her [[pride-and-humiliation|dignity]]

[011-145]rather than to establish it in that young girl’s eyes. So, being

[011-146]violently possessed with her idea of making use of this gentle

[011-147]acquaintance, ready and waiting at her own door, she took great pains

[011-148]to win her confidence at starting.

[011-149]Just before Grace’s departure the two chanced to pause before a mirror

[011-150]which reflected their faces in immediate juxtaposition, so as to bring

[011-151]into prominence their resemblances and their contrasts. Both looked

[011-152]attractive as glassed back by the faithful reflector; but Grace’s

[011-153]countenance had the effect of making Mrs. Charmond appear more than her

[011-154]full age. There are complexions which set off each other to great

[011-155]advantage, and there are those which antagonize, the one killing or

[011-156]damaging its [[community-and-kinship|neighbor]] unmercifully. This was unhappily the case here.

[011-157]Mrs. Charmond fell into a meditation, and replied abstractedly to a

[011-158]cursory remark of her companion’s. However, she parted from her young

[011-159]friend in the kindliest tones, promising to send and let her know as

[011-160]soon as her mind was made up on the arrangement she had suggested.

[011-161]When Grace had ascended nearly to the top of the adjoining slope she

[011-162]looked back, and saw that Mrs. Charmond still stood at the door,

[011-163]meditatively regarding her.

[011-164]Often during the previous night, after his call on the Melburys,

[011-165][[giles-winterborne|Winterborne]]’s thoughts ran upon Grace’s announced visit to Hintock

[011-166]House. Why could he not have proposed to walk with her part of the way?

[011-167]Something told him that she might not, on such an occasion, care for

[011-168]his company.

[011-169]He was still more of that opinion when, standing in his garden next

[011-170]day, he saw her go past on the journey with such a pretty pride in the

[011-171]event. He wondered if her father’s ambition, which had purchased for

[011-172]her the means of intellectual light and culture far beyond those of any

[011-173]other native of the village, would conduce to the flight of her future

[011-174]interests above and away from the local life which was once to her the

[011-175]movement of the world.

[011-176]Nevertheless, he had her father’s permission to win her if he could;

[011-177]and to this end it became desirable to bring matters soon to a crisis,

[011-178]if he ever hoped to do so. If she should think herself too good for

[011-179]him, he could let her go and make the best of his loss; but until he

[011-180]had really tested her he could not say that she despised his suit. The

[011-181]question was how to quicken events towards an issue.
