# Section 17

[017-001]While he was here in the garden somebody came to fetch him. It was

[017-002][[marty-south|Marty]] herself, and she showed her distress by her unconsciousness of a

[017-003]cropped poll.

[017-004]“Father is still so much troubled in his mind about that tree,” she

[017-005]said. “You know the tree I mean, Mr. [[giles-winterborne|Winterborne]]? the tall one in front

[017-006]of the house, that he thinks will blow down and kill us. Can you come

[017-007]and see if you can persuade him out of his notion? I can do nothing.”

[017-008]He accompanied her to the cottage, and she conducted him upstairs. John

[017-009]South was pillowed up in a chair between the bed and the window exactly

[017-010]opposite the latter, towards which his face was turned.

[017-011]“Ah, [[community-and-kinship|neighbor]] Winterborne,” he said. “I wouldn’t have minded if my life

[017-012]had only been my own to lose; I don’t vallie it in much of itself, and

[017-013]can let it go if ’tis required of me. But to think what ’tis worth to

[017-014]you, a young man rising in life, that do trouble me! It seems a trick

[017-015]of dishonesty towards ye to go off at fifty-five! I could bear up, I

[017-016]know I could, if it were not for the tree—yes, the tree, ’tis that’s

[017-017]killing me. There he stands, threatening my life every minute that the

[017-018]wind do blow. He’ll come down upon us and squat us dead; and what will

[017-019]ye do when the life on your property is taken away?”

[017-020]“Never you mind me—that’s of no consequence,” said Giles. “Think of

[017-021]yourself alone.”

[017-022]He looked out of the window in the direction of the woodman’s gaze. The

[017-023]tree was a tall elm, familiar to him from childhood, which stood at a

[017-024]distance of two-thirds its own height from the front of South’s

[017-025]dwelling. Whenever the wind blew, as it did now, the tree rocked,

[017-026]naturally enough; and the sight of its motion and sound of its sighs

[017-027]had gradually bred the terrifying illusion in the woodman’s mind that

[017-028]it would descend and kill him. Thus he would sit all day, in spite of

[017-029]persuasion, watching its every sway, and listening to the melancholy

[017-030]Gregorian melodies which the air wrung out of it. This fear it

[017-031]apparently was, rather than any organic disease which was eating away

[017-032]the health of [[john-south|John South]].

[017-033]As the tree waved, South waved his head, making it his flugel-man with

[017-034]abject obedience. “Ah, when it was quite a small tree,” he said, “and I

[017-035]was a little boy, I thought one day of chopping it off with my hook to

[017-036]make a clothes-line prop with. But I put off doing it, and then I again

[017-037]thought that I would; but I forgot it, and didn’t. And at last it got

[017-038]too big, and now ’tis my enemy, and will be the death o’ me. Little did

[017-039]I think, when I let that sapling stay, that a time would come when it

[017-040]would torment me, and dash me into my grave.”

[017-041]“No, no,” said Winterborne and Marty, soothingly. But they thought it

[017-042]possible that it might hasten him into his grave, though in another way

[017-043]than by falling.

[017-044]“I tell you what,” added Winterborne, “I’ll climb up this afternoon and

[017-045]shroud off the lower boughs, and then it won’t be so heavy, and the

[017-046]wind won’t affect it so.”

[017-047]“She won’t allow it—a strange woman come from nobody knows where—she

[017-048]won’t have it done.”

[017-049]“You mean [[felice-charmond|Mrs. Charmond]]? Oh, she doesn’t know there’s such a tree on

[017-050]her estate. Besides, shrouding is not felling, and I’ll risk that

[017-051]much.”

[017-052]He went out, and when afternoon came he returned, took a billhook from

[017-053]the woodman’s shed, and with a ladder climbed into the lower part of

[017-054]the tree, where he began lopping off—“shrouding,” as they called it at

[017-055][[little-hintock|Hintock]]—the lowest boughs. Each of these quivered under his attack,

[017-056]bent, cracked, and fell into the hedge. Having cut away the lowest

[017-057]tier, he stepped off the ladder, climbed a few steps higher, and

[017-058]attacked those at the next level. Thus he ascended with the progress of

[017-059]his work far above the top of the ladder, cutting away his perches as

[017-060]he went, and leaving nothing but a bare stem below him.

[017-061]The work was troublesome, for the tree was large. The afternoon wore

[017-062]on, turning dark and misty about four o’clock. From time to time Giles

[017-063]cast his eyes across towards the bedroom window of South, where, by the

[017-064]flickering fire in the chamber, he could see the old man watching him,

[017-065]sitting motionless with a hand upon each arm of the chair. Beside him

[017-066]sat Marty, also straining her eyes towards the skyey field of his

[017-067]operations.

[017-068]A curious question suddenly occurred to Winterborne, and he stopped his

[017-069]chopping. He was operating on another person’s property to prolong the

[017-070]years of a lease by whose termination that person would considerably

[017-071]benefit. In that aspect of the case he doubted if he ought to go on. On

[017-072]the other hand he was working to save a man’s life, and this seemed to

[017-073]empower him to adopt arbitrary measures.

[017-074]The wind had died down to a calm, and while he was weighing the

[017-075]circumstances he saw coming along the road through the increasing mist

[017-076]a figure which, indistinct as it was, he knew well. It was Grace

[017-077][[george-melbury|Melbury]], on her way out from the house, probably for a short evening

[017-078]walk before dark. He arranged himself for a greeting from her, since

[017-079]she could hardly avoid passing immediately beneath the tree.

[017-080]But Grace, though she looked up and saw him, was just at that time too

[017-081]full of the words of her father to give him any encouragement. The

[017-082]years-long regard that she had had for him was not kindled by her

[017-083]return into a flame of sufficient brilliancy to make her rebellious.

[017-084]Thinking that she might not see him, he cried, “[[grace-melbury|Miss Melbury]], here I

[017-085]am.”

[017-086]She looked up again. She was near enough to see the expression of his

[017-087]face, and the nails in his soles, silver-bright with constant walking.

[017-088]But she did not reply; and dropping her glance again, went on.

[017-089]Winterborne’s face grew strange; he mused, and proceeded automatically

[017-090]with his work. Grace meanwhile had not gone far. She had reached a

[017-091]gate, whereon she had leaned sadly, and whispered to herself, “What

[017-092]shall I do?”

[017-093]A sudden fog came on, and she curtailed her walk, passing under the

[017-094]tree again on her return. Again he addressed her. “Grace,” he said,

[017-095]when she was close to the trunk, “speak to me.” She shook her head

[017-096]without stopping, and went on to a little distance, where she stood

[017-097]observing him from behind the hedge.

[017-098]Her coldness had been kindly meant. If it was to be done, she had said

[017-099]to herself, it should be begun at once. While she stood out of

[017-100]observation Giles seemed to recognize her meaning; with a sudden start

[017-101]he worked on, climbing higher, and cutting himself off more and more

[017-102]from all intercourse with the sublunary world. At last he had worked

[017-103]himself so high up the elm, and the mist had so thickened, that he

[017-104]could only just be discerned as a dark-gray spot on the light-gray sky:

[017-105]he would have been altogether out of notice but for the stroke of his

[017-106]billhook and the flight of a bough downward, and its crash upon the

[017-107]hedge at intervals.

[017-108]It was not to be done thus, after all: plainness and candor were best.

[017-109]She went back a third time; he did not see her now, and she lingeringly

[017-110]gazed up at his unconscious figure, loath to put an end to any kind of

[017-111]hope that might live on in him still. “Giles— Mr. Winterborne,” she

[017-112]said.

[017-113]He was so high amid the fog that he did not hear. “Mr. Winterborne!”

[017-114]she cried again, and this time he stopped, looked down, and replied.

[017-115]“My silence just now was not accident,” she said, in an unequal voice.

[017-116]“My father says it is best not to think too much of that—engagement, or

[017-117]understanding between us, that you know of. I, too, think that upon the

[017-118]whole he is right. But we are friends, you know, Giles, and almost

[017-119]relations.”

[017-120]“Very well,” he answered, as if without surprise, in a voice which

[017-121]barely reached down the tree. “I have nothing to say in objection—I

[017-122]cannot say anything till I’ve thought a while.”

[017-123]She added, with emotion in her tone, “For myself, I would have married

[017-124]you—some day—I think. But I give way, for I see it would be unwise.”

[017-125]He made no reply, but sat back upon a bough, placed his elbow in a

[017-126]fork, and rested his head upon his hand. Thus he remained till the fog

[017-127]and the night had completely enclosed him from her view.

[017-128]Grace heaved a divided sigh, with a tense pause between, and moved

[017-129]onward, her heart feeling uncomfortably big and heavy, and her eyes

[017-130]wet. Had Giles, instead of remaining still, immediately come down from

[017-131]the tree to her, would she have continued in that filial acquiescent

[017-132]frame of mind which she had announced to him as final? If it be true,

[017-133]as [[gender-roles|women]] themselves have declared, that one of their sex is never so

[017-134]much inclined to throw in her lot with a man for good and all as five

[017-135]minutes after she has told him such a thing cannot be, the

[017-136]probabilities are that something might have been done by the appearance

[017-137]of Winterborne on the ground beside Grace. But he continued motionless

[017-138]and silent in that gloomy Niflheim or fog-land which involved him, and

[017-139]she proceeded on her way.

[017-140]The spot seemed now to be quite deserted. The light from South’s window

[017-141]made rays on the fog, but did not reach the tree. A quarter of an hour

[017-142]passed, and all was blackness overhead. Giles had not yet come down.

[017-143]Then the tree seemed to shiver, then to heave a sigh; a movement was

[017-144]audible, and Winterborne dropped almost noiselessly to the ground. He

[017-145]had thought the matter out, and having returned the ladder and billhook

[017-146]to their places, pursued his way homeward. He would not allow this

[017-147]incident to affect his outer conduct any more than the danger to his

[017-148]leaseholds had done, and went to bed as usual. Two simultaneous

[017-149]troubles do not always make a double trouble; and thus it came to pass

[017-150]that Giles’s practical anxiety about his houses, which would have been

[017-151]enough to keep him awake half the night at any other time, was

[017-152]displaced and not reinforced by his sentimental trouble about Grace

[017-153]Melbury. This severance was in truth more like a burial of her than a

[017-154]rupture with her; but he did not realize so much at present; even when

[017-155]he arose in the morning he felt quite moody and stern: as yet the

[017-156]second note in the gamut of such emotions, a tender regret for his

[017-157]loss, had not made itself heard.

[017-158]A load of oak [[timber-economy|timber]] was to be sent away that morning to a builder

[017-159]whose works were in a town many miles off. The proud trunks were taken

[017-160]up from the silent spot which had known them through the buddings and

[017-161]sheddings of their growth for the foregoing hundred years; chained down

[017-162]like slaves to a heavy timber carriage with enormous red wheels, and

[017-163]four of the most powerful of Melbury’s horses were harnessed in front

[017-164]to draw them.

[017-165]The horses wore their bells that day. There were sixteen to the team,

[017-166]carried on a frame above each animal’s shoulders, and tuned to scale,

[017-167]so as to form two octaves, running from the highest note on the right

[017-168]or off-side of the leader to the lowest on the left or near-side of the

[017-169]shaft-horse. Melbury was among the last to retain horse-bells in that

[017-170]neighborhood; for, living at Little Hintock, where the lanes yet

[017-171]remained as narrow as before the days of turnpike roads, these

[017-172]sound-signals were still as useful to him and his neighbors as they had

[017-173]ever been in former times. Much backing was saved in the course of a

[017-174]year by the warning notes they cast ahead; moreover, the tones of all

[017-175]the teams in the district being known to the carters of each, they

[017-176]could tell a long way off on a dark night whether they were about to

[017-177]encounter friends or strangers.
