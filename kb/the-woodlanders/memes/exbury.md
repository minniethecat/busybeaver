# Exbury

Exbury is a town beyond Sherton Abbas in Thomas Hardy's *The Woodlanders*, serving as a significant location in the dissolution of the Fitzpiers marriage. Both Grace Melbury and Edred Fitzpiers turn towards Exbury during their marital crisis — Grace as a place of intended escape, and Fitzpiers as a place of actual retreat. The town's distance from Hintock makes it a symbol of the widening gulf between husband and wife.

## 地理位置

Exbury lies beyond Sherton Abbas, reachable by a two-stage journey from the Hintock woodlands. Grace Melbury asks Giles Winterborne to help her "get by a roundabout way to Sherton, and so to Exbury" [054-124], establishing the route that connects the woodland hamlet to this more distant town. The geographical remove mirrors the emotional distance that has opened between Grace and her husband — Exbury is far enough to represent a genuine departure from the known world of Hintock.

## Grace's Intended Escape

During the crisis of her marriage, Grace Melbury determines to leave Hintock for Exbury. She appeals to Giles Winterborne for help with the journey, asking "About my getting to Exbury?" [055-134]. Giles, however, counsels her to stay where she is for the present, offering her shelter at his own dwelling rather than facilitating her departure to the distant town [055-136].

Grace's intended route passes through Sherton Abbey — a journey that Giles had already helped her with once before, accompanying her "a little way" towards Sherton [054-125]. The plan to go to Exbury represents Grace's first decisive act of separation from Fitzpiers, even if she does not ultimately follow through with it.

## Fitzpiers's Retreat

It is Edred Fitzpiers, not Grace, who actually spends time at Exbury. Following the collapse of his marriage, he "passed a month or two of great misery at Exbury, the place to which he had retired" [060-084]. The narrator notes that his suffering at Exbury was "quite as much misery indeed as Grace, could she have known of it, would have been inclined to inflict upon any living creature" [060-085]. This period of isolation and reflection at Exbury marks Fitzpiers's emotional low point, from which he eventually begins the slow process of seeking reconciliation.

## Social Connections

A school-fellow of Fitzpiers lives at Exbury [054-124], providing a personal connection that makes the town a plausible destination for both Grace and her husband. The schoolfellow serves as a narrative device — a reason why Exbury, rather than any other town, becomes the focal point of the couple's separation.

## 象征与氛围

Exbury functions in the novel as a threshold space — a place of "great misery" [060-084] that lies beyond the familiar geography of Hintock and Sherton. It is never directly depicted; the reader never sees Exbury, only hears of it as a destination. This narrative absence mirrors the emotional vacuum of the Fitzpiers marriage at its lowest point: Exbury is a blank on the map, a place where suffering happens offstage, beyond the reader's sight.
