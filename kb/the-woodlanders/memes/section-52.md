# Section 52

[052-001]CHAPTER XXXIX.

[052-002]All night did [[giles-winterborne|Winterborne]] think over that unsatisfactory ending of a

[052-003]pleasant time, forgetting the pleasant time itself. He feared anew that

[052-004]they could never be happy together, even should she be free to choose

[052-005]him. She was accomplished; he was unrefined. It was the original

[052-006]difficulty, which he was too sensitive to recklessly ignore, as some

[052-007]men would have done in his place.

[052-008]He was one of those silent, unobtrusive beings who want little from

[052-009]others in the way of favor or condescension, and perhaps on that very

[052-010]account scrutinize those others’ behavior too closely. He was not

[052-011]versatile, but one in whom a hope or belief which had once had its

[052-012]rise, meridian, and decline seldom again exactly recurred, as in the

[052-013]breasts of more sanguine mortals. He had once worshipped her, laid out

[052-014]his life to suit her, wooed her, and lost her. Though it was with

[052-015]almost the same zest, it was with not quite the same hope, that he had

[052-016]begun to tread the old tracks again, and allowed himself to be so

[052-017]charmed with her that day.

[052-018]Move another step towards her he would not. He would even repulse

[052-019]her—as a tribute to conscience. It would be sheer sin to let her

[052-020]prepare a pitfall for her happiness not much smaller than the first by

[052-021]inveigling her into a union with such as he. Her poor father was now

[052-022]blind to these subtleties, which he had formerly beheld as in noontide

[052-023]light. It was his own duty to declare them—for her dear sake.

[052-024]Grace, too, had a very uncomfortable night, and her solicitous

[052-025]embarrassment was not lessened the next morning when another letter

[052-026]from her father was put into her hands. Its tenor was an intenser

[052-027]strain of the one that had preceded it. After stating how extremely

[052-028]glad he was to hear that she was better, and able to get out-of-doors,

[052-029]he went on:

[052-030]“This is a wearisome business, the solicitor we have come to see being

[052-031]out of town. I do not know when I shall get home. My great anxiety in

[052-032]this delay is still lest you should lose Giles Winterborne. I cannot

[052-033]rest at night for thinking that while our business is hanging fire he

[052-034]may become estranged, or go away from the neighborhood. I have set my

[052-035]heart upon seeing him your husband, if you ever have another. Do, then,

[052-036]Grace, give him some temporary encouragement, even though it is

[052-037]over-early. For when I consider the past I do think God will forgive me

[052-038]and you for being a little forward. I have another reason for this, my

[052-039]dear. I feel myself going rapidly downhill, and late affairs have still

[052-040]further helped me that way. And until this thing is done I cannot rest

[052-041]in peace.”

[052-042]He added a postscript:

[052-043]“I have just heard that the solicitor is to be seen to-morrow.

[052-044]Possibly, therefore, I shall return in the evening after you get this.”

[052-045]The paternal longing ran on all fours with her own [[love-and-desire|desire]]; and yet in

[052-046]forwarding it yesterday she had been on the brink of giving offence.

[052-047]While craving to be a country girl again just as her father requested;

[052-048]to put off the old Eve, the fastidious miss—or rather madam—completely,

[052-049]her first attempt had been beaten by the unexpected vitality of that

[052-050]fastidiousness. Her father on returning and seeing the trifling

[052-051]coolness of Giles would be sure to say that the same perversity which

[052-052]had led her to make difficulties about marrying [[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Fitzpiers]] was now

[052-053]prompting her to blow hot and cold with poor Winterborne.

[052-054]If the latter had been the most subtle hand at touching the stops of

[052-055]her delicate soul instead of one who had just bound himself to let her

[052-056]drift away from him again (if she would) on the wind of her estranging

[052-057][[education-and-class-mobility|education]], he could not have acted more seductively than he did that

[052-058]day. He chanced to be superintending some temporary work in a field

[052-059]opposite her windows. She could not discover what he was doing, but she

[052-060]read his mood keenly and truly: she could see in his coming and going

[052-061]an air of determined abandonment of the whole landscape that lay in her

[052-062]direction.

[052-063]Oh, how she longed to make it up with him! Her father coming in the

[052-064]evening—which meant, she supposed, that all formalities would be in

[052-065]train, her [[marriage-in-the-woodlanders|marriage]] virtually annulled, and she be free to be won

[052-066]again—how could she look him in the face if he should see them

[052-067]estranged thus?

[052-068]It was a fair green evening in June. She was seated in the garden, in

[052-069]the rustic chair which stood under the laurel-bushes—made of peeled

[052-070]oak-branches that came to [[george-melbury|Melbury]]’s premises as refuse after

[052-071][[timber-economy|barking]]-time. The mass of full-juiced leafage on the heights around her

[052-072]was just swayed into faint gestures by a nearly spent wind which, even

[052-073]in its enfeebled state, did not reach her shelter. All day she had

[052-074]expected Giles to call—to inquire how she had got home, or something or

[052-075]other; but he had not come. And he still tantalized her by going

[052-076]athwart and across that [[orchard-culture|orchard]] opposite. She could see him as she sat.

[052-077]A slight diversion was presently created by [[robert-creedle|Creedle]] bringing him a

[052-078]letter. She knew from this that Creedle had just come from [[sherton-abbas|Sherton]], and

[052-079]had called as usual at the post-office for anything that had arrived by

[052-080]the afternoon post, of which there was no delivery at [[little-hintock|Hintock]]. She

[052-081]pondered on what the letter might contain—particularly whether it were

[052-082]a second refresher for Winterborne from her father, like her own of the

[052-083]morning.

[052-084]But it appeared to have no bearing upon herself whatever. Giles read

[052-085]its contents; and almost immediately turned away to a gap in the hedge

[052-086]of the orchard—if that could be called a hedge which, owing to the

[052-087]drippings of the [[nature-in-the-woodlanders|trees]], was little more than a bank with a bush upon it

[052-088]here and there. He entered the plantation, and was no doubt going that

[052-089]way homeward to the mysterious hut he occupied on the other side of the

[052-090]woodland.

[052-091]The sad sands were running swiftly through Time’s glass; she had often

[052-092]felt it in these latter days; and, like Giles, she felt it doubly now

[052-093]after the solemn and pathetic reminder in her father’s communication.

[052-094]Her freshness would pass, the long-suffering devotion of Giles might

[052-095]suddenly end—might end that very hour. Men were so strange. The thought

[052-096]took away from her all her former reticence, and made her action bold.

[052-097]She started from her seat. If the little breach, quarrel, or whatever

[052-098]it might be called, of yesterday, was to be healed up it must be done

[052-099]by her on the instant. She crossed into the orchard, and clambered

[052-100]through the gap after Giles, just as he was diminishing to a faun-like

[052-101]figure under the green canopy and over the brown floor.

[052-102]Grace had been wrong—very far wrong—in assuming that the letter had no

[052-103]reference to herself because Giles had turned away into the wood after

[052-104]its perusal. It was, sad to say, because the missive had so much

[052-105]reference to herself that he had thus turned away. He feared that his

[052-106]grieved discomfiture might be observed. The letter was from [[fred-beaucock|Beaucock]],

[052-107]written a few hours later than Melbury’s to his daughter. It announced

[052-108]failure.

[052-109]Giles had once done that thriftless man a good turn, and now was the

[052-110]moment when Beaucock had chosen to remember it in his own way. During

[052-111]his absence in town with Melbury, the lawyer’s clerk had naturally

[052-112]heard a great deal of the timber-merchant’s family scheme of justice to

[052-113]Giles, and his communication was to inform Winterborne at the earliest

[052-114]possible moment that their attempt had failed, in order that the young

[052-115]man should not place himself in a false position towards Grace in the

[052-116]belief of its coming success. The news was, in sum, that Fitzpiers’s

[052-117]conduct had not been sufficiently cruel to Grace to enable her to snap

[052-118]the bond. She was apparently doomed to be his wife till the end of the

[052-119]chapter.

[052-120]Winterborne quite forgot his superficial differences with the poor girl

[052-121]under the warm rush of deep and distracting love for her which the

[052-122]almost tragical information engendered.

[052-123]To renounce her forever—that was then the end of it for him, after all.

[052-124]There was no longer any question about suitability, or room for tiffs

[052-125]on petty tastes. The curtain had fallen again between them. She could

[052-126]not be his. The cruelty of their late revived hope was now terrible.

[052-127]How could they all have been so simple as to suppose this thing could

[052-128]be done?

[052-129]It was at this moment that, hearing some one coming behind him, he

[052-130]turned and saw her hastening on between the thickets. He perceived in

[052-131]an instant that she did not know the blighting news.

[052-132]“Giles, why didn’t you come across to me?” she asked, with arch

[052-133]reproach. “Didn’t you see me sitting there ever so long?”

[052-134]“Oh yes,” he said, in unprepared, extemporized tones, for her

[052-135]unexpected presence caught him without the slightest plan of behavior

[052-136]in the conjuncture. His manner made her think that she had been too

[052-137]chiding in her speech; and a mild scarlet wave passed over her as she

[052-138]resolved to soften it.

[052-139]“I have had another letter from my father,” she hastened to continue.

[052-140]“He thinks he may come home this evening. And—in view of his hopes—it

[052-141]will grieve him if there is any little difference between us, Giles.”

[052-142]“There is none,” he said, sadly regarding her from the face downward as

[052-143]he pondered how to lay the cruel truth bare.

[052-144]“Still—I fear you have not quite forgiven me about my being

[052-145]uncomfortable at the inn.”

[052-146]“I have, Grace, I’m sure.”

[052-147]“But you speak in quite an unhappy way,” she returned, coming up close

[052-148]to him with the most winning of the many pretty airs that appertained

[052-149]to her. “Don’t you think you will ever be happy, Giles?”

[052-150]He did not reply for some instants. “When the sun shines on the north

[052-151]front of Sherton Abbey—that’s when my happiness will come to me!” said

[052-152]he, staring as it were into the earth.

[052-153]“But—then that means that there is something more than my offending you

[052-154]in not liking The Three Tuns. If it is because I—did not like to let

[052-155]you kiss me in the Abbey—well, you know, Giles, that it was not on

[052-156]account of my cold feelings, but because I did certainly, just then,

[052-157]think it was rather premature, in spite of my poor father. That was the

[052-158]true reason—the sole one. But I do not want to be hard—God knows I do

[052-159]not,” she said, her voice fluctuating. “And perhaps—as I am on the

[052-160]verge of freedom—I am not right, after all, in thinking there is any

[052-161]harm in your kissing me.”

[052-162]“Oh God!” said Winterborne within himself. His head was turned askance

[052-163]as he still resolutely regarded the ground. For the last several

[052-164]minutes he had seen this great temptation approaching him in regular

[052-165]siege; and now it had come. The wrong, the social sin, of now taking

[052-166]advantage of the offer of her lips had a magnitude, in the eyes of one

[052-167]whose life had been so primitive, so ruled by purest household laws, as

[052-168]Giles’s, which can hardly be explained.

[052-169]“Did you say anything?” she asked, timidly.

[052-170]“Oh no—only that—”

[052-171]“You mean that it must _be already_ settled, since my father is coming

[052-172]home?” she said, gladly.

[052-173]Winterborne, though fighting valiantly against himself all this

[052-174]while—though he would have protected Grace’s good repute as the apple

[052-175]of his eye—was a man; and, as Desdemona said, men are not gods. In face

[052-176]of the agonizing seductiveness shown by her, in her unenlightened

[052-177]school-girl simplicity about the laws and ordinances, he betrayed a

[052-178]man’s weakness. Since it was so—since it had come to this, that Grace,

[052-179]deeming herself free to do it, was virtually asking him to demonstrate

[052-180]that he loved her—since he could demonstrate it only too truly—since

[052-181]life was short and love was strong—he gave way to the temptation,

[052-182]notwithstanding that he perfectly well knew her to be wedded

[052-183]irrevocably to Fitzpiers. Indeed, he cared for nothing past or future,

[052-184]simply accepting the present and what it brought, desiring once in his

[052-185]life to clasp in his arms her he had watched over and loved so long.
