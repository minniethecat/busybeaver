# Section 20

[020-001]“Ah!” said they altogether.

[020-002]“Her lawyer is instructed to say that [[felice-charmond|Mrs. Charmond]] sees no reason for

[020-003]disturbing the natural course of things, particularly as she

[020-004]contemplates pulling the houses down,” he said, quietly.

[020-005]“Only think of that!” said several.

[020-006][[giles-winterborne|Winterborne]] had turned away, and said vehemently to himself, “Then let

[020-007]her pull ’em down, and be d—d to her!”

[020-008][[robert-creedle|Creedle]] looked at him with a face of seven sorrows, saying, “Ah, ’twas

[020-009]that sperrit that lost ’em for ye, maister!”

[020-010]Winterborne subdued his feelings, and from that hour, whatever they

[020-011]were, kept them entirely to himself. There could be no doubt that, up

[020-012]to this last moment, he had nourished a feeble hope of regaining Grace

[020-013]in the event of this negotiation turning out a success. Not being aware

[020-014]of the fact that her father could have settled upon her a fortune

[020-015]sufficient to enable both to live in comfort, he deemed it now an

[020-016]absurdity to dream any longer of such a vanity as making her his wife,

[020-017]and sank into silence forthwith.

[020-018]Yet whatever the value of taciturnity to a man among strangers, it is

[020-019]apt to express more than talkativeness when he dwells among friends.

[020-020]The countryman who is obliged to judge the time of day from changes in

[020-021]external [[nature-in-the-woodlanders|nature]] sees a thousand successive tints and traits in the

[020-022]landscape which are never discerned by him who hears the regular chime

[020-023]of a clock, because they are never in request. In like manner do we use

[020-024]our eyes on our taciturn comrade. The infinitesimal movement of muscle,

[020-025]curve, hair, and wrinkle, which when accompanied by a voice goes

[020-026]unregarded, is watched and translated in the lack of it, till virtually

[020-027]the whole surrounding circle of familiars is charged with the reserved

[020-028]one’s moods and meanings.

[020-029]This was the condition of affairs between Winterborne and his neighbors

[020-030]after his stroke of ill-luck. He held his tongue; and they observed

[020-031]him, and knew that he was discomposed.

[020-032][[george-melbury|Mr. Melbury]], in his compunction, thought more of the matter than any

[020-033]one else, except his daughter. Had Winterborne been going on in the old

[020-034]fashion, Grace’s father could have alluded to his disapproval of the

[020-035]alliance every day with the greatest frankness; but to speak any

[020-036]further on the subject he could not find it in his heart to do now. He

[020-037]hoped that Giles would of his own accord make some final announcement

[020-038]that he entirely withdrew his pretensions to Grace, and so get the

[020-039]thing past and done with. For though Giles had in a measure acquiesced

[020-040]in the wish of her family, he could make matters unpleasant if he chose

[020-041]to work upon Grace; and hence, when Melbury saw the young man

[020-042]approaching along the road one day, he kept friendliness and frigidity

[020-043]exactly balanced in his eye till he could see whether Giles’s manner

[020-044]was presumptive or not.

[020-045]His manner was that of a man who abandoned all claims. “I am glad to

[020-046]meet ye, Mr. Melbury,” he said, in a low voice, whose quality he

[020-047]endeavored to make as practical as possible. “I am afraid I shall not

[020-048]be able to keep that mare I bought, and as I don’t care to sell her, I

[020-049]should like—if you don’t object—to give her to [[grace-melbury|Miss Melbury]]. The horse

[020-050]is very quiet, and would be quite safe for her.”

[020-051]Mr. Melbury was rather affected at this. “You sha’n’t hurt your pocket

[020-052]like that on our account, Giles. Grace shall have the horse, but I’ll

[020-053]pay you what you gave for her, and any expense you may have been put to

[020-054]for her keep.”

[020-055]He would not hear of any other terms, and thus it was arranged. They

[020-056]were now opposite Melbury’s house, and the [[timber-economy|timber]]-merchant pressed

[020-057]Winterborne to enter, Grace being out of the way.

[020-058]“Pull round the settle, Giles,” said the timber-merchant, as soon as

[020-059]they were within. “I should like to have a serious talk with you.”

[020-060]Thereupon he put the case to Winterborne frankly, and in quite a

[020-061]friendly way. He declared that he did not like to be hard on a man when

[020-062]he was in difficulty; but he really did not see how Winterborne could

[020-063]marry his daughter now, without even a house to take her to.

[020-064]Giles quite acquiesced in the awkwardness of his situation. But from a

[020-065]momentary feeling that he would like to know Grace’s mind from her own

[020-066]lips, he did not speak out positively there and then. He accordingly

[020-067]departed somewhat abruptly, and went home to consider whether he would

[020-068]seek to bring about a meeting with her.

[020-069]In the evening, while he sat quietly pondering, he fancied that he

[020-070]heard a scraping on the wall outside his house. The boughs of a monthly

[020-071]rose which grew there made such a noise sometimes, but as no wind was

[020-072]stirring he knew that it could not be the rose-tree. He took up the

[020-073]candle and went out. Nobody was near. As he turned, the light flickered

[020-074]on the whitewashed rough case of the front, and he saw words written

[020-075]thereon in charcoal, which he read as follows:

[020-076]“O Giles, you’ve lost your dwelling-place,

[020-077]And therefore, Giles, you’ll lose your Grace.”

[020-078]Giles went in-doors. He had his suspicions as to the scrawler of those

[020-079]lines, but he could not be sure. What suddenly filled his heart far

[020-080]more than curiosity about their authorship was a terrible belief that

[020-081]they were turning out to be true, try to see Grace as he might. They

[020-082]decided the question for him. He sat down and wrote a formal note to

[020-083]Melbury, in which he briefly stated that he was placed in such a

[020-084]position as to make him share to the full Melbury’s view of his own and

[020-085]his daughter’s promise, made some years before; to wish that it should

[020-086]be considered as cancelled, and they themselves quite released from any

[020-087]obligation on account of it.

[020-088]Having fastened up this their plenary absolution, he determined to get

[020-089]it out of his hands and have done with it; to which end he went off to

[020-090]Melbury’s at once. It was now so late that the family had all retired;

[020-091]he crept up to the house, thrust the note under the door, and stole

[020-092]away as silently as he had come.

[020-093]Melbury himself was the first to rise the next morning, and when he had

[020-094]read the letter his relief was great. “Very honorable of Giles, very

[020-095]honorable,” he kept saying to himself. “I shall not forget him. Now to

[020-096]keep her up to her own true level.”

[020-097]It happened that Grace went out for an early ramble that morning,

[020-098]passing through the door and gate while her father was in the

[020-099]spar-house. To go in her customary direction she could not avoid

[020-100]passing Winterborne’s house. The morning sun was shining flat upon its

[020-101]white surface, and the words, which still remained, were immediately

[020-102]visible to her. She read them. Her face flushed to crimson. She could

[020-103]see Giles and Creedle talking together at the back; the charred

[020-104]spar-gad with which the lines had been written lay on the ground

[020-105]beneath the wall. Feeling pretty sure that Winterborne would observe

[020-106]her action, she quickly went up to the wall, rubbed out “lose” and

[020-107]inserted “keep” in its stead. Then she made the best of her way home

[020-108]without looking behind her. Giles could draw an inference now if he

[020-109]chose.

[020-110]There could not be the least doubt that gentle Grace was warming to

[020-111]more sympathy with, and interest in, Giles Winterborne than ever she

[020-112]had done while he was her promised lover; that since his misfortune

[020-113]those social shortcomings of his, which contrasted so awkwardly with

[020-114]her later experiences of life, had become obscured by the generous

[020-115]revival of an old romantic attachment to him. Though mentally trained

[020-116]and tilled into foreignness of view, as compared with her youthful

[020-117]time, Grace was not an ambitious girl, and might, if left to herself,

[020-118]have declined Winterborne without much discontent or unhappiness. Her

[020-119]feelings just now were so far from latent that the writing on the wall

[020-120]had thus quickened her to an unusual rashness.

[020-121]Having returned from her walk she sat at breakfast silently. When her

[020-122]step-mother had left the room she said to her father, “I have made up

[020-123]my mind that I should like my engagement to Giles to continue, for the

[020-124]present at any rate, till I can see further what I ought to do.”

[020-125]Melbury looked much surprised.

[020-126]“Nonsense,” he said, sharply. “You don’t know what you are talking

[020-127]about. Look here.”

[020-128]He handed across to her the letter received from Giles.

[020-129]She read it, and said no more. Could he have seen her write on the

[020-130]wall? She did not know. Fate, it seemed, would have it this way, and

[020-131]there was nothing to do but to acquiesce.

[020-132]It was a few hours after this that Winterborne, who, curiously enough,

[020-133]had _not_ perceived Grace writing, was clearing away the tree from the

[020-134]front of South’s late dwelling. He saw [[marty-south|Marty]] standing in her door-way,

[020-135]a slim figure in meagre black, almost without womanly contours as yet.

[020-136]He went up to her and said, “Marty, why did you write that on my wall

[020-137]last night? It _was_ you, you know.”

[020-138]“Because it was the truth. I didn’t mean to let it stay, Mr.

[020-139]Winterborne; but when I was going to rub it out you came, and I was

[020-140]obliged to run off.”

[020-141]“Having prophesied one thing, why did you alter it to another? Your

[020-142]predictions can’t be worth much.”

[020-143]“I have not altered it.”

[020-144]“But you have.”

[020-145]“No.”

[020-146]“It is altered. Go and see.”

[020-147]She went, and read that, in spite of losing his dwelling-place, he

[020-148]would _keep_ his Grace. Marty came back surprised.

[020-149]“Well, I never,” she said. “Who can have made such nonsense of it?”

[020-150]“Who, indeed?” said he.

[020-151]“I have rubbed it all out, as the point of it is quite gone.”

[020-152]“You’d no business to rub it out. I didn’t tell you to. I meant to let

[020-153]it stay a little longer.”

[020-154]“Some idle boy did it, no doubt,” she murmured.

[020-155]As this seemed very probable, and the actual perpetrator was

[020-156]unsuspected, Winterborne said no more, and dismissed the matter from

[020-157]his mind.

[020-158]From this day of his life onward for a considerable time, Winterborne,

[020-159]though not absolutely out of his house as yet, retired into the

[020-160]background of human life and action thereabout—a feat not particularly

[020-161]difficult of performance anywhere when the doer has the assistance of a

[020-162]lost prestige. Grace, thinking that Winterborne saw her write, made no

[020-163]further sign, and the frail bark of fidelity that she had thus timidly

[020-164]launched was stranded and lost.

[020-165]CHAPTER XVI.

[020-166]Dr. [[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Fitzpiers]] lived on the slope of the hill, in a house of much less

[020-167]pretension, both as to architecture and as to magnitude, than the

[020-168]timber-merchant’s. The latter had, without doubt, been once the

[020-169]manorial residence appertaining to the snug and modest domain of Little

[020-170][[little-hintock|Hintock]], of which the boundaries were now lost by its absorption with

[020-171]others of its kind into the adjoining estate of Mrs. Charmond. Though

[020-172]the Melburys themselves were unaware of the fact, there was every

[020-173]reason to believe—at least so the parson said—that the owners of that

[020-174]little manor had been Melbury’s own ancestors, the family name

[020-175]occurring in numerous documents relating to transfers of land about the

[020-176]time of the civil wars.

[020-177]Mr. Fitzpiers’s dwelling, on the contrary, was small, cottage-like, and

[020-178]comparatively modern. It had been occupied, and was in part occupied

[020-179]still, by a retired farmer and his wife, who, on the surgeon’s arrival

[020-180]in quest of a home, had accommodated him by receding from their front

[020-181]rooms into the kitchen quarter, whence they administered to his wants,

[020-182]and emerged at regular intervals to receive from him a not unwelcome

[020-183]addition to their income.

[020-184]The cottage and its garden were so regular in their arrangement that

[020-185]they might have been laid out by a Dutch designer of the time of

[020-186]William and Mary. In a low, dense hedge, cut to wedge-shape, was a door

[020-187]over which the hedge formed an arch, and from the inside of the door a

[020-188]straight path, bordered with clipped box, ran up the slope of the

[020-189]garden to the porch, which was exactly in the middle of the house

[020-190]front, with two windows on each side. Right and left of the path were

[020-191]first a bed of gooseberry bushes; next of currant; next of raspberry;

[020-192]next of strawberry; next of old-fashioned flowers; at the corners

[020-193]opposite the porch being spheres of box resembling a pair of school

[020-194]globes. Over the roof of the house could be seen the [[orchard-culture|orchard]], on yet

[020-195]higher ground, and behind the orchard [[the-woodland-tracts|the forest]]-trees, reaching up to

[020-196]the crest of the hill.
