# Section 48

[048-001]By degrees, as she sat, [[felice-charmond|Felice]]’s mind—helped perhaps by the anticlimax

[048-002]of learning that her lover was unharmed after all her fright about

[048-003]him—grew wondrously strong in wise resolve. For the moment she was in a

[048-004]mood, in the words of Mrs. Elizabeth Montagu, “to run mad with

[048-005]discretion;” and was so persuaded that discretion lay in departure that

[048-006]she wished to set about going that very minute. Jumping up from her

[048-007]seat, she began to gather together some small personal knick-knacks

[048-008]scattered about the room, to feel that preparations were really in

[048-009]train.

[048-010]While moving here and there she fancied that she heard a slight noise

[048-011]out-of-doors, and stood still. Surely it was a tapping at the window. A

[048-012]thought entered her mind, and burned her cheek. He had come to that

[048-013]window before; yet was it possible that he should dare to do so now!

[048-014]All the servants were in bed, and in the ordinary course of affairs she

[048-015]would have retired also. Then she remembered that on stepping in by the

[048-016]casement and closing it, she had not fastened the window-shutter, so

[048-017]that a streak of light from the interior of the room might have

[048-018]revealed her vigil to an observer on the lawn. How all things conspired

[048-019]against her keeping faith with Grace!

[048-020]The tapping recommenced, light as from the bill of a little bird; her

[048-021]illegitimate hope overcame her vow; she went and pulled back the

[048-022]shutter, determining, however, to shake her head at him and keep the

[048-023]casement securely closed.

[048-024]What she saw outside might have struck terror into a heart stouter than

[048-025]a helpless woman’s at midnight. In the centre of the lowest pane of the

[048-026]window, close to the glass, was a human face, which she barely

[048-027]recognized as the face of [[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Fitzpiers]]. It was surrounded with the

[048-028]darkness of the night without, corpse-like in its pallor, and covered

[048-029]with blood. As disclosed in the square area of the pane it met her

[048-030]frightened eyes like a replica of the Sudarium of St. Veronica.

[048-031]He moved his lips, and looked at her imploringly. Her rapid mind pieced

[048-032]together in an instant a possible concatenation of events which might

[048-033]have led to this tragical issue. She unlatched the casement with a

[048-034]terrified hand, and bending down to where he was crouching, pressed her

[048-035]face to his with passionate solicitude. She assisted him into the room

[048-036]without a word, to do which it was almost necessary to lift him bodily.

[048-037]Quickly closing the window and fastening the shutters, she bent over

[048-038]him breathlessly.

[048-039]“Are you hurt much—much?” she cried, faintly. “Oh, oh, how is this!”

[048-040]“Rather much—but don’t be frightened,” he answered in a difficult

[048-041]whisper, and turning himself to obtain an easier position if possible.

[048-042]“A little water, please.”

[048-043]She ran across into the dining-room, and brought a bottle and glass,

[048-044]from which he eagerly drank. He could then speak much better, and with

[048-045]her help got upon the nearest couch.

[048-046]“Are you dying, Edgar?” she said. “Do speak to me!”

[048-047]“I am half dead,” said Fitzpiers. “But perhaps I shall get over

[048-048]it....It is chiefly loss of blood.”

[048-049]“But I thought your fall did not hurt you,” said she. “Who did this?”

[048-050]“Felice—my father-in-law!...I have crawled to you more than a mile on

[048-051]my hands and knees—God, I thought I should never have got here!...I

[048-052]have come to you—because you are the only friend—I have in the world

[048-053]now....I can never go back to [[little-hintock|Hintock]]—never—to the roof of the

[048-054]Melburys! Not poppy nor mandragora will ever medicine this bitter

[048-055]feud!...If I were only well again—”

[048-056]“Let me bind your head, now that you have rested.”

[048-057]“Yes—but wait a moment—it has stopped bleeding, fortunately, or I

[048-058]should be a dead man before now. While in the wood I managed to make a

[048-059]tourniquet of some half-pence and my handkerchief, as well as I could

[048-060]in the dark....But listen, dear Felice! Can you hide me till I am well?

[048-061]Whatever comes, I can be seen in Hintock no more. My practice is nearly

[048-062]gone, you know—and after this I would not care to recover it if I

[048-063]could.”

[048-064]By this time Felice’s tears began to blind her. Where were now her

[048-065]discreet plans for sundering their lives forever? To administer to him

[048-066]in his pain, and trouble, and poverty, was her single thought. The

[048-067]first step was to hide him, and she asked herself where. A place

[048-068]occurred to her mind.

[048-069]She got him some wine from the dining-room, which strengthened him

[048-070]much. Then she managed to remove his boots, and, as he could now keep

[048-071]himself upright by leaning upon her on one side and a walking-stick on

[048-072]the other, they went thus in slow march out of the room and up the

[048-073]stairs. At the top she took him along a gallery, pausing whenever he

[048-074]required rest, and thence up a smaller staircase to the least used part

[048-075]of the house, where she unlocked a door. Within was a lumber-room,

[048-076]containing abandoned furniture of all descriptions, built up in piles

[048-077]which obscured the light of the windows, and formed between them nooks

[048-078]and lairs in which a person would not be discerned even should an eye

[048-079]gaze in at the door. The articles were mainly those that had belonged

[048-080]to the previous owner of the house, and had been bought in by the late

[048-081]Mr. Charmond at the auction; but changing fashion, and the tastes of a

[048-082]young wife, had caused them to be relegated to this dungeon.

[048-083]Here Fitzpiers sat on the floor against the wall till she had hauled

[048-084]out materials for a bed, which she spread on the floor in one of the

[048-085]aforesaid nooks. She obtained water and a basin, and washed the dried

[048-086]blood from his face and hands; and when he was comfortably reclining,

[048-087]fetched food from the larder. While he ate her eyes lingered anxiously

[048-088]on his face, following its every movement with such loving-kindness as

[048-089]only a fond woman can show.

[048-090]He was now in better condition, and discussed his position with her.

[048-091]“What I fancy I said to [[george-melbury|Melbury]] must have been enough to enrage any

[048-092]man, if uttered in cold blood, and with knowledge of his presence. But

[048-093]I did not know him, and I was stupefied by what he had given me, so

[048-094]that I hardly was aware of what I said. Well—the veil of that temple is

[048-095]rent in twain!...As I am not going to be seen again in Hintock, my

[048-096]first efforts must be directed to allay any alarm that may be felt at

[048-097]my absence, before I am able to get clear away. Nobody must suspect

[048-098]that I have been hurt, or there will be a country talk about me.

[048-099]Felice, I must at once concoct a letter to check all search for me. I

[048-100]think if you can bring me a pen and paper I may be able to do it now. I

[048-101]could rest better if it were done. Poor thing! how I tire her with

[048-102]running up and down!”

[048-103]She fetched writing materials, and held up the blotting-book as a

[048-104]support to his hand, while he penned a brief note to his nominal wife.

[048-105]“The animosity shown towards me by your father,” he wrote, in this

[048-106]coldest of marital epistles, “is such that I cannot return again to a

[048-107]roof which is his, even though it shelters you. A parting is

[048-108]unavoidable, as you are sure to be on his side in this division. I am

[048-109]starting on a journey which will take me a long way from Hintock, and

[048-110]you must not expect to see me there again for some time.”

[048-111]He then gave her a few directions bearing upon his professional

[048-112]engagements and other practical matters, concluding without a hint of

[048-113]his destination, or a notion of when she would see him again. He

[048-114]offered to read the note to Felice before he closed it up, but she

[048-115]would not hear or see it; that side of his obligations distressed her

[048-116]beyond endurance. She turned away from Fitzpiers, and sobbed bitterly.

[048-117]“If you can get this posted at a place some miles away,” he whispered,

[048-118]exhausted by the effort of writing—“at [[shottsford-forum|Shottsford]] or Port-Bredy, or

[048-119]still better, [[budmouth|Budmouth]]—it will divert all suspicion from this house as

[048-120]the place of my refuge.”

[048-121]“I will drive to one or other of the places myself—anything to keep it

[048-122]unknown,” she murmured, her voice weighted with vague foreboding, now

[048-123]that the excitement of helping him had passed away.

[048-124]Fitzpiers told her that there was yet one thing more to be done. “In

[048-125]creeping over the fence on to the lawn,” he said, “I made the rail

[048-126]bloody, and it shows rather much on the white paint—I could see it in

[048-127]the dark. At all hazards it should be washed off. Could you do that

[048-128]also, Felice?”

[048-129]What will not [[gender-roles|women]] do on such devoted occasions? Weary as she was she

[048-130]went all the way down the rambling staircases to the ground-floor, then

[048-131]to search for a lantern, which she lighted and hid under her cloak;

[048-132]then for a wet sponge, and next went forth into the night. The white

[048-133]railing stared out in the darkness at her approach, and a ray from the

[048-134]enshrouded lantern fell upon the blood—just where he had told her it

[048-135]would be found. She shuddered. It was almost too much to bear in one

[048-136]day—but with a shaking hand she sponged the rail clean, and returned to

[048-137]the house.

[048-138]The time occupied by these several proceedings was not much less than

[048-139]two hours. When all was done, and she had smoothed his extemporized

[048-140]bed, and placed everything within his reach that she could think of,

[048-141]she took her leave of him, and locked him in.

[048-142]CHAPTER XXXVII.

[048-143]When her husband’s letter reached Grace’s hands, bearing upon it the

[048-144]postmark of a distant town, it never once crossed her mind that

[048-145]Fitzpiers was within a mile of her still. She felt relieved that he did

[048-146]not write more bitterly of the quarrel with her father, whatever its

[048-147][[nature-in-the-woodlanders|nature]] might have been; but the general frigidity of his communication

[048-148]quenched in her the incipient spark that events had kindled so shortly

[048-149]before.

[048-150]From this centre of information it was made known in Hintock that the

[048-151]doctor had gone away, and as none but the Melbury household was aware

[048-152]that he did not return on the night of his accident, no excitement

[048-153]manifested itself in the village.

[048-154]Thus the early days of May passed by. None but the nocturnal birds and

[048-155]animals observed that late one evening, towards the middle of the

[048-156]month, a closely wrapped figure, with a crutch under one arm and a

[048-157]stick in his hand, crept out from [[hintock-house|Hintock House]] across the lawn to the

[048-158]shelter of the trees, taking thence a slow and laborious walk to the

[048-159]nearest point of the turnpike-road. The mysterious personage was so

[048-160]disguised that his own wife would hardly have known him. Felice

[048-161]Charmond was a practised hand at make-ups, as well she might be; and

[048-162]she had done her utmost in padding and painting Fitzpiers with the old

[048-163]materials of her art in the recesses of the lumber-room.

[048-164]In the highway he was met by a covered carriage, which conveyed him to

[048-165][[sherton-abbas|Sherton]]-Abbas, whence he proceeded to the nearest port on the south

[048-166]coast, and immediately crossed the Channel.

[048-167]But it was known to everybody that three days after this time Mrs.

[048-168]Charmond executed her long-deferred plan of setting out for a long term

[048-169]of travel and residence on the Continent. She went off one morning as

[048-170]unostentatiously as could be, and took no maid with her, having, she

[048-171]said, engaged one to meet her at a point farther on in her route. After

[048-172]that, Hintock House, so frequently deserted, was again to be let.

[048-173]Spring had not merged in summer when a clinching rumor, founded on the

[048-174]best of evidence, reached [[the-hintock-parish|the parish]] and neighborhood. Mrs. Charmond

[048-175]and Fitzpiers had been seen together in Baden, in relations which set

[048-176]at rest the question that had agitated the little [[community-and-kinship|community]] ever since

[048-177]the winter.
