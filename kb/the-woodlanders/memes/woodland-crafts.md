The woodland crafts of Little Hintock form the material and symbolic core of *The Woodlanders*. The novel opens with Marty South making spars by firelight, and the rhythms of spar-making, copse-work, and timber trades provide the economic backdrop against which the human drama unfolds. These crafts are not mere local color — they define social identity, economic survival, and the precarious relationship between rural laborers and the natural world.

## Spar-Making

Spar-making is the novel's signature craft. A "spar" is a wooden rod used for thatching roofs, and "spar-gads" are the raw hazel rods from which spars are split [001-611]. The novel opens with a detailed portrait of Marty South at work:

> "She was making spars, such as are used by thatchers, and had a bundle of spar-gads — the raw material of her manufacture — on her right, a heap of chips and ends — the refuse — with which the fire was maintained." [001-603]

Marty's skill is exceptional. When Giles Winterborne inspects her work, he offers the highest praise a woodlander can give: "I never made a spar better than that. They are too good for the thatching of houses — they are good enough for the furniture" [001-149].

The economic reality is harsh. Marty earns a meager piece-rate:

> "How much do you get for making these spars?" "Haff-a-crown a bundle. Not much, is it?" [001-749]

The travelling spar-makers appear seasonally: "travelling spar-makers from White-hart Lane, who, when this kind of work began, made their appearance regularly, and when it ended, disappeared" [001-169]. Their itinerant existence underscores the precariousness of woodland labor.

## Copse-Ware and Coppice Work

Beyond spar-making, the woodland economy encompassed a range of "copse-ware" products — hurdles, sheep-cribs, ash poles, and thatching materials [001-110]. Melbury's yard exemplifies the diversity:

> "One was laden with sheep-cribs, another with hurdles, another with ash poles, and the fourth, at the foot of which she had placed her thatching-spars, was half full of similar bundles." [001-110]

George Melbury is "the timber, bark, and copse-ware merchant" [001-108] for whom Marty's father worked. The shed near his house was "used for spar-making, sawing, crib-framing, and copse-ware manufacture in general" [001-164].

"Copse-work," as Hardy notes, was an occupation pursued by "the secondary的手艺人" — those below the timber merchants in the woodland hierarchy [001-175].

## Bark-Stripping

Bark-stripping was a seasonal woodland occupation tied to the tanning industry. The bark of oak trees was peeled and sold to tanneries for leather production. In the woodland economy, "barking" represented another income stream for laborers, performed at specific times of year when the sap was rising and bark could be cleanly separated. The craft required skill and timing — a mistimed cut could damage the tree and reduce its timber value.

## Social Hierarchy of Woodland Labor

The crafts establish a clear social hierarchy. At the top stand timber merchants like George Melbury, who own the trees and control the trade. Below them are skilled craftsmen like Giles Winterborne, who plants trees, manages woodland, and travels to negotiate timber sales. At the base are piece-workers like Marty South and her father, who make spars by the bundle for meager pay, and the travelling spar-makers who appear seasonally.

This hierarchy is not merely economic but carries social and romantic consequences. Giles's loss of his houses — his lifehold property [001-710] — threatens to demote him from the class of independent craftsmen to that of dependent laborers, and thereby destroys his marriage prospects with Grace Melbury.

## The Aesthetic of Craft

Hardy invests the woodland crafts with a dignity that transcends their economic value. Marty's work is described with almost devotional attention:

> "The scene formed by the girlish spar-maker composed itself into a unity without flaw" [001-709]

The crafts embody a way of life in harmony with natural cycles — seasonal, skilled, and deeply connected to the woodland environment. Their threatened disappearance, like the precarious lifeholds, represents the erosion of an ancient rural order by modern economic forces.
