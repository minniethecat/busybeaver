# Felice Charmond

Felice Charmond is the wealthy widow who occupies Hintock House, the great house that presides over the woodland world. She is "Mrs. Charmond, the owner of the surrounding woods" (005-044) — a figure of glamour, power, and vulnerability whose involvement with Fitzpiers precipitates the novel's climactic crisis. Her name, "Felice Charmond," (036-013) with its French-latinate elegance, signals her distinction from the Anglo-Saxon plainness of the woodland world — a distinction that is at once her allure and her isolation.

## Biography

### The Widow of Hintock House

Mrs. Charmond enters the novel as an absence made present by wealth and property. She is "Mrs. Charmond" (002-126) — the title conveying both her marital status and her social position — the owner of the woods, the cottages, and the economic infrastructure on which the woodland community depends. Her late husband "brought her here" (011-122) — her presence in Little Hintock is the result of marriage, not native belonging, and this fact shapes her relationship to the community she dominates.

She is a woman alone. Having quarreled with her husband before his death, she is "left desolate" (011-034), her wealth unable to compensate for the emotional isolation that is its cost. "Mrs. Charmond was at the end of a gallery" (011-037) when Grace first encounters her — the physical image of her social position: remote, elevated, and entirely alone.

### The Patron and the Threat

Mrs. Charmond's relationship to the woodland community is fundamentally ambivalent. She is a patron — she "was wanting some refined young person" (004-002) as a companion, the phrase suggesting both her isolation and her power to command. Yet she is also a threat: her ownership of the life-hold houses means that she can, at will, dispossess the woodlanders who live in them. Her steward and agent are figures of fear as much as deference, their authority deriving from a woman who remains physically and emotionally remote from the community she governs.

Her purchase of Marty South's hair (002-126) is emblematic. The transaction — a wealthy woman buying the hair of a poor one to enhance her own beauty — condenses the economic relationship between Hintock House and the woodland cottages: wealth consumes poverty, and the consumption is invisible to the consumer.

### The Affair with Fitzpiers

Mrs. Charmond's affair with Fitzpiers is the novel's most socially complex relationship. They are, in class terms, equals — both outsiders to the woodland world, both possessors of a cultivation that distinguishes them from the locals. Their attraction is genuine, grounded in shared sensibility rather than mere appetite. Yet the affair is also destructive: it shatters Grace's marriage, accelerates Giles's decline, and produces consequences that ripple through the entire community.

The affair's discovery precipitates violence. Grace, confronting her rival, "confronted and attacked Felice Charmond _unguibus et rostris_" (040-047) — with tooth and claw, the Latin phrase dignifying a scene of raw female rage. The confrontation strips Mrs. Charmond of her social protection, revealing her as a woman vulnerable to the same passions she has provoked in others.

### Death

Mrs. Charmond's death, occurring offstage, is the novel's final commentary on her position. "The patient, and the woman had been Felice Charmond" (058-032) — her identity revealed only in death, her life reduced to a medical case. The woman who dominated the woodland world from Hintock House dies as she lived: alone, her fate noted by others only when it affects their own interests.

## Character and Personality

Mrs. Charmond is defined by the gap between her social power and her emotional vulnerability. She commands the economic life of the woodland community, yet she is personally isolated, "left desolate" by a husband she quarreled with and a society that offers wealthy widows no place of genuine belonging. Her cultivation is real — she "was far too well-practised a woman of the world" (011-142) — but it serves less as a resource than as a barrier, separating her from the very connections she craves.

Her attraction to Fitzpiers reflects this isolation. He is the one person in the woodland world who can meet her as a social equal, who shares her tastes and her detachment from the local community. That this attraction should prove destructive — to her, to him, to Grace, to Giles — is Hardy's characteristic irony: the very qualities that make them suited to each other make them dangerous to everyone around them.

## Role in the Narrative

Mrs. Charmond functions as the novel's embodiment of class power and its costs. She occupies the apex of the social pyramid — the owner of the woods, the mistress of the great house, the woman whose decisions determine the economic fate of every woodlander — yet she is herself a prisoner of her position, isolated by the very wealth that should liberate her.

Her structural role is that of the rival — the woman who threatens Grace's marriage by offering Fitzpiers what Grace cannot: social equality, shared cultivation, and the allure of the forbidden. Yet Hardy's treatment of her resists simple villainy. She is at once predator and prey, the agent of others' suffering and the victim of her own.

## Relationships

- **[[dr-edred-fitzpiers]]**: Her lover, the one man in the woodland world who can match her in cultivation and class. Their relationship is the novel's most socially symmetrical — and its most destructive.
- **[[grace-melbury]]**: Her rival, whose marriage she threatens and whose fury she endures. The confrontation between them is one of the novel's most powerful scenes, stripping both women of their social protections.
- **[[giles-winterborne]]**: Her tenant, whose houses revert to her estate upon the deaths of their life-holders — a property relationship that embodies the economic asymmetry between Hintock House and the woodland cottages.
- **[[marty-south]]**: The poor girl whose hair she purchases — a transaction that condenses the class dynamics of the entire novel into a single, devastating image.

## Critical Analysis

Hardy's treatment of Mrs. Charmond reveals the novelist's complex understanding of female power in Victorian society. She is at once the most powerful character in the novel — her wealth and property give her a freedom that Grace, Marty, and Suke cannot imagine — and one of the most vulnerable, isolated by the very position that should protect her. Her death, occurring offstage and noted only in passing, is the novel's bleakest commentary on the fate of women who, however powerful, remain dependent on men for the emotional connection that makes life meaningful.

> "Felice Charmond was in no mood to retire to rest" (047-149). The sentence captures her essential condition: alone, wakeful, haunted by the consequences of desires that she possessed the power to act on but not the power to control. She is the novel's most tragic figure — a woman whose wealth purchases everything except what she most needs.
