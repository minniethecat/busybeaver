# Section 29

[029-001]“’Twasn’t necessary that you should, ’Tis voluntary that rules in these

[029-002]things....Well, he has behaved very honorably, and asked my consent.

[029-003]You’ll know what to do when he gets here, I dare say. I needn’t tell

[029-004]you to make it all smooth for him.”

[029-005]“You mean, to lead him on to marry me?”

[029-006]“I do. Haven’t I educated you for it?”

[029-007]Grace looked out of the window and at the fireplace with no animation

[029-008]in her face. “Why is it settled off-hand in this way?” said she,

[029-009]coquettishly. “You’ll wait till you hear what I think of him, I

[029-010]suppose?”

[029-011]“Oh yes, of course. But you see what a good thing it will be.”

[029-012]She weighed the statement without speaking.

[029-013]“You will be restored to the society you’ve been taken away from,”

[029-014]continued her father; “for I don’t suppose he’ll stay here long.”

[029-015]She admitted the advantage; but it was plain that though [[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Fitzpiers]]

[029-016]exercised a certain fascination over her when he was present, or even

[029-017]more, an almost psychic influence, and though his impulsive act in the

[029-018]wood had stirred her feelings indescribably, she had never regarded him

[029-019]in the light of a destined husband. “I don’t know what to answer,” she

[029-020]said. “I have learned that he is very clever.”

[029-021]“He’s all right, and he’s coming here to see you.”

[029-022]A premonition that she could not resist him if he came strangely moved

[029-023]her. “Of course, father, you remember that it is only lately that

[029-024][[giles-winterborne|Giles]]—”

[029-025]“You know that you can’t think of him. He has given up all claim to

[029-026]you.”

[029-027]She could not explain the subtleties of her feeling as he could state

[029-028]his opinion, even though she had skill in speech, and her father had

[029-029]none. That Fitzpiers acted upon her like a dram, exciting her, throwing

[029-030]her into a novel atmosphere which biassed her doings until the

[029-031]influence was over, when she felt something of the [[nature-in-the-woodlanders|nature]] of regret for

[029-032]the mood she had experienced—still more if she reflected on the silent,

[029-033]almost sarcastic, criticism apparent in Winterborne’s air towards

[029-034]her—could not be told to this worthy couple in words.

[029-035]It so happened that on this very day Fitzpiers was called away from

[029-036][[little-hintock|Hintock]] by an engagement to attend some medical meetings, and his

[029-037]visits, therefore, did not begin at once. A note, however, arrived from

[029-038]him addressed to Grace, deploring his enforced absence. As a material

[029-039]object this note was pretty and superfine, a note of a sort that she

[029-040]had been unaccustomed to see since her return to Hintock, except when a

[029-041]school friend wrote to her—a rare instance, for the girls were

[029-042]respecters of persons, and many cooled down towards the [[timber-economy|timber]]-dealer’s

[029-043]daughter when she was out of sight. Thus the receipt of it pleased her,

[029-044]and she afterwards walked about with a reflective air.

[029-045]In the evening her father, who knew that the note had come, said, “Why

[029-046]be ye not sitting down to answer your letter? That’s what young folks

[029-047]did in my time.”

[029-048]She replied that it did not require an answer.

[029-049]“Oh, you know best,” he said. Nevertheless, he went about his business

[029-050]doubting if she were right in not replying; possibly she might be so

[029-051]mismanaging matters as to risk the loss of an alliance which would

[029-052]bring her much happiness.

[029-053][[george-melbury|Melbury]]’s respect for Fitzpiers was based less on his professional

[029-054]position, which was not much, than on the standing of his family in the

[029-055]county in by-gone days. That implicit faith in members of

[029-056]long-established families, as such, irrespective of their personal

[029-057]condition or character, which is still found among old-fashioned people

[029-058]in the rural districts reached its full intensity in Melbury. His

[029-059]daughter’s suitor was descended from a family he had heard of in his

[029-060]grandfather’s time as being once great, a family which had conferred

[029-061]its name upon a neighboring village; how, then, could anything be amiss

[029-062]in this betrothal?

[029-063]“I must keep her up to this,” he said to his wife. “She sees it is for

[029-064]her happiness; but still she’s young, and may want a little prompting

[029-065]from an older tongue.”

[029-066]CHAPTER XXIII.

[029-067]With this in view he took her out for a walk, a custom of his when he

[029-068]wished to say anything specially impressive. Their way was over the top

[029-069]of that lofty ridge dividing their woodland from the cider district,

[029-070]whence they had in the spring beheld the miles of apple-trees in bloom.

[029-071]All was now deep green. The spot recalled to Grace’s mind the last

[029-072]occasion of her presence there, and she said, “The promise of an

[029-073]enormous apple-crop is fulfilling itself, is it not? I suppose Giles is

[029-074]getting his mills and presses ready.”

[029-075]This was just what her father had not come there to talk about. Without

[029-076]replying he raised his arm, and moved his finger till he fixed it at a

[029-077]point. “There,” he said, “you see that plantation reaching over the

[029-078]hill like a great slug, and just behind the hill a particularly green

[029-079]sheltered bottom? That’s where Mr. Fitzpiers’s family were lords of the

[029-080]manor for I don’t know how many hundred years, and there stands the

[029-081]village of Buckbury Fitzpiers. A wonderful property ’twas—wonderful!”

[029-082]“But they are not lords of the manor there now.”

[029-083]“Why, no. But good and great things die as well as little and foolish.

[029-084]The only ones representing the family now, I believe, are our doctor

[029-085]and a maiden lady living I don’t know where. You can’t help being

[029-086]happy, Grace, in allying yourself with such a romantical family. You’ll

[029-087]feel as if you’ve stepped into history.”

[029-088]“We’ve been at Hintock as long as they’ve been at Buckbury; is it not

[029-089]so? You say our name occurs in old deeds continually.”

[029-090]“Oh yes—as yeomen, copyholders, and such like. But think how much

[029-091]better this will be for ’ee. You’ll be living a high intellectual life,

[029-092]such as has now become natural to you; and though the doctor’s practice

[029-093]is small here, he’ll no doubt go to a dashing town when he’s got his

[029-094]hand in, and keep a stylish carriage, and you’ll be brought to know a

[029-095]good many ladies of excellent society. If you should ever meet me then,

[029-096]Grace, you can drive past me, looking the other way. I shouldn’t expect

[029-097]you to speak to me, or wish such a thing, unless it happened to be in

[029-098]some lonely, private place where ’twouldn’t lower ye at all. Don’t

[029-099]think such men as [[community-and-kinship|neighbor]] Giles your equal. He and I shall be good

[029-100]friends enough, but he’s not for the like of you. He’s lived our rough

[029-101]and homely life here, and his wife’s life must be rough and homely

[029-102]likewise.”

[029-103]So much pressure could not but produce some displacement. As Grace was

[029-104]left very much to herself, she took advantage of one fine day before

[029-105]Fitzpiers’s return to drive into the aforesaid vale where stood the

[029-106]village of Buckbury Fitzpiers. Leaving her father’s man at the inn with

[029-107]the horse and gig, she rambled onward to the ruins of a castle, which

[029-108]stood in a field hard by. She had no doubt that it represented the

[029-109]ancient stronghold of the Fitzpiers family.

[029-110]The remains were few, and consisted mostly of remnants of the lower

[029-111]vaulting, supported on low stout columns surmounted by the _crochet_

[029-112]capital of the period. The two or three arches of these vaults that

[029-113]were still in position were utilized by the adjoining farmer as shelter

[029-114]for his calves, the floor being spread with straw, amid which the young

[029-115]creatures rustled, cooling their thirsty tongues by licking the quaint

[029-116]Norman carving, which glistened with the moisture. It was a degradation

[029-117]of even such a rude form of art as this to be treatad so grossly, she

[029-118]thought, and for the first time the family of Fitzpiers assumed in her

[029-119]imagination the hues of a melancholy romanticism.

[029-120]It was soon time to drive home, and she traversed the distance with a

[029-121]preoccupied mind. The idea of so modern a man in science and aesthetics

[029-122]as the young surgeon springing out of relics so ancient was a kind of

[029-123]novelty she had never before experienced. The combination lent him a

[029-124]social and intellectual interest which she dreaded, so much weight did

[029-125]it add to the strange influence he exercised upon her whenever he came

[029-126]near her.

[029-127]In an excitement which was not [[love-and-desire|love]], not ambition, rather a fearful

[029-128]consciousness of hazard in the air, she awaited his return.

[029-129]Meanwhile her father was awaiting him also. In his house there was an

[029-130]old work on medicine, published towards the end of the last century,

[029-131]and to put himself in harmony with events Melbury spread this work on

[029-132]his knees when he had done his day’s business, and read about Galen,

[029-133]Hippocrates, and Herophilus—of the dogmatic, the empiric, the

[029-134]hermetical, and other sects of practitioners that have arisen in

[029-135]history; and thence proceeded to the classification of maladies and the

[029-136]rules for their treatment, as laid down in this valuable book with

[029-137]absolute precision. Melbury regretted that the treatise was so old,

[029-138]fearing that he might in consequence be unable to hold as complete a

[029-139]conversation as he could wish with Mr. Fitzpiers, primed, no doubt,

[029-140]with more recent discoveries.

[029-141]The day of Fitzpiers’s return arrived, and he sent to say that he would

[029-142]call immediately. In the little time that was afforded for putting the

[029-143]house in order the sweeping of Melbury’s parlor was as the sweeping of

[029-144]the parlor at the Interpreter’s which wellnigh choked the Pilgrim. At

[029-145]the end of it [[mrs-melbury|Mrs. Melbury]] sat down, folded her hands and lips, and

[029-146]waited. Her husband restlessly walked in and out from the timber-yard,

[029-147]stared at the interior of the room, jerked out “ay, ay,” and retreated

[029-148]again. Between four and five Fitzpiers arrived, hitching his horse to

[029-149]the hook outside the door.

[029-150]As soon as he had walked in and perceived that Grace was not in the

[029-151]room, he seemed to have a misgiving. Nothing less than her actual

[029-152]presence could long keep him to the level of this impassioned

[029-153]enterprise, and that lacking he appeared as one who wished to retrace

[029-154]his steps.

[029-155]He mechanically talked at what he considered a woodland matron’s level

[029-156]of thought till a rustling was heard on the stairs, and Grace came in.

[029-157]Fitzpiers was for once as agitated as she. Over and above the genuine

[029-158]emotion which she raised in his heart there hung the sense that he was

[029-159]casting a die by impulse which he might not have thrown by judgment.

[029-160]Mr. Melbury was not in the room. Having to attend to matters in the

[029-161]yard, he had delayed putting on his afternoon coat and waistcoat till

[029-162]the doctor’s appearance, when, not wishing to be backward in receiving

[029-163]him, he entered the parlor hastily buttoning up those garments. Grace’s

[029-164]fastidiousness was a little distressed that Fitzpiers should see by

[029-165]this action the strain his visit was putting upon her father; and to

[029-166]make matters worse for her just then, old Grammer seemed to have a

[029-167]passion for incessantly pumping in the back kitchen, leaving the doors

[029-168]open so that the banging and splashing were distinct above the parlor

[029-169]conversation.

[029-170]Whenever the chat over the tea sank into pleasant desultoriness Mr.

[029-171]Melbury broke in with speeches of labored precision on very remote

[029-172]topics, as if he feared to let Fitzpiers’s mind dwell critically on the

[029-173]subject nearest the hearts of all. In truth a constrained manner was

[029-174]natural enough in Melbury just now, for the greatest interest of his

[029-175]life was reaching its crisis. Could the real have been beheld instead

[029-176]of the corporeal merely, the corner of the room in which he sat would

[029-177]have been filled with a form typical of anxious suspense, large-eyed,

[029-178]tight-lipped, awaiting the issue. That paternal hopes and fears so

[029-179]intense should be bound up in the person of one child so peculiarly

[029-180]circumstanced, and not have dispersed themselves over the larger field

[029-181]of a whole family, involved dangerous risks to future happiness.
