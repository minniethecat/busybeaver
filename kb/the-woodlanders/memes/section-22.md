# Section 22

[022-001]Nothing further passed between himself and the doctor in relation to

[022-002]Grace till they were on their way back. They had stopped at a way-side

[022-003]inn for a glass of brandy and cider hot, and when they were again in

[022-004]motion, [[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Fitzpiers]], possibly a little warmed by the liquor, resumed the

[022-005]subject by saying, “I should like very much to know who that young lady

[022-006]was.”

[022-007]“What difference can it make, if she’s only the tree your rainbow falls

[022-008]on?”

[022-009]“Ha! ha! True.”

[022-010]“You have no wife, sir?”

[022-011]“I have no wife, and no idea of one. I hope to do better things than

[022-012]marry and settle in [[little-hintock|Hintock]]. Not but that it is well for a medical man

[022-013]to be married, and sometimes, begad, ’twould be pleasant enough in this

[022-014]place, with the wind roaring round the house, and the rain and the

[022-015]boughs beating against it. I hear that you lost your life-holds by the

[022-016]death of South?”

[022-017]“I did. I lost in more ways than one.”

[022-018]They had reached the top of Hintock Lane or Street, if it could be

[022-019]called such where three-quarters of the road-side consisted of copse

[022-020]and [[orchard-culture|orchard]]. One of the first houses to be passed was [[george-melbury|Melbury]]’s. A

[022-021]light was shining from a bedroom window facing lengthwise of the lane.

[022-022][[giles-winterborne|Winterborne]] glanced at it, and saw what was coming. He had withheld an

[022-023]answer to the doctor’s inquiry to hinder his knowledge of Grace; but,

[022-024]as he thought to himself, “who hath gathered the wind in his fists? who

[022-025]hath bound the waters in a garment?” he could not hinder what was

[022-026]doomed to arrive, and might just as well have been outspoken. As they

[022-027]came up to the house, Grace’s figure was distinctly visible, drawing

[022-028]the two white curtains together which were used here instead of blinds.

[022-029]“Why, there she is!” said Fitzpiers. “How does she come there?”

[022-030]“In the most natural way in the world. It is her home. Mr. Melbury is

[022-031]her father.”

[022-032]“Oh, indeed—indeed—indeed! How comes he to have a daughter of that

[022-033]stamp?”

[022-034]Winterborne laughed coldly. “Won’t money do anything,” he said, “if

[022-035]you’ve promising material to work upon? Why shouldn’t a Hintock girl,

[022-036]taken early from home, and put under proper instruction, become as

[022-037]finished as any other young lady, if she’s got brains and good looks to

[022-038]begin with?”

[022-039]“No reason at all why she shouldn’t,” murmured the surgeon, with

[022-040]reflective disappointment. “Only I didn’t anticipate quite that kind of

[022-041]origin for her.”

[022-042]“And you think an inch or two less of her now.” There was a little

[022-043]tremor in Winterborne’s voice as he spoke.

[022-044]“Well,” said the doctor, with recovered warmth, “I am not so sure that

[022-045]I think less of her. At first it was a sort of blow; but, dammy! I’ll

[022-046]stick up for her. She’s charming, every inch of her!”

[022-047]“So she is,” said Winterborne, “but not to me.”

[022-048]From this ambiguous expression of the reticent woodlander’s, Dr.

[022-049]Fitzpiers inferred that Giles disliked [[grace-melbury|Miss Melbury]] because of some

[022-050]haughtiness in her bearing towards him, and had, on that account,

[022-051]withheld her name. The supposition did not tend to diminish his

[022-052]admiration for her.

[022-053]CHAPTER XVII.

[022-054]Grace’s exhibition of herself, in the act of pulling-to the

[022-055]window-curtains, had been the result of an unfortunate incident in the

[022-056]house that day—nothing less than the illness of [[grammer-oliver|Grammer Oliver]], a woman

[022-057]who had never till now lain down for such a reason in her life. Like

[022-058]others to whom unbroken years of health has made the idea of keeping

[022-059]their bed almost as repugnant as death itself, she had continued on

[022-060]foot till she literally fell on the floor; and though she had, as yet,

[022-061]been scarcely a day off duty, she had sickened into quite a different

[022-062]personage from the independent Grammer of the yard and spar-house. Ill

[022-063]as she was, on one point she was firm. On no account would she see a

[022-064]doctor; in other words, Fitzpiers.

[022-065]The room in which Grace had been discerned was not her own, but the old

[022-066]woman’s. On the girl’s way to bed she had received a message from

[022-067]Grammer, to the effect that she would much like to speak to her that

[022-068]night.

[022-069]Grace entered, and set the candle on a low chair beside the bed, so

[022-070]that the profile of Grammer as she lay cast itself in a keen shadow

[022-071]upon the whitened wall, her large head being still further magnified by

[022-072]an enormous turban, which was, really, her petticoat wound in a wreath

[022-073]round her temples. Grace put the room a little in order, and

[022-074]approaching the sick woman, said, “I am come, Grammer, as you wish. Do

[022-075]let us send for the doctor before it gets later.”

[022-076]“I will not have him,” said Grammer Oliver, decisively.

[022-077]“Then somebody to sit up with you.”

[022-078]“Can’t abear it! No; I wanted to see you, Miss Grace, because ’ch have

[022-079]something on my mind. Dear Miss Grace, _I took that money of the

[022-080]doctor, after all!_”

[022-081]“What money?”

[022-082]“The ten pounds.”

[022-083]Grace did not quite understand.

[022-084]“The ten pounds he offered me for my head, because I’ve a large brain.

[022-085]I signed a paper when I took the money, not feeling concerned about it

[022-086]at all. I have not liked to tell ye that it was really settled with

[022-087]him, because you showed such horror at the notion. Well, having thought

[022-088]it over more at length, I wish I hadn’t done it; and it weighs upon my

[022-089]mind. [[john-south|John South]]’s death of fear about the tree makes me think that I

[022-090]shall die of this....’Ch have been going to ask him again to let me

[022-091]off, but I hadn’t the face.”

[022-092]“Why?”

[022-093]“I’ve spent some of the money—more’n two pounds o’t. It do wherrit me

[022-094]terribly; and I shall die o’ the thought of that paper I signed with my

[022-095]holy cross, as South died of his trouble.”

[022-096]“If you ask him to burn the paper he will, I’m sure, and think no more

[022-097]of it.”

[022-098]“‘Ch have done it once already, miss. But he laughed cruel like. ‘Yours

[022-099]is such a fine brain, Grammer,’ ’er said, ‘that science couldn’t afford

[022-100]to lose you. Besides, you’ve taken my money.’...Don’t let your father

[022-101]know of this, please, on no account whatever!”

[022-102]“No, no. I will let you have the money to return to him.”

[022-103]Grammer rolled her head negatively upon the pillow. “Even if I should

[022-104]be well enough to take it to him, he won’t like it. Though why he

[022-105]should so particular want to look into the works of a poor old woman’s

[022-106]head-piece like mine when there’s so many other folks about, I don’t

[022-107]know. I know how he’ll answer me: ‘A lonely person like you, Grammer,’

[022-108]er woll say. ‘What difference is it to you what becomes of ye when the

[022-109]breath’s out of your body?’ Oh, it do trouble me! If you only knew how

[022-110]he do chevy me round the chimmer in my dreams, you’d pity me. How I

[022-111]could do it I can’t think! But ’ch was always so rackless!...If I only

[022-112]had anybody to plead for me!”

[022-113]“[[mrs-melbury|Mrs. Melbury]] would, I am sure.”

[022-114]“Ay; but he wouldn’t hearken to she! It wants a younger face than hers

[022-115]to work upon such as he.”

[022-116]Grace started with comprehension. “You don’t think he would do it for

[022-117]me?” she said.

[022-118]“Oh, wouldn’t he!”

[022-119]“I couldn’t go to him, Grammer, on any account. I don’t know him at

[022-120]all.”

[022-121]“Ah, if I were a young lady,” said the artful Grammer, “and could save

[022-122]a poor old woman’s skellington from a heathen doctor instead of a

[022-123]Christian grave, I would do it, and be glad to. But nobody will do

[022-124]anything for a poor old familiar friend but push her out of the way.”

[022-125]“You are very ungrateful, Grammer, to say that. But you are ill, I

[022-126]know, and that’s why you speak so. Now believe me, you are not going to

[022-127]die yet. Remember you told me yourself that you meant to keep him

[022-128]waiting many a year.”

[022-129]“Ay, one can joke when one is well, even in old age; but in sickness

[022-130]one’s gayety falters to grief; and that which seemed small looks large;

[022-131]and the grim far-off seems near.”

[022-132]Grace’s eyes had tears in them. “I don’t like to go to him on such an

[022-133]errand, Grammer,” she said, brokenly. “But I will, to ease your mind.”

[022-134]It was with extreme reluctance that Grace cloaked herself next morning

[022-135]for the undertaking. She was all the more indisposed to the journey by

[022-136]reason of Grammer’s allusion to the effect of a pretty face upon Dr.

[022-137]Fitzpiers; and hence she most illogically did that which, had the

[022-138]doctor never seen her, would have operated to stultify the sole motive

[022-139]of her journey; that is to say, she put on a woollen veil, which hid

[022-140]all her face except an occasional spark of her eyes.

[022-141]Her own wish that nothing should be known of this strange and grewsome

[022-142]proceeding, no less than Grammer Oliver’s own [[love-and-desire|desire]], led Grace to take

[022-143]every precaution against being discovered. She went out by the garden

[022-144]door as the safest way, all the household having occupations at the

[022-145]other side. The morning looked forbidding enough when she stealthily

[022-146]opened it. The battle between frost and thaw was continuing in mid-air:

[022-147]the [[nature-in-the-woodlanders|trees]] dripped on the garden-plots, where no vegetables would grow

[022-148]for the dripping, though they were planted year after year with that

[022-149]curious mechanical regularity of country people in the face of

[022-150]hopelessness; the moss which covered the once broad gravel terrace was

[022-151]swamped; and Grace stood irresolute. Then she thought of poor Grammer,

[022-152]and her dreams of the doctor running after her, scalpel in hand, and

[022-153]the possibility of a case so curiously similar to South’s ending in the

[022-154]same way; thereupon she stepped out into the drizzle.

[022-155]The nature of her errand, and Grammer Oliver’s account of the compact

[022-156]she had made, lent a fascinating horror to Grace’s conception of

[022-157]Fitzpiers. She knew that he was a young man; but her single object in

[022-158]seeking an interview with him put all considerations of his age and

[022-159]social aspect from her mind. Standing as she stood, in Grammer Oliver’s

[022-160]shoes, he was simply a remorseless Jove of the sciences, who would not

[022-161]have mercy, and would have sacrifice; a man whom, save for this, she

[022-162]would have preferred to avoid knowing. But since, in such a small

[022-163]village, it was improbable that any long time could pass without their

[022-164]meeting, there was not much to deplore in her having to meet him now.

[022-165]But, as need hardly be said, Miss Melbury’s view of the doctor as a

[022-166]merciless, unwavering, irresistible scientist was not quite in

[022-167]accordance with fact. The real Dr. Fitzpiers was a man of too many

[022-168]hobbies to show likelihood of rising to any great eminence in the

[022-169]profession he had chosen, or even to acquire any wide practice in the

[022-170]rural district he had marked out as his field of survey for the

[022-171]present. In the course of a year his mind was accustomed to pass in a

[022-172]grand solar sweep through all the zodiacal signs of the intellectual

[022-173]heaven. Sometimes it was in the Ram, sometimes in the Bull; one month

[022-174]he would be immersed in alchemy, another in poesy; one month in the

[022-175]Twins of astrology and astronomy; then in the Crab of German literature

[022-176]and metaphysics. In justice to him it must be stated that he took such

[022-177]studies as were immediately related to his own profession in turn with

[022-178]the rest, and it had been in a month of anatomical ardor without the

[022-179]possibility of a subject that he had proposed to Grammer Oliver the

[022-180]terms she had mentioned to her mistress.

[022-181]As may be inferred from the tone of his conversation with Winterborne,

[022-182]he had lately plunged into abstract philosophy with much zest; perhaps

[022-183]his keenly appreciative, modern, unpractical mind found this a realm

[022-184]more to his taste than any other. Though his aims were desultory,

[022-185]Fitzpiers’s mental constitution was not without its admirable side; a

[022-186]keen inquirer he honestly was, even if the midnight rays of his lamp,

[022-187]visible so far through the trees of Hintock, lighted rank literatures

[022-188]of emotion and passion as often as, or oftener than, the books and

[022-189]_matériel_ of science.
