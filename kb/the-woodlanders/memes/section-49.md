# Section 49

[049-001][[george-melbury|Melbury]] had entered the Valley of Humiliation even farther than Grace.

[049-002]His spirit seemed broken.

[049-003]But once a week he mechanically went to market as usual, and here, as

[049-004]he was passing by the conduit one day, his mental condition expressed

[049-005]largely by his gait, he heard his name spoken by a voice formerly

[049-006]familiar. He turned and saw a certain [[fred-beaucock|Fred Beaucock]]—once a promising

[049-007]lawyer’s clerk and local dandy, who had been called the cleverest

[049-008]fellow in [[sherton-abbas|Sherton]], without whose brains the firm of solicitors

[049-009]employing him would be nowhere. But later on Beaucock had fallen into

[049-010]the mire. He was invited out a good deal, sang songs at agricultural

[049-011]meetings and burgesses’ dinners; in sum, victualled himself with

[049-012]spirits more frequently than was good for the clever brains or body

[049-013]either. He lost his situation, and after an absence spent in trying his

[049-014]powers elsewhere, came back to his native town, where, at the time of

[049-015]the foregoing events in [[little-hintock|Hintock]], he gave legal advice for astonishingly

[049-016]small fees—mostly carrying on his profession on public-house settles,

[049-017]in whose recesses he might often have been overheard making

[049-018]country-people’s wills for half a crown; calling with a learned voice

[049-019]for pen-and-ink and a halfpenny sheet of paper, on which he drew up the

[049-020]testament while resting it in a little space wiped with his hand on the

[049-021]table amid the liquid circles formed by the cups and glasses. An idea

[049-022]implanted early in life is difficult to uproot, and many elderly

[049-023]tradespeople still clung to the notion that Fred Beaucock knew a great

[049-024]deal of law.

[049-025]It was he who had called Melbury by name. “You look very down, Mr.

[049-026]Melbury—very, if I may say as much,” he observed, when the

[049-027][[timber-economy|timber]]-merchant turned. “But I know—I know. A very sad case—very. I was

[049-028]bred to the law, as you know, and am professionally no stranger to such

[049-029]matters. Well, [[grace-melbury|Mrs. Fitzpiers]] has her remedy.”

[049-030]“How—what—a remedy?” said Melbury.

[049-031]“Under the new law, sir. A new court was established last year, and

[049-032]under the new statute, twenty and twenty-one Vic., cap. eighty-five,

[049-033]unmarrying is as easy as marrying. No more Acts of Parliament

[049-034]necessary; no longer one law for the rich and another for the poor. But

[049-035]come inside—I was just going to have a nibleykin of rum hot—I’ll

[049-036]explain it all to you.”

[049-037]The intelligence amazed Melbury, who saw little of newspapers. And

[049-038]though he was a severely correct man in his habits, and had no taste

[049-039]for entering a tavern with Fred Beaucock—nay, would have been quite

[049-040]uninfluenced by such a character on any other matter in the world—such

[049-041]fascination lay in the idea of delivering his poor girl from bondage,

[049-042]that it deprived him of the critical faculty. He could not resist the

[049-043]ex-lawyer’s clerk, and entered the inn.

[049-044]Here they sat down to the rum, which Melbury paid for as a matter of

[049-045]course, Beaucock leaning back in the settle with a legal gravity which

[049-046]would hardly allow him to be conscious of the spirits before him,

[049-047]though they nevertheless disappeared with mysterious quickness.

[049-048]How much of the exaggerated information on the then new divorce laws

[049-049]which Beaucock imparted to his listener was the result of ignorance,

[049-050]and how much of dupery, was never ascertained. But he related such a

[049-051]plausible story of the ease with which Grace could become a free woman

[049-052]that her father was irradiated with the project; and though he scarcely

[049-053]wetted his lips, Melbury never knew how he came out of the inn, or when

[049-054]or where he mounted his gig to pursue his way homeward. But home he

[049-055]found himself, his brain having all the way seemed to ring sonorously

[049-056]as a gong in the intensity of its stir. Before he had seen Grace, he

[049-057]was accidentally met by [[giles-winterborne|Winterborne]], who found his face shining as if

[049-058]he had, like the Law-giver, conversed with an angel.

[049-059]He relinquished his horse, and took Winterborne by the arm to a heap of

[049-060]rendlewood—as barked oak was here called—which lay under a

[049-061]privet-hedge.

[049-062]“Giles,” he said, when they had sat down upon the logs, “there’s a new

[049-063]law in the land! Grace can be free quite easily. I only knew it by the

[049-064]merest accident. I might not have found it out for the next ten years.

[049-065]She can get rid of him—d’ye hear?—get rid of him. Think of that, my

[049-066]friend Giles!”

[049-067]He related what he had learned of the new legal remedy. A subdued

[049-068]tremulousness about the mouth was all the response that Winterborne

[049-069]made; and Melbury added, “My boy, you shall have her yet—if you want

[049-070]her.” His feelings had gathered volume as he said this, and the

[049-071]articulate sound of the old idea drowned his sight in mist.

[049-072]“Are you sure—about this new law?” asked Winterborne, so disquieted by

[049-073]a gigantic exultation which loomed alternately with fearful doubt that

[049-074]he evaded the full acceptance of Melbury’s last statement.

[049-075]Melbury said that he had no manner of doubt, for since his talk with

[049-076]Beaucock it had come into his mind that he had seen some time ago in

[049-077]the weekly paper an allusion to such a legal change; but, having no

[049-078]interest in those desperate remedies at the moment, he had passed it

[049-079]over. “But I’m not going to let the matter rest doubtful for a single

[049-080]day,” he continued. “I am going to [[london-in-the-woodlanders|London]]. Beaucock will go with me,

[049-081]and we shall get the best advice as soon as we possibly can. Beaucock

[049-082]is a thorough lawyer—nothing the matter with him but a fiery palate. I

[049-083]knew him as the stay and refuge of Sherton in knots of law at one

[049-084]time.”

[049-085]Winterborne’s replies were of the vaguest. The new possibility was

[049-086]almost unthinkable by him at the moment. He was what was called at

[049-087]Hintock “a solid-going fellow;” he maintained his abeyant mood, not

[049-088]from want of reciprocity, but from a taciturn hesitancy, taught by life

[049-089]as he knew it.

[049-090]“But,” continued the timber-merchant, a temporary crease or two of

[049-091]anxiety supplementing those already established in his forehead by time

[049-092]and care, “Grace is not at all well. Nothing constitutional, you know;

[049-093]but she has been in a low, nervous state ever since that night of

[049-094]fright. I don’t doubt but that she will be all right soon....I wonder

[049-095]how she is this evening?” He rose with the words, as if he had too long

[049-096]forgotten her personality in the excitement of her previsioned career.

[049-097]They had sat till the evening was beginning to dye the garden brown,

[049-098]and now went towards Melbury’s house, Giles a few steps in the rear of

[049-099]his old friend, who was stimulated by the enthusiasm of the moment to

[049-100]outstep the ordinary walking of Winterborne. He felt shy of entering

[049-101]Grace’s presence as her reconstituted lover—which was how her father’s

[049-102]manner would be sure to present him—before definite information as to

[049-103]her future state was forthcoming; it seemed too nearly like the act of

[049-104]those who rush in where angels fear to tread.

[049-105]A chill to counterbalance all the glowing promise of the day was prompt

[049-106]enough in coming. No sooner had he followed the timber-merchant in at

[049-107]the door than he heard Grammer inform him that Mrs. [[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Fitzpiers]] was still

[049-108]more unwell than she had been in the morning. Old [[old-jones|Dr. Jones]] being in

[049-109]the neighborhood they had called him in, and he had instantly directed

[049-110]them to get her to bed. They were not, however, to consider her illness

[049-111]serious—a feverish, nervous attack the result of recent events, was

[049-112]what she was suffering from, and she would doubtless be well in a few

[049-113]days.

[049-114]Winterborne, therefore, did not remain, and his hope of seeing her that

[049-115]evening was disappointed. Even this aggravation of her morning

[049-116]condition did not greatly depress Melbury. He knew, he said, that his

[049-117]daughter’s constitution was sound enough. It was only these domestic

[049-118]troubles that were pulling her down. Once free she would be blooming

[049-119]again. Melbury diagnosed rightly, as parents usually do.

[049-120]He set out for London the next morning, Jones having paid another visit

[049-121]and assured him that he might leave home without uneasiness, especially

[049-122]on an errand of that sort, which would the sooner put an end to her

[049-123]suspense.

[049-124]The timber-merchant had been away only a day or two when it was told in

[049-125]Hintock that Mr. Fitzpiers’s hat had been found in the wood. Later on

[049-126]in the afternoon the hat was brought to Melbury, and, by a piece of

[049-127]ill-fortune, into Grace’s presence. It had doubtless lain in the wood

[049-128]ever since his fall from the horse, but it looked so clean and

[049-129]uninjured—the summer weather and leafy shelter having much favored its

[049-130]preservation—that Grace could not believe it had remained so long

[049-131]concealed. A very little of fact was enough to set her fevered fancy at

[049-132]work at this juncture; she thought him still in the neighborhood; she

[049-133]feared his sudden appearance; and her nervous malady developed

[049-134]consequences so grave that Dr. Jones began to look serious, and the

[049-135]household was alarmed.

[049-136]It was the beginning of June, and the cuckoo at this time of the summer

[049-137]scarcely ceased his cry for more than two or three hours during the

[049-138]night. The bird’s note, so familiar to her ears from infancy, was now

[049-139]absolute torture to the poor girl. On the Friday following the

[049-140]Wednesday of Melbury’s departure, and the day after the discovery of

[049-141]Fitzpiers’s hat, the cuckoo began at two o’clock in the morning with a

[049-142]sudden cry from one of Melbury’s apple-[[nature-in-the-woodlanders|trees]], not three yards from the

[049-143]window of Grace’s room.

[049-144]“Oh, he is coming!” she cried, and in her terror sprang clean from the

[049-145]bed out upon the floor.

[049-146]These starts and frights continued till noon; and when the doctor had

[049-147]arrived and had seen her, and had talked with [[mrs-melbury|Mrs. Melbury]], he sat down

[049-148]and meditated. That ever-present terror it was indispensable to remove

[049-149]from her mind at all hazards; and he thought how this might be done.

[049-150]Without saying a word to anybody in the house, or to the disquieted

[049-151]Winterborne waiting in the lane below, Dr. Jones went home and wrote to

[049-152]Mr. Melbury at the London address he had obtained from his wife. The

[049-153]gist of his communication was that Mrs. Fitzpiers should be assured as

[049-154]soon as possible that steps were being taken to sever the bond which

[049-155]was becoming a torture to her; that she would soon be free, and was

[049-156]even then virtually so. “If you can say it _at once_ it may be the

[049-157]means of averting much harm,” he said. “Write to herself; not to me.”

[049-158]On Saturday he drove over to Hintock, and assured her with mysterious

[049-159]pacifications that in a day or two she might expect to receive some

[049-160]assuring news. So it turned out. When Sunday morning came there was a

[049-161]letter for Grace from her father. It arrived at seven o’clock, the

[049-162]usual time at which the toddling postman passed by Hintock; at eight

[049-163]Grace awoke, having slept an hour or two for a wonder, and Mrs. Melbury

[049-164]brought up the letter.

[049-165]“Can you open it yourself?” said she.

[049-166]“Oh yes, yes!” said Grace, with feeble impatience. She tore the

[049-167]envelope, unfolded the sheet, and read; when a creeping blush tinctured

[049-168]her white neck and cheek.

[049-169]Her father had exercised a bold discretion. He informed her that she

[049-170]need have no further concern about Fitzpiers’s return; that she would

[049-171]shortly be a free woman; and therefore, if she should [[love-and-desire|desire]] to wed her

[049-172]old lover—which he trusted was the case, since it was his own deep

[049-173]wish—she would be in a position to do so. In this Melbury had not

[049-174]written beyond his belief. But he very much stretched the facts in

[049-175]adding that the legal formalities for dissolving her union were

[049-176]practically settled. The truth was that on the arrival of the doctor’s

[049-177]letter poor Melbury had been much agitated, and could with difficulty

[049-178]be prevented by Beaucock from returning to her bedside. What was the

[049-179]use of his rushing back to Hintock? Beaucock had asked him. The only

[049-180]thing that could do her any good was a breaking of the bond. Though he

[049-181]had not as yet had an interview with the eminent solicitor they were

[049-182]about to consult, he was on the point of seeing him; and the case was

[049-183]clear enough. Thus the simple Melbury, urged by his parental alarm at

[049-184]her danger by the representations of his companion, and by the doctor’s

[049-185]letter, had yielded, and sat down to tell her roundly that she was

[049-186]virtually free.
