# Section 58

[058-001]CHAPTER XLIII.

[058-002]She re-entered the hut, flung off her bonnet and cloak, and approached

[058-003]the sufferer. He had begun anew those terrible mutterings, and his

[058-004]hands were cold. As soon as she saw him there returned to her that

[058-005]agony of mind which the stimulus of her journey had thrown off for a

[058-006]time.

[058-007]Could he really be dying? She bathed him, kissed him, forgot all things

[058-008]but the fact that lying there before her was he who had loved her more

[058-009]than the mere lover would have loved; had martyred himself for her

[058-010]comfort, cared more for her self-respect than she had thought of

[058-011]caring. This mood continued till she heard quick, smart footsteps

[058-012]without; she knew whose footsteps they were.

[058-013]Grace sat on the inside of the bed against the wall, holding [[giles-winterborne|Giles]]’s

[058-014]hand, so that when her husband entered the patient lay between herself

[058-015]and him. He stood transfixed at first, noticing Grace only. Slowly he

[058-016]dropped his glance and discerned who the prostrate man was. Strangely

[058-017]enough, though Grace’s distaste for her husband’s company had amounted

[058-018]almost to dread, and culminated in actual flight, at this moment her

[058-019]last and least feeling was personal. Sensitive [[gender-roles|femininity]] was eclipsed

[058-020]by self-effacing purpose, and that it was a husband who stood there was

[058-021]forgotten. The first look that possessed her face was relief;

[058-022]satisfaction at the presence of the physician obliterated thought of

[058-023]the man, which only returned in the form of a sub-consciousness that

[058-024]did not interfere with her words.

[058-025]“Is he dying—is there any hope?” she cried.

[058-026]“Grace!” said [[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Fitzpiers]], in an indescribable whisper—more than

[058-027]invocating, if not quite deprecatory.

[058-028]He was arrested by the spectacle, not so much in its intrinsic

[058-029]character—though that was striking enough to a man who called himself

[058-030]the husband of the sufferer’s friend and nurse—but in its character as

[058-031]the counterpart of one that had its hour many months before, in which

[058-032]he had figured as the patient, and the woman had been [[felice-charmond|Felice Charmond]].

[058-033]“Is he in great danger—can you save him?” she cried again.

[058-034]Fitzpiers aroused himself, came a little nearer, and examined

[058-035]Winterborne as he stood. His inspection was concluded in a mere glance.

[058-036]Before he spoke he looked at her contemplatively as to the effect of

[058-037]his coming words.

[058-038]“He is dying,” he said, with dry precision.

[058-039]“What?” said she.

[058-040]“Nothing can be done, by me or any other man. It will soon be all over.

[058-041]The extremities are dead already.” His eyes still remained fixed on

[058-042]her; the conclusion to which he had come seeming to end his interest,

[058-043]professional and otherwise, in Winterborne forever.

[058-044]“But it cannot be! He was well three days ago.”

[058-045]“Not well, I suspect. This seems like a secondary attack, which has

[058-046]followed some previous illness—possibly typhoid—it may have been months

[058-047]ago, or recently.”

[058-048]“Ah—he was not well—you are right. He was ill—he was ill when I came.”

[058-049]There was nothing more to do or say. She crouched down at the side of

[058-050]the bed, and Fitzpiers took a seat. Thus they remained in silence, and

[058-051]long as it lasted she never turned her eyes, or apparently her

[058-052]thoughts, at all to her husband. He occasionally murmured, with

[058-053]automatic authority, some slight directions for alleviating the pain of

[058-054]the dying man, which she mechanically obeyed, bending over him during

[058-055]the intervals in silent tears.

[058-056]Winterborne never recovered consciousness of what was passing; and that

[058-057]he was going became soon perceptible also to her. In less than an hour

[058-058]the delirium ceased; then there was an interval of somnolent

[058-059]painlessness and soft breathing, at the end of which Winterborne passed

[058-060]quietly away.

[058-061]Then Fitzpiers broke the silence. “Have you lived here long?” said he.

[058-062]Grace was wild with sorrow—with all that had befallen her—with the

[058-063]cruelties that had attacked her—with life—with Heaven. She answered at

[058-064]random. “Yes. By what right do you ask?”

[058-065]“Don’t think I claim any right,” said Fitzpiers, sadly. “It is for you

[058-066]to do and say what you choose. I admit, quite as much as you feel, that

[058-067]I am a vagabond—a brute—not worthy to possess the smallest fragment of

[058-068]you. But here I am, and I have happened to take sufficient interest in

[058-069]you to make that inquiry.”

[058-070]“He is everything to me!” said Grace, hardly heeding her husband, and

[058-071]laying her hand reverently on the dead man’s eyelids, where she kept it

[058-072]a long time, pressing down their lashes with gentle touches, as if she

[058-073]were stroking a little bird.

[058-074]He watched her a while, and then glanced round the chamber where his

[058-075]eyes fell upon a few dressing necessaries that she had brought.

[058-076]“Grace—if I may call you so,” he said, “I have been already humiliated

[058-077]almost to the depths. I have come back since you refused to join me

[058-078]elsewhere—I have entered your father’s house, and borne all that that

[058-079]cost me without flinching, because I have felt that I deserved

[058-080][[pride-and-humiliation|humiliation]]. But is there a yet greater humiliation in store for me?

[058-081]You say you have been living here—that he is everything to you. Am I to

[058-082]draw from that the obvious, the extremest inference?”

[058-083]Triumph at any price is sweet to men and women—especially the latter.

[058-084]It was her first and last opportunity of repaying him for the cruel

[058-085]contumely which she had borne at his hands so docilely.

[058-086]“Yes,” she answered; and there was that in her subtly compounded [[nature-in-the-woodlanders|nature]]

[058-087]which made her feel a thrill of pride as she did so.

[058-088]Yet the moment after she had so mightily belied her character she half

[058-089]repented. Her husband had turned as white as the wall behind him. It

[058-090]seemed as if all that remained to him of life and spirit had been

[058-091]abstracted at a stroke. Yet he did not move, and in his efforts at

[058-092]self-control closed his mouth together as a vice. His determination was

[058-093]fairly successful, though she saw how very much greater than she had

[058-094]expected her triumph had been. Presently he looked across at

[058-095]Winterborne.

[058-096]“Would it startle you to hear,” he said, as if he hardly had breath to

[058-097]utter the words, “that she who was to me what he was to you is dead

[058-098]also?”

[058-099]“Dead—_she_ dead?” exclaimed Grace.

[058-100]“Yes. Felice Charmond is where this young man is.”

[058-101]“Never!” said Grace, vehemently.

[058-102]He went on without heeding the insinuation: “And I came back to try to

[058-103]make it up with you—but—”

[058-104]Fitzpiers rose, and moved across the room to go away, looking downward

[058-105]with the droop of a man whose hope was turned to apathy, if not

[058-106]despair. In going round the door his eye fell upon her once more. She

[058-107]was still bending over the body of Winterborne, her face close to the

[058-108]young man’s.

[058-109]“Have you been kissing him during his illness?” asked her husband.

[058-110]“Yes.”

[058-111]“Since his fevered state set in?”

[058-112]“Yes.”

[058-113]“On his lips?”

[058-114]“Yes.”

[058-115]“Then you will do well to take a few drops of this in water as soon as

[058-116]possible.” He drew a small phial from his pocket and returned to offer

[058-117]it to her.

[058-118]Grace shook her head.

[058-119]“If you don’t do as I tell you you may soon be like him.”

[058-120]“I don’t care. I wish to die.”

[058-121]“I’ll put it here,” said Fitzpiers, placing the bottle on a ledge

[058-122]beside him. “The sin of not having warned you will not be upon my head

[058-123]at any rate, among my other sins. I am now going, and I will send

[058-124]somebody to you. Your father does not know that you are here, so I

[058-125]suppose I shall be bound to tell him?”

[058-126]“Certainly.”

[058-127]Fitzpiers left the cot, and the stroke of his feet was soon immersed in

[058-128]the silence that pervaded the spot. Grace remained kneeling and

[058-129]weeping, she hardly knew how long, and then she sat up, covered poor

[058-130]Giles’s features, and went towards the door where her husband had

[058-131]stood. No sign of any other comer greeted her ear, the only perceptible

[058-132]sounds being the tiny cracklings of the dead leaves, which, like a

[058-133]feather-bed, had not yet done rising to their normal level where

[058-134]indented by the pressure of her husband’s receding footsteps. It

[058-135]reminded her that she had been struck with the change in his aspect;

[058-136]the extremely intellectual look that had always been in his face was

[058-137]wrought to a finer phase by thinness, and a care-worn dignity had been

[058-138]superadded. She returned to Winterborne’s side, and during her

[058-139]meditations another tread drew near the door, entered the outer room,

[058-140]and halted at the entrance of the chamber where Grace was.

[058-141]“What—[[marty-south|Marty]]!” said Grace.

[058-142]“Yes. I have heard,” said Marty, whose demeanor had lost all its

[058-143]girlishness under the stroke that seemed almost literally to have

[058-144]bruised her.

[058-145]“He died for me!” murmured Grace, heavily.

[058-146]Marty did not fully comprehend; and she answered, “He belongs to

[058-147]neither of us now, and your beauty is no more powerful with him than my

[058-148]plainness. I have come to help you, ma’am. He never cared for me, and

[058-149]he cared much for you; but he cares for us both alike now.”

[058-150]“Oh don’t, don’t, Marty!”

[058-151]Marty said no more, but knelt over Winterborne from the other side.

[058-152]“Did you meet my hus—Mr. Fitzpiers?”

[058-153]“No!”

[058-154]“Then what brought you here?”

[058-155]“I come this way sometimes. I have got to go to the farther side of the

[058-156]wood this time of the year, and am obliged to get there before four

[058-157]o’clock in the morning, to begin heating the oven for the early baking.

[058-158]I have passed by here often at this time.”

[058-159]Grace looked at her quickly. “Then did you know I was here?”

[058-160]“Yes, ma’am.”

[058-161]“Did you tell anybody?”

[058-162]“No. I knew you lived in the hut, that he had gied it up to ye, and

[058-163]lodged out himself.”

[058-164]“Did you know where he lodged?”

[058-165]“No. That I couldn’t find out. Was it at Delborough?”

[058-166]“No. It was not there, Marty. Would it had been! It would have

[058-167]saved—saved—” To check her tears she turned, and seeing a book on the

[058-168]window-bench, took it up. “Look, Marty, this is a Psalter. He was not

[058-169]an outwardly religious man, but he was pure and perfect in his heart.

[058-170]Shall we read a psalm over him?”

[058-171]“Oh yes—we will—with all my heart!”

[058-172]Grace opened the thin brown book, which poor Giles had kept at hand

[058-173]mainly for the convenience of whetting his pen-knife upon its leather

[058-174]covers. She began to read in that rich, devotional voice peculiar to

[058-175]women only on such occasions. When it was over, Marty said, “I should

[058-176]like to pray for his soul.”

[058-177]“So should I,” said her companion. “But we must not.”

[058-178]“Why? Nobody would know.”

[058-179]Grace could not resist the argument, influenced as she was by the sense

[058-180]of making amends for having neglected him in the body; and their tender

[058-181]voices united and filled the narrow room with supplicatory murmurs that

[058-182]a Calvinist might have envied. They had hardly ended when now and more

[058-183]numerous foot-falls were audible, also persons in conversation, one of

[058-184]whom Grace recognized as her father.

[058-185]She rose, and went to the outer apartment, in which there was only such

[058-186]light as beamed from the inner one. [[george-melbury|Melbury]] and [[mrs-melbury|Mrs. Melbury]] were

[058-187]standing there.

[058-188]“I don’t reproach you, Grace,” said her father, with an estranged

[058-189]manner, and in a voice not at all like his old voice. “What has come

[058-190]upon you and us is beyond reproach, beyond weeping, and beyond wailing.

[058-191]Perhaps I drove you to it. But I am hurt; I am scourged; I am

[058-192]astonished. In the face of this there is nothing to be said.”

[058-193]Without replying, Grace turned and glided back to the inner chamber.

[058-194]“Marty,” she said, quickly, “I cannot look my father in the face until

[058-195]he knows the true circumstances of my life here. Go and tell him—what

[058-196]you have told me—what you saw—that he gave up his house to me.”
