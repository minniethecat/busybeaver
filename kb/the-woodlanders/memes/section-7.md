# Section 7

[007-001]Nevertheless, this sort of support did not quite fill his children’s

[007-002]mouths, and they had to be filled. So, behind his house there was a

[007-003]little yard, reached by a passage from the back street, and in that

[007-004]yard was a pole, and under the pole a shop of quite another description

[007-005]than the ornamental one in the front street. Here on Saturday nights

[007-006]from seven till ten he took an almost innumerable succession of

[007-007]twopences from the farm laborers who flocked thither in crowds from the

[007-008]country. And thus he lived.

[007-009]Marty, of course, went to the front shop, and handed her packet to him

[007-010]silently. “Thank you,” said the barber, quite joyfully. “I hardly

[007-011]expected it after what you said last night.”

[007-012]She turned aside, while a tear welled up and stood in each eye at this

[007-013]reminder.

[007-014]“Nothing of what I told you,” he whispered, there being others in the

[007-015]shop. “But I can trust you, I see.”

[007-016]She had now reached the end of this distressing business, and went

[007-017]listlessly along the street to attend to other errands. These occupied

[007-018]her till four o’clock, at which time she recrossed the market-place. It

[007-019]was impossible to avoid rediscovering [[giles-winterborne|Winterborne]] every time she passed

[007-020]that way, for standing, as he always did at this season of the year,

[007-021]with his specimen apple-tree in the midst, the boughs rose above the

[007-022]heads of the crowd, and brought a delightful suggestion of orchards

[007-023]among the crowded buildings there. When her eye fell upon him for the

[007-024]last time he was standing somewhat apart, holding the tree like an

[007-025]ensign, and looking on the ground instead of pushing his produce as he

[007-026]ought to have been doing. He was, in fact, not a very successful seller

[007-027]either of his [[nature-in-the-woodlanders|trees]] or of his cider, his habit of speaking his mind,

[007-028]when he spoke at all, militating against this branch of his business.

[007-029]While she regarded him he suddenly lifted his eyes in a direction away

[007-030]from Marty, his face simultaneously kindling with recognition and

[007-031]surprise. She followed his gaze, and saw walking across to him a

[007-032]flexible young creature in whom she perceived the features of her she

[007-033]had known as Miss [[grace-melbury|Grace Melbury]], but now looking glorified and refined

[007-034]above her former level. Winterborne, being fixed to the spot by his

[007-035]apple-tree, could not advance to meet her; he held out his spare hand

[007-036]with his hat in it, and with some embarrassment beheld her coming on

[007-037]tiptoe through the mud to the middle of the square where he stood.

[007-038]Miss [[george-melbury|Melbury]]’s arrival so early was, as Marty could see, unexpected by

[007-039]Giles, which accounted for his not being ready to receive her. Indeed,

[007-040]her father had named five o’clock as her probable time, for which

[007-041]reason that hour had been looming out all the day in his forward

[007-042]perspective, like an important edifice on a plain. Now here she was

[007-043]come, he knew not how, and his arranged welcome stultified.

[007-044]His face became gloomy at her necessity for stepping into the road, and

[007-045]more still at the little look of embarrassment which appeared on hers

[007-046]at having to perform the meeting with him under an apple-tree ten feet

[007-047]high in the middle of the market-place. Having had occasion to take off

[007-048]the new gloves she had bought to come home in, she held out to him a

[007-049]hand graduating from pink at the tips of the fingers to white at the

[007-050]palm; and the reception formed a scene, with the tree over their heads,

[007-051]which was not by any means an ordinary one in [[sherton-abbas|Sherton Abbas]] streets.

[007-052]Nevertheless, the greeting on her looks and lips was of a restrained

[007-053]type, which perhaps was not unnatural. For true it was that Giles

[007-054]Winterborne, well-attired and well-mannered as he was for a yeoman,

[007-055]looked rough beside her. It had sometimes dimly occurred to him, in his

[007-056]ruminating silence at [[little-hintock|Little Hintock]], that external phenomena—such as

[007-057]the lowness or height or color of a hat, the fold of a coat, the make

[007-058]of a boot, or the [[fate-and-free-will|chance]] attitude or occupation of a limb at the

[007-059]instant of view—may have a great influence upon feminine opinion of a

[007-060]man’s worth—so frequently founded on non-essentials; but a certain

[007-061]causticity of mental tone towards himself and the world in general had

[007-062]prevented to-day, as always, any enthusiastic action on the strength of

[007-063]that reflection; and her momentary instinct of reserve at first sight

[007-064]of him was the penalty he paid for his laxness.

[007-065]He gave away the tree to a by-stander, as soon as he could find one who

[007-066]would accept the cumbersome gift, and the twain moved on towards the

[007-067]inn at which he had put up. Marty made as if to step forward for the

[007-068]pleasure of being recognized by Miss Melbury; but abruptly checking

[007-069]herself, she glided behind a carrier’s van, saying, dryly, “No; I baint

[007-070]wanted there,” and critically regarded Winterborne’s companion.

[007-071]It would have been very difficult to describe Grace Melbury with

[007-072]precision, either now or at any time. Nay, from the highest point of

[007-073]view, to precisely describe a human being, the focus of a universe—how

[007-074]impossible! But, apart from transcendentalism, there never probably

[007-075]lived a person who was in herself more completely a _reductio ad

[007-076]absurdum_ of attempts to appraise a woman, even externally, by items of

[007-077]face and figure. Speaking generally, it may be said that she was

[007-078]sometimes beautiful, at other times not beautiful, according to the

[007-079]state of her health and spirits.

[007-080]In simple corporeal presentment she was of a fair and clear complexion,

[007-081]rather pale than pink, slim in build and elastic in movement. Her look

[007-082]expressed a tendency to wait for others’ thoughts before uttering her

[007-083]own; possibly also to wait for others’ deeds before her own doing. In

[007-084]her small, delicate mouth, which had perhaps hardly settled down to its

[007-085]matured curves, there was a gentleness that might hinder sufficient

[007-086]self-assertion for her own good. She had well-formed eyebrows which,

[007-087]had her portrait been painted, would probably have been done in Prout’s

[007-088]or Vandyke brown.

[007-089]There was nothing remarkable in her dress just now, beyond a natural

[007-090]fitness and a style that was recent for the streets of Sherton. But,

[007-091]indeed, had it been the reverse, and quite striking, it would have

[007-092]meant just as little. For there can be hardly anything less connected

[007-093]with a woman’s personality than drapery which she has neither designed,

[007-094]manufactured, cut, sewed, or even seen, except by a glance of approval

[007-095]when told that such and such a shape and color must be had because it

[007-096]has been decided by others as imperative at that particular time.

[007-097]What people, therefore, saw of her in a cursory view was very little;

[007-098]in truth, mainly something that was not she. The woman herself was a

[007-099]shadowy, conjectural creature who had little to do with the outlines

[007-100]presented to Sherton eyes; a shape in the gloom, whose true description

[007-101]could only be approximated by putting together a movement now and a

[007-102]glance then, in that patient and long-continued attentiveness which

[007-103]nothing but watchful loving-kindness ever troubles to give.

[007-104]There was a little delay in their setting out from the town, and Marty

[007-105]South took advantage of it to hasten forward, with the view of escaping

[007-106]them on the way, lest they should feel compelled to spoil their

[007-107]_tête-à-tête_ by asking her to ride. She walked fast, and one-third of

[007-108]the journey was done, and the evening rapidly darkening, before she

[007-109]perceived any sign of them behind her. Then, while ascending a hill,

[007-110]she dimly saw their vehicle drawing near the lowest part of the

[007-111]incline, their heads slightly bent towards each other; drawn together,

[007-112]no doubt, by their souls, as the heads of a pair of horses well in hand

[007-113]are drawn in by the rein. She walked still faster.

[007-114]But between these and herself there was a carriage, apparently a

[007-115]brougham, coming in the same direction, with lighted lamps. When it

[007-116]overtook her—which was not soon, on account of her pace—the scene was

[007-117]much darker, and the lights glared in her eyes sufficiently to hide the

[007-118]details of the equipage.

[007-119]It occurred to Marty that she might take hold behind this carriage and

[007-120]so keep along with it, to save herself the mortification of being

[007-121]overtaken and picked up for pity’s sake by the coming pair.

[007-122]Accordingly, as the carriage drew abreast of her in climbing the long

[007-123]ascent, she walked close to the wheels, the rays of the nearest lamp

[007-124]penetrating her very pores. She had only just dropped behind when the

[007-125]carriage stopped, and to her surprise the coachman asked her, over his

[007-126]shoulder, if she would ride. What made the question more surprising was

[007-127]that it came in obedience to an order from the interior of the vehicle.

[007-128]Marty gladly assented, for she was weary, very weary, after working all

[007-129]night and keeping afoot all day. She mounted beside the coachman,

[007-130]wondering why this good-fortune had happened to her. He was rather a

[007-131]great man in aspect, and she did not like to inquire of him for some

[007-132]time.

[007-133]At last she said, “Who has been so kind as to ask me to ride?”

[007-134]“[[felice-charmond|Mrs. Charmond]],” replied her statuesque companion.

[007-135]Marty was stirred at the name, so closely connected with her last

[007-136]night’s experiences. “Is this her carriage?” she whispered.

[007-137]“Yes; she’s inside.”

[007-138]Marty reflected, and perceived that Mrs. Charmond must have recognized

[007-139]her plodding up the hill under the blaze of the lamp; recognized,

[007-140]probably, her stubbly poll (since she had kept away her face), and

[007-141]thought that those stubbles were the result of her own [[love-and-desire|desire]].

[007-142][[marty-south|Marty South]] was not so very far wrong. Inside the carriage a pair of

[007-143]bright eyes looked from a ripely handsome face, and though behind those

[007-144]bright eyes was a mind of unfathomed mysteries, beneath them there beat

[007-145]a heart capable of quick extempore warmth—a heart which could, indeed,

[007-146]be passionately and imprudently warm on certain occasions. At present,

[007-147]after recognizing the girl, she had acted on a mere impulse, possibly

[007-148]feeling gratified at the denuded appearance which signified the success

[007-149]of her agent in obtaining what she had required.

[007-150]“’Tis wonderful that she should ask ye,” observed the magisterial

[007-151]coachman, presently. “I have never known her do it before, for as a

[007-152]rule she takes no interest in the village folk at all.”

[007-153]Marty said no more, but occasionally turned her head to see if she

[007-154]could get a glimpse of the Olympian creature who as the coachman had

[007-155]truly observed, hardly ever descended from her clouds into the Tempe of

[007-156]the parishioners. But she could discern nothing of the lady. She also

[007-157]looked for Miss Melbury and Winterborne. The nose of their horse

[007-158]sometimes came quite near the back of Mrs. Charmond’s carriage. But

[007-159]they never attempted to pass it till the latter conveyance turned

[007-160]towards the park gate, when they sped by. Here the carriage drew up

[007-161]that the gate might be opened, and in the momentary silence Marty heard

[007-162]a gentle oral sound, soft as a breeze.

[007-163]“What’s that?” she whispered.

[007-164]“Mis’ess yawning.”

[007-165]“Why should she yawn?”

[007-166]“Oh, because she’s been used to such wonderfully good life, and finds

[007-167]it dull here. She’ll soon be off again on account of it.”

[007-168]“So rich and so powerful, and yet to yawn!” the girl murmured. “Then

[007-169]things don’t fay with she any more than with we!”

[007-170]Marty now alighted; the lamp again shone upon her, and as the carriage

[007-171]rolled on, a soft voice said to her from the interior, “Good-night.”

[007-172]“Good-night, ma’am,” said Marty. But she had not been able to see the

[007-173]woman who began so greatly to interest her—the second person of her own

[007-174]sex who had operated strongly on her mind that day.
