# Section 40

[040-001]He expected a scene at breakfast, but she only exhibited an extreme

[040-002]reserve. It was enough, however, to make him repent that he should have

[040-003]done anything to produce discomfort; for he attributed her manner

[040-004]entirely to what he had said. But Grace’s manner had not its cause

[040-005]either in his sayings or in his doings. She had not heard a single word

[040-006]of his regrets. Something even nearer home than her husband’s blighted

[040-007]prospects—if blighted they were—was the origin of her mood, a mood that

[040-008]was the mere continuation of what her father had noticed when he would

[040-009]have preferred a passionate [[jealousy-and-rivalry|jealousy]] in her, as the more natural.

[040-010]She had made a discovery—one which to a girl of honest [[nature-in-the-woodlanders|nature]] was

[040-011]almost appalling. She had looked into her heart, and found that her

[040-012]early interest in [[giles-winterborne|Giles Winterborne]] had become revitalized into

[040-013]luxuriant growth by her widening perceptions of what was great and

[040-014]little in life. His homeliness no longer offended her acquired tastes;

[040-015]his comparative want of so-called culture did not now jar on her

[040-016]intellect; his country dress even pleased her eye; his exterior

[040-017]roughness fascinated her. Having discovered by [[marriage-in-the-woodlanders|marriage]] how much that

[040-018]was humanly not great could co-exist with attainments of an exceptional

[040-019]order, there was a revulsion in her sentiments from all that she had

[040-020]formerly clung to in this kind: honesty, goodness, manliness,

[040-021]tenderness, devotion, for her only existed in their purity now in the

[040-022]breasts of unvarnished men; and here was one who had manifested them

[040-023]towards her from his youth up.

[040-024]There was, further, that never-ceasing pity in her soul for Giles as a

[040-025]man whom she had wronged—a man who had been unfortunate in his worldly

[040-026]transactions; while, not without a touch of sublimity, he had, like

[040-027]Horatio, borne himself throughout his scathing

[040-028]“As one, in suffering all, that suffers nothing.”

[040-029]It was these perceptions, and no subtle catching of her husband’s

[040-030]murmurs, that had bred the abstraction visible in her.

[040-031]When her father approached the house after witnessing the interview

[040-032]between [[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Fitzpiers]] and Mrs. Charmond, Grace was looking out of her

[040-033]sitting-room window, as if she had nothing to do, or think of, or care

[040-034]for. He stood still.

[040-035]“Ah, Grace,” he said, regarding her fixedly.

[040-036]“Yes, father,” she murmured.

[040-037]“Waiting for your dear husband?” he inquired, speaking with the sarcasm

[040-038]of pitiful [[love-and-desire|affection]].

[040-039]“Oh no—not especially. He has a great many patients to see this

[040-040]afternoon.”

[040-041][[george-melbury|Melbury]] came quite close. “Grace, what’s the use of talking like that,

[040-042]when you know—Here, come down and walk with me out in the garden,

[040-043]child.”

[040-044]He unfastened the door in the ivy-laced wall, and waited. This apparent

[040-045]indifference alarmed him. He would far rather that she had rushed in

[040-046]all the fire of jealousy to [[hintock-house|Hintock House]], regardless of

[040-047]conventionality, confronted and attacked [[felice-charmond|Felice Charmond]] _unguibus et

[040-048]rostro_, and accused her even in exaggerated shape of stealing away her

[040-049]husband. Such a storm might have cleared the air.

[040-050]She emerged in a minute or two, and they went inside together. “You

[040-051]know as well as I do,” he resumed, “that there is something threatening

[040-052]mischief to your life; and yet you pretend you do not. Do you suppose I

[040-053]don’t see the trouble in your face every day? I am very sure that this

[040-054]quietude is wrong conduct in you. You should look more into matters.”

[040-055]“I am quiet because my sadness is not of a nature to stir me to

[040-056]action.”

[040-057]Melbury wanted to ask her a dozen questions—did she not feel jealous?

[040-058]was she not indignant? but a natural delicacy restrained him. “You are

[040-059]very tame and let-alone, I am bound to say,” he remarked, pointedly.

[040-060]“I am what I feel, father,” she repeated.

[040-061]He glanced at her, and there returned upon his mind the scene of her

[040-062]offering to wed Winterborne instead of Fitzpiers in the last days

[040-063]before her marriage; and he asked himself if it could be the fact that

[040-064]she loved Winterborne, now that she had lost him, more than she had

[040-065]ever done when she was comparatively free to choose him.

[040-066]“What would you have me do?” she asked, in a low voice.

[040-067]He recalled his mind from the retrospective pain to the practical

[040-068]matter before them. “I would have you go to Mrs. Charmond,” he said.

[040-069]“Go to Mrs. Charmond—what for?” said she.

[040-070]“Well—if I must speak plain, dear Grace—to ask her, appeal to her in

[040-071]the name of your common womanhood, and your many like sentiments on

[040-072]things, not to make unhappiness between you and your husband. It lies

[040-073]with her entirely to do one or the other—that I can see.”

[040-074]Grace’s face had heated at her father’s words, and the very rustle of

[040-075]her skirts upon the box-edging bespoke hauteur. “I shall not think of

[040-076]going to her, father—of course I could not!” she answered.

[040-077]“Why—don’t ’ee want to be happier than you be at present?” said

[040-078]Melbury, more moved on her account than she was herself.

[040-079]“I don’t wish to be more humiliated. If I have anything to bear I can

[040-080]bear it in silence.”

[040-081]“But, my dear maid, you are too young—you don’t know what the present

[040-082]state of things may lead to. Just see the harm done a’ready! Your

[040-083]husband would have gone away to [[budmouth|Budmouth]] to a bigger practice if it had

[040-084]not been for this. Although it has gone such a little way, it is

[040-085]poisoning your future even now. Mrs. Charmond is thoughtlessly bad, not

[040-086]bad by calculation; and just a word to her now might save ’ee a peck of

[040-087]woes.”

[040-088]“Ah, I loved her once,” said Grace, with a broken articulation, “and

[040-089]she would not care for me then! Now I no longer love her. Let her do

[040-090]her worst: I don’t care.”

[040-091]“You ought to care. You have got into a very good position to start

[040-092]with. You have been well educated, well tended, and you have become the

[040-093]wife of a professional man of unusually good family. Surely you ought

[040-094]to make the best of your position.”

[040-095]“I don’t see that I ought. I wish I had never got into it. I wish you

[040-096]had never, never thought of educating me. I wish I worked in the woods

[040-097]like [[marty-south|Marty South]]. I hate genteel life, and I want to be no better than

[040-098]she.”

[040-099]“Why?” said her amazed father.

[040-100]“Because cultivation has only brought me inconveniences and troubles. I

[040-101]say again, I wish you had never sent me to those fashionable schools

[040-102]you set your mind on. It all arose out of that, father. If I had stayed

[040-103]at home I should have married—” She closed up her mouth suddenly and

[040-104]was silent; and he saw that she was not far from crying.

[040-105]Melbury was much grieved. “What, and would you like to have grown up as

[040-106]we be here in [[little-hintock|Hintock]]—knowing no more, and with no more [[fate-and-free-will|chance]] of

[040-107]seeing good life than we have here?”

[040-108]“Yes. I have never got any happiness outside Hintock that I know of,

[040-109]and I have suffered many a heartache at being sent away. Oh, the misery

[040-110]of those January days when I had got back to school, and left you all

[040-111]here in the wood so happy. I used to wonder why I had to bear it. And I

[040-112]was always a little despised by the other girls at school, because they

[040-113]knew where I came from, and that my parents were not in so good a

[040-114]station as theirs.”

[040-115]Her poor father was much hurt at what he thought her ingratitude and

[040-116]intractability. He had admitted to himself bitterly enough that he

[040-117]should have let young hearts have their way, or rather should have

[040-118]helped on her affection for Winterborne, and given her to him according

[040-119]to his original plan; but he was not prepared for her deprecation of

[040-120]those attainments whose completion had been a labor of years, and a

[040-121]severe tax upon his purse.

[040-122]“Very well,” he said, with much heaviness of spirit. “If you don’t like

[040-123]to go to her I don’t wish to force you.”

[040-124]And so the question remained for him still: how should he remedy this

[040-125]perilous state of things? For days he sat in a moody attitude over the

[040-126]fire, a pitcher of cider standing on the hearth beside him, and his

[040-127]drinking-horn inverted upon the top of it. He spent a week and more

[040-128]thus composing a letter to the chief offender, which he would every now

[040-129]and then attempt to complete, and suddenly crumple up in his hand.

[040-130]CHAPTER XXXI.

[040-131]As February merged in March, and lighter evenings broke the gloom of

[040-132]the woodmen’s homeward journey, the Hintocks Great and Little began to

[040-133]have ears for a rumor of the events out of which had grown the

[040-134][[timber-economy|timber]]-dealer’s troubles. It took the form of a wide sprinkling of

[040-135]conjecture, wherein no man knew the exact truth. Tantalizing phenomena,

[040-136]at once showing and concealing the real relationship of the persons

[040-137]concerned, caused a diffusion of excited surprise. Honest people as the

[040-138]woodlanders were, it was hardly to be expected that they could remain

[040-139]immersed in the study of their trees and gardens amid such

[040-140]circumstances, or sit with their backs turned like the good burghers of

[040-141]Coventry at the passage of the beautiful lady.

[040-142]Rumor, for a wonder, exaggerated little. There were, in fact, in this

[040-143]case as in thousands, the well-worn incidents, old as the hills, which,

[040-144]with individual variations, made a mourner of Ariadne, a by-word of

[040-145]Vashti, and a corpse of the Countess Amy. There were rencounters

[040-146]accidental and contrived, stealthy correspondence, sudden misgivings on

[040-147]one side, sudden self-reproaches on the other. The inner state of the

[040-148]twain was one as of confused noise that would not allow the accents of

[040-149]calmer reason to be heard. Determinations to go in this direction, and

[040-150]headlong plunges in that; dignified safeguards, undignified collapses;

[040-151]not a single rash step by deliberate intention, and all against

[040-152]judgment.

[040-153]It was all that Melbury had expected and feared. It was more, for he

[040-154]had overlooked the publicity that would be likely to result, as it now

[040-155]had done. What should he do—appeal to Mrs. Charmond himself, since

[040-156]Grace would not? He bethought himself of Winterborne, and resolved to

[040-157]consult him, feeling the strong need of some friend of his own sex to

[040-158]whom he might unburden his mind.

[040-159]He had entirely lost faith in his own judgment. That judgment on which

[040-160]he had relied for so many years seemed recently, like a false companion

[040-161]unmasked, to have disclosed unexpected depths of hypocrisy and

[040-162]speciousness where all had seemed solidity. He felt almost afraid to

[040-163]form a conjecture on the weather, or the time, or the fruit-promise, so

[040-164]great was his self-abasement.

[040-165]It was a rimy evening when he set out to look for Giles. The woods

[040-166]seemed to be in a cold sweat; beads of perspiration hung from every

[040-167]bare twig; the sky had no color, and the trees rose before him as

[040-168]haggard, gray phantoms, whose days of substantiality were passed.

[040-169]Melbury seldom saw Winterborne now, but he believed him to be occupying

[040-170]a lonely hut just beyond the boundary of Mrs. Charmond’s estate, though

[040-171]still within the circuit of the woodland. The timber-merchant’s thin

[040-172]legs stalked on through the pale, damp scenery, his eyes on the dead

[040-173]leaves of last year; while every now and then a hasty “Ay?” escaped his

[040-174]lips in reply to some bitter proposition.

[040-175]His notice was attracted by a thin blue haze of smoke, behind which

[040-176]arose sounds of voices and chopping: bending his steps that way, he saw

[040-177]Winterborne just in front of him. It just now happened that Giles,

[040-178]after being for a long time apathetic and unemployed, had become one of

[040-179]the busiest men in the neighborhood. It is often thus; fallen friends,

[040-180]lost sight of, we expect to find starving; we discover them going on

[040-181]fairly well. Without any solicitation, or desire for profit on his

[040-182]part, he had been asked to execute during that winter a very large

[040-183]order for hurdles and other [[woodland-crafts|copse-ware]], for which purpose he had been

[040-184]obliged to buy several acres of brushwood standing. He was now engaged

[040-185]in the cutting and manufacture of the same, proceeding with the work

[040-186]daily like an automaton.
