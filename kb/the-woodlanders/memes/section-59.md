# Section 59

[059-001]She sat down, her face buried in her hands, and Marty went, and after a

[059-002]short absence returned. Then Grace rose, and going out asked her father

[059-003]if he had met her husband.

[059-004]“Yes,” said [[george-melbury|Melbury]].

[059-005]“And you know all that has happened?”

[059-006]“I do. Forgive me, Grace, for suspecting ye of worse than rashness—I

[059-007]ought to know ye better. Are you coming with me to what was once your

[059-008]home?”

[059-009]“No. I stay here with _him_. Take no account of me any more.”

[059-010]The unwonted, perplexing, agitating relations in which she had stood to

[059-011][[giles-winterborne|Winterborne]] quite lately—brought about by Melbury’s own

[059-012]contrivance—could not fail to soften the natural anger of a parent at

[059-013]her more recent doings. “My daughter, things are bad,” he rejoined.

[059-014]“But why do you persevere to make ’em worse? What good can you do to

[059-015]Giles by staying here with him? Mind, I ask no questions. I don’t

[059-016]inquire why you decided to come here, or anything as to what your

[059-017]course would have been if he had not died, though I know there’s no

[059-018]deliberate harm in ye. As for me, I have lost all claim upon you, and I

[059-019]make no complaint. But I do say that by coming back with me now you

[059-020]will show no less kindness to him, and escape any sound of [[pride-and-humiliation|shame]].

[059-021]“But I don’t wish to escape it.”

[059-022]“If you don’t on your own account, cannot you wish to on mine and hers?

[059-023]Nobody except our household knows that you have left home. Then why

[059-024]should you, by a piece of perverseness, bring down my gray hairs with

[059-025]sorrow to the grave?”

[059-026]“If it were not for my husband—” she began, moved by his words. “But

[059-027]how can I meet him there? How can any woman who is not a mere man’s

[059-028]creature join him after what has taken place?”

[059-029]“He would go away again rather than keep you out of my house.”

[059-030]“How do you know that, father?”

[059-031]“We met him on our way here, and he told us so,” said [[mrs-melbury|Mrs. Melbury]]. “He

[059-032]had said something like it before. He seems very much upset

[059-033]altogether.”

[059-034]“He declared to her when he came to our house that he would wait for

[059-035]time and devotion to bring about his forgiveness,” said her husband.

[059-036]“That was it, wasn’t it, Lucy?”

[059-037]“Yes. That he would not intrude upon you, Grace, till you gave him

[059-038]absolute permission,” Mrs. Melbury added.

[059-039]This antecedent considerateness in [[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Fitzpiers]] was as welcome to Grace as

[059-040]it was unexpected; and though she did not [[love-and-desire|desire]] his presence, she was

[059-041]sorry that by her retaliatory fiction she had given him a different

[059-042]reason for avoiding her. She made no further objections to accompanying

[059-043]her parents, taking them into the inner room to give Winterborne a last

[059-044]look, and gathering up the two or three things that belonged to her.

[059-045]While she was doing this the two [[gender-roles|women]] came who had been called by

[059-046]Melbury, and at their heels poor [[robert-creedle|Creedle]].

[059-047]“Forgive me, but I can’t rule my mourning nohow as a man should, Mr.

[059-048]Melbury,” he said. “I ha’n’t seen him since Thursday se’night, and have

[059-049]wondered for days and days where he’s been keeping. There was I

[059-050]expecting him to come and tell me to wash out the cider-barrels against

[059-051]the making, and here was he— Well, I’ve knowed him from table-high; I

[059-052]knowed his father—used to bide about upon two sticks in the sun afore

[059-053]he died!—and now I’ve seen the end of the family, which we can ill

[059-054]afford to lose, wi’ such a scanty lot of good folk in [[little-hintock|Hintock]] as we’ve

[059-055]got. And now Robert Creedle will be nailed up in parish boards ’a

[059-056]b’lieve; and noboby will glutch down a sigh for he!”

[059-057]They started for home, Marty and Creedle remaining behind. For a time

[059-058]Grace and her father walked side by side without speaking. It was just

[059-059]in the blue of the dawn, and the chilling tone of the sky was reflected

[059-060]in her cold, wet face. The whole wood seemed to be a house of death,

[059-061]pervaded by loss to its uttermost length and breadth. Winterborne was

[059-062]gone, and the copses seemed to show the want of him; those young [[nature-in-the-woodlanders|trees]],

[059-063]so many of which he had planted, and of which he had spoken so truly

[059-064]when he said that he should fall before they fell, were at that very

[059-065]moment sending out their roots in the direction that he had given them

[059-066]with his subtle hand.

[059-067]“One thing made it tolerable to us that your husband should come back

[059-068]to the house,” said Melbury at last—“the death of Mrs. Charmond.”

[059-069]“Ah, yes,” said Grace, arousing slightly to the recollection, “he told

[059-070]me so.”

[059-071]“Did he tell you how she died? It was no such death as Giles’s. She was

[059-072]shot—by a disappointed lover. It occurred in Germany. The unfortunate

[059-073]man shot himself afterwards. He was that South Carolina gentleman of

[059-074]very passionate nature who used to haunt this place to force her to an

[059-075]interview, and followed her about everywhere. So ends the brilliant

[059-076][[felice-charmond|Felice Charmond]]—once a good friend to me—but no friend to you.”

[059-077]“I can forgive her,” said Grace, absently. “Did Edgar tell you of

[059-078]this?”

[059-079]“No; but he put a [[london-in-the-woodlanders|London]] newspaper, giving an account of it, on the

[059-080]hall table, folded in such a way that we should see it. It will be in

[059-081]the [[sherton-abbas|Sherton]] paper this week, no doubt. To make the event more solemn

[059-082]still to him, he had just before had sharp words with her, and left

[059-083]her. He told Lucy this, as nothing about him appears in the newspaper.

[059-084]And the cause of the quarrel was, of all people, she we’ve left behind

[059-085]us.”

[059-086]“Do you mean Marty?” Grace spoke the words but perfunctorily. For,

[059-087]pertinent and pointed as Melbury’s story was, she had no heart for it

[059-088]now.

[059-089]“Yes. [[marty-south|Marty South]].” Melbury persisted in his narrative, to divert her

[059-090]from her present grief, if possible. “Before he went away she wrote him

[059-091]a letter, which he kept in his pocket a long while before reading. He

[059-092]chanced to pull it out in Mrs. Charmond’s, presence, and read it out

[059-093]loud. It contained something which teased her very much, and that led

[059-094]to the rupture. She was following him to make it up when she met with

[059-095]her terrible death.”

[059-096]Melbury did not know enough to give the gist of the incident, which was

[059-097]that Marty South’s letter had been concerning a certain personal

[059-098]adornment common to herself and Mrs. Charmond. Her bullet reached its

[059-099]billet at last. The scene between Fitzpiers and Felice had been sharp,

[059-100]as only a scene can be which arises out of the mortification of one

[059-101]woman by another in the presence of a lover. True, Marty had not

[059-102]effected it by word of mouth; the charge about the locks of hair was

[059-103]made simply by Fitzpiers reading her letter to him aloud to Felice in

[059-104]the playfully ironical tones of one who had become a little weary of

[059-105]his situation, and was finding his friend, in the phrase of George

[059-106]Herbert, a “flat delight.” He had stroked those false tresses with his

[059-107]hand many a time without knowing them to be transplanted, and it was

[059-108]impossible when the discovery was so abruptly made to avoid being

[059-109]finely satirical, despite her generous disposition.

[059-110]That was how it had begun, and tragedy had been its end. On his abrupt

[059-111]departure she had followed him to the station but the train was gone;

[059-112]and in travelling to Baden in search of him she had met his rival,

[059-113]whose reproaches led to an altercation, and the death of both. Of that

[059-114]precipitate scene of passion and crime Fitzpiers had known nothing till

[059-115]he saw an account of it in the papers, where, fortunately for himself,

[059-116]no mention was made of his prior acquaintance with the unhappy lady;

[059-117]nor was there any allusion to him in the subsequent inquiry, the double

[059-118]death being attributed to some gambling losses, though, in point of

[059-119]fact, neither one of them had visited the tables.

[059-120]Melbury and his daughter drew near their house, having seen but one

[059-121]living thing on their way, a squirrel, which did not run up its tree,

[059-122]but, dropping the sweet chestnut which it carried, cried

[059-123]chut-chut-chut, and stamped with its hind legs on the ground. When the

[059-124]roofs and chimneys of the homestead began to emerge from the screen of

[059-125]boughs, Grace started, and checked herself in her abstracted advance.

[059-126]“You clearly understand,” she said to her step-mother some of her old

[059-127]misgiving returning, “that I am coming back only on condition of his

[059-128]leaving as he promised? Will you let him know this, that there may be

[059-129]no mistake?”

[059-130]Mrs. Melbury, who had some long private talks with Fitzpiers, assured

[059-131]Grace that she need have no doubts on that point, and that he would

[059-132]probably be gone by the evening. Grace then entered with them into

[059-133]Melbury’s wing of the house, and sat down listlessly in the parlor,

[059-134]while her step-mother went to Fitzpiers.

[059-135]The prompt obedience to her wishes which the surgeon showed did honor

[059-136]to him, if anything could. Before Mrs. Melbury had returned to the room

[059-137]Grace, who was sitting on the parlor window-bench, saw her husband go

[059-138]from the door under the increasing light of morning, with a bag in his

[059-139]hand. While passing through the gate he turned his head. The firelight

[059-140]of the room she sat in threw her figure into dark relief against the

[059-141]window as she looked through the panes, and he must have seen her

[059-142]distinctly. In a moment he went on, the gate fell to, and he

[059-143]disappeared. At the hut she had declared that another had displaced

[059-144]him; and now she had banished him.

[059-145]CHAPTER XLIV.

[059-146]Fitzpiers had hardly been gone an hour when Grace began to sicken. The

[059-147]next day she kept her room. [[old-jones|Old Jones]] was called in; he murmured some

[059-148]statements in which the words “feverish symptoms” occurred. Grace heard

[059-149]them, and guessed the means by which she had brought this visitation

[059-150]upon herself.

[059-151]One day, while she still lay there with her head throbbing, wondering

[059-152]if she were really going to join him who had gone before, Grammer

[059-153]Oliver came to her bedside. “I don’t know whe’r this is meant for you

[059-154]to take, ma’am,” she said, “but I have found it on the table. It was

[059-155]left by Marty, I think, when she came this morning.”

[059-156]Grace turned her hot eyes upon what Grammer held up. It was the phial

[059-157]left at the hut by her husband when he had begged her to take some

[059-158]drops of its contents if she wished to preserve herself from falling a

[059-159]victim to the malady which had pulled down Winterborne. She examined it

[059-160]as well as she could. The liquid was of an opaline hue, and bore a

[059-161]label with an inscription in Italian. He had probably got it in his

[059-162]wanderings abroad. She knew but little Italian, but could understand

[059-163]that the cordial was a febrifuge of some sort. Her father, her mother,

[059-164]and all the household were anxious for her recovery, and she resolved

[059-165]to obey her husband’s directions. Whatever the risk, if any, she was

[059-166]prepared to run it. A glass of water was brought, and the drops dropped

[059-167]in.

[059-168]The effect, though not miraculous, was remarkable. In less than an hour

[059-169]she felt calmer, cooler, better able to reflect—less inclined to fret

[059-170]and chafe and wear herself away. She took a few drops more. From that

[059-171]time the fever retreated, and went out like a damped conflagration.

[059-172]“How clever he is!” she said, regretfully. “Why could he not have had

[059-173]more principle, so as to turn his great talents to good account?

[059-174]Perhaps he has saved my useless life. But he doesn’t know it, and

[059-175]doesn’t care whether he has saved it or not; and on that account will

[059-176]never be told by me! Probably he only gave it to me in the arrogance of

[059-177]his skill, to show the greatness of his resources beside mine, as

[059-178]Elijah drew down fire from heaven.”
