# Grace's Return to Hintock

Grace Melbury's return from finishing school to Little Hintock is the event that sets the plot of *The Woodlanders* in motion. Transformed by education into a woman caught between two worlds, Grace arrives home to find that she no longer fits comfortably into either. Her return creates the central tension from which all the novel's subsequent tragedy flows.

## The Educated Daughter

George Melbury had invested heavily in his daughter's transformation. He spent years and money keeping her at "boarding-school" (005-143), far beyond what was customary for a timber merchant's daughter. His express purpose was to make her eligible for a match above her station — a goal he stated plainly: "I've been able to keep her at school without sacrifice" (005-157).

The education succeeded all too well. Grace returned with the manners and expectations of a lady, but with a father who remained a provincial timber merchant. The gap between what she had become and what she had returned to was the novel's founding instability.

## The Arrival

Grace's return was anticipated with excitement and anxiety. Giles Winterborne was dispatched to meet her in Sherton Abbas. Melbury instructed Giles nervously: coming from "boarding-school, she might feel shocked" (006-080) at Hintock's simplicity.

At the Sherton inn, Grace's transformation was unmistakable. She was no longer the woodland girl who had left. She "had known as Miss Grace Melbury, but now looking glorified and" transformed (007-033). Her presence "was not by any means an ordinary one in Sherton Abbas streets" (007-051). Hardy notes the difficulty of describing her (007-071), so thoroughly had education reshaped her.

The journey from Sherton to Little Hintock was a passage between worlds. Each mile brought Grace closer to her origins and further from the self she was trained to become.

## The Dislocation

Grace's return was not a homecoming but a dislocation. The cottages, timber yards, and simple manners of the villagers all appeared diminished through the lens of her education. She was caught between remaining affection for Giles and a newly acquired sense that her destiny lay elsewhere.

The community sensed the change. Villagers noted that "she takes no interest in the village folk at all" (007-152), capturing the real distance education had created. Melbury began to doubt his investment: "They shouldn't have schooled her so monstrous high" (014-176), a villager observed.

Grace was aware of the split within herself. She reflected that her attachments to Giles were formed "till I was sent away to school, so I cannot be radically" changed (032-168). Yet the change, while perhaps not radical, was real enough to make the old life impossible.

## The Initiation of the Plot

Grace's return initiates the novel's central conflict by placing her between two possible futures: the natural life with Giles that her origins promise, and the elevated match that her education has prepared her to expect. Fitzpiers's arrival provides the vehicle for the latter; Giles's steadfast love keeps the former alive.

The tragedy is that Grace cannot fully inhabit either world. She is too refined for the yeoman, too rustic for the doctor. Her return — the moment when these incompatible selves are forced to coexist — creates the conditions for all the misjudgments and betrayals that follow.
