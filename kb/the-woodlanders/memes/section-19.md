# Section 19

[019-001]Strict people of the highly respectable class, knowing a little about

[019-002]him by report, might have said that he seemed likely to err rather in

[019-003]the possession of too many ideas than too few; to be a dreamy ’ist of

[019-004]some sort, or too deeply steeped in some false kind of ’ism. However

[019-005]this may be, it will be seen that he was undoubtedly a somewhat rare

[019-006]kind of gentleman and doctor to have descended, as from the clouds,

[019-007]upon [[little-hintock|Little Hintock]].

[019-008]“This is an extraordinary case,” he said at last to [[giles-winterborne|Winterborne]], after

[019-009]examining South by conversation, look, and touch, and learning that the

[019-010]craze about the elm was stronger than ever. “Come down-stairs, and I’ll

[019-011]tell you what I think.”

[019-012]They accordingly descended, and the doctor continued, “The tree must be

[019-013]cut down, or I won’t answer for his life.”

[019-014]“’Tis [[felice-charmond|Mrs. Charmond]]’s tree, and I suppose we must get permission?” said

[019-015]Giles. “If so, as she is gone away, I must speak to her agent.”

[019-016]“Oh—never mind whose tree it is—what’s a tree beside a life! Cut it

[019-017]down. I have not the honor of knowing Mrs. Charmond as yet, but I am

[019-018]disposed to risk that much with her.”

[019-019]“’Tis [[timber-economy|timber]],” rejoined Giles, more scrupulous than he would have been

[019-020]had not his own interests stood so closely involved. “They’ll never

[019-021]fell a stick about here without it being marked first, either by her or

[019-022]the agent.”

[019-023]“Then we’ll inaugurate a new era forthwith. How long has he complained

[019-024]of the tree?” asked the doctor of [[marty-south|Marty]].

[019-025]“Weeks and weeks, sir. The shape of it seems to haunt him like an evil

[019-026]spirit. He says that it is exactly his own age, that it has got human

[019-027]sense, and sprouted up when he was born on purpose to rule him, and

[019-028]keep him as its slave. Others have been like it afore in Hintock.”

[019-029]They could hear South’s voice up-stairs “Oh, he’s rocking this way; he

[019-030]must come! And then my poor life, that’s worth houses upon houses, will

[019-031]be squashed out o’ me. Oh! oh!”

[019-032]“That’s how he goes on,” she added. “And he’ll never look anywhere else

[019-033]but out of the window, and scarcely have the curtains drawn.”

[019-034]“Down with it, then, and hang Mrs. Charmond,” said [[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Mr. Fitzpiers]]. “The

[019-035]best plan will be to wait till the evening, when it is dark, or early

[019-036]in the morning before he is awake, so that he doesn’t see it fall, for

[019-037]that would terrify him worse than ever. Keep the blind down till I

[019-038]come, and then I’ll assure him, and show him that his trouble is over.”

[019-039]The doctor then departed, and they waited till the evening. When it was

[019-040]dusk, and the curtains drawn, Winterborne directed a couple of woodmen

[019-041]to bring a crosscut-saw, and the tall, threatening tree was soon nearly

[019-042]off at its base. He would not fell it completely then, on account of

[019-043]the possible crash, but next morning, before South was awake, they went

[019-044]and lowered it cautiously, in a direction away from the cottage. It was

[019-045]a business difficult to do quite silently; but it was done at last, and

[019-046]the elm of the same birth-year as the woodman’s lay stretched upon the

[019-047]ground. The weakest idler that passed could now set foot on marks

[019-048]formerly made in the upper forks by the shoes of adventurous climbers

[019-049]only; once inaccessible nests could be examined microscopically; and on

[019-050]swaying extremities where birds alone had perched, the by-standers sat

[019-051]down.

[019-052]As soon as it was broad daylight the doctor came, and Winterborne

[019-053]entered the house with him. Marty said that her father was wrapped up

[019-054]and ready, as usual, to be put into his chair. They ascended the

[019-055]stairs, and soon seated him. He began at once to complain of the tree,

[019-056]and the danger to his life and Winterborne’s house-property in

[019-057]consequence.

[019-058]The doctor signalled to Giles, who went and drew back the printed

[019-059]cotton curtains. “’Tis gone, see,” said Mr. Fitzpiers.

[019-060]As soon as the old man saw the vacant patch of sky in place of the

[019-061]branched column so familiar to his gaze, he sprang up, speechless, his

[019-062]eyes rose from their hollows till the whites showed all round; he fell

[019-063]back, and a bluish whiteness overspread him.

[019-064]Greatly alarmed, they put him on the bed. As soon as he came a little

[019-065]out of his fit, he gasped, “Oh, it is gone!—where?—where?”

[019-066]His whole system seemed paralyzed by amazement. They were

[019-067]thunder-struck at the result of the experiment, and did all they could.

[019-068]Nothing seemed to avail. Giles and Fitzpiers went and came, but

[019-069]uselessly. He lingered through the day, and died that evening as the

[019-070]sun went down.

[019-071]“D—d if my remedy hasn’t killed him!” murmured the doctor.

[019-072]CHAPTER XV.

[019-073]When [[george-melbury|Melbury]] heard what had happened he seemed much moved, and walked

[019-074]thoughtfully about the premises. On South’s own account he was

[019-075]genuinely sorry; and on Winterborne’s he was the more grieved in that

[019-076]this catastrophe had so closely followed the somewhat harsh dismissal

[019-077]of Giles as the betrothed of his daughter.

[019-078]He was quite angry with circumstances for so heedlessly inflicting on

[019-079]Giles a second trouble when the needful one inflicted by himself was

[019-080]all that the proper order of events demanded. “I told Giles’s father

[019-081]when he came into those houses not to spend too much money on [[lifehold-tenure|lifehold]]

[019-082]property held neither for his own life nor his son’s,” he exclaimed.

[019-083]“But he wouldn’t listen to me. And now Giles has to suffer for it.”

[019-084]“Poor Giles!” murmured Grace.

[019-085]“Now, Grace, between us two, it is very, very remarkable. It is almost

[019-086]as if I had foreseen this; and I am thankful for your escape, though I

[019-087]am sincerely sorry for Giles. Had we not dismissed him already, we

[019-088]could hardly have found it in our hearts to dismiss him now. So I say,

[019-089]be thankful. I’ll do all I can for him as a friend; but as a pretender

[019-090]to the position of my son-in law, that can never be thought of more.”

[019-091]And yet at that very moment the impracticability to which poor

[019-092]Winterborne’s suit had been reduced was touching Grace’s heart to a

[019-093]warmer sentiment on his behalf than she had felt for years concerning

[019-094]him.

[019-095]He, meanwhile, was sitting down alone in the old familiar house which

[019-096]had ceased to be his, taking a calm if somewhat dismal survey of

[019-097]affairs. The pendulum of the clock bumped every now and then against

[019-098]one side of the case in which it swung, as the muffled drum to his

[019-099]worldly march. Looking out of the window he could perceive that a

[019-100]paralysis had come over [[robert-creedle|Creedle]]’s occupation of manuring the garden,

[019-101]owing, obviously, to a conviction that they might not be living there

[019-102]long enough to profit by next season’s crop.

[019-103]He looked at the leases again and the letter attached. There was no

[019-104]doubt that he had lost his houses by an accident which might easily

[019-105]have been circumvented if he had known the true conditions of his

[019-106]holding. The time for performance had now lapsed in strict law; but

[019-107]might not the intention be considered by the landholder when she became

[019-108]aware of the circumstances, and his moral right to retain the holdings

[019-109]for the term of his life be conceded?

[019-110]His heart sank within him when he perceived that despite all the legal

[019-111]reciprocities and safeguards prepared and written, the upshot of the

[019-112]matter amounted to this, that it depended upon the mere caprice—good or

[019-113]ill—of the woman he had met the day before in such an unfortunate way,

[019-114]whether he was to possess his houses for life or no.

[019-115]While he was sitting and thinking a step came to the door, and Melbury

[019-116]appeared, looking very sorry for his position. Winterborne welcomed him

[019-117]by a word and a look, and went on with his examination of the

[019-118]parchments. His visitor sat down.

[019-119]“Giles,” he said, “this is very awkward, and I am sorry for it. What

[019-120]are you going to do?”

[019-121]Giles informed him of the real state of affairs, and how barely he had

[019-122]missed availing himself of his [[fate-and-free-will|chance]] of renewal.

[019-123]“What a misfortune! Why was this neglected? Well, the best thing you

[019-124]can do is to write and tell her all about it, and throw yourself upon

[019-125]her generosity.”

[019-126]“I would rather not,” murmured Giles.

[019-127]“But you must,” said Melbury.

[019-128]In short, he argued so cogently that Giles allowed himself to be

[019-129]persuaded, and the letter to Mrs. Charmond was written and sent to

[019-130][[hintock-house|Hintock House]], whence, as he knew, it would at once be forwarded to

[019-131]her.

[019-132]Melbury feeling that he had done so good an action in coming as almost

[019-133]to extenuate his previous arbitrary conduct to nothing, went home; and

[019-134]Giles was left alone to the suspense of waiting for a reply from the

[019-135]divinity who shaped the ends of the Hintock population. By this time

[019-136]all the villagers knew of the circumstances, and being wellnigh like

[019-137]one family, a keen interest was the result all round.

[019-138]Everybody thought of Giles; nobody thought of Marty. Had any of them

[019-139]looked in upon her during those moonlight nights which preceded the

[019-140]burial of her father, they would have seen the girl absolutely alone in

[019-141]the house with the dead man. Her own chamber being nearest the stairs,

[019-142]the coffin had been placed there for convenience; and at a certain hour

[019-143]of the night, when the moon arrived opposite the window, its beams

[019-144]streamed across the still profile of South, sublimed by the august

[019-145]presence of death, and onward a few feet farther upon the face of his

[019-146]daughter, lying in her little bed in the stillness of a repose almost

[019-147]as dignified as that of her companion—the repose of a guileless soul

[019-148]that had nothing more left on earth to lose, except a life which she

[019-149]did not overvalue.

[019-150]South was buried, and a week passed, and Winterborne watched for a

[019-151]reply from Mrs. Charmond. Melbury was very sanguine as to its tenor;

[019-152]but Winterborne had not told him of the encounter with her carriage,

[019-153]when, if ever he had heard an affronted tone on a woman’s lips, he had

[019-154]heard it on hers.

[019-155]The postman’s time for passing was just after Melbury’s men had

[019-156]assembled in the spar-house; and Winterborne, who when not busy on his

[019-157]own account would lend assistance there, used to go out into the lane

[019-158]every morning and meet the post-man at the end of one of the green

[019-159]rides through the hazel copse, in the straight stretch of which his

[019-160]laden figure could be seen a long way off. Grace also was very anxious;

[019-161]more anxious than her father; more, perhaps, than Winterborne himself.

[019-162]This anxiety led her into the spar-house on some pretext or other

[019-163]almost every morning while they were awaiting the reply.

[019-164]Fitzpiers too, though he did not personally appear, was much

[019-165]interested, and not altogether easy in his mind; for he had been

[019-166]informed by an authority of what he had himself conjectured, that if

[019-167]the tree had been allowed to stand, the old man would have gone on

[019-168]complaining, but might have lived for twenty years.

[019-169]Eleven times had Winterborne gone to that corner of the ride, and

[019-170]looked up its long straight slope through the wet grays of winter dawn.

[019-171]But though the postman’s bowed figure loomed in view pretty regularly,

[019-172]he brought nothing for Giles. On the twelfth day the man of missives,

[019-173]while yet in the extreme distance, held up his hand, and Winterborne

[019-174]saw a letter in it. He took it into the spar-house before he broke the

[019-175]seal, and those who were there gathered round him while he read, Grace

[019-176]looking in at the door.

[019-177]The letter was not from Mrs. Charmond herself, but her agent at

[019-178][[sherton-abbas|Sherton]]. Winterborne glanced it over and looked up.

[019-179]“It’s all over,” he said.
