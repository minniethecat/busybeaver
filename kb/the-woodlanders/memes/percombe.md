## Overview

Barber Percombe is a tradesman in Sherton Abbas who serves as an intermediary between Mrs. Charmond and Marty South. His purchase of Marty's hair in the opening chapters sets in motion a chain of events that reverberate throughout the novel. Though a minor character, he functions as a plot catalyst who bridges the world of Little Hintock with the gentry at Hintock House.

## Role in the Plot

### The Hair Transaction

Percombe travels from Sherton Abbas to Little Hintock to persuade Marty South to sell her exceptionally beautiful hair to Mrs. Charmond. He encounters Marty at her cottage and attempts to convince her with money, though she is initially resistant [002-108]. Marty eventually accedes after realizing that her father John South's imminent death would free her of her attachment to Giles Winterborne [002-120].

Percombe's role as intermediary is significant: he acts not as a friend but as a business agent, and his description of Mrs. Charmond's request inadvertently reveals the social hierarchy at play. Marty correctly guesses the buyer's identity — " 'Tis she at the House — Mrs. Charmond!" [002-121] — demonstrating her sharp intuition despite her rural isolation.

### The Final Appearance

Percombe appears briefly near the end of the novel, standing at his shop door as Grace Melbury passes through Sherton Abbas [065-161]. This framing appearance — his involvement in both the opening and closing sections — reinforces the novel's cyclical structure and the enduring consequence of his initial transaction.

## Character

Percombe is portrayed as a practical, opportunistic businessman. He keeps a waxen woman in his shop window [001-158] — a sign of his trade's pretensions — and has abandoned the traditional barber's pole as "not genteel" [001-162]. His manner is ingratiating when it serves his purpose, but he is ultimately a hired agent acting on Mrs. Charmond's instructions.

His conversation with Marty reveals both his commercial cunning and her moral strength. Percombe's attempts to persuade Marty through flattery and money are met with her steadfast refusal until external circumstances break her resistance.
