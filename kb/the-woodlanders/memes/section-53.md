# Section 53

[053-001]She started back suddenly from his embrace, influenced by a sort of

[053-002]inspiration. “Oh, I suppose,” she stammered, “that I am really

[053-003]free?—that this is right? Is there _really_ a new law? Father cannot

[053-004]have been too sanguine in saying—”

[053-005]He did not answer, and a moment afterwards Grace burst into tears in

[053-006]spite of herself. “Oh, why does not my father come home and explain,”

[053-007]she sobbed, “and let me know clearly what I am? It is too trying, this,

[053-008]to ask me to—and then to leave me so long in so vague a state that I do

[053-009]not know what to do, and perhaps do wrong!”

[053-010][[giles-winterborne|Winterborne]] felt like a very Cain, over and above his previous sorrow.

[053-011]How he had sinned against her in not telling her what he knew. He

[053-012]turned aside; the feeling of his cruelty mounted higher and higher. How

[053-013]could he have dreamed of kissing her? He could hardly refrain from

[053-014]tears. Surely nothing more pitiable had ever been known than the

[053-015]condition of this poor young thing, now as heretofore the victim of her

[053-016]father’s well-meant but blundering policy.

[053-017]Even in the hour of [[george-melbury|Melbury]]’s greatest assurance Winterborne had

[053-018]harbored a suspicion that no law, new or old, could undo Grace’s

[053-019][[marriage-in-the-woodlanders|marriage]] without her appearance in public; though he was not

[053-020]sufficiently sure of what might have been enacted to destroy by his own

[053-021]words her pleasing idea that a mere dash of the pen, on her father’s

[053-022]testimony, was going to be sufficient. But he had never suspected the

[053-023]sad fact that the position was irremediable.

[053-024]Poor Grace, perhaps feeling that she had indulged in too much fluster

[053-025]for a mere kiss, calmed herself at finding how grave he was.

[053-026]“I am glad we are friends again anyhow,” she said, smiling through her

[053-027]tears. “Giles, if you had only shown half the boldness before I married

[053-028]that you show now, you would have carried me off for your own first

[053-029]instead of second. If we do marry, I hope you will never think badly of

[053-030]me for encouraging you a little, but my father is _so_ impatient, you

[053-031]know, as his years and infirmities increase, that he will wish to see

[053-032]us a little advanced when he comes. That is my only excuse.”

[053-033]To Winterborne all this was sadder than it was sweet. How could she so

[053-034]trust her father’s conjectures? He did not know how to tell her the

[053-035]truth and [[pride-and-humiliation|shame]] himself. And yet he felt that it must be done. “We may

[053-036]have been wrong,” he began, almost fearfully, “in supposing that it can

[053-037]all be carried out while we stay here at [[little-hintock|Hintock]]. I am not sure but

[053-038]that people may have to appear in a public court even under the new

[053-039]Act; and if there should be any difficulty, and we cannot marry after

[053-040]all—”

[053-041]Her cheeks became slowly bloodless. “Oh, Giles,” she said, grasping his

[053-042]arm, “you have heard something! What—cannot my father conclude it there

[053-043]and now? Surely he has done it? Oh, Giles, Giles, don’t deceive me.

[053-044]What terrible position am I in?”

[053-045]He could not tell her, try as he would. The sense of her implicit trust

[053-046]in his honor absolutely disabled him. “I cannot inform you,” he

[053-047]murmured, his voice as husky as that of the leaves underfoot. “Your

[053-048]father will soon be here. Then we shall know. I will take you home.”

[053-049]Inexpressibly dear as she was to him, he offered her his arm with the

[053-050]most reserved air, as he added, correctingly, “I will take you, at any

[053-051]rate, into the drive.”

[053-052]Thus they walked on together. Grace vibrating between happiness and

[053-053]misgiving. It was only a few minutes’ walk to where the drive ran, and

[053-054]they had hardly descended into it when they heard a voice behind them

[053-055]cry, “Take out that arm!”

[053-056]For a moment they did not heed, and the voice repeated, more loudly and

[053-057]hoarsely,

[053-058]“Take out that arm!”

[053-059]It was Melbury’s. He had returned sooner than they expected, and now

[053-060]came up to them. Grace’s hand had been withdrawn like lightning on her

[053-061]hearing the second command. “I don’t blame you—I don’t blame you,” he

[053-062]said, in the weary cadence of one broken down with scourgings. “But you

[053-063]two must walk together no more—I have been surprised—I have been

[053-064]cruelly deceived—Giles, don’t say anything to me; but go away!”

[053-065]He was evidently not aware that Winterborne had known the truth before

[053-066]he brought it; and Giles would not stay to discuss it with him then.

[053-067]When the young man had gone Melbury took his daughter in-doors to the

[053-068]room he used as his office. There he sat down, and bent over the slope

[053-069]of the bureau, her bewildered gaze fixed upon him.

[053-070]When Melbury had recovered a little he said, “You are now, as ever,

[053-071][[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Fitzpiers]]’s wife. I was deluded. He has not done you _enough_ harm. You

[053-072]are still subject to his beck and call.”

[053-073]“Then let it be, and never mind, father,” she said, with dignified

[053-074]sorrow. “I can bear it. It is your trouble that grieves me most.” She

[053-075]stooped over him, and put her arm round his neck, which distressed

[053-076]Melbury still more. “I don’t mind at all what comes to me,” Grace

[053-077]continued; “whose wife I am, or whose I am not. I do [[love-and-desire|love]] Giles; I

[053-078]cannot help that; and I have gone further with him than I should have

[053-079]done if I had known exactly how things were. But I do not reproach

[053-080]you.”

[053-081]“Then Giles did not tell you?” said Melbury.

[053-082]“No,” said she. “He could not have known it. His behavior to me proved

[053-083]that he did not know.”

[053-084]Her father said nothing more, and Grace went away to the solitude of

[053-085]her chamber.

[053-086]Her heavy disquietude had many shapes; and for a time she put aside the

[053-087]dominant fact to think of her too free conduct towards Giles. His

[053-088]love-making had been brief as it was sweet; but would he on reflection

[053-089]contemn her for forwardness? How could she have been so simple as to

[053-090]suppose she was in a position to behave as she had done! Thus she

[053-091]mentally blamed her ignorance; and yet in the centre of her heart she

[053-092]blessed it a little for what it had momentarily brought her.

[053-093]CHAPTER XL.

[053-094]Life among the people involved in these events seemed to be suppressed

[053-095]and hide-bound for a while. Grace seldom showed herself outside the

[053-096]house, never outside the garden; for she feared she might encounter

[053-097]Giles Winterborne; and that she could not bear.

[053-098]This pensive intramural existence of the self-constituted nun appeared

[053-099]likely to continue for an indefinite time. She had learned that there

[053-100]was one possibility in which her formerly imagined position might

[053-101]become real, and only one; that her husband’s absence should continue

[053-102]long enough to amount to positive desertion. But she never allowed her

[053-103]mind to dwell much upon the thought; still less did she deliberately

[053-104]hope for such a result. Her regard for Winterborne had been rarefied by

[053-105]the shock which followed its avowal into an ethereal emotion that had

[053-106]little to do with living and doing.

[053-107]As for Giles, he was lying—or rather sitting—ill at his hut. A feverish

[053-108]indisposition which had been hanging about him for some time, the

[053-109]result of a chill caught the previous winter, seemed to acquire

[053-110]virulence with the prostration of his hopes. But not a soul knew of his

[053-111]languor, and he did not think the case serious enough to send for a

[053-112]medical man. After a few days he was better again, and crept about his

[053-113]home in a great coat, attending to his simple wants as usual with his

[053-114]own hands. So matters stood when the limpid inertion of Grace’s

[053-115]pool-like existence was disturbed as by a geyser. She received a letter

[053-116]from Fitzpiers.

[053-117]Such a terrible letter it was in its import, though couched in the

[053-118]gentlest language. In his absence Grace had grown to regard him with

[053-119]toleration, and her relation to him with equanimity, till she had

[053-120]almost forgotten how trying his presence would be. He wrote briefly and

[053-121]unaffectedly; he made no excuses, but informed her that he was living

[053-122]quite alone, and had been led to think that they ought to be together,

[053-123]if she would make up her mind to forgive him. He therefore purported to

[053-124]cross the Channel to [[budmouth|Budmouth]] by the steamer on a day he named, which

[053-125]she found to be three days after the time of her present reading.

[053-126]He said that he could not come to Hintock for obvious reasons, which

[053-127]her father would understand even better than herself. As the only

[053-128]alternative she was to be on the quay to meet the steamer when it

[053-129]arrived from the opposite coast, probably about half an hour before

[053-130]midnight, bringing with her any luggage she might require; join him

[053-131]there, and pass with him into the twin vessel, which left immediately

[053-132]the other entered the harbor; returning thus with him to his

[053-133]continental dwelling-place, which he did not name. He had no intention

[053-134]of showing himself on land at all.

[053-135]The troubled Grace took the letter to her father, who now continued for

[053-136]long hours by the fireless summer chimney-corner, as if he thought it

[053-137]were winter, the pitcher of cider standing beside him, mostly untasted,

[053-138]and coated with a film of dust. After reading it he looked up.

[053-139]“You sha’n’t go,” said he.

[053-140]“I had felt I would not,” she answered. “But I did not know what you

[053-141]would say.”

[053-142]“If he comes and lives in England, not too near here and in a

[053-143]respectable way, and wants you to come to him, I am not sure that I’ll

[053-144]oppose him in wishing it,” muttered Melbury. “I’d stint myself to keep

[053-145]you both in a genteel and seemly style. But go abroad you never shall

[053-146]with my consent.”

[053-147]There the question rested that day. Grace was unable to reply to her

[053-148]husband in the absence of an address, and the morrow came, and the next

[053-149]day, and the evening on which he had requested her to meet him.

[053-150]Throughout the whole of it she remained within the four walls of her

[053-151]room.

[053-152]The sense of her harassment, carking doubt of what might be impending,

[053-153]hung like a cowl of blackness over the Melbury household. They spoke

[053-154]almost in whispers, and wondered what Fitzpiers would do next. It was

[053-155]the hope of every one that, finding she did not arrive, he would return

[053-156]again to France; and as for Grace, she was willing to write to him on

[053-157]the most kindly terms if he would only keep away.

[053-158]The night passed, Grace lying tense and wide awake, and her relatives,

[053-159]in great part, likewise. When they met the next morning they were pale

[053-160]and anxious, though neither speaking of the subject which occupied all

[053-161]their thoughts. The day passed as quietly as the previous ones, and she

[053-162]began to think that in the rank caprice of his moods he had abandoned

[053-163]the idea of getting her to join him as quickly as it was formed. All on

[053-164]a sudden, some person who had just come from [[sherton-abbas|Sherton]] entered the house

[053-165]with the news that Mr. Fitzpiers was on his way home to Hintock. He had

[053-166]been seen hiring a carriage at the [[earl-of-wessex-hotel|Earl of Wessex Hotel]].

[053-167]Her father and Grace were both present when the intelligence was

[053-168]announced.

[053-169]“Now,” said Melbury, “we must make the best of what has been a very bad

[053-170]matter. The man is repenting; the partner of his shame, I hear, is gone

[053-171]away from him to Switzerland, so that chapter of his life is probably

[053-172]over. If he chooses to make a home for ye I think you should not say

[053-173]him nay, Grace. Certainly he cannot very well live at Hintock without a

[053-174]blow to his pride; but if he can bear that, and likes Hintock best,

[053-175]why, there’s the empty wing of the house as it was before.”
