# Section 13

[013-001]“I dare say they are,” said [[giles-winterborne|Winterborne]].

[013-002]Before taking a single step in the preparations, Winterborne, with no

[013-003]great hopes, went across that evening to the [[timber-economy|timber]]-merchant’s to

[013-004]ascertain if Grace and her parents would honor him with their presence.

[013-005]Having first to set his nightly gins in the garden, to catch the

[013-006]rabbits that ate his winter-greens, his call was delayed till just

[013-007]after the rising of the moon, whose rays reached the [[little-hintock|Hintock]] houses but

[013-008]fitfully as yet, on account of the [[nature-in-the-woodlanders|trees]]. [[george-melbury|Melbury]] was crossing his yard

[013-009]on his way to call on some one at the larger village, but he readily

[013-010]turned and walked up and down the path with the young man.

[013-011]Giles, in his self-deprecatory sense of living on a much smaller scale

[013-012]than the Melburys did, would not for the world imply that his

[013-013]invitation was to a gathering of any importance. So he put it in the

[013-014]mild form of “Can you come in for an hour, when you have done business,

[013-015]the day after to-morrow; and Mrs. and [[grace-melbury|Miss Melbury]], if they have

[013-016]nothing more pressing to do?”

[013-017]Melbury would give no answer at once. “No, I can’t tell you to-day,” he

[013-018]said. “I must talk it over with the [[gender-roles|women]]. As far as I am concerned, my

[013-019]dear Giles, you know I’ll come with pleasure. But how do I know what

[013-020]Grace’s notions may be? You see, she has been away among cultivated

[013-021]folks a good while; and now this acquaintance with [[felice-charmond|Mrs. Charmond]]—Well,

[013-022]I’ll ask her. I can say no more.”

[013-023]When Winterborne was gone the timber-merchant went on his way. He knew

[013-024]very well that Grace, whatever her own feelings, would either go or not

[013-025]go, according as he suggested; and his instinct was, for the moment, to

[013-026]suggest the negative. His errand took him past the church, and the way

[013-027]to his destination was either across the church-yard or along-side it,

[013-028]the distances being the same. For some reason or other he chose the

[013-029]former way.

[013-030]The moon was faintly lighting up the gravestones, and the path, and the

[013-031]front of the building. Suddenly Mr. Melbury paused, turned ill upon the

[013-032]grass, and approached a particular headstone, where he read, “In memory

[013-033]of John Winterborne,” with the subjoined date and age. It was the grave

[013-034]of Giles’s father.

[013-035]The timber-merchant laid his hand upon the stone, and was humanized.

[013-036]“Jack, my wronged friend!” he said. “I’ll be faithful to my plan of

[013-037]making amends to ’ee.”

[013-038]When he reached home that evening, he said to Grace and [[mrs-melbury|Mrs. Melbury]],

[013-039]who were working at a little table by the fire,

[013-040]“Giles wants us to go down and spend an hour with him the day after

[013-041]to-morrow; and I’m thinking, that as ’tis Giles who asks us, we’ll go.”

[013-042]They assented without demur, and accordingly the timber-merchant sent

[013-043]Giles the next morning an answer in the affirmative.

[013-044]Winterborne, in his modesty, or indifference, had mentioned no

[013-045]particular hour in his invitation; and accordingly Mr. Melbury and his

[013-046]family, expecting no other guests, chose their own time, which chanced

[013-047]to be rather early in the afternoon, by reason of the somewhat quicker

[013-048]despatch than usual of the timber-merchant’s business that day. To show

[013-049]their sense of the unimportance of the occasion, they walked quite

[013-050]slowly to the house, as if they were merely out for a ramble, and going

[013-051]to nothing special at all; or at most intending to pay a casual call

[013-052]and take a cup of tea.

[013-053]At this hour stir and bustle pervaded the interior of Winterborne’s

[013-054]domicile from cellar to apple-loft. He had planned an elaborate high

[013-055]tea for six o’clock or thereabouts, and a good roaring supper to come

[013-056]on about eleven. Being a bachelor of rather retiring habits, the whole

[013-057]of the preparations devolved upon himself and his trusty man and

[013-058]familiar, [[robert-creedle|Robert Creedle]], who did everything that required doing, from

[013-059]making Giles’s bed to catching moles in his field. He was a survival

[013-060]from the days when Giles’s father held the homestead, and Giles was a

[013-061]playing boy.

[013-062]These two, with a certain dilatoriousness which appertained to both,

[013-063]were now in the heat of preparation in the bake-house, expecting nobody

[013-064]before six o’clock. Winterborne was standing before the brick oven in

[013-065]his shirt-sleeves, tossing in thorn sprays, and stirring about the

[013-066]blazing mass with a long-handled, three-pronged Beelzebub kind of fork,

[013-067]the heat shining out upon his streaming face and making his eyes like

[013-068]furnaces, the thorns crackling and sputtering; while Creedle, having

[013-069]ranged the pastry dishes in a row on the table till the oven should be

[013-070]ready, was pressing out the crust of a final apple-pie with a

[013-071]rolling-pin. A great pot boiled on the fire, and through the open door

[013-072]of the back kitchen a boy was seen seated on the fender, emptying the

[013-073]snuffers and scouring the candlesticks, a row of the latter standing

[013-074]upside down on the hob to melt out the grease.

[013-075]Looking up from the rolling-pin, Creedle saw passing the window first

[013-076]the timber-merchant, in his second-best suit, Mrs. Melbury in her best

[013-077]silk, and Grace in the fashionable attire which, in part brought home

[013-078]with her from the Continent, she had worn on her visit to Mrs.

[013-079]Charmond’s. The eyes of the three had been attracted to the proceedings

[013-080]within by the fierce illumination which the oven threw out upon the

[013-081]operators and their utensils.

[013-082]“Lord, Lord! if they baint come a’ready!” said Creedle.

[013-083]“No—hey?” said Giles, looking round aghast; while the boy in the

[013-084]background waved a reeking candlestick in his delight. As there was no

[013-085]help for it, Winterborne went to meet them in the door-way.

[013-086]“My dear Giles, I see we have made a mistake in the time,” said the

[013-087]timber-merchant’s wife, her face lengthening with concern.

[013-088]“Oh, it is not much difference. I hope you’ll come in.”

[013-089]“But this means a regular randyvoo!” said Mr. Melbury, accusingly,

[013-090]glancing round and pointing towards the bake-house with his stick.

[013-091]“Well, yes,” said Giles.

[013-092]“And—not [[great-hintock|Great Hintock]] band, and dancing, surely?”

[013-093]“I told three of ’em they might drop in if they’d nothing else to do,”

[013-094]Giles mildly admitted.

[013-095]“Now, why the name didn’t ye tell us ’twas going to be a serious kind

[013-096]of thing before? How should I know what folk mean if they don’t say?

[013-097]Now, shall we come in, or shall we go home and come back along in a

[013-098]couple of hours?”

[013-099]“I hope you’ll stay, if you’ll be so good as not to mind, now you are

[013-100]here. I shall have it all right and tidy in a very little time. I ought

[013-101]not to have been so backward.” Giles spoke quite anxiously for one of

[013-102]his undemonstrative temperament; for he feared that if the Melburys

[013-103]once were back in their own house they would not be disposed to turn

[013-104]out again.

[013-105]“’Tis we ought not to have been so forward; that’s what ’tis,” said Mr.

[013-106]Melbury, testily. “Don’t keep us here in the sitting-room; lead on to

[013-107]the bakehouse, man. Now we are here we’ll help ye get ready for the

[013-108]rest. Here, mis’ess, take off your things, and help him out in his

[013-109]baking, or he won’t get done to-night. I’ll finish heating the oven,

[013-110]and set you free to go and skiver up them ducks.” His eye had passed

[013-111]with pitiless directness of criticism into yet remote recesses of

[013-112]Winterborne’s awkwardly built premises, where the aforesaid birds were

[013-113]hanging.

[013-114]“And I’ll help finish the tarts,” said Grace, cheerfully.

[013-115]“I don’t know about that,” said her father. “’Tisn’t quite so much in

[013-116]your line as it is in your mother-law’s and mine.”

[013-117]“Of course I couldn’t let you, Grace!” said Giles, with some distress.

[013-118]“I’ll do it, of course,” said Mrs. Melbury, taking off her silk train,

[013-119]hanging it up to a nail, carefully rolling back her sleeves, pinning

[013-120]them to her shoulders, and stripping Giles of his apron for her own

[013-121]use.

[013-122]So Grace pottered idly about, while her father and his wife helped on

[013-123]the preparations. A kindly pity of his household management, which

[013-124]Winterborne saw in her eyes whenever he caught them, depressed him much

[013-125]more than her contempt would have done.

[013-126]Creedle met Giles at the pump after a while, when each of the others

[013-127]was absorbed in the difficulties of a _cuisine_ based on utensils,

[013-128]cupboards, and provisions that were strange to them. He groaned to the

[013-129]young man in a whisper, “This is a bruckle het, maister, I’m much

[013-130]afeared! Who’d ha’ thought they’d ha’ come so soon?”

[013-131]The bitter placidity of Winterborne’s look adumbrated the misgivings he

[013-132]did not care to express. “Have you got the celery ready?” he asked,

[013-133]quickly.

[013-134]“Now that’s a thing I never could mind; no, not if you’d paid me in

[013-135]silver and gold. And I don’t care who the man is, I says that a stick

[013-136]of celery that isn’t scrubbed with the scrubbing-brush is not clean.”

[013-137]“_Very_ well, very well! I’ll attend to it. You go and get ’em

[013-138]comfortable in-doors.”

[013-139]He hastened to the garden, and soon returned, tossing the stalks to

[013-140]Creedle, who was still in a tragic mood. “If ye’d ha’ married, d’ye

[013-141]see, maister,” he said, “this caddle couldn’t have happened to us.”

[013-142]Everything being at last under way, the oven set, and all done that

[013-143]could insure the supper turning up ready at some time or other, Giles

[013-144]and his friends entered the parlor, where the Melburys again dropped

[013-145]into position as guests, though the room was not nearly so warm and

[013-146]cheerful as the blazing bakehouse. Others now arrived, among them

[013-147][[farmer-bawtree|Farmer Bawtree]] and the hollow-turner, and tea went off very well.

[013-148]Grace’s disposition to make the best of everything, and to wink at

[013-149]deficiencies in Winterborne’s menage, was so uniform and persistent

[013-150]that he suspected her of seeing even more deficiencies than he was

[013-151]aware of. That suppressed sympathy which had showed in her face ever

[013-152]since her arrival told him as much too plainly.

[013-153]“This muddling style of house-keeping is what you’ve not lately been

[013-154]used to, I suppose?” he said, when they were a little apart.

[013-155]“No; but I like it; it reminds me so pleasantly that everything here in

[013-156]dear old Hintock is just as it used to be. The oil is—not quite nice;

[013-157]but everything else is.”

[013-158]“The oil?”

[013-159]“On the chairs, I mean; because it gets on one’s dress. Still, mine is

[013-160]not a new one.”

[013-161]Giles found that Creedle, in his zeal to make things look bright, had

[013-162]smeared the chairs with some greasy kind of furniture-polish, and

[013-163]refrained from rubbing it dry in order not to diminish the mirror-like

[013-164]effect that the mixture produced as laid on. Giles apologized and

[013-165]called Creedle; but he felt that the Fates were against him.

[013-166]CHAPTER X.

[013-167]Supper-time came, and with it the hot-baked meats from the oven, laid

[013-168]on a snowy cloth fresh from the press, and reticulated with folds, as

[013-169]in Flemish “Last Suppers.” Creedle and the boy fetched and carried with

[013-170]amazing alacrity, the latter, to mollify his superior and make things

[013-171]pleasant, expressing his admiration of Creedle’s cleverness when they

[013-172]were alone.

[013-173]“I s’pose the time when you learned all these knowing things, Mr.

[013-174]Creedle, was when you was in the militia?”

[013-175]“Well, yes. I seed the world at that time somewhat, certainly, and many

[013-176]ways of strange dashing life. Not but that Giles has worked hard in

[013-177]helping me to bring things to such perfection to-day. ‘Giles,’ says I,

[013-178]though he’s maister. Not that I should call’n maister by rights, for

[013-179]his father growed up side by side with me, as if one mother had twinned

[013-180]us and been our nourishing.”

[013-181]“I s’pose your memory can reach a long way back into history, Mr.

[013-182]Creedle?”

[013-183]“Oh yes. Ancient days, when there was battles and famines and

[013-184]hang-fairs and other pomps, seem to me as yesterday. Ah, many’s the

[013-185]patriarch I’ve seed come and go in this parish! There, he’s calling for

[013-186]more plates. Lord, why can’t ’em turn their plates bottom upward for

[013-187]pudding, as they used to do in former days?”
