# Section 6

[006-001]“He must be a curious young man,” mused the hollow-turner.

[006-002]“He must,” said [[timothy-tangs|Timothy Tangs]].

[006-003]“Nonsense,” said [[george-melbury|Mr. Melbury]], authoritatively, “he’s only a gentleman

[006-004]fond of science and philosophy and poetry, and, in fact, every kind of

[006-005]knowledge; and being lonely here, he passes his time in making such

[006-006]matters his hobby.”

[006-007]“Well,” said old Timothy, “’tis a strange thing about doctors that the

[006-008]worse they be the better they be. I mean that if you hear anything of

[006-009]this sort about ’em, ten to one they can cure ye as nobody else can.”

[006-010]“True,” said Bawtree, emphatically. “And for my part I shall take my

[006-011]custom from old Jones and go to this one directly I’ve anything the

[006-012]matter with me. That last medicine old Jones gave me had no taste in it

[006-013]at all.”

[006-014]Mr. Melbury, as became a well-informed man, did not listen to these

[006-015]recitals, being moreover preoccupied with the business appointment

[006-016]which had come into his head. He walked up and down, looking on the

[006-017]floor—his usual custom when undecided. That stiffness about the arm,

[006-018]hip, and knee-joint which was apparent when he walked was the net

[006-019]product of the divers sprains and over-exertions that had been required

[006-020]of him in handling [[nature-in-the-woodlanders|trees]] and [[timber-economy|timber]] when a young man, for he was of the

[006-021]sort called self-made, and had worked hard. He knew the origin of every

[006-022]one of these cramps: that in his left shoulder had come of carrying a

[006-023]pollard, unassisted, from Tutcombe Bottom home; that in one leg was

[006-024]caused by the crash of an elm against it when they were felling; that

[006-025]in the other was from lifting a bole. On many a morrow after wearying

[006-026]himself by these prodigious muscular efforts, he had risen from his bed

[006-027]fresh as usual; his lassitude had departed, apparently forever; and

[006-028]confident in the recuperative power of his youth, he had repeated the

[006-029]strains anew. But treacherous Time had been only hiding ill results

[006-030]when they could be guarded against, for greater accumulation when they

[006-031]could not. In his declining years the store had been unfolded in the

[006-032]form of rheumatisms, pricks, and spasms, in every one of which Melbury

[006-033]recognized some act which, had its consequence been contemporaneously

[006-034]made known, he would wisely have abstained from repeating.

[006-035]On a summons by [[grammer-oliver|Grammer Oliver]] to breakfast, he left the shed. Reaching

[006-036]the kitchen, where the family breakfasted in winter to save

[006-037]house-labor, he sat down by the fire, and looked a long time at the

[006-038]pair of dancing shadows cast by each fire-iron and dog-knob on the

[006-039]whitewashed chimney-corner—a yellow one from the window, and a blue one

[006-040]from the fire.

[006-041]“I don’t quite know what to do to-day,” he said to his wife at last.

[006-042]“I’ve recollected that I promised to meet [[felice-charmond|Mrs. Charmond]]’s steward in

[006-043]Round Wood at twelve o’clock, and yet I want to go for Grace.”

[006-044]“Why not let [[giles-winterborne|Giles]] fetch her by himself? ’Twill bring ’em together all

[006-045]the quicker.”

[006-046]“I could do that—but I should like to go myself. I always have gone,

[006-047]without fail, every time hitherto. It has been a great pleasure to

[006-048]drive into [[sherton-abbas|Sherton]], and wait and see her arrive; and perhaps she’ll be

[006-049]disappointed if I stay away.”

[006-050]“You may be disappointed, but I don’t think she will, if you send

[006-051]Giles,” said [[mrs-melbury|Mrs. Melbury]], dryly.

[006-052]“Very well—I’ll send him.”

[006-053]Melbury was often persuaded by the quietude of his wife’s words when

[006-054]strenuous argument would have had no effect. This second Mrs. Melbury

[006-055]was a placid woman, who had been nurse to his child Grace before her

[006-056]mother’s death. After that melancholy event little Grace had clung to

[006-057]the nurse with much [[love-and-desire|affection]]; and ultimately Melbury, in dread lest

[006-058]the only woman who cared for the girl should be induced to leave her,

[006-059]persuaded the mild Lucy to marry him. The arrangement—for it was little

[006-060]more—had worked satisfactorily enough; Grace had thriven, and Melbury

[006-061]had not repented.

[006-062]He returned to the spar-house and found Giles near at hand, to whom he

[006-063]explained the change of plan. “As she won’t arrive till five o’clock,

[006-064]you can get your business very well over in time to receive her,” said

[006-065]Melbury. “The green gig will do for her; you’ll spin along quicker with

[006-066]that, and won’t be late upon the road. Her boxes can be called for by

[006-067]one of the wagons.”

[006-068]Winterborne, knowing nothing of the timber-merchant’s restitutory aims,

[006-069]quietly thought all this to be a kindly [[fate-and-free-will|chance]]. Wishing even more than

[006-070]her father to despatch his apple-tree business in the market before

[006-071]Grace’s arrival, he prepared to start at once.

[006-072]Melbury was careful that the turnout should be seemly. The gig-wheels,

[006-073]for instance, were not always washed during winter-time before a

[006-074]journey, the muddy roads rendering that labor useless; but they were

[006-075]washed to-day. The harness was blacked, and when the rather elderly

[006-076]white horse had been put in, and Winterborne was in his seat ready to

[006-077]start, Mr. Melbury stepped out with a blacking-brush, and with his own

[006-078]hands touched over the yellow hoofs of the animal.

[006-079]“You see, Giles,” he said, as he blacked, “coming from a fashionable

[006-080]school, she might feel shocked at the homeliness of home; and ’tis

[006-081]these little things that catch a dainty woman’s eye if they are

[006-082]neglected. We, living here alone, don’t notice how the whitey-brown

[006-083]creeps out of the earth over us; but she, fresh from a city—why, she’ll

[006-084]notice everything!”

[006-085]“That she will,” said Giles.

[006-086]“And scorn us if we don’t mind.”

[006-087]“Not scorn us.”

[006-088]“No, no, no—that’s only words. She’s too good a girl to do that. But

[006-089]when we consider what she knows, and what she has seen since she last

[006-090]saw us, ’tis as well to meet her views as nearly as possible. Why, ’tis

[006-091]a year since she was in this old place, owing to her going abroad in

[006-092]the summer, which I agreed to, thinking it best for her; and naturally

[006-093]we shall look small, just at first—I only say just at first.”

[006-094]Mr. Melbury’s tone evinced a certain exultation in the very sense of

[006-095]that inferiority he affected to deplore; for this advanced and refined

[006-096]being, was she not his own all the time? Not so Giles; he felt

[006-097]doubtful—perhaps a trifle cynical—for that strand was wound into him

[006-098]with the rest. He looked at his clothes with misgiving, then with

[006-099]indifference.

[006-100]It was his custom during the planting season to carry a specimen

[006-101]apple-tree to market with him as an advertisement of what he dealt in.

[006-102]This had been tied across the gig; and as it would be left behind in

[006-103]the town, it would cause no inconvenience to Miss [[grace-melbury|Grace Melbury]] coming

[006-104]home.

[006-105]He drove away, the twigs nodding with each step of the horse; and

[006-106]Melbury went in-doors. Before the gig had passed out of sight, Mr.

[006-107]Melbury reappeared and shouted after—

[006-108]“Here, Giles,” he said, breathlessly following with some wraps, “it may

[006-109]be very chilly to-night, and she may want something extra about her.

[006-110]And, Giles,” he added, when the young man, having taken the articles,

[006-111]put the horse in motion once more, “tell her that I should have come

[006-112]myself, but I had particular business with Mrs. Charmond’s agent, which

[006-113]prevented me. Don’t forget.”

[006-114]He watched Winterborne out of sight, saying, with a jerk—a shape into

[006-115]which emotion with him often resolved itself—“There, now, I hope the

[006-116]two will bring it to a point and have done with it! ’Tis a pity to let

[006-117]such a girl throw herself away upon him—a thousand pities!...And yet

[006-118]’tis my duty for his father’s sake.”

[006-119]CHAPTER V.

[006-120]Winterborne sped on his way to Sherton Abbas without elation and

[006-121]without discomposure. Had he regarded his inner self spectacularly, as

[006-122]lovers are now daily more wont to do, he might have felt [[pride-and-humiliation|pride]] in the

[006-123]discernment of a somewhat rare power in him—that of keeping not only

[006-124]judgment but emotion suspended in difficult cases. But he noted it not.

[006-125]Neither did he observe what was also the fact, that though he cherished

[006-126]a true and warm feeling towards Grace Melbury, he was not altogether

[006-127]her fool just now. It must be remembered that he had not seen her for a

[006-128]year.

[006-129]Arrived at the entrance to a long flat lane, which had taken the spirit

[006-130]out of many a pedestrian in times when, with the majority, to travel

[006-131]meant to walk, he saw before him the trim figure of a young woman in

[006-132]pattens, journeying with that steadfast concentration which means

[006-133]purpose and not pleasure. He was soon near enough to see that she was

[006-134][[marty-south|Marty South]]. Click, click, click went the pattens; and she did not turn

[006-135]her head.

[006-136]She had, however, become aware before this that the driver of the

[006-137]approaching gig was Giles. She had shrunk from being overtaken by him

[006-138]thus; but as it was inevitable, she had braced herself up for his

[006-139]inspection by closing her lips so as to make her mouth quite

[006-140]unemotional, and by throwing an additional firmness into her tread.

[006-141]“Why do you wear pattens, Marty? The turnpike is clean enough, although

[006-142]the lanes are muddy.”

[006-143]“They save my boots.”

[006-144]“But twelve miles in pattens—’twill twist your feet off. Come, get up

[006-145]and ride with me.”

[006-146]She hesitated, removed her pattens, knocked the gravel out of them

[006-147]against the wheel, and mounted in front of the nodding specimen

[006-148]apple-tree. She had so arranged her bonnet with a full border and

[006-149]trimmings that her lack of long hair did not much injure her

[006-150]appearance; though Giles, of course, saw that it was gone, and may have

[006-151]guessed her motive in parting with it, such sales, though infrequent,

[006-152]being not unheard of in that locality.

[006-153]But nature’s adornment was still hard by—in fact, within two feet of

[006-154]him, though he did not know it. In Marty’s basket was a brown paper

[006-155]packet, and in the packet the chestnut locks, which, by reason of the

[006-156]barber’s request for [[secrecy-and-concealment|secrecy]], she had not ventured to intrust to other

[006-157]hands.

[006-158]Giles asked, with some hesitation, how her father was getting on.

[006-159]He was better, she said; he would be able to work in a day or two; he

[006-160]would be quite well but for his craze about the tree falling on him.

[006-161]“You know why I don’t ask for him so often as I might, I suppose?” said

[006-162]Winterborne. “Or don’t you know?”

[006-163]“I think I do.”

[006-164]“Because of the houses?”

[006-165]She nodded.

[006-166]“Yes. I am afraid it may seem that my anxiety is about those houses,

[006-167]which I should lose by his death, more than about him. Marty, I do feel

[006-168]anxious about the houses, since half my income depends upon them; but I

[006-169]do likewise care for him; and it almost seems wrong that houses should

[006-170]be leased for lives, so as to lead to such mixed feelings.”

[006-171]“After father’s death they will be Mrs. Charmond’s?”

[006-172]“They’ll be hers.”

[006-173]“They are going to keep company with my hair,” she thought.

[006-174]Thus talking, they reached the town. By no pressure would she ride up

[006-175]the street with him. “That’s the right of another woman,” she said,

[006-176]with playful malice, as she put on her pattens. “I wonder what you are

[006-177]thinking of! Thank you for the lift in that handsome gig. Good-by.”

[006-178]He blushed a little, shook his head at her, and drove on ahead into the

[006-179]streets—the churches, the abbey, and other buildings on this clear

[006-180]bright morning having the liny distinctness of architectural drawings,

[006-181]as if the original dream and vision of the conceiving master-mason,

[006-182]some mediaeval Vilars or other unknown to fame, were for a few minutes

[006-183]flashed down through the centuries to an unappreciative age. Giles saw

[006-184]their eloquent look on this day of transparency, but could not construe

[006-185]it. He turned into the inn-yard.

[006-186]Marty, following the same track, marched promptly to the

[006-187]hair-dresser’s, [[percombe|Mr. Percombe]]’s. Percombe was the chief of his trade in

[006-188]Sherton Abbas. He had the patronage of such county offshoots as had

[006-189]been obliged to seek the shelter of small houses in that ancient town,

[006-190]of the local clergy, and so on, for some of whom he had made wigs,

[006-191]while others among them had compensated for neglecting him in their

[006-192]lifetime by patronizing him when they were dead, and letting him shave

[006-193]their corpses. On the strength of all this he had taken down his pole,

[006-194]and called himself “Perruquier to the aristocracy.”
