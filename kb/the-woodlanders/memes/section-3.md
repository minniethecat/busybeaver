# Section 3

[003-001]A weak voice inside answered in the negative; adding, “I should be all

[003-002]right by to-morrow if it were not for the tree!”

[003-003]“The tree again—always the tree! Oh, father, don’t worry so about that.

[003-004]You know it can do you no harm.”

[003-005]“Who have ye had talking to ye down-stairs?”

[003-006]“A [[sherton-abbas|Sherton]] man called—nothing to trouble about,” she said, soothingly.

[003-007]“Father,” she went on, “can [[felice-charmond|Mrs. Charmond]] turn us out of our house if

[003-008]she’s minded to?”

[003-009]“Turn us out? No. Nobody can turn us out till my poor soul is turned

[003-010]out of my body. ’Tis life-hold, like Ambrose [[giles-winterborne|Winterborne]]’s. But when my

[003-011]life drops ’twill be hers—not till then.” His words on this subject so

[003-012]far had been rational and firm enough. But now he lapsed into his

[003-013]moaning strain: “And the tree will do it—that tree will soon be the

[003-014]death of me.”

[003-015]“Nonsense, you know better. How can it be?” She refrained from further

[003-016]speech, and descended to the ground-floor again.

[003-017]“Thank Heaven, then,” she said to herself, “what belongs to me I keep.”

[003-018]CHAPTER III.

[003-019]The lights in the village went out, house after house, till there only

[003-020]remained two in the darkness. One of these came from a residence on the

[003-021]hill-side, of which there is nothing to say at present; the other shone

[003-022]from the window of [[marty-south|Marty South]]. Precisely the same outward effect was

[003-023]produced here, however, by her rising when the clock struck ten and

[003-024]hanging up a thick cloth curtain. The door it was necessary to keep

[003-025]ajar in hers, as in most cottages, because of the smoke; but she

[003-026]obviated the effect of the ribbon of light through the chink by hanging

[003-027]a cloth over that also. She was one of those people who, if they have

[003-028]to work harder than their neighbors, prefer to keep the necessity a

[003-029][[secrecy-and-concealment|secret]] as far as possible; and but for the slight sounds of

[003-030]wood-splintering which came from within, no wayfarer would have

[003-031]perceived that here the cottager did not sleep as elsewhere.

[003-032]Eleven, twelve, one o’clock struck; the heap of spars grew higher, and

[003-033]the pile of chips and ends more bulky. Even the light on the hill had

[003-034]now been extinguished; but still she worked on. When the temperature of

[003-035]the night without had fallen so low as to make her chilly, she opened a

[003-036]large blue umbrella to ward off the draught from the door. The two

[003-037]sovereigns confronted her from the looking-glass in such a manner as to

[003-038]suggest a pair of jaundiced eyes on the watch for an opportunity.

[003-039]Whenever she sighed for weariness she lifted her gaze towards them, but

[003-040]withdrew it quickly, stroking her tresses with her fingers for a

[003-041]moment, as if to assure herself that they were still secure. When the

[003-042]clock struck three she arose and tied up the spars she had last made in

[003-043]a bundle resembling those that lay against the wall.

[003-044]She wrapped round her a long red woollen cravat and opened the door.

[003-045]The night in all its fulness met her flatly on the threshold, like the

[003-046]very brink of an absolute void, or the antemundane Ginnung-Gap believed

[003-047]in by her Teuton forefathers. For her eyes were fresh from the blaze,

[003-048]and here there was no street-lamp or lantern to form a kindly

[003-049]transition between the inner glare and the outer dark. A lingering wind

[003-050]brought to her ear the creaking sound of two over-crowded branches in

[003-051]the neighboring wood which were rubbing each other into wounds, and

[003-052]other vocalized sorrows of the [[nature-in-the-woodlanders|trees]], together with the screech of

[003-053]owls, and the fluttering tumble of some awkward wood-pigeon

[003-054]ill-balanced on its roosting-bough.

[003-055]But the pupils of her young eyes soon expanded, and she could see well

[003-056]enough for her purpose. Taking a bundle of spars under each arm, and

[003-057]guided by the serrated line of tree-tops against the sky, she went some

[003-058]hundred yards or more down the lane till she reached a long open shed,

[003-059]carpeted around with the dead leaves that lay about everywhere. Night,

[003-060]that strange personality, which within walls brings ominous

[003-061]introspectiveness and self-distrust, but under the open sky banishes

[003-062]such subjective anxieties as too trivial for thought, inspired Marty

[003-063]South with a less perturbed and brisker manner now. She laid the spars

[003-064]on the ground within the shed and returned for more, going to and fro

[003-065]till her whole manufactured stock were deposited here.

[003-066]This erection was the wagon-house of the chief man of business

[003-067]hereabout, Mr. [[george-melbury|George Melbury]], the [[timber-economy|timber]], bark, and [[woodland-crafts|copse-ware]]

[003-068]merchant for whom Marty’s father did work of this sort by the piece. It

[003-069]formed one of the many rambling out-houses which surrounded his

[003-070]dwelling, an equally irregular block of building, whose immense

[003-071]chimneys could just be discerned even now. The four huge wagons under

[003-072]the shed were built on those ancient lines whose proportions have been

[003-073]ousted by modern patterns, their shapes bulging and curving at the base

[003-074]and ends like Trafalgar line-of-battle ships, with which venerable

[003-075]hulks, indeed, these vehicles evidenced a constructed spirit curiously

[003-076]in harmony. One was laden with sheep-cribs, another with hurdles,

[003-077]another with ash poles, and the fourth, at the foot of which she had

[003-078]placed her thatching-spars was half full of similar bundles.

[003-079]She was pausing a moment with that easeful sense of accomplishment

[003-080]which follows work done that has been a hard struggle in the doing,

[003-081]when she heard a woman’s voice on the other side of the hedge say,

[003-082]anxiously, “George!” In a moment the name was repeated, with “Do come

[003-083]indoors! What are you doing there?”

[003-084]The cart-house adjoined the garden, and before Marty had moved she saw

[003-085]enter the latter from the timber-merchant’s back door an elderly woman

[003-086]sheltering a candle with her hand, the light from which cast a moving

[003-087]thorn-pattern of shade on Marty’s face. Its rays soon fell upon a man

[003-088]whose clothes were roughly thrown on, standing in advance of the

[003-089]speaker. He was a thin, slightly stooping figure, with a small nervous

[003-090]mouth and a face cleanly shaven; and he walked along the path with his

[003-091]eyes bent on the ground. In the pair Marty South recognized her

[003-092]employer Melbury and his wife. She was the [[mrs-melbury|second Mrs. Melbury]], the

[003-093]first having died shortly after the birth of the timber-merchant’s only

[003-094]child.

[003-095]“’Tis no use to stay in bed,” he said, as soon as she came up to where

[003-096]he was pacing restlessly about. “I can’t sleep—I keep thinking of

[003-097]things, and worrying about the girl, till I’m quite in a fever of

[003-098]anxiety.” He went on to say that he could not think why “she (Marty

[003-099]knew he was speaking of his daughter) did not answer his letter. She

[003-100]must be ill—she must, certainly,” he said.

[003-101]“No, no. ’Tis all right, George,” said his wife; and she assured him

[003-102]that such things always did appear so gloomy in the night-time, if

[003-103]people allowed their minds to run on them; that when morning came it

[003-104]was seen that such fears were nothing but shadows. “Grace is as well as

[003-105]you or I,” she declared.

[003-106]But he persisted that she did not see all—that she did not see as much

[003-107]as he. His daughter’s not writing was only one part of his worry. On

[003-108]account of her he was anxious concerning money affairs, which he would

[003-109]never alarm his mind about otherwise. The reason he gave was that, as

[003-110]she had nobody to depend upon for a provision but himself, he wished

[003-111]her, when he was gone, to be securely out of risk of poverty.

[003-112]To this Mrs. Melbury replied that Grace would be sure to marry well,

[003-113]and that hence a hundred pounds more or less from him would not make

[003-114]much difference.

[003-115]Her husband said that that was what she, Mrs. Melbury, naturally

[003-116]thought; but there she was wrong, and in that lay the source of his

[003-117]trouble. “I have a plan in my head about her,” he said; “and according

[003-118]to my plan she won’t marry a rich man.”

[003-119]“A plan for her not to marry well?” said his wife, surprised.

[003-120]“Well, in one sense it is that,” replied Melbury. “It is a plan for her

[003-121]to marry a particular person, and as he has not so much money as she

[003-122]might expect, it might be called as you call it. I may not be able to

[003-123]carry it out; and even if I do, it may not be a good thing for her. I

[003-124]want her to marry Giles Winterborne.”

[003-125]His companion repeated the name. “Well, it is all right,” she said,

[003-126]presently. “He adores the very ground she walks on; only he’s close,

[003-127]and won’t show it much.”

[003-128]Marty South appeared startled, and could not tear herself away.

[003-129]Yes, the timber-merchant asserted, he knew that well enough.

[003-130]Winterborne had been interested in his daughter for years; that was

[003-131]what had led him into the notion of their union. And he knew that she

[003-132]used to have no objection to him. But it was not any difficulty about

[003-133]that which embarrassed him. It was that, since he had educated her so

[003-134]well, and so long, and so far above the level of daughters thereabout,

[003-135]it was _wasting her_ to give her to a man of no higher standing than

[003-136]the young man in question.

[003-137]“That’s what I have been thinking,” said Mrs. Melbury.

[003-138]“Well, then, Lucy, now you’ve hit it,” answered the timber-merchant,

[003-139]with feeling. “There lies my trouble. I vowed to let her marry him, and

[003-140]to make her as valuable as I could to him by [[education-and-class-mobility|schooling]] her as many

[003-141]years and as thoroughly as possible. I mean to keep my vow. I made it

[003-142]because I did his father a terrible wrong; and it was a weight on my

[003-143]conscience ever since that time till this scheme of making amends

[003-144]occurred to me through seeing that Giles liked her.”

[003-145]“Wronged his father?” asked Mrs. Melbury.

[003-146]“Yes, grievously wronged him,” said her husband.

[003-147]“Well, don’t think of it to-night,” she urged. “Come indoors.”

[003-148]“No, no, the air cools my head. I shall not stay long.” He was silent a

[003-149]while; then he told her, as nearly as Marty could gather, that his

[003-150]first wife, his daughter Grace’s mother, was first the sweetheart of

[003-151]Winterborne’s father, who loved her tenderly, till he, the speaker, won

[003-152]her away from him by a trick, because he wanted to marry her himself.

[003-153]He sadly went on to say that the other man’s happiness was ruined by

[003-154]it; that though he married Winterborne’s mother, it was but a

[003-155]half-hearted business with him. Melbury added that he was afterwards

[003-156]very miserable at what he had done; but that as time went on, and the

[003-157]children grew up, and seemed to be attached to each other, he

[003-158]determined to do all he could to right the wrong by letting his

[003-159]daughter marry the lad; not only that, but to give her the best

[003-160]education he could afford, so as to make the gift as valuable a one as

[003-161]it lay in his power to bestow. “I still mean to do it,” said Melbury.

[003-162]“Then do,” said she.

[003-163]“But all these things trouble me,” said he; “for I feel I am

[003-164]sacrificing her for my own sin; and I think of her, and often come down

[003-165]here and look at this.”

[003-166]“Look at what?” asked his wife.

[003-167]He took the candle from her hand, held it to the ground, and removed a

[003-168]tile which lay in the garden-path. “’Tis the track of her shoe that she

[003-169]made when she ran down here the day before she went away all those

[003-170]months ago. I covered it up when she was gone; and when I come here and

[003-171]look at it, I ask myself again, why should she be sacrificed to a poor

[003-172]man?”

[003-173]“It is not altogether a sacrifice,” said the woman. “He is in [[love-and-desire|love]] with

[003-174]her, and he’s honest and upright. If she encourages him, what can you

[003-175]wish for more?”
