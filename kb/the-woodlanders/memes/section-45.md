# Section 45

[045-001][[marty-south|Marty]] hesitated. “From me,” she said, shyly, though with noticeable

[045-002]firmness.

[045-003]This letter contained, in fact, Marty’s declaration that she was the

[045-004]original owner of [[felice-charmond|Mrs. Charmond]]’s supplementary locks, and enclosed a

[045-005]sample from the native stock, which had grown considerably by this

[045-006]time. It was her long contemplated apple of discord, and much her hand

[045-007]trembled as she handed the document up to him.

[045-008]But it was impossible on account of the gloom for [[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Fitzpiers]] to read it

[045-009]then, while he had the curiosity to do so, and he put it in his pocket.

[045-010]His imagination having already centred itself on [[hintock-house|Hintock House]], in his

[045-011]pocket the letter remained unopened and forgotten, all the while that

[045-012]Marty was hopefully picturing its excellent weaning effect upon him.

[045-013]He was not long in reaching the precincts of the Manor House. He drew

[045-014]rein under a group of dark oaks commanding a view of the front, and

[045-015]reflected a while. His entry would not be altogether unnatural in the

[045-016]circumstances of her possible indisposition; but upon the whole he

[045-017]thought it best to avoid riding up to the door. By silently approaching

[045-018]he could retreat unobserved in the event of her not being alone.

[045-019]Thereupon he dismounted, hitched Darling to a stray bough hanging a

[045-020]little below the general browsing line of the [[nature-in-the-woodlanders|trees]], and proceeded to

[045-021]the door on foot.

[045-022]In the mean time [[george-melbury|Melbury]] had returned from [[shottsford-forum|Shottsford-Forum]]. The great

[045-023]court or quadrangle of the [[timber-economy|timber]]-merchant’s house, divided from the

[045-024]shady lane by an ivy-covered wall, was entered by two white gates, one

[045-025]standing near each extremity of the wall. It so happened that at the

[045-026]moment when Fitzpiers was riding out at the lower gate on his way to

[045-027]the Manor House, Melbury was approaching the upper gate to enter it.

[045-028]Fitzpiers being in front of Melbury was seen by the latter, but the

[045-029]surgeon, never turning his head, did not observe his father-in-law,

[045-030]ambling slowly and silently along under the trees, though his horse too

[045-031]was a gray one.

[045-032]“How is Grace?” said his wife, as soon as he entered.

[045-033]Melbury looked gloomy. “She is not at all well,” he said. “I don’t like

[045-034]the looks of her at all. I couldn’t bear the notion of her biding away

[045-035]in a strange place any longer, and I begged her to let me get her home.

[045-036]At last she agreed to it, but not till after much persuading. I was

[045-037]then sorry that I rode over instead of driving; but I have hired a nice

[045-038]comfortable carriage—the easiest-going I could get—and she’ll be here

[045-039]in a couple of hours or less. I rode on ahead to tell you to get her

[045-040]room ready; but I see her husband has come back.”

[045-041]“Yes,” said [[mrs-melbury|Mrs. Melbury]]. She expressed her concern that her husband

[045-042]had hired a carriage all the way from Shottsford. “What it will cost!”

[045-043]she said.

[045-044]“I don’t care what it costs!” he exclaimed, testily. “I was determined

[045-045]to get her home. Why she went away I can’t think! She acts in a way

[045-046]that is not at all likely to mend matters as far as I can see.” (Grace

[045-047]had not told her father of her interview with Mrs. Charmond, and the

[045-048]disclosure that had been whispered in her startled ear.) “Since Edgar

[045-049]is come,” he continued, “he might have waited in till I got home, to

[045-050]ask me how she was, if only for a compliment. I saw him go out; where

[045-051]is he gone?”

[045-052]Mrs. Melbury did not know positively; but she told her husband that

[045-053]there was not much doubt about the place of his first visit after an

[045-054]absence. She had, in fact, seen Fitzpiers take the direction of the

[045-055]Manor House.

[045-056]Melbury said no more. It was exasperating to him that just at this

[045-057]moment, when there was every reason for Fitzpiers to stay indoors, or

[045-058]at any rate to ride along the Shottsford road to meet his ailing wife,

[045-059]he should be doing despite to her by going elsewhere. The old man went

[045-060]out-of-doors again; and his horse being hardly unsaddled as yet, he

[045-061]told [[john-upjohn|Upjohn]] to retighten the girths, when he again mounted, and rode

[045-062]off at the heels of the surgeon.

[045-063]By the time that Melbury reached the park, he was prepared to go any

[045-064]lengths in combating this rank and reckless errantry of his daughter’s

[045-065]husband. He would fetch home Edgar Fitzpiers to-night by some means,

[045-066]rough or fair: in his view there could come of his interference nothing

[045-067]worse than what existed at present. And yet to every bad there is a

[045-068]worse.

[045-069]He had entered by the bridle-gate which admitted to the park on this

[045-070]side, and cantered over the soft turf almost in the tracks of

[045-071]Fitzpiers’s horse, till he reached the clump of trees under which his

[045-072]precursor had halted. The whitish object that was indistinctly visible

[045-073]here in the gloom of the boughs he found to be Darling, as left by

[045-074]Fitzpiers.

[045-075]“D—n him! why did he not ride up to the house in an honest way?” said

[045-076]Melbury.

[045-077]He profited by Fitzpiers’s example; dismounting, he tied his horse

[045-078]under an adjoining tree, and went on to the house on foot, as the other

[045-079]had done. He was no longer disposed to stick at trifles in his

[045-080]investigation, and did not hesitate to gently open the front door

[045-081]without ringing.

[045-082]The large square hall, with its oak floor, staircase, and wainscot, was

[045-083]lighted by a dim lamp hanging from a beam. Not a soul was visible. He

[045-084]went into the corridor and listened at a door which he knew to be that

[045-085]of the drawing-room; there was no sound, and on turning the handle he

[045-086]found the room empty. A fire burning low in the grate was the sole

[045-087]light of the apartment; its beams flashed mockingly on the somewhat

[045-088]showy Versaillese furniture and gilding here, in style as unlike that

[045-089]of the structural parts of the building as it was possible to be, and

[045-090]probably introduced by Felice to counteract the fine old-English gloom

[045-091]of the place. Disappointed in his hope of confronting his son-in-law

[045-092]here, he went on to the dining-room; this was without light or fire,

[045-093]and pervaded by a cold atmosphere, which signified that she had not

[045-094]dined there that day.

[045-095]By this time Melbury’s mood had a little mollified. Everything here was

[045-096]so pacific, so unaggressive in its repose, that he was no longer

[045-097]incited to provoke a collision with Fitzpiers or with anybody. The

[045-098]comparative stateliness of the apartments influenced him to an emotion,

[045-099]rather than to a belief, that where all was outwardly so good and

[045-100]proper there could not be quite that delinquency within which he had

[045-101]suspected. It occurred to him, too, that even if his suspicion were

[045-102]justified, his abrupt, if not unwarrantable, entry into the house might

[045-103]end in confounding its inhabitant at the expense of his daughter’s

[045-104][[pride-and-humiliation|dignity]] and his own. Any ill result would be pretty sure to hit Grace

[045-105]hardest in the long-run. He would, after all, adopt the more rational

[045-106]course, and plead with Fitzpiers privately, as he had pleaded with Mrs.

[045-107]Charmond.

[045-108]He accordingly retreated as silently as he had come. Passing the door

[045-109]of the drawing-room anew, he fancied that he heard a noise within which

[045-110]was not the crackling of the fire. Melbury gently reopened the door to

[045-111]a distance of a few inches, and saw at the opposite window two figures

[045-112]in the act of stepping out—a man and a woman—in whom he recognized the

[045-113]lady of the house and his son-in-law. In a moment they had disappeared

[045-114]amid the gloom of the lawn.

[045-115]He returned into the hall, and let himself out by the carriage-entrance

[045-116]door, coming round to the lawn front in time to see the two figures

[045-117]parting at the railing which divided the precincts of the house from

[045-118]the open park. Mrs. Charmond turned to hasten back immediately that

[045-119]Fitzpiers had left her side, and he was speedily absorbed into the

[045-120]duskiness of the trees.

[045-121]Melbury waited till Mrs. Charmond had re-entered the drawing-room, and

[045-122]then followed after Fitzpiers, thinking that he would allow the latter

[045-123]to mount and ride ahead a little way before overtaking him and giving

[045-124]him a piece of his mind. His son-in-law might possibly see the second

[045-125]horse near his own; but that would do him no harm, and might prepare

[045-126]him for what he was to expect.

[045-127]The event, however, was different from the plan. On plunging into the

[045-128]thick shade of the clump of oaks, he could not perceive his horse

[045-129]Blossom anywhere; but feeling his way carefully along, he by-and-by

[045-130]discerned Fitzpiers’s mare Darling still standing as before under the

[045-131]adjoining tree. For a moment Melbury thought that his own horse, being

[045-132]young and strong, had broken away from her fastening; but on listening

[045-133]intently he could hear her ambling comfortably along a little way

[045-134]ahead, and a creaking of the saddle which showed that she had a rider.

[045-135]Walking on as far as the small gate in the corner of the park, he met a

[045-136]laborer, who, in reply to Melbury’s inquiry if he had seen any person

[045-137]on a gray horse, said that he had only met Dr. Fitzpiers.

[045-138]It was just what Melbury had begun to suspect: Fitzpiers had mounted

[045-139]the mare which did not belong to him in mistake for his own—an

[045-140]oversight easily explicable, in a man ever unwitting in horse-flesh, by

[045-141]the darkness of the spot and the near similarity of the animals in

[045-142]appearance, though Melbury’s was readily enough seen to be the grayer

[045-143]horse by day. He hastened back, and did what seemed best in the

[045-144]circumstances—got upon old Darling, and rode rapidly after Fitzpiers.

[045-145]Melbury had just entered the wood, and was winding along the cart-way

[045-146]which led through it, channelled deep in the leaf-mould with large ruts

[045-147]that were formed by the timber-wagons in fetching the spoil of the

[045-148]plantations, when all at once he descried in front, at a point where

[045-149]the road took a turning round a large chestnut-tree, the form of his

[045-150]own horse Blossom, at which Melbury quickened Darling’s pace, thinking

[045-151]to come up with Fitzpiers.

[045-152]Nearer view revealed that the horse had no rider. At Melbury’s approach

[045-153]it galloped friskily away under the trees in a homeward direction.

[045-154]Thinking something was wrong, the timber-merchant dismounted as soon as

[045-155]he reached the chestnut, and after feeling about for a minute or two

[045-156]discovered Fitzpiers lying on the ground.

[045-157]“Here—help!” cried the latter as soon as he felt Melbury’s touch; “I

[045-158]have been thrown off, but there’s not much harm done, I think.”

[045-159]Since Melbury could not now very well read the younger man the lecture

[045-160]he had intended, and as friendliness would be hypocrisy, his instinct

[045-161]was to speak not a single word to his son-in-law. He raised Fitzpiers

[045-162]into a sitting posture, and found that he was a little stunned and

[045-163]stupefied, but, as he had said, not otherwise hurt. How this fall had

[045-164]come about was readily conjecturable: Fitzpiers, imagining there was

[045-165]only old Darling under him, had been taken unawares by the younger

[045-166]horse’s sprightliness.

[045-167]Melbury was a traveller of the old-fashioned sort; having just come

[045-168]from Shottsford-Forum, he still had in his pocket the pilgrim’s flask

[045-169]of rum which he always carried on journeys exceeding a dozen miles,

[045-170]though he seldom drank much of it. He poured it down the surgeon’s

[045-171]throat, with such effect that he quickly revived. Melbury got him on

[045-172]his legs; but the question was what to do with him. He could not walk

[045-173]more than a few steps, and the other horse had gone away.

[045-174]With great exertion Melbury contrived to get him astride Darling,

[045-175]mounting himself behind, and holding Fitzpiers round his waist with one

[045-176]arm. Darling being broad, straight-backed, and high in the withers, was

[045-177]well able to carry double, at any rate as far as [[little-hintock|Hintock]], and at a

[045-178]gentle pace.
