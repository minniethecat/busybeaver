# Section 25

[025-001]Looking through the [[nature-in-the-woodlanders|trees]] [[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Fitzpiers]] soon perceived the origin of the

[025-002]noise. The [[timber-economy|barking]] season had just commenced, and what he had heard was

[025-003]the tear of the ripping tool as it ploughed its way along the sticky

[025-004]parting between the trunk and the rind. [[george-melbury|Melbury]] did a large business in

[025-005]bark, and as he was Grace’s father, and possibly might be found on the

[025-006]spot, Fitzpiers was attracted to the scene even more than he might have

[025-007]been by its intrinsic interest. When he got nearer he recognized among

[025-008]the workmen the two Timothys, and [[robert-creedle|Robert Creedle]], who probably had been

[025-009]“lent” by [[giles-winterborne|Winterborne]]; [[marty-south|Marty South]] also assisted.

[025-010]Each tree doomed to this flaying process was first attacked by Creedle.

[025-011]With a small billhook he carefully freed the collar of the tree from

[025-012]twigs and patches of moss which incrusted it to a height of a foot or

[025-013]two above the ground, an operation comparable to the “little toilet” of

[025-014]the executioner’s victim. After this it was barked in its erect

[025-015]position to a point as high as a man could reach. If a fine product of

[025-016]vegetable nature could ever be said to look ridiculous it was the case

[025-017]now, when the oak stood naked-legged, and as if ashamed, till the

[025-018]axe-man came and cut a ring round it, and the two Timothys finished the

[025-019]work with the crosscut-saw.

[025-020]As soon as it had fallen the barkers attacked it like locusts, and in a

[025-021]short time not a particle of rind was left on the trunk and larger

[025-022]limbs. Marty South was an adept at peeling the upper parts, and there

[025-023]she stood encaged amid the mass of twigs and buds like a great bird,

[025-024]running her tool into the smallest branches, beyond the farthest points

[025-025]to which the skill and patience of the men enabled them to

[025-026]proceed—branches which, in their lifetime, had swayed high above the

[025-027]bulk of the wood, and caught the latest and earliest rays of the sun

[025-028]and moon while the lower part of [[the-woodland-tracts|the forest]] was still in darkness.

[025-029]“You seem to have a better instrument than they, Marty,” said

[025-030]Fitzpiers.

[025-031]“No, sir,” she said, holding up the tool—a horse’s leg-bone fitted into

[025-032]a handle and filed to an edge—“’tis only that they’ve less patience

[025-033]with the twigs, because their time is worth more than mine.”

[025-034]A little shed had been constructed on the spot, of thatched hurdles and

[025-035]boughs, and in front of it was a fire, over which a kettle sung.

[025-036]Fitzpiers sat down inside the shelter, and went on with his reading,

[025-037]except when he looked up to observe the scene and the actors. The

[025-038]thought that he might settle here and become welded in with this sylvan

[025-039]life by marrying [[grace-melbury|Grace Melbury]] crossed his mind for a moment. Why

[025-040]should he go farther into the world than where he was? The [[secrecy-and-concealment|secret]] of

[025-041]quiet happiness lay in limiting the ideas and aspirations; these men’s

[025-042]thoughts were conterminous with the margin of the [[little-hintock|Hintock]] woodlands,

[025-043]and why should not his be likewise limited—a small practice among the

[025-044]people around him being the bound of his desires?

[025-045]Presently Marty South discontinued her operations upon the quivering

[025-046]boughs, came out from the reclining oak, and prepared tea. When it was

[025-047]ready the men were called; and Fitzpiers being in a mood to join, sat

[025-048]down with them.

[025-049]The latent reason of his lingering here so long revealed itself when

[025-050]the faint creaking of the joints of a vehicle became audible, and one

[025-051]of the men said, “Here’s he.” Turning their heads they saw Melbury’s

[025-052]gig approaching, the wheels muffled by the yielding moss.

[025-053]The timber-merchant was on foot leading the horse, looking back at

[025-054]every few steps to caution his daughter, who kept her seat, where and

[025-055]how to duck her head so as to avoid the overhanging branches. They

[025-056]stopped at the spot where the bark-ripping had been temporarily

[025-057]suspended; Melbury cursorily examined the heaps of bark, and drawing

[025-058]near to where the workmen were sitting down, accepted their shouted

[025-059]invitation to have a dish of tea, for which purpose he hitched the

[025-060]horse to a bough. Grace declined to take any of their beverage, and

[025-061]remained in her place in the vehicle, looking dreamily at the sunlight

[025-062]that came in thin threads through the hollies with which the oaks were

[025-063]interspersed.

[025-064]When Melbury stepped up close to the shelter, he for the first time

[025-065]perceived that the doctor was present, and warmly appreciated

[025-066]Fitzpiers’s invitation to sit down on the log beside him.

[025-067]“Bless my heart, who would have thought of finding you here,” he said,

[025-068]obviously much pleased at the circumstance. “I wonder now if my

[025-069]daughter knows you are so nigh at hand. I don’t expect she do.”

[025-070]He looked out towards the gig wherein Grace sat, her face still turned

[025-071]in the opposite direction. “She doesn’t see us. Well, never mind: let

[025-072]her be.”

[025-073]Grace was indeed quite unconscious of Fitzpiers’s propinquity. She was

[025-074]thinking of something which had little connection with the scene before

[025-075]her—thinking of her friend, lost as soon as found, [[felice-charmond|Mrs. Charmond]]; of

[025-076]her capricious conduct, and of the contrasting scenes she was possibly

[025-077]enjoying at that very moment in other climes, to which Grace herself

[025-078]had hoped to be introduced by her friend’s means. She wondered if this

[025-079]patronizing lady would return to Hintock during the summer, and whether

[025-080]the acquaintance which had been nipped on the last occasion of her

[025-081]residence there would develop on the next.

[025-082]Melbury told ancient timber-stories as he sat, relating them directly

[025-083]to Fitzpiers, and obliquely to the men, who had heard them often

[025-084]before. Marty, who poured out tea, was just saying, “I think I’ll take

[025-085]out a cup to Miss Grace,” when they heard a clashing of the

[025-086]gig-harness, and turning round Melbury saw that the horse had become

[025-087]restless, and was jerking about the vehicle in a way which alarmed its

[025-088]occupant, though she refrained from screaming. Melbury jumped up

[025-089]immediately, but not more quickly than Fitzpiers; and while her father

[025-090]ran to the horse’s head and speedily began to control him, Fitzpiers

[025-091]was alongside the gig assisting Grace to descend. Her surprise at his

[025-092]appearance was so great that, far from making a calm and independent

[025-093]descent, she was very nearly lifted down in his arms. He relinquished

[025-094]her when she touched ground, and hoped she was not frightened.

[025-095]“Oh no, not much,” she managed to say. “There was no danger—unless he

[025-096]had run under the trees where the boughs are low enough to hit my

[025-097]head.”

[025-098]“Which was by no means an impossibility, and justifies any amount of

[025-099]alarm.”

[025-100]He referred to what he thought he saw written in her face, and she

[025-101]could not tell him that this had little to do with the horse, but much

[025-102]with himself. His contiguity had, in fact, the same effect upon her as

[025-103]on those former occasions when he had come closer to her than

[025-104]usual—that of producing in her an unaccountable tendency to

[025-105]tearfulness. Melbury soon put the horse to rights, and seeing that

[025-106]Grace was safe, turned again to the work-people. His daughter’s nervous

[025-107]distress had passed off in a few moments, and she said quite gayly to

[025-108]Fitzpiers as she walked with him towards the group, “There’s [[fate-and-free-will|destiny]] in

[025-109]it, you see. I was doomed to join in your picnic, although I did not

[025-110]intend to do so.”

[025-111]Marty prepared her a comfortable place, and she sat down in the circle,

[025-112]and listened to Fitzpiers while he drew from her father and the

[025-113]bark-rippers sundry narratives of their fathers’, their grandfathers’,

[025-114]and their own adventures in these woods; of the mysterious sights they

[025-115]had seen—only to be accounted for by supernatural agency; of white

[025-116]witches and black witches; and the standard story of the spirits of the

[025-117]two brothers who had fought and fallen, and had haunted [[hintock-house|Hintock House]]

[025-118]till they were exorcised by the priest, and compelled to retreat to a

[025-119]swamp in this very wood, whence they were returning to their old

[025-120]quarters at the rate of a cock’s stride every New-year’s Day, old

[025-121]style; hence the local saying, “On New-year’s tide, a cock’s stride.”

[025-122]It was a pleasant time. The smoke from the little fire of peeled sticks

[025-123]rose between the sitters and the sunlight, and behind its blue veil

[025-124]stretched the naked arms of the prostrate trees. The smell of the

[025-125]uncovered sap mingled with the smell of the burning wood, and the

[025-126]sticky inner surface of the scattered bark glistened as it revealed its

[025-127]pale madder hues to the eye. Melbury was so highly satisfied at having

[025-128]Fitzpiers as a sort of guest that he would have sat on for any length

[025-129]of time, but Grace, on whom Fitzpiers’s eyes only too frequently

[025-130]alighted, seemed to think it incumbent upon her to make a show of

[025-131]going; and her father thereupon accompanied her to the vehicle.

[025-132]As the doctor had helped her out of it he appeared to think that he had

[025-133]excellent reasons for helping her in, and performed the attention

[025-134]lingeringly enough.

[025-135]“What were you almost in tears about just now?” he asked, softly.

[025-136]“I don’t know,” she said: and the words were strictly true.

[025-137]Melbury mounted on the other side, and they drove on out of the grove,

[025-138]their wheels silently crushing delicate-patterned mosses, hyacinths,

[025-139]primroses, lords-and-ladies, and other strange and ordinary plants, and

[025-140]cracking up little sticks that lay across the track. Their way homeward

[025-141]ran along the crest of a lofty hill, whence on the right they beheld a

[025-142]wide valley, differing both in feature and atmosphere from that of the

[025-143]Hintock precincts. It was the cider country, which met the woodland

[025-144]district on the axis of this hill. Over the vale the air was blue as

[025-145]sapphire—such a blue as outside that apple-valley was never seen. Under

[025-146]the blue the orchards were in a blaze of bloom, some of the richly

[025-147]flowered trees running almost up to where they drove along. Over a gate

[025-148]which opened down the incline a man leaned on his arms, regarding this

[025-149]fair promise so intently that he did not observe their passing.

[025-150]“That was Giles,” said Melbury, when they had gone by.

[025-151]“Was it? Poor Giles,” said she.

[025-152]“All that blooth means heavy autumn work for him and his hands. If no

[025-153]blight happens before the setting the apple yield will be such as we

[025-154]have not had for years.”

[025-155]Meanwhile, in the wood they had come from, the men had sat on so long

[025-156]that they were indisposed to begin work again that evening; they were

[025-157]paid by the ton, and their time for labor was as they chose. They

[025-158]placed the last gatherings of bark in rows for the curers, which led

[025-159]them farther and farther away from the shed; and thus they gradually

[025-160]withdrew as the sun went down.

[025-161]Fitzpiers lingered yet. He had opened his book again, though he could

[025-162]hardly see a word in it, and sat before the dying fire, scarcely

[025-163]knowing of the men’s departure. He dreamed and mused till his

[025-164]consciousness seemed to occupy the whole space of the woodland around,

[025-165]so little was there of jarring sight or sound to hinder perfect unity

[025-166]with the sentiment of the place. The idea returned upon him of

[025-167]sacrificing all practical aims to live in calm contentment here, and

[025-168]instead of going on elaborating new conceptions with infinite pains, to

[025-169]accept quiet domesticity according to oldest and homeliest notions.

[025-170]These reflections detained him till the wood was embrowned with the

[025-171]coming night, and the shy little bird of this dusky time had begun to

[025-172]pour out all the intensity of his eloquence from a bush not very far

[025-173]off.

[025-174]Fitzpiers’s eyes commanded as much of the ground in front as was open.

[025-175]Entering upon this he saw a figure, whose direction of movement was

[025-176]towards the spot where he sat. The surgeon was quite shrouded from

[025-177]observation by the recessed shadow of the hut, and there was no reason

[025-178]why he should move till the stranger had passed by. The shape resolved

[025-179]itself into a woman’s; she was looking on the ground, and walking

[025-180]slowly as if searching for something that had been lost, her course

[025-181]being precisely that of Mr. Melbury’s gig. Fitzpiers by a sort of

[025-182]divination jumped to the idea that the figure was Grace’s; her nearer

[025-183]approach made the guess a certainty.
