# Section 63

[063-001][[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Fitzpiers]] winced visibly; and yet, upon the whole, she was right in

[063-002]telling it. Indeed, his perception that she was right in her absolute

[063-003]sincerity kept up his affectionate admiration for her under the pain of

[063-004]the rebuff. Time had been when the avowal that Grace had deliberately

[063-005]taken steps to replace him would have brought him no sorrow. But she so

[063-006]far dominated him now that he could not bear to hear her words,

[063-007]although the object of her high regard was no more.

[063-008]“It is rough upon me—that!” he said, bitterly. “Oh, Grace—I did not

[063-009]know you—tried to get rid of me! I suppose it is of no use, but I ask,

[063-010]cannot you hope to—find a little [[love-and-desire|love]] in your heart for me again?”

[063-011]“If I could I would oblige you; but I fear I cannot!” she replied, with

[063-012]illogical ruefulness. “And I don’t see why you should mind my having

[063-013]had one lover besides yourself in my life, when you have had so many.”

[063-014]“But I can tell you honestly that I love you better than all of them

[063-015]put together, and that’s what you will not tell me!”

[063-016]“I am sorry; but I fear I cannot,” she said, sighing again.

[063-017]“I wonder if you ever will?” He looked musingly into her indistinct

[063-018]face, as if he would read the future there. “Now have pity, and tell

[063-019]me: will you try?”

[063-020]“To love you again?”

[063-021]“Yes; if you can.”

[063-022]“I don’t know how to reply,” she answered, her embarrassment proving

[063-023]her truth. “Will you promise to leave me quite free as to seeing you or

[063-024]not seeing you?”

[063-025]“Certainly. Have I given any ground for you to doubt my first promise

[063-026]in that respect?”

[063-027]She was obliged to admit that he had not.

[063-028]“Then I think that you might get your heart out of that grave,” said

[063-029]he, with playful sadness. “It has been there a long time.”

[063-030]She faintly shook her head, but said, “I’ll try to think of you more—if

[063-031]I can.”

[063-032]With this Fitzpiers was compelled to be satisfied, and he asked her

[063-033]when she would meet him again.

[063-034]“As we arranged—in a fortnight.”

[063-035]“If it must be a fortnight it must!”

[063-036]“This time at least. I’ll consider by the day I see you again if I can

[063-037]shorten the interval.”

[063-038]“Well, be that as it may, I shall come at least twice a week to look at

[063-039]your window.”

[063-040]“You must do as you like about that. Good-night.”

[063-041]“Say ‘husband.’”

[063-042]She seemed almost inclined to give him the word; but exclaiming, “No,

[063-043]no; I cannot,” slipped through the garden-hedge and disappeared.

[063-044]Fitzpiers did not exaggerate when he told her that he should haunt the

[063-045]precincts of the dwelling. But his persistence in this course did not

[063-046]result in his seeing her much oftener than at the fortnightly interval

[063-047]which she had herself marked out as proper. At these times, however,

[063-048]she punctually appeared, and as the spring wore on the meetings were

[063-049]kept up, though their character changed but little with the increase in

[063-050]their number.

[063-051]The small garden of the cottage occupied by the Tangs family—father,

[063-052]son, and now son’s wife—aligned with the larger one of the

[063-053][[timber-economy|timber]]-dealer at its upper end; and when young Tim, after leaving work

[063-054]at [[george-melbury|Melbury]]’s, stood at dusk in the little bower at the corner of his

[063-055]enclosure to smoke a pipe, he frequently observed the surgeon pass

[063-056]along the outside track before-mentioned. Fitzpiers always walked

[063-057]loiteringly, pensively, looking with a sharp eye into the gardens one

[063-058]after another as he proceeded; for Fitzpiers did not wish to leave the

[063-059]now absorbing spot too quickly, after travelling so far to reach it;

[063-060]hoping always for a glimpse of her whom he passionately desired to take

[063-061]to his arms anew.

[063-062]Now Tim began to be struck with these loitering progresses along the

[063-063]garden boundaries in the gloaming, and wondered what they boded. It

[063-064]was, naturally, quite out of his power to divine the singular,

[063-065]sentimental revival in Fitzpiers’s heart; the fineness of tissue which

[063-066]could take a deep, emotional—almost also an artistic—pleasure in being

[063-067]the yearning _innamorato_ of a woman he once had deserted, would have

[063-068]seemed an absurdity to the young sawyer. Mr. and [[grace-melbury|Mrs. Fitzpiers]] were

[063-069]separated; therefore the question of affection as between them was

[063-070]settled. But his [[suke-damson|Suke]] had, since that meeting on their [[marriage-in-the-woodlanders|marriage]]-day,

[063-071]repentantly admitted, to the urgency of his questioning, a good deal

[063-072]concerning her past levities. Putting all things together, he could

[063-073]hardly avoid connecting Fitzpiers’s mysterious visits to this spot with

[063-074]Suke’s residence under his roof. But he made himself fairly easy: the

[063-075]vessel in which they were about to emigrate sailed that month; and then

[063-076]Suke would be out of Fitzpiers’s way forever.

[063-077]The interval at last expired, and the eve of their departure arrived.

[063-078]They were pausing in the room of the cottage allotted to them by Tim’s

[063-079]father, after a busy day of preparation, which left them weary. In a

[063-080]corner stood their boxes, crammed and corded, their large case for the

[063-081]hold having already been sent away. The firelight shone upon Suke’s

[063-082]fine face and form as she stood looking into it, and upon the face of

[063-083]Tim seated in a corner, and upon the walls of his father’s house, which

[063-084]he was beholding that night almost for the last time.

[063-085]Tim Tangs was not happy. This scheme of emigration was dividing him

[063-086]from his father—for old Tangs would on no account leave [[little-hintock|Hintock]]—and had

[063-087]it not been for Suke’s reputation and his own [[pride-and-humiliation|dignity]], Tim would at the

[063-088]last moment have abandoned the project. As he sat in the back part of

[063-089]the room he regarded her moodily, and the fire and the boxes. One thing

[063-090]he had particularly noticed this evening—she was very restless; fitful

[063-091]in her actions, unable to remain seated, and in a marked degree

[063-092]depressed.

[063-093]“Sorry that you be going, after all, Suke?” he said.

[063-094]She sighed involuntarily. “I don’t know but that I be,” she answered.

[063-095]“’Tis natural, isn’t it, when one is going away?”

[063-096]“But you wasn’t born here as I was.”

[063-097]“No.”

[063-098]“There’s folk left behind that you’d fain have with ’ee, I reckon?”

[063-099]“Why do you think that?”

[063-100]“I’ve seen things and I’ve heard things; and, Suke, I say ’twill be a

[063-101]good move for me to get ’ee away. I don’t mind his leavings abroad, but

[063-102]I do mind ’em at home.”

[063-103]Suke’s face was not changed from its aspect of listless indifference by

[063-104]the words. She answered nothing; and shortly after he went out for his

[063-105]customary pipe of tobacco at the top of the garden.

[063-106]The restlessness of Suke had indeed owed its presence to the gentleman

[063-107]of Tim’s suspicions, but in a different—and it must be added in justice

[063-108]to her—more innocent sense than he supposed, judging from former

[063-109]doings. She had accidentally discovered that Fitzpiers was in the habit

[063-110]of coming secretly once or twice a week to Hintock, and knew that this

[063-111]evening was a favorite one of the seven for his journey. As she was

[063-112]going next day to leave the country, Suke thought there could be no

[063-113]great harm in giving way to a little sentimentality by obtaining a

[063-114]glimpse of him quite unknown to himself or to anybody, and thus taking

[063-115]a silent last farewell. Aware that Fitzpiers’s time for passing was at

[063-116]hand she thus betrayed her feeling. No sooner, therefore, had Tim left

[063-117]the room than she let herself noiselessly out of the house, and

[063-118]hastened to the corner of the garden, whence she could witness the

[063-119]surgeon’s transit across the scene—if he had not already gone by.

[063-120]Her light cotton dress was visible to Tim lounging in the arbor of the

[063-121]opposite corner, though he was [[secrecy-and-concealment|hidden]] from her. He saw her stealthily

[063-122]climb into the hedge, and so ensconce herself there that nobody could

[063-123]have the least doubt her purpose was to watch unseen for a passer-by.

[063-124]He went across to the spot and stood behind her. Suke started, having

[063-125]in her blundering way forgotten that he might be near. She at once

[063-126]descended from the hedge.

[063-127]“So he’s coming to-night,” said Tim, laconically. “And we be always

[063-128]anxious to see our dears.”

[063-129]“He _is_ coming to-night,” she replied, with defiance. “And we _be_

[063-130]anxious for our dears.”

[063-131]“Then will you step in-doors, where your dear will soon jine ’ee? We’ve

[063-132]to mouster by half-past three to-morrow, and if we don’t get to bed by

[063-133]eight at latest our faces will be as long as clock-cases all day.”

[063-134]She hesitated for a minute, but ultimately obeyed, going slowly down

[063-135]the garden to the house, where he heard the door-latch click behind

[063-136]her.

[063-137]Tim was incensed beyond measure. His marriage had so far been a total

[063-138]failure, a source of bitter regret; and the only course for improving

[063-139]his case, that of leaving the country, was a sorry, and possibly might

[063-140]not be a very effectual one. Do what he would, his domestic sky was

[063-141]likely to be overcast to the end of the day. Thus he brooded, and his

[063-142]resentment gathered force. He craved a means of striking one blow back

[063-143]at the cause of his cheerless plight, while he was still on the scene

[063-144]of his discomfiture. For some minutes no method suggested itself, and

[063-145]then he had an idea.

[063-146]Coming to a sudden resolution, he hastened along the garden, and

[063-147]entered the one attached to the next cottage, which had formerly been

[063-148]the dwelling of a game-keeper. Tim descended the path to the back of

[063-149]the house, where only an old woman lived at present, and reaching the

[063-150]wall he stopped. Owing to the slope of the ground the roof-eaves of the

[063-151]linhay were here within touch, and he thrust his arm up under them,

[063-152]feeling about in the space on the top of the wall-plate.

[063-153]“Ah, I thought my memory didn’t deceive me!” he lipped silently.

[063-154]With some exertion he drew down a cobwebbed object curiously framed in

[063-155]iron, which clanked as he moved it. It was about three feet in length

[063-156]and half as wide. Tim contemplated it as well as he could in the dying

[063-157]light of day, and raked off the cobwebs with his hand.

[063-158]“That will spoil his pretty shins for’n, I reckon!” he said.

[063-159]It was a [[the-man-trap-incident|man-trap]].

[063-160]CHAPTER XLVII.

[063-161]Were the inventors of automatic machines to be ranged according to the

[063-162]excellence of their devices for producing sound artistic torture, the

[063-163]creator of the man-trap would occupy a very respectable if not a very

[063-164]high place.

[063-165]It should rather, however, be said, the inventor of the particular form

[063-166]of man-trap of which this found in the keeper’s out-house was a

[063-167]specimen. For there were other shapes and other sizes, instruments

[063-168]which, if placed in a row beside one of the type disinterred by Tim,

[063-169]would have worn the subordinate aspect of the bears, wild boars, or

[063-170]wolves in a travelling menagerie, as compared with the leading lion or

[063-171]tiger. In short, though many varieties had been in use during those

[063-172]centuries which we are accustomed to look back upon as the true and

[063-173]only period of merry England—in the rural districts more especially—and

[063-174]onward down to the third decade of the nineteenth century, this model

[063-175]had borne the palm, and had been most usually followed when the

[063-176]orchards and estates required new ones.

[063-177]There had been the toothless variety used by the softer-hearted

[063-178]landlords—quite contemptible in their clemency. The jaws of these

[063-179]resembled the jaws of an old woman to whom time has left nothing but

[063-180]gums. There were also the intermediate or half-toothed sorts, probably

[063-181]devised by the middle-natured squires, or those under the influence of

[063-182]their wives: two inches of mercy, two inches of cruelty, two inches of

[063-183]mere nip, two inches of probe, and so on, through the whole extent of

[063-184]the jaws. There were also, as a class apart, the bruisers, which did

[063-185]not lacerate the flesh, but only crushed the bone.
