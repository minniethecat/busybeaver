# Section 37

[037-001]During the day she made some inquiries, and learned for the first time

[037-002]that [[felice-charmond|Mrs. Charmond]] was staying at [[middleton-abbey|Middleton Abbey]]. She could not resist

[037-003]an inference—strange as that inference was.

[037-004]A few days later he prepared to start again, at the same time and in

[037-005]the same direction. She knew that the state of the cottager who lived

[037-006]that way was a mere pretext; she was quite sure he was going to Mrs.

[037-007]Charmond. Grace was amazed at the mildness of the [[love-and-desire|passion]] which the

[037-008]suspicion engendered in her. She was but little excited, and her

[037-009][[jealousy-and-rivalry|jealousy]] was languid even to death. It told tales of the [[nature-in-the-woodlanders|nature]] of her

[037-010]affection for him. In truth, her antenuptial regard for [[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Fitzpiers]] had

[037-011]been rather of the quality of awe towards a superior being than of

[037-012]tender solicitude for a lover. It had been based upon mystery and

[037-013]strangeness—the mystery of his past, of his knowledge, of his

[037-014]professional skill, of his beliefs. When this structure of ideals was

[037-015]demolished by the intimacy of common life, and she found him as merely

[037-016]human as the [[little-hintock|Hintock]] people themselves, a new foundation was in demand

[037-017]for an enduring and stanch affection—a sympathetic interdependence,

[037-018]wherein mutual weaknesses were made the grounds of a defensive

[037-019]alliance. Fitzpiers had furnished none of that single-minded confidence

[037-020]and truth out of which alone such a second union could spring; hence it

[037-021]was with a controllable emotion that she now watched the mare brought

[037-022]round.

[037-023]“I’ll walk with you to the hill if you are not in a great hurry,” she

[037-024]said, rather loath, after all, to let him go.

[037-025]“Do; there’s plenty of time,” replied her husband. Accordingly he led

[037-026]along the horse, and walked beside her, impatient enough nevertheless.

[037-027]Thus they proceeded to the turnpike road, and ascended Rub-Down Hill to

[037-028]the gate he had been leaning over when she surprised him ten days

[037-029]before. This was the end of her excursion. Fitzpiers bade her adieu

[037-030]with affection, even with tenderness, and she observed that he looked

[037-031]weary-eyed.

[037-032]“Why do you go to-night?” she said. “You have been called up two nights

[037-033]in succession already.”

[037-034]“I must go,” he answered, almost gloomily. “Don’t wait up for me.” With

[037-035]these words he mounted his horse, passed through the gate which Grace

[037-036]held open for him, and ambled down the steep bridle-track to the

[037-037]valley.

[037-038]She closed the gate and watched his descent, and then his journey

[037-039]onward. His way was east, the evening sun which stood behind her back

[037-040]beaming full upon him as soon as he got out from the shade of the hill.

[037-041]Notwithstanding this untoward proceeding she was determined to be loyal

[037-042]if he proved true; and the determination to love one’s best will carry

[037-043]a heart a long way towards making that best an ever-growing thing. The

[037-044]conspicuous coat of the active though blanching mare made horse and

[037-045]rider easy objects for the vision. Though Darling had been chosen with

[037-046]such pains by [[giles-winterborne|Winterborne]] for Grace, she had never ridden the sleek

[037-047]creature; but her husband had found the animal exceedingly convenient,

[037-048]particularly now that he had taken to the saddle, plenty of staying

[037-049]power being left in Darling yet. Fitzpiers, like others of his

[037-050]character, while despising [[george-melbury|Melbury]] and his station, did not at all

[037-051]disdain to spend Melbury’s money, or appropriate to his own use the

[037-052]horse which belonged to Melbury’s daughter.

[037-053]And so the infatuated young surgeon went along through the gorgeous

[037-054]autumn landscape of [[white-hart-vale|White Hart Vale]], surrounded by orchards lustrous

[037-055]with the reds of apple-crops, berries, and foliage, the whole

[037-056]intensified by the gilding of the declining sun. The earth this year

[037-057]had been prodigally bountiful, and now was the supreme moment of her

[037-058]bounty. In the poorest spots the hedges were bowed with haws and

[037-059]blackberries; acorns cracked underfoot, and the burst husks of

[037-060]chestnuts lay exposing their auburn contents as if arranged by anxious

[037-061]sellers in a fruit-market. In all this proud show some kernels were

[037-062]unsound as her own situation, and she wondered if there were one world

[037-063]in the universe where the fruit had no worm, and [[marriage-in-the-woodlanders|marriage]] no sorrow.

[037-064]Herr Tannhäuser still moved on, his plodding steed rendering him

[037-065]distinctly visible yet. Could she have heard Fitzpiers’s voice at that

[037-066]moment she would have found him murmuring—

[037-067]“...Towards the loadstar of my one desire

[037-068]I flitted, even as a dizzy moth in the owlet light.”

[037-069]But he was a silent spectacle to her now. Soon he rose out of the

[037-070]valley, and skirted a high plateau of the chalk formation on his right,

[037-071]which rested abruptly upon the fruity district of loamy clay, the

[037-072]character and herbage of the two formations being so distinct that the

[037-073]calcareous upland appeared but as a deposit of a few years’ antiquity

[037-074]upon the level vale. He kept along the edge of this high, unenclosed

[037-075]country, and the sky behind him being deep violet, she could still see

[037-076]white Darling in relief upon it—a mere speck now—a Wouvermans

[037-077]eccentricity reduced to microscopic dimensions. Upon this high ground

[037-078]he gradually disappeared.

[037-079]Thus she had beheld the pet animal purchased for her own use, in pure

[037-080]love of her, by one who had always been true, impressed to convey her

[037-081]husband away from her to the side of a new-found idol. While she was

[037-082]musing on the vicissitudes of horses and wives, she discerned shapes

[037-083]moving up the valley towards her, quite near at hand, though till now

[037-084][[secrecy-and-concealment|hidden]] by the hedges. Surely they were Giles Winterborne, with his two

[037-085]horses and cider-apparatus, conducted by [[robert-creedle|Robert Creedle]]. Up, upward

[037-086]they crept, a stray beam of the sun alighting every now and then like a

[037-087]star on the blades of the pomace-shovels, which had been converted to

[037-088]steel mirrors by the action of the malic acid. She opened the gate when

[037-089]he came close, and the panting horses rested as they achieved the

[037-090]ascent.

[037-091]“How do you do, Giles?” said she, under a sudden impulse to be familiar

[037-092]with him.

[037-093]He replied with much more reserve. “You are going for a walk, Mrs.

[037-094]Fitzpiers?” he added. “It is pleasant just now.”

[037-095]“No, I am returning,” said she.

[037-096]The vehicles passed through, the gate slammed, and Winterborne walked

[037-097]by her side in the rear of the apple-mill.

[037-098]He looked and smelt like Autumn’s very brother, his face being sunburnt

[037-099]to wheat-color, his eyes blue as corn-flowers, his boots and leggings

[037-100]dyed with fruit-stains, his hands clammy with the sweet juice of

[037-101]apples, his hat sprinkled with pips, and everywhere about him that

[037-102]atmosphere of cider which at its first return each season has such an

[037-103]indescribable fascination for those who have been born and bred among

[037-104]the orchards. Her heart rose from its late sadness like a released

[037-105]spring; her senses revelled in the sudden lapse back to nature

[037-106]unadorned. The consciousness of having to be genteel because of her

[037-107]husband’s profession, the veneer of artificiality which she had

[037-108]acquired at the fashionable schools, were thrown off, and she became

[037-109]the crude, country girl of her latent, earliest instincts.

[037-110]Nature was bountiful, she thought. No sooner had she been starved off

[037-111]by Edgar Fitzpiers than another being, impersonating bare and undiluted

[037-112]manliness, had arisen out of the earth, ready to hand. This was an

[037-113]excursion of the imagination which she did not encourage, and she said

[037-114]suddenly, to disguise the confused regard which had followed her

[037-115]thoughts, “Did you meet my husband?”

[037-116]Winterborne, with some hesitation, “Yes.”

[037-117]“Where did you meet him?”

[037-118]“At Calfhay Cross. I come from Middleton Abbey; I have been making

[037-119]there for the last week.”

[037-120]“Haven’t they a mill of their own?”

[037-121]“Yes, but it’s out of repair.”

[037-122]“I think—I heard that Mrs. Charmond had gone there to stay?”

[037-123]“Yes. I have seen her at the windows once or twice.”

[037-124]Grace waited an interval before she went on: “Did Mr. Fitzpiers take

[037-125]the way to Middleton?”

[037-126]“Yes...I met him on Darling.” As she did not reply, he added, with a

[037-127]gentler inflection, “You know why the mare was called that?”

[037-128]“Oh yes—of course,” she answered, quickly.

[037-129]They had risen so far over the crest of the hill that the whole west

[037-130]sky was revealed. Between the broken clouds they could see far into the

[037-131]recesses of heaven, the eye journeying on under a species of golden

[037-132]arcades, and past fiery obstructions, fancied cairns, logan-stones,

[037-133]stalactites and stalagmite of topaz. Deeper than this their gaze passed

[037-134]thin flakes of incandescence, till it plunged into a bottomless medium

[037-135]of soft green fire.

[037-136]Her abandonment to the luscious time after her sense of ill-usage, her

[037-137]revolt for the nonce against social law, her passionate desire for

[037-138]primitive life, may have showed in her face. Winterborne was looking at

[037-139]her, his eyes lingering on a flower that she wore in her bosom. Almost

[037-140]with the abstraction of a somnambulist he stretched out his hand and

[037-141]gently caressed the flower.

[037-142]She drew back. “What are you doing, Giles Winterborne!” she exclaimed,

[037-143]with a look of severe surprise. The evident absence of all

[037-144]premeditation from the act, however, speedily led her to think that it

[037-145]was not necessary to stand upon her [[pride-and-humiliation|dignity]] here and now. “You must

[037-146]bear in mind, Giles,” she said, kindly, “that we are not as we were;

[037-147]and some people might have said that what you did was taking a

[037-148]liberty.”

[037-149]It was more than she need have told him; his action of forgetfulness

[037-150]had made him so angry with himself that he flushed through his tan. “I

[037-151]don’t know what I am coming to!” he exclaimed, savagely. “Ah—I was not

[037-152]once like this!” Tears of vexation were in his eyes.

[037-153]“No, now—it was nothing. I was too reproachful.”

[037-154]“It would not have occurred to me if I had not seen something like it

[037-155]done elsewhere—at Middleton lately,” he said, thoughtfully, after a

[037-156]while.

[037-157]“By whom?”

[037-158]“Don’t ask it.”

[037-159]She scanned him narrowly. “I know quite well enough,” she returned,

[037-160]indifferently. “It was by my husband, and the woman was Mrs. Charmond.

[037-161]Association of ideas reminded you when you saw me....Giles—tell me all

[037-162]you know about that—please do, Giles! But no—I won’t hear it. Let the

[037-163]subject cease. And as you are my friend, say nothing to my father.”

[037-164]They reached a place where their ways divided. Winterborne continued

[037-165]along the highway which kept outside the copse, and Grace opened a gate

[037-166]that entered it.

[037-167]CHAPTER XXIX.

[037-168]She walked up the soft grassy ride, screened on either hand by

[037-169]nut-bushes, just now heavy with clusters of twos and threes and fours.

[037-170]A little way on, the track she pursued was crossed by a similar one at

[037-171]right angles. Here Grace stopped; some few yards up the transverse ride

[037-172]the buxom [[suke-damson|Suke Damson]] was visible—her gown tucked up high through her

[037-173]pocket-hole, and no bonnet on her head—in the act of pulling down

[037-174]boughs from which she was gathering and eating nuts with great

[037-175]rapidity, her lover Tim Tangs standing near her engaged in the same

[037-176]pleasant meal.

[037-177]Crack, crack went Suke’s jaws every second or two. By an automatic

[037-178]chain of thought Grace’s mind reverted to the tooth-drawing scene

[037-179]described by her husband; and for the first time she wondered if that

[037-180]narrative were really true, Susan’s jaws being so obviously sound and

[037-181]strong. Grace turned up towards the nut-gatherers, and conquered her

[037-182]reluctance to speak to the girl who was a little in advance of Tim.

[037-183]“Good-evening, Susan,” she said.

[037-184]“Good-evening, [[grace-melbury|Miss Melbury]]” (crack).

[037-185]“Mrs. Fitzpiers.”

[037-186]“Oh yes, ma’am—Mrs. Fitzpiers,” said Suke, with a peculiar smile.

[037-187]Grace, not to be daunted, continued: “Take care of your teeth, Suke.

[037-188]That accounts for the toothache.”

[037-189]“I don’t know what an ache is, either in tooth, ear, or head, thank the

[037-190]Lord” (crack).

[037-191]“Nor the loss of one, either?”

[037-192]“See for yourself, ma’am.” She parted her red lips, and exhibited the

[037-193]whole double row, full up and unimpaired.

[037-194]“You have never had one drawn?”
