# Section 62

[062-001]“That’s out of _Measure for Measure_,” said she, slyly.

[062-002]“Oh yes—I meant it as a citation,” blandly replied [[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Fitzpiers]]. “Well,

[062-003]then, why not give me a very little bit of your heart again?”

[062-004]The crash of a felled tree in the remote depths of the wood recalled

[062-005]the past at that moment, and all the homely faithfulness of

[062-006][[giles-winterborne|Winterborne]]. “Don’t ask it! My heart is in the grave with Giles,” she

[062-007]replied, stanchly.

[062-008]“Mine is with you—in no less deep a grave, I fear, according to that.”

[062-009]“I am very sorry; but it cannot be helped.”

[062-010]“How can you be sorry for me, when you wilfully keep open the grave?”

[062-011]“Oh no—that’s not so,” returned Grace, quickly, and moved to go away

[062-012]from him.

[062-013]“But, dearest Grace,” said he, “you have condescended to come; and I

[062-014]thought from it that perhaps when I had passed through a long state of

[062-015]probation you would be generous. But if there can be no hope of our

[062-016]getting completely reconciled, treat me gently—wretch though I am.”

[062-017]“I did not say you were a wretch, nor have I ever said so.”

[062-018]“But you have such a contemptuous way of looking at me that I fear you

[062-019]think so.”

[062-020]Grace’s heart struggled between the wish not to be harsh and the fear

[062-021]that she might mislead him. “I cannot look contemptuous unless I feel

[062-022]contempt,” she said, evasively. “And all I feel is lovelessness.”

[062-023]“I have been very bad, I know,” he returned. “But unless you can really

[062-024][[love-and-desire|love]] me again, Grace, I would rather go away from you forever. I don’t

[062-025]want you to receive me again for duty’s sake, or anything of that sort.

[062-026]If I had not cared more for your affection and forgiveness than my own

[062-027]personal comfort, I should never have come back here. I could have

[062-028]obtained a practice at a distance, and have lived my own life without

[062-029]coldness or reproach. But I have chosen to return to the one spot on

[062-030]earth where my name is tarnished—to enter the house of a man from whom

[062-031]I have had worse treatment than from any other man alive—all for you!”

[062-032]This was undeniably true, and it had its weight with Grace, who began

[062-033]to look as if she thought she had been shockingly severe.

[062-034]“Before you go,” he continued, “I want to know your pleasure about

[062-035]me—what you wish me to do, or not to do.”

[062-036]“You are independent of me, and it seems a mockery to ask that. Far be

[062-037]it from me to advise. But I will think it over. I rather need advice

[062-038]myself than stand in a position to give it.”

[062-039]“_You_ don’t need advice, wisest, dearest woman that ever lived. If you

[062-040]did—”

[062-041]“Would you give it to me?”

[062-042]“Would you act upon what I gave?”

[062-043]“That’s not a fair inquiry,” said she, smiling despite her gravity. “I

[062-044]don’t mind hearing it—what you do really think the most correct and

[062-045]proper course for me.”

[062-046]“It is so easy for me to say, and yet I dare not, for it would be

[062-047]provoking you to remonstrances.”

[062-048]Knowing, of course, what the advice would be, she did not press him

[062-049]further, and was about to beckon [[marty-south|Marty]] forward and leave him, when he

[062-050]interrupted her with, “Oh, one moment, dear Grace—you will meet me

[062-051]again?”

[062-052]She eventually agreed to see him that day fortnight. Fitzpiers

[062-053]expostulated at the interval, but the half-alarmed earnestness with

[062-054]which she entreated him not to come sooner made him say hastily that he

[062-055]submitted to her will—that he would regard her as a friend only,

[062-056]anxious for his reform and well-being, till such time as she might

[062-057]allow him to exceed that privilege.

[062-058]All this was to assure her; it was only too clear that he had not won

[062-059]her confidence yet. It amazed Fitzpiers, and overthrew all his

[062-060]deductions from previous experience, to find that this girl, though she

[062-061]had been married to him, could yet be so coy. Notwithstanding a certain

[062-062]fascination that it carried with it, his reflections were sombre as he

[062-063]went homeward; he saw how deep had been his offence to produce so great

[062-064]a wariness in a gentle and once unsuspicious soul.

[062-065]He was himself too fastidious to care to coerce her. To be an object of

[062-066]misgiving or dislike to a woman who shared his home was what he could

[062-067]not endure the thought of. Life as it stood was more tolerable.

[062-068]When he was gone, Marty joined [[grace-melbury|Mrs. Fitzpiers]]. She would fain have

[062-069]consulted Marty on the question of Platonic relations with her former

[062-070]husband, as she preferred to regard him. But Marty showed no great

[062-071]interest in their affairs, so Grace said nothing. They came onward, and

[062-072]saw [[george-melbury|Melbury]] standing at the scene of the felling which had been audible

[062-073]to them, when, telling Marty that she wished her meeting with Mr.

[062-074]Fitzpiers to be kept private, she left the girl to join her father. At

[062-075]any rate, she would consult him on the expediency of occasionally

[062-076]seeing her husband.

[062-077]Her father was cheerful, and walked by her side as he had done in

[062-078]earlier days. “I was thinking of you when you came up,” he said. “I

[062-079]have considered that what has happened is for the best. Since your

[062-080]husband is gone away, and seems not to wish to trouble you, why, let

[062-081]him go, and drop out of your life. Many [[gender-roles|women]] are worse off. You can

[062-082]live here comfortably enough, and he can emigrate, or do what he likes

[062-083]for his good. I wouldn’t mind sending him the further sum of money he

[062-084]might naturally expect to come to him, so that you may not be bothered

[062-085]with him any more. He could hardly have gone on living here without

[062-086]speaking to me, or meeting me; and that would have been very unpleasant

[062-087]on both sides.”

[062-088]These remarks checked her intention. There was a sense of weakness in

[062-089]following them by saying that she had just met her husband by

[062-090]appointment. “Then you would advise me not to communicate with him?”

[062-091]she observed.

[062-092]“I shall never advise ye again. You are your own mistress—do as you

[062-093]like. But my opinion is that if you don’t live with him, you had better

[062-094]live without him, and not go shilly-shallying and playing bopeep. You

[062-095]sent him away; and now he’s gone. Very well; trouble him no more.”

[062-096]Grace felt a guiltiness—she hardly knew why—and made no confession.

[062-097]CHAPTER XLVI.

[062-098]The woods were uninteresting, and Grace stayed in-doors a great deal.

[062-099]She became quite a student, reading more than she had done since her

[062-100][[marriage-in-the-woodlanders|marriage]]. But her [[rural-isolation|seclusion]] was always broken for the periodical visit

[062-101]to Winterborne’s grave with Marty, which was kept up with pious

[062-102]strictness, for the purpose of putting snow-drops, primroses, and other

[062-103]vernal flowers thereon as they came.

[062-104]One afternoon at sunset she was standing just outside her father’s

[062-105]garden, which, like the rest of the [[little-hintock|Hintock]] enclosures, abutted into

[062-106]the wood. A slight foot-path led along here, forming a [[secrecy-and-concealment|secret]] way to

[062-107]either of the houses by getting through its boundary hedge. Grace was

[062-108]just about to adopt this mode of entry when a figure approached along

[062-109]the path, and held up his hand to detain her. It was her husband.

[062-110]“I am delighted,” he said, coming up out of breath; and there seemed no

[062-111]reason to doubt his words. “I saw you some way off—I was afraid you

[062-112]would go in before I could reach you.”

[062-113]“It is a week before the time,” said she, reproachfully. “I said a

[062-114]fortnight from the last meeting.”

[062-115]“My dear, you don’t suppose I could wait a fortnight without trying to

[062-116]get a glimpse of you, even though you had declined to meet me! Would it

[062-117]make you angry to know that I have been along this path at dusk three

[062-118]or four times since our last meeting? Well, how are you?”

[062-119]She did not refuse her hand, but when he showed a wish to retain it a

[062-120]moment longer than mere formality required, she made it smaller, so

[062-121]that it slipped away from him, with again that same alarmed look which

[062-122]always followed his attempts in this direction. He saw that she was not

[062-123]yet out of the elusive mood; not yet to be treated presumingly; and he

[062-124]was correspondingly careful to tranquillize her.

[062-125]His assertion had seemed to impress her somewhat. “I had no idea you

[062-126]came so often,” she said. “How far do you come from?”

[062-127]“From [[exbury|Exbury]]. I always walk from [[sherton-abbas|Sherton]]-Abbas, for if I hire, people

[062-128]will know that I come; and my success with you so far has not been

[062-129]great enough to justify such overtness. Now, my dear one—as I _must_

[062-130]call you—I put it to you: will you see me a little oftener as the

[062-131]spring advances?”

[062-132]Grace lapsed into unwonted sedateness, and avoiding the question, said,

[062-133]“I wish you would concentrate on your profession, and give up those

[062-134]strange studies that used to distract you so much. I am sure you would

[062-135]get on.”

[062-136]“It is the very thing I am doing. I was going to ask you to burn—or, at

[062-137]least, get rid of—all my philosophical literature. It is in the

[062-138]bookcases in your rooms. The fact is, I never cared much for abstruse

[062-139]studies.”

[062-140]“I am so glad to hear you say that. And those other books—those piles

[062-141]of old plays—what good are they to a medical man?”

[062-142]“None whatever!” he replied, cheerfully. “Sell them at Sherton for what

[062-143]they will fetch.”

[062-144]“And those dreadful old French romances, with their horrid spellings of

[062-145]‘filz’ and ‘ung’ and ‘ilz’ and ‘mary’ and ‘ma foy?’”

[062-146]“You haven’t been reading them, Grace?”

[062-147]“Oh no—I just looked into them, that was all.”

[062-148]“Make a bonfire of ’em directly you get home. I meant to do it myself.

[062-149]I can’t think what possessed me ever to collect them. I have only a few

[062-150]professional hand-books now, and am quite a practical man. I am in

[062-151]hopes of having some good news to tell you soon, and then do you think

[062-152]you could—come to me again?”

[062-153]“I would rather you did not press me on that just now,” she replied,

[062-154]with some feeling. “You have said you mean to lead a new, useful,

[062-155]effectual life; but I should like to see you put it in practice for a

[062-156]little while before you address that query to me. Besides—I could not

[062-157]live with you.”

[062-158]“Why not?”

[062-159]Grace was silent a few instants. “I go with Marty to Giles’s grave. We

[062-160]swore we would show him that devotion. And I mean to keep it up.”

[062-161]“Well, I wouldn’t mind that at all. I have no right to expect anything

[062-162]else, and I will not wish you to keep away. I liked the man as well as

[062-163]any I ever knew. In short, I would accompany you a part of the way to

[062-164]the place, and smoke a cigar on the stile while I waited till you came

[062-165]back.”

[062-166]“Then you haven’t given up smoking?”

[062-167]“Well—ahem—no. I have thought of doing so, but—”

[062-168]His extreme complacence had rather disconcerted Grace, and the question

[062-169]about smoking had been to effect a diversion. Presently she said,

[062-170]firmly, and with a moisture in her eye that he could not see, as her

[062-171]mind returned to poor Giles’s “frustrate ghost,” “I don’t like you—to

[062-172]speak lightly on that subject, if you did speak lightly. To be frank

[062-173]with you—quite frank—I think of him as my betrothed lover still. I

[062-174]cannot help it. So that it would be wrong for me to join you.”

[062-175]Fitzpiers was now uneasy. “You say your betrothed lover still,” he

[062-176]rejoined. “When, then, were you betrothed to him, or engaged, as we

[062-177]common people say?”

[062-178]“When you were away.”

[062-179]“How could that be?”

[062-180]Grace would have avoided this; but her natural candor led her on. “It

[062-181]was when I was under the impression that my marriage with you was about

[062-182]to be annulled, and that he could then marry me. So I encouraged him to

[062-183]love me.”
