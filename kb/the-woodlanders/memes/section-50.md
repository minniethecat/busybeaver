# Section 50

[050-001]“And you’d better write also to the gentleman,” suggested [[fred-beaucock|Beaucock]],

[050-002]who, scenting notoriety and the germ of a large practice in the case,

[050-003]wished to commit [[george-melbury|Melbury]] to it irretrievably; to effect which he knew

[050-004]that nothing would be so potent as awakening the [[love-and-desire|passion]] of Grace for

[050-005][[giles-winterborne|Winterborne]], so that her father might not have the heart to withdraw

[050-006]from his attempt to make her love legitimate when he discovered that

[050-007]there were difficulties in the way.

[050-008]The nervous, impatient Melbury was much pleased with the idea of

[050-009]“starting them at once,” as he called it. To put his long-delayed

[050-010]reparative scheme in train had become a passion with him now. He added

[050-011]to the letter addressed to his daughter a passage hinting that she

[050-012]ought to begin to encourage Winterborne, lest she should lose him

[050-013]altogether; and he wrote to Giles that the path was virtually open for

[050-014]him at last. Life was short, he declared; there were slips betwixt the

[050-015]cup and the lip; her interest in him should be reawakened at once, that

[050-016]all might be ready when the good time came for uniting them.

[050-017]CHAPTER XXXVIII.

[050-018]At these warm words Winterborne was not less dazed than he was moved in

[050-019]heart. The novelty of the avowal rendered what it carried with it

[050-020]inapprehensible by him in its entirety.

[050-021]Only a few short months ago completely estranged from this

[050-022]family—beholding Grace going to and fro in the distance, clothed with

[050-023]the alienating radiance of obvious superiority, the wife of the then

[050-024]popular and fashionable [[dr-edred-fitzpiers|Fitzpiers]], hopelessly outside his social

[050-025]boundary down to so recent a time that flowers then folded were hardly

[050-026]faded yet—he was now asked by that jealously guarding father of hers to

[050-027]take courage—to get himself ready for the day when he should be able to

[050-028]claim her.

[050-029]The old times came back to him in dim procession. How he had been

[050-030]snubbed; how Melbury had despised his Christmas party; how that sweet,

[050-031]coy Grace herself had looked down upon him and his household

[050-032]arrangements, and poor [[robert-creedle|Creedle]]’s contrivances!

[050-033]Well, he could not believe it. Surely the adamantine barrier of

[050-034][[marriage-in-the-woodlanders|marriage]] with another could not be pierced like this! It did violence

[050-035]to custom. Yet a new law might do anything. But was it at all within

[050-036]the bounds of probability that a woman who, over and above her own

[050-037]attainments, had been accustomed to those of a cultivated professional

[050-038]man, could ever be the wife of such as he?

[050-039]Since the date of his rejection he had almost grown to see the

[050-040]reasonableness of that treatment. He had said to himself again and

[050-041]again that her father was right; that the poor ceorl, Giles

[050-042]Winterborne, would never have been able to make such a dainty girl

[050-043]happy. Yet, now that she had stood in a position farther removed from

[050-044]his own than at first, he was asked to prepare to woo her. He was full

[050-045]of doubt.

[050-046]Nevertheless, it was not in him to show backwardness. To act so

[050-047]promptly as Melbury desired him to act seemed, indeed, scarcely wise,

[050-048]because of the uncertainty of events. Giles knew nothing of legal

[050-049]procedure, but he did know that for him to step up to Grace as a lover

[050-050]before the bond which bound her was actually dissolved was simply an

[050-051]extravagant dream of her father’s overstrained mind. He pitied Melbury

[050-052]for his almost childish enthusiasm, and saw that the aging man must

[050-053]have suffered acutely to be weakened to this unreasoning desire.

[050-054]Winterborne was far too magnanimous to harbor any cynical conjecture

[050-055]that the [[timber-economy|timber]]-merchant, in his intense affection for Grace, was

[050-056]courting him now because that young lady, when disunited, would be left

[050-057]in an anomalous position, to escape which a bad husband was better than

[050-058]none. He felt quite sure that his old friend was simply on tenterhooks

[050-059]of anxiety to repair the almost irreparable error of dividing two whom

[050-060]Nature had striven to join together in earlier days, and that in his

[050-061]ardor to do this he was oblivious of formalities. The cautious

[050-062]supervision of his past years had overleaped itself at last. Hence,

[050-063]Winterborne perceived that, in this new beginning, the necessary care

[050-064]not to compromise Grace by too early advances must be exercised by

[050-065]himself.

[050-066]Perhaps Winterborne was not quite so ardent as heretofore. There is no

[050-067]such thing as a stationary love: men are either loving more or loving

[050-068]less. But Giles himself recognized no decline in his sense of her

[050-069]dearness. If the flame did indeed burn lower now than when he had

[050-070]fetched her from [[sherton-abbas|Sherton]] at her last return from school, the marvel was

[050-071]small. He had been laboring ever since his rejection and her marriage

[050-072]to reduce his former passion to a docile friendship, out of pure regard

[050-073]to its expediency; and their separation may have helped him to a

[050-074]partial success.

[050-075]A week and more passed, and there was no further news of Melbury. But

[050-076]the effect of the intelligence he had already transmitted upon the

[050-077]elastic-nerved daughter of the woods had been much what the old surgeon

[050-078]Jones had surmised. It had soothed her perturbed spirit better than all

[050-079]the opiates in the pharmacopoeia. She had slept unbrokenly a whole

[050-080]night and a day. The “new law” was to her a mysterious, beneficent,

[050-081]godlike entity, lately descended upon earth, that would make her as she

[050-082]once had been without trouble or annoyance. Her position fretted her,

[050-083]its abstract features rousing an aversion which was even greater than

[050-084]her aversion to the personality of him who had caused it. It was

[050-085]mortifying, productive of slights, undignified. Him she could forget;

[050-086]her circumstances she had always with her.

[050-087]She saw nothing of Winterborne during the days of her recovery; and

[050-088]perhaps on that account her fancy wove about him a more romantic tissue

[050-089]than it could have done if he had stood before her with all the specks

[050-090]and flaws inseparable from corporeity. He rose upon her memory as the

[050-091]fruit-god and the wood-god in alternation; sometimes leafy, and smeared

[050-092]with green lichen, as she had seen him among the sappy boughs of the

[050-093]plantations; sometimes cider-stained, and with apple-pips in the hair

[050-094]of his arms, as she had met him on his return from [[orchard-culture|cider-making]] in

[050-095][[white-hart-vale|White Hart Vale]], with his vats and presses beside him. In her [[secrecy-and-concealment|secret]]

[050-096]heart she almost approximated to her father’s enthusiasm in wishing to

[050-097]show Giles once for all how she still regarded him. The question

[050-098]whether the future would indeed bring them together for life was a

[050-099]standing wonder with her. She knew that it could not with any propriety

[050-100]do so just yet. But reverently believing in her father’s sound judgment

[050-101]and knowledge, as good girls are wont to do, she remembered what he had

[050-102]written about her giving a hint to Winterborne lest there should be

[050-103]risk in delay, and her feelings were not averse to such a step, so far

[050-104]as it could be done without danger at this early stage of the

[050-105]proceedings.

[050-106]From being a frail phantom of her former equable self she returned in

[050-107]bounds to a condition of passable philosophy. She bloomed again in the

[050-108]face in the course of a few days, and was well enough to go about as

[050-109]usual. One day [[mrs-melbury|Mrs. Melbury]] proposed that for a change she should be

[050-110]driven in the gig to Sherton market, whither Melbury’s man was going on

[050-111]other errands. Grace had no business whatever in Sherton; but it

[050-112]crossed her mind that Winterborne would probably be there, and this

[050-113]made the thought of such a drive interesting.

[050-114]On the way she saw nothing of him; but when the horse was walking

[050-115]slowly through the obstructions of Sheep Street, she discerned the

[050-116]young man on the pavement. She thought of that time when he had been

[050-117]standing under his apple-tree on her return from school, and of the

[050-118]tender opportunity then missed through her fastidiousness. Her heart

[050-119]rose in her throat. She abjured all such fastidiousness now. Nor did

[050-120]she forget the last occasion on which she had beheld him in that town,

[050-121]making cider in the court-yard of the [[earl-of-wessex-hotel|Earl of Wessex Hotel]], while she

[050-122]was figuring as a fine lady in the balcony above.

[050-123]Grace directed the man to set her down there in the midst, and

[050-124]immediately went up to her lover. Giles had not before observed her,

[050-125]and his eyes now suppressedly looked his pleasure, without the

[050-126]embarrassment that had formerly marked him at such meetings.

[050-127]When a few words had been spoken, she said, archly, “I have nothing to

[050-128]do. Perhaps you are deeply engaged?”

[050-129]“I? Not a bit. My business now at the best of times is small, I am

[050-130]sorry to say.”

[050-131]“Well, then, I am going into the Abbey. Come along with me.”

[050-132]The proposition had suggested itself as a quick escape from publicity,

[050-133]for many eyes were regarding her. She had hoped that sufficient time

[050-134]had elapsed for the extinction of curiosity; but it was quite

[050-135]otherwise. The people looked at her with tender interest as the

[050-136]deserted girl-wife—without obtrusiveness, and without vulgarity; but

[050-137]she was ill prepared for scrutiny in any shape.

[050-138]They walked about the Abbey aisles, and presently sat down. Not a soul

[050-139]was in the building save themselves. She regarded a stained window,

[050-140]with her head sideways, and tentatively asked him if he remembered the

[050-141]last time they were in that town alone.

[050-142]He remembered it perfectly, and remarked, “You were a proud miss then,

[050-143]and as dainty as you were high. Perhaps you are now?”

[050-144]Grace slowly shook her head. “Affliction has taken all that out of me,”

[050-145]she answered, impressively. “Perhaps I am too far the other way now.”

[050-146]As there was something lurking in this that she could not explain, she

[050-147]added, so quickly as not to allow him time to think of it, “Has my

[050-148]father written to you at all?”

[050-149]“Yes,” said Winterborne.

[050-150]She glanced ponderingly up at him. “Not about me?”

[050-151]“Yes.”

[050-152]His mouth was lined with charactery which told her that he had been

[050-153]bidden to take the hint as to the future which she had been bidden to

[050-154]give. The unexpected discovery sent a scarlet pulsation through Grace

[050-155]for the moment. However, it was only Giles who stood there, of whom she

[050-156]had no fear; and her self-possession returned.

[050-157]“He said I was to sound you with a view to—what you will understand, if

[050-158]you care to,” continued Winterborne, in a low voice. Having been put on

[050-159]this track by herself, he was not disposed to abandon it in a hurry.

[050-160]They had been children together, and there was between them that

[050-161]familiarity as to personal affairs which only such acquaintanceship can

[050-162]give. “You know, Giles,” she answered, speaking in a very practical

[050-163]tone, “that that is all very well; but I am in a very anomalous

[050-164]position at present, and I cannot say anything to the point about such

[050-165]things as those.”

[050-166]“No?” he said, with a stray air as regarded the subject. He was looking

[050-167]at her with a curious consciousness of discovery. He had not been

[050-168]imagining that their renewed intercourse would show her to him thus.

[050-169]For the first time he realized an unexpectedness in her, which, after

[050-170]all, should not have been unexpected. She before him was not the girl

[050-171][[grace-melbury|Grace Melbury]] whom he used to know. Of course, he might easily have

[050-172]prefigured as much; but it had never occurred to him. She was a woman

[050-173]who had been married; she had moved on; and without having lost her

[050-174]girlish modesty, she had lost her girlish shyness. The inevitable

[050-175]change, though known to him, had not been heeded; and it struck him

[050-176]into a momentary fixity. The truth was that he had never come into

[050-177]close comradeship with her since her engagement to Fitzpiers, with the

[050-178]brief exception of the evening encounter on [[rubdown-hill|Rubdown Hill]], when she met

[050-179]him with his cider apparatus; and that interview had been of too

[050-180]cursory a kind for insight.
