# Hintock House

Hintock House stands at the apex of Little Hintock's physical and social geography — the seat of the landed gentry that presides over the woodland community. As the residence of Mrs. Felice Charmond, it represents everything that the novel's other characters aspire to and are excluded from: wealth without labor, status without effort, the freedom that comes from property.

## Architecture and Grounds

Hardy describes Hintock House in terms that emphasize its separation from the village. It is approached through its own grounds, set apart from the working woodland that surrounds it. From the house, "the orchard" could be seen on one side, and "behind the orchard the forest-trees, reaching up" to the sky (020-194–195). The house commands a view of the landscape without participating in its labor — a spatial expression of the gentry's relationship to the productive economy.

The house is old enough to have accumulated the patina of generations but not so ancient as to be ruinous. It represents settled wealth rather than new money — the kind of property that is inherited rather than earned, and that confers upon its owner a status that no amount of timber-trading could purchase.

## The Seat of Gentry Power

Mrs. Charmond's ownership of Hintock House places her at the top of the novel's class hierarchy. She does not work; her wealth comes from land rather than labor. Her house is the destination toward which Grace Melbury's education aspires and from which Fitzpiers's professional status provides only partial access. The house is both a physical structure and a symbol — of the class boundary that Grace can never quite cross.

## Hintock House and the Community

The house's physical separation from the village mirrors its social separation. The inhabitants of Little Hintock speak of Hintock House with a mixture of deference and resentment. It is the source of local employment and the object of local envy; it shapes the community's economic life while remaining aloof from its daily rhythms.
