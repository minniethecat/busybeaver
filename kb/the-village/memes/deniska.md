# Deniska

Deniska is the son of Syery (the Grey Man) and a young cobbler of Durnovka — a figure who embodies the generation born after emancipation, the first Russians who never knew serfdom and who, in Bunin's rendering, are in no way improved by their freedom. If the Krasoff brothers embody the tragedy of the Russian character, Deniska embodies its degeneration: he is energetic, cunning, morally vacant, and entirely without awareness that anything might be wrong with him.

## Biography / Role in the Novel

Deniska appears throughout the novel's middle chapters as a figure of unchecked impulse and casual depravity. He works intermittently as a cobbler, drifts through the village with his cheap grey valise, and accumulates a collection of lurid songbooks and pamphlets that Tikhon finds both contemptible and indicative of a new, unsettling type of peasant (020-021).

His most striking appearance is at the railway station, where Tikhon Ilitch encounters him holding a "cheap grey valise, lavishly studded with tin nailheads and bound about with a rope" (020-007). Deniska, with his "immeasurably thick" mouse-coloured hair, earthy face, and languid dark eyes, is physically unprepossessing — his legs are "extremely short in comparison with his body," a disproportion he acknowledges with self-mocking detachment: "I have nothing but a body" (020-007).

The books in his pocket reveal his mental world. Tikhon examines them one by one: "There was the songbook *Marusya*, *The Woman Debauchee*, *An Innocent Young Girl in the Clutches of Violence*, *Congratulatory Verses to Parents, Teachers, and Benefactors*, *The Role —*" — at which point Deniska "briskly and modestly" supplies the title: "*The Role of the Proletariat in Russia*" (020-007). The juxtaposition of pornography, sentimentality, and revolutionary politics is Bunin's sardonic portrait of the peasant intellectual in embryo.

Kuzma tells a story about Deniska that reveals a deeper depravity. During a famine year, Deniska worked as a foreman in the Black Suburb and observed the starving prostitutes near the cemetery: "If you gave one of them half a pound of bread for her work she'd devour it to the last crumb, there under you." His response to this scene of degradation is not pity or horror but amusement: "It was downright ridiculous!" (013-008). Kuzma, recounting this, is visibly shaken — not by the story itself, which he has heard before, but by Deniska's apparently sincere inability to recognize human degradation when he causes it.

## Key Passages

> "Deniska, his head bowed in thought, was standing on the platform and holding in his right hand a cheap grey valise, lavishly studded with tin nailheads and bound about with a rope. His figure was badly built; his legs were extremely short in comparison with his body: 'I have nothing but a body,' he sometimes said of himself, with a laugh."
> — (020-007)

> "There was the songbook *Marusya*, *The Woman Debauchee*, *An Innocent Young Girl in the Clutches of Violence*, *Congratulatory Verses to Parents, Teachers, and Benefactors*, *The Role —* 'The Role of the Proletariat in Russia,' briskly and modestly prompted Deniska."
> — (020-022)

And they were hungry, the lean hags, extremely hungry! If you gave one of them half a pound of bread for her work she'd devour it to the last crumb, there under you. It was downright ridiculous!'"
> — (013-008)
