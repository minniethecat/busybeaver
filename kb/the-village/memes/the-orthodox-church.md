# The Orthodox Church

The Russian Orthodox Church is the institutional and spiritual backdrop of rural life in *The Village*. Its presence is registered through churches, priests, pilgrimages, feast days, almsgiving, and the ubiquitous holy icons that hang in peasant cottages and merchant houses alike.

But Bunin's portrayal of the Church is characteristically ambivalent. The institution provides the rhythms that structure village existence — the fasts, the festivals, the rites of baptism and burial — yet its spiritual authority seems hollow, its consolations insufficient against the weight of material misery.

Characters cross themselves, consult the "Oracle," give alms to pilgrims, and speak of God, but their faith does not transform them. The Church is everywhere and nowhere — a framework of meaning that has ceased to mean.

## Role in the Novel

The Church in *The Village* is less a source of moral teaching than a set of practices and spaces that characters inhabit without evident conviction. Tikhon Ilitch gives generously to churches and monasteries — a ten-ruble banknote to a pilgrim, contributions to the cathedral — but his piety is transactional: a payment against the spiritual debt he senses but cannot name.

He consults the "Oracle," a book of divination that combines folk superstition with garbled religious imagery, and is disturbed by its pronouncements. His faith, such as it is, belongs to the world of bargains and settlements that defines his commercial life.

> But as he went he was thinking in his heart that God would certainly chastise him because, in the bustle and cares of life, he went to church only for the service on Easter Day, and otherwise lived as if he were a Tatar.
> — (003-015)

Kuzma, the intellectual, occupies a more complex position. He is skeptical of organized religion — when asked if he goes to church, he grins and deflects — yet he cannot free himself from the Christian vocabulary of sin, judgment, and redemption. His despair at the Russian condition is, at bottom, a religious despair: if the people are fallen, what can redeem them? The novel provides no answer.

The minor characters fill out the spectrum of rural religiosity. The Pilgrim and Ivanushka represent the tradition of wandering holy men and women, figures of ambiguous sanctity who are respected, fed, and kept at a safe distance.

Their presence in the novel suggests the persistence of an older, pre-institutional Christianity — folk religion, pilgrimage, the charisma of the holy fool — that coexists uneasily with the official Church.

## Historical Background

The Russian Orthodox Church at the turn of the twentieth century was an institution in crisis. Since the reforms of Peter the Great, it had been governed not by a patriarch but by the Holy Synod, a body of bishops appointed by and subordinate to the Tsar. The Church was, in effect, a department of state — a status that compromised its spiritual authority and made it a target of radical critique.

The clergy were divided between the "black clergy" (monastics, who held the senior ranks of the hierarchy) and the "white clergy" (parish priests, who were typically married, poorly paid, and socially isolated).

The village priest — often the son of a priest, trapped in a hereditary clerical estate — occupied an ambiguous position: educated enough to be set apart from the peasants, poor enough to share their material conditions, and expected to serve as both spiritual counselor and agent of state surveillance (reporting on seditious activity in his parish).

By the time of *The Village*'s publication, the Church faced challenges from multiple directions: sectarian movements among the peasantry, the secularizing influence of socialist and liberal ideas, and an internal reform movement that sought to restore the patriarchate and liberate the Church from state control.

None of these currents appears directly in the novel, but their effects — the diminished authority of the parish priest, the coexistence of Orthodoxy with folk superstition, the gap between ritual observance and moral transformation — are everywhere visible in Bunin's rural world.

## Key Passages

> "Before the holy picture a simple peasant woman in a short cotton petticoat was kneeling, crossing herself fervently, her worn bast-slippers showing from under her skirt."
> — [003-002]

The peasant woman at prayer is one of the novel's few images of uncomplicated piety. The detail is precise and respectful: the "worn bast-slippers," the "fervent" crossing — Bunin records the gesture without irony. But the passage's context — Tikhon's restless, unfocused observation — suggests that this simple faith is not available to him.

> "Tikhon Ilitch consulted the 'Oracle' — a book of divination and prophecy — and was perturbed by what he read there."
> — [003-004]

The "Oracle" is the novel's image of popular religion in its most syncretic form: part Christian eschatology, part folk superstition, part commercial publishing. Tikhon's consultation of it — and his genuine perturbation — reveals a man for whom the boundaries between faith, magic, and anxiety have dissolved.

> "A sort of molokan," grinned Kuzma. "And do you go to church? If you don't go to church, you're a worse infidel than any molokan."
> — [011-028]

This exchange, from the brothers' reunion, captures Kuzma's religious position: too skeptical for Orthodoxy, too culturally Russian for secularism, too intelligent for superstition, and too honest to pretend otherwise. The grin is his defense against questions he cannot answer.

> "Peace be unto you, and tranquillity, Most God-loving Benefactor, Tikhon Ilitch!"
> — [003-016]

The pilgrim's greeting to Tikhon is formulaic — the language of the Church deployed as social lubricant. The gap between the sacred words and the transaction they facilitate (Tikhon sends a ten-ruble banknote) is Bunin's implicit comment on the Church's role in rural society: it sanctifies the existing order without challenging it.

> A church of crude hue reared itself starkly, harshly on the bare common. Beyond the church a tiny clayey pond gleamed in the sunlight below a dam of manure, a sheet of thick yellow water in which stood a herd of cows, incessantly discharging according to the demands of nature; and there a naked peasant was soaping his head.
> — (006-006)

The physical church in the novel is often a stark, unlovely presence — an architectural fact set amid squalor rather than a sanctified space set apart from it. The proximity of the church to the manure dam and the naked peasant captures Bunin's insistence on placing the sacred squarely within the profane materiality of village life.

> How brief, how devoid of meaning, was life! And what peace, what repose, was round about, in that sunny stillness within the enclosure of the ancient churchyard!
> — (005-011)

Tikhon Ilitch's walk through the ancient churchyard occasions one of the novel's few moments of existential reflection. The churchyard — the physical space of the Church's dominion over death — provides a setting for contemplation, yet Tikhon's thoughts lead not to faith but to a sense of life's meaninglessness.
