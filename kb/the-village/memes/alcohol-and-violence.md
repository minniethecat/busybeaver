# Alcohol and Violence

Drink and brutality are the twin demons of Bunin's rural world. Every chapter of *The Village* contains scenes of drinking; every relationship is shadowed by the threat of violence. But Bunin does not moralize. He presents alcohol and violence not as individual vices but as collective symptoms — the outward expression of a social body that has been degraded by centuries of servitude and decades of neglect. The Krasoff brothers themselves nearly kill each other in a drunken dispute over their peddling profits, and this fraternal violence is the novel's founding trauma.

## The Concept in the Novel

Alcohol in *The Village* is ubiquitous. The Krasoff father "took to drink" and died. Tikhon and Kuzma "came near cutting each other's throats — in a dispute over the division of the profits" and parted ways for years. Peasants drink at fairs, in pothouses, at home, alone and in company. Vodka is the solvent of inhibitions, the accelerant of violence, and the only available anesthetic for lives of unrelieved hardship.

Bunin's treatment of alcohol is ethnographic rather than didactic. He shows how drinking is woven into the fabric of rural life — the rituals of hospitality ("Your vodka is strong, Petrovna!"), the economics of the pothouse (the Krasoff father's failed venture), the gender dynamics (Nastasya Petrovna, the perpetually pregnant, vodka-pouring hostess). Drinking is not a deviation from normal life; it is normal life's constant companion.

Violence, too, is ordinary. The novel opens with an act of lethal violence — the great-grandfather hunted with wolfhounds — and proceeds through a catalogue of beatings, knife fights, and casual cruelty. Kuzma, the novel's moral center, is himself capable of sudden rage. The violence is not sensationalized; it is presented as a fact of rural existence, as predictable as the harvest and as inexplicable as the Russian soul.

## Historical Context

Alcohol consumption in late imperial Russia reached extraordinary levels. The state vodka monopoly, introduced in 1894–1902, made the government the largest purveyor of spirits in the empire and generated roughly one-quarter of all state revenue. Per capita consumption of vodka in Russia was among the highest in Europe, and alcohol-related mortality — from cirrhosis, alcohol poisoning, and violence — was correspondingly elevated.

The temperance movement gained momentum after 1900, but it was largely an urban, middle-class phenomenon that had little impact on the villages. For the peasantry, vodka was not merely a beverage but a social institution: it sealed bargains, celebrated festivals, mediated disputes, and provided the only reliable relief from the miseries of rural life. Bunin understood this, and his depiction of drinking reflects this understanding: he condemns neither the drinkers nor the drink, only the world that makes drinking necessary.

## Key Passages

> "One day, after having thus travelled about for the space of several years, the brothers came near cutting each other's throats — in a dispute over the division of the profits — and parted, out of harm's way."
> — (001-004)

The fratricidal impulse is the novel's primal scene of violence. Tikhon and Kuzma, bound by blood and shared poverty, are driven to mutual murder by the very commerce that was supposed to liberate them. The phrase "out of harm's way" is bitterly ironic: separation is the only safety they can offer each other.

> "'U‑ukh! Your vodka is strong, Petrovna! It has knocked me in the head, devil take it!'"
> — (002-006)

A moment of convivial drinking that captures the ambiguous social role of vodka. The exclamation is half complaint, half compliment. Petrovna's strong vodka is a matter of local pride, and the drinking scene is, on its surface, one of the novel's few moments of unforced social warmth. But the violence is latent: "devil take it" is a joke, but in this novel, devils are always listening.

> "The father of the Krasoffs was a petty huckster. He roved about the county, lived for a time in Durnovka, set up a pothouse and a little shop, failed, took to drink, returned to the town, and soon died."
> — (001-003)

The father's biography is a three-generation template. "Took to drink" is placed between "failed" and "soon died" as the middle term in a causal chain. The pothouse that was supposed to be his livelihood becomes his undoing. The sons inherit not his business but his trajectory — and spend their lives trying to escape it.
