Tula is a historic industrial city south of Moscow, referenced in *The Village* primarily through the misadventures of Deniska, the half-literate peasant who sets out for Tula in search of work and is robbed of everything at the station. It also appears as the location of Rusanoff's estate, where Syery briefly finds employment.

## Location & Geography

Tula lies on the rail line south of Moscow, accessible via Uzlovaya (Uzslova) junction. From the novel's setting in the Oryol Governorate, Tula is a significant journey — Deniska plans to ride the train without a ticket, hiding under the seat until Uzlovaya, then walking the remaining distance [020-050]. The Tula station, where Deniska is robbed, is a site of transit and danger for the inexperienced peasant traveller [022-005].

## Role in the Narrative

Tula functions in the novel as a symbol of the peasant's illusory hope for a better life in the city. Deniska, who has earned money selling newspapers during the summer, sets out for Tula with a valise and a pocketful of little books, convinced that "maybe some sort of a job will turn up there" [020-010]. Tikhon Ilitch scoffs at his naivety: "You're no dandy to make an impression in Tula!" [020-013]. Deniska's dreams of a hussar jacket and earned riches are cruelly dashed when he is robbed of everything at the Tula station [022-005].

After his return from Tula, Deniska drifts about the village without work, eventually becoming the reluctant bridegroom in Tikhon Ilitch's scheme to marry off the Bride. His Tula failure marks him as a man without prospects, whom Tikhon Ilitch can manipulate.

Tula also appears as the location of Rusanoff's estate, where Syery briefly works during Lent before quarrelling with the manager and leaving [038-001].
