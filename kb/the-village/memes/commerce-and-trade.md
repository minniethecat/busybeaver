# Commerce and Trade

Commerce in *The Village* is not the orderly exchange of goods in a settled market. It is a predatory, improvisational, morally ambiguous activity that takes place at the margins of rural society — in carts, at fairs, in pothouses, and on the doorsteps of the destitute. The Krasoff family's history is a history of trade: the father's failed pothouse, the sons' peddling cart, Tikhon's eventual ascent to merchant-landowner. Through this trajectory, Bunin shows commerce as both a path out of peasant poverty and a force of moral corrosion.

## The Concept in the Novel

The novel's commercial world is introduced in the very first chapter through the Krasoff brothers' peddling cart — a covered wagon stocked with "small mirrors, cheap soap, rings, thread, kerchiefs, needles, cracknels." This is not the dignified commerce of the guild merchant but the desperate, itinerant trade of the barely-solvent. The open cart carries not just merchandise but "dead cats, eggs, heavy linen, crash, rags" — whatever can be gathered, bartered, or sold. Commerce at this level is indistinguishable from scavenging.

The fair, which occupies several chapters, is the novel's great set piece of commercial life. Bunin renders it as a kind of capitalist carnival: muddled, discordant, "noisy and muddled," where "brooms, scythes, wooden tubs with handles, shovels, wheels lay about in heaps." The fair brings together every social type — Gypsies, Jews, gentry, peasants, police, merchants — in a temporary suspension of normal hierarchies. But the apparent chaos conceals a brutal logic: the strong prey on the weak, the shrewd on the foolish, and the fair ends as it always does, with the poor poorer and the rich richer.

Tikhon Ilitch is the novel's exemplary commercial man. His method — following the rural police on tax-collection days, buying distress grain, taking advantage of every forced sale — reveals commerce as a form of合法ized predation. His success is undeniable, but Bunin refuses to celebrate it. Tikhon's wealth brings him no happiness, no respect, no peace. He is, in the end, a richer version of his father: still rootless, still anxious, still spiritually empty.

## Historical Context

The late imperial Russian economy was undergoing a rapid, uneven commercialization. The construction of railways, the growth of grain exports, and the emergence of a banking system created new opportunities for those positioned to exploit them. But the benefits of commercialization were distributed with savage inequality. Peasants who could not compete with commercial grain farming were driven into debt and landlessness. Merchants who succeeded — often from Old Believer or sectarian backgrounds — were resented by both the gentry (who saw them as vulgar upstarts) and the peasantry (who saw them as exploiters).

The fair (*yarmarka*) was the traditional institution through which rural commerce was conducted. Held at fixed times of the year, often in conjunction with religious festivals, fairs brought together producers, traders, and consumers from a wide geographic area. By Bunin's time, the traditional fair was being displaced by permanent markets and shops, but it retained its cultural significance as a site of social mixing and economic drama.

## Key Passages

> "The merchandise consisted of small mirrors, cheap soap, rings, thread, kerchiefs, needles, cracknels — these in the covered shop. The open-body cart contained everything they gathered in: dead cats, eggs, heavy linen, crash, rags."
> — (001-004)

The contrast between the "covered shop" (respectable merchandise) and the "open-body cart" (scavenged goods) captures the dual nature of rural commerce: it is simultaneously a retail operation and a salvage enterprise. The dead cats and rags are as much a part of the Krasoffs' trade as the mirrors and rings.

> "The Fair, scattered over the town pasture land for a whole verst, was, as usual, noisy and muddled. Brooms, scythes, wooden tubs with handles, shovels, wheels lay about in heaps."
> — (004-003)

The fair is rendered as a landscape of objects. Bunin lists the commodities without differentiation — brooms, scythes, tubs, shovels, wheels — creating a sense of material saturation that is also material chaos. Nothing is organized, nothing is clean, nothing is beautiful. This is commerce as brute physical fact.

> "Indefatigably he followed up the rural police on those dull autumnal days when taxes are collected and forced sale follows forced sale."
> — (002-002)

The rhythm of rural commerce is set by the tax calendar. Tikhon's commercial genius lies in his ability to synchronize his buying with the state's extraction of revenue. He profits from the misery that the tax system creates, and his "indefatigable" energy is the energy of the vulture.
