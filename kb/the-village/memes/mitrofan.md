Mitrofan is a minor character in Ivan Bunin's novel *The Village* (1910), appearing in Part II, Chapter IX as one of three watchmen spending the night in a bathhouse. He is characterized by his stern, cutting manner of speech and his pragmatic, unsentimental view of poverty — a perspective that contrasts sharply with the more impassioned Akim.

## Role in the Novel

Mitrofan appears in a single extended scene in Part II, Chapter IX, where Kuzma spends the evening and night in an old bathhouse with three sick watchmen: a young baker, the elderly Akim, and Mitrofan. The chapter unfolds as a dialogue about poverty, illness, and the nature of the peasant condition.

Mitrofan serves as a pragmatic counterweight to Akim's emotional outbursts. When Akim rails against doctors, Mitrofan responds with sharp, dismissive comments. He speaks severely, snapping at Akim's foolishness, but his own views reveal a hardened acceptance of the social order.

> "Well, then, don't chatter nonsense about what you haven't seen," said Mitrofan severely, and turned to the baker: "Yes, and you're a nice one, too: making yourself out to be a victim."
> — [009-005]

When the conversation turns to how the poor are treated in Rostoff — thrown into manure pits to burrow like pigs — Mitrofan confirms the account with grim detachment. He is a "peasant from the roots up," as he says of Akim, and his worldview reflects the fatalism and emotional bluntness that Bunin identifies as essential peasant traits.

> "A peasant from the roots up. He lives, and he doesn't scratch himself," Mitrofan confirmed.
> — [009-010]

As the fire dies down, Mitrofan quietly rolls a cigarette, his passion spent as quickly as it flared. This rhythm of brief intensity followed by resignation mirrors the larger structure of peasant life as Bunin depicts it — moments of anger and argument that lead nowhere, subsumed by the endless, unchanging routine of survival.

## Relationships

- **Akim** — fellow watchman with whom Mitrofan argues about poverty, doctors, and the nature of suffering.
- **The Baker** — a young, sympathetic former baker, the third watchman in the bathhouse scene.
- **Kuzma Krasoff** — silent observer of the debate; Mitrofan is unaware of Kuzma's intellectual background.

## Key Passages

> "He is referring to us, the likes of himself," remarked Mitrofan. "I happened to mention that in Rostoff the poor folks are thrown into the manure."
> — [009-006]

## Literary Significance

Mitrofan embodies the hardened, unsentimental strain of peasant consciousness. Unlike Akim, who rages against his condition with the "irascibility of an idiot," Mitrofan accepts the brutality of the world with a kind of grim lucidity. Together, the three watchmen represent a spectrum of peasant responses to suffering: the baker's quiet sympathy, Akim's inarticulate fury, and Mitrofan's cold realism. Bunin uses this nocturnal debate to dramatize the intellectual and emotional limitations of the peasant world — a world where even the most intense arguments end in silence and sleep.
