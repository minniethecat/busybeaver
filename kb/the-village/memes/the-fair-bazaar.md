# The Fair and Bazaar

The fair (*yarmarka*) that occupies the early chapters of *The Village* is the novel's most vivid spatial set piece. Scattered over "the town pasture land for a whole verst," it is a temporary city of commerce, noise, and social mixing where the full spectrum of rural Russian types — from Gypsies to gentry — briefly coexist.

## Role in the Narrative

Bunin renders the fair as a capitalist carnival: "noisy and muddled," where "brooms, scythes, wooden tubs with handles, shovels, wheels lay about in heaps" (004-003). The apparent chaos conceals a brutal logic of predation: the strong exploit the weak, and the fair disperses leaving the poor poorer. It is here that Tikhon confronts the "vast wretchedness" (004-009) that haunts him.

## Key Passages

> "A dull, discordant roar hung over it all — the neighing of horses, the shrilling of children's whistles, the polkas and marches thundered out by the orchestrions of the merry-go-rounds."
> — (004-003)
