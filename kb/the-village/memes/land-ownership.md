# Land Ownership

Land is the fundamental axis around which all of life in Bunin's *The Village* rotates. Who owns the land, who works it, who loses it, and who hungers for it — these questions determine the fates of every character in the novel.

Bunin, himself a descendant of landed proprietors, portrays land not as inert property but as a living force that shapes consciousness, memory, and destiny. The soil of Durnovka and its surrounding estates carries the sediment of centuries: the violence of serfdom, the decline of the gentry, the grasping ascent of the peasant-turned-merchant, and the desperate hunger of the landless laborer.

## The Concept in the Novel

The novel traces the great transfer of land from the declining gentry to the rising merchant class through the figure of [[tikhon-krasoff]]. Tikhon's methodical acquisition of land — buying "standing grain on the stalk from the landed proprietors" and taking parcels "from the peasants, in small parcels, not scorning even half a meadow" — is the engine of his ascent [002-002]. His method is incremental, relentless, and indiscriminate. He acquires land the way a predator feeds: piece by piece, from the distressed and the dying.

Yet land ownership in *The Village* brings no peace. Tikhon's estates are a source of anxiety, not security. He "bargained and bought, went to the estate almost every day, kept watch with the eye of a vulture over every inch of land" [002-003]. The vulture metaphor is Bunin's central image for the new landowning class. Unlike the old gentry — who inherited their land and treated it with negligent grandeur — Tikhon watches over his property with predatory intensity. His relationship to the soil is purely extractive.

The moral weight of land ownership is established in the novel's very first sentence. The great-grandfather of the Krasoffs, a serf known as "The Gipsy," was hunted with wolfhounds by Cavalry Captain Durnovo for the crime of luring away his master's mistress [001-001]. That ancestral violence — the absolute power of one man over the body of another — haunts Tikhon's relationship to his own property. He is acquiring the land that once owned his ancestors. But the transaction is never morally complete; the soil retains the stain of its history.

Land also functions as the novel's great divisive force. The question of redistribution — will the peasants take the landlords' land? — rumbles through the narrative like distant thunder. Tikhon himself is consumed by the question: "We have more land than we can manage!" he declares, yet when "discussion connected with the land came up, his wrath awoke" [007-003] [007-005]. The contradiction is precise: the peasant-turned-landowner now fears the very forces that enabled his own rise.

The secondary characters of the novel fill out the spectrum of relationships to land. The petty gentry — "dusty, ground to powder" like the pasture at the Vorgol fair — cling to their diminished estates with bluster and self-deception [004-005]. The landless peasant Syery, who had "a fairly large amount of land — three desyatini" yet can barely subsist, represents the inadequacy of smallholdings in a system designed for large estates [037-005]. Kuzma Krasoff, the brother who inherits nothing, embodies the déclassé intelligentsia's rootlessness — a man of thought without a patch of earth to call his own.

## Historical Context

Land tenure in late imperial Russia was a patchwork of contradictions inherited from serfdom and reshaped — but never resolved — by the Emancipation of 1861. The reform transferred land from the gentry to the peasant communes, but on punitive terms: peasants paid redemption dues for decades, and the allotments they received were often of inferior quality and insufficient size to support a family. The gentry, deprived of serf labor, proved incapable of managing their estates as modern commercial enterprises. Between 1861 and 1905, gentry landholdings declined by approximately forty percent, much of the land passing to merchants and prosperous peasants — the Tikhon Krasoffs of rural Russia.

### The Landholding Classes in the Novel

| Class | Representative | Holdings | Relationship to Land |
|-------|---------------|----------|---------------------|
| Old Gentry | Cavalry Captain Durnovo | Large estates (depleted) | Hereditary, negligent, declining |
| Petty Gentry | Basoff, petty proprietors at the Fair | Small estates (mortgaged) | Anxious, boastful, clinging to status |
| Merchant-Kulak | Tikhon Ilitch Krasoff | Acquired estates (expanding) | Extractive, vigilant, morally conflicted |
| Peasant Smallholder | Syery (Gray) | 3 desyatini (inadequate) | Subsistence, precarious, entrapping |
| Landless Laborer | Yakoff, Oska | None | Dependent, vulnerable, mobile |

The Stolypin reforms (1906–1911) attempted to dismantle the peasant commune and create a class of independent, landowning farmers on the model of Western European peasant proprietors. *The Village*, written at the height of these reforms, registers their effects indirectly. Characters like Tikhon Krasoff embody the new type that the reforms were meant to cultivate — the peasant who rises through commerce to become a landowner. But Tikhon's moral condition — grasping, anxious, spiritually hollow — is Bunin's implicit judgment on the reformist project. The land can be acquired; the soul that acquisition requires cannot be saved.

## Key Passages

> "Unweariedly he bought standing grain on the stalk from the landed proprietors and took land from them and from the peasants, in small parcels, not scorning even half a meadow."
> — [002-002]

The adverb "unweariedly" captures the mechanical quality of Tikhon's accumulation. He does not desire land; he is driven to acquire it by a compulsion he does not understand and cannot resist. "Not scorning even half a meadow" — the phrase is devastating in its precision. There is no parcel too small, no transaction too petty, for a man whose hunger for land is rooted in ancestral landlessness.

> "He bargained and bought, went to the estate almost every day, kept watch with the eye of a vulture over every inch of land."
> — [002-003]

Bunin's vulture is not a casual simile but a structural principle. The vulture does not create; it feeds on the dead and dying. Tikhon's wealth is built on the carcasses of gentry estates and peasant allotments. His vigilance — going to the estate "almost every day" — is the vigilance of a scavenger who cannot afford to let any opportunity escape.

> "When he read or heard that land was to be taken from only such as owned more than five hundred desyatini, his wrath awoke. 'We have more land than we can manage!' said Tikhon Ilitch. But discussion connected with the land came up, and his wrath flared."
> — [007-005] [007-006]

Tikhon's contradictory response to the question of land redistribution — he admits there is too much land, but rages at the prospect of losing any — is the novel's sharpest diagnosis of the psychology of property. Ownership does not satisfy the desire for ownership; it intensifies it. The fear of losing land becomes indistinguishable from the fear of losing self.

> "Are they burning out the landed proprietors there?"
> — [028-009]

This single, stark question — asked in passing as Kuzma travels through the countryside — condenses the terror that simmered beneath rural life after 1905. The burning of manor houses during the revolution had demonstrated that the peasantry's relationship to gentry land was not merely economic but explosive. The question is never answered directly; it hangs in the air, a reminder of the violence that property relations both contain and provoke.

> "On one and the same day, the peasants had risen through almost the whole district. They had burned and sacked several manors, and then quieted down."
> — [009-009]

The peasant uprising is narrated with Bunin's characteristic flatness — "they had burned and sacked several manors" delivered in the same tone as a weather report. The understatement is deliberate. The burning of manor houses was not an aberration but a recurrence, a rhythmic feature of rural life. Each generation of landowners lived with the knowledge that the land beneath their feet might one day burn.

> "Syery had a fairly large amount of land — three desyatini. But he was too poor to work it properly; he had no horse, no plough, no seed."
> — [037-005]

The tragedy of the peasant smallholder is compressed into three sentences. Three desyatini — roughly eight acres — was, in the arithmetic of the emancipation settlement, sufficient land for a peasant family. But land without capital is a trap rather than a resource. Syery owns land and starves on it. His property is a parody of ownership, a legal title to misery.

> "On retiring from service he had settled in his native town, and still considered himself a great landed proprietor."
> — [034-001]

The petty gentry's delusion of grandeur — a retired official who "still considered himself a great landed proprietor" — is one of Bunin's characteristic satirical touches. The gentry's relationship to land had become performative: a matter of self-perception rather than economic reality. The land was slipping away, but the identity it conferred proved harder to relinquish.

## Related Characters & Places

- [[tikhon-krasoff]] — the peasant-turned-landowner whose voracious acquisition of estates drives the novel's economic plot and whose spiritual emptiness indicts the values of the new property-owning class
- [[durnovka]] — the estate and village that Tikhon acquires; the physical stage on which the drama of land ownership is enacted, from the ancestral violence of the Durnovo family to Tikhon's uneasy dominion
- [[kuzma-krasoff]] — the brother who inherits no land and embodies the déclassé intelligentsia's rootlessness in a society where identity is tied to the soil
- [[the-gentry]] — the declining class of hereditary landowners whose estates pass, piece by piece, into the hands of merchants and kulaks
