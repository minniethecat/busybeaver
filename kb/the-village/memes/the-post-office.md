# The Post Office

**The Post Office** at [[ulianovka]] is the nearest postal station serving [[durnovka]] and the surrounding villages. Run by the notorious chief [[sakharoff]], it functions as a symbol of rural bureaucracy — an outpost of officialdom where the outside world arrives in the form of newspapers and letters, filtered through the abrasive personality of its keeper. It is one of the few destinations that draws characters beyond the confines of the estate.

## Location

The post office is located in Ulianovka, the same village that is home to [[rodka]]'s family. It lies within traveling distance of the Durnovka farmstead; [[kuzma-krasoff]] visits it during the Great Fast, marking one of only two occasions he leaves the farm during the entire Lenten season [041-005].

## Role in the Narrative

### Sakharoff's Domain

The post office is defined by its chief, [[sakharoff]], a man famous for two things: his passion for ordering free price-lists — catalogues of guns, seeds, and musical instruments by mail — and his savage treatment of the peasants, who say: "When you pass in a letter, your hands and feet fairly shake!" [014-002]. Sakharoff is described as resembling a gorilla — grumpy, roaring at peasants, and demanding bribes of fowls or flour [041-005].

### The Outside World

The post office represents the village's fragile link to the wider world. [[koshel]] brings from the post office a newspaper "nearly six weeks old" [044-018] — a measure of Durnovka's distance from the currents of Russian life. Kuzma, who reads newspapers all day and records everything he sees, depends on this connection [036-018].

### Kuzma's Visits

Kuzma visits the post office during Lent — one of only two excursions from the farm (the other being to see his brother [[tikhon-krasoff|Tikhon]]) [041-005]. The experience is memorably bleak: "it was so boresome in the low-ceiled post-office at Ulianovka — boresome as it can be only in official offices in the wilds" [041-005]. Sakharoff interrogates him, demanding his name and family name, outraged that Kuzma has not brought the customary tribute [041-005].

A peasant character later recalled in the novel is unable to pronounce "post-office" properly, calling it "spost-office" — a small linguistic detail that underscores the alien nature of bureaucratic institutions in the peasant world [036-011].

## Key Passages

> Along the highway, gun over shoulder, came the chief of the post-office at Ulianovka, Sakharoff, famed because of his passion for ordering by letter free price-lists — catalogues of guns, seeds, musical instruments — and because of his manner of treating the peasants, which was so savage that they were wont to say: "When you pass in a letter, your hands and feet fairly shake!" [014-002]

> And it was so boresome in the low-ceiled post-office at Ulianovka — boresome as it can be only in official offices in the wilds. Grumpy Sakharoff, who resembled a gorilla, was roaring at the peasants, raging because it had not occurred to Kuzma to send him half a dozen fowls or, at least, a pood of flour. [041-005]

> Koshel brought from the post-office a newspaper nearly six weeks old. [044-018]

> He was unable to pronounce the word "post-office" properly: he called it "spost-office." [036-011]
