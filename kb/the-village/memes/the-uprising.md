# The Peasant Uprising

The peasant uprising is the culminating social event of *The Village*'s middle section — the moment when the simmering tensions between the Durnovka peasants and Tikhon Ilitch Krasoff, their landowner, erupt into open confrontation. It is the novel's most sustained depiction of collective peasant action, and Bunin renders it with characteristic ambivalence: the uprising is terrifying to Tikhon, briefly exhilarating to the peasants, and ultimately inconsequential to everyone.

## Prelude: Rumours and Anticipation

Before the uprising breaks out, the possibility of rebellion hangs in the air as a topic of anxious speculation. Tikhon Ilitch, driving with Yakoff, asks bluntly: "Well, and how are things going with you over there? Are you still expecting the rebellion?" (PN: P02-008). Yakoff, whose peasant secretiveness reasserts itself immediately, waves the question away: "What would we do with a rebellion? Our folks are peaceable. Yes, a peaceable lot" (PN: P02-008). But Tikhon is not reassured — he knows that Durnovka, with its thirty homesteads glaring across the ravine at the tiny manor, is waiting for some "order" to act (PN: P02-008).

## The Attack on the Manor

The order comes at last. One Sunday, a rumour circulates through Durnovka that the village assembly has worked out a plan for an attack upon the manor (PN: P02-009). Tikhon Ilitch, his eyes "maliciously merry" and seized by a feeling of unusual strength and daring, harnesses his colt and drives at high speed toward Durnovka (PN: P02-009).

The landscape he passes through seems to contradict the rumours of violence. The sun sets in greyish-red clouds after a rainy day; the birches glow crimson; larks warble their evening song in peace. But when Tikhon catches sight of the fallow-field beside the manor, thickly sown with sweet-clover, he sees a drove of peasant horses grazing there — the signal that the uprising has begun (PN: P02-009).

What follows is a scene of chaotic confrontation. The crowd, which seems immense to Tikhon, swarms across the gorge to the manor and fills the yard with uproar and abuse, massing on the porch and pinning him against the door (PN: P02-009). The harness-maker — "a vicious emaciated fellow with a sunken belly and a sharp nose, wearing tall boots and a lavender print shirt" — acts as spokesman, brandishing his stick and screeching that an order has been issued to "make an end of that outfit" across the entire government (PN: P02-009).

The demands are radical: hired labourers from outside are to be expelled from all estates and replaced with local workers at a ruble a day; the landowners themselves are to be driven out "neck and crop" (PN: P02-009). When Tikhon tries to shout the harness-maker down, calling him a tramp who has been "whetting himself on the deacon's son," the harness-maker turns the insult back: "Tramp yourself! You're an old fool! How much land do you own? Two hundred desyatini? But I own about as much ground as is covered by your porch! And why? Who are you, anyway?" (PN: P02-009).

Tikhon, his wits muddled, fights his way through the crowd to his runabout and flees, threatening to "pay you off for this" as unanimous laughter, yells, and whistling follow him (PN: P02-009).

## The Orchard Fire

From the crossroads, Tikhon watches in the darkness as the uprising reaches its climax. A dark-fiery column rises above the manor-house: the peasants have shaken all the immature fruit from the orchard trees and set fire to the watchman's hut. A pistol left behind by the gardener discharges itself in the flames (PN: P02-009).

## Aftermath and Scale

The uprising, it later becomes known, was county-wide — on one and the same day, peasants rose through almost the entire county, and the town inns were crowded for a long time afterward with landowners who had sought the protection of the authorities (PN: P02-009). Tikhon Ilitch himself also sought that protection, and afterward recalled it with shame — "because the whole uprising had been limited to the Durnovka people's shouting for a while, doing a lot of damage, and then quieting down" (PN: P02-009).

The harness-maker soon began presenting himself at Tikhon's shop in Vorgol as though nothing had happened, doffing his cap on the threshold as if unaware that Tikhon's face darkened at his appearance (PN: P02-009). But rumours persisted that the Durnovka folk intended to murder Tikhon, and he took to carrying a bulldog revolver in his pocket, vowing to burn Durnovka to the ground or poison its wells (PN: P02-009). The uprising's most concrete long-term effect was to convince Tikhon to rid himself of Durnovka entirely: "Real money is the money in your pocket, not the money you're going to inherit from your grandmother!" (PN: P02-009).

The uprising also marks a turning point in Tikhon's personal life. It is "soon after the rebellion" that Rodka returns from military service ahead of his time and minus an eye — an event that sets in motion the triangle involving Tikhon, Rodka, and the Bride (PN: P02-010). Months later, as winter approaches, Tikhon sits alone and "recalled to mind the summer, the rebellion, the Bride, his brother, his wife" — the rebellion now part of the accumulating weight of memory and regret (PN: P02-019).

## Fates of the Rebels

The novel's final section provides a grim postscript on the uprising's participants. Trifon chokes to death on raw ham at Assumption-tide. Komar, "one of the chief rioters, renowned for his strength, his cleverness, and his daring in his dealings with the members of the gentry class," falls into a malt-kiln at a distillery near Eletz while intoxicated and suffocates — having bent the door in his efforts to escape, but "evidently such a death had been written in his fate" (PN: P03-004). Vanka Krasny, whose voice was distinguishable above all others during the uprising, returns to the Donetz mines. The harness-maker drifts from estate to estate. The rebellion, like everything else in peasant life, leaves no lasting mark on the social order it briefly challenged.
