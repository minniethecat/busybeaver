# Serfdom

Although serfdom was legally abolished in 1861, its shadow looms over every page of Bunin's *The Village*. The novel is set approximately forty-five years after the Emancipation Reform of Tsar Alexander II, yet the characters — peasants and former serf-owners alike — continue to live within the psychological, economic, and social structures that serfdom created.

## The Concept in the Novel

Bunin does not treat serfdom as a distant historical event. Instead, he presents its abolition as a rupture that was never fully healed. The Krasoff brothers, Tikhon and Kuzma, are descendants of serfs, and their life trajectories are shaped by the incomplete liberation that emancipation represented. The novel portrays a countryside where freedom from bondage did not bring prosperity or dignity — it brought confusion, displacement, and a new kind of servitude to poverty, alcohol, and violence.

The character of Kuzma gives voice to the novel's most explicit reflection on serfdom's legacy. In a moment of passionate outburst, he confronts the impossible expectations placed on a people who had been enslaved for centuries and freed for only decades. His words reveal Bunin's central thesis: that the Russian peasant's alleged failings — ignorance, brutality, passivity — are not innate flaws but the direct inheritance of a system that treated human beings as property.

## Historical Context

Serfdom in Russia was formally abolished by the Emancipation Manifesto of 1861, signed by Tsar Alexander II. The reform freed approximately 23 million privately-owned serfs, but the terms of emancipation were deeply flawed. Peasants were required to pay redemption payments for the land they received, often at inflated prices, and the land was allocated to the village commune (the *mir*) rather than to individual peasants, restricting mobility and perpetuating collective poverty.

The forty-five years between emancipation and the publication of *The Village* saw the gradual unraveling of the old estate system. The landed gentry, deprived of free labor and burdened by debt, declined steadily. Some peasants prospered as merchants and landowners — Tikhon Ilitch Krasoff is one such example — but the vast majority remained trapped in a cycle of subsistence farming, periodic famine, and dependence on local power structures that had changed little since the days of serfdom.

## Key Passages

> "'Tis only forty-five years since serfdom was abolished — so what can be expected of the people? Yes, but who is to blame for that? The people themselves! The people themselves!'"
> — (028-013)

Kuzma's anguished cry encapsulates the novel's ambivalence. He recognizes both the brevity of freedom — forty-five years against centuries of bondage — and the self-destructive patterns that the peasantry seem unable to escape. The passage is characteristic of Bunin's refusal to offer easy moral judgments.

> "Dusty, ground to powder by feet, wheels, and hoofs, littered and covered with dung, the pasture was already being deserted — the Fair was dispersing. But Tikhon Ilitch, as if with deliberate intent to spite someone or other, persisted in keeping his unsold horses there in the heat, and sat on and on in his cart. It seemed as if he were overwhelmed not so much by illness as by the spectacle of the great poverty, the vast wretchedness which, not for the first time, he was beholding all around him."
> — (004-009)

This passage connects serfdom's legacy to the pervasive material degradation of rural life. Tikhon Ilitch, the self-made merchant who rose from servile origins, is paralyzed not by physical sickness but by the moral weight of witnessing what his people have become. The "vast wretchedness" he observes is the visible residue of a system that treated land and labor as interchangeable commodities.

> "The father of the Krasoffs was a petty huckster. He roved about the county, lived for a time in Durnovka, set up a pothouse and a little shop, failed, took to drink, returned to the town, and soon died. After serving for a while in shops his sons, Tikhon and Kuzma, who were almost of an age, also took to peddling."
> — (001-003)

The Krasoff family history, compressed into a few sentences, traces the arc from serfdom to petty commerce. The father — likely born a serf — lives a rootless, degraded existence. His sons rise to become shop assistants and peddlers, but their ascent is precarious, shadowed by violence and the constant threat of falling back into destitution.
