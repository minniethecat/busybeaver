Moscow is the ancient capital of Russia, referenced in *The Village* primarily as a symbol of the wider world that lies beyond the reach of the novel's provincial characters. It appears in Bunin's Autobiographical Note as his point of departure into exile, and in the narrative as a distant, almost mythical place that Tikhon Ilitch has never visited despite a lifetime of longing.

## Location & Geography

Moscow lies far to the north of the novel's Oryol Governorate setting. The daily post-train from Moscow arrives at the station near Vorgol, invariably an hour and a half late [014-010]. The Moscow Kremlin — with its towers and domes — appears in a chromo-lithograph hung on the wall of Tikhon Ilitch's cottage, a printed image that serves as his only visual connection to the capital [016-009].

## Role in the Narrative

Moscow functions in the novel as a measure of Tikhon Ilitch's confinement. Gazing at the lithograph of the Kremlin, he reproaches himself: "Shame on me! I've never been in Moscow since I was born!" [016-014]. His life of relentless commerce — the shop, the posting-station, the pothouse, Durnovka, the stallion, the boar-pigs — has consumed every moment, leaving him unable even to visit the birch grove across the highway, let alone Moscow. The city represents everything his life of accumulation has denied him.

For Kuzma, Moscow is a place of transient business: he once travelled there on his employer's affairs while working for a drover near Eletz [025-001]. The Uhlan landowner, grandson of the original Durnovo, also makes trips to Moscow that contribute to his financial ruin [034-001].

In Bunin's Autobiographical Note, Moscow is the city he left in May 1918, fleeing the Revolution to live in the South of Russia before emigrating in 1919 [P03-013]. The ancient Tsars of Moscow are referenced as the creators of the settlements from which the rich Russian language of the steppes developed [P03-004].

## Key Passages

> "But at this point he made haste to transfer his glance to the Moscow Kremlin. 'Shame on me!' he muttered. 'I've never been in Moscow since I was born!' No, he had not. And why? His pigs wouldn't let him! Now it was his petty trading which hindered, now the posting-station, then the pothouse, then Durnovka."
> — [016-014]

> "The windows and the chromo-lithograph which was nailed to the wall under the mirror — it depicted a giant in a yellow kaftan and red morocco boots, with a Russian banner in his hand, from beneath which peeped the towers and domes of the Moscow Kremlin — were also veiled in steam."
> — [016-009]

> "The post-train from Moscow, which was an hour and a half late every day, was due at the station."
> — [014-010]

> "I left Moscow in May, 1918, lived in the South of Russia (which passed back and forth from the hands of the 'Whites' into those of the 'Reds') and then emigrated in February, 1919, after having drained to the dregs the cup of unspeakable suffering and vain hopes."
> — [P03-013]
