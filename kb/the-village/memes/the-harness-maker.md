# The Harness-Maker

**The Harness-Maker** is an unnamed but vividly drawn Durnovka peasant who plays a leading role in the peasant uprising against Tikhon Krasoff's estate. Described as "a vicious emaciated fellow," he exemplifies the simmering resentment and opportunistic violence that periodically erupts in the village.

## The Uprising

During the organized peasant attack on Tikhon's manor, the harness-maker emerges as a ringleader. He is described as "a vicious emaciated fellow" who "disputatiously caught" the words of those who tried to calm the crowd, redirecting their arguments to inflame rather than pacify [009-003] [009-004]. His physical emaciation — suggesting hunger and desperation — is paired with a sharp, argumentative intelligence that makes him effective at mobilizing others.

## Aftermath

In one of Bunin's most telling details about village power dynamics, the harness-maker "began, before long, to present himself in the shop at Vorgol as though nothing whatever had happened, and doffed his cap on the threshold as if he did not perceive that Tikhon Ilitch's face darkened at his appearance" [009-009]. This brazen return to normal commercial relations — the same man who led the attack now politely buying goods from his intended victim — captures the strange, unresolved tension between violence and everyday life in the village.

> The harness-maker, a vicious emaciated fellow, disputatiously caught his words. [009-003] [009-004]

> The harness-maker began, before long, to present himself in the shop at Vorgol as though nothing whatever had happened. [009-009]

## Later Fate

The harness-maker survives the uprising and continues working in the district: "The harness-maker was working about on different estates" [037-001]. Unlike Komar (who dies in a distillery accident) and Vanka Krasny (who flees to the mines), the harness-maker adapts and persists, his skilled trade giving him mobility that unskilled laborers lack.

## Role in the Novel

The harness-maker illustrates several of Bunin's key observations about peasant rebellion: it is organized by the most desperate and articulate members of the community; it is motivated by genuine grievance rather than ideology; and it ends not in resolution but in a return to the status quo, with the same participants resuming their ordinary economic roles. His story is one of survival through adaptation — a less tragic but equally revealing fate than that of his fellow rebels.
