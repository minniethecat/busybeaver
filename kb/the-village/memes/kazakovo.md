# Kazakovo

**Kazakovo** (also Kazakoff) is a gentry estate and village located twelve versts from [[durnovka]]. It is the destination of [[kuzma-krasoff]]'s spring journey in Part II, where he hopes to lease a garden and orchard, only to confront the harsh realities of rural administration and his own romantic illusions.

## Location and Geography

Kazakovo lies twelve versts from Durnovka across muddy, nearly impassable roads. The journey from the railway station requires hiring a peasant conveyance; drivers initially demand seven rubles for the twelve-verst trip before settling for far less, reflecting the poverty of the region [029-001].

The estate itself is situated on sloping pasture land. Its centerpiece is a dark manor-park surrounded by a spacious courtyard ringed with decaying outbuildings and the ruins of a stone wall. At the courtyard's center, behind three withered fir-trees, stands the manor house — a grey-board-sheathed building with a rusty-red roof [030-007]. The grounds include a vegetable garden with an apiary and a tiny orchard of three or four wild apple-trees behind old bushes, plus an old, dark-hued grain rick [029-017].

## Role in the Narrative

Kazakovo represents the collision between Kuzma Krasoff's idealized vision of rural life and the grim reality of the countryside. Hearing that a garden in the village was available for lease, Kuzma hastens there full of hope in the spring [027-001]. He endures a punishing journey through mud and rain — a driving downpour of rain mingled with hail accompanies him the entire way [030-001].

Upon arrival, Kuzma discovers an estate in decay. The manor-park lies dark and neglected [030-007]. The affairs of the gentry are managed by a retired cavalry soldier — a tall, rough superintendent who receives Kuzma with insolent hostility, angrily informing him that the garden has already been "leased long ago" [030-009] [031-001].

The experience culminates in a moment of bitter clarity: "That night, in the orchard at Kazakovo, it became clear to him that his dreams of orchards were foolish" [033-001]. Kuzma later writes down his spring trip to Kazakovo and his conversations there in an old account book, recording all that he had seen and heard in the village [036-018].
