Rodka is a secondary character in Ivan Bunin's novel *The Village* (1910), a tall, thin, sullen young peasant from Ulianovka. He embodies the brutal, emotionally deadened peasant life that Bunin depicts as characteristic of rural Russia after the 1905 Revolution. His marriage to the Bride (Molodaya) and his subsequent treatment of her serve as one of the novel's starkest portrayals of domestic violence and the degradation of human feeling among the peasantry.

## Role in the Novel

Rodka first appears in Part I, Chapter X, introduced as having gone to live with Fedot (Yakoff's brother) two years earlier, where he married and buried Fedot after the latter died from drinking. He is described as returning ahead of schedule, having lost an eye — a detail that immediately marks him as physically and morally damaged.

> "Rodka, a tall, thin, sullen young fellow from Ulianovka, had gone two years previously to live with Fedot, the brother of Yakoff; he had married, and had buried Fedot, who had died from over-drinking."
> — [010-001]

Rodka's central narrative function is as the husband of the Bride (Molodaya), whom he beats "ferociously every day and every night." Tikhon Ilitch Krasoff witnesses one such thrashing and shouts at Rodka in indignation, but Rodka responds with unnerving calm, seating himself on the bench and quietly observing that Tikhon should mind his own affairs. This scene crystallizes the novel's theme of violence as an accepted, even banal feature of peasant life.

> "After that incident it was painful to the point of torment to see his wife, to see Rodka; to know that Rodka slept with the Bride, that he beat her ferociously every day and every night."
> — [010-010]

Tikhon Ilitch eventually picks a quarrel with Rodka over firewood and drives him out of Durnovka. Rodka subsequently works on the railway line, while the Bride remains at home idle, enduring her mother's reproaches. His removal allows Tikhon to feel "alert, at his ease," underscoring how Rodka functions as an obstacle to Tikhon's sense of order.

> "Before placing Durnovka in his brother's hands, he had picked a quarrel with Rodka over the firewood, and had driven him out of Durnovka."
> — [012-004]

## Relationships

- **The Bride (Molodaya)** — wife. Rodka's marriage is defined by routine violence; he beats her daily, yet she remains with him, her spirit crushed.
- **Tikhon Ilitch Krasoff** — employer turned antagonist. Tikhon initially employs Rodka but becomes tormented by witnessing the domestic abuse, eventually expelling him.
- **Fedot** — brother-in-law and former housemate, whom Rodka buried after Fedot's death from alcoholism.
- **Yakoff** — brother-in-law through Fedot.

## Key Passages

## Literary Significance

Rodka represents a particular type in Bunin's portrait of the Russian peasantry: the man brutalized by poverty, drink, and the harshness of village life, who in turn brutalizes those weaker than himself. He is not a villain in the conventional sense — he is a product of his environment, a figure through whom Bunin explores the cycle of violence and the erosion of human empathy in rural Russia. His missing eye serves as a physical emblem of his damaged nature, while his calm response to Tikhon's outrage reveals the normalization of violence in the peasant world.
