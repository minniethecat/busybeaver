## Endnotes

 - A verst is two-thirds of a mile. ↩︎

 - This muddling of “Emir of Bukhara” is only one example of the ignorant combinations and locutions used by the peasant characters. ↩︎

 - A play on words, “tar” in the second sentence meaning “liquor.” ↩︎

 - *Matushka* and *batiushka* (literally, “Little Mother” and “Little Father”) are the characteristic Russian formula for addressing elderly strangers, regardless of [[social-class|class]] distinctions. ↩︎

 - A *desyatina* is a unit of [[land-ownership|land]] measurement equalling 2.07 acres. ↩︎

 - When a man or woman begins to get on in the world his admiring neighbours signalize their appreciation by adding to the Christian name the patronymic, as if the clever one were of gentle (noble) birth. In this story, [[tikhon-krasoff|Tikhon]] soon receives the public acknowledgment of success, having begun as [[the-steppe|plain]] “Tikhon.” Peasant-fashion, “Nikititch” was transmuted into “Mikititch.” ↩︎

 - Sharpers who pretend to be the [[rural-poverty|poverty]]-stricken descendants of the Tatar Princes who ruled Kazan before it was conquered, during the rein of [[ivanushka|Ivan]] the Terrible. ↩︎

 - A straight, loose gown, falling from the armpits, worn by unmarried girls. ↩︎

 - A heretic. Literally, one who drinks milk (*moloko*) during the Fasts in defiance to the Orthodox Catholic Church. ↩︎

 - Probably a deliberate bit of insolence, as he must have known that the patronymic was “Ilitch,” not “Fomitch.” ↩︎

 - All priests and monks in the Orthodox Catholic Church wear the hair and beard long. Tikhon Ilitch refers to the superstition that it portends bad luck to meet an ecclesiastic when one is arranging something or going somewhere. ↩︎
