# Kuzma Krasoff

Kuzma Krasoff is the younger of the two Krasoff brothers and, in many respects, the moral and philosophical center of *The Village*. Where [[Tikhon-Krasoff]] embodies the peasant who rises through commerce and property, Kuzma embodies the peasant who rises through consciousness — and finds that consciousness is a curse, not a liberation.

He has read books, written verse, and thought seriously about Russian identity and destiny. His education has lifted him out of peasant ignorance but provided no escape from peasant misery. He is Bunin's most autobiographical character: the intellectual who understands everything about his people's degradation and can do nothing to remedy it.

## Biography / Role in the Novel

Kuzma's biography begins in the same peddling cart as Tikhon's. The sons of a failed huckster, both brothers "after serving for a while in shops... also took to peddling" [001-003]. But after the violent quarrel that nearly ends in fratricide — Tikhon "tore his shirt open, and shouted that he would kill his brother" — their paths diverge dramatically [001-004].

Kuzma hires himself to a drover. The break is not merely practical but existential: Tikhon chooses commerce; Kuzma chooses, or is chosen by, thought.

In the years that follow, Kuzma drifts through marginal occupations. He serves as a clerk with a landed proprietor named Kasatkin [011-002]. He writes verse that the townspeople dismiss — "Kuzma 'skinned' his verses from books" [011-004]. He drinks, stops drinking, and drinks again. He develops a passion for self-education that leads nowhere.

The novel's central plot turn is Kuzma's reconciliation with Tikhon and his reluctant acceptance of the management of Durnovka. This arrangement — the thinker installed as overseer of his brother's property — is Bunin's bitterest irony. Kuzma can see the injustice of the arrangement but cannot refuse the salary that keeps him alive.

His tenure at Durnovka becomes an extended moral crisis, a confrontation with the people he has thought about all his life and now, for the first time, must rule.

Kuzma is the vehicle for Bunin's most searching reflections on the Russian condition. It is Kuzma who, in a moment of anguished outburst, cries that serfdom was abolished "only forty-five years" ago — "so what can be expected of the people?" [028-013]. The question, with its double edge, captures the novel's refusal of easy moral judgment.

In the novel's closing pages, Kuzma is left stranded at Durnovka, watching Deniska and Molodaya drive away into the winter night. He has achieved nothing, saved no one, written nothing that matters. His consciousness — the very quality that distinguishes him from Tikhon — has become indistinguishable from paralysis.

## Relationships

- [[Tikhon-Krasoff]] — elder brother; the merchant whose material success is Kuzma's reproach, whose spiritual emptiness is his mirror

- [[Nastasya-Petrovna]] — sister-in-law; a figure of domestic routine whose childless marriage mirrors the sterility of both brothers' lives

- [[Deniska]] — young peasant and conscript; Kuzma is powerless to alter his fate

- [[Balashkin]] — accordion-player, freethinker, drinking companion; one of the few with whom Kuzma can converse as an equal

## Key Passages

> "'Tis only forty-five years since serfdom was abolished — so what can be expected of the people? Yes, but who is to blame for that? The people themselves! The people themselves!'"
> — [028-013]

The novel's most famous passage and its interpretive crux. Kuzma's cry contains a contradiction that Bunin refuses to resolve: the brevity of freedom is an argument for compassion, yet the peasantry's self-destructive patterns are undeniable.

> "Almost all his life long Kuzma had dreamed of writing, of obtaining an education. Verses did not count. He had dallied with verses as a mere child. He longed to narrate how he had come to naught; to depict, with unprecedented ruthlessness, his poverty and that dreadful factor in his commonplace life which had crippled him, made of him a barren fig-tree."
> — [023-001]

Kuzma's literary ambition is inseparable from self-accusation. He dreams of writing not to celebrate his rise from poverty but to document his own ruin. The phrase "barren fig-tree" is Kuzma's judgment on himself: a man whose consciousness has produced nothing.

> "He recalled his childhood — his youth — Kuzma. He walked to the far corner of the orchard, to the edge of the ravine, and sat down on the grass. How long ago all that was! And how wretched, how hopelessly wretched, was that childhood and youth!"
> — [005-016]

Kuzma's memory of his own peasant childhood is an accusation, not a nostalgia. The orchard and ravine evoke only wretchedness. The passage anticipates his lifelong inability to romanticize the people from whom he came.

> "Kuzma crossed himself with a grand flourish when, at last, he escaped from that slavery. But he was already nearly thirty years of age; his hair was noticeably grey; he had become more sober, more serious; he had abandoned his verses, had abandoned reading; he had become accustomed to eating-houses, to drinking-bouts. ... And, all the while, he was overwhelmed with the conviction that he was wasting — had wasted — his life."
> — [025-001]

This marks the pivot from youthful aspiration to middle-aged resignation. Escaping peddling brings no liberation — only the discovery that freedom, too, can be a form of waste. The conviction that he has "wasted his life" is the steady, corrosive undertone of his existence.

> "A sort of molokan," grinned Kuzma. "And do you go to church? If you don't go to church, you're a worse infidel than any molokan."
> — [011-028]

From the brothers' reunion scene. Kuzma is too skeptical for peasant Orthodoxy, too rooted in village life for secular intellectual circles. The grin is characteristic: intelligence expressing itself as irony, a defense against the pain of belonging nowhere.

> "For a long time he cared not whether the Russian people had a future or not. He roamed about the manor estate, the village; he sat for hours at a time on the doorsteps of the cottages."
> — [034-019]

Kuzma at Durnovka — roaming, sitting, watching — is the novel's image of the intellectual in paralysis. He has come to the village to manage it, to improve it, perhaps to write about it. Instead, he sits on doorsteps.

> "Words are one thing, deeds are quite another! 'Tis the genuine Russian tune, brother: I live disgustingly, pig-fashion, but nevertheless I am living, and I shall continue to live, pig-fashion! You're a type, brother!"
> — [011-033]

Kuzma's diagnosis of his brother doubles as a diagnosis of himself and of the national character. The "genuine Russian tune" is a tragic self-awareness that does not lead to change. The cry "You're a type, brother!" is aimed at Tikhon but rebounds upon the speaker.

> "He thought of the peasant women lying in their cottages, the little children, the cattle in the byres. He thought of the terrible winter nights, the wolves, the blizzards that swept the steppe."
> — [020-082]

A moment of nocturnal empathy — one of the novel's few passages of unguarded compassion. Kuzma's imagination extends beyond his own suffering to encompass the collective vulnerability of the peasant world.

> "He read the sort of thing which the newspapers print at the bottom of their columns among the advertisements: 'The Russian people are great, mighty, patient, long-suffering — but it is time to say, enough!'"
> — [011-036]

Kuzma reads the cliches of the patriotic press with the contempt of a man who has lived the reality they describe. The juxtaposition of "great, mighty, patient" with the advertisement section is Bunin's own satirical touch.

> "What sort of Russians are we?" Kuzma burst out. "What sort of people? We've been wearing bast-shoes for a thousand years, and we'll go on wearing them another thousand!"
> — [011-023]

The outburst is bitter, sardonic, and self-implicating. Kuzma says "we," not "they" — he includes himself in the indictment. The bast-shoe becomes a symbol of immemorial stasis: a thousand years of unchanging misery stretching forward and backward in time.

## Historical / Literary Significance

Kuzma Krasoff belongs to a recognizable Russian type: the peasant autodidact, the self-taught intellectual who rises from the people through literacy and returns to them as either a revolutionary or a conscience. The type has a long lineage in Russian literature, from Radishchev to Gorky.

Bunin's treatment is distinctive — and distinctively bleak. Kuzma is not a revolutionary in waiting; he is a man who has read enough to understand the arguments for revolution and lived enough to doubt they will change anything.

The character draws on Bunin's own biography. Bunin, though born into the gentry, was deeply preoccupied with the question of Russian national character. His 1910 portrait of Kuzma — the peasant intellectual who can diagnose but cannot cure — anticipates a generation of Russian writers and thinkers who would be destroyed or exiled by the revolution they had helped to imagine.
