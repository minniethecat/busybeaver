The railway station near Vorgol serves as a vital transit hub in the world of *The Village*, connecting the rural backwater of Durnovka to the wider Russian Empire. Visible from Tikhon Ilitch's property and permeated by the sounds and smells of locomotives, the station is a place of arrivals and departures, chance encounters, and the collision of peasant life with modernity.

## Location & Geography

The station lies to the right of Tikhon Ilitch's house and shop, screened from direct view by buildings but constantly present through its sounds — the hissing of locomotives and the rattling of freight trains [007-010]. The railway line runs from the southeast, intersecting the highway at a faraway watchtower visible from the road [008-001]. The red buildings of the station stand amid empty green fields on the outskirts of town [027-012]. The Moscow post-train passes through daily, though it is invariably an hour and a half late [014-010].

## Role in the Narrative

The railway station is the setting for several significant encounters. Tikhon Ilitch goes there to find Deniska, the young peasant who has returned from Tula, and the scene inside the station — thick with smoke, the odour of wet sheepskin and samovars, the jostling of peasants — is one of the novel's most vividly rendered crowd scenes [021-001]. It is at the station that Tikhon Ilitch negotiates with Deniska over his future, and where the station watchman and restaurant keeper appear as minor but memorable figures [020-006].

The station also represents the friction between peasant life and the encroaching modern world. Cabmen from Ulianovka wait entire weeks for a single passenger [021-001]. The telegraph clerk shouts at peasants. Carts carrying the baggage of landowners are weighed at the ticket-seller's window. It is a place of transit but also of stasis — of peasants waiting endlessly for something to happen.

For Kuzma, the station is the point of departure from town and the first station on his journey to Kazakovo, where he must hire a conveyance for the final twelve versts [025-001]. And it is near a railway station that Nastasya Petrovna dies suddenly, a death that precipitates the sale of Durnovka [043-013].

## Key Passages

> "The hot locomotive of the freight-train could be heard hissing and getting up steam beyond the trees, behind the buildings of the railway station. Tikhon Ilitch stood bareheaded beside his shop, screwing up his eyes and smiling slyly."
> — [007-010]

> "The railway station was permeated with the odours of wet sheepskin coats, the samovar, cheap tobacco, and kerosene. The smoke was so dense that it gripped one's throat; the lamps hardly shone through the clouds of it, and of the semidarkness, dampness, and cold. The doors squeaked and banged; peasants, whips in hand, jostled and yelled — cabmen from Ulianovka, who sometimes waited a whole week before they captured a passenger."
> — [021-001]

> "The gloomy sky descended lower and lower over the earth. The roads, flooded by the rain, gleamed with a leaden sheen. The post-train from Moscow, which was an hour and a half late every day, was due at the station. Only from the signal-bells, the humming sounds, the rumbling, and the odour of coal and samovars in the yards, did Tikhon Ilitch know that it had arrived and departed."
> — [014-010]
