## Overview

Alongside official Orthodox Christianity, the village world of Bunin's *The Village* is saturated with folk superstitions, supernatural beliefs, and pre-Christian practices that coexist uneasily with church ritual. These beliefs form a distinct layer of peasant consciousness, revealing how the village understands and copes with misfortune, illness, and death.

## Witchcraft and Folk Magic

Belief in witchcraft is presented as an accepted reality in Durnovka. The peasants recount matter-of-factly that "a witch had rolled in the form of a wheel" near the village [036-011], and describe how "the knot had had the sign of the cross made over it" to protect against enchantment [036-015]. A local woman—tall, emaciated, and living alone—"was reputed to be a witch" [041-005], treated as a fact of village geography rather than a matter for doubt or debate.

## The Sign of the Cross

The sign of the cross recurs as a protective gesture throughout the novel, often performed mechanically as much out of habit as conviction. Kuzma makes the sign of the cross "on brow and breast" when he bows to an ikona [034-018], and at the wedding the priest's hands "mechanically made the sign of the cross with the ikona in the air" [047-016]. Even Tikhon, a hard-nosed merchant, "removed his cap and made the sign of the cross" when visiting a cemetery [005-016]. The gesture bridges the gap between institutional religion and folk practice, functioning less as theological devotion than as apotropaic ritual.

## Folk Singers and Prophetic Figures

The blind man who appears at Makarka's house is a figure of folk authority, chanting prophetic verses. He "sang of the orphaned and the wandering, of how the Lord had given wisdom to the ignorant" [018-005] and responds to Makarka's questions with the gravity of an oracle: "At any rate, that's what the old men say" [018-025]. Figures like the blind singer and the reputed witch represent an alternate knowledge-system within the village, one the official Church does not entirely supplant.

## Syncretic Religion

The coexistence of Orthodoxy and folk belief creates a syncretic peasant spirituality. Nastasya Petrovna "whispered her prayers" at home [003-002], and Kuzma "murmured a prayer" when alone in a field [032-023], yet neither character draws a sharp boundary between church doctrine and the ambient supernaturalism of village life. The result is a worldview in which saints, witches, ikons, and enchanted knots occupy a single, undifferentiated cosmos.
