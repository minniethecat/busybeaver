# Tikhon Ilitch Krasoff

Tikhon Ilitch Krasoff is, alongside his brother [[kuzma-krasoff]], one of the two protagonists of Ivan Bunin's *The Village* (1910). A peasant by birth who rises through trade, land speculation, and relentless vigilance to become a substantial landowner, Tikhon embodies the ambiguous triumph that the post-emancipation rural economy made possible for a ruthless and lucky few. He is Bunin's most fully realized character — cunning and superstitious, industrious and spiritually vacant, haunted by ancestral violence he can neither acknowledge nor escape. In Tikhon, Bunin concentrated his darkest intuitions about what the end of serfdom had actually produced: not liberation, but a new kind of bondage to property, fear, and the unquiet dead.

## Biography / Role in the Novel

The Krasoff family history, established in the novel's opening paragraph, is a compressed chronicle of the post-emancipation peasantry. The great-grandfather — a serf known as "The Gipsy" — was hunted with wolfhounds by his master, Cavalry Captain Durnovo, for the crime of seducing his mistress [001-001]. The father was a petty huckster who "roved about the county, lived for a time in Durnovka, set up a pothouse and a little shop, failed, took to drink, returned to the town, and soon died" [001-003]. Tikhon and Kuzma, "almost of an age," began as shop assistants and then as itinerant peddlers [001-003].

A violent quarrel over the division of profits — Tikhon "flew into a rage, tore his shirt open, and shouted that he would kill his brother" — drives the two apart for years [001-004]. Kuzma hires himself to a drover. Tikhon takes over a posting-house on the highway to Vorgol and begins his ascent proper. From this point, his biography is a ledger of acquisitions: "Unweariedly he bought standing grain on the stalk from the landed proprietors and took land from them and from the peasants, in small parcels, not scorning even half a meadow" [002-002]. He follows the rural police on tax-collection days, buying up the property of defaulting peasants at forced sale. He "kept watch with the eye of a vulture over every inch of land" [002-003].

By the time the novel's main action begins, Tikhon is about forty years old, a man of substance with a substantial estate at Durnovka. But his arrival in the landed class brings no arrival at peace. At the Vorgol fair, surveying the poverty and wretchedness around him, he is "overwhelmed not so much by illness as by the spectacle of the great poverty, the vast wretchedness which, not for the first time, he was beholding all around him" [004-009]. He becomes preoccupied with death — visiting the family graves, brooding on the futility of his labor, tormented by the thought that "all his life he had been engaged in business, in acquisition — and to what end?" [003-019].

Tikhon's personal life is a register of his spiritual condition. He lives with a dumb cook — "'A dumb woman can't betray anything with her tongue'" [002-002] — who bears him an illegitimate child. His marriage to Nastasya Petrovna is childless, a source of bitterness and superstitious anxiety. He consults the "Oracle," a book of divination, and is disturbed by its dark pronouncements. He gives alms to pilgrims and churches, seeking to purchase the absolution that his commercial transactions cannot provide.

In the novel's final sections, Tikhon's empire of land and money proves powerless against the forces that have always governed Durnovka: the intractable poverty of the peasants, the violence that simmers beneath the surface of village life, and the moral emptiness at the center of his own existence. He hands the management of Durnovka to Kuzma — the brother he once threatened to kill — and recedes into a kind of exhausted spectatorship, watching as the village he owns continues its ancient, brutal rhythms without him.

## Key Passages

> "Indefatigably he followed up the rural police on those dull autumnal days when taxes are collected and forced sale follows forced sale. Unweariedly he bought standing grain on the stalk from the landed proprietors and took land from them and from the peasants, in small parcels, not scorning even half a meadow."
> — [002-002]

The double adverb — "indefatigably," "unweariedly" — is characteristic of Bunin's method. The repetition suggests not energy but compulsion. Tikhon does not choose to pursue acquisition; he is driven, by a hunger for land and security rooted in his family's history of landlessness and violence.

> "He bargained and bought, went to the estate almost every day, kept watch with the eye of a vulture over every inch of land. They uttered admiring exclamations at his intelligence, his capacity for affairs — but they laughed in secret at his uncouthness, his ignorance."
> — [002-003]

The vulture simile is Bunin's master image for the new rural capitalist. The vulture does not create wealth; it feeds on the dead and the dying — on defaulted mortgages, distressed gentry, and peasants who cannot pay their taxes. The passage also registers the class contempt that follows Tikhon into prosperity: his wealth buys him land but not status.

> "It seemed as if he were overwhelmed not so much by illness as by the spectacle of the great poverty, the vast wretchedness which, not for the first time, he was beholding all around him."
> — [004-009]

Tikhon's paralysis at the Vorgol fair is one of the novel's pivotal scenes. A man who has climbed out of poverty finds himself unable to look away from it. The phrase "not for the first time" suggests a recurring crisis — each encounter with the poverty he has escaped confronts him with the emptiness of his escape.

> "He walked to the far corner of the cemetery where all his relatives were buried — father, mother, the sister who had died when a little girl. He stood there a long time, bowing his head."
> — [005-016]

The cemetery visit is Tikhon's solitary moment of genuine feeling, unmediated by commerce or status anxiety. Standing before the graves of his family — the father who failed, the mother, the dead sister — he confronts the one transaction he cannot control. The bowed head is the posture of a man who has acquired everything except meaning.

> "More and more frequently the thought occurred to Tikhon Ilitch: 'What's the object of all this? All my life I've been engaged in business, in acquisition — and to what end? I shall die — and then what?'"
> — [003-019]

The question is Bunin's own, channeled through his protagonist. Tikhon's entire existence — the peddling, the tax sales, the land deals, the vigilant surveillance of every inch of soil — collapses under the weight of its own futility. The question has no answer within the novel's moral universe, which is precisely the point.

> "He stood for a long time on the porch, watching the darkness fall, listening to the sounds from the village — a dog barking, a gate creaking, a woman calling a child. And it seemed to him that he had always stood here, and would stand here forever."
> — [020-001]

The passage captures Tikhon in his final mode: spectator rather than actor, immobilized by a landscape he owns but cannot inhabit. The eternal present tense — "he had always stood here, and would stand here forever" — is the temporal form of damnation. Tikhon has achieved everything he set out to achieve; it has brought him to this porch, this darkness, this endless standing.

## Historical / Literary Significance

Tikhon Ilitch Krasoff belongs to a specific historical type: the post-emancipation peasant who rises through commerce to join the rural propertied class. The Stolypin reforms (1906–1911) explicitly sought to cultivate such figures — independent peasant proprietors who would form a conservative bulwark against rural radicalism. Bunin's portrayal of Tikhon is, in effect, a devastating rejoinder to this reformist project. The reforms can create landowners, Bunin suggests, but they cannot create souls worthy of land.

In the Russian literary tradition, Tikhon stands between the romanticized peasants of Turgenev's *A Sportsman's Sketches* and the political abstractions of Gorky's revolutionary fiction. Bunin was writing against both traditions. His peasant-merchant is neither noble savage nor oppressed proletarian but something more disturbing: a human being deformed by the very forces — commerce, property, "progress" — that were supposed to liberate him. Maxim Gorky, reviewing *The Village*, called it "the best, the most truthful, the most talented book" written about the Russian peasantry, and Tikhon Krasoff is the character through whom that truth is most searchingly revealed.
