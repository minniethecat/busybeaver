# The Village Commune (Mir)

The village commune — the *mir* or *obshchina* — is the foundational institution of rural Russian life and the invisible architecture of Bunin's *The Village*. Though its formal powers were in decline by the novel's early-twentieth-century setting, the commune's assumptions — collective land tenure, mutual responsibility for taxes, the subordination of the individual to the group — shaped every aspect of peasant existence and continued to structure the rural world even as that world disintegrated.

## Historical Background

The Russian village commune was the primary institution of peasant self-governance for centuries. Under serfdom, the commune served as an intermediary between the landlord and the peasants, responsible for allocating land, collecting dues, and maintaining order. After emancipation in 1861, the commune's role expanded: it became the legal owner of the land allocated to freed serfs, distributing strips of arable land among member households and periodically redistributing them to maintain rough equality.

The commune's internal logic was collective rather than individualistic. Land was not private property but a communal resource; the assembly (*skhod*) of household heads made decisions by consensus; and the commune was collectively responsible for the tax obligations and military conscription quotas of its members. This system produced a peasantry accustomed to collective decision-making but also deeply suspicious of individual initiative — a tension that Bunin explores throughout *The Village*.

The Stolypin reforms of 1906–1911 aimed to dissolve the commune by allowing individual peasants to claim their share of communal land as private property and consolidate their scattered strips into enclosed farms. By the time Bunin was writing *The Village*, the commune was disintegrating — but its mental and social habits persisted, creating the contradictions that the novel anatomizes.

## The Commune in the Novel

In *The Village*, the commune appears most directly in the background of the peasant uprising. "One Sunday a rumour began to circulate in Durnovka that the village assembly had worked out a plan for an attack upon the manor" (009-001). The "village assembly" is the *skhod* — the commune's decision-making body — and the rumour suggests that the commune could still mobilize collective action when grievances accumulated.

But the commune also appears indirectly, as the structural explanation for much of the peasant behaviour that Tikhon and Kuzma find baffling. The peasant's indifference to individual accumulation, his suspicion of "progress," his reliance on collective rumour and communal judgment — these are not innate traits but the internalized norms of communal life. When Kuzma observes Durnovka's thirty homesteads "glaring across the ravine at the tiny manor" (008-026), he is seeing the commune in its final, dysfunctional form: a community united by resentment but incapable of collective action that would change its circumstances.

Tikhon Ilitch, the self-made man who has escaped the commune's constraints, embodies its counter-type. His entire career — individual accumulation, vigilance over every inch of land, suspicion of his neighbours — is a repudiation of communal values. Yet Bunin shows that repudiation brings no liberation. Tikhon ends the novel as isolated and spiritually empty as any peasant trapped in the commune's collective poverty.

## Key Passages

> "But the 'order' came, at last. One Sunday a rumour began to circulate in Durnovka that the village assembly had worked out a plan for an attack upon the manor."
> — (009-001)

The "village assembly" is the commune's *skhod* — the gathering of household heads that made communal decisions. That the uprising originates here rather than from individual agitators reflects the commune's residual power to organize collective peasant action.

> "It was extremely mortifying to reflect that, because of wretched little Durnovka, affairs were escaping from his grasp. And there were only thirty homesteads there."
> — (008-026)

Tikhon's frustration with Durnovka's thirty homesteads reflects the commune's persistence as an obstacle to his individual control. The village is not a collection of independent households but a collective body with its own internal logic, resistant to the market rationality that Tikhon embodies.
