## Horses as Economic Assets

Horses are at the center of the village economy in *The Village*. They are bought and sold at the fair, discussed endlessly by the petty gentry, and represent the most visible form of movable wealth. At the agricultural fair, horses are the main commodity — a place where "the horses neighed, lights blazed" [004-002] and where status and skill are publicly contested.

> "The petty landed proprietors chatted among themselves, walking back and forth, discussing the paces of the horses they owned" [004-005]

The horse market is also a theater of deception. Buyers and sellers measure each other through the animal, assessing teeth, legs, and gait. A man like Khvostoff "shuffling up to the horse and casting a sidelong fiery glance at it" [004-006] embodies the combination of desire, calculation, and pretense that defines horse trading.

For Tikhon Ilitch, horses are a measure of success. He drives his own cart, judges other men by their animals, and understands the difference between a good horse and a "wretched little horse" [006-002] as clearly as the difference between a rich man and a poor one.

## The Horse in Daily Life

Horses are not merely commodities — they are woven into the fabric of daily existence. Peasants travel by horse and cart; merchants transport grain to the railway by horse; travelers bait their horses at the posting-house. The physical intimacy between peasant and horse is constant.

> "At the posting-house to bait their horses — with the result that there might be no oats left for the night" [003-004]

This line captures how the horse's needs structure human schedules. A journey is measured in stages between posting-houses where horses can be rested and fed. The horse's hunger is as urgent as the peasant's own.

## Cattle and Rural Survival

Cattle represent survival itself. The novel's attention to livestock — cows, pigs, sheep — grounds the narrative in the material conditions of peasant life. "The cattle-yard were closely connected with the living quarters" [008-001] — human and animal life share the same space, the same smells, the same winter.

> "Summer-workers who were taken on merely until the cattle were driven home from pasturage" [014-001]

The seasonal rhythm of labor is organized around cattle: grazing in summer, stabling in winter. The health of the herd determines whether the winter can be survived. After a hard winter, the cattle emerge thin and weak — and the peasants who depend on them emerge equally depleted.

## Animals as Social Indicators

The quality of a man's horse reveals his social position. The small proprietor with his "insignificant little horse" [004-005] is a figure of pathos. The wealthy trader with fine horses commands respect. Tikhon Ilitch's rise from peasant to proprietor is marked by his acquisition of better animals.

> "Syery had forgotten to hobble the mare" [044-001] — even the casual treatment of animals reveals character. Syery's carelessness with the mare is a mark of his general incompetence.
