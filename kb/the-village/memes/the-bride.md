## Identity

Throughout the novel, the central female figure in its final movement is known only as "The Bride" — she has no given name in the narrative. She is a young peasant woman from Durnovka, and her forced marriage to Deniska constitutes the novel's climactic sequence. Bunin's refusal to name her is itself a statement: she is every peasant woman, defined not by identity but by the social transaction imposed upon her.

> "The bride, a young woman with fine figure, was a beauty in her own way" [010-001]

The Bride represents the complete powerlessness of peasant women in the village economy. She is a body to be transferred, a domestic unit to be reallocated, a womb to produce children — never an agent. Her beauty is noted only as an asset in the marriage market, not as a quality of her personhood.

## The Forced Marriage

Tikhon Ilitch arranges the marriage between the Bride and Deniska as a kind of experiment in social engineering. He sees the union not as a human event but as a corrective to the moral decay of the village — a way to impose order on the disorderly lives of his dependents. His motivation is not kindness but control.

The marriage is opposed by nearly everyone. Kuzma, who is asked to give the blessing, is horrified by the arrangement. The Bride herself has no voice in the matter — she is handed from one household to another, a transfer of property dressed in wedding ritual.

> "The yard-gates of poor brides are smeared with tar!" — a proverb recalled during the wedding preparations [012-013]

The tar reference evokes the village practice of publicly shaming women whose virtue is questioned. The marriage of the Bride is marked from the start by this atmosphere of shame and coercion, not celebration.

## The Wedding

The wedding sequence occupies much of the novel's final chapters. Bunin renders the ritual in its full physical detail: the seating of the bride and groom, the blessing, the candles, the sledges in the snow. The ceremonial surface is intact, but beneath it lies violence.

> "Kuzma bestowed the blessing on her. The bridegroom arrived with Vaska, Yakoff" [047-013]

Kuzma's reluctant participation in the blessing is one of the novel's most painful moments. He knows the marriage is a travesty, but as the literate man, the man of some status, he is expected to sanctify it. His compliance implicates him in the system that crushes the Bride.

## Aftermath

The wedding's conclusion leaves the Bride still unnamed, still voiceless. The sledge carries her away from Durnovka into a future we are not shown. What we know is that the marriage was not a resolution but a continuation — of the violence, the poverty, the quiet desperation that defines peasant life for women.

> "It was awkward for Kuzma, in his short old undercoat, to admit that the bride was taller than he" [048-009] — even the physical details of the scene emphasize the wrongness, the mismatch, the structural injustice of the entire arrangement.

## Significance

The Bride is Bunin's most concentrated symbol of the condition of peasant women in early 20th-century Russia. She is simultaneously a specific character — young, beautiful, trapped — and an archetype of systemic oppression. Her namelessness is the novel's final comment on a social order in which women are not individuals but functions.
