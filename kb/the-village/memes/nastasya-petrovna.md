# Nastasya Petrovna

Nastasya Petrovna appears only briefly in *The Village*, but her image is among the novel's most indelible. She "walked like a duck, with her toes turned inward, and waddled, thanks to her incessant pregnancies which always ended up with dead girl-babies" (002-005). In a single sentence, Bunin condenses a life of reproductive trauma that is both uniquely horrific and representative of the fate of rural women.

## Biography / Role in the Novel

Nastasya Petrovna is married to the Krasoffs' brother or cousin, and serves as hostess in the household — pouring vodka, managing the domestic sphere, and enduring pregnancy after failed pregnancy. Bunin gives her no interiority, no voice of her own. She exists as a body — "yellow, puffy" — defined by its reproductive failure. This narrative treatment is itself Bunin's commentary: in the world of *The Village*, a peasant woman's life is her body, and her body is a site of unrelieved suffering.
