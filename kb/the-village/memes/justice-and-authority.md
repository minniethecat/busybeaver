# Justice and Authority

Authority in *The Village* is arbitrary, predatory, and absolute. The rural police (*uriadniki*) who accompany Tikhon on tax-collection days are agents not of justice but of extraction. The law exists to enforce the obligations of the poor to the state and the rich, never the obligations of the rich to the poor. Bunin, who "took no part in politics" and "never touched upon questions connected with politics," nonetheless offers a devastating portrait of institutional power in rural Russia — a power that protects property, punishes poverty, and has no relationship whatsoever to justice.

## The Concept in the Novel

Authority figures in the novel — the rural police, the "commissary," the village policeman — are presented without the cartoonish villainy of political satire. They are simply functionaries, doing what the system requires. Their presence is most visible during tax collection, when "forced sale follows forced sale" and the full weight of the state bears down on the peasant household. The law is not an abstraction in *The Village*; it is a specific, recurring event — the autumn day when the police arrive and the peasant's remaining possessions are inventoried and sold.

The novel's opening establishes authority at its most extreme: Cavalry Captain Durnovo hunting a serf with wolfhounds. This is authority before the law, authority as pure force. The post-emancipation authority of the rural police is a domesticated version of the same power — no longer lethal (usually), but still arbitrary, still absolute, still serving the interests of the propertied.

## Key Passages

> "Indefatigably he followed up the rural police on those dull autumnal days when taxes are collected and forced sale follows forced sale."
> — (002-002)

The rural police are the visible arm of state authority, and their primary function is tax collection. Tikhon's commercial method — following the police — reveals the symbiosis between state power and private accumulation.
