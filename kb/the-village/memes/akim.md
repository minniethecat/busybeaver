# Akim

Akim is a Durnovka peasant who appears in one of the novel's most extended village scenes — the night in the orchard garden where Kuzma Krasoff, wandering after his return to Durnovka, encounters three watchmen cooking porridge over an open fire. Akim, afflicted with night-blindness and possessed of a mocking, lisping intelligence, dominates the scene with his caustic commentary on doctors, fate, and the absurdities of peasant life. He represents the peasant as Bunin found him: neither noble savage nor degraded brute, but a man of rough vitality, sardonic wit, and an indifference to strangers that Kuzma finds both fascinating and infuriating.

## Biography / Role in the Novel

When Kuzma approaches the watchmen's hut in the orchard, he finds three sick men: one a former baker dismissed for striking, one a middle-aged consumptive named Mitrofan, and Akim, who "was afflicted with night-blindness — he could not see well in the half-light of twilight" (031-021). Akim brings water, makes the fire, and carries the conversation with a mocking, absent-minded tone.

Kuzma, hungry and irritable, listens to Akim's banter with growing irritation. When the baker teases Akim about his lack of a belt — "You ought to buy yourself at least a belt, Akimushka" — Akim replies with mocking deflection: "Just you wait. When we've lived here with the proprietor through the summer, I'll buy you boots with a squeak in them" (031-023). The exchange reveals the casual, ironic economy of peasant life: no one has anything, and everyone mocks the pretense of having anything.

The scene turns darker when Kuzma brings up the subject of doctors. Akim's composure shatters: "'Doctors!' he pronounced the word in a specially vicious way — 'doktogga!' Doctors, brother, have an eye on their pockets. I'd let out his guts for him, for such a doctor, so I would!" (031-037). His rage — sudden, incoherent, and fuelled by personal experience — reveals the bitter distrust of institutional medicine among peasants who had no access to effective treatment.

When Kuzma shouts into the hut that Akim hasn't even asked who he is or where he comes from, Akim replies with perfect indifference: "And why should I want to know?" (032-002). The moment crystallises the gulf between Kuzma's literate self-consciousness and the peasant's existential incuriosity — a gulf that is Bunin's central subject.
