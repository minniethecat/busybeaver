## Overview

Trading Street is a commercial thoroughfare in an unnamed provincial town — most likely Voronezh, where Kuzma Krasoff spent nearly ten years. It appears in two distinct contexts: first, as an object of Kuzma's nostalgic memory during his years as an itinerant peddler, when even the arrival in a town "seemed an immense happiness" [016-001]; second, as the scene of a violent incident during the revolutionary unrest, when a drunken Cossack slashes a cabman with his sabre outside the public library [038-031].

The street thus carries a double resonance: it is both the remembered site of urban pleasure and the stage for the social violence that marks the novel's closing chapters.

## Kuzma's Nostalgia

During his years as a peddler, Kuzma's rare visits to town provided the only respite from the grinding hardship of the road — the jolting cart, the unwashed clothes, the nights in strange cottages. His memory of Trading Street is intensely sensory:

> Rest, acquaintances, the odour of bakeshops and iron roofs, the pavement on Trading Street, fresh white rolls and the Persian March on the mechanical organ of the "Kars" eating-house. The floors in the shops watered from a teapot, the wood-notes of a famous quail in front of Rudakoff's door, the smell of the fish shops in the bazaar, of fennel and coarse tobacco. [016-001]

The pavement itself is a detail of significance: a metalled street represents urban order, a surface that does not turn to mud, a world categorically different from the unpaved village lanes of Durnovka. The mechanical organ playing the "Persian March" places the street in a recognizably modern, commercial culture — the world of eating-houses, shop displays, and public entertainments.

## The Revolutionary Incident

In the period of unrest following the 1905 Revolution, Trading Street becomes the scene of casual violence. A company of Cossacks has been sent to the town to maintain order; the fireman's band plays in the park; the streets buzz with talk of the Duma, riots, and conflagrations.

> A whole company of kazaks had been sent. And the day before yesterday, on Trading Street, one of them, when drunk, went up to the window of the public library and made an insulting gesture to the young lady librarian. An elderly cabman, who was standing near by, began to reprove him, but the kazak jerked out his sabre from its scabbard, slashed the cabman's shoulder, and, cursing violently, rushed down the street in pursuit of the people who were walking and driving past. [038-031]

The incident juxtaposes the street's two identities: the public library (culture, order, the young lady librarian) against the drunken Cossack with his sabre (state violence, disorder). The cabman is slashed for the crime of reproving indecency; the bystanders flee "crazed with fear." Trading Street, once the site of Kuzma's happiest memories, becomes indistinguishable from the general chaos overtaking provincial Russia.

## Narrative Function

Trading Street is the only named urban street in the novel, and it appears exclusively in Kuzma's consciousness — first in memory, then in narrated present tense. It belongs to Kuzma's private geography of longing, the world he wished to inhabit rather than the one he was born into. Its two appearances, separated by the arc of the novel, measure the distance between the young peddler dreaming of education and the middle-aged man watching the old world come apart.
