# The Garden

**The Garden** at Durnovka encompasses both the ornamental manor garden and the working vegetable garden on the grounds of the Krasoff estate. It serves as a charged space in the novel — simultaneously a place of seasonal beauty, peasant labor, illicit romance, and tragedy, most notably as the site where [[the-bride|the Bride]] takes her own life.

## Location and Geography

The garden lies within the Durnovka estate grounds, stretching from the manor house through an orchard and fir plantation toward the threshing-floor [035-004]. The grounds include a vegetable garden with an apiary and old bushes, fragrant with silvery hoarfrost on autumn mornings [035-002] [029-017]. Goldfinches perch on dry stalks of burdocks in the vegetable garden [035-003]. The garden grows dark as evening falls, illuminated by the blue aurora of the Russian summer night [031-035].

## Role in the Narrative

### The Bride's Refuge and Tragedy

During October, while [[rodka]] worked on the railway line, [[the-bride|the Bride]] sat at home enduring her mother's reproaches, earning occasional kopeks working in the manor garden [012-003]. Her behavior was split: at home she said nothing and wept; in the garden she was "shrilly merry, shouted with laughter, sang songs" with [[donka-the-goat]], an extremely stupid and pretty girl who lived with the petty burgher who leased the garden [012-003].

The Bride struck up a friendship with the Goat and made advances toward the Goat's brother. It was the Goat who, after the cart with the petty burghers had rolled away, discovered the Bride "naked to the waist, hanging from a tree" [012-003]. The dawn was dreary and overcast; a fine rain whispered through the garden. The Goat wept as she untied the Bride, vowing that lightning might strike her before the village learned what had happened [012-003].

[[tikhon-krasoff]], hearing of the event from his laborers, was once more jarred out of his rut, awaiting the impending scandal [012-004].

### Kuzma's Sojourn

In Part II, Kuzma hears of a garden for lease and journeys to investigate [027-001]. When he arrives, the superintendent angrily tells him the garden was "leased long ago" [031-001]. Kuzma nevertheless spends the evening and night in the garden, in the old bathhouse [031-001]. He later asks [[akim]]: "Do you live in the garden?" — to which Akim replies simply: "I wanted to lease the garden" [031-014] [031-018].

### Seasonal Beauty and Hardship

The garden provides moments of sensory richness amid the novel's grimness: silvery hoarfrost on storehouses, the fresh fragrance of foliage, steppe grass, apples, and dew [035-002]. But this beauty is framed by the surrounding poverty — the "interminable Durnovka winter" with its cottages, ice holes in the pond, and sickness [041-004].
