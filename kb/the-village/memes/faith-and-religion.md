# Faith and Religion

Religion in Bunin's *The Village* is not a matter of doctrine or institution but of lived experience — a current of feeling that runs beneath the surface of rural life, sometimes surfacing in moments of genuine spiritual intensity, more often submerged in the daily grind of poverty, violence, and resignation. Bunin's treatment of faith is characteristically ambivalent: he neither affirms Orthodox Christianity as a redemptive force nor dismisses it as mere superstition. Instead, he shows religion as it is actually practiced — imperfectly, inconsistently, but ineradicably — by people for whom it is less a belief system than a language for expressing their deepest fears and hopes.

## The Concept in the Novel

Faith appears in *The Village* in several registers. There is the institutional presence of the Orthodox Church — priests, cathedrals, liturgies, feast days — which provides the temporal and spatial rhythm of village life. There is the popular piety of the peasants, expressed in signs of the cross, pilgrimages, icons in the corner of the hut, and a fatalistic submission to God's will. And there is the spiritual seeking of characters like Kuzma Krasoff, who wrestles with questions of meaning, justice, and the silence of God in the face of human suffering.

Bunin, drawing on his own experience of a "violent religious crisis" in childhood (described in the Autobiographical Note), portrays religious feeling as intense but unstable. Faith flares up in moments of terror or beauty and subsides in the face of daily hardship. The peasant who crosses himself before an icon in the morning may be drunk and violent by evening. Bunin does not condemn this inconsistency; he presents it as a fact of the Russian religious temperament — a capacity for extremes of devotion and indifference that mirrors the broader pattern of the Russian soul.

The novel's deepest religious moments occur not in churches but in cemeteries, on roads, and in solitary reflection. When Tikhon Ilitch visits the family graves, or when Kuzma contemplates the vastness of the Russian landscape, the religious impulse manifests as an inarticulate longing — a yearning for rest, meaning, and reconciliation that the institutional Church cannot satisfy.

## Historical Context

Russian Orthodoxy in the late imperial period was a state church, closely tied to the Tsarist regime and deeply conservative in its social outlook. The parish clergy were often poor, poorly educated, and held in low esteem by both the educated classes and the peasantry. At the same time, popular religion — the faith of the peasants — retained elements of pre-Christian folk belief, including reverence for holy fools, wonder-working icons, and sacred springs.

The decades before the revolution saw a religious revival among the Russian intelligentsia, with figures like Vladimir Solovyov, Sergei Bulgakov, and Pavel Florensky attempting to reconcile Orthodox theology with modern philosophy. But this revival barely touched the villages. For the peasants of Durnovka, religion remained what it had always been: a framework for enduring the unendurable.

## Key Passages

> "As a boy, I was deeply affected by the death of my little sister, and passed through a violent religious crisis, which left, however, no morbid traces whatsoever in my soul."
> — (P03-005)

Bunin's autobiographical confession establishes the experiential basis for the novel's treatment of religion. The "violent religious crisis" is mentioned and immediately qualified — "no morbid traces whatsoever" — suggesting both the intensity of the experience and Bunin's wariness of making too much of it. This tension between religious feeling and psychological detachment runs throughout *The Village*.

> "His brow furrowed even more severely; he removed his cap and made the sign of the cross. He was pale, and still weak from his illness. He recalled his childhood — his youth — Kuzma. He walked to the far corner of the cemetery where all his relatives were buried — father, mother, the sister who had died when a little girl."
> — (005-016)

Tikhon Ilitch's visit to the family graves is one of the novel's most quietly religious moments. The sign of the cross is made automatically, habitually, but the memory that follows — childhood, youth, the dead sister — transforms the gesture into something deeper. Bunin shows religious practice as a portal to memory and to an awareness of mortality that lies beyond doctrine.

> "However, does it mean that my soul is filled only with darkness and despair? Not at all. 'As the hart panteth after the water brooks, so panteth my soul after thee, O God!'"
> — (P03-015)

The Psalm quotation is Bunin's most direct statement of religious feeling, and it is placed at the end of the Autobiographical Note as a kind of coda. The image of the thirsting hart is one of pure longing — a desire for God that exists independently of belief, institution, or consolation. This is faith reduced to its essence.
