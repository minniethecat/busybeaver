## The Fallen Aristocrat

Zhikhareff is the son of a wealthy landowner who has fallen into complete ruin through drink. He appears on the bare road from the Ulianovka forest, a walking emblem of the decay of the Russian gentry class. His very dress signals his fall: a round-topped hat that is now a beggar's hat, a short roundabout coat wholly inadequate for the weather.

> "Tikhon Ilitch recognized Zhikhareff, the son of a wealthy landowner, who had long since become a thoroughgoing drunkard" [014-011]

For Tikhon, a self-made man who has clawed his way to wealth, the sight of Zhikhareff provokes a painful contraction of the heart. He instinctively reaches for a coin — "in case of need, give him half a ruble" — but also fears the man: "'Tis not worth while to anger the vagabond: he's a spiteful fellow."

## The Highway Encounter

Their meeting on the highway is rendered with Bunin's characteristic physical precision. Zhikhareff is a figure of grotesque decomposition:

> "His face was blue with the cold, puffy with drunkenness; his eyes were red, and his mustache disheveled. He had turned up the collar of his short coat, which was buttoned to the chin" [014-012]

He chews the dead stub of a cigarette between clenched jaws, his head thrown back in a posture of defiant arrogance despite his degradation. His rusty, dilapidated high boots project below trousers strained tight over his knees — the clothing of a gentleman reduced to near-rags.

Zhikhareff has been visiting the forester — drinking and smoking while waiting for a train. He asks Tikhon if they might meet at the station, adding pointedly: "I believe you are fond of putting something behind your collar yourself?" The remark is a needle: it reminds Tikhon that he, too, drinks, and that the distance between successful merchant and ruined gentleman is thinner than it appears.

## The Haunting

Zhikhareff does not merely pass through Tikhon's evening — he stays with him:

> "As he fell asleep, the thought of Zhikhareff once more recurred distressingly to his mind. And athwart his slumbers a persistent thought obsessed him: that the shop was being looted and the horses driven away" [015-001]

The encounter with Zhikhareff triggers Tikhon's deepest fears: the loss of property, the fragility of wealth, the possibility that he might end up like this ruined man. Zhikhareff functions as a dark mirror — what Tikhon might become if fortune turned against him. The dream of looting and theft that follows is not merely about Zhikhareff; it is about the precariousness of everything Tikhon has built.

## Significance

Zhikhareff appears only briefly but serves as a powerful symbol of social decay in Bunin's village world. He represents the dissolution of the old gentry class, the waste of inherited advantage, and the thin line between respectability and ruin. For Tikhon Krasoff, he is a walking warning — and a source of nightmares.
