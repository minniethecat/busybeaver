# The Gentry

The gentry (*dvoryanstvo*) of Bunin's *The Village* are a class in terminal decline. Deprived of serf labor by emancipation, burdened by debt, incapable of managing their estates as commercial enterprises, they are selling their land — parcel by parcel, meadow by meadow — to the likes of Tikhon Ilitch Krasoff. Bunin, himself a scion of the gentry, depicts this decline with a mixture of anthropological precision and elegiac melancholy. The gentry's fall is both an economic inevitability and a spiritual catastrophe.

## The Concept in the Novel

The gentry appear in *The Village* not as central characters but as background figures — "sun-browned members of the gentry class of small estates, in sleeveless peasant over-jackets and caps" who drift through the fair, indistinguishable in dress from the peasants they once commanded. This visual conflation of gentry and peasant is Bunin's most pointed comment on the class's decline: the gentry have not merely lost their wealth but their very distinctness as a social type.

The Durnovo family, mentioned in the novel's opening sentence, represents the old gentry at its most brutal. Cavalry Captain Durnovo hunted the Krasoffs' great-grandfather with wolfhounds — the ultimate expression of the serf-owner's absolute power over human life. But the Durnovos are gone, and their estate has passed — through the mechanisms of debt and forced sale — to Tikhon Krasoff, the descendant of the man they hunted. The historical reversal is complete, but it brings no liberation.

Bunin's own family history — detailed in the Autobiographical Note — makes him uniquely positioned to chronicle the gentry's decline. He describes his ancestors as "landed proprietors" who were "always connected with the people and with the land." This connection is presented as organic and valuable, a form of rootedness that the new commercial class lacks. But Bunin is too honest a writer to romanticize what his own novel shows to be indefensible.

## Historical Context

The Russian gentry was created by Peter the Great's Table of Ranks (1722) and consolidated by Catherine the Great's Charter to the Nobility (1785), which granted the gentry exclusive rights to own land and serfs. By the mid-nineteenth century, however, the gentry was in deep structural crisis. Most gentry estates were mortgaged to state credit institutions, and the income from serf-based agriculture was insufficient to service the debt.

Emancipation in 1861 was intended to rescue the gentry from bankruptcy by converting their serfs into a cash compensation stream, but the reform had the opposite effect. Deprived of free labor, lacking the capital and skills to farm commercially, and burdened by the expectations of a Europeanized lifestyle they could no longer afford, the gentry sold their land at an accelerating rate. By 1905, the year of the first Russian Revolution, gentry landownership had declined by nearly half from its 1861 peak. The revolution — during which peasants burned gentry estates across central Russia — accelerated the process, and by the time Bunin wrote *The Village* in 1910, the gentry as a landowning class was effectively finished.

## Key Passages

> "The great-grandfather of the Krasoffs, known by the manor-house servants under the nickname of 'The Gipsy,' was hunted with wolfhounds by Cavalry Captain Durnovo."
> — (001-001)

Before the gentry were pitiable, they were terrifying. This opening sentence establishes the moral baseline of gentry power: the absolute, arbitrary, lethal authority of the serf-owner. The Durnovos are the gentry in their pre-reform, pre-decline incarnation — the class that treats human beings as game.

> "Sun-browned members of the gentry class of small estates, in sleeveless peasant over-jackets and caps."
> — (004-004)

A single line that captures a historical transformation. These gentry are "sun-browned" — darkened by outdoor labor, not by leisure. They wear "peasant over-jackets and caps" — the clothing of the class they once ruled. The "small estates" are all that remain of their inheritance. Bunin does not judge; he observes. But the observation is devastating.

> "All my ancestors had always been connected with the people and with the land; they were landed proprietors."
> — (P03-004)

Bunin's description of his own ancestry is notably free of defensiveness. He acknowledges his family's connection to the land and people without justifying serfdom or romanticizing the gentry's role. The phrase "connected with the people and with the land" suggests an organic relationship that the new commercial class — represented by Tikhon Krasoff — cannot replicate.
