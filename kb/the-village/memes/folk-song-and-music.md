## Overview

Singing and folk song permeate the soundscape of Bunin's *The Village*, serving as one of the few expressive outlets available to the peasantry. Songs accompany work, mark social gatherings, and give voice to emotions that the inarticulate villagers cannot otherwise express. Yet Bunin's treatment of song is characteristically ambivalent: the songs are "wildly mournful" [003-003], the singers are often drunk or deformed, and the music reveals poverty of spirit as much as it does authentic folk culture.

## Songs as Collective Expression

The novel's first dramatic description of singing comes from a group of peasants passing through the village: "they dashed past with wildly mournful songs and shouts, their accordions wailing" [003-003]. The phrase "wildly mournful" captures the dominant tonality of peasant music—expressive but unrefined, collective but directionless. In Durnovka, Kuzma "set about singing that song with more gusto than ever" after hearing it at the Fair [012-001], suggesting that songs circulate orally across the countryside, part of a shared but unrecorded cultural inheritance.

## The Blind Singer as Folk Authority

The blind man who appears at Makarka's gathering is the novel's most vivid embodiment of folk musical culture. He chants with an oracular gravity, his voice blending with Makarka's in a rough polyphony [018-005, 018-007, 018-011]. The content of his song—"the orphaned and the wandering, how the Lord had given wisdom to the ignorant" [018-005]—connects song to a world-view in which suffering carries spiritual meaning. The blind singer, like the traditional *kaliki perekhozhie* (wandering pilgrim-minstrels), represents an oral culture that persists alongside, and beneath, the official literacy of church and state.

## Music and Melancholy

Song in *The Village* is almost never joyful. On a winter evening, peasants "sat, weaving their bast shoes, and singing" [022-017]—the image of repetitive labor merged with song suggests not celebration but endurance. Kuzma listens to a nightingale's singing "cheeringly" [031-020], but the natural music of the bird contrasts with the human music, which remains tethered to toil and drink. Even at the wedding, the singing is "lewd" rather than festive [045-025].

## The Poverty of Repertoire

Kuzma, the novel's most self-aware character, recognizes the limitations of peasant song. "But with us there is only one song," he remarks bitterly [011-037], implying that the village's musical culture, like its material culture, is impoverished—repetitive, narrow, incapable of development. The same observation applies to the broader oral culture: "And the songs? The same thing, always the same" [013-002]. Folk song, which Romantic nationalists celebrated as the soul of the people, appears here as another symptom of the village's stagnation.
