## Overview

Although *The Village* is set in the aftermath of the 1905 Revolution and registers the political currents of its time, Bunin portrays modernity not as progress but as a distant, often incomprehensible disruption to village life. Newspapers, the State Duma, revolutionary talk, and grain speculations all intrude upon Durnovka, but they are absorbed into the village's own unchanging patterns of poverty, drink, and fatalism.

## The Revolution as Distant Rumor

The Russo-Japanese War and the 1905 Revolution appear as paired, almost interchangeable events that arrive from outside the village: "the war with Japan and the revolution" [007-001] are mentioned in a single breath, as catastrophes that happen to the peasantry rather than movements they initiate. "Everybody was talking about the revolution, the Revolution, while round about everybody was living in the same old way," Kuzma observes [007-005], capturing the chasm between political discourse and village reality. The revolution is a topic of talk—the local newspaper was "closed down three times" [033-002]—but it does not alter the material conditions of peasant life.

## The State Duma and Political Awakening

The State Duma serves in the novel as a symbol of the political world that the peasantry can discuss but not influence. Peasants ask one another "Have they assembled that Duma yet, merchant?" [029-013] and debate "what one expects to receive from the Duma" [032-003]. Tikhon Krasoff cynically observes that some peasants spend their autumn "waiting for favours from the Duma" [038-032], suggesting that political reform has produced only passive expectation rather than genuine empowerment.

## Newspapers and the New Literacy

Newspapers represent the ambiguous arrival of literacy and public discourse in the village. Kuzma "wrote articles for the city newspapers" [028-002] and is known for "bewildering" the local grain dealers with his journalism [025-001]. Newssheets "rustled in the hands of many" at the station [028-001], a sign that information from the wider world has penetrated even the rural backwater. Yet Kuzma's brother dismisses him as someone who merely "skinned his verses from books and newspapers" [011-004], and the papers themselves are described as hurled onto tables in frustration [033-003]—the new media arouse passion without providing agency.

## The Persistence of the Old

Against these fragile signs of modernization, the village's fundamental institutions remain unshaken. The Fair continues, the Orthodox Church maintains its rituals, and the landed gentry's memorial "reigned over this town and its whole county" [004-009] as it always has. Modernity in *The Village* is not a transformation but a surface agitation upon a deep, stagnant continuity.
