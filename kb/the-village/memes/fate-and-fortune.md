# Fate and Fortune

Belief in fate permeates *The Village* as both a cultural inheritance and a psychological necessity. From Tikhon Ilitch's desperate consultation of the Oracle to peasants who accept calamity as divine decree, fatalism serves as the village's unofficial philosophy—a way of making sense of suffering that, in a world without remedy, is perhaps the only comfort available. Bunin exposes this mindset with a combination of ethnographic precision and quiet irony, showing how the language of destiny both explains and enables the harshness of rural life.

## The Concept in the Novel

The most concentrated treatment of fortune-telling and fate occurs early in the novel, when a "New Complete Oracle and Magician" appears in the Krasoff household. Nastasya Petrovna consults it obsessively: "Does my husband love me?" The Oracle answers, "He loves you as a dog loves a stick." "How many children shall I have?"—"You are fated to die: the field must be cleared of weeds" [003-006, 003-008]. The Oracle's answers are "either insulting, menacing, or senseless" [003-004], yet the characters persist in consulting it—a ritual of divination that mirrors the broader pattern of seeking meaning in the arbitrary.

The Oracle episode establishes the novel's approach to fate: people search for signs and patterns, but what they find only confirms their anxieties. The fortune-telling is simultaneously absurd and psychologically revealing—the questions asked expose the askers' deepest fears more than any answer could.

Bunin frames the theme at a higher level in his Autobiographical Note. He writes that he "had felt a vague fear for the fate of Russia" [P03-014] while writing the novel—a presentiment that "reality... has justified my apprehensions beyond all measure" [P03-014]. The word "prophetic" appears as a label others have given his work, and Bunin neither fully claims nor disclaims it. The novelist becomes a kind of reluctant oracle himself.

The theme of fatalism pervades individual character arcs. Tikhon Ilitch's lifelong obsession with having children ends in bitter acceptance; Kuzma's literary ambitions crumble into poverty and disappointment. When Kuzma listens to the peasant Akim and reflects that "his thinking had been done for him... a thousand years ago" [032-023], he identifies the deepest form of fatalism—not conscious belief in fate, but an inherited passivity so complete that the question of agency never arises.

Makar-the-Pilgrim represents folk prophecy in its rawest form: he "rendered himself famous throughout the entire county by his prophecies—prophecies so ill-omened that people began to dread his visits as they dreaded fire" [P01-X]. The fear of prophecy coexists with its inevitability—the village cannot resist consulting its oracles, even knowing the answers will be terrible.

## Historical Context

Peasant fatalism in late imperial Russia had deep roots in Orthodox Christianity, which emphasized submission to God's will, and in the material conditions of rural life, where weather, disease, and state power were genuinely beyond individual control. The emancipation of 1861, followed by the upheavals of industrialization and revolution, reinforced a sense that historical forces were sweeping individuals along without their consent. Bunin's treatment of fate differs from both the romantic fatalism of earlier Russian literature and the Marxist determinism then gaining currency—it is more ethnographic, grounded in the everyday practices (fortune-telling, prayer, pilgrimage) through which ordinary people negotiated with the unknown.
