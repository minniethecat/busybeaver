# Education and Literacy

In a world of near-universal illiteracy, Kuzma Krasoff is an anomaly: a self-educated peasant who reads books, writes verse, and thinks about history and philosophy. His education is both his distinction and his curse — it separates him from his brother, his class, and his people, while providing no practical means of escape. Through Kuzma, Bunin explores the tragedy of the autodidact: the man who knows enough to understand his own degradation but not enough to transcend it.

## The Concept in the Novel

Kuzma's literacy is the defining fact of his character. Unlike Tikhon, who has mastered the commercial arts of bargaining, buying, and exploiting, Kuzma has mastered nothing useful. He reads, he thinks, he writes — and none of it matters. The novel charts his progressive disillusionment: from the hope that books might provide a key to understanding Russian life, to the recognition that the life he seeks to understand is beyond the reach of any book.

Bunin's treatment of education is characteristically double-edged. He respects Kuzma's intellectual seriousness while showing its practical impotence. Kuzma's learning makes him more sensitive to the suffering around him — and therefore more miserable. Tikhon, who cannot read a book, is the more effective man. The implication is uncomfortable: in the world of *The Village*, literacy is a disability.

## Key Passages

> "Kuzma, who was almost of an age, also took to peddling. They drove about in a peasant cart which had a carved front and a roofed, shop-like body."
> — (001-003)

Kuzma begins where Tikhon begins — in the peddling cart. His intellectual development is a departure from the family trajectory, and the novel never lets us forget that his learning rests on the same foundation of petty commerce and rural poverty as Tikhon's wealth.
