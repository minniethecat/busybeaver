The inn, also referred to as Khodoff's lodging-house, is a corner building in the provincial town where Kuzma Krasoff lives during a period of profound poverty and drift. It is a place of stifling heat, fleas, and intolerable boredom — a physical embodiment of Kuzma's stagnation between his failed literary ambitions and his eventual summons to Durnovka.

## Location & Geography

The lodging-house stands on a corner in the town, its walls baking in the sun through the long drought from May until August [033-001]. It overlooks a dusty, glaring white street. Nearby are the bazaar, Avdyeef's eating-house in the Women's Bazaar, and the town park where Kuzma sometimes wanders. The building has a hayloft where tenants might sleep, but it is infested with fleas and pervaded by the odour of the manure-yard [033-001].

## Role in the Narrative

The inn serves as the setting for Kuzma's nadir. After the failure of his self-published book of verses and the loss of his last employment, he sleeps in the common room of Khodoff's lodging-house through the winter [025-001]. He spends his days in eating-houses, picking up rumours of work that never materializes. By summer, the inn becomes a prison of heat and tedium: "Time flowed on in intolerable boredom. There were not even any visitors at the inn" [033-007].

The inn is also the place where Kuzma receives Tikhon Ilitch's note — delivered by Duck-Headed Matty — which breaks the noose tightening around him and summons him to Durnovka [033-007]. It thus marks the threshold between Kuzma's urban destitution and his reluctant return to rural life under his brother's patronage.

The innkeeper is described as fat, weak, and nervous as an old woman; he prohibits smoking in the hayloft [033-001]. The only guest during the whole of July is a youthful, eccentric deacon whose name, Krasnobaeff, is written up on the board in Latin as Benedictoff [033-007].

## Key Passages

> "Rain fell now and then in the countryside, but in town, day after day from May until August, an infernal drought held uninterrupted sway. The lodging-house, a corner building, baked in the sun. At night one's blood hammered in one's head from the stifling heat, and every noise which came through the open windows wakened one with a start."
> — [033-001]

> "Time flowed on in intolerable boredom. There were not even any visitors at the inn. During the whole of July the only person who put up there was a youthful deacon, rather a queer fellow, after the pattern of seminary queer sticks."
> — [033-007]

> "He slept in the 'common room,' among strangers, chance people who came and went; in the morning he crawled in the heat about the bazaar and to the eating-houses, where he picked up rumours concerning jobs; after dinner, he took a nap, then seated himself at the window and read Kostomaroff's *History*."
> — [025-001]
