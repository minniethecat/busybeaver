## Overview

The traditional peasant wedding occupies a prominent place in Bunin's *The Village* as both a social institution and a dramatic centerpiece. The novel's culminating chapters (Chapters 46–48) are devoted almost entirely to the preparation and celebration of a wedding, rendering it the most sustained ritual sequence in the novel. Through the wedding, Bunin exposes the economic calculations, class tensions, and folk customs that shape village life.

## The Wedding as Economic Transaction

Marriage in Durnovka is fundamentally an economic arrangement. Tikhon Krasoff orchestrates the marriage of the Bride (the cook nicknamed "the Bride") to Deniska, a dissolute peasant, and bears the full cost himself [044-005]. He views the match as an act of charity—or of spite—having earlier taunted the Bride with the prospect of marrying "one of those fellows" [044-004]. The household preparations reveal the material stakes: "what a wedding we made of it! As for the expense…" Tikhon reflects with mingled pride and resentment [038-037].

## Folk Customs and Religious Ritual

The wedding sequence is rich with traditional folk practices. The bride and bridegroom are seated "beneath the holy pictures" for the ceremonial blessing [046-023]. An ikona is used to make the sign of the cross over the couple mechanically in the air [047-016]. Candles are held "only by the bridegroom and the bride" during the rite [048-005]. These details establish the wedding as deeply embedded in Orthodox practice, yet the mechanical, almost perfunctory quality of the gestures suggests a faith hollowed out by custom.

## Social Discord at the Feast

The wedding feast reveals the fissures within village society. Kuzma Krasoff attends reluctantly, having "firmly made up his mind" against the match [044-005]. The drunken revelry echoes other festive scenes in the novel where alcohol fuels conflict rather than communion—earlier, a man "died from over-drinking at the wedding" at a previous celebration [010-001]. The Bride herself is described as "grieving with woe in marriage" [047-005], underscoring the human cost beneath the ritual surface.
