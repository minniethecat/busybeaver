# The Russo-Turkish War (1877–78)

The Russo-Turkish War of 1877–1878 was the most significant Russian military engagement between the Crimean War (1853–1856) and the Russo-Japanese War (1904–1905). Fought primarily in the Balkans and the Caucasus, it resulted in a Russian victory, the restoration of Bulgarian statehood after nearly five centuries of Ottoman rule, and the consolidation of Romanian, Serbian, and Montenegrin independence. For the generation of Russians depicted in *The Village*, the war was the defining event of the post-emancipation era — a moment of national glory that stood in sharp contrast to the poverty, stagnation, and social strife of rural life.

## Historical Background

The war had its immediate origins in the Balkan uprisings of 1875–1876 against Ottoman rule. The brutal Ottoman suppression of the Bulgarian April Uprising of 1876 provoked outrage across Europe and particularly in Russia, where pan-Slavic sentiment was strong. Tsar Alexander II declared war on the Ottoman Empire in April 1877. Russian forces crossed the Danube, besieged the strategic city of Plevna, and after months of hard fighting broke through the Balkan Mountains to threaten Constantinople itself.

The Treaty of San Stefano (March 1878) imposed a punitive peace on the Ottomans, creating a large autonomous Bulgarian state under Russian influence. The subsequent Congress of Berlin (June–July 1878), convened under pressure from the other Great Powers, substantially reduced the gains of San Stefano but confirmed the independence of Romania, Serbia, and Montenegro and established an autonomous Bulgarian principality. The war cost approximately 200,000 Russian lives, mostly from disease, and left a complex legacy: national triumph abroad, deepening social crisis at home, and the acceleration of the revolutionary movements that would culminate in the assassination of Alexander II in 1881.

## The Event in *The Village*

In Bunin's novel, the Russo-Turkish War appears not as a directly narrated event — none of the characters fought in it — but as a presence in the imaginative landscape of rural Russia. It is preserved in popular print culture, in the cheap lithograph portraits of war heroes that decorate peasant cottages, and in the conversation of men who, though they never saw battle, measure their own times against the remembered glory of the Turkish campaign.

The war functions in the novel as a reference point for a generation that came of age in its aftermath. Tikhon and Kuzma Krasoff, born around the 1850s, would have been young men during the war years. The conflict belongs to their parents' and grandparents' generation — a time when Russia acted with decisive force on the world stage, before the humiliations of the Japanese war and the revolutionary upheaval of 1905. The gap between the heroic lithographs and the degraded reality of the present — the poverty, the drinking, the "vast wretchedness" of the fair — is one of the novel's unstated but pervasive themes.

For Kuzma, the intellectual brother, the war is an object of conversation and reflection. He discusses it with Balashkin, the accordion-player freethinker, and the discussion becomes a vehicle for Kuzma's larger preoccupation: what is Russia, and what has it become? The war — a moment when Russia acted as a great power — provides a contrast to the paralysis and pettiness of the present.

## Key Passages

> "On the walls hung full-length portraits of heroes of the Russo-Turkish war, and underneath was an inscription: 'Our glorious warriors, who have covered themselves with immortal fame, and who have inscribed their names in the history of the fatherland.'"
> — [016-003]

The cheap lithographs, with their formulaic inscription, are Bunin's image of how history reaches the village: not as experience but as decoration, a set of fading images on a cottage wall. The gap between "immortal fame" and the grinding poverty of the present could hardly be wider.

> "They were discussing at the moment — the Russo-Turkish war: about how —"
> — [023-008]

The unfinished sentence is characteristic of Bunin's treatment of the war. The discussion trails off, incomplete, as if the subject cannot sustain the attention it briefly commands. The war is a topic of conversation, not a living memory — something to talk about when there is nothing else to say.

> "The father of the Krasoffs was a petty huckster. He roved about the county, lived for a time in Durnovka, set up a pothouse and a little shop, failed, took to drink, returned to the town, and soon died."
> — [001-003]

The Krasoff father's biography — rootless, alcoholic, ending in early death — belongs to the same generation that produced the heroes of the Turkish war. Bunin does not make the contrast explicit; he does not need to. The father's life is the war's implicit counter-narrative: here is what the fatherland actually gave its peasant sons.

> "'Tis only forty-five years since serfdom was abolished — so what can be expected of the people? The people themselves!"
> — [028-013]

In the context of Kuzma's larger outburst (which references military history among other subjects), the Russo-Turkish War appears as one element in a historical panorama that includes serfdom, the Mongol yoke, and the endless succession of conflicts that have shaped — or deformed — the Russian national character.
