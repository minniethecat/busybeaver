## A Farmhand's Life

Koshel is a farmhand on Tikhon Ilitch's estate, a man defined entirely by his labor and his survival instincts. Unlike the novel's major characters who brood over the meaning of their existence, Koshel simply works. He appears in scenes of physical toil — dragging frozen straw, walking the farmyard, performing the endless tasks that keep the estate functioning.

> "The farmhand Koshel, on the other hand, was a man not given to sentiment" [036-010]

This description captures Koshel's essential quality: practicality without sentiment. In a novel filled with characters who drink, despair, and philosophize, Koshel represents the peasant who simply endures. He does not question his condition — he navigates it.

## Relations with Others

Koshel's interactions with other characters reveal the web of petty conflict and mutual dependence that structures life on the estate. When he encounters the "witch" (a local woman suspected of sorcery), his response is neither fear nor credulity but casual dismissal.

> "'That witch waked up early in the morning,' Koshel observed dryly" [036-013]

Koshel's matter-of-factness extends to the supernatural. In a village where superstition holds powerful sway (see [[superstition-and-folk-belief]]), Koshel's refusal to be impressed marks him as a distinct type — the peasant whose materialism is so complete that even folk belief cannot gain purchase on him.

His relationship with Sery, the more colorful and chaotic peasant, is one of practical contrast. Where Sery drinks and philosophizes, Koshel works. The two represent complementary peasant types: the dreamer and the doer.

> "Koshel walked slowly about the farmyard" [036-018] — his presence is measured, unhurried, steady.

## Koshel and the Novel's Themes

Koshel is not a major character, but his presence illuminates several of the novel's central themes. He represents the laboring peasant body — the human engine that powers the rural economy. His relationship to Tikhon Ilitch is purely contractual; there is no loyalty, no love, only the exchange of labor for survival.

> "Koshel was dragging frozen, snow-coated straw from the truck-sled" [040-003]

The image is emblematic: Koshel in the snow, dragging straw, performing the work that makes the estate function while the novel's major characters are consumed by their dramas. He is the background against which the tragedy of Durnovka plays out — necessary, constant, and almost entirely taken for granted.
