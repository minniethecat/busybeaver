# The Famine

In *The Village*, famine is not a single catastrophic event with a calendar date but a recurring condition — the inevitable terminus of a rural economy organized around subsistence agriculture on inadequate land. Crop failure, livestock disease, the exhaustion of winter stores before the spring planting: these are the seasonal rhythms of peasant life as Bunin depicts them. Famine is the background against which all other events occur, the baseline of scarcity that shapes every character's calculations and fears.

## Historical Background

Famine was a periodic reality of rural Russian life throughout the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. The most devastating famine of the late imperial period occurred in 1891–1892, when crop failures across the black-earth region affected an estimated 35 million people and caused approximately 400,000 deaths. The government's response — belated, inadequate, and hampered by a commitment to grain exports that continued even as peasants starved — became a rallying point for the radical intelligentsia and a source of enduring peasant distrust of the state.

Smaller-scale famines and localized crop failures occurred with grim regularity. The agrarian crisis was structural: the peasant allotments distributed at emancipation were too small to support a family, agricultural techniques remained primitive, and the redemption payments imposed by the 1861 settlement left peasants with no reserves against a bad harvest. When the crops failed — and they failed often — the peasantry had nothing to fall back on.

## The Event in *The Village*

Bunin does not dramatize famine as a set-piece catastrophe. Instead, he registers it through the texture of daily life: the thinness of the soup, the anxiety with which the first spring shoots are watched, the desperation with which a peasant clings to three desyatini of land that cannot feed his family. Famine is the novel's atmospheric condition — always present, always threatening, always just one bad harvest away.

The novel's larger argument about the Russian peasantry is inseparable from this condition of chronic hunger. The violence, the drinking, the superstition, the apparent callousness — all are, in Bunin's implicit analysis, adaptations to a world in which starvation is a constant possibility. A peasant who has watched his children go hungry through a long winter is not, Bunin suggests, a peasant who can afford the luxury of moral refinement.

## Key Passages

> "They do nothing but discuss the Duma, riots and conflagrations, and meanwhile the people are perishing from disease and hunger."
> — [027-006]

Hunger is listed alongside disease as the elemental threat to peasant existence — a threat that continues regardless of whatever is being debated in the distant capital. The sentence compresses the novel's political argument: the institutions of reform are irrelevant as long as the people are dying of hunger.

> "The peasant women stood for days at a time in the dark bottle-green water, beating the hemp, their legs blue with cold, their faces pinched with hunger."
> — [040-002]

The detail is Bunin at his most devastating. The women stand in freezing water, working hemp, their faces "pinched with hunger" — a phrase that registers famine not as an event but as a face, a posture, a way of being in the world. They work because they must; they hunger because they have always hungered.

> "He thought of the peasant women lying in their cottages, the little children, the cattle in the byres. He thought of the terrible winter nights, the wolves, the blizzards that swept the steppe."
> — [020-082]

Kuzma's nocturnal vision of the peasant world joins hunger to cold, wolves, and the merciless elements. Famine is not separable from the larger condition of vulnerability that defines peasant existence. The children, the cattle, the women in their cottages — all are exposed to the same implacable forces.
