# The Peasantry

The peasantry is not merely a subject of Bunin's *The Village* — it is the novel's very texture. Every page is saturated with the sights, sounds, and smells of peasant life: the creak of cart wheels on rutted roads, the smoke of wood-fired stoves, the rough cloth of homespun overcoats, the cadence of rural speech. Bunin, writing in 1910 from decades of firsthand observation, produced what many critics consider the most unflinching portrayal of the Russian peasant ever committed to fiction. The novel refuses both the romantic idealization of the peasant found in Tolstoy and the polemical abstraction of the revolutionary intelligentsia. Instead, it presents the peasantry as they are: hungry, superstitious, resilient, cruel, tender, and trapped in a cycle of poverty that no reform has yet broken.

## The Concept in the Novel

Bunin's peasantry is not a monolith. The novel carefully distinguishes between different strata and types: the landless laborer eking out a subsistence, the rising kulak who apes the gentry, the village elder who wields petty authority, the peasant woman who bears the double burden of field work and domestic violence, and the rural peddler suspended between town and country. This granular attention to social reality is what separates *The Village* from both the romantic pastoral and the revolutionary tract.

The novel's two central figures embody the poles of peasant experience. [[tikhon-krasoff]] is the peasant who rises — a descendant of serfs who becomes a merchant and landowner, yet finds no peace in his ascent. [[kuzma-krasoff]] is the peasant intellectual, the self-taught thinker who understands the tragedy of his class but is powerless to escape it. Together, the Krasoff brothers map the entire terrain of peasant possibility in late imperial Russia: the drive for self-betterment, the shame of humble origins, the hunger for dignity, and the gravitational pull of violence and drink that drags so many back down.

The village of [[durnovka]] serves as the novel's laboratory for observing peasant life at scale. Through its collective portrait — the threshing floors, the pothouse brawls, the winter weddings, the spring famines — Bunin builds a composite image of a class in transition. The emancipation of the serfs in 1861 set this transition in motion, but forty-five years later the destination remains unclear. Some peasants acquire land and join the rural middle class. Others sink deeper into debt and despair. Most simply endure, generation after generation, in a rhythm governed by the agricultural calendar and the unpredictable violence of nature and human nature alike.

## Historical Context

At the time of *The Village*'s publication in 1910, the Russian Empire was still overwhelmingly agrarian. Over eighty percent of the population belonged to the peasant estate, a legal category inherited from the pre-reform era that carried specific obligations and disabilities. The Emancipation of 1861 had freed the serfs in law but left them bound in practice — to the village commune (the *mir*), to redemption payments that stretched decades into the future, and to a system of land tenure that fragmented holdings into strips too small to support a family.

The decades between emancipation and the 1905 Revolution saw the gradual emergence of a stratified peasantry. A small minority — the so-called "kulaks" — accumulated land, credit, and influence, becoming rural employers and moneylenders. The majority remained at subsistence level, vulnerable to crop failure, livestock disease, and the exactions of tax collectors and local officials. Below them was a growing class of landless laborers who hired themselves out by the day or season. The 1905 Revolution, though suppressed, sent a shock through the countryside: peasants burned manor houses, seized land, and announced that the old order was finished. The Stolypin reforms that followed attempted to create a stable class of peasant proprietors, but the reforms had barely begun to take effect when *The Village* appeared. Bunin's novel captures this precise historical moment — after the upheaval of 1905, before the reforms of Stolypin had time to reshape the land, and with the deeper cataclysm of 1917 still over the horizon.

## Key Passages

> "Dusty, ground to powder by feet, wheels, and hoofs, littered and covered with dung, the pasture was already being deserted — the Fair was dispersing. But Tikhon Ilitch, as if with deliberate intent to spite someone or other, persisted in keeping his unsold horses there in the heat, and sat on and on in his cart. It seemed as if he were overwhelmed not so much by illness as by the spectacle of the great poverty, the vast wretchedness which, not for the first time, he was beholding all around him."
> — [004-009]

This early passage establishes the novel's visual method: Bunin catalogues the physical evidence of peasant poverty — the dust, the dung, the unsold livestock — and lets it accumulate until the weight becomes almost unbearable. Tikhon Ilitch's paralysis is less illness than moral exhaustion, the fatigue of a man who has climbed out of poverty only to find himself surrounded by its intractable reality.

> "An idle, chattering throng of peasant men and women surged about in the heat, amid carts, crowds of horses, booths, and a strong smell of the fair. There were peasants from all parts: from the northern districts, from Tula, from the region of the Don Cossacks."
> — [004-003]

The fair at Vorgol serves as the novel's gathering point, a temporary city where the peasant world assembles itself in all its variety. Bunin distinguishes peasants by region, by dress, by the goods they bring to market — the passage is almost ethnographic in its precision, yet it never descends into mere documentation. The "idle, chattering throng" is humanity in its unedited state, and Bunin's gaze is unsparing.

> "Summer and winter alike in a peasant overcoat which had turned green with age, in bast-slippers, with tousled, matted hair which looked as if it had never seen water — Syery was one of those Russians who seem to live only in order to perpetuate their kind."
> — [024-002]

The portrait of Syery (Gray) is Bunin at his most pitiless. The character's very name suggests indistinction, a man who has been worn down to something barely human by the grind of rural existence. Yet the observation is not merely cruel — it is diagnostic. Bunin is asking what conditions produce a man like Syery, a creature of animal survival with no horizon beyond biological reproduction.

> "On one and the same day, the peasants had risen through almost the whole district. They had burned and sacked several manors, and then quieted down. The local authorities, as is always the case, became excited, an investigation was started, the Uhlans came."
> — [009-009]

The memory of peasant rebellion haunts the novel's background. This passage — describing the unrest that swept the countryside during the 1905 Revolution — exposes the fragility of the rural social order. The peasants rise, they burn, they quiet down, and the mechanisms of state repression grind into motion. Bunin's tone is almost offhand, but the implications are seismic.

> "The father of the Krasoffs was a petty huckster. He roved about the county, lived for a time in Durnovka, set up a pothouse and a little shop, failed, took to drink, returned to the town, and soon died. After serving for a while in shops his sons, Tikhon and Kuzma, who were almost of an age, also took to peddling."
> — [001-003]

The Krasoff genealogy, compressed into a single paragraph at the novel's opening, traces in miniature the trajectory of the post-emancipation peasantry: rootlessness, petty commerce, failure, drink, early death, and the uncertain inheritance passed to the next generation. The sons will rise further than the father, but the father's fate is never far behind them.

> "Kuzma recalled his childhood — his youth — Kuzma. He walked to the far corner of the orchard, to the edge of the ravine, and sat down on the grass. How long ago all that was! And how wretched, how hopelessly wretched, was that childhood and youth!"
> — [005-016]

Kuzma's memory of his own peasant childhood is not nostalgic but accusatory. The orchard and the ravine are the landscape of his youth, but they evoke not warmth but wretchedness. For Bunin, there is no pastoral golden age to recover — the peasant past was as brutal as the peasant present, and sentimentality about it is a form of dishonesty.

> "The storm raged long, and Kuzma could not sleep. He thought of the peasant women lying in their cottages, the little children, the cattle in the byres. He thought of the terrible winter nights, the wolves, the blizzards that swept the steppe."
> — [020-082]

Here Kuzma's imagination extends beyond his own suffering to the collective vulnerability of the peasant world. The cottages, the children, the cattle — all are exposed to the same merciless elements. The passage is a moment of genuine compassion, rare in the novel, and it reveals Kuzma as the character through whom Bunin channels his own complicated sympathy for the peasantry.

> "Some of the peasants were singing. Lord, what voices! Thick, powerful, with a kind of savage, ancient, animal quality. But the song itself was so sad, so endlessly, hopelessly sad."
> — [026-002]

The peasant voice — literal and metaphorical — is one of the novel's recurring motifs. Bunin hears in peasant singing something primal and pre-Christian, a sound that has survived centuries of bondage and hardship. The adjective "animal" is not dismissive but reverent: it acknowledges a vitality that cultivated art has lost.
