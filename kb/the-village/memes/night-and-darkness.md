# Night and Darkness

Night in *The Village* is not merely the absence of daylight. It is a distinct realm with its own rules, its own dangers, and its own possibilities. When darkness falls, the rigid hierarchies of the daylight village loosen: the inn fills with drinkers, travelers stumble along mud roads, suspicions become terrors, and the darkness itself becomes a character — impenetrable, animate, menacing.

## The Concept in the Novel

Bunin structures much of the novel around the alternation of day and night. The daylight world is the world of labor, commerce, and social visibility — the fields being ploughed, the fairground bustling, Tikhon inspecting his properties. But night dissolves these structures. In darkness, the peasants gather at the inn to drink and fight; Tikhon's anxieties surface; Kuzma reads through the night, unable to sleep; and the village's latent violence — the poison in the well, the arson, the beatings — is plotted and enacted.

The quality of Bunin's darkness is particular. It is not a romantic darkness or a gothic darkness but a rural darkness — the absolute black of a peasant hut without candles, the "impenetrable darkness" through which horses gallop at top speed, the darkening west against which columns of fire rise. This is a darkness born of poverty as much as nature: the darkness of homes without light, of roads without lamps, of lives lit only by shrine-lamps and the moon.

## Key Passages

> "It was so dark you could not see your hand before your face." [015-001] — Bunin's recurring formula for absolute darkness; the peasant hut as a space of sensory deprivation.

> "Through the impenetrable darkness, his splendid stallion, an animal worthy of a hero." [015-002] — Darkness as an active obstruction; even wealth (the stallion) cannot dispel it.

> "They planned to set fire to Durnovka some fine night, or poison the water." [009-009] — Darkness as the medium of conspiracy; violence belongs to the night.

> "On the night before the Feast of St. Michael..." [012-004] — Night as the threshold of sacred time; the vigil that precedes the holy day.

## Night as Social Space

The night dissolves daylight hierarchies. In the inn at night, Tikhon and his peasants drink together. On the road at night, a traveler's identity is obscured — he might be a beggar or a landowner. The darkness is democratic in its erasure of social markers, and this is precisely what makes it dangerous to those who depend on those markers. Tikhon fears the night; Kuzma, the intellectual who belongs nowhere, finds in it a strange companionship.

Night is also the time of storytelling, of the tales that transmit peasant culture — the legends of Vladimir, the songs, the superstitions. The novel's folklore lives in darkness; daylight has no use for it.

## Darkness and Poverty

Bunin never lets the reader forget that the novel's darkness is, in large part, an artifact of poverty. The peasant hut is dark because candles cost money. The road is dark because there are no streetlamps. The darkness that envelops Durnovka is not a cosmic condition but an economic one — and in this sense it is continuous with the novel's broader critique of rural deprivation.
