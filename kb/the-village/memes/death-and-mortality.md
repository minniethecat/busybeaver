# Death and Mortality

Death haunts every page of *The Village*. Characters die in the novel's opening sentence (the great-grandfather hunted with wolfhounds) and continue dying throughout: the Krasoff father, the little sister, Nastasya Petrovna's "dead girl-babies," the countless peasants claimed by famine and disease. Bunin presents death not as a dramatic culmination but as the ordinary texture of rural existence — a fact so pervasive that it shapes the living more than the dead.

## The Concept in the Novel

Death in the novel operates at several levels. There is the ancestral death that opens the novel — the great-grandfather killed by his owner, an act of violence that establishes the moral coordinates of the Krasoff family history. There is the intimate death of family members — the sister, the mother — that leaves absences around which characters organize their emotional lives. And there is the mass, anonymous death of the peasantry — from famine, disease, violence — that is too vast to be registered as individual loss.

Bunin's treatment of death is characteristically unsentimental. The dead are not mourned so much as remembered, and memory itself is presented as a form of ongoing relationship. When Tikhon visits the family graves, he is not performing grief; he is renewing a connection that persists despite — or perhaps because of — death. The cemetery inscriptions that "spoke touchingly and peacefully of rest, repose" suggest that death may offer what life cannot: peace, dignity, and a stable identity.

## Key Passages

> "The great-grandfather of the Krasoffs, known by the manor-house servants under the nickname of 'The Gipsy,' was hunted with wolfhounds by Cavalry Captain Durnovo."
> — (001-001)

The novel's first death is a murder — casual, legal, almost ceremonial. The great-grandfather is hunted like an animal by his owner. This primal death establishes the moral universe of the novel: life is cheap, power is absolute, and the dead do not rest.

> "He walked to the far corner of the cemetery where all his relatives were buried — father, mother, the sister who had died when a little girl."
> — (005-016)

> "Nastasya Petrovna, who walked like a duck, with her toes turned inward, and waddled, thanks to her incessant pregnancies which always ended up with dead girl-babies."
> — (002-005)

Death as reproductive failure. Nastasya Petrovna's body is a factory of the dead — pregnancy after pregnancy producing only corpses. Bunin's clinical tone ("always ended up with dead girl-babies") refuses the pathos that a lesser writer would deploy, making the fact of these deaths more, not less, devastating.
