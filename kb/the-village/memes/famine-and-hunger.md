# Famine and Hunger

"Not once in five years did five years pass without a famine." This grim observation in *The Village* condenses the defining rhythm of rural Russian life: a cycle of scarcity punctuated by catastrophe. Bunin presents famine not as a temporary aberration but as a structural feature of the peasant economy — an event that is always happening somewhere, always approaching, always remembered. Hunger is the bass note beneath every transaction, every relationship, every decision in the novel.

## The Concept in the Novel

Famine in *The Village* is never directly narrated — there is no chapter titled "The Famine" — but it pervades the novel as memory, fear, and explanation. Characters reference past famines as landmarks in personal and collective history. The economic behaviors that drive the plot — Tikhon's grain speculation, the peasants' desperate sales, the constant anxiety about harvests — are all responses to the ever-present threat of hunger.

Bunin's treatment of famine is characteristically indirect. He shows not the starving but the starved — bodies and souls shaped by generations of nutritional deprivation. The physical descriptions of peasants in the novel — "yellow, puffy," "thin, sunburned, yet pallid" — register the somatic effects of chronic hunger. Even Tikhon Ilitch, the wealthy merchant, carries in his body the marks of a childhood lived at the edge of subsistence.

The connection between famine and the broader themes of the novel is intimate. Famine is the economic manifestation of the same historical inheritance — serfdom, land hunger, communal backwardness — that shapes the Russian character. It is both cause and consequence of the "vast wretchedness" that Tikhon contemplates at the fair.

## Historical Context

Famine was a recurring feature of Russian rural life throughout the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. Major famines occurred in 1891–1892 (affecting 17 provinces and causing an estimated 375,000–400,000 deaths), 1897–1898, 1901, 1905–1906, and 1911–1912. The 1891 famine was a watershed event in Russian history: the government's inadequate response exposed the structural weaknesses of the Tsarist state and galvanized both liberal and revolutionary opposition.

The causes of Russian famines were multiple: an agricultural technology that had changed little since the Middle Ages, a climate characterized by erratic rainfall and early frosts, a transport infrastructure inadequate for moving grain from surplus to deficit regions, and a fiscal system that extracted taxes from peasants regardless of harvest conditions. The commune system, by discouraging individual investment in land improvement, compounded these structural vulnerabilities.

Bunin wrote *The Village* against this background of recurrent catastrophe. His refusal to sentimentalize the peasantry must be understood in this context: for a writer who had witnessed the famines of the 1890s and 1900s, the romantic image of the virtuous, long-suffering *muzhik* was an insult to the reality of rural degradation.

## Key Passages

> "The town was famous throughout Russia for its grain trade: not once in five years did five years pass without a famine, and the town grew rich on the hundred-fold profits from that grain trade."
> — (004-009)

The paradox of famine in a grain-exporting region is stated with bitter concision. The town grows rich on grain at the very moment that peasants are starving for want of it. Famine is not a failure of the market but a feature of it — the mechanism by which grain is extracted from the hungry and sold to the distant.

> "He grew extremely thin, sunburned, yet pallid, flew into bad tempers, and was conscious of being bored to death and of feeling weak all over. He got his stomach so badly out of order that he had cramps."
> — (004-008)

Tikhon's physical deterioration during the fair is rendered in language that blurs the boundary between illness and hunger. He is wealthy, yet his body registers the same symptoms — thinness, pallor, weakness, gastric distress — that afflict the starving peasants around him. The fair, which should be the celebration of his commercial triumph, reduces him to the condition of those he exploits.

> "Indefatigably he followed up the rural police on those dull autumnal days when taxes are collected and forced sale follows forced sale. Unweariedly he bought standing grain on the stalk from the landed proprietors."
> — (002-002)

The connection between tax policy, grain speculation, and famine is implicit but unmistakable. "Forced sale follows forced sale": the state extracts taxes, the peasant must sell his grain (or his land, or his labor) to pay, and the merchant buys at distress prices. This is the mechanism by which famine becomes profit.
