# Social Class

The social world of Bunin's *The Village* is structured by class distinctions that are at once rigid and unstable. The old hierarchy — gentry at the top, peasantry at the bottom, merchants and clergy in between — is crumbling under the pressure of economic change, but its psychological hold on the characters remains absolute. Bunin charts this dissolution with clinical precision, showing how the breakdown of traditional class boundaries produces not liberation but anxiety, resentment, and a pervasive sense of dislocation.

## The Concept in the Novel

The Krasoff family trajectory embodies the novel's analysis of class. The father is a "petty huckster" — a figure suspended between the peasantry (from which he likely emerged after emancipation) and the merchant class (which he never quite enters). His sons, Tikhon and Kuzma, begin their working lives as shop assistants and peddlers, but their paths diverge. Tikhon rises — through cunning, ruthlessness, and sheer stamina — to become a landowner and merchant of substance. Kuzma remains a marginal figure, a self-educated intellectual who belongs to no class and is at home nowhere.

The novel is rich in class types: the "sun-browned members of the gentry class of small estates, in sleeveless peasant over-jackets and caps" — gentry who have sunk to near-peasant status; the "wealthy merchant" and the "village policeman" — figures of local power; the "Gipsies" and "Jews from the southwest" — ethnic outsiders who occupy specific economic niches; and the vast, undifferentiated mass of peasants who form the novel's human background.

Bunin's treatment of class is notable for its refusal of ideological frameworks. He is neither a populist who romanticizes the peasantry nor a Marxist who sees class struggle as the engine of history. His concern is psychological and moral: how does class position shape consciousness, limit possibility, and deform character? The answer, in *The Village*, is bleak: class is a prison from which escape is rare and never complete.

## Historical Context

The class structure of late imperial Russia was in flux. The emancipation of the serfs in 1861 had legally abolished the gentry's monopoly on land and labor, but the economic decline of the gentry class was a slow process that accelerated after the agricultural depression of the 1880s. The merchant class — long stigmatized as coarse and semi-Asiatic by the Europeanized gentry — was rising in wealth and influence. The peasantry, freed in law but bound in practice by the commune system and redemption debts, remained a distinct estate (*soslovie*) with limited legal rights.

The revolution of 1905 had shaken the entire edifice, producing a brief moment of liberal constitutionalism followed by reaction. Bunin wrote *The Village* in the aftermath of this failed revolution, and the novel's atmosphere of stagnation and suppressed violence reflects the political impasse of the Stolypin era.

## Key Passages

> "The father of the Krasoffs was a petty huckster. He roved about the county, lived for a time in Durnovka, set up a pothouse and a little shop, failed, took to drink, returned to the town, and soon died."
> — (001-003)

The father's biography is a compressed history of class failure. Each phrase — "roved about," "set up," "failed," "took to drink" — marks a stage in a downward trajectory that ends in obscurity. The "petty huckster" is a liminal figure, neither peasant nor merchant, and his sons inherit his class ambiguity along with his debts.

> "Indefatigably he followed up the rural police on those dull autumnal days when taxes are collected and forced sale follows forced sale. Unweariedly he bought standing grain on the stalk from the landed proprietors and took land from them and from the peasants, in small parcels, not scorning even half a meadow."
> — (002-002)

Tikhon's rise is built on the economic distress of both gentry and peasantry. He profits from the gentry's inability to manage their estates ("standing grain on the stalk") and from the peasantry's inability to pay taxes ("forced sale follows forced sale"). Class mobility, in this account, is a zero-sum game: one man's ascent is purchased by another's ruin.

> "Gipsies came, blue-black of face; Jews from the southwest, grey of countenance, red-haired, covered with dust, in long, wide coats of canvas and boots down at the heel; sun-browned members of the gentry class of small estates, in sleeveless peasant over-jackets and caps; the commissary of rural police and the village policeman; the wealthy merchant."
> — (004-004)

This catalogue of fair-goers is a compressed sociology of rural Russia. Each group is identified by its physical appearance, dress, and economic role. The "gentry class of small estates" wearing "peasant over-jackets" is a particularly telling detail — gentry who have descended so far that they are indistinguishable from peasants in their dress. The passage is a masterclass in the literary rendering of class through surface detail.

> "He lived for a long time with his dumb cook — 'A dumb woman can't betray anything with her tongue' — and he had a child by her."
> — (002-002)

Tikhon's relationship with his "dumb cook" is a stark illustration of class power at its most intimate. The cook's muteness is not a disability but a convenience — it guarantees her silence. The child, presumably illegitimate and unacknowledged, is a human consequence of class exploitation that the novel registers without comment, leaving the moral judgment to the reader.
