# The Pond

**The Pond** at [[durnovka]] is a humble but symbolically charged body of water that serves the village's daily needs while reflecting the broader conditions of peasant life — its squalor, its moments of heroism, and the oppressive weight of winter. It lies beyond the church, below a dam of manure, and is visible from the manor porch alongside the storehouses and windmill.

## Location and Geography

The pond is situated beyond the Durnovka church, below a manure dam. It is a small, clayey body of water — "a tiny clayey pond gleamed in the sunlight below a dam of manure, a sheet of thick yellow water" [006-006]. From the porch of the manor house, half the village is visible, along with "beyond the storehouses the pond and a part of the promontory — one windmill and the schoolhouse" [035-001]. The pond lies at the convergence of ravines, where it sparkles in the sunlight [034-012].

## Role in the Narrative

### Daily Village Life

The pond serves as the primary water source for Durnovka's livestock. Cattle stand in the thick yellow water "incessantly discharging according to the demands of nature," while a naked peasant soaps his head nearby [006-006]. [[tikhon-krasoff]] drives directly into the pond, which "reeked of the cattle," calling for his horse to be unbridled [006-007].

### Syery's Heroic Moment

The pond becomes the stage for one of [[sery]]'s few moments of acclaim. In October, after inundating rains and an icy storm, the pond freezes over. A neighbor's boar-pig slips from an ice-clad mound, breaks through the ice, and begins to drown. Syery is "the first to arrive at full speed, leap into the water, and save it" [038-032]. His motivation is transparent: "In order that he might be the hero of the day, that he might have the right to rush from the pond into the servants' hall, demand vodka, tobacco, and a bite to eat" [038-032].

### Winter's Oppression

For [[kuzma-krasoff]], the pond becomes one of the emblematic images of Durnovka's interminable winter. The "holes in the ice of the pond" appear alongside cottages, horrid little boys, dogs on roofs, cold, dirt, sickness, and "the animal-like laziness of the peasant men" as the features that oppress him "to the point of pain" [041-004]. The pond, which sparkles freshly on an autumn morning [035-001], becomes by midwinter a frozen surface pocked with hazards — a fitting metaphor for the village's seasonal cycle of brief beauty and grinding hardship.

## Key Passages

> Beyond the church a tiny clayey pond gleamed in the sunlight below a dam of manure, a sheet of thick yellow water in which stood a herd of cows, incessantly discharging according to the demands of nature; and there a naked peasant was soaping his head. [006-006]

> "Unbridle my horse for me," said Tikhon Ilitch, driving into the pond, which reeked of the cattle. [006-007]

> In October, when, after inundating rains and an icy storm, the pond froze over and a neighbour's boar-pig slipped from an ice-clad mound, broke through the ice, and began to drown, Syery was the first to arrive at full speed, leap into the water, and save it. [038-032]

> Nevertheless, that interminable Durnovka winter oppressed him to the point of pain — those cottages, the holes in the ice of the pond, the horrid little boys, the dogs on the roofs, the cold, the dirt, the sickness, the animal-like laziness of the peasant men. [041-004]

> In the morning the pond glittered with a bright, fresh exhalation, and from the park behind the house was wafted an odour of foliage from evergreens and leaf trees, steppe grass, apples, and dew. [035-001]
