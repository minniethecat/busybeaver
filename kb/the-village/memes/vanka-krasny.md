# Vanka Krasny

**Vanka Krasny** ("Vanka the Red") is a Durnovka peasant and one of the participants in the uprising against Tikhon Krasoff's manor. His nickname "Krasny" ("Red") carries the double meaning of the color red and, in the revolutionary context of 1905 Russia, a political connotation — though Bunin leaves it ambiguous whether it refers to his hair, his politics, or both.

## The Uprising

During the peasant assembly that precedes the attack on the manor, Vanka Krasny's voice is prominent: "drowning all the voices the voice of Vanka Krasny, who had already been twice imprisoned" [009-008]. The detail that he has been imprisoned twice before the events of the novel establishes him as a repeat offender, a man for whom conflict with authority is not a singular event but a pattern of life.

## Flight

After the uprising subsides, Vanka Krasny does not remain in Durnovka. The narrator reports that "another rebel, Vanka Krasny, had again betaken himself to the Donetz mines" [037-001]. The word "again" suggests this is not his first flight to the industrial south — a pattern of rebellion, repression, and temporary exile that mirrors the broader experience of Russia's restless peasantry in the early twentieth century.

## Family

Vanka Krasny's wife appears in one of the novel's closing scenes: "the loud-mouthed wife of Vanka Krasny stood upright in the leading" wagon during the Bride's wedding procession [048-010]. Her presence — and her loudness — suggests that even in his absence, Vanka's disruptive energy persists through his family. The wife's prominence at a communal ritual without her husband underscores the fractured families that peasant migration and rebellion leave behind.

> Drowning all the voices the voice of Vanka Krasny, who had already been twice imprisoned. [009-008]

> Another rebel, Vanka Krasny, had again betaken himself to the Donetz mines. [037-001]

## Role in the Novel

Vanka Krasny represents a different trajectory of peasant resistance than Komar (who dies) or the harness-maker (who accommodates). His path — imprisonment, rebellion, flight to industrial labor — prefigures the pattern that millions of Russian peasants would follow in the decades after 1905: migration to the mines and factories of the Donbass, where rural grievances would fuse with industrial labor politics to produce a new kind of revolutionary consciousness.
