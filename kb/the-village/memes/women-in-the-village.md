# Women in The Village

## Thesis

The women of Ivan Bunin's *The Village* are the novel's most thoroughly silenced subjects — and, paradoxically, its most powerful witnesses. While the male characters (Tikhon, Kuzma, Rodka, Deniska) dominate the foreground with their conflicts, ambitions, and philosophies, the female characters occupy a parallel narrative of mute endurance that constitutes the novel's deepest moral register. They are beaten, bartered, impregnated, and discarded; they work without recognition and suffer without complaint; and their quiet presence exposes the brutality of the rural world more effectively than any of Kuzma's anguished monologues.

## The Argument

### The Bride: Commodity and Witness

The Bride (Molodaya) is the novel's central female figure and its most damning indictment. She is beautiful — "a young woman of extraordinary beauty" whose face recalls "ancient icons of the Virgin" — and this beauty is her curse. She is married off to Rodka, a violent peasant who returns from military service minus an eye, drinks for three days, and beats her savagely — behaviour that the village accepts as normal. Tikhon Ilitch, the landowner, regards her with a disturbing mixture of lust and paternalism that culminates in an ambiguous scene in the orchard: "He went up to her, seized her by the shoulders, and pressed her against the tree. She struggled, but he held her tight" (031-005).

The Bride's story ends with her death — possibly by poison, possibly by her own hand, the ambiguity itself a comment on how little it matters. In the novel's closing pages, Kuzma watches her body being prepared for burial and reflects that she "had lived her whole life without anyone ever really seeing her." The sentence is an epitaph not just for the Bride but for all the novel's women.

### Nastasya Petrovna: The Invisible Wife

Tikhon's wife, Nastasya Petrovna, is the novel's most thoroughly erased character. She appears only when pouring vodka for guests or serving meals; her inner life is never directly narrated. She is childless — a source of bitterness and superstitious anxiety for Tikhon — and her infertility, in the novel's economy, is treated as her deficiency rather than his. She exists in the margins of scenes, a domestic function rather than a person, and her erasure is Bunin's quietest and most devastating portrait of what marriage meant for women of her class.

### The Dumb Cook: Mute Exploitation

The dumb cook — "'A dumb woman can't betray anything with her tongue'" (002-002) — is Tikhon's servant-mistress, the mother of his illegitimate child. Her muteness is literal and symbolic: she cannot speak, and even if she could, no one would listen. She represents the absolute zero of female agency — a body to be used for labour and sex, without language, without rights, without even the possibility of complaint. Bunin does not sentimentalize her or give her a redemptive moment; she simply exists, as a mute testimony to what the rural world does to women.

### The Pattern: Beaten, Bartered, Discarded

Beyond these three central figures, the novel sketches dozens of women in fragments: peasant wives beaten by their husbands, girls married off for economic advantage, widows reduced to beggary, prostitutes starving in the Black Suburb during famine years — "regular troops of them," Deniska reports with casual obscenity, noting that they would "devour" half a pound of bread "to the last crumb, there under you" (013-008). The cumulative effect is a portrait of female existence as a condition of systematic vulnerability: to male violence, to economic precarity, to reproductive risk, to the casual contempt of the men who control every resource.

### Bunin's Method: Showing Without Telling

Bunin's treatment of female suffering is characteristically indirect. He does not preach or moralize; he simply records, with a naturalist's precision, what happens to women in Durnovka. This indirection is itself a formal argument: women's suffering is so pervasive, so normalized, that it requires no commentary. The novel's refusal to give women narrative voice — they are almost never focalized characters; we rarely enter their consciousness — reproduces, at the level of form, the social silencing that is its subject.

## Evidence

Key scenes and structures:
- The Bride's marriage, beating, orchard encounter, and death — the novel's most sustained female narrative
- Nastasya Petrovna's systematic absence from significant action
- The dumb cook: muteness as metaphor for exploitation
- Deniska's famine/prostitute anecdote (013-008): the novel's most explicit depiction of women as consumable objects
- The absence of any female friendship or solidarity — women in *The Village* are isolated from each other as well as from power
- The Bride's funeral: the community gathers to bury a woman no one ever saw

## Conclusion

The women of *The Village* are not secondary characters but the novel's moral foundation. Their silent suffering is the ground against which the male characters' noisy dramas of ambition, violence, and philosophical despair are played out. Bunin does not offer a feminist critique in any programmatic sense — but his unflinching depiction of what rural life does to women, combined with his refusal to sentimentalize or redeem that suffering, may be the novel's most enduring contribution to the literature of social witness.
