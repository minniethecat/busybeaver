# Komar

**Komar** is a Durnovka peasant identified as "one of the chief rioters" during the period of agrarian unrest that follows the 1905 Revolution in Ivan Bunin's *The Village*. Renowned among the villagers for his strength, cleverness, and boldness in confronting landowners, his accidental death serves as a darkly ironic commentary on the fate of peasant rebellion.

## Reputation

Komar is described as "renowned for his strength, his cleverness, and his daring in his dealings with the members of the gentry class" [037-001]. This triple endowment — physical power, native intelligence, and a willingness to challenge authority — marks him as a natural leader among the rebels. Unlike the harness-maker, whose rebellion is born of emaciation and desperation, Komar possesses qualities that might, in another social order, have made him a figure of genuine importance.

## Death

Komar's death is both absurd and grimly fitting. "At the beginning of September Komar, one of the chief rioters, renowned for his strength, his cleverness, and his daring in his dealings with the members of the gentry class, had entered a distillery near Eletz, fallen into the malt-kiln while in a state of intoxication, and been suffocated. No one had known that he was there, and the door had been bolted. Komar had bent the door in his efforts to escape into the air, but evidently such a death had been written in his fate" [037-001].

The detail of the bent door — evidence of Komar's tremendous strength even in his final moments — is characteristic of Bunin's method: a single physical detail that conveys both the man's power and its ultimate futility. The narrator's comment that "evidently such a death had been written in his fate" invokes the peasant fatalism that runs throughout the novel, suggesting that even Komar's exceptional qualities could not alter the course ordained for him.

> Komar, one of the chief rioters, renowned for his strength, his cleverness, and his daring in his dealings with the members of the gentry class, had entered a distillery near Eletz, fallen into the malt-kiln while in a state of intoxication, and been suffocated. [037-001]

## Role in the Novel

Komar's story arc — from rebellion to absurd death — encapsulates the novel's bleak view of peasant agency. His strength, cleverness, and daring are real qualities, but they lead not to liberation or even meaningful resistance but to a lonely, ignominious end in a bolted malt-kiln. The fate that "had been written" for him mirrors the larger fate of the Durnovka peasants, whose brief uprising (the attack on Tikhon's manor) dissolves into individual tragedies: death, flight, or grudging accommodation.
