# Labor and Toil

Labor is the unceasing background of life in *The Village*. Bunin's peasants are defined by work — by the physical toil that shapes their bodies, consumes their days, and provides the only measure of dignity available to them. Yet labor is never valorized as a simple virtue. It is presented as both necessity and curse: the means by which peasants survive, and the weight that crushes them into brutishness. The novel's labor is never redemptive in the Tolstoyan sense; it is merely relentless.

## The Concept in the Novel

Physical labor in *The Village* has a double character. On one hand, it is the source of whatever order and meaning peasant life possesses. The fields being ploughed, the grain being sown, the bread being baked — these are the rhythms that give shape to the rural year. On the other hand, labor is the visible sign of degradation. Tikhon Ilitch, having risen from peasant to proprietor, looks upon the workingmen with contempt: their bent backs and horny hands are marks of their animal condition. The novel refuses to romanticize manual work; Bunin's peasants do not find spiritual fulfillment in toil. They endure it.

Work also functions as a social marker. Those who have escaped physical labor — Tikhon, the gentry, the officials — occupy a different human category from those who cannot. When Rodka works on the railway line, when the widow drags a plough across the field, when the summer-workers are hired and discarded — each instance maps a position in the village's rigid hierarchy. Labor is not just what peasants do; it is what they *are*.

## Key Passages

> "The midday sun scorched; the rye blossomed in the fields, the carts wended their way along the roads, and lost themselves in the distance, in the sea of grain." [007-005] — Labor and landscape merge; the peasants are inseparable from the fields they work.

> "His hands were deformed by long toil, and his horny unbending fingers could not be straightened." [042-003] — The body as a record of labor; physical deformation as the mark of a peasant life.

> "The widow dragged a plough; alongside her walked a maiden, also dragging, and they sang a mournful ritual chant." [034-002] — Bunin's image of labor reduced to its starkest form: a woman harnessed like an animal, yet singing.

> "He may go break his back with work." [008-011] — Tikhon's dismissal of a peasant's labor; the landowner's contempt for the toil that sustains him.

> "By day she toiled, never pausing for a moment." [036-005] — The unceasing quality of peasant women's labor; work without rest, without Sabbath.

## Labor and Social Hierarchy

The novel charts a precise economy of labor. At the bottom are the landless who hire themselves out for daily wages — the most precarious and degraded. Above them are the peasants with allotments, who work their own land but remain trapped in cycles of debt and scarcity. Above them are the "summer-workers" — temporary laborers taken on by landowners like Tikhon, who views them as a necessary inconvenience. And above all are the proprietors and gentry, for whom physical work is alien, even shameful.

This hierarchy is not presented as natural or just. Bunin shows it as a system maintained by violence — the violence of poverty, of legal coercion, of the knout and the barracks. Labor in *The Village* is never freely chosen; it is always compelled.
