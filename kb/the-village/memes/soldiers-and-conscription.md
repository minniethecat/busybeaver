# Soldiers and Conscription

Military service in *The Village* is a paradoxical institution. It is simultaneously a dreaded obligation and a rare avenue of escape from the village; a source of brutalizing experience and a marker of wider-world knowledge; a destroyer of men and a shaper of the few who return. Through the figure of the discharged soldier, Bunin examines how the Russian state's most universal institution leaves its mark on rural life—transforming those it touches into figures who are at once more knowing and more dangerous than the peasants they left behind.

## The Concept in the Novel

The soldier appears throughout the novel in multiple guises, none of them simple. Rodka, the one-eyed husband of the Bride, is the most vivid example: "During his service as a soldier he had acquired a stock of Little Russian words and an accent" [010-007], and he returns from the army with a calm, methodical brutality that makes him "downright terrifying" [010-007]. His military bearing translates directly into domestic tyranny—he beats his wife "ferociously every day and every night" [010-007] and his response to any challenge is the same cold menace: "What's that you're remarking?"

Tikhon Ilitch understands the soldier's utility and danger. He hires Rodka and his wife because "nowadays it won't do to be without a soldier on the place" [010-005]—the soldier as instrument of order and intimidation. Yet when he witnesses Rodka's violence, even the village's most powerful man "made haste to retreat, slamming the door behind him" [010-010].

The human cost of conscription is rendered with bitter realism. Kuzma watches the baker Akim pray and remembers "how he had once been called for conscription. Five hundred men had been summoned, only one hundred and twenty being wanted" [032-023]. The rejected conscripts are described with clinical despair: "those naked youths—they resembled sparrows, with arms as thin as whiplashes and huge, solid bellies" [032-023]—bodies ravaged by poverty before the army even takes them.

The discharged soldier appears as a recurring type in the village landscape: "a discharged soldier, grey-haired, afflicted with a hoarse, whistling asthma" [020-025] working as a station watchman; the young soldier who "had lost his leg... his black eyes were mournful" [P02-004], reduced to begging; the soldier-teacher who "had lost in the service what small wits he had ever possessed" [P02-XXXIII] and speaks in baffling non-sequiturs. Each is marked—physically, mentally, or both—by his service.

Bunin's own Autobiographical Note frames military experience in apocalyptic terms: he fled Russia to avoid "being sent to the barracks to clean up the soldiers' filth" [P03-012], a phrase that equates military service with degradation and the collapse of civilized life.

## Historical Context

Universal military conscription was introduced in Russia in 1874, replacing the old system of long-term service (25 years) with a nominally shorter term. In practice, the peasant conscript faced years of harsh discipline, linguistic alienation (officers spoke French and German), and separation from his village. The Russo-Japanese War (1904–1905) and the 1905 Revolution—both within Bunin's living memory while writing *The Village*—placed the army at the center of national trauma, as defeat abroad and the use of troops to suppress domestic unrest made soldiers visible, controversial figures in rural society.
