## Overview

The town and the city occupy a distinct imaginative space in *The Village*, serving as counterpoints to the rural world of Durnovka. Where the village is static, bound by inherited custom and agricultural rhythm, the town represents commerce, mobility, and exposure to a wider world. Yet Bunin's towns are provincial and grim—neither fully modern nor meaningfully traditional—and the city that Kuzma visits offers little more than a dispiriting extension of the same provincial bleakness.

## The Provincial Town as Commercial Hub

The county town is the site of the Fair, an event so large and central to regional life that "the inns in the town were crowded for a long time" after it concluded [009-009]. Tikhon Ilitch "spent four days in the town at the Fair and got still more enraged" [004-001], finding commerce there as fraught as in the village. The bazaar is the town's persistent commercial heartbeat—a space where "freethinkers" hold forth [023-003], where Kuzma hears local gossip [024-001], and where the smell of "fish shops, fennel, and coarse tobacco" suffuses the air [024-017]. The bazaar reigned "supreme over the minds of the inhabitants" [023-013], its commercial logic colonizing every other sphere of life.

## Voronezh as Symbolic City

Voronezh, the historical provincial capital, recurs as a reference point for lives that might have been otherwise. Bunin himself was born there [P03-005], and characters repeatedly travel to, dream of, or die in Voronezh. Kuzma's brother Tikhon speculates that a forgotten relative "had died in Voronezh—and now no one knew for a certainty" [017-001]. Kuzma carries on a love affair "with a married woman" in Voronezh [025-001] and later "abandoned the hope of getting to Voronezh" as illness overtakes him [033-006]. The city becomes a symbol of unrealized possibility—always elsewhere.

## Alienation and Mobility

Contact with the town does not elevate village characters but often alienates them further. The Krasoff family's trajectory follows this pattern: the great-grandfather "went off with his family to the town—and soon distinguished himself" only to have the grandfather "take to drink, return to the town, and soon die" [001-002–001-003]. Movement between village and town is not upward mobility but a circulation of ruin. Kuzma writes "articles for the city newspapers" [028-002] yet remains impoverished, caught between the literate urban world and the oral village one.
