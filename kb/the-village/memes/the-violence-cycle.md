# The Violence Cycle: Alcohol, Fear, and Rebellion

## Thesis

Violence in Ivan Bunin's *The Village* is not random or incidental — it is systemic, following a predictable causal chain. Alcohol consumption lowers inhibition and fuels interpersonal aggression. Interpersonal aggression creates an atmosphere of pervasive fear — of husbands, of landowners, of neighbours, of the state. Fear, in turn, drives further alcohol consumption as escape and self-medication. The cycle is punctuated by periodic eruptions of collective violence (the peasant uprising) that briefly release accumulated pressure before subsiding into the same pattern. Bunin presents this cycle not as a moral failing of individuals but as the structural consequence of historical conditions — serfdom's legacy, material deprivation, and the absence of any institution capable of breaking the loop.

## The Argument

### The Alcohol-Aggression Link

Alcohol pervades every level of village society in *The Village*. The dram-shop, the pothouse, and the tavern are the central social institutions of rural life. Tikhon Ilitch himself ran a liquor-trade before the state vodka monopoly was imposed, and his wealth was built partly on selling drink to the peasants whose land he was simultaneously acquiring. Alcohol is both cause and symptom: it fuels violence, and it is consumed to forget the violence one has suffered or inflicted.

The novel's most vivid scenes of interpersonal brutality are invariably preceded by drinking. Rodka, returning from military service, drinks for three days and then beats the Bride. The harness-maker, the uprising's chief agitator, is described as a man perpetually soaked in alcohol. Syery, when drunk, boasts of his crimes and threatens his neighbours. Bunin rarely describes the drinking itself — he is more interested in its aftermath: the bruised faces, the broken furniture, the muttered threats, the casual acceptance of violence as simply what happens when peasants drink.

### Fear as Social Control

The omnipresence of violence creates an atmosphere of fear that functions as an informal system of social control. Peasant women fear their husbands. Peasant men fear the rural police, the tax collector, and above all the landowner. The landowner fears the peasants — Tikhon carries a bulldog revolver after the uprising, vowing to "burn Durnovka to the ground" or poison its wells (009-002). Even the state fears the peasantry, as evidenced by the heavy police presence during tax collection and the landowners' panicked flight to town after the uprising.

Fear is not merely a response to violence; it is a condition of life that Bunin presents as continuous and ambient. The novel's temporal structure reinforces this: the narrative proceeds not through dramatic climaxes but through the accumulation of small, wearying episodes of intimidation, humiliation, and dread. The uprising, when it comes, is almost a relief — at least something is happening.

### The Uprising as Pressure Release

The peasant uprising at Durnovka is the cycle's most dramatic expression. The peasants attack Tikhon's manor, shake the fruit from his orchard trees, and set fire to his watchman's hut. But Bunin is careful to emphasize the uprising's inconsequentiality. "The whole uprising had been limited to the Durnovka people's shouting for a while, doing a lot of damage, and then quieting down" (009-002). The landowners return from town. The harness-maker presents himself at Tikhon's shop as though nothing had happened. The cycle resumes, unchanged.

Bunin's portrayal of the uprising as both terrifying (to Tikhon) and futile (as political action) is central to his broader argument. Collective violence, like individual violence, does not transform the conditions that produce it; it merely releases accumulated pressure, after which the system returns to its previous equilibrium. This is not a revolutionary position but a profoundly conservative one — and it explains why *The Village* dissatisfied both the left (who wanted a call to revolution) and the right (who wanted a celebration of peasant virtue).

### The Absence of Exit

What makes the violence cycle truly tragic is the absence of any mechanism for breaking it. The Orthodox Church, represented by the priest Father Peter, is corrupt and ineffective. The state, represented by the rural police and the tax authority, is predatory rather than protective. The commune, theoretically a vehicle for collective self-governance, channels peasant grievances into sporadic, destructive outbursts rather than sustained reform movements.

The novel's final image — Tikhon standing on his porch, Kuzma managing the estate, the peasants continuing their ancient rhythms — is the image of a cycle that has no natural terminus. Alcohol, fear, and violence are not problems to be solved but conditions to be endured. Bunin's realism is, in this sense, anti-meliorist: he does not believe things will get better, and he refuses to pretend otherwise.

## Evidence

Key episodes that trace the cycle:
- Tikhon's liquor trade and its role in his enrichment (early chapters)
- Rodka's return from military service: drinking, beating the Bride, triangle with Tikhon
- Syery's drunken confessions and threats
- The harness-maker's alcoholism and his role as uprising spokesman
- The uprising: collective violence → fear of reprisal → return to normalcy
- Tikhon's bulldog revolver: the landowner's fear becomes armed preparation
- Komar's death in a distillery kiln: alcohol literally consumes the rebel

## Conclusion

The violence cycle in *The Village* is Bunin's most systematic argument about the condition of rural Russia. Violence is not an aberration or a moral failure — it is the predictable output of a social machine whose inputs are historical trauma (serfdom), material deprivation (poverty), and institutional vacuum (no effective church, state, or commune). The machine has no off switch, and Bunin does not pretend to supply one.
