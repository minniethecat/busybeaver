## The Egyptian Girl

Donka the Goat is introduced alongside the Bride in the orchard leased by petty burghers from the town. Bunin's physical description of her is startling and memorable:

> "Donka the Goat, an extremely stupid and pretty little girl who resembled an Egyptian" [012-003]

The Egyptian comparison — reinforced elsewhere by the phrase "her senselessly brilliant Egyptian eyes" — sets her apart from the pale, suffering Russian peasant type. She is vital, healthy, and foolish, a creature of the body rather than the mind.

## Friend to the Bride

Donka forms an unlikely friendship with the Bride during the autumn when both work gathering windfalls in the orchard. Their dynamic is sharply contrasted: at home the Bride is silent and weeping; in the garden, with Donka, she becomes "shrilly merry, shouted with laughter, sang songs."

> "The Goat amazed him by her health, her cheery stupidity, her senselessly brilliant Egyptian eyes" [035-005]

Kuzma, observing them in the orchard, is struck by Donka's robust vitality — a quality that throws the Bride's fragility into sharper relief. The two young women climb into the nettle patch for the ripest fruit, a small moment of freedom in lives otherwise constrained.

## Witness to Violence

Donka's most significant role in the novel is as witness to the assault on the Bride. On the night of the "evening party" in the watchman's hut, the petty burghers ply both girls with vodka and crude food. At dawn, the men seize the intoxicated Bride, strip her, bind her arms, and tie her skirts over her head.

> "The Goat started to run away, and made a headlong dive in her fright into the tall, wet steppe-grass. When she peeped out from that shelter... she espied the Bride, naked to the waist, hanging from a tree" [012-003]

Donka's terror is visceral — she dives into the grass, hides, trembles. When she emerges to find the Bride tied to a tree, she weeps "in streams" and her teeth chatter as she unties her. She swears an oath "by the memory of her father and mother" never to tell the village what happened. The oath is kept, but rumors spread regardless, and Donka's reputation for having a loose tongue is blamed.

## Significance

Donka the Goat occupies a minor but crucial narrative position. She is the only witness to the Bride's degradation, the only friend the Bride has, and the vehicle through which the novel registers the cost of silence. Her nickname — "the Goat" — suggests something animal, unthinking, a creature of instinct rather than reflection. Yet it is she who acts when no one else will: untying the Bride from the tree, swearing to protect her secret. In a novel filled with passive bystanders, Donka's frightened but genuine act of friendship is one of the few instances of moral action in the village.
