# Makar the Pilgrim

**Makar Ivanovitch**, known as "Makar the Pilgrim," is a wandering religious figure who appears sporadically in Ivan Bunin's *The Village*. A type of the Russian *strannik* (wanderer) and holy fool, Makar's unsettling presence and cryptic pronouncements inject an element of folk religiosity and irrational prophecy into the novel's otherwise grim realist texture.

## Character

Makar Ivanovitch belongs to the tradition of Russian pilgrims — rootless wanderers who traverse the countryside, sustained by alms and the residual reverence of the peasantry for religious ascetics. The narrator describes him as having been "known, once on a time" for his exploits [018-003], suggesting a figure whose reputation precedes him and whose past contains episodes that the villagers remember with a mixture of awe and unease.

## Appearances

Makar first appears unexpectedly in Durnovka, where his arrival is noted with significance: "the second was none other than Makar Ivanovitch!" [018-002]. The exclamation mark in Bunin's prose signals the weight the villagers attach to his presence. Later in the novel, his name resurfaces in connection with Deniska and the Krasoff brothers, suggesting his influence — or at least his reputation — persists throughout the narrative [045-040].

> The second was none other than Makar Ivanovitch! [018-002]

## Role in the Novel

Makar Ivanovitch functions as a representative of Russia's folk religious culture — a world of pilgrims, holy fools, and wandering ascetics that coexists uneasily with the institutional Orthodox Church. Unlike the formal clergy, who are portrayed as distant or corrupt, Makar embodies a more ambiguous spiritual authority: unsettling, unpredictable, and tied to the oral culture of prophecy and rumor that circulates through the village.

His relationship to Deniska and the question of the Bride's marriage links him to the novel's central plot threads, suggesting that the pilgrim's pronouncements carry weight in village decision-making, however irrational they may appear to the educated Kuzma or the pragmatic Tikhon.
