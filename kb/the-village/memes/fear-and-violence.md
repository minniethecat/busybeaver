# Fear and Violence

Violence in *The Village* is not exceptional but ambient—a constant low hum beneath the rhythms of rural life. Fear serves as the invisible architecture of social relations: the peasant fears the landlord, the wife fears the husband, the village fears the authorities, and even the landlord fears the peasant masses. Bunin's depiction of violence ranges from the casual cruelty of Tikhon Ilitch's whip to the institutional terror of conscription and rebellion, forming a portrait of a society held together as much by threat as by custom.

## The Concept in the Novel

Fear operates at every level of the social order. Tikhon Ilitch both inflicts and suffers it: he lashes passing peasants with his whip "with malicious delight" [P01-VI], yet he himself sits "overwhelmed with wrath, mortification, and terror" when the Durnovka peasants rise against him [009-009]. His fear is physical and visceral—"his heart beat violently, his hands trembled, his face burned, his hearing was as acute as that of a wild animal" [009-009]—the same animal fear he inflicts on others.

The casualness of the violence is striking. Tikhon's whip is always at hand; the drunken peasant with "his beard bloodstained, his nose swollen and clotted with dried blood" [P01-VI] is a routine sight, not an emergency. When Tikhon fantasizes about resolving the Durnovka crisis, he thinks of kazaks "with their whips" [P01-VIII]—violence as the default instrument of order.

Domestic violence is endemic. Rodka beats his young wife, the Bride, and when Tikhon Ilitch witnesses it, Rodka's response—"What's that you're remarking?"—is so calmly menacing that Tikhon "made haste to retreat, slamming the door behind him" [P01-X]. The husband's authority over his wife is absolute, and even the village's most powerful man dares not interfere.

Religious fear haunts the private sphere. Tikhon Ilitch "had never, from his childhood, liked shrine-lamps" [003-003], associating their "uncertain churchly light" with his father's death. His wife Nastasya Petrovna's desperate prayers for a child create an atmosphere where "the shrine-lamp burned uninterruptedly, and Tikhon Ilitch felt as if Nastasya Petrovna were carrying on some sort of secret affair with uncanny powers" [003-003]. When she hears the funeral song, she "experienced no confusion, felt no fear, but only fell a-weeping" [003-014]—suggesting that fear is so constant that its absence is noteworthy.

Bunin himself, in the Autobiographical Note, confesses to having "felt a vague fear for the fate of Russia" [P03-014] while writing the novel—a fear that subsequent events would vindicate. This authorial fear frames the novel's violence as prophetic rather than merely descriptive.

## Historical Context

The violence Bunin depicts has deep roots. The emancipation of 1861 abolished serfdom but left intact the structures of power that enabled physical coercion. Landlords could no longer legally own peasants, but economic dependency and social hierarchy ensured that the whip remained an instrument of control. The 1905 Revolution, which occurred during Bunin's work on the novel, brought peasant uprisings and brutal state repression—the "terror" and "rebellion" that Tikhon Ilitch experiences directly.
