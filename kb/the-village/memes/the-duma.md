# The State Duma

The State Duma — the elected legislative assembly established by Tsar Nicholas II in the wake of the 1905 Revolution — hovers at the margins of *The Village* as an object of ambiguous expectation. The peasants discuss it in the pothouse; the merchants ask after its progress at the fair; Kuzma Krasoff, the novel's intellectual, follows its doings in the newspapers that reach the provinces in fragments and delays. The Duma represents, for the characters who speak of it, the possibility — uncertain and increasingly remote — that the Russian state might reform itself, that the peasant might receive land from legislation rather than seizure, that the ancient injustices of rural life might be addressed by law rather than by violence.

## Role in the Novel

The Duma functions in *The Village* as a barometer of political hope and its inevitable disappointment. The First Duma (April–July 1906) and the Second Duma (February–June 1907) were both dissolved by the Tsar after brief, contentious sessions dominated by the land question. The Third Duma (1907–1912), in session when Bunin was writing the novel, had been elected under a restricted franchise that heavily favored landowners and the gentry over peasants and workers. The characters in *The Village* are aware, dimly, of these events — the Duma is "dissolved, but that did not seem to matter to anyone" [033-002] — and their awareness shades into indifference and cynicism.

Bunin's treatment of the Duma is characteristic of his larger skepticism toward political solutions to Russia's problems. The Duma is discussed endlessly and changes nothing. The peasants wait for land; the Duma debates; the land does not come. This cycle of expectation and disappointment is woven into the texture of village conversation, where the Duma is invoked not as an institution capable of action but as a conversational placeholder — something to talk about when there is nothing to be done.

## Historical Background

The State Duma was created by the October Manifesto of 1905, which Tsar Nicholas II issued under pressure from the revolutionary upheaval of that year. The manifesto promised civil liberties, an expanded franchise, and a legislature without whose approval no law could take effect. The First Duma convened in April 1906 in an atmosphere of intense expectation. The dominant party, the Constitutional Democrats (Kadets), pressed for radical land reform, including the compulsory expropriation of gentry estates with compensation. The Tsar dissolved the Duma after seventy-two days.

The Second Duma, elected in early 1907, was even more radical in composition and was dissolved after just over three months on the pretext of an alleged revolutionary conspiracy. The electoral law was then unilaterally amended to produce a more compliant assembly. The Third Duma, which sat from 1907 to 1912, was dominated by conservative and nationalist deputies and proved more durable. But the restrictions on the franchise — which gave the vote to only about fifteen percent of the adult male population, with landowners vastly overrepresented — meant that the Duma never acquired democratic legitimacy in the eyes of the peasantry or the urban working class.

## Key Passages

> "They do nothing but discuss the Duma, riots and conflagrations, and meanwhile the people are perishing from disease and hunger."
> — [027-006]

The complaint captures the rural perception of the Duma as a talking-shop disconnected from the material realities of village life. The juxtaposition of "discuss the Duma" with "perishing from disease and hunger" is Bunin's own indictment: constitutional politics, whatever its abstract merits, has done nothing for the people it claims to represent.

> "Have they assembled that Duma yet, merchant?"
> — [029-013]

This simple question, posed to a merchant at the fair, registers the distance between the Duma and the village. The peasant speaker does not know whether the Duma is in session — whether it even exists as a functioning body. The question is asked without urgency, as one might inquire about the weather. The Duma is news from a foreign country.

> "The Duma was dissolved, but that did not seem to matter to anyone."
> — [033-002]

The dissolution of the Duma — a constitutional crisis in St. Petersburg — is barely a ripple in Durnovka. "That did not seem to matter to anyone" is one of the novel's bleakest sentences. The principal achievement of Russian constitutionalism, such as it was, is to have produced an institution whose disappearance occasions no concern.

> "How much land he expects to receive from the Duma. What think you, Akimushka? How much?"
> — [032-003]

The peasant's expectation — that the Duma will give him land — is both naive and rational. The land question was the central issue before the First and Second Dumas, and the peasantry's hopes for a "black partition" of gentry estates were not unfounded. But the question — "How much?" — has no answer. The Duma has been stripped of its capacity to address the land question, and the peasant's hope is a phantom.

> "All the autumn he had roamed about the village, waiting for favours from the Duma."
> — [038-032]

The phrase "waiting for favours from the Duma" captures the peasant's relationship to the state in its essence: passive, dependent, and ultimately futile. He roams and waits. The Duma, like the Tsar, like God, dispenses favours — or does not. Agency lies elsewhere, always elsewhere.
