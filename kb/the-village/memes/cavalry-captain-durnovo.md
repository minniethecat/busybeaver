# Cavalry Captain Durnovo

Cavalry Captain Durnovo never appears directly in the novel — he belongs to the generation before the Krasoff brothers — but his act of hunting a serf with wolfhounds is the novel's founding event. He represents the old gentry at its most brutal: the class that treated human beings as game and whose power was absolute, arbitrary, and lethal.

## Biography / Role in the Novel

The novel opens with Durnovo's act: "The great-grandfather of the Krasoffs, known by the manor-house servants under the nickname of 'The Gipsy,' was hunted with wolfhounds by Cavalry Captain Durnovo" (001-001). This single sentence establishes the moral coordinates of the entire narrative. Durnovo is never judged or analyzed; he simply is — the fact of gentry violence against which all subsequent events must be measured.

The Durnovo estate eventually passes to Tikhon Krasoff — the descendant of the man Durnovo hunted. This historical reversal is the novel's deepest irony: the peasant inherits the master's land, but the violence that founded the estate can never be erased.
