## Overview

Duty in *Dr. Wortle's School* is the moral language through which characters navigate conflicting obligations—to the Church, to one's family, to the school, and to individual conscience. Trollope tests the concept by placing his characters in situations where duties collide, and their choices reveal the hierarchy of values they truly hold.

## Clerical Duty

[[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]'s relationship with clerical duty is complicated from the outset. Though a clergyman, he is primarily an educator:

> "He had explained that he would undertake no clerical duty. Education was his profession, and to that he intended to devote himself." (003-004)

Yet others consistently invoke clerical duty to constrain him. [[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]] believes it is her duty to teach him his obligations:

> "To teach Mr. Wortle his duty as a parish clergyman was evidently a task to which the Honourable Mrs. Stantiloup felt herself specially called." (002-004)

Ironically, the character most insistent on the language of duty is also the novel's most corrosive presence—suggesting that duty-talk can be a weapon as much as a virtue.

## Duty to Family

The novel's most powerful articulation of duty comes through [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]'s defense of his support for the Peacockes. He frames it as a sacred obligation transcending institutional loyalty:

> "If I were to do so, he would be neglecting a duty much more sacred than any he owed to the diocese." (007-056)

His wife [[mrs-wortle|Mrs. Wortle]] grounds duty in everyday domestic service. The novel suggests that duty performed without display is often truer than duty proclaimed from a pulpit.

[[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]]'s sense of duty to his wife is the most personal and immediate in the novel. "My first duty is to my wife" [006-032]—this simple declaration places marital obligation above all other claims, including professional and ecclesiastical ones.

## Conflicting Duties

The Peacocke crisis exposes the tension between competing duties. Mr. Peacocke feels a duty to his wife, to the school, to the boys in his care, and to the truth—and these duties cannot all be satisfied simultaneously. Dr. Wortle must choose between his duty to the diocese and his duty to protect the innocent:

> "I should. My first duty would be to my parish and to my conscience." (012-024)

The Bishop invokes duty as well, but his conception is institutional rather than personal. He tells Wortle "that he owed a primary duty to his parish, a second duty to the diocese" [012-026]—a hierarchy that places institutional obligations above individual conscience. The novel quietly rejects this ordering.

## Duty and Honour

Dr. Wortle's understanding of duty is inseparable from his conception of honour. "The line of conduct which he thought to be his duty—and also to do his duty to his family" [015-004] reflects a gentleman's code in which duty is not abstract obligation but the practical expression of character. The word "duty" appears throughout the novel as a touchstone: those who invoke it sincerely are judged differently from those who use it as a rhetorical shield.

## Duty Resolved

The novel's resolution does not dissolve the tension between duties, but shows that acting on principle, even when duties conflict, earns more respect than evading responsibility. Dr. Wortle's example suggests that the highest duty is to follow conscience, even at personal cost:

> "The execution of some presumed duty... the duty also had been dear, and had been the sweetest of the sweet." (020-017, 020-030)
