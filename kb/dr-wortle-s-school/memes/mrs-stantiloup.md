# The Honourable Mrs. Stantiloup

## Overview

The Honourable Mrs. Stantiloup is the primary antagonist of Anthony Trollope's *[[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]'s School* (1881). An aristocratic lady of considerable social influence, she wages a sustained campaign against [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]'s establishment after the irregularities in Mr. and [[mrs-peacocke|Mrs. Peacocke]]'s marriage come to light. Her character serves as a vehicle for Trollope's critique of social hypocrisy, the destructive power of gossip, and the moral cowardice of those who act through influence rather than direct confrontation.

## Role in the Narrative

Mrs. Stantiloup functions as the chief external antagonist to [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]. Unlike the moral and ecclesiastical authorities who question the Peacockes' position through formal channels, Mrs. Stantiloup operates through social pressure — letters, private conversations, and the coordinated withdrawal of pupils. Her campaign begins early in the novel and escalates through the middle chapters, culminating in a gathering at Buttercup where she persuades other parents, including [[mrs-momson|Mrs. Momson]] and [[mrs-talbot|Mrs. Talbot]], to remove their sons from [[bowick|Bowick]] School [016-010][016-012].

Her opposition is not rooted in any genuine moral scruple but in personal animus and social ambition. Trollope presents her as a figure whose power derives entirely from her willingness to damage others through insinuation and collective action. The narrator's description of her as a "virago" — a term put in [[bishop-of-broughton|the Bishop]]'s mouth — signals the contemporary judgement on women who wielded such influence outside formal structures [004-007].

## Character & Traits

Mrs. Stantiloup is portrayed with consistent moral clarity by the narrator. She is described as having "a tongue as loud as the town-crier's bell" [006-009] and is known for her implacability. Trollope draws her as a woman who substitutes moral outrage for moral reasoning, and whose power lies in her social position rather than in any personal virtue.

Her defining traits include:
- **Social ambition**: She leverages her title ("the Honourable") as a weapon, using it to command deference and amplify her opinions
- **Indirect aggression**: Rather than confronting [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]] directly, she works through letters, private meetings, and [[bishop-of-broughton|the Bishop]]
- **Tenacity**: Her campaign against the school persists across many chapters, showing no sign of relenting even when the facts of the Peacockes' case become clearer
- **Moral blindness**: She never acknowledges the injustice she inflicts on [[mrs-peacocke|Mrs. Peacocke]] or the damage to the school's innocent pupils

The Bishop himself, though aligned with her social circle, describes her as an "abominable virago" [004-007], suggesting that even her allies recognize the ugliness of her methods.

## Relationships

- **[[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]**: Her primary target; she regards his independence and his defence of the Peacockes as intolerable affronts to her social authority [016-032]
- **[[mrs-peacocke|Mrs. Peacocke]]**: The indirect target of her campaign; Mrs. Stantiloup treats her not as a person but as a symbol of impropriety to be expelled from the community
- **[[bishop-of-broughton|The Bishop of Broughton]]**: A social ally whom she attempts to enlist in her cause, though he maintains a more cautious distance
- **[[mrs-momson|Mrs. Momson]] and [[mrs-talbot|Mrs. Talbot]]**: Fellow parents whom she recruits at Buttercup to withdraw their children [016-010][016-012]
- **[[lady-de-lawle|Lady De Lawle]]**: A social connection who shares her aristocratic standing
