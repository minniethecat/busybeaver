## Overview

Charity—understood in its Christian sense as *caritas*, self-giving love—is the moral standard against which all characters in *Dr. Wortle's School* are measured. Those who act with charity, like [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]] and [[mrs-wortle|Mrs. Wortle]], are vindicated; those who act without it, like [[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]] and [[the-bishop-of-broughton|the Bishop]], are exposed as moral failures regardless of their social or ecclesiastical standing.

## Charity as Action

Charity in the novel is not sentiment but action. Dr. Wortle's support for the Peacockes is costly: it threatens his school, his reputation, and his standing in the diocese. It is precisely this cost that marks it as genuine charity:

> "It is for the sake of charity—for the dear love of Christ, as we are taught to think of it." (015-040)

The theological framing is deliberate: Christian charity is modelled on Christ's sacrificial love, not on comfortable benevolence.

## Mrs. Wortle's Charity

While Dr. Wortle articulates the theological case for charity, [[mrs-wortle|Mrs. Wortle]] practises it without theory. Her response to [[mrs-peacocke|Mrs. Peacocke]] is instinctive compassion:

> "Others perhaps may call it—charity." (015-080)

This quiet understatement is Trollope's preferred mode of moral expression. Charity that announces itself is suspect; charity that simply acts is genuine.

## The Absence of Charity

The novel's antagonists are defined by their lack of charity. [[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]]'s campaign against the Peacockes is driven by class resentment and self-righteousness. The Bishop's proceduralism substitutes institutional correctness for love. Both are judged and found wanting by the novel's moral calculus.

> "You would have no more charity than that?" (012-061)

This question, posed to a character who refuses to extend sympathy, is the novel's sharpest moral indictment. The absence of charity is not a minor flaw—it is a fundamental moral failure.
