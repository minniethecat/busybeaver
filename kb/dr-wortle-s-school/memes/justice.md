## Overview

Justice in Trollope's *Dr. Wortle's School* operates on two registers: the formal justice of ecclesiastical authority and social convention, and the deeper justice of individual conscience. The novel repeatedly shows these registers in tension, and consistently privileges the latter—personal justice, grounded in character—over the former.

## Dr. Wortle's Justice

[[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]'s character is introduced through the lens of justice. He does not merely dispense justice; he identifies with it:

> "He was just, and liked his justice to be recognised." (002-003)

This need for recognition reveals both the strength and the vulnerability of Wortle's moral position. He is genuinely committed to fairness, but he is also aware that justice, to be effective, must be publicly acknowledged.

## Injustice and Its Consequences

The novel's most searing passages concern injustice suffered by the Peacockes. [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]] rails against the treatment they have received:

> "A very scant measure of justice had been shown to the young lady." (008-002)

The injustice is not merely the withholding of sympathy but the active destruction of reputation and livelihood. The novel suggests that institutional injustice—of the kind perpetrated by [[the-bishop-of-broughton|the Bishop]] and [[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]]—is more damaging than individual cruelty because it carries the weight of authority.

## Justice and Ecclesiastical Authority

The Bishop embodies the gap between formal and substantive justice. He follows proper procedures and considers himself impartial, yet his actions produce profound injustice:

> "The cruel injustice which had been done him by the Bishop." (029-013)

Trollope's critique is structural: a system that prioritizes institutional reputation over individual truth will produce injustice even when operated by well-meaning people.

## Justice Restored

The novel's resolution restores justice not through institutional mechanisms but through individual action—Peacocke's journey to America, Dr. Wortle's unwavering support, and the gradual recognition by the community that they have been complicit in injustice. The restoration is personal rather than procedural, affirming Trollope's faith in character over system.
