# Dr. Jeffrey Wortle

## Overview

The Rev. Jeffrey Wortle, D.D. is the protagonist of Anthony Trollope's 1881 novel. He combines the roles of Rector of [[bowick|Bowick]] and headmaster of the school he has established in the village, embodying the Victorian ideal of the clergyman-educator. The novel traces his moral growth as he confronts a scandal that tests both his principles and his institution.

## Role in the Narrative

Dr. Wortle serves as the moral center of the novel. The narrator introduces him as "a man much esteemed by others—and by himself" (002-002), a characterization that captures both his genuine virtues and his susceptibility to pride. The central conflict—whether to stand by Mr. and [[mrs-peacocke|Mrs. Peacocke]] when their marriage is revealed to be irregular—tests his character against the pressures of social opinion, ecclesiastical authority, and institutional survival.

His role shifts through the novel from confident establishment figure to embattled defender of principle. When parents threaten to withdraw their sons, Dr. Wortle must weigh his personal conviction of the Peacockes' moral worth against the practical threat to his school's existence. His eventual decision to support them, at considerable personal and professional cost, constitutes the novel's moral climax.

## Character & Traits

Trollope draws Dr. Wortle with characteristic moral precision. He is a man of genuine principle, generous in his impulses but not immune to vanity. His self-regard—"he had been successful in both professions" (002-002)—is presented with authorial irony rather than condemnation. The narrator allows us to see both his dignity and his occasional absurdity, as when he reflects that "it would not be necessary to give Dr. Wortle's exact idea" (002-003) of his own importance.

His defining trait is a stubborn integrity that eventually overrides his concern for reputation. This makes him, in Trollope's moral universe, a genuinely admirable figure despite his flaws.
