## Overview

Class consciousness pervades Trollope's *Dr. Wortle's School*, structuring relationships between characters and determining how the central scandal is perceived and adjudicated. The novel maps a finely graded Victorian social hierarchy, from the titled aristocracy down through the professional classes to servants, and shows how class position shapes both moral judgment and social power.

## Aristocratic Pretension

[[mrs-stantiloup|the Honourable Mrs. Stantiloup]] embodies the weaponization of class. Her title—"the Honourable"—is her primary tool of social intimidation, used to assert authority over [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]] despite his position as a clergyman and schoolmaster. Her campaign against the Peacockes is conducted in the language of social propriety, but its energy comes from class resentment at being defied by a mere schoolmaster.

## The Professional Middle Class

Dr. Wortle occupies the ambiguous space of the Victorian professional. As a clergyman, he has social standing; as a schoolmaster, he is a tradesman. The novel registers this tension repeatedly. His independence from aristocratic pressure is both admirable and, in the eyes of characters like Mrs. Stantiloup, presumptuous.

## Aristocracy and Morality

The novel does not equate class with virtue. [[lord-bracy|Lord Bracy]] and [[lady-bracy|Lady Bracy]], despite their elevated rank, are among the more reasonable and decent characters. Their son [[lord-carstairs|Lord Carstairs]]'s presence at Bowick School is itself a class marker—the school's quality is validated by aristocratic patronage. Yet the Bracys' initial hesitation about the scandal shows how class solidarity can override individual judgment.

## Class and Marriage

The marriage plot involving [[mary-wortle|Mary Wortle]] and Lord Carstairs is explicitly structured by class. A schoolmaster's daughter marrying an earl's son crosses a social boundary, and the scandal surrounding the Peacockes threatens to make that boundary insurmountable. The novel's resolution, in which the marriage is permitted, affirms that character can override class—but only after the scandal is resolved.
