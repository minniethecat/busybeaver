# Broughton

## Overview

Broughton is the ecclesiastical centre of the diocese in which [[bowick|Bowick]] is situated, the seat of [[bishop-of-broughton|the Bishop of Broughton]], and the home of the cathedral close. It functions as the institutional and moral authority in the novel, the place from which investigations, judgments, and public opinion about the Peacocke scandal emanate. The "Palace of Broughton" is the Bishop's official residence, while the "Close at Broughton" represents the insular clerical community whose gossip and opinion-making drive much of the novel's social tension.

## Description

Broughton is depicted as a cathedral town where ecclesiastical authority is both physically centred and socially concentrated. The Bishop's residence is described with a touch of ironic grandeur: "worship reigned in the Palace of Broughton" [002-004]. The town contains a "close full of cathedral dignitaries" [012-027] — a community of clergy and their families who form the primary audience for the scandal. Broughton is connected to Bowick by road; characters travel between them by "hired gig" [030-008] and "Broughton flys" [029-048], a detail that establishes both the geographical proximity and the social distance between the two locations.

> "The Broughton Gazette, a newspaper which was much devoted to the affairs of the diocese" [018-002] functions as the local organ of public opinion and a vehicle for scandal-mongering.

The town also features an inn where visitors stay, as Mr. Peacocke does after a tense meeting: "still at the inn before he left Broughton" [023-002].

## Role in the Narrative

Broughton plays a dual role as the source of both institutional judgment and social gossip. The Bishop's investigation into the propriety of the Peacocke marriage originates from Broughton, and the "Broughton Gazette" becomes the primary vehicle for publicizing and amplifying the scandal [018-002–022]. The novel's exploration of reputation and moral judgment is geographically anchored in Broughton:

> "Of course, would take care that all Broughton, with its close full of cathedral dignitaries, should know the whole story" [012-027].

The Bishop hosts the Doctor at Broughton for "a couple of days" [014-045] during which crucial conversations about the Peacockes' situation take place. Dr. Wortle's defiance of Broughton opinion — "Who cares what the Broughton Gazette says?" [018-008] — represents his resistance to institutional moral authority.

Ultimately, Broughton's judgment is contained rather than reversed. When the truth emerges, "the Broughton Gazette inserted only a very short paragraph" [030-003], and "there was no more talk about it in the Close at Broughton" [029-057], signalling the dissipation of the scandal.

## Context & Background

Broughton exemplifies the Victorian cathedral town — a microcosm of ecclesiastical society where reputation, gossip, and institutional authority converge. Trollope, drawing on his intimate knowledge of Church of England structures, portrays Broughton's "Close" as a hothouse of clerical opinion that both sustains and threatens the individuals who serve within it. The "Broughton Gazette" represents the power of local journalism in the Victorian era to shape provincial public opinion, often with greater impact on individual lives than the national press.
