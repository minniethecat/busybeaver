## Overview

The *Broughton Gazette* is the local newspaper serving the diocese of Broughton. It enters the novel's narrative when it publishes a response to the London-based [[everybodys-business|*Everybody's Business*]] article about [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]] and the Peacocke scandal. Unlike the London paper, which operates at a national remove, the *Gazette* brings the scandal directly into the community where the events occurred, intensifying the local impact.

## Role in the Narrative

The *Broughton Gazette* appears at a critical moment in the scandal's escalation. After *Everybody's Business* publishes its sensational account, the local paper picks up the story, lending it the weight of local credibility:

> "The Broughton Gazette on the article in Everybody's Business." (019-009)

The *Gazette*'s involvement marks a turning point: what was once local gossip has now become published record, impossible to ignore or dismiss. [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]] is forced to confront not only the London paper's allegations but the local paper's amplification of them. The *Gazette* represents the way in which print media, even at the local level, transforms private matters into public controversies.

Unlike *Everybody's Business*, which is portrayed as purely sensational, the *Broughton Gazette* has a more ambiguous role. As a local paper, it has a responsibility to report on matters affecting the diocese, but its publication of the story inevitably fuels the scandal it reports.

## The Press Ecosystem

The interplay between the *Broughton Gazette* and *Everybody's Business* illustrates the Victorian press ecosystem. A national weekly publishes a sensational story; a local paper picks it up and gives it community credibility; the local response feeds back into the national narrative. Trollope, who was intimately familiar with the newspaper world as a contributor to many periodicals, portrays this feedback loop with sardonic precision.

The *Gazette*'s role also highlights the power asymmetry between national and local media. The London paper can publish with impunity, protected by distance and anonymity; the local paper must navigate its relationship with the community it serves. The *Gazette*'s ambiguous position—reporting a story that damages local figures while claiming journalistic duty—mirrors the ethical compromises of provincial journalism.

## Victorian Context

The 1870s–1880s saw an explosion of provincial newspapers in Britain, facilitated by the repeal of paper duties and the spread of the railways. By 1881, nearly every substantial town had its own newspaper, and these local papers were often the primary source of news for their communities. The *Broughton Gazette* represents this phenomenon: a local paper that both serves and shapes its community's understanding of events, with the power to make or break reputations in the process.
