# Louisiana

## Overview

Louisiana is the American state claimed by the Lefroy brothers as their place of origin and the location of their family plantation, "Kilbrack." It is part of the transatlantic backstory of the Peacocke scandal, representing the remote American South from which the Lefroys emerge and to which they are ultimately consigned. Mrs. Peacocke's father was "a planter in Louisiana" [008-012], grounding her backstory in the antebellum plantation economy.

## Description

Louisiana is described primarily through its association with the Lefroy family and Mrs. Peacocke's origins. The plantation called "Kilbrack" is identified as a Lefroy property: "Ferdinand Lefroy of Kilbrack, Louisiana" [023-081] is the repeated formula. The estate is described as a source of wealth and status — "the Lefroys owned property there" — though the truthfulness of these claims becomes questionable as the novel progresses.

> "Mrs. Peacocke's father was a planter in Louisiana, not far from New Orleans" [008-012] — this establishes her as the daughter of a Southern plantation owner, a detail that adds colour to her backstory and distinguishes her from the entirely English world of Bowick.

The plantation is located "not far from New Orleans" [008-012], situating it in the cotton-growing region of the lower Mississippi. Robert Lefroy's final destination advice — "You'd better go down to Louisiana and make your way straight from there" [026-047] — suggests the plantation as a place of retreat and concealment.

## Role in the Narrative

Louisiana functions as an origin myth for the Lefroy brothers — a place of wealth and status that provides their initial claim to respectability. Ferdinand Lefroy's identity as "Ferdinand Lefroy of Kilbrack, Louisiana" [026-002] is a social credential that impresses in England, where a Southern plantation suggests prosperity and gentility.

However, as the novel progresses, the Louisiana plantation becomes increasingly suspect. The question "Do you think we Lefroys hadn't property of our own down in Louisiana?" [026-049] is Robert Lefroy's blustering defense, but the narrative has already undermined the Lefroys' claims to respectability. By the novel's end, Louisiana represents not genuine wealth but the fabricated identity of men who have used geographical distance to construct false histories.

The connection between England and Louisiana through Mrs. Peacocke's parentage also introduces the transatlantic dimension of identity: she is the daughter of an American planter, married to an English schoolmaster, and entangled with men who claim a Louisiana plantation as their inheritance.

## Context & Background

Louisiana in the 1870s–80s was a post-Reconstruction Southern state, its plantation economy transformed by the Civil War and emancipation. Trollope's choice of Louisiana as the Lefroys' claimed origin exploits the Victorian English fascination with the American South — a place associated with wealth, slavery's legacy, and social codes different from England's. The name "Kilbrack" itself is notable: it sounds Irish rather than American, a detail that may hint at the fabricated nature of the Lefroys' claims. For Trollope's readers, a Louisiana plantation was exotic enough to be impressive but distant enough to be unverifiable — the perfect material for a confidence trick.
