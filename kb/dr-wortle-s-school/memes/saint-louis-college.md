## Overview

Saint Louis College is the American institution where [[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]] served as vice-president before coming to [[bowick|Bowick]]. Although never directly depicted in the novel, the college is the setting of the backstory that generates the entire plot: it was there that Peacocke met his future wife, then married to Ferdinand Lefroy, and there that the ambiguous circumstances of Lefroy's presumed death first arose.

## Role in the Narrative

The college exists in the novel primarily as a site of origin—the place where the tangled history of the Peacockes and the Lefroy brothers began. Peacocke's position there as vice-president is a mark of his scholarly credentials:

> "He had become vice-president of a classical college at Saint Louis in the State of Missouri." (003-002)

The college represents both Peacocke's academic distinction and his rootlessness. An Oxford man transplanted to the American frontier, he inhabits a world far from the English establishment that will later judge him. The narrator notes that he "deserted his college for the sake of establishing a school in England" [003-003], suggesting that the American institution was always a way station rather than a destination.

The college also connects to the novel's broader interest in education. Bowick is "a classical college" in miniature [010-014], and Peacocke's experience at Saint Louis informs his teaching at Bowick. The two institutions—one American, one English, one a college, one a preparatory school—form a transatlantic educational axis.
