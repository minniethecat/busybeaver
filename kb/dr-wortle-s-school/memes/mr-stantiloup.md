## Overview

The Honourable Mr. Stantiloup is a gentleman of the local aristocracy who lives with his wife, the Honourable [[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]], about twenty miles from [[bowick|Bowick]]. He is first mentioned at [002-005] as "a certain Honourable Mr. Stantiloup," involved in a dispute with [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]] concerning his son. Though titled and respectable, Mr. Stantiloup is a conspicuously passive figure — he is far less prominent than his wife, who emerges as one of the novel's secondary antagonists. He rarely speaks in his own voice and appears to follow his wife's lead rather than driving events himself.

## Role in the Narrative

Mr. Stantiloup's narrative function is twofold. First, the removal of his son from [[bowick|Bowick]] School provides the catalyst for the Stantiloup family's enduring grudge against [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]. The initial conflict is described at [002-005] and elaborated upon at [002-010], where Trollope refers to "the case of young Stantiloup" and the boy's withdrawal from the school. This rupture gives [[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]] the pretext she needs to wage her campaign of gossip and social sabotage against the Doctor.

Second, Mr. Stantiloup serves as a foil to his wife. While [[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]] is relentless, voluble, and malicious, Mr. Stantiloup remains a shadow — a titled gentleman whose passivity allows the campaign to proceed under the cover of family respectability. At [004-007], [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]] calls [[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]] "that abominable virago," but no such epithet is ever directed at Mr. Stantiloup, who seems to escape the Doctor's direct wrath precisely because he is so inconspicuous.

## Character & Traits

Mr. Stantiloup is defined more by what he is not than by what he is. He is not assertive; he is not the driving force behind the vendetta against [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]; and he is not, by the novel's end, a figure of any narrative consequence. Trollope sketches him as a member of the county gentry — titled, propertied, and socially secure — but entirely overshadowed by his formidable wife. The novel gives him no memorable speeches, no decisive actions, and no independent moral stance. Even when the narrator at [004-005–006] suggests that certain accusations "had perhaps origin[ated]" with him, the qualification is immediately undercut: the reader understands that [[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]] is the true author of the campaign. Mr. Stantiloup's role is essentially to lend his name and station to his wife's crusade without ever taking the reins himself.

## Relationships

- **[[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]]** — His wife, who dominates the marriage and the Stantiloup family's public posture. She is the real antagonist; he is her silent partner.
- **[[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]** — The Doctor and Mr. Stantiloup are adversaries by circumstance, not by personal enmity. The dispute over young Stantiloup's removal from [[bowick|Bowick]] sours relations between the families, but it is [[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]], not her husband, who prosecutes the feud.
- **Young Stantiloup (son)** — The boy whose removal from [[bowick|Bowick]] School at [002-010] triggers the family's lasting grudge. The son himself does not reappear as a character, but his case is the narrative seed of the conflict.
