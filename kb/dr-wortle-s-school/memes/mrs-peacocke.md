# Mrs. Peacocke

## Overview

Mrs. Peacocke (née Ella Beaufort) is the central female figure in Anthony Trollope's *[[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]'s School* (1881). The wife of the assistant master at [[bowick|Bowick]] School, she becomes the subject of a scandal when it emerges that her first husband — long presumed dead — is alive, rendering her marriage to [[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]] legally invalid. Her character exemplifies Trollope's interest in moral complexity: she is at once the victim of circumstances beyond her control and a figure whose presence tests the principles of those around her.

## Role in the Narrative

Mrs. Peacocke arrives at [[bowick|Bowick]] with her husband as a figure of refinement and capability. She is quickly recognized as "undoubtedly a lady" [003-003] and succeeds in her [[education|teaching]] duties, particularly in music [003-007]. Her social position seems secure until the revelation of her prior marriage in America forces a reckoning. The narrative presents her not as a wrongdoer but as a woman caught between two incompatible sets of moral and legal claims.

Her function in the novel is twofold. Structurally, she is the catalyst whose situation exposes the moral character of everyone around her — [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]'s integrity, [[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]]'s malice, [[bishop-of-broughton|the Bishop]]'s caution, the press's sensationalism. Thematically, she embodies the Victorian predicament of a woman whose social existence depends entirely on her marital status, and who can be destroyed by circumstances she did not create and cannot remedy.

## Character & Traits

Mrs. Peacocke is consistently portrayed with sympathy and admiration by the narrator. She is "a very beautiful woman" [018-040] whose appearance attracts attention and, in the scandal's grip, suspicion. But Trollope refuses to reduce her to a victim or an object; she possesses considerable moral courage and self-possession.

Her response to the newspaper attack reveals her character: when [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]] finds her with the offending paper, she does not collapse into despair but engages him directly on the question of justice [018-046]. She is capable of looking "up into [[[mrs-wortle|Mrs. Wortle]]'s] face" and naming the fear that haunts her — a gesture of honesty rather than evasion [024-004].

Her defining qualities include:

- **Dignity under pressure**: She bears the scandal with restraint, never demanding the protection she receives
- **Musical accomplishment**: Her [[education|teaching]] of music is noted as a particular success at the school [003-007]
- **Loyalty to her husband**: Her devotion to [[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]] is presented as genuine and unwavering
- **Moral clarity**: She understands the implications of her position without self-pity or self-deception

## Relationships

- **[[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]]**: Her second husband; their mutual devotion is one of the novel's moral certainties. His willingness to travel to America to resolve her legal status demonstrates the depth of their bond
- **[[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]**: Her employer and defender; his refusal to dismiss her even under intense social pressure is the novel's central moral action
- **[[mrs-wortle|Mrs. Wortle]]**: Becomes her closest friend and ally at [[bowick|Bowick]], visiting her when others would not [024-002]
- **[[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]]**: Her chief persecutor, who treats her as a symbol of impropriety to be expelled [016-032]
- **[[ferdinand-lefroy|Ferdinand Lefroy]]**: Her first husband, whose reappearance is the source of her predicament; married in St. Louis when she was young [004-009][004-010]

## The American Marriage

The circumstances of Mrs. Peacocke's first marriage are central to the novel's moral argument. As a young woman in St. Louis — Ella Beaufort, the daughter of a ruined Southern planter — she married [[ferdinand-lefroy|Ferdinand Lefroy]], who subsequently disappeared and was presumed dead [004-010]. Her marriage to [[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]] was undertaken in good faith, both parties believing her a widow. When Lefroy was discovered to be alive, her legal position became untenable: she was, in the eyes of the law, still married to her first husband, and her life with [[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]] was technically adulterous.

Trollope uses this legal-technical dilemma to explore the gap between moral reality and social forms. The novel's resolution — [[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]]'s journey to America and Lefroy's eventual death — restores legal order, but the moral argument has already been made: Mrs. Peacocke's innocence was never in genuine doubt.
