## Overview

The Bishop of Broughton is the senior [[church-of-england|Church of England]] clergyman in the diocese where [[bowick|Bowick]] School is located. Initially a supporter of [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]'s educational project, he becomes entangled in the scandal surrounding [[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]]'s irregular marriage. Acting on pressure from local society figures, the Bishop questions [[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]]'s moral fitness to teach, setting him on a collision course with [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]], who defends his assistant master. The Bishop's institutional authority and [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]'s personal sense of justice create the central institutional conflict of the novel.

## Role in the Narrative

The Bishop serves as a representative of institutional Church authority in tension with individual conscience. He is first introduced as a patron of [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]'s school:

> "This Bishop of Broughton had been one of that large band of parish clergymen who had determined that the Doctor's school should be the school for their sons." [002-004]

When rumors about [[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]]'s marriage begin to circulate, the Bishop feels compelled by his office to investigate:

> "The Doctor, instigated by the Bishop, had determined to ask some questions as to the circumstances of the school." [006-002]

The Bishop interviews [[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]] directly in Chapter 15, an encounter that reveals the gap between institutional procedure and human reality. Despite his office, the Bishop is portrayed as a man susceptible to social pressure from figures like [[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]] and [[lady-de-lawle|Lady De Lawle]]:

> "Certain old women have got hold of the Bishop, and made him feel that he ought to do something." [006-009]

His conflict with [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]] escalates through a formal letter of rebuke, which [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]] perceives as unjust and injudicious. The Bishop's authority is ultimately revealed as limited — he cannot actually force [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]] to dismiss [[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]], nor can he control the public opinion that eventually vindicates the Peacockes.

## Character & Traits

Trollope presents the Bishop as a man of decency and good intentions, but one whose office makes him a vehicle for institutional pressure:

> "Now the Bishop was a man who could be very courteous, but who, when he was driven, could be very stern." [016-016]

He is not a villain in the conventional sense. [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]] himself acknowledges that the Bishop acted without personal malice:

> "There is no malice in the Bishop's mind against me. He would if he could make me quite comfortable." [020-017]

Nevertheless, Trollope critiques the Bishop's susceptibility to social manipulation and his failure to exercise independent moral judgment. The Bishop represents the danger of institutional thinking — a good man doing harm because his role demands conformity over conscience.

## Relationships

- **[[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]]** — The Bishop interviews him and finds him intellectually formidable but morally suspect by conventional standards. The Bishop "was unable not to put in a word" [015-020] against [[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]]'s position.
- **[[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]** — Initially friends, their relationship fractures when the Bishop issues a formal rebuke. [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]] considers "going to law with the Bishop" [020-012] but ultimately chooses a different course.
- **[[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]]** — One of the "old women" who pressure the Bishop, using social influence to drive institutional action against the Peacockes.
- **[[lord-bracy|Lord Bracy]]** — A local aristocrat whose social position intersects with the Bishop's ecclesiastical authority.
- **[[mrs-rolland|Mrs. Rolland]]** — The Bishop's wife, who appears at the social gathering in Chapter 16 and aligns herself with conventional opinion.
