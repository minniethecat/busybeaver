## Overview

Lady De Lawle is a minor aristocratic figure who aligns herself with the Honourable [[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]] in the campaign against [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]] during the Peacocke scandal. She is the mother of a young son at [[bowick|Bowick]] School and is portrayed as a representative of the social pressure brought to bear upon [[bishop-of-broughton|the Bishop]] of [[diocese-of-broughton|Broughton]]. Her role, though limited in page-time, is significant in illustrating how the scandal mobilized local society against [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]] through informal networks of aristocratic women.

## Role in the Narrative

Lady De Lawle first appears as the mother of a [[bowick|Bowick]] pupil:

> "Young De Lawle, who was only nine years old, the only son of a Lady De Lawle, who had in early years been a friend of the Doctor's." [003-012]

Despite her earlier friendship with [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]], she becomes one of his opponents when the scandal breaks. Trollope reports her involvement alongside [[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]] as a key instigator:

> "But this was not the end of it. Lady De Lawle declared that she could not bear to think that her boy should be at a school in which such a person as Mr. Peacocke was allowed to teach." [004-004]

She is grouped with the forces pressuring [[bishop-of-broughton|the Bishop]]:

> "You are to know it all before the Doctor or the Bishop — before Mrs. Wortle or the Hon. Mrs. Stantiloup, or Lady De Lawle." [004-008]

Her "soft heart" is mentioned ironically — her compassion is selective, directed more towards social propriety than towards the individuals caught in the scandal:

> "Lady De Lawle's soft heart will rejoice because the boy will have been removed from danger." [004-019]

By the novel's later chapters, she serves as a marker of social difference: [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]'s conduct is "different in not accepting Lady De Lawle's hospitality" [007-021].

## Character & Traits

Lady De Lawle represents the respectable social opposition that [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]] faces — not villainous in intention, but harmful in effect. Her friendship with the Doctor is real but shallow, unable to survive a test of social courage. Trollope treats her with characteristic irony, noting the gap between her self-image as a woman of tender feeling and her willingness to participate in the ostracism of the Peacockes. She is one of those whom [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]'s principled stand exposes as governed more by social conformity than by individual conscience.

## Relationships

- **[[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]]** — Her principal ally in agitating against the school. Together they represent the organized social pressure that drives [[bishop-of-broughton|the Bishop]]'s investigation.
- **[[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]** — A former friend who becomes the target of her social disapproval. Their estrangement illustrates the cost of the Doctor's principled stand.
- **[[bishop-of-broughton|The Bishop of Broughton]]** — She is among those who "get hold of [[bishop-of-broughton|the Bishop]]" [006-009] and push him toward action against [[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]].
- **[[lord-carstairs|Lord Carstairs]]** — Not directly connected, but her son is a fellow student at [[bowick|Bowick]], and her withdrawal of him contributes to the school's crisis.
