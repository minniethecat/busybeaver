## Overview

Lady Altamont, the Marchioness of Altamont, is a minor aristocrat in the social orbit of [[bowick|Bowick]] School. She is mentioned only once in the narrative — when she pays a second visit to [[bowick|Bowick]] — but her presence in the text serves a structural purpose: she is one of the titled personages whose patronage establishes [[bowick|Bowick]] as a school of social consequence. In Trollope's world, the presence of a marchioness at a school function is never incidental; it is a marker of institutional prestige and a signal to the reader that [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]'s establishment is one where the aristocracy feel at home.

## Role in the Narrative

Lady Altamont appears as a single brushstroke in Trollope's portrait of [[bowick|Bowick]]'s social world. Chapter III, which introduces the school's setting and [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]'s position in the community, names her alongside other local dignitaries who frequent the school:

> "She was the eldest son of the Marchioness of Altamont; and when Lady Altamont paid a second visit to Bowick, the two mothers between them arranged that the young people should meet." (003-007)

The passage is brief but illuminating. Lady Altamont is not merely a name dropped for ornament; she is an active participant in the social engineering that Victorian aristocratic mothers conducted through school visits. The "arrangement" between Lady Altamont and another mother — the context suggests it concerns the introduction of eligible young people — typifies the secondary function of a school like [[bowick|Bowick]]: it is not only a place of [[education|learning]] but a theatre for social alliance.

Her title, Marchioness, places her just below a duchess in the peerage — high enough to confer genuine lustre on any gathering she attends. That she visits [[bowick|Bowick]] not once but twice ("a second visit") suggests a sustained relationship with the Wortle family, and her willingness to be seen there provides implicit endorsement of [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]'s establishment.

Lady Altamont does not reappear in the narrative, and her role in the Peacocke scandal — if any — is unrecorded. She belongs to the novel's background architecture: one of the many titled figures whose presence establishes the social register within which the drama unfolds.

## Key Appearances

Lady Altamont's sole appearance in the text is at [003-007], during the early chapters that establish [[bowick|Bowick]]'s setting and social standing. The reference is embedded in a passage that enumerates the school's aristocratic connections — a rhetorical strategy Trollope employs to persuade the reader that [[bowick|Bowick]] is not a provincial backwater but a school of real consequence.

The brevity of her appearance is not a sign of thin characterisation but of Trollope's narrative economy: Lady Altamont is exactly as important as she needs to be, and no more. Her name — "Altamont" — is itself resonant, suggesting (like "Bracy" and "[[lord-carstairs|Carstairs]]") the kind of landed family name that Trollope's contemporary readers would have recognised as authentically aristocratic.

## Relationships

- **[[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]** — Headmaster of [[bowick|Bowick]]. Lady Altamont's visits to the school imply a cordial relationship with the Wortle family, though no direct interaction is recorded.

- **[[bowick|Bowick]]** — The school she visits. Her presence, alongside that of other titled families, contributes to [[bowick|Bowick]]'s reputation as a school suitable for the sons of the aristocracy.
