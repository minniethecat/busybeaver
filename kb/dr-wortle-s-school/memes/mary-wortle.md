## Overview

Mary Wortle is the only child of [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Jeffrey Wortle]] and [[mrs-wortle|Mrs. Wortle]], and a central figure in the domestic life of [[bowick|Bowick]] School. She is described as beautiful and lovable, attracting the romantic attention of the [[lord-carstairs|young Lord Carstairs]], a student at the school. Her subplot — involving questions of class, social propriety, and parental concern — mirrors the novel's broader themes about the tension between personal feeling and social convention. She also appears as a perceptive observer of the Peacocke scandal, showing curiosity and concern about the matter that preoccupies her parents.

## Role in the Narrative

Mary is introduced early as [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]'s cherished daughter:

> "They had one child, a daughter, Mary, of whom it was said in Bowick that the Duke of Broughton himself had never seen a sweeter-favoured girl than Mary Wortle. Her father had been all his life so anxious for her welfare." [002-008]

Her presence raises a delicate question for the school: what happens when a young aristocrat like [[lord-carstairs|Lord Carstairs]] becomes romantically interested in the headmaster's daughter? [[mrs-wortle|Mrs. Wortle]] voices this anxiety:

> "'Will it not be bad for Mary?' Mrs. Wortle had said anxiously." [008-003]

The romantic subplot develops through walks, games of lawn-tennis, and private conversations. Mary is not a passive figure — she engages with [[lord-carstairs|Lord Carstairs]], asks after [[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]]'s mysterious wife, and shows awareness of the social complexities around her:

> "'I think it is about his wife,' said Mary." [014-011]

When [[lord-carstairs|Lord Carstairs]] confesses his feelings, Mary's response is measured and proper, reflecting her upbringing. The subplot resolves in the novel's final chapters, with [[lord-carstairs|Lord Carstairs]]'s father, [[lord-bracy|Lord Bracy]], ultimately approving the match after [[peacocke-scandal|the Peacocke affair]] is settled.

## Character & Traits

Trollope presents Mary Wortle as a model of natural grace and propriety. She is beautiful without vanity, perceptive without guile:

> "Nothing more lovable than Mary Wortle had he ever seen. He had almost made up his mind that he must have her for his wife." [021-008]

She possesses a quiet intelligence — she correctly intuits that [[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]]'s distress concerns his wife, and she understands the social stakes of her situation with [[lord-carstairs|Lord Carstairs]] without needing them explained. Her father's protectiveness and her mother's anxiety reflect the value placed upon her, and her own conduct throughout the novel justifies their high regard.

## Relationships

- **[[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]** — His beloved only daughter; he has been "all his life so anxious for her welfare" [002-008]. Her romantic situation tests his principles about class and character.
- **[[mrs-wortle|Mrs. Wortle]]** — [[mrs-wortle|Mrs. Wortle]] is acutely aware of the social risks to Mary's reputation from [[lord-carstairs|Lord Carstairs]]'s attentions.
- **[[lord-carstairs|Lord Carstairs]]** — The young aristocrat and student who falls in love with Mary. Their relationship crosses class boundaries and forms a romantic subplot.
- **[[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]]** — Mary observes his distress with concern and correctly intuits that it relates to his wife.
- **[[lord-bracy|Lord Bracy]]** — His eventual approval of the match between his son and Mary is a social validation that parallels the vindication of the Peacockes.
