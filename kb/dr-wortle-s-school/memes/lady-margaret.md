# Lady Margaret

## Overview

Lady Margaret is a minor character in Anthony Trollope's *[[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]'s School* (1881). The wife of a county member of Parliament, she is a lady of fashion, rank, and wealth who lives about ten miles from [[bowick|Bowick]]. She is first cousin to [[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]], with whom she shares both blood and a disposition towards aristocratic gossip. Her single visit to the Wortle household in Chapter XV serves as a narrative device through which Trollope illustrates how the Peacocke scandal rippled outward from its origin at [[bowick|Bowick]] School into the drawing-rooms of county society — and how the curiosity of the well-born could be as intrusive and wounding as open hostility.

## Role in the Narrative

Lady Margaret appears only once, in the aftermath of the revelations about the [[peacocke-scandal|Peacockes' marriage]]. She calls upon [[mrs-wortle|Mrs. Wortle]] ostensibly for a social visit, but her real purpose is to extract information. The narrator introduces her with careful social placement: she is "a lady, who lived about ten miles off, the wife of a county member, a lady of fashion, of rank, and of wealth" — and, significantly, "was first cousin to that [[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]]" [015-050]. This kinship immediately signals to the reader that her visit is not disinterested.

Her opening sally — "What a romance this is, [[mrs-wortle|Mrs. Wortle]], that has gone all through the diocese!" [015-051] — encapsulates the role she plays: the well-bred inquisitor who masks prurient interest behind the language of polite concern. When [[mrs-wortle|Mrs. Wortle]] attempts to defend the Peacockes, Lady Margaret's reply is coolly sceptical: "I suppose they thought so before" [015-057]. She is not present to be persuaded; she is present to gather material for the next round of county gossip.

Lady Margaret's significance extends beyond her single scene. [[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]] later invokes her name in the gossip network: "Lady Margaret — [[lady-bracy|Lady Bracy]] absolutely asked her to go to [[lord-carstairs|Carstairs]]!" [016-026]. This passing reference confirms that Lady Margaret is an active node in the circulation of rumour, transmitting information between the Wortle household, the Stantiloup circle, and the Bracy connection at [[lord-carstairs|Carstairs]].

## Character & Traits

Lady Margaret is drawn with Trollope's characteristic economy. She is not a villain in the mould of her cousin [[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]] — she mounts no campaign, writes no letters, organises no withdrawals. Yet her brief appearance is in some ways more damning, precisely because it is so ordinary. She represents the rank-and-file of county society: well-dressed, well-connected, and entirely convinced that the misfortunes of others are legitimate entertainment.

Her defining traits include:

- **Social entitlement**: As a woman of rank and wealth, she assumes an unquestioned right to pry into the private affairs of her neighbours. Her visit to [[mrs-wortle|Mrs. Wortle]] is conducted under the flag of friendship, but the questions she asks are those of a cross-examiner.
- **Moral incuriosity**: She arrives with her conclusions already formed. [[mrs-wortle|Mrs. Wortle]]'s attempts to explain the Peacockes' position are met not with reconsideration but with a weary, worldly scepticism [015-057].
- **Gossip as currency**: Lady Margaret trades in information. Her kinship with [[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]] ensures that whatever she gleans from [[mrs-wortle|Mrs. Wortle]] will be circulated through the most effective channels of social damage available in the diocese.
- **Polished intrusiveness**: Trollope conveys her manner not as coarse or openly hostile, but as smoothly inquisitorial — the drawing-room equivalent of a magistrate taking evidence, softened by tea and the forms of civility.

Unlike the strident [[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]], who is described even by her ally [[bishop-of-broughton|the Bishop]] as an "abominable virago" [004-007], Lady Margaret does not provoke the same narratorly condemnation. She is the more insidious figure: the respectable woman whose idle curiosity causes real harm without ever requiring her to raise her voice.

## Relationships

- **[[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]]**: Lady Margaret's first cousin and natural ally in gossip. Where [[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]] wages active war on the Wortle establishment, Lady Margaret serves as an informal intelligence-gatherer, feeding information back into the Stantiloup network [015-050, 016-026].
- **[[mrs-wortle|Mrs. Wortle]]**: The target of Lady Margaret's visit. [[mrs-wortle|Mrs. Wortle]] receives her with the hospitality due to a lady of rank, only to find herself subtly interrogated about the most painful episode in the school's recent history [015-051].
- **[[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]**: Not directly interacted with, but Lady Margaret's visit to his wife is unmistakably a reconnaissance mission aimed at assessing the headmaster's position and the viability of the Peacockes' continued presence at [[bowick|Bowick]].
- **[[lady-bracy|Lady Bracy]]**: Referenced in connection with Lady Margaret in [[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]]'s gossip about [[lord-carstairs|Carstairs]], suggesting that Lady Margaret moves in the same elevated social circles as the Bracy family [016-026].
- **[[lord-carstairs|Lord Carstairs]]**: Connected indirectly through the gossip chain; Lady Margaret's interest in [[bowick|Bowick]] affairs extends to the young aristocrat who is a pupil at the school.
