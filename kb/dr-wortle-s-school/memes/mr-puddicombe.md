## Overview

The Reverend Mr. Puddicombe is the vicar of the next parish to [[bowick|Bowick]] and a fellow clergyman in the [[diocese-of-broughton|Diocese of Broughton]]. Trollope introduces him as "a clergyman without a flaw who did his duty excellently in every station of life" [007-059] — a description that establishes him as both an unimpeachable moral figure and, initially, a potential irritant to the more worldly [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]].

Puddicombe is one of the novel's most carefully developed supporting characters, important enough to lend his name to two chapter titles: "[[mrs-wortle|Mrs. Wortle]] and Mr. Puddicombe" [009-004] and "Mr. Puddicombe's Boot" [017-002]. His narrative arc traces a quiet but significant transformation — from a neighbour whose well-meaning inquiries strike [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]] as intrusive, to the Doctor's most trusted local ally, to the clergyman who ultimately admits he has been "unkind and unjust" [018-037] and accompanies the Doctor to London to assist at the Peacockes' [[marriage]] [030-003].

Unlike the venomous [[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]] or the temporising Bishop, Puddicombe represents the conscientious Anglican clergyman grappling honestly with a difficult moral question. He has pretty daughters and gives dinners; he is comfortable, respectable, and entirely conventional — yet he proves capable of growth when confronted with the human reality of the [[peacocke-scandal]].

## Role in the Narrative

Puddicombe first appears when he calls on [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]] to inquire about the appointment of [[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]] as an assistant master. His questioning is "very different from [[mr-pearson|Mr. Pearson]]'s listless manner" [007-060] — he presses for details because he genuinely wants to understand what is happening in the parish next door. [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]], already prickly on the subject, receives these inquiries with barely concealed irritation. Puddicombe also brings word of [[bishop-of-broughton|the Bishop]]'s intentions regarding the appointment [007-065], positioning him early as a conduit between ecclesiastical authority and the Doctor's fiercely independent conscience.

When the scandal breaks, Puddicombe becomes [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]'s chosen confidant. The Doctor "sent over to Mr. Puddicombe and asked for some assistance" [012-025] and resolves to tell him the full story [012-027]. [[mrs-wortle|Mrs. Wortle]] narrates the circumstances of the Peacockes' [[marriage]] to him in detail [012-066] — a significant act of trust. Puddicombe's response is characteristically measured: he does not think the situation would alter his opinion of [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]'s school [012-093], offering the kind of temperate, considered judgment that the impulsive Doctor both needs and resents.

When the anonymous newspaper attack appears in the *[[diocese-of-broughton|Broughton]] Gazette*, [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]] again turns to Puddicombe [018-009], who reads the article [018-010] and offers counsel. It is in this conversation that Puddicombe makes his own moral turn, admitting his previous harshness: he acknowledges he has been "unkind and unjust" to [[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]] [018-037]. [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]'s response — that he will love his wife "in spite of all the Puddicombes in creation" [018-039] — is both a declaration of independence and an affectionate recognition of how far the two clergymen have come.

In the novel's final movement, Puddicombe takes on a quietly heroic role. When [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]] receives the letter that will change everything, he goes straight to Puddicombe [029-015], who advises: "not a word. Live it down" [029-025]. He then accompanies the Doctor to London [029-035] — a significant journey for a country vicar — and together they go to "marry them" [029-047], the Peacockes, in what becomes the novel's culminating act of vindication. At the wedding, "[[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]] and Mr. Puddicombe had assisted" [030-003], and Puddicombe's sympathy proves genuine, even if his "morals were perhaps not quite so strict" as those of more censorious observers [030-044].

## Character & Traits

Puddicombe is defined by a cluster of qualities that make him, in Trollope's hands, a more interesting character than the stock figure of the pious clergyman. His initial manner is one of earnest inquiry — he asks questions not from malice but from a sense of pastoral responsibility. Trollope notes that [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]] at one point "had hated Mr. Puddicombe" [014-002] — a strong word that captures the tension between the Doctor's instinctive resistance to outside judgment and Puddicombe's conscientious, sometimes uncomfortable, probity.

Yet Puddicombe is neither a hypocrite nor a prig. When confronted with the full human reality of the Peacockes' situation, he revises his opinion — a rare and admirable quality. His admission that he has been "unkind and unjust" [018-037] is the moral pivot of his character, distinguishing him from characters like [[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]] who never waver in their condemnation. He also possesses a practical streak, as seen when he suggests concrete steps to [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]] [014-004] and when he physically accompanies the Doctor to London — acts that demonstrate his loyalty is not merely verbal.

Trollope sketches his domestic life with characteristic economy: Puddicombe has pretty daughters, gives dinners, and occupies a comfortable place in the rural clerical hierarchy. He is, in many ways, the novel's vision of what the [[church-of-england|Church of England]] ought to be — a man of principle who is also a man of the world, capable of growth when principle and pastoral care come into conflict.

## Relationships

- **[[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]**: The central relationship. Initially wary, often irritated, [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]] comes to rely on Puddicombe as his most trusted local confidant. Their dynamic — the impetuous Doctor and the measured vicar — is one of the novel's most carefully modulated friendships. [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]'s declaration that he will act "in spite of all the Puddicombes in creation" [018-039] is, paradoxically, a tribute: Puddicombe is the standard against which the Doctor measures his own conscience.

- **[[mrs-wortle|Mrs. Wortle]]**: Entrusted with telling Puddicombe the full story of the Peacockes' situation [012-066], [[mrs-wortle|Mrs. Wortle]] acts as the bridge between the two clergymen, her gentle candour winning Puddicombe's sympathy where the Doctor's combativeness might not.

- **[[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]]**: Puddicombe's evolution on the Peacocke question is the measure of his character. He moves from cautious disapproval — shared with most of the diocese — to the frank admission that he has been unjust, and finally to the active role of assisting at the Peacockes' wedding. His journey mirrors the novel's larger argument for charitable judgment.

- **[[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]]**: Never directly connected in the text, the two represent opposite poles of clerical response to scandal. Where [[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]] weaponises moral outrage, Puddicombe wrestles with it — and ultimately transcends it.
