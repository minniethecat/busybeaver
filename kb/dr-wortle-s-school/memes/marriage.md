## Overview

Marriage in Anthony Trollope's *Dr. Wortle's School* is more than a plot device—it is the institution through which the novel examines legal validity, moral obligation, social respectability, and the nature of honour. The central question—whether [[mrs-peacocke|Mrs. Peacocke]]'s marriage to [[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]] is valid, given the possible survival of her first husband Ferdinand Lefroy—drives every major character to declare where they stand on the relationship between law, church teaching, and personal conscience.

## The Legal Question

The Peacocke case hinges on a specific legal and ecclesiastical problem: if a woman believed her first husband dead and remarried in good faith, is the second marriage valid if the first husband later proves to have been alive at the time of the remarriage? Victorian marriage law, shaped by the [[church-of-england|Church of England]]'s canonical rules and civil statutes, treated bigamy as a serious offence, even when unintentional.

The novel traces the ambiguity from rumour to crisis. Robert Lefroy's claim that his brother Ferdinand survived forces the question into the open, and the Peacockes must live under the shadow of potential illegitimacy until Peacocke's journey to America establishes the facts.

## Moral vs. Legal Validity

Trollope uses the marriage question to distinguish between legal technicality and moral reality. [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]'s position—that the Peacockes are "married in the sight of God" regardless of what the law might say—places conscience above ecclesiastical authority. [[mrs-wortle|Mrs. Wortle]] arrives at the same conclusion through a different route, her plain moral sense cutting through legal complexity:

> "A woman cannot be anyone's wife unless she be his wife." (012-054)

## Social Consequences

The threatened invalidity of the Peacocke marriage carries cascading social consequences. It affects Mr. Peacocke's fitness to teach boys, to preach in church, and to hold any position of trust. It threatens the reputation of [[bowick|Bowick School]]. And it jeopardizes the romantic prospects of [[mary-wortle|Mary Wortle]], whose association with the scandal might damage her marriageability in the eyes of aristocratic families like the Bracys.

## The Resolution

The novel's resolution—Peacocke's journey to America, the confirmation of Ferdinand Lefroy's death, and the validation of the marriage—restores social order. But Trollope's deeper point is that the moral validity of the marriage was never truly in question; the legal machinery and social gossip that threatened it are exposed as lesser authorities than individual conscience and Christian charity.
