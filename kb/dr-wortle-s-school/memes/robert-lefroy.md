## Overview

Robert Lefroy is the younger of two brothers from Louisiana, both styled "[[ferdinand-lefroy|Colonel Lefroy]]," and serves as the novel's most direct antagonist. He is the brother of [[ferdinand-lefroy|Ferdinand Lefroy]] — the man who married [[mrs-peacocke|Ella Beaufort]] (now [[mrs-peacocke|Mrs. Peacocke]]) in America and whose uncertain fate lies at the heart of the [[peacocke-scandal]]. Robert Lefroy arrives at [[bowick|Bowick]] uninvited, intent on extracting money from [[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]] by threatening to reveal that Ferdinand may still be alive, which would render the Peacockes' marriage invalid. Described as shabby, "rummy-looking," and insolent, he lodges at the local public-house and spreads damaging gossip throughout the parish before being bought off and ultimately accompanying [[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]] back to America.

## Role in the Narrative

Robert Lefroy functions as the external catalyst who transforms the Peacockes' private marital dilemma into a public scandal. Until his arrival, the question of whether [[ferdinand-lefroy|Ferdinand Lefroy]] was truly dead remained a secret known only to the Peacockes and [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]. Robert Lefroy's appearance at [[bowick|Bowick]] forces the issue into the open.

His motives are purely mercenary. He threatens to "tell it to all the world" [010-073] unless [[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]] pays him. He leverages his knowledge of his brother Ferdinand's movements — knowledge that could either confirm or deny the validity of the Peacockes' marriage — as a bargaining chip. The Doctor, [[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]], and Lefroy hold a conference at which a plan of action is agreed: Lefroy will return to America, with [[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]], to settle the matter. This journey occupies the latter portion of the novel.

Lefroy's death, reported in due course, resolves the central conflict. The news that "[[ferdinand-lefroy|Colonel Lefroy]] was dead" [011-045] arrives as a deliverance, clearing the way for the Peacockes' marriage to be recognized as valid.

## Key Appearances

- **Backstory (Chapter IV):** Robert Lefroy is first mentioned in [[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]]'s retrospective account. The two Lefroy brothers hailed from Louisiana; Ferdinand, the elder, married [[mrs-peacocke|Ella Beaufort]]. When Ferdinand disappeared, [[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]] sought out Robert Lefroy and learned that Ferdinand was believed dead [004-015]. It was Robert who provided the information that enabled the Peacockes to marry.
- **The Shocking Return (Chapter IV):** [[ferdinand-lefroy|Ferdinand Lefroy]] reappeared "all of a sudden" in his wife's little drawing-room [004-016], apparently alive and well. This revelation shattered the Peacockes' belief in the validity of their marriage.
- **Chapter VII — "Robert Lefroy":** Lefroy is given his own chapter title, underscoring his importance. He arrives at [[bowick|Bowick]], confrontational and mocking: "Come, come; that won't do. You know me well enough. I'm Robert Lefroy" [008-060]. [[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]] immediately recognizes him as the brother of his wife's first husband [008-061].
- **The Confrontation (Chapter X):** [[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]] realizes he must face Lefroy directly: "Robert Lefroy. I must go to him at once." [010-011]. The encounter is marked by Lefroy's insolence and mockery [010-012]. He points upstairs toward [[mrs-peacocke|Mrs. Peacocke]], a gesture of intimate threat [010-056 — 010-057]. [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]] intervenes, telling him bluntly: "The sooner you go the better" [010-082].
- **The Public-House Gossip (Chapter XIV):** Lefroy spreads scandal at the local inn. "He" who spread the gossip "had of course been Robert Lefroy" [014-007]. He tells the patrons everything he knows [014-021], making the scandal impossible to contain.
- **The Conference and Agreement (Chapter XIV):** At a meeting with [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]] and [[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]], a plan is formed. Lefroy assents to return to America, motivated by payment [014-045 — 014-051].
- **The Voyage to America (Chapter XXIII):** Lefroy accompanies [[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]] on the Atlantic crossing to [[san-francisco|San Francisco]]. The journey is tense and quarrelsome: they argue about the route and about money, and Lefroy proves suspicious and uncooperative [023-024 — 023-038]. [[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]] is forced to pay Lefroy's bills to maintain his "close connection" with him [023-002].
- **Death Reported (Chapter XI):** Word comes that [[ferdinand-lefroy|Colonel Lefroy]] is dead [011-045], a deliverance that resolves the marriage question and frees the Peacockes.

## Character & Traits

Robert Lefroy is depicted as an adventurer of the shabbiest kind — a man who trades on family secrets for personal gain. His appearance is distinctive and unsavoury: a black-and-grey streaked beard, a shabby military-style coat, and a general "rummy-looking" aspect that marks him as disreputable. Though he styles himself "[[ferdinand-lefroy|Colonel Lefroy]]," the title carries no genuine military distinction; it is part of the swaggering persona both brothers adopted.

His manner is insolent, mocking, and confrontational. He delights in the power his knowledge gives him over [[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]] and shows no scruple about exploiting it. At the same time, he is pragmatic: when offered money and a passage back to America, he accepts the bargain. He is neither a figure of pure evil nor a sympathetic rogue, but a grasping opportunist who happens to hold the key to the novel's central [[marriage]] question.

## Relationships

- **[[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]]:** Lefroy's antagonist relationship with [[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]] is defined by extortion. He knows that his brother Ferdinand's uncertain fate invalidates the Peacockes' marriage, and he uses this knowledge as leverage. Their interactions are charged with contempt on Lefroy's side and reluctant dependence on Peacocke's — Peacocke must pay Lefroy's bills and endure his company across the Atlantic to resolve the matter.
- **[[mrs-peacocke|Mrs. Peacocke]]:** As the former wife of his brother Ferdinand, [[mrs-peacocke|Mrs. Peacocke]] is Lefroy's sister-in-law under the law he invokes. His presence threatens her social standing, her marriage, and her peace of mind. He never confronts her directly in the narrative, but his pointing gesture upstairs toward her room is one of the novel's most menacing moments.
- **[[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]:** The Doctor despises Lefroy on sight and treats him with barely concealed contempt. It is [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]] who delivers the blunt dismissal — "The sooner you go the better" [010-082] — and who devises the plan to send Lefroy back to America. The Doctor's willingness to pay Lefroy for information [014-035] reflects his pragmatic recognition that money, not morality, moves the man.
- **[[ferdinand-lefroy|Ferdinand Lefroy]] (brother):** Though Ferdinand never appears directly in the narrative's present, his shadow defines Robert's role. Robert is, in every meaningful sense, his brother's surrogate — trading on Ferdinand's name, title, and marriage for profit. The news of Ferdinand's death, and later of Robert's own death, bookend the novel's central plot.
