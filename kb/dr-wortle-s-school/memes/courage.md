## Overview

Courage in *Dr. Wortle's School* is primarily moral rather than physical—the courage to defy public opinion, ecclesiastical authority, and social convention in defence of what is right. [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]'s stand on behalf of the Peacockes is the novel's central act of courage, and the narrative measures every other character against it.

## Dr. Wortle's Courage

Dr. Wortle's courage is tested most severely in his confrontation with [[the-bishop-of-broughton|the Bishop]]. He refuses to dismiss [[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]] despite the Bishop's demand, knowing that defiance may cost him his school, his clerical standing, and his livelihood. His courage is not bluster—it is the quiet determination to do right regardless of cost.

The novel contrasts Wortle's courage with the cowardice of those who know what is right but lack the nerve to act on it. Several parents privately sympathize with the Peacockes but publicly withdraw their sons to protect their own standing.

## Mrs. Peacocke's Fortitude

[[mrs-peacocke|Mrs. Peacocke]] displays a different kind of courage—the fortitude to endure social ostracism without self-pity or bitterness. Her refusal to be ashamed of her situation is an act of moral courage that rivals Wortle's more public stand:

> "I am not ashamed of anything." (028-051)

This quiet defiance is the novel's most radical assertion of moral independence. A woman who refuses the shame that society demands of her is, in Trollope's world, as courageous as any soldier.

## Courage and Social Class

The novel makes an interesting point about the relationship between courage and social position. Dr. Wortle's courage is partly enabled by his professional independence—he owns his school and cannot be dismissed. Others, like the parents who withdraw their sons, lack this independence and their moral failure is partly a function of their circumstances. The novel is not sentimental about this: it judges cowardice, but it understands the pressures that produce it.

## The Quiet Courage of Mrs. Wortle

[[mrs-wortle|Mrs. Wortle]] represents courage of a less dramatic but equally important kind—the courage to maintain ordinary kindness in extraordinary circumstances. She continues to visit [[mrs-peacocke|Mrs. Peacocke]], to speak well of her, and to refuse participation in the social campaign against her, all without making speeches or claiming moral credit. This quiet courage is, in the novel's moral economy, as valuable as her husband's public stand.

## The Cost of Courage

Trollope is careful to show that courage has real costs. Dr. Wortle suffers anxiety, loss of pupils, and strained relations with the diocese. [[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]] endures the humiliation of having his private life made public. The novel does not pretend that courage is rewarded with immediate vindication—it is rewarded, eventually, with the restoration of truth and honour, but the path is painful.

> "He felt as though he were almost ashamed to go into his own school." (020-009)

This moment of vulnerability—the courageous man brought low—is Trollope's most honest acknowledgment of what courage costs.
