## Overview

Jack De Lawle, referred to in the narrative as "young De Lawle" and "Little Jack De Lawle," is a pupil at [[bowick|Bowick]] School and the only son of [[lady-de-lawle|Lady De Lawle]] [003-012]. He is about ten years old at the time of the story. His most significant appearance comes in Chapter III, when he falls into the river during an outing with [[mrs-wortle|Mrs. Wortle]] and is pulled from the water — an incident that, though brief, establishes [[mrs-wortle|Mrs. Wortle]]'s maternal competence and the hazards of pastoral school life. His mother, [[lady-de-lawle|Lady De Lawle]], is a figure in the local aristocracy and a member of the social circle whose opinions shape the reputation of [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]'s establishment.

## Role in the Narrative

Jack De Lawle serves as a device in the early chapters to showcase the domestic character of [[bowick|Bowick]] School and the maternal role played by [[mrs-wortle|Mrs. Wortle]]. The novel's opening chapters labour to establish that [[bowick|Bowick]] is not merely a place of classical [[education|instruction]] but a home — one where the headmaster's wife takes pupils on outings, tends to their scrapes, and exercises a watchful care that extends well beyond the classroom.

The river incident is Trollope at his most economical: young De Lawle, on a spring outing with [[mrs-wortle|Mrs. Wortle]], runs ahead with another boy and a dog, falls into the river, and is pulled out "drenched indeed, frightened, but unharmed" [003-015]. The episode occupies barely a page, but it performs several narrative functions at once. It demonstrates [[mrs-wortle|Mrs. Wortle]]'s quickness and composure; it provides the occasion for [[lady-de-lawle|Lady De Lawle]]'s first appearance in the text; and it introduces the question — never quite resolved in these early chapters — of whether [[lady-de-lawle|Lady De Lawle]] will hold the accident against the school.

Jack's subsequent appearances are limited. He is "put to bed in one of the rooms at the school" after his soaking [004-002], and his mother's reaction — "[[lady-de-lawle|Lady De Lawle]] declared that she could not bear to think of it" [004-004] — sets up a minor tension that Trollope allows to dissipate without consequence. The boy does not reappear as a speaking character, but his existence as a named pupil contributes to the texture of school life against which the novel's central drama unfolds.

## Key Appearances

Jack De Lawle is introduced in Chapter III during the school's spring routine. The narrator identifies him as "a boy about ten years old, the only son of a [[lady-de-lawle|Lady De Lawle]]" [003-012]. His name is deliberately chosen: "De Lawle" is the kind of faintly French, mildly aristocratic surname that Trollope uses to signal gentry status without the full weight of a title.

The river incident unfolds across three paragraphs:

> "Mrs. Wortle had taken young De Lawle and another little boy with her" (003-013)

The outing is pastoral and benign until the dog runs ahead, the boys follow, and Jack falls in:

> "Two minutes afterwards young De Lawle, drenched indeed, frightened, but unharmed, was carried back to the house." (003-015)

The aftermath appears at [004-002], where "Little Jack De Lawle was put to bed in one of the rooms at the school," and his mother's reaction is recorded at [004-004] and [004-005], where "De Lawle Park" is named as the family seat. The park's mention — a classic Trollopian piece of social mapping — establishes the De Lawles as landed gentry within the novel's carefully calibrated class hierarchy.

## Relationships

- **[[lady-de-lawle|Lady De Lawle]]** — Mother. The widowed (or at any rate titled in her own right) [[lady-de-lawle|Lady De Lawle]] is Jack's guardian, and her response to the river incident — alarm followed by acceptance — mirrors the broader pattern of aristocratic mothers' relations with [[bowick|Bowick]].

- **[[mrs-wortle|Mrs. Wortle]]** — Caretaker. [[mrs-wortle|Mrs. Wortle]] takes Jack on the outing that leads to his accident and is the adult responsible for his rescue. The incident serves to establish her character as capable and calm under pressure.

- **[[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]** — Headmaster. Though Jack has no direct interaction with the Doctor in the text, he is a pupil at the Doctor's school, and the question of whether [[lady-de-lawle|Lady De Lawle]] will blame the Wortles for the accident hangs over the early chapters.
