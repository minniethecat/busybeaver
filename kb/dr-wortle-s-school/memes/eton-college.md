## Overview

Eton College, the most famous of England's public schools, functions in *Dr. Wortle's School* as both an aspirational benchmark and a marker of social status. Bowick School is explicitly described as "preparatory to Eton" [002-002], and Dr. Wortle's own history as a master at Eton establishes his credentials within the elite educational hierarchy.

## Role in the Narrative

Eton appears in the novel primarily as a destination—the school that Bowick's students are being prepared for. The educational trajectory is clearly mapped:

> "The old prescribed form of education for British aristocrats... namely, then Eton, and then Oxford." (003-012)

This progression—preparatory school to Eton to Oxford—is the gold standard of elite Victorian education, and Bowick's place at the start of this chain is central to its commercial viability. Parents send their sons to Bowick not as an end in itself but as a stepping stone to Eton.

[[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]'s own connection to Eton is a key element of his professional identity. He "had left his position at Eton" [002-004] to establish Bowick, and this pedigree gives him credibility with aristocratic parents. The departure from Eton was not entirely voluntary—"the Headmaster had required him to give up his house" [002-004]—but Wortle has transformed this setback into the foundation of his own school.

For students like [[lord-carstairs|Lord Carstairs]], the Bowick-to-Eton progression is the expected path: "he should do well when he goes to Eton" [016-007]. The novel uses this trajectory to measure educational success and to validate Wortle's methods.

## Victorian Context

Eton College, founded in 1440 by Henry VI, was by the late nineteenth century the pre-eminent public school in England, educating a disproportionate share of the nation's political and social elite. Its association with the aristocracy and the Church of England made it both admired and resented. Trollope, who was himself educated at Harrow (Eton's great rival) rather than Eton, was well placed to observe the school's cultural significance from a slight distance. The novel's use of Eton as an aspirational benchmark reflects the real hierarchy of Victorian education, in which a school's prestige was measured partly by its success in sending boys to Eton.
