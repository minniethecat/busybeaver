## Overview

Shame in *Dr. Wortle's School* is a complex emotion that cuts in multiple directions. Characters feel shame on their own behalf, on behalf of others, and—in the novel's most morally significant moments—refuse to feel shame when society expects them to. Trollope uses shame to distinguish between mere social embarrassment and genuine moral failure.

## Shame and Mrs. Peacocke

[[mrs-peacocke|Mrs. Peacocke]]'s relationship to shame is the novel's most psychologically acute portrait. Unlike [[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]], who feels shame on her behalf, she herself refuses it:

> "Nor was she in the least ashamed of her position in that respect." (003-008)

This refusal is not shamelessness in the pejorative sense, but moral clarity: she knows she acted in good faith and will not accept the judgment implicit in others' expectation that she should be ashamed.

## The Weaponization of Shame

[[mrs-stantiloup|the Honourable Mrs. Stantiloup]] and her allies deploy shame as a social weapon, seeking to make the Peacockes and their defenders feel disgrace in order to drive them from the community. This weaponized shame is what [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]] most resists:

> "He felt as though he were almost ashamed to go into his own school." (020-009)

Dr. Wortle's moment of near-shame reveals how effective the social pressure has been, even on a man of strong principles.

## Shame Refused

The novel's moral climax comes not when shame is overcome, but when it is refused entirely. [[mrs-peacocke|Mrs. Peacocke]] declares:

> "I am not ashamed of anything." (028-051)

And Dr. Wortle echoes:

> "I have never been wont to be ashamed of myself;—nor do I think that I shall now begin." (028-054)

This refusal is the novel's strongest assertion of moral independence: shame is only warranted by wrongdoing, and those who have done no wrong owe society no embarrassment.
