## Overview

The Diocese of Broughton is the ecclesiastical jurisdiction within which [[bowick|Bowick]] and Dr. Wortle's parish fall. Presided over by the Bishop of Broughton, it represents the institutional authority of the [[church-of-england|Church of England]] in the novel—an authority that is at once formidable in its social power and, as Trollope portrays it, morally equivocal in its exercise. The Bishop's attempts to manage the [[mr-peacocke|Peacocke]] scandal expose the tension between institutional prudence and Christian principle.

> Look round the diocese, said the Bishop in reply to Mrs. Stantiloup. (004-007)

## Institutional Authority

The Diocese embodies the hierarchical structure of the Victorian Church of England. The Bishop holds the power to license clergy, to approve or block appointments to curacies, and to shape the moral climate of his jurisdiction. His authority over [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]] is real but limited—the Doctor, as Rector of Bowick, holds a freehold that the Bishop cannot easily revoke. This legal independence allows Dr. Wortle to resist the Bishop's pressure to dismiss Peacocke, making the Diocese a stage for the conflict between institutional power and individual conscience.

## The Bishop of Broughton

The Bishop is a complex figure—neither villain nor hero, but a cautious administrator whose instinct is to avoid scandal at almost any cost. He is not personally malicious, but his institutional role inclines him toward prudence rather than justice. His letters to Dr. Wortle, urging the removal of Peacocke "for the good of the school" and "the peace of the diocese," represent the voice of institutional self-preservation.

Dr. Wortle's resistance to the Bishop is one of the novel's primary moral dramas. The Doctor must decide whether to obey his ecclesiastical superior or to follow his own conscience—and he chooses conscience, asserting that a Rector's duty to truth and friendship outweighs his duty to episcopal authority.

## The Cathedral at Broughton

The city of Broughton, with its cathedral close and ecclesiastical society, serves as a secondary social world in the novel, contrasting with Bowick's rural character. It is from Broughton that Mrs. Stantiloup—the Bishop's ally and Dr. Wortle's most persistent critic—wages her campaign of gossip.
