## Overview

The Reverend Mr. Peacocke is a [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]'s trusted assistant master at [[bowick|Bowick School]] and a central figure in Anthony Trollope's *[[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]'s School*. A former [[oxford|Oxford]] Classic and a man of considerable [[education|learning]], Peacocke arrives at [[bowick|Bowick]] with his wife under a cloud of mystery—the validity of their [[marriage]] is contested by the possible survival of [[mrs-peacocke|Mrs. Peacocke]]'s first husband, [[ferdinand-lefroy|Ferdinand Lefroy]]. Peacocke's struggle to clear his wife's name and his own honour, and [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]'s unwavering loyalty to him, form the moral core of the novel.

## Role in the Narrative

Peacocke serves as the catalyst for the novel's central conflict. Having spent time in America as Vice-President of a college in St. Louis (003-002), he returned to England with a wife whose first husband, the abusive [[ferdinand-lefroy|Ferdinand Lefroy]], was widely believed dead. When [[robert-lefroy|Robert Lefroy]]—the brother—appears at [[bowick|Bowick]] and claims Ferdinand may be alive, Peacocke's marriage is thrown into legal and social doubt.

Peacocke's response defines his character: rather than hide or dissemble, he resigns his position and travels to America to establish the truth. His quest takes him across the Atlantic and back, ultimately confirming [[ferdinand-lefroy|Ferdinand Lefroy]]'s death and validating his marriage. Throughout the crisis, he maintains a quiet dignity that earns him [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]'s steadfast support.

> "I think it is a story to tell," said Mr. Peacocke, very slowly. (006-022)

## Character & Traits

Mr. Peacocke is described as a small, wiry man of scholarly bent and considerable personal reserve. An [[oxford|Oxford]] Classic by training, he possesses the intellectual refinement that marks him as a gentleman, though his rolling-stone career—from [[oxford|Oxford]] to St. Louis to [[bowick|Bowick]]—has left him without secure worldly position.

> Mr. Peacocke was a small wiry man, anything but comely in the eyes of the Bowick ladies, but with a countenance which, upon the whole, was not without attraction. (003-006)

Peacocke's defining characteristic is his moral seriousness. He refuses to take the curacy offered by [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]] because it would mean preaching while his moral standing was questioned (007-059). He is neither cowardly nor evasive; when confronted, he tells his story directly and accepts the consequences. His love for his wife is absolute—he never questions the validity of their union and risks his career and reputation to defend it.

## Relationships

- **[[mrs-peacocke|Mrs. Peacocke]]** — His wife, whose previous marriage to [[ferdinand-lefroy|Ferdinand Lefroy]] is the source of the scandal. Peacocke's devotion to her is unwavering throughout the novel.
- **[[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]** — His employer and closest ally. Their friendship deepens through the crisis, with [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]] refusing to abandon Peacocke despite intense pressure from [[bishop-of-broughton|the Bishop]] and [[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]].
- **[[lord-carstairs|Lord Carstairs]]** — A student at [[bowick|Bowick]] who befriends Peacocke and invites him to the [[lord-carstairs|Carstairs]] estate. This friendship provides important social validation.
- **[[robert-lefroy|Robert Lefroy]]** — The antagonist who threatens to expose the irregularity of Peacocke's marriage for his own gain.
