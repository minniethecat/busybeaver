## Overview

[[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]]'s journey to America is the novel's central quest narrative, the action that transforms the Bowick scandal from a matter of rumour and speculation into one of documentary proof. By travelling to St. Louis and San Francisco to trace the fate of [[ferdinand-lefroy|Ferdinand Lefroy]], Peacocke takes the question of his marriage's validity out of the realm of gossip and into the realm of fact.

## Narrative Significance

The journey serves multiple narrative functions. It removes Peacocke from Bowick at the height of the scandal, creating narrative space for [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]] to defend him in his absence. It shifts the novel's geography from provincial England to the American frontier, introducing a transatlantic dimension that broadens the novel's scope:

> "Go to Missouri and find out all the truth." (014-035)

The Doctor's advice—that Peacocke must travel to America himself—reflects the novel's faith in personal investigation over institutional inquiry. Truth is not something to be received; it must be pursued.

## The Route

Peacocke's journey traces a path from [[bowick|Bowick]] to [[saint-louis-college|St. Louis, Missouri]]—where the Lefroys' story began—and onward to [[san-francisco|San Francisco]], where Ferdinand Lefroy supposedly died of cholera. The geographical sweep of the journey mirrors the moral distance Peacocke must travel: from the settled certainties of English village life to the uncertain terrain of the American West.

## The Outcome

The journey succeeds in its purpose. Peacocke obtains proof of Ferdinand Lefroy's death, validating his marriage to [[mrs-peacocke|Mrs. Peacocke]] and providing the documentary evidence needed to satisfy both ecclesiastical authority and public opinion. The journey is thus the novel's instrument of resolution: personal effort, undertaken at considerable cost, restores the order that institutional procedure could not.
