## Overview

The concept of the gentleman is the invisible architecture of Anthony Trollope's *Dr. Wortle's School*. It determines who is believed and who is doubted, who may teach boys and who may not, and ultimately who is worthy of trust. The novel probes the question of what makes a gentleman—birth, education, conduct, or moral character—and finds that the answer is less settled than Victorian society pretends. [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]'s defence of [[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]] is, at its deepest level, an assertion that a man's claim to gentility rests on his honour, not on his immunity from scandal.

## Birth vs. Conduct

Victorian society offered two competing definitions of the gentleman: one based on birth and station, the other on conduct and character. The novel stages this tension through its cast. [[lord-carstairs|Lord Carstairs]], the future Earl Bracy, is a gentleman by birth, yet his moral instincts must be educated. Mr. Peacocke, a scholar and clergyman without family connections, claims gentility through learning and integrity. Dr. Wortle, secure in both birth and profession, acts as the arbiter who insists that conduct—not pedigree—is the true test.

> Dr. Wortle was an openhanded, well-to-do gentleman in the village. (002-004)

## The Gentleman as Moral Category

Trollope uses "gentleman" as a moral category as much as a social one. A gentleman keeps his word, protects the vulnerable, and acts from principle rather than expediency. When the [[diocese-of-broughton|Bishop of Broughton]] urges Dr. Wortle to dismiss Peacocke for the sake of institutional peace, the Doctor's refusal is an assertion that gentlemanly honour requires standing by a friend, not bowing to pressure. The Bishop, for all his ecclesiastical rank, emerges as less of a gentleman than the embattled schoolmaster.

## Education and Gentility

The novel belongs to a tradition in which education—particularly [[oxford|Oxford]] education—confers a species of gentility. Dr. Wortle's school at [[bowick|Bowick]] is not merely a commercial enterprise but a project of character formation: it aims to make boys into gentlemen. This is why the Peacocke scandal threatens more than the school's finances—it threatens its moral credential as an institution fit to shape young gentlemen, including the sons of aristocrats like Earl Bracy.
