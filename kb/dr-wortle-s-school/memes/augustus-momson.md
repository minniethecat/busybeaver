# Augustus Momson

Augustus Momson, affectionately known as "dear little Gus," is a young student at [[bowick|Bowick]] School and the nephew of [[mrs-momson|Mrs. Momson]]. Though he never appears directly as a speaking character, he is frequently invoked by adults — particularly by his aunt and by [[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]] — as a justification for their interventions in the affairs of the school.

## "Dear Little Gus"

The boy is introduced in [[015-073]] through the anxious concern of his aunt: "anxious, you know, about dear little Gus. Dear little Gus was Augustus Momson, the lady's nephew, who was still at the school." His position as [[mrs-momson|Mrs. Momson]]'s nephew makes him an emotional anchor for her involvement in the scandal's fallout.

His presence at the school gives [[mrs-momson|Mrs. Momson]] a direct stake in its welfare. As she explains her worries, she frames them entirely around Gus's wellbeing. In [[015-074]], she states that "Augustus will not be hurt, I should say" — a hopeful but uncertain assessment that reveals her underlying anxiety.

## A Pawn in Adult Conflicts

[[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]], ever the opportunist, seizes on Augustus as evidence of the dangers posed by the school under [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]. In [[016-004]], she declares it a "matter of great regret that Augustus should have been sent there." This transforms the boy from a pupil into a rhetorical weapon: if even Augustus — nephew of a prominent local family — is at risk, then every boy at [[bowick|Bowick]] is at risk.

In [[016-007]], [[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]] presses his family further: "we are so anxious that Gus should do well when he goes to..." The ellipsis suggests ambitions beyond [[bowick|Bowick]] — perhaps Eton or another public school — that the scandal now threatens to derail.

## The Decision to Remove Him

The pressure on Augustus's family reaches a climax. In [[016-012]], [[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]] urges: "I do hope you'll be firm about Gus." She wants the boy withdrawn from [[bowick|Bowick]] as a symbolic act — a vote of no confidence in [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]].

By [[016-034]], the decision is made: [[mrs-momson|Mrs. Momson]] "notified his intention of taking little Gus away from the school." The removal of Augustus, however minor he may seem as a character, represents a concrete loss for [[bowick|Bowick]] — one fewer pupil, and a public statement that the scandal has real consequences.

[[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]'s reaction is characteristically sharp. In [[015-085]], we learn that "he would send young Momson away instantly if a word was said" — a display of Wortle's proud refusal to be dictated to by anxious parents or scheming gossips.

## Significance

Augustus Momson serves as a narrative device: the innocent child whose welfare is invoked to justify adult actions. Whether the anxiety about him is genuine or manufactured, his presence in the story illustrates how the Peacocke scandal affected every level of the school community, from the headmaster down to the youngest pupil.
