# Missouri

## Overview

Missouri is the American state where Mr. Peacocke's earlier career unfolded and where he first encountered the woman who would become his wife. It houses the college — referred to as "the College at Missouri" — where Peacocke served as Vice-President, and is the location where Ferdinand Lefroy served as President. Missouri represents the American chapter of Peacocke's life that becomes the source of the novel's central scandal.

## Description

Missouri is presented not through physical description but through its institutional association: "the College at Missouri" [008-007] is the institution where the key figures in the Peacocke scandal first intersected. Peacocke "was Vice-President of the College at Missouri when he first saw her" [004-011] — the "her" being Ella Beaufort, the future Mrs. Peacocke, then married to Ferdinand Lefroy.

> "He had established himself as a professor in Missouri, is uncommon, and it could hardly have been expected" [008-007] — this passage reflects on the improbability of an English gentleman-scholar like Peacocke finding himself in the American Midwest.

The College is described as a respectable but lesser institution than an English college: "It isn't as good, I reckon, as the Missouri College" [010-018] is Robert Lefroy's sardonic comparison of Bowick School to the Missouri institution, implying that even a remote American college might have its advantages.

## Role in the Narrative

Missouri is the crucible of the novel's backstory — the place where the tangled relationships between Peacocke, Ella, and the Lefroy brothers were formed. As Vice-President of the College, Peacocke had professional standing and respectability; as President, Ferdinand Lefroy held the superior position that made his claim on Ella socially legitimate.

When the scandal of the Peacockes' marriage threatens to unravel, Missouri becomes both the source of the problem and its potential solution. Mr. Peacocke proposes to "go again to Missouri and find out all the truth" [014-035], making the return journey to America the novel's central quest for documentary evidence of his wife's first husband's death.

> "For five hundred dollars he would go over to Missouri, and, if necessary to Texas, and bring back evidence" [014-045] — Robert Lefroy's offer to return to Missouri introduces the possibility of resolving the scandal through a transatlantic investigation, though his motives remain suspect.

The state thus functions as a distant, almost mythical location where crucial evidence might be found — the truth about Ferdinand Lefroy's death that would legitimize the Peacockes' marriage and restore their standing in English society.

## Context & Background

In Trollope's era, the American Midwest was to English readers a remote and slightly exotic region — not as culturally established as the Eastern seaboard but associated with the rapid expansion of American settlement and institutions after the Civil War. Missouri itself was a border state, admitted to the Union in 1821 as part of the Missouri Compromise, and by the 1870s its colleges would have been relatively young institutions. The presence of Englishmen like Peacocke and the Lefroys at a Missouri college reflects the Victorian-era movement of educated Britons to American institutions, though Trollope presents it as somewhat improbable — a sign of careers that have deviated from their expected course. For Trollope, who had travelled extensively in America and written about it, Missouri represented the American interior: a place of opportunity but also of distance from the centres of English civilization.
