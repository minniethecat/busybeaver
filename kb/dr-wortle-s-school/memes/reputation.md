## Overview

Reputation in Trollope's *Dr. Wortle's School* is the public face of private character—and the two are not always aligned. The novel explores how reputation can be unjustly damaged, how it can be jealously guarded at the expense of morality, and how some characters ultimately choose conscience over the preservation of their good name.

## The Fragility of Reputation

[[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]] is acutely aware that his school depends on reputation. As a commercial enterprise as well as an educational institution, Bowick School cannot survive a loss of public confidence:

> "Respectability was everything. Credit, he said to himself, was the one thing needed." (007-056)

This awareness makes his defence of the Peacockes all the more remarkable—he risks the very reputation on which his livelihood depends.

## Reputation as Social Currency

The novel maps reputation across social classes. For [[mrs-stantiloup|the Honourable Mrs. Stantiloup]], reputation is a weapon to be deployed against enemies. For [[the-bishop-of-broughton|the Bishop]], it is the diocese's public image that must be protected at all costs. For the Peacockes, reputation is something already lost, and the question is whether it can be restored.

## Reputation vs. Character

Trollope's key distinction is between reputation (what others think of you) and character (what you actually are). The novel's moral trajectory moves characters from anxiety about reputation toward confidence in character. [[mrs-peacocke|Mrs. Peacocke]] makes this transition most completely:

> "I am not ashamed of anything." (028-051)

A character who no longer fears for her reputation has achieved a kind of moral freedom that the reputation-obsessed can never know.

## Reputation Restored

The novel's resolution restores reputation through the establishment of truth. Once Ferdinand Lefroy's death is confirmed, the Peacockes' good name is rebuilt—but the restoration is contingent on institutional validation (legal proof, ecclesiastical acceptance) as well as personal vindication. The novel ends by suggesting that reputation, properly understood, should follow character rather than precede it.
