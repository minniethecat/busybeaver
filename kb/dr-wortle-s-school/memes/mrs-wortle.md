## Overview

Mrs. Wortle is the wife of [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Jeffrey Wortle]] and the mother of [[mary-wortle|Mary Wortle]]. A woman of firm moral principle and genuine kindness, she serves as a steadying influence on her headstrong husband and as a moral touchstone within the [[bowick|Bowick]] community. When the [[marriage]] scandal surrounding [[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]] threatens to engulf the school, Mrs. Wortle's reactions—at times anxious, at times fiercely loyal—reveal the social and domestic pressures the crisis imposes on the Wortle family.

## Role in the Narrative

Mrs. Wortle occupies a position between private conscience and public expectation. She is deeply concerned with propriety and the welfare of her daughter [[mary-wortle|Mary]], yet she also possesses an independent moral judgment that sometimes outstrips her husband's in its clarity. When she learns the details of [[mrs-peacocke|Mrs. Peacocke]]'s situation, her reaction is immediate and uncompromising:

> "A woman cannot be anyone's wife unless she be his wife." Mrs. Wortle said this with more of obstinacy than she was wont to show. (012-054)

Throughout the novel, Mrs. Wortle navigates the competing claims of loyalty to the Peacockes, concern for her daughter's social standing, and the incessant gossip of neighbours like [[lady-de-lawle|Lady De Lawle]] and the hostile [[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]]. Her quiet management of the household allows [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]] the freedom to wage his public battle with the [[diocese-of-broughton|Bishop of Broughton]].

## Character & Traits

Mrs. Wortle is portrayed as a woman of natural good sense and deep loyalty. Trollope describes her as having a "soft heart" yet capable of surprising firmness when her moral convictions are engaged. She is the social centre of the rectory, managing its hospitality and maintaining its standing in the community.

> There was no softer-hearted woman than Mrs. Wortle anywhere in the diocese, or one who more thoroughly believed in her husband. (012-065)

She balances anxiety about her daughter [[mary-wortle|Mary]]'s prospects with genuine compassion for the Peacockes. Her moral reasoning is often simpler and more direct than [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]'s—she reaches conclusions through feeling and principle rather than argument—but she is rarely wrong.
