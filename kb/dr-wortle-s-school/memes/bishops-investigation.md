## Overview

The Bishop of Broughton's investigation into the Peacocke affair is the novel's central institutional confrontation. Prompted by complaints from [[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]] and others, [[the-bishop-of-broughton|the Bishop]] undertakes an inquiry into [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]'s conduct in retaining [[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]] as an usher despite the questions surrounding his marriage.

## The Bishop's Approach

The Bishop approaches the investigation as a matter of ecclesiastical discipline. His concern is not primarily with the truth of the Peacockes' situation but with the propriety of Dr. Wortle's response to it. The investigation thus becomes a test of authority: can the Bishop compel a clergyman-schoolmaster to dismiss an assistant whose private life has become a public scandal?

> "In the course of the last week I have heard from various quarters a story which has greatly astonished me." (020-006)

The Bishop frames his inquiry as a duty to the diocese, but the novel exposes the gap between institutional procedure and moral judgment. The Bishop is not malicious, but his investigation is structured to protect the Church's reputation rather than to establish justice.

## The Confrontation

The climactic confrontation between the Bishop and Dr. Wortle is the novel's most charged institutional encounter. Wortle refuses to be intimidated by ecclesiastical authority:

> "If I have given just cause of scandal to the diocese I will retire from my position." (019-011)

This defiance transforms the investigation from a disciplinary proceeding into a moral drama. Wortle stakes his career on the principle that a clergyman's first duty is to conscience, not to institutional convenience.

## Resolution

The investigation concludes without the Bishop achieving his objective. Dr. Wortle retains Mr. Peacocke, and the subsequent confirmation of Ferdinand Lefroy's death vindicates Wortle's judgment. The Bishop's investigation, meant to enforce institutional order, ultimately demonstrates the limits of that order when it conflicts with individual moral conviction.
