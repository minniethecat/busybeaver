# Mrs. Rolland

Mrs. Rolland is the wife of the [[bishop-of-broughton|Bishop of Broughton]] and a participant in the social conversations that determine how the Peacocke scandal is perceived within the Church community. As [[bishop-of-broughton|the Bishop]]'s wife, she occupies a position of informal influence within the diocese and is courted by interested parties seeking to sway [[bishop-of-broughton|the Bishop]]'s judgment.

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Rolland first appears in [[016-002]] among a gathering of clerical wives: "[[mrs-momson|Mrs. Momson]], [[jeffrey-wortle|the Rector]]'s wife; Mrs. Rolland, the wife of [[bishop-of-broughton|the Bishop]]." This places her at the centre of the novel's ecclesiastical social network, where much of the novel's action unfolds through conversation and correspondence.

In [[016-005]], Mrs. Rolland expresses a moderate view about the school, remarking that the "school was quite popular." This is a careful, non-committal statement — one befitting [[bishop-of-broughton|the Bishop]]'s wife, who must weigh her words when speaking about a clergyman's establishment.

## Social Influence

Mrs. Rolland's position gives her significant informal power. As [[bishop-of-broughton|the Bishop]]'s wife, she is privy to clerical gossip and can shape the atmosphere in which decisions are made. In [[016-009]], when pressed further, she simply says, "No, indeed," deflecting a more pointed question about the scandal. This circumspection reveals her awareness that her words carry weight.

She is also a target of [[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]]'s campaign against the Wortle establishment. In [[019-009]], [[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]] visits Mrs. Rolland specifically — "having made quite a journey" — seeking to influence [[bishop-of-broughton|the Bishop]] through his wife. This attempt to work through Mrs. Rolland acknowledges her as a channel of influence.

## Personal Observations

Mrs. Rolland is not always diplomatic. In a letter quoted in [[016-032]], she is described unflatteringly as "such a dowdy in all my life as Mrs. Rolland. He is all very well, and looks..." — suggesting that while [[bishop-of-broughton|the Bishop]] commands respect, his wife does not impress in appearance. This humanising detail places Mrs. Rolland among the novel's many imperfect but real-seeming minor characters.

## The Bishop's Household

The Doctor sees Mrs. Rolland in the context of [[bishop-of-broughton|the Bishop]]'s family. In [[019-014]], [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]] observes her "talking with Mrs. Rolland and [[bishop-of-broughton|the Bishop]]'s daughters," a domestic scene that humanises the episcopal household and shows Mrs. Rolland in her natural setting as wife and mother within the Church's highest local family.
