## Overview

The Church of England is the institutional framework within which the clerical characters of *Dr. Wortle's School* operate. As the established church, it provided the legal, social, and doctrinal context for Victorian rural life. Three of the novel's central characters—[[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]], [[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]], and [[mr-puddicombe|Mr. Puddicombe]]—are clergymen of the Church of England, and their respective relationships to ecclesiastical authority define the moral geography of the novel.

> It behoves a clergyman of the Church of England to be master in his own parish. (015-004)

## The Clerical World

The novel presents a cross-section of the Victorian Church's clerical types. Dr. Wortle is the vigorous, independent-minded Rector who treats his parish as his domain and resists episcopal interference. Mr. Peacocke is the scholar-clergyman, an Oxford Classic whose intellectual credentials are impeccable but whose irregular domestic situation makes him vulnerable to ecclesiastical discipline. Mr. Puddicombe is the cautious, reputation-conscious parson who embodies institutional prudence.

## Church Authority and Individual Conscience

The central ecclesiastical conflict of the novel concerns the limits of church authority over individual clergymen. The [[diocese-of-broughton|Bishop of Broughton]] claims the right to judge the fitness of clergy within his jurisdiction; Dr. Wortle claims the right of a Rector to manage his own parish and protect his own assistant. This is not merely a jurisdictional dispute—it is a question about whether the Church's institutional machinery serves or hinders Christian justice.

Trollope, writing as a sharp observer of the Victorian Church (though himself a novelist rather than a clergyman), portrays the Church of England as an institution capable of both great good and bureaucratic cowardice. The Bishop represents the latter; Dr. Wortle, standing by Peacocke at great personal cost, represents the former.

## Clerical Marriage and Discipline

The Peacocke case engages a specific area of church law: the validity of a clergyman's [[marriage]] and its implications for his fitness to hold office. Because Mr. Peacocke is an ordained clergyman of the Church of England and serves as a curate-like assistant in Dr. Wortle's parish, any irregularity in his marriage is not merely a private matter but an ecclesiastical one, subject to the discipline of the Diocese and ultimately of the Church itself.
