## Overview

Truth in Trollope's *Dr. Wortle's School* is both a moral imperative and a practical problem. Characters repeatedly confront the question of what truth demands—full disclosure, partial revelation, or protective concealment. The novel explores truth not as a simple binary but as something that must be managed with wisdom and charity.

## The Burden of Concealment

[[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]] and [[mrs-peacocke|Mrs. Peacocke]] live for years with a hidden truth—that their marriage may be invalid. The strain of concealment is itself a form of suffering. When Peacocke finally confides in [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]], the relief is palpable, but the consequences are immediate:

> "In very truth the schoolmaster found it hard to answer the question." (010-027)

## Truth-Telling as Obligation

[[mrs-peacocke|Mrs. Wortle]] articulates the simplest moral position: truth must be told, regardless of cost:

> "As he is so much to you, as you love him so well, I am bound to tell him the truth." (007-045)

But the novel complicates this simplicity. Truth told without discretion can cause unnecessary harm. The question becomes not whether to tell the truth, but how and when.

## The Investigative Journey

The novel's second half becomes a quest for factual truth. Mr. Peacocke travels to America to establish whether Ferdinand Lefroy is truly dead:

> "Go to Missouri and find out all the truth." (014-035)

This journey transforms truth from a matter of private knowledge into a matter of public proof. Only documentary evidence can satisfy the demands of law and society.

## Truth and Reputation

The relationship between truth and reputation is particularly complex. [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]] refuses to conceal the truth to protect his school's reputation, but he also refuses to let the truth be weaponized by scandal-mongers:

> "But now it seemed as though the truth must be made known at Bowick." (010-045)

The novel suggests that truth, fully and courageously told, is ultimately the strongest defense against scandal—but only when the audience is willing to hear it.
