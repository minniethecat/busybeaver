## Overview

Lady Anne Clifford is a widowed aristocrat and the mother of two boys enrolled at [[bowick|Bowick]] School. She appears primarily in the latter half of the novel as a loyal supporter of [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]] during the Peacocke scandal. Unlike [[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]] and [[lady-de-lawle|Lady De Lawle]], who pressure [[bishop-of-broughton|the Bishop]] to act against [[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]], Lady Anne Clifford remains steadfast in her trust of [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]. Her letters to the Doctor and her quiet fidelity make her one of Trollope's sympathetic portraits of maternal devotion and social loyalty.

## Role in the Narrative

Lady Anne Clifford is introduced in Chapter 16 as part of the social world surrounding [[bowick|Bowick]]. She is identified as the widowed mother of [[clifford|the Clifford boys]]:

> "The Lady Anne spoken of was Lady Anne Clifford, the widowed mother of two boys at the school." [016-011]

She becomes more significant during the crisis, when she writes to [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]] expressing her continued confidence in him:

> "Here is one from Lady Anne Clifford. 'Dear Doctor—You know how strong my trust in you has always been...'" [016-035]

Trollope characterizes her with warmth:

> "Now Lady Anne Clifford was a sweet, confiding, affectionate woman, who believed in the Doctor with all her heart." [016-036]

When the scandal intensifies, she writes a "piteous letter" [019-002] to [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]], anxious about her boys' future at the school. She is contrasted with others who abandoned the Doctor — she and a few loyal families remain, forming the core of support that eventually sees [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]] through the crisis.

In the novel's conclusion, Lady Anne Clifford is among the neighbourhood figures present at the resolution, "devoted, with almost more than feminine affection, to the Doctor and his wife" [030-003].

## Character & Traits

Lady Anne Clifford embodies the virtues of loyalty and maternal devotion. She is not a complex or intellectually formidable character — Trollope presents her as a woman of feeling rather than intellect, whose judgments spring from the heart:

> "Lady Anne Clifford was not a beautiful woman, as was Mrs. Peacocke; nor was she clever." [019-004]

Yet her simplicity is her strength. While others waver under social pressure, she remains constant. Her trust in [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]] is absolute, not because she has analyzed the moral dimensions of the case, but because she trusts the man himself. In this, Trollope suggests that simple fidelity can be morally superior to sophisticated but socially-motivated judgment.

## Relationships

- **[[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]** — Her complete trust in the Doctor is a source of strength for him during the scandal. She writes him letters of support and never wavers.
- **[[clifford|The Clifford Boys]]** — Her two sons are students at [[bowick|Bowick]]. Their education is her primary concern, and her loyalty to the school is driven in part by her maternal investment.
- **[[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]]** — They occupy opposite poles of the social response to the scandal: Lady Anne is steadfastly loyal while [[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]] agitates against the school.
- **[[bishop-of-broughton|The Bishop of Broughton]]** — Her gentle persistence helps counterbalance the harder pressures [[bishop-of-broughton|the Bishop]] receives from other quarters.
