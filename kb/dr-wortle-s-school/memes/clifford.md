# Clifford

The **Clifford** brothers are two minor student characters at [[bowick|Bowick School]] in Anthony Trollope's *[[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]'s School*. Neither brother is individually named in the narrative; they are referred to collectively as "the two Clifford boys" or "the Cliffords."

## Overview

The Clifford boys are among the pupils enrolled at [[bowick|Bowick]] School during the events of the [[peacocke-scandal]]. Their significance lies not in their individual personalities or actions, but in their role as representatives of the parental alarm that swept through the school's constituency once rumours about [[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]] and his wife began to circulate. Their removal from the school marks a concrete consequence of the scandal for [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]].

## Role in the Narrative

The Cliffords serve a structural function in the novel: they embody the collective response of respectable families to the moral controversy surrounding [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]'s decision to retain [[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]] on the staff. When gossip spread by [[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]] reaches their parents, the boys are swiftly withdrawn — a tangible blow to the school's reputation and enrolment. Their departure illustrates the social pressure brought to bear on [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]] and underscores the precariousness of his position.

## Key Appearances

- **The younger Clifford boy** is mentioned in conversation with "little Jack [[talbot|Talbot]]" [008-052], indicating the Cliffords are integrated into the ordinary social life of the school before the crisis.
- [[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]] invokes the Cliffords by name when spreading her campaign against the school [016-026], singling them out as families to be warned.
- The definitive moment comes when "the two Clifford boys were taken away" [019-006], a departure directly attributed to the scandal.
- Shortly afterward, it is noted that [[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]]'s words have successfully reached "the Cliffords" [019-009], cementing the connection between her agitation and their removal.

## Character & Traits

As minor characters, the Clifford brothers are given no individual distinguishing traits. They are presented as ordinary schoolboys — indistinguishable from the general body of [[bowick|Bowick]] pupils — whose defining narrative action is not something they do, but something that is done to them: their removal by anxious parents. This very blankness makes them effective figures for Trollope's purpose; they could be any family's sons, and their departure signals a broad, impersonal threat to the school.

## Relationships

- **[[talbot|Talbot]] ([[talbot|Jack Talbot]])**: A schoolfellow; the younger Clifford boy is seen conversing with him, indicating normal friendships among the boys.
- **[[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]**: The headmaster who loses the Clifford boys as pupils — a loss he feels both financially and in terms of public standing.
- **[[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]]**: The instigator whose letters and gossip directly precipitate the Clifford parents' decision to withdraw their sons.
- **[[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]]**: The unwitting cause of the Cliffords' departure; the scandal surrounding him creates the conditions under which their parents act.
