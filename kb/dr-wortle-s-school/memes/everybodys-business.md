## Overview

*Everybody's Business* is a London weekly newspaper that functions as a plot catalyst in *Dr. Wortle's School*. It is through this publication that the scandal surrounding the [[mr-peacocke|Peacockes]]' marriage reaches a wider public, transforming a local diocesan controversy into a matter of national attention. Trollope uses the newspaper to satirize the emerging culture of sensational journalism and its capacity to damage private reputations under the guise of public interest.

## Role in the Narrative

The newspaper first appears when a friend sends [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]] a copy containing an article about the Bowick affair:

> "A friend sent to him a copy of Everybody's Business." (019-007)

The article, which the narrator describes as having "nearly drove the Doctor mad" [019-002], presents a distorted account of the Peacocke case. The newspaper's very title—*Everybody's Business*—is ironic: it suggests that private matters are legitimate subjects for public consumption, precisely the assumption that the novel contests.

The publication's effect is immediate and severe. "Everybody knew, she said, that the Doctor had behaved badly" [019-012]—the newspaper transforms rumour into what passes for established fact. [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]] is furious not only at the content but at the medium: anonymous journalism that can destroy a man's reputation without accountability.

> "So much had been effected by Everybody's Business, and its abominations." (022-026)

The novel's judgment on the newspaper is unambiguous. It represents everything Trollope disliked about the new journalism: sensationalism, anonymity, and the erosion of the boundary between public and private life.

## Victorian Context

The 1870s and 1880s saw the rapid expansion of popular journalism in Britain, with the rise of evening papers and weekly magazines aimed at a mass readership. Publications like *The Pall Mall Gazette* (which Trollope knew well, having contributed to it) pioneered a new style of sensational reporting that blurred the line between news and entertainment. *Everybody's Business* is Trollope's satirical portrait of this phenomenon—a newspaper that treats private tragedy as public spectacle and profits from the damage it causes.
