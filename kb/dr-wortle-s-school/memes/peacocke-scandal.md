## Overview

The Peacocke scandal is the central narrative engine of Anthony Trollope's *Dr. Wortle's School*. It begins with rumours about the validity of [[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]]'s [[marriage]] to his wife, escalates into a public crisis when Robert Lefroy arrives at [[bowick|Bowick]], forces the Peacockes to leave the school, and is ultimately resolved when Peacocke's journey to America confirms the death of [[mrs-peacocke|Mrs. Peacocke]]'s first husband. The scandal tests the loyalties of [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]], divides the [[bowick|Bowick]] community, and exposes the tension between institutional prudence and Christian charity.

## Background

Mr. Peacocke, a distinguished Oxford Classic, had served as Vice-President of a college in St. Louis, Missouri, before returning to England to take up the post of assistant master at Dr. Wortle's school. He arrived with a wife whose past was mysterious. Mrs. Peacocke had previously been married to Ferdinand Lefroy, a man of violent character who had abandoned her and was widely believed dead. She and Peacocke married in good faith. But the shadow of that first marriage hung over them.

> Then there arose a great scandal in the city as to the treatment which the woman had received from the man or upon the woman from the man. (004-011)

The question was simple but devastating: if Ferdinand Lefroy was alive when she married Peacocke, the second marriage was legally void—and Peacocke, a clergyman of the [[church-of-england|Church of England]], would be living in sin.

## The Revelation

The scandal breaks into the open through two channels. First, gossip spreads through the neighbourhood, driven by the hostile Mrs. Stantiloup and reaching the ears of the [[diocese-of-broughton|Bishop of Broughton]]. Second—and more dramatically—Robert Lefroy, the younger brother of Ferdinand, appears at Bowick itself.

Robert Lefroy's arrival at the rectory door is one of the novel's most tense scenes. He has come not from concern for his brother, but to extract money from Peacocke:

> "You know me well enough. I'm Robert Lefroy." (008-060)

The meeting between Peacocke and Robert Lefroy in Chapter X confirms the worst: Ferdinand may indeed have been alive at the time of the Peacockes' marriage. Peacocke understands immediately that his position at Bowick—and his wife's honour—are now unsustainable without proof of Ferdinand's death.

> The truth must be made known at Bowick, and that he and his wife must go. (010-045)

## The Crisis

The scandal unfolds on multiple fronts:

| Front | Key Players | Stakes |
|--------|------------|--------|
| **Ecclesiastical** | Bishop of Broughton, Dr. Wortle | Whether Peacocke may continue as a clergyman and teacher |
| **Social** | Mrs. Stantiloup, Lady De Lawle, the Bowick community | The reputation of the school and the Wortle family |
| **Domestic** | Mrs. Wortle, Mary Wortle | The Wortle household's stability and Mary's marriage prospects |
| **Personal** | Mr. and Mrs. Peacocke | Their marriage, honour, and future together |

Dr. Wortle's response defines his character. Pressured by the Bishop to dismiss Peacocke quietly, he refuses—not because he is certain of the legal facts, but because he believes in Peacocke's good faith and will not abandon a friend to placate institutional opinion.

## The Resolution

Peacocke travels to America to establish the truth. The journey is long and uncertain, and the Wortle household endures months of gossip and pressure in his absence. Ultimately, Peacocke confirms that Ferdinand Lefroy died before the second marriage took place. He returns to Bowick vindicated, and the marriage is validated. The scandal, which threatened to destroy the school and the Wortles' standing, instead reveals the strength of Dr. Wortle's character and the shallowness of his critics.

## Key Participants

- **[[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]]** — The assistant master whose marriage is the subject of the scandal. His quiet dignity under pressure and his determination to clear his wife's name drive the resolution.
- **[[mrs-peacocke|Mrs. Peacocke]]** — The wife whose first marriage to Ferdinand Lefroy is the source of the legal question. She endures social ostracism with courage.
- **[[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]]** — The Rector of Bowick whose refusal to abandon the Peacockes, despite intense pressure from the Bishop and the community, is the moral centre of the novel.
- **Robert Lefroy** — The antagonist whose arrival at Bowick forces the scandal into the open and whose claims must be disproved.
- **Ferdinand Lefroy** — The first husband whose survival or death determines the validity of the Peacockes' marriage.
- **The Bishop of Broughton** — The ecclesiastical authority who represents institutional prudence over individual justice.

## Consequences

The scandal's resolution affirms the novel's central moral argument: that personal honour and Christian charity outweigh institutional convenience. Dr. Wortle's school survives; the Peacockes' marriage is validated; and the Bishop's authority, while never formally challenged, is exposed as morally hollow. The scandal also clears the way for [[mary-wortle|Mary Wortle]]'s romance with [[lord-carstairs|Lord Carstairs]], as the Wortle family's demonstrated integrity outweighs the temporary social damage of the affair.
