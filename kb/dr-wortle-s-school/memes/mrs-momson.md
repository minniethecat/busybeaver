## Overview

Mrs. Momson is the wife of the local squire and the mother of [[augustus-momson|Augustus Momson]], a pupil at [[bowick|Bowick]] School. She appears primarily in Chapter 16, at a social gathering in the drawing-room of [[bishop-of-broughton|the Bishop]]'s palace, where the scandal surrounding [[mr-peacocke|Mr. Peacocke]] is the principal topic of conversation. Though described as "stupid" by [[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]], she is also acknowledged as "staunch" — a loyal figure whose simple fidelity to [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]] contrasts with the more calculated opposition of other society women.

## Role in the Narrative

Mrs. Momson is introduced among the company assembled at [[bishop-of-broughton|the Bishop]]'s palace:

> "In the drawing-room, were assembled Mrs. Momson, the squire's wife; Lady Margaret; and the Hon. Mrs. Stantiloup." [016-002]

She is drawn into the discussion of the scandal when [[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]] addresses her directly:

> "'Dear little Gus,' said Mrs. Stantiloup to Mrs. Momson. 'If we're not to put down this kind of thing, what will become of our darlings?'" [016-012]

This exchange — [[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]] using the language of maternal concern to manipulate another mother — reveals the social dynamics at work. Mrs. Momson also turns to question [[bishop-of-broughton|the Bishop]]'s wife about the matter:

> "'I wonder what the Bishop does think about it?' asked Mrs. Momson of the Bishop's wife." [016-013]

Later, she is characterized by [[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]] in a moment of revealing dismissiveness:

> "A woman so 'staunch' though so 'stupid' as Mrs. Momson, should be turned from her purpose." [016-035]

This characterization — attributed to [[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]]'s perspective — tells the reader as much about the speaker as the subject. Mrs. Momson's simplicity is, in Trollope's moral economy, preferable to [[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]]'s cunning.

## Character & Traits

Mrs. Momson is a minor figure, but her presence in the drawing-room scene serves an important narrative function. She represents the ordinary county matron — not intellectually gifted, not socially ambitious, but fundamentally decent. Her inclusion in the scene provides Trollope an opportunity to show how the scandal's ripples reached every level of local society, and how even those without a personal stake in the controversy were drawn into its orbit. [[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]]'s private contempt for her ("so stupid") exposes the snobbery at the heart of the anti-Wortle coalition.

## Relationships

- **[[mr-momson|Mr. Momson]]** — Her husband, the squire, whose social position places the family among the local gentry with a stake in [[bowick|Bowick]] School.
- **[[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]]** — [[mrs-stantiloup|Mrs. Stantiloup]] attempts to enlist her in the campaign against [[jeffrey-wortle|Dr. Wortle]], while privately regarding her with condescension.
- **[[bishop-of-broughton|The Bishop of Broughton]]** — Host of the gathering where Mrs. Momson appears; she is curious about his views on the scandal.
- **[[lady-margaret|Lady Margaret]]** — Fellow guest at [[bishop-of-broughton|the Bishop]]'s drawing-room, part of the same social circle.
- **[[augustus-momson|Augustus Momson]]** — Her son, known as "dear [[augustus-momson|little Gus]]," a pupil at [[bowick|Bowick]] School.
