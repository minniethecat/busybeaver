The panchayat is the traditional Santal village council, convened to resolve disputes and deliver justice. In the folklore, the panchayat proceeding is a formalized public event with established roles and procedures.

## The Proceeding

When a man demanded a panchayat to settle his dispute with the Raja, the Raja — though angry — went outside the city, met the panchayat, and ordered them to get to business quickly (099-004). The panchayat proceeding is public, held outside the city walls, with the disputing parties, the council members, and the Raja all present. The formality of the proceeding — the demand, the summons, the gathering — reflects actual Santal dispute resolution practice.

## The Unexpected Judge

The panchayat in the Grasping Raja story takes an unexpected turn when the man points to a jackal as his judge (099-004). The Raja is outraged — What, is this jackal your judge? — but the proceeding continues with the jackal presiding. The panchayat thus becomes the setting for the story's central inversion: the animal who proves wiser than the king. The formality of the proceeding contrasts with the absurdity of the judge, creating the story's comic and critical effect.

## Social Function

The panchayat in Santal folklore represents the ideal of communal justice — a public forum where even the Raja can be held accountable. The fact that the story ends with the Raja writing out a promise to restore the stolen cow and pay twenty-five rupees to the panchayat (099-007) affirms the institution's effectiveness: justice is achieved, and the powerful are made to comply.
