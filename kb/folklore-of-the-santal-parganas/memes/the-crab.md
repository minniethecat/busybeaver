The crab appears in a distinctive Santal tale as a servant figure whose song exposes wrongdoing and whose death becomes a matter of moral consequence. The Speaking Crab (chapter 106) is one of the more unusual animal tales in the collection.

## Description

The crab is a small aquatic creature with claws, inhabiting rice fields and water bodies. In the story, the crab is personified as a servant who works in the rice fields and possesses the ability to sing — its song serving as testimony.

## Behaviour

The crab's defining trait is its singing. While working in the fields, the crab sings about the farmer's household: describing the farmer as a servant and his wife as a maidservant (106-003). The song carries social commentary encoded in animal form. When the farmer kills the crab, the act has consequences — the farmer's wife, who had been away, can no longer be found, and the farmer's fortune declines. The crab's death is not merely the killing of a pest but a morally significant act.

## In Stories

The Speaking Crab (chapter 106) combines the animal tale with a moral fable about the consequences of silencing those who speak truth. The crab's song is annoying but true, and its killing brings misfortune.
