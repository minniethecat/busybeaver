Witch initiation is the ritual process by which new witches are created in Santal belief. Unlike voluntary religious initiations, witch initiation is coercive — girls are taken by force and compelled to participate in acts that bind them permanently to the witch community.

## The Ritual Process

When girls are initiated into witchcraft they are taken away by force and made to lead tigers about (176-001). The initiation culminates in a final test: the initiate must eat men — that is, consume human flesh. If any girl refuses to take this final step, she is caused to go mad or die (176-002). The coercion is absolute — there is no opting out once the process has begun.

## Gender and Power

The initiation is specifically gendered: it targets girls, transforming them into witches against their will. This coercive recruitment explains how women — who, in the origin story, received witchcraft knowledge by deception rather than divine intent — come to dominate the practice. Men may also learn witchcraft, but the initiation narrative is centered on the forcible transformation of girls into witches.

## Social Implications

The witch initiation narrative serves multiple functions in Santal society. It explains why witches exist, why they are predominantly female, and why individual women cannot be held fully responsible for their witchcraft — they were forced into it. At the same time, the initiation's coercive nature reinforces the fear and stigma surrounding witches: their power comes from a terrifying, involuntary transformation.
