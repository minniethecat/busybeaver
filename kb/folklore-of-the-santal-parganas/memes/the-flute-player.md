The flute-player is a recurring character type in Santal folklore — typically a young, unmarried man whose solitary musical practice at night attracts supernatural attention. His flute-playing serves as a bridge between the human and bonga worlds.

## Role in Stories

The flute-player appears in two major narratives. In the Prince Who Found Two Wives, the Prince used often to sit up half the night singing and playing to himself, and one night heard a laugh and saw a beautiful bonga girl (101-002). In the Marriage with Bongas narrative, one of the men who married a bonga was very fond of playing on the flute, and his playing attracted a bonga girl while he was tending buffaloes (148-002).

## The Music as Bridge

The flute-player's solitude is essential to the motif. He plays alone, at night or while herding, at the boundary between village and jungle. His music signals sensitivity and refinement — qualities that attract bonga attention across the boundary between worlds. The flute-player does not summon the bonga through ritual or intention; his music draws her involuntarily, as an aesthetic rather than magical attraction.
