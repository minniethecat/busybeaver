The leopard is one of the most dangerous predators in Santal folklore, distinct from [[Tigers]] in the specificity of its narrative role. Where tigers appear across many story types, the leopard belongs above all to the man-eater narrative — the solitary killer whose depredations grow so serious that a whole community mobilizes for [[The Great Hunt]].

Yet the leopard is not merely a menace. When cornered it bargains, and in the company of [[The Jackal]] it hunts, sacrifices, and negotiates like any other actor in the animal world.

## Leopard and Tiger

Bompas's tales treat the two great cats as related but distinct narrative figures. The contrast clarifies what the leopard is for:

| Trait | The Leopard | [[Tigers]] |
|-------|-------------|------------|
| Typical role | Man-eater, antagonist | Varied — menace, dupe, even friend |
| Signature story | The great hunt (107) | The tiger cub and the calf |
| Relation to humans | Preys on and betrays them | Sometimes allied, sometimes fooled |
| Cunning when trapped | Bargains, then reneges | Often outwitted |

## Description

The leopard is a large, spotted cat of the jungle. In the stories it is characterized chiefly by its appetite for human flesh and its cunning when threatened. Unlike the tiger, which sometimes appears in friendship narratives, the leopard is consistently presented as an antagonist.

## Behaviour

The leopard's defining behaviour is man-eating. Its depredations became so serious that the whole neighbouring population decided to have a great hunt and kill it (107-001). When the leopard hears the shouting and drumming of the approaching beaters, it flees, but its flight only leads to new encounters.

Hiding in a sack on a merchant's pack-bullock, the leopard bargains for its release, then immediately threatens to devour the merchant who freed it (107-003). This pattern — accepting help, then betraying the helper — aligns the leopard with the jackal in its treachery, though through force rather than cunning.

## In Stories

In another narrative a leopardess collaborates with a jackal and her cubs to hunt, killing a fine deer. The jackal then insists on offering the game as a [[Sacrifice]] to the spirit of their dead leopard companion before anyone may eat (123-002).

This episode reveals the leopard as integrated into a broader animal social world — hunting, sacrificing, negotiating — rather than existing only as a solitary menace on the edge of the village.
