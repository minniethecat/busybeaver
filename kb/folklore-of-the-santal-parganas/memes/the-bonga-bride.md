The bonga bride is a distinctive figure in Santal folklore — a female spirit who forms a marriage-like union with a human man. Unlike the witch, whose power is feared, the bonga bride is desired: she is consistently described as beautiful, and human men are drawn to her across the boundary between worlds.

## Role in Stories

The bonga bride appears in two major narrative contexts. In the Prince Who Would Not Marry, a beautiful bonga maiden was brought to the prison while the Prince slept, and when he awoke he fell in love with her at first sight, exchanging rings and promising marriage (100-001). In the Marriage with Bongas narrative, bonga girls come to human men who attract them — through flute-playing or simply through proximity — and form lasting domestic partnerships (148-002).

## Between Worlds

The bonga bride moves between the spirit and human worlds. She presents her human husband to her bonga parents, maintains a bonga household with snakes as furniture and tigers as guardians (148-002), and provides gifts that later prove illusory — dried rice and cakes that transform into leaves and cow-dung fuel (148-003). Even after her human husband marries a human wife, the bonga bride may continue to visit, or may vanish, depending on the narrative.
