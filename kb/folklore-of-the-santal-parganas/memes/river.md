Rivers in Santal folklore serve as liminal spaces — thresholds where human and supernatural worlds intersect, where identities are tested, and where fateful meetings occur. The river is simultaneously a practical feature of the Santal landscape and a symbolic boundary that characters must cross, literally and figuratively, to advance their stories.

## The River as Ritual Space

Bathing in the river is the ritual act that sets countless Santal plots in motion. A Raja's daughter discovers her destined husband while bathing when a _loa_ fruit containing a mysterious long hair floats past her (185-027). A man combs his hair on the riverbank after bathing, and the hair he discards becomes the object of a princess's vow (185-027). The riverbank is where the ordinary dissolves and the extraordinary begins.

This motif extends beyond romance. The approach to a pool or river at which people bathe is a culturally marked space in Santal life (185-005), carrying specific social protocols. Crossing a flooded river tests courage and reveals character (185-011). When a character is stranded at a riverbank, supernatural aid often appears — as when a great snake rises from the water to help a girl cross (185-012).

## Misunderstanding and Transformation at the River

The river also functions as a site of linguistic and social misunderstanding. In one story, a father-in-law tells his daughter-in-law to "fill the little river and the big river" — meaning to prepare rice beer — but visitors overhearing this think they are about to be sacrificed and buried in a riverbed (141-001, 141-002). The comic resolution, when the young woman explains the true meaning of the household idiom, transforms terror into laughter (141-003).

## The Banyan Tree by the River

On the banks of the Badi river stands a village called Sarjomghutu, where a large banyan tree grows. The villagers have no human headman, because any appointed headman invariably dies; instead, they have made a _bonga_ who lives in the banyan tree their headman (149-001). When matters must be decided, they meet at the tree, take out a stool, and invite the invisible headman to sit upon it (149-002). The riverbank here becomes a permanent sacred geography — a place where the boundary between human governance and spirit authority is ritually maintained.
