Dreams occupy a curious and narrow but significant place in Santal folklore. Rather than being portals to prophecy or divine communication, dreams in these stories are treated as ambiguous phenomena — potentially meaningless, yet capable of being deployed as narrative devices to outwit authority.

## The Ambiguity of Dreams

In the story of the Grasping Raja, the Raja is completely puzzled by a dream-based riddle and suggests that there is no coherency in dreams — if the jackal had had some meaningless dream, no one could guess it (099-006). This dismissive view of dreams as incoherent and uninterpretable represents one pole of the Santal attitude toward dreams: they are random mental noise, not messages from the supernatural.

## Dreams as Strategic Device

The jackal, however, turns the Raja's skepticism against him. By framing his clever judgment as a dream, the jackal exploits the Raja's own argument about the meaninglessness of dreams — forcing the Raja either to accept the dream as valid or to contradict his own position. The dream becomes not a source of supernatural knowledge but a rhetorical trap. This pragmatic deployment of dreams — as tools for the clever rather than revelations for the pious — is consistent with the trickster ethic that pervades Santal animal tales. Dreams in Santal folklore are thus not about what they reveal but about how they can be used.
