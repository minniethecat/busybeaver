Theft appears in Santal folklore across multiple registers: as supernatural mischief by the house bonga, as trickster behaviour by the jackal, and as human crime with social consequences. The motif serves to reveal character — who steals, from whom, and why — and to drive plots toward resolution through exposure and punishment.

## The House Bonga as Thief

The house bonga is a persistent thief, stealing every kind of grain and food — cooked and uncooked — from the villagers (151-001). The bonga's theft is supernatural but oddly domestic: it steals food from neighbours to enrich its host household, blurring the line between theft and providence. When a villager caught the bonga carrying a pot of stolen boiled rice and flung his stick at it, the bonga dropped the pot and fled — a rare moment of direct human intervention against supernatural theft (151-002).

## The Jackal as Thief

The jackal steals fowls from villages, dancing along with his tail tapping as he goes (112-007). His theft is performed with a trickster's flair — it is as much performance as crime. The jackal's stealing is integrated into his broader character as a clever but amoral figure who takes what he wants through wit rather than force.

## Human Theft

A farmer watched his rice field to ensure no one stole his seedlings (106-003). When a thief stole a goat and his household ate their fill of the meat, his sons were cautioned not to go near other boys lest they smell the meat on their breath and expose the crime (137-002, 137-003). These human theft narratives explore the social management of illicit acts — concealment, suspicion, and the fear of discovery that follows the stolen meal.
