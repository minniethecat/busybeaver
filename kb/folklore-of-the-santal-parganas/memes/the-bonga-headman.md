The bonga headman of Sarjomghutu is a unique figure in Santal folklore — a spirit who holds formal political office. Unlike bongas who interact with humans through marriage or mischief, the bonga headman is integrated into village governance as a recognized, if invisible, authority.

## Office and Function

The bonga headman lives in a banyan tree where the villagers have established their manjhi than — the headman's ceremonial seat. When any matter has to be decided, the villagers meet at the tree, take out a stool, and invite the invisible headman to sit on it (149-002). The ritual of invitation — treating the invisible spirit as a present and participating member of the council — demonstrates the bonga's integration into Santal political institutions.

## Relationship with Villagers

The villagers get on very well with the bonga headman. At weddings and chhau ceremonies, they offer rice, sindur, and a goat to the banyan tree and invite him (149-003). This is not fear-driven propitiation but ongoing social relationship — the bonga is a member of the community who happens to be invisible. The arrangement solves a practical problem: appointed human headmen invariably die, so the villagers made a bonga their headman instead (149-001).
