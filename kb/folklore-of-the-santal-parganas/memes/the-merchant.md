The merchant appears in Santal folklore as a traveller on the jungle roads, driving a pack-bullock laden with goods. His encounters with predators and tricksters place him at the intersection of the commercial and the wild.

## Role in Stories

The merchant's most notable appearance is in the Leopard Outwitted (chapter 107), where he is driving his pack-bullock along a jungle road when he encounters a leopard fleeing from hunters. The leopard begs to be hidden in the merchant's sack, promising not to harm him (107-002). The merchant agrees, but when the danger passes, the leopard immediately threatens to devour him.

## The Vulnerable Traveller

The merchant represents the commercial world's vulnerability to the jungle's dangers. He travels alone with his goods, outside the protection of the village, on roads that pass through predator territory. His willingness to help the leopard — an act of compassion or foolishness — is immediately betrayed, teaching the lesson that jungle predators do not honour bargains.
