The Kisar Bonga is a specific type of bonga distinguished by its benevolent relationship with a particular household. Unlike bongas who marry humans or cause disease at witches' bidding, the Kisar Bonga — sometimes described as a brownie — is a household spirit whose presence brings prosperity.

## Description

The Kisar Bonga takes up residence in a human house. It is not visible to the household members under normal circumstances, but its effects are tangible: food appears, wealth increases. The Kisar Bonga is a variant of the house bonga (chapter 151), distinguished by its consistent benevolence toward its host.

## Behaviour

The Kisar Bonga steals food for the master of the house and, unless offended, will cause him to grow rich. This theft is supernatural but directed toward the benefit of a specific human — the bonga as provider rather than menace. The conditionality is crucial: the Kisar Bonga's goodwill must be maintained. If offended, it may withdraw its favour or turn malevolent.

## In Stories

The Kisar Bonga is referenced in the introduction to Part IV as an example of bonga benevolence. The house bonga of chapter 151 — a persistent thief who steals food from villagers to enrich the headman's household — is a closely related figure, though that particular bonga is more morally ambiguous than the idealized Kisar Bonga.
