Bhuts are a distinct class of spirit in Santal belief — the ghosts of children who have died before reaching adulthood. Unlike ancestral bongas who receive sacrifices at the Flower and Sohrai festivals, bhuts occupy a more marginal and troubling position in the Santal spirit world.

## Description

When children die, they become bhuts rather than ancestral bongas (166-001). The distinction is categorical: adult death produces an ancestor who must be honoured; child death produces a spirit who is not integrated into the ritual cycle. The bhut's form is not described in detail in the stories — what matters is its ontological status, not its appearance.

## Behaviour

Bhuts do not receive sacrifices. Their exclusion from the ritual economy of offerings and festivals marks them as spirits outside the normal order — beings who exist but are not maintained by human attention. This exclusion may reflect the Santal understanding that children, not having achieved full social personhood through marriage and parenthood, cannot become full ancestors.

## In Stories

The bhut appears in the corpus primarily as a category rather than a character. The statement that children who die become bhuts (166-001) is classificatory rather than narrative — it establishes the taxonomy of the Santal afterlife without narrating interactions between bhuts and the living.
