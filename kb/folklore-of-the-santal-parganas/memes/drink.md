Drinking in Santal folklore is simultaneously social ritual and narrative pivot. Characters drink at rivers to seal friendships (111-001), brew rice beer for communal feasts (113-001), and encounter danger at watering places where tigers and leopards also come to drink (123-004). The drinking vessel — or its absence — marks social status with precision (139-012).

## Rice Beer and Social Bonding

Rice beer (_handi_) is the drink that makes friendship. When two parties wish to seal a bond, they brew rice beer and drink together — the shared intoxication is the contract (112-001). The quality of the beer matters: a woman brews such good beer that her guest drinks until drunk, and in his intoxication he reveals himself vulnerable (113-001). Rice beer is the medium through which social boundaries are dissolved and, sometimes, perilously crossed.

The hen's brewing party gathers all the birds, and the warning "Do not drink rice beer, O fowl!" (113-005) encodes a folk wisdom: drinking carries risk, and those who overindulge expose themselves to predation.

## Watering Places as Sites of Encounter

The tank and the river are shared drinking spaces where species meet. A leopard comes daily to a certain tank to drink, using the root of an _arjun_ tree as his path to the water (123-004). A tiger and a tigress, returning from a hunt, stop to drink at their accustomed river (111-001). These watering places are liminal zones where the domestic and the wild overlap — and where stories pivot on who is drinking when, and who is watching.

The absence of a proper drinking vessel is a mark of poverty that cuts deep. A poor man laments that he has not even a brass cup to drink from (139-012), and this lack drives him to seek fortune. The vessel is an index of worth.

## Intoxication and Vulnerability

Intoxication reveals character. When a man makes up his mind to drink all the beer before taking away the pot, his greed and the drinking become a single act (114-003). A woman who drinks rice beer loses her caution, and a watching suitor exploits the moment (113-001). The Santal tales do not moralise against drinking — it is a given of social life — but they are clear-eyed about its consequences.
