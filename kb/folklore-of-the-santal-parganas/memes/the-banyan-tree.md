The banyan tree at Sarjomghutu is the most vividly described sacred site in Santal folklore. It serves as the residence of the village's bonga headman and the location where village decisions are made and offerings are presented.

## Description

The banyan tree stands in Sarjomghutu village, on the banks of the Badi river. The villagers have established their manjhi than — the headman's ceremonial seat — at the tree. When matters need to be decided, the villagers meet at the tree, take out a stool, and invite the invisible bonga headman to sit on it (149-002).

## Ritual Function

The banyan tree is the site of regular offerings. At weddings and chhau ceremonies, the villagers offer rice, sindur, and a goat to the tree and invite the bonga headman (149-003). The tree thus functions as both shrine and council chamber — a place where governance and worship converge. The same villagers who consult the bonga headman on village matters also bring him wedding offerings, treating him as both magistrate and neighbour.
