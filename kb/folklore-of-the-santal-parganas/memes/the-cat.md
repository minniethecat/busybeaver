The cat occupies a unique position in Santal folklore as a creature that bridges the wild and domestic worlds. Its story is one of transition — from the tiger's hunting companion to a village-dwelling animal that lives among humans.

## Description

The cat is a small carnivore, physically unimpressive compared to its former hunting partners. In the story of the Tigers and the Cat, the cat's small size is both a limitation and an advantage — it cannot fight the tigers, but it can slip into the village where the tigers cannot follow.

## Behaviour

In former days, tigers and cats were friends and used to hunt together and share the game they caught, cooking it as men do rather than eating it raw (114-001). The cat's defining behavioural trait is its ability to navigate between worlds. When the tigers and cat killed a deer but had no fire, the tigers sent the cat to beg a light from the village — the cat was small enough to go where tigers could not (114-002).

But the cat's village mission becomes its escape. Pursued by the tigers who felt betrayed, the cat fled into the village where the dogs protected it. The cat's domestication is thus framed as a strategic defection — it chose the human world over the wild, and has lived among humans ever since.

## In Stories

The primary cat narrative is the Tigers and the Cat (chapter 114), which functions as an origin story for the cat's domestic status. The story explains why cats live in villages rather than jungles, and why tigers still chase cats when they encounter them — a holdover from the ancient betrayal.
