Dance appears in Santal folklore as a form of celebration, ritual, and social display. From the great communal hunts accompanied by drumming to the intimate shield and sword dance where witches cast their spells, dance marks occasions of heightened emotion and supernatural vulnerability.

## Communal Dance and Drumming

The most vivid description of collective dance comes from the great hunt, where beaters assembled with drums that made a noise as if the world were being turned upside down (107-001). This combination of drumming, shouting, and coordinated movement transformed the hunt from a practical activity into a communal ritual, binding participants in shared purpose and rhythm.

## The Shield and Sword Dance

The shield and sword dance occupies a special place in Santal tradition. It is at this dance that witches are said to cast their spells — on one occasion, they cast a spell on a youth until his clothes fell off him in shreds and he was ashamed to dance (176-006). The dance becomes a setting where supernatural forces intervene directly in human social life, exposing vulnerabilities and testing reputations.

## Dance as Deception

Dance can also serve as a cover for evasion. When a money-lender asked a man's wife where her husband was, she always replied that he was dancing the Backwards and Forwards dance (126-001). This mysterious dance, invoked as an excuse, suggests a repertoire of named dances with recognized social meanings — and the possibility of using dance as a pretext to avoid unwanted obligations.
