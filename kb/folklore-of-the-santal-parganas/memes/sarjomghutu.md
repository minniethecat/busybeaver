Sarjomghutu is a village about four miles from Barhait Bazar on the banks of the Badi river (149-001). It is notable in Santal folklore for its unique institution: a bonga who serves as the village headman, living in a banyan tree and adjudicating village affairs.

## Description

The village is situated on the Badi river, near the market town of Barhait Bazar. Its most distinctive feature is the banyan tree where the villagers have established their manjhi than — the headman's seat — and where the invisible bonga headman resides.

## The Bonga Headman

When any matter has to be decided, the villagers all meet at the banyan tree, where they have made their manjhi than. They take out a stool to the tree and invite the invisible headman to sit on it (149-002). The villagers report getting on very well with the bonga headman. If any of them has a wedding or a chhau, they offer rice, sindur, and a goat to the banyan tree and invite the bonga headman (149-003).

## Significance

Sarjomghutu represents the integration of bonga belief into Santal political institutions. The bonga is not merely a spirit to be appeased but an active participant in village governance — an invisible but recognized authority. The villagers of Sarjomghutu have solved the problem of the appointed headman who invariably dies by making a bonga their headman instead (149-001).
