The great hunt is a communal event in Santal folklore, organized in response to a specific threat — most often a man-eating [[The Leopard|leopard]] or [[Tigers|tiger]] whose depredations have become intolerable. The hunt mobilizes the entire community in a coordinated effort to eliminate the danger, and in doing so it turns private fear into public action.

As a narrative device it also opens the jungle: once the beaters drive the game, the fleeing predator meets travellers and tricksters, and new stories spill out of the hunt.

## Organization

When a man-eating leopard's depredations became so serious, the whole neighbouring population decided to have a great hunt and kill it (107-001). The hunt required beaters, drummers, and hunters working in coordination. The drums made a noise as if the world were being turned upside down (107-001), driving the game toward the waiting hunters.

This organization — collective, noisy, disciplined — is what transforms individual dread of the man-eater into a single concerted act of the whole neighbourhood.

## Narrative Significance

The great hunt serves as a framing device for encounters between humans and predators. The leopard, fleeing the beaters, comes upon a merchant on the road and hides in his sack (107-002). The hunt thus sets in motion a chain of encounters that extends well beyond the drive itself.

The event is at once climax and catalyst: climax, because the community finally acts against the threat; catalyst, because the action scatters the danger outward into fresh storylines, linking the hunt to the wider animal world of [[The Jackal]] and his kind.
