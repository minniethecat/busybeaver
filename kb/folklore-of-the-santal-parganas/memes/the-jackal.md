The jackal is one of the most frequently appearing characters in Santal folklore, functioning as the collection's quintessential trickster and standing beside the [[Bongas]] as a shaping force of its moral world. Clever, opportunistic, and often treacherous, he appears across many tales — alongside [[The Leopard]], the crow, and the hen — forming friendships only to betray them.

His stories entertain and instruct at once: the jackal is the charming companion whose word cannot be trusted, and each betrayal carries a warning for the listener.

## Recurring Appearances

The jackal is less a single character than a role, replayed with different partners. The pattern is remarkably stable across the tales:

| Partner | Bond formed | Jackal's turn | PN |
|---------|-------------|---------------|-----|
| The Crow | Mutual support across seasons | Repays hospitality with treachery | 110-001 |
| The Hen | Sworn brother and sister | Plots to eat the chickens by night | 112-001, 112-002 |
| The Crocodiles | Uneasy truce at the water | Escapes by his wits | 113 |
| The Leopardess | Joint hunt for a deer | Claims the game for a "sacrifice" | 123-002 |

In each case the jackal enters a relationship of trust and exits it having taken more than he gave.

## Character and Patterns

The jackal's defining trait is his willingness to betray those who trust him. In the story of the Jackal and the Crow, the two became bosom friends and agreed to support each other, the crow in the hot weather and the jackal in the rainy season (110-001). When the crow later invited the jackal to dinner, the jackal repaid hospitality with treachery.

The pattern repeats in the tale of the Jackal and the Chickens: a jackal and a hen were great friends and regarded each other as brother and sister, and they agreed to hold a feast to celebrate their friendship (112-001). But the jackal secretly planned to eat the chickens at night. The chickens saw through his intention and filled their sleeping shelf with razors and knives (112-002).

## The Trickster Figure

The jackal also embodies cleverness used for survival rather than pure malice. In the story of the Jackal and the Crocodiles he uses his wits to escape a deadly situation. In another tale a jackal takes a book from a bundle and sits down to read it aloud, pretending to be learned (117-002), a comic image that mocks the pretender.

His cunning even reaches into ritual. When a leopardess and her cubs kill a fine deer, the jackal insists on offering the game as a [[Sacrifice]] to the spirit of their dead leopard companion before anyone may eat (123-002) — a pious-sounding pretext that conveniently keeps the meat from being shared.

## Moral Function

The jackal's behaviour serves a didactic purpose. Listeners are warned against the charming but untrustworthy companion, while also being entertained by the stratagems of a character who lives by his wits in a dangerous world.

He is never punished as a villain would be; instead his victims must learn, as the chickens do, to see through him. In this the jackal is the negative twin of every honest friendship the folklore holds up as ideal.
