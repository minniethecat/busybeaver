Princesses in Santal folklore appear as objects of marriage quests, vessels of filial duty, and sometimes agents of their own destiny. Their stories revolve around betrothal conditions, fidelity in marriage, and the tensions between paternal authority and personal desire. In the Santal narrative world, a princess is rarely a passive figure — her decisions, vows, and sometimes her bathing rituals set entire story arcs in motion.

## Marriage and Vows

Marriage to a princess is never straightforward in these tales. It requires fulfilling extraordinary conditions. In one narrative, a Raja's daughter finds a long hair coiled inside a floating _loa_ fruit, and at once vows to marry no one except the man to whom the hair belongs (185-027). The Raja, unable to alter her determination, sends men up the river to search for the owner — a pattern of the princess's vow driving the entire plot forward.

The stakes of princess marriage extend beyond romance. When a princess is widowed, her father insists she remarry, and the search for a suitable match becomes a matter of family honour and social obligation (185-053). The princess's body and marital status are matters of kingdom-level concern, not private affairs.

## The Seven Princesses

A recurring motif involves seven princesses who bathe daily in a tank. Their bathing ritual — observed in secret — becomes the catalyst for encounter, courtship, or transgression (185-054). The number seven carries ritual significance, and the communal bathing scene establishes the princesses as both vulnerable and powerful: their beauty attracts suitors, but their collective presence forms a protective circle.

When a suitor does penetrate this circle, the consequences ripple through the palace. The princess's maid-servant notices the change in her mistress's behaviour before anyone else does (185-055), and rumour eventually reaches the Raja himself (185-056). The servant's observational role highlights the princess's lack of privacy — every detail of her life is surveilled and reported.
