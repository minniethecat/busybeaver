Tigers and leopards appear throughout Santal folklore as formidable predators, representing the ever-present danger of the jungle. Unlike the jackal — a trickster who survives by cunning — the tiger embodies raw physical threat. Stories involving tigers range from straightforward tales of man-eating beasts to complex narratives involving interspecies friendship, bonga control, and moral testing.

## Man-Eaters and Hunters

The tiger's role as a man-eater drives many plotlines. In one tale, a man-eating leopard's depredations became so serious that the whole neighbouring population decided to have a great hunt and kill it (107-001). When the leopard heard the shouting and drumming, it fled toward another jungle (107-002). These hunt narratives reflect the real danger that large predators posed to Santal villages, transformed into story form.

## Beyond the Predator Role

Not all tiger stories are straightforward predator-prey narratives. In the story of the Tiger Cub and the Calf, a tigress and a cow grazed together in a dense jungle, both with young (111-001). Their offspring played together happily until the calf discovered a bowl of milk, setting in motion events that would test the bond between the two families (111-002).

In another tale, tigers and cats were once friends who hunted together and shared their game, cooking it as men do rather than eating it raw (114-001). When they had killed a deer but had no fire, the tigers asked the cat to beg a light from the village — a request that reveals the cat's unique position between the wild and human worlds (114-002).

## Connection to Bongas and Witches

Tigers are closely linked to the supernatural in Santal belief. Bongas keep tigers and leopards crouching in their homes as household guardians (148-002). Witches, when initiated, are made to lead tigers about (176-001). This connection between tigers and supernatural forces elevates the tiger beyond a mere animal — it becomes an instrument of spiritual power, capable of being directed against human victims.
