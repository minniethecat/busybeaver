The Rani — the queen or Raja's wife — appears in Santal folklore primarily as a mother figure, defined by her relationship to her children and her grief at their loss. Her role is more intimate and emotional than the Raja's public authority.

## Role in Stories

The Rani is most vividly portrayed in the story of the Boy and His Fate (chapter 104), where a Raja and Rani had three sons, but they all died when only three or four months old (104-001). When their fourth son was fated to die at sixteen, the parents knew this in advance. The Rani's grief — watching her son play happily, knowing his fate — is the emotional centre of the story.

## Maternal Grief

The Rani embodies the Santal experience of child mortality transformed into narrative. Her knowledge of her son's impending death, combined with her powerlessness to prevent it, creates a specifically maternal tragedy. Unlike the Raja, whose grief may find expression in action or judgment, the Rani's grief is presented as quiet, internal, and unending.
