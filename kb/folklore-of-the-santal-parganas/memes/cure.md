Cure in Santal folklore occupies a liminal space between herbal knowledge, spiritual authority, and narrative resolution. The _ojha_ (medicine man) diagnoses and treats with jungle-gathered remedies (132-002), but some conditions — a father's grief, a daughter's wasting illness — resist all medicine and require supernatural intervention (179-002). The cure is never merely physiological; it restores a character to their place in the social order.

## The Ojha and Herbal Medicine

When a young woman falls ill, the family calls in ojhas to examine her. Only when they exhaust their remedies does the search turn to the jungle for a specific root (132-001, 132-002). The medicine is prepared from tubers, and the patient must remain covered with leaves until the cure is complete (132-003). Healing is a process with strict protocols — removing the covering too soon invites the ojhas' diagnostic scrutiny (132-004).

Herbal knowledge is discovered, not invented. A man suffering from toothache chews a certain bark, finds that it cures the pain, and from that time the plant becomes a recognised remedy (163-001). The Santal pharmacopoeia grows through accident and observation, narrated as gifts of experience rather than systematic research.

## Incurable Conditions

Not everything can be cured. A father, seeing his daughter pine away, declares: "This is a sorrow which you cannot cure" (145-001). The statement marks the boundary of medicine — grief, love-sickness, and spirit-induced maladies lie beyond the ojha's reach. When a woman loses her senses and ojhas try to cure her in vain, the narrative signals that the cause is supernatural, requiring a different kind of intervention (179-002).

Even a king's resources prove insufficient. When a Raja's sore resists all medical treatment, the search for a cure becomes a quest narrative — a journey to the one healer who possesses the secret (139-010). The incurable condition drives the plot forward, sending characters beyond the boundaries of the known world.
