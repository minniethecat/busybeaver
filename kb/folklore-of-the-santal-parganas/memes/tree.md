Trees are not background scenery in Santal folklore — they are actors. A tree is asked for an opinion and delivers a moral verdict on human behaviour (107-003). A tamarind tree shelters a stranger seeking wisdom (131-001). An _arjun_ tree root becomes the leopard's path to water (123-004). In a narrative world where the forest is both home and wilderness, trees anchor stories in place and sometimes step into them as agents.

## The Tree as Witness and Judge

The most striking tree in the Santal corpus is the one that speaks. When a character, seeking validation, asks a tree for its opinion, the tree replies: "Men cut me down and burn me — why should I speak well of them?" (107-003). The tree's grievance is ecological but its function is judicial — it delivers the moral perspective that human self-interest obscures. The tree is witness to human destructiveness across generations, and its memory is longer than any person's.

This speaking tree is not an isolated marvel. It belongs to a pattern in which the natural world — animals, trees, rivers — is consulted for judgment and responds. The tree's voice carries the authority of the disinterested party: it has nothing to gain from flattery and nothing to lose from truth.

## Trees in the Landscape

Specific trees mark specific places. The tamarind tree at whose foot a stranger sits becomes the setting for a lesson about the nature of life (131-001). The _arjun_ tree whose root extends into the water structures the daily movement of a leopard to its drinking place (123-004). Shady trees line a journey, and at each one the traveller stops to rest, creating a rhythm of movement and pause (128-001). Trees are the landmarks by which Santal geography is organised.

Birds nest in the same tree, and when their nestlings begin to quarrel, the tree becomes a contested territory whose ownership must be adjudicated by a human arbiter living nearby (120-001, 120-002). The tree is property, habitat, and disputed ground — a microcosm of social conflict.
