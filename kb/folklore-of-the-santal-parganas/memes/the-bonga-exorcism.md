The bonga exorcism is a ritual practice described in several stories in Part IV of Bompas's collection. When a bonga becomes troublesome — causing illness, deluding people, or stealing crops — ritual action is taken to expel or appease it.

## Ritual Procedure

Exorcism procedures vary by circumstance. When villagers discovered that a bonga was deluding them, they concluded they must offer some sacrifice to appease him (155-002). In another case, a man who had sacrificed a pig to the boundary bonga every year before harvest found that the bonga always reaped part of the crop anyway (158-001). For a more serious case, rams were sacrificed and vows were uttered over nails, which were then driven into a peepul tree — a ritual fixing of the bonga in place (159-005).

## Ambiguity of Results

The exorcism's effectiveness is often ambiguous. The boundary bonga continued to take part of the crop despite annual pig sacrifices (158-001). The bonga exorcised in chapter 159 is driven into a peepul tree, but whether this permanently resolves the problem is unclear. This ambiguity reflects the Santal understanding that bongas, like natural forces, can be managed but not eliminated.
