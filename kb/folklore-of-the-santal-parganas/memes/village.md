The village in Santal folklore is more than a backdrop — it is the organising principle of social life. Villages are where judgments are rendered, where the collective mobilises against threats, and where domestic tensions play out under the watchful eyes of neighbours. The village is simultaneously a place of safety and a stage for exposure.

## The Village as Arbiter

When disputes arise, the villagers gather to judge between the parties. In one story, two brothers quarrel over property and ask the villagers to come and judge between them (130-001). The villagers agree to meet the next morning, and their collective authority — rather than any formal legal institution — provides the mechanism for resolution. The village functions as an informal panchayat, its moral weight derived from shared presence rather than codified law.

This communal judgment extends to family matters. Women go to the jungle with other village women to gather firewood, leaving children in the care of elders (131-001). When something goes wrong in this network of shared responsibility, the entire village becomes involved in the aftermath. The house alone at the end of the village street is both physically and socially on the margin — a position that renders its inhabitants vulnerable (131-004).

## The Village Under Threat

When jackals, tigers, or other predators threaten the village, the response is collective. In one tale, a jackal enters a village to steal fowls, and the villagers flee at first, mistaking the jackal's tail-tapping song for an approaching army (112-009). But when they discover the deception, the villagers regroup: one man proposes making an image covered with birdlime to trap the trickster (112-011). The plan succeeds — the collective intelligence of the village defeats individual cunning.

The village street is the artery along which news, danger, and judgment flow. A woman passing down the village street is observed and assessed by every household (102-003). Newcomers to a village are scrutinised: when strangers take waste land from the headman and begin to cultivate, the village watches their progress with a mixture of curiosity and suspicion (129-001).

## Village and the Outside

The village's identity is defined partly by what lies beyond it. The jungle begins at the village edge — a space of danger, wild animals, and spirits. The village provides sanctuary from these threats, but only to those who belong. Outsiders must negotiate their place: taking shelter in a village during rain (131-003), or seeking hospitality at the house of a potter (121-002). The movement between village and jungle, between belonging and exile, is one of the fundamental rhythms of Santal narrative.
