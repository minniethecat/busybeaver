Music — particularly singing and flute-playing — appears throughout Santal folklore as a medium of emotional expression, a means of attracting supernatural attention, and a marker of refined character. The figure of the solitary flute-player is recurrent, his music bridging the human and spirit worlds.

## The Flute-Player

The most distinctive musical motif is the young man playing the flute alone at night. The Prince who would not marry used often to sit up half the night singing and playing to himself (101-002). His music attracted the attention of a bonga girl, initiating a supernatural romance. Similarly, in Darbar village, a young man was very fond of playing on the flute, and his playing attracted a bonga girl who came to him looking like a human girl while he was tending buffaloes (148-002).

The flute-player's solitude is essential to the motif: it is in moments of private musical expression that the boundary between human and spirit worlds becomes permeable. Music signals sensitivity and refinement — qualities that attract bonga attention and mark the musician as worthy of supernatural connection.

## Drumming and Ritual

Drums serve a different function from the flute. They are associated with collective action and supernatural communication. Hunters use drums to drive game, their noise filling the jungle (107-001). The jackal makes a drum and plays it daily beneath a bank (121-004) — an act that mimics human ritual practice through an animal trickster lens. Drumming at the shield and sword dance provides the occasion for witches to cast their spells (176-006).
