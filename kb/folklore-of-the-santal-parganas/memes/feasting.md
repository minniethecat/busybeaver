Feasting is a central social ritual in Santal folklore, marking friendship, celebration, and community bonds. The preparation and sharing of food — especially rice beer — appears across animal tales and human stories alike, often serving as the setting for both genuine hospitality and treacherous betrayal.

## The Friendship Feast

The feast to celebrate friendship is a recurring motif in the animal tales. The jackal and the crow, having become bosom friends, marked their bond with reciprocal feasts: the crow gave the jackal frogs and grasshoppers, and the jackal invited the crow to dine in return (110-001). Similarly, a jackal and a hen, who regarded each other as brother and sister, agreed to have a feast to celebrate their friendship and both brewed rice beer for the occasion (112-001). In another tale, a hen and a jackal decided to have a feast and each brewed beer — the hen with rice, maize, and millet, the jackal with lizards (113-001).

The details of brewing and food preparation ground these stories in Santal domestic practice: rice beer, the staple celebratory drink, is brewed by both parties, and the quality of the ingredients reflects the character of the host.

## Feasting and Betrayal

The feast is also the setting for treachery, particularly in jackal stories. The jackal's invitation to dinner is often a pretext for predation. After inviting a hen to feast, the jackal secretly planned to return at night and eat the chickens (112-002). The feast thus embodies a central tension in Santal animal tales: the social ritual that should cement bonds becomes the mechanism for their violation.

## Supernatural Feasting

Feasting extends into the supernatural realm. When a human visits a bonga household, he is hospitably received with a meal of dried rice, curds, and gur, and afterwards smokes a pipe with his bonga father-in-law (148-002). But the true nature of the food is revealed when the gifts are unwrapped: the provisions turn into meral leaves and cow-dung cakes (148-003). Supernatural feasting thus carries an ambiguity — the hospitality is genuine, but the substance is illusory.
