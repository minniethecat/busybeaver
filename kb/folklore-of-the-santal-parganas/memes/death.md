Death in Santal folklore is not an ending but a transformation. The deceased do not simply vanish — they become ancestral bongas or bhuts, continuing to influence the living world and receiving ritual attention from their descendants. Stories about death reveal Santal beliefs about the soul, the obligations of the living toward the dead, and the distinctive fates of adults and children.

## Death and Transformation

When grown-up people die, they become ancestral bongas, and sacrifices are offered to them at the Flower and Sohrai festivals. When children die, however, they become bhuts — a different class of spirit (166-001). This distinction between adult and child afterlife reflects the Santal understanding that full personhood, and thus full ancestral status, requires reaching maturity.

The transformation at death carries obligations for the living. Ancestral bongas must be honoured with regular offerings at prescribed festivals. Failure to do so risks offending the ancestors, whose continued goodwill is necessary for the prosperity of the household.

## Premature Death

The premature death of children is a recurring tragic motif. Stories of parents who lost multiple sons in infancy or childhood appear across the collection. One Raja and Rani had three sons, but they all died when only three or four months old (104-001). A Brahman had four sons born to him, but they all died young (105-001). These narratives of repeated loss frame death not as an occasional visitation but as a persistent threat woven into the fabric of family life.
