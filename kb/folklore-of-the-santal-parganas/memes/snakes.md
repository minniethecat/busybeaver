Snakes appear in Santal folklore chiefly in connection with the [[Bongas]] and the supernatural. They are not merely natural dangers but markers of the spirit world, most vividly seen in the bonga household, where the seats are great coiled-up snakes (148-002).

Unlike the actively predatory [[Tigers]] and [[The Leopard]], the snake threatens less by attack than by presence: its stillness tells the human visitor that he has crossed into a space governed by other rules.

## Where Snakes Appear

The snake's meaning shifts with its setting. The table below traces its two principal roles:

| Setting | Form | Function | PN |
|---------|------|----------|-----|
| The bonga household | Coiled seats | Furniture; sign of the spirit world | 148-002 |
| The jungle / tank | Natural serpent | Latent danger, supernatural omen | 155-002 |

In both settings the snake is passive rather than aggressive — a symbol to be read rather than an enemy to be fought.

## Description

In their natural form, snakes are jungle-dwelling reptiles. But in the context of bonga dwellings they serve as furniture, coiled on the floor as seats for the spirit inhabitants. The human visitor to a bonga home is terrified by the sight of snakes serving this domestic function, which signals that he has entered a place governed by different rules than the human world.

## Behaviour

Snakes in Santal folklore are not active predators but passive markers of supernatural presence. They do not attack the human visitor; they simply are there, coiled and waiting, their very presence communicating that he has crossed into bonga territory. This passivity distinguishes them from the actively predatory tigers and leopards that share the bonga household.

## In Stories

The most detailed snake episode appears in the Marriage with Bongas narrative, where a young man visiting his bonga father-in-law finds the seats in the house are great coiled-up snakes, with tigers and leopards crouching on one side (148-002). His terror is immediate, but the snakes take no action against him — they are furniture, not adversaries.

In another tale a bonga deludes villagers until they realize they must offer [[Sacrifice]] to appease him (155-002). The serpent here belongs to the same grammar of the supernatural: a sign that unseen powers are at work and must be answered with ritual.
