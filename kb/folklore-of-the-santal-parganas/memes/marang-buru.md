Marang Buru is a significant supernatural being in Santal belief, credited in the folklore with the decision to introduce witchcraft to humanity. The name appears in the witchcraft origin story as the initiator of a knowledge transmission that shaped Santal religious practice.

## Role in Belief

Marang Buru is described as a great spirit — the term Marang means great or superior, and Buru may refer to a mountain or hill spirit. In the witchcraft origin story, Marang Buru decided that he would teach men witchcraft (175-001). This decision sets the entire witchcraft tradition in motion, positioning Marang Buru as a source of esoteric knowledge rather than a moral legislator.

## The Knowledge Transfer

Marang Buru did not teach witchcraft directly to all humanity but summoned the men to receive instruction. The men went home and told their wives to wash their clothes well, as they were going to Thakur to learn witchcraft (175-002). The women, however, deceived their husbands and went in their place, receiving the knowledge that was intended for men. Marang Buru does not reappear in the narrative after issuing the initial summons — his role is to initiate, not to supervise.

## Significance

Marang Buru represents the Santal belief in a higher spiritual authority that controls the distribution of powerful knowledge. The gender inversion in the knowledge transfer — women receiving what was meant for men — may reflect historical tensions around witchcraft practice in Santal society, where witches were predominantly female despite the tradition that men were the original intended recipients.
