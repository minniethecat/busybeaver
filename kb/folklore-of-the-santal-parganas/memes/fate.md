Fate is a recurring concept in Santal folklore, understood as a predetermined course of events that no human effort can alter. Characters in the stories may know their fate in advance — or have it known to their parents or supernatural beings — but the narrative tension lies not in whether fate can be changed, but in how it unfolds.

## Predestined Death

The most common manifestation of fate in Santal tales is the predestined death of children. In one story, it was fated that a boy should die when he was sixteen years old, and his parents knew this. When they saw him coming happily home from his games of play, their eyes filled with tears at the thought of what was to come (104-001). In another tale of a Brahman whose four sons had all died young, a fifth son was born and grew to boyhood — but it was fated that he too should die before reaching manhood (105-001).

The parents' foreknowledge of their children's fate creates a particular kind of dramatic irony: the reader shares the parents' awareness of the coming tragedy while the child remains blissfully unaware, playing and laughing in the present moment.

## Inescapability

Santal stories do not present fate as something that can be bargained with or evaded. Characters who attempt to circumvent their destiny invariably fail. Those who have been deceived about their fate reproach the deceiver for not having warned them of the fate in store for them (141-003). The inescapability of fate serves as a narrative device that drives plots toward their inevitable conclusions, and as a worldview that places individual agency within a larger, predetermined cosmic order.
