Witchcraft is a deeply held belief in Santal folklore, treated not as superstition but as a practical reality with documented cases and observed consequences. While the higher castes do not believe in witchcraft (174-001), the Santals know of many cases to prove that it is a reality (174-002). Witches are said to cause illness: in one recorded instance, a boy lay ill and senseless, and the cause was attributed to witchcraft (174-003).

## Origins and Initiation

According to Santal tradition, witchcraft was first taught to humanity by Marang Buru, who decided that he would teach men witchcraft (175-001). The men who were summoned told their wives to wash their clothes well in preparation, as they were going to Thakur to learn witchcraft (175-002). However, the women went in their husbands' place. The women deceived their husbands by telling them that Marang Buru had postponed the appointment, while secretly attending the instruction themselves. After the women had returned with their new knowledge, their husbands gradually recovered their senses and remembered their original appointment, but the knowledge had already passed to the women (175-003).

The initiation of girls into witchcraft is a forceful process. When girls are initiated, they are taken away by force and made to lead tigers about (176-001). If any girl refuses to take the final step and will not eat men, she is caused to go mad or die (176-002). This coercive initiation ensures the continuation of witchcraft practice across generations.

## Practitioners and Practices

Generally it is only women who are witches, but there are men who have learned witchcraft, and there are others who, without being initiated, have kept company with witches (176-003). Men may also be taken to witch gatherings to beat the drum, or if a man is very much in love with a girl, he may be allowed to accompany her and learn witchcraft (176-004).

Witches are believed to work harm through spells and supernatural means. At a shield and sword dance, witches once cast a spell on a youth until his clothes fell off him in shreds and he was ashamed to dance (176-006). The consequences of encountering witches can be fatal: the son-in-law of Surai of Karmatane village, named Khade, died from meeting witches and described the experience as he lay dying (176-010).

Although it is true that there are witches and that they consume human victims, witnesses report that you will never see them except when you are alone (176-009). This invisibility to groups reinforces the difficulty of proving or disproving witchcraft claims, and sustains the belief system within the community.
