The fox appears in Santal folklore in a domestic comedy about marriage and deception. Unlike the jackal — the dominant trickster of the animal tales — the fox is portrayed in a more intimate, familial context.

## Description

The fox is a small, cunning carnivore of the jungle. In Santal tales it is distinguished from the jackal less by appearance than by narrative role: where the jackal is a public trickster who deceives friends and strangers alike, the fox's story concerns private domestic relations.

## Behaviour

In the story of A Fox and His Wife (chapter 116), the fox engages in a battle of wits with his spouse — a domestic rather than public deception. The tale explores themes of trust, cleverness, and the dynamics of marriage through the lens of animal characters, a pattern common across the Santal bestiary.

## In Stories

The primary fox narrative is A Fox and His Wife (chapter 116). The story uses the fox couple to examine marital dynamics in a form that is at once entertaining and instructive — the animal masks allowing for a frankness about human relationships that might be less comfortable in a purely human story.
