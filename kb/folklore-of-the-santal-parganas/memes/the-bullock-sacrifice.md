The bullock sacrifice is a scheduled village ritual described in the story of the Bullock Sacrifice (chapter 136). It represents the intersection of Santal religious practice with external political authority, as the sacrifice is disrupted by the Raja's officials.

## The Scheduled Sacrifice

The men of a certain village had fixed a day for sacrificing a bullock (136-001). The advance scheduling — fixing a day — indicates that the bullock sacrifice was a planned communal event, not an emergency propitiation. The whole village participated, and the sacrifice had been prepared for.

## Disruption by Authority

The very day before the sacrifice was to take place, the Raja's sipahis came to the village and carried off all the men to do five days forced labour at the Raja's capital (136-001). The timing — the day before — is cruelly precise. The women were left alone without their men, and the sacrifice could not proceed. This disruption of Santal ritual by state authority represents a recurring tension in the folklore: the Santal religious calendar versus the demands of political power.
