Cattle occupy a foundational position in Santal folklore, functioning simultaneously as economic assets, markers of social status, and narrative catalysts. The loss of a cow triggers legal disputes (099-002); the grazing of cattle structures daily life (135-002); and cattle ownership — or its loss — signals a family's rise or fall in fortune. The pastoral landscape of the Santal Parganas, where large herds once grazed on abundant pastures (147-001), provides the backdrop for these stories.

## Cattle as Wealth

In the Santal narrative economy, cattle are the primary measure of prosperity. A man's herd size directly reflects his standing. When fortune turns against a character, the decline is measured in cattle: all his horses and cattle die and his lands are sold (139-002). Conversely, divine favour manifests as cattle multiplication — a man whose crops are good finds his cattle increased and multiplied (159-002).

The identification of cattle with wealth is so complete that the theft or loss of a cow becomes the premise for entire story arcs. In "The Grasping Raja," the entire plot hinges on a peasant's attempt to recover a single cow that has strayed into the Raja's herd (099-002). The cow is not merely property — it is livelihood, dignity, and the catalyst that sets the jackal-trickster's justice into motion.

## Cattle in Daily Rhythm

The grazing of cattle structures the daily cycle of Santal village life. Plough cattle are taken out for grazing before sunrise (135-002); boys tend the herds together in the jungle (137-003); and at evening, cowherds drive the cattle home — a moment that in folklore often coincides with supernatural encounters (159-006). The pastoral routine provides both the setting and the occasion for story events.

The Sohrae festival, a major Santal harvest celebration, involves the ritual anointing of cattle with oil — a practice attested in the tales (138-003). This festival link shows cattle not merely as economic assets but as beings deserving ritual attention, integrated into the ceremonial cycle of Santal religion.

## Cattle as Narrative Device

Beyond their economic role, cattle serve specific narrative functions. A character disguised as a cowherd can move unnoticed through social spaces (142-003). Buffaloes grazing near jungle edges create moments of vulnerability where wild animals strike (185-001). The cattle pen attached to the house becomes a hiding place, a site of surveillance, or a point of danger (150-001). Cattle are never mere background — they are active elements in the machinery of Santal storytelling.
