The friendship feast is a recurring narrative event in Santal animal tales. Two characters — typically a jackal and a bird — agree to celebrate their friendship by brewing rice beer and sharing a meal. The feast establishes bonds that the jackal almost invariably betrays.

## The Ritual Pattern

The friendship feast follows a consistent pattern across multiple stories. Two characters become friends and agree to celebrate with a feast. Both brew rice beer — the ingredients revealing their natures (the hen brews with rice, maize, and millet; the jackal with lizards). The feast is held, and the jackal subsequently plans to eat his friend (110-001, 112-001, 113-001).

## Social Function

The friendship feast serves as both celebration and test. The hospitality offered and received reveals character: the hen gives generously, the jackal takes predatorily. The feast is the moment when the friendship is supposed to be cemented but is instead exposed as hollow. The repetition of this pattern across multiple stories suggests it was a well-established narrative formula in Santal oral tradition — a set-piece that audiences would recognize and anticipate.
