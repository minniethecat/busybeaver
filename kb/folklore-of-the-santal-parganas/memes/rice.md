Rice in Santal folklore is more than food — it is the axis around which the agricultural year turns and the medium through which social bonds are forged. From the transplanting of seedlings (106-002) to the brewing of rice beer for friendship feasts (112-001), rice marks the rhythm of work, celebration, and ritual in the Santal world.

## Rice in Agriculture and Daily Life

The transplanting season demands total communal effort. When a family is so busy transplanting rice that they cannot watch over their seedlings, they leave them in the care of a crab — a compact that mixes trust with the precariousness of depending on the natural world (106-002, 106-003). The rice field is a place of labour but also of encounter: when a character gives up on his quest, he turns to the rice fields to look for fish and crabs (112-006), finding subsistence where ambition failed.

The cooking of rice is a ritual of preparation. Before setting out on a journey, a bridegroom is allowed to cook and eat his rice — the meal is both practical sustenance and a marker of departure, a last domestic act before the trials ahead (104-003).

## Rice in Ritual and Feasting

Rice beer (_handi_) is the drink of friendship and celebration. Two friends brew rice beer together and drink at each other's houses to seal their bond (112-001). The hen brews with rice, maize, and millet for a great occasion, and the fowl is warned: "Do not drink rice beer, O fowl!" (113-001, 113-005) — a caution that links rice beer to loss of control and vulnerability.

In the wedding feast, rice becomes a medium for hidden messages. Two thorns concealed in the rice served to the bride and groom test their alertness and composure (105-005, 105-007). The rice dish is not merely nourishment but a surface on which social dramas are inscribed.
