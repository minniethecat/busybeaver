The elephant appears in Santal animal tales as a creature of immense physical power whose size paradoxically makes it vulnerable to much smaller adversaries. The story of the Elephant and the Ants (chapter 115) presents the classic folk motif of the mighty brought low by the humble.

## Description

The elephant is the largest land animal, characterized by its great strength and imposing presence. In the Santal landscape, elephants inhabit the deep jungle. In the tales, the elephant's size is its defining trait — and its narrative vulnerability.

## Behaviour

The elephant's power is physical, not intellectual. When ants infest the elephant's ears and cause unbearable irritation, the elephant's strength is useless against such small adversaries. The tale illustrates a fundamental Santal folk wisdom: power comes in forms other than size, and the mighty should not despise the small.

## In Stories

The Elephant and the Ants (chapter 115) tells of an elephant tormented by ants who enter its ears. Despite its enormous strength, the elephant cannot dislodge them, and the ants triumph through persistence and numbers rather than individual power. The story belongs to the broader tradition of Santal animal tales that invert expected hierarchies.
