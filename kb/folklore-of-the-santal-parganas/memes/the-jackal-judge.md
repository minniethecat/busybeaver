The jackal's role as judge in the Grasping Raja story represents the fullest expression of the trickster-as-social-critic motif in Santal folklore. More than merely clever, the jackal in this role delivers justice — using wit to achieve what established authority cannot or will not.

## The Judge's Method

The jackal's judicial method is indirection. Rather than hear evidence and render a verdict, the jackal poses a riddle based on a dream, forcing the Raja to acknowledge the dream's validity or contradict his own claim that dreams are meaningless (099-006). When the Raja is trapped by his own logic, the jackal reveals the riddle as an allegory of the Raja's own theft. The method is Socratic: the judge leads the accused to condemn himself.

## Social Commentary

The jackal-as-judge embodies a distinctly Santal critique of power. The Raja has grown old in judging cases and finding out the truth (099-005), yet his experience counts for nothing against the jackal's cleverness. The story suggests that justice is not a function of authority but of intelligence — and that intelligence may be found in unexpected places. For a tribal community often subject to the authority of non-Santal rulers, this message carries clear social implications.
