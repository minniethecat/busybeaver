Crocodiles appear in Santal animal tales as dangerous but outwittable adversaries. Unlike the supernatural snakes of bonga dwellings, crocodiles are natural creatures whose threat can be neutralized through cleverness — a theme consistent with the trickster tradition in Santal folklore.

## Description

Crocodiles inhabit the rivers, tanks, and water bodies of the Santal landscape. In the tales they are presented as physically formidable — large, toothy, aquatic — but intellectually limited. Their power is brute force, not cunning.

## Behaviour

The crocodile's defining behavioural trait in Santal tales is gullibility. In the story of the Jackal and the Crocodiles, the jackal encounters a family of crocodiles and must use his wits to escape being eaten. Each morning he takes the five little crocodiles out of the water and attempts to teach them to repeat a nonsense phrase — a stratagem that plays upon their willingness to follow instruction (117-007). The crocodiles' inability to recognize the trick mirrors the jackal's other victims — the hen, the crow — who trust where they should not.

## In Stories

The primary crocodile narrative is the Jackal and the Crocodiles (chapter 117). The jackal's encounter with the crocodiles follows the established pattern of jackal tales: a physically weaker protagonist faces a stronger adversary and prevails through wit. The crocodiles, despite their aquatic advantage and fearsome appearance, are no match for the jackal's cleverness.
