The Brahmin appears in Santal folklore as a figure from the wider Hindu social world that surrounds Santal society. As members of the priestly caste, Brahmins in these stories often represent the perspective of the higher castes on Santal beliefs and practices.

## Role in Stories

The Brahmin's most prominent appearance is in the story of the Boy and His Fate (chapter 105), where a Brahmin had four sons born to him, but they all died young — a fifth son grew to boyhood, but it was fated that he too should die before reaching manhood (105-001). The Brahmin father's repeated loss frames fate as a force that transcends caste — even the priestly class is subject to destiny.

In another story, a Brahmin girl's relations did not give her in marriage, and she died unmarried (163-001). The Brahmin family's failure to arrange marriage for their daughter results in tragedy, suggesting that social obligations apply regardless of caste status.

## High Caste Perspective

Brahmins in Santal folklore sometimes represent the skeptical outsider. The statement that higher castes do not believe in witchcraft (174-001) is voiced from a Brahminical perspective, contrasting with Santal belief in witchcraft as a practical reality. This tension between caste perspectives is a recurring subtext in the collection.
