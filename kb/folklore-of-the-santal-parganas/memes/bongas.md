Bongas are the spirit beings that occupy the central place in Santal folklore and cosmology, standing at the meeting point of nearly every other theme in the collection, from [[Witchcraft]] and [[Sacrifice]] to [[Marriage]] and [[Death]].

The Santals believe bongas exist everywhere in the world and take an active, if not usually visible, part in human affairs. They are not regarded as divine — that older, simpler devotion belonged to [[Thakur]] — but are rather analogous to fairies or nature spirits: frequently malignant, yet capable of gratitude and benevolence.

Bongas are known to assume human form, particularly as young men or women, and to form lasting connections with human beings of the opposite sex. (148-001)

## Kinds of Bonga

Santal belief does not treat bongas as a single undifferentiated class. The stories distinguish several overlapping kinds by dwelling, function, and disposition:

| Kind | Dwelling | Disposition | Representative story |
|------|----------|-------------|----------------------|
| Bonga bride / husband | Jungle, tank, spirit household | Ambivalent — devoted but dangerous | Marriage with Bongas (148) |
| House bonga | Human dwellings | Mischievous thief | The Greedy House Bonga (151) |
| Village headman bonga | Banyan tree | Benevolent, protective | The Invisible Headman (149) |
| [[Kisar Bonga]] (brownie) | The master's house | Benevolent if unoffended | — |
| Witchcraft bonga | Summoned by witches | Malignant | Witch initiation (176) |

These categories are not rigid: a single bonga may shift from benefactor to menace depending on whether it is honoured or offended.

## In Santal Belief

The Santal worldview treats bongas as a pervasive and practical reality rather than remote mythology. There have been many cases recorded of Santals marrying bonga girls — not with formal marriage ceremonies, but the marriage which results from merely living together (148-001).

In Darbar village near Silingi, two men are known to have married bongas (148-002). The relationships were understood by the community as real unions, with the bonga partners visiting and living with their human spouses over extended periods.

Bongas are also bound up with the practice of [[Witchcraft]]. At the bidding of witches they are said to cause disease, and they may hound on [[Tigers]] to catch men. When girls are initiated into witchcraft, they are taken away by force and made to lead tigers about (176-001).

Girls who refuse to take the final step and will not eat men are caused to go mad or die (176-002). Yet the Santals also recognize benevolent bongas: the [[Kisar Bonga]], or household brownie, steals food for the master of the house and, unless offended, will cause him to grow rich.

Some bongas take on institutional roles within Santal village life. In Sarjomghutu village near [[Barhait Bazar]], the villagers have made a bonga who lives in a banyan tree their invisible headman (149-001). When any matter has to be decided, the villagers meet at the banyan tree and invite the invisible headman to sit on a stool (149-002).

## Manifestations and Household Life

Bongas frequently assume the form of young women and men to interact with humans. One account from Darbar village tells of a young man whose flute-playing attracted a bonga girl. She came to him looking like a human girl while he was tending buffaloes, and she later invited him to visit her parents (148-002).

The bonga household proved terrifying to the human visitor: the seats in the house were great coiled-up [[Snakes]], and tigers and leopards crouched on one side (148-002). Despite his fear, he was hospitably received, smoked a pipe with his bonga father-in-law, and departed with gifts of dried rice and cakes (148-002).

When he later opened the bundle, however, the food had transformed into meral leaves and cow-dung cakes, revealing its supernatural origin (148-003).

The house bonga is a particular type that lives in human dwellings. In one account, a house bonga in the headman's house was a persistent thief, stealing every kind of grain and food, cooked and uncooked, from the villagers (151-001). When a villager caught it carrying a pot of stolen boiled rice, it dropped the pot and fled (151-002, 151-003).

## Ancestors and the Bonga World

The bonga population is continually renewed from the human world. When grown-up people die they become ancestral bongas, and sacrifices are offered to them at the [[Flower Festival]] and [[Sohrai Festival]]; when children die they become [[Bhuts]] (166-001).

This transformation makes the boundary between the living and the spirit world permeable in both directions: the living may marry into the bonga world, and the dead join it. The reciprocal duty of the living is to feed the ancestral bongas through [[Sacrifice]], and in return the bongas watch over their descendants.
