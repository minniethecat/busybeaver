Judgment and dispute resolution are central themes in Santal folklore, particularly in stories featuring the panchayat — the traditional village council that adjudicates disputes. The narrative pattern often involves an established authority (the Raja) being outwitted by a clever outsider who proves to be the better judge.

## The Panchayat

When a man lost his cow to the Raja's herds, the jackal advised him to go back to the Raja and demand a panchayat to settle the matter (099-003). The panchayat was the recognized forum for dispute resolution, and demanding one was a way of asserting one's rights against even royal authority. The Raja, though angry at being challenged, went outside the city, met the panchayat, and ordered them to get to business quickly (099-004). This procedural framework — the council, the demand for justice, the public hearing — reflects actual Santal dispute resolution practices embedded in narrative form.

## The Clever Judge

The story of the Grasping Raja subverts the expected hierarchy of judgment. The jackal — a lowly animal — declares himself a cleverer judge than the Raja, who has grown old in judging cases and finding out the truth (099-005). The jackal proceeds to demonstrate superior wisdom through a dream-based riddle. The tale ultimately has the Raja write out a promise to restore the cow and pay twenty-five rupees to the panchayat (099-007) — justice achieved through cleverness rather than authority. This inversion — where the powerless out-judge the powerful — is a recurring Santal narrative pattern with clear social implications.
