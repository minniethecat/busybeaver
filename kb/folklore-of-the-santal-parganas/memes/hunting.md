Hunting in Santal folklore is primarily a communal activity. When a man-eating leopard's depredations became so serious, the whole neighbouring population decided to have a great hunt and kill it (107-001). The hunt was organized with beaters and drums, transforming individual danger into collective action. In former days, tigers and cats were friends and used to hunt together and share the game they caught, cooking it as men do rather than eating raw (114-001). Some figures commanded such fear that no men dared hunt in their forest, providing protection to those who sheltered under their authority (119-001).

## Communal Organization

The great hunt required coordination: beaters assembled with drums, making noise to drive the game toward waiting hunters (107-001). The drumming was so loud it seemed as if the world were being turned upside down. This collective mobilization against a shared threat — the man-eater — illustrates how hunting transcended individual skill to become a community undertaking.

## Predator and Prey

The hunt narrative often inverts, with the hunter becoming the hunted. When a leopard escaped the beaters, it encountered a merchant on the road and demanded to be released from the sack where it had hidden (107-003). The boundary between hunter and hunted is permeable — those who pursue may find themselves pursued.
