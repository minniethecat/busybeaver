The Santal Parganas is the historic region in eastern India (now part of Jharkhand) that forms the geographic and cultural setting for all the stories in Cecil Henry Bompas's collection. The region is the traditional homeland of the Santal people, one of the largest indigenous tribal communities in India.

## Description

The Santal Parganas is a forested region of hills and valleys, with villages scattered through the jungle. The landscape of the stories — dense jungle, rice fields, grazing grounds, rivers and tanks — reflects the actual geography of the region. Villages like Darbar, Sarjomghutu, and Barhait Bazar, named in the stories, are real places within the Parganas.

## In Stories

The Santal Parganas is not merely a backdrop but an active presence in the folklore. The jungle is where bongas live, where tigers hunt, and where young men tend buffaloes and play their flutes. The village is where panchayats convene, where witches operate, and where festivals are celebrated. The boundary between village and jungle — between human order and supernatural wildness — is a recurring spatial theme.

The stories were collected by Bompas while he served as a district officer in the Santal Parganas, giving the collection its geographic specificity. The places named in the stories can often be located on a map, grounding the folklore in a real and specific landscape.
