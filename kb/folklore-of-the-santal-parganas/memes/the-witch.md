The witch is a central figure in Santal folklore — predominantly female, initiated through coercion, and believed to wield genuine supernatural power over disease, tigers, and human victims. Unlike the distant authority of the Raja or the abstract force of fate, the witch is an immediate and personal presence in Santal village life.

## Role in Belief

Generally it is only women who are witches, though some men have learned witchcraft and others, without initiation, have kept company with witches (176-003). The association of witchcraft with women is so strong that the initiation narrative — in which men were the original intended recipients of Marang Buru's teaching — functions partly to explain why women rather than men became the primary practitioners.

## Initiation and Power

When girls are initiated into witchcraft they are taken away by force and made to lead tigers about (176-001). If any girl refuses to take the final step and will not eat men, she is caused to go mad or die (176-002). This coercive initiation distinguishes the witch from the voluntary religious practitioner — she does not choose her power; it is forced upon her.

## In the Community

Witches operate at the intersection of fear and necessity. They are feared for their power to cause illness and death — a boy lay ill and senseless from witchcraft (174-003) — but also consulted. In one account, villagers threatened a witch woman that if her nephew's family got ill again they would kill her, and told her that as her secret had been discovered she must henceforth be their ojha and cure their diseases (183-004). The witch is thus both outcast and healer, feared and indispensable.
