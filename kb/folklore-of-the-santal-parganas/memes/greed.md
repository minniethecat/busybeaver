Greed in Santal folklore is the cardinal moral failing — the vice that sets stories in motion and the flaw that brings the mighty low. It is most vividly embodied in the figure of the grasping Raja, whose refusal to return a poor man's cow triggers a chain of events that exposes the Raja's arrogance and delivers poetic justice through the agency of a trickster jackal (099-001, 099-002). Greed is not merely condemned; it is narratively punished with precision.

## The Grasping Raja

The archetypal story of greed in the Santal corpus is "The Grasping Raja" (Story XCIX). The Raja is described as a stern man, overbearing, who would brook no contradiction. His defining trait is his grasping nature: if a cow or goat strayed into his herds, he would return it only if claimed the same day, and would not listen to any claim made later (099-001). This procedural cruelty — dressing theft in the language of rules — is the hallmark of institutionalised greed.

When a poor man's cow strays into the Raja's herd, the Raja refuses to return it. The man's friends are too afraid to help, and he sits by a stream weeping until Thakur takes pity and sends a jackal to his aid (099-003). The jackal orchestrates a panchayat — a public assembly — and presents the Raja with a riddle about three deaths: one from sleepiness, one from anger, and one from greed (099-005).

## The Riddle of the Three Deaths

The jackal's riddle functions as an embedded moral fable within the larger story. An elephant, returning from a night's feeding, falls asleep blocking the entrance to a snake's hole. The snake, angered by the obstruction, bites and kills the elephant. A jackal, finding the carcass, gorges so greedily that it burrows deep inside the elephant — and when the sun shrinks the hide, the jackal is trapped and dies (099-007). The elephant dies from sleepiness, the snake from anger, the jackal from greed.

The riddle's moral architecture is precise: greed is placed alongside anger and sloth as one of three fatal flaws, but it is the greed of the jackal — the trickster himself — that serves as the climactic warning. Even the clever are destroyed by excess desire.

## The Bonga of Greed

Greed in Santal folklore is not always a human failing. A villager, troubled by mysterious household misfortunes, is told that there is "a greedy _bonga_ in your house who gobbles up everything" (151-003). The supernatural world mirrors the moral world: spirits, too, can be grasping. Exorcising greed — whether from a house or from a heart — requires recognition, confrontation, and sometimes the intervention of a clever outsider.
