The hen is a recurring character in Santal animal tales, appearing most prominently in two jackal stories. As a domestic bird, the hen represents the vulnerable villager — small, edible, but capable of outsmarting the predator through collective action and clever stratagems.

## Description

The hen is a domestic fowl kept in Santal villages for eggs and meat. In the tales, the hen is personified as a friend and potential victim of the jackal — small and physically vulnerable, but possessed of a practical cunning that the jackal underestimates.

## Behaviour

The hen's defining trait is collective cleverness. When a jackal and a hen became great friends and agreed to have a feast, they both brewed rice beer — the hen's brew made with rice, maize, and millet, the jackal's with lizards (113-001). But the hen sees through the jackal's predatory intentions. In the story of the Jackal and the Chickens, the chickens realize the jackal means to eat them at night and fill their sleeping shelf with razors and knives (112-002). The hen's intelligence is not individual genius but practical, collective, and defensive — the wisdom of the vulnerable.

## In Stories

The hen appears in two closely related jackal tales (chapters 112 and 113). Both follow the same pattern: friendship established, feast planned, jackal's predatory intent revealed, hen's countermeasure succeeds. The duplication suggests this was a popular and well-loved narrative pattern in Santal oral tradition.
