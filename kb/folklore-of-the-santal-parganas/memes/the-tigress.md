The tigress appears in a distinctive Santal tale about interspecies friendship and the tension between nature and nurture. The story of the Tiger Cub and the Calf (chapter 111) presents a tigress not as a man-eater but as a mother who forms a friendship that transcends predator-prey boundaries.

## Description

The tigress is a female tiger, a mother with a cub. In the story, she grazes in a dense jungle alongside a cow, both with young (111-001). The tigress and cow become great friends and agree to marry their children to each other — a pact that treats the two species as equivalent social actors.

## Behaviour

The tigress's friendship with the cow is remarkable precisely because it violates expected predator-prey relations. Their cubs play together happily, and the tigress treats the calf as a potential son-in-law rather than prey. The story does not deny the tigress's predatory nature — it simply suspends it in the name of friendship, creating a narrative space where natural enmities can be overcome through social bonds.

## In Stories

The Tiger Cub and the Calf (chapter 111) explores the fragility of cross-species friendship. The bond between tigress and cow, and between their offspring, is genuine but precarious — the discovery of their different natures (milk for the calf, meat for the cub) threatens to undo what affection has built.
