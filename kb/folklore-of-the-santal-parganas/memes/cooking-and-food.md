Cooking and food preparation are grounding details in Santal folklore, anchoring supernatural and extraordinary events in the familiar rhythms of domestic life. Rice is the staple food, and the act of cooking it — boiling rice, preparing meals — recurs as a narrative marker of normalcy against which extraordinary events unfold.

## Rice and the Meal

A prince facing death asked to be allowed to cook and eat his rice before starting, and he put sufficient rice into the pot to feed all his captors, offering them a meal (104-003). His captors consulted together as to whether they should eat it, but their appetites got the better of their caution. In another story, visitors agreed to eat only if the host used no salt in the cooking (105-001). These details of cooking negotiation — what to cook, how to season, whom to feed — reveal food preparation as a domain of social interaction and trust.

## Food and Social Boundaries

Eating together marks community membership. When farmers stayed in the fields transplanting rice, they had their meals there and did not go home until the evening (106-002). The shared meal in the fields dissolves the boundary between work and domesticity, creating a temporary community defined by shared labour and shared food.
