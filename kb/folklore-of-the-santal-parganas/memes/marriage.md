Marriage is a recurring theme in Santal folklore, appearing not merely as background but as a plot-driving force that reveals social values, tensions between obligation and desire, and the porous boundary between the human and spirit worlds. Stories about marriage range from arranged unions negotiated by families to the extraordinary marriages between Santals and bonga girls.

## Arranged Marriage and Bride Selection

The importance of arranged marriage is reflected in the story conventions. When the Prince grew up, the courtiers proposed to the Raja that he should arrange for his son's marriage (101-001). Marriage negotiations involved parties of men visiting villages to assess whether a certain girl would make a suitable bride for the son of one of their friends (103-001). These procedural details ground the stories in recognizable Santal social practice.

Not all arranged marriages succeeded. One story tells of a Brahmin girl whose relations did not give her in marriage, and she died unmarried (163-001) — a tragic outcome that underscores the social expectation that families must secure marriages for their daughters.

## Marriage with Bongas

The most distinctive marriage motif in Santal folklore is the union between humans and bongas. There have been many cases of Santals marrying bonga girls — not with formal marriage ceremonies, but the marriage which results from merely living together (148-001). These unions followed recognizable patterns: the bonga paid the usual brideprice and applied sindur to the woman's forehead (177-001), mirroring human marriage customs.

The bonga marriage carried lasting supernatural consequences. Even after the women married human men, they would still go away to visit the bonga, and when they did so they sent in their place a bonga woman exactly like them in appearance and voice, so that the husband could not tell the difference (177-002). This substitution motif reveals the anxiety underlying the bonga marriage belief — that supernatural unions could never be fully severed.
