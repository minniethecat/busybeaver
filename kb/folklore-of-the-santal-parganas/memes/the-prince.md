The Prince is a recurring protagonist in Santal folklore, typically a young man whose refusal to marry, or whose fated death, drives the narrative forward. Unlike [[The Raja]] — defined by authority and pride — the Prince is defined by youth, potential, and vulnerability, which is precisely what makes him open to the [[Bongas]] and to [[Fate]].

He is less a fixed individual than a role played across several tales, each turning on the same threshold between boyhood and the married life of a grown man.

## The Prince Across the Tales

The Prince's appearances share a structure: a young royal held back from marriage, and a supernatural resolution that carries him across the threshold.

## Role in Stories

In the Prince Who Would Not Marry, the Prince declared that he would not take a wife (100-001), prompting the Raja to imprison him. In the Prince Who Found Two Wives, his solitude and flute-playing attracted a bonga bride (101-002). In the Boy and His Fate, the Prince was fated to die when he reached sixteen (104-001).

The [[The Flute-Player|flute]] recurs as his instrument: it is the Prince's music that reaches across the boundary between the human and the bonga worlds, drawing a spirit bride out of the invisible.

## Youth and Fate

The Prince's defining characteristic is his position between youth and adulthood. His refusal to marry, or his parents' delay in arranging a marriage, keeps him in a liminal state. This liminality makes him available for supernatural encounter.

The bonga bride comes to him in his solitude, and his flute-playing bridges the human and spirit worlds. The Prince who survives his fated death transitions to full adulthood; the Prince who marries a bonga enters a permanent state of between-worlds existence.
