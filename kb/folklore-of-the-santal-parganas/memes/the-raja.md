The Raja is a recurring character type in Santal folklore — the king or ruler who represents established authority. Across the stories, the Raja is consistently portrayed as proud, often unjust, and susceptible to being outwitted by those he considers beneath him.

## Role in Stories

The Raja's narrative function is to serve as the powerful antagonist who must be overcome through cleverness rather than force. In the Grasping Raja (chapter 99), the Raja is described as a stern man, overbearing, who would brook no contradiction. He was a grasping man who would not return stray animals claimed after the same day (099-001). This characterization — proud, rigid, greedy — is established immediately and drives the entire plot toward the jackal's clever judgment.

## The Outwitted Authority

The Raja's pride is his vulnerability. When confronted by the jackal acting as judge, the Raja protests that he has grown old in judging cases and finding out the truth — and yet the jackal, a mere animal, proves the cleverer judge (099-005). The Raja is forced to write out a promise to restore the stolen cow and pay twenty-five rupees to the panchayat (099-007). The humiliation of royal authority by animal cleverness is a recurring Santal narrative pleasure.

In other stories, the Raja's authority is more benign but still challenged. Courtiers propose marriage for the Prince, but the Raja wishes to postpone it (101-001). The Raja's authority over his son's marriage sets in motion the events that lead to the Prince's encounter with the bonga bride.
