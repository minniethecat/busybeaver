Sacrifice is a fundamental religious practice in Santal tradition, performed to appease bongas, honour ancestral spirits, and secure blessings for the community. Animals — bullocks, pigs, goats, fowls, and rams — are the primary offerings, though the tradition also includes the more shadowy practice of human sacrifice, which is alluded to but never openly admitted.

## Animal Sacrifice

The most common form of sacrifice involves animals offered at appointed times or in response to specific needs. In one account, the men of a certain village had fixed a day for sacrificing a bullock, but the very day before the sacrifice was to take place, the Raja's sipahis came and carried off all the men to do forced labour (136-001). The disruption of scheduled sacrifice by external authority highlights the tension between Santal religious practice and outside political power.

Animal sacrifice is closely tied to the agricultural calendar and the propitiation of territorial spirits. One man used to sacrifice a pig to the boundary bonga every year before harvest; but nevertheless the bonga always reaped part of the crop (158-001). Similarly, when villagers realized that some bonga was deluding them, they concluded that they must offer some sacrifice to appease him (155-002). Sacrifices were performed with specific ritual actions: they sacrificed the rams and, uttering vows over nails, drove them into the peepul tree (159-005).

When grown-up people die they become ancestral bongas, and sacrifices are offered to them at the Flower and Sohrai festivals (166-001). During Sohrai, pigs and goats are sacrificed and their meat distributed among the community: one elderly woman remarked that with the pigs and goats sacrificed during the Sohrai, there had been plenty of meat to eat lately, yet she did not feel as if she had had any (181-002). Sacrifices are offered to bongas on all occasions (185-005).

## Human Sacrifice

Human sacrifice is a darker undercurrent in Santal tradition. The villagers of Sarjomghutu, who have a bonga as their invisible headman, are said to sometimes offer human sacrifices — but they will never admit it, for it would bring them bad luck to speak about it (149-002). The silence surrounding this practice reflects both its gravity and the community's awareness of outside judgment.

## Sacrifice and Witchcraft

Sacrifice also figures in witchcraft rituals. Women witches used to sacrifice fowls and goats and make a man servant skin them and cut them up; he then had to roast cakes of the flesh and give them to the Parganna's wife, who distributed them among the other women (176-003). This inversion of normal sacrificial practice — where women rather than men control the ritual — marks witchcraft sacrifice as a distinct domain from communal religious offerings.
