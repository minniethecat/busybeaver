Thakur is a divine figure in Santal folklore who stands apart from the crowded world of the [[Bongas]]. He appears in two distinct contexts: as the sole object of worship during the [[Champa]] period, and as the destination to which men were summoned to receive [[Witchcraft]] instruction from [[Marang Buru]].

Where the bongas are many, local, and morally mixed, Thakur represents an older and simpler devotion — a single high god remembered from the community's remembered past.

## Two Faces of Thakur

The collection preserves Thakur in two different registers, one nostalgic and one uncanny:

| Context | Register | What Thakur is | PN |
|---------|----------|----------------|-----|
| The Champa period | Pious memory | The one god of a "simple and religious" people | 160-003 |
| The witchcraft origin | Uncanny | A place or teacher of forbidden knowledge | 175-002 |

The distance between these two faces is itself telling: the same name anchors both the golden age of pure worship and the shadowy source of witchcraft.

## Role in Belief

In the historical memory of the Champa period, the Santals are described as very simple and religious and only worshipped Thakur (160-003). This positions Thakur as the original, pre-bonga object of Santal devotion — a purer form of religiosity that predated the complex spirit ecology of the present.

The memory carries an implicit contrast. To say the ancestors "only worshipped Thakur" is to measure the present, with its house bongas, witchcraft bongas, and ancestral bongas, against a lost simplicity.

## In the Witchcraft Narrative

Thakur also appears in the witchcraft origin story, where men told their wives they were going to Thakur to learn witchcraft (175-002). Whether Thakur is here the location, the teacher, or both is left ambiguous.

The conjunction of Thakur and [[Marang Buru]] in this narrative suggests a layered theology in which several supernatural beings interact — the high god's name attached, unexpectedly, to the transmission of a dangerous art.
