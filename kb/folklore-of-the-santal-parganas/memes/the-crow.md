The crow appears in Santal animal tales as a secondary character — clever enough to form friendships with the jackal, but ultimately susceptible to the trickster's betrayals, and sometimes a trickster in its own right.

## Description

The crow is a black bird that nests in trees and forages near human settlements. In the stories it is characterized by its intelligence and its vulnerability — it is smart enough to negotiate, but not smart enough to see through the jackal's schemes.

## Behaviour

The crow's friendship with the jackal is established through a bargain: the crow will support the jackal in the hot weather and the jackal will support the crow in the rainy season (110-001). The crow honours its side of the agreement, giving the jackal frogs and grasshoppers. But when the jackal reciprocates, the feast is a pretext for predation — the jackal intends to eat the crow's family.

In another story, the crow is the aggressor. A mother egret returns to her nest to find ugly little black crows in place of her own eggs and immediately taxes the crow with the theft (120-001). This inversion — the crow as thief rather than victim — suggests that the boundary between trickster and tricked is permeable in Santal animal tales.

## In Stories

The crow appears in the Jackal and the Crow (chapter 110) as the jackal's friend and intended victim, and in the Egrets and the Crows (chapter 120) as the nest-usurping trickster. These contrasting roles demonstrate the flexibility of animal characterization in Santal folklore — no creature is fixed in a single moral position.
