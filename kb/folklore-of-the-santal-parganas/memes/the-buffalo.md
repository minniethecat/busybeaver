The buffalo appears in Santal folklore primarily as a domestic animal — herded, sacrificed, and integrated into the economic and ritual life of the village. Unlike the wild predators of the jungle, the buffalo is a creature of the human world.

## Description

The buffalo is a large bovine, domesticated and used for labour, milk, and sacrifice. In the Santal economy, buffaloes represent wealth and subsistence. In the tales, they appear as property to be protected, stolen, or offered.

## Behaviour

Buffaloes are grazing animals, tended by herdsmen. In one story, a bonga girl visits a young man while he is tending buffaloes (148-002). The herding context — solitary, pastoral, at the edge of the village and jungle — provides the setting for supernatural encounter. In another tale, a merchant drives a pack-bullock along a jungle road when he encounters a fleeing leopard (107-002).

## In Stories

The buffalo's most significant narrative role is as an object of sacrifice. The men of a village had fixed a day for sacrificing a bullock, but on the day before the sacrifice, the Raja's men carried them off for forced labour (136-001). The disruption of the sacrifice by external authority highlights the tension between Santal ritual practice and outside political power.
