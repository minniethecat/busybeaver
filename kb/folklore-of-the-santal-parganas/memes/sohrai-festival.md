The Sohrai festival is one of the two great Santal festivals recorded in Bompas's collection, alongside the [[Flower Festival]]. It is a harvest celebration during which [[Sacrifice]] is offered to the ancestral [[Bongas]], and the whole community gathers to feast on the meat of the sacrificed animals.

More than a harvest rite, Sohrai is a scheduled meeting between the living and the dead — the one time of year when [[Death]] is answered with feeding rather than mourning.

## What Happens at Sohrai

The festival braids together three strands — the agricultural, the ritual, and the social:

| Strand | Activity | Meaning | PN |
|--------|----------|---------|-----|
| Agricultural | Marks the harvest | Thanks for the year's crop | 166-001 |
| Ritual | Pigs and goats sacrificed | Feeds the ancestral bongas | 181-002 |
| Social | Communal feasting on the meat | Binds the living community | 181-002 |

## Practice

At Sohrai, pigs and goats are sacrificed and their meat is distributed among the community. The festival is marked by abundant eating — one elderly woman remarked that with the pigs and goats sacrificed during this Sohrai, there had been plenty of meat to eat lately (181-002).

The communal consumption of sacrificial meat binds the community together in shared ritual and shared sustenance, so that the act of feeding the dead is also an act of [[Feasting]] among the living.

## Significance

When grown-up people die they become ancestral bongas, and sacrifices are offered to them at the Flower and Sohrai festivals (166-001). Sohrai is thus not merely a harvest celebration but a moment of ritual contact between the living and the dead.

The ancestors are fed through sacrifice, and in return they continue to watch over their descendants. The festival maintains a reciprocal bond between generations: the living honour the dead, and the dead protect the living — the same exchange that governs Santal dealings with every bonga.
