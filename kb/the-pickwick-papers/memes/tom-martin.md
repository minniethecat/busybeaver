## Overview

Tom Martin, nicknamed "the Butcher," is an inmate of [[debtors-prison|the Fleet]] Prison whose past exploits are recalled with nostalgic admiration by the turnkey [[Tom Roker]]. Martin is a man of few words and sardonic expression, known for a characteristic smile of pity and an economy of language that contrasts with the more theatrical personalities in the prison, such as [[Zephyr]]. His nickname and reputation suggest a former life of physical vigour and pugilistic prowess, now reduced to the confined world of the debtors' prison.

## Role in the Novel

[[mr-martin|Mr. Martin]] appears as one of the established inhabitants of [[debtors-prison|the Fleet]], a member of the chummage system through which new arrivals like [[Mr. Pickwick]] must negotiate for accommodation. He functions as a representative of the prison's permanent population — men who have been there long enough to have developed nicknames, reputations, and an entire social order within the walls. His interactions with [[tom-roker|Roker]], Neddy, and [[mr-simpson|Mr. Simpson]] reveal the internal economy of favours, small sums, and sardonic commentary that governs life in [[debtors-prison|the Fleet]]. (014-042)

## Key Scenes

**[[tom-roker|Roker]]'s Reminiscence** (014-042): The turnkey [[tom-roker|Tom Roker]], while entering [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]]'s name in the prison ledger, is reminded of a former inmate: "A butcher. What a thorough-paced goer he used to be sure-ly! You remember Tom Martin, Neddy?" The recollection, addressed to another man paring mud off his shoes with a pocket-knife, summons the memory of Martin as a legendary figure whose exploits — including whipping a coal-heaver at Fox-under-the-Hill — are still talked of in the prison lodge.

**The Nickname** (014-065): When [[mr-simpson|Mr. Simpson]] thrusts his head out of a window and shouts some word aloud, [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] cannot distinguish what was said, but "rather inferred that it must be some nickname which distinguished [[mr-martin|Mr. Martin]]: from the fact of a great number of gentlemen on the ground below, immediately proceeding to cry 'Butcher!' in imitation of the tone in which that useful class of society are wont, diurnally, to make their presence known at area railings." The moment captures how thoroughly Martin's past identity as a butcher has become his sole identity within the prison — a calling cry that follows him through the yard.

**Stronger Terms** (014-069): When [[mr-simpson|Mr. Simpson]] expresses an opinion, "[[mr-martin|Mr. Martin]] expressed the same opinion in rather stronger terms." The description suggests a man who does not waste words but makes them count — his contributions to conversation being emphatic rather than elaborate.

**Twopence More** (014-075): Discussing the financial arrangements of the chummage system, Martin pragmatically observes: "Well, I don't mind that; it's only twopence a-piece more." The remark reveals the petty economics that dominate prison life, where every twopence is reckoned, and accommodation is a matter of constant small negotiation.

**The Gesture** (014-080–014-081): When asked a question, "[[mr-martin|Mr. Martin]] looked, with a countenance of excessive surprise, at his two friends, and then each gentleman pointed with his right thumb over his left shoulder." The gesture, "imperfectly described in words by the very feeble term of 'over the left,'" conveys light and playful sarcasm. Martin follows the gesture with "a smile of pity" — an expression so characteristic that it seems to define his entire attitude toward the world and his fellow prisoners.
