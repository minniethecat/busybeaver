# Eatanswill

**Eatanswill** is a fictional borough that appears in *The [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] Papers* as the setting for one of the novel's most sustained satirical episodes: the corrupt and riotous parliamentary election. The town's name — a compound suggesting both eating (consumption) and swilling (drinking) — signals Dickens's satirical intent from the outset.

## The Eatanswill Election

The Eatanswill election (Chapter 23) is the novel's definitive satire of pre-Reform British electoral politics. The borough is contested between two parties, the Blues and the Buffs, whose ideological differences are indistinguishable but whose mutual hatred is absolute. The election is characterized by:

- **Bribery**: Voters are plied with food, drink, and money by both parties.
- **Violence**: The streets of Eatanswill are the scene of running battles between supporters of the rival candidates.
- **Partisan Press**: The town supports two newspapers — the [[Eatanswill Gazette]] (Whig/Buff) and the [[Eatanswill Independent]] (Tory/Blue) — each of which denounces the other in language of extraordinary virulence.

[[mr-pickwick|Mr. Pickwick]], visiting the town during the election, observes the proceedings with a mixture of bemusement and dismay. The election scenes, with their mobs, marching bands, and drunken enthusiasm, constitute one of the most vivid set-pieces in the novel.

## Social Geography

Eatanswill functions as a microcosm of early 19th-century provincial England. Its social world includes:

- **The [[mr-pott|Pott]] household**: [[Mr. Pott]], editor of the Gazette, and his wife entertain [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] and expose him to the town's political culture.
- **The Peacock Inn**: The coaching inn where travellers stop (020-135), including [[mr-pickwick|Mr. Pickwick]] and his companions.
- **The rival editors**: [[mr-pott|Pott]] and [[Mr. Slurk]] of the Independent maintain a feud that culminates in physical combat.

## Satirical Significance

Eatanswill represents Dickens's critique of the unreformed electoral system. The borough's name echoes the "rotten boroughs" of the pre-1832 era — constituencies with tiny electorates that were effectively controlled by wealthy patrons. The farcical nature of the Eatanswill election, in which principle is entirely absent and both sides are equally corrupt, is Dickens's comment on a political culture in which party loyalty has entirely replaced public service.
