## Overview

Wills and inheritance form a recurring legal motif in *The Pickwick Papers*, appearing in both the main narrative and inset stories. Dickens portrays the probate process as a site of legal predation, family drama, and comic absurdity — the disposal of property after death becomes a lens through which the novel examines greed, bureaucracy, and human folly.

## Definition

The concept of wills and inheritance encompasses the legal instruments of testamentary disposition, the probate process, the roles of executors and legatees, and the social customs surrounding death and property transfer. In Dickens's novel, these elements serve both as plot mechanics and as vehicles for satire of the legal profession. The probate system functions as a microcosm of the broader legal apparatus — expensive, slow, and rewarding to lawyers at the expense of beneficiaries.

## In Context

The most extended treatment of wills appears through Mr. Pell, the lawyer who handles probate matters. Pell is introduced in the context of a contested will — "he makes me his executor, and leaves the rest to me" (016-053) — establishing the executor's role as both fiduciary and potential beneficiary. Pell's professional demeanor is simultaneously obsequious and predatory: "You are the executor, I see" (027-058), he says to a client, casting an appraising eye over the potential fees.

Sam Weller's earthy summary of the probate process captures its absurdity: "'Cos it must be proved, and probated, and swore to, and all manner of fiddlestrings" (027-016). His comic reduction of legal procedure to "fiddlestrings" is the novel's implicit commentary — the elaborate machinery of probate serves the profession more than the parties. Pell cheerfully confirms the jargon: "Probate, my dear sir, probate" (027-056), savoring the word as if it were a delicacy.

The probate party that follows — "issuing forth, the executor, legatee, attorney, and umpires" (027-081) — satirizes the parasitic ecosystem that attaches itself to inheritance. Every functionary takes a cut, and the process consumes the estate it purports to administer. The executor "whispered" about "the Counsel's Office" (027-111), invoking yet another layer of the legal apparatus that must be fed.

## Examples from the Novel

### The Fleet Prison Debtor's Will

A darker treatment appears in the Fleet Prison debtor's story: a contested will consumes a thousand pounds in Chancery fees, leaving the beneficiary imprisoned for costs far exceeding the estate's value (016-061). This is the novel's most damning commentary on the probate system — the law designed to transfer property instead destroys it. The debtor's situation is a parable of the legal system's capacity to consume its own purpose: "when I was going to take out a probate of the will, the nieces and nephews" (016-057) contested it, and the ensuing litigation devoured everything.

### The Fortunate Legatee

The "fortunate legatee" (016-139) provides comic contrast — a beneficiary whose inheritance arrives with serendipitous timing. Dickens treats the windfall as a plot convenience that speaks to the arbitrary nature of inheritance: fortune, not merit, determines who benefits. Mr. Pell inquires of the other parties, "I presume, are legatees, are they?" (027-060), his tone implying that legatee status is more a matter of legal classification than moral desert.

### Inheritance and Violence in the Bagman's Tale

The bagman's story incorporates inheritance obliquely through the Marquess of Filletoville, the villainous suitor described as "the only son of the powerful Marquess" (021-067). The young lady's remark that she has "cut off the entail" by running the heir through with a sword (021-068) is a pun that collapses inheritance law into physical violence, suggesting Dickens's satirical view of the entire system. The entail — a legal device for controlling inheritance across generations — is severed not by law but by steel, a fantasy of direct action against legal complexity.
