# The Magpie and Stump

The **Magpie and Stump** is a public house in Clare Market, [[london|London]], frequented by lawyers' clerks from the nearby Inns of Court. It appears in Chapter 19 of *The [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] Papers* as the venue for a gathering of legal functionaries and the setting for one of [[job-trotter|Job Trotter]]'s schemes.

## Location and Clientele

Clare Market, the Magpie and Stump's neighbourhood, lies in the vicinity of Lincoln's Inn and the other Inns of Court, making it a natural gathering place for the clerks and functionaries of the legal profession. The tavern's back parlour is the scene of a regular meeting of lawyers' clerks, presided over by [[Mr. Lowten]], a clerk in the office of [[mr-perker|Perker]] and [[mr-perker|Perker]].

The tavern's clientele reflects the social world of the legal underclass — the clerks, copyists, and runners who do the practical work of the law without sharing in its prestige. Dickens, who had personal experience of this world from his own time as a solicitor's clerk, renders it with an insider's eye for its rituals and hierarchies.

## The Clerks' Gathering

The scene at the Magpie and Stump (019-003) presents a convivial assembly of legal clerks who have formed their own miniature society, complete with a chairman ([[mr-lowten|Lowten]]), a system of fines for rule-breaking, and a programme of songs. The gathering is a comic mirror of the more formal institutions that surround it, from [[pickwick-club|the Pickwick Club]] to the House of Commons.

[[mr-lowten|Lowten]], as the clerk to [[mr-perker|Perker]], occupies a position of authority within this society, and the scene at the Magpie and Stump serves to establish his character and social world.

## Job Trotter's Misadventure

The Magpie and Stump also figures in the machinations of [[Job Trotter]], who uses the tavern as a rendezvous point. [[job-trotter|Trotter]]'s schemes — invariably involving disguise, manipulation, and rapid flight — bring a note of farce to the tavern's atmosphere (019-021). The scene exemplifies the way Dickens uses [[london|London]]'s public houses as stages for the novel's intersecting plots.

## The Public House in Dickens

The Magpie and Stump belongs to the extensive Dickensian geography of public houses, taverns, and inns. Like the [[George and Vulture]] or the [[White Hart Hotel]], it provides a contained social world where characters from different stations briefly intersect. Unlike the more respectable coaching inns, however, the Magpie and Stump is a place of clerks and minor functionaries — a pub rather than a hotel, a setting for workaday conviviality rather than gentlemanly travel.
