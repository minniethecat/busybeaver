## Overview

Mr. Sanders is an offstage character — the suitor, and later presumably the husband, of [[mrs-sanders|Susannah Sanders]] ([[Mrs. Sanders]]), who appears as a witness at the trial of *Bardell v. [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]]*. He never speaks a line of dialogue, nor is he ever physically present in any scene. He exists entirely in the testimony of others, reconstructed from love letters and a memorable faint. Yet through these few forensic fragments, Dickens sketches a complete little comic portrait: a man "particularly fond of ducks" who called his sweetheart a "duck" in his correspondence — and whose proposal made her faint "stone dead." (006-166)

## Role in the Novel

Mr. Sanders's function is doubly satirical. Within the trial scene, his love letters — read aloud and parsed for their culinary vocabulary — become part of [[serjeant-buzfuz|Serjeant Buzfuz]]'s absurd forensic strategy of reading romantic intent into [[mr-pickwick|Mr. Pickwick]]'s note about "Chops and Tomato sauce." The contrast between [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]]'s innocent "chops" and Sanders's affectionate "duck" is used by the defence to demonstrate that terms of endearment come in many flavours. Beyond the courtroom, Sanders's relationship with Susannah forms a minor romantic subplot that parallels the novel's marriage theme: the couple's engagement, like several others in the novel, is played for comedy but also for a certain tenderness — she fainted "stone dead" when he asked, which, as [[mrs-sanders|Mrs. Sanders]] herself testifies, is what "everybody as called herself a lady would do, under similar circumstances." (006-167)

## Key Scenes

**The Love Letters Revealed** (006-166–006-167): Mr. Sanders's entire character emerges during the cross-examination of [[mrs-sanders|Susannah Sanders]] at the Bardell v. [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] trial. Asked by the court about her correspondence with Mr. Sanders, [[mrs-sanders|Mrs. Sanders]] testifies that she "had received love-letters, like other ladies." In these letters, Mr. Sanders "had often called her a 'duck,' but never 'chops,' nor yet 'tomato sauce.'" The follow-up — "He was particularly fond of ducks" — is a perfect Dickensian non-sequitur, collapsing the distinction between the lady and the waterfowl into a single absurd image. The testimony then adds, with deadpan logic: "Perhaps if he had been as fond of chops and tomato sauce, he might have called her that, as a term of affection." Sanders's romantic vocabulary thus becomes evidence in a legal argument about the semantics of love — a scene that encapsulates the trial's genius for turning the most intimate human emotions into ridiculous public spectacle. (006-167)

**The Faint** (006-166): Earlier in her testimony, [[mrs-sanders|Mrs. Sanders]] had volunteered that she "fainted away stone dead when Mr. Sanders asked *her* to name the day" — a detail she offers to corroborate her claim that [[mrs-bardell|Mrs. Bardell]]'s faint in [[mr-pickwick|Mr. Pickwick]]'s arms was the natural response of any lady to a marriage proposal. The symmetry is exact and comic: just as [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] allegedly proposed to [[mrs-bardell|Mrs. Bardell]], so Sanders proposed to Susannah; just as [[mrs-bardell|Mrs. Bardell]] fainted, so Susannah fainted. The parallel is meant to strengthen [[mrs-bardell|Mrs. Bardell]]'s case; in practice, it merely makes both women's susceptibility to fainting seem like an occupational hazard of courtship in Goswell Street. (006-166)

## Relationships

- **[[Mrs. Sanders|Susannah Sanders]]** — His intended (and later wife); her testimony at the trial provides the only window into his character
- **[[Mrs. Bardell]]** — His fiancée's friend and neighbour, whose breach-of-promise case inadvertently brings Mr. Sanders's love letters into the public record
- **[[Mr. Pickwick]]** — The defendant in the case; Sanders's romantic vocabulary ("duck") is used by the defence to contrast with [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]]'s innocent "chops and tomato sauce"
