## Overview

The [[fleet-prison|Fleet Prison]] is the dark legal culmination of the [[Bardell v. Pickwick Trial]], where [[Mr. Pickwick]] chooses imprisonment over paying what he considers an unjust judgment. (012-031)

Dickens's depiction of the Fleet in chapters 12–19 of *The [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] Papers* is one of the novel's most memorable sequences: a miniature society within walls, complete with its own economy, class hierarchy, and codes of conduct.

## Location

The Fleet stood on the eastern side of Farringdon Street, near the Fleet River from which it took its name. The prison was accessed through a gate on Fleet Street. (012-096)

Within the walls, a complex internal geography existed: the "poor side" where the most impoverished debtors lived; the "rules" where those who could pay for the privilege enjoyed relative liberty; and the common areas where prisoners mixed, traded, and drank away their confinement. (013-004)

The prison houses a gallery of Dickensian characters: the caged man who posts a man-eating legend (014-108), the impoverished Chancery prisoner, and the comfortable "habitual" debtors who prefer prison life to the outside world.

## Social Commentary

Dickens uses the Fleet as a vehicle for reformist critique. [[Mr. Pickwick]]'s observation that "imprisonment for debt is scarcely any punishment at all" — because it falls hardest on the most honest debtors — is bitterly ironic. (013-030) The truly unscrupulous, the novel shows, enjoy prison as a refuge from creditors.

> "It strikes me, Sam, that imprisonment for debt is scarcely any punishment at all." (013-030)

The Fleet was closed in 1842, just a few years after publication — Dickens's vivid depiction contributed to the public pressure for its abolition.
