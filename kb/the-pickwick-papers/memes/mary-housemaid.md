## Overview

Mary is a pretty housemaid employed in the household of [[mr-nupkins|Mr. Nupkins]], the mayor of [[ipswich|Ipswich]], whose courtship by [[sam-weller|Sam Weller]] constitutes one of the novel's most charming romantic subplots. (005-097) First mentioned obliquely in a valentine sent to her by [[tony-weller|Tony Weller]] on [[sam-weller|Sam]]'s behalf, Mary emerges as a warm, sensible young woman whose growing affection for [[sam-weller|Sam]] is conveyed through a series of tender and comic dialogues punctuated by [[sam-weller|Sam]]'s declarations and Mary's demure responses. (011-035)

## Role in the Novel

Mary's narrative function is to humanise [[sam-weller|Sam Weller]] and to provide a domestic resolution to his story arc. Where [[sam-weller|Sam]] is all Cockney wit and streetwise banter, Mary brings out his gentler, more earnest side — their courtship scenes show [[sam-weller|Sam]] at his most vulnerable and sincere. Their eventual marriage plans, discussed in the novel's closing chapters, offer a companion-piece to the more socially elevated romance of [[nathaniel-winkle|Mr. Winkle]] and [[arabella-allen|Arabella Allen]]. (024-005–028)

## Relationships

- **[[Sam Weller]]** — Mary's suitor and eventual fiancé. [[sam-weller|Sam]]'s courtship of Mary reveals a gentler, more earnest side of the novel's most famous comic character, as he shifts from Cockney wit to sincere affection. (011-035)
- **[[Tony Weller]]** — [[tony-weller|Tony]] acts as [[sam-weller|Sam]]'s proxy in the early stages of the courtship, writing the valentine that opens the relationship. Mary's reaction to Weller senior is not directly depicted, but the elder Weller's involvement underscores the family dimension of [[sam-weller|Sam]]'s romance. (005-081)
- **[[mr-nupkins|Mr. Nupkins]]** — Mary's employer, the mayor of [[ipswich|Ipswich]]; her position in his household provides the setting for her meetings with [[sam-weller|Sam]].
