## Overview

Mrs. Trundle is the former Isabella [[mr-wardle|Wardle]] — known to the family as "[[arabella-allen|Bella]]" — who married young [[mr-trundle|Mr. Trundle]] and settled into domestic life at or near [[dingley-dell|Dingley Dell]]. She appears only in the novel's closing chapters, during the flurry of preparations for [[Emily Wardle]]'s wedding to [[Augustus Snodgrass]]. At this point she is in a "delicate state of health" — almost certainly pregnant — but her determination to attend the ceremony, her dispatch to [[muggleton|Muggleton]] for a new cap and black satin gown, and her spirited rejoinder to the doctor's caution paint the picture of a woman of firm will and social enthusiasm. Though her page-time is minimal, she represents one thread in the novel's larger pattern: the marriage plot, seen not from the bride's perspective but from that of a sister who has already crossed that threshold and is now preparing to welcome another [[mr-wardle|Wardle]] daughter into the married state. (029-017)

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Trundle's role is domestic and structural. Along with her husband [[Mr. Trundle]], she populates the extended [[mr-wardle|Wardle]] family network that surrounds the novel's central marriages. Her presence at [[emily-wardle|Emily]]'s wedding helps to create the sense of a large, warm, slightly chaotic family gathering — the kind of scene Dickens orchestrates as a counterweight to the novel's urban settings and legal machinations. Her "delicate state" also serves a quiet narrative purpose: it nods towards the next generation, the children who will be born into the world the novel has spent its many chapters assembling. In the novel's closing paragraph, [[mr-trundle|Mr. Trundle]] is listed among those who repeatedly apply to [[Mr. Pickwick]] "to act as godfather to their offspring" — a detail that retrospectively confirms Mrs. Trundle's condition and places the Trundles among the novel's many newly fruitful marriages. (029-038)

## Key Scenes

**The Wedding News** (029-017): When old [[mr-wardle|Wardle]] arrives in [[london|London]] to arrange [[emily-wardle|Emily]]'s wedding, Mrs. Trundle must be informed of "all the mighty preparations that were making." Because of her delicate health, the news is conveyed through her husband — but "it was not too much for her, inasmuch as she at once wrote off to [[muggleton|Muggleton]], to order a new cap and a black satin gown, and moreover avowed her determination of being present at the ceremony." The sentence is classic Dickens: the mock-solemn preparation ("lest the news should be too much for her") is immediately undercut by the brisk, practical response of a woman whose primary concern is having the right clothes for the occasion. (029-017)

**The Doctor's Consultation** (029-017): [[mr-trundle|Mr. Trundle]], alarmed by his wife's determination, calls in the doctor. The ensuing exchange is a miniature comedy of medical pragmatism. The doctor says "Mrs. Trundle ought to know best how she felt herself," to which Mrs. Trundle replies "that she felt herself quite equal to it, and that she had made up her mind to go." The doctor — "who was a wise and discreet doctor, and knew what was good for himself as well as for other people" — then opines that perhaps if Mrs. Trundle stayed at home "she might hurt herself more by fretting, than by going, so perhaps she had better go." She does go, with "half a dozen of medicine, to be drunk upon the road." The scene is a deft satire of medical authority, which in this novel is rarely allowed to stand unchallenged. (029-017)

**The Wedding** (029-021): At Dulwich Church, the bridal party includes "the [[mr-wardle|Wardle]]s, and the [[nathaniel-winkle|Winkle]]s, and the bride and bridesmaids, and the Trundles." The plural "Trundles" — Mr. and Mrs. together — quietly affirms the couple's place in the family constellation. After the ceremony, the party returns to [[mr-pickwick|Mr. Pickwick]]'s house for breakfast, where "every face shone forth joyously" and "nothing was to be heard but congratulations and commendations." Mrs. Trundle, like the rest of the assembled company, is absorbed into the general happiness of the occasion — a minor figure, but one whose presence helps to fill out the picture of a family complete. (029-021)

## Relationships

- **[[Mr. Trundle]]** — Her husband, who married "[[arabella-allen|Bella]]" [[mr-wardle|Wardle]]; solicitous of her health, he calls the doctor when she insists on attending the wedding
- **[[Mr. Wardle]]** — Her father, the genial owner of [[manor-farm|Manor Farm]], [[dingley-dell|Dingley Dell]]
- **[[Emily Wardle]]** — Her sister, whose marriage to [[augustus-snodgrass|Augustus Snodgrass]] occasions Mrs. Trundle's appearance
- **[[Mr. Pickwick]]** — The family friend who later acts as godfather to the Trundles' children
- **[[Augustus Snodgrass]]** — Her new brother-in-law, the bridegroom
