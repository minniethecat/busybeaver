## Overview

Prince Bladud is the protagonist of "The True Legend of Prince Bladud," a comic manuscript that [[Mr. Pickwick]] discovers tucked into an inkstand drawer in his [[bath|Bath]] lodgings. The legend — a parody of antiquarian myth-making — casts Bladud as the mighty founder of [[bath|Bath]], whose tears of disappointed love welled up through the earth to become the city's famed hot springs. Dickens uses the inset narrative to satirise both the credulity of local legend and the conventions of romantic historiography, all while positioning [[bath|Bath]] itself as a city built, literally and figuratively, on the management of romantic disappointment. (008-001)

## Role in the Novel

Bladud never appears as a living character; he exists entirely within the manuscript [[mr-pickwick|Mr. Pickwick]] reads. His story serves a double function. On the surface, it provides a moment of leisurely digression — a story-within-a-story — that enriches the [[bath|Bath]] sequence with mock-historical texture. At a deeper level, the legend acts as a thematic mirror for the novel's own marriage plot: Bladud's thwarted love for the Athenian maid parallels the romantic entanglements of the [[pickwick-club|Pickwickians]], while the transformation of his grief into healing waters offers a gently ironic gloss on [[bath|Bath]]'s function as a resort for "elderly ladies and gentlemen who have been disappointed in procuring partners." The narrator's final winking observation — that "large numbers of elderly ladies and gentlemen" annually repair to [[bath|Bath]] to drink the waters — collapses the distance between legend and the novel's present, suggesting that all of [[bath|Bath]] society is, in some sense, still living out the Bladud story. (008-035)

## Key Scenes

**The Old Legend** (008-015–008-022): The manuscript first recounts the "old legend": Prince Bladud, afflicted with leprosy upon his return from studying in Athens, shuns his father's court and consorts with pigs. Among the herd, he befriends a "pig of grave and solemn countenance" — an animal "superior to his fellows, whose grunt was terrible, and whose bite was sharp." Observing the pig's habit of bathing in mud during winter, the Prince tries the same waters and is cured. He hastens back to court, then returns to found [[bath|Bath]]. But the pig himself, having "imprudently taken a bath at too high a temperature," has died — "the natural philosopher was no more!" The absurdity of the pig-as-mentor motif sets the comic tone for everything that follows. (008-015)

**The True Legend — King Lud and the [[marriage-and-courtship|Marriage Plot]]** (008-027–008-030): The "true" legend begins with King Lud Hudibras, a monarch of formidable girth ("the earth shook when he walked: he was so very stout") who sends his son Bladud to finishing school in Athens. Upon the Prince's return, Lud demands that he marry a neighbouring king's daughter. Bladud, however, has already fallen in love with "the fair daughter of a noble Athenian." The narrator pauses to observe that if the Prince had lived in later, more civilised times, "he might at once have married the object of his father's choice, and then set himself seriously to work, to relieve himself of the burden" — a sardonic commentary on Georgian marital mores that glances forward to the novel's own treatment of marriage as social arrangement. (008-028)

**Imprisonment and Escape** (008-030): King Lud, enraged by his son's defiance, orders Bladud confined to "a lofty turret." After the greater part of a year, the Prince escapes — "considerately leaving his dinner knife in the heart of his gaoler, lest the poor fellow (who had a family) should be considered privy to his flight." The parenthetical aside about the gaoler's family is a classic Dickensian touch: a moment of dark comedy that undercuts the romantic grandeur of the escape narrative. Lud, meanwhile, vents his grief by striking off the Lord Chamberlain's "pension and his head together." (008-030)

**The Tears of [[bath|Bath]]** (008-035): Learning that his Athenian love has married another, Bladud flees into the wilderness. Wandering far from his intended route to Athens, he arrives at the site of present-day [[bath|Bath]]. Sinking onto the turf, he weeps and cries: "Would that my wanderings might end here! Would that these grateful tears with which I now mourn hope misplaced, and love despised, might flow in peace for ever!" The pagan gods, who "used occasionally to take people at their words, with a promptness, in some cases, extremely awkward," oblige: the ground opens, swallows the Prince, and closes — "save where his hot tears welled up through the earth, and where they have continued to gush forth ever since." The legend thus transforms [[bath|Bath]]'s thermal springs into the material residue of romantic grief — an irony not lost on the spa-going public. (008-035)

## Relationships

- **King Lud Hudibras** — Bladud's father, a monarch of enormous girth and temper, who imprisons his son for refusing the arranged marriage
- **The Athenian Maid** — The unnamed foreign princess whom Bladud secretly loves, and whose marriage to another precipitates his flight and death
- **The Neighbouring Princess** — The bride King Lud selects; Bladud's refusal to marry her triggers the conflict
- **The Sagacious Pig** — In the burlesque "old legend," the pig whose mud-bathing habits teach Bladud the curative properties of the springs; dies a martyr to science
