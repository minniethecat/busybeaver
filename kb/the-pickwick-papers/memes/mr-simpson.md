## Overview

Mr. Simpson is one of three chum-mates — the others being the "butcher" ([[mr-martin|Mr. Martin]]) and the "drunken chaplain" — who share a cramped, filthy ward in the poor side of [[debtors-prison|the Fleet]] Prison with [[Mr. Pickwick]]. Simpson is introduced via a memorable piece of physical comedy: [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] sees only his leg, thrust out of the bedclothes, and it is "the leg" that first addresses him. Along with his companions, Simpson negotiates the "chummage" payment by which [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] secures a private room, contributing to the scene's vivid depiction of [[debtors-prison|the Fleet]]'s internal economy. His language, marked by "a variety of expletive adjectives" let "loose upon society without any substantive to accompany them," paints him as a man of coarse but colourfully ineffective rhetoric. (014-063)

## Role in the Novel

Mr. Simpson appears solely in Chapter 14, during [[mr-pickwick|Mr. Pickwick]]'s first morning in the poor side of [[debtors-prison|the Fleet]]. His role is to help dramatise the prison's degrading living conditions and the elaborate system of informal payments — chummage, "paying out" — through which inmates negotiated their own micro-hierarchy within the institution's walls. Simpson's linguistic tic, the unmoored expletive, is a minor but effective piece of characterisation: it suggests a man who has the impulse for forceful expression without the discipline to direct it, a verbal disorderliness that mirrors the physical squalor of the shared ward. (014-069)

## Key Scenes

**The Leg** (014-063–014-065): [[mr-pickwick|Mr. Pickwick]] wakes to find a strange leg protruding into his space. After a discontented pause, the leg — which "was" Mr. Simpson — speaks: "I think [[tom-roker|Roker]] might have chummed you somewhere else." [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] wisely remains silent. Simpson then thrusts his head out of the window, whistles shrilly, and shouts a word several times — a word [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] cannot distinguish but which he infers is some nickname for [[mr-martin|Mr. Martin]], based on the chorus of "Butcher!" that rises from the yard below. This moment, in which the prison's population communicates through a system of shouted nicknames and trade-calls, establishes [[debtors-prison|the Fleet]] as a self-contained subculture with its own vernacular. (014-063–014-065)

**The Chummage Negotiation** (014-069–014-077): When [[mr-martin|Mr. Martin]] (the butcher) and the chaplain arrive, the three chum-mates examine [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]]'s billet and begin the ritual of "paying him out." The negotiation — "What will you take to be paid out? The regular chummage is two-and-six. Will you take three bob?" — is conducted in a language [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] cannot initially follow. Simpson's contribution to the bargaining is characteristically unstructured: he "let[s] a variety of expletive adjectives loose upon society without any substantive to accompany them," then tucks up his sleeves and begins washing greens for dinner — a gesture that suggests both the domestic squalor of the ward and Simpson's pragmatic indifference to the negotiation's outcome. When the price reaches three-and-sixpence a week, Simpson adds the clincher: "And stand a gallon of beer down. There!" (014-077) The chaplain adds "And drink it on the spot. Now!" and the deal is done.

## Relationships

- **[[Mr. Pickwick]]** — The newcomer assigned to share the ward, whom Simpson helps negotiate out of it
- **[[mr-martin|Mr. Martin]]** — Simpson's chum-mate; a butcher who leads the chummage negotiation
- **The "drunken chaplain"** — Simpson's other chum-mate; a clergyman whose "very coarse red face" belies his profession
- **[[Tom Roker]]** — The turnkey who assigns [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] to the ward and thus sets the scene in motion
