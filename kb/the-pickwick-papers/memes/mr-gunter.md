## Overview

Mr. Gunter is a very minor character in _The [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] Papers_ who appears briefly in Chapter 4 during the aftermath of the [[rochester|Rochester]] ball. His sole contribution to the narrative is a heated verbal exchange with [[Dr. Slammer]], in which he informs the doctor that he is "no gentleman" and adds a derogatory remark about Dr. Slammer's father. The altercation, though brief, serves to heighten the atmosphere of wounded pride and social rivalry that surrounds the ball, and provides additional motivation for Dr. Slammer's challenge to [[Mr. Winkle]].

## Role in the Novel

Gunter's role is entirely functional: his insult to Dr. Slammer adds fuel to the doctor's already-inflamed sense of honor following his perceived slight by [[Mr. Winkle]] (actually [[Mr. Tupman]] in [[nathaniel-winkle|Winkle]]'s coat). By inserting a second, unrelated insult immediately after the [[nathaniel-winkle|Winkle]] affair, Dickens compounds Dr. Slammer's humiliation and makes his determination to secure satisfaction via duel more psychologically credible. Gunter disappears from the narrative as abruptly as he enters it.

## Key Scenes

**The Aftermath of the [[rochester|Rochester]] Ball (Chapter 4)**

Mr. Gunter appears in a single rapid-fire exchange with [[Dr. Slammer]]. The doctor, already smarting from his encounter at the ball, seizes upon the opportunity to inform Mr. Gunter of a supposed slight:

> "I take this opportunity of informing Mr. Gunter that he is no gentleman." (004-096)

Gunter's response is immediate and escalatory:

> "I know the snug and aristocratic street in which you reside," said Mr. Gunter, "but I'm afraid I shall be under the necessity of troubling you to call at my office." (004-097)

Dr. Slammer presses his point, and the exchange devolves into a rapid-fire series of retorts:

> "What I say, sir," replied Mr. Gunter. (004-099)
> "I'll answer you in half a minute, sir," replied Mr. Gunter. (004-101)
> "I never said anything of the kind, sir," replied Mr. Gunter. (004-103)
> "I'm very glad to see you, sir," replied Mr. Gunter. (004-105)
> "I'll lock up the spoons," replied Mr. Gunter. (004-107)

The final exchange is the most cutting — when Dr. Slammer makes a remark about Gunter's father, Gunter retorts that "his father was quite as respectable as Mr. Gunter's father." (004-108) This genealogical insult, the gravest in the gentlemanly code of the period, brings the scene to its climax before the narrative moves on.

The entire altercation occupies only thirteen paragraphs and is distinguished by Dickens's use of rapid, unadorned dialogue — "said Mr. Gunter" / "replied Mr. Gunter" — to convey the staccato rhythm of an escalating quarrel.
