## Overview

Mr. Namby is a bailiff — formally styled "Officer to the Sheriffs of [[london|London]]" (012-032) — who appears in Chapter 12 of _The [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] Papers_ when he arrives to arrest [[mr-pickwick|Mr. Pickwick]] for the damages awarded in _Bardell v. [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]]_. He is a figure of comic vanity: his "shining beaver" hat, gold toothpick, and large gold ring mark him as a man who has prospered in the unglamorous trade of debt enforcement and who is keen to advertise that prosperity. Dickens treats Namby with the amused contempt he reserves for minor functionaries who mistake their legal authority for personal dignity.

## Role in the Novel

Namby's role is instrumental in the plot: he physically removes [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] from the [[george-and-vulture|George and Vulture]] and delivers him to [[debtors-prison|the Fleet]] Prison, thereby initiating the novel's great pivot from comedy of the road to social critique of the debtors' prison system. His function is to embody the mechanical operation of the law — impersonal, self-interested, and impervious to moral argument. He is not a villain in any grand sense; he is simply a man doing an unpleasant job with more vanity than compassion. His "select dinner-party" (012-057) and his wife's "square piano" (012-048) hint at a domestic prosperity built on the misery of debtors.

## Key Scenes

**The Arrest at the [[george-and-vulture|George and Vulture]] (Chapter 12)**
Namby arrives at [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]]'s inn with his assistant [[mr-smouch|Smouch]]. He announces himself with professional formality:

> "Namby's the name," said the sheriff's deputy, as Mr. Pickwick took his spectacles from under the pillow, and put them on, to read the card. "Namby, Bell Alley, Coleman Street." (012-020)

His self-regard is signaled by his headwear: [[sam-weller|Sam Weller]] fixes his eyes on "Mr. Namby's shining beaver" (012-021). [[sam-weller|Sam]] promptly knocks the hat "to the other side of the room, with such violence, that he had very nearly caused him to swallow the gold toothpick into the bargain" (012-024). Namby is forced to pick up his own hat while issuing threats that [[sam-weller|Sam]] receives "with perfect composure" (012-031).

**At Namby's Office (Chapter 12)**
The coach arrives at "a house with iron bars to all the windows" bearing the inscription "Namby, Officer to the Sheriffs of [[london|London]]" (012-032). Inside, [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] has "the advantage of hearing Mrs. Namby's performance on a square piano overhead" (012-048). Later, the party must wait "half an hour or so for Mr. Namby, who had a select dinner-party and could on no account be disturbed before" (012-057) — a detail that underscores the bailiff's comfortable domesticity.

**The Journey to [[debtors-prison|the Fleet]] (Chapter 12)**
As [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] is escorted toward the prison, "Namby, who kept close beside him, sucking a large gold ring on his little finger" (012-060), embodies the intimate, almost parasitic proximity of the law to its prey.
