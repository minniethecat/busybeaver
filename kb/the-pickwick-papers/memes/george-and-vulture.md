## Overview

The George and Vulture is one of the principal coaching inns of [[London]] in the 1830s, and the establishment most closely associated with the [[pickwick-club|Pickwickians]]' travels. As both a place and a commercial enterprise — an inn is a business with staff, regulars, and social identity — it straddles the boundary between *place* and *organization*.

It is treated here as an organisation: a functioning business whose role in the novel's social architecture makes it as much a character as a setting. The inn's name appears repeatedly in the novel's coaching scenes, representing the commercial heart of the [[Coaching Era]].

## Nature and Purpose

A coaching inn in the 1830s was a complex commercial enterprise. It provided stabling for horses, booking services for stage-coaches, food and lodging for travellers, and a public room where business was transacted.

The George and Vulture, located in [[london|the City]] of [[London]], was one of the historic inns serving the eastern coaching routes. Its name — combining the English patron saint with a bird of prey — reflects the heraldic naming convention of [[london|London]] inns.
