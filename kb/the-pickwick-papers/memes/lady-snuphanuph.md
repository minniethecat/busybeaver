## Overview

The Dowager Lady Snuphanuph is a minor character in the [[bath|Bath]] chapters of _The [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] Papers_, where she represents the upper tier of the spa town's fashionable society. Dickens introduces her through the narration of [[Angelo Cyrus Bantam]], the Master of Ceremonies, who identifies her as one of [[bath|Bath]]'s resident dignitaries: "That's the Dowager Lady Snuphanuph." (007-120) She appears in the card-room at the Assembly Rooms, where her coaxing familiarity with [[angelo-cyrus-bantam|Bantam]] and her good-humoured card-play with [[Mr. Pickwick]] provide a comic glimpse of aristocratic leisure that is at once gracious and faintly ridiculous.

## Role in the Novel

Lady Snuphanuph's part in the narrative is confined to the social panorama of Chapter 7 and a brief reappearance in Chapter 8. She functions as a marker of social altitude: the word "Dowager" and the exotic, resonant surname set her apart from the merely fashionable, placing her in the titled stratum that the [[pickwick-club|Pickwickians]] observe with a mixture of fascination and bemusement. Her card-game with [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] — partnered with [[Mrs. Colonel Wugsby]] against [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] and [[bolo|Miss Bolo]] — is a set-piece of trivial sociability that Dickens renders with affectionate irony.

## Key Scenes

**Entering the Card-Room (Chapter 7)**
Lady Snuphanuph makes her entrance with two other ladies "of an ancient and whist-like appearance." (007-139) She immediately addresses the Master of Ceremonies with familiar authority:

> "Dear Bantam," said the Dowager Lady Snuphanuph, coaxingly, "find us some nice creature to make up a rubber." (007-140)

[[angelo-cyrus-bantam|Bantam]] obliges by introducing [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]]: "[[mr-pickwick|Mr. Pickwick]], Lady Snuphanuph — [[colonel-wugsby|Mrs. Colonel Wugsby]] — [[bolo|Miss Bolo]]." (007-141)

**The Card Game**
The rubber is arranged: "[[mr-pickwick|Mr. Pickwick]] and [[bolo|Miss Bolo]] against Lady Snuphanuph and [[colonel-wugsby|Mrs. Colonel Wugsby]]." (007-142) At the climax of the hand, when uncertainty hangs over which card was the right one, "Lady Snuphanuph would throw herself back in her chair, and laugh and chuckle, and fan herself." (007-149) Her reaction — laughter, fanning, and throwing herself back — is a gesture of aristocratic insouciance that Dickens records with comic precision.

**The Morning After (Chapter 8)**
Lady Snuphanuph reappears the next day alongside "the Honourable [[mr-crushton|Mr. Crushton]], the Dowager Lady Snuphanuph, [[colonel-wugsby|Mrs. Colonel Wugsby]], and all the great people of [[bath|Bath]]" (008-004) — confirming her place in the recurring cast of the spa's fashionable world.
