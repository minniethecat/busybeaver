## Overview

Emily Wardle is one of the two daughters of [[Mr. Wardle]] of [[manor-farm|Manor Farm]], [[dingley-dell|Dingley Dell]], and the romantic interest of [[Augustus Snodgrass]], the poetical [[pickwick-club|Pickwickian]]. Her first spoken line — "Oh, he'll catch his death of cold" (002-127), addressed to her father about the skating [[Mr. Pickwick]] — immediately establishes her as a character of natural sympathy and domestic warmth. Emily's love affair with [[Augustus Snodgrass|Snodgrass]] forms the quietest and least troubled of the novel's three romantic subplots, contrasting with the legal complications of [[Mrs. Bardell]]'s [[Breach of Promise]] suit and the financial imprudence of [[Arabella Allen]]'s secret marriage to [[Nathaniel Winkle]]. Emily and [[augustus-snodgrass|Snodgrass]]'s courtship, conducted in whispers, blushes, and poetry, culminates in [[Mr. Wardle]]'s genial blessing — a resolution that affirms the novel's preference for domestic affection over worldly calculation.

## Role in the Novel

Emily's narrative function is that of the idealised romantic object: she is pretty, good-natured, and utterly without the complications that attend the other love plots. Her relationship with [[Augustus Snodgrass|Snodgrass]] provides the novel with its gentlest treatment of the [[Marriage and Courtship]] theme — a courtship conducted entirely within the bounds of propriety and family approval, requiring only the revelation of its existence (not its rectification) to achieve resolution. When the secret of their attachment is exposed by [[Joe (the fat boy)]] at the Adelphi dinner (026-199–204), [[Mr. Wardle]]'s reaction is not anger at the lovers but at the concealment: "I detest meanness and deceit; this is unjustifiable and indelicate in the highest degree" (026-204). Emily's response — "Augustus, for Heaven's sake, explain it!" (026-205) — defers to the gentleman to make things right, and [[Mr. Perker]]'s timely arrival with news of the elder [[nathaniel-winkle|Mr. Winkle]]'s softening facilitates the reconciliation. Within a page, [[augustus-snodgrass|Snodgrass]] is dining at Emily's side "without the smallest opposition on the old gentleman's part" (026-215).

## Relationships

- **[[Mr. Wardle]]** — Emily's father. [[mr-wardle|Wardle]]'s affection for his daughter is never in doubt; his anger at the concealment (026-204) stems from the breach of trust, not the attachment. His rapid forgiveness exemplifies the novel's endorsement of domestic sentiment over rigid paternal authority.
- **[[Augustus Snodgrass]]** — Emily's lover and eventual husband. Theirs is the novel's most untroubled romance: a poet and a gentlewoman whose attachment needs only acknowledgment to be blessed.
- **[[Arabella Allen]]** — Emily's close friend and confidante. Their parallel romantic situations — both attached to [[pickwick-club|Pickwickians]] — create a bond of shared experience and mutual support (026-093).
- **[[Joe (the fat boy)]]** — The [[mr-wardle|Wardle]]s' servant, whose inadvertent revelation of the secret attachment (026-199) precipitates the crisis and its swift resolution.
- **[[Mary]]** — The housemaid who, like Emily, is involved in a cross-class romance with a [[gentleman|Pickwickian]] servant ([[Sam Weller]]), creating a parallel romantic subplot.
