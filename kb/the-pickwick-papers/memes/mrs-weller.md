## Overview

Mrs. Weller (née Susan) is the second wife of [[Tony Weller]] and stepmother to [[Sam Weller]]. A widow and publican by trade, she is characterised above all by her fervent religiosity and her devotion to the Reverend [[Mr. Stiggins]], the red-nosed "deputy shepherd" of the Brick Lane Temperance Society. Her marriage to [[tony-weller|Tony Weller]] creates a domestic battleground between her evangelical piety and her husband's worldly scepticism. Her death partway through the novel is referenced in later chapters, and her savings form part of [[sam-weller|Sam Weller]]'s eventual inheritance.

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Weller appears primarily in Chapter 17, where her religious melancholy—stoked by [[mr-stiggins|Mr. Stiggins]]—forms the centrepiece of a broadly comic domestic scene. She serves as a satirical portrait of hypocritical religiosity: she weeps over her husband's spiritual state while her "shepherd" consumes the household's provisions and drink. Her death, mentioned in passing at (024-056), represents one of the novel's few off-stage fatalities and paves the way for [[tony-weller|Tony Weller]]'s later reflections on marriage and widowerhood.

## Key Scenes

**First Mention (Chapter 5)**
Mrs. Weller is first referenced in a brief report on her health:
> "'Mrs. Weller passed a very good night, but is uncommon perwerse and unpleasant this mornin'.'" (005-027)

This early reference establishes both her existence and the tone of domestic exasperation that characterises the Weller marriage.

**The Domestic Scene with [[mr-stiggins|Mr. Stiggins]] (Chapter 17)**
The longest and most significant scene featuring Mrs. Weller takes place at the Weller home. [[sam-weller|Sam Weller]] arrives to find his stepmother in a state of religious agitation alongside [[Mr. Stiggins]]. (017-048) Mrs. Weller is deeply distressed by her husband's irreverence:
> "Oh, Samuel! This is dreadful." (017-048)
>
> "Oh, Samuel! Your father makes me wretched. Will nothing do him good?" (017-063)

She earnestly urges [[sam-weller|Sam]] to convert his father, declaring that religion would do him good. (017-101–017-103) When [[sam-weller|Sam]] responds with characteristic irreverence, she cries out:
> "Scoffer!" exclaimed Mrs. Weller. (017-105)

Throughout the scene, Mrs. Weller alternates between weeping, serving food and drink to [[mr-stiggins|Mr. Stiggins]], and lamenting her husband's spiritual state. Her emotional manipulation and genuine distress are rendered with Dickens's typical blend of comedy and pathos.

**Death and Legacy**
Mrs. Weller dies sometime between Chapter 17 and Chapter 24. (024-056) Her death is noted in passing—"the late Mrs. Weller"—reflecting the novel's tendency to dispatch minor characters off-stage. Her "funded savings" are later administered by [[tony-weller|Tony Weller]] for [[sam-weller|Sam]]'s benefit, providing a material legacy despite her brief appearance in the narrative. (027-124)
