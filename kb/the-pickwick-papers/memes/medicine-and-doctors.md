## Overview

Medicine and doctors appear throughout *The [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] Papers* primarily through the comic figures of Bob Sawyer and Benjamin Allen, two medical students whose boisterous habits and dubious professionalism provide some of the novel's funniest episodes.

Dickens portrays the medical profession of the 1830s with affectionate satire — the "Sawbones" of [[sam-weller|Sam Weller]]'s slang, the brandy-fueled practitioners, and the aspiring surgeons who are more interested in punch than in patients.

## Definition

In the novel, "medicine and doctors" refers to the loosely regulated medical profession of early 19th-century England, where the distinctions between physicians, surgeons, and apothecaries were still evolving. Medical students like Bob Sawyer "weren't reg'lar thorough-bred Sawbones; they're only in trainin'" (002-014), apprenticed to practising surgeons rather than attending university medical schools.

## In Context

The Medical Act of 1858 (two decades after *[[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]]*) would eventually unify the profession. In the 1830s, medicine was still a trade learned by apprenticeship. Surgeons, who also cut hair and pulled teeth, occupied a lower social rung than physicians. The medical student — hard-drinking, debt-ridden, and theatrically dissolute — was a recognized comic type in Regency and early Victorian literature.

## Examples from the Novel

### "A Couple o' Sawbones"

[[sam-weller|Sam Weller]] introduces the medical theme on [[christmas|Christmas]] morning at Dingley Dell (002-008 to 002-017). When [[sam-weller|Sam]] announces "a couple o' Sawbones downstairs," [[mr-pickwick|Mr. Pickwick]] is uncertain "whether it was a live animal, or something to eat" (002-011).

[[sam-weller|Sam]] explains: "I thought everybody know'd as a Sawbones was a surgeon" (002-012), but qualifies that these particular specimens "ain't reg'lar thorough-bred Sawbones; they're only in trainin'" (002-014). [[mr-pickwick|Mr. Pickwick]], ever generous, declares them "fine fellows; very fine fellows; with judgments matured by observation and reflection" (002-017) — a verdict [[sam-weller|Sam]] silently undermines by reporting their actual activity: smoking cigars by the kitchen fire (002-018).

### Bob Sawyer's Party

The medical students' dissolute lifestyle reaches its peak in Chapter IV, at Bob Sawyer's lodgings in Lant Street. The party features a "scorbutic youth" and "a gentleman in a shirt emblazoned with pink anchors" (004-070), punch in a white jug, and a landlord who observes that "every gentleman as has ever lived here, has kept up a constant acquaintance with the gutter" (004-020).

### Surgery as Comic Performance

Bob Sawyer's surgical competence is demonstrated at the [[christmas|Christmas]] breakfast when he relates "an agreeable anecdote, about the removal of a tumour on some gentleman's head: which he illustrated by means of an oyster-knife and a half-quartern loaf" (002-057). The performance is received "to the great edification of the assembled company" and establishes Bob as a character whose medical knowledge is inseparable from his appetite for food and drink.
