## Overview

*The Pickwick Papers* remains one of the funniest novels in the English language, and its humor operates on multiple levels simultaneously: broad farce, verbal wit, satirical portraiture, and the gentler comedy of character. Dickens's comic range — from the slapstick of the shooting party to the linguistic inventiveness of Sam Weller's proverbial wisdom — established him as the preeminent humorist of his age.

## Definition

Humor in *Pickwick Papers* encompasses several distinct modes. **Farce** involves exaggerated physical comedy and improbable situations (the cricket match, the skating disaster). **Verbal wit** includes Jingle's telegraphic monologue, Weller's misapplied proverbs ("Wellerisms"), and Dickens's own ironic narrator. **Satire** targets specific institutions and types: the law, electoral politics, journalism, temperance hypocrites. **Character comedy** derives from fixed personality traits repeatedly tested by circumstance: Pickwick's naive goodwill, Winkle's sporting pretensions, Mrs. Bardell's sentimental self-delusion.

## In Context

Dickens began his career writing comic sketches (the *Sketches by Boz*), and *Pickwick* developed from a commission to provide comic text to accompany sporting illustrations. This origin in visual comedy — the illustrator Robert Seymour's plates came first, with Dickens writing to them — shaped the novel's episodic, visually vivid humor. The novel also draws on the 18th-century comic tradition of Fielding and Smollett, combining picaresque incident with satirical observation.

## Examples from the Novel

Mr. Jingle's speech exemplifies one strain of Dickensian humor: "Ah! you should keep dogs — fine animals — sagacious creatures — dog of my own once — pointer — surprising instinct — out shooting one day" (001-002). The telescoping of syntax mirrors the telescoping of his confidence tricks. Sam Weller's proverbs are another: "Beware of the vidders" and "It's over, and can't be helped, and that's one consolation, as they always says in Turkey, ven they cuts the wrong man's head off." The novel's extended set-pieces — the Eatanswill election, the trial of Bardell v. Pickwick, the Fleet Prison — sustain comic invention across chapters, combining farce with sharp social critique.
