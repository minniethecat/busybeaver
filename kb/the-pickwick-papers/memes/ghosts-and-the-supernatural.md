## Overview

Ghosts and the supernatural feature prominently in *The Pickwick Papers* through its inset stories — tales told by characters within the novel that introduce gothic and fantastic elements into an otherwise comic and realistic narrative. These interpolated tales provide tonal contrast and reflect the Victorian appetite for ghost stories, while Dickens consistently undercuts the supernatural with rational or comic framing.

## Definition

The supernatural in *The Pickwick Papers* encompasses apparitions, goblins, spectral mail-coaches, and dreamlike visions that appear exclusively within stories told by characters, not in the primary narrative. Dickens deploys these elements as literary devices — framed tales that allow the novel to explore darker themes while maintaining the comic tone of the main plot. Crucially, the supernatural is always mediated through a storyteller, creating an ironic distance that permits both belief and skepticism.

## In Context

The novel's first inset story, "The Story of the Goblins who Stole a Sexton," appears in the Christmas chapters and establishes the pattern. Gabriel Grub, a misanthropic sexton, encounters goblins described in vivid, grotesque detail: they have "various peaks, which served the goblin in lieu of ruff or neckerchief" (001-018). The goblins subject Grub to a series of visions that reform his character, blending the supernatural with moral instruction.

The goblin king's interrogation of Grub about the contents of his bottle (001-025) escalates into a quasi-legal proceeding, as the goblins invoke "the excise department of the goblins" (001-026) — a comic bureaucratization of the supernatural. The goblin repeatedly demands, "Who drinks spirits alone, and in a churchyard, on such a night as this?" (001-023, 001-027), his refrain gaining menace with each repetition. Dickens exploits the double meaning of "spirits" — liquor and ghosts — to humorous effect, a wordplay that resonates with Grub's intoxicated state.

The bagman's story in Chapter XXI presents a different kind of ghostly encounter — one the narrator frames with ironic distance. The bagman's uncle, after a night of heavy drinking in Edinburgh, stumbles into a wheelwright's yard where he witnesses "the ghosts of mail-coaches and horses, guards, coachmen, and passengers" making their nightly journeys (021-100). The bagman insists his uncle "believed he was the only living person who had ever been taken as a passenger on one of these excursions" (021-100).

Dickens carefully maintains ambiguity throughout the bagman's tale. The uncle "fell into a kind of doze" (021-048) before the ghostly coaches appear, and the setting — a coach-maker's yard full of "old worn-out" vehicles (021-047) — invites a rational explanation: the sleeping man's imagination animating the decaying coaches around him. The supernatural is rendered as a possible dream, a narrative strategy that lets Dickens have his ghosts while keeping the novel grounded.

The landlord's response to the tale is characteristically practical: "I wonder what these ghosts of mail-coaches carry in their bags?" To which the bagman replies, "The dead letters, of course" (021-102–021-103). This deflation of the supernatural into bureaucratic metaphor — dead letters as ghostly correspondence — is quintessentially Dickensian, transforming metaphysical mystery into social satire.

## Examples from the Novel

### Gabriel Grub and the Goblins

"Gabriel Grub" stands as the most elaborate supernatural episode. The goblins are rendered with physical precision — the king goblin "leered maliciously at the terrified sexton" (001-029) and "grinned a broader grin than before" (001-032) — combining horror with grotesque comedy. The visions the goblins show Grub — of families whose happiness he has never known — turn supernatural spectacle into Dickensian social conscience. The goblins' moral purpose is unmistakable: "the spirits flew before his eyes" (001-047), and Grub awakens reformed. Yet Dickens's narrator undercuts the tone: after Grub's transformation, the narrator remarks sardonically that the sexton had seen "spirits" (001-047) of a different kind — the ambiguity of alcohol and apparition is maintained to the end.

### The Bagman's Uncle

The bagman's uncle encounter is supernatural with a materialist twist: the phantom mail-coaches are decayed physical objects, and the entire episode may be a drunken dream. Dickens adds a chilling postscript: the bagman's uncle kept a piece of the coach the ghostly passengers rode in — "a piece of iron" — as a memento, blurring the line between physical proof and tall-tale embellishment (021-105). The bagman's own narration frames the story as possibly true and possibly not, inviting the reader to join the game of belief that the supernatural inset story always plays.

### Sam Weller and Folk Supernaturalism

Outside the inset tales, the supernatural appears in popular speech and folk belief. Sam Weller, discussing a mysterious event, uses the idiom "as they says o' the ghosts" (023-018), demonstrating how supernatural language permeated everyday expression even among the most pragmatic characters. This casual reference reminds the reader that ghost lore was a living element of popular culture, not merely a literary convention.

## Related Concepts

Ghost stories function as a subspecies of [[Storytelling in Pickwick]], the broader pattern of inset narratives. The Christmas setting of Gabriel Grub's tale connects to [[Christmas]] as a time when supernatural visitations were traditional. The drinking that precipitates both Grub's and the bagman's uncle's visions links to [[Inns and Taverns]] and the culture of conviviality. The comic deflation of the supernatural — turning ghostly mail-coaches into carriers of "dead letters" — exemplifies [[Humor and Satire]] as Dickens's characteristic mode.
