## Overview

Mr. Jackson is a law clerk at the firm of [[Dodson and Fogg]], the attorneys prosecuting [[mrs-bardell|Mrs. Bardell]]'s breach of promise suit against [[Mr. Pickwick]]. He appears at key junctures in the legal narrative, serving subpoenas and escorting witnesses with a professionally affable manner that barely conceals the coercive machinery of the law. His encounters with [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]], [[sam-weller|Sam Weller]], [[mrs-bardell|Mrs. Bardell]], and [[mrs-cluppins|Mrs. Cluppins]] make him one of the novel's most effective recurring minor characters in the legal subplot.

## Role in the Novel

Mr. Jackson's primary dramatic function is to propel the legal machinery of _Bardell v. [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]]_ forward. He delivers the initial legal notice to [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] at the [[george-and-vulture|George and Vulture]], serves subpoenas on the principal witnesses, and later escorts [[mrs-bardell|Mrs. Bardell]] to her fateful encounter with the law. In each appearance, his smooth professional manner—"affable," "dexterous," always smiling—stands in ironic contrast to the aggressive litigation he represents.

## Key Scenes

**Serving the Subpoena at the [[george-and-vulture|George and Vulture]] (Chapter 3)**
Mr. Jackson first appears at the inn where [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] is staying, announcing himself with professional brevity:

> "I've come on business. If you're the gentleman, I've a little matter to serve you with." (003-007)

He presents himself to [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] as an emissary from [[dodson-and-fogg|Dodson and Fogg]], serving a legal notice in the breach of promise action. (003-014) With practised efficiency, he identifies the relevant parties and distributes subpoenas:

> "I have subpoenas for the two witnesses to this action." (003-027)

The narrator notes Jackson's "dexterous" handling of the legal documents, a quality that defines his professional manner. (003-027) [[sam-weller|Sam Weller]]'s irreverent responses briefly puzzle the otherwise unflappable clerk. (003-050)

**At [[mrs-bardell|Mrs. Bardell]]'s House (Chapter 18)**
Jackson reappears at [[mrs-bardell|Mrs. Bardell]]'s residence, where he encounters [[Mrs. Cluppins]] and young Tommy:
> "Well, if it ain't Mr. Jackson, the young man from Dodson and Fogg!" (018-071)

Despite [[mrs-bardell|Mrs. Bardell]]'s eager hospitality—she offers him refreshment—Jackson maintains a businesslike reserve. (018-084–018-087) The scene concludes with Jackson escorting [[mrs-bardell|Mrs. Bardell]] into a coach, a seemingly courteous act that carries darker implications for her liberty. (018-093)

**At Court (Chapter 6)**
Jackson appears briefly in the courtroom, escorting [[mrs-bardell|Mrs. Bardell]] and [[mrs-cluppins|Mrs. Cluppins]] through the proceedings. (006-096)

## Relationships

- **[[Dodson and Fogg]]** — His employers; he executes their legal strategy with mechanical efficiency
- **[[Mr. Pickwick]]** — The defendant he serves with legal papers
