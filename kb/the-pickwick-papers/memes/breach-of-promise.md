# Breach of Promise

## Overview

Breach of promise of marriage is a legal action in which one party sues another for breaking an engagement to marry. In *The [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] Papers*, it serves as the central plot device around which the trial of *Bardell v. [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]]* revolves. Mrs. Bardell, [[mr-pickwick|Mr. Pickwick]]'s landlady, brings an action against him for £1,500 in damages, leading to one of the most memorable courtroom scenes in English literature.

## Definition

In 19th-century English law, a breach of promise suit allowed a jilted party (typically a woman) to seek monetary damages when a promised marriage did not take place. The action required proof that a definite promise to marry had been made and subsequently broken.

## In Context

The breach of promise action frames Chapters VI (the trial) and XXIX (the aftermath) of *The [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] Papers*. Dickens uses the lawsuit to satirize the legal profession, the mercenary motives of lawyers like [[dodson-and-fogg|Dodson and Fogg]], and the vulnerability of the court system to manipulation.

## Examples from the Novel

The trial opens with urgency: "breach of promise trial—court is generally full" (006-007). Serjeant Buzfuz, counsel for the plaintiff, formally introduces the charge to the jury: "this is an action for a breach of promise of marriage, in which the damages are laid at £1500" (006-056). The jury, after Justice Stareleigh's summing up, finds for the plaintiff (006-225).

In the novel's closing chapter, the narrator reports that Mrs. Bardell "never brought any more actions for breach of promise of marriage" after her attorneys, [[dodson-and-fogg|Dodson and Fogg]], extracted their costs from the judgment (029-035).
