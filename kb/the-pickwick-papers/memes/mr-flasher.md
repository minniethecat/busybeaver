## Overview

Wilkins Flasher, Esquire, is a City stockbroker who appears in a single chapter of the novel, where he handles the financial transactions arising from the death of [[Mrs. Weller|Mrs. Weller (Susan)]]. Dickens presents him as a man entirely at home in a world where money, betting, and human misfortune are all reduced to entries in a little gold-pencilled book. Flasher's casual wager on whether a bankrupt colleague named Boffer will kill himself, placed with the same insouciance as a bet on port at auction, makes him one of the novel's sharpest satirical portraits of the financial classes. Yet he is competent at what he does: the estate business is transacted swiftly and correctly. The indictment is not of his professional skill but of the moral atmosphere in which that skill operates. (027-068)

## Role in the Novel

Flasher's scene is brief but concentrated. He serves as a window into the world of the Stock Exchange — a place of noise, bustle, and clerks eating ham sandwiches "all day long" as part of the "system." His function in the plot is mechanical: he executes the transfer of [[mrs-weller|Mrs. Weller]]'s funds, paying [[Mr. Weller|Tony Weller]] five hundred and thirty pounds and handing [[sam-weller|Sam]] his two hundred. But the character work is richer than the plot function requires. In his betting-book exchanges with [[mr-simmery|Frank Simmery]], Flasher embodies a particular strain of Dickensian villainy — not the melodramatic villainy of a Dodson or a Fogg, but the everyday moral vacancy of a man for whom everything, including death, is a trading opportunity. (027-084)

## Key Scenes

**The Bet on Boffer** (027-068–027-084): When [[mr-pell|Mr. Pell]] and the Wellers enter Flasher's office, they find him balancing on two legs of an office stool, spearing a wafer-box with a pen-knife, and betting with his colleague [[mr-simmery|Frank Simmery]]. Flasher and [[mr-simmery|Simmery]], both sporting "very open waistcoats and very rolling collars, and very small boots, and very big rings," have just noticed that a man named Boffer has been "expelled the house." Flasher immediately offers ten guineas to five that Boffer cuts his throat. He then pauses thoughtfully: "Perhaps he may hang himself." The bet is revised to "kills himself, in fact" — ten guineas to five, within ten days. Both men enter the wager in their little gold-pencilled books with perfect sang-froid. Flasher then observes, "I'm very sorry he has failed. Capital dinners he gave." The scene distills the ethos of the trading floor — where everything is commodified, no human outcome is too grave to be gambled on, and a man's worth is measured by the quality of his port. (027-068)

**The Estate Transaction** (027-084–027-109): Having concluded his betting, Flasher "condescended to receive Mr. [[mr-pell|Solomon Pell]]'s instructions," fills out printed forms, and leads the party to the Bank. He leaves the Wellers standing in the busy courtyard while he and [[mr-pell|Pell]] go upstairs to the Will Office. On returning, Flasher guides them through the transaction, disappears briefly to the Stock Exchange, and returns with a cheque for five hundred and thirty pounds. Having been paid his commission, he drops the money "carelessly into his coat pocket and lounged back to his office." The casualness of the gesture — the money vanishing into a pocket as if it were of no consequence — contrasts sharply with the life-changing significance the sum holds for the Wellers. (027-109)
