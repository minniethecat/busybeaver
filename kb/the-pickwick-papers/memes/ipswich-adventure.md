## Overview

The Ipswich Adventure — sometimes called the "double-bedding mix-up" — is one of the most frequently anthologised comic episodes in *The [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] Papers*. While staying at the Great White Horse Inn in [[Ipswich]], [[Mr. Pickwick]] loses his way in the dark corridors and inadvertently enters a lady's bedroom, where he is discovered in a state of undress. The resulting scandal lands him before Mayor [[George Nupkins]], who must sort out the confusion. (006-163)

> "Him as drives a Ipswich coach, and uses our parlour," rejoined Sam. (005-014)

## The Event

[[Mr. Pickwick]] and [[Sam Weller]] travel to [[Ipswich]] to investigate the doings of [[Alfred Jingle]], who has been reported in the area. At the Great White Horse Inn, [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] retires for the night but becomes hopelessly lost in the inn's labyrinthine corridors. Opening what he believes to be his own door, he finds himself in the bedroom of a middle-aged lady in yellow curl-papers, who screams in alarm.

The sequence unfolds:

| Moment | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| Lost in Corridors | [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] navigates the dark hallways of the inn |
| Wrong Room | Entering the lady's chamber |
| Discovery | The lady screams; [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] freezes |
| Before the Mayor | [[mr-nupkins|George Nupkins]], Esq., magistrate and justice |
| Resolution | [[sam-weller|Sam Weller]] talks the party out of trouble |

The episode is a triumph of comic timing, with [[Sam Weller]] providing the verbal ingenuity that extricates his master from the embarrassment: "Forcibly carried before [[mr-nupkins|George Nupkins]], Esq., magistrate and justice of the peace." (006-163)

## Significance

The Ipswich Adventure epitomizes the farcical strain in Dickens's comedy. The episode's success lies in the contrast between [[Mr. Pickwick|Pickwick]]'s absolute innocence and the damning circumstantial evidence: a gentleman in a lady's bedroom at midnight can only be one thing, whatever the truth. The scene also showcases the lodging-house and coaching-inn as Dickensian stages for chaos — a theme that recurs at [[Bob Sawyer]]'s [[lant-street|Lant Street]] party. (004-002)
