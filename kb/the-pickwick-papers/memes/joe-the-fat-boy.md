## Overview

Joe, universally referred to as "the fat boy," is [[Mr. Wardle]]'s serving lad at [[manor-farm|Manor Farm]], [[dingley-dell|Dingley Dell]] — a character of such extraordinary corpulence and somnolence that Dickens's narrator introduces him with the awed observation: "He had never seen such a fat boy, in or out of a travelling caravan" (026-003). Joe exists in a perpetual state hovering between sleep and waking, consuming vast quantities of food and dozing off at every opportunity. His most consequential moment in the narrative — and the moment that transforms him from pure comic relief into a plot mechanism — occurs when he accidentally reveals [[Emily Wardle]]'s secret attachment to [[Augustus Snodgrass]] during the dinner at the Adelphi (026-199), precipitating the crisis that [[Mr. Wardle]] resolves with surprising speed and good humour. Joe is, in miniature, one of Dickens's most distinctive comic achievements: a character whose physical absurdity coexists with a genuine, if simple, emotional life.

## Role in the Novel

Joe's primary function is comic: he is a grotesque in the tradition of Shakespeare's fat knights and Hogarth's engorged figures, his body a walking exaggeration that provokes laughter by its mere description. His constant sleeping and eating constitute a kind of pure physical comedy that requires no plot justification — he is funny because he exists. But Dickens elevates Joe beyond mere caricature by giving him a secondary function as unwitting plot catalyst. Joe's revelation of the [[Emily Wardle]]–[[Augustus Snodgrass|Snodgrass]] attachment (026-199) — blurted out in terror when [[Mr. Wardle]] accuses him of being drunk — converts him from passive observer to active agent in the novel's romantic resolution. His subsequent bribery by [[Arabella Allen]] (026-094–097) and [[Mary]] (026-135) adds a note of mercenary cunning to his simplicity: Joe may be slow, but he understands the value of a half-crown.

## Relationships

- **[[Mr. Wardle]]** — Joe's master. [[mr-wardle|Wardle]]'s treatment of Joe oscillates between exasperation and affection: he shouts at Joe, shakes him awake, and accuses him of drunkenness, but the fat boy remains an indispensable part of the [[manor-farm|Manor Farm]] household.
- **[[Emily Wardle]]** — Joe's young mistress. [[emily-wardle|Emily]] calls Joe "a very good boy" (026-095) when she needs his discretion, and "you stupid creature" (026-089) when she fears his indiscretion. Joe's revelation of her secret is entirely accidental.
- **[[Arabella Allen]]** — Arabella bribes Joe with smiles and shillings (026-094–097), recognising that his simplicity makes him both vulnerable to manipulation and oddly trustworthy.
- **[[Mary]]** — The pretty housemaid with whom Joe shares the memorable meat-pie scene (026-103–141). Joe's clumsy attempt at romantic gallantry — "You're her. I know you are" (026-123) — is one of the novel's oddest and most endearing moments.
- **[[Sam Weller]]** — [[sam-weller|Sam]] interacts with Joe during the [[christmas|Christmas]] scenes at [[manor-farm|Manor Farm]] and later encounters him delivering [[emily-wardle|Emily]]'s note (028-177). The contrast between [[sam-weller|Sam]]'s Cockney wit and Joe's bovine simplicity is a study in Dickens's range as a comic writer.
