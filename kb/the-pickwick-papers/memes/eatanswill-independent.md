# The Eatanswill Independent

The **Eatanswill Independent** is a provincial newspaper serving the borough of [[eatanswill|Eatanswill]], edited by [[Mr. Slurk]]. It is the principal Blue (Tory) organ in the town and the sworn rival of the Whig-leaning [[Eatanswill Gazette]]. Where the Gazette supports the Honourable [[sam-weller|Sam]]uel [[mr-slumkey|Slumkey]], the Independent champions the opposing Blue candidate, and the two papers maintain a state of perpetual editorial warfare.

## Editorial Character

The Independent is described by its rival, the Gazette, as a "scurrilous print" — a characterization it returns with equal vigour. The Independent's editorial style mirrors that of the Gazette: both papers traffic in personal invective and partisan denunciation, differing only in the colour of the party they serve. This symmetry is Dickens's satirical point: the two organs are functionally identical, distinguished solely by their political allegiance (023-101).

## The Eatanswill Election

During the [[eatanswill|Eatanswill]] election, the Independent serves as the Blue party's mouthpiece. While the Gazette sings the praises of the Buff candidate, the Independent does the same for the Blues, and each paper devotes its columns to attacking the other's candidate with equal ferocity.

## Mr. Slurk

[[Mr. Slurk]] is the editor of the Independent. A man of combative disposition, Slurk shares his rival [[mr-pott|Pott]]'s conviction that his newspaper is a bastion of truth and that the opposing paper is a sink of iniquity. His professional rivalry with [[Mr. Pott]] of the Gazette culminates in a physical confrontation at the Peacock Inn, where the two editors, having insulted each other in print for years, finally come to blows with improvised weapons. The scene is one of the novel's most memorable comic set-pieces.

## The Rivalry

The Gazette–Independent rivalry serves Dickens's broader satire of provincial journalism and electoral politics. The two papers' mutual hatred is so intense, and their arguments so perfectly symmetrical, that they expose the emptiness of partisan discourse:

- The Gazette calls the Independent "scurrilous"; the Independent returns the compliment.
- Both papers claim to represent "the public feeling of [[eatanswill|Eatanswill]]" while in fact manufacturing it.
- Neither paper is distinguishable from the other except by the names of its editor and its party.
