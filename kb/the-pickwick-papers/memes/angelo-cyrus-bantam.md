## Overview

Angelo Cyrus Bantam, Esquire, M.C., is the Master of Ceremonies at [[Bath]], a position of considerable social consequence in Regency England. Introduced by [[Mr. Dowler]] as his friend, (007-068) Bantam receives [[Mr. Pickwick]] with an elaborate courtesy that borders on the absurd: his expressions of condescension are so overwhelming that [[mr-pickwick|Mr. Pickwick]], normally untroubled by social anxiety, is "completely overpowered." (007-081) Dickens uses Bantam to satirize the rigid, performative etiquette of spa-town society, where a man's worth is measured by the elegance of his bow and the floridity of his compliments.

## Role in the Novel

Bantam's narrative function is to facilitate [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]]'s entry into [[bath|Bath]] society during the chapters set in that city. He guides [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] through the ball at the Assembly Rooms, introducing him to the [[lady-snuphanuph|Dowager Lady Snuphanuph]] and other worthies. His elaborate, "cork-screwing" manner of movement through a crowd (007-115) is one of Dickens's most vivid pieces of physical characterization, compressing an entire social type into a single verb. Beyond his local role, Bantam reappears by correspondence in Chapter 9, arranging a dinner invitation through [[Mr. Smauker]], (009-010) extending his influence into the footmen's social sphere.

## Key Scenes

**The Introduction (Chapter 7)**
Bantam is first introduced by [[mr-dowler|Mr. Dowler]]: "my friend, Angelo Cyrus Bantam, Esquire, M.C." (007-068) He receives [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] with "expressions of condescension at which Angelo Bantam was even more overpowered than [[mr-pickwick|Mr. Pickwick]] himself," (007-081) suggesting a man so practiced in self-importance that even he is moved by his own graciousness. He then ushers [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] into his study, where [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] waits patiently while Bantam arranges his dress. (007-101)

**The Ball at the Assembly Rooms (Chapter 7)**
That evening, Bantam appears at the Assembly Rooms "in a blue coat, with bright buttons, and a white waistcoat, and an immense display of shirt-frill and jewellery." (007-114) Upon spotting [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]], he "cork-screwed his way through the crowd" to greet him. (007-115) He introduces [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] to the [[lady-snuphanuph|Dowager Lady Snuphanuph]] and various other figures of local importance, and later seats [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] at the supper table "between Angelo Bantam and the two Miss Matinters." (007-149) Throughout the evening, he is addressed familiarly by his peers — "Veway hot, Bantam" (007-127) and "Bantam" by the Honourable [[mr-crushton|Mr. Crushton]]. (007-130)

**The Dinner Invitation (Chapter 9)**
Though absent from the scene, Bantam's influence persists. A letter is received "begging to inform [[mr-pickwick|Mr. Pickwick]] that their mutual acquaintance, Mr. Bantam, a few days since, begs to invite [[mr-pickwick|Mr. Pickwick]] to dine." (009-010) Later, through the footmen's grapevine, [[Sam Weller]] learns that Mr. Smauker has "promised Bantam" to arrange the gathering. (009-124) Bantam also contributes a piece of comic local lore, recounting the curious case of a man on Clapham Green "who lost the use of his limbs from a fit of the gout." (007-076)
