## Overview

Benjamin Allen — invariably called Ben Allen — is a medical student who appears first at [[dingley-dell|Dingley Dell]] and later in his lodgings with [[bob-sawyer|Bob Sawyer]]. He is characterised by his deep, brandy-soaked melancholy over his sister Arabella's romantic affairs, a grief he expresses by waving a poker, quoting morbid sentiments, and drinking himself into a stupor. (010-050) His function is primarily comic, but through him Dickens also advances the courtship plot between [[nathaniel-winkle|Mr. Winkle]] and [[arabella-allen|Arabella Allen]].

## Role in the Novel

Ben Allen serves a dual function: as a source of farcical humour in the medical-student scenes and as a reluctant intermediary in the romance between [[nathaniel-winkle|Mr. Winkle]] and his sister [[arabella-allen|Arabella Allen]]. His exaggerated grief over Arabella's attachment to [[nathaniel-winkle|Winkle]] — expressed by seizing a poker and brandishing it with "bacchanalian fury" (010-062–063) — provides a comic counterpoint to the novel's more serious treatments of love and marriage. His eventual reconciliation to the match completes his modest character arc.

## Relationships

- **[[Bob Sawyer]]** — Ben Allen's inseparable companion and fellow medical student; the two share lodgings, brandy, and a robust indifference to the finer points of their profession. Their partnership provides some of the novel's most boisterous comedy.
- **[[Arabella Allen]]** — Ben's sister, whose romantic involvement with [[nathaniel-winkle|Mr. Winkle]] is the cause of Ben's theatrical despair. Despite his protests, he ultimately blesses the union.
- **[[Nathaniel Winkle]]** — The object of Ben's sister's affections and therefore the target of Ben's brandy-fuelled wrath — until he becomes a brother-in-law.
- **[[Mr. Pickwick]]** — [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] treats Ben Allen with his characteristic benevolence, intervening to help resolve the romantic complications with Arabella.
