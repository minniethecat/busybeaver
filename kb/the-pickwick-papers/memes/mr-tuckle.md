## Overview

Mr. Tuckle is a minor character in _The [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] Papers_ who appears exclusively in Chapter 9 as the chairman of a servants' dinner at [[bath|Bath]]. Distinguished by his crimson livery and a self-important manner, Tuckle presides over the gathering with an air of exaggerated authority, carving the leg of mutton, enforcing the "rules" of the society, and engaging in verbal sparring with an underling named Harris. His pomposity is gently satirized by Dickens as a comic mirror of more elevated social rituals.

## Role in the Novel

Tuckle's role is confined to a single extended scene — the [[bath|Bath]] servants' swarry (soirée) to which [[Mr. John Smauker]] brings [[Sam Weller]] as a guest. As chairman of the evening, Tuckle represents the comic pretensions of servant-class society mimicking its betters. His interactions with [[sam-weller|Sam Weller]] provide an opportunity for Dickens to showcase [[sam-weller|Sam]]'s wit in contrast to Tuckle's self-importance. The scene, though brief, contributes to the novel's broader satire of social hierarchy and ritual.

## Key Scenes

**The [[bath|Bath]] Servants' Dinner (Chapter 9)**

Mr. Tuckle first appears when [[Mr. John Smauker]] greets him at the servants' society gathering, where it is noted that "Tuckle was in spirits to-night" (009-058). Smauker attempts an introduction, addressing Tuckle familiarly (009-059), and Tuckle greets [[Sam Weller]] with a "familiar nod" and a remark about the fire "off" Weller. (009-061) A near-confrontation occurs when [[sam-weller|Sam]]'s remark about keeping oneself "pretty warm in the hold" is interpreted as a "personal allusion to Mr. Tuckle's crimson livery." (009-063)

Once the company is seated, Mr. Tuckle assumes the chair, supported by a gentleman in orange plush at the vice. (009-066) He exercises his authority immediately:

> "Harris," said Mr. Tuckle in a commanding tone. (009-067)

When Harris reluctantly hands over the carving knife, Tuckle takes offense at Harris's manner:

> "What do you mean by that, sir?" said Mr. Tuckle, with great asperity. (009-074)

Tuckle's rebuke of Harris reveals his inflated sense of importance:

> "The very best opinion of you is, Harris," said Mr. Tuckle with a most impressive air, "that you are a well-meaning creature, with no harm in you." (009-076)

He proceeds to carve the leg of mutton "to show his superiority" (009-082) and enforces a society rule when [[sam-weller|Sam]] arrives late to the toast:

> "Against the rules," said Mr. Tuckle. "Too late, too late." (009-084)

The evening concludes with Tuckle, despite his earlier sternness, not being "proof against" a humorous song about "King Richard the Third." (009-128) He walks home the same way as [[sam-weller|Sam Weller]] and is prevailed upon to stop at Mr. Smauker's door for a final oyster supper. (009-129)
