## Overview

Mrs. Sanders is a minor character in _The [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] Papers_, appearing as one of [[Mrs. Bardell]]'s devoted friends and a supporting witness at the celebrated trial of _Bardell v. [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]]_. She is a woman of pronounced sentimentality — prone to tears, moaning, and falling dramatically asleep in moments of emotional exhaustion — whose testimony at the trial reveals a lively romantic history involving love-letters exchanged during her courtship with a certain [[mr-sanders|Mr. Sanders]]. 

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Sanders serves as a secondary witness for the plaintiff in _Bardell v. [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]]_. Her function is both comic and structural: her tearful, highly emotional presence in the courtroom underscores the theatricality of [[Serjeant Buzfuz]]'s prosecution, while her later appearance at [[mrs-bardell|Mrs. Bardell]]'s home in Chapter 18 ties the trial storyline to the domestic sphere of the novel's female characters. Her relationship to [[Mrs. Cluppins]] and her connection to the boarding-house world of [[Mrs. Raddle]] embed her within the novel's network of gossiping, mutually supportive women.

## Key Scenes

**The Trial — Bardell v. [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] (Chapter 6)**

Mrs. Sanders first appears leading [[Mrs. Bardell]] into the courtroom, her face arranged in a "melancholy" expression for the occasion. (006-039) She sits among the crowd, her features described alongside those of [[Mrs. Cluppins]] (006-065), and assists [[Mrs. Bardell]] physically during the proceedings. (006-075) After both women are "politely taken out of court" (006-096), she is later called as a witness under her full name, Susannah Sanders. (006-166) Her testimony reveals that, during her courtship with [[mr-sanders|Mr. Sanders]], she had received "love-letters, like any other gentlewoman," a detail that adds a comic, humanizing note to the otherwise formulaic courtroom examination. (006-167)

**The Visit to [[mrs-bardell|Mrs. Bardell]] (Chapter 18)**

Mrs. Sanders visits [[Mrs. Bardell]]'s home and participates in the collective female lamentation over [[mrs-bardell|Mrs. Bardell]]'s troubles. (018-020, 018-036) When the ladies discuss [[mrs-cluppins|Mrs. Cluppins]]'s sister, Mrs. Sanders makes the suggestion (018-046), demonstrating her familiarity with the network of Goswell Street connections. She reacts melodramatically to [[Mr. Jackson]]'s arrival:

> "More company!" said Mrs. Sanders. (018-069)

During the subsequent legal discussion with [[mr-jackson|Mr. Jackson]], both Mrs. Sanders and [[Mrs. Cluppins]] fall asleep from sheer emotional exhaustion. (018-094) She must later be shaken awake to depart in the coach. (018-103) Her exit is characteristically abrupt — she "made off, without more ado" (018-111), bringing her appearance to a comic close.
