## Overview

London is the gravitational centre of the [[gentleman|Pickwickian]] world, from which the [[pickwick-club|Pickwickians]] depart on their coaching adventures and to which they repeatedly return to face legal peril and romantic complications.

In the 1830s, the London of *The [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] Papers* is a city of sharp contrasts: the respectable squares of legal London, the squalid [[Fleet Prison]], the bustling coaching inns of the [[Coaching Era]], and the pretentious watering-places of the shabby-genteel.

## Location

The legal quarter around Freeman's Court, Cornhill, houses the offices of [[Dodson and Fogg]]. (003-005) The [[fleet-prison|Fleet Prison]] on the eastern edge of the City holds debtors in conditions that Dickens describes with reformist outrage. (012-032)

The [[white-hart-hotel|White Hart Inn]] and the [[George and Vulture]] serve as coaching inns where travellers gather and depart. Goswell Street in Clerkenwell, where [[mrs-bardell|Mrs. Bardell]] lets lodgings to [[Mr. Pickwick]], represents the lower-middle-class London of lodging-houses. (003-006)

## Historical Context

Dickens's London of the mid-1830s was a city in transition. The coaching era was at its peak — the railways had not yet transformed travel — and the coaching inns of the City were still thriving hubs.

The [[fleet-prison|Fleet Prison]], a debtors' prison operating since the 12th century, would be closed in 1842, just a few years after publication. Dickens's depiction of its conditions contributed to public pressure for its abolition.

> "The last evening paper from London was read and re-read with an eagerness which only a provincial journal can inspire." (023-005)
