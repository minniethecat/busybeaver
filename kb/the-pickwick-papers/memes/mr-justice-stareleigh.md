## Overview

Mr. Justice Stareleigh is the presiding judge at the trial of _Bardell v. [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]]_, one of the most celebrated comic set-pieces in English literature. Dickens draws him as a judicial grotesque: a short man of irritable temper who falls asleep during the proceedings, awakens to scribble notes with theatrical solemnity, and delivers a summing-up that is as mechanically prejudicial as it is, in the author's phrase, "old-established." (006-225) Through Stareleigh, Dickens mocks the somnolence and irascibility of the unreformed judiciary, where a judge's personal comfort and class sympathies shape the administration of justice.

## Role in the Novel

Stareleigh presides over the breach-of-promise trial brought by [[Mrs. Bardell]] against [[Mr. Pickwick]]. He sits in the absence of the Chief Justice, (006-019) and his conduct of the case is marked by impatience, drowsiness, and a barely concealed bias toward the plaintiff. His exchanges with [[Serjeant Buzfuz]] are deferential, while his treatment of [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] — who appears without counsel — is dismissive to the point of hostility. His summing-up instructs the jury that if they believe [[Mrs. Cluppins]]'s testimony, they must find for the plaintiff, effectively directing the verdict that lands [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] in the [[Fleet Prison]].

## Key Scenes

**Taking His Seat (Chapter 6)**
Justice Stareleigh enters the courtroom and takes his place. Dickens describes him as "a short man, and so short as to be almost invisible." (006-019) The physical description — diminutive stature and reddening complexion — immediately establishes him as a figure of petty, combustible authority.

**The Exchange with [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]]**
When [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] attempts to address the court, Stareleigh cuts him off:

> "On what grounds, sir?" said Mr. Justice Stareleigh. (006-027)

When [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] explains he cannot afford counsel, the judge's reply is blunt and unsympathetic:

> "Can't help that, sir," replied Mr. Justice Stareleigh. "You should hire one." (006-029, 006-031)

Dickens adds that the judge reddened as he spoke, "for Mr. Justice Stareleigh's temper bordered on the irritable." (006-031)

**The Sleeping Judge**
During [[serjeant-buzfuz|Serjeant Buzfuz]]'s lengthy address for the plaintiff, the courtroom falls silent — and "the silence awoke Mr. Justice Stareleigh, who immediately wrote down something with great solemnity." (006-063) The irony is sharp: the judge, having missed an unknown portion of the argument, compensates with a theatrical display of attentiveness. When [[serjeant-buzfuz|Buzfuz]] finally sits down, "Mr. Justice Stareleigh woke up." (006-072) The repetition of the sleep-wake cycle turns the judge into a pure comic mechanism.

**The Summing-Up (Chapter 6)**
At the trial's conclusion, Justice Stareleigh sums up "in the old-established form," (006-225) delivering directions to the jury that are formulaic but weighted decisively in the plaintiff's favour. His instructions — that belief in [[mrs-cluppins|Mrs. Cluppins]]'s testimony compels a verdict for [[mrs-bardell|Mrs. Bardell]] — seal [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]]'s fate and set in motion the novel's prison chapters.
