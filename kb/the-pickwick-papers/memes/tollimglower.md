## Overview

Lady Tollimglower is mentioned only in death, as the mother of a bride whose wedding furnishes one of the bagman's stories. (029-016) She belongs to that category of Dickensian characters who exist entirely as a point of social reference: the name "Tollimglower" — absurd, vaguely Gothic, faintly menacing — signals that this is an aristocratic family of the kind that populates the interpolated tales, where titled ladies make disastrous marriages and melancholy hangs over great houses. The one-eyed bagman, in telling the story of the wedding, is described as being "very eloquent on the subject of Lady Tollimglower" (029-024), suggesting that her name carries a narrative weight disproportionate to her actual presence in the novel.

## Role in the Novel

Lady Tollimglower appears as a genealogical footnote in a tale told at a coaching inn. The bagman recounts the marriage of her eldest daughter "which occupied three pages of the magazine" (029-016), a detail that positions the story as a parody of the sentimental magazine fiction of Dickens's day. The deceased Lady Tollimglower functions as the absent matriarch whose death leaves her daughter vulnerable to an unhappy match — a stock situation in the Gothic and sentimental traditions that the bagman's tale both invokes and undermines.
