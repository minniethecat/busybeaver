## Overview

The bagman's stories constitute one of the principal inset narratives in *The Pickwick Papers*. Introduced in Chapter XX, the one-eyed bagman — a commercial traveler — regales the company at the inn with the tale of his uncle's extraordinary adventure among the ghosts of mail-coaches. The story exemplifies Dickens's technique of interpolated narrative: a tale within a tale, framed by the convivial setting of the inn and the bagman's self-conscious, ironic narration.

## Definition

The bagman's stories are oral narratives told by an unnamed commercial traveler — "a bagman" — within the novel's frame. The primary tale, "The Story of the Bagman's Uncle," occupies the entirety of Chapter XXI. It blends the gothic supernatural with the comic skepticism of its narrator, who repeatedly undermines his own tale's credibility while insisting on its truth. The bagman himself is introduced as a storyteller of established reputation: he is identified as "the intimate friend and companion of Tom Smart, of the great house of Bilson and Slum" (021-003), invoking a name that the Pickwickians would recognize from their earlier travels.

## In Context

The bagman first appears ladling out negus and smoking a Dutch pipe (020-143), establishing the convivial, fireside setting that frames the tale. His storytelling authority derives from both his professional experience — a commercial traveler's life on the road — and his personal connection to the tale's protagonist. He prefaces the story by declaring that "my uncle was one of the merriest, pleasantest, cleverest fellows that ever lived" (021-002), immediately establishing the tall-tale tone — extravagant praise that the narrator himself qualifies with comic asides.

The frame setting is carefully constructed. The company has dined, the fire is bright, and drink circulates freely — the traditional conditions for oral storytelling. The one-eyed bagman presides over this ritual, "ladling out a glass of negus" (020-143) as he launches into his tale. Dickens understood that the supernatural tale requires a specific social context — warmth, conviviality, and mild intoxication — to suspend the listener's disbelief.

The tale proper follows the uncle, a commercial traveler like the bagman himself, through a night of heavy drinking in Edinburgh. After a lavish supper featuring "kippered salmon, and Finnan haddocks, and a lamb's head, and a haggis" (021-031), the uncle wanders through the old town and stumbles into a wheelwright's yard filled with derelict mail-coaches.

At the stroke of two, the abandoned coaches spring to life: doors rematerialize, lamps ignite, porters and guards appear, and passengers in eighteenth-century dress — "powdered wigs," "sky-blue coats trimmed with silver," "knee breeches and silk stockings" (021-030–021-031) — board the spectral vehicles. The uncle finds himself booked as an inside passenger on the Edinburgh-to-London mail.

## Examples from the Novel

### Narrative Frame and Comic Undermining

The bagman's narration is marked by self-undermining commentary. When describing his uncle's philosophical reflections on the decaying coaches — "he thought of the numbers of people to whom one of those crazy, mouldering vehicles had borne, night after night, for many years... the anxiously expected intelligence, the eagerly looked-for remittance" (021-047) — the bagman immediately punctures the sentiment: "I rather suspect he learnt it out of some book afterwards" and "I am, therefore, decidedly of opinion, gentlemen, that my uncle fell into a kind of doze, without having thought about anything at all" (021-048).

This narrative technique — building up a sentimental or gothic passage only to deflate it with comic skepticism — is characteristically Dickensian. The bagman claims authority as the tale's teller while simultaneously admitting he may be embellishing or inventing. The effect is to give the reader permission to enjoy the supernatural story without having to believe it.

### The Spectral Mail-Coach Journey

The adventure climaxes with a sword fight in a ruined house, the rescue of a beautiful young lady from forced marriage, and a wild coach chase. The uncle defeats two villains, receives the lady's promise of eternal fidelity, and drives the spectral mail-coach at breakneck speed — only to wake at dawn, shivering on the box of a derelict coach in the wheelwright's yard (021-097).

A telling detail: the uncle had sat himself down in "a high-backed chair" (021-060) and taken "an accurate measure and survey" of a spectral lady's features while seated in it. This domestic object amid ghostly chaos — a chair, a lamp, a supper table — grounds the supernatural in the familiar, a technique Dickens would develop throughout his career.

### The Dead Letters Coda

The story's coda is characteristically Dickensian: the landlord asks what ghosts of mail-coaches carry in their bags, and the bagman replies, "The dead letters, of course" (021-103). This deflation of the supernatural into bureaucratic metaphor encapsulates the novel's treatment of the fantastic — always framed, always ironized, never fully endorsed. The "dead letters" joke also glances at the reality of the postal system, a vast bureaucratic apparatus that Dickens knew intimately from his own childhood experience and would later make the centerpiece of his legal satire.
