# United Grand Junction Ebenezer Temperance Association

The **United Grand Junction Ebenezer Temperance Association** (Brick Lane Branch) is a temperance society that holds monthly meetings in a large room in Brick Lane, [[london|London]]. The association is most notable as the platform for [[Mr. Stiggins]], the red-nosed deputy shepherd whose enthusiastic denunciations of alcohol are spectacularly belied by his own consumption of it.

## Meetings and Character

The monthly meetings of the Brick Lane Branch are held in a room "pleasantly and airily situated at the top of a flight of stairs" (005-116). The gatherings follow a formula: the members assemble, sing hymns, and listen to addresses from the association's officers. The proceedings are characterized by an earnestness that Dickens treats with characteristic irony, particularly given the hypocrisy of the association's most vocal advocate.

The association's name itself is a target of Dickens's satire. The concatenation of grand titles — "United Grand Junction Ebenezer" — mocks the pretensions of the temperance movement, whose rhetoric far outstrips its actual influence.

## Mr. Stiggins and the Association

[[Mr. Stiggins]] is the association's most conspicuous member and frequent speaker. His addresses at the monthly meetings are delivered with "tearful earnestness," and he regularly denounces the evils of drink while, as [[sam-weller|Sam Weller]] observes, consuming large quantities of the association's pineapple rum. The association thus serves as the setting for one of the novel's sharpest satirical portraits: a temperance advocate whose devotion to the cause is entirely theatrical.

The association's meetings are attended by Mr. Weller Sr., who has been dragooned into membership by his wife (a devoted follower of [[mr-stiggins|Stiggins]]) and who provides a running commentary on the proceedings from the back of the room.

## Brick Lane

The association's location in Brick Lane places it in the East End of [[london|London]], a district associated with the city's working poor. The setting reinforces the novel's interest in the social institutions of early 19th-century [[london|London]], from the law courts to the temperance halls.
