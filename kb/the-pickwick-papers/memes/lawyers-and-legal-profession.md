## Overview

The legal profession is one of Dickens's most sustained satirical targets in *The Pickwick Papers*. Through the portrayal of attorneys like the unscrupulous firm of Dodson and Fogg and the canny but decent Mr. Perker, Dickens exposes a profession that profits from human misery, exploits legal technicalities, and operates a system where justice is secondary to fees.

## Definition

In the novel, "the legal profession" encompasses solicitors (like Dodson and Fogg, who initiate the Bardell v. Pickwick case), barristers (like Sergeant Buzfuz, who argues in court), and legal clerks. Attorneys in the 1830s were distinct from barristers: solicitors dealt directly with clients and prepared cases, while barristers argued in court.

## In Context

Dickens himself worked as a solicitor's clerk and a court reporter before becoming a novelist, giving him intimate knowledge of the legal world. His critique of lawyers in *Pickwick Papers* reflects widespread public resentment of the legal profession, which was seen as parasitic and self-serving. The Court of Common Pleas scenes draw directly on Dickens's reporting experience.

## Examples from the Novel

Dodson and Fogg, the firm that brings the breach-of-promise suit against Pickwick, are portrayed as professional extortionists who deliberately target vulnerable defendants and inflate costs. When Pickwick visits their office, he is met with studied indifference. Mr. Perker, Pickwick's own solicitor, is more sympathetically drawn — a small, bustling man who knows every trick of the trade but uses them in his client's defense rather than for predatory profit. The trial of *Bardell v. Pickwick* (Chapter VI, 006-001) is the novel's centerpiece of legal satire, with Sergeant Buzfuz's outrageous rhetorical flourishes and the jury's sympathy for the weeping Mrs. Bardell standing in for any real examination of evidence.
