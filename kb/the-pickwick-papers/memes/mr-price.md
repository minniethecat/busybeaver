## Overview

Mr. Price is one of the first inhabitants of [[debtors-prison|the Fleet]] Prison whom [[Mr. Pickwick]] encounters upon his voluntary incarceration. A debtor living in the poor side of the prison, Price is characterised by a disconcerting blend of destitution and bravado — he cracks jokes, drinks brandy-and-water, and writes facetious letters to his father, all while acknowledging that he has not "half the ghost of" a chance of release. His brief appearance serves as Dickens's vehicle for establishing the bizarre, morally inverted atmosphere of [[debtors-prison|the Fleet]], where poverty and imprisonment are met not with despair but with a kind of defiant, hollow merriment.

## Role in the Novel

Mr. Price appears solely in Chapter 12 ([[debtors-prison|the Fleet]] Prison sequence) and functions as a piece of atmospheric characterisation rather than a participant in the plot. His role is to illustrate, through dialogue and demeanour, the psychological coping mechanisms that the prison fosters: a gallows humour that masks utter hopelessness. His exchange with the "young boy" (a fellow inmate he treats as an admiring audience) and his dismissive assessment of the "chancery prisoner" who has not shaved in a week — because the man is "so certain he's going out in half an hour's time" — map the spectrum of delusion and resignation that defines [[debtors-prison|the Fleet]]'s debtor population. (012-037)

## Key Scenes

**First Encounter in the Poor Side** (012-037–012-046): [[Mr. Pickwick]], newly arrived in [[debtors-prison|the Fleet]], is shown into a cramped ward where Mr. Price is holding court. Price's companion — a young boy whom Price treats as a protégé — laughs at all his jokes, and Price obligingly performs the role of the worldly-wise veteran of the prison system. The narrative presents Price as a man who has made a kind of career out of imprisonment: he writes to his father for money with the self-conscious skill of a professional correspondent, requiring "a stimulant" (brandy-and-water) to "pitch it strong enough into the old boy." (012-041)

**The Chancery Prisoner Anecdote** (012-037–012-040): Price directs [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]]'s attention to a fellow prisoner — a man who has been in [[debtors-prison|the Fleet]] for a week yet has never once shaved, because he is "so certain he's going out in half an hour's time, that he thinks he may as well put it off till he gets home." When [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] asks, with characteristic sympathy, whether the man's chances of release are really so great, Price's response is brutally frank: "Chances be d—d; he hasn't half the ghost of one. I wouldn't give _that_ for his chance of walking about the streets this time ten years." The gesture — snapping his fingers contemptuously — encapsulates [[debtors-prison|the Fleet]]'s harsh epistemology: everyone claims they are about to leave; almost nobody does. (012-038–012-040)

**Departure of the "Genteel Strangers"** (012-046): The scene ends when two or three strangers "of genteel appearance" enter the ward. The boy immediately throws his cigar into the fire and whispers to Price that "they had come to make it all right for him" — a phrase that suggests, without elaborating, the semi-legal, semi-corrupt arrangements by which prisoners negotiated better accommodation. Price and the boy join the newcomers at a far table, and [[mr-pickwick|Mr. Pickwick]], "feeling not a little disgusted with this dialogue, as well as with the air and manner of the two beings by whom it had been carried on," is left to contemplate the moral squalor of his surroundings.

## Relationships

- **[[Mr. Pickwick]]** — The newcomer to [[debtors-prison|the Fleet]] whom Price treats with a mixture of condescension and showmanship
- **The "young boy"** — Price's admiring companion and audience, a fellow inmate who "had looked at [life] through the dirty panes of glass in a bar door" (012-045)
- **Crookey** — The attendant to whom Price gives orders for paper and brandy (012-041)
- **[[Tom Roker]]** — The turnkey who assigns [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] to the ward; Price is one of [[tom-roker|Roker]]'s charges
