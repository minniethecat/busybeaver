# Mr. Dodson

## Overview

Mr. Dodson is the senior partner of the law firm [[Dodson and Fogg]] of Freeman's Court, Cornhill. Together with his partner [[Mr. Fogg]], he serves as the solicitor for [[Mrs. Bardell]] in her breach-of-promise suit against [[Mr. Pickwick]]. (003-005) The firm of [[dodson-and-fogg|Dodson and Fogg]] is portrayed throughout the novel as shrewd, unscrupulous, and motivated by pecuniary gain rather than any genuine commitment to justice.

Mr. Dodson appears as the more senior and deliberate partner, often playing the straight man to [[Mr. Fogg]]'s more openly cynical remarks. Together they represent the moral bankruptcy of the legal profession in Dickens's satirical vision.

## Role in the Novel

Mr. Dodson, along with his partner [[Mr. Fogg]], functions as an antagonist in the Bardell v. [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] subplot. The firm initiates the lawsuit against [[Mr. Pickwick]] on behalf of [[Mrs. Bardell]], and their conduct throughout the trial demonstrates a callous disregard for both their client and the defendant. (006-214)

He appears at key moments in the trial — escorting witnesses into the box, conferring with [[Serjeant Buzfuz]], and rubbing his hands in satisfaction at the proceedings. After the trial, he and [[Mr. Fogg]] pursue [[Mr. Pickwick]] for costs, leading to the latter's imprisonment in the [[Fleet Prison]]. (017-119) In a significant late scene, [[Mr. Pickwick]] confronts [[dodson-and-fogg|Dodson and Fogg]] directly, denouncing their practices. (025-112)

## Key Scenes

- **The Trial Preparation** — Mr. Dodson escorts witnesses, including [[Mrs. Cluppins]], into court, having prepared an umbrella and pattens for the occasion. (006-039)
- **During the Trial** — Dodson watches the proceedings with satisfaction, nodding admiration of [[Serjeant Buzfuz]]'s oratory. (006-057)
- **Mr. Weller's Testimony** — [[Sam Weller]] praises the "honourable conduct" of [[dodson-and-fogg|Dodson and Fogg]] with heavy irony, causing them to turn red. (006-214)
- **After the Verdict** — [[dodson-and-fogg|Dodson and Fogg]] encounter [[Mr. Pickwick]] outside court, "rubbing their hands with every demonstration of lively satisfaction." (006-234)
- **The Confrontation** — [[Mr. Pickwick]] visits [[dodson-and-fogg|Dodson and Fogg]]'s office; Mr. Dodson addresses him with "remarkably cool and sarcastic exordia" about the costs. (025-122)

## Relationships

- **[[Mr. Fogg]]** — His junior partner in the firm of [[Dodson and Fogg]]. They operate as a pair, with Fogg being more openly cynical while Dodson maintains a veneer of professional propriety.
- **[[Mrs. Bardell]]** — Their client in ''Bardell v. [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]]'', whom they exploit for their own financial gain.
- **[[Mr. Pickwick]]** — The defendant in the suit; Mr. Dodson treats him with cool, professional contempt.
- **[[Serjeant Buzfuz]]** — Counsel for the plaintiff; [[dodson-and-fogg|Dodson and Fogg]] confer with him during the trial. (006-205)
- **[[Sam Weller]]** — [[Mr. Pickwick]]'s servant, who delivers the most devastating satire of [[dodson-and-fogg|Dodson and Fogg]]'s conduct in his trial testimony. (006-214)

## Character Analysis

Mr. Dodson represents the respectable face of legal sharp practice. While [[Mr. Fogg]] openly admits that the firm's business model relies on costs regardless of outcome, Dodson maintains the language of professional rectitude — addressing [[Mr. Pickwick]] with elaborate sarcasm rather than outright hostility. (025-112) This duality makes him, in some ways, more insidious than his partner.

Dickens's portrayal of [[dodson-and-fogg|Dodson and Fogg]] forms part of his sustained critique of the Victorian legal system, where attorneys could profit from litigation regardless of its merits. The firm's name — with its abrupt, percussive syllables — reinforces their predatory character.
