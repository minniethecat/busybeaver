## Overview

Mrs. Cluppins is a minor but memorable character in _The [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] Papers_, serving as [[mrs-bardell|Mrs. Bardell]]'s primary witness and moral support during the breach of promise trial. She is consistently described as "little Mrs. Cluppins," a woman of small stature but considerable volubility, whose testimony at the trial provides much of the comic texture of the courtroom scenes. She is later revealed to be the sister of [[Mrs. Raddle]], connecting her to the novel's boarding-house subplot.

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Cluppins functions primarily as a witness for the plaintiff in _Bardell v. [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]]_. Her testimony, drawn out over a lengthy examination by [[Serjeant Buzfuz]], is filled with dramatic agitation and strategic reluctance—she claims she would "scorn" to eavesdrop, yet proceeds to relate in detail a conversation she overheard between [[Mr. Pickwick]] and [[mrs-bardell|Mrs. Bardell]]. This blend of coyness and loquacity makes her one of the novel's most effective comic witnesses.

Beyond the courtroom, Mrs. Cluppins reappears in Chapter 18 as a visitor to [[Mrs. Bardell]]'s house, where she encounters [[Mr. Jackson]] of [[Dodson and Fogg]]. Her persistent attempts to extract information from Tommy Bardell showcase her inquisitive, meddlesome nature.

## Key Scenes

**The Trial — Bardell v. [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] (Chapter 6)**
Mrs. Cluppins enters the courtroom supporting [[mrs-bardell|Mrs. Bardell]]. (006-039) After a careful survey of the court, she provides the prosecution's key testimony. (006-041) Called by [[serjeant-buzfuz|Serjeant Buzfuz]], she is questioned about her relationship with [[mrs-bardell|Mrs. Bardell]]. (006-076–006-077) Her most notable moment comes when she describes overhearing [[Mr. Pickwick]]'s conversation with [[mrs-bardell|Mrs. Bardell]], while maintaining with "majestic" dignity that she "would scorn the haction" of eavesdropping. (006-091)

> "My Lord and Jury," said Mrs. Cluppins, with interesting agitation, "I will not deceive you." (006-082)

Her testimony, once the "ice" is broken, becomes voluble and detailed, providing the prosecution with its strongest evidence. (006-096) The judge later instructs the jury that if they find Mrs. Cluppins's evidence credible, they must find for the plaintiff. (006-225)

**The Visit to [[mrs-bardell|Mrs. Bardell]] (Chapter 18)**
Mrs. Cluppins visits [[mrs-bardell|Mrs. Bardell]] at her home and encounters young Tommy. Her persistent questioning of the boy reveals her gossipy nature. (018-013–018-029) When [[mr-jackson|Mr. Jackson]] arrives from [[dodson-and-fogg|Dodson and Fogg]], Mrs. Cluppins is present for the uncomfortable encounter. She is later introduced to [[mr-raddle|Mr. Raddle]]:
> "Mr. Raddle, ma'am; Mrs. Cluppins, ma'am; Mrs. Raddle, ma'am." (018-045)

It is revealed that Mrs. Cluppins is [[mrs-raddle|Mrs. Raddle]]'s sister, establishing the network of female characters surrounding the Bardell household. (018-046)
