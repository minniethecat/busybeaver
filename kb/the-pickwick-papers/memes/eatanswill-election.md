## Overview

The [[eatanswill|Eatanswill]] election is one of the novel's most sustained pieces of political satire, appearing in Chapter XXIII. [[mr-pickwick|Mr. Pickwick]] visits the provincial town of [[eatanswill|Eatanswill]] as the guest of [[mr-pott|Mr. Pott]], editor of the *[[eatanswill-gazette|Eatanswill Gazette]]*, during a parliamentary by-election. What he witnesses is a carnival of corruption: open bribery, organised mob violence, and two rival newspapers — the *Gazette* and the *Independent* — whose mutual vituperation has "blinded the public feeling of [[eatanswill|Eatanswill]] by animosity." (023-168) The episode draws on Dickens's own experience as a parliamentary reporter in the 1830s, giving it the bite of authentic observation.

## Background

The election takes place in [[eatanswill|Eatanswill]], a fictional provincial town that stands as Dickens's allegory of the unreformed British electoral system. In the years before the Great Reform Act of 1832 and its aftermath, parliamentary boroughs were notorious for corruption. Dickens, who reported on elections for the *Morning Chronicle*, had witnessed bribery, intimidation, and the manipulation of small electorates first-hand. The [[eatanswill|Eatanswill]] episode transposes this reality into farce.

[[mr-pickwick|Mr. Pickwick]] is drawn into this world through his acquaintance with [[mr-pott|Mr. Pott]], editor of the *[[eatanswill-gazette|Eatanswill Gazette]]*:

> "How are matters going on in Eatanswill?" inquired Mr. Pickwick. (023-054)

## The Event

The election pits the Blue interest (supporting the Honourable [[sam-weller|Sam]]uel [[mr-slumkey|Slumkey]]) against the Buff interest. The two parties are indistinguishable in policy but locked in mortal combat. The *[[eatanswill-gazette|Eatanswill Gazette]]* (Blue) and the *[[eatanswill-independent|Eatanswill Independent]]* (Buff) trade insults of escalating absurdity.

> "At this, the assembled selections laughed very heartily." (009-058)

## Aftermath

The [[eatanswill|Eatanswill]] election leaves no lasting mark on the novel's plot — it is a self-contained satirical set-piece. But its inclusion signals Dickens's growing ambition to use the novel as a vehicle for social criticism. The election's absurdity is its point: two indistinguishable parties, two interchangeable candidates, and a populace whose "public feeling" is manufactured by partisan newspapers. The satire is gentle compared to the later *Bleak House*, but it is unmistakably political.

## Significance

The [[eatanswill|Eatanswill]] election represents Dickens's first extended political satire in novel form. Coming from a journalist who had covered parliamentary debates, it has documentary authenticity beneath the farce. The rivalry of the *Gazette* and the *Independent* — each calling the other corrupt while practising the same corruption — prefigures the newspaper satire of later Victorian novels. The name "[[eatanswill|Eatanswill]]" itself (eat-and-swill) encapsulates the greed and consumption that the episode presents as the real engine of electoral politics.
