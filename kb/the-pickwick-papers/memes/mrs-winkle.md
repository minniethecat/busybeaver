# Mrs. Winkle

## Overview

Mrs. Winkle is the widowed mother of [[Mr. Winkle]] ([[nathaniel-winkle|Nathaniel Winkle]]), residing in Birmingham. She is introduced late in the novel when the question of [[Mr. Winkle]]'s marriage to [[Arabella Allen]] requires paternal — or rather maternal — approval. (019-070) Though her on-page appearances are brief, her role as the gatekeeper of her son's marital prospects makes her a significant figure in the resolution of the novel's romantic plot.

Mrs. Winkle is portrayed as a woman of maternal feeling and discernment, whose initial distress at her son's secret engagement gives way to acceptance upon meeting the young couple.

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Winkle serves a key function in the resolution of [[Mr. Winkle]]'s romantic storyline. Her son's engagement to [[Arabella Allen]] cannot proceed without her blessing, and much of the dramatic tension in the later chapters hinges on whether she will approve the match.

When [[Mr. Winkle]] returns to Birmingham and confesses his marriage, Mrs. Winkle is initially tearful and distressed. (019-070) However, [[Mr. Pickwick]]'s diplomatic intervention — visiting her in Birmingham and advocating on behalf of the young couple — proves decisive. (028-114) Moved by [[Mr. Pickwick]]'s testimony and by meeting [[Arabella Allen]], Mrs. Winkle ultimately receives the couple "fully into favour." (029-030)

## Key Scenes

- **The Confession** — [[Mr. Winkle]] returns home, drops to his knees, and confesses his marriage to his mother. Mrs. Winkle responds with tears. (019-070)
- **Drying Her Eyes** — After her initial distress, Mrs. Winkle dries her eyes and begins "coaxing" [[Mr. Winkle]], suggesting her maternal affection overcomes her disappointment. (019-088)
- **The Toast** — At a dinner, a toast is drunk to the "health and happiness of Mr. and Mrs. Winkle," indicating acceptance of the marriage. (020-118)
- **[[mr-pickwick|Mr. Pickwick]]'s Visit** — [[Mr. Pickwick]] arrives in Birmingham and inquires whether Mrs. Winkle is staying at the hotel. He is shown to her room, unannounced. (028-114)
- **Full Reconciliation** — Mr. and Mrs. Winkle are "fully received into favour" by her, completing the reconciliation arc. (029-030)

## Relationships

- **[[Mr. Winkle]]** ([[nathaniel-winkle|Nathaniel Winkle]]) — Her son, whose secret marriage causes her distress but whom she ultimately forgives and supports.
- **[[Mr. Pickwick]]** — Acts as an intermediary, visiting Mrs. Winkle in Birmingham to plead on behalf of her son. (028-114)
- **[[Arabella Allen]]** — Her son's wife, whom she comes to accept "fully into favour." (029-030)
- **Mrs. Winkle's Maid** — Mentioned as a person of some interest to [[Sam Weller]], who displays "more than a friendly feeling" towards her. (028-092)

## Character Analysis

Mrs. Winkle exemplifies Dickens's treatment of parental figures as obstacles that must be overcome for the younger generation to achieve happiness — a pattern repeated across multiple subplots in the novel. Unlike the more obstructive figures (such as [[Mr. Wardle]]'s initial resistance to [[Mr. Trundle]]), Mrs. Winkle is relatively easily won over, suggesting that her resistance is born of maternal protectiveness rather than rigid opposition.

Her widowhood is significant: as the sole surviving parent, she holds absolute authority over [[Mr. Winkle]]'s marital prospects. The fact that she is "drying her eyes" and moving toward acceptance within the same scene as her son's confession indicates a fundamentally sympathetic character.
