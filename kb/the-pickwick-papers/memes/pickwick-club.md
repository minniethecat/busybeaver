# Pickwick Club

## Overview

The Pickwick Club is the framing device for Charles Dickens's *The [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] Papers*. Founded by the novel's protagonist, [[mr-pickwick|Samuel Pickwick]], the club's Corresponding Society sends its members on journeys throughout England to report back on their observations. The novel's episodic structure is built around these travels and the comic misadventures that ensue.

## Definition

A fictional gentleman's club devoted to the advancement of knowledge through travel and observation, the Pickwick Club consists of its founder and chairman, [[mr-pickwick|Mr. Pickwick]], and three fellow members: Mr. Tupman, Mr. Snodgrass, and [[nathaniel-winkle|Mr. Winkle]]. The club's formal mission — "the advancement of knowledge, and the diffusion of learning" — is treated with Dickens's characteristic blend of affection and gentle satire.

## In Context

[[gentleman|Gentleman]]'s clubs were a prominent feature of Regency and early Victorian [[london|London]] society. Dickens's portrayal of the Pickwick Club both celebrates the spirit of inquiry and good fellowship while parodying the self-importance and pomposity of such institutions.

## Examples from the Novel

The novel's final chapter provides the club's epitaph: "The Pickwick Club exists no longer" (029-011). Its dissolution is attributed to the objections of the members' wives and families, "who consider the title of '[[gentleman|Pickwickian]]' as decidedly vulgar" (029-011). Yet throughout the preceding chapters, the club serves as the organizing principle for the characters' travels and the source of the novel's distinctive blend of comedy and social observation.
