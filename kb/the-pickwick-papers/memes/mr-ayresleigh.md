## Overview

Mr. Ayresleigh is one of the debtors confined in [[debtors-prison|the Fleet]] Prison whom [[mr-pickwick|Mr. Pickwick]] encounters on the morning after his own incarceration. His sole contribution to the narrative is the offer of a razor — a small gesture of communal hospitality that captures the strange domesticity of life inside the prison walls. (012-035) That another inmate immediately undercuts him by remarking that his real name is [[mr-price|Price]] (012-037) adds a note of comic uncertainty: in [[debtors-prison|the Fleet]], even identity is contingent.

## Role in the Novel

Ayresleigh appears in a single brief exchange that illustrates the makeshift sociability of [[debtors-prison|the Fleet]]. The prisoners have formed their own internal economy of favours and small kindnesses, and the offer of a razor to a newcomer is presented as a matter of routine. Dickens uses this moment, and the casual revelation that Ayresleigh may not be who he claims, to suggest the moral ambiguity of the prison's inhabitants — they are debtors, but they are also neighbours, and the line between the two identities is never quite fixed. (012-035–037)
