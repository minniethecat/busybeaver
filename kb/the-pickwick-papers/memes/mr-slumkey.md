## Overview

The Honourable Mr. Slumkey is the parliamentary candidate favoured by the [[eatanswill-gazette|Eatanswill Gazette]] in the celebrated [[eatanswill|Eatanswill]] election, one of the most sustained satirical set-pieces in the novel. (023-090) Though he never appears directly as a speaking character, his name is invoked as a rallying cry by [[mr-pott|Mr. Pott]]'s newspaper, which frames the contest in apocalyptic terms: the election is not merely a choice between two candidates but a battle between the forces of civilisation and chaos. Slumkey thus functions as a blank screen onto which the Gazette projects its most extravagant rhetoric — a cipher that reveals more about the newspaper's methods than about the man himself.

## Role in the Novel

Slumkey exists as a political abstraction rather than a character. His name appears in the [[eatanswill-gazette|Eatanswill Gazette]]'s editorial, where [[mr-pott|Mr. Pott]] exhorts readers to support "that distinguished and excellent representative, the Honourable Mr. Slumkey — that Slumkey whom we, long before his becoming a candidate, had frequently occasion to mention in terms of eulogium." (023-090) This self-congratulatory rhetoric — the newspaper taking credit for the candidate it now supports — is typical of the satire Dickens directs at provincial journalism throughout the [[eatanswill|Eatanswill]] chapters.

The [[eatanswill|Eatanswill]] election (023-042–172) pits the Gazette against the rival [[eatanswill-independent|Eatanswill Independent]], each paper representing one of the two parliamentary candidates. [[mr-pickwick|Mr. Pickwick]] and his companions arrive in [[eatanswill|Eatanswill]] (020-135) at the height of the contest, witnessing the bribery, mob violence, and journalistic excess that Dickens presents as the machinery of representative government in a provincial borough.
