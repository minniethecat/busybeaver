## Overview

Mrs. Raddle is a minor but vividly drawn character in _The [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] Papers_, serving as [[Bob Sawyer]]'s landlady and tormentor. She first appears in Chapter 4 when she demands to speak with Sawyer about an unpaid matter, and over the course of the chapter she escalates from lofty politeness to "intense fierceness," driving Sawyer to retreat in fear of being "waylaid" by her. (004-041) Dickens describes her lodging-house with characteristic irony: "there never was a lodging-house more laudably conducted, or a landlord and landlady whose characters were more highly respectable." (004-074) She is later revealed to be the sister of [[Mrs. Cluppins]], connecting the medical-student subplot to the Bardell trial storyline.

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Raddle functions as a comic obstacle in the [[bob-sawyer|Bob Sawyer]]–[[ben-allen|Ben Allen]] storyline. Her relentless pursuit of unpaid rent forces Sawyer to adopt a deferential, almost groveling tone, and her escalating indignation provides the chapter's comic rhythm. Beyond Chapter 4, the revelation in Chapter 18 that she is [[mrs-cluppins|Mrs. Cluppins]]'s sister (018-046) retrospectively links the rowdy Sawyer household to the more respectable Goswell Street world of [[mrs-bardell|Mrs. Bardell]], tightening Dickens's web of minor-character relationships.

## Key Scenes

**The Rent Confrontation (Chapter 4)**
Mrs. Raddle's entrance is announced by a servant: "Please, Mister Sawyer, Missis Raddle wants to speak to _you_." (004-014) [[bob-sawyer|Bob Sawyer]] responds deferentially, but Mrs. Raddle is unmoved, tossing her head and biting her lips. (004-020) She plants herself firmly on a chair and demands satisfaction. (004-023) Sawyer's apologetic stammering only encourages her:

> "Very good, sir," responded Mrs. Raddle, with lofty politeness. "Then p'raps you'll pay me, sir. That's all I have to say." (004-030)

When Sawyer attempts to deflect, she interrupts "with intense fierceness," demanding payment for the previous quarter's rent. (004-036) She exits backing toward the door, and Sawyer, dreading another encounter, "did not venture to leave the room for at least a quarter of an hour afterwards, lest he should be waylaid by Mrs. Raddle." (004-041)

**The Coal Incident (Chapter 4)**
Mrs. Raddle's authority extends to the kitchen. When Sawyer's guests call for more coal, the servant-girl reports: "Missis Raddle said you warn't to have none." (004-084) She has already "raked out the kitchen fire," extinguishing the household's source of heat as a further pressure tactic. (004-087)

**Family Connection Revealed (Chapter 18)**
Mrs. Raddle reappears in Chapter 18 when [[Mr. Jackson]] arrives at [[mrs-bardell|Mrs. Bardell]]'s. The introduction —"[[mr-raddle|Mr. Raddle]], ma'am; [[mrs-cluppins|Mrs. Cluppins]], ma'am; Mrs. Raddle, ma'am." (018-045)—establishes her family connection with [[Mrs. Cluppins]], placing her within the wider network of women orbiting the Bardell household. (018-046)
