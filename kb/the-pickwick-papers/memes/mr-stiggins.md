## Overview

The Reverend Mr. Stiggins — often referred to with heavy-handed irony as "the Reverend" — is a dissenting preacher and temperance advocate who is particularly popular among the female members of his congregation. His piety, however, dissolves rapidly in the presence of [[tony-weller|Tony Weller]]'s pineapple rum, leading to a series of farcical confrontations between the red-nosed shepherd and the exasperated coachman. (005-147) Stiggins embodies Dickens's lifelong satirical target: those who preach abstinence while practising indulgence, wrapping moral condemnation in the language of sanctimony.

## Role in the Novel

Stiggins functions as a comic antagonist to [[tony-weller|Tony Weller]], whose second wife Susan has fallen under the shepherd's influence. Weller's running battle with Stiggins — culminating in a physical ejection so vigorous that the reverend gentleman is "put to bed in the horse-trough" — provides some of the novel's most robust slapstick comedy. Beyond the farce, however, Stiggins serves Dickens's broader critique of Evangelical cant, a theme that would recur throughout the author's career. (017-050–079)

## Relationships

- **[[Tony Weller]]** — The coachman is Stiggins's implacable enemy, viewing the shepherd as both a domestic intruder and a hypocrite of the first water. Weller's repeated physical assaults on Stiggins are among the novel's most celebrated comic set-pieces. (005-165–168)
- **[[Sam Weller]]** — [[sam-weller|Sam]] treats Stiggins with carefully disguised contempt, his Cockney wit providing a running satirical commentary on the shepherd's pieties. (017-050)
- **[[mrs-weller|Mrs. Weller]] (Susan)** — Stiggins's chief patron and protector, whose devotion to the shepherd enables his intrusion into the Weller household. She is [[tony-weller|Tony Weller]]'s second wife, and her death severs Stiggins's domestic foothold.
