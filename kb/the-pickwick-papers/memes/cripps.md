## Overview

Mrs. Cripps is a minor figure whose sole function in the novel is to serve as a human signpost. When [[mr-pickwick|Mr. Pickwick]] visits the Borough in search of [[bob-sawyer|Bob Sawyer]], he finds the former surgery at [[lant-street|Lant Street]] boarded up with the faded inscription "Sawyer, late Nockemorf" and a handbill directing inquiries to "Mrs. Cripps, over the way." (022-018) She is one of those fleeting Dickensian characters who exist only to be asked a question, yet in that single gesture she embodies the interconnectedness of the Borough's working-class community — a world where neighbours keep track of one another's movements and a stranger can find his way by asking at the right door.

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Cripps serves a purely practical narrative function: she provides the information that redirects [[mr-pickwick|Mr. Pickwick]] from the abandoned [[lant-street|Lant Street]] surgery to [[bob-sawyer|Bob Sawyer]]'s new location. Her presence is so brief that she is not even directly quoted; the narrative merely reports that the handbill instructs the visitor to inquire of her. She represents the neighborhood infrastructure of the Borough, where commerce, medicine, and domestic life intermingle in the shadow of [[debtors-prison|the Fleet]] Prison. (022-018)
