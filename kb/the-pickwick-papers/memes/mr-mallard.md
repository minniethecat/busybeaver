## Overview

Mr. Mallard is a minor legal functionary who appears in two chapters of _The [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] Papers_ as the clerk of [[serjeant-snubbin|Serjeant Snubbin]], the eminent barrister retained to defend [[mr-pickwick|Mr. Pickwick]] in the case of _Bardell v. [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]]_. He serves as the gatekeeper between the outside world and the great Serjeant — a familiar type in Dickens's legal world, where clerks and functionaries exercise quiet, procedural power over access to their masters. Mr. Mallard is competent, obliging, and entirely absorbed into his subordinate role.

## Role in the Novel

Mr. Mallard's role is purely functional: he facilitates the plot machinery around [[mr-pickwick|Mr. Pickwick]]'s legal defence. He first appears when [[mr-perker|Perker]], [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]]'s solicitor, calls at [[serjeant-snubbin|Serjeant Snubbin]]'s chambers to brief him on the case. Mallard manages the threshold — [[mr-perker|Perker]] must coax and cajole him to secure an audience with the Serjeant (003-123). Later, at the trial itself, Mallard appears in his ceremonial capacity, carrying the Serjeant's "large crimson bag" into court (006-015). He says little, does what he is told, and vanishes from the narrative once his utility is spent. He is the very model of the Dickensian legal clerk: discreet, deferential, and slightly comical in his self-importance.

## Key Scenes

**The Visit to [[serjeant-snubbin|Serjeant Snubbin]]'s Chambers (Chapter 3)**
[[mr-perker|Perker]] arrives at the Serjeant's chambers and encounters Mr. Mallard:

> "Is the Serjeant in his room, Mr. Mallard?" inquired Perker, offering his box with all imaginable courtesy. (003-115)

When informed that the Serjeant is busy, [[mr-perker|Perker]] attempts gentle persuasion:

> "But, Mr. Mallard, my dear friend," said Perker, suddenly recovering his gravity, and drawing the great man's great man into a corner, by the lappel of his coat; "you must persuade the Serjeant to see me and my client here." (003-123)

Later, after the briefing, Snubbin issues an instruction:

> "Mr. Mallard, send round to Mr. — Mr. ——" (003-148)

Mallard "departed to execute his commission" (003-150), the efficient functionary in motion.

**The Trial at the Guildhall (Chapter 6)**
At the trial, Mallard's ceremonial role is on display:

> Mr. Serjeant Snubbin appeared, followed by Mr. Mallard, who half hid the Serjeant behind a large crimson bag, which he placed on the table, and after shaking hands with Perker, withdrew. (006-015)

The image of Mallard "half hid" behind the bag is a small but characteristic Dickens touch — the clerk dwarfed by the paraphernalia of his master's profession.
