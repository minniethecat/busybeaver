## Overview

Bath is the fashionable spa town in Somerset to which the [[pickwick-club|Pickwickians]] travel by coach in Chapter VII ("[[mr-pickwick|Mr. Pickwick]] Thinks He Had Better Go to Bath and Goes Accordingly"). In the 1830s, Bath was at the height of its Georgian elegance — a destination for pleasure-seekers, invalids taking the waters, and social climbers. Dickens uses the town's atmosphere of refined pretension as a backdrop for encounters with colourful minor characters, including the belligerent [[mr-dowler|Mr. Dowler]] and the enigmatic "Bagman's Story" narrator. (007-011) The coach journey from [[London]] to [[Bath]] epitomises the [[Coaching Era]] at its peak.

## Location

Bath lies in the Avon valley of Somerset, approximately 100 miles west of [[London]]. In the 1830s, the journey from [[london|London]] to Bath by stage-coach took roughly 12 hours. The city's centrepiece is the Great Pump Room, where visitors drank the mineral waters, and the King's Bath, where they bathed. (007-065) The coach from [[london|London]] put up at a coaching inn in the town centre, where [[mr-pickwick|Mr. Pickwick]] and his friends alight and immediately encounter the town's social whirl.

> "Bath being full, the company and the sixpences for drinking the waters were nearly double what they were the preceding season." (007-111)

## Historical Context

Bath in the 1830s was nearing the end of its Georgian golden age. The coaching era, celebrated in Dickens's novel, was about to be overtaken by the railways — the Great Western Railway would reach Bath in 1840, just a few years after *The [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] Papers* was published. The Pump Room, built in 1795, was the social heart of the town, where visitors "took the waters" as a cure for ailments real and imagined. Dickens, who visited Bath several times, captures both the elegance and the absurdity of spa-town culture: the mingling of genuine invalids with social poseurs, the inflated prices, and the peculiar rituals of public bathing and water-drinking.

> "The celebrated waters of Bath bubbled, and sparkled, and cured, as of yore." (008-017)
