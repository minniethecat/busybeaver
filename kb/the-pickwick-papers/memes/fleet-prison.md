# Fleet Prison

## Overview

The Fleet Prison was a notorious [[london|London]] debtors' prison, operating from the 12th century until 1844. In *The [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] Papers*, [[mr-pickwick|Mr. Pickwick]] is confined there after refusing to pay damages in the breach of promise case brought by Mrs. Bardell. Dickens uses the prison as a vehicle for social commentary on the injustice of imprisoning those too poor to pay their debts.

## Definition

The Fleet was one of several [[london|London]] prisons designated for debtors and those convicted of contempt of court. Prisoners were held until their debts were paid, creating a paradoxical system in which those without money were locked up at their own expense, with no means of earning the funds needed for release.

## In Context

Dickens had firsthand knowledge of debtors' prisons — his own father was imprisoned in the Marshalsea for debt when Charles was a boy. The Fleet Prison chapters (XII–XVII) represent some of the novel's darkest social realism, depicting the "miserable and abject class of debtors" confined within its walls (014-108).

## Examples from the Novel

[[mr-pickwick|Mr. Pickwick]] is taken to "the Warden of the Fleet Prison, and there detained" after his refusal to pay the Bardell judgment (012-087). Within the prison, he observes "a great number of debtors, the major part of whom were seated at little tables" (017-177). The experience profoundly affects him, exposing him to the harsh realities of poverty and the injustice of the legal system. In a moment of defiance, he declares: "Here am I in the Fleet Prison. Well; good. What then?" (013-088).
