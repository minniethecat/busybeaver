## Overview

Mr. Skimpin is a minor character in _The [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] Papers_ who appears exclusively during the trial of _Bardell v. [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]]_ in Chapter 6. As junior counsel to [[Serjeant Buzfuz]], Skimpin performs the practical work of examining witnesses for the plaintiff, most notably [[Mr. Winkle]]. He is described as "a promising young man of two or three and forty" whose technique relies heavily on insinuating glances at the jury and demands for yes-or-no answers — tactics that Dickens satirizes as the stock-in-trade of a certain kind of courtroom advocate.

## Role in the Novel

Skimpin's sole function in the novel is to prosecute the plaintiff's case through witness examination. He opens the case for [[Mrs. Bardell]] (006-052) and later examines [[Mr. Winkle]] as a hostile witness. His method — loaded questions, theatrical appeals to the jury, and constant insinuation — provides Dickens with a vehicle for satirizing the adversarial tactics of the English courtroom. Skimpin's performance, particularly his triumphant sitting-down after a supposedly devastating question, embodies the empty theatricality that the novel implicitly critiques in the legal system.

## Key Scenes

**The Trial — Bardell v. [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] (Chapter 6)**

Mr. Skimpin is first identified to [[Mr. Pickwick]] by [[Mr. Perker]]:

> "That gentleman behind him is Mr. Skimpin, his junior." (006-017)

When the court is called to order, the crier announces "[[serjeant-buzfuz|Serjeant Buzfuz]] and Mr. Skimpin for the plaintiff." (006-048) After a brief silence, Skimpin proceeds to open the case (006-052), laying out the prosecution's narrative before the jury.

His most substantial moment comes during the examination of [[Mr. Winkle]]. Called to the stand (006-097), [[nathaniel-winkle|Winkle]] is examined by Skimpin, who is described as "a promising young man of two or three and forty" and noted for his habit of "looking at the jury all the time he was examining a witness." (006-101) Skimpin's questions are characteristically insinuating:

> "Now, sir," said Mr. Skimpin, "have the goodness to let his Lordship and the Jury know what your name is, will you?" (006-102)

When [[nathaniel-winkle|Winkle]] struggles to give a straight answer, Skimpin presses him relentlessly:

> "Now, Mr. Winkle," said Mr. Skimpin, "attend to me, if you please, sir." (006-117)

His technique of demanding binary answers is on full display:

> "Come, sir," said Mr. Skimpin, "yes or no, if you please." (006-123)

Skimpin repeatedly uses the phrase "never mind" to dismiss [[nathaniel-winkle|Winkle]]'s attempts at context — a rhetorical device designed to strip the testimony of nuance:

> "Never mind your friends," said Mr. Skimpin, with another expressive look at the jury. (006-139)

He finally lands what he considers a decisive blow with a question about [[nathaniel-winkle|Winkle]]'s location on a staircase (006-145), and upon receiving [[nathaniel-winkle|Winkle]]'s reply, sits down "with a triumphant countenance." (006-146)
