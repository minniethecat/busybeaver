## Overview

Elections and political culture form a major satirical target in *The Pickwick Papers*. Dickens skewers the absurdities of pre-Reform Act electoral practices through the fictional borough of Eatanswill, where the two rival factions — the Blues and the Buffs — wage a farcical contest that has nothing to do with principle and everything to do with bribery, violence, and partisan journalism.

## Definition

In the context of the novel, "elections and politics" refers to the system of parliamentary elections as they operated in England before the 1832 Reform Act, particularly in pocket boroughs where a handful of voters could be bribed or coerced, and where the local press served as propaganda organs rather than sources of information.

## In Context

At the time Dickens was writing (1836–1837), England's electoral system was undergoing reform. The Reform Act of 1832 had only recently addressed the worst abuses of "rotten boroughs," but Dickens sets his satire in 1827, just before Reform, to capture the full absurdity of the unreformed system. The Eatanswill election draws on Dickens's own experience as a parliamentary reporter from 1831 to 1836.

## Examples from the Novel

The most sustained treatment of politics appears in the Eatanswill election sequence. The town's two newspapers — the *Eatanswill Gazette* (Blue) and the *Eatanswill Independent* (Buff) — engage in hyperbolic rhetorical warfare, each denouncing the other's candidate in language of extravagant vitriol. Mr. Pott, editor of the *Gazette*, refers to the *Independent* as a "reptile contemporary" that has "sweltered forth his black venom" (023-090). The election itself involves bribery, drunken mobs, and physical intimidation — a complete mockery of democratic principle.
