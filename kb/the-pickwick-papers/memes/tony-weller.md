## Overview

Tony Weller (also rendered as "Tony Veller" in the novel's phonetic orthography) is the father of [[Sam Weller]] and one of Dickens's most memorable minor characters. A veteran stage-coachman with a distinctively broad philosophy of life, he communicates through letters and pronouncements rendered in his own idiosyncratic spelling. His experiences with his second wife Susan and her association with the Reverend [[Mr. Stiggins]] and the temperance movement furnish some of the novel's sharpest satire on religious hypocrisy.

## Role in the Novel

Mr. Weller Senior functions as [[sam-weller|Sam]]'s confidant and a source of working-class wisdom that often undercuts the pretensions of the novel's more elevated characters. A widower by the novel's later chapters, his distrust of widows and "widders" becomes a running comic motif. His philosophy of the coachman as "a sort o' con-nectin' link betwixt singleness and marriage" (024-092) typifies the Dickensian gift for having low characters articulate unexpected profundity. His role culminates in [[debtors-prison|the Fleet]] Prison chapters, where his loyalty to both his son and [[Mr. Pickwick]] is tested and confirmed.

## Key Scenes

**The Certificate of [[mrs-weller|Mrs. Weller]]** (005-027): Tony Weller presents his son with a document recording his wife's medical bulletin: "'[[mrs-weller|Mrs. Weller]] passed a very good night, but is uncommon perwerse and unpleasant this mornin'. Signed upon oath, S. Veller, Esquire, Senior.'" This becomes the model for the wonderfully bureaucratic domestic comedy that surrounds the elder Wellers' marriage.

**The Legal Document** (005-089): When required to sign a legal paper, the oldest surviving proprietor of the name commands: "Sign it, Veller." The moment captures both the Weller family's longevity in the coaching trade and Tony's position within a lineage.

**Presenting His Card** (007-086): Arriving at an appointment, he instructs [[sam-weller|Sam]]: "jist you step into him with that 'ere card, and say Mr. Veller's a waitin', will you?" and coolly walks into the hall. The scene demonstrates his self-possession and the dignity he claims despite his modest station.

**Susan's Deathbed Confession** (024-064): In one of the novel's most emotionally complex passages, Tony recounts his dying wife's words: "'Veller,' she says, 'I'm afeard I've not done by you quite wot I ought to have done; you're a wery kind-hearted man, and I might ha' made your home more comfortabler.'" She laments that while attending to religion, she neglected her domestic duties: "if a married 'ooman vishes to be religious, she should begin vith dischargin' her dooties at home." The speech, rendered in Tony's phonetic recollection, is one of Dickens's most powerful critiques of religious hypocrisy — made all the more devastating by the widow's too-late self-awareness.

**Named Executor** (027-011): After Susan's death, her will appoints "my husband, Mr. Tony Veller, who I appint as my sole eggzekiter," bequeathing two hundred pounds' worth of reduced counsels to [[sam-weller|Sam]]. The will's creative spelling — "eggezekiter," "counsels," "appint" — suggests that Tony's orthographic style may be a family trait, or that [[mrs-weller|Mrs. Weller]] herself adopted his phonetic approach.

**To [[sam-weller|Sam]]ivel Veller** (028-081): Tony raises a toast "To [[sam-weller|Sam]]ivel Veller!" — his pronunciation of his son's name serving as a term of endearment and a marker of their unbreakable bond.

**[[sam-weller|Sam]]'s Vow of Loyalty** (028-109): [[sam-weller|Sam]]'s declaration that "[[sam-weller|Sam]] Veller, as you took from the old inn in the Borough, sticks by you, come what come may" encapsulates the father-son relationship and the values Tony has instilled — loyalty, rootedness, and stubborn fidelity.

## Relationships

- **[[Sam Weller]]** — His beloved son, whose sharp wit clearly descends from paternal example
- **[[mrs-weller|Susan Weller]]** — His second wife, whose deathbed confession reveals the cost of her religious enthusiasm
- **[[Mr. Stiggins]]** — The red-nosed deputy shepherd whose influence over [[mrs-weller|Susan Weller]] torments Tony
- **[[Mr. Pickwick]]** — His son's master, whom Tony comes to respect and whose imprisonment brings both Wellers to [[debtors-prison|the Fleet]]
