## Overview

Mr. Whiffers is a minor character in [[debtors-prison|the Fleet]] Prison chapters of _The [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] Papers_, appearing briefly but memorably as a speaker at one of the debtors' social gatherings. Introduced with ceremonial formality — "Gentlemen, our friend Mr. Whiffers" (009-113) — he delivers a short address on the struggles of life in domestic service, a speech that is both a personal confession and a piece of comic oratory. His complaint that he feared a part of his employer's establishment had "gone Imperence" (009-118) is a small gem of Dickensian dialogue, capturing in a single mangled word the collision of servant-class grievance with the aspirational vocabulary of the respectable.

## Role in the Novel

Whiffers appears in a single scene in Chapter 9, where he contributes to the ensemble portrait of [[debtors-prison|the Fleet]] Prison as a self-contained society with its own rituals, hierarchies, and rhetorical performances. His speech — part lament, part self-justification — is one of several set-pieces that Dickens uses to humanise the prison's inmates while simultaneously mocking their pretensions. Whiffers is neither tragic nor villainous; he is another specimen in the gallery of Fleet characters, his "Imperence" adding a distinct comic note to the carceral chorus.

## Key Scenes

**The Prison Speech (Chapter 9)**
Whiffers is introduced in the most formal terms:

> "Gentlemen, our friend Mr. Whiffers" — everybody looked at the individual indicated — "our friend Mr. Whiffers has been in the service." (009-113, 009-115)

Rather than delivering the speech himself, the introducer defers: "I will beg Mr. Whiffers to state them [his experiences] himself, for the benefit of the company." (009-115) The motion being "loudly approved of, Mr. Whiffers explained." (009-116)

Whiffers takes the floor and "said he certainly had been in the service" (009-116), launching into an account of his grievances:

> Mr. Whiffers then added that he feared a part of the establishment had "gone Imperence." (009-118)

The malapropism — "Imperence" for "impertinence" or possibly some bureaucratic term — is pure Dickens, and it is met with laughter and applause: "Mr. Whiffers' address was responded to with" approval. (009-119) The speech, brief as it is, cements Whiffers's place in the prison's oral culture as a voice of aggrieved respectability.
