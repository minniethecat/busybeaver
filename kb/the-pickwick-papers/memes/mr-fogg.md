# Mr. Fogg

## Overview

Mr. Fogg is the junior partner of the law firm [[Dodson and Fogg]] of Freeman's Court, Cornhill. Along with his partner [[Mr. Dodson]], he serves as solicitor for [[Mrs. Bardell]] in her breach-of-promise suit against [[Mr. Pickwick]]. (003-005)

Where [[Mr. Dodson]] maintains a veneer of professional respectability, Mr. Fogg is the more openly cynical of the two partners. He speaks with unapologetic frankness about the firm's business model, creating a memorable portrait of legal venality in Dickens's satirical gallery.

## Role in the Novel

Mr. Fogg functions as one of the principal antagonists in the legal subplot of the novel. Together with [[Mr. Dodson]], he prosecutes [[Mrs. Bardell]]'s case against [[Mr. Pickwick]] with a calculating eye toward costs rather than justice. (003-085)

During the trial, Mr. Fogg is an active presence, conferring with [[Serjeant Buzfuz]], escorting witnesses, and reacting to the proceedings with evident satisfaction. After the verdict, he and [[Mr. Dodson]] pursue [[Mr. Pickwick]] relentlessly for their costs — a pursuit that ultimately leads to [[Mr. Pickwick]]'s imprisonment in the [[Fleet Prison]]. (017-119)

In the novel's climactic confrontation, [[Mr. Pickwick]] visits the office of [[dodson-and-fogg|Dodson and Fogg]] to denounce their practices. It is Mr. Fogg who attempts to manage the encounter, putting up his pocket-book and urging his partner to remain calm. (025-112) When [[Mr. Pickwick]] becomes physically agitated, Mr. Fogg calls out, "Let him assault you, [[mr-dodson|Mr. Dodson]]; don't return it on any account," revealing his strategic mind even in moments of crisis. (025-131)

## Key Scenes

- **The Trial** — Mr. Fogg attends the trial of ''Bardell v. [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]]'', escorting witnesses and nodding in admiration at [[Serjeant Buzfuz]]'s oratory alongside [[Mr. Dodson]]. (006-039)
- **[[sam-weller|Sam Weller]]'s Satire** — [[Sam Weller]]'s devastating ironic testimony about the "honourable conduct" of [[dodson-and-fogg|Dodson and Fogg]] causes Mr. Fogg to turn red. (006-214)
- **After the Verdict** — Mr. Fogg joins [[Mr. Dodson]] in rubbing his hands with satisfaction after the trial's conclusion. (006-234)
- **The Office Confrontation** — When [[Mr. Pickwick]] confronts the partners, Mr. Fogg puts up his pocket-book and attempts to manage the situation. (025-112)
- **Inviting Assault** — Mr. Fogg coolly instructs [[Mr. Dodson]] not to retaliate if [[Mr. Pickwick]] assaults him, hoping to add an assault charge to their case. (025-131)

## Relationships

- **[[Mr. Dodson]]** — His senior partner in the firm of [[Dodson and Fogg]]. They operate as a complementary pair: Fogg is the openly cynical one, Dodson the more polished.
- **[[Mrs. Bardell]]** — Their client, exploited for the costs her case generates.
- **[[Mr. Pickwick]]** — The defendant, whom Mr. Fogg pursues with calculating persistence.
- **[[Mr. Jackson]]** — The firm's clerk, who delivers the initial legal notice to [[Mr. Pickwick]] from "[[dodson-and-fogg|Dodson and Fogg]]'s." (003-014)
- **[[Sam Weller]]** — [[Mr. Pickwick]]'s servant, whose courtroom testimony subjects the firm to public ridicule.

## Character Analysis

Mr. Fogg represents the naked, unapologetic face of legal sharp practice. Unlike [[Mr. Dodson]], who wraps the firm's avarice in the language of professional duty, Mr. Fogg is disarmingly direct. His instruction to [[Mr. Dodson]] — "Let him assault you, [[mr-dodson|Mr. Dodson]]; don't return it on any account" — reveals a mind that instantly converts any situation into potential legal advantage. (025-131)

The dichotomy between [[dodson-and-fogg|Dodson and Fogg]] is a classic Dickensian pairing: the respectable villain and the honest villain. Together they exemplify the critique of the Victorian legal profession that runs throughout ''The [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] Papers'' — a system in which attorneys could profit handsomely from litigation without any genuine concern for its merits or its human cost.

Mr. Fogg's name, with its suggestion of murkiness and obscurity, is typical of Dickens's onomastic technique. It pairs with Dodson's more percussive name to create a firm whose very title suggests sharp dealing.
