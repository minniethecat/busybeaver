## Overview

[[Mr. Pickwick]]'s imprisonment in the [[debtors-prison|Fleet Prison]] is the novel's most sustained and powerful sequence: eight chapters in which the comic tone darkens into Dickens's first major piece of social criticism. Refusing to pay the £750 damages awarded in the [[Bardell v. Pickwick Trial]], [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] is arrested at the suit of [[Dodson and Fogg]] and committed to [[debtors-prison|the Fleet]] as a debtor. (012-031)

What follows is a descent into a miniature world — the prison as microcosm — where Dickens anatomises the degradations of imprisonment for debt with controlled outrage.

## Background

After the [[Bardell v. Pickwick Trial]], [[Mr. Pickwick]] declares he will "never pay a farthing" of the unjust damages. (007-002) [[Dodson and Fogg]], having secured their costs, have him arrested. The alternative — paying the damages — would vindicate the legal system that [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] believes has wronged him.

> "You can go to the Fleet, my dear sir, if you're determined to go." (012-053)

Within the prison, [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] encounters a gallery of characters the equal of any he met outside: the caged man who posts a legend about being a cannibal (014-108), the Chancery prisoner ground down by the law's delays, the comfortable "habitual" debtors who prefer [[debtors-prison|the Fleet]] to freedom. The most significant encounter is with [[Alfred Jingle]] and [[Job Trotter]], now reduced to abject poverty within the prison. [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]]'s forgiveness of the man who once deceived him is the novel's moral climax.

> "Here am I in the Fleet Prison. Well; good. What then?" — Sam Weller (013-088)

## Aftermath

The impasse is resolved only when [[mrs-bardell|Mrs. Bardell]] herself is arrested for non-payment of costs — poetic justice that leaves [[Dodson and Fogg]] with nothing. [[Mr. Pickwick]] pays her costs, freeing them both, and emerges from [[debtors-prison|the Fleet]] after three months — "three months and better in [[debtors-prison|the Fleet]], sir" (019-105) — a changed man.

## Significance

The [[fleet-prison|Fleet Prison]] sequence marks the transformation of *The [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] Papers* from episodic comedy into a novel with a social conscience. Dickens's depiction of the debtors' prison — its internal economy, its degrading "poor side," its casual brutalities — was a direct contribution to the campaign for the abolition of imprisonment for debt. The Fleet itself would close in 1842, just a few years after publication.

The sequence also completes [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]]'s character arc: from naive benevolence, through the test of principle, to a chastened but deepened humanity that can forgive even [[Alfred Jingle]].
