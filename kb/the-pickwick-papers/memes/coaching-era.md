## Overview

The coaching era — roughly 1820 to 1840 — represents the peak of horse-drawn stage-coach travel in England, the period immediately before railways transformed the landscape of transport and social life.

*The [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] Papers* is the great literary monument of this era: its episodic structure is shaped by coach journeys, its comic rhythm governed by arrivals and departures at coaching inns, and its social vision enabled by the random encounters that coach travel made inevitable.

## Definition

Stage-coach travel in the 1830s operated on a network of turnpike roads radiating from [[London]]. Coaches departed from coaching inns — the White Hart, the [[George and Vulture]], the Bull — on fixed schedules.

The journey from [[london|London]] to [[Bath]] took roughly 12 hours, with changes of horses at staging posts. The coach carried mail, parcels, and passengers of all classes, making it a uniquely democratic social space.

## In Context

The coaching era was simultaneously at its height and on the brink of extinction when Dickens wrote *The [[mr-pickwick|Pickwick]] Papers* (1836–37). The Liverpool and Manchester Railway had opened in 1830; the [[london|London]] and Birmingham would follow in 1838.

The coaching inns, the turnpike roads, the coachmen with their elaborate coats and professional pride — all would be swept away within a generation. Tony Weller, [[sam-weller|Sam]]'s father, represents the coachman as a vanishing type.

> "Him as drives a Ipswich coach, and uses our parlour," rejoined the boots. (005-014)
