## Overview

Gabriel Grub is the central figure of "The Story of the Goblins who stole a Sexton," an embedded narrative recounted by the clergyman during the [[pickwick-club|Pickwickians]]' first [[christmas-at-dingley-dell|Christmas at Manor Farm]]. He is a misanthropic sexton who takes grim satisfaction in digging graves and singing mournful songs, until a [[christmas|Christmas Eve]] encounter with a troupe of goblins in the churchyard transforms his character entirely.

## Role in the Novel

Gabriel Grub appears only within an interpolated tale, not in the main narrative. The story is told to [[Mr. Pickwick]] and his companions by the clergyman at [[mr-wardle|Mr. Wardle]]'s [[christmas|Christmas]] gathering. As an embedded narrative, it serves thematic purposes: it illustrates the redemptive power of [[christmas|Christmas]] cheer, foreshadows the novel's interest in transformation through companionship, and provides a Dickensian variation on the [[christmas|Christmas]] ghost-story tradition that would later flourish in *A [[christmas|Christmas]] Carol*.

## Key Scenes

**The Misanthropic Sexton** (001-002–001-005): Gabriel Grub is introduced as "the sexton and grave-digger in the churchyard" (001-002), a man who takes perverse pleasure in his grim occupation. The narrator describes him as someone who greeted the sight of children playing with bitterness: "when groups of children gathered round the churchyard gate, their mirth was gall and wormwood to the heart of Gabriel Grub" (001-003). He chuckles heartily at others' misfortunes (001-004).

**[[christmas|Christmas Eve]] in the Churchyard** (001-012–001-015): On [[christmas|Christmas Eve]], Grub sits on a flat tombstone in the churchyard, drinking from a wicker bottle and striking the gravestones with his spade to accompany his own discordant singing. He laughs with grim satisfaction: "'Ho! ho!' laughed Gabriel Grub, as he sat himself down on a flat tombstone" (001-012).

**The Goblin Appears** (001-018–001-028): A goblin rises from the earth before Grub, "grinning at Gabriel Grub with such a grin as only a goblin could assume" (001-018). The creature is of supernatural appearance, with an unearthly expression. Gabriel Grub is "paralysed" (001-020), unable to move or speak. The goblin mocks him by shouting his name repeatedly: "Gabriel Grub! Gabriel Grub!" (001-024, 001-028).

**The Goblin Court** (001-045–001-059): The goblin transports Grub to an underground cavern where a host of goblins sit in state, "whom Gabriel Grub imagined to be courtiers" (001-050). They subject him to a series of visions showing the consequences of misanthropy and the goodness in human hearts. The goblin king turns "his large face towards Gabriel Grub" (001-057) and delivers moral instruction, after which the goblins administer "a good sound kick to Gabriel Grub" (001-059).

**The Transformation** (001-063–001-067): Grub awakens on [[christmas|Christmas]] morning a changed man. "Many a lesson it taught to Gabriel Grub" (001-063) — he abandons his misanthropic ways, leaves the village, and is later rumoured to have become a reformed character. "The day had broken when Gabriel Grub awoke, and found himself lying on the flat gravestone in the churchyard" (001-064). His eventual "unlooked-for reappearance" (001-067) some ten years later confirms his transformation into a kind-hearted man.
