## Overview

General Alexei Alexeyevich Brusilov was one of the most capable commanders in the [[imperial-russian-army|Imperial Russian Army]]. He achieved fame as the architect of the [[brusilov-offensive|Brusilov Offensive of 1916]], widely regarded as Russia's most successful operation of the [[world-war-i|First World War]]. In the summer of 1917, after the [[february-revolution|February Revolution]], he briefly served as Commander-in-Chief under the Provisional Government. In [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka Bochkareva]]'s memoir, Brusilov appears at a critical moment — it is to him, alongside [[alexander-kerensky|Kerensky]] and [[michael-rodzianko|Rodzianko]], that she must justify her proposal for a women's battalion, and his endorsement proves decisive.

## Biography

Born in 1853 in Tiflis (now Tbilisi, Georgia) to a military family, Brusilov was educated at the Corps of Pages and commissioned into the cavalry. He served in the Russo-Turkish War of 1877–78 and rose steadily through the ranks. In 1916, as commander of the Southwestern Front, he planned and executed the [[brusilov-offensive|Brusilov Offensive]], which shattered the Austro-Hungarian army and nearly knocked Austria-Hungary out of the war.

After the [[february-revolution|February Revolution]], [[alexander-kerensky|Kerensky]] appointed Brusilov Commander-in-Chief in June 1917, hoping his popularity with the troops would revive the flagging war effort. Brusilov attempted to reconcile military discipline with revolutionary democracy — an impossible task. He was replaced by Kornilov in August 1917. Unlike many former Tsarist officers, Brusilov later cooperated with the [[soviet|Soviet]] government and served as an inspector of cavalry for the Red Army. He died in [[moscow|Moscow]] in 1926.

## Role in Narrative

Brusilov's role in [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]]'s story is concentrated in a single crucial episode: her first approach to the military high command about the women's battalion.

[[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]]'s initial proposal needed military as well as political backing. [[michael-rodzianko|Rodzianko]] wisely directed her to Brusilov, recognizing that the Commander-in-Chief's endorsement was essential:

> "Rodzianko telephoned, suggesting that before the matter was broached to the War Minister, Kerensky, it would be wise to take it up with the Commander-in-Chief, General Brusilov, who could judge it from the military standpoint." — PN 014-035

[[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] and [[captain-dementiev|Captain Dementiev]] traveled to Army Headquarters at Mogilev to meet the general:

> "Brusilov shook hands with us cordially. He had heard of me. He called in his Chief of Staff, General Lukomsky, and they both discussed the project from all points of view." — PN 014-037

Brusilov's judgment was measured but ultimately favorable. He recognized the symbolic power of women soldiers:

> "The project gained Brusilov's cautious approval. If the men saw women marching to the trenches, they might be shamed into following." — PN 014-038

Later, at the Marinsky Theatre, [[michael-rodzianko|Rodzianko]] reminded [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] to speak boldly before the assembled leadership — Brusilov among them:

> "'Just tell them what you told Rodzianko, Brusilov and Kerensky. Just tell them how you feel about the army and the country.'" — PN 014-047

After the Bolshevik takeover, [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] reflected that Brusilov too had once believed in her mission:

> "There was a time when even Rodzianko believed as I did, and Brusilov and Kerensky had thought that the self-sacrifice of the women would rouse the army." — PN 020-084

## Key Relationships

- **[[maria-bochkareva]]** — As Commander-in-Chief, Brusilov provided the military endorsement essential for the Women's Battalion's authorization.
- **[[alexander-kerensky]]** — [[alexander-kerensky|Kerensky]] appointed Brusilov Commander-in-Chief and worked with him during the summer of 1917.
- **[[michael-rodzianko]]** — [[michael-rodzianko|Rodzianko]] directed [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] to Brusilov, recognizing that military credibility required the general's support.
- **[[lavr-kornilov]]** — Brusilov's successor as Commander-in-Chief; both men struggled with the impossible task of maintaining discipline in a revolutionary army.
