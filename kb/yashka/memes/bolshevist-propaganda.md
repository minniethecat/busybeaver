## Overview

Bolshevist propaganda was the organized campaign of political agitation that the Bolshevik faction conducted within the Russian army throughout 1917. Operating through soldiers' committees, mass meetings, and printed materials, Bolshevik agitators promoted a simple and devastatingly effective message: immediate peace, land for the peasants, and the overthrow of the "bourgeois" officer class. In [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka Bochkareva]]'s memoir, Bolshevist propaganda appears as the corrosive agent that transformed disciplined soldiers into an ungovernable mob.

## Explanation

Bolshevist propaganda operated on several levels simultaneously. Printed leaflets and newspapers — often distributed freely at the front — presented the war as a capitalist enterprise in which soldiers were dying for the profit of the ruling class. Agitators embedded in every regiment held continuous meetings, arguing that peace could be achieved immediately if soldiers simply refused to fight. The propaganda exploited genuine grievances: the exhaustion of three years of war, the hunger at home, the visible inequality between officers' quarters and soldiers' trenches. Its core message — "peace, bread, and land" — required no ideological sophistication to understand, and its call to "turn the imperialist war into a civil war" provided a framework for soldiers' growing resistance to authority.

## Role in Narrative

[[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] encountered Bolshevist propaganda directly during her service at the front and recognized it as a mortal threat to the army's survival. She records that her own Battalion was deliberately targeted:

> "It was the first time I ever heard of the Bolsheviks. It was May, 1917. Bolshevist propaganda had already begun its work in the army, causing the desertion of several troops." — PN 014-012/013

The agitators operated freely, protected by the committees and by the revolutionary atmosphere that made it impossible to discipline them:

> "The Bolshevik agitators did not take the precaution of concealing their identity. They came openly into the barracks, distributed leaflets, and harangued the soldiers. The officers could do nothing." — PN 014-085

By the summer of 1917, the propaganda had thoroughly penetrated even units initially loyal to the government. A Bolshevik demonstration against the Women's Battalion revealed the depth of the indoctrination:

> "A long procession, with Bolshevist banners, flowed into the great square. The banners read: 'Down with the War! Long Live the Revolutionary Democracy! Peace Without Annexations or Indemnities!'" — PN 016-011

The corrosive effect extended to military justice itself, with convicted agitators being released back to their units:

> "The company that came to relieve us contained some criminals and a number of Bolshevist agitators. In a word, they were the worst elements the rear could produce." — PN 016-057

## Historical Context

The [[bolshevik-party|Bolsheviks]] were the only major political faction to oppose Russia's continued participation in [[world-war-i|World War I]] unconditionally. While the Mensheviks and Socialist Revolutionaries supported "revolutionary defensism" — defending the revolution against German militarism — [[vladimir-lenin|Lenin]] argued that the war was an imperialist conflict in which Russia's defeat would hasten world revolution. This position gave the [[bolshevik-party|Bolsheviks]] a unique appeal among war-weary soldiers. German military intelligence actively facilitated Bolshevik propaganda, recognizing its potential to knock Russia out of the war. [[vladimir-lenin|Lenin]]'s return to Russia in April 1917 via a sealed train through [[germany-wwi|Germany]] was itself a calculated German operation. By October 1917, Bolshevik propaganda had so thoroughly undermined military authority that when [[vladimir-lenin|Lenin]] launched the coup, no loyal force remained to stop it.

## Related Concepts

Bolshevist propaganda was the practical instrument through which [[bolshevism]] spread from a fringe ideology to a mass movement in the army. It exploited the institutional platform provided by [[committee-rule]], using committee meetings as its primary venue for agitation. Its explicit goal was the destruction of [[military-discipline]], which it framed as "[[counterrevolution|counterrevolutionary]]" oppression rather than military necessity.
