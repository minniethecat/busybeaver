## Overview

Mobocracy — rule by the mob — is the term that [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka Bochkareva]]'s editor uses to describe what [[bolshevism|Bolshevism]] became in practice. It denotes a political condition in which organized violence by crowds replaces lawful authority, in which accusation equals conviction, and in which the distinction between political justice and personal vengeance disappears. In [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]]'s memoir, mobocracy is not an aberration of the revolution but its logical endpoint: when all traditional authority is destroyed and no new authority commands consent, the vacuum is filled by whoever is most willing to use force.

## Explanation

Mobocracy differs from democracy in that it operates through direct intimidation rather than deliberation, and from tyranny in that it is diffuse rather than centralized. Under mobocracy, there is no single tyrant — there are thousands of petty tyrants, each exercising the power of life and death over those weaker than themselves. The soldiers' committee that votes to shoot an officer, the village [[soviet|Soviet]] that confiscates a neighbor's grain, the armed gang that searches a train for "counterrevolutionaries" — these are the everyday manifestations of mobocracy. What distinguishes it from ordinary lawlessness is its ideological justification: each act of mob violence is framed as an act of revolutionary justice, each victim redefined as an enemy of the people.

## Role in Narrative

The concept of mobocracy is introduced in the memoir's introductory essay as the central interpretive framework for understanding [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]]'s experience:

> "There follows a blood-freezing narrative of the rule of mobocracy that will live forever in the memory of the reader." — PN P02-011

This single explicit reference is programmatic rather than incidental: the entire memoir can be read as an illustration of mobocracy in action. [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]]'s account of the soldiers who turned on their officers, the mob that attacked her Battalion, the committees that countermanded [[military-discipline|military order]]s, and the [[bolshevik-party|Bolsheviks]] who declared that "all things are permitted" under the cover of democracy — all of these are instantiations of mobocracy.

The condition reached its purest expression in the period after October 1917, when even the pretense of legal process was abandoned:

> "The death penalty has become so customary here that nobody pays any attention. They shoot people in the streets, in the courtyards, in the cellars." — PN 023-042

This is mobocracy distilled: violence so normalized that it no longer requires justification, so diffuse that it no longer has authors, so pervasive that it has become the ordinary condition of life. [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] herself described the atmosphere of a capital city under mob rule:

> "It was evident at a glance that an atmosphere of depression weighed heavily on the Russian capital. The smiles and rejoicings were gone from the streets. There was gloom in the air and in everybody's eyes. Food was very scarce. Red Guards were plentiful. Bolshevism walked the streets openly and defiantly, as if its day had already come." — PN 018-125

## Historical Context

The phenomenon of mobocracy was not unique to the Russian Revolution. The French Revolution's Reign of Terror (1793–1794) had demonstrated how quickly revolutionary justice could degenerate into mob violence, and the Paris Commune of 1871 had shown how easily revolutionary committees could become instruments of terror. What made the Russian case distinctive was the scale and duration of the phenomenon. From the soldiers' mutinies of 1917 through the Red Terror of 1918–1922 and into the collectivization campaigns of the 1930s, mob violence was not merely tolerated by the Bolshevik regime — it was instrumentalized as a tool of state-building. The mob was the revolution's shock force; its violence cleared the ground on which the new state would be built.

## Related Concepts

Mobocracy was the practical reality that the theoretical promise of [[social-equality]] produced when implemented through revolutionary means. It was the negation of [[liberty]], replacing the rule of law with the rule of force. It was the antithesis of [[brotherhood]], transforming the ideal of human solidarity into the practice of class extermination. The [[death-penalty]]'s trajectory from legal sanction to informal ubiquity was both a symptom and a cause of mobocracy's advance.
