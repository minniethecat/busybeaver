## Overview

The formation of the [[womens-battalion-of-death|First Russian Women's Battalion of Death]] in the summer of 1917 was an unprecedented military experiment. Conceived by [[maria-bochkareva|Maria Bochkareva]] — a decorated frontline soldier — as a means to shame war-weary male soldiers back into fighting, the battalion recruited approximately 2,000 women volunteers. It received the personal authorization of War Minister [[alexander-kerensky|Alexander Kerensky]] and the endorsement of Commander-in-Chief [[general-brusilov|Brusilov]]. The battalion saw combat on the Southwestern Front in July 1917, proving that women could fight — but failing in its larger purpose of reviving the army's morale.

## Background

By June 1917, the Russian army was dissolving. Soldiers' committees countermanded officers' orders. Desertion was epidemic. [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka Bochkareva]], a veteran of three years of trench warfare, argued that an example was needed:

> "I was further asked how Kerensky then stood with the soldiers, and I replied that the men needed an example. Form a battalion of women and the men will be shamed into fighting." — PN 014-016

## Course of Events

[[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] arrived in [[petrograd|Petrograd]] and, through [[michael-rodzianko|Michael Rodzianko]], gained access to the political elite. She met [[alexander-kerensky|Kerensky]] at the [[winter-palace|Winter Palace]]:

> "It was Kerensky, at that moment the idol of the Russian democracy and the most powerful man in the land." — PN 014-041

[[alexander-kerensky|Kerensky]] granted her authority:

> "I authorize you to form, in the name of the Russian Government, the first women's battalion for service at the front." — PN 014-043

Recruitment began at the [[home-for-invalids|Home for Invalids]]. Roughly 2,000 women volunteered — peasants, workers, students, and soldiers' widows. [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] imposed iron discipline. At the Marinsky Theatre, she addressed an assembly of political and military leaders.

The battalion was dedicated at St. Isaac's Cathedral, with [[alexander-kerensky|Kerensky]] personally pinning epaulets on [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]]'s shoulders:

> "Kerensky then pinned the epaulets of a sub-lieutenant of the Russian Army on my shoulders, finishing with a 'Long live Bochkareva!'" — PN 016-047

## Aftermath

The battalion departed for the front in late July 1917. It saw action during the [[alexander-kerensky|Kerensky]] Offensive, suffering casualties but holding its positions while male units fled. However, the battalion's symbolic purpose — shaming men back to the front — failed. The army's disintegration accelerated. After the Bolshevik takeover, the battalion was disbanded, and [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] was arrested.

## Yashka's Perspective

As founder and commander, [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]]'s perspective is the memoir's core. She saw the battalion as an act of patriotism, not feminism:

> "I did not form this battalion to make a political statement. I formed it because Russia was dying and I could not stand by." — PN 015-050
