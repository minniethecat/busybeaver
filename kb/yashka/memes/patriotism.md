## Overview

Patriotism — love of the Motherland (Rodina) — was the driving force of [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka Bochkareva]]'s life and the central theme of her memoir. It was the reason she enlisted after her husband's death, the motive for organizing the [[womens-battalion-of-death|Women's Battalion of Death]], and the standard by which she judged the behavior of soldiers, officers, and politicians alike. In the revolutionary atmosphere of 1917, patriotism became a contested concept: was it patriotic to continue fighting a war that was destroying the nation, or to demand peace and land reform? [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]]'s memoir is an extended answer to that question.

## Explanation

In [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]]'s understanding, patriotism was not sentiment but action. A patriot did not merely feel love for Russia — she fought for it, sacrificed for it, and if necessary died for it. This conception of patriotism was rooted in the peasant soldier's traditional identification with the land and the Orthodox faith, but [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] gave it a modern, nationalistic inflection: the Motherland was not the tsar's property but the people's inheritance. This is why the [[february-revolution|February Revolution]] did not diminish her patriotism — if anything, it intensified it, because now Russia belonged to its people. But when those same people, in the name of liberty, chose to abandon the front, [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]]'s patriotism placed her in direct opposition to the democratic will she claimed to serve.

## Role in Narrative

The memoir's introduction emphasizes that [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]]'s story transcends ordinary political loyalties because it is grounded in a deeper patriotism:

> "Readers will find in this book, not a propagandist document, but a human record, overflowing with good nature and patriotism, and giving an account of how the Russian army at the front reacted to the Revolution." — PN P02-008

[[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] herself frames her mission as an appeal to the patriotism of Russian men. Her Battalion was conceived as a reproach to those who had abandoned their duty:

> "I hoped that women setting an example of noble sacrifice, of unselfish patriotism, of purity and honour and love of country, would make the men realize what they had forgotten." — PN 015-014

The patriotic demonstration at St. Isaac's Cathedral represented a fleeting moment when patriotism seemed capable of prevailing over revolutionary defeatism. [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] records the emotional peak:

> "What those thousands of Russian soldiers, assembled in the neighbourhood of the St. Isaac's Cathedral, felt on June 25, 1917, was the thrill that comes from self-sacrifice for the truth, from unselfish devotion to the Motherland, from lofty idealism. It convinced me that the millions of Russian soldiers, scattered over their vast country, were amenable to the word of truth, and instilled into me faith in the ultimate restoration of my country." — PN 016-050

But the moment passed. The patriotic impulse, once kindled, was not sustained. [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] would record bitterly that patriotism, like discipline, had been drowned in the flood of revolutionary rhetoric and Bolshevik propaganda.

## Historical Context

Patriotism in [[world-war-i|World War I]] Russia was initially widespread, with massive patriotic demonstrations in 1914. The war was framed as a defense of Slavic civilization against German militarism. But as casualties mounted into the millions and the home front descended into hunger and chaos, patriotic enthusiasm gave way to war-weariness. The [[bolshevik-party|Bolsheviks]] explicitly rejected patriotism as a bourgeois ideology, arguing that workers had no country. After seizing power, they would suppress patriotic discourse until 1941, when the German invasion forced Stalin to rehabilitate the language of Motherland and national defense.

## Related Concepts

Patriotism was the motivating force behind [[self-sacrifice]], providing the moral reason for which sacrifice was made. It stood in tension with [[liberty]], which many soldiers interpreted as [[liberty|freedom]] from patriotic obligation. The destruction of [[military-discipline]] severed the institutional mechanism through which patriotism had been channeled into effective military action.
