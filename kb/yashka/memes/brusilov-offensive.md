## Overview

The Brusilov Offensive of June-September 1916 was the Russian Empire's most successful military operation of the [[world-war-i|First World War]]. Planned and executed by [[general-brusilov|General Alexei Brusilov]], it employed innovative tactics — short, accurate artillery barrages followed by shock-troop assaults — to break through Austro-Hungarian lines across a broad front in Galicia. The offensive nearly knocked Austria-Hungary out of the war and forced [[germany-wwi|Germany]] to divert troops from Verdun. However, Russian casualties were catastrophic, and the offensive's very success deepened the war-weariness that would fuel the 1917 revolutions.

## Background

By early 1916, the Western Front was deadlocked at Verdun and the Somme. The Allies pressed Russia to launch an offensive to relieve pressure. Previous Russian offensives had failed with immense losses. [[general-brusilov|Brusilov]], commanding the Southwestern Front, proposed an unconventional approach: attack simultaneously at multiple points along a broad front, preventing the enemy from concentrating reserves.

## Course of Events

The offensive opened on June 4, 1916. [[general-brusilov|Brusilov]]'s armies broke through the Austro-Hungarian lines across a 300-mile front, advancing up to 60 miles in some sectors. Hundreds of thousands of prisoners were captured. But German reinforcements stabilized the front by August, and Russian casualties exceeded one million. The offensive continued into September before exhaustion and logistics forced a halt.

## Aftermath

The offensive demonstrated Russian military capability but at a devastating human cost. The losses accelerated discontent in the ranks. [[general-brusilov|Brusilov]]'s reputation soared, and he would be appointed Commander-in-Chief in 1917. The operation's tactical innovations influenced Allied planning for 1917.

## Yashka's Perspective

[[maria-bochkareva|Yashka Bochkareva]] was at the front during the Brusilov Offensive and participated in the advance. Her memoir records the experience:

> "We had been pushed back at this sector by General Brusilov for a great many miles. The comrades who fell in that advance were the bravest I ever knew." — PN 010-072

The offensive proved to her that Russian soldiers could fight brilliantly when well-led — a conviction that later drove her to form the Women's Battalion.
