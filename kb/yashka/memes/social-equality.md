## Overview

Social equality was the core promise of the Bolshevik program and the second term of the revolutionary triad. Unlike political liberty, which concerned the relationship between the citizen and the state, social equality concerned the relationships between citizens themselves: the abolition of hereditary privilege, the leveling of economic hierarchies, and the creation of a society in which a peasant's voice carried the same weight as a nobleman's. [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka Bochkareva]], herself a peasant, did not reject this ideal — she rejected its corruption. In her memoir, the promise of social equality becomes the justification for [[mobocracy|mob rule]], and the language of egalitarianism becomes a shield behind which the worst human impulses are indulged.

## Explanation

The demand for social equality in revolutionary Russia drew on centuries of accumulated grievance. Under tsarism, the nobility enjoyed legal privileges, tax exemptions, and exclusive access to high office. The peasantry bore the burdens of conscription, taxation, and corporal punishment. The Orthodox Church sanctified this hierarchy as divinely ordained. The revolution promised to invert it: the first would be last, and the last first. This inversion had a powerful moral logic, but it also had a dangerous implication: if all hierarchies were illegitimate, then all authority — including the authority of officers over soldiers, of judges over criminals, of teachers over students — was equally suspect. Social equality, pursued without restraint, became the enemy of all order.

## Role in Narrative

[[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]]'s memoir addresses social equality most directly in its introduction, where the editor contrasts theoretical [[bolshevism|Bolshevism]] — the promise of equality — with practical [[bolshevism|Bolshevism]] — the reality of [[mobocracy|mob rule]]:

> "With the claims of theoretical Bolshevism to establish an order of social equality on earth Bochkareva has no quarrel. She said so to Lenin and Trotsky personally. But then come her experiences with Bolshevism in practice, and there follows a blood-freezing narrative of the rule of mobocracy that will live forever in the memory of the reader." — PN P02-011

This distinction — between the ideal of equality and its revolutionary implementation — is the thread that runs through [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]]'s political observations. She does not argue that peasants should remain subordinate to nobles. She argues that the revolutionary method of achieving equality — the destruction of all authority, the encouragement of class hatred, the delegitimization of expertise and command — produces not equality but chaos.

[[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]]'s own life embodied a different path to equality: the peasant woman who became a decorated soldier and an officer not by tearing others down but by proving her worth. She records the intoxication of the soldiers when equality was first proclaimed:

> "Soldiers and officers were now equal, it declared. All the citizens of Free Russia were henceforth equal. There would be no more discipline." — PN 013-010

The abolition of rank and hierarchy — equality in its crudest form — meant the abolition of the army as a fighting force. [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] observed the consequences in the behavior of soldiers who took equality to mean the right to do as they pleased:

> "Was it for this that the revolution was made — to destroy the soul of the army, and to abolish discipline? The men were full of enthusiasm for liberty, but they understood it only as the right to do as they pleased." — PN 013-021

The revolution's tragedy, in her account, was that it chose the shortcut of destruction over the harder path of transformation.

## Historical Context

The demand for social equality was not invented by the [[bolshevik-party|Bolsheviks]]. The French Revolution's Declaration of the Rights of Man (1789) proclaimed that "men are born and remain free and equal in rights." Russian radicals had argued for decades that this principle was incompatible with autocracy. But the [[bolshevik-party|Bolsheviks]] gave equality a distinctive inflection: it was not merely a legal principle but a program of expropriation. Equality meant taking from those who had and giving to those who had not — by force if necessary. The Decree on Land (October 1917) abolished private ownership of land without compensation; the Decree on the Abolition of Estates and Civil Ranks (November 1917) eliminated all hereditary titles and privileges. These measures were enormously popular among peasants and workers, but they also established the principle that equality could and should be achieved through confiscation — a principle that would be extended, under Stalin, to the confiscation of life itself.

## Related Concepts

Social equality was the positive content of [[liberty]]'s negative [[liberty|freedom]]: liberty freed you from oppression, equality promised you a share in what was freed. It was the second term of the triad with [[brotherhood]], and its corruption — equality achieved through violence — produced [[mobocracy]]. The Bolshevik version of social equality was fundamentally different from the liberal version advocated by the Provisional Government: for [[bolshevik-party|Bolsheviks]], equality required the destruction of the bourgeoisie as a class; for [[revolutionary-democracy]], it required only the abolition of legal privilege.
