## Overview

Maria Leontievna Bochkareva, known universally by the diminutive "Yashka," was a Russian peasant woman who became one of the most remarkable figures of [[world-war-i|World War I]]. Escaping an abusive marriage and exile in [[siberia|Siberia]], she enlisted in the [[imperial-russian-army|Imperial Russian Army]] in 1914 — one of the first women to do so with the Tsar's personal permission. She fought in the trenches, was wounded multiple times, and rose to the rank of sub-lieutenant. In 1917, with the backing of [[alexander-kerensky|Alexander Kerensky]], she organized and led the [[womens-battalion-of-death|First Russian Women's Battalion of Death]], an all-female combat unit created to shame male soldiers back into fighting. After the [[october-revolution|Bolshevik Revolution]], she undertook a secret mission to [[lavr-kornilov|General Kornilov]]'s White Army and eventually traveled to the [[united-states|United States]], where she dictated this memoir.

## Biography

Born in 1889 to illiterate peasant parents in the Novgorod region, Maria Bochkareva endured a childhood of brutal poverty and toil. At fifteen, she was married to [[afanasy-bochkarev|Afanasy Bochkarev]], a peasant who proved to be an abusive drunk:

> "Afanasy's low intelligence was a sufficient trial. But his heavy drinking was a greater source of suffering to me. He made a habit of beating me, and grew to be unendurable." — PN 003-029

She fled to [[yakov-buk|Yakov Buk]], a Jewish trader who became her common-law husband, but he too abused her and was eventually exiled to [[siberia|Siberia]] for criminal activity. Yashka followed him into exile.

> "There goes Buk-Bochkareva!" people would point at me, with a mixture of scorn and pity and respect. — PN 004-043

### Military Service

When [[world-war-i|World War I]] broke out, Yashka resolved to enlist — an almost unthinkable act for a woman at the time. Her commander, impressed by her determination, suggested she petition the Tsar directly:

> "I therefore insisted on my determination to go to the front as a regular soldier. The Commander was deeply impressed by my obstinacy, and wanted to help me. He suggested that I should send a telegram to the Tsar, telling him of my desire to defend the country." — PN 008-024

With the personal intervention of Tsar Nicholas II, she obtained permission:

> "He came from headquarters with the news that a telegram had arrived from the Tsar, authorizing the Commander to enlist me as a soldier, and that the Commander wanted to see me." — PN 008-030

She served with the [[fifth-regiment|5th Regiment]] on the front, distinguishing herself in battle and earning the respect of her male comrades:

> "I am Maria Bochkareva, of the Fifth Regiment," I explained to the sentry. "I have been at the front for three years. I have been wounded three times." — PN 008-077

After recovering from wounds and partial paralysis, she returned to the front repeatedly:

> "You can't go to the trenches, Bochkareva," my Commander addressed me upon his return from the Commandant. — PN 009-009

### The Women's Battalion

In 1917, as the army disintegrated under the influence of revolutionary committees, Yashka conceived the idea of an all-female combat unit:

> "I was further asked how Kerensky then stood with the soldiers, and I replied that the men needed an example. Form a battalion of women and the men will be shamed into fighting." — PN 014-016

With [[alexander-kerensky|Kerensky]]'s authorization, she recruited and trained approximately 2,000 women volunteers. The battalion saw action on the Southwestern Front in July 1917.

### Post-Revolution Journey

After the Bolshevik takeover, Yashka was arrested, escaped, and undertook a harrowing journey to Kornilov's White Army. A fellow traveler told her of a route through the front lines:

> "Sometimes the peasants of the neighbouring villages are allowed by both sides to pass through to Novocherkassk, Kornilov's headquarters." — PN 021-133

She crossed the front and reached Kornilov's line:

> "I am going to see General Kornilov," I said, laughing. — PN 022-019

She later traveled to the [[united-states|United States]], where she met President Wilson and dictated this memoir. Returning to Russia, she was captured by the [[bolshevik-party|Bolsheviks]] and executed by firing squad in 1920.

## Role in Narrative

As both author and protagonist, Maria Bochkareva is the memoir's central figure. The narrative is organized around her journey from peasant girl to decorated soldier to political actor caught in the vortex of revolution. Her personal courage and moral clarity provide the lens through which the reader witnesses the collapse of the Russian Empire.

Yashka's self-portrayal is consistent: she is a patriot who loves Russia, a soldier who believes in discipline and duty, and a peasant who speaks truth to power. She describes her encounters with the great figures of the revolution — [[alexander-kerensky|Kerensky]], [[vladimir-lenin|Lenin]], [[leon-trotsky|Trotsky]], Kornilov — with the directness of a soldier rather than the analysis of a politician.

## Historical Context

Maria Bochkareva's life (1889–1920) spanned one of the most turbulent periods in Russian history. Born into the rigid class structure of late Imperial Russia, she came of age during the [[russo-japanese-war|Russo-Japanese War]] (1904–1905) and the [[tsarism|1905 Revolution]], events that shook Tsar Nicholas II's autocracy and led to the creation of the [[state-duma|State Duma]].

Her military service during [[world-war-i|World War I]] placed her at the center of Russia's catastrophic Eastern Front campaign. The [[imperial-russian-army|Imperial Russian Army]] suffered immense casualties — over 1.7 million dead by 1917 — and by the time Bochkareva formed the [[womens-battalion-of-death|Women's Battalion]], the army was in a state of advanced disintegration. [[military-discipline|Military discipline]] had collapsed under the combined pressure of [[committee-rule|soldiers' committees]], [[order-no-1|Order No. 1]], and [[bolshevist-propaganda|Bolshevist anti-war propaganda]].

The [[february-revolution|February Revolution]] of 1917 toppled the Tsar and brought the [[provisional-government|Provisional Government]] to power under first Prince Lvov and then [[alexander-kerensky|Alexander Kerensky]]. Bochkareva worked closely with Kerensky during this period, gaining his authorization for the Women's Battalion. The [[october-revolution|October Revolution]] later that year brought the [[bolshevik-party|Bolsheviks]] to power, leading to the [[russian-civil-war|Russian Civil War]] (1917–1922) — the conflict that ultimately claimed Bochkareva's life when she was executed by the [[bolshevik-party|Bolsheviks]] in 1920.

Her memoir, dictated in the United States in 1918–1919, represents a rare first-person account of these events from the perspective of a woman soldier. It stands as one of the few English-language primary sources documenting the collapse of the Russian army from the viewpoint of a front-line combatant rather than a political observer or general.

## Key Relationships

- **[[alexander-kerensky]]** — War Minister who authorized and supported her battalion; their relationship soured as she witnessed his political weakness.
- **[[lavr-kornilov]]** — Her most trusted military leader, for whom she risked her life to cross Bolshevik lines.
- **[[vladimir-lenin]]** and **[[leon-trotsky]]** — Bolshevik leaders whom she confronted personally and whose propaganda she blamed for destroying the army.
- **[[michael-rodzianko]]** — President of the Duma, who championed her cause and introduced her to the political world.
- **[[afanasy-bochkarev]]** — Her first husband, an abusive peasant whose surname she retained.
- **[[yakov-buk]]** — Her second partner, a trader exiled to [[siberia|Siberia]], whom she followed into exile.
