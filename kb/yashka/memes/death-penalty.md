## Overview

The death penalty was the ultimate sanction of military discipline and the most contentious issue in the revolutionary army. Abolished by the Provisional Government in March 1917 as one of the first acts of the revolution, its absence removed the only mechanism by which desertion and mutiny could be punished. By the time of the [[october-revolution|Bolshevik seizure of power]], the death penalty had returned not through law but through practice — summary executions, revolutionary tribunals, and the casual violence of armed mobs. In [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]]'s memoir, the death penalty's trajectory from abolition to informal restoration traces the arc of the revolution itself.

## Explanation

In the pre-revolutionary army, the death penalty was the foundation of discipline. Officers could order the execution of soldiers for desertion, cowardice, or insubordination in the field. The system was brutal but effective: it created a binary choice — fight or die — that left no room for negotiation. The revolution abolished this sanction in the name of liberty and democracy, but failed to provide an alternative mechanism for maintaining order. Without the credible threat of death for disobedience, the officer's command became a request that the soldier was free to decline. The result was predictable: desertion rates soared, units voted to abandon their positions, and military operations became impossible.

## Role in Narrative

[[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] records the abolition of the death penalty as a fact that fundamentally altered the relationship between officers and soldiers:

> "The death penalty had already been abolished in the army. There was no longer any means of enforcing discipline. The soldiers knew this and acted accordingly." — PN 016-060

By the time of Bolshevik rule, the absence of formal capital punishment had been replaced by its informal ubiquity. The death penalty had not been abolished — it had been democratized:

> "Besides, the death penalty has become so customary here that nobody pays any attention. They shoot people in the streets, in the courtyards, in the cellars." — PN 023-042

This is perhaps the darkest irony in [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]]'s memoir: the revolution that abolished the death penalty in the name of humanity produced more executions than the tsarist army had ever carried out. [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] herself faced the demand for her execution from the soldiers she had once led:

> "The men demanded my instant execution. They crowded around the staff car, shouting for my blood." — PN 020-028

The difference was that under the old regime, executions were acts of law; under the new, they were acts of lawlessness.

## Historical Context

The abolition of the death penalty in March 1917 was one of the Provisional Government's most popular measures, reflecting the widespread revulsion against the tsarist courts-martial that had executed thousands of soldiers during the war. [[alexander-kerensky|Kerensky]] attempted to restore the death penalty at the front in July 1917, after the failure of the June offensive, but the measure was so unpopular that it was never effectively enforced. The [[bolshevik-party|Bolsheviks]] formally abolished the death penalty again in October 1917, only to restore it in June 1918 for "counterrevolutionaries, spies, and enemy agents." The Red Terror that followed would claim tens of thousands of lives.

## Related Concepts

The abolition of the death penalty was the logical consequence of [[military-discipline]]'s destruction — if soldiers could no longer be commanded, they certainly could not be executed. The informal restoration of capital punishment through mob violence was the practical expression of [[mobocracy]], in which the power of life and death passed from courts to crowds. The death penalty's trajectory mirrors that of [[brotherhood]]: both were revolutionary promises that the revolution itself made impossible to keep.
