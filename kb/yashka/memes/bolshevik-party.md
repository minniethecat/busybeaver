# Bolshevik Party

The **Bolshevik Party** — known in [[Maria Bochkareva]]'s memoir as "the Bolsheviki" — was the revolutionary Marxist organization led by [[Vladimir Lenin]] that seized power in the [[October Revolution]] of 1917. Through [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]]'s eyes, the party appears not as a political movement with a theoretical programme but as a destructive force that exploited soldiers' war-weariness, dismantled military discipline, and imposed a reign of terror that she considered worse than the [[Tsarism]] it replaced.

## Origins and Ideology

The Bolsheviks originated as a faction of the Russian Social Democratic Labour Party, splitting from the more moderate Mensheviks in 1903. Under [[vladimir-lenin|Lenin]]'s leadership, they advocated for a small, disciplined party of professional revolutionaries who would lead the working class to power through insurrection rather than parliamentary means.

| Period | Status | Key Developments |
|--------|--------|-----------------|
| 1903–1914 | Underground faction | Split from Mensheviks; [[vladimir-lenin|Lenin]]ist organizational model |
| 1914–1916 | Anti-war agitation | Opposed [[world-war-i|World War I]] as an imperialist conflict |
| Feb–Oct 1917 | Rapid growth | Exploited Provisional Government weakness; gained army support |
| Oct 1917 | Seizure of power | Overthrew Provisional Government at Smolny |
| 1918 | Consolidation | Red Terror; civil war against White forces |

## The Army as Recruiting Ground

The Bolsheviks' most effective strategy — and the one most vividly documented in [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]]'s memoir — was their systematic penetration of the Russian army. As the [[Provisional Government]] under [[Alexander Kerensky]] continued the war, the Bolsheviks offered soldiers what they most wanted: peace.

> "Bolshevism is spreading rapidly in the army. The soldiers have lost all sense of duty and are ready to follow anyone who promises them an end to the war." [017-076]

[[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] describes Bolshevik agitators moving freely among the troops, distributing pamphlets, holding meetings, and turning soldiers against their officers:

> "There were emissaries of the Petrograd Soviet. Almost every day there arrived delegations bearing Bolshevik literature." [013-027]

The party's opposition to the war was not principled pacifism but tactical genius: every soldier they converted was a vote for capitulation and a recruit against the existing order.

## From Legal Opposition to Armed Power

The Bolsheviks' rise followed a clear trajectory in the memoir. In the summer of 1917, they were one faction among many:

> "The Bolsheviki were still a minority in the Soviet, but their influence was growing daily." [017-091]

By autumn, they dominated the streets:

> "Bolshevism walked the streets openly and Red Guards were plentiful. The Government seemed powerless to act." [018-125]

After the October coup, they became the state itself:

> "Petrograd seemed populated by Red Guards. Every corner had its armed sentry, every building its Bolshevik committee." [021-002]

## Bochkareva's Confrontation

[[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]]'s memoir records two remarkable personal encounters with the Bolshevik leadership. She confronted both [[vladimir-lenin|Lenin]] and [[leon-trotsky|Trotsky]] at Smolny, the Bolshevik headquarters, arguing for the preservation of [[military-discipline|military order]] and the continuation of the war against [[germany-wwi|Germany]]:

> "I faced Lenin and Trotsky at Smolny. I told them they were betraying Russia to the Germans. Lenin listened with a cold smile; Trotsky dismissed me with a wave of his hand." [021-050]

The encounter confirmed her worst fears. These were not patriots who happened to disagree about tactics; they were, in her view, agents of Russia's destruction — whether knowingly or through ideological blindness.

## The Red Terror

After consolidating power, the Bolsheviks unleashed what [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] calls the Red Terror — a campaign of arrests, executions, and forced disappearances targeting officers, landowners, clergy, and anyone associated with the old order:

> "Red Terror was rampant in Petrograd. The river was full of corpses floating downstream — officers and students, mostly, with their hands tied behind their backs." [021-073]

Her description anticipates the systematic nature of Bolshevik repression that would become fully apparent during the Civil War years. She documents the creation of the [[Cheka|secret police]], the establishment of concentration camps, and the practice of taking hostages:

> "It was a resurrection of the worst methods of Tsarism, but applied with a modern efficiency and ideological certainty that made the old oppression seem almost quaint." [023-070]

## Propaganda Methods

[[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] was a close observer of Bolshevik propaganda techniques, which she found both crude and effective:

| Method | Target | Effect Observed |
|--------|--------|----------------|
| Anti-officer agitation | Front-line soldiers | Officers stripped of rank, sometimes murdered |
| Peace promises | War-weary troops | Mass desertion, fraternization with enemy |
| Land redistribution rhetoric | Peasant soldiers | Soldiers abandoned posts to claim land |
| Class warfare language | Factory workers | Factory committees seized control of production |
| Anti-Allied propaganda | General population | Britain and France portrayed as imperialist exploiters |

## Assessment in the Memoir

[[afanasy-bochkarev|Bochkarev]]a's assessment of the Bolshevik Party is unequivocal and personal. She does not engage with Marxist theory or [[vladimir-lenin|Lenin]]'s political writings; she judges by results — the destruction of the army she had given her life to, the murder of officers she had served alongside, and the transformation of her country into what she describes as a charnel house:

> "Bolshevism has perverted the minds of the soldiers. They have become the cruel, stupid, and unintelligent enemy of the Motherland. All their talk of liberty is a mockery — they have created the most complete tyranny Russia has ever known." [022-031]

Her memoir ends with her escape from Russia, but her final judgments on the Bolshevik Party are among the earliest published by a Russian who had witnessed its rise from within — and who had paid for that witness with near-execution.
