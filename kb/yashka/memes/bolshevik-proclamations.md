## Overview

The Bolshevist proclamations and manifestoes were a body of propaganda literature produced and distributed by the [[bolshevik-party|Bolshevik Party]] among the soldiers of the Russian Imperial Army during the revolutionary year of 1917. These materials — ranging from printed manifestoes to handwritten leaflets — advocated for an immediate end to the war, the transfer of land to the peasantry, and the overthrow of the Provisional Government. [[maria-bochkareva|Maria Bochkareva]]'s memoir documents their corrosive effect on military discipline from the perspective of a front-line soldier who witnessed the transformation first-hand.

## Content Summary

The memoir records the content of the Bolshevist propaganda through its observed effects rather than direct quotation of the texts. The materials' central themes included: the war as an imperialist enterprise serving capitalist interests rather than the Russian people; soldiers' right to elect and dismiss their officers; the illegitimacy of the Provisional Government; and the promise of land redistribution. These messages were spread through printed leaflets distributed at the front, speeches by Bolshevik agitators who infiltrated military units, and proclamations posted in public spaces.

[[afanasy-bochkarev|Bochkarev]]a also records a personal counter-proclamation — issued by her upon returning from America in 1918 — which she caused to be posted in village squares and country churches across the Archangel region:

> "I am a Russian peasant and soldier. At the request of the soldiers and peasants I went to America and Great Britain to ask these countries for military help for Russia." — PN P01-033

> "Soldiers and peasants! Remember that only a full, clean sweep of the Germans from our soil can give you the free Russia you long for." — PN P01-036

## Historical Context

The Bolshevik propaganda campaign of 1917 operated in conditions uniquely favorable to its message. The Russian army, exhausted by three years of war, was receptive to any argument that promised an end to the fighting. [[order-no-1|Order No. 1]], issued by the [[petrograd-soviet|Petrograd Soviet]] in March 1917, had already stripped officers of disciplinary authority, creating a power vacuum that agitators quickly filled.

[[afanasy-bochkarev|Bochkarev]]a describes the mechanism of propaganda diffusion with the precision of an eyewitness. Agitators would arrive at the front, address assembled soldiers, and distribute printed materials. The effect was immediate and devastating to unit cohesion:

> "He [Lenin] allows Lenin to carry on his propaganda in the army without hindrance, to corrupt the soldiers, to destroy the front." — PN P02-011 (paraphrased reference)

The memoir notes that efforts at counter-propaganda were largely futile in the prevailing atmosphere:

> "It was impossible to organize a counter-propaganda, to convince the Russian peasant soldier that Bolshevism meant the ruin of Russia." — PN P02-022

## Significance

The Bolshevist propaganda materials represent a case study in the power of political messaging during a period of institutional collapse. Their effectiveness cannot be separated from the material conditions of the Russian army in 1917: hunger, exhaustion, and the breakdown of the [[military-discipline|chain of command]] created an audience primed for radical simplification. The memoir's account is valuable precisely because it is not a theoretical analysis but a first-person record of watching disciplined units dissolve into what [[afanasy-bochkarev|Bochkarev]]a termed "mobocracy" under the influence of these materials.

The counter-proclamation that [[afanasy-bochkarev|Bochkarev]]a herself issued — appealing to national unity across party lines — serves as a historical contrast, demonstrating the asymmetry between the [[bolshevik-party|Bolsheviks]]' simple, radical message and the more complex appeal of those who sought to preserve [[military-discipline|military order]] while acknowledging the need for political change.
