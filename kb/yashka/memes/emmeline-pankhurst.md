## Overview

Emmeline Pankhurst (1858–1928) was the leader of the British suffragette movement and founder of the Women's Social and Political Union. In [[maria-bochkareva|Maria Bochkareva]]'s memoir, she appears as a figure of international solidarity — a champion of women's empowerment who recognized in the Russian peasant-turned-soldier a kindred spirit. Pankhurst visited [[afanasy-bochkarev|Bochkarev]]a in revolutionary [[petrograd|Petrograd]], and later hosted her as a "valued friend" in London.

> "Mrs. Emmeline Pankhurst called her the greatest woman of the century." — PN P02-007

## Role in Bochkareva's Narrative

Pankhurst entered [[afanasy-bochkarev|Bochkarev]]a's life in the summer of 1917, during the peak period of the [[womens-battalion-of-death|Women's Battalion of Death]]. She visited [[petrograd|Petrograd]] accompanied by Princess Kikuatova, and [[afanasy-bochkarev|Bochkarev]]a records the encounter matter-of-factly — Pankhurst was one of "the two callers" who came to see the woman who had organized an all-female combat unit (PN P02-014).

The relationship endured beyond the revolutionary year. After [[afanasy-bochkarev|Bochkarev]]a's escape from Bolshevik Russia, she found refuge at Pankhurst's London home:

> "I had found refuge at the home of my valued friend, Mrs. Emmeline Pankhurst in London." — PN P02-024

Pankhurst's assessment of [[afanasy-bochkarev|Bochkarev]]a — "the greatest woman of the century" — was cited by Isaac Don Levine in the memoir's Introduction, where he compared [[afanasy-bochkarev|Bochkarev]]a to Joan of Arc. This endorsement from the world's most famous feminist leader of the era lent international credibility to [[afanasy-bochkarev|Bochkarev]]a's cause and her memoir.

## Historical Significance

Pankhurst's connection to [[afanasy-bochkarev|Bochkarev]]a bridges two distinct currents of early 20th-century women's history: the constitutional and militant suffragette campaigns in Britain, and the unprecedented military role of women in revolutionary Russia. Their meeting in 1917 represents a convergence of the political fight for women's suffrage with the practical demonstration of women's capacity for combat leadership.

Pankhurst, who had suspended her suffrage campaign to support the British war effort in 1914, saw in [[afanasy-bochkarev|Bochkarev]]a's battalion not merely a military innovation but a proof of the broader claim that women were capable of full citizenship — including its ultimate obligation, the defense of the nation.
