# Women's Battalion of Death

The **Women's Battalion of Death** was the first all-female combat unit in modern European military history, formed in [[Petrograd]] in June 1917 by [[Maria Bochkareva]] with the backing of the [[Provisional Government]] under [[Alexander Kerensky]]. Its explicit purpose was twofold: to set an example of patriotic self-sacrifice that would shame Russian soldiers into returning to the front, and to serve as an effective combat unit in its own right. The Battalion became an international sensation, but its military effectiveness was ultimately limited by the broader collapse of the [[Imperial Russian Army]].

## Formation

### Bochkareva's Vision

[[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] conceived the Battalion after witnessing the disintegration of [[military-discipline|army discipline]] under [[Committee Rule|committee rule]]. Her reasoning was pragmatic: if men would no longer fight, perhaps women could demonstrate what [[patriotism|patriotic duty]] looked like:

> "I had conceived the idea of forming a women's battalion. If the men would not fight, the women would show them how to die for their country." [014-013]

She insisted — and [[alexander-kerensky|Kerensky]] agreed — that the Battalion would operate without soldiers' committees, the institution that had paralyzed the regular army:

> "I made it a condition that there should be no committees in my battalion. I would run it on the regular army principles of unquestioning obedience to superior officers." [014-025]

### Kerensky's Authorization

[[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] petitioned [[alexander-kerensky|Kerensky]] directly at the War Ministry in [[Petrograd]]:

> "I authorize you to form, in the name of the Russian Government and of the Russian Commander-in-Chief, the first women's battalion for service at the front." [014-043]

The authorization was unprecedented. No European army had ever fielded an all-female combat unit, and the decision generated both enthusiasm and controversy.

### Recruitment

The response to recruitment exceeded all expectations:

> "Commander of the Military District of Petrograd. The same evening two thousand women volunteered for service." [014-066]

The volunteers represented every social class of revolutionary Russia:

| Social Background | Motivation |
|-------------------|------------|
| Peasant women | [[patriotism|Patriotism]], escape from rural poverty |
| Factory workers | Revolutionary idealism, steady pay |
| University students | Feminist conviction, desire to prove women's capability |
| Noble women | Traditional aristocratic sense of duty |
| Soldiers' widows | Revenge, desire to continue their husbands' fight |

After [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] imposed strict military discipline — early morning drills, no political meetings, absolute obedience — the initial flood of volunteers shrank dramatically:

> "Of the two thousand who had volunteered, barely three hundred remained after the first week of my discipline." [014-075]

## Structure and Training

The Battalion was organized on traditional military lines, explicitly rejecting the committee system imposed on the regular army by [[Order No. 1]]. [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] served as commander with the rank of sub-lieutenant (later lieutenant), awarded personally by [[alexander-kerensky|Kerensky]] at the battalion's dedication:

> "Kerensky then pinned the epaulets of a sub-lieutenant of the Russian Army on my shoulders, finishing with a 'Long live Bochkareva!'" [016-047]

The unit was trained at the Kolomensk Women's Institute under the oversight of [[General Polovtzev]], Commander of the [[petrograd|Petrograd]] Military District. Training included rifle drill, trench construction, gas mask procedures, and bayonet practice. The Battalion answered directly to the Provisional Government through the War Ministry and was assigned to the 10th Army on the Western Front.

## Baptism of Fire

The Battalion saw its first and most significant combat during the [[alexander-kerensky|Kerensky]] Offensive of June–July 1917. The performance of [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]]'s women exceeded all expectations and embarrassed the regular units on their flanks:

> "The Battalion maintained its position while the men on both flanks retreated. Of the three hundred women who went into battle, only a fraction returned unwounded." [016-057]

The women advanced under heavy German artillery fire, took enemy trenches, and held their ground while surrounding male units collapsed. The contrast between the women's discipline and the men's disintegration became a propaganda point for the Provisional Government — and a source of resentment among the regular soldiers.

## The Dedication at St. Isaac's

One of the memoir's most vivid set-pieces is the battalion's dedication ceremony at [[St. Isaac's Cathedral]] in [[petrograd|Petrograd]]. The event was a major public spectacle, attended by [[alexander-kerensky|Kerensky]], foreign military attachés, and a crowd of thousands:

> "The ceremony at St. Isaac's was the proudest moment of my life. The Metropolitan blessed our colors, and Kerensky spoke of us as the saviors of Russia's honor." [016-044]

Photographs of the ceremony circulated internationally, making [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] — and her battalion — famous far beyond Russia.

## Demise

The Battalion was ultimately destroyed not by the Germans but by the soldiers it was meant to inspire. During the Bolshevik consolidation of power in late 1917, the soldiers' committees surrounding the battalion turned on it:

> "The soldiers surrounding the Battalion demanded its surrender. They threatened to execute me as a counter-revolutionary. My own women formed a guard around me and refused to let them pass." [020-028]

[[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] narrowly escaped with her life. After the Bolshevik takeover, the Battalion was formally disbanded. Many of its members were persecuted, arrested, or executed as "Kornilovites" or "White Guardists." Some escaped with [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]]; others melted back into civilian life, their service a dangerous secret in the new [[soviet|Soviet]] order.

## Historical Significance

The Women's Battalion of Death occupies a unique place in military history:

1. **First female combat unit in modern Europe** — Predated by decades any other national experiment with women in ground combat roles
2. **Demonstrated female combat effectiveness** — The Battalion's performance under fire answered, at least temporarily, the question of whether women could fight
3. **Symbol of Provisional Government's desperation** — The very existence of the Battalion testified to how thoroughly the regular army had collapsed
4. **Counter-revolutionary symbol** — The [[bolshevik-party|Bolsheviks]] treated the Battalion as a symbol of bourgeois patriotism and persecuted its members accordingly
