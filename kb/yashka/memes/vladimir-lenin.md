## Overview

Vladimir Ilyich Lenin was the founder of the Bolshevik faction of the Russian Social Democratic Labour Party and the principal architect of the [[october-revolution|October Revolution]] of 1917. A Marxist theorist and revolutionary strategist, he returned to Russia from Swiss exile in April 1917 and rapidly transformed the [[bolshevik-party|Bolsheviks]] from a fringe group into the ruling party. In [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka Bochkareva]]'s memoir, Lenin represents the dark culmination of revolutionary chaos — a leader whose promises of peace, bread, and land seduced the war-weary masses while his methods destroyed the army and plunged Russia into civil war. [[afanasy-bochkarev|Bochkarev]]a confronted Lenin personally and recorded her impressions of the man she held responsible for her country's ruin.

## Biography

Lenin was born Vladimir Ilyich Ulyanov in 1870 in Simbirsk — the same town as [[alexander-kerensky|Alexander Kerensky]]. Radicalized by the execution of his older brother Alexander for plotting against Tsar Alexander III, he devoted his life to revolutionary politics. After years of exile in Western Europe, he returned to Russia in April 1917 via the famous "sealed train" through [[germany-wwi|Germany]]:

> "They are a group led by one Lenin, who has just returned from abroad by way of Germany, and Trotsky, Kollontai and other political exiles who have been permitted to come back." — PN 014-015

In November 1917, Lenin's [[bolshevik-party|Bolsheviks]] overthrew the Provisional Government. As Chairman of the [[council-of-peoples-commissars|Council of People's Commissars]], he presided over Russia's withdrawal from [[world-war-i|World War I]], the redistribution of land, and the establishment of a one-party [[soviet|Soviet]] state. He died in 1924 after a series of strokes.

## Role in Narrative

Lenin appears in [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]]'s memoir as both a distant propagandist and a personal adversary. His name is invoked by soldiers as a rallying cry and a curse in equal measure. The memoir records how [[alexander-kerensky|Kerensky]]'s government allowed Lenin to operate freely in the army — a decision that [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] and [[lavr-kornilov|General Kornilov]] viewed as suicidal:

> "'Today he allows Lenin to carry on his propaganda in the army without hindrance. Tomorrow Lenin will have got the upper hand, and everything will be wrecked.'" — PN 017-076

### The Personal Encounter

The most vivid passage concerning Lenin is [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]]'s account of being brought before him and [[leon-trotsky|Trotsky]] after her arrest. Lenin began the encounter with an apology:

> "Lenin apologized for my arrest, explaining that there had been a misunderstanding." — PN 021-023

But the conversation quickly revealed the doctrinal gulf between them. When [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] spoke of patriotism and duty, Lenin and [[leon-trotsky|Trotsky]] responded with amusement:

> "Lenin and Trotsky laughed. I could not understand what was so amusing. I had spoken from my heart." — PN 021-032

[[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] recorded her impression of Lenin's ideological blindness:

> "Perhaps surpassing all else in interest is the horrible picture we get of Bolshevism in action. With the claims of theoretical Bolshevism to establish an order of social equality on earth Bochkareva has no quarrel. She said so to Lenin and Trotsky personally. But then come her experiences with Bolshevism in practice, and there follows a blood-freezing narrative of the rule of mobocracy." — PN P02-011

### Lenin's Regime in Practice

Throughout her journey across revolutionary Russia, [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] heard ordinary people curse Lenin's name. The promises of peace had brought only a harsher war:

> "They wanted an end to the war, but Lenin has got us into a worse position than when we were fighting the Germans." — PN 024-080

In [[moscow|Moscow]], she encountered the reality of Lenin's government:

> "I could no longer sympathize any longer with Lenin's regime. He had left four sons at the front, sacrificed them to his ideas." — PN 024-138

And she channeled the rage of the silenced:

> "I would, myself, shoot Lenin and Trotsky for this outrageous betrayal of Russia!" — PN 024-015

## Key Relationships

- **[[leon-trotsky]]** — Lenin's closest collaborator in the [[october-revolution|Bolshevik seizure of power]], with whom he jointly confronted and interrogated [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]].
- **[[alexander-kerensky]]** — Lenin's political rival; [[alexander-kerensky|Kerensky]]'s government allowed Lenin to conduct propaganda in the army, a fatal miscalculation.
- **[[lavr-kornilov]]** — The general who warned [[alexander-kerensky|Kerensky]] about Lenin's growing influence and later led the White opposition to Lenin's regime.
- **[[alexandra-kollontai]]** — Bolshevik activist who, along with Lenin and [[leon-trotsky|Trotsky]], returned to Russia to agitate against the war and the Provisional Government.
- **[[maria-bochkareva]]** — The memoir's author, who confronted Lenin personally and devoted herself to opposing his regime.
