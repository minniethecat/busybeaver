## Overview

General **Lavr Georgiyevich Kornilov** was a career officer in the [[Imperial Russian Army]] who rose to become Supreme Commander-in-Chief under the [[Provisional Government]] in the summer of 1917. Of Cossack origin, he was known for his blunt speech, personal courage, and unyielding commitment to military discipline. In [[Maria Bochkareva|Yashka Bochkareva]]'s memoir, Kornilov emerges as the counterweight to [[Alexander Kerensky]] — the soldier who understood that without discipline the army would collapse, and who became [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]]'s most trusted military leader. Their relationship extended beyond the battlefield; after the [[Bolshevik Party|Bolshevik]] seizure of power, [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] undertook a perilous journey across revolutionary Russia to reach Kornilov's [[White Volunteer Army]] in the south.

## Biography

Kornilov was born in 1870 in Ust-Kamenogorsk, in what is now Kazakhstan, to a Cossack father and a mother of Kalmyk origin. He graduated from the Mikhailovsky Artillery School and the General Staff Academy, distinguishing himself as a military explorer in Central Asia and Persia. During the [[World War I|First World War]], he commanded the 48th Infantry Division and was captured by the Austrians in 1915, only to escape and return to Russia.

After the [[February Revolution]], Kornilov commanded the [[petrograd|Petrograd]] Military District before being appointed commander of the Southwestern Front. In July 1917, [[Alexander Kerensky]] appointed him Commander-in-Chief. But the alliance between the politician and the general quickly frayed over the question of restoring discipline to the disintegrating army. In September 1917, [[alexander-kerensky|Kerensky]] dismissed Kornilov and accused him of plotting a coup — an event known to history as the "Kornilov Affair."

After the Bolshevik takeover, Kornilov escaped from confinement and, together with [[General Alexeiev]], organized the [[white-volunteer-army|White Volunteer Army]] in the [[Don Region]]. He was killed in action in April 1918 during the assault on Ekaterinodar, at the age of forty-seven.

## Command Philosophy

Kornilov's military philosophy can be reduced to a single principle: without discipline, there is no army. His diagnosis of the army's collapse was blunt and unambiguous:

> "Kornilov's words were like sword-thrusts. He said that the army was being drugged by committees and politics, and that nothing short of a return to iron discipline could save the front." [017-075]

Unlike [[alexander-kerensky|Kerensky]], who believed oratory and democratic persuasion could sustain the war effort, Kornilov saw the soldiers' committees — created by [[Order No. 1]] — as the primary instrument of disintegration. His proposed solution — the death penalty for desertion, officers' authority restored, committees abolished — was militarily logical but politically impossible.

> "A soldier who has once cast aside his rifle and broken faith with his Motherland will never again take up arms in her defense." [017-076]

His hard line on [[Committee Rule|committee rule]] made him enemies among the revolutionary politicians even as it earned him the loyalty of officers and soldiers who still believed in victory.

## Role in Narrative

Kornilov appears in [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]]'s memoir from mid-1917 onward. Their first meaningful encounter came after his appointment as Commander-in-Chief of the Southwestern Front:

> "This was Kornilov. He spoke little, but every word was a gem, and his every gesture bespoke strength." [016-025]

### A Soldier's Bond

When Kornilov met [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] at the dedication of her battalion, he represented the army that [[alexander-kerensky|Kerensky]] was losing control of. He spoke plainly about what he saw and [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] instinctively trusted his military judgment:

> "He is an honest, straightforward soldier. I don't believe it of Kornilov. This is Kerensky's old game — suspecting everybody." [017-072]

### The Tsarism Question

A critical moment in their relationship came when [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] confronted the accusation that Kornilov sought to restore the [[Tsarism|Tsarist]] autocracy. She traveled personally to his headquarters to ask him directly:

> "I dreaded to think of a return of Tsarism. If Kornilov was for the old regime, I could not be for him." [017-070]

Kornilov's response — that he was no Tsarist but sought only to save Russia from anarchy — satisfied her. She emerged from the meeting convinced that his intentions were patriotic, not reactionary:

> "I want order restored. I want the army to fight, instead of debating politics in the trenches. Is that Tsarism?" [017-074]

### The Winter Palace Confrontation

[[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] was present for the fateful confrontation at the [[Winter Palace]] between [[alexander-kerensky|Kerensky]] and Kornilov. She witnessed Kornilov's directness clash with [[alexander-kerensky|Kerensky]]'s political maneuvering:

> "Kerensky heatedly rejected Kornilov's request." [017-103]

> "'Scoundrel!' exclaimed Kornilov, and he left Kerensky's study." [017-105]

After the breach, Kornilov confided his fears to [[michael-rodzianko|Rodzianko]]:

> "Kornilov told Rodzianko that if Kerensky carried out his threat he would become a traitor to the country." [017-106]

### Journey to the White Army

The most dramatic chapters of [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]]'s memoir concern her journey after the Bolshevik takeover to reach Kornilov's White forces in the [[Don Region]]. Branded a "Kornilovka" — a follower of Kornilov — she faced constant danger:

> "'She's a Kornilovka! She is for Kornilov and the bourgeoisie!'" [021-098]

After escaping Bolshevik arrest, she finally reached Kornilov's lines. Their reunion was emotional:

> "Kornilov was notified by telephone of my arrival and held out his hand to me." [022-028]

Kornilov shared his strategic assessment of the national situation:

> "This, in substance, was Kornilov's view of conditions in Russia..." [022-032]

He gave [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] a letter of protection for her parents and a mission to carry news of the White cause abroad — a final act of trust between the general and the soldier.

## Key Relationships

- **[[Alexander Kerensky]]** — Kornilov's superior-turned-adversary. Their rivalry over military discipline and political authority fatally weakened the anti-Bolshevik forces.
- **[[Maria Bochkareva]]** — Kornilov was [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]]'s most trusted military leader. She risked her life to cross Bolshevik lines and reach his headquarters.
- **[[Michael Rodzianko]]** — President of the [[State Duma]] who believed in Kornilov's integrity and argued on his behalf before [[alexander-kerensky|Kerensky]].
- **[[General Alexeiev]]** — Fellow White Army general who worked alongside Kornilov in organizing the Volunteer Army.
- **[[Vladimir Lenin]]** — The Bolshevik leader whose propaganda in the army Kornilov warned [[alexander-kerensky|Kerensky]] about repeatedly.
