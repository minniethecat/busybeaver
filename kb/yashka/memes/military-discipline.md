## Overview

Military discipline — the principle that soldiers must obey officers' orders without question — was the foundation of all armies in the early twentieth century. In the Russian Imperial Army, discipline was harsh, enforced by corporal punishment and the threat of execution. After the [[february-revolution|February Revolution of 1917]], military discipline was abolished by revolutionary decree and replaced with the committee system. [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka Bochkareva]]'s memoir is, at its core, a sustained argument that the destruction of military discipline doomed both the army and the democratic revolution it was meant to serve.

## Explanation

Traditional military discipline operated through two mechanisms: the officer's unquestioned authority to command and the soldier's unconditional duty to obey. This system was maintained by a strict code of military law that prescribed severe penalties — including execution — for disobedience, desertion, or insubordination. The system was harsh but effective: it enabled the coordinated movement of millions of men, the execution of complex battlefield maneuvers, and the maintenance of order under conditions of extreme stress. When the [[petrograd-soviet|Petrograd Soviet]] abolished this system through [[order-no-1|Order No. 1]], it removed the mechanism by which armies function without replacing it with anything workable. The result was not a more democratic army but an army that could no longer fight.

## Role in Narrative

[[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]]'s experience with military discipline was formative. She served in the pre-revolutionary army, where discipline, though severe, produced effective soldiers. She recalls the rigorous standards:

> "The discipline was so rigorous that no one dared to disobey. Orders were carried out instantly and without question. That was the secret of the old army's strength." — PN 010-007/056

When [[order-no-1|Order No. 1]] arrived, she witnessed the immediate and catastrophic consequences:

> "There would be no more discipline. The hated officers were enemies of the people and should no longer be obeyed and kept at their posts. The common soldier would now rule the army." — PN 013-010

When organizing the [[womens-battalion-of-death|Women's Battalion of Death]], [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] made the restoration of discipline her central demand:

> "I said: 'This organization should have no committees and be run on the regular army principles. That is the only way to help towards the restoration of discipline.'" — PN 014-025

Her insistence on traditional military order distinguished her Battalion from every other unit and earned her the enmity of the revolutionary committees. Yet even her disciplined unit could not survive the surrounding chaos.

## Historical Context

The erosion of military discipline in 1917 had multiple causes. The pre-war officer corps was drawn disproportionately from the nobility, while the soldiers were overwhelmingly peasants; the class antagonism inherent in this division was exacerbated by three years of catastrophic losses. [[order-no-1|Order No. 1]] did not create this resentment — it legitimized and institutionalized it. [[lavr-kornilov|General Kornilov]]'s failed attempt to restore discipline in August 1917 was the last serious effort to reverse the trend. After Kornilov's defeat, discipline collapsed entirely, and by the time of the [[october-revolution|Bolshevik seizure of power]] in October, no military force existed that could or would defend the Provisional Government.

## Related Concepts

Military discipline was the direct victim of [[order-no-1]], which abolished it by decree, and of [[committee-rule]], which replaced it with a system of collective deliberation incompatible with command. Its defenders — including [[Kornilov]] and [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] herself — were branded as [[counterrevolution]]aries. The [[bolshevik-party|Bolsheviks]], after seizing power, would eventually restore a version of military discipline in the Red Army through the reintroduction of the death penalty and officer authority, tacitly acknowledging the necessity of what they had helped destroy.
