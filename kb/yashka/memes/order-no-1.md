## Overview

Order No. 1 was the decree issued by the [[petrograd-soviet|Petrograd Soviet]] of Workers' and Soldiers' Deputies on March 1, 1917 (March 14 by the Western calendar), in the immediate aftermath of the [[february-revolution|February Revolution]]. The order fundamentally restructured the Russian army by declaring officers and soldiers equal, mandating the election of soldiers' committees in every unit, and placing weapons under committee control. In [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka Bochkareva]]'s memoir, Order No. 1 marks the decisive moment when revolutionary idealism began to destroy the army's fighting capacity.

## Explanation

The order's key provisions were radical in their departure from military tradition. It declared that soldiers and officers were equal citizens of Free Russia, that orders from officers were subject to committee approval, that weapons must be controlled by company and battalion committees rather than officers, and that soldiers were to elect representatives to the [[petrograd-soviet|Petrograd Soviet]]. While its stated purpose was to democratize the army and prevent [[counterrevolution|counterrevolutionary]] officers from using troops against the revolution, its practical effect was to dissolve the [[military-discipline|chain of command]]. Officers lost authority to issue binding orders; soldiers gained the right to debate and vote on whether to obey. The result was the rapid erosion of discipline across the entire Russian front.

## Role in Narrative

[[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] describes the arrival of Order No. 1 at the front with vivid detail. The initial reaction was euphoric — the soldiers believed they had been liberated from an oppressive system:

> "Then came Order No. 1, signed by the Petrograd Soviet of Workmen and Soldiers. Soldiers and officers were now equal, it declared. All the citizens of Free Russia were henceforth equal. There would be no more discipline. The hated officers were enemies of the people and should no longer be obeyed and kept at their posts. The common soldier would now rule the army. Let the rank and file elect their best men and institute committees; let there be Company, Regimental, Corps and Army committees." — PN 013-010

The order's effect was immediate and catastrophic for military readiness. Discipline dissolved, and meetings replaced duty. The committees that Order No. 1 created became vehicles for [[bolshevist-propaganda|Bolshevik agitation]] and eventually instruments that countermanded any attempt to restore fighting order. [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] describes the immediate aftermath: the soldiers were intoxicated by the fine phrases:

> "We were dazzled by this wealth of fine-sounding phrases. The men went about as if intoxicated. For four days the festival continued unabated, so wild with delight were the men. There were meetings, meetings and meetings. Day and night the Regiment seemed to be in continuous session, listening to speeches that dwelt almost exclusively on the words of peace and freedom." — PN 013-011/012

The front became, in [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]]'s words, "a veritable lunatic asylum" (PN 013-013), with soldiers abandoning their posts to attend endless political meetings.

## Historical Context

Order No. 1 was drafted by the Soldiers' Section of the [[petrograd-soviet|Petrograd Soviet]], dominated by socialist intellectuals and radicalized soldiers. It reflected the deep distrust between enlisted men and officers that had festered throughout the war, exacerbated by the class divide in a military where noble-born officers commanded peasant soldiers. While the Provisional Government formally endorsed the order's democratic spirit, it attempted — unsuccessfully — to limit its application. Historians regard Order No. 1 as one of the primary causes of the Russian army's collapse in 1917, as it made coordinated military operations nearly impossible and opened the door to the [[october-revolution|Bolshevik seizure of power]] that October.

## Related Concepts

Order No. 1 was the founding document of [[committee-rule]], creating the institutional framework through which soldiers' committees would eventually paralyze the [[military-discipline|chain of command]]. It was issued by the [[soviet]], representing that institution's assertion of authority over military affairs in direct competition with the Provisional Government. The order created the conditions in which [[bolshevist-propaganda]] could flourish, as committee meetings became platforms for Bolshevik agitators.
