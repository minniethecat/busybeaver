## Overview

The October Revolution was the armed insurrection that overthrew the [[provisional-government|Russian Provisional Government]] and brought the [[bolshevik-party|Bolshevik Party]] to power. On the night of November 6-7, 1917 (October 24-25 in the Russian calendar), [[red-guards|Red Guards]] and Bolshevik-loyal soldiers occupied strategic points in [[petrograd|Petrograd]], culminating in the storming of the [[winter-palace|Winter Palace]]. The Provisional Government offered almost no resistance. [[alexander-kerensky|Kerensky]] fled. [[vladimir-lenin|Lenin]] proclaimed [[soviet|Soviet]] power.

## Background

The Provisional Government had been fatally weakened by the Kornilov Affair, which discredited [[alexander-kerensky|Kerensky]] and forced him to arm the Bolshevik [[red-guards|Red Guards]]. Soldiers demanded peace, peasants demanded land, and workers demanded bread. [[vladimir-lenin|Lenin]], from hiding in Finland, pressed the Bolshevik Central Committee to seize power before the Constituent Assembly could convene.

## Course of Events

On the night of November 6, Bolshevik forces secured bridges, telegraph offices, railway stations, and the State Bank. The cruiser Aurora fired a blank shot signaling the attack. By 2 AM on November 7, the [[winter-palace|Winter Palace]] was taken and the ministers arrested. That evening, the Second Congress of [[soviet|Soviet]]s approved the transfer of power. [[vladimir-lenin|Lenin]] issued decrees on peace and land.

## Aftermath

Russia withdrew from [[world-war-i|World War I]] via the Brest-Litovsk Treaty. The [[bolshevik-party|Bolsheviks]] dissolved the Constituent Assembly in January 1918. A devastating civil war followed, pitting the Bolshevik Red forces against the White armies of Kornilov, Denikin, and others. The [[soviet|Soviet]] state would endure for seventy-four years.

## Yashka's Perspective

[[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] was in [[petrograd|Petrograd]] during the October Revolution and recorded the moment with despair. She had visited the War Ministry just two days earlier:

> "Such was the impression made upon me by Kerensky and Verkhovsky two days before the Bolshevik uprising." — PN 020-010

She watched the army she had fought for disintegrate in triumph:

> "News arrived of the overthrow of Kerensky and the Bolshevists' victory. The soldiers around me cheered. 'We are going home! Hurrah for Lenin! Hurrah for Trotsky!'" — PN 020-032

For [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]], the October Revolution was not liberation but betrayal — the victory of the forces that had destroyed discipline and honor.
