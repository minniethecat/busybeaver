## Overview

The Soviet (Russian for "council") was a revolutionary institution that emerged during the 1905 Russian Revolution and re-emerged with far greater power after the [[february-revolution|February Revolution of 1917]]. The [[petrograd-soviet|Petrograd Soviet]] of Workers' and Soldiers' Deputies became the most influential of these councils, functioning as a rival government to the Provisional Government. In [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka Bochkareva]]'s memoir, the Soviet appears as a destabilizing force that undermined military authority, dissolved discipline, and created the conditions for the army's collapse.

## Explanation

Soviets operated as direct democratic assemblies where workers and soldiers elected delegates to represent their interests. Unlike the Provisional Government, which derived its authority from the Duma and pursued constitutional governance, the Soviets claimed to speak directly for the masses. The [[petrograd-soviet|Petrograd Soviet]] issued its own decrees — most famously [[order-no-1|Order No. 1]] — that bypassed and often contradicted the military [[military-discipline|chain of command]]. By establishing a system of soldiers' committees that answered to the Soviet rather than to officers, this parallel power structure created what contemporary observers called "dual power," where no single authority could govern effectively.

## Role in Narrative

[[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]]'s memoir traces the Soviet's influence from its first appearance in the army as a liberating force to its eventual role in destroying [[military-discipline|military order]]. The defining moment came with [[order-no-1|Order No. 1]]:

> "Then came Order No. 1, signed by the Petrograd Soviet of Workmen and Soldiers. Soldiers and officers were now equal, it declared. All the citizens of Free Russia were henceforth equal. There would be no more discipline. The hated officers were enemies of the people and should no longer be obeyed and kept at their posts. The common soldier would now rule the army. Let the rank and file elect their best men and institute committees; let there be Company, Regimental, Corps and Army committees." — PN 013-010

As weeks passed, the initial enthusiasm gave way to paralysis. [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] records that the soldiers' committees, empowered by the Soviet, began to dictate military decisions:

> "It is the Soviet, Kerensky and its other leading spirits that are responsible for the collapse of the army." — PN 013-075

By the time Bolshevik power consolidated, Soviets had spread to every level of society, becoming instruments of enforcement rather than liberation:

> "Every village had its committee or Soviet. They were supposed to carry on the work of local government, but in practice they functioned as organs of surveillance and control." — PN 021-052

## Historical Context

The first Soviet was formed during the 1905 Revolution in [[petrograd|St. Petersburg]] as a strike committee. After the [[february-revolution|February Revolution of 1917]], Soviets re-formed spontaneously across Russia, initially dominated by Mensheviks and Socialist Revolutionaries. The [[petrograd-soviet|Petrograd Soviet]]'s [[order-no-1|Order No. 1]] (March 1917) fundamentally altered the relationship between officers and soldiers, contributing to the army's disintegration. [[vladimir-lenin|Lenin]]'s slogan "All Power to the Soviets" became the Bolshevik rallying cry, and after October 1917, the Soviets became the constitutional foundation of the new Bolshevik state, though in practice power shifted to the Communist Party. The Soviet system would endure as the formal government structure of the USSR until 1991.

## Related Concepts

The Soviet system directly enabled [[committee-rule]], as soldiers' committees derived their authority from Soviet decrees rather than military law. The Soviets provided the institutional pathway through which [[bolshevism]] eventually seized power, as Bolshevik agitators gained majorities in key Soviet bodies during the summer and autumn of 1917. The concept of [[dual-power]] — two governments claiming authority simultaneously — was the direct political consequence of Soviet institutions existing alongside the Provisional Government.
