# Moscow

**Moscow** — the ancient capital of Russia and the empire's second city — appears in [[Maria Bochkareva]]'s memoir as a place of recovery, political intrigue, and ultimately escape. Less politically charged than [[Petrograd]] during the revolutionary period, Moscow provided [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] with medical care for her battlefield wounds and later served as a way-station on her perilous journey out of Bolshevik-controlled Russia.

## Hospital and Recovery

[[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]]'s first significant encounter with Moscow came after she was severely wounded at the front. Her injuries were so grave that only specialized care in the capital could save her:

> "Then I was sent on to Moscow, where I lay in the Ekaterina Hospital. The doctors said I would never recover the use of my right arm." [010-071]

The Moscow hospital period was a turning point. Hovering between life and death, she confronted the possibility that her soldiering days were over:

> "I lay in the Moscow Hospital hovering between life and death. I could not move even a finger." [010-079]

Her eventual recovery — against medical expectations — became one of the memoir's most striking demonstrations of her physical resilience.

| Medical Event | Location | Outcome |
|--------------|----------|---------|
| Initial wounding | Front line | Severe shrapnel injuries |
| Transfer | Moscow, Ekaterina Hospital | Specialized treatment |
| Recovery period | Moscow | Partial recovery of right arm function |
| Discharge | Moscow | Return to active duty |

## Political Crossroads

As the Provisional Government's authority crumbled in the autumn of 1917, Moscow became a secondary center of political maneuvering. [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] traveled there from the front to investigate rumors about Kornilov's political intentions:

> "I boarded a train for Moscow, where I had been invited to address a meeting of soldiers and workers." [017-077]

The Moscow she found was deeply divided. At a rally of women volunteers, she was shocked by the indiscipline:

> "When I saw the girls who had enlisted in the Moscow unit, I nearly fainted at the sight of their frivolity." [017-078]

Her meeting with [[General Verkhovsky]], Commander of the Moscow Military District, confirmed her growing fears about the army's disintegration:

> "General Verkhovsky, Commander of the Moscow Military District, was very pessimistic about the situation at the front." [017-083]

## Under Bolshevik Rule

After the [[october-revolution|October Revolution]], Moscow — like [[petrograd|Petrograd]] — fell under Bolshevik control. [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]]'s return to the city in early 1918 revealed the transformation:

> "I saw an official of the Soviet government in Moscow. He looked at my papers with suspicion." [024-108]

The city had become dangerous for anyone associated with the old regime or the White movement. Yet it was also the place where [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] found unexpected allies — the Vasiliev family, old acquaintances who sheltered her:

> "The only people I could go to in Moscow. They lived on the outskirts of the city and kept a low profile." [024-002]

## The British Connection

Moscow proved crucial to [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]]'s escape from Russia. Through the Vasilievs, she made contact with the British Consulate:

> "With the aid of the Vasilievs, I secured an interview with the British Consul in Moscow." [024-072]

The consul provided documentation that would prove essential for her journey across Russia to [[Vladivostok]]:

> "He found a letter from the Moscow Consul to his Vladivostok colleague, requesting that every assistance be rendered to me." [024-138]

This British connection — forged in Moscow — ultimately saved her life. Without consular protection papers, she would likely have been arrested and executed at one of the many checkpoints between Moscow and the Pacific.

## Escape Route

Moscow was the starting point of [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]]'s escape trajectory:

```
Moscow (British Consul) → Train east → encounters with [[red-guards|Red Guards]] →
[[vladivostok|Vladivostok]] → Pacific crossing → [[united-states|United States]]
```

Every step of this journey was threatened by Bolshevik authorities:

> "Orders had been received from Moscow or Irkutsk to detain me. As there was a change of Government just at that time, the orders got tangled." [024-142]

The bureaucratic chaos of the early [[soviet|Soviet]] state — departments issuing contradictory orders, telegrams crossing in transit — created the gaps through which she slipped.
