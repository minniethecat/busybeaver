## Overview

Liberty (svoboda) was the rallying cry of the [[february-revolution|February Revolution]] and the most potent word in the political vocabulary of 1917 Russia. The overthrow of tsarism was celebrated as the dawn of freedom: freedom from autocracy, freedom from the secret police, freedom from the rigid class hierarchy. In [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka Bochkareva]]'s memoir, liberty appears as both a genuine aspiration and a fatal misunderstanding — the soldiers who embraced it interpreted freedom to mean the end of all obligation, including the obligation to defend their country.

## Explanation

The concept of liberty in revolutionary Russia contained an unresolved tension. To the liberal and moderate socialist leaders who formed the Provisional Government, liberty meant constitutional governance, civil rights, and the rule of law — freedoms compatible with the continued functioning of the state and the army. To the soldiers and peasants, liberty meant something simpler and more immediate: the end of compulsion. Why should a free man obey an officer? Why should a free citizen fight in a war he did not start? The [[bolshevik-party|Bolsheviks]] exploited this ambiguity ruthlessly, equating liberty with the right to walk away from the front, to seize land, and to settle accounts with the old ruling class.

## Role in Narrative

[[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] records the intoxicating effect of liberty's arrival at the front. The soldiers greeted the revolution's promises with ecstasy:

> "Freedom, Equality and Brotherhood! How sweet were these words to our ears! We were transported. There were tears of joy, embraces, dancing. It all seemed a dream, a wonderful dream." — PN 013-006

The Commander's appeal that soldiers defend their "newly won liberty" initially produced a rousing response:

> "The Commander read to us the manifesto, which concluded with a fervent appeal to us to hold the line with greater vigilance than ever, now that we were free citizens, and to defend our newly won liberty from the attacks of the Kaiser and his supporters." — PN 013-007

But [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] quickly recognized that the soldiers' understanding of liberty was self-destructive. The men understood freedom as license:

> "Was it for this that the revolution was made — to destroy the soul of the army, and to abolish discipline? The men were full of enthusiasm for liberty, but they understood it only as the right to do as they pleased." — PN 013-021

The revolution, she saw, "had brought them the boon of liberty and equality" (PN 013-026) but had failed to teach them that liberty entailed responsibility. That failure would prove fatal.

## Historical Context

The [[february-revolution|February Revolution]] abolished the legal foundations of the tsarist autocracy overnight: censorship was lifted, political prisoners were freed, the Okhrana (secret police) was dissolved, and universal civil liberties were proclaimed. But the sudden removal of coercion without the establishment of new institutions of civic responsibility created a vacuum. The [[bolshevik-party|Bolsheviks]] filled this vacuum by redefining liberty as liberation from the old order's obligations — including the obligation to fight. After October 1917, they would replace the liberty of February with a new system of coercion far more comprehensive than tsarism had ever been.

## Related Concepts

Liberty was the foundational promise from which [[revolutionary-democracy]] derived its legitimacy. Its military application through [[order-no-1]] proved catastrophic, as soldiers interpreted freedom to mean the abolition of [[military-discipline]]. The [[soviet]] system initially presented itself as the institutional expression of liberty, though it would become its grave.
