# Bolshevism

**Bolshevism** was the revolutionary socialist ideology advanced by the Bolshevik faction under [[Lenin]] and [[Trotsky]], which seized power in Russia during the [[October Revolution]] of 1917. Through the lens of [[Maria Bochkareva]]'s memoir, Bolshevism appears not as an abstract political theory but as a lived catastrophe — a force that dismantled military discipline, turned soldiers against officers, and ultimately drove the Russian army into chaos.

## Historical Context

Bolshevism emerged from the split in the Russian Social Democratic Labour Party in 1903, when [[vladimir-lenin|Lenin]]'s faction (the "[[bolshevik-party|Bolsheviks]]," from the Russian word for "majority") broke with the more moderate Mensheviks. After the [[February Revolution]] of 1917 toppled the [[Tsarism|Tsarist]] autocracy, the [[bolshevik-party|Bolsheviks]] exploited the power vacuum created by the [[Provisional Government]]'s indecision and the war-weariness of the Russian people.

> "Bolshevism is spreading rapidly in the army." [017-076]

By the autumn of 1917, Bolshevik influence had penetrated every corner of Russian society — factories, barracks, villages. [[afanasy-bochkarev|Bochkarev]]a records with alarm how [[red-guards|Red Guards]] openly paraded through the streets and Bolshevik agitators worked systematically to undermine what remained of military authority.

## Core Tenets as Observed

From [[afanasy-bochkarev|Bochkarev]]a's account, Bolshevism manifested through several observable doctrines:

1. **Abolition of Military Hierarchy** — Soldiers' committees replaced officers' authority; orders could be debated or vetoed by rank-and-file votes
2. **Immediate Peace** — The [[bolshevik-party|Bolsheviks]] promised an end to the war with [[germany-wwi|Germany]], regardless of the cost to national honor or territorial integrity
3. **Class Warfare** — Officers, landowners, and the bourgeoisie were branded class enemies; violence against them was sanctioned
4. **Confiscation and Redistribution** — Private property was seized in the name of the people

## Bochkareva's Perspective

[[afanasy-bochkarev|Bochkarev]]a, a decorated soldier who had created the [[Battalion of Death|Women's Battalion of Death]] to restore discipline at the front, saw Bolshevism as the antithesis of patriotism. To her, it was a German-financed operation designed to destroy Russia's capacity to fight:

> "Bolshevism has perverted the minds of the soldiers. They have become the cruel, stupid, and unintelligent enemy of the Motherland." [022-031]

She confronted both [[Lenin]] and [[Trotsky]] in person at Smolny, arguing for the preservation of [[military-discipline|military order]]. Her memoir records their dismissive response — they saw her as a relic of a dying social order. Her subsequent arrest and near-execution confirmed her view that Bolshevism was fundamentally incompatible with the values of duty and country she held sacred.

## Impact on the Russian Army

The memoir provides vivid testimony of how Bolshevism transformed the Russian soldier:

| Stage | Soldier Behavior |
|-------|------------------|
| Pre-Bolshevik (1914–1916) | Obedient, patriotic, willing to die for the [[patriotism|Motherland]] |
| Early 1917 | Politicized, debating orders, forming committees |
| Late 1917 | Mutinous, fraternizing with the enemy, deserting en masse |
| 1918 | Turned against former officers, carrying out extrajudicial violence |

> "The horrible picture we get of Bolshevism in action. With the claims of freeing the people, it turned the honest soldier into a savage." [P02-011]

By the time of the Bolshevik coup in October 1917, [[afanasy-bochkarev|Bochkarev]]a estimates that the Russian army had effectively ceased to exist as a fighting force. Soldiers who had once defended their country now cursed Bolshevism — but only in private, for public dissent invited execution:

> "Soldiers were actually cursing Bolshevism, denouncing Lenin and Trotsky as German spies." [024-030]

## Legacy in the Memoir

[[afanasy-bochkarev|Bochkarev]]a's account ends with her escape from Bolshevik Russia in 1918, but her descriptions of Bolshevism's methods — the manipulation of soldiers' committees, the use of terror to silence opposition, and the systematic destruction of traditional authority structures — prefigure much of what later observers would document about early [[soviet|Soviet]] rule. Her memoir stands as one of the earliest first-person critiques of Bolshevism by a Russian who experienced its rise from within.
