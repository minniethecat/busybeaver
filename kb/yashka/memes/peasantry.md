## Overview

The peasantry (krestyanstvo) was the largest social class in Imperial Russia, comprising approximately eighty percent of the population. Russian peasants — muzhiks — were the human material from which the army was drawn, the class [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka Bochkareva]] was born into, and the constituency whose loyalties determined the fate of every government between the Tsar and the [[bolshevik-party|Bolsheviks]]. [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]]'s memoir is, in part, a portrait of the peasant soul: its capacity for endurance, its hunger for land, its susceptibility to propaganda, and its spasmodic violence.

## Explanation

The Russian peasant lived at the intersection of ancient tradition and modern dislocation. Before emancipation in 1861, most peasants were serfs bound to the land and to noble owners. After emancipation, they remained tied to the village commune (mir), which collectively owned the land and allocated strips to individual households. This system bred a distinctive mentality: fierce attachment to the land, deep suspicion of outsiders and the state, communal solidarity, and a fatalistic acceptance of suffering. When millions of peasants were conscripted into the army in 1914, they brought this mentality with them. They fought bravely when led well, but they did not fight for abstractions like "the nation" or "the alliance" — they fought for their village, their family, and their land.

## Role in Narrative

[[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]]'s identity as a peasant woman is central to her memoir. Her father, Frolkov, was born into serfdom:

> "My father, Borissovitch Frolkov, was born into serfdom at Nikolsko, a village in the province of Novgorod." — PN 002-002

Her husband Afanassi was a typical peasant soldier: "a common muzhik, of rough appearance and vulgar, with a red nose and dishevelled hair" (PN 003-023/024). [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] herself is transformed by military service, shedding her peasant identity for that of a soldier, yet she never loses her instinctive understanding of the peasant mind.

That understanding informs her critique of revolutionary policy. She knows that the peasant soldier will not fight under a committee — he needs a commander he trusts, a cause he understands, and the promise of land at the end. The revolution's failure to deliver any of these is, in her analysis, the root of the army's collapse.

The peasants who filled the ranks were not political; they were practical:

> "We muzhiks were created to suffer and endure. That was our lot from birth." — PN 003-042

When suffering became unbearable and endurance exhausted, the peasant soldier walked home — not as a revolutionary act, but as a simple calculation: if the government could not win the war or give him land, he would secure both by his own means.

## Historical Context

The Russian peasantry underwent profound transformation in the half-century before 1917. The emancipation of 1861 freed the serfs legally but bound them economically to redemption payments that lasted until 1907. The Stolypin reforms (1906–1911) attempted to create a class of independent peasant farmers, but most peasants remained in the communal system. [[world-war-i|World War I]] accelerated all these processes: conscription removed millions of men from the land, food requisitioning impoverished the countryside, and the collapse of authority in 1917 enabled a massive peasant land seizure that the [[bolshevik-party|Bolsheviks]] would later legitimize through the Decree on Land.

## Related Concepts

The peasantry's hunger for [[liberty]] was concrete rather than abstract: liberty meant land, not constitutions. The ideal of [[self-sacrifice]] that [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] championed drew on peasant traditions of communal obligation and Orthodox martyrdom. The [[bolshevik-party|Bolsheviks]]' [[social-equality]] promise appealed directly to the peasant's resentment of the landowning class, even as their eventual policy of forced collectivization would destroy the peasant way of life.
