## Overview

General Polovtzev served as Commander of the [[petrograd|Petrograd]] Military District during the summer of 1917, making him directly responsible for the military administration of the capital. It was to Polovtzev that [[alexander-kerensky|Alexander Kerensky]] delegated the practical task of organizing [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka Bochkareva]]'s [[womens-battalion-of-death|Women's Battalion of Death]] — providing quarters, supplies, uniforms, and maintaining order among the volunteers. Polovtzev's interactions with [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]], recorded in vivid detail in her memoir, reveal the tensions between military tradition and the unprecedented project of an all-female fighting unit.

## Biography

General Polovtzev was a career officer in the [[imperial-russian-army|Imperial Russian Army]] who served in the [[petrograd|Petrograd]] Military District during the tumultuous months of 1917. Though his life before and after the revolution remains obscure, his role as military administrator of the capital placed him at the center of the Provisional Government's efforts to maintain order. He was succeeded by General Vasilkovsky later in 1917.

## Role in Narrative

Polovtzev enters [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]]'s narrative as the officer to whom [[alexander-kerensky|Kerensky]] delegates her:

> "Kerensky's eyes fell on General Polovtzev. He asked him to give me all necessary assistance." — PN 014-043

### Practical Organization

Polovtzev was initially skeptical but became cooperative. He discussed with [[captain-dementiev|Captain Dementiev]] the scheme for organizing the battalion:

> "A conference was held immediately between Captain Dementiev and General Polovtzev, who made the following suggestion: that the women should be quartered in the Home for Invalids." — PN 014-044

When [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] applied for equipment and supplies, she went through Polovtzev's office:

> "I applied for these to General Polovtzev, Commander of the Military District." — PN 014-066

### Conflict and Tension

Relations between [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] and Polovtzev were not always smooth. The general attempted to impose a more conventional structure on the battalion, which [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] resisted:

> "'You must let us work as we see fit. The work thereby saved,' General Polovtzev tried to persuade me. But I would not listen." — PN 015-023

When three of her volunteers went to complain to Polovtzev about [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]]'s strict discipline, he supported her:

> "'I haven't done anything to her,' Polovtzev answered, amazed at this threat. 'She is your commander. I cannot interfere.'" — PN 015-061

Later, however, Polovtzev clashed with [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] directly over her insubordination to [[alexander-kerensky|Kerensky]]:

> "'I will kill you!' Polovtzev thundered at me, trembling with rage." — PN 015-076

Despite these tensions, when the battalion was ready to depart for the front, Polovtzev sent a telegram and a ceremonial banner:

> "Together with the telegram came from General Polovtzev there also arrived a banner, embroidered with gold." — PN 016-002

### Dedication Ceremony

Polovtzev attended the battalion's dedication at St. Isaac's Cathedral alongside [[alexander-kerensky|Kerensky]], Kornilov, and [[michael-rodzianko|Rodzianko]]:

> "The congregation included Kerensky, Rodzianko, Milyukov, Kornilov, Polovtzev and others." — PN 016-040

After the official dignitaries departed, it was Polovtzev who took charge of the remainder of the ceremony:

> "The high officials departed and General Polovtzev took charge for the rest of the day." — PN 016-048

## Key Relationships

- **[[maria-bochkareva]]** — [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]]'s military superior for administrative purposes, with whom she had a combative but ultimately productive working relationship.
- **[[alexander-kerensky]]** — [[alexander-kerensky|Kerensky]] delegated the Women's Battalion's organization to Polovtzev and relied on him to manage the practical details.
- **[[captain-dementiev]]** — [[alexander-kerensky|Kerensky]]'s adjutant who worked closely with Polovtzev on battalion logistics and administration.
