## Overview

Michael Vladimirovich Rodzianko was a Russian politician who served as President of the [[state-duma|State Duma]] from 1911 until its dissolution in 1917. A large, bluff, deeply patriotic man, he was one of the key figures who persuaded Tsar Nicholas II to abdicate and who attempted, unsuccessfully, to hold the center against both revolutionary chaos and reaction. In [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka Bochkareva]]'s memoir, Rodzianko appears as perhaps her most loyal political supporter — the man who first recognized her potential, introduced her to the corridors of power, and stood beside her through the most critical moments of 1917.

## Biography

Rodzianko was born in 1859 to a wealthy landowning family in the Ekaterinoslav region. He served as a cavalry officer before entering politics, and was elected to the [[state-duma|State Duma]] as a member of the [[octobrist-party|Octobrist Party]] — a conservative-liberal faction that supported the October Manifesto's constitutional reforms. He became Duma President in 1911 and held the office through the [[world-war-i|First World War]] and the [[february-revolution|February Revolution]].

During the [[february-revolution|February Revolution]], Rodzianko played a crucial role as head of the Provisional Committee of the [[state-duma|State Duma]]. He personally negotiated with Tsar Nicholas II and military commanders to secure the abdication. After the formation of the Provisional Government, he remained Duma President and continued to work behind the scenes, though he held no ministerial portfolio.

Following the Bolshevik takeover, Rodzianko fled to the Don region and supported the White movement. He died in exile in Yugoslavia in 1924.

## Role in Narrative

Rodzianko is one of the most sympathetically portrayed figures in [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]]'s memoir. Their relationship began when [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] arrived in [[petrograd|Petrograd]], a wounded soldier seeking an audience:

> "With the address of Rodzianko on my lips I went about making inquiries." — PN 014-005

### First Meeting

Their first encounter set the tone for their entire relationship. Rodzianko received her with warmth and immediately grasped her potential:

> "President Rodzianko then appeared, exclaiming cordially: 'I have heard about you. You are the woman soldier.'" — PN 014-007

After hearing her story over dinner at his home, he took her to the [[tauride-palace|Tauride Palace]] to address the Duma:

> "After dinner Rodzianko took me to the Tauride Palace. There were many deputies in the great hall." — PN 014-020

He endorsed her proposal for a women's battalion:

> "Rodzianko approved of my idea. 'Provide an example to the men at the front,' he said. 'Form a battalion of women.'" — PN 014-024

### Patron and Protector

Throughout the memoir, Rodzianko acts as [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]]'s steadfast patron. When others doubted a peasant woman could lead soldiers, he defended her:

> "'Well,' said Rodzianko in my defence, 'she isn't such a helpless creature as you imagine. She has been through three years of war.'" — PN 016-031

He escorted her to meetings, arranged introductions, and visited her when she was recovering from illness:

> "President Rodzianko visited me the following day. He was the only one who came." — PN 017-058

### The Kerensky-Kornilov Mediation

Rodzianko's most pivotal role came when he tried to mediate between [[alexander-kerensky|Kerensky]] and Kornilov. He brought [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] to the [[winter-palace|Winter Palace]] for the fateful confrontation:

> "The three of us went to Petrograd to see Kerensky. It was on the eve of the great Moscow Conference." — PN 017-085

During the meeting, Rodzianko spoke with characteristic bluntness:

> "'Destroying Russia!' exclaimed Rodzianko, angered by Kerensky's arrogance. 'The blood of the people will be on your head!'" — PN 017-094

When his plea failed, he could only retreat:

> "Rodzianko and I moved to the exit. At the threshold Rodzianko turned once more..." — PN 017-097

### Lasting Trust

[[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] considered Rodzianko her most reliable ally:

> "I look upon Rodzianko as my best friend among the political leaders." — PN 017-071

Even after the Bolshevik takeover, she remembered that Rodzianko had once shared her faith in Russia:

> "There was a time when even Rodzianko believed as I did, and Brusilov and Kerensky had thought that the self-sacrifice of the women would rouse the army." — PN 020-084

## Key Relationships

- **[[maria-bochkareva]]** — [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]]'s most loyal political patron; he discovered her, championed her cause, and stood by her through every crisis.
- **[[alexander-kerensky]]** — Fellow politician whom Rodzianko tried repeatedly to convince to support Kornilov's reforms; their relationship deteriorated as [[alexander-kerensky|Kerensky]] grew more autocratic.
- **[[lavr-kornilov]]** — Rodzianko believed deeply in Kornilov's integrity and argued passionately for his military reform program.
- **[[general-alexeiev]]** — Fellow moderate who shared Rodzianko's despair at the army's collapse.
- **[[orlov]]** — The Cossack officer who presented [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] to Rodzianko at their first meeting.
