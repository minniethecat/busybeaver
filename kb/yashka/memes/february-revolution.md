## Overview

The February Revolution was the first of two revolutions that convulsed Russia in 1917. Sparked by food shortages, war weariness, and the collapse of public confidence in the [[tsarism|Tsarist autocracy]], mass demonstrations by workers and soldiers in [[petrograd|Petrograd]] escalated into a general uprising that forced Tsar Nicholas II to abdicate on March 15, 1917. A Provisional Government was formed under Prince Lvov and later [[alexander-kerensky|Alexander Kerensky]], while the [[petrograd-soviet|Petrograd Soviet]] emerged as a rival center of power — the "dual power" that would paralyze Russia until the [[bolshevik-party|Bolsheviks]] resolved it by force in October.

## Background

By early 1917, Russia had suffered catastrophic losses in the [[world-war-i|First World War]]. Over two million soldiers had been killed, millions more wounded, and the home front was collapsing under food shortages, inflation, and the breakdown of the railway system. The Tsar's personal assumption of military command in 1915 had tied the monarchy directly to military failure, while the influence of Rasputin at court had discredited the dynasty even among conservatives.

Strikes broke out in [[petrograd|Petrograd]] on International Women's Day (March 8 in the Western calendar, February 23 in the Russian). The strikes spread rapidly. When the Tsar ordered the [[petrograd|Petrograd]] garrison to suppress the demonstrations, the soldiers mutinied and joined the crowds.

## Course of Events

The revolution unfolded with astonishing speed. Within a week, the Tsarist police and administration had dissolved. The [[state-duma|State Duma]] formed a Provisional Committee to restore order, while workers and soldiers created the [[petrograd-soviet|Petrograd Soviet]] — a council of deputies modeled on the 1905 revolution.

Tsar Nicholas II, stranded at the front, attempted to return to [[petrograd|Petrograd]] but was blocked by mutinous railway workers. On March 15, his generals — led by [[general-alexeiev|Alexeiev]] — and Duma politicians — led by [[michael-rodzianko|Rodzianko]] — convinced him to abdicate. He signed the abdication manifesto aboard his imperial train. His brother, Grand Duke Michael, refused the throne the following day. Three centuries of Romanov rule were over.

## Aftermath

The Provisional Government inherited a disintegrating state. Its decision to continue the war, its delay in calling elections for a Constituent Assembly, and its failure to address land reform alienated peasants, workers, and soldiers. The [[petrograd-soviet|Petrograd Soviet]]'s [[order-no-1|Order No. 1]], which subordinated military officers to elected soldiers' committees, accelerated the army's collapse. The conditions created by the February Revolution made the [[october-revolution|October Revolution]] almost inevitable.

## Yashka's Perspective

[[maria-bochkareva|Yashka Bochkareva]] was at the front when the February Revolution erupted. Her memoir records the moment with a mixture of bewilderment and anger:

> "The miracle had happened! Tsarism, which had enslaved us and our ancestors for three hundred years, had fallen. But was it a miracle? The soldiers around me did not know what to think." — PN 013-006

[[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] was shocked not by the revolution itself — she had no love for [[tsarism|Tsarism]] — but by the immediate impact on the army. Soldiers interpreted the revolution as a license to abandon the front:

> "'We have overthrown the Tsar and we want peace,' the soldiers cried. But peace through desertion was no peace at all." — PN 013-035

Her memoir captures the paradox of the February Revolution from a soldier's perspective: the destruction of an oppressive system was welcome, but the destruction of the army's fighting capacity was catastrophic. The revolution freed Russia from the Tsar but opened the door to the committee system that would destroy the front:

> "Why do you continue the war?" asked our men. "We have overthrown the Tsar and we want peace, but your Kaiser insists on war. Get rid of your Kaiser and then both sides will go home." — PN 013-035
