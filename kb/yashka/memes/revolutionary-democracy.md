## Overview

Revolutionary democracy was the dominant political doctrine among Russia's moderate socialist parties — the Mensheviks and Socialist Revolutionaries — during the period between the February and [[october-revolution|October Revolution]]s of 1917. It held that the revolution had transformed Russia into the world's most democratic state and that the army must now be defended as the army of a free people rather than of a tsar. In [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka Bochkareva]]'s memoir, revolutionary democracy is encountered as a set of resonant but ultimately hollow slogans that proved incapable of sustaining either military discipline or political order.

## Explanation

The doctrine of revolutionary democracy rested on two contradictory premises. First, it asserted that the fall of tsarism had made Russia a beacon of liberty, obligating soldiers to defend their newfound [[liberty|freedom]] against German militarism. Second, it insisted that this defense must be conducted democratically — through committees, debate, and the voluntary consent of the governed — rather than through traditional military hierarchy. The contradiction was fatal: democratic deliberation is incompatible with military command, and the soldiers, once empowered to debate whether to obey, increasingly chose not to. The slogan "Long live the revolutionary democracy!" became a ritual incantation at meetings where soldiers voted to abandon their positions.

## Role in Narrative

[[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] encountered the rhetoric of revolutionary democracy at the front, where it served as cover for the disintegration of [[military-discipline|military order]]. She records a massive Bolshevik demonstration where the slogan was deployed against her unit:

> "A long procession, with Bolshevist banners, flowed into the great square. The banners read: 'Down with the War! Long Live the Revolutionary Democracy! Peace Without Annexations or Indemnities!'" — PN 016-011

The term "democracy" itself became a shield behind which indiscipline flourished. [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] describes how soldiers justified their refusal of orders and their abuse of authority under its banner:

> "There were those who acted with the utmost ruthlessness under the cover of 'democracy.' These sought no excuses. They simply took what they wanted and abused whomever they wished, claiming that in a democracy all things were permitted." — PN 015-054

The patriotism that revolutionary democracy was meant to inspire proved fragile. Within the same battalion that [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] led, she witnessed how the slogan of democracy was weaponized against discipline itself. The fifteen hundred women who initially volunteered included many who, in [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]]'s assessment, had joined under false pretenses:

> "The others were cowards, masquerading their worthlessness under the cover of 'democracy.' These sought no excuses. The prospect of complete self-sacrifice did not daunt them." — PN 015-054

The context makes clear that "democracy" had become a rhetorical device for avoiding hardship and duty — exactly the opposite of what its proponents intended. The revolutionary democrats promised both liberty and discipline, but the former swallowed the latter whole.

## Historical Context

The concept of revolutionary democracy was developed by socialist theorists in exile during the tsarist period and implemented after February 1917 by figures like [[alexander-kerensky|Alexander Kerensky]] and Irakli Tsereteli. It represented an attempt to reconcile socialist ideals with the practical demands of governing a nation at war. The Mensheviks, in particular, argued that Russia was not ready for a socialist revolution and must first pass through a bourgeois-democratic phase; revolutionary democracy was the ideological expression of this transitional stage. The doctrine collapsed under the weight of its own contradictions in the autumn of 1917, when [[alexander-kerensky|Kerensky]]'s government, having failed to deliver either peace or victory, was swept aside by [[vladimir-lenin|Lenin]]'s [[bolshevik-party|Bolsheviks]]. The very soldiers whom revolutionary democracy had been designed to mobilize for the defense of [[liberty|freedom]] had become, through the mechanisms that democracy had established, the instrument of [[liberty|freedom]]'s destruction.

## Related Concepts

Revolutionary democracy competed directly with [[bolshevism]], which rejected the notion that the revolution was complete and demanded the immediate transfer of all power to the soviets. It was institutionally expressed through the [[soviet]] system, which revolutionary democrats hoped to use as a vehicle for gradual reform rather than as a revolutionary battering ram. Its military application — [[committee-rule]] — proved to be its undoing, as committees became platforms for Bolshevist rather than democratic agitation.
