# Tsarism

**Tsarism** was the autocratic political system of the [[Russian Empire]], in which absolute power rested with the Tsar. For [[Maria Bochkareva]] — born a peasant serf, married at fifteen, and exiled to [[Siberia]] for political activity — Tsarism was the first tyranny she knew. Her memoir traces the arc from oppressing Tsarism through its overthrow to the bitter irony of seeing it replaced by a system she considered worse.

## The Nature of Tsarist Autocracy

Under Tsarism, the Tsar ruled by divine right. There was no parliament with real power, no free press, and no independent judiciary until the concessions wrung from [[Nicholas II]] after the 1905 Revolution. The secret police (Okhrana) suppressed dissent, and [[Siberia|Siberian exile]] was the fate of thousands who challenged the regime.

> "So deep-rooted was the institution of Tsarism that, with all their secret committees and their underground propaganda, the revolutionaries made slow headway." [011-074]

[[afanasy-bochkarev|Bochkarev]]a's description captures the resilience of the Tsarist system: it was not merely a government but a cultural institution woven into the fabric of Russian life, sustained by tradition, the Orthodox Church, and the peasantry's ingrained deference to authority.

## The Overthrow of Tsarism

The collapse of Tsarism in the [[February Revolution]] of 1917 was sudden and, for many, miraculous. [[afanasy-bochkarev|Bochkarev]]a, who had suffered under the old regime, records the moment with a soldier's bluntness:

> "The miracle had happened! Tsarism, which had enslaved us and flogged us like beasts, was overthrown." [013-006]

The abdication of Tsar [[Nicholas II]] in March 1917 ended three centuries of Romanov rule. For a brief moment, Russia experienced what [[afanasy-bochkarev|Bochkarev]]a describes as unprecedented [[liberty|freedom]] — soldiers and workers celebrating in the streets, political prisoners released, and the promise of democratic governance through the [[Provisional Government]].

## Tsarism vs. Bolshevism: The Memoir's Central Contrast

[[afanasy-bochkarev|Bochkarev]]a's memoir draws a sharp and painful comparison between the old tyranny and the new. While she never romanticizes Tsarism — she herself was a victim of its brutality — she finds the Bolshevik regime that replaced it even more oppressive:

> "You, the Bolsheviki, are worse than the Tsar. All your life you fought Tsarism. Now you are even worse than the Tsar's government." [017-102]

This accusation — delivered in person to Bolshevik officials — forms the memoir's moral center. [[afanasy-bochkarev|Bochkarev]]a measures both regimes by the same standard: how they treat the common soldier and the Russian people. By that measure, the Tsarist system, for all its cruelty, at least maintained order and national defense; [[bolshevism|Bolshevism]], in her view, delivered chaos, fratricide, and national humiliation.

## The Kornilov Controversy

A key tension in the memoir concerns General [[Lavr Kornilov]], whom [[afanasy-bochkarev|Bochkarev]]a supported as a potential savior of Russia. Her critics accused Kornilov of seeking to restore Tsarism:

> "I dreaded to think of a return of Tsarism. If Kornilov was for the old regime, I could not be for him." [017-070]

[[afanasy-bochkarev|Bochkarev]]a investigated these accusations herself, traveling to Kornilov's headquarters to confront him directly. She emerged convinced that Kornilov was no Tsarist restorationist — he sought a military dictatorship to restore order, not the divine right of the Romanovs. This distinction, however subtle, was crucial to her decision to support him.

## Legacy and Resonance

The memoir's treatment of Tsarism is not a work of political theory but of lived experience. [[afanasy-bochkarev|Bochkarev]]a never analyzes Tsarism as a system in the abstract; she knows it through the lashes on her back, the exile of her comrades, and the suffocating weight of a regime that treated peasants as property. Yet she also knows what replaced it, and her verdict is unambiguous:

> "It was a resurrection of the worst methods of Tsarism." [023-070]

This line — describing Bolshevik terror tactics — encapsulates her view: the revolution that promised [[liberty|freedom]] had simply dressed the old tyranny in new ideological clothes, adding modern organizational methods and a more comprehensive brutality.
