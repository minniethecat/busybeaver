## Overview

"Yashka: My Life as Peasant, Officer and Exile" is the autobiography of [[maria-bochkareva|Maria Bochkareva]] (known as [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] among soldiers), a Russian peasant woman who enlisted in the [[imperial-russian-army|Imperial Russian Army]] during [[world-war-i|World War I]], rose to command the [[womens-battalion-of-death|Women's Battalion of Death]], and became a central figure in the revolutionary turmoil of 1917. The work was produced when [[afanasy-bochkarev|Bochkarev]]a, unable to write fluently herself, dictated her life story in Russian to Isaac Don Levine, who transcribed it verbatim into English over approximately one hundred hours across three weeks in the summer of 1918.

> "Bochkareva recited to me in Russian the story of her life, and I recorded it in English in longhand, making every effort to set down her narrative verbatim." — PN P02-013

## Content Summary

The memoir spans [[afanasy-bochkarev|Bochkarev]]a's entire life up to 1918, organized into four parts. Part I ("Youth") covers her childhood of peasant toil, marriage at fifteen, and escape from [[siberia|Siberia]]n exile. Part II ("War") chronicles her enlistment — by personal telegram from the Tsar — her experiences in No Man's Land, and her wounding and capture by German forces. Part III ("Revolution") documents the collapse of military discipline at the front, her organization of the [[womens-battalion-of-death|Women's Battalion of Death]], and her mission as an envoy from [[alexander-kerensky|Kerensky]] to Kornilov during the Kornilov Affair. Part IV ("Terror") narrates the [[october-revolution|Bolshevik seizure of power]], her confrontation with [[vladimir-lenin|Lenin]] and [[leon-trotsky|Trotsky]], and her narrow escape from execution.

The narrative is distinguished by its immediacy: [[afanasy-bochkarev|Bochkarev]]a, semi-literate by her own account, speaks with a directness unmediated by education or ideology. Levine notes that the work originated purely as a personal document:

> "It must be made unmistakably clear here that the motives responsible for this book were purely personal. In its origin this work is exclusively a human document, a record of an exuberant life." — PN P02-025

## Historical Context

The memoir was created during a pivotal moment. In the summer of 1918, [[afanasy-bochkarev|Bochkarev]]a traveled to the [[united-states|United States]] seeking support for the anti-Bolshevik cause and met with President Wilson. Her decision to record her autobiography at this juncture served multiple purposes: to correct the sensationalized newspaper accounts that had proliferated about her, to document the disintegration of the Russian army under revolutionary committee rule, and to present her personal testimony against [[bolshevism|Bolshevism]].

> "At our first session Bochkareva made it clear that what she was going to tell me would be very different from the stories about her related in the press. She would reveal her innermost self and break open for the first time the sealed book of her past." — PN P02-015

The work was published in 1919 by Frederick A. Stokes Company in New York, at a time when Western audiences were intensely interested in first-hand accounts of the Russian Revolution. It provided one of the few female perspectives on the front-line experience of [[world-war-i|World War I]].

## Significance

The memoir holds value on multiple levels. As a human document, it records the extraordinary trajectory of a peasant woman who broke through the rigid hierarchies of [[tsarism|Tsarist Russia]] to command male and female soldiers in combat. As a historical document, it preserves vivid, nonpartisan testimony of the February and [[october-revolution|October Revolution]]s, the breakdown of [[military-discipline|military order]], and the personalities who shaped these events — [[alexander-kerensky|Kerensky]], Kornilov, [[vladimir-lenin|Lenin]], [[leon-trotsky|Trotsky]], and [[michael-rodzianko|Rodzianko]] among them.

> "The reader gets a picture of Kerensky in action that completely effaces all that has hitherto been said of this tragic but typical product of the Russian intelligentsia. Kornilov, Rodzianko, Lenin and Trotsky and some other outstanding personalities of the Russian revolution appear in these pages exactly as they are in reality." — PN P02-025

Contemporary observers recognized the memoir's importance. [[emmeline-pankhurst|Emmeline Pankhurst]] called [[afanasy-bochkarev|Bochkarev]]a "the greatest woman of the century," and a correspondent in July 1917 described her as the modern parallel to Joan of Arc (PN P02-021). The narrative's unvarnished depiction of [[bolshevism|Bolshevism]] in practice — as distinct from [[bolshevism|Bolshevism]] in theory, a distinction [[afanasy-bochkarev|Bochkarev]]a herself made to [[vladimir-lenin|Lenin]]'s face — provides a primary-source counterpoint to the triumphant revolutionary narratives that would dominate 20th-century historiography.
