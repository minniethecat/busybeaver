## Thesis

The collapse of military discipline in the Russian Army during 1917 was not a spontaneous moral failure but a structured process driven by identifiable mechanisms: the [[order-no-1|Order No. 1]] of the [[petrograd-soviet|Petrograd Soviet]], the institution of [[committee-rule|soldiers' committees]], the deliberate [[bolshevist-propaganda|propaganda campaign]] waged by the [[bolshevik-party|Bolshevik Party]], and the physical reality of mass desertion. Yashka Bochkareva's memoir documents each of these mechanisms from the perspective of a soldier who fought against them — and who ultimately lost.

## The Pre-War Army: Discipline Through Hierarchy

The [[imperial-russian-army|Imperial Russian Army]] that entered [[world-war-i|World War I]] in 1914 was the largest in the world, but its discipline rested on a fragile foundation: the absolute authority of the officer corps, most of whom were drawn from the nobility, over a conscripted peasant soldiery. This was the army Bochkareva joined as a volunteer. Her memoir's early chapters portray a force that, for all its flaws, functioned: orders were obeyed, positions were held, and individual courage was recognized:

> "I am Maria Bochkareva, of the Fifth Regiment," I explained. "I have been at the front for three years. I have been wounded three times." — PN 008-077

This was the army before the revolution — hierarchical, harsh, but coherent.

## Mechanism 1: Order No. 1

The [[petrograd-soviet|Petrograd Soviet]]'s [[order-no-1|Order No. 1]] (March 14, 1917) was the first institutional blow to military discipline. Issued in the chaotic days after the [[february-revolution|February Revolution]], it directed that:

- Soldiers' committees be elected in every military unit
- All weapons be placed under committee control
- Officers' orders be subject to committee approval
- Saluting and other disciplinary formalities be abolished

Bochkareva saw the order's effects immediately. The army that had fought the Germans for three years suddenly became an armed debating society. Orders that had once been obeyed without question were now "discussed" — and often rejected:

> "'Why do you continue the war?' asked our men. 'We have overthrown the Tsar and we want peace.'" — PN 013-035

## Mechanism 2: Soldiers' Committees

The [[committee-rule|committee system]] institutionalized the breakdown. Elected committees at every level — company, regiment, division, army — debated operational orders that in any functional army would have been executed without discussion. Officers who attempted to assert authority were denounced as "counterrevolutionary" and in many cases physically attacked or killed.

Bochkareva's memoir records numerous instances of committees overriding military necessity. A commander who ordered his men to hold a position against a German advance was overruled by the committee, which voted to retreat. Soldiers who had fought bravely for years were transformed by the committee system into a mob:

> "The soldiers now asked no one's permission. They simply left." — Bochkareva's observation on desertion (Chapter 13)

Committees were not inherently destructive — collective representation had legitimate grievances to address. But in the context of a war army under enemy fire, the substitution of democratic deliberation for command authority was catastrophic.

## Mechanism 3: Bolshevik Propaganda

The [[bolshevik-party|Bolsheviks]] understood that the army was both the primary obstacle to revolution and its most valuable prize. Their [[bolshevist-propaganda|propaganda campaign]] targeted soldiers with a simple, devastatingly effective message: peace, land, and bread — all immediately, all without qualification.

Bochkareva confronted this propaganda directly. She met [[vladimir-lenin|Lenin]] and [[leon-trotsky|Trotsky]] in person and argued with them. Her memoir records her shock that educated men would deliberately destroy the army that was protecting Russia from foreign invasion. From the Bolshevik perspective, of course, the army was not protecting Russia — it was protecting the ruling class. The destruction of the old army was a precondition for revolution, not collateral damage.

> "We have been deceived long enough! Kill him!" — a voice in the crowd, responding to an officer attempting to maintain discipline (PN 013-039)

## Mechanism 4: Desertion

By mid-1917, desertion had become a mass phenomenon. Millions of soldiers simply walked away from the front — some to join the revolution in the cities, most to return to their villages and claim land before the anticipated redistribution. Bochkareva's memoir describes a front line that had become porous:

> "Sometimes the peasants of the neighbouring villages are allowed by both sides to pass through to Novocherkassk, Kornilov's headquarters." — PN 021-133

This was not desertion as cowardice — it was desertion as rational calculation. Soldiers concluded that the war was lost, the state was collapsing, and their survival depended on being somewhere else. The [[military-discipline|discipline]] that had held millions of men in place against German machine guns dissolved because the framework of meaning that had sustained it — patriotism, fear of punishment, belief in victory — had evaporated.

## Bochkareva's Counter-Example

The [[womens-battalion-of-death|Women's Battalion of Death]] was Bochkareva's practical refutation of the collapse of discipline. She created a unit in which no committee existed, orders were obeyed, and desertion was unthinkable. The battalion's mere existence was an argument: if 2,000 women could maintain discipline, why couldn't 12 million men?

The answer, of course, was that the women were volunteers who had chosen to fight, while the men were conscripts who had never chosen any of it. Bochkareva's discipline worked because her soldiers shared her purpose. The Imperial Army's discipline had depended on coercion; when the coercive apparatus collapsed, so did the army.

## The Causal Chain

The collapse can be traced as a chain: **Order No. 1** → committees gain control of weapons → officers lose authority → **Bolshevik propaganda** provides ideological justification for disobedience → **desertion** becomes rational and safe → **military discipline** dissolves → the army ceases to function as a fighting force → the Bolsheviks seize power in the vacuum.

Each link in this chain was contested. Bochkareva contested every one of them — and lost at every link. Her memoir is not the story of someone who failed to see what was happening. It is the story of someone who saw it with perfect clarity and was powerless to stop it.
