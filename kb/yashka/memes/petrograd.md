# Petrograd

**Petrograd** (formerly St. Petersburg) was the capital of the [[Russian Empire]] and the central stage upon which the drama of the 1917 Revolution unfolded. For [[Maria Bochkareva]], Petrograd was the city where she petitioned [[Alexander Kerensky]] for authorization to form the [[Womens Battalion of Death]], where she witnessed the fateful confrontation between [[alexander-kerensky|Kerensky]] and [[Lavr Kornilov]], and where she later navigated the terror of [[Bolshevik Party|Bolshevik]] rule following the [[October Revolution]].

## The Revolutionary Capital

By 1917, Petrograd was a city of over two million people — factory workers, soldiers of the garrison, government officials, and a radicalized intelligentsia. The [[Petrograd Soviet]] of Workers' and Soldiers' Deputies, formed during the [[February Revolution]], shared an uneasy dual power with the [[Provisional Government]], both headquartered in the city.

> "Order No. 1, signed by the Petrograd Soviet of Workmen and Soldiers' Deputies, was read at the front. Its effect was instantaneous and catastrophic." [013-010]

The city's garrison — some 200,000 soldiers — was the critical military force in the capital. Their loyalties swung between the Provisional Government and the [[soviet|Soviet]], and ultimately to the [[bolshevik-party|Bolsheviks]] who promised peace and bread.

## Bochkareva in Petrograd

### First Arrival

[[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] first came to Petrograd in the summer of 1917, summoned by [[Michael Rodzianko]] after her battlefield heroism drew public attention:

> "Come to me in Petrograd, little heroine, and I will see that you get the recognition you deserve." [013-082]

Her journey to the capital marked her transition from frontline soldier to national figure:

> "The journey to Petrograd was uneventful. The train was crowded with soldiers returning from leave, all talking politics." [014-002]

### Meeting Kerensky

It was in Petrograd that [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] had her pivotal meeting with [[alexander-kerensky|Kerensky]] at the War Ministry. The encounter would define her subsequent role:

> "It was Kerensky, at that moment the idol of the Russian democracy and the most powerful man in the land." [014-041]

[[alexander-kerensky|Kerensky]] authorized her to form the first women's combat battalion — a decision made in Petrograd that would give [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] her historical legacy.

### The Battalion's Formation

Petrograd became the recruiting and training ground for the Women's Battalion:

> "Commander of the Military District of Petrograd. The same evening two thousand women volunteered." [014-066]

The city's response exceeded all expectations. Women from every social class — peasants, factory workers, university students, even noblewomen — presented themselves for service:

> "The Astoria, the leading hotel in Petrograd, at which Kerensky and the various members of the Government were staying." [014-084]

### The Kornilov-Kerensky Confrontation

One of the memoir's most dramatic scenes takes place in Petrograd at the [[Winter Palace]], where [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] witnessed the breakdown between the two most powerful men in Russia:

> "The three of us went to Petrograd to see Kerensky. It was on the occasion of Kornilov's demand for dictatorial powers." [017-085]

> "Upon our arrival at Petrograd we all went together to the Winter Palace." [017-087]

### Bolshevik Petrograd

After the [[october-revolution|October Revolution]], [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] returned to a transformed Petrograd. The city she had known as the hopeful capital of democratic Russia had become a landscape of terror:

> "Petrograd seemed populated by Red Guards." [021-002]

The contrast is stark and brutal:

> "Red Terror was rampant in Petrograd. The river was full of corpses floating downstream." [021-073]

She navigated this dangerous city under constant threat, eventually securing passage south to join Kornilov's forces.

## Strategic Significance

Petrograd was not merely a backdrop but a character in the memoir. Its geography — the Neva River, the great squares, the railway stations — shaped the events [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] describes. The city's concentration of political power made it the prize for which all factions contended. Whoever controlled Petrograd controlled Russia — a lesson the [[bolshevik-party|Bolsheviks]] understood better than [[alexander-kerensky|Kerensky]].

> "The Germans are threatening Petrograd, and the opposition of the Red Guards is melting away." [021-072]
