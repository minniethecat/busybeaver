## Overview

**Alexander Fyodorovich Kerensky** was a Russian lawyer, politician, and statesman who served as the second Minister-Chairman of the Russian [[Provisional Government]] from July to November 1917. A charismatic orator and member of the [[Socialist Revolutionary Party]], Kerensky rose to power during the turbulent months between the [[February Revolution]] and the [[October Revolution]]. In [[Maria Bochkareva|Yashka Bochkareva]]'s memoir, Kerensky appears as both an inspirational figure and a tragically flawed leader — the man who personally authorized and supported her [[Womens Battalion of Death]], but whose political vacillation and deteriorating relationship with General [[Lavr Kornilov]] contributed to the army's collapse and the [[Bolshevik Party|Bolshevik]] seizure of power.

## Biography

Kerensky was born in 1881 in Simbirsk, Russia — the same town where [[Vladimir Lenin]] was born. He studied law at [[petrograd|St. Petersburg]] University and gained prominence as a defence lawyer in political trials. Following the 1905 Revolution, he was elected to the [[State Duma]], where he established himself as a powerful orator and critic of the [[Tsarism|Tsarist]] regime.

After the [[february-revolution|February Revolution of 1917]], Kerensky became one of the most visible figures in the new government. He served initially as Minister of Justice, then as Minister of War, and finally as Minister-Chairman of the Provisional Government. During his tenure as War Minister, he embarked on personal tours of the front to rally the demoralized troops — the so-called "Kerensky Offensive" of June 1917.

> "As Kerensky had just completed a tour of the front and was to report on it to the Cabinet, it was decided to postpone our call until the next day." [017-005]

His oratory could galvanize soldiers temporarily, but the effect proved fleeting:

> "Kerensky's oratory, lost heart when the first regiments were cut to pieces by the German artillery after the initial successes." [017-014]

After the Bolshevik [[october-revolution|October Revolution]], Kerensky fled [[petrograd|Petrograd]] and spent the remainder of his life in exile, eventually settling in the [[united-states|United States]], where he died in 1970.

## Character Portrait in the Memoir

Kerensky is one of the most frequently referenced figures in [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]]'s memoir, appearing across at least ten chapters. [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]]'s portrait of him is psychologically acute — she captures both his magnetism and his fatal flaws.

### The Idol of Democracy

Their first meeting captured the electric atmosphere surrounding him in the summer of 1917:

> "It was Kerensky, at that moment the idol of the Russian democracy and the most powerful man in the land." [014-041]

At the Marinsky Theatre, his effect on soldiers was almost messianic:

> "Kerensky appeared and was given a tremendous reception. The soldiers went wild with joy." [014-054]

### The Authorizer

Kerensky granted [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] full authority to form the Women's Battalion, a decision that would define her legacy:

> "I authorize you to form, in the name of the Russian Government and of the Russian Commander-in-Chief, the first women's battalion for service at the front." [014-043]

At the battalion's dedication ceremony at St. Isaac's Cathedral, Kerensky personally bestowed military honors:

> "Kerensky then pinned the epaulets of a sub-lieutenant of the Russian Army on my shoulders, finishing with a 'Long live Bochkareva!'" [016-047]

### The Indecisive Leader

Despite his rhetorical gifts, Kerensky's paralysis under pressure forms the memoir's central critique of him. [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] witnessed this directly during the crisis with Kornilov:

> "Kerensky turned red, then white as a corpse when Rodzianko pronounced his warning. Pride and fear fought for mastery in his face." [017-095]

His increasing isolation after the Kornilov break is noted with clinical precision:

> "It also appeared that Kerensky was growing more and more autocratic." [017-107]

### The Final Collapse

In the weeks before the Bolshevik takeover, [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] made a final visit to Kerensky at the War Ministry. The scene is devastating:

> "Kerensky seized his head in his hands and moaned: 'Everything is lost.'" [018-135]

When [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] demanded action against the gathering Bolshevik threat, she found a man already defeated:

> "Kerensky, livid, motionless, answered not a word." [018-146]

## The Kerensky-Kornilov Rift

The memoir's central political drama concerns the breakdown between Kerensky and Kornilov. [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] found herself caught between the two men she respected — sent by Kerensky as an emissary to spy on the general:

> "I realized immediately that Kerensky was asking me to act for him as a secret agent." [017-068]

She witnessed the confrontation at the [[winter-palace|Winter Palace]] where the breach became irreparable. Kornilov's warning to [[michael-rodzianko|Rodzianko]] was prophetic:

> "Kornilov told Rodzianko that if Kerensky carried out his threat he would become a traitor to the country." [017-106]

The rift fatally divided the anti-Bolshevik forces. By dismissing Kornilov, Kerensky alienated the military establishment that was his last line of defense against [[vladimir-lenin|Lenin]].

## Key Relationships

- **[[Maria Bochkareva]]** — As War Minister, Kerensky authorized [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]]'s Women's Battalion and acted as her political patron. Their relationship evolved from mutual admiration to tension as [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] grew frustrated with his indecisiveness.
- **[[Lavr Kornilov]]** — Kerensky appointed Kornilov Commander-in-Chief but then turned against him, suspecting a coup. Their rivalry fatally weakened the Provisional Government.
- **[[Michael Rodzianko]]** — President of the Duma who frequently mediated between Kerensky and Kornilov, and who openly challenged Kerensky's judgment.
- **[[Vladimir Lenin]]** — Kerensky's government allowed [[vladimir-lenin|Lenin]] to conduct propaganda in the army, a decision [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] and Kornilov viewed as catastrophic.
- **[[Leon Trotsky]]** — Along with [[vladimir-lenin|Lenin]], [[leon-trotsky|Trotsky]] was one of the Bolshevik leaders whose agitation Kerensky failed to suppress.
