## Overview

Committee rule refers to the system of governance by elected soldiers' councils that replaced the traditional [[military-discipline|chain of command]] in the Russian army following the [[february-revolution|February Revolution of 1917]]. Mandated by [[order-no-1|Order No. 1]] and empowered by the [[petrograd-soviet|Petrograd Soviet]], company, regimental, corps, and army committees assumed authority over military decisions, discipline, and the use of weapons. In [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka Bochkareva]]'s memoir, committee rule is portrayed as the primary mechanism through which the army's fighting capacity was systematically destroyed.

## Explanation

Under committee rule, every military unit elected its own committee from the ranks of common soldiers. These committees possessed veto power over officers' orders, controlled the distribution and custody of weapons, and served as the primary channel for political agitation. Decisions that had previously been made instantly by commanding officers now required debate, vote, and committee approval. The committees' authority derived not from military law but from revolutionary legitimacy — they answered to the [[petrograd-soviet|Petrograd Soviet]] rather than to the military hierarchy. This dual authority created a situation where an officer's command was binding only if the committee chose to honor it, effectively abolishing the principle of military obedience on which all armies depend.

## Role in Narrative

[[maria-bochkareva|Yashka Bochkareva]] was among the first to recognize the destructive potential of committee rule and took deliberate steps to exclude committees from her own unit. When organizing the [[womens-battalion-of-death|Women's Battalion of Death]], she insisted on traditional military discipline:

> "I said: 'This organization should have no committees and be run on the regular army principles. Such is the will of the great Russian people through their Provisional Government.'" — PN 014-025

When challenged, she argued forcefully against committee governance:

> "I replied that it was not a time to run an army by committees. I am a common peasant myself, and I know the soul of the soldier. He will not fight under a committee. He will talk and hold meetings, but his thoughts will be of home, not of battle. An army is not a parliament." — PN 014-032

Her Battalion's refusal to adopt committees distinguished it from nearly every other unit and contributed to its initial effectiveness. But the surrounding army, governed by committees, was crumbling. Committee meetings became platforms for Bolshevik propaganda, and by the autumn of 1917, the committees were actively preventing officers from maintaining even minimal order:

> "The soldiers' committees began to exercise their functions. They issued passes, took censuses, and generally interfered with everything the officers attempted to do. The authority of the commanders dwindled to nothing." — PN 013-024

## Historical Context

The committee system emerged spontaneously in the vacuum of authority created by the [[february-revolution|February Revolution]]. Soldiers, drawn predominantly from the peasantry and long resentful of the aristocratic officer corps, seized the opportunity to assert control over their own units. The Provisional Government, dependent on [[soviet|Soviet]] support, could not effectively oppose the committees' spread. Even [[alexander-kerensky|Kerensky]], the War Minister, could only appeal to the committees' patriotism — he could not order them to disband. By the time of the [[october-revolution|Bolshevik seizure of power]] in October 1917, the committee system had so thoroughly dismantled military authority that no force existed to defend the Provisional Government.

## Related Concepts

Committee rule was the direct institutional expression of [[order-no-1]], which provided its legal framework. It was authorized and encouraged by the [[soviet]], which saw soldiers' committees as vehicles for revolutionary consciousness. Committee meetings became the primary forum for [[bolshevist-propaganda]], as agitators used them to spread defeatist ideas. The abolition of committee rule was the explicit goal of figures like [[Kornilov]], whose failed attempt to restore military discipline triggered the final crisis of the Provisional Government.
