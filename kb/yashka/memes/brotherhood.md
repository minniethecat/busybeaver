## Overview

Brotherhood (bratstvo) was the third term in the revolutionary triad: [[liberty|Liberty]], Equality, and Brotherhood. If liberty was the negative [[liberty|freedom]] from tsarist oppression and equality the positive promise of social leveling, brotherhood was the emotional and spiritual core of the revolution — the vision of a society in which Russians of all classes would regard one another as brothers rather than as masters and servants. In [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka Bochkareva]]'s memoir, brotherhood appears initially as a genuine hope and then, increasingly, as a bitter irony: the revolution that promised brotherhood produced fratricide.

## Explanation

The concept of brotherhood in the revolutionary context drew on multiple sources: the Orthodox Christian ideal of the community of believers, the narodnik (populist) tradition of educated youth identifying with the peasant masses, and the socialist vision of a classless society. In the heady days of February 1917, brotherhood seemed immanent — officers and soldiers embraced, political prisoners were freed, and the old hierarchies appeared to dissolve overnight. But brotherhood, unlike liberty and equality, could not be legislated. It required trust, and trust between classes that had spent centuries as masters and serfs could not be manufactured by manifesto.

## Role in Narrative

The promise of brotherhood appears early in [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]]'s narrative, in the immediate aftermath of the [[february-revolution|February Revolution]]:

> "Freedom, Equality and Brotherhood! How sweet were these words to our ears! We were transported. There were tears of joy, embraces, dancing." — PN 013-006

But the intoxication was brief. The soldiers' committees that were meant to embody brotherhood became instruments of class vengeance. [[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]] records a commander's bitter question:

> "Where was justice? Where was brotherhood? Where was manhood and decency?" — PN 017-046

By the time of Bolshevik rule, brotherhood had curdled into its opposite. The brotherhood of the revolution became the brotherhood of the execution squad:

> "At first when the revolution was born there was universal brotherhood and joy. Now intolerance and hatred reigned everywhere." — PN 020-083

[[maria-bochkareva|Yashka]]'s final invocation of brotherhood is elegiac rather than hopeful:

> "We wanted peace, happiness, brotherhood, not civil war, foreign invasion, and the rule of the mob." — PN 024-096

## Historical Context

The slogan "[[liberty|Liberty]], Equality, Brotherhood" was borrowed from the French Revolution and adapted to Russian conditions. It appeared on banners, in manifestos, and in the rhetoric of every political faction from the Kadets (Constitutional Democrats) to the [[bolshevik-party|Bolsheviks]]. But each faction interpreted it differently: for liberals, brotherhood meant civic solidarity under law; for moderate socialists, it meant class reconciliation in the service of national defense; for [[bolshevik-party|Bolsheviks]], it meant the violent elimination of class enemies. The slogan's very universality was its weakness — it meant everything and therefore nothing, and when the moment came to choose between brotherhood and power, every faction chose power.

## Related Concepts

Brotherhood was the emotional complement to [[liberty]] (political [[liberty|freedom]]) and [[social-equality]] (economic justice), completing the revolutionary promise. The failure of brotherhood was the failure of [[revolutionary-democracy]], which had promised that democratic deliberation could reconcile class differences. The [[bolshevik-party|Bolsheviks]]' answer to the failure of brotherhood was class war — the explicit rejection of solidarity across class lines.
