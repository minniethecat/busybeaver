# Justification by Faith

## Definition

Justification by faith (sola fide) is the Protestant doctrine that God declares a sinner righteous — not on the basis of their own merits or works, but solely through faith in Jesus Christ, whose righteousness is imputed to the believer. This was the central theological principle of the Reformation, articulated by Martin Luther and systematized by John Calvin. In the Scottish Presbyterian context of Hogg's novel, the doctrine was one of the most fiercely contested points of theology, and its radical interpretation by Antinomian preachers forms the ideological core of the novel's tragedy.

## Theological Background

The doctrine of justification by faith alone was the material principle of the Protestant Reformation. Calvin developed it further in his *Institutes*, arguing that justification is a forensic declaration — God declares the sinner righteous, but does not make them inherently righteous (that is sanctification, a separate process). The danger Hogg's novel explores is what happens when justification is severed from sanctification entirely. The Antinomian wing of Scottish Calvinism taught that since justification is by faith alone and God's decree is eternal, the justified person's subsequent sins cannot affect their standing before God. This became known as the "justification-without-sanctification" error.

The historical context is important: the 17th-century Antinomian Controversy in England involved figures such as Tobias Crisp, John Eaton, and John Saltmarsh, who were accused of teaching that the elect are so completely justified that their sins — past, present, and future — are already forgiven, rendering repentance and moral effort superfluous. These ideas migrated to Scotland through the networks of Puritan correspondence and found fertile soil in the extreme Presbyterianism of the Covenanting tradition. Hogg sets his novel in this precise theological moment, circa 1687–1712, when these doctrinal debates were at their most intense.

> "…some incoherent words about justification by faith alone and absolute and eternal predestination having been the ruin of his house." (004-194)
>
> — The dying Laird of Dalcastle's final ravings, in which he recognizes — too late — the destructive power of the doctrines that have consumed his family

## In the Novel

In Hogg's novel, the doctrine of justification by faith is the theological foundation upon which Robert Wringhim builds his murderous career. His father, the Reverend Wringhim, preaches a radical version of the doctrine, emphasizing that "a justified person can do no wrong" (004-042). This teaching, absorbed by Robert from childhood, becomes the rationalization for every crime he commits.

The key dramatic moment comes when Robert's companion Gil-Martin assures him of his justification. Robert records that he had "assurance of his justification in the sight of God" before he had committed his worst acts (006-352). The novel thus traces a direct line from the doctrine of justification by faith — through its Antinomian distortion — to the complete collapse of moral conscience.

The novel's satirical edge is sharpest in its portrayal of how easily "justification by faith" becomes "justification of sin." When Robert doubts whether his deeds can be righteous, Gil-Martin directs him back to the doctrine: all things are preordained, therefore all things — including murder — are justified for the elect (006-251). The theological vocabulary of grace becomes indistinguishable from the vocabulary of license; "liberty" in Christ becomes liberty to commit any act without consequence.

> "I began to express my doubts regarding the justification of all the deeds of perfect men…" (006-251)
>
> — Robert's faint moral qualm, immediately neutralized by Gil-Martin's redirection to the doctrine of preordination

## The Antinomian Distortion

The novel's theological critique is precise: it does not attack the Reformation doctrine of justification by faith itself, but rather its Antinomian distortion. The critical error, as Hogg presents it, is the severing of justification from sanctification. In orthodox Calvinism, the justified person is also being progressively sanctified — made actually holy through the work of the Holy Spirit. The two doctrines are distinct but inseparable. The Antinomians collapsed this distinction, teaching that justification renders sanctification unnecessary — or, in its most extreme form, that the elect *cannot* sin, regardless of what they do.

The Reverend Wringhim's theology occupies precisely this extreme position. His teaching that "a justified person can do no wrong" (004-042) is not the orthodox Calvinist position — which holds that the justified person can and does sin, but that those sins are covered by Christ's righteousness — but its antinomian perversion, in which the category of "sin" simply ceases to apply to the elect. The novel dramatizes the catastrophic consequences of this theological move: when "sin" is redefined as "the righteous actions of the elect," moral language collapses entirely, and there is no act so monstrous that it cannot be redescribed in the vocabulary of election.

> "…the faith of the promises, and justification by grace, that they may read and tremble…" (006-001)
>
> — From Robert's opening address to his readers, in which the irony of "trembling" at grace rather than judgment reveals the extent of his theological inversion

## The Psychological Mechanism

Beyond its theological content, the novel offers a penetrating analysis of the *psychological* function of the doctrine of justification. Robert's belief that he is justified serves not only to rationalize his crimes to others but — more importantly — to rationalize them to himself. The doctrine becomes a mechanism of cognitive dissonance reduction: when Robert's actions produce guilt (as they occasionally do — see 006-229), the doctrine supplies an immediate reinterpretation: what *feels* like guilt cannot be guilt, because the justified cannot sin. The feeling is therefore reframed as a temptation to doubt, a satanic attack on his assurance, to be resisted rather than heeded.

This psychological dynamic makes the novel strikingly modern. Hogg anticipates the 20th-century interest in the mechanisms of self-deception, showing how a sophisticated theological system can function as what we would now call an ideological apparatus — a set of concepts that systematically prevents its adherents from perceiving the moral reality of their own actions. Robert is not simply a hypocrite who knows he is evil and pretends otherwise; he is a true believer whose belief system makes moral self-knowledge literally impossible.

> "I was astonished at my own resolutions, for I had before been a feeble and irresolute creature; but now I felt a power, an energy, and a spirit, which I had never before experienced — and all sprung from a confidence, that I was one of the elect, and that the eternal God had from all eternity decreed my salvation." (006-103)
>
> — Robert describes the psychological empowerment that accompanies the conviction of justification, conflating spiritual assurance with the license to act without moral constraint

## The Editor's Counter-Narrative

The Editor's Narrative provides the theological counterpoint to Robert's antinomianism. The Editor, an Enlightenment rationalist, treats the doctrine of justification by faith as a species of dangerous enthusiasm — not wrong in itself, perhaps, but demonstrably prone to misuse. The Laird of Dalcastle, the Editor's representative within the story, articulates this moderate skepticism: he doubts "the great standard doctrine of absolute predestination" (004-032) not on systematic theological grounds but on the pragmatic ground that it produces bad effects — broken families, fanaticism, and moral chaos.

The novel ultimately refuses to resolve the theological debate. The Editor's rationalism is too thin to account for the genuinely supernatural elements of the story; Robert's supernaturalism is too toxic to be credible. The reader is left suspended between the two, forced to recognize that the doctrine of justification — like any powerful idea — is neither inherently destructive nor inherently safe, but becomes what its adherents make of it.

## Critical Significance

Hogg's treatment of justification by faith is a case study in how sound theology, when isolated from other doctrines and pushed to an extreme, becomes a mechanism of self-deception. The novel does not attack the doctrine itself — rather, it dramatizes how the *psychological* effect of believing oneself justified can overwhelm the *ethical* constraints that the doctrine, properly understood, should reinforce. This makes the novel not merely a theological satire but a psychological study of how belief systems can be weaponized by the human capacity for self-justification.

The novel's particular contribution to the literary treatment of this doctrine is its recognition that "justification by faith" is, in psychological terms, a story we tell ourselves about ourselves — and that stories, once told, have consequences their tellers cannot control. Robert's tragedy is not that he believes the wrong doctrine but that he believes it in the wrong way: as a guarantee of his own righteousness rather than as a declaration of God's mercy to the unrighteous. The distinction is subtle but absolute, and the novel's genius is to show how easily — how naturally — the one slides into the other.
