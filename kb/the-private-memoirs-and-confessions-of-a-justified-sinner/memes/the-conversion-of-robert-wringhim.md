# The Conversion of Robert Wringhim

## Description

Robert Wringhim's "conversion" — the moment when he receives "the long and earnestly desired assurance of my acceptance with the Almighty" (006-074) — is the psychological turning point of the novel. From this moment, Robert is no longer a young man seeking assurance of his election but a man who *knows* he is elect — and whose certainty makes him impervious to moral challenge.

## Narrative Significance

The conversion exemplifies the novel's deepest insight: that religious experience, however subjectively powerful, is no guarantee of moral transformation. Robert's conversion produces certainty without sanctification, assurance without repentance. It is, in the novel's dark vision, not a conversion to Christ but a conversion to his own self-justifying narrative.
