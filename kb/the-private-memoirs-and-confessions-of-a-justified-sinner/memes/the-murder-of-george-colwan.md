# The Murder of George Colwan

## Summary

The murder of George Colwan by his half-brother Robert Wringhim — possibly with Gil-Martin's direct involvement — is the central event of Hogg's novel and the act that transforms theological abstraction into criminal reality. George Colwan is stabbed in the back on an Edinburgh street after an evening spent in the vicinity of the Black Bull Inn. The crime is recounted twice: once in the Editor's Narrative (from external evidence and witness testimony) and once in Robert's Confessions (from the murderer's perspective), creating an epistemological gap that is the novel's most powerful structural device. Robert presents the killing not as a crime but as a logical consequence of his election and George's reprobation — "I considered this to be a judgment from heaven" (006-228). The reader, having access to both accounts, is left to navigate the gap between external evidence and internal self-deception.

## Background

The murder is the culmination of a long campaign of harassment that Robert and Gil-Martin have waged against George. From their first encounter at the tennis court, where Robert impedes George's play and is repeatedly "shoved aside in no very ceremonious way" (004-066), to the confrontation on Arthur's Seat where Robert screams "Murder! murder!" while George attempts only to restrain him (004-144), the brothers' relationship is defined by Robert's escalating aggression and George's bewildered forbearance. George complains to his father that his brother "harassed him with his presence at all times, and in all places" (004-169), but old Dalcastle dismisses the matter.

The immediate prelude to the murder involves an altercation between George and a Mr. Drummond. On the night before the killing, "a gentleman... chanced to arise between George Colwan and a Mr. Drummond" (004-182), and the two repair to a house near the Black Bull to settle their dispute. This quarrel — and Drummond's later conviction for the murder — provides the legal misdirection that allows Robert to escape justice. Crucially, Robert's companion Gil-Martin has the ability to assume the likeness of others, a power that enables him to implicate Drummond while shielding Robert. A witness later testifies to the uncanny resemblance: "So like was he that there was not one item in dress, form, feature, nor voice, by which I could distinguish the one from the other" (004-290).

The theological background is equally important. Robert has been taught that "a justified person can do no wrong" (004-042) and that the reprobate are outside the pale of moral concern. George, as a moderate churchman who refuses to adopt the Antinomian creed, is by definition among the reprobate — and therefore, in Robert's logic, a legitimate target of divine judgment. The murder is not an impulsive act but the working-out of a theological program that has systematically dehumanized its victim.

## Narrative Accounts

| Perspective | Source | Nature of Account |
|-------------|--------|-------------------|
| External / Legal | Editor's Narrative | Evidence, witness testimony, circumstantial |
| Internal / Theological | Robert's Confessions | Self-justifying narrative of righteous action |

The dual narration is the novel's masterstroke. The Editor's Narrative assembles the external evidence: the body is discovered and deposited in "the dead-room in the Guard-house" (004-187), witnesses come forward with fragmentary testimony, and suspicion initially falls on Drummond, who is "pronounced guilty of the murder; outlawed for not appearing" (004-191). Miss Logan's persistent investigation gradually uncovers the truth — Bell Calvert's testimony that the killer "was the very man who stabbed George Colwan in the back" (004-323) — but by the time the evidence points conclusively to Robert, he has vanished.

Robert's account in the Confessions is radically different. He describes how he and Gil-Martin stalked George through Edinburgh's streets, how Gil-Martin assumed the likeness of Drummond to create confusion, and how, in the moment of the killing, Robert "conceived myself completely justified on that score" (006-228). The theological language is chilling: Robert frames the murder as "a judgment from heaven" (006-228) and treats the ensuing trial not as a proceeding to determine guilt but as a test of his faith. The structural irony is that Robert's confession, while factually accurate about his own role, is morally deluded — his "justification" is a theological term that has swallowed its own ethical content.

## Aftermath

The immediate aftermath is a failed investigation. Despite "the arm of justice was not idle" (004-188), the wrong man — Thomas Drummond — is convicted and outlawed. George's father never recovers: "After the funeral of this promising and excellent young man, his father never more held up his head" (004-192). Miss Logan, however, refuses to accept the official verdict and dedicates herself to uncovering the truth. Her investigation, conducted with "all her art" (004-192), eventually extracts a confession from Bell Calvert, who identifies Robert as the killer and reveals Gil-Martin's role as instigator — "the murderer of the accomplished George Colwan has been his own brother, set on by a hellish counsellor" (004-309).

Robert and his father bring the matter to court preemptively, confident that their theological standing will protect them. Their counsel assures them "of a complete victory, and that banishment would be the mildest award of the law" (006-230). The trial results in Robert's acquittal — not because he is innocent, but because the legal system cannot penetrate the theological self-deception that makes him genuinely unable to recognize his actions as crimes. Robert's acquittal is thus the novel's darkest irony: justice is served by the courts and simultaneously mocked by them.

## Thematic Significance

The murder is the hinge on which the novel turns. Before it, Robert is a religious zealot; after it, he is a murderer — and his theological self-justification is tested against the reality of a dead body. The fact that Robert is acquitted by the courts but condemned by his own narrative is the novel's most powerful demonstration of the gap between legal and moral truth. Hogg uses the murder to dramatize the failure of Enlightenment institutions — the court, the law, the rational investigation — to comprehend theological fanaticism from within. The court looks for motives in the conventional register of human passions (envy, greed, revenge) and finds none; Robert's actual motive — that he is elect and his brother reprobate — is literally incomprehensible to the legal mind.

The murder also exposes the complicity of Gil-Martin, whose shape-shifting abilities blur the question of who actually wielded the knife. When Robert says "I considered this to be a judgment from heaven" (006-228), he may be describing Gil-Martin's action as much as his own — the two have become so identified that Robert can no longer distinguish his deeds from his companion's. This collapse of individual moral agency is the murder's deepest horror: it is not simply that Robert kills his brother, but that he does so in a state of such profound self-deception that he experiences the act as an expression of divine will.
