## Description

Penpunt is a village mentioned in Robert Wringhim's private memoir as the home of his servant Samuel Scrape. The name struck Wringhim as "so ludicrous" that he took to calling Scrape by the name of his native village rather than his Christian name — an appellation of which Scrape was, characteristically, very proud (006-330).

From Scrape's anecdote — a comic tale involving a Galloway cattle-drover and a Penpunt Cameronian — we learn that Penpunt is a community of Cameronian Covenanters. "The folk's a' Cameronians there," Scrape explains, and the village serves as an emblem of the distinctive folk-religious culture of the Scottish south-west.

## In the Narrative

Penpunt enters the novel through the person of Samuel Scrape, whom Wringhim is forced to rely upon as a companion during his period of social isolation and legal persecution. Scrape's country tales, told in broad Scots dialect, provide some of the novel's most vivid passages of rural humour and folk wisdom.

The key Penpunt anecdote (006-333–006-334) is a comic morality tale in which a Galloway drover, having already paid a Penpunt Cameronian for a cow, later meets the same Cameronian before a crowd of fellow dealers and offers to pay again — testing whether the Cameronian will preserve his reputation for honesty at the cost of taking double payment. The Cameronian, faced with the choice between principles and profit, "pits his principles aff at the side, to be kind o' sleepin' partner" and pockets the money. As Scrape concludes with characteristic candour: "A Cameronian's principles never came atween him an' his purse."

The anecdote, though comic in tone, carries serious thematic weight within the novel's larger argument: it exposes the gap between doctrinal profession and actual conduct that pervades the world Hogg depicts, from the humblest cottar to the most fanatical theologian.

## Key Passages

The origin of "Penpunt" as Scrape's nickname:

> He said he came from a place called Penpunt, which I thought a name so ludicrous that I called him by the name of his native village, an appellation of which he was very proud, and answered everything with more civility and perspicuity when I denominated him Penpunt, than Samuel, his own Christian name. (006-330)

The Galloway drover anecdote:

> "In sic a case as this, sir, it disna hinge upon principles, but a piece o' good manners; an' I can tell you that, at sic a crisis, a Cameronian is a gay-an weel-bred man. He's driven to this, and he maun either make a breach in his friend's good name, or in his purse; an' oh, sir, whilk o' thae, think you, is the most precious?" (006-334)

Wringhim's invitation to hear the local gossip:

> "Now, Penpunt, you may tell me all that passed between you and the wives of the clachan. I am better of that stomach qualm, with which I am sometimes seized, and shall be much amused by hearing the sentiments of noted witches regarding myself and my connections." (006-343)

## Literary Significance

Penpunt functions in the novel less as a geographical location than as a metonym for a particular religious culture — the Cameronian Covenanters of the Scottish south-west, who combined strict Reformed orthodoxy with a pragmatic, unsentimental approach to material self-interest. The comic anecdote Scrape tells is a miniature version of the novel's central critique: that professed principles, however lofty, are perpetually at risk of being set aside when they conflict with worldly advantage.

The village's absurd-sounding name, which Wringhim finds so amusing, also participates in the novel's broader comic-naming pattern — alongside characters like Lucky Shaw and place-names like the West Lowmond — that tempers the theological darkness with earthy, demotic humour.
