# Mrs. Logan

## Biography

Arabella Logan first appears as "Miss Logan, an old and intimate acquaintance of the laird's, and a very worthy respectable lady, of good connections, whose parents had lost their patrimony in the civil wars" (004-036). She is a close friend of [[laird-of-dalcastle|George Colwan, the Laird of Dalcastle]], and becomes something of a surrogate aunt to his son [[george-colwan|George Colwan]].

After the murder of young [[george-colwan|George Colwan]], Mrs. Logan transforms from a background domestic figure into the novel's most determined investigator. "In all her outgoings and incomings Mrs. Logan (as she was now styled) never lost sight of this one object" (004-195) — to bring the murderer to justice. She had internalized the old laird's dying conviction: "The murderer of the accomplished George Colwan has been his own brother" (004-309).

Her investigation leads her to Bell Calvert in prison (004-199–222), to the village near Dalcastle where she and Calvert witness [[robert-wringhim|Robert Wringhim]] walking with [[gil-martin|Gil-Martin]] (004-323), and finally to the confrontation where she declares: "I heard thee confess it again and again this same hour" (004-360). She and Calvert then take their evidence to [[lord-craigie|Lord Craigie]], the Lord Justice Clerk (004-371).

## Role in the Narrative

Mrs. Logan is the novel's embodiment of the thirst for justice — human, persistent, and ultimately thwarted by institutional failure. She represents the network of ordinary relationships that [[robert-wringhim|Robert Wringhim]]'s crimes tear apart, and her dogged pursuit of the truth stands in contrast to the legal system's inability to deliver a conviction.

Her role also illustrates the novel's gendered dimension: it is the women — Mrs. Logan, Bell Calvert, [[bessy-gillies|Bessy Gillies]] — who see through the masculine world of theological sophistry and legal procedure to the truth beneath.

## Key Passages

Mrs. Logan's recognition of Robert on the road with Gil-Martin:

> Mrs. Logan ran to the window, and, behold, there was indeed Robert Wringhim Colwan (now the Laird of Dalcastle) coming forward almost below their window, walking arm in arm with another young man. (004-323)

Her accusation at their confrontation:

> "I heard thee confess it again and again this same hour," said Mrs. Logan. (004-360)

Her speech in court, refusing to condemn another woman:

> "Whatever penalties she was doomed to abide, she was determined she would not bear witness against a woman's life, from a certain conviction that it could not be a woman who broke her house." (004-277)

Her reaction to Bell Calvert's revelation:

> "The dark suspicions of my late benefactor have been just, and his last prediction is fulfilled," cried she. "The murderer of the accomplished George Colwan has been his own brother." (004-309)

## Relationships

- **[[george-colwan|George Colwan]]**: The young man she loved as a nephew, whose murder she devotes her life to avenging.

- **[[robert-wringhim|Robert Wringhim]]**: The murderer she pursues relentlessly.
- **Bell Calvert / [[arabella-calvert|Arabella Calvert]]**: Her unlikely ally — the criminal who witnessed the murder.
- **[[bessy-gillies|Bessy Gillies]]**: Her maid, who testifies alongside her.
- **[[lord-craigie|Lord Craigie]]**: The justice clerk to whom she delivers her evidence.
