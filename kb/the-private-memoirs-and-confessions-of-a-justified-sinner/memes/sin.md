# Sin

## Definition

In Reformed theology, sin is not merely wrong action but the fundamental condition of humanity after the Fall — a state of "total depravity" in which every faculty (will, intellect, affections) is corrupted and inclined toward evil. Sin is both inherited (original sin, from Adam) and actual (committed by each person). The remedy is not moral effort — which the unregenerate will is incapable of — but the sovereign grace of God in election and regeneration.

## Theological Background

The Westminster Confession teaches that "every sin, both original and actual, being a transgression of the righteous law of God, and contrary thereunto, doth, in its own nature, bring guilt upon the sinner." In orthodox Reformed soteriology, the recognition of one's own sinfulness is the necessary precondition for receiving grace — one must know oneself as a sinner before one can know Christ as a Savior.

### Sin in the Reformed Ordo Salutis

In classical Reformed theology, the awareness of sin functions as the "first use of the law" (*usus elenchticus*): the law reveals human sinfulness and drives the sinner to Christ. Without this conviction of sin, the gospel is unnecessary — a solution to a problem one does not know one has. The Puritan spiritual autobiographies that Hogg parodies in Robert's memoir typically follow this pattern: conviction of sin, despair of self, turning to Christ, assurance of salvation.

What Hogg shows, with devastating irony, is what happens when the *ordo salutis* is short-circuited. Robert never experiences conviction of sin — not because he is sinless, but because his Antinomian theology has eliminated the category of personal sinfulness for the elect. He moves directly from the abstract premise "I am elect" to the conclusion "I am righteous," without the intervening stage of self-examination. The result is a protagonist who commits murder while believing himself to be an instrument of divine justice.

## In the Novel

Hogg's novel deploys "sin" with devastating irony. The Reverend Wringhim and his followers speak constantly of sin — but always the sins of others: "the sins of the wicked and unconverted" (004-041). Robert himself is taught from childhood that the elect are beyond the reach of sin's consequences, that "the old hoary sinner might be cut off in the fullness of his iniquity" (004-062) while the elect remain secure.

The novel's irony reaches its peak when Robert, having committed murder, describes his victim as the sinner and himself as the instrument of righteousness. Sin, in the Antinomian vocabulary Hogg critiques, has ceased to be a category of self-examination and become instead a category of condemnation directed exclusively outward.

### The Two Universes of Sin

The Reverend Wringhim's most theologically explicit pronouncement establishes the novel's fundamental moral structure:

> "Evil, madam, may be either operative, or passive. To them it is an evil, but to us none. We have no more to do with the sins of the wicked and unconverted here than with those of an infidel Turk; for all earthly bonds and fellowships are absorbed and swallowed up in the holy community of the Reformed Church." (004-041)

This passage creates a theological taxonomy in which the same act can be both sin and not-sin depending on who commits it. For the unconverted, sin is "operative" — it actively harms them. For the elect, the same act is merely "passive" — whatever its external appearance, it cannot touch their justified state. This is the theological foundation for every crime that follows.

### Robert's Self-Assessment

In his memoir, Robert provides a detailed moral inventory that reveals the Antinomian mind at work:

> "I kept myself also free of the sins of idolatry and misbelief, both of a deadly nature; and, upon the whole, I think I had not then broken, that is, absolutely broken, above four out of the ten commandments; but, for all that, I had more sense than to regard either my good works, or my evil deeds, as in the smallest degree influencing the eternal decrees of the Almighty concerning me." (006-071)

This is Antinomian moral reasoning in its purest form. Robert can acknowledge having broken commandments while simultaneously asserting that this fact has no bearing on his standing before God. Sin is admitted and dismissed in the same breath. The phrase "absolutely broken" reveals the mechanism: only a full, final, unqualified breach of a commandment would count as genuine sin — and by that definition, almost nothing qualifies.

### Robert as Sufferer, Not Sinner

When Robert is imprisoned, he does not interpret his suffering as the consequence of his own actions but as a sign of his righteousness:

> "I was not sorry at being thus honoured to suffer in the cause of righteousness, and at the hands of sinful men; and, as soon as I was alone, I betook myself to prayer, deprecating the long-suffering of God towards such horrid sinners." (006-176)

The irony is total: Robert, the murderer, prays for God to judge "such horrid sinners" — meaning those who have imprisoned him. The category of sin has been so thoroughly externalized that Robert cannot even recognize his own crimes as crimes.

## Antinomian Distortion

| Orthodox View | Antinomian Distortion |
|---------------|----------------------|
| All are sinners in need of grace | The unconverted are sinners; the elect are righteous |
| Sin requires repentance | The elect need not repent — their sins are already forgiven |
| Recognition of sin leads to Christ | Recognition of sin in others justifies their condemnation |
| "Filthy rags" describes all human righteousness without Christ | "Filthy rags" describes only the unconverted's good works |
| Conviction of sin is the Spirit's work | Conviction of sin is a sign of weak faith |

## The Rhetoric of Sin

A distinctive feature of Hogg's treatment is his attention to how the word "sin" *sounds* in the mouths of different characters. For the [[rev-robert-wringhim|Reverend Wringhim]], "sin" is a word of power — spoken from the pulpit, it condemns. For [[arabella-colwan|Arabella]], it is a word of flattery — applied to others, it confirms her own election. For [[robert-wringhim|Robert]], it is a word of self-justification — his "suffering at the hands of sinful men" becomes proof of his righteousness. Only for [[george-colwan|George]] and the [[laird-of-dalcastle|Laird]] does "sin" retain anything like its traditional meaning — a universal human condition requiring humility and grace.

Hogg's achievement is to show that the most dangerous sin in the novel is not murder, but the theological pride that abolishes the very concept of personal sin. Murder is merely the consequence.

## Critical Significance

Hogg's treatment of sin is the key to the novel's theological critique. He does not deny the reality of sin — the novel is saturated with evil. Rather, he shows how the doctrine of sin, when weaponized by Antinomian theology, becomes a tool for exempting oneself from moral scrutiny while intensifying scrutiny of others. The ultimate irony is that Robert, who speaks most fluently of sin, is the character least capable of recognizing his own. The novel suggests that the first casualty of Antinomian theology is the capacity for self-knowledge.
