# Effectual Calling

**Effectual Calling** (or *efficacious grace*) is, in Reformed theology, the inward work of the Holy Spirit by which the elect are irresistibly drawn to faith in Christ. It is distinguished from the *outward* or *general* call of the gospel, which is addressed to all hearers but is only "effectual" — that is, productive of saving faith — in the elect. In *The Private Memoirs and Confessions of a Justified Sinner*, the doctrine becomes the occasion for one of the novel's most revealing theological exchanges.

## The Catechism Scene

The concept is introduced early in Robert Wringhim's memoir, during the Sabbath evening catechism in the Reverend Wringhim's household:

> "I was the second son of this unhappy marriage, and, ere ever I was born, my father according to the flesh disclaimed all relation or connection with me... It fell to my mother to ask Effectual Calling at me. I said the answer with propriety and emphasis. 'Now, madam,' added I, 'my question to you is: What is Ineffectual Calling?'" (006-003)

This scene is remarkable for several reasons. First, it demonstrates the young Robert's precocious theological intelligence — he can recite the catechism answer "with propriety and emphasis." Second, and more importantly, it reveals his instinct for theological subversion: rather than accepting the received categories, he invents a counter-concept ("Ineffectual Calling") that turns the doctrine against itself.

## Ineffectual Calling: Robert's Theological Innovation

Robert defines Ineffectual Calling as "the outward call of the gospel without any effect on the hearts of unregenerated and impenitent sinners" (006-005). He then applies it to specific persons:

> "Has not Ardinferry the same? Has not Patrick M'Lure the same? Has not the Laird of Dalcastle and his reprobate heir the same? And will any tell me that this is not Ineffectual Calling?" (006-005)

Robert's innovation is theologically coherent — the distinction between effectual and ineffectual calling is implicit in the Reformed doctrine of election — but socially destructive. By naming specific individuals as recipients of merely "ineffectual" calling, Robert transforms a theological abstraction into a weapon of spiritual condemnation. He judges his neighbours eternally damned based on a doctrine that, in orthodox usage, is meant to emphasise God's mercy, not human discrimination.

## Theological Significance

The doctrine of effectual calling occupies a specific place in the Reformed *ordo salutis* (order of salvation): after predestination and before justification. It is the moment at which God's eternal decree of election becomes actual in the life of the believer. For Robert, the subjective experience of having been "called" becomes indistinguishable from the objective fact of election, and this subjective certainty licenses everything that follows.

Hogg's critique is not directed at the doctrine itself but at the psychological use to which it is put. When the inward call becomes a private experience that cannot be tested against outward conduct, it ceases to be a theological category and becomes a tool of self-deception.
