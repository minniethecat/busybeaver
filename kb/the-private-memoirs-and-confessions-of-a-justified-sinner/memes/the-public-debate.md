# The Public Theological Debate

## Description

The public debate between the Reverend Wringhim and the Laird of Dalcastle is the novel's dramatization of the theological conflict that drives its plot. Wringhim defends the most extreme implications of predestinarian theology; the Laird argues for a moderate, reasonable Calvinism that leaves room for moral responsibility. The debate has no winner — the two positions are incommensurable — but it serves to clarify what is at stake in the theological controversy that will destroy the Colwan family.

> "the two got warm in their arguments, always in proportion as they receded from nature, utility, and common sense." (004-038)

> "everyone in the room was up talking with the utmost vociferation, all on the same subject, and all taking the same side in the debate." (004-080)

> "The arguments of the latter prevailed, and the laird was driven to sullen silence." (004-345)

## Narrative Significance

The debate externalizes the theological conflict that the rest of the novel internalizes. It shows that the Antinomian controversy was a public, institutional matter — not merely a private psychological crisis — and that reasonable arguments were powerless against the zealot's certainty.
