# Grace

**Grace**, in Reformed theology, is the unmerited favour of God by which the elect are saved. It is the foundation of the doctrine of justification by faith alone: righteousness is a gift received, not a quality achieved. In *The Private Memoirs and Confessions of a Justified Sinner*, James Hogg explores how this doctrine, when severed from moral accountability, can be transformed into a warrant for lawlessness.

## Grace in Robert Wringhim's Theology

Robert Wringhim's confession opens with an invocation of grace that sets the tone for everything that follows:

> "My life has been a life of trouble and turmoil... My sorrows have all been for a slighted gospel, and my vengeance has been wreaked on its adversaries. Therefore, in the might of Heaven, I will sit down and write: I will let the wicked of this world know what I have done in the faith of the promises, and justification by grace..." (006-001)

This opening paragraph is theologically precise and psychologically revealing. Robert invokes "justification by grace" — the orthodox Reformed doctrine — but immediately connects it to "vengeance" against "adversaries." Grace, in his usage, is not a source of humility but a license for aggression. The grammar of his sentence subordinates grace to vengeance: he writes *in order to* let the wicked know what he has done, and grace is merely the theological framework that authorises the telling.

## The Rhetoric of Unmerited Favour

Robert's spiritual father, the Reverend Wringhim, articulates a version of grace that emphasises unconditional election while minimising the transformative effects of salvation:

> "...the stains which adhere to us by reason of this mixture, which is unavoidable, shall all be washed away..." (006-021)

The passive construction — "shall be washed away" — removes any sense of human agency in sanctification. Sin is something that *happens to* the elect through contact with the reprobate, not something the elect actively commit. This understanding of grace makes moral vigilance unnecessary: if the stains are automatically removed, why struggle against sin?

## The Antinomian Distortion

The novel traces how the doctrine of grace, in the hands of the Wringhims, becomes indistinguishable from antinomianism. Robert reflects on his own moral state with a telling combination of self-satisfaction and theological evasion:

> "I kept myself also free of the sins of idolatry and misbelief, both of a deadly nature; and, upon the whole, I think I had not then broken, that is, absolutely broken, above four out of the ten commandments..." (006-071)

This is not the language of grace producing gratitude and obedience. It is the language of grace producing self-congratulation. Robert keeps a moral ledger — "above four out of the ten commandments" — as though grace were a point system in which partial compliance is sufficient. The irony is that Robert invokes grace while measuring himself by the law he claims to have transcended.

The novel's diagnostic achievement is to show that the doctrine of grace, when combined with the subjective certainty of election, can produce the very moral indifference it is designed to prevent.
