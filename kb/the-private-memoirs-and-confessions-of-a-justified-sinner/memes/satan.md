## Satan in Calvinist Theology

In the Reformed tradition that shapes the novel's theological world, Satan is not a mythological embellishment but a structural necessity. Within the Calvinist framework of predestination, Satan functions as the executor of God's reprobation — the agent who tempts, deceives, and ultimately claims those whom God has not elected to salvation. The Westminster Confession, the doctrinal standard of the Church of Scotland in Hogg's era, affirms both the reality of spiritual warfare and the sovereignty of God over all creaturely agency, including the demonic.

Robert Wringhim's theology amplifies this framework to an extreme. For Wringhim, the world is a battlefield between the elect and the reprobate, and Satan is the "firmest friend of the reprobate" (006-341). This conviction shapes his interpretation of every event: opposition to his will is evidence of satanic influence, and those who resist him are "limbs of Satan" (004-167). The theological category of Satan thus becomes a tool for delegitimising all criticism while reinforcing Wringhim's self-perception as a soldier in a cosmic war.

## Gil-Martin as Satanic Figure

The novel's most significant contribution to the literary representation of Satan is the character of Gil-Martin. Introduced as a mysterious stranger who bears an unsettling resemblance to Wringhim, Gil-Martin progressively reveals attributes that align him with the satanic: shape-shifting ("he seemed to have some demon for a familiar," 004-169), supernatural knowledge of Wringhim's thoughts, and a programme of temptation that escalates from theological persuasion to murder.

Gil-Martin's satanic identity is never declared unambiguously, and this ambiguity is central to the novel's literary effect. The Editor's narrative repeatedly uses demonic language to describe George Colwan's mysterious persecutor: "a fiend of more malignant aspect" (004-131), "the attendance of a demon on some devoted being" (004-135), "some horrid demon by which he was haunted" (004-143). When the Editor finally exclaims "If a demon could inherit flesh and blood" (004-365), the conditional phrasing preserves the epistemological uncertainty that the memoir's first-person perspective demands. The reader, like Wringhim, cannot be certain whether Gil-Martin is literally Satan, a psychological projection, or something between the two.

## The Temptation Narrative

Gil-Martin's method of temptation mirrors the classical satanic strategy of exploiting theological conviction for destructive ends. He does not persuade Wringhim to abandon his faith; rather, he persuades him that his faith authorises — indeed, demands — actions that conventional morality would condemn. When Gil-Martin declares "Satan, I fear, has been busy with you" (006-095), the irony is that the accusation comes from the tempter himself, employing the satanic technique of projection to deepen Wringhim's dependence on his counsel.

The novel's treatment of satanic temptation thus functions as a critique of Antinomian theology. By making the satanic figure the enforcer of Antinomian logic — the logic that the elect are above moral law — Hogg suggests that Antinomianism is itself a satanic deception. The devil's most effective strategy, the novel implies, is not to make men godless but to make them misidentify evil as divine will.

## Narrative Function

Satan serves a crucial narrative function beyond the theological. The uncertainty about Gil-Martin's true nature generates the epistemological instability that distinguishes *Confessions* from conventional gothic fiction. Is Wringhim haunted by a supernatural being, or is he suffering from a psychological disturbance that his Calvinist vocabulary interprets as demonic? The novel sustains both readings simultaneously, making Satan the figure through which theological and psychological explanations compete without resolution.

The closing scenes reinforce this ambiguity. Wringhim is pursued by "all the devils in hell" (006-428) and accused of being "an incarnate devil" (006-366) himself. The identification of Wringhim with the satanic — the tempter become the tormented — completes the novel's arc: the one who claimed to be among the elect dies crying out to the fiends to "take their prey" (006-436).
