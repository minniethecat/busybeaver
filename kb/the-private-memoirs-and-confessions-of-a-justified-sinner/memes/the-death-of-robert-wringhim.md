# The Death of Robert Wringhim

## Description

Robert Wringhim dies in 1712, by his own hand or by supernatural agency — the text is deliberately ambiguous. His final words invoke "the same hope here on earth, and the same assurance of acceptance above" (006-443). He dies as he lived: convinced of his election, sealed in his delusion, unrepentant to the end.

## Narrative Significance

Robert's death is the novel's final and most terrible theological statement. There is no deathbed repentance, no eleventh-hour moral awakening. The Antinomian system Robert inherited and embraced is so complete that it follows him to the grave. His death is not redemption but confirmation — the final proof that a theology without repentance produces a death without hope.
