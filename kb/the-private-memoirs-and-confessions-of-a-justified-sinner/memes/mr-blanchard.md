# Mr. Blanchard

## Biography

Mr. Blanchard was a respected minister of the Church of [[scotland|Scotland]], "reckoned a worthy, pious divine" (006-128). He encountered [[robert-wringhim|Robert Wringhim]] and [[gil-martin|Gil-Martin]] during one of their walks and engaged them in theological debate — specifically challenging the [[antinomianism|Antinomian]] conviction that the elect cannot sin. His challenge profoundly unsettled [[robert-wringhim|Robert]], though [[gil-martin|Gil-Martin]] dismissed Blanchard's concerns.

Shortly after this confrontation, Mr. Blanchard died under suspicious circumstances. [[robert-wringhim|Robert]] himself records that "the overthrow and death of Mr. Blanchard was the first step by which I obtained power over my fellow creatures" (006-137), implying that [[gil-martin|Gil-Martin]] orchestrated his death — or that [[robert-wringhim|Robert]] participated in it — as an initiation into a life of crime.

## Role in the Narrative

Mr. Blanchard serves as a moral counterpoint to the novel's [[antinomianism|Antinomian]] theology. His questioning of [[robert-wringhim|Robert]] — asking how a person can claim assurance of salvation while contemplating sin — represents orthodox Christian moral reasoning against which [[robert-wringhim|Robert]]'s antinomianism is measured and found wanting. His death is the first in the chain of murders that defines the novel's plot, marking the transition from theological abstraction to violent action.

Blanchard's role is structurally significant: he appears only briefly, but his challenge plants a seed of doubt in [[robert-wringhim|Robert]]'s mind — a doubt that [[gil-martin|Gil-Martin]] must work to extinguish through further indoctrination. His death also establishes the pattern of eliminating those who question [[robert-wringhim|Robert]]'s theological self-understanding.

## Key Passages

Blanchard's unsettling challenge to Robert:

> He, however, would not drop it, but took both my principles and me fearfully to task, for Blanchard was an eloquent and powerful-minded old man; and, before we parted, I believe I promised to drop my new acquaintance, and was all but resolved to do it. (006-128)

Gil-Martin's dismissal of Blanchard:

> "Do you not perceive what mighty powers of mind he is possessed of?" said I, "and also how clear and unhesitating he is on some of the most interesting points of divinity?" (006-127)

The death of Blanchard as Robert's initiation into crime:

> "The overthrow and death of Mr. Blanchard was the first step by which I obtained power over my fellow creatures." (006-137)

## Relationships

- **[[robert-wringhim|Robert Wringhim]]**: Blanchard challenged [[robert-wringhim|Robert]]'s [[antinomianism|Antinomian]] convictions, triggering [[gil-martin|Gil-Martin]]'s intervention. His death was [[robert-wringhim|Robert]]'s "first step" toward murder.
- **[[gil-martin|Gil-Martin]]**: Blanchard's theological challenge provoked [[gil-martin|Gil-Martin]]'s fierce rebuttal; [[gil-martin|Gil-Martin]] likely orchestrated Blanchard's death.
- **[[rev-robert-wringhim|Rev. Robert Wringhim]]**: As an orthodox minister, Blanchard represents the moderate Presbyterianism that the elder [[robert-wringhim|Wringhim]]'s extreme Calvinism had departed from.

## Key Appearances

- **The Meeting (006-128–132)**: [[robert-wringhim|Robert]] and [[gil-martin|Gil-Martin]] encounter Blanchard during a walk. Blanchard questions [[robert-wringhim|Robert]]'s theology "fearfully," and [[gil-martin|Gil-Martin]] responds with anger.
- **[[the-public-debate|The Debate]] (006-134–135)**: Over several days, Blanchard's "doubts and doctrines" are the subject of discussion between [[robert-wringhim|Robert]] and [[gil-martin|Gil-Martin]], who dismisses Blanchard as one beginning a journey toward error.
- **The Death (006-137)**: [[robert-wringhim|Robert]] records Blanchard's death as the "first step" in his criminal career.

## Critical Interpretation

Blanchard represents the tradition of orthodox pastoral care — a minister who cares enough to challenge a young man heading toward theological disaster. His death signals the novel's dark turn: when the voice of moral reason is eliminated, nothing stands between [[robert-wringhim|Robert]] and the logic of [[antinomianism|Antinomian]] violence. Critics have noted that Blanchard is one of the few genuinely good characters in the novel whose goodness is not complicated by theological extremism or self-interest.
