# Robert Wringhim

## Biography

Robert Wringhim (also known as Robert Colwan) is the protagonist and first-person narrator of the "Confessions" portion of [[james-hogg|Hogg]]'s novel. Raised by his mother [[arabella-colwan|Rabina]] and the Reverend Robert Wringhim — who is strongly implied to be his biological father — Robert is educated from childhood in the most extreme form of Calvinist predestinarian theology (004-038). He is taught that the elect are infallible and that "a justified person can do no wrong" (004-042).

> "I was born an outcast in the world, in which I was destined to act so conspicuous a part. My mother was a burning and a shining light, in the community of Scottish worthies, and in the days of her virginity had suffered much in the persecution of the saints. But it so pleased Heaven that, as a trial of her faith, she was married to one of the wicked; a man all over spotted with the leprosy of sin." (006-002)
>
> — Robert frames his birth in theological terms: a child of the elect cast among the reprobate

> "My life has been a life of trouble and turmoil; of change and vicissitude; of anger and exultation; of sorrow and of vengeance. My sorrows have all been for a slighted gospel, and my vengeance has been wreaked on its adversaries." (006-001)
>
> — Robert Wringhim's opening self-characterization in his "Confessions"

As a young man, Robert encounters the mysterious [[gil-martin|Gil-Martin]], who becomes his constant companion and spiritual advisor. Under [[gil-martin|Gil-Martin]]'s influence, Robert's theological convictions harden into a comprehensive moral immunity: he becomes convinced that, as one of the elect, all his actions — including murder — are foreordained and therefore righteous (006-103). His first victim is his half-brother [[george-colwan|George Colwan]], whom he harasses and eventually murders (006-273). He goes on to kill his mother (006-357) and attempts to kill others before finally taking his own life in 1712 (006-443).

Robert's childhood is marked by a precocious and unsettling theological fluency. As a boy, he debates the doctrine of "Ineffectual Calling" with his mother, insisting on its reality despite her demurral (006-004). His education under his "reverend father" produces a young man whose intellect is formidable but whose moral sensibility has been systematically disabled by the doctrines he has absorbed.

> "Had I not been sensible that a justified person could do nothing wrong, I should not have been at my ease concerning the statement I had been induced to give on this occasion. I could easily perceive that, by rooting out the weeds from the garden of the Church, I heightened the growth of righteousness; but, as to the tardy way of giving false evidence on matters of such doubtful issue, I confess I saw no great propriety in that." (006-229)
>
> — Robert's conscience stirs faintly at bearing false witness, but his theological certainty overrides all moral hesitation

## Role in the Narrative

Robert is both the novel's protagonist and its theological case study. Through his first-person "Confessions," [[james-hogg|Hogg]] dramatizes the psychological interior of [[antinomianism|Antinomianism]]: how it feels from the inside to be absolutely certain of one's own righteousness while committing objectively terrible acts. The power of the novel lies in Robert's narrative voice — calm, self-justifying, theologically fluent, and utterly devoid of moral self-awareness.

> "I was astonished at my own resolutions, for I had before been a feeble and irresolute creature; but now I felt a power, an energy, and a spirit, which I had never before experienced — and all sprung from a confidence, that I was one of the elect, and that the eternal God had from all eternity decreed my salvation." (006-103)

His narrative is framed by an anonymous "[[the-editor|Editor]]'s Narrative" that provides the external, rationalist perspective on the same events (004-001). The gap between Robert's self-understanding and the [[the-editor|Editor]]'s account of him is the novel's central structural device — and its central theological argument. Where Robert sees himself as a champion of righteousness, the reader increasingly perceives a murderer who has simply learned to baptize his crimes in theological language. The narrative structure forces the reader into an act of moral triangulation: the truth is not simply halfway between the Editor's rationalism and Robert's fanaticism, but emerges from their irreconcilable collision.

The novel's most disturbing psychological insight occurs when Robert begins to suspect — but cannot fully acknowledge — that [[gil-martin|Gil-Martin]] may commit crimes while wearing Robert's own form, leaving Robert with no memory of deeds that others attribute to him (006-327). This fragmentation of identity is the novel's masterstroke: Robert's "justification" has so thoroughly dissolved his moral self that he can no longer distinguish his own actions from those of his demonic double.

## Multi-Perspective Views

### The Editor's View

[[the-editor|The Editor]] presents Robert as a case study in religious fanaticism, documenting his crimes with the dispassionate tone of an Enlightenment rationalist. Where Robert sees divine ordination, the Editor sees pathology:

> "The guilty native of the land was obliged to take to flight; and, for the sake of his family, to leave his native country, and his paternal inheritance in the hands of strangers." (004-363)

The Editor's account draws on legal documents, parish records, and eyewitness testimony — the apparatus of Enlightenment empiricism — to reconstruct events that Robert's confession interprets through a supernatural and theological lens. This epistemological contrast is not neutral: the Editor's confidence in rational explanation is itself subtly ironized by the inexplicable elements of the story that resist his framework, including the mysterious figure of Gil-Martin whom even the Editor cannot fully explain away.

### Gil-Martin's View

[[gil-martin|Gil-Martin]] treats Robert as both protégé and puppet — a soul already predisposed to self-deception who needs only the gentlest push toward damnation. He flatters Robert's sense of election while systematically stripping away every moral restraint (006-124). Gil-Martin's manipulation exploits Robert's deepest psychological vulnerability: the need to believe himself specially chosen, and the terror of discovering otherwise (006-116).

### Bessy Gillies's View

[[bessy-gillies|Bessy Gillies]], the old servant, sees through Robert's companion immediately, recognizing the supernatural evil that Robert himself cannot (or will not) perceive. Her folk wisdom and traditional piety stand in stark contrast to Robert's sophisticated theological self-deception (004-363).

### Modern Critical Views

Critics have read Robert through psychoanalytic, theological, and narratological lenses. The ambiguity of his reliability as a narrator — and the question of whether [[gil-martin|Gil-Martin]] is external demon or internal projection — makes Robert one of the most psychologically complex characters in 19th-century fiction.

## Relationships

- **[[gil-martin|Gil-Martin]]**: Robert's "friend" and spiritual advisor, who may be the devil. [[gil-martin|Gil-Martin]] systematically reinforces Robert's [[antinomianism|Antinomian]] convictions and urges him toward ever-greater crimes (006-124).
- **[[george-colwan|George Colwan]]**: Robert's half-brother, whom Robert envies and eventually murders (006-273). [[george-colwan|George]] represents the rational, moderate, moral life that Robert's theology has rendered impossible for him.
- **Reverend Robert Wringhim**: Robert's spiritual father (and biological father), whose preaching of absolute predestination shapes Robert's entire worldview (004-012).
- **[[arabella-colwan|Rabina]] ([[arabella-colwan|Arabella]]) [[george-colwan|Colwan]]**: Robert's mother, a religious zealot who separates from her husband over theological differences and raises Robert in the [[antinomianism|Antinomian]] sect (004-038).

## Key Appearances

- **Introduction (006-001)**: Robert's memoir opens with an account of his religious upbringing and his conviction of his own election.
- **Meeting [[gil-martin|Gil-Martin]] (006-076)**: Robert's first encounter with his mysterious companion, whose strange appearance and immediate recognition of Robert's "elect" status foreshadow the uncanny doubling to come.
- **Religious Conversion (006-103)**: Robert describes his conviction of election — the theological turning point that enables all his subsequent crimes.
- **Theological Indoctrination (006-116)**: Gil-Martin systematically teaches Robert "the infallibility of the elect, and the preordination of all things that come to pass."
- **Murder of [[george-colwan|George]] (006-273)**: Robert's account of killing his brother, presented in the dispassionate language of theological justification.
- **The Two-Souls Theory (006-327)**: Robert begins to suspect that "two souls" inhabit his body — the most striking expression of the novel's doppelgänger theme and of Robert's psychological disintegration.
- **Final Days (006-443)**: Robert's death, which he faces with the same assurance of election he has maintained throughout.

## Critical Interpretation

Robert Wringhim has been read as a study in religious fanaticism, a case study in self-deception, and — in psychoanalytic readings — as a representation of the divided self. The ambiguity of whether [[gil-martin|Gil-Martin]] is a real supernatural being or a projection of Robert's own psyche makes Robert one of the most psychologically complex characters in 19th-century fiction.

[[james-hogg|Hogg]]'s achievement is to make Robert simultaneously monstrous and pitiable. The reader who expects a Gothic villain encounters instead a young man whose evil is the product of sincerely-held theological convictions — a far more disturbing proposition than simple malice. Robert's narrative is a landmark in the development of the unreliable narrator, anticipating Dostoevsky's underground man and the modernist exploration of subjective consciousness.

Robert Wringhim stands at the head of a tradition of unreliable first-person narrators in fiction that would come to include Poe's murderers, Dostoevsky's Notes from Underground (1864), and ultimately the great modernist experiments with subjective consciousness in Joyce, Woolf, and Nabokov. What distinguishes Robert from his successors is the specifically theological nature of his unreliability: he does not lie in the conventional sense, but rather interprets the world through a theological framework that systematically transvalues evil into righteousness. His narrative is not a confession of guilt but a celebration of election — the "Confessions" of the title are Augustinian in form but anti-Augustinian in substance, since Robert confesses not sin but sanctity. This ironic appropriation of the confessional mode is one of Hogg's most audacious literary moves.

The novel also anticipates the psychological novel of the twentieth century in its treatment of the double or doppelgänger. Robert's relationship with Gil-Martin — in which he cannot always distinguish his own actions from his companion's, and in which he suspects "two souls" may share his body (006-327) — prefigures Freud's concept of the uncanny and the psychoanalytic interest in the divided self. Stevenson, who acknowledged Hogg's influence on *Dr. Jekyll and Mr. Hyde* (1886), found in Robert Wringhim the template for the respectable man whose dark side takes on a life of its own.
