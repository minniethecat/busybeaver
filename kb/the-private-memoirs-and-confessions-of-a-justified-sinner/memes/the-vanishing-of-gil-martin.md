## Summary

The Vanishing of Gil-Martin is not a single event but a recurring motif: Gil-Martin repeatedly disappears from view in ways that defy natural explanation. These vanishings occur throughout both the Editor's Narrative and Robert Wringhim's Private Memoir, serving as the novel's most persistent supernatural signifier. The pattern begins with Gil-Martin's first mysterious appearance and escalates through several key scenes — on Arthur's Seat, in the Edinburgh wynds, and finally in the open country — until his final, permanent disappearance leaves Robert Wringhim to face his fate alone.

## Background

Gil-Martin first appears to Robert Wringhim shortly after the latter's "conversion" and election. From the outset, his nature is ambiguous: he seems to know Robert's inmost thoughts, can appear and disappear at will, and exercises an uncanny influence over Robert's actions. The vanishings are the most visible manifestation of what Robert himself comes to suspect — that Gil-Martin is not a man but a supernatural being, perhaps the Devil in human form.

Robert's own theological framework, steeped in the antinomian Calvinism of his upbringing, provides no reliable means of distinguishing a divine visitation from a diabolical one. The vanishings, therefore, function as both supernatural phenomena and psychological symptoms — the external sign of an internal possession.

## Narrative Accounts

### The Editor's Narrative (004-xxx)

From the Editor's empirical perspective, Gil-Martin's vanishings are reported at second hand, through the testimony of witnesses and the investigation into George Colwan's murder. The Editor notes how Gil-Martin "vanished towards Leith Wynd" (004-289) after being observed following George Colwan through the Edinburgh streets — a scene that establishes the pattern of Gil-Martin as a spectral stalker whose movements cannot be accounted for by ordinary means.

At Arthur's Seat, the multitude that had gathered to watch George Colwan's confrontation with his mysterious pursuer "had vanished like so many thousands of phantoms" (004-086), leaving only the key figures — a narrative detail that blurs the line between Gil-Martin's supernatural disappearances and the more general atmosphere of unreality that surrounds him.

### The Private Memoir (006-xxx)

Robert Wringhim's first-person account provides the most vivid descriptions of the vanishings:

> "I saw him bound from the rocks above the holy well, and vanish. I persuaded myself that I had still a hold of him" (006-212)

This scene, set near St. Anthony's Well on Arthur's Seat, is particularly significant: Robert physically grasps at Gil-Martin, only to have him dissolve from his hands. The verb "persuaded myself" is telling — Robert's grip on reality, like his grip on Gil-Martin, is slipping.

The final vanishing is the most consequential:

> "The door that stood open, in a moment he vanished and left me in the lurch" (006-417)

Here, Gil-Martin abandons Robert at the critical moment, disappearing through an open door and leaving Robert to face the consequences of their shared crimes alone. The phrase "left me in the lurch" — with its connotations of betrayal and abandonment — captures the emotional devastation of this moment.

## Aftermath

Each vanishing deepens Robert's isolation and psychological disintegration. The early vanishings create an atmosphere of uncanny dependency — Robert cannot escape Gil-Martin because Gil-Martin can appear and disappear at will. The later vanishings, particularly the final abandonment, remove the one figure who had ordered Robert's world (however destructively), leaving him utterly alone to face arrest, trial, and death.

The vanishings also serve a crucial narrative function: they repeatedly confirm the supernatural reading of events that the Editor's rationalist frame might otherwise dismiss as delusion or coincidence. By showing Gil-Martin vanishing before witnesses (including George Colwan and the crowd on Arthur's Seat), Hogg ensures the novel maintains its dual epistemological register — both natural and supernatural explanations remain viable.

## Thematic Significance

The Vanishing of Gil-Martin operates on multiple symbolic levels:

- **Demonic ontology**: In Reformed demonology, the Devil can assume bodily form but cannot sustain a true physical body — the vanishings mark Gil-Martin as a being who participates in the material world without being bound by its laws.
- **The instability of evil**: Gil-Martin's ability to appear and disappear mirrors his moral and ontological slipperiness — he is never quite what he seems, always "vanishing" from one identity into another.
- **The collapse of antinomian certainty**: Robert's theological system gave him absolute assurance of election; Gil-Martin's vanishings undermine that certainty by revealing that what Robert took for divine guidance was diabolical deception. The ground literally disappears beneath him.
- **Narrative epistemology**: The vanishings are the novel's most direct challenge to the Editor's rationalist framework. They cannot be explained away, yet they cannot be definitively proven — they exist in the gap between the two narrators' accounts that the reader must navigate.
