## Summary

The Funeral of the Suicide is the concluding event of the novel, recounted in the brief final chapter ("End of the Memoir"). After Robert Wringhim hangs himself — or is driven to hang himself — his body is buried in unconsecrated ground at Cowan's-Croft, a remote hilltop where three estates meet. The burial is performed hastily, under cover of darkness, by five men who consider the suicide a disgrace to the family. Nearly a century later, the Editor visits the grave, which has been recently exhumed, and discovers physical evidence — including a preserved body with a peculiar hairstyle matching descriptions from the Private Memoir — that seems to confirm the manuscript's authenticity. The event closes the novel's frame narrative and offers its final, ambiguous gesture toward the supernatural.

## Background

Robert Wringhim's death by suicide — or at least death under circumstances consistent with suicide — is the culmination of the spiritual and psychological trajectory traced in his Private Memoir. Having been abandoned by Gil-Martin, convicted (or driven to confess) of his crimes, and stripped of his antinomian assurance, Robert ends his life in despair.

The suicide presents a theological and social problem for the community: suicides could not be buried in consecrated ground. The solution — burial at a remote boundary point where three proprietors' lands meet — is a traditional Scottish practice for suicides and outcasts, placing the body in a liminal space that belongs to no single parish or landowner.

The Editor's investigation of this grave, prompted by a letter published in *Blackwood's Magazine* (August 1823), brings the novel full circle: the frame narrative that began with the discovery of the manuscript ends with the discovery of the body.

## Narrative Accounts

The event is narrated in a single voice — the Editor's — drawing on the eyewitness account of James Anderson and the testimony of Robert Laidlaw, one of the original buriers.

### The Original Burial (c. 1712)

The burial, as recounted by Laidlaw to James Anderson (who then reports it to the Editor), was a grim and hurried affair:

> "By the time they reached the top of the Faw-Law, the sun was beginning to skair the east. On this they laid down the body, and digged a deep grave with all expedition; but, when they had done, it was too short, and, the body being stiff, it would not go down; on which Mr. David Anderson, looking to the east and perceiving that the sun would be up on them in a few minutes, set his foot on the suicide's brow, and tramped down his head into the grave with his iron-heeled shoe, until the nose and skull crashed again, and at the same time uttered a terrible curse on the wretch who had disgraced the family" (007-020).

This violent detail — the iron-heeled shoe crushing the suicide's skull — is one of the most visceral images in the novel. It literalises the contempt in which the suicide is held and foreshadows the physical evidence that will be unearthed a century later.

### The Exhumation and Discovery (1823)

The Editor visits the grave after it has been partially exhumed by curiosity-seekers, prompted by the *Blackwood's* letter. He examines the remains and finds — or believes he finds — evidence that corroborates the Private Memoir: the body is unusually preserved, the hair is of a distinctive colour and texture matching Robert Wringhim's self-description, and the skull shows signs of the trampling Laidlaw described.

## Aftermath

The discovery of the body serves as the novel's final evidentiary gesture. The Editor, having presented the Private Memoir as a document of uncertain authenticity, now offers physical corroboration. Yet the evidence is characteristically double-edged: the preserved body seems to confirm the manuscript's account, but the supernatural preservation of a suicide's corpse is itself a phenomenon that troubles the Editor's rationalist frame.

The novel ends not with resolution but with a characteristic Hoggian ambiguity — the evidence is before the reader, but what it proves remains undecided.

## Thematic Significance

**The suicide's grave as narrative boundary**: The grave at Cowan's-Croft, situated where "the lands of three proprietors meet all at one point" (007-003), is a spatial emblem of the novel's own liminality — poised between genres (Gothic, theological treatise, psychological case study), between narrators (Editor and Sinner), and between explanations (natural and supernatural).

**The body as evidence**: The exhumed body is the novel's final and most literal "discovery" — the physical counterpart to the textual discovery of the manuscript. Both discoveries promise to resolve the novel's mysteries; both ultimately deepen them.

**The iron-heeled shoe**: David Anderson's brutal act — trampling the suicide's head into the grave — is an image of the violence that religious and social condemnation inflicts on the outcast. It echoes the novel's broader concern with the cruelty that can be enacted in the name of righteousness.
