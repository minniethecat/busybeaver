# Samuel Scrape

## Biography

Samuel Scrape is a Cameronian servant from the village of [[penpunt|Penpunt]] in Dumfriesshire whom [[robert-wringhim|Robert Wringhim]] is forced to rely upon as a companion during his period of social isolation and legal persecution. Wringhim introduces him as "my man Samuel Scrape, who was a very honest blunt fellow, a staunch Cameronian, but withal very little conversant in religious matters. He said he came from a place called Penpunt, which I thought a name so ludicrous that I called him by the name of his native village, an appellation of which he was very proud" (006-330).

Scrape is one of the characters who knew [[robert-wringhim|Robert]] during a period that [[robert-wringhim|Robert]] himself cannot remember — he insists that [[robert-wringhim|Robert]] hired him personally and paid him wages, while [[robert-wringhim|Robert]] denies ever having seen him before (006-331–332). This fundamental discrepancy, inserted casually into a comic exchange, is one of the novel's most unsettling suggestions that [[robert-wringhim|Robert]]'s identity has been split or doubled by [[gil-martin|Gil-Martin]].

It is Scrape who delivers the shattering news that Andrew [[andrew-handyside|Handyside]] "has been in his grave these six months" (006-306) — revealing to [[robert-wringhim|Robert]] that he has lost months of time, during which his double has been living his life.

## Role in the Narrative

Scrape's country tales, told in broad Scots dialect, provide comic relief while simultaneously deepening the novel's central themes. His anecdote about the Galloway drover and the Penpunt Cameronian (006-334) is a miniature version of the novel's critique of hypocrisy: "A Cameronian's principles never came atween him an' his purse." His tale of the "wives of the clachan" and their folk beliefs about the devil (006-341–344) introduces the novel's great embedded parable — the Auchtermuchty story — which reframes the novel's theological argument in the language of folk superstition.

Scrape represents the ordinary, unlettered folk of early 18th-century [[scotland|Scotland]] whose earthy common sense and religious pragmatism offer an alternative perspective on the theological extremism of [[robert-wringhim|Robert Wringhim]] and [[rev-robert-wringhim|his father].

## Key Passages

The Galloway drover anecdote — a comic morality tale about religious principle and self-interest:

> "An' oh, sir, whilk o' thae, think you, is the most precious? For instance, an a Galloway drover had comed to the town o' Penpunt, an' said to a Cameronian (the folk's a' Cameronians there), 'Sir, I want to buy your cow' ... The Cameronian then turns out to be a civil man, an' canna bide to make the man baith a feele an' liar at the same time, afore a' his associates; an' therefore he pits his principles aff at the side, to be kind o' sleepin' partner, as it war, an' brings up his good breeding to stand at the counter: he pockets the money, gies the Galloway drover time o' day, an' comes his way. An' wha's to blame? Man mind yoursel is the first commandment. A Cameronian's principles never came atween him an' his purse." (006-334)

Scrape's perplexity at Wringhim's denial of their acquaintance:

> "Ye may as weel say, master, that water's no water, or that, stanes are no stanes. But that's just your gate, an' it's a great pity, aye to do a thing an profess the clean contrair." (006-332)

The witches' folk-theology of the devil and proud professors:

> "Gin ever he observes a proud professor, wha has mae than ordinary pretensions to a divine calling, and that reards and prays till the very howlets learn his preambles, that's the man Auld Simmie fixes on to mak a dishclout o'." (006-344)
