# Repentance

## Definition

Repentance (Greek *metanoia*, "change of mind") is the act of turning from sin to God, involving intellectual recognition of sin, emotional sorrow for it, and volitional turning toward righteousness. In Reformed theology, repentance is inseparable from faith — they are two aspects of the same conversion — but repentance is not the ground of justification; that is faith alone. In Hogg's novel, repentance is the missing element: Robert Wringhim's tragedy is not that he sins, but that he never repents.

## Theological Background

The Westminster Confession (Chapter 15) defines "repentance unto life" as an evangelical grace whereby "a sinner, out of the sight and sense not only of the danger, but also of the filthiness and odiousness of his sins... turns from them all to God." Crucially, the Confession teaches that repentance is a gift of God, not a human achievement — "the grace of repentance is not to be denied to such as fall into sin." This pastoral provision is precisely what Robert Wringhim cannot receive, because his Antinomian theology has taught him that he has no sins from which to repent.

## In the Novel

The theme of repentance — or more precisely, its absence — runs through the novel like a dark thread. Robert's father teaches that "the grace of repentance" is "withheld from me" (006-061), a statement that Robert repeats to justify his impenitence: "I could not help that, the grace of repentance being withheld from me" (006-070). This is the novel's darkest theological irony: the very doctrine of grace becomes a reason *not* to repent.

The novel's conclusion drives the point home. Robert, facing death, expresses no repentance — only terror. His final words invoke the "day of grace" and "the hope of acceptance," but there is no trace of sorrow for his crimes (006-443). Hogg's point is clear: a theology that eliminates repentance eliminates the very mechanism by which a sinner might be restored.

## Key Passages

> "...the grace of repentance withheld from me..." (006-061)

> "...I could not help that, the grace of repentance being withheld from me..." (006-070)

> "...but now that I hope the day of grace is returned, I am again drawn..." (006-320)

> "The same hope here on earth, and the same assurance of acceptance above! Amen." (006-443)

## Critical Significance

Hogg's treatment of repentance is arguably the novel's most devastating theological critique. By showing how the Antinomian system logically eliminates the need for repentance, Hogg exposes a fatal flaw in any theology that so emphasizes divine sovereignty that it renders human moral response irrelevant. The novel becomes, in this reading, a powerful argument for the necessity of repentance — not as a work that earns salvation, but as the indispensable evidence that faith is genuine.
