# Glasgow

## Description

Glasgow in the early 18th century was Scotland's second city, a growing commercial center on the River Clyde whose merchants were beginning to build the trading networks — particularly with the Americas — that would make the city wealthy in the following decades. It was smaller and less cosmopolitan than Edinburgh but already developing its distinctive character as a commercial and industrial city. The cathedral and the university gave Glasgow ecclesiastical and intellectual weight, while its position on the Clyde made it a gateway for trade with Ireland and the Atlantic world.

## In the Narrative

Glasgow plays a crucial role in the novel's marital geography. The Laird of Dalcastle marries the daughter of a Glasgow magistrate — a match that proves disastrous:

> "...the daughter of a Baillie Orde, of Glasgow. This proved a conjunction anything but agreeable to the parties contracting..." (004-002)

The city serves as a place of refuge and return throughout the narrative. After the wedding night goes poorly, the bride returns to her father's house:

> "...returned to her father's house in Glasgow, after having been a night on the road..." (004-023)

Letters are dispatched between Dalcastle and Glasgow as the estranged couple negotiate their fractured marriage:

> "...letter after letter was dispatched to Glasgow; and at length, to the lady's great content, she was brought back..." (004-038)

The city also appears in the novel's complex web of disputed paternity. George Colwan the younger is taunted with the suggestion that the "crazy minister's son from Glasgow" — young Robert Wringhim — is his mother's true son:

> "Mercy be about us, Sir! Is this the crazy minister's son from Glasgow?" (004-074)

A lady from Glasgow had been a follower of the charismatic minister whose preaching transforms the Dalcastle household:

> "...whose lady was originally from Glasgow, and had been a hearer and, of course, a great admirer of the late minister..." (004-093)

Later, Glasgow becomes the scene of crisis and public disorder. After the murder of a popular preacher, the entire city is thrown into upheaval:

> "Great was the confusion that day in Glasgow. The most popular of all their preachers of morality was (what they called) murdered in cold blood, and a strict and extensive search was made for the assassin." (006-156)

> "...the alarm in and about Glasgow was prodigious. The country was divided into two political parties, the court and the country party, the former held meetings, issued proclamations, and offered rewards..." (006-157)

## Historical Context

Glasgow in the decades around 1700 had a population of perhaps 12,000 to 15,000 — a fraction of Edinburgh's — but it was growing rapidly. The city's prosperity was built on trade: tobacco from Virginia, sugar from the Caribbean, linen manufacture, and coastal shipping. The Union of 1707, while deeply unpopular with many Scots, opened the English colonial markets to Glasgow merchants, leading to the dramatic expansion of the tobacco trade in the following decades.

Religiously, Glasgow was a stronghold of Presbyterian orthodoxy but also a site of sectarian division. The city had a significant Episcopalian minority and was a center of the early Secession movement. The figure of "a Baillie Orde" who gives his daughter in marriage to the Laird of Dalcastle reflects the social world of the Glasgow merchant elite — prosperous, politically connected, and concerned with the maintenance of religious respectability. Baillies were senior magistrates in Scottish burghs, occupying the intersection of commercial, judicial, and ecclesiastical authority.

The Glasgow described in the novel is a city where religious fervor could ignite public disorder — the "prodigious" alarm following the preacher's murder (006-157) reflects the volatile mix of theology, politics, and mob violence that characterized urban Scotland in the period before and after the Union.

## Key Scenes

**The failed marriage alliance (004-002)**: The novel's foundational conflict is launched through a cross-city marital arrangement — the Laird of Dalcastle marries the Glasgow Baillie's daughter — that fractures along religious lines.

**The bride's return to Glasgow (004-023)**: After the disastrous wedding night, the bride immediately retreats to her father's house in Glasgow, establishing the city as a sanctuary from the irreligious atmosphere of Dalcastle.

**The epistolary negotiation (004-038)**: Letters exchanged between Dalcastle and Glasgow represent the attempt to repair the broken marriage through the formal channels of respectability — an attempt that ultimately fails.

**The murder and public alarm (006-156–006-157)**: The killing of the popular morality preacher in Glasgow turns the city into a theater of partisan conflict, with both political factions exploiting the event for their own purposes.

**The Glasgow cousin and the bridal bed (004-017)**: The laird finds his bride "in the same bed with her Glasgow cousin who had acted as bridesmaid" — an episode that combines farce with the suggestion of female solidarity against the laird's coarse advances.

## Significance

Glasgow's presence in the novel, while secondary to Edinburgh, contributes to the narrative's documentary texture. By referencing real Scottish locations at various scales — from Arthur's Seat to Edinburgh to Glasgow to the Highlands — Hogg grounds his theological fable in a recognizable geographical world. Glasgow specifically anchors the marital plot: it is the bride's home city, the site of the Baillie Orde connection, and a place of public disturbance that mirrors the inner turmoil of the protagonist.

The city also serves as a marker of religious respectability distinct from both Edinburgh's Enlightenment rationalism and the rural piety of the Covenanting tradition. The Glasgow connection represents a particular form of urban Presbyterianism — commercially oriented, socially respectable, but capable of generating the intense religious enthusiasm that draws the lady of Dalcastle toward the minister Wringhim and his radical teachings.
