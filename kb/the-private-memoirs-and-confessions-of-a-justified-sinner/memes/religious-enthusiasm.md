# Religious Enthusiasm

**Religious enthusiasm** was a contested category in 18th-century British religious discourse, referring to the claim of direct divine inspiration or revelation independent of — and often contrary to — reason, ecclesiastical authority, and the established order. In *The Private Memoirs and Confessions of a Justified Sinner*, James Hogg portrays enthusiasm not as a merely historical phenomenon but as the live psychological force that enables Robert Wringhim's moral dissolution.

## Historical Context

### The 18th-Century Discourse of Enthusiasm

In the decades following the Restoration (1660) and the Glorious Revolution (1688), "enthusiasm" became a term of condemnation in British public discourse. It was associated with the sectarian excesses of the Civil War period — the Levellers, Diggers, Ranters, and Fifth Monarchists — and was understood as a threat to both civil order and rational religion. John Locke devoted a chapter of his *Essay Concerning Human Understanding* (1689) to the critique of enthusiasm, defining it as the substitution of "the ungrounded Fancies of a Man's own Brain" for "both Reason and Revelation."[^1]

In Scotland, the legacy of the Covenanters and the ongoing disputes between Moderate and Evangelical parties within the Church of Scotland kept the question of enthusiasm alive. The fear was that claims of direct spiritual experience would undermine the authority of Scripture, the institutional Church, and the moral law.

### The Seceder Tradition

The novel's Rev. Wringhim belongs to a recognisable type: the seceding Presbyterian minister who has departed from the established Church of Scotland over issues of doctrinal purity. The seceder churches, formed after the splits of 1733 and 1761, were often characterised by their opponents as breeding grounds for enthusiasm — places where doctrinal rigour combined with institutional isolation to produce extravagant claims of spiritual experience.

## Enthusiasm in the Novel

### Rev. Wringhim's Zeal

The novel's first characterisation of enthusiasm appears in its description of the elder Rev. Wringhim's preaching. His doctrines are presented as a heightened, distorted form of Reformation teaching:

> "Hers were not the tenets of the great reformers, but theirs mightily overstrained and deformed. Theirs was an unguent hard to be swallowed; but hers was that unguent embittered and overheated until nature could not longer bear it." (004-003)

The metaphor of "overheating" is precise: enthusiasm, in the 18th-century understanding, was doctrine brought to a pathological temperature. The "flaming predestinarian divine" (004-003) is literally inflamed — his zeal has passed from conviction to pathology.

### Robert Wringhim's Inflamed Zeal

The younger Robert inherits and intensifies this enthusiastic disposition. His confession repeatedly uses the language of "zeal" to describe his actions, even — especially — his worst ones:

> "...for it was done with all the zeal and enthusiasm of a devotee to the true cause." (006-104)

The irony is devastating. The "true cause" Robert serves is murder, and the "zeal and enthusiasm" with which he pursues it is indistinguishable, in his own account, from religious devotion. Hogg's point is precisely that enthusiasm blurs the distinction between holy zeal and homicidal fanaticism.

The novel's final retrospective judgement on Robert explicitly names enthusiasm as the root cause:

> "...some great mind led astray by enthusiasm or some overpowering passion." (006-352)

The ambiguity between "enthusiasm" and "overpowering passion" is deliberate — enthusiasm *is* the overpowering passion, religious emotion detached from moral reason.

### Enthusiasm as Communal Contagion

Hogg portrays enthusiasm not as an individual pathology but as a communicable one. Robert's zeal is stoked by his mysterious companion:

> "...though he often aroused my zeal to the highest pitch, still my heart trembled." (006-144)

The relationship between Robert and the stranger (Gil-Martin / the double) functions as an enthusiasm feedback loop: each inflames the other's zeal, creating a closed system of mutual reinforcement that excludes all external correction.

## Enthusiasm and Antinomianism

Religious enthusiasm and Antinomianism are structurally linked in the novel. Enthusiasm provides the psychological mechanism — the felt certainty of divine approval — while Antinomianism provides the theological justification — the doctrine that the elect are freed from the moral law. Together, they form a closed system in which any action can be re-described as righteous:

1. **Enthusiasm** supplies the subjective conviction: "I feel God's approval."
2. **Antinomianism** supplies the objective warrant: "The elect cannot sin."
3. **Together** they produce the conclusion: "My action (whatever it is) is righteous."

This toxic synthesis is what the novel diagnoses as the distinctive spiritual danger of hyper-Calvinism.

## Relation to Other Concepts

### Religious Delusion

Where [[religious-delusion]] describes the *cognitive* error — the false belief that one is specially favoured by God — enthusiasm describes the *affective* state that sustains it: the heat of conviction that makes the delusion feel like revelation. See [[religious-delusion]].

### Fanaticism

The novel uses "fanaticism" once explicitly:

> "I have heard much of the rage of fanaticism in former days, but nothing exceeds this." (004-375)

Fanaticism, in this context, is the social expression of enthusiasm — the point at which private zeal becomes public violence. If enthusiasm is the inward fire, fanaticism is the outward conflagration.

### Assurance of Salvation

The orthodox Reformed doctrine of [[assurance-of-salvation]] held that believers could be confident of their salvation through the inward witness of the Holy Spirit, confirmed by the outward evidence of a changed life. Enthusiasm short-circuits this: it claims assurance without the confirming evidence of moral transformation.[^2]

## Hogg's Diagnostic Purpose

Hogg was writing in a post-Enlightenment Scotland where the "age of enthusiasm" was supposedly past. By setting his novel in the early 18th century and locating enthusiasm in the seceder tradition, he was making a double argument: first, that the psychological conditions for enthusiasm persisted within Scottish Presbyterianism; and second, that the Enlightenment's dismissal of enthusiasm as mere superstition underestimated its destructive power.

The novel's formal innovation — the dual narrative of editor and sinner — enacts this diagnostic purpose. The editor's rational, empirical narrative cannot comprehend Robert's actions; only Robert's own enthusiastic confession, read against itself, reveals the inner logic of fanaticism.
