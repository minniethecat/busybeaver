# George Colwan

## Overview

George Colwan is the moral center of the novel's first half — the character whose decency makes [[robert-wringhim|Robert]]'s fanaticism visible by contrast. He is "the accomplished George Colwan" (004-309), a young man of "unprecedented unpopularity" only among those who have been taught to despise moderation itself (004-130). His murder is the novel's central crime, the act that proves, more than any theological argument could, the destructive power of [[antinomianism|Antinomian]] conviction when it enters the sphere of human action.

## Biography

George Colwan was born to the Laird of [[dalcastle|Dalcastle]] and his wife [[arabella-colwan|Rabina]], and raised by his father after his mother's religious fanaticism drove the couple apart. In 1687, his father "George Colwan succeeded his uncle of the same name" to the [[dalcastle|Dalcastle]] estate (004-001), establishing the family line that George would inherit. He grew into a moderate, sociable young man — the antithesis of his zealot half-brother [[robert-wringhim|Robert]].

His life is cut short when [[robert-wringhim|Robert]], driven by envy and convinced by [[gil-martin|Gil-Martin]] that George is an obstacle to be removed, stalks and ultimately murders him. "The very hour, on which George Colwan was slain" (004-241) is established through witness testimony, marking the murder as a historically verifiable event within the novel's frame narrative.

### Education and Youth

George was raised in a household defined by the rupture between his parents' religious convictions. Unlike [[robert-wringhim|Robert]], who was raised under the [[rev-robert-wringhim|Reverend Wringhim]]'s Antinomian tutelage, George received a conventional education:

> "George was brought up with his father, and educated partly at the parish school, and partly at home, by a tutor hired for the purpose. He was a generous and kind-hearted youth; always ready to oblige, and hardly ever dissatisfied with anybody." (004-062)

This characterization establishes George's fundamental disposition: generosity, kindness, and an easy-going temperament. The contrast with Robert, who is described in the same passage as being raised by the Reverend Wringhim under very different moral instruction, is deliberate and structural. George's moderation is not a philosophical position he has arrived at through reasoning — it is the natural fruit of a life lived without theological terror.

### The Edinburgh Years

George was sent to Edinburgh for his education, entering the social world of the capital's young gentry:

> "...the young laird of Dalcastle and Balgrennan. George, being his only son, was sent to the College of Edinburgh..." (004-071)

In Edinburgh, George distinguished himself in athletic pursuits, particularly tennis, where "the prowess and agility of the young squire drew forth the loudest plaudits of approval from his associates" (004-065). His physical vitality and social ease are important counterpoints to [[robert-wringhim|Robert]]'s brooding, solitary fanaticism. George inhabits his body and his world naturally; Robert inhabits neither.

### The Stalking and Murder

The novel's most sustained dramatic sequence is Robert's campaign of stalking against George, culminating in his murder. George's response to the persecution reveals his character: rather than retaliating, he tries to avoid conflict, seeking the open air of [[arthurs-seat|Arthur's Seat]] for relief:

> "George was, from infancy, of a stirring active disposition and could not endure confinement; and, having been of late much restrained in his youthful exercises by this singular persecutor, he grew uneasy under such restraint, and, one morning, chancing to awaken very early, he arose to make an excursion to the top of Arthur's Seat, to breathe the breeze of the summer dawn." (004-138)

On the summit of Arthur's Seat, George experiences the novel's most sublime moment — the Brocken spectre, a "sublunary rainbow, this terrestrial glory" (004-140) spread beneath his feet, with a giant shadow-figure visible in the mist. The apparition, widely interpreted as a manifestation of [[gil-martin|Gil-Martin]], terrifies George — it is the first intrusion of the supernatural (or the psychologically uncanny) into his rational world, and a premonition of his death.

## Character Through Contrast

Hogg constructs George's character primarily through contrast with [[robert-wringhim|Robert]]. The novel's diptych structure — the Editor's Narrative followed by Robert's Memoir — presents two radically different ways of seeing the same events. In the Editor's Narrative, George appears as he was to the external, social world: a decent young man, well-liked, persecuted for no comprehensible reason. In Robert's Memoir, George barely registers as a person — he is "the young laird his brother" (004-065), an obstacle, a target, one among many "sinful men" (006-176) who oppose the righteous.

This asymmetry is central to the novel's moral vision. George's very inability to comprehend Robert's theological hatred is both his virtue and his doom. He lives in a world where character, reputation, and social bonds matter; Robert lives in a world where they are irrelevant next to the eternal decree. The collision between these two worlds is what the novel narrates, and George is its first casualty.

## Role in the Narrative

George functions as the narrative's moral norm. He appears primarily in the "[[the-editor|Editor]]'s Narrative" portion of the novel, where he is described from an external, rationalist perspective. This structural positioning is significant: George belongs to the world of evidence, reason, and observable behavior — the world that [[robert-wringhim|Robert]]'s theology has taught him to despise.

The "unpopularity of the younger George Colwan could no longer be concealed from the world" (004-130) only after [[robert-wringhim|Robert]]'s campaign of whispers and stalking has done its work, illustrating how even a good reputation can be destroyed by sustained theological malice.

## Relationships

- **[[robert-wringhim|Robert Wringhim]]**: His half-brother and murderer. George seems almost unaware of [[robert-wringhim|Robert]]'s animosity until it is too late.
- **The [[laird-of-dalcastle|Laird of Dalcastle]]**: His father, who raises him and whose moderate religious views he inherits. Described as "the young laird of [[dalcastle|Dalcastle]] and Balgrennan" (004-071).
- **[[miss-logan|Miss Logan]]**: A family friend who suspects [[robert-wringhim|Robert]]'s guilt and tries to bring him to justice.
- **Mr. [[drummond-of-logie|Drummond]]**: A "young gentleman" with whom George has a fateful altercation (004-182).
- **[[adam-gordon|Adam Gordon]]**: George's "young and intimate friend" (004-130), who tries to convince him to take the threat seriously.

## Critical Interpretation

George is the novel's most straightforwardly sympathetic character, but he is not merely a passive victim. His rationality and moderation represent a philosophical alternative to [[robert-wringhim|Robert]]'s fanaticism — an alternative that the novel neither fully endorses nor rejects. The fact that George's reasonableness cannot save him from [[robert-wringhim|Robert]]'s theological certainty is one of the novel's darkest implications: decency is not self-defending.

George Colwan also represents a particular Scottish type: the moderate, sociable, post-Union gentleman who moves easily in Edinburgh's polite society, attends the College, plays tennis, and maintains the old traditions of hospitality. He is the heir to an older Scotland of feasting and conviviality — the Scotland his father the [[laird-of-dalcastle|Laird]] embodies — and his death is also, symbolically, the death of that Scotland at the hands of a new, more ideologically rigid order.
