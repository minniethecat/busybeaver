# Finnieston

## Description

Finnieston in the early 18th century was a wooded dell on the western outskirts of Glasgow, part of the estate that gave the area its name. Before its transformation by 19th-century industrial development, it was a rural landscape of fields, woods, and streams — a place of quiet walks and private conversation.

## In the Novel

Finnieston appears in the novel as the setting for the Reverend Wringhim's private walks with his adopted son Robert, where theological instruction takes place in pastoral seclusion:

> "...through the field and wood of Finnieston, in which my reverend preceptor and I spent many an hour of sacred contemplation..." (006-106)

Later, after Robert's encounters with Gil-Martin have taken a darker turn, Finnieston becomes a place of anxious solitude:

> "...I seemed to be walking by himself in Finnieston Dell the greater part of Friday, pondering on the strange events of the preceding days..." (006-141)

The transformation of Finnieston from a site of peaceful instruction to one of solitary dread mirrors the novel's broader trajectory — the corruption of innocence by theological狂热.

## Significance

Finnieston functions as a moral-geographical marker in the novel. The dell is first introduced as a setting for the transmission of Calvinist doctrine from father to son; later, after Wringhim's encounters with Gil-Martin, it becomes the scene of troubled isolation. This doubleness — the same place carrying opposite emotional registers — echoes the novel's structural device of the double narrative.
