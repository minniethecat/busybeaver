## Theological Context

In Reformed theology, the relationship between faith and works is governed by a careful distinction: good works are the evidence, not the ground, of salvation. The Westminster Confession affirms that believers are "created in Christ Jesus unto good works" (Ephesians 2:10), and that "good works, done in obedience to God's commandments, are the fruits and evidences of a true and lively faith." This is not works-righteousness (salvation earned by moral effort) but works as the necessary consequence and visible sign of genuine faith.

The Antinomian distortion — which the novel attributes to the Reverend Wringhim's teaching and which Robert Wringhim embraces — collapses this distinction. By treating all good works as inherently meritorious in the wrong sense (as attempts to earn salvation), Antinomian theology concludes that the truly elect have no need of them. This is the logic that Robert articulates explicitly: "the preachers up of good works I abhorred, and to this hour I abhor them" (006-062).

## Wringhim's Rejection of Good Works

Wringhim's memoir documents his progressive rejection of the category of good works with theological precision. His abhorrence is not a passing sentiment but a systematic position derived from his understanding of grace. He refuses to "regard either my good works, or my evil deeds, as in the smallest degree influencing the decrees of the Almighty concerning my eternal salvation" (006-071), a formulation that is orthodox in its denial of merit but Antinomian in its practical consequence: if neither good nor evil actions affect one's standing before God, moral distinctions become theologically irrelevant.

This position reaches its rhetorical climax in Wringhim's declaration that he "would hold no communion with God at all, on account of good works" (006-081). The language of "communion" is theologically significant: in Reformed piety, communion with God is the goal of the spiritual life, and good works are one of the means by which that communion is cultivated. To reject communion on account of good works is to sever the connection between ethics and relationship with God entirely.

The Editor's summary judgment reinforces the critique: Wringhim was characterised by "the idea of any dependence on good works, and morality, forsooth!" (006-400). The dismissive "forsooth" captures the Antinomian contempt for moral effort — a contempt that the novel portrays as spiritually catastrophic.

## The Social Consequences

The rejection of good works has social as well as theological consequences. Wringhim's refusal to evaluate actions by their moral content enables the escalation from doctrinal error to murder. When he describes his brother's death as advancing "the cause of righteousness" (006-169), the irony depends on the reader's recognition that "righteousness" in Wringhim's vocabulary has been emptied of moral content and filled with the merely forensic — it means only "declared righteous by God," with no implication of actual righteousness in conduct.

The novel's critique is that Antinomianism's rejection of good works does not merely produce theological error but active harm. A theology that declares moral categories irrelevant to salvation does not make its adherents morally neutral; it makes them morally unaccountable, and the unaccountable will reliably do more harm than the merely weak.

## The Novel's Implicit Theology of Works

Against Wringhim's Antinomianism, the novel offers an implicit theology of works through its narrative structure. The Editor's narrative consistently evaluates characters by their actions: George Colwan is presented as morally admirable because of what he does — his defence of his mother, his restraint under provocation, his refusal to retaliate against his brother. This narrative valuation of conduct constitutes a tacit argument that good works matter, not as the ground of salvation but as the visible evidence of character and the foundation of social life.

The tragedy of Wringhim is that his theology has deprived him of this common-sense moral framework. He cannot recognise good works when he sees them because his theological categories have pre-classified them as irrelevant. The novel thus functions as a parable about the moral consequences of bad theology: the rejection of good works may begin as a doctrinal refinement, but it ends as a licence for evil.
