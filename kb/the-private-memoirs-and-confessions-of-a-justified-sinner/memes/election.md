# Election

## Definition

Election (or unconditional election) is the Calvinist doctrine that God, from all eternity, has chosen specific individuals to be recipients of saving grace. This choice is "unconditional" — it is not based on any foreseen faith, virtue, or merit in the elect, but rests solely on God's sovereign will. The elect are those predestined to eternal life; the non-elect (the reprobate) are passed over and ordained to damnation. In Hogg's novel, the question of who is and is not among the elect — and how one can know — is the psychological engine that drives the plot toward tragedy. The doctrine's epistemological problem (how can a finite human being know an eternal divine decree?) becomes the narrative's central dramatic tension.

## Theological Background

The doctrine of election was formalized at the Synod of Dort (1618–1619), which responded to the Arminian Remonstrance by affirming the "five points of Calvinism" (TULIP): Total depravity, Unconditional election, Limited atonement, Irresistible grace, and Perseverance of the saints. The Synod taught that election is "the unchangeable purpose of God, whereby, before the foundation of the world, He hath, out of mere grace, according to the sovereign good pleasure of His own will, chosen from the whole human race... a certain number of persons to redemption in Christ."

In Scotland, the doctrine was preached with particular intensity during the Covenanting period and its aftermath. The emotional dynamics of election preaching — the alternation between terror (for those who feared they were reprobate) and exultation (for those convinced they were elect) — created a distinctive religious psychology that Hogg knew intimately from his own Border upbringing. By Hogg's time (early 19th century), the more extreme applications of the doctrine — particularly the claim that the elect could infallibly know their own election — were increasingly controversial and became a target of satirical treatment. Hogg's novel represents the most sustained literary engagement with the psychological consequences of election theology in Scottish literature.

## In the Novel

Election is the status Robert Wringhim believes he possesses — and it is this belief that authorizes everything he does. His father's sermons teach that the elect are infallible, and his companion Gil-Martin constantly reassures him that he is among the chosen. The novel traces how the *subjective conviction* of election becomes a license for objectively criminal behavior.

The concept appears at several crucial narrative junctures:

- **The Sermon on Election**: The Reverend Wringhim's sermon on "justification and final election" (004-059) reduces his congregation to emotional collapse, but leaves them with a dangerous assurance. The sermon handles "some small point somewhere between justification and final election that the minister, in the heat of his zeal, had cut short" (004-059) — a satirical indication that the doctrine is being manipulated for rhetorical effect rather than carefully expounded.

- **The Calling and Election**: "The calling and election of one of its members; or rather... the membership of one who was of the elect" (006-103) becomes the occasion for Robert's formal induction into the Antinomian circle. The slippage between "calling" (a visible church process) and "election" (an invisible divine decree) is characteristic of the Antinomian confusion that the novel critiques.

- **The Public Preaching of Election**: Robert preaches publicly on "the great doctrine of the election of grace" (006-180), using it to justify his worldview and to interrogate his hearers: "Now, friend, you must tell me if you pertain to this chosen number. It is in every man's power to ascertain this, and it is every man's duty to do it" (006-180). The claim that election is within every man's power to "ascertain" is precisely the Antinomian error — it reduces an eternal divine decree to a subjective human judgment.

- **The Certainty of Election Becomes Damnation**: By the novel's end, Robert's "assurance of final election, and confirmation, by an eternal decree" (006-425) is revealed as demonic deception. He clings to the doctrine that once saved him psychologically even as it damns him spiritually. The irony is devastating: the election he believes himself to possess is the very mechanism of his destruction.

The psychological dynamic that Hogg explores is the way election functions as a closed epistemic system. Once Robert is convinced he is elect, any evidence against this conviction is dismissed as temptation or the language of reprobation; any evidence for it — including flattery from Gil-Martin — is eagerly embraced. This is the psychology of the "echo chamber" avant la lettre, and Hogg's novel is among the first literary works to trace its mechanisms in detail.

## Critical Significance

Hogg's use of the election doctrine is particularly sophisticated because he does not simply satirize it — he dramatizes its *epistemological* problem. The novel asks: if election is an eternal decree hidden in the mind of God, how can any human being know they are elect? Robert's tragedy is that he accepts subjective feelings and the flattery of Gil-Martin as proof of his election, when in fact these are evidence of his damnation. The novel thus exposes the psychological danger of a doctrine that, in the wrong hands, replaces moral self-examination with metaphysical self-certainty.

Moreover, Hogg shows how the doctrine of election, when combined with its corollary doctrine of reprobation, creates a binary moral universe in which the elect are beyond criticism and the reprobate are beyond redemption. This binary is the novel's deepest structure: every character is classified as elect or reprobate, and this classification determines how they are treated. The murder of George Colwan is, in Robert's mind, simply the working-out of this classification — he is elect, his brother is reprobate, and the reprobate have no claim on the elect's moral consideration. The novel's enduring power lies in its demonstration that the most dangerous ideas are not those that are obviously false but those that are true in a sense — and false in the sense that matters most.
