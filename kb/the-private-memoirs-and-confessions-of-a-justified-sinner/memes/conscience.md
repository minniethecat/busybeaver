# Conscience

## Definition

Conscience is the human capacity for moral self-reflection and judgment — the "inner voice" that evaluates one's own actions against a standard of right and wrong. In the Christian tradition, conscience is understood as a God-given faculty, part of the *imago Dei*, though it can be corrupted, hardened, or silenced by sin. In Hogg's novel, the destruction of conscience — its progressive silencing through bad theology and worse companionship — is the central psychological tragedy.

## Literary Context

The exploration of conscience was a central theme in Romantic and Gothic literature. Writers such as Mary Shelley (*Frankenstein*), James Hogg, and later Robert Louis Stevenson (*Dr. Jekyll and Mr. Hyde*) were fascinated by the phenomenon of the divided self and the possibility that conscience could be compartmentalized or extinguished. Hogg's contribution to this tradition is distinctive: he traces the silencing of conscience not to psychological pathology but to *theological conviction* — specifically, to the Antinomian belief that the elect are beyond moral judgment. This makes *Confessions* a uniquely theological contribution to the Gothic tradition of the double.

## In the Novel

Conscience in the novel is not absent — it is systematically suppressed. Robert Wringhim records moments of doubt and moral unease, but each time, Gil-Martin redirects him to the doctrines of election and predestination: if Robert is elect, his conscience can have nothing legitimate to say. The novel traces this process with clinical precision:

- Early in the memoir, Robert feels twinges of unease about his actions but dismisses them as the remnants of his "unregenerate" state.
- Gil-Martin explicitly teaches Robert to distrust his conscience: moral feelings are the voice of the law, and the elect are beyond the law.
- By the novel's end, Robert's conscience is fully cauterized — he murders without hesitation and describes his crimes in the dispassionate language of theological reasoning.
- The novel's final horror is not that Robert has a guilty conscience, but that he has *no* conscience at all: he goes to his death unrepentant, still convinced of his election.

## Critical Significance

Hogg's treatment of conscience represents a major contribution to the Gothic exploration of the divided self. Unlike the scientific or psychological explanations of the double that would become common later in the 19th century, Hogg's account is theological: conscience is destroyed not by trauma or mental illness but by *doctrine*. This makes the novel a profound study of how belief systems can restructure the moral self — for good or for ill.
