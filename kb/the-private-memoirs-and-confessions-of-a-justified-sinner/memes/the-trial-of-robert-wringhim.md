# The Trial of Robert Wringhim

## Summary

After George Colwan's murder, Robert Wringhim is brought to trial before the Edinburgh courts. Despite the accumulation of circumstantial evidence assembled by Miss Logan and the testimony of Bell Calvert, Robert is acquitted. The court, operating within the constraints of legal evidence and reasonable doubt, cannot reach the truth that the reader — having access to Robert's Confessions — already knows. The acquittal represents not a miscarriage of justice in the ordinary sense, but a failure of the entire epistemological framework on which legal justice depends.

## Background

The trial is the culmination of a long investigation into George Colwan's death. Initially, suspicion fell on Thomas Drummond, who was tried in absentia, convicted, and outlawed (004-191). But Miss Logan, the victim's devoted friend, was not satisfied. She had observed Robert Wringhim's obsessive stalking of his half-brother — the "constant and sullen attendance of the one brother upon the other" (004-176) — and was convinced of his guilt.

The breakthrough came through Bell Calvert, a woman of dubious reputation who had witnessed key events and possessed knowledge that no innocent observer could have. Together, Miss Logan and Bell Calvert pieced together the case against Robert. They took their evidence to Sir Thomas Wallace of Craigie, the Lord Justice Clerk, who "examined Calvert very minutely, and seemed deeply interested in her evidence" (004-371). On the strength of their testimony, it was "judged proper to apprehend the present Laird of Dalcastle, and bring him to his trial" (004-372).

## Narrative Accounts

### The Editor's Account (004-371–004-374)

The Editor's narrative presents the trial from the perspective of the two women who brought the case. Bell Calvert delivers her testimony, identifying Robert as the man she had seen acting in concert with a mysterious companion — the figure whom the reader recognizes as Gil-Martin:

> "...was the very man who stabbed George Colwan in the back, and she said she knew it with certainty" (004-323)

Miss Logan corroborates with her own observations. The scene in which the two women watch Robert walking "arm in arm with another young man" who "looked up and made a sly signal" (004-323) provides the novel's most chilling visual confirmation of the alliance between Robert and his demonic friend.

Yet the evidence remains circumstantial. The court cannot accept the supernatural dimension of the story — the identity of Gil-Martin, the nature of Robert's delusion — and so the full truth remains hidden:

> "The Honourable Thomas Drummond became a distinguished officer in the Austrian service, and died in the memorable year for Scotland, 1715; and this is all with which history, justiciary records, and tradition, furnish me relating to these matters." (004-374)

### The Sinner's Account (006-228, 006-261, 006-325)

Robert's own narrative provides the missing interior dimension. He records his defiance in the face of legal scrutiny:

> "Never shall it be said that I shrunk from such a trial as this... I will go and show myself in every public place, that no slanderous tongue may wag against me. I have shed the blood of sinners, but of these deaths I am guiltless; therefore I will face every tribunal, and put all my accusers down." (006-325)

The irony is devastating: Robert truly believes his own words. He has "shed the blood of sinners" — but the blood of George Colwan is, in his mind, not among them, because his brother's death was foreordained by God and executed (or at least sanctioned) by Gil-Martin.

Robert's response to the earlier court proceedings — when George brought charges against him — reveals the same pattern of self-exculpation:

> "When my reverend father beheld me bleeding a second time by the hand of a brother, he was moved to the highest point of displeasure; and, relying on his high interest and the justice of his cause, he brought the matter at once before the courts." (006-228)

After the acquittal, Robert's fear does not subside — but neither does his delusion. He is "greatly disturbed in my mind for many days, knowing that the transaction had been witnessed, and sensible also of the perilous situation I occupied, owing to the late judgment of the court against me" (006-261). The "late judgment" referred to here is not the murder trial but an earlier assault case — the legal net is closing around Robert, but the gap between legal guilt and theological self-justification remains unbridgeable.

## Aftermath

Robert's acquittal brings no peace to anyone. For Miss Logan and Bell Calvert, the verdict is a bitter confirmation of what they already knew: the court cannot deliver the justice that common observation demands. For Robert, the acquittal is the beginning of a downward spiral. Though legally exonerated, he is increasingly hunted — by his own conscience, by Gil-Martin's oppressive presence, and by the community that knows his guilt regardless of the verdict. His flight into the Highlands and eventual suicide are the confession the court could not extract.

## Thematic Significance

The trial is the novel's most penetrating critique of Enlightenment rationality. The court is not corrupt or incompetent — it operates exactly as it should, weighing evidence according to established legal standards. The failure is systemic: the legal framework cannot accommodate the reality of supernatural evil or the psychology of religious delusion. A man who genuinely believes that his murders are acts of divine justice passes every test of rational interrogation because he is not lying in any ordinary sense.

Hogg's point is that there are forms of certainty (theological conviction) and forms of doubt (legal reasonable doubt) that can coexist without contradiction. Robert Wringhim is guilty in fact but innocent in law — and this gap exposes the limits of all human institutions that claim to establish truth through evidence and procedure. The trial thus stands as a companion piece to the novel's theological critique: just as the General Assembly cannot regulate the Antiburgher minister, the law courts cannot judge the Antinomian murderer.
