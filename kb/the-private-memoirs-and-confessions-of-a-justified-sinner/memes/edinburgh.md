# Edinburgh

## Overview

Edinburgh in the early 18th century was a city of stark contrasts: the cramped, towering tenements of the Old Town on the Royal Mile, the elegant new developments, and the wild, volcanic landscape of Arthur's Seat rising on its eastern edge. It was the intellectual and ecclesiastical capital of Scotland — home to the University, the General Assembly of the Church of Scotland, and a thriving print culture. In Hogg's novel, Edinburgh is the urban stage where the drama of faith, murder, and failed justice unfolds.

## Historical Context

### Edinburgh at the Turn of the 18th Century

The Edinburgh of Hogg's novel (set c. 1687–1705) was a city in transition. The Glorious Revolution of 1688–89 had established Presbyterianism as the settled church of Scotland, but the settlement was contested. Episcopalians, Jacobites, Cameronian extremists, and moderate Presbyterians competed for influence in church and state. The political tensions described in the novel — the mob violence between Whig and Tory factions, the presence of the "Cameronian Regiment" called from the Castle to quell street fighting — are historically accurate.

The city itself was undergoing physical transformation. The Old Town, built along the spine of the High Street running from the Castle to Holyrood, was one of the most densely populated urban areas in Europe. Tenements rose to ten or twelve stories, with the wealthy occupying the middle floors and the poor crowded into the attics and cellars. The wynds and closes running off the High Street — narrow, dark, labyrinthine passages — provided the novel's architecture of concealment, the spaces where [[robert-wringhim|Robert]] could stalk his brother unseen.

> "But the High Street of Edinburgh, which they soon reached, is a dangerous place in which to make an open attack upon a mob." (004-086)

This understatement — the High Street as "dangerous" — captures something essential about Edinburgh in the period. It was a city where political passions could ignite into mob violence at any moment, where armed gentlemen brawled in the streets, and where the institutions of civil order were perpetually struggling to maintain control.

### The Mob and the City

The mob is a recurring presence in the novel's Edinburgh scenes. Political factions — Whigs and Tories, Revolutionists and Jacobites — regularly clashed in the streets, and the city's dense architecture made these confrontations particularly dangerous:

> "Great was the alarm and confusion that night in Edinburgh; for everyone concluded that it was a party scuffle, and, the two parties being so equal in power, the most serious consequences were anticipated. The agitation was so prevailing that every party in town, great and small, was broken up; and the lord-commissioner thought proper to go to the Castle, on account of the disturbance." (004-089)

The mob functions in the novel as a secular counterpart to religious fanaticism: a collective force that overwhelms individual reason and institutional authority. Just as [[robert-wringhim|Robert]] is swept along by the logic of Antinomian theology, so the Edinburgh crowd is swept along by political passion — and both forms of collective irrationality prove lethal.

### Edinburgh and the Scottish Enlightenment

By the time Hogg was writing (the 1820s), Edinburgh had become the epicenter of the Scottish Enlightenment — the city of David Hume, Adam Smith, and the *Edinburgh Review*. Hogg's choice to set his novel in the Edinburgh of a century earlier is significant. The city that in Hogg's day prided itself on rational inquiry and polite culture was, in the novel's timeframe, a city of theological violence and institutional failure. The implication is uncomfortable: the Enlightenment did not so much solve the problems of fanaticism as forget them.

## In the Novel

Edinburgh provides the backdrop for the novel's central drama. George Colwan moves in Edinburgh's social circles — the tennis courts, the taverns, the college — while Robert Wringhim lurks in its shadows, stalking his half-brother through the city's wynds and closes. The Edinburgh of the novel is a city where theological controversy is a public spectacle and where a young man can be murdered without anyone being brought to justice.

### George's Edinburgh

For [[george-colwan|George]], Edinburgh represents opportunity, society, and freedom:

> "...the young laird of Dalcastle and Balgrennan. George, being his only son, was sent to the College of Edinburgh..." (004-071)

The College (now the University of Edinburgh) was the great educational institution of the Scottish elite, and George's presence there marks him as a member of the governing class. In Edinburgh, he plays tennis, socializes with young gentlemen, and enjoys the public life of a young man of means. The city is, for him, a place of visibility and pleasure.

### Robert's Edinburgh

For [[robert-wringhim|Robert]], Edinburgh is an entirely different city. He arrives not as a student but as the companion of his "reverend father," drawn by theological business:

> "It so happened that my reverend father was called to Edinburgh about this time, to assist with his counsel in settling the national affairs. At my earnest request I was permitted to accompany him, at which both my associate and I rejoiced, as we were now about to move in a new and extensive field." (006-159)

The "new and extensive field" is, in [[gil-martin|Gil-Martin]]'s terms, a theater of operations — a place to exercise influence, to stalk, and ultimately to kill. Where George sees tennis courts and college halls, Robert sees opportunities for persecution. The same physical city contains two entirely different moral geographies.

### The City's Dark Spaces

The novel repeatedly draws attention to the wynds and closes that were the hidden arteries of Edinburgh's Old Town. It is in these narrow passages — invisible from the High Street, beyond the reach of public observation — that the novel's darkest events occur:

> "It was at the foot of one of the north wynds of Edinburgh." (004-232)

This location, given in witness testimony, is where evidence of the murder is found. The specificity is telling: the north wynds were among the poorest and most crowded parts of the Old Town. The murder, committed in one of these hidden spaces, goes unpunished — a fact that implicates not just the killer but the city itself.

The close becomes a recurring setting for sinister encounters:

> "At the foot of a close in Edinburgh! and at this time of the night!" (004-287)

This exclamation — from a mysterious English lady encountered in the city's dark passages — captures the sense of Edinburgh as a city where respectable people should not be found in certain places after dark. The closes were thresholds between the public world of the High Street and the private world of hidden violence, and the novel uses them as literal passages between visibility and concealment, between civic order and theological crime.

### Edinburgh as Refuge

In the novel's final act, [[robert-wringhim|Robert]] flees toward Edinburgh as a place of refuge, only to find it transformed into a place of judgment. His approach to the city is described in language of desperate flight:

> "Miserable, forlorn, and dreading every person that I saw, either behind or before me, I hasted on towards Edinburgh, taking all the by and unfrequented paths; and, the third night after I left the weaver's house, I reached the West Port, without meeting with anything remarkable." (006-397)

The West Port was one of the main entrances to the Old Town — a gate in the city wall. Robert enters Edinburgh not as the confident predestinarian of his earlier chapters but as "miserable, forlorn," a hunted man. The city that once offered him a "new and extensive field" for his activities has become the scene of his undoing. His death, soon after this passage, at a rural location outside the city, completes the arc: Edinburgh could neither contain nor punish the evil it harbored; the resolution must come from beyond its boundaries.

## Significance

Edinburgh functions in the novel as more than mere setting. As the center of the Scottish Enlightenment, it represents the rational, public world that Robert's Antinomian subjectivity rejects. The city's institutions — church, law, university — are the structures that should prevent the kind of private violence Robert commits, but which in the novel fail to do so. The contrast between Edinburgh's public, social spaces and the dark, narrow closes where violence can occur unseen mirrors the novel's concern with the visible and invisible dimensions of human action.

Edinburgh is also, in Hogg's rendering, a city of failed justice. The law courts acquit Robert. The church provides the theological justification for his crimes. The university educates George but cannot protect him. The city's claim to be a center of rational civilization is undermined by everything that happens within its walls.
