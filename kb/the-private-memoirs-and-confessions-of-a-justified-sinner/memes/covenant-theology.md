# Covenant Theology

## Definition

Covenant (or Federal) theology is the Reformed framework for understanding redemptive history as a series of covenants between God and humanity. The two principal covenants are the Covenant of Works — made with Adam before the Fall, requiring perfect obedience — and the Covenant of Grace — made after the Fall, promising salvation through faith in Christ. Orthodox Reformed theology insists on maintaining the distinction between law (the Covenant of Works) and gospel (the Covenant of Grace), while affirming that both are expressions of God's will. The Antinomian error in Hogg's novel arises from effectively abolishing the Covenant of Works altogether.

## Theological Background

Covenant theology was systematized by Reformed theologians in the 17th century, notably Johannes Cocceius and Herman Witsius, and codified in the Westminster Confession of Faith (1646). The Confession teaches that while believers are freed from the law as a "covenant of works" (it can no longer condemn them), the moral law continues to bind them as a "rule of life" — the so-called "third use of the law." The Antinomian controversy was, at root, a dispute about whether the law retains any role for the justified believer. Hogg's novel enters this controversy on the side of orthodoxy, showing what happens when the law is abolished in the name of grace.

## In the Novel

The novel does not use the technical vocabulary of covenant theology, but the underlying structure of the controversy is present throughout. The Reverend Wringhim's theology is implicitly anti-covenantal: he preaches grace in a way that leaves no room for law. Robert internalizes this as the conviction that "a justified person can do no wrong" (004-042) — a claim that would be true only if the Covenant of Works were entirely abrogated for the elect. The novel's critique is that this position, while superficially honoring grace, actually destroys the moral fabric of the gospel by eliminating the law as a guide to holy living.

The novel's resolution implicitly affirms the covenantal framework: Robert's destruction demonstrates that grace without law is not grace at all but license, and that the law continues to speak — if only in judgment — even to those who claim to be beyond its reach.

## Key Passages

> "How delightful to think that a justified person can do no wrong!" (004-042)

> "...working up a system of justifying-grace against all breaches of all laws..." (004-056)

> "...carrying your ideas of absolute predestination, and its concomitant appendages, to an extent that overthrows all religion and morality..." (006-130)

## Critical Significance

Hogg's novel can be read as a powerful literary argument for covenant theology's insistence on the abiding validity of the moral law. By dramatizing the consequences of Antinomianism, Hogg makes the case — through narrative rather than treatise — that the law-gospel distinction, while theologically necessary, must not become a law-gospel *separation*. The novel thus functions as both a theological critique and a psychological study of what happens when the moral law is silenced in the name of grace.
