## The Confessional Form

*The Private Memoirs and Confessions of a Justified Sinner* announces its genre in its title. The confessional form — a first-person narrative in which the speaker recounts their transgressions — is among the oldest literary genres, originating in Augustine's *Confessions* (c. 397–400) and flourishing in the Puritan spiritual autobiography of the seventeenth century. Hogg's innovation is to write a confession whose narrator does not actually confess: Robert Wringhim narrates his deeds in exhaustive detail but consistently reinterprets them as acts of righteousness rather than sin.

The title's juxtaposition of "Memoirs" and "Confessions" signals this ambiguity. A memoir is a record of events; a confession is an acknowledgment of wrongdoing. Wringhim produces the former while believing he is producing the latter — and the gap between the two is the novel's central irony. He invites the reader to "read over these confessions" (006-140) and predicts that "such a one will judge for himself" (006-140), but his own faculty of judgment has been disabled by the theological certainties he brings to the act of narration.

## The Unconfessing Confessor

Wringhim's memoir is a confession that systematically refuses the work of confession. In the Christian tradition, confession requires three elements: acknowledgment of the act, acknowledgment of its sinful character, and a turning away (repentance). Wringhim acknowledges his acts — the murder of his brother, the deception of his mother, his complicity in Gil-Martin's schemes — but consistently describes them in the language of righteous warfare rather than sinful transgression.

The reader's experience of the memoir is thus one of deepening horror, not because the events described are uniquely terrible (gothic fiction offers more lurid crimes) but because the narrator's moral vocabulary has been so thoroughly corrupted that he cannot name what he has done. When Wringhim declares that the reader "will wonder at this confession, and deem it savage and unnatural" (006-170), he acknowledges the anticipated response without understanding its basis. He knows his narrative will shock; he does not know why.

## The Editor's Counter-Confession

The dual-narrative structure provides an implicit correction to Wringhim's failed confession. The Editor's frame narrative — "The Editor's Narrative" — functions as a secular confession on Wringhim's behalf, reconstructing the events from external evidence and supplying the moral judgments that Wringhim cannot make. The Editor's repeated characterisation of Wringhim in moral terms ("hypocrite," "sensualist," "unnatural fiend") performs the evaluative work of confession that the memoir itself refuses.

The effect is to make the novel itself the confession that Wringhim cannot write. The reader, positioned between the memoir and the editorial frame, is invited to perform the act of moral recognition that Wringhim cannot: to see wickedness named as wickedness, and to understand the theological machinery that has made such naming impossible for the narrator.

## Theological Significance

The failure of confession in the novel is not a psychological accident but a theological consequence. Within the Antinomian framework, confession is theologically incoherent: if the elect cannot fall from grace, and if their actions do not affect their standing before God, then there is nothing to confess. Wringhim's memoir is thus the genre's limit case — a confession produced by a theology that has abolished the category of sin for the elect, leaving only the formal gesture of narration without its penitential substance.

Hogg's achievement is to make this theological failure the engine of a compelling narrative. The confessional form, designed to produce moral clarity, becomes instead a document of moral confusion so profound that it requires the Editor's frame to become legible.
