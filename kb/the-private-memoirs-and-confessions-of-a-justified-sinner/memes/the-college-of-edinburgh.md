# The College of Edinburgh

## Description

The College of Edinburgh (later the University of Edinburgh) was, by the early 18th century, one of the leading centers of learning in Europe. It was the institutional heart of the Scottish Enlightenment -- home to philosophers, scientists, and moderate clergymen who embodied the rational, empirical approach to knowledge that defines the Editor's Narrative.

## In the Novel

The College appears in the novel as the institution that has formed George Colwan's mind and character. George's education there represents everything that Robert's Antinomian upbringing has denied him: critical thinking, intellectual breadth, and a moderate, reasonable approach to religion. The contrast between the College-educated George and the sect-educated Robert is one of the novel's central structural oppositions.

George's college education situates him in the urban, fashionable world of Enlightenment Edinburgh. When George first encounters Robert, he "took him for some impertinent student of divinity, rather set upon a joke than anything else" (004-066) -- the phrase "student of divinity" marking the clerical and educational world Robert inhabits, albeit in its most narrow and bigoted form.

> "George took him for some impertinent student of divinity, rather set upon a joke than anything else. He perceived a lad with black clothes, and a methodistical face, whose countenance and eye he disliked exceedingly." (004-066)

The College is situated within the lively, sometimes dangerous urban world of Edinburgh. George moves through the High Street, the center of the city's social life, where street brawls between Whig and Tory factions erupt -- the same city that houses the intellectual life of the College. The novel's Edinburgh is thus both a seat of learning and a scene of violent factionalism.

> "But the High Street of Edinburgh, which they soon reached, is a dangerous place in which to make an open attack upon a mob." (004-086)

The novel also plays on the cultural associations of education and theological learning in its mock-pastoral encounter when the fleeing Robert, in a later section, claims: "I said I was a poor student of theology, on my way to Oxford" (006-426) -- a self-description that ironically deflates his pretensions while recalling the theological education he never properly received.

> "I said I was a poor student of theology, on my way to Oxford. They stared at one another with expressions of wonder, disappointment, and fear." (006-426)

## Significance

The College represents Enlightenment values -- reason, evidence, moderation -- that the novel both deploys and questions. While the Editor's rationalist perspective is clearly preferable to Robert's fanaticism, the novel also suggests that pure rationality cannot fully account for the depths of human evil and supernatural possibility that the story discloses.
