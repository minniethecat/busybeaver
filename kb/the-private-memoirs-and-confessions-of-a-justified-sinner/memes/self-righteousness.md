# Self-Righteousness

**Self-righteousness**, in the theological vocabulary of Reformed Christianity, denotes the belief that one is righteous before God by virtue of one's own merits or works, rather than through the imputed righteousness of Christ received by faith. In *The Private Memoirs and Confessions of a Justified Sinner*, James Hogg explores a deeper and more unsettling form of self-righteousness: not the Pelagian error of trusting in one's own good works, but the Antinomian error of being so certain of one's election that one becomes incapable of recognising one's own evil.

## The Paradox of Calvinist Self-Righteousness

### From Humility to Arrogance

Orthodox Calvinist theology is, in principle, radically hostile to self-righteousness. The doctrine of total depravity insists that human beings are utterly incapable of contributing to their own salvation. The doctrine of justification by faith alone insists that righteousness is a gift received, not a quality achieved. As Robert himself quotes:

> "...holding all the righteousness of man as filthy rags..." (006-071)

Yet the novel demonstrates how this very system can produce the self-righteousness it is designed to prevent. The logic is perverse but psychologically intelligible:

1. God has elected a fixed number of persons to salvation.
2. I have experienced what I believe to be the signs of election.
3. Therefore, I am among the elect.
4. The elect are righteous before God, regardless of their actions.
5. Therefore, I am righteous — and those who lack these signs are not.

The move from step 2 to step 3 is an act of self-certification that the Calvinist system, as Hogg portrays it, has no institutional mechanism to challenge. The subjective experience of election becomes, in practice, the ground of a profound spiritual arrogance.

### The Editorial Frame's Warning

The novel's opening editorial frame explicitly names self-righteousness as its central concern. The editor, reflecting on the manuscript he has discovered, writes:

> "...showing the dreadful danger of self-righteousness..." (007-001)

This is the novel's thesis statement, placed at the threshold of Robert's confession. The editor — whose rational, empirical worldview is itself a form of self-righteousness, as the novel subtly suggests — locates the moral of the tale in the "dreadful danger" of believing oneself to be uniquely righteous.

## Self-Righteousness in Robert Wringhim

### The Mockery of the Crowd

The novel's most explicit depiction of Robert's self-righteousness comes not from his own confession but from the community that observes him. After his downfall, the crowd mocks him:

> "...exclaiming: 'There goes the self-righteous one! There goes the consecrated one!'" (006-270)

The pairing of "self-righteous" and "consecrated" is precise. Robert's public identity — his visible piety, his moral severity, his doctrinal exactness — is what the community recognises as self-righteousness. His "consecration" is the public performance of a private delusion.

### Righteousness as a "Cause"

Throughout his confession, Robert repeatedly describes his actions as serving "the cause of righteousness" (006-042, 006-169, 006-204). This phrase functions as an ironic refrain: every time Robert invokes "righteousness," he is describing or justifying a sin. The "cause of righteousness" becomes, in his usage, indistinguishable from the cause of Robert Wringhim.

This linguistic habit is Hogg's most acute psychological observation. The self-righteous person does not *know* they are self-righteous; they genuinely believe they are serving righteousness itself. The pathology lies precisely in the inability to distinguish between one's own interests and the interests of God.

### The Irony of "Filthy Rags"

The theological irony of Robert's self-righteousness reaches its peak when he deploys the orthodox language of human unworthiness *in order to assert his own superiority*:

> "...holding all the righteousness of man as filthy rags..." (006-071)

Robert means this sincerely — he genuinely believes that human righteousness is worthless. But he exempts himself from the category "man." His righteousness is not human righteousness; it is the righteousness of the elect, which is God's own righteousness imputed to him. The orthodox doctrine becomes, through a slight but catastrophic shift, a warrant for treating one's own actions as beyond moral scrutiny.

## Self-Righteousness and the Double

### Externalisation of Conscience

The novel's supernatural apparatus — the mysterious stranger, Gil-Martin, the double — can be read as an externalisation of Robert's self-righteousness. The double is, among other things, the part of Robert that *knows* he is sinning but has been silenced by the self-righteous conviction that the elect cannot sin.

Robert's tormented late confession — his alternation between assertions of righteousness and moments of terrified self-awareness — maps the internal war that self-righteousness produces. The self-righteous person is not at peace; they are engaged in a constant, exhausting labour of self-persuasion.

## Relation to Other Themes

### Antinomianism

Self-righteousness and [[antinomianism]] form a paradoxical pair. Antinomianism — the belief that the moral law does not bind the elect — would seem to be the opposite of self-righteousness, which is an excessive concern with one's own moral status. But the novel shows how they converge: the Antinomian's certainty of election is itself a form of self-righteousness, because it substitutes subjective religious experience for the objective evidence of moral conduct.

### Hypocrisy

The crowd's mockery — "There goes the self-righteous one!" — also implies [[hypocrisy]], another key theme. The self-righteous person is necessarily a hypocrite, because the public performance of righteousness cannot be sustained without concealing the gap between profession and conduct.

### Religious Enthusiasm

Self-righteousness is the cognitive content of [[religious-enthusiasm]], which supplies the affective heat. Enthusiasm makes the self-righteous conviction *feel* like divine revelation, elevating what is actually a psychological state to the status of theological certainty.

## Hogg's Diagnostic Achievement

Hogg's insight — one that places him among the most acute psychological novelists of his era — is that the doctrine designed to *eliminate* self-righteousness (justification by faith alone) can, under certain conditions, *produce* it. The mechanism is the subjective experience of election: once a person is convinced that they belong to the elect, the entire system of Calvinist theology becomes a machine for generating self-righteousness rather than humility.

This is not, for Hogg, an argument against Calvinism as such. It is a warning about the psychological conditions under which any system of belief can be turned to the service of self-deception. The "dreadful danger of self-righteousness" (007-001) awaits anyone who confuses their own certainty with God's.
