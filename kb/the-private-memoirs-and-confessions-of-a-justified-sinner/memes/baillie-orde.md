# Baillie Orde

**Baillie Orde** is a minor character in *The Private Memoirs and Confessions of a Justified Sinner*, the father of Arabella Colwan (later Lady Dalcastle). A Glasgow magistrate — a *baillie* being a municipal officer in Scottish burghs — he appears in the early chapters of the Editor's Narrative and provides one of the novel's rare instances of comic relief, albeit of a darkly ironic sort.

## Role in the Narrative

Baillie Orde is introduced in connection with his daughter's marriage to George Colwan, the Laird of Dalcastle:

> "This George was a rich man, or supposed to be so, and was married, when considerably advanced in life, to the sole heiress and reputed daughter of a Baillie Orde, of Glasgow." (004-002)

His most significant appearance occurs after Arabella flees her husband's house and returns to her father's home in Glasgow. Rather than offering comfort or mediating the dispute, the Baillie responds with a blend of paternal indignation and comic brutality:

> "Aye, aye, Raby! An' sae I find that Dalcastle has actually refused to say prayers with you when you ordered him; an' has guidit you in a rude indelicate manner, outstepping the respect due to my daughter — as my daughter. But, wi' regard to what is due to his own wife, of that he's a better judge nor me. However, since he has behaved in that manner to MY DAUGHTER, I shall be revenged on him for aince; for I shall return the obligation to ane nearer to him: that is, I shall take pennyworths of his wife — an' let him lick at that." (004-025)

The logic is pure farce: because Dalcastle has mistreated Orde's daughter, Orde will punish Dalcastle's wife — which is, of course, his own daughter. He proceeds to administer a mock beating, "his strokes... not indeed very deadly, but he made a mighty flourish in the infliction" (004-028), and then locks Arabella in an upper room on bread and water.

## Character and Significance

Baillie Orde embodies a worldly, pragmatic ethos. He is offended not by Dalcastle's theological errors but by the "rude indelicate manner" of his behaviour. His punishment of Arabella is absurd — punishing the victim on behalf of the perpetrator — but it is also recognisable as the kind of face-saving ritual that secular authority performs when it cannot actually address the underlying problem.

The Baillie's response to Arabella's marital crisis stands in pointed contrast to the response of the Reverend Wringhim, who treats the same events as cosmic spiritual warfare. Where Wringhim sees election and reprobation, Orde sees bad manners and injured pride. This contrast between worldly and religious frames of reference is one of the novel's structuring tensions.

The Baillie also represents the civic order of Glasgow — mercantile, pragmatic, and ultimately ineffectual in the face of the theological passions that drive the novel's central tragedy. His authority as a magistrate cannot reach the spiritual domain where the real conflict unfolds.
