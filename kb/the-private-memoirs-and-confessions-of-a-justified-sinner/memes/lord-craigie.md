# Lord Craigie

## Biography

Lord Craigie — formally Sir Thomas Wallace of Craigie — was the Lord Justice Clerk of [[scotland|Scotland]] during the period of the novel's trial scenes. A figure of legal authority within the Scottish judicial system, he is introduced in connection with his nephew Thomas [[drummond-of-logie|Drummond]], on whose behalf "Lord Craigie, his uncle, had taken care" to secure legal representation (004-190).

When the case of [[george-colwan|George Colwan]]'s murder reached the court, it was to "Sir Thomas Wallace of Craigie, the Lord Justice Clerk" that the advocates presented their arguments (004-371). Lord Craigie directed the proceedings, overseeing the trial in which [[robert-wringhim|Robert Wringhim]] was ultimately acquitted despite substantial evidence against him.

## Role in the Narrative

Lord Craigie represents the institutional legal system of early 18th-century [[scotland|Scotland]] — a system that, in the novel's account, fails to deliver justice. His connection to the [[drummond-of-logie|Drummond]] family through his nephew Thomas highlights the web of patronage and familial ties that shaped legal proceedings in the period. He collected evidence on his nephew's behalf, producing Drummond's sword "on which there was neither blood nor blemish" and surgeon testimony that both wounds "had been given behind" (004-190).

When [[mrs-logan|Mrs. Logan]] and Bell [[arabella-calvert|Calvert]] brought their eyewitness evidence before him, "he examined Calvert very minutely, and seemed deeply interested in her evidence — said he knew she was relating the truth, and, in testimony of it, brought a letter of young Drummond's from his desk" (004-371). He then "directed the two women to the proper authorities" and ordered the apprehension of [[robert-wringhim|Robert Wringhim]] (004-372).

His role is largely procedural: he directs the advocates, receives evidence, and presides over a trial that ends, ambiguously, without conviction. The failure of the legal system under his oversight — whether due to corruption, insufficient evidence, or the sheer improbability of the supernatural elements — is part of the novel's broader theme of justice eluded.

## Key Passages

Craigie's collection of evidence for his nephew:

> On the other hand, there were some evidences on Drummond's part, which Lord Craigie, his uncle, had taken care to collect. He produced the sword which his nephew had worn that night, on which there was neither blood nor blemish; and, above all, he insisted on the evidence of a number of surgeons, who declared that both the wounds which the deceased had received had been given behind. (004-190)

Receiving the women's evidence:

> Accordingly the two women went to Sir Thomas Wallace of Craigie, the Lord Justice Clerk (who was, I think, either uncle or grandfather to young Drummond, who was outlawed and obliged to fly his country on account of Colwan's death), and to that gentleman they related every circumstance of what they had seen and heard. (004-371)

Directing the proceedings:

> Lord Craigie, therefore, directed the two women to the proper authorities, and, after hearing their evidence there, it was judged proper to apprehend the present Laird of Dalcastle, and bring him to his trial. (004-372)

## Relationships

- **Thomas [[drummond-of-logie|Drummond]]**: Lord Craigie's nephew, on whose behalf Craigie arranged legal representation.
- **[[robert-wringhim|Robert Wringhim]]**: The defendant in the trial over which Craigie presided; [[robert-wringhim|Wringhim]] was acquitted despite evidence.
- **[[george-colwan|George Colwan]]**: The murder victim whose case came before Craigie's court.
- **[[mrs-logan|Mrs. Logan]]**: The witness who brought evidence before him.
- **Bell [[arabella-calvert|Calvert]]**: The witness whose testimony Craigie found "deeply interested" in.

## Key Appearances

- **Legal Representation (004-190)**: Craigie is mentioned as having arranged counsel for his nephew Thomas [[drummond-of-logie|Drummond]], demonstrating his influence within the legal system.
- **The Trial (004-371–372)**: As Lord Justice Clerk, Craigie "directed the two" advocates and presided over the unreported trial that failed to convict [[robert-wringhim|Robert Wringhim]].

## Critical Interpretation

Lord Craigie embodies the institutional framework within which the novel's events are judged — or fail to be. His presence grounds the supernatural and psychological drama in the real-world structures of early 18th-century Scottish law. Critics have noted that the novel's legal dimension — the trial that fails — mirrors its theological dimension: just as the legal system cannot convict [[robert-wringhim|Robert]], [[robert-wringhim|Robert]]'s own conscience, shaped by [[antinomianism|Antinomianism]], cannot convict him either.
