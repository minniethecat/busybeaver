## Definition and Theological Context

Hypocrisy in *The Private Memoirs and Confessions of a Justified Sinner* is not merely the gap between public profession and private conduct. It is a specifically theological category rooted in the Calvinist doctrine of election: the hypocrite is one who claims the benefits of justification (being declared righteous by faith alone) while repudiating the obligations of sanctification (the progressive transformation of character). Robert Wringhim embodies this contradiction: he is "justified, sanctified, and received into a communion" (006-163) yet commits murder with the conviction that his elect status renders him immune to moral accountability.

The novel's critique operates on two levels. At the narrative level, Wringhim's self-deception is progressively exposed through the Editor's frame and the memoir's confessional structure. At the theological level, Hogg targets the Antinomian perversion of predestination — the belief that the elect cannot fall from grace, and therefore their outward actions are irrelevant to their spiritual standing. The editor's narrative explicitly distinguishes true religion from "the cant of the bigot or the hypocrite, no reasoning can aught avail" (004-012).

## Narrative Manifestations

### Robert Wringhim as the Hypocrite

Wringhim's hypocrisy is inseparable from his theological certainties. Adopted by the Reverend Wringhim and raised under a rigorous Calvinism that emphasises election to the exclusion of moral effort, he internalises the conviction of his own righteousness so completely that every contrary action — theft, deception, murder — is reinterpreted as divinely sanctioned. His memoir reveals a mind for whom the categories of sin and virtue have been inverted: "wicked men, froward boys, and deceitful women; and I rejoiced, even in my solitude, that I was not as they" (006-061).

The Pharisee allusion crystallises this self-assessment. When a weaver tells Wringhim, "Ye are the just Pharisee, sir, that gaed up wi' the poor publican" (006-043), the biblical echo of Luke 18:9–14 is deliberate: the Pharisee who thanks God he is not like other men is the archetype of religious hypocrisy, and Wringhim is cast in precisely this role. The irony is that Wringhim cannot recognise the allusion as condemnation; his theological framework has disabled the very faculty of self-examination that might expose his error.

### The Editor's Diagnostic Gaze

The Editor's narrative provides the external counterpoint that Wringhim's memoir cannot supply. The Editor describes Wringhim as "an accomplished vagabond, hypocrite, and sensualist" (006-266), a summary judgment that collapses the pretensions of the memoir into a worldly diagnosis. The Editor's repeated insistence on Wringhim's "deceit" (006-330) and his characterisation of the memoir as a "labyrinth of deceit" (006-062) frame the entire confessional document as an artifact of self-deception rather than genuine penitence.

## Literary and Theological Significance

Hypocrisy in the novel is not a static character trait but a narrative engine. The gap between Wringhim's self-perception and the Editor's external assessment generates the ironic tension that drives the reader's engagement. This dual-perspective structure — the fanatic's inner certainty versus the reasonable man's outer observation — makes *Confessions* an early study in unreliable narration, anticipating Dostoevsky's *Notes from Underground* and the modern psychological novel.

Theologically, Hogg's treatment of hypocrisy participates in a broader Romantic-era critique of Calvinist orthodoxy. By making Wringhim's hypocrisy the direct product of his theological training — "a disposition tainted with deceit" (006-012) — Hogg argues that the doctrine of unconditional election, when severed from the complementary doctrine of sanctification, becomes a machine for producing hypocrites. The novel thus functions as a moral argument disguised as a gothic tale: the Antinomian distortion of grace is not merely intellectually erroneous but spiritually catastrophic, capable of transforming a religiously scrupulous young man into a murderer who thanks God for the opportunity to kill.
