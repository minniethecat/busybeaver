# Bessy Gillies

## Biography

Bessy Gillies is an elderly servant in the [[george-colwan|Colwan]] household who possesses a keen folk wisdom that allows her to see through [[gil-martin|Gil-Martin]]'s deceptions. When [[gil-martin|Gil-Martin]] attempts to shapeshift and deceive her, she recognizes him as a "warlock" and refuses to be taken in. Her simple piety and traditional Scottish folk beliefs provide a striking contrast to the sophisticated theological self-deception of [[robert-wringhim|Robert Wringhim]].

She also appears as a witness at the trial of Bell Calvert for the robbery of [[mrs-logan|Mrs. Logan]]'s house, where her "flippant and fearless" testimony (004-242) in broad Scots dialect provides some of the novel's most vivid comic relief. Her shrewd observation — that "it was nae woman's fingers that broke up the bolts an' the locks" (004-259) — cuts through the prosecution's assumptions and helps exonerate the accused.

## Role in the Narrative

Bessy represents an alternative religious epistemology: the unlettered believer whose instincts are sounder than the theological system-builder's. Her brief appearance is one of the novel's most pointed critiques of [[antinomianism|Antinomian]] sophistication — simple faith recognizes evil when learned theology does not.

## Relationships

- **[[gil-martin|Gil-Martin]]**: She recognizes his true nature.
- **[[robert-wringhim|Robert Wringhim]]**: She distrusts him instinctively.
- **The [[george-colwan|Colwan]] family**: She serves them faithfully.
- **[[mrs-logan|Mrs. Logan]]**: Her mistress, whom she accompanies to court (004-241).

## Key Passages

Bessy's confrontation with [[gil-martin|Gil-Martin]] is one of the few moments in the novel when the deceiver is seen for what he is. Her court testimony reveals both her folk wit and her innate sense of justice:

> "Because it was nae woman's fingers that broke up the bolts an' the locks that were torn open that night." (004-259)

> "Sir, the thieves didna stand upon particulars: they were halesale dealers in a' our best wares." (004-249)

> "Was I at hame, say ye? Na, faith-ye, lad! An' I had been at hame, there had been mair to dee. I wad hae raised sic a yelloch!" (004-243)
