## Theological Definition

Total depravity is the first point of the TULIP acronym that summarises the Canons of Dort (1618–1619), the defining confessional statement of Reformed orthodoxy against Arminianism. The doctrine affirms that as a consequence of the Fall, every aspect of human nature — intellect, will, and affections — is corrupted by sin. "Total" does not mean that humans are as evil as they could possibly be (utter depravity), but that sin has penetrated the entirety of human nature, leaving no faculty untainted and no capacity for self-redemption intact. In the language of the Westminster Confession, humanity is "wholly defiled in all the faculties and parts of soul and body."

The direct practical consequence is that salvation must be entirely God's work. If human nature is totally depraved, no unaided act of the human will — no decision, no moral effort, no religious observance — can contribute to salvation. This is the negative premise from which the positive doctrines of unconditional election and irresistible grace follow: because humanity cannot choose God, God must choose humanity.

## Depravity in the Novel's World

Hogg embeds the doctrine of total depravity in the novel's descriptive textures. The Editor's narrative repeatedly characterises the world in terms of corruption: "haunts of vice and depravity, the nurses of all that is precipitately bad" (004-191). Wringhim's memoir adopts the same language, describing his youth as "those days of depravity and corruption" (006-012) and characterising human nature as "his own wicked and corrupt heart" (006-226). Through the theological vocabulary shared across both narratives, the novel establishes total depravity as the anthropological constant against which the drama of election unfolds.

The doctrine appears most explicitly in the catechism scene between young Robert and the Reverend Wringhim, where the distinction between effectual and ineffectual calling is debated. The Reverend's question — "What is Ineffectual Calling?" (006-003) — and Robert's confident reply that "Ineffectual Calling is the outward call of the gospel without any consideration" (006-005) introduces the theological vocabulary that will structure Robert's entire self-understanding. The premise of total depravity underlies this exchange: the "outward call" is ineffectual precisely because depraved human nature cannot respond to it; only the inward, effectual call of the Spirit can overcome the will's corruption.

## Narrative Consequences

### Moral Paralysis

The doctrine of total depravity produces a specific form of moral paralysis in the novel. If human nature is irredeemably corrupt, then moral effort is either futile (for the reprobate) or unnecessary (for the elect). Wringhim's repeated failures to resist Gil-Martin illustrate this paralysis. He recognises his own "corrupt nature" (006-175) being roused to violence yet lacks the theological resources to resist it, because his version of Calvinism has severed the connection between human effort and moral outcomes.

### The Corruption of the Elect

The novel's most radical theological move is to suggest that the elect are not exempt from total depravity — they are, if anything, more vulnerable to its effects precisely because their theological certainty blinds them to their own corruption. Wringhim is described as "a deplorable instance of human depravity and self-conceit" (006-400), a formulation that links depravity with the pride of election. The point is not that Wringhim is unusually wicked by nature, but that his doctrine of election has become an instrument of his depravity rather than a remedy for it.

### The Body as Site of Corruption

The novel also deploys the language of depravity in relation to the physical body. Wringhim describes his mortal frame as "this frame of dross and corruption" (006-438), echoing the Calvinist identification of the body as the seat of sinful impulses. The body's corruption becomes the visible sign of depravity, and Wringhim's physical deterioration through the novel's final chapters mirrors his spiritual collapse.

## Critical Function

Hogg's treatment of total depravity is neither simple affirmation nor rejection. The novel accepts the descriptive claim that human nature is radically flawed — the narrative provides abundant evidence — while questioning whether the Calvinist doctrinal apparatus exacerbates rather than heals that condition. By making the doctrine of total depravity the premise of a theology that produces murderers, Hogg suggests that the error lies not in the diagnosis but in the prescribed cure: the doctrine of unconditional election, when severed from any requirement of moral transformation, turns the recognition of human sinfulness into a licence for sin.
