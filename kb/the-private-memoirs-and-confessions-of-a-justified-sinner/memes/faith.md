# Faith

## Definition

In Reformed theology, faith is the instrument — and the sole instrument — by which the believer receives the righteousness of Christ and is justified before God. Faith is itself a gift of God (Ephesians 2:8–9), not a human work or merit. The Reformation slogan *sola fide* ("by faith alone") encapsulated the Protestant conviction that justification is received by faith, not achieved by works. In Hogg's novel, "faith" is constantly on the lips of the characters — but almost invariably as a term emptied of its ethical content. The weaver's accusation — "your justification by faith an' awthegither" (006-045) — captures the popular perception that Antinomian "faith" is a doctrine that makes religious profession a cover for moral licence.

## Theological Background

The Westminster Confession defines saving faith as "a grace whereby the elect are enabled to believe to the saving of their souls." Faith has three components in Reformed orthodoxy: *notitia* (knowledge of the gospel), *assensus* (intellectual assent to its truth), and *fiducia* (personal trust and reliance on Christ). The Antinomian error that Hogg critiques collapses this threefold structure into mere intellectual assent — "I believe the doctrine of election, therefore I am elect" — while discarding the fiducia that should produce love and good works. The Scottish Presbyterian tradition, shaped by the Covenanting struggles of the 17th century, placed particular emphasis on the experiential dimension of faith — the "exercise" of faith in the soul — and it is this experiential intensity that the novel both captures and critiques.

The historical context of the novel's treatment of faith is the Antinomian Controversy that troubled English and Scottish Protestantism in the 17th century. Antinomian preachers, drawing on a distorted reading of Paul's epistles, taught that the moral law has no binding force on believers, who are "under grace, not law." In Hogg's Scotland, memories of the Antinomian excesses of the Cromwellian period were still vivid, and the novel's satirical portrait of the Reverend Wringhim draws on this history. The key Antinomian move — severing faith from the moral transformation it should produce — is precisely what the novel dramatizes and condemns.

## In the Novel

The novel repeatedly uses "faith" in contexts that expose its abuse. Robert's mother declares that "your justification by faith" is all that he possesses — a statement that, in the novel's context, is devastatingly ironic, since Robert's "faith" has produced no moral fruit whatsoever (006-045). The Reverend Wringhim's preaching emphasizes that "every effort after righteousness only aggravates our offences" (006-011), a doctrine that, taken to its logical extreme, makes moral effort itself a mark of unbelief. The Laird of Dalcastle eventually "mocked at her wire-drawn degrees of faith, hope, and repentance" (004-032), a response that the novel treats as entirely understandable given the abuses it has documented.

Hogg's most concentrated treatment of faith appears in the sermon excerpts and theological debates that punctuate the narrative. Robert reports preaching on "the faith of the promises, and justification by grace, that they may read and tremble" (006-001), a formulation that uses the language of orthodoxy while evacuating it of ethical content. The key structural feature of the novel's treatment of faith is that it is always *reported* — we hear about Robert's faith from Robert himself, and its nature must be inferred from the gap between his professions and his actions. This gap is the novel's primary hermeneutic device: faith is measured not by what the believer says about it but by what it produces in conduct.

A crucial scene occurs when the Reverend Wringhim catechizes Robert, teaching him that "In my own strength I can do nothing, but in the strength of my Lord and Redeemer I can do all things" (006-117). The orthodox form of this statement masks its Antinomian application: since Robert is confident he acts "in the strength of the Lord," any action he takes — including murder — is by definition righteous. Faith, in this perverted schema, is not trust in Christ that produces good works but a metaphysical guarantee that whatever the believer does is good. The novel traces how this conviction licenses Robert's escalating crimes.

## Key Concepts

| Concept | Orthodox Meaning | Antinomian Distortion |
|---------|-----------------|----------------------|
| *Sola Fide* | Faith alone justifies, but true faith produces works | Faith abolishes the need for works |
| Assurance | Confidence in salvation grounded in Christ | Presumption that election guarantees salvation regardless of conduct |
| Imputed Righteousness | Christ's righteousness credited to the believer | "Filthy rags" — all human righteousness is worthless (006-071) |

## Critical Significance

Hogg's treatment of faith exemplifies the novel's broader method: taking orthodox theological language and showing how it can be psychologically weaponized. The novel does not argue against faith — it dramatizes how faith, when divorced from the moral transformation it should produce, becomes indistinguishable from presumption. As Robert himself declares, "I, like him, am a preacher of righteousness" (006-042) — yet his "righteousness" is a thin veneer over murder.

The novel's satirical treatment of faith also reflects Hogg's engagement with the Scottish Calvinist inheritance. Born into a devout Presbyterian culture, Hogg knew the language of faith intimately — and his critique comes from within that tradition, not from outside it. The novel does not reject the doctrine of justification by faith; it rejects the Antinomian abuse of that doctrine. By making Robert a genuinely convinced believer rather than a cynical hypocrite, Hogg makes his critique more powerful: the problem is not that people pretend to have faith but that real faith, when perverted by bad theology, can produce real evil. The novel's warning applies far beyond the specific Antinomian controversy to any religious system that privileges doctrinal correctness over ethical practice.
