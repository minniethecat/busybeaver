# The Editor

## Biography

The Editor is the anonymous figure who frames the novel. The full title of the work announces his role from the outset:

> *WITH A DETAIL OF CURIOUS TRADITIONARY FACTS, AND OTHER EVIDENCE, BY THE EDITOR* (001-002)

He opens the novel by presenting the "Editor's Narrative" — a third-person account of the events leading up to [[george-colwan|George Colwan]]'s murder, compiled from letters, court records, and eyewitness testimony. He begins with the methodological modesty of the antiquarian:

> It appears from tradition, as well as some parish registers still extant, that the lands of Dalcastle (or Dalchastel, as it is often spelled) were possessed by a family of the name of Colwan, about one hundred and fifty years ago, and for at least a century previous to that period. (004-001)

He freely admits the limits of his knowledge, maintaining the persona of a compiler rather than an author:

> She was likewise summoned, or requested, I know not which, being ignorant of these matters... (004-197)

At the conclusion of his narrative, he defers entirely to his sources, acknowledging the incompleteness of his account:

> The Honourable Thomas Drummond became a distinguished officer in the Austrian service, and died in the memorable year for Scotland, 1715; and this is all with which history, justiciary records, and tradition, furnish me relating to these matters. (004-374)

He then introduces Robert's first-person "Confessions" — the document found among Robert's effects after his death — with a remarkable act of editorial restraint:

> I have now the pleasure of presenting my readers with an original document of a most singular nature, and preserved for their perusal in a still more singular manner. I offer no remarks on it, and make as few additions to it, leaving everyone to judge for himself. (004-375)

The Editor never reveals his identity, but his voice — rational, skeptical, methodical — embodies the Enlightenment values that the novel both deploys and questions. He represents the world of evidence and reason that [[robert-wringhim|Robert]]'s [[antinomianism|Antinomian]] theology has rejected.

After presenting the Confessions, the Editor returns in the final section of the novel to recount his own investigation into the physical remains of the suicide — a section that brings his rationalist methods into direct contact with the inexplicable. He describes his initial skepticism about the discovery of the preserved corpse:

> so often had I been hoaxed by the ingenious fancies displayed in that Magazine, that when this relation met my eye I did not believe it; but, from the moment that I perused it, I half formed the resolution of investigating these wonderful remains personally, if any such existed (007-013)

His personal investigation culminates in the acquisition of the manuscript itself:

> With very little trouble, save that of a thorough drying, I unrolled it all with ease, and found the very tract which I have here ventured to lay before the public, part of it in small bad print, and the remainder in manuscript. (007-026)

## Role in the Narrative

The Editor's structural function is crucial to the novel's meaning. By juxtaposing his rationalist, evidentiary account with [[robert-wringhim|Robert]]'s delusional "Confessions," the novel creates an epistemological gap that is never fully closed. The Editor's very confidence in his own methods becomes, in Hogg's hands, another form of partial understanding — no single perspective, rationalist or religious, can fully account for the events.

The Editor operates as a framing narrator in the tradition of the eighteenth-century novel — collecting documents, weighing evidence, presenting testimony — but Hogg subverts this convention. The documents the Editor presents do not resolve into a coherent picture; they contradict each other. The Editor's narrative offers a plausible, naturalistic account of events driven by jealousy, inheritance, and family hatred. Robert's Confessions offer a supernatural account in which a demonic double orchestrates everything. Neither account fully explains the other, and the Editor's refusal to adjudicate between them — "I offer no remarks on it... leaving everyone to judge for himself" (004-375) — makes the reader the final arbiter of an irresolvable mystery.

The Editor also embodies a specifically Scottish Enlightenment sensibility. His methods — reliance on parish registers, justiciary records, tradition, eyewitness testimony — are those of the legal and historical rationalism that characterized the Scottish Enlightenment. But the novel subjects these methods to radical pressure: what do you do when the evidence includes a demonic double, a supernaturally preserved corpse, and a confession that attributes every crime to theological certainty?

## Key Appearances

- **The Editor's Narrative (003-002--004-375)**: The entire first half of the novel, in which the Editor compiles the story of the Colwan family from documentary sources. His voice is that of the reasonable antiquarian, careful to distinguish what is known from what is conjectured.

- **The transition to the Confessions (004-375)**: The Editor's decision to present the manuscript "without remarks" — a crucial narratological moment that establishes the novel's double-voiced structure.

- **The investigation of the suicide's grave (007-013--007-026)**: The Editor, initially skeptical, travels to the Scottish Borders to verify the account of the preserved corpse. He personally exhumes the remains and recovers the manuscript of the Confessions — a scene in which empirical investigation yields supernatural results.

- **The framing of the entire work (001-001--001-002, 007-001)**: The Editor's presentation of the novel as a found document, complete with the apparatus of authenticating letters and eyewitness accounts, is itself a fiction that implicates the reader in the act of judgment.

## Critical Interpretation

The Editor has been the subject of extensive critical debate. Some readers treat him as a reliable guide — the voice of reason in a novel of fanaticism. Others see him as a figure of Enlightenment limitation — his rationalism is genuine but inadequate to the phenomena he confronts. The Editor cannot process the supernatural, and his refusal to comment on the Confessions may be less an act of editorial neutrality than a failure of nerve.

The Editor's most significant critical function is to create the novel's double perspective. By presenting two incompatible accounts — one naturalistic, one supernatural — without resolving them, Hogg creates what critics have called the novel's "epistemological vertigo." The reader cannot choose between the Editor's and Robert's versions because the novel provides no stable ground from which to judge. The Editor is thus not merely a framing device but the mechanism by which the novel stages its central question: how do we know what we know, and who has the authority to tell us?

The Editor's role has also been read as Hogg's commentary on the genre of the "found manuscript" novel — a tradition that includes works by Scott and Godwin. Where those novelists use the found-manuscript device to authenticate their fictions, Hogg uses it to destabilize the very notion of authentication. The Editor's evidence does not prove what he thinks it proves; the Confessions do not confess what they claim to confess. The framing apparatus, meant to secure meaning, instead secures ambiguity.
