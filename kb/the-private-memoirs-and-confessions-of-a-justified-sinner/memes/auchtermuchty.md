## Description

Auchtermuchty is a real market town in Fife, Scotland, situated in the Howe of Fife between the Ochil Hills and the Lomond Hills. In the novel, it appears exclusively within an embedded folk-tale told by the old wife Lucky Shaw to Samuel Scrape — a richly detailed and darkly comic parable that runs for several pages of dialect prose.

The town is depicted as a hotbed of religious fervour, where even the humblest cottar became "a shining light in ither towns an' parishes" (006-344). The inhabitants spend every waking moment in preaching, praying, and catechising, to the point that "the deils in the farrest nooks o' Hell were alarmed, and moved to commotion."

The physical landmarks mentioned include the parish kirk (church), the West Lowmond hill overlooking the town, and "the brae north of the town" where a vast tent-meeting is erected to accommodate the thousands who flock to hear the mysterious preacher.

## In the Narrative

Auchtermuchty appears not as a setting for the novel's main action but as the subject of an interpolated cautionary tale — a story-within-the-story that functions as a theological and psychological allegory for the novel's central concerns.

Lucky Shaw tells the tale to illustrate the danger of spiritual pride and the ease with which even the most zealous professors of religion can be deceived. The story unfolds in three acts:

**Act I — The City of Saints.** The people of Auchtermuchty become so "rigidly righteous" that their religious activity attracts the attention of Hell itself. The devils, alarmed at the competition, dispatch one of their own to corrupt the town from within.

**Act II — The False Prophet.** On a Sunday morning when the real minister fails to appear, a mysterious stranger in a flowing black robe enters the kirk and preaches a sermon of "striking, sublime, and awful" power from Ezekiel 21:27: "I will overturn, overturn, overturn it; and it shall be no more, until he come, whose right it is, and I will give it him." He interprets the "it" as the town of Auchtermuchty itself, condemning all its inhabitants to perdition and demanding a "radical change" in their worship. The people, perversely delighted by their own damnation, receive him as a prophet and an angel.

**Act III — The Cloven Foot.** Only Robin Ruthven, a cunning old man who "had been in the hands o' the fairies when he was young," sees through the deception — having overheard two corbie-craws (raven-like crows) plotting the town's destruction the night before. At the moment of the preacher's greatest triumph, Robin lifts the hem of the stranger's gown before the assembled multitude, revealing a pair of cloven feet. The Devil, exposed, "dartit into the air like a fiery dragon, an' keust a reid rainbow o'er the taps o' the Lowmonds" (006-350).

The tale's coda is the most pointed: ever since, it has been nearly impossible to make an Auchtermuchty man either listen to a sermon or applaud one, "for he thinks aye that he sees the cloven foot peeping out frae aneath ilka sentence."

## Literary Significance

The Auchtermuchty tale is one of the most extended and artistically accomplished embedded narratives in the novel. It serves multiple functions:

- **Satire of religious enthusiasm**: The tale mocks the self-congratulatory piety that mistakes spectacular damnation-preaching for true gospel ministry.
- **The cloven-foot motif**: The image of the Devil's hoof concealed beneath the preacher's gown is a thematic key to the entire novel — a visual emblem of the hidden evil beneath a profession of righteousness, directly paralleling the figure of Gil-Martin.
- **The Robin Ruthven counterpoint**: Robin, the simple man with "second sight" from his fairy encounters, discerns what the learned and the zealous cannot — a pattern that echoes the novel's broader epistemological critique of antinomian certainty.
- **Narrative distancing**: By embedding the tale in the voice of Lucky Shaw (reported by Samuel Scrape to Robert Wringhim), Hogg creates multiple layers of narrative distance that complicate any simple moral reading.
