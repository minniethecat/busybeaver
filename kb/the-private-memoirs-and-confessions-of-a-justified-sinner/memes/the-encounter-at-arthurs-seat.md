# The Encounter at Arthur's Seat

## Description

The encounter on Arthur's Seat is one of the novel's most atmospheric and ambiguous scenes. On the wild slopes of the ancient volcano overlooking Edinburgh, characters meet in circumstances that blur the boundary between natural and supernatural, rational and inexplicable. The scene is described differently in the Editor's Narrative and Robert's Confessions, each account revealing the limitations of its perspective.

The Editor describes the excursion explicitly:

> "George was requested by some of his associates of the College to go with him to take a week's excursion to the top of Arthur's Seat, to breathe the breeze of the sea, and to witness the sunrise over the hills of Fife." (004-138)

> "They found, on reaching the top of Arthur's Seat, that this sublunary rainbow, the offspring of the morning dew and the sun's rays, had vanished." (004-140)

## Narrative Significance

Arthur's Seat functions as a liminal space where the novel's epistemological crisis becomes spatial. On this wild hill, the Editor's rational explanations lose their force and Robert's supernatural worldview gains a plausibility that it lacks in the city below. The mysterious figure who appears there — whether [[gil-martin|Gil-Martin]] or some other presence — embodies the novel's deepest ambiguity: "being confounded between the shadow and the substance, he knew not which was which" (004-143).
