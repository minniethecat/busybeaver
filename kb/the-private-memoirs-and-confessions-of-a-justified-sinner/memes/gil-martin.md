# Gil-Martin

## Biography

Gil-Martin is the most enigmatic figure in [[james-hogg|Hogg]]'s novel. He first appears to [[robert-wringhim|Robert Wringhim]] shortly after [[robert-wringhim|Robert]]'s "conversion" experience, recognizing him immediately as one of the elect and offering to become his constant companion (006-076). His origins are never explained: he seems to appear from nowhere, has no family or history, and possesses supernatural abilities — including shapeshifting, appearing and disappearing at will, and the power to take on the exact physical appearance of [[robert-wringhim|Robert]] himself (006-144).

> "You think I am your brother," said he; "or that I am your second self. I am indeed your brother, not according to the flesh, but in my belief of the same truths, and my assurance in the same mode of redemption, than which I hold nothing so great or so glorious on earth." (006-078)
>
> — Gil-Martin's first words to Robert, establishing the theological frame for their relationship and hinting at the demonic parody of brotherhood

> "Very well, you may call me Gil-Martin. It is not my Christian name; but it is a name which may serve your turn." (006-124)
>
> — Gil-Martin introduces himself to Robert, withholding his true identity from the start

The narrative strongly implies — but never definitively confirms — that Gil-Martin is the devil. His name ("Gil-Martin" suggests "guile-Martin" or perhaps a reference to the folkloric "Gille Martin," a trickster figure), his shapeshifting abilities (006-144), and his systematic destruction of [[robert-wringhim|Robert]]'s moral sense all point to a demonic identity. However, the novel also allows for a psychological reading in which Gil-Martin is a projection of [[robert-wringhim|Robert]]'s own psyche — his "double" or dark side externalized.

> "I have no parents save one, whom I do not acknowledge," said he proudly. "Therefore, pray drop that subject, lest it should give rise to lengthened explanations." (006-126)
>
> — Gil-Martin's refusal to disclose his parentage, hinting at a Satanic origin while refusing to confirm it

> "The appearance was as if there had been a veil drawn over me, so nigh that I put up my hand to feel it; and then Gil-Martin frowned, and asked me what I was doing." (006-144)
>
> — Robert's description of one of Gil-Martin's supernatural manifestations

## Role in the Narrative

Gil-Martin functions as the novel's engine of destruction. He systematically:
- Reinforces [[robert-wringhim|Robert]]'s [[antinomianism|Antinomian]] theology, assuring him that the elect cannot sin (006-116)
- Provides theological rationalizations for every crime [[robert-wringhim|Robert]] contemplates
- Isolates [[robert-wringhim|Robert]] from anyone who might challenge his convictions (006-149)
- Urges [[robert-wringhim|Robert]] toward increasingly serious crimes, from harassment to murder (006-273)
- Shames and bullies [[robert-wringhim|Robert]] when he shows any sign of moral hesitation (006-227)

His most disturbing power is his ability to *become* [[robert-wringhim|Robert]] — to take on his physical form and commit crimes that [[robert-wringhim|Robert]] himself cannot remember. This blurring of identity is the novel's most powerful representation of the divided self. Gil-Martin explains his shapeshifting as a natural gift that allows him to sympathize with those he meets:

> "Yes," said he, "and it is that which attaches me to you. By assuming your likeness yesterday, I became acquainted with your character, and was no less astonished at the profundity and range of your thoughts than at the heroic magnanimity with which these were combined." (006-115)
>
> — Gil-Martin's explanation of his shapeshifting masks manipulation as flattery

## The Nature of Gil-Martin

The identity and ontological status of Gil-Martin constitute the central enigma of the novel. [[james-hogg|Hogg]] deliberately sustains the ambiguity between a supernatural and a psychological reading, and the text supplies evidence for both interpretations.

### The Demonic Reading

Multiple textual clues point to Gil-Martin's Satanic identity. Robert's mother immediately suspects diabolic agency when she hears of the stranger: "It is one of the Devil's most profound wiles to appear like one" (006-099). Gil-Martin's shapeshifting ability aligns with the traditional demonic power of assuming pleasing forms to deceive. His name — a compound of "guile" (deception) and "Martin" (possibly referring to the folkloric trickster "Gille Martin" or to Martin Luther, whom the Antinomians would have repudiated) — encodes duplicity in its very syllables. His systematic destruction of Robert's soul, culminating in abandonment (004-363), follows the classic pattern of demonic temptation: the devil offers friendship, enables sin, and then discards the ruined soul.

Crucially, Gil-Martin never explicitly claims to be anything other than what he is — he simply allows Robert's theological framework to supply a benign interpretation. When Robert's father interrogates him about the stranger's doctrine, Robert reports that he adhered fully to the family's theological principles, and his father concludes: "Then he was no agent of the Wicked One with whom you held converse" (006-102). This is ironic confirmation of the opposite: the devil's most effective disguise is perfect doctrinal orthodoxy.

### The Psychological Reading

Equally compelling is the reading of Gil-Martin as a projection of Robert's psyche — the externalization of his repressed moral knowledge that his deeds are evil. On this reading, Gil-Martin is Robert's "shadow" in the Jungian sense: the disowned aspects of the self that, when denied, return with destructive force. The fact that Gil-Martin first appears immediately after Robert's "conversion" experience (006-103) — the moment at which his ego becomes most invested in seeing himself as righteous — supports the psychological interpretation: the more strenuously Robert insists on his own goodness, the more powerfully his knowledge of his own evil demands external embodiment.

The novel's most striking passage in support of the psychological reading comes when Robert articulates the "two souls" theory:

> "If this that you tell me be true," said I, "then is it as true that I have two souls, which take possession of my bodily frame by turns, the one being all unconscious of what the other performs; for as sure as I have at this moment a spirit within me, fashioned and destined to eternal felicity, as sure am I utterly ignorant of the crimes you now lay to my charge." (006-327)

This moment is both theological crisis and psychological breakthrough: Robert almost arrives at self-knowledge, recognizing that the "Gil-Martin" self and the "Robert" self are aspects of a single, divided consciousness. But the insight is immediately foreclosed by his theological framework, which can only interpret the division as possession rather than repression.

### The Theological Reading

A third reading, compatible with the first two, treats Gil-Martin as the *logical consequence* of [[antinomianism|Antinomian]] theology made flesh. On this reading, Gil-Martin *is* what happens when the doctrine of absolute predestination is internalized by a human psyche: the doctrine itself becomes a demonic agent, systematically destroying the moral sense it was supposed to support. Gil-Martin never teaches Robert anything that is not already implicit in his father's theology — he merely draws out the implications that the Reverend Wringhim's sermons had kept safely abstract. The devil's work, in this novel, consists largely of taking theology seriously.

## Relationships

- **[[robert-wringhim|Robert Wringhim]]**: Gil-Martin's "friend" and protégé — or victim. The relationship is a masterclass in psychological manipulation disguised as spiritual mentorship (006-124).
- **[[george-colwan|George Colwan]]**: Gil-Martin views [[george-colwan|George]] as an obstacle to be eliminated, and encourages [[robert-wringhim|Robert]]'s murderous intent toward him (006-149).
- **[[bessy-gillies|Bessy Gillies]]**: One of the few characters who recognizes Gil-Martin for what he is, calling him a "warlock" and refusing to be deceived by his shapeshifting (004-363).

## Key Appearances

- **First Meeting (006-076)**: Gil-Martin appears to Robert as a mysterious young man on the moor and immediately claims spiritual kinship.
- **Self-Identification (006-124)**: Gil-Martin reluctantly supplies a name, refusing to give his "Christian name."
- **Theological Indoctrination (006-116)**: Gil-Martin teaches [[robert-wringhim|Robert]] "the infallibility of the elect, and the preordination of all things."
- **The Ambush (006-149)**: Gil-Martin participates in the attempted murder of [[george-colwan|George Colwan]], loading a weapon alongside Robert.
- **The Murder (006-273)**: Gil-Martin participates in — or directly commits — the murder of [[george-colwan|George Colwan]], while [[robert-wringhim|Robert]]'s memory of the event is unclear.
- **Final Abandonment (004-363)**: In the novel's final pages, Gil-Martin abandons [[robert-wringhim|Robert]] to his fate, his work of destruction complete.

## Critical Interpretation

Gil-Martin has been variously interpreted as a literal devil, a figment of [[robert-wringhim|Robert]]'s diseased imagination, a representation of the Jungian "shadow," and a symbol of the destructive potential of bad theology. The novel's genius is that it sustains all these interpretations simultaneously: Gil-Martin *is* the devil, *and* a psychological projection, *and* the logical consequence of [[antinomianism|Antinomian]] theology made flesh.

The character draws on multiple literary and theological traditions: the Miltonic Satan (a sophisticated tempter who deploys theological argument), the folkloric trickster (Gille Martin of Scottish tradition), and the Calvinist conception of the devil as one who can quote Scripture for his purposes. His shapeshifting ability — particularly his capacity to assume [[robert-wringhim|Robert]]'s exact form — has been read as a powerful metaphor for how evil does not always appear alien: it can wear the face of the self.

> "I had no time for an alternative: Gil-Martin's ball had not taken effect, which was altogether wonderful, as the old man's breast was within a few yards of him." (006-153)
>
> — The mysterious failure of Gil-Martin's shot, hinting at supernatural forces at work

Scholars have noted that Gil-Martin anticipates several major figures in 19th-century Gothic and supernatural fiction. His ability to assume another's form while that person is unconscious of the impersonation prefigures the doppelgänger traditions in Stevenson, Wilde, and later horror fiction. His role as the suave, intellectually gifted tempter who never directly lies but always misleads draws on the Miltonic tradition of Satan as the most eloquent of the fallen angels — one whose arguments are technically true but whose conclusions are damnable. The critical consensus holds that Gil-Martin is one of the most successful representations of supernatural evil in English fiction precisely because his demonic nature is never definitively established: the ambiguity is the point.
