# Grace (Doctrine)

## Definition

In Reformed theology, grace is the unmerited, sovereign favor of God extended to sinners through Jesus Christ. It is the sole efficient cause of salvation: election is by grace, justification is by grace, sanctification is by grace, and glorification is by grace. The Reformed tradition distinguishes between "common grace" (God's general benevolence to all creation) and "special" or "saving grace" (extended only to the elect). In Hogg's novel, the language of grace is repeatedly invoked — but invariably in contexts that expose its abuse. The novel's key insight is that the most consoling of Christian doctrines can become the most dangerous when it is detached from the moral transformation it is meant to produce.

## Theological Background

The Reformation was, at its core, a recovery of the Augustinian doctrine of grace against what the Reformers saw as the Pelagian tendencies of medieval Catholicism. Calvin's *Institutes* made grace the organizing principle of soteriology: salvation is wholly of grace, from election to glorification. The Synod of Dort (1618–1619) affirmed that grace is "irresistible" — the elect cannot finally reject it. This doctrine was preached with particular fervor in Scotland, where the Covenanting tradition emphasized the sovereignty of grace over against any hint of human merit or cooperation in salvation.

The pastoral challenge of grace preaching was perennial: how to preach the freeness of grace without encouraging moral licence. Paul had faced this objection in Romans 6:1 — "Shall we continue in sin, that grace may abound?" — and the Reformed tradition developed careful distinctions to answer it. Grace frees the believer *from* sin, not *for* sin; the same grace that justifies also sanctifies. The Antinomian error was precisely to claim the first half of this formula (grace frees from sin's penalty) while denying the second (grace frees from sin's power). In the Scottish context, the emotional intensity of grace preaching — with its dramatic contrasts between human depravity and divine mercy — created conditions in which this error could flourish. It is this emotional intensity that Hogg's novel captures — and critiques.

## In the Novel

Grace appears throughout the novel, but almost always in contexts of irony or abuse. The Reverend Wringhim preaches a system of "justifying-grace against all breaches of all laws" (004-056) — a formulation that effectively abolishes law in the name of grace. The Laird of Dalcastle's rebuke to Wringhim is pointed: "Your righteousness consists in splitting the doctrines of Calvin into thousands of undistinguishable fragments, and in dealing out these with a sullen, proud, and affected malignity" (004-056). The accusation is that Wringhim's "grace" is not the biblical doctrine but a weaponized version of it, used to condemn others while exempting himself.

Robert Wringhim internalizes this teaching, declaring that he "depended entirely on the bounty of free grace" for his standing before God, while simultaneously committing murder (006-071). This passage is the novel's most concentrated statement of the Antinomian perversion of grace:

> "I had more sense than to regard either my good works, or my evil deeds, as in the smallest degree influencing the eternal decrees of God with regard to my acceptance or reprobation. I depended entirely on the bounty of free grace, holding all the righteousness of man as filthy rags, and all his doings as a vain show." (006-071)

The logic is impeccable Antinomian reasoning: if grace is truly free, then works — good or evil — are irrelevant to one's standing before God. The conclusion Robert draws is that he need not concern himself with moral conduct at all. The novel dramatizes where this logic leads.

The novel's sharpest satire appears in the phrase "the election of grace" (006-180) — a biblical phrase (Romans 11:5) that Robert uses to justify his sense of superiority over others. He preaches "the great doctrine of the election of grace" and then demands of his hearers: "Now, friend, you must tell me if you pertain to this chosen number" (006-180). Grace, which should produce humility and gratitude, produces instead arrogance and interrogation. Hogg shows how easily the language of grace, when detached from the moral transformation it is meant to produce, becomes a vocabulary of self-exoneration and other-condemnation.

Robert also preaches "redemption by grace, preordination, and eternal predestination" (006-367), a formulation that bundles grace with the more controversial doctrines of predestination in a way that makes the whole package seem equally authoritative. The rhetorical strategy is to make questioning Antinomian grace equivalent to questioning the gospel itself — a move that, as the novel shows, is both intellectually dishonest and pastorally destructive.

## Critical Significance

Hogg's treatment of grace is a study in how the most consoling of Christian doctrines can become the most dangerous when psychologically distorted. The novel does not deny the reality of grace — but it shows how the *language* of grace can be weaponized by those who wish to evade moral accountability. In this sense, the novel anticipates modern critiques of religious discourse as a vehicle for self-deception.

The novel's method is not theological polemic but narrative demonstration. Hogg does not argue that the Reformed doctrine of grace is false; he shows, through the unfolding disaster of Robert's life, that a particular way of *using* the doctrine — as a guarantee of immunity from moral consequence — is catastrophically wrong. The fact that Robert's "grace" language is often formally orthodox (he uses biblical phrases and credal formulations) makes the critique more, not less, powerful: the problem is not that the language is false but that it is being used to serve a psychological need (the need for certainty, for superiority, for exoneration) rather than to describe a theological reality. Grace, in Robert's hands, is not a description of God's attitude toward him but a tool for managing his own anxiety — and as such, it is infinitely malleable and morally inert.
