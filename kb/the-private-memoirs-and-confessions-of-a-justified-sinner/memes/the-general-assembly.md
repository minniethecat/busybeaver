# The General Assembly of the Church of Scotland

## Overview

The General Assembly is the highest court of the Church of Scotland, consisting of ministers and ruling elders commissioned from every Presbytery in the country. It meets annually in Edinburgh and has final authority in matters of doctrine, discipline, worship, and church law within the national church. In Hogg's novel, the General Assembly is the ecclesiastical establishment against which the Antiburgher sectarians have defined their identity.

## Historical Context

Established after the Scottish Reformation, the General Assembly was both a religious and a political institution. In the late 17th and early 18th centuries — the period of the novel's setting — the Assembly was deeply entangled with the political struggles between Presbyterianism and Episcopacy, Covenanters and Crown. The Revolution Settlement of 1690 re-established Presbyterian church government in Scotland, making the Assembly once again the supreme ecclesiastical authority.

The Secession of 1733 — and the subsequent Antiburgher split of 1747 — were direct challenges to the Assembly's authority. Seceders argued that the Assembly had compromised on essential matters (patronage, doctrine, discipline), and they withdrew to form their own church courts. The Antiburghers, led by Adam Gib, took the stricter position that the burgess oath taken by town councillors implied an endorsement of the established church and was therefore sinful. This is the historical background against which the Reverend Wringhim's isolation from the established church must be understood. The Assemblies of the 1690s and early 1700s were particularly contentious: debates over the Union of 1707, the toleration of Episcopalians, and the lingering influence of the Covenanting tradition all divided the church's supreme court.

The Assembly's authority extended beyond strictly ecclesiastical matters. Its annual meetings were major events in Edinburgh's civic calendar, drawing ministers and elders from across Scotland and attracting public attention. The Assembly could address the Crown on matters of national importance, and its decisions carried weight far beyond the church. To be excluded from the Assembly — as the Seceders were — was to be excluded from a central institution of Scottish public life.

## In the Novel

The General Assembly does not appear directly in the novel as a dramatic setting, but its presence is felt as the institutional Other — the established church from which the Antiburghers have separated. When the narrative notes that the Reverend Wringhim is "a strict Antiburgher" (004-032), it identifies him as someone who has rejected the authority of the General Assembly and the established church it governs.

This rejection is radical in its implications. In orthodox Presbyterian polity, ministers are answerable to their Presbytery and, ultimately, to the General Assembly. Doctrinal innovations, disciplinary actions, and pastoral decisions are subject to review by the higher courts. The Reverend Wringhim's Antiburgher stance severs this chain of accountability. His teaching of absolute predestination "in its most rigid form" (004-032) would have been subject to challenge in the Assembly's courts — but Wringhim has placed himself beyond their reach.

The novel also depicts the Assembly — or rather the world of the established church — indirectly through the figure of the Laird of Dalcastle, who mocks his wife's extreme piety and the minister who feeds it:

> "...he cut her short in all her futile attempts at spiritualization, and mocked at her wire-drawn degrees of faith, hope, and repentance. He also dared to doubt of the great standard doctrine of absolute predestination..." (004-032)

The Assembly thus functions as a symbolic background: the moderate, institutional, accountable form of Presbyterianism that the novel's Antinomian characters have left behind. Its absence from the direct action is itself significant — the tragedy unfolds precisely in the space where institutional oversight has been rejected.

The wider religious culture shaped by the Assembly's authority — the public worship, the preaching, the expectation of moral accountability — is visible in the novel's many references to churches, assemblies, and religious gatherings that form the texture of Scottish life:

> "In the gallery of the Parliament House, in the boxes of the play-house, in the church, in the assembly, in the streets, suburbs, and the fields..." (004-135)

## Theological/Legal Significance

The General Assembly represents the principle of collective, institutional accountability in church governance — precisely what the Antiburgher sect lacks. In orthodox Presbyterian polity, no doctrinal innovation or disciplinary action is valid without the concurrence of the relevant church court. The novel's tragedy unfolds in a space where this accountability has been severed: the Reverend Wringhim answers to no Session, no Presbytery, and no Assembly. His radical teaching is the product of institutional isolation.

The legal parallel is instructive. Just as the earthly law courts fail to convict Robert Wringhim of murder, the ecclesiastical courts — had they retained jurisdiction — might have been able to challenge his theology before it produced its murderous fruit. The novel suggests that institutional accountability is not merely a bureaucratic formality but a safeguard against the spiritual corruption that flourishes in isolation. The General Assembly, whatever its historical failings, represents a form of church governance in which power is distributed, decisions are debated, and no single voice — however charismatically certain of its election — can claim unquestionable authority.
