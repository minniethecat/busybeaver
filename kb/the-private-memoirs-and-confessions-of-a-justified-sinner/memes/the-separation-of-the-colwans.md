# The Separation of the Colwans

## Description

The separation of the Laird of Dalcastle and his wife Arabella over irreconcilable religious differences is the originating event of the novel's tragedy. Arabella, having fallen under the influence of the Reverend Wringhim's Antinomian preaching, finds her husband's moderate religion intolerable. The marriage dissolves, and Arabella takes the infant Robert with her — ensuring that he will be raised entirely within the Antinomian sect.

The Editor records this domestic rupture with characteristic understatement:

> "She therefore bespoke a separate establishment, and, before the last days of autumn were gone, she was settled in a convenient house, and all the country could not prevent it." (004-032)

The religious cause of the separation is evident in the same passage: "She knew that her husband's principles were loose, and that he had no idea of family worship" — the doctrinal chasm between the lax, moderate laird and the zealously pious wife making cohabitation impossible.

## Narrative Significance

The separation establishes the novel's central theme: that theological extremism destroys families. It also creates the structural division that drives the plot — two brothers raised apart, in radically different religious environments, whose paths will fatally intersect. [[robert-wringhim|Robert Wringhim]] himself later reflects on this separation in his Confessions, describing how "no disposition in any of us to separate, he drove me home before him" (006-069) — indicating how deeply the pattern of division and flight has marked his psyche.
