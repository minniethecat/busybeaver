# Arabella (Rabina) Colwan

## Biography

Arabella Colwan (also called Rabina) is the daughter of Baillie Orde of Glasgow, the wife of [[laird-of-dalcastle|the Laird]] of [[dalcastle|Dalcastle]], and mother of both [[george-colwan|George Colwan]] and [[robert-wringhim|Robert Wringhim]]. Her story, as presented in the Editor's Narrative and inflected by Robert's own Confessions, is a study in how religious extremism destroys the domestic sphere.

Her marriage to the Laird of Dalcastle was a mismatch from its very first night. While the laird treated the wedding as an occasion for "feasting, dancing, piping, and singing" (004-004), Arabella could think only of prayer:

> "Sure, Mr. Colwan, you won't go to bed to-night, at such an important period of your life, without first saying prayers for yourself and me." (004-007)

The laird's worldly response — "Prayers, Mistress! Lord help your crazed head, is this a night for prayers?" (004-009) — establishes the fundamental incompatibility that will define their marriage. Arabella's answer is a "torrent of profound divinity" (004-010) that leaves the laird bewildered and his bride confirmed in her sense of righteous isolation.

The marriage deteriorates rapidly. Arabella refuses to share her husband's bed, declaring:

> "Sir, I will let you know that I detest your principles and your person alike. It shall never be said, Sir, that my person was at the control of a heathenish man of Belial—a dangler among the daughters of women—a promiscuous dancer—and a player of unlawful games." (004-018)

Within a day of the wedding, she has abandoned the marital home and returned to her father in Glasgow:

> Matters, without all doubt, had been very bad between the new-married couple; for in the course of the day the lady deserted her quarters, and returned to her father's house in Glasgow, after having been a night on the road; stage-coaches and steam-boats having then no existence in that quarter. (004-023)

Her father the Baillie, far from comforting his daughter, beats her for the trouble her marriage has caused him, and she is soon returned to Dalcastle. But the marriage never recovers. The theological gulf between husband and wife only widens:

> The laird kept his temper a long while, but at length his patience wore out; he cut her short in all her futile attempts at spiritualization, and mocked at her wire-drawn degrees of faith, hope, and repentance. He also dared to doubt of the great standard doctrine of absolute predestination, which put the crown on the lady's Christian resentment. She declared her helpmate to be a limb of Antichrist... (004-032)

Shortly after her marriage, Arabella falls under the influence of the Reverend [[rev-robert-wringhim|Robert Wringhim]], an extreme Calvinist preacher whose arrival at Dalcastle she greets as salvation itself. She becomes his devoted follower, and the narrative strongly implies that her relationship with the minister is adulterous and that [[robert-wringhim|Robert]] is the product of this union.

After the birth of her first son, George, whom the laird acknowledges as his heir, Arabella shows no maternal interest: the nurse is given "permission to take the boy to his mother's presence if ever she should desire to see him; but, strange as it may appear, she never once desired to see him from the day that he was born" (004-061).

When her second son, Robert, is born, she raises him entirely within the Antinomian sect, inculcating in him the doctrine of absolute predestination that will become the theological justification for his crimes:

> Such were the tenets in which it would appear young Robert was bred. He was an acute boy, an excellent learner, had ardent and ungovernable passions, and, withal, a sternness of demeanour from which other boys shrunk. He was the best grammarian, the best reader, writer, and accountant in the various classes that he attended, and was fond of writing essays on controverted points of theology... (004-063)

Robert's own account of his origins, from his Confessions, confirms his mother's influence while casting it in the language of spiritual pride:

> My mother was a burning and a shining light, in the community of Scottish worthies, and in the days of her virginity had suffered much in the persecution of the saints. But it so pleased Heaven that, as a trial of her faith, she was married to one of the wicked; a man all over spotted with the leprosy of sin. (006-002)

The tragic irony of Arabella's life is that the son she raised in the strictest Antinomian principles becomes her murderer. During one of Robert's periods of oblivion — when, the novel suggests, his consciousness is displaced by that of [[gil-martin|Gil-Martin]] or by a secondary self — Arabella is killed. Robert has no memory of the act, but Gil-Martin confronts him with the accusation:

> "You are accused of having made away with your mother privately; as also of the death of a beautiful young lady, whose affections you had seduced." (006-322)

Arabella's body is discovered buried in the sands of the linn, and the discovery triggers the final cascade of events that leads to Robert's flight and eventual suicide.

## Role in the Narrative

Arabella is the novel's portrait of how religious fanaticism destroys families. Her zealotry is not merely personal but structural: it breaks the marriage bond, alienates one son from his father, and provides the environment in which [[robert-wringhim|Robert]]'s destructive theology can flourish unchecked. She appears relatively briefly in the Editor's Narrative but her influence pervades the entire novel.

Her presence in the narrative also serves to ground the theological abstractions of the novel in domestic reality. The doctrine of predestination is not merely debated in pulpits and pamphlets — it estranges husbands from wives, mothers from children, and sets brother against brother. Arabella is the character through whom theology enters the household and becomes fate.

## Relationships

- **The [[laird-of-dalcastle|Laird of Dalcastle]]**: Her husband, whom she abandons over theological differences. Their marriage is a microcosm of the conflict between worldly moderation and religious extremism.
- **Reverend [[rev-robert-wringhim|Robert Wringhim]]**: Her spiritual guide and implied lover. His influence transforms her from a pious young woman into a fanatic.
- **[[george-colwan|George Colwan]]**: Her firstborn son, whom she leaves to be raised by his father and never desires to see.
- **[[robert-wringhim|Robert Wringhim]]**: Her second son, whom she raises in the [[antinomianism|Antinomian]] faith — and by whom she is eventually murdered.
- **[[miss-logan|Arabella Logan]]**: Her rival for the laird's affection and, after Arabella's death, her son's most determined accuser. The two share a given name, suggesting a dark structural parallel.
- **[[bell-calvert|Bell Calvert]]**: Shares her given name "Arabella" — a doubleness that gestures toward the novel's themes of split identity and parallel lives.

## Key Appearances

- **The wedding night (004-004--004-019)**: Arabella's demand for prayers, her torrent of divinity, and her refusal to sleep with her husband. The scene establishes the irreconcilable conflict that drives the novel's domestic plot.

- **The desertion (004-023)**: Arabella returns to her father's house in Glasgow the day after her wedding — an act that sets the pattern for her life: withdrawal from the world into religious isolation.

- **The arrival of Reverend Wringhim (004-038)**: Arabella welcomes her spiritual guide to Dalcastle with "great consolation." From this point, the household is divided into upper and lower spheres — religious and worldly — that correspond to the novel's thematic divisions.

- **The birth of George (004-061)**: Arabella gives birth to her first son but "never once desired to see him from the day that he was born" — a chilling detail that establishes the unnatural character of her maternal feeling.

- **Robert's upbringing (004-063)**: The Editor's description of Robert's education in his mother's tenets shows how theology becomes character.

- **Robert's account of his mother (006-001--006-003)**: Robert's Confessions open with a description of his mother as "a burning and a shining light" — an account that reveals how fully he has internalized her self-understanding.

- **Robert's growing hatred of his mother (006-301)**: As Robert's relationship with Gil-Martin intensifies, his mother becomes increasingly obnoxious to him: "The very sound of her voice at a distance went to my heart like an arrow, and made all my nerves to shrink."

- **The murder and discovery (006-322, 006-355--006-357)**: Arabella's death at Robert's hands (or at the hands of the being who shares his form) and the discovery of her body precipitate the novel's final crisis.

## Critical Interpretation

Arabella functions as a cautionary figure: her sincerity is never in doubt, but her zealotry is shown to be destructive to everyone around her. She embodies the novel's warning that religious extremism is not merely an intellectual error but a force that destroys families, marriages, and lives.

Feminist readings of the novel have found in Arabella a more complex figure than the misogynist caricature she might initially appear. Her religious extremism is also a form of resistance — the only language of power available to a woman in a patriarchal marriage.

Her refusal of her husband's bed, her cultivation of an alternative spiritual authority through Reverend Wringhim, and her claim to superior theological knowledge are all assertions of agency within a system that grants women very little. That these assertions prove destructive is part of the novel's tragic vision.

Her relationship to her sons has been read as a study in the pathology of religious parenting. She loves the son who reflects her theology (Robert) and rejects the son who reflects her husband's worldliness (George). This division of maternal feeling along theological lines replays the division of the household and, ultimately, of Scotland itself in the period after the Reformation.

The fact that Arabella shares her given name with both Arabella Logan and Arabella (Bell) Calvert creates a web of doubling that critics have connected to the novel's larger exploration of split identity.

The three Arabellas represent different responses to the same theological culture: one succumbs to fanaticism (Lady Dalcastle), one pursues justice (Miss Logan), and one survives through marginality (Bell Calvert).
