# The Double (Doppelgänger)

## Definition

The double (German: *Doppelgänger*, "double-goer") is a literary motif in which a character encounters a duplicate or counterpart of themselves — often representing a repressed, darker, or morally antithetical aspect of their own personality. The double embodies the psychological reality of internal conflict, dramatizing the experience of being "divided against oneself." Hogg's *Confessions of a Justified Sinner* is one of the earliest and most influential treatments of this motif in English literature, and its theological framing of the double distinguishes it from later, purely psychological treatments of the theme.

## Literary Context

The doppelgänger motif emerged in German Romanticism (Jean Paul, E.T.A. Hoffmann) and was developed in British Gothic fiction through works such as Hogg's *Confessions* (1824) and, later, Stevenson's *Dr. Jekyll and Mr. Hyde* (1886), Wilde's *The Picture of Dorian Gray* (1890), and Conrad's "The Secret Sharer" (1910). Hogg's contribution is distinctive in two respects: first, the double is given a theological explanation (Gil-Martin may be the devil, or a psychological projection, or both — the novel deliberately sustains both readings); second, the narrative structure itself mirrors the theme of doubleness through its split into an "Editor's Narrative" and Robert's "Confessions."

The theological explanation is crucial to understanding Hogg's originality. In the German Romantic tradition, the double typically represents a psychological projection — the return of the repressed, the dark side of the self. Hogg retains this psychological dimension but adds a theological one: Gil-Martin is not merely a projection but possibly a demonic entity that exploits Robert's psychological vulnerabilities. This dual reading — psychological and supernatural — is the source of the novel's ambiguity and power. As one character warns, "It is incalculable what evil such a person as he may do, if so disposed. There is a sublimity in his ideas, with which there is to me a mixture of terror" (006-130).

## In the Novel

The double in Hogg's novel operates on multiple levels:

- **Character level**: Gil-Martin is Robert's double — or Robert is Gil-Martin's. When they first meet, Gil-Martin declares: "You think I am your brother... or that I am your second self" (006-078). Their identities blur; Robert cannot distinguish his own actions from Gil-Martin's, and at times they seem to occupy the same body. Gil-Martin reveals his shape-shifting power explicitly: "If I contemplate a man's features seriously, mine own gradually assume the very same appearance and character. And what is more, by contemplating a face minutely, I am drawn insensibly into the same current of thought with that person" (006-113). This is more than mimicry — it is a form of ontological absorption in which the double takes on not just the appearance but the inner life of the person it imitates.

- **Narrative level**: The novel is split into two conflicting accounts — the Editor's rationalist third-person narrative and Robert's first-person confession. These two narratives represent two ways of understanding the same events, neither of which is fully adequate. The Editor's account has the facts but misses the psychology; Robert's account has the psychology but is morally deranged. The reader is left suspended between them, unable to fully trust either.

- **Theological level**: Robert's self is divided between the "justified" self (the elect person who cannot sin) and the "sinner" self (who murders, lies, and deceives). Hogg's theological insight is that Antinomianism creates a psychological double: by denying that the elect can truly sin, it forces the believer to construct a second self who commits the sins. Gil-Martin is, in this reading, the externalization of Robert's disowned moral agency — the self that does what the "justified" self cannot acknowledge doing.

The shape-shifting theme extends beyond Robert and Gil-Martin. When Robert's father encounters him after his encounter with Gil-Martin, he is alarmed: "Something has indeed befallen you, either in body or mind, boy, for you are transformed, since the morning, that I could not have known you" (006-091). The transformation is both physical and spiritual — to be in Gil-Martin's company is to become, literally, someone else.

The novel also explores doubleness through the motif of mistaken identity and blurred likeness. The question of Robert's paternity — is he the Laird's son or the Reverend's? — turns on physical resemblance, and the servant John insists that the likeness is "fer closer" than mere coincidence (006-043). Likewise, Gil-Martin's ability to assume the appearance of Drummond means that the wrong man is convicted of murder: "So like was he that there was not one item in dress, form, feature, nor voice, by which I could distinguish the one from the other" (004-290). Doubleness in the novel is not merely a psychological phenomenon but a pervasive structural principle that governs identity, testimony, and justice.

## Critical Significance

Hogg's treatment of the double is a major landmark in the literary history of the motif. By giving the doppelgänger a theological rather than merely psychological explanation, Hogg achieves something that later, purely secular treatments of the double cannot: he makes the divided self not merely a psychological curiosity but a *moral catastrophe*. The novel asks not just "what would it be like to be divided against oneself?" but "what would it *mean* — morally, spiritually, eternally?"

The novel's influence on subsequent literature is profound. Stevenson acknowledged his debt to Hogg in the creation of *Jekyll and Hyde*, and the novel's exploration of the divided self anticipates Freudian and Jungian models of the unconscious. But Hogg's double is more disturbing than later versions precisely because it resists resolution into a single explanatory framework. Gil-Martin may be the devil, a psychological projection, a real person with paranormal powers, or all three — and the novel's refusal to decide makes the experience of reading it genuinely unsettling in a way that more neatly resolved treatments of the double are not.

The theological dimension is also what gives the novel its distinctive ethical seriousness. Later treatments of the double tend to focus on the psychology of repression and release — the double represents what the conscious self refuses to acknowledge. Hogg's treatment goes further: the double represents not merely repressed desire but disowned *moral agency*. By externalizing his capacity for evil into Gil-Martin, Robert achieves a state of psychological comfort that is indistinguishable from damnation. The novel's warning is that the divided self is not a solution to the problem of moral responsibility but the ultimate evasion of it.
