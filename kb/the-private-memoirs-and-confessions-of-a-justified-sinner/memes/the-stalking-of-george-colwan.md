# The Stalking of George Colwan

## Description

In the weeks leading up to George Colwan's murder, Robert Wringhim and Gil-Martin conduct a campaign of stalking and psychological harassment against him. They follow him through Edinburgh's streets, appear at his social engagements, and create an atmosphere of menace that George cannot explain or escape. The stalking is described in the Editor's Narrative with documentary precision, yet its motivations remain opaque — visible from the outside but inaccessible to rational understanding.

The Editor describes the stalking with chilling specificity:

> "Mr. Robert Wringhim attended him as constantly as his shadow; was always in his way as with intention to impede him, and ever and anon his deep and malignant eye met those of his elder brother with a glance so quick that it seemed too momentary to import anything, and yet conveyed something that was never to be forgotten." (004-066)

> "He seemed to have a delight in following him to all places of public resort; it was seldom that he saw him either following him in the streets, or entering any house... The thing became so well known that George was constrained to keep at home." (004-135)

Robert himself confesses the campaign in his *Confessions*, though he frames it as persecution *of* him rather than *by* him:

> "For a month I saw no one but such as came into my room, and, for all that, it will be seen that there were plenty of the same set to attest upon oath that I saw my brother every day during this period; that I persecuted him, with my presence day and night, while all the time I never saw his face save in a delusive dream." (006-198)

## Narrative Significance

The stalking episodes are the novel's most sustained depiction of Robert's psychological derangement as it manifests in observable behavior. They show that Antinomian conviction is not merely a private theological error but a force that enters the public world and has real, terrifying consequences for others.
