# Bell Calvert

## Biography

Bell Calvert (born Arabella Calvert) is a woman of deeply troubled life and questionable reputation whose eyewitness testimony becomes crucial to the investigation of [[george-colwan|George Colwan]]'s murder. Her own introduction of herself captures the instability of her social position:

> "My name is Arabella Calvert," said the other. "Miss, mistress, or widow, as you choose, for I have been all the three, and that not once nor twice only. Ay, and something beyond all these. But, as for you, you have never been anything!" (004-209)

Mrs. Logan's response confirms Calvert's notoriety — she is a person "to be heard of, not seen":

> "Ay, ay! and so you are Bell Calvert? Well, I thought so—I thought so," said Mrs. Logan; and, helping herself to a seat, she came and sat down close by the prisoner's knee. "So you are indeed Bell Calvert, so called once. Well, of all the world you are the woman whom I have longed and travailed the most to see. But you were invisible; a being to be heard of, not seen." (004-210)

Calvert's life had been one of serial victimization by the worst of men. She recounts her history to Mrs. Logan:

> The tale that she told of her misfortunes was of the most distressing nature, and was enough to stir up all the tender, as well as abhorrent feelings in the bosom of humanity. She had suffered every deprivation in fame, fortune, and person. She had been imprisoned; she had been scourged, and branded as an impostor; and all on account of her resolute and unmoving fidelity and truth to several of the very worst of men, every one of whom had abandoned her to utter destitution and shame. (004-283)

In particular, she was betrayed and abandoned by a criminal figure in York — a man whom the narrative invites readers to associate with the same demonic presence that haunts [[robert-wringhim|Robert Wringhim]]:

> "I had been abandoned in York by an artful and consummate fiend; and found guilty of being art and part concerned in the most heinous atrocities, and, in his place, suffered what I yet shudder to think of. I was banished the county, begged my way with my poor outcast child up to Edinburgh..." (004-286)

Despite her marginal social status, her eyewitness account carries weight precisely because it aligns with other evidence that more "respectable" witnesses have ignored or suppressed. Her character illustrates the novel's recurring theme that truth may come from unexpected sources.

## Role in the Narrative

Bell functions as an unlikely truth-teller: a person whom respectable society would dismiss, but whose testimony proves reliable. Her presence in the narrative challenges the reader to consider who is and is not a credible witness — a question that resonates with the novel's broader epistemological concerns about who can know the truth about [[robert-wringhim|Robert]]'s crimes.

Crucially, Calvert is the only eyewitness to the actual moment of [[george-colwan|George Colwan]]'s murder. She had been present at the scene of the duel because she was associated with the criminal world that intersected with [[gil-martin|Gil-Martin]]'s activities — her very marginality is what positions her to see what others could not.

The Sheriff who examined her found her credible despite her background, and her testimony was corroborated by another witness — a prisoner in the Tolbooth "whose discrimination being so well known as to be proverbial all over the land" (004-372). This independent corroboration underscores a central theme: truth may speak through unlikely vessels.

## Relationships

- **[[robert-wringhim|Robert Wringhim]]**: The man whose crimes she witnesses and against whom she testifies.
- **[[miss-logan|Miss Logan]]**: Fellow truth-seeker and investigative partner. Their alliance bridges class divisions in pursuit of justice.
- **[[george-colwan|George Colwan]]**: The murder victim whose death she witnessed.
- **[[gil-martin|Gil-Martin]]**: The mysterious companion whose presence at the murder links Calvert's testimony to the supernatural dimension of the novel.
- **The criminal associate in York**: The "artful and consummate fiend" (004-286) who abandoned her — a figure whose description resonates with Gil-Martin's demonic character.

## Key Appearances

- **Prison meeting with Mrs. Logan (004-209--004-225)**: Calvert and Mrs. Logan meet in the Tolbooth, where Calvert is being held. Mrs. Logan identifies her as the key to solving George Colwan's murder. The encounter establishes Calvert as a woman who has survived through all stations of life — "Miss, mistress, or widow."

- **The journey to Dalcastle (004-321--004-326)**: The two women travel together toward Dalcastle, disguised as country goodwives. At a village near the estate, Calvert spots Robert Wringhim walking "arm in arm with another young man" (004-323) — the mysterious companion who made the same sly signal she had seen on the night of the murder.

- **The confrontation on the path (004-344--004-367)**: Calvert and Mrs. Logan hide in the underwood and witness Robert Wringhim approaching. When Robert discovers Mrs. Logan and attacks her, Calvert reveals herself as the eyewitness to his crime:
  > "I saw thee do it with these eyes that now look thee in the face; ay, when his back was to thee, too, and while he was hotly engaged with thy friend," said Mrs. Calvert. (004-359)

  With no Gil-Martin present to assist Robert, the two women overpower and bind him. Calvert's description of Robert's demonic appearance is one of the novel's most chilling passages:

  > "I never in my life saw any human being," said Mrs. Calvert, "whom I thought so like a fiend. If a demon could inherit flesh and blood, that youth is precisely such a being as I could conceive that demon to be. The depth and the malignity of his eye is hideous. His breath is like the airs from a charnel house, and his flesh seems fading from his bones, as if the worm that never dies were gnawing it away already." (004-365)

- **Testimony to Lord Justice Clerk (004-371--004-372)**: The two women present their evidence to Sir Thomas Wallace of Craigie, the Lord Justice Clerk. He examines Calvert "very minutely" and finds her credible, leading to the order for Robert Wringhim's arrest.

- **The restless night (004-339--004-340)**: After the confrontation, Calvert and Mrs. Logan spend a hideous night haunted by the mystery of what and who they have seen — "their conversation was wholly about the dead, who seemed to be alive" (004-340).

## Critical Interpretation

Bell Calvert embodies the novel's democratizing impulse regarding truth and testimony. In a narrative dominated by theological elites (ministers, the "justified") and social elites (lairds, gentlemen), Calvert — a branded criminal, a woman of "ill repute," a survivor of imprisonment and scourging — becomes the conduit for the truth that society's respectable figures cannot or will not see.

Her name — "Arabella Calvert" — is significant. She shares a given name with [[arabella-colwan|Arabella (Rabina) Colwan]], the fanatical mother of Robert Wringhim. This doubling suggests a structural parallel: both women are damaged by the theological extremism that the novel critiques, but Calvert, unlike Lady Dalcastle, moves toward truth rather than delusion.

Her testimony also provides the only external corroboration of the supernatural dimension of the novel. Calvert sees what the rationalist Editor cannot — the demonic quality of Robert's appearance, the mysterious companion whose identity shifts, the "signals" between Robert and Gil-Martin. She is, paradoxically, the voice of empirical observation in a novel where the empirical and the supernatural converge.

Her function as witness also implicates the reader. By making a disreputable woman the bearer of crucial truth, Hogg challenges the reader to examine their own assumptions about credibility, social status, and the nature of evidence — questions that lie at the heart of the novel's epistemological project.
