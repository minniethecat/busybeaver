## Theological Definition

Original sin is the Christian doctrine that humanity's fallen condition is not merely the accumulation of individual transgressions but an inherited corruption of nature transmitted from Adam's first disobedience. In the Reformed tradition codified by the Westminster Confession, original sin means that "we are utterly indisposed, disabled, and made opposite to all good, and wholly inclined to all evil." Every human being is born sinful — not because they have committed actual sins, but because they inherit a nature that is constitutionally oriented away from God.

This doctrine is the anthropological foundation for the Calvinist system. Total depravity (the first point of TULIP) is simply original sin comprehensively described: the corruption affects every faculty, leaving no part of human nature capable of meritorious action before God. The doctrine of election follows directly: if all are born in sin and none can rectify their condition, salvation can only originate in God's sovereign choice.

## Original Sin in the Novel's Imagery

Hogg embeds the doctrine of original sin in the novel's metaphorical vocabulary. The most explicit reference occurs in the Editor's explanation that Robert Wringhim was removed from his mother "that the young stem of the corrupt trunk might also be taken from a world" (004-062). The "corrupt trunk" is Adam — the root of humanity from which all branches inherit corruption — and the "young stem" is Robert, who inherits not only his mother's theological fanaticism but, in the novel's Calvinist framework, the Adamic corruption that predestines him to spiritual ruin.

The imagery of corruption pervades both narratives. Wringhim describes his youth as "those days of depravity and corruption" (006-012), and his final despair is expressed through the language of bodily decay: "this frame of dross and corruption" (006-438). The body itself becomes the visible sign of original sin, its corruption mirroring the spiritual corruption that the doctrine describes.

## Narrative Implications

The doctrine of original sin shapes the novel's moral universe in two ways. First, it universalises sin: no character in the novel is morally pure. The Editor's narrative, for all its moral certainty, documents a world in which everyone — laird, lady, minister, magistrate — is implicated in failure. The doctrine provides the theological vocabulary for this universal corruption: sin is not aberrant but normative, the default condition of fallen humanity.

Second, the doctrine creates the theological problem that the novel's Antinomian solution purports to solve. If all are born in sin and none can contribute to their own salvation, then salvation must be entirely God's work — and if salvation is entirely God's work, then human actions are irrelevant to one's spiritual standing. This is the logic that the novel traces from orthodox premise (original sin) through orthodox conclusion (unconditional election) to Antinomian perversion (moral indifference).

## Critical Function

The novel's use of original sin is strategically ambiguous. On one level, Hogg deploys the doctrine straightforwardly as descriptive anthropology: the narrative world is one of universal corruption, and the doctrine names this condition accurately. On another level, the novel suggests that the Calvinist emphasis on original sin — when combined with the doctrine of unconditional election — produces a fatalism about moral effort that is socially destructive. The doctrine that was meant to humble becomes, in Wringhim's hands, a doctrine that excuses: if corruption is universal and inescapable, why resist it?
