# Blasphemy

## Definition

Blasphemy is the act of speaking against God, divine things, or sacred doctrines — in Christian theology, a grave sin. In the Reformed tradition, blasphemy against the Holy Spirit is identified as the "unpardonable sin" (Mark 3:29). The concept is central to the theological drama of Hogg's novel: characters repeatedly cross the line between bold doctrinal assertion and blasphemous self-exaltation, and the narrative itself raises the question of whether extreme Calvinist confidence in election constitutes a form of blasphemy.

## Theological Background

In 17th- and 18th-century Scottish Presbyterianism, blasphemy was both a theological category and a legal offense. The Westminster Confession (1647) treats blasphemy as a violation of the third commandment. The Antinomian controversy of the 17th century — in which certain sects claimed that the elect were freed from moral law — was frequently condemned as blasphemous by orthodox ministers, since it implied that God's grace licensed sin.

The novel draws on this historical context: the Antiburgher sect to which Robert Wringhim belongs was part of the broader Secession movement, and its more extreme predestinarian rhetoric was accused by opponents of tending toward blasphemy.

## In the Novel

Blasphemy pervades *Justified Sinner* at multiple levels:

- **Robert Wringhim's theology**: Robert's conviction that "a justified person can do no wrong" (004-042) is, from an orthodox perspective, inherently blasphemous — it appropriates divine prerogative to human action. His mother's minister-father teaches doctrines that Robert's interlocutors call "scarcely short of blasphemy" (006-137).

- **The Editor's judgment**: The Editor describes the Confessions manuscript as "a medley of lies and blasphemy" (006-405), ordering it to be destroyed — a judgment that reflects the orthodox reaction to Robert's self-justifying narrative.

- **Gil-Martin's rhetoric**: Robert's mysterious companion is described as "advancing blasphemies" with such subtlety that Robert felt "a secret dread" (006-081). Gil-Martin's skill at making blasphemy sound like piety is central to his seductive power.

- **The vocabulary of blasphemy**: Characters and narrators deploy the term across the novel's two narrative frames: in the Editor's Narrative, Gil-Martin is a "devilish-looking youth" (004-066); in the Confessions, Robert records that his enemies "turned [his work] into blasphemy and ridicule" (006-341). The meta-textual dimension — the charge that the novel itself is blasphemous — mirrors the accusation against its protagonist.

## Key Passages

> Robert's account of Gil-Martin's blasphemies: "He had such a way with him… that I felt a secret dread he was advancing blasphemies" (006-081).

> The Editor's condemnation of Robert's manuscript: "a medley of lies and blasphemy, and ordered the whole to be destroyed" (006-405).

> On the doctrines of Robert's sect: "his tenets scarcely short of blasphemy; they were such as I had never heard" (006-137).

> The accusation of blasphemy as social weapon: "thereby turning it into blasphemy and ridicule" (006-341).

## Critical Significance

The charge of blasphemy in *Justified Sinner* functions as a meta-critical device. The novel itself — with its portrayal of a justified sinner who commits murder without conscience — was considered blasphemous by some contemporary readers, who saw it as mocking Calvinist piety. Hogg anticipates this charge by embedding the accusation within the narrative: the Editor calls the Confessions blasphemous, and Robert's enemies call his doctrines blasphemous. The novel thus pre-emptively frames its own reception, daring the reader to decide whether the real blasphemy lies in Antinomian theology, in the novel's representation of it, or in the reader's own theological certainties.
