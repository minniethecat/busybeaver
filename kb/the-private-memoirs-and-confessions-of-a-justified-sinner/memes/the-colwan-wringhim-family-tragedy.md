# The Colwan-Wringhim Family Tragedy

## Thesis

The Colwan-Wringhim family is not merely the site of domestic dysfunction in James Hogg's *The Private Memoirs and Confessions of a Justified Sinner* — it is a theological allegory in which each family member embodies a distinct posture toward Calvinist predestinarian doctrine. The family's progressive dissolution — separation, estrangement, and ultimately fratricide — dramatizes how an extreme predestinarian theology, when severed from the moderating checks of natural affection and institutional authority, dissolves the very bonds of kinship it purports to sanctify. The domestic tragedy is the doctrine made flesh.

## The Marriage as Theological Mismatch

The union of the [[laird-of-dalcastle|Laird of Dalcastle]] and [[arabella-colwan|Arabella Colwan]] (nee Orde) is a mismatch from its inception, and Hogg wastes no time identifying the nature of the incompatibility. The Laird is described as "a rich man, or supposed to be so" who maintains the convivial, moderate religiosity of the old Scottish gentry (004-002). His new wife, by contrast, has been raised under the influence of the [[rev-robert-wringhim|Rev. Robert Wringhim]], a fierce proponent of an extreme Antinomian Calvinism. The marriage night itself becomes a theological battleground:

> "Sure, Mr. Colwan, you won't go to bed to-night, at such an important period of your life, without first saying prayers for yourself and me." (004-007)

The Laird's refusal to perform family worship — and the Lady's horror at this refusal — is not a dispute about piety but about authority. Who defines the spiritual life of the household? For Rabina, prayer is a performance of the elect identity, and her husband's refusal is tantamount to declaring himself reprobate. For the Laird, her demand represents the intrusion of a clerical authority — the Rev. Wringhim — into the privacy of the marriage chamber. The theological conflict is, from the first night, inseparable from the domestic one.

## The Separation as Doctrinal Schism

The [[the-separation-of-the-colwans|separation]] is swift and absolute. Within a day of the wedding, "the lady deserted her quarters, and returned to her father's house in Glasgow" (004-023). What appears as a domestic quarrel is, in Hogg's rendering, something more systematic: the physical division of the household enacts the theological division between the two parties. [[baillie-orde|Baillie Orde]] frames his response not in terms of wounded paternal feeling but as doctrinal vengeance: "I mean to be revenged on that villain Dalcastle" (004-027).

The separation produces an almost laboratory-like condition in which the two sons — [[george-colwan|George]], raised by the Laird, and [[robert-wringhim|Robert]], raised by Rabina and the Rev. Wringhim — can be formed in isolation from each other, each receiving a pure, undiluted version of his parent's theological orientation. The household at [[dalcastle]] becomes "the uppermost story" and "the lower parts," with prayers said in the former "against the Canaanitish woman" in the latter (004-038). The physical architecture of the house mirrors the theological architecture of the novel: a house divided against itself.

The Rev. Wringhim's role in this schism is decisive. He visits the separated Lady and provides the theological rationale that converts domestic grievance into cosmic warfare:

> "Evil, madam, may be either operative, or passive. To them it is an evil, but to us none. We have no more to do with the sins of the wicked and unconverted here than with those of an infidel Turk; for all earthly bonds and fellowships are absorbed and swallowed up in the holy community of the Reformed Church." (004-041)

This is the theological logic that dissolves the marriage: if earthly bonds are "absorbed and swallowed up" in the community of the elect, then a husband who is not of that community is not truly a husband at all. The doctrine of election does not merely permit the dissolution of natural bonds — it demands it.

## The Two Sons as Theological Foils

The novel's most structurally elegant feature is its pairing of the two sons as living embodiments of rival theological anthropologies. [[george-colwan|George Colwan]] represents natural morality — generous, sociable, hot-tempered but fundamentally decent, "a generous and kind-hearted youth; always ready to oblige" (004-062). His religion is conventional, unexamined, and integrated with ordinary social life. He does not claim election, and he does not need to; his moral instincts function without a theological superstructure.

[[robert-wringhim|Robert Wringhim]] represents the opposite: a morality entirely derived from theological premises, severed from natural feeling. He is "an acute boy, an excellent learner, had ardent and ungovernable passions, and, withal, a sternness of demeanour from which other boys shrunk" (004-063). Where George's moral life is instinctive and social, Robert's is doctrinal and interior. He does not refrain from cruelty because it feels wrong but because — and only when — his theological framework prohibits it.

The two brothers' first meeting, a tennis match in [[edinburgh|Edinburgh]], is a miniature of their entire relationship. George plays; Robert watches with "impertinent remarks" and "a derisive laugh" (004-066). George inhabits the world of action and social pleasure; Robert inhabits the world of judgment and theological surveillance. The tennis court becomes a theater of moral epistemology: how do you know what is right? By doing it and feeling it, or by deducing it from first principles?

The narrator of the Confessions, Robert himself, provides the sharpest statement of his own formation:

> "I was the second son of this unhappy marriage, and, ere ever I was born, my father according to the flesh disclaimed all relation or connection with me." (006-003)

The phrase "my father according to the flesh" is precise theological language — it distinguishes biological paternity from spiritual paternity, and in doing so, it declares where Robert's true allegiance lies. The Laird of Dalcastle may be his biological father, but the Rev. Wringhim is his father "according to the spirit." The family is broken not merely by physical separation but by a theological redefinition of kinship itself.

## The Rev. Wringhim as Spiritual and Biological Father

The [[rev-robert-wringhim|Rev. Robert Wringhim]] occupies the most disturbing position in the family structure. He is at once Rabina's spiritual director, the family's pastor, and — the novel heavily implies — Robert's biological father. This conflation of paternal and pastoral authority is the novel's deepest pathology. When the Rev. Wringhim speaks to Robert, as he does in the election ceremony, his words carry the weight of both offices:

> "In this frame of mind was I when my reverend father one morning arose from his seat, and, meeting me as I entered the room, he embraced me, and welcomed me into the community of the just upon earth." (006-074)

The phrase "my reverend father" contains the entire tragedy in miniature. Is he a father because he is a reverend? Or a reverend because he is a father? The ambiguity is deliberate and devastating. Robert has no access to a purely secular, purely biological father; his only frame for paternal authority is theological. When that theological authority tells him he is elect, the claim has the irresistible force of both a father's love and a pastor's spiritual certification.

The ceremony of Robert's "calling and election" is the pivot on which the family's fate turns. As Robert describes it, the Rev. Wringhim "laid his hands upon my head and blessed me, and then dedicated me to the Lord in the most awful and impressive manner — it was done with all the zeal and enthusiasm of a devotee to the true cause" (006-104). This is simultaneously a religious rite and a paternal performance — the father-pastor conferring on the son-parishioner an identity that will destroy them both.

## Gil-Martin as the "Third Brother"

If George is the son of nature and Robert is the son of doctrine, [[gil-martin|Gil-Martin]] is the son of pure theological logic — a principle that requires no family, no body, no fixed identity. He enters the narrative immediately after Robert's election is certified, as if he were summoned into being by the ceremony itself. His relationship to Robert is fraternal in form — he is "a friend," a companion, a co-conspirator — but demonic in substance.

Gil-Martin completes the destruction that the family structure initiated. The Rev. Wringhim alienated Robert from his biological father; Gil-Martin alienates Robert from everyone else, including his spiritual father. He is the "third brother" who makes the fratricide of the first two brothers possible — not by committing the murder himself, but by dissolving Robert's remaining scruples:

> "The deed would be alike meritorious, either way. But may we not wait for years before we find an opportunity? My advice is to challenge him, as privately as you will, and there cut him off." (006-245)

Where the Rev. Wringhim provided the theological permission (the elect can do no wrong), Gil-Martin provides the tactical implementation. The family's theological pathology needed only an agent to convert doctrine into deed, and Gil-Martin is that agent.

## The Family's Destruction as Theological Consequence

The novel's family arc moves through four stages, each corresponding to a theological escalation. First, the marriage's collapse into doctrinal incompatibility, which splits the household physically and spiritually. Second, the raising of the two sons in theological isolation, each absorbing an undiluted version of his parent's worldview. Third, the Rev. Wringhim's certification of Robert's election, which severs the last link between theology and moral accountability. And fourth, Gil-Martin's appearance, which converts internal theological conviction into external violence.

The [[the-murder-of-george-colwan|murder of George]] — brother killing brother — is the natural terminus of this arc. It is not an aberration but the fulfillment of the family's theological logic. When Robert, urged on by Gil-Martin, confronts his brother and kills him, he is not violating the family's founding principles but consummating them. The doctrine that dissolves earthly bonds finds its final expression in the dissolution of the most primal earthly bond of all.

Nor does the destruction end with George's death. [[arabella-colwan|Rabina]] dies in spiritual despair, having "rendered herself exceedingly obnoxious" even to her beloved son (006-301). The Rev. Wringhim is exposed as a fraud, his authority shattered. Robert, abandoned by Gil-Martin, dies by suicide — a death that, in the Calvinist framework he professed, would itself be proof of reprobation. The family is not merely damaged by its theology; it is annihilated by it.

The novel's most chilling revelation is that the family was never merely a family. It was always already a theological argument. The Colwan-Wringhim tragedy is Hogg's demonstration that a theology that claims to transcend nature does not transcend it — it destroys it.

## Conclusion

Hogg's rendering of the Colwan-Wringhim family is not a domestic novel interrupted by theological debate but a theological argument staged as domestic drama. Each family member is a position in a doctrinal dispute, and the family's annihilation is the dispute's resolution. The Laird's moderate rationalism cannot coexist with Rabina's zealotry; George's natural morality cannot survive Robert's antinomian election; the Rev. Wringhim's pastoral authority cannot be disentangled from his biological paternity; and Gil-Martin cannot appear until the theological ground has been fully prepared for him. The family tragedy is not a story about people who happen to have theological opinions — it is a story about theology that happens to have taken human form.
