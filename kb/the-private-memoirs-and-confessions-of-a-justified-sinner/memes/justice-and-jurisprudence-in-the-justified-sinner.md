# Justice and Jurisprudence in the Justified Sinner

## Thesis

James Hogg's *The Private Memoirs and Confessions of a Justified Sinner* is organized around two competing systems of justice — divine (predestinarian) and human (legal) — and the novel's most disquieting achievement is to demonstrate that both are radically inadequate. Divine justice, in the hands of the Antinomian elect, becomes a theological mask for murder. Human justice, embodied in the Scottish legal system, fails at every point to identify or punish the guilty. Between these two failed systems stands a third possibility — the marginal truth-telling of figures like [[bell-calvert|Bell Calvert]] and the empirical investigation of [[the-editor|the Editor]] — but these too prove incapable of delivering what either theology or law promises: justice that is both true and effective.

## Divine Justice as Murder's Alibi

The novel's most sustained critique of divine justice operates through the consciousness of [[robert-wringhim|Robert Wringhim]] himself. Robert does not merely commit murder and then seek theological justification after the fact; he understands the murder *as an act of divine justice* from the outset. His election is not merely a status that protects him from the consequences of sin — it is a commission to execute judgment:

> "From the moment, I conceived it decreed, not that I should be a minister of the gospel, but a champion of it, to cut off the enemies of the Lord from the face of the earth; and I rejoiced in the commission, finding it more congenial to my nature to be cutting sinners off with the sword than to be haranguing them from the pulpit, striving to produce an effect which God, by his act of absolute predestination, had foreclosed." (006-105)

This passage is the novel's most compressed statement of its central thesis about divine justice. Robert does not claim to be acting on his own authority — he claims to be executing a divine decree. The "act of absolute predestination" that has foreclosed the possibility of converting the reprobate simultaneously commissions the elect to kill them. Divine justice, in this logic, is not an alternative to violence but its authorization.

The theological mechanism that enables this transformation is the Antinomian doctrine that "a justified person can do no wrong" (004-042). If the elect are incapable of sin by definition, then any action performed by an elect person is, by that very fact, righteous. Divine justice becomes a closed logical system: the elect are the agents of divine justice, and whatever they do is just because they are elect. The circularity is perfect and impenetrable — there is no external criterion by which an elect person's actions could be judged, because the elect person's status supplies the criterion.

Robert deploys this logic explicitly in the context of the legal proceedings that follow his first confrontation with George:

> "Had I not been sensible that a justified person could do nothing wrong, I should not have been at my ease concerning the statement I had been induced to give on this occasion." (006-229)

The "statement" in question is perjured testimony. Robert is aware that he has lied under oath — but because he is justified, the lie cannot be wrong. The divine justice that licenses murder also licenses perjury, because both are actions performed by the elect. The system of divine justice, in its Antinomian perversion, is not merely inadequate to restrain evil — it actively produces it.

## Human Justice and Its Failures

If divine justice is perverted into a license for murder, the novel's representation of human justice — the Scottish legal system of the early eighteenth century — is scarcely more reassuring. The legal proceedings that follow George Colwan's death constitute a comprehensive catalogue of institutional failure.

The first failure is political. When George initially complains to the authorities about his brother's stalking, the sheriff — "a Whig" (004-175) — dismisses his complaint. Political allegiance determines judicial outcome: "It is well known how differently the people of the present day, in Scotland, view the cases of their own party-men and those of opposite political principles" (004-175). The law, which purports to be impartial, is revealed as an extension of political faction.

The second failure is procedural. The [[the-trial-of-robert-wringhim|trial of Robert Wringhim]] for the murder of his brother should be the moment at which human justice asserts itself against theological license. Instead, the trial demonstrates the legal system's incapacity. Robert and his spiritual father, as prosecutors of George, are confident of victory: "Our counsel assured us of a complete victory, and that banishment would be the mildest award of the law on the offender" (006-230). But the court acquits George — and then fails entirely to identify the true murderer. The verdict is not justice but its evasion: the guilty escape, the innocent are not vindicated, and the truth is buried under procedural ambiguity.

The third failure is epistemological. The legal system depends on testimony, but the novel systematically undermines the reliability of witnesses. The key witness to the murder — [[bell-calvert|Bell Calvert]] — is a convicted criminal, a former prostitute, a woman whose word carries no legal weight. Her testimony, though true, is dismissed because of who she is:

> "I could not, for I then fell under the power and guidance of a wretch who durst not for the soul of him be brought forward in the affair. And, what was worse, his evidence would have overborne mine, for he would have sworn that the man who called out and fought Colwan was the same he met leaving my apartment, and there was an end of it." (004-312)

The legal system is not designed to discover truth; it is designed to weigh the credibility of witnesses according to social status. The truth-teller who lacks social standing cannot be heard; the liar who possesses it cannot be refuted. Human justice, no less than divine justice, is a closed system that produces the outcomes its structure predetermines.

## Bell Calvert: The Marginal Truth-Teller

[[bell-calvert|Arabella Calvert]] is the novel's most interesting figure in relation to justice, precisely because she occupies a position entirely outside both systems. She is not elect — she is a criminal, a fallen woman, a social outcast. She is not a credible legal witness — her testimony is dismissed by the authorities. And yet she is, in a narrative sense, the most reliable witness the novel provides. She sees what happened; she tells the truth about it; and nobody in a position of authority believes her.

Bell Calvert's own life story, which she recounts to [[miss-logan|Mrs. Logan]], is itself a narrative of failed justice. She was "abandoned in York by an artful and consummate fiend" and "found guilty of being art and part concerned in the most heinous atrocities" while the true perpetrator — the man who exploited her — escaped (004-286). Her life is a case study in how legal systems punish the vulnerable and protect the powerful.

Yet Bell Calvert is not merely a victim of injustice. When she and Mrs. Logan capture Robert after George's murder, they subject him to a form of extra-legal justice — binding him, terrifying him, extracting a confession. Robert's response reveals the collapse of his theological confidence:

> "You are liars and witches! and creatures fitted from the beginning for eternal destruction. I'll have your bones and your blood sacrificed on your cursed altars! O Gil-Martin! Gil-Martin! Where art thou now? Here, here is the proper food for blessed vengeance!" (004-362)

The cry for Gil-Martin — the supernatural agent who has enabled all of Robert's crimes — goes unanswered. "There was no friend, no Gil-Martin there to hear or assist him: he was in the two women's mercy" (004-363). Divine justice, which Robert invoked to authorize murder, abandons him at the moment he needs it. Human justice, which should have protected George, is impotent. The only justice actually delivered in the novel is the rough justice of two women acting outside the law.

## The Editor's Investigation as a Third Model

[[the-editor|The Editor]] represents a third approach to justice — neither theological nor legal but empirical. His [[the-editors-investigation|investigation]] proceeds by collecting documents, interviewing witnesses, and weighing probabilities. He is, in effect, a private detective a century before the detective story was invented. His method is the method of the Scottish Enlightenment: gather evidence, test testimony, draw provisional conclusions.

The Editor's approach yields genuine results. He uncovers the manuscript that contains Robert's confession. He preserves the folk testimony about the strange figure accompanying Robert at the end. He provides the reader with the evidence that the legal system failed to gather. In a limited sense, his investigation succeeds where the trial failed: the reader of the novel knows, with reasonable certainty, that Robert killed George, and why.

But the Editor's justice is also radically incomplete. He cannot punish the guilty — Robert is already dead. He cannot vindicate the innocent — George's reputation remains clouded by the trial's ambiguous outcome. He cannot even achieve certainty about the central mystery of the narrative — the nature of Gil-Martin. The Editor's empiricism, for all its methodological superiority to both theological dogmatism and legal proceduralism, is finally unable to penetrate the mystery at the novel's heart.

This is the novel's deepest insight about justice: no human system — not theological, not legal, not empirical — can fully grasp or adequately respond to the reality of evil. Each system captures part of the truth and misses part; each delivers a partial justice that is also a partial injustice.

## Robert's Death: Escape or Judgment?

The novel's final ambiguity concerns the ultimate question of justice: does Robert face divine judgment for his crimes? His death by suicide — if it is suicide — would, in the Calvinist framework, be the final proof of his reprobation. Suicide is self-murder, the sin of despair, the unforgivable rejection of God's sovereignty over life and death. A man who dies by his own hand cannot be among the elect.

But the novel refuses to state definitively that Robert dies by suicide. The Editor reports that Robert's body was found, but the circumstances are ambiguous. Did he kill himself in despair after Gil-Martin abandoned him? Did Gil-Martin kill him? Did he simply die, his body worn out by the spiritual and physical ordeals he had endured? The novel withholds the answer, just as it withholds the answer to every other ultimate question.

This final ambiguity is the novel's last word on justice. Divine justice — the judgment of God on the soul of Robert Wringhim — is hidden from human eyes. We cannot know whether Robert is damned or saved, just as we cannot know whether Gil-Martin is demon or delusion, just as we cannot know whether our own assurance of election is genuine or self-deception. The epistemological limits that the novel establishes from its first page are maintained to its last. Justice, like truth, like election, like the supernatural — is finally inaccessible to human certainty.

## Conclusion

Hogg's novel offers no comfort to anyone who believes that justice — whether divine or human — is reliably achieved in this world. Divine justice, in the hands of the Antinomian elect, becomes indistinguishable from murder. Human justice, in the hands of partisan judges and status-conscious courts, becomes indistinguishable from politics. The marginal truth-tellers who see what happened cannot make themselves heard; the empirical investigator who reconstructs the facts cannot punish the guilty or vindicate the dead. The novel leaves us not with the satisfaction of justice done but with the unease of justice undone — and the recognition that this unease may be the most honest response to the reality of evil that fiction can offer.
