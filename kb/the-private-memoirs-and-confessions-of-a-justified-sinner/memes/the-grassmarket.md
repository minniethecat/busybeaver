# The Grassmarket

## Description

The Grassmarket is a broad, open space in Edinburgh's Old Town, lying in the shadow of Edinburgh Castle. In the 18th century, it was the city's principal marketplace and the site of public executions. Its name derives from its original use as a grazing area, but by the period of Hogg's novel, it was a bustling commercial center surrounded by taverns, shops, and tenements.

## In the Novel

The Grassmarket appears as part of the novel's Edinburgh topography — the public, commercial world through which the characters move. It represents the ordinary life of the city that continues unaware of the theological drama unfolding in its midst. Its historical association with public executions also provides a dark undertone to its appearances.

The novel references the Grass Market directly as a place of execution, when a condemned prisoner appeals for a witness to attend:

> "Then will you be so kind as to come to the Grass Market and see me put down?" (004-204)

It also appears as a site of everyday commerce and fashion, mentioned casually by a witness describing clothing:

> "I hae seen Mrs. Butler in the Grass Market wearing too: I rather think it is the same." (004-272)

## Significance

As a site of both commerce and execution, the Grassmarket embodies the duality that pervades the novel: the coexistence of ordinary life and extraordinary violence, the public and the hidden, the social and the unspeakable.
