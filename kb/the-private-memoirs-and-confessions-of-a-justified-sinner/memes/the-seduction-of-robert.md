# The Seduction of Robert Wringhim

## Description

Gil-Martin's seduction of Robert is the novel's central psychological drama — but it is a seduction of the mind, not the body. Gil-Martin offers Robert what he most craves: certainty of his election, freedom from moral anxiety, and a companion who understands him completely. The seduction works through theological flattery: Robert is special, one of the elect, beyond ordinary moral categories. Every step Robert takes toward crime is framed as a step toward spiritual maturity.

> "The things that he strove most to inculcate on my mind were the infallibility of the elect, and the preordination of all things that come to pass." (006-116)

> "We carried on our fellowship from day to day, and all the while I knew not who he was... I rejoiced in him, was proud of him, and soon could not live without him." (006-119)

> "Very well, you may call me Gil-Martin. It is not my Christian name; but it is a name which may serve your turn." (006-124)

## Narrative Significance

The seduction is the novel's most sophisticated exploration of how religious desire can be weaponized. Gil-Martin does not tempt Robert to sin *against* his theology but to sin *through* it — to become the fully realized Antinomian that his theology logically requires.
