# Christian Liberty

**Christian liberty** is a Reformed theological doctrine teaching that believers are freed from the ceremonial law of the Old Testament and from the condemnation of the moral law through Christ's atonement. In *The Private Memoirs and Confessions of a Justified Sinner*, the doctrine becomes a site of dangerous distortion, as Robert Wringhim and his spiritual mentor twist the concept into an Antinomian licence to sin without consequence.

## The Doctrine in Reformed Theology

In orthodox Reformed teaching, Christian liberty means that believers are no longer under the law as a covenant of works — their salvation does not depend on perfect obedience — but they remain under the law as a rule of life. The distinction is crucial: liberty from the law's condemnation, not liberty to transgress the law's precepts.

The Westminster Confession of Faith (1647) carefully defines Christian liberty as freedom from "the yoke of the ceremonial law," from "the guilt of sin," and from "the dominion of sin," while affirming that believers are "not thereby to Sin because Grace hath abounded."[^1]

## Antinomian Distortion in the Novel

### The Theology of Rev. Wringhim

The novel presents the Antinomian interpretation of Christian liberty through the figure of the Reverend Robert Wringhim, a "flaming predestinarian divine" (004-003) whose doctrines are described as "mightily overstrained and deformed" versions of Reformation principles. His teaching effectively collapses the distinction between liberty from and liberty to — suggesting that the elect, being predestined to salvation, may act with impunity.

This theological distortion is not merely abstract; it directly enables the moral catastrophe of the novel. The younger Wringhim (Robert) internalises his namesake's teaching and proceeds to commit murder under the conviction that his elect status guarantees his salvation regardless of his actions.

### Liberty as Licence

The crucial passage establishing the Antinomian abuse of Christian liberty appears early in the novel, in a dialogue that reveals the seductive logic of the distortion:

> "Who would not envy the liberty wherewith we are made free? Go on, then, and whosoever thou persuadest to the truth, remember yet to make it a free gift, and perfectly unconditional." (004-042)

The speaker (the mysterious stranger — likely the Devil or Robert's double) frames Christian liberty not as freedom *from* sin's dominion but as freedom *to* act without restraint. The "perfectly unconditional" nature of the gift becomes a warrant for antinomianism.

### The Fruits of Distorted Liberty

Robert Wringhim's later confession reveals the full consequence of this theological error. He describes his state as:

> "...a specimen of that liberty by which the chosen and elect are made free — and I rejoice to say it — in that I am free from all the shackles of the moral law." (006-320)

The progression is complete: liberty, properly understood as freedom from condemnation, has become a justification for licentiousness and murder. The "shackles of the moral law" are precisely what orthodox Reformed theology insisted Christian liberty did *not* remove.

## Relation to Other Doctrines

### Predestination

The distortion of Christian liberty depends on a particular understanding of predestination. If one's election is "absolute and eternal" (004-194), and if no sin can undo what God has decreed, then the moral law appears to lose its binding force. The novel traces this chain of reasoning to its horrific conclusion.

### Justification by Faith

The Reformation doctrine of *sola fide* — justification by faith alone — is similarly vulnerable to antinomian abuse. The novel's Rev. Wringhim preaches a version of this doctrine so extreme that it becomes "a stumbling block to many of his brethren" (004-003). If faith alone saves, and works contribute nothing, then works (including moral works) may seem irrelevant.

### Antinomianism

Christian liberty, in its distorted form, is the doctrinal engine of Antinomianism. The term itself means "against law," and the novel dramatises what happens when the rejection of the law's *condemning* power becomes a rejection of the law's *guiding* function. See [[antinomianism]] for the full theological framework.

## Narrative Function

Hogg uses the doctrine of Christian liberty as a structural device. The novel's two-part structure — the "Editor's Narrative" presenting external events, and the "Private Memoirs" revealing Robert's inner Antinomian logic — forces the reader to witness the gap between the sinner's self-justifying theology and the observable consequences of his actions. Christian liberty becomes the theological vocabulary through which Robert rationalises what the editor's narrative shows to be straightforward wickedness.

## Hogg's Theological Critique

James Hogg was writing within a Scottish Presbyterian context still shaped by the legacy of the Marrow Controversy (1718–1722) and ongoing debates about the relationship between grace, assurance, and moral obligation. By locating the Antinomian distortion of Christian liberty in the household of a "flaming predestinarian divine," Hogg was contributing to a live theological conversation about the dangers of hyper-Calvinism.

The novel's implicit argument is that the doctrine of Christian liberty, while orthodox in its proper formulation, contains within it a potential for abuse that requires constant vigilance — a vigilance that the institutional Church of Scotland, in Hogg's depiction, failed to exercise over figures like Rev. Wringhim.
