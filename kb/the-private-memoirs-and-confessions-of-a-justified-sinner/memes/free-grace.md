## Theological Definition

Free grace is the Reformation principle that salvation is God's unmerited gift, given without regard to human deserving. It is the positive corollary of the doctrine of justification by faith alone: if justification is not earned, it must be received as gift. The Apostle Paul's antithesis — "by grace you have been saved through faith, and this is not your own doing; it is the gift of God, not a result of works" (Ephesians 2:8–9) — is the biblical foundation, and the Reformers' insistence on *sola gratia* (grace alone) is its confessional expression.

In Reformed orthodoxy, free grace is not licence. The Westminster Confession carefully distinguishes between the free character of grace and its moral effects: those who receive grace are "renewed in the spirit of their mind" and enabled "to perform good works" — not as the ground of salvation but as its evidence. Grace is free in its origin but transformative in its effect.

## Wringhim's Distortion

Robert Wringhim invokes free grace in the novel's most concentrated statement of his theological position: "I depended entirely on the bounty of free grace, holding all the righteousness of man as filthy rags" (006-071). The language is orthodox — "filthy rags" alludes to Isaiah 64:6, a standard proof-text for the worthlessness of human righteousness — but the context subverts it. Wringhim is not humbly acknowledging his dependence on God's mercy; he is declaring his indifference to moral categories altogether.

The key word is "entirely." In orthodox Reformed theology, the dependence on free grace is entire with respect to the ground of salvation but not with respect to the conduct of the Christian life. Wringhim's "entirely" collapses this distinction: because grace is free, nothing else matters — not good works, not moral effort, not the avoidance of evil. The Antinomian move is to take a true premise (grace is free) and draw a false conclusion (therefore moral effort is irrelevant).

## The Rhetoric of "Bounty"

Wringhim's phrase "the bounty of free grace" is theologically significant. "Bounty" suggests abundance, generosity, overflowing supply — the language of gift rather than contract. The orthodox use of this language emphasises God's generosity in saving the undeserving; Wringhim's use emphasises God's indifference to human behaviour. The difference is subtle but decisive: orthodox free grace humbles the recipient; Antinomian free grace immunises the recipient against moral accountability.

The novel traces the consequences of this distortion. Because Wringhim believes that "the grace of repentance" has been "withheld from me" (006-070), he concludes that he is not responsible for his failure to repent. Free grace, in his understanding, covers the absence of repentance as it covers the presence of sin — and the result is a theology in which nothing the believer does or fails to do can affect their standing before God.

## Narrative Function

Free grace functions in the novel as the positive theological value that Antinomianism distorts into its opposite. In orthodox Calvinism, free grace produces gratitude, humility, and moral transformation. In Wringhim's Antinomianism, free grace produces presumption, arrogance, and murder. The novel does not reject the doctrine of free grace; it demonstrates what happens when the doctrine is severed from its moral context and made the sole article of theological conviction.
