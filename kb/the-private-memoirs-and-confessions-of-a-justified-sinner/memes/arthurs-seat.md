# Arthur's Seat

## Description

Arthur's Seat is the 251-meter (823-foot) volcanic hill that dominates Holyrood Park on the eastern edge of Edinburgh. Formed from the remains of an extinct volcano system that erupted approximately 350 million years ago, its dramatic geological formations — including the basalt cliffs of Salisbury Crags, the lion-shaped summit, and the deep glacial valleys — make it one of the most distinctive urban landscapes in Europe.

In the early 18th century, when Hogg's novel is set, Arthur's Seat lay at the margins of Edinburgh, a wild and largely uninhabited space beyond the eastern edge of the Old Town. It was a place where one could walk in solitude, take the air above the city's reek, or — as the novel demonstrates — encounter figures one would rather not meet.

## In the Narrative

Arthur's Seat appears in the novel as a site of liminal encounter — a place where the boundary between natural and supernatural, rational and inexplicable, becomes porous. It is the setting for one of the novel's most atmospheric and unsettling scenes: the early-morning excursion during which [[george-colwan|George Colwan]] encounters what appears to be the spectral double of his brother [[robert-wringhim|Robert Wringhim]].

The episode begins with George seeking escape from his mysterious persecutor:

> George was, from infancy, of a stirring active disposition and could not endure confinement; and, having been of late much restrained in his youthful exercises by this singular persecutor, he grew uneasy under such restraint, and, one morning, chancing to awaken very early, he arose to make an excursion to the top of Arthur's Seat, to breathe the breeze of the dawning, and see the sun arise out of the eastern ocean. The morning was calm and serene; and as he walked down the south back of the Canongate, towards the Palace, the haze was so close around him that he could not see the houses on the opposite side of the way. (004-138)

The atmospheric conditions create an extraordinary optical phenomenon — a "glory" or Brocken spectre, the circular rainbow that appears around a viewer's shadow when mist and sunlight converge:

> As he approached the swire at the head of the dell—that little delightful verge from which in one moment the eastern limits and shores of Lothian arise on the view—as he approached it, I say, and a little space from the height, he beheld, to his astonishment, a bright halo in the cloud of haze, that rose in a semicircle over his head like a pale rainbow. He was struck motionless at the view of the lovely vision... (004-139)

> George did admire this halo of glory, which still grew wider, and less defined, as he approached the surface, of the cloud. But, to his utter amazement and supreme delight, he found, on reaching the top of Arthur's Seat, that this sublunary rainbow, this terrestrial glory, was spread in its most vivid hues beneath his feet. Still he could not perceive the body of the sun, although the light behind him was dazzling; but the cloud of haze lying dense in that deep dell that separates the hill from the rocks of Salisbury, and the dull shadow of the hill mingling with that cloud made the dell a pit of darkness. (004-140)

The beauty of the scene — "this sublunary rainbow, this terrestrial glory" — gives way to terror when George perceives within the optical phenomenon a distorted, gigantic human figure:

> George conceived it to be a spirit. He could conceive it to be nothing else; and he took it for some horrid demon by which he was haunted, that had assumed the features of his brother in every lineament, but, in taking on itself the human form, had miscalculated dreadfully on the size, and presented itself thus to him in a blown-up, dilated frame of embodied air, exhaled from the caverns of death or the regions of devouring fire. (004-143)

The confrontation that follows is at once physical and metaphysical. George, the rational man of the world, seizes and interrogates the apparition:

> "Tell me then, Sir," said George, resolved to make the most of the wretch's terror—"tell me for what purpose it is that you haunt my steps? Tell me plainly, and instantly, else I will throw you from the verge of that precipice." (004-145)

The scene is remarkable for its ambiguity: is the figure on Arthur's Seat really Robert Wringhim, pursuing his brother? Is it a diabolical apparition taking Robert's form? Or is it a psychological projection — George's guilt and anxiety externalized by the peculiar optical conditions of the morning? The novel provides no resolution, and the scene on Arthur's Seat establishes the novel's central epistemological problem.

## Historical Context

Arthur's Seat has been a site of human significance for millennia. Archaeological evidence indicates occupation dating to the Iron Age, with the remains of a hill fort on the summit. In the medieval period, the hill formed part of the royal hunting park of Holyrood Abbey. By the time of Hogg's novel, it had become a popular destination for Edinburgh residents seeking recreation and fresh air — the very use to which George Colwan puts it.

The optical phenomenon Hogg describes — the "glory" or "spectre of the Brocken" — is a real atmospheric effect, well documented in scientific literature by Hogg's time. The Brocken spectre occurs when a viewer's shadow is cast onto cloud or mist below, surrounded by a rainbow-like halo. Hogg's use of this genuine natural phenomenon as the vehicle for a supernatural encounter exemplifies his technique of destabilizing the boundary between the explicable and the inexplicable.

James Hogg himself was deeply familiar with the landscape around Edinburgh, having walked extensively in the area during his years in the city. His description of the topography of Arthur's Seat — the "swire at the head of the dell," "the rocks of Salisbury," "that deep dell that separates the hill from the rocks" — is geographically precise and reflects firsthand knowledge.

## Key Scenes

- **The morning excursion (004-138--004-141)**: George Colwan's early-morning walk to Arthur's Seat to escape his persecutor. The atmospheric description of the haze, the "terrestrial glory," and the dramatic landscape establishes the setting for the supernatural encounter.

- **The apparition (004-142--004-145)**: Seated on "the pinnacle of the rocky precipice, a little within the top of the hill to the westward" (004-142), George encounters the spectral figure of his brother in the Brocken spectre. The confrontation on the precipice — with George threatening to throw the apparition "from the verge of that precipice" (004-145) — is one of the novel's most vivid scenes.

- **The aftermath (004-141)**: George relates the morning's events to his father and Mr. Adam Gordon, introducing into the narrative the question of what precisely was witnessed on the hill — a question the novel never resolves.
