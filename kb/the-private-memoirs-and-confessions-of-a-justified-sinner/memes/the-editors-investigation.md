# The Editor's Investigation

## Description

The Editor's investigation into the events surrounding George Colwan's murder and Robert Wringhim's life is the framing action of the novel. Working from letters, court records, eyewitness testimony, and Robert's discovered manuscript, the Editor reconstructs the tragedy in the mode of an Enlightenment rationalist — assembling evidence, weighing testimony, and presenting conclusions. The investigation produces the "Editor's Narrative" that comprises the first half of the novel.

> "I mentioned to him Hogg's letter, asking him if the statement was founded at all on truth. His answer was: 'I suppose so. For my part I never doubted the thing, having been told that there has been a deal of talking about it up in the Forest for some time past.'" (007-014)

> "It appears from tradition, as well as some parish registers still extant, that the lands of Dalcastle (or Dalchastel, as it is often spelled) were possessed by a family of the name of Colwan, about one hundred and fifty years ago." (004-001)

> "but of the matter furnished by the latter of these powerful monitors, I have no reason to complain: It has been handed down to the world in unlimited abundance; and I am certain that, in recording the hideous events which follow, I am only relating to the greater part of the inhabitants of at least four counties of Scotland matters of which they were before perfectly well informed." (004-001)

## Narrative Significance

The investigation is the novel's formal representation of the Enlightenment commitment to evidence-based truth. But the investigation's limits — its inability to fully explain Gil-Martin, to penetrate Robert's self-deception, to determine whether the events are natural or supernatural — suggest the limits of Enlightenment rationality itself.
