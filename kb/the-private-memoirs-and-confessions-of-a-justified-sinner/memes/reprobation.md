# Reprobation

## Definition

Reprobation is the Calvinist doctrine that God, in His eternal decree, has passed over certain persons — the reprobate — ordaining them to everlasting punishment. It is the logical corollary of unconditional election: if some are chosen for salvation, others are necessarily not chosen. Reformed theology distinguishes between "preterition" (God's passing over the non-elect) and "condemnation" (God's just punishment of them for their sins). In Hogg's novel, the fear of being among the reprobate — and the desperate need for assurance that one is not — drives the psychological dynamic of the protagonist. The doctrine also functions as a weapon of social classification: those labeled reprobate are treated as outside the pale of moral concern.

## Theological Background

The doctrine of reprobation was affirmed alongside election at the Synod of Dort (1618–1619), which taught that "not all, but some only, are elected, while others are passed by in the eternal decree." This "double predestination" — election to life and reprobation to death — was among the most controversial aspects of Calvinist theology. The Canons of Dort carefully distinguished between the divine decree (which is sovereign and inscrutable) and the actual damnation of the reprobate (which is just, because it is punishment for sin). Nevertheless, the pastoral implications were troubling: if God has decreed from eternity that certain persons will be damned, what motive remains for preaching the gospel to them, or for treating them with ordinary human compassion?

In Scotland, the pastoral problem was particularly acute. The Covenanting tradition's emphasis on personal religious experience created intense anxiety about one's spiritual state. Preachers who emphasized the terrors of reprobation could drive hearers to despair — the phenomenon known as "the fear of the unregenerate" — while those who emphasized assurance could foster Antinomian presumption. Hogg's novel dramatizes both dangers: the terror of reprobation that haunts Robert's early religious experience, and the Antinomian confidence that eventually replaces it.

## In the Novel

The fear of reprobation haunts the novel from its opening pages. Robert Wringhim's entire psychology is shaped by the terror that he might be among the reprobate — and his eventual conviction that he is among the elect is, in part, a reaction *against* this terror. The doctrine is wielded as a weapon of psychological control: those who are deemed reprobate are treated as outside the pale of moral concern.

Key moments where reprobation surfaces in the narrative:

- **The Terror of Reprobation**: Robert's earliest religious experiences are dominated by fear: "My heart quaked with terror when I thought of being still living in a state of reprobation, subjected to the awful issues of death, judgment, and eternal misery, by the slightest accident or casualty" (006-011). This fear is not assuaged but intensified by his religious practice — he prays "three times every day, and seven times on the Sabbath" but finds no relief. The very intensity of his fear suggests to him that he may be reprobate, and this suggestion in turn intensifies his fear. It is a psychological trap from which only the false assurance provided by his father and Gil-Martin can release him.

- **Using Reprobation as a Weapon**: John Barnet, a servant who questions Robert's theology, is described as "still living in a state of reprobation, subjected to the awful issue of such a dreadful dispensation" (006-011). This casual damnation of a fellow human being is characteristic of how the doctrine functions in the novel: it transforms theological conviction into social cruelty. Robert's classification of his own family is even starker: "They are reprobates, castaways, beings devoted to the Wicked One, and, like him, workers of every species of iniquity with greediness" (006-164). The language of reprobation provides a theological vocabulary for dehumanization.

- **Reprobation as Shield**: When challenged about his behavior, Robert dismisses criticism with "the language of reprobation cannot affect me" (006-046). This is a crucial moment in the novel's psychological analysis. Robert has so internalized the binary of elect/reprobate that any criticism of his conduct is automatically classified as "the language of reprobation" — the speech of the damned, which the elect can safely ignore. The doctrine thus functions as a perfect shield against moral accountability: if you are elect, you cannot be wrong; if you are reprobate, your criticism of the elect is by definition invalid.

- **Passive Before Reprobation**: Robert's theology leads him to depend "entirely on the bounty of free grace" regarding his "acceptance or reprobation" (006-071), a position that renders him passive in the face of his own moral choices. If election and reprobation are determined by an eternal decree, human action is irrelevant — and Robert draws the Antinomian conclusion that moral effort itself is a form of unbelief. The doctrine that was meant to humble human pride becomes, in Robert's hands, a license for moral indifference.

- **Reprobation and the Double**: The most disturbing application of the doctrine comes through Gil-Martin's manipulation. By assuring Robert that he is elect and that others are reprobate, Gil-Martin creates a world in which Robert's violence against the reprobate is not merely permitted but required. The murder of George Colwan is, in this theological framework, not a crime but the execution of a divine decree. Robert's classification of his brother as reprobate precedes and authorizes the act of killing — the doctrine constructs the victim before the knife finds him.

## Critical Significance

Reprobation is the shadow side of election, and Hogg uses it to expose the pastoral and psychological cruelty latent in a rigorously applied predestinarian system. The novel shows how easily the category of "reprobate" becomes a tool for dehumanizing others — those outside the elect are treated as objects of divine wrath rather than as fellow human beings. At the same time, the *fear* of being reprobate is what makes Robert so vulnerable to Gil-Martin's flattering assurance that he is among the elect. The doctrine thus functions both as a weapon against others and as a psychological trap for the believer himself.

Hogg's treatment of reprobation also anticipates modern critiques of binary thinking in religion and politics. The elect/reprobate binary creates a world in which moral complexity is impossible — every person is either saved or damned, every action is either righteous or sinful, and there is no middle ground. This binary is psychologically appealing (it eliminates ambiguity and uncertainty) but morally catastrophic (it eliminates the possibility of nuanced moral judgment). The novel's enduring relevance lies in its demonstration that the desire for moral clarity can produce moral monstrosity.

The novel also shows how the doctrine of reprobation functions as a mechanism of social control. The Reverend Wringhim uses it to maintain authority over his congregation: the threat of reprobation keeps the faithful in line, and the assurance of election rewards compliance. Robert inherits this mechanism and applies it to everyone he meets — his first question about any new acquaintance is whether they are elect or reprobate. The doctrine thus colonizes all of Robert's social relationships, transforming them from encounters between persons into exercises in theological classification.
