# The Antiburgher Church

## Overview

The Antiburgher Church was a strict Presbyterian denomination that emerged from the 18th-century Scottish Secession movement. In Hogg's novel, the Antiburgher sect — to which the Reverend Robert Wringhim belongs — serves as the institutional vehicle for the Antinomian theology that the narrative critiques. The Reverend Wringhim's church represents a form of Calvinism pushed to its most radical extreme: absolute predestination, the worthlessness of human righteousness, and the irrelevance of moral conduct to the state of one's election.

## Historical Context

The historical Antiburghers were one of two factions that emerged from the First Secession (1733) of the Church of Scotland over the issue of patronage. The "Burgher" vs. "Antiburgher" split of 1747 turned on whether Seceders could take the Burgess Oath in Scottish burghs — the Antiburghers refused, holding that such oaths implied recognition of the established Church of Scotland. The Antiburghers were generally the more rigorous and doctrinally exacting of the two factions.

Though the novel is set earlier (c. 1687–1705), Hogg appears to project the Antiburgher rigorism backward onto the novel's Calvinist characters. The historical inaccuracy is less important than the theological point: the Antiburgher label signals a form of Presbyterianism that prioritizes doctrinal purity above all other considerations.

## In the Novel

The Reverend Wringhim functions as the Antiburgher sect's representative in the narrative. He preaches "the standard doctrine of absolute predestination, which put the crown on the system of the Reformation" (004-032) and his influence over his congregation — particularly over Arabella Colwan — drives the domestic tragedy that opens the novel. The church's teaching that "every effort after righteousness only aggravates our offences" (006-011) provides the theological rationale for Robert's moral indifference.

The sect's physical presence in the novel is centered on "the Wringhim Chapel" — a private meeting-house where the Reverend preaches and where young Robert absorbs the doctrines that will license his crimes.

## Theological/Legal Significance

The Antiburgher Church in the novel represents a specific danger: religious institutions that teach doctrines which, when internalized by vulnerable individuals, eliminate the capacity for moral self-examination. The church is not portrayed as corrupt in the ordinary sense — the Reverend is sincerely devout. Rather, it is portrayed as theologically coherent but ethically catastrophic. The novel's critique is not of organized religion per se but of a particular theological system that, taken to its logical conclusion, makes murder a matter of divine will rather than human responsibility.
