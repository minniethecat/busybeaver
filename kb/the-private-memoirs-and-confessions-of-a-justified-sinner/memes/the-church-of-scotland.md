# The Church of Scotland

## Description

The Church of Scotland (the Kirk) was the established Presbyterian church of Scotland, formally re-established after the Glorious Revolution of 1688-89. It was governed by a hierarchy of church courts -- kirk sessions, presbyteries, synods, and the General Assembly -- and claimed spiritual jurisdiction over the entire Scottish nation. Its theology was Calvinist, but in the moderate form defined by the Westminster Confession of Faith (1646).

## In the Novel

The novel's religious drama takes place largely at the margins of the established Church. The Reverend Wringhim operates within the Church formally but holds views that push beyond its doctrinal boundaries into Antinomianism. The novel thus explores the tension between institutional orthodoxy and the radical enthusiasm that the Church could not fully contain.

> "You are, Sir, a presumptuous, self-conceited pedagogue, a stirrer up of strife and commotion in church, in state, in families, and communities." (004-056)

The Church's institutional discipline is also visible in the novel. The laird's refusal to attend the baptism of Robert means the boy has to "remain an alien from the visible church for a year and a day" (004-061), showing how seriously church membership was taken in this society. Wringhim is portrayed as one who has "levelled all the arrows of church censure with unsparing might" (004-092), yet himself operates at the doctrinal fringes.

> "he refused to acknowledge him in other respects. He neither would countenance the banquet nor take the baptismal vows on him in the child's name; of course, the poor boy had to live and remain an alien from the visible church for a year and a day" (004-061)

The kirk session, the local church court, appears as a point of reference: Robert's paternity is gossiped about in terms that "no oath before a kirk session was necessary to prove who was my dad" (006-022), reflecting the session's role in regulating moral and social order.

> "no oath before a kirk session was necessary to prove who was my dad, for that he had never seen a son so like a father as I was like mine." (006-022)

## Significance

The Church of Scotland represents institutional, confessional Calvinism -- the moderate center against which both the novel's Antinomians and its rationalists define themselves. Hogg's critique of Antinomianism is, implicitly, a defense of the established Church's moderate Calvinism against its enthusiastic distortion.
