# The Abandonment by Gil-Martin

## Summary

The abandonment is not a single moment but a progressive transformation of the relationship between [[robert-wringhim|Robert Wringhim]] and [[gil-martin|Gil-Martin]]. Throughout the early sections of Robert's Confessions, Gil-Martin functions as Robert's constant companion, guide, and tempter — a being who shares Robert's theological certainty and directs him toward increasingly violent acts in the service of "purifying" the Church. Over time, however, Robert's feelings toward his companion shift from admiration to unease, then dread, and finally desperate longing for escape. Gil-Martin's withdrawal — initially experienced as relief, then revealed as a calculated torment — marks the point at which the companion becomes the persecutor.

## Background

From their first meeting, Gil-Martin presented himself to Robert as a fellow believer in the doctrine of [[antinomianism|absolute predestination]] — one who understood that the elect are justified before God regardless of their earthly actions. Robert described him with wonder:

> I confess that I was greatly flattered by these compliments paid to my abilities by a youth of such superior qualifications; by one who, with a modesty and affability rare at his age, combined a height of genius and knowledge almost above human comprehension. (006-116)

Gil-Martin gave himself a name only reluctantly, and on terms that signal his elusiveness:

> "Oh, you must have a surname too, must you!" replied he. "Very well, you may call me Gil-Martin. It is not my Christian name; but it is a name which may serve your turn." (006-124)

Their relationship intensified into a fusion of identities — a merging that Robert initially welcomed but came to experience as a violation:

> "Our beings are amalgamated, as it were, and consociated in one, and never shall I depart from this country until I can carry you in triumph with me." (006-318)

Under Gil-Martin's influence, Robert participated in the murder of Mr. Blanchard, the attempted murder of his brother [[george-colwan|George Colwan]], and — as Gil-Martin later reveals — the murder of his own mother and a young woman whose affections he had seduced, though Robert has no conscious memory of these latter crimes.

## Narrative Accounts

### The Initial Withdrawal — Relief

The first sign of the abandonment comes when Robert awakens from a period of unconsciousness to discover that Gil-Martin has been absent. His servant Scrape informs him:

> "I dinna ken him, sir," said Scrape, "but have heard it said that the strange mysterious person that attended you, him that the maist part of folks countit uncanny, had gane awa wi' a Mr. Ringan o' Glasko last year, and had never returned." (006-308)

Robert's immediate response is relief — even gratitude:

> I thanked the Lord in my heart for this intelligence, hoping that the illustrious stranger had returned to his own land and people, and that I should thenceforth be rid of his controlling and appalling presence. (006-309)

This relief is compounded by the news that his mother is also gone:

> I took a cup of wine and water; put on my black clothes and walked out. For all the perplexity that surrounded me, I felt my spirits considerably buoyant. It appeared that I was rid of the two greatest bars to my happiness, by what agency I knew not. My mother, it seemed, was gone, who had become a grievous thorn in my side of late; and my great companion and counsellor, who tyrannized over every spontaneous movement of my heart, had likewise taken himself off. This last was an unspeakable relief... (006-314)

### The Return — Terror

Robert's relief is short-lived. Walking at the extremity of the Colwan wood, he encounters a figure whose approach sends a physical shock through his body:

> At the extremity of the Colwan wood, I perceived a figure approaching me with slow and dignified motion. The moment that I beheld it, my whole frame received a shock as if the ground on which I walked had sunk suddenly below me. Yet, at that moment, I knew not who it was; it was the air and motion of someone that I dreaded, and from whom I would gladly have escaped; but this I even had not power to attempt. (006-315)

Gil-Martin's greeting makes explicit the shift from companion to accuser:

> "So! Thou shudderest at my approach now, dost thou?" said he. "Is this all the gratitude that you deign for an attachment of which the annals of the world furnish no parallel? An attachment which has caused me to forego power and dominion, might, homage, conquest and adulation..." (006-316)

Robert's plea for release is direct and desperate:

> "Great and magnificent prince," said I humbly; "let me request of you to abandon a poor worthless wight to his own wayward fortune, and return to the dominion of your people. I am unworthy of the sacrifices you have made for my sake; and, after all your efforts, I do not feel that you have rendered either more virtuous or more happy. For the sake of that which is estimable in human nature, depart from me to your own home, before you render me a being either altogether above or below the rest of my fellow creatures." (006-317)

Gil-Martin refuses absolutely:

> "Sooner shall you make the mother abandon the child of her bosom; nay, sooner cause the shadow to relinquish the substance, than separate me from your side. Our beings are amalgamated, as it were, and consociated in one, and never shall I depart from this country until I can carry you in triumph with me." (006-318)

### The Explanation — Gil-Martin's Account of the Withdrawal

Gil-Martin explains his absence as a consequence of Robert's own actions — not an abandonment but a forcible separation:

> "Your crimes and your extravagances forced me from your side for a season," said he, "but now that I hope the day of grace is returned, I am again drawn towards you by an affection that has neither bounds nor interest..." (006-320)

He then reveals the crimes Robert allegedly committed during his period of oblivion:

> "You have certainly been left to yourself for a season," returned he, "having gone on rather like a person in a delirium than a Christian in his sober sense. You are accused of having made away with your mother privately; as also of the death of a beautiful young lady, whose affections you had seduced." (006-322)

Robert has no memory of these acts, and the novel leaves fundamentally ambiguous whether Gil-Martin committed them in Robert's form, or whether Robert committed them in a state for which Gil-Martin was responsible.

### The Final Phase — Persecution

What began as companionship, passed through abandonment and return, culminates in relentless persecution. Robert resolves to flee:

> I resolved to shake him off, cost what it would, even though I should be reduced to beg my bread in a foreign land. To do it at home was impossible, as he held my life in his hands, to sell it whenever he had a mind; and, besides, his ascendancy over me was as complete as that of a huntsman over his dog. (006-353)

But Gil-Martin anticipates every move:

> But, if I had taken my measures to abscond and fly from my native place, in order to free myself of this tormenting, intolerant, and bloody reformer, he had likewise taken his to expel me, or throw me into the hands of justice. (006-354)

The pursuit becomes a supernatural harassment that drives Robert toward suicide — the fate Gil-Martin has clearly intended all along. Robert's final journal entries record the persecution in increasingly desperate terms:

> August 3, 1712.—This morning the hind has brought me word from Redesdale, whither he had been for coals, that a stranger gentleman had been traversing that country, making the most earnest inquiries after me, or one of the same appearance; and, from the description that he brought of this stranger, I could easily perceive who it was. Rejoicing that my tormentor has lost traces of me for once... (006-443)

> My last hour is arrived: I see my tormentor once more approaching me in this wild. Oh, that the earth would swallow me up, or the hill fall and cover me! Farewell for ever! (006-449)

> September 18, 1712.—Still am I living, though liker to a vision than a human being; but this is my last day of mortal existence. Unable to resist any longer, I pledged myself to my devoted friend that on this day we should die together, and trust to the charity of the children of men for a grave. (006-453)

### The Editor's Perspective

The Editor's Narrative corroborates the abandonment from the outside. When Bell Calvert and Mrs. Logan confront Robert on the path to Dalcastle, he calls out for his absent companion — and receives no answer:

> "You are liars and witches!" said he, foaming with rage, "and creatures fitted from the beginning for eternal destruction. I'll have your bones and your blood sacrificed on your cursed altars! O Gil-Martin! Gil-Martin! Where art thou now? Here, here is the proper food for blessed vengeance! Hilloa!" (004-362)

> There was no friend, no Gil-Martin there to hear or assist him: he was in the two women's mercy, but they used it with moderation. (004-363)

This external view confirms the pattern: Gil-Martin's presence and absence are not random but strategic. He is present when his presence serves his purposes; he absents himself when Robert's suffering is the objective.

## Aftermath

The abandonment initiates the final movement of the novel. Robert, stripped of his companion's confirming presence and hunted by both human justice and supernatural persecution, flees across the Scottish Borders. His journey — through weavers' cottages, farm-houses, and wilderness — is a descent into paranoia and despair. He is, by the end, "liker to a vision than a human being" (006-453).

The abandonment also sets up the novel's most radical undecidability. Has Gil-Martin abandoned Robert, or has Robert's psyche fractured, leaving him pursued by a projection of his own guilt? The novel provides evidence for both readings and refuses to resolve them.

## Thematic Significance

The abandonment crystallizes the novel's most unsettling theological proposition: what if the assurance of election is indistinguishable from demonic deception? Gil-Martin's withdrawal leaves Robert in a state of radical doubt — the very condition that Calvinist doctrine of assurance was designed to prevent. The event is thus not merely a plot turn but a theological crisis rendered as psychological horror.

The pattern of the abandonment — companionship, withdrawal, return as persecution — also mirrors the structure of Calvinist anxiety about assurance. The believer seeks signs of election; the signs prove ambiguous; the search for certainty yields only deeper uncertainty. Gil-Martin's movement from friend to tormentor enacts, in Gothic form, the psychological trajectory of a theology that cannot deliver the assurance it promises.

The scene in which Robert pleads to be abandoned — "let me request of you to abandon a poor worthless wight to his own wayward fortune" (006-317) — inverts the expected relationship between the human and the demonic. Robert begs for the very abandonment that Gil-Martin refuses to grant, because presence has become more terrifying than absence. This inversion is characteristic of Hogg's theological critique: the doctrines meant to secure the believer become the instruments of his destruction.
