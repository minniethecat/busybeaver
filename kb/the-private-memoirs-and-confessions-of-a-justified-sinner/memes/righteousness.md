# Righteousness

## Definition

In Reformed soteriology, righteousness has a double reference: the righteousness of Christ (His perfect obedience to the law) is *imputed* to the believer in justification, while *actual* righteousness (sanctification) is progressively produced in the believer by the Holy Spirit. The crucial distinction is between the forensic declaration "you are righteous" (justification) and the transformative process "you are being made righteous" (sanctification). Hogg's novel dramatizes what happens when this distinction collapses.

## Theological Background

The doctrine of imputed righteousness was the material principle of the Reformation. Luther called it the "sweet exchange" — Christ takes the sinner's sin, and the sinner receives Christ's righteousness. Calvin systematized this in his doctrine of "double grace": justification (imputed righteousness) and sanctification (imparted righteousness) are distinct but inseparable. The Antinomian error was to affirm the former while denying the necessity of the latter. In Scotland, this error was associated with certain extreme Covenanting preachers who so emphasized the freeness of grace that they downplayed the necessity of holy living.

## In the Novel

Righteousness in the novel is almost always ironic. Robert speaks of "holding all the righteousness of man" while describing his dependence on grace (006-071) — a theological incoherence typical of his self-deception. The novel repeatedly exposes the gap between Robert's *claimed* righteousness (as one of the elect) and his *actual* behavior (murder, deceit, cruelty).

The novel's most pointed critique comes through Gil-Martin, who inculcates in Robert the belief that the elect possess a righteousness that places them above ordinary moral categories. "The righteousness of the elect," Gil-Martin implies, is not a righteousness that can be judged by outward actions. This is, of course, the Antinomian heresy in its purest form — and the novel traces its consequences to their logical and horrifying conclusion.

## Key Passages

> "...holding all the righteousness of man to be as filthy rags..." (006-071)

> "...the election of grace; and then I added: 'Now, friends, you have your faith...'" (006-180)

> "...preaching up redemption by grace, preordination, and eternal predestination..." (006-367)

## Critical Significance

Hogg's treatment of righteousness exposes the central paradox of Antinomian theology: the more one claims Christ's imputed righteousness, the less one may exhibit actual righteousness. The novel thus functions as a powerful vindication of the Reformed insistence that justification and sanctification, while distinct, must never be separated. Hogg shows — with psychological acuity — that a "righteousness" that produces no righteous behavior is not the righteousness of Christ at all, but a theological fiction serving human corruption.
