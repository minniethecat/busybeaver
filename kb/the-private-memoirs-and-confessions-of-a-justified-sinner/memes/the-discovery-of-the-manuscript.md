# The Discovery of the Manuscript

## Description

After Robert Wringhim's death in 1712, a manuscript is discovered among his effects — his "Private Memoirs and Confessions," written in his own hand. This manuscript is the source of the second half of Hogg's novel. The Editor presents it to the public with only minor editorial interventions, allowing Robert's self-justifying narrative to stand as its own refutation.

> "With very little trouble, save that of a thorough drying, I unrolled it all with ease, and found the very tract which I have here ventured to lay before the public, part of it in small bad print, and the remainder in manuscript." (007-026)

> "The title page is written and is as follows:" (007-026)

> "among such a confusion, we had hard work to find out all his pockets... we found, to our great astonishment, that it contained a printed pamphlet." (007-024)

## Narrative Significance

The discovery of the manuscript is the foundational event of the novel's frame structure. It explains how the Editor came to possess Robert's confession and justifies the novel's two-part form. The manuscript itself — a physical object, handwritten, discovered among a dead man's effects — functions as a symbol of the material evidence that the Editor's rationalist worldview depends on, even as its *content* challenges that worldview.
