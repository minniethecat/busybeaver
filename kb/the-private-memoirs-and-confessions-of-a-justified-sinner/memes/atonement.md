# Atonement

## Definition

Atonement is the Christian doctrine that Christ's death on the cross reconciles sinful humanity to God. In Reformed theology, this doctrine took the specific form of "limited" or "definite" atonement — the teaching that Christ's sacrifice was intended only for the elect, not for all humanity. This is one of the "five points" of Calvinism (the "L" in TULIP) and is a crucial theological premise of the world Hogg's novel inhabits and critiques.

## Theological Background

The doctrine of limited atonement was formulated at the Synod of Dort (1618–1619) in response to the Arminian contention that Christ died for all people. The Dort canons asserted that "the saving efficacy of the most holy death of Christ extends to all the elect" — implying, by negation, that it does not extend to the reprobate. This teaching created a sharp pastoral problem: if Christ died only for the elect, how can an individual know whether the atonement covers them? The search for assurance of this coverage became a central preoccupation of Calvinist piety.

## In the Novel

Hogg's novel shows the psychological consequences of limited atonement pushed to its extreme. Robert Wringhim's religious education teaches him that Christ's blood is effective only for a predetermined elect — and that he, Robert, is among that number. This conviction renders the atonement not a comfort but a weapon: those outside the elect are beyond its reach, and Robert owes them no moral consideration because "the blood of the atonement can never, never reach" them (006-020).

The novel's critique is embodied in a single devastating line about Robert's (adoptive) father's prayer: "he prayed… for the elect, and for no one else; and he declared that to put up prayers for those who were not elected, was to put discredit on the great atonement, in which you trust" (006-117). Here, limited atonement becomes logically self-sealing: praying for the non-elect would actually *dishonor* Christ's sacrifice. The doctrine, meant to magnify grace, has become a reason to withhold compassion.

## Key Passages

> The exclusivity of the atonement: "the blood of the atonement can never, never reach [them]" (006-020).

> Prayer as discredit to atonement: "to put up prayers for those who were not elected, was to put discredit on the great atonement, in which you trust" (006-117).

## Critical Significance

The treatment of atonement in *Justified Sinner* exemplifies Hogg's method of theological critique through dramatic embodiment. Rather than arguing against limited atonement in abstract terms, he shows what it looks like when a character fully internalizes it — a world where the cross divides rather than unites, where Christ's blood builds walls instead of breaking them down. This is the novel's most radical theological move: it suggests that certain ways of exalting grace actually destroy charity.
