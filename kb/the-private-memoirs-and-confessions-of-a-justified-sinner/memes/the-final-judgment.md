# The Final Judgment

## Description

The novel never depicts Robert Wringhim facing divine judgment — it does not need to. The judgment is performed by the narrative structure itself: Robert's "Confessions," presented without commentary by the Editor, function as a self-indictment. The reader, not God, is the judge — and the evidence is Robert's own theological self-justification. This narrative judgment is more devastating than any explicit damnation could be.

> "My heart quaked with terror when I thought of being still living in a state of reprobation, subjected to the awful issues of death, judgment, and eternal misery, by the slightest accident or casualty." (006-011)

> "I thought of plunging a soul so abruptly into Hell, or, at the best, sending it to hover on the confines of that burning abyss — of its appearance at the bar of the Almighty to receive its sentence. And then I thought: 'Will there not be a sentence pronounced against me there, by a jury of the just made perfect, and written down in the registers of Heaven?'" (006-224)

> "if anyone shall ever take the trouble to read over these confessions, such a one will judge for himself." (006-140)

## Narrative Significance

The "final judgment" is the novel's supreme theological irony. Robert spent his life certain of his election, but his own words — read honestly — reveal a man whose assurance was delusion and whose faith was presumption. The novel's structure thus performs what its theology implies: the elect are known by their fruits, and Robert's fruits are murder.
