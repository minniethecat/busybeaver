# The Law Courts

## Description

The Edinburgh law courts in the early 18th century were among the most important institutions of Scottish public life. Scotland retained its own legal system after the 1707 Union with England, and the Edinburgh courts were the venue for the most significant criminal and civil cases in the kingdom.

## In the Novel

The law courts appear at a crucial juncture: after George Colwan's murder, Robert is tried — and acquitted.

> "Finally it turned out that George was honourably acquitted, and young Wringhim bound over to keep the peace, with heavy penalties and securities." (004-179)

The failure of the legal system to convict a guilty man is one of the novel's most disturbing implications: the institutions of rational justice are powerless against a criminal who genuinely believes in his own innocence.

> "I heard the whole trial, and so did Gil-Martin; but we left the journeyman preacher to his fate, and from that time forth I have had no faith in the justice of criminal trials. If once a man is prejudiced on one side, he will swear anything in support of such prejudice." (006-156)

The law can judge actions but cannot penetrate the theological self-deception that enables those actions.

## Significance

The courts represent the failure of institutional Enlightenment: reason, evidence, and legal procedure prove inadequate to the task of seeing through Robert's Antinomian self-justification.

> "Upon the whole, there was a division in the court, but a majority decided it. Drummond was pronounced guilty of the murder; outlawed for not appearing, and a high reward offered for his apprehension." (004-191)

This is one of the novel's most radical critiques — not of law per se, but of the limits of rational institutions in the face of religious conviction.
