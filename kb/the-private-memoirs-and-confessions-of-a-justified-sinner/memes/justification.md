# Justification

## Definition

Justification is the Reformation doctrine that sinners are declared righteous before God not on the basis of their own merits but solely through faith in Christ, whose righteousness is imputed to them. The Westminster Shorter Catechism (1647) defines it as "an act of God's free grace, wherein he pardoneth all our sins, and accepteth us as righteous in His sight, only for the righteousness of Christ imputed to us, and received by faith alone." The distinction between justification (being declared righteous) and sanctification (being made righteous) is fundamental to Reformed soteriology: the former is an instantaneous forensic act, the latter a progressive transformation.

The novel's theological drama unfolds precisely at the point where this distinction collapses. The Antinomian teaching that Robert Wringhim receives from his adoptive father reduces justification to a permanent status that renders all subsequent actions morally irrelevant: "a justified person can do no wrong" (004-042). This is not the orthodox Reformed doctrine but its Antinomian perversion — a perversion that the novel traces to its logical and lethal conclusion. The weaver's accusation that Robert possesses only "your justification by faith an' awthegither" (006-045) captures the novel's central critique: justification without sanctification is a theological fiction that enables moral catastrophe.

## Theological Background

The doctrine of justification by faith alone (*sola fide*) was the "article of the standing or falling church" (*articulus stantis et cadentis ecclesiae*) for the Protestant Reformers. Luther called it "the doctrine by which the church stands or falls." In the Reformed tradition, justification is distinguished from sanctification but never separated from it: those whom God justifies He also sanctifies. The Westminster Confession (Chapter 11) carefully guards against the Antinomian inference: "They who are effectually called and regenerated... are further sanctified, really and personally, through the virtue of Christ's death and resurrection."

The Antinomian Controversy of the 17th century tested this careful balance. Antinomian preachers such as Tobias Crisp and John Saltmarsh taught that the elect are justified from eternity — before they are born, before they believe, before they commit any sin — and that their subsequent sins cannot affect their justified status. This teaching, which effectively collapsed sanctification into justification, was condemned by the Westminster Assembly and by Scottish church courts, but it persisted in various forms through the 18th century. Hogg's novel participates in this long-running theological controversy by dramatizing, in narrative form, the moral consequences of Antinomian teaching.

## In the Novel

### The Catechism of Error

The novel embeds its theological argument in a series of doctrinal exchanges between Robert and the Reverend Wringhim. The most significant concerns "justification by grace" (006-001) and the claim that "a justified person could do nothing wrong" (006-229). The Reverend's teaching elides the distinction between justification (God's declarative act) and the moral law (God's prescriptive will), producing a theology in which the justified person is exempt from moral accountability. Lady Dalcastle herself articulates the seductive logic: "How delightful to think that a justified person can do no wrong! Who would not envy the liberty wherewith we are made free?" (004-042). The irony is that this "liberty" will produce servitude to sin.

The Reverend Wringhim's preaching systematically undermines moral effort by teaching that "every effort after righteousness only aggravates our offences" (006-011). If righteousness is imputed rather than achieved, then efforts to achieve it are not merely futile but actually sinful — an inference that, while logically coherent within a certain reading of Reformed theology, is pastorally devastating. Robert internalizes this teaching completely.

### Justification Without Sanctification

The novel's most sustained theological critique concerns the separation of justification from sanctification. In orthodox Reformed theology, the two are distinct but inseparable: those whom God justifies, He also sanctifies. The Antinomian teaching depicted in the novel severs this connection, producing "justified" persons who exhibit no evidence of moral transformation. The Editor's narrative supplies the visible evidence of this failure: Wringhim is "justified, sanctified, and received into a communion" (006-163) by his own account, yet his actions are indistinguishable from those of the reprobate.

Robert internalises this teaching so completely that it becomes the hermeneutic through which he interprets every action. When he murders his brother, he "conceived myself completely justified on that score" (006-228). The word "justified" here operates on two levels: the colloquial sense of "having adequate reason" and the theological sense of "declared righteous by God." The collapse of this distinction is the novel's diagnosis of Antinomian error: theological justification has been confused with moral justification, and the result is murder performed in good conscience.

Robert also reports that "I was assured of my justification in the sight of God before the throne of grace" (006-352), a statement that occurs in the context of his growing terror of Gil-Martin. The juxtaposition of claimed justification with existential dread is the novel's most compressed statement of its thesis: Antinomian assurance is compatible with — and may even reinforce — the deepest spiritual and moral disintegration.

### The Social Consequences

The doctrine of justification, when perverted into Antinomianism, has social as well as theological consequences. The weaver's accusation — "your justification by faith an' awthegither" (006-045) — captures the popular perception of Antinomianism as a doctrine that makes religious profession a cover for moral licence. The weaver's full statement is devastating: "Here stands the poor sinner, John Barnet, your beadle an' servantman, wha wadna change chances wi' you in the neist world, nor consciences in this, for ten times a' that you possess" (006-045). The accusation is that Robert's justification is a possession that substitutes for conscience — it allows him to do without the moral self-awareness that even "the poor sinner" retains.

The novel dramatises this perception: Wringhim's certainty of his own justification becomes the ground of his cruelty, his deceptions, and ultimately his murders. The Reverend Wringhim's initial accusation against the Laird of Dalcastle — "You are, Sir, a presumptuous, self-conceited pedagogue, a stirrer up of strife and commotion in church, in state, in families, and communities" (004-056) — is an exercise in projection that the novel's unfolding narrative will redirect onto the accuser himself.

## Critical Significance

Justification functions in the novel not merely as a theological concept but as a narrative mechanism. The gap between Wringhim's self-perception ("a justified person") and the reader's perception (a murderer) generates the ironic tension that sustains the memoir. Every time Wringhim invokes his justification, the reader recognises the disjunction between theological status and moral reality — and in that recognition, the novel performs its critique of Antinomian theology.

Hogg's treatment of justification is remarkable for its psychological realism. Robert is not a hypocrite in the conventional sense — he genuinely believes what he says about his justified status. The novel's horror is that sincere belief in a perverted doctrine is more dangerous than cynical manipulation of a true one. By making Robert a convinced Antinomian rather than a knowing fraud, Hogg raises the stakes: the problem is not individual bad faith but a theological system that makes self-deception structurally inevitable.

The novel also anticipates modern concerns about the relationship between belief and behavior. Robert's justification functions as what psychologists would now call a "moral disengagement mechanism" — a belief that allows him to commit harmful actions without experiencing the moral emotions (guilt, shame, remorse) that would normally inhibit such behavior. The novel is thus not merely a period piece about a 17th-century theological controversy but a timeless exploration of how conviction can become a mechanism of self-exoneration.
