# Scotland

## Description

Early 18th-century Scotland was a nation defined by religious fracture, political union with England (1707), and the persistence of older clan-based social structures alongside emerging Enlightenment rationality. The landscape itself — from the closes of Edinburgh's Old Town to the wild Border hills to the Highland mountains — provided a geography of extremes that Hogg exploits to mirror the psychological states of his characters.

## In the Narrative

Scotland is not merely a backdrop but an active participant in the narrative. The novel's geography is precise and meaningful: Edinburgh represents the world of law, learning, and rational inquiry; the Border estates like Dalcastle embody the landed gentry's uneasy negotiation between tradition and modernity; and the wilder regions into which Robert Wringhim flees in his final days represent the dissolution of all social and moral order.

The novel's opening establishes the national scope immediately — the Dalcastle estate is situated in the context of post-Union Scotland's landed society. The marriage that begins the drama is brokered across the Edinburgh-Glasgow axis, connecting the two poles of Lowland urban Scotland:

> "...the daughter of a Baillie Orde, of Glasgow..." (004-002)

The narrative repeatedly invokes the country as a whole in moments of crisis — during the alarm that follows Wringhim's crimes, all of Scotland seems mobilized:

> "...the country was roused from one end to the other..." (006-157)

The religious character of Scotland is established from the opening, where the narrator notes the powerful hold of Reformation principles on the Scottish people:

> "It is well known that the Reformation principles had long before that time taken a powerful hold of the hearts and affections of the people of Scotland, although the feeling was by no means general, or in equal degrees..." (004-002)

This uneven religious landscape — some fervently Reformed, others indifferent or conforming outwardly — creates the conditions for the novel's theological conflicts.

## Historical Context

Scotland in the late 17th and early 18th centuries was a nation undergoing profound transformation. The Glorious Revolution of 1688–89 had re-established Presbyterian church government, but the Revolution Settlement of 1690 left deep divisions: Episcopalians resented their displacement, while radical Covenanters (the "Society people" from whom the Antiburghers later emerged) regarded the settlement as a betrayal of the Covenants. The Union of 1707, which merged the Scottish and English parliaments, created new political alignments and deepened existing fissures between those who embraced the new British state and those who regarded it with suspicion.

The Scotland of Hogg's novel is thus a patchwork of competing loyalties — to kirk and king, to Covenant and Crown, to clan and commerce. The Border region, where Dalcastle is situated, was a zone of particular complexity: neither fully Lowland nor Highland, with its own traditions of riding clans and cross-border lawlessness. Edinburgh, the capital, was the seat of the Court of Session, the General Assembly, and the University — institutions that embodied the rational, ordered Scotland of the Enlightenment. But the wilder regions — the Highlands and the remote Border hills — still belonged to an older Scotland of folk belief, supernatural encounter, and extra-legal violence.

The year 1715, mentioned in the novel's closing as "the memorable year for Scotland" (004-374), was the year of the first Jacobite Rising, when supporters of the exiled Stuart dynasty attempted to overthrow the Hanoverian succession. This rebellion, though ultimately defeated, exposed the fragility of the post-Union settlement and the enduring power of older loyalties. Hogg, writing in 1824, was acutely aware of the 1715 and its legacy, and the novel's pervasive atmosphere of hidden violence and divided allegiance reflects this historical memory.

## Key Scenes

**The opening at Dalcastle (004-001–004-002)**: The novel begins by establishing the Scottish setting with documentary precision — parish registers, local traditions, and the social landscape of the Borders — before introducing the religious division that will drive the plot.

**The country roused (006-157)**: After the murder of the Glasgow preacher, the narrator reports that "The alarm in and about Glasgow was prodigious. The country was divided into two political parties, the court and the country party..." This passage reveals how local violence was immediately interpreted through Scotland's polarized political framework.

**Wringhim's flight across the Tweed (006-426)**: Robert's crossing of the Tweed into England marks his departure from the land that has formed him and his entry into a liminal zone where his persecutor's power is undiminished. The border is not just a geographical line but a symbolic threshold between order and dissolution.

**The grave on the Faw-Law (007-020)**: The novel's final discovery — Robert's suicide grave on a Border hill — is disputed by a local shepherd who corrects "Mr. Hogg's description" of the location. This metafictional moment roots the story in actual Scottish topography, identified by real place-names and land boundaries:

> "He said there was hardly a bit o't correct, for the grave was not on the hill of Cowan's-Croft nor yet on the point where three lairds' lands met, but on the top of a hill called the Faw-Law, where there was no land that was not the Duke of Buccleuch's within a quarter of a mile." (007-020)

## Significance

Hogg's Scotland is a nation caught between worlds: between Catholic and Protestant, between clan loyalty and legal rationality, between the supernatural worldview of folk tradition and the skeptical empiricism of the Enlightenment. The novel's theological drama — antinomian certainty leading to murder — can only unfold in a culture where Calvinist doctrine has saturated the collective imagination. Scotland provides not just the setting but the conditions of possibility for every event in the novel.

The political dimension is equally important. The narrator's comment that "the sheriff was a Whig" (004-175) and that Scots view "the cases of their own party-men and those of opposite political principles" differently is a pointed critique of the partisan corruption that pervaded Scottish justice. The novel's events unfold against "the memorable year for Scotland, 1715" (004-374), anchoring private tragedy in national history.
