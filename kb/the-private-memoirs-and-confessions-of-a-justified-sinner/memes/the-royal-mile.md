# The Royal Mile

## Description

The Royal Mile is the succession of streets running downhill from Edinburgh Castle to the Palace of Holyroodhouse — Castlehill, the Lawnmarket, the High Street, and the Canongate. In the early 18th century, it was the main artery of Edinburgh life: a dense, vertical city where rich and poor lived in the same tenement buildings, with the wealthiest on the middle floors and the poorest in the attics and cellars.

> "The evening being fine, and the streets thronged, the cry ran from mouth to mouth through the whole city." (004-082)

## In the Novel

The Royal Mile is the urban stage upon which much of the novel's Edinburgh scenes are played out. Characters move along its length, encounter each other in its closes and wynds, and conduct their public lives in its taverns, churches, and public buildings. Its dense, vertical architecture — with dark closes branching off the main thoroughfare — provides the spatial correlative for the novel's concern with visibility and concealment.

> "But the High Street of Edinburgh, which they soon reached, is a dangerous place in which to make an open attack upon a mob." (004-086)

> "It was at the foot of one of the north wynds of Edinburgh." (004-232)

## Significance

The Royal Mile's distinctive architecture — public thoroughfare by day, dark and dangerous closes by night — mirrors the novel's central theme: the coexistence of public respectability and hidden violence, of orthodox profession and Antinomian practice.
