# Andrew Handyside

## Biography

Andrew Handyside was a servant at [[dalcastle|Dalcastle]] who "served at table that evening" (006-306) — the night of [[george-colwan|George Colwan]]'s murder. By the time [[robert-wringhim|Robert Wringhim]] returned to inquire about the events of that night, Handyside had already been dead for six years: "Andrew Handyside has been in his grave these six years" (006-305).

The timing of Handyside's death is suggestive. As a servant present at the fatal dinner, he was a potential witness to the events surrounding [[george-colwan|George Colwan]]'s death. His death — whether natural or otherwise — meant that his testimony was permanently unavailable, removing one more obstacle to [[robert-wringhim|Robert]]'s acquittal.

## Role in the Narrative

Handyside's role in the novel is small but structurally significant. He represents the category of witnesses whose accounts might have incriminated [[robert-wringhim|Robert]] but who, for various reasons, could not testify. His death "these six years" past adds to the accumulating pattern of suspicious circumstances surrounding the [[george-colwan|Colwan]] murder — circumstances that individually mean little but collectively point to [[robert-wringhim|Robert]]'s guilt.

The detail of Handyside's death also contributes to the novel's atmosphere of retrospective uncertainty. By the time the [[the-editor|Editor]] investigates, key witnesses are dead, memories have faded, and the full truth of the events is unrecoverable — a pattern that mirrors the theological uncertainty at the novel's core.

## Key Passages

Robert's inquiry after Handyside upon his return to Dalcastle:

> I asked for Andrew Handyside, the servant who had waited at table the night before; but the man answered with a stare and a smile. (006-303)

The devastating reply:

> "Ye sanna want a drink, maister," said the fellow. "Tak a hearty ane, and see if it will wauken ye up something, sae that ye dinna ca' for ghaists through your sleep. Surely ye haena forgotten that Andrew Handyside has been in his grave these six months?" (006-305)

The effect on Robert:

> This was a stunning blow to me. I could not answer further, but sunk back on my pillow as if I had been a lump of lead, refusing to take a drink or anything else at the fellow's hand. (006-306)

## Relationships

- **[[george-colwan|George Colwan]]**: Handyside served at the dinner table on the night of [[george-colwan|George]]'s murder and would have witnessed the events of that evening.
- **[[robert-wringhim|Robert Wringhim]]**: The inquiry into Handyside came from [[robert-wringhim|Robert]] himself, who "asked for Andrew Handyside, the servant who had waited at table" (006-303) upon his return to [[dalcastle|Dalcastle]].
- **[[samuel-scrape|Samuel Scrape]]**: The servant who delivered the news of Handyside's death to [[robert-wringhim|Robert]].

## Key Appearances

- **The Inquiry (006-303–306)**: [[robert-wringhim|Robert]] returns to [[dalcastle|Dalcastle]] and asks for Handyside, the servant who had waited at table on the night of the murder. He is told that Handyside has been dead for six years.

## Critical Interpretation

Handyside illustrates what literary critics have called the novel's "epistemology of absence" — the systematic removal of evidence that might resolve the narrative's central ambiguities. Each dead witness, each lost document, each fading memory contributes to the impenetrability of the truth. Handyside's six-year-old grave is one more sealed door in the novel's architecture of uncertainty.
