# The Kirk Session

## Overview

The Kirk Session is the governing body of a local Presbyterian parish, composed of the minister (as moderator) and the ruling elders. In Presbyterian polity, the Session is responsible for church discipline, the examination of moral offences, the oversight of the poor, and the general spiritual welfare of the parish. It was, in 17th–18th century Scotland, one of the most immediate forms of institutional authority in daily life.

## Historical Context

The Presbyterian system of church government, established in Scotland after the Reformation of 1560, organized ecclesiastical authority into ascending courts: the Kirk Session (parish level), the Presbytery (regional), the Synod (provincial), and the General Assembly (national). The Session met weekly or monthly and exercised extensive powers over the moral conduct of parishioners — including investigating fornication, Sabbath-breaking, drunkenness, and doctrinal error. Its proceedings were public and its censures carried social as well as spiritual weight.

## In the Novel

The Kirk Session is notable in Hogg's novel primarily by its absence. In a narrative saturated with theological discourse and moral violation, the institutional mechanisms that should address such matters — discipline, investigation, pastoral oversight — are conspicuously missing. Reverend Wringhim's church operates as a private domain of radical teaching rather than as part of the broader Presbyterian court system. The failure of institutional Presbyterianism to intervene in the Wringhim family's spiritual and moral descent is one of the novel's implied criticisms.

When the Reverend "held the doctrine of absolute predestination in its most rigid form," he did so not under the discipline of a Session or Presbytery that might correct extreme teaching, but in a private chapel where his word was law. The institutional vacuum allows theological error to flourish unchecked.

## Theological/Legal Significance

The Kirk Session represents the institutional alternative to the private, unaccountable religiosity that the novel critiques. In orthodox Presbyterian polity, no minister is autonomous — he is subject to the collective authority of the Session, which in turn answers to the Presbytery and General Assembly. The novel's Antinomian characters operate entirely outside this system of mutual accountability. The Session's absence is thus a structural condition of the tragedy: without institutional checks, theological conviction becomes indistinguishable from personal fanaticism.
