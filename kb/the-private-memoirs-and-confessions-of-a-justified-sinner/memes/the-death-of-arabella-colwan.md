# The Death of Arabella Colwan

## Description

Robert Wringhim murders his own mother, Arabella Colwan — the woman who raised him in the Antinomian faith and from whom he learned the theology that now justifies her killing. The matricide is the novel's darkest irony: the theological system Arabella instilled in her son becomes the instrument of her own destruction.

## Narrative Significance

Arabella's death at Robert's hands completes the novel's critique of Antinomianism as a force that destroys families across generations. The mother who taught her son that the elect can do no wrong is killed by that son in the name of that doctrine. The circle of theological violence is closed.

The investigation into Arabella's disappearance involves [[bell-calvert]]'s crucial testimony. As the narrative records:

> "My name is Arabella Calvert," said the other. "Miss, mistress, or widow, as you choose. I am somewhat, so you may call me; but in truth I am nothing. I have no name that I am entitled to, and none that I am not." (004-209)

> "And at last to know if the unfortunate Arabella Calvert would be admitted to the presence of my lady." (004-281)

The disappearance of the mother, like so much in the novel, remains shrouded in ambiguity — the narrator reports that "the lady of the place was missing, and could not be found, dead or alive" (004-372).
