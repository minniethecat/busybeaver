# Predestination

## Definition

Predestination (or absolute predestination) is the Calvinist doctrine that God, from all eternity, has sovereignly and unconditionally decreed the eternal destiny of every human being. By this decree, some are elected to salvation (the elect) and others passed over and ordained to damnation (the reprobate). This choice is entirely independent of any foreseen merit, faith, or works — it rests solely on God's inscrutable will. In Hogg's novel, the doctrine is more often termed "absolute predestination" or "eternal predestination," and is central to the theological worldview that enables the protagonist's moral catastrophe.

## Theological Background

The doctrine of predestination was most systematically articulated by John Calvin in his *Institutes of the Christian Religion* (1536) and codified by the Synod of Dort (1618–1619) in the Canons of Dort, which affirmed unconditional election, limited atonement, and the perseverance of the saints. In the Scottish Presbyterian context of Hogg's novel (set in the late 17th and early 18th centuries), these doctrines were fiercely debated. The "Auchtermuchty" tradition depicted in the novel represents an extreme Antinomian wing of Scottish Calvinism, which held that the elect are not only predestined to salvation but are also freed from the obligations of the moral law — since their sins, being foreordained, cannot affect their final salvation.

The novel's clergyman, the Reverend Robert Wringhim (father of the protagonist), preaches a radical version of this doctrine. His sermons emphasize that justification is by faith alone and that the elect can do no wrong, a teaching that provides the theological justification for his son's subsequent crimes (004-032). The theological system Hogg satirizes had real historical referents: the "Antinomian Controversy" that roiled 17th-century English and Scottish Puritanism, in which figures like Tobias Crisp and John Saltmarsh were accused of teaching that the elect are freed from the moral law. Hogg transplants this controversy to the Scottish context and dramatizes its psychological consequences.

## In the Novel

Predestination functions in the novel as the ideological engine of the plot. The protagonist, Robert Wringhim (the younger), is raised under his father's teaching that "a justified person can do no wrong" (004-042). Having been assured by his spiritual mentor — the mysterious Gil-Martin — that he is among the elect, Robert becomes convinced that all his actions, including murder, are foreordained and therefore righteous. This conviction is the psychological mechanism by which religious certainty becomes a license for evil.

Several key moments in the novel trace this descent:

- Robert's father, the Reverend Wringhim, preaches the "standard doctrine of absolute predestination" as the crown of Christian theology (004-032). His sermons are described as emotionally overwhelming, leaving his congregation in "pangs of the most unendurable anguish."
- Gil-Martin systematically inculcates in Robert "the infallibility of the elect, and the preordination of all things that come to pass" (006-116). This teaching becomes the foundation for Robert's self-justification.
- After the murder of Mr. Blanchard, Robert and Gil-Martin debate the theological implications of the crime — and Gil-Martin's arguments consistently redirect Robert toward the conclusion that all things, including murder, are foreordained (006-251).
- After committing his first murder (of his brother George), Robert's internal reasoning reveals how deeply he has internalized the doctrine: he believes that all his deeds, being those of one of the elect, are "justified before God" (004-366).
- By the novel's end, Robert's belief in his own predestined election is revealed to be a delusion — or worse, a demonic deception — that has led him to damnation rather than salvation.

The novel's most devastating insight is that predestination, as a psychological experience, is indistinguishable from its opposite. Robert *feels* elected; Gil-Martin confirms his election; his father's theology assures him that election is irreversible — and yet the entire edifice is demonstrably leading him to perdition. The doctrine that was supposed to guarantee his salvation has become the mechanism of his damnation.

## Predestination and Narrative Form

Hogg's novel does not merely *depict* the doctrine of predestination; it structurally mirrors it. The Editor's Narrative establishes the "eternal decree" of events — the facts of what happened, documented and fixed — before Robert's Confessions offers the "elect" perspective on those same events. The reader, like the Calvinist God, knows the outcome before the protagonist narrates his version of it. This narrative architecture creates a peculiar reading experience: Robert's self-justifications are always already undercut by the Editor's prior testimony, just as, in Calvinist theology, the reprobate's protestations of innocence are always already overruled by the divine decree.

The temporal structure of the novel reinforces this theological parallel. The Editor's Narrative is set in the narrative present (the early 19th century), looking back on events of the late 17th and early 18th centuries with the documentary certainty of historical fact. Robert's Confessions, by contrast, unfold in the experiential present tense of a man who does not yet know his own end — though the reader, having already read the Editor's account, knows it all too well. This produces the novel's characteristic tragic irony: the reader watches Robert construct elaborate theological justifications for actions whose damning consequences are already established.

## Critical Significance

The novel's treatment of predestination operates on multiple levels. On the surface, it is a satirical attack on the Antinomian extreme of Calvinist theology — Hogg shows how the doctrine, taken to its logical conclusion, annihilates moral responsibility. But the novel is more than a theological polemic. The doctrine of predestination also serves as a psychological device: Robert's belief in his own election is the mechanism by which Hogg explores the theme of self-deception and the divided self. The question the novel poses is not merely theological ("Is predestination true?") but epistemological ("How can anyone *know* they are among the elect?"). The answer the novel dramatizes is grim: subjective certainty of election is indistinguishable from satanic delusion.

This makes Hogg's novel a profound exploration of what happens when theological abstraction meets human psychology — a theme that would later be taken up by writers such as Robert Louis Stevenson (who acknowledged Hogg's influence on *Dr. Jekyll and Mr. Hyde*) and, in the 20th century, by writers such as Graham Greene and Flannery O'Connor, who similarly explored the psychological consequences of extreme Calvinist doctrines. The novel's enduring power lies in its recognition that the doctrine of predestination, whatever its theological merits, is psychologically catastrophic: it replaces moral reasoning with a hermeneutics of the self, in which every action is interpreted not by its ethical content but by its presumed place in a pre-written cosmic narrative.
