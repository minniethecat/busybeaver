# The Scottish Highlands

## Description

The Scottish Highlands in the early 18th century were a region of dramatic mountain scenery, clan-based social organization, and limited penetration by the institutions of the Scottish state and established Church. The Highlands represented, in the Lowland imagination, a world that was wilder, older, and less touched by Enlightenment rationality — a place where older beliefs and supernatural convictions persisted.

## In the Novel

The Highlands appear in the novel as a remote space — a place of retreat, escape, and heightened supernatural possibility. When characters flee to the Highlands, they are fleeing not just geographically but symbolically: away from the rational, institutional world of Edinburgh and toward something wilder and less certain.

> "...wanted to go with Gordon to the Highlands for a space. The day was accordingly set..." (004-170)

> "...their visit to the Highlands, though thus long delayed, was..." (004-180)

## Significance

The novel's use of Highland landscape participates in the Romantic fascination with wild nature and the sublime. The Highlands represent a realm where the rational explanations of the Editor's Narrative lose their force and the supernatural possibilities of Robert's Confessions become more plausible.

> "...I heard the young man in the Highland garb say indignantly, 'Hush,...'" (004-299)
