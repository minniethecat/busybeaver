# Drummond of Logie

## Biography

The Honourable Thomas Drummond was the second son of a John, Duke of Melfort, "who lived abroad with the royal family of the Stuarts" (004-188). He lived in Scotland with the relations of his mother, one of whom — [[lord-craigie|Lord Craigie]] — was a Lord of Session and his uncle. Drummond was one of [[george-colwan|George Colwan]]'s companions in the [[edinburgh|Edinburgh]] social scenes of [[the-editor|the Editor]]'s Narrative.

On the night of [[george-colwan|George Colwan]]'s murder, Drummond had a quarrel with [[george-colwan|George]] at a dinner party. "Some of them had heard the threat uttered by Drummond on leaving the house, and one only had noted him lay his hand on his sword" (004-189). When [[george-colwan|George]] was later found murdered, Drummond became the prime suspect. His friends "compelled him, sorely against his will, to retire into concealment till the issue of the proof that should be led was made known" (004-188).

The evidence against Drummond was circumstantial but compelling — including testimony from "one of the women" who "swore that she heard the voice distinctly at the door" (004-189). However, [[lord-craigie|Lord Craigie]] produced counter-evidence, including the fact that "both the wounds which the deceased had received had been given behind" (004-190) — a detail inconsistent with a face-to-face quarrel.

Ultimately, it was the testimony of Bell [[arabella-calvert|Calvert]] to [[lord-craigie|Lord Craigie]] that cleared Drummond's name (004-371), revealing that [[robert-wringhim|Robert Wringhim]] was the true murderer. But by then the damage was done. "The Honourable Thomas Drummond became a distinguished officer in the Austrian service, and died in the memorable year for Scotland, 1715" (004-374).

## Role in the Narrative

Drummond's fate illustrates the novel's concern with justice miscarried. He is an innocent man whose life is destroyed by circumstance — his name stigmatised "of having stabbed a friend behind his back" (004-371) — while the true murderer, [[robert-wringhim|Robert]], evades the law. His story parallels the novel's theological theme: just as the law cannot see past appearances to the truth of the crime, so conventional morality cannot see past doctrinal profession to the reality of sin.

## Key Passages

Drummond's angry departure from the dinner party:

> "What the devil is the matter?" cried one. "What ails Drummond?" cried another. "Who has he quarrelled with?" asked a third. "Don't know."—"Can't tell, on my life."—"He has quarrelled with his wine, I suppose, and is going to send it a challenge." (004-415–418)

Lord Craigie's counter-evidence on his nephew's behalf:

> He produced the sword which his nephew had worn that night, on which there was neither blood nor blemish; and, above all, he insisted on the evidence of a number of surgeons, who declared that both the wounds which the deceased had received had been given behind. (004-190)

Bell Calvert's confirmation of his innocence:

> "It was not the gentleman who was accused, found guilty, and would have suffered the utmost penalty of the law had he not made his escape. It was not he, I say, who slew your young master, nor had he any hand in it." (004-284)

Drummond's own letter, protesting his innocence:

> He begged of his relative, if ever an opportunity offered, to do his endeavour to clear up that mystery, and remove the horrid stigma from his name in his country, and among his kin, of having stabbed a friend behind his back. (004-371)

His ultimate fate:

> The Honourable Thomas Drummond became a distinguished officer in the Austrian service, and died in the memorable year for Scotland, 1715; and this is all with which history, justiciary records, and tradition, furnish me relating to these matters. (004-374)

## Relationships

- **[[george-colwan|George Colwan]]**: His friend and companion, whose murder he was wrongly accused of — "of having stabbed a friend behind his back" (004-371).
- **[[lord-craigie|Lord Craigie]]**: His uncle, the Lord Justice Clerk, who collected evidence in his defence (004-190, 004-371).
- **[[robert-wringhim|Robert Wringhim]]**: The true murderer whose crime Drummond was accused of.
- **[[arabella-calvert|Arabella Calvert]]**: The witness whose testimony ultimately cleared him.
