# Rev. Robert Wringhim

## Biography

The Reverend [[robert-wringhim|Robert Wringhim]] is the [[antinomianism|Antinomian]] minister whose preaching provides the theological foundation for his son's crimes. He is the spiritual leader of a small sect within the Church of [[scotland|Scotland]] that holds to an extreme form of Calvinism, emphasizing absolute predestination and the infallibility of the elect. His sermons are described as emotionally overwhelming, driving his congregation to "pangs of the most unendurable anguish" (004-032).

The narrative strongly implies that he is [[robert-wringhim|Robert]]'s biological father through an adulterous relationship with [[arabella-colwan|Rabina]] ([[arabella-colwan|Arabella]]) [[george-colwan|Colwan]], [[george-colwan|the Laird]]'s wife. When the Laird's marriage to Rabina founders on theological differences, it is the Reverend Wringhim who is summoned from Glasgow to provide spiritual counsel — and who promptly moves into Dalcastle as the lady's permanent spiritual advisor (004-038). The servant John Barnet's pointed remarks about the young Robert's resemblance to the minister confirm what the narrative has already implied (006-051).

> "Letter after letter was dispatched to Glasgow; and at length, to the lady's great consolation, the Rev. Mr. Wringhim arrived safely and devoutly in her elevated sanctuary. Marvellous was the conversation between these two jugs of grace, on the first evening after their meeting." (004-038)
>
> — The Editor's ironic description of the minister's arrival and his immediate intimacy with Lady Dalcastle

He raises [[robert-wringhim|Robert]] as his spiritual protégé, inculcating in him from childhood the doctrines that will eventually destroy him. The boy is educated not in general knowledge but in theological precision: he learns to debate fine points of Calvinist soteriology before he has developed any moral sensibility that might resist their implications (006-004).

> "One Scripture text followed another, not in the least connected, and one sentence of the profound Mr. Wringhim's sermons after another, proving the duty of family worship, till the laird lost patience." (004-012)
>
> — Lady Dalcastle's method of argumentation reveals the extent to which Wringhim's teachings have become her entire intellectual framework

## The Wringhim Theology

The Reverend Wringhim's theological system can be reconstructed from his sermons and his reported teachings. Its key tenets include:

1. **Absolute Predestination**: God has from all eternity decreed the salvation of the elect and the damnation of the reprobate, without reference to any foreseen merit or demerit (004-032).
2. **The Infallibility of the Elect**: Those who are truly among the elect cannot finally fall away, and their actions — whatever they may appear to human judgment — are righteous by virtue of the agent's elect status (004-042).
3. **Justification Without Sanctification**: The justified person is declared righteous forensically, and this declaration is not contingent on any subsequent moral transformation. The elect remain elect regardless of their conduct.
4. **Assurance as the Mark of Election**: The subjective certainty of one's own election is itself evidence of election — a circular logic that makes doubt itself a sign of reprobation.

This last point is particularly destructive. By making assurance the mark of election, Wringhim's system creates an intolerable psychological pressure: to doubt is to be damned. The only acceptable emotional state is absolute confidence, and any moral qualm must be suppressed lest it be interpreted as evidence of reprobation (006-234). This is the mechanism by which Wringhim's theology, transmitted through his son, produces the catastrophe of the novel.

> "They being both children of adoption, and secured from falling into snares, or anyway under the power of the wicked one, it was their custom, on each visit, to sit up a night in the same apartment, for the sake of sweet spiritual converse; but that time, in the course of the night, they differed so materially on a small point somewhere between justification and final election that the minister, in the heat of his zeal, got up and drove her home." (004-059)
>
> — The Editor's account of a typical nocturnal visit by Wringhim, with the detail that even a "small point" of doctrine could provoke such wrath that he "drove her home" — revealing the volatility beneath the spiritual rhetoric

## Role in the Narrative

The Reverend Wringhim appears primarily in the early chapters of the novel, but his influence pervades the entire narrative. He is the architect of the theological system that [[robert-wringhim|Robert]] inherits. His most destructive teaching — that "a justified person can do no wrong" (004-042) — becomes the central rationalization for [[robert-wringhim|Robert]]'s crimes. [[james-hogg|Hogg]]'s portrait of the minister is satirical but not caricatured: the Reverend Wringhim is sincere, learned, and utterly wrong.

His relationship with [[robert-wringhim|Robert]] is both paternal and pedagogical. The novel shows him interrogating the boy about his theological opinions, praising correct answers, and modeling the combination of intellectual rigor and moral blindness that will define his son's character. When Robert reports his encounter with the mysterious stranger (Gil-Martin), the Reverend's interrogation focuses entirely on doctrinal orthodoxy — not on the boy's spiritual state or moral well-being:

> "Woman, hold thy peace!" said my reverend father. "Thou pretendest to teach what thou knowest not. Tell me this, boy: did this stranger, with whom you met, adhere to the religious principles in which I have educated you?" (006-100)
>
> — The Reverend silences his wife's intuitive suspicion of the stranger and reduces the inquiry to a doctrinal checklist, demonstrating the fatal limitation of his theological method

The irony is devastating: the father's doctrinal test is perfectly satisfied by the devil, who knows his catechism as well as any saint.

## Relationships

- **[[robert-wringhim|Robert Wringhim]]**: His son and theological heir, whom he raises from childhood in his [[antinomianism|Antinomian]] system. The relationship is both intimate and destructive — the Reverend genuinely loves his son, and his love takes the form of imparting the theology that will destroy him.
- **[[arabella-colwan|Rabina]] ([[arabella-colwan|Arabella]]) [[george-colwan|Colwan]]**: His spiritual follower and implied lover; he effectively takes her from her husband. Their relationship is presented as a grotesque parody of spiritual companionship, in which theological fervor and erotic attraction are indistinguishable (004-059).
- **The [[laird-of-dalcastle|Laird of Dalcastle]]**: His theological and personal antagonist — [[george-colwan|the Laird]] represents the moderate, rational religion against which the Reverend defines himself. The Laird's refusal to accept Wringhim's authority over his household precipitates the domestic crisis (004-012).
- **[[george-colwan|George Colwan]]**: He has minimal direct relationship with [[george-colwan|George]], but his theology provides the justification for [[george-colwan|George]]'s murder. George is, in a sense, the Reverend's victim at one remove.

## Key Appearances

- **Arrival at Dalcastle (004-038)**: The Reverend arrives to console Lady Dalcastle and effectively replaces the Laird as the spiritual head of the household.
- **Sermons (004-032)**: His emotionally charged preaching on predestination is the novel's first extended theological set-piece, establishing the doctrine that will drive the plot.
- **The Nocturnal Visits (004-059)**: His late-night sessions with Lady Dalcastle, ostensibly for "sweet spiritual converse," are ironized by the Editor as something considerably less spiritual.
- **[[robert-wringhim|Robert]]'s Education**: The novel implies that [[robert-wringhim|Robert]]'s entire childhood was spent absorbing his father's theology. The boy is examined on points of doctrine, praised for orthodoxy, and never once asked about charity, humility, or compassion (006-004).

## Critical Interpretation

The Reverend Wringhim represents the institutional face of [[antinomianism|Antinomianism]] — the false teacher whose influence extends beyond his own life into the lives of those he has shaped. [[james-hogg|Hogg]]'s critique is not merely individual but institutional: bad theology, when it occupies a position of ecclesiastical authority, can produce spiritual devastation across generations.

The figure of the Reverend Wringhim draws on a long tradition of satirical portraits of Calvinist clergy in Scottish literature, from Robert Burns's "Holy Willie's Prayer" to the caricatures of the Edinburgh *Blackwood's Magazine* circle in which Hogg himself moved. But Hogg's portrayal is more complex than satire alone. The Reverend is not a hypocrite in the conventional sense — he genuinely believes what he preaches, and his affection for his son is real. His tragedy is that his sincere faith, channeled through a fatally flawed theological system, becomes an engine of destruction. This is a more disturbing proposition than simple clerical hypocrisy: the novel suggests that sincerity, combined with bad doctrine, can be more dangerous than cynicism.

The Reverend also serves as a structural counterpoint to [[the-editor|the Editor]]. Both are figures of authority who claim to interpret events correctly; both are revealed to have limitations. The Editor's rationalism cannot fully account for the supernatural dimensions of the story; the Reverend's supernaturalism cannot account for the moral dimensions. Between them, the novel suggests, lies the truth — but it is a truth that neither perspective alone can compass.
