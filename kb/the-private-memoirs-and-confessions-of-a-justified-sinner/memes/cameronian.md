# Cameronian

The **Cameronians** were a radical Presbyterian sect in Scotland, named after Richard Cameron (1648–1680), a field preacher killed at the Battle of Airds Moss. They rejected the Restoration settlement of church and state, refused to swear allegiance to uncovenanted monarchs, and maintained a distinct identity into the eighteenth century as the Reformed Presbyterian Church. In *The Private Memoirs and Confessions of a Justified Sinner*, Cameronian principles represent the folk-religious atmosphere of extreme Calvinist piety that produces the novel's theological world.

## Historical Background

The Cameronians trace their origin to the Covenanter movement of the seventeenth century. After the Restoration of Charles II in 1660, the re-established episcopacy persecuted those who adhered to the National Covenant (1638) and the Solemn League and Covenant (1643). Richard Cameron and his followers declared war on the Stuart monarchy in the Sanquhar Declaration (1680), denouncing Charles II as a tyrant and usurper. Cameron was killed shortly afterward, but his followers — the "Society People" — survived persecution and eventually organised as the Reformed Presbyterian Church.

By the time of Hogg's novel (set in the late seventeenth and early eighteenth centuries), Cameronian principles had become associated with a particular temperament: fierce doctrinal purity, suspicion of worldly authority, and a tendency toward schism. It is this temperament, rather than the specific historical details, that Hogg draws upon.

## Cameronians in the Novel

The Cameronian presence in the novel is both structural and atmospheric. The most direct reference comes during the riots in Edinburgh, when the authorities deploy military force:

> "The town-guard was now on the alert; and two companies of the Cameronian Regiment, with the Hon. Captain Douglas, rushed down from the Castle to the scene of action..." (004-088)

The Cameronian Regiment — a real historical unit raised from among the Cameronians after the Revolution of 1688 — appears as an agent of civil order, an irony given the sect's historical opposition to state authority. This deployment of Cameronian soldiers to suppress a riot that is itself fuelled by religious extremism creates a layered commentary on the relationship between religious zeal and civil peace.

The novel's folk-religious texture is Cameronian in spirit. The characters speak in a world saturated with theological categories, where debates about election, reprobation, and grace are conducted not in academic settings but in kitchens and fields. The servant John Barnet's exchange about "Cameronian principles" versus "good manners" (006-333) captures this: even commercial transactions are adjudicated by theological criteria.

## Folk Religion and Extremism

The folk-religious dimension of Cameronianism is crucial to Hogg's critique. The sect's emphasis on personal covenanting, its suspicion of educated clergy, and its tradition of lay preaching created a religious culture in which theological certainty was democratised. This democratisation had a dark side: anyone could claim direct access to divine truth, and there was no institutional mechanism to distinguish genuine piety from delusion.

Robert Wringhim's spiritual father embodies this tendency. He is a minister but operates outside the established church structures, gathering a personal following and cultivating an atmosphere of spiritual intensity. The Cameronian suspicion of worldly authority becomes, in his hands, a suspicion of any authority that might challenge his own.
