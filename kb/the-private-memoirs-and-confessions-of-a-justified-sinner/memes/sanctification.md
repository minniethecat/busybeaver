# Sanctification

## Definition

Sanctification is the work of God's free grace whereby believers are renewed in the image of God and enabled to die to sin and live to righteousness. In Reformed theology, sanctification is distinct from but inseparable from justification: justification is a once-for-all forensic declaration; sanctification is a progressive transformation. The Westminster Shorter Catechism (Q. 35) defines it as "the work of God's free grace, whereby we are renewed in the whole man after the image of God, and are enabled more and more to die unto sin, and live unto righteousness." Hogg's novel dramatizes the catastrophe that follows when sanctification is denied.

## Theological Background

The Reformation insisted that sanctification is the necessary consequence of justification — not the ground of acceptance with God, but the inevitable evidence of genuine faith. Calvin's *Institutes* devotes extensive space to the Christian life, arguing that union with Christ produces both justification and sanctification. The Antinomian error was to affirm justification while neglecting — or outright denying — the necessity of sanctification. This error was repeatedly condemned by Reformed confessions, but it persisted in various forms, particularly on the radical fringes of Scottish Presbyterianism.

## In the Novel

Sanctification is the great absence in Robert Wringhim's spiritual life. He claims justification, election, and assurance, but there is no trace of progressive holiness. The novel's structure underscores this: Robert's memoir records no growth in grace, no struggle against sin, no fruits of the Spirit — only a steady descent into murder and madness, accompanied by an ever-more-confident assertion of his elect status.

Hogg's point is devastating: a "justification" that produces no sanctification is not genuine justification but a demonic counterfeit. Gil-Martin functions as the anti-sanctifier — the figure who systematically dismantles every impulse toward moral self-examination that might lead Robert to repentance and growth in holiness.

## Critical Significance

By centering his critique on the absence of sanctification, Hogg aligns his novel with orthodox Reformed theology against its Antinomian distortion. The novel is not an attack on Calvinism but a vindication of Calvinism's insistence that election produces holiness. Robert Wringhim is the reductio ad absurdum of a theology without sanctification — and his destruction is the novel's most powerful argument for its necessity.
