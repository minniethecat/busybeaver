# The Tron Kirk

## Description

The Tron Kirk (Christ's Kirk at the Tron) was one of Edinburgh's principal Presbyterian churches, located on the High Street (the Royal Mile). Built in the 17th century, it was a center of the city's religious life during the period of Hogg's novel. Its prominent position on the High Street made it a visible symbol of the established Church's authority.

## In the Novel

> "But the High Street of Edinburgh, which they soon reached, is a dangerous place in which to make an open attack upon a mob." (004-086)

While the novel's Antinomian sect operates largely outside the established Church structures, the ecclesiastical landscape of Edinburgh — including institutions like the Tron Kirk — forms the background against which the theological drama is played out. The novel's critique of Antinomian extremism implicitly affirms the moderate Presbyterianism represented by the established Church.

> "For instance, he remembered one day of setting out with the intention of going to attend divine worship in the High Church, and when, within a short space of its door, he was overtaken by young Kilpatrick of Closeburn, who was bound to the Grey-Friars to see his sweetheart, as he said." (004-133)

> "It was he who directed my studies aright, both in the learning of the ancient fathers and the doctrines of the reformed church, and designed me for his assistant and successor in the holy office." (006-003)

## Significance

The Tron Kirk represents institutional, moderate religion — the kind that the Reverend Wringhim's sect rejects and that Hogg, through the novel, implicitly defends against Antinomian excess.
