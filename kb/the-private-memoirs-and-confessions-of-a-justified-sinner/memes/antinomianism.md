# Antinomianism

## Definition

Antinomianism (from Greek *anti*, "against," and *nomos*, "law") is the heretical belief that Christians, being justified by faith and freed from the condemnation of the law, are not bound by the moral law as a rule of life. In its extreme form, it holds that the sins of the elect are not really sins — or, if sins, are already forgiven by the eternal decree, and therefore require no repentance. This is the theological error at the heart of Hogg's *Confessions of a Justified Sinner*.

## Theological Background

The Antinomian controversy erupted repeatedly in 17th- and 18th-century British Protestantism. In England, it was associated with John Eaton and Tobias Crisp; in Scotland, with certain extreme Covenanting preachers. The orthodox Reformed position — articulated at the Westminster Assembly (1643–1649) — was that the moral law continues to bind believers as a "rule of life," even though they are freed from it as a "covenant of works."

The Antinomians denied even this, arguing that to preach moral duties to the justified was to mix law and gospel, undermining the freeness of grace. Hogg's novel is the most sustained literary treatment of Antinomianism in English literature.

### Historical Antinomian Controversy

The roots of Antinomianism predate the Reformation, but its distinctively Protestant form emerged in the wake of Luther's sharp law/gospel distinction. In the 1530s, Luther himself contended against Johannes Agricola, who argued that the law had no place whatsoever in the Christian life — not even to drive sinners to Christ. Luther's response, *Against the Antinomians* (1539), established the orthodox Lutheran position: the law remains necessary to convict of sin, guide Christian conduct, and maintain civil order.

In England, the Antinomian controversy flared during the 1640s and 1650s, intertwined with the chaos of the Civil War. Tobias Crisp (1600–1643), a popular London preacher, taught that the elect were justified from eternity — before they believed, before they were born — and that their sins, past, present, and future, were already forgiven in the divine decree. Crisp's sermons, published posthumously as *Christ Alone Exalted*, became the touchstone of English Antinomianism.

His teaching that "the believer hath no sin" and that God sees no sin in the elect scandalized the Puritan establishment. The Westminster Confession (1647) was drafted partly in response to such teachings, affirming that "God continues to forgive the sins of those that are justified" — implying that sins do exist and require ongoing forgiveness.

In Scotland, Antinomian tendencies surfaced among the more extreme wing of the Covenanters. The "Marrow Controversy" (1718–1723) — sparked by the republication of Edward Fisher's *The Marrow of Modern Divinity* — centered on whether the offer of the gospel was universal or restricted to the elect, and whether assurance was of the essence of faith. The General Assembly condemned the "Marrow Men" for allegedly teaching Antinomianism, though the Marrow Men themselves denied the charge.

Nevertheless, the controversy revealed how close certain strains of Scottish Calvinism came to Antinomian conclusions. Hogg, writing a century after the Marrow Controversy, was keenly aware of this theological legacy — his novel can be read as a fictional exploration of where the Marrow's logic leads when unmoored from orthodox discipline.

### The Logic of Antinomian Reasoning

Antinomianism is not an arbitrary error but a seductive chain of theological reasoning:

1. **Eternal Justification**: The elect are justified from eternity, before creation, in the divine decree. Their sins are already forgiven — indeed, never counted against them.

2. **No Conditional Covenant**: Since justification is unconditional, no subsequent act — including sin — can alter the elect's standing before God. To suggest otherwise is to introduce works-righteousness through the back door.

3. **Sin as Instrument**: What the world calls "sin" may, in the lives of the elect, be an instrument of God's sovereign will. David's adultery and murder, after all, did not cause him to lose his elect status.

4. **Moral Self-Examination as Unbelief**: The elect person who questions whether his actions are righteous is, in effect, doubting the sufficiency of Christ's atonement. True faith means believing that one's sins are forgiven — *regardless of what one does*.

5. **The Spiritual/Wicked Distinction**: As the Reverend Wringhim teaches, the sins of the unconverted are "operative" (damaging to themselves), but to the elect they are merely "passive" — they cannot harm the justified soul. This effectively creates two moral universes.

Hogg's novel traces each link in this chain with clinical precision, showing how Robert moves from accepting the premises to committing murder without ever losing his conviction of righteousness.

## In the Novel

Although the word "Antinomian" never appears in the novel's text, Antinomianism is the theological atmosphere the characters breathe. The Reverend Wringhim preaches "a system of justifying-grace against all breaches of all laws" (004-056), a formulation that effectively abolishes the moral law. Robert Wringhim absorbs this teaching and extends it to its logical conclusion: if he is elect, nothing he does can be wrong. Murder becomes not merely permissible but, in a dark parody of Calvinist reasoning, *foreordained* and therefore righteous.

Gil-Martin functions as the Antinomian tempter, systematically dismantling Robert's moral scruples. He teaches "the infallibility of the elect" (006-116), a doctrine that makes moral self-examination not merely unnecessary but actually a sign of weak faith. By the novel's end, Robert has become the Antinomian nightmare made flesh: a man who has committed terrible crimes while maintaining absolute conviction of his own righteousness.

### Antinomian Reasoning in Dialogue

The novel presents Antinomian thinking through direct dialogue, allowing the reader to see its internal logic. When Arabella expresses concern about the spiritual state of her husband, the Reverend Wringhim dismisses all moral obligation toward the unconverted:

> "Evil, madam, may be either operative, or passive. To them it is an evil, but to us none. We have no more to do with the sins of the wicked and unconverted here than with those of an infidel Turk; for all earthly bonds and fellowships are absorbed and swallowed up in the holy community of the Reformed Church." (004-041)

This passage establishes the Antinomian principle that the elect and the reprobate inhabit fundamentally different moral orders. Arabella's response shows how easily the doctrine is received by those it flatters:

> "Ah, that is a sweet and comfortable saying, Mr. Wringhim! How delightful to think that a justified person can do no wrong! Who would not envy the liberty wherewith we are made free?" (004-042)

The most explicit statement of Antinomian reasoning comes from Gil-Martin, who argues that to doubt one's justification is to "put discredit on the great atonement":

> "Is there not enough of merit in the blood of Jesus to save thousands of worlds, if it was for these worlds that he died? Now, when you know, as you do (and as every one of the elect may know of himself) that this Saviour died for you, namely and particularly for you, as well as for the thief on the cross and the murderer of Saul of Tarsus, how darest thou, whom the most High has chosen and elected to a glorious inheritance, to doubt of the effect of that great atonement?" (006-117)

This is the Antinomian trap: any moral qualm becomes a failure of faith. To feel guilt is to doubt Christ. To repent is to question the decree. The only acceptable posture is absolute confidence in one's own righteousness — the very posture that enables Robert's crimes.

## Critical Significance

Hogg's treatment of Antinomianism is the most ambitious theological satire in Scottish literature. The novel is not merely a critique of a historical heresy but a timeless exploration of how religious certainty can become a mechanism of moral self-destruction.

By tracing Antinomianism from doctrine to psychology to criminal behavior, Hogg shows how bad theology has real-world consequences. The novel's enduring power lies in its demonstration that the line between religious conviction and self-deception is perilously thin — and that the most dangerous heresies are those that tell people exactly what they want to hear.
