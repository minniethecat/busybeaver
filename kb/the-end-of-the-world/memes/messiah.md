The Messiah is the hinge figure of Abrahamic eschatology — the one whose coming signals the transition from the present age to the age to come. In Dennis's book, the Messiah appears not as a theological proposition to be defended but as a historical given that shaped two thousand years of end-time expectation, comet interpretation, and prophetic calculation.

## Definition

The Hebrew *mashiach* ("anointed one") originally referred to the kings and high priests of Israel, consecrated with oil as a sign of divine appointment. During the Babylonian exile and Second Temple period, the term acquired an eschatological meaning: a future Davidic king who would restore Israel, defeat its enemies, and inaugurate an era of peace and righteousness. In some strands of Jewish apocalyptic, this figure was joined by a priestly Messiah, a prophetic Messiah, or a transcendent "Son of Man" figure drawn from Daniel 7.

Christianity originated as a messianic movement within Second Temple Judaism, identifying Jesus of Nazareth as the promised Messiah — with the crucial modification that the Messiah's work was accomplished in two stages: a first coming in humility (the incarnation and crucifixion) and a second coming in glory (the *parousia*, or Second Coming). This two-stage schema generated the distinctive Christian eschatological posture: the Messiah has already come, but the end has not yet arrived. The tension between the "already" and the "not yet" has powered Christian apocalyptic expectation for two millennia.

Islam recognizes Jesus (*Isa*) as the Messiah (*al-Masih*), though not as divine, and expects his return at the end of time to defeat the *Dajjal* (Antichrist) and establish justice before the Day of Judgment.

## In the Book

### Context

Dennis encounters the Messiah most directly in his survey of comet lore (Chapter 2), where celestial portents are read as announcements of messianic events. "A star in the east proclaimed the young Redeemer of mankind" at the death of Augustus (002-011). The same chapter notes that a comet "foretold the destruction of Jerusalem" — a catastrophe that Christian interpretation linked to Christ's own prophecy. The Messiah thus appears in Dennis's text as a figure whose birth and death are written in the sky, part of the cosmic text that the book decodes.

Chapter 10's history of prophetic date-setting is, in large part, a history of messianic expectation redirected. When the early Church's expectation of Christ's immediate return faded, the energy of messianic hope was channeled into numerical prophecy — the calculation of the date on which the absent Messiah would return. Every failed prophecy was a failed messianic date, and every recalibration was a renewal of messianic hope.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis's treatment of the Messiah is characteristically indirect. He does not argue for or against the messianic claim; he records its effects. The phrase "Redeemer of mankind" (002-011) is presented without commentary, as a datum of cultural history — something people believed, and something that shaped how they interpreted the sky. Dennis's interest is not in whether the Messiah is real but in what messianic belief does: it organizes time, interprets catastrophe, and sustains hope against the evidence.

The tension between messianic hope and cosmic pessimism runs through the book. Dennis's cosmos is one in which entropy is irreversible and all energy runs down. A Messiah who returns to renew the world must be strong enough to reverse entropy — to wind the clock back up. Dennis does not say this is impossible, but the weight of his physical argument presses against it. The Messiah, in Dennis's framework, is the great counter-argument to cosmic despair — and the book leaves it to the reader to decide whether the counter-argument is strong enough.
