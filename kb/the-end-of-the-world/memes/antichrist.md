# Antichrist

The apocalyptic adversary whose identification with living political figures has been the engine of two millennia of date-setting — treated by Dennis not as a theological reality to be affirmed or denied, but as a historical phenomenon revealing the human need to locate oneself at the culminating moment of history.

## Definition

In Christian eschatology, "Antichrist" (Greek: ἀντίχριστος, *antichristos*) denotes a figure who appears in the last days to oppose Christ, deceive the faithful, and preside over a period of tribulation before being defeated at the Second Coming. The term appears in the Johannine epistles (1 John 2:18, 2:22; 4:3; 2 John 1:7), though the figure described aligns closely with the "man of lawlessness" in 2 Thessalonians 2 and the Beast of Revelation 13. Dennis treats Antichrist neither as a theological postulate nor as a superstitious fantasy, but as a cultural constant: the name that every generation gives to its own feared contemporary, and the key that numerologists use to unlock the date of the world's end.

The Antichrist is distinct from the Beast in Dennis's treatment, though they overlap substantially: the Beast is a symbol, a number, a computational problem; the Antichrist is a person, a political body, a candidate for historical identification. Together they form the twin poles of numerical prophecy — the Beast's number supplies the calculation, the Antichrist's identity supplies the urgency.

## In the Book

### Context

The Antichrist appears most explicitly in Chapter 10 ("This Year"), Dennis's panoramic survey of millenarian prophecy from biblical times to his own 1930s present. The chapter traces the history of end-time prediction across Babylonian, Jewish, early Christian, medieval, and modern contexts, and the Antichrist is the recurring protagonist of this story: whoever holds power at the moment of predicted apocalypse is identified as the adversary. Dennis's purpose is not to debunk the concept but to document the resilience of Antichrist-identification in the face of repeated predictive failure. "Every prophecy concords: Apocalypse with Apocrypha, the Pyramid corridors with Daniel's chronology; Kemal's inauguration is Antichrist's World Coronation, Ismet's number (count it) the number of the Beast" (010-109).

The Antichrist also appears by implication in Chapter 12 ("Some Time"), where Dennis considers the psychological effect of apocalyptic belief. When the predicted end fails to arrive, the faithful do not typically abandon their framework; they recalibrate. The Antichrist identification shifts to a new candidate, and the cycle begins again. This pattern — identification, prediction, disconfirmation, recalibration — is, for Dennis, the central dynamic of eschatological history.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis approaches the Antichrist with characteristic irony and genuine curiosity. He does not mock those who have identified Nero, the Pope, Napoleon, or the Kaiser as Antichrist; he is interested in what the persistence of this identification reveals about human psychology and the sociology of belief. The Antichrist serves a function: by personifying evil in a single figure, the chaotic and morally ambiguous flow of history is rendered legible. A world with an Antichrist is a world with a plot — and a plot implies an ending.

Dennis traces the Antichrist through the Reformation, when Protestant exegetes identified the Papacy as the Beast and the Pope as Antichrist — an identification that made eschatology a weapon of ecclesiastical polemic. He follows it into the modern era, when Napoleon, then the Kaiser, then Kemal Atatürk each took their turn as the prophesied adversary. The 1917 prophecy that identified Kaiser Wilhelm II as the Beast is particularly instructive: "The 1917 prophecy, based on fresh researches in Revelation, connected up with the marks of the Beast (the Kaiser), and made known throughout England by showers of pamphlets" (010-107). The war gave the identification its plausibility; the peace gave it its disconfirmation.

Yet the pattern continued. Dennis notes with a kind of admiration that the Prophetical Society of Dallas, writing in 1930, had already identified new candidates: "For the immediate future there is 1931, next year as I write. The Prophetical Society of Dallas, Texas, the most powerful catastrophic organization in the world" (010-109). The machinery of Antichrist-identification runs on, indifferent to its own history of error.
