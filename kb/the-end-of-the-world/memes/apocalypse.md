# Apocalypse

In Geoffrey Dennis's *The End of the World*, the Apocalypse — both as a specific biblical book and as a mode of historical thinking — is the engine that has driven two millennia of end-of-the-world expectation in the West.

## Definition

The term Apocalypse derives from Greek *apokalypsis* (unveiling, revelation). In Dennis's usage it refers primarily to the Book of Revelation (the Apocalypse of John) and secondarily to the genre of revelatory literature that it exemplifies — texts that claim to unveil the hidden divine plan for history's catastrophic termination. Dennis treats the Apocalypse not as a source of theological truth but as a historical force: a text whose vivid imagery and ambiguous chronology have repeatedly been mapped onto contemporary events by generations of interpreters convinced they were living in the final days.

The apocalyptic mode, as Dennis understands it, is defined by three features: it is *revelatory* (claiming access to knowledge unavailable through ordinary means), *catastrophic* (envisioning the end as a violent rupture rather than a gradual transition), and *urgent* (insisting that the end is imminent). These features are structurally linked: if the end is truly imminent, there is no time for gradual reform; if it is catastrophic, there is no point in incremental improvement. The apocalyptic frame forecloses all responses except preparation — and preparation, in practice, often means heightened credulity about signs and dates.

## In the Book

### Context

Dennis's most sustained engagement with apocalyptic thinking appears in Chapters 10 and 11 ("This Year" and "Next Year"). Chapter 10 catalogs the history of date-setting: the Year 1000, the Millerite movement of 1844, the 1917 Kaiser-Beast identification. Chapter 11 steps back to examine the structural conditions that make apocalypticism possible — the tension between "Near" and "Far" expectations of the end, the role of fear and revealed religion in promoting the expectation of imminence, and the psychological mechanisms by which failed prophecies are absorbed and reinterpreted.

Dennis identifies fear as the primal driver of apocalyptic conviction. "Fear prompted this faith. Man dreaded the end; therefore he believed it near, as he believed all his enemies near, and ascribed danger to them, to fight them and kill them" (011-003). The psychological logic is counterintuitive but powerful: by believing the dreaded thing is near, one gains a kind of control over it — or at least relief from the unbearable suspense of not knowing when it will arrive.

"Seconding and sanctifying fear, revealed religion encouraged the early expectation. Organized religion enforced it, as usefully fostering the childlike dependence and the obedience of its congregation" (011-004). Dennis is attentive to the institutional dimension: apocalyptic expectation is not merely a psychological phenomenon but a tool of ecclesiastical control. A congregation convinced the end is near is a congregation that remains attentive, obedient, and generous.

In Chapter 10, Dennis presents a snapshot of his own historical moment: 1931, when the Prophetical Society of Dallas, Texas, had concluded that "every sign has indeed been accomplished" and the end was imminent — "Perhaps in spring, perhaps in the autumn, rather more probably at Christmas" (010-109). The passage is both ethnographic and ironic: Dennis records the conviction and lets the specificity of the prediction speak for itself.

In Chapter 11, Dennis steps back to survey the first centuries of Christian apocalypticism, when the Synoptic Gospels and the Apocalypse "took the time-phrases literally" and exercised "greater sway" than they would in later, more allegorically-minded centuries (011-010). The passage is one of the book's most rhetorically powerful: a catalog of horrors — earthquakes, eruptions, wars, sieges, cannibalism — that first-century believers experienced as the fulfillment of apocalyptic prophecy. "Such were the days in which our religion began. No syllable of the Messianic Woes was lacking" (011-011). The claim is devastating: Christianity was born in an atmosphere of apocalyptic terror, and that terror is not an aberration but an inheritance.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis's approach to the Apocalypse is characteristic of his method throughout the book: he neither affirms nor dismisses, but accumulates and juxtaposes. The 1931 prophecy sits alongside the first-century catastrophes; the Prophetical Society of Dallas alongside Nero's persecution of Christians. The cumulative effect is to show apocalyptic thinking as a permanent feature of the Western imagination — endlessly refuted by events and endlessly renewed.

> Every prophecy concords: Apocalypse with Apocrypha, the Pyramid corridors with Daniel's chronology; Kemal's inauguration is Antichrist's World Coronation, Ismet's number (count it) the number of the Beast, the Red Tsar the Scarlet Woman. (010-109)

> The Synoptics and the Apocalypse, however, which took the time-phrases literally, had greater sway over the first centuries. None of the portents was lacking. (011-010)

> Lord of the World was Antichrist: "Strike the womb which bore Nero!" cried Agrippina, the dead womb he came to peer at and to mock. (011-010)

Dennis pays particular attention to the hermeneutic machinery of apocalyptic interpretation — the method by which ancient texts are made to yield precise contemporary references. The Beast, the Scarlet Woman, the Millennium — these are not fixed symbols but empty vessels into which each generation pours its own anxieties. Kemal Atatürk becomes the Antichrist; Lenin becomes the Scarlet Woman; the Great Pyramid's corridors become a chronometer of world history. The method is infinitely adaptable because it is infinitely vague.

The chapter's structural insight is that apocalyptic thinking operates on two incompatible timelines. "Aside from the named year, early or late, stand the two chief temporal expectations, broadly grouped and broadly contrasted: Near and Far" (011-001). The "Near" expectation is the province of religion and fear; the "Far" expectation belongs to science and reason. But Dennis notes that the distinction is unstable: the "Near" keeps encroaching on the "Far," as scientific projections of cosmic death (the dying sun, the heat death) acquire their own apocalyptic urgency. "If soon, sudden" (011-024) — the formula is terse but encapsulates a genuine insight: the speed of an ending determines its existential character.

Dennis's treatment of the apocalyptic tradition culminates in a quiet but radical suggestion: that the Apocalypse has been the most generative text in Western history. "No subject that has ever engaged the minds of men has been more thought about or more written about than the words of Christ and His Apostles touching the end of the world" (011-008). This is not a claim about theological truth but about cultural productivity — the sheer volume of intellectual energy that has been poured into interpreting a single book's cryptic visions.
