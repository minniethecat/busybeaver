# America

America appears in Dennis's survey in two contrasting roles: as the "New World" that Columbus mistook for Paradise, and as the birthplace of Adventist end-of-the-world movements that shaped modern apocalyptic expectation.

## Location

The Americas — the New World continents "discovered" by Columbus in 1492, stretching from the Arctic to Tierra del Fuego, with particular focus on the United States as the center of 19th-century Adventist denominations [011-015] [011-016]. The Mayan civilization of Yucatan also features as an indigenous apocalyptic tradition [013-007].

## In the Book

### Context

America is referenced across six chapters, primarily in connection with end-of-the-world prophecy movements (Chapter 10, "This Year"), exploration and the Paradise myth (Chapter 11, "Next Year"), and geological/cosmological themes including Martian speculation (Chapter 16, "Earth") [010-058] [011-015] [016-019].

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis treats America as a case study in how eschatological expectation migrates and mutates. Columbus, believing he had found the earthly Paradise east of India, instead discovered a new continent — a mistake that Dennis uses to illustrate the gap between apocalyptic hope and geographical reality [011-015]. The United States becomes the primary theater for Adventist date-setting movements, with Dennis noting how American denominations avoided fixed dates after the Great Disappointment, preferring indefinite expectation [011-016].

> "Columbus died still firm in the belief that he had reached the coast of Asia; the new world turned out to be only America, he had discovered it in mistake for Paradise" [011-015]

> "the Adventist denominations in America who by avoiding fixed dates and substituting an everlasting 'soon' preserve their force and their membership" [011-016]

## Significance

America serves Dennis's broader argument about the cultural embeddedness of apocalyptic thinking. The nation that emerged from a geographical mistake became the world's leading incubator of end-of-the-world sects. Dennis uses this irony to suggest that eschatological certainty is always a kind of category error — mistaking one thing for another. The American case also demonstrates how religious movements adapt after failed predictions, transitioning from date-setting to perpetual expectation [011-016] [010-058].
