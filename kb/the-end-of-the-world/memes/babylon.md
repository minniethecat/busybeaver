# Babylon

The city on the Euphrates that, at its height under Nebuchadnezzar II, was the largest metropolis in the world — and that became, through the experience of the Jewish exile, the archetypal symbol of worldly power destined for [[divine-judgment|divine destruction]]. Dennis treats Babylon as more than a historical city: it is the *source code* of Western eschatology, the place where historical catastrophe was first elevated into a cosmic pattern.

## Location

Babylon was located on the Euphrates River in central Mesopotamia, approximately 85 kilometers south of modern Baghdad. Under Nebuchadnezzar II (reigned 605–562 BCE), it was rebuilt on a monumental scale — the Ishtar Gate, the Hanging Gardens, the great ziggurat Etemenanki (the likely inspiration for the Tower of Babel). It was to Babylon that the Jewish elite were deported after the destruction of [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]] in 586 BCE, and it was in Babylon that the prophets [[daniel|Daniel]] and Ezekiel received their apocalyptic visions.

Dennis invokes Babylon at the intersection of history and symbol. The physical city fell to Cyrus in 539 BCE. The symbolic Babylon — "Babylon the Great," the mother of harlots, the city destined for destruction — has been falling ever since, in every new apocalypse that announces the judgment of worldly power.

## In the Book

### Context

Babylon appears across multiple registers in Dennis's text: as the first city, the source of sacred numerology, the birthplace of Jewish apocalyptic, and the archetype of imperial doom. Each appearance reinforces Dennis's central thesis: that eschatology is born from historical trauma, and that the patterns established in Babylon continue to shape Western expectations of the world's end.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis's most concentrated treatment of Babylon comes in a passage that moves from astronomy to geology to history in a single, sweeping gesture:

> Babylon and Nineveh are under the sand, Arabia Petraea waxes. In the Holiest Land not all the gold of Zionism will make the milk and honey flow again; the waves of the Sahara now greet the waves of the sea and have long engulfed the richest granary of Rome, devouring Rome.

Babylon is the paradigm case of a city "under the sand" — the greatest metropolis of its age reduced to a tell in the desert. Dennis deploys this image to collapse the distance between past and future: what happened to Babylon will happen to Rome, and what happened to Rome will happen to the world. Desertification is not merely an environmental process but an eschatological one — the end of the world, written in sand.

Dennis's second Babylon appears in his discussion of apocalyptic numerology:

> The Great Sacred Number of the Babylonians was 12,960,000, giving a date similar to some yesterday's estimates of the sun's life. Jewish Apocalyptic, a stream from the waters of Babylon, flows from the Captivity to far beyond the Dispersion with a ceaseless glitter of Rabbinical numbers.

Here Babylon is the source not of a city but of a *method* — the practice of calculating the end of the world through sacred numbers. Dennis traces a direct lineage from Babylonian astronomical theology through Jewish apocalyptic to the date-setting prophets of his own day. The numbers change, but the impulse — to read the cosmos as a countdown — originates in Babylon.

### The Symbolic Afterlife

Dennis is keenly aware that "Babylon" escaped its geographical referent long ago. In Christian apocalyptic, the name became a cipher for any worldly power in opposition to God — first Rome, then the Papacy, then whatever empire happened to be current. Dennis notes the irony: the city that fell in 539 BCE has been "falling" ever since, in an endless series of prophetic fulfillments that never quite reach completion.

> city of cities, Babylon the third city, Caput Mundi, then lives the world. When she falls, the world falls.

This identification of city with world — the microcosm whose fate is the macrocosm's — is, for Dennis, the essential move of apocalyptic thinking. Babylon is the first and greatest instance of a pattern that will recur throughout his survey: the local catastrophe that the imagination universalizes into cosmic terminus.

## Significance

Babylon represents, for Dennis, the *origin point* of Western eschatology. It was in Babylon — in the experience of exile, in the visions of Daniel, in the calculations of sacred numbers — that historical trauma was first transformed into a cosmic schema. The pattern established there — empire rises, empire falls, the fall is judgment, judgment is universal — has shaped every subsequent expectation of the world's end for two and a half millennia.
