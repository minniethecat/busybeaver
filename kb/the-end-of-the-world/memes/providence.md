Providence, in Dennis's usage, is not an affirmed theological principle but a skeptical foil — the idea that a world so well-ordered could hardly be destined for catastrophic ruin, introduced only to be questioned.

## Definition

In traditional Christian theology, Providence refers to God's sovereign guidance and care over creation — the belief that the world is not merely set in motion and abandoned but actively sustained and directed toward a good end. Dennis invokes the concept in its popular Enlightenment inflection: the optimistic assumption that the universe is rationally ordered and benevolently governed.

## In the Book

### Context

Providence appears in "Comet"（002）, the chapter surveying historical beliefs about comets as omens of disaster. After cataloguing centuries of comet-panics and their often-fulfilled prophecies of doom, Dennis pauses to consider the philosophical objection — that so much destruction seems incompatible with a well-designed cosmos.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis's single reference to Providence is sardonic. He writes that "A world so perfect and so progressive could not be destined for such harm — Providence would not be so foolish — while of goodness it had enough, without help from the heavens"（002-020）. The sentence is structured as a reassurance that immediately undermines itself. The world *appears* perfect and progressive; Providence *ought* to prevent catastrophe; yet the rest of the book catalogues the many ways it might all end. Providence is presented as wishful thinking — an argument from design that the facts of cosmic violence refuse to honour.
