# Triceratops

**Triceratops** — the "three-horned face" — was one of the last and most abundant of the non-avian [[dinosaur|dinosaurs]], living in the final two million years of the Cretaceous and disappearing at precisely the K-Pg boundary that marks the end of the Mesozoic Era. Its fossils are found in such numbers in the uppermost Cretaceous strata of western North America that it serves as one of the most vivid illustrations of Dennis's extinction thesis: here is a species at the peak of its success, abundant and widespread, and then — nothing.

## Identity

*Triceratops horridus* was a large ceratopsian dinosaur: up to 9 meters long, 3 meters tall at the hips, and weighing 6–12 metric tons. Its three facial horns — one short nasal horn and two long brow horns that could exceed a meter in length — and its massive bony frill made it one of the most heavily armed herbivores in Earth's history. It was the dinosaurian equivalent of a modern rhinoceros, but far larger and more abundantly preserved: hundreds of *Triceratops* skulls have been recovered, making it one of the best-known dinosaurs in the fossil record.

Its abundance in the uppermost Cretaceous — including the Hell Creek Formation, which preserves the K-Pg boundary itself — means that *Triceratops* was thriving right up until the moment of extinction. This is a crucial point for Dennis's argument: extinction is not a gradual fading but often a sudden cutoff, in which healthy, successful populations are eliminated by events beyond their control or comprehension.

## Extinction Narrative

*Triceratops* was present at the very end of the Cretaceous, its fossils found in strata immediately below the iridium-rich K-Pg boundary layer that marks the Chicxulub [[cosmic-collision|impact]]. It did not survive beyond that boundary. The extinction was geologically instantaneous — a point that Dennis, though writing before the impact hypothesis was firmly established, would have recognized as consistent with his emphasis on the suddenness with which worlds can end.

The K-Pg extinction eliminated *Triceratops* along with every other non-avian dinosaur, approximately 75% of all species, and terminated the 170-million-year Mesozoic era in a single catastrophic event. *Triceratops*, abundant and well-defended though it was, could not withstand the collapse of photosynthesis, the collapse of the food web, and the collapse of the climate system that followed the impact. This is extinction as Dennis understood it: not a judgment on the species but a statement about the nature of the universe — that it can, at any moment, produce conditions in which life as we know it cannot persist.
