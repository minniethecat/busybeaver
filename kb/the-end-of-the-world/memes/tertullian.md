Tertullian (c. 155 – c. 220 CE) was a prolific early Christian author from Carthage, often called the father of Latin Christianity. In the book, he is cited as a representative of chiliasm — the belief in a literal thousand-year reign of Christ on earth — before that doctrine was suppressed as heresy.

## Identity

Tertullian was the first Christian writer to produce an extensive body of Latin theological literature. His works include apologetics against paganism (*Apologeticus*), polemics against heresies (*Adversus Marcionem*, *Adversus Praxean*), and treatises on Christian morality. Later in life, he associated with the Montanist movement, a prophetic and ascetic sect that emphasized the imminent end of the world. His chiliasm — the expectation of a literal millennial kingdom — aligns him with the apocalyptic current in early Christianity（010-023）.

## In the Book

### Appearances

Tertullian appears in Chapter 10, in a passage surveying the early Christian chiliasts — those who took the "thousand years" of Revelation 20 as a literal prophecy. Dennis lists him alongside Commodian, Cerinthus, Apollinaris, Julius Africanus, and others as proponents of this view, before describing its eventual suppression（010-023）.

### Role in Dennis's Argument

Dennis uses Tertullian and his fellow chiliasts to illustrate the trajectory of apocalyptic belief within Christianity: from a widely-held, even mainstream, expectation of an imminent earthly millennium, to a doctrine increasingly marginalized and eventually declared heretical. Tertullian represents the "before" in this before-and-after narrative. His chiliasm was not fringe — he was one of the most important Latin theologians of his age. The suppression of chiliasm after Augustine is, in Dennis's telling, a story about institutional self-interest prevailing over scriptural literalism: bishops reconciled to the Roman Empire "found it impolitic, when not impossible, to consider its thousand years a thousand years of sin ending in fire and doom"（010-023）.

> Tertullian was a chiliast, and Commodian; also Cerinthus, Apollinaris, Julius Africanus, Nepos of Egypt, Methodius of Tyre. Many ardent sects, chiefly on the still uncertain borderline between Judaism and Christianity, Ebonists and Montanists and others, made chiliasm their main tenet.（010-023）

## Significance

Tertullian's presence in the book serves two functions. First, he establishes the intellectual respectability of early Christian apocalypticism — chiliasm was not the province of obscure fanatics but of major theologians. Second, the eventual condemnation of chiliasm as heresy exemplifies what Dennis sees as a recurring historical pattern: the domestication of apocalyptic expectation by institutional power. The Church, once it became the Empire's partner, could not sustain the subversive implications of a literal end-time.
