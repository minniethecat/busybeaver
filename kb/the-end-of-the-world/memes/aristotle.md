## Earthquake Theory

The corpus references Aristotle's explanation of earthquakes — that subterranean winds, trapped beneath the earth, cause the ground to tremble when they seek escape — as one of the classical theories of seismic causation:

> This one, delivers Aristotle, brought the Achaean earthquake [002-008]

The passage appears in a discussion of towns and cities that collapsed in catastrophic seismic events. By naming Aristotle, the corpus invokes the authority of ancient natural philosophy while also marking its limits: Aristotle's theory of pneumatic earthquakes was an attempt to explain natural disaster without recourse to divine agency, yet the corpus's own frame remains resolutely [[apocalypse|apocalyptic]] — earthquakes are not merely physical events but signs.

## Among the Greatest Greeks

The corpus places Aristotle alongside his teacher Plato in a brief but definitive appraisal of Greek intellectual achievement:

> yet of the Greeks Plato and Aristotle, the two greatest; the glorious [016-006]

The fragmentary syntax — "the two greatest; the glorious" — suggests a passage that originally enumerated exemplars of human greatness from various nations before arriving at the Greeks. The pairing of Plato and Aristotle as the summit of Greek thought was standard in late antique and medieval doxography; the corpus reproduces this consensus without elaboration.

## Comets and the Sublunary World

Though the corpus does not cite Aristotle's cosmology directly, Aristotle's classification of [[comet|comets]] as sublunary phenomena — atmospheric exhalations rather than celestial bodies — shaped two thousand years of debate about the nature and meaning of cometary apparitions. The corpus registers this tradition indirectly: when it treats the [[comet]] that appeared after the death of [[julius-caesar|Julius Caesar]] [011-010], it participates in a discourse that Aristotle's cosmology had framed — were comets signs in the heavens, or merely vapors in the air? The question was not settled until Tycho Brahe and the seventeenth century.

## Aristotle and Eschatology

While Aristotle's own philosophical system — with its emphasis on the eternity of the world and the unmoved mover — stood in tension with Christian doctrines of creation and [[apocalypse]], his scientific and logical works became foundational to medieval scholasticism. Thomas Aquinas synthesized Aristotelian philosophy with Christian theology, creating the intellectual framework within which many medieval and early modern eschatological debates were conducted. The corpus's very method — assembling evidence, arguing from authorities, cataloguing natural and historical phenomena as signs — owes a debt, however indirect, to the Aristotelian tradition of systematic inquiry. That a work so thoroughly [[apocalypse|apocalyptic]] in content should depend on a method so thoroughly Aristotelian in form is one of the ironies the corpus does not pause to acknowledge.
