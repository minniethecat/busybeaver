# Apollo

The Greek god of the sun, prophecy, music, and knowledge — one of the most complex figures in the Olympian pantheon — whom Dennis cites in his catalogue of solar deities and whose oracle at Delphi embodies the human desire to know the future, especially the date and manner of the world's end.

## Identity

Apollo (also Phoebus Apollo, Phoebus, or Helios in his specifically solar aspect) was the son of Zeus and Leto, twin brother of Artemis. His domains included the sun, prophecy (through his oracle at Delphi), music, healing, and archery. In Dennis's catalogue of solar names, Apollo appears alongside Egyptian, Persian, and Incan solar deities: "Helios, Phaethon, Phoebus Apollo — he has had many names" (006-009). Apollo is significant to Dennis not only as a solar figure but as the god of prophecy — the divine source of knowledge about what is to come.

## In the Book

### Appearances

Apollo appears primarily in Chapter 6 ("Cold") as part of the solar deity catalogue (006-009). But his presence is felt more broadly through the theme of prophecy: the Delphic oracle, Apollo's mouthpiece, was the most famous institution of divination in the ancient world, and Dennis's extensive survey of doomsday prediction is, in a sense, a continuation of the Apollonian project by other means.

### Role in Dennis's Argument

Apollo serves a dual function. As a solar deity, he belongs to the long human tradition of worshipping the sun — and fearing its failure. As the god of prophecy, he embodies the human drive to know the future, a drive Dennis examines at length in his chapters on eschatological prediction. The two functions converge in Dennis's argument: the sun is worshipped because it is the source of life, and its future is anxiously scrutinized because it is known to be finite.

## Significance

Apollo represents, for Dennis, the fusion of solar physics with prophetic psychology. The same god who drives the chariot of the sun across the sky also speaks through the Pythia at Delphi — and the questions put to the oracle were often questions about fate, time, and the end of things. Dennis's survey of doomsday prophecy is, in this sense, a secular continuation of an Apollonian tradition: the attempt to know what cannot be known but must be asked.
