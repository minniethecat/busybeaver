# Ice

The solid phase of water and the agent of planetary refrigeration. Dennis treats ice — in the form of the great ice ages — as evidence that the Earth's climate is inherently unstable, capable of swinging from temperate abundance to frozen wasteland without warning or cause.

## The Ice Age as Precedent

The book invokes the geological record of glaciation as the most compelling evidence that global catastrophe is not hypothetical but recurrent. The "Great Drift, ushering in the last ice age" [002-041] is read as a demonstration that the planet has visited frozen extinction upon its inhabitants before:

> "A greater comet may in time bring back the ice." [002-041]

The "great Thaw that followed the last ice age" [004-010] is treated not as a permanent reprieve but as an interglacial interval — a temporary warming between inevitable freezes. The newer theories of cosmic causation suggest that ice ages are triggered by external events, and what has happened before will happen again.

## Mechanism of the Frozen End

The physical mechanism of ice-driven extinction is straightforward: declining temperatures reduce habitable land area, shorten growing seasons, collapse agriculture, and trigger mass famine. Unlike the instantaneous violence of cometary impact, the ice-end is gradual — a slow strangulation of the biosphere as the freeze line advances toward the equator.

The book's chapter on "Cold" catalogs the various ways that temperature decline could extinguish life: "beneficent bodies bearing carbon and warmth" could cease their passage (002-021), leaving the planet to radiate its remaining heat into space.

## Ice and Fire

In the elemental economy of the corpus, ice is fire's opposite but not its contradiction. Both are terminal states — the difference is only temperature. Whether the world ends in conflagration or glaciation, the result for life is the same. The "choice is thus various" [002-033] but the outcome invariant.

## Ice in the Cosmic Cycle

Ice occupies a distinctive position in the book's temporal architecture: it is not merely a possible future but a confirmed past. The ice ages are not speculation — they are recorded in the drift, the boulders, the fossil record. This evidentiary basis gives the frozen-end scenario a scientific authority that other apocalypse mechanisms lack. Heat death and galactic collision are theoretical extrapolations; glaciation has already happened, repeatedly, and each return was an extinction event for whatever lived in the latitudes it consumed.

Dennis exploits this geological certainty to collapse the distance between past and future. What has happened will happen again — the only variables are when and whether humanity will be present for the next freeze. The "greater comet" that "may in time bring back the ice" (002-041) is not a unique trigger but one of many possible causes for a recurring pattern. The ice does not need a reason to return; it needs a reason to stay away, and the universe offers none.
