# Joseph Wolff

Joseph Wolff (1795–1862), a Jewish-born Christian missionary and millenarian, known for his extensive travels across the Middle East and Central Asia. Dennis mentions him among the date-setters who awaited the end of the world at specific times and places.

## Identity

Born in Germany to a Jewish family, Wolff converted to Christianity and became one of the most widely traveled missionaries of the 19th century. His journeys took him through Persia, India, and Central Asia. A fervent millenarian, he believed in the imminent return of Christ and settled in Jerusalem to await the end（010-060）.

## In the Book

### Appearances

Wolff appears in Dennis's catalog of 19th-century apocalyptic enthusiasts — figures whose predictions of the end were spurred by rivalry and who waited in specific locations for the fulfillment of prophecy. He is listed alongside Harriet Livermore and Captain Saunders as one of several contemporaries who fixed dates and places for the apocalypse（010-060）.

### Role in Dennis's Argument

Wolff exemplifies the 19th-century intensification of apocalyptic expectation — a period Dennis treats with ironic detachment. The specificity of Wolff's waiting "in Jerusalem" underscores the geographical literalism that characterized much millenarian thinking. For Dennis, such figures illustrate the folly of fixing dates for what cannot be known（010-060）.

> Joseph Wolff, of Jerusalem, was waiting for the end in Jerusalem（010-060）

## Significance

Wolff represents the modern, globalized phase of apocalyptic expectation — no longer confined to a single tradition but drawing on missionary networks that spanned continents. His presence in the book connects biblical prophecy to 19th-century millenarianism.
