Emanuel Swedenborg (1688–1772) was a Swedish scientist, philosopher, and mystic theologian whose visions of the spiritual world inspired the Swedenborgian Church. In the book, he is cited — alongside figures like Giordano Bruno and the ancient atomists — as part of a long intellectual tradition that has entertained the possibility of life on other worlds.

## Identity

Swedenborg began his career as a scientist and inventor, making contributions to metallurgy, anatomy, and cosmology. In his mid-fifties, he underwent a spiritual crisis and began reporting visions of heaven, hell, and the spirit world. His major theological works, including *Heaven and Hell* (1758), describe a cosmos in which spiritual realities correspond to material ones and in which multiple worlds may host intelligent life. His followers founded the New Church (Swedenborgianism) after his death（016-005, 016-014）.

## In the Book

### Appearances

Swedenborg appears in Chapter 16, which surveys the history of speculation about plural worlds — the idea that the universe contains many inhabited planets. Dennis names him among the "great poem of great names" that stretches from the Vedas and the pre-Socratics through to modern thinkers（016-005）. He is listed again in a later paragraph discussing religious traditions that are compatible with cosmic pluralism（016-014）.

### Role in Dennis's Argument

Swedenborg contributes to one of the book's most provocative themes: the possibility that the universe contains other intelligent, moral beings, and the implications this has for Christian cosmology. Dennis groups Swedenborgianism with Buddhism and Pantheism as religious outlooks that "if they do not always posit, do not ever exclude material life on other material worlds"（016-014）. Swedenborg is thus positioned as a bridge figure — a Christian mystic whose vision expanded beyond the narrow anthropocentrism of traditional theology.

> The religious temper in general, as distinct from Christian dogma, inclines the other way. God is everywhere, and therefore Man. Buddhism, Swedenborgianism, Pantheism, if they do not always posit, do not ever exclude material life on other material worlds.（016-014）

## Significance

Swedenborg's role in the book is modest but telling. Dennis invokes him as evidence that the question of other worlds — and thus other possible eschatologies — has a long and distinguished intellectual pedigree, one that includes serious theological thinkers as well as scientific speculators. In a book fundamentally concerned with *this* world's end, the gesture toward other worlds serves to relativize and complicate the apocalyptic narrative.
