# Christian

The religious tradition originating in the first-century Jewish apocalyptic movement centered on Jesus of Nazareth, whose expectation of an imminent end of the world Dennis traces from the early Church through medieval Catholicism to Reformation Protestantism as the dominant Western framework for eschatological thought.

## Definition

"Christian" in Dennis's usage refers broadly to the religion, culture, and intellectual tradition that grew from the teachings of Jesus and the apostles, with particular emphasis on its eschatological content. Dennis is not interested in Christian theology as systematic doctrine but in Christianity as a historical force that shaped Western attitudes toward the end of the world — and, reciprocally, was shaped by the repeated failure of its end-time predictions.

The Christian tradition supplies the book with its central narrative template: the world was created, it fell into sin, it will be judged, it will be destroyed and remade. Every variant of this template — the imminence of the early Church, the allegorizing delay of the medieval Church, the numerical precision of the Protestant Reformers — receives sustained attention. Dennis treats Christianity not as divine revelation but as a cultural system whose eschatological expectations reveal deep truths about human psychology.

## In the Book

### Context

Christianity is the gravitational center of the book's historical chapters. Chapter 10 ("This Year") traces Christian prophecy from the apostolic age through the Great Disappointment of 1844. Dennis notes that early Christianity, "still a persecuted creed offering no hope of present triumph, needed some such faith in not too distant punishment" (010-022). The expectation of Christ's imminent return was not a doctrinal ornament but a survival mechanism — the assurance that present suffering would be vindicated by future judgment.

The transition from Judaism to Christianity is presented as a shift in prophetic intensity: "the borderline between Judaism and Christianity, Ebonists and Montanists" (010-023) was also the borderline between deferred and imminent eschatology. Christianity "from the lips of its Founder downward" taught that the end was near, and "earth's speedy judgment filled Christianity from its cradle" (011-007, 011-011).

In Chapter 16 ("God"), Dennis confronts Christian theology directly: "Christianity speaks unequivocally on one point — the finality of judgment" (016-013). The Christian doctrine of eternal punishment, Dennis suggests, is the dark precipitate of the apocalyptic imagination — what remains when the expectation of imminent cosmic justice is stripped of its temporal urgency and projected into eternity.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis's treatment of Christianity is respectful but relentlessly critical. He admires the moral seriousness of Christian eschatology while cataloging its destructive effects: the terror of eternal damnation, the repeated humiliation of failed prophecy, the exploitation of apocalyptic fear by "the orthodox doctrine of the Church, which acted if not with love then with coercion" (016-013). Christianity is simultaneously the book's richest source of material and the tradition it most thoroughly deconstructs.
