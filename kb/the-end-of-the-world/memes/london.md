# London

London appears in Dennis's survey as a theater of urban catastrophe — the Plague of 1665, the Great Fire, and comet-induced public panic — each serving as evidence that city-dwelling populations are particularly susceptible to apocalyptic interpretation of natural disasters.

## Location

Capital of England, situated on the River Thames in southeast England. Dennis references London in its 17th-century context — a rapidly growing early modern city whose density made it vulnerable to plague, fire, and mass panic [002-016] [002-018] [006-020].

## In the Book

### Context

London is referenced in three chapters: as the site of comet-induced panic (Chapter 2, "Comet"), as an example of climatic evidence buried underground (Chapter 6, "Cold"), and as the subject of a Nero-parallel fire (Chapter 10, "This Year") [002-016] [006-020] [010-043].

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis uses London to demonstrate the recurring pattern of urban catastrophe and apocalyptic interpretation. When a comet appeared, Londoners panicked — Queen Elizabeth's court included [002-016]. The Plague of 1665 killed thousands, and the Great Fire of 1666 followed immediately — a conjunction that many interpreted as divine judgment [010-043]. Even the London Underground tunnels serve Dennis as evidence of past climatic warmth, their depth revealing "trees that once waved" [006-020].

> "Queen Elizabeth, who took the comet as a personal sign, though it had 'a beard' — In London" [002-016]

> "The Plague of London in 1665, as the sleeping-sickness following the comet" [002-018]

> "Were not the London tubes, a few yards below our feet, dug through subtropical vegetation?" [006-020]

## Significance

London illustrates Dennis's argument that urban density amplifies apocalyptic psychology. A comet seen by scattered farmers is one thing; a comet seen by a million Londoners produces mass movements. The Plague-Fire sequence of 1665–66 created a template for interpreting disaster-as-judgment that would repeat in later centuries. Dennis uses London to show that the "end of the world" is not only a cosmic event but also a civic one — experienced, narrated, and memorialized within specific urban spaces [010-043] [002-018].
