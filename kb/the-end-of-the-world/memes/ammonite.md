# Ammonite

**Ammonites** were among the most abundant and diverse marine organisms of the Mesozoic Era, their spiral shells accumulating in such numbers that they serve as index fossils for dating Mesozoic rocks worldwide. Their extinction at the K-Pg boundary — simultaneous with that of the [[dinosaur|dinosaurs]] — reinforces Dennis's point that extinction operates across all domains of life, terrestrial and marine alike, and that no abundance, however prodigious, guarantees survival.

## Identity

Ammonites were cephalopod mollusks, related to modern squid, octopus, and the living nautilus. They first appeared in the Devonian Period (~409 million years ago) and diversified explosively through the Mesozoic, producing thousands of species with shells ranging from a few millimeters to over 2 meters in diameter. Their shells — typically planispiral, though some were uncoiled or irregular — are among the most recognizable and abundant fossils in the world, forming vast limestone deposits composed almost entirely of their remains.

The ammonites were extraordinarily successful by any measure: they survived the end-Permian extinction that killed the [[trilobite|trilobites]], recovered and diversified in the Triassic, and became the dominant nektonic predators of Jurassic and Cretaceous seas. Their fossil record is so complete and so well-studied that they provide one of the finest examples of evolutionary change through geological time.

## Extinction Narrative

Ammonites were eliminated at the K-Pg boundary 66 million years ago, the same event that claimed the non-avian [[dinosaur|dinosaurs]] and approximately 75% of all species on Earth. The likely cause — the Chicxulub asteroid [[cosmic-collision|impact]] combined with Deccan Traps volcanism — generated a cascade of environmental effects including global darkness, acid rain, and the collapse of marine food webs. The ammonites, like so many other groups, could not survive the disruption.

The poignancy of the ammonite extinction, from the perspective of Dennis's argument, lies in the contrast between abundance and vulnerability. Ammonites were not rare or marginal; they were everywhere, in staggering numbers. Their shells litter the rocks of the Mesozoic world like a continuous fossil snowfall. And yet, at the K-Pg boundary, they simply stop. The abundance of one moment becomes the silence of the next — and this, Dennis would remind us, is the pattern of all extinction.
