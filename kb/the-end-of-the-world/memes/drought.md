# Drought

The absence of water as a destructive force. Dennis treats drought not merely as a regional disaster but as one pole of the elemental oscillation — flood and drought alternating as the cosmic pendulum — that has repeatedly brought civilizations to their knees and may one day bring the species to its end.

## Definition

Drought is a prolonged period of abnormally low rainfall, leading to a shortage of water. In the corpus, drought is understood as one half of a binary: "Flood certain; drought certain — how far is there parallelism?" [005-035]. The two extremes are not opposites but manifestations of the same underlying cosmic instability.

## In History and Myth

The book notes that while flood narratives are globally distributed, drought lacks an equivalent mythology: "Yet, though there's no drought story spread wide to match the deluge legends" [005-003], its destructive power is no less absolute. The raven's cry that "breaks drought" [005-004] is a survival motif — the promise that water will return.

Drought operates through a chain of cascading failures:

> "He shall fail in his multitudes through drought and following famine." [001-015]

The sequence — drought → crop failure → famine → mass death — is one of the most reliable mechanisms of population collapse in human history. Dennis treats it as a natural analog to divine judgment: impersonal, indifferent, and devastatingly effective.

## As Planetary Threat

The book considers drought not merely as a local catastrophe but as a potential contributor to species-level extinction. The "range of cold and heat, damp and drought, dearth and plenty, is itself a threat" [015-019] — the natural variability of climate, amplified or accelerated, could render the biosphere incapable of sustaining human life.

The corpus links drought to the broader theme of elemental hostility: "death may come through... drought or through many waters" [001-018]. The same cosmos that floods can also dry. There is no safe mean — only oscillation between extremes.

> "Science, who cancels flood by drought and drought by flood, laughs at the idea of a stable middle." [006-002]

## Drought and the Logic of Gradual Extinction

Unlike the sudden violence of cometary impact or the instantaneous rupture of earthquake, drought operates on a temporal scale that makes it uniquely insidious. It is extinction by degrees — the slow withdrawal of the conditions that sustain life, measured not in seconds but in seasons. Dennis positions drought within a broader argument about the diversity of catastrophic timescales: some endings arrive in a flash, others across generations. The drought-end belongs to the latter category, but its gradual nature does not make it less certain. A world that loses its water loses everything, and the geological record shows that worlds do lose their water — by solar evolution, by orbital shift, by the same cosmic accidents that can bring flood instead.

This gradualism also makes drought peculiarly difficult to mythologize. The flood has its narrative arc — forty days and forty nights, the ark, the dove. Drought has no such story, no "drought story spread wide" (005-003), because it lacks the dramatic structure of sudden catastrophe. It is, in Dennis's framing, the quieter apocalypse — the one that arrives without spectacle and leaves without survivors, unnoticed until it is too late.
