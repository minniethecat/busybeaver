The Arctic Circle appears in Dennis's book as a striking illustration of climate change across geological time — a region now frozen that once supported tropical life, including, he claims, the origin of primates.

## Location

The Arctic Circle is the most northerly of the five major circles of latitude, marking the southern boundary of the Arctic region. Its latitude is approximately 66°33′ north of the Equator. In the book, the Arctic is invoked not as a contemporary geographical boundary but as a deep-time paleontological site.

## In the Book

### Context

The Arctic Circle appears in "Cold"（006）, the chapter examining whether the world will ultimately freeze to death. Dennis uses Arctic fossils as evidence that the Earth's climate has undergone radical reversals — warmth where there is now ice, ice where there is now warmth.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis cites two key observations. First, the presence of Arctic fossils in now-temperate Southern France — "the famous reindeer of Southern France" — demonstrates that cold-adapted fauna once roamed much farther south（006-020）. Conversely, tropical fossils in polar regions prove the reverse migration of climate. Second, and most provocatively, he asserts a specific claim about human origins: "The primates, we and the monkeys, originated in the Arctic Circle; Peary found Eden"（006-020）. The chapter "Life"（015） reinforces the theme of climatic endurance, noting that plants have survived epochs of both tropical heat and polar cold, "flourished like the bay tree or Arctic shrub" as conditions demanded（015-019）.

> The primates, we and the monkeys, originated in the Arctic Circle; Peary found Eden.（006-020）

> If Arctic fossils are found embedded in what are now warmer climes, as the famous reindeer of Southern France, tropical fauna and flora are far oftener found in what are now colder regions.（006-020）

## Significance

The Arctic Circle serves Dennis's larger argument about cosmic and climatic mutability. If the Arctic was once a garden, and tropical species once flourished where glaciers now grind, then no earthly climate is permanent — and the cold death he fears is not merely a theoretical possibility but the continuation of a cycle that has already reshaped the planet many times. The Eden-in-the-Arctic image also dramatizes the book's recurring theme of paradise lost to geological time.
