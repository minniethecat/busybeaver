# Space

The vast physical expanse that contains all matter and energy, portrayed in the book as both the crowded thoroughfare of comets and worlds, and the silent void into which planetary atmospheres and stellar energy dissipate.

## Definition

In physics, space is the boundless three-dimensional extent in which objects and events have relative position and direction. Dennis uses "space" in three overlapping senses: (a) outer space — the near-vacuum through which comets, planets, and stars move [007-011] [007-013]; (b) spatial extent — the finite or infinite volume that cosmic bodies occupy and traverse [013-005] [013-018]; and (c) philosophical space — a category of existence contrasted with time and eternity, often found wanting in metaphysical significance [008-012] [009-003] [010-113].

## In the Book

### Context

Space appears throughout the work as the physical medium of cosmic hazard. Comets travel through "the crowded streets of space" [007-011], and their orbital trajectories through the solar system bring them into occasional — and catastrophic — proximity with Earth [002-042] [007-013]. The book also examines space as the ultimate heat sink: planetary atmospheres slowly bleed into it [005-017], stellar energy radiates uselessly across it [003-019], and the entropy of the universe increases as matter and energy disperse into its vastness.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis deploys the metaphor of crowdedness to challenge the intuitive picture of space as empty. "The streets of space are crowded; they are strewn" with debris and wandering bodies [007-015], and the apparent void is an illusion born of scale — at cosmic distances, solid objects appear as negligible points. This crowding has direct eschatological implications: a collision in the "celestial spaces" [007-013] could, and in the book's cosmology periodically does, reset the conditions for life on Earth.

On the philosophical register, Dennis subordinates space to eternity. "Eternity more awful than the [terror] to come, is more terrible than space" [009-003] — space, however vast, remains finite and measurable, whereas eternity transcends spatial categories entirely. This ranking reflects a broader argument that "cause and effect, time and space" form "a vicious round of false notions" [008-012] when taken as ultimate realities rather than contingent features of physical existence.

> "The 'crowded streets of space' are streets of voidness, and yet they are traversed by wanderers whose paths intersect our own with consequences that can be measured in the extinction of whole orders of being." [007-011] [007-013]

> "To forget that infinite space redresses the balance is to misunderstand the scale on which cosmic justice operates." [013-018]
