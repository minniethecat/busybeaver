# War

**War** — in Dennis's assessment of humanity's capacity for self-[[perish|destruction]] — is the **most damning evidence** that the world does not need a comet or a freezing sun to end: human beings are perfectly capable of ending it themselves. "Organized slaughter," as he calls it, "in open battle alone has recently accounted for two million young men in one year, and will do far better — next time" [001-015].

## Overview

War is organized, large-scale armed conflict between states, nations, or factions. Throughout recorded history, war has been among the most reliable causes of mass death, social collapse, and [[human-progress|civilization]]al regression. In Dennis's analysis, war is not simply a historical phenomenon but a **structural feature** of human existence — a recurring [[catastrophe|disaster]] that escalates in scale and lethality with every advance in technology.

## In the Book

### Context

War appears throughout the book, but most prominently in Chapter 1 ("How?") as one of the hazards that make individual survival improbable, in Chapter 2 ("Comet") where comets are omens of coming wars, and in Chapter 11 ("Some [[time|Time]]") as one of the portents that early [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians read as signs of the approaching end.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis treats war as a **signature of the human condition** — not an aberration but a constant. Comets presage war because war is always coming; the omen is reliable not because of celestial magic but because war is statistically inevitable:

> These dire predictions proved usually right. The old magi knew it long ago, Old Moore knows it now: that few are the days which pass by on this planet without some evil happenings somewhere. [002-007]

Dennis catalogs historical wars with grim efficiency: [[julius-caesar|Caesar]] and Pompey's civil wars ("those eighteen Rubicon years of slaughter" [002-011]), the [[perish|destruction]] of [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]] by Titus [002-012], and the First World War — his own generation's [[disaster|catastrophe]] and the proof that "organized slaughter" was not a relic of antiquity but an accelerating modern phenomenon.

> One came and twenty years afterward another, and Mithridates of evil fame and poison-proof bowel was born and twenty years afterward was king. Then destinate Rome. [002-008]

> One appeared to her in 48 BC — to usher in her bloodiest civil wars: Caesar and Pompey and those eighteen Rubicon years of slaughter. [002-011]

In the eschatological framework, war joins famine, [[plague|pestilence]], and earthquake as one of the four classic end-time signs: "nation rising up against nation and kingdom against kingdom" [010-109].

## Significance

War is, for Dennis, the **anthropic mode of world-ending** — the proof that human beings do not require cosmic intervention to bring about their own [[perish|destruction]]. The escalation from swords to artillery to chemical weapons to, prospectively, weapons that could end [[human-progress|civilization]] entirely, is a trajectory that parallels the geological and astronomical [[fate|doom]] scenarios. If the world ends by war, it will be the most **self-inflicted** of all possible [[revelation|apocalypse]]s — and, Dennis implies, perhaps the most fitting.
