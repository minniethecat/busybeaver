# Atlantis

Atlantis is the most symbolically loaded place in Dennis's survey — not merely a mythical island but the geological paradigm for all catastrophic earth-change. Dennis uses it to connect Plato's legend, continental drift theory, and the comet-impact hypothesis into a single argument: worlds do end, and Atlantis is the proof.

## Location

Traditionally placed beyond the Pillars of Hercules (Strait of Gibraltar) in the Atlantic Ocean. Dennis extends the concept to "Gondwanaland, the old great Indian Atlantis" — the southern supercontinent whose breakup he treats as a real geological event of Atlantean scale [005-029] [002-041].

## In the Book

### Context

Atlantis appears in three chapters: as the possible result of comet impact (Chapter 2, "Comet"), as a flood narrative (Chapter 4, "Water"), and as geological evidence in the drought/desert chapter (Chapter 5, "Drought") [002-041] [004-018] [005-029]. Across these appearances, Dennis treats Atlantis not as myth but as geological fact.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis deploys Atlantis as the unifying image for his entire catastrophic geology. A comet "did strike the earth, sinking Atlantis" — the impact theory of extinction becomes a theory of continental loss [002-041]. He asks rhetorically: "Where is Atlantis now?" — the answer being that it is beneath the Atlantic, exactly where geological processes would put it [004-018]. The phrase "the old great Indian Atlantis" for Gondwanaland shows Dennis collapsing myth, geology, and paleontology into a single narrative of destruction and renewal [005-029].

> "a comet did strike the earth, sinking Atlantis, causing the Great Drift" [002-041]

> "Last time flood a hundred thousand years ago? Where is Atlantis now?" [004-018]

> "the old great Indian Atlantis; the aquophobe cycle replied by slowly raising the Himalayas" [005-029]

## Significance

Atlantis is the master metaphor of Dennis's book. Every other catastrophe — flood, fire, comet, ice age, drought — is measured against the Atlantean template of total disappearance. Dennis uses it to argue that what happened to Atlantis (catastrophic sinking) has happened repeatedly in Earth's history and will happen again. The rhetorical question "Where is Atlantis now?" is the book's central challenge: if one world can vanish without trace, why not this one? [002-041] [004-018].
