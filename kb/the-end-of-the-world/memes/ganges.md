The Ganges is a major river of the Indian subcontinent, referenced in Dennis's book primarily in the context of catastrophic flooding and delta formation — illustrating both the threat of sudden meltwater inundation and the geological processes by which rivers build new land.

## Location

The Ganges flows from the Himalayas through northern India into the Bay of Bengal. In the book, it appears in connection with two distinct geographical contexts: as a region threatened by meltwater from Himalayan snowfields, and as a major delta-building river system alongside the Brahmaputra（005-014）.

## In the Book

### Context

The Ganges appears in three chapters — "Water"（004）, "Drought"（005）, and "Cold"（006）— serving different argumentative functions. In "Water", it exemplifies a region vulnerable to catastrophic flooding from solar expansion. In "Drought", it is cited as evidence that rivers ultimately reclaim more land than the sea takes away, part of Dennis's argument against total obliteration.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis uses the Ganges in two contrasting ways. First, as a warning: should the sun grow mightier, Himalayan snowmelt would "torrent" down into the over-peopled Ganges plains, drowning one of the world's most densely populated regions（004-003）. Second, as reassurance: the vast Gangetic delta demonstrates that what mountains lose to erosion is not lost to the Earth — it is re-deposited as fertile land（005-029）.

> from the Himalayas into the over-peopled Ganges plains, torrenting from Everest to Comorin（004-003）

> Ganges and Brahmaputra share a mouth, whose long teeth, every year longer, gnaw southward into the Gulf of Bengal（005-014）

## Significance

The Ganges serves as a double symbol in the book. Geologically, it represents the land-making power of rivers — the Gangetic delta is Dennis's Exhibit A that erosion does not diminish the Earth's total landmass but redistributes it. Apocalyptically, it represents the human vulnerability concentrated in fertile floodplains: the very geography that sustains civilization also places it directly in the path of catastrophic deluge.
