# Flood Myth

The idea of a universal flood as an agent of world-destruction — a concept that Dennis traces across mythology, religion, and science, from Noah's ark to the modern hypothesis of cometary inundation.

## Definition

The flood myth, in Dennis's treatment, is the belief that the world — or at least its living inhabitants — can be destroyed by overwhelming water. Dennis begins Chapter 4 with the stark alternative: "Not fire but flood" (004-001). Historically, this is the most widely distributed end-of-world narrative in human culture: "The races without any flood record are few: only the Negrito and the Fuegian" (004-007). Physically, water could end the world through rising seas, cometary deluge, or the collapse of geological barriers. Psychologically, the flood taps into a primal fear of dissolution — the loss of boundaries, the return to formlessness — that makes it, in Dennis's view, the nightmare underlying all other eschatological nightmares.

## In the Book

### Context

Dennis devotes Chapter 4 ("Water") to the flood as a mode of world-ending, and the concept recurs across half a dozen other chapters. It appears in Chapter 2 ("Comet"), where comets could "produce tidal waves and worldwide floods" (002-031). In Chapter 5 ("Drought"), flood and drought are presented as cosmic twins — opposite modes of the same elemental threat, with "the drought story spread wide to match the flood story" (005-003). In Chapter 6 ("Cold"), "the man of science, who cancels flood by drought and drought by flood, laughs at all fiery threatenings" (006-002) — yet even he cannot cancel the cold. In Chapter 8 ("God"), flood appears as one of the divine instruments: "fire, flood or ice, being supernaturally sent of God" (008-004). And in Chapter 15 ("Man"), Dennis notes the asymmetry: "Flood will not finish the fishes" (015-003), but it will certainly finish humanity.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis's approach to the flood is characteristically multi-register. He does not argue for or against any specific scenario; rather, he catalogues the possibilities and traces the common psychological thread. The flood myth spans every scale: local floods that drown valleys (005-022), catastrophic cometary tsunamis (002-031), and the long geological cycle of marine transgressions that have repeatedly covered continents (004-010).

> The flood-fearers had one advantage over the fire-fearers — their instrument was more familiar. (004-012)

This familiarity is Dennis's key insight. Unlike fire from the sky or stellar collision — events no living human has experienced — floods happen regularly. Everyone who lives near a river or a coast has seen water rise destructively. The flood myth is thus not an exotic speculation but an extrapolation from lived experience: if a local flood can drown a valley, a universal flood could drown the world.

Dennis also explores the moral dimension of the flood narrative. In the biblical and Mesopotamian traditions, the flood is punishment — "the flood before is due reward for a race's wickedness" (015-017). This moral framing transforms the flood from a physical event into a narrative of judgment and redemption, with the ark or the chosen remnant as the point of continuity between the old world and the new. The flood, in this reading, is not an absolute end but a boundary — a death that is also a rebirth.

> For centuries, however, the flood-fear halted until, during the nineteenth century, geology revived it. (004-014)

The scientific revival of the flood hypothesis — through the discovery of ice ages, marine fossils on mountaintops, and evidence of prehistoric inundations — gave new life to an ancient fear. Dennis's treatment of science is consistently that it does not abolish old terrors but gives them new forms.
