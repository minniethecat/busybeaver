# Calvary

Calvary (Golgotha, "the Place of the Skull") is the site outside Jerusalem's walls where Jesus Christ was crucified, and which Dennis invokes as the theological fulcrum around which Christian eschatology turns: the death that, in Christian understanding, secured the possibility of salvation at the world's end.

## Location

Calvary was located outside the walls of first-century Jerusalem, near enough to the city to be accessible to passersby but far enough to serve Rome's purpose of public execution as spectacle and deterrent. The exact location is debated, but the traditional site has been venerated since at least the fourth century within what is now the Church of the Holy Sepulchre.

The name "Calvary" derives from the Latin *Calvariae Locus* (Place of the Skull), translating the Aramaic *Gulgolta* (Golgotha). The origin of the name is uncertain — possibly referring to the shape of the rocky outcrop, possibly to the site's function as a place of execution where skulls accumulated.

## In the Book

### Context

Calvary appears in Dennis's argument primarily through its connection to the cosmic signs reported in the Gospels. At Christ's death, according to Matthew, "the earth did quake, and the rocks rent; and the graves were opened." Dennis notes that comets and other celestial phenomena were reported at the deaths of significant figures — Julius Caesar as well as Christ — and that this tradition of reading cosmic signs in the death of kings and saviors was absorbed into Christian apocalyptic expectation (002-011).

The crucifixion at Calvary is thus, for Dennis, not merely a historical event but a template: the death of the divine figure, marked by cosmic portents, becomes the pattern for every subsequent expectation that the world will end with signs and wonders. Calvary is the first act; the Apocalypse is the final curtain.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis treats Calvary with the same respectful distance he maintains toward all Christian sacred sites. He does not contest the historicity of the crucifixion or deny its theological significance, but he is interested primarily in its function within the apocalyptic imagination: the death that guarantees the end, the sacrifice that ensures the judgment.

> [A]t whose death, perhaps from the selfsame star, both Caesar and Christ dying under cometary portents. (002-011)

## Significance

Calvary's broader significance to the book lies in its role as the hinge between the first and second comings of Christ. Christian eschatology is structured around the interval between Calvary and the Parousia — the time between the death that saved the world and the return that will end it. Dennis's entire survey of prophetic date-setting is, in this sense, a history of attempts to measure the Calvary-to-Apocalypse interval.
