# Greek

The civilization, philosophy, and mythological imagination of ancient Greece, which Dennis treats as the originating intellectual tradition for Western cosmology — the source of both the scientific impulse to understand the heavens and the mythological impulse to narrate the world's end.

## Definition

"Greek" in Dennis's usage encompasses the full span of ancient Hellenic culture: pre-Homeric religion, classical philosophy, Hellenistic science, and the mythological corpus that supplied the Roman and later Christian imagination with its vocabulary of cosmic catastrophe. Dennis draws a sharp typological contrast between Greeks and Romans: the Greeks are "romantic," speculative, intellectually adventurous; the Romans are "sound," practical, and skeptical (016-006). This distinction is not merely aesthetic but argumentative — it maps onto the book's recurring tension between visionary prophecy and empirical restraint.

The Greek intellectual tradition is, for Dennis, the source of nearly every cosmological idea the book surveys. The doctrine of cosmic conflagration (*ekpyrosis*) comes from the Stoics; the atomic theory of cosmic dissolution from Democritus and Epicurus; the astronomical models that made cometary prediction possible from Ptolemy and the Alexandrian school. Even the Christian apocalyptic imagination, Dennis implies, is constructed from Greek intellectual materials — Platonized eschatology, Stoic physics, Pythagorean numerology.

## In the Book

### Context

The Greeks appear most prominently in the early chapters. In Chapter 2 ("Comets"), Dennis contrasts Greek and Roman attitudes toward celestial phenomena: "Such were the Greeks, who unlike the sterner Romans saw in comets not portents of doom but natural phenomena to be classified" (002-015). Here the Greeks represent the nascent scientific spirit — the willingness to observe rather than merely to fear.

In Chapter 3 ("Fire"), Dennis traces the pre-Homeric Greeks as one of the cultures that practiced fire-worship and held doctrines of eventual conflagration: "such as the pre-Homeric Greeks" (003-006). The Greek mythological imagination thus supplies one of the book's primary end-of-world mechanisms — destruction by fire — with its earliest Western articulation.

In Chapter 16 ("God"), Dennis returns to the Greeks in his summary of intellectual history, positioning Plato and Aristotle as the apex of pre-Christian thought: "of the Greeks Plato and Aristotle with their marvelous intellects" (016-006). The Greeks here are not merely historical background but the gold standard of rational inquiry against which all subsequent eschatology is measured.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis's treatment of the Greeks is deeply admiring but not uncritical. He credits Greek philosophy with inventing the conceptual tools his own inquiry deploys — observation, classification, rational inference — while also recognizing that Greek mythology supplied the narrative templates for the apocalyptic imagination he spends the book critiquing. The Greeks are both the disease and the cure: the source of end-of-world mythology and the source of the rationalism that might liberate humanity from it.
