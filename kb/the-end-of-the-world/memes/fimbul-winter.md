Fimbul-Winter (Old Norse: *Fimbulvetr*) is the apocalyptic winter of Norse mythology, referenced in the book as an ancient anticipation of the scientific prediction that the world will end in cold and darkness.

## Definition

In the Norse mythological cycle, Fimbul-Winter is the "Great Winter" that heralds Ragnarok — the twilight of the gods and the destruction of the world. It consists of three consecutive winters without an intervening summer, during which snow drives in from all directions, the sun grows dim, and all warmth abandons the earth. The concept appears primarily in the *Poetic Edda* (*Vafþrúðnismál*) and Snorri Sturluson's *Prose Edda* (*Gylfaginning*).

## In the Book

### Context

Fimbul-Winter appears in "Cold"（006）, the chapter that argues for the freezing death of the Earth as the most scientifically plausible end. Dennis invokes it in the very first passage, as he aligns the scientific consensus with older prophetic traditions.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis places Fimbul-Winter alongside the Hebrew prophetic tradition as parallel foreshadowings of the cold death. He writes that the man of science "is not here in conflict with older prophets: some Hebrew ones — the day of the Lord, cried Amos, it is darkness and not light — and Norse ones, who foretold that Balder god of brightness must die in the end, when Fimbul-Winter will return again and forever"（006-003）. The pairing of Amos and Norse myth is characteristic of Dennis's method: the cold-death hypothesis is not merely a modern scientific conjecture but the latest iteration of an ancient human intuition that darkness and cold are the ultimate fate of all things.

> Nor is he here in conflict with older prophets: some Hebrew ones — the day of the Lord, cried Amos, it is darkness and not light — and Norse ones, who foretold that Balder god of brightness must die in the end, when Fimbul-Winter will return again and forever.（006-003）
