## The Practical Man

The corpus introduces Joshua V. Hines as the foil to [[william-miller|William Miller]], the humble farmer-preacher whose biblical calculations launched the movement:

> William Miller was a poor stick — so thought Elder Joshua V. Hines — a humble illuminé quite unfit to look after the business side, and the brass-band side, of what was already a mighty Movement [010-097]

Where Miller was the visionary, Hines was the organizer. The phrase "business side, and the brass-band side" captures the dual character of his role: he managed both the finances and the publicity — the money and the spectacle — of a movement that swelled to perhaps one hundred thousand adherents across the northeastern United States.

## The Ecstasy of Leadership

The corpus describes Hines as a man who reveled in the movement's energy:

> Hypocrites are fewer than alleged, and deceivers most often self-deceivers; the movement was a splendid one to be in; Joshua derived pathological delight from its excitements, delicious pride from its leadership [010-097]

The characterization is nuanced: Hines is not depicted as a simple fraud. The corpus grants that "hypocrites are fewer than alleged" — that most participants, even the organizers, believed what they proclaimed. Yet the phrase "pathological delight" suggests something more than sincere faith: a pleasure in power and mass excitement that exceeded, and perhaps corrupted, genuine religious conviction.

## The Ascension Robes and the Aftermath

A third reference records a practical — and ultimately embarrassing — suggestion Hines made for the faithful awaiting Christ's return:

> In another place a group waited in white ascension-robes; a suggestion of Elder Joshua's, though when these wedding garments proved ineffective he promptly denied it [010-103]

When Christ did not return on the appointed dates — first in 1843, then again on October 22, 1844, the "Great Disappointment" — Hines, like many leaders, distanced himself from the more theatrical elements of the expectation. The corpus's final word is terse and damning:

> the prophecy was a delusion, William and Joshua deceivers [010-104]

## The Accusation of Booty

The corpus records, in a parenthetical whisper, the most serious charge against Hines:

> (it was whispered) ample booty from the openhandedness of its adherents [010-097]

Whether this accusation was just is impossible to determine from the corpus alone. The text presents it as rumor — "it was whispered" — rather than established fact, leaving the question of Hines's financial conduct suspended between the "delicious pride" of leadership and the possibility of exploitation.
