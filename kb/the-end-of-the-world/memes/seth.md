# Seth

The third son of Adam and Eve, born after the murder of Abel, who figures in Dennis's narrative as the recipient of Adam's firsthand account of Paradise and the ancestor through whom antediluvian memory was transmitted to later generations.

## Identity

Seth appears in Genesis 4:25-26 as the son granted to Adam and Eve in place of Abel, whom Cain had killed. In Jewish and Christian tradition, Seth is the ancestor of Noah and thus of all post-Flood humanity. Extra-biblical traditions — particularly in the pseudepigraphic "Life of Adam and Eve" — elaborate Seth's role as the custodian of Adam's memories of Eden.

Dennis draws on this tradition in Chapter 6, noting that "Adam long afterwards told Seth" (006-023) of the splendors of the primordial world — the light that shone daily, the abundance that preceded the Fall and the Flood. Seth is thus the bridge between the lost paradise and the historical world: the last person to receive direct testimony of Eden from the one who had been expelled from it.

## In the Book

### Appearances

Seth appears once in the corpus, in Chapter 6 ("Cold"), during Dennis's survey of the Edenic past that geological evidence seems to confirm. The reference is brief but thematically significant: Adam's testimony to Seth is presented as a parallel to the geological record, both pointing toward a warmer, more abundant past from which the world has been in steady decline. "Adam long afterwards told Seth, shine daily, all day, delicious" (006-023) — the memory of Paradise preserved in words even as the reality receded into geology.

### Role in Dennis's Argument

Seth serves a minor but telling role in Dennis's larger argument about the relationship between myth and science. The Adam-Seth tradition posits a golden age in the human past, a time of warmth and abundance from which history has been a falling-away. Dennis's geological evidence — the discovery of tropical fossils in the Arctic — seems to confirm this intuition, albeit through entirely different mechanisms. Seth is thus an example of the way mythology and science can converge on the same insight through different paths.

> [A]s Adam long afterwards told Seth, shine daily, all day, delicious. (006-023)

## Significance

Seth's broader significance to the book's themes is modest but real. He represents the human need to preserve memory of a better past — the insistence that the present world is not the best possible world, that something has been lost. This is the psychological foundation of every eschatology that looks backward as well as forward: the end of the world is tragic only because the world was once, or could have been, worth saving.
