# Isaiah

[[judgment]] of the 8th century BCE, whose visions of divine judgment by fire supply some of the most vivid apocalyptic imagery in the biblical canon. Dennis contrasts Isaiah's fiery prophecies with Amos's aquatic imagery.

## Identity

Isaiah ben Amoz prophesied in the Kingdom of Judah during the 8th century BCE, a period of Assyrian expansion and political upheaval. His book is one of the longest prophetic texts in the Hebrew Bible and a foundational source for both Jewish and Christian eschatology. Isaiah's visions of the "Day of the Lord" as a consuming fire shaped centuries of apocalyptic imagination（004-013）.

## In the Book

### Appearances

Isaiah appears in a pointed contrast with the prophet Amos: "Watery Amos counters fiery Isaiah." This single line encapsulates Dennis's sensitivity to the elemental imagery that differentiates prophetic voices — water versus fire, mercy versus judgment（004-013）.

### Role in Dennis's Argument

The contrast between "watery Amos" and "fiery Isaiah" serves Dennis's larger argument about the diversity of prophetic traditions and their competing visions of the end. Isaiah's fire aligns with Dennis's central image of cosmic conflagration, making him an implicit ally in the book's rhetorical architecture（004-013）.

> Watery Amos counters fiery Isaiah（004-013）

## Significance

Isaiah represents the fire tradition in biblical prophecy — the strand most congenial to Dennis's eschatology of conflagration. His presence in the book underscores the deep biblical roots of the idea that the world will end in flame.
