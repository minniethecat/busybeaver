# Cosmos

The totality of physical existence — all matter, energy, space, and time — which in Dennis's argument serves as both the arena of eschatological speculation and the ultimate object of it: not merely what ends, but the question of whether an end is even conceivable for something so vast.

## Definition

Dennis uses "cosmos" to denote the entire physical universe, from the smallest atom to the most distant nebula. His cosmos is a hierarchy of scale: the earth, the solar system, the Milky Way, and beyond — "a thousand million times wider than the trillion-mile pitiful corner seen from Mount Wilson" (017-011). Throughout the book, the cosmos functions as a limit concept: each specific disaster scenario (comet, fire, flood) is local and parochial compared to the cosmic scale, and the question that haunts Dennis's later chapters is whether the cosmos itself — not just the earth or the sun — is subject to terminal decline.

A central tension in the book is between the ancient idea of an infinite, eternal cosmos and the modern scientific picture of a finite, evolving one. In an infinite cosmos, as Dennis acknowledges, "entropy of infinity is a phrase that has no sense" (017-011). But the cosmology of De Sitter and Einstein — which Dennis accepts — compels a bounded universe, and a bounded universe is subject to irreversible processes.

## In the Book

### Context

The cosmos enters Dennis's argument most explicitly in the later chapters, particularly Chapter 8 ("God") and Chapter 17 ("What After?"). In Chapter 8, the cosmos appears as evidence against divine design: "The little world we think we see may have subsisted and subsist forever and forever; the whole cosmos vanish tonight" (008-012). The indifference of cosmic scale to human concerns is a recurring theme.

Chapter 17 is where Dennis grapples most directly with cosmology. Here the cosmos is examined as a physical system governed by thermodynamic law. The question shifts from "how will the world end?" to "can the cosmos itself end, and if so, what does 'end' mean for a totality that includes space and time?" Dennis traces a journey outward from the earth through the solar system, the Milky Way, and the galaxy clusters, noting at each step the fragility and contingency of the structures observed (017-008).

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis's treatment of the cosmos is marked by a distinctive blend of scientific exposition and metaphysical vertigo. He presents the modern cosmological consensus — a finite universe originating in a primordial state and destined for terminal equilibrium — but he cannot resist pressing against its boundaries. What if the cosmos were different? What if we had "one sense more"? Then "the cosmos would be different again, a place now unimagined and unimaginable, though still itself remaining (who knows?) the same cosmos" (017-019).

This speculative vein reaches its peak in the passage on cosmic nightmare: "Yet gravitation may fail tonight, time run backwards, and the whole cosmos, like a nightmare sky, tear asunder or tumble together in hideous confusion and lunatic chaos" (017-032). Here the cosmos is not the stable backdrop of human existence but a precarious, possibly temporary arrangement — a thought that Dennis finds both terrifying and intellectually exhilarating.

Yet the book ends on a note of qualified optimism about the cosmic process itself. Despite the inevitability of local deaths — of species, of worlds, of suns — the cosmos as a whole has demonstrated a directional tendency: "From shapes inconceivable, lower than matter, to matter; from matter to life, life to mind, mind to man, the cosmos has walked onwards and worked upwards" (017-043). Whether this upward walk can continue indefinitely, or whether it too must eventually succumb to entropy, is the question Dennis leaves unresolved.

> That whole cosmos of theirs, which embraces the uttermost nebulae, which sweeps a thousand million times wider than the trillion-mile pitiful corner seen from Mount Wilson, around which light, swiftest of all things, takes a hundred thousand centuries to travel — it is but a bubble enwombed in the ether. (017-011)
