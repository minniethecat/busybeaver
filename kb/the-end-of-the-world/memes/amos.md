# Amos

The Hebrew prophet of the eighth century BCE, a shepherd from Tekoa, whose stark vision of divine judgment — "darkness and not light" — Dennis invokes as the biblical antecedent to the scientific forecast of universal cold and darkness.

## Identity

Amos was a prophet active in the northern Kingdom of Israel during the reign of Jeroboam II (c. 786–746 BCE). Unlike the court prophets of his time, Amos was a herdsman and a dresser of sycamore trees, called from rural obscurity to deliver an uncompromising message of judgment. His book — one of the earliest of the prophetic writings — denounces social injustice and religious complacency, warning that "the day of the Lord" will not be the triumph his audience expects but a catastrophe.

## In the Book

### Appearances

Amos appears in three distinct contexts in Dennis's book. In Chapter 4 ("Water"), he is set against Isaiah as the prophet of water versus fire: "Watery Amos counters fiery Isaiah" (004-013). In Chapter 6 ("Cold"), Dennis quotes his signature line — "the day of the Lord, cried Amos, it is darkness and not light" (006-003) — to demonstrate that the scientific forecast of a dark, frozen earth is not in conflict with prophetic tradition but in continuity with it. And in Chapter 10 ("This Year"), Dennis notes that Amos was dismissed by some contemporaries as "Crazy Amos, the local drunk" (010-103), a reminder that prophets are rarely honored in their own time.

### Role in Dennis's Argument

Amos serves Dennis as evidence of a recurring pattern: the convergence of scientific and prophetic imagination on the same terminal images. The physicist who predicts heat death and the shepherd who foresaw divine darkness are describing, in different idioms, the same end. Dennis does not claim that Amos knew thermodynamics; he claims that the human mind, confronted with the possibility of total annihilation, independently arrives at images of cold, darkness, and silence.

> The day of the Lord, cried Amos, it is darkness and not light. (006-003)

This single line does enormous work in Dennis's argument. It bridges the Hebrew Bible and modern cosmology, prophecy and physics, ancient terror and modern certainty. Amos becomes, in Dennis's reading, not a primitive precursor to science but a parallel witness — someone who, using the conceptual tools of his own tradition, arrived at the same bleak conclusion that the most sophisticated instruments of the twentieth century would confirm.

## Significance

Amos represents the prophetic tradition at its starkest — no consolation, no redemption, just darkness. Dennis values him for the same reason the scientist values cold hard data: he does not flinch from the worst. The "day of the Lord" as darkness and not light is, in Dennis's hands, the theological equivalent of the Wärmetod — a terminal state with no remainder, no sequel, and no appeal.
