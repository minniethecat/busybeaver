## Burial Not Burning

The corpus names Adam as the archetypal example of inhumation — burial rather than cremation — in a passage defending the Christian practice of interring the dead:

> Many great names had burial not burning: the first Adam, who was inhumed on Calvary [003-007]

This reference draws on an extracanonical Christian tradition that Adam's body was buried at Golgotha — the very hill where Christ ("the Second Adam") was crucified. The typological symmetry is deliberate: the first Adam's bones rest beneath the cross of the Second, so that the blood of redemption literally falls upon the dust from which the first man was taken. The corpus places this tradition alongside the burials of other great figures to argue that inhumation, not cremation, is the proper Christian practice.

## Adam and His Sons

The corpus references Adam in connection with two of his sons. Of Abel, whose murder by Cain made him the first human to die, the text notes:

> He taught Adam bury Abel, eldest of burials [005-004]

The passage presents burial itself as a divine instruction — God teaching Adam how to lay his murdered son to rest, establishing the practice that would become universal. To Seth, Adam transmitted knowledge of the primordial world:

> did not the sun, as Adam long afterwards told Seth, shine daily, all day, deliciously? [006-023]

This passage invokes a tradition preserved in apocryphal literature (the *Life of Adam and Eve* and related texts) in which the dying Adam sends Seth back toward Eden to recount the glories of the prelapsarian world. The "delicious" sunlight of Adam's memory is the light of an unfallen creation.

## The Stature of the First Man

In a passage questioning the literal truth of biblical claims about primordial humanity, the corpus asks:

> If Adam was not the largest man, nor all the old Rabbinical estimates true, nor only giants in the land in those days, nor all men more splendid [015-015]

The reference gathers several traditions: the rabbinic speculation that Adam's original stature filled the cosmos (itself reduced after the Fall), the antediluvian giants of Genesis 6:4, and the general belief that the earliest humans were physically superior to their descendants. The corpus treats these claims skeptically — asking what remains of biblical authority if the "literal and inspired untruth of every word" is conceded — but records the traditions nonetheless.
