# Balder

Balder (Old Norse Baldr), the Norse god of brightness, beauty, and goodness, whose prophesied death is one of the events that heralds Ragnarok — the end of the world in Norse mythology — appears in Dennis's survey as an example of the pre-Christian eschatological imagination.

## Identity

Balder was the son of Odin and Frigg, beloved by all the gods for his beauty and goodness. According to the Norse myths preserved in the Prose Edda and Poetic Edda, Balder began to have dreams foretelling his death. His mother Frigg extracted an oath from every thing in creation — every plant, every stone, every metal — not to harm him. Only the mistletoe, deemed too young and insignificant to swear an oath, was overlooked.

Loki, the trickster god, learned of this vulnerability and fashioned a dart of mistletoe. At a gathering of the gods, where they amused themselves by throwing weapons at the now-invulnerable Balder, Loki guided the blind god Hodr to throw the mistletoe dart, which killed Balder instantly. The death of Balder is one of the precipitating events of Ragnarok — the Norse apocalypse in which the gods themselves are destroyed and the world is consumed in fire and flood.

## In the Book

### Appearances

Balder appears once in the corpus, in Chapter 6 ("Cold"), during Dennis's survey of mythological end-of-world traditions: "Norse ones, who foretold that Balder god of brightness must die in the last days" (006-003). The reference is brief but thematically significant — Dennis presents the Norse eschatology as one of several pagan traditions that parallel Christian apocalyptic, suggesting that the expectation of a dying god and a dying world is a human universal rather than a Christian peculiarity.

### Role in Dennis's Argument

Balder serves Dennis as a data point in his argument for the universality of eschatological thinking. The Norse myths are one of several non-Christian traditions he cites to demonstrate that the expectation of a world-ending catastrophe — often triggered by the death of a divine or semi-divine figure — is not unique to the Judeo-Christian tradition. Every culture, Dennis implies, tells the same story in different idioms: a god dies, and the world dies with him.

> Norse ones, who foretold that Balder god of brightness must die in the last days. (006-003)

## Significance

Balder's broader significance to the book lies in his typological relationship to Christ: a divine figure whose death is foretold, whose death is accomplished through betrayal, and whose death heralds cosmic catastrophe. The parallel is not exact — Balder does not rise, and Ragnarok is not a judgment but a cycle — but the structural similarity is part of Dennis's larger argument that eschatology is a universal human language, spoken in different mythological dialects.
