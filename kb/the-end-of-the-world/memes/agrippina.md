## The Cry of Agrippina

The corpus presents Agrippina in a single, visceral line within a long passage cataloguing the horrors of Nero's reign. When Nero came to examine his mother's corpse after having her killed, the dead body was said to cry out:

> "Strike the womb which bore Nero!" cried Agrippina, the dead womb he came to peer at and to mock [011-010]

This passage forms part of a longer indictment of the Roman imperial order — famines, plagues, [[earthquake|earthquakes]], and the slaughter of the legions — that the corpus frames as signs of cosmic crisis preceding the destruction of [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]] and the triumph of early Christianity.

## The Quadruple Crown of Nero

Agrippina's murder was one of four killings that earned Nero what the corpus describes as the "quadruple tiara" of crimes:

> the quadruple tiara of prolicide, uxoricide, fratricide, matricide — Britannicus, Octavia, Agrippina, Poppaea — after these he aspired to deicide also when he turned on Christ in the person of His Saints [011-010]

The sequence is methodical: Nero murdered his brother Britannicus, beheaded his wife Octavia and sent her head to [[poppaea|Poppaea]], killed his mother Agrippina, and then kicked his pregnant wife Poppaea to death. Only after extinguishing his own bloodline did he turn outward, torturing and executing [[christian|Christians]] in public spectacles — a pivot that early Christian writers seized upon as evidence that Nero was a type of the [[antichrist|Antichrist]].

## Agrippina and the Julio-Claudian Dynasty

Agrippina belonged to the [[julio-claudian|Julio-Claudian]] dynasty, the first imperial family of [[rome|Rome]]. She was the sister of Emperor Caligula, the wife (and niece) of Emperor Claudius, and the mother of Nero — whom she positioned for the succession by persuading Claudius to adopt him. After Claudius died (possibly poisoned on Agrippina's orders), the seventeen-year-old Nero ascended the throne in 54 CE, with Agrippina initially dominating the court as regent. Within five years, Nero had grown tired of her influence and ordered her execution. The historian Tacitus records that when the assassins came, Agrippina pointed to her womb and said, "Strike here" — an account that the corpus condenses into the single, electrifying cry recorded at [011-010].

## In Early Christian Apocalyptic

The corpus draws Agrippina into the eschatological frame of early Christian thought. Nero's domestic slaughters — of which the matricide was the centerpiece — were read by Christian writers as an inversion of natural order that presaged cosmic dissolution. The dead mother crying out from the corpse became, in the corpus's treatment, a sign that the created order itself was groaning toward [[judgment]].
