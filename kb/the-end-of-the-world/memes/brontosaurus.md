# Brontosaurus

The **Brontosaurus** — whose very name means "thunder lizard" — was one of the largest terrestrial animals in Earth's history, a creature of such immense scale that its footsteps would have literally shaken the ground. In the context of Dennis's argument about [[organic-extinction|extinction]], the brontosaurus embodies the same paradox as [[tyrannosaurus-rex|T. rex]]: that size, strength, and apparent invincibility count for nothing when the world itself changes.

## Identity

*Brontosaurus excelsus* was a sauropod dinosaur of the Late Jurassic Period, approximately 156 to 146 million years ago, in what is now western North America. Adults reached lengths of 22 meters and weights of 15–20 metric tons, supported by four pillar-like legs and balanced by an extraordinarily long neck and tail. For most of the 20th century, paleontologists considered *Brontosaurus* synonymous with *Apatosaurus*, but a comprehensive 2015 revision restored it as a distinct genus.

Like all sauropods, *Brontosaurus* was herbivorous, using its long neck to browse vegetation at heights unreachable by other herbivores. Its immense size — a classic example of the evolutionary trend toward gigantism in sauropods — was its primary defense against predators. No predator of its era, not even the large theropods that hunted in packs, would have easily threatened a healthy adult.

## Extinction Narrative

The brontosaurus lineage disappeared at the end of the Jurassic, approximately 146 million years ago, though other sauropods persisted through the Cretaceous until the K-Pg [[cosmic-collision|event]] 66 million years ago. The causes of the Jurassic-Cretaceous transition extinctions remain debated — likely a combination of climate change, sea-level fluctuation, and ecological restructuring — but the pattern is consistent with Dennis's analysis: no adaptive strategy is permanent, no niche is secure.

Dennis does not name *Brontosaurus* in his text, but his treatment of the [[dinosaur|dinosaurs]] as a collective case study in natural extinction applies with full force. The brontosaurus is, if anything, the most striking illustration of his theme: a creature so large that its very existence seems like a challenge to the laws of biology, yet so thoroughly vanished that for most of human history we did not even know it had lived. The "thunder lizard" is silent now — and Dennis would have us ask: what will be silent after us?
