# Mount Wilson Observatory

The astronomical observatory in the San Gabriel Mountains of California, whose 100-inch Hooker telescope — the most powerful in the world when Dennis wrote — provided the observational evidence for the expanding universe and the finite cosmos that underpin his thermodynamic argument.

## Location

Mount Wilson Observatory sits at an elevation of 1,740 meters in the San Gabriel Mountains, above Pasadena, California. It was founded in 1904 by George Ellery Hale and for decades housed the world's largest telescopes. The 100-inch Hooker telescope (completed 1917) was the instrument through which Edwin Hubble discovered the expansion of the universe — the observational foundation of modern cosmology and, by implication, of Dennis's argument about cosmic finitude.

## In the Book

### Context

Mount Wilson appears twice in Dennis's text, both times in Chapter 17 ("What After?"). It is the vantage point from which the scale of the visible universe is measured: "from Mount Wilson, through the great telescope there, they behold stars which shine, or rather once shone, one hundred and forty million light-years away, and behold two million extragalactic nebulae" (017-008). The observatory is the modern counterpart to the ancient temples catalogued in Chapter 6 — a site of cosmic contemplation, but one that uses lenses rather than liturgy.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis uses Mount Wilson as a rhetorical device: the telescope becomes a symbol of the vertigo-inducing scale of modern cosmology. "Though from Mount Wilson, through the great telescope there, they behold stars which shine... yet no such series of wandering miles and wearying zeros attains infinity" (017-008). The observatory reveals the immensity of the universe — and simultaneously reveals its finitude.

> From Mount Wilson, through the great telescope there, they behold stars which shine, or rather once shone, one hundred and forty million light-years away, and behold two million extragalactic nebulae, a fraction of those ere long to be revealed. (017-008)

## Significance

Mount Wilson is the modern scientific counterpart to the sacred sites Dennis catalogues earlier — not a place of worship but a place of observation, whose instruments reveal a cosmos vaster than any ancient temple could conceive, yet finite, and therefore mortal.
