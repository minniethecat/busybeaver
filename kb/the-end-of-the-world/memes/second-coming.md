# Second Coming

The Second Coming (Parousia) is the event around which Christian eschatology is organized — the promised return of Christ "in power and great glory" to judge the living and the dead, inaugurate the Millennium, and terminate the present world order. In Geoffrey Dennis's *The End of the World*, the Second Coming is the master expectation that generates nineteen centuries of subordinate expectations, disappointments, and recalibrations.

## Definition

The term Parousia (Greek παρουσία, "presence" or "arrival") appears in the New Testament primarily in the Pauline epistles and the Synoptic Gospels. In Christian theology it denotes the visible, bodily return of Jesus Christ at the end of the age, distinguished from the First Coming (the Incarnation) and from the various modes of spiritual presence claimed by different traditions. Dennis's interest is not theological but historical: the Second Coming is the promise whose perceived imminence drove the early Church's missionary urgency, whose deferral forced the development of institutional Christianity, and whose timing has been the subject of continuous reinterpretation from the apostolic age to the present.

## In the Book

### Context

Dennis engages the Second Coming across three sustained passages in Chapter 10 ("This Year") and Chapter 11 ("Next Year"). His treatment follows a chronological arc: from the apostolic generation's literal expectation, through the institutional Church's strategic reinterpretation, to the modern critical scholarship that reads the eschatological sayings as culturally conditioned.

The earliest phase is the most poignant. "When the Twelve had gone to their expectation above without seeing it realized here below; when Paul of Tarsus, his imminent hope fast dwindling through his Epistles, had been martyred for the Lord Whose Second Coming, like the First, he saw not" (010-005). Dennis captures in a single sentence the cognitive dissonance of the apostolic generation: they had staked everything on an imminent return, and they died without seeing it.

The second phase is institutional. As Christianity grew from a persecuted sect to a state religion, the Second Coming was reframed from an imminent hope to a deferred reward. For the persecuted, millenarianism offered "not too distant punishment, and not too distant reward and joy" — the end of the world "only as incidental to the Parousia, the Second Coming, the New Earth" (010-022). The Christians "grew rapidly" on this promise, but as Dennis implies, the growth brought institutional pressures that diluted the original expectation.

### Dennis's Treatment

In Chapter 11, Dennis surveys the interpretive history with characteristic erudition. He notes that Jesus was "an unlettered Jew" who "merely took over the popular eschatology of Jewish Apocalyptic, adding a special niche for Himself as hero of the Second Coming" — or, alternatively, that He was the "Son of God" who "foretold the end as men now know it will be" (011-008). Dennis refuses to adjudicate between these readings, but he observes a curious pattern in the gospels themselves: the eschatological sayings "are most frequent, most physical and most fearful in the first gospel, fewest and least disastrous in the last." This "graduated rarefying of the catastrophic atmosphere" corresponds to what is known of the four evangelists — "from Matthew the orthodox Hebrew through practical Mark and professional Luke to John the Hellenized philosopher" (011-008).

The observation is Dennis at his most subtle: he does not say the Second Coming is a fiction, but he shows how its textual representation evolves over time, becoming less catastrophic, less imminent, less literal, as the Church moves from Jewish apocalypticism to Hellenistic philosophy.

> When the Twelve had gone to their expectation above without seeing it realized here below; when Paul of Tarsus, his imminent hope fast dwindling through his Epistles, had been martyred for the Lord Whose Second Coming, like the First, he saw not. (010-005)

> The end of the world — surely. But only as incidental to the Parousia, the Second Coming, the New Earth, and Christ's millennial reign upon it great and glorious, which only His children would share. (010-022)

> This graduated rarefying of the catastrophic atmosphere corresponds with oddest exactitude to what little is known of the four evangelists, from Matthew the orthodox Hebrew through practical Mark and professional Luke to John the Hellenized philosopher. (011-008)
