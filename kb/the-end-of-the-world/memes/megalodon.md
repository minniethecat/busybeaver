# Megalodon

**Otodus megalodon** — commonly called megalodon — was the largest predatory fish ever to inhabit Earth's oceans, a shark of such size that its teeth alone are larger than a human hand. Its extinction approximately 3.6 million years ago, while geologically more recent than the [[dinosaur|dinosaurs]], is no less instructive for Dennis's thesis: supremacy in one era is no guarantee of a place in the next.

## Identity

*Otodus megalodon* dominated the world's oceans from the early Miocene (~23 million years ago) through the late Pliocene (~3.6 million years ago), a reign of nearly 20 million years. Estimates of its size vary, but most paleontologists agree on a length of 15–18 meters — roughly three times the length of the largest recorded great white shark — with a body mass of up to 50 metric tons. Its teeth, which could exceed 18 centimeters in height, are its most abundant fossil remains and give the species its scientific name: "megalodon" means "big tooth."

As an apex predator, *megalodon* preyed on whales, seals, sea turtles, and large fish, using its massive jaws — with an estimated bite force of up to 180,000 newtons, the most powerful of any known animal — to crush the bones and shells of its prey. It was, in ecological terms, the unquestioned ruler of the Miocene and Pliocene seas.

## Extinction Narrative

*Megalodon* went extinct approximately 3.6 million years ago, at a time of significant global change. The formation of the Isthmus of Panama altered ocean circulation patterns, the onset of Northern Hemisphere glaciation cooled ocean temperatures, and many of the large whale species on which *megalodon* depended declined or shifted their ranges. Competition from emerging predators — including the ancestors of the modern great white shark and the killer whale — may have compounded these pressures.

As with the [[smilodon|saber-toothed cat]] on land, the extinction of *megalodon* illustrates the vulnerability of apex predators to ecosystem disruption. A predator cannot survive without sufficient prey, and prey cannot survive without a stable environment. The collapse of one link in the chain — whether caused by climate change, oceanographic shifts, or competition — reverberates upward, until even the most powerful hunter in Earth's history succumbs. This is Dennis's extinction logic applied to the marine realm.
