# Tribulation

In Geoffrey Dennis's *The End of the World*, tribulation is the experience that makes apocalyptic expectation plausible — the suffering that convinces each generation that its own hour must be the final one.

## Definition

Tribulation (Latin *tribulatio*, from *tribulum*, a threshing-sledge) carries the sense of being crushed or ground down. In the New Testament, the "Great Tribulation" (*thlipsis megale*) of Matthew 24:21 and Revelation 7:14 is the period of unparalleled suffering that precedes the return of Christ. Dennis uses the term in a more capacious sense: his subject is not only the specific biblical prophecy but the universal human experience of historical catastrophe, and the equally universal tendency to interpret that experience as eschatologically significant.

## In the Book

### Context

Dennis's sole explicit use of "tribulation" occurs in Chapter 2 ("Comet"), in a passage about the comet of 43 BCE — interpreted by the Romans as the soul of Julius Caesar ascending to heaven. The comet appeared during the civil wars that ended the Roman Republic: "Caesar and Pompey and those eighteen Rubicon years of slaughter" (002-011). The comet's message, as Dennis reports it, was that "with Julius gone she must accomplish her days of tribulation."

The passage is brief but it establishes a pattern that runs through the entire book: a celestial sign (comet) coincides with historical catastrophe (civil war), and the coincidence is interpreted as cosmic endorsement of a political transition (Caesar's deification). The tribulation is real — eighteen years of slaughter — but its meaning is constructed after the fact by the victors. "Artful Augustus was well pleased, for if Julius were a god then his murderers were not homicides but deicides: a notion most helpful to the policy of artful Augustus" (002-011).

### Dennis's Treatment

Though Dennis uses the word only once, the concept of tribulation pervades the book. Every chapter catalogs suffering — ice ages, floods, fires, plagues, wars, sieges — that historical actors experienced as the Great Tribulation. Dennis's method is to let the accumulation of these episodes make his point: if every generation believes its tribulations are the Tribulation, then the concept loses its predictive force and becomes simply a name for history itself.

> to usher in her bloodiest civil wars: Caesar and Pompey and those eighteen Rubicon years of slaughter. The proud visitant of 43 was Caesar's own soul, triumphing through the worlds, ranging through heaven after reigning on earth, come back to tell Rome that with Julius gone she must accomplish her days of tribulation. (002-011)
