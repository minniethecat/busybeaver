# Mars

The red planet, fourth from the sun, whose cold, thin atmosphere and barren surface Dennis glances at as a possible preview of the earth's distant future — a warning, written in rust, of what happens when a planet loses its air and water.

## Location

Mars orbits the sun at an average distance of 228 million kilometers, roughly 1.5 times earth's distance. It is about half the diameter of earth, with a surface area roughly equal to earth's land area. Its atmosphere is less than 1% as dense as earth's and consists primarily of carbon dioxide. Surface temperatures average about −60°C. Liquid water cannot exist on its surface today, though geological evidence indicates it once did. Dennis references Mars in Chapter 15 ("Man") in the context of earth's long-term habitability: "the round earth, if discoloured a little for lunar and Martian eyes, all-unperturbed in her course" (015-001).

## In the Book

### Context

Mars appears primarily as an astronomical neighbor — one of the "lunar and Martian eyes" (015-001) that would observe the earth continuing in its orbit long after life has vanished from its surface. Dennis's point is that the earth, like Mars, will one day be a dead planet — a tomb travelling through space, indistinguishable from its barren neighbors.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis does not develop Mars as a major theme, but its brief appearances are telling. Mars is what earth will become: a planet whose atmosphere has thinned, whose water has vanished, whose surface is cold and sterile. The red planet is not an alien world in Dennis's vision but a mirror — a glimpse of our own future.

## Significance

Mars serves Dennis as a memento mori at the planetary scale. If the solar system contains one dead world, it can contain two. The processes that stripped Mars of its atmosphere and water are not unique; they are general. What happened there will, in time, happen here.
