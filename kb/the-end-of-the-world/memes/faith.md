# Faith

The disposition to believe in the unseen and unproven, treated by Dennis as the foundational posture of all religious eschatology — at once the most human and the most dangerous of epistemic stances, since it enables both moral courage and catastrophic self-deception.

## Definition

Faith is strong belief in a doctrine or set of principles, typically religious, based on spiritual apprehension rather than empirical proof. In Christian theology, faith (pistis) is the first of the three theological virtues, the ground from which hope and charity grow. Dennis examines faith from two angles: (a) as a psychological phenomenon — the human capacity to maintain conviction in the absence of evidence, including the "faith in man's ultimate destiny" [012-003] that persists despite all contrary indications; and (b) as a historical force — the driver of apocalyptic movements, persecutions, and the formation of religious institutions from "the faithful everywhere" [002-012] to the modern fundamentalists "of all faiths and ages" [008-012].

## In the Book

### Context

Faith is most intensively examined in the theological and historical chapters, particularly in the discussion of how early Christian eschatology took shape under conditions of persecution. The book observes that a "persecuted creed offering no hope of present triumph needed some such faith in not too distant punishment" of its enemies [010-022] — faith, in this analysis, is not a pure spiritual impulse but a social-psychological adaptation to powerlessness. The faithful "grasped gladly" at signs and portents [010-105], filling the gap between present suffering and promised vindication with ever more elaborate doctrinal constructions.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis's stance toward faith is the most unambiguously critical of any position he takes toward the theological virtues. Where love receives a measure of sympathetic treatment, and hope is dismissed with irony, faith is subjected to something closer to outright indictment. "Fear prompted this faith. Man dreaded the end; therefore he believed it would not come" [011-003] — the genealogical argument that faith is born of terror rather than revelation. The Zarathustrians "holding that fire was God" [003-006] are not presented as admirable for their devotion but as exemplars of a universal human tendency to sacralize what one fears.

The book's most devastating line on faith comes near its end: "No trick, no faith, can assuage; can alter, that sentence of death" [017-050]. Faith, whatever its psychological consolations, does not change the facts. The cosmic processes that will end all life are indifferent to human belief. "Christ is dead in His heaven" [015-022] — the death of faith is itself an eschatological event, the collapse of the one framework that promised meaning beyond the terminus.

> "Fear prompted this faith. Man dreaded the end; therefore he believed it would not come — or that if it came, it would come as judgment, as meaning, as something other than the blind collision of indifferent matter." [011-003]

> "For triumph faith needs the torch." [003-003]
