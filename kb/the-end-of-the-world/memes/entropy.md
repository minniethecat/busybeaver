# Entropy

The physical principle of irreversible energy degradation — from usable form to heat, from heat to equilibrium — which, in Dennis's argument, guarantees that the universe must eventually reach a state of total stillness: the calorific death, or *Wärmetod*.

## Definition

Entropy, as used by Dennis, means the universal process by which energy is progressively and irreversibly converted into heat, and heat passes irreversibly from warmer to colder bodies (017-002). The consequence is the "final equalization of temperature" — a state in which all free energy is exhausted, no transmission is possible, and the universe reaches a motionless, soundless, lightless equilibrium (017-003). Dennis draws explicitly on the Second Law of Thermodynamics, but extends the concept beyond physics into a cosmic philosophical principle: the entropy of matter is also the entropy of spirit (017-044).

## In the Book

### Context

Entropy enters Dennis's argument most fully in Chapter 17 ("What After?"), where he considers what remains after all possible modes of world-ending have run their course. The preceding chapters catalogue individual catastrophes — comet, fire, water, drought, cold, crash, divine intervention — but entropy transcends them all because it is not one agent among others. It is a law: the underlying arrow of all cosmic change (017-005). Even the sun itself, which Dennis explores in detail in Chapter 1 ("How?") and Chapter 6 ("Cold"), is a temporary concentration of energy destined to dissipate by this same principle.

The concept also appears in Chapter 3 ("Fire"), where the "non-solar prophecies of calorific end" are invoked: fire is a localized, parochial version of entropy's universal scope (003-035). The "calorific death" — a phrase Dennis borrows from nineteenth-century physics — thus runs through the book as the ultimate horizon against which all other ends must be judged.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis treats entropy as both a scientific inevitability and a philosophical problem. He presents the thermodynamic argument straightforwardly: "In the end no lucid atoms will be left; the transmission of energy will have ceased; entropy, the final equalization of temperature, will be accomplished; the clock will have run down" (017-003). The image of the clock running down — borrowed from classical mechanics — domesticates the cosmic scale and makes it felt.

But Dennis does not leave the matter at scientific finality. He stages an inner drama of resistance. First, the mind rebels: could cyclic collisions release fresh energy? "They are mere accidents, incidents, powerless to stem or deflect the stream ineluctable of sidereal evolution" (017-005). Then, more radically, he appeals to infinity: if space were infinite, the concept of universal death would lose meaning — how can something that has no boundaries reach an end? "And entropy of infinity is a phrase that has no sense" (017-011). This rebellion, however, collapses under the weight of the finite universe revealed by modern cosmology. De Sitter and Einstein compel a bounded cosmos, and to a bounded cosmos, entropy is irreversible (017-009).

> The Universe shall not die! Space shall be infinite. That whole cosmos of theirs ... it is but a bubble enwombed in the ether, in empty ether stretching out to infinity. And entropy of infinity is a phrase that has no sense — no, not even in transcendental physics. (017-011)

Finally — and this is Dennis's most original move — he extends entropy into the psychical realm: "spirit and matter being different aspects of the same thing, same movement, same finite contents — the law of death and degradation must apply to both. Physical entropy will include psychical entropy, matter-death spirit-death" (017-044). This is the *Wärmetod* of souls: the suggestion that not only bodies and stars, but mind and will themselves, run down.
