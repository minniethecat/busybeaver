# Millerite

The Millerite movement was the mass religious phenomenon that coalesced around William Miller's prediction that Christ would return on October 22, 1844, and whose dramatic rise and crushing disappointment provide Dennis with his most detailed case study of modern prophetic enthusiasm.

## Definition

The Millerites were followers of William Miller, a Baptist lay preacher from upstate New York who calculated — using the 2,300-day prophecy of Daniel 8:14 — that the Second Coming would occur in 1844. The movement grew from a local Bible study group in the 1830s to a nationwide phenomenon by the early 1840s, with estimates of followers ranging from 50,000 to over 100,000. Dennis treats the Millerites not as a fringe curiosity but as a representative case of a recurring human type: the community organized around a specific date for the world's end.

What distinguishes the Millerites in Dennis's account is the movement's survival. Unlike many date-setting movements that dissolved after failure, the Millerites transformed — splintering into groups that reinterpreted the prophecy (the sanctuary doctrine of Seventh-day Adventism) or abandoned date-setting for ongoing expectation (various Advent Christian bodies). This resilience is Dennis's central exhibit for the thesis that failed prophecy does not kill prophetic movements but mutates them.

## In the Book

### Context

The Millerites dominate the latter portion of Chapter 10 ("This Year"). Dennis traces the movement's arc with novelistic attention to social detail: "Within a few months the Millerites had become a sect, spreading like wildfire" (010-096). The speed of the movement's growth, the variety of its adherents, the mockery it attracted, and the charismatic entrepreneurs who exploited it — all receive attention.

Dennis is particularly interested in the social psychology of the movement's final days. "At Westford, Mass., the local Millerites had assembled in their farmhouses" (010-103), waiting through the night of October 22 for the trumpet that never sounded. The scene is rendered with pathos but without condescension — Dennis recognizes that the Millerites were not fools but ordinary people acting on beliefs that two thousand years of Christian tradition had encouraged them to take seriously.

The aftermath is equally important to Dennis's argument. Some Millerites abandoned faith; others doubled down; still others found ingenious ways to preserve the essentials of their belief while explaining away the failure. "Nor need the Elder's followers be blamed overmuch for a credulity that was a characteristic of their age" (010-097).

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis's treatment of the Millerites is sympathetic but unsentimental. He presents the movement as a natural experiment in eschatological belief: what happens when a specific date is set, widely believed, and then passes uneventfully? The answer — reinterpretation, fragmentation, survival — tells us something essential about the human relationship to prophecy.
