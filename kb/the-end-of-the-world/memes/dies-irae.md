The Dies Irae ("Day of Wrath") is the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian eschatological day of [[judgment|divine judgment]], most famously articulated in the medieval Latin hymn. In Dennis's book, it represents the intensity of apocalyptic [[hope|expectation]] — the [[faith|conviction]] that the signs of the End are always already present.

## Overview

The Dies Irae is the day when, according to [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian tradition, the dead shall be raised and all souls judged. The medieval hymn describes cosmic dissolution — heaven and earth dissolving into ash — as the prelude to [[judgment|final judgment]]. In Dennis's usage, it is less about the event itself than about the psychology of waiting for it.

## In the Book

### Context

The Dies Irae appears in "God"（010）, the chapter examining how religious apocalypticism has shaped human responses to the End. Dennis cites two references: the repeated annual proclamations of "this generation" as the Dies Irae（010-004）, and the observation that "of the signs of the Dies Irae not one was wanting"（010-037）.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis's treatment is sociological rather than theological. He observes that in the early [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian era, "one year after another of 'this generation' was proclaimed the Dies Irae"（010-004）— a pattern of repeated failed [[prophecy|prediction]]s that, rather than extinguishing [[faith|belief]], seemed to intensify it. The signs were always present, the date always imminent, the disappointment always survivable. This pattern of deferred [[revelation|apocalypse]] is central to Dennis's analysis of millennial psychology.

> One year after another of "this generation" was proclaimed the Dies Irae（010-004）

> Of the signs of the Dies Irae not one was wanting（010-037）

## Significance

The Dies Irae exemplifies what Dennis sees as the fundamental pattern of apocalyptic [[faith|belief]]: the signs are always present, the date is always now, and the failure of [[prophecy|prediction]] never diminishes [[faith|conviction]]. It is the archetype of the [[fire|Fire]]-End in religious imagination — [[judgment|divine judgment]] by cosmic [[fire|conflagration]] — and serves as a bridge between ancient [[doomsday-prophecy|eschatology]] and modern [[fate|doom]]-scenarios.
