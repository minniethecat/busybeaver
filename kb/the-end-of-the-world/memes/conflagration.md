## The Fire That Ends All Things

The corpus identifies conflagration as one of the primary modes of world-ending, alongside flood, cold, and impact. "In colossal conflagration flamed into new life, a glorious nova" [(001-008)] — fire is destruction but also transformation, the old world consumed so that something new might emerge. This duality — destruction as prelude to renewal — runs throughout the text's treatment of fire.

The specific mechanism of atmospheric conflagration is described with scientific precision: "Its gases might combine and combust with our oxygen, causing conflagration of the air; concremation of our bodies" [(002-028)]. A comet passing through the atmosphere could ignite the very air we breathe, turning the sky into a furnace. This is not mythic fire but chemical combustion, scaled to planetary dimensions.

## Fire as Purifier

The corpus draws on deep cultural associations between fire and purification. "For thus he cleansed his soul" [(003-003)] — fire in many traditions, particularly Zoroastrian, is not merely destructive but sacred. "Holding that fire was God Himself" [(003-006)], the Parsees regarded death by flame as communion with the divine. The text records that "flames have made even self-murder meritorious suicide" [(003-003)], a startling claim that reveals fire's ambivalent moral status.

"Life itself is a flame, as was said long ago, and remains to be" [(003-009)] — this ancient metaphor, attributed to Heraclitus, positions fire not as an external threat but as the essence of existence. To live is to burn; to die is to be extinguished. The world-ending conflagration is thus the logical culmination of a universe already defined by combustion.

## Mechanism and Likelihood

The text presents conflagration through multiple causal pathways. A stellar collision could produce "colossal conflagration" [(001-008)]. A comet's gases combining with atmospheric oxygen could cause "conflagration of the air" [(002-028)]. The "great balefire of combustion" [(007-024)] could arise from geothermal sources. Each mechanism leads to the same result: "all life shall be food for the flames. Souls into slag and embers" [(003-027)].

The text assigns "fair chances" to fire as the mode of ending, noting that it is "also flaming ends" [(003-001)] that await the world. The plural — "ends" — suggests that conflagration is not a single event but a category encompassing multiple possible fire-deaths.

## Cultural and Philosophical Echoes

The Stoic doctrine of ekpyrosis — periodic cosmic conflagration in which the universe is consumed by fire and then reborn — provides the most direct philosophical parallel to the corpus's treatment. Norse mythology's Surtr, whose flaming sword consumes the world at Ragnarök, represents a similar archetype. The corpus weaves these traditions together while grounding the mechanism in the scientific language of its era.

The conflagration concept also resonates with modern anxieties about nuclear fire, though the corpus predates the atomic age. The "swords of flame that foretokened war" [(002-007)] suggest that human violence may be the proximate cause of the ultimate fire.
