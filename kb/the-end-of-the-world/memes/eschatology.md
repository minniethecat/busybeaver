# Eschatology

**Eschatology** (from Greek *eschatos*, "last") is the theological study of final things: the ultimate destiny of individuals, humanity, and the cosmos. It provides the doctrinal framework within which end-of-the-world narratives gain their meaning and urgency.

## Christian Eschatology

The text explicitly references "Christian eschatology" as confirming and foretelling "the final destruction of the earth" (003-014). This places the book's subject matter squarely within an established theological tradition — one that does not merely fear the end but anticipates it as a divinely ordained consummation. Christian eschatology transforms the end from mere catastrophe into fulfillment.

> Christian eschatology confirms, foretelling the final destruction of the earth.
> (003-014)

## Jewish Apocalyptic Roots

The text acknowledges the historical lineage: Christian eschatology "merely took over the popular eschatology of Jewish Apocalyptic" (011-008), adding its own distinctive elements. This genealogical note is important — it situates the Christian end-times imagination not as a novel invention but as an inheritance and transformation of older Jewish traditions of visionary revelation and cosmic judgment.

## The Shape of Eschatological Time

Eschatology is distinguished from ordinary futurology by its understanding of time. Eschatological time is not merely the continuation of present trends but a rupture — an intervention from outside history that brings history to its proper close. This is why eschatology deals not in probabilities but in certainties: the end is guaranteed not by natural law but by divine decree.

## The Eschatological Mood

Every eschatology carries an affective charge. It can be terrifying (the *dies irae*), hopeful (the coming kingdom), or resigned (the inevitable dissolution). The text's own eschatological mood is complex — it registers both the terror of destruction and something close to awe at the scale of cosmic processes. This ambivalence is characteristic of modern eschatological reflection.
