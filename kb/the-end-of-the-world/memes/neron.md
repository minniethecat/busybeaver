Nero Claudius Caesar Augustus Germanicus (37–68 CE) was the fifth Roman Emperor, reigning from 54 to 68 CE. In the book, Nero appears both as a figure of personal depravity and, more significantly, as the archetype behind the "Number of the Beast" (666) in the Book of Revelation — his name, rendered in Hebrew gematria, yields that infamous number.

## Identity

Nero was the last emperor of the Julio-Claudian dynasty. His reign was marked by political murders (including those of his mother Agrippina, his stepbrother Britannicus, and his wife Octavia), the Great Fire of Rome (64 CE), and the first imperial persecution of Christians. He died by suicide in 68 CE amid military rebellion, triggering the "Year of the Four Emperors." Early Christians, writing in the aftermath of his persecution, encoded him as the Beast whose number is 666（010-043, 011-010）.

## In the Book

### Appearances

Dennis references Nero in Chapter 10 and Chapter 11. In Chapter 10, the "Neronic" year 1666 is noted as a focal point of numerical prophecy — the Beast's number applied to a calendar year, coinciding with the Great Fire of London（010-043）. Chapter 11 paints a wider canvas of first-century horrors, placing Nero at the center of a world convulsed by natural disasters, wars, and imperial cruelty（011-010）.

### Role in Dennis's Argument

Nero serves Dennis as the historical anchor for the Antichrist archetype. The author shows how the apocalyptic imagination of the early Church crystallized around a specific, historical figure of evil — Nero — and then generalized that figure into a recurring pattern. The "Neronic" year 1666 represents the persistence of this numerological reading of history, which Dennis treats as emblematic of the broader apocalyptic habit of finding cosmic significance in terrestrial events（010-043）. In Chapter 11, the vivid catalogue of Nero's crimes — the murder of Agrippina, the poisoning of Britannicus, the dismemberment of Octavia — is juxtaposed against the cosmic-scale disasters of the era, making the point that human evil and natural catastrophe were read as a unified eschatological sign（011-010）.

> Lord of the World was Antichrist: "Strike the womb which bore Nero!" cried Agrippina, the dead womb he came to peer at and to mock; at brotherly table young Britannicus was killed with treacherous wine-cup; of Octavia each innocent vein was opened ere he flung her into the sea.（011-010）

## Significance

Nero is arguably the most consequential single person in the book's eschatological framework. He is not merely one tyrant among many; he is the historical prototype from which the entire Western tradition of the Antichrist derives. Dennis uses Nero to illustrate a central thesis: that apocalyptic thought is not abstract theology but a response to lived historical trauma. The early Christians who suffered under Nero encoded their experience into sacred text, and that encoding — the Beast, 666, the Antichrist — has haunted Western imagination for two millennia.
