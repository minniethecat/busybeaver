Determinism is the philosophical backbone of Dennis's cosmology: the conviction, whether expressed in theological or scientific language, that the end is not an accident but a consequence. Every mode of world-end he surveys — from cometary collision to solar cooling to the Wärmetod — is deterministic in structure: given the initial conditions and the relevant laws, the outcome follows necessarily.

## Definition

Determinism, in its classical (Laplacian) formulation, holds that the state of the universe at any given moment, together with the complete set of natural laws, uniquely determines the state of the universe at every other moment — past and future. Laplace's demon, a hypothetical intelligence that knows the position and momentum of every particle in the universe, could predict the entire future and retrodict the entire past with perfect accuracy. In this framework, the end of the world is not a surprise; it is a theorem waiting to be derived from the axioms of physics.

Theological determinism — the doctrine that God has eternally decreed all events — is structurally identical, substituting divine will for physical law. Predestination is theological determinism applied specifically to the fate of souls. Fatalism is the folk version: the belief that outcomes are fixed regardless of human action. Strict determinism differs from fatalism in that it preserves the causal chain — actions matter, but they are themselves determined.

Twentieth-century physics complicated classical determinism without refuting it. Quantum mechanics introduced irreducible probabilities at the micro-scale, but whether this amounts to genuine indeterminism or merely epistemic limitation remains contested. Chaos theory demonstrated that deterministic systems can be unpredictable in practice, but unpredictability is not indeterminacy. The deterministic core of Dennis's physical argument — the Second Law of Thermodynamics, the irreversible increase of entropy — is robust against these developments.

## In the Book

### Context

Dennis does not use the word "determinism," but his book is saturated with deterministic reasoning. The sun *will* cool (Chapter 6). The comet *will* strike (Chapter 2). Entropy *will* reach equilibrium (Chapter 17). The modal verb throughout is "will," not "might" or "could." Dennis's cataloguing of world-end scenarios is not a menu of possibilities among which we might choose; it is a list of inevitabilities, each with its own time scale and probability, but all converging on the same outcome.

The structure of the book enacts determinism. By starting with the question "in what way the world's death is most likely to befall?" (001-019) and proceeding through every conceivable mechanism, Dennis implies that the answer is already determined — we simply do not yet know which mechanism will claim priority. The book is an exercise in retrospective determinism: once we know all the ways the world can end, we recognize that one of them must.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis's treatment of determinism is most vivid in the cold chapter, where he stages the struggle between human agency and cosmic necessity. "Man will fight his fate. He will adapt himself. Even against the dying of the sun he will struggle, and perhaps prevail for a time" (006-024). The word "fate" — not "circumstance" or "likelihood" — reveals the deterministic framework. Fate is what cannot be outrun; adaptation is what delays the inevitable but does not cancel it. The qualification "for a time" is the deterministic punchline: the struggle is real, but the outcome is fixed.

The entropy chapter (017) is still more explicit. "Entropy is irreversible. There is no opposite process" (017-006). This is determinism in its purest form — a law that admits no exceptions, no reversals, no loopholes. Dennis considers and rejects the cyclic alternative: "They are mere accidents, incidents, powerless to stem or deflect the stream ineluctable of sidereal evolution" (017-005). The word "ineluctable" — inescapable — is the lexicon of determinism. The universe is a stream, and it flows in one direction only.

Dennis's extension of entropy into the psychical realm — "matter-death spirit-death" (017-044) — is determinism's final conquest. If even mind and will are subject to the same irreversible degradation as matter and energy, then there is no realm exempt from determinism. Consciousness is not a refuge from causality; it is one of causality's products, and it runs down with everything else.

> Entropy is irreversible. There is no opposite process. (017-006)
