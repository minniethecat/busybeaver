# Solomon

Son of David and king of Israel, builder of the First Temple in Jerusalem, and paragon of wisdom in the [[judgment]]. Dennis invokes the destruction of Solomon's Temple as a historical archetype of divine judgment upon sacred institutions.

## Identity

Solomon (c. 990–931 BCE) ruled the united Kingdom of Israel at its zenith. According to the Book of Kings, he built the First Temple and was granted divine wisdom. His reign represents the high-water mark of Israelite power and the ideal of godly kingship in Jewish and Christian memory（011-010）.

## In the Book

### Appearances

Solomon appears in the context of the Temple's destruction during the Roman siege of Jerusalem — the catastrophic event that, for Dennis, serves as a prototype of divine judgment. The fall of Solomon's Temple resonates through the book's eschatological imagery of cosmic conflagration（011-010）.

### Role in Dennis's Argument

The Temple of Solomon functions as a symbolic anchor in Dennis's argument: its destruction demonstrates that no human institution, however sacred, is immune to divine judgment. The pattern of building, corruption, and destruction that characterizes the Temple's history mirrors Dennis's vision of the end of the world — what was once the glory of nations becomes fuel for the flames（011-010）.

> the Temple of Solomon delivered at last to the flames and Mount Zion to the sword and the vulture（011-010）

## Significance

Solomon's dual legacy — wisdom and the Temple — makes him a pivotal figure in Dennis's eschatology. The Temple's destruction supplies the historical precedent for the coming universal conflagration, while the wisdom literature attributed to him provides the moral vocabulary for divine judgment.
