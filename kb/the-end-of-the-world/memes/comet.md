# Comet

The wandering celestial body that bridges the gap between astronomical fact and eschatological fear. Dennis surveys comets from two angles: as physical objects whose collision with Earth could end all life, and as cultural symbols whose appearance has always been read as a portent of doom. A comet is a small Solar System body that, when passing close to the Sun, warms and releases gases — producing a visible atmosphere or coma, and sometimes a tail. The book describes comets as "heavenly body of debatable character" [002-003], noting that their movements fall into three categories: elliptic, hyperbolic, and parabolic. It is the third category — the "universe-wandering or parabolic comet, lured into the solar system" [002-005] — that poses the existential threat.

## As Omen

Across civilizations, comets have been interpreted not as physical objects but as supernatural signs. The book catalogs the historical reading of comets as divine messengers of catastrophe:

> "To seers of other days comets were not so interesting for their physical characters as because they came as signs." [002-007]

The Romans built temples to comets; the death of princes was read in their trajectories. The book quotes Virgil: "nec diri toties arsere cometae" — so often have dread comets blazed [002-008]. This cultural encoding of comets as doom-signs persists even into the modern era.

## From Portent to Physics

The book traces a crucial epistemological shift in how humanity understands comets. The "old astrologers" saw comets as messages from the gods — meaningful, intentional, directed at specific rulers and peoples. Dennis's own century was the moment when that framework gave way to a physical one: comets became objects with measurable orbits, predictable returns, and material consequences (002-002). Yet the transition was incomplete. Even as Halley computed the orbit of the comet that bears his name, the cultural reflex to read comets as omens persisted. Dennis exploits this tension: the comet is simultaneously a Newtonian body obeying gravitational law and a sign "searing the naked firmament" (002-012), its physical danger amplified by its symbolic resonance.

This dual identity — object and omen, physics and portent — makes the comet uniquely powerful among end-of-world mechanisms. A solar death or a heat death is pure physics, abstract and remote. A comet is physics you can see, a visible threat crossing the sky, activating both the scientific and the superstitious mind at once.

## Physical Threat

The book devotes sustained attention to the possibility of cometary collision as a literal end-of-world mechanism. A comet striking the Earth would release energy sufficient to "dissolve the globe" [002-032]. The scenario is treated with scientific seriousness:

> "The utter danger, that a comet would accomplish the end of the world, is by no means negligible." [002-019]

A "nine days' terrible comet came searing the naked firmament" [002-012] during the millenary year, and Dennis notes the "maximum belief that one day a comet would destroy the whole earth" [002-014] — a belief grounded not in superstition but in the statistical inevitability of orbital intersection.

### Varieties of Cometary End

The corpus catalogs several cometary doom scenarios:

- **Direct collision**: The comet strikes Earth, releasing heat "sufficient to melt the globe" [002-032]
- **Atmospheric poisoning**: Even without impact, passage through "cometary gases" could render the atmosphere unbreathable — "Thou shalt die in a comet's tail" [002-018]
- **Gravitational disruption**: A near passage could "pull up" the oceans [004-003], creating a "watery end" from a cometary cause
