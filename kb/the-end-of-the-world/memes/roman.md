# Roman

The civilization, empire, and cultural tradition of ancient Rome, which serves in Dennis's argument as both a historical touchstone for apocalyptic thinking and a cautionary example of imperial hubris and decline.

## Definition

"Roman" in the book refers not merely to the city or empire but to an entire civilizational complex: the political, military, literary, and religious culture that dominated the Mediterranean world from roughly the second century BCE through the fifth century CE and whose afterimages persist in Western eschatology to the present day. Dennis mobilizes the Roman world in three distinct argumentative roles: as the historical crucible in which Christian apocalypticism was forged, as a case study in the interpretation of comets as political portents, and as a foil to the Greek intellectual tradition.

The distinction between Roman and Greek is one of the book's recurring cultural typologies. Dennis characterizes the Romans as "sound" and practical, in contrast to the "romantic" Greeks — a distinction that maps onto his broader tension between scientific sobriety and imaginative excess (016-006).

## In the Book

### Context

The Roman world appears across multiple chapters. In Chapter 2 ("Comets"), Roman history provides the parade examples of cometary portents: the comet that appeared at Julius Caesar's death in 44 BCE ("Caesaris astrum"), and the broader Roman tradition of reading celestial signs as omens of political catastrophe. Dennis notes that comets were "connected particularly with the deaths of Roman Emperors" (002-011), establishing a pattern that would later be absorbed into Christian apocalyptic.

In Chapter 3 ("Fire"), Roman reactions to the doctrine of cosmic conflagration illustrate the cultural divide between Greek philosophical speculation and Roman practical skepticism: "the gainsayers, who cap each pyromaniac might with fireproof won't" (003-028). The Romans here are the voice of empirical resistance to apocalyptic excess — a role they play throughout the book.

In Chapter 10 ("This Year"), the Roman Church becomes the institutional setting for the development of numerical prophecy. Dennis traces how "in the Roman Church itself, numerical prophecy has had slightest renewal" (010-043), noting that the prophetic impulse migrated from the institutional center to the Protestant periphery. The Roman world thus serves both as the origin point and the restraining force for apocalyptic speculation.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis's attitude toward the Roman tradition is complex. He respects Roman practicality and legal order — "sound Romans rather than the romantic Greeks" (016-006) — but he also catalogs Roman violence and imperial decadence with evident disapproval. The Roman Empire is both the political matrix of Christianity and the Beast of Revelation, the persecutor whose destruction was eagerly anticipated by the early Church.

> [S]ound Romans rather than the romantic Greeks, yet of the Greeks Plato and Aristotle with their marvelous intellects. (016-006)

> In the Roman Church itself, numerical prophecy has had slightest renewal; it was into the hands of the Rabbis and the Protestants, witches of Endor and warlocks of England, that the game now passed. (010-043)
