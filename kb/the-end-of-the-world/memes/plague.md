# Plague

**Plague** — in Dennis's catalogue of the forces that thin the human herd — is the **invisible killer**, death by microbe rather than by fire or flood. Alongside famine, earthquake, and war, plague completes the quartet of classical end-time signs, and Dennis catalogs its depredations from the [[black-death|Black Death]] of the medieval period to the pestilence that "slew thirty thousand in the Urbs" in a single year [011-010].

## Overview

Plague is a contagious epidemic disease — historically, [[black-death|bubonic plague]] (Yersinia pestis) spread by fleas on rats, but in Dennis's broader usage the term encompasses all epidemic disease as a mode of mass death. Plagues have repeatedly reshaped human history: the Plague of Justinian (541–542), the [[black-death|Black Death]] (1347–1351), and numerous lesser outbreaks. In Dennis's treatment, plague is both a historical fact and a **symbol of human vulnerability** — proof that forces invisible to the naked eye can kill at scales that rival any [[catastrophe|cosmic catastrophe]].

## In the Book

### Context

Plague and pestilence appear across multiple chapters, always in company with the other classical end-time signs. In Chapter 2 ("Comet"), pestilence follows the celestial omens [002-012]. In Chapter 10 ("Next Year"), the Dallas Prophetical Society lists pestilence among the fulfilled signs that point to 1931 as the year of the end [010-109]. In Chapter 11 ("Some [[time|Time]]"), Dennis catalogs the first-century portents: "cyclones and floods, famines and plagues, eclipses and comets, eruptions and earthquakes, wars and rumours of wars" [011-010].

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis treats plague with a mixture of **statistical detachment** and **moral horror**. He notes the numbers: thirty thousand dead in the Urbs in a single year, the Black Plague of the 990s. But he also sees plague as evidence for the book's deeper thesis — that death comes in endless variety, and that no force, however small, can be discounted as a potential agent of world-ending.

> In one year the pestilence slew thirty thousand in the Urbs. [011-010]

> Of the black plague of the nine-nineties none die now, but pestilences never cease. [010-037]

The escalation is implicit: if a single bacterium can kill a city, a pandemic — or a deliberately engineered pathogen — could kill a [[human-progress|civilization]]. Dennis wrote before the age of biological weapons, but his logic extends there naturally.

> The catalogue too is longer; doctors have fought not more bravely to reduce the numbers borne off by disease than to increase the numbers of diseases. [001-015]

## Significance

Plague serves Dennis as a reminder that **the agents of world-ending need not be visible or sudden**. Unlike a comet or a volcanic eruption, plague operates silently, through mechanisms that for most of human history were not understood. It is, in Dennis's framework, the most **insidious** of the end-time signs — always present, never fully extinguished, capable of explosive resurgence.

The pairing of plague with famine, earthquake, and war creates a complete eschatological vocabulary: earth (earthquake), fire (volcano), water (flood), [[famine|hunger]] (famine), violence (war), and disease (plague). Dennis's point is that every dimension of human vulnerability has its corresponding mode of [[disaster|catastrophe]], and that the end, when it comes, will likely be overdetermined — multiple forces converging.
