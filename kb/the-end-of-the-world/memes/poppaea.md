## Death of Poppaea

The corpus records Poppaea's fate within a single, dense passage cataloguing the horrors of Nero's reign. After Nero killed his first wife Octavia, her severed head was brought to Poppaea:

> her fair head carried to Poppaea (soon herself, though with womb living, to be kicked to death) [011-010]

The parenthetical aside — "soon herself, though with womb living, to be kicked to death" — emphasizes the speed and brutality of Poppaea's own end, which followed shortly after she received Octavia's head as a grisly trophy. The phrase "with womb living" underscores that Poppaea was carrying Nero's unborn child at the time of her murder, making the crime simultaneously uxoricide and filicide.

## The Quadruple Crown

Poppaea's murder was the fourth in the sequence of killings that the corpus refers to as Nero's "quadruple tiara":

> the quadruple tiara of prolicide, uxoricide, fratricide, matricide — Britannicus, Octavia, Agrippina, Poppaea — after these he aspired to deicide also when he turned on Christ in the person of His Saints [011-010]

After killing his brother Britannicus, his wife Octavia, his mother [[agrippina|Agrippina]], and his pregnant wife Poppaea, Nero turned on the [[christian|Christians]], torturing and executing them in public spectacles. The progression from domestic murder to the persecution of the saints supplied early Christian apocalyptic with its most potent political symbol.

## The Historicity of Nero's Crime

The Roman historians Tacitus and Suetonius both record Nero's killing of Poppaea, though with variations. Suetonius reports that Nero kicked Poppaea to death during a quarrel while she was pregnant and ill — a moment of sudden rage rather than premeditated murder. The corpus does not distinguish between impulse and design; it draws Poppaea into a single arc of Nero's escalating crimes that culminates in his persecution of the [[christian|Christians]] and, by typological extension, in deicide itself. For the corpus's early Christian sources, the domestic violence of the emperor was not merely biography — it was [[apocalypse|apocalyptic]] premonition.

## Poppaea and the Julio-Claudian End

Poppaea was the last wife in the Julio-Claudian sequence that the corpus traces. Her death — pregnant, at the hands of the man who should have protected her — marks the terminal degradation of the imperial family, after which Nero's violence found no more members of his own household to consume and turned outward toward the world. In the corpus's eschatological frame, this is the pivot: when the emperor exhausts his own bloodline, the [[end-of-the-world|end]] cannot be far behind.
