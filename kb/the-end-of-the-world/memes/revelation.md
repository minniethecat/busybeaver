# Revelation

The disclosure of otherwise inaccessible knowledge — above all, knowledge of the end of the world — whether through direct divine communication to a prophet or through the interpretive decoding of scriptural texts, a concept that Dennis examines as the epistemological foundation of all eschatological prediction.

## Definition

Dennis treats "revelation" in a double sense. In its narrow theological meaning, revelation is God's act of making known what would otherwise remain hidden — the divine self-disclosure that grounds prophetic authority. In its broader functional sense, revelation is any claim to have accessed privileged knowledge about the future through non-empirical means: a vision, a dream, a numerical calculation from sacred numbers, or a flash of interpretive insight. What matters to Dennis is not the mechanism but the claim: "I know something you cannot know except through me."

The book's deepest engagement with revelation comes through the figure of William Miller, the nineteenth-century American farmer-prophet who "asked and obtained leave from heaven, and publicly announced his revelation to some friends" (010-060). Miller's case is paradigmatic for Dennis because it exhibits revelation's structure with unusual clarity: a date is received, a community forms around it, the date passes, and the community must choose between abandoning the revelation and reinterpreting it.

## In the Book

### Context

Revelation as a concept runs through the book in multiple registers. In Chapter 8 ("God"), Dennis considers whether divine revelation provides any reliable information about the world's end — concluding that the scriptural record, like the prophetic movements it has spawned, is too ambiguous to serve as a calendar. In Chapter 10 ("This Year"), revelation becomes the central subject: the history of people who have claimed, on the basis of revealed knowledge, to know *when* the end will come. Chapter 11 ("Next Year") extends this analysis to the interpretive strategies by which failed revelations are rehabilitated.

The biblical Book of Revelation (the Apocalypse of John) is the ultimate source text for this tradition, and Dennis treats it with an ambivalence that borders on admiration for its literary power combined with dismay at its historical effects. Its visions — the seals, the trumpets, the bowls, the Beast, the New Jerusalem — have furnished the raw material for two millennia of date-setting, each generation finding in its imagery a reflection of its own anxieties.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis's analysis of revelation is characteristically double-edged. He reports the Millerite episode in detail, letting the facts speak for themselves: a man receives a revelation, announces a date (1843), gathers followers, watches the date pass, refines the date (October 22, 1844), watches it pass again. "The Year was all he had ever announced as part of the revelation proper; that gave them nine months yet, till the last day of December" (010-105). The pathos of this narrowing window — the refusal to accept that nothing happened — is, for Dennis, the essential drama of revealed eschatology.

Dennis is alert to the social dynamics of revelation. The Millerite tabernacle not being ready for dedication until May 1843 — "some weeks after the Equinox" — becomes a point of controversy: "more malignant tongues averred" that the delay was deliberate, while believers "replied that only the year was a revelation" (010-098). The boundary between revelation and its institutional management is always contested, and Dennis enjoys the irony that the most transcendent claims are always entangled with the most mundane logistics.

Underlying Dennis's treatment is a deeper philosophical question: if revelation is the only route to eschatological knowledge, and all recorded revelations have been falsified by the passage of time, what grounds remain for any claim to know the future? Dennis does not answer this question directly, but his historical survey implies an answer: none. The proper response to the record of failed revelation is not to seek a better revelation but to abandon the project of predictive eschatology altogether.
