## "Behold, I Come Quickly"

The corpus places John in a catalogue of New Testament witnesses to the imminence of the end:

> Jesus said: "Verily I say unto you, that this generation shall not pass till all these things be done." St. Paul wrote, "The ends of the world are come"; St. Peter, "The end of all things is at hand"; St. John of the Revelation, "Behold, I come quickly." [011-007]

The quotation is from Revelation 22:20 — the penultimate verse of the Christian Bible. The placement is significant: John's "I come quickly" is not merely one apostolic voice among many but the final word of scripture itself, the last promise the canon records before its closing benediction. The corpus draws upon this terminal position to give John's testimony a culminating weight in its own apocalyptic argument.

## The Hellenized Philosopher

The corpus distinguishes John from the other Evangelists in intellectual terms:

> Mark and professional Luke to John the Hellenized philosopher [011-008]

This characterization — "the Hellenized philosopher" — reflects a long tradition in New Testament scholarship. John's Gospel, with its Logos prologue ("In the beginning was the Word"), its extended discourses, and its philosophical dualism of light and darkness, drew upon Greek philosophical vocabulary to articulate Christian theology. Where Mark is direct and narrative, Luke is careful and historical, John is speculative and theological — the evangelist who most deliberately translated the Palestinian Jewish movement into the language of [[greek|Greek]] thought.

## John and the Apocalypse

The corpus's use of John bridges the apostolic age and its own eschatological concerns. John was, according to tradition, the only apostle to die a natural death — he lived to extreme old age, exiled to the island of Patmos under Domitian, where he received the visions recorded in Revelation. That the youngest apostle should be the last survivor, and that his final testimony should be the most [[apocalypse|apocalyptic]] book in scripture, gave John a unique position in Christian memory: the man who had known Christ in the flesh and who, decades later, sealed the canon with the promise of Christ's return.
