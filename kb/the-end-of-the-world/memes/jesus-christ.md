# Jesus Christ

The founder of [[christianity]], whose eschatological teachings — particularly the promise of an imminent return and end of the world — Dennis examines as the most culturally influential doomsday prophecy in Western history. In Dennis's [[providential-design|providential]] framework, Christ stands at the intersection of [[divine-judgment]] and [[doomsday-prophecy]], his promised return functioning as both theological doctrine and psychological necessity.

## Identity

Jesus of Nazareth, called Christ (the Anointed One), lived and taught in Roman-occupied Palestine in the early first century CE. His followers believed him to be the Messiah whose return would herald the end of the present age and the establishment of God's kingdom. Dennis's interest is not in Jesus as a historical figure or theological subject, but as the source of the most consequential end-of-world expectation in the Western tradition: "Christ's 'this generation' meant this generation" (010-021) — a prophecy of imminent eschaton that has been reinterpreted, deferred, and revived across two millennia.

## In the Book

### Appearances

Jesus Christ appears throughout Dennis's survey of religious eschatology. In Chapter 2 ("Comet"), Dennis notes how comets were interpreted in the Christian era as heavenly signs presaging the Second Coming (002-012). In Chapter 3 ("Fire"), the Christian inheritance from Jewish apocalyptic thought is traced: "Israel, and the Christians who inherited from both" (003-011) maintained the expectation of a fiery end. The most sustained treatment comes in Chapter 8 ("God") and Chapter 10 ("This Year"), where Dennis examines early Christian apocalyptic chronology and its psychological function: "Christianity, still a persecuted creed offering no hope of present triumph, needed some such faith in not too distant punishment" (010-022).

The stream of apocalyptic expectation flowed into Christianity from its Jewish and Near Eastern sources: "The stream flowed through into the nascent empire of Christianity, which it watered" (010-004). Dennis traces how figures like Tertullian, Commodian, and other early Church Fathers were "chiliasts" (010-023) — believers in a literal thousand-year reign of Christ on earth — and how this millenarian conviction shaped the intellectual climate in which the faith consolidated its hold on the Roman world.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis uses Christian eschatology as the primary case study of a broader phenomenon: the human need to believe that history has a destination, and that this destination is imminent. The early Christian community's expectation that "this generation" would not pass before the end (010-021) is, for Dennis, the archetype of all doomsday predictions — wrong in its timing but psychologically indispensable to the community that held it.

> Christianity, still a persecuted creed offering no hope of present triumph, needed some such faith in not too distant punishment, and in not too distant reward and joy, to enable it to increase its strength and maintain its courage. (010-022)

Dennis's treatment is remarkable for its lack of polemic. He does not argue that Christian eschatology is true or false; he argues that it is *human* — a response to the same existential pressure that produces scientific accounts of the world's end. The figure of Christ serves as the pivot between the theological and the naturalistic: "Christian eschatology confirms, foretells" (003-014) the same fiery end that physics independently predicts.

The deepest layer of Dennis's treatment concerns the question of mercy. Chapter 8 opens with the stark inquiry "Will He have mercy?" (008-001) — a question that applies with particular force to the figure of Christ, who in Christian theology is both judge and advocate, both the one who pronounces the sentence and the one who offers to commute it. Dennis does not resolve this paradox; he presents it as the central unresolved tension of Christian eschatology, and by extension of all providential accounts of the world's end.

## Significance

Jesus Christ represents, for Dennis, the intersection of providential design and historical psychology. His prophecies of the end — regardless of their theological status — have shaped Western civilization's imagination of catastrophe for two thousand years. The persistence of Christian apocalyptic expectation, through repeated falsification of specific dates, is evidence of the depth of the human investment in eschatology: we need the world to end meaningfully, and Christ is the figure through whom that meaning has been most widely articulated in the West.
