# Mary of Bethezub

A woman of Jerusalem who, during the Roman siege of the city (70 CE), was driven to the ultimate act of desperation — eating her own child. Dennis invokes this harrowing image as the nadir of human suffering before the Temple's destruction.

## Identity

Mary of Bethezub is known from Josephus's account of the siege of Jerusalem, where famine drove the population to unspeakable acts. Her story represents the complete breakdown of civilization under the pressure of divine judgment — a mother consuming her infant as the legions closed in（011-010）.

## In the Book

### Appearances

Mary appears in the book's most intense passage on the siege of Jerusalem — a sequence of escalating horrors culminating in the destruction of the Temple. Dennis places her at the climax of human suffering, the point beyond which only divine intervention in the form of total destruction remains（011-010）.

### Role in Dennis's Argument

Mary of Bethezub serves as the ultimate emblem of what Dennis calls "the cruellest siege we have word of." Her story demonstrates that divine judgment is not abstract — it unfolds in the flesh, in the dissolution of the most fundamental human bonds. The sequence moves from her cannibalism directly to the Temple's burning, linking human depravity to cosmic destruction（011-010）.

> Mary of Bethezub ate the child at her breast, the city that had destroyed the Redeemer herself was destroyed（011-010）

## Significance

Mary of Bethezub embodies the intersection of personal tragedy and eschatological judgment in Dennis's vision. Her story is the dark inverse of the Madonna — a mother consuming rather than nurturing — and signals the absolute inversion of the natural order that precedes the end.
