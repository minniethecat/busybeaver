# Nebular Hypothesis

The theory that the solar system originated from a vast rotating nebula of hot gas, which cooled and condensed over billions of years, throwing off rings that became the planets while the central mass became the sun. Dennis presents it as the first and most influential of modern scientific accounts of cosmic origins — and, by implication, of cosmic endings.

## Definition

The nebular hypothesis, as Dennis describes it, holds that "there was once a great fluid cloud or rotating chaos of fire, far-flung beyond the present orbit of Neptune" (001-004). Over "prodigious years," this cloud cooled and contracted. At its fringes, it threw off successive rings of matter; these rings coalesced into the planets. The main mass shrank to form the sun. The theory was first systematically developed by Pierre-Simon Laplace in the late eighteenth century and remained the dominant scientific account of solar system formation through the period of Dennis's writing. For Dennis, the nebular hypothesis is significant not only as a physical theory but as a narrative template: a story of progressive differentiation, from uniform chaos to ordered complexity — a story whose natural terminus is the reversal of that process in entropy.

## In the Book

### Context

The nebular hypothesis appears in the opening chapter ("How?") as the first of seven competing theories of the world's origin. Its placement is strategic: Dennis presents it first because it is, in his view, the most scientifically respectable account, and because it establishes a pattern that the other theories either modify or oppose. The nebular hypothesis also provides the framework for Dennis's later discussion of solar evolution: if the sun formed by condensation from a nebula, it has a finite history and a finite future — a beginning that implies an end.

Dennis returns to the nebular theme in Chapter 6 ("Cold"), where the sun's formation from a nebula is the starting point for tracing its eventual death, and in Chapter 7 ("Crash"), where the collisional alternative to the nebular hypothesis (a dead star revived by collision) underscores the contingency of all origin accounts.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis treats the nebular hypothesis with respect but not credulity. He presents it as the best available scientific account — better, certainly, than the magical or mythological alternatives — but he is careful to note that it remains a hypothesis, not a demonstrated fact. "Knowing nothing, Man has variously and valiantly guessed. Seven chief guesses jostle for primacy" (001-003). The nebular hypothesis is merely the first guess, and its priority is chronological rather than epistemological.

> There was once a great fluid cloud or rotating chaos of fire, far-flung beyond the present orbit of Neptune. Gradually, through prodigious years, it cooled and condensed, at its dwindling fringes throwing off successive rings which became the several planets, while the main mass shrank to form our central sun. (001-004)

The language — "great fluid cloud," "rotating chaos," "prodigious years" — combines scientific precision with a kind of mythic resonance. Dennis is aware that any origin story, however scientific, functions as a myth: it provides a narrative of how things came to be that satisfies the same psychological need that creation myths have always satisfied. The difference is that the nebular hypothesis, unlike Genesis, is subject to revision — it can be tested, modified, or replaced by better evidence. This fallibilism is, for Dennis, the defining virtue of scientific thinking, even as he acknowledges that it makes scientific narratives less psychologically satisfying than their religious counterparts.
