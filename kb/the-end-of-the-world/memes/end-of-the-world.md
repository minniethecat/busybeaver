# End of the World

The termination of the human world — whether by fire, water, cold, collision, or divine decree — which Dennis examines not as a single event but as a permanent feature of the human imagination, a recurring expectation that has structured Western thought from antiquity to the present.

## Definition

The phrase "the end of the world" carries in Dennis's usage a deliberate ambiguity. It can mean the physical destruction of the Earth; the extinction of humanity; the termination of a historical era; or the fulfillment of divine purpose in a final judgment. Dennis exploits this ambiguity throughout the book, moving between literal and figurative meanings to illuminate the psychological and cultural functions of end-of-world belief.

Dennis announces the theme in the book's opening pages: "in what way the world's death is most likely to befall?" (001-019). The question is posed as a scientific inquiry, but the book's structure reveals a deeper purpose. The end of the world, Dennis shows, is never merely a hypothesis about the future — it is always also a commentary on the present, a way of measuring the moral and spiritual condition of the age that imagines its own termination.

The title phrase appears at key structural moments. Chapter 10 ("This Year") opens with the declaration that "The end of the world — surely" (010-022), immediately qualified: "But only as incidental to the relief of persecuted Christians." Chapter 11 ("Next Year") begins with the counterpoint: "The end of the world is at hand; it is very far off" (011-001), establishing the dialectical rhythm that governs the book's second half. Chapter 12 returns to the biblical source: "the sign of Thy coming and of the end of the world?" (012-068), the disciples' question on the Mount of Olives that launched two millennia of apocalyptic speculation.

## In the Book

### Context

The concept appears in every chapter but takes on specific urgency in the central sequence (Chapters 10-15). In Chapter 10, the end is expected imminently — "The Year came; the end of the world came not" (010-039), the disappointed refrain of every date-setting prophecy. In Chapter 11, it is indefinitely deferred. In Chapter 12, it is encoded in scriptural signs. In Chapter 15, it bifurcates into two distinct ends: "the interval between the end of the world in the one sense and the end of the world in the other" (015-001) — the gap between planetary destruction and human extinction that is the book's ultimate philosophical space.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis never resolves the question he poses. The book ends not with a verdict but with a survey of possible verdicts, each presented with its strongest arguments. The cumulative effect is to show that the question "how will the world end?" is inseparable from the question "what is the world?" — and that the latter question is one humanity has never been able to answer definitively.

> The end of the world — surely. But only as incidental to the relief of persecuted Christians. (010-022)

> The Year came; the end of the world came not. (010-039)

> [T]he interval between the end of the world in the one sense and the end of the world in the other. (015-001)
