# Persia

The ancient empire centered on the Iranian plateau, whose state religion — Zoroastrianism — developed one of the earliest and most sophisticated eschatologies in world history, with doctrines of cosmic dualism, final judgment, and the renovation of the world that profoundly influenced Jewish, Christian, and later Islamic apocalyptic.

## Location

Persia (modern Iran) was the heartland of the Achaemenid Empire (c. 550–330 BCE), which at its height under Darius I stretched from the Indus Valley to the Aegean Sea. Its capital cities — Persepolis, Susa, Ecbatana — were centers of a civilization that produced one of the world's first systematically developed eschatologies. Dennis references Persia in his catalogue of solar temples: "many temples from Persia to Mexico" (006-009), linking Persian sun worship to a global pattern.

## In the Book

### Context

Persia appears in Dennis's survey of ancient religions and their eschatological traditions. Zoroastrianism — the Persian state religion — taught that history is a cosmic struggle between Ahura Mazda (the wise lord, representing truth and light) and Angra Mainyu (the destructive spirit, representing falsehood and darkness). This struggle would culminate in a final battle, a judgment of all souls, and a renovation (*frashokereti*) in which the world would be purified by fire and made perfect.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis treats Persian eschatology as a crucial source for the apocalyptic traditions that would later develop in Judaism and Christianity. The dualistic framework — light versus darkness, good versus evil, with a definitive end in which good triumphs — originated in Persia and migrated westward, shaping the imaginative framework within which Western eschatology still operates.

## Significance

Persia represents, for Dennis, the historical moment when eschatology became systematic — when scattered myths of world-ending were organized into a coherent theological framework with a clear narrative arc. The Zoroastrian influence on Jewish apocalyptic during and after the Babylonian exile is one of the great intellectual transmissions in religious history, and Dennis is attentive to its importance.
