# Paradise

In Geoffrey Dennis's *The End of the World*, Paradise appears as both the primordial past and the projected future — the Eden from which humanity has fallen and the destination toward which, according to one strand of eschatological thought, history is moving.

## Definition

Paradise, in the book's usage, carries the dual biblical sense: the lost garden of Eden (Genesis) and the promised new creation (Revelation). Dennis invokes the concept at three distinct points in his argument — as a paleontological jest, as a historical motive for apocalyptic expectation, and as the rhetorical endpoint of progressive optimism. The word itself derives from Old Persian *pairidaeza* (enclosed garden), entering Western discourse through the Septuagint's use of *paradeisos* for the Garden of Eden.

## In the Book

### Context

Dennis first mentions Paradise in Chapter 6 ("Cold"), during his survey of Earth's cooling climate history. The context is geological rather than theological: the discovery that the Arctic was once temperate, that "Greenland was once green," and that Peary found in the far north botanical evidence of a warmer world. Paradise here is a provocation — the warm past from which the planet has been steadily declining (006-020).

The second appearance comes in Chapter 11 ("Next Year"), where Dennis catalogs the recurrent waves of apocalyptic expectation in Christian Europe. Columbus, he notes, sailed west believing he would reach Paradise — the terrestrial Eden thought to lie east of India (011-015). Martin Luther hurried to finish his German Bible "in time." Dennis treats these as specimens of a recurring pattern: each generation's conviction that the end is imminent, that Paradise lost is about to be Paradise regained.

The third and most significant appearance is in Chapter 15 ("Man"), where Dennis stages the climactic debate between the survivalists and the pessimists. Here Paradise is the rhetorical prize — the survivalists claim that human evolution leads "to Paradise," the pessimists that it leads "from Arcadia to Armageddon" (015-021, 015-022).

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis does not take sides explicitly, but his structural choices reveal a clear skepticism toward the progressive view. The Eden reference in Chapter 6 is undercut by the chapter's argument: the earth is not warming toward another Eden but cooling toward universal death. The Paradise of Chapter 11 is the object of repeated historical delusion — Columbus found America, not Eden; Luther's deadline passed; every subsequent date-setting scheme failed.

> The primates, we and the monkeys, originated in the Arctic Circle; Peary found Eden. Greenland was once green; amid her Icy Mountains did not the breadfruit trees once wave? (006-020)

> though that new world turned out to be only America, he had discovered it in mistake for Paradise; which he thought lay eastward of India, for which he set sail ere it should be too late. (011-015)

> Evolution is creative, not passive; Man makes, not encounters, the circumstances before him; makes, not suffers, himself. He is the path and the pathfinder; they are one; they lead to Paradise. (015-021)

The final mention — "they lead to Paradise" — is the survivalists' peroration, placed by Dennis immediately before the pessimists' rebuttal. The structural effect is to make Paradise the hinge of the book's central debate: is the human project heading toward a garden or a grave?
