# Milky Way

The spiral galaxy that contains our solar system — a vast archipelago of some hundred billion stars, one of which is our sun — which Dennis uses as a yardstick to convey the scale of the cosmos whose death he contemplates.

## Location

The Milky Way is a barred spiral galaxy approximately 100,000 light-years in diameter, containing an estimated 100–400 billion stars. Our solar system occupies a minor arm of this galaxy, approximately 26,000 light-years from the galactic center. From earth, the Milky Way appears as a luminous band across the night sky — an edge-on view of our own galactic disk. For Dennis, the Milky Way is not merely our cosmic address but the first step in a journey outward that reveals the terrifying scale of the universe.

## In the Book

### Context

The Milky Way appears in Chapter 17 ("What After?") as part of Dennis's journey outward from earth: "Traverse the Milky Way, through darkness, past single suns, binary suns... through droves and broods of suns, great blazing archipelagos of suns" (017-008). The galaxy is presented as a way-station on a journey that extends far beyond it — to other galaxies, other clusters, other island-universes — none of which, however remote, attains infinity.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis uses the Milky Way as a device of scale and wonder. His prose rises to meet the subject: "through darkness, past single suns, binary suns, steady and variable suns, dwarf and giant suns, protean families of suns, through droves and broods of suns, great blazing archipelagos of suns" (017-008). The language is almost incantatory — a cosmic litany that accumulates detail until the reader's sense of scale is overwhelmed, at which point Dennis delivers the argument: even this is finite.

## Significance

The Milky Way is, for Dennis, the first lesson in cosmic humility. Our galaxy, which once seemed to be the entire universe, is merely one among billions. And all of them — every star in every galaxy — will one day go dark.
