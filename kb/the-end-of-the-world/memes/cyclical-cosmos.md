# Cyclical Cosmos

The hypothesis that the universe does not proceed in one irreversible direction from birth to death, but cycles endlessly — each destruction followed by a new creation, each death by a new birth — a possibility Dennis raises and refutes within the framework of thermodynamic physics.

## Definition

A cyclical cosmos is one in which the end of the world (or of the universe) is not final but periodic — a phase in an eternal rhythm of destruction and regeneration. Dennis traces this idea through multiple traditions: the Stoic *ekpyrosis* (conflagration that periodically consumes and renews the cosmos), the Hindu cycles of *yugas*, Nietzsche's eternal recurrence, and the modern astrophysical hypothesis that "cyclic collisions, systole and diastole of worlds, release fresh energy forever" (017-005). The cyclical cosmos is the oldest and most persistent alternative to the linear, terminal eschatology that dominates Judeo-Christian thought — and, in Dennis's reading, modern thermodynamics.

## In the Book

### Context

Dennis addresses the cyclical cosmos most directly in Chapter 17 ("What After?"), where it is the first objection to entropy that the "rebellious mind" raises. The question is straightforward: if the universe is subject to cyclical collisions — expanding and contracting, igniting and extinguishing — then perhaps the calorific death never truly arrives. Each cycle would reset the clock. The cosmos would have no beginning and no end; it would simply pulse.

Dennis also touches on cyclical thinking in Chapter 7 ("Crash"), where the idea that "collision makes; collision kills" (007-025) hints at a cyclical relationship between creation and destruction. And in Chapter 1 ("How?"), the nebular hypothesis itself implies a kind of cycle: a contracting cloud that, after its stellar phase, might be disrupted by collision and begin again.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis treats the cyclical cosmos with intellectual respect but ultimate rejection. His counter-argument is thermodynamic: each cycle would produce less available energy than the last. "They are mere accidents, incidents, powerless to stem or deflect the stream ineluctable of sidereal evolution. They are dying flares, each time paltrier" (017-005). The cyclical cosmos is not a perpetual motion machine but a damped oscillator — each swing smaller, each fire dimmer, until the final stillness.

> Could not the cyclic collisions, systole and diastole of worlds, release fresh energy forever? — They are mere accidents, incidents, powerless to stem or deflect the stream ineluctable of sidereal evolution. (017-005)

The image of "dying flares, each time paltrier" is characteristically vivid. Dennis's language here is almost elegiac: the cyclical cosmos is a beautiful idea, and it is with something like regret that he consigns it to the category of wishful thinking. The universe, he concludes, is not a phoenix. It burns once and is gone.

Dennis's treatment is notable for what it does not do: he does not mock the cyclical cosmos as primitive or pre-scientific. He treats it as a serious intellectual position with ancient pedigree and modern defenders, and he refutes it on its own terms — with physics, not rhetoric. This respect for defeated arguments is one of the book's defining qualities.
