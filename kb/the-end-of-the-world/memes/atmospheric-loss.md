# Atmospheric Loss

The gradual escape of the earth's gaseous envelope into space, a process Dennis invokes to explain why the cooling of the sun will be doubly fatal: the earth will lose its thermal blanket just as the furnace dims.

## Definition

Atmospheric loss, as Dennis deploys it, is the thinning and eventual disappearance of the air that surrounds and protects the planet. The precedent is the moon: a body that once possessed an atmosphere and now has none. "Our vanishing air — not only the vanishing water in it — secretly collaborates. It is going the way of the moon's lost atmosphere" (006-006). The mechanism is gravitational: lighter molecules, energized by solar radiation, reach escape velocity and drift into space. As the atmosphere thins, its capacity to retain heat diminishes — a positive feedback loop that accelerates cooling. Without an atmosphere, the earth's surface temperature would plummet to something like that of the airless moon.

## In the Book

### Context

Dennis introduces atmospheric loss in Chapter 6 ("Cold") as one of several cooperating mechanisms that make the cold-death scenario compelling. The atmosphere is not a permanent fixture but a temporary accretion, held by gravity against the constant leakage of molecules into space. Water vapor — a powerful greenhouse gas — is especially vulnerable. As Dennis notes, "Water, which absorbs, retains and gives out again the heat the sun sendeth, in slowly disappearing from the atmosphere is rendering it slowly colder" (006-005). The loss of water from the atmosphere is a double blow: less heat retained, and less water available for life.

The concept also connects to Chapter 3 ("Fire"), where the atmosphere is a medium through which destructive forces travel, and to Chapter 15 ("Man"), where the biological consequences of atmospheric change — "the chemical causes of cerebral energy" (015-011) — are linked to the composition of the air we breathe.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis treats atmospheric loss as a quiet catastrophe — an end that is already in progress but too slow to notice. The atmosphere is thinning by a few molecules per second per square meter; over geological time, this is a fatal hemorrhage. The collaboration between atmospheric loss and solar cooling is central to his argument: neither alone would necessarily doom life, but together they form an inescapable trap.

> It is going the way of the moon's lost atmosphere; with it will go our best protection from the cold; without it we shall succumb. It will lessen as the sun's heat lessens, just when we need it most. (006-006)

The passage is notable for its quiet, almost clinical tone. Unlike the drama of cometary collision or the poetry of decreation, atmospheric loss is presented as a matter-of-fact physical process. There is no spectacle in it, no fire or flood — just a leak, persistent and irreversible, that will one day leave the earth as barren as its satellite.

Dennis's most striking observation is that atmospheric loss is uncoupled from human agency. Unlike climate change caused by industrialization (a concept that barely existed when Dennis was writing), this is a process wholly indifferent to human action. No technological fix can plug the leak at the top of the atmosphere. The air, like the sun that sustains it, is finite.
