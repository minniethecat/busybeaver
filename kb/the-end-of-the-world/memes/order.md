# Order

The principle of regularity and structure — cosmic, moral, mathematical — that Dennis treats ambivalently: as a real feature of the universe whose stars move "on some regular plan, unknown but harmonious" (007-013), and as a human projection whose evidence is "considerable" only "to minds which forget they are cage-bound" (017-032).

## Definition

Order in Dennis's usage spans several distinct but interrelated meanings: (1) **cosmic order** — the regular, predictable motion of celestial bodies, "the stars have their orbits like the planets" (007-013); (2) **teleological order** — the question of whether the universe exhibits design or purpose, "evidence of some plan or scheme... of a measure of presiding harmony" (017-032); and (3) **moral order** — the notion that the universe is structured by justice, that righteousness is rewarded and wickedness punished. Dennis is sympathetic to the first, skeptical of the second, and largely silent on the third.

The concept's importance in *The End of the World* lies in its relationship to catastrophe. The order of the heavens is real but not absolute: "disasters disprove it" (007-017). The collision that destroys the world is not an exception to cosmic law but an inevitable consequence of it — given enough time, the orderly motion of stars will, by the very laws that govern it, produce disorder. Order and chaos are not opposites in Dennis's cosmos; they are phases of the same process. "Out of the chance riot of stars came worlds... In new chance riot we shall depart. The universe forms, un-forms, re-forms" (007-026).

## In the Book

### Context

Order appears most prominently in Chapter 7 ("Collision"), where Dennis confronts the tension between the apparent harmony of the heavens and the inevitability of catastrophic collision. The chapter opens with the measured claim that the stars move in regular orbits and that "a collision only happens when something goes wrong" (007-013), but immediately complicates this with the observation that, on a cosmic timescale, something is always going wrong. The order of the heavens is statistical, not absolute — a temporary arrangement within a longer rhythm of formation and dissolution.

Order also appears in the philosophical reflections of Chapter 17 ("In the Mind of an Angel"), Dennis's final synthesis. Here the question shifts from cosmic mechanics to epistemology: what kind of evidence for design is available to a creature whose perspective is irreducibly limited? "Evidence of some plan or scheme, of a certain order within certain ordered limits, of a measure of presiding harmony, is considerable; to minds which forget they are cage-bound, and give to the pattern they themselves have woven the name of cosmic law" (017-032). The qualification is devastating: order may be real, but our perception of it is shaped by the very cognitive structures we bring to the observation.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis's treatment of order is characteristic of his larger method: he grants the empirical evidence for regularity while withholding the metaphysical conclusion that regularity implies design. The stars do move in ordered patterns, and the fact that collisions are rare is evidence of this order. But Dennis insists on the full temporal picture: order is a phase, not a permanent condition. The harmony of the spheres is real while it lasts; it will not last forever.

This temporal qualification distinguishes Dennis from both the theistic optimist (who sees order as evidence of benevolence) and the nihilistic pessimist (who denies order altogether). Dennis's position is closer to that of a long-view astronomer: the universe is structured, but the structure is dynamic, and dissolution is built into its logic. "Some order in the star-streams, harmony of the heavens, music of the spheres, is predictable; the Empyrean as a perfect mechanism without flaw or disturbance is not" (007-017).
