# Infinity

The concept that Dennis deploys in Chapter 17 ("In the Mind of an Angel") as the limit case of human reason — the question ("Is the Universe infinite?") to which every answer is inconceivable, and the theological and scientific fantasies of boundlessness that collapse under their own weight when examined.

## Definition

Infinity in Dennis's usage takes three principal forms: (1) **spatial infinity** — the question of whether the universe extends without limit, "empty ether stretching out to infinity" (017-011); (2) **temporal infinity** — the question of whether time has a beginning or end, "Time is boundless. There is time for this" (002-039); and (3) **mathematical infinity** — the abstract infinite of "wearying zeros" (017-008) that appears in cosmological models like de Sitter's.

Dennis's treatment of infinity is predominantly negative — not in the sense of refutation, but in the sense of establishing its unthinkability. "Finite or infinite, both are inconceivable. Brains nor hearts can imagine neither edge nor rim, end nor beginning; nor can imagine the world without margin or limit, without beginning or end. Both are totally equally irrevocably inconceivable" (017-042). Infinity is not a concept that can be grasped; it is a limit that reason encounters and cannot cross. The very attempt to think infinity produces contradiction.

The practical import of this epistemological limit is that the question of infinity cannot be resolved by more data or better theory. The problem is structural — a consequence of the fact that "we are in it, we are of it, we are it; but what it is we do not know, world without beginning, world without end" (017-026).

## In the Book

### Context

Infinity receives its fullest treatment in Chapter 17, Dennis's philosophical conclusion. The chapter is structured as a series of questions — "Is the Universe finite? Is it infinite? Will it end?" — each pursued to the point where reason exhausts itself. Infinity is the framing question: the universe's spatial and temporal extent determines which eschatological scenario is possible, but the question cannot be answered.

Dennis situates his discussion of infinity within the scientific cosmology of his time. He references de Sitter's model of an expanding universe, the entropy argument for a finite cosmos, and the observational evidence from Mount Wilson. But the scientific data does not resolve the philosophical problem. "Space is not infinite. Leave Neptune behind and, cutting a path through the void, strike for the nearest star: Proxima Centauri, but twenty-six million million miles away, four and a quarter years at light-speed" (017-008). What science measures is always finite; the question of whether there is anything beyond the measurable is beyond its scope.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis's method in treating infinity is characteristically recursive: he pursues both horns of the dilemma — finite and infinite — and finds each equally impossible to accept. If the universe is infinite, then either it is cyclic (the same events repeating eternally) or evolutionary (perpetually creating new configurations). Both alternatives generate paradoxes. If the universe is finite, then the entropy argument applies: "Physical entropy will include psychical entropy, matter-death spirit-death" (017-044). Either way, the mind cannot rest in its conclusion.

This recursive structure is Dennis's deepest philosophical move. Infinity is not a concept to be mastered but a limit to be acknowledged. The appropriate response is not belief or disbelief but what Dennis calls "the reality unseizable, unattainable" (017-026) — a posture of intellectual humility that the chapter's title, "In the Mind of an Angel," ironizes: even an angelic intelligence, Dennis implies, would find the question of infinity unanswerable.

> Finite or infinite, both are inconceivable. Brains nor hearts can imagine neither edge nor rim, end nor beginning; nor can imagine the world without margin or limit, without beginning or end. Both are totally equally irrevocably inconceivable. (017-042)
