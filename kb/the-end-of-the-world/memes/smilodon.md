# Smilodon

**Smilodon**, commonly called the saber-toothed cat or saber-toothed tiger, was one of the most specialized predators in mammalian evolutionary history. Its extinction — simultaneous with that of much of its prey — is a textbook illustration of the ecological interdependence that makes species vulnerable to cascading collapse, a pattern that Dennis's argument about world-ending extends from the biological to the planetary scale.

## Identity

*Smilodon fatalis* inhabited North and South America during the Pleistocene, with the largest species (*S. populator*) reaching weights of up to 400 kilograms. Its defining feature was its pair of elongated canine teeth, up to 28 centimeters in length, which were used to deliver a precise killing bite to the throat or abdomen of large prey. This was a highly specialized hunting strategy, dependent on a suite of anatomical adaptations: powerful forelimbs for grappling, a jaw that could open to an angle of 120 degrees, and neck muscles capable of driving the canines deep into flesh.

This specialization was extraordinarily effective while the Pleistocene megafauna — giant bison, horses, camels, ground sloths, young mammoths — remained abundant. But specialization is a double-edged sword: what makes a species successful under one set of conditions makes it vulnerable when those conditions change. When the large herbivores of the Americas declined at the end of the Pleistocene, *Smilodon* had no alternative prey base to fall back on.

## Extinction Narrative

*Smilodon* went extinct approximately 10,000 years ago, as part of the end-Pleistocene megafaunal extinction that eliminated most of the large mammals of the Americas. The immediate cause was the disappearance of its prey species — [[megatherium]], horses, camels, and young [[woolly-mammoth|mammoths]] — which themselves fell victim to the combined pressures of climate change and human hunting. *Smilodon* was not directly hunted to extinction; rather, its ecological support system was pulled out from under it.

This is the point that connects *Smilodon* to Dennis's larger argument. Extinction is rarely a single cause operating on a single species; it is a cascade, a network failure, in which the disappearance of one species destabilizes others, which in turn destabilize still others, until an entire ecosystem collapses. The saber-toothed cat died not because it was weak or maladapted but because the world it was adapted to — a world of giant herbivores and open grasslands — ceased to exist, taking the predator with it.
