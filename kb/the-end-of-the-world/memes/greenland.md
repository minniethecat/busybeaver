Greenland appears in Dennis's book as a single, striking image — an icy wasteland that was once warm enough for tropical vegetation, proving the mutability of climate across geological time.

## Location

Greenland is the world's largest island, lying between the Arctic and Atlantic Oceans, east of the Canadian Arctic Archipelago. Today it is largely covered by an ice sheet, but its fossil record reveals a much warmer past.

## In the Book

### Context

Greenland is invoked in "Cold"（006）, in the closing passage of the chapter where Dennis surveys the geological evidence for radical climate shifts. It serves as the culminating image in a series of fossil-based arguments that the Earth's temperature has changed dramatically before — and will change again.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis presents Greenland in a single rhetorical question that encapsulates his argument: "Greenland was once green; amid her Icy Mountains did not the breadfruit trees once wave?"（006-020）. The image is deliberately paradoxical — ice and breadfruit in the same sentence — to dramatize the scale of climatic reversal. The breadfruit tree, a tropical species, could only have grown in Greenland under vastly warmer conditions. For Dennis, this is not merely a curiosity of paleontology but evidence that the cold death he fears is the natural terminus of a cycle already in motion.

> Greenland was once green; amid her Icy Mountains did not the breadfruit trees once wave?（006-020）

## Significance

Greenland functions as a compressed symbol of Dennis's entire cold-death thesis. In one sentence, he conjures a world turned upside down — ice where life once flourished. The line resonates with the chapter's earlier image of the Arctic Circle as the birthplace of primates, reinforcing the argument that the habitable zone of the Earth is not fixed but wanders, and that its next migration may be away from us forever.
