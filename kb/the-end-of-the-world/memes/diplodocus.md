# Diplodocus

**Diplodocus** was one of the most distinctive of the giant sauropods — a creature of extraordinary length and relatively slender build, adapted with surgical precision to its Jurassic environment. In the logic of Dennis's extinction argument, *Diplodocus* illustrates a crucial point: that extreme [[adaptation]] to specific conditions is itself a form of vulnerability. The more perfectly a species fits its world, the more completely it is undone when that world changes.

## Identity

*Diplodocus carnegii* lived during the Late Jurassic, approximately 154 to 152 million years ago, in what is now western North America. It was among the longest dinosaurs ever discovered, reaching lengths of up to 25 meters, though its relatively slender build meant it weighed only 10–16 metric tons — far less than the bulkier [[brontosaurus]]. Its most distinctive feature was its extraordinarily long, whip-like tail, which may have been used for defense, communication, or both.

*Diplodocus* was a herbivore that fed on low to medium-height vegetation, using its peg-like teeth to strip leaves from branches. Its long neck allowed it to cover a wide feeding arc without moving its massive body — an elegant solution to the energetic challenges of gigantism. The genus is known from numerous well-preserved specimens, including the famous *Diplodocus carnegii* skeleton that Andrew Carnegie donated to museums worldwide, making it one of the most-viewed dinosaurs in history.

## Extinction Narrative

*Diplodocus* vanished at the end of the Jurassic, part of a broader turnover in sauropod diversity that preceded the terminal K-Pg extinction. The causes are complex — changing sea levels, climate shifts, floral turnover — but the pattern is consistent with the book's framework: extinction is not a single event but a process that operates continuously, pruning lineages across all timescales.

Dennis's argument does not require that every [[dinosaur]] died in the same catastrophe. What matters is that they *all died* — eventually, completely, without exception. *Diplodocus*, like [[brontosaurus]] and [[tyrannosaurus-rex]], is a data point in the great cumulative case that Dennis builds: that extinction is the rule, survival the exception, and that humanity's apparent exemption from this rule is a temporary condition.
