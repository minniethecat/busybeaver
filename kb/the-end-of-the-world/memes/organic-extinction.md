# Organic Extinction

The death of all living things on earth — a concept Dennis distinguishes carefully from the end of the world in the physical sense, noting that the two events may be separated by vast intervals of time and involve entirely different mechanisms.

## Definition

Organic extinction, in Dennis's precise usage, means the disappearance of every living organism — from humans to microbes — not the destruction of the planet as a material body. Dennis draws a sharp distinction: "Earth's journey with living passengers is like to be much shorter than her time as a tomb" (015-002). The earth may continue to orbit the sun for quintillions of years after the last bacterium has perished. This temporal gap between biological death and physical destruction is a recurring theme, and Dennis uses it to argue that the end of the world is really two ends: one for life, one for matter.

## In the Book

### Context

Dennis addresses extinction most directly in Chapter 15 ("Man"), but the concept underpins much of the book. Each chapter on a specific mode of ending — fire, water, drought, cold — is implicitly about the extinction of life, since the planet itself would survive all of them except stellar collision. Dennis is careful to note that human extinction and total extinction are distinct: "Man going need not mean all life going" (015-003). Some organisms — "salamanders if it is Fire," "parasites who dote on congelation, if it be the Cold" — would outlast humanity. But even these survivors would persist only for "a space so short" on the geological scale that Dennis treats human and total extinction as effectively simultaneous (015-003).

The concept also surfaces in Chapter 17 ("What After?"), where the thermodynamic argument guarantees that "all life, light, movement, vibration will be over" (017-003) — not just human life but all life, everywhere, forever. And in Chapter 6 ("Cold"), the chilling of the planet is specifically a biological end: the earth survives, frozen but intact, while every living thing upon it perishes.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis's treatment of extinction is marked by a kind of equanimity that borders on the unsettling. He does not lament; he catalogues. The book's dispassionate tone is perhaps most striking in passages that describe the total annihilation of the biosphere as if it were a chemical reaction reaching equilibrium.

> Those who could survive us (and most will succumb sooner) would survive for a space so short that here it will be ignored altogether, and human extinction held to imply and include the extinction of every living thing. (015-003)

This rhetorical move — collapsing all extinction into human extinction for convenience — reveals Dennis's anthropocentric baseline, even as he is ostensibly transcending it. The extinction of "every living thing" matters because it includes us; the extinction of the planet matters because it includes the extinction of life. Dennis never quite resolves the tension between his cosmic perspective (we are a momentary flicker on a minor planet) and his human perspective (that flicker is everything to us).

The most philosophically significant passage on extinction comes near the end of the book, where Dennis links biological death to the more fundamental thermodynamic death: "Physical entropy will include psychical entropy, matter-death spirit-death. It is the supernatural stalemate, the Wärmetod of Souls" (017-044). In this vision, organic extinction is not merely the cessation of biological processes but the extinction of consciousness itself — the final darkening of every mind that ever thought about the end of the world.
