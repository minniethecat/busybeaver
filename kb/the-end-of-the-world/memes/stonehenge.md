# Stonehenge

The prehistoric monument in southern England — a ring of standing stones weighing up to thirty tons, aligned with the solstices — which Dennis cites in his global survey of solar worship as evidence that the human impulse to track and revere the sun predates all written records.

## Location

Stonehenge stands on Salisbury Plain in Wiltshire, England. Built in several phases between approximately 3000 and 2000 BCE, its most famous feature is the alignment of its central axis with the midsummer sunrise and midwinter sunset. Dennis includes it in his solar temple catalogue: "many temples from Persia to Mexico, from Stonehenge to Xauxa" (006-009). Stonehenge is the earliest monument in this catalogue, predating the Roman, Egyptian, Persian, and Inca temples by millennia.

## In the Book

### Context

Stonehenge appears in Chapter 6 ("Cold") as the earliest entry in Dennis's survey of sacred solar sites. Its placement at the beginning of the chronological sequence is significant: it establishes that the human relationship with the sun is not a product of civilization but precedes it, reaching back into prehistory.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis treats Stonehenge with the same respect he accords to the great telescopes of Mount Wilson. Both are instruments for observing the sky; both are products of the same impulse to understand the cosmic order. The difference — stone alignments versus glass lenses — is technological, not spiritual. Dennis's refusal to condescend to prehistoric astronomy is characteristic of his method.

## Significance

Stonehenge represents the deepest stratum of Dennis's intellectual history of eschatology — the recognition that human beings have been tracking the sun's movements for at least five thousand years, and that the fear of cosmic disorder is as old as the monuments built to ward against it.
