# Fate

In Dennis's treatment, fate is the question of whether the world's end is contingent or necessary — something that might be averted, or something sealed from the beginning.

## Definition

Fate, from Latin *fatum* ("that which has been spoken"), is the idea that events are predetermined and inevitable. In classical thought, fate (*moira*, *heimarmene*) binds even the gods; in Christian theology, fate is subsumed under divine providence — events are predetermined, but by a personal God with intentions. Dennis draws on both traditions, and on the modern scientific notion of causal determinism, to ask whether the end of the world is something that happens *to* us or something that was always inscribed in the structure of things.

The question is not merely academic. If the world's end is fated, then human action is irrelevant — we can neither hasten nor delay it. If it is contingent, then human choices matter, and the book's catalog of possible deaths becomes not a preview of inevitability but a menu of risks to be managed.

## In the Book

### Context

Fate appears at critical junctures across several chapters. In the comet chapter (002), it enters through the ancient belief that comets and the fates of rulers are linked: "between the fiery visitant and the fate of princes, between comets and lungs" (002-013) — a sardonic coupling of the cosmic and the pulmonary that captures Dennis's method of juxtaposing the sublime with the mundane.

The collision chapter (007) gives fate its starkest formulation: "Collision, the one fate that will outstay the others" (007-024). Here fate is cosmic and impersonal — no amount of human ingenuity can prevent a sufficiently large impactor from ending all terrestrial life. "Collision, the one fate that destroys not only man but the whole platform of his existence" (007-025) — the planet itself is vulnerable, and with it every human project.

In the human-response chapter (006), fate meets resistance: "Man will fight his fate. He will adapt himself" (006-024). Dennis grants human agency its due — we are not passive before fate — but the chapter's arc bends toward the recognition that adaptation only delays, never defeats. The human capacity to "fight his fate" is real but bounded; beyond a certain threshold of cosmic violence, agency evaporates.

The prophecy chapter (010) re-frames fate in historical terms. "No latter year of fate has had quite such a following" (010-107) as 1917 — a year that, like 1000 and 1033 and 1844 before it, concentrated apocalyptic expectation into a single date. Each "year of fate" is a wager on determinism: if the end is scheduled, knowing the schedule is salvation. When the date passes uneventfully, the prophecy is revised, and fate is deferred.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis treats fate with the same ambivalence he brings to all theological concepts. He documents the long history of fate-reading — in comets, in prophecy, in astronomical calculation — without adjudicating among its claims. His own voice emerges most clearly in the collision chapter, where fate is stripped of supernatural connotation and becomes simply the name for a physical certainty: eventually, something will hit us.

> Collision, the one fate that destroys not only man but the whole platform of his existence, deciding the fate of each phase of existence. (007-025, 010-115)

The repetition of "fate" across chapters links the personal, the political, and the cosmic. A king's death is fated (002-013); a planet's destruction is fated (007-024); and somewhere between these scales, each individual life meets its own fate — "tears for man's fate, rain of blood, rivers of blood" (010-037). Dennis does not resolve these scales into a single doctrine; he holds them in tension, and the tension is the point.
