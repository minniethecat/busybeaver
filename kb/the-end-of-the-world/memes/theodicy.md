# Theodicy

The philosophical and theological problem of justifying God's goodness in the face of evil — the unstated question that, in Dennis's implicit argument, every eschatology attempts to answer by projecting justice onto the end of time.

## Definition

Theodicy, from the Greek *theos* (God) and *dike* (justice), is the attempt to reconcile the existence of a good, all-powerful God with the manifest existence of evil and suffering. The term was coined by Leibniz in 1710, but the problem is as old as monotheism itself. If God is good, God would want to prevent evil; if God is all-powerful, God could prevent evil; evil exists; therefore either God is not good, or God is not all-powerful, or God does not exist.

Dennis never uses the term "theodicy" directly — it does not appear in the chapter corpus — but the problem animates his entire survey of eschatological thought. Every end-of-world scenario that includes a judgment (and most do) is, at bottom, a theodicy: a claim that the apparent injustice of the present world will be corrected at the end of time. The eschatological imagination is a theodicy machine — it takes the raw material of present suffering and projects a future in which that suffering is retroactively justified.

## In the Book

### Context

The theodicy problem surfaces most clearly in Dennis's treatment of Christian eschatology. The early Church's expectation of Christ's imminent return was driven, Dennis argues, by the experience of persecution: "Christianity, still a persecuted creed offering no hope of present triumph, needed some such faith in not too distant punishment" (010-022). The delay of justice was the problem; the Apocalypse was the solution.

In Chapter 15 ("Man"), the theodicy problem takes secular form. The survivalists argue that human progress itself constitutes a kind of theodicy — that the suffering of past generations is justified by the achievements of future ones. The pessimists reject this as an evasion, insisting that no amount of future progress can justify present pain. The debate is unresolved, and Dennis's refusal to close it is itself a philosophical position: theodicy is a problem that admits of no solution, only of various more or less honest ways of living with it.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis treats theodicy as a deep structure rather than a surface argument. He never addresses it head-on, but his relentless catalog of failed prophecies, disappointed expectations, and the sheer indifference of cosmic processes to human values amounts to a sustained critique of every theodicy. The universe, Dennis implies, does not owe us an explanation — and the demand for one may be the deepest and most dangerous of human illusions.

> Christianity, still a persecuted creed offering no hope of present triumph, needed some such faith in not too distant punishment. (010-022)
