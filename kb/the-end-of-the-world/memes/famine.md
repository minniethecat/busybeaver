# Famine

**Famine** — in Dennis's enumeration of the ways the world may end — is not a single cataclysmic event but a **distributed [[disaster|catastrophe]]**: death by deprivation, operating at the scale of millions, slow enough to be accepted as normal, lethal enough to rival any comet or [[fire|conflagration]]. Famine appears in the book as both a traditional eschatological sign and a contemporary fact of "organized hunger which holds nearly half this best of all possible worlds in its cruel grip" [001-015].

## Overview

Famine is widespread scarcity of food caused by drought, crop failure, war, or economic collapse. Throughout human history, famine has been among the deadliest phenomena — killing through starvation, [[plague|disease]], and social breakdown. In Dennis's framework, famine occupies a unique position: it is **simultaneously ancient and modern**, a biblical curse that persists in the age of mechanized agriculture, proving that technological progress does not automatically end human suffering.

## In the Book

### Context

Famine appears across multiple chapters: in Chapter 1 ("How?") as a mode of individual death through drought, in Chapter 2 ("Comet") as one of the historic [[catastrophe|disaster]]s following celestial omens, in Chapter 5 ("Drought") as drought's offspring, and in Chapter 10 ("Next Year") and Chapter 11 ("Some [[time|Time]]") as one of the standard end-time signs alongside earthquakes and [[plague|pestilence]].

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis treats famine on two fronts. **As personal fate**, famine is one of the many ways an individual life can end — "desire of water, if he fall of thirst in the desert or fail in his multitudes through drought and following famine" [001-015]. **As collective [[fate|doom]]**, famine is a sign that the entire social order has failed:

> The toll of hazard is high, every step forward by mechanical science increasing both the likelihood and the variety of murderous mischance. [001-015]

> These dire predictions proved usually right. [002-007]

Dennis also connects famine to the apocalyptic [[prophecy|prediction]]s of his own time. The Dallas Prophetical Society, looking toward 1931, listed famine among the fulfilled signs: "Every sign has indeed been accomplished: famines and [[plague|pestilence]]s and earthquakes" [010-109].

> In one week the inundation drowned twenty thousand in the Delta, in one year the pestilence slew thirty thousand in the Urbs. [011-010]

## Significance

Famine is, for Dennis, the **most human of the end-time signs** — not a cosmic accident but a failure of distribution, organization, and will. Unlike a comet strike or a [[milky-way|galactic]] collision, famine is a [[disaster|catastrophe]] that human beings could, in principle, prevent. That they do not — that "organized hunger" persists — is itself a kind of evidence for the book's deeper pessimism: the world ends not only from forces beyond our control but from our own indifference.

The recurrence of famine across every era — classical, biblical, medieval, modern — reinforces Dennis's structural point: the mode of death may vary, but death itself is constant. Famine, like earthquake and war, is a reminder that the end is always happening somewhere, to someone, and that the global end will simply be the universalization of what is already locally routine.
