# Stegosaurus

**Stegosaurus** — the "roof lizard" — is among the most instantly recognizable of all [[dinosaur|dinosaurs]], its alternating row of bony plates and spiked tail (the "thagomizer") making it unmistakable in the fossil record and in the popular imagination. In the context of Dennis's extinction argument, *Stegosaurus* embodies the principle of evolutionary specialization as prelude to extinction: the features that made it successful in the Jurassic became liabilities when the world changed.

## Identity

*Stegosaurus stenops* lived during the Late Jurassic, approximately 155 to 150 million years ago, in what is now western North America. Adults reached lengths of 9 meters and weighed up to 5 metric tons. Its most distinctive features — the alternating double row of large bony plates along its back and the four long spikes on its tail — have been the subject of intense paleontological debate, with theories about the plates' function ranging from thermoregulation to display to species recognition. The tail spikes were almost certainly defensive, capable of inflicting serious injury on Jurassic predators.

Despite its formidable defenses, *Stegosaurus* was a herbivore with a brain famously small relative to its body size — a feature long interpreted as evidence of limited behavioral flexibility. Modern paleontology has complicated this picture, but the core point for Dennis's argument remains: organisms are shaped by their environments, and when those environments change, the shapes that once served become hindrances.

## Extinction Narrative

*Stegosaurus* went extinct at the end of the Jurassic, approximately 150 million years ago, though the broader stegosaurian lineage persisted into the mid-Cretaceous before vanishing entirely around 100 million years ago. The Jurassic-Cretaceous transition saw significant floral changes, with the spread of flowering plants altering herbivore food sources. Specialized feeders like *Stegosaurus*, with its narrow beak and limited dentition, may have been particularly vulnerable to these changes.

The pattern is the one Dennis identifies throughout the fossil record: organisms become ever more finely tuned to their specific environments, only to discover that environments are not permanent. The very features that represent evolutionary success — the plates, the spikes, the specialized feeding apparatus — become, in a changed world, a burden that natural selection cannot overcome quickly enough.
