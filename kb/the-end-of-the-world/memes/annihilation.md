# Annihilation

**Annihilation** is destruction taken to its logical extreme: not the transformation of something into something else but its reduction to nothing. In eschatological terms, it names the possibility that the end is not merely an ending but an un-making — a return to the nothingness that preceded creation.

## Mutual Annihilation

The text describes celestial bodies that "bombard each other, whose mutual annihilation secures their common eternity" (006-016). The paradox is striking: mutual destruction as the guarantor of permanence. Two forces, each capable of destroying the other, hold each other in check — their capacity for annihilation is precisely what prevents either from exercising it. This is a vision of cosmic order born from the threat of its dissolution.

> Bodies that bombard each other, whose mutual annihilation secures their common eternity.
> (006-016)

## Astral Annihilation

The "terminal collision of astral annihilation" (007-008) represents the endpoint of cosmic trajectories. Unlike the mutual annihilation that secures stability, this is unilateral and final — the collision that ends rather than balances. It is the eschatological moment rendered as physics: bodies moving along paths that converge on destruction.

## Annihilation and Identity

Annihilation raises the deepest question of eschatology: does the end mean transformation or disappearance? To be annihilated is not to become something else — it is to cease to be anything at all. This is the terror that distinguishes annihilation from mere death or destruction, which leave traces, remains, memories.

## Philosophical Dimensions

In philosophical terms, annihilation touches the problem of being and nothingness. If something can be annihilated, then being is not necessary — it is contingent, capable of being revoked. The eschatological fear of annihilation is thus also a metaphysical anxiety: that existence itself has no guarantee, that the fact of being offers no security against the possibility of non-being.
