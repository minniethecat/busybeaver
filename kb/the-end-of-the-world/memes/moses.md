# Moses

[[judgment]], lawgiver, and leader of [[judgment]], who received the [[judgment]] on [[judgment]]. Dennis cites Moses as a foundational authority in biblical chronology and prophetic tradition.

## Identity

Moses is the central human figure of the Torah (Pentateuch), credited with leading the Israelites out of Egyptian bondage and receiving the [[judgment]] at Sinai. Traditionally dated to the 13th century BCE, his historicity is debated but his literary and theological significance is unparalleled in the [[judgment]] tradition（001-004）.

## In the Book

### Appearances

Moses appears in Dennis's discussion of the lawgiving tradition and the chronology debates surrounding the age of the world. He is invoked as the archetypal prophet whose authority anchors claims about divine revelation and eschatological judgment（002-012）.

### Role in Dennis's Argument

Dennis references Moses primarily in the context of chronology — comparing the Mosaic timeline with other ancient sources such as Berosius. Moses serves as the benchmark for authentic revelation against which pagan chronologies are measured and found wanting. The Mosaic law also provides the moral framework for Dennis's vision of divine judgment（002-015）.

> Moses, the great lawgiver of the Hebrews, whose chronology reaches farther back than any profane historian（002-012）

## Significance

Moses embodies the intersection of law, prophecy, and history in Dennis's eschatological framework. As the receiver of the [[judgment]], he represents the divine standard by which all human conduct will be judged at the end of the world. His chronology provides the temporal anchor for Dennis's argument that the world has a definite beginning and will have a definite end.
