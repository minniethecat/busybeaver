Julius Caesar was a [[Roman]] military leader and dictator whose death and posthumous deification — marked by the appearance of a comet in 43 BCE — became a recurring motif in discussions of cosmic signs and portents in the ancient world.

## In the Book

### Caesar and Rome's Civil Wars

> in her bloodiest civil wars: Caesar and Pompey and those eighteen centuries of iron paganism, the Rome that went forth slaughtering the legions [002-011]

The same passage underscores the gravity of Caesar's absence, noting that even after his death, Rome was to "accomplish her destiny" [002-011].

### The Comet of 43 BCE

Following Caesar's assassination, a comet appeared in the sky in 43 BCE — the "Caesaris astrum" — which was interpreted by Romans as a sign of his deification. This event later fed into early Christian apocalyptic literature, where comets were routinely read as harbingers of divine judgment. The corpus devotes an entire chapter to the taxonomy and theological significance of comets:

> According to the latter, a comet is a heavenly body of debatable origin and meaning [002-003]

The "old astrologers" viewed comets as portents of catastrophe, linking them to the deaths of rulers and the fall of cities [002-002].

### Rome After Caesar

The corpus connects Caesar's era to the moral decay that later Roman authors — and Christian apologists — would cite as evidence of divine judgment awaiting the empire. After Caesar, Rome experienced the rule of Augustus and the Julio-Claudian dynasty:

> Such as Augustus; such as Seneca, the stoic moralist [002-016]

The passage links the philosophical currents — including the cynics present in Rome — to the imperial milieu Caesar helped create.

## Significance

### Chronographic Legacy

A separate strand of the corpus mentions Julius Africanus, a third-century Christian chronographer whose name echoes the Julian legacy in early Christian historiography:

> also Cerinthus, Apollinaris, Julius Africanus, Nepos of Egypt [010-023]

Julius Africanus would compile one of the first Christian universal chronicles, situating Roman history within biblical chronology — a project that Caesar's calendar reform had inadvertently enabled.

### Civil War as Eschatological Archetype

The corpus uses the Roman civil wars — Caesar against Pompey, and the conflicts that followed — as an archetype of societal collapse and the end of political orders. This pattern recurs throughout eschatological literature: the dissolution of a great power is read as a sign of cosmic dissolution.
