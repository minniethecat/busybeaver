# Justice

The expectation that moral order will ultimately be vindicated — the demand that wrongs be punished and righteousness rewarded — which Dennis identifies as the deepest psychological driver of eschatological belief: the end of the world is, at bottom, the moment when justice is finally done.

## Definition

Justice, in Dennis's usage, is not primarily a legal or ethical concept but a cosmic expectation. The book's argument implies that every eschatology — whether theological or secular — is ultimately an attempt to solve the problem of injustice. The world as it exists is manifestly unjust: the wicked prosper, the innocent suffer, and the mechanisms of human justice are partial and corrupt. The end of the world, in the eschatological imagination, is the event that corrects this — the final court, the irreversible verdict, the cosmic settling of accounts.

Dennis is less interested in defining justice than in observing its operation as a psychological force. The early Christians "needed some such faith in not too distant punishment" (010-022) not because they were especially vengeful but because the alternative — a world in which persecution goes unpunished forever — was existentially unbearable. Justice deferred becomes justice denied; justice denied fuels apocalyptic expectation. The end of the world is the escape from this cycle: a future event that retroactively justifies present suffering.

## In the Book

### Context

The demand for justice surfaces most explicitly in Chapter 10, where Dennis traces the early Church's apocalyptic expectations to the experience of persecution. Christianity, "still a persecuted creed offering no hope of present triumph" (010-022), projected its hope for justice onto the future. The end of the world was not merely a catastrophe but a vindication — the moment when the persecutors would be punished and the faithful rewarded.

Chapter 15 deepens the theme by contrasting two models of justice: the survivalist model, in which justice is achieved through human progress and the gradual improvement of social conditions, and the pessimist model, in which justice is impossible within history and can only be achieved by history's termination. Dennis observes that "injustice everywhere has waxed: from clan to tribe, from tribe to nation, from nation to empire" (015-017), suggesting that the arc of history does not bend naturally toward justice — it must be bent, if at all, by some force beyond history.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis's treatment of justice is characteristically ambivalent. He does not deny the reality of injustice, but he questions whether eschatological justice is anything more than wishful thinking projected onto a cosmic screen. The history of failed prophecy suggests that the end of the world, if it comes at all, will come on its own schedule and for its own reasons, indifferent to human conceptions of fairness.

> [I]njustice everywhere has waxed: from clan to tribe, from tribe to nation, from nation to empire. (015-017)
