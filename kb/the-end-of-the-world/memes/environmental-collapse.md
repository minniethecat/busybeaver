# Environmental Collapse

The catastrophic breakdown of the natural systems that sustain human civilization — climate, ecosystems, resources — which appears in Dennis's survey as a background possibility, implicit in his discussions of cold, water, and the limits of planetary habitability.

## Definition

Environmental collapse, in the context of Dennis's argument, refers to the failure of the ecological conditions necessary for human survival: the disruption of climate patterns, the exhaustion of essential resources, the collapse of food systems, and the cascading failures that follow from any single major environmental shock. Dennis does not treat environmental collapse as a distinct chapter-length topic, but the concept pervades his discussions of planetary cooling (Chapter 6), flooding (Chapter 5), and the general fragility of the conditions that make human life possible.

The book was written before "climate change" and "environmental collapse" entered popular discourse as distinct apocalyptic scenarios, but Dennis's treatment of cold and ice as existential threats — the prospect of a new Ice Age, the slow cooling of the Earth toward uninhabitability — anticipates the structure of modern environmental apocalypticism. The mechanism is different (cooling rather than warming), but the logic is the same: human civilization depends on a narrow band of environmental conditions that are not guaranteed to persist.

## In the Book

### Context

The concept surfaces most explicitly in Chapter 6 ("Cold"), where Dennis surveys the geological evidence for past climate shifts and their implications for the future. The discovery that the Arctic was once temperate — that Greenland was green, that tropical plants once grew in polar regions — becomes, in Dennis's hands, an argument for the fragility of the present climate regime. What nature has changed before, it can change again.

Chapter 5 ("Water") contributes the complementary threat: the possibility of catastrophic flooding, whether from melting ice caps, geological displacement, or divine intervention. Dennis catalogs flood myths from cultures around the world, treating the universal human fear of inundation as evidence that environmental catastrophe is a permanent feature of the human imagination.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis treats environmental threats with the same ambivalence he brings to all end-of-world scenarios: they are real possibilities backed by scientific evidence, but their timing is uncertain, and humanity has a demonstrated capacity to adapt to changing conditions. The survivalist-pessimist debate of Chapter 15 is, in essence, a debate about whether human ingenuity can outrun environmental constraints — a question Dennis leaves open.
