# Cosmic Collision

The idea that the earth — or the entire solar system — could be destroyed by physical impact with another celestial body, whether a comet, meteor, wandering planet, or another star. Dennis devotes two full chapters to collision scenarios and returns to the theme throughout the book.

## Definition

A cosmic collision, in Dennis's framework, is any catastrophic encounter between our world and another massive body. The scale ranges from the local (comet striking earth) to the absolute (star smashing into star). Dennis distinguishes several classes: cometary collision — the most ancient and psychologically resonant fear (002-001); meteoric impact — statistically real but too small to destroy the world (007-004); planetary collision — ruled out by orbital stability (007-005); and stellar collision — the one truly terminal impact, which would vaporise earth entirely (007-008). Collision is unique among Dennis's modes of world-ending because it destroys the physical globe itself, not merely its living inhabitants.

## In the Book

### Context

Dennis treats collision across two chapters. In Chapter 2 ("Comet"), he examines the oldest and most culturally embedded form — the comet-strike — tracing it from ancient Chinese astrologers through medieval prophecy to modern astronomy. The comet is a transitional object between science and superstition: "a heavenly body of debatable origin" that "is as much without the realm of wizardry and within the ordinary realm of ordered nature as the plain moon herself" (002-003). In Chapter 7 ("Crash"), Dennis escalates to stellar collision, the only mode that could physically annihilate the planet. The collision concept also surfaces in Chapter 1 ("How?"), where the collisional hypothesis is one of seven theories of the world's origin: "Our parent star... crashed head on into another star, and in colossal conflagration flamed into new life" (001-008).

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis's strategy is to move from the familiar to the barely conceivable. He begins with comets — terrifying yet survivable. He calculates the odds: even direct collision with a comet would not annihilate the earth, merely devastate its surface (002-036). Meteors are even less threatening: "No bethel ever fallen from heaven... has stood higher than three of its worshippers" (007-004).

But then the argument shifts. Planetary orbits are regular, but stellar encounters are not. The sun, moving through galactic space, must eventually encounter another star: "A star's chance of collision... on the analogy of molecules in a gas, has been (dubiously) computed" (007-012). When that happens, the result is total: a grazing would strip away atmosphere and oceans; a direct hit would dissolve earth into incandescent gas.

> Yet collision, which thus includes grazing contact and near-miss alike, may be mortal shorn of direct hit. Let another great body but pass close enough, and its tidal pull would heave the seas from their trenches... or wrench the globe from its orbit and send it spinning into the outer darkness. (007-021)

Dennis closes with a dialectical reversal: collision is not only the end but also the beginning. The very same forces that could destroy the world are what created it: "Collision makes; collision kills" (007-025). This is the deepest philosophical insight of the collision chapters — that creation and destruction are two faces of one process, and the force that began the solar system may one day end it.
