Germany is referenced in Dennis's book in two distinct contexts: as a region vulnerable to catastrophic flooding from Alpine snowmelt, and as a point of contrast in the sociology of apocalyptic belief — where millennial fears counted for less than in the United States.

## Location

Germany occupies the north-central European lowlands, lying north of the Alps. In the book's flood scenario, it is among the "over-civilized European lowlands" that would be drowned by meltwater pouring from the Alpine heights（004-003）.

## In the Book

### Context

Germany appears in "Water"（004）and "God"（010）. The first reference places it within a catalogue of regions threatened by catastrophic glacial melt; the second uses it as a sociological counterpoint to America's intense millenarianism.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis treats Germany as representative of European civilization — sophisticated, industrialized, yet geologically vulnerable. In the meltwater scenario, Alpine torrents would sweep into Germany alongside Italy and France, part of a continental-scale catastrophe（004-003）. In the book's later analysis of apocalyptic psychology, Germany serves as a negative case: on the European continent, "even in Germany and disastrous Russia, such fears have counted for less" than in America, where millennial sects flourished（010-058）.

> from the Alps into the over-civilized European lowlands, into Italy, Germany, France（004-003）

> On the European Continent, even in Germany and disastrous Russia, such fears have counted for less; in the United States of America for much more（010-058）

## Significance

Germany's role in the book is dual. Geographically, it illustrates the reach of catastrophic flooding across an entire continent — no European nation is spared. Sociologically, it represents a baseline of relative secular calm against which Dennis measures America's distinctive apocalyptic fervor.
