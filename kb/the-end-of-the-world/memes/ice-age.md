# Ice Age

The ice age occupies a unique position in Dennis's catalogue of world-endings: it is the only mode of [[death|extinction]] for which we possess *physical evidence* — the glacial drift, the mammoth fossils, the carved landscapes of the northern hemisphere. Unlike the [[biblical-flood|flood]] (which is attested only in texts) or the [[fire|comet]] (which is attested only in rare and disputed observations), the ice age has left its signature on the very rocks beneath our feet. For Dennis, this makes it the most *credible* of the world-endings — and, in a characteristically dark turn, the most disturbing, because it demonstrates that the Earth can become uninhabitable *without any external cause*, simply through the ordinary operation of its own climate.

## Overview

The Pleistocene ice [[time|ages]] were a series of glacial advances and interglacial retreats spanning roughly 2.6 million years, with the most recent glacial maximum occurring approximately 20,000 years ago. During glacial periods, ice sheets up to three kilometers thick covered much of [[america|North America]], northern Europe, and Asia, lowering global sea levels by over 100 meters and extinguishing countless species — including, Dennis notes, the [[mastodon]] and mammoth whose frozen carcasses provided nineteenth-century science with its most dramatic evidence of prehistoric [[disaster|catastrophe]].

Dennis was writing in the 1920s, when the ice age was still a relatively recent scientific discovery. The recognition that the Earth had undergone such radical climate transformations — and could do so again — was still sinking into the popular imagination. Dennis exploits this freshness: the ice age is, for him, the *scientific* [[revelation|apocalypse]], the one that requires no faith, no scripture, no prophetic tradition — only the patient accumulation of geological evidence and the uncomfortable inference that what has happened before will happen again.

## In the Book

### Context

The ice age appears across multiple chapters of Dennis's survey, functioning as both a standalone threat and a bridge between the mythological and scientific modes of world-ending. In Chapter 2 ("Comet"), Dennis speculates that a cometary [[cosmic-collision|impact]] might have triggered glacial conditions: "a greater comet may in the future bring back the Glacial Epoch" (002-041) — the comet is not only a direct destroyer but an indirect one, capable of altering the climate so radically that ice, rather than [[cosmic-collision|impact]], does the killing.

In Chapter 4 ("Water"), Dennis draws a provocative equivalence: the ice age is "one vast flood of frozen water" (004-014), the geological counterpart to the biblical [[flood-myth|deluge]]. Where the ancients imagined liquid water rising to cover the mountains, modern science reveals frozen water descending from the poles — but the result, from the perspective of the species that perish, is identical.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis's treatment of the ice age is distinctive for its engagement with the fossil evidence. He describes the mammoth remains discovered in Siberia — "the position of bones in the frozen tundra" (004-010) — as a kind of eschatological time capsule. These [[soul|anima]]ls did not die gradually or peacefully; they were overtaken by cold so sudden that their flesh was preserved, undecayed, into the present. The ice age is not merely a climate event but a *mass [[death|extinction]] event*, and Dennis wants his readers to feel its violence.

> The Great Thaw that followed the last ice age, the newer theories of continental subsidence, the very latest and most spectacular discoveries of geology — all point to a planet that has repeatedly transformed itself beyond recognition, and will do so again. (004-010)

This is the core of Dennis's ice age argument: *precedent*. If the Earth has undergone catastrophic climate transformation before, it can — and will — do so again. The ice age is not a curiosity of the geological past; it is a preview of the planetary future. Dennis connects this to the Norse myth of Fimbul-Winter, the great cold that precedes [[ra|Ra]]gnarök, and to the Hebrew prophets who foresaw "darkness and not light" ([[amos]]). Science and myth converge on the same terrifying insight: the world's default state is cold, and the warm interval in which human [[human-progress|civilization]] has flourished is the exception, not the rule.

### The Mammoth as Eschatological Symbol

Dennis devotes particular attention to the mammoth fossils of Siberia — not merely as scientific evidence but as a *moral* symbol:

> "Even though the ice ages, like the floods, are said to be due reward for a race that has sinned" (015-017)

The mammoth, in Dennis's reading, is innocent — a creature that did nothing to deserve [[death|extinction]] and yet was extinguished nonetheless. This is the ice age's darkest implication: world-ending does not discriminate. The righteous and the wicked, the human and the [[soul|anima]]l, perish together. The ice age is an [[revelation|apocalypse]] without a moral — a [[disaster|catastrophe]] that serves no providential purpose and delivers no lesson, except perhaps the one Dennis has been making all along: the [[cosmos|universe]] is indifferent to our survival.

## Significance

The ice age represents, in Dennis's taxonomy, the *geological* mode of world-ending — the one grounded not in prophecy or myth but in the physical evidence of the rocks. Its significance for his larger argument is threefold:

1. **Precedent**: The ice age proves that catastrophic climate change is real and recurrent. What has happened before can happen again.
2. **Immanence**: Unlike the comet or the colliding star, the ice age requires no external trigger. It arises from the Earth's own climate system — a system that, Dennis reminds us, we barely understand and cannot control.
3. **Indifference**: The ice age killed the mammoth as readily as it would kill us. It is an [[revelation|apocalypse]] without a covenant, without a rainbow, without a [[noah|Noah]].
