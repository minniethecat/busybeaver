# Heaven

In Dennis's work, heaven operates in two registers simultaneously: as the physical sky from which comets and celestial signs appear, and as the theological realm of divine agency that will ultimately "vanish away like smoke" (003-014) in the final conflagration.

## Definition

Heaven (Hebrew *shamayim*, Greek *ouranos*) denotes both the visible sky and the dwelling-place of God in Judeo-Christian cosmology. Dennis exploits this double meaning throughout his work. When comets appear as "heavenly signs" (002-012), they are phenomena of the physical sky interpreted through a theological lens. When the biblical assertion that "in the beginning God created the heaven and the earth" (001-010) is invoked, heaven is the created order's upper boundary — as much a part of the cosmos as the earth itself, and equally subject to dissolution.

The concept's ambiguity is the point: Dennis moves fluidly between the astronomical and the theological, treating heaven as both a location in space and a symbol of transcendence. This allows him to draw on both scientific and religious traditions without committing fully to either.

## In the Book

### Context

Heaven appears most prominently in the comet chapter (chapter 002), where Dennis catalogs historical instances of comets interpreted as heavenly portents. "In the new Christian era, heavenly signs came thick and fast" (002-012) — a summary of the medieval tendency to read every celestial anomaly as divine communication. William the Conqueror "took a comet as a sign of heaven's blessing and at once sailed forth for England" (002-015), illustrating how heavenly signs could be enlisted to justify political action.

The physical reality of these signs receives equal attention. A comet is "a heavenly body of debatable origin" (002-003), and Dennis weighs competing theories — atmospheric, astronomical, and supernatural — without fully resolving the question. The comets that "announced from the heavens fire and destruction on earth" (002-007) serve his broader argument: the heavens contain real threats, not merely symbolic ones.

In the fire chapter (003), heaven appears in eschatological context: "the heavens shall vanish away like smoke" (003-014), quoting or paraphrasing biblical language about the final dissolution. The funeral pyres of antiquity are built "heaven-high" (003-004), and "funeral eagles soar into heaven" (003-005) — images that connect the physical upward movement of flame and smoke to the theological hope of ascent.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis approaches heaven with characteristic ambivalence. He neither dismisses the theological interpretation of celestial signs as primitive superstition, nor endorses it as revealed truth. Instead he documents the historical record of heaven-reading — the long human practice of scanning the sky for meaning — and leaves the reader to weigh the evidence.

> A giant rent the heavens, and Troy town fell. This one constellation of marvels seems to have been the archetype and inspiration, if not the source, of all the later cometary wonders. (002-008)

The passage is typical: a classical reference (the fall of Troy) is yoked to a general claim about cometary interpretation, and the reader is invited to see the pattern without being told what to conclude.

Dennis's ultimate point is that heaven — whether understood as sky or as divine realm — offers no permanent refuge. The same fires that consume the earth will consume the heavens. The distinction between above and below collapses in the final "decreation" (008-004), when the entire created order is unmade.
