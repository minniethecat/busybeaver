# Christianity

Geoffrey Dennis's *The End of the World* is, in large measure, a history of Christian eschatology — the two-thousand-year sequence of expectations, disappointments, and reinterpretations generated by the conviction that "the end is at hand." Christianity appears in the book not as a theological system to be evaluated but as a historical force whose apocalyptic framework has shaped the Western imagination of catastrophe.

## Definition

In Dennis's usage, Christianity is defined primarily by its eschatological orientation: a religion born with the proclamation that the Kingdom of God is imminent, organized around a sacred text whose final book (the Apocalypse) describes the end of the present world, and committed — across all its subsequent institutional accommodations — to the conviction that history has a divinely ordained terminus. Dennis is interested not in Christianity's truth claims but in the cultural machinery those claims set in motion: the date-setting, the sign-reading, the recurrent conviction that one's own generation is the terminal one.

## In the Book

### Context

Dennis's most concentrated treatment of Christianity appears in Chapter 10 ("This Year"), where he traces the arc from the apostolic "at hand" through the medieval millennial panics to the sophisticated date-setting of his own era. The chapter's argument is that Christianity's eschatological orientation was both a strength and a vulnerability: it "watered abundantly" the nascent movement by offering imminent justice to the persecuted (010-004), but it also committed the faith to predictions that could be — and repeatedly were — empirically falsified.

Dennis traces a perceptible institutional shift. The early Church, persecuted and powerless, needed "some such faith in not too distant punishment, and in not too distant reward and joy" (010-022). Millenarianism was a "recruiting sergeant" that made martyrdom bearable. But once Christianity became the established religion of the Roman Empire, the calculus changed: "Bishops reconciled to the Empire found it impolitic, when not impossible, to consider its thousand years a thousand years of sin ending in fire and doom" (010-023). Chiliasm was declared a heresy; the Church, now a stakeholder in the present order, could no longer afford to prophesy its imminent destruction.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis's account is notably unsentimental. He sees the institutional Church's retreat from apocalypticism as a matter of interest, not of conviction — "Self-interest, re-christened commonsense, forbade them to think millennially" (010-023). Yet the apocalyptic impulse was never fully suppressed. It migrated to the margins — "Only madmen, sorcerers, Asiatics, could believe in such Rabbinical vapouring" — and periodically erupted back into the center, as in the terrors of the tenth century, when "of the signs of the Dies Irae not one was wanting" (010-037).

Dennis then adds, in what may be the chapter's most characteristic sentence: "True, the hour was gloomy. But what hour of human history is not?" (010-037). This is Dennis's fundamental insight: Christianity's apocalypticism is not a pathology but a permanent possibility of the human response to suffering. Every generation has its tribulations; every generation can, if it chooses, read them as the Tribulation.

> Christianity, still a persecuted creed offering no hope of present triumph, needed some such faith in not too distant punishment, and in not too distant reward and joy, to enable it to increase its strength and maintain its courage. (010-022)

> Self-interest, re-christened commonsense, forbade them to think millennially. Only madmen, sorcerers, Asiatics, could believe in such Rabbinical vapouring. Only heretics; after Augustine chiliasm was proclaimed a heresy. (010-023)

> True, the hour was gloomy. But what hour of human history is not? (010-037)
