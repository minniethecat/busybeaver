# Doomsday Prophecy

The persistent human practice of forecasting the end of the world, which Dennis examines not for its predictive accuracy (which is uniformly nil) but for what it reveals about the psychology of eschatological thinking.

## Definition

Doomsday prophecy, in Dennis's sense, is any claim to know when and how the world will end, whether derived from scripture, astrology, mystical revelation, or pseudo-scientific calculation. Dennis surveys a vast range: biblical chronologies ("when the fixed stars have made a revolution unto the points from whence they first set out" (008-004)), astrological predictions keyed to planetary conjunctions (010-042), numerological schemes, and modern scientific extrapolations. His interest is not in which prophecy is correct — he takes it as given that none have been — but in what the impulse to prophesy reveals about the human relationship to time and mortality.

## In the Book

### Context

Doomsday prophecy is a thread that runs through nearly every chapter. In Chapter 2 ("Comet"), Dennis notes how comets were historically interpreted as omens of the end: "the people from their beds would rise / To behold that horror in the skies" (002-012). In Chapter 8 ("God"), the full range of religious eschatology — "each religion, each sect, each sub-sect holding its different guess as to His choice of hour" (008-003) — is catalogued with anthropological detachment. In Chapter 9 ("When?"), the question of timing is examined directly: "Man has ever concerned himself more passionately with the hour than with the way of destruction" (009-004). In Chapter 10 ("This Year") and Chapter 11 ("Next Year"), Dennis explores the psychology of near-term prediction — the hope or fear that the end is imminent.

Chapter 12 ("Some Time") addresses biblical chronology specifically, examining attempts to extract a date from prophetic texts. And Chapter 13 ("Never") considers the opposite extreme — the conviction that the world will not end at all, that some force (divine or natural) guarantees its permanence.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis treats doomsday prophecy with a combination of sympathy and detachment. He does not ridicule the prophets — he recognizes that the impulse to know the end is as fundamental as the impulse to know the beginning. But he is clear-eyed about the track record: every predicted date has passed without incident, and every new prediction is made with the same conviction as the last.

> Astrologers have in all ages foretold the Birthday Death... prophets and peoples the End as Punishment... magicians, saints and mystics the Decreation. (008-004)

Dennis's survey is comprehensive but compressed — he moves through centuries and cultures in paragraphs, accumulating examples until the pattern becomes visible. The pattern is this: every culture produces end-of-world narratives; every narrative identifies a different mechanism and a different timeline; and all of them, so far, have been wrong. The persistence of the impulse despite its record of failure is, for Dennis, evidence that eschatology is not about prediction but about meaning. To predict the end is to assert that history has a shape, that time is not merely a succession of moments but a story with a destination.

> Fear deferred maketh the heart sick. (009-001)

This line, borrowed from Proverbs, captures Dennis's psychological insight: the uncertainty is worse than the certainty. Any date, however terrifying, is preferable to not knowing — because a date gives form to fear, and form, however terrible, is bearable.
