# The End of the World

The central concept and title phrase of Geoffrey Dennis's survey of eschatological thought — the termination of the human world in all its possible modes, from cosmic catastrophe to divine judgment, from geological gradualism to prophetic imminence.

## Definition

"The End of the World" in Dennis's titular usage is deliberately capacious. It encompasses the physical destruction of the Earth (by fire, water, cold, or collision), the biological extinction of humanity, the theological culmination of history in a final judgment, and the psychological experience of confronting one's own mortality writ large. Dennis exploits the phrase's ambiguity: "the world" can mean the planet, the human species, civilization, or the individually experienced lifeworld. "The end" can mean destruction, completion, purpose, or judgment.

The book's method is to survey every possible meaning of the phrase in turn, moving from the cosmic to the personal, from the scientific to the theological, from the certain (the sun will die) to the speculative (Christ will return). The result is less an argument for any particular eschatology than an anatomy of eschatological thinking itself — an inquiry into why human beings are so persistently drawn to imagine their own collective ending.

## In the Book

### Context

The title phrase pervades every chapter but is explicitly unpacked at key structural moments. Chapter 10 ("This Year") opens with the historical core of the concept: "The end of the world — surely. But only as incidental to the relief of persecuted Christians" (010-022). Here the end is something desired, not merely feared — a vindication, a rescue, the event that makes sense of present suffering.

Chapter 11 ("Next Year") complicates the picture: "The end of the world is at hand; it is very far off" (011-001). The paradox is central to Dennis's argument. The end is always both imminent and indefinitely deferred — a structural feature of eschatological discourse, not a bug in any particular prediction. Every generation believes the end is near; every generation dies without seeing it.

Chapter 15 ("Man") introduces the book's most important distinction: "the interval between the end of the world in the one sense and the end of the world in the other" (015-001). The end of humanity and the end of the planet are not the same event, and the gap between them is the space in which the book's deepest questions are posed.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis never resolves the question his title poses. The book ends with a survey of options — survival and extinction, providence and chance, design and meaninglessness — each given its strongest case, none given the last word. The effect is to transform "the end of the world" from a prediction into a problem: not something to be calculated and awaited, but something to be thought about, and thought about honestly.
