Predestination is the theological claim that the end was determined before the beginning — that God, from eternity, decreed not only the broad arc of cosmic history but the individual destiny of every human soul. In an eschatological context, predestination transforms the end of the world from a possible future into an inevitable one: the apocalypse is not something that *might* happen but something that *must* happen, because it was always meant to happen.

## Definition

In Christian theology, predestination refers to God's eternal decree determining all events and, in its strongest form (associated with Augustine, Aquinas, Calvin, and Jansen), the eternal election of some souls to salvation and the reprobation of others to damnation. The doctrine has deep roots in Pauline thought: "Those whom he predestined he also called" (Romans 8:30). It was systematized by Augustine in his anti-Pelagian writings and radicalized by Calvin in the *Institutes*, where double predestination — the decree of both election and reprobation — became a cornerstone of Reformed orthodoxy.

Philosophically, predestination raises the problem of free will: if the outcome is fixed, is human choice real? Theologically, it raises the problem of theodicy: if God predestines the damned, is God the author of evil? These tensions have generated centuries of debate, from the semi-Pelagian compromise to the Molinist middle-knowledge theory to the Arminian doctrine of conditional election.

In Islam, a parallel concept appears as *qadar* (divine decree), one of the six articles of faith. The Quran states that "No disaster strikes upon the earth or among yourselves except that it is in a register before We bring it into being" (57:22). The tension between divine decree and human responsibility generated the same debates in Islamic theology (*kalam*) as in Christian — and produced similar compromises.

## In the Book

### Context

Dennis's book is, in a secular mode, an exploration of predestination without a predestinator. The universe he describes runs by deterministic physical laws — the entropy of Chapter 17, the solar evolution of Chapter 6, the celestial mechanics of Chapter 2 — that leave no room for contingency. "The clock will have run down" (017-003) is a statement about entropy, but it is also a statement about fate: the end is built into the system from the start.

The theological concept of predestination surfaces implicitly in Dennis's treatment of prophecy. When prophets calculate the date of the Apocalypse from scriptural numbers, they are attempting to read the divine decree — to discover what has already been determined. "Every prophecy concords" (010-109) is the hermetic conviction that the decree is coherent and discoverable. The failure of every prophecy to date does not disprove predestination; it only disproves the prophets' ability to read the decree.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis does not address predestination as a formal theological doctrine. But his treatment of fate and cosmic determinism creates the intellectual space in which predestination becomes thinkable. "Man will fight his fate" (006-024) acknowledges fate as the opponent — something against which human agency struggles. The qualification "and perhaps prevail for a time" concedes that the struggle may have temporary success, but the trajectory is set. The word "fate" — rather than "circumstance" or "probability" — is Dennis's own choice, and it carries the weight of predestinarian thinking.

His survey of the Second Law of Thermodynamics (Chapter 17) functions as a scientific predestination: entropy is not a possibility but an inevitability. "Entropy is irreversible. There is no opposite process" (017-006). In a universe governed by irreversible entropy, the end is predestined in the only sense that matters: it cannot not happen. The theological doctrine of predestination and the scientific doctrine of entropy converge on the same conclusion — the world will end, and nothing can prevent it.
