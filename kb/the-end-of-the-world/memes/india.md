# India

India appears in Dennis's survey primarily as a directional marker — the land east of which Columbus believed Paradise lay — and as an ancient source of cosmic-cycle mythology, including the world-destroying fire motif.

## Location

The Indian subcontinent, bounded by the Himalayas to the north and surrounded by ocean on three sides. In Dennis's references, "ancient India" serves as a civilizational counterpoint to the Judeo-Christian eschatological tradition [003-003] [003-041].

## In the Book

### Context

India is invoked in three distinct contexts: ancient religious practices (Chapter 3, "Fire"), flood mythology (Chapter 4, "Water"), and the geographical confusion that drove Columbus's voyage (Chapter 11, "Next Year") [003-003] [004-006] [011-015]. Each reference positions India as both a source of alternative world-ending narratives and a geographical misunderstanding.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis draws on India as a deep well of non-Western apocalyptic thinking. The ancient Indian "pious one" who committed self-immolation by fire becomes a parallel to Western martyr traditions [003-003]. The "five hundred new years" motif — a dove flying away into India — links Eastern and Western renewal myths [003-041]. Most tellingly, Dennis uses India as Columbus's intended destination: the explorer sailed west seeking the earthly Paradise believed to lie east of India, and found America instead [011-015].

> "In ancient India the pious one committed merit by mounting the pyre" [003-003]

> "the priest and flew away into India, for five hundred new years of life" [003-041]

## Significance

India functions in Dennis's argument as the geographical anchor of Paradise mythology. The belief that Eden lay somewhere east of India drove European exploration and, through Columbus's error, shaped the entire colonial encounter with the Americas. Dennis uses this chain of misconceptions to illustrate how eschatological geography — the mapping of sacred space onto the physical world — has repeatedly produced world-historical consequences from theological premises [011-015].
