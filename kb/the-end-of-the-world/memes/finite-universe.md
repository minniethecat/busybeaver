# Finite Universe

The cosmological claim — drawn from Einstein, de Sitter, and the new astronomy of the early twentieth century — that space is finite, bounded, and curved back upon itself, a fact Dennis deploys to foreclose the hope that an infinite cosmos might escape thermodynamic death.

## Definition

A finite universe is one in which space is not unbounded but closed — a sphere (Einstein) or a cylinder (de Sitter) in which a beam of light, travelling indefinitely in one direction, would eventually return to its starting point. "De Sitter's toy world is a sphere (Einstein's a cylinder), space bending back on itself" (017-008). The radius is immense — "so big that light, swiftest of all things, takes one hundred thousand million years to go round it" (017-008) — but finite nonetheless. This finitude is, for Dennis, the death of the only serious objection to the calorific death: the argument that an infinite universe cannot reach equilibrium. "To an infinite Universe the calorific death might come or might not; to this finite Universe, the Universe that is, in irreversibility of entropy it must" (017-009).

## In the Book

### Context

The concept of the finite universe appears primarily in Chapter 17 ("What After?"), where it is the pivot on which Dennis's entire thermodynamic argument turns. Throughout the preceding chapters, the book has catalogued specific modes of ending — comet, fire, water, and so on — each of which could be argued away as merely local or contingent. The finite universe provides the master argument that overrides all such objections: if the cosmos is a closed system, the Second Law of Thermodynamics applies to it in its entirety, and universal death is inescapable.

Dennis prepares the ground for this concept earlier in the book. In Chapter 1 ("How?"), the competing origin theories all imply a universe of limited duration — one that began and will therefore end. In Chapter 9 ("When?"), the question of timing forces an engagement with cosmic scale. But it is in Chapter 17 that the concept receives its full exposition, in one of the densest passages of astronomical prose in the book.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis's treatment of the finite universe is remarkable for its fusion of poetry and precision. The passage describing the journey outward from earth — past Neptune, past Proxima Centauri, through the Milky Way, past Magellanic nebulae, "on to new Milky Ways, lone island-universes of prodigal immensity" (017-008) — is one of the book's set-pieces, a tour de force of astronomical writing that conveys the vertigo of cosmic scale even as it argues that the scale is finite.

> Though number and size and removedness thus tower and riot and appal — yet no such series of wandering miles and wearying zeros attains infinity. (017-008)

The rhetorical strategy is to overwhelm the reader with immensity, then reveal that even this immensity has a limit. The effect is paradoxical: the universe feels larger and more terrifying after we learn it is finite than it did when we could imagine it as infinite.

The philosophical consequence is stark. If the universe is finite, there is no escape from entropy. The argument from infinity — "And entropy of infinity is a phrase that has no sense" (017-011) — is revealed as wishful thinking. Dennis does not celebrate this conclusion; he presents it as a fact that must be faced, a constraint on hope that no amount of scientific advance can remove. In a finite universe, the clock will run down. Every clock. Everywhere.
