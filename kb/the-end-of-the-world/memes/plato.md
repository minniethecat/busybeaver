# Plato

The Athenian philosopher, founder of the Academy, whose cosmological speculations — particularly in the *Timaeus* — established a framework for thinking about cosmic origins and endings that influenced two millennia of Western eschatology.

## Identity

Plato (c. 428–348 BCE) was a student of Socrates and the teacher of Aristotle. His dialogue *Timaeus* presents a creation myth in which a divine craftsman (the Demiurge) fashions the cosmos according to eternal mathematical patterns. The created world, being made, is subject to decay — but Plato also suggests that the Demiurge's work is so well-wrought that it may endure indefinitely. This tension between cosmic mortality and immortality has shaped Western eschatological thinking ever since.

Dennis groups Plato with [[aristotle]] as the twin peaks of Greek philosophy — "of the Greeks Plato and Aristotle, the two greatest" (016-006) — whose cosmological frameworks stood in contrast to the prophetic eschatology of the Hebrew tradition. This opposition between Greek cyclical time and Judeo-Christian linear time is one of the structuring tensions of Dennis's intellectual history.

## In the Book

### Appearances

Plato appears in Dennis's survey of philosophical traditions, cited as a representative of the classical view that the cosmos is ordered, rational, and potentially eternal — a counterpoint to the Judeo-Christian insistence on a created world with a definite beginning and end. The neo-Platonist tradition, which Dennis mentions alongside the Gnostics (010-023), represents the Hellenistic fusion of Plato's philosophy with mystical and religious elements.

Dennis's treatment is brief but knowing: Plato's cosmos is invoked as the philosophical alternative to apocalyptic linearity. In this genealogy, [[immanuel-kant]] stands as Plato's distant heir — both philosophers insist on the limits of what human reason can definitively establish about cosmic origins and endings.

### Role in Dennis's Argument

Plato serves Dennis as the archetype of the philosophical — as opposed to the prophetic — approach to cosmic endings. Where the Hebrew prophets saw divine judgment, Plato saw gradual decay within an ordered framework. Dennis does not adjudicate between these traditions; he presents them as parallel streams, both feeding into the modern synthesis that is his book's subject.

## Significance

Plato represents the Greek philosophical tradition that runs alongside the Hebrew prophetic tradition in Dennis's genealogy of eschatology. The *Timaeus*, with its vision of a mathematically ordered cosmos, is the distant ancestor of Laplace's nebular hypothesis and Einstein's cylindrical universe — and Dennis's method is precisely to trace these genealogies across centuries and cultures. In doing so, Dennis reveals the philosophical architecture beneath the modern scientific eschatology examined in the [[biblical-flood]] and [[ice-age]] discussions.
