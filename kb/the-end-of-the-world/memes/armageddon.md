# Armageddon

In Geoffrey Dennis's *The End of the World*, Armageddon functions not as a prophecy to be believed but as a metaphor to be interrogated — the terminal point toward which, depending on one's philosophical commitments, history is either advancing or collapsing.

## Definition

Armageddon (Hebrew *Har Megiddo*, the Mount of Megiddo) is the site named in Revelation 16:16 as the gathering place for the final battle between the forces of God and the forces of evil. In Dennis's usage, the term has been secularized and universalized: it stands for the conviction, shared across both religious and secular apocalypticisms, that history culminates in a catastrophic confrontation. Dennis deploys it at two structurally significant points in his argument — early, in his catalog of cometary fears (Chapter 2), and late, in the climactic debate between optimism and pessimism (Chapter 15).

## In the Book

### Context

Dennis first invokes Armageddon in Chapter 2 ("Comet"), during his survey of the roles comets have played in human eschatology. He observes that the Great War of 1914–1918, despite widespread expectation that it would be the prophesied final conflict, produced no confirming celestial sign: "no worthy one announced Armageddon, the 1914 skies spake peace" (002-022). The comets, traditional heralds of doom, were silent — and yet the war happened anyway. Dennis's point is subtle: the absence of supernatural confirmation does not make catastrophe less real, only less meaningful.

The second and more philosophically freighted appearance comes in Chapter 15 ("Man"), where Dennis gives the floor to the pessimists — those who hold that evolution is "decreative" and that the human project is not ascending but descending. In their framing, history's trajectory runs "from Arcadia to Armageddon, and now is shooting to the Abyss" (015-022). Armageddon here is not the prelude to a divine new creation but the penultimate stage of an irreversible slide into nothingness.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis's handling of Armageddon is marked by restraint. He does not debunk the concept, nor does he endorse it. Instead, he lets it function as a hinge between two worldviews: the religious imagination that sees catastrophic endings as meaningful transitions, and the secular pessimism that sees them as merely catastrophic. The 1914 skies "spake peace" — the absence of a sign is itself the sign.

> Pessimists, on the other hand, though with many a reserve and proviso to prevent themselves looking ridiculous, are returning to a catastrophic view; of a comet not as herald or harbinger of doom — no worthy one announced Armageddon, the 1914 skies spake peace — but as its actual agent. (002-022)

> The road? It has brought him from Arcadia to Armageddon, and now is shooting to the Abyss. The drift and net movement is downward — each heart of us knows it, wallow in make-believe how we may. (015-022)
