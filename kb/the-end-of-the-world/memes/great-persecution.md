The Great Persecution was the last and most severe Roman persecution of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians under Emperor Diocletian (303–313 CE). In Dennis's book, it represents a pivotal moment in apocalyptic psychology: all the prophesied signs were accomplished, yet the End did not come.

## Overview

The Great Persecution under Diocletian was the Roman Empire's final systematic attempt to suppress [[christianity|Christianity]]. Churches were destroyed, scriptures burned, and believers tortured and executed. For early [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians steeped in apocalyptic [[hope|expectation]], this was one of the tribulations that were to precede the [[second-coming|Second Coming]].

## In the Book

### Context

The Great Persecution appears in "God"（010）, in a passage tracing the transformation of early [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian apocalypticism. Dennis recounts the sequence of disappointments that forced the new [[faith|religion]] to adapt its [[doomsday-prophecy|eschatology]]: the Apostles died without seeing the [[second-coming|Second Coming]], Paul was martyred with his "imminent hope fast dwindling," and then "the Great Persecution had raged over, and [[jerusalem|the Holy City]] fallen, and all the signs been accomplished save only the End"（010-005）.

### Dennis's Treatment

For Dennis, the Great Persecution is a data point in the history of deferred [[revelation|apocalypse]]. Every sign listed in scripture had been fulfilled — persecution, the fall of [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]], the preaching of the gospel to all nations — and still [[jesus-christ|Christ]] did not return. "Then the new [[faith|religion]] had to" adapt, Dennis writes, trailing off into the institutionalization of [[christianity|Christianity]]: from imminent [[hope|expectation]] to institutional church.

## Significance

The Great Persecution illustrates the defining crisis of apocalyptic [[faith|belief]]: what happens when all the signs are fulfilled but the predicted event does not occur? Dennis uses it to show that religious apocalypticism is remarkably resilient — it transforms rather than collapses when prophecy fails. This pattern of survival-through-adaptation is relevant to his broader argument about humanity's psychological relationship with the End.
