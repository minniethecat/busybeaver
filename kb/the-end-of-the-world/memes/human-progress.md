# Human Progress

The idea that humanity's capacity for scientific, technological, and moral advancement might allow the species to outrun the natural forces that otherwise guarantee its extinction. Dennis subjects this optimistic position to sustained scrutiny across multiple chapters.

## Definition

Human progress, in the specific sense relevant to Dennis's argument, is the claim that the end of the world is not inevitable — or at least, not inevitably the end of humanity. It is the secular alternative to providential design: where religion promises salvation by divine intervention, the doctrine of progress promises survival by human ingenuity. Dennis explores this position most fully in Chapter 15 ("Man"), where he asks "Shall we go, without compulsion from outside nature, before we must?" (015-004). The answer he develops is nuanced: human progress can forestall certain ends, cannot forestall others, and may itself be the vehicle of new ends.

## In the Book

### Context

The concept of human progress appears throughout the book, but three chapters are especially relevant. In Chapter 5 ("Drought"), Dennis considers whether human technological adaptation — irrigation, climate engineering — could outpace the desiccation of the planet. In Chapter 15 ("Man"), he examines the full range of human vulnerabilities: biological, psychological, social. In Chapter 17 ("What After?"), he concludes that progress is ultimately bounded by thermodynamics: "Physical entropy will include psychical entropy" (017-044).

The nineteenth-century faith in perpetual progress — the Victorian conviction that science, democracy, and moral improvement were irreversibly advancing together — forms the background against which Dennis writes. His book, published in the shadow of the First World War, is in part a rebuttal to that faith.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis's treatment of progress is dialectical. He first grants its genuine achievements: humanity has already survived ice ages, plagues, and catastrophes that would have extinguished less adaptable species. The question is whether this adaptive capacity has a limit.

> Without harm from the heavens, will the race fail? Shall we go, without compulsion from outside nature, before we must? (015-004)

He identifies several internal threats that progress itself creates: war, which grows more destructive as technology advances; the exhaustion of natural resources, which accelerates with industrialization; and the psychological fragility that may accompany civilization — "the chemical causes of cerebral energy" (015-011) that may prove unstable under conditions of advanced development. In this sense, progress is not the solution but part of the problem: each advance creates new vulnerabilities.

Dennis also considers the argument that humanity might colonize other worlds, but dismisses it as escaping the question rather than answering it. The universe as a whole is doomed; moving from one doomed planet to another merely postpones the reckoning. Ultimately, the second law of thermodynamics — entropy — applies to human civilization as surely as it applies to stars: "the law of death and degradation must apply to both. Physical entropy will include psychical entropy, matter-death spirit-death" (017-044).

The chapter on Man closes not with a definite verdict but with a somber possibility: "Man going need not mean all life going" (015-003). The species that believed itself the pinnacle of creation may turn out to be merely one more transient form in a universe indifferent to its passing.
