Corinth, the ancient Greek city, appears in Dennis's book as an example from classical antiquity of the association between comets and natural disasters — and of how comets were interpreted as divine omens by historical actors.

## Location

Corinth is located on the isthmus connecting the Peloponnese to mainland Greece, controlling both the Saronic Gulf to the east and the Gulf of Corinth to the west. In the classical world, it was a major commercial and naval power.

## In the Book

### Context

Corinth appears in "Comet"（002）, the chapter cataloguing historical comet apparitions and their perceived consequences. Dennis cites two distinct Corinthian incidents: an earthquake associated with a comet, and the military expedition of Timoleon.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis draws on Aristotelian comet lore to note that a specific comet "brought the Achaean earthquake" and "the storm of Corinth"（002-008）— pairing natural disasters with celestial portents in the ancient worldview. More concretely, he recounts how Timoleon of Corinth, hesitating before his Sicilian campaign, "took a comet as a sign of heaven's blessing and at once sailed forth for Syracuse," ultimately capturing the city "without the loss of a single man"（002-015）. For Dennis, Timoleon exemplifies how comets served as decisive omens that shaped the course of history.

> This one, delivers Aristotle, brought the Achaean earthquake; that one the storm of Corinth（002-008）

> Timoleon of Corinth, undecided whether or no to set out on his Sicilian expedition, took a comet as a sign of heaven's blessing（002-015）

## Significance

Corinth serves a double illustrative function in the book. The earthquake-and-comet pairing supports the chapter's survey of cometary disasters — building the historical case that comets have always been associated with catastrophe. The Timoleon anecdote adds nuance: comets were not only feared but sometimes embraced as favorable omens, showing that humanity's relationship with celestial signs has always been complex and consequential.
