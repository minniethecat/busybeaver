# Time

The dimension of sequential existence, explored in the book as both the incomprehensibly vast backdrop of cosmic and geological processes, and the finite horizon against which all eschatological scenarios unfold.

## Definition

In general philosophical usage, time is the indefinite continued progress of existence and events in the past, present, and future. In Dennis's treatment, time functions on two distinct scales: the "deep time" of geology and cosmology — spans of millions and billions of years that dwarf human civilization — and the eschatological "end of time," the terminal point toward which all apocalyptic narratives arc. Dennis consistently contrasts human temporal experience (a few thousand years of recorded history) with the orders of magnitude that govern stellar evolution, geological stratification, and cosmic entropy [001-002] [002-035].

## In the Book

### Context

The concept of time permeates nearly every chapter of the work. It appears most prominently in three argumentative roles: (a) as the measure of geological and cosmic processes that operate on scales far beyond human intuition [001-002] [002-035]; (b) as the marker of historical recurrence — the observation that civilizations, species, and even worlds have "times" of flourishing and "times" of ending [002-012] [003-043]; and (c) as the ultimate eschatological boundary — the "end of time" when sequential existence itself may cease [003-043] [004-013].

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis deploys time as a rhetorical device to unsettle anthropocentric assumptions. By juxtaposing the "some short thousand million years" of geological time against the brevity of recorded history, he frames human civilization as a negligible flicker in the cosmic chronology [001-002]. The book repeatedly invokes temporal scale to argue that extinction-level events — whether cometary, volcanic, or thermal — are not anomalies but inevitabilities when viewed against a sufficiently long timeline [002-034] [002-035] [004-005].

Dennis also explores the cyclical conception of time through the lens of eternal recurrence: "having lived an eternity of times, with an eternity of times to come" [003-043]. This sits in tension with the linear, terminal eschatology of the Biblical flood narrative, creating an unresolved philosophical friction that runs throughout the work [004-003] [004-013].

> "When geological time begins, some short thousand million years before the present moment, the Earth was a spinning cloud of gas and dust..." [001-002]

> "It would have no time to scorch, poison, pull up the forests and shovel them into ocean deeps..." [002-035]
