# Eden

The Garden of Eden — the biblical paradise of Genesis, where humanity originated in a state of innocence before the Fall — appears in Dennis's argument as both a geological jest and a theological symbol, the lost perfection toward which every eschatology gestures.

## Location

Eden's location in the book is deliberately unstable. In Chapter 6 ("Cold"), Dennis reports that the Arctic explorer Robert Peary "found Eden" in the far north — a wry conflation of geological discovery with biblical mythology (006-020). The discovery that Greenland was once temperate, that tropical plants once grew in polar latitudes, becomes an occasion for Dennis to note that Paradise has been located in every unexplored corner of the map. Columbus sailed west believing he would reach the terrestrial Eden (011-015); Peary found it in the ice.

Theologically, Eden is located at the beginning of time — the state from which humanity fell and to which, in one strand of eschatological thought, history is returning. Dennis uses this temporal location to structure his argument: the world's end is always imagined as either a return to Eden or a final departure from it.

## In the Book

### Context

Eden appears at three key moments in the book's argument. In Chapter 6, as noted, it is a geological jest — the warm past that mocks the cooling present. In Chapter 11, it is a historical motive: Columbus's search for the terrestrial Paradise drove the discovery of the New World, a fact Dennis presents as emblematic of the way eschatological expectation has shaped actual history. In Chapter 15, Eden is the rhetorical prize in the debate between survivalists and pessimists: does human history lead back to Paradise or forward to extinction?

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis treats Eden with characteristic irony. He never denies its power as a symbol, but he consistently undercuts the literal search for it. Every expedition that set out to find Eden found something else — America, the Arctic, disappointment. The garden is real only as an idea, but as an idea it is among the most powerful ever conceived.

> Peary found Eden. Greenland was once green; amid her Icy Mountains did not the breadfruit trees once wave? (006-020)

> [T]hough that new world turned out to be only America, he had discovered it in mistake for Paradise. (011-015)

## Significance

Eden's broader significance to the book lies in its dual function as origin and destination. Every end-of-world scenario, Dennis implies, is at some level a story about Eden — about what was lost and whether it can be regained. The scientific eschatologies of cold, fire, and collision are secular translations of the Eden narrative: the golden age that preceded human history, the catastrophe that ended it, the hope of restoration.
