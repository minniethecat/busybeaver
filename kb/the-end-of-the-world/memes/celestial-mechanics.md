# Celestial Mechanics

The physical laws governing the motion of celestial bodies — orbits, rotations, gravitational interactions, and tidal forces — which Dennis examines as the invisible architecture that sustains the world and may also doom it.

## Definition

Celestial mechanics, as Dennis deploys the concept, encompasses three interrelated phenomena: the earth's orbital stability, which keeps it at a safe distance from the sun and other planets; its rotational dynamics, which produce the diurnal cycle and the seasons; and tidal interactions, which act as a brake on rotation and a bridge between gravitational and thermal processes. Dennis's interest is in the fragility of this mechanism — the fact that it is not guaranteed forever. Orbits are stable until they are not; rotations slow inexorably; tides consume the energy that sustains motion. The "celestial machine" is, in Dennis's vision, a clockwork that is running down, and the question is not whether it will stop but when.

## In the Book

### Context

Dennis introduces celestial mechanics in Chapter 1 ("How?"), where each origin hypothesis — nebular, tidal, collisional, planetesimal — implies a different set of mechanical principles governing the solar system's formation. The concept recurs prominently in Chapter 6 ("Cold"), where the earth's rotational deceleration becomes a primary mechanism of climatic catastrophe: "Earth's two different forms of rotation proceeding at different speeds, partition strains result; the strains become tides; the tides act as brakes" (006-007). In Chapter 7 ("Crash"), the stability of planetary orbits is what makes collision with another planet improbable — but not, Dennis reminds us, with another star.

The mechanical view of the cosmos reaches its philosophical apex in Chapter 17 ("What After?"), where Dennis confronts the thermodynamic argument: "The universe is a mechanism transforming energy into heat, never to the same extent heat back into energy" (017-006). Celestial mechanics and thermodynamics are, in the end, the same story told in different languages — one in terms of forces and orbits, the other in terms of heat and entropy.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis treats celestial mechanics with a kind of horrified admiration. The laws are beautiful in their precision — "Their courses are ordered like our own, and will remain separate and regular while ours does" (007-005) — but the beauty conceals a grim implication: order is temporary, and the machine is running down.

> Earth's two different forms of rotation proceeding at different speeds, partition strains result; the strains become tides; the tides act as brakes. Forspent, the world slows down; halts, wearily creeps to paralysis. (006-007)

The passage on rotational deceleration is one of the book's most technically vivid. Dennis traces a chain of causation: differential rotation → strain → tides → braking → slowed rotation → synchronized rotation → one hemisphere permanently in darkness, the other in fading light. This is not a catastrophic end — it is a gradual, mechanical unwinding, more terrible in its inevitability than any sudden impact.

Dennis's treatment of planetary orbits is similarly double-edged. The fact that planets do not collide is, on the one hand, reassuring — but it is a contingent reassurance, dependent on initial conditions that could have been otherwise and may not persist indefinitely. Stellar encounters, unlike planetary ones, are not governed by the same orderly constraints: "A collision only happens when something goes wrong. And something has gone wrong" (007-013).
