# Mortality

The inescapable human condition of being subject to death — the individual finitude that, in Dennis's implicit argument, is the psychological template for every vision of the world's end.

## Definition

Mortality, from the Latin *mors* (death), is the condition of being mortal: subject to death, finite in duration, bounded by nonexistence at both ends. Dennis treats mortality not primarily as a biological fact but as a psychological condition — the awareness of one's own eventual death that distinguishes human consciousness and that drives, at the collective level, the imagination of the world's death.

The book's argument implies a homology between individual mortality and cosmic eschatology. The end of the world is mortality writ large: the human refusal to die alone, the insistence that if consciousness must end, then everything must end with it. Dennis does not state this thesis directly, but it is the deep logic of his survey: every eschatology is, at bottom, a strategy for managing the terror of death.

## In the Book

### Context

Mortality surfaces explicitly in Chapter 15, where Dennis discusses the hope of immortality: "as hope or fear of immortality decides" (015-025). The phrase captures a central theme: attitudes toward the end of the world are functions of attitudes toward personal death. Those who hope for immortality tend to imagine a world-end that is also a world-renewal — a passage to something better. Those who fear mortality tend to imagine a world-end that is pure loss — the final triumph of nothingness.

The book's opening chapter establishes mortality as the framing condition of the entire inquiry. Dennis's catalogue of all the ways a person can die — "struck by shot, or shell, or death-ray; or burned alive" (001-015) — is both a literal enumeration of hazards and a metaphor for the human condition. Every life ends; the world is the aggregate of lives; therefore the question of how the world ends is the question of how life ends, multiplied by every life that has ever been lived.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis treats mortality with a seriousness that verges on the elegiac. He never mocks the fear of death or the hope of immortality; he simply notes that these are the existential constants from which every eschatology is constructed. The book's final, unresolved posture — survivalist optimism and pessimist despair presented side by side, neither given the last word — is itself a response to mortality: an acknowledgment that the question of death admits of no definitive answer, only of more or less honest ways of posing it.

> [A]s hope or fear of immortality decides. (015-025)
