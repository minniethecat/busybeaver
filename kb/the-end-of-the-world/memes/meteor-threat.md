# Meteor Threat

The hypothesis that falling celestial bodies — meteors, bolides, meteorite swarms — could destroy the earth or extinguish life upon it, a scenario Dennis examines with scientific care before concluding that meteors alone are too small to qualify as world-enders.

## Definition

A meteor, in Dennis's terminology, is any solid celestial fragment that enters the earth's atmosphere and either burns up (a shooting star) or survives to strike the ground (a meteorite or bolide). The meteor threat, as a mode of world-ending, is the claim that a sufficiently large meteor or dense meteor swarm could deliver enough kinetic energy to sterilize the planet. Dennis examines this claim in Chapter 7 ("Crash") and Chapter 2 ("Comet"), treating it as a scientifically legitimate but ultimately insufficient hypothesis.

## In the Book

### Context

Dennis discusses meteors primarily in Chapter 7 ("Crash"), where they are evaluated alongside comets, planets, and stars as potential impactors. The historical record of meteor strikes is thin: "The meteor death-roll through history is so far two: a peasant once in India, a cow long ago in Brazil" (007-004). No meteor ever observed has been large enough to cause significant regional damage, let alone global extinction.

However, Dennis does not dismiss the possibility entirely. He notes that "meteorites come from outside the solar system" and that therefore "how affirm anything absolute, whether as to their maximum size or their maximum speed?" (007-004). The known meteor population may not sample the full range of possibilities. A meteor many orders of magnitude larger than any observed — a stray fragment from some ancient stellar catastrophe — could, theoretically, do what comets are said to do.

The meteor concept also appears in Chapter 1 ("How?"), where the meteoric hypothesis of planetary formation — "Millions of meteor-swarms moved nearer together, by progressive agglomeration and concretion formed themselves into solid earths" (001-006) — presents meteors as creators rather than destroyers. This dialectical reversal (creation through aggregation, destruction through impact) mirrors Dennis's treatment of collision more generally in Chapter 7.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis treats the meteor threat with characteristic caution. He does not rule it out — the universe is too large and too strange for absolute pronouncements — but he makes clear that, on present evidence, meteors are the least threatening of all catastrophic agents.

> No bethel ever fallen from heaven and erected on earth has stood higher than three of its worshippers; not great Diana of the Ephesians, nor the baetylical idol of Zeus Teleios at Tegea, nor Jacob's pillar of thanksgiving at Luz-Bethel itself. (007-004)

The list of sacred stones — Diana, Zeus Teleios, Jacob's pillar — is typical of Dennis's erudite style. He uses cultural history to make a physical point: stones that fall from the sky are objects of wonder and worship precisely because they are rare and small. A meteor large enough to threaten the world would be of an entirely different order — not a shrine but an apocalypse.

> There may of course be meteor-troops many times more numerous, with each trooper many times bigger, than anything deducible either from past knowledge, or from present acquaintance with friendly Aquarids and Gremenids and Leonids. (007-004)

The concession is important. Dennis is a careful enough thinker to acknowledge the limits of induction: past experience does not guarantee future experience. But the concession does not change his practical conclusion — that the meteor threat, while real in principle, is so unlikely as to be negligible in practice.
