# Ra

The Egyptian sun god, one of the most enduring and widely worshipped deities in human history, whose mythology Dennis draws upon to illustrate the deep cultural roots of solar reverence — and solar fear.

## Identity

Ra (also Re) was the principal solar deity of ancient Egypt, typically depicted as a falcon-headed man crowned with a solar disk. He was believed to sail across the sky each day in his solar barque and journey through the underworld each night, fighting the serpent Apophis to ensure the sun's rebirth at dawn. For Dennis, Ra represents the oldest stratum of solar worship: "many names; many temples from Persia to Mexico, from Stonehenge to Xauxa" (006-009). Ra, Hor, Sol — these are not separate gods but local names for the same primal recognition: the sun is the source of all life, and if it should fail, all life fails with it.

## In the Book

### Appearances

Ra appears in Chapter 6 ("Cold") as part of Dennis's remarkable catalogue of solar deities across cultures (006-009). The passage is one of the book's most cosmopolitan moments — Egyptian Ra alongside Greek Helios, Roman Sol, Hindu Sūrya, Incan Inti — a litany that suggests the universality of solar worship and, by implication, the universality of the fear that the sun might one day not return.

### Role in Dennis's Argument

Ra represents the mythic imagination that precedes and parallels scientific astronomy. Dennis does not treat Ra as a primitive error that science has corrected; he treats Ra as evidence that human beings have always understood, at some level, that their existence depends on the sun's stability. The Egyptian priest who prayed to Ra for the sun's return and the modern astronomer who calculates the sun's remaining fuel are engaged in the same fundamental project — reckoning with our dependence on a finite star.

## Significance

Ra is, for Dennis, the personification of a truth that physics would later formalize: the sun is not eternal, and its death is our death. The myth of Ra's nightly battle with Apophis — the serpent of darkness — is a narrative encoding of the thermodynamic reality that Dennis explores scientifically. The difference is one of idiom, not of insight.
