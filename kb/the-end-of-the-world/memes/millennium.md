# Millennium

In Geoffrey Dennis's *The End of the World*, the Millennium is the single most consequential number in the history of Western eschatology — the thousand-year period of Revelation 20 that has been mapped onto every subsequent thousand-year period, and every round-numbered year within them, producing an unbroken chain of disappointed expectations.

## Definition

The Millennium (from Latin *mille*, thousand; also called the Chiliad, from Greek *chilioi*) is the period of Christ's earthly reign prophesied in Revelation 20:1–6. In Christian eschatology it has generated three major interpretive traditions — premillennialism (Christ returns before the Millennium), postmillennialism (Christ returns after a golden age brought about by the Church), and amillennialism (the Millennium is a symbolic description of the present Church age). Dennis is interested not in these theological distinctions but in the Millennium's function as a chronological magnet: the conviction that history is organized around a round-numbered terminus, and that one's own era is that terminus.

## In the Book

### Context

Dennis treats the Millennium in Chapter 10 ("This Year"), within his catalog of date-setting schemes. He notes that "before that date and, when it turned out to be wrong, for more than eight centuries after it, all other numeral years" — the Year of Days (365), the Grand Climacteric (6,300), the giant numbers of First Chronicles — "were swallowed up by the only figure specified in the New Testament itself: the χίλια ἔτη of Revelation Twenty" (010-021).

The year 1000 — the first Millennium — became The Year. When it passed uneventfully, the chronological framework did not collapse; it was simply recalibrated. "Christ's 'this generation' meant this millennium, this chiliad. The year One Thousand was The Year" (010-021). Dennis's phrasing is compact but devastating: the slippage from "this generation" to "this millennium" reveals the interpretive elasticity that allows apocalyptic expectation to survive every empirical disconfirmation.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis's treatment of the Millennium is characteristic of his broader argument about apocalyptic chronology: the specificity of the predictions is what makes them compelling, and it is also what makes them falsifiable. The Millennium of Revelation — like the "this generation" of the Synoptics — was taken literally by the early Church. When the literal reading failed, it was replaced not by skepticism but by reinterpretation. The number remained; only the referent changed.
