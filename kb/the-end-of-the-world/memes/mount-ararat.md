# Mount Ararat

The volcanic massif in eastern Turkey where, according to the biblical account, Noah's ark grounded as the floodwaters receded — the point of contact between destruction and survival, catastrophe and renewal, that Dennis invokes in his chapter on the flood as a mode of world-ending.

## Location

Mount Ararat (Ağrı Dağı in Turkish) is a dormant volcanic massif in eastern Anatolia, near the borders with Armenia and Iran. Its highest peak reaches 5,137 meters, making it the highest mountain in Turkey. Genesis 8:4 records that "the ark rested... upon the mountains of Ararat" after the flood. For millennia, this mountain has been identified as the landing place of Noah's vessel — the geographical point where the universal deluge ended and the world began again.

## In the Book

### Context

Ararat appears in Chapter 4 ("Water") as the implicit destination of the ark narrative. Dennis is interested less in the mountain's geography than in its symbolic function: Ararat is the place where the flood stops. Every deluge narrative needs such a place — a point of transition from destruction to renewal. The mountain is the hinge between the old world and the new.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis treats Ararat not as a physical location to be verified (he is not interested in ark-eology) but as a narrative necessity. The flood story requires a mountain for the ark to land on; without it, the story has no end. Ararat is the narrative resolution of the flood — the place that makes survival possible and, by making it possible, makes the thought of total destruction bearable.

## Significance

Mount Ararat represents the spatial dimension of eschatological hope in Dennis's analysis. Every vision of total destruction requires a point of exception — a place, however small, that the destruction does not reach. Ararat is that place in the flood narrative, and its function is repeated in every subsequent eschatology that imagines a remnant, a seed, a chosen few who survive the end.
