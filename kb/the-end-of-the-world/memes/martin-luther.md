Martin Luther (1483–1546) was the German theologian, monk, and reformer whose Ninety-five Theses (1517) sparked the Protestant Reformation. In the book, he appears briefly but memorably — as a man in a hurry, racing to complete his German translation of the Bible before the expected end of the world.

## Identity

Luther was an Augustinian friar, professor of theology at the University of Wittenberg, and the central figure of the Reformation. His doctrines of justification by faith alone (*sola fide*) and the authority of Scripture alone (*sola scriptura*) broke with the Roman Catholic Church and reshaped Western Christianity. His translation of the Bible into German (published in full in 1534) was a landmark of vernacular Scripture and profoundly influenced the German language. Luther shared the widespread expectation of his era that the end of the world was near（011-015）.

## In the Book

### Appearances

Luther appears in Chapter 11, in a passage about the persistence of apocalyptic expectation through the Reformation era. Dennis notes that Luther "hurried also, to finish his Bible in time" — that is, in time for the anticipated end of the world. This detail is placed alongside Columbus's mistaken belief that he had discovered Paradise, as examples of the way apocalyptic time-pressure shaped the actions of otherwise sober historical figures（011-015）.

### Role in Dennis's Argument

Dennis uses Luther to illustrate a crucial point: apocalyptic belief is not merely a doctrine professed in sermons but a force that shapes practical action. Luther's urgency in completing his Bible translation was driven by the conviction that time was short. The Reformation coincided with — and was partly fueled by — a widespread sense that history was approaching its divinely appointed terminus. The comet-watching and sign-reading of the era, "though with dwindling force, and dwindling effects on life and conduct," persisted as a "wide and serious" belief through multiple centuries（011-015）.

> Martin Luther hurried also, to finish his Bible in time. For a while again every comet, every least change in the constellations, was taken for a sign of the approaching day.（011-015）

## Significance

Luther exemplifies the productive dimension of apocalyptic belief — the way eschatological urgency can drive cultural achievement. His Bible translation, one of the foundational texts of the German language, was accelerated by the conviction that there might be no tomorrow in which to finish it. In Dennis's narrative, this is both a testament to the power of apocalyptic conviction and an ironic counterpoint: the end did not come, but the Bible endured.
