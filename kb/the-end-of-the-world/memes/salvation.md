Salvation — deliverance from sin, death, and divine judgment — is the implied counter-concept to the destruction that occupies Dennis's book. It appears not as a direct theme but through the language of redemption that surfaces at key moments in the historical narrative.

## Definition

In Christian theology, salvation (*soteria*) is the deliverance of humanity from sin and its consequences through the life, death, and resurrection of Jesus Christ. More broadly, in comparative religion, salvation refers to any doctrine of liberation from existential peril — whether conceived as sin, ignorance, rebirth, or cosmic destruction.

## In the Book

### Context

Salvation as an explicit term does not appear in Dennis's text, but its cognate concepts — Redeemer, redemption, messianic expectation — run through the chapters that deal with Christian eschatology. The star that "proclaimed the young Redeemer of mankind" at Augustus's death（002-011）and the comet that heralded Jerusalem's destruction both belong to the same theological framework in which salvation and destruction are not opposites but complementary outcomes of a single divine plan.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis mentions salvation indirectly, through the figure of Christ as "Redeemer"（002-011）and through the eschatological framework within which comets were interpreted as signs. In the Christian centuries he surveys, every celestial portent carried a double meaning: doom for the unrepentant, deliverance for the faithful. The destruction of Jerusalem by Titus, foretold by a "titan sword" comet, was simultaneously a catastrophe for the Jews and, in Christian interpretation, a vindication of Christ's prophecy. Dennis does not explore soteriology as a theme in its own right, but the presence of redemption-language throughout the historical chapters suggests an awareness that every apocalypse has its survivors — and that the boundary between destruction and salvation is drawn by faith.
