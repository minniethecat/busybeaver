# Velociraptor

**Velociraptor** was a small dromaeosaurid theropod of the Late Cretaceous, known for its relative intelligence among [[dinosaur|dinosaurs]] and its sophisticated predatory behavior. Its extinction — part of the broader K-Pg elimination of non-avian dinosaurs — illustrates a point that Dennis's argument returns to repeatedly: intelligence and adaptability, however impressive on an individual scale, provide no guarantee of species survival when the planetary environment undergoes catastrophic change.

## Identity

*Velociraptor mongoliensis* lived approximately 75 to 71 million years ago in what is now Mongolia. It was considerably smaller than its popular-culture depictions suggest: about 2 meters long, 0.5 meters tall at the hip, and weighing approximately 15 kilograms. Its most distinctive features were the enlarged, sickle-shaped claw on each hind foot — used to pin and dispatch prey — and a relatively large braincase suggesting problem-solving capabilities beyond those of most reptiles.

The discovery of *Velociraptor* and its relatives in the dromaeosaurid family revolutionized the understanding of dinosaur behavior and physiology. These were not slow, dim-witted reptiles but active, possibly warm-blooded predators with complex social and hunting behaviors. They represented the evolutionary apex of non-avian theropod intelligence — and yet, when the K-Pg boundary came, they died like all the rest.

## Extinction Narrative

*Velociraptor* itself may have gone extinct slightly before the terminal K-Pg event (its youngest fossils are ~71 million years old), but its close relatives — the dromaeosaurids, including the dromosaurs mentioned by Dennis in his extinction catalogue (015-020) — persisted until the boundary and were eliminated along with all other non-avian dinosaurs.

The dromaeosaurid extinction at K-Pg is especially instructive for Dennis's framework because these were, by dinosaurian standards, "smart" animals — adaptable, quick, equipped with sophisticated sensory and motor capabilities. If intelligence were a defense against extinction, the dromaeosaurids should have survived. That they did not is evidence that extinction operates at a level where individual capabilities are irrelevant: when the ecosystem collapses, cognitive sophistication counts for nothing.
