# Beast

The apocalyptic symbol of bestial opposition to divine order — most famously the Beast of Revelation whose number is 666 — which Dennis examines as the dark heart of numerical prophecy: the persistent attempt to identify contemporary figures with the Beast and thereby fix the date of the world's end.

## Definition

In the apocalyptic vocabulary, "the Beast" (Greek: θηρίον, *thērion*) denotes the monstrous antagonist of Revelation 13, a composite creature rising from the sea with ten horns and seven heads, empowered by the dragon (Satan) to persecute the saints and demand worship. The Beast's number — 666, "the number of a man" (Revelation 13:18) — has been the single most productive source of numerical eschatology in Christian history. Dennis treats the Beast not as a theological reality but as a cultural and psychological phenomenon: the compulsion to decode the number, to identify the Beast with a living political figure, and to derive from that identification a date for the Apocalypse.

The Beast functions in numerological prophecy as a variable slot: Nero, the Pope, Napoleon, the Kaiser — each generation finds its own candidate. What Dennis finds remarkable is not the identification itself but its resilience in the face of repeated disconfirmation. "Even 1666, the Beast's, the Neronic, though with London in flames it stood not signless, ended tamely; by merely ending" (010-043).

## In the Book

### Context

The Beast appears in Chapter 10 ("This Year"), Dennis's extended study of millenarian prophecy and its repeated failures. The chapter surveys two millennia of date-setting for the end of the world, and the Beast's number is the master key in this tradition. Dennis traces the Beast through its major historical identifications: the early Church's identification with Nero, the medieval linkage to Islam, the Reformation's application to the Papacy, and the modern Protestant targeting of figures from Napoleon to Kaiser Wilhelm.

The Beast is also entangled with Dennis's treatment of the Great War's aftermath. "The 1917 prophecy, based on fresh researches in Revelation, connected up with the marks of the Beast (the Kaiser), and made known throughout England by showers of pamphlets" (010-107). The war, with its unprecedented scale of destruction, seemed to many contemporaries to be the tribulation foretold — and the Kaiser the Beast who presided over it.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis writes about the Beast with a tone of weary, almost affectionate irony. He has no sympathy for the numerologists who treat Revelation as a cryptogram, but he is genuinely interested in what the persistence of Beast-identification reveals about the human need to personify evil and to locate oneself at the culminating moment of history. "Every prophecy concords: Apocalypse with Apocrypha, the Pyramid corridors with Daniel's chronology; Kemal's inauguration is Antichrist's World Coronation, Ismet's number (count it) the number of the Beast" (010-109). The deadpan catalogue of concordances is Dennis's way of showing that the system is hermetically sealed — any number can be made to work if the will to believe is strong enough.

The Beast also appears in a more existential register. In Chapter 12 ("Some Time"), Dennis considers what happens to human nature when the props of civilization are removed: "In hearts stripped naked by terror, the beast will hack through and triumph forth" (012-076). Here the Beast is not an external apocalyptic figure but an internal potential — the brute within, waiting for the collapse of social order to emerge. This doubled usage — external eschatological symbol and internal psychological reality — is characteristic of Dennis's method: the mythological is always shadowed by the anthropological.
