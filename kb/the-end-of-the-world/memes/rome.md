# Rome

The city that ruled the Mediterranean world for half a millennium, whose solar cult and imperial destiny Dennis invokes as a case study in the relationship between political power and eschatological imagination.

## Location

Rome, on the banks of the Tiber in central Italy, was the capital of the Roman Republic and later the Roman Empire. At its height in the second century CE, the empire stretched from Britain to Mesopotamia, from the Rhine to the Sahara. Dennis's Rome is specifically the city of the Quirinal Hill — site of a major temple to the sun — and the golden Rome of imperial self-presentation: "golden Rome — *Urbs solaris imperialis* — to golden Cuzco" (006-009). Rome represents the western pole of the ancient solar worship that Dennis surveys.

## In the Book

### Context

Rome appears in Chapter 6 ("Cold") as part of Dennis's global survey of solar cults. The Roman temple of the sun on the Quirinal is paired with temples from Persia, Egypt, Mexico, Inca Peru, and Stonehenge — a chain of sacred sites spanning the ancient world, all dedicated to the same star. The juxtaposition of Rome and Cuzco (the Inca capital) is characteristic of Dennis's cosmopolitanism: imperial centers on opposite sides of the world, unknown to each other, worshipping the same sun.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis uses Rome as a symbol of the intersection between imperial power and solar religion. The emperor who claimed divine status and the sun that was worshipped as a god converge in the image of golden Rome — a city that saw itself as the center of the world, illuminated by a star it believed was eternal. Dennis's treatment is subtly ironic: the Eternal City fell, and the sun it worshipped will also fall.

> Hor, Sol, Ra; Sūrya Mitra, Savitr; Helios, Phaethon, Phoebus Apollo — he has had many names; many temples from Persia to Mexico, from Stonehenge to Xauxa, from the high Quirinal in Rome, golden Rome — *Urbs solaris imperialis* — to golden Cuzco. (006-009)

## Significance

Rome serves Dennis as a memento mori for civilizations. The city that called itself eternal is now a ruin; the empire that worshipped the unconquered sun has vanished. If Rome could fall, Dennis implies, so can anything — including the civilization that now, like Rome before it, believes itself permanent.
