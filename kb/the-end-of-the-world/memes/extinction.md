# Extinction

Extinction is the permanent disappearance of a species from the Earth — and, in Dennis's eschatological frame, the death of humanity itself as the ultimate existential threat around which all other end-of-world scenarios orbit.

## Definition

Extinction, from the Latin *exstinguere* (to quench, to put out), denotes the complete and irreversible termination of a biological lineage. In the book, Dennis deploys the term at two levels: species extinction in the ordinary biological sense, and human extinction as the catastrophic endpoint toward which every apocalyptic mechanism — fire, water, cold, collision, entropy — ultimately tends.

Dennis's treatment of extinction is naturalistic rather than theological. He does not present extinction as divine punishment but as a fact of natural history, one that has claimed countless species before humanity and will, in the fullness of cosmic time, claim humanity itself. "Human extinction held to imply and include the extinction of the world itself" (015-003) captures the conceptual merger at the heart of the book: for human observers, the end of humanity is the end of the world, because there is no world without a witness to inhabit it.

## In the Book

### Context

The concept of extinction pervades the book's entire structure. Dennis organizes his survey of end-of-world mechanisms as a series of possible extinction pathways — by fire (Chapters 3-4), by water (Chapter 5), by cold (Chapter 6), by collision (Chapters 1-2), and by the slow decay of the cosmos itself (Chapter 7 onward). Each chapter evaluates not merely whether a given force *could* end the world, but whether it is *likely* to produce human extinction.

In Chapter 15 ("Man"), extinction becomes the explicit subject of debate. Dennis stages a sustained argument between the survivalists — who believe human ingenuity can outrun any extinction threat — and the pessimists, who argue that extinction is the default condition of biological existence. The chapter opens with the distinction between "the end of the world in the one sense and the end of the world in the other" (015-001), namely the difference between planetary destruction and human extinction. Dennis insists that these are not identical, but that for all practical purposes they converge: the world that matters is the world of human experience, and its end is extinction.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis treats extinction with characteristic ambivalence. On the one hand, he catalogs extinction threats with encyclopedic thoroughness, lending each its full rhetorical weight. On the other hand, he repeatedly draws back from the conclusion that extinction is imminent, noting that every previous prediction of the end has failed and that the human species has survived every catastrophe that nature and history have thrown at it so far.

> [H]uman extinction held to imply and include the extinction of the world itself. (015-003)

This passage is the conceptual linchpin: Dennis is less interested in whether the planet will physically survive than in whether humanity will. The planet can outlast its inhabitants; geology demonstrates as much. But the "world" in the book's title is not the planet — it is the human world, the world of meaning, history, and experience. When that goes dark, the world ends, regardless of whether the Earth continues to orbit the sun.
