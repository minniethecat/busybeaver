# William Miller

William Miller (1782-1849), the American farmer turned Baptist preacher whose calculation that Christ would return on October 22, 1844, sparked one of the largest prophetic movements in American history and provided Dennis with his paradigmatic case study of modern end-of-world prediction.

## Identity

Miller was a simple New Englander — "a poor stick," as Dennis notes one of his critics called him (010-097) — who, after a period of religious doubt, undertook an intensive study of the Bible that led him to conclude that the Second Coming was precisely dateable. Using the 2,300 evenings-and-mornings of Daniel 8:14 as his key, Miller calculated that the sanctuary would be cleansed — which he interpreted as Christ's return — in 1844.

Miller began preaching his conclusions in 1831. By the early 1840s, his movement had grown from a local curiosity to a nationwide phenomenon involving tens of thousands of followers. Dennis describes the movement's rapid expansion: "nationwide. Its protagonist was William Miller" (010-058). Miller's followers, known as Millerites, abandoned their farms, sold their possessions, and gathered in expectation of the appointed day.

## In the Book

### Appearances

Miller is the central figure of Chapter 10's modern section. Dennis introduces him as the latest in a two-thousand-year succession of prophetic calculators, each convinced that scriptural numbers yielded a calendar date for the Apocalypse. "William Miller was a simple New Englander. He had studied his Bible with the aid of a concordance" (010-059). The ordinariness of the man is essential to Dennis's point: prophecy is not the province of fanatics and charlatans alone, but of ordinary people who take an ancient text with deadly seriousness.

Within months of Miller's preaching, the Millerites had become "a sect, spreading like wildfire" (010-096). Dennis catalogs the social dynamics — the exploitation by more cynical figures, the mockery from outsiders, the intense communal expectation — with ethnographic detachment. "Nor need the Elder's followers be blamed overmuch for a credulity that was a characteristic of their age" (010-097).

When October 22, 1844, passed without incident — the Great Disappointment — the movement fractured. Some abandoned faith entirely; others reinterpreted the prophecy, concluding that the date was correct but the event had been misidentified (Christ had cleansed the heavenly sanctuary, not returned to Earth). "It was the end also of old William Miller, who died brokenhearted" (010-106).

### Role in Dennis's Argument

Miller serves Dennis as the modern type of the prophetic calculator. Unlike the ancient prophets, Miller operated in a world of printing presses, newspapers, and mass communication. His movement's rise and fall demonstrates that prophetic enthusiasm is not a medieval relic but a permanent feature of the modern mind — and that the failure of prophecy does not extinguish the prophetic impulse but merely redirects it.

> William Miller was a simple New Englander. He had studied his Bible with the aid of a concordance. (010-059)

> Within a few months the Millerites had become a sect, spreading like wildfire. (010-096)

> It was the end also of old William Miller, who died brokenhearted. (010-106)

## Significance

Miller's importance to the book extends beyond his own story. He represents the democratization of prophecy — the shift from ecclesiastical authority to individual biblical interpretation that the Reformation enabled. His movement's survival beyond the Great Disappointment (as Seventh-day Adventism and other groups) illustrates Dennis's thesis that failed prophecy does not end prophetic movements but transforms them.
