# Noah

The biblical patriarch of Genesis 6–9 — the one righteous man whom God spared from the universal [[biblical-flood|flood]] — and in Dennis's treatment, the archetype of all those who hope, against reason and evidence, to be the exception to the [[universal-death|world's end]]. Dennis deploys Noah not as a theological figure but as a *psychological type*: the survivor whose existence makes the thought of total extinction bearable, even as the text quietly asks whether the next catastrophe will "spare no Noah" (004-013).

## Identity

Noah, son of Lamech, is the central human figure in the biblical flood narrative (Genesis 6–9). Warned by [[divine-judgment|God]] of the coming deluge, he builds an ark to preserve his family and a breeding pair of every animal species. After the flood recedes, God establishes a covenant with Noah, promising never again to destroy the earth by water — a promise symbolized by the rainbow. The story's reach is vast: "every child in Christendom" knows it (004-006), transmitted from the Pentateuch through the cultural memory of the West until the ark has become a symbol independent of its scriptural origins.

For Dennis, however, Noah is more than a biblical character. He is the human face of an existential question: can anyone truly escape the end? The ark represents the fantasy of the *remnant* — a seed that continues after everything else is gone — and Noah is the figure through whom that fantasy has been most durably expressed across three millennia of Western culture.

## In the Book

### Appearances

Noah appears primarily in Chapter 4 ("Water"), Dennis's sustained treatment of flood as a mode of world-ending. "The erosionists clasp Noah's Ark" (004-004) — the ark is pressed into service as a geological hypothesis alongside its theological one, a testament to the story's explanatory flexibility. Dennis traces the flood narrative's global diffusion: "The races without any flood record are few: only the Negroes" (004-007), noting how the story's near-universality across human cultures makes it a genuine candidate for species-wide myth. When flood traditions promise no future Noah — "Next time spare no Noah" (004-013) — Dennis isolates the story's darkest implication: the exception is not guaranteed, and the next world-ending may have no ark.

### Role in Dennis's Argument

Noah represents, for Dennis, the psychological function of the survivor narrative. The end of the world is easier to contemplate if we imagine a remnant — a seed that will continue. But Dennis is careful to note that the ark is not a solution to the problem of world-ending; it is merely an *evasion*. The world still ends; only Noah is saved.

> The waters prevailed exceedingly upon the earth; and all the high hills, that were under the whole heaven, were covered, with only Noah remaining alive and they that were with him in the ark. (004-006)

Dennis's citation of this passage is telling. He emphasizes not the rainbow or the covenant — the hopeful elements of the Noah story — but the *totality* of the destruction. The point is not that Noah survived but that everyone else perished. This is the characteristic Dennis move: invoking biblical authority while simultaneously undermining the comfort it seems to offer.

> Next time spare no Noah. (004-013)

The sentence is devastating in its brevity. After pages surveying the flood traditions of the world — Deucalion and Pyrrha (004-006), the fire-fearers who preferred flood (004-012), the erosionists who needed the ark for their geological theories — Dennis arrives at this single, irreducible point: the cosmos has no obligation to provide survivors. The first flood had its Noah; the next one may not.

In a broader register, Dennis connects Noah to the psychology of cosmic pessimism. The human mind, faced with the prospect of its own extinction, reflexively generates escape narratives. Noah is the archetype of that reflex — the part of us that, even while intellectually accepting that the [[solar-death|sun will die]] and the [[heat-death|universe will cool]], still whispers: *but maybe I will be the exception*.

## Significance

Noah is the figure through whom Dennis explores the psychology of survivorship. To imagine the end of the world is, for most people, impossible without also imagining a vantage point from which to witness it — and a survivor who lives to tell the tale. Noah is that survivor, and his ark is the narrative device that makes the thought of total extinction bearable.

But Dennis's treatment is characteristically double: he invokes Noah's authority while simultaneously undermining the comfort he represents. The covenant of the rainbow is, in Dennis's reading, less a divine promise than a human wish — the hope that the next catastrophe, whatever form it takes, will leave *someone* behind to remember.
