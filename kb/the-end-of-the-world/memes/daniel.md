# Daniel

The biblical prophet and courtier of the Babylonian exile, whose apocalyptic visions — especially the dream of four beasts and the prophecy of seventy weeks — established a framework for calculating the end of the world that Dennis examines as the prototype of chronologically specific eschatology.

## Identity

Daniel, according to the biblical book that bears his name, was a Jewish exile in Babylon who rose to high office through his gift of interpreting dreams and visions. His prophecies — the statue with feet of clay, the four beasts rising from the sea, the ram and the goat, the seventy weeks — are among the most symbolically dense and chronologically specific in the Bible. They have generated centuries of attempts to calculate the exact date of the end, a tradition Dennis surveys in his chapters on eschatological timing.

## In the Book

### Appearances

Daniel appears in Dennis's survey of prophetic chronology, particularly in the chapters dealing with the problem of timing ("When?", "This Year", "Next Year", "Some Time"). Dennis is interested in Daniel not as a theologian but as a source of data for the eschatological calculator: the "seventy weeks" of Daniel 9 became, in subsequent Jewish and Christian interpretation, a cipher for the duration of the world — a number to be decoded, a date to be extracted.

### Role in Dennis's Argument

Daniel represents the quantitative turn in eschatology — the shift from general warnings ("the day of the Lord is coming") to specific chronologies ("seventy weeks are determined upon thy people"). This shift is crucial to Dennis's argument because it transforms eschatology from a poetic or moral discourse into a quasi-scientific one: if the date can be calculated, the prophecy can be tested. And every test, so far, has falsified the prediction — but not, Dennis observes, the impulse to predict.

## Significance

Daniel stands at the boundary between prophetic poetry and computational chronology. His visions have generated more attempted end-date calculations than any other biblical text. For Dennis, this makes him the patron saint of a tradition whose intellectual interest lies not in its accuracy (which is nil) but in its persistence — the refusal to accept that the end of the world is unknowable.
