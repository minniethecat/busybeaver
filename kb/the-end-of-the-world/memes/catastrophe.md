# Catastrophe

The cosmic concussion — stellar collision, orbital derangement, nebular immersion — that Dennis presents in Chapter 7 ("Collision") as the most comprehensive of all world-end scenarios: a destruction that eliminates not only humanity but the planet itself, and in doing so reveals the creative-destructive cycle at the heart of the universe.

## Definition

In Dennis's taxonomy of world-end mechanisms, catastrophe denotes the class of endings caused by collision with another celestial body. It is distinguished from fire (Chapter 3), cold (Chapter 5), and drought (Chapter 4) by its scale: these other ends destroy life or climate, but collision destroys the globe itself. "Collision, the one fate that destroys not only man but his home, alone is destroyer and creator" (007-025). Catastrophe is thus the most absolute of the eschatological modes — there is no remnant, no survivor, no Phoenix-like rebirth for the individual world.

Dennis distinguishes several varieties of catastrophic end: direct collision with another star, near-miss gravitational derangement that de-orbits the Earth into the sun, and passage through a nebular region whose "solid matter, stardust, in-drifting meteorites" (007-018) acts as a brake on the sun's motion. Each variant produces the same result — the liquefaction and dispersal of the Earth — but the mechanisms differ in their probability and in the cosmic timescale over which they operate.

## In the Book

### Context

Catastrophe is the subject of Chapter 7 ("Collision"), which occupies a central position in Dennis's sequence of eschatological modes. The chapter follows the solar death of Chapter 5 and the stellar cooling of Chapter 6, and it marks a shift from gradual, entropic ends to sudden, violent ones. Dennis's treatment of collision is notably richer in astronomical detail than his treatment of fire or cold — he draws on contemporary astrophysics to construct a scenario that is both scientifically grounded and rhetorically heightened.

The chapter's argument is that collision is not merely possible but inevitable, given sufficient time. "Sooner or later, in process of unwearying time (time that goes on forever), each star of heaven encounters each other star" (007-009). The universe is not a static mechanism but a dynamic and occasionally violent system; catastrophe is built into its structure.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis approaches catastrophe with a characteristic blend of scientific exposition and rhetorical intensification. The chapter opens with a measured, almost textbook survey of collision mechanisms — comets, meteors, dark stars, nebular drag — but builds toward a passage of extraordinary rhetorical force, in which Dennis successively rejects the word "collision" as inadequate to its subject:

"Collision, which thus includes grazing and mere propinquity, is too narrow a word. Collision, which implies splintering impact of solid bodies, is too massive a word. Collision, which, in lubberly portrayal of unliving earths in lumbering percussion, conjures up no vision of the dying crying souls upon them, is too abstract, too astronomical a word" (007-022–024).

This passage is a key exhibit in Dennis's method: the scientific vocabulary is precise but insufficient; it fails to capture the human meaning of the event it describes. Dennis does not reject the science — he incorporates it — but he insists on supplementing it with a language adequate to the terror of annihilation.

The chapter's conclusion generalizes catastrophe from a physical event to a cosmic principle. "Out of the chance riot of stars came worlds, came living existence, came we. In new chance riot we shall depart. The heavens go crashing and whirling. The universe forms, un-forms, re-forms" (007-026). Catastrophe is not an exception to cosmic order; it is the mechanism by which the cosmos continually remakes itself.
