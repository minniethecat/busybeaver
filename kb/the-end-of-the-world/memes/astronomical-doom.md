# Astronomical Doom

The class of end-of-world scenarios originating beyond Earth — from cometary impact to stellar collision to the eventual death of the sun — which Dennis surveys in the book's opening chapters as the frame within which all terrestrial eschatologies must ultimately be measured.

## Definition

Astronomical doom encompasses every mechanism by which the cosmos itself might destroy the Earth: collision with a comet or meteor, near-passage of another star disrupting planetary orbits, the sun's eventual exhaustion of its nuclear fuel, and the long-term thermodynamic death of the universe. These are, in Dennis's taxonomy, the most scientifically credible forms of world-end — not because they are imminent, but because they are inevitable on a sufficiently long timescale.

Dennis distinguishes astronomical doom from theological and geological doom primarily by its impersonality. A comet does not judge; a dying star does not punish. Astronomical doom is the end of the world stripped of moral significance — pure physics, unsupplemented by meaning. This is both its philosophical power (it is immune to theological rebuttal) and its existential limitation (it offers no narrative, no redemption, no justice).

## In the Book

### Context

The book's first two chapters are devoted to astronomical doom. Chapter 1 ("The Sun") traces the life cycle of the sun from its turbulent formation through its stable middle age to its eventual death, establishing the cosmic clock against which all other end-of-world timetables must be calibrated. "Whirling around a star little different if at all from millions of others" (001-002) — the Copernican principle applied to eschatology.

Chapter 2 ("Comets") catalogs the history of cometary fear and its gradual displacement by scientific understanding. Dennis traces the transformation of comets from divine portents to predictable celestial bodies — a case study in the demystification of astronomical doom. The chapter's argument is that understanding a threat is the first step toward surviving it, or at least toward facing it without superstition.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis treats astronomical doom with a mixture of scientific respect and existential resignation. He takes the physics seriously — the book's early chapters are dense with astronomical data — but he draws back from the conclusion that cosmic inevitability renders human life meaningless. The opposite: awareness of astronomical timescales is, for Dennis, a form of liberation from the urgent terrors of prophecy and judgment.

> [W]hirling around a star little different if at all from millions of others. (001-002)
