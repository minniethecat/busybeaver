The Black Death was the catastrophic bubonic plague pandemic of the fourteenth century (1346–1353) that killed an estimated one-third to one-half of Europe's population. In Dennis's book, it appears briefly as an example of a [[catastrophe|disaster]] preceded by a comet.

## Overview

The Black Death swept across Eurasia in the mid-fourteenth century, transmitted by fleas on rats along trade routes. In Europe, it caused demographic collapse, economic disruption, and widespread social and religious upheaval — including intensified apocalyptic [[hope|expectation]]s and waves of anti-Semitic violence.

## In the Book

### Context

The Black Death appears in "Comet"（002）, within Dennis's catalogue of historical comet apparitions and their associated [[catastrophe|disaster]]s. He notes tersely that "in the fourteenth century the Black Death was narrowly heralded"（002-012）— implying a cometary precursor.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis's treatment is characteristically brief and suggestive. The Black Death is one data point in his survey of comet-[[catastrophe|disaster]] correlations, adding to the cumulative weight of historical examples. The word "narrowly" — meaning shortly or closely — suggests the comet preceded the plague by a small interval, reinforcing the ancient association between celestial portents and terrestrial [[disaster|catastrophe]].

> In the fourteenth century the Black Death was narrowly heralded（002-012）

## Significance

The Black Death serves a minor but emblematic role in the book. As the worst pandemic in recorded history — a [[disaster|catastrophe]] that seemed to contemporaries like the End of the World — its alleged association with a comet strengthens Dennis's thesis that comets have always been regarded as harbingers of [[fate|doom]]. The brevity of the reference also reflects the book's method: rapid accumulation of examples rather than deep historical analysis.
