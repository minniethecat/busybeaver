The Great Flood is the universal [[flood-myth|deluge]] myth found in cultures worldwide. In Dennis's book, it appears as the culminating [[disaster|catastrophe]] in the Maya sequence of world Ages, and serves as the archetypal image of [[perish|destruction]] by water.

## Overview

In the Maya cosmology recorded by the Yucatan people, four past Ages or "Suns" each ended in a world-destroying [[disaster|catastrophe]]: the Sun of Earth (drought), the Sun of [[fire|Fire]] ([[fire|conflagration]]), the Sun of Air (hurricane), and the Sun of Water ([[biblical-flood|the Great Flood]]). Dennis notes that [[biblical-flood|the Great Flood]] is "in world tradition generally the last general world [[catastrophe|disaster]]"（013-007）, citing its near-universal presence across human cultures.

## In the Book

### Context

The Great Flood appears in "Next Year"（013）, the chapter surveying worldwide apocalyptic traditions. Dennis presents it within the Maya four-Ages framework, drawing a parallel between ancient cosmologies and his own catalogue of possible world-ends.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis treats the Maya sequence as remarkably prescient — "[[cosmos|the whole]] sequence is an accurate account" of the actual geological-catastrophic possibilities he enumerates elsewhere in the book（013-007）. The Great Flood, in particular, corresponds to his Water-End scenario: the comet striking ocean, the melting of ice caps, the drowning of continents. By citing the Maya, Dennis suggests that ancient peoples intuited the same [[fate|doom]]-scenarios that modern astronomy confirms.

> The Mayas of Yucatan taught that there had been four past Ages or Suns: the Sun of Earth, ended by the Great Drought; the Sun of Fire, ended by the Great Conflagration; the Sun of Air, ended by the Great Hurricane; the Sun of Water, ended by the Great Flood — in world tradition generally the last general world disaster（013-007）

## The Water-End

Dennis opens Chapter 4 ("Water") with the terse declaration: "Not fire but flood" (004-001). It is one of the book's most arresting chapter openings — a reversal of expectation in a work otherwise dominated by fiery endings. Dennis devotes sustained attention to the flood as a world-ending mechanism, examining it through geological, mythological, and psychological lenses.

### The Comet-Flood Connection

Dennis's Water-End is not independent of his [[comet-impact|comet]] hypothesis but a variant of it. A comet striking the ocean would produce tidal waves on a planetary scale, "worldwide floods; colliding with the land" (002-031). The distinction between fire-end and water-end is, in Dennis's physical framework, largely a matter of where the comet strikes: land yields fire, ocean yields flood. He classifies flood-through-heat as "a mere incidental water-end" (004-004), acknowledging it as a secondary effect of the same [[comet-impact|impact]] mechanism rather than an independent mode of destruction. The water-end shares its causal origin with the fire-end; what differs is the medium of destruction — rising seas rather than burning skies.

### The Universal Deluge Tradition

Dennis is struck by the near-universality of flood mythology. "The races without any flood record are few: only the Negroes, the Japanese and the Egyptians" (004-007). He catalogs the biblical account — "how the windows of heaven were opened and all the fountains of the great deep were broken up" (004-006) — alongside the Maya Sun of Water, the Greek Deukalion myth, and other traditions. The flood story's distribution across cultures separated by oceans and millennia is, for Dennis, evidence that the human imagination has independently converged on the same apocalyptic image.

Yet Dennis does not take this universality as proof that a single worldwide deluge occurred. "Despite this almost universal tradition, no quiet universal deluge may ever have taken place" (004-008). His skepticism is characteristically double-edged: the myth is too widespread to dismiss entirely, yet too various in its particulars to reduce to a single historical event. What matters to Dennis is not the flood's historicity but its psychological grip — "that man believed the Flood had happened, is what matters here; and believed it might happen again, and next time spare no Noah" (004-013).

### Flood Psychology and the Nineteenth-Century Revival

Dennis notes a practical advantage that flood-believers held over their fire-fearing counterparts: "they had precedents for protecting themselves — and knew how" (004-012). Noah and Deukalion provided models of survival through preparation, a narrative template unavailable to those who expected destruction by fire.

The flood-fear, Dennis observes, underwent a peculiar revival in the nineteenth century. "For centuries, however, the flood-fear halted until, during the nineteenth, it was given new life by certain geologists" (004-014). The discovery of marine fossils on mountaintops, the evidence of ancient seas covering now-dry land, and the emerging theory of ice ages all lent scientific credibility to the idea that water had once transformed the world — and might do so again. Dennis sees in this geological turn a convergence of ancient myth and modern science: the flood story, long dismissed as primitive superstition, found unexpected corroboration in the rocks.

## Significance

The Great Flood serves as the mythological archetype of Dennis's Water-End. Its universality — appearing independently across cultures — is presented as evidence that the human imagination has always anticipated the same basic [[disaster|catastrophe]] scenarios. The Maya four-Ages framework also maps neatly onto Dennis's methodical typology of [[fate|doom]], lending ancient authority to his modern catalogue.
