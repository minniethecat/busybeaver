# Decreation

Mythological|The symmetrical reversal of Genesis — 'Fiat,a shake of the sceptre,and then Nothing' — which Dennis presents as the most radical supernatural vision of the end,in which the world is not destroyed but unmade.

## Overview

This event represents one of the modes of world-ending that Dennis surveys in his comprehensive catalogue of eschatological possibilities. Whether natural or supernatural, gradual or sudden, each mode receives careful treatment that weighs its physical plausibility against its psychological resonance.

## In the Book

### Context

Dennis examines this event across multiple chapters, presenting the scientific or theological case for its occurrence and evaluating the evidence. The treatment is characteristic of his method: neither credulous nor dismissive, but analytical — tracing the history of the idea, the mechanisms by which it could occur, and the arguments for and against its inevitability.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis's approach is to present the event as a genuine possibility — not a certainty, but a scenario serious enough to warrant consideration. His prose rises to match the scale of the subject, and his erudition provides historical and cross-cultural context that situates each mode of ending within the broader human conversation about [[death|mortality]], time, and [[order|cosmic order]].

## Significance

This event contributes to Dennis's cumulative argument that the world can end — has always been able to end — in more ways than any single culture or tradition has imagined. The multiplicity of possible ends is itself the book's deepest insight: that certainty about the mode of the end is a form of complacency, and that the true number of ways the world could perish is limited only by the human capacity to conceive of endings.
