## More Worlds to Conquer

The corpus invokes Alexander not for his military achievements but for the restless ambition that survived his conquest of the known world:

> Alexander the Great was not sure, but he wanted to hold this view: there would have been more worlds to conquer. [016-005]

The passage captures a famous anecdote: upon realizing the extent of his conquests, Alexander wept because there were no more worlds to conquer. The corpus's version is more nuanced — Alexander "was not sure" whether more worlds existed, but "he wanted to hold this view." The desire for further conquest preceded the certainty that further conquest was possible. The line appears in a meditation on philosophers and the limits of knowledge, where the corpus catalogs the ambitions of great men — [[plato|Plato]], [[aristotle|Aristotle]], Alexander — and asks what remains when all human striving reaches its boundary.

## The Pupil of Aristotle

Alexander was the most famous pupil of [[aristotle|Aristotle]], who tutored him from the age of thirteen. The philosophical education Aristotle provided — in ethics, politics, natural science, and literature — shaped the young conqueror's self-conception. He carried a copy of Homer's *Iliad* annotated by Aristotle on his campaigns and saw himself as a new Achilles. The corpus's pairing of Alexander with [[aristotle|Aristotle]] and [[plato|Plato]] in the same passage [016-005] draws on this teacher-pupil relationship: the greatest conqueror was shaped by the greatest philosopher, and both — the man of action and the man of thought — confronted the same ultimate limit: the finitude of the world.
