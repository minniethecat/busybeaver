# Sibyl

Prophetess of Greco-Roman antiquity, author of the Sibylline Books — collections of oracular utterances that were consulted in Rome for prophetic guidance. Dennis references the Sibylline tradition as part of the sprawling network of apocalyptic sources.

## Identity

The Sibyl (or Sibyls) were female prophets in classical antiquity, most famously the Cumaean Sibyl who guided Aeneas to the underworld. The Sibylline Books, kept in the Temple of Jupiter Capitolinus and consulted by the Roman Senate in times of crisis, blended Greek, Roman, and Jewish apocalyptic themes into a syncretic prophetic corpus（010-042）.

## In the Book

### Appearances

The Sibylline tradition surfaces in Dennis's survey of date-setters and apocalyptic calculators. The Sibylline Books are listed alongside other esoteric sources — horoscopes, abacuses, swanpans — that enthusiasts pore over in their quest to determine the date of the end（010-002）.

### Role in Dennis's Argument

The Sibylline references serve Dennis's ironic portrait of apocalyptic enthusiasm. By grouping the Sibylline Books with horoscopes and abacuses, he suggests that the quest for precise dating is a form of pagan superstition rather than genuine prophetic insight. Pseudo-Methodius is credited with rediscovering the Sibylline Leaves as a "date-mine"（010-042）.

> they will ignore their rivals, and continue to pore over their abacuses and swanpans and horoscopes and sibylline books（010-002）

## Significance

The Sibyl bridges classical and Christian apocalyptic traditions. Her presence in the book highlights the syncretic character of Western eschatology — the blending of pagan oracles, Jewish prophecy, and Christian revelation into a single tradition of end-times speculation.
