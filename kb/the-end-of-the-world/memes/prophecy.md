# Prophecy

The claim to foreknowledge of future events — above all, of the world's end — whether derived from scriptural exegesis, numerical computation, or direct revelation, which Dennis examines in Chapter 10 ("This Year") as one of humanity's most persistent and most consistently disappointed intellectual enterprises.

## Definition

Dennis's use of "prophecy" spans the full range of predictive speech: biblical prophecy (the apocalyptic discourses of Daniel, Revelation, and the Olivet discourse), numerical prophecy (the calculation of end-dates from scriptural numbers, especially the 1,260 days of Revelation and the 2,300 evenings-and-mornings of Daniel), and personal prophetic revelation (the direct announcement of a date claimed to have been disclosed by God). What unifies these varieties is their shared structure: a sacred text or experience is treated as a cryptogram whose correct decoding yields a calendar date for the Apocalypse.

Dennis is interested less in the theology of prophecy than in its sociology and psychology. "What proportion of the people held by this prophecy we have no worthy evidence" (010-021). The question that matters to him is not whether prophecy is true — he takes its falsity as demonstrated by the historical record — but why it continues to compel belief despite a perfect track record of failure. Prophecy, in Dennis's telling, is not a theological error but a human constant: "The end has endlessly been predicted for a given year, named and numbered" (010-001). The opening sentence of Chapter 10 establishes the pattern at once — endless prediction, endless disappointment, endless renewal.

The prophetic impulse draws on deep psychological springs. Dennis notes that early Christianity, "still a persecuted creed offering no hope of present triumph, needed some such faith in not too distant punishment" (010-022). Prophecy was not a fringe enthusiasm but a survival mechanism — the assurance that cosmic justice, however delayed, would arrive on schedule. This insight reframes prophecy from a doctrinal curiosity into a window on the human condition: the refusal to accept that the future is genuinely opaque.

## In the Book

### Context

Prophecy is the central subject of Chapter 10 ("This Year"), which traces the history of end-time prediction from the early Church through the Reformation and into Dennis's own early twentieth century. The chapter catalogs dozens of specific prophecies and their inevitable disappointments: the Year 1000, the Joachite Age of the Spirit, the Millerite Great Disappointment of 1844, the 1917 Kaiser-Beast identification. Each episode follows the same arc: fervent expectation, exact calculation, widespread conviction, failed prediction, and — crucially — reinterpretation rather than abandonment.

The chapter's title, "This Year," captures the recurring refrain of prophecy: the end is always *this year*, imminent and specific. When the year passes uneventfully, the date is recalculated, the prophecy re-explained, and the cycle begins again. Dennis traces the migration of numerical prophecy from the early Church Fathers through the medieval Rabbis to the Reformation Protestants: "In the Roman Church itself, numerical prophecy has had slightest renewal; it was into the hands of the Rabbis and the Protestants, witches of Endor and warlocks of England, that the game now passed" (010-043).

Dennis situates the prophetic tradition within a broader history of chiliastic expectation. The early Church expected Christ's return within the lifetime of the apostles; when that failed, the expectation was deferred but not abandoned. "Though discouraged, the chiliasts were not dismayed. There had been a slight miscalculation; it was not the year One Thousand, but the one thousandth year" (010-040). The pattern of recalibration — the slight miscalculation, the minor misreading, the corrupted version — is the engine that drives two millennia of prophetic persistence. "Ah! 'twas a trifling error, a minor misreading of the text, reliance on a corrupted version" (010-050). Every failed prophecy generates its own exculpatory narrative.

The close of the Middle Ages saw a revival driven by newly rediscovered texts: "Pseudo-Methodius, rediscovered, proved a rich date-mine; also the Sibylline Leaves" (010-042). The Reformation supercharged the enterprise by placing the Bible in lay hands and encouraging private interpretation — the very conditions under which numerical prophecy flourishes.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis treats prophecy with sustained irony but not contempt. He recognizes in the failed prophets something genuinely human: the desire to know, the refusal to accept epistemic limits, the need for cosmic narrative. When William Miller's predicted date passed without incident, Dennis notes the human cost: "So everywhere the night had come and gone; the prophecy was a delusion, William and Joshua deceivers" (010-104). The word "delusion" is carefully chosen — not fraud, but self-deception so deep that it can only be experienced as revelation.

Dennis is attentive to the rhetorical structure of prophetic argument. He notes how "every prophecy concords: Apocalypse with Apocrypha, the Pyramid corridors with Daniel's chronology" (010-109). The concordance method — finding cross-confirmations between unrelated texts — is the engine that drives prophetic conviction. Once you accept the premise that all sacred and semi-sacred texts encode the same hidden timetable, every seeming coincidence becomes evidence, and evidence accumulates without ever facing the risk of disconfirmation.

Dennis's most penetrating observation about prophecy is its endless resourcefulness in the face of failure. When the year passes, the prophecy is not abandoned but refined: "The Year was all he had ever announced as part of the revelation proper; that gave them nine months yet, till the last day of December" (010-105). The narrowing window, the reinterpretation of the original claim, the discovery of a calendar error — these are not bugs in the prophetic enterprise but features, the mechanisms by which prophecy immunizes itself against falsification. Dennis captures this dynamic in one of his most sardonic sentences: "But saints, like scientists, have rich subterfuge in modification that laymen must envy them, and a hold on their following that scientists may not claim" (010-105).

> So everywhere the night had come and gone; the prophecy was a delusion, William and Joshua deceivers. (010-104)

The psychological portrait Dennis draws is remarkably modern. He describes William Miller not as a charlatan but as a sincere believer trapped by his own hermeneutic: "William had no fear for himself; only fear that the time might be too short for him to spread the tidings to those who were chosen" (010-060). This is not the profile of a fraud but of a man whose epistemological framework admits no possibility of error — and who, when error arrives, can only process it as a recalibration rather than a refutation.

> But saints, like scientists, have rich subterfuge in modification that laymen must envy them, and a hold on their following that scientists may not claim. (010-105)

Dennis extends his analysis into his own present moment. Writing around 1930, he catalogs the most recent failures: "Nor more successful this last decade's fatal choices — '21 (April), '24, '28 — culled by new desperate subtlety with the Book of Daniel and the Great Pyramid" (010-108). And he looks ahead with a kind of weary prescience to the dates that will occupy future prophets: "For our children and children's children there are the 2000 and, two years before it, the 1998. One is the Thousand years twice told, the pre-ordinate" (010-110). Dennis could not have known how precisely this prediction of predictions would be fulfilled — the year 2000 did indeed generate a fresh wave of apocalyptic prophecy, confirming his thesis that the pattern is inexhaustible.

In Chapter 12, Dennis steps back to examine prophecy not as a historical phenomenon but as a symptom of a deeper intellectual habit: the preference for vast, round numbers over the patient accumulation of evidence. He contrasts the "intensive figure-play" of older prophecy with the "bold zeros" of modern cosmological speculation (012-020). The prophetic mind and the scientific mind, he suggests, share a common temptation — to let the grandeur of the number substitute for the rigor of the method.
