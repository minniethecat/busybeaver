# Sahara

The great desert of North Africa — once fertile savanna traversed by rivers and inhabited by diverse fauna — which Dennis invokes as geological proof that the earth's climate is not stable and that regions once lush can become uninhabitable.

## Location

The Sahara spans approximately 9.2 million square kilometers across North Africa, from the Atlantic to the Red Sea. It is the largest hot desert in the world. But it was not always so: geological evidence shows that during the African Humid Period (roughly 14,000 to 5,000 years ago), the Sahara was a green savanna with lakes, rivers, and abundant wildlife. Dennis uses this transformation as evidence that climate change on a catastrophic scale is not hypothetical but historical.

## In the Book

### Context

The Sahara appears in Dennis's discussion of drought and climate change (Chapters 5 and beyond) as an example of the kind of transformation that could, under different circumstances, become global. The desiccation of the Sahara was a regional catastrophe; the desiccation of the entire earth — through solar evolution, atmospheric loss, or other mechanisms — would be the same process writ large.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis treats the Sahara as a cautionary example: here is a place that was once fertile and is now barren, not through any human action but through natural climate cycles. The lesson is that habitability is not guaranteed. What happened to the Sahara could, on a longer timescale and through different mechanisms, happen to the entire planet.

## Significance

The Sahara functions in Dennis's argument as concrete evidence for the fragility of the conditions that support life. It is not necessary to imagine a future catastrophe; the geological record provides examples of past catastrophes that have already transformed entire regions from garden to desert.
