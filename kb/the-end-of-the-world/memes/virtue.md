# Virtue

The moral quality that fire has been believed to possess across cultures — purifying soul and body alike — examined by Dennis in Chapter 3 ("Fire") as a thread connecting ancient funeral rites to apocalyptic expectation, and in Chapter 12 as the fragile human achievement that terror can strip away.

## Definition

"Virtue" in Dennis's usage carries a double sense: the conventional moral meaning (righteousness, goodness of character) and the older, alchemical sense of a power or efficacy inherent in a substance or act. It is this second sense that dominates Chapter 3, where Dennis observes that "many lands and ages have believed in its purifying virtue alike for soul and body" (003-002): fire was thought to possess an intrinsic moral cleansing power, capable of burning away impurity. The word "virtue" thus bridges Dennis's treatment of physical destruction and moral transformation — the fire that consumes the world is also the fire that cleanses it.

In Chapter 12, virtue takes its more familiar moral sense: the qualities of character — compassion, restraint, fellow-feeling — that civilization cultivates and that apocalyptic terror can dissolve. "In a few hearts, terror may work an instantaneous salvation, filling the empty rooms with incense of propitiatory virtue and pious works" (012-076). The ambiguity is deliberate: does terror produce genuine virtue, or only its propitiatory counterfeit? Dennis leaves the question open.

## In the Book

### Context

Virtue as purifying power appears in Chapter 3 ("Fire"), Dennis's exhaustive typology of fire as eschatological agent. The chapter surveys fire in mythology, religion, and science: funeral pyres, heretics' stakes, solar death, terrestrial self-immolation. Throughout, Dennis returns to the idea that fire's destructive power has been consistently interpreted as morally significant — fire does not merely destroy; it judges. The purifying virtue attributed to flame is, for Dennis, one of the deep structures of eschatological thinking: the end of the world is imagined as fire precisely because fire carries this moral connotation.

In Chapter 12 ("Some Time"), virtue appears in a different register. Dennis considers what happens to human character when eschatological prediction fails — or when it seems to be coming true. Terror, he suggests, can work in two directions: it can strip away the thin veneer of civilization, releasing "the beast within" (012-076), or it can, in a few rare cases, produce a desperate turn toward righteousness. The virtue that emerges under apocalyptic pressure is ambiguous — it may be genuine moral transformation, or it may be the propitiatory gesture of a frightened soul.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis's treatment of virtue is characteristically unsentimental. He does not preach virtue or celebrate it; he observes how the concept functions in eschatological discourse. The purifying virtue of fire is, for him, a historical fact about human belief — not a metaphysical claim but an anthropological one. That "the impurer the flesh or spirit the purer had the flames to be" (003-002) tells us something about the logic of sacrificial thinking, not about the nature of fire itself.

This anthropological approach extends to Chapter 12's treatment of moral virtue under apocalyptic pressure. Dennis is interested in what terror reveals about human nature: the beast within, yes, but also the capacity for "propitiatory virtue" (012-076) — the desperate attempt to be found righteous when the end arrives. He offers no easy consolation; the ambiguity between true virtue and mere propitiation is left unresolved.

> Fire is the same word as pure, and many lands and ages have believed in its purifying virtue alike for soul and body. The impurer the flesh or spirit the purer had the flames to be. (003-002)
