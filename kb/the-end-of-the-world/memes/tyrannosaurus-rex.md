# Tyrannosaurus Rex

*Tyrannosaurus rex* represents, in the popular imagination and in the logic of Dennis's argument alike, the ultimate test case for the universality of [[organic-extinction|extinction]]. If the most powerful predator that ever walked the Earth could not survive a changing planet, what chance does any species have — including our own?

## Identity

*Tyrannosaurus rex* was a theropod dinosaur that lived during the last two million years of the Cretaceous Period in western North America. It was among the largest terrestrial carnivores ever known: up to 12 meters in length, 4 meters tall at the hips, and weighing up to 9 metric tons. Its bite force — estimated at up to 57,000 newtons — was the most powerful of any terrestrial animal ever studied. It was, by any measure, the apex of predatory evolution on land.

Yet for all its power, *T. rex* vanished at the K-Pg boundary 66 million years ago, along with every other non-avian dinosaur. Its extinction is the most compressed possible illustration of Dennis's thesis: that might, dominance, and evolutionary success provide no immunity against planetary-scale change. The [[dinosaur|dinosaurs]] as a group are Dennis's archetype of natural extinction, and *T. rex* is the most dramatic member of that group — the one whose disappearance seems most impossible, and therefore most instructive.

## Extinction Narrative

The extinction of *T. rex* was part of the K-Pg mass extinction event, now attributed to the Chicxulub asteroid [[cosmic-collision|impact]] combined with massive volcanic activity in the Deccan Traps. Whatever the precise cause, the result was the elimination of approximately 75% of all plant and animal species on Earth. The [[dinosaur|dinosaurs]] — dominant for 170 million years — were wiped out in what was, on geological timescales, a single catastrophic moment.

Dennis does not name *T. rex* specifically in his text, but the logic of his argument encompasses it entirely. When he writes of the [[dinosaur|dinosaurs]] as evidence that planetary-scale death can occur without human agency or divine intervention, *T. rex* is the most vivid exemplar. The king of the [[dinosaur|dinosaurs]] died like all the rest — not because it was weak, but because the world it was adapted to ceased to exist.

This is the core eschatological lesson that Dennis extracts from the fossil record: extinction is not a judgment on the unfit but a condition of existence in a changing universe. The [[death|extinction]] of *T. rex* is not a tragedy that befell an inferior creature but a demonstration that superiority is always contextual — and contexts always change.
