# Jupiter

The supreme deity of the Roman pantheon, god of sky and thunder, who appears throughout Dennis's survey of mythological and astrological eschatology as a personification of the cosmic forces that may destroy what they once ordered.

## Identity

Jupiter (Greek Zeus) was the chief god of the Roman state religion, associated with the sky, thunder, lightning, law, and sovereignty. His temple on the Capitoline Hill — the Temple of Jupiter Optimus Maximus — was the religious center of the Roman Republic and Empire. Dennis invokes Jupiter primarily in the context of planetary astrology: as the largest planet in the solar system, Jupiter's astrological position in conjunction with other planets was historically taken to portend catastrophe. "Saturn, Mars and Jupiter in conjunction" (010-042) signified flood in astrological lore.

## In the Book

### Appearances

Jupiter appears in Chapter 10 ("This Year") as part of an astrological configuration thought to predict world-ending events (010-042). In Chapter 6 ("Cold"), Dennis notes the solar cult that rivaled Jupiter-worship: the sun's Roman temple on the Quirinal — "golden Rome — *Urbs solaris imperialis*" (006-009) — suggesting that even Jupiter's supremacy gave way to solar worship as the declining empire sought a more universal deity.

### Role in Dennis's Argument

Jupiter functions as a symbol of the astrological worldview — the belief that planetary bodies govern human destiny. Dennis treats astrology not as a rival to astronomy but as its ancestor: "Astrology, returning to the paths of her stepmother astronomy, might cast a fatal horoscope" (015-005). Jupiter and his fellow planetary gods represent an earlier stage in humanity's attempt to read cosmic patterns — a stage that modern astronomy has not abolished but transformed.

## Significance

Jupiter represents the convergence of mythology, astrology, and early astronomy in Dennis's intellectual history of eschatology. The god who hurls thunderbolts and the planet whose orbital position foretells doom are the same figure seen through different lenses — and Dennis's method is precisely to hold these lenses together rather than replacing one with the other.
