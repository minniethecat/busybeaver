Resurrection is the theological claim that death is not final — that at the culmination of history, the dead shall be raised bodily to face judgment and enter either eternal life or eternal loss. In an eschatological framework concerned with "the end of the world," resurrection is the hinge between destruction and renewal: the moment when the world's death becomes the individual's new birth.

## Definition

In Christian doctrine, resurrection (*anastasis*) refers specifically to the raising of the dead at the Last Day, modeled on the resurrection of Jesus Christ as the "firstfruits" (1 Corinthians 15:20). It is distinguished from mere immortality of the soul: resurrection is bodily, physical, and collective — it happens to everyone at once, at the end of history. In Jewish apocalyptic thought, resurrection emerged during the Second Temple period as an answer to the problem of theodicy: if the righteous suffer and die without vindication, justice requires that they be raised to receive their reward.

Across religious traditions, the idea of resurrection takes different forms: the Islamic *yawm al-qiyamah* (Day of Resurrection), the Zoroastrian *frashokereti* (final renovation), and various forms of rebirth and renewal in Hindu and Buddhist eschatology. What unites these traditions is the conviction that the world's end is not a simple extinguishing but a transition — that something, and someone, survives the catastrophe.

## In the Book

### Context

Resurrection as an explicit term does not appear in Dennis's surveyed text, but the concept pervades the Christian eschatological framework within which much of the book's source material operates. Every prophecy of the Second Coming, every calculation of the millennium, every vision of the New Jerusalem presupposes resurrection — without it, the apocalyptic drama has no audience beyond the generation alive when the end arrives.

Dennis's focus on death in all its modes — by fire, water, cold, comet, collision — raises the question that resurrection was invented to answer: if the world ends in total destruction, what becomes of the dead who died before the end? The doctrine of resurrection solves this problem by retroactively including all of human history in the final event. No one misses the apocalypse; everyone is raised to witness it.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis approaches resurrection indirectly, through his treatment of death and the hopes that surround it. "Man's same hopes of defeating death: hopes born of cowering fear" (001-014) names the psychological engine behind resurrection belief: the refusal to accept that death is the last word. Dennis does not argue for or against resurrection; rather, he situates it within a taxonomy of human strategies for managing the terror of mortality. Resurrection is, in this reading, the most ambitious of those strategies — not merely delaying death but reversing it.

The book's cumulative movement toward cosmic death — the heat death of the universe, the exhaustion of all energy — poses an implicit challenge to resurrection doctrine. If the physical universe itself runs down, what substrate remains for resurrected bodies? Dennis does not pose this question directly, but the logic of his argument presses toward it. The cold death of the sun and the entropic death of the cosmos leave no obvious stage for the resurrection drama. Yet Dennis also notes that "Man will fight his fate. He will adapt himself" (006-024). Resurrection may be understood as the ultimate adaptation — the refusal to accept that the cosmic clock, once run down, cannot be wound again.
