# Julio-Claudian

The Julio-Claudian dynasty — the first five Roman emperors (Augustus, Tiberius, Caligula, Claudius, Nero), ruling from 27 BCE to 68 CE — represents in Dennis's argument the political world within which early Christian eschatology was forged and against which its apocalyptic imagery was directed.

## Definition

The Julio-Claudian dynasty was the founding imperial family of Rome, established by Augustus (the grandnephew and adopted son of Julius Caesar) and ending with Nero, whose death without an heir plunged the empire into the Year of the Four Emperors. The dynasty's name reflects its dual lineage: the Julii (the family of Caesar) and the Claudii (the family of Tiberius and Claudius).

For Dennis's purposes, the Julio-Claudians matter primarily as the political context of the New Testament. Jesus was born under Augustus ("there went out a decree from Caesar Augustus"); his ministry and crucifixion occurred under Tiberius; the early Church spread through the reign of Claudius (who expelled the Jews from Rome) and Nero (under whom Peter and Paul were traditionally martyred). The Beast of Revelation, with its seven heads and ten horns, was widely interpreted — both in antiquity and by Dennis's contemporaries — as a coded reference to the Julio-Claudian succession and the imperial cult.

## In the Book

### Context

The Julio-Claudians appear indirectly but pervasively. Chapter 2's discussion of cometary portents is anchored in the death of Julius Caesar and the "Caesaris astrum" of 43 BCE — the comet that inaugurated the tradition of reading imperial deaths in the sky (002-011). This tradition extended through the Julio-Claudian period: comets were reported at the deaths of Claudius and other members of the dynasty.

Chapter 10's survey of early Christian prophecy is set against the backdrop of Julio-Claudian (and later Flavian) persecution. The early Church's apocalyptic expectations were shaped, Dennis argues, by the experience of imperial violence — the conviction that the Beast (Rome, the Julio-Claudian system) would soon be destroyed by divine intervention.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis treats the Julio-Claudians as historical furniture rather than as subjects of sustained analysis. They are the backdrop — the political reality that made apocalyptic hope both necessary and, as history turned out, premature. Rome did not fall in the first century; the Julio-Claudians were succeeded by the Flavians, then the Antonines; the empire Christianized itself rather than being destroyed by Christianity. The apocalyptic expectations of the Julio-Claudian period are thus, in Dennis's framework, the first in a long series of disappointed end-time predictions.
