# Venus

The second planet from the sun, earth's closest planetary neighbor, and — in its actual physical conditions — a preview of what the earth could become under different astronomical circumstances: a greenhouse inferno with surface temperatures hot enough to melt lead.

## Location

Venus orbits the sun at an average distance of 108 million kilometers — closer than earth, and consequently hotter. Its thick carbon dioxide atmosphere creates a runaway greenhouse effect, producing surface temperatures of approximately 465°C and atmospheric pressure ninety times that of earth. Dennis's references to Venus are primarily in the context of planetary astronomy: the planet as a data point in the inventory of possible worlds. But the implication — that Venus represents one possible evolutionary path for an earth-like planet — is consistent with the book's wider argument about the contingency of habitability.

## In the Book

### Context

Venus appears in Dennis's astronomical survey, referenced alongside other planets in the context of orbital mechanics and solar system evolution. Its mythological associations — Venus as the goddess of love, the morning star, Lucifer — are present in Dennis's literary background but not foregrounded in his scientific treatment.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis references Venus briefly, primarily as an astronomical body. But the planet's physical character — a world rendered uninhabitable by a runaway greenhouse effect — resonates with the book's themes in ways that Dennis leaves largely implicit. Venus is the counter-example to earth: a planet of similar size and composition that nonetheless became a furnace rather than a garden.

## Significance

Venus represents the path not taken — the evolutionary possibility that earth avoided but could, under different initial conditions, have followed. Its presence in Dennis's astronomical inventory is a reminder that habitability is not a given but a rare and potentially fragile achievement.
