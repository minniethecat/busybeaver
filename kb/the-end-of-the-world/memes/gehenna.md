Gehenna — the Valley of Hinnom outside Jerusalem, which became the Jewish and Christian symbol of hellfire — is invoked in Dennis's book as the archetypal image of total destruction by fire. The comet's impact, he writes, would bore "huge holes through to Gehenna beneath."

## Location

Gehenna refers to the Valley of Hinnom (Ge-Hinnom in Hebrew), a ravine south of Jerusalem. In biblical history, it was a site of child sacrifice; by the New Testament period, its perpetually burning refuse fires had made it a metaphor for the place of final punishment.

## In the Book

### Context

Gehenna appears in "Comet"（002）and "Crash"（007）. In both, it serves as the ultimate image of subterranean fire — the Earth's interior as an inferno waiting to be breached.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis deploys Gehenna as the culminating image in his catalogue of comet-impact horrors. Should a comet strike the land, it would not merely crater the surface but "bore huge holes through to Gehenna beneath, new craters of burning, wherefrom in every direction cracks in the earth's rind would radiate"（002-031）. Fire would meet fire in a chain reaction of planetary combustion. In the later chapter, he returns to the image more abstractly: the Fire-End is "destinate, ultimate, Sheol-Gehenna, the great balefire of combustion"（007-024）— fusing the Hebrew terms for the underworld with his own vision of cosmic conflagration.

> colliding with the land, would bore huge holes through to Gehenna beneath, new craters of burning（002-031）

> It is the Fire-End, destinate, ultimate, Sheol-Gehenna, the great balefire of combustion（007-024）

## Significance

Gehenna's role in the book is archetypal rather than geographical. Dennis uses the ancient image of subterranean fire to give mythological resonance to his geological catastrophe. The comet does not merely destroy the surface — it breaches the boundary between the living world and the underworld, releasing the fires of Gehenna into the Earth's crust. The term "Sheol-Gehenna" compounds the Hebrew words for the grave (Sheol) and the place of punishment (Gehenna), suggesting that the comet-impact is simultaneously death and judgment — an apocalypse in the fullest theological sense.
