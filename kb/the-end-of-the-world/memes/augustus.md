Augustus, born Gaius Octavius and later known as Octavian, was the first Roman Emperor, ruling from 27 BCE until his death in 14 CE. In the book, he appears as a figure of calculated political cynicism — a ruler who exploited the deification of his adoptive father Julius Caesar for political advantage, and who faced celestial omens with pragmatic indifference.

## Identity

Augustus (63 BCE – 14 CE) was the grand-nephew and posthumously adopted son of Julius Caesar. After Caesar's assassination in 44 BCE, he emerged victorious from the ensuing civil wars to become Rome's first emperor, inaugurating the Pax Romana. His reign marked the transition from the Roman Republic to the Roman Empire, and he was deified after death as *Divus Augustus*（002-011, 002-016）.

## In the Book

### Appearances

Dennis invokes Augustus primarily in Chapter 2, where he is discussed in the context of cometary omens and the political manipulation of celestial events. The author contrasts the superstitious terrors attributed to comets with the cool-headed exploitation of those terrors by figures like Augustus（002-011, 002-016）.

### Role in Dennis's Argument

Augustus serves Dennis's argument in two ways. First, he exemplifies the cynical ruler who turns supernatural belief to political profit: when a comet appeared in 43 BCE, interpreted as Julius Caesar's soul ascending to heaven, Augustus "was well pleased, for if Julius were a god then his murderers were not homicides but deicides" — a useful doctrine for consolidating power（002-011）. Second, Dennis places Augustus among the historical "cynics" who refused to be intimidated by celestial signs, alongside Seneca, Vespasian, and Queen Elizabeth — figures who looked at terrifying comets and carried on with their business regardless（002-016）.

> The proud visitant of 43 was Caesar's own soul, triumphing through the worlds, ranging through heaven after reigning on earth, come back to tell Rome that with Julius gone she must accomplish her days of tribulation. Artful Augustus was well pleased, for if Julius were a god then his murderers were not homicides but deicides: a notion most helpful to the policy of artful Augustus.（002-011）

## Significance

Augustus represents a pivotal figure in Dennis's taxonomy of human responses to eschatological signs. He is neither a true believer in celestial prophecy nor a fearful observer of cosmic portents; rather, he is the "artful" politician who understands that the masses *do* believe, and who wields that belief as an instrument of power. In this sense, Augustus prefigures the broader historical pattern Dennis traces throughout the book: the tension between sincere apocalyptic expectation and its political exploitation by those who profit from it.
