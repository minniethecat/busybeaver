# Cuzco

The sacred capital of the Inca Empire, high in the Peruvian Andes, whose Coricancha (Temple of the Sun) — sheathed in gold and dedicated to Inti, the sun god — Dennis pairs with Rome as one of the twin poles of ancient solar worship.

## Location

Cuzco (also Cusco) sits at an elevation of 3,400 meters in the Andes of southeastern Peru. It was the historical capital of the Inca Empire from the thirteenth century until the Spanish conquest in 1533. The Coricancha (Quechua for "Golden Courtyard") was the most important temple in the Inca Empire, its walls reportedly covered in gold sheets, its courtyard filled with golden statues. Dennis pairs it with Rome: "from the high Quirinal in Rome, golden Rome — *Urbs solaris imperialis* — to golden Cuzco, gold within, gold without, *Cosa Sagrada*" (006-009).

## In the Book

### Context

Cuzco appears in Chapter 6 ("Cold") as the western-hemisphere endpoint of Dennis's global survey of solar cults. The catalogue moves from Persia to Mexico, from Stonehenge to Xauxa, and culminates in the pairing of Rome and Cuzco — two imperial capitals, unknown to each other, independently covering their temples in gold in honor of the same sun.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis uses Cuzco as evidence for the universality of solar religion — and by extension, the universality of solar anxiety. The Incas who worshipped Inti and the Romans who worshipped Sol Invictus did not know of each other's existence, yet both arrived at the same ritual logic: gild the temple to honor the golden star that sustains all life. Dennis's implication is that this convergence is not accidental. The sun is objectively the source of terrestrial energy; it is natural that every civilization, upon reaching a certain level of wealth and organization, would build monuments to it.

## Significance

Cuzco represents the independent convergence of eschatological imagination across unconnected cultures. The Inca expectation of a periodic renewal of the cosmos (*pachacuti*) is structurally similar to the Stoic *ekpyrosis* and the Hindu *yuga* cycle — yet it developed in complete isolation. For Dennis, this is evidence that eschatology is not culturally transmitted but independently generated by the human confrontation with cosmic time.
