# Alexander

Alexander III of Macedon (356-323 BCE), known as Alexander the Great, appears in Dennis's *The End of the World* through the lens of celestial portent and the broader theme of reading cosmic signs in the careers of conquerors and kings.

## Identity

Alexander was a Macedonian king and military commander who created one of the largest empires of the ancient world, stretching from Greece to northwestern India. Tutored by Aristotle, he embodied the fusion of Greek intellectual culture with Macedonian military power. His early death at age 32 in Babylon — and the rapid disintegration of his empire — made him a figure of enduring fascination for ancient historians and astrologers, who sought celestial explanations for his meteoric rise and sudden fall.

In the book, Alexander is referenced in connection with the tradition of interpreting comets and other celestial phenomena as portents of political upheaval. The ancient world regularly associated extraordinary astronomical events with the births, deaths, and campaigns of great rulers — a tradition that Dennis traces from Alexander through Julius Caesar to Christ.

## In the Book

### Appearances

Alexander is mentioned alongside other historical figures whose lives were enveloped in astrological and apocalyptic interpretations. The corpus places him within the broader classical context that Dennis draws upon to illustrate the ancient habit of celestial interpretation — the conviction that the heavens were a text in which human destiny could be read.

The book's treatment of Alexander is characteristically brief and allusive, assuming the reader's familiarity with the classical tradition. Dennis uses Alexander not as a subject of sustained analysis but as a data point in his larger argument: every age reads its hopes and fears in the sky, and conquerors are among the most reliable attractors of celestial meaning.

### Role in Dennis's Argument

Alexander serves as an example of the pre-Christian, classical background against which Christian apocalypticism developed. The habit of reading comets as portents of political change — the death of kings, the rise and fall of empires — was well established in the Hellenistic world that Alexander created, and Christian eschatology inherited this habit wholesale, redirecting it from imperial politics to cosmic judgment.

## Significance

Alexander's broader significance to the book's eschatological themes lies in his role as a bridge figure: the Greek-educated conqueror who spread Hellenistic culture across the known world, creating the cultural conditions in which Jewish apocalypticism and Greek philosophy would eventually fuse into Christian eschatology. He is a background figure in Dennis's narrative, but one whose historical consequences permeate every chapter.
