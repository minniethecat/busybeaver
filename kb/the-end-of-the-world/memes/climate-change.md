# Climate Change

The large-scale alteration of Earth's climate — a threat that Dennis intuits through his survey of geological history, particularly the evidence that the planet has repeatedly shifted between temperate and frozen states, each shift bringing extinction and transformation in its wake.

## Definition

Climate change, in the contemporary sense of anthropogenic global warming, was not a recognized concept when Dennis wrote in the early twentieth century. But the broader category — the natural variability of Earth's climate and its capacity to render the planet uninhabitable — runs throughout the book's scientific chapters. Dennis surveys the geological evidence for past climate shifts (Chapter 6), the possibility of catastrophic flooding (Chapter 5), and the general fragility of the conditions that make human civilization possible.

The book's treatment of climate is dominated by the threat of cooling rather than warming — the prospect of a new Ice Age, the slow decline of the sun, the inexorable march toward a frozen Earth. This reflects the scientific consensus of Dennis's era, which understood Ice Ages as recent and potentially recurring events rather than as anomalies in a warming trend. The mechanism differs from modern climate discourse, but the underlying anxiety is the same: the planetary systems on which civilization depends are not guaranteed to persist.

## In the Book

### Context

Climate concerns surface most explicitly in Chapter 6 ("Cold"), where Dennis traces the Earth's climatic history from its molten formation through successive Ice Ages to the present interglacial period. The discovery that Greenland was once temperate — that "Peary found Eden" (006-020) in the far north — becomes evidence not of a stable climate but of a radically unstable one. What has changed before can change again.

Chapter 5 ("Water") contributes the complementary threat: catastrophic flooding, whether from melting ice, geological displacement, or divine judgment. Dennis catalogs flood myths from cultures around the world, treating their universality as evidence that environmental catastrophe is among the deepest of human fears.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis treats climate threats with the same measured ambivalence he applies to all end-of-world scenarios. They are real possibilities, grounded in scientific evidence, but their timing is uncertain and human adaptability is a powerful counterforce. The book's unresolved debate between survivalists and pessimists is, in effect, a debate about whether human ingenuity can outpace environmental change — a debate that has only grown more urgent in the century since Dennis wrote.

> Peary found Eden. Greenland was once green; amid her Icy Mountains did not the breadfruit trees once wave? (006-020)
