# Paul the Apostle

The apostle to the Gentiles, whose letters form the earliest stratum of Christian scripture and encode a vivid expectation of the imminent *parousia* (second coming) — an expectation Dennis analyzes as the psychological source of early Christianity's remarkable growth and, simultaneously, the origin of its most persistent eschatological disappointment.

## Identity

Paul of Tarsus (born Saul) was a Pharisaic Jew who, after a dramatic conversion experience on the road to Damascus, became the most energetic missionary of the early Christian movement. His letters — written in the 50s and 60s CE, two decades before the Gospels — are the earliest surviving Christian documents. They express a fervent expectation that Christ would return within the lifetime of Paul's correspondents: "we which are alive and remain shall be caught up together with them in the clouds" (1 Thessalonians 4:17). This imminent expectation, deferred and reinterpreted across centuries, became the engine of Christian apocalyptic thought.

## In the Book

### Appearances

Paul appears in Dennis's treatment of early Christian chronology and the psychology of deferred expectation (010-021–010-023). Dennis is interested in the social function of apocalyptic belief: for a persecuted minority, the promise of imminent vindication — and the threat of imminent punishment for persecutors — was a source of psychological resilience. "Christianity, still a persecuted creed offering no hope of present triumph, needed some such faith in not too distant punishment" (010-022). Paul's letters, with their urgent expectation of the end, exemplify this dynamic.

### Role in Dennis's Argument

Paul represents the origin point of a tradition that Dennis traces through two millennia: the prediction of an imminent end, followed by its non-occurrence, followed by reinterpretation and re-dating, followed by new predictions. The Pauline expectation that "this generation" would witness the *parousia* (010-021) is the template for every subsequent doomsday prophecy — and the fact that the expectation survived its falsification is, for Dennis, the most telling evidence of the depth of the human investment in eschatology.

## Significance

Paul's importance to Dennis's argument is structural rather than theological. He is the first in a long chain of Christians who believed the end was near — and who, when it did not arrive, found ways to sustain the belief rather than abandon it. This pattern — prediction, disappointment, reinterpretation — is the backbone of Dennis's sociology of eschatology.
