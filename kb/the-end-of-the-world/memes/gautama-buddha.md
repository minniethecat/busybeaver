# Gautama Buddha

The founder of Buddhism, a figure Dennis invokes to illustrate that the impulse to periodize cosmic time and interpret catastrophe in moral terms is culturally universal — spanning the Indic as well as the Abrahamic traditions.

## Identity

Siddhartha Gautama, known as the Buddha ("the Awakened One"), lived and taught in northeastern India in the sixth to fifth centuries BCE. His teachings, preserved in the Pali Canon and later Mahayana sutras, describe a cosmos of vast kalpas (eons) punctuated by periodic destruction and renewal, with human moral conduct determining the conditions of rebirth within this cosmic cycle. Dennis's interest in the Buddha is comparativist: he uses Buddhist eschatology to demonstrate that the patterns visible in Jewish and Christian apocalyptic are not culturally specific but reflect universal human concerns.

## In the Book

### Appearances

The Buddha appears three times in Dennis's text. In Chapter 11 ("Next Year"), he is cited in the context of numerological eschatology: "eightfold Path of Buddha; eight-hours day also and Eight-Hour Bill" (011-021) — an associative leap characteristic of Dennis's style, linking sacred numerology to secular politics. In Chapter 13 ("Never"), Dennis notes that "Buddha divided the time of the world" (013-004) into vast cosmic periods, a schema that dwarfs even the astronomical timescales of modern science. And in Chapter 15 ("Man"), Dennis asks: "If Buddha walked again on the Kapilavastu road..." (015-017), using the Buddha as a moral touchstone for evaluating whether humanity merits survival.

### Role in Dennis's Argument

The Buddha functions in Dennis's argument as a witness from outside the Western tradition. His presence in the text serves to universalize the book's concerns: eschatology is not a peculiarity of the Abrahamic religions but a near-universal feature of human thought. Dennis is careful not to treat Buddhist eschatology as exotic or inferior; he presents it as a parallel system, equally sophisticated, addressing the same questions through different conceptual resources.

> Buddha divided the time of the world. (013-004)

The division of cosmic time into kalpas — periods so vast that they are measured by metaphors (a bird wearing down a mountain with a silk scarf once every hundred years) — anticipates and perhaps exceeds the "quintillions" of years that Dennis deploys in his scientific register. The Buddha, in this sense, is not a rival to modern cosmology but a predecessor who arrived at comparable scales of time through meditative rather than mathematical methods.

## Significance

The Buddha's presence in Dennis's argument is modest but important. He demonstrates the breadth of Dennis's eschatological survey and reinforces the book's implicit claim: that thinking about the end of the world is not the province of any single culture or tradition but a universal human activity, found wherever people have looked at the stars and wondered how long they will shine.
