# Collision

The direct physical encounter between celestial bodies. Dennis treats collision as the ur-mechanism of cosmic destruction: the simplest, most inevitable, and most comprehensively fatal of all end-of-world scenarios.

## The Collisional Origin and End

The book frames collision as both genesis and terminus. The solar system itself was born of collision — "our parent star, most like, was formed by collision" [001-008] — which makes the collisional end not an aberration but a return to origins. What assembled the planets can disassemble them:

> "The collisional. Our parent star, most like, was formed by the collision of two suns." [001-008]

This symmetry — creation through collision, destruction through collision — runs through the entire cosmology of the book. The forces that gave us existence are the same forces that will take it away.

## Varieties of Impact

The corpus catalogs a spectrum of collisional threats, distinguished by scale:

- **Cometary collision**: A comet striking Earth — "the worst — direct collision — would be survivable" [002-036] only if the comet is small. Larger bodies pose an existential threat.
- **Asteroid collision**: The "boulders and sand as wide as Russia, collision with which would be the catastrophe" [002-042] — debris fields large enough to sterilize continents.
- **Stellar collision**: Stars colliding with other stars — "Which end is collision, impact, dissolving crash" [007-002] — an event that would vaporize planets as a side effect.
- **Galactic collision**: The ultimate scale — "terminal collision of astral annihilation" [007-008] — in which entire galaxies merge and their constituent worlds are destroyed.

> "Observed collisions are not rare; collisions unobserved may be the norm." [007-009]

## The Honest End

The book expresses a peculiar admiration for collision as a mode of ending. Unlike supernatural judgment or gradual decay, collision is direct, honest, and physically transparent: "not removed; but by honest collision, not by magic" [002-019]. It requires no faith, no interpretation, no theology — only physics.

The "arithmetical likelihood of a collision, or of even a nearish encounter" [002-034] is treated as a statistical certainty over cosmic timescales. The question is not whether collision will occur, but when.

## Collision and Cosmic Time

Dennis situates collision risk within the vastness of cosmic time, where statistical improbability at the human scale becomes inevitability at the astronomical scale. The book treats the sheer duration of the universe — billions of years past, billions yet to come — as the guarantor that every possible impact will eventually occur. This temporal framing transforms collision from a remote possibility into the default ending: given enough time, something will hit something else hard enough to end everything.

The "accident" that formed the solar system (001-018) was not a one-time fluke but a recurring pattern. Each collision that built the planets foreshadows the collision that will destroy them. The temporal scale collapses the distinction between the improbable and the certain: "collisions unobserved may be the norm" (007-009) suggests that the visible universe captures only a thin slice of the total collisional history, and that what we see as rare is, in cosmic terms, routine.
