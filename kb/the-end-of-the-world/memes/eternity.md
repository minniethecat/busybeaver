# Eternity

In Dennis's usage, eternity is the vast and incomprehensible span of time against which all earthly things — including the Earth itself — are measured and found fleeting.

## Definition

Eternity, in its philosophical and theological sense, denotes time without beginning or end — infinite duration. In the context of Dennis's eschatological framework, eternity serves as the ultimate backdrop against which the finitude of all created things becomes starkly visible. The Earth is not eternal; time is "not for her, and eternity against her" (001-018). Dennis deploys the concept to underscore the brevity of human existence, the mortality of planets, and the humbling scale of cosmic time.

Within the Western theological tradition, eternity is an attribute of God alone — the created order has a beginning and, in apocalyptic frameworks, an end. Dennis draws on this tradition while extending it into a naturalistic register: the physical universe itself operates on timescales so vast that they approach, "though short of eternity," a magnitude that renders human comprehension almost impossible (013-002).

## In the Book

### Context

The concept of eternity appears across multiple chapters of Dennis's work, serving different rhetorical functions. In the opening chapter on geological time (001-018), eternity is introduced as the inhuman measure against which Earth's lifespan is judged — a prelude to the book's central argument that the world will end. In the chapter on fire (003-043), eternity appears in connection with cosmic destruction: "mythical worlds, having lived an eternity of times, with an eternity of destruction, annihilation secures their common eternity as heat" — a passage that equates final conflagration with a return to an eternal, undifferentiated state.

The astronomical chapters invoke eternity to describe the scale of celestial processes. Ancient civilizations — "the Chaldeans, the Pawnees, the Shawaks, Aztecs" — "have implied the eternity of the series," recognizing that cosmic cycles operate on timescales dwarfing human history (013-007). The Mayas of Yucatan similarly encoded eternal recurrence in their calendrical systems.

In the theological chapters, eternity takes on an existential register. Dennis asks, "Where is a Jesus for eternity?" (015-022), linking the concept of eternal duration to the human need for meaning and salvation that extends beyond death. The question is rhetorical: if eternity is real, what provision exists for the individual soul within it?

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis treats eternity not as a speculative abstraction but as a concrete reality that bears directly on human existence. His approach alternates between the scientific — depicting eternity as the vast timescale of astrophysical processes — and the existential — confronting the reader with the terror of infinite time as experienced by a finite consciousness.

> Millions of meteors fall annually upon the Earth and are consumed by its atmosphere. Their annihilation secures their common eternity as heat. (006-016)

This passage exemplifies Dennis's method: a physical fact (meteor combustion) is reframed as a metaphysical transformation (annihilation into eternal heat). Matter is not destroyed but converted into an enduring state — a scientific principle pressed into the service of cosmic poetry.

At the close of the book, eternity returns as a cry of the heart: "I scream in my heart for eternity. Sometimes no sound escapes me, but I cry and cry in my heart for eternity" (017-050). Here eternity is no longer a cosmological concept but an object of desperate longing — the human refusal to accept finitude.
