# Jerusalem

The city sacred to Judaism, Christianity, and Islam — and in Dennis's survey of apocalyptic expectation, the geographical anchor of end-times prophecy: the place where the world's end was expected to begin, where prophets gathered to await it, and whose historical destruction provided the template for every subsequent vision of [[divine-judgment|divine judgment]] upon a sinful world.

## Location

Jerusalem sits on a plateau in the Judaean Mountains between the Mediterranean and the Dead Sea. For Jews, it is the site of the Temple — the dwelling place of God on earth — whose destruction by the [[babylon|Babylonians]] in 586 BCE and again by the Romans in 70 CE shaped the prophetic imagination of exile and return. For Christians, it is the city of Christ's crucifixion and resurrection, and the place to which he was expected to return. For Muslims, it is the site of the Prophet's night journey to heaven.

Dennis's Jerusalem, however, is not a geographical place but an *apocalyptic stage*. It is the city where cosmic drama becomes local event — where heaven sends signs and history delivers verdicts.

## In the Book

### Context

Jerusalem appears at three critical junctures in Dennis's argument. First, in the historical survey of heavenly omens: "A titan sword pointed down towards Jerusalem, and soon by Titus' sword the Holy City was laid waste forever" (002-012). The comet that presaged Jerusalem's fall is, for Dennis, the archetype of all heavenly signs — every subsequent comet that "foretells" disaster is repeating this original pattern of celestial warning and terrestrial catastrophe.

Second, in Dennis's account of the nineteenth-century millenarian revival, Jerusalem functions as a geographical magnet for apocalyptic expectation. "Joseph Wolff, of Jerusalem, was waiting for the end in Jerusalem" (010-060) — the city's name is repeated deliberately, as if the location itself confers prophetic authority. Wolff joins William Miller and Captain Saunders in a transatlantic network of end-watchers, each staking a claim on a specific date and place for the world's termination.

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis's most powerful treatment of Jerusalem comes in his description of the Roman siege — a passage of almost unbearable intensity:

> through the wide oecumene the legions were slaughtering: Boadicea and her Britons in the island West, Teutons in the boundaried north, Parthians in Parthia, the Chosen People in their chosen land, till, after the cruellest siege we have word of, when without the walls was death and within them was death, when men tortured each other for a morsel of bread or of offal and Mary of Bethezub ate the child at her breast, the city that had destroyed the Redeemer herself was destroyed, the Temple of Solomon delivered at last to the flames and Mount Zion to the sword and the vulture. (011-010)

This is Dennis at his most rhetorically charged. The destruction of Jerusalem is not merely a historical event but a *cosmic pattern*: the city that destroyed the Redeemer is herself destroyed, judgment answering judgment in an inexorable symmetry. The fall of Jerusalem becomes, in Dennis's reading, the template for the fall of the world — the local catastrophe that the apocalyptic imagination extrapolates to universal scale.

### Role in Dennis's Argument

Jerusalem serves Dennis as the spatial counterpart to [[noah|Noah's]] psychological function. If Noah is the *person* through whom we imagine surviving the end, Jerusalem is the *place* where we imagine it happening. All apocalyptic thinking requires a stage, and Jerusalem — with its millennia of prophecy, destruction, and expectation — is the archetypal stage. Dennis's point is that the localization of the universal is a characteristic move of the eschatological imagination: the end of *everything* must begin *somewhere*.

## Significance

Jerusalem represents, for Dennis, the spatial dimension of eschatology: the belief that cosmic events have a geographical address. The "titan sword" that points toward Jerusalem (002-012) is a sign in heaven, but its target is a specific point on earth. This conjunction of the cosmic and the local — the comet and the city — is, for Dennis, the essence of apocalyptic thinking. The world ends not in abstract theory but in a particular place, on a particular date, witnessed by particular people.
