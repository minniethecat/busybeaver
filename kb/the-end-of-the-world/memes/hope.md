# Hope

The anticipation of a desired future, examined in the book as one of the fundamental human responses to mortality and cosmic threat — at times a source of resilience, at times a self-deception that obscures the statistical inevitability of catastrophe.

## Definition

Hope is the expectation or desire for a particular thing to happen. In theological usage, it is one of the three theological virtues (alongside faith and charity). Dennis treats hope as a psychological phenomenon with two distinct manifestations: (a) secular hope — the human capacity to face probable annihilation with "a smile, and let us hope an oath also" [002-016], a kind of existential courage in the face of cosmic indifference; and (b) theological hope — the eschatological expectation of divine intervention, millennial renewal, or personal survival of death, which Dennis subjects to sustained critique [001-014] [010-040] [010-058].

## In the Book

### Context

Hope appears most frequently in the theological chapters, particularly in the discussion of early Christian eschatology and the "millennial hope" [010-040] [010-058]. The book traces how the early Church's expectation of an imminent parousia shaped its worldview, and how the disappointment of that hope — "his imminent hope fast dwindling through his Epistles" [010-005] — forced theological adaptation. Beyond the theological register, hope also appears as a natural human response to the bleakness of the cosmic picture: the reader is invited to contemplate the end "now with hope, now with bleak terror" [001-019].

### Dennis's Treatment

Dennis maintains an ironic distance from hope throughout the work. He acknowledges its psychological necessity — few can face extinction without some consolatory expectation — but repeatedly demonstrates its empirical fragility. The hopes placed in comets (that they might miss, or that their effects might be survivable) are presented as statistically unwarranted [002-015]. The "hopes of defeating death" are described as "born of the same fear" [001-014] that drives religious belief. And the millennial hope is presented as a historical phenomenon that "lay down beside the millennial hope, buried together" [010-040] — a hope that dies with the events that were supposed to fulfill it.

The book's most direct statement on hope is also its most dismissive: "Leave all such hopes and counter-hopes" [006-018], an exhortation to abandon wishful thinking and confront the cosmic facts as they are. Yet the very act of writing a book about the end of the world implies a residual hope — that readers might, through understanding, achieve some measure of equanimity.

> "Men are prepared with man's same hopes of defeating death: hopes born of the same fear that drives them to build, to pray, to write books about the end of all things." [001-014]

> "Leave all such hopes and counter-hopes. Leave aside the consolations that have been bequeathed to us by those who could not bear to look unblinking at the darkness." [006-018]
