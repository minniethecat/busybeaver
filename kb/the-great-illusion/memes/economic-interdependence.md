# Economic Interdependence

The cornerstone concept of Norman Angell's argument in *The Great Illusion*: the proposition that the economies of civilized nations have become so deeply interwoven through credit, commerce, and the division of labor that no nation can benefit from the military conquest of another — the wealth of a modern society is intangible and cannot be seized by force.

## Definition

Angell defines economic interdependence as the condition in which "wealth in the economically civilized world is founded upon credit and commercial contract (these being the outgrowth of an economic interdependence due to the increasing division of labor and greatly developed communication)" (P05-004). The key insight is that modern wealth is not physical — it consists of credit relationships, commercial contracts, and the complex network of obligations that bind financial centers together. Unlike land or gold, this form of wealth cannot be captured by an invading army.

International finance, Angell argues, "has become so interdependent and so interwoven with trade and industry that the intangibility of an enemy's property extends to his trade" (P05-005). The result is that "political and military power can in reality do nothing for trade" (P05-005).

## Position in the Book's Argument

Economic interdependence is the foundational concept of **Part 1** (The Economics of the Case, Chapters 1–8). Angell builds his entire economic argument upon it:

1. **Chapters 1–3** establish the traditional assumption — that national prosperity depends on military power and territorial expansion — and then demolish it
2. **Chapters 4–5** develop the positive case: the "delicate interdependence of international finance" (005-002) makes conquest self-defeating
3. **Chapters 6–8** extend the argument to trade, colonies, and the practical impossibility of confiscating enemy property

The concept reappears in **Part 2** (The Human Nature of the Case) as the material basis for rejecting the psychological and biological arguments for war, and in **Part 3** (The Practical Outcome) as the foundation for Angell's proposed alternative to military rivalry.

## Core Argument

### The Positive Case

Angell presents interdependence as an observable fact of the modern world, not a normative ideal. "The vital interdependence here indicated, cutting athwart frontiers, is largely the work of the last forty years" (005-012). He emphasizes its scale and complexity:

> The complexity of modern finance makes New York dependent on London, London upon Paris, Paris upon Berlin, to a degree undreamt of even fifty years ago (005-012).

The mechanism is the **division of labor**, intensified by modern communications and credit systems. "When the division of labor was so little developed that every homestead produced all that it needed, it mattered nothing if part of the community was cut off from the world" (005-011). But in the modern economy, disruption to any significant financial center propagates instantly:

> A financial crisis in New York sends up the English bank rate to 7 percent, thus involving the ruin of many English businesses which might otherwise have weathered a difficult period (005-016).

### The Negative Implication

If economies are interdependent, then military conquest is economically futile. A conqueror cannot "transfer" the wealth of a conquered nation because that wealth consists of credit relationships and commercial contracts — intangible assets that are destroyed by the very act of conquest. "If credit and commercial contract are tampered with in an attempt at confiscation, the credit-dependent wealth of the conqueror's own subjects would collapse" (P05-004).

Angell goes further: the conqueror would not only fail to gain — it would actively harm itself. The German invader who looted the Bank of England would find that "German finance would represent a condition altogether worse than that of the conquered enemy" (005-015).

### The Historical Moment

Angell stresses that this interdependence is a *new* historical condition. It "is largely the work of the last forty years" (005-012) — roughly since the 1870s, with the expansion of international telegraph networks, the gold standard, and the first wave of financial globalization. This temporal claim is essential to his argument: earlier empires (Roman, Napoleonic) operated under different economic conditions where conquest *could* be profitable. The "Great Illusion" is the failure to recognize that those conditions no longer obtain.

## Error: Confusing the Argument

A common misreading conflates economic interdependence with a guarantee of peace. Angell does not claim interdependence *prevents* war — he claims it makes war *futile as a means of enrichment*. The distinction is critical. Nations may still go to war out of mistaken beliefs, nationalist passion, or miscalculation. Angell's project is to dispel the *economic rationale* for war, not to predict its elimination.
