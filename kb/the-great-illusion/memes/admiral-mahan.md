# Admiral Mahan

**Alfred Thayer Mahan** (1840–1914) was an American naval officer and strategic theorist whose work on sea power profoundly influenced naval doctrine worldwide. In *The Great Illusion*, Norman Angell engages Mahan as the **principal Anglo-Saxon exponent of the armament philosophy** — a respected authority whose arguments for naval supremacy are subjected to systematic critique. Mahan's prominence as a counterexample is strategic: if Angell can refute the most sophisticated defender of militarism among the English-speaking nations, the lesser apologists fall by implication.

## Life and Background

Mahan served as a captain in the United States Navy and later as president of the Naval War College. His 1890 work *The Influence of Sea Power upon History, 1660–1783* became a foundational text of naval strategy, arguing that national greatness depended on maritime dominance. His ideas were particularly influential on [[Theodore Roosevelt]], the German Kaiser [[Wilhelm II]], and the architects of [[British Empire|British]] and [[Germany|German]] naval expansion.

## Role in the Book

Mahan appears in *The Great Illusion* as **both target and representative**. Angell treats him not as an outlier but as the most articulate voice of a widely held doctrine — that armaments are necessary for survival and that the "commercial ideal" is a form of national decadence.

### The 1912 Critique

Angell quotes Mahan's 1912 criticism of *The Great Illusion* at length:

> Thus, writing in 1912, Admiral Mahan criticizes this book as follows: that it assumes a degree of rationality in international affairs which does not exist, and that the economic interdependence of nations — even if real — would not prevent war because nations do not act on economic motives alone. [012-007]

Angell's response is notable for its precision. He does not dismiss Mahan; he points out an inconsistency:

> Hardly four years previously Admiral Mahan had himself outlined the elements of an international order based on precisely the economic rationality that he now dismisses as utopian. [012-008]

### The Money Question

Angell's sharpest rejoinder to Mahan concerns the relationship between economic interests and military power:

> Admiral Mahan, it is true, anticipates this criticism by arguing that right and wrong are not settled by economic problems, with money. Does Admiral Mahan mean us to take him at his word — that the United States Navy exists for purposes having nothing to do with the protection of American trade? [012-012]

This passage reveals Angell's argumentative method: rather than refuting Mahan on strategic grounds, he exposes the **inconsistency** between Mahan's general philosophy (war is about honor, not money) and the actual policy implications of his naval doctrine (protect trade routes and markets).

## Core Views

Mahan's position, as characterized by Angell, contains four interlocking claims:

| Claim | Angell's Response |
|-------|-------------------|
| Armaments are a necessity of national life, not a policy choice | If all nations accept this, the arms race has no logical endpoint [004-019] |
| The commercial ideal is decadent; nations need martial virtues | "Almost every defence of militarism includes a sneer at the 'commercial ideal'" [014-050] |
| Economic interdependence does not prevent war | The evidence of the credit system suggests it imposes prohibitive costs [012-010] |
| Right and wrong are not questions of money | But the policy Mahan advocates is entirely directed at protecting material interests [012-012] |

Angell also cites Mahan's article "The Place of Power in International Relations" [023-046], which argued that international order ultimately rests on force — a position Angell treats as internally coherent but empirically false under modern conditions.

## Influence and Legacy

Mahan's ideas were enormously influential in their time, shaping the naval policies of the United States, Britain, Germany, and Japan. The [[Anglo-German Naval Race]] — which Angell analyzes as a case study in self-defeating competition — was driven in part by Mahanite assumptions about the relationship between naval power and national prosperity.

Angell's engagement with Mahan is significant beyond the particular points of dispute. By choosing Mahan — rather than a jingoistic journalist — as his principal Anglo-Saxon antagonist, Angell implicitly concedes that the pro-armament case is not merely chauvinistic propaganda but has **intellectual substance**. The book's credibility depends on being able to answer Mahan, not merely dismiss him.

> I have quoted Admiral Mahan as follows: That extension of political control through military power is a natural and inevitable expression of national vitality. If this is true, then the armament competition has no natural terminus — and my argument fails. But is it true? [016-034]

The question is rhetorical, but the framing is genuine: Angell takes Mahan's challenge seriously.
