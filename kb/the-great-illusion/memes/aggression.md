# Aggression

**Aggression** — the threat of external military attack — is the spectre that Norman Angell identifies as the primary psychological driver of the armament spiral. In *The Great Illusion*, the concept of aggression is systematically dismantled: Angell argues that under modern economic conditions, the conquest of one industrialized State by another confers no economic benefit, making deliberate aggression an irrational policy. The concept thus stands as the mirror image of [[Defence|defence]] in Angell's argument, the feared threat whose reality he denies.

## The Logic of Aggression in Angell

Angell's treatment of aggression begins with a concession: if one nation genuinely could profit from conquering another, then other nations would be rational to arm for self-protection. The entire edifice of the armament system rests on this premise:

> To attenuate the motive for aggression is to undertake a work of defence. If you can show that conquest does not pay, you have destroyed the motive for aggression and therefore the need for armament. [018-002]

The statement is programmatic — Angell's economic chapters (Part 1) are designed to demonstrate precisely this: that conquest does not pay. The concept of aggression is thus the fulcrum on which the book's entire argument turns.

## Commercial vs. Military Aggression

Angell distinguishes between two forms of aggression that are commonly conflated:

| Form | Mechanism | Angell's Verdict |
|------|-----------|------------------|
| Military aggression | Armed invasion and annexation of territory | Irrational under modern credit economy — the conqueror cannot confiscate wealth without destroying it [016-011] |
| Commercial aggression | Trade competition and market rivalry | Falsely analogized to war — Swiss "commercial aggression" against Britain cannot be met with battleships [006-015] |

The second form receives particular attention. Angell satirizes the notion of "commercial aggression" by pointing to Switzerland:

> Britain need not defend herself against the commercial "aggression" of Switzerland by building a battleship. Switzerland, with no navy and negligible army, "aggresses" upon British markets daily — and profits from it, as does Britain. [006-015]

This distinction is central to his thesis: the forms of competition that actually occur between modern States are **non-military**, and treating them as military problems produces the wrong response.

## The German Question

The historical context of the book places Germany in the role of the feared aggressor. Angell addresses this directly:

> If we believe there is, from German aggression, we must arm. I do not preach disarmament in the face of a threat. What I question is whether the threat is real, and whether arming against it makes us safer. [018-020]

He analyzes the German case in detail, noting that German commercial expansion was proceeding rapidly without military conquest [018-014] — suggesting that Germany had no rational motive for the aggression attributed to it. This argument, published in 1910, would later be cited by critics as naive, though Angell's 1933 edition argued that the First World War had confirmed rather than refuted his economic thesis.

## The Psychological Mechanism

Beyond the economic argument, Angell explores why the fear of aggression persists despite its irrationality:

> Much of the present motive to aggression will cease to be operative, and with the disappearance of prospective aggression disappears also the need for defence. [P05-010]

This passage, from the Synopsis, encapsulates the **sequential logic**: first demonstrate that aggression is irrational, then the fear of aggression dissolves, then the armament system collapses. The psychological dimension is explored through the role of [[National Prestige|prestige]] and the [[Human Nature in Angell|human tendency]] to project hostile intent onto rivals.

## Aggression and the State

Angell introduces a crucial distinction absent from popular discussion of his era: aggression between States does not correspond to aggression between individuals:

> Why aggression upon a State does not correspond to aggression upon an individual is the central question. The State is not a person; its wealth is not transferable by force in the way that a purse is transferable by a thief. [016-002]

This distinction underlies the entire economic argument. The physical force that restrains aggression upon persons [016-008] cannot cover a case of aggression upon "the moral and economic interests of a State" [016-012] — those interests are protected by the credit system, contract law, and economic interdependence, not by battleships.
