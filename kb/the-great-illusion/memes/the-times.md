# The Times

**The Times** of London was Britain's most authoritative establishment newspaper in the Edwardian era, widely regarded as the voice of the governing class. In *The Great Illusion*, Angell cites *The Times* alongside the [[Daily Mail]] and [[Morning Post]] as part of a **cross-spectrum press consensus** that, regardless of party affiliation or social register, converged on the same militarist assumptions about naval power, imperial competition, and the German threat.

## Role in the Book

Angell's treatment of *The Times* is less extensive than his engagement with the *Daily Mail*, but it serves a specific rhetorical function: by including *The Times* — the paper of the establishment, not the populist tabloid — Angell demonstrates that the Great Illusion is not merely a sensationalist excess of the popular press but a **governing-class assumption** embedded in the most respected organs of opinion.

> "The Times has been hardly less insistent than the Daily Mail in its warnings of German encirclement and the necessity of maintaining British naval supremacy." (018-014)

This passage groups *The Times* with the *Daily Mail*, but the qualifier "hardly less insistent" draws a subtle distinction: *The Times* makes the same arguments in a more restrained register, which makes them, if anything, **more influential** — because they reach a readership that would dismiss the *Daily Mail* as sensationalism.

## The Establishment Voice

The *Times* mattered in Angell's analysis because it spoke to and for the political class. Its editorials were read in Whitehall and Westminster; its foreign correspondents shaped parliamentary understanding of international affairs. When *The Times* endorsed naval expansion or warned of the German threat, it was not merely reporting opinion but **forming** it — and forming it within the very institutions that Angell hoped to persuade.

Angell's frustration with *The Times* is not that it is dishonest but that it is **complacent**: it repeats the assumptions of the armament system without subjecting them to the economic analysis that would reveal their incoherence.
