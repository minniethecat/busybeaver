# The Saturday Review

British weekly periodical founded in 1855, known in the Edwardian period for its conservative editorial stance and vigorous advocacy of British military and naval expansion. In *The Great Illusion*, Angell lists the *Saturday Review* among the publications whose editorial line "facilitates the profession" of militarism — a brief but pointed characterization that places it within the jingoistic press culture his book critiques.

## 基本信息

The *Saturday Review* was a London-based weekly covering literature, politics, and current affairs. By the early twentieth century, under the editorship of figures including G.S. Street (1898–1914), it had acquired a reputation for trenchant conservative commentary and was counted among the influential organs of "Die-Hard" imperialist opinion. Its readership overlapped substantially with that of the *Spectator* and the *National Review*.

## 在本书中的引用

Angell's single reference to the *Saturday Review* appears in an early chapter, where he groups it with other British periodicals that promote alarmist views of German intentions:

> ...the *Observer*, the *Outlook*, the *Saturday Review*, etc., facilitate the profession...[003-031]

The context is Angell's opening diagnosis of the intellectual climate in which the naval race unfolded. By listing the *Saturday Review* alongside other periodicals, he identifies it as part of a media ecosystem that, in his view, manufactured the perception of threat that justified ever-increasing military expenditure. The deliberately dismissive "etc." suggests that Angell regarded these publications as interchangeable voices of a single cultural phenomenon.

## 关键行动 / 立场

The *Saturday Review* was among the periodicals that treated German naval expansion as prima facie evidence of hostile intent. Its editorial position reflected the broader Unionist and imperialist conviction that British security required unquestioned maritime supremacy, and that any challenge to that supremacy must be met with force.

### 与本书论点一致的方面

There is no evidence in the text that the *Saturday Review* endorsed any aspect of Angell's argument. Its inclusion is purely as an exhibit in his case against the jingoistic press.

### 被本书批评的方面

Angell's criticism is implicit but clear: by "facilitating the profession" of militarism, the *Saturday Review* and similar publications helped sustain a political climate in which the economic irrationality of the arms race went unexamined. They are, in his framework, part of the machinery of illusion his book seeks to dismantle.
