# Struggle for Existence

The Social Darwinist concept that Norman Angell devotes Part 2 of *The Great Illusion* to refuting: the claim that war between nations is a biologically necessary mechanism of evolutionary progress, eliminating the "unfit" and strengthening the species. Angell regards this as perhaps the most dangerous form of the Great Illusion because it dresses a political choice in the language of scientific inevitability.

## Definition

The "struggle for existence" in Angell's usage refers to the application of Darwinian concepts — natural selection, survival of the fittest, competition as the engine of progress — to relations between nations. Its proponents argued that war serves an evolutionary function: it eliminates weak nations, strengthens strong ones, and drives civilizational progress through competitive pressure.

Angell's response is characteristically two-pronged. First, he challenges the biological analogy on its own terms: the evolutionary struggle that produces progress is not between organisms of the same species destroying one another, but between organisms and their environment. Second, he argues that even if the analogy were valid, modern war selects for the wrong traits — not intelligence or cooperation but the capacity for organized destruction.

## Position in the Book's Argument

The "struggle for existence" is the primary target of Part 2's first several chapters. Angell must neutralize the biological argument for war because it is the most powerful intellectual obstacle to his case. If war is biologically necessary, then economic arguments about its futility are irrelevant — nature does not care about efficiency.

## Core Argument

### The Biological Critique

Angell argues that the Social Darwinists misunderstand Darwin. Natural selection operates primarily through adaptation to environment, not through direct combat between members of the same species. Cooperation — not combat — is the distinctive human evolutionary strategy. The nations that have prospered most are those that have developed complex systems of cooperation, not those that have been most militarily successful.

### The Selection Problem

Even granting the analogy, Angell argues that modern war selects *against* the traits that make nations successful. War kills the young, the physically fit, the courageous — precisely the population that a nation most needs for peaceful prosperity. The "fittest" in military terms are not the fittest in economic or cultural terms.

### The Historical Evidence

Angell points to historical counterexamples: nations that have lost wars have often prospered afterward (France after 1870, Germany's economic rise despite military defeat). Meanwhile, victorious nations have often stagnated. If war were an evolutionary selection mechanism for national fitness, the data would look very different.

## Error: The Pacifist Misreading

Angell's critique of Social Darwinism is sometimes read as a pacifist argument that all competition is bad. This is incorrect. Angell is not opposed to competition — he is opposed to the claim that military competition is the *necessary* or *beneficial* form that competition must take. Economic competition, intellectual competition, cultural competition — these are the genuinely progressive forms of human rivalry, and they do not require organized killing.
