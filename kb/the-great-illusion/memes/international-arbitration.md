# International Arbitration

The institutional alternative to war that Norman Angell advocates in Part 3 of *The Great Illusion*. International arbitration represents the practical translation of Angell's economic and psychological analysis into a concrete proposal for reforming international relations.

## Definition

Angell understands international arbitration as a system in which disputes between nations are settled by impartial adjudication according to agreed-upon legal principles, rather than by the exercise of military force. It is the institutional expression of the insight that national interests are better served by cooperation than by conflict.

Arbitration is not, for Angell, a utopian fantasy — it is a practical extension of the same logic that governs domestic law: "In theory international arbitration denies the inexorability of natural forces" (011-015). Just as individuals in civil society do not resolve disputes through private violence, nations in an interdependent world can — and ultimately must — resolve theirs through law.

## Position in the Book's Argument

Arbitration belongs to **Part 3** (The Practical Outcome, Chapters 15–18). Angell's structure is methodical:

1. **Part 1** establishes the *fact* of economic interdependence
2. **Part 2** addresses the *psychological* obstacles to recognizing that fact
3. **Part 3** proposes the *institutional* reforms that follow from the analysis

Arbitration is the primary institutional mechanism Angell advocates, though he is realistic about its limitations: nations will not submit "all questions which could affect their existence as independent Powers" (013-016) to arbitration, and the system must be designed with this constraint in mind.

## Core Argument

### The Domestic Analogy

Angell's argument for arbitration rests on a powerful analogy: within nations, individuals once settled disputes by force, but gradually developed legal institutions that made private violence obsolete. The same evolution, he argues, is possible — and indeed necessary — at the international level.

The logic of the domestic analogy is strengthened by Angell's economic analysis. If nations are economically interdependent, they are already in a condition analogous to members of a civil society. The missing element is not the *interest* in peaceful settlement but the *institutions* to achieve it.

### The Limitation Problem

Angell is candid about the limitations of arbitration. He acknowledges that states will not entrust their fundamental security to an external tribunal: it is "impossible ... to allow arbitration to cover all causes of difference" (013-016). But he argues that this does not invalidate the principle — it merely defines the scope within which arbitration can operate.

The practical question is not whether arbitration can eliminate all war, but whether it can reduce the *frequency* of war by removing economic disputes — the very disputes that Angell has shown to be based on false premises — from the domain of military resolution.

### From Analysis to Institution

Angell's discussion of arbitration reveals the ultimate purpose of his entire project. The economic analysis of Parts 1–2 is not mere intellectual exercise — it is groundwork for institutional reform. If statesmen understood that military power cannot achieve economic ends, they would have no rational objection to submitting economic disputes to arbitration. The "Great Illusion" is not merely a theoretical error — it is a practical obstacle to institutional progress.

## Error: The Sovereignty Objection

The most common objection to international arbitration is that it infringes on national sovereignty. Angell anticipates this: nations value their independence and will not subordinate fundamental decisions to outside authority. His response is that sovereignty is already constrained by economic interdependence — the question is whether constraints will be managed through law or through periodic catastrophic war. The sovereignty objection, in his framework, is another form of the Great Illusion: the belief that formal independence can be preserved through military means when material interdependence has already made it obsolete.
