# War Indemnity

The financial mechanism through which victorious nations have historically extracted wealth from the defeated — and which Norman Angell argues has become economically impossible in the modern credit economy. The war indemnity is the paradigmatic case of the "Great Illusion" at the level of specific policy.

## Definition

A war indemnity is a payment extracted by a victor from a defeated nation, typically as compensation for the costs of war or as punitive tribute. Historically, indemnities were a primary motive for conquest: the victor expected to be repaid for its military expenditure, with interest.

Angell's argument is that in a credit-based economy, large indemnities are self-defeating. A defeated nation can only pay an indemnity out of its productive economy — its exports. But those exports must be *bought* by someone, and the victor's own economy is the most likely buyer. The indemnity thus functions as a loan, not a transfer: the victor "receives" goods that it pays for through the normal operation of trade.

## Core Argument

Angell uses the historical example of the French indemnity after the Franco-Prussian War: "the French paid off the indemnity with such astonishing rapidity" (006-029) because the French economy continued to export. Germany "received" the indemnity but simultaneously paid for French goods. The net transfer was far smaller than the nominal sum suggested.
