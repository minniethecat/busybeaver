# General von Bernhardi

German cavalry general and military writer (1849–1930), author of *Germany and the Next War* (1912), whom Norman Angell treats as the most explicit and intellectually serious spokesman for the militarist worldview his book aims to refute.

## Life and Background

Friedrich von Bernhardi served as a cavalry officer in the Prussian Army and became one of the most widely-read German military writers of the pre-World War I period. His book argued that Germany must wage a preventive war against Britain and France to secure its "place in the sun."

## Role in the Book

Bernhardi appears in *The Great Illusion* as the paradigmatic **critic** — the militarist whose views are stated so clearly that they require no caricature. Angell quotes him extensively: "from General Bernhardi, a distinguished cavalry General" (011-009) whose pronouncements represent the highest intellectual expression of militarism.

Bernhardi serves a crucial rhetorical function: if Angell can refute the most articulate militarist, he can refute all lesser versions of the argument. "Books like those of General Bernhardi, idealizing the employment of force in the modern world" (014-062) are Angell's primary target — not because they are extreme but because they are honest.

## Core Views

Bernhardi argued that war is a biological necessity, that nations must fight or decay, and that Germany's demographic and economic growth entitled it to territorial expansion. Angell's response: Bernhardi's premises about how wealth is acquired in the modern world are factually wrong. The economic analysis of Part 1 is the direct refutation of Bernhardi's worldview.
