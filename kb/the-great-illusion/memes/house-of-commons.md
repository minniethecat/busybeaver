# House of Commons

The **House of Commons**, the elected lower house of the [[British Parliament]], was the principal forum for public debate on British policy in the Edwardian era. In *The Great Illusion*, Angell draws on Commons debates to document two things: the **pervasiveness** of militarist assumptions in mainstream political discourse and the **glimmers of dissent** that anticipated his own argument.

## Role in the Book

### The Speaker's Warning

> "The Speaker of the House of Commons in a speech at Greystoke, reported in the Times of August 27, 1910: 'There are plenty of people who will tell you that the way to keep peace is to be strong for war.'" (003-015)

Angell quotes the Speaker of the House of Commons — one of the most respected and impartial figures in British public life — to make a point about the **respectability** of the argument he is attacking. When even the Speaker feels compelled to warn against the "be strong for war" doctrine, the doctrine has become so embedded in public discourse that it passes for common sense.

### Sir Charles Dilke and South Africa

> "Asquith, as representing the British Government, admitted — and Sir Charles Dilke, in the debate in the House of Commons on the South African Bill, made the same point — that the British Government had no power to protect the property rights of British citizens in the Transvaal." (008-017)

Sir Charles Dilke (1843–1911) was a prominent radical Liberal MP and a recognised authority on imperial affairs. Angell's citation of Dilke serves two purposes: it establishes that the government's admission of its own impotence was confirmed by an independent parliamentary authority, and it demonstrates that the radical tradition within British politics had long been sceptical of the argument that military power could protect economic interests.

### The "Poisonous Parliament"

> "Fisher's 'poll-parrot,' and the House of Commons is the 'poisonous Parliament'" (023-060)

This is a hostile characterisation — attributed to the militarist polemicist Leo Maxse of the [[National Review]] — that Angell quotes to illustrate the contempt with which the armament lobby regarded parliamentary criticism. When Parliament questions naval expenditure, the navalists denounce Parliament; when Parliament approves naval expenditure, the navalists demand more. There is, in this logic, no level of expenditure that would satisfy the critics of parliamentary restraint.

## Significance

The House of Commons matters in Angell's argument as the institution that **ought** to be able to subject military expenditure to rational scrutiny but, in practice, could not — because the assumptions on which the armament case rested were so deeply embedded in the political culture that they were invisible even to the MPs who were supposed to be questioning them.
