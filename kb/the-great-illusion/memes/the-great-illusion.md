# The Great Illusion

The titular concept of Norman Angell's 1910 book: the persistent, widely-held, and demonstrably false belief that a nation can enrich itself, expand its trade, or enhance the welfare of its people through military conquest and political domination of other nations.

## Definition

The Great Illusion is not merely an intellectual error — it is a socially embedded false belief that shapes the behavior of nations, drives the armament race, and makes war possible. Angell's book is an attempt to dispel it through sustained economic, psychological, and political argument.

The Illusion has three components: (1) the economic error that military power can capture the wealth or trade of another nation; (2) the psychological error that national greatness requires military greatness; (3) the practical error that there is no alternative to military competition as a basis for international order.

Angell does not claim to have originated the insight — he acknowledges that "the outbreak of the Balkan War The Great Illusion was subjected to much criticism" (022-002). His contribution is to have developed the argument systematically across all three domains.

## Position in the Book's Argument

The Great Illusion is the organizing concept of the entire work. Every chapter, every argument, every piece of evidence serves either to demonstrate the Illusion's falsity (Parts 1–2) or to propose institutional mechanisms for overcoming it (Part 3).

## Core Argument

Angell's method is to grant his opponents their premises — that nations act in their rational self-interest — and then demonstrate that rational self-interest, properly understood, points toward cooperation rather than conflict. This is what distinguishes his argument from moral pacifism: he does not ask nations to be good; he asks them to be intelligent.

The Illusion persists not because of stupidity but because of historical lag. The conditions that made conquest profitable — simple economies, physical wealth, limited interdependence — obtained for most of human history. The Illusion is yesterday's truth, still governing today's policy.

## Error: The Pacifist Misreading

Angell has been widely misunderstood as predicting that war would never occur. He explicitly does not do this. The Illusion thesis states only that war cannot achieve its stated goals — not that nations will refrain from it. Nations may persist in futile behavior, especially when driven by mistaken beliefs.
