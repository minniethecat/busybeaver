# Belgium

**Belgium** — a small, neutral European state with negligible military power — appears repeatedly in *The Great Illusion* as Norman Angell's **paradigm case** of a nation whose prosperity and security derive not from armed force but from economic interdependence and international legal order. The Belgian example is central to Angell's argument because it demonstrates in miniature the thesis he extends to great powers: that wealth, credit, and security are functions of economic integration, not military capacity.

## Geographic Context

A constitutional monarchy situated between France, Germany, the Netherlands, and the North Sea, Belgium in 1910 was one of the most densely industrialized nations in Europe. Its strategic position at the crossroads of Western Europe made it a potential battleground — a vulnerability that Angell turns into the cornerstone of his argument. Far from being precarious, Belgium's position was rendered secure by the very economic interests that its destruction would harm.

## Role in the Book

### The Bond-Market Test

Angell's most striking use of Belgium is his comparison of government bond prices — the market's judgment of security:

> "The Three percents of powerless Belgium are quoted at 96, and the Three percents of mighty Germany at a lower figure. The market judges that Belgian credit is more secure than German — and the market has no interest in flattering pacifist theories." [004-014]

This empirical claim is designed to bypass ideological debate: **the bond market** — the most hard-nosed of institutions — prices Belgian debt as safer than German, despite Germany's vastly superior military. Angell's inference is that the market understands something the militarists do not: that military power and economic security are not correlated in the way that common sense assumes.

### Small States as the Norm, Not the Exception

Angell generalizes from Belgium to a broader category of militarily weak but economically successful states:

> "The citizens of Switzerland, Belgium, or Holland, countries without armies or navies worth the name, are more secure in their property and more prosperous in their trade than the citizens of any great power." [004-020]

This claim is repeated with variations throughout the book. Belgium is grouped with [[Switzerland]], Holland, Denmark, Sweden, and Norway [004-011] — all small states that "have fitted themselves for war with nobody, and are therefore fitted for trade with everybody" [014-057].

### The Conquest Counterfactual

Angell addresses the obvious objection — that Belgium *could* be conquered — by changing the question from capability to incentive:

> "If Germany conquered Holland or Belgium tomorrow, she would have to leave their inhabitants in possession of their property, or the conquest would be economically worthless." [004-027]

The argument is that even a successful military conquest of Belgium would not transfer Belgian wealth to Germany, because modern wealth takes the form of **credit instruments, contracts, and market relationships** that cannot be physically seized. The conquering power inherits territory but not prosperity.

### The Trade Test

A less abstract argument concerns Belgium as a trading partner:

> "If many orders are going to Belgium, the question arises: What is Britain to do? Build a battleship to prevent Belgian manufacturers from offering better terms? The battleship is irrelevant to the competition." [006-015]

This passage ties the Belgian example to Angell's broader thesis about the **non-military nature of economic competition**: the "threat" that Belgium poses to British industry is commercial, not military, and military instruments are categorically incapable of addressing it.

## The Ironic Coda

Angell could not have known in 1910 that Belgium would become the *casus belli* for British entry into the First World War in 1914 — the German violation of Belgian neutrality. In the 1933 edition, Angell addressed this directly, arguing that the German decision to invade Belgium, far from disproving his thesis, demonstrated its tragic accuracy: Germany destroyed Belgian neutrality, triggered a war that cost Germany its empire, and gained nothing economically from the occupation.
