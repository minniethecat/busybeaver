# Europe's Optical Illusion

A pamphlet by Norman Angell, published in Europe at the end of 1909, which formed the seed of *The Great Illusion*. Angell describes it as embodying the same principles as the later book but "published obscurely" — a modest beginning for a work that would, within two years, become one of the most discussed political texts in the English-speaking world.

## 基本信息

The pamphlet was published in Europe in late 1909 in a small edition. It contained the core thesis that Angell would expand into *The Great Illusion*: that military conquest in an economically interdependent world brings no economic advantage to the conqueror, and that the belief in such advantage is an "optical illusion" — a misperception of how modern economies actually function.

Angell explicitly traces the lineage from pamphlet to book in the Preface to the Fourth American Edition:

> This volume is the outcome of a large pamphlet published in Europe at the end of 1909...[P04-001]

## 在本书中的引用

Angell references the pamphlet at three points in *The Great Illusion*, each serving a distinct rhetorical purpose. In the Preface, he establishes the genetic relationship between the two works [P04-001]. In a later chapter, responding to critics who had misinterpreted his argument, he notes:

> My pamphlet does not attack the sentiment of patriotism...[018-020]

This clarification — that he criticizes not patriotism itself but the assumption that patriotism requires military competition — became one of the most frequently misunderstood points of his thesis. The pamphlet's brevity made such misunderstandings more likely; the book-length treatment was in part an attempt to preempt them.

In the final chapters, Angell reflects on the pamphlet's trajectory:

> The same body of principles was embodied in a brief pamphlet and published obscurely without any of the adventitious aid of a great controversy...[020-035]

This retrospective framing presents the pamphlet as evidence that the power of an idea does not depend on the apparatus of publicity — an argument that aligns with his broader thesis that real influence operates through economic and intellectual channels, not military ones.

## 关键内容

The pamphlet's core argument — later elaborated across 400+ pages of *The Great Illusion* — contained three interlocking propositions:

1. **The economic futility of conquest**: In a world of credit, trade, and international division of labor, the conqueror cannot extract wealth from the conquered without destroying the economic base that generates it.

2. **The irrelevance of political sovereignty to prosperity**: The wealth of modern nations depends on economic factors (trade, investment, innovation) that operate independently of who holds political jurisdiction over territory.

3. **The "optical illusion"**: The apparent connection between military power and national prosperity is a cognitive error — we see the wealth of powerful nations and infer causation, when in fact both wealth and power flow from deeper economic and social sources.
