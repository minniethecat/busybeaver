# British Army

Britain's principal land warfare force. In *The Great Illusion*, Angell references the British Army once, in a hypothetical comparison designed to illustrate his thesis that domestic political and economic conflicts matter more to citizens' lives than international military rivalries.

## 基本信息

The British Army was a professional volunteer force (unlike the continental powers' conscript armies) during the period of Angell's writing. Its relatively small size compared to the continental armies reflected Britain's strategic reliance on naval power and its historical aversion to a large standing army.

## 在本书中的引用

Angell's single reference to the British Army appears in Part 2, in a passage arguing that real political divisions run within nations, not between them. He contends that the British individualist or Tory has more to fear from domestic political opponents than from foreign armies:

> ...the German individualist will see that it is more important to protect his freedom and property against the Socialist and trade unionist, who can and do attack them, than against the British Army, which cannot. [012-047]

The point is rhetorical but precise: the British Army, unlike domestic political movements, cannot expropriate property or alter the social order. The comparison serves Angell's broader argument that the "real divisions" in society are ideological and economic, not national — and that the military threats that dominate public discourse are, in practical terms, less consequential than the domestic political conflicts that receive less attention.

## 关键行动 / 立场

The British Army in this period was primarily engaged in imperial policing and colonial campaigns, not in preparation for a European land war. Angell does not discuss its specific operations; he invokes it only to make the structural point that foreign armies pose less concrete threat to individual citizens than domestic political movements.

### 与本书论点一致的方面

The army's inability to "attack" domestic political opponents illustrates Angell's thesis that military power is irrelevant to domestic political outcomes — one of the pillars of his argument that international military competition is a misplaced priority.

### 被本书批评的方面

Angell does not criticize the British Army directly. Its role is purely illustrative: an institution invoked to demonstrate the difference between what armies can and cannot do to citizens.
