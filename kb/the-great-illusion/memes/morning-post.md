# Morning Post

The **Morning Post** was a Conservative British daily newspaper published from 1772 to 1937. In *The Great Illusion*, the *Morning Post* appears alongside the [[Daily Mail]] and [[The Times]] as an exemplar of the **militarist press** — newspapers whose editorial stance systematically promoted armament and imperial expansion, providing the intellectual infrastructure for the Great Illusion.

## Role in the Book

Angell cites the *Morning Post* on several specific occasions, always as a representative of the view he is arguing against:

### The Conquest Argument

> "The London Morning Post argued that conquest is the natural mode of national expansion, and that to deny this is to deny the lessons of history. The concluding phrase would seem to imply that what has always been true must always remain true." (016-013)

Angell's response turns on the **fallacy of historical induction**: the fact that conquest was profitable in the pre-industrial era does not mean it remains so under conditions of credit-based wealth and economic interdependence.

### The War Scare of 1912

> "The unsatiated appetite of Germany for territory and power must sooner or later bring her into a fight with this country" [Morning Post, March 1, 1912, cited in Angell 023-121]

This passage exemplifies the genre of war-scare journalism that Angell identifies as a **self-fulfilling prophecy**: by repeatedly asserting that Germany would inevitably fight Britain, the *Morning Post* contributed to the climate of opinion that made such a fight more likely.

### Additional Citations

Angell also cites the *Morning Post* of February 1, 1912 (023-042) and April 21, 1910 (023-113), typically in footnotes that document the pervasiveness of the militarist perspective across the British press landscape.

## Significance

The *Morning Post* is significant in Angell's argument not for any distinctive intellectual contribution but as evidence of **consensus**: across the political spectrum (the *Daily Mail* was populist, the *Morning Post* Conservative, *The Times* establishment), British newspapers converged on the same militarist assumptions. This cross-ideological consensus, Angell argues, is what gives the armament system its durability — it is not the product of any single interest group but of a **shared cognitive framework** that spans institutions and political factions.
