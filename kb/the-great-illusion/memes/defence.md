# Defence

**Defence** is the central justification invoked by nations for maintaining military armaments. In *The Great Illusion*, Norman Angell argues that the universal plea of "defence" collapses under scrutiny: when every nation arms for defensive purposes, the outcome is indistinguishable from aggressive arming, and the economic logic of conquest — which defence is meant to guard against — is itself an illusion. The concept of defence thus becomes a pivot point between the [[The Great Illusion|book's economic argument]] and its analysis of [[Human Nature in Angell|human psychology]].

## Definition in Angell's Argument

Angell defines the plea of defence not as a military doctrine but as a logical claim: "each nation pleads the need for defence; but this implies that someone is preparing to attack" [P05-001]. The circularity is immediate — if every nation's stated purpose is defensive, the "attacker" that defence is meant to deter does not exist as a declared policy but only as an inference from the other side's armament. Angell later sharpens this:

> "We must take measures for defence" is equivalent to saying, "Someone is about to attack us." And the "someone" turns out to be whoever is taking similar measures. [018-008]

The concept functions in Angell's framework as a **rhetorical gateway**: once a nation accepts that armament is "for defence," it becomes politically impossible to question the necessity of armament itself.

## The Defence-Aggression Collapse

The most radical element of Angell's treatment of defence is his argument that **defence and aggression are operationally indistinguishable**:

> Necessity for defence arises from the existence of aggression; but aggression, in the sense of an attack by one modern State on another, is practically non-existent. [018-002]

He pushes this further by noting that the very mechanism that supposedly provides defence — armament — simultaneously creates the appearance of aggression to the observer:

> Armaments are for defence, not for aggression. But if each nation describes its own armament as defensive, and every other nation's as potentially aggressive, the distinction has no objective content. [018-005]

This collapse is not merely semantic: it has concrete consequences for arms races. When [[Anglo-German Naval Race|Britain and Germany]] both built navies for "defence," the result was a spiral that left neither side more secure.

## Economic Dimension

Angell's treatment of defence is inseparable from his economic thesis: the futility of military conquest. He quotes Charles Kingsley's defence of the [[Crimean War]] as a "just war" [011-022], then dismantles the economic premises:

> The English base part of the defence of armaments on the plea that their trade and empire would be insecure without them. But the security of trade rests on credit and contract, not on the flag that flies over the territory. [014-064]

The argument extends to colonial possessions: relieving colonies of the cost of their own defence [004-012], [008-009] does not make the imperial power richer — it merely shifts a cost without generating corresponding benefit.

## Sentimental and Moral Defence

Beyond the economic argument, Angell identifies a **sentimental defence of war** that operates on different premises:

| Category | Claim | Angell's Response |
|----------|-------|-------------------|
| Moral purification | War purifies national character | "The sentimental defence of war as the purifier of human nature ignores that modern war is mechanized slaughter, not individual heroism" [015-002] |
| Biological necessity | Struggle is natural and inevitable | Confuses individual struggle with organized interstate violence [015-039] |
| Honour and prestige | Nations must defend their honour | [[National Prestige]] is a psychological construct, not a material interest |

He is particularly dismissive of the moral-defence argument:

> Almost every defence of militarism includes a sneer at the "commercial ideal" — yet the nations most devoted to the military ideal are those whose policy is most nakedly directed toward material ends. [014-050]

## The Logical Endpoint

If the distinction between defence and aggression cannot be maintained, then "the need for defence disappears" [018-009]. Angell's conclusion is not that nations should be undefended, but that the **framework of defence-as-justification** must be replaced by a framework of **mutual interest and contract**:

> I do not object to a man keeping a revolver in his house for self-defence. I object to the illusion that the revolver makes him safer when every neighbor also keeps one, and all insist that they do so only because the others do. [018-020]

This places defence within the broader structure of [[Economic Interdependence|economic interdependence]]: the real security of modern nations derives from the credit system and division of labor, not from the capacity to repel hypothetical invasions.
