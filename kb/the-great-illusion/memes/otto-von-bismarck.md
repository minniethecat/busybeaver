# Otto von Bismarck

Prussian statesman and first Chancellor of the German Empire (1815–1898), who appears in *The Great Illusion* as a crucial historical witness: the architect of German unification through "blood and iron" who, after achieving his military victories, discovered their economic limitations.

## Life and Background

Bismarck served as Minister President of Prussia (1862–1890) and first Chancellor of the German Empire (1871–1890). He engineered the wars of German unification against Denmark (1864), Austria (1866), and France (1870–71), establishing the German Empire as Europe's dominant military power.

## Role in the Book

Bismarck serves as an **example** of Angell's thesis from within the militarist tradition itself. Angell notes that Bismarck "underestimated the capacity of France to pay" the indemnity after the Franco-Prussian War (006-029), and that "Bismarck's later life was clouded by the fear of French recovery" (007-007). The conqueror who had achieved everything military force could achieve spent his later years anxious about the inadequacy of that achievement.

Angell also quotes Bismarck's own words: "Bismarck said practically the same thing" as Angell's thesis about the impossibility of destroying a nation's economic life through military means (004-023). The Iron Chancellor himself recognized what militarists of Angell's era denied — that modern economic life resists military control.

## Historical Significance

Bismarck's trajectory — from military triumph to anxious recognition of its limits — is the paradigmatic case study for Angell's argument. If even the most successful military leader of the nineteenth century could not make conquest pay, what hope do twentieth-century militarists have?
