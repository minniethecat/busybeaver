# Colonies and Conquest

The concrete test case for Norman Angell's economic futility thesis. If military conquest cannot enrich the conqueror, then colonial possessions — the prize of imperial competition — must likewise fail to deliver the promised economic benefits. Angell devotes sustained attention to colonies because they represent the most intuitive counterexample to his argument.

## Definition

Angell reconceptualizes colonies not as possessions but as economic relationships. His key claim is that "colonies are now neither colonies nor possessions; they are independent communities with whom we trade" (012-015). This is not a normative claim about what colonies *should* be — it is a descriptive claim about what modern economic conditions have *made* them.

The implication is profound: if colonies are trading partners rather than possessions, then the military capture of a colony does not transfer its economic value to the conqueror. The value lies in the trade relationship, which depends on the colony's own prosperity, not on who formally "owns" it.

## Position in the Book's Argument

The colonies concept is the **application level** of Part 1's argument. Chapters 1–5 establish the theoretical framework (interdependence, credit economy, division of labor). Chapters 6–8 apply it to the specific domains that imperialist ideology treats as prizes: trade routes, colonial territory, and naval power.

Colonies are particularly important to Angell's rhetorical strategy because the Anglo-German naval race — the most dangerous military competition of his era — was explicitly justified in terms of colonial rivalry.

## Core Argument

### The Trade Decoupling

Angell's central empirical claim is that trade does not follow the flag. He cites historical cases: "Germany lost her colonies, and it is since her defeat that her trade has expanded most remarkably" (006-030). The counterexample is devastating to imperialist logic: if losing colonies coincides with trade expansion, then colonies cannot be the economic asset that imperialists claim.

### The Cost-Benefit Analysis

Even where colonies provide some economic benefit, Angell argues that the cost of acquiring and defending them exceeds any plausible return. The "price which nations pay for armament" (002-006) must be weighed against the economic value of what armament secures — and that value, he insists, is largely illusory.

### The Modernization Argument

The most subtle strand of Angell's argument concerns what we would now call "soft power." Modern colonies, he observes, are allowed to "choose their own fiscal policy, their own tariffs, often hostile to the Mother Country" (012-043). The formal possession is hollow; the real economic relationship is determined by comparative advantage, not political control.

## Error: The Imperial Dividend

The intuition Angell must overcome is that empires *feel* profitable — they provide prestige, strategic depth, and a sense of national greatness. His response is that these psychological benefits are themselves manifestations of the Great Illusion. Prestige that must be paid for in blood and treasure, and that produces no material benefit, is not prestige — it is self-deception.
