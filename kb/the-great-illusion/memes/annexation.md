# Annexation

The specific mechanism of territorial conquest that Norman Angell subjects to detailed economic analysis in Part 1 of *The Great Illusion*. Annexation — the formal incorporation of another nation's territory — represents the most complete form of military victory, and Angell argues that even this extreme case cannot deliver economic benefit.

## Definition

Annexation in Angell's analysis means the formal absorption of conquered territory into the victor's state — not merely occupation or tribute, but the legal extinction of the conquered state and the extension of the conqueror's sovereignty. This is the maximal case: if annexation cannot enrich the conqueror, no lesser form of military domination can.

Angell's counterintuitive claim is that "the exaction of a large indemnity so costly directly and indirectly to the conquered that the conqueror may for a time seem to have benefited" (004-009) is an optical illusion. The short-term transfer is more than offset by the long-term destruction of the economic relationships that generate wealth for both parties.

## Position in the Book's Argument

Annexation is the **limiting case** of Angell's economic argument. If even total annexation — the complete absorption of one nation by another — cannot make the conqueror richer, then the entire imperialist project is economically bankrupt.

## Core Argument

### The Indemnity Paradox

Angell's key argument about annexation concerns indemnities — the payments extracted from conquered peoples. He notes the historical pattern: "the French paid off the indemnity with such astonishing rapidity" (006-029) after the Franco-Prussian War. The apparent "gain" to Germany was in fact a loan from France that France repaid out of its productive economy — an economy that would have been more valuable to Germany as a trading partner than as a defeated tributary.

### The Administration Problem

An annexed territory must be administered, garrisoned, and policed — all of which consume resources that could otherwise be invested productively. Angell argues that the administrative costs of empire consistently exceed the economic benefits extracted from subject peoples.

### The Modern Irrelevance

Angell's most radical claim is that in the modern credit economy, annexation has become structurally impossible in any economically meaningful sense. You can occupy territory, but you cannot "own" the economic relationships that generate wealth within it. Those relationships are governed by market forces, not military commands.
