# Futility of Military Power

The practical conclusion of Norman Angell's entire economic argument: military and political power, however overwhelming, cannot secure economic benefit for the conquering nation, nor can it impose cultural or social institutions upon a conquered people. This is the "apparent paradox" (P05-004) at the heart of *The Great Illusion*.

## Definition

Angell states the thesis directly: "military power is socially and economically futile, and can have no relation to the prosperity of the people exercising it" (P05-003). The claim is not that military power achieves *nothing* — it can destroy, kill, and occupy territory. Rather, the claim is that it cannot achieve the *economic purpose* for which nations pursue it: enrichment, trade advantage, or the capture of another nation's wealth.

The corollary extends beyond economics: "The forces which have brought about the economic futility of military power have also rendered it futile as a means of enforcing a nation's moral ideals or imposing social institutions upon a conquered people" (P05-006).

## Position in the Book's Argument

This concept is the **destination** of Part 1 (Chapters 1–8). The entire economic argument is structured to reach this conclusion:

1. Chapters 1–3 dismantle the traditional assumption that prosperity requires military power
2. Chapters 4–5 build the positive case for economic interdependence
3. Chapters 6–8 demonstrate the futility thesis across specific domains — trade, colonies, cultural domination

The concept then serves as the **foundation** for Parts 2 and 3 — if military power is economically futile, then the psychological and biological arguments for war (Part 2) must be addressed on their own terms, and practical alternatives (Part 3) must be found.

## Core Argument

### The Economic Dimension

The economic futility thesis rests on the nature of modern wealth. If wealth consists of credit relationships and commercial contracts rather than physical assets, then it "is impossible for one nation to seize by force the wealth or trade of another" (P05-003). The act of conquest destroys the very thing it seeks to capture.

Angell is careful to distinguish his claim from pacifism or moral opposition to war. He does not argue that war is *wrong* — he argues that it is *futile as a means to the ends nations actually pursue*. The distinction is critical to understanding his rhetorical strategy: he addresses the statesman and the nationalist on their own terms, using their own assumptions about national self-interest.

### The Cultural Dimension

The extension to cultural domination is equally important. Angell argues that even total military victory cannot Germanize Canada or Anglicize India in the modern era: "The necessary security in their material possessions enjoyed by the inhabitants of such conquered provinces, quick intercommunication by a cheap press, widely-read literature, enable even small communities to become articulate and effective in the defense of their national character" (P05-006).

This was a far-sighted observation in 1910. The very infrastructure of modernity — cheap printing, telegraphy, mass literacy — that creates economic interdependence also makes cultural assimilation by force impossible.

### The Qualification

Angell does not claim that military power is *useless* — only that its uses are limited to what it can actually accomplish (defense, destruction) rather than what nations imagine it can accomplish (enrichment, cultural transformation). The "Great Illusion" is not the belief that armies exist, but the belief that they can deliver economic benefit.

## Error: The Prediction Problem

Angell has been widely misunderstood as predicting that war would never occur. He explicitly does not do this. The futility thesis states only that war *cannot achieve its stated goals* — not that nations will refrain from it. Nations may persist in futile behavior, especially when driven by mistaken beliefs. The outbreak of World War I four years after publication did not refute Angell's thesis; if anything, the economic devastation it caused all participants served to confirm it.
