# Herbert Spencer

English philosopher, biologist, and sociologist (1820–1903), the most influential Social Darwinist of the Victorian era. Angell engages Spencer as the intellectual source of the biological argument for war — the claim that military conflict serves an evolutionary function by eliminating the "unfit" and strengthening the species.

## Life and Background

Spencer was one of the most widely-read philosophers of the nineteenth century, known for applying evolutionary concepts to society before Darwin published *On the Origin of Species*. He coined the phrase "survival of the fittest" and argued that competition — including military competition — was the engine of social and biological progress.

## Role in the Book

Spencer functions as the primary **critic** target of Part 2. Angell traces the "struggle for existence" doctrine from "cannibalism to Herbert Spencer" (013-002), arguing that Spencer's application of biological concepts to international relations is a category error. The evolutionary progress Spencer celebrates has, in Angell's account, moved beyond the stage where military competition is adaptive.

Angell acknowledges the intellectual power of Spencer's position — "there was a justification for Herbert Spencer's sweeping generalization" (014-012) in earlier historical eras — but insists that modern economic conditions have rendered it obsolete. The same Spencerian logic that once described reality now sustains the Great Illusion.

## Core Views Engaged

Spencer's claim that competition between human groups drives civilizational advancement is the target of Angell's biological counter-argument. Angell's response is not that Spencer was wrong about biology, but that he misapplied biology to a domain — international relations — governed by different principles.
