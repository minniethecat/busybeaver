# Frederic Harrison

British jurist, historian, and Positivist philosopher (1831–1923) whom Norman Angell cites extensively in the early chapters of *The Great Illusion* as an authority on the Anglo-German naval rivalry. Harrison represents the tradition of English liberal anti-militarism that Angell's economic argument systematizes.

## Life and Background

Harrison was a prominent Victorian intellectual, a follower of Auguste Comte's Positivism, and an active participant in public debates about empire, war, and social reform. He wrote widely on historical and political subjects and was known for his opposition to British imperialism.

## Role in the Book

Angell deploys Harrison as an **authority** witness — a respected English intellectual whose lifelong anti-imperialism carries weight precisely because of his patriotic credentials. Harrison is described as one "who all his life had been known as the champion of a great navy party in England" (002-007), making his warnings about the naval race especially significant.

The extended quotation from Harrison in Chapter 3 serves a key rhetorical function: it demonstrates that the argument against military competition is not a foreign or pacifist import but emerges from within the English intellectual tradition itself. "I make no apology for giving the following from the pen of Mr. Frederic Harrison" (003-032), Angell writes, using Harrison's authority to pre-empt the charge that his own argument is unpatriotic.

## Core Views

Harrison argued that British security depended not on naval supremacy but on the economic and social strength of British society — a view that Angell's economic analysis provides the theoretical foundation for. Where Harrison argued from historical and moral premises, Angell provides the economic mechanism.
