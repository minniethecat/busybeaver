# The Contemporary Review

British monthly periodical founded in 1866, covering politics, literature, and international affairs. In *The Great Illusion*, Angell cites the *Contemporary Review* once — an article by Dr. E.J. Dillon of October 1911 — in the context of documenting the alarmist language used by British commentators to characterize German foreign policy.

## 基本信息

The *Contemporary Review* was established as a Church of England-oriented alternative to the secular *Fortnightly Review*, but by the early twentieth century it had broadened into a general periodical of political and literary commentary. It published contributions from statesmen, diplomats, and journalists on both sides of the Anglo-German debate.

## 在本书中的引用

Angell's single reference to the *Contemporary Review* appears in the endnotes, where he cites Dr. E.J. Dillon's article of October 1911:

> Dr. Dillon, *Contemporary Review*, October, 1911 — "without ruth" [023-121]

The quoted phrase captures the tone of British commentary that depicted German policy as uniquely aggressive and unrestrained. Angell uses this citation as part of his broader documentation of how the British press constructed an image of Germany as an implacable enemy bent on destruction — a perception he argues was exaggerated by the very military and naval rivalry it was used to justify.

Dr. Emile Joseph Dillon was a well-known foreign correspondent and linguist who wrote extensively on European politics for the British press. His warnings about German intentions carried considerable weight with the British reading public.

## 关键行动 / 立场

Like the *Fortnightly Review*, the *Contemporary Review* served as a conduit for the alarmist discourse Angell critiques. By publishing Dillon's stark characterization of German policy, it contributed to the climate of opinion that made increased naval expenditure politically popular.

### 与本书论点一致的方面

The *Contemporary Review*'s role in the book is purely as evidence of the prevailing discourse Angell challenges.

### 被本书批评的方面

The criticism is indirect: the *Contemporary Review* is cited as an example of how British intellectual culture had absorbed and was propagating a view of Germany that Angell considered exaggerated and, in its consequences, dangerous.
