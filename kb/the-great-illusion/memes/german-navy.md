# German Navy

The **Imperial German Navy** (Kaiserliche Marine) was the naval force of the German Empire, dramatically expanded under Kaiser [[Wilhelm II]] and Admiral Alfred von Tirpitz from the 1890s onward. In *The Great Illusion*, the German Navy is not merely an antagonist to the [[British Navy]] but a **mirror image** of it — both navies justified their expansion in identical defensive terms, and both were sustained by the same institutional logic that Angell identifies as the engine of the armament system.

## Role in the Book

Angell's discussion of the German Navy is concentrated in Chapter 19, where he examines the naval competition as the **purest case** of the Great Illusion at work. His treatment is notable for its symmetry: he does not argue that German naval expansion is uniquely aggressive or British uniquely defensive, but that both are products of the same structural forces.

### The "Luxury" Fleet

> "The outcome of such a method — The German Navy as a 'luxury'" (019-002)

Angell's characterisation of the German Navy as a "luxury" is deliberately provocative. His argument is not that Germany has no right to a navy but that the scale of the fleet — a battle fleet designed to challenge Britain in the North Sea — serves no identifiable economic interest. Germany's trade depends on the same international credit system and the same open sea lanes as Britain's, and a German victory in a naval war would destroy the commercial relationships on which German prosperity depends.

### The Preamble as Evidence

> "The German Navy Law in its preamble might have been filched from the British Navy League catechism." (023-123)

This is one of Angell's most quoted lines because it captures the **closed logical circle** of the naval competition. Each side's official justification for expansion is structurally identical to the other's: the fleet exists to protect trade and deter aggression. The problem is that this logic, applied symmetrically, produces not equilibrium but escalation — because each side's "defensive" expansion is read by the other as preparation for aggression.

### German Resentment

> "How apt to resent the increased German Navy as implying aggression upon England." (023-124)

Angell here is describing British perception, not endorsing it. His point is precisely that the British interpretation of German naval expansion — that it "implies aggression" — is both natural and **logically flawed**. If naval expansion implies aggression, then British expansion implies the same thing, and the arms race becomes a competition in mutual misreading.

## Significance

The German Navy case illustrates what Angell considers the **structural tragedy** of the pre-1914 international system: two powers, each rationally pursuing its perceived security interests, produce a collective outcome — a ruinously expensive naval race — that benefits neither and increases the probability of the war both claim to want to avoid.
