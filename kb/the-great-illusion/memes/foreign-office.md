# The Foreign Office

The British government department responsible for foreign affairs and diplomatic relations. In *The Great Illusion*, Angell references the Foreign Office once, in an endnote, through an anecdote about a diplomat who served there for over fifty years — a story used to challenge the narrative of inevitable Anglo-German hostility.

## 基本信息

The Foreign Office, located in Whitehall, was the department through which British foreign policy was formulated and executed. During the period of Angell's writing (1909–1913), it was headed by Sir Edward Grey, the Foreign Secretary whose policy of diplomatic alignment with France and Russia formed the backdrop to the Anglo-German tensions Angell analyzed.

## 在本书中的引用

Angell's sole reference to the Foreign Office is indirect but pointed: he recounts the testimony of a diplomat who served there for more than half a century, emphasizing that this veteran of British diplomacy saw no inherent necessity for conflict with Germany:

> When he first joined the Foreign Office, rather more than fifty years ago...[023-118]

The diplomat's observation — drawn from five decades of direct experience with Anglo-German relations — supports Angell's contention that the supposed inevitability of conflict between the two powers was a recent and artificial construction, not a permanent feature of European politics. By invoking the authority of a Foreign Office insider, Angell seeks to separate professional diplomatic judgment from the alarmist discourse of the popular press.

## 关键行动 / 立场

The Foreign Office under Grey pursued a policy of maintaining the European balance of power through diplomatic alignments (the Entente Cordiale with France and the Anglo-Russian Convention). Angell does not directly critique this policy in his book; rather, he argues that the assumptions underlying it — that German power threatened British interests — were economically unfounded.

### 与本书论点一致的方面

The long-serving diplomat's testimony supports Angell's historical argument that Anglo-German antagonism was contingent, not structural, and that professional diplomats recognized this even as the press and public opinion pushed toward confrontation.

### 被本书批评的方面

The Foreign Office as an institution is not directly criticized. Angell's target is the broader assumption — shared by diplomats, politicians, and the public — that national interests necessarily conflict and that military power is the ultimate guarantor of security.
