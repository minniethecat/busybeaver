# Public Opinion

The lever of change in Norman Angell's theory of international reform. If the "Great Illusion" persists because of mistaken beliefs, then the solution is to correct those beliefs — and the mechanism for doing so is the cultivation of an informed, critical public opinion that can hold political leadership accountable to rational national interest.

## Definition

Angell uses "public opinion" in a specific sense: not mere popular sentiment (which he regards as easily manipulated), but the *informed judgment* of educated citizens who understand the real basis of national prosperity. "The general character of European public opinion, on which alone depends the security of civilized international relationships" (012-029) is his formulation — public opinion as the ultimate guarantor of rational policy.

The concept has both a descriptive dimension (how opinion actually functions in modern democracies) and a prescriptive one (how it could function, if properly informed by the economic analysis Angell provides).

## Position in the Book's Argument

Public opinion is the **connecting mechanism** between Angell's analysis and his proposed reforms. The argument unfolds:

1. **Part 1**: Economic interdependence is a fact; military power is futile
2. **Part 2**: The "Great Illusion" persists because of psychological forces — patriotism, habit, the "old moralities"
3. **Part 3**: These psychological forces can be countered through the improvement of public opinion, which "depends upon the improvement of public opinion and the best general methods of appealing to the popular mind" (P03-012)

Public opinion is therefore the *transmission belt* from intellectual insight to political change. Angell writes not for policymakers directly but for the educated public that shapes the environment in which policymakers operate.

## Core Argument

### The Democratic Constraint

Angell's theory assumes that in modern states, political leaders are ultimately constrained by public opinion. Even in non-democratic systems, the need for popular support imposes limits on what leaders can do. If the public believes that national prosperity requires military power, leaders will pursue military power — regardless of whether the belief is true.

The corollary is optimistic: if the public can be brought to understand the true basis of national prosperity, leaders will be forced to pursue peaceful policies. "The public opinion of the countries of Europe" is, in Angell's account, the ultimate determinant of war and peace (002-017).

### The Press and the Intellectual

Angell assigns a central role to what we would now call "public intellectuals" — writers, journalists, academics — in shaping public opinion. "If I desired to indicate real public opinion, not merely scholarly opinion..." (004-004) he writes, distinguishing between elite discourse and mass sentiment while implying that the former shapes the latter over time.

This elite-driven theory of opinion formation is characteristic of the Edwardian progressive mindset. Angell assumes that rational argument, if sufficiently clear and widely disseminated, will eventually prevail over prejudice and habit. Whether this assumption is warranted is one of the deepest questions his book raises.

### The Alternative to Force

Public opinion functions in Angell's system as the *alternative* to military force as a mechanism of international order. If nations are too interdependent to settle disputes through war without mutual destruction, they must settle them through some other means. Arbitration provides the institutional form; informed public opinion provides the political will.

## Error: The Elite Assumption

Angell's theory of public opinion rests on an assumption that may be questioned: that mass opinion follows elite opinion, and that rational argument can displace emotional attachment. The history of the twentieth century — with its demonstrations of mass mobilization around irrational ideologies — provides grounds for skepticism. Angell himself would likely respond that the mass irrationality of fascism and communism merely confirms his diagnosis: public opinion *can* be shaped, and the question is *who* shapes it and *toward what end*.
