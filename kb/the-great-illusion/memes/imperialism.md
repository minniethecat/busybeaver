# Imperialism

The policy of extending a nation's authority by territorial acquisition or by establishing political and economic control over other territories. In *The Great Illusion*, Angell subjects imperialism to economic scrutiny, arguing that under modern conditions of international credit and trade, imperial expansion does not benefit the conquering power — and may actively harm it.

## 在本书论证中的位置

Imperialism is the practical manifestation of the militarist worldview Angell critiques. If militarism is the doctrine, imperialism is the policy — the actual attempt to extend national power through territorial acquisition. Angell's economic analysis targets the core assumption of imperialism: that owning territory makes a nation richer. His counter-argument, developed across multiple chapters, is that the wealth of modern nations comes from trade, investment, and productivity — all of which operate through market mechanisms that are indifferent to political borders.

## 核心论点

Angell's case against imperialism is not moral but economic. He does not argue that imperialism is wrong because it harms the colonized (though he acknowledges this); he argues that it is irrational because it does not benefit the colonizer. The British Empire, far from being a source of British wealth, is in his analysis an "anomalous structure" whose costs — military, administrative, diplomatic — exceed any economic return [003-032]. The German push for colonial expansion is similarly misguided: Germany's prosperity came from its industry, science, and commerce, not from overseas territories it did not possess.

The argument extends to the historical record. Angell points to Spain's loss of empire in 1898 as evidence: far from impoverishing Spain, the loss of its colonies preceded an economic and cultural renaissance — proof that national wealth is not a function of imperial extent.

## 本书批评的方面

Angell's critique of imperialism is distinctive in treating it as a cognitive error rather than a moral failing. Imperialists are not wicked; they are mistaken about how the modern economy works. This framing is central to his rhetorical strategy: by avoiding moral condemnation, he seeks to engage imperialists on their own terms — the language of national interest and economic rationality — and demonstrate that their position fails even by its own criteria.
