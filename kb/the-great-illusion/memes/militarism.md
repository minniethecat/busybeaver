# Militarism

The doctrine that military power is the primary or ultimate basis of national security, prosperity, and greatness. In *The Great Illusion*, Angell treats "militarism" not merely as a policy preference but as a comprehensive worldview — a set of interconnected assumptions about human nature, international relations, and economic life that he argues are factually mistaken and practically dangerous.

## 在本书论证中的位置

Militarism occupies a dual position in Angell's argument. It is both his primary target (the "great illusion" of the title is the militarist worldview) and his primary evidence (the persistence of militarist belief despite its economic irrationality demonstrates the psychological mechanisms he analyzes in Part 2). The book's structure — economics (Part 1), human nature (Part 2), practical outcome (Part 3) — maps directly onto Angell's diagnosis of militarism: it is economically unfounded, psychologically explicable, and practically disastrous.

## 核心论点

Angell's critique of militarism rests on three pillars. First, the economic pillar: in a world of international credit, trade, and division of labor, military conquest does not enrich the conqueror — the costs of war exceed any conceivable gain, and even victory destroys the economic base that generates wealth. Second, the psychological pillar: militarism persists not because it is true but because it satisfies psychological needs — the desire for group cohesion, the thrill of conflict, the comfort of simple narratives in a complex world. Third, the practical pillar: militarism is self-defeating; the arms races it generates make all participants less secure, not more, by diverting resources from productive investment and increasing the probability of catastrophic war.

The *Spectator* and similar publications "facilitate the profession" of militarism by treating German naval expansion as prima facie evidence of hostile intent — constructing a narrative of inevitable conflict that the facts, Angell argues, do not support [003-031]. In the same way, the National Review and the *Spectator* repeatedly present military strength as synonymous with national greatness, a conflation Angell systematically unpacks [013-036].

## 本书批评的方面

Angell does not deny the reality of military power or the existence of international conflict. His critique is more specific: militarism is wrong about the *consequences* of military power. It assumes that conquest pays, that security requires supremacy, and that national greatness is measured in battleships — all propositions that Angell argues are empirically false under modern economic conditions. The tragedy of militarism, in his framework, is that it is a solution to a problem that no longer exists — a psychological relic of a pre-economic age that persists through institutional inertia and the vested interests of the "profession" of arms.
