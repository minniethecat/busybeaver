# The Fortnightly Review

British monthly periodical founded in 1865 by Anthony Trollope and others, known for publishing serious political and intellectual debate.

In *The Great Illusion*, Angell cites a single article from the *Fortnightly Review* — Benjamin Kidd's contribution of April 1910 — as an example of the Social Darwinist premises that underpinned much contemporary militarist argument.

## 基本信息

The *Fortnightly Review* (despite its name, published monthly) was one of the most intellectually ambitious British periodicals of the late Victorian and Edwardian eras. It gave space to lengthy, serious treatments of politics, science, and philosophy, serving as a platform for thinkers across the ideological spectrum.

By the time of Angell's writing, it had published work by figures including T.H. Huxley, Herbert Spencer, and Leslie Stephen — writers who shaped the intellectual climate in which the debate over militarism and interdependence took place.

## 在本书中的引用

Angell references Benjamin Kidd's *Fortnightly Review* article of April 1, 1910 in his Preface, categorizing it alongside Admiral Mahan's writing as an expression of the geopolitical assumptions his book challenges:

> Mr. Benjamin Kidd in the *Fortnightly Review*, April 1, 1910 — It is idle to...[003-015]

Kidd was a British sociologist and author of *Social Evolution* (1894), which argued that the evolutionary struggle between groups, not individuals, drove human progress. His *Fortnightly Review* article applied this framework to international politics, treating Anglo-German rivalry as an inevitable clash of civilizations — precisely the assumption Angell's book was written to challenge.

## 关键行动 / 立场

The *Fortnightly Review* itself did not take an institutional position in the Angell debate; its relevance lies in having provided a prestigious platform for Kidd's Social Darwinist analysis, which reached the same educated audience Angell was trying to persuade.

### 与本书论点一致的方面

The periodical's openness to serious intellectual debate meant that Angell's own arguments could, in principle, find a hearing in its pages. There is no evidence in the text that Angell regarded the *Fortnightly Review* as hostile — only that it had published a prominent example of the reasoning he sought to correct.

### 被本书批评的方面

Angell's criticism is directed at Kidd's article specifically, not the *Fortnightly Review* as an institution. The article is used as an exhibit in Angell's broader case that even sophisticated intellectuals accepted the "biological necessity of war" premise without examining its economic foundations.
