# Human Nature in Angell

The organizing concept of Part 2 of *The Great Illusion*: Norman Angell's analysis of the psychological and biological arguments for war. Having established in Part 1 that military conquest is economically futile, Angell must now address the objection that war arises not from economic calculation but from innate human drives — pugnacity, the "fighting instinct," the will to power.

## Definition

Angell's treatment of human nature is pragmatic rather than philosophical. He does not offer a theory of human nature in the abstract. Instead, he engages with the specific claim that war is inevitable because human beings are innately aggressive, and he offers a counter-argument: human nature is plastic, shaped by institutions and beliefs, and capable of being redirected toward peaceful ends.

The key move is to refuse the false choice between denying human aggressiveness and accepting war as inevitable. Angell accepts that humans can be aggressive, but denies that aggression must take the form of organized interstate violence. The same human energies that fuel war can fuel commerce, competition, and creative endeavor.

## Position in the Book's Argument

Human nature is the **bridge concept** of the entire book. Part 1 says: war is economically futile. Part 2 says: but people fight wars for non-economic reasons — pride, fear, habit, the "fighting instinct." Part 3 says: these non-economic drivers can be addressed through education, institutional reform, and the cultivation of informed public opinion.

Without the human nature analysis, Angell's economic argument would be vulnerable to the obvious objection that wars are not fought for economic reasons. With it, he can address the deepest sources of international conflict.

## Core Argument

### Against Biological Determinism

Angell engages critically with Social Darwinist arguments that war is a necessary mechanism of evolutionary progress — that it strengthens the "fit" and eliminates the "weak." He regards this as a misapplication of biological concepts to social phenomena, characteristic of the "old moralities" that his book aims to displace.

### The Plasticity Claim

Angell's positive argument is that human nature is historically variable. What appears to be "innate" aggression is often culturally specific — the product of particular institutions, educational systems, and belief structures. Change the beliefs (the purpose of his economic analysis) and the institutions (the purpose of his practical proposals), and human behavior will change accordingly.

### The Elite Theory of Change

Implicit in Angell's approach is a theory of how human nature is shaped: not through direct transformation of the masses, but through the influence of educated opinion on political leadership. If elites can be convinced that war is futile, they will cease to prepare for it — and without preparation, war becomes impossible.

## Error: The Underestimation Problem

Angell's critics argue that he underestimates the autonomous power of aggressive emotion — that even perfectly informed publics may choose war for reasons that have nothing to do with economic calculation. This criticism has force. Angell's response, implicit in his elite-driven theory of change, is that mass emotion is itself shaped by elite discourse. Change the discourse, and the emotion will follow. Whether this is empirically adequate is perhaps the deepest question the book raises.
