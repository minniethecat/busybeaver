# Daily Mail

The **Daily Mail**, founded in 1896 by Alfred Harmsworth (later Lord Northcliffe), was Britain's first mass-circulation tabloid newspaper and a powerful voice for imperial patriotism and naval expansion. In *The Great Illusion*, Norman Angell cites the *Daily Mail* repeatedly as his **representative case** of the jingoistic press — a media institution whose coverage of Anglo-German relations systematically exaggerated foreign threats and promoted armament as the only rational response.

## Role in the Book

Angell's treatment of the *Daily Mail* is pointed but not merely polemical. He engages with specific articles and arguments, granting the paper's editorial position the dignity of rational rebuttal even while mocking its rhetorical excesses. The *Daily Mail* functions in his argument as an **institutional mechanism** of the Great Illusion — not the cause of militarism but its amplifier.

> "Here is the London Daily Mail: 'The bigger nations are armed to the teeth, therefore we must arm.' This is the logic of the lunatic asylum: everyone else is mad, so I must be mad too." (018-011)

### Specific Engagements

Angell references several *Daily Mail* articles directly:

- A 1910 piece on German naval expansion that Angell rebuts point by point (004-030)
- A series of articles by F.A. McKenzie arguing that German commercial competition represented a strategic threat (006-014)
- A review of Homer Lea's militarist book *The Valor of Ignorance* in which the *Daily Mail* reviewer "cannot refrain" from endorsing Lea's thesis (013-035)
- The paper's December 15, 1910 critique of *The Great Illusion* itself (023-020)

### The Three-Month Reversal

Angell's sharpest critique of the *Daily Mail* involves a **temporal inconsistency**:

> "The criticism of the Daily Mail was made within three months of the same paper having published articles arguing precisely the opposite — that German prosperity was no threat to Britain because Germany was Britain's best customer." (018-013)

This reversal, Angell argues, is not hypocrisy but the **structural product** of an editorial model that treats each news cycle as independent of the last. The paper's business model — selling sensation — drove its editorial line more strongly than any commitment to analytical consistency.

## Significance

The *Daily Mail* is significant in Angell's argument beyond its specific claims. By selecting a mass-circulation newspaper rather than an academic journal as his example, Angell makes a point about **audience**: it is not intellectuals who drive the armament system but popular sentiment, and popular sentiment is shaped by institutions like the *Daily Mail* far more than by books like *The Great Illusion*.

He acknowledges his debt to the paper's editor:

> "In the Daily Mail, to whose Editor I am indebted for the opportunity of restating certain aspects of the case." (023-137)

This collegial tone is characteristic of Angell's method: even his targets are treated as potential interlocutors, not enemies.
