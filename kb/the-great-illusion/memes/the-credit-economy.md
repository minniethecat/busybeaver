# The Credit Economy

The financial infrastructure underlying Norman Angell's argument about the futility of military conquest. If modern wealth is based on credit and contract rather than land and gold, then the traditional mechanisms of conquest — seizure, confiscation, tribute — cannot capture it.

## Definition

Angell defines the credit economy as the condition in which "wealth in the economically civilized world is founded upon credit and commercial contract" (P05-004). The defining feature is that wealth is no longer primarily physical — it consists of promises, obligations, and relationships that exist only within a functioning legal and financial order. Unlike a granary that can be looted, a credit relationship cannot be captured — it can only be maintained or destroyed.

## Position in the Book's Argument

The credit economy is the mechanism-level concept within Part 1's broader argument. Where economic interdependence describes the condition and the division of labor describes the cause, the credit economy describes the substrate — the actual form that modern wealth takes.

## Core Argument

The intangibility thesis: a nation's wealth is not its gold reserves or its factories — it is the claims that its citizens hold against one another and against foreigners. These claims — bank deposits, bonds, commercial contracts — are enforceable only within a stable legal order. Military conquest disrupts that order, destroying the value of the very claims the conqueror hoped to seize.

The contagion mechanism compounds this: "the whole credit system, being thus at the mercy of a financial panic, would collapse like a house of cards" (005-023). The conqueror does not need to be defeated militarily to be ruined economically.
