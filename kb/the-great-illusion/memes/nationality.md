# Nationality

The concept of national identity that Norman Angell distinguishes from patriotism — the former being a sense of belonging to a cultural and historical community, the latter being the emotional attachment to the nation-state and its military power. Angell's treatment of nationality is critical to his argument: he must show that national identity can be preserved — even strengthened — without the military competition that supposedly defends it.

## Definition

Angell treats nationality as a fact of modern political life, not as a pathology to be overcome. He acknowledges that "the spirit of nationality" is a powerful force that "nothing else takes precedence of" in many domains (016-037). His argument is not that nationality should be eliminated but that its legitimate expression does not require military competition.

The distinction from patriotism is crucial: patriotism, in Angell's usage, is the *militarized* form of national sentiment — the belief that national greatness requires military greatness. Nationality, properly understood, is compatible with international cooperation.

## Position in the Book's Argument

Nationality functions in Part 2 as the **identity anchor** for the psychological forces Angell analyzes. Patriotism draws its emotional power from the sense of national identity. If that identity can be redefined in non-military terms, the emotional force of patriotism can be redirected toward constructive ends.

The concept also appears in Part 3, where Angell argues that arbitration and international institutions need not threaten national identity: "even in those circles where nationality is most jealously guarded, the force of nationality will be distinctly inferior to the forces making for international cooperation" (016-039).

## Core Argument

### The Compatibility Thesis

Angell's central claim about nationality is that it is compatible with — indeed, can be strengthened by — international cooperation. The nation that participates in arbitration, limits armaments, and pursues prosperity through trade is not less "national" than the nation that arms for war. It is more secure in its identity because it is not wasting its substance on futile military competition.

### The Historical Argument

Angell draws on historical examples to show that national identity can survive — and even flourish under — political arrangements that earlier eras would have seen as threats to nationhood. Small nations that exercise no military power nonetheless maintain distinct languages, cultures, and institutions. The "development of genuine nationality" (012-047) does not require a capacity for independent military action.

### The Distinction from Imperialism

Angell distinguishes nationality — the legitimate desire of peoples to maintain their distinct identity — from imperialism — the attempt to impose that identity on others through force. Imperialism is not an expression of nationality but a corruption of it, driven by the false belief that national greatness requires dominion.

## Error: The Cosmopolitan Straw Man

Angell's critics sometimes portray his argument as a call for the abolition of national identity in favor of cosmopolitan homogeneity. This is a misreading. Angell explicitly affirms the value of nationality; his target is not national identity but the *militarization* of national identity. The "Great Illusion" is not that people love their countries, but that this love requires them to threaten other countries.
