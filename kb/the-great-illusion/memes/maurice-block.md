# Maurice Block

French statistician (1816–1901) cited by Angell on the economic interdependence of European nations. Cited by Norman Angell in *The Great Illusion* as an intellectual authority supporting aspects of his argument against the economic and psychological rationale for war.

## Life and Background

A contemporary of Angell whose published work addressed the political, economic, and legal questions at the center of *The Great Illusion*. Angell draws on their authority to demonstrate that his argument is not idiosyncratic but draws on established intellectual traditions.

## Role in the Book

Angell cites this figure as an **authority** whose analysis aligns with or anticipates aspects of his own. By invoking established intellectual voices, Angell positions his argument within a broader tradition of rational internationalism rather than presenting it as a novel or radical departure.

The rhetorical function is clear: if respected authorities in law, history, and economics have reached conclusions compatible with Angell's, the burden of proof shifts to his critics to explain why their contrary views should prevail.

## Core Views

The specific views Angell draws on concern the relationship between military force and national prosperity, the nature of international legal obligation, and the economic conditions that make cooperation among nations both possible and necessary.
