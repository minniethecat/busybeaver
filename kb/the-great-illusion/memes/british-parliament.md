# British Parliament

The **British Parliament** — the supreme legislative body of the United Kingdom, composed of the House of Commons and the House of Lords — appears in *The Great Illusion* primarily as an institution whose **formal sovereignty** over the British Empire stands in revealing contrast to its practical inability to protect British economic interests in territories it theoretically governs.

## Role in the Book

Angell's discussion of the British Parliament is concentrated in Chapter 8, where he uses parliamentary debates and admissions to demonstrate the gap between legal authority and economic reality.

### Parliamentary Sovereignty and Its Limits

> "From the legal point of view, the British Parliament is supreme over the Parliaments of the self-governing Dominions — but what does this supremacy amount to in practice?" (008-015)

This rhetorical question introduces Angell's argument about the **futility of conquest**. The British Parliament can, in theory, legislate for Canada or Australia; in practice, it cannot compel those Dominions to adopt policies contrary to their own interests. If parliamentary sovereignty is this limited within the Empire, Angell asks, what would it amount to over a conquered territory whose population resists British rule?

### The Commons as an Institution

> "A common catchphrase, even in the British Parliament, until an English country parson exposed its absurdity." (014-039)

Angell's passing reference to the British Parliament in Chapter 14 treats it as a site where **conventional wisdom** circulates and is occasionally challenged. The "country parson" who exposed the absurdity of a militarist catchphrase is Thomas Malthus — and Angell's point is that even the most august institutions are susceptible to ideas that cannot survive serious scrutiny.

## Institutional Significance

The British Parliament matters in Angell's argument as an example of an institution whose **formal powers** are impressive but whose **effective powers** — particularly over economic outcomes — are sharply constrained by the realities of interdependence. A Parliament that can declare war cannot thereby transfer wealth from the conquered to the conqueror, because wealth in the modern economy is not a physical asset that can be seized but a web of relationships that military force can only disrupt.
