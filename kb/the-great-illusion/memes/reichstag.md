# Reichstag

The **Reichstag** was the lower house of parliament of the German Empire (1871–1918), elected by universal male suffrage. In *The Great Illusion*, Angell references the Reichstag to make a point about the **structural character** of the armament system: even a popularly elected legislature, representing constituents whose economic interests would be damaged by war, could not resist the institutional pressures that sustained competitive military expenditure.

## Role in the Book

### Bismarck's Warning

> "Speaking in the Reichstag on May 2, 1879, he said: 'We should find ourselves ruined.'" (007-018)

Angell quotes Bismarck — the architect of German unification — warning the Reichstag that a policy of punitive indemnities against France would be economically self-defeating. The passage is significant because Bismarck is not a pacifist but a realist: his opposition to excessive reparations is based on **economic calculation**, not moral principle. Angell cites Bismarck as an unexpected ally — a statesman whose hard-headed analysis of national interest led him to conclusions that the soft-hearted peace movement had been advocating for decades.

### The Kaiser and Parliamentary Constraint

> "von Oldenburg, member of the Reichstag — for the German Emperor to say that the German Navy was built to protect German commerce." (014-055)

This passage captures the institutional dynamic Angell finds significant: a Reichstag member is quoting the Kaiser's public justification for naval expansion. The Kaiser tells the Reichstag that the [[German Navy]] exists to protect commerce, and the Reichstag — representing voters who would bear the costs of both the naval budget and any war — has no effective mechanism to test this claim against the economic evidence.

## Institutional Significance

Angell's interest in the Reichstag is not primarily about the German political system but about a **universal problem** of democratic governance: how can elected legislatures evaluate claims about military necessity when those claims are made by governments that control the relevant information? The Reichstag's experience — voting naval budgets it could not effectively scrutinise — is offered as a warning, not an indictment.
