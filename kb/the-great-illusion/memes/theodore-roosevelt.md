# Theodore Roosevelt

American president, historian, and advocate of the "strenuous life" (1858–1919), whom Angell cites as the most prominent American spokesman for the view that military conflict builds national character — a view Angell groups among the "old moralities" that sustain the Great Illusion.

## Life and Background

Roosevelt served as the 26th President of the United States (1901–1909) and was known for his doctrine of "speak softly and carry a big stick," his advocacy of American naval power, and his celebration of martial virtues as essential to national greatness.

## Role in the Book

Angell cites Roosevelt alongside Moltke and Renan as an intellectual authority who endorses the view that war is a necessary condition of human progress. Roosevelt's prominence gives weight to the militarist position, and Angell's engagement with him demonstrates that the argument takes its most serious opponents seriously.

## Core Views

Roosevelt's "strenuous life" philosophy held that nations, like individuals, require hardship and conflict to develop strength and character. Angell's response is not to deny the value of strenuous effort but to argue that it can be channeled into peaceful competition — commerce, science, exploration — rather than military violence.
