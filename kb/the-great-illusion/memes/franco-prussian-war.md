# Franco-Prussian War

The **Franco-Prussian War** (1870–1871) serves as Norman Angell's principal **historical case study** of conquest that appeared to succeed but failed economically. Prussia's decisive victory — including the annexation of [[Alsace-Lorraine]] and the imposition of a five-billion-franc indemnity — seemed to demonstrate that military conquest *could* pay. Angell argues that France's rapid post-war recovery proves the opposite: even the most apparently profitable conquest in modern European history left the victor with no lasting economic advantage.

## Background

The war was triggered by the Ems Dispatch and the Hohenzollern candidacy for the Spanish throne, but its deeper cause was [[Otto von Bismarck]]'s project of German unification under Prussian leadership. France, under Napoleon III, went to war to prevent the emergence of a unified German state on its eastern border. The French calculation proved catastrophic: within six weeks, the Prussian army had defeated the French at Sedan, captured the Emperor, and laid siege to Paris.

The Treaty of Frankfurt (May 1871) imposed harsh terms: France ceded Alsace and much of Lorraine, paid an indemnity of five billion francs, and accepted German occupation until the debt was discharged. By historical precedent, this was a successful conquest — territory taken, wealth extracted, the enemy humiliated.

## The Economic Rebuttal

Angell's use of the Franco-Prussian War is distinctive: he **grants the facts** of Prussian victory and asks what the victor actually gained. His central claim is that France's post-war recovery was so rapid that it negated any economic benefit to Germany:

> "Ten years after the Franco-Prussian War France was in a better financial position than Germany, despite having paid the indemnity and lost two provinces." [006-030]

This is a remarkable claim if true, and Angell supports it with comparative trade and financial data. The indemnity, which was supposed to cripple French finance, was paid off ahead of schedule through French savings and credit — the same credit system that Angell argues makes conquest futile in general:

> "The losses due to the Franco-Prussian War were, for France, substantially recovered within a decade; the permanent damage was political and psychological, not economic." [023-003]

### Alsace-Lorraine as Test Case

The annexation of [[Alsace-Lorraine]] provides Angell with a specific test of the conquest-pays hypothesis. Germany acquired territory rich in iron ore and textile manufacturing — exactly the kind of prize that militarists promise. But Angell points to the economic data:

> "The annexation of Alsace-Lorraine was in no way connected with subsequent German economic growth, since the tendency was already established before 1870 and continued along the same trajectory." [007-025]

In other words, Germany's post-1871 industrial expansion was driven by the same forces driving all industrial nations — technology, capital accumulation, education — and the addition of Alsace-Lorraine was incidental to it. Meanwhile, the annexation created a permanent source of French grievance:

> "England has not even the shadow of a claim to Alsace-Lorraine, yet the bitterness generated by that annexation poisoned European diplomacy for forty years and contributed directly to the conditions that produced the Great War." [008-017]

## Argumentative Value

The Franco-Prussian War occupies a strategic position in Angell's argument as the **strongest prima facie counterexample** to his thesis. If any war in modern history appeared to pay, this was it — a swift victory, territorial gain, a massive indemnity. By demonstrating that even *this* war produced no durable economic advantage for the victor, Angell seeks to establish the general validity of his thesis.

The case also illustrates his distinction between **military success** and **economic benefit**. The Prussian army achieved everything it set out to achieve on the battlefield. The failure was not military but economic — the conquest did not and could not deliver the wealth transfer that the conquerors expected because modern wealth is not a physical asset that can be seized.
