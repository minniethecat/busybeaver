# Disarmament

The reduction or limitation of military armaments, particularly naval forces. In *The Great Illusion*, Angell presents disarmament not as a moral imperative but as the rational policy conclusion of economic analysis — if military power does not provide the benefits claimed for it, the vast expenditure on armaments is a deadweight loss that benefits no one.

## 在本书论证中的位置

Disarmament appears in the "Practical Outcome" section (Part 3) of Angell's argument, where he transitions from diagnosis to prescription. His case for disarmament is distinctive in avoiding the moral and sentimental appeals characteristic of the peace movement. Instead, he argues that disarmament follows logically from the economic facts: if the conquest of territory does not enrich the conqueror, the arms required for such conquest serve no rational purpose and represent pure waste.

## 核心论点

Angell's disarmament argument has three steps. First, the existing level of armament is economically irrational — the Anglo-German naval race consumed resources that could have funded social programs, infrastructure, or private investment, without making either nation genuinely more secure. Second, the "security dilemma" is self-reinforcing: each increase in British naval strength provokes a German response, and vice versa, in a spiral that leaves both sides worse off. Third, the obstacle to disarmament is not German (or British) ill-intent but the "optical illusion" that military superiority translates into national advantage — an illusion that, once recognized, dissolves the rationale for the arms race.

Angell notes that even modest proposals for arms limitation, such as those discussed at the Hague Conferences, failed because each nation insisted on its own security requirements while dismissing the other's as pretext [021-002][021-003]. The conferences represented "an attempt not to achieve disarmament but to achieve security without disarmament" — a contradiction he argues is inherent in the militarist worldview [021-004].

## 本书批评的方面

Angell is careful to distinguish his position from that of the traditional peace movement. He does not argue that disarmament is morally obligatory or that nations should disarm unilaterally as a gesture of good faith. His argument is narrower and, he believes, more compelling: disarmament is in each nation's rational self-interest because the arms it would renounce do not, in fact, provide the security they promise. The obstacle to disarmament is not wickedness but error — and errors, unlike sins, can be corrected by evidence and argument.
