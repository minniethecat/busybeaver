# The Admiralty

The British government department responsible for the command and administration of the Royal Navy. In *The Great Illusion*, Angell references the Admiralty in the context of the naval expenditure controversy — specifically citing a statement by Winston Churchill, then First Lord of the Admiralty, alongside one by an English pacifist, to illustrate that identical facts could support opposite policy conclusions.

## 基本信息

The Admiralty was the department of state charged with the direction of Britain's naval affairs, headed by the First Lord of the Admiralty (a political appointee and Cabinet minister) and administered by the Board of Admiralty.

At the time of *The Great Illusion*'s publication (1910–1913), the First Lord was Winston Churchill (appointed October 1911), who presided over the largest naval building program in British history — the very expansion that Angell's book called into question. The Admiralty estimated in 1912 that Germany would have seventeen dreadnoughts completed by 1915, figures that Churchill presented to Parliament to justify the accelerated British program.

## 在本书中的引用

Angell's single direct reference to the Admiralty appears in the Appendix, where he compares a statement by Churchill with one from an English pacifist writer:

> Churchill, the First Lord of the Admiralty, and the other from the English pacifist...[022-002]

Both, Angell observes, agree on the factual situation (German naval expansion is real and significant), yet draw opposite conclusions: Churchill demands more warships, the pacifist demands diplomatic engagement. Angell's point is that facts alone do not dictate policy; the same evidence is interpreted through fundamentally different frameworks of assumption about what war can and cannot achieve.

The Admiralty's own procurement figures demonstrated that Britain's industrial capacity far exceeded Germany's, making the naval race economically sustainable for Britain — a fact that, in Angell's framework, underscores that the alarm was driven by political psychology rather than material necessity.

## 关键行动 / 立场

The Admiralty under Churchill pursued a policy of maintaining a 60% superiority in dreadnoughts over Germany — the "two-power standard" adapted to the new naval reality. This policy was publicly justified on the grounds that British security depended on undisputed command of the sea, and that any narrowing of the margin would invite attack.

### 与本书论点一致的方面

The Admiralty's own estimates demonstrated that Germany's naval program could be matched and exceeded by British industrial capacity. If Britain could outbuild Germany indefinitely, the "panic" about falling behind was economically unfounded even on its own terms [022-002].

### 被本书批评的方面

Angell's critique of the Admiralty's position is implicit but fundamental: the department's entire raison d'être rested on the assumption that naval supremacy was essential to national survival — the very assumption his book systematically challenges. The Churchill statement in the Appendix is offered as evidence that even the most authoritative institutional voices operate within a framework of unexamined premises about the relationship between military power and national well-being.
