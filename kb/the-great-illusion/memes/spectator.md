# The Spectator

British weekly magazine founded in 1828, widely regarded in the Edwardian era as an influential organ of Liberal-Unionist opinion. In *The Great Illusion*, Angell treats the London *Spectator* as the principal journalistic exponent of the militarist case for British naval supremacy — citing it repeatedly, quoting its editorials, and conducting a public exchange with its reviewer.

## 基本信息

The *Spectator* was a London-based weekly review of politics, culture, and current affairs, edited from 1899 to 1925 by John St Loe Strachey. By the period Angell addresses (1909–1911), it had established itself as the most persistent press advocate for increased naval expenditure, framing the Anglo-German naval rivalry as an existential struggle in which Britain must maintain overwhelming superiority [013-036]. The magazine spoke for a substantial segment of educated middle-class opinion that regarded German naval expansion as a direct threat to British security.

## 在本书中的引用

Angell engages with the *Spectator* more extensively than with any other periodical — thirteen direct references span the book, concentrated in the chapters on "The Human Nature of the Case" (Part 2) and "The Practical Outcome" (Part 3). The engagement follows a deliberate arc: Angell first cites the *Spectator* as an authority that inadvertently concedes his economic premises, then challenges its inconsistency in drawing militarist conclusions from those same premises.

The *Spectator* occupies a distinctive position in Angell's argument because it "admits the truth of" his economic analysis while insisting that non-rational factors override economic calculation:

> In the same way the London *Spectator* while admitting the truth of...[011-006]

Angell quotes the *Spectator*'s position that "for ourselves, as far as the strictly economic problem is concerned, there is no reason for panic," while noting that the paper nevertheless demands naval expansion on grounds of national honor and the "unquantifiable" element of German intentions [020-016].

At the heart of the book's Part 2, Angell reproduces a direct exchange: his letter to the *Spectator* challenging the reviewer's conflation of economic interdependence with military vulnerability, followed by the *Spectator* critic's rejoinder [018-020][018-021]. He then dissects the rejoinder point by point, arguing that the *Spectator* reviewer's inferences, even if granted, lead to conclusions opposite to those the reviewer draws [018-025].

## 关键行动 / 立场

The *Spectator* maintained throughout the naval debates of 1909–1911 that Britain must build against the German Navy at a ratio of two-to-one, regardless of cost. It framed this as a matter not of economic interest but of "national existence":

> For ourselves, as far as the strictly economic problem is concerned, there is no reason for panic... [Yet] we should fight for a word or a sign, the *Spectator* tells us, when their patriotic sentiment or their national honor is involved [020-016][020-030]

### 与本书论点一致的方面

The *Spectator* did concede core elements of Angell's economic case. In December 1910 it acknowledged that Britain's economic position was not threatened by German competition [023-067]. In January 1897 it had already pointed out "the hopeless position" of a would-be conqueror attempting to extract wealth from a modern industrial society [023-124]. These admissions form part of Angell's cumulative case that even the most committed militarist voices recognize the factual basis of his argument.

### 被本书批评的方面

Angell's primary criticism targets the *Spectator*'s logical inconsistency: conceding the economic facts while insisting on policies that treat those facts as irrelevant. He argues that the *Spectator* appeals to "patriotic sentiment" and "instinct" precisely because the rational case for its position has collapsed — the facts are admitted, yet the militarist conclusion persists [020-030]. This, for Angell, is the core pathology he seeks to diagnose: a civilization that knows better but acts as though it does not.
