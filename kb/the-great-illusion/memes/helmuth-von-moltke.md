# Helmuth von Moltke

Prussian field marshal (1800–1891), chief of the Prussian General Staff, and architect of the military victories that unified Germany. Angell cites Moltke as the most distinguished European military authority to have endorsed the view that war is a divinely-ordained necessity — and uses Moltke's prestige to illustrate the depth of the "Great Illusion."

## Life and Background

Moltke served as chief of the Prussian (later German) General Staff from 1857 to 1888, planning and directing the wars against Denmark, Austria, and France that created the German Empire. He was widely regarded as the greatest military strategist of his era and his writings on military theory were influential throughout Europe.

## Role in the Book

Moltke appears as a **critic** whose authority makes his error especially significant. "We know that Moltke expressed a similar view in his famous declaration that peace was a dream" (011-018), Angell writes, setting up the contrast between Moltke's military prestige and what Angell regards as his economic ignorance.

Angell's treatment of Moltke is revealing of his overall rhetorical strategy. He does not dismiss Moltke as a warmonger — he treats him as an honest spokesman for a coherent worldview. The problem is not Moltke's character but his premises: he believed that eternal peace was impossible because he could not imagine an institutional alternative to military competition. Angell's response is not moral condemnation but institutional imagination.

## The Moltke Paradox

At the very time Moltke was "voicing this sentiment, a whole series of forces was at work which was making the military factor less and less decisive in human affairs" (011-019). The irony is that Moltke's own era — the high point of Prussian military achievement — was also the era in which economic interdependence was rendering that achievement obsolete.
