# The Nineteenth Century

**The Nineteenth Century** (later *The Nineteenth Century and After*) was a prestigious British monthly review founded in 1877 by James Knowles. It was one of the leading intellectual periodicals of the Victorian and Edwardian eras, publishing contributions by prominent scientists, politicians, and literary figures. In *The Great Illusion*, Angell describes it as "the premier English review" (019-028) and draws on its content to demonstrate that the Great Illusion was sustained by **elite intellectual opinion** as much as by popular journalism.

## Role in the Book

Angell cites *The Nineteenth Century* on several specific occasions, always treating it as a source of **serious argument** rather than the sensationalism he associates with the daily press.

### Sir Harry Johnston on Africa

> "Sir Harry H. Johnston, in the Nineteenth Century for December, 1910, comes as a good illustration of the way in which the philosophic imperialist mind works." (009-025)

Johnston was a distinguished explorer, colonial administrator, and naturalist — not a tabloid journalist. Angell's decision to engage with Johnston's arguments, rather than dismissing them, is characteristic of his method: the most formidable versions of the militarist case deserve the most careful refutation. Johnston's contribution to the *Nineteenth Century* serves as a **case study** in how even sophisticated minds could advance arguments that, under Angell's economic analysis, proved self-defeating.

### The Premier Review and Naval Debate

> "The premier English review, the Nineteenth Century, for April, 1910: Is there no limit to this madness?" (019-028)

Angell quotes this rhetorical question from the *Nineteenth Century* to show that even within the establishment press, there were voices questioning the armament trajectory. The *Nineteenth Century* functions here as a **split witness** — it published both the militarist arguments Angell opposed and the dissenting voices that anticipated his own.

### Kropotkin's Contribution

> "Prince Kropotkin's article, Nineteenth Century, July, 1910, in which he shows that the modern State is far less warlike than its mediaeval predecessor." (023-073)

Peter Kropotkin was a prominent anarchist theorist and geographer — an unusual contributor to an establishment review. Angell's citation of Kropotkin serves two purposes: it demonstrates the range of opinion that the *Nineteenth Century* published, and it provides independent support for Angell's claim that modern states are, contrary to militarist assumptions, **less** warlike than their predecessors.

## Significance

The *Nineteenth Century* exemplifies the **intellectual infrastructure** of Edwardian Britain — the periodicals, reviews, and journals in which the governing class debated the questions that shaped policy. By engaging with this infrastructure rather than ignoring it, Angell makes a point about his own intellectual seriousness: *The Great Illusion* is not a pamphlet for the converted but a contribution to the same forum of rational debate that the *Nineteenth Century* represented.
