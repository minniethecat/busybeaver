# National Review

The **National Review** was a Conservative British monthly periodical founded in 1883 and edited from 1893 to 1932 by Leopold Maxse (Leo Maxse), a prominent imperialist and navalist polemicist. In *The Great Illusion*, the *National Review* appears as one of Angell's most frequently cited sources — not because Angell agrees with it, but because its contributors made the militarist case with sufficient clarity and intellectual confidence that Angell found them the most **useful foils** for his own argument.

## Role in the Book

Angell's engagement with the *National Review* is extensive and textspecific. He quotes individual articles, names specific authors, and treats the *Review* not as a monolithic voice but as a **platform** for a distinctive genre of argument that he seeks to dismantle.

### H.W. Wilson and the Decadence Thesis

> "H. W. Wilson in the National Review, May, 1909: Sea-power is the last resort of every vigorous nation, and the lapse from it is a symptom of decadence." (003-015)

This quotation is central to Angell's argumentative strategy. Wilson's claim — that naval power is a measure of national vitality — is exactly the sort of argument that Angell grants for the sake of debate and then undermines on its own terms. If sea power is "the last resort of every vigorous nation," then nations without great navies (like Switzerland or Belgium) must be decadent — a conclusion that Wilson himself would presumably reject.

### The Pattern of Quotation

Angell groups the *National Review* with other Conservative periodicals:

> "Quotations from the National Review, the Observer, the Outlook, the Morning Post, Blackwood's Magazine — all repeating the same theme." (003-031)

This grouping is deliberate: Angell is documenting a **consensus** across the Conservative press, not an isolated voice. The *National Review* matters in his argument not for its distinctiveness but for its **representativeness** — it speaks for a class of opinion that shaped British policy in the pre-1914 years.

> "Publications as Blackwood's, the National Review, the London Spectator, the London Morning Post — all making the same argument." (013-036)

By listing the *National Review* alongside the *Morning Post*, *Blackwood's*, and the *Spectator*, Angell establishes that the militarist consensus spanned multiple publications and editorial traditions — it was not the product of a single newspaper or political faction.

### Leo Maxse's Polemic

> "Mr. Leo Maxse, in the National Review for February, 1911, indulges in the usual rhetoric of German encirclement." (023-060)

Angell's tone here is dismissive — "the usual rhetoric" — because by this point in the book he has established his counter-argument and is simply cataloguing further examples. Maxse's contribution to the *National Review* is treated as a representative specimen of a genre that Angell has already analysed and rejected.

## Significance

The *National Review* is important in Angell's argument as evidence of the **intellectual quality** of the militarist case. Unlike the popular press (*Daily Mail*, *Morning Post*), the *National Review* published lengthy, argued essays by named contributors. By engaging with these essays in detail, Angell demonstrates that the Great Illusion is not merely a popular prejudice but an **intellectual position** — one that can be refuted by reasoning, not merely dismissed as jingoism.
