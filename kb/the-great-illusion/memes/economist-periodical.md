# The Economist

British weekly newspaper founded in 1843, focusing on economics, politics, and international affairs. In *The Great Illusion*, Angell cites a single letter published in *The Economist* — a contribution by Douglas Owen of May 28, 1910 — in the context of the naval expenditure debate.

## 基本信息

Founded by James Wilson to campaign for free trade, *The Economist* had by the early twentieth century established itself as the leading British periodical for economic and financial analysis. Its editorial position was broadly Liberal, favoring free trade, fiscal orthodoxy, and limited government intervention — positions that generally aligned with Angell's economic framework, though the periodical's stance on naval expenditure was more nuanced.

## 在本书中的引用

Angell references *The Economist* once in his Preface, specifically a letter from Douglas Owen, a shipping and insurance expert who wrote on naval matters:

> Douglas Owen in a letter to the *Economist*, May 28, 1910...[003-015]

Owen was a recognized authority on marine insurance whose writings on naval strategy emphasized the economic rationale for British naval supremacy. Angell cites him — alongside Benjamin Kidd and Admiral Mahan — as one of the authorities whose assumptions about the necessity of armed force his book seeks to examine. The placement of this citation in the Preface signals that Angell regarded *The Economist*'s forum as part of the serious intellectual debate he was entering.

## 关键行动 / 立场

*The Economist* provided a platform for economic arguments about naval strategy, reflecting its readership's interest in the financial dimensions of the arms race. Angell's citation of Owen's letter indicates that even within a publication oriented toward economic reasoning, the assumption that military power was essential to commercial prosperity went largely unexamined.

### 与本书论点一致的方面

*The Economist*'s free-trade editorial philosophy was broadly compatible with Angell's economic argument that international commerce creates mutual benefit and interdependence. The periodical's own writings on trade and finance contributed to the intellectual climate from which Angell's thesis emerged.

### 被本书批评的方面

Angell does not criticize *The Economist* directly. The citation of Owen's letter serves to illustrate that even economically literate commentators failed to apply free-trade logic to the question of military competition — treating the economic and military spheres as governed by separate principles, a separation Angell considered incoherent.
