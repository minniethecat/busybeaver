# British Government

The **British Government** — the executive authority of the United Kingdom in the pre-1914 era — appears throughout *The Great Illusion* as both the **principal policy actor** and an object of Angell's institutional analysis. Angell's treatment of the government is distinctive: he does not accuse it of malign intent or imperialist conspiracy but argues that even a well-intentioned government, operating within the institutional logic of the state system, will produce militarist outcomes that its individual members would rationally reject.

## Role in the Book

Angell's engagement with the British Government is structured around three illustrative episodes:

### The Confiscation Scenario

> "Let us assume that the British Government has done this, and is proceeding to confiscate German-held securities. The action of the British Government, therefore, renders the stock worthless — and British holders, who outnumber German holders, are impoverished along with their targets." [005-021–023]

This hypothetical — the government confiscating enemy assets during a crisis — illustrates Angell's **interdependence thesis** at the level of state action: even the most aggressive policy the government could adopt would damage British interests more than German ones, because British capital is more deeply enmeshed in international finance than German.

### The Transvaal Example

Angell cites the government's own admissions about the limits of imperial power:

> "Asquith, as representing the British Government, admitted that this was the case — that the British Government had no power to protect the property rights of British citizens in the Transvaal." [008-017–018]

This is a pointed example: the British Government could not protect its own citizens' economic interests in a small Boer republic, despite having just fought a costly war to annex it. The implication is that the government's military capacity is **systematically mismatched** to the economic interests it is supposed to protect:

> "How many shares in the goldmines does the British Government hold? How many mines have been transferred to British ownership as a result of the war? The fact is, of course, that the British Government does not hold a cent's worth of shares." [008-031–032]

### The Arbitration Counterfactual

Angell contrasts the government's actual behavior with an alternative institutional arrangement:

> "Small states regularly refer their differences to the British Government for arbitration, but if they do, is Great Britain willing to submit its own disputes to similar arbitration?" (016-009)

The rhetorical question exposes an asymmetry: the government functions as an arbiter for others while reserving for itself the right of unilateral action — a posture that Angell argues is inconsistent with the principle of rational dispute resolution that the government itself advocates for weaker powers.

## Institutional Analysis

Angell's treatment of the British Government is notable for what it **does not** argue. He does not suggest that the government is captured by arms manufacturers or imperial interests. His analysis is structural rather than conspiratorial:

- The government makes decisions within an **institutional framework** that assumes the necessity of military power
- Individual ministers — Asquith, Grey, Churchill — are treated as rational actors who would, in a different institutional setting, make different choices
- The problem is not bad leaders but a **bad institutional logic** that no leader within the system can escape

This analysis anticipates later institutionalist theories of international relations and distinguishes Angell from simpler anti-government polemicists of his era.
