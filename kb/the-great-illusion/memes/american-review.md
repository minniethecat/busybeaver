# The North American Review

American literary and political periodical, the oldest in the United States (founded 1815). In *The Great Illusion*, Angell cites the *North American Review* in his endnotes as a source for contemporary analysis of international relations.

The periodical's articles on Spain's post-1898 transformation provided Angell with a concrete illustration of his central thesis: that military defeat does not equal national ruin, and that economic vitality can flourish independently of imperial power.

## 基本信息

Founded in Boston in 1815, the *North American Review* was by the early twentieth century a nationally distributed monthly covering literature, politics, and international affairs.

It published contributions from prominent academics, diplomats, and public intellectuals on both sides of the Atlantic. Angell's citations to the Review appear exclusively in the scholarly apparatus of his book (the Endnotes), suggesting he drew on it as a source of corroborating evidence rather than as a direct interlocutor in the public debate.

## 在本书中的引用

Angell references the *North American Review* in four endnotes, all within the scholarly apparatus appended to the main text. The most significant reference is to a December 1910 article about post-imperial Spain:

> In the December (1910) *North American Review* as follows: "The new Spain..." [023-019]

This illustration supports one of Angell's central empirical claims: that the wealth and vitality of a modern nation do not depend on its military power or territorial extent. Spain, having lost its empire in the Spanish-American War of 1898, experienced an economic and cultural renaissance that belied the popular assumption that military defeat spells national ruin.

Further citations reference articles from March 1912 and January 1912 on "International Relations," indicating that the *North American Review* served as a forum for the same broad debate about nationalism, interdependence, and the economics of war in which Angell was participating [023-043][023-046][023-053].

## 关键行动 / 立场

As an American publication, the *North American Review* stood at one remove from the immediate Anglo-German naval controversy, and its editorial line was less directly engaged with Angell's thesis than the British periodicals. Its role in the book is that of a corroborating witness: the articles Angell cites provide independent evidence from American scholars and commentators that military power and economic prosperity are not causally linked in the way popular opinion assumed.

### 与本书论点一致的方面

The December 1910 article on post-imperial Spain provides a concrete historical case study that aligns with Angell's thesis: military defeat and territorial loss did not impoverish Spain; on the contrary, the nation experienced renewal. This challenged the widespread assumption that national greatness requires armed strength [023-019].

### 被本书批评的方面

Angell does not criticize the *North American Review* directly. Its role is purely evidentiary — a source cited in support of the argument rather than a target of it.
