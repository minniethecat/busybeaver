# German Government

The **Imperial German Government** under Kaiser [[Wilhelm II]] was the executive authority of the German Reich in the pre-1914 period. In *The Great Illusion*, the German Government appears as the **structural counterpart** to the [[British Government]] — not a malign actor pursuing conquest but an institution whose normal operations, conducted within the assumptions of the state system, produce militarist outcomes that its individual members would not rationally choose.

## Role in the Book

Angell's treatment of the German Government spans both economic and political analysis, with particular attention to the government's role as an economic actor and its pre-1914 foreign policy.

### The Hamburg Government Confiscation

> "The action of the German Government that had produced the general insecurity... private property, and part was German Government, or rather Hamburg Government" (005-015, 005-022)

Angell uses a hypothetical scenario — the British Government confiscating German-held securities during a crisis — to illustrate his interdependence thesis. The passage treats the German Government not as an aggressor but as a **counterparty** in a commercial web so dense that any disruption would damage all parties. The inclusion of the Hamburg Government (a municipal authority) underscores the point: in a modern economy, governmental entities at every level are enmeshed in international finance.

### The Transfer Argument

> "Transferred from individuals to the German Government, who cannot use it so profitably" (007-012)

This is a key component of Angell's anti-conquest argument. Even if Britain could seize German assets, the transfer from private hands to a government bureaucracy would itself destroy value — governments are less efficient allocators of capital than the markets they displace. The German Government in this passage is a **stand-in for all governments**: the argument applies equally to any conquering power.

### The Pre-1914 Arms Race

> "Quite a simple matter for the German Government to keep up the game?" (019-021)

Angell poses this as a rhetorical question about the sustainability of the naval competition. His argument is that governments can "keep up the game" of competitive armament indefinitely — because each new ship built by one power triggers a matching build by the other — but that this game has no terminus other than war or bankruptcy. The German Government's participation in this dynamic is not uniquely blameworthy; it is **structurally compelled** by the same logic that drives the British Government's naval programme.

### German Policy in British Press

> "The German Government is straining every nerve, with the deliberate purpose of destroying the power and influence of the British Empire." (023-121)

Angell quotes this from the *Morning Post* (March 1912) not to endorse it but to document the **perception gap** between how the German Government's actions were interpreted in the British press and what those actions actually signified. The quote exemplifies the war-scare journalism that Angell identifies as a driver of the armament system.

## Institutional Analysis

Angell's treatment of the German Government mirrors his treatment of the British Government in its **structural** rather than personal or moral framing. He does not attribute German naval policy to the Kaiser's personality (though Wilhelm's role is noted elsewhere) but to the institutional logic of a state system in which each government measures its security by its capacity to inflict violence.
