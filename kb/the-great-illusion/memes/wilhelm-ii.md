# Wilhelm II

German Emperor and King of Prussia (1859–1941), whose naval expansion program and competitive diplomacy with Britain formed the immediate political context in which Norman Angell wrote *The Great Illusion*.

## Life and Background

Wilhelm II ascended to the German throne in 1888 and pursued a policy of naval expansion (the *Tirpitz Plan*) designed to challenge British naval supremacy. His reign saw the intensification of the Anglo-German rivalry that Angell diagnosed as an economically irrational competition driven by the "Great Illusion."

## Role in the Book

Wilhelm appears in Angell's argument as the living embodiment of the "Great Illusion" — a ruler who believes that national greatness requires military power and acts on that belief, to the detriment of his own nation's prosperity. Angell's comparison of "Attila and the Kaiser" (005-002) makes the point directly: what worked for a fifth-century warlord does not work for a twentieth-century emperor.

The Kaiser's Germany is Angell's primary case study. If the most militarily successful power in Europe cannot benefit from further conquest, then no power can. The German case is chosen not because it is exceptional but because it is exemplary: Germany's military prowess makes the economic futility of that prowess all the more striking.
