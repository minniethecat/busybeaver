# British Navy

The **British Navy** (the Royal Navy) was the world's largest and most powerful fleet in the pre-1914 era, the primary instrument of British imperial defence and the centrepiece of the Anglo-German naval competition. In *The Great Illusion*, Angell does not dispute the Navy's technical capacity or the professionalism of its officers. He disputes the **economic rationale** for its scale and cost — arguing that the Navy had become detached from any defensible theory of national interest and was instead sustained by an institutional lobby that had mastered the art of turning military prestige into political pressure.

## Role in the Book

Angell's treatment of the British Navy is focused and specific: he engages almost entirely with the **British Navy League** and its published arguments, treating the League as the most articulate and therefore most vulnerable expositor of the navalist position.

### The Navy League Catechism

The British Navy League, a pressure group founded in 1894 to campaign for increased naval expenditure, published a widely circulated "catechism" that Angell quotes verbatim:

> "The British Navy League catechism says: 'Defence of the country's trade-routes and food-supply is the Navy's primary duty.'" (019-016)

Angell's response is not to deny that trade routes need protection but to ask what exactly threatens them. His answer — that no other power has an interest in disrupting trade that benefits it as much as Britain — is the book's central economic argument applied to the specific case of naval strategy.

> "As a matter of simple fact, both the British Navy League, by its demand for two keels to one, and the German Navy League, by its claim that Germany's 'future lies on the water,' are driven by the same illusion." (019-017)

### The Supremacy Paradox

> "The supremacy of the British Navy is the best security for the peace of the world." (019-024)

Angell quotes this Navy League claim not to endorse it but to expose its logical flaw: if British naval supremacy guarantees peace, then every other power must logically seek a countervailing force — and the result is not peace but an accelerating arms race. The paradox is that the Navy League's **most pacific rhetoric** (the Navy as guarantor of peace) generates the **most belligerent outcome** (an arms race with no natural terminus).

### The German Mirror

Angell draws a deliberate parallel between the British and German navy leagues:

> "The German Navy Law in its preamble might have been filched from the British Navy League catechism." (023-123)

The point is not that the British and German positions are morally equivalent but that they are **structurally identical**: each side justifies its own expansion as defensive and the other's as aggressive, creating a closed loop of mutual threat perception that no amount of shipbuilding can resolve.

## Institutional Context

The Navy League was not a marginal pressure group but a well-connected lobby whose public campaigns shaped both parliamentary debate and popular sentiment. Angell's choice to engage with the League's published catechism — rather than with Admiralty strategic documents — is deliberate: he is targeting the **public discourse** that makes naval expenditure politically popular, not the professional judgment of naval officers.
