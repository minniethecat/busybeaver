# Patriotism

The emotional force that, in Norman Angell's analysis of human nature (Part 2), sustains the "Great Illusion" despite its demonstrable irrationality from an economic standpoint. Patriotism is the bridge between Angell's economic argument and his psychological one — it explains why nations persist in self-destructive behavior.

## Definition

Angell treats patriotism not as a simple emotion but as a complex social phenomenon with both rational and irrational dimensions. He distinguishes between the sentiment itself (which he does not condemn) and the *uses to which it is put* by those who invoke it to justify military policies.

The patriot, in Angell's account, is typically sincere: "The patriot admits that war must involve dreadful sacrifice, but he believes that the sacrifice is necessary for the protection of vital national interests" (012-005). The error lies not in the emotion but in the factual premises to which it is attached.

## Position in the Book's Argument

Patriotism is the central concept of **Part 2** (The Human Nature of the Case, Chapters 9–14). Having established in Part 1 that military conquest is economically futile, Angell must now explain why nations nevertheless prepare for and wage war. Patriotism — and the associated psychology of national rivalry — is his answer.

The concept straddles two of Angell's argumentative domains:
- **Human Nature** (Part 2): Why do people continue to believe in the utility of force?
- **Practical Outcome** (Part 3): How can education and institutional reform channel patriotic sentiment into peaceful forms?

## Core Argument

### The Rationality Problem

Angell identifies a fundamental tension: "the passion of patriotism, as divorced from material interest, would seem to be able to provoke war regardless of economic considerations" (013-030). If this is true, then even a perfect demonstration of the economic futility of war would not prevent it — because wars are fought for emotional, not economic, reasons.

His response is nuanced. He does not claim patriotism is "false" or should be eliminated. Instead, he argues that patriotism is *misdirected* — attached to a theory of national interest that is factually wrong. Correct the factual understanding, and the emotion will attach itself to more constructive objects.

### The Critique of Patriotic Rhetoric

Angell is sharply critical of the *rhetoric* of patriotism when it serves to obscure rather than illuminate:

> Not until there is a change in the general intellectual outlook of Europe, a change in the current philosophy of State relationship, will the talk about bravery and destiny and patriotism produce any effect other than the perpetuation of the present anarchy (014-013).

The critique is not of patriotic feeling but of patriotic *thinking* — the unexamined assumption that national greatness requires military greatness.

### The Defensive Use

Angell repeatedly emphasizes that his argument does not attack patriotism itself: "This book does not attack the sentiment of patriotism (unless a criticism of the dumb-driven-cattle theory of patriotism can be called an attack on patriotism)" (018-020). His target is "the economic folly of patriotism, as commonly understood" (018-021) — the belief that patriotic sentiment requires military belligerence.

## Error: Dismissing Emotion

A common criticism of Angell is that he underestimates the independent power of emotion — that patriotism can motivate war even when all factual premises are corrected. Angell's response, implicit throughout Part 2, is that he is not writing for the mob but for the educated opinion-former. His theory of change is elite-driven: correct the thinking of the influential, and mass sentiment will follow.
