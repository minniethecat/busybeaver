# The Influence of Sea Power upon History, 1660–1783

**The Influence of Sea Power upon History, 1660–1783** is Admiral [[Alfred Thayer Mahan]]'s 1890 treatise on naval strategy, widely regarded as one of the most influential works of military theory ever written. In *The Great Illusion*, Norman Angell treats Mahan's book as the **intellectual foundation** of the armament philosophy he seeks to dismantle — a work of genuine scholarship whose conclusions, however plausible in the age of sail, no longer hold under modern economic conditions.

## Content and Argument

Mahan's central thesis is that national greatness — defined as wealth, power, and civilizational influence — has historically depended on **command of the sea**. He argues this through detailed historical case studies of Anglo-Dutch and Anglo-French naval competition from 1660 to 1783, identifying six principal conditions for sea power: geographical position, physical conformation, extent of territory, population, national character, and governmental policy.

The book's policy implications were clear and were eagerly adopted by navalists worldwide: a nation that wishes to be great must build a battle fleet capable of defeating its rivals in a decisive engagement. This "Mahanian doctrine" shaped the naval policies of the United States, Britain, Germany, and Japan in the decades before the First World War.

## Role in The Great Illusion

Angell's engagement with Mahan's work is respectful but sharply critical. He does not dispute Mahan's historical analysis — that sea power mattered in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries — but argues that Mahan's conclusions **do not transfer** to the twentieth-century economy. In the age of sail, a navy could capture physical cargo, blockade ports, and seize colonies. In the age of credit and electrical telegraphy, Angell argues, none of these operations transfers wealth from the conquered to the conqueror.

> "I have quoted Admiral Mahan as follows: That extension of political control through military power is a natural and inevitable expression of national vitality. If this is true, then the armament competition has no natural terminus — and my argument fails. But is it true?" (016-034)

Angell's strategy is not to deny the internal logic of Mahan's argument but to question its **empirical premises** under changed economic conditions.

## Influence

Mahan's book directly influenced the German naval expansion under [[Wilhelm II]], the Anglo-Japanese naval race, and the U.S. Navy's transition from a coastal defense force to a global fleet. In Angell's analysis, the book's influence was a case study in the **power of ideas** — specifically, the power of ideas that had outlived the conditions that made them true. A theory developed from the experience of eighteenth-century naval warfare had become the intellectual foundation for a twentieth-century arms race whose costs vastly exceeded any conceivable benefit.
