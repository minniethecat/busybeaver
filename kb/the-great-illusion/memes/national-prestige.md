# National Prestige

The non-material driver of international conflict that Norman Angell identifies as perhaps the most powerful force sustaining the "Great Illusion." National prestige — the belief that a nation's standing, honor, and influence depend on its military power — operates independently of economic calculation and can drive nations to war even when no material interest is at stake.

## Definition

Angell treats prestige not as a delusion but as a real political force with real consequences. His argument is not that prestige doesn't matter, but that the *content* of prestige — what confers it — is historically contingent. In one era, military success conferred prestige; in another, economic and cultural achievement might. The task is to change what prestige is attached to, not to eliminate the desire for it.

## Position in the Book's Argument

Prestige spans **both Part 1 and Part 2**. It is the psychological variable that connects the economic analysis (which shows conquest is futile) to the human-nature analysis (which explains why nations pursue it anyway). Prestige is what makes the Great Illusion *sticky* — even when its economic premises are refuted, the psychological satisfaction of military greatness sustains it.

## Core Argument

Angell's treatment of prestige is subtle. He acknowledges its power but insists on its *malleability*. What confers prestige is a social construction, and social constructions can be changed. The same psychological need that drives the armament race could drive peaceful competition — if the belief system that attaches prestige to military power is corrected.
