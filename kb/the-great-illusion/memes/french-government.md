# French Government

The **French Government** of the Third Republic (1870–1940) was the executive authority of France in the pre-1914 period. In *The Great Illusion*, the French Government serves as a **comparative case** — a state that had been the target of Prussian conquest in 1870, had paid a massive indemnity, and had nonetheless found that the indemnity did not cripple its economy as the conquerors had hoped and the conquered had feared.

## Role in the Book

### The Indemnity Revisited

Angell discusses the French Government in the context of the Franco-Prussian War indemnity — the five billion francs that France paid to Germany after the war of 1870–71. The conventional view, shared by the French Government at the time, was that the indemnity would cripple France for a generation. Angell's argument, developed at length in Chapter 6, is that the indemnity did nothing of the sort — because the economic disruption was temporary and France's underlying productive capacity was untouched.

> "The French Government radically altered its attitude in the matter of the policy which had been imposed upon it by the German Government." (008-027)

The passage in which this appears discusses how governments that have been the victims of conquest adapt their policies to economic realities — and how the **policy adjustments** matter far more for economic outcomes than the formal transfer of territory or the payment of indemnities.

### The Structural Argument

The French Government functions in Angell's argument as a piece of **evidence** rather than a subject of extended analysis. The Franco-Prussian War indemnity is one of his key exhibits in demonstrating that conquest does not pay: the German Government extracted five billion francs from France and gained, in Angell's telling, very little lasting benefit, while France recovered rapidly and continued to compete economically with Germany.

## Significance

The French Government's experience is important in Angell's argument because it is a **historical case** rather than a hypothetical. Angell does not need to ask "what would happen if Germany conquered France?" — Germany did conquer parts of France in 1870–71, extracted an indemnity, and annexed Alsace-Lorraine. The economic results, Angell argues, are a matter of historical record, and they confirm rather than refute his thesis.
