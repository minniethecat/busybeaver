# Armament Race

The concrete phenomenon that Norman Angell's *The Great Illusion* was written to address: the escalating military competition among European powers, particularly the Anglo-German naval rivalry, driven by the mistaken belief that national prosperity requires military supremacy.

## Definition

Angell characterizes the armament race as a collective action problem rooted in a shared false belief. Each nation arms because it believes its rivals' armaments threaten its security and prosperity. But if the underlying belief is false — if armaments cannot actually secure prosperity — then the entire system is a self-perpetuating mechanism of mutual impoverishment.

The "present rivalry in armaments in Europe — notably such as that between England and Germany" (002-003) is both Angell's immediate concern and his paradigmatic example of the Great Illusion in action. The Anglo-German naval race of 1906–1914 was the most dangerous military competition of the era, and Angell's book was an intervention in the public debate about it.

## Position in the Book's Argument

The armament race concept bridges **Part 1** (economic analysis) and **Part 3** (practical proposals). In Part 1, Angell demonstrates that armaments cannot achieve their stated purpose. In Part 3, he proposes alternatives. The armament race is the negative case — what happens when the Great Illusion governs policy — against which the positive case for arbitration and public opinion is made.

## Core Argument

### The Self-Defeating Logic

Angell argues that armament races are self-defeating in two senses. First, economically: "The sacrifice involved in armament is the price which nations pay" (002-006) for a security that armaments cannot provide. Second, strategically: each nation's attempt to gain security through armament provokes its rivals to do the same, leaving all less secure than before.

### The Economic Burden

The economic dimension is central to Angell's case. Armaments consume resources that could otherwise support prosperity: taxation diverts capital from productive investment, naval construction competes with commercial shipbuilding for materials and skilled labor, and the fiscal burden falls disproportionately on the working classes whose welfare Angell claims to champion.

### The Ideological Function

Angell is attentive to the ideological function of armament advocacy: it sustains the "old moralities" of national rivalry and military virtue. The "solution of the problem of armaments and warfare" (P03-009) requires not just institutional reform but a transformation of the beliefs that make armaments seem necessary.

## Error: The Security Dilemma

Critics of Angell often argue that even if armaments are economically wasteful, they are nonetheless necessary because other nations arm. This is the classic security dilemma. Angell's response is that the security dilemma is real but *soluble through institutional means* — specifically, through the same logic that resolved the security dilemma among individuals within nations. If arbitration and law can replace private violence domestically, they can do so internationally.
