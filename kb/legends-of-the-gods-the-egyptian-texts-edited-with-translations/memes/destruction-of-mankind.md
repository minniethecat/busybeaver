# Destruction of Mankind

The **Destruction of Mankind** is one of the most significant events in Egyptian mythology — humanity's rebellion against the aged [[Ra]], the divine slaughter by [[Hathor]] in her Sekhet form, and the clever ruse with dyed beer that saved the human race from extinction. It marks Ra's abdication from earthly kingship and his ascent to the heavens.

## Participants

- [[Nu]] — The primeval god, Ra's father and advisor
- [[Hathor]] (as Sekhet) — The Eye of Ra who carries out the slaughter
- [[Shu]], [[Tefnut]], [[Seb]], [[Nut]] — The elder gods consulted by Ra
- [[Thoth]] — Messenger god who aids Ra's plan

## Narrative

When Ra grew old — his bones "become like silver, his members turned into gold, and his hair like real lapis-lazuli" (001-025) — mankind began to murmur against him. Ra summoned the elder gods to a secret council at the Great House in [[Heliopolis]]:

> "The Majesty of Ra spake unto the god Nu: 'Behold, mankind have taken counsel against me. Tell me what thou wouldst do in this matter.'" *(001-030)*

[[Nu]] advised that Ra send his Eye — the goddess [[Hathor]] — against the rebels. She went forth and slew men and women on the desert mountain. When she returned, she declared her satisfaction:

> "When I gained the mastery over men and women it was sweet to my heart." *(001-040)*

Horrified, Ra devised a plan. Mandrakes were brought from [[Elephantine]], crushed, and mixed with blood into seven thousand vessels of beer. Poured over the fields at night, the red liquid deceived [[Hathor]] at dawn — she drank deeply, became drunk, and "perceived not men and women" (001-050). Humanity was spared.

## Outcome and Significance

This event marks the pivotal transition in Egyptian mythology: Ra's retirement from earthly kingship and his ascent to heaven on the back of [[Nut]], the Celestial Cow. It establishes divine retribution tempered by mercy, and provides the foundation for the Egyptian practice of offering beer to Sekhet.

The narrative also explains why Ra no longer dwells among men and why the [[Soul|souls of Ra]] act as independent agents in the world — the aged god, weary of direct rule, delegates cosmic governance to his divine emanations.
