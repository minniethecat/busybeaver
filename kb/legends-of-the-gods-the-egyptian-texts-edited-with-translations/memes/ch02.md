## THE LEGEND OF HERU-BEHUTET AND THE WINGED DISK.

[002-001] The text of this legend is cut in hieroglyphics on the walls of the
[002-002] temple of Edfu in Upper Egypt, and certain portions of it are
[002-003] illustrated by large bas-reliefs. Both text and reliefs were published
[002-004] by Professor Naville in his volume entitled Mythe d'Horus, fol., plates
[002-005] 12-19, Geneva, 1870. A German translation by Brugsch appeared in the
[002-006] Ahandlungen der Gottinger Akademie, Band xiv., pp. 173-236, and another
[002-007] by Wiedemann in his Die Religion, p. 38 ff. (see the English
[002-008] translation p. 69 ff.). The legend, in the form in which it is here
[002-009] given, dates from the Ptolemaic Period, but the matter which it
[002-010] contains is far older, and it is probable that the facts recorded in it
[002-011] are fragments of actual history, which the Egyptians of the late period
[002-012] tried to piece together in chronological order. We shall see as we
[002-013] read that the writer of the legend as we have it was not well
[002-014] acquainted with Egyptian history, and that in his account of the
[002-015] conquest of Egypt he has confounded one god with another, and mixed up
[002-016] historical facts with mythological legends to such a degree that his
[002-017] meaning is frequently uncertain. The great fact which he wished to
[002-018] describe is the conquest of Egypt by an early king, who, having subdued
[002-019] the peoples in the South, advanced northwards, and made all the people
[002-020] whom he conquered submit to his yoke. Now the King of Egypt was always
[002-021] called Horus, and the priests of Edfu wishing to magnify their local
[002-022] god, Horus of Behutet, or Horus of Edfu, attributed to him the
[002-023] conquests of this human, and probably predynastic, king. We must
[002-024] remember that the legend assumes that Ra, was still reigning on earth,
[002-025] though he was old and feeble, and had probably deputed his power to his
[002-026] successor, whom the legend regards as his son.

[002-038] In the 363rd year of his reign Ra-Harmakhis[FN#23] was in Nubia with
[002-039] his army with the intention of destroying those who had conspired
[002-040] against him; because of their conspiracy (auu) Nubia is called "Uaua"
[002-041] to this day. From Nubia Ra-Harmakhis sailed down the river to Edfu,
[002-042] where Heru-Behutet entered his boat, and told him that his foes were
[002-043] conspiring against him. Ra-Harmakhis in answer addressed Heru-Behutet
[002-044] as his son, and commanded him to set out without delay and slay the
[002-045] wicked rebels. Then Heru-Behutet took the form of a great winged Disk,
[002-046] and at once flew up into the sky, where he took the place of Ra, the
[002-047] old Sun-god. Looking down from the height of heaven he was able to
[002-048] discover the whereabouts of the rebels, and he pursued them in the form
[002-049] of a winged disk. Then he attacked them with such violence that they
[002-050] became dazed, and could neither see where they were going, nor hear,
[002-051] the result of this being that they slew each other, and in a very short
[002-052] time they were all dead. Thoth, seeing this, told Ra that because
[002-053] Horus had appeared as a great winged disk he must be called "Heru-
[002-054] Behutet," and by this name Horus was known ever after at Edfu. Ra
[002-055] embraced Horus, and referred with pleasure to the blood which he had
[002-056] shed, and Horus invited his father to come and look upon the slain. Ra
[002-057] set out with the goddess Ashthertet (`Ashtoreth) to do this, and they
[002-058] saw the enemies lying fettered on the ground. The legend here
[002-059] introduces a number of curious derivations of the names of Edfu, &c.,
[002-060] which are valueless, and which remind us of the derivations of place-
[002-061] names propounded by ancient Semitic scribes.

[FN#23] i.e., Ra on the horizon.

[002-067] In gladness of heart Ra proposed a sail on the Nile, but as soon as his
[002-068] enemies heard that he was coming, they changed themselves into
[002-069] crocodiles and hippopotami, so that they might be able to wreck his
[002-070] boat and devour him. As the boat of the god approached them they
[002-071] opened their jaws to crush it, but Horus and his followers came quickly
[002-072] on the scene, and defeated their purpose. The followers of Horus here
[002-073] mentioned are called in the text "Mesniu," i.e., "blacksmiths," or
[002-074] "workers in metal," and they represent the primitive conquerors of the
[002-075] Egyptians, who were armed with metal weapons, and so were able to
[002-076] overcome with tolerable ease the indigenous Egyptians, whose weapons
[002-077] were made of flint and wood. Horus and his "blacksmiths" were provided
[002-078] with iron lances and chains, and, baying cast the chains over the
[002-079] monsters in the river, they drove their lances into their snouts, and
[002-080] slew 651 of them. Because Horus gained his victory by means of metal
[002-081] weapons, Ra decreed that a metal statue of Horus should be placed at
[002-082] Edfu, and remain there for ever, and a name was given to the town to
[002-083] commemorate the great battle that had taken place there. Ra applauded
[002-084] Horus for the mighty deeds which be had been able to perform by means
[002-085] of the spells contained in the "Book of Slaying the Hippopotamus."
[002-086] Horus then associated with himself the goddesses Uatchet and Nekhebet,
[002-087] who were in the form of serpents, and, taking his place as the winged
[002-088] Disk on the front of the Boat of Ra, destroyed all the enemies of Ra
[002-089] wheresoever he found them. When the remnant of the enemies of Ra, saw
[002-090] that they were likely to be slain, they doubled back to the South, but
[002-091] Horus pursued them, and drove them down the river before him as far as
[002-092] Thebes. One battle took place at Tchetmet, and another at Denderah,
[002-093] and Horus was always victorious; the enemies were caught by chains
[002-094] thrown over them, and the deadly spears of the Blacksmiths drank their
[002-095] blood.

[002-096] After this the enemy fled to the North, and took refuge in the swamps
[002-097] of the Delta, and in the shallows of the Mediterranean Sea, and Horus
[002-098] pursued them thither. After searching for them for four days and four
[002-099] nights he found them, and they were speedily slain. One hundred and
[002-100] forty-two of them and a male hippopotamus were dragged on to the Boat
[002-101] of Ra, and there Horus dug out their entrails, and hacked their
[002-102] carcases in pieces, which he gave to his Blacksmiths and the gods who
[002-103] formed the crew of the Boat of Ra. Before despatching the
[002-104] hippopotamus, Horus leaped on to the back of the monster as a mark of
[002-105] his triumph, and to commemorate this event the priest of Heben, the
[002-106] town wherein these things happened, was called "He who standeth on the
[002-107] back ever after."

[002-108] The end of the great fight, however, was not yet. Another army of
[002-109] enemies appeared by the North Lake, and they were marching towards the
[002-110] sea; but terror of Horus smote their hearts, and they fled and took
[002-111] refuge in Mertet-Ament, where they allied themselves with the followers
[002-112] of Set, the Arch-fiend and great Enemy of Ra. Thither Horus and his
[002-113] well-armed Blacksmiths pursued them, and came up with them at the town
[002-114] called Per-Rerehu, which derived its name from the "Two Combatants," or
[002-115] "Two Men," Horus and Set. A great fight took place, the enemies of Ra
[002-116] were defeated with great slaughter, and Horus dragged 381 prisoners on
[002-117] to the Boat of Ra, where he slew them, and gave their bodies to his
[002-118] followers.

[002-129] Then Set rose up and cursed Horus because he had slain his allies, and
[002-130] he used such foul language that Thoth called him "Nehaha-her," i.e.,
[002-131] "Stinking Face," and this name clung to him ever after. After this
[002-132] Horus and Set engaged in a fight which lasted a very long time, but at
[002-133] length Horus drove his spear into the neck of Set with such violence
[002-134] that the Fiend fell headlong to the ground. Then Horus smote with his
[002-135] club the mouth which had uttered such blasphemies, and fettered him
[002-136] with his chain. In this state Horus dragged Set into the presence of
[002-137] Ra, who ascribed great praise to Horus, and special names were given to
[002-138] the palace of Horus and the high priest of the temple in commemoration
[002-139] of the event. When the question of the disposal of Set was being
[002-140] discussed by the gods, Ra ordered that he and his fiends should be
[002-141] given over to Isis and her son Horus, who were to do what they pleased
[002-142] with them. Horus promptly cut off the heads of Set and his fiends in
[002-143] the presence of Ra and Isis, and be dragged Set by his feet through the
[002-144] country with his spear sticking in his head and neck. After this Isis
[002-145] appointed Horus of Behutet to be the protecting deity of her son Horus.

[002-146] The fight between the Sun-god and Set was a very favourite subject with
[002-147] Egyptian writers, and there are many forms of it. Thus there is the
[002-148] fight between Heru-ur and Set, the fight between Ra and Set, the fight
[002-149] between Heru-Behutet and Set, the fight between Osiris and Set, and the
[002-150] fight between Horus, son of Isis, and Set. In the oldest times the
[002-151] combat was merely the natural opposition of light to darkness, but
[002-152] later the Sun-god became the symbol of right and truth as well as of
[002-153] light, and Set the symbol of sin and wickedness as well as of darkness,
[002-154] and ultimately the nature myth was forgotten, and the fight between the
[002-155] two gods became the type of the everlasting war which good men wage
[002-156] against sin. In Coptic literature we have the well-known legend of the
[002-157] slaughter of the dragon by St. George, and this is nothing but a
[002-158] Christian adaptation of the legend of Horus and Set.

[002-159] After these things Horus, son of Ra, and Horus, son of Isis, each took
[002-160] the form of a mighty man, with the face and body of a hawk, and each
[002-161] wore the Red and White Crowns, and each carried a spear and chain. In
[002-162] these forms the two gods slew the remnant of the enemies. Now by some
[002-163] means or other Set came to life again, and he took the form of a mighty
[002-164] hissing or "roaring" serpent, and hid himself in the ground, in a place
[002-165] which was ever after called the "place of the roarer." In front of his
[002-166] hiding-place Horus, son of Isis, stationed himself in the form of a
[002-167] hawk-headed staff to prevent him from coming out. In spite of this,
[002-168] however, Set managed to escape, and he gathered about him the Smai and
[002-169] Seba fiends at the Lake of Meh, and waged war once more against Horus;
[002-170] the enemies of Ra were again defeated, and Horus slew them in the
[002-171] presence of his father.

[002-177] Horus, it seems, now ceased to fight for some time, and devoted himself
[002-178] to keeping guard over the "Great God" who was in An-rut-f, a district
[002-179] in or near Herakleopolis. This Great God was no other than Osiris, and
[002-180] the duty of Horus was to prevent the Smai fiends from coming by night
[002-181] to the place. In spite of the power of Horus, it was found necessary
[002-182] to summon the aid of Isis to keep away the fiends, and it was only by
[002-183] her words of power that the fiend Ba was kept out of the sanctuary. As
[002-184] a reward for what he had already done, Thoth decreed that Horus should
[002-185] be called the "Master-Fighter." Passing over the derivations of place-
[002-186] names which occur here in the text, we find that Horus and his
[002-187] Blacksmiths were again obliged to fight bodies of the enemy who had
[002-188] managed to escape, and that on one occasion they killed one hundred and
[002-189] six foes. In every fight the Blacksmiths performed mighty deeds of
[002-190] valour, and in reward for their services a special district was
[002-191] allotted to them to dwell in.

[002-192] The last great fight in the North took place at Tanis, in the eastern
[002-193] part of the Delta. When the position of the enemy had been located,
[002-194] Horus took the form of a lion with the face of a man, and he put on his
[002-195] head the Triple Crown. His claws were like flints, and with them he
[002-196] dragged away one hundred and forty-two of the enemy, and tore them in
[002-197] pieces, and dug out their tongues, which he carried off as symbols of
[002-198] his victory.

[002-199] Meanwhile rebellion had again broken out in Nubia, where about one-
[002-200] third of the enemy had taken refuge in the river in the forms of
[002-201] crocodiles and hippopotami. Ra counselled Horus to sail up the Nile
[002-202] with his Blacksmiths, and when Thoth had recited the "Chapters of
[002-203] protecting the Boat of Ra" over the boats, the expedition set sail for
[002-204] the South. The object of reciting these spells was to prevent the
[002-205] monsters which were in the river from making the waves to rise and from
[002-206] stirring up storms which might engulf the boats of Ra and Horus and the
[002-207] Blacksmiths. When the rebels and fiends who had been uttering, treason
[002-208] against Horus saw the boat of Ra, with the winged Disk of Horus
[002-209] accompanied by the goddesses Uatchet and Nekhebet in the form of
[002-210] serpents, they were smitten with fear, and their hearts quaked, and all
[002-211] power of resistance left them, and they died of fright straightway.
[002-212] When Horus returned in triumph to Edfu, Ra ordered that an image of the
[002-213] winged Disk should be placed in each of his sanctuaries, and that in
[002-214] every place wherein a winged Disk was set, that sanctuary should be a
[002-215] sanctuary of Horus of Behutet. The winged disks which are seen above
[002-216] the doorways of the temples still standing in Egypt show that the
[002-217] command of Ra, was faithfully carried out by the priests.
