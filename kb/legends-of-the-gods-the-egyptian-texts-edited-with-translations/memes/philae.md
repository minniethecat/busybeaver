# Philae

**Philae** (Egyptian **Pilak**, meaning "the Island of the Time of Ra") was a sacred island in the Nile near the First Cataract, marking the traditional boundary between Egypt and Nubia. It was the site of the great Temple of [[Isis]], one of the last strongholds of ancient Egyptian religion, where worship of the goddess persisted into the sixth century CE.

## Overview

Philae occupied a liminal position both geographically and culturally — situated at the frontier between Egypt and [[Nubia]], it was the last Egyptian temple to remain active after the Christianization of the Roman Empire. The island's name, meaning "Sweet, sweet island" (017-055), reflects the reverence with which it was regarded as a place of particular sanctity and beauty.

## A Sacred Boundary

Philae marked the northern extent of the Kens region in northern Nubia. The texts explicitly define the boundary:

> "The Kens extended south from Philae as far as Korosko." *(017-103)*

The road "which leads from Mahallah to Philae" (004-009) connected the island to the wider Egyptian road network, making it a strategic as well as a religious waypoint. As the southernmost major temple complex before Nubian territory, Philae functioned as a gateway between two worlds.

## The Temple of Isis

The Temple of Isis at Philae was among the most celebrated sanctuaries of the Late Period and Ptolemaic era. The texts record its enduring significance:

> "At Philae... Osiris and Isis were worshipped at Philae until the reign of the Roman emperor Justinian." *(023-596, 023-721)*

The temple's closure under Justinian (c. 535–537 CE) marked the formal end of institutionalized Egyptian pagan worship. For over three thousand years, the cult of Isis had flourished; Philae was where it finally fell silent. The island thus represents both the longevity and the ultimate terminus of ancient Egyptian religion.

## The Festival at Philae

Plutarch describes a ritual performed at Philae, possibly connected to the Osiris festivals that were observed throughout Egypt:

> "The ceremonies which he describes as taking place at Philae..." *(023-694)*

These observances formed part of the network of Osiris commemorations that linked cult centers from the Delta to the First Cataract, with Philae representing the southernmost node in this sacred geography.

## In the Texts

Beyond the Plutarch references, Philae appears in the nome boundary definitions of the text. Its position at the First Cataract, near [[Elephantine]], placed it at the frontier where Egyptian civilization confronted the lands to the south. The dual worship of Isis and Osiris on the island reinforced the mythological connection between the Nile's source, the Osirian afterlife, and the generative power of the goddess.
