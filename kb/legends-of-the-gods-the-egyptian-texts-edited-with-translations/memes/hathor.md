# Hathor

## Overview

Hathor (Egyptian *Het-hert*, "House of Horus") is a major Egyptian goddess with dual aspects: she is both the **destructive Eye of Ra** who slaughters humanity, and the benevolent goddess of love, music, and intoxication. Her cult centred on festivals of drunkenness that re-enacted her pacification. She features centrally in the **Legend of the Destruction of Mankind** (Chapter 1) and her festival month of Hathor was among the most important in the Egyptian calendar.

## Identity and Role

Hathor embodies a dramatic duality. In her **fierce aspect** she is the Eye of Ra — the burning solar eye sent forth to destroy the rebellious: "Ra sent forth his Eye in the form of Hathor to destroy" [001-052], [009-041]. She wades in the blood of her victims and rejoices in slaughter [001-054], [001-057].

In her **pacified aspect** she becomes the patroness of festivals, music, dance, and drunkenness. The beer dyed with red ochre that Ra pours out to deceive her becomes the ritual drink of her festivals [001-076], [001-087]. She is called upon with the greeting "Come in peace, O Hathor, for the work is accomplished" [009-044].

## Family and Relationships

Hathor is associated with **Ra** as his Eye and emissary [001-051]. Her name *Het-hert* ("House of Horus") connects her to **Horus**, though this relationship varies across traditions. In the Destruction of Mankind narrative, she acts as the agent of Ra's wrath, not as a wife or mother figure.

The priestesses of Hathor participated in ritual performances that imitated the goddess, particularly during the festivals where "vessels of the beer which maketh sleep" were consumed [009-084], [001-089]. The women who took part in these festivals "were supposed to resemble Hathor" [001-089].

## Major Myths and Narratives

### The Destruction of Mankind

The most prominent narrative of Hathor concerns her role in the destruction and subsequent salvation of humanity. When mankind plotted against the aged Ra, the gods advised him to send forth his Eye in the form of Hathor to slaughter the rebels [001-052]. She descended and "took them and destroyed them" [001-054].

> "Hathor waded about in the blood of men from Herakleopolis" [001-057]

To prevent the complete annihilation of humanity, Ra devised a clever plan: he commanded servants to brew seven thousand vessels of beer dyed with red ochre to resemble blood, and poured them over the fields [001-076]. When Hathor returned in the morning, she drank the beer, became intoxicated, and forgot her destructive mission [001-078]. This aetiological myth explained the origin of the festival of Hathor, in which ritual drunkenness commemorated the goddess's pacification [001-087].

### The Festival of Hathor

The festivals of Hathor were major celebrations. On these occasions "vessels of the beer which maketh sleep" were prepared and consumed [009-084]. The month of Hathor (the third month of the Egyptian inundation season) was regarded as a particularly sacred time. Plutarch records that the seventeenth day of the month of Hathor was connected with the rites of Osiris [023-983], [023-1016].

## Cult and Worship

Hathor was worshipped widely across Egypt, with major cult centres at **Dendera** and **Herakleopolis** [001-057]. Her festivals were among the most popular in the Egyptian religious calendar, featuring music, dance, and ritual intoxication in imitation of the goddess's mythic pacification. The priestesses of Hathor were known as singers and dancers, and her cult was particularly accessible to women.
