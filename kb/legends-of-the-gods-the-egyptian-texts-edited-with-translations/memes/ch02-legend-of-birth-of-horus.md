## LEGEND OF THE BIRTH OF HORUS, SON OF ISIS AND OSIRIS.

[004a-001] PLATE XIV.
[004a-002] The Procreation of Horus, son of Isis.

[004a-003] The text which contains this legend is found cut in hieroglyphics upon
[004a-004] a stele which is now preserved in Paris. Attention was first called to
[004a-005] it by Chabas, who in 1857 gave a translation of it in the Revue
[004a-006] Archeologique, p. 65 ff., and pointed out the importance of its
[004a-007] contents with his characteristic ability. The hieroglyphic text was
[004a-008] first published by Ledrain in his work on the monuments of the
[004a-009] Bibliotheque Nationale in Paris,[FN#24] and I gave a transcript of the
[004a-010] text, with transliteration and translation, in 1895.[FN#25]

[FN#24] Les Monuments Egyptiens (Cabinet des Medailles et Antiques),
[004a-011] In the Bibliotheque de l'Ecole des Hautes Etudes, Paris, 1879-1882,
[004a-012] plate xxii. ff.

[FN#25] First Steps in Egyptian, pp. 179-188.

[004a-013] The greater part of the text consists of a hymn to Osiris, which was
[004a-014] probably composed under the XVIIIth Dynasty, when an extraordinary
[004a-015] development of the cult of that god took place, and when he was placed
[004a-016] by Egyptian theologians at the head of all the gods. Though unseen in
[004a-017] the temples, his presence filled all Egypt, and his body formed the
[004a-018] very substance of the country. He was the God of all gods and the
[004a-019] Governor of the Two Companies of the gods, he formed the soul and body
[004a-020] of Ra, he was the beneficent Spirit of all spirits, he was himself the
[004a-021] celestial food on which the Doubles in the Other World lived. He was
[004a-022] the greatest of the gods in On (Heliopolis), Memphis, Herakleopolis,
[004a-023] Hermopolis, Abydos, and the region of the First Cataract, and so. He
[004a-024] embodied in his own person the might of Ra-Tem, Apis and Ptah, the
[004a-025] Horus-gods, Thoth and Khnemu, and his rule over Busiris and Abydos
[004a-026] continued to be supreme, as it had been for many, many hundreds of
[004a-027] years. He was the source of the Nile, the north wind sprang from him,
[004a-028] his seats were the stars of heaven which never set, and the
[004a-029] imperishable stars were his ministers. All heaven was his dominion,
[004a-030] and the doors of the sky opened before him of their own accord when he
[004a-031] appeared. He inherited the earth from his father Keb, and the
[004a-032] sovereignty of heaven from his mother Nut. In his person he united
[004a-033] endless time in the past and endless time in the future. Like Ra he
[004a-034] had fought Seba, or Set, the monster of evil, and had defeated him, and
[004a-035] his victory assured to him lasting authority over the gods and the
[004a-036] dead. He exercised his creative power in making land and water, trees
[004a-037] and herbs, cattle and other four-footed beasts, birds of all kinds, and
[004a-038] fish and creeping things; even the waste spaces of the desert owed
[004a-039] allegiance to him as the creator. And he rolled out the sky, and set
[004a-040] the light above the darkness.

[004a-041] The last paragraph of the text contains an allusion to Isis, the sister
[004a-042] and wife of Osiris, and mentions the legend of the birth of Horus,
[004a-043] which even under the XVIIIth Dynasty was very ancient, Isis, we are
[004a-044] told, was the constant protectress of her brother, she drove away the
[004a-045] fiends that wanted to attack him, and kept them out of his shrine and
[004a-046] tomb, and she guarded him from all accidents. All these things she did
[004a-047] by means of spells and incantations, large numbers of which were known
[004a-048] to her, and by her power as the "witch-goddess." Her "mouth was
[004a-049] trained to perfection, and she made no mistake in pronouncing her
[004a-050] spells, and her tongue was skilled and halted not." At length came the
[004a-051] unlucky day when Set succeeded in killing Osiris during the war which
[004a-052] the "good god" was waging against him and his fiends. Details of the
[004a-053] engagement are wanting, but the Pyramid Texts state that the body of
[004a-054] Osiris was hurled to the ground by Set at a place called Netat, which
[004a-055] seems to have been near Abydos.[FN#26] The news of the death of Osiris
[004a-056] was brought to Isis, and she at once set out to find his body. All
[004a-057] legends agree in saying that she took the form of a bird, and that she
[004a-058] flew about unceasingly, going hither and thither, and uttering wailing
[004a-059] cries of grief. At length she found the body, and with a piercing cry
[004a-060] she alighted on the ground. The Pyramid Texts say that Nephthys was
[004a-061] with her that "Isis came, Nephthys came, the one on the right side, the
[004a-062] other on the left side, one in the form of a Hat bird, the other in the
[004a-063] form of a Tchert bird, and they found Osiris thrown on the ground in
[004a-064] Netat by his brother Set." The late form of the legend goes on to say
[004a-065] that Isis fanned the body with her feathers, and produced air, and that
[004a-066] at length she caused the inert members of Osiris to move, and drew from
[004a-067] him his essence, wherefrom she produced her child Horus.

[FN#26] Pepi I., line 475; Pepi II., line 1263.

[004a-068] This bare statement of the dogma of the conception of Horus does not
[004a-069] represent all that is known about it, and it may well be supplemented
[004a-070] by a passage from the Pyramid Texts,[FN#27] which reads, "Adoration to
[004a-071] thee, O Osiris.[FN#28] Rise thou up on thy left side, place thyself on
[004a-072] thy right side. This water which I give unto thee is the water of
[004a-073] youth (or rejuvenation). Adoration to thee, O Osiris! Rise thou up on
[004a-074] thy left side, place thyself on thy right side. This bread which I
[004a-075] have made for thee is warmth. Adoration to thee, O Osiris! The doors
[004a-076] of heaven are opened to thee, the doors of the streams are thrown wide
[004a-077] open to thee. The gods in the city of Pe come [to thee], Osiris, at
[004a-078] the sound (or voice) of the supplication of Isis and Nephthys. . . . .
[004a-079] Thy elder sister took thy body in her arms, she chafed thy hands,
[004a-080] she clasped thee to her breast [when] she found thee [lying] on thy
[004a-081] side on the plain of Netat." And in another place we read:[FN#29] "Thy
[004a-082] two sisters, Isis and Nephthys, came to thee, Kam-urt, in thy name of
[004a-083] Kam-ur, Uatchet-urt, in thy name of Uatch-ur . . . . . . . Isis and
[004a-084] Nephthys weave magical protection for thee in the city of Saut, for
[004a-085] thee their lord, in thy name of 'Lord of Saut,' for their god, in thy
[004a-086] name of 'God.' They praise thee; go not thou far from them in thy name
[004a-087] of 'Tua.' They present offerings to thee; be not wroth in thy name of
[004a-088] 'Tchentru.' Thy sister Isis cometh to thee rejoicing in her love for
[004a-089] thee.[FN#30] Thou hast union with her, thy seed entereth her. She
[004a-090] conceiveth in the form of the star Septet (Sothis). Horus-Sept issueth
[004a-091] from thee in the form of Horus, dweller in the star Septet. Thou
[004a-092] makest a spirit to be in him in his name 'Spirit dwelling in the god
[004a-093] Tchentru.' He avengeth thee in his name of 'Horus, the son who avenged
[004a-094] his father.' Hail, Osiris, Keb hath brought to thee Horus, he hath
[004a-095] avenged thee, he hath brought to thee the hearts of the gods, Horus
[004a-096] hath given thee his Eye, thou hast taken possession of the Urert Crown
[004a-097] thereby at the head of the gods. Horus hath presented to thee thy
[004a-098] members, he hath collected them completely, there is no disorder in
[004a-099] thee. Thoth hath seized thy enemy and hath slain him and those who
[004a-100] were with him." The above words are addressed to dead kings in the
[004a-101] Pyramid Texts, and what the gods were supposed to do for them was
[004a-102] believed by the Egyptians to have been actually done for Osiris. These
[004a-103] extracts are peculiarly valuable, for they prove that the legend of
[004a-104] Osiris which was current under the XVIIIth Dynasty was based upon
[004a-105] traditions which were universally accepted in Egypt under the Vth and
[004a-106] VIth Dynasties.

[FN#27] Mer-en-Ra, line 336; Pepi II., line 862.

[FN#28] I omit the king's names.

[FN#29] Teta, line 274; Pepi I., line 27; Mer-en-Ra, line 37; and Pepi  
[004a-107] II., line 67.

[FN#30] Pyramid Text, Teta, l. 276.

### PLATE XV.

[004a-108] PLATE XVI.
[004a-109] The Stele recording the casting out of a devil from the Princess of
[004a-110] Bekhten.

[004a-111] The hymn concludes with a reference to the accession of Horus, son of
[004a-112] Isis, the flesh and bone of Osiris, to the throne of his grandfather
[004a-113] Keb, and to the welcome which he received from the Tchatcha, or
[004a-114] Administrators of heaven, and the Company of the Gods, and the Lords of
[004a-115] Truth, who assembled in the Great House of Heliopolis to acknowledge
[004a-116] his sovereignty. His succession also received the approval of Neb-er-
[004a-117] tcher, who, as we saw from the first legend in this book, was the
[004a-118] Creator of the Universe.
