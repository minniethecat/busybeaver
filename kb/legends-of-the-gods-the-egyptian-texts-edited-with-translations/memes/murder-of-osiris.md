# Murder of Osiris by Set

## Overview

The Murder of Osiris by Set is the central tragedy of the Osirian myth cycle. Jealous of his brother Osiris's kingship, Set (also called Seba or Typhon) killed Osiris during a war. According to the Egyptian texts, the body of Osiris was hurled to the ground by Set at a place called Netat, near Abydos. Later Greek accounts, notably Plutarch's, elaborated the story with the motif of the chest: Set tricked Osiris into lying in a decorated chest at a banquet, then sealed it and cast it into the Nile. This murder set in motion the entire cycle of Isis's search, Osiris's resurrection, and Horus's vengeance.

## Narrative

### The War Against Set

Before the murder, Osiris had fought and defeated Set in battle. The Pyramid Texts speak of Osiris's victory over "Seba, or Set, the monster of evil," which "assured to him lasting authority over the gods and the dead." [PN=009-029] However, this victory was not final:

> "At length came the unlucky day when Set succeeded in killing Osiris during the war which the 'good god' was waging against him and his fiends." [PN=009-033]

### The Death at Netat

The Pyramid Texts provide a stark account of the murder. Set struck Osiris down at a specific location near Abydos:

> "Details of the engagement are wanting, but the Pyramid Texts state that the body of Osiris was hurled to the ground by Set at a place called Netat, which seems to have been near Abydos." [PN=009-034]

The texts further describe Isis and Nephthys finding Osiris "thrown on the ground in Netat by his brother Set," the two sisters taking the form of birds — one a Hat bird, the other a Tchert bird. [PN=009-035]

### The Chest Tradition

A later development of the story, preserved most fully in Plutarch but alluded to in Egyptian sources, added the detail of the chest. Set constructed a beautifully decorated chest and, at a banquet, promised it to whomever it fitted. When Osiris lay in it, Set and his conspirators sealed the lid and cast it into the Nile. The chest floated to Byblos, where it became embedded in a tree.

Egyptian literature is filled with allusions to these events:

> "Egyptian literature is full of allusions to events which took place in the life of Osiris, and to his persecution, murder, and resurrection, and numerous texts of all periods describe the love and devotion of his sister and wife Isis, and the filial piety of Horus." [PN=009-001]

### The Aftermath

The murder was not merely a crime but a cosmic disruption. Set seized the kingdom, and Isis fled into hiding. The texts speak of a "dwelling" where Set imprisoned Isis after the murder:

> "Isis, it seems, was shut up in some dwelling by Set after he murdered Osiris, probably with the intention of forcing her to marry him and thus legitimize his seizure of the kingdom." [PN=005-134]

## Outcome and Significance

The Murder of Osiris had far-reaching consequences in Egyptian mythology and religion:

1. **Cosmic Disorder**: The murder represented the triumph of chaos (Isfet) over order (Maat). Set's act disrupted the cosmic balance established by Osiris's reign.

2. **Catalyst for the Myth Cycle**: The murder initiated the entire sequence of Isis's search, the conception and birth of Horus, and the eventual war of succession — the foundational narrative of Egyptian kingship.

3. **Origin of Death**: Osiris's death made him the first being to experience mortality, and his subsequent resurrection made him the prototype for all deceased Egyptians who hoped for rebirth in the afterlife.

4. **Justification for Horus's Vengeance**: The murder provided the moral and legal basis for Horus's claim to the throne and his duty to avenge his father. As the texts state: "He avenged his father Osiris by vanquishing Set." [FN#206]
