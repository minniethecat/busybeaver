# Purification

## Overview

**Purification** (ritual cleansing) was a fundamental requirement of Egyptian religious practice. Before approaching a god, entering a temple, or reciting sacred texts, both priest and layperson had to be purified — physically through washing with Nile water, and spiritually through abstinence from certain foods and behaviours. Purification was not merely hygiene; it was a ritual transformation that rendered the mortal fit to stand in the divine presence.

## Core Doctrine

### Water and Sprinkling

Water — specifically Nile water, believed to carry the creative potency of the inundation — was the primary agent of purification:

> "The water of purification was sprinkled [upon them]" [017-132]

Priests "sprinkled him with holy water" [003-043] before the king entered the shrine. Libations of Nile water symbolized the seed of [[osiris|Osiris]] that "germinated and brought forth fruit" [006-105].

### Sevenfold Purification

The most solemn purification rite required a sevenfold repetition over multiple days. When [[thoth|Thoth]] wished to recite the sacred composition on behalf of [[ra|Ra]], "he must perform a sevenfold purification for three days, and priests and [ordinary] men shall do likewise" [013-010].

This was accompanied by specific ritual preparations: anointing with olive oil and unguent, "propitiatory offerings on both his hands of incense" [013-003], pure natron behind the ears, a new double tunic, white leather sandals, and the figure of the goddess Maat drawn on the tongue in green ochre [013-003].

### Abstinence and Moral Purity

The Egyptian tradition, as recorded by Plutarch, distinguished between external washing and internal purity. Priests undergoing purification "abstain from wine wholly, and they give themselves up entirely to the study of those things which relate to the gods" [023-167]. The goal was "one uniform and temperate course of life, and an abstinence from many kinds of meat" [023-030], for purification was a state of being, not a single act.

> "Whilst they are in a course of purification, they are at so much pains to keep their bodies chaste and free from the impurity of carnal lusts" [023-114]

### Temple Purity

The temple itself required purification. "Libations and purifications and offerings shall be made to their images" [015-331] — purification was paired with offering as two inseparable acts of divine service. The ground was sprinkled to purify it before rituals, and the sanctuary was kept free from all pollution.

## Significance

Purification was the threshold of Egyptian religious experience. Without it, no prayer was heard, no offering accepted, no oracle received. The elaborate purification protocols — washing, anointing, fasting, sexual abstinence, and the wearing of fresh linen — were not superstitious taboos but a coherent system for ritually transforming the human body into a fit vessel for divine encounter.

The sevenfold purification in particular echoes across Egyptian literature as the gold standard of ritual readiness, required before the most powerful words of power could be spoken.
