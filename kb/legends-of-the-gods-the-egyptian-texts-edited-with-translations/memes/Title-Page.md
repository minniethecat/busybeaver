## PREFACE

[P01-006] The welcome which has been accorded to the volumes of this Series, and
[P01-007] the fact that some of them have passed into second and third editions,
[P01-008] suggest that these little books have been found useful by beginners in
[P01-009] Egyptology and others. Hitherto the object of them has been to supply
[P01-010] information about the Religion, Magic, Language, and History of the
[P01-011] ancient Egyptians, and to provide editions of the original texts from
[P01-012] which such information was derived. There are, however, many branches
[P01-013] of Egyptology which need treatment in a similar manner in this Series,
[P01-014] and it has been suggested in many quarters that the time has now
[P01-015] arrived when the publication of a series of groups of texts
[P01-016] illustrating Egyptian Literature in general might well be begun.
[P01-017] Seeing that nothing is known about the authors of Egyptian works, not
[P01-018] even their names, it is impossible to write a History of Egyptian
[P01-019] Literature in the ordinary sense of the word. The only thing to be
[P01-020] done is to print the actual works in the best and most complete form
[P01-021] possible, with translations, and then to put them in the hands of the
[P01-022] reader and leave them to his judgment.

[P01-023] With this object in view, it has been decided to publish in the Series
[P01-024] several volumes which shall be devoted to the reproduction in
[P01-025] hieroglyphic type of the best and most typical examples of the various
[P01-026] kinds of Egyptian Literature, with English translations, on a much
[P01-027] larger scale than was possible in my "First Steps in Egyptian" or in my
[P01-028] "Egyptian Reading Book." These volumes are intended to serve a double
[P01-029] purpose, i.e., to supply the beginner in Egyptian with new material and
[P01-030] a series of reading books, and to provide the general reader with
[P01-031] translations of Egyptian works in a handy form.

[P01-032] The Egyptian texts, whether the originals be written in hieroglyphic or
[P01-033] hieratic characters, are here printed in hieroglyphic type, and are
[P01-034] arranged with English translations, page for page. They are printed as
[P01-035] they are written in the original documents, i.e., the words are not
[P01-036] divided. The beginner will find the practice of dividing the words for
[P01-037] himself most useful in acquiring facility of reading and understanding
[P01-038] the language. The translations are as literal as can reasonably be
[P01-039] expected, and, as a whole, I believe that they mean what the original
[P01-040] writers intended to say. In the case of passages where the text is
[P01-041] corrupt, and readings are mixed, or where very rare words occur, or
[P01-042] where words are omitted, the renderings given claim to be nothing more
[P01-043] than suggestions as to their meanings. It must be remembered that the
[P01-044] exact meanings of many Egyptian words have still to be ascertained, and
[P01-045] that the ancient Egyptian scribes were as much puzzled as we are by
[P01-046] some of the texts which they copied, and that owing to carelessness,
[P01-047] ignorance, or weariness, or all three, they made blunders which the
[P01-048] modern student is unable to correct. In the Introduction will be found
[P01-049] brief descriptions of the contents of the Egyptian texts, in which
[P01-050] their general bearing and importance are indicated, and references
[P01-051] given to authoritative editions of texts and translations.
