# Sept

## Overview

Sept (Egyptian *Sopdet*, Greek *Sothis*) is the goddess of the star Sirius, the brightest star in the night sky. Her heliacal rising — when Sirius first becomes visible on the eastern horizon just before dawn after a period of invisibility — marked the beginning of the Egyptian New Year and heralded the annual Nile inundation upon which all Egyptian life depended.

## Identity and Role

Sept personifies the Dog-star (Sirius), whose appearance in the sky was the celestial signal for the flooding of the Nile:

> goddess Sept (Sothis, or the Dog-star) [004-105]

She is the divine herald of the inundation — as the star rises, so [[hapi]] (the Nile) pours forth his waters. Her celestial role placed her among the great gods:

> The goddess of the star Sept (Sothis), the goddess Anqet, Hap (the Nile-god), Shu, Keb, Nut, Osiris, Horus, Isis, and Nephthys [017-106]

The rising of Sothis was also associated with the coming of light and the renewal of life. In the Pyramid Texts, the goddess is described as the spirit whose appearance causes light to shine:

> she was the spirit of the Dog-star which heralded the coming of the Nile flood [006-076]

## Family and Relationships

Sept is closely associated with [[isis]], with whom she was often identified. In the Osirian theology, the star Sothis is the celestial manifestation of Isis, just as [[osiris]] is identified with the constellation Orion:

> Orion and the Dog-star, the former being sacred to Horus and the latter to Isis [023-763]

In the context of divine conception, Sept plays a role in the rebirth of Osiris through [[horus]]:

> Thy sister Isis cometh to thee rejoicing in her love for thee. Thou hast union with her, thy seed entereth her. She conceiveth in the form of the star Septet (Sothis). Horus-Sept issueth from thee in the form of Horus, dweller in the star Septet. [004-105]

This remarkable passage identifies the star Septet (Sothis) as the celestial form in which [[isis]] conceives, and from which Horus-Sept emerges — linking the star goddess directly to the Osirian myth cycle and the divine birth of [[horus]].

She is grouped among the Cataract deities alongside [[khnemu]], [[anqet]], and [[hapi]], reflecting her role in the inundation cycle centered at Elephantine.

## Major Myths and Narratives

**The Herald of the Inundation.** Sept's primary mythic function is heraldic: her appearance in the sky signals the Nile's rise. The heliacal rising of Sirius, which occurred in mid-July in the ancient Egyptian calendar after approximately 70 days of invisibility, was the most carefully observed astronomical event of the year. Priests watched for it from the temple roofs, and its sighting triggered the New Year festival (*Wep Renpet*, "Opening of the Year").

**The Celestial Mother.** In the deepest mystery of the Osirian rebirth, Sept becomes the star-body through which Isis conceives Horus. The text from the Pyramid Age preserves this identification: Isis "conceiveth in the form of the star Septet" [004-105], and from this conception Horus-Sept is born — a fusion of the son-god [[horus]] with the star-goddess's celestial identity.

## Cult and Worship

Sept had no independent cult centre of major scale, but she was worshipped in association with the temples of the Cataract region and at major Isis sanctuaries. Her rising was celebrated at the New Year festival throughout Egypt, with offerings cast into the Nile to coincide with the star's appearance. The goddess was also a protector of the dead, guiding the deceased through the celestial regions as they joined the "imperishable stars" (*ikhemu-seku*).
