# Maat

## Overview

Maat is the Egyptian goddess of truth, law, justice, and cosmic order. She is the personification of the fundamental principle upon which the universe was built — the divine order that governs both the natural world and human society. In the Egyptian worldview, the sun-god [[ra]] himself ruled by Maat, and the king's primary duty was to uphold her.

## Identity and Role

Maat embodies the abstract concept of truth and harmonious order (*maat*) that stands in opposition to *isfet* (chaos, falsehood). She is usually depicted as a woman wearing an ostrich feather on her head — the same feather against which the heart of the deceased was weighed in the Hall of Judgment. She is closely associated with [[thoth]], the god of wisdom and writing:

> In arranging his thoughts and their visible forms Khepera was assisted by the goddess Maat, who is usually regarded as the goddess of law, order, and truth, and in late times was held to be the female counterpart of Thoth, "the heart of the god Ra." [001-197]

Maat's role in creation was foundational: she was the principle of order by which [[khepera]] laid the foundations of the universe:

> I laid the foundation [of things] by Maat, and I made everything which had form. [007-015]

## Family and Relationships

Maat is the daughter of [[ra]] and the female counterpart of [[thoth]]. Together, Thoth and Maat represent the twin aspects of divine wisdom — Thoth as the recording scribe and Maat as the living principle of truth. In the Judgment Hall of Osiris, Thoth records the verdict while Maat's feather determines the fate of the deceased.

In some traditions, Maat is considered the consort of [[thoth]]. Her presence is essential in the solar barque of [[ra]], where she accompanies the sun-god on his daily journey, ensuring the maintenance of cosmic order against the chaos serpent [[apep]].

## Major Myths and Narratives

**The Creation.** At the beginning of time, [[khepera]] (or [[ra]]) was assisted by Maat in bringing order out of the primordial chaos. She was the wisdom by which the creator "laid the foundation" of all things, analogous to the figure of Wisdom in the Biblical Book of Proverbs.

**The Judgment of the Dead.** Maat's most prominent role is in the Hall of the Two Truths (*Maati*), where the heart of the deceased is weighed against her feather:

> Maati, the mysterious (or, hidden) Soul, the Lord of the Two Truths [016-007]

If the heart balances with the feather, the deceased is declared "true of voice" (*maa kheru*) and granted eternal life with [[osiris]]. If the heart is heavier, it is devoured by the beast Ammit.

**The Tongue of Maat.** In funerary ritual, the deceased was required to have the figure of Maat drawn on the tongue, signifying that they would speak only truth in the presence of the gods:

> a figure of Maat (Truth) must be painted on his tongue with green paint. [001-197]

> a figure of the goddess Maat shall be drawn upon his tongue with green paint. [013-008]

**The Chief of Maat.** The god [[thoth]] bears the title "Chief of Maat" in both heaven and earth [021-003], signifying his role as the supreme arbiter of truth and justice in the divine tribunal.

## Cult and Worship

Maat was worshipped throughout Egypt, though she had few dedicated temples — her presence was integrated into every other cult, as all gods and kings were bound to uphold her principle. The king's primary epithet included references to Maat, as seen in the throne name of Rameses II: *User-Maat-Ra-setep-en-Ra* ("Powerful is the Truth of Ra, Chosen of Ra") [016-207].

Judges and magistrates in ancient Egypt were known as "priests of Maat," and they wore a small image of the goddess as a badge of office. Offerings to the gods were accompanied by the presentation of a small figure of Maat, symbolizing that the offering was made in truth and accordance with divine order.
