# Horus

**Horus** (Egyptian **Heru**) is the falcon-headed sky god, one of the most significant deities in the Egyptian pantheon. He appears in multiple forms — as the son of [[Isis]] who avenges [[Osiris]], as the winged sun-disk warrior of [[Edfu]], and as the divine prototype of pharaonic kingship. His right eye is the sun, his left eye the moon.

## Identity and Role

The name Horus encompasses several distinct but related divine manifestations:

- **Horus, son of Isis**: Born to [[Isis]] after [[Osiris]]'s murder. He avenges his father and reclaims the throne from [[Set]].
- **Heru-Behutet**: The warrior of [[Edfu]] who battles [[Ra]]'s enemies, taking the form of a great winged disk.
- **Horus the Child** (Harpocrates): The infant god of healing and protection from venom.

## Family and Relationships

Horus is the son of [[Osiris]] and [[Isis]]. After [[Set]] murdered [[Osiris]], [[Isis]] conceived Horus through magical means. He is thus both the rightful heir to Osiris's throne and the divine avenger. In some traditions, [[Hathor]] is his consort.

In the Edfu theology, Horus is identified as the son of [[Ra]] — "Thou art my exalted son who didst come forth from me" (002-021) — reflecting the syncretism of Ra-Harmachis.

## Major Myths and Narratives

### The Winged Disk

As **Heru-Behutet**, Horus fought against the enemies of [[Ra]]-Harmachis. Taking the form of a great winged disk, he flew up into the sky:

> "Then Heru-Behutet took the form of a great winged disk, and he set himself upon the prow of the boat of Ra. And he placed himself over the enemies of Ra." *(002-045)*

Ra declared that Horus should be called "Behutet" (He of Edfu), and the [[Winged Disk]] became his eternal symbol.

### Battle Against Set

Horus pursued the enemies of [[Ra]] in the form of a lion, slaughtering them across the Nile valley. He captured hundreds of foes with chains, and with **Horus, son of Isis**, he cut off the head of [[Set]] himself (002-149):

> "Then Horus, son of Isis, cut off the head of Set, and the gods of the train of Ra celebrated the victory with hymns of praise." *(002-173)*

### Death and Resurrection

The Metternich Stele records how Horus was stung by a scorpion and died, causing great lamentation. Through the magical intervention of [[Thoth]] and the words of power spoken by [[Isis]], Horus was restored to life — a prototype of resurrection (018-001).

## Cult and Worship

Horus was worshipped throughout Egypt, with major cult centers at [[Edfu]] (as Heru-Behutet), where the great Temple of Horus still stands. The pharaoh was considered the living Horus, and the [[Winged Disk]] became one of Egypt's most ubiquitous protective symbols, placed above temple entrances throughout the land.
