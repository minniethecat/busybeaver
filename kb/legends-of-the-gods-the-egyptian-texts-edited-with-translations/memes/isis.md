# Isis

## Overview

Isis is one of the most important goddesses in the Egyptian pantheon — the divine mother, wife of [[osiris]], and mother of [[horus]]. She embodies magical power, protective motherhood, and queenly authority. Her cult spread throughout Egypt and later across the Greco-Roman world.

## Identity and Role

Isis is the daughter of [[nut]] and [[keb]], sister-wife of [[osiris]], and mother of [[horus]]. She is known as the "Great of Magic" (*Weret Hekau*), wielding spells and incantations powerful enough to heal the sun-god [[ra]] himself. As the archetypal devoted wife and protective mother, she is the central figure in the Osiris myth cycle: she searches for, reassembles, and revives the body of Osiris after his murder by [[set]], and thereafter guards her son Horus from Set's attacks.

Her iconography typically shows a woman wearing a throne-shaped headdress (the hieroglyph for her name) or the solar disk and cow horns borrowed from [[hathor]]. In the Pyramid Texts, Isis and her sister [[nephthys]] appear as the two mourners who guard the dead Osiris:

> Isis, it seems, was shut up in the same house as the Scorpion-god, and the cries which she uttered when she arrived at the door of the house were heard by the Scorpion and his wife [005-131]

> Thus it seems as if Isis, the sister-wife of Osiris, who had the head of Ra, the bull, and who was born to the bull of the sky [005-105]

## Family and Relationships

Isis is the eldest daughter of [[keb]] (Earth) and [[nut]] (Sky), born as one of the Great Ennead of Heliopolis:

> the Nile-god, Shu, Keb, Nut, Osiris, Isis, Nephthys, and Horus. [004-106]

She is the devoted wife of [[osiris]] and the fiercely protective mother of [[horus]]. Her sister [[nephthys]] is her constant companion in mourning rituals and protective magic. Together they stand as the two *kites* (mourning women) who guard the bier of Osiris.

After the murder of Osiris by [[set]], Isis hides the infant Horus in the marshes of the Delta, shielding him from Set's attacks through her magic. The texts portray her as alternately a tender mother and a formidable sorceress:

> Horus and Isis capturing the Hippopotamus-fiend [002-037]

> given over to Isis and her son Horus, who were to avenge the murder of Osiris [002-141]

## Major Myths and Narratives

**The Osiris Cycle.** After Set murders [[osiris]], Isis searches the length of Egypt to find his dismembered body. She reassembles it and, through her magic, conceives [[horus]]. She then protects the infant Horus from [[set]], who continues to threaten the child who will one day claim the throne.

**The Contendings of Horus and Set.** Isis supports her son [[horus]] in his legal and physical battles against [[set]] for the throne of Egypt. When Horus is stung by a scorpion, Isis heals him. When Set tries to assault Horus, Isis intervenes with trickery:

> the presence of Ra and Isis, and be dragged Set by his feet through the land [002-143]

> fight between Horus, son of Isis, and Set. In the oldest times the followers of Horus and Set fought periodically [002-150]

**The Secret Name of Ra.** Isis crafts a serpent from the spittle of the aging [[ra]] and causes it to bite him. In agony, Ra offers Isis his secret name in exchange for healing, granting her ultimate magical power. The text notes that:

> Thoth and Isis, and holds Typhonic beasts at bay [005-079]

**The Scorpion Sting.** When Horus is stung by a scorpion while Isis and [[nephthys]] are away, Isis returns and heals him with her magic — a myth widely used in healing spells and protective amulets throughout Egyptian history:

> Isis, it seems, was shut up in the same house as the Scorpion-god [005-133]

> through the sting of a scorpion. Isis, as we have already seen, had access to powers of healing [005-136]

## Cult and Worship

Isis was worshipped throughout Egypt, with major cult centres at [[heliopolis]], Behbeit el-Hagar in the Delta, and Philae on the southern border. Her cult at Philae survived into the 6th century CE — the last functioning Egyptian temple.

Her worship spread far beyond Egypt: during the Ptolemaic and Roman periods, the cult of Isis became one of the most widespread mystery religions of the Mediterranean world, with temples in Rome, Pompeii, Athens, and as far as London. Her festival cycle re-enacted the Osiris myth, and her priests were famous for their ritual purity and knowledge of sacred texts.

The Book of the Dead contains numerous hymns to Isis, and her image as the nursing mother (*Isis Lactans*) profoundly influenced early Christian iconography of the Virgin Mary:

> to summon the aid of Isis to keep away the fiends, and to make the deceased secure [002-182]
