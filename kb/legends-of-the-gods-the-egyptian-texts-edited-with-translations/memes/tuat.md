# Tuat

## Overview

The **Tuat** (also *Duat*) was the ancient Egyptian underworld — a subterranean realm of darkness, water, and mystery through which the sun-god [[Ra]] traveled each night in his Boat of Millions of Years. Unlike the later Greek and Christian conceptions of Hell as a place of punishment, the Tuat was a complex domain containing both paradisiacal regions (such as Sekhet-Aaru, the Field of Reeds) and zones of danger populated by serpents, demons, and the fiends of chaos. It was also the kingdom of [[Osiris]], where the justified dead enjoyed eternal life.

## Core Doctrine

### The Night Journey of Ra

Each evening at sunset, Ra entered the western horizon and began his perilous twelve-hour voyage through the Tuat. In his boat, accompanied by a crew of gods and the justified dead, he traversed the darkness, bringing light and life to the inhabitants of the underworld. The journey was opposed by [[apep]] (Apophis), the great serpent of chaos who sought to devour the sun and plunge the cosmos into eternal darkness.

### The Realm of the Dead

The Tuat was simultaneously the destination of the dead. Those who passed the judgment in the Hall of Osiris and were declared *maakheru* ("true of voice") could dwell in the Tuat as blessed spirits (*akhu*), enjoying offerings and participating in Ra's nightly journey. The Tuat contained the [[sekhmet|Sekhet-Aaru]] (Field of Reeds), where the deceased could continue an idealized version of earthly life.

### Geography

Egyptian funerary texts, particularly the *Book of Gates* and the *Amduat*, describe the Tuat as divided into twelve regions corresponding to the twelve hours of night. Each region had its own gate, guardian deities, and dangers. The Tuat was bounded by mountains and traversed by a celestial Nile.

## Significance

The Tuat represents one of the most elaborate underworld conceptions in ancient religion. Its detailed cartography — recorded in royal tombs and on papyri — reflects the Egyptian obsession with order (*maat*). Even in the realm of death, every region, gate, and hour was named, measured, and governed. The Tuat was not chaos but structured cosmos — darkness tamed by divine knowledge and ritual power.
