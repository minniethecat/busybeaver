# Koptos

**Koptos** (Egyptian **Gebtu**, modern **Qift**) was a city of Upper Egypt near the Wadi Hammamat, a vital trade route to the Red Sea. It was the cult center of the fertility god **Menu** (Greek Min) and served as one of the burial sites of Osiris's dismembered body.

## Overview

Koptos held strategic and religious significance throughout Egyptian history. As the eastern terminus of the Wadi Hammamat route, it controlled access to the gold mines and quarries of the Eastern Desert. Religiously, it was the principal sanctuary of Menu, the ithyphallic fertility god, and a site where Isis was said to have interred a portion of Osiris's body after his murder by [[Set]].

## The City of Mourning

The most distinctive legend associated with Koptos concerns its name. According to the Greek historian Plutarch, the city's name was reinterpreted through the myth of Osiris:

> "He states that the name has since been called 'Koptos,' or the 'city of mourning.'" *(023-463)*

This etymology links the city's name to the Egyptian word for mourning (*kep*), reflecting the grief of Isis as she travelled through Egypt burying the scattered remains of Osiris. The texts confirm that one of the body parts was interred there:

> "The portions of his body are: Koptos..." *(023-595)*

## The Cult of Menu, Lord of Koptos

Menu was the principal deity of Koptos, described in the magical texts with reverent invocations:

> "O Menu, Lord of Coptos, the child of the White Shat..." *(021-155–156)*

Menu was depicted as an ithyphallic mummiform figure, a god of male fertility and agricultural abundance. His cult at Koptos was among the oldest in Upper Egypt, and his title "Lord of Coptos" permanently bound the god to his city sanctuary.

## Custom of the Donkey Sacrifice

Plutarch records a distinctive ritual custom practiced at Koptos:

> "The inhabitants of Koptos are in the habit of sacrificing a donkey..." *(023-848)*

This ritual, throwing a donkey from a height, was associated with the cult of Set, whose theriomorphic form was the donkey. The custom at Koptos may have been a ritual enactment of the punishment of Set, connecting the city to the broader Osirian mythological cycle through a unique local observance.

## In the Texts

Koptos appears both in the magical invocations to Menu and in Plutarch's detailed account of the Osiris burial sites. The burial at Koptos forms part of the tradition that each city where Isis buried a piece of Osiris could claim a tomb of the god, explaining the multiple sepulchres of Osiris across Egypt. This pattern of distributed burial legitimized local cult centers and their claims to Osirian sanctity.
