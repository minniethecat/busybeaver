# Boat of Ra

The **Boat of Ra** (the **Solar Barque** or **Boat of Millions of Years**) is the celestial vessel in which [[Ra]] travels across the sky by day and through the [[Tuat|underworld]] by night. It is one of the most pervasive symbols in Egyptian religion — the model for royal funerary barques and the framework for understanding the daily solar cycle.

## Core Doctrine

Two boats carry [[Ra]] through his daily cycle. At dawn he embarks in **Sekti** (the morning boat), sailing across the heavens. At sunset he transfers to **Matet** (the evening boat) for the perilous night journey through the [[Tuat]]. The boat is crewed by gods who row and defend it against [[Apep]], the serpent of chaos who attacks nightly:

> "The gods who formed its crew resumed their rowing, and the Boat of Ra moved onward, traversing the waters of Nu." *(001-008)*

## Sources and References

The Boat of Ra appears throughout the corpus, especially in the **Destruction of Mankind** legend. After Ra's abdication from earthly rule, he ascends to heaven in his boat, establishing the eternal solar cycle (001-008). Two boats are depicted in the tomb of Seti I — immediately beneath the belly of the Cow of Heaven, the boats called Semketet and Mantchet are painted (001-007).

The ritual iconography of the solar barque is precisely described:

> "A boat with a rudder and a double shrine shall be therein, and Aten (the Disk) shall be above it, and Ra shall be in it." *(002-001)*

This detailed description from the Edfu texts shows how the abstract solar myth was concretized in temple decoration and ritual objects.

## Significance

The Boat of Ra transforms the solar movement concept into a concrete narrative. Sunrise and sunset become a divine journey, and the barque serves as model for funerary boats carrying deceased kings to join [[Ra]].
