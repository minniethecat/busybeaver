# Divine Company

## Overview

The **Divine Company** (Egyptian: *Paaut*) was the great assembly of gods in ancient Egyptian religion, convened in the Great House of Heliopolis. Together with the *Tchatcha* (Administrators of Heaven) and the *Lords of Truth*, the Divine Company formed the celestial council that acknowledged royal and divine sovereignty, participated in judgment, and maintained cosmic order. The concept appears throughout the funerary texts, where the deceased seeks acceptance into this august body.

## Core Doctrine

The Divine Company served three primary functions in Egyptian cosmology:

1. **Sovereignty Acknowledgment**: The Company assembled to recognize the authority of the sun-god Ra and, by extension, the deceased king who identified with him. This acknowledgment was essential for legitimate rule in both the celestial and earthly realms.

2. **Judicial Function**: As part of the tribunal that judged the dead, the Company — together with the Lords of Truth — witnessed the weighing of the heart and confirmed the verdict of justification (*maakheru*).

3. **Cosmic Maintenance**: The Company participated in the daily renewal of cosmic order (*maat*), opposing the forces of chaos (*isefet*) embodied by Apep and the Sebau fiends.

## Sources and References

The Company of the Gods appears primarily in the funerary spells and hymns preserved in the corpus:

> "Well-pleasing [is thy] face, O Prince of the Company of the Gods..." [016-050]

> "...my brethren, the Prince of the Company of the Gods, and the Great Company of the Gods, and thou art the beloved one..." [016-083]

> "...within the house of Keb, and the Company of the Gods, the Lords of Thebes, the beloved..." [016-117]

## Significance

The Divine Company represents the Egyptian conception of collective divine authority. Unlike the monotheistic divine court, the Egyptian council was a pluralistic body reflecting the polycentric nature of Egyptian religion — gods from different nomes and cult centers (Thebes, Heliopolis, Memphis) all had representation. The Company thus served as both a theological concept and a political metaphor, mirroring the earthly royal court.
