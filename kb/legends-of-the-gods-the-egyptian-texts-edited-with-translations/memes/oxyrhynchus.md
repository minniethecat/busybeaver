# Oxyrhynchus

**Oxyrhynchus** (Egyptian **Per-Medjed**, modern **el-Bahnasa**) was a city in Upper Egypt, capital of the nineteenth nome, renowned throughout the ancient world for its distinctive cult of the **Oxyrhynchus fish** — a sharp-nosed, sturgeon-like fish that was held sacred and strictly tabooed as food.

## Overview

The city derived its Greek name from the Oxyrhynchus fish (*Mormyrus* or *Medjed* in Egyptian), which was the object of a potent religious taboo. The inhabitants of Oxyrhynchus refused to touch, eat, or harm this fish under any circumstances, a prohibition that distinguished them from other Egyptians and became famous across the Mediterranean world.

## The Fish Taboo

Plutarch devotes particular attention to the Oxyrhynchus fish and its associated prohibition:

> "The inhabitants of Oxyrhynchus will not touch any thing in which there is a fish." *(023-187)*

The fish gave its name to the city — "Oxyrhynchus" means "sharp-nosed" in Greek — and the local taboo against consuming it was absolute. This prohibition extended beyond food to any contact with fish, making the inhabitants of Oxyrhynchus unique among Egyptians in their dietary restrictions.

## The Osirian Connection

The veneration of the Oxyrhynchus fish was rooted in the myth of Osiris's dismemberment. According to Plutarch, when [[Set]] dismembered the body of [[Osiris]] into fourteen pieces, the phallus of the god was thrown into the Nile and consumed by three fishes — one of which was the Oxyrhynchus:

> "The Oxyrhynchus Fish, from whence the city received its name, was among those that devoured the phallus of Osiris." *(023-189)*

Because the fish had consumed a part of the god's body, it became sacred. What could have been seen as an act of desecration was transformed into sanctification — the fish became a vessel of the divine, and those who honoured it by abstention participated in the veneration of Osiris:

> "At Oxyrhynchus, fish which above all others are held in honour..." *(023-587)*

## In the Texts

The Oxyrhynchus fish cult exemplifies the Egyptian practice of local animal worship tied to mythological events. Each nome had its own sacred animals, taboos, and origin stories, and Oxyrhynchus's fish taboo was among the most famous of these local cults. The city later became famous in the modern era for the Oxyrhynchus Papyri — a vast trove of Greek and Latin documents discovered at the site, though these postdate the mythological period described in the corpus.
