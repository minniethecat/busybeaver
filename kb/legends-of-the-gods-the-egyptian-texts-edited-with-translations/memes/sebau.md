# Sebau

## Overview

The **Sebau** (also *Seba* or *Sma/Smai*) were the serpent fiends and demons of darkness in ancient Egyptian mythology. These hostile beings populated the deepest regions of the [[tuat|Tuat]] (Underworld) and the watery chaos beyond the created world. They were the allies and servants of [[apep]] (Apophis), the great chaos serpent, and of [[set]], the god of disorder, in their unending war against the forces of [[maat]] (cosmic order). The defeat of the Sebau was celebrated as one of the principal victories of [[ra|Ra]] and [[horus|Horus]].

## Core Doctrine

### Nature and Origin

The Sebau were primordial beings of chaos (*isefet*), pre-dating the creation of the ordered cosmos. When Ra emerged from the [[nu|World-Ocean]] and established the created world, the Sebau retreated to the margins — the dark abyss, the depths of the Tuat, and the hostile deserts beyond Egypt's borders. From these margins they periodically emerged to assault the sun-boat and threaten cosmic stability.

### The War Against Ra

The mythological cycle recounts multiple campaigns against the Sebau. [[heru-behutet|Heru-Behutet]] (Horus of Edfu) fought them as the winged sun-disk, spearing them with metal darts and dragging them in chains:

> "And Heru-Behutet looked and saw one [only] of these Sebau fiends there on the spot, and he was by himself. And he threw one metal dart, and brought (or, dragged) them along straightway, and he slaughtered them..." [015-405]

### Set's Alliance

After his own defeat, [[set|Set]] rallied the surviving fiends: "he gathered about him the Smai and Seba fiends at the Lake of Meh, and waged war once more against Horus." The Sebau thus represent the recurring nature of chaos — never fully destroyed, always regrouping for another assault.

### Protective Measures

The [[uraeus]] (sacred cobra), worn on the brow of Ra and the pharaoh, was specifically deployed to terrify the Sebau: "he made the goddess Nekhebit and the goddess Uatchit to be with him in the form of serpents, so that they might make the Sebau fiends to quake in [all] their limbs."

## Significance

The Sebau embody an essential Egyptian theological insight: chaos is not eliminated by a single act of creation but must be perpetually subdued. Every sunrise, every royal ritual, every magical spell was a re-enactment of the primordial victory over the Sebau. This cycle of threat and triumph gave the Egyptian religious system its dynamic, recursive character — order required eternal vigilance.
