# Amen

## Overview

Amen (Egyptian *imn*, "the Hidden One") is one of the most important gods of the Egyptian pantheon. Originating as the local god of Thebes, he rose to supreme national prominence as **Amen-Ra**, the king of the gods. He is described as a beneficent creator god, "born of Mut, begotten of Heru-khuti," and is addressed as "Father Amen, the Lord of the thrones of the Two Lands" [016-118], [016-147].

## Identity and Attributes

Amen's name literally means "the hidden one" [023-297], reflecting his nature as an invisible, omnipresent creative force. He is closely associated with the **ram**, which serves as his sacred animal [023-130]. In his syncretic form as **Amen-Ra**, he combines the hidden, primordial power of Amen with the visible, life-giving force of the sun god Ra.

The god is invoked as a healer and protector. In the Bentresh legend, the prince of Bekhten appeals to the king of Egypt to send "the god Amen" to cure his afflicted daughter, recognizing the god's power to expel evil spirits [003-043], [003-060].

Amen is called "the son of Amen, born of Mut, begotten of Heru-khuti" in royal titularies, reflecting his role as the divine father of the pharaoh [016-118]. The feet of "Amen-Horus" are listed among the sacred body parts in ritual texts [005-109].

## Major Myths and Narratives

### The Bentresh Legend

The most detailed narrative featuring Amen occurs in the Bentresh legend. During the reign of Rameses II, the prince of Bekhten sent an envoy to Egypt seeking a physician for his sister Bentresh, who was afflicted by an evil spirit. The Egyptian king consulted **Khensu Nefer-hetep**, the son of Amen, who gave his protection to another form of Khensu that journeyed to Bekhten [003-043], [003-062].

> "His Majesty was celebrating a festival of Amen-Ra in the Temple of Luxor" [003-043]

The spirit possessing Bentresh recognized the authority of Amen and his son Khensu, declaring: "I am thy servant, and I will depart unto the place whence I came" [003-072]. The prince of Bekhten, impressed by the power of the Egyptian god, kept the statue of Khensu in Bekhten for three years and nine months before returning it to Egypt.

### Amen and the Pharaoh

The pharaohs of the New Kingdom regarded Amen as their divine father. Royal protocols describe the king as "the beneficent god, the son of Amen," affirming the close bond between the god and the throne [016-118]. The Festival of Amen was one of the most important state celebrations, during which the king himself participated in ritual performances [016-180].

## Cult and Worship

Amen's primary cult centre was **Thebes** (modern Luxor), where the great Temple of Amen at Karnak became one of the largest religious structures in the ancient world. The Temple of Luxor was a secondary centre, connected to Karnak by an avenue of sphinxes.

The worship of Amen was characterized by:
- **Annual festivals**: The Festival of Amen, coinciding with key agricultural dates [016-180]
- **Royal patronage**: Pharaohs made offerings and built monuments to honour "Father Amen" [016-147]
- **Theban triad**: Amen, Mut, and Khensu formed the sacred family of Thebes
- **Oracular practices**: The god's statue gave oracular responses through ritual movements
