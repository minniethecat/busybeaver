# Hermopolis

**Hermopolis** (Egyptian **Khemenu**, or **Khmunu**) was the city of [[Thoth]], the god of wisdom, writing, and magic, located in Middle Egypt. Its Egyptian name Khemenu means "the City of Eight," referencing the Ogdoad, the eight primordial deities of the Hermopolitan creation myth. In the Greek period it was known as Hermopolis Magna, named after Hermes, the Greek equivalent of Thoth.

## Overview

Hermopolis ranked among the greatest cult centers of ancient Egypt, mentioned alongside [[Heliopolis]], [[Memphis]], [[Abydos]], and [[Suten-Henen]] (Herakleopolis) as one of the principal religious cities. Thoth's temple at Hermopolis was a center of learning, scribal training, and philosophical inquiry, and the city was celebrated as the seat of the "Two Muses" who presided over the arts and sciences.

## Mythological Significance

The city was inseparably identified with Thoth, who is described in the texts with the title:

> "Thoth, the Twice Great, Lord of Khemenu (Hermopolis), the Chief of the..." *(019-018)*

This title — "Twice Great, Lord of Khemenu" — permanently links the deity to his cult city. The epithet "Twice Great" (*megas, megas* in Greek) reflects Thoth's dual nature as the master of both divine and earthly wisdom, a title inherited by the later Hermetic tradition. The city's creation myth centered on the Ogdoad, eight primeval frog and serpent deities who existed before the creation of the world.

The texts identify Hermopolis as one of the burial sites of Osiris's dismembered body, with Isis burying a piece there during her journey across Egypt (023-595). The city also appears in the list of the great cult centers:

> "One of the greatest cult centers... Heliopolis, Memphis, Hermopolis, Herakleopolis, Abydos..." *(016-018, 017-038)*

## The Two Muses

Plutarch, the Greek historian who recorded the Osiris myth, identified Hermopolis as the home of the two Muses — Isis and Nephthys — who were associated with philosophy and the arts:

> "The former of the two Muses at Hermopolis..." *(023-070)*

The reference situates Hermopolis as a center not only of religious worship but of intellectual and artistic activity, befitting the city of Thoth.

## In the Texts

Beyond the Thoth references, Hermopolis appears in the nome listing of Lower Egypt as "Hermopolis of Lower Egypt" (023-597). The city's prominence in Egyptian religious geography is confirmed by its regular mention alongside Heliopolis and Memphis as one of the foundational cult centers of Egyptian civilization. The temple library of Hermopolis, though not explicitly described in our surviving texts, was renowned throughout the ancient world.
