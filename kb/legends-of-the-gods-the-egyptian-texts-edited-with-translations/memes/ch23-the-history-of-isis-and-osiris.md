#### WITH EXPLANATIONS OF THE SAME, COLLECTED BY PLUTARCH, AND SUPPLEMENTED BY HIS OWN VIEWS.

[023-001] I. Though it be the wise man's duty, O Clea,[FN#259] to apply to the
[023-002] gods for every good thing which he hopes to enjoy, yet ought he more
[023-003] especially to pray to them for their assistance in his search after
[023-004] that knowledge which more immediately regards themselves, as far as
[023-005] such knowledge may be attained, inasmuch as there is nothing which they
[023-006] can bestow more truly beneficial to mankind, or more worthy themselves,
[023-007] than truth. For whatever other good things are indulged to the wants
[023-008] of men, they have all, properly speaking, no relation to, and are of a
[023-009] nature quite different from, that of their divine donors. For 'tis not
[023-010] the abundance of their gold and silver, nor the command of the thunder,
[023-011] but wisdom and knowledge which constitute the power and happiness of
[023-012] those heavenly beings. It is therefore well observed by Homer (Iliad,
[023-013] xiii. 354), and indeed with more propriety than be usually talks of the
[023-014] gods, when, speaking of Zeus and Poseidon, he tells us that both were
[023-015] descended from the same parents, and born in the same region, but that
[023-016] Zeus was the elder and knew most; plainly intimating thereby that the
[023-017] empire of the former was more august and honourable than that of his
[023-018] brother, as by means of his age he was his superior, and more advanced
[023-019] in wisdom and science. Nay, 'tis my opinion, I own, that even the
[023-020] blessedness of that eternity which is the portion of the Deity himself
[023-021] consists in that universal knowledge of all nature which accompanies
[023-022] it; for setting this aside, eternity might be more properly styled an
[023-023] endless duration than an enjoyment of existence.

[023-024] Delphicus.

[023-025] II. To desire, therefore, and covet after truth, those truths more
[023-026] especially which concern the divine nature, is to aspire to be
[023-027] partakers of that nature itself, and to profess that all our studies
[023-028] and inquiries are devoted to the acquisition of holiness. This
[023-029] occupation is surely more truly religious than any external
[023-030] purifications or mere service of the temple can be. But more
[023-031] especially must such a disposition of mind be highly acceptable to that
[023-032] goddess to whose service you are dedicated, for her especial
[023-033] characteristics are wisdom and foresight, and her very name seems to
[023-034] express the peculiar relation which she bears to knowledge. For
[023-035] "Isis"[FN#260] is a Greek word, and means "knowledge," and
[023-036] "Typhon,"[FN#261] the name of her professed adversary, is also a Greek
[023-037] word, and means "pride and insolence." This latter name is well
[023-038] adapted to one who, full of ignorance and error, tears in pieces and
[023-039] conceals that holy doctrine which the goddess collects, compiles, and
[023-040] delivers to those who aspire after the most perfect participation in
[023-041] the divine nature. This doctrine inculcates a steady perseverance in
[023-042] one uniform and temperate course of life, and an abstinence from
[023-043] particular kinds of foods, as well as from all indulgence of the carnal
[023-044] appetite, and it restrains the intemperate and voluptuous part within
[023-045] due bounds, and at the same time habituates her votaries to undergo
[023-046] those austere and rigid ceremonies which their religion obliges them to
[023-047] observe. The end and aim of all these toils and labours is the
[023-048] attainment of the knowledge of the First and Chief Being, who alone is
[023-049] the object of the understanding of the mind; and this knowledge the
[023-050] goddess invites us to seek after, as being near and dwelling
[023-051] continually with her. And this also is what the very name of her
[023-052] temple promiseth to us, that is to say, the knowledge and understanding
[023-053] of the eternal and self-existent Being (tou ontas)-now, it is called
[023-054] "Iseion," which suggests that if we approach the temple of the goddess
[023-055] rightly, and with purity, we shall obtain the knowledge of that eternal
[023-056] and self-existent Being (to on).

[023-058] III. The goddess Isis is said by some authors to be the
[023-059] daughter[FN#262] of Hermes, [FN#263] and by others of Prometheus, both
[023-060] of them famous for their philosophic turn of mind. The latter is
[023-061] supposed to have first taught mankind wisdom and foresight, as the
[023-062] former is reputed to have invented letters and music.

[FN#262] According to the Egyptian Heliopolitan doctrine, Isis was the
[023-063] daughter of Keb, the Earth-god, and Nut, the Sky-goddess; she was the
[023-064] wife of Osiris, mother of Horus, and sister of Set and Nephthys.

[023-065] mathematics, &c. He was the "heart of Ra," the scribe of the gods, and
[023-066] he uttered the words which created the world; he composed the "words of
[023-067] power," or magical formulae which were beneficial for the dead, and the
[023-068] religious works which were used by souls in their journey from this
[023-069] world to the next.

[023-070] They likewise call the former of the two Muses at Hermopolis[FN#264]
[023-071] Isis as well as Dikaiosune,[FN#265] she being none other, it is said,
[023-072] than Wisdom pointing out the knowledge of divine truths to her
[023-073] votaries, the true Hierophori and Hierostoli. Now, by the former of
[023-074] these are meant such who carry about them looked up in their souls, as
[023-075] in a chest, the sacred doctrine concerning the gods, purified from all
[023-076] such superfluities as superstition may have added thereto. And the
[023-077] holy apparel with which the Hierostoli adorn the statues of these
[023-078] deities, which is partly of a dark and gloomy and partly of a more
[023-079] bright and shining colour, seems aptly enough to represent the notions
[023-080] which this doctrine teaches us to entertain of the divine nature
[023-081] itself, partly clear and partly obscure. And inasmuch as the devotees
[023-082] of Isis after their decease are wrapped up in these sacred vestments,
[023-083] is not this intended to signify that this holy doctrine still abides
[023-084] with them, and that this alone accompanies them in another life? For
[023-085] as 'tis not the length of the beard or the coarseness of the habit
[023-086] which makes a philosopher, so neither will these frequent shavings, or
[023-087] the mere wearing of a linen vestment, constitute a votary of Isis. He
[023-088] alone is a true servant or follower of this goddess who, after he has
[023-089] heard, and has been made acquainted in a proper manner with the history
[023-090] of the actions of these gods, searches into the hidden truths which lie
[023-091] concealed under them, and examines the whole by the dictates of reason
[023-092] and philosophy.

[023-095] IV. Nor, indeed, ought such an examination to be looked on as
[023-096] unnecessary whilst there are so many ignorant of the true reason even
[023-097] of the most ordinary rites observed by the Egyptian priests, such as
[023-098] their shavings[FN#266] and wearing linen garments. Some, indeed, there
[023-099] are who never trouble themselves to think at all about these matters,
[023-100] whilst others rest satisfied with the most superficial accounts of
[023-101] them: "They pay a peculiar veneration to the sheep,[FN#267] therefore
[023-102] they think it their duty not only to abstain from eating its flesh, but
[023-103] likewise from wearing its wool. They are continually mourning for
[023-104] their gods, therefore they shave themselves. The light azure blossom
[023-105] of the flax resembles the clear and bloomy colour of the ethereal sky,
[023-106] therefore they wear linen"; whereas the true reason of the institution
[023-107] and observation of these rites is but one, and that common to all of
[023-108] them, namely, the extraordinary notions which they entertain of
[023-109] cleanliness, persuaded as they are, according to the saying of Plato,
[023-110] "none but the pure ought to approach the pure." Now, no superfluity of
[023-111] our food, and no excrementitious substance, is looked upon by them as
[023-112] pure and clean; such, however, are all kinds of wool and down, our hair
[023-113] and our nails. It would be the highest absurdity, therefore, for those
[023-114] who, whilst; they are in a course of purification, are at so much pains
[023-115] to take off the hair from every part of their own bodies, at the same
[023-116] time to clothe themselves with that of other animals. So when we are
[023-117] told by Hesiod "not to pare our nails whilst we are present at the
[023-118] festivals of the gods,"[FN#268] we ought to understand that he intended
[023-119] hereby to inculcate that purity wherewith we ought to come prepared
[023-120] before we enter upon any religious duty, that we have not to make
[023-121] ourselves clean whilst we ought to be occupied in attending to the
[023-122] solemnity itself. Now, with regard to flax, this springs out of the
[023-123] immortal earth itself; and not only produces a fruit fit for food, but
[023-124] moreover furnishes a light and neat sort of clothing, extremely
[023-125] agreeable to the wearer, adapted to all the seasons of the year, and
[023-126] not in the least subject, as is said, to produce or nourish vermin; but
[023-127] more of this in another place.

[023-129] bodies shaved off" (No. 10,188, col. 1), but they are also ordered to
[023-130] wear fillets of rams' wool on their heads.

[023-136] V. Now, the priests are so scrupulous in endeavouring to avoid
[023-137] everything which may tend to the increase of the above-mentioned
[023-138] excrementitious substances, that, on this account, they abstain not
[023-139] only from most sorts of pulse, and from the flesh of sheep and swine,
[023-140] but likewise, in their more solemn purifications, they even exclude
[023-141] salt from their meals. This they do for many reasons, but chiefly
[023-142] because it whets their appetites, and incites them to eat more than
[023-143] they otherwise would. Now, as to salt being accounted impure because,
[023-144] as Aristagoras tells us, many little insects are caught in it whilst it
[023-145] is hardening, and are thereby killed therein-this view is wholly
[023-146] trifling and absurd. From these same motives also they give the Apis
[023-147] Bull his water from a well specially set apart for the purpose,[FN#269]
[023-148] and they prevent him altogether from drinking of the Nile, not indeed
[023-149] that they regard the river as impure, and polluted because of the
[023-150] crocodiles which are in it, as some pretend, for there is nothing which
[023-151] the Egyptians hold in greater veneration than the Nile, but because its
[023-152] waters are observed to be particularly nourishing[FN#270] and
[023-153] fattening. And they strive to prevent fatness in Apis as well as in
[023-154] themselves, for they are anxious that their bodies should sit as light
[023-155] and easy about their souls as possible, and that their mortal part
[023-156] should not oppress and weigh down the divine and immortal.

[FN#269] It is quite possible that Apis drank from a special well, but
[023-157] the water in it certainly came from the Nile by infiltration. In all
[023-158] the old wells at Memphis the water sinks as the Nile sinks, and rises
[023-159] as it rises.

[023-161] VI. The priests of the Sun at Heliopolis[FN#271] never carry wine into
[023-162] their temples, for they regard it as indecent for those who are devoted
[023-163] to the service of any god to indulge in the drinking of wine whilst
[023-164] they are under the immediate inspection of their Lord and King.[FN#272]
[023-165] The priests of the other deities are not so scrupulous in this respect,
[023-166] for they use it, though sparingly. During their more solemn
[023-167] purifications they abstain from wine wholly, and they give themselves
[023-168] up entirely to study and meditation, and to the hearing and teaching of
[023-169] those divine truths which treat of the divine nature. Even the kings,
[023-170] who are likewise priests, only partake of wine in the measure which is
[023-171] prescribed for them in the sacred books, as we are told by Hecataeus.
[023-172] This custom was only introduced during the reign of Psammetichus, and
[023-173] before that time they drank no wine at all. If they used it at any
[023-174] time in pouring out libations to the gods, it was not because they
[023-175] looked upon it as being acceptable to them for its own sake, but they
[023-176] poured it out over their altars as the blood of their enemies who had
[023-177] in times past fought against them. For they believe the vine to have
[023-178] first sprung out of the earth after it was fattened by the bodies of
[023-179] those who fell in the wars against the gods. And this, they say, is
[023-180] the reason why drinking its juice in great quantities makes men mad and
[023-181] beside themselves, filling them, as it were, with the blood of their
[023-182] own ancestors. These things are thus related by Eudoxus in the second
[023-183] book of his Travels, as he had them from the priests themselves.

[023-184] great solar cult of Egypt. It is the "On" of the Bible.

[FN#272] The Sun-god was called Ra.

[023-185] VII. As to sea-fish, the Egyptians in general do not abstain from all
[023-186] kinds of them, but some from one sort and some from another. Thus, for
[023-187] example, the inhabitants of Oxyrhynchus[FN#273] will not touch any that
[023-188] have been taken with an angle; for as they pay especial reverence to
[023-189] the Oxyrhynchus Fish,[FN#274] from whence they derive their name, they
[023-190] are afraid lest perhaps the hook may be defiled by having been at some
[023-191] time or other employed in catching their favourite fish. The people of
[023-192] Syene[FN#275] in like manner abstain from the Phagrus Fish[FN#276]; for
[023-193] as this fish is observed by them to make his first appearance upon
[023-194] their coasts just as the Nile begins to overflow, they pay special
[023-195] regard to these voluntary messengers as it were of that most joyful
[023-196] news. The priests, indeed, entirely abstain from all sorts in
[023-197] general.[FN#277] Therefore, upon the ninth day of the first month,
[023-198] when all the rest of the Egyptians are obliged by their religion to eat
[023-199] a fried fish before the door of their houses, they only burn them, not
[023-200] tasting them at all. For this custom they give two reasons: the first
[023-201] and most curious, as falling in with the sacred philosophy of Osiris
[023-202] and Typhon, will be more properly explained in another place. The
[023-203] second, that which is most obvious and manifest, is that fish is
[023-204] neither a dainty nor even a necessary kind of food, a fact which seems
[023-205] to be abundantly confirmed by the writings of Homer, who never makes
[023-206] either the delicate Pheacians or the Ithacans (though both peoples were
[023-207] islanders) to feed upon fish, nor even the companions of Ulysses during
[023-208] their long and most tedious voyage, till they were reduced thereto by
[023-209] extreme necessity. In short, they consider the sea to have been forced
[023-210] out of the earth by the power of fire, and therefore to lie out of
[023-211] nature's confines; and they regard it not as a part of the world, or
[023-212] one of the elements, but as a preternatural and corrupt and morbid
[023-213] excrement.

[023-218] VIII. This much may be depended upon: the, religious rites and
[023-219] ceremonies of the Egyptians were never instituted upon irrational
[023-220] grounds, never built upon mere fable and superstition, but founded with
[023-221] a view to promote the morality and happiness of those who were to
[023-222] observe them, or at least to preserve the memory of some valuable piece
[023-223] of history, or to represent to us some of the phenomena of nature. As
[023-224] concerning the abhorrence which is expressed for onions, it is wholly
[023-225] improbable that this detestation is owing to the loss of Diktys, who,
[023-226] whilst he was under the guardianship of Isis, is supposed to have
[023-227] fallen into the river and to have been drowned as he was reaching after
[023-228] a bunch of them. No, the true reason of their abstinence from onions
[023-229] is because they are observed to flourish most and to be in the greatest
[023-230] vigour at the wane of the moon, and also because they are entirely
[023-231] useless to them either in their feasts[FN#278] or in their times of
[023-232] abstinence and purification, for in the former case they make tears
[023-233] come from those who use them, and in the latter they create thirst.
[023-234] For much the same reason they likewise look upon the pig as an impure
[023-235] animal, and to be avoided, observing it to be most apt to engender upon
[023-236] the decrease of the moon, and they think that those who drink its milk
[023-237] are more subject to leprosy and such-like cutaneous diseases than
[023-238] others. The custom of abstaining from the flesh of the pig[FN#279] is
[023-239] not always observed, for those who sacrifice a sow to Typhon once a
[023-240] year, at the full moon, afterwards eat its flesh. The reason they give
[023-241] for this practice is this: Typhon being in pursuit of this animal at
[023-242] that season of the moon, accidentally found the wooden chest wherein
[023-243] was deposited the body of Osiris, which he immediately pulled to
[023-244] pieces. This story, however, is not generally admitted, there being
[023-245] some who look upon it, as they do many other relations of the same
[023-246] kind, as founded upon some mistake or misrepresentation. All agree,
[023-247] however, in saying that so great was the abhorrence which the ancient
[023-248] Egyptians expressed for whatever tended to promote luxury, expense, and
[023-249] voluptuousness, that in order to expose it as much as possible they
[023-250] erected a column in one of the temples of Thebes, full of curses
[023-251] against their king Meinis, who first drew them off from their former
[023-252] frugal and parsimonious course of life. The immediate cause for the
[023-253] erection of the pillar is thus given: Technatis,[FN#280] the father of
[023-254] Bocchoris, leading an army against the Arabians, and his baggage and
[023-255] provisions not coming up to him as soon as he expected, was therefore
[023-256] obliged to eat some of the very poor food which was obtainable, and
[023-257] having eaten, he lay down on the bare ground and slept very soundly.
[023-258] This gave him a great affection for a mean and frugal diet, and induced
[023-259] him to curse the memory of Meinis, and with the permission of the
[023-260] priests he made these curses public by cutting them upon a
[023-261] pillar.[FN#281]

[023-263] teeth" of Horus. The onion was largely used in medicine.

[023-267] IX. Now, the kings of Egypt were always chosen either out of the
[023-268] soldiery or priesthood, the former order being honoured and respected
[023-269] for its valour, and the latter for its wisdom. If the choice fell upon
[023-270] a soldier, he was immediately initiated into the order of priests, and
[023-271] by them instructed in their abstruse and hidden philosophy, a
[023-272] philosophy for the most part involved in fable and allegory, and
[023-273] exhibiting only dark hints and obscure resemblances of the truth. This
[023-274] the priesthood hints to us in many instances, particularly by the
[023-275] sphinxes, which they seem to have placed designedly before their
[023-276] temples as types of the enigmatical nature of their theology. To this
[023-277] purpose, likewise, is that inscription which they have engraved upon
[023-278] the base of the statue of Athene[FN#282] at Sais, whom they identify
[023-279] with Isis: "I am everything that has been, that is, and that shall be:
[023-280] and my veil no man hath raised." In like manner the word "Amoun," or
[023-281] as it is expressed in the Greek language, "Ammon," which is generally
[023-282] looked upon as the proper name of the Egyptian Zeus, is interpreted by
[023-283] Manetho[FN#283] the Sebennite[FN#284] to signify "concealment" or
[023-284] "something which is hidden."[FN#285] Hecataeus of Abdera indeed tells
[023-285] us that the Egyptians make use of this term when they call out to one
[023-286] another. If this be so, then their invoking Amoun is the same thing as
[023-287] calling upon the supreme being, whom they believe to be "hidden" and
[023-288] "concealed" in the universal nature, to appear and manifest itself to
[023-289] them. So cautious and reserved was the Egyptian wisdom in those things
[023-290] which appertained to religion.

[023-292] produced her son, the Sun-god, without union with a god. In an address
[023-293] to her, quoted by Mallet (Culte de Neit, p. 140), are found the words,
[023-294] "thy garment hath not been unloosed," thus Plutarch's quotation is
[023-295] correct.

[023-298] X. And this is still farther evinced from those voyages which have
[023-299] been made into Egypt by the wisest men among the Greeks, namely, by
[023-300] Solo, Thales Plato, Eudoxus, Pythagoras, and, as some say, even by
[023-301] Lycurgus himself, on purpose to converse with the priests. And we are
[023-302] also told that Eudoxus was a disciple of Chnouphis the Memphite, Solo
[023-303] of Sonchis the Saite, and Pythagoras of Oinuphis the Heliopolite. But
[023-304] none of these philosophers seems either to have been more admired and
[023-305] in greater favour with the priests, or to have paid a more especial
[023-306] regard to their method of philosophising, than this last named, who has
[023-307] particularly imitated their mysterious and symbolical manner in his own
[023-308] writings, and like them conveyed his doctrines to the world in a kind
[023-309] of riddle. For many of the precepts of Pythagoras come nothing short
[023-310] of the hieroglyphical representations themselves, such as, "eat not in
[023-311] a chariot," "sit not on a measure (choenix)," "plant not a palm-tree,"
[023-312] and "stir not the fire with a sword in the house." And I myself am of
[023-313] the opinion that, when the Pythagoreans appropriated the names of
[023-314] several of the gods to particular numbers, as that of Apollo to the
[023-315] unit, of Artemis to the duad, of Athene to the seven, and of Poseidon
[023-316] to the first cube, in this they allude to something which the founder
[023-317] of their sect saw in the Egyptian temples, or to some ceremonies
[023-318] performed in them, or to some symbols there exhibited. Thus, their
[023-319] great king and lord Osiris is represented by the hieroglyphics for an
[023-320] eye and a sceptre,[FN#286] the name itself signifying "many-eyed," as
[023-321] we are told by some[FN#287] who would derive it from the words
[023-322] os,[FN#288] "many," and iri,[FN#289] an "eye," which have this meaning
[023-323] in the Egyptian language. Similarly, because the heavens are eternal
[023-324] and are never consumed or wax old, they represent them by a heart with
[023-325] a censer placed under it. Much in the same way are those statues of
[023-326] the Judges at Thebes without hands, and their chief, or president, is
[023-327] represented with his eyes turned downwards, which signifies that
[023-328] justice ought not to be obtainable by bribes, nor guided by favour or
[023-329] affection. Of a like nature is the Beetle which we see engraven upon
[023-330] the seals of the soldiers, for there is no such thing as a female
[023-331] beetle of this species; for they are all males, and they propagate
[023-332] their kind by casting their seed into round balls of dirt, which afford
[023-333] not only a proper place wherein the young may be hatched, but also
[023-334] nourishment for them as soon as they are born.

[023-338] XI. When you hear, therefore, the mythological tales which the
[023-339] Egyptians tell of their gods, their wanderings, their mutilations, and
[023-340] many other disasters which befell them, remember what has just been
[023-341] said, and be assured that nothing of what is thus told you is really
[023-342] true, or ever happened in fact. For can it be imagined that it is the
[023-343] dog[FN#290] itself which is reverenced by them under the name of
[023-344] Hermes[FN#291]? It is the qualities of this animal, his constant
[023-345] vigilance, and his acumen in distinguishing his friends from his foes,
[023-346] which have rendered him, as Plato says, a meet emblem of that god who
[023-347] is the chief patron of intelligence. Nor can we imagine that they
[023-348] think that the sun, like a newly born babe, springs up every day out of
[023-349] a lily. It is quite true that they represent the rising sun in this
[023-350] manner,[FN#292] but the reason is because they wish to indicate thereby
[023-351] that it is moisture to which we owe the first kindling of this
[023-352] luminary. In like manner, the cruel and bloody king of Persia, Ochus,
[023-353] who not only put to death great numbers of the people, but even slew
[023-354] the Apis Bull himself, and afterwards served him up in a banquet to his
[023-355] friends, is represented by them by a sword, and by this name he is
[023-356] still to be found in the catalogue of their kings. This name,
[023-357] therefore, does not represent his person, but indicates his base and
[023-358] cruel qualities, which were best suggested by the picture of an
[023-359] instrument of destruction. If, therefore, O Clea, you will hear and
[023-360] entertain the story of these gods from those who know how to explain it
[023-361] consistently with religion and philosophy, if you will steadily persist
[023-362] in the observance of all these holy rites which the laws require of
[023-363] you, and are moreover fully persuaded that to form true notions of the
[023-364] divine nature is more acceptable to them than any sacrifice or mere
[023-365] external act of worship can be, you will by this means be entirely
[023-366] exempt from any danger of falling into superstition, an evil no less to
[023-367] be avoided than atheism itself.

[023-371] XII. Now, the story of Isis and Osiris, its most insignificant and
[023-372] superfluous parts being omitted, runs thus:—

[023-373] The goddess Rhea,[FN#293] they say, having accompanied with
[023-374] Kronos[FN#294] by stealth, was discovered by Helios[FN#295] who
[023-375] straightway cursed her, and declared that she should not be delivered
[023-376] in any month or year. Hermes, however, 'being also in love with the
[023-377] same goddess, in return for the favours which he had received from her,
[023-378] went and played at dice with Selene,[FN#296] and won from her the
[023-379] seventieth part of each day. These parts he joined together and made
[023-380] from them five complete days, and he added them to the three hundred
[023-381] and sixty days of which the year formerly consisted. These five days
[023-382] are to this day called the "Epagomenae,"[FN#297] that is, the
[023-383] superadded, and they are observed by them as the birthdays of their
[023-384] gods.[FN#298] On the first of these, they say, Osiris was born, and as
[023-385] he came into the world a voice was heard saying, "The Lord of
[023-386] All[FN#299] is born." Some relate the matter in a different way, and
[023-387] say that a certain person named Pamyles, as he was fetching water from
[023-388] the temple of Dios at Thebes, heard a voice commanding him to proclaim
[023-389] aloud that the good and great king Osiris was then born, and that for
[023-390] this reason Kronos committed the education of the child to him, and
[023-391] that in memory of this event the Pamylia were afterwards instituted,
[023-392] which closely resemble the Phallephoria or Priapeia of the Greeks.
[023-393] Upon the second of these days was born Aroueris,[FN#300] whom some call
[023-394] Apollo, and others the Elder Horus. Upon the third day Typhon was
[023-395] born, who came into the world neither at the proper time nor by the
[023-396] right way, but he forced a passage through a wound which he made in his
[023-397] mother's side. Upon the fourth day Isis was born, in the marshes of
[023-398] Egypt,[FN#301] and upon the fifth day Nephthys, whom some call Teleute,
[023-399] or Aphrodite, or Nike, was born. As regards the fathers of these
[023-400] children, the first two are said to have been begotten by Helios, Isis
[023-401] by Hermes, and Typhon and Nephthys by Kronos. Therefore, since the
[023-402] third of the superadded days was the birthday of Typhon, the kings
[023-403] considered it to be unlucky,[FN#302] and in consequence they neither
[023-404] transacted any business in it, nor even suffered themselves to take any
[023-405] refreshment until the evening. They further add that Typhon married
[023-406] Nephthys,[FN#303] and that Isis and Osiris, having a mutual affection,

[023-408] that from this commerce sprang Aroueris, whom the Egyptians likewise
[023-409] call Horus the Elder, and the Greeks Apollo.

[023-418] XIII. Osiris having become king of Egypt, applied himself to
[023-419] civilizing his countrymen by turning them from their former indigent
[023-420] and barbarous course of life. He taught them how to cultivate and
[023-421] improve the fruits of the earth, and he gave them a body of laws
[023-422] whereby to regulate their conduct, and instructed them in the reverence
[023-423] and worship which they were to pay to the gods. With the same good
[023-424] disposition he afterwards travelled over the rest of the world,
[023-425] inducing the people everywhere to submit to his discipline, not indeed
[023-426] compelling them by force of arms, but persuading them to yield to the
[023-427] strength of his reasons, which were conveyed to them in the most
[023-428] agreeable manner, in hymns and songs, accompanied with instruments of
[023-429] music. From this last circumstance the Greeks identified him with
[023-430] their Dionysos, or Bacchus. During the absence of Osiris from his
[023-431] kingdom, Typhon had no opportunity of making any innovations in the
[023-432] state, Isis being extremely vigilant in the government, and always upon
[023-433] her guard. After his return, however, having first persuaded seventy-
[023-434] two other people to join with him in the conspiracy, together with a
[023-435] certain queen of Ethiopia called Aso, who chanced to be in Egypt at
[023-436] that time, he formed a crafty plot against him. For having privily
[023-437] taken the measure of the body of Osiris, he caused a chest to be made
[023-438] of exactly the same size, and it was very beautiful and highly
[023-439] decorated. This chest he brought into a certain banqueting room, where
[023-440] it was greatly admired by all who were present, and Typhon, as if in
[023-441] jest, promised to give it to that man whose body when tried would be
[023-442] found to fit it. Thereupon the whole company, one after the other,
[023-443] went into it, but it did not fit any of them; last of all Osiris
[023-444] himself lay down in it. Thereupon all the conspirators ran to the
[023-445] chest, and clapped the cover upon it, and then they fastened it down
[023-446] with nails on the outside, and poured melted lead over it. They next
[023-447] took the chest to the river, which carried it to the sea through the
[023-448] Tanaitic mouth of the Nile; and for this reason this mouth of the Nile
[023-449] is still held in the utmost abomination by the Egyptians, and is never
[023-450] mentioned by them except with marks of detestation. These things, some
[023-451] say, took place on the seventeenth day of the month of Hathor, when the
[023-452] sun was in Scorpio, in the twenty-eighth year of the reign of Osiris,
[023-453] though others tell us that this was the year of his life and not of his
[023-454] reign.

[023-455] XIV. The first who had knowledge of the accident which had befallen
[023-456] their king were the Pans and Satyrs, who inhabited the country round
[023-457] about Chemmis,[FN#304] and they having informed the people about it,
[023-458] gave the first occasion to the name of Panic Terrors, which has ever
[023-459] since been made use of to signify any sudden fright or amazement of a
[023-460] multitude. As soon as the report reached Isis, she immediately cut off
[023-461] one of the locks of her hair, and put on mourning apparel in that very
[023-462] place where she happened to be; for this reason the place has ever
[023-463] since been called "Koptos," or the "city of mourning," though some are
[023-464] of opinion that this word rather signifies "deprivation." After this
[023-465] she wandered round about through the country, being full of disquietude
[023-466] and perplexity, searching for the chest, and she inquired of every
[023-467] person she met, including some children whom she saw, whether they knew
[023-468] what was become of it. Now, it so happened that these children had
[023-469] seen what Typhon's accomplices had done with the body, and they
[023-470] accordingly told her by what mouth of the Nile it had been conveyed to
[023-471] the sea. For this reason the Egyptians look upon children as endued
[023-472] with a kind of faculty of divining, and in consequence of this notion
[023-473] are very curious in observing the accidental prattle which they have
[023-474] with one another whilst they are at play, especially if it be in a
[023-475] sacred place, forming omens and presages from it. Isis meanwhile
[023-476] having been informed that Osiris, deceived by her sister Nephthys, who
[023-477] was in love with him, had unwittingly enjoyed her instead of herself,
[023-478] as she concluded from the melilot-garland which he had left with her,
[023-479] made it her business likewise to search out the child, the fruit of
[023-480] this unlawful commerce (for her sister, dreading the anger of her
[023-481] husband Typhon, had exposed it as soon as it was born). Accordingly,
[023-482] after much pains and difficulty, by means of some dogs that conducted
[023-483] her to the place where it was, she found it and bred it up; and in
[023-484] process of time it became her constant guard and attendant, and
[023-485] obtained the name of Anubis, and it is thought that it watches and
[023-486] guards the gods as dogs do men.

[023-653] XX. Such then are the principal circumstances of this famous story,
[023-654] the more harsh and shocking parts of it, such as the cutting up of
[023-655] Horus and the beheading of Isis, being omitted. Now, if such could be
[023-656] supposed to be the real sentiments of the Egyptians concerning those
[023-657] divine Beings whose most distinguishing characteristics are happiness
[023-658] and immortality, or could it be imagined that they actually believed
[023-659] what they thus tell us ever to have actually taken place, I should not
[023-660] need to warn you, O Clea, you who are already sufficiently averse to
[023-661] such impious and absurd notions of the God, I should not, I say, have
[023-662] need to caution you, to testify your abhorrence of them, and, as
[023-663] Aeschylus expresses it, "to spit and wash your mouth" after the recital
[023-664] of them. In the present case, however, it is not so. And I doubt not
[023-665] that you yourself are conscious of the difference between this history
[023-666] and those light and idle fictions which the poets and other writers of
[023-667] fables, like spiders, weave and spin out of their own imaginations,
[023-668] without having any substantial ground or firm foundation to work upon.
[023-669] There must have been some real distress, some actual calamity, at the
[023-670] bottom as the ground-work of the narration; for, as mathematicians
[023-671] assure us, the rainbow is nothing else but a variegated image of the
[023-672] sun, thrown upon the sight by the reflection of his beams from the
[023-673] clouds; and thus ought we to look upon the present story as the
[023-674] representation, or rather reflection, of something real as its true
[023-675] cause. And this notion is still farther suggested to us as well by
[023-676] that solemn air of grief and sadness which appears in their sacrifices,
[023-677] as by the very form and arrangement of their temples, which extend into

### [FIRST EXPLANATION OF THE STORY.]

[023-740] XXII. Now as to those who, from many things of this kind, some of
[023-741] which are proclaimed openly, and others are darkly hinted at in their
[023-742] religious institutions, would conclude that the whole story is no other
[023-743] than a mere commemoration of the various actions of their kings and
[023-744] other great men, who, by reason of their excellent virtue and the
[023-745] mightiness of their power, added to their other titles the honour of
[023-746] divinity, though they afterwards fell into many and grievous
[023-747] calamities, those, I say, who would in this manner account for the
[023-748] various scenes above-mentioned, must be owned indeed to make use of a
[023-749] very plausible method of eluding such difficulties as may arise about
[023-750] this subject, and ingeniously enough to transfer the most shocking
[023-751] parts of it from the divine to the human nature. Moreover, it must be
[023-752] admitted that such a solution is not entirely destitute of any
[023-753] appearance of historical evidence for its support. For when the
[023-754] Egyptians themselves tell us that Hermes had one hand shorter than
[023-755] another, that Typhon was of red complexion, Horus fair, and Osiris
[023-756] black, does not this show that they were of the human species, and
[023-757] subject to the same accidents as all other men?[FN#323] Nay, they go
[023-758] farther, and even declare the particular work in which each was engaged
[023-759] whilst alive. Thus they say that Osiris was a general, that Canopus,
[023-760] from whom the star took its name, was a pilot, and that the ship which
[023-761] the Greeks call Argo, being made in imitation of the ship of Osiris,
[023-762] was, in honour of him, turned into a constellation and placed near
[023-763] Orion and the Dog-star, the former being sacred to Horus and the latter
[023-764] to Isis.

[FN#323] Red is the colour attributed to all fiends in the Egyptian
[023-765] texts. One of the forms of Horus is described as being "blue-eyed,"
[023-766] and the colour of the face of Osiris is often green, and sometimes
[023-767] black.

[023-768] XXIII. But I am much afraid that to give in to this explanation of the
[023-769] story will be to move things which ought not to be moved; and not only,
[023-770] as Simonides says, "to declare war against all antiquity," but likewise
[023-771] against whole families and nations who are fully possessed with the
[023-772] belief in the divinity of these beings. And it would be no less than
[023-773] dispossessing those great names of their heaven, and bringing them down
[023-774] to the earth. It would be to shake and loosen a worship and faith
[023-775] which have been firmly settled in nearly all mankind from their
[023-776] infancy. It would be to open a wide door for atheism to enter in at,
[023-777] and to encourage the attempts of those who would humanize the divine
[023-778] nature. More particularly it would give a clear sanction and authority
[023-779] to the impostures of Euhemerus the Messenian, who from mere
[023-780] imagination, and without the least appearance of truth to support it,

[023-786] than the Tryphillians, whom he mentions at the same time with them, he
[023-787] does not inform us. Nor can I learn that any other person, whether
[023-788] Greek or Barbarian, except himself, has ever yet been so fortunate as
[023-789] to meet with these imaginary countries.

### [SECOND EXPLANATION OF THE STORY.]

[023-796] XXV. There is another and a better method which some employ in
[023-797] explaining this story. They assert that what is related of Typhon,
[023-798] Osiris, and Isis is not to be regarded as the afflictions of gods, or
[023-799] of mere mortals, but rather as the adventures of certain great Daemons.
[023-800] These beings, they say, are supposed by some of the wisest of the Greek
[023-801] philosophers, that is to say, Plato, Pythagoras, Xenocrates, and
[023-802] Chrysippus, in accordance with what they had learned from ancient
[023-803] theologians, to be stronger and more powerful than men, and of a nature
[023-804] superior to them. They are, at the same time, inferior to the pure and
[023-805] unmixed nature of the gods, as partaking of the sensations of the body,
[023-806] as well as of the perceptions of the soul, and consequently liable to
[023-807] pain as well as pleasure, and to such other appetites and affections,
[023-808] as flow from their various combinations. Such affections, however,
[023-809] have a greater power and influence over some of them than over others,
[023-810] just as there are different degrees of virtue and vice found in these
[023-811] Daemons as well as in mankind. In like manner, the wars of the Giants
[023-812] and the Titans which are so much spoken of by the Greeks, the
[023-813] detestable actions of Kronos, the combats between Apollo and the
[023-814] Python, the flights of Dionysos, and the wanderings of Demeter, are
[023-815] exactly of the same nature as the adventures of Osiris and Typhon.
[023-816] Therefore, they all are to be accounted for in the same manner, and
[023-817] every treatise of mythology will readily furnish us with an abundance
[023-818] of other similar instances. The same thing may also be affirmed of
[023-819] those other things which are so carefully concealed under the cover of
[023-820] mysteries and imitations.

### [THIRD EXPLANATION OF THE STORY.]

[023-877] XXXII. Such then are the arguments of those who endeavour to account
[023-878] for the above-mentioned history of Isis and Osiris upon a supposition
[023-879] that they were of the order of Daemons; but there are others who
[023-880] pretend to explain it upon other principles, and in more philosophical
[023-881] manner. To begin, then, with those whose reasoning is the most simple
[023-882] and obvious. As the Greeks allegorize their Kronos into Time, and
[023-883] their Hera into Air, and tell us that the birth of Hephaistos is no
[023-884] other but the change of air into fire, so these philosophers say that
[023-885] by Osiris the Egyptians mean the Nile, by Isis that part of the country
[023-886] which Osiris, or the Nile, overflows, and by Typhon the sea, which, by
[023-887] receiving the Nile as it runs into it, does, as it were, tear it into
[023-888] many pieces, and indeed entirely destroys it, excepting only so much of
[023-889] it as is admitted into the bosom of the earth in its passage over it,
[023-890] which is thereby rendered fertile. The truth of this explanation is
[023-891] confirmed, they say, by that sacred dirge which they make over Osiris
[023-892] when they bewail "him who was born on the right side of the world and
[023-893] who perished on the left."[FN#325] For it must be observed that the
[023-894] Egyptians look upon the east as the front or face of the world,[FN#326]
[023-895] upon the north as its right side,[FN#327] and upon the south as its
[023-896] left.[FN#328] As, therefore, the Nile rises in the south, and running
[023-897] directly northwards is at last swallowed up by the sea, it may rightly
[023-898] enough be said to be born on the right and to perish on the left side.
[023-899] This conclusion, they say, is still farther strengthened from that
[023-900] abhorrence which the priests express towards the sea, as well as salt,
[023-901] which they call "Typhon's foam." And amongst their prohibitions is one
[023-902] which forbids salt being laid on their tables. And do they not also
[023-903] carefully avoid speaking to pilots, because this class of men have much
[023-904] to do with the sea and get their living by it? And this is not the
[023-905] least of their reasons for the great dislike which they have for fish,
[023-906] and they even make the fish a symbol of "hatred," as is proved by the
[023-907] pictures which are to be seen on the porch of the temple of Neith at
[023-908] Sais. The first of these is a child, the second is an old man, the
[023-909] third is a hawk, and then follow a fish and a hippopotamus. The
[023-910] meaning of all these is evidently, "O you who are coming into the
[023-911] world, and you who are going out of it (i.e., both young and old), God
[023-912] hateth impudence." For by the child is indicated "all those who are
[023-913] coming into life"; by the old man, "those who are going out of it"; by
[023-914] the hawk, "God"; by the fish, "hatred," on account of the sea, as has
[023-915] been before stated; and by the hippopotamus, "impudence," this creature
[023-916] being said first to slay his sire, and afterwards to force his
[023-917] dam.[FN#329] The Pythagoreans likewise may be thought perhaps by some
[023-918] to have looked upon the sea as impure, and quite different from all the
[023-919] rest of nature, and that thus much is intended by them when they call
[023-920] it the "tears of Kronos."

### [FIFTH EXPLANATION OF THE STORY.]

[023-1033] [Sec. XLIV. The philosophers say that the story is nothing but an
[023-1034] enigmatical description of the phenomena of Eclipses. In Sec. XLV.
[023-1035] Plutarch discusses the five explanations which he has described, and
[023-1036] begins to state his own views about them. It must be concluded, he
[023-1037] says, that none of these explanations taken by itself contains the true
[023-1038] explanation of the foregoing history, though all of them together do.
[023-1039] Typhon means every phase of Nature which is hurtful and destructive,
[023-1040] not only drought, darkness, the sea, &c. It is impossible that any one
[023-1041] cause, be it bad or even good, should be the common principle of all
[023-1042] things. There must be two opposite and quite different and distinct
[023-1043] Principles. In Sec. XLVI., Plutarch compares this view with the Magian
[023-1044] belief in Ormazd and Ahriman, the former springing from light (Sec.
[023-1045] XLVII.), and the latter from darkness. Ormazd made six good gods, and
[023-1046] Ahriman six of a quite contrary nature. Ormazd increased his own bulk
[023-1047] three times, and adorned the heaven with stars, making the Sun to be
[023-1048] the guard of the other stars. He then created twenty-four other gods,
[023-1049] and placed them in an egg, and Ahriman also created twenty-four gods;
[023-1050] the latter bored a hole in the shell of the egg and effected an
[023-1051] entrance into it, and thus good and evil became mixed together. In Sec.
[023-1052] XLVIII. Plutarch quotes Empedocles, Anaxagoras, Aristotle, and Plato in
[023-1053] support of his hypothesis of the Two Principles, and refers to Plato's
[023-1054] Third Principle. Sec. XLIX. Osiris represents the good qualities of the
[023-1055] universal Soul, and Typhon the bad; Bebo[FN#344] is a malignant being
[023-1056] like Typhon, with whom Manetho identifies him. Sec. L. The ass,
[023-1057] crocodile, and hippopotamus are all associated with Typhon; in the form
[023-1058] of a crocodile Typhon escaped from Horus.[FN#345]
