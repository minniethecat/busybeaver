# Burial of Osiris's Pieces

## Overview

The Burial of Osiris's Pieces is the final act of Isis's devotion following the murder and dismemberment of her husband. After Set discovered the chest containing Osiris's body, he tore it into fourteen pieces and scattered them across Egypt. Isis then undertook a second journey — distinct from her initial search for the intact body — to recover each scattered portion, burying each piece where it was found. This act established the many sepulchres of Osiris throughout Egypt and became central to the cult of Osiris, with numerous cities claiming to house a tomb of the god.

## Narrative

### The Discovery and Dismemberment

After Isis had recovered the chest containing Osiris's body from Byblos, she deposited it in a remote and unfrequented place while she went to visit her son Horus, who was being reared at Buto. Set, however, discovered the chest while hunting:

> "When Isis had come to her son Horus, who was being reared at Buto, she deposited the chest in a remote and unfrequented place. One night, however, when Typhon was hunting by the light of the moon, he came upon it by chance, and recognizing the body which was enclosed in it, he tore it into several pieces, fourteen in all, and scattered them in different places up and down the country." [PN=023-740]

### The Search for the Scattered Pieces

When Isis learned what Set had done, she undertook a second search — this time not for an intact body, but for its scattered parts:

> "When Isis knew what had been done, she set out in search of the scattered portions of her husband's body." [PN=023-740]

To navigate the marshy lower reaches of the country more easily, she constructed a boat made of the papyrus plant. The text notes an enduring consequence of this:

> "For this reason, they say, either fearing the anger of the goddess, or else venerating the papyrus, the crocodile never injures anyone who travels in this sort of vessel." [PN=023-740]

### The Burial of Each Piece

Isis's methodical devotion defined the cult geography of Osiris: wherever she found one of the scattered portions of her husband's body, there she buried it:

> "And this, they say, hath given rise to the report that there are very many different sepulchres of Osiris in Egypt, for wherever Isis found one of the scattered portions of her husband's body, there she buried it." [PN=023-740]

The same chapter records the number of pieces with more specificity:

> "The fourteen pieces into which the body of Osiris was broken are compared to the days of the moon." [PN=023-1022]

### The Many Sepulchres of Osiris

The narrative records an alternative explanation for the multiple tombs, attributed to the political strategy of the queen:

> "Others, however, contradict this story, and tell us that the variety of sepulchres of Osiris was due rather to the policy of the queen, who, instead of the real body, as she pretended, presented to these cities only an image of her husband. This she did in order to increase the honours which would by these means be paid to his memory, and also to defeat Typhon, who, if he were victorious in his fight against Horus in which he was about to engage, would search for the body of Osiris, and being distracted by the number of sepulchres would despair of ever being able to find the true one." [PN=023-741]

The text further notes that the true sepulchre of Osiris is held to lie in a particular place:

> "The true sepulchre of Osiris lies in that little island which is near Philae." [PN=023-742]

### The Unrecovered Phallus

Despite all her efforts, Isis was never able to discover one crucial part of Osiris's body:

> "We are told, moreover, that notwithstanding all her efforts, Isis was never able to discover the phallus of Osiris, which, having been thrown into the Nile immediately upon its separation from the rest of the body, had been devoured by the Lepidotus, the Phagrus, and the Oxyrhynchus, fish which above all others, for this reason, the Egyptians have in more especial avoidance." [PN=023-744]

To compensate for this loss, Isis consecrated a phallus made in imitation of it and instituted a solemn festival to its memory, "which is even to this day observed by the Egyptians." [PN=023-744]

## Outcome and Significance

The Burial of Osiris's Pieces is a foundational event for the cult and geography of Osiris worship:

1. **Cult Geography**: The burial of each piece established multiple sacred sites across Egypt, each claiming to be a sepulchre of Osiris. This distributed sanctity made the Osiris cult geographically pervasive and reinforced the god's connection to the entire land of Egypt.

2. **Theological Integration of Osiris with Egypt**: By scattering and burying the pieces throughout the country, Set unwittingly made Osiris physically present in every region of Egypt. The dismemberment that was meant to annihilate the god instead ensured his omnipresence.

3. **Lunar Symbolism**: The fourteen pieces explicitly correspond to the fourteen days of the waning moon: "The fourteen pieces into which the body of Osiris was broken are compared to the days of the moon when she wanes." [PN=023-1022] This connects Osiris to the lunar cycle and the theme of death and rebirth.

4. **The Phallus Festival**: The loss and symbolic replacement of Osiris's phallus — and the institution of a festival around it — reinforced the generative power of Osiris even in death. Isis's conception of Horus from the restored body of Osiris depended on this magical continuity.

5. **The Papyrus Boat**: Isis's use of a papyrus boat to navigate the marshes established a lasting protection: crocodiles would not harm those traveling in papyrus vessels, a belief that persisted in Egyptian folklore.

6. **Distinction from the Initial Search**: This event is distinct from the initial Search for Osiris (in which Isis found the intact body at Netat). The burial of the pieces occurs after Set's second discovery of the body, forming the third act of the Osiris cycle: murder → search → dismemberment → burial of pieces → resurrection.
