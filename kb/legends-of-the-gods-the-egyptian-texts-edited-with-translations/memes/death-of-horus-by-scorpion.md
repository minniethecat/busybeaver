# Death of Horus by a Scorpion

## Overview

The Legend of the Death of Horus by a Scorpion recounts the tragic death of the infant [[Horus]] in the Reed Swamps, stung by the scorpion Uhat sent by [[Set]], and his miraculous resurrection through the intervention of [[Thoth]], who descended from the Boat of [[Ra]] bearing words of power. This event, inscribed on the Metternich Stele, served as the mythological foundation for Egyptian scorpion-sting remedies: the recitation of the spells that revived Horus was believed to cure any victim of venom.

## Narrative

### The Flight of Isis

After the murder of [[Osiris]] by Set, Isis was imprisoned and threatened with forced marriage to the usurper. She had been made pregnant by Osiris after his death. [[Thoth]] appeared to her in captivity, advising her to hide herself with her unborn child: "he promised her that her son should succeed in due course to his father's throne." With Thoth's help she escaped, accompanied by the Seven Scorpion-goddesses [005-131–005-142].

Travelling to the Reed Swamps, Isis sought shelter in the town of Per-Sui. A woman shut her door against the fugitive goddess. In retribution, one of the Scorpion-goddesses forced entry and stung the woman's child to death. Moved by the mother's grief, Isis laid her hands upon the child and "having uttered one of her most potent spells over him, the poison of the scorpion ran out of his body, and the child came to life again" — a foretelling of the resurrection she would later need for her own son [005-143–005-156].

### The Death of Horus

Soon after, a terrible calamity struck. Isis had gone to perform religious ceremonies in honour of Osiris at a temple near Hetep-hemt, leaving Horus concealed in Sekhet-An. In her absence, the scorpion Uhat — sent by Set — forced its way into the hiding-place and stung Horus to death [005-157–005-167].

When Isis returned to find the dead body, she "burst forth in lamentations, the sound of which brought all the people from the neighbouring districts to her side." She placed her nose in the mouth of Horus to discover if he still breathed, but "there was no breath in his throat." Examining the wound, she found "traces of poison" — the venom of Uhat had killed her son [005-168–005-176].

### The Resurrection by Thoth

In her despair, Isis cried out to the heavens. Her lamentation reached the Boat of Ra, and Thoth descended from the sky:

> "I have come from heaven to save the child for his mother" [005-207].

Thoth uttered the words of power that Isis had learned from him. He declared that the Boat of Ra would stand still and the Sun would not move until Horus was restored. He spoke the potent spells, and "the poison of the scorpion flowed out of him, and he revived." The child Horus was brought back from death [005-206–005-220].

## Outcome and Significance

This event holds profound significance in Egyptian religion and magic:

1. **Foundation of Magical Healing**: The spells spoken by Thoth to revive Horus were inscribed on stelae like the Metternich Stele, believed to be infallible remedies against scorpion stings and serpent bites. Every Egyptian who recited these words invoked the same power that resurrected Horus [005-001–005-011].

2. **Isis as Healer**: The episode demonstrates the dual role of [[Isis]] — she who mourns and she who heals. Her grief became a pattern for all mourning, and her magical knowledge became the source of all curative spells.

3. **Thoth as Divine Physician**: Thoth's descent from the Boat of Ra established him as the divine physician who could overcome even death, stopping the solar barque itself until healing was complete.

4. **The Vulnerable Divine Child**: The death of the infant Horus symbolised the vulnerability of divine order to the forces of chaos (Set). His resurrection affirmed that divine order ultimately triumphs.
