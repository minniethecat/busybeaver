# Isis Searches for Osiris

## Overview

The Search for Osiris is one of the most poignant episodes in Egyptian mythology. Upon learning of her husband's murder by Set, Isis immediately set out to find his body. Taking the form of a bird, she flew over Egypt ceaselessly, uttering wailing cries of grief. With her sister Nephthys, she eventually found the body of Osiris thrown on the ground at Netat, near Abydos. The search and its aftermath — the lamentation, the magical restoration, and the conception of Horus — form the emotional and theological core of the Osiris myth.

## Narrative

### The News of the Murder

The news of Osiris's death reached Isis, and she immediately began her quest:

> "The news of the death of Osiris was brought to Isis, and she at once set out to find his body." [PN=009-034]

Isis had been the constant protectress of her brother-husband. She had driven away the fiends that wanted to attack him, guarded his shrine and tomb, and protected him by means of spells and incantations. Her "mouth was trained to perfection, and she made no mistake in pronouncing her spells, and her tongue was skilled and halted not." [PN=009-033] Yet Set found his moment and struck.

### The Search in Bird Form

All Egyptian traditions agree on the form Isis assumed during her search:

> "All legends agree in saying that she took the form of a bird, and that she flew about unceasingly, going hither and thither, and uttering wailing cries of grief." [PN=009-035]

The Hymn to Osiris elaborates on the tireless nature of Isis's search:

> "She flew round and round over this earth uttering wailing cries of grief, and she did not alight on the ground until she had found him. She made light [to come forth] from her feathers, she made air to come into being by means of her two wings, and she cried out the death cries for her brother." [PN=010-001]

### The Discovery at Netat

At length, Isis found what she sought:

> "At length she found the body, and with a piercing cry she alighted on the ground." [PN=009-035]

Nephthys joined her sister at the site:

> "The Pyramid Texts say that Nephthys was with her — that 'Isis came, Nephthys came, the one on the right side, the other on the left side, one in the form of a Hat bird, the other in the form of a Tchert bird, and they found Osiris thrown on the ground in Netat by his brother Set.'" [PN=009-035]

### The Magical Awakening

Having found the body, Isis did not simply mourn. She acted with her magical power:

> "Isis fanned the body with her feathers, and produced air, and at length she caused the inert members of Osiris to move, and drew from him his essence, wherefrom she produced her child Horus." [PN=009-036]

The Pyramid Texts add further detail to the intimate ritual of restoration:

> "Thy elder sister took thy body in her arms, she chafed thy hands, she clasped thee to her breast [when] she found thee [lying] on thy side on the plain of Netat." [PN=009-037]

## Outcome and Significance

The Search for Osiris had profound consequences:

1. **Discovery and Resurrection**: Finding the body of Osiris was the necessary first step toward his resurrection. Without the search, Osiris would remain lost and unavenged.

2. **Conception of Horus**: By drawing forth the essence of Osiris, Isis conceived Horus — the avenger who would reclaim the throne. The Hymn to Osiris states: "She made to rise up the helpless members of him whose heart was at rest, she drew from him his essence, and she made therefrom an heir." [PN=010-002]

3. **Model of Devotion**: Isis's tireless search became the paradigm of wifely devotion and magical power in Egyptian religion. Her lamentations were ritualized in the Festival Songs of Isis and Nephthys.

4. **Establishment of Funerary Rites**: The search and the subsequent rites performed by Isis and Nephthys provided the mythological template for Egyptian funerary practices, particularly the Opening of the Mouth ceremony.
