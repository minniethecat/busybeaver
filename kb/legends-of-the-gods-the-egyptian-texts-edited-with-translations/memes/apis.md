# Apis

## Overview

The Apis bull (Hapis) was the sacred living bull-god of Memphis, regarded as the *ba* (soul) of [[ptah]] and later as the embodiment of [[osiris]]. The cult of the Apis was one of the oldest and most enduring in Egypt — attested from the earliest dynasties through to the Roman period, where it evolved into the worship of Serapis.

## Identity and Role

Apis was not a mythical figure but a living god — a bull selected for specific markings (a white triangle on the forehead, a scarab-shaped mark under the tongue, and double hairs on the tail) and housed in the temple of Ptah at Memphis. He was the intermediary between the divine and human realms, delivering oracles through his movements and receiving petitions from worshippers.

The city of Memphis itself bore his name:

> the city of Memphis, which was also called the "City of Apis" [001-091]

> and is the Apis city of classical writers [001-092]

## Relationship to Osiris

After death the Apis bull was identified with [[osiris]], becoming "Osiris-Apis" — a fusion cult that Greek settlers later developed into Serapis (Sarapis), the syncretic god of the Ptolemaic period:

> Apis is called the "life of Osiris" [023-718]

> Apis, or Sarapis. The famous Serap... [023-721]

This identification with Osiris transformed the Apis from a local Memphite cult into an international deity whose worship spread throughout the Mediterranean world.

## Major Myths and Narratives

**The Apis Bull Himself.** The classical tradition, preserved in the legends through Plutarch, records the beliefs surrounding the Apis:

> the Apis Bull himself, and afterwards... [023-354]

> the Apis Bull, who is considered to be... [023-689]

The Apis was believed to be conceived by a flash of lightning from heaven — a divine impregnation that marked the calf from birth as the chosen vessel of Ptah.

**Offerings to Apis.** The cult practice prescribed specific rituals to maintain the bull's sacred status:

> by the same motives also they give the Apis [023-146]

> they strive to prevent fatness in Apis as well as in [023-153]

> it is quite possible that Apis drank from a special well [023-156]

These detailed prescriptions — controlling the bull's diet, restricting its water to a sacred well, and limiting its lifespan — reflect the meticulous care with which the living god was tended.

**The Apis Sarcophagi.** Upon death the Apis bull was embalmed and buried with full pharaonic honours in the Serapeum at Saqqara — a vast underground gallery of granite sarcophagi, each weighing up to seventy tons. The death of an Apis was a national event, and the search for its successor began immediately.

## Cult and Worship

The cult of the Apis was centred at the temple of Ptah in Memphis, where the living bull resided in a special stall with a courtyard for public audiences. His birthday was celebrated annually with a seven-day festival. When an Apis died, all Egypt mourned; the embalmed bull was carried in procession to the Serapeum, where it was interred with inscriptions recording the year of its birth, installation, and death — a chronological record so precise that ancient historians used the Apis stelae to reconstruct Egyptian chronology.

The Greek and Roman worlds received the Apis through the cult of Serapis — a deliberate creation of Ptolemy I to unite Egyptian and Hellenistic religious traditions, combining the Apis-Osiris of Memphis with the iconography of Zeus and Hades.
