# Isis and the Seven Scorpions

## Overview

The narrative of Isis and the Seven Scorpions combines two connected events: the goddess's escape from Set's captivity with the aid of seven scorpion-goddesses, and the subsequent death and revival of the infant Horus from a scorpion's sting. Set after murdering Osiris imprisoned Isis, intending to force her into marriage and legitimize his seizure of the throne. With Thoth's counsel, Isis escaped, accompanied by the Seven Scorpions — Tefen, Befen, Mestet, Mestetef, Petet, Thetet, and Maatet. When a wealthy woman refused her shelter, the scorpions avenged the slight by stinging the woman's child to death; Isis, moved by compassion, healed the child with her spells. The story culminates in the death of Horus by the scorpion Uhat, sent by Set, and his miraculous revival by Thoth.

## Narrative

### Imprisonment and Escape

After Set murdered Osiris, he shut Isis up in a dwelling, probably with the intention of forcing her to marry him and thus legalize his seizure of the kingdom:

> "Isis, it seems, was shut up in some dwelling by Set after he murdered Osiris, probably with the intention of forcing her to marry him, and so assist him to legalize his seizure of the kingdom." [PN=005-148]

Isis had been made pregnant by her husband after his death. Thoth appeared to her and advised her to hide herself with her unborn child and bring him forth in secret, promising that her son would succeed to his father's throne. With Thoth's help she escaped from captivity:

> "With the help of Thoth she escaped from her captivity, and went forth accompanied by the Seven Scorpion-goddesses, who brought her to the town of Per-Sui, on the edge of the Reed Swamps." [PN=005-149]

### The Seven Scorpions' March

The text supplies the names of the Seven Scorpions and their formation around Isis:

> "I came forth [from the dwelling] at the time of evening, and there came forth the Seven Scorpions which were to accompany me and to strike(?) for me with [their] stings. Two scorpions, Tefen and Befen, were behind me, two scorpions, Mestet and Mestetef, were by my side, and three scorpions, Petet, Thetet, and Maatet (or, Martet), were for preparing the road for me." [PN=021-008]

Isis charged the scorpions strictly: "Have no knowledge of [any], make no cry to the Tesheru beings, and pay no attention to the 'son of a man' (i.e., anyone) who belongeth to a man of no account," and instructed them, "Let your faces be turned towards the ground [that ye may show me] the way." [PN=021-009]

### The Woman Who Refused Shelter

Arriving at the town of Pa-Sui, Isis sought shelter:

> "She applied to a woman for a night's shelter, but the woman shut her door in her face." [PN=005-149]

The text identifies this woman as a lady of quality who "was sick at heart by reason of those [scorpions] which were with me." [PN=021-012] A poor woman named Taha (or Tah), however, opened her door to the goddess.

To punish the woman who refused shelter, the Seven Scorpions took counsel and "they all at one time shot out their venom on the tail of the scorpion Tefen." [PN=021-013] Tefen then entered the house:

> "Then the scorpion Tefen entered in under the leaves of the door and smote (i.e., stung) the son of Usert, and a fire broke out in the house of Usert, and there was no water there to extinguish it; [but] the sky rained upon the house of Usert, though it was not the season for rain." [PN=021-014]

### Isis Heals the Child

The woman Usert was overcome with grief, wandering through the town crying for help, but none came. Isis, moved by compassion despite the insult, resolved to save the innocent child:

> "Now mine own heart was grievously sad for the sake of the child, and [I wished] to make to live [again] him that was free from fault." [PN=021-015]

Isis summoned the lady, declaring: "Verily my mouth (?) possesseth life. I am a daughter [well] known in her town, [and I] can destroy the demon of death by the spell (or, utterance) which my father taught me to know." [PN=021-015]

Placing her hands on the child, Isis uttered the healing spell, commanding each scorpion's poison by name to depart:

> "O poison of the scorpion Tefent, come forth and appear on the ground! Thou shalt neither enter nor penetrate [further into the body of the child]. O poison of the scorpion Befent, come forth and appear on the ground! I am Isis, the goddess, the lady (or, mistress) of words of power, and I am the maker of words of power (i.e., spells), and I know how to utter words with magical effect." [PN=021-016]

She continued, calling out the poisons of Mestet, Mestetef, Petet and Thetet, and Maatet, commanding each to fall to the ground. The child revived:

> "To punish her one of the Scorpion-goddesses forced her way into the woman's house, and stung her child to death. The grief of the woman was so bitter and sympathy-compelling that Isis laid her hands on the child, and, having uttered one of her most potent spells over him, the poison of the scorpion ran out of his body, and the child came to life again." [PN=005-148]

The fire in the house was extinguished, and heaven was satisfied with the utterance of Isis. Overcome with gratitude and repentance, Usert brought quantities of possessions as gifts:

> "When the woman saw that her son had been brought back to life by Isis, she was filled with joy and gratitude, and, as a mark of her repentance, she brought large quantities of things from her house as gifts for Isis, and they were so many that they filled the house of the kind, but poor, woman who had given Isis shelter." [PN=005-149]

The words of the spell were cut on the Stele, "and they were treasured by the Egyptians as an infallible remedy for scorpion stings." [PN=005-148]

### The Death of Horus by the Scorpion Uhat

Soon after this episode, a calamity struck: Horus himself was stung by a scorpion and died. Isis had left her child concealed in Sekhet-An while she performed religious ceremonies in honor of Osiris at a temple near Hetep-hemt:

> "During her absence the scorpion Uhat, which had been sent by Set, forced its way into the biding-place of Horus, and there stung him to death." [PN=005-152]

The gods conveyed the news to Isis, who ran out distraught:

> "Isis, stabbed with pain at the news, as if a knife had been driven into her body, ran out distraught with grief." [PN=005-152]

When Isis found the body, she burst into lamentations. Examining the wound, she saw "traces of poison" left by the fiend Aun-Ab. Clasping Horus in her arms:

> "She uttered a series of heartbreaking laments, each of which begins with the words 'Horus is bitten.' The heir of heaven, the son of Un-Nefer, the child of the gods, he who was wholly fair, is bitten! He for whose wants I provided, he who was to avenge his father, is bitten! He for whom I cared and suffered when he was being fashioned in my womb, is bitten! He whom I tended so that I might gaze upon him, is bitten! He whose life I prayed for is bitten! Calamity hath overtaken the child, and he hath perished." [PN=005-153]

### Thoth Descends to Revive Horus

Nephthys arrived with the scorpion-goddess Serqet and advised Isis to pray to heaven. Isis cried out, and her voice reached the Boat of Millions of Years. The Disk ceased to move, and Thoth descended:

> "From the Boat Thoth descended, being equipped with words of power and spells of all kinds, and bearing with him the 'great command of maa-kheru,' i.e., the WORD, whose commands were performed, instantly and completely, by every god, spirit, fiend, human being and by every thing, animate and inanimate, in heaven, earth, and the Other World." [PN=005-154]

Isis, impatient in her grief, questioned the value of all spells if Horus lay dead:

> "'What is the good,' she asks, 'of all thy spells, and incantations, and magical formulae, and the great command of maa-kheru, if Horus is to perish by the poison of a scorpion, and to lie here in the arms of Death? Evil, evil is his destiny, for it hath entailed the deepest misery for him and death.'" [PN=005-154]

Thoth reassured her, declaring he had come from heaven to heal the child. He pointed out that Horus was under the divine protection of the Dweller in his Disk (Aten), the Great Dwarf, the Mighty Ram, the Great Hawk, the Holy Beetle, the Hidden Body, and the Divine Bennu. Then he uttered the great spell:

> "By his words of power Thoth transferred the fluid of life of Ra, and as soon as this came upon the child's body the poison of the scorpion flowed out of him, and he once more breathed and lived." [PN=005-154]

Thoth returned to the Boat of Ra, the gods resumed their rowing, and the Disk continued its journey across the sky. The gods in heaven sang songs of joy, and Isis was restored to hope that her son would avenge his father and occupy his throne:

> "The final words of Thoth comforted her greatly, for he told her that he would take charge of the case of Horus in the Judgment Hall of Anu." [PN=005-155]

## Outcome and Significance

The narrative of Isis and the Seven Scorpions carries multiple layers of theological and magical significance:

1. **The Power of Words of Power (Heka)**: The spells uttered by Isis to heal the child and by Thoth to revive Horus demonstrate the Egyptian belief in the creative and restorative power of correctly spoken words. Isis is repeatedly titled "the lady of words of power" and "the maker of words of power." The spell she used was treasured by Egyptians "as an infallible remedy for scorpion stings." [PN=005-148]

2. **Compassion as a Divine Attribute**: Isis's decision to heal the child of the woman who refused her shelter demonstrates the goddess's compassion — a defining trait that would sustain her throughout the Osiris cycle and make her the most beloved of Egyptian deities.

3. **Symbolic Death and Resurrection**: The death and revival of Horus prefigures the larger resurrection of Osiris. Thoth's descent from the Boat of Ra — which halts the sun itself — emphasizes the cosmic significance of the event: the heir of Osiris cannot be allowed to die.

4. **Set's Relentless Opposition**: Set's dispatch of the scorpion Uhat shows that the murder of Osiris was not the end of Set's campaign against the family of his brother. The threat to Horus continues, and the protection of the divine child becomes a central motif.

5. **Healing Magic in Egyptian Practice**: The "Chapter of the Stinging of Scorpions" that follows the narrative became a standard magical text for protection against venomous creatures. The specific naming of each scorpion and its poison in Isis's healing spell exemplifies the Egyptian magical principle that knowing the true name of a force grants power over it.
