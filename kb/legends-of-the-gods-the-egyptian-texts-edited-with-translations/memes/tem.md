# Tem

## Overview

Tem (also called Temu, Atum) is the **evening form** of the Egyptian sun god, representing the sun at its setting and completing the daily solar cycle. He is one of the oldest creator gods in the Egyptian pantheon, associated with the Great Ennead of **Heliopolis**. The texts describe him as the third phase of the sun: "Khepera in the morning, and Ra at the culmination, and Temu in the evening" [014-090].

## Identity and Attributes

Tem is a primordial deity whose name may be related to "completeness" or "totality," reflecting his role as the sun that sets after completing its daily journey. He is described as the creator who "transferred the magical power" to the gods and humans [003-111].

The **two vessels of milk of Temu** are mentioned in magical texts as sacred objects associated with purification and protection [003-113]. Tem's mouth is listed among the divine body parts in ritual enumerations, alongside "the mouth of Neb-er-tcher" [005-106].

## The Solar Triad

Tem is the third and final phase of the **three principal forms of the sun god**:

| Form | Time of Day | Meaning |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Khepera** | Morning / Dawn | Becoming, self-creation |
| **Ra** | Noon / Culmination | Full power and glory |
| **Tem** | Evening / Sunset | Completion, rest |

The Egyptian text explains: "Khepera, Ra, and Temu were the three principal forms of the Sun-god, representing the rising sun, the midday sun, and the setting sun respectively" [014-130]. This tripartite division reflects the Egyptian understanding of the solar cycle as a continuous process of birth, power, and completion.

## The Heliopolitan Creation

Tem is central to the **Heliopolitan creation myth**, where he is the primeval god who emerged from the waters of **Nu** and brought forth the first divine pair through self-generation. In one account:

> "I am Temu, the creator of heaven and the things which are in it, and I came into being from out of the primeval matter" [008-014]

This Ennead (group of nine gods) of Heliopolis traces its origin to Tem, who produced **Shu** (air) and **Tefnut** (moisture), who in turn produced **Seb** (earth) and **Nut** (sky), establishing the cosmic order.

## Myths and Narratives

### The Evening Sun

The setting of the sun was understood as Tem's return to the primeval waters, completing the daily cycle of birth, power, and death. This made Tem particularly important in **funerary contexts**, where the deceased hoped to follow the sun's path through the underworld and be reborn like Khepera at dawn.

### The Magical Vessels

In the Bentresh legend, the **two vessels of milk of Temu** appear as ritual objects:

> "The two vessels of milk of Temu are the sacred vessels which restore life and protection" [003-113]

These vessels were used in healing rituals and are associated with the purifying and life-giving power of the creator god. Their invocation in the Bentresh narrative connects the healing of the princess to the primordial creative force of Tem.

## Cult and Worship

Tem's primary cult centre was **Heliopolis** (Egyptian *Iunu*, "the Pillar"), one of the oldest and most important religious sites in Egypt. Key aspects of his cult included:

- **The Ennead**: Tem headed the Great Ennead of Heliopolis, the nine-god council [014-048]
- **Association with the ben-ben**: The sacred stone of Heliopolis was associated with Tem's first emergence
- **Funerary role**: The deceased identified with Tem in his journey through the underworld, hoping to share in his renewal

Tem was also associated with **Heru-Hekennu**, a form of Horus connected with praise and adoration [014-048].
