# Tcheser

## Overview

Tcheser (also known as Djoser or Zoser) is a king of the **IIIrd Dynasty** of Egypt, famous in legend as the pharaoh during whose reign a great **seven-year famine** devastated the land. The narrative of the famine, inscribed on a stele on the island of Sehel near Elephantine, records how Tcheser appealed to the god **Khnemu** to restore the inundation of the Nile [004-022], [004-027].

## Historical Context

Tcheser reigned during the Old Kingdom, a period of pyramid building and the consolidation of the Egyptian state. The famine stele, though composed in a later period, uses Tcheser's name to give authority to its claims about temple endowments:

> "The seven years' famine took place in the reign of Tcheser, a king of the IIIrd Dynasty" [004-022]

The association of a great famine with Tcheser's reign may preserve the memory of a genuine historical climatic event: "according to the legend, king Tcheser had been made to suffer through a seven years' famine because the god Khnemu was angry" [004-027], [004-053].

## Major Myths and Narratives

### The Seven Years' Famine

The most significant narrative associated with Tcheser is the legend of the seven years' famine:

**The Cause**: The famine was not a random natural disaster but a divine punishment. The god **Khnemu**, lord of the cataract region, withheld the annual Nile inundation, causing the fields to dry up and the granaries to empty [004-027]. The connection between the cessation of the famine and Tcheser is tied to the idea that the king's neglect of Khnemu's cult had angered the god [004-032].

**The King's Appeal**: When the famine had become severe, "the king Tcheser sent to Matar, the Erpa Prince, who was the governor of the South, to ask him about the god of the inundation" [004-074]. Matar informed Tcheser about Khnemu's powers and the offerings due to him.

**The Divine Intervention**: In a dream or vision, Khnemu appeared to Tcheser and promised to restore the inundation in exchange for the king's vow to maintain the god's temple and provide offerings:

> "The name of Tcheser was to be associated with the god Khnemu for all time, and the king vowed to endow the temple of Khnemu with lands and offerings" [004-164]

**The Resolution**: The Nile rose again, the famine ended, and Tcheser issued a decree granting lands and revenues to the temple of Khnemu at Elephantine. This decree became the legal precedent for the temple's claims to these endowments in later periods.

### Tcheser and the Golden Horus

Tcheser bears the title of the "Golden Horus," one of the five traditional names of the pharaoh:

> "Neter-Khat, of the Golden Horus Tcheser" [017-003]

This title connects him to **Heru** (Horus) in his golden aspect, representing the incorruptible and eternal nature of the pharaoh's divine authority. The use of this title on the famine stele lends the narrative the weight of royal protocol and emphasizes the pharaoh's role as intermediary between the gods and humanity [017-003], [017-024].

## The Tcheser Stele

The primary source for the Tcheser narrative is the **Famine Stele**, discovered on the island of Sehel near the First Cataract. Though the text was composed during the Ptolemaic period, it purports to record a decree issued by Tcheser himself during the IIIrd Dynasty [004-022], [017-024].

The stele served a practical purpose: by attributing the temple endowments to a king as ancient and authoritative as Tcheser, the priests of Khnemu sought to legitimize their territorial and economic claims.

## Cult and Legacy

Tcheser is best known archaeologically as the builder of the **Step Pyramid** at Saqqara — the first monumental stone structure in human history, designed by his vizier Imhotep. While the pyramid is not mentioned in the mythological texts, the association of a great building project with his reign complements the legend of his authority and his relationship with the gods.

In the Egyptian mythological tradition, Tcheser represents the **ideal pharaoh** who, when faced with divine displeasure, sought the correct intercession rather than relying on force: "when Matar, the Erpa Prince and the governor of the temple, had informed him of the god Khnemu, the king made offerings and the famine ceased" [004-074].
