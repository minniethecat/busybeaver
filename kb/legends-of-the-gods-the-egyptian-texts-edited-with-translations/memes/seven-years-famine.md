# Seven Years' Famine

## Overview

The Seven Years' Famine was a catastrophic period during the reign of King [[Tcheser]] (IIIrd Dynasty) when the Nile failed to rise to its proper height for seven consecutive years. Grain became scarce, vegetables vanished, and society descended into collapse. The famine was ended through the intervention of the god [[Khnemu]] of Elephantine, who appeared to the king in a dream and promised to restore the annual inundation — on the condition that his neglected temple lands and revenues be restored. King Tcheser issued a royal decree endowing the temple of Khnemu with extensive territories and tithes, and the Nile rose once more.

## Narrative

### The Royal Despatch

In the eighteenth year of King Tcheser's reign, a royal despatch was brought to Matar, the governor of the temple-cities. The king's message conveyed the depth of the crisis [017-001–017-006]:

> "This is to inform thee that misery hath laid hold upon me as I sit upon the great throne... Hapi hath not come forth in my time to the proper height for seven years. Grain is very scarce, vegetables are lacking altogether, every kind of thing which men eat for their food hath ceased, and every man now plundereth his neighbour."

The king described a society in collapse: "Men wish to walk, but are unable to move, the child waileth, the young man draggeth his limbs along, and the hearts of the aged folk are crushed with despair; their legs give way under them, and they sink down to the ground" [017-007–017-018].

The storehouses, when opened, yielded nothing but wind. "Everything is in a state of ruin" [017-019–017-021].

### The Search for the Source of the Nile

In desperation, the king's mind turned to former times, recalling the advocate I-em-hetep (Imhotep), the son of [[Ptah]]. He asked: where is the birthplace of Hapi? What god presides over the Nile flood? Determined to find answers, he declared: "I would enter into the House of Life, I would unfold the written rolls therein, and I would lay my hand upon them" [017-022–017-038].

### Matar's Report

Governor Matar set out and returned with instruction concerning the increase of Hapi. He reported to the king that the Nile's source lay at [[Abu]] (Elephantine), the "City of the Beginning," where the god Khnemu dwelt. Matar described Khnemu's dwelling and his power over the inundation [017-040–017-066]:

> "He smiteth the ground with his sandals, and its fulness becometh abundant; he openeth the bolt of the door with his hand, and he throweth open the double door of the opening through which the water cometh" [017-063–017-066].

Khnemu was said to keep an account of the products of the Land of the South and the Land of the North, distributing to every god his proper share. The cord for measuring land and the tablet whereon the register was kept were in his possession [017-067–017-076].

### The Dream-Vision and Divine Promise

The king entered the temple of Khnemu at Elephantine, made offerings, and the god appeared before him. Khnemu opened his eyes and spoke with authority [017-138–017-142]:

> "I am Khnemu, who fashioned thee. My two hands were about thee and knitted together thy body, and made healthy thy members; and it is I who gave thee thy heart."

The god lamented that no man had worked the minerals to build or restore the houses of the gods, "notwithstanding that I am the Lord and the Creator" [017-142–017-150].

Khnemu then proclaimed his divine identity and made his promise:

> "I will make Hapi rise for thee, and in no year shall he fail, and he shall spread himself out in rest upon every land. Green plants and herbs and trees shall bow beneath the weight of their produce... Misery shall pass away, and the emptiness of their store-houses of grain shall come to an end" [017-166–017-177].

### The Royal Decree

Awakening with renewed courage, King Tcheser issued a decree endowing the temple of Khnemu [017-178–017-180]:

- All lands for twenty *schoinos* on both banks of the Nile around Abu, with gardens and river front
- A tithe (one tenth) of all produce, fish, fowl, game, and metals brought into the region
- A tithe of all calves for daily burnt offerings
- Exemption for temple craftsmen and sculptors from tithing
- The decree was to be inscribed upon a stele in the holy place, following the original document cut upon wood [017-239–017-246].

## Outcome and Significance

The Seven Years' Famine legend holds multiple layers of significance:

1. **Theological Dominion of Khnemu**: The story establishes Khnemu as the divine controller of the Nile inundation, a role that the priests of Elephantine promoted to secure their temple's economic position [004-041–004-048].

2. **Royal-Temple Economy**: King Tcheser's decree formalised a system of tithes and land grants to the temple, encoding a broader pattern of Egyptian royal endowment of temples in exchange for divine favour.

3. **Famine as Divine Punishment**: The failure of the Nile was interpreted as a consequence of neglected temple worship — a causal link between human piety and cosmic order that underpinned Egyptian religion.

4. **Historical Basis**: While the stele was composed centuries after Tcheser's reign, references to "several famines which lasted for seven years" in Egyptian records suggest a historical core to the legend. Arab chroniclers also record seven-year famines in Egypt [004-047–004-061].

5. **Nile as Life-Giver**: The vivid description of the Nile's embrace bringing life to "every nose" encapsulates the Egyptian understanding of the river as the sole source of existence in an otherwise desert land [017-162–017-164].
