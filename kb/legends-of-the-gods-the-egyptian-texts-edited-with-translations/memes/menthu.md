# Menthu

## Overview

Menthu (later *Montu*) is the falcon-headed war-god of [[thebes]] (*Waset*), embodying martial prowess and destructive strength. His name is invoked in royal inscriptions comparing the pharaoh to his battle might, and his divine body parts are called upon in the magical healing spells of the Book of the Dead, where the "thighs of Menthu" lend their power to counteract poison.

## Identity and Role

Menthu is the god of war and battle, the divine warrior whose strength is legendary. Budge glosses him as "the War-god of Thebes" [FN#156]. In the Theban region, he was worshipped as Menthu-Ra, a solar-warrior synthesis combining the ferocity of the war-god with the creative power of the sun.

His primary attribute is might in combat — he is "the mighty one of strength" [016-126], and to "come forth on the day of battle like Menthu" [016-125] is the highest martial compliment that can be paid to a king:

> the king who is a bull, [and] a god who cometh forth on the day of battle like Menthu, the mighty one of strength like the son of Nut [016-124]

This passage, from a hymn describing [[rameses]] II, demonstrates how Menthu's name became synonymous with battlefield supremacy in royal ideology.

## Family and Relationships

Menthu's position in the divine genealogies of Thebes is less prominent than the Theban triad of [[amen]], Mut, and [[khensu]]. In some traditions, he is associated with the goddess Tjenenet or with Raet-Tawy as his consort. The corpus preserved in this text does not elaborate his family connections, focusing instead on his martial attributes and his role in magical protection.

The "son of Nut" epithet in the royal comparison [016-126] may align Menthu with the celestial realm, as [[nut]] is the sky goddess who births the sun and stars.

## Major Myths and Narratives

**The Divine Body of the Cat.** In the healing spell of the Cat (Chapter 19 of the corpus), Menthu's thighs form part of the composite divine body invoked to protect against scorpion venom. Each part of the Cat's body is identified with a different deity, creating a collective divine defense:

> the neck of Neheb-ka, the breast of Thoth, the heart of Ra, the hands of the gods, the belly of Osiris, the thighs of Menthu, the legs of Khensu [005-107]

The same motif recurs in a second version of the spell:

> thy thighs are the thighs of the god Menthu, who shall make thy thighs to stand up, and shall bring the poison to the ground [019-036]

In both instances, Menthu's thighs specifically provide the power to stand firm and resist the poison's weakening effect — his martial strength redirected to magical healing.

**The Pharaoh as Menthu Incarnate.** The comparison of [[rameses]] II to Menthu in the Hymn to Osiris [016-124] reflects the long-standing Egyptian tradition of identifying the reigning king with the war-god in battle contexts. The pharaoh "cometh forth on the day of battle like Menthu," embodying the god's irresistible charge.

## Cult and Worship

Menthu's principal cult centres were in the Theban nome, particularly at Medamud (ancient *Mabu*), north-east of [[thebes]], where a temple dedicated to him has been excavated; at Armant (ancient *Iuny*), south of Thebes; and at Tod (ancient *Djerty*). These three cult sites formed a triad of Menthu temples in the Theban region.

During the 11th Dynasty, whose rulers bore names compounded with Menthu (Mentuhotep, "Menthu is satisfied"), the god enjoyed particular prominence. His cult declined after the 12th Dynasty as [[amen]] rose to supremacy in Thebes, though Menthu retained his identity as the pre-eminent war-god of the region throughout Egyptian history.
