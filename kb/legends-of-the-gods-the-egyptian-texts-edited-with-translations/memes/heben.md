# Heben

**Heben** was a city in the region of [[Edfu]] in Upper Egypt, the seat of **Horus of Heben**, a local form of the falcon god. It was the site of a celebrated battle between Horus and the followers of [[Set]] on the river-bank, after which a priest established an annual festival to commemorate the god's victory.

## Overview

Heben appears in the **Legend of the Winged Sun Disk**, the inscription on the walls of the temple of Edfu that recounts the wars of [[Horus of Behutet]] against Set and his confederates. The city gave its name to a local manifestation of Horus — "Horus of Heben" — and became the site of a lasting cultic observance.

## The Battle at the River-Bank

The decisive episode at Heben occurred when Horus confronted Set's followers at the river-bank of the city:

> "They reached the river-bank of the city of Heben. Then Ra said unto Thoth, 'Let us call this place Heben from this day forward.'" *(015-122, 015-128)*

The naming of the city itself is presented as a consequence of this battle — Ra and [[Thoth]] decreed that the location should bear the name Heben in perpetuity, commemorating the divine victory. This etiological myth explains the city's name through the lens of divine action, a common pattern in Egyptian sacred geography.

## Horus of Heben

The form of Horus worshipped at Heben received direct address from Ra during the battle:

> "And Ra said unto Horus of Heben, 'O Winged Disk, thou great god...'" *(015-114)*

This invocation established Horus of Heben as a recognized local hypostasis of the great falcon god, distinguished by his association with this specific locale and its defining battle. The multiplicity of Horus forms — Horus of Behutet at Edfu, Horus of Heben — reflects the Egyptian practice of localizing major deities at specific cult sites.

## The Priest's Commemoration

The battle's memory was preserved through an annual festival instituted by the priest of Heben:

> "To commemorate this event the priest of Heben..." *(002-105)*

This festival functioned as a ritual reenactment, keeping the divine victory present in the community's religious calendar. The priest of Heben thus served as the custodian not only of the cult but of the city's foundational narrative.

## In the Texts

The lands of "the city of Heben, in a..." (015-130) are mentioned as the broader territory associated with the settlement. Heben's integration into the Edfu temple inscription — one of the most extensive surviving mythological texts — confirms its recognized place in the sacred geography of Upper Egypt during the Ptolemaic period.
