# Khensu

## Overview

Khensu (Khonsu) is the Theban moon-god, son of Amun and Mut in the Theban triad. He is known in two principal forms: **Khensu Nefer-hetep** ("Khensu the Merciful"), the great god who grants oracles and healing, and **Khensu Pa-ari-sekher** ("Khensu the Doer of Plans"), the form who drives out possessing spirits. His temple at Thebes (Karnak) was a centre of healing pilgrimage.

## Identity and Role

Khensu functions as a divine healer and protector, with power to expel disease-causing spirits. In his aspect as Nefer-hetep, he receives prayers and grants favourable oracles [003-073]; as Pa-ari-sekher, he actively combats spiritual affliction [003-101]. The priests of his temple at Thebes were adept at magnifying his reputation for miraculous interventions [003-020].

## Family and Relationships

In the Theban triad, Khensu is the son of Amun-Ra and Mut. His temple precinct lies within the larger Karnak complex. He collaborates with other deities: the priests of Khensu carried his statue to where the statue of Pa-ari-sekher stood to transfer divine power [003-068], [003-122].

## Major Myths and Narratives

The principal narrative featuring Khensu is the **Legend of the Possessed Princess of Bekhten** (Chapter 3). When the chief of Bekhten brings tribute to Egypt, he seeks divine help for his sister Bent-reshy, who is possessed by a spirit. Rameses II entreats Khensu Nefer-hetep to look favourably upon the afflicted princess [003-072].

> "Then the priests of Khensu Nefer-hetep carried the statue..." [003-068]

The statue of Khensu is brought before the princess, and magical power is transferred to Khensu Pa-ari-sekher so that he may expel the possessing spirit [003-101]. The spirit, recognizing Khensu's authority, departs peacefully after being granted a feast. This text, found in the temple of Khensu at Thebes, demonstrates his role as a divine exorcist [003-003].

## Cult and Worship

The temple of Khensu at **Thebes** (within the Karnak precinct) was the primary cult centre. His statue served as an oracle and healing medium. The Bent-reshy stele—discovered in his temple—was likely commissioned by the priests to magnify the god's healing reputation [003-020]. Khensu Nefer-hetep was regarded as a great god capable of granting petitions made by pharaohs [003-076].
