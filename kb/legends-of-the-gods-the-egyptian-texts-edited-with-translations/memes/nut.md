# Nut

## Overview

Nut is the goddess of the sky, one of the Great Ennead of [[heliopolis|Heliopolis]]. She is the daughter of [[shu|Shu]] and [[tefnut|Tefnut]], sister-wife of Keb (the earth), and mother of [[osiris|Osiris]], [[isis|Isis]], [[set|Set]], and Nephthys. Her arched body forms the vault of heaven, and she plays a central role in solar mythology as the mother who swallows and rebirths the sun each day.

## Identity and Role

Nut personifies the visible sky—the star-speckled arc above the earth. She is the cosmic mother from whose womb the stars and gods emerge. In the Legend of Ra and the Destruction of Mankind (Chapter 1), she transforms into a cow to carry the aged Ra upon her back after his decision to withdraw from the world [001-100], [001-101].

> "Then the goddess Nut trembled in her position as she bore the great god" [001-125]

When Ra declares his intention to leave the earth, Nut is commanded to bear him aloft [001-115]. Her body becomes the heavens, and [[shu|Shu]] is placed beneath her to support her weight [001-129]. The text describes how she shook under the burden of carrying the creator-god.

## Family and Relationships

Nut is the daughter of [[shu|Shu]] (air) and [[tefnut|Tefnut]] (moisture). She is the sister and consort of **Keb** (earth-god), and through him the mother of the four great gods of the Osirian cycle: [[osiris|Osiris]], [[isis|Isis]], [[set|Set]], and Nephthys [004-106]. The creation texts record that Seb and Nut "brought forth" the next generation of deities from their union [007-043].

The Heliopolitan cosmology places Nut as the third generation of the Ennead, following [[ra|Ra]] (the creator), then [[shu|Shu]] and [[tefnut|Tefnut]], then Nut and Keb, and finally the Osirian gods.

## Major Myths and Narratives

Nut appears in several key narratives. In the **Destruction of Mankind** (Chapter 1), she transforms into the Heavenly Cow at Ra's command, bearing him upon her back as he departs from the corrupt world below [001-101], [001-115].

In the **Creation of the Gods** (Chapter 7), Nut is brought forth as part of the unfolding of the cosmos, with [[shu|Shu]] and [[tefnut|Tefnut]] producing Seb and Nut from their primordial union [007-043].

In funerary texts (Chapter 9), Nut appears as a protective mother-figure who guards the deceased: "thou goddess Nut, place him..." [009-098]. She speaks directly in these texts, addressing the creator and receiving the dead into her celestial embrace. Ra commands her: "my daughter Nut, guard thou for me the support of the sky" [009-133].

One passage describes the creator uniting with Nut to bring forth the next cosmic cycle [009-116].

## Cult and Worship

Nut was venerated throughout Egypt as a cosmic deity. Her image appears on countless tomb ceilings, where her star-covered body arches protectively over the deceased. She had no single major cult centre but was universally recognized as the mother of gods. Her iconography shows a woman arched over the earth with stars across her body, sometimes depicted as a cow.

She was invoked in funerary contexts as "she who bore the gods" and the one who would receive the deceased into the sky [009-127].
