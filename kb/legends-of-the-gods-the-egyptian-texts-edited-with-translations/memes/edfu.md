# Edfu

**Edfu** (Egyptian **Behutet**, Greek **Apollinopolis Magna**) was the cult center of [[Horus|Heru-Behutet]] (Horus of Edfu), the site of his great victory over the enemies of [[Ra]], and the origin of the [[Winged Disk]] symbol. Located in Upper Egypt between Aswan and Luxor, it houses the best-preserved temple in Egypt.

## Overview

Edfu stands on the west bank of the Nile in Upper Egypt. Known in ancient Egyptian as **Behutet** or **Djeba**, it was the cult center of **Heru-Behutet** — the form of [[Horus]] worshipped as the winged protector of Egypt. The Temple of Horus at Edfu, completed during the Ptolemaic period, is the best-preserved ancient Egyptian temple and contains extensive reliefs depicting the Winged Disk myth.

## Mythological Significance

Edfu is the primary setting of the **Legend of Horus of Behutet and the Winged Disk**. When the enemies of [[Ra]] gathered before the city, [[Horus]] transformed into a great winged disk and attacked them from the sky:

> "His Majesty of Ra-Harmachis said to Heru-Behutet, 'Thou art my exalted son who didst come forth from me. Thou hast overthrown the enemies of the god.'" *(002-021)*

The local priests of Edfu attributed many victories to their god, using the legend to magnify the importance of their temple:

> "The priests of Edfu, wishing to magnify their local god, Horus of Behutet, or Horus of Edfu, attributed to him the acts and deeds of several other gods." *(002-022)*

After his triumph, [[Horus]] was given the name **Behutet** — "He of Edfu" — and this name was recorded ever after at the temple. When [[Horus]] returned to Edfu in victory, [[Ra]] ordered that an image of the winged disk be placed in every sanctuary throughout Egypt (002-212).

## In the Texts

The corpus devotes an extensive section to the events at Edfu (chapter 2). [[Ra-Harmachis]] sailed down the river to Edfu after the initial battle, and there he beheld the carnage wrought by [[Horus]]:

> "Then spake Ra-Harmachis unto his son Heru-Behutet: 'Thou hast done well, O my son. The enemies of the god have been slain before the city of Edfu.'" *(015-059)*

The Winged Disks placed at Edfu are among the most famous iconographic representations in Egyptian art. Two such disks are represented at the temple of Edfu, flanking the entrance — symbols of Horus's eternal protection of the sanctuary (015-451).
