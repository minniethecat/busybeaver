# Shu

## Overview

Shu is the Heliopolitan god of air, light, and the dry atmosphere—the space between sky and earth. As the first act of physical creation, Shu separated his children [[nut|Nut]] (the sky) from Keb (the earth), lifting her body above and holding her in eternal position. He is one of the Great Ennead of [[heliopolis|Heliopolis]], the creator-god [[ra|Ra]]'s first offspring.

## Identity and Role

Shu embodies the life-giving dry air and light that fills the cosmos. In the cosmogony of Heliopolis, he was self-created from the primordial waters (Nu) or emerged from Ra's spittle. His defining act is the separation of sky and earth: when Nut changed herself into a cow to carry the aged Ra upon her back, Ra commanded Shu to support her [001-100], [001-101].

> "Thus Shu stood with his hands beneath Nut, holding her up in position" [001-129]

Shu also appears as a protective deity: Ra entreated Shu to protect his creations, and Shu spread himself between heaven and earth as a shield [001-128]. He is associated with the breath of life, and one text records that magical power was transferred from Ra to Shu [003-111].

## Family and Relationships

Shu is the son of [[ra|Ra]] (or the self-created one from Nu) and brother-husband of [[tefnut|Tefnut]], the goddess of moisture. His children are **Keb** (the earth-god) and **Nut** (the sky-goddess), whom he eternally separates [004-106]. Through them he is grandfather of [[osiris|Osiris]], [[isis|Isis]], [[set|Set]], and Nephthys.

The creator text of Chapter 7 describes Shu and Tefnut emerging together from the primordial waters, rejoicing as the first pair [007-028]. They "brought forth" the next generation of deities from their union [007-042], [007-043].

## Major Myths and Narratives

The primary myth of Shu is the **Separation of Heaven and Earth**. As Ra grew weary of dwelling among mortals, Nut transformed into a cow. Ra commanded Shu to position himself beneath her and hold her aloft, creating the space in which all life exists [001-101], [001-129].

In the Heliopolitan creation account (Chapter 7), Shu and Tefnut are the first differentiated beings, brought forth from the mouth of the creator. After a period of being "covered up" in the primordial waters, they emerged and rejoiced, then set about producing the next gods [007-024], [007-025], [007-028].

Shu was synthetically linked with [[khnemu|Khnemu]] in later theology, as both held creative and sustaining functions [001-190].

## Cult and Worship

Shu was worshipped primarily at **Heliopolis** (Annu) as part of the Great Ennead, and at **Leontopolis** where he was the chief deity paired with Tefnut. His iconography shows a man wearing an ostrich feather (the hieroglyph for his name) and with arms raised in the act of supporting the sky.
