# Hapi

## Overview

Hapi is the divine personification of the Nile River and its annual inundation — the flood that deposited fertile silt on the riverbanks and made Egyptian agriculture possible. Unlike most Egyptian deities, Hapi is not associated with a specific cult centre or elaborate mythology; rather, he embodies the river itself as a benevolent, life-sustaining force.

## Identity and Role

Hapi is the Nile in its divine aspect — the "father of the gods" whose waters give life to Egypt. He is typically depicted as a plump, androgynous figure with pendulous breasts (symbolizing fertility and nourishment) and aquatic plants on his head. His annual flood, known as the "Inundation" (*Akhet*), was the most important event in the Egyptian calendar:

> the god allowed Hapi to pour forth his streams from the caverns in the Cataract, and to flood the land with abundance. [004-034]

Hapi's waters were believed to emerge from two caverns at the First Cataract near Elephantine (Abu). The god [[khnemu]] controlled the bolt that released the flood:

> Here is the bed of Hapi (the Nile), wherein he reneweth his youth [in his season]. "'Abu' was its name in ancient time." [017-057]

> Hapi, who riseth according to his will, in his season [017-157]

## Family and Relationships

Hapi occupies a unique position in the Egyptian pantheon. He is listed among the company of the great gods:

> the Nile-god, Shu, Keb, Nut, Osiris, Isis, Nephthys, and Horus. [004-106]

> Anqet, Hap (the Nile-god), Shu, Keb, Nut, Osiris, Horus, Isis, and Nephthys [017-106]

He is closely associated with [[khnemu]], the ram-headed god of the Cataract region who controlled the gates through which the Nile waters flowed. The goddess [[anqet]] (Anukis) and the goddess [[sept]] (Sothis) are his companions in the southern region — Sothis, the Dog-star, whose heliacal rising heralded the beginning of the inundation.

## Major Myths and Narratives

**The Famine Stela.** The most detailed account of Hapi's role appears in the legend of the Seven Years' Famine during the reign of King [[tcheser]]. When the king neglected the worship of [[khnemu]], the god withheld the Nile flood:

> [which hath happened] because Hapi (i.e., the Nile) hath not come in his time for a period of seven years. [017-010]

Upon restoring Khnemu's temple and offerings, Tcheser was rewarded:

> the god allowed Hapi to pour forth his streams from the caverns in the Cataract [004-034]

**The Mystery of Hapi's Source.** An ancient ritual question preserved in the texts asks:

> Where is the place of birth of Hapi (the Nile)? What god, or what goddess, presideth over it? [017-032]

The answer identifies the region of the First Cataract, near Elephantine, where two *qerti* (caverns) served as the breasts of the Nile from which the waters flowed:

> the two caverns, which were the breasts of the Nile-god, that it rose to a great height [004-035]

**Hapi and the Dead.** In funerary texts, the deceased identifies himself with Hapi's life-giving power:

> the waters of Hapi (the Nile) burst forth, I am the Being [014-085]

> flesh just as Hapi (i.e., the Nile) taketh possession of the land [014-034]

## Cult and Worship

Hapi had no single cult centre; instead, he was worshipped throughout Egypt wherever the Nile flowed. Offerings were cast into the river at the start of the inundation season, and hymns to Hapi celebrated his bounty. The Nile-god was praised as the provider of all sustenance:

> He gave me instruction concerning the increase of Hapi, and he showed me the stream wherefrom Hapi maketh his appearance [017-046]
