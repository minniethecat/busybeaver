# Khnemu

## Overview

Khnemu (Khnum) is the ram-headed creator-god of the First Cataract region, worshipped primarily at Elephantine and throughout Upper Egypt. He is the divine potter who fashions the bodies of all living beings on his wheel and controls the life-giving flood of the Nile. Chapter 4 of the corpus preserves the Legend of Khnemu and the Seven Years' Famine, a major text inscribed on a rock at the island of Sehel.

## Identity and Role

Khnemu holds two central offices: he is the **controller of the Nile-flood**, releasing its waters from the twin caverns beneath Elephantine, and the **divine potter** who shapes human forms. The priests of Khnemu asserted that he was coeval with the oldest dynasties, making his worship of great antiquity [004-048], [004-051]. His power over the inundation was absolute: when he drew the bolts of the Nile-caverns, the waters rose [004-099].

> "Khnemu, god of the First Cataract" [004-041]

The god's appearance combines a human body with a ram's head bearing horizontal twisting horns. He was sometimes identified with [[shu|Shu]] in later theological synthesis [004-102].

## Family and Relationships

Khnemu headed a divine company at Elephantine that included the Nile-god (Hep), [[shu|Shu]], Keb, [[nut|Nut]], [[osiris|Osiris]], [[isis|Isis]], Nephthys, and [[heru|Heru]] [004-106]. He was also associated with Satis and Anukis, who formed the Elephantine triad with him. The text records that Nubian envoys and Egyptian princes alike came to his temple bearing offerings [004-111].

## Major Myths and Narratives

The **Legend of Khnemu and the Seven Years' Famine** (Chapter 4) is the defining myth. During the reign of King Tcheser (Djoser of the Third Dynasty), Egypt suffers seven years of famine when the Nile fails to rise. The king consults his high priest, who discovers that Khnemu controls the inundation from his seat at Elephantine [004-099].

King Tcheser restores the neglected offerings of Khnemu and re-endows his sanctuary with lands and revenues [004-033]. The god appears to the king in a dream, promising abundant harvests in return for proper worship. This text—inscribed on a rock at Sehel—was likely commissioned by the priests of Khnemu to assert their temple's ancient privileges [004-042].

## Cult and Worship

The primary cult centre was at **Elephantine** (Abu), the island at the First Cataract near modern Aswan. The temple of Khnemu there housed the sacred ram and maintained the rites governing the Nile's inundation. Worship of Khnemu extended throughout the cataract region, and his fame spread to Nubia and beyond [004-111].
