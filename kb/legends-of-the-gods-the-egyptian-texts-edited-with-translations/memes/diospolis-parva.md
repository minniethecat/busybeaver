# Diospolis Parva

**Diospolis Parva** (Egyptian **Sma-Behutet**, also known as **Diospolis of Lower Egypt** or "Thebes of the North") was a city in the Nile Delta, designated as the capital of a Delta nome. It was the site of a seven-cubit Nile measure and a burial place of one of the dismembered pieces of [[Osiris]].

## Overview

Diospolis Parva — "the Lesser City of Zeus" — was the Delta counterpart to [[Thebes]] (Diospolis Magna, "the Greater City of Zeus"). Despite its small size, the city held ritual significance as a Nile-measuring station and an Osiris burial site. The name Sma-Behutet associates it with **Horus of Behutet**, the winged sun disk deity, though the city's primary mythological importance derives from its role in the Osiris cycle.

## The Seven-Cubit Nile Measure

The Nile's height at Diospolis Parva was carefully recorded as part of the Egyptian system of Nilometers that tracked the annual inundation:

> "The Nile rose to the height of seven cubits at Sma-Behutet, or Diospolis Parva in the Delta..." *(004-098)*

This measurement of seven cubits placed Diospolis Parva among the network of Nilometer stations that measured the flood's progress from south to north — from the twenty-eight cubits at [[Elephantine]] to the seven cubits at Diospolis Parva in the Delta (017-062). These measurements were crucial for predicting agricultural yields and determining taxation.

## An Osiris Burial Site

Diospolis Parva was one of the cities where Isis buried a portion of Osiris's dismembered body during her journey across Egypt. This tradition, recorded by Plutarch, gave each city a claim to possess a tomb of Osiris:

> "The portions of his body are: Koptos... Diospolis Parva..." *(023-595)*

By interring a piece of the god's body at Diospolis Parva, Isis sanctified the city and established it as a site of Osirian veneration. This pattern of distributed burial legitimized local cult centers throughout Egypt, each able to claim a direct connection to the god.

## In the Texts

The city's dual identity — as both a pragmatic Nilometer station and a sacred Osiris burial site — reflects the Egyptian integration of practical geography and mythological landscape. Though Diospolis Parva has fewer direct corpus references than major cities like Heliopolis or Abydos, its inclusion in the comprehensive nome listing and the Osiris burial tradition confirms its recognized place in the sacred geography of Egypt.
