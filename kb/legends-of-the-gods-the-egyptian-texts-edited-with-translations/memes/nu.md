# Nu

## Overview

Nu (also Nun) is the primordial water-god of Egyptian cosmology: the limitless, dark World-Ocean that existed before creation. He is the father of [[ra|Ra]], the source of the "celestial waters" that feed the Nile, and the eternal abyss upon which the ordered world floats. Unlike the active creator-deities who emerged from him, Nu remains a personified substance — yet he speaks, commands, and participates in the drama of creation.

## Identity and Role

Nu is the "great celestial ocean" from which all things originate. Before creation, there existed a vast mass of water called Nu — a state of formless, motionless inactivity. The creator-god [[khepera|Khepera]] raised himself out of the ocean Nu, and from that moment the ordered cosmos came into being.

Nu is addressed as a deity with full personhood. The texts describe "Addressing Nu, the personification of the World-Ocean" [001-044]. He is called "father": the creator declares "My father Nu saith" [008-040], and speaks of being "in Nu side by side with my god Nu" [009-010]. He is the "first-born god" from whom Ra himself receives counsel [001-153].

> "Then Ra spake unto Nu, saying: 'O thou first-born god, from whom I came into being…'" [001-153]

Nu remains present as the sustaining water-source throughout the myths. From his waters, all life first stirred, and to his depths the sun returned each night. The great celestial ocean called Nu is the ultimate source of the Nile's inundation [001-044].

## Family and Relationships

Nu is the father-progenitor of all creation. From his waters emerged the first active gods: [[ra|Ra]], [[khepera|Khepera]], [[shu|Shu]], and [[tefnut|Tefnut]] [007-012], [007-024]. Unlike the later generations of deities who marry and beget children, Nu has no consort — he is the solitary source, the undifferentiated One from whom all multiplicity arises.

The creator addresses him as "my father Nu" [008-040], and in the Destruction of Mankind legend, the aged Ra repeatedly turns to Nu for advice [001-153]. Nu commands Shu to support Nut, demonstrating his authority over the highest cosmic structures [001-100].

## Major Myths and Narratives

**Creation from the Abyss**: The creator ([[khepera|Khepera]] / [[neb-er-tcher|Neb-Er-Tcher]]) speaks from within Nu's waters, declaring: "I found no place to stand. I raised myself up from out of Nu, from a state of inactivity" [008-021]. From this emergence, the ordered sequence of creation unfolds — Shu and Tefnut first, then Seb and Nut, then the full Ennead [007-012], [007-043].

> "I myself raised them up from out of Nu, from a state of inactivity" [007-012]

**The Destruction of Mankind**: Ra, grown old and beset by rebellious humanity, commands [[shu|Shu]] to "go to the god Nu, and to tell him to set a guard" [001-153]. Nu brings forth the celestial cow ([[nut|Nut]]) to carry Ra into the heavens. Later, Ra "embraced the god Nu" as he prepared to depart the earth [001-100].

**Ra's Self-Declaration**: The god [[ra|Ra]] "declared himself to be Nu, the Great God, who came into being of himself" [004-129] — identifying the creator with the primordial source itself.

## Cult and Worship

Nu had no formal cult centre or temple in the manner of [[ra|Ra]] or [[osiris|Osiris]]. As a primordial abstract deity, he was invoked in cosmogonic hymns, funerary texts, and the theology of Hermopolis (where he formed one of the Ogdoad — eight primeval deities paired with Naunet). His enduring influence is felt in the concept that the Nile's annual flood issues from the "great celestial ocean called Nu."
