# Succession of Horus

## Overview

The Succession of Horus is the culmination of the Osirian myth cycle. After the murder of Osiris, the search by Isis, the birth and hidden childhood of Horus, and the judgment in Anu, Horus finally inherited the throne of his father. By the will of Osiris and the verdict of the divine tribunal, Horus was vindicated as the rightful heir and proclaimed ruler of the Two Lands (Upper and Lower Egypt). This event established the mythological foundation of Egyptian kingship: every pharaoh was identified with Horus, the legitimate son who avenged his father and restored cosmic order.

## Narrative

### The Will of Osiris

The Egyptian texts consistently emphasize that Horus inherited the throne by his father's express will, not merely by conquest:

> "Horus inherited the throne by his father's will, a fact which is so often emphasized in the texts that it seems there may be some ground for Plutarch's view." [FN#313]

This emphasis on testamentary succession distinguished Horus's claim from Set's, which rested purely on usurpation. Horus was "the son of Osiris, whose heart is firmly stablished, the triumphant one, the son of Isis, the flesh and bone of Osiris." [PN=010-002]

### The Divine Tribunal's Verdict

The succession was secured through legal process as well as rightful inheritance. Thoth's advocacy in the Judgment Hall of Anu and Ra's endorsement provided the highest divine sanction:

> "The final words of Thoth comforted her greatly, for he told her that he would take charge of the case of Horus in the Judgment Hall of Anu, wherein Osiris had been judged, and that as his advocate he would make any accusations which might be brought against Horus to recoil on him that brought them." [PN=005-1787]

Ra himself served as Horus's advocate, establishing an unbroken chain of divine endorsement from father to son:

> "Thoth also promised Isis that Ra himself should act as the advocate of Horus, even as he had done for his father Osiris." [PN=005-1795]

### Enthronement and Vindication

Isis, who had guided Horus from birth through hidden childhood to this moment of triumph, saw her son seated on his father's throne:

> "She made to rise up the helpless members of him whose heart was at rest, she drew from him his essence, and she made therefrom an heir... His hand is mighty (or, victorious) within the house of Keb, and the Company of the Gods rejoice greatly at the coming of Horus, the son of Osiris." [PN=010-002]

The succession was celebrated by the entire divine assembly. The Tchatcha of Truth, the Company of the Gods, Neb-er-tcher himself, and the Lords of Truth all gathered together in acknowledgment of Horus's rightful rule. [PN=010-002]

### The Vengeance Fulfilled

Horus did not merely inherit — he avenged. The texts explicitly connect his succession to the defeat of his father's murderer:

> "He avenged his father Osiris by vanquishing Set." [FN#206]

The Pyramid Texts elaborate: "He avengeth thee in his name of 'Horus, the son who avenged his father.'" [PN=009-038] And further: "Horus hath given thee his Eye, thou hast taken possession of the Urert Crown thereby at the head of the gods." [PN=009-038]

## Outcome and Significance

The Succession of Horus established principles that defined Egyptian civilization:

1. **Divine Kingship**: The pharaoh was identified with Horus in life (and with Osiris in death). Every coronation of an Egyptian king was a reenactment of Horus's succession.

2. **Legitimate Inheritance**: The succession established the principle that kingship passes from father to son by right and by will — not by force or usurpation. This legitimized the dynastic principle central to Egyptian monarchy.

3. **Restoration of Maat**: Horus's succession restored the cosmic order (Maat) that Set's murder of Osiris had disrupted. The triumph of Horus represented order over chaos, legitimacy over usurpation, and life over death.

4. **Fulfillment of the Myth Cycle**: The succession completed the narrative arc: Osiris's coronation → murder by Set → Isis's search → resurrection → birth of Horus → judgment → succession. This cycle became the central myth of Egyptian religion.

5. **Model of Filial Piety**: Horus's devotion to avenging and succeeding his father made him the ideal son, a model emulated in Egyptian funerary texts where the deceased hoped their own sons would perform the same rites.
