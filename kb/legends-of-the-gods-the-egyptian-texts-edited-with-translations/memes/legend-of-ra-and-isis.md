# Legend of Ra and Isis

## Overview

The Legend of Ra and Isis tells how the goddess [[Isis]], ambitious to become mistress of the earth and equal to the Sun-god, crafted a sacred serpent from the spittle of the aging [[Ra]] and set it upon his path. When the serpent's venom struck Ra down, Isis offered to heal him — but only on the condition that he reveal his secret, hidden name to her. Through this cunning, Isis obtained the knowledge of the divine name and with it supreme magical power over the creator of the universe.

## Narrative

### Isis's Ambition

The text opens by describing Ra as the divine god who created himself and made the heavens, the earth, and all living things — whose "names by reason of their multitude are unknowable, for even the gods know them not." Against this omnipotent creator, the goddess Isis, living in the form of a woman who possessed knowledge of words of power, began to scheme [014-001–014-013].

> "Was it not possible to become even as was Ra in heaven and upon earth, and to make herself mistress of the earth, and a mighty goddess — thus she meditated in her heart — by the knowledge of the Name of the holy god?" [014-011–014-014]

### The Divine Spittle and the Serpent

Ra had grown old. "He dribbled at the mouth, and he let his emissions go forth from him upon the earth, and his spittle fell upon the ground." Isis gathered this spittle, kneaded it with dust, and fashioned it into a sacred serpent — shaped as a dart so that none might escape it alive. She left it lying on the road where the great god travelled daily about the two lands [014-016–014-023].

### The Serpent Strikes

As Ra passed on his daily journey, the sacred serpent shot its fang into him. "The living fire was departing from the god's own body." Ra cried out, his voice reaching the heavens. The company of the gods gathered, asking what had happened — but the god "found no words wherewith to answer concerning himself." His jaws shook, his lips trembled, and the poison consumed his flesh as the Nile flood consumes the land [014-024–014-035].

### Ra's Lamentation

Gathering his courage, Ra addressed the gods who had come forth from his own body:

> "I have been smitten by some deadly thing, of which my heart hath no knowledge, and which I have neither seen with my eyes nor made with my hand... I am a Prince, the son of a Prince... My father and my mother uttered my name, and hid it in my body at my birth so that none of those who would use against me words of power might succeed in making their enchantments have dominion over me." [014-040–014-051]

### The Bargain for the Name

Isis came forward with her words of magic — she whose "words putteth together destroy diseases, and her words make to live those whose throats are choked." She declared that the serpent's venom would be overthrown, but then made her demand: "O my divine father, tell me thy name, for he who is able to pronounce his name liveth" [014-062–014-076].

Ra responded by reciting his great titles: maker of heaven and earth, creator of the waters, he who opens his eyes and light comes, he who shuts his eyes and there is darkness, Khepera in the morning, Ra at noon, and Temu in the evening. But the poison did not retreat. Isis pressed him: "Among the things which thou hast said unto me thy name hath not been mentioned. O declare thou it unto me, and the poison shall come forth" [014-077–014-096].

### The Surrender of the Name

The poison burned with blazing fire, stronger than any flame. Ra could endure no more. He said, "I will allow myself to be searched through by Isis, and my name shall come forth from my body and go into hers." The divine one hid himself from the gods, the throne in the Boat of Millions of Years was empty, and the name passed from Ra into Isis [014-097–014-105].

Then Isis declared:

> "Flow on, poison, and come forth from Ra... I have worked, and I make the poison to fall on the ground, for the venom hath been mastered. Verily the name hath been taken away from the great god. Let Ra live, and let the poison die; and if the poison live then Ra shall die." [014-106–014-114]

## Outcome and Significance

The Legend of Ra and Isis encapsulates several foundational concepts in Egyptian religion:

1. **The Power of the Divine Name**: Knowledge of a god's true name conferred mastery over that god. Ra's secret name, hidden in his body from birth, was his ultimate talisman — and its loss to Isis diminished his absolute power [014-049–014-051].

2. **Isis as Supreme Enchantress**: By obtaining the name, Isis became "the great lady, the mistress of the gods," possessing knowledge of Ra "in his own name." The text explicitly states this as the moment she acquired her legendary magical authority [014-113–014-114].

3. **Spittle in Egyptian Magic**: The creation of the serpent from Ra's own bodily emission (spittle) reflects the Egyptian belief that spittle carried both creative and destructive magical potency — "another instance of the important part which spittle played in magical ceremonies that were intended to produce evil effects" [014-117–014-118].

4. **The Aging of the Divine**: Ra's physical decline — dribbling at the mouth — humanises the creator god and introduces vulnerability into the invulnerable, a necessary premise for the myth to unfold.
