# Plutarch

## Overview

Plutarch of Chaeronea (Greek *Ploutarchos*, c. 46–120 CE) was a Greek biographer, essayist, and priest at Delphi. His treatise **De Iside et Osiride** ("On Isis and Osiris") is one of the most important Classical sources for Egyptian mythology — providing a Greek interpretation of the Osiris myth, details of Egyptian ritual practice, and allegorical explanations of the gods. Chapter 23 of this corpus reproduces Budge's translation of Plutarch's treatise with extensive editorial notes.

## Identity and Role

Plutarch serves as a **bridge between Greek and Egyptian religious thought**. As Budge notes in his Introduction, "the famous treatise of Plutarch entitled *De Iside et Osiride*" fills many of the gaps in the native Egyptian record [006-002]. His work is essential because it preserves details about Egyptian belief and ritual that are not found in surviving Egyptian texts.

Budge characterises Plutarch as "a learned man and a student of comparative religion and mythology" [006-016]. His interpretations reflect a Greek philosophical perspective on Egyptian religion, attempting to reconcile myth with Platonic and Stoic philosophy while preserving genuine cultic details.

## Family and Relationships

Plutarch lived during the Roman imperial period and served as a priest of Apollo at Delphi. His connection to Egypt is scholarly rather than personal: he collected Egyptian traditions through research and inquiry, not through residence. His treatise draws on earlier Greek writers including Herodotus and Hellenistic accounts of Egyptian religion.

## Major Works

### De Iside et Osiride

Plutarch's treatise on Isis and Osiris forms the entirety of Chapter 23 in this corpus. The work presents:

- The **narrative of Osiris's death** and Isis's search for his body [023-057]
- **Allegorical interpretations** of Egyptian deities, identifying Osiris with the Nile, Isis with the fertile earth, and Typhon (Set) with the sea and drought [023-970]
- Details of **Egyptian ritual**, including the priestly tonsure, the festival calendar, and the intercalary days [023-128], [023-414]
- **Comparative mythology**, connecting Egyptian beliefs to Greek and other traditions [023-790], [023-821], [023-1052]

Plutarch's treatise is invaluable for preserving details like the birthdays of the five epagomenal gods (Osiris, Horus, Set, Isis, and Nephthys) [023-398], [023-414], the seventeenth of Hathor as a sacred date [023-451], and the cult practices of various Egyptian temples.

### Historical Reliability

Budge's editorial notes occasionally correct Plutarch's interpretations. He notes that "Plutarch seems to have erred" in several etymologies [023-948] and that certain of his allegorical connections appear strained [023-962]. Nevertheless, the treatise remains "the essential facts" that complement the Egyptian sources [006-004].
