# Osiris

## Overview

Osiris is the god of the dead, resurrection, and divine kingship in ancient Egyptian religion. His myth — murder by his brother [[set]], resurrection by his wife [[isis]], and reign over the Underworld — is the central narrative of Egyptian funerary belief and the theological foundation for the pharaoh's divine right to rule.

## Identity and Role

Osiris is the eldest son of [[keb]] (Earth) and [[nut]] (Sky), brother and husband of [[isis]], father of [[horus]], and brother of [[set]] and [[nephthys]]. He is one of the Great Ennead of [[heliopolis]]:

> the Nile-god, Shu, Keb, Nut, Osiris, Isis, Nephthys, and Horus. [004-106]

Osiris was originally a king on earth, ruling Egypt with justice and wisdom. After his murder by [[set]], he became the ruler of the Underworld (*Duat*), where he presides over the judgment of the dead. Every deceased Egyptian who passes the Weighing of the Heart is identified with Osiris and granted eternal life. In the Pyramid Texts, the dead king is directly called Osiris:

> Osiris had been judged, and that as the result of the judgment he became the lord of the Underworld [005-227]

His iconography depicts him as a mummiform figure, green or black skin symbolising regeneration, wearing the *Atef* crown and holding the crook and flail of kingship. He was crowned at Suten-henen:

> Osiris also was crowned at Suten-henen [001-061]

## Family and Relationships

Osiris is the son of [[nut]] and [[keb]], husband of [[isis]], and father of [[horus]]. His brother [[set]] murders him out of jealousy, and his sister-wife [[isis]] searches for and revives his body. The myth of Osiris defines the relationships among all the major Ennead gods:

> his father Osiris. He was also careful to allude to the judgement [005-234]

After his death, Osiris's right leg was said to be preserved at a sacred site:

> in later days the right leg of Osiris was said to be preserved [001-107]

The throne of Egypt passed from Osiris to his son [[horus]], establishing the principle that each pharaoh is Horus during life and Osiris after death:

> pass it to his son Osiris. Thus those who are ready to travel to the Underworld [001-163]

## Major Myths and Narratives

**The Murder and Resurrection.** [[set]] murders Osiris, dismembers his body, and scatters the pieces across Egypt. [[isis]], aided by [[nephthys]] and [[thoth]], finds and reassembles the body. Through her magic, she revives Osiris long enough to conceive [[horus]]. This myth is the foundation of Egyptian funerary practice: every mummification ritual re-enacts the reassembly of Osiris's body.

> the fight between Osiris and Set, and the fight between Heru-Behutet and Set [002-149]

**The Judgment of the Dead.** As ruler of the Underworld, Osiris presides over the Weighing of the Heart ceremony. The deceased stands before Osiris and the forty-two assessors; their heart is weighed against the feather of [[maat]]. Those who pass the judgment join Osiris in the Field of Reeds:

> The Great God was no other than Osiris, and the judgment was described [002-179]

**The Osirian Mysteries.** An elaborate religious ceremony was performed in temples near Abydos, re-enacting the death and resurrection of Osiris:

> religious ceremony in honour of Osiris in a temple near Abydos [005-164]

**The Belly of Osiris.** In cosmic symbolism, parts of Osiris's body were identified with celestial and geographical features:

> the belly of Osiris, the thighs of Menthu, the legs of the Nile [005-108]

## Cult and Worship

Osiris was worshipped throughout Egypt, but his primary cult centre was at Abydos, where his head was said to be buried. Pilgrimage to Abydos — to erect a memorial stele or leave an offering — was one of the most important acts of personal piety an Egyptian could perform. The annual Osiris Mysteries at Abydos drew pilgrims from across the country.

His cult also flourished at [[heliopolis]], Busiris in the Delta, and Philae. By the Late Period, every Egyptian who could afford it sought burial at Abydos or at least a cenotaph there. The Book of the Dead identifies the deceased with Osiris in nearly every spell:

> The history of Isis and Osiris given in the text is taken from Plutarch's *De Iside et Osiride* [006-001]
