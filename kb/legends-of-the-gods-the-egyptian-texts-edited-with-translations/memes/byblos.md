# Byblos

**Byblos** (Phoenician **Gebal**, modern **Jbeil** in Lebanon) was an ancient Phoenician port city on the Mediterranean coast. In Egyptian mythology, it was the foreign land where the chest containing the body of [[Osiris]] drifted after [[Set]] cast it into the Nile, and where [[Isis]] travelled to recover her husband's remains.

## Overview

Byblos was one of the oldest continuously inhabited cities in the world, a centre of trade between Egypt and the Levant. Its appearance in the Osiris myth reflects the historical reality of Egyptian-Phoenician contact — cedar wood from Byblos was imported into Egypt for shipbuilding and temple construction. In myth, this trading relationship was transformed into a sacred geography where the fate of a god played out on a foreign shore.

## The Chest at Byblos

After Set murdered Osiris and cast the chest into the Nile, the currents carried it northward to the Mediterranean:

> "The chest was carried by the waves of the sea to the coast of Byblos, and there it lodged in a heath-tree." *(023-488, 023-497)*

The tree — a cedar or tamarisk — grew rapidly around the chest, encasing it within its trunk. The king of Byblos, **Melkander**, impressed by the tree's size and fragrance, had it cut down and used as a pillar in his palace. Unbeknownst to him, the pillar concealed the body of the Egyptian god (023-507).

## Isis at Byblos

Isis, searching for Osiris, followed rumours of the fragrant tree to Byblos. Setting herself down by a fountain, she was discovered by the queen's maidens and eventually brought to the palace, where she became the nurse of the royal child:

> "At Byblos, where, setting herself down by the fountain, Isis was found by the maidens of the queen." *(023-497)*

In some versions of the story, Isis revealed her divine nature by placing the child in the fire each night to burn away his mortality — a motif common to Mediterranean mythology. When the queen discovered this, Isis revealed her true identity, demanded the pillar, and extracted the chest containing Osiris's body:

> "When this was done, Isis threw herself upon the chest and lamented with such a loud voice that the younger son of the king died of fright." *(023-524)*

## In the Texts

The Byblos episode appears in Plutarch's account of Isis and Osiris (Chapter XXIII, Part II), which preserves the most detailed surviving version of this narrative. The story bridges Egyptian and Phoenician worlds, with Byblos serving as a liminal space where the divine drama of Egypt unfolded on foreign soil. The retrieval of Osiris's body from Byblos was a crucial step before Isis could conceive [[Horus]] and begin the process of Osiris's resurrection.
