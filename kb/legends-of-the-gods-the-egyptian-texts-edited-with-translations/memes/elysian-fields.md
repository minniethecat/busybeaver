# Elysian Fields

## Overview

The **Elysian Fields** (Egyptian: *Sekhet-hetep*, "Field of Peace," and *Sekhet Aaru*, "Field of Reeds") were the blessed region of the Egyptian afterlife where the righteous dead enjoyed eternal life in perfect abundance.

Created at the dawn of the cosmos by the word of the god [[tem|Tem]] (Atum), this paradise was the goal of every justified spirit who passed the judgment of [[osiris|Osiris]].

The Elysian Fields were located in the [[tuat|Tuat]] (Underworld) and were surrounded by a wall of iron, within which the deceased planted, harvested, and feasted for eternity.

## Core Doctrine

### Creation at the Dawn

The Elysian Fields came into being at the first moment of creation. When Tem spoke the creative words:

> "Straightway Sekhet-hetep, or the Elysian Fields, came into being" [001-119]

> "Sekhet-hetep was the Elysian Fields of the Egyptians" [001-122]

The Fields were thus part of the original created order — not a later addition or afterthought, but a fundamental component of the cosmos. Alongside Sekhet-hetep came Sekhet Aaru: "Sekhet Aaru, or the Field of Reeds, came into being" [001-121].

### Geography and Nature

One tradition places the Elysian Fields in the eastern heavens, the abode of the sun-god [[ra|Ra]]:

> "One tradition places the Elysian Fields in the east, where Ra rises daily" [023-714]

Within their iron wall, the Fields contained canals, fertile land, and abundant grain — taller than any grown on earth. The deceased lived exactly as they had in life, but without toil, hunger, or death.

Servant figurines (*ushabti*) performed the agricultural labor on behalf of the deceased.

### The Offering Fields

A related concept was *Sekhet of the Offerings*, a place where the gods received tribute: "Sekhet of the offerings waded therein" [009-048].

The goddess [[sekhet]] presided over this region: "the majesty of this god gave them to Sekhet, the goddess who dwelleth therein" [009-056].

## Significance

The Elysian Fields represent the Egyptian vision of perfected earthly life projected into eternity. Unlike later Western notions of a purely spiritual heaven, Sekhet-hetep was a physical paradise where bodily pleasures — eating, drinking, companionship — continued.

Entry required both moral justification (the weighing of the heart before Osiris) and ritual knowledge: the [[words-of-power|words of power]] spoken from the [[book-of-the-dead|Book of the Dead]].
