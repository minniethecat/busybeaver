# Rameses II

## Overview

Rameses II (also spelled Ramesses II) is a pharaoh of the **XIXth Dynasty**, historically one of Egypt's longest-reigning and most powerful rulers. In the Egyptian mythological texts, he appears as the royal patron in the **Bentresh legend**, where he petitions the god Khensu to heal a foreign princess. His full royal name is given as *Ra-meses-meri-Amen*, meaning "Ra bore him, beloved of Amen" [016-115].

## Historical Context

Rameses II reigned for sixty-six years, during which Egypt reached the height of its imperial power [003-062]. The Bentresh legend is set in a specific historical moment:

> "In the thirty-fifth year of his reign, whilst Rameses II was celebrating a festival of Amen-Ra in the Temple of Luxor, one of the princes of Bekhten sent a second envoy to Rameses II" [003-060]

This event took place in the context of Egyptian relations with Western Asian principalities, specifically the region of Bekhten (possibly Bactria or a Syrian principality), demonstrating the pharaoh's influence beyond Egypt's borders [003-013], [003-019].

## Majors Myths and Narratives

### The Bentresh Legend

The Bentresh legend, inscribed on a stele discovered at the Temple of Khensu in Thebes, is the primary narrative involving Rameses II in the mythological corpus. The story unfolds as follows:

**The Princess's Affliction**: The prince of Bekhten had a daughter named **Bentresh**, who was possessed by an evil spirit [003-060]. Her father sent an envoy to Rameses II, beseeching him to send a physician to heal her [003-060].

**Consulting Khensu**: The Egyptian physicians were unable to cure the princess. Rameses II then turned to divine intervention:

> "When Rameses II had made his petition to Khensu Nefer-hetep, the god gave his protection and magical power to another form of Khensu" [003-096]

The king entreated **Khensu Nefer-hetep**, the son of Amen, to send his healing power with the statue of Khensu that travelled to Bekhten [003-072].

**The Healing and Aftermath**: The Khensu statue journeyed to Bekhten, where the possessing spirit acknowledged the god's authority and departed. The prince of Bekhten, profoundly impressed, kept the statue for three years and nine months before returning it to Egypt [003-096].

## Royal Identity

Rameses II's full protocol appears in the texts:

| Element | Meaning |
|---------|---------|
| Ra-meses-meri-Amen | "Ra bore him, beloved of Amen" [016-115] |
| Son of Ra | Traditional royal title affirming solar descent [016-127] |
| Beneficent god | Epithet emphasizing the pharaoh's divine role [016-118] |

His titularly connects him explicitly to **Amen**, the great god of Thebes, and reflects the XIXth Dynasty's particular devotion to the Theban triad of Amen, Mut, and Khensu [016-118], [016-147].

## Cult and Legacy

Rameses II's connection to the gods is highlighted by his role as the intermediary between the divine and human realms. In the Bentresh legend, he acts as the petitioner who secures divine intervention from Khensu, and his petitions are heard because of his favoured status with the gods [003-096].

> "The thirty-sixth year of the reign of Rameses II, and the god Khensu was entreated in the Temple of Luxor" [003-062]

The festival of Amen, during which the Bentresh embassy arrived, was one of the most important state celebrations, and Rameses II's participation demonstrated the fusion of royal and divine authority [003-043].
