# Creation Myth

The Egyptian creation myth describes the origin of the cosmos from a state of primeval chaos, centered on the self-creation of the creator god and the subsequent emanation of all existence.

## The Primeval Creator

The god **Neb-er-tcher** ("Lord to the uttermost limit") is the supreme creator deity who came into being in the form of the god **Khepera** (the "Roller" or "Becoming One"). As described in the text:

> "I am he who came into being in the form of the god Khepera, and I am the creator of that which came into being, that is to say, I am the creator of everything which came into being." (007-005)

Khepera represents the principle of becoming and transformation — the rolling motion through which formless matter takes shape.

## The Act of Creation

Neb-er-tcher describes creating himself from **primeval matter** (Ausares/Osiris), the fundamental substance that existed before all things (008-001). From this self-creation, all other things emerged through his mouth — a concept where divine speech acts as the instrument of creation (007-007).

The text emphasizes that the creator existed alone in the beginning:

> "I came into being in primeval time. I came into being in the form of Khepera, and I am the creator of what did come into being, that is to say, I formed myself out of the primeval matter." (008-004)

## Cosmological Significance

The creation narrative establishes several core Egyptian theological concepts:

- **Self-creation**: The god creates himself before creating the world (008-005)
- **Primeval matter**: The substance from which all existence is formed (008-006)
- **Divine speech**: Creation through the spoken word of the god (007-007)
- **Khepera as demiurge**: The "Roller" who transforms chaos into order (007-001)
