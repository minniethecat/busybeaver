# Ashtoreth

## Overview

Ashtoreth (Egyptian *Ashthertet* / *Asthertet*) is a Semitic war goddess adopted into the Egyptian pantheon. She is chiefly associated with chariot warfare and appears in the legend of [[heru-behutet]] (Horus of Behutet) as the divine charioteer who tramples the enemies of [[ra]] and accompanies the sun-god to view the aftermath of battle.

## Identity and Role

Ashtoreth is a goddess of war and the chariot, imported into Egypt from Syro-Palestinian religious traditions. In the Egyptian texts, she is synonymous with the goddess known in the Levant as Astarte/Ashtart. Her primary mythological role in the Edfu corpus is as Ra's companion in battle — she rides with him in his chariot to survey the defeated foes of Horus.

Budge identifies her with the "goddess Ashthertet" and notes the parenthetical gloss "Ashtoreth" in the hieroglyphic texts:

> set out with the goddess Ashthertet (`Ashtoreth) to do this, and they saw the enemies lying fettered on the ground [002-057]

She is depicted iconographically in the Edfu temple reliefs driving her war chariot over the prostrate bodies of the enemy:

> Ashthertet ('Ashtoreth') driving her chariot over the prostrate foe [002-123]

## Family and Relationships

Ashtoreth's relationships within the Egyptian pantheon are not elaborated in the surviving corpus. She appears primarily as a companion and ally of [[ra]], the sun-god, in his battle against the Typhonic foes. Her Semitic origins place her in the broader context of foreign deities adopted into Egyptian religion during the New Kingdom, alongside figures such as [[resheph]].

In the Levantine pantheon from which she derives, Ashtoreth (Astarte) is the consort of Baal and a prominent goddess of war and fertility. These associations are largely absent from the Egyptian texts preserved in this corpus.

## Major Myths and Narratives

**The Battle Against the Enemies of Ra.** The primary narrative featuring Ashtoreth is the legend of [[heru-behutet]] and the Winged Disk at [[edfu]]. After Horus of Behutet, in the form of a great winged disk, slaughters the rebels who had opposed Ra, the sun-god sets out to inspect the battlefield:

> thereupon the Majesty of Ra set out on the way, and the goddess Asthertet ('Ashtoreth?) was with him, and he saw the enemies overthrown on the ground, each one of them being fettered [015-038]

This episode establishes Ashtoreth as a witness to and participant in divine victory, present at the moment of cosmic triumph over chaos.

**The Chariot of Victory.** The Edfu temple plates depict Ashtoreth in her signature martial pose — driving her war chariot over the defeated enemies. This iconographic motif parallels her West Semitic depictions as a chariot-riding warrior goddess and reinforces her imported character within the Egyptian religious landscape.

## Cult and Worship

As a foreign deity, Ashtoreth's formal cult in Egypt was limited but attested during the New Kingdom and later periods. Her presence in the Edfu temple reliefs (Ptolemaic period) demonstrates the longevity of her integration into Egyptian religious iconography, appearing alongside native deities such as [[horus]], [[ra]], and [[heru-behutet]] centuries after her initial introduction.
