# Uraeus

## Overview

The **Uraeus** (Egyptian: *Iaret*, "the rearing cobra") was the sacred cobra emblem worn on the forehead of Egyptian deities and pharaohs. Representing the goddess [[uatchit]] (Wadjet) of Lower Egypt, the uraeus symbolized sovereignty, divine protection, and the fiery destructive power of the [[ra|Eye of Ra]]. It was an essential component of the royal regalia, affixed to the crown, and believed to spit fire at the enemies of the king.

## Core Doctrine

### Symbol of Sovereignty

The uraeus cobra, often paired with the vulture of [[nekhebet]] (Nekhbet) of Upper Egypt, formed the Two Ladies — the dual emblem of unified Egyptian kingship. Together they declared the pharaoh's dominion over both Lower and Upper Egypt. The rearing cobra, with its hood spread in a threat display, communicated vigilance and invincible authority.

### The Eye of Ra

The uraeus was intimately connected with the [[hathor|Eye of Ra]], the destructive solar power that could manifest as a goddess ([[sekhet|Sekhmet]], [[tefnut|Tefnut]], or [[hathor|Hathor]]) to punish rebels. When Ra "bestowed upon my Eye the uraeus of fire," the cobra became the living embodiment of divine wrath — a weapon that guarded cosmic order.

### Protective Function

Beyond its political symbolism, the uraeus was a magical protective device. Its image appeared on temple pylons, funerary equipment, and royal furniture, warding off evil spirits and hostile forces. The goddess [[nekhebet|Nekhebit]] and [[uatchit|Uatchit]] assumed serpent form to accompany Ra in his boat and terrify the Sebau fiends.

The texts describe the uraeus as both a physical ornament and a theophanic manifestation — it *is* the goddess in serpent form, not merely her symbol.

## Significance

The uraeus demonstrates the Egyptian fusion of natural observation, political symbolism, and theological concept. The cobra's biology — its venom, threatening posture, and ability to strike — was elevated into a cosmic principle of protective power. Every pharaoh who wore the uraeus on his brow declared himself the legitimate heir of Ra, protected by the same fire that consumed the enemies of the sun-god.
