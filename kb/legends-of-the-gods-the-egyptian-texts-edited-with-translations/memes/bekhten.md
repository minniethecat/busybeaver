# Bekhten

**Bekhten** was a foreign kingdom whose prince sent an embassy to Egypt bearing rich gifts and seeking the aid of an Egyptian god to cure his possessed daughter. The story of this diplomatic mission and its miraculous outcome forms the subject of Chapter III, the **Legend of the Possessed Princess**.

## Overview

Bekhten was a land beyond Egypt's borders, ruled by a prince whose daughter **Bent-enth-resh** became possessed by a malignant spirit. After an Egyptian envoy failed to cure her, the prince requested a divine intervention, resulting in the journey of the god **Khensu Pa-ari-sekher** from Thebes to Bekhten. The seventeen-month journey and the subsequent exorcism established a lasting diplomatic and cultic relationship between the two lands.

## The Embassy of the Prince of Bekhten

The legend begins during the reign of Rameses II, when the Prince of Bekhten sent gifts to the Egyptian court:

> "...sent gifts was the Prince of Bekhten, and at the head of all his gifts was his eldest daughter Bent-enth-resh." *(003-036)*

Accompanied by ambassadors bearing many gifts, the prince sought Egyptian help to heal his daughter, who was possessed by an *akehu* spirit (003-045). Rameses II initially dispatched **Tehuti-em-heb**, a scribe "of the heart and deft fingers," to Bekhten. Upon arrival, however, the scribe found that the spirit was too powerful, and the princess's condition remained unchanged (003-056, 003-059).

## The Divine Intervention

When human skill proved insufficient, the Prince of Bekhten sent a second messenger to Egypt, requesting nothing less than a god. Rameses II consulted the oracle of [[Khensu]] at Thebes:

> "The king caused the great god Khensu Nefer-hetep to be brought, and he besought him to allow Khensu Pa-ari-sekher to go to Bekhten to drive the devil out of the Princess of Bekhten." *(003-074–075)*

Khensu Nefer-hetep consented, transferring his divine power to his aspect Khensu Pa-ari-sekher, the "Expeller of Spirits." The god, carried in his sacred boat, set out for Bekhten accompanied by five boats of attendants, a journey that took seventeen months (003-128).

## The Miracle at Bekhten

When Khensu Pa-ari-sekher arrived in Bekhten, he was "cordially welcomed by the Prince of Bekhten" (003-132). The god performed the exorcism, and the spirit was expelled from Bent-enth-resh:

> "The spirit which dwelt in her departed, and she was healed straightway." *(003-144, 003-150)*

The Prince of Bekhten was so pleased that he held a great feast in the god's honour. Khensu Pa-ari-sekher remained in Bekhten for **three years**, during which the prince contemplated keeping the god in his land permanently (003-146, 003-155).

## In the Texts

The legend appears in both Chapter III and an extended, fuller narrative in Chapter XVI. The Prince of Bekhten's second embassy is recorded with striking detail:

> "And the Prince of Bekhten sent a messenger a second time to Egypt, saying: 'O Pharaoh, send a god to heal the daughter of the Prince of Bekhten.'" *(016-174, 016-184)*

In the fuller account, the prince even attempted to retain the god permanently, "keeping back nothing of the gifts which the Prince of Bekhten had given them" (003-155). A dream vision ultimately compelled the prince to release the divine image to return to Egypt.
