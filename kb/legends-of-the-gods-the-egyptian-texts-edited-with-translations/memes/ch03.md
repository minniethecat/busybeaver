### A LEGEND OF KHENSU NEFER-HETEP[FN#31] AND THE PRINCESS OF BEKHTEN.

[FN#31] In the headlines of this section, p. 106 ff., for Ptah  
[003-001] Nefer-hetep read Khensu Nefer-hetep.

[003-002] The text of this legend is cut in hieroglyphics upon a sandstone stele,
[003-003] with a rounded top, which was found in the temple of Khensu at Thebes,
[003-004] and is now preserved in the Bibliotheque Nationale at Paris; it was
[003-005] discovered by Champollion, and removed to Paris by Prisse d'Avennes in
[003-006] 1846. The text was first published by Prisse d'Avennes,[FN#32] and it
[003-007] was first translated by Birch[FN#33] in 1853. The text was republished
[003-008] and translated into French by E. de Rouge in 1858,[FN#34] and several
[003-009] other renderings have been given in German and in English since that
[003-010] date.[FN#35] When the text was first published, and for some years
[003-011] afterwards, it was generally thought that the legend referred to events
[003-012] which were said to have taken place under a king who was identified as
[003-013] Rameses XIII., but this misconception was corrected by Erman, who
[003-014] showed[FN#36] that the king was in reality Rameses II. By a careful
[003-015] examination of the construction of the text he proved that the
[003-016] narrative on the stele was drawn up several hundreds of years after the
[003-017] events described in it took place, and that its author was but
[003-018] imperfectly acquainted with the form of the Egyptian language in use in
[003-019] the reign of Rameses II. In fact, the legend was written in the
[003-020] interests of the priests of the temple of Khensu, who wished to magnify
[003-021] their god and his power to cast out devils and to exorcise evil
[003-022] spirits; it was probably composed between B.C. 650 and B.C. 250.[FN#37]

[003-028] The legend, after enumerating the great names of Rameses II., goes on
[003-029] to state that the king was in the "country of the two rivers," by which
[003-030] we are to understand some portion of Mesopotamia, the rivers being the
[003-031] Tigris and Euphrates, and that the local chiefs were bringing to him
[003-032] tribute consisting of gold, lapis-lazuli, turquoise, and logs of wood
[003-033] from the Land of the God. It is difficult to understand how gold and
[003-034] logs of wood from Southern Arabia and East Africa came to be produced
[003-035] as tribute by chiefs who lived so far to the north. Among those who
[003-036] sent gifts was the Prince of Bekhten, and at the head of all his
[003-037] tribute he sent his eldest daughter, bearing his message of homage and
[003-038] duty. Now the maiden was beautiful, and the King of Egypt thought her
[003-039] so lovely that be took her to wife, and bestowed upon her the name "Ra-
[003-040] neferu," which means something like the "beauties of Ra." He took her
[003-041] back with him to Egypt, where she was installed as Queen.

[003-042] During the summer of the fifteenth year of his reign, whilst Rameses
[003-043] II. was celebrating a festival of Amen-Ra in the Temple of Luxor, one
[003-044] came to him and reported that an envoy had arrived from the Prince of
[003-045] Bekhten, bearing with him many gifts for the Royal Wife Ra-neferu.
[003-046] When the envoy had been brought into the presence, he addressed words
[003-047] of homage to the king, and, having presented the gifts from his lord,
[003-048] he said that he had come to beg His Majesty to send a "learned man,"
[003-049] i.e., a magician, to Bekhten to attend Bent-enth-resh, His Majesty's
[003-050] sister-in-law, who was stricken with some disease. Thereupon the king
[003-051] summoned the learned men of the House of Life, i.e., the members of the
[003-052] great College of Magic at Thebes, and the qenbetu officials, and when
[003-053] they had entered his presence, he commanded them to select a man of
[003-054] "wise heart and deft fingers" to go to Bekhten. The choice fell upon
[003-055] one Tehuti-em-heb, and His Majesty sent him to Bekhten with the envoy.
[003-056] When they arrived in Bekhten, Tehuti-em-heb found that the Princess
[003-057] Bent-enth-resh was possessed by an evil spirit which refused to be
[003-058] exorcised by him, and he was unable to cast out the devil. The Prince
[003-059] of Bekhten, seeing that the healing of his daughter was beyond the
[003-060] power of the Egyptian, sent a second envoy to Rameses II., and besought
[003-061] him to send a god to drive out the devil. This envoy arrived in Egypt
[003-062] in the summer of the twenty-sixth year of the reign of Rameses II., and
[003-063] found the king celebrating a festival in Thebes. When he heard the
[003-064] petition of the envoy, he went to the Temple of Khensu Nefer-hetep "a
[003-065] second time,"[FN#38] and presented himself before the god and besought
[003-066] his help on behalf of his sister-in-law.

[FN#38] Thus the king must have invoked the help of Khensu on the
[003-067] occasion of the visit of the first envoy.

[003-068] Then the priests of Khensu Nefer-hetep carried the statue of this god
[003-069] to the place where was the statue of Khensu surnamed "Pa-ari-sekher,"
[003-070] i.e., the "Worker of destinies," who was able to repel the attacks of
[003-071] evil spirits and to drive them out. When the statues of the two gods
[003-072] were facing each other, Rameses II. entreated Khensu Nefer-hetep to
[003-073] "turn his face towards," i.e., to look favourably upon Khensu. Pa-ari-
[003-074] sekher, and to let him go to Bekhten to drive the devil out of the
[003-075] Princess of Bekhten. The text affords no explanation of the fact that
[003-076] Khensu Nefer-hetep was regarded as a greater god than Khensu Pa-ari-
[003-077] sekher, or why his permission had to be obtained before the latter
[003-078] could leave the country. It is probable that the demands made upon
[003-079] Khensu Nefer-hetep by the Egyptians who lived in Thebes and its
[003-080] neighbourhood were so numerous that it was impossible to let his statue
[003-081] go into outlying districts or foreign lands, and that a deputy-god was
[003-082] appointed to perform miracles outside Thebes. This arrangement would
[003-083] benefit the people, and would, moreover, bring much money to the
[003-084] priests. The appointment of a deputy-god is not so strange as it may
[003-085] seem, and modern African peoples are familiar with the expedient.
[003-086] About one hundred years ago the priests of the god Bobowissi of
[003-087] Winnebah, in the Tshi region of West Africa, found their business so
[003-088] large that it was absolutely necessary for them to appoint a deputy.
[003-089] The priests therefore selected Brahfo, i.e., "deputy," and gave out
[003-090] that Bobowissi had deputed all minor matters to him, and that his
[003-091] utterances were to be regarded as those of Bobowissi. Delegates were
[003-092] ordered to be sent to Winnebah in Ashanti, where they would be shown
[003-093] the "deputy" god by the priests, and afterwards he would be taken to
[003-094] Mankassim, where he would reside, and do for the people all that
[003-095] Bobowissi had done hitherto.[FN#39]

[FN#39] Ellis, Tshi-speaking Peoples, p. 55.

[003-096] When Rameses II. had made his petition to Khensu Nefer-hetep, the
[003-097] statue of the god bowed its head twice, in token of assent. Here it is
[003-098] clear that we have an example of the use of statues with movable limbs,
[003-099] which were worked, when occasion required, by the priests. The king
[003-100] then made a second petition to the god to transfer his sa, or magical
[003-101] power, to Khensu Pa-ari-sekher so that when he had arrived in Bekhten
[003-102] he would be able to heal the Princess. Again the statue of Khensu
[003-103] Nefer-hetep bowed its head twice, and the petition of the king was
[003-104] granted. The text goes on to say that the magical power of the greater
[003-105] god was transferred to the lesser god four times, or in a fourfold
[003-106] measure, but we are not told how this was effected. We know from many
[003-107] passages in the texts that every god was believed to possess this
[003-108] magical power, which is called the "sa of life," or the "sa of the
[003-109] god,".[FN#40] This sa could be transferred by a god or goddess to a
[003-110] human being, either by an embrace or through some offering which was
[003-111] eaten. Thus Temu transferred the magical power of his life to Shu and
[003-112] Tefnut by embracing them,[FN#41] and in the Ritual of the Divine
[003-113] Cult[FN#42] the priest says, The two vessels of milk of Temu are the "sa
[003-114] of my limbs." The man who possessed this sa could transfer it to his
[003-115] friend by embracing him and then "making passes" with his hands along
[003-116] his back. The sa could be received by a man from a god and then
[003-117] transmitted by him to a statue by taking it in his arms, and this
[003-118] ceremony was actually performed by the king in the Ritual of the Divine
[003-119] Cult.[FN#43] The primary source of this sa was Ra, who bestowed it
[003-120] without measure on the blessed dead,[FN#44] and caused them to live for
[003-121] ever thereby. These, facts make it tolerably certain that the magical
[003-122] power of Khensu Nefer-hetep was transferred to Khensu Pa-ari-sekher in
[003-123] one of two ways: either the statue of the latter was brought near to
[003-124] that of the former and it received the sa by contact, or the high
[003-125] priest first received the sa from the greater god and then transmitted
[003-126] it to the lesser god by embraces and "passes" with his hands. Be this
[003-127] as it may, Khensu Pa-ari-sekher received the magical power, and having
[003-128] been placed in his boat, he set out for Bekhten, accompanied by five
[003-129] smaller boats, and chariots and horses which marched on each side of
[003-130] him.

[003-131] When after a journey of seventeen months Khensu Pa-ari-sekher arrived
[003-132] in Bekhten, he was cordially welcomed by the Prince, and, having gone
[003-133] to the place where the Princess who was possessed of a devil lived, he
[003-134] exercised his power to such purpose that she was healed immediately.
[003-135] Moreover, the devil which had been cast out admitted that Khensu Pa-
[003-136] ari-sekher was his master, and promised that he would depart to the
[003-137] place whence he came, provided that the Prince of Bekhten would
[003-138] celebrate a festival in his honour before his departure. Meanwhile
[003-139] the Prince and his soldiers stood by listening to the conversation
[003-140] between the god and the devil, and they were very much afraid.
[003-141] Following the instructions of Khensu Pa-ari-sekher the Prince made
[003-142] a great feast in honour of the supernatural visitors, and then the
[003-143] devil departed to the "place which he loved," and there was general
[003-144] rejoicing in the land. The Prince of Bekhten was so pleased with the
[003-145] Egyptian god that he determined not to allow him to return to Egypt.
[003-146] When the statue of Khensu Pa-ari-sekher had been in Bekhten for three
[003-147] years and nine months, the Prince in a vision saw the god, in the form
[003-148] of a golden hawk, come forth from his shrine, and fly up into the air
[003-149] and direct his course to Egypt. Realizing that the statue of the god
[003-150] was useless without its indwelling spirit, the Prince of Bekhten
[003-151] permitted the priests of Khensu Pa-ari-sekher to depart with it to
[003-152] Egypt, and dismissed them with gifts of all kinds. In due course they
[003-153] arrived in Egypt and the priests took their statue to the temple of
[003-154] Khensu Nefer-hetep, and handed over to that god all the gifts which the
[003-155] Prince of Bekhten had given them, keeping back nothing for their own
[003-156] god. After this Khensu Pa-ari-sekher returned to his temple in peace,
[003-157] in the thirty-third year of the reign of Rameses II., having been
[003-158] absent from it about eight years.
