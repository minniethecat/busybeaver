# Set

## Overview

Set (or Seth) is the god of chaos, storms, the desert, and foreign lands. He is the murderer of [[osiris]] and the eternal adversary of [[horus]] in the conflict for the throne of Egypt. Yet Set is not purely evil: in earlier Egyptian religion, he was a protector of [[ra]] against the serpent [[apep]] and a defender of the solar barque.

## Identity and Role

Set is the son of [[nut]] and [[keb]], brother of [[osiris]], [[isis]], and [[nephthys]]. He is the "Arch-fiend and great Enemy of Ra" in later texts:

> of Set, the Arch-fiend and great Enemy of Ra [002-112]

His iconography shows a mysterious animal with a curved snout, square-tipped ears, and a forked tail — the "Set animal," which has never been conclusively identified with any living species. His symbolic colour is red, associated with the desert and chaos.

Set's role in Egyptian religion evolved dramatically. In the earliest periods, he was a powerful and ambivalent god; by the Late Period, he was increasingly demonised as the embodiment of evil — the Egyptian equivalent of the Greek [[typhon]]:

> the fight between the Sun-god and Set was a very favourite subject in Egyptian art [002-146]

## Family and Relationships

Set is the son of [[nut]] and [[keb]], the brother of [[osiris]], [[isis]], and [[nephthys]]. He is the husband of [[nephthys]], though the union is barren or troubled in most versions. After the murder of [[osiris]], Set becomes the sworn enemy of [[horus]], leading to an epic struggle:

> fight between Heru-ur and Set, the fight between Ra and Set, the fight between Heru-Behutet and Set, the fight between Osiris and Set [002-148]

> fight between Horus, son of Isis, and Set. In the oldest times the followers of Horus and Set fought periodically [002-150]

The cosmology portrays Set as the adversary not merely of one god but of divine order (*maat*) itself. Yet paradoxically, he is one of the "Two Men" (with [[horus]]) who represent the dual forces of Egyptian kingship:

> "Two Men," Horus and Set. A great fight took place, the battle lasted many days [002-115]

## Major Myths and Narratives

**The Murder of Osiris.** Set murders [[osiris]] out of jealousy, dismembers his body, and scatters the pieces across Egypt. This act is the foundational crime that sets the Osiris myth cycle in motion. [[isis]] and [[nephthys]] search for the body, and [[horus]] eventually avenges his father:

> fight between Osiris and Set, and the fight between Heru-Behutet and Set [002-149]

**The Contendings of Horus and Set.** The legal and physical battles between [[horus]] and Set for the throne of Egypt form one of the most detailed mythological cycles. Set curses Horus, and Thoth rebukes him:

> Then Set rose up and cursed Horus because he had triumphed over him [002-129]

> Horus and Set engaged in a fight which lasted three days [002-132]

Horus ultimately triumphs, driving his spear into Set's neck:

> drive his spear into the neck of Set with such violence that Set fell to the ground [002-133]

> In this state Horus dragged Set into the presence of Ra and the gods [002-136]

**Set as Protector of Ra.** Despite his demonisation, Set retains his ancient role as defender of the solar barque. He stands at the prow of [[ra]]'s boat and spears the serpent [[apep]], who threatens cosmic order each night:

> the presence of Ra and Isis, and be dragged Set by his feet through the land [002-143]

> promptly cut off the heads of Set and his fiends in the presence of Ra [002-142]

**The Disposal of Set.** After his defeat, the gods debate what to do with Set:

> the question of the disposal of Set was being discussed by the gods [002-139]

## Cult and Worship

Set was worshipped at Ombos (Naqada) in Upper Egypt and at Avaris in the Delta — the capital of the Hyksos kings, who identified Set with their own storm god, Baal. During the Ramesside Period, Set enjoyed royal favour: pharaohs [[seti-i]] and [[seti-ii]] (Seti I and II) were named after him.

However, after the Hyksos period, Set's cult declined sharply. By the Late Period, his images were defaced, his name erased from monuments (*damnatio memoriae*), and he became the Egyptian equivalent of the Greek monster Typhon. Temples to Set were repurposed or destroyed. The hippopotamus — one of Set's animal forms — became a symbol ritually harpooned in temple ceremonies.
