# Nephthys

## Overview

Nephthys (Egyptian *Nebt-het*, "Lady of the House") is a goddess of the Heliopolitan Ennead, daughter of Geb and Nut. She is the sister-wife of Set and the sister of Isis and Osiris. In the Osirian cycle she appears as Isis's loyal companion, weeping over the body of Osiris and participating in the funerary rites. Plutarch identifies her with the Greek Teleute and interprets her as representing "the furthest parts of the earth" [023-970].

## Identity and Role

Nephthys functions primarily as a **funerary goddess**, paired with Isis in mourning and protecting the dead. In funerary texts she is described as spinning and weaving while Isis works beside her: "Verily Isis weaveth and Nephthys spinneth" [019-054]. She represents the liminal space between life and death, and together with Isis she forms the archetypal pair of divine mourners whose tears and cries provide ritual protection for the deceased [022-219].

Plutarch identifies Nephthys with the Greek deity Teleute ("End") and interprets her as representing the barren lands at the edge of Egypt — complementing Isis who represents the fertile Nile valley [023-970]. Her connection to Set as consort also associates her with the desert and its dryness.

## Family and Relationships

In the Heliopolitan genealogy, Nephthys is the daughter of **Geb** (Earth) and **Nut** (Sky) [004-106]. Her siblings are Osiris, Isis, and Set; she is the sister-wife of Set and the mother of Anubis through Osiris (according to Plutarch) [023-974].

Her relationship with her sister Isis is central: the two goddesses are frequently paired in scenes of mourning and protection. When Osiris is slain, they weep together [005-188]: "Then came Nephthys shedding tears and uttering cries" [022-149]. Thoth addresses Isis and Nephthys together, commanding them in the work of preserving the dead [005-208].

## Major Myths and Narratives

### The Mourning of Osiris

Nephthys's primary narrative role is in the Osirian cycle, where she aids Isis in mourning, searching for, and reassembling the body of Osiris. When the body is found, "Isis and Nephthys" weep over it together [022-219]. The funerary rites they establish become the paradigm for Egyptian burial practices.

Plutarch adds that Nephthys, disguised as Isis, deceived Osiris and conceived a child (Anubis) with him, whom Isis then raised [023-474], [023-476]. This narrative explains Anubis's role as the guardian of the dead born of a union between the life-god and the death-goddess.

### The Birthday of Nephthys

Plutarch records that the fifth epagomenal day (the intercalary days added to the Egyptian year) was celebrated as the birthday of Nephthys [023-414]. On the seventeenth day of the month of Hathor, Isis and Nephthys together performed rites connected to the Osirian mysteries [023-1003].

## Cult and Worship

Nephthys was honoured alongside Isis as a co-protector of the dead. Priestesses of Isis and Nephthys are described as having "the hair of their heads cut off" as part of their ritual appearance [023-128]. Funerary texts frequently invoke the pair together, calling upon Nephthys not to be anxious and to join in the protection of the body [022-182].
