# Khensu and the Princess of Bekhten

## Overview

The Legend of Khensu and the Princess of Bekhten recounts how the statue of the Egyptian god Khensu Pa-ari-sekher journeyed to the distant land of Bekhten to drive out an evil spirit that possessed Bent-enth-resh, the sister-in-law of Rameses II. The event took place during the 26th to 33rd years of Rameses II's reign and was inscribed on a sandstone stele in the temple of Khensu at Thebes. The narrative illustrates the power of divine statues in Egyptian theology, the ritual transfer of the *sa* (magical life-force) between deities, and the political prestige of Egyptian religion abroad.

## Narrative

### The First Appeal

During the summer of the fifteenth year of his reign, Rameses II was celebrating a festival of [[Amen-Ra]] at the Temple of Luxor when an envoy arrived from the Prince of Bekhten bearing gifts for the Royal Wife Ra-neferu. The envoy petitioned the king to send a "learned man" to Bekhten to attend Her Majesty's sister-in-law, Bent-enth-resh, who was stricken with disease [003-042–003-050].

The king summoned the learned men of the House of Life at Thebes, and they selected Tehuti-em-heb, a man of "wise heart and deft fingers," who accompanied the envoy to Bekhten. However, upon arriving, Tehuti-em-heb discovered that Bent-enth-resh was possessed by an evil spirit that "refused to be exorcised by him" — the first magician was powerless [003-051–003-060].

### The Second Appeal

Eleven years later, in the 26th year of Rameses II's reign, a second envoy arrived from the Prince of Bekhten, this time requesting that a god be sent to drive out the devil. The king went to the Temple of [[Khensu Nefer-hetep]] and presented himself before the god [003-061–003-066].

The priests carried the statue of Khensu Nefer-hetep to face the statue of Khensu Pa-ari-sekher, the god who was "able to repel the attacks of evil spirits and to drive them out." Rameses II entreated Khensu Nefer-hetep to permit the lesser god to travel to Bekhten. The statue of Khensu Nefer-hetep bowed its head twice in assent, a remarkable use of a statue with movable limbs operated by the priests [003-068–003-098].

The king then petitioned the god to transfer his *sa* — the magical life-power — to Khensu Pa-ari-sekher. The greater god transmitted his power fourfold, and Khensu Pa-ari-sekher, placed in his boat with five smaller boats, chariots, and horses, set out for Bekhten [003-099–003-130].

### The Exorcism and Aftermath

After a journey of seventeen months, Khensu Pa-ari-sekher arrived in Bekhten and was cordially welcomed. The god exercised his power to such purpose that the Princess was "healed immediately." The expelled devil acknowledged Khensu Pa-ari-sekher as his master and agreed to depart to the place whence he came, provided the prince celebrated a festival in his honour [003-131–003-140].

The Prince of Bekhten, so pleased with the Egyptian god, determined not to allow the statue to return to Egypt. For three years and nine months the statue remained in Bekhten. Then the prince saw the god in a vision, in the form of a golden hawk, "come forth from his shrine, and fly up into the air and direct his course to Egypt." Realizing that the statue was useless without its indwelling spirit, the prince permitted the priests to depart with it [003-144–003-151].

The priests returned to Egypt and handed over all the gifts from the Prince of Bekhten to Khensu Nefer-hetep, keeping nothing back. Khensu Pa-ari-sekher returned to his temple in peace in the 33rd year of Rameses II's reign, having been absent eight years [003-152–003-158].

## Outcome and Significance

The legend demonstrates several key aspects of Egyptian theology and ritual practice:

1. **Divine Statue Agency**: The statue was not merely a representation — it was the god's dwelling-place. When the spirit departed in the form of a golden hawk, the statue became powerless.

2. **The *Sa* Transfer**: The ritual transmission of magical power from Khensu Nefer-hetep to Khensu Pa-ari-sekher illustrates the Egyptian concept of the *sa of life*, a transmissible divine fluid that could be conveyed through proximity or priestly ritual [003-107–003-126].

3. **Deputy-God Arrangement**: Khensu Pa-ari-sekher functioned as a "deputy" for the greater god, handling cases beyond Thebes — a practical arrangement that "would benefit the people, and would, moreover, bring much money to the priests" [003-076–003-095].

4. **Egyptian Hegemony**: The story served the interests of the priests of Khensu, magnifying their god's power internationally and reinforcing Egyptian religious prestige over foreign lands.
