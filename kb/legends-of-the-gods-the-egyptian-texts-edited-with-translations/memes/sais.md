# Sais

**Sais** (Egyptian **Sau**, modern **Sa el-Hagar**) was a major city of Lower Egypt situated in the Western Nile Delta. It was the cult center of the goddess **Neith**, a creator and war goddess identified by the Greeks with Athene, and served as one of the principal venues for the annual commemoration festivals of Osiris.

## Overview

Sais was the capital of the fifth nome of Lower Egypt and achieved particular prominence during the Twenty-sixth (Saite) Dynasty, when it served as the royal capital. The city's great temple housed a famous statue of Neith, whom Plutarch describes as the Egyptian Athene. Sais was also associated with the Isis-Osiris cycle, serving as one of the gathering places for the annual lamentation of the god's death.

## The Goddess of Sais

The city's chief deity was Neith, described as a creator goddess associated with war, hunting, and weaving. Plutarch identifies her temple statue with the Greek Athene:

> "The statue of Athene at Sais, whom they identify with Isis..." *(023-278)*

The identification of Neith with Athene, and further with Isis, reflects the syncretic interpretation of Egyptian religion during the Hellenistic period. Neith was a goddess of the Western Delta, and her temple at Sais was celebrated across the ancient world:

> "Neith, the goddess of Sais, in the Western Delta. She was a creator goddess..." *(023-291)*

## The Osiris Festival at Sais

Sais played an integral role in the annual Osiris festivals. The city was one of the designated gathering places where the faithful assembled to mourn and commemorate the murdered god:

> "The people gathered for their brother Un-nefer at Sais; on the 19th day..." *(023-1004)*

This annual observance formed part of a cycle of festivals that moved through multiple Delta cities, each hosting a different stage of the commemoration of Osiris's death, search, and eventual resurrection. The festival at Sais fell on the 19th day, following observances at other cult centers.

## In the Texts

Sais appears in the comprehensive nome listing of Lower Egypt, placed alongside Letopolis and Hermopolis of Lower Egypt (023-597). The city's significance in the texts centers on its dual identity as both a political capital (during the Saite period) and a religious center intimately connected with the Neith-Isis-Athene complex and the Osirian festival calendar.
