# Bennu

## Overview

The Bennu (also the Divine Bennu, the Great Bennu) is the sacred bird-deity of ancient Egypt: the *ba* (soul) of [[ra|Ra]], the incarnation of [[osiris|Osiris]], and the custodian of the "book of destiny." Associated with the city of Suten-henen and the balsam-trees of Heliopolis, the Bennu was identified by classical authors with the Phoenix — a bird that cyclically renews itself. In Egyptian funerary texts, to be called "the Great Bennu" is to be identified with the imperishable divine life that transcends death.

## Identity and Role

The Bennu occupies a unique position as both an independent divine entity and a manifestation-form (*kheperu*) of greater gods. He is described as "the soul of Ra and the incarnation of Osiris" [022-219] — uniting the solar and Osirian poles of Egyptian afterlife theology. In magical protection spells, "Horus is protected as the Divine Bennu who alighteth in front of his two Eyes" [022-207], making the Bennu an active protective power.

The texts also name the Bennu as "the keeper of the book of destiny" [021-135]. This role as custodian of fate connects the bird to the divine council and the ordering of cosmic events. The Bennu is not merely a symbol; he is an actor in the divine drama — invoked alongside other theomorphic forms (the Holy Beetle, the Hidden Body) in Thoth's great healing spell [005-213].

> "Horus is protected as the Divine Bennu who alighteth in front of his two Eyes" [022-207]

## Family and Relationships

The Bennu does not belong to a conventional divine genealogy. Instead, he stands in a relationship of *identity* to the supreme gods. As the footnote to Chapter 22 explains: "The Bennu was the soul of Ra and the incarnation of Osiris" [022-219]. This makes the Bennu a unique intermediary — simultaneously expressing the essence of the sun-god [[ra|Ra]] and the resurrected god [[osiris|Osiris]].

The Bennu's connection to [[horus|Horus]] is one of active guardianship: the spell invokes the Bennu's protective power specifically for Horus [022-207]. In turn, Horus's vindication is tied to the Bennu's own mythic presence.

## Major Myths and Narratives

**The Bennu at Suten-henen**: The city of Suten-henen (Heracleopolis) was the Bennu's mythic dwelling-place. It was here that "Ra rose as king for the first time" and Osiris was crowned; the city was also home to "the great Bennu bird, or Phoenix, and the 'Crusher of Bones'" [001-062]. This places the Bennu at the very origin-point of Egyptian kingship.

> "the great Bennu bird, or Phoenix, and the 'Crusher of Bones' mentioned in the Negative Confession" [001-062]

**Thoth's Healing Spell**: When Horus lay poisoned, [[thoth|Thoth]] invoked a litany of protective forms — "the Dweller in his Disk (Aten), the Great Dwarf, the Mighty Ram, the Great Hawk, the Holy Beetle, the Hidden Body, the Divine Bennu" — and "proceeded to utter the great spell which restored Horus to life" [005-213]. The inclusion of the Divine Bennu in this exalted company confirms his stature as a protective divine form.

**Born on the Balsam-Tree**: In the funerary identification texts, the deceased proclaims: "Thou wast the Great Bennu, who art born (or, produced) on the top of the balsam-trees which are in the House of the Aged One in Anu (Heliopolis)" [021-117]. Heliopolis (Anu), the chief solar cult centre, is thus the birthplace of the Bennu — a detail that reinforces his solar nature and connects him to the priesthood of [[ra|Ra]].

> "Thou wast the Great Bennu, who art born on the top of the balsam-trees which are in the House of the Aged One in Anu (Heliopolis)" [021-117]

**Keeper of the Book of Destiny**: A footnote in the texts specifies that the Bennu is "the keeper of the book of destiny" [021-135], a role referenced in Chapter 17 of the Book of the Dead. This identifies the Bennu as the divine archivist who holds the record of fates — a function of immense cosmological importance.

## Cult and Worship

The Bennu was venerated at Heliopolis (Anu), where he was said to be born atop the balsam-trees in the "House of the Aged One" [021-117]. His association with the Phoenix gave him a prominent place in classical accounts of Egyptian religion. The Bennu's enduring significance lies in his identity as the soul of Ra: through the Bennu, the daily solar cycle and the Osirian resurrection were united in a single, self-renewing divine symbol.
