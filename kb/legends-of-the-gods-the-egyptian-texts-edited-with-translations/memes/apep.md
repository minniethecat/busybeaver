# Apep

## Overview

Apep (also known by the Greek name Apophis) is the great serpent of chaos — the archenemy of the sun-god [[ra]] and the personification of all that opposes divine order (*maat*). Every night, Apep attempted to waylay the solar barque as it journeyed through the underworld (Tuat), seeking to plunge the world into eternal darkness. His defeat each dawn was the central drama of Egyptian cosmology.

## Identity and Role

Apep is the "Leader-in-Chief of the hosts of darkness" — a monstrous serpent who lurks in the Tuat (underworld) and attempts to destroy [[ra]] during his nightly journey:

> The Leader-in Chief of the hosts of darkness was a fiend called Apep who appeared in the sky in the form of a monster serpent, and, marshalling all the fiends of the Tuat, attempted to keep the Sun-god imprisoned in the kingdom of darkness. [005-095]

Unlike most Egyptian deities, Apep is never worshipped — he is purely a force of destruction (*isfet*), the cosmic opposite of [[maat]] (truth, order). He represents the primordial chaos that existed before creation and that constantly threatens to reassert itself. In the serpent's form, Apep "resembled the intestines" [005-095], a vast coiling darkness that sought to swallow the light.

## Family and Relationships

Apep has no family in the traditional sense — he is not part of any divine genealogy. He stands alone as the embodiment of chaos, opposed by virtually every other deity in the Egyptian pantheon. He is the chief adversary of:

- [[ra]] — the sun-god whom Apep attempts to destroy each night
- [[horus]] — whose spells cause Apep to vomit his poison
- [[set]] — who, despite his ambiguous nature, stands on the prow of the solar barque to fight Apep
- [[serq]] (Serqet) — the scorpion-goddess who executes Apep's punishment

> the chief of these was Apep, the great enemy of Ra, who took the form of a huge serpent [005-095]

## Major Myths and Narratives

**The Book of Overthrowing Apep.** An entire ritual corpus was dedicated to the destruction of Apep. The "Book of Overthrowing Apep, the Enemy of Ra, the Enemy of Un-Nefer" [005-095] contained spells recited in temples to ensure the sun's safe passage. These rituals involved the creation and destruction of wax figures of the serpent, accompanied by potent incantations:

> The legend is entitled: Book of knowing the evolutions of Ra [and of] overthrowing Apep. [005-095]

The very recitation of these spells was believed to be "fraught with deadly effect for Apep and his friends" [005-095].

**The Poison of Apep.** One of the key spells against snakebite invokes the power of [[horus]] over Apep's venom:

> the poison of Apep, and was to be recited over anyone who was bitten by a snake. When uttered by Horus it made Apep to vomit [005-100]

This identifies Apep not only as a cosmic threat but also as the mythic source of all serpent venom — every snakebite was, in essence, an attack by Apep.

**The Soul of Apep in Mount Bakhau.** Apep's dwelling was identified with Mount Bakhau, the mythical mountain at the eastern horizon where the sun rises:

> it is the Soul of Apep in Mount Bakhau (i.e., the Mount of Sunrise) [013-032]

The cosmic battle between [[ra]] and Apep was thus located at the liminal space between night and day, darkness and light.

**The Overthrow of Apep.** The god AKER guarded Apep's hands and arms after his defeat, ensuring the serpent could not rise again:

> for the overthrowing of Apep, over whose hands and arms AKER keepeth guard [008-055]

This image of Apep bound and guarded became a standard motif in funerary and temple reliefs.

**The Winding Serpent.** A spell from the Book of the Dead directly confronts Apep:

> Get thee back, Apep, thou enemy of Ra, thou winding serpent in the form of the intestines [018-001]

## Cult and Worship

Apep received no worship — he was the object of execration rituals. In temple ceremonies, priests moulded wax images of Apep, spat upon them, trampled them, and burned them, ritually re-enacting his defeat. These rites were performed daily in the cult of [[ra]] at [[heliopolis]] and in the great temples of Thebes. The "Book of Overthrowing Apep" was recited as a protective measure for both the living and the dead, ensuring that chaos would not triumph over order.
