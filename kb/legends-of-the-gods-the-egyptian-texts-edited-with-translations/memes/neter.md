# Neter

**Neter** (plural **neteru**) is the ancient Egyptian word for "god" or "divine power." It is one of the most fundamental terms in Egyptian theology, used throughout the corpus to designate the deities — from the supreme creator [[Ra]] to local protective spirits — who created and maintain the cosmos.

| Attribute | Detail |

## Meaning and Usage

In the corpus, neter refers to the supreme creator ([[Ra]] is "the ONE god, self-begotten and self-created"), the company of gods who form Ra's divine court, divine powers associated with specific functions, and the deified dead who become neteru in the afterlife.

The texts emphasize the distinction between the creator god who "came into being in primeval time" and the neteru who were created subsequently:

> "The father-gods and the mother-gods who were with me, even when I was in Nu — these are the neteru of ancient time, the ancestors." *(001-025)*

## Occurrences in the Texts

The term permeates the entire corpus. The [[Ennead]] of [[Heliopolis]] — nine neteru from [[Ra]] through [[Osiris]] and [[Isis]] — forms the standard divine genealogy. In the [[Creation Myth]], the neteru are the instruments of cosmic order, and in the [[Book of the Dead]], the deceased aspires to become one of them:

> "The Majesty of this god spake unto the neteru who were in his following, saying: 'Come ye, let us take counsel.'" *(009-053)*
