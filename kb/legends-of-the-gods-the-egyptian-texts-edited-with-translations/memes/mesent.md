# Mesent

**Mesent** (also **Kau-Mesent**) was a double town in Egyptian mythological geography, appearing in the **Legend of the Winged Sun Disk** inscribed at [[Edfu]]. It comprised **Mesent of the West** and **Mesent of the East**, and was granted to the blacksmiths of [[Heru-Behutet]] as their domain in reward for slaying the enemies of [[Ra]].

## Overview

Mesent was established by decree of [[Thoth]] during the wars of Horus of Behutet against [[Set]] and his followers. The domain was assigned to the blacksmiths who served in the bodyguard of Heru-Behutet, and the god himself received the title "Lord of Mesent." The name "Kau-Mesent" marks it as the settlement of these blacksmiths (*kau*), whose work in crafting weapons for the divine war was commemorated in the town's foundation myth.

## Mythological Significance

### Foundation

The town was founded after the blacksmiths of Heru-Behutet defeated the enemies of Ra in the Middle Domains. Ra, satisfied with their work, declared through Thoth that the domain should be named in their honor:

> "The [Middle] Domains shall be called after the names of these blacksmiths from this day onwards, and the god who dwelleth among them, Heru-Behutet, shall be called the 'Lord of Mesent' from this day onwards, and the domain shall be called 'Mesent of the West' from this day onwards." *(015-338 to 015-343)*

The founding narrative thus ties the town's name and identity directly to the cosmic victory over chaos — a pattern common in Egyptian sacred geography, where mythological events are inscribed onto specific locations.

### The Double Town

Mesent was conceived as a pair of settlements with distinct orientations. **Mesent of the West** faced eastward toward the rising sun, while **Mesent of the East** — associated with the eastern blacksmiths — faced south toward the city of Behutet, the sanctuary of Heru-Behutet:

> "As concerning Mesent of the West, the face thereof shall be towards [the East], towards the place where Ra riseth, and this Mesent shall be called 'Mesent of the East' from this day onwards. As concerning the double town of Mesent, the work of these blacksmiths of the East, the face thereof shall be towards the South, towards the city of Behutet, the hiding-place of Heru-Behutet." *(015-341 to 015-346)*

This double orientation — eastward toward the solar rebirth and southward toward the god's sanctuary — mirrors the dual nature of the blacksmiths' service: cosmic (defending Ra) and local (serving Heru-Behutet).

### The Blacksmiths of Mesent

The Mesentiu blacksmiths appear in the Edfu reliefs as armed warriors in boats, spearing crocodiles alongside Heru-Behutet. The **Mesentiu of the West** led the god's boat into battle, while a representative of the **Mesentiu of the East** followed behind, both depicted in the act of destroying the crocodile-form enemies:

> "The Mesentiu of the West are represented by an armed warrior in a boat, who is spearing a crocodile, and leads the way for Heru-Behutet. In a boat behind the great god is a representative of the Mesentiu of the East spearing a crocodile." *(015-358 to 015-364)*

Ra rewarded the blacksmiths with permanent sanctuaries, regular offerings, and a dedicated priesthood ministering by the month and by the hour in all their god-houses.

## Features and Landmarks

The decree establishing Mesent included the naming of several features:

| Feature | Egyptian Name | Description |
|---------|--------------|-------------|
| **Stream** | Asti | The waterway of the domain |
| **Great House** | Abet | The principal sanctuary |
| **Priest** | Qen-aha | Title of the officiating priest |
| **Domain** | Kau-Mesent | "Mesent of the Blacksmiths" — the full name of the settlement |

Four annual festivals were decreed for Mesent, to be observed on the second day of Thoth (1st month of Akhet), the twenty-fourth day of Choiak (4th month of Akhet), the seventh day of Tybi (1st month of Pert), and the twenty-first day of Mechir (2nd month of Pert). All rites and ceremonies of Heru-Behutet were to be performed at Mesent on these days.

## Lord of Mesent

The title "Lord of Mesent" became a permanent epithet of Heru-Behutet. In the later sections of the Edfu text, the god is invoked with the full formula:

> "Heru-Behutet, the great god, the lord of heaven, the lord of Mesent, the president of the Ater of the North." *(015-3126 to 015-3127)*

This title pairs with his presidency over the northern half of heaven (the Ater of the North), with the goddess Uatchit placed upon him in serpent form. The symmetrical southern counterpart — "president of the Ater of the South" — is also associated with Heru-Behutet but without the Mesent epithet, suggesting the title belonged specifically to the northern celestial jurisdiction.

## In the Texts

The primary source for Mesent is the **Legend of the Winged Sun Disk** (PN=015), the Ptolemaic inscription on the temple walls of Edfu. Twelve references span the sections concerning the blacksmiths' reward (015-324 to 015-355) and the later recitation of Heru-Behutet's titles (015-382, 015-441 to 015-446). Additional mentions appear in the descriptions of the temple reliefs that accompanied the text (Naville, *Mythe d'Horus*, pl. 17).

The Mesentiu warriors are also depicted in the sculptural program of the Edfu temple, where they appear as spear-wielding figures in boats, visually reinforcing the textual narrative of the crocodile hunt.
