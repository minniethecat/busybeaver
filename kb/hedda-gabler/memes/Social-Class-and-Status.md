# Social Class and Status

The world of *Hedda Gabler* is rigidly stratified, and every character's position in the class hierarchy determines their possibilities, their vulnerabilities, and their fate.

[[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]]'s tragedy is in significant part a class tragedy: the daughter of an aristocrat trapped in a bourgeois marriage, with aristocratic values she cannot live by and bourgeois constraints she cannot accept.

## The Class Structure

Ibsen maps the play's social world with ethnographic precision:

| Class | Representative | Defining Feature |
|-------|---------------|-----------------|
| Aristocracy (military) | [[General-Gablers-Shadow|General Gabler]] (absent) | Honor, action, aesthetic code, pistols |
| Upper Bourgeoisie (professionals) | [[Judge-Brack]], [[George-Tesman]] | Respectability, income, education, social connections |
| Middle Bourgeoisie (dependent) | [[Aunt-Julia]], [[Aunt-Rina]] | Annuities, thrift, domestic virtue, vulnerability |
| Intelligentsia (precarious) | [[Eilert-Lovborg]] | Talent without position, brilliance without security |
| Gentry (provincial) | [[Mrs-Elvsted]], Sheriff Elvsted | Rural respectability, limited horizons |
| Working Class (invisible) | [[Berta]] | Service, silence, immunity to scandal |
| Underclass (criminalized) | [[Mademoiselle-Diana]] | Danger, scandal, the outside world |

## Hedda's Class Position

Hedda occupies a uniquely contradictory class position. She is born into the aristocracy (General Gabler's daughter) but married into the upper bourgeoisie (Tesman's wife). She has aristocratic tastes and values but bourgeois resources and constraints.

She despises the bourgeoisie but cannot return to the aristocracy she came from — the General is dead, the money is gone, and the world that supported her class position no longer exists.

This class dislocation is the material basis of her boredom and cruelty. An aristocrat with an estate, horses, and social power might have outlets for her intelligence and energy. A bourgeois wife with domestic duties and social visits has none.

## Tesman's Class Aspiration

[[George-Tesman|Tesman]] married "above himself" — a move he is both proud of and anxious about. The villa, the professorship, the "distinguished" wife — all of these are class markers he has acquired through luck and devotion rather than birth.

His aunts' sacrifice (mortgaging their annuities) to secure the villa reveals the precariousness of his class position.

Tesman's obliviousness to Hedda's misery is partly a class blindness: he cannot imagine that the villa, the professorship, and his devotion might not be enough, because for someone of his class position, they would be more than enough.

## Brack's Class Security

[[Judge-Brack]] occupies the most secure class position in the play.

He is a judge — the embodiment of bourgeois law and order — with the "correctness and respectability" (007-212) that are "beyond all question." His class security is what enables his power: he can threaten scandal because he is scandal-proof.

The same society that would destroy Hedda for the knowledge Brack possesses protects Brack absolutely.

## The Invisible Working Class

[[Berta]] is the play's sole representative of the working class, and her presence is both essential and invisible. She enables the domestic world — cleaning, cooking, answering doors — while being excluded from its social logic.

Scandal cannot touch her because she has no reputation to lose. The class hierarchy that traps Hedda liberates Berta: she can leave, she can find another position, she can remain herself regardless of what happens in the drawing room.

## Thematic Significance

Class in *Hedda Gabler* is not background — it is the primary determinant of character and fate. Every relationship is a class relationship. Every choice is bounded by class position.

Hedda's tragedy is that she was born into a class that no longer exists, trained in values that cannot be lived, and married into a class she cannot respect.
