# The Drawing Room as Dramatic Space

The drawing room of the Tesman villa is one of Ibsen's most carefully constructed domestic interiors. Every piece of furniture, every doorway, every curtain functions as a node in the play's symbolic geography.

## Layout and Power

The room is arranged as a map of social relations. The sofa defines the "public" space where visitors are received; the inner room is the "private" space of retreat and, ultimately, suicide. The glass door opens onto the garden — a threshold Hedda never crosses. The stove is the room's hot center, site of warmth and destruction. Each character's movement through this geography maps their social position and trajectory.

## The Curtains

[[The-Curtains]] are the room's most active architectural feature. They are drawn and redrawn throughout the play, creating temporary boundaries between public and private, seen and unseen. Hedda's manipulation of the curtains — closing them to hide, opening them to perform — mirrors her broader strategy of controlling visibility and access.

## Director's Stage

Ibsen's stage directions are unusually precise, and the drawing room functions as a "director's stage" where Hedda orchestrates scenes. She arranges bodies in space — seating Lovborg here, positioning Thea there — with the deliberate eye of a theatrical director. The room is not merely a setting but an instrument she plays, until Brack takes over the instrument in the final act.
