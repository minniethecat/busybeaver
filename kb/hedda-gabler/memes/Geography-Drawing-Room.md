# Geography: The Drawing-Room as Theater

The drawing-room in *Hedda Gabler* is not merely the play's setting — it is its primary stage, and "stage" is the operative word. Every character who crosses its threshold performs: Tesman performs scholarly diligence, Brack performs worldly ease, Thea performs plucky devotion, and [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] performs the role of the gracious hostess who is secretly bored. The drawing-room is the theater of appearances (006-008), and Hedda is its most accomplished actor.

Hedda's mastery of this space is complete. She knows exactly how to arrange herself, how to deploy the "nervous" laugh, how to extract confidences under the cover of polite conversation. Her skills — manipulation, charm, aesthetic judgment — are drawing-room skills, honed through years of aristocratic socializing.

But mastery of the drawing-room is also imprisonment within it. Hedda can perform the drawing-room perfectly because she has no other stage. Her skills are useless outside its walls: a woman of her class has no profession, no independent income, no public existence. The drawing-room is the space where she is most powerful and most trapped — a paradox that defines her tragedy. She can manipulate everyone within its walls; she can never function beyond them.

The drawing-room's relationship to [[The-Inner-Room|the inner room]] is crucial: public performance yields to private chaos. What Hedda suppresses in the drawing-room erupts in the inner room — the wild piano, the manuscript-burning, the suicide. The [[Tesman-Villa|villa]]'s geography is a psychological diagram, and the drawing-room is its public face.
