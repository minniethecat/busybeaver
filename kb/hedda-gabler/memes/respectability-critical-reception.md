# Critical Reception of Respectability

Scholars have identified respectability as the central thematic concern of *Hedda Gabler*, arguing that the play is less a character study than a sociological analysis of how social codes produce and constrain identity.

The introduction notes that Ibsen regarded the play as depicting "human beings, human emotions, and human destinies, upon a groundwork of certain of the social conditions and principles of the present day" (001-028) -- respectability being the primary such principle.

Critics have drawn connections between the play's respectability economy and Ibsen's critique of bourgeois marriage across his oeuvre. In *A Doll's House*, Nora's exit from respectability is voluntary and liberating; in *Hedda Gabler*, Hedda's exit is involuntary and lethal. The difference reflects Ibsen's darkening assessment of the possibility of escape from social constraint.

> The introduction's framing is explicit: the play is built on "a groundwork of certain of the social conditions and principles of the present day" (001-028). [[Respectability]] is not a theme among themes -- it is the ground itself, the condition of possibility for every action and reaction in the play's world.
