[007-001]HEDDA.

[007-002]Of course I cannot have my man in livery just yet.

[007-003]TESMAN.

[007-004]Oh, no, unfortunately. It would be out of the question for us to keep a

[007-005]footman, you know.

[007-006]HEDDA.

[007-007]And the saddle-horse I was to have had--

[007-008]TESMAN.

[007-009][Aghast.] The saddle-horse!

[007-010]HEDDA.

[007-011]--I suppose I must not think of that now.

[007-012]TESMAN.

[007-013]Good heavens, no!--that's as clear as daylight!

[007-014]HEDDA.

[007-015][Goes up the room.] Well, I shall have one thing at least to kill time

[007-016]with in the meanwhile.

[007-017]TESMAN.

[007-018][Beaming.] Oh thank heaven for that! What is it, Hedda. Eh?

[007-019]HEDDA.

[007-020][In the middle doorway, looks at him with covert scorn.] My pistols,

[007-021]George.

[007-022]TESMAN.

[007-023][In alarm.] Your pistols!

[007-024]HEDDA.

[007-025][With cold eyes.] General Gabler's pistols.

[007-026][She goes out through the inner room, to the left.

[007-027]TESMAN.

[007-028][Rushes up to the middle doorway and calls after her:] No, for heaven's

[007-029]sake, Hedda darling--don't touch those dangerous things! For my sake

[007-030]Hedda! Eh?

[007-031]ACT SECOND.

[007-032]The room at the TESMANS' as in the first Act, except that the

[007-033]piano has been removed, and an elegant little writing-table

[007-034]with the book-shelves put in its place. A smaller table

[007-035]stands near the sofa on the left. Most of the bouquets have

[007-036]been taken away. MRS. ELVSTED'S bouquet is upon the large

[007-037]table in front.--It is afternoon.

[007-038]HEDDA, dressed to receive callers, is alone in the room. She

[007-039]stands by the open glass door, loading a revolver. The

[007-040]fellow to it lies in an open pistol-case on the writing-

[007-041]table.

[007-042]HEDDA.

[007-043][Looks down the garden, and calls:] So you are here again, Judge!

[007-044]BRACK.

[007-045][Is heard calling from a distance.] As you see, Mrs. Tesman!

[007-046]HEDDA.

[007-047][Raises the pistol and points.] Now I'll shoot you, [[Judge-Brack|Judge Brack]]!

[007-048]BRACK.

[007-049][Calling unseen.] No, no, no! Don't stand aiming at me!

[007-050]HEDDA.

[007-051]This is what comes of sneaking in by the back way.(7) [She fires.

[007-052]BRACK.

[007-053][Nearer.] Are you out of your senses--!

[007-054]HEDDA.

[007-055]Dear me--did I happen to hit you?

[007-056]BRACK.

[007-057][Still outside.] I wish you would let these pranks alone!

[007-058]HEDDA.

[007-059]Come in then, Judge.

[007-060]JUDGE BRACK, dressed as though for a men's party, enters by

[007-061]the glass door. He carries a light overcoat over his arm.

[007-062]BRACK.

[007-063]What the deuce--haven't you tired of that sport, yet? What are you

[007-064]shooting at?

[007-065]HEDDA.

[007-066]Oh, I am only firing in the air.

[007-067]BRACK.

[007-068][Gently takes the pistol out of her hand.] Allow me, madam! [Looks at

[007-069]it.] Ah--I know this pistol well! [Looks around.] Where is the case? Ah,

[007-070]here it is. [Lays the pistol in it, and shuts it.] Now we won't play at

[007-071]that game any more to-day.

[007-072]HEDDA.

[007-073]Then what in heaven's name would you have me do with myself?

[007-074]BRACK.

[007-075]Have you had no visitors?

[007-076]HEDDA.

[007-077][Closing the glass door.] Not one. I suppose all our set are still out

[007-078]of town.

[007-079]BRACK.

[007-080]And is Tesman not at home either?

[007-081]HEDDA.

[007-082][At the writing-table, putting the pistol-case in a drawer which she

[007-083]shuts.] No. He rushed off to his aunt's directly after lunch; he didn't

[007-084]expect you so early.

[007-085]BRACK.

[007-086]H'm--how stupid of me not to have thought of that!

[007-087]HEDDA.

[007-088][Turning her head to look at him.] Why stupid?

[007-089]BRACK.

[007-090]Because if I had thought of it I should have come a little--earlier.

[007-091]HEDDA.

[007-092][Crossing the room.] Then you would have found no one to receive you;

[007-093]for I have been in my room changing my dress ever since lunch.

[007-094]BRACK.

[007-095]And is there no sort of little chink that we could hold a parley

[007-096]through?

[007-097]HEDDA.

[007-098]You have forgotten to arrange one.

[007-099]BRACK.

[007-100]That was another piece of stupidity.

[007-101]HEDDA.

[007-102]Well, we must just settle down here--and wait. Tesman is not likely to

[007-103]be back for some time yet.

[007-104]BRACK.

[007-105]Never mind; I shall not be impatient.

[007-106]HEDDA seats herself in the corner of the sofa. BRACK lays his

[007-107]overcoat over the back of the nearest chair, and sits down, but

[007-108]keeps his hat in his hand. A short silence. They look at each

[007-109]other.

[007-110]HEDDA.

[007-111]Well?

[007-112]BRACK.

[007-113][In the same tone.] Well?

[007-114]HEDDA.

[007-115]I spoke first.

[007-116]BRACK.

[007-117][Bending a little forward.] Come, let us have a cosy little chat, Mrs.

[007-118]Hedda.(8)

[007-119]HEDDA.

[007-120][Leaning further back in the sofa.] Does it not seem like a whole

[007-121]eternity since our last talk? Of course I don't count those few words

[007-122]yesterday evening and this morning.

[007-123]BRACK.

[007-124]You mean since our last confidential talk? Our last _tete-a-tete_?

[007-125]HEDDA.

[007-126]Well yes--since you put it so.

[007-127]BRACK.

[007-128]Not a day passed but I have wished that you were home again.

[007-129]HEDDA.

[007-130]And I have done nothing but wish the same thing.

[007-131]BRACK.

[007-132]You? Really, Mrs. Hedda? And I thought you had been enjoying your tour

[007-133]so much!

[007-134]HEDDA.

[007-135]Oh yes, you may be sure of that!

[007-136]BRACK.

[007-137]But Tesman's letters spoke of nothing but happiness.

[007-138]HEDDA.

[007-139]Oh, Tesman! You see, he thinks nothing is so delightful as grubbing

[007-140]in libraries and making copies of old parchments, or whatever you call

[007-141]them.

[007-142]BRACK.

[007-143][With a smile of malice.] Well, that is his vocation in life--or part of

[007-144]it at any rate.

[007-145]HEDDA.

[007-146]Yes, of course; and no doubt when it's your vocation--. But _I_! Oh, my

[007-147]dear Mr. Brack, how mortally bored I have been.

[007-148]BRACK.

[007-149][Sympathetically.] Do you really say so? In downright earnest?

[007-150]HEDDA.

[007-151]Yes, you can surely understand it--! To go for six whole months without

[007-152]meeting a soul that knew anything of our circle, or could talk about

[007-153]things we were interested in.

[007-154]BRACK.

[007-155]Yes, yes--I too should feel that a deprivation.

[007-156]HEDDA.

[007-157]And then, what I found most intolerable of all--

[007-158]BRACK.

[007-159]Well?

[007-160]HEDDA.

[007-161]--was being everlastingly in the company of--one and the same person--

[007-162]BRACK.

[007-163][With a nod of assent.] Morning, noon, and night, yes--at all possible

[007-164]times and seasons.

[007-165]HEDDA.

[007-166]I said "everlastingly."

[007-167]BRACK.

[007-168]Just so. But I should have thought, with our excellent Tesman, one

[007-169]could--

[007-170]HEDDA.

[007-171]Tesman is--a specialist, my dear Judge.

[007-172]BRACK.

[007-173]Undeniable.

[007-174]HEDDA.

[007-175]And specialists are not at all amusing to travel with. Not in the long

[007-176]run at any rate.

[007-177]BRACK.

[007-178]Not even--the specialist one happens to love?

[007-179]HEDDA.

[007-180]Faugh--don't use that sickening word!

[007-181]BRACK.

[007-182][Taken aback.] What do you say, Mrs. Hedda?

[007-183]HEDDA.

[007-184][Half laughing, half irritated.] You should just try it! To hear of

[007-185]nothing but the history of civilisation, morning, noon, and night--

[007-186]BRACK.

[007-187]Everlastingly.

[007-188]HEDDA.

[007-189]Yes yes yes! And then all this about the domestic industry of the middle

[007-190]ages--! That's the most disgusting part of it!

[007-191]BRACK.

[007-192][Looks searchingly at her.] But tell me--in that case, how am I to

[007-193]understand your--? H'm--

[007-194]HEDDA.

[007-195]My accepting [[George-Tesman|George Tesman]], you mean?

[007-196]BRACK.

[007-197]Well, let us put it so.

[007-198]HEDDA.

[007-199]Good heavens, do you see anything so wonderful in that?

[007-200]BRACK.

[007-201]Yes and no--Mrs. Hedda.

[007-202]HEDDA.

[007-203]I had positively danced myself tired, my dear Judge. My day was

[007-204]done-- [With a slight shudder.] Oh no--I won't say that; nor think

[007-205]it either!

[007-206]BRACK.

[007-207]You have assuredly no reason to.

[007-208]HEDDA.

[007-209]Oh, reasons-- [Watching him closely.] And George Tesman--after all, you

[007-210]must admit that he is correctness itself.

[007-211]BRACK.

[007-212]His correctness and respectability are beyond all question.

[007-213]HEDDA.

[007-214]And I don't see anything absolutely ridiculous about him.--Do you?

[007-215]BRACK.

[007-216]Ridiculous? N--no--I shouldn't exactly say so--

[007-217]HEDDA.

[007-218]Well--and his powers of research, at all events, are untiring.--I see no

[007-219]reason why he should not one day come to the front, after all.

[007-220]BRACK.

[007-221][Looks at her hesitatingly.] I thought that you, like every one else,

[007-222]expected him to attain the highest distinction.

[007-223]HEDDA.

[007-224][With an expression of fatigue.] Yes, so I did.--And then, since he was

[007-225]bent, at all hazards, on being allowed to provide for me--I really don't

[007-226]know why I should not have accepted his offer?

[007-227]BRACK.

[007-228]No--if you look at it in that light--

[007-229]HEDDA.

[007-230]It was more than my other adorers were prepared to do for me, my dear

[007-231]Judge.

[007-232]BRACK.

[007-233][Laughing.] Well, I can't answer for all the rest; but as for myself,

[007-234]you know quite well that I have always entertained a--a certain respect

[007-235]for the marriage tie--for marriage as an institution, Mrs. Hedda.

[007-236]HEDDA.

[007-237][Jestingly.] Oh, I assure you I have never cherished any hopes with

[007-238]respect to you.

[007-239]BRACK.

[007-240]All I require is a pleasant and intimate interior, where I can make

[007-241]myself useful in every way, and am free to come and go as--as a trusted

[007-242]friend--

[007-243]HEDDA.

[007-244]Of the master of the house, do you mean?

[007-245]BRACK.

[007-246][Bowing.] Frankly--of the mistress first of all; but of course of the

[007-247]master too, in the second place. Such a triangular friendship--if I may

[007-248]call it so--is really a great convenience for all the parties, let me

[007-249]tell you.

[007-250]HEDDA.

[007-251]Yes, I have many a time longed for some one to make a third on our

[007-252]travels. Oh--those railway-carriage _tete-a-tetes_--!

[007-253]BRACK.

[007-254]Fortunately your wedding journey is over now.

[007-255]HEDDA.

[007-256][Shaking her head.] Not by a long--long way. I have only arrived at a

[007-257]station on the line.

[007-258]BRACK.

[007-259]Well, then the passengers jump out and move about a little, Mrs. Hedda.

[007-260]HEDDA.

[007-261]I never jump out.

[007-262]BRACK.

[007-263]Really?

[007-264]HEDDA.

[007-265]No--because there is always some one standing by to--

[007-266]BRACK.

[007-267][Laughing.] To look at your ankles, do you mean?

[007-268]HEDDA.

[007-269]Precisely.

[007-270]BRACK.

[007-271]Well but, dear me--

[007-272]HEDDA.

[007-273][With a gesture of repulsion.] I won't have it. I would rather keep my

[007-274]seat where I happen to be--and continue the _tete-a-tete_.

[007-275]BRACK.

[007-276]But suppose a third person were to jump in and join the couple.

[007-277]HEDDA.

[007-278]Ah--that is quite another matter!

[007-279]BRACK.

[007-280]A trusted, sympathetic friend--

[007-281]HEDDA.

[007-282]--with a fund of conversation on all sorts of lively topics--

[007-283]BRACK.

[007-284]--and not the least bit of a specialist!

[007-285]HEDDA.

[007-286][With an audible sigh.] Yes, that would be a relief indeed.

[007-287]BRACK.

[007-288][Hears the front door open, and glances in that direction.] The triangle

[007-289]is completed.

[007-290]HEDDA.

[007-291][Half aloud.] And on goes the train.

[007-292]GEORGE TESMAN, in a grey walking-suit, with a soft felt hat,

[007-293]enters from the hall. He has a number of unbound books under

[007-294]his arm and in his pockets.

[007-295]TESMAN.

[007-296][Goes up to the table beside the corner settee.] Ouf--what a load for

[007-297]a warm day--all these books. [Lays them on the table.] I'm positively

[007-298]perspiring, Hedda. Hallo--are you there already, my dear Judge? Eh?

[007-299][[Berta]] didn't tell me.

[007-300]BRACK.

[007-301][Rising.] I came in through the garden.

[007-302]HEDDA.

[007-303]What books have you got there?

[007-304]TESMAN.

[007-305][Stands looking them through.] Some new books on my special subjects

[007-306]--quite indispensable to me.

[007-307]HEDDA.

[007-308]Your special subjects?

[007-309]BRACK.

[007-310]Yes, books on his special subjects, Mrs. Tesman.

[007-311][BRACK and HEDDA exchange a confidential smile.

[007-312]HEDDA.

[007-313]Do you need still more books on your special subjects?

[007-314]TESMAN.

[007-315]Yes, my dear Hedda, one can never have too many of them. Of course one

[007-316]must keep up with all that is written and published.

[007-317]HEDDA.

[007-318]Yes, I suppose one must.

[007-319]TESMAN.

[007-320][Searching among his books.] And look here--I have got hold of Eilert

[007-321]Lovborg's new book too. [Offering it to her.] Perhaps you would like to

[007-322]glance through it, Hedda? Eh?

[007-323]HEDDA.

[007-324]No, thank you. Or rather--afterwards perhaps.

[007-325]TESMAN.

[007-326]I looked into it a little on the way home.

[007-327]BRACK.

[007-328]Well, what do you think of it--as a specialist?

[007-329]TESMAN.

[007-330]I think it shows quite remarkable soundness of judgment. He never wrote

[007-331]like that before. [Putting the books together.] Now I shall take all

[007-332]these into my study. I'm longing to cut the leaves--! And then I must

[007-333]change my clothes. [To BRACK.] I suppose we needn't start just yet? Eh?

[007-334]BRACK.

[007-335]Oh, dear no--there is not the slightest hurry.

[007-336]TESMAN.

[007-337]Well then, I will take my time. [Is going with his books, but stops in

[007-338]the doorway and turns.] By-the-bye, Hedda--Aunt Julia is not coming this

[007-339]evening.

[007-340]HEDDA.

[007-341]Not coming? Is it that affair of the bonnet that keeps her away?

[007-342]TESMAN.

[007-343]Oh, not at all. How could you think such a thing of Aunt Julia? Just

[007-344]fancy--! The fact is, [[Aunt-Rina|Aunt Rina]] is very ill.

[007-345]HEDDA.
