# The Professor's Villa: Architecture of Respectability

The [[Tesman-Villa]] embodies the respectability economy of *Hedda Gabler*. [[George-Tesman|Tesman]] purchased it with borrowed money because [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] "must have a house worthy of General Gabler's daughter" (003-248). [[Aunt-Julia]] mortgaged her annuity to help furnish it. The villa is the outward sign of the Tesmans' social position.

> A spacious, handsome, and tastefully furnished drawing room, decorated in dark colours. In the back, a wide doorway with curtains drawn back, leading into a smaller room decorated in the same style as the drawing-room.
>
> (002-142)

The villa's spatial organization maps onto the play's moral geography. The drawing-room is the stage for [[Respectability|respectable]] performance; [[The-Inner-Room]] is concealment -- where [[Heddas-Pistols|the pistols]] are kept, where private conversations happen; [[The-Glass-Door]] admits the outside world's judgment.

Hedda's dissatisfaction with the villa is her dissatisfaction with respectability itself. [[Aunt-Julia]] calls it "the house that love built" (004-019) -- but Hedda knows love had nothing to do with it. It was built on borrowed money, social aspiration, and her own unwillingness to imagine any other life. The villa is not a home; it is a mortgage on her existence.
