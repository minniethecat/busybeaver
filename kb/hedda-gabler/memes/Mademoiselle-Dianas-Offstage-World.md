# Mademoiselle Diana's: The Offstage World

Ibsen deliberately keeps Mademoiselle Diana's offstage, and this choice is fundamental to the play's dramatic structure. The unified setting — the [[Tesman-Villa|Tesman villa]]'s drawing room — means the audience, like [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]], experiences Lovborg's death entirely through [[Judge-Brack|Brack]]'s narration.

This narrative structure makes Diana's a mirror: what happens there reflects and contaminates the drawing room without ever appearing in it.

The offstage structure enacts a class hierarchy of space. The drawing room is visible, narrated, and respectable; the boudoir is invisible, hearsay, and scandalous. Yet by the play's end, Brack has made the scandal as real as the furniture. He invokes "this same Mademoiselle Diana" (015-149) as a threat, and the distinction between the two spaces dissolves — the offstage has colonized the stage.

The red-haired Mademoiselle Diana herself — modeled on details from the real-life "Holm" (002-004) — is never seen. She exists only through Brack's words. This makes her all the more potent as a symbol of the world Hedda cannot control. She is a specter, an absence, a name spoken in fragments — and her invisibility is her power.

Diana's also functions as the anti-villa. Every quality the Tesman villa asserts — respectability, privacy, orderly domesticity — Diana's negates. Yet Brack moves freely between them, and his knowledge connects them irrevocably. The offstage world is always present, always threatening, and always beyond Hedda's control.

When Brack threatens to make the connection public, he is threatening not just exposure but the collapse of the spatial order that sustains Hedda's identity.
