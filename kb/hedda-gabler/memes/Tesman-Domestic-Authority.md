# Tesman: Oblivious Domestic Authority

[[George-Tesman]]'s power is the most diffuse and least self-aware of the masculine authorities that structure Hedda's world — which makes it, in her eyes, the most contemptible. He holds authority by default: as husband, as breadwinner, as the man who secured the [[Tesman-Villa|villa]] she idly admired. His scholarship — "trying to grub up his little atoms of knowledge" (002-058) — is industrious but uninspired, the intellectual masculinity of a man whose ambitions run no higher than a professorship.

Tesman does not need to dominate because he cannot conceive that domination is at issue. He assumes Hedda's contentment as he assumes his aunts' devotion: it is simply what the world owes a decent man. When [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] burns [[The-Manuscript|Lovborg's manuscript]], Tesman's response is not horror at the act but wonder that she would do it "for his sake" — a misreading that reveals his entire understanding of his marriage.

His domestic authority is real (she lives in his house, spends his money, carries his child) but invisible to him, and that invisibility makes it unbearable. Hedda can fight an enemy she can see; she cannot fight a power that does not know itself. Tesman's obliviousness is not benign — it is a form of control that forecloses even the possibility of resistance, because resistance requires an adversary who acknowledges the conflict.

Tesman's authority also has an institutional dimension: his academic career gives him a public identity, while Hedda has none. He moves between the villa and the university; she moves between the drawing-room and the inner room. His freedom is not malicious, but it is structural, and it defines the terms of their marriage.
