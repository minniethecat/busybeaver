# Aunt Julia — Critical Reception

Aunt Julia has received less critical attention than almost any other named character in *Hedda Gabler*, yet her dramatic function is essential to the play's moral architecture.

## Early Reception

Early critics tended to dismiss Aunt Julia as comic relief — a figure of bourgeois sentimentality whose fussing over bonnets and morning visits provided necessary tonal contrast to Hedda's intensity. This reading placed her in the tradition of the "comic servant" or "foolish old woman" of 19th-century drama.

## Revisionist Reading

Later scholarship has revalued Julia significantly. Feminist critics note that she is the only character in the play whose love is neither transactional nor destructive. She has sacrificed her annuity to support George's career (001-088); she has raised two motherless children; she endures Hedda's cruelty with dignity. Her "bourgeois" values — thrift, devotion, duty — are shown to be more genuinely moral than Hedda's aestheticized cruelty.

## Structural Role

The most sophisticated recent readings focus on Julia's structural function. She opens and closes the play's action — arriving in Act I to welcome the newlyweds, departing in Act IV to nurse the dying Rina. Her presence creates a frame of domestic continuity around the destruction in the drawing room. Without Julia, the play would lack any moral reference point against which Hedda's coldness could be measured.

## From the Text

> TESMAN.
> Security? You? My dear Aunt Julia--what sort of security could you give?
> MISS TESMAN.
> I have given a mortgage on our annuity.
> TESMAN.
> [Jumps up.] What! On your--and Aunt Rina's annuity!
> MISS TESMAN.
> Yes, I knew of no other plan, you see.
> TESMAN.
> [Placing himself before her.] Have you gone out of your senses, Auntie? Your annuity--it's all that you and Aunt Rina have to live upon.
> MISS TESMAN.
> Well well--don't get so excited about it. It's only a matter of form you know--Judge Brack assured me of that.
> TESMAN.
> Oh Auntie--will you never be tired of making sacrifices for me!
> MISS TESMAN.
> [Rises and lays her hand on his shoulders.] Have I any other happiness in this world except to smooth your way for you, my dear boy.
