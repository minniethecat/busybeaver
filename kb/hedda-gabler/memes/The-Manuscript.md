# The Manuscript

Eilert Lovborg's magnum opus — a visionary work on "the social development of the future" (002-043) — co-created with [[Mrs-Elvsted|Thea Elvsted]] and destroyed by [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda Gabler]] in Act III.

The manuscript is the play's central symbol: of creative partnership, moral regeneration, intellectual parenthood, and the destructive power of envy.

Its trajectory — creation, loss, destruction, reconstruction — traces the play's deepest dialectic between making and unmaking.

## Significance in the Play

The manuscript appears at the midpoint of the drama and drives every subsequent action. [[Eilert-Lovborg|Lovborg]] carries it to [[The-Bachelor-Party|Brack's party]]; [[George-Tesman|Tesman]] finds it dropped in the street; [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] burns it in [[The-Stove|the stove]];

Lovborg's despair over its loss leads to his death at [[Mademoiselle-Dianas]]; and [[Mrs-Elvsted|Thea]] and [[George-Tesman|Tesman]]'s reconstruction of it provides the play's closing image.

The manuscript is the only object in the play that travels through every character's hands: written by Lovborg and Thea, dropped by Lovborg, found by Tesman, hidden by Hedda, burned by Hedda, mourned by Lovborg, reconstructed by Thea and Tesman.

It is the play's bloodstream.

## Dramatic Functions

The manuscript serves multiple dramatic purposes simultaneously:

| Function | Mechanism | Consequence |
|----------|-----------|-------------|
| **Economic** | Its publication would secure Lovborg the professorship over Tesman | Raises the rivalry stakes from personal to professional |
| **Symbolic** | It represents the "child" of Lovborg and Thea's creative partnership | Makes Hedda's destruction an act of symbolic infanticide |
| **Moral** | Its destruction is Hedda's most unequivocally evil act | Severs any remaining audience sympathy for the protagonist |
| **Structural** | Its reconstruction by Tesman and Thea closes the play | Provides an image of renewal — creation survives destruction |

## The Child Metaphor

The most powerful and disturbing aspect of the manuscript is its identification with parenthood. When Lovborg tells Thea he has destroyed it, she protests with the play's most anguished line: "But that is the child, Eilert!

That is our child!" The manuscript is the product of their shared intellectual labor, more real to Thea than any biological offspring.

This metaphor cuts directly and lethally at [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]], who is pregnant with [[George-Tesman|Tesman]]'s child — a biological fact she finds repulsive and tries to conceal even from herself. The contrast is absolute:

| Dimension | Manuscript-Child | Biological Child |
|-----------|-----------------|------------------|
| **Creation** | Freely chosen, collaborative | Imposed, biological accident |
| **Parents** | Lovborg + Thea (intellectual equals) | Tesman + Hedda (she despises him) |
| **Gender** | Thea is co-creator, not vessel | Hedda is vessel, not creator |
| **Value** | Future of civilization | Unwanted burden |
| **Agency** | Both partners chose this | Hedda never chose this |

When Hedda burns the manuscript page by page, whispering "Now I am burning your child, Thea!" (013-125), she is destroying what she most envies — a creation freely chosen rather than a pregnancy unwillingly endured.

## Creation and Collaboration

[[Mrs-Elvsted|Thea Elvsted]] describes her role in the manuscript's creation with pride and precision: she followed [[Eilert-Lovborg|Lovborg]] from the provinces, organized his notes, discussed his ideas,.

and "made a clean copy of the whole." The manuscript is therefore a product of genuine intellectual partnership — the equal creative relationship that [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]], for all her intelligence, has never achieved and may not even be capable of.

The collaboration model is striking for its time: Thea is not merely Lovborg's amanuensis but his intellectual partner.

She "inspired" him, she "discussed everything" with him, and when he dictates, she takes down every word because "there mustn't be the slightest mistake." This is not the traditional model of male genius and female support —.

it is a vision of collaborative creation between equals.

Hedda's exclusion from this model is central to her tragedy. She has intelligence but no outlet; she has ambition but no project; she has a pistol but no target.

The manuscript represents everything she could have been — and the burning is her acknowledgment, in the only language she speaks (destruction), of what she is not.

## Destruction: The Burning

Hedda burns the manuscript in [[The-Stove|the stove]] while Lovborg searches desperately outside. She burns it page by page, watching the flames, and explicitly frames the act as infanticide:

"Now I am burning your child, Thea! — You with your curly hair — your child and Eilert Lovborg's. Now I am burning — burning the child" (013-125).

The page-by-page method is crucial: Hedda does not throw the whole manuscript at once. She savors each page's destruction, drawing out the act into a ritual.

The repetition of "burning" — four times in a single speech — reveals the ecstatic, almost sexual quality of the destruction. This is not rage; it is release.

The historical resonance was deliberate. Ibsen drew on the real-life case of a Norwegian composer's wife who burned her husband's symphony manuscript in a fit of jealous rage (001-146).

But Hedda's act is not a fit — it is methodical, premeditated, and conducted with full awareness of its meaning.

[[Berta]] later "makes up the fire" — the servant unknowingly completing what the mistress began. Domestic labor becomes accessory to destruction; the household's ordinary rhythms absorb the extraordinary crime.

## Reconstruction and Legacy

The play ends not with the manuscript's destruction but with its reconstruction.

[[George-Tesman|Tesman]] and [[Mrs-Elvsted|Thea]], united in grief and scholarly devotion, sit together at a table near [[The-Stove|the stove]] — the same stove that consumed the original — and begin the painstaking work of rebuilding Lovborg's ideas from Thea's notes.

> "I have all the loose scraps he used to dictate from" — Thea's quiet claim contains the play's most hopeful assertion: the raw material of creation survives the fire. Ideas cannot be burned, only their current form.

The closing image is Ibsen's final dialectical move: destruction and creation, death and rebirth, share the same room, the same furniture, the same fire.

The manuscript dies so that it can be reborn — and the rebirth is more collaborative, more egalitarian, and more hopeful than the original creation ever was.


## Key Passages from the Text

> Ibsen had no doubt heard how the wife of a well-known Norwegian
> composer, in a fit of raging jealousy excited by her husband's prolonged
> absence from home, burnt the manuscript of a symphony which he had just
> finished. The circumstances under which Hedda burns Lovborg's manuscript
> are, of course, entirely different and infinitely more dramatic;
> but here we have merely another instance of the dramatisation or
> "poetisation" of the raw material of life.
>
> (001-146–001-152)
