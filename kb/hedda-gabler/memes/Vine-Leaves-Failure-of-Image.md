# Vine Leaves: Failure of the Image

The vine leaves fantasy fails, and its failure is the play's turning point. When [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] learns the details of [[Eilert-Lovborg|Lovborg]]'s death, her first question is aesthetic: "Was it — beautiful?" (014-090). [[Judge-Brack|Brack]]'s answer — "The shot has hit him in a vital part ... the stomach" (014-106) — destroys the fantasy at its root.

Hedda immediately revises: "In the breast. I say in the breast" (014-120). This is not confusion but aesthetic correction. The gut-shot is ugly, accidental, degrading. The breast-shot is clean, intentional, beautiful. Hedda cannot accept the death that happened, so she rewrites it into the death that should have happened.

> He had shot himself--in the breast.
>
> (014-191)

This revision is the moment the vine leaves fantasy collapses. Lovborg was supposed to die crowned, heroic, a beautiful spectacle. Instead he died in a brothel brawl, shot with a pistol he claimed was lost, over a purse that never existed. Every element of the imagined beautiful death — the temple-shot, the vine leaves, the heroic solitude — is travestied by the actual death.

Hedda's own suicide is her attempt to succeed where Lovborg failed. She orchestrates every detail for maximum aesthetic effect: the temple-shot, the piano playing the wild dance, the audience in the next room. "Done beautifully" — the cruel imperative she gave Lovborg — is now applied to herself. The vine leaves transfer, in death, from Lovborg to Hedda. She becomes the Dionysian figure he could not be. But the transfiguration costs her life, and it is witnessed only by people who cannot understand it. The beautiful death exists, but only in the moment of its enactment — and only for an audience of one.
