# Tesman's Childlike Dependence

[[George-Tesman|Tesman]] is, in emotional terms, still a child. Raised by [[Aunt-Julia|his aunts]] after his parents' death, he has never completed the transition to adult autonomy. His relationship with Aunt Julia is one of warm, guileless dependence — he greets her with "Aunt Julia! Dear Aunt Julia!" (003-062), delights in the slippers she brings him (004-079), and accepts her sacrifices with the unquestioning entitlement of a beloved child.

Aunt Julia mortgaged her annuity for his house (003-248), and Tesman accepts this without visible guilt. His warmth toward her is genuine, but it is the warmth of a child who has never been asked to reckon with the costs of his care. She has done the work of raising him; now she finances his marriage; her next role, already implicit, is to help raise his child.

> Security? You? My dear Aunt Julia--what sort of security can you give?
>
> (003-248)

Tesman moves from one maternal structure to another — from aunts to wife — without ever becoming the parent rather than the child. This arrested development is not presented as pathological. Ibsen treats Tesman's filial warmth with respect — his love for his aunts is the one uncomplicated emotional truth in the play. But it is a love that has never been tested by adulthood.

The slippers are the play's perfect emblem of this dynamic. They are infantile objects — soft, worn, associated with childhood and domestic comfort — and Tesman treats them with the reverence a child gives a transitional object. Hedda's revulsion at the slippers is revulsion at everything they represent: the extended childhood of a man who should be a husband, the domestic warmth she cannot feel, the family love that asks nothing of her and receives nothing in return.
