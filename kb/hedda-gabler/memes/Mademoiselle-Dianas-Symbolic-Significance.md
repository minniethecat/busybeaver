# Mademoiselle Diana's: Symbolic Significance

Mademoiselle Diana's functions as the play's symbolic underworld along several dimensions, each reinforcing its dramatic power.

**Sexuality**: Unlike the [[Tesman-Villa|Tesman villa]], where even pregnancy goes unmentioned, Diana's establishment acknowledges physical desire openly. It is the site of the "debauchery" that [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] simultaneously fears and envies — a world of bodily freedom that she can imagine but never inhabit. The red-haired Mademoiselle Diana herself echoes Hedda's own coloring, a symbolic doubling that suggests what Hedda might become outside the constraints of [[Respectability|respectability]].

**Class Transgression**: Lovborg's death among prostitutes and brawlers is the final humiliation for a man Hedda imagined dying as a tragic hero. The establishment degrades the "beautiful death" into a police matter. The boudoir replaces the battlefield; the brawl replaces the duel; the "lost purse" replaces the lost manuscript. Every heroic category is travestied.

**Information Asymmetry**: [[Judge-Brack|Brack]] knows the details because he was involved in the investigation. Hedda knows only what he chooses to tell her — and what he withholds gives him power. His threat to invoke "this same Mademoiselle Diana" (015-149) transforms the establishment into blackmail material.

**Public Exposure**: Brack's warning that the matter will come "before the court" (015-143) transforms private scandal into threatened public testimony. Diana's is not just shameful — it is evidence. The pistol can be traced; the testimony can be compelled; the offstage can colonize the stage.
