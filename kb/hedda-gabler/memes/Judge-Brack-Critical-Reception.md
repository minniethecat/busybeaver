# Judge Brack — Critical Reception

Brack has been interpreted as Ibsen's critique of the legal-bureaucratic class — a judge whose "correctness" (007-212) conceals a complete absence of moral substance. His function as the play's structural antagonist has drawn comparisons to the raisonneur figure in French well-made plays, though Ibsen subverts the type: Brack's clarity of perception serves domination, not enlightenment.

Scholars have noted that Brack's power is specifically gendered — he can threaten exposure because 19th-century society punishes female transgression while protecting male respectability. His "triangle" metaphor has been read as a critique of bourgeois marriage as a system of covert polygamy, where men maintain both public wives and private mistresses under the cover of "friendship."

Brack's closing line — "People don't do such things!" — has drawn particular critical attention. It is the play's moral punctuation, and what disturbs Brack most is not [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]]'s death but her violation of the rules, a move outside the game that exposes the hollowness of his worldview.
