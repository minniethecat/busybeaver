# Character Positions in the Respectability Economy

[[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] is simultaneously the character most obsessed with respectability and least able to live within its constraints. She is General Gabler's daughter -- a title she values more than "Mrs. Tesman." Her marriage, her house, her social calls: all are chosen for how they look.

> Hedda's profound coldness -- "the chill materialism of Hedda" (001-076) -- is not an absence of feeling but its redirection into the maintenance of appearances.

She married [[George-Tesman|Tesman]] because he was respectable; she despises him for being pedestrian. She fears scandal above all things; she lives in permanent resentment toward the society that judges her.

[[Judge-Brack]] plays the game to win. His "correctness and respectability" are beyond question (007-212), and this immunity makes him dangerous. His "triangle" metaphor (012-150) captures his view: respectability is managing appearances, knowledge, and people.

[[George-Tesman]] represents aspirational respectability -- the scholar who needs a professorship, a house, a wife who "looks the part," all funded with borrowed money to perform the role of respectable academic.

[[Mrs-Elvsted|Thea Elvsted]] is the exception. She left her husband, works alongside [[Eilert-Lovborg|Lovborg]], and weeps publicly. Her courage, as discussed in [[Courage-and-Cowardice]], is the courage to live outside respectability -- to value creation over appearance.
