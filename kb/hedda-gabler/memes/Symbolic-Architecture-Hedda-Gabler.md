# Symbolic Architecture in Hedda Gabler

The [[Tesman-Villa|Tesman villa]]'s spatial arrangement is Ibsen's most ambitious symbolic construction. The play's three spaces — drawing room, inner room, and the unseen domestic quarters — map three levels of existence, each operating within a realist surface that makes the symbolism nearly invisible.

**The Drawing Room** is the zone of public performance, social identity, and the theater of [[Respectability|respectability]]. Here [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] is Mrs. Tesman, the gracious hostess, the General's daughter making conversation with [[Judge-Brack|Brack]]. Every interaction follows the script of bourgeois manners; every gesture is watched.

**[[The-Inner-Room|The Inner Room]]** is the zone of private feeling, authentic expression, and retreat from performance. Here is the piano (General Gabler's), the [[The-Curtains|curtains]] Hedda manipulates, and ultimately Hedda's body after the shot. It is more honest than the drawing room but no less charged — the private is just as structured as the public.

**The Domestic Quarters** are the unseen space of [[Berta]]'s labor: the kitchen, the back rooms, the working-class infrastructure that makes the drawing room possible. These spaces are invisible, essential, and completely outside the symbolic economy governing the other two zones.

This spatial symbolism is so precisely integrated into the realist surface that it is easy to miss. The villa is a real house with real rooms — and it is also a diagram of the play's moral universe. The spatial hierarchy is the social hierarchy made architecture: the visible, the partially visible, and the invisible, each sustaining the others in a structure that no character fully sees.
