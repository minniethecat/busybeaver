# Sheriff Elvsted — Background and Marriage

Sheriff Elvsted is the district sheriff from the northern parish where [[Mrs-Elvsted|Thea]] lived before following [[Eilert-Lovborg|Lovborg]] to the city. He never appears on stage, but his marriage to Thea is the social structure from which she must escape.

Thea married Sheriff Elvsted as a young woman — her former name was Miss Rysing (004-254). The marriage was not a love match: Thea had been employed as governess to the Sheriff's children from his first marriage, and the union seems to have been one of convenience and domestic management rather than affection.

The Sheriff is "twenty years older" than Thea, and [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] remembers her former schoolmate as timid — "I used to pull her hair" (004-307). The image of Hedda as schoolyard bully and Thea as victim is one of the play's most efficient character-establishing moments.

It also sets up the play's central irony: the "timid" Thea proves capable of an act of courage — leaving her husband — that the "bold" Hedda cannot imagine. The Sheriff's distant, paternalistic relationship with Thea defines the domestic confinement that the play critiques. Like [[Mademoiselle-Dianas]] and [[Aunt-Rina]], the Sheriff exerts dramatic force through absence — his marriage is the cage Thea must leave, just as the [[Tesman-Villa]] is the cage Hedda cannot.
