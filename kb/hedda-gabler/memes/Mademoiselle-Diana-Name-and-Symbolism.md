# Mademoiselle Diana — Name and Symbolism

Her name — Diana, the virgin huntress — carries heavy irony across multiple registers.

**Classical**: The goddess Diana hunted with bow and arrow; Mademoiselle Diana hunts with a different kind of establishment. The virgin goddess becomes a madam — Ibsen's pointed subversion of romantic convention: the name promises myth, the reality delivers farce.

**The hunt**: [[Eilert-Lovborg|Lovborg]] is the prey — tracked through the city, cornered at Diana's, killed by a pistol meant for a beautiful suicide. The "hunt" for pleasure ends in death, and Diana's establishment is where the quarry is finally brought down.

**Female autonomy**: Diana the goddess was one of the few female deities who existed outside marriage and male control. Mademoiselle Diana similarly operates outside the domestic economy — she makes her living from pleasure, not from a husband. Her position is the most transgressive in the play's female economy, and also the most precarious.

Diana's position illuminates the play's three female economic models:

| Woman | Economic Basis | Freedom | Cost |
|-------|---------------|---------|------|
| **Mademoiselle Diana** | Pleasure (brothel) | Operates outside marriage | Legal and social precarity |
| **Thea Elvsted** | Leaves marriage; relies on Lovborg/Tesman | Partial (dependent on men) | Social disapproval |
| **Hedda Gabler** | Marriage to Tesman | None (trapped) | Loss of self; suicide |

Diana appears in the text only through [[Judge-Brack|Brack]]'s report, never on stage. She is spoken about but never speaks — a figure from the world beyond the drawing-room who enters the play only as rumor and consequence.
