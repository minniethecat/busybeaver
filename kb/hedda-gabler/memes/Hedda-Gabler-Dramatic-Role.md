# Hedda Gabler: Dramatic Role

Hedda is the centrifugal force of the play. Every major action orbits her will: she lures Lovborg to drink, burns his manuscript, gives him a pistol, and shoots herself when Brack threatens exposure.

| Function | Mechanism | Outcome |
|----------|-----------|---------|
| **Temptress** | Entices Lovborg to drink | His relapse and death |
| **Destroyer** | Burns the manuscript in [[The-Stove|the stove]] | Erases Lovborg and Thea's work |
| **Armorer** | Gives Lovborg a [[Heddas-Pistols|pistol]] | The instrument of his death |
| **Self-executioner** | Shoots herself when Brack closes the triangle | The play's final act |

Ibsen depicted "human beings, human emotions, and human destinies, upon a groundwork of certain of the social conditions and principles of the present day" (001-028). Hedda embodies the collision between aristocratic individualism and bourgeois domesticity.

> Ibsen intended that "Hedda, as a personality, is to be regarded rather as her father's daughter than her husband's wife" (001-025).

From the play's opening moments, Hedda is identified not as Tesman's wife but as "General Gabler's daughter" — a lineage that defines her before she speaks. (003-013) Her dramatic function is to stage the fatal collision between an aristocratic hunger for beauty and power and the suffocating conventions of bourgeois domesticity.
