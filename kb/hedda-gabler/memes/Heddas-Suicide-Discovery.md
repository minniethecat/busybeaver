# Hedda's Suicide: The Discovery

The discovery of [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]]'s body is one of the most precisely choreographed moments in Ibsen's dramatic corpus. Three characters react, and each reaction reveals everything about the character who makes it.

[[Judge-Brack|Brack]] is the first to understand. His body "half rises" (015-237) — the only physical description Ibsen gives of his reaction. He says nothing. He knows. The man who has spent the entire play mastering every situation through knowledge and control encounters the one act he could not predict and cannot process. His silence is not grief — it is the shock of a strategist who has been outmaneuvered by a corpse.

[[George-Tesman|Tesman]], characteristically, does not understand at first. He pulls aside [[The-Curtains|the curtains]] and sees Hedda's body. His cry — "Shot herself! Shot herself in the temple! Think of that!" (015-240) — is Tesman distilled: genuine shock expressed in the language of mild surprise. The repetition, the exclamation, the invitation to marvel — all of it reveals a man for whom even death is an academic curiosity. He cannot feel what has happened because he never understood the woman it happened to.

[[Mrs-Elvsted|Thea]]'s reaction is not recorded. She has already retreated into the work that will define her future — the manuscript reconstruction with Tesman. Hedda's death is the event that removes the obstacle to that work, and Thea processes it by not processing it. Her silence is the play's final comment on the feminine strategy of endurance: creation continues, regardless of destruction.
