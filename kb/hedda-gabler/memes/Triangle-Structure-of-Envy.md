# Triangle: The Structure of Envy

[[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]]'s envy of [[Mrs-Elvsted|Thea]] is the triangle's emotional engine, and it is crucial to understand that this is not romantic envy. Hedda does not want [[Eilert-Lovborg|Lovborg]] as a lover. She wants what Thea has done — and what she herself cannot do.

Thea has left a sterile marriage; Hedda married into one. Thea has built a creative partnership; Hedda can only manipulate. Thea has produced something of lasting value; Hedda can only consume and destroy. Thea has the "courage to live" (002-086) that Hedda famously admires but cannot practice. Each of Thea's achievements is a mirror reflecting Hedda's failures.

Hedda's cruelty toward Thea — extracting confidences under false friendship, then using those confidences to destroy Lovborg — is the rage of the spectator who cannot become a participant. She is excluded from creation not by external barriers but by internal incapacity, and that makes the exclusion unbearable. The triangle is not about who loves whom; it is about who creates, who supports creation, and who destroys what others have made.

The play's deepest argument emerges from this structure: creation requires courage — the courage to risk failure, to commit, to be seen trying. Destruction requires only the will to annihilate. Thea risks everything and creates; Hedda risks nothing and destroys; Lovborg risks everything but cannot sustain creation without the support Thea provides and Hedda withdraws. The asymmetry is moral as well as practical: creation is harder, braver, and more fragile than destruction. That is why Thea survives and Hedda does not.
