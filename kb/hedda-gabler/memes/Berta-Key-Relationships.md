# Berta — Key Relationships

Berta's relationships trace the fault lines of the Tesman household, revealing where loyalty, class, and power intersect.

**Aunt Julia** (former employer, moral anchor): Berta's transfer from Aunt Julia's household to [[George-Tesman|George]]'s represents a continuity of care. She is visibly more comfortable with Aunt Julia's old-fashioned warmth than with [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]]'s cold formality. The bond between servant and former mistress is the play's most stable relationship — built on years of mutual loyalty.

**Hedda Gabler** (mistress): Hedda's discomfort with Berta borders on class anxiety. "Not get on with Berta?" George asks (004-099), surprised — for him, Berta has always been part of the family. For Hedda, a servant who speaks familiarly is an intrusion into aristocratic distance.

**George Tesman** (master): George's relationship with Berta is uncomplicated affection. He calls her "dear Berta" (003-004) — the same endearment he uses for his aunt. For George, Berta is family; for Hedda, she is a problem without a solution.

**The Inner Room** (domain): Berta moves through [[The-Inner-Room]] more than any other character [009-035, 010-256, 013-139, 015-055], making her the play's most frequent occupant of Hedda's private sanctuary. The space Hedda jealously guards is, in practice, the servant's thoroughfare.

These relationships illuminate the play's class structure: Berta serves everyone, belongs to the old world, and is the uncomfortable reality that Hedda's aristocratic fantasies cannot accommodate.
