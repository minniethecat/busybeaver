# The Stove: The Burning

The burning of [[The-Manuscript|Lovborg's manuscript]] in the [[The-Stove|stove]] occurs between Acts III and IV, and it is the play's most symbolically charged act. [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] carries the manuscript into [[The-Inner-Room|the inner room]], takes out the pages, and feeds them one by one into the fire, whispering: "Now I am burning your child, Thea! You, with your curly hair!" (013-125).

The act is deliberate, sustained, and intimate. Hedda does not throw the whole manuscript at once — she burns it page by page, savoring the destruction. The word "child" reframes intellectual creation as biological reproduction, and the burning from vandalism to symbolic infanticide. She is destroying not just a text but the product of [[Eilert-Lovborg|Lovborg]] and [[Mrs-Elvsted|Thea]]'s creative union.

[[Berta]] later "makes up the fire" — an ordinary domestic act that unknowingly completes Hedda's work. The servant who tends the hearth becomes an accessory to destruction. Ibsen's juxtaposition of the ordinary and the monstrous is characteristic: genocide by housekeeping, annihilation disguised as household maintenance.

The stove does not discriminate. The same fire that warms the Tesmans' home consumes the manuscript. The same hearth that symbolizes domestic nurture becomes a pyre. This indifference is the stove's deepest meaning: destruction and warmth share the same source, the same room, the same servant's tending hand. The domestic hearth and the funeral pyre are, in Ibsen's staging, the same cast-iron object.
