# Hedda's Suicide

[[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]]'s suicide is the play's climax, its most analyzed moment, and the act that has defined the play's reception for over a century. It is not surrender — it is the play's most forceful assertion of will (015-239).

The [[Heddas-Suicide-Context|immediate context]] is the total collapse of her position: Brack knows about the pistol, Lovborg died ugly, the triangle is the only offered "way out." Every door has closed.

The [[Heddas-Suicide-The-Shot|shot itself]] is Ibsen's most economical stagecraft: wild piano dance, temple-shot behind curtains, every detail fulfilling the aesthetic code Lovborg betrayed (015-239). The [[Heddas-Suicide-Discovery|discovery]] reveals each character: Brack's silence, Tesman's banal shock, Thea's retreat into work.

The [[Heddas-Suicide-Interpretive-Traditions|interpretive traditions]] — feminist, existential, aesthetic, tragic — all have textual support, and the play refuses to settle among them.
