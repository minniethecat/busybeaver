# Bachelor Party Context

The bachelor party is arranged at the end of Act II, but its true starting point is [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]]'s manipulation of [[Eilert-Lovborg|Lovborg]] moments before.

Lovborg has just refused to drink punch with Hedda and [[George-Tesman|Tesman]], proudly asserting his reformed character. He has brought [[The-Manuscript|his manuscript]] — the visionary book he wrote under [[Mrs-Elvsted|Thea Elvsted]]'s influence — to read to Tesman. He tells Hedda he intends to return for the evening gathering: "I am booked for my bachelor's party this evening" (006-246).

> BRACK. But my dear fellow, you are booked for my bachelor's party this evening. You promised on the pier last night. HEDDA. Had you forgotten, Tesman?
>
> (006-246–006-248)

Hedda, jealous of Thea's influence and determined to test Lovborg's reformation, manipulates him into drinking by suggesting he is afraid. She asks whether "poor, helpless little Mrs. Elvsted has more courage than you" (002-085). The manipulation is exquisitely calibrated: she targets precisely the insecurity that Thea's influence had healed — Lovborg's need to be seen as bold, daring, unafraid.

Lovborg, stung by the accusation of cowardice, drinks the punch he had previously refused and departs for the party with [[Judge-Brack|Brack]] and Tesman. In this single moment, the reformed man is undone. The party itself — ostensibly a celebration of Tesman's professorship — is now loaded with the weight of Lovborg's compromised sobriety. Everything that follows — the drinking, the loss of the manuscript, the brothel visit, the death — traces back to Hedda's calculated provocation in the drawing-room.
