# Berta — Key Moments

Berta appears at four key moments, each deepening her role as the household's quiet witness and moral continuity.

| Act | Moment | Significance |
|-----|--------|-------------|
| I | Welcomes Aunt Julia | Berta understands the household's true loyalties (003-044) |
| II | Posting the letter | Berta is trusted with correspondence — Hedda relies on her (006-086) |
| III | Lighting the fire | Berta stokes the stove as Hedda burns Lovborg's manuscript (010-256) |
| IV | Tending the dying Rina | Berta's ultimate loyalty lies with Aunt Julia and the old household (014-024) |

In **Act I**, Berta's warm greeting of Aunt Julia establishes where the household's emotional center truly lies. The servant's welcome is warmer than anything [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] offers — a quiet judgment delivered through domestic routine.


> "So you've got a new mistress now, my dear Berta. Heaven knows it was a wrench to me to part with you."
>
> (002-189)

**Act II** complicates the dynamic: Hedda, who disdains Berta, must nevertheless trust her with correspondence. The servant who "didn't tell me" (007-299) is nonetheless the bearer of the household's secrets.

**Act III** is the most symbolically charged moment: Berta lights the fire in [[The-Stove|the stove]] as Hedda prepares to burn [[The-Manuscript|Lovborg's manuscript]]. The servant stokes the fire; the mistress feeds it pages. Berta's domestic labor makes Hedda's destruction possible — an uncomfortable collusion neither acknowledges.

**Act IV** reveals Berta's ultimate loyalty: tending the dying [[Aunt-Rina|Rina]] alongside [[Aunt-Julia]], she returns to the old household where her affections truly lie. The new household has failed to claim her, just as it has failed to claim Hedda.
