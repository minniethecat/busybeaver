# Respectability and the Play's Conclusion

The play ends with respectability intact but hollow. [[George-Tesman|Tesman]] and [[Mrs-Elvsted|Thea]] will reconstruct [[The-Manuscript|Lovborg's manuscript]] -- the respectability of intellectual labor, restored through cooperative effort. [[Judge-Brack|Brack]] retains his power over the household through the threat of exposure. [[Aunt-Julia]] tends the dying Rina, performing the respectable role of the self-sacrificing woman.

Only [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] has stepped outside the system entirely -- through the one door that cannot be closed. Her suicide is her final and most thorough rejection of [[Respectability|respectability]]. She dies not just beyond the code but in deliberate defiance of it, using the very [[Heddas-Pistols|pistols]] that were her father's legacy and her only claim to a world beyond the drawing-room.

Brack's final line -- "People don't do such things!" (015-242) -- is the voice of respectability itself, shocked not by death but by its manner. A respectable suicide might be acceptable (an overdose, a quiet drowning); a gunshot to the temple in the drawing-room is not. In her last act, [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] refuses even the decorum of death.
