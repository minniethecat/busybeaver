# Hedda's Suicide: Brack's Verdict

"People don't do such things" (015-242). [[Judge-Brack|Brack]]'s final line is the play's last word and its most famous. It is not grief. It is not shock. It is not even condemnation. It is the voice of bourgeois respectability confronted with something it cannot categorize — and the line's power lies precisely in what it refuses to do.

Brack has spent the entire play mastering every situation through knowledge and control. His strategy — cultivate access, extract confidences, gather intelligence, deploy leverage — has worked flawlessly against [[George-Tesman|Tesman]]'s obliviousness, [[Eilert-Lovborg|Lovborg]]'s fragility, and [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]]'s trapped desperation. By Act IV, every person in the villa is mapped onto Brack's information network, and every piece of knowledge is an asset.

Hedda's suicide is the one act he could not predict, could not prevent, and cannot understand. It defeats his strategy not by opposing it but by exceeding its terms. Brack's power operates within the social world — the world of scandal, reputation, and the triangle. Hedda's suicide removes her from that world entirely. You cannot blackmail a corpse.

The line is also the play's final irony. "People" — Brack's people, the people of the drawing room and the triangle — don't do such things. But Hedda was never Brack's people. She was [[General-Gablers-Shadow|General Gabler]]'s daughter, and she died as Gabler: armed, deliberate, and beyond Brack's comprehension. The verdict reveals the judge's limits.
