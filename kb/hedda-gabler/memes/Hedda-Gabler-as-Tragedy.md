# Hedda Gabler as Tragedy

Is *Hedda Gabler* a tragedy? The question has divided critics since 1891. The play has a noble protagonist, a fall, and a catastrophe — but lacks a traditional tragic flaw and, most critically, catharsis.

[[Classical-Template-Hedda-Gabler|The classical template]] fits uneasily: Hedda satisfies noble protagonist, peripeteia (Lovborg's ugly death), and anagnorisis ("in your power"). But her hamartia is structural — she was born a woman of intelligence in a world that has no use for her.

The [[Modern-Problem-Hedda-Gabler|modern problem]] is Ibsen's substitution of social forces for cosmic ones. Respectability replaces gods; gender hierarchy replaces fate. The forces are as implacable as divine will but entirely human — creating tension between tragic inevitability and political possibility.

[[Hedda-as-Tragic-Heroine|Hedda as tragic heroine]] is fitting but problematic: larger than her world, choosing her fate — but her cruelty tests the limits of the form.

The [[Missing-Catharsis-Hedda-Gabler|missing catharsis]] is the most significant departure. Brack's incomprehension, Tesman's banal shock, Thea's return to work — the bourgeois world cannot absorb tragedy.

> Good God!--people don't do such things!
>
> (015-242)

This double effect is Ibsen's deepest formal achievement.
