# The Pistol as Information Object

[[Heddas-Pistols]] are not just weapons — they are the play's most potent information objects, carrying data about ownership, provenance, and responsibility. A pistol, unlike poison or a fall, is traceable: it can be identified, its ownership established, its trajectory reconstructed. When [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] gives [[Eilert-Lovborg|Lovborg]] one of her father's pistols, she creates an information trail that [[Judge-Brack|Brack]] follows directly to her.

Brack traces the pistol from [[Mademoiselle-Dianas|Mademoiselle Diana's establishment]] (012-121) back to the Tesman household. The police report identifies the weapon; the weapon identifies its owner; the owner's motive becomes Brack's leverage. The pistol — her only possession connecting her to her father's world of military honor and masculine autonomy — becomes the instrument of her capture rather than her liberation.

Hedda gave Lovborg the pistol with an aesthetic intention: die beautifully. "Do it beautifully, Eilert Lovborg. Promise me that" (012-080). She was arming a man for a "beautiful death." Instead, she armed Brack with information. Lovborg died squalidly, and the pistol — the tangible link between Hedda and the death — became the evidence that placed her in Brack's power.

The pistol's dual nature — weapon and information, instrument of freedom and chain of evidence — embodies the play's central insight about how objects carry meaning beyond their intended function. Hedda thought she was giving Lovborg an exit; she was giving Brack a key.
