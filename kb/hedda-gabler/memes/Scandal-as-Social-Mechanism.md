# Scandal as Social Mechanism

Scandal is the invisible antagonist of *Hedda Gabler* — the social force that [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda Gabler]] fears more than death itself. Unlike [[Respectability]], which is the positive code of social standing (what one must appear to be), scandal is respectability's negative pole: the destruction of reputation through public exposure.

In the world of the play, scandal operates through a deceptively simple mechanism: private knowledge made public. What is known "between ourselves" is safe; what becomes known to "people" is dangerous. [[Judge-Brack|Brack]]'s power over Hedda derives entirely from this distinction. His threat — "Henceforth, as before, every respectable house will be open to you — to both of you — so long as things are kept — between ourselves" (012-150) — is the play's definitive statement on how scandal functions.

The ellipsis before "between ourselves" is Brack's entire strategy condensed into punctuation: the pause, the implication, the unsaid threat. He does not say "or I'll expose you." He does not need to. The fear of scandal does the work for him.

Every major action in the play is shadowed by this mechanism. Hedda's marriage to Tesman was a retreat from the scandal-risk of Lovborg. Her manipulations are conducted from behind [[The-Curtains|the curtain]], never in the open. Her terror is not of punishment but of being *seen* — of the gap between her performed respectability and her actual desires becoming visible to the world that judges her.

The mechanism is gendered: women are exponentially more vulnerable than men. Brack is scandal-proof; Hedda is scandal-trapped. The double standard is the architecture of the entire system.
