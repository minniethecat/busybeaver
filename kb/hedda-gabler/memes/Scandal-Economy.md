# Scandal Economy

The play constructs a precise economy of scandal in which different characters occupy different positions based on their vulnerability to exposure and their capacity to wield exposure against others.

[[Judge-Brack|Brack]] sits at the top: immune. His "correctness and respectability are beyond all question" (007-212), making him scandal-proof. This immunity is the platform from which he controls everyone else — he can wield scandal against others because scandal cannot touch him. His weapon is knowledge: he controls what becomes public.

[[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] occupies the most vulnerable position. Her entire social identity depends on respectability, yet she is the character with the most to hide. She has no weapon — only reaction, never preemption. The one time she tries to exercise power (giving Lovborg the pistol), the act immediately becomes evidence against her.

[[Eilert-Lovborg|Lovborg]] represents a different relationship entirely: he has already been through scandal. His return as a reformed man is an attempt to rebuild, but one night undoes it. His already-compromised status makes him indifferent in a way Hedda can never be — but his death in [[Mademoiselle-Dianas]] proves that past scandal can be revived with devastating force.

[[George-Tesman|Tesman]] is protected by obliviousness. He cannot imagine scandal, and this ignorance shields him. [[Berta]] the servant is simply outside the system — the working class has no reputation to lose, and scandal is a weapon of the middle class, wielded against the middle class.

The economy is rigidly hierarchical, gendered, and class-bound. It is the architecture of social control.
