# The Morning-After Scene

Act III opens on the morning after the bachelor party at Brack's lodgings. The scene functions as the play's information hub — characters arrive, report, and depart, each carrying fragments of knowledge that will be assembled into catastrophe.

## The Gathering of Information

The scene is structured as a series of arrivals, each bringing new intelligence: [[Mrs-Elvsted|Thea]] arrives anxious about Lovborg; Tesman arrives with the found manuscript; Brack arrives with news of the party. The audience, like the characters, pieces together the night's events from fragmentary reports. No single character knows everything; only the audience achieves full knowledge.

## The Manuscript

Tesman's discovery of the manuscript is the scene's pivotal event. He picks it up, recognizes its value, and immediately decides to return it to Lovborg — an act of academic integrity that goes entirely unrewarded. The manuscript then passes through multiple hands: Tesman gives it to Hedda for safekeeping; Hedda hides it; Lovborg lies about losing it; Hedda burns it. The object's trajectory maps the play's moral economy.

## The Decision Chain

The morning-after scene is a study in cascading decisions. Hedda conceals the manuscript from Lovborg, choosing manipulation over honesty. Lovborg lies about its loss, choosing pride over truth. Thea leaves Lovborg, choosing self-preservation over loyalty. Each decision is individually defensible; collectively they produce catastrophe. The scene demonstrates Ibsen's mastery of dramatic causation — no single villain, only a chain of plausible choices leading to destruction.
