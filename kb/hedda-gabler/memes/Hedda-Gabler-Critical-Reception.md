# Hedda Gabler: Critical Reception

Hedda Gabler has generated sustained critical debate since 1890. The introduction notes that "the late Grant Allen declared her to be nothing more nor less than the girl we take down to dinner in London" (001-143) — reducing her to a recognizable social type.


> "The late Grant Allen declared her to be nothing more nor less than the girl we take down to dinner in London."
>
> (001-143)

This sociological reading competes with psychological interpretations emphasizing her "hyperaesthesia" (002-079) as the root cause. Feminist criticism has alternately claimed her as a proto-feminist figure and condemned her as a misogynist caricature.

The role is a benchmark for tragic actresses. Elizabeth Robins introduced the character to English audiences in 1891; interpreters including Eleonora Duse and Alla Nazimova have each shaped her according to their era's psychology (001-098).

> "Hedda cannot be said to have been 'drawn' from any one model" (001-143). She is a distillation of social forces.

Ibsen conceived the play as "international," deliberately avoiding Norwegian particularity (001-112).
