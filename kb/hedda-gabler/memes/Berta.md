# Berta

The Tesman family's long-serving maid, inherited from [[Aunt-Julia]]'s household along with the family's domestic traditions. Berta opens the play, admitting Aunt Julia into the Tesman villa on the morning after George and Hedda's return from their honeymoon — her first lines establish the domestic world that Hedda will spend the rest of the play rebelling against.

Berta's presence throughout the play provides a constant witness to the domestic drama, and her loyalties remain firmly with the Tesman family's old order. She is the human embodiment of the domestic sphere Hedda despises: dutiful, discreet, and utterly conventional. Her character anchors the play in everyday reality even as the central characters spiral toward tragedy.

## From the Text

> MISS TESMAN. So you've got a new mistress now, my dear Berta. Heaven knows
> it was a wrench to me to part with you.
>
> BERTA. [On the point of weeping.] And do you think it wasn't hard for me,
> too, Miss? After all the blessed years I've been with you and Miss Rina.
>
> MISS TESMAN. We must make the best of it, Berta. There was nothing else to
> be done. George can't do without you, you see — he absolutely can't.
>
> (002-189 to 002-195)
