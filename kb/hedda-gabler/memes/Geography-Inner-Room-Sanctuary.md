# Geography: The Inner Room as Sanctuary

[[The-Inner-Room]] is the drawing-room's shadow — the private space where public performances are suspended and secrets are enacted. If the [[The-Geography-of-Entrapment|drawing-room]] is the theater of appearances, the inner room is the sanctuary where those appearances dissolve. It functions as the play's unconscious: the space where repressed content surfaces, where the rules of bourgeois decorum no longer apply.

Three of the play's most significant acts occur in the inner room. First, [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] burns [[The-Manuscript|Lovborg's manuscript]] here, feeding pages one by one into [[The-Stove|the stove]] — "Now I'm burning your child, Thea!" (013-126). The act is too intimate, too destructive, for the public drawing-room. Second, she plays the wild piano dance in Act V — a chaotic music that expresses what she cannot say in words (015-229). The inner room is the only space where honesty is possible.

Third, and most consequential, Hedda retreats behind [[The-Curtains|the curtains]] of the inner room to shoot herself. The inner room becomes a tomb. The sanctuary of secrets becomes the site of the play's ultimate secret: a self-inflicted death that neither Brack, nor Tesman, nor Thea can comprehend.

The inner room's relationship to the drawing-room structures the play's rhythm: performance in public, chaos in private, return to performance. Hedda's tragedy is that the private chaos eventually cannot be contained — it leaks through the glass door, through the curtains, into the drawing-room where Brack waits with his knowing smile.
