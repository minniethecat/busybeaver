# The Hair Motif

Hair is one of *Hedda Gabler*'s most persistent and peculiar motifs. Thea's abundant hair, Hedda's violent response to it, and the childhood memory of hair-pulling create a network of associations linking hair to fertility, sexuality, and cruelty.

## Thea's Hair

Thea is repeatedly described in terms of her hair — abundant, "irritating," insubordinate (009-050). For Hedda, Thea's hair is an object of visceral disgust. It represents the fertility and physicality that Hedda rejects: hair grows, proliferates, exceeds control. Thea's hair is the body's refusal of discipline, and Hedda, who disciplines everything, cannot bear it.

## The Childhood Memory

Hedda's memory of pulling Thea's hair in school (009-055) retroactively explains the adult fixation. "I was going to burn it off," she recalls — the verb "burn" anticipating the manuscript's destruction. The childhood cruelty established a template: Thea as victim, Hedda as aggressor, destruction as the expression of intolerable feeling.

## Hair and Sexuality

In the symbolic vocabulary of 19th-century literature, abundant hair signifies female sexuality. Thea's hair marks her as fertile, desirable, vital — everything Hedda refuses to be. Hedda's violent response to Thea's hair is a response to everything Thea represents. The hair-pulling is displaced sexual aggression.

## The Burning Echo

When Hedda burns the manuscript — "I am burning your child, Thea!" (009-067) — she is symbolically burning what she once threatened to burn literally: Thea's hair. The fire that destroys the manuscript is the same fire she imagined for Thea's hair. The motif links childhood cruelty, adult destruction, and the play's central opposition between fertility and sterility.
