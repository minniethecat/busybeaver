# Thea and Hedda — The Contrast

The contrast between [[Mrs-Elvsted|Thea Elvsted]] and [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda Gabler]] is the play's central moral axis. Every quality Hedda possesses, Thea inverts; every action Hedda takes, Thea's counterpoints.

## Creation vs. Destruction

Thea creates; Hedda destroys. Thea co-authors a manuscript with Lovborg; Hedda burns it page by page. Thea reconstructs the manuscript with Tesman after Lovborg's death; Hedda shoots herself. The structural opposition is absolute: Thea is associated with fertility, collaboration, and survival; Hedda with sterility, isolation, and death.

## Courage vs. Cowardice

The play's most explicit moral judgment comes in the introduction: "Where Thea Elvsted dared to venture all, Hedda, in her cowardice, only hankered and refrained" (002-086). Thea "ventured all" — left her husband, pursued Lovborg, risked social ruin. Hedda "hankered and refrained" — wanted intensity but feared scandal, desired freedom but married Tesman. Thea's courage is practical and unglamorous; Hedda's is theatrical and ultimately self-destructive.

## Fertility vs. Sterility

The manuscript-as-child metaphor (008-166) positions Thea as the play's generative figure and Hedda as its destructive one. Thea's "child" is the manuscript; Hedda's pregnancy (if real) is unwanted and unacknowledged. Thea creates; Hedda burns; Thea rebuilds. The play's biological subtext aligns Thea with life and Hedda with death.

## The Moral Victor

In the play's moral economy, Thea wins. She survives the play's catastrophes and begins again — with Tesman, of all people. Her resilience is the quiet triumph the play offers its audience. Hedda's suicide is spectacular but terminal; Thea's persistence is unglamorous but enduring.
