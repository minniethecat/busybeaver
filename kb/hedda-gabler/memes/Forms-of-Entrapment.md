# The Forms of Entrapment

[[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]]'s entrapment is not a single cage but a series of concentric ones, each reinforcing the others. The six forms are distinct but interlocking, and together they create a structure from which exit is structurally impossible.

Gender is the most fundamental constraint. A woman of Hedda's class in 1890s Christiania has no profession, no independent income, no social existence outside marriage. Her intelligence and courage have no legitimate outlet. Marriage compounds this: she married [[George-Tesman|Tesman]] for security, but the security is its own cage. His interests bore her; his values repel her; his devotion suffocates her.

Pregnancy — never explicitly named but continuously invoked through Hedda's physical revulsion — is the ultimate biological entrapment. A child will bind her permanently to Tesman, to the villa, to the domestic world she despises. The [[Tesman-Villa]] itself is simultaneously a symbol of status and a physical prison, its geography mapping Hedda's psychological state.

[[Social-Class-and-Status|Class]] traps her by the same code that defines her: General Gabler's daughter cannot work, cannot leave her husband, cannot openly rebel. And [[Scandal]] — [[Judge-Brack|Brack]]'s knowledge of the [[Heddas-Pistols|pistol]] — converts all these structural constraints into one absolute, inescapable cage: the threat of public exposure.

Each form of entrapment is structural, not personal; each depends on the others; and together they create a world where the only exit is the one Hedda takes.
