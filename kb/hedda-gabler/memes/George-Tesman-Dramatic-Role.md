# George Tesman — Dramatic Role

Tesman serves as the play's moral and intellectual baseline. He is neither villain nor hero — merely a decent, limited man whose inability to understand his wife precipitates disaster. His scholarly obsession with "the domestic industries of Brabant during the Middle Ages" (004-002) marks him as a conscientious drudge, the antithesis of [[Eilert-Lovborg|Lovborg]]'s bold synthesis.

His dramatic function is threefold:

1. **The domestic cage**: He represents the bourgeois marriage [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] chose and now despises. The [[Tesman-Villa|Falk Villa]] he bought on credit — because she once admired it — is both his love letter and her prison.

2. **The unwitting catalyst**: When he recovers [[The-Manuscript|Lovborg's manuscript]] from the street, he hands Hedda the instrument of destruction. His honesty (returning lost property) enables her crime.

3. **The survivor**: After Hedda's death and Lovborg's death, Tesman sits with [[Mrs-Elvsted|Thea]] reconstructing the manuscript. His plodding diligence — mocked throughout — becomes the play's only constructive force.

> Tesman's scholarly self-description — "collecting and arranging — no one can beat you at that" (004-010) — is both accurate and damning. He is a compiler, not a creator.
