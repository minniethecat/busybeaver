# Mademoiselle Diana's as Dramatic Space

[[Mademoiselle-Diana|Mademoiselle Diana]]'s establishment is the play's dark counter-space to the Tesman villa drawing room. Where the drawing room is bourgeois, respectable, and controlled, Diana's is louche, violent, and beyond the reach of social convention.

## The Counter-Space

The establishment functions as a spatial antithesis to the Tesman villa. The drawing room is defined by its rituals (morning visits, afternoon calls); Diana's is defined by their absence. The drawing room is female-dominated (Hedda, Thea, Julia); Diana's is exclusively male. The drawing room is domestic; Diana's is commercial. The spatial opposition maps the play's moral geography.

## Site of Violence

Diana's is where violence erupts into the play's world. Lovborg's confrontation with the "red-haired woman" (012-040), his pistol wound to the stomach, his squalid death — all occur in or near Diana's establishment. The violence is not stylized or aesthetic but messy, physical, and degrading.

## The Structure of Degradation

The movement from the bachelor party at Brack's lodgings to the brawl at Diana's traces a structure of progressive degradation. Each step away from the Tesman drawing room is a step down the social ladder — from respectable entertaining to louche carousing to physical violence. Lovborg's trajectory maps this descent with terrible precision.

## Hedda's Horror

Hedda's response to learning the details of Lovborg's death is visceral disgust (012-050). It is not grief but aesthetic revulsion: this is not how a beautiful death looks. Diana's establishment, as a space, is the refutation of everything Hedda's suicide is designed to achieve. The temple-shot is her answer to the stomach wound at Diana's.
