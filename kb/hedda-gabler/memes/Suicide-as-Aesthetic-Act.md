# Suicide as Aesthetic Act

Hedda's suicide is not an act of despair but of aesthetic completion. She orchestrates every detail — the temple-shot, the piano playing, the audience gathered in the next room — as a director stages a finale.

## The Performance

When Hedda retreats to the inner room for the final time, she is not fleeing but staging. She plays a "wild dance" on the piano (015-230), a musical farewell that only [[Judge-Brack|Brack]] understands too late. The shot to the temple — "in the temple" Brack specifies (015-242) — is her final rebuke to the squalor of [[Eilert-Lovborg|Lovborg]]'s death in a brothel. Where he died messily, she will die beautifully.

## The Audience

Hedda's suicide is designed to be witnessed — or rather, overheard. She knows the gentlemen are in the next room. She knows Brack will recognize the pistol. She knows Tesman will be horrified. Every element is calculated for maximum theatrical effect. Even in death, she refuses to cede directorial control.

## Aesthetic Victory or Moral Defeat?

Critics have long debated whether Hedda's suicide represents triumph or failure. On one reading, it is her only authentic act — the one choice she makes entirely for herself, uncoerced by Brack's blackmail or Tesman's expectations. On another, it is the ultimate cowardice: a woman who could not face scandal, could not face freedom, could not face life itself.

The play's final line — "People don't do such things!" (015-245) — captures this ambiguity. Judge Brack, who thought he understood Hedda completely, is stunned. The suicide exceeds his categories. It is both the most controlled and the most uncontrollable act in the play.
