# Judge Brack — Key Relationships

## Key Passages from the Text

> BRACK.
>
> [Bending a little forward.] Come, let us have a cosy little chat, Mrs.
> Hedda.
>
> HEDDA.
>
> [Leaning further back in the sofa.] Does it not seem like a whole
> eternity since our last talk?
>
> BRACK.
>
> You mean since our last confidential talk? Our last _tete-a-tete_?
>
> (007-117–007-124)

## Hedda Gabler (object and rival)

Brack is attracted to [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]]'s intelligence and bored by her marriage. He courts her through shared contempt for provincial life and the promise of "entertainment." Their first on-stage exchange, in which Hedda fires a pistol at him (007-047), establishes the dynamic immediately: threat and immunity, provocation and unflappability. Later, Hedda's confession — "how mortally bored I have been" (007-147) — reveals to Brack the vulnerability he will exploit.

When Hedda realizes Brack knows she gave [[Eilert-Lovborg|Lovborg]] the [[Heddas-Pistols|pistol]], her autonomy collapses: "I am in your power, Judge. You have me at your beck and call, from this time forward" (015-161). This is the play's climactic reversal — the predator becomes prey.

## George Tesman (useful tool)

Brack cultivates [[George-Tesman|Tesman]]'s friendship as cover for his visits to the villa. Tesman never suspects — his naivety is Brack's most reliable resource. Tesman calls him "my dearest fellow"; Brack drinks his punch, uses his drawing room, and pursues his wife.

## Eilert Lovborg (obstacle)

[[Eilert-Lovborg|Lovborg]] is, briefly, Brack's rival for Hedda's attention. Brack attends the [[The-Bachelor-Party|bachelor party]], drinks alongside him, and later withholds information about Lovborg's death to strengthen his hold over Hedda. The efficiency is chilling.
