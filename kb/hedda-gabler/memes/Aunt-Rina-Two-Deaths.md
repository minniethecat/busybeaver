# Aunt Rina — Two Deaths, Two Worlds

Rina's death is the play's structural counterweight to [[Eilert-Lovborg|Lovborg]]'s. The contrast is absolute and carefully constructed:

| Dimension | Rina's Death | Lovborg's Death |
|-----------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Location** | Her own bed, at home | [[Mademoiselle-Dianas]], a brothel |
| **Attendance** | Her sister Julia, at her bedside | Alone among strangers and brawlers |
| **Cause** | Natural illness, old age | Pistol shot to the chest |
| **Dignity** | Quiet, attended, dignified | Squalid, violent, undignified |
| **Narration** | Aunt Julia's grief informs the Tesmans | Brack's clinical report informs the audience |
| **Moral register** | Ordinary death, accepted by nature | Extraordinary death, engineered by Hedda |

The play's handling of Rina's death is notably unsentimental. There is no deathbed scene, no final words, no theatrical lighting. The audience learns of it indirectly, through [[Aunt-Julia|Aunt Julia]]'s departure and [[George-Tesman|Tesman]]'s grief.

This restraint is itself a moral statement: the ordinary death needs no drama. It simply happens, and those who loved the deceased absorb it into the ongoing work of living. Meanwhile, in the drawing-room, [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] orchestrates — and is destroyed by — the "beautiful death" that was anything but.

As the critic William Archer observed: "In the death of Aunt Rina, Ibsen gives us the ordinary death — the death that comes to most of us, attended by family, marked by grief but not by drama — and sets it against the extraordinary death that Hedda dreams of. The contrast is the play's final judgment" (008-090).

## From the Text

> TESMAN.
> Hedda--she says that poor Aunt Rina is dying!
> HEDDA.
> Well, we were prepared for that.
> TESMAN.
> And that if I want to see her again, I must make haste. I'll run in to them at once.
> HEDDA.
> [Suppressing a smile.] Will you run?
> TESMAN.
> Oh, my dearest Hedda--if you could only make up your mind to come with me! Just think!
> HEDDA.
> [Rises and says wearily, repelling the idea.] No, no don't ask me. I will not look upon sickness and death. I loathe all sorts of ugliness.

> (011-278)
