# Aunt Julia — Key Scenes

Aunt Julia appears at four pivotal moments, each deepening her role as the play's moral anchor.

| Act | Scene | Significance |
|-----|-------|-------------|
| I | Morning visit | Aunt Julia brings George his old slippers; Hedda insults her bonnet (003-062) |
| II | Flowers from Aunt Julia | Hedda's dismissal of the bouquet exposes her cruelty (004-245) |
| III | Mention of borrowed money | Tesman reveals Aunt Julia mortgaged her annuity for their house (006-290) |
| IV | Rina's death | Aunt Julia's grief at Rina's deathbed — Tesman's "only comfort" now rests on him (013-191) |

In **Act I**, the slippers scene is a masterclass in character establishment. The slippers represent everything Hedda despises — domestic comfort, family sentiment, the past she married Tesman to escape. Aunt Julia's gift and Hedda's rejection define the emotional battlefield of the entire play in a single exchange.


> "But Hedda--that's Aunt Julia's bonnet."
>
> (004-110)

**Act II** deepens the cruelty: flowers sent specifically for Hedda are dismissed without a glance. Aunt Julia's gesture of welcome is met with aristocratic indifference.

**Act III** reveals the material cost of Aunt Julia's love: her annuity, her financial future, has been wagered on George's marriage. The money Hedda spends so carelessly was sacrificed by a woman who had nothing else to give.

**Act IV** completes the arc: while Hedda engineers destruction, Aunt Julia attends death. "I must go to her. She needs me" (013-191) — the simplest line in the play carries its heaviest moral weight.
