# Geography: The Glass Door as Boundary

[[The-Glass-Door]] is the villa's most symbolically charged architectural feature. It is transparent — the outside world can see in — but it is also a door — one can pass through. It represents the boundary between private and public, domestic and social, enclosed and exposed. Every character's relationship to the glass door defines their relationship to the villa's enclosure.

[[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] complains about it being open (002-183) — she wants the boundary sealed, the outside world excluded. She craves a space she can control absolutely, and the glass door's permeability threatens that fantasy of control. In contrast, [[George-Tesman|Tesman]] opens it eagerly to greet visitors — he welcomes the permeability, the social connection, the world streaming in through the glass.

[[Judge-Brack|Brack]] uses the glass door strategically, entering through the garden as a "back way" (006-096). He moves freely across boundaries that constrain others — the door is not a barrier to him but an access point. And [[Eilert-Lovborg|Lovborg]] exits through it after his relapse — the permeable boundary becomes an exit to destruction, to [[Mademoiselle-Dianas]], to the ugly death Hedda cannot aestheticize.

The glass door is the architectural embodiment of the play's central tension: the desire for enclosure versus the pressure of the outside world. The villa cannot be sealed; the glass door ensures that visibility and access are permanent conditions of life within.
