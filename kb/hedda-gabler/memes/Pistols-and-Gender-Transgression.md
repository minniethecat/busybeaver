# Pistols and Gender Transgression

[[Heddas-Pistols|Hedda's pistols]] are the play's central symbol of gender transgression — objects that belong to the masculine world and that Hedda's use of crosses a boundary her society enforces absolutely.

The pistols belong to [[General-Gablers-Shadow|General Gabler]] — to the masculine world of military honor, direct violence, and aristocratic autonomy. They are not decorative; they are functional weapons, and their presence in the [[Tesman-Villa|villa]]'s drawing room is an intrusion of the masculine-military into the feminine-domestic. When Hedda handles them, she crosses a gender line.

Her target practice — "I am bored, that is all" (008-193) — is a woman playing with masculine power, testing its weight, learning its mechanisms. The boredom is significant: the pistols are the only objects in her world that offer the intensity her aesthetic code demands. Everything else — the villa, the marriage, the pregnancy — is tedium. The pistols are danger.

The ultimate transgression is the [[Heddas-Suicide|suicide]]. Hedda dies by the pistol, as a man would — the temple-shot, clean and intentional — rather than by poison or drowning, the conventional female suicides of nineteenth-century drama. She dies as Gabler, not as Tesman; as her father's daughter, not as her husband's wife. The pistol that was her father's legacy becomes her own instrument.

The gender transgression is complete: a woman uses a man's weapon to perform a man's death, and Brack's "People don't do such things" (015-242) registers the categorical violation. Women don't shoot themselves in the temple. But Hedda does.

> BRACK. [Half-fainting in the arm-chair.] Good God! — people don't do such things.
>
> (015-242)
