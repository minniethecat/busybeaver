# Berta — Character Traits

Berta's character is defined by a set of qualities that distinguish her from every other figure in the play.

**Loyalty**: Berta has served the Tesman family for years and maintains her allegiance to [[Aunt-Julia]]. When the household shifts to the [[Tesman-Villa]], she adapts without complaint, but her true loyalty remains with the old household. She is visibly more comfortable with Aunt Julia's warmth than with [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]]'s cold formality.

**Discretion**: She overhears much and says little. Her famous line "Berta didn't tell me" (007-299) when asked about visitors reveals a finely honed instinct for self-protection. In a household of secrets and manipulations, Berta survives by knowing what not to know.

**Practical competence**: While the Tesmans fret about money and Hedda dreams of liveried footmen, Berta keeps the house running — fires lit, punch served, visitors announced. Her competence is the invisible foundation on which the drawing-room drama rests.

**Moral simplicity**: Unlike every other character, Berta has no hidden agenda. She serves; she observes; she endures. Her wants are straightforward (to serve a kind mistress, to maintain the household she knows), and her actions are transparent.

Ibsen listed Berta among the "subordinate characters" whose distinctness contributes to the play's texture (002-189). That distinctness is precisely her simplicity — in a play of hidden motives and unspoken histories, Berta is exactly what she appears to be.
