# Sheriff Elvsted — Two Marriages, Two Cages

The comparison between the Sheriff's marriage and Tesman's is the play's most compressed argument about marriage as institution.

| Dimension | Sheriff Elvsted + Thea | George Tesman + Hedda |
|-----------|----------------------|----------------------|
| **Age gap** | 20+ years | Minimal |
| **Basis** | Convenience (governess becomes wife) | Misunderstanding (casual remark becomes mortgage) |
| **Husband's awareness** | Absent, neglectful, unaware of Thea's inner life | Present, loving, unaware of Hedda's inner life |
| **Wife's response** | Leaves | Shoots herself |
| **Outcome** | Freedom + creative reconstruction | Death as aesthetic escape |

Two fundamentally similar situations — a wife trapped with a husband who doesn't see her — produce radically different outcomes. The difference is not in the marriages but in the women: Thea has the [[Courage-and-Cowardice|courage]] to leave; Hedda has only the courage to die.

The Sheriff's age and distance make his marriage obviously oppressive; Tesman's youth and affection make his seem benign. But the structural similarity — a wife whose inner life is invisible to her husband — is identical. Ibsen's point is that the quality of the cage (neglectful vs. affectionate) matters less than the prisoner's capacity to escape.

> MRS. ELVSTED is a woman of fragile figure, with pretty, soft features. Her eyes are light blue, large, round, and somewhat prominent, with a startled, inquiring expression.
>
> (004-279)

Thea walks out of one cage into creative life; Hedda walks out of the other into death. The Sheriff's marriage is the play's control case — the experiment that proves the variable is not the marriage but the woman trapped inside it.
