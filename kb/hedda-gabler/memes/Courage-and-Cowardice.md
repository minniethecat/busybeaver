# Courage and Cowardice

The opposition between courage and cowardice runs through *Hedda Gabler* like a fracture line — every major character is measured against it, and no one passes unambiguously.

[[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]], who plays with [[Heddas-Pistols|pistols]] and dreams of "beautiful" deaths, is revealed as a moral coward trapped by fear of scandal. [[Eilert-Lovborg|Lovborg]], who reforms his life through Thea's influence and writes a visionary book, dies in a brawl.

[[Mrs-Elvsted|Thea Elvsted]], who seems timid, exhibits the courage to leave her husband and rebuild Lovborg's destroyed [[The-Manuscript|manuscript]]. [[George-Tesman|Tesman]], who appears weak, shows the steady courage of patient scholarship.

Ibsen's introduction frames this theme directly: "What sort of man is Tesman? Is he a man? Is Hedda a real woman, or is she a 'coward' — a 'coward' who escapes from life because she cannot face its bourgeois reality?" (001-025).

The question is not rhetorical — the play leaves it deliberately unresolved, inviting the audience to interrogate what courage truly means beneath the performance of [[Respectability]].


> "...another woman has had the courage to love and venture all, where she, in her cowardice, only hankered and refrained."
>
> (002-085–002-086)

## Hedda: Cowardice Masked as Audacity

[[Hedda-Gabler]] performs courage with remarkable skill. She handles [[Heddas-Pistols|pistols]] "with the ease of a general's daughter" (007-050). She burns [[The-Manuscript|Lovborg's manuscript]] (011-101). She mocks [[George-Tesman|Tesman]]'s domesticity and [[Judge-Brack|Brack]]'s bourgeois propriety.

Every gesture of her body reads defiance.

Yet the play systematically exposes this performance as a mask for profound cowardice:

- **Fear of scandal**: "What will people say?" is, as [[Judge-Brack]] observes, Hedda's true master. She is "terrified of a scandal" (012-150) — the fear that controls her every significant choice.
- **Marriage as retreat**: She married [[George-Tesman|Tesman]] not from love but because he was available, safe, and offered no emotional risk. He was "the only one offering" (004-189).
- **Inability to love**: She envies [[Mrs-Elvsted|Thea Elvsted]]'s "courage to love and to live for another" (002-085). Hedda cannot bring herself to do either.
- **Suicide as escape**: Her final act — shooting herself in the temple — is presented not as courage but as the ultimate evasion. Brack's final line — "People don't do such things!" — underscores that Hedda's suicide is not heroic but profoundly antisocial.

Ibsen scholar Francis Bull noted that Hedda "combines a keenly critical, relentlessly solvent intelligence with a morbid shrinking from all the gross and prosaic detail of the sensual life" (002-079). Her intelligence is real; her courage is not.

## Lovborg: The Ambiguity of Romantic Courage

[[Eilert-Lovborg]] represents a complex case. He has reformed from alcoholism under [[Mrs-Elvsted|Thea Elvsted]]'s influence and written a groundbreaking book. [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] imagines him dying "beautifully" with vine-leaves in his hair — a romantic death worthy of a Greek hero.

But Lovborg's death is anything but beautiful. He dies in a brothel brawl, shot not in the chest (as Hedda hoped) but "in the bowels" (012-190) — a grotesque, undignified end.

His courage was real in his intellectual work ("The courage to live his life after his own fashion" (014-325)), but it did not extend to resisting the temptations Hedda deliberately set before him.

Lovborg raises the question the play cannot answer: is there a courage that can survive the world without [[Mrs-Elvsted|Thea Elvsted]]'s sustaining faith?

The play suggests not — without her, Lovborg unravels in a single afternoon, his [[The-Manuscript|manuscript]] destroyed, his death squalid rather than beautiful.

## Thea Elvsted: Quiet Courage

[[Mrs-Elvsted|Thea Elvsted]] is the play's most consistent embodiment of true courage — courage that is invisible to [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] because it looks nothing like pistol-fire or romantic death:

- She left her husband, Sheriff Elvsted, to follow [[Eilert-Lovborg|Lovborg]] to the city — an act of remarkable social courage for a woman of her time, defying the code of [[Respectability]].
- She defied Hedda's bullying as a schoolgirl and has built a life of genuine purpose.
- After Lovborg's death, she does not collapse — she sits with [[George-Tesman|Tesman]] and begins reconstructing Lovborg's [[The-Manuscript|manuscript]] from her notes, the "child" they created together.

Thea's courage, as the play presents it, is the courage to create where Hedda can only destroy, to nurture where Hedda can only manipulate.

As the introduction notes, "another woman has had the courage to love and to live for another" (002-085) — this is Thea's quiet triumph over Hedda's noisy despair.

## George Tesman: Dutiful Perseverance

[[George-Tesman|Tesman]] is the character least associated with courage, yet Ibsen complicates this.

Tesman's scholarship may be pedestrian — "trying to grub up his little atoms of knowledge" (002-058) — but his willingness to sit with [[Mrs-Elvsted|Thea]] and painstakingly reconstruct [[The-Manuscript|Lovborg's manuscript]] is itself a form of intellectual courage:

the courage to do humble work honestly.

His question — "What sort of man is Tesman? Is he a man?" — (001-025) is answered not in grand gestures but in the steady, unglamorous labor of reconstruction that closes the play.

## Judge Brack: The Courage to Dominate

[[Judge-Brack]] represents a perversion of courage: the courage of cynicism. He is "a man of the world" (007-212) — fearless in his manipulations, secure in his position, untroubled by sentiment.

His threat to [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] is terrifying precisely because he has nothing to lose. His "courage" is the courage of the predator — amoral, efficient, and ultimately hollow.

## The Pistols as Instrument

The General's pistols — [[Heddas-Pistols]], inherited by Hedda — are the play's physical symbol of courage. Hedda fires them (into the garden from [[The-Glass-Door|the glass door]]), gives one to [[Eilert-Lovborg|Lovborg]] (who uses it to kill himself), and ultimately uses one on herself.

The pistols represent the masculine, military courage of General Gabler's world — a world Hedda was raised in but cannot inhabit.

Her use of the pistols is always incomplete: firing at nothing, lending one to a suicide, turning one on herself in a gesture of defeat rather than triumph.

## Comparative Table

| Character | Type of Courage | Limitation | Outcome |
|-----------|----------------|------------|---------|
| [[Hedda-Gabler]] | Performative audacity | Cowardice before scandal | Suicide (escape) |
| [[Eilert-Lovborg]] | Intellectual daring | Moral weakness | Degrading death |
| [[Mrs-Elvsted]] | Quiet devotion | Social vulnerability | Reconstructive work |
| [[George-Tesman]] | Patient labor | Pedestrian ambition | Scholarship continues |
| [[Judge-Brack]] | Cynical fearlessness | Amoral emptiness | Triumphs temporarily |

> The play's final judgment is implicit: the courage that matters is not the courage to die beautifully, but the courage to live honestly — a courage [[Mrs-Elvsted|Thea]] has and [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] lacks.
