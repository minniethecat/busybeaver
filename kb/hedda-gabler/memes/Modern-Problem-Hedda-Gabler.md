# The Modern Problem in Hedda Gabler

The play's tragic credentials are complicated by its modernity — specifically, by Ibsen's substitution of social forces for the cosmic ones that drive classical tragedy. In Sophocles, the gods determine the protagonist's downfall. In *Hedda Gabler*, the gods have been replaced by bourgeois respectability, gender hierarchy, and the scandal economy.

> Particular traits and tendencies of the Hedda type are very common in modern life, and not only among women. Hyperaesthesia lies at the root of her tragedy. With a keenly critical, relentlessly solvent intelligence, she combines a morbid sensibility.
>
> (002-078–002-080)

These forces are as implacable as fate — Hedda cannot escape them any more than Oedipus can escape the prophecy — but they are entirely human in origin. [[Respectability]] is not divine; it is a social code enforced by people like [[Judge-Brack|Brack]]. The [[Scandal|scandal]] economy is not cosmic; it is maintained by gossip, reputation, and the threat of exposure. [[Gender-and-Power-in-Hedda-Gabler|Gender hierarchy]] is not natural; it is a social arrangement sustained by law, custom, and violence.

This substitution creates a deep formal tension. If the forces that destroy [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] are human, they are also changeable. The play's implicit politics — this world could be different — undermines its tragic effect — this fate is inevitable. The audience is caught between two responses: the tragic (Hedda's fall is noble and meaningful) and the political (Hedda's fall is unnecessary and enraging).

Ibsen does not resolve this tension. The play works as tragedy and as social critique simultaneously, and this double effect is its greatest formal achievement. The modern problem is not a flaw in the play — it is the play's subject. Tragedy in a world without gods is tragedy that indicts the world.
