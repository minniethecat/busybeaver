# Curtains: Meta-Theatrical Dimension

The [[The-Curtains|curtains]] of the [[Tesman-Villa]] carry a meta-theatrical dimension beyond their literal function. A curtain on stage is always already the boundary between drama and reality — the audience watches characters who are themselves watching and being watched, hiding and being exposed.

When [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] stands at the curtained [[The-Glass-Door|glass door]], she is caught in a triple frame: the literal curtain she manipulates, the stage curtain that separates actors from audience, and the social curtain of [[Respectability|respectability]] that separates private desire from public conduct. Each frame encloses the next, and Hedda is trapped at the center of all three.

The inner room curtain creates a second stage within the stage. Characters who pass through it move from one theatrical register to another — from the drawing-room's social performance to the inner room's private truth. The audience watches both spaces simultaneously, seeing what the characters in the drawing room cannot. This dual visibility is meta-theatrical irony: we are watching a play about watching.

The play's final gesture — Hedda's suicide behind the inner room curtains, heard but not seen — is the ultimate curtain call. The audience sees the drawing room, the curtained doorway, and hears the shot. Ibsen denies us the spectacle of death while giving us everything we need to imagine it. The stage curtain does not fall at the play's end — the play's literal curtains do. The meta-theatrical frame shatters at the moment of maximum violence, leaving the audience to confront the shot's echo in a room now stripped of all its concealments.
