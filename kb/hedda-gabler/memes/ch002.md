[002-001]Some time elapsed, and again Ibsen received a postal packet from Holm.

[002-002]This one contained his will, in which Ibsen figured as his residuary

[002-003]legatee. But many other legatees were mentioned in the instrument--all

[002-004]of them ladies, such as Fraulein Alma Rothbart, of Bremen, and Fraulein

[002-005]Elise Kraushaar, of Berlin. The bequests to these meritorious spinsters

[002-006]were so generous that their sum considerably exceeded the amount of

[002-007]the testator's property. Ibsen gently but firmly declined the proffered

[002-008]inheritance; but Holm's will no doubt suggested to him the figure of

[002-009]that red-haired "[[Mademoiselle-Diana|Mademoiselle Diana]]," who is heard of but not seen

[002-010]in _[[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda Gabler]]_, and enabled him to add some further traits to the

[002-011]portraiture of Lovborg. When the play appeared, Holm recognised

[002-012]himself with glee in the character of the bibulous man of letters,

[002-013]and thereafter adopted "[[Eilert-Lovborg|Eilert Lovborg]]" as his pseudonym. I do not,

[002-014]therefore, see why Dr. Brandes should suppress his real name; but I

[002-015]willingly imitate him in erring on the side of discretion. The poor

[002-016]fellow died several years ago.

[002-017]Some critics have been greatly troubled as to the precise meaning of

[002-018]Hedda's fantastic vision of Lovborg "with vine-leaves in his hair."

[002-019]Surely this is a very obvious image or symbol of the beautiful, the

[002-020]ideal, aspect of bacchic elation and revelry. Antique art, or I am much

[002-021]mistaken, shows us many figures of Dionysus himself and his followers

[002-022]with vine-leaves entwined their hair. To Ibsen's mind, at any rate, the

[002-023]image had long been familiar. In _Peer Gynt_ (Act iv. sc. 8), when Peer,

[002-024]having carried off Anitra, finds himself in a particularly festive mood,

[002-025]he cries: "Were there vine-leaves around, I would garland my brow."

[002-026]Again, in _Emperor and Galilean_ (Pt. ii. Act 1) where Julian, in the

[002-027]procession of Dionysus, impersonates the god himself, it is directed

[002-028]that he shall wear a wreath of vine-leaves. Professor Dietrichson

[002-029]relates that among the young artists whose society Ibsen frequented

[002-030]during his first years in Rome, it was customary, at their little

[002-031]festivals, for the revellers to deck themselves in this fashion. But the

[002-032]image is so obvious that there is no need to trace it to any personal

[002-033]experience. The attempt to place Hedda's vine-leaves among Ibsen's

[002-034]obscurities is an example of the firm resolution not to understand which

[002-035]animated the criticism of the 'nineties.

[002-036]Dr. Brandes has dealt very severely with the character of Eilert

[002-037]Lovborg, alleging that we cannot believe in the genius attributed to

[002-038]him. But where is he described as a genius? The poet represents him as a

[002-039]very able student of sociology; but that is quite a different thing from

[002-040]attributing to him such genius as must necessarily shine forth in every

[002-041]word he utters. Dr. Brandes, indeed, declines to believe even in his

[002-042]ability as a sociologist, on the ground that it is idle to write about

[002-043]the social development of the future. "To our prosaic minds," he says,

[002-044]"it may seem as if the most sensible utterance on the subject is that of

[002-045]the fool of the play: 'The future! Good heavens, we know nothing of the

[002-046]future.'" The best retort to this criticism is that which Eilert himself

[002-047]makes: "There's a thing or two to be said about it all the same." The

[002-048]intelligent forecasting of the future (as Mr. H. G. Wells has shown)

[002-049]is not only clearly distinguishable from fantastic Utopianism, but is

[002-050]indispensable to any large statesmanship or enlightened social activity.

[002-051]With very real and very great respect for Dr. Brandes, I cannot think

[002-052]that he has been fortunate in his treatment of Lovborg's character.

[002-053]It has been represented as an absurdity that he would think of reading

[002-054]abstracts from his new book to a man like Tesman, whom he despises. But

[002-055]though Tesman is a ninny, he is, as Hedda says, a "specialist"--he is a

[002-056]competent, plodding student of his subject. Lovborg may quite naturally

[002-057]wish to see how his new method, or his excursion into a new field,

[002-058]strikes the average scholar of the Tesman type. He is, in fact, "trying

[002-059]it on the dog"--neither an unreasonable nor an unusual proceeding. There

[002-060]is, no doubt, a certain improbability in the way in which Lovborg is

[002-061]represented as carrying his manuscript around, and especially in Mrs.

[002-062]Elvsted's production of his rough draft from her pocket; but these are

[002-063]mechanical trifles, on which only a niggling criticism would dream of

[002-064]laying stress.

[002-065]Of all Ibsen's works, _Hedda Gabler_ is the most detached, the most

[002-066]objective--a character-study pure and simple. It is impossible--or so

[002-067]it seems to me--to extract any sort of general idea from it. One cannot

[002-068]even call it a satire, unless one is prepared to apply that term to the

[002-069]record of a "case" in a work of criminology. Reverting to Dumas's dictum

[002-070]that a play should contain "a painting, a judgment, an ideal," we may

[002-071]say the _Hedda Gabler_ fulfils only the first of these requirements. The

[002-072]poet does not even pass judgment on his heroine: he simply paints her

[002-073]full-length portrait with scientific impassivity. But what a portrait!

[002-074]How searching in insight, how brilliant in colouring, how rich in

[002-075]detail! Grant Allen's remark, above quoted, was, of course, a whimsical

[002-076]exaggeration; the Hedda type is not so common as all that, else the

[002-077]world would quickly come to an end. But particular traits and tendencies

[002-078]of the Hedda type are very common in modern life, and not only among

[002-079]women. Hyperaesthesia lies at the root of her tragedy. With a keenly

[002-080]critical, relentlessly solvent intelligence, she combines a morbid

[002-081]shrinking from all the gross and prosaic detail of the sensual life.

[002-082]She has nothing to take her out of herself--not a single intellectual

[002-083]interest or moral enthusiasm. She cherishes, in a languid way, a petty

[002-084]social ambition; and even that she finds obstructed and baffled. At the

[002-085]same time she learns that another woman has had the courage to love and

[002-086]venture all, where she, in her cowardice, only hankered and refrained.

[002-087]Her malign egoism rises up uncontrolled, and calls to its aid her quick

[002-088]and subtle intellect. She ruins the other woman's happiness, but in

[002-089]doing so incurs a danger from which her sense of personal dignity

[002-090]revolts. Life has no such charm for her that she cares to purchase it at

[002-091]the cost of squalid humiliation and self-contempt. The good and the bad

[002-092]in her alike impel her to have done with it all; and a pistol-shot

[002-093]ends what is surely one of the most poignant character-tragedies in

[002-094]literature. Ibsen's brain never worked at higher pressure than in the

[002-095]conception and adjustment of those "crowded hours" in which Hedda,

[002-096]tangled in the web of Will and Circumstance, struggles on till she is

[002-097]too weary to struggle any more.

[002-098]It may not be superfluous to note that the "a" in "Gabler" should be

[002-099]sounded long and full, like the "a" in "Garden"--NOT like the "a" in

[002-100]"gable" or in "gabble."

[002-141]ACT FIRST.

[002-142]A spacious, handsome, and tastefully furnished drawing room,

[002-143]decorated in dark colours. In the back, a wide doorway with

[002-144]curtains drawn back, leading into a smaller room decorated

[002-145]in the same style as the drawing-room. In the right-hand

[002-146]wall of the front room, a folding door leading out to the

[002-147]hall. In the opposite wall, on the left, a glass door, also

[002-148]with curtains drawn back. Through the panes can be seen

[002-149]part of a verandah outside, and trees covered with autumn

[002-150]foliage. An oval table, with a cover on it, and surrounded

[002-151]by chairs, stands well forward. In front, by the wall on

[002-152]the right, a wide stove of dark porcelain, a high-backed

[002-153]arm-chair, a cushioned foot-rest, and two footstools. A

[002-154]settee, with a small round table in front of it, fills the

[002-155]upper right-hand corner. In front, on the left, a little

[002-156]way from the wall, a sofa. Further back than the glass

[002-157]door, a piano. On either side of the doorway at the back

[002-158]a whatnot with terra-cotta and majolica ornaments.--

[002-159]Against the back wall of the inner room a sofa, with a

[002-160]table, and one or two chairs. Over the sofa hangs the

[002-161]portrait of a handsome elderly man in a General's uniform.

[002-162]Over the table a hanging lamp, with an opal glass shade.--A

[002-163]number of bouquets are arranged about the drawing-room, in

[002-164]vases and glasses. Others lie upon the tables. The floors

[002-165]in both rooms are covered with thick carpets.--Morning light.

[002-166]The sun shines in through the glass door.

[002-167]MISS JULIANA TESMAN, with her bonnet on a carrying a parasol,

[002-168]comes in from the hall, followed by BERTA, who carries a

[002-169]bouquet wrapped in paper. MISS TESMAN is a comely and pleasant-

[002-170]looking lady of about sixty-five. She is nicely but simply

[002-171]dressed in a grey walking-costume. BERTA is a middle-aged

[002-172]woman of plain and rather countrified appearance.

[002-173]MISS TESMAN.

[002-174][Stops close to the door, listens, and says softly:] Upon my word, I

[002-175]don't believe they are stirring yet!

[002-176]BERTA.

[002-177][Also softly.] I told you so, Miss. Remember how late the steamboat got

[002-178]in last night. And then, when they got home!--good Lord, what a lot the

[002-179]young mistress had to unpack before she could get to bed.

[002-180]MISS TESMAN.

[002-181]Well well--let them have their sleep out. But let us see that they get a

[002-182]good breath of the fresh morning air when they do appear.

[002-183][She goes to the glass door and throws it open.

[002-184]BERTA.

[002-185][Beside the table, at a loss what to do with the bouquet in her hand.]

[002-186]I declare there isn't a bit of room left. I think I'll put it down here,

[002-187]Miss. [She places it on the piano.

[002-188]MISS TESMAN.

[002-189]So you've got a new mistress now, my dear [[Berta]]. Heaven knows it was a

[002-190]wrench to me to part with you.

[002-191]BERTA.

[002-192][On the point of weeping.] And do you think it wasn't hard for me, too,

[002-193]Miss? After all the blessed years I've been with you and Miss Rina.(1)

[002-194]MISS TESMAN.

[002-195]We must make the best of it, Berta. There was nothing else to be done.

[002-196]George can't do without you, you see-he absolutely can't. He has had you

[002-197]to look after him ever since he was a little boy.

[002-198]BERTA.

[002-199]Ah but, Miss Julia, I can't help thinking of Miss Rina lying helpless

[002-200]at home there, poor thing. And with only that new girl too! She'll never

[002-201]learn to take proper care of an invalid.
