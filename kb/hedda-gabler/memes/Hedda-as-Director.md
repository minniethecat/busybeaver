# Hedda as Director

Hedda approaches human relationships as a theatrical director approaches a production. She casts roles, blocks scenes, and orchestrates encounters with precise attention to dramatic effect. Her tragedy is that the other characters refuse to follow her direction.

## Casting

Hedda assigns roles with the clarity of a director assembling a company. Lovborg is cast as the tragic hero ("vine leaves in his hair"), Thea as the rival, Tesman as the dull husband who enables the plot, Brack as the confidant who observes from the wings. Each character is assigned a part in her production; each eventually rebels against the casting.

## Blocking

Hedda arranges bodies in space with theatrical precision. She seats Lovborg here, Thea there; she positions herself between them; she orchestrates entrances and exits. The drawing room is her stage, and she moves through it blocking the scenes that will become her drama. When Brack appropriates the stage in Act IV, her directorial control is already collapsing.

## The Lost Script

Hedda's tragedy is that she cannot write the script — she can only direct. The other characters' motivations, desires, and histories are not hers to control. Lovborg relapses in ways she didn't intend; Thea proves more resilient than expected; Brack learns to direct better than she does. The director loses control of her production.

## The Confession of Powerlessness

Hedda's directorial ambition finds its most explicit statement in Act II, in her confession to Thea — the one moment where she articulates what she actually wants:

> HEDDA. I want for once in my life to have power to mould a human destiny.
>
> MRS. ELVSTED. Have you not the power?
>
> HEDDA. I have not — and have never had it.
>
> (010-279 to 010-283)

The exchange is remarkable for its honesty. Hedda, who spends the entire play performing control, admits here that the performance is hollow. She has never had power over anyone — not her father, not her husband, not Lovborg. The confession explains everything that follows: the manipulation of Thea, the sending of Lovborg to the bachelor party, the burning of the manuscript. Each act is an attempt to acquire the power she has just confessed she lacks.

The tragedy is that these attempts work — but in the wrong direction. Hedda does gain power, but only the power to destroy. She cannot make Lovborg "come home with vine leaves in his hair" (010-263); she can only hand him a pistol and demand he die beautifully. The director who cannot create can only destroy the production.

## The Final Production

Hedda's suicide is her last directorial act. She stages it with the care she once gave to other people's scenes: the piano music (sound design), the temple-shot (blocking), the audience in the next room (audience). It is the only production she controls from beginning to end — and it ends the director.
