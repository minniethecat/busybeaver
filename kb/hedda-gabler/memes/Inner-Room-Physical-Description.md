# Inner Room: Physical Description

The inner room of the [[Tesman-Villa]] occupies the upstage area, "in the back" (002-159), separated from the drawing-room by a curtained doorway. Unlike the drawing-room, which is dominated by the [[The-Glass-Door|glass door]] and its flood of morning sun, the inner room is dimmer, more enclosed, and furnished for informal use rather than social performance.

Ibsen's stage directions describe: "Against the back wall of the inner room a sofa, with a table, chairs, and whatnot" (002-159). The whatnot — a Victorian display stand — holds porcelain and decorative objects, echoing the drawing-room's conventions in a more intimate register. A piano stands in the foreground, which [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] later moves to assert spatial control.

Above the sofa hangs General Gabler's portrait, establishing paternal authority over the private chamber. The [[The-Stove|stove]] — a cast-iron fixture — is located in or immediately adjacent to the inner room, linking domestic warmth to the play's climactic act of destruction.

The curtained doorway, not a solid door, means the inner room is always permeable. Sound passes through; figures glimpsed within remain partially visible to the audience. This porosity is a deliberate dramaturgical choice: the inner room is private but never sealed, secluded but never invisible.
