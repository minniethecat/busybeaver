# Manuscript: Dramatic Functions

The manuscript serves multiple dramatic purposes simultaneously, making it one of Ibsen's most efficient plot devices.

Its economic function is to raise the stakes of the Lovborg-Tesman rivalry. The manuscript's publication would secure [[Eilert-Lovborg|Lovborg]] the professorship over [[George-Tesman|Tesman]], transforming their competition from personal history into professional consequence. Tesman's finding of the manuscript is thus not merely a discovery but a crisis: his rival's work, the key to his rival's advancement, fallen into his hands.

Its symbolic function is to represent the "child" of Lovborg and [[Mrs-Elvsted|Thea]]'s creative partnership. This identification makes [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]]'s destruction of the manuscript not merely an act of sabotage but an act of symbolic infanticide — the play's most unequivocally evil moment, severing any remaining audience sympathy for the protagonist.

Its moral function is to serve as the object of Hedda's most unambiguous crime. She lies to Tesman about having the manuscript, lies to Lovborg about burning it, and burns it while he searches desperately outside. No other act in the play is so methodically cruel.

Its structural function is to provide the play's closing image. The reconstruction of the manuscript by Tesman and Thea — sitting together near [[The-Stove|the stove]] that consumed the original — provides an image of renewal. Creation survives destruction; the collaborative labor of rebuilding replaces the isolated act of burning. The manuscript dies so that it can be reborn.
