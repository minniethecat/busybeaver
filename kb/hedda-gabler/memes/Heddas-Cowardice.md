# Hedda's Cowardice

[[Hedda-Gabler]] performs courage with remarkable skill. She handles [[Heddas-Pistols|pistols]] "with the ease of a general's daughter" (007-050). She burns [[The-Manuscript|Lovborg's manuscript]] (011-101). She mocks [[George-Tesman|Tesman]]'s domesticity and [[Judge-Brack|Brack]]'s bourgeois propriety. Every gesture reads defiance.

Yet the play systematically exposes this performance as a mask for profound cowardice. Her central fear is scandal: "What will people say?" is, as Brack observes, Hedda's true master. She is "terrified of a scandal" (012-150) — the fear that determines every significant choice.

Her marriage to Tesman was a retreat, not a commitment. She married him because he was available, safe, and offered no emotional risk — "the only one offering" (004-189). She envies [[Mrs-Elvsted|Thea Elvsted]]'s "courage to love and to live for another" (002-085) but cannot bring herself to do either.

Her cruelty — to Aunt Julia, to Thea, to Lovborg — is itself a form of cowardice: safer to wound from behind the curtain than to act openly. Her final suicide, shot in the temple beneath her father's portrait, is presented not as courage but as ultimate evasion. [[Judge-Brack|Brack]]'s final line — "People don't do such things!" — underscores that this death is not heroic but antisocial.


> "Yes, Hedda, you are a coward at heart. ... A terrible coward."
>
> (009-309–009-311)

Ibsen scholar Francis Bull noted that Hedda "combines a keenly critical, relentlessly solvent intelligence with a morbid shrinking from all the gross and prosaic detail of the sensual life" (002-079). Her intelligence is real; her courage is not. The pistols are the perfect emblem: she can shoot but cannot act, threaten but cannot build, destroy but cannot create.
