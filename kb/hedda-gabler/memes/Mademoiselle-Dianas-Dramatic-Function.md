# Mademoiselle Diana's: Dramatic Function

Mademoiselle Diana's never appears on stage but exerts constant gravitational pull on the action of *Hedda Gabler*. It represents the world [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] fantasizes about — the "vine leaves" of bacchic abandon — and the ugly reality that awaits those who actually venture there.

The establishment's offstage presence creates dramatic irony. The audience and [[Judge-Brack|Brack]] know what happened there; Hedda clings to her idealized vision of [[Eilert-Lovborg|Lovborg]]'s "beautiful" death. Each time Brack alludes to the establishment, the gap between Hedda's fantasy and the actual events widens.

The establishment is named three times in rapid succession in Act II — "Mademoiselle Diana's?" (012-073) — as Brack and Tesman feed Hedda's curiosity with carefully calibrated fragments. Each reference tightens the dramatic knot. Hedda wants to hear about the debauchery but only on her own terms: as romantic excess, not sordid detail.

Diana's functions as the symbolic underworld of the play's moral geography. The [[Tesman-Villa|Tesman villa]] is the over-world of respectability, performance, and drawn curtains; Diana's is the underworld of license, violence, and exposure. The membrane between them is permeable — Brack moves freely between both worlds, and his threat to expose the connection is the engine of the play's final crisis.

Diana presided over "a soiree, to which many of her circle of admirers were bidden" (012-075). The word "soiree" — borrowed from polite society — is viciously ironic applied to a brothel. It mimics the drawing-room vocabulary of the Tesman villa to underscore how thin the membrane between the two worlds really is.
