# Tesman Villa as Cage

For [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]], the [[Tesman-Villa|Tesman villa]] is a prison furnished in dark colours. She has no professional occupation, no creative outlet, and no escape from the drawing room except to the verandah — itself a liminal space between domesticity and the outside world, visible through [[The-Glass-Door|the glass door]] but entered only briefly.

Her trajectory through the villa's spaces maps her psychological decline. Act I: she moves restlessly between rooms, redirects furniture — small assertions of ownership over a space she cannot leave. Act II: she manipulates the curtains, the lighting, the arrivals — controlling the environment she is trapped within. Act III: she is confined increasingly to [[The-Inner-Room|the inner room]] as her options narrow. Act IV: she shoots herself in that inner room, beneath her father's portrait — the private space becomes a tomb.

Ibsen's spatial arrangement creates a nested hierarchy of exposure and concealment: public drawing room, inner room, offstage chambers, [[Mademoiselle-Dianas]] (invisible). Each layer is more private, more dangerous, and less controllable from Hedda's position. The drawing room is the stage of social performance; the inner room is the threshold of private violence; the offstage is where secrets gestate; Mademoiselle Diana's is where they detonate.

> I am only looking at the leaves. They are so yellow--so withered.
>
> (004-182)

The villa's architecture is the play's moral structure made visible. Hedda can move between its rooms — she is the mistress of the house, after all — but she cannot leave. The cage is gilded, tastefully furnished, decorated in dark colours, and inescapable. Her final act transforms the villa from home to mausoleum: the place she once admired during a careless walk becomes the site of her suicide.
