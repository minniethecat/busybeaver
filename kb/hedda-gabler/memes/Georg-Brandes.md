Georg Brandes (1842–1927) was a Danish critic, literary scholar, and a leading figure in the Scandinavian modern breakthrough. He was a contemporary and interpreter of Henrik Ibsen, and his biographical research on *Hedda Gabler* provides much of the documentary evidence about the play's origins. (001-036)

## Ibsen's Biographer

Brandes traced the real-life models for Ibsen's characters. He shows that two of a certain lady's intimate friends had features that reappear in Hedda Gabler. (001-144) According to Brandes, Ibsen fashioned his drama out of small and scattered traits observed in actual individuals. (001-162) He also identified a man of letters, whom he calls Holm, as an enthusiast for a lady whose characteristics resemble Hedda's. (001-166)

> "Thus," says Dr. Brandes, "out of small and scattered traits of reality Ibsen fashioned his close-knit and profoundly thought-out works of art."
>
> (001-162–001-163)

## Critical Analysis of the Play

Brandes dealt severely with certain interpretations of the play's characters, insisting on the importance of the actual biographical sources behind Ibsen's creations. (002-036) He declined to believe some of the more fanciful theories about the figures in the drama, preferring to ground his analysis in documentary evidence of Ibsen's social circle. (002-041) Despite his sharp critical judgments, there was great respect for Brandes's method of tracing literary figures back to their real-life prototypes. (002-051)

## Theatrical Connection

A member of the Brandes family performed the role of Hedda at a significant early production of the play. (001-106)
