# Hedda's Suicide: Interpretive Traditions

[[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]]'s suicide has generated more critical commentary than any other moment in the play, and the volume of interpretation reflects the act's genuine ambiguity. Four major traditions dominate the discourse, each with textual support and each capturing a dimension of the act while missing others.

The feminist reading interprets the suicide as Hedda's only authentic act of agency — the one choice [[Judge-Brack|Brack]] cannot take from her. In a world that has systematically denied her autonomy, the suicide is autonomy's final, desperate assertion. She dies by her own hand, on her own terms, with her father's pistol. No one controls this act but her.

The existential reading sees the suicide as the logical endpoint of Hedda's refusal of inauthentic existence. She cannot be Tesman's wife, Brack's mistress, or Thea's rival. Every role offered to her is a form of bad faith. The suicide is her rejection of being defined by others, even at the cost of being at all.

The aesthetic reading holds that the suicide is Hedda's only successful work of art. She could not achieve the beautiful death through [[Eilert-Lovborg|Lovborg]]; she achieves it through herself. The temple-shot, the wild piano, the drawn curtains — every detail is orchestrated for maximum dramatic effect. Her life produced nothing but boredom; her death produces meaning.

The tragic reading positions the suicide as the classical tragic act — the noble death that proves the protagonist's greatness. The play's refusal to settle among these interpretations is its deepest formal achievement.
