# Manuscript: Child Metaphor

The most powerful and disturbing aspect of the manuscript is its identification with parenthood. When [[Eilert-Lovborg|Lovborg]] tells [[Mrs-Elvsted|Thea]] he has destroyed it, she protests with the play's most anguished line: "But that is the child, Eilert! That is our child!" The manuscript is the product of their shared intellectual labor, more real to Thea than any biological offspring.

This metaphor cuts directly at [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]], who is pregnant with [[George-Tesman|Tesman]]'s child — a biological fact she finds repulsive and tries to conceal even from herself. The contrast is absolute and deliberately cruel.

The manuscript-child is freely chosen, collaboratively made, the product of intellectual equals working toward the future of civilization. The biological child is imposed, a biological accident, the product of a marriage Hedda entered for security and now finds suffocating. Thea is co-creator, not vessel; Hedda is vessel, not creator. Both partners chose the manuscript-child; Hedda never chose hers.

When Hedda burns the manuscript page by page, whispering "Now I am burning your child, Thea!" (013-125), she is destroying what she most envies — a creation freely chosen rather than a pregnancy unwillingly endured. The act is competitive, vindictive, and self-implicating: by calling it infanticide, Hedda names exactly what she is doing and why.

The child metaphor inverts the play's gender dynamics. In a world where women are defined by biological reproduction, Thea has found a way to create beyond biology — and Hedda, trapped in biology, destroys that alternative.
