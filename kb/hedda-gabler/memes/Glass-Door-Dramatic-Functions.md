# Glass Door: Dramatic Functions

The glass door of the [[Tesman-Villa]] serves multiple dramatic functions, each mapping a character's relationship to freedom, exposure, and the boundary between domestic interior and external world.

**Threshold Between Worlds**: The glass door is the primary entrance for visitors. [[Judge-Brack|Brack]] enters "through the glass door" (007-061), carrying his light overcoat — bypassing the front door's social protocols. The door mediates between the garden (public world) and the drawing-room (private drama), and Brack's free use of it signals his privileged access.

**Hedda's Ambivalence**: [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]]'s relationship to the glass door reveals her fundamental conflict. She opens the curtains to look out, then draws them closed. She stands "at the glass door, drumming on the pane" (008-316) — the transparent barrier between her and freedom. Her choreography is one of restless approach and retreat, desire and refusal.

> At the glass door, drumming on the pane.
>
> (008-316)

**Pistol Practice**: In one of the play's most iconic images, Hedda stands "by the open glass door, loading a revolver" and fires into the garden (007-039). The glass door frames her as General Gabler's daughter, exercising the only power she possesses. [[Judge-Brack|Brack]] enters immediately after — through the same door — and is nearly shot.

**Symbol of Exposure**: Being transparent, the glass door symbolizes the exposure Hedda fears above all. Light enters through it (truth, revelation, scandal); curtains cover it (concealment, secrecy). [[Courage-and-Cowardice|Courage]] would be to open the door and step through; Hedda can only stand at the pane, drumming her fingers against the glass.
