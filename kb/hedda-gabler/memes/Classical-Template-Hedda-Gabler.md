# The Classical Template in Hedda Gabler

Mapping *Hedda Gabler* against Aristotle's criteria for tragedy reveals a play that fulfills the formal requirements while subverting their traditional content. The template fits, but uneasily — and that unease is the play's point.

[[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] satisfies the requirement for a noble protagonist: she is General Gabler's daughter, aristocrat by birth rather than achievement. Her nobility is inherited, not earned — but it is real, and her fall is genuinely from a height. The hamartia (tragic flaw) is the first complication. What is Hedda's flaw? Her boredom? Her cruelty? Her aestheticism? None qualifies as an error in judgment in the classical sense. Her "flaw" is structural: she was born a woman of intelligence and will in a world that has no use for such a woman. The flaw is not in her character but in her situation.

The peripeteia (reversal) is Lovborg's death — the ugly, accidental gut-shot (014-106) that destroys her fantasy of the beautiful death and exposes her to [[Judge-Brack|Brack]]'s power. The anagnorisis (recognition) arrives when Hedda understands she is "in your power" (015-127) — the moment she sees that Brack controls her fate.

But catharsis — the purging of pity and fear that Aristotle identified as tragedy's purpose — is conspicuously absent. The audience does not feel purged; it feels disturbed. "People don't do such things" (015-242) registers not catharsis but its failure. The classical template is present but broken, and the break is Ibsen's deepest statement about modernity.
