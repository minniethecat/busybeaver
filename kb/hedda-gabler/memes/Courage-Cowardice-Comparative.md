# Courage and Cowardice: A Comparative Analysis

Ibsen's introduction frames the question directly: "What sort of man is Tesman? Is he a man? Is Hedda a real woman, or is she a 'coward' — a 'coward' who escapes from life because she cannot face its bourgeois reality?" (001-025). The question is not rhetorical — the play leaves it deliberately unresolved, inviting the audience to measure each character against a standard of courage that shifts with every act.

> LOVBORG.
> Yes, Hedda, you are a coward at heart.
> HEDDA.
> A terrible coward.
>
> (009-309)

[[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] offers performative audacity without substance. She handles pistols and burns manuscripts, but her courage collapses before the threat of scandal. Her suicide is not triumph but escape — the final evasion of a life she could not face.

[[Eilert-Lovborg|Lovborg]] possesses genuine intellectual courage — "the courage to live his life after his own fashion" (014-325) — but it proves dependent on [[Mrs-Elvsted|Thea]]'s sustaining faith. Without her, he unravels into squalid death.

[[Mrs-Elvsted|Thea]] embodies quiet, constructive courage: leaving her husband, defying respectability, and rebuilding what was destroyed. Her courage endures where Lovborg's collapses and Hedda's was never real.

[[George-Tesman|Tesman]], the character least associated with courage, shows the steady courage of patient labor — sitting with Thea to reconstruct the manuscript page by page. [[Judge-Brack|Brack]] represents a perversion: the courage of cynicism, fearless because he has nothing to lose and no one to care for.

The play's final judgment is implicit: the courage that matters is not the courage to die beautifully, but the courage to live honestly and to create what can survive destruction. Thea has it. Hedda does not.
