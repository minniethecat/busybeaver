# The Drawing Room Furniture

Ibsen's stage directions for *Hedda Gabler* specify the drawing room's furnishings with unusual precision. Each piece of furniture functions as a dramatic element, marking zones of power, intimacy, and exclusion.

## The Sofa

The sofa defines the room's "public" zone — the area where visitors are received and formal conversations occur. Characters seated on the sofa are in performance mode, observed by others and observing themselves. The sofa is the stage within the stage.

## The Whatnot

The whatnot beside the sofa (004-077) is a minor but significant piece. Its contents — bric-a-brac, ornaments, the detritus of respectable domesticity — represent the material culture Hedda despises. Aunt Julia touches it fondly; Hedda ignores it. The whatnot is the physical embodiment of the bourgeois world Hedda finds suffocating.

## The Armchair

The armchair by the stove is the room's most intimate seat. Characters who occupy it are claiming comfort and permanence. Brack appropriates it casually, signaling his comfort in a household not his own. Hedda rarely sits there — she prefers to move, to stand, to control space through movement rather than occupation.

## The Writing Table

The writing table is the site of intellectual labor — Tesman's research, Lovborg's manuscript notes, the final reconstruction. It represents the world of scholarship and creation that Hedda can observe but not enter. When Thea and Tesman gather around it in the final scene, they are claiming a space Hedda never truly occupied.
