# Inner Room: Dramatic Functions

The inner room of the [[Tesman-Villa]] serves four distinct dramatic functions, each deepening the play's architecture of visibility and concealment.

**Passage and Transition**: The inner room is first of all a thoroughfare. [[Berta]] "comes from the right into the inner room" (003-053); characters exit through it to the left toward [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]]'s private chambers. This constant flow of entrances and exits — always visible through the curtained doorway — drives the play's pacing and reminds the audience that private space is never truly sealed.

**Retreat and Conspiracy**: When [[George-Tesman|Tesman]] and [[Judge-Brack|Brack]] withdraw to the inner room to drink punch, they form a masculine enclave separate from Hedda's domain. The stage direction specifies they "seat themselves, drink punch, and talk in low voices" (009-101). This spatial separation enacts the play's gender politics: the men are literally behind Hedda's back.

> He and BRACK go into the inner room, seat themselves, drink punch, smoke cigarettes, and carry on a lively conversation during what follows.
>
> (009-101)

**Surveillance**: At critical moments, characters glance toward or eavesdrop from the inner room. Brack "glances toward the inner room" when warning Hedda to hush (006-074), and later "keeps an eye from time to time" on it (009-166). The inner room is where one watches without being seen watching — the architecture of the informant.

**The Crime Scene**: The inner room is where Hedda burns [[The-Manuscript|Lovborg's manuscript]] in the stove. She does not destroy it in the drawing-room; she retreats to the back chamber. In Ibsen, privacy is the zone of crime.
