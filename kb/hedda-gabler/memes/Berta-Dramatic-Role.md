# Berta — Dramatic Role

Berta's primary dramatic function is structural: she enters and exits through [[The-Inner-Room]], announcing visitors, serving punch, lighting lamps, and maintaining the domestic machinery that the other characters take for granted. But her role goes deeper — she is the play's "ordinary ear," hearing (and sometimes misunderstanding) the privileged conversations around her.

When [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] tells Berta she needn't clear the dishes, Berta is "puzzled" — "She said she would do it herself" (003-084). This small moment encapsulates the play's class tensions: Hedda, the general's daughter, cannot abide depending on a servant, yet cannot function without one.

Berta is the first character the audience sees. Act I opens with Berta in [[The-Inner-Room]], arranging flowers and setting the household in order before the Tesmans' return to the [[Tesman-Villa]] (003-004). This establishes the domestic world before any of the principals arrive — a world of work, of preparation, of invisible labor that makes the drawing-room drama possible.

She moves through the inner room more than any other character [009-035, 010-256, 013-139, 015-055], making her the play's most frequent occupant of Hedda's private sanctuary. The irony is sharp: Hedda's sacred space is most often occupied by the servant she disdains.

## From the Text

> MISS TESMAN.
> Oh, I shall manage to train her... You needn't be uneasy about my poor sister, my dear Berta.
> BERTA.
> Well, but there's another thing, Miss. I'm so mortally afraid I shan't be able to suit the young mistress.
> MISS TESMAN.
> Oh well--just at first there may be one or two things--
> BERTA.
> Most like she'll be terrible grand in her ways.
> MISS TESMAN.
> Well, you can't wonder at that--General Gabler's daughter! Think of the sort of life she was accustomed to in her father's time. Don't you remember how we used to see her riding down the road along with the General? In that long black habit--and with feathers in her hat?
> BERTA.
> Yes, indeed--I remember well enough!--But, good Lord, I should never have dreamt in those days that she and Master George would make a match of it.

> (003-001)
