General Gabler is the deceased father of Hedda Gabler. Though he never appears on stage, his presence permeates the play through his portrait on the wall and the pair of pistols he bequeathed to his daughter. (003-013)(007-025)

## Symbolic Legacy

"General Gabler's pistols" are a recurring motif in the play, extending the General's military and aristocratic influence beyond his death. (007-025) The pistols represent both Hedda's connection to her father's world of power and masculine authority, and her means of maintaining distance from the bourgeois domesticity of her marriage to George Tesman.

> [With cold eyes.] General Gabler's pistols.
>
> (007-025)

## Hedda as the General's Daughter

Hedda is frequently identified as "General Gabler's daughter," a label that defines her social identity. (003-013) This paternal identification places her above the Tesman family's middle-class world and contributes to her sense of entrapment in a marriage beneath her station.

> Well, you can't wonder at that — General Gabler's daughter! Think of what it means.
>
> (003-013)
