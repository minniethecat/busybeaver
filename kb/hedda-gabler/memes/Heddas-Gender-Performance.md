# Hedda's Gender Performance

[[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]]'s relationship to her own gender is complex and contradictory — and this contradiction is the source of both her power and her destruction. She occupies an impossible position: a woman who cannot be a woman, who also cannot be a man, stranded between categories in a world that demands categorization.

> My intention in giving it this name was to indicate that Hedda, as a personality, is to be regarded rather as her father's daughter than as her husband's wife.
>
> (001-025)

She has internalized masculine values — courage, action, aesthetic judgment — from [[General-Gablers-Shadow|her father]]. The General's code is her code, and it is a military-aristocratic code that has no provision for the feminine. At the same time, she despises the feminine in every form: pregnancy revolts her, [[Aunt-Julia]]'s domestic fussing irritates her, [[Mrs-Elvsted|Thea]]'s "irritating" hair provokes her cruelty (009-050).

Yet she uses femininity as a weapon. Her charm, her status as General Gabler's daughter, her position as Tesman's wife — all of these are deployed strategically. She knows how to perform the drawing-room hostess, how to extract confidences through the guise of friendship, how to manipulate men through the suggestion of intimacy.

Most significantly, she refuses every role offered to her: the devoted wife (she despises Tesman), the mother (she refuses to acknowledge her pregnancy), the mistress (she rejects Brack's triangle). She cannot be a woman because "woman" means submission, domesticity, renunciation of self. She cannot be a man because she is biologically female, legally subordinate, confined to a woman's sphere. The impossible position produces her destructiveness — and her suicide is the only resolution it allows.
