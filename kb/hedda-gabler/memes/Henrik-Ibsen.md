Henrik Ibsen (1828–1906) is the author of *Hedda Gabler*, which he wrote in Munich during the summer of 1890. (001-007) By July of that year, he reported the play was nearly complete, and he did not leave Munich until it was finished. (001-013) The manuscript was sent to Copenhagen for publication on December 16, 1890. (001-079)

## Composition and Publication

Ibsen wrote to the Swedish poet Count Carl Snoilsky from Munich on June 29, 1890, describing the progress of the new play. (001-007) He completed the work that summer and the play was published in Copenhagen later that year. (001-079) *Hedda Gabler* was the first of Ibsen's plays to be translated directly from the original Norwegian text rather than from a German translation. (001-080)

> From Munich, on June 29, 1890, Ibsen wrote to the Swedish poet, Count Carl Soilsky: "Our intention has all along been to spend the summer in the Tyrol again. But circumstances are against our doing so. I am at present engaged upon a new dramatic work, which for several reasons has made very slow progress, and I do not leave Munich until I can take with me the completed first draft. There is little or no prospect of my being able to complete it in July." Ibsen did not leave Munich at all that season.
>
> (001-007–001-013)

## Ibsen and Christiania

It seems quite clear that Ibsen had Christiania in mind as the setting for the play, drawing upon the city he himself had known in his earlier years there. (001-114)(001-121) The social world of the play reflects the Christiania of a somewhat earlier period than the date of composition. (001-115)

## Creative Method

Ibsen is commonly understood to have used real-life models for his characters, much as a painter uses sitters. (001-137) He met a young woman at Gossensass in the Tyrol who may have provided inspiration for elements of Hedda's character. (001-036) Ibsen reportedly said he had fashioned his close-knit drama from scattered observations of actual people. (001-163)

> The word "model" must be taken in a very different sense from that in which it is commonly used in painting. Ibsen undoubtedly used models for this trait and that, but never for a whole figure. If his characters can be called portraits at all, they are composite portraits.
>
> (001-136–001-139)

## Critical Reception

*Hedda Gabler* became one of Ibsen's most popular works. (001-093) The play was first performed at the Residenz Theater in Munich on January 31, 1891, and reached Christiania by February 25 of the same year. (001-083)(001-087) Its introduction to English audiences was aided significantly by the translation of Edmund Gosse and William Archer. (001-096)
