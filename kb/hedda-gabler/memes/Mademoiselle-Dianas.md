# Mademoiselle Diana's

A disreputable establishment in Christiania, presided over by the red-haired "Mademoiselle Diana," who is "heard of but not seen" (002-009) in the play. It is the site of [[Eilert-Lovborg|Lovborg]]'s fatal encounter — the offstage world of drinking, brawling, and sexual license that stands in visceral contrast to the [[Tesman-Villa]]'s drawing-room propriety.

For its dramatic function as offstage counterpoint, see [[Mademoiselle-Dianas-Dramatic-Function]]. The account of Lovborg's sordid death — the gut-shot that dismantles Hedda's fantasy — appears in [[Mademoiselle-Dianas-Lovborgs-Death]].

[[Mademoiselle-Dianas-Symbolic-Significance]] examines the establishment along dimensions of sexuality, class, information, and exposure. [[Mademoiselle-Dianas-Offstage-World]] explores Ibsen's choice to keep Diana's invisible, enacting a class hierarchy of space — until [[Judge-Brack|Brack]] collapses the distinction.

Diana's is the play's symbolic underworld. Every quality the Tesman villa asserts — respectability, privacy, orderly domesticity — Diana's negates. Yet Brack moves freely between them, and his threat to expose the connection is the engine of the play's final crisis.

## From the Text

The establishment and its proprietor exist entirely offstage, yet they haunt the play's action. The character was drawn from life — Ibsen fused a real inheritance dispute with a real red-haired woman:

> There was also in Bergen at the time a red-haired "Mademoiselle Diana," who is heard of but not seen in *Hedda Gabler*, and enabled him to add some further traits to the portraiture of Lovborg. When the play appeared, Holm recognised himself with glee in the character of the bibulous man of letters, and thereafter adopted "Eilert Lovborg" as his pseudonym.
>
> (002-019 to 002-027)

The biographical origin illuminates Ibsen's method: real people, transformed, compressed, and repositioned within a dramatic structure that makes them serve purposes their originals never imagined. Mademoiselle Diana is both a real woman from Bergen and a symbolic necessity — the offstage darkness without which the onstage light has no meaning.
