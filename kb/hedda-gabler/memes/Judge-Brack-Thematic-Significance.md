# Judge Brack — Thematic Significance

Brack represents the social order that [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] both despises and fears — respectability without morality, the judge who judges nothing but his own advantage. His face, which [[George-Tesman|Tesman]] reads as transparent (010-091), is actually unreadable — Brack's power lies precisely in the gap between what others think they see in him and what he is actually thinking.

He is also the play's most damning portrait of masculine power. Brack doesn't need to be strong, intelligent, or even particularly malicious. He only needs to be immune to scandal in a world where women are destroyed by it. His power is structural, not personal — and therefore unassailable.

The character functions as Ibsen's anatomy of how bourgeois society protects its own. The judge's "correctness" (007-212) is not the armor of a good man but the camouflage of a predator. In the play's moral economy, Brack demonstrates that institutional respectability and personal corruption are not opposites — they are, in fact, mutually enabling.
