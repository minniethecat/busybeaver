# The Coldness Motif

Coldness pervades *Hedda Gabler* at every level — physical, emotional, and symbolic. The play opens with Hedda complaining of the cold, and closes with her cold corpse. Between these poles, temperature imagery defines character relationships and thematic tensions.

## Physical Cold

The Tesman villa is constantly described in thermal terms. Hedda complains the house is cold (001-030); the stove must be lit (002-001); windows are opened and closed in a ongoing negotiation between warmth and ventilation. The villa's physical discomfort mirrors its emotional atmosphere — a household where no one is truly warm.

## Emotional Cold

Hedda's defining characteristic is emotional coldness. She cannot bear Aunt Julia's physical affection; she recoils from Tesman's touch; her response to Lovborg's death is not grief but aesthetic disappointment. Ibsen's stage directions repeatedly note her "cold" demeanor and "frigid" politeness. The cold is not a mask but her essential nature.

> HEDDA: [With cold eyes.] General Gabler's pistols.
> [She goes out through the inner room, to the left.]
>
> (007-025)

## The Stove as Counterpoint

[[The-Stove]] is the play's primary source of warmth — and the site of its most destructive act. Hedda burns Lovborg's manuscript there (009-013), converting physical heat into symbolic destruction. The stove that should warm becomes an engine of annihilation. The manuscript burns; the room grows warmer; the moral temperature drops below freezing.

## The Final Cold

Hedda's suicide leaves a body cooling in the inner room while the gentlemen play cards in the next. The physical cold of death merges with the emotional cold she embodied throughout. "She must be quite cold by now," someone might have said — but Hedda was always cold.
