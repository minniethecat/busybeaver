# The Double Standard in Hedda Gabler

The gender politics of *Hedda Gabler* are built on a foundation of systematic asymmetry that operates across every domain of life. This double standard is not background context — it is the play's engine, structuring every choice and constraining every possibility.

In the domain of profession, [[George-Tesman|Tesman]], [[Judge-Brack|Brack]], and [[Eilert-Lovborg|Lovborg]] all have careers and incomes. [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] and [[Mrs-Elvsted|Thea]] have no independent economic existence — their survival depends entirely on men. In sexuality, Brack's bachelor life and Lovborg's "dissipation" are tolerated or envied, while Hedda's desires must be hidden and Thea's escape from her husband is scandalous.

In space, men move freely between domestic and public spheres; women are confined to the interior. Brack enters through the garden (006-096) as easily as through the front door. Hedda's world is the drawing-room and the inner room — there is no third space.

In scandal, the asymmetry is absolute. Brack is scandal-proof — his "correctness and respectability are beyond all question" (007-212). Lovborg has already survived scandal and built a new reputation. But scandal would destroy Hedda completely, which is precisely why Brack's knowledge of the [[Heddas-Pistols|pistol]] is such effective leverage. The double standard is not a background condition — it is the mechanism by which power operates in the play's world.
