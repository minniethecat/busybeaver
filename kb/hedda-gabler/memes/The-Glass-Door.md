# The Glass Door

The glass door on the left wall of the [[Tesman-Villa]] drawing-room is a French window "with curtains drawn back" through which morning sunlight pours and visitors arrive (002-147). It is the boundary between domestic interior and external world, and characters' relationships to it map onto their psychological states.

For a detailed physical description, see [[Glass-Door-Physical-Description]]. Its functions as threshold, frame for Hedda's pistol practice, and symbol of exposure are covered in [[Glass-Door-Dramatic-Functions]].

An act-by-act account of the door's changing state appears in [[Glass-Door-Pattern-of-Use]], while [[Glass-Door-Thematic-Resonance]] examines its spatialization of the play's central themes.

[[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] stands behind it, looks through it, drums her fingers on its pane — but never passes through it into the life she imagines. The door is both invitation and exit, and she can accept neither. Its position opposite [[The-Inner-Room|the inner room]] creates a spatial dialectic between exposure and concealment that defines her tragedy.
