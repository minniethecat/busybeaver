# The Temple Shot

Hedda's decision to shoot herself in the temple rather than in the heart or mouth is among the most carefully motivated details in Ibsen's play. The temple-shot is simultaneously an aesthetic choice, a psychological signature, and a theatrical coup.

## Aesthetic Significance

The temple is the seat of thought, the locus of the intellectual life Hedda values above all else. To shoot herself there is to destroy her mind — the one part of herself she considers worth destroying. A shot to the heart would be sentimental; a shot to the mouth would be ugly. The temple is clean, precise, and carries the weight of classical precedent. [[Judge-Brack|Brack]] specifies the location — "in the temple" (015-242) — because it is the location that makes the act interpretable.

## Contrast with Lovborg

Hedda's temple-shot is explicitly positioned against Lovborg's death. He died in a brothel, shot in the stomach — a messy, squalid, unattractive death (012-040). Hedda's response to learning the details is revulsion: "Everything I touch becomes ludicrous and mean" (012-050). Her own suicide is a systematic correction of Lovborg's failure. Temple, not stomach. Elegance, not squalor. Control, not chaos.

## The Vine Leaves

The temple-shot is Hedda's last attempt to realize the "vine leaves" ideal she projected onto Lovborg (002-079). If he could not achieve a beautiful death, she will. The temple is where Dionysus's vine-leaf crown rests — the site of ecstatic vision. Hedda's bullet passes through the very spot where, in her fantasy, Lovborg should have been crowned.

## Performance Traditions

Productions vary widely in staging the temple-shot. Some literalize it (a visible wound); some abstract it (darkness and sound); some relocate it (shooting into the mouth or heart, to varying interpretive effect). The debate reflects a deeper question: is Hedda's suicide meant to be beautiful, or is Ibsen showing us that the very idea of a beautiful suicide is a category error?

## Key Passages from the Text

> Life has no such charm for her that she cares to purchase it at the cost
> of squalid humiliation and self-contempt. The good and the bad in her
> alike impel her to have done with it all; and a pistol-shot ends what is
> surely one of the most poignant character-tragedies in literature.
> Ibsen's brain never worked at higher pressure than in the conception and
> adjustment of those "crowded hours" in which Hedda, racked between the
> equal hells of secrecy and scandal, is inexorably crushed.
>
> (002-090–002-095)
