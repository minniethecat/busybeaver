# Hedda's Suicide: The Shot

The moment of [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]]'s suicide is Ibsen's most economical and devastating piece of stagecraft. Every detail matters, and every detail has been prepared by the preceding four acts.

First, the piano prelude. Ibsen's stage direction is precise: "She goes into the inner room and draws the curtains together after her. ... Then she is heard to play a wild dance on the piano" (015-228). The wild dance — a *tarantella* in many productions — serves multiple functions. It masks the sound of preparation.

It asserts her presence one last time, filling the villa with music that expresses what she cannot say. It is General Gabler's piano, General Gabler's daughter playing it. The word Ibsen chose — "wild" (*vild* in Norwegian) — is the antithesis of drawing-room decorum.

Then the shot. The stage direction is devastatingly simple: "A shot is heard within" (015-235). Ibsen gives no further description of the act itself. The shot interrupts the wild dance; the dance stops; the survivors react. The economy of the writing gives the moment its power.

> A shot is heard within.
>
> (015-231)

The shot is the temple-shot Hedda demanded of [[Eilert-Lovborg|Lovborg]]: "Do it beautifully" (012-080). She dies as she wanted him to die — clean, intentional, aesthetic. The same [[Heddas-Pistols|pistols]], the same [[The-Inner-Room|inner room]], the same aesthetic code — but this time, the code is fulfilled rather than betrayed.

The shot behind [[The-Curtains|drawn curtains]] is her last act of controlling visibility: she decides who sees what, and when.
