# Tesman Villa: Setting and Description

Ibsen's stage directions describe the [[Tesman-Villa|Tesman villa]] with uncharacteristic precision: "A spacious, handsome, and tastefully furnished drawing room, decorated in dark colours" (002-139). The "dark colours" are not incidental — they establish the play's emotional register before a character speaks. Autumn foliage visible through [[The-Glass-Door|the glass door]] locates the action in a season of decline.

Key features of the set include the glass door on the left wall (opening onto a veranda), [[The-Inner-Room|the inner room]] doorway at the back (with drawn curtains), General Gabler's portrait over the inner room sofa, the dark porcelain [[The-Stove|stove]], a piano, and decorative whatnots. Each object is both a realistic furnishing and a symbolic node in the play's dramatic machinery.

The verandah — visible but unreachable through the glass door's panes — becomes a spatial metaphor for [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]]'s relationship to freedom. She can see the outside world but cannot inhabit it.

> Part of a verandah outside, and trees covered with autumn foliage.
>
> (002-149)

The autumn trees, "covered with autumn foliage" (002-142), are beautiful in their dying — a visual premonition of the play's conclusion.

Ibsen specified that the villa should suggest Christiania of an earlier period, deliberately avoiding electric lights or telephones. This temporal displacement reinforces the play's atmosphere of social transition — an older aristocratic world yielding to bourgeois modernity (001-114). The villa, like its inhabitants, is suspended between epochs.
