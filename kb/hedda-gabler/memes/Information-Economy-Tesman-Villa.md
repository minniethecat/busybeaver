# Information Economy in the Tesman Villa

In *Hedda Gabler*, power is not exercised through force, wealth, or institutional authority. It is exercised through knowledge — specifically, through the control of what becomes public and what remains private. The [[Tesman-Villa|villa]] operates as a closed information economy in which secrets are currency and exposure is the ultimate threat.

Every character possesses or seeks knowledge that confers advantage. [[Judge-Brack|Brack]] knows about Lovborg's death circumstances, Hedda's pistol, and the "triangle" dynamics — and conceals his own intentions behind unassailable respectability (007-212). [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] knows about Lovborg's relapse and the manuscript's fate but is vulnerable to Brack's knowledge of her pistol. [[George-Tesman|Tesman]] knows academic gossip but is transparent himself — his naivety makes him everyone else's information source. [[Mrs-Elvsted|Thea]] knows Lovborg's creative process but is shielded by her willingness to disregard social disapproval.

> BRACK. Not a single home. Henceforth, as before, every respectable house will be closed against Eilert Lovborg.
>
> (012-150–012-151)

Knowledge flows asymmetrically. Brack receives information from everyone and gives nothing in return. Tesman volunteers information freely and receives deception in return. Hedda extracts confidences from Thea and Lovborg but cannot control how her own secrets circulate. The information economy mirrors the power structure: those who take and withhold rise; those who give and trust fall.

The threat of exposure is the play's central dramatic mechanism. Brack's power over Hedda derives entirely from what he knows and what he might reveal. He never needs to act on this knowledge — the threat is sufficient. His offer of "protection" (012-150) makes the terms explicit: silence in exchange for submission.
