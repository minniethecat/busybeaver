# Brack's Gendered Power

[[Judge-Brack]] is the play's most explicit embodiment of patriarchal power, and Ibsen's analysis of how that power operates is remarkably precise. Each dimension of Brack's power is specifically gendered — it works because of, not despite, the gender hierarchy that structures the play's world.

His power is informational, not physical. He knows things — the pistol's provenance, the death's circumstances, Hedda's complicity — and knowledge is currency in the villa's [[Power-and-Knowledge-in-the-Tesman-Villa|information economy]]. Physical force would be crude and deniable; information is clean and undeniable.

His power is passive, not aggressive. He offers "protection" — his silence in exchange for access. The triangle (012-150) is presented not as a demand but as an "arrangement," not as blackmail but as "understanding." The passivity is strategic: it allows Brack to maintain his respectability while exercising absolute control.

> HEDDA. [Looks up at him.] So I am in your power, Judge Brack. You have me at your beck and call, from this time forward.
>
> (015-161)

His power is institutional. He is a judge — the embodiment of the law — and his "correctness and respectability are beyond all question" (007-212) because the system he represents defines what correctness is. He does not need to prove his virtue; his position proves it for him.

Most importantly, his power is gendered in its mechanism. He can threaten Hedda because a woman's reputation is her only social asset. The same information about a man — Lovborg's brothel death, Tesman's financial imprudence — is manageable. About Hedda, it is fatal. Brack's power is not personal genius but structural position: the system empowers him to control her, and he plays his role perfectly.
