# Inner Room: Key Scenes

The inner room of the [[Tesman-Villa]] hosts or adjoins the play's most significant off-stage actions, each act marking a deepening of its dramatic weight.


> "Against the back wall of the inner room a sofa, with a table, and one or two chairs. Over the sofa hangs the portrait of the handsome elderly man in a General's uniform."
>
> (002-159–002-161)

**Act I** establishes the room's domestic function. [[Berta]] arranges flowers and prepares the household, moving between the drawing-room and the inner room (003-044). The inner room is introduced as a space of servant labor — functional, unremarkable, yet permanently visible through the curtained doorway.

**Act II** activates the room's social function. [[George-Tesman|Tesman]] and [[Judge-Brack|Brack]] retreat there for punch while [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] remains in the drawing-room with [[Eilert-Lovborg|Lovborg]] (009-101). This spatial division enacts the play's gender politics with diagrammatic clarity: masculine conspiracy in the back room, dangerous intimacy in the front.

**Act III** transforms the room into a crime scene. Hedda carries [[The-Manuscript|the manuscript]] into the inner room and burns it page by page in the [[The-Stove|stove]], whispering "Now I am burning your child, Thea!" (013-125). [[Berta]] later "makes up the fire," unknowingly completing the destruction.

**Act IV** unfolds the aftermath. Berta carries the manuscript's remains into the inner room (015-055). Tesman and [[Mrs-Elvsted|Thea]] reconstruct the manuscript from her notes at a table near the stove — creation in the same space as destruction (015-219). Finally, Hedda shoots herself in the inner room, beneath General Gabler's portrait. The shot is heard but not seen; the inner room, which has concealed so many secrets, now conceals her body.
