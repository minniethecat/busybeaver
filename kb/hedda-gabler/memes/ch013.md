[013-001]LOVBORG.

[013-002]Yes, you are right. It is a sort of child-murder.

[013-003]MRS. ELVSTED.

[013-004]How could you, then--! Did not the child belong to me too?

[013-005]HEDDA.

[013-006][Almost inaudibly.] Ah, the child--

[013-007]MRS. ELVSTED.

[013-008][Breathing heavily.] It is all over then. Well well, now I will go,

[013-009]Hedda.

[013-010]HEDDA.

[013-011]But you are not going away from town?

[013-012]MRS. ELVSTED.

[013-013]Oh, I don't know what I shall do. I see nothing but darkness before me.

[013-014][She goes out by the hall door.

[013-015]HEDDA.

[013-016][Stands waiting for a moment.] So you are not going to see her home, Mr.

[013-017]Lovborg?

[013-018]LOVBORG.

[013-019]I? Through the streets? Would you have people see her walking with me?

[013-020]HEDDA.

[013-021]Of course I don't know what else may have happened last night. But is it

[013-022]so utterly irretrievable?

[013-023]LOVBORG.

[013-024]It will not end with last night--I know that perfectly well. And the

[013-025]thing is that now I have no taste for that sort of life either. I won't

[013-026]begin it anew. She has broken my courage and my power of braving life

[013-027]out.

[013-028]HEDDA.

[013-029][Looking straight before her.] So that pretty little fool has had her

[013-030]fingers in a man's destiny. [Looks at him.] But all the same, how could

[013-031]you treat her so heartlessly.

[013-032]LOVBORG.

[013-033]Oh, don't say that I was heartless!

[013-034]HEDDA.

[013-035]To go and destroy what has filled her whole soul for months and years!

[013-036]You do not call that heartless!

[013-037]LOVBORG.

[013-038]To you I can tell the truth, Hedda.

[013-039]HEDDA.

[013-040]The truth?

[013-041]LOVBORG.

[013-042]First promise me--give me your word--that what I now confide in you Thea

[013-043]shall never know.

[013-044]HEDDA.

[013-045]I give you my word.

[013-046]LOVBORG.

[013-047]Good. Then let me tell you that what I said just now was untrue.

[013-048]HEDDA.

[013-049][[About]] the manuscript?

[013-050]LOVBORG.

[013-051]Yes. I have not torn it to pieces--nor thrown it into the fiord.

[013-052]HEDDA.

[013-053]No, no--. But--where is it then?

[013-054]LOVBORG.

[013-055]I have destroyed it none the less--utterly destroyed it, Hedda!

[013-056]HEDDA.

[013-057]I don't understand.

[013-058]LOVBORG.

[013-059]Thea said that what I had done seemed to her like a child-murder.

[013-060]HEDDA.

[013-061]Yes, so she said.

[013-062]LOVBORG.

[013-063]But to kill his child--that is not the worst thing a father can do to

[013-064]it.

[013-065]HEDDA.

[013-066]Not the worst?

[013-067]LOVBORG.

[013-068]Suppose now, Hedda, that a man--in the small hours of the morning--came

[013-069]home to his child's mother after a night of riot and debauchery, and

[013-070]said: "Listen--I have been here and there--in this place and in that.

[013-071]And I have taken our child with--to this place and to that. And I have

[013-072]lost the child--utterly lost it. The devil knows into what hands it may

[013-073]have fallen--who may have had their clutches on it."

[013-074]HEDDA.

[013-075]Well--but when all is said and done, you know--this was only a book--

[013-076]LOVBORG.

[013-077]Thea's pure soul was in that book.

[013-078]HEDDA.

[013-079]Yes, so I understand.

[013-080]LOVBORG.

[013-081]And you can understand, too, that for her and me together no future is

[013-082]possible.

[013-083]HEDDA.

[013-084]What path do you mean to take then?

[013-085]LOVBORG.

[013-086]None. I will only try to make an end of it all--the sooner the better.

[013-087]HEDDA.

[013-088][A step nearer him.] [[Eilert-Lovborg|Eilert Lovborg]]--listen to me.--Will you not try

[013-089]to--to do it beautifully?

[013-090]LOVBORG.

[013-091]Beautifully? [Smiling.] With vine-leaves in my hair, as you used to

[013-092]dream in the old days--?

[013-093]HEDDA.

[013-094]No, no. I have lost my faith in the vine-leaves. But beautifully

[013-095]nevertheless! For once in a way!--Good-bye! You must go now--and do not

[013-096]come here any more.

[013-097]LOVBORG.

[013-098]Good-bye, Mrs. Tesman. And give [[George-Tesman|George Tesman]] my love.

[013-099][He is on the point of going.

[013-100]HEDDA.

[013-101]No, wait! I must give you a memento to take with you.

[013-102][She goes to the writing-table and opens the drawer and the

[013-103]pistol-case; then returns to LOVBORG with one of the pistols.

[013-104]LOVBORG.

[013-105][Looks at her.] This? Is this the memento?

[013-106]HEDDA.

[013-107][Nodding slowly.] Do you recognise it? It was aimed at you once.

[013-108]LOVBORG.

[013-109]You should have used it then.

[013-110]HEDDA.

[013-111]Take it--and do you use it now.

[013-112]LOVBORG.

[013-113][Puts the pistol in his breast pocket.] Thanks!

[013-114]HEDDA.

[013-115]And beautifully, Eilert Lovborg. Promise me that!

[013-116]LOVBORG.

[013-117]Good-bye, [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda Gabler]]. [He goes out by the hall door.

[013-118][HEDDA listens for a moment at the door. Then she goes up to

[013-119]the writing-table, takes out the packet of manuscript, peeps

[013-120]under the cover, draws a few of the sheets half out, and

[013-121]looks at them. Next she goes over and seats herself in the

[013-122]arm-chair beside the stove, with the packet in her lap.

[013-123]Presently she opens the stove door, and then the packet.

[013-124]HEDDA.

[013-125][Throws one of the quires into the fire and whispers to herself.] Now I

[013-126]am burning your child, Thea!--Burning it, curly-locks! [Throwing one

[013-127]or two more quires into the stove.] Your child and Eilert Lovborg's.

[013-128][Throws the rest in.] I am burning--I am burning your child.

[013-129]ACT FOURTH.

[013-130]The same rooms at the TESMANS'. It is evening. The drawing-

[013-131]room is in darkness. The back room is lighted by the hanging

[013-132]lamp over the table. The curtains over the glass door are

[013-133]drawn close.

[013-134]HEDDA, dressed in black, walks to and fro in the dark room.

[013-135]Then she goes into the back room and disappears for a moment

[013-136]to the left. She is heard to strike a few chords on the

[013-137]piano. Presently she comes in sight again, and returns to

[013-138]the drawing-room.

[013-139]BERTA enters from the right, through the inner room, with a

[013-140]lighted lamp, which she places on the table in front of the

[013-141]corner settee in the drawing-room. Her eyes are red with

[013-142]weeping, and she has black ribbons in her cap. She goes

[013-143]quietly and circumspectly out to the right. HEDDA goes up

[013-144]to the glass door, lifts the curtain a little aside, and

[013-145]looks out into the darkness.

[013-146]Shortly afterwards, MISS TESMAN, in mourning, with a bonnet

[013-147]and veil on, comes in from the hall. HEDDA goes towards her

[013-148]and holds out her hand.

[013-149]MISS TESMAN.

[013-150]Yes, Hedda, here I am, in mourning and forlorn; for now my poor sister

[013-151]has at last found peace.

[013-152]HEDDA.

[013-153]I have heard the news already, as you see. Tesman sent me a card.

[013-154]MISS TESMAN.

[013-155]Yes, he promised me he would. But nevertheless I thought that to

[013-156]Hedda--here in the house of life--I ought myself to bring the tidings of

[013-157]death.

[013-158]HEDDA.

[013-159]That was very kind of you.

[013-160]MISS TESMAN.

[013-161]Ah, Rina ought not to have left us just now. This is not the time for

[013-162]Hedda's house to be a house of mourning.

[013-163]HEDDA.

[013-164][Changing the subject.] She died quite peacefully, did she not, Miss

[013-165]Tesman?

[013-166]MISS TESMAN.

[013-167]Oh, her end was so calm, so beautiful. And then she had the unspeakable

[013-168]happiness of seeing George once more--and bidding him good-bye.--Has he

[013-169]not come home yet?

[013-170]HEDDA.

[013-171]No. He wrote that he might be detained. But won't you sit down?

[013-172]MISS TESMAN.

[013-173]No thank you, my dear, dear Hedda. I should like to, but I have so much

[013-174]to do. I must prepare my dear one for her rest as well as I can. She

[013-175]shall go to her grave looking her best.

[013-176]HEDDA.

[013-177]Can I not help you in any way?

[013-178]MISS TESMAN.

[013-179]Oh, you must not think of it! Hedda Tesman must have no hand in such

[013-180]mournful work. Nor let her thought dwell on it either--not at this time.

[013-181]HEDDA.

[013-182]One is not always mistress of one's thoughts--

[013-183]MISS TESMAN.

[013-184][Continuing.] Ah yes, it is the way of the world. At home we shall be

[013-185]sewing a shroud; and here there will soon be sewing too, I suppose--but

[013-186]of another sort, thank God!

[013-187]GEORGE TESMAN enters by the hall door.

[013-188]HEDDA.

[013-189]Ah, you have come at last!

[013-190]TESMAN.

[013-191]You here, Aunt Julia? With Hedda? Fancy that!

[013-192]MISS TESMAN.

[013-193]I was just going, my dear boy. Well, have you done all you promised?

[013-194]TESMAN.

[013-195]No; I'm really afraid I have forgotten half of it. I must come to you

[013-196]again to-morrow. To-day my brain is all in a whirl. I can't keep my

[013-197]thoughts together.

[013-198]MISS TESMAN.

[013-199]Why, my dear George, you mustn't take it in this way.

[013-200]TESMAN.

[013-201]Mustn't--? How do you mean?

[013-202]MISS TESMAN.

[013-203]Even in your sorrow you must rejoice, as I do--rejoice that she is at

[013-204]rest.

[013-205]TESMAN.

[013-206]Oh yes, yes--you are thinking of [[Aunt-Rina|Aunt Rina]].

[013-207]HEDDA.

[013-208]You will feel lonely now, [[Aunt-Julia|Miss Tesman]].

[013-209]MISS TESMAN.

[013-210]Just at first, yes. But that will not last very long, I hope. I daresay

[013-211]I shall soon find an occupant for Rina's little room.

[013-212]TESMAN.

[013-213]Indeed? Who do you think will take it? Eh?

[013-214]MISS TESMAN.

[013-215]Oh, there's always some poor invalid or other in want of nursing,

[013-216]unfortunately.

[013-217]HEDDA.

[013-218]Would you really take such a burden upon you again?

[013-219]MISS TESMAN.

[013-220]A burden! Heaven forgive you, child--it has been no burden to me.

[013-221]HEDDA.

[013-222]But suppose you had a total stranger on your hands--

[013-223]MISS TESMAN.

[013-224]Oh, one soon makes friends with sick folk; and it's such an absolute

[013-225]necessity for me to have some one to live for. Well, heaven be praised,

[013-226]there may soon be something in this house, too, to keep an old aunt

[013-227]busy.

[013-228]HEDDA.

[013-229]Oh, don't trouble about anything here.

[013-230]TESMAN.

[013-231]Yes, just fancy what a nice time we three might have together, if--?

[013-232]HEDDA.

[013-233]If--?

[013-234]TESMAN.

[013-235][Uneasily.] Oh nothing. It will all come right. Let us hope so--eh?

[013-236]MISS TESMAN.

[013-237]Well well, I daresay you two want to talk to each other. [Smiling.] And

[013-238]perhaps Hedda may have something to tell you too, George. Good-bye! I

[013-239]must go home to Rina. [Turning at the door.] How strange it is to think

[013-240]that now Rina is with me and with my poor brother as well!

[013-241]TESMAN.

[013-242]Yes, fancy that, Aunt Julia! Eh?

[013-243][MISS TESMAN goes out by the hall door.

[013-244]HEDDA.

[013-245][Follows TESMAN coldly and searchingly with her eyes.] I almost believe

[013-246]your Aunt Rina's death affects you more than it does your Aunt Julia.

[013-247]TESMAN.

[013-248]Oh, it's not that alone. It's Eilert I am so terribly uneasy about.

[013-249]HEDDA.

[013-250][Quickly.] Is there anything new about him?

[013-251]TESMAN.

[013-252]I looked in at his rooms this afternoon, intending to tell him the

[013-253]manuscript was in safe keeping.

[013-254]HEDDA.

[013-255]Well, did you find him?

[013-256]TESMAN.

[013-257]No. He wasn't at home. But afterwards I met [[Mrs-Elvsted|Mrs. Elvsted]], and she told

[013-258]me that he had been here early this morning.

[013-259]HEDDA.

[013-260]Yes, directly after you had gone.

[013-261]TESMAN.

[013-262]And he said that he had torn his manuscript to pieces--eh?

[013-263]HEDDA.

[013-264]Yes, so he declared.

[013-265]TESMAN.

[013-266]Why, good heavens, he must have been completely out of his mind! And I

[013-267]suppose you thought it best not to give it back to him, Hedda?

[013-268]HEDDA.

[013-269]No, he did not get it.

[013-270]TESMAN.

[013-271]But of course you told him that we had it?

[013-272]HEDDA.

[013-273]No. [Quickly.] Did you tell Mrs. Elvsted?

[013-274]TESMAN.

[013-275]No; I thought I had better not. But you ought to have told him. Fancy,

[013-276]if, in desperation, he should go and do himself some injury! Let me have

[013-277]the manuscript, Hedda! I will take it to him at once. Where is it?

[013-278]HEDDA.

[013-279][Cold and immovable, leaning on the arm-chair.] I have not got it.

[013-280]TESMAN.

[013-281]Have not got it? What in the world do you mean?

[013-282]HEDDA.

[013-283]I have burnt it--every line of it.

[013-284]TESMAN.

[013-285][With a violent movement of terror.] Burnt! Burnt Eilert's manuscript!

[013-286]HEDDA.

[013-287]Don't scream so. The servant might hear you.

[013-288]TESMAN.

[013-289]Burnt! Why, good God--! No, no, no! It's impossible!

[013-290]HEDDA.

[013-291]It is so, nevertheless.

[013-292]TESMAN.

[013-293]Do you know what you have done, Hedda? It's unlawful appropriation of

[013-294]lost property. Fancy that! Just ask [[Judge-Brack|Judge Brack]], and he'll tell you what

[013-295]it is.

[013-296]HEDDA.

[013-297]I advise you not to speak of it--either to Judge Brack or to anyone

[013-298]else.

[013-299]TESMAN.

[013-300]But how could you do anything so unheard-of? What put it into your head?

[013-301]What possessed you? Answer me that--eh?

[013-302]HEDDA.

[013-303][Suppressing an almost imperceptible smile.] I did it for your sake,

[013-304]George.

[013-305]TESMAN.

[013-306]For my sake!

[013-307]HEDDA.

[013-308]This morning, when you told me about what he had read to you--
