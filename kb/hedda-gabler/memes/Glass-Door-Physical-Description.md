# Glass Door: Physical Description

The glass door in the [[Tesman-Villa]] drawing-room is a French window — glazed panels set in a door frame, opening directly onto a veranda or garden. Ibsen's stage directions describe it as "a glass door, also with curtains" (002-147), located on the "left wall" of the drawing-room. Through its panes, "part of a verandah outside, and trees covered with autumn foliage" are visible (002-142).

Its position on the left wall places it opposite the doorway to [[The-Inner-Room|the inner room]] at the back, creating the play's fundamental spatial axis: outside (glass door, garden) to drawing-room (social arena) to inner room (private chambers). This axis is also chronological — morning sun streams through the glass door in Act I; evening lamps replace it by Act IV.

The curtains over the glass door are equally important. Unlike the inner room doorway (which has only a curtain, no solid closure), the glass door has both — a door that can be opened and closed, and curtains that can be drawn or opened. [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] manipulates both, controlling not just who may pass but what may be seen.

> On the opposite wall, on the left, a glass door, also with curtains... and trees covered with autumn foliage. An oval table, with a cover on it, surrounded by chairs.
>
> (002-147)

The autumn foliage visible through the glass door is a deliberate seasonal marker. Ibsen sets the play in September, a season of decline; the trees "covered with autumn foliage" (002-142) are beautiful but dying. The view through the glass door is always a view of a world in recession.
