# Judge Brack — The Triangle

Brack's ambition is explicitly and repeatedly stated. His metaphor:

> "A cock of the walk needs a hareem" — but as a bachelor, he modifies the model: "All I need is one trusted and congenial friend who can come and go freely."

The "triangle" is the play's most sinister image: a closed system of knowledge and control from which [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] cannot escape. Brack doesn't want to replace [[George-Tesman|Tesman]]; he wants to add himself. Tesman provides the domestic cover; Brack provides the "entertainment"; Hedda provides the captive audience.

The triangle is respectability's shadow — the arrangement that preserves appearances while destroying their meaning. It is Ibsen's sharpest critique of bourgeois marriage as a system that tolerates covert exploitation so long as the surface remains intact.

Brack's genius is to recognize that he doesn't need to defeat Tesman or seduce [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]]. He only needs to be present — the trusted friend with keys to every door — and let the structure of provincial life do the rest. The triangle is power made architectural.
