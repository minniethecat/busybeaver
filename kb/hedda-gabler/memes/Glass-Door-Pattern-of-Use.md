# Glass Door: Pattern of Use

The glass door's state — open or closed, curtained or exposed — changes across every act, and each configuration maps [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]]'s psychological condition.

**Act I** opens with curtains drawn back and morning sun shining through. [[Berta]] "throws open the glass door" (002-183) in her first action, establishing the door as a site of tension between domestic enclosure and the outside world. Hedda immediately complains about the sunlight (004-148) — her first words about the door are a rejection of its openness.

**Act II** finds Hedda "at the glass door, impatiently" (004-148), waiting for [[Eilert-Lovborg|Lovborg]]. She leaves it, walks away, returns — a choreography of restless desire. Later, she stands "by the open glass door, loading a revolver" and fires into the garden (007-039). [[Judge-Brack|Brack]] enters through the same door moments later; she closes it after him (007-077).


> "stands by the open glass door, loading a revolver. The fellow to it lies in an open pistol-case on the writing-table."
>
> (007-039–007-041)

**Act III** shows Hedda "at the glass door, drumming on the pane" (008-316). The curtains are open but she cannot pass through — the threshold is visual, not traversable. She "draws back the curtains" at another moment (011-090), adjusting the light to suit her mood.

**Act IV** ends with "the curtains over the glass door drawn" and evening lamps lit (013-132). The door is closed, the curtains are drawn, the world is shut out. This final configuration — permanent enclosure — is the spatial equivalent of Hedda's suicide minutes later: there is no longer any outside to reach.
