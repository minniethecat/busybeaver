# Sheriff Elvsted and Lovborg

The Sheriff's household is the site of the intellectual partnership between [[Mrs-Elvsted|Thea]] and [[Eilert-Lovborg|Lovborg]] — a relationship conducted literally under the Sheriff's roof. Lovborg served as tutor to the Sheriff's children, and during this time he and Thea developed their collaborative working method.

The domestic arrangement is richly ironic: the Sheriff employs Lovborg to educate his children, and Lovborg instead educates (and is educated by) the Sheriff's wife. The intellectual "child" — [[The-Manuscript|the manuscript]] — is conceived in the Sheriff's own house, under his unknowing patronage.

The absent patriarch funds his own cuckolding — not sexually (the play is ambiguous on this point) but intellectually, which Ibsen presents as the more significant betrayal. What Thea and Lovborg create together is a work of genuine originality, produced in the house of a man who has no idea what intellectual creation even means.

The Sheriff's obliviousness is the condition of possibility for the play's central creative act. Without his neglect, Thea never sits with Lovborg organizing notes and discussing ideas. The manuscript that threatens Tesman's career, awakens Hedda's jealousy, and drives the play's catastrophe — all of it originates in the gap the Sheriff left when he went "to town a good deal" (006-188).
