# Hedda's Suicide as Refusal

Hedda's suicide is, before anything else, her refusal of [[Judge-Brack|Brack]]'s triangle. She cannot undo what he knows. She cannot stop him from using it. She cannot endure being "in his power." The temple-shot is her only available exit — and it is, crucially, an exit that Brack cannot follow.

His final line — "People don't do such things!" (015-242) — is not grief, not shock, not even particularly moral. It is the protest of a player whose game has been interrupted by a move outside the rules. Brack understood everything about [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] except her capacity to destroy herself rather than accept his terms.

> Brack's closing words — "People don't do such things!" (015-242) — function as the play's moral punctuation. What shocks bourgeois society is not cruelty, manipulation, or blackmail, but the violation of its unspoken rules. Hedda's suicide is not a tragedy to Brack; it is a social embarrassment.

The triangle breaks because one of its points removes itself. In Ibsen's design, this is the only victory available to Hedda: a refusal so absolute it invalidates the game entirely.
