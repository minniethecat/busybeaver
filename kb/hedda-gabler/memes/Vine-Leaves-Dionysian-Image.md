# Vine Leaves: The Dionysian Image

"Vine leaves in his hair" (009-185) is an unmistakably classical reference. In Greek and Roman iconography, vine leaves crown Dionysus/Bacchus, the god of wine, ecstasy, theater, and dissolution. The Dionysian represents the abandonment of rational control, the ecstatic merging of self with something larger, the triumph of intensity over restraint.

For [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]], the vine leaves are not about drunkenness — they are about transfiguration. She wants [[Eilert-Lovborg|Lovborg]] transformed by intensity into something beautiful, to transcend the respectable categories that imprison her. The vine leaves are the opposite of [[Respectability|respectability]], of the [[Tesman-Villa|Tesman villa]], of everything bourgeois and correct.

Dionysus is also the god of theater — the one divine power that rules the stage. Hedda's fantasy of vine leaves is thus doubly theatrical: she imagines Lovborg as a performer in a drama of her own scripting, crowned for a role she has assigned him. The Dionysian ecstasy she desires is staged ecstasy, performed ecstasy, ecstasy at a safe distance.

The vine leaves have no place in the drawing room of the Tesman villa. They belong to a world of myth and ritual, of sacred intoxication and beautiful violence — a world that cannot coexist with mortgage payments, morning calls, and porcelain whatnots. Hedda's tragedy is that she can imagine this world perfectly, and she is separated from it by the glass door she cannot open.

## From the Text

The vine-leaves image was not invented for *Hedda Gabler* — it recurs across Ibsen's work, always carrying the same Dionysian charge:

> Some critics have been greatly troubled as to the precise meaning of Hedda's fantastic vision of Lovborg "with vine-leaves in his hair." Surely this is a very obvious image or symbol of the beautiful, the ideal, aspect of bacchic elation and revelry. Antique art, or I am much mistaken, shows us many figures of Dionysus himself and his followers with vine-leaves entwined their hair. To Ibsen's mind, at any rate, the image had long been familiar. In *Peer Gynt*, when Peer, having carried off Anitra, finds himself in a particularly festive mood, he cries: "Were there vine-leaves around, I would garland my brow."
