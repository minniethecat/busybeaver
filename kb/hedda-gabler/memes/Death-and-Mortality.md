# Death and Mortality

Death saturates *Hedda Gabler* from its first reference to the late General Gabler to its final gunshot. Three characters die over the course of the action — [[Aunt-Rina]], [[Eilert-Lovborg]], and [[Hedda-Gabler]] — and their deaths form a triptych that maps the play's moral and aesthetic universe: the natural death, the scandalous death, and the aesthetic death.

## The Triptych of Deaths

### Aunt Rina: The Natural Death

[[Aunt-Rina]] dies offstage, in bed, attended by [[Aunt-Julia]], between Act III and Act IV. Her death is natural, expected, and domestic — the kind of death that poses no philosophical problems and leaves no unanswered questions. [[George-Tesman|Tesman]] receives the news with appropriate sadness and immediately returns to his manuscript-reconstruction with [[Mrs-Elvsted|Thea]].

Hedda's response to Rina's death is revealing: "What a release for her" (014-025). This is not callousness so much as aesthetic boredom — Rina's death is too ordinary to be interesting. It is the death that happens when nothing dramatic happens.

### Eilert Lovborg: The Scandalous Death

[[Eilert-Lovborg|Lovborg]]'s death is Rina's opposite in every particular: violent, public, scandalous, and deeply ambiguous. He dies in [[Mademoiselle-Dianas]], shot in the stomach, in what may have been a brawl over a nonexistent purse. [[Judge-Brack|Brack]]'s clinical report — "The shot has hit him in a vital part ... the stomach" (014-106) — emphasizes the death's ugliness.

### Hedda Gabler: The Aesthetic Death

Hedda's suicide is her attempt to achieve the beautiful death she demanded of Lovborg. The temple-shot, the piano playing the wild dance, the inner room — every detail is orchestrated for maximum dramatic effect. Her death answers Lovborg's failure with aesthetic success, and Rina's naturalness with theatrical intensity.

But the victory is pyrrhic — it costs her life, and the witnesses cannot comprehend what they have witnessed.

## Hedda's Thanatological Aesthetics

[[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] judges deaths as she judges everything else: aesthetically. The criteria for a beautiful death:

- **Voluntary**: The death must be chosen, not suffered

- **Clean**: The temple, not the gut — precision over mess

- **Witnessed**: The death must have an audience, even if that audience doesn't understand what it sees

- **Meaningful**: The death must be a statement, not an accident

## Death and the Pistols

The [[Heddas-Pistols|Hedda's pistols]] are the instrument that links all three deaths — Lovborg's death is caused by one of them (returned to Hedda with the fatal shot fired), and Hedda's death is achieved by the other. The pistols, inherited from General Gabler, carry the death-drive through the play: objects from a military past that find their purpose only in destruction.

## Thematic Significance

Death in *Hedda Gabler* is the limit case of the play's central concerns:

- **Freedom**: Death is the only absolute freedom available to Hedda. Brack can control her life; he cannot control her death.

- **Beauty**: The beautiful death is Hedda's ultimate aesthetic project — her only successful creation.

- **Class**: The manner of death reflects class identity. Rina's bourgeois, domestic death; Lovborg's proletarian, scandalous death; Hedda's aristocratic, theatrical death — each dies according to their social position.

- **Gender**: Only Hedda's death is chosen. Rina dies naturally; Lovborg dies accidentally; Hedda alone seizes death as an act of will.
