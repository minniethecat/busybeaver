# The Epilogue Question

*Hedda Gabler* ends with a question that it refuses to answer: what happens after the curtain falls on Hedda's corpse, the card game, and the reconstruction of Lovborg's manuscript?

## The Reconstructed Manuscript

Tesman and Thea are rebuilding Lovborg's manuscript from his notes and Thea's memory (015-050 to 015-080). The project is absurd — two people who barely understand the work attempting to reconstruct a genius's magnum opus from fragments — but Ibsen presents it with surprising tenderness. The manuscript may survive its author, not through individual inspiration but through communal effort.

## The Unanswered Questions

The play leaves multiple threads unresolved: Does Tesman ever learn the full truth about Hedda's role in Lovborg's death? What becomes of Brack's knowledge — does he expose her, or does the scandal die with her? Does Thea stay with Tesman? The refusal to answer is deliberate: Ibsen understood that resolution is not truth, and that the most honest ending is the one that leaves the audience unsettled.

## Critical Debate

Critics divide sharply on the ending's implications. For some, the manuscript reconstruction represents genuine hope — creation outlasting destruction, community outlasting isolation. For others, it is deeply ironic — Tesman and Thea, the play's two most limited characters, attempting to reconstruct a work they could never have created. The debate reflects a deeper disagreement about the play's ultimate moral vision.

## The Card Game's Persistence

That the card game continues after the suicide is Ibsen's final comment on the relationship between tragedy and ordinary life. The world does not stop for tragedy; the card game goes on; the manuscript gets rebuilt. Whether this is consoling or devastating depends on the production and the audience.
