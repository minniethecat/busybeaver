"Holm" is the pseudonym used by Dr. Georg Brandes in his biographical account of the origins of *Hedda Gabler*. Brandes describes him as a man of letters who was an enthusiastic admirer of a young Viennese woman — the same woman Ibsen met at Gossensass and who contributed traits to the character of Hedda. (001-166)

## The Holm Anecdote

According to Brandes's account, Holm was so devoted to the young woman that he carried her photograph with him and had presented it to Ibsen. (001-170) Some time later, Holm appeared at Ibsen's rooms in Munich. (001-175) During a carouse, Holm lost the manuscript of a work he had been writing, an incident that may have contributed to the atmosphere of creative destruction that pervades the play. (001-184)

> Ibsen was astonished to receive, in Munich, a parcel addressed from Berlin by this young man, containing, without a word of explanation, a packet of his (Ibsen's) letters, and a photograph which he had presented to Holm. Ibsen brooded and brooded over the incident, and at last came to the conclusion that the young man had intended to return her letters and photograph to a young lady.
>
> (001-168–001-171)

## Later Contact

Ibsen later received a postal packet from Holm, suggesting their acquaintance extended beyond the initial Munich encounter. (002-001)
