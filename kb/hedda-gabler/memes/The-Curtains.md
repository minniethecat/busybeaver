# The Curtains

The curtains of the [[Tesman-Villa]] — over [[The-Glass-Door|the glass door]] and across the doorway to [[The-Inner-Room|the inner room]] — are among the most frequently manipulated objects in the play. [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] draws them, opens them, stands beside them — her relationship to the curtains is a physical register of her psychological state.

For the two sets of curtains and their function as permeable barriers, see [[Curtains-Location-and-Function]]. An act-by-act account of Hedda's obsessive manipulation appears in [[Curtains-Heddas-Manipulation]].

[[Curtains-and-Brack]] examines how [[Judge-Brack|Brack]] threatens to tear down the curtains entirely, revealing his function as the agent of exposure. [[Curtains-Meta-Theatrical]] explores the curtains' meta-theatrical dimension: the curtain on stage as the boundary between drama and reality.

The curtains are Hedda's most direct expression of agency — and their limitation (they cannot become walls) is the limitation of her power. When Brack promises "In future I shall be invisible," he declares the end of the curtained boundary between private and public.
