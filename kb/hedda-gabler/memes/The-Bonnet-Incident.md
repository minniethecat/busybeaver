# The Bonnet Incident

In Act I, Aunt Julia arrives wearing a new bonnet purchased to impress her nephew's new wife. Hedda pretends to mistake it for the servant's cap (001-050). The cruelty is small, casual, and devastating — and it establishes the template for every subsequent act of destruction in the play.

## The Scene

Aunt Julia enters in her new bonnet, purchased specifically for this visit. She is proud of it — it represents her best attempt at respectability, her offering to the new household. Hedda, seeing it from across the room, affects not to recognize it: "What is that? The servant's cap?" (001-050). The "mistake" is deliberate and calibrated for maximum wound.

## The Cruelty

The cruelty of the incident operates on multiple levels. Socially, it reduces Julia to servant status — a devastating slight to a woman whose identity depends on bourgeois respectability. Aesthetically, it dismisses Julia's taste as indistinguishable from a servant's. Personally, it wounds Julia where she is most vulnerable: her desire for Hedda's approval.

## Dramatic Function

The bonnet incident is the play's first demonstration of Hedda's method. She does not confront or attack directly; she pretends not to recognize, not to understand, not to intend harm. This plausible deniability — "I didn't mean it" — is her signature technique, and it will recur in progressively more destructive forms throughout the play.

## Template for Destruction

Every subsequent act of destruction follows the bonnet incident's pattern: an apparent triviality that carries disproportionate weight, a victim who cannot effectively respond, a perpetrator who remains technically innocent. The burning of the manuscript, the gift of the pistol, the suicide itself — all elaborate the template established in this one small, cruel moment.

## From the Text

> TESMAN. [With the bonnet in his hand, looks at it from all sides.] Why, what
> a gorgeous bonnet you've been investing in!
>
> MISS TESMAN. I bought it on Hedda's account.
>
> TESMAN. On Hedda's account? Eh?
>
> MISS TESMAN. Yes, so that Hedda needn't be ashamed of me if we happened to
> go out together.
>
> TESMAN. [Patting her cheek.] You always think of everything, Aunt Julia.
> [Lays the bonnet on a chair beside the table.]
>
> (003-102 to 003-119)
