# Bourgeois Domesticity in Hedda Gabler

*Hedda Gabler* is among Ibsen's most incisive critiques of bourgeois domestic life. The Tesman villa, with its drawing room rituals and morning visits, is presented not as a home but as a mechanism of social control.

## The Villa as Institution

The Tesman villa is purchased on credit — mortgaged against Aunt Julia's annuity, furnished beyond the couple's means, maintained through social pretense. From its opening scene, the villa functions as an institution rather than a home: visitors are announced, rituals observed, appearances maintained. The domestic space is a theater of respectability, and Hedda is its reluctant performer.

## The Rituals of Visiting

Morning visits, afternoon calls, evening gatherings — the play is structured around the rituals of bourgeois sociability. Aunt Julia's morning visits (001-045), Brack's informal calls (006-030), the bachelor party at his lodgings — each social event is a performance of class membership that simultaneously reinforces the class's constraints.

## The Servant Economy

[[Berta]]'s presence is a constant reminder of the class relations that underpin bourgeois domesticity. She serves, observes, and endures — her labor making possible the leisure of the drawing room. The play never allows us to forget that the aesthetic world Hedda despises is built on practical foundations she never acknowledges.

## Domesticity as Entrapment

For Hedda, domesticity is entrapment in its purest form. The villa's rooms are cages; its rituals are performances; its inhabitants are jailers. Her suicide is her only exit from a domestic order that demands her submission. The play's final image — Brack's incomprehension, Tesman's absorption, the card game continuing — is the domestic order reasserting itself after tragedy, as if nothing had happened.
