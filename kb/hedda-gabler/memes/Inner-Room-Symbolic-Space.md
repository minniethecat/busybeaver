# Inner Room: Symbolic Space

The inner room of the [[Tesman-Villa]] operates as the symbolic antithesis of the drawing-room, encoding the play's moral structure in spatial terms. Where the drawing-room is the arena of social performance, [[Respectability|respectability]], and exposure to the [[The-Glass-Door|glass door]]'s sunlight, the inner room is the zone of retreat, secrecy, and hidden acts.

This binary operates along multiple axes. Socially, the drawing-room hosts manners and public conversation; the inner room shelters conspiracy and punch-drinking masculine enclaves. Morally, the drawing-room preserves appearances; the inner room conceals the burning of [[The-Manuscript|the manuscript]]. In terms of action, the drawing-room is where events are discussed and denied; the inner room is where they are actually done.

The spatial binary is also gendered. The drawing-room is nominally [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]]'s domain as mistress of the house, yet she constantly defers to the conventions governing it. The inner room, by contrast, is where Tesman and [[Judge-Brack|Brack]] form their masculine alliance over punch — and where Hedda retreats only when she acts destructively. Neither space is fully her own.

The curtained doorway between the two rooms is the permeable membrane across which these oppositions constantly threaten to collapse. When Brack's knowledge of the [[Heddas-Pistols|pistols]] threatens to expose the inner room's secrets, the boundary between private and public dissolves. The inner room's symbolic function is ultimately tragic: it offers the illusion of concealment in a world where every secret is eventually known.
