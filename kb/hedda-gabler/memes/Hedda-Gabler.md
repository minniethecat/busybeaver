# Hedda Gabler

The protagonist of Henrik Ibsen's 1890 play — a general's daughter whose aristocratic pride and pathological fear of scandal drive her to manipulate, destroy, and ultimately take her own life. Ibsen described her as "rather her father's daughter than her husband's wife" (001-025), a woman shaped by military discipline and social convention into a creature of restless, destructive boredom. She is both the play's centrifugal force and its central mystery: a woman capable of anything except living.

Hedda is trapped between aristocratic individualism and bourgeois domesticity in late 19th-century Norway. She marries the scholarly George Tesman out of convenience, despises the ordinary, and channels her thwarted energies into manipulation. She lures her former intimate Eilert Lovborg back to drinking, burns his manuscript in [[The-Stove|the stove]], gives him one of [[Heddas-Pistols|General Gabler's pistols]], and — when Judge Brack threatens to expose her complicity — shoots herself in the temple.

Her "hyperaesthesia" (002-079) makes her incapable of tolerating ordinary life; her tragedy is that of a woman bred for command who finds herself trapped in a world demanding submission. The introduction captures the play's moral core: "Where Thea Elvsted dared to venture all, Hedda, in her cowardice, only hankered and refrained" (002-086). Her suicide is both victory and defeat — a final aesthetic gesture arranged with the same cold control she exerted over every other scene.


> The title of the play is _Hedda Gabler_. My intention in giving it this name was to indicate that Hedda, as a personality, is to be regarded rather as her father's daughter than as her husband's wife. It was not my desire to deal in this play with so-called problems. What I principally wanted to do was to depict human beings, human emotions, and human destinies, upon a groundwork of certain of the social conditions and principles of the present day.
>
> (001-024)
