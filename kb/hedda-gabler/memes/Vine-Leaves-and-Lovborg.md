# Vine Leaves and Lovborg

The vine leaves fantasy depends on a specific version of [[Eilert-Lovborg|Lovborg]]: the unreformed man, the Lovborg of "storm and stress," who existed before [[Mrs-Elvsted|Thea]] "inspired" him into respectability. The reformed Lovborg — the one writing the "new book," refusing punch, planning a future with Thea — has lost his vine leaves.

Hedda's project in Act II is to un-reform Lovborg. She strips away Thea's influence through insinuation and manipulation: questioning Thea's faith in him, suggesting that Thea fears scandal, planting the seed of doubt about his ability to resist drink. Her goal is not his destruction but his restoration — the recovery of the dangerous, beautiful man she remembers from their earlier intimacy.

She succeeds. She sends him to [[The-Bachelor-Party|the bachelor party]], where he drinks, fights, and loses [[The-Manuscript|the manuscript]]. But the result is not beauty. It is ugliness: a brawl in [[Mademoiselle-Dianas|a brothel]], a shot in the gut, a body in the morgue. The unreformed Lovborg is not a Dionysian hero but a drunk dying in a boudoir.

> Ibsen's fantastic vision of Lovborg "with vine-leaves in his hair."
>
> (002-018)

The gap between the fantasy and the reality is the play's judgment on Hedda's aesthetic project. Vine leaves in the hair require a stage, a script, and a controlled performance. The actual world — the world beyond the drawing room's [[The-Glass-Door|glass door]] — does not cooperate. Lovborg cannot be managed into beauty any more than Hedda can be managed into happiness. The vine leaves fantasy, confronted with reality, produces not transfiguration but degradation.
