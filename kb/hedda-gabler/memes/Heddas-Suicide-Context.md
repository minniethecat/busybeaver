# Hedda's Suicide: The Immediate Context

By the final scene of Act IV, [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]]'s position has collapsed to a single, inescapable point. Every avenue she has navigated throughout the play has closed. The immediate context makes her suicide not just plausible but, in the play's own logic, inevitable.

First, [[Judge-Brack|Brack]] knows she gave [[Eilert-Lovborg|Lovborg]] the pistol — and that the pistol killed him. His knowledge is not a suspicion but a certainty, traceable through the police report from [[Mademoiselle-Dianas]] (012-121). Second, Lovborg died not beautifully (as she demanded) but in a brawl, shot in the stomach (014-106). The aesthetic death she orchestrated has been betrayed by reality.

Third, Brack has made his threat explicit: "the only possible way out" is the triangle — his permanent presence, his permanent knowledge, his permanent power (015-100). He does not demand; he "offers." And the offer is indistinguishable from a cage.

> Now that you are the one cock in the basket--
>
> (015-230)

Fourth, [[George-Tesman|Tesman]] and [[Mrs-Elvsted|Thea]] have retreated into the inner room to reconstruct [[The-Manuscript|Lovborg's manuscript]] — the creative work that will replace the one Hedda burned.

Hedda is alone in the drawing room with Brack. He has offered her a choice: the triangle or exposure. She has refused the triangle. Now she needs a third option, and the pistol in the inner room is the only one the play provides. The context is complete: every other door has closed.
