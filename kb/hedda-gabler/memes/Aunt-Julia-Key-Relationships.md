# Aunt Julia — Key Relationships

Aunt Julia's relationships form the moral infrastructure of the Tesman household — a web of love and obligation that stands in opposition to Hedda's transactional world.

**George Tesman** (nephew): George is Aunt Julia's "only comfort in the world" — she raised him, financed his education, and now mortgages her future for his home. He loves her genuinely but is oblivious to her sacrifices. The asymmetry between her giving and his receiving is never acknowledged by either of them.

> TESMAN. [In the doorway between the rooms.] Aunt Julia! Dear Aunt Julia! [Goes up to her and shakes hands warmly.] Come all this way — so early! Eh? MISS TESMAN. Why, of course I had to come and see how you were getting on.
>
> (003-062–003-064)

**Hedda Gabler** (niece-by-marriage): Hedda treats Aunt Julia with barely concealed contempt, mocking her bonnet, her morning calls, and her bourgeois sentimentality. Aunt Julia, for her part, is bewildered but never bitter. She cannot understand Hedda's cruelty because she cannot imagine the world from which it comes.

**Aunt Rina** (sister): The invalid sister whom Aunt Julia nurses. Rina's illness defines Julia's daily life and shapes her moral character. The devotion Julia shows Rina is the model for all her other relationships — care that asks nothing in return.

**Berta** (servant): Aunt Julia's old servant, now in the Tesman household. Their shared history and mutual loyalty represent a world of stable domestic relations that Hedda cannot comprehend. Berta's transfer to the Tesman household carries Aunt Julia's values with her.

These relationships define a moral economy based on love and obligation rather than power and appearance. Aunt Julia has sacrificed her own comfort for others' advancement — a fact Hedda finds "grotesque" rather than noble. The tragedy is that Hedda cannot value what Aunt Julia represents, and cannot escape needing it.
