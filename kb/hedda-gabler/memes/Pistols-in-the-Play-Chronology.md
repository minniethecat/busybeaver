# Pistols in the Play: A Chronology

The pistols appear at four crucial moments, each marking a shift in power relations.

In Act I, Hedda fires a warning shot at [[Judge-Brack|Brack]] as he approaches through the garden (007-020). It is a "joke" that establishes her appetite for threat, her disregard for convention, and — crucially — Brack's immunity. His response — "General Gabler's pistols" (007-025) and "People don't do such things!" — frames the weapons as identity and frames the line that will return, transfigured, as the play's final words.

In Act III, Hedda gives [[Eilert-Lovborg|Lovborg]] one pistol (013-103), urging him to "die beautifully" with "vine-leaves in his hair." She imagines orchestrating his death as an aesthetic event — her father's weapon, her direction, her control. But the pistol passes beyond her control the moment it leaves her hand.

> Eilert Lovborg--listen to me.--Will you not try to--to do it beautifully?
>
> (013-088)

Lovborg dies not beautifully but squalidly — in [[Mademoiselle-Dianas]], shot in the chest, in a brawl. The pistol meant for tragic suicide becomes the instrument of a sordid killing. Brack's question — "since, to see whether both the pistols are there?" (015-106) — transforms the weapon from symbol to evidence.

In Act IV, Hedda uses the remaining pistol to shoot herself in the temple — beneath her father's portrait, in [[The-Inner-Room|the inner room]]. She dies as Gabler. Brack's closing line — "People don't do such things!" — echoes his Act I protest, but the register has shifted from comedy to horrified incomprehension. The trajectory is complete: from threat to gift to evidence to self-destruction.
