# Hedda's Terror of Scandal

[[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]]'s defining characteristic — more fundamental than her boredom, her cruelty, or her aestheticism — is her terror of scandal. This fear explains nearly every major choice she has made.

She married [[George-Tesman|Tesman]] rather than [[Eilert-Lovborg|Lovborg]] because Tesman was safe, respectable, unlikely to generate scandal. Lovborg was danger — exciting but socially toxic. The choice was not between love and security but between exposure and safety, and Hedda chose safety every time.

> HEDDA.
> Because I have such a dread of scandal.
>
> (009-307)

She never openly defies convention. Her manipulations are conducted from behind [[The-Curtains|the curtain]], never in the spotlight. Open rebellion invites exposure, and exposure is what she cannot risk. Her entire performance of respectable wifehood — the villa, the social calls, the proper receptions — is a shield against the gaze of "people."

[[Judge-Brack|Brack]]'s threat is so devastating because it removes the curtain entirely — not by exposing her (yet), but by making clear that exposure is always possible. The "triangle" he constructs is a ménage à trois of knowledge: Hedda, Brack, and the secret they share. As long as Hedda accepts the triangle, the secret stays between them. The moment she resists, the triangle collapses into public exposure.

Her terror is not of punishment but of being *seen* — of the gap between her performed respectability and her actual desires becoming visible. The respectable Hedda Gabler, General Gabler's daughter, cannot be revealed as a woman who gave a pistol to her former lover and urged him to suicide. The pistol that killed Lovborg can be traced to her; Brack knows this; and the fact that he knows it — and she knows he knows — is the cage she cannot escape except through death.
