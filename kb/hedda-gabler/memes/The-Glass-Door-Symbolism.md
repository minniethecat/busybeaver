# The Glass Door Symbolism

The glass door leading from the drawing room to the garden is the play's most significant architectural feature. It is simultaneously a threshold and a barrier — transparent yet impassable, promising freedom while enforcing enclosure.

## Physical Description

Ibsen specifies a glass door that opens onto the garden (001-001). The glass is significant: unlike a solid door that conceals, the glass door reveals what lies beyond while preventing access to it. Characters can see the garden, the sky, the outside world — but the door mediates every passage between interior and exterior.

## Dramatic Use

Characters enter and exit through the glass door throughout the play. Brack uses it for his informal visits, arriving from the garden rather than the front door (006-030). Lovborg departs through it for the bachelor party. The door's use maps social relationships: formal visitors use the front entrance; intimates (or those claiming intimacy) use the glass door.

## The Garden Beyond

The garden represents the outside world — nature, freedom, the space beyond domestic enclosure. Hedda never enters it. She moves between drawing room and inner room but never crosses the threshold to the garden. The glass door shows her what she cannot reach: the fresh air, the open sky, the life outside the villa's walls.

## Symbolic Function

The glass door's transparency is crueler than opacity would be. Hedda can see freedom but cannot reach it; she can see the world but cannot join it. The door's glass is the material equivalent of her psychological condition — trapped within sight of escape, enclosed within sight of openness.
