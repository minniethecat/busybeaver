# Mademoiselle Diana — Dramatic Function

Diana functions as the keeper of the threshold between the respectable and the unrespectable. Her establishment — "Mademoiselle Diana's boudoir" (014-362) — is the physical location where the play's two worlds collide: the world of drawing-rooms, professorships, and morning calls, and the world of drinking, brawling, and death.

[[Eilert-Lovborg|Lovborg]] moves from one to the other in a single night, and Diana's boudoir is the point of no return. His trajectory — from polished guest at the [[Tesman-Villa]] to dead body at a brothel — traces a collapse that Diana's establishment makes geographically concrete.

She also serves as [[Judge-Brack|Brack]]'s instrument of control. It is Brack who reports the circumstances of Lovborg's death — using his privileged knowledge of Diana's establishment to demonstrate his mastery over the shadow-world. "This same Mademoiselle Diana" (015-149) is both a person and a piece of information: Brack knows about her, Hedda didn't, and that asymmetry of knowledge is power.

Brack's ability to move between the two worlds — visiting the Tesman drawing-room by day and Diana's boudoir by night — makes him the play's most dangerous character. He knows both spheres intimately; he controls the flow of information between them. Diana is the figure who makes that control possible, the keeper of a world that Brack can access and Hedda cannot.
