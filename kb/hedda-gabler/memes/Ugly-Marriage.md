# The Ugly Marriage

[[George-Tesman|Tesman]] offends [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]]'s aesthetic sensibility in every particular. The marriage is not just unhappy — it is, by Hedda's own exacting standards, ugly. And that ugliness is the source of her cruelty.

Tesman's scholarship is the antithesis of beauty: "medieval domestic crafts" (006-075), footnotes, file-cards, archival drudgery. His intellectual labor produces information, not insight — the accumulation of "little atoms of knowledge" (002-058) that form no coherent vision. For Hedda, whose aesthetic code values intensity and danger, this plodding industry is contemptible.

> It will deal with the domestic industries of Brabant during the Middle Ages.
>
> (004-002)

His physical description compounds the offense. Ibsen specifies Tesman as "rather stout," with a "round, open, cheerful face" — the physique of comfort, not of danger. He contrasts absolutely with the lean, reckless beauty of [[Eilert-Lovborg|Lovborg]], the man with "vine-leaves in his hair" (009-185). Tesman's body is the body of the drawing room, not the storm.

His pleasures are equally offensive: the villa, the domestic comfort, the prospect of a professorship, the devotion of his aunts. All of this is existentially ugly to Hedda — not because it is evil, but because it is small. Her cruelty to [[Aunt-Julia]] — pretending to mistake her new bonnet for the servant's (001-058) — is an aesthetic crime: punishing ugliness with humiliation.

The ugly marriage is not a matter of personal incompatibility. It is structural: Hedda married for security, and security is the opposite of beauty. The [[Tesman-Villa|villa]], the annuity, the professorship — every material benefit of the marriage is an aesthetic affront.
