# Pistols as Antidote

[[Heddas-Pistols|Hedda's pistols]] are the physical manifestation of her rebellion against boredom. Inherited from [[General-Gablers-Shadow|General Gabler]], they are objects from her past life of intensity, riding, and masculine freedom. When she handles them, she accesses a self that existed before the boredom of marriage swallowed her.

"I have one bent, at any rate ... shooting." (008-193) This line, delivered with the pistol in her hand, is loaded with symbolic weight. The pistol is the opposite of the knitting, the footnotes, the tea-cups. It is danger, beauty, and death — everything boredom is not. In a life stripped of intensity, the pistol is the only object that promises intensity on demand.

[[Berta]] reports that Hedda spent the honeymoon "practising pistol-shooting every day" — the newlywed practicing marksmanship while her husband researches medieval crafts. The image is a compressed portrait of the marriage: two people occupying the same space but living in different worlds.

But the pistols are an antidote that is also a poison. They offer the illusion of agency — the power to threaten, to intimidate, to kill — but every use of them reduces Hedda's actual freedom. The warning shot at [[Judge-Brack|Brack]] establishes his immunity. The gift to [[Eilert-Lovborg|Lovborg]] produces evidence that Brack can use against her. The temple-shot in Act IV is the only exit they can provide, and it is an exit from life itself.

The pistols promise intensity but deliver only death. They are the perfect symbol of Hedda's predicament: the only cure for her boredom is the one thing that also destroys her.
