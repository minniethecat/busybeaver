# The Scandal Motif

Scandal is the gravitational center around which every character in *Hedda Gabler* orbits. The fear of public exposure — of "what people will say" — drives the play's action more powerfully than any explicit motivation.

## Hedda's Terror

Hedda's defining fear is scandal. "What will people say?" is, as [[Judge-Brack|Brack]] observes, her true master (010-091). She can contemplate Lovborg's death, burn a manuscript, manipulate multiple lives — but the thought of public exposure paralyzes her. Her aristocratic pride is not moral but aesthetic: scandal is ugly, and ugliness is intolerable.

## Scandal as Social Currency

In the play's economy, scandal functions as a currency that Brack accumulates and deploys. He trades in the threat of exposure, converting knowledge of Hedda's pistol into power over her person. The scandal he threatens is specific — complicity in Lovborg's death, ownership of the weapon — but its power is general. Once Brack controls the scandal narrative, he controls Hedda.

## The "Beautiful Death" as Scandal Antidote

Hedda's obsession with a "beautiful death" can be read as a fantasy of scandal-free suicide. Lovborg's actual death — squalid, in a brothel, stomach wound — is scandal incarnate. Her own suicide — temple-shot, piano music, controlled setting — is an attempt to die without scandal, to exit the play on her own aesthetic terms rather than Brack's.

## Scandal as Structural Principle

Ibsen uses scandal as a structural principle: the play moves from the threat of scandal (Lovborg's return to respectable society) through the eruption of scandal (the death, the investigation) to Hedda's attempt to escape scandal through suicide. The final line — "People don't do such things!" (015-245) — is the voice of scandal itself, shocked that anyone would defy its jurisdiction.
