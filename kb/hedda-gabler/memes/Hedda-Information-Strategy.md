# Hedda's Information Strategy

[[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] is not a passive victim of the villa's information economy — she attempts to play the game herself, with strategies that mirror [[Judge-Brack|Brack]]'s in ambition if not in execution. Her approach has three components, each of which ultimately fails.

First, emotional manipulation. She extracts [[Mrs-Elvsted|Thea]]'s secrets under the guise of old school friendship — "Now we can talk like the old days" (005-013) — using intimacy as a cover for intelligence gathering. She draws out Thea's fears about [[Eilert-Lovborg|Lovborg]]'s relapse, her flight from her husband, her creative partnership — all information that becomes useful later.

Second, aesthetic intimidation. Hedda cultivates an aura of aristocratic superiority that makes others confide in her. Her status as General Gabler's daughter, her position as Tesman's wife, her command of the drawing-room — all of these create a gravitational field that pulls confidences toward her.

Third, secrecy as identity. Her entire existence is a performance of concealment — her pregnancy goes unnamed, her boredom goes unexpressed, her despair goes unseen. She believes that controlling what others know about her is a form of power.

But Hedda's strategy fails for a structural reason: she cannot control information once it leaves her sphere. Lovborg tells Thea. Thea tells Tesman. Brack attends the party. The pistol is traced. Information, once released, follows its own logic — and that logic always leads back to Brack, who has positioned himself at every node in the network. Hedda can play the game; she cannot win it.
