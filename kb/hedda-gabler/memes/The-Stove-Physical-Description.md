# The Stove: Physical Description

The stove in the [[Tesman-Villa]]'s [[The-Inner-Room|inner room]] is a cast-iron fixture — dark, solid, and permanent — whose ordinary domestic function (heating the house) is transformed by [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] into an instrument of destruction. It is located in or immediately adjacent to the inner room, the back chamber that mediates between the public drawing-room and the private quarters.

The stove's presence is noted in passing until the moment of crisis. [[Berta]], the servant, tends it throughout the play, "making up the fire" at intervals. Her domestic duty keeps the fire burning regardless of what moral catastrophe unfolds around it. The ordinary question — "Shan't I put some wood on the fire?" (010-356) — acquires retrospective horror after Hedda's act.

The stove represents the domestic hearth, traditionally the heart of the home. In Ibsen's staging, its placement in the inner room rather than the drawing-room displaces it from its symbolic center. The household's heart is in its most private chamber, unseen by visitors, tended by a servant. This displacement is significant: the source of warmth is hidden; the site of destruction is concealed.

The stove's materiality matters. It is iron, not porcelain — heavy, utilitarian, industrial. Unlike the drawing-room's decorative objects (the porcelain whatnot, the tasteful furnishings), the stove belongs to the world of labor, not leisure. When Hedda uses it to burn [[The-Manuscript|the manuscript]], she appropriates a working-class tool for aristocratic violence.
