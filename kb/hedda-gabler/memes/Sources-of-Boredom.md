# Sources of Boredom

Hedda's boredom has multiple, interlocking sources — it is not a personal failing but a structural condition produced by the intersection of her marriage, her class, her gender, and her historical moment.

Marriage to [[George-Tesman|Tesman]] is the primary source. Tesman is kind, devoted, and completely dull. His world is footnotes and file-cards; Hedda's is beauty, danger, and intensity. She married him for security, and security — once achieved — is profoundly boring. The gap between the life she imagined and the life she has is the gap between General Gabler's daughter and Professor Tesman's wife.

The [[Tesman-Villa]] compounds the problem. It is both Hedda's cage and her stage — large enough to trap her, small enough to offer no escape. The constant presence of [[Berta]], the visits from [[Aunt-Julia]], the domestic rituals, the long dark evenings — all combine into an oppressive routine she cannot break.

Pregnancy adds biological finality. A child will bind Hedda permanently to Tesman's world of domestic mediocrity. The future she did not choose is being written into her body, cell by cell.

Gender is the underlying condition that makes all other sources inescapable. A man in her position could travel, work, or dissipate. A woman can receive visitors, play the piano, and wait.

> She, in her cowardice, only hankered and refrained.
>
> (002-086)

The gap between desire and possibility — "a world of storm and stress" versus Tesman's "scholarly" conversation — is the unbridgeable chasm at the center of her existence. Boredom is not a mood but a prison.
