# Hedda as Tragic Heroine

[[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] satisfies many of the traditional criteria for a tragic hero, though the satisfaction is uneasy and the unease is Ibsen's point. She is a tragic heroine who fits the form while testing its limits.

She is larger than her world: her intelligence, her will, her aesthetic standards exceed what the [[Tesman-Villa|drawing room]] can contain. A tragic hero must be exceptional — someone whose fall matters because of the height from which they fall — and Hedda's exceptionalism is established from the opening scene. She is not like the people around her, and that difference is both her greatness and her doom.

She chooses her fate. The [[Heddas-Suicide|suicide]] is an act of will, not a passive suffering. Unlike the classical tragic hero whose fate is determined by gods or prophecy, Hedda actively selects her ending. The temple-shot is a decision, not a destiny — she takes the pistol, draws the curtains, plays the wild dance, and pulls the trigger.

She achieves dignity in death. Tesman's inadequate but genuine response — "Shot herself in the temple! Think of that!" (015-240) — registers that something extraordinary has occurred. The death transforms her, even in the eyes of a man who never understood her.

But she is also a deeply problematic tragic heroine. Her cruelty is real; her destruction of [[Eilert-Lovborg|Lovborg]] is deliberate; her aestheticism is morally bankrupt. We pity her, but we cannot fully admire her. She fights against constraints that are genuinely oppressive, but she fights with weapons — manipulation, destruction, cruelty — that implicate her in the world she despises.
