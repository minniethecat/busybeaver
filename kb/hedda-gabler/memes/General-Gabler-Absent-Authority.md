# General Gabler: Absent Military-Aristocratic Authority

[[General-Gablers-Shadow|General Gabler]] never appears on stage, but his absence structures the play's entire conception of masculine authority. Ibsen's prefatory note that [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] "is to be regarded rather as her father's daughter than as her husband's wife" (001-025) establishes the General as the invisible foundation upon which the play's gender politics are built.

> My intention in giving it this name was to indicate that Hedda, as a personality, is to be regarded rather as her father's daughter than as her husband's wife.
>
> (001-024–001-025)

The General's power is military-aristocratic: direct, honorable, governed by a code that values beauty over utility and death over dishonor. This is the only form of masculine power Hedda respects — and it is the form most absent from the bourgeois world she inhabits. The General is dead, the money gone, the aristocratic world replaced by Tesman's professorship and Brack's knowing smile.

Hedda inherits the [[Heddas-Pistols|pistols]] but not the world that gave them meaning; she internalizes the code but cannot live it. Her target practice — "I am bored, that is all" (008-193) — is a woman playing with a power that belonged to a dead man and a dead world.

The General's portrait hangs in the [[Tesman-Villa|villa]], a silent witness to his daughter's entrapment. His code — the clean death, the temple shot, the refusal of dishonor — is the code Hedda fulfills in her suicide. She dies beneath his portrait, in the inner room, with his pistol. She dies as Gabler, not as Tesman.

The tragedy of the General's authority is that it is the only masculine power in the play that does not directly harm Hedda — because it no longer exists. The code survives, but the world that sustained it is gone. Hedda is a general's daughter in a world without generals.
