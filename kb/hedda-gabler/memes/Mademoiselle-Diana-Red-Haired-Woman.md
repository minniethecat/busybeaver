# Mademoiselle Diana — The Red-Haired Woman

The introduction to the play notes a "red-haired 'Mademoiselle Diana'" who is mentioned "but not seen" (002-009). This visual detail — red hair — links her symbolically to fire and passion, the same elemental forces that [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] channels when she burns [[The-Manuscript|Lovborg's manuscript]] in [[The-Stove|the stove]].

Diana is the keeper of an alternative feminine space that exists outside the [[Tesman-Villa|drawing-room]], the [[Respectability|respectable]] home, and the patriarchal order. Her establishment — "Mademoiselle Diana's boudoir" (014-362) — is where Lovborg spends his final hours.

It is described as a "coffee-party" (012-075) by [[Judge-Brack|Brack]]: "It was Mademoiselle Diana that was giving the soiree" (012-075). The euphemism — "coffee-party," "soiree" — reveals the double-speak of respectable society. Everyone knows what happens there; no one says it directly.

> It was Mademoiselle Diana that was giving the soiree, to a select circle of her admirers and her lady friends.
>
> (012-075)

The establishment represents the world of actual transgression that Hedda only plays at. Diana's red hair, her boudoir, her "soirees" — all belong to a demimonde that exists in parallel to the Tesman drawing-room, accessible to men like Brack and Lovborg but off-limits to the women of the play. Hedda can glimpse it only through Brack's reports.
