# Judge Brack — Character Traits

| Trait | Expression | Dramatic Function |
|-------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Cynical intelligence** | Sees through every social pretense — knows [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] is bored, [[George-Tesman|Tesman]] is limited, [[Eilert-Lovborg|Lovborg]] is unstable | Exploits all three weaknesses simultaneously |
| **Information mastery** | Knows about the pistols, the manuscript, Mademoiselle Diana's, the "triangle" dynamics | Converts private knowledge into social power |
| **Scandal immunity** | His "correctness and respectability are beyond all question" (007-212) | Can threaten exposure because he himself is immune |
| **Sexual predation** | Pursues a "triangle" — thinly veiled blackmail for access to [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] | Transforms power into intimacy without risk |
| **Amoral pragmatism** | Never acts from malice — acts from self-interest | His closing line "People don't do such things!" is social horror, not moral outrage |

Brack's cynicism is the engine of the play's plot. He sees through every pretense and exploits every weakness, but never from passion — always from calculation. His most dangerous quality is his immunity to the very scandal he wields as a weapon.

Brack's power stems from his ability to perceive what others conceal — particularly Hedda's profound dissatisfaction, which she confesses to him directly: "how mortally bored I have been." (007-147) His cynicism is not misanthropy but a diagnostic skill, a clinical precision in reading the weaknesses of those around him for his own advantage.
