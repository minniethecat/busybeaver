[008-001]She always is.

[008-002]TESMAN.

[008-003]Yes, but to-day she is much worse than usual, poor dear.

[008-004]HEDDA.

[008-005]Oh, then it's only natural that her sister should remain with her. I

[008-006]must bear my disappointment.

[008-007]TESMAN.

[008-008]And you can't imagine, dear, how delighted Aunt Julia seemed to be--

[008-009]because you had come home looking so flourishing!

[008-010]HEDDA.

[008-011][Half aloud, rising.] Oh, those everlasting Aunts!

[008-012]TESMAN.

[008-013]What?

[008-014]HEDDA.

[008-015][Going to the glass door.] Nothing.

[008-016]TESMAN.

[008-017]Oh, all right. [He goes through the inner room, out to the right.

[008-018]BRACK.

[008-019]What bonnet were you talking about?

[008-020]HEDDA.

[008-021]Oh, it was a little episode with [[Aunt-Julia|Miss Tesman]] this morning. She had laid

[008-022]down her bonnet on the chair there--[Looks at him and smiles.]--and I

[008-023]pretended to think it was the servant's.

[008-024]BRACK.

[008-025][Shaking his head.] Now my dear Mrs. Hedda, how could you do such a

[008-026]thing? To the excellent old lady, too!

[008-027]HEDDA.

[008-028][Nervously crossing the room.] Well, you see--these impulses come over

[008-029]me all of a sudden; and I cannot resist them. [Throws herself down in

[008-030]the easy-chair by the stove.] Oh, I don't know how to explain it.

[008-031]BRACK.

[008-032][Behind the easy-chair.] You are not really happy--that is at the bottom

[008-033]of it.

[008-034]HEDDA.

[008-035][Looking straight before her.] I know of no reason why I should be--

[008-036]happy. Perhaps you can give me one?

[008-037]BRACK.

[008-038]Well-amongst other things, because you have got exactly the home you had

[008-039]set your heart on.

[008-040]HEDDA.

[008-041][Looks up at him and laughs.] Do you too believe in that legend?

[008-042]BRACK.

[008-043]Is there nothing in it, then?

[008-044]HEDDA.

[008-045]Oh yes, there is something in it.

[008-046]BRACK.

[008-047]Well?

[008-048]HEDDA.

[008-049]There is this in it, that I made use of Tesman to see me home from

[008-050]evening parties last summer--

[008-051]BRACK.

[008-052]I, unfortunately, had to go quite a different way.

[008-053]HEDDA.

[008-054]That's true. I know you were going a different way last summer.

[008-055]BRACK.

[008-056][Laughing.] Oh fie, Mrs. Hedda! Well, then--you and Tesman--?

[008-057]HEDDA.

[008-058]Well, we happened to pass here one evening; Tesman, poor fellow, was

[008-059]writhing in the agony of having to find conversation; so I took pity on

[008-060]the learned man--

[008-061]BRACK.

[008-062][Smiles doubtfully.] You took pity? H'm--

[008-063]HEDDA.

[008-064]Yes, I really did. And so--to help him out of his torment--I happened to

[008-065]say, in pure thoughtlessness, that I should like to live in this villa.

[008-066]BRACK.

[008-067]No more than that?

[008-068]HEDDA.

[008-069]Not that evening.

[008-070]BRACK.

[008-071]But afterwards?

[008-072]HEDDA.

[008-073]Yes, my thoughtlessness had consequences, my dear Judge.

[008-074]BRACK.

[008-075]Unfortunately that too often happens, Mrs. Hedda.

[008-076]HEDDA.

[008-077]Thanks! So you see it was this enthusiasm for Secretary Falk's villa

[008-078]that first constituted a bond of sympathy between [[George-Tesman|George Tesman]] and me.

[008-079]From that came our engagement and our marriage, and our wedding journey,

[008-080]and all the rest of it. Well, well, my dear Judge--as you make your bed

[008-081]so you must lie, I could almost say.

[008-082]BRACK.

[008-083]This is exquisite! And you really cared not a rap about it all the time?

[008-084]HEDDA.

[008-085]No, heaven knows I didn't.

[008-086]BRACK.

[008-087]But now? Now that we have made it so homelike for you?

[008-088]HEDDA.

[008-089]Uh--the rooms all seem to smell of lavender and dried rose-leaves.--But

[008-090]perhaps it's Aunt Julia that has brought that scent with her.

[008-091]BRACK.

[008-092][Laughing.] No, I think it must be a legacy from the late Mrs. Secretary

[008-093]Falk.

[008-094]HEDDA.

[008-095]Yes, there is an odour of mortality about it. It reminds me of a

[008-096]bouquet--the day after the ball. [Clasps her hands behind her head,

[008-097]leans back in her chair and looks at him.] Oh, my dear Judge--you cannot

[008-098]imagine how horribly I shall bore myself here.

[008-099]BRACK.

[008-100]Why should not you, too, find some sort of vocation in life, Mrs. Hedda?

[008-101]HEDDA.

[008-102]A vocation--that should attract me?

[008-103]BRACK.

[008-104]If possible, of course.

[008-105]HEDDA.

[008-106]Heaven knows what sort of a vocation that could be. I often wonder

[008-107]whether-- [Breaking off.] But that would never do either.

[008-108]BRACK.

[008-109]Who can tell? Let me hear what it is.

[008-110]HEDDA.

[008-111]Whether I might not get Tesman to go into politics, I mean.

[008-112]BRACK.

[008-113][Laughing.] Tesman? No really now, political life is not the thing for

[008-114]him--not at all in his line.

[008-115]HEDDA.

[008-116]No, I daresay not.--But if I could get him into it all the same?

[008-117]BRACK.

[008-118]Why--what satisfaction could you find in that? If he is not fitted for

[008-119]that sort of thing, why should you want to drive him into it?

[008-120]HEDDA.

[008-121]Because I am bored, I tell you! [After a pause.] So you think it quite

[008-122]out of the question that Tesman should ever get into the ministry?

[008-123]BRACK.

[008-124]H'm--you see, my dear Mrs. Hedda--to get into the ministry, he would

[008-125]have to be a tolerably rich man.

[008-126]HEDDA.

[008-127][Rising impatiently.] Yes, there we have it! It is this genteel poverty

[008-128]I have managed to drop into--! [Crosses the room.] That is what makes

[008-129]life so pitiable! So utterly ludicrous!--For that's what it is.

[008-130]BRACK.

[008-131]Now _I_ should say the fault lay elsewhere.

[008-132]HEDDA.

[008-133]Where, then?

[008-134]BRACK.

[008-135]You have never gone through any really stimulating experience.

[008-136]HEDDA.

[008-137]Anything serious, you mean?

[008-138]BRACK.

[008-139]Yes, you may call it so. But now you may perhaps have one in store.

[008-140]HEDDA.

[008-141][Tossing her head.] Oh, you're thinking of the annoyances about this

[008-142]wretched professorship! But that must be Tesman's own affair. I assure

[008-143]you I shall not waste a thought upon it.

[008-144]BRACK.

[008-145]No, no, I daresay not. But suppose now that what people call--in elegant

[008-146]language--a solemn responsibility were to come upon you? [Smiling.] A

[008-147]new responsibility, Mrs. Hedda?

[008-148]HEDDA.

[008-149][Angrily.] Be quiet! Nothing of that sort will ever happen!

[008-150]BRACK.

[008-151][Warily.] We will speak of this again a year hence--at the very outside.

[008-152]HEDDA.

[008-153][Curtly.] I have no turn for anything of the sort, [[Judge-Brack|Judge Brack]]. No

[008-154]responsibilities for me!

[008-155]BRACK.

[008-156]Are you so unlike the generality of women as to have no turn for duties

[008-157]which--?

[008-158]HEDDA.

[008-159][Beside the glass door.] Oh, be quiet, I tell you!--I often think there

[008-160]is only one thing in the world I have any turn for.

[008-161]BRACK.

[008-162][Drawing near to her.] And what is that, if I may ask?

[008-163]HEDDA.

[008-164][Stands looking out.] Boring myself to death. Now you know it. [Turns,

[008-165]looks towards the inner room, and laughs.] Yes, as I thought! Here comes

[008-166]the Professor.

[008-167]BRACK.

[008-168][Softly, in a tone of warning.] Come, come, come, Mrs. Hedda!

[008-169]GEORGE TESMAN, dressed for the party, with his gloves and hat

[008-170]in his hand, enters from the right through the inner room.

[008-171]TESMAN.

[008-172]Hedda, has no message come from [[Eilert-Lovborg|Eilert Lovborg]]? Eh?

[008-173]HEDDA.

[008-174]No.

[008-175]TESMAN.

[008-176]Then you'll see he'll be here presently.

[008-177]BRACK.

[008-178]Do you really think he will come?

[008-179]TESMAN.

[008-180]Yes, I am almost sure of it. For what you were telling us this morning

[008-181]must have been a mere floating rumour.

[008-182]BRACK.

[008-183]You think so?

[008-184]TESMAN.

[008-185]At any rate, Aunt Julia said she did not believe for a moment that he

[008-186]would ever stand in my way again. Fancy that!

[008-187]BRACK.

[008-188]Well then, that's all right.

[008-189]TESMAN.

[008-190][Placing his hat and gloves on a chair on the right.] Yes, but you must

[008-191]really let me wait for him as long as possible.

[008-192]BRACK.

[008-193]We have plenty of time yet. None of my guests will arrive before seven

[008-194]or half-past.

[008-195]TESMAN.

[008-196]Then meanwhile we can keep Hedda company, and see what happens. Eh?

[008-197]HEDDA.

[008-198][Placing BRACK'S hat and overcoat upon the corner settee.] And at the

[008-199]worst Mr. Lovborg can remain here with me.

[008-200]BRACK.

[008-201][Offering to take his things.] Oh, allow me, Mrs. Tesman!--What do you

[008-202]mean by "At the worst"?

[008-203]HEDDA.

[008-204]If he won't go with you and Tesman.

[008-205]TESMAN.

[008-206][Looks dubiously at her.] But, Hedda dear--do you think it would quite

[008-207]do for him to remain here with you? Eh? Remember, Aunt Julia can't come.

[008-208]HEDDA.

[008-209]No, but [[Mrs-Elvsted|Mrs. Elvsted]] is coming. We three can have a cup of tea together.

[008-210]TESMAN.

[008-211]Oh yes, that will be all right.

[008-212]BRACK.

[008-213][Smiling.] And that would perhaps be the safest plan for him.

[008-214]HEDDA.

[008-215]Why so?

[008-216]BRACK.

[008-217]Well, you know, Mrs. Tesman, how you used to gird at my little bachelor

[008-218]parties. You declared they were adapted only for men of the strictest

[008-219]principles.

[008-220]HEDDA.

[008-221]But no doubt Mr. Lovborg's principles are strict enough now. A converted

[008-222]sinner-- [BERTA appears at the hall door.

[008-223]BERTA.

[008-224]There's a gentleman asking if you are at home, ma'am--

[008-225]HEDDA.

[008-226]Well, show him in.

[008-227]TESMAN.

[008-228][Softly.] I'm sure it is he! Fancy that!

[008-229]EILERT LOVBORG enters from the hall. He is slim and lean;

[008-230]of the same age as TESMAN, but looks older and somewhat

[008-231]worn-out. His hair and beard are of a blackish brown, his

[008-232]face long and pale, but with patches of colour on the cheeks.

[008-233]He is dressed in a well-cut black visiting suit, quite new.

[008-234]He has dark gloves and a silk hat. He stops near the door,

[008-235]and makes a rapid bow, seeming somewhat embarrassed.

[008-236]TESMAN.

[008-237][Goes up to him and shakes him warmly by the hand.] Well, my dear

[008-238]Eilert--so at last we meet again!

[008-239]EILERT LOVBORG.

[008-240][Speaks in a subdued voice.] Thanks for your letter, Tesman.

[008-241][Approaching HEDDA.] Will you too shake hands with me, Mrs. Tesman?

[008-242]HEDDA.

[008-243][Taking his hand.] I am glad to see you, Mr. Lovborg. [With a motion of

[008-244]her hand.] I don't know whether you two gentlemen--?

[008-245]LOVBORG.

[008-246][Bowing slightly.] Judge Brack, I think.

[008-247]BRACK.

[008-248][Doing likewise.] Oh yes,--in the old days--

[008-249]TESMAN.

[008-250][To LOVBORG, with his hands on his shoulders.] And now you must make

[008-251]yourself entirely at home, Eilert! Mustn't he, Hedda?--For I hear you

[008-252]are going to settle in town again? Eh?

[008-253]LOVBORG.

[008-254]Yes, I am.

[008-255]TESMAN.

[008-256]Quite right, quite right. Let me tell you, I have got hold of your new

[008-257]book; but I haven't had time to read it yet.

[008-258]LOVBORG.

[008-259]You may spare yourself the trouble.

[008-260]TESMAN.

[008-261]Why so?

[008-262]LOVBORG.

[008-263]Because there is very little in it.

[008-264]TESMAN.

[008-265]Just fancy--how can you say so?

[008-266]BRACK.

[008-267]But it has been very much praised, I hear.

[008-268]LOVBORG.

[008-269]That was what I wanted; so I put nothing into the book but what every

[008-270]one would agree with.

[008-271]BRACK.

[008-272]Very wise of you.

[008-273]TESMAN.

[008-274]Well but, my dear Eilert--!

[008-275]LOVBORG.

[008-276]For now I mean to win myself a position again--to make a fresh start.

[008-277]TESMAN.

[008-278][A little embarrassed.] Ah, that is what you wish to do? Eh?

[008-279]LOVBORG.

[008-280][Smiling, lays down his hat, and draws a packet wrapped in paper, from

[008-281]his coat pocket.] But when this one appears, George Tesman, you will

[008-282]have to read it. For this is the real book--the book I have put my true

[008-283]self into.

[008-284]TESMAN.

[008-285]Indeed? And what is it?

[008-286]LOVBORG.

[008-287]It is the continuation.

[008-288]TESMAN.

[008-289]The continuation? Of what?

[008-290]LOVBORG.

[008-291]Of the book.

[008-292]TESMAN.

[008-293]Of the new book?

[008-294]LOVBORG.

[008-295]Of course.

[008-296]TESMAN.

[008-297]Why, my dear Eilert--does it not come down to our own days?

[008-298]LOVBORG.

[008-299]Yes, it does; and this one deals with the future.

[008-300]TESMAN.

[008-301]With the future! But, good heavens, we know nothing of the future!

[008-302]LOVBORG.

[008-303]No; but there is a thing or two to be said about it all the same. [Opens

[008-304]the packet.] Look here--

[008-305]TESMAN.

[008-306]Why, that's not your handwriting.

[008-307]LOVBORG.

[008-308]I dictated it. [Turning over the pages.] It falls into two sections. The

[008-309]first deals with the civilising forces of the future. And here is the

[008-310]second--[running through the pages towards the end]--forecasting the

[008-311]probable line of development.

[008-312]TESMAN.

[008-313]How odd now! I should never have thought of writing anything of that

[008-314]sort.

[008-315]HEDDA.

[008-316][At the glass door, drumming on the pane.] H'm--. I daresay not.

[008-317]LOVBORG.

[008-318][Replacing the manuscript in its paper and laying the packet on the

[008-319]table.] I brought it, thinking I might read you a little of it this

[008-320]evening.

[008-321]TESMAN.

[008-322]That was very good of you, Eilert. But this evening--? [Looking back at

[008-323]BRACK.] I don't see how we can manage it--

[008-324]LOVBORG.

[008-325]Well then, some other time. There is no hurry.

[008-326]BRACK.

[008-327]I must tell you, Mr. Lovborg--there is a little gathering at my house

[008-328]this evening--mainly in honour of Tesman, you know--
