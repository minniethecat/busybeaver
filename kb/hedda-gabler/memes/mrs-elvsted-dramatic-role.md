# Mrs. Elvsted — Dramatic Role

Mrs. Elvsted enters as a desperate woman: she has left her husband (a provincial sheriff) to follow Lovborg to town, terrified he will relapse. Her entrance is marked by Tesman's recollection — "Is it really? Miss Rysing that was" (004-254) — establishing her history of transformation.

> Is it really? Sheriff Elvsted's wife? Miss Rysing that was.
>
> (004-254)

Her function reveals Hedda's character through contrast. Where Thea possesses courage, devotion, and creativity, Hedda possesses only vacancy and fear (002-086). Thea serves as **moral foil**: every virtue she embodies is what Hedda lacks.

As **creative partner**, she preserved Lovborg's notes and made "a clean copy of the whole" — the productive partnership Hedda cannot sustain. As **survivor**, she sits with Tesman reconstructing the manuscript after Lovborg's death, proving creation outlasts destruction.

The introduction frames the contrast precisely: "Where Thea Elvsted dared to venture all, Hedda, in her cowardice, only hankered and refrained" (002-086). This is the moral architecture of the entire play.
