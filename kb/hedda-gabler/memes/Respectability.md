# Respectability

Respectability is the governing moral economy of the world of *Hedda Gabler* — a system of social credit maintained by appearances, threatened by scandal, and enforced by a community that watches and judges. Every character in the play navigates this system, from [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]]'s pathological terror of exposure to [[George-Tesman|Tesman]]'s anxious pursuit of his professorship to [[Judge-Brack|Brack]]'s weaponization of social knowledge.

The word itself appears only rarely in the text, but its logic pervades every line. [[Judge-Brack|Brack]] describes himself as "a man of the world" whose "correctness and respectability are beyond all question" (007-212) — and this social standing gives him the power to threaten [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] with exposure. The threat is not legal but social; its force derives entirely from the respectability economy. The play's central theme of [[Courage-and-Cowardice|courage versus cowardice]] is inseparable from this economy, since the thing Hedda fears most — scandal — is the opposite of respectability.

> The introduction identifies "respectability" as the invisible architecture of Ibsen's dramatic world: "Hedda cannot be said to have been 'drawn' from any one model" (001-143) precisely because she is drawn from a social type — the respectable woman whose respectability conceals a void. The play exposes what respectability conceals.
