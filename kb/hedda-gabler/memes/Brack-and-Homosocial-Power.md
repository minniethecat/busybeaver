# Brack and Homosocial Power

[[Judge-Brack|Brack]]'s power over [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] is not merely personal but structural — it is the power of the homosocial world from which she is excluded by her gender.

## The Gentlemen's World

The play is punctuated by scenes of male fellowship from which women are barred: the bachelor party at Brack's lodgings, the card games, the conversations about careers and reputations conducted "between ourselves." Brack moves effortlessly through this world; Hedda can only peer in through the glass door. Her exclusion is structural, not personal — a function of gender, not character.

## Information as Male Property

The play's information economy is gendered. Brack controls information because he has access to male spaces (the party, the police investigation) where women cannot go. Hedda's attempts to gather intelligence are channeled through male intermediaries (Tesman, Lovborg) or acquired by eavesdropping. She is always one step removed from the information that Brack possesses directly.

## The Triangle as Homosocial Contract

Brack's "triangle" fantasy — "a cock of the walk" with exclusive access to the household (007-212) — is a vision of homosocial power disguised as sociability. He offers Hedda the appearance of a confidante relationship while structuring it as surveillance. The triangle is not a geometry of equals but a structure of male control disguised as friendship.

> HEDDA: [Looks up at him.] So I am in your power, Judge Brack. You have me at
> your beck and call, from this time forward.
>
> (015-161)

## Hedda's Resistance

Hedda's suicide can be read as her ultimate refusal of the homosocial order. She will not be Brack's creature; she will not be Tesman's wife; she will not be Lovborg's inspiration. The temple-shot is her exit from a world governed by male power — the one act Brack cannot control or contain.
