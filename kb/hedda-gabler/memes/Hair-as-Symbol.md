# Hair as Symbol

Hair in *Hedda Gabler* is not merely a physical attribute — it is a symbolic language through which Ibsen maps the play's central conflict between the natural and the controlled, the fertile and the sterile, the feminine and the masculine. The contrast between [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]]'s controlled appearance and [[Mrs-Elvsted|Thea]]'s "irritating" abundant hair is one of the play's most compressed and revealing symbolic oppositions.

## Thea's Hair

Thea's hair is described as "unusually abundant, fair, and almost white-gold in color" (008-050) — a description that associates her with fertility, natural abundance, and the life-force. The hair spills out of its constraints; it is abundant where Hedda is controlled, natural where Hedda is artificial, fertile where Hedda (for all her fear of pregnancy) is sterile.

Hedda's reaction to Thea's hair is immediate and visceral: "that irritating hair of hers" (009-050). The irritation is not aesthetic — it is existential. Thea's hair represents everything Hedda has rejected: fertility, domesticity, the feminine, the natural. In their school days, Hedda threatened to "burn it off" (009-055) — the same verb (burn) she will later apply to [[The-Manuscript|the manuscript]], Thea's other "child."

## Hedda's Hair

Ibsen never describes Hedda's hair in detail — the omission is significant. Where Thea's hair is abundant, visible, and repeatedly mentioned, Hedda's is controlled, invisible, unremarked. Hedda's appearance is a performance of aristocratic restraint: everything in its place, nothing spilling over, nothing natural.

This contrast maps the play's gender politics: Thea is *too* feminine (too fertile, too abundant, too natural); Hedda is *not enough* feminine (too controlled, too masculine, too sterile). Both women are punished for their deviation from the unmarked feminine ideal — Thea through Hedda's cruelty, Hedda through the social system that has no place for her.

## The Burning Threat

Hedda's school-day threat to "burn off" Thea's hair is the play's most revealing childhood memory. The young Hedda wanted to destroy the symbol of Thea's feminine abundance — and the adult Hedda burns Thea's other "child" (the manuscript) with the same impulse.

The burning threat also associates hair with the play's central fire imagery: the stove (where the manuscript burns), the pistols (firearms), and ultimately Hedda's own "burning" intensity. Hair, manuscript, child — all are manifestations of creative, fertile life that Hedda must destroy because she cannot create.

## Hair and the Father

Hedda's relationship to hair is also inflected by her relationship to [[General-Gablers-Shadow|General Gabler]]. The General is described (in memory) with military precision — the opposite of Thea's natural abundance. Hedda has internalized her father's values: control, discipline, the rejection of anything that "spills over" or escapes its bounds.

Thea's hair — abundant, uncontrolled, natural — is the sensory opposite of the military-aristocratic code. It offends Hedda not because it is ugly but because it represents a form of life (feminine, fertile, natural) that her father's code cannot accommodate.

## Thematic Significance

Hair operates as a condensed symbol of the play's deepest tensions:
- **Nature vs. Culture**: Thea's natural abundance vs. Hedda's artificial control
- **Fertility vs. Sterility**: Thea creates (the manuscript, the reconstruction); Hedda destroys
- **Feminine vs. Masculine**: Thea accepts the feminine role; Hedda aspires to the masculine
- **Past vs. Present**: The hair-burning threat from school days reverberates into the play's present action
