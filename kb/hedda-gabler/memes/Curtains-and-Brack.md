# Curtains and Brack

[[Judge-Brack|Brack]]'s relationship to the [[The-Curtains|curtains]] reveals his function in the dramaturgy of *Hedda Gabler*. He enters through [[The-Glass-Door|the glass door]] without announcement, bypassing the front door's social protocols. The curtained threshold — meant to control access — is nothing to him.

The inner room curtain covers a space where secrets are stored and destroyed: the burning of [[The-Manuscript|the manuscript]], [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]]'s whispered confession, her final suicide beneath the paternal portrait. This is the space Brack yearns to penetrate. He "glances toward the inner room" (006-074) and "keeps an eye from time to time" on it (009-166). The curtain is to him what a locked door is to a thief — an invitation.

His ultimate threat is to tear down the curtains entirely. When he promises "In future I shall be invisible," he is declaring the end of privacy. The villa will become a stage with no curtain, no backstage, no retreat. The curtained boundary that [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] has manipulated so carefully — her last remaining instrument of control — will dissolve.

Brack's power is the power to expose. He knows about the [[Heddas-Pistols|pistols]], about [[Mademoiselle-Dianas|Diana's establishment]], about Hedda's role in the scandal. The curtains that protect her from that knowledge becoming public are fabric, and fabric tears. When Brack says "this same Mademoiselle Diana" (015-149), he is not naming a place — he is naming the destruction of the curtain between private scandal and public exposure. His promised invisibility is the ultimate curtain: the one that conceals the watcher, not the watched.
