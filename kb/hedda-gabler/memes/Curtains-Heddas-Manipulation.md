# Curtains: Hedda's Manipulation

[[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]]'s control of the curtains is obsessive and precisely mapped across the four acts. Each manipulation is a choice: to expose or conceal, to welcome or repel.

**Act I**: Hedda complains about the sunlight and has the [[The-Glass-Door|glass door]] curtains drawn. Her first words about the house express a preference for dimness and enclosure — a rejection of the outside world before it has even impinged.

**Act II**: She "draws back the curtains" from the glass door (011-090) in a rare moment of openness, signaling anticipation of [[Eilert-Lovborg|Lovborg]]'s visit. The gesture is hopeful, even vulnerable — curtains opened for a specific person.

**Act III**: She stands "at the glass door, drumming on the pane" (008-316). The curtains are open but she cannot pass through — the threshold is visual, not traversable. She "draws back the curtains" again (011-090), adjusting the light to suit her mood, but the gesture has lost its hopeful quality.

**Act IV**: "The curtains over the glass door are drawn" and evening lamps are lit (013-132). The world is closed in completely — the ultimate withdrawal. The curtains will not open again in this play.

Each manipulation is [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]]'s most direct physical expression of agency. The curtains respond to her hand without resistance, unlike the people around her. But their limitation is the limitation of her power: they can dim a room but cannot darken it; they can conceal but cannot lock; they offer the illusion of control over visibility in a world where every secret is eventually seen.
