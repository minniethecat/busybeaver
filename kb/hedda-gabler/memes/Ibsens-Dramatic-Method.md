# Ibsen's Dramatic Method in Hedda Gabler

*Hedda Gabler* represents the culmination of Ibsen's mature dramatic method. Written in 1890, it deploys techniques he had been refining for over a decade with unprecedented economy and precision.

> "Thus," says Dr. Brandes, "out of small and scattered traits of reality Ibsen fashioned his close-knit and profoundly thought-out works of art."
>
> (001-162–001-163)

## Retrospective Exposition

Like *Ghosts* and *Rosmersholm*, *Hedda Gabler* uses retrospective exposition: the crucial events have already occurred before the curtain rises. Hedda's past relationship with Lovborg, her marriage to Tesman, Thea's flight from her husband — all are revealed through dialogue rather than dramatized directly. The technique creates a sense of inescapable consequences: the past is not receding but advancing, tightening around the characters like a noose.

## The Four-Character Core

The play's dramatic engine is powered by four characters: Hedda, Lovborg, Thea, and Brack. Tesman, Julia, Berta, and others orbit this core but never enter its gravitational center. The four-character structure allows Ibsen to explore relationships with unusual depth while maintaining a single-location unity.

## Symbolic Realism

Ibsen's late method has been called "symbolic realism": objects and actions are simultaneously realistic and symbolic. The stove is a stove — and an instrument of destruction. The pistols are pistols — and carriers of inherited violence. The curtains are curtains — and mechanisms of theatrical control. This doubling creates dramatic density without sacrificing verisimilitude.

## The Open Ending

*Hedda Gabler* ends not with resolution but with a question. Tesman and Thea begin rebuilding the manuscript; Brack is stunned; Hedda lies dead. What happens next? The play refuses to answer. The open ending, characteristic of Ibsen's late work, shifts the burden of interpretation onto the audience.
