# Judge Brack — Dramatic Role

Brack's dramatic function mirrors [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]]'s but inverts it. Where Hedda manipulates through charm, cruelty, and emotional leverage, Brack manipulates through information, social standing, and the threat of exposure. He understands power as a game of knowledge — whoever controls what becomes public controls the players.

He is the play's structural counterweight: Hedda's intelligence is destructive and self-consuming; Brack's intelligence is instrumental and self-serving. He never acts from passion — only from calculation. His pursuit of the "triangle" is not desire but acquisition: he wants access to [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] on terms that preserve his bachelor freedom while binding her to him.

The critic William Archer noted that Brack functions as "the visible embodiment of the scandal Hedda fears" (012-150) — and this visibility is his power. He doesn't need to expose her; he only needs her to know that exposure is always possible.

> Brack is the only character in the play who understands power as a structural condition rather than a personal attribute. His "correctness and respectability are beyond all question" (007-212) — which means he can wield scandal as a weapon while remaining immune to it himself.
