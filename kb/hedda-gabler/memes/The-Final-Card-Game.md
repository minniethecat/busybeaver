# The Final Card Game

While Hedda stages her suicide in the inner room, the gentlemen play cards in the drawing room. The card game is Ibsen's most audacious piece of dramatic counterpoint — the mundane and the tragic occurring simultaneously, separated only by a curtain.

## Dramatic Structure

Ibsen intercuts between the card game and the inner room throughout the final scene. The rhythm of play — deal, bid, trick, deal — provides a mechanical counterpoint to Hedda's escalating intensity. The contrast is deliberate: the card game represents the ordinary world, the world of social ritual and domestic routine, the world Hedda finds intolerable.

## What They Play Through

The card game continues through Tesman and Thea beginning their manuscript reconstruction, through Brack's revelation that he controls Hedda, through the "wild dance" on the piano, through the shot to the temple. The game's persistence through crisis is not callousness but dramatic irony — the characters are absorbed in their game while the play's most decisive action occurs just out of sight.

## Brack's Recognition

Only Brack recognizes the gunshot for what it is (015-242). His line — "She has shot herself in the temple" — breaks the card game's rhythm, imposing tragic recognition on mundane routine. He is the only character who understands what has happened, and his understanding is terrible.

## "People Don't Do Such Things"

The final line, spoken by Brack (015-245), reflects the card game's perspective on tragedy. Tragedy is what happens when people do things that "people don't do." The card game represents the world of "what people do" — the ordinary, the social, the rule-bound — and Hedda's suicide is its absolute negation. The line is not judgment but incomprehension: the card game cannot process what has just occurred.
