# George Tesman — Thematic Significance

Tesman embodies the play's critique of bourgeois marriage with unusual sympathy. He is not cruel, not stupid, not even particularly selfish. His tragedy is that he is *insufficient* — a "specialist" (002-055) whose narrow competence cannot fill the void of [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]]'s existence. His scholarly interest in "Brabant during the Middle Ages" is deliberately bathetic: a subject so parochial it underscores the gap between his modest ambitions and Hedda's aristocratic hunger for grandeur.

He is also the play's argument about the limits of decency. Tesman is decent in every particular — honest, loving, hardworking, grateful — and none of it matters. His decency cannot save his wife, cannot protect his aunt's savings, cannot compete with [[Judge-Brack|Brack]]'s cynicism or [[Eilert-Lovborg|Lovborg]]'s brilliance. In Ibsen's moral universe, decency without insight is just another form of incapacity.

His closing partnership with [[Mrs-Elvsted|Thea]] to reconstruct the manuscript offers the play's quietest hope: the compiler finds his purpose not in original creation but in preservation.
