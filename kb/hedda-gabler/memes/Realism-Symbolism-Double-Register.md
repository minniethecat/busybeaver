# Realism and Symbolism: The Double Register

The genius of *Hedda Gabler* lies in its ability to operate simultaneously on two registers: the realist surface of domestic conversation and the symbolic depth beneath. Every significant object in the play functions on both levels.

> Hedda's fantastic vision of Lovborg "with vine-leaves in his hair." Surely this is a very obvious image or symbol of the beautiful, the ideal, aspect of bacchic elation and revelry. Antique art, or I am much mistaken, shows us many figures of Dionysus himself and his followers with vine-leaves entwined their hair.
>
> (002-018)

[[Heddas-Pistols|The pistols]] are General Gabler's actual dueling pistols, kept in a case and used for target practice — a plausible object for a general's daughter to possess. They are also the instruments of the beautiful death, the legacy of aristocratic masculinity, the only objects [[Hedda-Gabler|Hedda]] truly values.

[[The-Manuscript|The manuscript]] is a real scholarly work on cultural history by [[Eilert-Lovborg|Lovborg]] — a plausible product of the intellectual world the play depicts. It is also "our child," the creative union of Lovborg and [[Mrs-Elvsted|Thea]], the rival to Hedda's unborn child, the object of her most destructive act.

The [[Tesman-Villa|villa]] is a perfectly realistic suburban house, purchased on credit, furnished to bourgeois taste. It is also the cage of marriage, the theater of [[Respectability|respectability]], the architecture of entrapment. The [[The-Glass-Door|glass door]] is a standard architectural feature and also the play's central image of the threshold between inside and outside, freedom and constraint.

This double register is Ibsen's great formal achievement. The symbols never announce themselves as symbols. They work on the audience beneath conscious registration — until the play's final moments, when the accumulated symbolic weight becomes unbearable and the gunshot releases it. The pistols are pistols; the pistols are also everything. That is the method.
