## Overview

"The Unpasturable Fields" is a tale from Section 3 of *Fifty-One Tales* (1915) by Lord Dunsany. It presents a dialogue between the mountains — ancient, immovable, self-proclaimed "most imperishable" — and the clouds that pass above them. The mountains assert their permanence, but the clouds respond with the quiet boast that upon their unpasturable fields dwells Pegasus, who feeds on song brought by larks each morning.

## Summary

The tale opens with the mountains speaking in their own voice, declaring themselves the oldest of things:

> "Behold us, even us; the old ones, the grey ones, that wear the feet of Time. Time on our rocks shall break his staff and stumble: and still we shall sit majestic, even as now..."
>
> [003-006]

They claim fellowship with the sea and describe how cities rise and fall at their feet like crocuses, while they remain. The mountains see themselves as the ultimate witness — immovable, eternal, looking down upon all passing things:

> "We are the most imperishable mountains."
>
> [003-013]

The clouds gather from far places — clouds shaped like Caucasus, like Himalaya — and look down upon the mountains from heights far greater. The mountains dismiss them: "Ye pass away." But the clouds answer with unexpected dignity:

> "We pass away, indeed we pass away, but upon our unpasturable fields Pegasus prances. Here Pegasus gallops and browses upon song which the larks bring to him every morning from far terrestrial fields."
>
> [003-021]

The tale ends with a striking image: Pegasus breathing the dawn-wind with dilated nostrils, staring from tremendous heights at "far-future wonderful wars rage in the creases and the folds of the togas that cover the knees of the gods."

## Analysis

The tale is a meditation on permanence and value. The mountains believe their immovability is the ultimate claim to importance, but the clouds reveal that their transience is what makes them — literally — the habitation of myth. Pegasus, the winged horse of poetry and inspiration, lives not on solid rock but on unpasturable fields of vapor. The clouds pass away, yes, but they carry song.

Dunsany's irony is characteristically gentle: the mountains' boast of permanence is not wrong, only incomplete. What matters is not how long you endure, but what you host. The cloud-fields, though transient, are where Pegasus prances — and Pegasus is the bearer of poetry, of the lark's morning song, of far visions of "wonderful wars" in the folds of gods' garments.

The image of "unpasturable fields" is a paradox that lies at the heart of Dunsany's aesthetic: the most beautiful things cannot be grazed, cannot be used, cannot be owned. They exist only to be seen, and they pass.
