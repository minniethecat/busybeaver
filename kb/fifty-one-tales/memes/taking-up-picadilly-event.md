## Overview

"Taking Up Picadilly" [001-039] is a surreal prose piece in Lord Dunsany's *Fifty-One Tales* in which the famous London thoroughfare is physically taken up and removed. The event blends the mundane (a street) with the impossible (its removal), creating a characteristic Dunsanyan juxtaposition.

## Narrative

The tale imagines the literal dismantling of Piccadilly — not through war or disaster, but through a deliberate, almost bureaucratic act of removal. The event is narrated with a matter-of-fact tone that heightens its strangeness. A street that has existed for centuries, that thousands walk upon daily, is simply... taken up.

> They took up Piccadilly, and carried it away [001-039].

What makes the event compelling is its combination of specificity (a real, named London street) and absurdity (its impossible removal). Dunsany treats the familiar urban world as contingent — anything, even the ground beneath one's feet, can be revoked.

## Significance

The taking up of Piccadilly belongs to a cluster of tales in the collection that unsettle the apparent stability of the human world. It connects to the urban transformations in [[the-trouble-in-leafy-green-street|The Trouble in Leafy Green Street]] and the broader theme of impermanence that runs through the collection. The event suggests that civilization itself — symbolized by its streets — is provisional.
