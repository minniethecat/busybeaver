## Overview

"The Little City" is a reflective, almost painterly vignette from Section 1 of *Fifty-One Tales* (1915). A traveller on a train — the "pre-destined 11.8 from Goraghwood to Drogheda" — glimpses a small city in a valley and is struck by its beauty and fragility. The tale moves from visual description to philosophical meditation, placing the little city's smoke and fields against the ancient, indifferent mountains that have watched civilizations rise and fall "like crocuses." In fewer than two hundred and fifty words, Dunsany sketches an entire worldview: human settlements are brief and precious, and the landscape that contains them is eternal.

## Summary

The narrator, riding a train through the Irish landscape, suddenly sees a little city in a valley [001-020]. Its smoke is caught by the sun and turned golden, so that the scene looks "like an old Italian picture where angels walk in the foreground and the rest is a blaze of gold." Beyond the golden smoke, the narrator knows, lie "the paths of the roving ships."

All around, small fields patchwork the hillsides; snow has fallen tentatively, and birds have moved to sheltered places "for every omen boded more to fall." Far hills blaze "like an aureate bulwark broken off by age and fallen from the earthward rampart of Paradise," while aloof and dark mountains stare "unconcernedly seawards" [001-020].

The narrator reflects: the mountains have sat where they sit while the cities of "the civilization of Araby and Asia arose like crocuses, and like crocuses fell." He wonders for how long there will be smoke in the valley and little fields on the hills [001-020].

## Analysis

"The Little City" is Dunsany in his most meditative mode, closer to the nature writing of the Romantic tradition than to the mythological fabulism of his other tales. The specific geography — Goraghwood to Drogheda, the Irish landscape — grounds the tale in a real journey, and the precision of "the pre-destined 11.8" gives the opening a train-timetable ordinariness that makes the subsequent vision all the more striking.

The tale's central technique is the juxtaposition of scales. The little city — its smoke, its fields, its inhabitants — exists on a human timescale of years and generations. The mountains exist on a geological timescale, and the cities of Araby and Asia — "arose like crocuses, and like crocuses fell" — have already demonstrated how brief even the grandest civilizations are. The crocus simile is perfect: spring flowers that bloom brilliantly and are gone in weeks. Dunsany applies it not to the little city but to entire civilizations, compressing the history of Araby and Asia into a single seasonal image.

The tale is also a study in golden light. The sun catching the smoke is the transformative element — without it, the city is simply a small industrial settlement; with it, the scene becomes "an old Italian picture." Dunsany suggests that beauty is not inherent in things but is conferred by light and perspective — and that the traveller, passing through, is privileged to witness it fleetingly.

The closing question — "for how long there would be smoke in the valley and little fields on the hills" — is left unanswered. The mountains do not reply. They stare "unconcernedly seawards," and their unconcern is the tale's final, quiet verdict on human permanence.

## Characters

- **The narrator** — a traveller on a train, whose sudden vision of the little city prompts a meditation on beauty, time, and impermanence. He is both observer and philosopher, turning a passing glimpse into a cosmic reflection.
- **The mountains** — not personified but characterized: "aloof and dark," "grey and watchful," staring "unconcernedly seawards." They are the silent witnesses to all human endeavor, and their indifference is the tale's deepest statement.
- **The little city** — unnamed, unreachable, visible only from a train window. It is the tale's subject and symbol: a fragile human creation glowing briefly in the sun.
