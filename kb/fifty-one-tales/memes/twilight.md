## Overview

Twilight in *Fifty-One Tales* is more than a time of day — it is the signature atmosphere of Dunsany's narrative world. It is the threshold hour when the ordinary recedes and the extraordinary becomes visible: old gods are glimpsed recumbent in the fading light, prophecies unfold, and the boundaries between the actual and the imagined grow porous. For Dunsany, twilight is the medium through which wonder enters the tale.

## In the Tales

The revelatory power of twilight appears early in the collection: "when they saw there, suddenly in the twilight, that old recumbent" figure — a moment of recognition that depends upon the half-light (001-121). The word "suddenly" pairs with "twilight" to suggest that this hour enables discoveries that full daylight would obscure. In another passage, a figure "went to gardens" to gather "stars while yet it is twilight, secretly one by one" (005-104), an act of delicate cosmic maintenance possible only in the transitional hour between day and night.

Twilight also attends moments of tranquil contemplation. One narrator gazes upon a scene "so far below me that in the tranquil twilight" (007-065) the distance itself becomes meditative. The fatal quality of twilight emerges in another passage: "there in the twilight, and this must have been fatal" (007-076), linking the hour not only to beauty and revelation but to irreversible consequence. Throughout the tales, twilight serves as the connective atmosphere — the shared element in which shepherds, gods, dreamers, and the dead all move.
