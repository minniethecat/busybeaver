## Overview

Ithaca is the island kingdom of [[odysseus|Odysseus]] in Greek mythology. In the Fifty-One Tales, it appears in "[[death-and-odysseus|Death and Odysseus]]," where [[death|Death]] — motivated by Love's mockery in the [[olympus|Olympian courts]] — travels to Ithaca to frighten the hero.

## In "Death and Odysseus"

[[death|Death]]'s journey to Ithaca is the central action of the tale:

> And he came soon to Ithaca and the hall that Athene knew. [002-109]

[[death|Death]] finds [[odysseus|Odysseus]] in his hall and attempts to frighten him — but [[odysseus|Odysseus]], the man who has outwitted gods and monsters, is not easily intimidated. The tale plays on the audience's knowledge of the *Odyssey*: this is the same hall where the suitors were slaughtered, the same hero who returned from the dead to reclaim his kingdom.

## Thematic Significance

Ithaca in Dunsany's tale is not the Ithaca of Homer's pastoral nostalgia — the place [[odysseus|Odysseus]] spent twenty years trying to reach. Instead, it is the domestic seat of a hero who has already survived everything, including a journey to the underworld. [[death|Death]]'s arrival at Ithaca is therefore charged with dramatic irony: what can [[death|Death]] do to a man who has already been among the dead?

The mention that the hall is one "that [[athene|Athene]] knew" invokes the goddess's protection of [[odysseus|Odysseus]] throughout the *Odyssey*. Even in Dunsany's abbreviated treatment, the hall carries the weight of the Homeric tradition — it is a place where gods have walked and heroes have fought.
