## Overview

The Immortals are the central revelation of Lord Dunsany's prose poem "Alone the Immortals" from Section 1 of *Fifty-One Tales* (1915). When the narrator journeys to a legendary valley to mourn the dead years, he discovers not fragile remnants but colossal beings — the years themselves, transfigured into immortal creators of all that exists in time. The tale inverts conventional ideas of mortality and permanence: what seems most fleeting (a single year) is revealed as most enduring.

## Role in the Tales

The narrator hears a rumor that "on the wrong side of the deserts of Cathay" lies a valley where all the dead years are shut away. He resolves to journey there "by ways of dream" with a wreath of mourning to lay at their feet:

> And I said: I will take a wreath, a wreath of mourning, and lay it at their feet in token of my sorrow for their dooms.
>
> [001-033]

Finding no flower fragile enough to honor the years — "the lily looked too large and the laurel looked too solemn" — he makes a slender wreath of daisies. He arrives at the valley but finds only empty grass. Looking upward in moonlight, he sees the truth:

> I suddenly saw colossi sitting near, and towering up and blotting out the stars and filling the night with blackness; and at those idols' feet I saw praying and making obeisance kings and the days that are and all times and all cities and all nations and all their gods.
>
> [001-033]

Asked who these colossal beings are, a voice answers:

> "Alone the Immortals."
>
> [004-169]

The narrator protests — he came to mourn frail, delicate years, not to see "dread gods." The voice corrects him:

> "These _are_ the years that are dead, alone the immortals; all years to be are Their children — They fashioned their smiles and their laughter; all earthly kings They have crowned, all gods They have created; all the events to be flow down from their feet like a river, the worlds are flying pebbles that They have already thrown, and Time and all his centuries behind him kneel there with bended crests in token of vassalage at Their potent feet."
>
> [004-173]

The narrator returns to his own land "comforted" — the years he mourned are not lost but transfigured into the very creators of all that exists.

## Analysis

"Alone the Immortals" is one of Dunsany's most condensed theological inversions. In a single paragraph of revelation, the tale reverses every conventional hierarchy: the dead years, seemingly the most ephemeral of things, are in fact the only immortals. Time — elsewhere in the collection a malevolent destroyer — kneels to them in vassalage. The gods men worship are mere creations of the dead years. The image of worlds as "flying pebbles that They have already thrown" compresses entire cosmogonies into a gesture.

The tale's title is itself the punchline withheld until the final passage: "alone the immortals" — only the dead years are immortal. Everything else, including the gods and Time itself, passes.
