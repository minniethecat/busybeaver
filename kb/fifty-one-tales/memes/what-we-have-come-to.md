## Overview

"What We Have Come To" is perhaps the most savage single paragraph in *Fifty-One Tales*. An advertiser, seeing cathedral spires over the downs, weeps — and then reveals that his tears are not for their beauty but for the missed opportunity: "If only this were an advertisement of Beefo." In fewer than a hundred words, Dunsany delivers a complete indictment of the commercial imagination and its inability to perceive value that cannot be monetized.

## Summary

An advertiser looks out over the downs and sees cathedral spires in the distance. He weeps at the sight [008-007].

But his tears are not those of a man moved by beauty. "If only," he says, "this were an advertisement of Beefo, so nice, so nutritious, try it in your soup, ladies like it" [008-007]. The cathedral, in his eyes, is wasted — not because it fails to be beautiful but because it fails to sell something.

## Analysis

### The Colonized Imagination

The tale's horror is not that the advertiser is evil but that his imagination has been colonized by commerce so thoroughly that he cannot see a cathedral as anything other than unrealized advertising space. His tears are genuine — he is truly moved — but the emotion is instantly routed through the only channel available to him: the logic of the sell. The cathedral is beautiful; beauty attracts attention; attention should be monetized; therefore the cathedral's failure to have a brand logo on it is a kind of tragedy.

### Beefo as Anti-Symbol

"Beefo" — a name both absurd and plausibly commercial — is Dunsany's invention of a product that stands for all products. It is a meat extract, a soup base, a household staple, the kind of thing advertised on trams and hoardings. By naming it, Dunsany makes the advertiser's lament specific and therefore more damning: he is not weeping for commerce in the abstract but for a particular brand of bouillon cube.

### The Downs and the Spire

The setting — cathedral spires over the downs — juxtaposes two kinds of English sublimity: the ancient landscape and the medieval church. Both predate commerce, both resist its logic, and both are, to the advertiser, wasted opportunities. Dunsany's point is not that advertising is ugly but that it renders everything else invisible except as unrealized advertising.

## Characters

- **The advertiser** — the tale's sole figure, a man whose soul has been so thoroughly shaped by commerce that he perceives beauty only as a missed marketing opportunity. He is not a villain; he is a casualty.

## Themes

- **Commerce and the soul** — the tale diagnoses a spiritual condition in which value exists only insofar as it can be sold.
- **[[beauty]] and perception** — the advertiser *sees* the cathedral but cannot *perceive* it; his perceptual apparatus has been rewired.
- **Modernity** — the tale is a lament for what has been lost in the transition from a world of cathedrals to a world of Beefo advertisements.
