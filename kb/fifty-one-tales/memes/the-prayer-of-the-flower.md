## Overview

"The Prayer of the Flower" (titled "The Prayer of the Flowers" in the corpus) is a lyrical lament for the natural world in the age of industry. Flowers speak on the West wind — "the lovable, the old, the lazy West wind, blowing ceaselessly, blowing sleepily, going Greecewards" — and cry out against the loss of the woods, the advance of great engines, and the "cancrous cities" that spread over the grass. The tale is a dialogue between the modern world's victims and the ancient gods: Pan, from far Arcady, answers them with a single line of reassurance — "Be patient a little, these things are not for long."

## Summary

The tale opens with the voice of the flowers carried on the West wind [001-018]. Their lament is direct and mournful: "The woods have gone away, they have fallen and left us; men love us no longer, we are lonely by moonlight." The great engines rush over the fields, their ways "hard and terrible up and down the land." The cities — "cancrous" — spread over the grass, clattering in their lairs, blemishing the night with their glitter.

The flowers' cry rises to a desperate invocation:

> "The woods are gone, O Pan, the woods, the woods. And thou art far, O Pan, and far away."
>
> [001-018]

The narrator then identifies himself: standing by night between two railway embankments on the edge of a Midland city, watching trains pass — "once in every two minutes" on one embankment, "twice in every five" on the other. The factories glare, and the sky wears "the fearful look that it wears in dreams of fever."

Then, beating musically up the wind, comes Pan's answer from Arcady:

> "Be patient a little, these things are not for long."
>
> [001-018]

## Analysis

"The Prayer of the Flower" is Dunsany's most explicit engagement with industrialization, and it reads as a prophecy delivered in 1915 that the twentieth century would only confirm. The tale's structure is a prayer and its answer: the flowers cry out, and Pan — the god of wild places — replies. But Pan's comfort is not a promise of restoration. It is a promise of impermanence. "These things are not for long" — the factories, the engines, the cities — will pass, as all human works pass. The woods may be gone now, but the city will follow them into oblivion.

The narrator's position is significant. He stands *between* two railway embankments, literally caught in the machinery he observes. He is not in Arcady; he is in the Midlands, on the edge of a city, watching trains. He is a modern man, and his ability to hear both the flowers' cry and Pan's reply suggests that the old gods are still accessible — but only to those who listen.

The tale's imagery is apocalyptic. The sky above the factories wears "the fearful look that it wears in dreams of fever" — a simile that transforms industrial pollution into a symptom of disease. The cities are "cancrous," a word Dunsany uses deliberately: they are not merely ugly, but a malignancy consuming the body of the world.

Yet the final note is not despair but patience. Pan does not say "I will save you" or "I will destroy the cities." He says, simply, "Be patient a little." The implication is cosmic: on the scale of geological or divine time, the Industrial Revolution is a passing irritation. The flowers — and Pan — have been here far longer, and will remain far longer, than the engines.

## Characters

- **The flowers** — the tale's speakers, whose voice travels on the West wind. They mourn the loss of the woods and the advance of the city, but they are not passive victims — they cry out, and they are answered.
- **Pan** — present only as a voice from Arcady, beating "musically up wind." His single line of dialogue is the tale's theological center: an assurance that the modern world's dominion is temporary.
- **The narrator** — a figure standing between two railway embankments in the Midlands. He is the witness, the listener, the one who can hear both the flowers' lament and Pan's reply.
