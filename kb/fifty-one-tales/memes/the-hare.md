## Overview

The Hare is an animal character in Lord Dunsany's tale "The True History of the Hare and the Tortoise," published in *Fifty-One Tales*. Unlike the familiar Aesopian fable where the hare loses through overconfidence and a nap, Dunsany's Hare faces a different kind of defeat: he is talked out of the race entirely by the tortoise's supporters, who convince him that racing is futile and undignified.

## Role in the Tales

The tale begins with a debate over whether the Hare or the Tortoise is faster. It is decided that they shall race five hundred yards. The Hare is openly contemptuous, calling the whole affair "ridiculous nonsense," while the Tortoise welcomes the contest with calm confidence.

As the race begins, the Hare dashes off for about a hundred yards, then stops. But it is not sleep that defeats him — it is the persistent chatter of the Tortoise's supporters. They engage the Hare in philosophical debate about the purpose of racing, and eventually the Hare simply loses interest. "What is the use of it?" the Hare asks, stopping for good.

> The Hare dashed off for about a hundred yards, and then stopped. "Ridiculous nonsense!" said the Hare, and it was all his backers could do to get him to run any further.
>
> — 004-098

The tale is a satire on debate and public opinion, where the swifter competitor is defeated not on the field but in the mind, through an excess of reasoning about the meaninglessness of the contest itself.
