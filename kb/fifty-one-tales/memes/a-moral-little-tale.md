## Overview

"A Moral Little Tale" is one of the ultra-short tales from Section 1 of *Fifty-One Tales* (1915) by Lord Dunsany. The entire tale is its title — four words that promise a story with a moral, a lesson, a point, and deliver none of these things. The tale is an anti-fable: it announces the moral form and then refuses to perform it, leaving the reader with only the expectation of instruction and the comedy of its absence.

## Summary

The tale is its title alone:

> "A Moral Little Tale"
>
> [001-034]

Nothing follows. The title promises what the text withholds — a "moral little tale" that is, in fact, nothing but the promise of itself. The adjective "little" carries a double edge: it is self-deprecating (the conventional humility of the fabulist), but also a sly admission that the tale is so "little" as to be nonexistent.

## Analysis

### The Anti-Fable

Dunsany positions this piece as a joke at the expense of the didactic tradition in literature. The fable — from Aesop through La Fontaine to the Victorian moral tale — exists to deliver a lesson. Its narrative is instrumental: the events exist not for their own sake but to illustrate a general truth about virtue, vice, or human nature.

"A Moral Little Tale" acknowledges this tradition by invoking its conventions (the title promises morality, the diminutive "little" signals the genre of children's lessons) and then subverts them through pure absence. The reader who opens a collection of tales and encounters this title expects to find, beneath it, a brief story ending with "The moral of this tale is…" Dunsany gives them four words and stops.

The effect is a kind of meta-fictional laughter — not at the tale, but at the reader's own expectation. We have been trained by centuries of didactic literature to expect stories to justify themselves through instruction. Dunsany asks: what if the story refuses? What if the tale is its title and nothing more?

### The Collection's Rhythmic Structure

"A Moral Little Tale" belongs to the cluster of ultra-short pieces in Section 1 of *Fifty-One Tales* — a deliberate compositional strategy. After longer meditations like "The Assignation" and "Charon," Dunsany shifts to a rapid-fire sequence of titles that function almost as a single poem. Each title is a complete thought; the sequence of them — "A Mistaken Identity," "The True History of the Hare and the Tortoise," "Alone the Immortals," "A Moral Little Tale," "The Return of Song" — creates a rhythm of expectations raised and subverted.

This technique was unusual for 1915, but it prefigures the fragmentary and conceptual strategies of later modernist and postmodernist writing. Dunsany is, in these pieces, treating the tale collection not as an anthology of discrete works but as a unified composition in which even absence — the empty space beneath a title — is a meaningful formal element.
