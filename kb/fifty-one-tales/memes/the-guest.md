## Overview

"The Guest" is among Dunsany's most chillingly urbane tales. Set in an ornate London restaurant, it follows a young man who has reserved a table for two but dines alone — or appears to. Throughout an elaborate dinner, he converses with the empty chair opposite him, dropping references to King Seti, Cheops, Helen of Troy, and Cleopatra with the casualness of an old acquaintance. The tale's final revelation — that the guest is Death, and that the young man has poisoned his own coffee — transforms a comic monologue into something far darker: a suicide arranged as a dinner party, a mortal man summoning Death to his own table on his own terms.

## Summary

A young man arrives alone at an ornate London restaurant at eight o'clock, where a table for two has been reserved [001-015]. He has chosen the dinner carefully by letter a week in advance. When the waiter asks about the other guest, he replies: "You probably won't see him till the coffee comes" [001-015].

Throughout the meal, the young man addresses the empty chair, carrying on a monologue. Over the soup he says, "I think you knew my father." Over the Burgundy he becomes more voluble, naming mutual acquaintances: King Seti of Egypt, Cheops (who "has left the house that he built for your reception"), Helen of Troy, Cleopatra. He treats Death as a colleague, even teasing him — "You never knew the mermaids nor the fairies nor the lovely goddesses of long ago, that's where we have the best of you" [001-015].

The conversation turns to London, and to a lady the young man had hoped would join him. "She may not after all be as lovely as Helen of Troy," he muses. Then, with the coffee, he drops a tabloid into his cup, drinks, and falls to the floor:

> Then having drunk his coffee he fell on to the floor by a foot of the empty chair, and a doctor who was dining in the room bent over him and announced to the anxious manager the visible presence of the young man's guest.
>
> [001-015]

Death has arrived — not as an intruder, but as a dinner guest, summoned by invitation.

## Analysis

"The Guest" is a masterpiece of tonal control. Dunsany opens in the mode of social comedy — the fussy young man, the elaborate dinner ordered by post, the waiter's polite confusion — and sustains it almost to the final sentence. The monologue to the empty chair reads, for most of its length, as the self-indulgent chatter of a wealthy eccentric. But the references accumulate with a darker logic: Seti, Cheops, Helen, Cleopatra — all dead, all acquainted with the guest at the empty chair.

The tale's power lies in its inversion of expectations. Death is not a skeletal horror crashing through the restaurant door; he is an invited guest, seated politely, served a dinner he does not eat. The young man has planned everything — the menu, the wine, the timing — and his suicide is not an act of despair but of control. He will meet Death when and where he chooses, over a good Burgundy.

Dunsany also uses the restaurant setting to ironic effect. The ornate dining room, with its anxious manager and professional waiters, is a temple of civilized behaviour — and into it walks Death, not as an intruder but as a diner. The final image — "the visible presence of the young man's guest" — implies that Death, having been acknowledged, becomes visible to all. The empty chair is empty no longer.

The tale shares DNA with the medieval *danse macabre* tradition, but updates it for the modern city. Death does not come to the young man in a plague-ridden village; he comes to a London restaurant, in evening dress, over coffee. The horror is all the greater for its politeness.

## Characters

- **The young man** — the unnamed protagonist who has planned his own death with the same care he gives to ordering dinner. He treats Death as an equal, even a subordinate, and his suicide is an act of agency rather than surrender.
- **Death** — never described, never seen directly, present only as the occupant of an empty chair. He is the listener to the young man's monologue, and at the tale's end he becomes "visible."
- **The waiter** — a minor figure who serves as the voice of ordinary incomprehension: he sees the empty chair but cannot see its occupant.
- **The doctor** — another diner who, at the critical moment, confirms Death's presence to the anxious manager.
