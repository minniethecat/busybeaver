## Overview

Pegasus, the winged horse of classical mythology, receives a brief but evocative mention in *Fifty-One Tales*. Dunsany invokes him not as a character in a narrative but as a symbol — a creature of open fields and unfettered movement whose very name conjures a world of mythic freedom that stands in contrast to the constrained, mortal world of the tales' human figures.

## Role in the Tales

Pegasus is mentioned in a single passage in Section 3, where Dunsany lists him among the inhabitants of an imagined landscape of classical abundance:

> fields Pegasus prances. Here Pegasus gallops and browses upon...
>
> [003-022]

The passage places Pegasus in a mythological panorama — a landscape populated by the figures of ancient story, existing in a timeless realm parallel to but separate from the mortal world. In Dunsany's characteristic mode, the mythical is not treated as something that once existed and is now lost, but as something that continues to exist in a register just beyond human perception.

The brokenness of the quotation — it begins and ends mid-sentence — is itself revealing. Pegasus appears in the tale as a fragment, a glimpse, as though the narrator has briefly tuned into a frequency on which the mythic world broadcasts, catching a few words before the signal fades. This technique of partial revelation runs throughout *Fifty-One Tales*: the supernatural is never fully displayed, only half-overheard.

Pegasus, as a creature of flight and poetry, belongs to the same imaginative cosmos as [[the-muses|the Muses]] and [[pan|Pan]] — beings who represent a mode of existence unburdened by the gravity, both literal and figurative, that weighs upon human life.

## Symbolic Significance

In the classical tradition, Pegasus carries multiple associations: the winged horse born from Medusa's blood, tamed by Bellerophon, and ultimately placed among the stars as a constellation. Dunsany strips away the narrative mythology and keeps only the image — a horse galloping in open fields — which he deploys as pure symbol.

The contrast is between movement and stillness, between the open field and the enclosed space. Pegasus "prances" and "gallops" — verbs of free, joyful motion — in a passage that otherwise catalogues the static possessions of a classical golden age. His presence suggests that the defining quality of the mythic is not grandeur but freedom: a mode of being unconstrained by mortality, geography, or the narrow purposes that circumscribe human life.
