## Overview

Moleshill Street is a [[london|London]] street that appears in "[[the-trouble-in-leafy-green-street|The Trouble in Leafy Green Street]]." It is the location of the idol-shop where an old man mumbles and sells small gods — including The God of Rainy Cheerfulness, whose worship brings down an unspecified doom on [[leafy-green-street]].

## In "The Trouble in Leafy Green Street"

The tale opens with the protagonist's visit to Moleshill Street:

> She went to the idol-shop in Moleshill Street, where the old man mumbles, and said: "I want a god to worship when it is wet." [006-172]

The old man warns her about the penalties of idolatry, but she persists. Later, on a rainy day when her spirits have fallen:

> she bethought her of Moleshill Street and the mumbling man. [006-185]

He brings forth the grey stone idol, mumbles of guarantees ("although he put nothing on paper"), and she pays his "preposterous price."

## Thematic Significance

Moleshill Street is a place where the supernatural is commercialized. The old man's shop is not a temple but a retail establishment — he "mumbles" in the manner of a trader rather than a priest, and his guarantees carry the irony of warranty language applied to divinity. The street's name itself — "Moleshill" — is suitably diminutive: this is not a grand avenue of gods but a small, obscure [[london|London]] street where idols are sold like umbrellas.

The transaction on Moleshill Street is the efficient cause of the doom that descends on [[leafy-green-street|Leafy Green Street]]. The idol-shop is the point where the mundane world (a woman trying to cheer herself up on a rainy day) intersects with forces that exceed it — and the old man's mumbled warnings suggest he knows exactly what he is selling.
