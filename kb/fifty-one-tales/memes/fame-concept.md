## Overview

The concept of Fame runs through *Fifty-One Tales* as a meditation on artistic ambition and the nature of recognition. Dunsany, himself a successful writer whose literary reputation would later fade, treats fame with characteristic irony — as something simultaneously desirable and worthless, pursued by poets and cheapened by the marketplace.

## In the Tales

Fame is treated most directly in the untitled prose-poem beginning at [001-059], which personifies Fame as a woman courted by a poet. The tale traces the arc of artistic devotion: the poet makes his offerings, Fame accepts them casually, and when the poet finally protests her indifference, she abandons him. The allegory is transparent — artistic recognition is a gift, not a wage:

> "Lovely Fame, even in the highways and the byways you have not forgotten to speak pleasantly of me."
>
> [001-069]

The poet's plea captures the artist's essential vulnerability: the need to be remembered, to have one's work acknowledged by the world. Dunsany's answer is bleak — Fame does not owe the artist anything, and resentment only drives her further away.

The theme deepens in Section 4 [004-058–004-063], where Fame encounters Notoriety, who claims her name. True Fame's silent retreat is a satire of how genuine achievement is drowned out by mere publicity:

> "'Who are you?' Fame said to her. 'I am Fame,' said Notoriety. Then Fame stole softly away."
>
> [004-061–004-063]

## Interpretation

Dunsany's treatment of Fame is shaped by both classical tradition and modernist irony. The personification echoes Greek and Roman depictions of Fama (rumor), but Dunsany strips away the grandeur. His Fame is a woman walking on highways — accessible, mundane, and ultimately indifferent. She is not the winged goddess spreading news across the world; she is a passer-by who may or may not glance in your direction.

The Fame/Notoriety contrast anticipates twentieth-century media criticism. Dunsany saw, with remarkable prescience, that mass culture would blur the distinction between achievement and visibility. The true Fame "stole softly away" — a prophecy of the modern condition where depth yields to noise.

## Fame and the Classical Tradition

Dunsany's Fame draws on a rich classical lineage. In Greek mythology, Pheme (Φήμη) was the personification of fame and rumor — a winged messenger who spread reports, both true and false. In Roman tradition, Fama (described memorably by Virgil in *Aeneid* Book IV) was a monstrous bird-like creature with countless eyes, ears, and tongues, who "clung to false and wrong as willingly as true."

Dunsany's Fame is notably different from these classical models. Where Virgil's Fama is a terrifying force of chaos, Dunsany's Fame is quiet, almost passive — a woman walking on highways, accessible and mundane. And where classical Fame deals in public rumor, Dunsany's Fame operates in the intimate domain of the artist's relationship with posterity:

> "Fame singing in the highways, and trifling as she sang, with sordid things."
>
> [001-059]

This domestication of the classical figure reflects Dunsany's characteristic move: taking cosmic and mythological forces and reducing them to human scale. His Fame is not the monster of Virgil but something closer to an indifferent passer-by — perhaps more terrifying for being ordinary.

## The Artist's Complaint

The poet's complaint against Fame is one of the oldest themes in Western literature. From Horace's *Exegi monumentum* ("I have built a monument more lasting than bronze") to Shakespeare's sonnets promising immortality through verse, poets have always negotiated with posterity. Dunsany's innovation is to strip away the grandeur and leave only the bitterness:

> "And Fame turned her back on him and walked away, but in departing she turned and spoke."
>
> [001-072]

What Fame says as she departs is not recorded. The silence is deliberate — the poet, and the reader, are left to imagine the worst. It is a characteristic Dunsany touch: the most devastating rejection is the one left unspoken.
