## Overview

Athene (Athena) is the Greek goddess of wisdom, war, and craftsmanship, who appears in a single brief reference within Lord Dunsany's tale "Death and Odysseus" in *Fifty-One Tales* (1915). Her mention is fleeting — the narrative notes in passing that Odysseus returned "to Ithaca and the hall that Athene knew" [002-109] — but it carries the weight of the entire Homeric tradition behind it, anchoring Dunsany's tale in the mythological landscape of the *Odyssey*.

## Role in the Tales

Athene's presence in "Death and Odysseus" is atmospheric rather than active. She does not appear as a character; she is invoked through architectural memory — the hall in Ithaca that she once knew. This single line evokes the goddess's long relationship with Odysseus in Homer's epic, where she was his divine patron, guiding and protecting him through his wanderings.

> …to Ithaca and the hall that Athene knew…
>
> [002-109]

In Dunsany's tale, which imagines a post-Homeric encounter between the aged Odysseus and Death, the reference to Athene serves as a reminder of the divine world that once intervened in mortal affairs — and that now seems distant. Odysseus, in Dunsany's telling, must face Death alone, without the goddess who once shielded him.

## Character Analysis

Dunsany's Athene is notable primarily for her absence. In Homer, she is an active presence — appearing in disguise, offering counsel, intervening in battle. In *Fifty-One Tales*, she is reduced to a memory, a name attached to a hall. This diminishment is characteristic of Dunsany's treatment of the Greek gods throughout the collection: they have receded from the world, leaving mortals to face the cosmic forces — Death, Time, Fate — without divine mediation.

The reference also serves a structural function within "Death and Odysseus," linking Dunsany's tale to the Homeric canon and establishing the temporal distance between the world of the *Odyssey* and the world of the tale. Athene's hall is still there; Athene herself is not.
