## Overview

Pity in *Fifty-One Tales* operates at the threshold between the mortal and the divine. It is the emotion that breaches the impassive order of the universe — the moment when a cosmic force like Death or Time pauses to acknowledge the fragility of living things. Dunsany treats pity not as weakness but as the one interruption in an otherwise mechanical cosmos, the single crack through which something like grace might enter.

## In the Tales

The most striking instance occurs when "Death wept out of pity for his opponent, or because he knew that he" must claim even the cleverest of mortals (006-005). Here, Death — the ultimate impersonal force — is momentarily humanized by compassion, suggesting that pity can reach across the gulf between the eternal and the ephemeral.

In another tale, the plea arises: "be not good to him but pity us, and let lovely things live on" (006-112). This prayer inverts conventional morality, valuing pity over justice and beauty over desert. The contrast with "unpitying Time" (003-147) underscores the stakes: where pity is absent, the cosmos grinds forward without regard for what it destroys. The prayer offered "on the little forgotten thing" (006-086) extends this compassion to the smallest and most overlooked — a gesture Dunsany seems to endorse as the proper response to a universe that otherwise offers none.
