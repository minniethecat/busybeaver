## Overview

"The Return of the Exiles" is a tale in Lord Dunsany's *Fifty-One Tales* [001-050] that treats the event of exiles returning to their homeland. Like "The Return of Song," it participates in Dunsany's recurring pattern of departure and return. But where Song's return is purely restorative, the return of exiles carries a more ambivalent emotional weight: those who return may find that home has changed, or that they themselves have become strangers to the place they once knew.

## Narrative

The tale imagines exiles — people driven from their homeland — making the journey back. The return is an event loaded with expectation and anxiety. What will they find? Will the place recognize them? Will they recognize the place? Dunsany's characteristic restraint leaves much unsaid, allowing the reader to inhabit the emotional space of uncertain homecoming.

The exiles' return mirrors the broader thematic concern in *Fifty-One Tales* with things that have been lost and may or may not be recovered. Unlike [[the-return-of-song|Song's return]], which is presented as pure restoration, the exiles' return carries the weight of time passed, changes undergone, and the possibility that what was lost cannot truly be regained.

## Significance

The Return of the Exiles resonates with one of the most ancient narrative patterns in world literature: the nostos, or homecoming. From Odysseus returning to Ithaca to the Israelites returning from Babylon, the return of exiles is a foundational story of identity and belonging. Dunsany strips this pattern to its essence — the event of return itself, without elaboration of what came before or what follows.

The tale also speaks to the condition of exile as a metaphor for the human experience. In Dunsany's cosmos, where [[time|Time]] destroys cities and [[nature|Nature]] begs at doors, all humans are in some sense exiles — displaced from an imagined home of permanence and security. The Return of the Exiles, like [[the-return-of-song|The Return of Song]], offers hope but not certainty.

The pairing of this tale with "The Return of Song" in the collection suggests a deliberate thematic architecture. Song and Exiles — art and people — both depart and both return. The world is not static; it is defined by cycles of loss and recovery, departure and homecoming.

## Exile in Dunsany's Cosmos

Exile is a recurrent condition in Dunsany's imagined universe. His gods are exiled from human affairs, his Nature begs at doors, his Fame walks highways indifferent to those who call her name. The Return of the Exiles [001-050] universalizes this condition: the exiles are not specific people driven from a specific place but an archetype of displacement.

The tale's placement in Section 1, near the end of the collection, gives it a summary quality. After encounters with Death [001-016], Fame [001-059], Time [001-051], and the doomed citadel of Thlunrana [001-037], the return of exiles offers something rare in Dunsany's cosmos: hope. Not triumph, not resolution, but the simple possibility that what was lost may yet be regained.

## The Homecoming Motif in Early Modernism

Dunsany wrote *Fifty-One Tales* in the years immediately before the First World War — a period when the theme of exile and return would soon acquire terrible urgency. The war would create millions of exiles and refugees; the idea of "return" would become charged with grief and impossibility.

Dunsany's tale, written before these events, captures the homecoming motif in its archetypal form — not yet burdened by the specific tragedies of the twentieth century. The exiles return; what they find is left for the reader to imagine. This restraint is, in retrospect, the tale's greatest strength. It does not prescribe the emotion of homecoming; it simply stages the event and lets the reader inhabit it.
