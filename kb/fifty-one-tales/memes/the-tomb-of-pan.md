## Overview

"The Tomb of Pan" closes Section 8 of *Fifty-One Tales* and serves as a companion piece to "[[the-death-of-pan]]," revisiting the theme of the old god's passing from a different angle. Where the earlier tale found Pan alive and laughing, this one finds him commemorated — entombed in marble, his head humbled, angels' feet on his neck. The enlightened people congratulate themselves on having outgrown him. But a few weep apart. The tale is an elegy not for Pan, who may or may not be dead, but for the capacity to mourn him honestly.

## Summary

The people of the "enlightened lands" declare that old-time Pan is dead and resolve to build a tomb and monument, "that the dreadful worship of long ago may be remembered and avoided by all" [008-008].

Builders raise a white marble tomb. It gleams after sunset with the rays of the departed sun. While they build, some mourn Pan; others revile him; some call the builders to stop and weep; others call them to leave no memorial at all. But the builders build on steadily [008-008].

When the tomb is finished, it stands like a "steep sea-cliff." Pan is carved with humbled head, angels' feet pressed upon his neck. The afterglow is rosy on his huge marble bulk [008-008].

The enlightened people come, see the tomb, and "all deplored him and his wicked age." But a few weep apart — "because of the death of Pan" [008-008].

## Analysis

### The Two Mournings

The tale distinguishes between two kinds of response to Pan's death. The enlightened majority deplore him and his age; their relationship to Pan is one of relieved superiority. The few who "weep apart" mourn not the wickedness of Pan's age but Pan himself. Their grief is not political or moral but personal and theological. Dunsany does not say which response is correct — but the tale's closing image, the rosy afterglow on Pan's marble bulk, suggests that the god, even in effigy, retains a beauty the enlightened cannot account for.

### The Monument as Irony

The tomb is explicitly built so that Pan's worship "may be remembered and avoided." But a monument is an act of remembrance, and remembrance is the first step toward revival. By building the tomb, the enlightened ensure that Pan will never be forgotten. Every generation that gazes on his humbled image may produce a few who weep apart — and from those few, the old worship may be reborn.

### The Afterglow

Dunsany's use of light is precise. The sun has set — the age of Pan is over — but the afterglow remains, rosy on the marble. The afterglow is not sunlight but its memory, not the god but the afterimage of the god. It is enough to move the few who can still see it.

## Characters

- **The enlightened people** — the builders of the tomb, who commemorate Pan in order to celebrate his passing. Their enlightenment consists in having outgrown the god, but their act of commemoration ensures his persistence.
- **The builders** — anonymous, steady, indifferent to the debates around them. They build on regardless.
- **The few who weep** — the remnant who mourn Pan not as a wicked relic but as a lost god. They are the tale's true subjects.

## Themes

- **[[pan]] and the old gods** — the tale revisits Dunsany's recurring meditation on the death and possible survival of the ancient divinities.
- **Memory and monument** — the tomb is meant to bury Pan but instead preserves him.
- **Enlightenment and loss** — what the enlightened gain in superiority they lose in the capacity for genuine mourning.
