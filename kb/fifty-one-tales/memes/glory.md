## Overview

Glory in *Fifty-One Tales* is the visible summit of human endeavor — the city, the civilization, the achievement that Man builds as testimony to his own significance. Yet Dunsany's treatment of glory is always shadowed by irony: the higher the glory rises, the more precarious its position. Glory is not an enduring state but a peak from which the only direction is down.

## In the Tales

The definitive statement comes in the image of civilization's apex: "Man raised on high the final glory of his civilization" (008-119). The word "final" is crucial — this glory is not one among many but the last, the ultimate, implying that after it comes nothing. The glory rests upon "the furthest rampart of the final glory of Man" (008-125), a precarious perch at the edge of the known world. The achievement is described as "this glory of Man's achievement, this city" (008-127), equating urban civilization with glory itself.

Yet glory is not exclusively urban or monumental. In a quieter register, something may shine "even as the glory of morning upon the water" (006-099), suggesting that glory can also be natural, transient, and beautiful without being constructed. The builders who erected "their gorgeous home, their city of glory, above the lair" (008-141) embody the dual nature of glory — elevated and magnificent, yet resting above something dark that waits beneath.
