## Overview

"Alone the Immortals" is a short, dreamlike meditation from Section 4 of *Fifty-One Tales* (1915). The narrator sets out on a journey of mourning, seeking the valley where dead years are said to lie, intending to lay a wreath at their feet. What he finds subverts his entire premise: the years he thought fragile and perished are in fact towering, indestructible colossi before whom [[time]] himself bows. The tale inverts the conventional relationship between time and mortality, suggesting that past years do not die — they become immortal.

## Summary

The tale opens with the narrator hearing a rumour: far away, on the wrong side of the deserts of Cathay, in a country of winter, lies a valley that holds all the years that are dead, visible only to the moon and to those who dream in its rays [004-169].

The narrator resolves to go there by "ways of dream" and to lay a wreath of mourning at the feet of the dead years. Searching among the flowers for something suitably frail — the lily is too large, the laurel too solemn — he settles on a wreath of daisies, as fragile as the years he means to mourn [004-169].

Travelling by paths of mystery to the valley beneath the mountainous moon, he searches the grass for the remains of those poor slight years, but finds nothing. He concludes: "Time has shattered them and swept them away and left not even any faint remains" [004-169].

But when he looks up, he sees not scattered remnants but colossal figures towering into the night, blotting out the stars, at whose feet kings, nations, cities, and gods kneel in obeisance. Neither incense nor sacrificial smoke reaches their heads. They sit "not to be measured, not to be over-thrown, not to be worn away" [004-169].

> "Who are those?" I said.
>
> One answered: "Alone the Immortals."
>
> [004-169]

The narrator protests: he came not to see dread gods but to mourn little dead years. The answer transforms his understanding:

> "These _are_ the years that are dead, alone the immortals; all years to be are Their children — They fashioned their smiles and their laughter; all earthly kings They have crowned, all gods They have created; all the events to be flow down from their feet like a river, the worlds are flying pebbles that They have already thrown, and Time and all his centuries behind him kneel there with bended crests in token of vassalage at Their potent feet."
>
> [004-169]

The narrator turns away with his wreath and returns home, comforted [004-169].

## Analysis

### The Inversion of Mortality

Dunsany's central move is an ontological reversal. The narrator, like the reader, assumes that years are perishable things — they come, they go, they die. The wreath of daisies is chosen precisely because it is as fragile as the years the narrator intends to mourn. But the revelation at the valley is that the relationship is exactly inverted: the years do not die; they are the only things that *do not* die. Everything else — kings, nations, gods, Time itself — kneels before them.

This is a genuinely startling philosophical proposition disguised as a fairy tale. The dead year of one's childhood, far from being lost, is immortal; the present moment is merely its child.

### The Daisies as False Premise

The narrator's careful selection of flowers serves as a dramatic prop whose meaning is wholly reversed by the tale's end. The lily is too large, the laurel too solemn — so he weaves daisies, "scarce less fragile or less frail than one of those delicate forgotten years" [004-169]. When the revelation comes, the wreath becomes an object of gentle self-mockery. He brought flowers to mourn immortals.

The daisies are significant in another sense: they are flowers of the field, common, unprepossessing — the kind of thing Time might overlook. And indeed the narrator made them "in the manner that I had seen them made in one of the years that is dead" [004-169], making the wreath itself a small act of resurrection, a pattern recovered from a year now proven immortal.

### Colossi and the Sublime

The description of the immortal years as colossi draws on the language of the sublime: they "tower up and blot out the stars," they fill the night with blackness, they cannot be measured or overthrown [004-169]. This is not the gentle nostalgia the narrator set out to indulge. The dead years are not objects of pity; they are objects of awe. The shift in register — from the delicate, floral language of the journey to the overwhelming, architectural language of the revelation — enacts the tale's argument in its very prose.

### Time as Vassal

The climax of the revelation is that Time itself — along with "all his centuries behind him" — kneels before the dead years "with bended crests in token of vassalage" [004-169]. This is a striking image: Time, conventionally the destroyer, is here the servant. The years that Time has supposedly carried away are, in Dunsany's vision, Time's masters.

## Characters

- **The narrator** — a first-person dreamer who journeys by "paths of mystery" to mourn the dead years and instead discovers their immortality. His arc is from elegiac sentimentality to quiet comfort, having had his assumptions overturned.
- **The years (the Immortals)** — colossi enthroned in a moonlit valley, served by kings, nations, and Time itself. They are silent, indifferent to incense and sacrifice, and answer no prayers directly. A voice speaks for them, but they themselves do not stir.
- **The answering voice** — an unnamed informant who corrects the narrator's misunderstanding with the tale's key revelation. Possibly a dream-figure, a guardian, or a personification of the valley's truth.

## Themes

- **[[immortality]]** — the tale's core premise is that what we think of as dead (past years) is in fact the only immortal thing.
- **[[time]]** — time is reconceived not as destroyer but as vassal, kneeling to the years it supposedly devours.
- **[[dream]] and journey** — the frame of a dream-quest allows Dunsany to present philosophical argument as visionary experience.
- **Mourning and comfort** — the narrator begins in sorrow and ends comforted, though the comfort comes not from sentimental consolation but from a metaphysical revelation that makes mourning irrelevant.
