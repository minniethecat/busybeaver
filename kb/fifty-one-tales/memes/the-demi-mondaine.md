## Overview

The Demi-mondaine is one of two title characters in Dunsany's compressed satirical dialogue "The Demagogue and the Demi-monde." She represents a life lived outside respectable society — a woman who traded companionship for money — yet her self-awareness at the gate of Paradise proves more honest, and ultimately more forgivable, than the Demagogue's hollow political rhetoric.

## Role in the Tales

She appears in a single brief scene: arriving at the gate of Paradise alongside a demagogue, both newly dead and called to account. When the Saint asks why she lived as she did, her answer is disarmingly simple:

> A demagogue and a demi-mondaine chanced to arrive together at...
> [003-087]

> "I wanted money," said the demi-mondaine.
> [003-095]

Unlike the Demagogue, who offers a convoluted defense about representing public sentiment, the Demi-mondaine gives a direct, unadorned confession. Dunsany's irony is sharp: the woman whom society despises tells the truth about herself, while the man whom society elevates offers only self-serving evasion. The Saint's subsequent judgment — allowing her into Paradise despite her acknowledged unworthiness — suggests Dunsany's sympathy lies with honest sinners over righteous frauds.
