## Overview

"A Mistaken Identity" is a brief allegorical tale from Section 4 of *Fifty-One Tales* (1915) by Lord Dunsany. Fame, walking through a city at evening, encounters Notoriety beneath a gas-lamp. When Notoriety claims to be Fame, the true Fame withdraws in silence — leaving Notoriety to lead her followers to "her native Pit."

## Summary

The tale unfolds in a single, swift encounter:

> "Fame as she walked at evening in a city saw the painted face of Notoriety flaunting beneath a gas-lamp, and many kneeled unto her in the dirt of the road."
>
> [004-056]

Fame asks the figure who she is. "I am Fame," says Notoriety. Rather than contest the claim, Fame acts with quiet dignity:

> "Then Fame stole softly away so that no one knew she had gone."
>
> [004-061]

Notoriety goes forth with her worshippers following, "and she led them, as was most meet, to her native Pit."

## Analysis

In only eighty words, Dunsany sketches a complete moral fable about the difference between Fame and Notoriety — a distinction that, the tale suggests, most people fail to make. The worshippers kneel "in the dirt of the road" before Notoriety, their devotion itself a debasement. The "painted face" and "flaunting beneath a gas-lamp" mark Notoriety as artificial, urban, and cheap — the gas-lamp's harsh light contrasting with the natural evening through which Fame walks.

Fame's response — stealing "softly away so that no one knew she had gone" — is the tale's moral center. True fame does not argue with its counterfeits; it simply withdraws. The silence of Fame's departure is itself the proof of her identity: Notoriety demands attention, Fame does not.

The final phrase — "as was most meet" — carries a quiet, archaic satisfaction. It is fitting, right, and proper that Notoriety should lead her followers to the Pit. Dunsany does not spell out what the Pit is; the word is enough.
