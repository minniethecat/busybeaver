## Overview

Babylon, the ancient Mesopotamian city, appears in several of the Fifty-One Tales as the archetype of the fallen civilization. It is invoked whenever Dunsany wants to gesture at the scale of time's destruction — Babylon's winged bulls, its kings and queens, and its very wreckage serve as markers of impermanence.

## Appearances

### Section 2 (Preface)

In the preface to Section 2, Dunsany contemplates Oblivion's sea, where the wreckage of ancient civilizations drifts:

> See now the wreckage of Babylon floating idly, and something there that once was Nineveh; already their kings and queens are in the deeps among the weedy masses of old centuries. [002-014]–[002-016]

Babylon and [[nineveh]] are paired as twin emblems of vanished empire — their monarchs now submerged among "weedy masses of old centuries."

### *The Latest Thing*

In the closing passage of "[[the-latest-thing|The Latest Thing]]," the narrator imagines a future in which time and death have been defeated:

> Then shall our children laugh at him who slew Babylon's winged bulls, and smote great numbers of the gods and fairies. [001-153]

The "winged bulls" are the *lamassu* — the colossal guardian statues of Assyrian and Babylonian palaces. Their slayer is [[time|Time]] himself, who in the imagined future will be mocked by children.

### *Nature and Time*

In "[[nature-and-time|Nature and Time]]," [[time|Time]] defends himself against [[nature|Nature]]'s accusation of neglect by cataloguing his destructions:

> "Did I not throw down Babylon for you? And is not Nineveh gone?" [008-006]

Babylon here is a credential — proof of [[time|Time]]'s diligence in the work of ruin.

## Thematic Significance

Babylon in the Fifty-One Tales is never a real place that one might visit. It exists entirely as *ruin* — as evidence of time's work. The "winged bulls" are the most vivid detail Dunsany provides: they give Babylon a specific, sculptural reality even as the city is invoked only to demonstrate its obliteration. This is Dunsany's characteristic treatment of the ancient world: it is real, it was magnificent, and it is gone.
