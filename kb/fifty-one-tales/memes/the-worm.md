## Overview

The Worm is the protagonist of "The Worm and the Angel," a brief allegorical tale from Section 1 of *Fifty-One Tales* (1915) by Lord Dunsany. As the lowest of earthly creatures — silent, sightless, dwelling in darkness — the Worm represents existence at its most elemental. In Dunsany's democratic mysticism, even this humblest being commands the attention of the cosmos: an angel, a being of pure celestial light, deigns to encounter it.

## Role in the Tales

The Worm appears in a single tale, which bears its name. The narrative is starkly simple: a worm and an angel meet. The gulf between them is cosmological — the worm belongs to the earth, the angel to the sky. Yet the encounter, in Dunsany's characteristic manner, does not simply assert the worm's insignificance:

The Worm does not speak, does not aspire, does not even recognize the magnitude of the being before it. It simply is — and its existence is acknowledged even by heaven. The angel does not crush the worm; the worm does not seek to become the angel. Their meeting is not a contest but a juxtaposition, an image of the vast distances that structure Dunsany's imagined cosmos.

## Character Analysis

Dunsany's choice of the worm as protagonist is characteristic of his commitment to dignifying the overlooked. Throughout *Fifty-One Tales*, he consistently grants significance to the small, the mundane, the ordinarily invisible: a tradesman bargaining with Time, a lonely idol forgotten in a jungle, a little city that dreams of being great. The Worm extends this pattern to its logical extreme — a creature so low that it cannot even articulate its own existence.

The tale's brevity mirrors its subject. Like the Worm, the tale itself is small. And like the Worm, it contains within its smallness a complete vision of the cosmic order: heaven and earth, light and darkness, the eternal and the mortal, all present in a miniature.

The Worm belongs to a long literary tradition of humble creatures serving as vehicles for cosmic meditation, from Shakespeare's "poor beetle" in *Measure for Measure* to Blake's "worm that flies in the night." Dunsany's innovation is to pair the worm not with a human observer but with an angel — removing the comfortable middle ground of human perspective and suspending the reader between the worm and the angel, uncertain of their own place in the cosmic hierarchy.
