## Overview

"The Return of Song" is a tale in Lord Dunsany's *Fifty-One Tales* [001-035] that narrates an event of aesthetic renewal — the moment when Song, having departed from the world, returns to it. Like many of Dunsany's tales, it treats an abstract concept ([[song|Song]]) as a living entity whose presence or absence shapes the quality of human existence. The event is not a battle or a political change but a restoration of beauty to a world grown silent.

## Narrative

The tale imagines a world from which Song has departed, leaving behind silence and emptiness. The title itself — "The Return of Song" — frames the narrative as an event of restoration. What was lost has come back; what was absent is now present. This minimalist structure is characteristic of Dunsany's prose-poems, where the event is less a plot than a metaphysical shift.

> "The swans are singing again," said to one another the gods. And looking downwards, for my dreams had taken me to some fair and far Valhalla, I saw below me an iridescent bubble not greatly larger than a star shine beautifully but faintly, and up and up from it looking larger and larger came a flock of white, innumerable swans, singing and singing and singing, till it seemed as though even the gods were wild ships swimming in music.
>
> [001-035]

The narrator, observing from a dream-Vision of Valhalla, watches the swans ascend from a dying world. He asks a humble god what is happening. The answer is both cosmic and matter-of-fact: "Only a world has ended, and the swans are coming back to the gods returning the gift of song" [001-038].

The narrator protests — "A whole world dead!" — and the god replies with one of the collection's most resonant lines:

> "Dead. The worlds are not for ever; only song is immortal."
>
> [001-039]

The tale ends with the god pointing forward: "Look! Look! There will be a new one soon" [001-040]. Death and renewal are simultaneous. A world dies; the swans carry song back to the gods; a new world will be born.

## Significance

The Return of Song participates in a broader pattern in *Fifty-One Tales*: the cycle of departure and return. This pattern appears in multiple tales:
- [[the-return-of-the-exiles|The Return of the Exiles]] [001-050] — exiles coming home
- [[the-return-of-song|The Return of Song]] [001-035] — song returning to the world
- The springtime motifs in several tales where life returns after winter

Together, these tales suggest a worldview in which loss is never final. Things depart — song, people, beauty — but they may also return. This cyclical vision contrasts with the linear, entropic vision of [[time|Time]] consuming cities and gods. Where Time destroys irreversibly, Song returns.

The tale also speaks to Dunsany's own relationship with artistic inspiration. As a prolific writer, he understood creative drought and resurgence intimately. The Return of Song may be read as an allegory of the artist's experience: the periods of silence followed by the inexplicable, welcome return of the creative impulse.

The pairing of [[the-songless-country|The Songless Country]] [001-023] and "The Return of Song" [001-035] in Section 1 is deliberate. The Songless Country establishes the world from which Song has departed — a place defined by what it lacks. The Return of Song answers that absence with restoration. The reader experiences the absence before the restoration. The narrative order itself enacts the cycle: first the loss, then the return.

## The Metaphysics of Absence

Dunsany's tales of return are built on a prior condition of absence. "The Songless Country" [001-023] establishes the world from which Song has departed — a place defined by what it lacks. The Return of Song [001-035] is meaningful precisely because the absence was felt. This structure — departure, absence, return — is one of the deepest patterns in mythological narrative, from the Eleusinian mysteries (Persephone's descent and return) to the Christian resurrection.

Dunsany strips the pattern of its religious trappings and presents it as pure aesthetic event. Song does not return because of divine intervention or heroic quest; it returns because that is what Song does. The universe, in Dunsany's vision, is characterized by cycles that operate without explanation — as natural and inexorable as the seasons.

The god's declaration that "only song is immortal" [001-039] is the collection's most explicit statement on what outlasts worlds. Not empires — they drown in Oblivion's sea, as [[the-raft-builders|The Raft-Builders]] [001-226] reminds us. Not years — they sit as colossi in a distant valley, as [[alone-the-immortals|Alone the Immortals]] [004-169] reveals. Not even worlds — they end. Only song. Only the swans returning to the gods.

## Art and the Natural World

The Return of Song can be read alongside [[nature|Nature]] as a meditation on the relationship between art and the natural world. Song is a human creation — it is art, culture, expression. Its departure and return mirror the cycles of the natural world (seasons, migrations, growth and decay) while remaining distinctly human.

The image of swans ascending from a dying world — an "iridescent bubble not greatly larger than a star" [001-035] — is one of Dunsany's most beautiful cosmic visions. The scale is vast (worlds dying and being born) but the perspective is intimate (a single observer watching from Valhalla, a humble god explaining what he sees). This combination of cosmic scale and intimate perspective is characteristic of the collection's finest moments.

The swans themselves are a resonant symbol. In classical mythology, swans sing most beautifully just before death — the "swan song." Dunsany inverts the trope: the swans sing not before their own death but at the death of a world, and their song is not a farewell but a return — the gift of song carried back to its divine source, to be bestowed again on a new world.
