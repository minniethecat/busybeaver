[002-001]When we break up under the heavy years and go down into eternity

[002-002]with all that is ours our thoughts like small lost rafts float on awhile

[002-003]upon Oblivion's sea. They will not carry much over those tides, our

[002-004]names and a phrase or two and little else.

[002-005]They that write as a trade to please the whim of the day, they are like

[002-006]sailors that work at the rafts only to warm their hands and to distract

[002-007]their thoughts from their certain doom; their rafts go all to pieces

[002-008]before the ship breaks up.

[002-009]See now Oblivion shimmering all around us, its very tranquility deadlier

[002-010]than tempest. How little all our keels have troubled it. [[time|Time]] in its deeps

[002-011]swims like a monstrous whale; and, like a whale, feeds on the littlest

[002-012]things--small tunes and little unskilled songs of the olden, golden

[002-013]evenings--and anon turneth whale-like to overthrow whole ships.

[002-014]See now the wreckage of Babylon floating idly, and something there

[002-015]that once was Nineveh; already their kings and queens are in the

[002-016]deeps among the weedy masses of old centuries that hide the sodden

[002-017]bulk of sunken Tyre and make a darkness round Persepolis.

[002-018]For the rest I dimly see the forms of foundered ships on the sea-floor

[002-019]strewn with crowns.

[002-020]Our ships were all unseaworthy from the first.

[002-021]There goes the raft that Homer made for Helen.

[002-022]THE WORKMAN

[002-023]I saw a workman fall with his scaffolding right from the summit of

[002-024]some vast hotel. And as he came down I saw him holding a knife

[002-025]and trying to cut his name on the scaffolding. He had time to try and

[002-026]do this for he must have had nearly three hundred feet to fall. And I

[002-027]could think of nothing but his folly in doing this futile thing, for not

[002-028]only would the man be unrecognizably dead in three seconds, but the

[002-029]very pole on which he tried to scratch whatever of his name he had

[002-030]time for was certain to be burnt in a few weeks for firewood.

[002-031]Then I went home for I had work to do. And all that evening I thought

[002-032]of the man's folly, till the thought hindered me from serious work.

[002-033]And late that night while I was still at work, the ghost of the workman

[002-034]floated through my wall and stood before me laughing.

[002-035]I heard no sound until after I spoke to it; but I could see the grey

[002-036]diaphanous form standing before me shuddering with laughter.

[002-037]I spoke at last and asked what it was laughing at, and then the ghost

[002-038]spoke. It said: "I'm a laughin' at you sittin' and workin' there."

[002-039]"And why," I asked, "do you laugh at serious work?"

[002-040]"Why, yer bloomin' life 'ull go by like a wind," he said, "and yer 'ole

[002-041]silly civilization 'ull be tidied up in a few centuries."

[002-042]Then he fell to laughing again and this time audibly; and, laughing

[002-043]still, faded back through the wall again and into the eternity from

[002-044]which he had come.

[002-045]THE GUEST

[002-046]A young man came into an ornate restaurant at eight o'clock in

[002-047]London.

[002-048]He was alone, but two places had been laid at the table which was

[002-049]reserved for him. He had chosen the dinner very carefully, by letter

[002-050]a week before.

[002-051]A waiter asked him about the other guest.

[002-052]"You probably won't see him till the coffee comes," the young man

[002-053]told him; so he was served alone.

[002-054]Those at adjacent tables might have noticed the young man continually

[002-055]addressing the empty chair and carrying on a monologue with it

[002-056]throughout his elaborate dinner.

[002-057]"I think you knew my father," he said to it over the soup.

[002-058]"I sent for you this evening," he continued, "because I want you to

[002-059]do me a good turn; in fact I must insist on it."

[002-060]There was nothing eccentric about the man except for this habit of

[002-061]addressing an empty chair, certainly he was eating as good a dinner

[002-062]as any sane man could wish for.

[002-063]After the Burgundy had been served he became more voluble in his

[002-064]monologue, not that he spoiled his wine by drinking excessively.

[002-065]"We have several acquaintances in common," he said. "I met King Seti

[002-066]a year ago in Thebes. I think he has altered very little since you knew

[002-067]him. I thought his forehead a little low for a king's. Cheops has left the

[002-068]house that he built for your reception, he must have prepared for you

[002-069]for years and years. I suppose you have seldom been entertained like

[002-070]that. I ordered this dinner over a week ago. I thought then that a lady

[002-071]might have come with me, but as she wouldn't I've asked you. She may

[002-072]not after all be as lovely as Helen of Troy. Was Helen very lovely? Not

[002-073]when you knew her, perhaps. You were lucky in Cleopatra, you must

[002-074]have known her when she was in her prime.

[002-075]"You never knew the mermaids nor the fairies nor the lovely goddesses

[002-076]of long ago, that's where we have the best of you."

[002-077]He was silent when the waiters came to his table, but rambled merrily

[002-078]on as soon as they left, still turned to the empty chair.

[002-079]"You know I saw you here in London only the other day. You were

[002-080]on a motor bus going down Ludgate Hill. It was going much too fast.

[002-081]London is a good place. But I shall be glad enough to leave it. It was

[002-082]in London that I met the lady I that was speaking about. If it hadn't

[002-083]been for London I probably shouldn't have met her, and if it hadn't

[002-084]been for London she probably wouldn't have had so much besides

[002-085]me to amuse her. It cuts both ways."

[002-086]He paused once to order coffee, gazing earnestly at the waiter and

[002-087]putting a sovereign in his hand. "Don't let it be chicory," said he.

[002-088]The waiter brought the coffee, and the young man dropped a tabloid

[002-089]of some sort into his cup.

[002-090]"I don't suppose you come here very often," he went on. "Well, you

[002-091]probably want to be going. I haven't taken you much out of your way,

[002-092]there is plenty for you to do in London."

[002-093]Then having drunk his coffee he fell on to the floor by a foot of the

[002-094]empty chair, and a doctor who was dining in the room bent over

[002-095]him and announced to the anxious manager the visible presence of

[002-096]the young man's guest.

[002-097]DEATH AND ODYSSEUS

[002-098]In the Olympian courts Love laughed at [[death|Death]], because he was

[002-099]unsightly, and because She couldn't help it, and because he never

[002-100]did anything worth doing, and because She would.

[002-101]And Death hated being laughed at, and used to brood apart thinking

[002-102]only of his wrongs and of what he could do to end this intolerable

[002-103]treatment.

[002-104]But one day Death appeared in the courts with an air and They all

[002-105]noticed it. "What are you up to now?" said Love. And Death with

[002-106]some solemnity said to Her: "I am going to frighten Odysseus"; and

[002-107]drawing about him his grey traveller's cloak went out through the

[002-108]windy door with his jowl turned earthwards.

[002-109]And he came soon to Ithaca and the hall that Athene knew, and

[002-110]opened the door and saw there famous Odysseus, with his white

[002-111]locks bending close over the fire, trying to warm his hands.

[002-112]And the wind through the open door blew bitterly on Odysseus.

[002-113]And Death came up behind him, and suddenly shouted.

[002-114]And Odysseus went on warming his pale hands.

[002-115]Then Death came close and began to mouth at him. And after a

[002-116]while Odysseus turned and spoke. And "Well, old servant," he said,

[002-117]"have your masters been kind to you since I made you work for me

[002-118]round Ilion?"

[002-119]And Death for some while stood mute, for he thought of the laughter

[002-120]of Love.

[002-121]Then "Come now," said Odysseus, "lend me your shoulder," and he

[002-122]leaning heavily on that bony joint, they went together through the open

[002-123]door.

[002-124]DEATH AND THE ORANGE

[002-125]Two dark young men in a foreign southern land sat at a restaurant

[002-126]table with one woman.

[002-127]And on the woman's plate was a small orange which had an evil

[002-128]laughter in its heart.

[002-129]And both of the men would be looking at the woman all the time, and

[002-130]they ate little and they drank much.

[002-131]And the woman was smiling equally at each.

[002-132]Then the small orange that had the laughter in its heart rolled

[002-133]slowly off the plate on to the floor. And the dark young men both

[002-134]sought for it at once, and they met suddenly beneath the table, and

[002-135]soon they were speaking swift words to one another, and a horror

[002-136]and an impotence came over the Reason of each as she sat helpless

[002-137]at the back of the mind, and the heart of the orange laughed and the

[002-138]woman went on smiling; and Death, who was sitting at another table,

[002-139]tete-a-tete with an old man, rose and came over to listen to the quarrel.

[002-140]THE PRAYER OF THE FLOWERS

[002-141]It was the voice of the flowers on the West wind, the lovable, the

[002-142]old, the lazy West wind, blowing ceaselessly, blowing sleepily, going

[002-143]Greecewards.

[002-144]"The woods have gone away, they have fallen and left us; men love

[002-145]us no longer, we are lonely by moonlight. Great engines rush over

[002-146]the beautiful fields, their ways lie hard and terrible up and down the

[002-147]land.

[002-148]"The cancrous cities spread over the grass, they clatter in their lairs

[002-149]continually, they glitter about us blemishing the night.

[002-150]"The woods are gone, O [[pan|Pan]], the woods, the woods. And thou art

[002-151]far, O Pan, and far away."

[002-152]I was standing by night between two railway embankments on the

[002-153]edge of a Midland city. On one of them I saw the trains go by, once

[002-154]in every two minutes, and on the other, the trains went by twice in

[002-155]every five.

[002-156]Quite close were the glaring factories, and the sky above them wore

[002-157]the fearful look that it wears in dreams of fever.

[002-158]The flowers were right in the stride of that advancing city, and

[002-159]thence I heard them sending up their cry. And then I heard, beating

[002-160]musically up wind, the voice of Pan reproving them from [[arcady|Arcady]]--

[002-161]"Be patient a little, these things are not for long."

[002-162]TIME AND THE TRADESMAN

[002-163]Once Time as he prowled the world, his hair grey not with weakness

[002-164]but with dust of the ruin of cities, came to a furniture shop and entered

[002-165]the Antique department. And there he saw a man darkening the wood

[002-166]of a chair with dye and beating it with chains and making imitation

[002-167]wormholes in it.

[002-168]And when Time saw another doing his work he stood by him awhile

[002-169]and looked on critically.

[002-170]And at last he said: "That is not how I work," and he turned the man's

[002-171]hair white and bent his back and put some furrows in his little cunning

[002-172]face; then turned and strode away, for a mighty city that was weary

[002-173]and sick and too long had troubled the fields was sore in need of him.

[002-174]THE LITTLE CITY

[002-175]I was in the pre-destined 11.8 from Goraghwood to Drogheda, when

[002-176]I suddenly saw the city. It was a little city in a valley, and only seemed

[002-177]to have a little smoke, and the sun caught the smoke and turned it

[002-178]golden, so that it looked like an old Italian picture where angels walk

[002-179]in the foreground and the rest is a blaze of gold. And beyond, as one

[002-180]could tell by the lie of land although one could not see through the

[002-181]golden smoke, I knew that there lay the paths of the roving ships.

[002-182]All round there lay a patchwork of small fields all over the slopes of

[002-183]the hills, and the snow had come upon them tentatively, but already

[002-184]the birds of the waste had moved to the sheltered places for every

[002-185]omen boded more to fall. Far away some little hills blazed like an

[002-186]aureate bulwark broken off by age and fallen from the earthward

[002-187]rampart of Paradise. And aloof and dark the mountains stared

[002-188]unconcernedly seawards.
