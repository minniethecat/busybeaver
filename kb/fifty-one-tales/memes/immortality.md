## Overview

Immortality in Lord Dunsany's *Fifty-One Tales* (1915) is treated with characteristic ambivalence. The collection contains one of Dunsany's most striking metaphysical reversals — the discovery that past years do not die but become immortal colossi before whom Time itself kneels — alongside stories that treat the human desire for immortality as both noble and faintly absurd. The concept oscillates between the cosmic (years that outlast Time's dominion) and the personal (a dying workman scratching his name into a wall).

## In the Tales

**Years as Immortals.** The title of the tale "Alone the Immortals" itself suggests collective immortality: the immortals are not individuals but the past years themselves. The narrator travels to a valley where dead years are said to lie, intending to mourn them — only to discover that they are not fragile ghosts but towering, indestructible entities [004-169].

> But looking upwards in the blaze of the moon I suddenly saw colossi sitting near, and towering up and blotting out the stars and filling the night with blackness; and at those idols' feet I saw praying and making obeisance kings and the days that are and all times and all cities and all nations and all their gods.
>
> [004-169]

Time, that great destroyer of all other things, kneels before them in vassalage. The reversal is complete: what we thought perished is what endures; what we thought was Time's victim is Time's master [004-175]. Alone the immortals remain — alone because nothing else shares their condition, not even the force that we imagine destroys everything.

> He answered me: "These _are_ the years that are dead, alone the immortals; all years to be are Their children — They fashioned their smiles and their laughter; all earthly kings They have crowned, all gods They have created; all the events to be flow down from their..."
>
> [004-175]

**The Human Bid for Immortality.** Two tales examine the human yearning to leave something behind. In "The Workman," a dying builder scratches his name into a wall with his last strength — a gesture the narrator initially dismisses as vanity, until a ghost points out that the narrator's own "serious work" is equally ephemeral from the perspective of eternity. In "The Raft-Builders," Dunsany assembles a catalogue of drowned civilizations — Babylon, Nineveh, Tyre, Persepolis — each a culture that thought itself immortal and is now indistinguishable from the sea-floor. But the tale's final image is of Homer's raft, still floating after three thousand years: the poem that outlasts the civilization that produced it [001-226]. Immortality, in this vision, is not a guarantee but a possibility — and the possibility is enough.

**The Tradesman's Furniture.** In "Time and the Tradesman," the desire for permanence through craft — furniture "built to last for generations" — is shown to be futile. Time can reduce the best-made chair to warped wood with a glance. The tale is a companion piece to "The Workman": the tradesman, like the workman scratching his name, seeks to extend himself through the things he makes.

**Song's Immortality.** In [[the-return-of-song|The Return of Song]] [001-035], a god declares: "The worlds are not for ever; only song is immortal" [001-039]. This is perhaps the collection's most direct statement on what achieves permanence: not worlds, not empires, not furniture or scratched names, but art — the swans returning to the gods with the gift of song.

## Interpretation

Dunsany's treatment of immortality refuses both easy consolation and easy cynicism. The years are immortal — that is a fact of the cosmic order, not a promise to human beings. The workman's scratched name is both touching and absurd — but the ghost's laughter at the narrator is the corrective: from the right vantage point, all bids for permanence are the same bid, and all deserve the same complex sympathy.

The collection's deepest statement on immortality may be the one from "The Raft-Builders": most rafts sink, but one floats. The writer, the workman, the tradesman, the king — all are raft-builders on sinking ships. The ship will sink. But the raft might not. And the gap between "might not" and "will not" is where the arts of civilization live.

The contrast between the immortal years and mortal human beings also illuminates the peculiar status of [[time|Time]] in the collection. Time is the great destroyer of human things — but he kneels before the years. He is not ultimate. The ultimate things, in Dunsany's cosmos, are not the forces that destroy but the things that endure — the years, the song, the raft that Homer made for Helen.
