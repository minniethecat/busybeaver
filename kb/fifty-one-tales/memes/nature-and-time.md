## Overview

"Nature and Time" stages a stark allegorical procession through the industrial streets of Coventry. Time strides ahead, indifferent and triumphant; Nature stumbles behind, ragged and reproachful, clutching at his sleeve. The tale is one of Dunsany's most direct laments for the industrial transformation of England, and its setting — Coventry, a city synonymous with manufacturing — is no accident. Where other tales in *Fifty-One Tales* (1915) operate through indirection and fable, "Nature and Time" names its antagonists plainly and gives them voices.

## Summary

Through the streets of Coventry on a winter's night strides a "triumphant spirit" — Time. Behind him, stooping, unkempt, utterly ragged, an "ill-used spirit" — Nature — tries to keep pace, continually plucking at his sleeve and crying out [007-029].

It is a bitter night, but Nature does not seem to fear the cold. What she winces from are "the trams and the ugly shops and the glare of the factories"; the pavement hurts her feet [007-029]. Time, by contrast, seems to care for nothing — hot or cold, silent or noisy, pavement or open fields — he merely strides on.

Nature catches up and clutches his elbow. Her voice, barely audible over the traffic, delivers the accusation that is the tale's core:

> "You have forgotten me," she complained to him. "You have forsaken me here."
>
> [007-029]

She gestures at Coventry and "seemed to indicate other cities beyond." Time gruffly tells her to keep pace and denies forsaking her. But Nature continues her lament:

> "My anemones are dead for miles," she said, "all my woods are fallen and still the cities grow. My child Man is unhappy and my other children are dying, and still the cities grow and you have forgotten me!"
>
> [007-029]

## Analysis

### Allegorical Personification

The tale belongs to Dunsany's mode of direct allegorical personification, alongside tales like "[[death-and-odysseus]]" and "[[time-and-the-tradesman]]." But where those tales often deploy wit and reversal, "Nature and Time" is unrelievedly elegiac. Nature is not clever or resourceful; she is a figure of pathos — ragged, whining, reproachful. Time is not malign; he is simply indifferent, striding forward without malice but also without care.

### Coventry as Metonym

The choice of Coventry is precise and significant. By 1915, Coventry was a major centre of bicycle, automobile, and munitions manufacturing — the very engines that Dunsany's rooks in "[[furrow-maker]]" identify as Man's sickness. "Nature and Time" makes Coventry a metonym for industrial civilization as a whole: "she seemed to indicate other cities beyond" [007-029]. The trams, the "ugly shops," the glare of factories, the pavement that hurts her feet — each detail is a small indictment.

### The Accusation

Nature's charge against Time is carefully structured. She begins with the personal — "You have forgotten me" — then expands to the particular: dead anemones, fallen woods. Then she widens the frame to include Man and "my other children," all of whom are dying. The accusation is not that Time destroys (that is Time's nature) but that Time has *forgotten* Nature — has ceased to notice her, has left her behind in the rush forward. The crime is neglect, not violence.

### Time's Reply

Time's response — "he gruffly told her to keep pace with him and that he did not forsake her" — is the tale's coldest moment. It is not a denial of Nature's suffering but an insistence that she should simply endure it. Time does not dispute the facts; he merely refuses to slow down. The relationship between Time and Nature, as Dunsany presents it, is not a partnership but a forced march in which Nature is perpetually lagging, perpetually reproaching, and perpetually ignored.

## Characters

- **Time** — a triumphant spirit striding through Coventry, indifferent to his surroundings. He does not deny Nature's accusations but offers only the command to "keep pace."
- **Nature** — a ragged, weeping spirit, ill-used and outcast. Her children are dying, her woods have fallen, and the cities pain her. She is a figure of lament, not resistance.

## Themes

- **Industry and [[nature]]** — the tale is an elegy for the natural world displaced by factories, trams, and pavement.
- **[[time]] and neglect** — Time does not destroy Nature directly; he simply leaves her behind.
- **The city** — Coventry stands for all industrial cities, places hostile to the spirits of the old world.
