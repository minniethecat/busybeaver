## Overview

"The Giant Poppy" is a tale from Section 3 of *Fifty-One Tales* (1915) by Lord Dunsany. In a dream-vision, the narrator returns to the hills of his youth — hills from which you could once see the walls of Troy and the plains of Roncesvalles — only to find the woods and fairies gone. In their place stands a single giant poppy, humming "Remember not." A poet-piper sits beside it, playing an ancient tune to hold the poppy back.

## Summary

The narrator dreams he returns to hills he knew — hills that once offered distant glimpses of Ilion (Troy) and Roncesvalles, the mythic battlefields of the ancient and medieval worlds. Woods had crowned those hills, with moonlit clearings where fairies danced:

> "But there were no woods when I went back, no fairies nor distant glimpse of Ilion or plains of Roncesvalles, only one giant poppy waved in the wind, and as it waved it hummed 'Remember not.'"
>
> [003-110]

Beside the poppy's oak-like stem sits a poet, dressed as a shepherd, playing an ancient tune on a pipe. The narrator asks if the fairies have passed that way. The poet answers with a diagnosis of the world's illness:

> "The poppy has grown apace and is killing gods and fairies. Its fumes are suffocating the world, and its roots drain it of its beautiful strength."
>
> [003-116]

The poet explains why he sits there piping: the tune is "bad for the poppy," slowing its growth. And his brotherhood of pipers serves a larger purpose — if they ceased to play, "men would stray over the world and be lost or come to terrible ends." The tale ends on a note of desperate hope: "We think we have saved Agamemnon."

The wind among the poppy's petals continues its murmur: "Remember not. Remember not."

## Analysis

The giant poppy is Dunsany's symbol for the modern force of forgetting — the opium of collective amnesia that erases myth, fairies, ancient battles, and the beautiful impractical things. The poppy's refrain, "Remember not," is the anthem of an age that prides itself on progress and disdains what came before.

The poet-piper is the counter-force: the artist whose work is "bad for the poppy." His ancient tune slows the poppy's growth, just as poetry and myth preserve what commerce and modernity would erase. The brotherhood of pipers — the community of artists across time — exists to keep humanity from straying and being lost.

The final claim, "We think we have saved Agamemnon," is both magnificent and fragile. Agamemnon, the legendary king of Mycenae, would be utterly forgotten if not for Homer — if not for poetry. The artists have saved one legend; the poppy threatens to take all the rest. The tale's closing murmur, "Remember not. Remember not," is the sound of a world losing its myths one by one.
