## Overview

The Sphinx appears as a personified monumental figure in two tales from *Fifty-One Tales* (1915) by Lord Dunsany: "The Sphinx at Giza" (Section 1) and "The Sphinx in Thebes (Massachusetts)" (Section 1). In the first tale she is the ancient Egyptian monument whose painted face has charmed Time into lingering at her feet, sparing her alone among all painted things. In the second she reappears in a surprising American incarnation, extending Dunsany's meditation on the relationship between the eternal and the ephemeral across continents.

## Role in the Tales

In "The Sphinx at Giza," the Sphinx is portrayed as a figure of strategic artifice. She has painted her face in order to captivate the great destroyer:

> She had painted her face in order to ogle Time. And he has spared no other painted face in all the world but hers.
>
> [001-129]

The narrator observes that while Time destroys everything else — cities, civilizations, even "Delilah" who "is dust" — he lingers at the Sphinx's feet, distracted and entranced:

> Time dallies like a fool at her feet when he should be smiting cities. Time never wearies of her silly smile.
>
> [001-136]

The tale imagines a strange bargain: if the Sphinx would betray Time's secrets, humanity could blind and enslave him — only for the reverie to end in dread at the thought of a blinded Time destroying the world in his death throes.

In "The Sphinx in Thebes (Massachusetts)," Dunsany transposes the ancient monument to a modern New England setting, playing with the incongruity of the ancient symbol displaced into the mundane American landscape. The contrast highlights the Sphinx's essential timelessness — she remains herself regardless of geography.

## Character Analysis

The Sphinx in Dunsany's treatment is not a riddle-posing menace of Greek myth but a figure of enduring beauty whose power lies in stillness and artifice. Her painted face — an emblem of vanity — becomes a weapon that brute force cannot match. Time, who has crushed every other beautiful thing, is helpless before her smile.

Dunsany's dual treatment of the Sphinx — Egyptian and American — suggests that the archetype transcends any single cultural location. The Sphinx is wherever ancient mystery confronts the passing of time, whether in the desert of Giza or the streets of a Massachusetts town.
