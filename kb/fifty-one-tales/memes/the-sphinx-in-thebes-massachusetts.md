## Overview

"The Sphinx in Thebes (Massachusetts)" is one of Dunsany's sharpest satires on modern consumer culture. The title itself is a joke — Thebes, the ancient city of the Sphinx's riddle, has a comic double in a Massachusetts town. The wealthy woman at the tale's centre treats a sphinx as one more acquisition, a pet to complete her collection, and is destroyed not by claws or violence but by a question. The tale weds the oldest narrative logic — the riddle that kills — to the newest: the consumer who believes everything can be bought.

## Summary

A woman in a steel-built city possesses everything money can purchase — "gold and dividends and trains and houses" — and she has pets, but she has no sphinx [006-116]. She orders her agents to find her one. They search menageries, forests, and desert places, and find nothing. She would settle for a lion, but a woman she knows already owns one.

At last, in a desert at evening, the agents find a sphinx "watching a ruined temple whose gods she had eaten hundreds of years ago when her hunger was on her" [006-116]. They chain the sphinx — who waits with "ominous stillness" — and transport her to the steel-built city.

The woman is delighted with her acquisition. But one day the sphinx stares into her eyes and softly asks a riddle. The woman cannot answer. And she dies.

The tale closes with a final, unsettling image:

> And the sphinx is silent again and none knows what she will do.
>
> [006-116]

## Analysis

The tale's central irony is that the sphinx cannot be domesticated. The woman has mastered the modern world — dividends, trains, houses — but the sphinx belongs to an older order, one governed by riddles rather than transactions. The riddle is not a puzzle to be solved with money; it is a test of worth, and the woman fails it.

Dunsany's choice of a sphinx over any other mythical creature is thematically precise. The sphinx of Greek myth destroyed those who could not answer; it was a gatekeeper, a being whose function was to discriminate between those who could pass and those who could not. The woman treats it as a possession, but the sphinx continues to function as a sphinx. She owns it; it tests her; she dies.

The tale's other joke is geographical. Thebes, Massachusetts exists — it is not mythical. By placing a sphinx there, Dunsany maps the ancient onto the modern with comic precision. The Thebes of Oedipus becomes a New England town; the riddle that once challenged a hero is now asked of a consumer. The diminishment is intentional: what killed kings now kills collectors.

The sphinx's backstory — "whose gods she had eaten hundreds of years ago" — adds a layer of ancient menace. This is not a creature that has been waiting to serve; it is a creature that has already devoured one pantheon and is patient enough to wait for the next. The steel-built city is merely its latest temple.

The final line — "none knows what she will do" — is classic Dunsany. The story does not resolve; it opens outward. The sphinx is loose in the modern world, silent, unpredictable, and no one knows what comes next. The consumer's death is not the end of the story but the beginning of a more troubling one.

## Characters

- **The wealthy woman** — unnamed, a collector whose wealth has made her believe the world is a catalogue. She dies of a question.
- **The sphinx** — ancient, patient, and lethal. She has eaten gods and asks riddles. She is not a pet.
- **The agents** — faceless enablers of the woman's desires, capable of finding and chaining a sphinx but incapable of understanding what they have brought home.
