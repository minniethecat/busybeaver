## Overview

The Muses are divine figures from Greek mythology who appear in Lord Dunsany's tale "The Messengers" in *Fifty-One Tales*. Far from the benevolent inspirers of classical tradition, Dunsany's Muses are distant, relentless beings who repeatedly send messengers from Mount Parnassus to the Golden Town, each bearing a divine message and a token of their origin — only to see each messenger rejected and stoned by the town's wise men.

## Role in the Tales

In "The Messengers," a man wandering near Parnassus chasing hares hears the high Muses singing. They call him by name and commission him to carry a message to the Golden Town, giving him a wreath of laurels carved from emeralds as a token of his divine origin. Despite his reluctance, he delivers the message — and is stoned to death by the elders who declare, "Thou art not from the Muses."

> And the Muses called him by name. "Take us a message," they said, "to the Golden Town." And the man was downcast for he would have chased hares.
>
> — 008-049

The pattern repeats: the Muses send a second messenger with an ivory wand carved with all the beautiful stories of the world, and a third with a golden garland of kingcups. Each is stoned, and each time the Golden Town carves the message on gold and preserves it in their temples. The tale ends with the haunting refrain: "When will the Muses rest? When are they weary?"
