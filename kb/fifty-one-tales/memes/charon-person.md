## Overview

Charon is the title character of Dunsany's tale "Charon" — the ferryman of Greek mythology who transports the souls of the dead across the river Styx. Dunsany's portrayal emphasizes Charon's immense weariness and mechanical devotion to duty, rowing endlessly without curiosity about his passengers, until one soul does something unprecedented: makes him smile.

## Role in the Tales

Charon appears in the tale bearing his name, where Dunsany subverts the mythological figure by focusing not on his supernatural role but on his exhaustion. The tale opens with Charon leaning forward to row, and his weariness is its central motif:

> Charon leaned forward and rowed. All things were one with his weariness.
> [001-078]

Dunsany emphasizes that Charon does not wonder about his passengers — it is neither his duty nor his nature:

> It was neither Charon's duty nor his wont to ponder why these things might be. Charon leaned forward and rowed.
> [001-091]

This mechanical repetition — leaning forward, rowing, never questioning — defines him. The turning point comes when a passenger says something that breaks through Charon's impassivity:

> No one had ever made Charon smile before, no one before had...
> [001-108]

The identity of this passenger and what they said is left deliberately mysterious — a quintessentially Dunsanian touch that transforms the tale from myth into meditation.

## Character Analysis

Charon in Dunsany's hands becomes a figure of existential weariness. He is not the grim ferryman of classical mythology but a tired workman performing an endless task. The repetition of "Charon leaned forward and rowed" throughout the tale creates a rhythmic quality that mirrors the endless, circular nature of his labor.

The smile at the tale's end is the crucial detail: after eons of unchanging duty, a single moment of human connection breaks through. Dunsany does not explain what was said, preserving the mystery and suggesting that some experiences are too profound for narration. The contrast between Charon's "great and weary" nature and this unexpected smile captures the collection's recurring theme — that small moments of beauty or connection can pierce even the heaviest cosmic indifference.
