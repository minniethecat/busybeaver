## Overview

The Tradesman is a character in Lord Dunsany's allegorical fable "Time and the Tradesman," from Section 2 of *Fifty-One Tales* (1915). A furniture maker who takes pride in his well-crafted wares, he represents human ambition and the desire for permanence through craft. His encounter with the personified Time, who reveals how every piece will one day warp and decay, serves as a meditation on the limits of mortal endeavor.

## Role in the Tales

The Tradesman appears in a single tale, where he functions as a dramatic foil to Time. When the ancient wanderer enters his furniture shop, the Tradesman proudly displays his finest pieces — chairs, tables, and cabinets built to last for generations. His professional pride embodies the craftsman's hope that well-made things might endure beyond their maker.

Time examines each piece and, with a touch or a glance, reveals how it will warp, crack, and decay. The Tradesman protests that his work is built to endure, but Time shows him — and through him, the reader — that nothing crafted by human hands escapes the passage of ages. He is not a villain or a fool; rather, he is the universal human figure who believes in the permanence of what he builds.

## Character Analysis

Dunsany uses the Tradesman with characteristic economy of characterization. The figure is not named beyond his profession, making him an Everyman of craft and commerce. His pride is not hubris — it is the natural attachment any maker feels toward what they have made. His shock when Time demonstrates the inevitable fate of his furniture is not that of a man who has been foolish, but of one who has been made to see a truth he had until that moment been able to ignore.

The tale, at roughly 130 words, is one of the collection's shortest. The Tradesman's dialogue, if any exists, is implied rather than quoted — the compressed narrative form leaves his protest as a dramatic posture rather than a speech. This compression is characteristic of Dunsany's late prose-poem style, in which a character can be fully realized through a single situation and a single reversal.
