## Overview

The Assignation is the narrative center of the opening tale of *Fifty-One Tales* (1915). A poet receives a summons from Fame to meet in a golden city beyond the sunset. The journey and the encounter that follows form one of Dunsany's most direct meditations on the relationship between the artist and recognition, devotion and ambition, the call and the answer.

## Narrative

The tale opens with the poet receiving a command. Fame — personified, demanding, severe — has summoned him to a meeting (an "assignation") in a city of gold. The poet cannot refuse: the command comes not as a request but as a claim on his being. He is a poet because he has been called, and he must answer because he is a poet.

The journey takes him beyond the sunset, out of the ordinary world and into a landscape that is at once physical and mythological. The golden city, when he reaches it, is everything he imagined — and more. But Fame is not what he expected. The meeting is not the triumph of recognition but a trial: Fame is exacting, demanding more than he has yet given, and the assignation becomes an examination of whether his devotion matches his ambition.

The tale does not resolve with a clear victory or defeat. The poet returns — or perhaps he does not, perhaps he is still there, still in the golden city, still being tested. The ambiguity is characteristic of Dunsany: the artist's relationship with Fame is never finally settled.

## Significance

The Assignation serves as a prologue in miniature to the entire collection. The poet who is called, who journeys beyond the ordinary, who meets the personified force of his own ambition — this is the archetypal Dunsanyan figure. The tale establishes the collection's central concern: what does it mean to make art, and what debt does the artist owe to the forces that call art into being?

The word "assignation" itself carries a double meaning — a meeting (often romantic, often secret) and an assignment (a task given). Fame both summons the poet and gives him his task. The two meanings cannot be separated: to be called is to be commanded.
