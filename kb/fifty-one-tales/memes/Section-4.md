[004-027]THE STORM

[004-028]They saw a little ship that was far at sea and that went by the name

[004-029]of the _Petite Esperance_. And because of its uncouth rig and its

[004-030]lonely air and the look that it had of coming from strangers' lands they

[004-031]said: "It is neither a ship to greet nor desire, nor yet to succor when in

[004-032]the hands of the sea."

[004-033]And the sea rose up as is the wont of the sea and the little ship from

[004-034]afar was in his hands, and frailer than ever seemed its feeble masts

[004-035]with their sails of fantastic cut and their alien flags. And the sea made

[004-036]a great and very triumphing voice, as the sea doth. And then there

[004-037]arose a wave that was very strong, even the ninth-born son of the

[004-038]hurricane and the tide, and hid the little ship and hid the whole of the

[004-039]far parts of the sea. Thereat said those who stood on the good dry

[004-040]land:

[004-041]"'Twas but a little, worthless alien ship and it is sunk at sea, and it is

[004-042]good and right that the storm have spoil." And they turned and watched

[004-043]the course of the merchant-men, laden with silver and appeasing spice;

[004-044]year after year they cheered them into port and praised their goods and

[004-045]their familiar sails. And many years went by.

[004-046]And at last with decks and bulwarks covered with cloth of gold; with

[004-047]age-old parrots that had known the troubadours, singing illustrious

[004-048]songs and preening their feathers of gold; with a hold full of emeralds

[004-049]and rubies; all silken with Indian loot; furling as it came in its way-worn

[004-050]alien sails, a galleon glided into port, shutting the sunlight from the

[004-051]merchantmen: and lo! it loomed the equal of the cliffs.

[004-052]"Who are you?" they asked, "far-travelled wonderful ship?"

[004-053]And they said: "The _Petite Esperance_."

[004-054]"O," said the people on shore. "We thought you were sunk at sea."

[004-055]"Sunk at sea?" sang the sailors. "We could not be sunk at sea--we

[004-056]had the gods on board."

[004-057]A MISTAKEN IDENTITY

[004-058]Fame as she walked at evening in a city saw the painted face of

[004-059]Notoriety flaunting beneath a gas-lamp, and many kneeled unto her

[004-060]in the dirt of the road.

[004-061]"Who are you?" Fame said to her.

[004-062]"I am Fame," said Notoriety.

[004-063]Then Fame stole softly away so that no one knew she had gone.

[004-064]And Notoriety presently went forth and all her worshippers rose and

[004-065]followed after, and she led them, as was most meet, to her native Pit.

[004-066]THE TRUE HISTORY OF THE HARE AND THE TORTOISE

[004-067]For a long time there was doubt with acrimony among the beasts as

[004-068]to whether the Hare or the Tortoise could run the swifter. Some said

[004-069]the Hare was the swifter of the two because he had such long ears,

[004-070]and others said the Tortoise was the swifter because anyone whose

[004-071]shell was so hard as that should be able to run hard too. And lo, the

[004-072]forces of estrangement and disorder perpetually postponed a decisive

[004-073]contest.

[004-074]But when there was nearly war among the beasts, at last an

[004-075]arrangement was come to and it was decided that the Hare and the

[004-076]Tortoise should run a race of five hundred yards so that all should

[004-077]see who was right.

[004-078]"Ridiculous nonsense!" said the Hare, and it was all his backers could

[004-079]do to get him to run.

[004-080]"The contest is most welcome to me," said the Tortoise, "I shall not

[004-081]shirk it."

[004-082]O, how his backers cheered.

[004-083]Feeling ran high on the day of the race; the goose rushed at the fox

[004-084]and nearly pecked him. Both sides spoke loudly of the approaching

[004-085]victory up to the very moment of the race.

[004-086]"I am absolutely confident of success," said the Tortoise. But

[004-087]the Hare said nothing, he looked bored and cross. Some of his

[004-088]supporters deserted him then and went to the other side, who were

[004-089]loudly cheering the Tortoise's inspiriting words. But many remained

[004-090]with the Hare. "We shall not be disappointed in him," they said. "A

[004-091]beast with such long ears is bound to win."

[004-092]"Run hard," said the supporters of the Tortoise.

[004-093]And "run hard" became a kind of catch-phrase which everybody

[004-094]repeated to one another. "Hard shell and hard living. That's what

[004-095]the country wants. Run hard," they said. And these words were

[004-096]never uttered but multitudes cheered from their hearts.

[004-097]Then they were off, and suddenly there was a hush.

[004-098]The Hare dashed off for about a hundred yards, then he looked

[004-099]round to see where his rival was.

[004-100]"It is rather absurd," he said, "to race with a Tortoise." And he sat

[004-101]down and scratched himself. "Run hard! Run hard!" shouted some.

[004-102]"Let him rest," shouted others. And "let him rest" became a

[004-103]catch-phrase too.

[004-104]And after a while his rival drew near to him.

[004-105]"There comes that damned Tortoise," said the Hare, and he got up

[004-106]and ran as hard as could be so that he should not let the Tortoise

[004-107]beat him.

[004-108]"Those ears will win," said his friends. "Those ears will win; and

[004-109]establish upon an incontestable footing the truth of what we have

[004-110]said." And some of them turned to the backers of the Tortoise and

[004-111]said: "What about your beast now?"

[004-112]"Run hard," they replied. "Run hard."

[004-113]The Hare ran on for nearly three hundred yards, nearly in fact as far

[004-114]as the winning-post, when it suddenly struck him what a fool he looked

[004-115]running races with a Tortoise who was nowhere in sight, and he sat

[004-116]down again and scratched.

[004-117]"Run hard. Run hard," said the crowd, and "Let him rest."

[004-118]"Whatever is the use of it?" said the Hare, and this time he stopped

[004-119]for good. Some say he slept.

[004-120]There was desperate excitement for an hour or two, and then the

[004-121]Tortoise won.

[004-122]"Run hard. Run hard," shouted his backers. "Hard shell and hard

[004-123]living: that's what has done it." And then they asked the Tortoise what

[004-124]his achievement signified, and he went and asked the Turtle. And the

[004-125]Turtle said, "It is a glorious victory for the forces of swiftness." And

[004-126]then the Tortoise repeated it to his friends. And all the beasts said

[004-127]nothing else for years. And even to this day, "a glorious victory for

[004-128]the forces of swiftness" is a catch-phrase in the house of the snail.

[004-129]And the reason that this version of the race is not widely known is

[004-130]that very few of those that witnessed it survived the great forest-fire

[004-131]that happened shortly after. It came up over the weald by night with

[004-132]a great wind. The Hare and the Tortoise and a very few of the beasts

[004-133]saw it far off from a high bare hill that was at the edge of the trees, and

[004-134]they hurriedly called a meeting to decide what messenger they should

[004-135]send to warn the beasts in the forest.

[004-136]They sent the Tortoise.

[004-137]ALONE THE IMMORTALS

[004-138]I heard it said that far away from here, on the wrong side of the

[004-139]deserts of Cathay and in a country dedicate to winter, are all the years

[004-140]that are dead. And there a certain valley shuts them in and hides them,

[004-141]as rumor has it, from the world, but not from the sight of the moon nor

[004-142]from those that dream in his rays.

[004-143]And I said: I will go from here by ways of dream and I will come to

[004-144]that valley and enter in and mourn there for the good years that are

[004-145]dead. And I said: I will take a wreath, a wreath of mourning, and lay

[004-146]it at their feet in token of my sorrow for their dooms.

[004-147]And when I sought about among the flowers, among the flowers for

[004-148]my wreath of mourning, the lily looked too large and the laurel looked

[004-149]too solemn and I found nothing frail enough nor slender to serve as an

[004-150]offering to the years that were dead. And at last I made a slender

[004-151]wreath of daisies in the manner that I had seen them made in one of

[004-152]the years that is dead.

[004-153]"This," said I, "is scarce less fragile or less frail than one of those

[004-154]delicate forgotten years." Then I took my wreath in my hand and

[004-155]went from here. And when I had come by paths of mystery to that

[004-156]romantic land, where the valley that rumour told of lies close to the

[004-157]mountainous moon, I searched among the grass for those poor slight

[004-158]years for whom I bought my sorrow and my wreath. And when I found

[004-159]there nothing in the grass I said: "[[time|Time]] has shattered them and swept

[004-160]them away and left not even any faint remains."

[004-161]But looking upwards in the blaze of the moon I suddenly saw colossi

[004-162]sitting near, and towering up and blotting out the stars and filling the

[004-163]night with blackness; and at those idols' feet I saw praying and making

[004-164]obeisance kings and the days that are and all times and all cities and all

[004-165]nations and all their gods. Neither the smoke of incense nor of the

[004-166]sacrifice burning reached those colossal heads, they sat there not to

[004-167]be measured, not to be over-thrown, not to be worn away.

[004-168]I said: "Who are those?"

[004-169]One answered: "Alone the Immortals."

[004-170]And I said sadly: "I came not to see dread gods, but I came to shed

[004-171]my tears and to offer flowers at the feet of certain little years that are

[004-172]dead and may not come again."

[004-173]He answered me: "These _are_ the years that are dead, alone the

[004-174]immortals; all years to be are Their children--They fashioned their

[004-175]smiles and their laughter; all earthly kings They have crowned, all

[004-176]gods They have created; all the events to be flow down from their

[004-177]feet like a river, the worlds are flying pebbles that They have already

[004-178]thrown, and Time and all his centuries behind him kneel there with

[004-179]bended crests in token of vassalage at Their potent feet."

[004-180]And when I heard this I turned away with my wreath, and went back

[004-181]to my own land comforted.
