## Overview

The Flood of the Centuries is a cosmic event described by the poet in Lord Dunsany's "The Latest Thing" (Section 3 of *Fifty-One Tales*, 1915). When enough centuries have melted on the hills of Fate, the river of Time "terribly floods" — a cleansing deluge that sweeps away the accumulated waste of civilization. It is the collection's most explicit eschatological image.

## Narrative

The event is not directly narrated but prophesied by the poet who walks beside the river. A narrator observes an "unclean-feeder" standing waist-deep in the river, watching for the next piece of filth to drift by — an allegorical figure for those who feed on scandal, sensation, and the worst that civilization produces. The narrator points the man out to the poet, asking what will become of him.

The poet's answer is the tale's crescendo: "Whenever the centuries melt on the hills of Fate the river terribly floods" ([003-084]). The flood is not a punishment but a natural consequence — the river's periodic response to the accumulation of time and waste. The unclean-feeder, with his back to the hills of Fate, cannot see the source that will one day sweep him away.

## Significance

The Flood of the Centuries transforms the geographical metaphor of "the hills of Fate" into an eschatological mechanism. Fate is not a goddess dispensing judgment but a topography: the hills are the source of the river, and when enough time has accumulated — when "the centuries melt" — the hills release a flood. The mechanism is impersonal, inevitable, and indifferent to human merit or guilt.

This is characteristic of Dunsany's treatment of cosmic forces. The flood does not punish the unclean-feeder; it simply sweeps away everything in the river, clean and unclean alike. The only defense is the poet's perspective: to look toward the hills rather than into the water.
