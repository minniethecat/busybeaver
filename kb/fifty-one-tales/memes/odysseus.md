## Overview

Odysseus appears in Lord Dunsany's tale "Death and Odysseus" as an aged version of the Homeric hero. Far from the cunning warrior of the *Odyssey*, Dunsany's Odysseus is a weary old man who, confronted by Death at his door, uses his famous wit not to fight but to negotiate — asking Death to lend him a shoulder so he may warm his hands at the fire one last time.

## Role in the Tales

Odysseus appears in a single tale, "Death and Odysseus," but his encounter with Death is among the collection's most memorable scenes. When Death comes for him, Odysseus does not cower or fight — he engages Death in conversation, then makes a request:

> And Death stood there, and the door and saw there famous Odysseus, with his white hair, and his face all wrinkled.
> [002-110]

The cold wind blows through the open door, and Odysseus — rather than pleading for his life — asks Death to lend him a shoulder so he might warm his pale hands at the hearth one final time:

> And Odysseus went on warming his pale hands...
> [002-114]

> Then "Come now," said Odysseus, "lend me your shoulder," and...
> [002-121]

This quiet, domestic negotiation with Death transforms the epic hero into something more human — a tired old man seeking comfort rather than glory.

## Character Analysis

Dunsany's Odysseus represents the twilight of heroism. Where Homer's Odysseus defeated monsters and outwitted gods, Dunsany's version faces the one adversary no cunning can permanently defeat: Death itself. The genius of the portrayal lies in Odysseus's response — he does not attempt to escape Death but merely to delay, to warm his hands one last time. This small, human gesture against the infinite is quintessentially Dunsanian.

The epithet "famous Odysseus" in the text carries irony: all his fame, all his adventures, have brought him to the same door that awaits every mortal. Yet the fact that he can still negotiate, still find a moment of warmth, suggests that even against Death there is room for the human spirit.
