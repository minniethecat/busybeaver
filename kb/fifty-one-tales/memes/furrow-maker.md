## Overview

"Furrow-Maker" is one of Dunsany's most unusual narrative experiments: a conversation between two rooks about the mortality of Man. The tale belongs to Section 7 of *Fifty-One Tales* (1915) and deploys a non-human perspective to assess humanity's industrial turn. The rooks, who have followed the plough "time out of mind," fear they may lose their ancient provider — not to death, exactly, but to the sickness of cities and engines. The tale's formal daring lies in its refusal to show Man directly; he appears only as a distant figure on a bicycle, a subject of rumour, and finally as a voice in his own heart, promising himself "just one invention more."

## Summary

The tale opens with two rooks — one dressed in black, the other in brown — members of "two old families" [007-025]. They discuss the state of Man, whom they call Furrow-Maker, a name that identifies him by his most essential function in their world: the turning of earth that exposes food.

The rooks note with concern that Man has been changing "of late almost every century" [007-025]. A man passes in the distance on a bicycle, emblem of all this restless alteration. Man, they observe, has taken to cities — reported to them by the black rook's cousin who "lives in belfries" [007-025]. In the cities, Man grows lean and sick.

The rooks debate whether Man might actually die. The brown rook has heard reports from Pigeon — who "came back all dirty" — and from Hare, who ventured to the edge of the cities and found Man too sick to chase him [007-025]. The brown rook believes Man and his "wicked friend Dog" may both perish.

But the black rook is adamant: "Furrow-maker will not die. We must not lose furrow-maker" [007-025]. This is not sentiment but ancestral knowledge — "My family and his have understood each other times out of mind" [007-025]. The black rook knows what follies will kill a species and what it may survive; he judges that engines and cities are a sickness from which Man will eventually recover.

The tale closes with the only direct access to Man's interiority, an isolated sentence:

> And Man said in his heart: "Just one invention more. There is something I want to do with petrol yet, and then I will give it all up and go back to the woods."
>
> [007-025]

## Analysis

### The Non-Human Gaze

The central formal device of "Furrow-Maker" is its total exclusion of the human point of view until the final line. For almost the entire tale, Man is an object of zoological observation — the way a naturalist might discuss the fate of a declining species. The rooks' taxonomy is precise: Man is Furrow-Maker, his defining trait is the plough, his aberrations (cities, engines) are sicknesses. This defamiliarization is comic but also unsettling; it reduces human civilization to a set of symptoms.

### Ancestral Knowledge

The rooks' confidence about Man's survival is not optimism but inherited wisdom. The black rook's family has understood Man's family "times out of mind" [007-025] — a span of centuries that makes the industrial revolution look like a momentary fever. The rooks operate on a different timescale. Their patience is geological; Man's "almost every century" of change is, to them, a worrying acceleration.

### The Final Irony

The closing sentence — Man's private resolution — is the tale's most devastating irony. Man is not unaware of his sickness; he simply intends to indulge it once more before reforming. "Just one invention more" is the addict's logic, and it suggests that Furrow-Maker may indeed not die — but neither will he stop. The rooks' confidence and Man's self-deception sit side by side, unresolved.

The title itself encodes the tale's argument. Man is not Thinker, not Builder, not Lord of Creation — he is Furrow-Maker. His essence, from the rooks' perspective, is not what he builds but what he turns over: the patient, repetitive work that feeds other creatures. Everything else is a distraction, and perhaps a fatal one.

## Characters

- **The black rook** — a member of an old family that has followed Man's plough for centuries. Confident in ancestral knowledge, he believes Man will survive his cities and engines. He speaks with the authority of long observation.
- **The brown rook** — the black rook's friend, more credulous of rumour, more willing to entertain the possibility of Man's extinction. He repeats reports from Pigeon and Hare.
- **Man / Furrow-Maker** — the unseen subject of the rooks' discussion, visible only as a distant cyclist. His one line of inner speech reveals him as simultaneously aware of his sickness and powerless to stop it.

## Themes

- **[[nature]] and civilization** — the rooks represent an older order, watching with concern as Man abandons the plough for the engine.
- **[[time]]** — the conflict between the rooks' ancestral timescale and Man's accelerating pace of change.
- **Mortality and species death** — the tale contemplates, through non-human eyes, the possibility that humanity might not be permanent.
- **The land** — Furrow-Maker's name roots his value in the earth he turns, not the cities he builds.
