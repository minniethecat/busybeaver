## Overview

Nature in Lord Dunsany's *Fifty-One Tales* is not the benign pastoral goddess of Romantic tradition but a diminished, sorrowful figure — an ancient mother whose children have been domesticated or driven away. She appears most vividly in the tale "Nature and Time" [001-051], and in a haunting passage in Section 8 where she comes begging "in frocks" at the doors of human habitation.

## In the Tales

Dunsany returns to the concept of Nature across multiple tales, consistently portraying her as an elder force displaced by human civilization. In the passage from Section 8, Nature is imagined as a beggar:

> "It was Nature that came thus begging in frocks"
>
> [008-128]

The image is striking: Nature, who once ruled uncontested, now wears human clothing and begs at human doors. Her sons are described as "the forlorn one's terrible children" [008-135] — wild things that no longer have a place in the ordered world of farms and cities.

The tale "Nature and Time" [001-051] pairs Nature with [[time|Time]] as two ancient forces observing the transience of human endeavor. Where Time is destructive and capricious, Nature is portrayed with more pathos — she endures, but she has been diminished. The [[thlunrana|city of Thlunrana]] and its doom represents the tension between civilization's walls and the wild forces that ultimately reclaim them.

## Interpretation

Dunsany's treatment of Nature reflects the Edwardian anxiety about urbanization and the loss of wilderness. Writing on the eve of the First World War, he captured a moment when technological civilization seemed both triumphant and fragile. Nature, in these tales, is not a nurturing mother but a displaced sovereign — still powerful, but forced to beg at the gates of the world she once owned.

This conception of Nature as a tragic figure distinguishes Dunsany from the Romantic pantheism of Wordsworth or the Darwinian struggle of Tennyson. Nature in *Fifty-One Tales* is neither benevolent nor hostile — she is simply ancient, patient, and increasingly irrelevant to the humans who have forgotten her.

## Nature and the City

Throughout *Fifty-One Tales*, [[thlunrana|Thlunrana]] serves as the archetype of civilization's doomed resistance to Nature. The city's walls, its prophecies, its terror — all are ultimately helpless against the forces that Nature represents:

> "…its enemy would come upon Thlunrana. And the date of its doom was known."
>
> [005-115]

> "…the prophecy was fulfilled, and Thlunrana was never more a terror."
>
> [005-160]

The fall of Thlunrana can be read as Nature's revenge upon the citadel — a return of the wild order that civilization had temporarily suppressed. In this reading, Nature is not passive or diminished but patient, waiting for the walls to crumble so she can reclaim what was always hers.

## Nature in the Edwardian Imagination

Dunsany wrote *Fifty-One Tales* at a pivotal moment in British environmental consciousness. The industrial revolution had transformed the landscape; urbanization was accelerating; the First World War would soon demonstrate technology's destructive power on an unprecedented scale. Nature, in this context, appeared both vulnerable — threatened by the machine — and terrifying — capable of overwhelming human order in an instant.

Dunsany's Nature reflects both aspects. She comes "begging in frocks" [008-128], a supplicant at the doors of civilization; but her sons are "the forlorn one's terrible children," creatures that remain wild despite domestication. This duality — Nature as both victim and threat — is characteristic of the Edwardian imagination, poised between Romantic reverence and modern anxiety.
