## Overview

"The Return of Song" is one of the shortest and most lyrical tales in *Fifty-One Tales* (1915). Set in a dream-vision of "some fair and far Valhalla," it imagines the life-cycle of worlds as a circulation of song: when a world dies, its swans carry music back to the gods; when a new world is born, larks fly down to seed it. Fewer than two hundred words, it reads less as a narrative than as a single image — a myth in miniature, concerning not gods or men but the cosmic economy of beauty.

## Summary

The narrator, carried by dreams to Valhalla, hears the gods murmur to one another: "The swans are singing again" [005-047]. Looking down, he sees an iridescent bubble — a world — from which white swans rise in an innumerable flock, "singing and singing and singing," until the gods themselves seem like "wild ships swimming in music."

He asks a humble god what is happening, and receives the reply: "Only a world has ended, and the swans are coming back to the gods returning the gift of song." The narrator exclaims at the thought of a whole world dead, and the god answers with the tale's central axiom:

> "The worlds are not for ever; only song is immortal."
>
> [005-047]

The god then points: "Look! Look! There will be a new one soon." The narrator looks and sees the larks, "going down from the gods" — descending to a world about to begin [005-047].

## Analysis

The tale's theological economy is elegant and strange. Worlds are disposable; song is permanent. The gods do not create worlds directly — they receive song back from dead worlds and send larks down to new ones. Song is both the currency and the continuity: what passes between creation and destruction, what outlasts the death of planets.

Dunsany's choice of swans and larks is deliberate. Swans ascend — white, large, ceremonial, carrying the completed gift. Larks descend — small, darting, urgent, the first seed of a world's music. The direction of flight encodes the direction of creation: up to the gods for safekeeping, down from the gods for renewal.

The tone is crucial. There is no mourning for the dead world, no celebration of the new one. The gods observe with a calm that borders on detachment; the humble god reports a cosmic fact. The effect is not coldness but scale — the frame is large enough that individual worlds are like notes in a longer song. What matters is not any one world but the continuity of music across them.

The iridescent bubble image — a world seen from Valhalla as small and shimmering — anticipates later science-fictional depictions of planets from orbit. But Dunsany's bubble is not fragile in the scientific sense; it is fragile in the sense that all beautiful things are, and its beauty is precisely that it will not last.

## Characters

- **The narrator** — a dreamer who finds himself in Valhalla and witnesses the cosmic cycle. He is a passive observer, an "I" who asks questions rather than acts.
- **The humble god** — an unnamed deity who answers the narrator's questions with directness and calm. His humility contrasts with the grandeur of what he describes.
- **The gods** — plural, unnamed, present only as a collective murmur and a vantage point. They are not creators but custodians of song.
