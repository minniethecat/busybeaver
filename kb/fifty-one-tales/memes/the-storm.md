## Overview

"The Storm" is a tale from Section 4 of *Fifty-One Tales* (1915) by Lord Dunsany. Shore-dwellers watch a small, peculiar ship — the _Petite Esperance_ — caught in a storm and dismiss it as worthless when it vanishes beneath a wave. Years later, the same ship returns as a galleon laden with gold and jewels, its crew explaining simply: "We could not be sunk at sea — we had the gods on board."

## Summary

The tale begins with onlookers watching a little ship far out at sea:

> "Because of its uncouth rig and its lonely air and the look that it had of coming from strangers' lands they said: 'It is neither a ship to greet nor desire, nor yet to succor when in the hands of the sea.'"
>
> [004-028]

The sea rises and a great wave — "the ninth-born son of the hurricane and the tide" — hides the _Petite Esperance_ from view. The onlookers dismiss it: "'Twas but a little, worthless alien ship and it is sunk at sea," and return their attention to the familiar merchantmen "laden with silver and appeasing spice."

Years pass. Then a galleon glides into port — its decks covered with cloth of gold, age-old parrots singing illustrious songs, a hold full of emeralds and rubies. It looms "the equal of the cliffs," shutting sunlight from the merchantmen:

> "'Who are you?' they asked, 'far-travelled wonderful ship?'
>
> And they said: 'The _Petite Esperance_.'
>
> 'O,' said the people on shore. 'We thought you were sunk at sea.'
>
> 'Sunk at sea?' sang the sailors. 'We could not be sunk at sea — we had the gods on board.'"
>
> [004-051]

## Analysis

"The Storm" is a parable of humility and the limits of judgment. The onlookers evaluate the _Petite Esperance_ by its appearance — uncouth rig, lonely air, alien flags — and dismiss it as unworthy even of rescue. Their preference for the familiar merchantmen, "laden with silver and appeasing spice," represents the human bias toward the conventional and immediately profitable. But the ship they dismissed carries the gods.

The ship's name — _Petite Esperance_, "Little Hope" — is chosen with Dunsany's characteristic precision. What looks like frailty to the world may carry what the world most needs; the small hope is the one that survives the storm.

The return of the ship as a galleon "laden with Indian loot" reconfigures the earlier dismissal into a moral lesson. The onlookers who cheered the merchantmen "year after year" are confronted with the ship they abandoned. The sailors' answer — "We had the gods on board" — is the key to the tale. The ship did not sink because it carried something weightier than cargo: it carried the divine.

Dunsany does not specify which gods, and the ambiguity is deliberate. The gods aboard the _Petite Esperance_ are whatever the reader believes in — or whatever the world has dismissed as unworthy. The tale's form echoes a parable, but its conclusion offers no moralizing commentary; the sailors simply sing their reply and the tale ends, leaving the reader to absorb the reversal.
