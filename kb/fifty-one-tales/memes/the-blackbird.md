## Overview

The Blackbird appears in Dunsany's "The Song of the Blackbird" as a personified creature-artist whose song is born from wonder. When the Poet asks how it produces its beautiful singing, the Blackbird explains that it once saw the most wonderful she-blackbird in the world and opened its beak in astonishment — and has been singing ever since.

## Role in the Tales

The Blackbird's entire role occupies the tale "The Song of the Blackbird," where it serves as both character and allegory for artistic creation. The Poet, passing by a thorn-tree, hears the blackbird sing:

> As the poet passed the thorn-tree the blackbird sang.
> [008-014]

When the Poet asks how it manages to produce such beauty, the Blackbird's answer is disarmingly simple:

> "It was like this," said the blackbird. "It really was the most..."
> [008-016]

The Blackbird explains that its song is not technique or craft but the natural overflow of wonder. It saw a she-blackbird of extraordinary beauty — "the only really wonderful she-blackbird in the world" — and opened its beak in astonishment:

> ...the only really wonderful she-blackbird in the world, that I opened...
> [008-028]

The song, the Blackbird implies, is simply the sound of that wonder continuing to echo. When the blackbird finishes its story, the Poet shares it with an old man who recalls hearing the same song in his youth:

> ...the poet told the old man the blackbird's wonderful...
> [008-038]

This continuity — that the song has been sung for generations — suggests that genuine wonder at beauty is timeless and self-renewing.

## Character Analysis

The Blackbird is Dunsany's most direct allegory for the artist. It does not "compose" in any technical sense — its song is the involuntary expression of having witnessed beauty. This aligns with the Poet's own situation in "The Assignation," where he too creates from devotion rather than calculation.

The tale's structure — a poet asking a bird about art — is deliberately naive, almost childlike. But through this simplicity Dunsany suggests something profound: that the truest art comes not from ambition or technique but from wonder, and that such art can outlast its creator, passed down through generations of listeners who recognize its authenticity.

The she-blackbird who inspires the song is described in superlatives ("the most beautiful," "the only really wonderful"), suggesting that the object of artistic devotion is always singular and irreplaceable in the artist's eyes — much as Fame was singular to the Poet in "The Assignation."
