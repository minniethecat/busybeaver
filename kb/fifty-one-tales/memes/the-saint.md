## Overview

The Saint is the gatekeeper of Paradise in Dunsany's brief satirical dialogue "The Demagogue and the Demi-monde." Unlike the stern, doctrinal gatekeepers of conventional religious allegory, Dunsany's Saint is sorrowful — a figure who has seen too many hollow lives to be surprised by any of them, but who has not yet grown indifferent to the sadness of wasted existence.

## Role in the Tales

When a demagogue and a demi-mondaine arrive together at the gate of Paradise, the Saint questions each about their earthly life. His questioning is not inquisitorial but diagnostic — he already knows the answers, and his sorrow comes from having to hear them spoken aloud:

> the gate of Paradise. And the Saint looked sorrowfully at them both.
> [003-088]

To the demi-mondaine, who confesses she wanted money, the Saint's judgment is surprisingly gentle — a weary admission rather than a condemnation:

> And after some moments' thought the Saint said: "Well, come in; though you don't deserve to."
> [003-096]

The Saint embodies Dunsany's characteristic theological stance: judgment is real but compassion tempers it. The gatekeeper of Paradise has learned that most human failings are comprehensible, even when they are not excusable.
