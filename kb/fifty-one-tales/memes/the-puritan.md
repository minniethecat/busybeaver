## Overview

The Puritan is a minor but structurally crucial character in Dunsany's revisionist fable "The True History of the Hare and the Tortoise." Where Aesop's original fable celebrates the Tortoise's steady perseverance, Dunsany's version reveals that the famous race was rigged — and the Puritan, with his rigid moral certainty, becomes the unwitting dupe through whom the deception is authenticated.

## Role in the Tales

The tale opens by introducing the Puritan as its moral frame:

> There was once an earnest Puritan who held it wrong to dance.
> [005-002]

The Hare and Tortoise, having colluded to stage a fake race, exploit the Puritan's moral authority to certify their fraud. When the Puritan denounces dancing, the two animals point to their supposed race as proof that steady effort — not joyful movement — is the proper conduct. The Puritan's earnestness, Dunsany implies, makes him easily manipulated: his certainty about right and wrong blinds him to the actual deceit unfolding before him:

> "It is you," said the Puritan, "that put into their hearts the evil desire..."
> [005-025]

Dunsany treats the Puritan with the same irony he applies to the Demagogue and other figures of hollow authority — the Puritan's moral seriousness is real, but it serves ends he never intended.
