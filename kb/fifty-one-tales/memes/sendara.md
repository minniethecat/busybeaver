## Overview

Sendara is one of the invented lands in the Demagogue's seductive vision in "[[the-demagogue-and-the-demi-monde|The Demagogue and the Demi-monde]]." It is a pastoral place of shepherds and rumour, the first stop on a chain of romantic lands through which heralds carry the news of a woman's beauty.

## In "The Demagogue and the Demi-monde"

[[the-demagogue|The Demagogue]], addressing the Demi-mondaine, paints an elaborate picture of the lands through which her fame will spread if she consents to accompany him. The heralds' journey begins at Sendara:

> Do this and I will send heralds far from here with tidings of thy beauty; and they shall run and come to Sendara and men shall know it there who herd brown sheep; and from Sendara the rumour shall spread on, down either bank of the holy river of Zoth, till the people that make wattles in the plains shall hear of it and sing. [004-008]–[004-010]

## Geographical Context

Sendara is the first named land in the Demagogue's geography of romance. From Sendara, the heralds' route passes along the river [[zoth]] to the golden city of [[sooma]], then to [[ingra]] (the joyous city of dancers), and finally across the desert to [[nirid]] (the mountain monasteries).

Sendara's defining characteristic is its simplicity — it is a land of shepherds herding "brown sheep," and its role in the Demagogue's vision is as the origin-point of rumour, the first place beyond the immediate where fame begins to spread.

## Thematic Significance

Sendara belongs to a class of Dunsany-invented place-names that function by their sonic and associative power rather than by detailed description. The name itself — soft, sibilant, vaguely Eastern — evokes a pastoral remoteness that is more atmospheric than geographical. Together with [[zoth|Zoth]], [[sooma|Sooma]], [[ingra|Ingra]], and [[nirid|Nirid]], Sendara forms part of a chain of names that constitute the Demagogue's fantasy-geography: a landscape conjured entirely through the persuasive music of language.

[[the-demagogue|The Demagogue]]'s entire vision is, of course, a rhetorical performance — and the fact that the Demi-mondaine "faintly yet perceptibly shook her head" [004-025] suggests that these beautiful lands exist only in the Demagogue's speech.
