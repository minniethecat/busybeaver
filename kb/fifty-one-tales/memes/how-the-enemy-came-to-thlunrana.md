## Overview

"How the Enemy Came to Thlunrana" is a tale from Section 5 of *Fifty-One Tales* (1915) by Lord Dunsany. It recounts the fall of Thlunrana — a dark citadel of wizardry that terrorizes a valley — through a prophecy fulfilled in the most unexpected way: not by fire or sword, but by a man's laughter at the sight of the inner mystery.

## Summary

The doom of Thlunrana has long been prophesied: an enemy would come, and the date and the gate of entry are known, but the enemy's identity remains obscure — only that he is "of the gods though he dwelt with men."

Thlunrana is described as a secret lamaserai, a cathedral of wizardry, whose narrow windows at night seem to regard men with "the demoniac leer of something that had a secret in the dark":

> "Who were the magicians and the deputy-magicians and the great arch-wizard of that furtive place nobody knew, for they went veiled and hooded and cloaked completely in black."
>
> [005-124]

On the prophesied night, a man from a black-thatched cottage by five pine-trees ventures up to Thlunrana to witness its end. He enters through the southward gate (the Gate of the Doom), climbs a marble stairway, and passes through chambers of increasing darkness and silence — past whispering magicians, past fleeing rats:

> "All the rats in the place were passing away, going whimpering down the stairway."
>
> [005-146]

In the innermost chamber of black marble, a silk pavilion stands — the holy of holies. When the man can bear the stillness no longer, he draws aside the curtain:

> "and saw the inner mystery, and laughed."
>
> [005-159]

The prophecy is fulfilled. Thlunrana is never more a terror; the magicians flee wailing:

> "laughter was the enemy that was doomed to come against Thlunrana through her southward gate (that was named the Gate of the Doom), and it is of the gods but dwells with man."
>
> [005-163]

## Analysis

### The Weapon of Laughter

Dunsany reverses the reader's expectation at every turn. Thlunrana is built up as a place of primal horror — veiled magicians, demoniac windows, the Gate of the Doom — and the reader naturally anticipates a confrontation with some cosmic evil. Instead, the climactic moment is laughter: the simplest, most human response to absurdity.

The inner mystery of Thlunrana, when finally revealed, is not terrifying but ridiculous. Dunsany never tells us what the man saw behind the curtain — and this omission is the tale's masterstroke. Whatever it was, it made him laugh. The most elaborate system of dread in the valley collapses not because it was evil, but because it was, at its core, absurd.

This is a deeply humanistic vision. The great terrors of the world — the institutions, the mysteries, the powers that frighten us into submission — may be, when finally confronted, merely laughable. The man from the cottage is not a hero in any conventional sense; he is simply someone brave enough to look, and human enough to laugh.

### Prophecy and Irony

The prophecy is perfectly fulfilled but in a way no one in the tale could have anticipated. The enemy is "of the gods though he dwelt with men" — laughter is divine in origin (joy, mirth, the comic spirit belong to the gods of revelry) yet it lives among humans as a natural faculty. The southward Gate of the Doom is indeed the point of entry; the date is exact.

This is the irony of prophecy in Dunsany's world: oracles are always correct, but their correctness depends on a reading no one thinks to make. The citizens of the valley, the magicians themselves, and perhaps even the prophets expected a martial enemy — an army, a monster, a curse. None imagined that laughter could be the doom of anything.

The tale thus functions as a parable about the nature of power. Systems of terror depend on being taken seriously. The moment someone laughs at them, they are already defeated.

### Dunsany's Architectural Imagination

Thlunrana is rendered with Dunsany's characteristic architectural precision — the narrow windows, the marble stairway, the three chambers of increasing darkness, the silk pavilion. Each detail builds the atmosphere of dread, but also serves the final reversal: all this architecture exists to protect a mystery that, when seen, is merely funny.

The fleeing rats are a particularly effective touch. Even the vermin of this place know its hour has come; they whimper down the stairs before the man even reaches the inner chamber. Nature itself — in the form of these small, despised creatures — anticipates the human laughter that will end the reign of Thlunrana.
