## Overview

"A Losing Game" is a brief allegorical tale from Section 1 of *Fifty-One Tales* (1915) by Lord Dunsany. In the span of a single dialogue — or rather, a monologue addressed to a silent partner — Man attempts to console Death, whom he believes is sulking over repeated defeats. The tale's closing question reveals a deeper possibility: that Death's tears are not for himself at all.

## Summary

Once in a tavern, Man meets Death face to skull. Man enters cheerfully, but Death sits morosely over an ominous wine and gives no greeting:

> "Come, come," said Man, "we have been antagonists long, and if I were losing yet I should not be surly."
>
> [001-038]

Death remains silent. Man moves closer, speaking cheerfully: "You must not resent defeat." Still Death will not look up. Man, who "hated gloom either in beast or god," tries harder. He cites Death's triumphs — the Dinatherium, the Moon — as proof that Death is still formidable, still capable of victory:

> "Have you not slain the Dinatherium?" he said. "Have you not put out the Moon? Why! you will beat me yet."
>
> [001-038]

At this, Death weeps — "with a dry and barking sound" — and says nothing. Man rises and walks away, wondering. He cannot decide whether Death weeps out of pity for his opponent, or out of grief that the old game is ending and he will never have such sport again, or whether — perhaps — he could never repeat on Earth his triumph over the Moon.

## Analysis

The tale works through dramatic irony. Man assumes he is the victor and Death the loser, and his consolations are founded on that assumption — he magnanimously reassures the defeated. But the reader, observing Death's silence and his "ominous wine," suspects that the game is not what Man thinks it is. Death's grief may be for the coming end of Man himself — the one opponent who made the contest meaningful.

The catalog of Death's victories is revealing. "The Dinatherium" — an enormous prehistoric mammal — and "the Moon" represent conquests at vastly different scales: the biological and the cosmic. Death has extinguished species and celestial bodies alike. That Man might be his next triumph, and that Death might regret it, is the tale's central inversion: the destroyer mourns what he is compelled to destroy.

Structurally, the tale belongs to a group of Dunsany's dialogue-driven pieces in which a figure of immense power — Death, Fame, Time — is reduced to a silent, almost pitiable presence. The bustling cheerfulness of Man, his insistence on treating Death as a rival rather than an inevitability, is both comic and poignant. The "dry and barking sound" of Death's weeping is the only sound he makes in the entire tale — an inarticulate noise that refuses to resolve into explanation.
