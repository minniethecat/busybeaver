## Overview

Olympus, the mountain home of the Greek gods, appears in the Fifty-One Tales in "[[death-and-odysseus|Death and Odysseus]]." Dunsany refers to it as the "Olympian courts," evoking a divine palace where the gods hold their assemblies — and where personal rivalries among immortals set mortal events in motion.

## In "Death and Odysseus"

The tale opens in the divine realm:

> In the Olympian courts Love laughed at Death, because he was unsightly, and because She couldn't help it, and because he never did anything worth doing, and because She would. [005-033]

[[death|Death]], humiliated by Love's laughter, broods on his wrongs and resolves to take revenge — not on Love herself, but on a mortal beloved by the gods: [[odysseus|Odysseus]]. The tale thus makes Olympus the point of origin for its narrative: the divine slight in the Olympian courts cascades down into the mortal world, where [[death|Death]] sets out to frighten [[odysseus|Odysseus]].

## Thematic Significance

Dunsany's Olympus is not a place of serene divinity but of petty emotions and personal vendettas. Love laughs at [[death|Death]] "because She couldn't help it" — the gods are subject to their natures as much as mortals are. [[death|Death]] is "unsightly" and sensitive about it; Love is capricious and cruel.

This domesticating treatment of Olympus — reducing the august seat of the gods to a venue for divine cattiness — is characteristic of Dunsany's ironic relationship to classical mythology. The gods exist, but they are no nobler than the humans they rule. The Olympian courts are a workplace, not a paradise.
