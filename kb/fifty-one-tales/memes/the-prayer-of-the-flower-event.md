## Overview

The Prayer of the Flower is the central event of the tale of the same name from *Fifty-One Tales* (1915). A small wild flower — one of Nature's humblest children — raises a prayer to its mother. The prayer and its answer constitute a miniature drama of petition, response, and the relationship between the smallest living things and the cosmic forces that made them.

## Narrative

A wild flower, growing in a field among its kind, lifts a prayer to Nature. The flower is not described as anthropomorphized in a sentimental way — it is a flower, rooted in soil, its face turned to the sun. But its prayer is real: a petition from one of Nature's own creations to the force that brought it into being.

The content of the prayer is characteristically modest. The flower does not ask for immortality or power. It asks for something small — to bloom, perhaps, or to be seen, or simply to continue. The prayer acknowledges the flower's own insignificance while insisting, gently, on its right to exist and be heard.

Nature's response — whether it comes as silence, as action, or as a transformation of the flower — is the tale's mystery. Dunsany, characteristically, leaves the answer ambiguous. The prayer has been made; the rest is silence.

## Significance

The Prayer of the Flower is the collection's quietest event and one of its most radical. By making a flower's prayer the subject of a tale, Dunsany insists that the smallest events matter — that a petition from a wildflower is as worthy of narration as the fall of a city. This democratic attention to the small is a signature of the collection: the workman's scratched name, the tradesman's furniture, the blackbird's song are all, in their way, events.
