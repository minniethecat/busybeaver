## Overview

Dawn is a recurring natural motif in [[lord-dunsany|Lord Dunsany]]'s *Fifty-One Tales*, appearing at the boundary between night and day. It is associated with the larks who bring morning song from far terrestrial fields to the gods, with [[pegasus|Pegasus]] who breathes the dawn-wind on celestial slopes, and with the cyclical rhythms that govern both the natural and divine worlds. Dawn bridges the terrestrial and the transcendent: it is a moment when the mundane world is touched by something numinous.

## In the Tales

### The Hen

In [[the-hen|The Hen]], morning arrives with a purposeful wind from the North, setting the swallows on their migration southward:

> "And very early one morning, the wind being from the North, the swallows all soared suddenly and felt the wind in their wings; and a strength came upon them and a strange old knowledge and a more than human faith..."
>
> [001-174]

The hen, in contrast, merely flutters to the garden at the end of the road. Dawn in this tale marks the division between those who truly travel and those who only pretend — between the migratory instinct that answers the morning wind and the domestic complacency that mistakes a garden for the South.

### The Unpasturable Fields

In [[the-unpasturable-fields|The Unpasturable Fields]], the clouds speak of Pegasus on their slopes at sunrise, where the dawn-wind becomes a living presence:

> "His hoof-beats ring upon our slopes at sunrise as though our fields were of silver. And breathing the dawn-wind in dilated nostrils, with head tossed upwards and with quivering wings, he stands and stares from our tremendous heights, and snorts and sees far-future wonderful wars rage in the creases and the folds of the togas that cover the knees of the gods."
>
> [003-025]

The larks serve as messengers of dawn, carrying song upward to the gods' domain each morning:

> "...the larks bring to him every morning from far terrestrial fields the song..."
>
> [003-023]

### The Return of Song

In [[the-return-of-song|The Return of Song]], dawn is the moment of cosmic renewal. When a world ends, the swans return song to the gods; when a new world begins, the larks descend:

> "And I looked and saw the larks, going down from the gods."
>
> [005-050]

This brief line — coming after the revelation that "only song is immortal" — positions dawn as the hinge between cosmic death and rebirth. The larks do not merely sing at dawn; they bring dawn itself, carrying the gift of morning from the gods to each new world.

### A Moral Little Tale

In the tale that would later be titled [[a-moral-little-tale|A Moral Little Tale]], the Devil mocks divine creation, dismissing dawn as one of God's useless inventions:

> "...colors," he said, "and useless dawns on hill-slopes facing South, and butterflies flapping along them as soon as the sun rose high..."
>
> [005-028]

The Devil's list — colors, dawns, butterflies, maidens dancing, the West wind, Love — is a catalogue of everything beautiful and gratuitous in the world. That dawn appears first in this list is telling: dawn is beauty without utility, the purest expression of creation's excess.

### The Lonely Idol

In [[the-lonely-idol|The Lonely Idol]], the speaker's prayer uses dawn as a metaphor for the brevity of all beauty:

> "...all that is beautiful hath not remained but is even as the glory of morning upon the water."
>
> [006-065]

Here dawn is not renewal but evanescence — the morning glory on water that vanishes as soon as it appears. This double nature of dawn — renewal and loss, arrival and departure — lies at the heart of Dunsany's treatment of the motif.

### The Messengers

In [[the-messengers|The Messengers]], morning and night become the endless rhythm of the Muses' plea:

> "But morning and night they cried and through long evenings."
>
> [008-098]

Dawn here is no longer a moment of beauty but of compulsion — the time when the divine call is most insistent, most impossible to ignore or refuse.

## Interpretation

Dawn in Dunsany's tales bridges the earthly and the divine. The larks who carry morning song from terrestrial fields to the gods serve as intermediaries between worlds, and the dawn-wind itself becomes a sacred presence breathed in by Pegasus on the unpasturable slopes of the clouds. Yet dawn is also vulnerable to being dismissed — in "A Moral Little Tale," its beauty is deemed "useless" by the Devil, who speaks for a world that values only utility. In "The Lonely Idol," dawn is the emblem of transience, the morning glory that vanishes on water.

This tension between dawn-as-renewal and dawn-as-loss, between the eternal rhythm of morning and the modern impulse to declare it useless, is a central thread across the collection. Dawn is the moment when the world is remade — and also the moment when we are reminded that everything beautiful passes.
