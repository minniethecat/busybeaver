## Overview

Tyre, the ancient Phoenician port city, appears in the Fifty-One Tales alongside [[babylon]], [[nineveh]], and [[persepolis]] as one of the great cities brought low by time. It is invoked both in the cosmic vision of Oblivion's sea and in [[time|Time]]'s self-justifying catalogue of destructions.

## Appearances

### Section 2 (Preface)

In Dunsany's meditation on Oblivion, the wreckage of ancient civilizations drifts on a dark sea:

> the sodden bulk of sunken Tyre and make a darkness round Persepolis. [002-017]

Tyre's epithet here — "sunken" — is particularly apt. The historical Tyre was an island city, its walls rising from the sea. Dunsany's image of Tyre "sunken" literalizes the metaphor: the city that once commanded the waves is now beneath them.

An earlier passage in the same preface lists Tyre among the maritime powers of antiquity:

> from Tyre, eight fisher-fleets and ninety ships of the line. [001-220]

This detail from "[[the-raft-builders|The Raft-Builders]]" gives Tyre a specificity beyond mere ruin: it was a naval power with numbered fleets, a place of measurable might.

### *Nature and Time*

In "[[nature-and-time|Nature and Time]]," [[time|Time]] recites his accomplishments to the grieving goddess:

> "Where is Persepolis that troubled you? Where Tarshish and Tyre? And you have said I forget you." [008-005]–[008-007]

Tyre is a bullet-point in [[time|Time]]'s résumé — one more city whose erasure proves his diligence.

## Thematic Significance

Tyre in the Fifty-One Tales is the maritime twin to [[babylon|Babylon]]'s imperial ruin. Where [[babylon|Babylon]] is associated with winged bulls and sculptural grandeur, Tyre is associated with fleets and the sea — a commercial rather than a military power. Its "sodden bulk" in Oblivion's sea is the most materially vivid of the four fallen cities: we can feel the waterlogged mass of a city that once floated.

Dunsany's choice of Tyre (rather than, say, Carthage or Rome) reflects his taste for the ancient Near East as a reservoir of exotic, half-remembered grandeur. These are cities whose names carry weight even when their histories are obscure — they function as incantations.
