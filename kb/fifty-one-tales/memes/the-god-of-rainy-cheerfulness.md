## Overview

The God of Rainy Cheerfulness is a satirically conceived minor deity in Dunsany's "The Lonely Idol." Unlike the classical gods who govern thunder or harvest, this god presides over a distinctly English concern: the melancholy of rainy weather. He is not found in a temple but purchased from a shop, and his worship is not ancient ritual but a modern woman's private devotion.

## Role in the Tales

The God of Rainy Cheerfulness appears in "The Lonely Idol" (Section 6), where a woman, confined to her house by weather, visits an idol-shop in Moleshill Street and asks for "a god to worship when it is wet." The old shopkeeper produces a grey-stone figurine with what he calls a "propitious look":

> propitious look and was named, as the old man mumbled, The God of Rainy Cheerfulness.
>
> [006-179]

The woman purchases the idol and, on the next wet day, prays to it — with consequences that descend upon Leafy Green Street. Later, the woman returns to the shop, having been satisfied with her purchase:

> she bethought her of Moleshill Street and the mumbling man.
>
> [006-185]

Dunsany's treatment is characteristically ironic: the god is both a piece of commercial goods and, perhaps, genuinely capable of answering prayer. The tale refuses to settle whether the idol is fraud or divinity, leaving the question suspended in the grey London drizzle.

## Character Analysis

The God of Rainy Cheerfulness functions as a comic counterpoint to the Lonely Idol within the same tale. Where the swine-faced idol is tragic — abandoned, un-prayed-to, a failed god — the God of Rainy Cheerfulness is a commercial success, bought and sold, performing to specification. The tale thus sets up a spectrum of divinity: at one end, the ancient, forsaken deity whose power has drained away; at the other, the manufactured, retail god who delivers exactly what was paid for.

This juxtaposition raises an unsettling question that Dunsany never answers: is the manufactured god more real than the forgotten one? The woman's prayer is answered (rainy cheerfulness presumably follows), while the narrator's compassionate prayer to the Lonely Idol asks for the impossible — the arrest of time — and receives only silence. Efficacy, the tale darkly suggests, may have nothing to do with authenticity.

The god's portfolio — cheerfulness during rain — is itself satirical. Classical deities govern cosmic forces; this one manages a minor mood during bad weather. Dunsany's humour here is gentle but precise, deflating the grandiosity of traditional religion by miniaturizing it to the scale of a London sitting-room.
