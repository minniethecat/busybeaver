## Overview

"The Food of Death" is one of Lord Dunsany's most sharply satirical tales from Section 1 of *Fifty-One Tales* (1915). At barely a hundred words, it is an epigram disguised as a fable: Death falls sick, and modern industrial food — the bread whitened with alum, the tinned meats of Chicago, the "cheap Indian tea" — restores him to ravening strength. The moral is implicit and devastating.

## Summary

Death was sick. His attendants brought him the products of the modern food industry:

> bread that the modern bakers make, whitened with alum, and the tinned meats of Chicago, with a pinch of our modern substitute for salt.
>
> [001-042]

They moved him into the dining-room of a great hotel, where "in that close atmosphere Death breathed more freely." They gave him cheap Indian tea, a bottle of something called champagne. They brought a newspaper and consulted the patent medicine advertisements; they gave him the foods recommended for invalids and a little of the prescribed medicine. They gave him milk with borax — "such as children drink in England."

The final sentence, in a single line: "Death arose ravening, strong, and strode again through the cities."

## Analysis

The tale is structured as a catalog — an inventory of modern adulterations, each one making Death stronger. The alum-whitened bread, the Chicago tinned meats, the borax-laced milk: these are not generic complaints about industrial food but specific, contemporary references that an Edwardian reader would have recognized. The "modern substitute for salt" and "cheap Indian tea" locate the critique in the global supply chain of empire — the adulteration of food is bound up with the adulteration of labor and trade.

The satire works by inversion. Food is supposed to sustain life; in this tale, it restores Death. The "close atmosphere" of the great hotel dining-room — a mark of luxury — is precisely the air in which Death thrives. The newspaper, with its patent medicine advertisements, becomes Death's physician. Every institution of modern civilization — the bakery, the cannery, the hotel, the press — conspires to put Death back on his feet.

The tale's immense brevity is part of its force. Dunsany does not linger over the indictment; he lists the items and delivers the result. The single-sentence conclusion — "Death arose ravening, strong, and strode again through the cities" — has the rhythm of a biblical verse. *Ravening* carries the double sense of extreme hunger and violent plunder. Death has been fed by civilization and now repays the favor by consuming it.

This is one of the handful of Dunsany's tales that can be read as direct social critique. Unlike "The Prayer of the Flower," which mourns the loss of a pastoral world, "The Food of Death" aims its satire squarely at the products and practices of early twentieth-century consumer culture. The irony is that nothing has changed: the adulterated bread and patent medicines of 1915 have their precise equivalents in the ultra-processed foods and wellness industries of the present.
