## Overview

Nineveh, the ancient capital of the Assyrian Empire, appears in the Fifty-One Tales paired with [[babylon]] as a symbol of imperial ruin. Its kings and queens drift among "the weedy masses of old centuries" in Dunsany's vision of Oblivion's sea.

## Appearances

### Section 2 (Preface)

In the meditation on Oblivion that prefaces Section 2, Nineveh appears alongside the other fallen cities:

> See now the wreckage of Babylon floating idly, and something there that once was Nineveh; already their kings and queens are in the deeps among the weedy masses of old centuries that hide the sodden bulk of sunken Tyre and make a darkness round Persepolis. [002-014]–[002-017]

The phrasing "something there that once was Nineveh" is notable: where [[babylon|Babylon]]'s wreckage is identifiable, Nineveh is already so far gone that only "something" remains. The city is not even a recognizable ruin — it is a smear on the water.

### *Nature and Time*

[[time|Time]] invokes Nineveh as evidence of his diligence:

> "Did I not throw down Babylon for you? And is not Nineveh gone?" [008-004]

The rhetorical question assumes Nineveh's destruction is common knowledge — a touchstone of ruin that requires no explanation.

## Thematic Significance

In the quartet of fallen cities — [[babylon|Babylon]], Nineveh, [[tyre|Tyre]], [[persepolis|Persepolis]] — Nineveh occupies the position of the least distinct, the most thoroughly erased. Its "kings and queens" are specified, giving it a human dimension, but the city itself is reduced to a pronoun ("something there that once was Nineveh"). This progressive dissolution — from [[babylon|Babylon]]'s identifiable wreckage through Nineveh's near-invisibility to [[tyre|Tyre]]'s sunken bulk and [[persepolis|Persepolis]]'s darkness — traces a gradient of oblivion.
