## Overview

The End of the World is not a single narrated event in Lord Dunsany's *Fifty-One Tales* (1915) but the implied horizon against which all the collection's events are measured. It appears as the flood of the centuries in "The Latest Thing," as the immortality of past years in "Alone the Immortals," and as the shipwreck of civilizations in "The Raft-Builders." The End is never shown directly — it is always the next thing, the thing after, the thing that makes the present moment urgent.

## Narrative

Dunsany never narrates the End of the World. Instead, he assembles fragments of eschatological imagery across multiple tales:

**The Flood.** In "The Latest Thing," the poet prophesies that "whenever the centuries melt on the hills of Fate the river terribly floods." The flood is periodic rather than terminal — it cleanses rather than destroys — but each flood is a kind of ending for what it sweeps away.

**The Immortals.** In "Alone the Immortals," the past years are discovered to be towering, indestructible colossi before whom Time himself kneels. If the years are immortal, then the End of the World is not the end of everything — something will survive, and what survives will be what has already happened.

**The Drowned Civilizations.** In "The Raft-Builders," the sea-floor is "strewn with crowns" — the drowned powers of Babylon, Nineveh, Tyre, Persepolis. Each civilization's end is a world's end, from the perspective of those who lived in it. The End of the World is not a single event but a recurring condition: worlds end all the time, and the story is what survives them.

## Significance

By never narrating the End directly, Dunsany makes it more powerful, not less. The End is the unshown thing, the silence after the last line, the flood that will one day come. Its function in the collection is to give weight to the present: if the world will end, then every flower's prayer, every tradesman's chair, every blackbird's song matters more, not less. The End is what makes the small things count.
