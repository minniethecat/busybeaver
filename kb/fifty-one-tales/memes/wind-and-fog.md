## Overview

"Wind and Fog" is a brief prose fable from Section 1 of *Fifty-One Tales* (1915) by Lord Dunsany. The tale stages a confrontation between two elemental forces: the North Wind, arrogant and loud, who demands that the Fog make way for him; and the Fog, silent and apparently meek, who retreats without a word. But when darkness falls, the Fog reveals itself as the greater destroyer — quietly cataloguing the hundreds of ships, from Spanish galleons to modern battleships, that it has claimed over the millennia. The tale is a parable about the difference between loud pride and quiet power.

## Summary

The North Wind sweeps down upon the sea on "an errand of old Winter" and finds the grey Fog lying silently along the tides. He demands passage in the language of a warrior-prince:

> "Way for us," said the North Wind, "O ineffectual fog, for I am Winter's leader in his age-old war with the ships."
>
> [001-182]

The Wind catalogues his own powers: he overwhelms ships suddenly, drives icebergs against them, crosses oceans while the Fog "moves a mile." He feeds the sea with wreckage and claims that wherever he appears, ships bow to Winter [001-182].

The Fog says nothing. It merely rises, trails away from the sea, crawls up long valleys, and takes refuge among the hills. The Wind's victory seems complete.

Then night falls. In the stillness, the Fog begins to mumble to itself — and the narrator, listening, hears the true catalogue of its power:

> "A hundred and fifteen galleons of old Spain, a certain argosy that went from Tyre, eight fisher-fleets and ninety ships of the line..."
>
> [001-182]

The list extends across millennia and civilizations: Tyre, Spain, modern battleships, nine thousand admirals. The narrator flees from the "fearful contamination" of the Fog's long memory.

## Analysis

The tale's structure is one of perfect reversal. The Wind's opening boast occupies nearly half the text — an aria of self-congratulation rich with military metaphor ("leader in his age-old war," "drive upon them the huge seafaring bergs"). The Fog's response is a single word: nothing. Its silence reads as weakness. The Wind, like many bullies, mistakes quiet for submission.

But Dunsany's genius is in the revelation scene. The Fog, alone in the dark hills, does not boast. It *reminisces* — mumbling to itself "infamously," as though reciting a private ledger of destruction far vaster than anything the Wind has achieved. The catalogue form (galleons, argosies, fisher-fleets, ships of the line, quinquiremes, triremes, modern battleships) spans the entire history of seafaring, from ancient Tyre to the present day. The Wind destroys ships in the present tense; the Fog has been destroying them for three thousand years.

The tale is also a study in the rhetoric of power. The Wind speaks in imperatives and declarations; the Fog speaks in numbers. The Wind's language is heroic; the Fog's is bureaucratic — a ledger of death. And the bureaucratic, Dunsany suggests, is the more terrible.

## Characters

- **The North Wind** — personified as an arrogant warrior, Winter's lieutenant "in his age-old war with the ships." He is loud, proud, and utterly unaware of his own relative insignificance.
- **The Fog** — a silent, patient destroyer whose power lies not in sudden violence but in slow, inexorable accumulation. His catalogue spans three millennia of maritime disaster.
