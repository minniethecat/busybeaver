## Overview

Pan is the Greek god of the wild, shepherds, and rustic music, traditionally
depicted with the legs and horns of a goat. In *Fifty-One Tales*, Lord Dunsany
uses Pan as the central figure in a diptych of tales — [[the-death-of-pan|"The Death of Pan"]] and
[[the-tomb-of-pan|"The Tomb of Pan"]] — that together form a meditation on belief, modernity, and
the persistence of the mythic. Pan is reported dead by travellers from [[london|London]],
mourned and monumentalized, and yet he is never truly gone: the collection's
final line reveals him laughing at his own tomb.

## In the Tales

### The Death of Pan

When London travellers enter [[arcady|Arcady]], they discover the god lying still:

> Horned Pan was still and the dew was on his fur; he had not the look of a
> live animal. And then they said, "It is true that Pan is dead." (001-114)

But Pan is not dead — merely indifferent. When Arcadian maidens arrive and
begin to laugh at the travellers, Pan stirs:

> And at the sound of their laughter Pan leaped up and the gravel flew from
> his hooves as he galloped down the slope, and the travellers feared and
> fled away. (001-124)

### The Tomb of Pan

Despite Pan's revival, the human impulse to memorialize cannot be resisted:

> "Seeing," they said, "that old-time Pan is dead, let us now make a tomb
> for him and a monument, that the dreadful worship of long ago may not
> be remembered." (008-174)

A great marble tomb is erected on a sea-cliff, carved with Pan's humbled image
(008-186). Some mourn, some revile — but Pan himself has the last word:

> softly along the hills, Pan saw the tomb and laughed. (009-002)

### Pan as Distant Voice

Between the two tomb-tales, Pan's voice appears as a refrain — distant,
musical, reproving. In one passage, flowers cry out against the advancing
city, and Pan answers from Arcady:

> "Be patient a little, these things are not for long." (002-160)

## Interpretation

Pan serves as Dunsany's emblem for the mythic imagination itself. The
travellers from London — representatives of the modern, rational world —
declare Pan dead, build him a tomb, and attempt to close the book on the
old gods. But Pan repeatedly defies their pronouncements: he leaps up
laughing, his voice carries on the wind, and the collection ends with his
[[laughter]] directed at his own monument.

This tension — between the modern need to declare myth dead and myth's
stubborn refusal to stay buried — is central to *Fifty-One Tales*.
Pan is not actually a character who develops; he is a condition, a
presence that persists regardless of belief. His laughter echoes the
collection's broader treatment of [[doom]] and [[fate]]: forces that, like
Pan, operate beyond human comprehension or control.

## Pan and Other Mythological Figures

Pan stands at the center of Dunsany's mythological landscape, but he is
not alone. [[charon|Charon]], the ferryman, appears in another Section 1 tale,
navigating the boundary between the living and the dead just as Pan
straddles the line between belief and disbelief. [[the-god-of-rainy-cheerfulness|The God of Rainy Cheerfulness]]
represents another aspect of the old divinities — diminished, domesticated,
but not extinguished. [[pegasus|Pegasus]], the winged horse, appears briefly as
another emblem of a mythic past that has been captured and caged by modernity.
Together these figures suggest that the old gods have not vanished; they
have merely retreated to the margins.
