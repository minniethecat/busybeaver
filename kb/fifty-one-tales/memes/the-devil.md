## Overview

The Devil appears in one of Lord Dunsany's tales in *Fifty-One Tales* as a sardonic supernatural figure who visits a Puritan in a dream. Rather than the traditional tempter, Dunsany's Devil presents a surprising argument: it is God, not the Devil, who created the things the Puritan opposes — the colors, the butterflies, the dancing, and Love itself. The Devil merely endures the torment of hearing people being happy.

## Role in the Tales

The tale centers on an earnest Puritan with an honest face and flowing white beard who has spent his life suppressing dancing, laughter, and mirth. One night the Devil appears to him in a dream and says "Well done." When the Puritan recoils, the Devil claims him as a faithful servant: "Have you not put apart the couples that would dance? Have you not checked their laughter and their accursed mirth?"

The Devil reveals his true nature not as the instigator of earthly pleasures but as one tormented by them, confessing: "You do not know what a detestable thing it is to sit in hell and hear people being happy, and singing in theatres and singing in the fields."

> And the Devil laughed contemptuously and spoke. "He only made the silly colors," he said, "and useless dawns on hill-slopes facing South, and butterflies flapping along them as soon as the sun rose high, and foolish maidens coming out to dance, and the warm mad West wind, and worst of all that pernicious influence Love."
>
> — 005-027

When the Puritan resolves to fight the Devil upon waking, the Devil delivers the tale's grim conclusion: "You don't wake up out of this sleep." The two are drawn arm in arm through Hell's black steel doors into eternal punishment.
