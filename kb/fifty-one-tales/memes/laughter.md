## Overview

Laughter appears in Lord Dunsany's tale "How the Enemy Came to Thlunrana" (Section 1 of *Fifty-One Tales*, 1915) as the personified force of destruction prophesied to bring down the sinister citadel of wizardry. Unlike conventional enemies of sword or sorcery, Laughter is a divine power that dwells among men — humble, familiar, and therefore unstoppable against the pretensions of darkness.

## Role in the Tales

The tale builds Thlunrana as a place of dread — "that secret lamaserai, that chief cathedral of wizardry" — with hooded magicians, whispered incantations, and a "demoniac leer" from its narrow windows. The doom is prophesied in advance: an enemy would enter through the southward Gate of the Doom, and "none had prophesied of the enemy who he was save that he was of the gods though he dwelt with men."

A brave man from "the black-thatched cottage by the five pine-trees" ventures into Thlunrana on its final night. Passing through chambers of increasing darkness, he reaches the holy of holies — a silk pavilion curtained in black velvet, guarded by silent figures. He draws aside the curtain:

> and saw the inner mystery, and laughed.
>
> [001-037]

The laughter itself is the fulfillment. The tale reveals:

> laughter was the enemy that was doomed to come against Thlunrana through her southward gate (that was named the Gate of the Doom), and it is of the gods but dwells with man.
>
> [001-037]

The magicians flee wailing through open fields, and Thlunrana is never again a terror. The elaborate apparatus of fear is undone by a single, natural human response.

## Analysis

Dunsany's choice of Laughter as the prophesied destroyer is characteristic of his ironic theology. The magicians have prepared for every supernatural threat, but the enemy that defeats them is not arcane — it is the most ordinary and divine of human capacities. The man does not cast a spell or wield a weapon; he simply *sees* the inner mystery for what it is and laughs. The tale suggests that the truly divine powers are those inseparable from human nature, not those cloaked in ritual and darkness.
