## Overview

"The Sphinx at Giza" is a tale from Section 1 of *Fifty-One Tales* (1915) by Lord Dunsany. Taking the form of a first-person meditation, the narrator gazes upon the Egyptian Sphinx and muses on her singular achievement: she has painted her face and thereby captivated Time, who dallies at her feet while cities and civilizations crumble around her. The tale is at once a reflection on vanity's strange power over destruction and a fantasy of what humanity might accomplish if it could disarm Time altogether.

## Summary

The tale opens with the narrator observing the Sphinx's painted face — an act of cosmetic artifice that serves an extraordinary purpose: "She had painted her face in order to ogle Time" [001-130]. Time, the universal destroyer who has erased every other painted face in the world, has spared hers alone:

> Delilah was younger than she, and Delilah is dust. Time hath loved nothing but this worthless painted face.
>
> [001-132]

The narrator catalogues Time's failures while entranced: temples stand unspoiled around her, an old man passes untouched [001-139], and the seven gates of Thebes — which Time once carried away — are forgotten while he lingers at her feet.

The meditation then pivots to an imagined future: if the Sphinx would only betray Time's secret, humanity could blind him, rendering him harmless. The blind Time would stumble and dance for humanity's amusement, ripening corn and performing menial work [001-150]. He would be imprisoned in the great chamber of the Pyramid of Cheops, brought out only for feasts [001-156].

But the reverie ends on a note of dread. The narrator imagines Time, "in his ultimate anguish," blindly seizing the world and the moon and pulling down the House of Man upon himself [001-160].

## Analysis

Dunsany's tale operates on two planes simultaneously. On the surface, it is a whimsical fantasy about time, vanity, and the ancient Sphinx. The image of Time as a besotted fool — "his foolish hair all spread about her paws" [001-143] — is comic in the manner of a tall tale. But beneath the whimsy lies a darker meditation on the human relationship with mortality.

The narrator's fantasy of blinding and enslaving Time is the ultimate revenge fantasy: humanity, which has always been Time's victim, would become his master. Yet Dunsany undercuts this fantasy with the final image — Time, blinded and desperate, destroying everything in his death throes. The implication is clear: to disarm Time is to invite total annihilation. Time may be a tyrant, but he is also the framework within which human life has meaning.

The Sphinx herself is an ambiguous figure. Her painted face — an emblem of feminine vanity — is also the weapon that has disarmed the great destroyer. Dunsany seems to suggest that beauty and artifice possess a power that brute force cannot match: Time, who has "smitten us and mocked us" [001-149], is helpless before a painted smile.

## Characters

- **The Sphinx at Giza** — the ancient Egyptian monument, personified as a feminine figure who paints her face to captivate Time. She is at once a symbol of enduring beauty and of the strategic power of artifice.
- **Time** — personified as a destructive but susceptible force. He destroys cities, kills children, and carries away civilizations — yet lingers foolishly at the Sphinx's feet, distracted by her smile.
- **The narrator** — an unnamed "I" who observes the Sphinx and meditates on the relationship between humanity, Time, and the ancient monument. His voice blends wonder, calculation, and fear.

## Text

> I saw the other day the Sphinx's painted face.
>
> She had painted her face in order to ogle Time.
>
> And he has spared no other painted face in all the world but hers.
>
> [001-129]

> Time dallies like a fool at her feet when he should be smiting cities.
>
> Time never wearies of her silly smile.
>
> [001-136]

> And yet I fear that in his ultimate anguish he may take hold blindly of the world and the moon, and slowly pull down upon him the House of Man.
>
> [001-160]
