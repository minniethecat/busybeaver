## Overview

Time in *Fifty-One Tales* is less an abstraction than a character — an ancient,
grey-haired wanderer whose work is the slow ruin of all things built by human hands.
Lord Dunsany gives Time a sardonic personality: he is neither cruel nor kind, but
possesses the weary patience of one who has outlasted empires. His most vivid
appearance comes in "Time and the Tradesman," where he encounters a furniture
maker artificially aging a chair — a moment of dark comedy that captures Dunsany's
thematic concern with the tension between the genuine and the counterfeit.

## In the Tales

### Time and the Tradesman

The central appearance of Time comes in the tale that bears his name:

> Once Time as he prowled the world, his hair grey not with weakness but with
> dust of the ruin of cities, came to a furniture shop and entered the Antique
> department. And there he saw a man darkening the wood of a chair with dye
> and beating it with chains and making imitation wormholes in it. (002-163)

Time's response is characteristically understated. He does not rage or punish —
he simply corrects the man's work with the authentic article:

> And at last he said: "That is not how I work," and he turned the man's hair
> white and bent his back and put some furrows in his little cunning face; then
> turned and strode away, for a mighty city that was weary and sick and too
> long had troubled the fields was sore in need of him. (002-170)

The contrast is pointed: the furniture-maker's artificial aging is revealed as a
petty imitation beside Time's casual, cosmic destruction. The tradesman wanted to
simulate age; Time gave him the real thing — on his own body.

### Time as Background Presence

Beyond his named tale, Time appears as a recurring presence throughout the
collection. He is the force that "carried away the seven gates of Thebes" (001-140),
the one who "dallies like a fool at her feet when he should be smiting cities" (001-136),
and the silent enemy whom the flowers beg to be freed from (006-104). In each
appearance, Time is neither villain nor hero — he is simply the oldest and most
patient force in the cosmos, indifferent to human concerns and human schedules.

## Interpretation

Dunsany's Time is closer to a classical god than a modern abstraction. He walks
the world in human form, capable of conversation and critical judgment. But unlike
the Olympians, who scheme and compete, Time simply proceeds. His rebuke to the
tradesman is not delivered in anger but in something closer to professional
pride — "That is not how I work" could be the motto of a master craftsman
correcting an apprentice.

This portrayal aligns with the collection's broader treatment of cosmic forces as
characters in a drama that humanity can observe but never control. Time, like
Death and Pan, is a figure from a world older than modernity — diminished in
belief but not in power.
