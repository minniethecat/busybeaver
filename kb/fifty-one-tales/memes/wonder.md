## Overview

Wonder is the characteristic emotional register of [[lord-dunsany|Lord Dunsany]]'s *Fifty-One Tales*. It is the response to beauty that exceeds explanation, to events that defy ordinary causality, and to the sudden recognition of the sublime in the everyday. Unlike fear or sorrow, wonder is an elevating emotion — it lifts the observer out of the mundane and into the mythic. Yet wonder can also be a response to horror, as when the narrator encounters the [[angel|Angel]] building Hell.

## In the Tales

### Wonder as Creative Source

The blackbird in [[the-song-of-the-blackbird|The Song of the Blackbird]] embodies wonder as the source of artistic creation:

> "One day last Spring... I was thinking how wonderful she was and it seemed so extraordinary to think that I should ever have seen her, the only really wonderful she-blackbird in the world, that I opened my beak to give a shout, and then this song came..."
>
> [008-026]

The blackbird's song — the first song — emerges not from skill or practice but from the overwhelming experience of wonder at beauty. The word "wonderful" and "extraordinary" recur throughout the passage, emphasizing that true art begins in astonishment.

The old wanderer who later hears the story offers a different perspective: the song is ancient, made by God [008-040]. But this does not diminish the blackbird's wonder — it deepens it, suggesting that wonder is the human (or avian) experience of touching something eternal.

### Wonder at Cosmic Scale

In [[the-raft-builders|The Raft-Builders]], wonder takes on a darker, more cosmic dimension. The narrator stands among mountains that have witnessed the entire span of civilization:

> "And when I saw those grey and watchful mountains sitting where they sat while the cities of the civilization of Araby and Asia arose like crocuses, and like crocuses fell, I wondered for how long there would be smoke in the valley and little fields on the hills."
>
> [003-003]

Here wonder is the vertigo of contemplating deep time — the realization that entire civilizations are as brief as flowers, and that one's own world is equally transient.

### Wonder and Terror

In [[the-reward|The Reward]], the narrator's dreaming spirit encounters a [[angel|huge angel with a hammer]] building a new wing of Hell [006-142]. He "wondered what he did in that dreadful place" [006-143]. The wonder here mingles with terror — the angel's matter-of-fact explanation that "they are adding to Hell, to keep pace with the times" [006-145] transforms wonder into moral horror.

In [[a-losing-game|A Losing Game]], after Man confronts [[death|Death]] and sees him weep, he "arose and went wondering away; for he knew not if Death wept out of pity for his opponent, or because he knew that he could never win" [006-004]. Wonder here is the state of unresolved mystery — the inability to parse the moral meaning of what one has witnessed.

## Interpretation

Dunsany suggests that wonder is not merely a passive emotion but an active, creative force. The blackbird's wonder produces song; the poet's wonder produces verse. Wonder is the bridge between the mortal perceiver and the eternal beauty that exists independently of perception. When the Golden Town loses its beauty, wonder departs with it — and with wonder goes art ("the old songs are gone" [008-105]). Yet wonder also marks the limit of human understanding — the point beyond which meaning cannot be parsed, as when Man wonders at Death's ambiguous tears.
