## Overview

"Taking Up Picadilly" is a very short fantastical tale from Section 1 of *Fifty-One Tales* (1915) by Lord Dunsany. It captures a single surreal moment on a London street: workmen dismantling Picadilly not as a civic improvement but as a complete removal — with the night sky visible beneath the paving stones.

## Summary

Walking down Picadilly toward Grosvenor Place, the narrator sees workmen with pickaxes, in corduroy trousers with leather bands below the knee — the "York-to-London" bands of the working class. They are working with such unusual vehemence that he stops and asks what they are doing:

> "We are taking up Picadilly," he said to me.
>
> "But at this time of year?" I said. "Is it usual in June?"
>
> "We are not what we seem," said he.
>
> [001-039]

The narrator guesses they are doing it for a joke, or for a bet. Neither is correct. Then he looks down into the section they have already picked up, and:

> though it was broad daylight over my head it was darkness down there, all full of the southern stars.
>
> [001-039]

The foreman explains simply: "It was noisy and bad and we grew aweary of it. We are not what we appear." They were taking up Picadilly altogether.

## Analysis

The tale is built on a single, perfectly executed reversal. The opening mimics the tone of a pedestrian anecdote — a man walking through London, noticing workmen. The detail of the "York-to-London" bands grounds the scene in the material reality of Edwardian labor. But with "We are not what we seem," the frame cracks, and by the time the narrator peers beneath the excavated street and sees southern stars, the mundane has collapsed into the cosmic.

The identity of the workmen is never revealed. They could be gods — classical deities grown tired of the racket of modernity — or they could be Dunsany's own fanciful invention. The ambiguity is intentional: the tale works whether one reads it as pagan mythology transposed to London or as a dream in broad daylight. The central image — a London street lifted away to reveal the stars beneath — has the quality of a Surrealist painting composed three decades before Surrealism existed.

The brevity is part of the effect. At roughly two hundred words, the tale does not explain, justify, or elaborate. It records an encounter and walks away, leaving the reader — like the narrator — with only the image of a city street opening onto the firmament. The laconic final sentence, "They were taking up Picadilly altogether," refuses the closure of explanation. It is a fact stated with the same matter-of-factness as the opening observation about London streets, but the fact it states is the undoing of London itself.
