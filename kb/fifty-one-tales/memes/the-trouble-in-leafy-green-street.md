## Overview

"The Trouble in Leafy Green Street" is a suburban ghost story stripped to its bones. A woman, afflicted by rainy-day melancholy, buys a god from a shop — the God of Rainy Cheerfulness, carved of grey stone — and prays to it on the next wet day. The result is "that doom of which all men speak." Dunsany tells us nothing of what the doom actually is; the tale's horror is the horror of a door left ajar on darkness, a suburban house at the corner of Leafy Green Street that has become the site of something unnameable.

## Summary

A woman visits the idol-shop in Moleshill Street, where an old man mumbles, and makes her request: "I want a god to worship when it is wet" [006-171]. The old man recites the penalties for idolatry, but she is unmoved: "Give me a god to worship when it is wet."

The old man retrieves from the back of his shop a grey-stone idol with "a propitious look," named the God of Rainy Cheerfulness. She pays his "preposterous price" and takes it home.

On the next rainy day, she prays to the grey-stone idol — "who knows with what ceremony or what lack of it?" — and the tale ends with a single, freighted sentence:

> And so brought down on her in Leafy Green Street, in the preposterous house at the corner, that doom of which all men speak.
>
> [006-171]

## Analysis

The tale's power lies in what it refuses to describe. We never learn what "that doom" is — only that "all men speak" of it, which suggests it is notorious, perhaps legendary, a cautionary tale told in the neighbourhood. The gap between the comic setup (a suburban woman buying a god like an umbrella for rainy days) and the ominous ending is the story's engine. The doom could be death, madness, disappearance, or something worse — Dunsany's silence invites the reader to supply the worst thing they can imagine.

The name "Leafy Green Street" is itself a weapon. It could not be more innocuous — a suburban lane, probably with trees, probably with pleasant houses. "The preposterous house at the corner" is the only detail we get about her dwelling: it is preposterous, excessive, somehow wrong even before the doom arrives. Suburbia is not safe; the ordinary streets have their own shadows.

The old man's mumbled warnings about idolatry are legally correct but practically useless — he enumerates the penalties and then sells her the god anyway. He is a shopkeeper first, a guardian of souls second. The tale thus functions as a tiny allegory of consumer capitalism: the transaction is warned against, completed anyway, and the consequences arrive on schedule with the rain.

The god itself — the "God of Rainy Cheerfulness" — is a parody of niche deities. It does not promise salvation or meaning; it promises cheerfulness in wet weather. Its domain is comically narrow, and yet it is still a god, and treating a god as a consumer product has consequences. The tale does not tell us whether the doom comes from the god or from the act of buying one; the ambiguity is the point.

## Characters

- **The woman** — unnamed, undeterred by warnings, a consumer of gods. Her doom is the tale's only resolution.
- **The old man** — a mumbling idol-seller in Moleshill Street who recites the dangers of idolatry but sells the idol regardless. He is commerce incarnate.
- **The God of Rainy Cheerfulness** — a grey-stone idol with a "propitious look," silent and patient. It is a god; it does what gods do.
