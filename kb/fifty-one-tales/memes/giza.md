## Overview

Giza (spelled "Gizeh" in the original text) is the site of the Great Sphinx on the Giza Plateau in Egypt. In the Fifty-One Tales, it appears in "[[the-sphinx-at-giza|The Sphinx at Giza]]" (titled "[[the-sphinx|The Sphinx]] at Gizeh" in the collection), where the Sphinx is portrayed as a conscious being who paints her face to attract the attention of [[time|Time]].

## In "The Sphinx at Giza"

The tale is brief and concentrated:

> I saw the other day the Sphinx's painted face. She had painted her face in order to ogle Time. And he has spared no other painted face in all the world but hers. [001-010]–[001-012]

The narrator reflects that Delilah, younger than the Sphinx, is already dust — but [[time|Time]] has preserved the Sphinx alone among painted faces.

## Thematic Significance

Giza, as the location of the Sphinx, functions in this tale as the meeting-point of the ancient and the eternal. [[the-sphinx|The Sphinx]]'s "painted face" is both literal (the ancient Egyptian practice of painting statuary) and metaphorical — she has made herself up to attract [[time|Time]] himself, and [[time|Time]], who destroys all other beauties, has spared her.

The contrast with Delilah (biblical, mortal, remembered only for a single act) emphasizes the Sphinx's unique status: she is older than the oldest stories, and [[time|Time]], who "hath loved" her, has preserved her while consuming everything else of her era.

Dunsany's spelling "Gizeh" reflects the early 20th-century English transliteration of the Arabic. The tale treats the location not as a tourist site but as the Sphinx's domain — a place outside human chronology.
