## Overview

"The Workman" is a tale from Section 2 of *Fifty-One Tales* (1915) in which Lord Dunsany turns the genre of the ghost story into a vehicle for cosmic satire. A narrator sees a workman fall from a great height and, in his final seconds, try to carve his name into the scaffolding — an act the narrator dismisses as futile. But that night, the workman's ghost returns, laughing, to deliver a more uncomfortable verdict: the narrator's own "serious work" is no less futile, and his "whole silly civilization" will be tidied up in a few centuries.

## Summary

The narrator witnesses a workman fall from the summit of "some vast hotel" — a fall of nearly three hundred feet. In his last seconds, the workman pulls out a knife and tries to carve his name into the scaffolding. The narrator is contemptuous of this "futile thing": the man will be unrecognizably dead in moments, and the pole itself will be burnt for firewood within weeks [002-001].

The narrator goes home to his own work, but the image of the falling workman distracts him. Late that night, while he is still labouring, the workman's ghost floats through the wall:

> It said: "I'm a laughin' at you sittin' and workin' there."
>
> [002-001]

The narrator demands to know why the ghost laughs at "serious work." The ghost's reply — rendered in Dunsany's phonetic rendering of working-class speech — is devastating:

> "Why, yer bloomin' life 'ull go by like a wind," he said, "and yer 'ole silly civilization 'ull be tidied up in a few centuries."
>
> [002-001]

The ghost fades back through the wall, laughing audibly, into eternity.

## Analysis

The tale is structured as a double reversal. In the first half, the narrator — a "serious" man with "serious work" — looks down on the dying workman's attempt to carve his name. The workman's gesture seems the epitome of vanity: a pointless, desperate bid for immortality by a man who has seconds to live. The narrator returns to his desk feeling morally superior.

In the second half, the ghost turns the judgment back on the narrator. From the perspective of eternity, the narrator's "serious work" is no less futile than the workman's scratched name. The difference between three hundred feet and a few centuries is, in cosmic terms, negligible. Both men are engaged in the same enterprise — trying to leave a mark before the darkness takes them — and the ghost, having crossed over, finds both efforts equally funny.

Dunsany's choice to render the ghost in dialect ("yer bloomin' life," "'ole silly civilization") is significant. The workman, whom the narrator dismissed as a fool, becomes the vehicle of genuine wisdom. The tale inverts the expected hierarchy: the uneducated labourer understands eternity better than the educated gentleman.

The tale also echoes "The Raft-Builders" [001-226] in its meditation on the ephemerality of human effort. But where "The Raft-Builders" is elegiac, "The Workman" is comic — the laughter of the dead is the only honest response to the pretensions of the living.

## Characters

- **The workman** — a labourer who falls to his death and, in his final seconds, tries to carve his name into the scaffolding. After death, his ghost returns to mock the living.
- **The narrator** — a "serious" man engaged in "serious work," whose self-importance is dismantled by the laughing ghost. He represents the educated class and its unexamined assumptions about significance.
- **The ghost** — the workman's spirit, grey and diaphanous, shuddering with laughter at the futility of human civilization. He speaks in working-class dialect, and his laughter is the tale's central symbol: the cosmic joke at the expense of human vanity.

## Text

> I saw a workman fall with his scaffolding right from the summit of some vast hotel. And as he came down I saw him holding a knife and trying to cut his name on the scaffolding. He had time to try and do this for he must have had nearly three hundred feet to fall.
>
> [002-001]

> "I'm a laughin' at you sittin' and workin' there."
>
> "And why," I asked, "do you laugh at serious work?"
>
> "Why, yer bloomin' life 'ull go by like a wind," he said, "and yer 'ole silly civilization 'ull be tidied up in a few centuries."
>
> [002-001]

> Then he fell to laughing again and this time audibly; and, laughing still, faded back through the wall again and into the eternity from which he had come.
>
> [002-001]
