[003-001]And when I saw those grey and watchful mountains sitting where

[003-002]they sat while the cities of the civilization of Araby and Asia arose

[003-003]like crocuses, and like crocuses fell, I wondered for how long there

[003-004]would be smoke in the valley and little fields on the hills.

[003-005]THE UNPASTURABLE FIELDS

[003-006]Thus spake the mountains: "Behold us, even us; the old ones, the

[003-007]grey ones, that wear the feet of [[time|Time]]. Time on our rocks shall break

[003-008]his staff and stumble: and still we shall sit majestic, even as now,

[003-009]hearing the sound of the sea, our old coeval sister, who nurses the

[003-010]bones of her children and weeps for the things she has done.

[003-011]"Far, far, we stand above all things; befriending the little cities until

[003-012]they grow old and leave us to go among the myths.

[003-013]"We are the most imperishable mountains."

[003-014]And softly the clouds foregathered from far places, and crag on

[003-015]crag and mountain upon mountain in the likeness of Caucasus upon

[003-016]Himalaya came riding past the sunlight upon the backs of storms and

[003-017]looked down idly from their golden heights upon the crests of the

[003-018]mountains.

[003-019]"Ye pass away," said the mountains.

[003-020]And the clouds answered, as I dreamed or fancied,

[003-021]"We pass away, indeed we pass away, but upon our unpasturable

[003-022]fields Pegasus prances. Here Pegasus gallops and browses upon

[003-023]song which the larks bring to him every morning from far terrestrial

[003-024]fields. His hoof-beats ring upon our slopes at sunrise as though our

[003-025]fields were of silver. And breathing the dawn-wind in dilated nostrils,

[003-026]with head tossed upwards and with quivering wings, he stands and

[003-027]stares from our tremendous heights, and snorts and sees far-future

[003-028]wonderful wars rage in the creases and the folds of the togas that

[003-029]cover the knees of the gods."

[003-030]THE WORM AND THE ANGEL

[003-031]As he crawled from the tombs of the fallen a worm met with an angel.

[003-032]And together they looked upon the kings and kingdoms, and youths

[003-033]and maidens and the cities of men. They saw the old men heavy in

[003-034]their chairs and heard the children singing in the fields. They saw far

[003-035]wars and warriors and walled towns, wisdom and wickedness, and

[003-036]the pomp of kings, and the people of all the lands that the sunlight knew.

[003-037]And the worm spake to the angel saying: "Behold my food."

[003-038]"Be dakeon para Thina poluphloisboio Thalassaes," murmured the

[003-039]angel, for they walked by the sea, "and can you destroy that too?"

[003-040]And the worm paled in his anger to a greyness ill to behold, for for

[003-041]three thousand years he had tried to destroy that line and still its

[003-042]melody was ringing in his head.

[003-043]THE SONGLESS COUNTRY

[003-044]The poet came unto a great country in which there were no songs.

[003-045]And he lamented gently for the nation that had not any little foolish

[003-046]songs to sing to itself at evening.

[003-047]And at last he said: "I will make for them myself some little foolish

[003-048]songs so that they may be merry in the lanes and happy by the

[003-049]fireside." And for some days he made for them aimless songs such

[003-050]as maidens sing on the hills in the older happier countries.

[003-051]Then he went to some of that nation as they sat weary with the

[003-052]work of the day and said to them: "I have made you some aimless

[003-053]songs out of the small unreasonable legends, that are somewhat akin

[003-054]to the wind in the vales of my childhood; and you may care to sing

[003-055]them in your disconsolate evenings."

[003-056]And they said to him:

[003-057]"If you think we have time for that sort of nonsense nowadays

[003-058]you cannot know much of the progress of modern commerce."

[003-059]And then the poet wept for he said: "Alas! They are damned."

[003-060]THE LATEST THING

[003-061]I saw an unclean-feeder by the banks of the river of Time. He crouched

[003-062]by orchards numerous with apples in a happy land of flowers; colossal

[003-063]barns stood near which the ancients had stored with grain, and the sun

[003-064]was golden on serene far hills behind the level lands. But his back was

[003-065]to all these things. He crouched and watched the river. And whatever

[003-066]the river chanced to send him down the unclean-feeder clutched at

[003-067]greedily with his arms, wading out into the water.

[003-068]Now there were in those days, and indeed still are, certain uncleanly

[003-069]cities upon the river of Time; and from them fearfully nameless things

[003-070]came floating shapelessly by. And whenever the odor of these came

[003-071]down the river before them the unclean-feeder plunged into the dirty

[003-072]water and stood far out, expectant. And if he opened his mouth one

[003-073]saw these things on his lips.

[003-074]Indeed from the upper reaches there came down sometimes the

[003-075]fallen rhododendron's petal, sometimes a rose; but they were useless

[003-076]to the unclean-feeder, and when he saw them he growled.

[003-077]A poet walked beside the river's bank; his head was lifted and his

[003-078]look was afar; I think he saw the sea, and the hills of Fate from which

[003-079]the river ran. I saw the unclean-feeder standing voracious, up to his

[003-080]waist in that evil-smelling river.

[003-081]"Look," I said to the poet.

[003-082]"The current will sweep him away," the poet said.

[003-083]"But those cities that poison the river," I said to him.

[003-084]He answered: "Whenever the centuries melt on the hills of Fate the

[003-085]river terribly floods."

[003-086]THE DEMAGOGUE AND THE DEMI-MONDE

[003-087]A demagogue and a demi-mondaine chanced to arrive together at

[003-088]the gate of Paradise. And the Saint looked sorrowfully at them both.

[003-089]"Why were you a demagogue?" he said to the first.

[003-090]"Because," said the demagogue, "I stood for those principles that

[003-091]have made us what we are and have endeared our Party to the great

[003-092]heart of the people. In a word I stood unflinchingly on the plank of

[003-093]popular representation."

[003-094]"And you?" said the Saint to her of the demi-monde.

[003-095]"I wanted money," said the demi-mondaine.

[003-096]And after some moments' thought the Saint said: "Well, come in;

[003-097]though you don't deserve to."

[003-098]But to the demagogue he said: "We genuinely regret that the limited

[003-099]space at our disposal and our unfortunate lack of interest in those

[003-100]Questions that you have gone so far to inculate and have so ably

[003-101]upheld in the past, prevent us from giving you the support for which

[003-102]you seek."

[003-103]And he shut the golden door.

[003-104]THE GIANT POPPY

[003-105]I dreamt that I went back to the hills I knew, whence on a clear day

[003-106]you can see the walls of Ilion and the plains of Roncesvalles. There

[003-107]used to be woods along the tops of those hills with clearings in them

[003-108]where the moonlight fell, and there when no one watched the fairies

[003-109]danced.

[003-110]But there were no woods when I went back, no fairies nor distant

[003-111]glimpse of Ilion or plains of Roncesvalles, only one giant poppy waved

[003-112]in the wind, and as it waved it hummed "Remember not." And by its

[003-113]oak-like stem a poet sat, dressed like a shepherd and playing an

[003-114]ancient tune softly upon a pipe. I asked him if the fairies had passed

[003-115]that way or anything olden.

[003-116]He said: "The poppy has grown apace and is killing gods and

[003-117]fairies. Its fumes are suffocating the world, and its roots drain it

[003-118]of its beautiful strength." And I asked him why he sat on the hills I

[003-119]knew, playing an olden tune.

[003-120]And he answered: "Because the tune is bad for the poppy, which

[003-121]would otherwise grow more swiftly; and because if the brotherhood

[003-122]of which I am one were to cease to pipe on the hills men would stray

[003-123]over the world and be lost or come to terrible ends. We think we have

[003-124]saved Agamemnon."

[003-125]Then he fell to piping again that olden tune, while the wind among the

[003-126]poppy's sleepy petals murmured "Remember not. Remember not."

[003-127]ROSES

[003-128]I know a roadside where the wild rose blooms with a strange

[003-129]abundance. There is a beauty in the blossoms too of an almost

[003-130]exotic kind, a taint of deeper pink that shocks the Puritan flowers.

[003-131]Two hundred generations ago (generations, I mean, of roses) this

[003-132]was a village street; there was a floral decadence when they left their

[003-133]simple life and the roses came from the wilderness to clamber round

[003-134]houses of men.

[003-135]Of all the memories of that little village, of all the cottages that stood

[003-136]there, of all the men and women whose homes they were, nothing

[003-137]remains but a more beautiful blush on the faces of the roses.

[003-138]I hope that when London is clean passed away and the defeated fields

[003-139]come back again, like an exiled people returning after a war, they may

[003-140]find some beautiful thing to remind them of it all; because we have loved

[003-141]a little that swart old city.

[003-142]THE MAN WITH THE GOLDEN EAR-RINGS

[003-143]It may be that I dreamed this. So much at least is certain--that I

[003-144]turned one day from the traffic of a city, and came to its docks and

[003-145]saw its slimy wharves going down green and steep into the water, and

[003-146]saw the huge grey river slipping by and the lost things that went with it

[003-147]turning over and over, and I thought of the nations and unpitying Time,

[003-148]and saw and marvelled at the queenly ships come newly from the sea.

[003-149]It was then, if I mistake not, that I saw leaning against a wall, with his

[003-150]face to the ships, a man with golden ear-rings. His skin had the dark

[003-151]tint of the southern men: the deep black hairs of his moustache were

[003-152]whitened a little with salt; he wore a dark blue jacket such as sailors

[003-153]wear, and the long boots of seafarers, but the look in his eyes was

[003-154]further afield than the ships, he seemed to be beholding the farthest

[003-155]things.

[003-156]Even when I spoke to him he did not call home that look, but

[003-157]answered me dreamily with that same fixed stare as though his

[003-158]thoughts were heaving on far and lonely seas. I asked him what ship

[003-159]he had come by, for there were many there. The sailing ships were

[003-160]there with their sails all furled and their masts straight and still like a

[003-161]wintry forest; the steamers were there, and great liners, puffing up idle

[003-162]smoke into the twilight. He answered he had come by none of them.

[003-163]I asked him what line he worked on, for he was clearly a sailor; I

[003-164]mentioned well-known lines, but he did not know them. Then I asked

[003-165]him where he worked and what he was. And he said: "I work in the

[003-166]Sargasso Sea, and I am the last of the pirates, the last left alive." And

[003-167]I shook him by the hand I do not know how many times. I said: "We

[003-168]feared you were dead. We feared you were dead." And he answered

[003-169]sadly: "No. No. I have sinned too deeply on the Spanish seas: I am

[003-170]not allowed to die."

[003-171]THE DREAM OF KING KARNA-VOOTRA

[003-172]King Karna-Vootra sitting on his throne commanding all things said:

[003-173]"I very clearly saw last night the queenly Vava-Nyria. Though partly

[003-174]she was hidden by great clouds that swept continually by her, rolling

[003-175]over and over, yet her face was unhidden and shone, being full of

[003-176]moonlight.

[003-177]"I said to her:

[003-178]"'Walk with me by the great pools in many-gardened, beautiful

[003-179]Istrakhan where the lilies float that give delectable dreams; or,

[003-180]drawing aside the curtain of hanging orchids, pass with me thence

[003-181]from the pools by a secret path through the else impassable jungle

[003-182]that fills the only way between the mountains that shut in Istrakhan.

[003-183]They shut it in and look on it with joy at morning and at evening when

[003-184]the pools are strange with light, till in their gladness sometimes there

[003-185]melts the deadly snow that kills upon lonely heights the mountaineer.

[003-186]They have valleys among them older than the wrinkles in the moon.
