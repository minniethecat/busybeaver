## Overview

"The Dream of King Karna-Vootra" is a brief tale from Section 3 of *Fifty-One Tales* (1915) by Lord Dunsany. King Karna-Vootra, enthroned and commanding all things, recounts a dream in which he saw the queenly Vava-Nyria — her face radiant with moonlight among sweeping clouds — and offered to walk with her through the garden-pools and secret jungle paths of beautiful Istrakhan.

## Summary

The tale opens with the King speaking from his throne:

> "I very clearly saw last night the queenly Vava-Nyria. Though partly she was hidden by great clouds that swept continually by her, rolling over and over, yet her face was unhidden and shone, being full of moonlight."
>
> [003-163]

He addresses her directly within the dream, offering to walk together through the fabled city of Istrakhan, where lilies float on great pools and give delectable dreams. He imagines drawing aside a curtain of hanging orchids to pass with her along a secret path through impassable jungle — the only passage between the mountains that shut in Istrakhan:

> "They shut it in and look on it with joy at morning and at evening when the pools are strange with light, till in their gladness sometimes there melts the deadly snow that kills upon lonely heights the mountaineer. They have valleys among them older than the wrinkles in the moon."
>
> [003-174]

## Analysis

This is one of Dunsany's most purely atmospheric tales — it is almost entirely description, without narrative event. Its power lies in the accumulation of exotic names and images: Vava-Nyria, Istrakhan, lilies that give delectable dreams, hanging orchids, impassable jungle, mountains that look upon the city with joy. The language is incantatory, building a dream-city out of sounds and light.

The King himself is barely characterized. He sits "on his throne commanding all things," yet the dream he recounts is not of conquest or power but of aesthetic pleasure — walking with a beautiful figure through a beautiful place. This inversion of what a king might be expected to dream of is typical of Dunsany's gentle irony: the ruler of all things dreams not of dominion but of companionship and gardens.

The final image — the mountains' valleys "older than the wrinkles in the moon" — compresses geological and lunar time into a single simile. It is a characteristically Dunsanian temporal gesture, making the human-scale action of walking between pools seem fragile against the immense antiquity of the landscape.
