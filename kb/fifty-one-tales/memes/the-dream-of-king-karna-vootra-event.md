## Overview

The Dream of King Karna-Vootra is the prophetic dream at the center of the tale of the same name from Section 1 of *Fifty-One Tales* (1915). King Karna-Vootra dreams of an approaching enemy host — the forces of "How the Enemy Came to Thlunrana" — and must decide whether to act on a vision that may or may not be true. The event encapsulates Dunsany's recurring concern with the unreliability of vision and the weight of decision.

## Narrative

King Karna-Vootra, a ruler of a peaceful kingdom, experiences a dream in which he sees an enemy army advancing toward his city. The dream is vivid and terrifying: he sees the banners, the spears, the dust rising from the marching columns. He wakes convinced that the vision is true.

The king faces a crisis of epistemology and statecraft. He can mobilize his army and prepare for a siege — but if the dream is merely a dream, he will have spent his treasury and alarmed his people for nothing. He can dismiss the dream — but if it is prophecy, his city will fall undefended.

The tale traces the king's struggle with the ambiguous status of dream-knowledge. The dream is the only evidence he has, and dreams are not evidence. Yet the dream's intensity — its felt truth — will not let him dismiss it. The event is less the dream itself than the decision it forces: what does a king do when the only warning he has is a vision in the night?

## Significance

King Karna-Vootra's dream is one of several prophetic or visionary events in *Fifty-One Tales* that blur the line between dream and reality. Like the narrator of "It may be that I dreamed this," the king must act on knowledge whose ontological status is uncertain. The tale is Dunsany's study of the political consequences of dream-knowledge: what begins as a private vision in a king's bedroom ends as a military decision that determines the fate of a city.
