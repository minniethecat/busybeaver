## Overview

"The Hen" is one of Dunsany's most overtly humorous tales in *Fifty-One Tales* (1915). Set in a farmyard where swallows gather each autumn before their great migration, the story follows a hen who — having heard the swallows' talk of the South — decides to undertake the journey herself. Her "expedition" reaches only as far as a garden down the road, but her report to the poultry-yard is received as a masterpiece of travel literature. The tale is a gentle satire on armchair travellers, the gap between aspiration and achievement, and the human capacity to mistake the familiar for the exotic.

## Summary

The tale opens with swallows on the farmyard gables, restless with the coming of autumn and thoughts of their southern migration. When they depart, the poultry-yard buzzes with discussion of their journey. One hen, caught up in the excitement, announces:

> "I think I shall go South myself next year."
>
> [001-170]

A year passes. The swallows return and depart again, and Dunsany lavishes his most lyrical prose on their actual journey — the "huge and homeless sea," the "glittering fog-banks," the "old islands lifting their heads," the divers seeking pearls, the lands at war — before they reach the mountains they sought and descend into an austral valley where "Summer sometimes sleeping and sometimes singing song" [001-174].

Then the hen makes her move. Testing the wind, she spreads her wings, runs fluttering out of the poultry-yard, down the road, and into a garden. At evening she returns, panting:

> "How extremely interesting," the poultry said, "and what a really beautiful description!"
>
> [001-172]

Winter passes, spring returns, the swallows come back from across the sea — but the poultry-yard refuses to believe there is a sea in the South: "You should hear our hen," they say [001-172].

## Analysis

The tale's comic structure depends on the gulf between Dunsany's two registers: the soaring, lyrical prose he lavishes on the swallows' actual migration, and the hen's comically limited "expedition" to a garden at the end of the road. The swallows navigate by "grey sea-currents" and see "the slow quests of the wandering ships"; the hen finds a garden with roses and a gardener "with his braces on." Both are, in their own terms, travellers — but only one has truly crossed an ocean.

The satire operates on multiple levels. Most obviously, it mocks the armchair traveller who mistakes a day trip for an expedition. But it also targets the audience — the poultry-yard — who prefer a comfortable, familiar account ("beautiful roses!") to the unsettling reality of an actual sea. The final line is devastating: the poultry, having heard the hen's tale, refuse to believe the swallows' report of the ocean. The provincial has consumed the universal.

Dunsany may also be making a quiet point about literary culture: the hen's travelogue, for all its absurdity, is a story well-told — and a well-told story, in the right audience, can displace truth itself.

## Characters

- **The hen** — the protagonist, an ambitious but limited creature who mistakes aspiration for achievement. She genuinely believes she has travelled South, because the garden at the end of the road is, for her, as far as imagination can reach.
- **The swallows** — the genuine travellers, whose annual migration across the sea is described in Dunsany's most vivid and lyrical prose. They represent true adventure and earned knowledge.
- **The poultry-yard** — the audience, who prefer comfortable fiction to unsettling fact. They function as a Greek chorus and a satirical mirror of human provincialism.
