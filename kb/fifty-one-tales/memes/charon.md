## Overview

"Charon" is the second tale in *Fifty-One Tales* (1915) by [[lord-dunsany|Lord Dunsany]]. Drawing on Greek mythology, it depicts Charon — the ferryman who carries souls across the river Styx — as an eternally weary figure whose labor stretches across all of time. The tale blends classical myth with Dunsany's characteristic melancholy, using the figure of Charon to reflect on endless toil, cosmic indifference, and the final exhaustion of humanity.

## Summary

The tale opens with Charon leaning forward to row, his weariness encompassing all things:

> Charon leaned forward and rowed. All things were one with his weariness.
>
> It was not with him a matter of years or of centuries, but of wide floods of time, and an old heaviness and a pain in the arms that had become for him part of the scheme that the gods had made and was of a piece with Eternity.
>
> [001-078]

Dunsany establishes Charon's burden as fundamentally cosmic in scale — his pain has become "part of the scheme that the gods had made." Charon does not merely endure; his suffering is woven into the fabric of existence. Even a contrary wind, the narrator muses, would have provided a landmark in the featureless expanse of his labor [001-078].

The tale notes that the dead are arriving in unprecedented numbers. "They were coming in thousands where they used to come in fifties" [001-091]. Charon does not question why — it is neither his duty nor his nature to ponder such things:

> It was neither Charon's duty nor his wont to ponder in his grey soul why these things might be. Charon leaned forward and rowed.
>
> [001-091]

The refrain — "Charon leaned forward and rowed" — recurs throughout the tale like the stroke of oars, reinforcing the mechanical eternity of his task.

Then a pause comes. The river falls silent. "It was not usual for the gods to send no one down from Earth for such a space. But the gods knew best" [001-092]. Finally, a single passenger arrives — a "little, silent, shivering ghost" who sits alone on the bench:

> And great and weary Charon rowed on and on beside the little, silent, shivering ghost.
>
> [001-097]

The river itself seems to embody grief:

> And the sound of the river was like a mighty sigh that Grief in the beginning had sighed among her sisters, and that could not die like the echoes of human sorrow failing on earthly hills, but was as old as time and the pain in Charon's arms.
>
> [001-102]

When they reach the shore, the little shade speaks: "I am the last." This single line transforms the tale. The unprecedented crowds were not a phenomenon but a trend reaching its conclusion. Humanity has ended. The last soul has crossed. And Charon — who has never smiled, never wept — does both:

> No one had ever made Charon smile before, no one before had ever made him weep.
>
> [001-108]

## Analysis

At roughly 350 words, "Charon" exemplifies Dunsany's gift for the mythopoeic miniature. The tale takes a figure from Greek mythology and expands him beyond his classical context into a cosmic symbol. Charon becomes not merely the ferryman of the dead but a representation of all endless, unacknowledged labor — and, in the tale's devastating final image, of a being who discovers emotion only at the moment his purpose ends.

The story's power lies in its temporal scale: Dunsany compresses aeons into sentences, using the accumulation of ages to convey the weight of Charon's burden. The repeated refrain — "Charon leaned forward and rowed" — creates a hypnotic rhythm that mirrors the eternal repetition of the ferryman's task, while the sudden rupture of that rhythm in the final paragraph delivers the tale's emotional climax.

### Themes

The tale engages several themes that recur throughout [[lord-dunsany|Dunsany]]'s collection. The idea of **eternal, meaningless labor** echoes the cosmic weariness found in tales like [[time-and-the-tradesman|Time and the Tradesman]]. The **indifference of the gods** — "the gods knew best" — is a refrain that appears throughout the collection, always with a note of bitter irony. And the **end of humanity**, presented here with quiet pathos rather than apocalyptic grandeur, connects to the eschatological concerns of tales like [[the-latest-thing|The Latest Thing]].

The image of Charon both smiling and weeping at the news that humanity is gone is among the most complex emotional moments in *Fifty-One Tales*. Charon's labor has been endless and thankless — yet its end means the extinction of the species he served. His smile is relief; his tears are grief. In a single sentence, Dunsany captures the ambivalence of purpose: to be freed from eternal toil is also to lose one's place in the cosmic order.

## Characters

- **Charon**: The ferryman of the dead from Greek mythology, reimagined as a cosmic figure of eternal, wearying labor. He rows through all of time, carrying the souls of the dead without end — until the last soul comes.
- **The Last Man**: A "little, silent, shivering ghost" [001-097] who speaks only four words: "I am the last." He is the final passenger, and his arrival marks both the completion and the end of Charon's purpose.
