## Overview

The Lonely Idol is a small, forgotten deity at the center of Dunsany's tale "The Lonely Idol" from Section 6 of *Fifty-One Tales* (1915). Carved from "an old outlandish stone" with a swine-like face, the idol sits cross-legged, holding a broken scourge, receiving no prayers and no sacrifices. The narrator, moved to pity, becomes the idol's sole worshipper — praying not for gain but out of compassion for a god abandoned by time.

## Role in the Tales

The idol appears only in "The Lonely Idol," where it is introduced as a gift from a friend:

> I had from a friend an old outlandish stone, a little swine-faced idol to whom no one prayed.
>
> [006-089]

Its former divinity is described in a single, devastating parenthetical:

> (and no one heeded the scourge and no one prayed and no one came with squealing sacrifice; and he had been a god)
>
> [006-090]

Moved by its "melancholy case," the narrator humbles himself and prays — not in the old, sacrificial way but with a lament for the passing of all beautiful things. The prayer is addressed to the idol by its physical attributes:

> "O idol, idol of the hard pale stone, invincible to the years, O scourge-holder, give ear for behold I pray."
>
> [006-089]

The prayer itself is a Dunsanian elegy for transience: leaves falling, beauty fading, children growing old, the "whimper of the years running behind [Time] hound-like." The narrator asks the idol to "be not any more the friend of Time" and to "let lovely things live on for the sake of our tears."

The tale ends with the narrator's self-description — praying "out of compassion one windy day to the snout-faced idol to whom no one kneeled."

## Character Analysis

The Lonely Idol embodies one of Dunsany's most distinctive imaginative moves: the inversion of worship. Rather than a supplicant approaching a god for favors, the narrator gives worship where none is asked — a purely gratuitous act of compassion directed at a being who cannot reciprocate. The idol's ugliness (its "swine-face") and its broken scourge mark it as a failed god, stripped of power and dignity. Yet this very failure is what draws the narrator's pity.

The tale's prayer is not petition but lament — a meditation on mutability addressed to a piece of stone precisely because stone is "invincible to the years." The prayer asks the idol to oppose Time itself, to preserve beautiful things from decay. This is an impossible request, and the idol's silence is the tale's unspoken answer. But the value of the prayer lies not in its efficacy but in the act of praying — the narrator's compassion is its own justification.

The idol thus functions as a mirror: the narrator sees in its abandonment a reflection of all transient beauty, and his prayer becomes a way of honoring what must pass. In this sense, the Lonely Idol is the collection's most concentrated image of Dunsany's elegiac temperament.
