## Overview

Picadilly (modern spelling: Piccadilly) is one of [[london|London]]'s most famous thoroughfares, running from Piccadilly Circus to Hyde Park Corner. In the Fifty-One Tales, Dunsany uses it as the setting for "[[taking-up-picadilly|Taking Up Picadilly]]," a brief, surreal story in which the familiar city street is dismantled to reveal a cosmic darkness beneath.

## In "Taking Up Picadilly"

The tale opens with the narrator walking down Picadilly one afternoon:

> Going down Picadilly one day and nearing Grosvenor Place I saw, if my memory is not at fault, some workmen with their coats off — or so they seemed. They had pickaxes in their hands and wore corduroy trousers and that little leather band below the knee that goes by the astonishing name of "York-to-London." [006-010]

When the narrator asks what they are doing, a workman replies:

> "We are taking up Picadilly," he said to me. [006-017]

The narrator is puzzled by the season (June) and guesses it must be a joke or a bet. But when he looks into the excavation, he sees not pipes or foundations but something far stranger:

> And then I looked at the bit that they had already picked, and though it was broad daylight over my head it was darkness down there, all full of the southern stars. [006-025]

The workman explains simply:

> "It was noisy and bad and we grew aweary of it," said he that wore corduroy trousers. "We are not what we appear." [006-027]

The tale concludes with the definitive statement:

> They were taking up Picadilly altogether. [006-029]

## Thematic Significance

The tale is one of Dunsany's most concentrated exercises in the fantastic. Picadilly — the epitome of Edwardian [[london|London]]'s commercial and social life — is revealed to be a mere surface laid over cosmic depths. The workmen are not workmen; the street is not merely a street. The southern stars beneath the pavement suggest that the mundane world is a thin veneer, and that beyond it lies an entirely different order of reality.

The tale also echoes a theme found elsewhere in the collection: that the works of civilization are provisional. Just as [[the-field|the fields]] will one day reclaim [[london|London]] in "[[roses|Roses]]," here Picadilly is actively being dismantled — not by catastrophe but by deliberate, methodical labor, as though its time has simply run out.
