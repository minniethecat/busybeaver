## Overview

"Compromise" is one of the shortest and most cryptic tales in *Fifty-One Tales*. A city is built atop an earthquake's lair. The earthquake stirs; a compromise is reached. The city stands, but the terms of the compromise are never stated — only its cost is hinted at. The tale is a parable about the bargains civilizations make with the forces that will eventually destroy them, and about the price of postponing what cannot ultimately be avoided.

## Summary

In the "shining youth of the world," a city of marble and gold is built above the lair of the earthquake. Its inhabitants feast, fight, call their city immortal, and sing songs to the gods [008-006].

None heeds the stirring beneath them. When the earthquake finally wakes, a compromise is reached — the city is spared, but something essential is given in exchange. The tale leaves the precise nature of the bargain deliberately obscure, but the implication is clear: the city survives at the cost of its soul [008-006].

## Analysis

The tale's power lies in what it withholds. We are never told the terms of the compromise — only that one was made, and that the city continued. This silence forces the reader to supply the cost: freedom, truth, courage, the willingness to face destruction honestly. The compromise, whatever it was, allows the city to stand but ensures it will never be what it was. The earthquake is not defeated; it is bribed.

## Themes

- **Compromise and corruption** — the tale suggests that some bargains are more destructive than the destruction they avert.
- **Civilization and its costs** — the city's survival is purchased with something unnameable but essential.
- **Hubris** — building above an earthquake's lair is an act of denial that must eventually be paid for.
