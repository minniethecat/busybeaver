## Overview

The Poet appears as a recurring character across several of Lord Dunsany's *Fifty-One Tales*. Unlike named mythological figures, the Poet is an archetypal artist figure — unnamed, universal, and defined entirely by his relationship to his craft and to the world's indifference. He is most fully developed in "The Assignation," where his pursuit of Fame forms the tale's central allegory.

## Role in the Tales

In "The Assignation," the Poet crafts his "little chaplets of song" for Fame, who ignores him in favor of the "worthless garlands" of boisterous citizens. His bitterness at this rejection drives the tale's conflict:

> Fame singing in the highways, and trifling as she sang, with sordid adventurers, passed the poet by.
> [001-060]

> And still the poet made for her little chaplets of song, to deck her forehead in the courts of Time.
> [001-061]

The Poet's persistence in creating beauty despite rejection is his defining trait. He continues making his chaplets even as they die at evening, and when he finally rebukes Fame, he does so from a place of genuine devotion rather than mere wounded pride:

> And one day in his bitterness the poet rebuked her, and said to her: "Lovely Fame, even in the highways and the byways you have not foreborne to laugh and shout and jest with worthless men, and I have toiled for you and dreamed of you and you mock me and pass me by."
> [001-068]

In later tales, the Poet appears in more contemplative roles. In "The Return of Song," he walks beside a river and observes the passing of time. In "The Song of the Blackbird," he converses with a blackbird about the nature of artistic creation, asking the bird how it produces its song.

## Character Analysis

The Poet embodies the Dunsanian theme of the artist's relationship to posterity. Unlike Fame — who is personified as a fickle, laughing figure — the Poet is earnest, laboring in quiet devotion. The chaplets of song he weaves are fragile things, described as dying at evening, yet they represent the only lasting counterweight to Oblivion that Dunsany's universe allows.

The Poet's anonymous, archetypal nature is significant: Dunsany does not name him, making him a stand-in for all artists who create for an audience that may never come. This aligns with the collection's broader meditation on transience and the smallness of human effort against cosmic time.
