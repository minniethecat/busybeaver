## Overview

The Tortoise is an animal character in Lord Dunsany's tale "The True History of the Hare and the Tortoise," published in *Fifty-One Tales*. Unlike the traditional Aesopian fable where the tortoise wins through steady perseverance, Dunsany's Tortoise wins through an entirely different strategy: his supporters engage [[the-hare|The Hare]] in existential debate about the meaning of racing, ultimately convincing the faster creature that the contest is pointless.

## Role in the Tales

The tale opens with a debate: some say the Hare is swifter, while others argue the Tortoise is faster because "anyone could see he was much the slower of the two." It is decided that the two shall race five hundred yards. Unlike the contemptuous Hare, who calls the affair "ridiculous nonsense," the Tortoise welcomes the contest with calm confidence, saying it is "most welcome" and that he shall not disappoint his supporters.

As the race begins, the Hare dashes ahead while the Tortoise's supporters loudly cheer his "inspiriting words." But rather than racing, the Tortoise's camp engages in philosophical argument, debating the purpose and meaning of the race itself until the Hare, worn down by existential doubt, simply stops running.

> "Confident of success," said the Tortoise. But the supporters of the Hare said nothing, while the crowd loudly cheered the Tortoise's inspiriting words.
>
> — 004-092

The tale functions as a satire on public debate and sophistry, where victory is achieved not through speed or endurance but through the power of persuasion to make one's opponent question the value of the contest itself.
