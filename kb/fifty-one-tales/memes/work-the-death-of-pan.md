## Overview

"The Death of Pan" is a brief, sharp-edged tale from Section 1 of *Fifty-One Tales* (1915). In fewer than two hundred words, Dunsany stages a confrontation between modern travellers and the ancient god Pan — a creature of myth whom they assume to be dead. Their lament is sincere, but it is also presumptuous; Pan is not dead at all, merely still. The tale turns on a moment of irreverent laughter: Arcadian maidens find the prone god "silly," and their laughter — not solemn worship — is what revives him. The traveller's elegy is exposed as sentimental self-importance, and the old gods prove far more resilient than modernity imagines.

## Summary

Travellers from London enter Arcady and, encountering the prone body of Pan, lament his death to one another [001-009]. Horned Pan lies still, the dew on his fur, having "not the look of a live animal." They confirm, to one another, "It is true that Pan is dead."

Evening comes, a star appears, and Arcadian maidens arrive from a nearby valley, singing idly. When they see the recumbent god in the twilight, they stop and whisper. Then comes the verdict:

> "How silly he looks," they said, and thereat they laughed a little.
>
> [001-009]

The effect is immediate. Pan leaps up, "the gravel flew from his hooves," and for as long as the travellers listen, the crags and hilltops of Arcady ring with the sounds of pursuit [001-009].

## Analysis

The tale's structure is a miniature joke with a theological punchline. The setup — solemn travellers, a dead god, an elegy — is undone in a single line of dialogue. The maidens' laughter is the catalyst: not prayer, not ritual, not even recognition of the god, but simple irreverent amusement. Pan is not offended by being laughed at; he is *awakened* by it.

Dunsany's choice of Arcadian maidens as the agents of revival is deliberate. They are not travellers, not visitors — they belong to the landscape in a way the Londoners never can. The travellers come to Arcady to mourn; the maidens live there and laugh. Pan responds to his own, not to tourists.

The tale also functions as a quiet rebuttal to the Victorian "death of the gods" narrative. The travellers *want* Pan to be dead — it confirms their worldview, their sophistication, their elegiac sensibility. But Pan is not dead, and never was. He was simply waiting for something worth waking for.

The final image — pursuit echoing through the hills — is left deliberately unresolved. Whom is Pan pursuing? The travellers? The maidens? The reader? Dunsany does not say. The open ending is itself a kind of theological statement: the old gods are still here, still active, and still unpredictable.

## Characters

- **Pan** — the horned god of Arcady, shepherds, and wild places. Stiff and still at the tale's opening, he is revived by the laughter of Arcadian maidens. Not dead, merely dormant — and quick to pursue.
- **The travellers from London** — unnamed, self-important visitors who come to Arcady expecting to find a dead god and find instead a living one. They are the tale's objects of gentle satire.
- **The Arcadian maidens** — local girls who laugh at Pan's prone form and, in doing so, restore him to life. They are instinctive, irreverent, and closer to the old gods than any traveller.
