## Overview

The Himalaya — the great mountain range of Asia — appears in a single passage in "[[the-unpasturable-fields|The Unpasturable Fields]]," paired with the [[caucasus|Caucasus]] as the twin exemplars of terrestrial mountain grandeur. The clouds, gathering to answer the mountains' boast of permanence, assume forms "in the likeness of [[caucasus|Caucasus]] upon Himalaya."

## In "The Unpasturable Fields"

> And softly the clouds foregathered from far places, and crag on crag and mountain upon mountain in the likeness of Caucasus upon Himalaya came riding past the sunlight upon the backs of storms and looked down idly from their golden heights upon the crests of the mountains. [003-015]–[003-017]

The phrase "[[caucasus|Caucasus]] upon Himalaya" stacks one great range atop another — an impossible geography that makes the clouds' formation more magnificent than any single earthly range could be. The clouds "look down idly from their golden heights" upon the mountains below, who had just declared themselves "the most imperishable mountains" [003-013].

## Thematic Significance

The Himalaya here represents the farthest reach of the geographical imagination — a mountain range whose name alone, in 1915, conveyed scale beyond European experience. Coupled with the [[caucasus|Caucasus]] (the classical boundary of the known world), the Himalaya extends the clouds' display across the full span of terrestrial grandeur.

For Dunsany, whose imaginative geography often drew on the ancient Near East and classical Mediterranean, the inclusion of the Himalaya is unusual — it gestures toward an Orient that is not merely decorative but sublime, a place of genuinely transcendent scale that the clouds must match to win their argument with the mountains.
