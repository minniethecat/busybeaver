## Overview

The Golden Town is a central symbol in [[the-messengers|The Messengers]], representing a once-sacred place that has lost its beauty and holiness. The Muses, speaking from [[parnassus|Parnassus]], repeatedly command messengers to carry their word there — yet the city has fallen so far that it stones every messenger who comes [008-045], carving their messages on gold only after they are dead. By the time the narrator is called, the Golden Town is the Golden Town no longer.

## The Tale of the Messengers

The tale unfolds in a series of escalating failures. Three messengers are sent before the narrator is called:

The first messenger carries a wreath of laurels "carved out of emeralds as only the Muses can carve" [008-045]. He runs through the gateway in scarlet silks and cries his message — but the wise men, cross-legged before their houses reading an older message from the Muses, declare him false and stone him to death. Afterwards, they carve his message upon gold and read it in their temples on holy days [008-056].

The second messenger carries a wand of ivory carved with "all the beautiful stories of the world wondrously carved thereon" [008-072]. He too is stoned, and his message likewise preserved on gold. The refrain sounds for the first time:

> "When will the Muses rest? When are they weary?"
>
> [008-078]

The third messenger wears a garland of gold kingcups fashioned by the Muses. He too is stoned [008-088]. The pattern is fixed: each messenger is authenticated by a divine token, each is rejected because the token differs from the last, and each message is preserved only after its bearer is dead.

## The Narrator's Refusal

Then the Muses call the narrator — not once but repeatedly, "morning and night... and through long evenings" [008-098]. Unlike the earlier messengers, the narrator knows the truth about the Golden Town:

> The Golden Town is the Golden Town no longer. They have sold their pillars for brass and their temples for money, they have made coins out of their golden doors. It is become a dark town full of trouble, there is no ease in its streets, beauty has left it and the old songs are gone.
>
> [008-105]

This is the tale's central passage — the elegy for a fallen city. The transformation from gold to brass, from temples to money, from beauty to darkness, signals a spiritual as well as material decline. The "old songs" are gone, and with them the wonder and reverence that made the city holy.

The Muses do not understand. "Go take our message," they cry. The narrator pleads: "You do not understand. You have no message for the Golden Town, the holy city no longer" [008-109]. But the Muses cannot comprehend that their holy city has ceased to exist. They exist outside [[time|Time]]; the narrator exists within it.

When the narrator hears the message itself, he makes excuses, "dreading to speak such things in the Golden Town" [008-107]. Finally he refuses outright:

> "I will not go. None will believe me."
>
> [008-110]

The tale ends not with resolution but with the Muses' cry continuing through the night:

> "And still the Muses cry to me all night long. They do not understand. How should they know?"
>
> [008-111]

## The Three Messengers and the Pattern of Rejection

The three messengers who precede the narrator form a deliberate pattern. Each comes with a different token — emerald laurels, ivory wand, golden kingcups — and each is rejected precisely *because* their token differs from the last. The wise men of the Golden Town do not evaluate each messenger on his own terms; they compare him against a precedent that has already been carved on gold. The living messenger is always false; the carved message of the dead messenger is always true. This is the tragedy of institutions: they preserve the message but kill the messenger, then enshrine the killing as wisdom.

> One wandering nigh Parnassus chasing hares heard the high Muses. "Take us a message to the Golden Town." Thus sang the Muses.
>
> [008-043]

The Golden Town's rejection of its messengers mirrors the broader theme of [[fame-concept|Fame]] and its hollow rewards explored elsewhere in the collection. Like Fame, the Golden Town confers recognition only upon the dead — the living are stoned, the dead are carved in gold.

## Significance

The Golden Town serves as a symbol of lost beauty and the corruption of the sacred by the profane. It resonates with other golden cities in the collection — [[sooma|Sooma]], the "golden city" where kings sit "in their lofty alabaster house" in [[the-dream-of-king-karna-vootra|The Dream of King Karna-Vootra]] — but where Sooma remains a place of wonder and distant rumor, the Golden Town has fallen.

The Muses, representing divine inspiration, cannot comprehend that their holy city no longer exists — they continue to demand that their message be delivered to a place that has ceased to be worthy of it. The narrator's repeated refusal captures the tragic distance between the Muses' eternal perspective and the narrator's knowledge of the world's decay.

This tension echoes a broader theme in [[lord-dunsany|Lord Dunsany]]'s *Fifty-One Tales*: the conflict between the timeless world of myth and beauty and the fallen, materialistic present. The Golden Town is what happens when that conflict is resolved in favor of the present — when gold is melted into coins, when temples are sold, when the old songs are gone.

The "dark town full of trouble" [008-105] also echoes the desolation described in [[the-songless-country|The Songless Country]], where [[song|Song]] has departed and silence prevails. Both places suffer from the same absence — [[beauty|Beauty]] has left them, and what remains is mere existence without wonder.
