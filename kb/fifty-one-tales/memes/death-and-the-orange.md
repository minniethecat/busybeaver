## Overview

"Death and the Orange" is the shortest tale in Section 1 of *Fifty-One Tales* (1915), and one of the most compressed narratives Dunsany ever wrote. In roughly one hundred and thirty words, it sketches a triangle — two men, one woman, one orange — and lets the ancient machinery of jealousy and violence do its work. Death is not the agent of the story but its audience: dining at another table, he rises to listen when the quarrel begins. The tale is a miniature of fate, in which the most trivial object becomes the pivot on which lives turn.

## Summary

Two dark young men sit at a restaurant table with one woman in a foreign southern land [001-017]. On the woman's plate rests a small orange "which had an evil laughter in its heart." Both men watch the woman, eating little, drinking much; she smiles equally at each.

The orange rolls off the plate to the floor. Both men dive under the table to retrieve it, meet there suddenly, and begin speaking swift, angry words. The woman sits helpless — "a horror and an impotence came over the Reason of each" — while the orange's heart laughs. And Death, who had been dining tête-à-tête with an old man at another table, "rose and came over to listen to the quarrel" [001-017].

## Analysis

The tale's brevity is its argument. Dunsany gives us only the essentials: two men, one woman, an orange, and Death. There is no backstory, no psychology, no explanation. The orange's "evil laughter" is not explained — it simply is, a malevolent presence in the heart of a piece of fruit.

The setting — a southern land, a restaurant, evening — is generic and archetypal. Dunsany is not telling a specific story but the story: jealousy, violence, and Death arriving to claim the result. The fact that Death is already dining in the same restaurant is the tale's darkest joke. He was always there, at the next table, waiting for something to happen.

The woman's smile — "equally at each" — is the catalyst, but the tale does not blame her. She sits "helpless" as the quarrel erupts beneath the table. The orange, not the woman, is the active agent: it rolls, it laughs, it triggers the fatal meeting. Dunsany externalizes jealousy into an object, giving it a life and will of its own.

The tale's perspective is remarkable: "a horror and an impotence came over the Reason of each." Reason is personified as a helpless spectator — a woman "at the back of the mind" — unable to stop what passion and chance have set in motion.

## Characters

- **The two dark young men** — unnamed, interchangeable in their desire and their violence. They are types, not individuals, representing the universality of jealous rage.
- **The woman** — equally unnamed, smiling equally at both men. She is the object of desire but also a bystander to the violence she unwittingly provokes.
- **The orange** — the tale's most vivid character: it has "an evil laughter in its heart," rolls deliberately off the plate, and laughs as the men quarrel. A malevolent object that sets fate in motion.
- **Death** — dining at another table with an old man. He is a spectator, not an agent — he rises only to listen, not to intervene. The quarrel will provide his work; he need only wait.
