## Overview

The Death of Pan is the pivotal mythic event of *Fifty-One Tales*, occurring
across the paired tales [[the-death-of-pan|"The Death of Pan"]] and [[the-tomb-of-pan|"The Tomb of Pan"]], with a
closing echo in the collection's final line. [[london|London]] travellers enter [[arcady|Arcady]]
and declare [[pan|Pan]] dead; the Arcadian maidens laugh and Pan revives; humanity
builds a tomb anyway; and Pan, at the last, laughs at his own monument. The
sequence forms a cycle of death, resurrection, memorialization, and ironic
afterlife that encapsulates Dunsany's treatment of myth in the modern age.

## The Event Sequence

### Phase 1: Discovery and Declaration

Travellers from London enter Arcady and find Pan lying still:

> Horned Pan was still and the dew was on his fur; he had not the look of
> a live animal. And then they said, "It is true that Pan is dead." (001-114)

The declaration is delivered with the certainty of modern empiricism — the
London travellers see stillness and conclude death. They do not consider
that a god might simply be resting.

### Phase 2: Resurrection

Arcadian maidens arrive, laugh at the travellers' solemnity, and their
[[laughter]] — the natural sound of a place that still believes — wakes the god:

> And at the sound of their laughter Pan leaped up and the gravel flew from
> his hooves as he galloped down the slope. (001-124)

The travellers flee. The death was never real; it was a failure of
perception.

### Phase 3: Monumentalization

Despite Pan's evident survival, humanity proceeds to memorialize him
as though dead:

> "Seeing," they said, "that old-time Pan is dead, let us now make a tomb
> for him and a monument." (008-174)

A great marble tomb rises on a sea-cliff, carved with Pan's humbled image.
The builders complete their work, mourners mourn, revilers revile — all
directed at a god who is not actually dead. The tomb itself, like the
monuments of [[thebes]] and [[babylon]], is a human attempt to fix meaning
against the flow of time — and like them, it is rendered absurd by what
it claims to commemorate.

### Phase 4: The Last Laugh

The collection's final sentence closes the cycle:

> softly along the hills, Pan saw the tomb and laughed. (009-002)

Pan's laughter is the event's true conclusion. It is neither angry nor
bitter — it is the amusement of something immortal at humanity's need to
declare it dead and build monuments to its passing.

## Significance

The Death of Pan event is not a narrative in the conventional sense — it
has no plot, no character development, no resolution beyond Pan's laughter.
It is, rather, an emblem: a demonstration that myth does not depend on
belief to survive, and that the modern impulse to declare the old gods
dead says more about modernity than about the gods.

This event resonates across the collection. [[charon|Charon]], another figure from
Section 1, navigates a similar boundary between life, death, and the
mythic. [[nature-and-time|Nature and Time]] explores the same cosmic forces that Pan
embodies — vast, indifferent, and untouched by human declarations.
The [[death]] that humans imagine for Pan is not the death that Dunsany
depicts elsewhere in the collection; it is a peculiarly modern death,
a death of belief rather than being.

## Historical Context

The cry "Great Pan is dead" originates in Plutarch (*De Defectu Oraculorum*),
where a sailor named Thamus hears a divine voice announcing the death of
the great god Pan. Plutarch's account became a touchstone for the
nineteenth-century sense that the old gods had perished with the rise
of Christianity and, later, scientific rationalism. Dunsany's treatment
is characteristic: he accepts the premise (Pan is dead, or so they say)
and then subverts it (Pan is not dead; he was just waiting to laugh).
