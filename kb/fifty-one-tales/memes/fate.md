## Overview

Fate in Lord Dunsany's *Fifty-One Tales* (1915) is not personified as a goddess but imagined as a landscape — the "hills of Fate" from which the river of Time runs toward the sea. This geographic metaphor appears most vividly in "The Latest Thing" [003-078], where a poet's distant gaze encompasses both the source and the destination of all things. Fate in Dunsany's treatment is neither cruel nor kind: it is the topography that gives Time its direction and its floods their inevitability.

## In the Tales

Fate appears most explicitly in **"The Latest Thing"** (Section 3), where a poet walks beside a river, his eyes lifted not to its current contents but to its source:

> "I think he saw the sea, and the hills of Fate from which the river ran."
> [003-078]

The poet's visionary perspective contrasts with that of the "unclean-feeder" — an allegorical figure who stands waist-deep in the same river, watching only for the next piece of filth to drift by [003-069]. When asked about the cities that poison the river, the poet offers the tale's culminating vision:

> "Whenever the centuries melt on the hills of Fate the river terribly floods."
> [003-084]

This is Fate as eschatology: the hills are not merely the river's origin but the mechanism of its periodic cleansing. When enough centuries have "melted" — when enough time has accumulated — the hills send a flood that sweeps away the accumulated waste of civilization. The unclean-feeder, with his back to beauty, cannot see the source that will one day overwhelm him.

> I saw an unclean-feeder by the banks of the river of Time. He crouched by orchards numerous with apples in a happy land of flowers; colossal things of glittering gold were bound by vast chains and fastened to the river's bank, but his back was to all these things. He crouched and watched the river.
>
> [003-069]

The conceptual framework established in "The Latest Thing" — Fate as source, Time as river, the flood as cosmic cleansing — underlies much of Dunsany's other work in the collection, even where Fate is not named explicitly. The city-builders of "The Little City" and the makers of "The Latest Thing" are all downstream of the same hills, subject to the same eventual flood.

## Interpretation

Dunsany's treatment of Fate as geography rather than deity is a distinctive literary move. The hills of Fate are not beings with intentions but features of a cosmic landscape — they have always been there, they will always send their floods, and human beings can either look toward them (like the poet) or turn their backs (like the unclean-feeder).

This depersonalization of Fate aligns with the collection's broader treatment of cosmic forces. [[time|Time]] is a prowler, [[death|Death]] is a ferryman, [[nature|Nature]] is a beggar — each is personified, but each is also treated as an inescapable condition rather than a moral agent. Fate, by remaining a landscape rather than a character, is the most radically impersonal of these forces. It cannot be bargained with, because it has no will to bargain.

The contrast between the poet and the unclean-feeder in [[the-latest-thing|The Latest Thing]] is the tale's ethical core. Both live beside the same river; both are subject to the same floods. But the poet looks upstream, toward the hills of Fate, and sees the pattern. The unclean-feeder looks downstream, into the filthy water, and sees only what floats by. The difference is not in their circumstances but in the direction of their gaze — a metaphor for the choice between wisdom and appetite that runs through the entire collection.

The hills of Fate also connect to the vision of [[immortality|Immortality]] in "Alone the Immortals" [004-169], where the dead years sit as colossi before whom even Time kneels. Both images — the hills and the colossi — are features of a cosmic landscape that dwarfs human scale. The years may be immortal, but they too flow from the hills of Fate.
