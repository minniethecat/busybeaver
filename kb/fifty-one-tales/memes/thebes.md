## Overview

Thebes appears in two distinct guises in [[lord-dunsany|Lord Dunsany]]'s *Fifty-One Tales*: as the ancient Egyptian city whose seven gates are threatened by [[time|Time]], and in the satirical title "[[the-sphinx-in-thebes-massachusetts|The Sphinx in Thebes (Massachusetts)]]" [001-044]. Dunsany uses Thebes as a symbol of impermanence — even the mightiest cities of antiquity cannot withstand the erosion of ages.

## Description

The ancient Thebes is invoked in a passage of cosmic dread, where gods and immortals scheme to blind and imprison [[time|Time]] before he can destroy more of the world's beauty:

> "Time that has carried away the seven gates of Thebes!"
>
> [001-140]

This exclamation captures the central anxiety: [[time|Time]] is a destroyer who has already claimed one of the ancient world's great cities. The seven gates of Thebes, famous in Greek mythology, represent a pinnacle of human architectural achievement — and their loss to [[time|Time]] is an irreparable diminishment of the world's beauty.

The satirical "Sphinx in Thebes (Massachusetts)" [001-044] transposes the grandeur of ancient Egypt onto a New England town, creating a comic disjunction. [[the-sphinx|The Sphinx]] — that ancient, inscrutable monument — finds itself transported to a place of modernity and mundanity, much as Dunsany's own mythic sensibility was at odds with the industrial age.

## Significance

Thebes functions as a double symbol in Dunsany's tales. As the ancient city, it represents all that is worth preserving — gates, temples, the accumulated beauty of human civilization — and all that [[time|Time]] inevitably destroys. The lament over the seven gates is not merely antiquarian nostalgia; it is an existential cry against entropy itself.

In its Massachusetts incarnation, Thebes becomes a symbol of cultural displacement. The ancient has been reduced to the provincial, the mythic to the mundane. The title "[[the-sphinx-in-thebes-massachusetts|The Sphinx in Thebes (Massachusetts)]]" is itself a joke — a collision of registers that captures Dunsany's ironic relationship with modernity.

The paired references to Thebes and [[thlunrana|Thlunrana]] create a geography of doomed cities in the tales: [[thlunrana|Thlunrana]], the mythical fortress whose fall is prophesied; Thebes, the historical city whose gates have already been carried away. Together they argue that no human construction, however magnificent, can resist the forces of [[time|Time]] and [[nature|Nature]].

## Thebes and the Sphinx

[[the-sphinx-at-giza|The Sphinx at Giza]] and the Sphinx in Thebes are connected in Dunsany's imagination through the figure of [[time|Time]]. In the passage centered on the Sphinx at [[giza|Giza]] [001-140], [[immortality|the immortal]]s scheme to capture [[time|Time]] and render him harmless so that he can no longer destroy the world's beauty:

> "I saw an old man go by, and Time never touched him."
>
> [001-140]

[[the-sphinx|The Sphinx]] is imagined as [[time|Time]]'s lover — a painted face that distracts him from his destructive work. The immortals hope the Sphinx will betray [[time|Time]]'s secret, so that "he shall dance to us and make sport." The passage is simultaneously comic and horrifying: cosmic forces reduced to a domestic drama, with Thebes's seven gates as collateral damage.

> "She had painted her face in order to ogle Time. And he has spared no other painted face in all the world but hers."
>
> [005-076 area]

## Thebes in the Modern World

The satirical twist of "[[the-sphinx-in-thebes-massachusetts|The Sphinx in Thebes (Massachusetts)]]" [001-044] deserves closer attention. Dunsany is mocking not Thebes but the American habit of borrowing ancient names for new settlements — a practice that was particularly visible in New England. The joke is at the expense of cultural pretension: a Sphinx transplanted to Massachusetts is a Sphinx made ridiculous.

But there is also a gentler reading. Thebes, Massachusetts represents the continuity of human civilization — the same names, the same monuments, the same anxieties about [[time|Time]] and decay, persisting across millennia and oceans. [[the-sphinx|The Sphinx]] in Massachusetts is absurd, but it is also a testament to the enduring power of ancient symbols. Even in a New England town, the Sphinx guards its secrets and [[time|Time]] carries away the gates.
