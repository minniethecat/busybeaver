## Overview

The field — and the fields — appear throughout the Fifty-One Tales as a recurring pastoral counterpoint to the city. Unlike named places such as [[sendara]] or [[london]], the field is archetypal rather than specific: it represents the domain of nature, childhood, [[poetry|song]], and the eventual return of the wild after human civilization has passed.

## Appearances

### *The Unpasturable Fields*

In this tale, the mountains and clouds debate permanence. The mountains claim to be imperishable; the clouds reply that "upon our unpasturable fields Pegasus prances" [003-021]. The fields here are celestial — cloud-pastures where the winged horse of poetry browses on [[poetry|song]] brought by larks.

### *The Worm and the Angel*

The worm and the angel look together upon the world and see "the old men heavy in their chairs and heard the children singing in the fields" [003-034]. The fields here are the domain of childhood and innocence — the place where singing still happens, contrasted with the heaviness of old age.

### *The Latest Thing*

In this eschatological vision, the narrator imagines the eventual defeat of time and death:

> Then shall our children laugh at him who slew Babylon's winged bulls. [001-153]

The fields throughout the collection represent what the city has displaced and what will return when the city falls.

## Thematic Significance

The field in Dunsany's tales is not merely agricultural land but a symbol of the pre-modern, the natural, and the eternal. It is where children sing, where Pegasus grazes, and where — as the narrator of "[[roses|Roses]]" hopes — the defeated landscape will one day return to reclaim [[london|London]].

The dialectic between field and city runs through the collection: [[london]] is the city that will pass; the fields are what will come back "like an exiled people returning after a war" [003-137]. In this sense, the field is the ultimate place in the Fifty-One Tales — not a location but a condition, the baseline reality to which the world reverts when human effort exhausts itself.
