[001-058]THE ASSIGNATION

[001-059]Fame singing in the highways, and trifling as she sang, with sordid

[001-060]adventurers, passed the poet by.

[001-061]And still the poet made for her little chaplets of song, to deck her

[001-062]forehead in the courts of Time: and still she wore instead the worthless

[001-063]garlands, that boisterous citizens flung to her in the ways, made out of

[001-064]perishable things.

[001-065]And after a while whenever these garlands died the poet came to her

[001-066]with his chaplets of song; and still she laughed at him and wore the

[001-067]worthless wreaths, though they always died at evening.

[001-068]And one day in his bitterness the poet rebuked her, and said to her:

[001-069]"Lovely Fame, even in the highways and the byways you have not

[001-070]foreborne to laugh and shout and jest with worthless men, and I have

[001-071]toiled for you and dreamed of you and you mock me and pass me by."

[001-072]And Fame turned her back on him and walked away, but in departing

[001-073]she looked over her shoulder and smiled at him as she had not smiled

[001-074]before, and, almost speaking in a whisper, said:

[001-075]"I will meet you in the graveyard at the back of the Workhouse in a

[001-076]hundred years."

[001-077]CHARON

[001-078]Charon leaned forward and rowed. All things were one with his

[001-079]weariness.

[001-080]It was not with him a matter of years or of centuries, but of wide

[001-081]floods of time, and an old heaviness and a pain in the arms that had

[001-082]become for him part of the scheme that the gods had made and was

[001-083]of a piece with Eternity.

[001-084]If the gods had even sent him a contrary wind it would have divided

[001-085]all time in his memory into two equal slabs.

[001-086]So grey were all things always where he was that if any radiance

[001-087]lingered a moment among the dead, on the face of such a queen

[001-088]perhaps as Cleopatra, his eyes could not have perceived it.

[001-089]It was strange that the dead nowadays were coming in such numbers.

[001-090]They were coming in thousands where they used to come in fifties. It

[001-091]was neither Charon's duty nor his wont to ponder in his grey soul why

[001-092]these things might be. Charon leaned forward and rowed.

[001-093]Then no one came for a while. It was not usual for the gods to send

[001-094]no one down from Earth for such a space. But the gods knew best.

[001-095]Then one man came alone. And the little shade sat shivering on a

[001-096]lonely bench and the great boat pushed off. Only one passenger:

[001-097]the gods knew best. And great and weary Charon rowed on and on

[001-098]beside the little, silent, shivering ghost.

[001-099]And the sound of the river was like a mighty sigh that Grief in the

[001-100]beginning had sighed among her sisters, and that could not die like

[001-101]the echoes of human sorrow failing on earthly hills, but was as old

[001-102]as time and the pain in Charon's arms.

[001-103]Then the boat from the slow, grey river loomed up to the coast of

[001-104]Dis and the little, silent shade still shivering stepped ashore, and

[001-105]Charon turned the boat to go wearily back to the world. Then the

[001-106]little shadow spoke, that had been a man.

[001-107]"I am the last," he said.

[001-108]No one had ever made Charon smile before, no one before had ever

[001-109]made him weep.

[001-110]THE DEATH OF PAN

[001-111]When the travellers from London entered [[arcady|Arcady]] they lamented one to

[001-112]another the death of Pan.

[001-113]And anon they saw him lying stiff and still.

[001-114]Horned Pan was still and the dew was on his fur; he had not the look

[001-115]of a live animal. And then they said, "It is true that Pan is dead."

[001-116]And, standing melancholy by that huge prone body, they looked for

[001-117]long at memorable Pan.

[001-118]And evening came and a small star appeared.

[001-119]And presently from a hamlet of some Arcadian valley, with a sound

[001-120]of idle song, Arcadian maidens came.

[001-121]And, when they saw there, suddenly in the twilight, that old recumbent

[001-122]god, they stopped in their running and whispered among themselves.

[001-123]"How silly he looks," they said, and thereat they laughed a little.

[001-124]And at the sound of their laughter Pan leaped up and the gravel flew

[001-125]from his hooves.

[001-126]And, for as long as the travellers stood and listened, the crags and

[001-127]the hill-tops of Arcady rang with the sounds of pursuit.

[001-128]THE SPHINX AT GIZEH

[001-129]I saw the other day the Sphinx's painted face.

[001-130]She had painted her face in order to ogle Time.

[001-131]And he has spared no other painted face in all the world but hers.

[001-132]Delilah was younger than she, and Delilah is dust. Time hath loved

[001-133]nothing but this worthless painted face.

[001-134]I do not care that she is ugly, nor that she has painted her face, so

[001-135]that she only lure his secret from Time.

[001-136]Time dallies like a fool at her feet when he should be smiting cities.

[001-137]Time never wearies of her silly smile.

[001-138]There are temples all about her that he has forgotten to spoil.

[001-139]I saw an old man go by, and Time never touched him.

[001-140]Time that has carried away the seven gates of Thebes!

[001-141]She has tried to bind him with ropes of eternal sand, she had hoped

[001-142]to oppress him with the Pyramids.

[001-143]He lies there in the sand with his foolish hair all spread about her paws.

[001-144]If she ever finds his secret we will put out his eyes, so that he shall

[001-145]find no more our beautiful things--there are lovely gates in Florence

[001-146]that I fear he will carry away.

[001-147]We have tried to bind him with song and with old customs, but they

[001-148]only held him for a little while, and he has always smitten us and

[001-149]mocked us.

[001-150]When he is blind he shall dance to us and make sport.

[001-151]Great clumsy time shall stumble and dance, who liked to kill little

[001-152]children, and can hurt even the daisies no longer.

[001-153]Then shall our children laugh at him who slew Babylon's winged bulls,

[001-154]and smote great numbers of the gods and fairies--when he is shorn

[001-155]of his hours and his years.

[001-156]We will shut him up in the Pyramid of Cheops, in the great chamber

[001-157]where the sarcophagus is. Thence we will lead him out when we

[001-158]give our feasts. He shall ripen our corn for us and do menial work.

[001-159]We will kiss they painted face, O Sphinx, if thou wilt betray to us Time.

[001-160]And yet I fear that in his ultimate anguish he may take hold blindly

[001-161]of the world and the moon, and slowly pull down upon him the

[001-162]House of Man.

[001-163]THE HEN

[001-164]All along the farmyard gables the swallows sat a-row, twittering

[001-165]uneasily to one another, telling of many things, but thinking only of

[001-166]Summer and the South, for Autumn was afoot and the North wind

[001-167]waiting.

[001-168]And suddenly one day they were all quite gone. And everyone

[001-169]spoke of the swallows and the South.

[001-170]"I think I shall go South myself next year," said a hen.

[001-171]And the year wore on and the swallows came again, and the year

[001-172]wore on and they sat again on the gables, and all the poultry discussed

[001-173]the departure of the hen.

[001-174]And very early one morning, the wind being from the North, the

[001-175]swallows all soared suddenly and felt the wind in their wings; and a

[001-176]strength came upon them and a strange old knowledge and a more

[001-177]than human faith, and flying high they left the smoke of our cities and

[001-178]small remembered eaves, and saw at last the huge and homeless sea,

[001-179]and steering by grey sea-currents went southward with the wind. And

[001-180]going South they went by glittering fog-banks and saw old islands lifting

[001-181]their heads above them; they saw the slow quests of the wandering

[001-182]ships, and divers seeking pearls, and lands at war, till there came in

[001-183]view the mountains that they sought and the sight of the peaks they

[001-184]knew; and they descended into an austral valley, and saw Summer

[001-185]sometimes sleeping and sometimes singing song.

[001-186]"I think the wind is about right," said the hen; and she spread her

[001-187]wings and ran out of the poultry-yard. And she ran fluttering out on

[001-188]to the road and some way down it until she came to a garden.

[001-189]At evening she came back panting.

[001-190]And in the poultry-yard she told the poultry how she had gone South

[001-191]as far as the high road, and saw the great world's traffic going by,

[001-192]and came to lands where the potato grew, and saw the stubble upon

[001-193]which men live, and at the end of the road had found a garden, and

[001-194]there were roses in it--beautiful roses!--and the gardener himself was

[001-195]there with his braces on.

[001-196]"How extremely interesting," the poultry said, "and what a really

[001-197]beautiful description!"

[001-198]And the Winter wore away, and the bitter months went by, and the

[001-199]Spring of the year appeared, and the swallows came again.

[001-200]"We have been to the South," they said, "and the valleys beyond

[001-201]the sea."

[001-202]But the poultry would not agree that there was a sea in the South:

[001-203]"You should hear our hen," they said.

[001-204]WIND AND FOG

[001-205]"Way for us," said the North Wind as he came down the sea on an

[001-206]errand of old Winter.

[001-207]And he saw before him the grey silent fog that lay along the tides.

[001-208]"Way for us," said the North Wind, "O ineffectual fog, for I am

[001-209]Winter's leader in his age-old war with the ships. I overwhelm

[001-210]them suddenly in my strength, or drive upon them the huge seafaring

[001-211]bergs. I cross an ocean while you move a mile. There is mourning in

[001-212]inland places when I have met the ships. I drive them upon the rocks

[001-213]and feed the sea. Wherever I appear they bow to our lord the Winter."

[001-214]And to his arrogant boasting nothing said the fog. Only he rose up

[001-215]slowly and trailed away from the sea and, crawling up long valleys,

[001-216]took refuge among the hills; and night came down and everything was

[001-217]still, and the fog began to mumble in the stillness. And I heard him

[001-218]telling infamously to himself the tale of his horrible spoils. "A

[001-219]hundred and fifteen galleons of old Spain, a certain argosy that went

[001-220]from Tyre, eight fisher-fleets and ninety ships of the line, twelve

[001-221]warships under sail, with their carronades, three hundred and

[001-222]eighty-seven river-craft, forty-two merchantmen that carried spice,

[001-223]four quinquiremes, ten triremes, thirty yachts, twenty-one battleships

[001-224]of the modern time, nine thousand admirals...." he mumbled and chuckled

[001-225]on, till I suddenly arose and fled from his fearful contamination.
