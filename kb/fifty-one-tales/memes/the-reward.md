## Overview

"The Reward" is one of Dunsany's most explicit satires on modern commerce. Framed as a dream-vision, it depicts a Hell that is expanding to accommodate new kinds of sinners — not murderers or blasphemers, but the inventors of a cheap yeast whose advertising slogan will be burned into the eyes of the damned for eternity. Written in the age of mass advertising, the tale is a direct assault on the moral emptiness of huckster culture, delivered with the deadpan logic of a parable.

## Summary

The narrator's spirit wanders by night from a factory city and arrives at "the edge of Hell" [006-138]. The place is foul with cinders and jagged, half-buried things. A huge angel is building with plaster and steel.

The narrator asks what is being built. "We are adding to Hell," the angel replies, "to keep pace with the times." The narrator protests — "Don't be too hard on them" — but the angel assures him the new Hell will be worse than the old.

When the narrator questions how a Minister of Grace can reconcile such punishment with his conscience, the angel delivers the tale's devastating answer: "They have invented a new cheap yeast."

The narrator looks at the wall of the new Hell, where words are written in flame, changing colour every fifteen seconds:

> "Yeasto, the great new yeast, it builds up body and brain, and something more."
>
> [006-138]

"They shall look at it for ever," the angel says.

The narrator objects that the yeast-makers "drove a perfectly legitimate trade" and "the law allowed it." The angel does not answer — he goes on hammering. When the narrator accuses him of being "very revengeful," the angel replies:

> "I rested one Christmas Day, and looked and saw little children dying of cancer. I shall go on now until the fires are lit."
>
> [006-138]

## Analysis

The tale's rhetorical structure is a trap that springs shut in three stages. First, the reader expects a conventional Hell — fire, torment, sinners of the familiar sort. Second, the "new cheap yeast" is introduced, and the reader laughs at the absurdity. Third, the angel's Christmas Day revelation — children dying of cancer — reframes the whole. The yeast is not just a joke about advertising; it is a cipher for a world that poisons and profits. The Hell being built is not for the product but for the advertisement: the lie told about the product, the cheerful slogan that masks harm.

Dunsany's angel is not a figure of mercy but of justice, and his justice is terrifyingly precise. He does not punish the yeast-makers for violating a law — they did not. He punishes them for something the law cannot reach: the gap between what they sold and what they knew. The "legitimate trade" defence falls away; the angel's hammer answers it.

The tale's form — a dream-vision — places it in a long literary lineage (Dante, Langland, Bunyan), but the content is aggressively modern. Hell is being renovated in plaster and steel, like a factory expansion. The advertising slogan changes colour every fifteen seconds, like a neon sign. The eternal punishment is not fire but an endless commercial — the thing the damned spent their lives making, played back without cease.

The final line — "I shall go on now until the fires are lit" — leaves the tale suspended between construction and judgement. The Hell is not yet finished; the fires are not yet lit. The angel is still hammering. The yeast is still being sold. The children are still dying. The tale ends in the present continuous, and the present continuous is where the reader lives.

## Characters

- **The narrator** — a dreamer from "a compromising age and a weakening country," who brings the habits of his city's discourse ("How can you reconcile it with your conscience as a Minister of Grace") to a place where such language is meaningless.
- **The angel** — a builder, not a comforter. He works with plaster and steel, and his logic is inexorable. His Christmas Day memory of dying children is the tale's moral fulcrum.
