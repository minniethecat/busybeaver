## Overview

"Lobster Salad" is one of the most formally peculiar tales in *Fifty-One Tales* (1915). It presents a dream-narrative of pure pursuit and escape, with no allegorical scaffolding announced. The narrator climbs the sheer face of a colossal palace wall, pursued by "sable apes" with tigerish teeth, while far below lie the cruel peaks of mountains. The escape, when it comes, is not through the dream-logic of the wall but through a deliberate act of will reaching back into the waking world — a pin on a chest of drawers, jabbed into an arm. The tale's title, never explained within the text, adds a final layer of surrealist disjunction.

## Summary

The narrator is climbing the sheer face of the Palace of Colquonhombros, far above mountains whose "craggy tops" are barely visible below [007-027]. He climbs in his night-shirt but in stout leather boots, whose edges find purchase in the narrow cracks between boulders. His fingers and wrists ache; stopping would be fatal.

He notes, in a parenthetical aside characteristic of Dunsany's dream-logic, that "the thing was all a dream is beside the point" — for it is "well known that if in one of those falls you ever hit the ground — you die" [007-027]. Having looked at the menacing mountaintops, he knows the fall would be terminal.

Each boulder presents a different terror: some too sharp, some too flush with the wall, some crumbling. And behind him follow "sable apes that I have not mentioned as yet, things that had tigerish teeth and were born and bred on that wall" [007-027].

He reaches a breach in the wall — earthquake, lightning, or war — too wide to cross. To go around would mean descending a thousand feet, and the apes would catch him. He cannot go farther. The mountains below seem "surer of me than the apes."

At this moment of extremity, the narrator performs an extraordinary act of mental reach:

> I reached out with a desperate effort of will towards where the pin lay on the chest of drawers. I groped about. I found it! I ran it into my arm. Saved!
>
> [007-027]

## Analysis

### Dream Logic as Narrative Form

Dunsany's dream-tales operate by a consistent internal logic: the dream is real while you are in it, and what happens there has consequences. The aside about falling — "it is well known that if in one of those falls you ever hit the ground — you die" — establishes the stakes without breaking the dream-frame. This is not allegory; it is reportage from an adjacent reality.

### The Pin as Portal

The pin is the tale's single brilliant invention. It is not magical, not symbolic (or not only symbolic), but practical — a sharp object in the waking world that can be used to force awakening. The narrator does not defeat the apes or cross the breach; he changes the terms of the problem entirely by switching realities. The pin on the chest of drawers is a kind of emergency exit, and Dunsany treats the act of reaching for it across the dream-barrier as an act of genuine will.

### The Title

"Lobster Salad" is never mentioned in the tale. Its presence as a title is either pure Dadaist provocation (the tale was published in 1915, predating Dada's formal launch) or a deliberate framing device: the triviality of the title contrasts with the life-or-death intensity of the narrative, suggesting that the entire episode is, from the waking world's perspective, no more significant than a dinner menu. The title deflates the tale's terror retroactively, which may be exactly the point.

## Characters

- **The narrator** — a first-person dreamer in a night-shirt and stout leather boots, resourceful and aware of dream-logic's rules. His defining trait is the will to reach across the dream-barrier when physical escape is impossible.
- **The sable apes** — tiger-toothed pursuers "born and bred on that wall," introduced with an almost comic belatedness ("that I have not mentioned as yet"). They are dream-predators, implacable but ultimately circumvented.

## Themes

- **[[dream]] and waking** — the permeable boundary between dream and reality, and the will as the faculty that can cross it.
- **Escape and ingenuity** — the solution is not to overcome the obstacle but to exit the frame in which the obstacle exists.
- **Surrealism** — the tale's disjunctive title and dream-logic anticipate surrealist techniques by several years.
