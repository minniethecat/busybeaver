## Overview

Parnassus is the mountain of the Muses in Greek mythology. In the Fifty-One Tales, it appears in "[[the-messengers|The Messengers]]," where a man wandering near its slopes encounters the Muses and is called to deliver their message to the Golden Town.

## In "The Messengers"

The tale opens with a man engaged in the most mundane of activities:

> One wandering nigh Parnassus chasing hares heard the high Muses. [008-044]

The Muses command him:

> "Take us a message to the Golden Town." [008-045]

The man protests that he is not the kind of person the Muses speak to — he is a hare-hunter, not a poet or hero. But the Muses call him by name and repeat their command. The man is "downcast for he would have chased hares" — he would rather continue his simple pursuit than become the bearer of a divine message.

## Thematic Significance

Parnassus in "[[the-messengers|The Messengers]]" serves a function familiar in Dunsany's work: the mythic breaking into the ordinary. The mountain is the traditional seat of poetic inspiration, but the man who encounters the Muses there is no poet — he is a hunter chasing hares. The Muses' choice of him is arbitrary, almost comic, and his reluctance is entirely human.

The tale echoes a theme found elsewhere in the collection: the call to something greater than oneself, and the resistance to that call. The man on Parnassus, like the workman on his scaffolding or the young man dining with [[death|Death]] in [[london|London]], is confronted with a reality that exceeds his expectations — and his first impulse is to return to what he knows.
