## Overview

"The Latest Thing" is a tale from Section 3 of *Fifty-One Tales* (1915) by Lord Dunsany. The narrator observes an "unclean-feeder" crouching by the river of Time, greedily snatching at whatever foul things drift down from the cities upstream. A poet walks nearby, his gaze fixed on distant things. When the narrator draws the poet's attention to the unclean-feeder, the poet's answer — about floods and centuries melting — carries an apocalyptic warning wrapped in calm.

## Summary

The tale opens with a vivid, repulsive image: an unclean-feeder crouches by the river of Time, his back turned to orchards, barns of ancient grain, and golden hills. He watches only the river, and whatever it sends him:

> "And whatever the river chanced to send him down the unclean-feeder clutched at greedily with his arms, wading out into the water."
>
> [003-065]

Certain "uncleanly cities" upstream send "fearfully nameless things" floating down the river. When their odor reaches him, the unclean-feeder plunges into the dirty water, standing far out, expectant — and these foul things appear on his lips. When a fallen rhododendron petal or a rose drifts down from the upper reaches, he growls at their uselessness.

A poet walks beside the river's bank, his head lifted, his gaze far away:

> "I think he saw the sea, and the hills of Fate from which the river ran."
>
> [003-078]

When the narrator points out the unclean-feeder standing waist-deep in the foul river, the poet says calmly: "The current will sweep him away." The narrator presses — what about the cities that poison the river? The poet's answer is the tale's crescendo:

> "Whenever the centuries melt on the hills of Fate the river terribly floods."
>
> [003-084]

## Analysis

The "unclean-feeder" is an allegorical figure for those who feed on scandal, sensation, and the latest foul thing — the news-hungry, the gossip-ravenous, the consumers of the worst that civilization produces. He has his back to beauty (orchards, ancient grain, golden hills) and watches only the river of Time for the next piece of filth.

Dunsany's structure is masterful. The poet sees the same river but looks at its source — the hills of Fate, the distant sea — rather than its immediate contents. His indifference is not callousness but perspective: the unclean-feeder is already doomed by the very current he feeds in. The river that brings him his sustenance will one day sweep him away.

The final line about the river flooding when "the centuries melt" suggests an eschatological vision: the accumulation of civilization's filth must eventually be cleansed. The tale is a retort to the appetite for "the latest thing" — what matters is not what drifts by today but what the river's source intends.
