## Overview

"Time and the Tradesman" is a short allegorical tale from *Fifty-One Tales* (1915) by Lord Dunsany. In a furniture shop, the personified [[time|Time]] confronts a tradesman with the impermanence of his wares — and by extension, of all human endeavor. The tale is one of Dunsany's most compressed fables, using the furniture shop as a stage for a meditation on mortality and craft.

## Summary

Time, personified with hair grey from the dust of ruined cities, enters a furniture shop. The tradesman proudly shows him his finest pieces — well-crafted chairs, tables, and cabinets meant to last for generations. Time examines each piece and, with a touch or a glance, reveals how it will warp, crack, and decay.

The tradesman protests that his work is built to endure, but Time shows him that nothing endures forever. The tale concludes with the somber recognition that all crafted things — no matter how well made — must succumb to time's passage.

## Analysis

This tale, at roughly 130 words, is one of the collection's shortest entries. It works through a simple dramatic structure: the proud craftsman, the silent critic, and the inevitable revelation. Time is not portrayed as malevolent but as simply true — his hair grey not from age but from "the dust of the ruin of cities," making him a witness to all that has ever fallen.

The furniture shop functions as a microcosm: every human creation, from a chair to a civilization, faces the same fate. Dunsany's prose here is particularly economical, compressing a complete dramatic arc and philosophical meditation into what amounts to a single paragraph.

## Characters

- **Time**: Personified as an ancient wanderer whose grey hair comes not from weakness but from the accumulated dust of ruined cities. He serves as the tale's truth-teller.
- **The Tradesman**: A craftsman proud of his furniture, representing human ambition and the desire for permanence through craft.

## Text

> Once Time as he prowled the world, his hair grey not with weakness but with dust of the ruin of cities, came to a furniture shop and entered.
>
> And the tradesman, with a smile, showed him his finest wares.
