## Overview

King Karna-Vootra is a ruler who appears in Lord Dunsany's tale "The Dream of King Karna-Vootra" from Section 3 of *Fifty-One Tales* (1915). Seated on his throne "commanding all things," the King does not issue decrees or lead armies; instead, he recounts a dream — a vision of the radiant Vava-Nyria and an imagined walk through the beautiful, secluded gardens of Istrakhan. In the Dunsanian inversion of power, the master of all things dreams not of dominion but of companionship and beauty.

## Role in the Tales

King Karna-Vootra speaks only a single passage in the corpus, but his speech is the entirety of the tale that bears his name. He opens with a declaration of supernatural clarity:

> "I very clearly saw last night the queenly Vava-Nyria. Though partly she was hidden by great clouds that swept continually by her, rolling over and over, yet her face was unhidden and shone, being full of moonlight."
>
> [003-163]

He then addresses Vava-Nyria directly, inviting her to walk with him:

> "Walk with me by the great pools in many-gardened, beautiful Istrakhan where the lilies float that give delectable dreams; or, drawing aside the curtain of hanging orchids, pass with me thence from the pools by a secret path through the else impassable jungle that fills the only way between the mountains that shut in Istrakhan."
>
> [003-172]

The King's language is incantatory rather than commanding — he builds the dreamscape through accumulation of exotic names and sensory images. His final observation, that the mountains "have valleys among them older than the wrinkles in the moon," compresses geological and lunar time into a single simile, shifting the scale from the intimate walk he proposes to the immense antiquity of the landscape.

## Character Analysis

King Karna-Vootra embodies a characteristic Dunsanian theme: the tension between worldly power and imaginative longing. Though he sits "commanding all things," his inner life is given over entirely to aesthetic contemplation. The dream he recounts is not a prophecy or a warning, but a purely sensory invitation — lilies, orchids, moonlight on water, a secret path through jungle. The King's power, in Dunsany's treatment, is real but irrelevant; what matters is the capacity to dream beautifully.

The figure of Vava-Nyria in the dream is partly obscured by clouds, her face shining with moonlight. This partial visibility — revelation mixed with concealment — is typical of Dunsany's treatment of the feminine and the divine throughout the tales. The King sees but does not possess; he invites but does not command.
