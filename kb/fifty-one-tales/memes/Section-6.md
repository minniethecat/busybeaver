[006-001]"Have you not slain the Dinatherium?" he said. "Have you not put out

[006-002]the Moon? Why! you will beat me yet."

[006-003]And with a dry and barking sound [[death|Death]] wept and nothing said; and

[006-004]presently Man arose and went wondering away; for he knew not if

[006-005]Death wept out of pity for his opponent, or because he knew that he

[006-006]should not have such sport again when the old game was over and

[006-007]Man was gone, or whether because perhaps, for some hidden reason,

[006-008]he could never repeat on Earth his triumph over the Moon.

[006-009]TAKING UP PICADILLY

[006-010]Going down Picadilly one day and nearing Grosvenor Place I saw,

[006-011]if my memory is not at fault, some workmen with their coats off--or

[006-012]so they seemed. They had pickaxes in their hands and wore corduroy

[006-013]trousers and that little leather band below the knee that goes by the

[006-014]astonishing name of "York-to-London."

[006-015]They seemed to be working with peculiar vehemence, so that I

[006-016]stopped and asked one what they were doing.

[006-017]"We are taking up Picadilly," he said to me.

[006-018]"But at this time of year?" I said. "Is it usual in June?"

[006-019]"We are not what we seem," said he.

[006-020]"Oh, I see," I said, "you are doing it for a joke."

[006-021]"Well, not exactly that," he answered me.

[006-022]"For a bet?" I said.

[006-023]"Not precisely," said he.

[006-024]And then I looked at the bit that they had already picked, and

[006-025]though it was broad daylight over my head it was darkness down

[006-026]there, all full of the southern stars.

[006-027]"It was noisy and bad and we grew aweary of it," said he that wore

[006-028]corduroy trousers. "We are not what we appear."

[006-029]They were taking up Picadilly altogether.

[006-030]AFTER THE FIRE

[006-031]When that happened which had been so long in happening and the

[006-032]world hit a black, uncharted star, certain tremendous creatures out

[006-033]of some other world came peering among the cinders to see if there

[006-034]were anything there that it were worth while to remember. They

[006-035]spoke of the great things that the world was known to have had;

[006-036]they mentioned the mammoth. And presently they saw man's temples,

[006-037]silent and windowless, staring like empty skulls.

[006-038]"Some great thing has been here," one said, "in these huge places."

[006-039]"It was the mammoth," said one. "Something greater than he," said

[006-040]another.

[006-041]And then they found that the greatest thing in the world had been

[006-042]the dreams of man.

[006-043]THE CITY

[006-044]In time as well as space my fancy roams far from here. It led me

[006-045]once to the edge of certain cliffs that were low and red and rose

[006-046]up out of a desert: a little way off in the desert there was a city. It

[006-047]was evening, and I sat and watched the city.

[006-048]Presently I saw men by threes and fours come softly stealing out

[006-049]of that city's gate to the number of about twenty. I heard the hum

[006-050]of men's voices speaking at evening.

[006-051]"It is well they are gone," they said. "It is well they are gone. We

[006-052]can do business now. It is well they are gone." And the men that

[006-053]had left the city sped away over the sand and so passed into the

[006-054]twilight.

[006-055]"Who are these men?" I said to my glittering leader.

[006-056]"The poets," my fancy answered. "The poets and artists."

[006-057]"Why do they steal away?" I said to him. "And why are the people

[006-058]glad that they have gone?"

[006-059]He said: "It must be some doom that is going to fall on the city,

[006-060]something has warned them and they have stolen away. Nothing

[006-061]may warn the people."

[006-062]I heard the wrangling voices, glad with commerce, rise up from

[006-063]the city. And then I also departed, for there was an ominous look

[006-064]on the face of the sky.

[006-065]And only a thousand years later I passed that way, and there was

[006-066]nothing, even among the weeds, of what had been that city.

[006-067]THE FOOD OF DEATH

[006-068]Death was sick. But they brought him bread that the modern bakers

[006-069]make, whitened with alum, and the tinned meats of Chicago, with a

[006-070]pinch of our modern substitute for salt. They carried him into the

[006-071]dining-room of a great hotel (in that close atmosphere Death breathed

[006-072]more freely), and there they gave him their cheap Indian tea. They

[006-073]brought him a bottle of wine that they called champagne. Death

[006-074]drank it up. They brought a newspaper and looked up the patent

[006-075]medicines; they gave him the foods that it recommended for invalids,

[006-076]and a little medicine as prescribed in the paper. They gave him some

[006-077]milk and borax, such as children drink in England.

[006-078]Death arose ravening, strong, and strode again through the cities.

[006-079]THE LONELY IDOL

[006-080]I had from a friend an old outlandish stone, a little swine-faced idol

[006-081]to whom no one prayed.

[006-082]And when I saw his melancholy case as he sat cross-legged at

[006-083]receipt of prayer, holding a little scourge that the years had broken

[006-084](and no one heeded the scourge and no one prayed and no one

[006-085]came with squealing sacrifice; and he had been a god), then I took

[006-086]pity on the little forgotten thing and prayed to it as perhaps they prayed

[006-087]long since, before the coming of the strange dark ships, and humbled

[006-088]myself and said:

[006-089]"O idol, idol of the hard pale stone, invincible to the years, O

[006-090]scourge-holder, give ear for behold I pray.

[006-091]"O little pale-green image whose wanderings are from far, know

[006-092]thou that here in Europe and in other lands near by, too soon there

[006-093]pass from us the sweets and song and the lion strength of youth:

[006-094]too soon do their cheeks fade, their hair grow grey and our beloved

[006-095]die; too brittle is beauty, too far off is fame and the years are gathered

[006-096]too soon; there are leaves, leaves falling, everywhere falling; there is

[006-097]autumn among men, autumn and reaping; failure there is, struggle,

[006-098]dying and weeping, and all that is beautiful hath not remained but is

[006-099]even as the glory of morning upon the water.

[006-100]"Even our memories are gathered too with the sound of the ancient

[006-101]voices, the pleasant ancient voices that come to our ears no more;

[006-102]the very gardens of our childhood fade, and there dims with the speed

[006-103]of the years even the mind's own eye.

[006-104]"O be not any more the friend of [[time|Time]], for the silent hurry of his

[006-105]malevolent feet have trodden down what's fairest; I almost hear the

[006-106]whimper of the years running behind him hound-like, and it takes few

[006-107]to tear us.

[006-108]"All that is beautiful he crushes down as a big man tramples daises,

[006-109]all that is fairest. How very fair are the little children of men. It is

[006-110]autumn with all the world, and the stars weep to see it.

[006-111]"Therefore no longer be the friend of Time, who will not let us be,

[006-112]and be not good to him but pity us, and let lovely things live on for

[006-113]the sake of our tears."

[006-114]Thus prayed I out of compassion one windy day to the snout-faced

[006-115]idol to whom no one kneeled.

[006-116]THE SPHINX IN THEBES (MASSACHUSETTS)

[006-117]There was a woman in a steel-built city who had all that money

[006-118]could buy, she had gold and dividends and trains and houses, and

[006-119]she had pets to play with, but she had no sphinx.

[006-120]So she besought them to bring her a live sphinx; and therefore they

[006-121]went to the menageries, and then to the forests and the desert places,

[006-122]and yet could find no sphinx.

[006-123]And she would have been content with a little lion but that one was

[006-124]already owned by a woman she knew; so they had to search the

[006-125]world again for a sphinx.

[006-126]And still there was none.

[006-127]But they were not men that it is easy to baffle, and at last they found

[006-128]a sphinx in a desert at evening watching a ruined temple whose gods

[006-129]she had eaten hundreds of years ago when her hunger was on her.

[006-130]And they cast chains on her, who was still with an ominous stillness,

[006-131]and took her westwards with them and brought her home.

[006-132]And so the sphinx came to the steel-built city.

[006-133]And the woman was very glad that she owned a sphinx: but the

[006-134]sphinx stared long into her eyes one day, and softly asked a riddle

[006-135]of the woman.

[006-136]And the woman could not answer, and she died.

[006-137]And the sphinx is silent again and none knows what she will do.

[006-138]THE REWARD

[006-139]One's spirit goes further in dreams than it does by day. Wandering

[006-140]once by night from a factory city I came to the edge of Hell.

[006-141]The place was foul with cinders and cast-off things, and jagged,

[006-142]half-buried things with shapeless edges, and there was a huge angel

[006-143]with a hammer building in plaster and steel. I wondered what he did

[006-144]in that dreadful place. I hesitated, then asked him what he was

[006-145]building. "We are adding to Hell," he said, "to keep pace with the

[006-146]times." "Don't be too hard on them," I said, for I had just come out

[006-147]of a compromising age and a weakening country. The angel did not

[006-148]answer. "It won't be as bad as the old hell, will it?" I said. "Worse,"

[006-149]said the angel.

[006-150]"How can you reconcile it with your conscience as a Minister of

[006-151]Grace," I said, "to inflict such a punishment?" (They talked like this

[006-152]in the city whence I had come and I could not avoid the habit of it.)

[006-153]"They have invented a new cheap yeast," said the angel.

[006-154]I looked at the legend on the walls of the hell that the angel was

[006-155]building, the words were written in flame, every fifteen seconds they

[006-156]changed their color, "Yeasto, the great new yeast, it builds up body

[006-157]and brain, and something more."

[006-158]"They shall look at it for ever," the angel said.

[006-159]"But they drove a perfectly legitimate trade," I said, "the law allowed

[006-160]it."

[006-161]The angel went on hammering into place the huge steel uprights.

[006-162]"You are very revengeful," I said. "Do you never rest from doing

[006-163]this terrible work?"

[006-164]"I rested one Christmas Day," the angel said, "and looked and

[006-165]saw little children dying of cancer. I shall go on now until the fires

[006-166]are lit."

[006-167]"It is very hard to prove," I said, "that the yeast is as bad as you

[006-168]think."

[006-169]"After all," I said, "they must live."

[006-170]And the angel made no answer but went on building his hell.

[006-171]THE TROUBLE IN LEAFY GREEN STREET

[006-172]She went to the idol-shop in Moleshill Street, where the old man

[006-173]mumbles, and said: "I want a god to worship when it is wet."

[006-174]The old man reminded her of the heavy penalties that rightly attach

[006-175]to idolatry and, when he had enumerated all, she answered him as

[006-176]was meet: "Give me a god to worship when it is wet."

[006-177]And he went to the back places of his shop and sought out and

[006-178]brought her a god. The same was carved of grey stone and wore a

[006-179]propitious look and was named, as the old man mumbled, The God

[006-180]of Rainy Cheerfulness.

[006-181]Now it may be that long confinement to the house affects adversely

[006-182]the liver, or these things may be of the soul, but certain it is that on

[006-183]a rainy day her spirits so far descended that those cheerful creatures

[006-184]came within sight of the Pit, and, having tried cigarettes to no good

[006-185]end, she bethought her of Moleshill Street and the mumbling man.

[006-186]He brought the grey idol forth and mumbled of guarantees, although

[006-187]he put nothing on paper, and she paid him there and then his

[006-188]preposterous price and took the idol away.

[006-189]And on the next wet day that there ever was she prayed to the

[006-190]grey-stone idol that she had bought, the God of Rainy Cheerfulness

[006-191](who knows with what ceremony or what lack of it?), and so

[006-192]brought down on her in Leafy Green Street, in the preposterous

[006-193]house at the corner, that doom of which all men speak.
