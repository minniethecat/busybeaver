## Overview

"Roses" is a brief tale from Section 3 of *Fifty-One Tales* (1915) by Lord Dunsany. It meditates on a roadside where wild roses bloom with unusual abundance and color — and discovers that the richer blush of the blossoms is the sole surviving memory of a village that stood there two hundred rose-generations ago.

## Summary

The narrator describes a roadside where wild roses bloom with a strange, almost exotic beauty:

> "I know a roadside where the wild rose blooms with a strange abundance. There is a beauty in the blossoms too of an almost exotic kind, a taint of deeper pink that shocks the Puritan flowers."
>
> [003-121]

Two hundred generations ago — generations of roses — this was a street in a village. When the village was abandoned, the roses emerged from the wilderness to clamber around the houses of men. Of all the village's memories, cottages, and inhabitants:

> "nothing remains but a more beautiful blush on the faces of the roses."
>
> [003-130]

The tale closes with a personal hope: that when London is "clean passed away" and the fields return like an exiled people returning after a war, they may find some beautiful thing to remind them of the city — "because we have loved a little that swart old city."

## Analysis

Like many of Dunsany's shortest pieces, "Roses" works through a single, compressed image. The deeper pink of the roses — "a taint of deeper pink that shocks the Puritan flowers" — is framed as a relic of human habitation, a "floral decadence" that lingers long after the village itself has vanished. The rose is nature remembering civilization, and remembering it as beauty.

The phrase "two hundred generations ago (generations, I mean, of roses)" is characteristic Dunsany: a deliberate shift of temporal scale that places human history within a botanical calendar. A rose generation is perhaps five to ten years; two hundred rose-generations may span only a millennium or two. Human time, the tale suggests, is fleetingly brief — and what outlasts us is not our monuments but a blush on a petal.

The final sentence shifts from observation to prayer: the hope that London, too, may leave behind a beautiful trace when it falls. The adjective "swart" — dark, swarthy — is unexpected and affectionate, treating the city not as a grand imperial capital but as something loved because it is familiar, imperfect, and ours.
