## Overview

"The True History of the Hare and the Tortoise" is one of the ultra-short tales from Section 1 of *Fifty-One Tales* (1915) by Lord Dunsany. The entire tale consists solely of its title — nine words that promise to overturn one of the most famous fables in Western literature, yet deliver no narrative at all. The tale is a gesture, a literary joke, a metaphysical absence.

## Summary

The tale is its title. There is no text beyond it:

> "The True History of the Hare and the Tortoise"
>
> [001-032]

The absence itself is the work. Dunsany gestures toward Aesop's fable — a story so universally known that its characters, plot, and moral require no retelling — and then simply withholds. The "true history" remains unwritten, implying perhaps that no such history exists, or that the truth of the fable lies precisely in what everyone already knows.

## Analysis

### Subversion of the Fable Tradition

Dunsany's title-only tale performs a radical act of literary economy. By promising a "true history" of the hare and the tortoise — a promise the reader instantly recognizes as impossible, since the characters are fictional — Dunsany collapses the distance between title and text, announcement and execution.

The fable of the hare and the tortoise stands as perhaps the most widely told moral story in Western culture: the swift but arrogant hare races the slow but steady tortoise, and the tortoise wins through persistence. The moral — "slow and steady wins the race" — is so embedded that Dunsany need only gesture at it with his title. The reader's mind supplies the entire narrative.

To call this a "true history" is doubly ironic: first, because the hare and tortoise never existed; second, because the very concept of a single "true" version of a fable contradicts the oral tradition from which the fable emerged, where each telling is both original and derivative.

### The Minimalist Tale as Form

This piece is one of several title-only or single-sentence tales in *Fifty-One Tales*. Others like "A Mistaken Identity," "Alone the Immortals," and "A Moral Little Tale" operate similarly — they are narrative gestures rather than narratives, titles that contain their entire content.

In this, Dunsany anticipates the experimental minimalism of twentieth-century literature. The title-only tale is not a fragment — it is a complete work in which the title does all the work. The reader becomes the co-author, filling the empty space with the cultural memory of the fable.

Dunsany's choice of subject is precise: only the most famous stories can sustain this treatment. Had he titled a piece "The True History of a Minor Byzantine Official," the reader would be lost. But the hare and the tortoise are available to every reader who has encountered a children's storybook — which is to say, nearly every reader.
