## Overview

"The Songless Country" is a tale from Section 3 of *Fifty-One Tales* (1915) by Lord Dunsany. A poet arrives in a nation that has no songs — no "little foolish songs to sing to itself at evening." He crafts simple, aimless melodies for them, only to be told that modern commerce leaves no room for such frivolity. The tale is a compact lament for the death of the impractical in an age of industry.

## Summary

The poet comes to a great country and finds it utterly songless:

> The poet came unto a great country in which there were no songs. And he lamented gently for the nation that had not any little foolish songs to sing to itself at evening.
>
> [003-044]

He laments for a people who have no music for their evenings. Determined to remedy this, he makes "aimless songs out of the small unreasonable legends, that are somewhat akin to the wind in the vales of my childhood":

> "I will make for them myself some little foolish songs so that they may be merry in the lanes and happy by the fireside."
>
> [003-047]

After crafting these songs, he brings them to the weary workers at the end of their day. His offer is gentle and self-deprecating — he does not claim great art, only small pleasures for "disconsolate evenings." But the response is devastating in its banality:

> "If you think we have time for that sort of nonsense nowadays you cannot know much of the progress of modern commerce."
>
> [003-057]

The tale ends with the poet's verdict, delivered through tears: "Alas! They are damned."

## Analysis

This is one of Dunsany's sharpest critiques of modernity, compressed into fewer than 200 words. The people of the songless country are not evil — they are simply busy. They have replaced the evening song with commerce, the aimless legend with productivity. Their damnation is not moral but aesthetic: they have lost the capacity to receive the useless gift of song.

The poet's self-description of his songs as "little foolish songs" and "aimless" is crucial. He is not offering high art or moral instruction — he is offering beauty with no instrumental value. And the modern mind, trained to measure everything by utility, cannot even see the offering.

The tale pairs naturally with "The Unpasturable Fields," the preceding tale in Section 3. Where that tale contrasts the solid and the vaporous, this one contrasts the useful and the beautiful. The verdict is the same: what passes away (clouds, songs) may be more valuable than what endures.
