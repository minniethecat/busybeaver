## Overview

Leafy Green Street is a residential [[london|London]] street that serves as the setting for "[[the-trouble-in-leafy-green-street|The Trouble in Leafy Green Street]]." The street's bland, suburban name — with its suggestion of verdant respectability — forms an ironic counterpoint to the supernatural doom that descends upon it.

## In "The Trouble in Leafy Green Street"

The tale concerns a woman afflicted by low spirits on rainy days. She visits an idol-shop in [[moleshill-street|Moleshill Street]] and purchases a small grey-stone god named The God of Rainy Cheerfulness. The old shopkeeper warns her of "the heavy penalties that rightly attach to idolatry," but she insists on the purchase.

On the next wet day, she prays to the idol, with unspecified ceremony, and:

> brought down on her in Leafy Green Street, in the preposterous house at the corner, that doom of which all men speak. [006-192]

## Thematic Significance

The tale is remarkable for what it withholds. We learn nothing of the nature of the doom — only that "all men speak" of it. The contrast between the domesticity of Leafy Green Street and the cosmic weight of the unspecified doom is characteristic of Dunsany's method: the supernatural erupts not in ancient temples or mythical kingdoms, but in a "preposterous house at the corner" of an ordinary [[london|London]] street.

The adjective "preposterous," applied to both the idol's price and the house itself, suggests a world out of joint — where the mundane and the terrible sit awkwardly side by side.
