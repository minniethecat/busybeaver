## Overview

"The Raft-Builders" is among the most self-reflective tales in *Fifty-One Tales* (1915). Dunsany steps back from the mythic and fabulous to consider the act of writing itself, comparing all authors to sailors on sinking ships who build small rafts from whatever materials are at hand. The rafts — our thoughts, our phrases, our names — may float for a while upon Oblivion's sea, but the ships are doomed, and most rafts go to pieces before the vessel breaks up. The tale is a memento mori for writers, a reckoning with the ephemerality of literary reputation, and yet — in its very existence — an act of defiance against the oblivion it describes.

## Summary

The tale opens with a startling image:

> All we who write put me in mind of sailors hastily making rafts upon doomed ships.
>
> [001-226]

Dunsany elaborates: when the ship of life breaks up under "the heavy years," our thoughts float on for a while as small lost rafts upon Oblivion's sea. But they carry little — "our names and a phrase or two and little else" [001-226].

He distinguishes two kinds of writers. Those who write "as a trade to please the whim of the day" are sailors who build rafts only to warm their hands and distract themselves from doom — and their rafts go to pieces before the ship even sinks [001-226]. The implication is clear: commercial writing, written for the moment, dies even faster than the writer.

Dunsany then surveys the sea of Oblivion itself. Its tranquility is "deadlier than tempest" [001-226]. [[time|Time]] swims in its deeps "like a monstrous whale," feeding on the littlest things — "small tunes and little unskilled songs of the olden, golden evenings" — and occasionally overturning whole ships. The wreckage of Babylon floats idly on the surface; Nineveh is somewhere below, among "the weedy masses of old centuries that hide the sodden bulk of sunken Tyre and make a darkness round Persepolis" [001-226].

The tale ends with two images: the sea-floor "strewn with crowns" — the markers of drowned civilizations — and a single surviving raft: "There goes the raft that Homer made for Helen" [001-226].

## Analysis

"The Raft-Builders" is Dunsany's ars poetica in miniature. The central metaphor — writers as raft-builders on sinking ships — is at once humbling and ennobling. The ship *will* sink; every writer dies, and most work dies with them. But the act of building — of shaping something that might, against all odds, float — is what defines the writer's vocation.

The tale also functions as a compressed history of literary civilization. Dunsany's catalogue of drowned powers — Babylon, Nineveh, Tyre, Persepolis — spans the ancient world, each name a synecdoche for an entire culture that has vanished. The sea-floor "strewn with crowns" is an image of almost unbearable melancholy: all the kings and queens who thought themselves [[immortality|immortal]] are now indistinguishable from the weeds.

But the final image turns the meditation on its head. [[homer|Homer]]'s raft — the stories he told of Helen — still floats. Against the vast darkness of Oblivion, Dunsany places a single point of light: the poem that has survived three thousand years. It is not a promise — most rafts sink — but it is a possibility. And for a writer, possibility is enough.

The tale also quietly critiques its own era's literary marketplace. The "sailors that work at the rafts only to warm their hands" are the hacks and journalists of Dunsany's day, writing for money rather than permanence. Their rafts "go all to pieces before the ship breaks up" — they are forgotten even before they die. Dunsany, by contrast, is building his raft with care.

The image of Time as a monstrous whale in Oblivion's deeps connects this tale to the collection's broader treatment of cosmic forces. Unlike the [[fate|Fate]]-driven floods of [[the-latest-thing|The Latest Thing]], which cleanse the river periodically, the whale of Oblivion feeds continuously and indiscriminately. The sea is a gentler destroyer than the flood — but no less absolute.

## Characters

- **The narrator / "we who write"** — Dunsany speaks in the first-person plural, aligning himself with all writers who take their craft seriously. The voice is elegiac but not despairing.
- **Time** — personified as "a monstrous whale" that feeds on the littlest things — small songs, old tunes, and occasionally whole ships. A figure of indiscriminate, patient consumption.
- **Homer** — invoked only through his work: "the raft that Homer made for Helen." The archetype of the writer whose work survives.

## Text

> All we who write put me in mind of sailors hastily making rafts upon doomed ships.
>
> When we break up under the heavy years and go down into eternity with all that is ours our thoughts like small lost rafts float on awhile upon Oblivion's sea. They will not carry much over those tides, our names and a phrase or two and little else.
>
> [001-226]

> See now Oblivion shimmering all around us, its very tranquility deadlier than tempest. How little all our keels have troubled it. Time in its deeps swims like a monstrous whale; and, like a whale, feeds on the littlest things.
>
> [001-226]

> Our ships were all unseaworthy from the first.
>
> There goes the raft that Homer made for Helen.
>
> [001-226]
