## Overview

Song in *Fifty-One Tales* represents the human impulse to create beauty and meaning in the face of indifference. Dunsany treats song as both gift and torment — an offering of devotion that may go unappreciated, a pastoral echo of a fading world, and a sound that can either bind the divine or deepen the anguish of the damned. Across the collection, song bridges the mortal and the eternal, the joyful and the sorrowful, and in one of the collection's most striking cosmic visions, song alone outlasts the death of worlds.

### The Poet's Offering

The most sustained treatment of song appears in *[[the-assignation|The Assignation]]*, where the poet offers [[fame-concept|Fame]] "little chaplets of song, to deck her forehead in the courts of Time" (001-061). Fame, however, prefers the "worthless garlands" of popular acclaim — "perishable things" that die by evening. The poet persists:

> "And after a while whenever these garlands died the poet came to her with his chaplets of song; and still she laughed at him and wore the worthless wreaths, though they always died at evening."
> (001-066)

Song here embodies true artistic devotion, contrasted with the ephemeral rewards of fame. The poet's persistence in the face of rejection makes song a symbol of integrity — a gift offered without guarantee of appreciation.

### Pastoral Song and Divine Loss

In the pastoral mode, "Arcadian maidens came" with "a sound of idle song" (001-120), evoking a pre-modern world where music arises naturally from human community in [[arcady|Arcady]]. This idyll stands in tension with the forces of modernity and divine withdrawal. In *[[the-death-of-pan|The Death of Pan]]*, song becomes a desperate binding ritual:

> "We have tried to bind him with song and with old customs, but they only held him for a little while, and he has always smitten us and mocked us."
> (001-147)

Through song and tradition, mortals attempt to hold onto the dying god [[pan|Pan]], but the effort proves temporary — [[time|Time]] eventually breaks all bonds.

### Song as Torment

The darkest treatment of song occurs in the tale of hell, where a damned soul laments:

> "O friend, friend, you do not know what a detestable thing it is to sit in hell and hear people being happy, and singing in theatres and singing in the fields, and whispering after dances under the moon."
> (005-022–005-024)

Here, song is not consolation but torment — the sound of joy from which one is forever excluded, transforming music into an instrument of suffering. The damned soul's exclusion from song mirrors the Puritan's exclusion from [[the-devil|the Devil]]'s dance — both are locked out of beauty by their own natures.

### The Songless Country

In *[[the-songless-country|The Songless Country]]*, Dunsany explores what happens when a nation loses its capacity for song. The [[the-poet|poet]] arrives in "a great country in which there were no songs" (003-044) and laments "for the nation that had not any little foolish songs to sing to itself at evening" (003-046). Moved by pity, he makes "aimless songs such as maidens sing on the hills in the older happier countries" (003-050) — songs "out of the small unreasonable legends, that are somewhat akin to the wind in the vales of my childhood" (003-053–003-054).

The nation's response reveals the tale's critique of modernity: "If you think we have time for that sort of nonsense nowadays..." The rejection of aimless beauty in favor of utilitarian busyness marks the spiritual poverty of a world that has forgotten how to sing.

### The Immortality of Song

The collection's most transcendent vision of song appears in *[[the-return-of-song|The Return of Song]]*. The narrator dreams of [[valhalla|Valhalla]], where the gods announce:

> "The swans are singing again."

A world dies, and "up and up from it looking larger and larger came a flock of white, innumerable swans, singing and singing and singing, till it seemed as though even the gods were wild ships swimming in music" (005-052–005-054). A humble god explains:

> "The worlds are not for ever; only song is immortal."
> (005-060)

This is the collection's definitive statement on song: worlds perish, civilizations crumble, but song returns to its divine source, and the [[lark|larks]] go down from the gods to seed a new world with music. Song is not merely beautiful — it is the one created thing that survives cosmic destruction.

### The Origin of Song

In *[[the-song-of-the-blackbird|The Song of the Blackbird]]*, Dunsany offers a comic meditation on artistic originality. The [[the-blackbird|blackbird]] claims to have composed his song in a moment of inspiration upon seeing "the most beautiful she-blackbird": "I opened my beak to give a shout, and then this song came, and there had never been anything like it before" (008-029). But an old wanderer dismisses the claim:

> "That song new? Not a bit of it. God made it years ago. All the blackbirds used to sing it when I was young."
> (008-040)

The tale suggests that all true song has a divine origin — no artist creates ex nihilo; the melodies we think new are ancient gifts returning through us. The theme echoes *The Return of Song*, where the larks descend from the gods bearing the gift of song to a new world.
