## Overview

The Caucasus Mountains appear in "[[the-unpasturable-fields|The Unpasturable Fields]]" in a single, striking simile. When the clouds gather above the mountains, they do so in the likeness of the world's great ranges — the Caucasus and the [[himalaya|Himalaya]] — coming "riding past the sunlight upon the backs of storms."

## In "The Unpasturable Fields"

The mountains proclaim their imperishability. The clouds respond by gathering into forms that rival the mountains' own:

> And softly the clouds foregathered from far places, and crag on crag and mountain upon mountain in the likeness of Caucasus upon Himalaya came riding past the sunlight upon the backs of storms and looked down idly from their golden heights upon the crests of the mountains. [003-015]–[003-017]

The Caucasus and [[himalaya|Himalaya]] here function as the ultimate exemplars of mountain grandeur. When the clouds wish to upstage the mountains, they assume the forms of the greatest ranges on Earth — the Caucasus representing the western end of the mountain chain, the [[himalaya|Himalaya]] the eastern.

## Thematic Significance

The mention of the Caucasus alongside the [[himalaya|Himalaya]] places Dunsany's geographical imagination within a classical framework. The Caucasus was known to the ancient Greeks as the boundary of the known world — the place where Prometheus was chained. The [[himalaya|Himalaya]], by contrast, represents the farthest reaches of the East. Together, they span the geographical imagination from West to East, making the clouds' display a comprehensive one.

The image of mountains "riding past the sunlight upon the backs of storms" is one of Dunsany's most vivid creations — the clouds are not merely shaped like mountains but are *in motion*, passing in review like cavalry.
