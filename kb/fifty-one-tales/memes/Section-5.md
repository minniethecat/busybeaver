[005-001]A MORAL LITTLE TALE

[005-002]There was once an earnest Puritan who held it wrong to dance. And

[005-003]for his principles he labored hard, his was a zealous life. And there

[005-004]loved him all of those who hated the dance; and those that loved the

[005-005]dance respected him too; they said "He is a pure, good man and acts

[005-006]according to his lights."

[005-007]He did much to discourage dancing and helped to close several

[005-008]Sunday entertainments. Some kinds of poetry, he said, he liked, but

[005-009]not the fanciful kind as that might corrupt the thoughts of the very young.

[005-010]He always dressed in black.

[005-011]He was quite interested in morality and was quite sincere and there

[005-012]grew to be much respect on Earth for his honest face and his flowing

[005-013]pure-white beard.

[005-014]One night the Devil appeared unto him in a dream and said "Well done."

[005-015]"Avaunt," said that earnest man.

[005-016]"No, no, friend," said the Devil.

[005-017]"Dare not to call me 'friend,'" he answered bravely.

[005-018]"Come, come, friend," said the Devil. "Have you not done my work? Have

[005-019]you not put apart the couples that would dance? Have you not checked

[005-020]their laughter and their accursed mirth? Have you not worn my livery

[005-021]of black? O friend, friend, you do not know what a detestable thing

[005-022]it is to sit in hell and hear people being happy, and singing in

[005-023]theatres and singing in the fields, and whispering after dances under

[005-024]the moon," and he fell to cursing fearfully.

[005-025]"It is you," said the Puritan, "that put into their hearts the evil desire

[005-026]to dance; and black is God's own livery, not yours."

[005-027]And the Devil laughed contemptuously and spoke.

[005-028]"He only made the silly colors," he said, "and useless dawns on

[005-029]hill-slopes facing South, and butterflies flapping along them as soon

[005-030]as the sun rose high, and foolish maidens coming out to dance, and

[005-031]the warm mad West wind, and worst of all that pernicious influence

[005-032]Love."

[005-033]And when the Devil said that God made Love that earnest man

[005-034]sat up in bed and shouted "Blasphemy! Blasphemy!"

[005-035]"It's true," said the Devil. "It isn't I that send the village fools

[005-036]muttering and whispering two by two in the woods when the

[005-037]harvest moon is high, it's as much as I can bear even to see them

[005-038]dancing."

[005-039]"Then," said the man, "I have mistaken right for wrong; but as soon

[005-040]as I wake I will fight you yet."

[005-041]"O, no you don't," said the Devil. "You don't wake up out of this sleep."

[005-042]And somewhere far away Hell's black steel doors were opened, and

[005-043]arm in arm those two were drawn within, and the doors shut behind

[005-044]them and still they went arm in arm, trudging further and further into

[005-045]the deeps of Hell, and it was that Puritan's punishment to know that

[005-046]those that he cared for on Earth would do evil as he had done.

[005-047]THE RETURN OF SONG

[005-048]"The swans are singing again," said to one another the gods. And

[005-049]looking downwards, for my dreams had taken me to some fair and

[005-050]far Valhalla, I saw below me an iridescent bubble not greatly larger

[005-051]than a star shine beautifully but faintly, and up and up from it looking

[005-052]larger and larger came a flock of white, innumerable swans, singing

[005-053]and singing and singing, till it seemed as though even the gods were

[005-054]wild ships swimming in music.

[005-055]"What is it?" I said to one that was humble among the gods.

[005-056]"Only a world has ended," he said to me, "and the swans are coming

[005-057]back to the gods returning the gift of song."

[005-058]"A whole world dead!" I said.

[005-059]"Dead," said he that was humble among the gods. "The worlds are

[005-060]not for ever; only song is immortal."

[005-061]"Look! Look!" he said. "There will be a new one soon."

[005-062]And I looked and saw the larks, going down from the gods.

[005-063]SPRING IN TOWN

[005-064]At a street corner sat, and played with a wind, Winter disconsolate.

[005-065]Still tingled the fingers of the passers-by and still their breath was

[005-066]visible, and still they huddled their chins into their coats when turning

[005-067]a corner they met with a new wind, still windows lighted early sent out

[005-068]into the street the thought of romantic comfort by evening fires; these

[005-069]things still were, yet the throne of Winter tottered, and every breeze

[005-070]brought tidings of further fortresses lost on lakes or boreal hill-slopes.

[005-071]And not any longer as a king did Winter appear in those streets, as when

[005-072]the city was decked with gleaming white to greet him as a conqueror and

[005-073]he rode in with his glittering icicles and haughty retinue of prancing

[005-074]winds, but he sat there with a little wind at the corner of the street like

[005-075]some old blind beggar with his hungry dog. And as to some old blind

[005-076]beggar [[death|Death]] approaches, and the alert ears of the sightless man

[005-077]prophetically hear his far-off footfall, so there came suddenly to

[005-078]Winter's ears the sound, from some neighbouring garden, of Spring

[005-079]approaching as she walked on daisies. And Spring approaching looked

[005-080]at huddled inglorious Winter.

[005-081]"Begone," said Spring.

[005-082]"There is nothing for you to do here," said Winter to her. Nevertheless

[005-083]he drew about him his grey and battered cloak and rose and called to

[005-084]his little bitter wind and up a side street that led northward strode away.

[005-085]Pieces of paper and tall clouds of dust went with him as far as the city's

[005-086]outer gate. He turned then and called to Spring: "You can do nothing

[005-087]in this city," he said; then he marched homeward over plains and sea

[005-088]and heard his old winds howling as he marched. The ice broke up

[005-089]behind him and foundered like navies. To left and to right of him flew

[005-090]the flocks of the sea-birds, and far before him the geese's triumphant

[005-091]cry went like a clarion. Greater and greater grew his stature as he went

[005-092]northwards and ever more kingly his mien. Now he took baronies at

[005-093]a stride and now counties and came again to the snow-white frozen

[005-094]lands where the wolves came out to meet him and, draping himself

[005-095]anew with old grey clouds, strode through the gates of his invincible

[005-096]home, two old ice barriers swinging on pillars of ice that had never

[005-097]known the sun.

[005-098]So the town was left to Spring. And she peered about to see

[005-099]what she could do with it. Presently she saw a dejected dog coming

[005-100]prowling down the road, so she sang to him and he gambolled. I saw

[005-101]him next day strutting by with something of an air. Where there were

[005-102]trees she went to them and whispered, and they sang the arboreal

[005-103]song that only trees can hear, and the green buds came peeping out as

[005-104]stars while yet it is twilight, secretly one by one. She went to gardens

[005-105]and awaked from dreaming the warm maternal earth. In little patches

[005-106]bare and desolate she called up like a flame the golden crocus, or its

[005-107]purple brother like an emperor's ghost. She gladdened the graceless

[005-108]backs of untidy houses, here with a weed, there with a little grass.

[005-109]She said to the air, "Be joyous."

[005-110]Children began to know that daisies blew in unfrequented corners.

[005-111]Buttonholes began to appear in the coats of the young men. The work

[005-112]of Spring was accomplished.

[005-113]HOW THE ENEMY CAME TO THLUNRANA

[005-114]It had been prophesied of old and foreseen from the ancient days that

[005-115]its enemy would come upon Thlunrana. And the date of its doom was

[005-116]known and the gate by which it would enter, yet none had prophesied

[005-117]of the enemy who he was save that he was of the gods though he dwelt

[005-118]with men. Meanwhile Thlunrana, that secret lamaserai, that chief

[005-119]cathedral of wizardry, was the terror of the valley in which it stood

[005-120]and of all lands round about it. So narrow and high were the windows

[005-121]and so strange when lighted at night that they seemed to regard men

[005-122]with the demoniac leer of something that had a secret in the dark. Who

[005-123]were the magicians and the deputy-magicians and the great arch-wizard

[005-124]of that furtive place nobody knew, for they went veiled and hooded and

[005-125]cloaked completely in black.

[005-126]Though her doom was close upon her and the enemy of prophecy

[005-127]should come that very night through the open, southward door that

[005-128]was named the Gate of the Doom, yet that rocky edifice Thlunrana

[005-129]remained mysterious still, venerable, terrible, dark, and dreadfully

[005-130]crowned with her doom. It was not often that anyone dared wander

[005-131]near to Thlunrana by night when the moan of the magicians invoking

[005-132]we know not Whom rose faintly from inner chambers, scaring the

[005-133]drifting bats: but on the last night of all the man from the black-thatched

[005-134]cottage by the five pine-trees came, because he would see Thlunrana

[005-135]once again before the enemy that was divine, but that dwelt with men,

[005-136]should come against it and it should be no more. Up the dark valley he

[005-137]went like a bold man, but his fears were thick upon him; his bravery

[005-138]bore their weight but stooped a little beneath them. He went in at the

[005-139]southward gate that is named the Gate of the Doom. He came into a

[005-140]dark hall, and up a marble stairway passed to see the last of Thlunrana.

[005-141]At the top a curtain of black velvet hung and he passed into a chamber

[005-142]heavily hung with curtains, with a gloom in it that was blacker than

[005-143]anything they could account for. In a sombre chamber beyond, seen

[005-144]through a vacant archway, magicians with lighted tapers plied their

[005-145]wizardry and whispered incantations. All the rats in the place were

[005-146]passing away, going whimpering down the stairway. The man from

[005-147]the black-thatched cottage passed through that second chamber: the

[005-148]magicians did not look at him and did not cease to whisper. He passed

[005-149]from them through heavy curtains still of black velvet and came into a

[005-150]chamber of black marble where nothing stirred. Only one taper burned

[005-151]in the third chamber; there were no windows. On the smooth floor and

[005-152]under the smooth wall a silk pavilion stood with its curtains drawn close

[005-153]together: this was the holy of holies of that ominous place, its inner

[005-154]mystery. One on each side of it dark figures crouched, either of men

[005-155]or women or cloaked stone, or of beasts trained to be silent. When

[005-156]the awful stillness of the mystery was more than he could bear the

[005-157]man from the black-thatched cottage by the five pine-trees went up

[005-158]to the silk pavilion, and with a bold and nervous clutch of the hand

[005-159]drew one of the curtains aside, and saw the inner mystery, and laughed.

[005-160]And the prophecy was fulfilled, and Thlunrana was never more a terror

[005-161]to the valley, but the magicians passed away from their terrific halls and

[005-162]fled through the open fields wailing and beating their breasts, for

[005-163]laughter was the enemy that was doomed to come against Thlunrana

[005-164]through her southward gate (that was named the Gate of the Doom),

[005-165]and it is of the gods but dwells with man.

[005-166]A LOSING GAME

[005-167]Once in a tavern Man met face to skull with Death. Man entered

[005-168]gaily but Death gave no greeting, he sat with his jowl morosely over

[005-169]an ominous wine.

[005-170]"Come, come," said Man, "we have been antagonists long, and if I

[005-171]were losing yet I should not be surly."

[005-172]But Death remained unfriendly watching his bowl of wine and gave

[005-173]no word in answer.

[005-174]Then Man solicitously moved nearer to him and, speaking cheerily

[005-175]still, "Come, come," he said again, "you must not resent defeat."

[005-176]And still Death was gloomy and cross and sipped at his infamous

[005-177]wine and would not look up at Man and would not be companionable.

[005-178]But Man hated gloom either in beast or god, and it made him

[005-179]unhappy to see his adversary's discomfort, all the more because he

[005-180]was the cause, and still he tried to cheer him.
