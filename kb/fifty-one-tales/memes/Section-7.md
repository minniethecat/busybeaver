[007-001]THE MIST

[007-002]The mist said unto the mist: "Let us go up into the Downs." And

[007-003]the mist came up weeping.

[007-004]And the mist went into the high places and the hollows.

[007-005]And clumps of trees in the distance stood ghostly in the haze.

[007-006]But I went to a prophet, one who loved the Downs, and I said to

[007-007]him: "Why does the mist come up weeping into the Downs when it

[007-008]goes into the high places and the hollows?"

[007-009]And he answered: "The mist is the company of a multitude of souls

[007-010]who never saw the Downs, and now are dead. Therefore they come

[007-011]up weeping into the Downs, who are dead and never saw them."

[007-012]FURROW-MAKER

[007-013]He was all in black, but his friend was dressed in brown, members

[007-014]of two old families.

[007-015]"Is there any change in the way you build your houses?" said he in

[007-016]black.

[007-017]"No change," said the other. "And you?"

[007-018]"We change not," he said.

[007-019]A man went by in the distance riding a bicycle.

[007-020]"He is always changing," said the one in black, "of late almost every

[007-021]century. He is uneasy. Always changing."

[007-022]"He changes the way he builds his house, does he not?" said the

[007-023]brown one.

[007-024]"So my family say," said the other. "They say he has changed of late."

[007-025]"They say he takes much to cities?" the brown one said.

[007-026]"My cousin who lives in belfries tells me so," said the black one.

[007-027]"He says he is much in cities."

[007-028]"And there he grows lean?" said the brown one.

[007-029]"Yes, he grows lean."

[007-030]"Is it true what they say?" said the brown one.

[007-031]"Caw," said the black one.

[007-032]"Is it true that he cannot live many centuries?"

[007-033]"No, no," said the black one. "Furrow-maker will not die. We must

[007-034]not lose furrow-maker. He has been foolish of late, he has played

[007-035]with smoke and is sick. His engines have wearied him and his cities

[007-036]are evil. Yes, he is very sick. But in a few centuries he will forget

[007-037]his folly and we shall not lose furrow-maker. [[time|Time]] out of mind he

[007-038]has delved and my family have got their food from the raw earth

[007-039]behind him. He will not die."

[007-040]"But they say, do they not?" said the brown one, "his cities are

[007-041]noisome, and that he grows sick in them and can run no longer, and

[007-042]that it is with him as it is with us when we grow too many, and the

[007-043]grass has the bitter taste in the rainy season, and our young grow

[007-044]bloated and die."

[007-045]"Who says it?" replied the black one.

[007-046]"Pigeon," the brown one answered. "He came back all dirty.

[007-047]And Hare went down to the edge of the cities once. He says it

[007-048]too. Man was too sick to chase him. He thinks that Man will die,

[007-049]and his wicked friend Dog with him. Dog, he will die. That nasty

[007-050]fellow Dog. He will die too, the dirty fellow!"

[007-051]"Pigeon and Hare!" said the black one. "We shall not lose

[007-052]furrow-maker."

[007-053]"Who told you he will not die?" his brown friend said.

[007-054]"Who told me!" the black one said. "My family and his have

[007-055]understood each other times out of mind. We know what follies

[007-056]will kill each other and what each may survive, and I say that

[007-057]furrow-maker will not die."

[007-058]"He will die," said the brown one.

[007-059]"Caw," said the other.

[007-060]And Man said in his heart: "Just one invention more. There is

[007-061]something I want to do with petrol yet, and then I will give it all

[007-062]up and go back to the woods."

[007-063]LOBSTER SALAD

[007-064]I was climbing round the perilous outside of the Palace of

[007-065]Colquonhombros. So far below me that in the tranquil twilight

[007-066]and clear air of those lands I could only barely see them lay the

[007-067]craggy tops of the mountains.

[007-068]It was along no battlements or terrace edge I was climbing, but

[007-069]on the sheer face of the wall itself, getting what foothold I could

[007-070]where the boulders joined.

[007-071]Had my feet been bare I was done, but though I was in my

[007-072]night-shirt I had on stout leather boots, and their edges somehow

[007-073]held in those narrow cracks. My fingers and wrists were aching.

[007-074]Had it been possible to stop for a moment I might have been lured

[007-075]to give a second look at the fearful peaks of the mountains down

[007-076]there in the twilight, and this must have been fatal.

[007-077]That the thing was all a dream is beside the point. We have fallen

[007-078]in dreams before, but it is well known that if in one of those falls

[007-079]you ever hit the ground--you die: I had looked at those menacing

[007-080]mountaintops and knew well that such a fall as the one I feared

[007-081]must have such a termination. Then I went on.

[007-082]It is strange what different sensations there can be in different

[007-083]boulders--every one gleaming with the same white light and every

[007-084]one chosen to match the rest by minions of ancient kings--when

[007-085]your life depends on the edges of every one you come to. Those

[007-086]edges seemed strangely different. It was of no avail to overcome

[007-087]the terror of one, for the next would give you a hold in quite a

[007-088]different way or hand you over to death in a different manner.

[007-089]Some were too sharp to hold and some too flush with the wall,

[007-090]those whose hold was the best crumbled the soonest; each rock

[007-091]had its different terror: and then there were those things that followed

[007-092]behind me.

[007-093]And at last I came to a breach made long ago by earthquake,

[007-094]lightning or war: I should have had to go down a thousand feet

[007-095]to get round it and they would come up with me while I was doing

[007-096]that, for certain sable apes that I have not mentioned as yet, things

[007-097]that had tigerish teeth and were born and bred on that wall, had

[007-098]pursued me all the evening. In any case I could have gone no

[007-099]farther, nor did I know what the king would do along whose wall

[007-100]I was climbing. It was time to drop and be done with it or stop and

[007-101]await those apes.

[007-102]And then it was that I remembered a pin, thrown carelessly down

[007-103]out of an evening-tie in another world to the one where grew that

[007-104]glittering wall, and lying now if no evil chance had removed it on a

[007-105]chest of drawers by my bed. The apes were very close, and hurrying,

[007-106]for they knew my fingers were slipping, and the cruel peaks of those

[007-107]infernal mountains seemed surer of me than the apes. I reached out

[007-108]with a desperate effort of will towards where the pin lay on the chest

[007-109]of drawers. I groped about. I found it! I ran it into my arm. Saved!

[007-110]THE RETURN OF THE EXILES

[007-111]The old man with a hammer and the one-eyed man with a spear were

[007-112]seated by the roadside talking as I came up the hill.

[007-113]"It isn't as though they hadn't asked us," the one with the hammer said.

[007-114]"There ain't no more than twenty as knows about it," said the other.

[007-115]"Twenty's twenty," said the first.

[007-116]"After all these years," said the one-eyed man with the spear. "After

[007-117]all these years. We might go back just once."

[007-118]"O' course we might," said the other.

[007-119]Their clothes were old even for laborers, the one with the hammer

[007-120]had a leather apron full of holes and blackened, and their hands

[007-121]looked like leather. But whatever they were they were English, and

[007-122]this was pleasant to see after all the motors that had passed me that

[007-123]day with their burden of mixed and doubtful nationalities.

[007-124]When they saw me the one with the hammer touched his greasy cap.

[007-125]"Might we make so bold, sir," he said, "as the ask the way to

[007-126]Stonehenge?"

[007-127]"We never ought to go," mumbled the other plaintively. "There's

[007-128]not more than twenty as knows, but...."

[007-129]I was bicycling there myself to see the place so I pointed out the

[007-130]way and rode on at once, for there was something so utterly servile

[007-131]about them both that I did not care for their company. They seemed

[007-132]by their wretched mien to have been persecuted or utterly neglected

[007-133]for many years, I thought that very likely they had done long terms

[007-134]of penal servitude.

[007-135]When I came to Stonehenge I saw a group of about a score of men

[007-136]standing among the stones. They asked me with some solemnity if

[007-137]I was expecting anyone, and when I said No they spoke to me no

[007-138]more. It was three miles back where I left those strange old men,

[007-139]but I had not been in the stone circle long when they appeared,

[007-140]coming with great strides along the road. When they saw them all

[007-141]the people took off their hats and acted very strangely, and I saw

[007-142]that they had a goat which they led up then to the old altar stone.

[007-143]And the two old men came up with their hammer and spear and

[007-144]began apologizing plaintively for the liberty they had taken in coming

[007-145]back to that place, and all the people knelt on the grass before them.

[007-146]And then still kneeling they killed the goat by the altar, and when the

[007-147]two old men saw this they came up with many excuses and eagerly

[007-148]sniffed the blood. And at first this made them happy. But soon the

[007-149]one with the spear began to whimper. "It used to be men," he

[007-150]lamented. "It used to be men."

[007-151]And the twenty men began looking uneasily at each other, and the

[007-152]plaint of the one-eyed man went on in that tearful voice, and all of

[007-153]a sudden they all looked at me. I do not know who the two old men

[007-154]were or what any of them were doing, but there are moments when

[007-155]it is clearly time to go, and I left them there and then. And just as I

[007-156]got up on to my bicycle I heard the plaintive voice of the one with the

[007-157]hammer apologizing for the liberty he had taken in coming back to

[007-158]Stonehenge.

[007-159]"But after all these years," I heard him crying, "After all these

[007-160]years...."

[007-161]And the one with the spear said: "Yes, after three thousand years...."

[007-162]NATURE AND TIME

[007-163]Through the streets of Coventry one winter's night strode a

[007-164]triumphant spirit. Behind him stooping, unkempt, utterly ragged,

[007-165]wearing the clothes and look that outcasts have, whining, weeping,

[007-166]reproaching, an ill-used spirit tried to keep pace with him. Continually

[007-167]she plucked him by the sleeve and cried out to him as she panted

[007-168]after and he strode resolute on.

[007-169]It was a bitter night, yet it did not seem to be the cold that she feared,

[007-170]ill-clad though she was, but the trams and the ugly shops and the glare

[007-171]of the factories, from which she continually winced as she hobbled on,

[007-172]and the pavement hurt her feet.

[007-173]He that strode on in front seemed to care for nothing, it might be hot

[007-174]or cold, silent or noisy, pavement or open fields, he merely had the

[007-175]air of striding on.

[007-176]And she caught up and clutched him by the elbow. I heard her

[007-177]speak in her unhappy voice, you scarcely heard it for the noise of

[007-178]the traffic.

[007-179]"You have forgotten me," she complained to him. "You have forsaken

[007-180]me here."

[007-181]She pointed to Coventry with a wide wave of her arm and seemed

[007-182]to indicate other cities beyond. And he gruffly told her to keep

[007-183]pace with him and that he did not forsake her. And she went on

[007-184]with her pitiful lamentation.

[007-185]"My anemones are dead for miles," she said, "all my woods are

[007-186]fallen and still the cities grow. My child Man is unhappy and my other

[007-187]children are dying, and still the cities grow and you have forgotten me!"
