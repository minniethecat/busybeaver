## Overview

Persepolis, the ceremonial capital of the Achaemenid Persian Empire, appears in the Fifty-One Tales as the last in the catalogue of fallen ancient cities. In Dunsany's vision, it makes "a darkness round" itself on Oblivion's sea — a city whose ruin is so profound that it occludes the surrounding deeps.

## Appearances

### Section 2 (Preface)

In the cosmic vision of Oblivion's sea:

> the sodden bulk of sunken Tyre and make a darkness round Persepolis. [002-017]

The image of Persepolis generating darkness is distinctive among the four fallen cities. [[babylon|Babylon]] drifts; [[nineveh|Nineveh]] is barely recognizable; [[tyre|Tyre]] is a sodden bulk. But Persepolis *radiates* — it "makes a darkness round" itself, as though its ruin is an active force rather than a passive state.

### *Nature and Time*

In [[time|Time]]'s self-justification to [[nature|Nature]]:

> "Where is Persepolis that troubled you? Where Tarshish and Tyre? And you have said I forget you." [008-005]–[008-007]

Persepolis is characterized as having "troubled" [[nature|Nature]] — an unusual verb for a city. It suggests that Persepolis was not merely a city but an irritant, a disturbance in the natural order that required [[time|Time]]'s intervention to remove.

## Thematic Significance

Persepolis occupies a special position among the fallen cities. Where [[babylon|Babylon]] is known for winged bulls (sculpture) and [[tyre|Tyre]] for fleets (commerce), Persepolis is associated with darkness — an almost metaphysical quality. The city that "troubled" [[nature|Nature]] and now darkens the sea suggests a place of particular grandeur, whose erasure was correspondingly more dramatic.

Historically, Persepolis was burned by Alexander the Great in 330 BCE — an act of deliberate destruction that may inform Dunsany's treatment of it as a city whose fall was more than mere decay.
