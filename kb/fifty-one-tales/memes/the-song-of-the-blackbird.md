## Overview

"The Song of the Blackbird" is a comic fable about the gap between inspiration and expression. A poet, hearing a blackbird's song, asks the bird — in bird language — how he does it. The blackbird's answer is a rambling, digressive, warmly personal account of a she-blackbird, a spring day, and the unremarkable circumstances from which the song emerged. The tale gently mocks the Romantic idea of inspiration as mystical visitation, replacing it with something more domestic and, in its own way, more beautiful.

## Summary

As a poet passes a thorn-tree, a blackbird sings. The poet, who knows bird language, asks the secret of the song [008-001].

The blackbird's reply occupies most of the tale. It was last Spring, he says, and there was the most beautiful she-blackbird the world has ever seen — her eyes blacker than lakes at night, her feathers blacker than night itself, her beak impossibly yellow, her speed faster than lightning [008-001]. He did not dare go near her because she was so wonderful.

One day when it grew warm, thinking how extraordinary it was merely to have seen her, he began to sing — and the song came out. The blackbird's account is full of digressions about berries, the weather, the precise quality of the light, and the exact sensation of being a blackbird on that particular day [008-001].

When the blackbird finishes, the poet says nothing. The tale implies, without stating, that the poet has learned something about the ordinariness of inspiration — that a song comes not from mystical transport but from a particular bird, a particular day, a particular feeling too powerful to contain.

## Analysis

### The Comic Anti-Climax

Dunsany sets up a classic Romantic scene — poet, thorn-tree, birdsong — and then systematically deflates it. The blackbird does not speak of the Muses or the sublime; he speaks of berries, weather, and a she-blackbird who was "not an ordinary she-blackbird." The contrast between the poet's implied expectations and the blackbird's earthy reality is the tale's central joke.

### Inspiration Demystified

The tale's argument, if it can be said to have one, is that art comes from life — specific, ordinary, irreplaceable life. The blackbird's song is beautiful, but its origin is a particular spring, a particular bird, a particular feeling. The poet, who came seeking a technique, receives instead a story. The implication is that stories are the technique.

## Characters

- **The poet** — a nameless figure who understands bird language and seeks the secret of the blackbird's song. He serves as the reader's surrogate, asking the question the reader might ask.
- **The blackbird** — the tale's true protagonist, a garrulous and warm-hearted narrator whose account of his song's origin is digressive, personal, and utterly convincing.

## Themes

- **[[poetry]] and creation** — the tale demystifies artistic inspiration, locating it in lived experience rather than divine visitation.
- **[[beauty]]** — the she-blackbird's beauty is the efficient cause of the song, but the blackbird's description of her is itself a kind of poetry.
- **The ordinary** — Dunsany finds the sublime not in grand abstractions but in a bird's memory of a spring day.
