[008-001]And then he turned angrily on her, almost stopping in that stride of

[008-002]his that began when the stars were made.

[008-003]"When have I ever forgotten you?" he said, "or when forsaken you

[008-004]ever? Did I not throw down Babylon for you? And is not Nineveh

[008-005]gone? Where is Persepolis that troubled you? Where Tarshish and

[008-006]Tyre? And you have said I forget you."

[008-007]And at this she seemed to take a little comfort. I heard her speak

[008-008]once more, looking wistfully at her companion. "When will the fields

[008-009]come back and the grass for my children?"

[008-010]"Soon, soon," he said: then they were silent. And he strode away,

[008-011]she limping along behind him, and all the clocks in the towers chimed

[008-012]as he passed.

[008-013]THE SONG OF THE BLACKBIRD

[008-014]As the poet passed the thorn-tree the blackbird sang.

[008-015]"How ever do you do it?" the poet said, for he knew bird language.

[008-016]"It was like this," said the blackbird. "It really was the most

[008-017]extraordinary thing. I made that song last Spring, it came to me

[008-018]all of a sudden. There was the most beautiful she-blackbird that

[008-019]the world has ever seen. Her eyes were blacker than lakes are at

[008-020]night, her feathers were blacker than the night itself, and nothing was

[008-021]as yellow as her beak; she could fly much faster than the lightning.

[008-022]She was not an ordinary she-blackbird, there has never been any

[008-023]other like her at all. I did not dare go near her because she was so

[008-024]wonderful. One day last Spring when it got warm again--it had been

[008-025]cold, we ate berries, things were quite different then, but Spring came

[008-026]and it got warm--one day I was thinking how wonderful she was and

[008-027]it seemed so extraordinary to think that I should ever have seen her,

[008-028]the only really wonderful she-blackbird in the world, that I opened

[008-029]my beak to give a shout, and then this song came, and there had

[008-030]never been anything like it before, and luckily I remembered it, the

[008-031]very song that I sang just now. But what is so extraordinary, the most

[008-032]amazing occurence of that marvellous day, was that no sooner had I

[008-033]sung the song than that very bird, the most wonderful she-blackbird

[008-034]in the world, flew right up to me and sat quite close to me on the same

[008-035]tree. I never remember such wonderful times as those.

[008-036]"Yes, the song came in a moment, and as I was saying...."

[008-037]And an old wanderer walking with a stick came by and the blackbird

[008-038]flew away, and the poet told the old man the blackbird's wonderful

[008-039]story.

[008-040]"That song new?" said the wanderer. "Not a bit of it. God made it

[008-041]years ago. All the blackbirds used to sing it when I was young. It

[008-042]was new then."

[008-043]THE MESSENGERS

[008-044]One wandering nigh Parnassus chasing hares heard the high Muses.

[008-045]"Take us a message to the Golden Town."

[008-046]Thus sang the Muses.

[008-047]But the man said: "They do not call to me. Not to such as me speak

[008-048]the Muses."

[008-049]And the Muses called him by name.

[008-050]"Take us a message," they said, "to the Golden Town."

[008-051]And the man was downcast for he would have chased hares.

[008-052]And the Muses called again.

[008-053]And when whether in valleys or on high crags of the hills he still

[008-054]heard the Muses he went at last to them and heard their message,

[008-055]though he would fain have left it to other men and chased the fleet

[008-056]hares still in happy valleys.

[008-057]And they gave him a wreath of laurels carved out of emeralds as

[008-058]only the Muses can carve. "By this," they said, "they shall know that

[008-059]you come from the Muses."

[008-060]And the man went from that place and dressed in scarlet silks

[008-061]as befitted one that came from the high Muses. And through the

[008-062]gateway of the Golden Town he ran and cried his message, and his

[008-063]cloak floated behind him. All silent sat the wise men and the aged,

[008-064]they of the Golden Town; cross-legged they sat before their houses

[008-065]reading from parchments a message of the Muses that they sent long

[008-066]before.

[008-067]And the young man cried his message from the Muses.

[008-068]And they rose up and said: "Thou art not from the Muses. Otherwise

[008-069]spake they." And they stoned him and he died.

[008-070]And afterwards they carved his message upon gold; and read it in

[008-071]their temples on holy days.

[008-072]When will the Muses rest? When are they weary? They sent

[008-073]another messenger to the Golden Town. And they gave him a

[008-074]wand of ivory to carry in his hand with all the beautiful stories of

[008-075]the world wondrously carved thereon. And only the Muses could

[008-076]have carved it. "By this," they said, "they shall know that you come

[008-077]from the Muses."

[008-078]And he came through the gateway of the Golden Town with the

[008-079]message he had for its people. And they rose up at once in the

[008-080]Golden street, they rose from reading the message that they had

[008-081]carved upon gold. "The last who came," they said, "came with a

[008-082]wreath of laurels carved out of emeralds, as only the Muses can

[008-083]carve. You are not from the Muses." And even as they had stoned

[008-084]the last so also they stoned him. And afterwards they carved his

[008-085]message on gold and laid it up in their temples.

[008-086]When will the Muses rest? When are they weary? Even yet once

[008-087]again they sent a messenger under the gateway into the Golden

[008-088]Town. And for all that he wore a garland of gold that the high Muses

[008-089]gave him, a garland of kingcups soft and yellow on his head, yet

[008-090]fashioned of pure gold and by whom but the Muses, yet did they

[008-091]stone him in the Golden Town. But they had the message, and what

[008-092]care the Muses?

[008-093]And yet they will not rest, for some while since I heard them call to me.

[008-094]"Go take our message," they said, "unto the Golden Town."

[008-095]But I would not go. And they spake a second time. "Go take our

[008-096]message," they said.

[008-097]And still I would not go, and they cried out a third time: "Go take

[008-098]our message."

[008-099]And though they cried a third time I would not go. But morning and

[008-100]night they cried and through long evenings.

[008-101]When will the Muses rest? When are they weary? And when they

[008-102]would not cease to call to me I went to them and I said: "The

[008-103]Golden Town is the Golden Town no longer. They have sold their

[008-104]pillars for brass and their temples for money, they have made coins

[008-105]out of their golden doors. It is become a dark town full of trouble,

[008-106]there is no ease in its streets, beauty has left it and the old songs are

[008-107]gone."

[008-108]"Go take our message," they cried.

[008-109]And I said to the high Muses: "You do not understand. You have

[008-110]no message for the Golden Town, the holy city no longer."

[008-111]"Go take our message," they cried.

[008-112]"What is your message?" I said to the high Muses.

[008-113]And when I heard their message I made excuses, dreading to speak

[008-114]such things in the Golden Town; and again they bade me go.

[008-115]And I said: "I will not go. None will believe me."

[008-116]And still the Muses cry to me all night long.

[008-117]They do not understand. How should they know?

[008-118]THE THREE TALL SONS

[008-119]And at last Man raised on high the final glory of his civilization,

[008-120]the towering edifice of the ultimate city.

[008-121]Softly beneath him in the deeps of the earth purred his machinery

[008-122]fulfilling all his needs, there was no more toil for man. There he sat

[008-123]at ease discussing the Sex Problem.

[008-124]And sometimes painfully out of forgotten fields, there came to his

[008-125]outer door, came to the furthest rampart of the final glory of Man,

[008-126]a poor old woman begging. And always they turned her away.

[008-127]This glory of Man's achievement, this city was not for her.

[008-128]It was Nature that came thus begging in from the fields, whom they

[008-129]always turned away.

[008-130]And away she went again alone to her fields.

[008-131]And one day she came again, and again they sent her hence. But

[008-132]her three tall sons came too.

[008-133]"These shall go in," she said. "Even these my sons to your city."

[008-134]And the three tall sons went in.

[008-135]And these are Nature's sons, the forlorn one's terrible children,

[008-136]War, Famine and Plague.

[008-137]Yea and they went in there and found Man unawares in his city

[008-138]still poring over his Problems, obsessed with his civilization, and

[008-139]never hearing their tread as those three came up behind.

[008-140]COMPROMISE

[008-141]They built their gorgeous home, their city of glory, above the lair

[008-142]of the earthquake. They built it of marble and gold in the shining

[008-143]youth of the world. There they feasted and fought and called their

[008-144]city immortal, and danced and sang songs to the gods. None heeded

[008-145]the earthquake in all those joyous streets. And down in the deeps

[008-146]of the earth, on the black feet of the abyss, they that would conquer

[008-147]Man mumbled long in the darkness, mumbled and goaded the

[008-148]earthquake to try his strength with that city, to go forth blithely at

[008-149]night and to gnaw its pillars like bones. And down in those grimy

[008-150]deeps the earthquake answered them, and would not do their

[008-151]pleasure and would not stir from thence, for who knew who they

[008-152]were who danced all day where he rumbled, and what if the lords

[008-153]of that city that had no fear of his anger were haply even the gods!

[008-154]And the centuries plodded by, on and on round the world, and one

[008-155]day they that had danced, they that had sung in that city, remembered

[008-156]the lair of the earthquake in the deeps down under their feet, and made

[008-157]plans one with another and sought to avert the danger, sought to

[008-158]appease the earthquake and turn his anger away.

[008-159]They sent down singing girls, and priests with oats and wine, they

[008-160]sent down garlands and propitious berries, down by dark steps to

[008-161]the black depths of the earth, they sent peacocks newly slain, and

[008-162]boys with burning spices, and their thin white sacred cats with collars

[008-163]of pearls all newly drawn from sea, they sent huge diamonds down in

[008-164]coffers of teak, and ointment and strange oriental dyes, arrows and

[008-165]armor and the rings of their queen.

[008-166]"Oho," said the earthquake in the coolth of the earth, "so they are

[008-167]not the gods."

[008-173]THE TOMB OF PAN

[008-174]"Seeing," they said, "that [[pan|old-time Pan]] is dead, let us now make

[008-175]a tomb for him and a monument, that the dreadful worship of long

[008-176]ago may be remembered and avoided by all."

[008-177]So said the people of the enlightened lands. And they built a

[008-178]white and mighty tomb of marble. Slowly it rose under the hands

[008-179]of the builders and longer every evening after sunset it gleamed with

[008-180]rays of the departed sun.

[008-181]And many mourned for Pan while the builders built; many reviled

[008-182]him. Some called the builders to cease and to weep for Pan and

[008-183]others called them to leave no memorial at all of so infamous a god.

[008-184]But the builders built on steadily.

[008-185]And one day all was finished, and the tomb stood there like a

[008-186]steep sea-cliff. And Pan was carved thereon with humbled head

[008-187]and the feet of angels pressed upon his neck. And when the tomb

[008-188]was finished the sun had already set, but the afterglow was rosy on

[008-189]the huge bulk of Pan.

[008-190]And presently all the enlightened people came, and saw the tomb

[008-191]and remembered Pan who was dead, and all deplored him and his

[008-192]wicked age. But a few wept apart because of the death of Pan.
