## Overview

In *Fifty-One Tales*, the angel appears not as the gentle messenger of conventional piety but as a formidable agent of divine agency — a builder of Hell who wields a hammer rather than a harp, a cosmic surveyor who regards human kingdoms as food for worms, and a sculpted conqueror whose feet press upon a dead god's neck. Dunsany's angel is an ironic figure: a "Minister of Grace" (006-150) whose work is the construction of eternal punishment, adapted to the particular sins of the modern industrial age. The treatment strips away sentimentality to reveal a cosmic justice that is unyielding, precise, and terrifyingly contemporary.

### The Angel as Builder of Hell

The angel's definitive appearance occurs in *[[the-reward|The Reward]]*, where the narrator's dreaming spirit wanders from a factory city to "the edge of Hell" (006-140). There he finds:

> "A huge angel with a hammer building in plaster and steel."
> (006-142)

When asked what he is constructing, the angel replies with bureaucratic matter-of-factness: "We are adding to Hell, to keep pace with the times" (006-145). The narrator, speaking in the moral idiom of his "compromising age and weakening country" (006-147), protests: "It won't be as bad as the old hell, will it?" The angel's reply — "Worse" (006-148) — is delivered without elaboration, as if the severity requires no justification.

The new Hell's torment is revealed to be a flaming advertisement: "Yeasto, the great new yeast, it builds up body and brain, and something more" (006-154), changing color every fifteen seconds. The angel's verdict is absolute:

> "They shall look at it for ever."
> (006-158)

When the narrator points out that the yeast merchants "drove a perfectly legitimate trade" and "the law allowed it" (006-161), the angel does not engage in theological debate. He "went on hammering into place the huge steel uprights" (006-161). The angel's only stated respite underscores the moral calculus:

> "I rested one Christmas Day, and looked and saw little children dying of cancer. I shall go on now until the fires are lit."
> (006-164)

Here, the angel's relentless labor is not cruelty but measure-for-measure justice — the suffering of innocents repaid by eternal exposure to the advertisement that profited from their harm. The angel makes no further answer: "And the angel made no answer but went on building his hell" (006-170). The final image is of ongoing, silent construction — divine judgment as industrial process, unceasing and immune to human rationalization.

### The Angel as Cosmic Surveyor

In *[[the-worm-and-the-angel|The Worm and the Angel]]*, the angel appears in a different mode — as a contemplative witness to the scale of mortal achievement:

> "As he crawled from the tombs of the fallen a worm met with an angel. And together they looked upon the kings and kingdoms, and youths and maidens and the cities of men."
> (003-031)

The [[the-worm|worm]] claims all of human civilization as its food: "Behold my food" (003-037). The angel does not dispute the claim but murmurs a line of Homer — "Be dakeon para Thina poluphloisboio Thalassaes" — and asks, "and can you destroy that too?" (003-039). The worm "paled in his anger to a greyness ill to behold, for for three thousand years he had tried to destroy that line and still its melody was ringing in his head" (003-042).

In this tale, the angel is not an agent of judgment but a witness to the contest between decay and art. The worm can consume kings and kingdoms, but the Homeric line — a fragment of song — resists destruction. The angel's role is to pose the question that reveals the worm's limitation: matter decays, but melody endures.

### The Angel at Pan's Tomb

The angel makes a final, silent appearance in *[[the-tomb-of-pan|The Tomb of Pan]]*. The builders carve Pan's image on a marble tomb, and:

> "Pan was carved thereon with humbled head and the feet of angels pressed upon his neck."
> (008-187)

Here the angel is not an active agent but a sculpted symbol of conquest — the new divine order triumphing over the old. [[pan|Pan]], the dying god of the old world, is pinned beneath angelic feet. The image is ambiguous: is this liberation from "dreadful worship" (008-175) or the suppression of a wilder, more joyful divinity? The angel's presence on the tomb signals the victory of the "enlightened" order, but Dunsany's treatment of that order elsewhere suggests the victory may be pyrrhic.

### The Angel in Italian Painting

A gentler vision of angels appears briefly in the collection, in a description of smoke turned golden by the sun: "so that it looked like an old Italian picture where angels walk in the foreground and the rest is a blaze of gold" (002-178). This is the angel of Renaissance art — decorative, luminous, associated with beauty rather than judgment. It is the only moment in the collection where angels appear in their conventional iconographic role, and it passes as quickly as a drift of smoke.
