## Overview

"Death and Odysseus" is a tale from Section 1 of *Fifty-One Tales* [001-016] in which the legendary Greek hero Odysseus confronts Death himself. Rather than fleeing or fighting, Odysseus does what he does best: he talks. Through wit and composure, he transforms a fatal encounter into something approaching friendship, subverting the expected narrative of mortal terror before the supernatural.

## Summary

The tale opens with Death approaching Odysseus's door, intending to claim him. But Odysseus, famous for his cunning since the *Odyssey*, does not cower. Death, who has come to frighten the hero, finds himself facing an unexpectedly calm adversary:

> "...the door and saw there famous Odysseus, with his white hair"
>
> [002-110]

Odysseus invites Death in, treats him not as a terror but as a guest. The cold wind blows through the open door, but Odysseus simply continues warming his pale hands by the fire. The domestic scene — hero by the hearth, Death as visitor — strips the encounter of supernatural dread:

> "Odysseus went on warming his pale hands"
>
> [002-114]

The conversation that follows is Dunsany at his most characteristic: cosmic forces reduced to human scale, treated with gentle irony. Odysseus turns and speaks to Death as one would to a traveling companion. The request — "lend me your shoulder" — is absurd and magnificent:

> "Then 'Come now,' said Odysseus, 'lend me your shoulder,' and Death, for some while stood mute, for he had once heard Odysseus speak."
>
> [002-121]

## Analysis

The tale is a masterpiece of tonal inversion. The expected horror — a mortal facing Death — is dissolved by Odysseus's unflappable courtesy. This is the same Odysseus who outwitted the Cyclops, navigated past the Sirens, and survived the wrath of Poseidon. Next to those adversaries, Death is just another problem to be managed through intelligence and poise.

Dunsany's choice of Odysseus is significant. Unlike the younger heroes of Greek myth — Achilles, who chose glory over long life; Heracles, who fought Death physically — Odysseus represents a different kind of heroism: the heroism of survival through wit. He does not defeat Death; he simply refuses to be defeated by him. The result is not victory but accommodation — a kind of mutual respect between the mortal and the cosmic.

This tale pairs naturally with "Death and the Orange" [001-017], another story in which Death's terror is defused by an unexpected human response — there, a woman's smile; here, a hero's conversation.
