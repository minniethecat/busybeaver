## Overview

"The Lonely Idol" is one of Dunsany's most personal and elegiac tales, an extended prayer addressed not to a high god but to a forgotten trinket — a "little swine-faced idol" carved from pale-green stone. The narrator's prayer is not for intercession or favour; it is a lament for transience itself. Framed as an act of pity for a god who has outlived his worshippers, the tale becomes a meditation on beauty, memory, and the autumnal drift of all mortal things.

## Summary

The narrator receives from a friend "an old outlandish stone, a little swine-faced idol to whom no one prayed" [006-079]. Seeing the idol's melancholy condition — cross-legged, holding a broken scourge, abandoned by worshippers and sacrifice alike — he takes pity on it. He recalls that it had once been a god, and determines to pray to it "as perhaps they prayed long since, before the coming of the strange dark ships."

His prayer is a single sustained paragraph, a cascade of things that do not last. He names the fading of cheeks and hair, the dying of beloved ones, the brittleness of beauty, the remoteness of fame. He speaks of "leaves, leaves falling, everywhere falling; there is autumn among men, autumn and reaping." He mourns that "all that is beautiful hath not remained but is even as the glory of morning upon the water."

The prayer reaches its climax with a lament for memory itself:

> "Even our memories are gathered too with the sound of the ancient voices, the pleasant ancient voices that come to our ears no more; the very gardens of our childhood fade, and there dims with the speed of the years even the mind's own eye."
>
> [006-079]

The tale ends without resolution or divine response — only the image of the narrator, humbled before a god no one else will pray to, offering words that ask for nothing because nothing can be given.

## Analysis

The tale inverts the normal logic of prayer. The narrator does not approach the idol to ask for something — health, wealth, protection. He approaches because the idol is lonely, and prayer, even prayer without petition, is a form of companionship. The idol is pitiable not because it is powerless but because it is forgotten; the narrator's act is not worship but witness.

Dunsany's catalogue of transience is relentless but never strident. Each loss is named simply — fading cheeks, greying hair, dying beloveds, falling leaves — and the accumulation produces a quiet devastation. The prayer's structure mirrors its argument: the losses are not arranged by importance but by rhythm, one following another like waves, each pulling a little more of the world away.

The "strange dark ships" that displaced the idol's original worshippers are a ghost of history — perhaps Phoenician traders, perhaps Roman galleys, perhaps something older still. Dunsany does not specify, and the vagueness enlarges the tale: the idol is not merely a colonial-era artefact but a remnant of a genuinely lost world, a god whose name and cult are beyond recovery.

The final movement — the dimming of the "mind's own eye" — is the tale's deepest cut. External losses (beauty, youth, loved ones) are wounds; the loss of the ability even to picture the gardens of childhood is an amputation. The prayer becomes an act of defiance against this final fading: to say the memories aloud is to hold them a moment longer against the speed of the years.

## Characters

- **The narrator** — a compassionate observer who prays out of pity rather than faith. His prayer is a literary act as much as a devotional one.
- **The idol** — a "little swine-faced" stone figurine, pale-green, cross-legged, holding a broken scourge. Silent, motionless, it is a god who has become a relic — and is, for that very reason, the perfect recipient of a prayer about loss.
