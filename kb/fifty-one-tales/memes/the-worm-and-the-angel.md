## Overview

"The Worm and the Angel" is a short allegorical tale from Section 1 of *Fifty-One Tales* [001-022]. It stages an encounter between the lowest of earthly creatures — a worm — and a being of pure celestial origin — an [[angel|angel]]. The stark contrast between the two figures creates a meditation on scale, significance, and the possibility of dignity even in the most humble existence.

## Summary

The tale opens with an image of emergence and encounter:

> As he crawled from the tombs of the fallen a worm met with an angel.
>
> [001-022]

Together they survey the entire panorama of human existence: "kings and kingdoms, and youths and maidens and the cities of men" [001-023]. They see old men in their chairs, children singing in the fields, far wars, walled towns, wisdom, wickedness, and "the pomp of kings, and the people of all the lands that the sunlight knew" [001-024].

The worm speaks first, claiming all of it as its food: "Behold my food" [001-028].

The angel's response is one of the collection's most striking moments. Walking by the sea, the angel murmurs a line of Homer in Greek: "Be dakeon para Thina poluphloisboio Thalassaes" [001-029] — the resonant Homeric phrase for the loud-resounding sea — and asks: "and can you destroy that too?"

The worm pales in anger "to a greyness ill to behold" [001-031], for it has tried for three thousand years to destroy that line, and still its melody rings in its head [001-032].

## Analysis

Dunsany's choice of the worm as protagonist is characteristic of his democratic mysticism. Throughout *Fifty-One Tales*, he consistently dignifies the small, the overlooked, the mundane. The worm has no heroic qualities — it cannot speak eloquently, cannot fight, cannot think in any recognizable sense. And yet it exists, and its existence is acknowledged even by angels.

> And the worm spake to the angel saying: "Behold my food."
>
> "Be dakeon para Thina poluphloisboio Thalassaes," murmured the angel, for they walked by the sea, "and can you destroy that too?"
>
> And the worm paled in his anger to a greyness ill to behold, for for three thousand years he had tried to destroy that line and still its melody was ringing in his head.
>
> [001-028]

The tale's brevity — it is among the shortest in the collection — mirrors its subject. Like the worm, the tale itself is small. And like the worm, it contains within its smallness a complete vision of the cosmic order. The angel and the worm, heaven and earth, light and darkness, the immortal line of poetry and the devouring mouth of oblivion — all the great polarities of existence are present in this miniature.

The tale also resonates with the collection's broader concern with the relationship between the eternal and the mortal. Unlike [[death-and-odysseus|Death and Odysseus]], where a mortal hero engages a cosmic force as an equal, "The Worm and the Angel" presents a relationship of pure hierarchy. The worm does not converse with the angel; it simply is, and the angel simply is, and the distance between them is the measure of creation itself.

But the tale's final irony complicates the hierarchy. The worm claims all kingdoms and cities as its food — and in the long run, it is right. Empires fall, civilizations crumble, all mortal things return to the earth where the worm waits. Yet the one thing the worm cannot consume is a line of poetry. The angel's question — "can you destroy that too?" — is answered by the worm's impotent anger. Art, in Dunsany's vision, is the one thing that outlasts both empires and the creatures that feed on them.

This makes the tale a companion piece to [[the-raft-builders|The Raft-Builders]] [001-226], where Homer's raft for Helen still floats on Oblivion's sea while Babylon and Nineveh have sunk. In both tales, the word outlasts the world.

## Characters

- **The Worm** — the humblest earthly creature, crawling from the tombs of the fallen. It claims all mortal things as its food — and in the long run of [[time|Time]], it is right. But it cannot consume immortal art.
- **The Angel** — celestial being, representing the heights of the cosmic order. Unlike the hammer-wielding angel of [[the-reward|The Reward]] [006-142], this angel is a witness and guardian, defending a line of Homer against the worm of oblivion.

## The Worm in Literary Tradition

Dunsany's worm belongs to a long tradition of humble creatures serving as vehicles for cosmic meditation. From the "poor beetle" in Shakespeare's *Measure for Measure* ("The sense of death is most in apprehension; / And the poor beetle, that we tread upon, / In corporal sufferance finds a pang as great / As when a giant dies") to Blake's "worm that flies in the night," the worm has been a symbol of mortality, humility, and the persistence of life at its most basic level.

Dunsany's innovation is to pair the worm not with a human observer but with an angel — a being as far above humanity as humanity is above the worm. This removes the comfortable middle ground of human perspective. We are not looking down at the worm; we are suspended between the worm and the angel, uncertain of our own place in the cosmic hierarchy.

## Narrative Structure

"The Worm and the Angel" is among the shortest tales in the collection, and its brevity is essential to its effect. A longer treatment would risk sentimentality or moralizing. By presenting the encounter as a single image — worm meets angel — Dunsany achieves what more discursive writers would take pages to approximate: a complete metaphysical statement in the space of a breath.

This compression is characteristic of Dunsany's prose-poem technique, influenced by the French symbolists and the Biblical cadences of the King James Version. The tale does not explain; it presents. The reader, like the worm, simply encounters — and the encounter is the meaning.
