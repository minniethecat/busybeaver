## Overview

Fame appears as a personified figure in Lord Dunsany's *Fifty-One Tales*, most notably in the untitled prose-poem that opens with her name. She is depicted as a beautiful but indifferent woman who walks the highways, trifling with mortal affections. Dunsany treats Fame not as glory earned but as an arbitrary force — desired, elusive, and ultimately hollow.

## Role in the Tales

The primary tale of Fame is the untitled prose-poem beginning at [001-059], where a poet makes chaplets of song to adorn her:

> "Fame singing in the highways, and trifling as she sang, with sordid things."
>
> [001-059]

The poet repeatedly courts Fame with his verses, but she remains indifferent. When he finally rebukes her, she abandons him entirely:

> "Lovely Fame, even in the highways and the byways you have not forgotten to speak pleasantly of me."
>
> [001-069]

> "And Fame turned her back on him and walked away, but in departing she turned and spoke."
>
> [001-072]

The poet's plea and Fame's departure capture the essential asymmetry of the artist's relationship with recognition. Fame cannot be earned, only bestowed or withheld by caprice.

Fame appears again in Section 4 [004-058–004-063], where she encounters her double, Notoriety. When Notoriety claims "I am Fame," the true Fame "stole softly away" — a sharp satire on how genuine renown is displaced by mere celebrity. This brief scene carries Dunsany's characteristic irony: Fame is so fragile that even her name can be stolen.

## Character Analysis

Dunsany's Fame is distinctly Pre-Raphaelite in conception — a silent, beautiful woman who passes through the world bestowing or withholding her favor without explanation. She is not a reward for merit but an entity with her own inscrutable will. The poet's frustration echoes a universal artistic anxiety: the fear that creative work will go unrecognized not because it lacks value, but because Fame simply does not care.

The encounter with Notoriety in Section 4 adds a modern edge to this allegory. Dunsany, writing in 1915 as mass media and celebrity culture were emerging, seems to anticipate the twentieth century's confusion of fame with notoriety. The true Fame's silent retreat is both comic and melancholic.

## Dunsany's Commentary on Artistic Fame

The Fame tales can be read as Dunsany's oblique reflection on his own literary career. By 1915, Dunsany was a celebrated author — his early collections had been praised by Yeats and others — yet he must have been aware of fame's fickleness. The poet in the tale, who makes his offerings only to be abandoned, mirrors every artist's fear: that recognition, once given, can be withdrawn without cause or warning.

The doubling of Fame and Notoriety is particularly pointed. Dunsany was a lord, a published author, a public figure — he knew both the allure of recognition and its shallow substitutes. The true Fame, who "stole softly away," is the fame that matters: the serious literary reputation. Notoriety, who claims her name, is the cheap substitute of publicity and scandal.

## Legacy and Interpretation

Dunsany's treatment of Fame has outlived its Edwardian moment. In an age of viral celebrity and social media metrics, the distinction between Fame and Notoriety is more relevant than ever. The poet's plea — "even in the highways and the byways you have not forgotten to speak pleasantly of me" — could be the cry of any creator hoping to be remembered. That Fame ultimately walks away, indifferent, is Dunsany's sobering verdict on all such hopes.
