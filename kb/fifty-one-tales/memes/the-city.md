## Overview

"The City" is a brief visionary tale from Section 1 of *Fifty-One Tales* (1915) by Lord Dunsany. A traveler watches from the cliffs as poets quietly abandon a thriving metropolis — and returns a thousand years later to find it vanished without a trace. It is a meditation on what a city is, and what — when its commerce has run silent — remains.

## Summary

The narrator's fancy carries him "in time as well as space" to the edge of low red cliffs above a desert. Below, at evening, a city stands. Presently about twenty men come softly stealing out of the city's gate:

> "It is well they are gone," they said. "It is well they are gone. We can do business now. It is well they are gone."
>
> [001-041]

The departing men pass into the twilight. The narrator asks his guide who they are. "The poets," comes the answer. "The poets and artists." He asks why they have fled, and why the people are glad. The guide replies:

> "It must be some doom that is going to fall on the city, something has warned them and they have stolen away. Nothing may warn the people."
>
> [001-041]

The wrangling voices of commerce rise from the city below. The narrator departs, for "there was an ominous look on the face of the sky." A thousand years later he passes that way again:

> there was nothing, even among the weeds, of what had been that city.
>
> [001-041]

## Analysis

"The City" belongs to a recurring pattern in Dunsany's work: the vision of a civilization destroyed not by external force but by its own spiritual emptiness. The poets leave, and the merchants rejoice. The departure of beauty is experienced not as loss but as convenience — "we can do business now." The city is condemned not in spite of its commerce but because it has room for nothing else.

The scale is characteristically Dunsanyesque. The narrator's "fancy" roams in time as well as space; the return visit is "only a thousand years later," spoken with the casualness of a traveler revisiting a neighborhood. The final image — "nothing, even among the weeds" — is a double erasure. Not even the rubble remains to mark the spot. The weeds, nature's tender archivists, have forgotten it.

The role of the poets is striking. They do not fight the doom; they do not warn the people. They simply leave. The guide says "something has warned them" — and that word, *warned*, carries a weight: the poets and artists are seismographs, the first to feel what the merchants cannot. Their departure is the city's true death — what follows is only the taking-down of the empty stage.

The tale's brevity underscores its severity. There is no lament, no elegy for the fallen city. The narrator sees, notes, departs, and returns to confirm the annihilation. The prose is as clean and remorseless as the desert that swallows the city.
