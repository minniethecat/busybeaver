## Overview

"The Assignation" is the opening tale of *Fifty-One Tales* (1915) by Lord Dunsany. In this short allegorical fable, [[fame|Fame]] — personified as a woman singing in the highways — consistently rejects [[the-poet|the poet]]'s carefully crafted "chaplets of song" in favor of the "worthless garlands" thrown by boisterous citizens. The tale explores the tension between artistic integrity and popular acclaim.

## Summary

The tale follows an unnamed poet who creates beautiful chaplets of song to adorn Fame. She ignores these offerings, preferring instead the garlands made of "perishable things" that citizens throw to her in the streets. Each time these garlands die, the poet returns with fresh chaplets, but Fame continues to laugh at him and wear the worthless wreaths — though they always die by evening.

The turning point comes when Fame one day decides to accept the poet's chaplet, thinking it might suit her well enough. She returns to where she had last seen the poet, but he is no longer there. The tale ends with the haunting line: "And the poet was dead."

## Analysis

This is one of Dunsany's most compact and resonant allegories, at roughly 200 words. It belongs to the tradition of the prose poem, using personification (Fame) to examine the relationship between the artist and public recognition. The tale's irony is layered: Fame only turns to the poet's work when it is too late, suggesting that genuine artistic recognition often arrives posthumously.

The tale sets the tone for the entire collection: brief, mythopoeic, and concerned with timeless themes — [[death]], art, fame, and the indifference of the world to creative work. As the opening tale, it establishes the collection's central concern with the fate of [[poetry]] and [[song]] in a world that prefers the perishable to the permanent.

## Characters

- **Fame**: Personified as a woman singing in the highways, trifling with sordid adventurers. She represents public recognition and acclaim. Also explored as a concept in [[fame-concept]].
- **The poet**: The unnamed artist figure who crafts "chaplets of song." He represents the sincere creative spirit, unrecognized in his lifetime. See [[the-poet]].
- **Boisterous citizens**: The crowd that throws "worthless garlands" — representing popular taste and transient approval.

## Text

> Fame singing in the highways, and trifling as she sang, with sordid adventurers, passed the poet by.
>
> And still the poet made for her little chaplets of song, to deck her forehead in the courts of Time: and still she wore instead the worthless garlands, that boisterous citizens flung to her in the ways, made out of perishable things.
>

> And one day in his bitterness the poet rebuked her, and said to her: "Lovely Fame, even in the highways and the byways you have not foreborne to laugh and shout and jest with worthless men, and I have toiled for you and dreamed of you and you mock me and pass me by."
>
> And Fame turned her back on him and walked away, but in departing she looked over her shoulder and smiled at him as she had not smiled before, and, almost speaking in a whisper, said:
>
> "I will meet you in the graveyard at the back of the Workhouse in a hundred years."

## Themes

The tale's central irony — that Fame acknowledges the poet only after his death — connects it to other works in the collection that explore the asymmetry between artistic creation and public recognition. [[the-raft-builders|The Raft-Builders]] meditates on which works survive oblivion; [[the-worm-and-the-angel|The Worm and the Angel]] contrasts the eternal with the ephemeral. Like the poet's chaplets, the raft-builders' creations may or may not float upon the sea of time. The thread connecting these tales is the uncertainty of [[immortality]] through art — and the dignity of creating nevertheless.

The assignation itself — the meeting in the graveyard — is a promise deferred beyond the poet's lifetime. It is a dark echo of the romantic assignation, transposed into the key of [[death]]. Fame's whisper carries the ambivalence of all Dunsany's cosmic forces: a promise and a mockery at once.
