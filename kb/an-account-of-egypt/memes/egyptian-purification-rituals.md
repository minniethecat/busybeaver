## Overview

Purification practices in ancient Egypt, as documented by Herodotus, were woven deeply into both religious observance and daily life. The Egyptians combined temple-based codes of ritual cleanliness with regular bodily purging routines, creating a culture in which physical purity and spiritual sanctity were closely intertwined. Herodotus singles out the Egyptians' standards of temple purity as notably stricter than those of most other peoples he knew, while also recording their systematic approach to bodily health through monthly purgation. (006-002) (008-001)

## Temple Purity

Among the most distinctive Egyptian religious innovations, Herodotus records the prohibition against sexual activity in temples and the requirement of bathing before entering sacred spaces after contact with women. He explicitly contrasts the Egyptian and Hellenic practice with that of "almost all other men," who, he disapprovingly notes, treat temples no differently from their homes in this regard — behaving as beasts do, coupling freely in sacred enclosures. The Egyptians, by contrast, erected a firm boundary between the profane and the sacred, insisting that physical cleanliness was a prerequisite for approaching the gods. This was, in Herodotus's taxonomy of customs, one of the defining features that set Egyptian religion apart. (006-002)

## Monthly Bodily Purging

Beyond temple conduct, the Egyptians maintained a systematic regimen of bodily purification. For three consecutive days each month, they purged themselves, "hunting after health" with emetics and clysters. Their underlying belief — that all diseases were produced by the food one consumed — drove this monthly cycle of internal cleansing. Herodotus observes that the Egyptians were, in his opinion, the most healthy people in the world after the Libyans, a condition he attributed primarily to the stability of the seasons (since seasonal change, he theorized, was the chief cause of illness). But their purging practices, no doubt, contributed to this reputation for healthiness. (008-001)

This monthly purge was linked to their broader conception of diet, which Herodotus describes in some detail. The Egyptians ate bread made from maize, which they called *kyllestis*, and drank a wine brewed from barley — vines being absent from their land. Of fish, some they dried in the sun and ate raw, others they cured in brine. Of birds, they consumed quails, ducks, and small birds without cooking, after first curing them. All other birds and fish — except those set apart as sacred — were eaten roasted or boiled. This dietary system, combined with the monthly purge, formed a coherent health regimen that reflected the Egyptians' distinctive understanding of the relationship between food, purity, and disease. (008-001)

## Purity and the Death Awareness Custom

Herodotus records one custom that bridged the domains of purification, diet, and spiritual contemplation. At the entertainments of the wealthy, when the feasting concluded, a man carried around a wooden figure of a dead body in a coffin, made as lifelike as possible by painting and carving, measuring about a cubit or two each way. Showing it to each guest in turn, he said: "When thou lookest upon this, drink and be merry, for thou shalt be such as this when thou art dead." This *memento mori*, embedded in the drinking custom, reveals how Egyptian ideas about bodily purity and health were ultimately framed against the certainty of death — the purification of the living body being, in a sense, a preparation for the embalming that would follow. (008-001)
