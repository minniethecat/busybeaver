# Syria

## Overview

Syria, the region bordering [[egypt|Egypt]] to the northeast, appears in [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s [[egypt|Egypt]]ian account as both a geographical reference and a cultural contact zone. The Syrians who dwelt in Palestine were, according to [[herodotus|Herodotus]], among the peoples who adopted [[egypt|Egypt]]ian customs — most notably circumcision. Syria also features in the military history of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian kings, from [[necos|Necos]]'s campaigns to the sieges of Azotos, and as the borderland between the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian and [[persian|Persian]] spheres of influence.

## Description

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] identifies Syria as [[egypt|Egypt]]'s northeastern neighbor: "from Mount Casion, which is the boundary between [[egypt|Egypt]] and Syria, the distance is exactly a thousand furlongs to the Arabian gulf" (015-004). The geographical proximity facilitated cultural transmission. In his discussion of circumcision, [[herodotus|Herodotus]] records that "the [[phoenicians|Phenicians]] and the Syrians who dwell in Palestine confess themselves that they have learnt it from the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians" (009-006), presenting the Syrians as recipients of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian cultural practice. He extends this observation to other Syrian peoples: "the Syrians about the river Thermodon and the river Parthenios, and the Macronians, who are their neighbors, say that they have learnt it lately from the Colchians" (009-006), tracing a chain of cultural diffusion from [[egypt|Egypt]] outward.

Syria was a theater for [[egypt|Egypt]]ian military action. The pharaoh [[necos|Necos]] "engaged battle at Magdolos with the Syrians, and conquered them; and after this he took Cadytis, which is a great city of Syria" (015-005). [[psammetichos|Psammetichos]] conducted the longest siege in recorded history against Azotos, "a great city of Syria, besieging it, until at last he took it: and this Azotos of all cities about which we have knowledge held out for the longest time under a siege" (015-003). The fugitive king [[sethos|Sethos]] also spent time "a fugitive in Syria" during periods of political upheaval (014-008).

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] uses Syria as a geographical anchor in his broader discussion of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian topography. He describes how [[nile-river|the Nile]]'s course "tended from the South towards Syria, the gulfs boring in so as almost to meet at their extreme points" (002-001), using Syrian geography to frame his analysis of the landscape.

## Significance

Syria serves [[herodotus|Herodotus]] as a key example in his diffusionist argument: [[egypt|Egypt]]ian customs radiate outward to neighboring peoples. The Syrian adoption of circumcision is evidence that [[egypt|Egypt]] was the source of practices later found across the eastern Mediterranean. At the same time, Syria's role in [[egypt|Egypt]]ian military history — as the target of campaigns by [[necos|Necos]], [[psammetichos|Psammetichos]], and others — demonstrates that [[egypt|Egypt]]'s relationship with its northeastern neighbor was not merely cultural but political and strategic. Syria was the gateway between [[egypt|Egypt]] and the wider Near Eastern world, including the [[persian|Persian]] Empire.
