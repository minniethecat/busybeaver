## Overview

[[egypt|Egypt]]ian festivals, as recorded by [[herodotus|Herodotus]] in his fifth-century BCE travelogue, were large-scale religious gatherings that combined solemn worship, sacrifice, mass pilgrimage, and communal celebration. The [[egypt|Egypt]]ians were, according to [[herodotus|Herodotus]], the first people to institute solemn assemblies, processions, and ritual approaches to temples -- practices later adopted by the [[hellenes|Hellenes]] (006-002). These festivals were held not once a year but frequently, at multiple major temple cities across [[egypt|Egypt]], each dedicated to a specific deity and each marked by distinctive rituals and customs.

## Description

The [[egypt|Egypt]]ian festival system operated on several levels. At its core was the idea of the solemn assembly (Greek: _panegyris_), a mass gathering of worshippers who travelled, often by river, to a temple city for sacrifice and celebration. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] enumerates six principal festival sites: [[bubastis|Bubastis]] (for [[artemis|Artemis]]), Busiris (for [[isis|Isis]]), [[sais|Sais]] (for Athene), [[heliopolis|Heliopolis]] (for the Sun), [[buto|Buto]] (for [[leto|Leto]]), and Papremis (for Ares) (006-002). To [[heliopolis|Heliopolis]] and [[buto|Buto]], worshippers went year by year to perform sacrifice only; at the other sites, more elaborate observances were held (006-003).

Festivals typically involved multiple elements: the journey to the sacred city, often a boat pilgrimage with music, singing, and dancing; the performance of sacrifices, sometimes on a massive scale; ritualised mourning or combat; and nocturnal ceremonies involving the lighting of lamps. The scale of these gatherings was extraordinary: at [[bubastis|Bubastis]] alone, [[herodotus|Herodotus]] reports that as many as seven hundred thousand men and women assembled, excluding children (006-002). Wine consumption at this festival exceeded the total for the entire rest of the year.

### Major Festival Sites

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] identifies six principal locations where the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians held their most important solemn assemblies:

- **[[bubastis|Bubastis]]** (for [[artemis|Artemis]]/Bastet): The most popular and zealous festival, involving a river pilgrimage and mass celebration.
- **Busiris** (for [[isis|Isis]]/Demeter): A festival marked by ritual mourning, where both men and women beat themselves after sacrifice (006-002).
- **[[sais|Sais]]** (for Athene/Neith): The site of the lamp-lighting festival known as Lychnocaia, where lamps were kindled throughout the city (006-002).
- **[[heliopolis|Heliopolis]]** (for the Sun/Helios): A place of annual sacrifice, without the elaborate assemblies of other sites (006-003).
- **[[buto|Buto]]** (for [[leto|Leto]]/Wadjet): Annual sacrifices; also the location of the most truthful oracle in [[egypt|Egypt]] (006-003), (014-008).
- **Papremis** (for Ares): Notable for its unique ritual combat between priests (006-003).

Each site's festival operated independently, and the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians did not observe a unified calendar of festivals across the whole country. The diversity of rites reflected the regional nature of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian religious practice, where local temples maintained their own traditions and deity associations.

### The Festival of Bubastis

The festival at [[bubastis|Bubastis]] receives [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s most detailed attention and serves as the archetype of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian festival culture. The journey to [[bubastis|Bubastis]] was itself a performative act: men and women sailed together in crowded boats, the women shaking rattles, the men playing flutes, while the rest sang and clapped hands (006-002). At each city along the riverbank, the boats drew to land: some women continued their music, others shouted jeers at the local women, some danced, and others stood up and pulled up their garments -- a gesture of ritual obscenity or fertility display that [[herodotus|Herodotus]] notes without further commentary.

Upon arrival at [[bubastis|Bubastis]], the festival proper commenced with great sacrifices and the consumption of wine on a scale unmatched anywhere else in [[egypt|Egypt]]. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] reports with evident astonishment that more grape-wine was drunk during this single festival than in the whole of the rest of the year. The gathering reached numbers of seven hundred thousand people, as reported by the natives -- a figure that, even if exaggerated, conveys the immense draw of the festival (006-002).

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] also notes that the feast celebrated after the return of king [[rhampsinitos|Rhampsinitos]] from the underworld was still observed in his own time. The priests wove a robe in a single day, blindfolded one of their number, and led him by wolves (or priests masked as wolves) to the temple of Demeter and back -- a ritual that [[herodotus|Herodotus]] records but declines to endorse (012-001).

### The Lamp-Lighting and Papremis Festivals

Two other [[egypt|Egypt]]ian festivals merit particular attention for their distinctive character. **The Festival of Lamps (Lychnocaia)** took place at [[sais|Sais]] on a designated night during the sacrificial gathering. The [[egypt|Egypt]]ians kindled numerous lamps in the open air around the houses -- saucers filled with salt and oil mixed together, with a floating wick that burned throughout the entire night (006-002). [[herodotus|Herodotus]] emphasises that the observance was not confined to [[sais|Sais]]: those who could not attend the assembly nonetheless observed the night at home, lighting lamps so that all [[egypt|Egypt]] was illuminated simultaneously. The reason for this nocturnal illumination was, [[herodotus|Herodotus]] tells us, the subject of a sacred story that he declines to recount (006-003).

**The Festival of Papremis** featured a unique and violent ritual. As the sun began to set, a few priests attended to the gilded wooden shrine containing the image of Ares. On the day before, the image had been removed to another sacred building. The priests drew the shrine on a four-wheeled wain, while other priests stationed in the temple gateway attempted to block its entry using wooden clubs. The worshippers, numbering over a thousand and acting under a vow, came to the god's aid, striking the defenders with staves. The resulting melee was so severe that, according to [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s own assessment, many must have died of their wounds -- though the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians themselves maintained that no one perished (006-003).

## Historical Context

By the 5th century BCE, the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian festival system was already of immense antiquity. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] notes that the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians were the first people to institute solemn assemblies, processions, and approaches to temples, and that their celebrations had been held "from a very ancient time" while Hellenic equivalents were "introduced but lately" (006-002). The festival at [[bubastis|Bubastis]] alone drew crowds that [[herodotus|Herodotus]] estimated at seven hundred thousand — a number that, whether precise or not, indicates the festivals' role as the largest mass gatherings in the Mediterranean world of his era. The regional character of these festivals, with each major temple city hosting its own distinct rites, reflected the political structure of [[egypt|Egypt]] itself: a unified kingdom under a divine king, yet composed of nome-based communities each with their own patron deities and traditions.

## Herodotus's Commentary

[[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s account of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian festivals serves several purposes in his larger ethnographic project. First, he uses the antiquity of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian religious assemblies to assert [[egypt|Egypt]]ian cultural priority over the [[hellenes|Greeks]], claiming that the [[hellenes|Hellenes]] learned their own festival practices from [[egypt|Egypt]] (006-002). This is part of a broader pattern in [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s work: the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians are consistently portrayed as the originators of customs, religious rites, and doctrines later found among the [[hellenes|Greeks]].

Second, [[herodotus|Herodotus]] draws implicit and explicit contrasts between [[egypt|Egypt]]ian and Greek practice. He dismisses the Greek story of [[heracles|Heracles]] being led in procession to be sacrificed at an [[egypt|Egypt]]ian altar as absurd, arguing that the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians, who sacrifice only select animals and abhor human sacrifice, would never have countenanced such a rite (005-003). This correction serves both to defend [[egypt|Egypt]]ian religious practice and to critique Greek credulity.

## Significance

These festival descriptions give modern readers unparalleled access to the lived religious experience of ordinary [[egypt|Egypt]]ians in the Late Period. The mass participation, the river journeys, the music and dance, the ritual combat, and the universal lamp-lighting all attest to a religious culture that was popular, participatory, and embedded in daily life — not confined to a priestly elite. The festivals were also crucial mechanisms of social cohesion: they brought together populations from across [[egypt|Egypt]], reinforced regional identities tied to specific deities and temples, and provided occasions for the ritual expression of the relationship between the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian people, their gods, and the institution of kingship.
