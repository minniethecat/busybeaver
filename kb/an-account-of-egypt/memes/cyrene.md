## Overview

[[Cyrene]] was a Greek colony on the coast of [[Libya]], west of Egypt. In Herodotus's *Account of Egypt*, Cyrene appears as a geographical reference point — one of the cities and regions that defined the Libyan frontier of the Egyptian world. Founded by settlers from Thera in the 7th century BCE, Cyrene was, by Herodotus's time, a flourishing polis and a point of contact between the Greek, Libyan, and Egyptian cultural spheres.

## Geographical Context

Herodotus situates Cyrene within the broader geography of Libya, which bordered Egypt to the west. The Egyptians distinguished themselves from the Libyans by the defining feature of Egyptian identity — the Nile — and by the contrasting soils of the two regions: the black, crumbling mud of Egypt versus the "reddish and rather sandy" soil of Libya (002-001). Cyrene, as the most prominent Greek settlement in Libya, represented the westernmost extent of the Hellenic world's contact with the Egyptian sphere.

## In Herodotus's Narrative

Cyrene is mentioned in the *Account of Egypt* primarily as a geographical referent rather than as a subject of extended description. The colony's significance lies in its position at the intersection of African and Mediterranean worlds — a Greek city on Libyan soil, within the broader orbit of Egyptian cultural influence that Herodotus traces throughout his second book.

## Significance

Cyrene exemplifies Herodotus's practice of situating Egypt within a wider geographical framework. By referencing cities and regions from Cyrene in the west to [[Tyre]] and [[Arabia]] in the east, the historian constructs a map of the known world in which Egypt occupies the center — a land defined by the Nile but connected by commerce, colonization, and cultural exchange to the entire Mediterranean basin.

## Sources

Herodotus's account of the founding of Cyrene and its history appears primarily in Book Four of the *Histories*, rather than in the Egyptian ethnography of Book Two. Within the Egyptian context, Cyrene is referenced as a geographical boundary marker rather than as a narrative subject.
