## Overview

[[Egyptian Customs]], as recorded by [[Herodotus]] in his "Account of [[egypt|Egypt]]" (Book II of the *Histories*), encompass the distinctive manners, [[Egyptian Religion|religious practices]], dietary habits, and social conventions that characterized life in [[Egypt|ancient Egypt]]. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] presents these customs as fundamentally different from those of the [[Ancient Greece|Greeks]] — often describing them as the inverse of [[Hellenic]] practice — and devotes several chapters to cataloguing them in detail. The customs he describes range from [[Egyptian Circumcision|bodily rituals]] and [[Egyptian Dietary Laws|food laws]] to [[Animal Sacrifice|sacrificial protocols]] and [[Egyptian Festivals|festival observances]], forming one of the earliest and most comprehensive ethnographic accounts of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian daily life in Western literature.

## Description

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] outlines a wide range of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian customs, beginning with the observation that the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians "established for themselves manners and customs in a way opposite to other men in almost all matters" (004-004). This inversion extends to daily life: women conduct business in the marketplace while men weave at home; priests shave their entire bodies; and writing proceeds from right to left.

### Religious Practices

The [[egypt|Egypt]]ians are described as "religious excessively beyond all other men" (004-005). Their religious customs include daily rituals of purification — drinking from bronze cups that are rinsed every day, wearing freshly washed linen garments, and practicing circumcision as a matter of cleanliness rather than custom. The [[egypt|Egypt]]ians were, according to [[herodotus|Herodotus]], the first people to prohibit intercourse in temples and to require bathing before entering sacred spaces (006-004).

The [[Egyptian Priesthood]] observed particularly stringent customs. Priests shaved their entire bodies every other day to prevent vermin, wore only linen garments and papyrus sandals, and bathed in cold water twice each day and twice each night (004-005). They were allotted a fixed daily allowance of bread, beef, goose-meat, and wine, but were forbidden to eat fish. The priestly class enjoyed considerable material support from temple estates, yet their lives were governed by an elaborate code of ritual purity that distinguished them sharply from the ordinary [[egypt|Egypt]]ian population.

### Animal Sacrifice and Worship

The [[egypt|Egypt]]ians maintained elaborate protocols for animal sacrifice. Oxen were tested for ritual purity — a single black hair rendered the beast unfit for sacrifice (004-006). Different regions observed different dietary prohibitions: those devoted to the [[zeus|Theban Zeus]] sacrificed goats and abstained from sheep (005-001), while the pig was universally regarded as an abominable animal (005-005). [[Sacred Animals of Egypt|Sacred animals]] were dedicated to specific deities, and the killing of certain animals carried the death penalty.

The [[egypt|Egypt]]ians' reverence for animals extended well beyond sacrificial contexts. Individual animals were consecrated to particular gods: the [[crocodile]] was sacred in some regions but consumed in others; the [[ichneumon]] was revered for its role in controlling serpents; and cats, hawks, and ibises were universally venerated. When a cat died a natural death in a house, [[herodotus|Herodotus]] reports that all inhabitants would shave their eyebrows in mourning, and dead cats were transported to [[Bubastis]] for embalming and burial in sacred repositories (005-008).

### Festivals and Assemblies

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] credits the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians as the first people to institute solemn assemblies, processions, and approaches to temples, from whom the [[hellenes|Greeks]] later adopted these practices (006-002). The festival of lamps at [[Sais]] was particularly notable, with lamps lit across all of [[egypt|Egypt]] on a single night (006-003). During the festival of [[Artemis]] at [[bubastis|Bubastis]], [[herodotus|Herodotus]] records that as many as seven hundred thousand men and women — besides children — would assemble and drink more wine than during the entire rest of the year (006-006).

The [[Egyptian Festivals|festival calendar]] was intimately tied to the agricultural rhythm of the [[Nile River|Nile]] and to the mythological narratives of the gods. The festival of [[Isis]] at Busiris involved tens of thousands of participants who, after the sacrifice, beat themselves in a ritual of mourning that [[herodotus|Herodotus]] compares — without naming — to the mysteries of [[Demeter]] in Greece (006-007).

## Historical Context

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] wrote in the mid-5th century BCE, during a period when [[egypt|Egypt]] was under [[Persian]] rule following [[Cambyses]]'s conquest in 525 BCE. His informants were primarily priests at [[Memphis]], [[Heliopolis]], and [[thebes|Thebes]] — members of the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian religious establishment who preserved and transmitted traditional knowledge. The customs [[herodotus|Herodotus]] describes predate the [[persian|Persian]] period and reflect practices that had developed over millennia of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian civilization along [[nile-river|the Nile]].

[[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s perspective is that of a Greek observer encountering a civilization far older than his own. His frequent comparisons between [[egypt|Egypt]]ian and Greek customs reflect both genuine ethnographic curiosity and the Greek tendency to measure foreign cultures against their own norms. Crucially, [[herodotus|Herodotus]] frequently grants [[egypt|Egypt]]ian priority: the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians "were the first" to establish many of the customs he describes, and the [[hellenes|Greeks]], in his account, are often borrowers rather than originators.

## Herodotus's Commentary

Throughout his account, [[herodotus|Herodotus]] adopts a posture of careful observation, often distinguishing between what he personally witnessed and what he heard from informants. He expresses admiration for [[egypt|Egypt]]ian religiosity and cleanliness while occasionally registering skepticism about particular claims. His observation that "the naming of almost all the gods has come to Hellas from [[egypt|Egypt]]" (005-006) reflects the broader Greek belief in the antiquity and authority of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian religious tradition.

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] is particularly struck by what he perceives as the systematic inversion of Greek norms. The [[egypt|Egypt]]ians, in his telling, do everything differently — and yet their way is often the original from which Greek practice derived. This dual perception of [[egypt|Egypt]] as simultaneously opposite and prior is one of the most distinctive features of [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s ethnographic vision, and it shapes his presentation of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian customs at every turn.

## Significance

[[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s account of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian customs is one of the earliest surviving examples of systematic ethnography in Western literature. His detailed descriptions of religious rites, dietary laws, purification rituals, and social conventions provide invaluable evidence for understanding daily life in ancient [[egypt|Egypt]]. Moreover, his comparative framework — contrasting [[egypt|Egypt]]ian practices with those of the [[hellenes|Greeks]] — established a method of cultural description that influenced subsequent historians and ethnographers for centuries.

The account also illuminates the broader cultural dynamics of the 5th-century BCE Mediterranean. [[egypt|Egypt]], though politically subjugated by Persia, retained immense cultural prestige in the Greek imagination. [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s customs catalogue, by emphasizing [[egypt|Egypt]]ian priority and Greek derivation, participates in a long tradition of Greek acknowledgment of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian cultural authority — a tradition that would persist through the Hellenistic period and beyond.

> The Egyptians in agreement with their climate, which is unlike any other, and with the river, which shows a nature different from all other rivers, established for themselves manners and customs in a way opposite to other men in almost all matters.
> (004-004)

> They are religious excessively beyond all other men, and with regard to this they have customs as follows:—they drink from cups of bronze and rinse them out every day, and not some only do this but all: they wear garments of linen always newly washed, and this they make a special point of practice.
> (004-005)

> The Egyptians were the first who made it a point of religion not to lie with women in temples, nor to enter into temples after going away from women without first bathing: for almost all other men except the Egyptians and the Hellenes lie with women in temples.
> (006-004)

> Moreover, it is true also that the Egyptians were the first of men who made solemn assemblies and processions and approaches to the temples, and from them the Hellenes have learnt them.
> (006-002)

> The priests of the gods in other lands wear long hair, but in Egypt they shave their heads: among other men the custom is that in mourning those whom the matter concerns most nearly have their hair cut short, but the Egyptians, when they lose any of those near to them, let their hair grow long.
> (004-005)
