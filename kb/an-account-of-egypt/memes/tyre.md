## Overview

Tyre was a major Phoenician city-state on the Mediterranean coast, mentioned by Herodotus in the "Account of Egypt" as a place he personally visited to investigate the cult of Heracles. The historian describes the city's temple of Heracles as richly endowed, featuring two remarkable pillars — one of pure gold and the other of emerald. Tyre also appears in the narrative as a naval power in conflict with the Egyptian king Apries. (005-002) (015-009)

## Herodotus's Visit

Driven by his desire to learn everything possible about the worship of Heracles, Herodotus made a voyage to Tyre specifically to visit its temple of Heracles. He reports that the temple was richly furnished with votive offerings, the most notable being two pillars: one made of refined gold and the other of emerald stone that shone brilliantly at night. (005-002)

The historian questioned the priests of the temple about its antiquity and learned that the sanctuary had been founded at the same time as the city itself, which the priests claimed had been inhabited for two thousand three hundred years. This testimony contributed to Herodotus's argument that Heracles was a very ancient god, contradicting the Greek belief that he was a relatively recent figure. (005-002)

## The Camp of the Tyrians

Within Egypt itself, Herodotus records that a district near the temple of Proteus at Memphis was inhabited by Phoenicians of Tyre. This entire region was called the Camp of the Tyrians, indicating a permanent Tyrian trading colony or diplomatic quarter within the Egyptian capital. (010-004)

## Naval Conflict with Apries

The Egyptian king Apries led a naval expedition against Tyre during his 25-year reign. Herodotus reports that Apries fought a sea-battle against the king of Tyre after having previously campaigned against Sidon. This conflict places Tyre within the military and diplomatic sphere of 26th Dynasty Egypt. (015-009)
