## Overview

The [[Wadi Tumilat]] is a valley running eastward from the [[Nile Delta]] towards the [[Erythraian Sea]] (the modern Gulf of Suez), through which the great canal connecting the Nile to the Erythraian Sea was cut. Herodotus describes this canal as one of the great engineering projects of Egypt — begun by King [[Necos]], abandoned after an oracle warned he was labouring for a foreign power, and ultimately completed under the Persian king [[Dareios]] (015-004).

## The Canal Project

The canal was designed to link the Nile — at a point near [[Bubastis]] — with the northern end of the Erythraian Sea, passing through the Wadi Tumilat. Herodotus records its dimensions: the canal was broad enough for two triremes to be rowed abreast, and its length required a four-day voyage to traverse (015-004).

Necos, the son of [[Psammetichos]], was the first to attempt the canal. According to Herodotus, 120,000 Egyptians perished during the digging before Necos desisted — deterred by an oracle warning that he was toiling for a foreign power. The oracular prophecy proved accurate: the canal was later completed under Dareios I after the Persian conquest of Egypt, serving Persian strategic interests by linking the Mediterranean to the Erythraian Sea.

## Strategic Significance

The canal through the Wadi Tumilat represented an ancient precursor to the modern Suez Canal, connecting the Mediterranean basin — via the Nile — with the maritime trade routes of the Erythraian Sea and the Indian Ocean beyond. Its construction reflected both the ambition of Saïte-period Egyptian kings to assert naval power and the continuity of Persian imperial infrastructure across conquered territories.

## Herodotus's Account

Herodotus mentions the canal in the context of Necos's reign, treating it alongside Necos's other achievements: his trireme-building programme and his land battle with the Syrians at Magdolos. The juxtaposition of the canal's massive human cost (120,000 dead) with the oracle's warning introduces a somber note into Herodotus's account of Egyptian engineering ambition — the labour of myriads yielding a project ultimately serving a foreign empire.
