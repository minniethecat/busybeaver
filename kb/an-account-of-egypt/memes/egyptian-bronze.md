## Overview

Bronze was the primary metal for everyday vessels and ritual implements in the Egypt described by Herodotus. Unlike gold, which was reserved for the temples and the gods, bronze served the practical and hygienic needs of the Egyptian people. Its use in drinking cups, ceremonial libations, and even royal headgear illustrates the metal's versatility and ubiquity across all strata of Egyptian society. (004-005)

## Bronze in Daily and Religious Life

Herodotus records that the Egyptians were "religious excessively beyond all other men" and that their piety expressed itself in material customs. They drank exclusively from cups of bronze, which they scrupulously rinsed out every day — a practice observed universally, not merely by the wealthy or the priestly class. This daily ritual of cleansing bronze vessels was of a piece with their broader commitment to cleanliness, which extended to the wearing of freshly washed linen garments and the practice of circumcision. (004-005)

## Bronze in Royal Ceremony

Bronze played a decisive role in the political history of Egypt as recounted by Herodotus. During the reign of the twelve kings, a critical moment of transition occurred at a libation ceremony in the temple of Hephaistos. The chief priest mistakenly brought out only eleven golden cups for the twelve kings. Psammetichos, standing last in order and finding himself without a cup, took off his bronze helmet and used it to pour the libation. This act, performed without any thought of malice or intent, nevertheless fulfilled an oracle that had foretold that the king who poured libation with a bronze vessel would rule all Egypt. The incident led to Psammetichos's exile and eventual return as sole ruler. (014-008)


## The Oracle of the Bronze Cup

The significance of bronze in the political history of Egypt was underscored by the oracle that set the stage for Psammetichos's rise. When the twelve kings first assumed joint rule, they made a pact of mutual friendship, reinforced by intermarriage and strong oaths. The pact was motivated by an oracle given to them at the outset of their reign: whichever of the twelve poured a libation with a bronze cup in the temple of [[Hephaistos]] would become king of all Egypt. This prophecy hung over their collective rule, and it was the accidental fulfillment of this oracle — when Psammetichos, lacking a golden cup, used his bronze helmet for the libation — that triggered his exile and eventual return as sole ruler. (014-005)

## Bronze and Prophecy: The Men of Bronze

Psammetichos, after being driven into the fenlands, sought vengeance against the eleven kings who had exiled him. He sent to the Oracle of Leto at [[Buto]], the most truthful oracle in Egypt, and received the reply that vengeance would come "when men of bronze appeared from the sea." Psammetichos was initially skeptical that bronze men would come to his aid. However, not long afterward, a company of Ionians and Carians, driven ashore by circumstance while on a plundering expedition, landed in Egypt clad in their bronze armor. Word reached Psammetichos that "bronze men" had come from the sea and were plundering the plain — thus fulfilling the prophecy through the same metal that had set his destiny in motion. (014-008)
