## Overview

Herodotus's account of Egypt is notable for its detailed observations on the status and customs of Egyptian women. The Greek historian found many Egyptian practices regarding women to be strikingly different from those of his own culture — so much so that he characterized Egyptian customs as being "opposite to other men in almost all matters." His descriptions cover women's economic roles, their participation in religious life, their marriage customs, and their prominent place in mourning rituals. (004-004)

## Economic and Social Roles

Herodotus observed a remarkable reversal of gender roles in Egyptian economic life. Whereas in Greece men conducted business in the marketplace, in Egypt it was the women who frequented the market and carried on trade, while the men remained at home engaged in weaving. Women carried burdens upon their shoulders rather than their heads, and the obligation to support aged parents fell upon daughters — who were compelled to do so whether willing or not — rather than upon sons, who faced no such compulsion. (004-004)

In matters of dress, Herodotus notes that Egyptian men wore two garments each while women wore but one. The Egyptians were particular about the cleanliness of their linen garments, always keeping them newly washed. Both sexes wore linen tunics about the legs with fringes, called *calasiris*, with garments of white wool thrown over them — though woolen garments were forbidden within temples and in burial, a rule shared with Orphic and Pythagorean observances. (007-005) (004-005)

## Religious Roles

The religious sphere presented a paradox for Egyptian women. Herodotus states categorically that no woman served as a minister or priestess of either male or female divinity — all priestly offices, for all gods and goddesses, were held by men. This exclusion is striking given the prominence of women in other aspects of Egyptian life. (004-004)

Nevertheless, women were central to Egyptian religious mythology and the founding of oracular traditions. The priests of Thebes told Herodotus that two consecrated women in the service of the temple of Zeus at Thebes had been carried away by Phoenicians and sold — one into Libya and the other into Greece. These women, the Egyptians claimed, founded the first prophetic seats among those nations: the oracle of Zeus Ammon in Libya and the oracle of Zeus at Dodona in Greece. The priestesses of Dodona offered a parallel account involving two black doves that flew from Thebes. Herodotus himself reasons that if the Phoenicians really did carry away the consecrated women, then it was natural that a former temple servant of Zeus at Thebes would establish a sanctuary of Zeus in her new land. (006-001)

## Marriage Customs

Herodotus provides several glimpses of Egyptian marriage practices. The swineherd class, considered impure because of the Egyptian abomination of pigs, was excluded from intermarriage with the rest of the population — swineherds married only among themselves, and no one would give a daughter to a swineherd or take a wife from one. (005-005)

One of the most vivid marriage stories involves King Amasis (Ahmose II), who married a Greek woman named Ladike from Cyrene to cement an alliance. When Amasis found himself unable to have intercourse with Ladike — though he had no such trouble with his other wives — he accused her of giving him drugs and threatened her with death. Ladike vowed to Aphrodite that she would send a dedicatory image to Cyrene if the goddess restored Amasis's potency. The vow was successful: Amasis's ability returned that very night, and he became greatly attached to Ladike. The statue Ladike dedicated to Aphrodite still stood at Cyrene in Herodotus's time. (017-001)

## Mourning Customs

Women played the most visible role in Egyptian funerary rituals. When a man of any regard died, the women of his household immediately plastered mud over their heads and faces. Then, leaving the corpse within the house, they went about the city beating themselves, with their garments bound by a girdle and their breasts exposed. All the female relatives of the deceased joined in this public display of grief, while the men, also girded, beat themselves on the other side. (008-002)

An unusual practice attended the death of high-ranking women: the wives of men of rank, and women of great beauty or reputation, were not given to the embalmers immediately upon death. The body was held for three or four days before being delivered. The Egyptians explained that this was a precaution — one embalmer had been caught violating the corpse of a recently deceased woman, and his fellow craftsman had informed against him. (008-002)

## Significance

Herodotus's account of Egyptian women reveals a society in which gender roles were in many respects the inverse of Greek norms — yet not in the direction of equality as modern readers might expect. Women controlled commerce but were excluded from the priesthood; they were the public face of mourning but had no formal religious office; a daughter was legally obligated to support her parents while a son was not. This complex picture, filtered through Herodotus's Greek lens of wonder at the "opposite" customs of Egypt, provides one of the earliest surviving ethnographic accounts of women's lives in the ancient world.
