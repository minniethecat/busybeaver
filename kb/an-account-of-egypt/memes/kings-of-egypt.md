## Overview

The [[Kings of Egypt]] as recorded by [[Herodotus]] in his *Account of Egypt* span from the legendary first king [[Min]] (Menes), who founded [[Memphis]], through to [[Amasis]], the prosperous 26th Dynasty ruler who died shortly before the [[Persian]] conquest. Herodotus's king-list is not a systematic chronology but an episodic genealogy interwoven with marvels, building projects, conquests, and moral exempla. The kings he describes fall into distinct groups: the early dynasts and pyramid-builders, the conquerors, the pious and impious kings, and the Saïte rulers of the historian's own era.

## Commentary

Herodotus's king-list reflects several distinctive features of his historiographical method. First, chronology is largely absent: kings are linked by succession but rarely dated, and reign-lengths are given sporadically and sometimes implausibly. Second, the kings function as vehicles for ethnographic and moral themes: Sesostris demonstrates Egyptian global reach, Cheops embodies tyranny, Mykerinos illustrates the fragility of justice, and Amasis exemplifies wisdom born of humble origins. Third, the Egyptian priests of Memphis, Heliopolis, and Thebes are Herodotus's primary informants, and their institutional perspective — concerned with temple foundations, dedications, and priestly privileges — shapes which kings are remembered and how they are described.

The inclusion of Proteus, a figure from the Trojan War era known from Homer, reflects Herodotus's broader project of reconciling Greek epic tradition with Egyptian priestly knowledge. By placing Proteus in the king-list, he integrates Egyptian chronology into the Greek myth-historical framework.
