## Overview

Incense and aromatic substances played an important role in Egyptian religious practice. Herodotus records several instances of incense-burning and perfume-use in temple rituals, particularly at Memphis and Heliopolis, where the priests described offerings of aromatic substances to the gods. Frankincense itself was a product of the distant mountains of Arabia, brought to Egypt through trade routes that connected the Nile to the spice-producing regions. (001-009)

The Egyptians employed aromatics not only in temple worship but also in the elaborate art of embalming, where fragrant substances were used to preserve the dead and honour them in burial. The significance of myrrh and incense extended into Egyptian mythology, most notably in the legend of the phoenix at Heliopolis.

## Description

### Incense in Temple Sacrifice

Herodotus describes the ritual use of incense in Egyptian temple worship. During the examination of sacrificial oxen at the temple of Epaphos, priests would inspect the animal for ritual purity, mark it as clean with sealed papyrus, and lead it to the altar. A fire was kindled upon the altar, and incense was cast upon the flames as part of the sacrificial rite. The priests performed these sacrifices after a period of fasting, and while the offerings burned, all present beat themselves in mourning before setting forth the unburnt portions as a feast. (004-006)

### Frankincense Trade

The frankincense used in Egyptian temples came from the distant mountains at the southern limits of the known world. Herodotus was informed that the mountain ranges turning towards the East produced frankincense — a region far beyond Egypt, accessible only through the trade networks that connected the Nile valley to the spice routes of Arabia. (001-009)

### The Phoenix and Myrrh

The phoenix legend, as recounted by the priests of Heliopolis, attests to the central importance of myrrh in Egyptian sacred tradition. According to the priests, the phoenix appeared only once every five hundred years. When its parent died, the bird would form an egg of myrrh as large as it could carry, hollow it out, and place the parent's body inside, plastering it over with more myrrh. It would then convey this myrrh-encased body to the temple of the Sun at Heliopolis for burial. (007-003)

Herodotus acknowledges the story with scepticism ("I cannot believe the story," he writes), but its narration by the priests of Heliopolis demonstrates the profound association of myrrh with death-rites, resurrection symbolism, and solar worship in Egyptian religion. (007-003)

### Castor Oil and Daily Anointing

Beyond the temples, aromatics also figured in daily Egyptian life. The marsh-dwelling Egyptians of the Delta used an oil extracted from the castor-berry, which they called *kiki*. These plants, which grew wild in Hellas, were sown along the banks of Egyptian rivers and pools, producing berries in great quantity. The oil, though possessing a disagreeable smell, was rich and suitable both for anointing the body and for burning in lamps. (009-001)

### Embalming and Aromatics

The Egyptian art of embalming, practised by a distinct professional class, relied heavily on aromatic substances. After removing the internal organs, the embalmers cleansed the body cavity with palm wine and pounded aromatics, then filled it with pure myrrh, cassia, and every other fragrant substance except frankincense. The body was then sewn up and covered in natron for seventy days before being wrapped in fine linen. (008-003)
