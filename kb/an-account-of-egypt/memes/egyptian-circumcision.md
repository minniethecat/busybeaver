## Overview

[[egypt|Egypt]]ian circumcision is the practice of male circumcision as observed and described by [[herodotus|Herodotus]] in the 5th century BCE. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] identifies it as one of the most distinctive [[egypt|Egypt]]ian customs — a practice the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians adopted for the sake of cleanliness and religious purity, and one that set them apart from nearly all other peoples of the ancient world. (004-004) (004-005)

## Description

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] records circumcision as part of a broader pattern of customs in which the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians behaved in ways opposite to other nations. Whereas other men, except those who had learned the practice from the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians, "have their members as nature made them, the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians practice circumcision." (004-004) The practice is described not as a medical procedure or a mark of social status but as a deliberate cultural choice rooted in the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian conception of bodily purity.

The text places circumcision among a list of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian reversals of ordinary custom: women trading in the market while men weave at home, men carrying burdens on their heads while women use their shoulders, and the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians writing from right to left. (004-004) In this framing, circumcision is presented not as an isolated rite but as one element of a comprehensive cultural system that the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians oriented around the perceived inversion of norms relative to other peoples.

### Religious and Hygienic Motivation

The primary motivation [[herodotus|Herodotus]] attributes to [[egypt|Egypt]]ian circumcision is cleanliness. He writes that the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians "circumcise themselves for the sake of cleanliness, preferring to be clean rather than comely." (004-005) This passage appears in a detailed description of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian religious scrupulousness, immediately following observations about daily washing of bronze drinking cups and the wearing of freshly washed linen garments — indicating that circumcision was understood as part of a broader regime of ritual and bodily purity.

The priests, who represented the highest standard of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian religious observance, extended the principle of bodily purity even further: they shaved their entire bodies every other day to prevent lice or any "foul thing" from being upon them while ministering to the gods, and they washed in cold water twice by day and twice by night. (004-005) [[herodotus|Herodotus]] presents circumcision as the layperson's counterpart to the priestly regime of purification — a permanent alteration of the body in service of the same ideal of cleanliness that the priests maintained through daily ritual.

### Transmission to Other Peoples

One of the most significant passages in [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s account concerns the diffusion of circumcision from [[egypt|Egypt]] to other peoples. He observes that "the Colchians, [[egypt|Egypt]]ians, and [[ethiopia-region|Ethiopia]]ns alone of all the races of men have practised circumcision from the first." (009-006) The [[phoenicians|Phoenicians]] and the [[syria|Syrians of Palestine]], by contrast, "confess themselves that they have learnt it from the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians," while the [[syria|Syrians]] dwelling near the rivers Thermodon and Parthenios, and their neighbors the Macronians, "say that they have learnt it lately from the Colchians." (009-006)

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] uses circumcision as ethnographic evidence for the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian origin of the Colchian people — a conclusion he reached after observing that Colchians shared the practice with [[egypt|Egypt]]ians, along with dark skin, curly hair, methods of flax-working, and similarities in language and manner of living. (009-006) He is unable to determine whether the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians or the [[ethiopia-region|Ethiopia]]ns originated the custom, describing it as "undoubtedly a most ancient custom," but he cites as proof of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian priority the observation that "those of the [[phoenicians|Phoenicians]] who have intercourse with Hellas cease to follow the example of the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians in this matter, and do not circumcise their children." (009-006) This detail suggests that circumcision was understood as a distinctly non-Greek, non-Hellenic practice — one that [[phoenicians|Phoenicians]] abandoned when they adopted Greek customs.

## Historical Context

By the 5th century BCE, circumcision was already an ancient and firmly established practice in [[egypt|Egypt]]. Evidence from Egyptian temple reliefs and mummified remains confirms that the practice predated [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s visit by at least two millennia. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] himself acknowledges its antiquity, calling it "undoubtedly a most ancient custom" whose ultimate origin he cannot determine — whether [[egypt|Egypt]]ian or [[ethiopia-region|Ethiopia]]n (009-006). In the wider Mediterranean world, circumcision was one of the most visible markers distinguishing [[egypt|Egypt]]ians and other non-Greek peoples from the [[hellenes|Hellenes]]. Its absence among the [[hellenes|Greeks]] was so definitive that, as [[herodotus|Herodotus]] notes, [[phoenicians|Phoenicians]] who adopted Greek customs abandoned the practice (009-006).

## Herodotus's Commentary

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] approaches circumcision with the same comparative ethnographic method he applies throughout his account of [[egypt|Egypt]]. He does not condemn or endorse the practice but treats it as a cultural datum that illuminates relationships between peoples. His observation that the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians prefer cleanliness over comeliness (004-005) implicitly contrasts [[egypt|Egypt]]ian values with Greek aesthetics — a comparison made explicit in his description of the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians as "religious excessively beyond all other men." (004-005)

The historian's treatment of circumcision also serves his broader argument about [[egypt|Egypt]]ian cultural primacy. Throughout the second book of his Histories, [[herodotus|Herodotus]] advances the thesis that many Greek religious and cultural institutions derive from [[egypt|Egypt]]. Circumcision is one of the few customs he does not claim was adopted by the [[hellenes|Greeks]]; instead, he notes its conspicuous absence among Hellenized peoples, reinforcing the boundary between [[egypt|Egypt]]ian-derived practice and Greek norms. (009-006) This restraint — acknowledging a practice that did not cross the cultural boundary — strengthens the credibility of his wider claims about [[egypt|Egypt]]ian influence.

## Significance

[[egypt|Egypt]]ian circumcision, as recorded by [[herodotus|Herodotus]], is significant for several reasons. First, it is one of the earliest written attestations of the practice in the Mediterranean world, providing evidence that circumcision was established in [[egypt|Egypt]] by at least the 5th century BCE. Second, [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s account demonstrates a sophisticated ethnographic method: he uses the distribution of circumcision as a cultural marker to trace relationships between peoples, distinguishing between independent originators ([[egypt|Egypt]]ians, [[ethiopia-region|Ethiopia]]ns, Colchians) and secondary adopters ([[phoenicians|Phoenicians]], [[syria|Syrians]]). (009-006)

Third, the framing of circumcision as a practice of cleanliness rather than a covenant or tribal mark reflects a distinctly [[egypt|Egypt]]ian rationale that differs from the later Israelite understanding recorded in the Hebrew Bible. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] preserves a pre-Abrahamic rationale for the practice rooted in [[egypt|Egypt]]ian religious culture. Fourth, the fact that the practice is discussed in a work of Greek historiography — and that [[herodotus|Herodotus]] feels the need to explain it to a Greek audience — underscores how alien the custom was to Hellenic norms and highlights the cultural distance between Greek and [[egypt|Egypt]]ian societies in the classical period.

## Sources

The following passages from [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s "An Account of [[egypt|Egypt]]" constitute the primary-source evidence for [[egypt|Egypt]]ian circumcision.

> The Egyptians in agreement with their climate, which is unlike any other, and with the river, which shows a nature different from all other rivers, established for themselves manners and customs in a way opposite to other men in almost all matters ... whereas other men, except such as have learnt otherwise from the Egyptians, have their members as nature made them, the Egyptians practice circumcision.
> (004-004)

> They are religious excessively beyond all other men, and with regard to this they have customs as follows:--they drink from cups of bronze and rinse them out every day, and not some only do this but all: they wear garments of linen always newly washed, and this they make a special point of practice: they circumcise themselves for the sake of cleanliness, preferring to be clean rather than comely.
> (004-005)

> The Colchians, Egyptians, and Ethiopians alone of all the races of men have practised circumcision from the first. The Phenicians and the Syrians who dwell in Palestine confess themselves that they have learnt it from the Egyptians, and the Syrians about the river Thermodon and the river Parthenios, and the Macronians, who are their neighbors, say that they have learnt it lately from the Colchians. These are the only races of men who practise circumcision, and these evidently practise it in the same manner as the Egyptians. Of the Egyptians themselves however and the Ethiopians, I am not able to say which learnt from the other, for undoubtedly it is a most ancient custom; but that the other nations learnt it by intercourse with the Egyptians, this among others is to me a strong proof, namely that those of the Phenicians who have intercourse with Hellas cease to follow the example of the Egyptians in this matter, and do not circumcise their children.
> (009-006)
