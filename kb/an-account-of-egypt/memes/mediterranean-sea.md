## Overview

The Mediterranean Sea forms the northern boundary of Egypt and is the maritime highway through which Egypt connected to the wider world in Herodotus's account. While Herodotus does not name it as "the Mediterranean" — the term is later — he constantly references the sea as the medium of Hellenic, Phoenician, and Egyptian interaction. Egyptian kings launched naval expeditions upon it, Phoenician traders traversed it, and it was across this sea that Helen of Troy was carried to Egypt.

## Location / Geography

The sea washes Egypt's northern coast from the Canobic mouth of the Nile in the west to the Pelusiac mouth in the east. Herodotus describes the coastline as a low-lying deltaic plain, with the city of Canopus at the western approach and Pelusium guarding the eastern entrance. Beyond Egypt's shore, the sea stretches north to the coasts of Asia Minor, Hellas, and the islands of the Aegean, and west to Libya (Africa). The seven mouths of the Nile empty into this sea, creating the marshy Delta that Herodotus so carefully catalogued.

## Historical Significance

The Mediterranean was central to Egypt's foreign relations in the period Herodotus describes. Sesostris was said to have been the first Egyptian king to acquire warships and campaign upon it (009-006). Necos commissioned Phoenician sailors to circumnavigate Libya, departing from the Erythraian Sea and returning through the Mediterranean via the Pillars of Heracles. The sea was also the route by which the Ionians and Carians arrived as mercenaries under Psammetichos, establishing Egypt's first sustained contact with the Hellenic world. Trade with Phoenicia, particularly through the port of Tyre, brought timber, purple dye, and luxury goods into Egypt.

## Herodotus's Description

Herodotus notes the Egyptian priests' careful distinction between the sea and the river — the Nile's water was drinkable and life-giving, while the sea was salt and foreign. The Delta's coastline was, in the priests' account, once underwater, having been built up over millennia by Nile silt (002-001). Herodotus also records that the Egyptian priests disdained seafaring, viewing the sea as an unclean element, which made the naval achievements of kings like Sesostris and Necos all the more remarkable in their telling.
