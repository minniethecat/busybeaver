## Overview

Pheros was a legendary [[egypt|Egypt]]ian king, the son of the great conqueror [[sesostris|Sesostris]], whose story [[herodotus|Herodotus]] records from the priests of [[memphis|Memphis]]. Unlike his father, Pheros undertook no military campaigns. His reign is remembered for a striking tale of impiety, blindness, and miraculous healing — a narrative that blends [[egypt|Egypt]]ian folklore with Greek storytelling conventions. (010-003)

## Background / Biography

Pheros inherited the kingdom after the death of his father [[sesostris|Sesostris]]. He made no warlike expedition, distinguishing himself from the militaristic pattern of his father's reign. According to the priests, Pheros's defining experience was a personal tragedy: he became blind. (010-003)

## Role in the Account

**The blinding of Pheros.** [[herodotus|Herodotus]] relates that during the reign of Pheros, [[nile-river|the Nile]] rose to an unprecedented height of eighteen cubits, flooding the land with extraordinary violence. Enraged by the destruction, Pheros seized a spear and hurled it into the midst of the raging flood. Immediately afterward, he was stricken with blindness — an affliction the priests presented as divine punishment for his impiety. (010-003)

**The healing.** Pheros remained blind for ten years. In the eleventh year, an oracle from the city of [[buto|Buto]] declared that the time of his punishment was complete, and that his sight would be restored if he washed his eyes with the urine of a woman who had been faithful to her husband and known no other man. (010-003)

Pheros tested the remedy first with his own wife, with no result. He then summoned the wives of many [[egypt|Egypt]]ians, testing each one. The vast majority failed to restore his sight. Only one woman succeeded — a fact that led Pheros to gather together all the unfaithful women in a city and burn it, then marry the faithful woman. His sight restored, Pheros made offerings at all the principal temples, most notably two stone obelisks dedicated at the temple of Helios. (010-003)

## Herodotus's Portrayal

The story of Pheros is a classic Herodotean set-piece, blending divine retribution, folk justice, and an aetiological explanation for the two obelisks at [[heliopolis|Heliopolis]]. The tale reflects [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s willingness to incorporate priestly narratives that blur the line between historical record and moral fable. The test of marital fidelity through urine — bizarre though it may seem — is presented matter-of-factly, characteristic of the historian's ethnographic method. (010-003)
