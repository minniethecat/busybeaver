## Overview

Cadytis was a great city of Syria, captured by the Egyptian king Necos (Necho II) following his victory over the Syrians at Magdolos. It represents the northernmost extent of Necos's military campaigns on land, complementing his naval activities in both the Mediterranean and Red Seas. Herodotus records the conquest as a milestone in Necos's reign. (015-005)

## Historical Context

In the reign of Necos (Necho II), Egypt pursued an ambitious program of both maritime and military expansion. Necos attempted to construct a canal from the Nile to the Erythraian (Red) Sea — a project later completed by the Persian king Dareios — and built triremes for both the Northern Sea (Mediterranean) and the Arabian Gulf. (015-005)

On land, Necos led his forces against the Syrians. After the battle at Magdolos, he captured Cadytis, which Herodotus notes was a great city of Syria. As a votive offering commemorating these victories, Necos dedicated the ceremonial dress he had worn in the campaigns to the god Apollo, sending it to the oracle at Branchidai, a Milesian sanctuary. (015-005)

## Significance

Cadytis is often identified by scholars with Gaza (ancient Kadytis), a major Philistine and later Syrian city on the coastal route between Egypt and the Levant. Its capture represented Egyptian control over the strategically vital corridor connecting Africa and Asia. Necos reigned for sixteen years in total before being succeeded by his son Psammis. (015-005)
