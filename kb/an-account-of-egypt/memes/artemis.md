## Overview

In [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s *Account of [[egypt|Egypt]]*, [[Artemis]] is the Greek name by which the historian identifies the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian cat-goddess Bastet (also called [[bubastis|Bubastis]]). The identification follows the pattern of *interpretatio graeca* found throughout the work. The festival of Artemis at the city of [[Bubastis]] was, according to [[herodotus|Herodotus]], the most zealously celebrated of all [[egypt|Egypt]]ian religious assemblies.

## The Festival at Bubastis

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] records that the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians were the first people to institute solemn assemblies, processions, and temple approaches — customs which the [[hellenes|Greeks]] later adopted. Among all [[egypt|Egypt]]ian religious gatherings, the festival of [[Artemis]] at [[Bubastis]] was celebrated "especially and with the greatest zeal and devotion" (006-002). This festival drew enormous crowds: [[herodotus|Herodotus]] reports that men and women sailing together to [[bubastis|Bubastis]] numbered as many as seven hundred thousand, excluding children (006-002).

The prominence of this festival reflects the central role of the goddess Bastet in [[egypt|Egypt]]ian popular religion during the Late Period. The city of [[Bubastis]] (modern [[bubastis|Tell Basta]]) in [[nile-river|the Nile]] [[nile-delta|Delta]] was the cult center of the goddess and a major pilgrimage destination.

## The Temple at Buto

The oracle of [[Leto]] at [[Buto]] contained "a temple of [[apollo|Apollo]] and Artemis" alongside the great temple-house of [[leto|Leto]] herself (015-001). This triad of [[leto|Leto]], [[apollo|Apollo]], and Artemis — the classic Delian triad of Greek religion — was mapped by [[herodotus|Herodotus]] onto [[egypt|Egypt]]ian deities, with Artemis identified as a daughter of [[Dionysos]] and [[Isis]]. According to the [[egyptian-priesthood|Egyptian priests]], "[[apollo|Apollo]] and Artemis are children of Dionysos and of [[isis|Isis]], and that [[leto|Leto]] became their nurse and preserver" (015-002). This genealogy differs markedly from the standard Greek account (where Artemis is daughter of [[zeus|Zeus]] and [[leto|Leto]]) and reflects [[egypt|Egypt]]ian theological traditions projected through a Greek lens.

## Divine Genealogy

The [[egypt|Egypt]]ian myth recorded by [[herodotus|Herodotus]] places [[Artemis]] and [[Apollo]] within a divine lineage distinct from Greek tradition. Their parents are said to be [[Dionysos]] (identified with [[osiris|Osiris]]) and [[Isis]], making Artemis and [[apollo|Apollo]] siblings of Horus in the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian scheme. [[Leto]] — rather than being their mother as in Greek myth — serves as their nurse and protector, hiding the children on the floating island of [[chemnis|Chemmis]] near [[Buto]] to shield them from [[Typhon]] (Set) (015-002).

## Significance

The association of [[Artemis]] with Bastet illustrates how [[herodotus|Herodotus]] navigated the complexities of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian religion for a Greek audience. By equating the cat-goddess of [[bubastis|Bubastis]] with Artemis, he rendered [[egypt|Egypt]]ian cult practice intelligible while recording distinctive features — such as the scale of the pilgrimage and the goddess's [[egypt|Egypt]]ian parentage — that preserved the foreignness of the original.
