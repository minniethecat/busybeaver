## Overview

Polycrates, son of Aiakes, was the tyrant of Samos and a guest-friend of the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian king [[amasis|Amasis]] ([[amasis|Ahmose II]]). [[herodotus|Herodotus]] mentions him in the "Account of [[egypt|Egypt]]" in connection with [[amasis|Amasis]]'s dedication of offerings at the temple of Hera on Samos, motivated by their personal bond of friendship. (017-002)

## Background / Biography

Polycrates ruled Samos during a period of extraordinary prosperity in the mid-6th century BCE, building a powerful navy and patronizing the arts. In [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s broader "Histories," Polycrates is the subject of a famous narrative about the dangers of excessive good fortune, in which [[amasis|Amasis]] advises him to discard something precious to avert divine envy. The "Account of [[egypt|Egypt]]" preserves only a fragment of this relationship, focused on [[amasis|Amasis]]'s dedications. (017-002)

## Role in the Account

Polycrates appears in the "Account of [[egypt|Egypt]]" as the recipient of [[amasis|Amasis]]'s goodwill. The [[egypt|Egypt]]ian king dedicated two wooden images of himself to Hera in the great temple at Samos, where they remained standing behind the temple doors until [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s own time. This dedication was explicitly motivated by the guest-friendship between [[amasis|Amasis]] and Polycrates, demonstrating [[amasis|Amasis]]'s active cultivation of diplomatic and personal ties with Greek rulers. (017-002)

The Samian dedications were part of a broader pattern of [[amasis|Amasis]]'s philhellenic religious patronage, which also included offerings at [[cyrene|Kyrene]] and Lindos.

## Sources
