## Overview

Rhodopis was a Greek courtesan (hetaira) of Thracian origin who lived in Egypt during the reign of [[Amasis]] (sixth century BCE). She is notable in Herodotus's account primarily because certain Greeks erroneously attributed to her the construction of one of the pyramids — specifically the third pyramid at Giza (that of Mycerinus). Herodotus emphatically corrects this misconception, providing biographical details that establish she could not possibly have possessed the immense wealth required for such a monument. Her story illustrates the intersection of Greek and Egyptian worlds in the Saite period, and the tendency of Greek popular tradition to embellish tales of Egypt with romantic attributions. (013-001)(013-002)

## Background / Biography

Rhodopis was brought to Egypt as a slave by Xanthes of Samos, intended to practise her profession as a courtesan. While in Egypt, she was redeemed from slavery for a considerable sum by Charaxos, a man from Mytilene on Lesbos, who was the son of Scamandronymos and the brother of the famous lyric poet [[Sappho]]. After gaining her freedom, Rhodopis chose to remain in Egypt. (013-002)

By her beauty and charm, Rhodopis accumulated substantial wealth — substantial, Herodotus notes, for a person of her station, though nowhere near the scale that would be required to build a pyramid. Herodotus emphasizes this point explicitly: those who attribute the pyramid to her do not even know who Rhodopis was, for they are unaware that she lived during the reign of [[Amasis]], many generations after the pyramid-building kings of the Old Kingdom. (013-001)(013-002)

As a votive offering, Rhodopis dedicated a remarkable gift at Delphi: a large number of iron roasting-spits (obeloi), which Herodotus himself saw heaped together behind the altar of the Chians. This dedication, he notes, served as a memorial of her wealth, and he mentions it as a curiosity worthy of record — iron spits being an unusual and distinctive offering. (013-002)

## Role in the Account

Rhodopis appears in [[Herodotus]]'s narrative as part of a larger Egyptian digression on the pyramids and their builders. After describing the pyramids of the great pharaohs Cheops, Chephren, and Mycerinus, Herodotus addresses a popular Greek tradition that attributed Mycerinus's pyramid to Rhodopis. He rejects this attribution as manifestly mistaken, using it as an occasion to demonstrate the superiority of his own investigative method — consulting Egyptian sources and chronology — over uncritical Greek hearsay. (013-001)

Herodotus's correction of the Rhodopis tradition is characteristic of his approach throughout the Egyptian account: he records popular stories but subjects them to scrutiny based on chronology, physical evidence, and the testimony of informed local sources. In this case, the sheer scale of the pyramid and the cost of its construction — "innumerable thousands of talents" — make a courtesan's involvement impossible, quite apart from the chronological discrepancy. (013-001)

## Sources

> The Hellenes say was built by the courtesan Rhodopis, not therein speaking rightly: and besides this it is evident to me that they who speak thus do not even know who Rhodopis was, for otherwise they would not have attributed to her the building of a pyramid like this, on which have been spent innumerable thousands of talents. Moreover they do not know that Rhodopis flourished in the reign of Amasis, and not in this king's reign.
> (013-001)

> Rhodopis came to Egypt brought by Xanthes the Samian, and having come thither to exercise her calling she was redeemed from slavery for a great sum by a man of Mytilene, Charaxos son of Scamandronymos and brother of Sappho the lyric poet. Thus was Rhodopis set free, and she remained in Egypt and by her beauty won so much liking that she made great gain of money.
> (013-002)
