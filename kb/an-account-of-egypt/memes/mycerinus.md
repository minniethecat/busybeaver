Mycerinus (Egyptian Menkaure) was a pharaoh of the [4th Dynasty](Old Kingdom) who built the third and smallest of the three great pyramids at Giza. He appears in [[Herodotus]]'s account as the son and successor of [[Cheops]], though Herodotus presents him as a ruler of markedly different character — one who reversed the injustices of his predecessors.

## The Pyramid of Mycerinus

Herodotus describes the third pyramid at Giza as a square measuring two hundred and eighty feet on each side, built of Ethiopian stone up to half its height. (013-001) Though far smaller than the pyramids of Cheops and Chephren, Herodotus notes that some Greeks nevertheless considered it the finest of the three works, and that a popular tradition erroneously attributed its construction to the courtesan [[Rhodopis]]. (013-001) Herodotus emphatically rejects this attribution, pointing out that [[Rhodopis]] lived many years later, during the reign of [[Amasis]], and that her wealth — though considerable — "was not enough to suffice for the cost of such a pyramid as this." (013-002)

## A Just King

Herodotus presents Mycerinus in a favourable light compared to his predecessors. Whereas [[Cheops]] and [[Chephren]] were said in the Egyptian tradition to have closed the temples and oppressed the people to build their pyramids, Mycerinus reopened the temples and allowed the people, who had been ground down to extreme misery, to return to their sacrifices and festivals. (013-003) Herodotus reports that Mycerinus was the most righteous judge of all the kings of Egypt, and on this account he received praise surpassing all others.

## The Oracle and the Shortened Reign

Despite his just rule, Mycerinus was struck by calamity: the death of his daughter, whom Herodotus records was buried in a hollow wooden cow overlaid with gold at Sais. An oracle from the city of [[Buto]] declared to Mycerinus that he had only six years left to live. In response, the king contrived to double his time by turning night into day — burning lamps throughout the night to enjoy twelve years' worth of life in six. (013-003)

## Succession

Mycerinus was succeeded by [[Asychis]], who was particularly noted for his reforms in the administration of justice. [[Asychis]] enacted a law permitting a man to borrow money on the security of his father's mummy — a custom that bound debt to the most sacred familial obligation in a culture devoted to the proper treatment of the dead. (013-003)

## Herodotus's Assessment

Herodotus's portrait of Mycerinus reflects a broader theme in his Egyptian account: the tension between monumental ambition and righteous rule. The Greek historian uses Mycerinus to illustrate that great pyramids are not necessarily the work of great kings, and that the Egyptian traditions he collected preserved nuanced judgments of rulers — distinguishing the builder from the tyrant.
