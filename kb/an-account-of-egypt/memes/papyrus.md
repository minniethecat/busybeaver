## Overview

Papyrus was one of the most versatile and economically significant plants of ancient Egypt. Herodotus mentions it in his introductory survey of Egyptian marvels — alongside the phoenix, the crocodile, and the pyramids — noting its use as both a food source and a material of daily life. (001-003) The plant was particularly important to the Egyptians who dwelt in the fenlands of the Delta.

Beyond its role as food, papyrus served in boat-building, sail-making, priestly sandals, and as the medium for Egypt's extensive historical records. Its cultivation and harvest were annual undertakings of the marsh-dwelling population, and its products reached every stratum of Egyptian society — from the priests of the great temples to the fishermen of the Delta fens.

## Description

### Food Source

The marsh-dwelling Egyptians harvested papyrus as a staple food. Each year, when the Nile flood receded, the papyrus grew abundantly in the fens. The inhabitants pulled up the plant and cut off the upper portions for other uses, keeping the lower portion — about a cubit in length — for consumption. Those who desired the finest quality would bake the lower stem in a red-hot oven before eating it; the remainder was eaten or sold in the markets. (008-005)

### Boat-Building and Sailing

Papyrus was essential to Egyptian watercraft. While large cargo vessels (called *baris*) were constructed from the thorny acacia, their internal seams were caulked with papyrus. The sails of these boats were also made from papyrus. These vessels, some capable of carrying many thousands of talents' burden, were abundant on the Nile. (009-002)

Smaller craft and rafts were bound together directly from bundles of papyrus stalks, providing lightweight vessels suited to the marshes and shallows of the Delta.

### Priestly Equipment

The Egyptian priesthood, whose purity regulations were exacting, wore sandals made exclusively of papyrus. Together with linen garments (always freshly washed), these papyrus sandals were the only footwear permitted to priests when they ministered to the gods. They washed themselves in cold water twice daily and twice nightly, and shaved their entire bodies every other day to prevent any impurity. (004-005)

### Ritual and Administrative Use

Papyrus served an official function in temple sacrifice. When a sacrificial ox was inspected and found ritually pure — free of black hairs and with naturally growing tail hairs — a priest would mark the animal by rolling a piece of papyrus around its horns, sealing it with earth and the impression of his signet-ring. Only then could the beast be taken for sacrifice. (004-006)

### Historical Records

Papyrus was the medium of Egyptian historical memory. When Herodotus visited the temple at Egyptian Thebes, the priests enumerated to him from a papyrus roll the names of three hundred and thirty kings who had ruled Egypt after the first king — of whom eighteen were Ethiopians, one was a native Egyptian woman (named Nitocris, the same as the Babylonian queen), and the rest were Egyptian men. (009-004)
