## Overview

Psammetichos (Psamtik I) was the first pharaoh of the 26th (Saite) Dynasty and one of the most consequential rulers in [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s "An Account of [[egypt|Egypt]]." Originally one of twelve kings who jointly ruled [[egypt|Egypt]], he rose to sole sovereignty through a combination of oracular fortune, foreign alliance, and strategic patience. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] devotes more narrative attention to Psammetichos than to almost any other [[egypt|Egypt]]ian king, presenting him as both a scientific inquirer and a shrewd political operator whose fifty-four-year reign (015-003) transformed [[egypt|Egypt]]'s relationship with the Greek world.

## Background / Biography

Psammetichos was the son of [[necos|Necos]], an [[egypt|Egypt]]ian ruler who was killed by the [[ethiopia-region|Ethiopia]]n king [[sabacos|Sabacos]]. After his father's death, Psammetichos fled to [[syria|Syria]] as a fugitive (014-008). When [[sabacos|Sabacos]] departed [[egypt|Egypt]] following a visionary dream, [[egypt|Egypt]]ians of the Saite district brought Psammetichos back from exile and restored him to his ancestral position.

[[egypt|Egypt]] was then governed by twelve kings who shared power and ruled justly. At a temple feast of [[hephaistos|Hephaistos]] in [[memphis|Memphis]], the chief priest mistakenly brought out only eleven golden libation cups for the twelve rulers. Psammetichos, standing last in line, received no cup and instead used his bronze helmet to pour a libation (014-008). The other eleven kings recalled an oracle declaring that whichever of them made libation with a bronze vessel would become sole king of [[egypt|Egypt]]. Though they determined Psammetichos had acted without premeditation, they stripped him of most of his authority and banished him to the fen-country.

Exiled for a second time, Psammetichos consulted the Oracle of [[leto|Leto]] at [[buto|Buto]], which gave the cryptic reply that "vengeance would come when men of bronze appeared from the sea" (014-008). Shortly afterwards, Ionian and Carian raiders landed in [[egypt|Egypt]] wearing bronze armour. Recognizing the fulfilment of the prophecy, Psammetichos persuaded these Greek mercenaries to join his cause and, with their support and that of loyal [[egypt|Egypt]]ians, overthrew the eleven kings to become sole ruler of all [[egypt|Egypt]] (015-001).

## Role in the Account

Psammetichos appears prominently across multiple chapters of [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s narrative in three distinct roles.

**The experimental king.** [[herodotus|Herodotus]] recounts that Psammetichos, wishing to determine which nation was the oldest, devised a famous linguistic experiment: he gave two newborn infants to a shepherd who raised them in total silence to discover what word they would first utter. After two years the children spoke "bekos," the Phrygian word for bread, leading the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians to concede that the Phrygians were an older people (001-008). The same spirit of empirical inquiry appears when Psammetichos tested the reported bottomless depth of [[nile-river|the Nile]]'s source by lowering a rope of many thousand fathoms and finding no bottom (003-005).

**The builder.** As sole king, Psammetichos commissioned the southern gateway of [[hephaistos|Hephaistos]]'s temple at [[memphis|Memphis]] and constructed a pillared court for the Apis bull, roofed by colossal statues twelve cubits high (014-008).

**The patron of Greek settlement.** Psammetichos granted land to his Ionian and Carian mercenaries on opposite banks of the Pelusian mouth of [[nile-river|the Nile]], known as the "Encampments." He also placed [[egypt|Egypt]]ian boys with them to learn Greek, creating the hereditary class of interpreters in [[egypt|Egypt]] (014-008). [[herodotus|Herodotus]] explicitly notes that through these settlers "we who are [[hellenes|Hellenes]] know by intercourse with them the certainty of all that which happened in [[egypt|Egypt]] beginning from king Psammetichos and afterwards" (014-008).

**The conqueror.** Psammetichos conducted the longest siege known to [[herodotus|Herodotus]], sitting before the [[syria|Syria]]n city of Azotos for twenty-nine of his fifty-four years before finally capturing it (015-003).

## Herodotus's Portrayal

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] treats Psammetichos with notable respect, presenting him as an intellectually curious and effective ruler. The child-language experiment, though framed skeptically (he notes the variant Greek story that Psammetichos cut out women's tongues), is nonetheless reported as the authoritative account from Memphite priests (001-008). The historian's regard is further evident when he describes [[apries|Apries]] as the most prosperous king "except only his forefather Psammetichos" (015-009), establishing Psammetichos as the benchmark of royal success.

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] also credits Psammetichos's reign as the historical watershed after which Greek knowledge of [[egypt|Egypt]] becomes reliable. The settlement of Ionian and Carian mercenaries created a continuous channel of information between [[egypt|Egypt]] and the Greek world, and [[herodotus|Herodotus]] implicitly acknowledges his own debt to this legacy (014-008).

> Now Psammetichos, when he was not able by inquiry to find out any means of knowing who had come into being first of all men, contrived a device of the following kind:--Taking two newborn children belonging to persons of the common sort he gave them to a shepherd to bring up at the place where his flocks were, with a manner of bringing up such as I shall say, charging him namely that no man should utter any word in their presence, and that they should be placed by themselves in a room where none might come, and at the proper time he should bring them she-goats, and when he had satisfied them with milk he should do for them whatever else was needed. These things Psammetichos did and gave him this charge wishing to hear what word the children would let break forth first after they had ceased from wailings without sense.
> (001-008)

> Then that one of them who was standing last in order, namely Psammetichos, since he had no cup took off from his head his helmet, which was of bronze, and having held it out to receive the wine he proceeded to make libation: likewise all the other kings were wont to wear helmets and they happened to have them then. Now Psammetichos held out his helmet with no treacherous meaning; but they taking note of that which had been done by Psammetichos and of the oracle, namely how it had been declared to them that whosoever of them should make libation with a bronze cup should be sole king of Egypt, recollecting, I say, the saying of the Oracle, they did not indeed deem it right to slay Psammetichos, since they found by examination that he had not done it with any forethought, but they determined to strip him of almost all his power and to drive him away into the fen-country.
> (014-008)

> So then holding that he had been grievously wronged by them, he thought how he might take vengeance on those who had driven him out: and when he had sent to the Oracle of Leto in the city of Buto, where the Egyptians have their most truthful Oracle, there was given to him the reply that vengeance would come when men of bronze appeared from the sea. And he was strongly disposed not to believe that bronze men would come to help him; but after no long time had passed, certain Ionians and Carians who had sailed forth for plunder were compelled to come to shore in Egypt, and they having landed and being clad in bronze armour, came to the fen-land and brought a report to Psammetichos that bronze men had come from the sea and were plundering the plain.
> (014-008)

> As for the fathomless depth of the source, he said that Psammetichos king of Egypt came to a trial of this matter; for he had a rope twisted of many thousand fathoms and let it down in this place, and it found no bottom.
> (003-005)
