## Overview

Egyptian statuary, as recorded by Herodotus, encompassed a wide range of stone monuments — colossal royal figures, temple images of gods and heroes, inscribed pillars marking the extent of conquest, and votive dedications sent abroad to Greek sanctuaries. Herodotus personally visited the great temples at Memphis and Thebes, examined inscribed rock-carvings in Ionia and Syria, and reported both Egyptian traditions and foreign-dedicated statues. His account offers a unique contemporary witness to the monumental landscape of Egypt in the mid-fifth century BCE, before the Persian conquest and subsequent transformations. (001-009)

What distinguishes Herodotus's treatment of Egyptian statuary from his descriptions of Greek or Persian monuments is his insistence on autopsy — the visual evidence of his own eyes. He explicitly travelled to Heliopolis, Thebes, and Memphis to cross-check the accounts of local priests, and in Syria-Palestine he examined the Sesostris pillars firsthand. This methodological commitment gives his catalogue of Egyptian statues and pillars a documentary weight unusual in classical ethnography.

## Description

### Royal Colossi as Memorials

 The most impressive category of Egyptian statuary in Herodotus's account consists of the colossal royal figures erected by pharaohs as memorials of their reigns. Sesostris (identified by modern scholars with one or more Middle Kingdom pharaohs) "left as memorials of himself in front of the temple of Hephaistos two stone statues of thirty cubits each, representing himself and his wife, and others of twenty cubits each representing his four sons." (010-002) The scale is extraordinary — thirty cubits (approximately 45 feet) for the royal couple — and the placement before the temple of Hephaistos at Memphis ensured maximum visibility.

Rhampsinitos (Rameses), the successor of Proteus, "left as a memorial of himself that gateway to the temple of Hephaistos which is turned towards the West, and in front of the gateway he set up two statues, in height five-and-twenty cubits." (011-007) Herodotus adds a fascinating cultic detail: the Egyptians called the northern statue "Summer" and the southern "Winter," and "to that one which they call Summer they do reverence and make offerings, while to the other which is called Winter they do no such things." (011-007) This differential treatment suggests that these colossi were not merely commemorative but were integrated into an active ritual programme — possibly relating to the seasonal cycle of the Nile's inundation and recession.

### Sesostris's Inscribed Pillars and Rock-Carvings

 Herodotus devotes considerable attention to the commemorative pillars erected by Sesostris throughout the lands he conquered. These pillars bore inscriptions recording his victories and, in some cases, carved emblems indicating the character of the conquered peoples. Herodotus states that "the pillars which Sesostris king of Egypt set up in the various countries are for the most part no longer to be seen extant; but in Syria Palestine I myself saw them existing with the inscription upon them which I have mentioned and the emblem." (009-006) The distinction between those peoples who submitted willingly (receiving pillars with a female emblem) and those conquered by force (receiving pillars without the emblem) is a detail Herodotus claims to have verified by autopsy.

Beyond the pillars, Herodotus describes rock-cut figures in Ionia: "Moreover in Ionia there are two figures of this man carved upon rocks, one on the road by which one goes from the land of Ephesos to Phocaia, and the other on the road from Sardis to Smyrna. In each place there is a figure of a man cut in the rock, of four cubits and a span in height, holding in his right hand a spear and in his left a bow and arrows." (009-006) Herodotus is explicit about the Egyptian and Ethiopian character of the equipment, and notes that "from the one shoulder to the other across the breast runs an inscription carved in sacred characters." Modern scholars identify these as Hittite or Luvian rock-reliefs rather than Egyptian, but Herodotus's attribution to Sesostris reflects the fifth-century Greek tendency to associate all monumental rock-carvings in Anatolia with the legendary Egyptian conqueror.

### Temple Images of Gods

 Within Egyptian temples, cult statues of gods and heroes served as focal points of worship. At the city of Chemmis in the Theban district, Herodotus describes a temple of Perseus son of Danae "of a square shape" with date-palms growing around it and "a gateway built of stone and of very great size, and at the entrance of it stand two large stone statues." (008-003) The temple housed an image of Perseus, whom the locals claimed appeared frequently in their land. The cult image of Zeus (Amun) was particularly distinctive: "the Egyptians make the image of Zeus with the face of a ram" — a practice shared with the Ammonians, whom Herodotus identifies as settlers from both Egypt and Ethiopia. (005-001)

### Votive Dedications Abroad

 Amasis, the last great pharaoh of independent Egypt, dedicated offerings at Greek sanctuaries — a practice that reflects his deliberate policy of cultivating alliances with the Greek world. Herodotus records that Amasis dedicated "at Kyrene an image of Athene covered over with gold and a figure of himself made like by painting; then in the temple of Athene at Lindos two images of stone and a corslet of linen worthy to be seen; and also at Samos two wooden figures of himself dedicated to Hera, which were standing even to my own time in the great temple, behind the doors." (017-002) The range of materials — gilded image, painted portrait, stone statues, wooden figures — and the geographic spread from Libya (Kyrene) to Rhodes (Lindos) to Samos attest to the scope of Amasis's diplomatic programme.

### Funerary Statuary

 Herodotus also records the Egyptian practice of creating wooden funerary figures. After the most expensive form of embalming, the kinsfolk "have a wooden figure made in the shape of a man, and when they have had this made they enclose the corpse, and having shut it up within, they store it then in a sepulchral chamber, setting it to stand upright against the wall." (008-002) These wooden enclosures — effectively anthropoid coffins — represent a different category of Egyptian statuary, one associated with private burial rather than royal or divine commemoration.

### Forced Labour and Monumental Construction

 The colossi of Sesostris were not merely artistic achievements — they were products of conquest. Herodotus records that Sesostris "employed the multitude which he had brought in of those whose lands he had subdued" to draw the enormous stones to the temple of Hephaistos and to dig the canals of Egypt. (010-002) This linkage between military expansion, captive labour, and monumental construction is a theme that runs throughout Herodotus's account of Egyptian kingship.

## Materials and Techniques

Herodotus's account implicitly catalogues the range of materials employed in Egyptian statuary. Stone was the predominant medium for royal monuments — the colossi of Sesostris, the temple statues at Chemmis, and the inscribed pillars were all carved from Egyptian granite and limestone quarried from the eastern desert and the Aswan region. The precision of Egyptian stone-carving is attested by the survival of these monuments into Herodotus's own day.

Wood was used for votive statues, such as Amasis's two figures dedicated to Hera at Samos, and for funerary enclosures in the form of anthropoid coffins. Gold was applied as gilding — Amasis's image of Athene at Kyrene was "covered over with gold" — and painting was employed for portrait figures, as with Amasis's painted self-portrait dedicated at Kyrene.

The scale of the royal colossi required extraordinary logistical coordination. Herodotus's account of the forced labour used to "draw the stones" to the temple of Hephaistos provides a rare glimpse of the human cost behind monumental construction. The stones were of "very good size," and the workforce consisted of prisoners of war — a pattern repeated throughout Egyptian dynastic history, from the pyramid-builders of the Old Kingdom to the temple-builders of the New Kingdom.

## Historical Context

The statuary Herodotus describes spans several centuries of Egyptian history, though his chronology compresses multiple dynasties into a continuous narrative. The colossi attributed to Sesostris are likely products of the Middle Kingdom (c. 2055–1650 BCE), specifically the reign of Senusret III or Amenemhat III, whose military campaigns into Nubia and the Levant align with Herodotus's account of the conqueror-king. Rhampsinitos corresponds to Ramesses II (c. 1279–1213 BCE) of the Nineteenth Dynasty, whose building programme at Memphis included the western gateway of the temple of Ptah (whom Herodotus identifies as Hephaistos).

The votive dedications of Amasis (Ahmose II, r. 570–526 BCE) belong to the final decades of independent Egypt before the Persian conquest. His gifts to Greek sanctuaries were part of a calculated diplomatic strategy — by cultivating alliances with Greek city-states and their oracles, Amasis sought to counterbalance the growing power of the Persian Empire.

## Significance

Herodotus's treatment of Egyptian statuary serves multiple functions in his *Histories*. As an autopsy-based account, it provides a first-hand description of monuments — some extant, some already ruined — that defined the Egyptian landscape in the mid-fifth century BCE. His attention to the inscriptions, materials, and cultic functions of statues distinguishes his approach from mere marvel-mongering. The recurring motif of royal colossi as "memorials" (*mnemata*) left by kings connects statuary to Herodotus's broader interest in how rulers project power through permanent monuments — a theme that resonates with his accounts of Persian, Lydian, and Babylonian royal display elsewhere in the *Histories*.

The differential cultic treatment of the Summer and Winter statues before the western gateway of the Hephaistos temple is particularly significant. It suggests that Egyptian temple statuary was not merely decorative or commemorative but was embedded in an active cycle of seasonal ritual — a dimension of Egyptian monumentality that purely archaeological approaches might miss.
