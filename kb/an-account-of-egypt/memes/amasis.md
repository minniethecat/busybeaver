## Overview

[[Amasis]] (Ahmose II) was a pharaoh of the [[26th Dynasty]] who reigned circa 570--526 BCE. He rose from common origins to overthrow his predecessor [[Apries]] and presided over what [[Herodotus]] describes as the most prosperous period in [[egypt|Egypt]]ian history (016-004). Amasis is notable in the *Account* for his shrewd political wisdom, his fondness for the [[Hellenes]], the extensive building works and dedications he sponsored both within [[Egypt]] and abroad, and his pivotal role in granting the city of [[Naucratis]] to Greek settlers.

## Background / Biography

Amasis came from the district of [[Sais]], specifically the city of Siuph, and was of undistinguished birth (016-003). Before becoming king, his reputation was that of a drinker and a jester rather than a serious-minded man (016-003). [[herodotus|Herodotus]] records that during his private life Amasis would resort to theft when his means ran short, and was alternately convicted or absolved by various oracles — a pattern that later shaped his religious patronage as king, when he honoured only those gods whose oracles had convicted him (016-003).

The manner of his rise to power was accidental in origin. [[apries|Apries]], facing a revolt after a disastrous expedition against [[Cyrene]], sent Amasis to persuade the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian troops to disperse. Instead, one of the rebels placed a helmet on Amasis's head and declared him king (015-009). Amasis accepted the role, prepared for war, and — after a defiant response to [[apries|Apries]]'s envoy Patarbemis — met [[apries|Apries]] at [[Momemphis]] and defeated him in battle (016-002). [[apries|Apries]] was taken alive and later handed over to the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians, who strangled him (016-002).

## Role in the Account

Amasis figures prominently in the later chapters of [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s narrative, where he exemplifies the theme of a wise ruler who, through prudence rather than force, secures the loyalty of his subjects. When the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians initially despised him for his common origins, Amasis won them over with the parable of the golden foot-basin: he melted down a basin used for washing feet and had it remade into a divine image, then pointed out that just as the basin was now revered, so too should he be — a commoner who had become king (016-003).

His domestic achievements include the law requiring every [[egypt|Egypt]]ian to declare his source of livelihood annually to his district ruler, a law that [[Solon]] later adopted for [[Athens]] (016-004). His foreign policy was marked by a strategic embrace of the Hellenic world: he formed alliances with [[Cyrene]], married the Kyrenian princess [[Ladike]], sent gifts to Greek cities including [[Lindos]] and [[Samos]], and was the first [[egypt|Egypt]]ian ruler to conquer [[Cyprus]] and impose tribute (017-002). Amasis also maintained guest-friendship with [[Polycrates]], the tyrant of Samos — though the relationship was famously terminated by [[polycrates|Polycrates]]' excessive prosperity (018-001).

The chronology of even the [[egyptian-religion|Egyptian gods]] is anchored to Amasis's reign by [[herodotus|Herodotus]], who notes that the twelve gods — including [[Heracles]] — were begotten seventeen thousand years before the beginning of the reign of Amasis (005-002).

## Herodotus's Portrayal

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] portrays Amasis with a mixture of admiration and colour. While he credits Amasis with making [[egypt|Egypt]] more prosperous than ever before and praises his wisdom, he also recounts anecdotes of the king's roguish youth and his habit of dividing the day between serious business in the morning and drinking and jesting in the afternoon (016-003). When his friends rebuked him for this, Amasis replied with a simile of the bow:

> "They who have bows stretch them at such time as they wish to use them, and when they have finished using them they loose them again; for if they were stretched tight always they would break, so that the men would not be able to use them when they needed them. So also is the state of man."
> (016-003)

This blend of statesmanship and irreverence, along with the story of his theft-ridden youth, suggests that [[herodotus|Herodotus]] — or his [[egypt|Egypt]]ian informants — regarded Amasis as a figure whose very human flaws made his rise and success all the more remarkable. His story also illustrates a broader Herodotean theme: that wisdom and effective rule do not require noble birth, and that men of humble origin may surpass their aristocratic predecessors in achievement.

## Sources

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] describes the prosperity of [[egypt|Egypt]] under Amasis in sweeping terms:

> "In the reign of Amasis it is said that Egypt became more prosperous than at any other time before, both in regard to that which comes to the land from the river and in regard to that which comes from the land to its inhabitants, and that at this time the inhabited towns in it numbered in all twenty thousand."
> (016-004)

The king's openness to the [[hellenes|Hellenes]] is a central theme in the *Account*:

> "Amasis became a lover of the Hellenes; and besides other proofs of friendship which he gave to several among them, he also granted the city of Naucratis for those of them who came to Egypt to dwell in."
> (016-005)

On his strategic diplomacy with [[cyrene|Cyrene]]:

> "Amasis moreover made an agreement for friendship and alliance with the Kyrenians; and he resolved also to marry a wife from them."
> (017-001)

Further passages cover his rise from commoner to king (015-009, 016-002--016-003), his alliance and marriage with [[ladike|Ladike]] of [[cyrene|Cyrene]] (017-001), his numerous dedications in Hellas, and his conquest of Cyprus (017-002). Even the chronology of the [[egyptian-religion|Egyptian gods]] is anchored to his reign, as [[herodotus|Herodotus]] notes that the twelve gods — including [[heracles|Heracles]] — were begotten seventeen thousand years before the beginning of the reign of Amasis (005-002).
