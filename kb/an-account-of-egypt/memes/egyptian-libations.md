## Overview

Libations — the ritual pouring of wine as an offering to the gods — appear in Herodotus's account of Egypt in two significant contexts: the standard procedure of ox sacrifice, and the fateful bronze-cup oracle that determined the sole kingship of Egypt. In both cases, the act of pouring wine was not merely a gesture of piety but a formal procedure governed by precise rules, the violation of which could carry grave personal and political consequences. (004-006)

## Libations in Egyptian Sacrifice

Herodotus describes the standard Egyptian sacrificial procedure in considerable detail. After a beast had been examined and declared clean by a priest — a process that involved inspecting the animal's hairs, tongue, and tail for any sign of impurity — the animal was sealed with papyrus and signet-ring and led to the altar. The sacrificers then kindled a fire and poured libations of wine over the altar so that the wine ran down upon the victim. Only after calling upon the god did they cut the animal's throat. The pouring of wine was not an optional flourish but an integral step in the sacrificial sequence, positioned between the kindling of the fire and the slaughter itself. (004-006)

The libation was followed by other distinctive rites: the head was severed and, after imprecations were spoken over it, sold to Greek traders in the marketplace — or, where no Greeks were present, cast into the river. None of the Egyptians, Herodotus notes, would eat the head of any animal, a prohibition directly linked to this sacrificial custom. The libation of wine thus served as the ritual bridge between the mundane preparation of the animal and its transformation into a sacred offering. (004-006)

## The Libation Oracle of the Twelve Kings

The most politically consequential libation in Herodotus's Egyptian narrative occurred not at a cattle sacrifice but at a festival in the temple of Hephaistos at Memphis. Twelve kings ruled Egypt jointly, having made a pact to live in friendship and not to depose one another. An oracle had declared that whichever of them poured a libation with a bronze cup in the temple of Hephaistos would become sole king of all Egypt. The kings therefore took care to use only golden cups when they assembled in the temples. (014-005)

The fateful moment came on the last day of a feast. After performing the sacrifice, the chief-priest brought out the golden cups for the libation but miscounted, producing only eleven cups for the twelve kings. Psammetichos, who stood last in the order, found himself without a cup. Resourcefully, he removed his bronze helmet from his head, held it out to receive the wine, and poured his libation with it. The other kings, remembering the oracle, recognized the portent: though they determined that Psammetichos had acted without forethought, they stripped him of most of his power and drove him into the fen-country. This expulsion, which was meant to neutralize the oracle, instead set in motion the chain of events that would fulfill it — Psammetichos, aided by Ionian and Carian mercenaries in bronze armor (the "men of bronze" foretold by another oracle), overthrew the eleven kings and became sole ruler of Egypt. (014-008)

## Significance

The libation customs described by Herodotus reveal several features of Egyptian religious and political culture. First, ritual procedure was governed by meticulous rules: the sacrificial animal was examined for purity, sealed by a priest, and the wine offering was poured at a precise moment in the sacrificial sequence. Second, the libation was a public act of such gravity that even the vessel used — gold or bronze — could carry oracular weight. The bronze-cup story highlights the Egyptian belief that the gods communicated through ritual objects and actions, and that a man's kingship could be signified by something as simple as the cup from which he poured wine. Finally, the narrative illustrates Herodotus's method of interweaving religious custom with political history: the technical description of sacrificial libation in 004-006 provides the ritual background against which the dramatic story of Psammetichos and the helmet in 014-008 becomes legible.
