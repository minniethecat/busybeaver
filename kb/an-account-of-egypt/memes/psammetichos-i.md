## Overview

Psammetichos I (Psamtik I) was the founder of the Twenty-sixth (Saite) Dynasty and one of the most prominent pharaohs in Herodotus's *Account of Egypt*. Herodotus credits him with the famous experiment to determine the oldest race of mankind, records his rise from one of twelve co-ruling kings to sole ruler of Egypt, and describes his fifty-four-year reign marked by military campaigns, garrison establishments, and the consultation of oracles.

## The Child-Language Experiment

Herodotus relates that before Psammetichos, the Egyptians believed themselves to be the oldest race of mankind. Psammetichos, desiring to test this claim, devised an experiment: he took two newborn children and gave them to a shepherd to raise in isolation, with instructions that no one should speak a word in their presence. (001-008) The shepherd was to observe what word the children would first utter spontaneously. After two years, the children stretched out their hands and cried "bekos," which the king's investigators found to be the Phrygian word for bread. From this, Psammetichos concluded that the Phrygians, not the Egyptians, were the most ancient race. (001-008)

## Rise to Power: The Twelve Kings

Herodotus recounts that Egypt was once ruled by twelve kings who governed jointly and justly after the reign of the priest-king Sethos. (014-008) An oracle had foretold that whichever of them poured a libation with a bronze cup in the temple of Hephaistos would rule all Egypt. During a feast, the chief priest inadvertently brought out only eleven golden cups for the twelve kings. Psammetichos, standing last, took off his bronze helmet and used it to pour the libation — unwittingly fulfilling the oracle. (014-008) The other kings, recognizing the omen, exiled Psammetichos to the marshes. But he consulted the Oracle of Leto at Buto and received the prophecy that "vengeance would come from the sea, when bronze men should appear" (014-008) — a prophecy fulfilled when Ionian and Carian pirates in bronze armor landed on the Egyptian coast. With their support, Psammetichos defeated the other kings and became sole ruler. (014-008)

## Reign and Achievements

Psammetichos ruled Egypt for fifty-four years, "of which for thirty years save one he was sitting before Azotos, a great city of Syria, besieging it, until at last he took it" (015-003) — the longest siege in recorded history, according to Herodotus. Under his reign, garrisons were established at three strategic frontier positions: at Elephantine facing Ethiopia, at Daphnai of Pelusion facing Arabia and Assyria, and at Marea facing Libya. (003-008)

He also attempted to measure the depth of the fabled source of the Nile, "having a rope twisted of many thousand fathoms and let it down in this place, and it found no bottom" (003-005) — an endeavor that confirmed the unfathomable character of the Nile's origin in Egyptian cosmology.

Herodotus also reports that Psammetichos consulted the Oracle of Leto at Buto after his exile, an episode that led to the establishment of the Ionian and Carian mercenary presence in Egypt. (015-001) He was considered the most prosperous of all Egyptian kings up to his time, later surpassed only by Amasis. (015-009)

## Legacy

Psammetichos was succeeded by his son Necos, who began the canal to the Erythraian Sea later completed by Dareios the Persian. (015-004) His grandson [[Psammis]] ruled for six years, and his great-grandson [[Apries]] for twenty-five years. Psammetichos I thus founded a dynasty that, through his descendants, shaped Egypt's relationship with the Greek world — a legacy Herodotus traces in detail throughout the second half of his *Account*.
