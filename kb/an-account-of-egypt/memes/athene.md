## Overview

Athene, known to the Greeks as Athena, appears in Herodotus's "Account of Egypt" through her identification with the Egyptian goddess worshipped at the city of Sais. Herodotus records that the Egyptians held a major festival in her honour at Sais, known as the _Lychnocaia_ (Feast of Lamps), and describes her temple as the site of royal tombs and oracular practice. The identification of Athene with the Saitic goddess (Neith in Egyptian theology) illustrates the interpretatio graeca that Herodotus consistently applies to Egyptian religion throughout his account.

## In Herodotus's Account

Herodotus mentions Athene in several contexts. The scribe of the sacred treasury of Athene at Sais claimed to know the sources of the Nile, describing two mountains between Syene and Elephantine from whose midpoint the river's fathomless sources flowed (041). The festival of Athene at Sais, the _Lychnocaia_, was held at night with thousands of lamps burning in the open air — saucers of salt and oil with floating wicks (083). The temple of Athene at Sais served as the burial place for the kings of that district, including Apries, who was buried "in the temple of Athene, close to the sanctuary, on the left hand as you enter" (245). Amasis, who came from the district of Sais, built a temple-gateway for Athene that Herodotus describes as "a great marvel," surpassing all previous works in height and the size of its stones (247). Amasis also dedicated offerings to Athene abroad, including a gilded image at Kyrene and stone images at Lindos (257).

## Oracle and Divination

Herodotus lists Athene among the gods who possessed oracles in Egypt, alongside Heracles, Apollo, Artemis, Ares, Zeus, and Leto (105). The Egyptians, he notes, were the first to assign the art of divination to specific gods rather than to individual men. Amasis, before becoming king, was said to have been brought before various oracles when accused of theft, being sometimes convicted and sometimes absolved — an experience that later shaped which temples he honoured as king (247).

## Festival at Sais

The festival of Athene at Sais, the _Lychnocaia_, was one of the six great Egyptian religious assemblies enumerated by Herodotus. He describes the Egyptians kindling "lamps many in number in the open air round about the houses" throughout the night, with saucers filled with salt and oil, the wick floating on the surface. This festival was one of several that Herodotus uses as evidence that the Hellenes learned their religious practices — solemn assemblies, processions, and temple approaches — from the Egyptians (083).

## Sources

> As to the sources of the Nile, not one either of the Egyptians or of the Libyans or of the Hellenes, who came to speech with me, professed to know anything, except the scribe of the sacred treasury of Athene at the city of Sais in Egypt.
> (041)

> At the times when they gather together at the city of Sais for their sacrifices, on a certain night they all kindle lamps many in number in the open air round about the houses; now the lamps are saucers full of salt and oil mixed, and the wick floats by itself on the surface, and this burns during the whole night; and to the festival is given the name Lychnocaia.
> (083)

> They strangled him, and after that buried him in the burial-place of his fathers: this is in the temple of Athene, close to the sanctuary, on the left hand as you enter.
> (245)

> First in Sais he built and completed for Athene a temple-gateway which is a great marvel, and he far surpassed herein all who had done the like before, both in regard to height and greatness, so large are the stones and of such quality.
> (247)
