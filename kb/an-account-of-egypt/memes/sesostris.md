## Overview

Sesostris is the most celebrated conqueror-king in [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s *Account of [[egypt|Egypt]]*. According to the [[egyptian-priesthood|Egyptian priests]] who served as [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s informants, Sesostris led naval expeditions from the [[erythraian-sea|Arabian Gulf]], marched his army across Asia and into Europe as far as Scythia and Thrace, and erected commemorative pillars in the lands he subdued. (009-006) Upon his return to [[egypt|Egypt]], he survived an assassination attempt by his brother, took vengeance, and then employed the captives from his campaigns to construct the temple of [[hephaistos|Hephaistos]] and dig the canal network that reshaped [[egypt|Egypt]]'s geography. (010-001) (010-002) [[herodotus|Herodotus]] credits Sesostris with being the only [[egypt|Egypt]]ian king to rule over [[ethiopia-region|Ethiopia]], and his legacy endured long enough that the priest of [[hephaistos|Hephaistos]] invoked his memory to rebuff [[dareios|Darius]] the [[persian|Persian]]. (010-002)

## Background / Biography

The [[egyptian-priesthood|priests of Egypt]] placed Sesostris in the succession of early [[egypt|Egypt]]ian kings, coming after a queen named Nitocris. (009-006) [[herodotus|Herodotus]] provides no precise dates for his reign, but his position in the narrative—before [[pheros|Pheros]], [[proteus|Proteus]], [[rhampsinitos|Rhampsinitos]], and the pyramid-building kings—places him in what would later be identified as the Middle Kingdom period, corresponding approximately to the 19th century BCE. Sesostris was said to have six sons and a wife who accompanied him on his campaigns and whose counsel proved decisive in a moment of mortal danger. (010-001) After his death, the throne passed to his son [[pheros|Pheros]], who undertook no military expeditions and whose reign was marked by a personal tragedy involving blindness. (010-003)

## Role in the Account

Sesostris occupies a central place in [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s narrative of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian kings. The historian presents him as the archetypal conqueror whose achievements set the standard against which later rulers—[[egypt|Egypt]]ian and foreign alike—were measured. His first campaign was a naval expedition launched from the [[erythraian-sea|Arabian Gulf]], subduing the peoples along the [[erythraian-sea|Erythraian Sea]] until shoals made further navigation impossible. (009-006) His second and more extensive campaign was a land march across the continent, where he subdued every nation in his path. In the lands of those who resisted valiantly, he erected pillars inscribed with his name, his country, and an account of his victory; in the lands of those who surrendered without a fight, he added the emblem of female genitalia to the pillar, signifying cowardice. (009-006)

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] reports that Sesostris crossed from Asia into Europe and subdued the Scythians and Thracians, marking what he believed was the furthest extent of the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian army. (009-006) On his return journey, he may have left behind a portion of his army at the river Phasis, and [[herodotus|Herodotus]] marshals this as evidence that the Colchians are of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian descent—citing shared practices of circumcision, linen-working, language, and physical appearance. (009-006)

After his return to [[egypt|Egypt]], Sesostris's brother (who had been left as regent) attempted to murder him and his sons by trapping them in a house set ablaze at Daphnai in the district of [[pelusion|Pelusion]]. (010-001) Sesostris survived only because his wife advised him to lay two of his six sons across the fire as a human bridge, allowing the rest of the family to escape. (010-001)

Having taken vengeance on his brother, Sesostris directed the captives from his wars to major construction projects. They hauled the great stones for the temple of [[hephaistos|Hephaistos]] and dug the network of canals that crisscrossed [[egypt|Egypt]], transforming a land previously suitable for horses and chariots into one accessible only by boat. (010-002) He instituted a land-redistribution system that, in [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s estimation, gave rise to the art of geometry: each [[egypt|Egypt]]ian received an equal square plot and paid rent proportional to its size, with the king dispatching surveyors to remeasure whenever [[nile-river|the Nile]] flood eroded a holding. (010-002) The canal network that Sesostris's captives excavated outlasted his reign: in a later context, the [[ethiopia-region|Ethiopia]]n king [[sabacos|Sabacos]] employed [[egypt|Egypt]]ian wrongdoers to raise embankments, building upon the earthworks first created by Sesostris's captives. (013-004)

Sesostris was, according to the priests, the only [[egypt|Egypt]]ian king to hold dominion over [[ethiopia-region|Ethiopia]]. He erected colossal statues before the temple of [[hephaistos|Hephaistos]]: two of thirty cubits representing himself and his wife, and four of twenty cubits representing his sons. (010-002) These statues feature in a later anecdote: when [[dareios|Darius]] the [[persian|Persian]] wished to erect his own statue in front of them, the priest of [[hephaistos|Hephaistos]] refused, arguing that [[dareios|Darius]] had not matched Sesostris's conquests—specifically noting that [[dareios|Darius]] had failed to subdue the Scythians whereas Sesostris had succeeded. (010-002)

## Herodotus's Portrayal

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] treats Sesostris with evident admiration, presenting him as the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian king par excellence. His account relies heavily on the testimony of [[egyptian-priesthood|Egyptian priests]], whom he cites repeatedly with the formula "the priests said" or "they told me." (009-006) (010-001) (010-002) The historian inserts his own judgment at several points—for instance, when he states his personal conviction that the Colchians are of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian origin, and when he corrects those who misidentify the Ionian rock-carvings of Sesostris as depictions of Memnon. (009-006) The portrayal blends historical kernel with legendary embellishment. The pillars and rock-carvings that [[herodotus|Herodotus]] claims to have seen with his own eyes in [[syria|Syria]] Palestine and Ionia lend an empirical basis to the narrative, even as the scale of Sesostris's conquests—from the [[erythraian-sea|Erythraian Sea]] to Scythia—strains credulity. (009-006) [[herodotus|Herodotus]] does not question the priests' account; instead he amplifies it with his own observations and ethnographic theories about cultural diffusion, particularly the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian origins of Colchian civilization.

## Sources

Primary-source quotations from [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s *An Account of [[egypt|Egypt]]* (trans. G. C. Macaulay):

> He (the priests said) first of all set out with ships of war from the Arabian gulf and subdued those who dwelt by the shores of the Erythraian Sea, until as he sailed he came to a sea which could no further be navigated by reason of shoals: then secondly, after he had returned to Egypt, according to the report of the priests he took a great army and marched over the continent, subduing every nation which stood in his way: and those of them whom he found valiant and fighting desperately for their freedom, in their lands he set up pillars which told by inscriptions his own name and the name of his country, and how he had subdued them by his power; but as to those of whose cities he obtained possession without fighting or with ease, on their pillars he inscribed words after the same tenor as he did for the nations which had shown themselves courageous, and in addition he drew upon them the hidden parts of a woman, desiring to signify by this that the people were cowards and effeminate.
> (009-006)

> Then Sesostris, having returned to Egypt and having taken vengeance on his brother employed the multitude which he had brought in of those whose lands he had subdued, as follows:—these were they who drew the stones which in the reign of this king were brought to the temple of Hephaistos, being of very good size; and also these were compelled to dig all the channels which now are in Egypt; and thus (having no such purpose) they caused Egypt, which before was all fit for riding and driving, to be no longer fit for this from thenceforth... He moreover alone of all the Egyptian kings had rule over Ethiopia; and he left as memorials of himself in front of the temple of Hephaistos two stone statues of thirty cubits each, representing himself and his wife, and others of twenty cubits each representing his four sons: and long afterwards the priest of Hephaistos refused to permit Dareios the Persian to set up a statue of himself in front of them, saying that deeds had not been done by him equal to those which were done by Sesostris the Egyptian; for Sesostris had subdued other nations besides, not fewer than he, and also the Scythians; but Dareios had not been able to conquer the Scythians: wherefore it was not just that he should set up a statue in front of those which Sesostris had dedicated, if he did not surpass him in his deeds.
> (010-002)
