# Typhon

## Overview

Typhon, in [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s account of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian religion, is a malevolent deity who opposed the ruling gods of [[egypt|Egypt]]. He appears in the context of the [[osiris|Osiris]] myth cycle, where he is described as seeking to destroy the son of [[osiris|Osiris]]. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] identifies him through the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian theological framework, where Typhon is the adversary overcome by Oros (whom the [[hellenes|Hellenes]] call [[apollo|Apollo]]), marking the transition from divine to human rulership of [[egypt|Egypt]] (013-009).

## Description

In the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian cosmology as recorded by [[herodotus|Herodotus]], Typhon occupied a specific position in the divine succession. The [[egyptian-priesthood|priests of Egypt]] recounted that gods once ruled over the land directly, and the last of these divine rulers was Oros the son of [[osiris|Osiris]], "whom the [[hellenes|Hellenes]] call [[apollo|Apollo]]: he was king over [[egypt|Egypt]] last, having deposed Typhon" (013-009). This deposition marked the end of divine kingship in [[egypt|Egypt]].

The second context in which Typhon appears is the myth of the floating island at [[buto|Buto]]. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] records that [[leto|Leto]], one of the eight primordial gods dwelling at [[buto|Buto]], received [[apollo|Apollo]] from [[isis|Isis]] for safekeeping, "concealing him in the island which is said now to be a floating island, at that time when Typhon came after him seeking everywhere and desiring to find the son of [[osiris|Osiris]]" (015-002). Typhon's role here is that of a pursuer, hunting the infant [[apollo|Apollo]] across [[egypt|Egypt]].

## Significance

Typhon's presence in [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s narrative serves two purposes. First, it establishes the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian theological chronology — the gods ruled, Typhon was deposed by Oros, and thereafter human kings governed [[egypt|Egypt]] under the priesthood. Second, it provides the mythological backdrop for the Oracle of [[buto|Buto]], one of the most important religious sites [[herodotus|Herodotus]] describes. The account of Typhon's pursuit of [[apollo|Apollo]] explains the sacred geography of the floating island at [[buto|Buto]].

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] also uses the Typhon-[[osiris|Osiris]] identification to bridge [[egypt|Egypt]]ian and Greek religion. He notes that "[[osiris|Osiris]] in the tongue of Hellas is Dionysos" (013-009), and that [[apollo|Apollo]] and [[artemis|Artemis]] are children of "Dionysos and of [[isis|Isis]]" (015-002), establishing a syncretic framework where [[egypt|Egypt]]ian and Hellenic pantheons correspond.
