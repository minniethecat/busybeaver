## Overview

Linen was the fundamental textile of ancient Egyptian life. From the garments of commoners to the vestments of priests, linen was universally worn, prized for its purity and cleanliness. Herodotus records that the Egyptians took particular care with their linen clothing, wearing it "always newly washed" as a mark of their exceptional piety. (004-005)

## Linen and Egyptian Piety

Herodotus describes the Egyptians as "religious excessively beyond all other men," and their devotion to linen was a visible expression of this piety. Every Egyptian, not merely the wealthy or the priesthood, made a point of wearing freshly washed linen garments. The priests in particular were bound by strict rules: they wore linen garments exclusively, along with sandals made of papyrus. No wool or any other material was permitted to touch their bodies when they ministered to the gods. They bathed in cold water twice each day and twice each night, further reinforcing the connection between linen, cleanliness, and ritual purity. (004-005)

## The Calasiris and Daily Dress

The characteristic garment of the Egyptians was the *calasiris* — a linen tunic with fringes worn about the legs. Over this they threw a white woolen cloak, though Herodotus notes that woolen garments were strictly forbidden inside the temples and could not be used for burial. This prohibition aligned Egyptian practice with the so-called Orphic and Bacchic mysteries of the Greeks, which Herodotus believed were actually of Egyptian origin. (007-005)

Linen also served practical purposes beyond clothing. Herodotus notes that Egyptians in the fenlands used linen sheets for sleeping, wrapping themselves in them for protection against the abundant gnats. However, the gnats could bite through linen, which led the marsh-dwellers to use their fishing nets as mosquito netting at night — a resourceful adaptation to the Egyptian environment. (009-001)

## Linen in Burial Custom

While the woolen garment was forbidden for burial, linen accompanied the dead. The embalming process and the wrapping of bodies in linen bandages were deeply connected to the Egyptian conception of purity and the afterlife — a practice Herodotus describes in his account of Egyptian mourning and burial customs. (008-002)
