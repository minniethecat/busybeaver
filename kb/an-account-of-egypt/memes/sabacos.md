## Overview

Sabacos (also known as Shabaka or Sabaco) was an [[ethiopia-region|Ethiopia]]n king who invaded and conquered [[egypt|Egypt]], ruling during what modern historians identify as the 25th ([[ethiopia-region|Kush]]ite) Dynasty. In [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s *Account of [[egypt|Egypt]]*, Sabacos appears as a foreign conqueror whose reign was marked by a distinctive policy of judicial punishment and who ultimately abandoned [[egypt|Egypt]] after receiving a divine vision in a dream. (013-004)

## Background / Biography

Sabacos was the king of the [[ethiopia-region|Ethiopia]]ns who marched into [[egypt|Egypt]] during the reign of [[anysis|Anysis]], [[anysis|the blind king]] of the city of [[anysis|Anysis]]. According to the [[egyptian-priesthood|Egyptian priests]] who served as [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s informants, [[anysis|Anysis]] fled before Sabacos into the fen-country and remained in hiding there for fifty years, during which time Sabacos ruled all [[egypt|Egypt]]. (013-004)

Sabacos's rule was notable for his unusual approach to criminal justice. Rather than executing wrongdoers, he sentenced them to raise the embankments of their native cities — a form of penal labour that [[herodotus|Herodotus]] notes had already been employed, on a smaller scale, under [[sesostris|Sesostris]]. (013-004) This policy of productive punishment became the enduring mark of his reign, so that the cities whose embankments were raised under his direction were seen to be situated on even higher ground in [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s own day. (013-004)

## Role in the Account

Sabacos occupies three distinct episodes in [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s narrative. His reign is first introduced in the context of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian king-lists, where the priests recount the succession from [[anysis|Anysis]] through the [[ethiopia-region|Ethiopia]]n conquest. (013-004) The second episode describes his departure: after fifty years of rule, Sabacos fled [[egypt|Egypt]] in response to a dream in which a figure appeared to him and counselled that he should assemble all the [[egyptian-priesthood|priests of Egypt]] and cut each of them in two. (013-006) Taking this as a divine warning, Sabacos chose voluntary exile rather than commit impiety, telling the priests that the gods were showing him what he must suffer if he remained — a decision [[herodotus|Herodotus]] presents as consistent with [[egypt|Egypt]]ian oracles which foretold that the [[ethiopia-region|Ethiopia]]n would rule [[egypt|Egypt]] for fifty years. (013-006)

The third reference to Sabacos appears in the genealogy of [[psammetichos|Psammetichos]]: it was Sabacos who killed [[necos|Necos]], the father of [[psammetichos|Psammetichos]] — an act that sent the young [[psammetichos|Psammetichos]] into exile in [[syria|Syria]] and set in motion the chain of events that would later lead to the rise of the 26th Dynasty. (014-008)

## Herodotus's Portrayal

[[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s portrayal of Sabacos is notably restrained. Unlike the detailed moral judgments he passes on [[egypt|Egypt]]ian pharaohs such as [[cheops|Cheops]] or [[mykerinos|Mykerinos]], the narrative presents Sabacos as a foreign ruler whose departure was governed by divine will rather than human agency. The dream-vision and its interpretation suggest that even a conqueror is subject to the same oracular framework that governs [[egypt|Egypt]]ian kingship. Sabacos's fifty-year reign is presented without explicit condemnation, and his penal-labour policy is described with a matter-of-fact tone that neither praises nor condemns.

> (013-006)
