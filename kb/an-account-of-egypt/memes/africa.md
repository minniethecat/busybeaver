## Overview

In Herodotus's "Account of Egypt", the continent of Africa appears under the name "Libya" (Λιβύη), a term the Greeks used for the entire landmass west and south of Egypt. Herodotus treats Libya as one of the three great divisions of the known world alongside Asia and Europe, and the Egyptian priests provided him with accounts of Libyan customs, oracles, and geographical features that he compared with those of Egypt.

## Location / Geography

Herodotus describes Libya as the land stretching westward from Egypt along the Mediterranean coast and southward into the interior beyond the Nile. He notes that the Nile's course through Egypt is known, but its upper reaches in the Libyan interior remain a mystery that neither Egyptians nor Libyans could explain to him (003-005). The boundary between Egypt and Libya was marked by the Canobic mouth of the Nile and the desert approaches to the west. Herodotus also observes that the Nile flows through Ethiopia before entering Egypt, placing the African interior within the Ethiopian realm rather than Libya proper.

## Historical Significance

Libya held a dual significance in Herodotus's ethnography. It was a source of religious diffusion — Herodotus reports the Egyptian belief that the oracle of Zeus Ammon at the Siwa oasis, located in Libya, shared a common origin with the oracle at Dodona in Hellas, both deriving from Egyptian priestly traditions (006-001). Libya was also the home of peoples who, according to the priests, had once been ruled by Egyptian kings. Sesostris was said to have campaigned across Libya, erecting pillars to mark his conquests.

## Herodotus's Description

Herodotus records that he personally visited Libyan territory to investigate reports. He mentions travelling to the region to inquire about the oracle of Ammon and the customs of the people there (001-009). He notes that the naming of the Greek gods came largely from Egypt, but acknowledges that some elements may have passed through Libya as an intermediary. His interest in Libya was part of his broader comparative method — he uses Libyan customs as a foil to highlight the distinctiveness of Egyptian practices.

## Sources

> As to the sources of the Nile, not one either of the Egyptians or of the Libyans or of the Hellenes, who came to speech with me, professed to know anything.
> (003-005)

> As regards the Oracles both that among the Hellenes and that in Libya, the Egyptians tell the following tale. The priests of the Theban Zeus told me that two women in the service of the temple had been carried away by Phoenicians.
> (006-001)

> Moreover I visited both Thebes and Heliopolis, and also went to Libya to make inquiries.
> (001-009)
