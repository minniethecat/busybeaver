## Overview

[[Alexander]] — called [[Paris]] by the [[hellenes|Hellenes]] and Alexander in the [[Homeric]] epics — is the Trojan prince whose abduction of [[Helen of Troy|Helen]] of [[Sparta]] triggered the [[Trojan War]]. In [[Herodotus]]'s *Account of [[egypt|Egypt]]*, however, Alexander appears not as a warrior on the plains of [[Ilion]] but as a fugitive driven by storms to the [[Egypt|Egyptian]] coast, where he is tried, convicted, stripped of his stolen prize, and banished by the righteous King [[Proteus]] (010-005). This [[egypt|Egypt]]ian counter-narrative to the Homeric tradition forms a central part of [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s critical engagement with the epic poets.

## Background / Biography

Alexander was the son of [[Priam]], king of [[Troy]]. In the Homeric tradition, his abduction of [[Helen]] — the wife of his host [[Menelaos]] of Sparta — was the immediate cause of the Trojan War. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] accepts this basic premise but complicates it with an alternative version obtained from the [[Egyptian Priesthood|Egyptian priests]] of [[Memphis]].

According to the priests, Alexander succeeded in carrying Helen away from Sparta but was prevented from reaching [[troy|Troy]] by contrary winds that blew him across the [[Aegean Sea]] into the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian Sea. He came to shore at the Canobic mouth of the [[Nile River|Nile]], near the city of [[Taricheiai]], where a temple of [[Heracles]] stood (010-005). It was here that his own attendants — taking advantage of a local custom of sanctuary for runaway slaves — fled to the temple and denounced him, revealing the whole story of his abduction and breach of hospitality (010-005).

The local warden, [[Thonis]], sent a message to [[proteus|Proteus]] at [[memphis|Memphis]] reporting the arrival of a stranger who had committed a grave impiety against a Hellene. [[proteus|Proteus]] ordered Alexander seized and brought to the capital (010-005). Confronted by the king and convicted by the testimony of the suppliants, Alexander attempted to dissemble about Helen's presence — but was exposed. [[proteus|Proteus]] delivered his sentence: Alexander himself would be spared, in keeping with the principle of not killing strangers driven to [[egypt|Egypt]] by winds, but Helen and all the stolen wealth would be confiscated and held for [[menelaos|Menelaos]]. Alexander and his crew were expelled from [[egypt|Egypt]] within three days (010-005).

## Role in the Account

Alexander's role in the *Account of [[egypt|Egypt]]* is threefold. First, he is the antagonist whose breach of hospitality (*xenia*) — a sacred obligation in both Greek and [[egypt|Egypt]]ian moral codes — sets the entire episode in motion. Second, his trial before [[proteus|Proteus]] serves as the vehicle for [[herodotus|Herodotus]] to present the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian alternative to the Homeric tradition: a narrative in which [[troy|Troy]] was destroyed over a woman who was never there. Third, Alexander's wanderings are used by [[herodotus|Herodotus]] as evidence in a literary-critical argument about Homer's sources.

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] cites lines from the *Iliad* describing Alexander's wanderings past [[Sidon]] in [[Phoenicia]], which he takes as proof that Homer knew of the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian episode but chose a different version more suitable to epic poetry (010-006). This argument — that the poet knowingly omitted a true story for aesthetic reasons — is a remarkable piece of ancient literary criticism. In a further passage from the *Iliad*, which the [[hellenes|Hellenes]] of a later generation wrongly attributed to the *Cyprian Epic*, Homer makes reference to Alexander's swift return to [[troy|Troy]] — a detail that, in [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s reading, confirms the poet's awareness of the real geography and timeline (011-003).

## Herodotus's Portrayal

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] presents Alexander with unambiguous moral condemnation. The priests' narrative, which [[herodotus|Herodotus]] endorses, characterises Alexander as having committed "a most impious deed" against his host [[menelaos|Menelaos]] (010-005). [[proteus|Proteus]]'s sentencing speech explicitly brands him as "most base of men" — a rare instance of direct moral language applied to an individual in the *Account of [[egypt|Egypt]]*, and one that underscores the gravity with which the violation of hospitality was regarded.

Yet Alexander also serves a function in the narrative beyond his own villainy. His presence in [[egypt|Egypt]], and his trial, are the mechanism by which [[herodotus|Herodotus]] establishes that the Homeric version of the Trojan War is historically untenable. The historian uses Alexander not as a character but as a piece of evidence — his detour to [[egypt|Egypt]] is the fact that unravels the epic tradition. This is characteristic of [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s method: mythic figures are not dismissed as fictions but are interrogated as historical actors whose movements can be traced and whose actions have consequences across cultures.

> At length Proteus declared to them this sentence, saying, "Were it not that I count it a matter of great moment not to slay any of those strangers who being driven from their course by winds have come to my land hitherto, I should have taken vengeance on thee on behalf of the man of Hellas, seeing that thou, most base of men, having received from him hospitality, didst work against him a most impious deed."
> (010-005)

> (011-003)
