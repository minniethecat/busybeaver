## Overview

Ladike was a Greek woman from [[cyrene|Kyrene]] who became the wife of the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian king [[amasis|Amasis]] ([[amasis|Ahmose II]]) of the 26th Dynasty. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] recounts a remarkable story in which Ladike, threatened with death after [[amasis|Amasis]] accused her of using drugs to prevent intercourse, made a vow to Aphrodite that resulted in both the restoration of conjugal relations and [[amasis|Amasis]]'s deep affection for her. After the [[persian|Persian]] conquest of [[egypt|Egypt]], [[cambyses|Cambyses]] recognized her status and sent her safely back to [[cyrene|Kyrene]]. (017-001)

## Background / Biography

Ladike's origins are uncertain even in [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s account: some sources named her as the daughter of Battos, the founder of the Kyrenian dynasty; others said she was the daughter of Arkesilaos, a later Kyrenian king; and still others identified her father as Critobulos, a citizen of repute. This ambiguity suggests that the story of Ladike had circulated in multiple versions by the time [[herodotus|Herodotus]] recorded it. (017-001)

[[amasis|Amasis]]'s marriage to Ladike was part of a broader diplomatic initiative. The king had concluded a treaty of friendship and alliance with [[cyrene|Kyrene]] and sought a wife from that city — whether from genuine desire for a Greek wife or as a political gesture, [[herodotus|Herodotus]] does not determine. (017-001)

## Role in the Account

**The crisis and the vow.** After the marriage, [[amasis|Amasis]] found himself unable to have intercourse with Ladike, though he associated normally with his other wives. When this occurred repeatedly, the king accused Ladike of administering drugs and threatened her with a death more miserable than any other. In desperation, Ladike made a silent vow to Aphrodite: if [[amasis|Amasis]] could have intercourse with her that very night — the only remedy for her danger — she would dedicate a statue to the goddess at [[cyrene|Kyrene]]. (017-001)

The vow was immediately effective. [[amasis|Amasis]] had intercourse with Ladike that night, and thereafter whenever he came to her the same result followed. The king's anger transformed into deep attachment. (017-001)

**The dedication at [[cyrene|Kyrene]].** Fulfilling her vow, Ladike commissioned an image of Aphrodite and sent it to [[cyrene|Kyrene]]. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] reports that the statue was still preserved in his own time, standing with its face turned away from the city of the Kyrenians — a distinctive detail that lends verisimilitude to the account. (017-001)

**The [[cambyses|Cambyses]] episode.** After [[cambyses|Cambyses]] conquered [[egypt|Egypt]], he learned Ladike's identity and, recognizing her status, sent her back unharmed to [[cyrene|Kyrene]]. This act of restraint from the [[persian|Persian]] conqueror closes the narrative arc of Ladike's [[egypt|Egypt]]ian sojourn. (017-001)

## Herodotus's Portrayal

The Ladike narrative serves multiple functions in [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s account. It illustrates the personal dimension of [[amasis|Amasis]]'s philhellenism — not only did the king ally with Greek cities, he married a Greek woman. The story also functions as an aetiology, explaining the origin of a votive statue visible at [[cyrene|Kyrene]] in [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s day. Finally, Ladike's narrow escape from death and subsequent elevation to [[amasis|Amasis]]'s beloved wife echoes a recurring Herodotean theme: the reversal of fortune through divine intervention. (017-001)
