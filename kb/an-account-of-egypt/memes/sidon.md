## Overview

[[Sidon]] was one of the great Phoenician city-states of the Levantine coast, referenced several times in Herodotus's *Account of Egypt*. It appears as a destination in the wanderings of [[Alexander Paris]] as recorded by Homer, as a geographical reference point in Herodotus's argument about the authorship of the *Cyprian Epic*, and as the target of an Egyptian naval expedition under King [[Apries]].

## Location and Geography

Sidon lay on the Mediterranean coast of [[Phoenicia]], in the region of Syria. Herodotus notes that "Syria borders upon Egypt and the Phenicians, of whom is Sidon, dwell in Syria" (011-003). This geographical proximity made Sidon a natural point of contact — and conflict — between Egypt and the Phoenician world throughout the Late Period.

## Sidon in Homeric Geography

Herodotus invokes [[Sidon]] as evidence for his theory that [[Homer]] knew the true story of [[Helen]]'s sojourn in Egypt during the Trojan War. In the *Iliad*, Homer describes Alexander (Paris) "wandering to various lands, and that he came among other places to Sidon in Phenicia" (010-006). Herodotus argues that Homer deliberately departed from the Egyptian account of Helen's stay with [[Proteus]] because it was less suitable for epic poetry, but that the mention of Sidon reveals the poet's knowledge of the Near Eastern geography connected to the Helen story.

This reference to [[Alexander Paris]] and [[Helen]] links Sidon to one of the central narrative threads running through Herodotus's *Account* — his argument, developed at length in Sections 010–011, that Homer was aware of the Egyptian version of the Trojan War but chose a different poetic path.

## Apries's Campaign

King [[Apries]] (Wahibre Haaibre, 589–570 BCE), the son of [[Psammis]] and grandson of [[Necos]], undertook military action against the Phoenician coast during his twenty-five-year reign. Herodotus records that Apries "led an army against Sidon and fought a sea-fight with the king of Tyre" (015-009). This campaign formed part of Apries's broader Mediterranean policy, which ultimately ended in misfortune — a fate Herodotus attributes to divine will, promising to relate the details "at greater length in the Libyan history."
