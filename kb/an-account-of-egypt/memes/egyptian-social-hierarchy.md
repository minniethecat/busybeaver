## Overview

In his account of Egyptian society, Herodotus records that the Egyptians were divided into seven distinct classes, or occupational orders, each named for the work its members performed. This classification reflects a society in which occupation was hereditary and social identity was intimately tied to one's livelihood. The seven classes were: priests, warriors, cowherds, swineherds, shopkeepers, interpreters, and pilots. Of these, the priests and the warriors enjoyed the highest status and the most significant privileges. (015-011)

## The Seven Classes

Herodotus enumerates the Egyptian class order with businesslike precision: the priests first, then the warriors, followed by cowherds, swineherds, shopkeepers, interpreters, and pilots. He notes that the names of the classes were derived directly from the occupations their members followed — a system that made social rank immediately legible from a man's work. Unlike Greece, where military prowess or political influence could elevate a man irrespective of his trade, the Egyptian system was structured around the function a man and his family performed for the state. (015-011)

The inclusion of interpreters among the seven orders is noteworthy, for it suggests that mediation between the Egyptian and non-Egyptian worlds — Greek traders, Carian mercenaries, Ionian settlers — had become such an established part of Egyptian life that it warranted its own hereditary class. Likewise, the pilots who navigated the Nile and the coastal waters were considered a distinct order, reflecting the centrality of the river to Egyptian transport and commerce.

## Priests and Warriors: The Privileged Orders

The priests occupied the apex of Egyptian society, enjoying not only religious authority but substantial material advantages. Herodotus records that each priest was granted twelve yokes of land — approximately twelve acres — completely free from tax. This was a privilege extended to no other class, and it points to the immense economic power concentrated in the temples. The priesthood was hereditary, passing from father to son, and its members were subject to distinctive purity regulations governing diet, dress, and bodily cleanliness. (016-001)

The warriors constituted the second elite order. They too received grants of land — twelve yokes each, free from tax — and were maintained at the state's expense. Their loyalty was critical to the stability of the monarchy, as the story of Psammetichos and the helmet oracle demonstrates: the eleven kings who ruled Egypt jointly stripped Psammetichos of his power and drove him into the fen-country, a punishment that reflected the warrior class's capacity to enforce or withdraw support from a ruler. (014-008)

The warriors' importance was such that when King Amasis neglected and slighted them — Herodotus says he "held in no regard the warrior class of the Egyptians, considering that he would have no need of them" — he fatally undermined his own reign. Amasis's dismissal of the warriors and his reliance on foreign mercenaries instead was a direct cause of the military rebellion that would eventually lead to his overthrow. (013-008)

## The Lower Orders

Below the priests and warriors stood the remaining five classes, whose distinction was functional rather than honorific. The cowherds and swineherds, though lower in rank, played essential roles in Egyptian subsistence. However, Herodotus records that swineherds were regarded as unclean — they were forbidden from entering any temple, and marriages between swineherds and families outside their own class were prohibited. The shopkeepers formed the commercial stratum, while the interpreters mediated communication with foreigners. The pilots and boatmen, who navigated the Nile, formed the final recognized order. All of these hereditary occupations bound Egyptian society into a rigid but orderly structure, in which each man's place was determined by birth and ratified by the function he performed for the community.

## Significance

Herodotus's classification of Egyptian society into seven occupational orders reveals the Greek historian's method of understanding foreign cultures by mapping them onto recognizable categories. The seven-class schema is simultaneously informative and simplifying — it captures the structural logic of Egyptian social organization while omitting the nuances that a native Egyptian would take for granted. What the account does convey unmistakably is the hereditary character of Egyptian society, the privileged status of the sacerdotal and military orders, and the low regard in which certain occupations — most notably swineherding — were held. These observations align with what is known from Egyptian sources about the power of the temple estates and the importance of a professional military class.
