## Overview

Herodotus provides a detailed enumeration of the seven hereditary classes into which Egyptian society was divided (015-011). This classification, obtained from the priests, represents one of the earliest sociological descriptions of Egyptian social structure. Each class was defined by occupation and passed down through families, with limited mobility between them. Herodotus's list has been a foundational source for understanding Egyptian social organisation, though modern scholarship recognises it as an idealised schema rather than a complete picture.

## Commentary

Herodotus notes that the priests and warriors were the only classes accorded special honour and privilege. The warriors were divided into two sub-classes — the Calasirians and the Hermotybians — each associated with particular regions of Egypt. The Calasirians were concentrated in the Delta nome of Calasiris, while the Hermotybians were based in Upper Egypt. Herodotus also records that the warrior class was forbidden to practise any manual trade, as the Egyptians believed that military skill required exclusive devotion. The class of interpreters emerged as a distinct group during the Saite period, reflecting Egypt's increasing engagement with the Greek world.

> Now of the Egyptians there are seven classes, and of these one class is called that of the priests, and another that of the warriors, while the others are the cowherds, swineherds, shopkeepers, interpreters, and boatmen. This is the number of the classes of the Egyptians, and their names are given them from the occupations which they follow.
> (015-011)
