# Persian

## Overview

The Persians, ruled by the Achaemenid dynasty, are the central antagonists of [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s *Histories* and appear throughout his account of [[egypt|Egypt]] as a foreign imperial power. Cambodia (Cambyses), the son of Cyrus, conquered [[egypt|Egypt]], incorporating it into the Persian Empire. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] documents Persian rule in [[egypt|Egypt]] through the reigns of Cambyses and [[dareios|Dareios]] ([[dareios|Darius]]), framing the Persian presence as the political context within which [[egypt|Egypt]]ian customs and institutions persisted.

## Description

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] introduces the Persian connection to [[egypt|Egypt]] at the very beginning of his [[egypt|Egypt]]ian narrative: "When Cyrus had brought his life to an end, Cambyses received the royal power in succession... and he proceeded to march an army against [[egypt|Egypt]], taking with him as helpers not only other nations of which he was ruler, but also those of the [[hellenes|Hellenes]] over whom he had power besides" (002-001). This establishes the Persian conquest as the immediate backdrop for [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s travels and inquiries.

The Persian presence is documented in major infrastructure. The canal connecting [[nile-river|the Nile]] to the [[erythraian-sea|Erythraian Sea]] ([[erythraian-sea|Red Sea]]) was initiated by the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian king [[necos|Necos]], but "[[dareios|Dareios]] the Persian afterwards completed" it (015-004), demonstrating Persian investment in [[egypt|Egypt]]ian infrastructure. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] describes the canal as a four-day voyage, broad enough for two triremes to pass side by side.

At the level of religious institutions, [[herodotus|Herodotus]] reports Persian reverence for [[egypt|Egypt]]ian sacred sites. During [[dareios|Dareios]]'s reign, the Persians respected [[egypt|Egypt]]ian temples and priests, though Cambyses is portrayed as having committed outrages against [[egypt|Egypt]]ian religion — a contrast that may reflect the sources [[herodotus|Herodotus]] accessed at [[egypt|Egypt]]ian temples.

## Historical Context

The Persian conquest of [[egypt|Egypt]] occurred in 525 BCE under Cambyses II, making [[egypt|Egypt]] a satrapy of the Achaemenid Empire. By [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s own lifetime (c. 484–425 BCE), [[egypt|Egypt]] had experienced nearly a century of Persian rule, punctuated by periods of rebellion. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] wrote at a time when the Persian Empire remained the dominant power in the eastern Mediterranean, and his Greek audience would have understood the Persians as both a political reality and a cultural foil to Hellenic freedom.

## Significance

The Persians in [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s [[egypt|Egypt]]ian account serve as a historical frame that legitimizes his inquiries. The Persian administrative apparatus — with its roads, communication systems, and multi-ethnic governance — made travel and inquiry possible in ways earlier periods might not have. Yet [[herodotus|Herodotus]] also uses the Persian presence to highlight [[egypt|Egypt]]ian cultural endurance: despite conquest, [[egypt|Egypt]]ian religion, priestly traditions, and customs persisted unchanged. This contrast between political domination and cultural continuity is central to [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s method.
