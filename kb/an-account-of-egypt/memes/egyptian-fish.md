## Overview

Fish occupied a central place in Egyptian life — as a staple food, as objects of religious veneration, and as a source of royal revenue. Herodotus provides a detailed account of the Nile's fish, including their breeding habits, the dietary customs surrounding their consumption, and the distinction between ordinary fish and those deemed sacred. (007-002) (007-005)

## Fish in the Egyptian Diet

Herodotus records that fish formed a major component of the Egyptian diet, especially among those who dwell in the cultivated regions. Some fish were dried in the sun and eaten without cooking, while others were preserved in brine. The Egyptians consumed both fresh and preserved fish, along with quails, ducks, and other small birds which they cured before eating. Only those species designated as sacred were excluded from the table. (007-005)

The marsh-dwellers of the Delta relied on fish even more heavily. Herodotus notes that "some too of these people live on fish alone," catching them, removing the entrails, and drying them in the sun for later consumption. These fenland communities supplemented their fish diet with lotus roots, papyrus stalks, and other marsh plants. (008-005)

## Sacred Fish

Not all fish were permitted for consumption. Herodotus records that the Egyptians held certain species to be sacred — particularly the *lepidotos* and the eel, both of which were considered sacred to the Nile. The river-horse (hippopotamus) was sacred only in the district of Papremis, while otters were universally regarded as sacred throughout Egypt. (007-002)

Fish also played a role in the maintenance of sacred animals. When Egyptians made vows to the gods, they would shave the heads of their children, weigh the hair against silver, and give the equivalent value to the keepers of sacred animals — who would then "cut up fish of equal value" to feed the beasts. This votive economy sustained the feeding of Egypt's sacred creatures. (006-005)

## Fish Breeding and the Nile

Herodotus provides a remarkably detailed natural history of the Nile fish. He describes how shoaling fish breed in the lakes rather than the rivers, swimming out toward the sea when the breeding instinct takes them. The males lead, shedding their milt, while the females follow and swallow it to become impregnated. When they return from the sea, the females take the lead, releasing their eggs a few grains at a time for the males to swallow. The surviving eggs produce the next generation. (008-006)

When the Nile began to swell, Herodotus observed that the hollow places along the riverbank filled with tiny fish. He correctly inferred that these arose from eggs laid in the mud during the previous year's flood, which hatched when the waters returned. (008-006)

## Economic Value

Fish also contributed to the royal treasury. Herodotus records that Lake Moeris, the great artificial lake connected to the Nile by canal, yielded a talent of silver per day from the fish caught during the six months when water flowed out of the lake, and twenty pounds of silver during the six months when water flowed in. This made the lake's fishery a significant source of crown revenue. (014-007)
