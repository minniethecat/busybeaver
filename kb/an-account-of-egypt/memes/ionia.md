# Ionia

Ionia is a region on the western coast of Anatolia (modern Turkey), inhabited by Greek-speaking Ionians. It is mentioned several times by Herodotus in his "Account of Egypt," particularly in the context of Ionian mercenaries serving in Egypt and Ionian trading communities established at Naucratis.

## Ionians in Herodotus's Egyptian Ethnography

Herodotus engages critically with Ionian geographical theories about Egypt. The Ionians held that only the Nile Delta constituted Egypt proper, defining its boundaries from the watch-tower of Perseus to Pelusion, and inland as far as Kercasoros. (015-025) Herodotus rejects this view, arguing that if the Delta alone were Egypt, then in former times — before the alluvial formation of the Delta — the Egyptians would have had no land at all. (027-029)

## Ionian Mercenaries in Egypt

Ionians played a decisive role in Egyptian political history as mercenaries. When Pharaoh Psammetichos (Psamtik I) was driven into the fen-country by the eleven other kings, the Oracle of Buto foretold that vengeance would come when "men of bronze appeared from the sea." Ionian and Carian raiders, clad in bronze armour, were forced ashore in Egypt and came to Psammetichos's aid. (213) With their help, Psammetichos overthrew the rival kings and became sole ruler of Egypt.

In gratitude, Psammetichos granted the Ionians and Carians land to settle, placing them opposite one another with the Nile between — these settlements were called "Encampments." He also placed Egyptian boys with them to learn Greek, creating the first class of interpreters in Egypt. (213)

## Ionian Trading Presence at Naucratis

By the reign of Amasis (Ahmose II), Ionian cities were firmly established in Egyptian trade. Amasis granted the city of Naucratis to Greek settlers, and the great sanctuary called the Hellenion was jointly founded by several Ionian cities: Chios, Teos, Phocaia, and Clazomenai. (251) Additional sanctuaries were established by Samians (to Hera) and Milesians (to Apollo).

## Sesostris Monuments in Ionia

Herodotus records having personally seen rock-cut figures in Ionia that he attributed to the Egyptian conqueror Sesostris. Two figures, each about four cubits and a span in height, were carved on the roadside: one on the road from Ephesos to Phocaia, another on the road from Sardis to Smyrna. Each bore an inscription in Egyptian sacred characters reading "This land with my shoulders I won for myself." (129)
