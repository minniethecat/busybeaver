## Overview

Silver held a notable place in the economic and religious life of ancient Egypt as described by Herodotus. While gold was more closely associated with the divine and royal spheres, silver was used as a medium of exchange, a measure of value in religious offerings, and a material for recording royal expenditures. Its presence in Egyptian inscriptions and customs attests to the integration of precious metals into both sacred and secular transactions. (006-005)

## Silver in Religious Offerings

Egyptian religious practice incorporated silver as a measure of votive offerings. When an Egyptian made a vow to a god associated with a particular sacred animal, a distinctive custom was observed: the worshipper would shave the head of a child — either entirely, by half, or by a third — and then weigh the hair against silver. The weight of the hair determined the monetary value of the offering, which was then given to the appointed caretaker of the sacred animals. This practice linked the personal act of devotion with a quantifiable silver contribution to the temple economy. (006-005)

## Silver in Royal Inscriptions

The construction of the great pyramid of Cheops involved enormous expenditure, portions of which were recorded in silver. Herodotus reports an inscription on the pyramid that itemised the cost of radishes, onions, and leeks supplied to the workmen, amounting to one thousand six hundred talents of silver. This inscription, translated for Herodotus by an interpreter, demonstrates that silver was the standard unit of accounting for monumental royal projects, even for the provisioning of labourers with the most humble of foodstuffs. (012-003)

## Sources

> Persons have been appointed of the Egyptians, both men and women, to provide the food for each kind of beast separately... and those who dwell in the various cities perform vows to them thus, that is, when they make a vow to the god to whom the animal belongs, they shave the head of their children either the whole or the half or the third part of it, and then set the hair in the balance against silver, and whatever it weighs, this the man gives to the person who provides for the animals.
> (006-005)

> A sum of one thousand six hundred talents of silver was spent; and if this is so, how much besides is likely to have been expended upon the iron tools used in the work, and upon the food and clothing of the workmen?
> (012-003)
