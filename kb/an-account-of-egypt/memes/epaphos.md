Epaphos is an Egyptian deity whom [[Herodotus]] identifies with the [[Apis]] bull. In Herodotus's account, the Egyptians considered male oxen to belong to Epaphos, and on account of this association, sacrificial cattle were subjected to an elaborate purity inspection before being declared clean for sacrifice. (004-006)

## Herodotus's Identification

Herodotus explicitly equates Epaphos with the Hellenic conception: "Now Apis is in the tongue of the Hellenes Epaphos." (014-008) This identification is part of Herodotus's broader effort to translate Egyptian religion into terms familiar to his Greek audience — a practice he applies consistently throughout the Egyptian account with deities such as [[Isis]] (identified with Demeter) and [[Osiris]] (identified with Dionysos).

## Role in Egyptian Sacrifice

The association of male oxen with Epaphos was central to Egyptian sacrificial practice. A priest appointed for this purpose examined every candidate beast both standing and lying on its back, checking for any black hair that would render it unclean. (004-006) The priest also drew out the animal's tongue to verify additional appointed signs. If any imperfection was found — even a single black hair — the animal was rejected. Once approved, the beast was sealed with the priest's signet, and sacrificing an unsealed animal carried the penalty of death. (004-006)

This rigorous testing procedure is described by Herodotus in great detail, and it reflects the broader Egyptian concern with ritual purity that governed all aspects of their sacrificial system. The procedure was thorough and methodical — involving examination of the animal standing, lying on its back, and with tongue drawn out — before the priest marked it with papyrus, plastered sealing-earth over it, and set his signet upon it. (004-006)

## Epaphos and the Apis Bull

The identification of Epaphos with Apis connects this figure to one of the most important Egyptian cults. The Apis bull was a living sacred animal worshipped at [[Memphis]], believed to be the herald or incarnation of Ptah. Its cult persisted across millennia of Egyptian history, and the elaborate burial of the Apis bulls in the Serapeum at [[Saqqara]] (a later development not recorded by Herodotus) reflects the enduring significance of this tradition.

## Significance in Herodotus's Account

Herodotus presents the cult of Epaphos as evidence of Egyptian religious sophistication. The sacrificial testing of oxen demonstrates the systematic, priestly regulation of religious practice in Egypt — a contrast to less formalised Greek sacrificial customs. The detail that no Egyptian would use a Hellene's knife even on a clean animal further underscores the strict cultural boundaries maintained in Egyptian sacrificial practice. (004-006)
