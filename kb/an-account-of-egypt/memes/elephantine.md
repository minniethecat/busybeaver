## Overview

Elephantine was an island city in the Nile at the southern boundary of Egypt, below the First Cataract and facing the modern city of Aswan. In Herodotus's account, Elephantine marks the frontier between Egypt and Ethiopia and serves as the reference point for his description of the Nile's upper course. (003-008) It was also the source of the great monolithic stone chamber that Amasis transported to Sais — a block so large that two thousand men laboured for three years to move it. (016-003)

## Location / Geography

Elephantine occupied a key position at the southern limit of Egypt, controlling access to the Nile's upper reaches. Herodotus reports that the Nile is navigable for four months' journey beyond Elephantine, though the river passes through a steep, narrow gorge where boats must be towed like oxen from the bank. (003-008) The city sat opposite Syene (modern Aswan), which the Egyptians considered the place directly under the sun's zenith at the summer solstice. (003-001)

## Historical Significance

Elephantine represented the southern boundary of Egyptian power throughout most of the dynastic period. It was the administrative centre for Egyptian control of Nubia and the departure point for trade and military expeditions into Africa. The island housed an important temple of Khnum, the ram-headed god who was believed to control the Nile's annual inundation.
