## Overview

The Carians were a people from Caria, a region in southwestern Asia Minor (modern Turkey), who appear in Herodotus's account of Egypt as mercenaries and settlers. They, along with the Ionians, were the first foreign-speaking peoples to settle in Egypt, arriving during the reign of [[Psammetichos I]] in the seventh century BCE. Herodotus notes their presence in Egypt across multiple contexts: as military allies, as religious participants, and as a permanent foreign community whose descendents served as interpreters. (014-008)(006-002)

## Description

The Carians first entered Egypt when a group of Ionian and Carian seafarers, sailing for plunder, were compelled by circumstance to land on the Egyptian coast. They disembarked wearing bronze armour — a sight unfamiliar to the Egyptians — and word was brought to [[Psammetichos I]], who at that time was one of the Twelve Kings ruling Egypt. Recognizing an opportunity, Psammetichos befriended the newcomers, made them generous promises, and persuaded them to aid him in his struggle against his fellow kings. With their help, he succeeded in becoming sole ruler of Egypt. (014-008)

As a reward for their service, Psammetichos granted the Ionians and Carians portions of land to settle, situated opposite one another on either side of the Nile. These settlements, called the Camps, lay towards the sea, a short distance below the city of [[Bubastis]], on the Pelusian mouth of the Nile. (014-008) The Carians occupied these lands for a long time, and from their community arose the class of interpreters in Egypt — descendents of the original settlers who, being raised bilingual, served as intermediaries between Egyptians and Greek-speaking visitors. (014-008)

Later, during the reign of [[Amasis]], the Carians and Ionians were relocated from their original settlements to [[Memphis]], where the king employed them as his personal bodyguard, considering them more trustworthy than native Egyptians. At Memphis, their original dwelling-place became known as the Hellenion, the quarter granted to the Greek-speaking settlers. (015-010)

## Religious Practices

Herodotus records a striking detail about the Carians resident in Egypt: during religious festivals, particularly those honoring [[Isis]] at [[Busiris]], they were known to surpass even the Egyptians themselves in the fervour of their mourning rites. The Carians dwelling in Egypt would gash their foreheads with knives during the ceremonies, making themselves conspicuous among the worshippers. This extreme display, Herodotus suggests, distinguished the foreign devotees from the native participants. (006-002)

## Significance

The Carians represent a key element in Herodotus's account of Egypt's interactions with the wider Mediterranean world. Their presence illustrates the pivotal role of foreign mercenaries in Egyptian dynastic politics — Psammetichos's reliance on Ionian and Carian soldiers was instrumental in his rise to sole power. The Carian settlements also mark the beginning of a permanent Greek-speaking presence in Egypt, with their bilingual descendents forming the first institutionalized class of interpreters. This community laid the groundwork for the later Greek influence that would culminate in the Ptolemaic period. Herodotus's mention of their extreme religious enthusiasm further illuminates the cultural dynamics of Egyptian festival life and the participation of resident foreigners in native cult.
