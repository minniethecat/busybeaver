## Overview

Pelusion (Pelusium in Latin, modern Tell el-Farama) was the easternmost city of the Nile Delta, guarding the entrance to Egypt from the Sinai and Asia. It occupies a pivotal place in Herodotus's narrative as the site of the miraculous deliverance of Egypt under the priest-king Sethos, when field-mice sent by Hephaistos gnawed through the weapons of the invading Assyrian army. (013-008) It was also near Pelusion that the Ionian and Carian mercenaries landed, fulfilling the oracle of Buto. (014-008)

## Location / Geography

Pelusion was situated on the easternmost branch of the Nile, the Pelusiac mouth, approximately thirty kilometres east of modern Port Said. Its position at the frontier of Egypt made it the first line of defence against invasions from the east — a role it fulfilled from the New Kingdom through the Persian, Hellenistic, and Roman periods.

## Historical Significance

The most dramatic event associated with Pelusion in Herodotus's account is the miraculous defeat of Sennacherib's Assyrian army. Abandoned by the Egyptian warrior class, the priest-king Sethos led his artisans and tradesmen to Pelusion to confront the invaders. During the night, a plague of field-mice swarmed through the Assyrian camp, devouring their quivers, bows, and shield-handles, leaving the army defenceless by morning. (013-008)
