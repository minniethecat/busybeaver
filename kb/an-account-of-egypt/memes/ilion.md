# Ilion

Ilion (Troy) is the celebrated city of the Trojan War, located in the Troad region of northwestern Anatolia. Herodotus refers to Ilion in his "Account of Egypt" primarily to present an alternative Egyptian version of the events surrounding Helen of Troy — an account that fundamentally challenges the traditional Homeric narrative.

## Ilion in Geographical Comparison

Herodotus invokes Ilion as part of a geological argument about the formation of the Nile Delta. He compares the alluvial plain of Egypt to regions around Ilion, Teuthrania, Ephesos, and the plain of the Maiander, noting that these were once gulfs of the sea that were filled in by river deposits. (023) He explicitly states that "small these are in comparison" to Egypt, since none of the rivers that formed those regions compare in volume to the Nile.

## The Egyptian Account of the Trojan War

The most substantial reference to Ilion in Herodotus's work comes through the Egyptian priests' retelling of the Trojan War. According to the priests at Memphis, whom Herodotus consulted, the traditional Greek story was fundamentally mistaken: Helen never reached Ilion.

The priests told Herodotus that after Paris (Alexander) abducted Helen from Sparta, contrary winds drove his ship to Egypt. There, King Proteus, learning of Paris's crime, confiscated Helen and the stolen treasure, banishing Paris from Egypt. (153) When the Greek army arrived at Ilion and demanded Helen's return, the Trojans (Teucrians) truthfully maintained that Helen was not within their walls — she was in Egypt. The Greeks, believing themselves mocked, besieged the city until they finally took it, only then discovering that the Trojans had spoken the truth.

After the city's fall, Menelaos himself sailed to Egypt, where he recovered Helen unharmed along with his wealth. (153)

## Herodotus's Own Reasoning

Herodotus explicitly endorses the Egyptian version, adding his own rational argument: if Helen had truly been in Ilion, King Priam and the Trojans would certainly have returned her to the Greeks rather than endure the destruction of their city, the deaths of Priam's sons, and the suffering of their people. He notes that Hector, as Priam's elder son and heir, would have had both the authority and the duty to compel his brother Alexander to return Helen, given the catastrophic consequences for Troy. (155)

## Chronological Context

Herodotus uses the Trojan War as a chronological reference point when discussing the antiquity of Egyptian gods. He notes that Pan, according to Greek tradition, "came into being later than the wars of Troy, about eight hundred years before my time" — contrasting this with Egyptian accounts that place their gods thousands of years earlier. (199)
