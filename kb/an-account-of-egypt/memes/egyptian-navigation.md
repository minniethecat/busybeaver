## Overview

Navigation on the Nile was central to Egyptian life, as recorded by Herodotus. The river served as the primary artery of transport, and the Egyptians developed distinctive boat-building techniques adapted to the unique conditions of the Nile, using locally available materials and ingeniously simple construction methods. (009-002)

The importance of river transport extended into every facet of Egyptian civilization — commerce, administration, religious festivals, and military expeditions. During the annual inundation, when the Nile overflowed its banks and transformed the land into a vast inland sea, boats became the sole means of travel between the island-like cities that rose above the floodwaters. (009-003)

## Description

### Cargo Vessels: The Baris

Herodotus provides a detailed account of Egyptian cargo boats, called *baris*. These vessels were constructed from the wood of the thorny acacia tree, whose form resembled the Kyrenian lotos and exuded gum. The builders cut the wood into pieces approximately two cubits in length and arranged them like bricks, fastening the vessel together with a great number of long bolts driven through the planks. Cross-pieces were laid over the top, but no ribs were used for the sides. The internal seams were caulked with papyrus fiber. (009-002)

Each *baris* was equipped with a single steering oar that passed through the bottom of the boat, a mast of acacia wood, and sails made of papyrus. These boats were built in great numbers, and some could carry cargoes of many thousands of talents' burden. (009-002)

### Upstream and Downstream Travel

The *baris* could not sail upstream against the Nile's current without a very fresh following wind; otherwise, they were towed from the bank. Downstream travel employed an ingenious method: a door-shaped crate made of tamarisk wood and reed mats was allowed to float ahead of the boat, fastened by a rope. The force of the stream pressing upon the crate pulled the vessel forward swiftly. Meanwhile, a perforated stone weighing about two talents was dragged behind on another rope, sunk deep in the water, to keep the vessel's course straight. The crate drew the boat forward while the stone acted as a trailing anchor. (009-002)

### The Nile in Flood

During the annual inundation, the Nile transformed Egypt's geography into a maritime landscape. The cities alone rose above the water, resembling the islands of the Aegean Sea, while the rest of Egypt became a sea. At such times, boats passed not along the river channels but directly over the midst of the plains. For instance, the passage from Naucratis to Memphis during the flood ran close by the pyramids — a route quite different from the usual course, which followed the point of the Delta and the city of Kercasoros. (009-003)

### Naval Expeditions

The Egyptians also undertook seaborne military expeditions. The priests told Herodotus that King Sesostris was the first to set out with ships of war from the Arabian Gulf and subdue the peoples dwelling along the shores of the Erythraean Sea, sailing until he reached waters too shallow for navigation. After returning to Egypt, he marched overland with a great army across the continent. (009-006)

### Religious and Ritual Navigation

Boats were central to Egyptian religious life. The great festival processions to Bubastis involved vast flotillas: men and women sailed together in boats, the women shaking rattles, the men playing flutes, and others singing and clapping. At each riverside city they passed, the boats put to land, and the celebrations continued with dancing, music, and playful jeering at the local women. (006-002)

The river also held sacred significance in death. When any person — Egyptian or stranger — was killed by a crocodile or drowned in the Nile, the inhabitants of the city where the body washed ashore were required to embalm it in the finest manner, bury it in a consecrated burial-place, and treat it as something more than human. No relative or friend was permitted to touch the body; only the priests of the Nile could handle it. (008-002)

A more mundane religious use of river transport was the collection of ox bones. After the carcasses of sacrificed oxen had decomposed in the suburban burial grounds, boats from the city of Atarbechis, on the island of Prosopitis, traveled throughout Egypt gathering the bones for deposit in a single sacred site, where a temple of Aphrodite stood. (004-006)
