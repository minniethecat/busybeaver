# Danaos

Danaos (Danaus) is a figure of Greek mythology whom Herodotus, in his "Account of Egypt," presents as having Egyptian origins. His story forms part of Herodotus's broader argument about the deep Egyptian roots of Greek religion and culture.

## Egyptian Origins

According to Herodotus, the people of Chemmis in Egypt claimed that Danaos and his brother Lynkeus were men of their city who sailed to Hellas. From them, the Chemmites traced their descent down to the hero Perseus. (111) This genealogical connection was used by the Chemmites to explain why Perseus, a Greek hero, was said to appear in their land — he was, they maintained, returning to his ancestral home.

Herodotus also notes that the city called "the City of Archander" in Egypt was named after Archander, the son-in-law of Danaos, who was the son of Phthios, the son of Achaios. (123)

## The Daughters of Danaos and Greek Religion

The most significant claim Herodotus makes concerning Danaos relates to his daughters. He states unequivocally that "the daughters of Danaos were they who brought this rite out of Egypt and taught it to the women of the Pelasgians" — referring to the mystic rites of Demeter, which the Greeks called Thesmophoria. (245)

According to Herodotus, after the Dorian invasion drove out the inhabitants of the Peloponnese, the rite was lost everywhere except among the Arcadians, who remained in place and preserved it. (245)

## The Danaides at Lindos

Herodotus provides additional testimony about the daughters of Danaos in connection with Amasis's dedications. He records that Amasis dedicated offerings at the temple of Athene in Lindos, on the island of Rhodes, because "the temple of Athene at Lindos is said to have been founded by the daughters of Danaos, who had touched land there at the time when they were fleeing from the sons of Aigyptos." (257)
