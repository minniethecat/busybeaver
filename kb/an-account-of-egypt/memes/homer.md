## Overview

[[Homer]] is invoked repeatedly in Herodotus's *Account of Egypt* not as a historical source but as a poet whose works contain traces of Egyptian knowledge. Herodotus argues that Homer was aware of the Egyptian tradition that [[Helen of Troy]] spent the Trojan War years in Egypt under [[Proteus]]'s protection — a tradition the poet "dismissed" because it was "not so suitable to the composition of his poem" (010-006). Herodotus also uses Homeric evidence to dispute the authorship of the *Cyprian Epic*, date the poet relative to his own time, and trace the origins of Greek religious vocabulary.

## Homer's Knowledge of the Egyptian Helen

The centerpiece of Herodotus's engagement with Homer concerns the fate of Helen. The Egyptian priests told Herodotus that [[Alexander Paris]], having abducted Helen from [[Sparta]], was blown off course to Egypt, where Proteus detained Helen and banished Alexander. (010-005) Herodotus "supposes that Homer also had heard this story" but chose a different poetic path — one in which Helen reached [[Troy]]. (010-006) Yet Homer left clues of his true knowledge: in the *Iliad*, he described Alexander "wandering to various lands, and that he came among other places to Sidon in Phenicia" (010-006). This wandering route, via Sidon, is inconsistent with a direct voyage to Troy but consistent with the Egyptian tradition of a storm-driven detour.

Herodotus further deploys Homer to refute the *Cyprian Epic*'s authorship. The *Cyprian Epic* claimed Alexander "on the third day after leaving Sparta … came to Ilion bringing with him Helen, having had a gently-blowing wind and a smooth sea" — but Homer's *Iliad* says Alexander "wandered from his course when he brought her." (011-003) This contradiction proves, Herodotus argues, that the *Cyprian Epic* "was not written by Homer but by some other man." (011-003)

## Chronology and the Theogony

Herodotus dates Homer to "four hundred years before my time and not more," placing him (together with Hesiod) as the poet who "made a theogony for the Hellenes and gave the titles to the gods and distributed to them honours and arts." (005-007) This attribution makes Homer a foundational figure in Greek religion — the one who systematized the divine hierarchy and nomenclature as the Greeks knew it. Herodotus's chronological estimate (placing Homer around 850 BCE) was influential in ancient discussions of the poet's date.

## The Invention of "Ocean"

In his discussion of the geography of the Nile, Herodotus mentions Homer in passing as the possible inventor of the name "Ocean" — a mythological river encircling the world: "I know of no river Ocean existing, but I think that Homer or one of the poets who were before him invented the name and introduced it into his verse." (003-002) This aside reveals Herodotus's willingness to subject even the greatest poetic authority to rational geographical scrutiny.
