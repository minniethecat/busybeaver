## Overview

Religious offerings and sacrifices formed a central part of Egyptian temple practice as described by [[Herodotus]]. He credits the Egyptians with being the first people to institute solemn assemblies, processions, and formal approaches to the temples — customs which the Hellenes subsequently adopted from them. The scale and variety of Egyptian offerings reflected a religious culture that Herodotus considered more elaborate and more ancient than that of any other people known to him. (006-002)

## Description

Herodotus describes several distinct offering customs among the Egyptians, each embedded in a specific regional or ritual context.

### Animal Sacrifice

Animal sacrifice varied by region and deity. Those devoted to the Theban Zeus sacrificed goats and abstained from sheep, while those of the Mendesian district abstained from goats and sacrificed sheep. All Egyptians, regardless of regional differences, universally revered [[Isis]] and [[Osiris]]. (005-001) The ritual of sacrifice was elaborate: priests examined the ox offered to [[Epaphos]] with extreme care — a single black hair rendered the animal impure, and the animal would be rejected after a lengthy inspection involving the tongue, tail-hairs, and overall condition. (004-006)

### Royal Offerings

The king played a role in major offerings. Herodotus recounts how the [[The Twelve Kings|twelve kings]], ruling jointly, were about to make libation at the temple of [[Hephaistos]] during the last day of a feast when a prophetic incident occurred: the high priest brought out only eleven golden cups instead of twelve, foretelling the eventual displacement of one of their number. (014-004) Beyond Egypt, kings also dedicated offerings abroad — [[Amasis]] sent gilded statues and painted images of himself to temples in Hellas, including at [[Kyrene]] and Lindos. (017-003)

### Solemn Assemblies and Processions

The Egyptians were, according to Herodotus, the first people to institute solemn assemblies, processions, and formal approaches to the temples. He considered this claim well-founded because the Egyptian celebrations of these rites had been held from a very ancient time, whereas comparable Hellenic observances were introduced only recently. These processions were public manifestations of Egyptian piety, bringing the community together at the temples in structured acts of collective worship. (006-002)

### The Festival of Lamps

One of the most striking Egyptian observances was the great lamp festival, centred at the city of [[Sais]] but observed throughout all Egypt. On the night of this festival, all Egyptians who did not attend the solemn assembly at Sais nevertheless observed the occasion by lighting lamps — not only in the city itself but in every household across the land. Herodotus noted that the reason why light and honour were allotted to this particular night was the subject of a sacred story, which he recorded but did not elaborate upon in his surviving account. (006-003)

## Herodotus's Commentary

Herodotus was struck by the antiquity and originality of Egyptian offering customs, which he considered the source of similar Hellenic practices. His claim that the Hellenes learned solemn assemblies and processions from the Egyptians reflects his broader conviction — repeated throughout his account — that Egyptian religion was the oldest and most developed in the known world. The lamp festival particularly impressed him, both for its universal observance across Egypt and for the sacred narrative that justified it, a narrative he alluded to but declined to relate, respecting the boundary between public custom and secret religious knowledge.

## Significance

Egyptian offerings reveal a religious system in which ritual practice, rather than theological doctrine, formed the primary mode of worship. The diversity of offering types — animal sacrifices, libations, votive statues, and communal festivals — demonstrates that Egyptian piety operated on multiple scales simultaneously, from the individual household lamp to the king's international dedications. The Egyptian claim to have originated the very forms of temple worship acknowledged by the Hellenes places offerings at the centre of Herodotus's broader argument for Egyptian cultural primacy.
