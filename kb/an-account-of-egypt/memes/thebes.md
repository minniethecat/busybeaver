## Overview

Thebes (Egyptian: Waset, Greek: Thebai) was the great city of Upper Egypt and, during the New Kingdom, the religious and political capital of the Egyptian empire. Herodotus refers to it primarily as the seat of the oracle of Ammon (Zeus) and as a city of immense antiquity and wealth. (002-001) (014-004)

## Location / Geography

Thebes was situated on the eastern bank of the Nile in Upper Egypt, approximately 800 km south of the Mediterranean. Herodotus notes that the distance from the sea to Thebes was a journey of nine days upriver from the coast, or 4,860 furlongs — roughly 612 miles by his calculation. (002-001)

## Historical Significance

Thebes was the cult centre of Ammon (Amun-Ra), the king of the Egyptian gods, whom the Greeks identified with their own Zeus. The oracle of Ammon at Thebes was one of the two most important oracles in Egypt (along with that of Leto at Buto), and its pronouncements carried immense authority. (014-004) The city was also the seat of the Theban dynasty that expelled the Hyksos and established the New Kingdom, though Herodotus does not directly discuss this history.

## Herodotus's Description

Herodotus describes Thebes primarily through measurements and distances. He records the nine-day river journey from the coast, the furlong count, and the narrow width of the Nile valley in this region — between the Arabian and Libyan mountains — calculated from his own observations and those of others. (002-001)
