## Overview

Necos (also known as Necho II) was a pharaoh of the Twenty-Sixth Dynasty who reigned from approximately 610 to 595 BCE. He was the son of [[psammetichos|Psammetichos]] I. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] records three major undertakings of his reign: an ambitious canal connecting [[nile-river|the Nile]] to the [[erythraian-sea|Erythraian Sea]] (the modern [[erythraian-sea|Red Sea]]), naval construction on both the Mediterranean and the [[erythraian-sea|Red Sea]], and successful military campaigns in [[syria|Syria]] (015-004) (015-005).

## Background / Biography

Necos inherited the throne from his father [[psammetichos|Psammetichos]], who had unified [[egypt|Egypt]] and established the [[sais|Sais]] dynasty (015-004). Earlier in his life, before becoming king, Necos's father [[psammetichos|Psammetichos]] had been a fugitive from the [[ethiopia-region|Ethiopia]]n king [[sabacos|Sabacos]], who had killed Necos's grandfather — also named Necos — but [[psammetichos|Psammetichos]] eventually returned and overthrew the twelve kings who then ruled [[egypt|Egypt]] (014-008). The younger Necos reigned for a total of sixteen years, a period marked by grand engineering projects and military expansion (015-005).

## Role in the Account

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] presents Necos primarily as a builder and a warrior. His most celebrated project was the channel from [[nile-river|the Nile]] to the [[erythraian-sea|Erythraian Sea]] — a precursor to the modern Suez Canal — which was dug wide enough for two triremes to pass abreast and required a four-day voyage to traverse (015-004). However, Necos abandoned the project after an oracle warned him that he was labouring for the Barbarian, which [[herodotus|Herodotus]] explains means all who do not speak the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian tongue (015-004). The canal was later completed by the [[persian|Persian]] king [[dareios|Dareios]]. Necos also constructed triremes for both the Mediterranean and the [[erythraian-sea|Erythraian Sea]], and he engaged in a successful land campaign, defeating the [[syria|Syrians]] at Magdolos and capturing the great city of Cadytis (015-005). He dedicated the armour he wore in these victories to [[apollo|Apollo]] at Branchidai of the Milesians (015-005). Twelve myriads of [[egypt|Egypt]]ians perished during the canal digging (015-004).

## Herodotus's Portrayal

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] presents Necos with respect, as an active and ambitious ruler. Unlike the pyramid builders of the Old Kingdom, Necos faces no moral condemnation from the narrator. His canal project is described in precise geographic and engineering detail, suggesting [[herodotus|Herodotus]] had access to reliable information. The oracle's interruption of the canal project introduces a characteristic Herodotean theme: even the mightiest human undertakings are subject to divine will.

> The son of Psammetichos was Necos, and he became king of Egypt. This man was the first who attempted the channel leading to the Erythraian Sea, which Dareios the Persian afterwards completed: the length of this is a voyage of four days, and in breadth it was so dug that two triremes could go side by side driven by oars; and the water is brought into it from the Nile.
> (015-004)

> On land Necos engaged battle at Magdolos with the Syrians, and conquered them; and after this he took Cadytis, which is a great city of Syria: and the dress which he wore when he made these conquests he dedicated to Apollo, sending it to Branchidai of the Milesians.
> (015-005)

> In the reign of Necos there perished while digging it twelve myriads of the Egyptians. Now Necos ceased in the midst of his digging, because the utterance of an Oracle impeded him, which was to the effect that he was working for the Barbarian: and the Egyptians call all men Barbarians who do not agree with them in speech.
> (015-004)
