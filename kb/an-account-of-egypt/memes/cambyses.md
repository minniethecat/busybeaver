## Overview

Cambyses (Old [[persian|Persian]]: 𐎣𐎲𐎢𐎪𐎡𐎹, Kambūjiya) was the second king of the [[persian|Achaemenid]] [[persian|Persian]] Empire, the son and successor of Cyrus the Great. In [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s "An Account of [[egypt|Egypt]]," Cambyses appears as the [[persian|Persian]] conqueror of [[egypt|Egypt]] and as a figure in the story of [[ladike|Ladike]], a Greek woman who became the wife of the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian king [[amasis|Amasis]]. (001-007) (017-001)

## Background / Biography

Cambyses inherited the [[persian|Persian]] throne upon the death of his father Cyrus. His mother was Cassandane, daughter of Pharnaspes, whose death before Cyrus's own had prompted the great king to declare an empire-wide period of mourning. Cambyses's most consequential act as king was the conquest of [[egypt|Egypt]], which brought the 26th Dynasty to an end and incorporated [[nile-river|the Nile]] kingdom into the [[persian|Achaemenid]] Empire. (001-007)

[[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s "Histories" contains an extensive account of Cambyses's [[egypt|Egypt]]ian campaign in a later book, but in the "Account of [[egypt|Egypt]]" (Book II, Euterpe), his role is mentioned primarily in connection with the [[ladike|Ladike]] narrative and as Cyrus's successor.

## Role in the Account

**Conquest of [[egypt|Egypt]].** [[herodotus|Herodotus]] records that Cambyses conquered [[egypt|Egypt]], an event that serves as a chronological anchor in the narrative. The historian notes the legendary endurance of the skulls of the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians slain at the battle of Papremis, which was fought during Cambyses's invasion. (017-001)

**The [[ladike|Ladike]] episode.** Cambyses appears incidentally in the story of [[ladike|Ladike]], a Greek woman from [[cyrene|Kyrene]] who became the wife of king [[amasis|Amasis]]. After Cambyses conquered [[egypt|Egypt]], he learned of [[ladike|Ladike]]'s identity from her and sent her back unharmed to [[cyrene|Kyrene]]. This detail — Cambyses's mercy toward a foreign woman — stands in contrast to his broader reputation for cruelty in the later books of the Histories. (017-001)

## Herodotus's Portrayal

[[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s brief mentions of Cambyses in the "Account of [[egypt|Egypt]]" are neutral in tone. The historian records Cambyses's succession and conquest without editorial comment. The [[ladike|Ladike]] episode presents Cambyses as capable of restraint and recognition of personal status — a nuanced portrait that complements the fuller, more negative depiction of Cambyses found in Book III of the Histories, where his alleged madness and desecration of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian temples are detailed. (001-007) (017-001)
