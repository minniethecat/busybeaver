## Overview

[[Troy]] (Ilion) appears in Herodotus's *Account of Egypt* not through direct description but as the destination that was never reached — the city to which [[Alexander (Paris)|Alexander]] was sailing with [[Helen of Troy|Helen]] when contrary winds drove him to Egypt instead. The city is the geographical backdrop to Herodotus's alternative Egyptian tradition of the Trojan War, in which the Trojans truthfully insisted that Helen was not in their city but had been detained in Egypt by King [[Proteus]] (011-004).

## The Egyptian Alternative

According to the priests of [[Memphis]], when the Hellenic army arrived at Troy and demanded Helen's return, the Teucrians (Trojans) told them the truth — "both with oath and without oath, namely that in deed and in truth they had not Helen nor the wealth for which demand was made, but that both were in Egypt" (011-004). The Hellenes, believing themselves mocked, persisted in their siege until they took the city. Only when they breached the walls and found no Helen did they finally credit the story and dispatch [[Menelaos]] himself to Egypt.

This tradition casts Troy in a tragic light: the city was destroyed for a woman who was never there. Herodotus does not pass explicit judgment on this version, but his presentation of the Egyptian priests' account — with its emphasis on Trojan honesty and Greek obstinacy — implicitly questions the moral foundations of the Homeric war narrative.

## Homeric Context

Herodotus engages directly with Homer's treatment of the Trojan War, arguing that the poet was aware of the Egyptian variant but chose a different version more suitable to epic poetry. He cites lines from the *Iliad* describing Alexander's wanderings past Sidon as evidence that Homer knew of the Egyptian tradition (010-006). In this reading, Homer suppressed the Egyptian version in favour of a narrative that placed Helen at Troy — a choice with profound consequences for the subsequent Greek literary tradition.

## Troy in Herodotus's Method

Troy serves Herodotus as an example of how foreign sources — in this case, the institutional memory of the Egyptian priesthood — can correct or complicate Greek tradition. The city that Homer made the center of the greatest epic in Greek literature is, in Herodotus's Egyptian digression, revealed as the site of a catastrophic misunderstanding: a ten-year siege fought for a prize that was, all the while, safe in Egypt under Proteus's protection.
