## Overview

Egyptian priests occupied a distinctive position in ancient Egyptian society, governed by elaborate purity regulations and daily rituals that [[Herodotus]] documented during his consultations with priests at [[Memphis]], [[Heliopolis]], and Thebes. Their practices reflected a culture that prioritized religious cleanliness above all else, and their economic privileges — including tax-free land grants — sustained a priestly class whose authority rested equally on ritual expertise and historical knowledge. (004-005) (016-001)

## Description

### Purity and Grooming

The priests maintained extraordinary standards of personal cleanliness. They shaved their entire bodies every other day to prevent lice or any impurity from being present while serving the gods. They wore garments of linen, always newly washed, and made this a special point of practice. They bathed in cold water twice each day and twice each night. (004-005)

### Circumcision and Cleanliness

The Egyptians practiced circumcision for the sake of cleanliness, preferring to be clean rather than comely. This custom was observed by all Egyptians, though the priests adhered to an especially rigorous standard of bodily purity. Herodotus noted this distinction between Egyptian and other peoples' attitudes toward the body. (004-005)

### Dietary Restrictions

Priests observed strict dietary rules. They drank from cups of bronze, which they rinsed out every day — a practice followed not by some but by all priests. They were forbidden from eating fish and from tasting beans, which the Egyptians did not sow in their land at all. (004-005)

### Priestly Knowledge

The priests of [[Heliopolis]] were said to be the most learned in records of the Egyptians. [[Herodotus]] visited both Thebes and Heliopolis specifically to verify whether the priests at these places would agree in their accounts with those at [[Memphis]], using cross-verification as a method of historical inquiry. (001-009)

### Social Class and Land Privileges

Among the seven classes into which Egyptian society was divided, the priests formed a distinct order alongside the warriors, cowherds, swineherds, shopkeepers, interpreters, and boatmen. The priests enjoyed a special economic privilege: each priest was granted twelve yokes of land held free from all imposts. The Egyptian yoke of land measured one hundred Egyptian cubits in each direction, the Egyptian cubit being equal to that of Samos. This tax-free land grant provided the economic foundation that sustained the priesthood and its elaborate temple operations. (015-011) (016-001)

### Role in Sacrificial Testing

Priests performed the critical function of examining sacrificial animals for purity. When an ox was brought for sacrifice, a priest appointed for the purpose examined the beast standing and lying on its back, drew out its tongue to verify the appointed signs, and checked the hairs of its tail for natural growth. If the animal passed all tests, the priest marked it with papyrus rolled around the horns, plastered sealing-earth over the papyrus, and impressed his signet-ring upon it. No animal could be sacrificed without this priestly seal — the penalty for sacrificing an unsealed beast was death. (004-006)

## Herodotus's Commentary

Herodotus regarded the Egyptians as "religious excessively beyond all other men," and his description of the priesthood constitutes the most detailed evidence for this judgment. He was particularly struck by the priests' role as his primary informants — the learned men of Heliopolis, Memphis, and Thebes whose consistent testimony across cities gave him confidence in his account. The economic privilege of the priesthood (twelve tax-free yokes of land) is reported without judgment, but Herodotus's choice to record the precise measurement of the Egyptian yoke in cubits and to equate it with the Samian cubit reflects his characteristic method of rendering foreign customs intelligible to a Hellenic audience through systematic comparison.

## Significance

The Egyptian priesthood embodied the civilization's exceptional religious devotion. Their rigorous purity practices, specialized knowledge, and role as custodians of Egyptian history made them the principal informants for Herodotus's account of the land. The intersection of religious authority, economic privilege, and social hierarchy in the priestly class reveals a society in which the sacred and the political were inseparable — priests controlled not only access to the gods through sacrificial testing but also the historical narratives that defined Egyptian identity.
