## Overview

The [[Pillars of Heracles]] (Herakles) appear in Herodotus's *Account of Egypt* in two distinct but related contexts: as the geographical landmark at the Strait of Gibraltar marking the western boundary of the known world, and more prominently, through the commemorative pillars erected by the Egyptian king [[Sesostris]] during his campaigns across Asia and into Europe. The pillars of Sesostris served as propaganda monuments, their inscriptions recording his conquests and, for those who surrendered without resistance, bearing the image of female genitalia to mark their cowardice.

## Sesostris's Pillars

The most detailed account of royal pillars in the *Account* concerns the great conqueror [[Sesostris]] (Senusret III or a composite figure of Middle Kingdom pharaohs). According to the Egyptian priests, Sesostris first launched a naval expedition from the [[Arabian Gulf]] (Red Sea) into the [[Erythraian Sea]], subduing coastal peoples until shoals rendered further navigation impossible (009-006).

Returning to Egypt, Sesostris then "took a great army and marched over the continent, subduing every nation which stood in his way" (009-006). In the lands of those who fought valiantly, he erected pillars inscribed with "his own name and the name of his country, and how he had subdued them by his power." For cities that surrendered without resistance, the pillars additionally bore "the hidden parts of a woman, desiring to signify by this that the people were cowards and effeminate" (009-006).

These pillars thus functioned as both territorial markers and moral judgments on conquered peoples, distinguishing the brave from the cowardly through gendered iconography. Sesostris's campaign carried him across Asia and ultimately "over to Europe from Asia," where he subdued the Scythians and Thracians — the Egyptian army having advanced, Herodotus notes, as far as any army is known to have marched (009-006).

## Geographical Significance

The Pillars of Heracles as a geographical marker — the Strait of Gibraltar — represented the westernmost limit of the known world for both Greeks and Egyptians. Herodotus uses the pillars as a reference point for discussing the Nile's westward-flowing upper course, noting the testimony of men from [[Cyrene]] who had traveled far into the interior seeking the river's sources (004-001). The Erythraian Sea to the east and the Pillars of Heracles to the west framed the geographical imagination of the ancient Mediterranean world.

## Heracles in Egypt

Herodotus distinguishes between the Egyptian [[Heracles]], whom the priests counted among the twelve great gods, and the Greek Heracles (son of Amphitryon and Alcmene), arguing that the Egyptians did not borrow the name from the Greeks but that the Greeks borrowed it from the Egyptians (005-002). The Egyptian Heracles was thus a far more ancient figure than his Greek counterpart, and the pillars on Sesostris's monuments — like the geographical pillars of Gibraltar — bore his name in this older Egyptian sense.
