## Overview

The [[Nile Mouths]] are the terminal distributaries through which the [[Nile River]] empties into the [[Mediterranean Sea]]. [[Herodotus]] enumerates seven mouths in total, of which he records five as navigable — correcting the [[Ionians|Ionian]] tradition, which claimed only three existed (002-003). The mouths served as the principal gateways for trade, travel, and invasion between [[Egypt]] and the Mediterranean world. Each mouth was associated with a city or region, and the enumeration of the mouths reflects both the geographical reality of the [[Nile Delta]] and the administrative divisions of [[Lower Egypt]].

## The Seven Mouths

### Navigable Mouths

| Mouth | Modern Equivalent | Associated City | Notes |
|-------|------------------|-----------------|-------|
| Pelusiac | Easternmost branch | [[Pelusion]] | Eastern frontier; watch-tower of Perseus marked its western limit |
| Mendesian | East of Damietta branch | [[Mendes]] | Named for the cult city of the ram-god Mendes |
| Sebennytic | Central Delta | Sebennytus | Central navigable channel connecting interior Delta to the sea |
| Saïtic | Western Delta | [[Sais]] | Associated with the 26th Dynasty capital |
| Canobic | Westernmost navigable | [[Canobus]] | Gateway for Greek travelers; site of Heracles's temple at the mouth |

### Non-Navigable Mouths

| Mouth | Notes |
|-------|-------|
| Bolbitine | Smaller western channel; associated with later Rosetta |
| Bucolic (Phatnitic) | Eastern minor channel; noted by later geographers |

## Commentary

Herodotus's correction of the Ionian count — from three to seven mouths — is characteristic of his method: he prioritises Egyptian priestly knowledge over Greek geographical tradition, treating the former as more authoritative because it is local and institutionally preserved. His listing of the mouths serves both a descriptive purpose (mapping the Delta's coastline) and a polemical one (correcting Ionian error).

The identification of navigable versus non-navigable mouths also reflects practical maritime knowledge. The five navigable mouths were the channels through which ship traffic entered and departed Egypt, and their enumeration would have been of direct interest to Herodotus's Greek audience, for whom Egyptian trade and travel were matters of economic and strategic importance.

The mouths were not static features; they shifted over time due to the Nile's sediment deposition and changes in sea level. Herodotus's account thus captures a snapshot of the Delta's geography in the 5th century BCE, before major changes occurred in the Hellenistic and Roman periods.
