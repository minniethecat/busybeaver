## Overview

Lake Moeris (modern Birket Qarun in the Fayum depression) was one of the greatest engineering achievements of ancient Egypt — an immense artificial lake created by the Middle Kingdom pharaohs to regulate the Nile's flood and reclaim agricultural land. Herodotus describes the lake and its associated Labyrinth with particular awe, declaring the lake "a cause for marvel even greater than" the Labyrinth itself. (014-006)

## Location / Geography

Lake Moeris lay in the Fayum depression, a natural basin west of the Nile valley, connected to the river by the channel known as Bahr Yusuf (Joseph's Canal). Herodotus reports that the lake's circuit measured 3,600 furlongs (sixty schoines) — the same length, he notes, as "the extent of Egypt itself along the sea." (014-006) The lake extended lengthwise from north to south, reaching a depth of fifty fathoms at its deepest point. The surrounding country was "very deficient in water," so the lake was fed artificially by a canal from the Nile: for six months of the year water flowed in from the river, and for six months it flowed back out. (014-007)

## The Two Pyramids

In the middle of the lake stood two pyramids, each rising above the water to a height of fifty fathoms, with an equal height built below the water — making each pyramid a total of one hundred fathoms (one furlong, or roughly six hundred Greek feet). (014-006) Herodotus calculates: "the fathom being measured as six feet or four cubits, the feet being four palms each, and the cubits six." (014-007) Upon each pyramid was placed a colossal stone statue seated upon a chair.

## Revenue and Engineering

Herodotus records a striking economic detail: whenever the water flowed out of the lake into the Nile for six months, the royal treasury collected a talent of silver per day from the fish caught; when water flowed in, the revenue was only twenty pounds per day. (014-007) This seasonal fishery made Lake Moeris not only an engineering marvel but a significant source of royal income.

The construction of the lake raised the question of where the excavated earth was disposed. Herodotus personally investigated this: "since I did not see anywhere existing the earth dug out of this excavation (for that was a matter which drew my attention), I asked those who dwelt nearest to the lake where the earth was which had been dug out." (014-007) The locals told him it had been carried to the Nile, and Herodotus accepted this explanation, comparing it by report to a similar feat at Nineveh — where thieves dug an underground tunnel to steal the treasure of King Sardanapallos, carrying the excavated earth to the Tigris by night. (014-007) In Egypt, the work was done openly by day: "as they dug the Egyptians carried to the Nile the earth which was dug out; and the river, when it received it, would naturally bear it away and disperse it." (014-007)

## The Underground Channel Legend

Herodotus also records a local tradition that the lake had an underground outlet flowing westward to the Syrtis in Libya, turning toward the interior of the continent and "running along by the mountain which is above Memphis." (014-007) Whether this represents a genuine geological observation, a folk tradition, or simple misinformation, Herodotus does not adjudicate — he simply reports what the "natives of the place" told him.
