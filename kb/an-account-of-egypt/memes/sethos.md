## Overview

Sethos is a unique figure in [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s *Account of [[egypt|Egypt]]*: a priest of the god [[hephaistos|Hephaistos]] who ascended to the throne and ruled [[egypt|Egypt]] as both religious and political leader. His reign is most dramatic for the miraculous deliverance of [[egypt|Egypt]] from an invading Assyrian army — a deliverance effected not by [[egypt|Egypt]]ian warriors but by a plague of field-mice sent by the god himself. (013-008) Sethos represents the only instance in [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s narrative of a priest who became a king, and his story serves as both a conclusion to the long sequence of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian rulers and a testament to the power of divine intervention.

## Background / Biography

Sethos succeeded to the throne after the departure of the [[ethiopia-region|Ethiopia]]n king [[sabacos|Sabacos]] and the brief restoration of [[anysis|Anysis]], [[anysis|the blind king]] of the city of [[anysis|Anysis]]. (013-006) (013-008) Unlike his predecessors, Sethos came from the priestly class rather than the warrior class. He served as priest of [[hephaistos|Hephaistos]] — the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian god of craftsmen and metallurgy, known to the [[hellenes|Greeks]] as [[hephaistos|Ptah]] — and his rise to kingship appears to have been a direct elevation from the temple to the throne.

## Role in the Account

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] presents Sethos's reign as the final chapter in the long history of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian kingship as recorded by the priests. (013-009) The priest-king occupies two distinct narrative functions in the *Account*.

**The Neglected Warrior Class.** Upon becoming king, Sethos showed contempt for the warrior class of [[egypt|Egypt]]. He treated them with disrespect and even confiscated the twelve acres of choice land that each [[egypt|Egypt]]ian soldier had traditionally held under previous kings. (013-008) His reasoning, as [[herodotus|Herodotus]] implies, was that as a priest-king he considered divine protection sufficient and had no need of a military establishment. This systematic alienation of the warrior class set the stage for a crisis: when Sennacherib, king of the Arabians and Assyrians, marched against [[egypt|Egypt]] with a great army, the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian warriors refused to fight for Sethos. (013-008)

**The Miracle of the Field-Mice.** Abandoned by his army and facing certain destruction, Sethos withdrew into the inner sanctuary of the temple and lamented before the image of the god. [[hephaistos|Hephaistos]] appeared to him in a dream, promising deliverance: if Sethos went forth to meet the enemy at [[pelusion|Pelusion]], led by his artisans and tradesmen, the god himself would ensure victory. (013-008) Sethos obeyed, and during the night before the battle, an uncountable multitude of field-mice swarmed into the Assyrian camp, gnawing through their quivers, their bows, and the handles of their shields. The next morning the Assyrians, stripped of their weapons, fled in panic and were slaughtered. (013-008)

**The Memorial.** In commemoration of this divine rescue, a stone statue of Sethos was erected in the temple of [[hephaistos|Hephaistos]], holding a field-mouse in its hand and bearing the inscription: "Let him who looks upon me learn to be pious." This statue was still standing in [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s own day, and the historian reports having seen it himself. (013-008)

## Herodotus's Portrayal

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] presents Sethos without explicit moral judgment, but the narrative contains an implicit critique of the priest-king's abandonment of the warrior class. The kingdom's vulnerability to invasion is portrayed as a direct consequence of Sethos's policy, and the miraculous deliverance — while celebrated — does not erase the fact that [[egypt|Egypt]]'s army was deliberately weakened by a king who prioritised piety over military preparedness.

The story also serves as a transition point in [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s narrative architecture. Sethos is the last of the rulers in the priestly king-list, and his reign closes the epoch of independent [[egypt|Egypt]]ian monarchy before the period of the twelve kings and the eventual rise of the 26th Dynasty under [[psammetichos|Psammetichos]]. (013-009) (014-004)

## Sources

> After him there came to the throne the priest of Hephaistos, whose name was Sethos. This man, they said, neglected and held in no regard the warrior class of the Egyptians, considering that he would have no need of them; and besides other slights which he put upon them, he also took from them the acres which had been given to them as a special gift in the reigns of the former kings, twelve acres to each man. After this, Sennacherib king of the Arabians and of the Assyrians marched a great host against Egypt. Then the warriors of the Egyptians refused to come to his rescue, and the priest, being driven into a strait, entered into the sanctuary of the temple and bewailed to the image of the god the danger which was impending over him.
> (013-008)

> After this, when the priest came to Pelusion, there came upon the enemy in the night a multitude of field-mice, which ate up their quivers and their bows, and moreover the handles of their shields, so that on the next day, their arms having been thus gnawed, they fled, and great numbers fell. And at the present time this king stands in the temple of Hephaistos in stone, holding upon his hand a mouse, and by letters inscribed he says these words: "Let him who looks upon me learn to be pious."
> (013-008)

> So far in the story the Egyptians and the priests were they who made the report, declaring that from the first king down to this priest of Hephaistos who reigned last, there had been three hundred and forty-one generations of men.
> (013-009)
