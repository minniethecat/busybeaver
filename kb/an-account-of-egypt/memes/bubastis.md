## Overview

Bubastis was a major city in the eastern Nile Delta and the cult centre of the cat-goddess Bast (also called Bubastis by the Greeks). Herodotus describes the city and its temple in detail, devoting particular attention to the annual festival of Bast — which he calls the most enthusiastically celebrated of all Egyptian religious gatherings, drawing as many as seven hundred thousand pilgrims. (005-003) (005-007)

## Location / Geography

Bubastis was situated in the eastern Delta on the Pelusiac branch of the Nile, approximately eighty kilometres northeast of modern Cairo. The city lay on an island surrounded by canals, with the temple of Bast occupying the city centre in a grove of trees.

## Historical Significance

The temple of Bast at Bubastis was, according to Herodotus, the most beautiful temple in Egypt — not the largest, but the most pleasing to the eye. Built on raised ground in the centre of the city, it commanded a view over the entire urban landscape from all sides, while the surrounding streets were built up to the level of the temple wall. (005-007) The annual festival attracted pilgrims from across Egypt, who travelled by boat, playing music, singing, and engaging in the celebratory customs that Herodotus describes with ethnographic precision. (005-003)

## Sources
