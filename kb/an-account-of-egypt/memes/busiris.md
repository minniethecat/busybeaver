## Overview

Busiris was an Egyptian city situated in the middle of the Nile Delta, notable in Herodotus's account for its great temple of Isis and the solemn assembly held there in honor of the goddess. The city also gave its name to one of the two military districts of the Hermotybians, one of the Egyptian warrior classes. (006-002) (015-011)

## Religious Significance

Herodotus records that after the festival of Artemis at Bubastis, the Egyptians proceeded to Busiris for the solemn assembly of Isis. The city contained what the historian describes as "a very great temple of Isis," situating Busiris among the principal religious centers of Lower Egypt. In Herodotus's syncretic framework, Isis is identified with the Greek goddess Demeter. (006-002)

The prominence of Busiris as a cult center for Isis reflects the widespread reverence for the goddess throughout Egypt, a devotion Herodotus emphasizes by noting that "all the Egyptians equally reverence" Isis and Osiris. (005-001)

## Military Administration

Busiris served as the namesake of one of the districts of the Hermotybians, the Egyptian warrior class. The Hermotybians, whose numbers reached one hundred sixty thousand at their peak, were drawn from specific districts including Busiris, Sais, Chemmis, Papremis, the island of Prosopitis, and half of Natho. From birth, these men were dedicated exclusively to military training, forbidden from practicing any handicraft. (015-011)
