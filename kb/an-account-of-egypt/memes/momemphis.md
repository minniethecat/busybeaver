## Overview

[[Momemphis]] was a city in the Egyptian Delta that served as the site of the climactic battle in the civil war between King [[Apries]] (Wahibre Haaibre) and the usurper [[Amasis]] (Ahmose II) in the sixth century BCE. The battle at Momemphis marked the end of Apries's reign and the beginning of Amasis's rule — a pivotal turning point in the history of the 26th (Saite) Dynasty as recorded by Herodotus.

## The Battle of Momemphis

Herodotus describes the confrontation in vivid terms. [[Amasis]], having been proclaimed king by the Egyptian troops who revolted against Apries, advanced at the head of the entire native Egyptian army. Apries, in response, led his foreign mercenaries — the Ionian and Carian troops upon whom he had long relied. The two forces "in their approach to one another had come to the city of Momemphis" where they engaged in battle (016-002).

Despite fighting well, the foreign mercenaries were "much inferior in number" and were defeated by Amasis's larger Egyptian force. Herodotus records that Apries had grown so overconfident in his rule that "not even a god would be able to cause him to cease from his rule, so firmly did he think that it was established" (016-002). This hubris proved fatal: Apries was defeated, captured alive, and brought to [[Sais]] — formerly his own palace, now the residence of Amasis.

## Prelude to Battle

The conflict between Apries and Amasis had been building through a series of provocations. Apries had sent [[Patarbemis]], one of his most distinguished courtiers, to summon Amasis to his presence. Amasis responded with defiance, prompting Apries to mutilate Patarbemis by cutting off his ears and nose in a fit of violent rage (015-010). This act of cruelty turned the remaining Egyptian nobles against Apries, driving them to join Amasis and swelling the native army that would face the mercenaries at Momemphis.

## Aftermath

Following the battle, Apries was held in the palace at Sais under good treatment, but popular pressure forced Amasis to hand the deposed king over to the Egyptians, who strangled him. Amasis then established himself as pharaoh, ushering in a period of prosperity and Greek-Egyptian cooperation that Herodotus describes in detail. The battle of Momemphis thus marks the transition from one chapter of Saite history to the next.
