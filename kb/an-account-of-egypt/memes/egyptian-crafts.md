## Overview

Herodotus's account of Egypt provides valuable glimpses into the craft traditions and artisanal practices of ancient Egyptian society. While the *Histories* does not offer a systematic survey of Egyptian crafts, scattered passages illuminate several important domains of skilled production: the hereditary craft of embalming, the weaving of linen and wool, the quarrying and working of stone for monumental architecture, the casting and working of metals, and the carving of wooden and stone figures. Together, these references sketch a society in which craft specialization was well developed and in which certain occupations were organized as inherited professions. (008-002) (004-004)

## Description

### Embalming: The Hereditary Craft

The most detailed account of an Egyptian craft in Herodotus is his description of the embalmers. This occupation, he reports, was practiced by "certain persons who employ themselves regularly and inherit this as a craft." When a corpse was brought to them, the embalmers displayed wooden models of corpses, painted to resemble reality, illustrating three tiers of service at three different price points. In the most costly method, the brain was extracted through the nostrils with a crooked iron tool, the abdomen was cut open with a sharp Ethiopian stone, the cavity was cleansed with palm-wine and pounded spices, and the body was then filled with pure myrrh, cassia, and other spices (except frankincense) before being sewn up and covered in natron for seventy days. After this period, the corpse was washed, wrapped in fine linen bands smeared with gum, enclosed in a wooden figure shaped like a man, and stored upright in a sepulchral chamber. The middle and least expensive methods used progressively simpler techniques. (008-002)

The embalmers' trade was organized with craft-specific ethical rules. Herodotus records that the wives of prominent men and women of great beauty were not delivered to the embalmers until three or four days after death. The reason, the Egyptians explained, was that one embalmer had been caught violating the corpse of a recently deceased woman, and his fellow-craftsman had reported him — an indication that the craft possessed internal mechanisms of accountability. (008-002)

### Weaving and Textile Production

Textile crafts, particularly weaving, were practiced in the domestic sphere and displayed the characteristic Egyptian inversion of gender roles. Whereas in Greece weaving was women's work, in Egypt the men remained at home and wove while the women went to the market. The Egyptians also pushed the woof downward in weaving, whereas other peoples pushed it upward — yet another technological inversion. The products of Egyptian weaving included the fine linen tunics (*calasiris*) worn by all Egyptians, as well as linen corslets of exceptional quality. King Amasis dedicated a linen corslet "worthy to be seen" at the temple of Athene in Lindos, and linen bandages were the essential material of the embalmer's art. (004-004) (017-002)

### Stone-Working and Monumental Construction

The construction of the pyramids under King Cheops represents the largest-scale mobilization of craft labor in Herodotus's Egyptian narrative. A hundred thousand men worked at a time, in three-month shifts, for ten years on the causeway alone — a project that involved quarrying stones from the Arabian mountains, transporting them across the Nile by boat, and hauling them to the Libyan mountains. The pyramid itself, a further twenty years in the building, was constructed in tiers like steps, with stones raised by machines made of short wooden planks. (012-002)

The decorative crafts applied to monumental architecture are described in connection with the temple of Hephaistos at Memphis. King Asychis built its eastern gateway, "by far the most beautiful and the largest of the gateways," richly adorned with carved figures and "innumerable ornaments of building" surpassing all others. These carvings represent the work of skilled sculptors and stone-carvers whose craft adorned the major religious buildings of Egypt. (013-003)

### Metalworking and Sculpture

Egyptian metalworking is illustrated through Herodotus's story of King Amasis, a commoner who became king. Amasis possessed a golden foot-basin in which he and his guests washed their feet. To win the respect of the Egyptians, who initially despised his humble origins, Amasis broke the basin apart and had it remade into the image of a god, setting it up in the city where the people came to revere it. When the Egyptians gathered to do reverence to the image, Amasis revealed its origin, explaining that he himself was like the foot-basin: once a common man, now their king, and therefore worthy of honor. The story demonstrates both the skill of Egyptian metalworkers in casting and repurposing gold, and the cultural value placed on divine images as objects of public veneration. (016-003)

Amasis's dedications in Greece further illustrate the range of Egyptian craft products. At Cyrene he dedicated an image of Athene covered with gold and a painted figure of himself. At Lindos he gave two stone images, and at Samos two wooden figures of himself, still standing behind the doors of the great temple of Hera in Herodotus's day. These offerings span the full range of Egyptian craft materials — gold, stone, wood, linen, and paint — and demonstrate the international reach of Egyptian artisanal production. (017-002)

### Wooden Figure-Making

The craft of carving and painting wooden figures appears in several contexts. In the entertainments of the wealthy, a man carried around a wooden figure of a dead body in a coffin, "made as like the reality as may be both by painting and carving," measuring about one or two cubits each way. This figure was shown to each guest with the words, "When thou lookest upon this, drink and be merry, for thou shalt be such as this when thou art dead." The production of such figures — combining woodcarving, painting, and the realistic representation of the human form — represents a specialized craft tradition distinct from monumental sculpture. (007-005)

## Historical Context

By the 5th century BCE, Egyptian craft traditions already represented millennia of accumulated technical knowledge. The pyramids that [[herodotus|Herodotus]] describes — built by [[cheops|Cheops]] and [[chephren|Chephren]] — were already over two thousand years old at the time of his visit. The craft of embalming, with its elaborate three-tier system of service, had been refined over countless generations of hereditary practitioners. [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s account captures these traditions at a moment when Egypt was under [[persian|Persian]] rule, yet the craft system — with its guild-like organization, hereditary transmission, and internal ethical codes — continued to function as it had for centuries.

## Herodotus's Commentary

[[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s treatment of Egyptian crafts is characteristically attentive to what set them apart from Greek practice. His observation that Egyptian men wove at home while women went to the market — the reverse of Greek norms — and that Egyptians pushed the woof downward rather than upward when weaving, reveals his ethnographic interest in technological and social inversion. The story of [[amasis|Amasis]] and the golden foot-basin serves a double purpose: it illustrates Egyptian metalworking skill while also delivering a pointed parable about social mobility and the nature of honor. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] also notes that the embalmers were a hereditary profession — a pattern of occupational inheritance that differed from the more fluid Greek system of craft apprenticeship.

## Significance

Herodotus's scattered references to Egyptian crafts reveal a society with highly developed artisanal traditions spanning multiple materials and techniques. Some crafts, like embalming, were hereditary professions with codified procedures, price tiers, and ethical rules. Others, like weaving, were domestic activities organized around distinctive gender roles. Monumental stone-working was mobilized on an unprecedented scale for royal projects, while metalworking and sculpture produced objects of both practical use and religious veneration. The quality of Egyptian craftwork was recognized internationally, as evidenced by Amasis's dedications of Egyptian-made objects in Greek temples.
