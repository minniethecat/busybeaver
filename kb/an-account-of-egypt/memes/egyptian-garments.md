## Overview

Egyptian garments, as described by Herodotus, were predominantly made of linen and reflected the distinctive customs of a people who, in many matters, did "the opposite to other men." The historian observed that while Greek men wore one garment each, Egyptian men wore two and their women but one. (004-004) Clothing was closely tied to religious observance, social status, and ritual purity — linen was mandated for priests and temples, wool was forbidden in sacred contexts, and the washing of garments was practiced with exceptional diligence. In describing Egyptian dress, Herodotus revealed a culture in which what one wore was inseparable from who one was before the gods.

## Linen Tunics and the Calasiris

The basic garment of the Egyptians was a linen tunic worn about the legs with fringes, called the *calasiris*. Over this they wore garments of white wool thrown across the shoulders as an outer layer. (007-005) This combination of linen undergarment and woolen outer garment was distinctive, and the linen — fresh and newly washed — was a mark of the Egyptian concern for cleanliness that Herodotus repeatedly noted.

The Egyptians' skill with linen was renowned beyond their borders. Herodotus records that the Colchians, whom he believed to be of Egyptian descent, were the only other people who worked flax in the same fashion as the Egyptians; the linen of Colchis was called "Sardonic" by the Hellenes, while that from Egypt was simply "Egyptian." (009-006) The linen corselet dedicated by King Amasis at the temple of Athene in Lindos was described as "worthy to be seen" — a testament to the quality of Egyptian linen craftsmanship. (017-002)

## Priestly Garments and Purity

The priests of Egypt observed the strictest dress code. They wore linen tunics only, always freshly washed, and sandals made of papyrus — any other garment or sandal was forbidden. (004-005) Their commitment to physical purity extended beyond clothing: they shaved their entire bodies every other day, so that "no lice or any other foul thing may come to be upon them when they minister to the gods," and they washed themselves in cold water twice each day and twice each night. (004-005)

The general population also practiced rigorous garment hygiene. All Egyptians — "not some only but all" — drank from cups of bronze that they rinsed daily, and wore garments of linen always newly washed, making a special point of the practice. (004-005)

## Wool and Sacred Prohibitions

Woolen garments, though worn as outer layers in daily life, were strictly forbidden within temples and in burials. Herodotus noted that this prohibition was not unique to Egypt: the Orphic and Bacchic mysteries (which he believed to be of Egyptian origin) and the Pythagoreans observed the same rule — that no initiate should be buried in woolen garments. The historian observed that "about this there is a sacred story told," though he declined to relate it. (007-005)

## Garments and Mourning

In times of death, the Egyptians employed their garments in distinctive rituals of lamentation. When a man of reputation died, the women of the household plastered their heads and faces with mud, and then — leaving the body at home — wandered about the city beating themselves, their garments bound up by a girdle and their breasts exposed, accompanied by all female relatives. The men beat themselves in the same manner, their garments likewise bound up. (008-002) This practice stood in contrast to Greek mourning, where those most closely affected cut their hair short; the Egyptians, by contrast, let both the hair of the head and the chin grow long during mourning, having been close-shaven before. (004-004)

## Garments and Ritual Impurity

The pig was accounted an abominable animal by the Egyptians, and a person who merely touched one in passing was required to plunge into the river at once — together with their garments — to cleanse themselves. (005-005) This practice illustrates how clothing was integrally bound to the Egyptian conception of ritual purity: garments that had contacted impurity could not simply be put aside; they had to be washed upon the body in the purifying water of the Nile.

## Practical Adaptations

Herodotus also records a practical use of garments against the natural environment. In the fen-land of the Delta, where gnats were abundant, the inhabitants slept under casting-nets — for the gnats, he noted, would bite through a garment or linen sheet if a person slept wrapped in one, but through the mesh of a net they would not even attempt to bite. (009-001)
