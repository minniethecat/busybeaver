## Overview

In [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s *Account of [[egypt|Egypt]]*, **Leto** is one of the eight primordial [[egyptian-religion|Egyptian gods]] who "came into being first" — the oldest generation of the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian pantheon, preceding the twelve gods that included [[Heracles]] and [[Hephaistos]]. Her cult center was at [[Buto]], where her temple was the site of one of [[egypt|Egypt]]'s great annual solemn assemblies and housed the remarkable floating island of [[Chemnis]] (015-002).

## The Eight Primordial Gods

Leto belonged to the first generation of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian deities — the eight gods who preceded the twelve. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] records the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian chronology: the twelve gods were begotten of the eight gods seventeen thousand years before the reign of [[Amasis]], a timescale vastly exceeding Greek chronologies (005-002). Leto's membership in the eight marks her as one of the most ancient divinities in the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian pantheon, predating even the gods universally worshipped throughout [[egypt|Egypt]].

## The Temple at Buto

The temple of Leto at [[buto|Buto]] was one of [[egypt|Egypt]]'s six great festival centers. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] lists the annual assemblies in order of importance: first at [[Bubastis]] for [[artemis|Artemis]], second at Busiris for [[Isis and Osiris|Isis]], third at [[Sais]] for Athene, fourth at [[Heliopolis]] for the Sun, fifth at [[buto|Buto]] for Leto, and sixth at Papremis for Ares (006-002). Worshippers travelled to [[buto|Buto]] annually to offer sacrifices — unlike the grand celebrations at [[bubastis|Bubastis]] or the mourning rites at Busiris, the Leto festival appears to have been a more subdued observance of sacrifice alone.

## The Floating Island of Chemnis

The most remarkable feature of Leto's cult was the island of [[chemnis|Chemnis]], located near the temple at [[buto|Buto]]. The [[egypt|Egypt]]ians maintained that [[chemnis|Chemnis]] was a floating island — a claim [[herodotus|Herodotus]] treats with cautious skepticism, noting that he "did not see it either floating or moved, and I am surprised when I hear that there is an island which really floats" (015-002). The sacred narrative surrounding [[chemnis|Chemnis]] was central to [[egypt|Egypt]]ian religion: Leto, one of the eight primordial gods, received the infant [[apollo|Apollo]] (Horus) from [[isis|Isis]] and hid him on this island, protecting him from [[typhon|Typhon]] (Set), who was "seeking everywhere, desiring to find the son of [[osiris|Osiris]]" (015-002).

## Significance

Leto's role in the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian divine narrative — the protector of the infant Horus — parallels her role in Greek myth as the mother of [[apollo|Apollo]] and [[artemis|Artemis]], hidden from Hera's persecution on the floating island of Delos. [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s identification of the Egyptian Leto with the Greek goddess of the same name is particularly apt, as both traditions associate her with a floating island that served as refuge. The [[chemnis|Chemnis]]/Delos parallel is one of the few instances in the *Account of [[egypt|Egypt]]* where [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s *interpretatio graeca* maps an [[egypt|Egypt]]ian deity onto a Greek counterpart with genuinely convergent mythology rather than superficial functional equivalence.
