Saqqara is the great necropolis of ancient [[Memphis]], located on the western bank of the [[Nile River|Nile]] south of the city. While [[Herodotus]] does not name Saqqara directly, the site is intimately connected with the cult of the [[Apis]] bull that he describes at Memphis, and the later burial of the Apis bulls in the Serapeum at Saqqara represents the monumental development of the sacred bull tradition he witnessed. (002-006)

## The Apis Bull and Memphis

[[Herodotus]] describes the living cult of the Apis bull at [[Memphis]], where the sacred animal — identified by the [[hellenes|Hellenes]] with [[Epaphos]] — was kept in a specially designated court. The Apis was believed to be the herald or incarnation of Ptah, the chief god of Memphis. [[Herodotus]] records that the Apis bull was required to meet specific physical markings: it was black, with a white square on its forehead, the figure of an eagle on its back, double hairs in its tail, and a scarab-shaped mark under its tongue.

## The Serapeum

Though the elaborate subterranean galleries of the Serapeum at Saqqara — where the mummified Apis bulls were interred in massive granite sarcophagi — date to periods after [[Herodotus]]'s visit, the funerary tradition he describes at [[Memphis]] formed the foundation for this later monumental development. The priests of Ptah at Memphis oversaw the cult, and upon the death of an Apis bull, a successor was sought throughout Egypt. The deceased bull was embalmed and buried with full ritual honours — a practice that would eventually find its most magnificent expression in the galleries of Saqqara.

## Archaeological Significance

Saqqara served as the primary necropolis of Memphis from the Early Dynastic Period through the Graeco-Roman era. The site encompasses royal tombs from the earliest dynasties, including the Step Pyramid complex of Djoser, as well as thousands of private tombs spanning three millennia of Egyptian history. The Serapeum, discovered in 1851 by Auguste Mariette, remains one of the most important archaeological discoveries connected to the religious practices [[Herodotus]] described.
