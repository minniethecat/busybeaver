## Overview

Moiris was an [[egypt|Egypt]]ian king remembered by the priests of [[memphis|Memphis]] as the last of his line to achieve lasting renown. [[herodotus|Herodotus]], who learned of Moiris from the priests, records his two great works: the excavation of the lake of Moiris — a vast artificial basin southwest of [[memphis|Memphis]] — and the construction of the northern gateway of the temple of [[hephaistos|Hephaistos]]. The lake served as both a hydraulic regulator and a monument, with pyramids built within it. (009-005)

## Background / Biography

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] places Moiris in a genealogical succession of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian kings, though he records little about his reign other than his building projects. The priests of [[memphis|Memphis]] told the historian that of the three hundred and thirty kings who ruled [[egypt|Egypt]] after [[min|Menes]], Moiris alone achieved lasting renown — a striking testament to how infrastructure could secure a ruler's legacy in a civilization that prized monumental works. (009-005)

The priests also reported that during the reign of Moiris, [[nile-river|the Nile]]'s inundation was sufficient at a mere eight cubits to water [[egypt|Egypt]] below [[memphis|Memphis]]; by [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s own time, sixteen or fifteen cubits were required. This hydrological change was cited as evidence of the land's gradual elevation through silt deposition. (002-001)

## Role in the Account

Moiris appears in [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s narrative primarily through his architectural legacy:

**The lake of Moiris.** [[herodotus|Herodotus]] describes the lake as a wonder surpassing even the labyrinth in scale, measuring three thousand six hundred furlongs in circumference — equal to the entire coastline of [[egypt|Egypt]]. The lake extends north to south, reaches fifty fathoms at its deepest point, and contains two pyramids rising three hundred feet above the water, each with a colossal seated statue upon it. The water was channeled from [[nile-river|the Nile]] through an underground conduit. (014-006)

**The temple gateway.** Moiris built the northern-facing gateway of the temple of [[hephaistos|Hephaistos]] at [[memphis|Memphis]], which the priests cited alongside the lake as his two principal memorials. (009-005)

**Crocodile veneration.** The inhabitants of the lake of Moiris region, along with those of [[thebes|Thebes]], hold crocodiles to be most sacred. Each community keeps one tame crocodile adorned with ornaments of gold and molten stone in its ears and anklets on its front feet. (007-001)

## Herodotus's Portrayal

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] presents Moiris as a figure of genuine accomplishment rather than legend. The historian's claim to have personally seen the lake and labyrinth adds authority to his account, and his measurement of the lake's dimensions — however exaggerated — reflects an effort at empirical verification. The priests' characterization of Moiris as the sole king of renown among hundreds carries an implicit critique of kingship: lasting fame, in the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian context, came not from conquest but from construction. (014-005) (014-006)
