## Overview

Psammis was an [[egypt|Egypt]]ian king of the 26th Dynasty, the son and successor of [[psammetichos|Psammetichos]] ([[psammetichos|Psammetichos]] II). [[herodotus|Herodotus]] records a single notable episode from his brief six-year reign: the visit of Eleian envoys who challenged the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians to improve upon the rules of the Olympic games. Psammis's response — convening the wisest men of [[egypt|Egypt]] to evaluate the Eleian regulations — became an anecdote about [[egypt|Egypt]]ian wisdom. (015-005) (015-006)

## Background / Biography

Psammis inherited the throne from his father [[psammetichos|Psammetichos]], who had reigned for sixteen years. His own reign lasted only six years, ending with his death shortly after a military expedition to [[ethiopia-region|Ethiopia]]. Upon his death, the kingdom passed to his son [[apries|Apries]], who would become one of the most prosperous kings of [[egypt|Egypt]]. (015-005) (015-009)

The brevity of Psammis's reign, combined with his father's long rule and his son's prosperity, places him as a transitional figure in the 26th Dynasty succession.

## Role in the Account

**The Eleian embassy.** [[herodotus|Herodotus]] recounts that during the reign of Psammis, envoys arrived from Elis in Greece, boasting that the regulations of the Olympic games were the most just and honorable possible. They believed that not even the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians — whom they acknowledged as the wisest of men — could suggest any improvement. (015-006)

Psammis summoned the wisest men of [[egypt|Egypt]] to examine the Eleian rules. Upon hearing the details, the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians identified an obvious flaw: the Eleians themselves were permitted to compete, creating an inherent conflict of interest. No matter how just an Eleian judge intended to be, the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians argued, he would inevitably favor his own countryman. This critique revealed the structural bias the Eleians had overlooked. (015-006)

**[[ethiopia-region|Ethiopia]]n expedition.** [[herodotus|Herodotus]] notes tersely that Psammis undertook an expedition to [[ethiopia-region|Ethiopia]] and died shortly thereafter, suggesting a military campaign that may have contributed to or coincided with his death. (015-009)

## Herodotus's Portrayal

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] uses the Psammis episode to illustrate [[egypt|Egypt]]ian wisdom and impartiality — a recurring theme throughout the "Account." The [[egypt|Egypt]]ians are cast as impartial arbiters of Greek institutions, able to perceive flaws that the [[hellenes|Greeks]] themselves could not. The anecdote also serves as a gentle satire of Greek self-regard, with the Eleians arriving in full confidence only to be shown a fundamental flaw in their supposedly perfect system. (015-006)
