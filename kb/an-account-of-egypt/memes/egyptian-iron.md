## Overview

Iron was a rare and valued material in the Egypt of Herodotus's account. Unlike bronze, which was in common daily use, iron appears in the narrative primarily in specialised contexts: the tools of embalmers, the implements of monumental construction, and as a material for noteworthy dedications. Its relative scarcity and the specialised skill required to work it gave iron a distinct place in Egyptian material culture. (008-002) (012-003)

## Iron in Embalming

The Egyptian practice of embalming, described in detail by Herodotus, employed iron tools at a critical stage of the process. In the most elaborate and costly method of embalming, the practitioners first extracted the brain through the nostrils using a crooked iron instrument. This specialised tool was part of the embalmer's professional equipment and was used with precision to prepare the body for its long preservation. The use of iron, rather than bronze, for this delicate operation suggests that the hardness and durability of iron were recognised and valued for specific technical tasks. (008-002)

## Iron in Monumental Construction and Dedication

The construction of the great pyramid, as recorded by Herodotus, involved the use of iron tools on a massive scale. The historian, having noted the silver expenditure recorded in an inscription on the pyramid, wondered aloud how much more must have been spent on "the iron tools used in the work." This remark indicates that iron implements were essential to Egypt's most ambitious building projects. In a different context, iron appears as a material for personal dedication: Rhodopis, the celebrated courtesan, used a tithe of her wealth to commission a large number of iron roasting-spits and dedicated them at Delphi as a memorial of herself — an act that Herodotus records as historical fact. (012-003) (013-002)
