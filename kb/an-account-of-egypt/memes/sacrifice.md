## Overview

Sacrifice was a central act of Egyptian religious life in the fifth century BCE, as recorded by Herodotus. The Egyptians offered animal sacrifices to their gods according to strict ritual rules, with different districts observing distinct prohibitions based on which animals were sacred to their local deities.

## Description

The Egyptians practiced animal sacrifice with elaborate ritual procedures. Before any beast could be offered, a priest examined it for purity — checking for any single black hair upon an ox, inspecting the tongue, and verifying other appointed signs while the animal stood upright and lay on its back. (004-006) An animal deemed unclean was rejected and could not be offered.

Strict regional variations governed which animals could be sacrificed where. The Thebans and those of the district of Thebes sacrificed goats but abstained entirely from sheep, because they held the ram sacred to Zeus-Ammon (whom they called the Theban Zeus). (005-001) Conversely, the Mendesians abstained from goats and sacrificed sheep, because they identified Pan among their eight primordial gods and represented him with a goat's face and legs in their paintings and sculptures. (005-004) The pig was universally regarded as an abominable animal: any Egyptian who accidentally touched a pig would immediately plunge into the river, garments and all, to cleanse themselves. Swineherds, though native Egyptians, were forbidden from entering any temple and could only marry among themselves. (005-005)

Sacrificial meat was consumed communally. After the god's portion was offered at the altar, the worshippers feasted on the remainder. At major festival centers such as Bubastis (for Artemis), Sais (for Athene), Heliopolis, Buto, and Papremis, great numbers assembled to offer sacrifice and make merry.

## Herodotus's Commentary

Herodotus directly compares Egyptian and Greek sacrificial traditions, asserting that the Greeks learned the practice from Egypt. He notes that the Hellenes' story about Heracles being led in procession by Egyptians to be sacrificed to Zeus is "especially foolish" — the Egyptians would never sacrifice a human being, as their customs strictly forbade it. (005-003) He also remarks that the naming of almost all the gods came to Hellas from Egypt, and the sacrificial rites accompanied these names. (005-006)

## Significance

The Egyptian sacrificial system reveals several aspects of their civilization that Herodotus found noteworthy: the regional diversity of religious practice within a unified kingdom, the elaborate ritual purity requirements enforced by a professional priesthood, and the interconnectedness of animal cults with specific deities. The strict prohibitions — such as the Theban abstention from sheep — demonstrate how deeply local religious identity was woven into daily Egyptian life. Herodotus's emphasis on sacrifice as a point of comparison between Egyptian and Greek religion reflects his broader project of tracing Hellenic cultural origins to Egypt.
