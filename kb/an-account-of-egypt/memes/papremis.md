## Overview

Papremis was a city in the western Nile Delta, notable in Herodotus's account for two distinctive features: it was the site of the festival of Ares, celebrated with a unique ritualised mock battle, and it was the only district in Egypt where the hippopotamus was considered a sacred animal. The city later gained historical significance as the site of a battle between the Egyptians under Inaros and the Persian forces during the Egyptian revolt of the mid-fifth century BCE.

## Location / Geography

Papremis lay in the western Delta, within the nome of the same name. Its exact location is uncertain, but Herodotus places it among the Delta cities with distinctive local cults, alongside Sais (sacred to Athene), Buto (sacred to Leto), and Heliopolis. The surrounding district encompassed wetlands and marshland — habitat suitable for the hippopotamus, which the local inhabitants revered (007-002).

## Historical Significance

The principal Herodotean account of Papremis centres on its festival of Ares. According to Herodotus, the Egyptians celebrated this festival with a ritual combat in which two groups of worshippers faced each other in mock battle before the temple. One group, representing the temple's defenders, stood at the entrance armed with clubs, while the other attempted to force entry carrying an image of the god. The Egyptians explained to Herodotus the aetiological story: Ares, raised apart from his mother, returned as a man seeking entry to her chambers, and her servants barred him until he forced his way in (006-002).

## Herodotus's Description

Herodotus describes the Papremite festival in vivid detail. On the eve of the festival, worshippers throughout Egypt who could not travel to Papremis lit lamps in their homes — a custom Herodotus compares to the lamp-lighting at the Sais festival of Athene, with sacred stories explaining both (006-003). The hippopotamus of the Papremite district receives one of Herodotus's most detailed animal descriptions: he carefully catalogues its four-footed stance, cloven hooves, flat nose, horse-like mane and tail, and the extraordinary thickness of its hide (007-002).

## Sources

> The Egyptians hold their solemn assemblies not once in the year but several times, and especially and most zealously at the city of Papremis for Ares. To this city they sail in barges, a great multitude of men and women, and they do as follows.
> (006-002)

> The river-horse is sacred in the district of Papremis, but for the other Egyptians he is not sacred; and this is the appearance which he presents: he is four-footed, cloven-hoofed like an ox, flat-nosed, with a mane like a horse and showing teeth like tusks, with a tail and voice like a horse.
> (007-002)

> Moreover those of the Egyptians who have not come to this solemn assembly observe the night of the festival and themselves also light lamps all of them, and thus not in Sais alone are they lighted, but over all Egypt.
> (006-003)
