## Overview

Egyptian measurements, as documented by Herodotus, comprised a set of standardized units for length, distance, and area. The Greek historian recorded these units while surveying Egyptian monuments, waterways, and territorial boundaries, providing one of the earliest comparative accounts of metrology from the ancient world. His descriptions reveal a sophisticated system based on the human body — cubit, palm, finger — adapted to the monumental scale of Egyptian construction and land administration.

## Description

Herodotus records the cubit as the fundamental unit of length in Egypt, used in construction and river measurement. The Nile's annual flood was gauged by its height in cubits: in the reign of Pheros, the river rose to "a height of eighteen cubits, higher than ever before that time" (010-002). The cubit was subdivided into six palms, and each palm into four fingers, yielding a twenty-four-finger cubit — a system distinct from the Greek equivalents with which Herodotus was familiar.

For larger distances, Egyptians employed the furlong and the schoine (_schoinos_). The schoine was a land-measure used extensively in territorial descriptions: the Ionian reckoning of Egypt's coastline from the Watch-tower of Perseus to Pelusion was "a distance of forty schoines" (002-001). Herodotus equates sixty schoines to three thousand six hundred furlongs when describing the circuit of Lake Moiris, confirming a ratio of sixty furlongs to the schoine (014-005).

The fathom, measuring six feet or four cubits, was used for vertical measurement. Herodotus reports that the pyramids of Cheops were "a hundred fathoms high" (014-006). He also uses the furlong and the day's journey as units for extended routes — notably the canal of Necos, a "voyage of four days" from the Nile to the Erythraian Sea (015-003).

## Land Measurement and the Warrior Class

Herodotus provides concrete instances of Egyptian measurement in administrative contexts. The Egyptian warrior class — the Calasirians and Hermotybians — were each granted twelve yokes of land, free from all imposts, as a special privilege (016-001). Herodotus defines the yoke precisely: it measured "a hundred Egyptian cubits every way," and he notes that the Egyptian cubit was, coincidentally, equal to that of Samos. This grant of twelve square yokes — twelve hundred cubits by one hundred cubits — represented a substantial estate, and when serving as the king's bodyguard for their annual rotation, each warrior received an additional daily allowance: five pounds of bread, two pounds of beef, and four half-pints of wine (016-001). The precision with which Herodotus records these quantities demonstrates both the systematic nature of Egyptian land administration and the historian's attentiveness to metrological detail.

## Measurement and the Nile Flood

Herodotus uses measurement to support his geological argument about the formation of Egypt. The priests of Memphis told him that in the reign of King Moiris, whenever the Nile reached a height of at least eight cubits, it watered all Egypt below Memphis (002-001). By Herodotus's own time — less than nine hundred years after Moiris — the river needed to rise to sixteen cubits, or fifteen at the least, to achieve the same coverage. This silting-up of the land, measured in cubits of Nile rise, was for Herodotus compelling evidence that Egypt was once a gulf of the sea, gradually filled by the river's deposits. He further observed that if the land continued to rise at this rate, the Egyptians of the Delta would one day suffer the same fate they themselves predicted for the Hellenes — famine from lack of water — since the river would no longer be able to overflow fields elevated beyond its reach (002-001).

## Significance

Herodotus's record of Egyptian measurements serves as a bridge between Greek and Egyptian metrological systems. His explicit conversions — four cubits to a fathom, six hundred feet to a furlong — provided his Greek audience with a framework for comprehending the scale of Egyptian achievements. The precision of his reported figures, from the eighteen-cubit flood to the hundred-fathom pyramid, demonstrates the importance the Egyptians themselves attached to measurement, which was integral to their administration of land, taxation, and monumental construction along the Nile.
