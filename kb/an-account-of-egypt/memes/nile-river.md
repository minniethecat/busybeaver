## Overview

The Nile River is the defining geographic feature of Egypt and the central natural subject of Herodotus's *Account of Egypt*. The river's annual flood sustained Egyptian civilization, its silt created the arable land of the Delta, and its mysterious behaviour — rising in summer when all other rivers fell — prompted the historian to conduct one of the earliest documented natural-science inquiries in Western literature. Throughout his seventeen-chapter account, Herodotus returns repeatedly to the Nile: its geological history, its animals, its sources, its mouths, and the life it enabled along its banks.

The Nile passages also reveal Herodotus's distinctive approach to inquiry. He gathered testimony from priests at Memphis, Thebes, and Heliopolis; he critically examined competing theories; and he advanced his own hypothesis based on reason and observable evidence. He also frankly acknowledged the limits of his knowledge: "as to the sources of the Nile, not one either of the Egyptians or of the Libyans or of the Hellenes, who came to speech with me, professed to know anything" (003-005). The one man who claimed certain knowledge — the scribe of the sacred treasury of Athene at Sais — offered a tale of two pointed mountains, Crophi and Mophi, between which fathomless springs fed the river, a story Herodotus found unconvincing (003-005).

## Geographic Course

Herodotus describes the Nile as flowing from the south, cutting Egypt through the midst from the Cataract and the city of [[Elephantine]] to the sea (002-004). Above Elephantine, the river passes through steeply sloping country where vessels must be roped on both sides to prevent them from being carried away by the violence of the stream — a voyage of four days through winding channels resembling the river Maeander (003-008).

Beyond this, the Nile flows past the island of Tachompso, shared by Egyptians and Ethiopians, and into a great lake surrounded by Ethiopian nomad tribes (003-008). After a forty-day overland journey past impassable reefs, the traveller reaches Meroë, the mother-city of Ethiopia. Further south still, the Nile passes through the land of the Deserters (*Asmach*) — Egyptian warriors who revolted and joined the Ethiopians (003-008).

Above all, the Nile is the boundary between Asia and Libya (Africa) in Greek geography. Herodotus contests the Ionian view that the Delta alone constituted Egypt, arguing instead that all land watered by the Nile and inhabited by those who drink from it is Egypt — a definition confirmed, he notes, by the [[Oracle of Ammon]] (002-004).

## The Annual Flood

The [[Nile Flood]] was the lifeblood of Egyptian agriculture. Unlike all other rivers, the Nile began to rise at the summer solstice and increased for a hundred days, then receded through the winter (003-001). During the inundation, the cities alone rose above the water, resembling islands in the Aegean Sea, while the rest of Egypt became a sea. Navigation changed entirely: travellers sailed across the open plain rather than along the usual river channels (009-003).

The flood enabled a uniquely effortless form of agriculture. The Egyptians of the Delta neither ploughed nor hoed; instead, they waited for the river to rise, water the fields, and recede, then sowed their seed and drove swine over it to tread it into the ground. The same swine later threshed the grain (002-001). Herodotus noted that these Egyptians gathered in their crops with less labour than any other people, and remarked that they were the healthiest of all men next to the Libyans.

The sufficiency of the flood was measured in cubits. In the reign of King Moiris, eight cubits sufficed to water Egypt below Memphis; by Herodotus's time, the river required fifteen or sixteen cubits — a rising threshold that Herodotus recognized as a sign of long-term environmental change from silt accumulation (002-001).

### Theories of the Flood

Herodotus pressed the Egyptian priests for an explanation of the Nile's unusual behaviour but received no satisfactory answer (003-001). He then examined and rejected three Greek theories through reasoned argument:

1. **The Etesian Wind Theory**: that seasonal north winds prevented the Nile from flowing into the sea. Herodotus countered that the Nile continued to rise even when these winds failed, and that other rivers flowing against such winds were not similarly affected (003-001).

2. **The Ocean Theory**: that the Nile flowed from the great river Ocean encircling the earth. Herodotus dismissed this as an unverifiable tale — he knew of no river Ocean, and suggested that Homer or an earlier poet had invented the name (003-002).

3. **The Melting Snow Theory**: that the Nile was fed by melting snow in distant mountains. Herodotus refuted this with multiple observations: the region was the hottest on earth, its inhabitants were black-skinned from burning heat, swallows and kites lived there year-round, and cranes wintered there — all evidence that snow could not exist at the Nile's source (003-002).

Herodotus offered his own heliocentric theory: the Sun, driven by winter storms, passed over the upper parts of Libya during winter, drawing up water from the Nile by evaporation. When the Sun returned north in summer, the Nile resumed its full volume (003-003). While incorrect, this theory demonstrated Herodotus's method of reasoning from observable natural phenomena rather than mythology.

## The Nile Delta

The [[Nile Delta]] is the great triangular alluvial plain at the river's Mediterranean terminus, formed over millennia by the river's deposits of rich silt. Herodotus devoted extensive attention to the Delta, which he understood as land gradually won from the sea: shells were found on the mountains, salt efflorescence formed on the surface (even eating away at the pyramids), and the black, easily crumbling soil of Egypt was, as he described it, "mud and silt brought down from Ethiopia by the river" (002-001).

Herodotus engaged extensively with the dispute over the Delta's boundaries. The Ionians claimed that the Delta alone was Egypt, but Herodotus — supported by the priests and the [[Oracle of Ammon]] — argued that all land watered by the Nile was Egyptian territory. He marshalled geological evidence to show that the Delta was a relatively recent formation, and that its silt accumulation was gradually raising the land surface beyond the river's reach — a slow-motion environmental crisis that he predicted would one day bring famine to the Delta's inhabitants (002-001).

### The Seven Mouths

At its terminus, the Nile divided into [[Nile Mouths|seven mouths]], of which Herodotus recorded five as navigable: the Pelusiac, Mendesian, Sebennytic, Saïtic, and Canobic (002-003). The two non-navigable mouths were the Bolbitine and Bucolic. This enumeration corrected the Ionian tradition, which claimed only three mouths existed.

Each mouth was associated with a city or region, and the mouths served as the principal gateways for trade, travel, and invasion between Egypt and the Mediterranean world. The Canobic mouth, the westernmost navigable channel, was the primary entry point for Greek travellers — including, probably, Herodotus himself.

## Canal Network

The Nile's waters were distributed through an extensive canal network, begun under [[Sesostris]] and expanded under later kings. Sesostris's canal-building programme was simultaneously defensive and economic: by cutting canals across the Delta, he rendered the chariot unusable while facilitating irrigation and transport (010-002). Later, [[Necos]] attempted to extend this network by digging a canal from the Nile near [[Bubastis]] through the [[Wadi Tumilat]] to the Erythraian Sea — a project completed only under the Persian king [[Dareios]] (015-004).

The [[Lake Moeris]] basin, connected to the Nile by an artificial channel, was another major hydraulic work. Constructed under King Moiris, it served as a reservoir that supplemented the Nile's irrigation waters during low floods, and was considered one of the greatest engineering achievements of ancient Egypt.

## The Nile in Egyptian Life

The Nile held profound religious significance for the Egyptians. The priests of the Nile were a distinct class who handled the burial of anyone — Egyptian or stranger — whose death was caused by a crocodile or the river itself. Such victims were considered "something more than man" and were embalmed and buried in sacred burial-places, with no relative or friend permitted to touch the body (008-002). The priests of the Nile alone handled the corpse and conducted the burial rites.

The Egyptians also regarded the Nile as the boundary marker of their nation. The [[Oracle of Ammon]] declared that "that land was Egypt where the Nile came over and watered, and that those were Egyptians who dwelling below the city of Elephantine drank of that river" (002-004).

The river teemed with life. Herodotus devotes considerable attention to the animals of the Nile, describing crocodiles, hippopotamuses, otters, and various fish species. The crocodile — an animal that "having done no harm becomes a prey and having done harm is sacred" — received particular attention (008-004). The river teemed with fish, especially during the flood, when the hollow places and depressions along the banks filled with water and "at once they are all filled with little fishes" hatched from eggs laid in the mud during the previous year's receding waters (008-006).
