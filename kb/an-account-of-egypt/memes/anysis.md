## Overview

[[Anysis]] was an [[egypt|Egypt]]ian king who ruled from a city bearing his name in the [[Nile Delta]]. He appears in [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s *Account of [[egypt|Egypt]]* as a figure of refuge and survival: when the [[ethiopia-region|Ethiopia]]n king [[Sabacos]] invaded [[egypt|Egypt]], the blind Anysis fled into the [[nile-delta|Delta]] fen-country, where he lived in hiding on an island for fifty years while [[sabacos|Sabacos]] ruled. His story, brief but vivid, is interwoven with the dream-vision that drove [[sabacos|Sabacos]] from [[egypt|Egypt]] and the eventual restoration of native [[egypt|Egypt]]ian rule.

## The Flight into the Fens

Anysis was king of [[egypt|Egypt]] when [[sabacos|Sabacos]] the [[ethiopia-region|Ethiopia]]n invaded the country. Rather than fight, Anysis fled into the marshy fen-country of the [[nile-delta|Delta]] — a region of islands and waterways accessible only by boat — and there remained concealed. The fens, formed by [[nile-river|the Nile]]'s distributaries and the [[nile-delta|Delta]]'s low-lying terrain, provided natural refuge: the same canal network that [[Sesostris]] had built to render chariots unusable now served to shelter a fugitive king from a foreign conqueror.

## The Island and the Ashes

During his fifty-year concealment, Anysis lived on an island that the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians kept supplied with provisions. However, [[herodotus|Herodotus]] records a curious detail: Anysis ordered the people of the fen-country to bring him ashes mixed with their provisions, a request they fulfilled without understanding its purpose. When [[sabacos|Sabacos]] eventually departed from [[egypt|Egypt]] — driven out by a dream-vision in which a man stood over him counselling him to cut all the [[egyptian-priesthood|Egyptian priests]] in half — Anysis emerged from hiding and discovered that the ashes had, over the decades, formed a substantial mound on his island. This mound, [[herodotus|Herodotus]] notes, was still visible in his own time as a landmark in the [[nile-delta|Delta]] fen-country (013-006).

## Sabacos and the Return

The [[ethiopia-region|Ethiopia]]n king [[sabacos|Sabacos]] ruled [[egypt|Egypt]] for fifty years — the entire duration of Anysis's concealment. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] records two notable features of [[sabacos|Sabacos]]'s reign: his policy of commuting capital punishment to forced labour on public works (a reform that raised the embankments of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian cities still higher against [[nile-river|the Nile]] flood), and his eventual departure in response to the oracular dream that warned him he must "cut the priests in half" to avoid disaster — a command [[sabacos|Sabacos]] interpreted as a test of divine favour and a sign that the gods desired his withdrawal from [[egypt|Egypt]] (013-004).

After [[sabacos|Sabacos]]'s voluntary departure, Anysis returned from the fens and resumed his rule. His survival and restoration represent one of the few instances in [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s [[egypt|Egypt]]ian royal narratives of a king who outlasted a foreign conqueror through concealment rather than military resistance.

## The City of Anysis

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] mentions that Anysis gave his name to the city from which he ruled — presumably located in the [[nile-delta|Delta]], close to the fen-country that provided his refuge. The city of Anysis is not otherwise prominent in [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s narrative, but its identification with a blind king adds a layer of personal geography to the historian's mapping of the [[nile-delta|Delta]] region.

## Significance

Anysis's story illuminates the geography of refuge in the [[nile-delta|Delta]] — the same marshlands that produced the papyrus and lotus harvested by the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians also served as a sanctuary for hunted kings. The motif of the fifty-year exile, the mysterious request for ashes, and the landmark mound that remained visible centuries later all reflect [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s interest in the physical traces that past events leave upon the landscape. In the broader structure of the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian royal narrative, Anysis represents the period of [[ethiopia-region|Ethiopia]]n domination and the resilience — if passive — of native [[egypt|Egypt]]ian kingship.
