---## Overview

The dietary laws of ancient [[egypt|Egypt]], as documented by [[herodotus|Herodotus]] in the 5th century BCE, constituted a complex system of food prohibitions, priestly privileges, regional variations, and ritual observances that governed what the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians ate, how they prepared it, and with whom they could share it. Unlike the later dietary codes of the Hebrews, [[egypt|Egypt]]ian food laws were inseparable from their religious system: animals were sacred or abominable according to local cults, the priesthood enjoyed exclusive dietary privileges while observing its own restrictions, and the daily diet of ordinary [[egypt|Egypt]]ians reflected both the agricultural abundance of [[nile-river|the Nile]] Valley and a deep concern for bodily purity. (004-005)

## Description

### Priestly Diet and Privileges

The [[egyptian-priesthood|Egyptian priests]] occupied a uniquely privileged position in the food economy. They "do not consume or spend anything of their own substance, but there is sacred bread baked for them and they have each great quantity of flesh of oxen and geese coming in to them each day, and also wine of grapes is given to them." (004-005) This daily allocation of meat, bread, and wine — drawn from temple stores supplied by offerings — sustained a hereditary priestly class in comfort. Yet their privileges were accompanied by restrictions: it was "not permitted to them to taste of fish." (004-005)

The priests' dietary restrictions extended beyond fish. Beans were regarded as "an unclean kind of pulse" (004-005); the priests did not even endure to look upon them, and [[egypt|Egypt]]ians as a whole "do not at all sow them in their land, and those which they grow they neither eat raw nor boil for food." (004-005) This bean prohibition created a boundary between pure and impure food that paralleled the animal codes described elsewhere.

### Food and Social Hierarchy

The dietary laws of [[egypt|Egypt]] were inseparable from its class structure. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] identifies seven hereditary classes — priests, warriors, cowherds, swineherds, shopkeepers, interpreters, and boatmen — each distinguished by occupation and, implicitly, by relationship to food production and consumption. (015-011) The swineherds, as already noted, were ritually and socially degraded by their association with the unclean pig. The cowherds, by contrast, tended the most revered of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian animals and likely shared in the prestige attached to cattle.

The strict separation of Greek and [[egypt|Egypt]]ian foodways reveals another layer of dietary law. The [[egypt|Egypt]]ians would not kiss a Greek on the mouth, "nor will they use a knife or roasting-spits or a caldron belonging to a Hellene, nor taste the flesh even of a clean animal if it has been cut with the knife of a Hellene." (004-006) Food prepared by Greek hands or with Greek implements was ritually contaminated, regardless of the animal's own purity. This dietary xenophobia reinforced the boundary between [[egypt|Egypt]]ian and foreigner in the most intimate and daily of practices.

### The Abominable Pig

Among all animals, the pig was "accounted by the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians an abominable animal." (005-005) The pollution it carried was contagious: if an [[egypt|Egypt]]ian in passing touched a pig, "he goes into the river and dips himself forthwith in the water together with his garments." (005-005) The ritual pollution extended to the social sphere — swineherds, though native [[egypt|Egypt]]ians, "do not enter any of the temples in [[egypt|Egypt]], nor is anyone willing to give his daughter in marriage to one of them or to take a wife from among them; but the swineherds both give in marriage to one another and take from one another." (005-005) This social exclusion was so absolute that swineherds formed a distinct endogamous class, listed by [[herodotus|Herodotus]] among the seven hereditary classes of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian society. (015-011)

Yet the pig was not universally forbidden. To the Moon and to Dionysos alone, and "at the same time and on the same full-moon," the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians sacrificed swine and ate their flesh — though on any day after this they would not taste of it. (005-005) [[herodotus|Herodotus]] knew the sacred story explaining this anomaly but refused to relate it, deeming it "not a seemly one for me to tell." (005-005) The poor, unable to afford a live pig, "shape pigs of dough and having baked them they offer these as a sacrifice." (005-005)

### Cattle: Sacred and Sacrificial

The ox held a dual status in the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian dietary system. Male oxen, once tested for ritual purity — a single black hair rendered the beast "not clean for sacrifice" (004-006) — could be offered to the gods and their flesh consumed. The sacrificial procedure involved pouring wine over the victim, cutting its throat, and severing the head. Critically, no [[egypt|Egypt]]ian ate the head of any animal, for upon the severed head the priests uttered imprecations, "praying that if any evil be about to befall either themselves who are offering sacrifice or the land of [[egypt|Egypt]] in general, it may come rather upon this head." (004-006) The heads were sold in the marketplace where [[hellenes|Hellenes]] were present, or cast into the river where they were not.

Cows, however, were categorically excluded from sacrifice and consumption. Sacred to [[isis|Isis]], who was "in the form of a woman with cow's horns," cows were "revered far more than any other kind of cattle" by all [[egypt|Egypt]]ians without distinction. (004-006) This reverence extended to a prohibition on eating cattle that had died naturally: females were cast into the river, while males were buried with their horns protruding to mark the spot. After decomposition, boats from the island of Prosopitis collected the bones for communal burial at the city of Atarbechis. (004-006) The [[egypt|Egypt]]ians had "the same law laid down" for all their cattle, "and these also they abstain from killing." (004-006)

### Regional Divisions: Goats and Sheep

[[egypt|Egypt]]ian dietary practice was not uniform across the land. The Thebans and those of the Theban district "sacrifice goats and abstain from sheep," while the [[mendes|Mendesian]]s "abstain from goats and sacrifice sheep." (005-001) Both practices were grounded in local theology: the Thebans revered the ram as sacred to [[ammon|Zeus-Ammon]], covering the divine image with a ram's fleece on the annual feast, while the [[mendes|Mendesian]]s venerated Pan — called [[mendes|Mendes]] in the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian tongue — whom they represented with the face and legs of a goat. Only [[isis-and-osiris|Isis and Osiris]] were universally worshipped by all [[egypt|Egypt]]ians without regional variation. (005-001)

The [[mendes|Mendesian]]s' veneration of goats was particularly intense: "all goats" were reverenced, males more than females, and one he-goat especially was singled out. When this goat died, "there is great mourning in all the [[mendes|Mendesian district]]." (005-004)

### The Daily Diet of Ordinary Egyptians

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] provides a detailed summary of the common [[egypt|Egypt]]ian diet: "they eat bread, making loaves of maize, which they call _kyllestis_, and they use habitually a wine made out of barley, for vines they have not in their land." (007-005) The absence of grapevines meant that barley beer was the universal beverage, consumed at every level of society.

Fish, abundant in [[nile-river|the Nile]] and its lakes, formed a staple protein source. The [[egypt|Egypt]]ians dried some fish in the sun and ate them without cooking; others they preserved in brine. Of birds, they ate quails, ducks, and small birds without cooking after first curing them. "Everything else which they have belonging to the class of birds or fishes, except such as have been set apart by them as sacred, they eat roasted or boiled." (007-005)

The [[egypt|Egypt]]ians also pursued internal dietary discipline. For three successive days each month, they purged themselves "hunting after health with emetics and clysters," believing that "all the diseases which exist are produced in men by the food on which they live." (007-005) This practice was embedded in a broader conviction that the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians were "the most healthy of all men next after the Libyans" — a condition [[herodotus|Herodotus]] attributes to the unchanging seasons, but which the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians connected to their dietary regimen. (007-005)

### Fen-Dwellers' Economy of Food

The inhabitants of [[nile-river|the Nile]] marshlands, though sharing the general customs of other [[egypt|Egypt]]ians, had developed a distinctive food economy adapted to their environment. When the river flooded the plains, great numbers of water-lilies, called _lotos_, grew in the shallows. The fen-dwellers cut these with a sickle, dried them in the sun, and pounded the seed-vessel — "which is like the head of a poppy" — into flour, from which they baked loaves. (008-005) The lotus root, "round in shape and about the size of an apple," was also edible and had "a rather sweet taste." (008-005)

In addition to the lotus, the marsh-dwellers harvested the papyrus plant: "the upper parts of it they cut off and turn to other uses, but that which is left below for about a cubit in length they eat or sell; and those who desire to have the papyrus at its very best bake it in an oven heated red-hot, and then eat it." (008-005) A second variety of lily produced edible seeds "of the size of an olive-stone" that grew in a vessel resembling a wasp's comb; these were "eaten either fresh or dried." (008-005) Some marsh-dwellers subsisted "on fish alone, which they dry in the sun after having caught them and taken out the entrails, and then when they are dry, they use them for food." (008-005)

## Historical Context

The dietary laws [[herodotus|Herodotus]] describes reflect the institutionalized temple culture of Late Period [[egypt|Egypt]]. By the 5th century BCE, [[egypt|Egypt]] was under [[persian|Persian]] rule following Cambyses's conquest in 525 BCE, yet the temples at [[memphis|Memphis]], [[thebes|Thebes]], and [[heliopolis|Heliopolis]] continued to function as centers of traditional religious life, maintaining dietary regulations that had evolved over millennia. The hereditary priesthood — whose post passed from father to son (004-005) — preserved detailed knowledge of which animals were pure, which were abominable, and how each should be approached.

[[egypt|Egypt]]'s agricultural abundance, dependent on the annual [[nile-river|Nile]] flood, provided the material foundation for these dietary laws. Bread from maize, barley beer, fish from the river and lakes, and the flesh of ritually acceptable animals sustained a population that [[herodotus|Herodotus]] estimated at twenty thousand inhabited towns in the reign of [[amasis|Amasis]]. (016-004) The dietary restrictions — the prohibition on beans, the exclusion of fish from the priestly diet, the regional divisions over goats and sheep — were not practical adaptations to scarcity but expressions of religious and social identity.

## Herodotus's Commentary

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] approaches [[egypt|Egypt]]ian dietary laws with his characteristic blend of ethnographic reportage and comparative analysis. He frames the dietary code within his broader portrait of the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians as "religious excessively beyond all other men" (004-005) and "excessively careful in their observances." (006-005) The elaborate food prohibitions, for [[herodotus|Herodotus]], are evidence of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian piety rather than irrational superstition.

He is not, however, entirely credulous. When recording the story explaining why swine are sacrificed to the Moon despite being abominated at all other feasts, he notes that he knows the story but finds it "not a seemly one for me to tell." (005-005) This reticence preserves [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s persona as a respectable Greek narrator even as he acknowledges the existence of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian sacred narratives that would offend Greek sensibilities. His detailed account of the dietary practices of the fen-dwellers, with their lotus loaves, papyrus stalks, and sun-dried fish, reads as a prototype of ethnographic fieldwork — attentive to the material ingenuity of people adapting to their environment.

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] also uses [[egypt|Egypt]]ian food practices as evidence for historical arguments about cultural transmission. The prohibition on wool in temples and burial, which applied equally to [[egyptian-priesthood|Egyptian priests]] and to initiates of the Orphic, Bacchic, and Pythagorean mysteries, is one of several instances where [[herodotus|Herodotus]] claims that Greek religious knowledge ultimately derived from the older and more venerable [[egypt|Egypt]]ian tradition. (007-005)

## Significance

The dietary laws of ancient [[egypt|Egypt]], as recorded by [[herodotus|Herodotus]], illuminate the intersection of religion, social structure, and daily life in [[nile-river|the Nile]] Valley. Food was never merely sustenance: every meal was framed by ritual prohibitions that marked boundaries between pure and impure, sacred and profane, [[egypt|Egypt]]ian and foreigner, priest and layman, one region's cult and another's. The pig's anomalous status — abominated yet ritually consumed on exactly two days of the year — exemplifies the complexity of a system that modern readers might otherwise reduce to a simple list of permitted and forbidden foods.

[[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s account also preserves evidence of a food culture that has largely vanished from the archaeological record: the baking of kyllestis loaves from maize, the brewing of barley beer in a land without vines, the lotus-economy of the marsh-dwellers, and the communal feasting on sacrificial flesh after the burning of the divine portion. His eye for regional variation — the Thebans abstaining from sheep, the [[mendes|Mendesian]]s from goats, the priests from fish — captures a religious landscape far more decentralized than the monumental uniformity of pyramids and temples might suggest.
