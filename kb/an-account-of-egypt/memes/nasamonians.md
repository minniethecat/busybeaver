## Overview

The Nasamonians were a Libyan tribe dwelling in the region of the Syrtis (modern Gulf of Sidra) and eastward along the North African coast. Herodotus mentions them briefly but memorably in his "Account of Egypt" through a second-hand account: their king Etearchos of the Ammonians told visitors from Cyrene how a group of young Nasamonian men undertook a remarkable expedition across the Sahara desert, reaching a great river that was conjectured to be the distant Nile (051). This story, transmitted through multiple intermediaries, represents one of the earliest recorded accounts of trans-Saharan exploration in classical literature.

## The Desert Expedition

According to the account Herodotus received from men of Cyrene who had visited the Oracle of Ammon, Etearchos king of the Ammonians told them that certain Nasamonians once came to him. When asked about the unknown interior of Libya, they related that sons of their chief men, being of unruly disposition, had drawn lots and selected five of their number "to go to see the desert parts of Libya and to try whether they could discover more than those who had previously explored furthest" (051). Well-supplied with water and provisions, the young men journeyed first through inhabited country, then through a land of wild beasts, and finally across a "terribly waterless and utterly desert" expanse of sand. After many days they reached a level place with fruit-bearing trees, where they were seized by small black-skinned men who carried them through great swamps to a city beside a great river flowing from west to east, containing crocodiles.

## Significance in Herodotus's Account

The Nasamonian expedition is embedded within Herodotus's broader inquiry into the sources of the Nile, a mystery that he pursued diligently throughout his travels in Egypt. Etearchos conjectured that the river the Nasamonians described was the Nile, and Herodotus concurs, reasoning that the Nile flows from Libya, cuts through the continent, and must originate at a distance from its mouth comparable to that of the Danube (Ister) from its own source (051). This conjectural geography, though incorrect, illustrates the interconnected nature of ancient knowledge networks: Libyan tribesmen, an Ammonian king, Greek colonists at Cyrene, and a Greek historian from Halicarnassus all contributing to a chain of information about Africa's interior.

## Geographic Context

The Nasamonians inhabited the coastal region of the Syrtis, in what is now northern Libya. Herodotus places them among the many Libyan races extending along the northern coast of Africa from Egypt to the headland of Soloeis (Cape Spartel in modern Morocco), distinguishing the inhabited coastal belt from the interior, which he describes as progressively wild beasts, then sand desert (051). The Nasamonians' proximity to Cyrene and the Oracle of Ammon placed them within the sphere of Greek and Egyptian knowledge networks.

## Sources

> Etearchos said that once there came to him men of the Nasamonians (this is a Libyan race which dwells in the Syrtis, and also in the land to the East of the Syrtis reaching to no great distance), and when the Nasamonians came and were asked by him whether they were able to tell him anything more than he knew about the desert parts of Libya, they said that there had been among them certain sons of chief men, who were of unruly disposition.
> (051)

> These young men then (said they), being sent out by their companions well furnished with supplies of water and provisions, went first through the inhabited country, and after they had passed through this they came to the country of wild beasts, and after this they passed through the desert, making their journey towards the West Wind; and having passed through a great tract of sand in many days, they saw at last trees growing in a level place.
> (051)

> Of the account given by Etearchos the Ammonian let so much suffice as is here said, except that, as the men of Kyrene told me, he alleged that the Nasamonians returned safe home, and that the people to whom they had come were all wizards.
> (051)
