## Overview

Marea was an Egyptian city situated in the western Delta on the border with Libya. Herodotus records it as one of three frontier garrison posts established by Psammetichos, guarding the approach from Libya. Its inhabitants, dwelling on the margins of Egypt, developed an ambiguous identity — considering themselves Libyans rather than Egyptians and resenting the dietary restrictions of Egyptian religious practice. (003-008) (002-004)

## Strategic Role

Psammetichos established Marea as the western anchor of Egypt's defensive perimeter. Alongside Elephantine in the south (against the Ethiopians) and Daphnai in the east (against Arabia and Assyria), Marea protected the Libyan frontier. Herodotus notes that the Persian administration maintained these same garrison positions in his own day, confirming the enduring strategic value of these sites. (003-008)

## Identity and Religion

The inhabitants of Marea and the neighboring city of Apis presented an interesting case of borderland identity in Herodotus's account. Though dwelling within Egyptian territory, these people considered themselves Libyans rather than Egyptians. Their primary grievance was religious: they resented the Egyptian prohibition on eating the flesh of cows. This dietary restriction, central to Egyptian cult practice, weighed heavily on a frontier community with closer cultural ties to Libya. (002-004)

Unwilling to accept this religious burden, the Mareians appealed to the oracle of Ammon for an exemption. They argued that since their land was not part of Egypt proper — the Delta being below the oracular boundary — they should not be bound by Egyptian religious law. The oracle, however, refused their petition, reaffirming that all the land watered by the Nile belonged to Egypt and was subject to its customs. (002-004)

## Herodotus's Use of Marea

Herodotus employs the Mareian appeal to the oracle of Ammon as evidence for his geological theory about Egypt. The oracle's ruling established that "Egypt is all that which the Nile comes over and waters," a definition the historian then extends to argue that Egyptian territory — and its inhabitants — include the Delta region fully. The Mareian controversy thus serves both as an ethnographic case study and as a building block in Herodotus's natural-philosophical argument about Egypt's boundaries. (002-004)

## Sources

> (002-004)
