## Overview

Herodotus's Egyptian narrative contains significant information about the military organization of Egypt during the Saite period (seventh–sixth centuries BCE). While his account is focused on the customs and history of Egypt rather than its military campaigns, several passages illuminate key aspects of the Egyptian armed forces: the hereditary warrior class, the growing reliance on Greek and Carian mercenaries, and the political consequences of military reform. (013-008)

## The Warrior Class

Herodotus identifies a distinct warrior class (machimoi) among the Egyptians, one of seven hereditary occupational classes into which Egyptian society was divided. The warriors, like the priests, held special privileges. Each warrior was granted twelve yokes (arourae) of corn-land, free of tax — a grant that had been maintained under former kings. These land grants supported the warrior and his family, freeing him from agricultural labour so that he could devote himself to military service. (013-008)

The machimoi were not, however, a unified body under a single command. They were divided into two groups, the Calasirians and the Hermotybians, each associated with specific nomes and each with distinct numerical strength. The Hermotybians, who came from the Delta and its vicinity, numbered up to a hundred and sixty thousand men at their peak. The Calasirians, drawn from the Thebaid and Upper Egypt, numbered up to two hundred and fifty thousand. Together, they constituted a formidable standing force — at least in tradition, if not in the reduced circumstances of Herodotus's own day. (013-008)

## The Erosion of the Warrior Class

By the reign of Psammetichos, the warrior class had lost much of its former standing. A previous king, whom Herodotus calls Asychis, had neglected and held in no regard the warrior class, considering that he would have no need of them. He even took from them their traditional yokes of corn-land. This erosion of privilege bred resentment, and when Psammetichos later ascended the throne and also favoured foreign mercenaries over Egyptian troops, a large body of the machimoi — two hundred and forty thousand men — deserted and marched south into Ethiopia, where they settled and served the Ethiopian king. This mass defection represents one of the most dramatic social ruptures in Herodotus's Egyptian history. (013-008) (014-008)

## Mercenaries and the Army of Amasis

The vacuum left by the departing machimoi was filled by foreign mercenaries. Psammetichos, after being driven into the fen-country by the eleven kings, was reinforced by Ionian and Carian soldiers of fortune who landed on the Egyptian coast in bronze armour — the "men of bronze" foretold by an oracle. With these mercenaries, Psammetichos defeated the eleven kings at the battle of Momemphis and established himself as sole ruler. In gratitude, he granted the mercenaries lands in the Delta, at the Pelusian mouth of the Nile, where they were settled as a permanent military colony. (014-008)

Amasis, the last great king of the Saite dynasty, relied heavily on his foreign troops. When the Egyptians mocked his humble origins, Herodotus records that Amasis had about him Carian and Ionian mercenaries numbering thirty thousand, whom he used as his personal bodyguard. The final confrontation between Amasis and his predecessor Apries was a clash of military bases: Apries leading his foreign mercenaries, and Amasis at the head of the whole body of the Egyptians. Amasis prevailed, and the reliance on mercenaries continued under his rule. (015-010) (016-002)

## Ships of War

Herodotus also records Egyptian naval capability. Sesostris, he reports, was the first to set out with ships of war from the Arabian Gulf (the Red Sea) and subdue the peoples dwelling along the shores of the Erythraian Sea, reaching as far as a region where the sea was no longer navigable because of shoals. This expedition, if historical, would represent an early Egyptian naval enterprise into the Indian Ocean — though modern scholars generally treat the Sesostris narratives as a composite of several pharaohs' achievements. (009-006)

## Significance

The Egyptian military as depicted by Herodotus was in transition from a traditional, land-based, hereditary warrior system to a hybrid force dependent on foreign mercenaries. The tension between the native machimoi and the foreign troops was not merely a military matter but a political fault line that shaped the rise and fall of the Saite kings. The desertion of the machimoi weakened Egypt's native military capacity; the mercenaries who replaced them brought technical skill and political loyalty to individual kings, but their presence deepened the divide between the Egyptian people and their rulers.
