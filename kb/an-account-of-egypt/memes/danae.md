## Herodotus's Account

Danae appears in Herodotus's narrative through her connection to the city of Chemmis in the Theban district of Egypt, where a temple of her son Perseus was located.

### The Temple of Perseus at Chemmis

Herodotus describes the city of Chemmis, near Neapolis in the Theban district, as a place where the inhabitants do not follow Egyptian customs alone [002-109]. In this city stands a temple of Perseus, the son of Danae, "which is of a square shape, and round it grow date-palms: the gateway of the temple is built of stone and of very great size, and at the entrance of it stand two great statues of stone."

### The Cult of Perseus

The people of Chemmis told Herodotus that Perseus was wont to appear to them in their land, and that they honored him with Greek-style athletic games, including every kind of contest, offering cattle and cloaks and skins as prizes. When Herodotus inquired why Perseus appeared only to them and why they differed from other Egyptians in their customs, they answered that Perseus belonged to their city by descent — for Danaos and Lynkeus were men of Chemmis who had sailed to Hellas.

## Mythological Context

In Greek mythology, Danae was the daughter of Acrisius, king of Argos. An oracle foretold that her son would kill his grandfather, so Acrisius imprisoned Danae in a bronze chamber. Zeus visited her in the form of a golden shower, and she gave birth to Perseus.

## Connection to Egypt

The Chemmite claim of descent from Danaos connects Danae's lineage to Egypt through the Danaid tradition, which holds that the daughters of Danaos brought important religious rites — including the mysteries of Demeter — from Egypt to Greece.
