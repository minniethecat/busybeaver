## Overview

Helen is a figure of Greek legend whose story occupies a significant digression in [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s account of Egypt. According to the [[egyptian-priesthood|Egyptian priests]] at Memphis, after [[alexander-paris|Alexander (Paris)]] abducted her from Sparta, contrary winds drove his ship to the coast of [[egypt|Egypt]] rather than directly to Troy. Helen was detained there by King [[proteus|Proteus]] of Memphis, who kept her and the stolen wealth safe until [[menelaos|Menelaos]] could retrieve her after the fall of Troy. Herodotus endorses this Egyptian version of the story and uses it to argue that Homer knew the true account but chose a different version for his epic. (010-004)(010-005)(011-004)

## Background / Biography

Helen was the daughter of Tyndareus and the wife of [[menelaos|Menelaos]], the king of Sparta. (010-004) In the conventional Greek story, her abduction by [[alexander-paris|Paris]] (called Alexander by Herodotus) triggered the Trojan War. However, Herodotus records a markedly different tradition learned from the priests of Egypt. (010-005)

The Egyptian priests told Herodotus that when Alexander carried Helen away from Sparta, he did not reach Troy directly. Instead, contrary winds on the Aegean Sea drove him to the coast of Egypt, where he landed at the Canobic mouth of the Nile. (010-005) There, his own attendants deserted him and sought sanctuary at a nearby temple of Heracles, denouncing their master to the temple priests and to [[thonis|Thonis]], the warden of the river-mouth, and revealing the full story of his impious abduction of his host's wife. (010-005)

The priests further stated that the temple within the sacred enclosure of [[proteus|Proteus]] at Memphis, known as the temple of the "foreign Aphrodite," was in truth dedicated to Helen. Herodotus reports this as his own conjecture, noting that he had heard the story of Helen's stay in Egypt and found the temple's dedication consistent with it. (010-004)

## Role in the Account

Helen appears in [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s account not as an active agent but as the central figure in a lengthy digression on the credibility of the Homeric tradition concerning the Trojan War. After being brought before [[proteus|Proteus]], Helen was taken under the king's protection. Proteus refused to execute Alexander—citing his principle of sparing strangers driven to his land by winds—but confiscated Helen and all the stolen treasure, keeping them safe for [[menelaos|Menelaos]]. He then banished Alexander and his crew from Egypt within three days. (010-005)

When the Greek army later besieged Troy and demanded Helen's return, the Trojans truthfully replied that she and the wealth were in Egypt. The Greeks, believing themselves mocked, persisted until they had taken the city. Finding no Helen there, they finally believed the account and dispatched [[menelaos|Menelaos]] to Egypt to retrieve her. (011-004) Menelaos sailed up the Nile to Memphis, where he was received hospitably by [[proteus|Proteus]], recovered Helen unharmed along with his wealth, and was given great entertainment. (011-004)

## Herodotus's Portrayal

Herodotus treats the Egyptian version of Helen's story as the historical truth and uses it to critique the Homeric tradition. He acknowledges that Homer knew the Egyptian account—citing lines from the Iliad that reference Alexander's wanderings to [[sidon|Sidon]]—but argues that Homer chose the alternate version because it was more suitable to epic composition. (010-006)(011-003)

Herodotus expressly aligns himself with the Egyptian account, adding the rational consideration that if Helen had truly been in Troy, the Trojans—and especially [[priam|Priam]], who was neither mad nor indifferent to his family's welfare—would have surrendered her to avert destruction. (011-005) He concludes that the Greeks did not believe the Trojans because divine power was purposing their utter destruction, making it manifest that great wrongs incur great chastisements. (011-006)
