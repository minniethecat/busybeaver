## Overview

The Gulf of Suez is the northwestern arm of the Red Sea, forming the western boundary of the Sinai Peninsula. In Herodotus's account it appears as part of the "Arabian Gulf" (Ἀράβιος κόλπος) and as the destination of the channel that king Necos attempted to construct from the Nile to the Erythraian Sea. The gulf was a strategic waterway connecting Egypt to the incense routes of Arabia and beyond.

## Location / Geography

Herodotus describes the Arabian Gulf as the body of water into which the Nile's easternmost branches might have once flowed, noting that the priests claimed the land between the Nile and the gulf was alluvial deposit (002-001). The gulf extends from the Strait of Bab-el-Mandeb in the south to the Isthmus of Suez in the north. In Herodotus's time the head of the gulf lay close to the Bitter Lakes region, where the city of Patumos (later Pithom) stood at the canal's entrance.

## Historical Significance

The gulf's primary significance in Herodotus's narrative is tied to the canal-building efforts of the Egyptian kings. Necos (Necho II) was the first to attempt a channel from the Nile to the Erythraian Sea, a project later completed by the Persian king Dareios (015-004). The priests also recorded that Sesostris, a much earlier king, was the first to launch warships from the Arabian Gulf to subdue the coastal peoples (009-006). The gulf thus served as Egypt's maritime gateway to the east, predating the Mediterranean-facing ports of the Delta.

## Herodotus's Description

Herodotus notes that the canal built by Necos was wide enough for two triremes to row abreast, and that it took a course through the Wadi Tumilat to reach the sea near the Arabian Gulf. Although Necos did not live to see it completed, the canal remained a celebrated engineering feat. Herodotus also mentions that Dareios's continuation of the canal was one of the Persian king's notable works in Egypt, alongside his reorganisation of the tribute system.
