## Overview

The crocodile occupies a unique position in Herodotus's account of Egypt — it is both an object of detailed natural-historical observation and a creature of profound religious significance. Herodotus devotes a substantial passage to its physical characteristics and habits, noting that it grows to a greater size than any other creature known to him. At the same time, the crocodile was venerated in certain regions of Egypt, particularly at [[Crocodilopolis]] (the City of Crocodiles), where it was kept and adorned as a sacred animal, while in other regions it was hunted and eaten. (007-001)(014-005)

## Natural History

Herodotus provides one of the earliest systematic descriptions of the crocodile in Western literature. According to his account, the crocodile is a four-footed amphibious creature that spends the daylight hours on dry land and the entire night in the river, because the water remains warmer than the open air and dew. During the four wintry months, it eats nothing at all. (007-001)

The crocodile lays and hatches its eggs on land. Herodotus notes its remarkable growth trajectory: it grows from a very small hatchling to become, in his estimation, the largest of all mortal creatures, reaching a length of seventeen cubits (approximately 7.6 metres) and even more. (007-001)

Herodotus describes the crocodile's distinctive physical features: it has the eyes of a pig, large teeth that protrude in proportion to its body, and — uniquely among animals — no tongue. It does not move its lower jaw, but instead brings its upper jaw down upon the lower, another trait he reports as singular. The creature's skin is covered with hard, impenetrable scales, and it has no power of movement in its neck. Although formidable in the water, the crocodile is said to be blind when out of it. (007-001)

## Religious Significance

The crocodile's status in Egyptian religion was divided. In some parts of Egypt, particularly around Thebes and Lake Moeris, the crocodile was regarded as sacred and was kept in temples, fed with special foods, adorned with ornaments on its forefeet and earrings of glass and gold, and embalmed after death for burial in sacred vaults. (007-001) The city of Crocodilopolis (modern Medinet el-Fayum), situated near Lake Moeris, was the principal cult centre of the crocodile god Sobek, whom the Greeks identified with aspects of their own pantheon.

However, in other regions — notably around [[Elephantine]] — the crocodile was not considered sacred at all, and was in fact hunted and eaten. This regional variation in the treatment of the crocodile reflects the broader pattern of local cult differences that Herodotus observes throughout Egypt. (007-001)

The crocodile was also associated with death and the Nile. Herodotus reports that if a person was carried off by a crocodile or drowned in the river, the inhabitants of the city where the body washed ashore were required to embalm the deceased in the finest manner and bury them in a sacred burial place, treating the body as something more than human. (008-002)

## Sources

> Of the crocodile the nature is as follows: during the four most wintry months this creature eats nothing. She has four feet and is an animal belonging to the land and the water both; for she produces and hatches eggs on the land, and the most part of the day she remains upon dry land, but the whole of the night in the river, for the water in truth is warmer than the unclouded open air and the dew. Of all the mortal creatures of which we have knowledge this grows to the greatest bulk from the smallest beginning.
> (007-001)

> Those who dwell about Thebes and about the lake of Moeris hold crocodiles to be most sacred, and each of these two peoples keeps one crocodile selected especially, which has been trained to be tame with the hand.
> (007-001)
