---## Overview

Egyptian burial customs, as recorded by Herodotus in the fifth century BCE, were among the most elaborate and carefully graded of the ancient world. The Egyptians' treatment of their dead was governed by a sophisticated system that offered different methods of [[embalming]] at different prices, each suited to the means of the deceased. Herodotus also documented the distinctive [[egyptian-mourning|mourning practices]] that accompanied death — practices that, characteristically, reversed Greek conventions — and the burial of [[sacred-animals]] in consecrated tombs. The historian's account remains one of the most detailed surviving descriptions of Egyptian funerary practice from any ancient source.

## Description

### The Three Tiers of Embalming

Herodotus describes a tiered system of embalming in which the practitioners — a professional guild — showed the family of the deceased wooden models of corpses, painted to lifelike realism, representing three methods at three price points. (008-002)

The most elaborate and expensive method — whose originator Herodotus declined to name "when speaking of a matter of such a kind" — began with the extraction of the brain through the nostrils using a crooked iron instrument, partly drawn out thus and partly dissolved by pouring in drugs. (008-002) The embalmers then made an incision in the flank with a sharp Ethiopian stone, removed the internal organs, and cleansed the cavity with palm wine and pounded spices. The body was then packed with pure ground myrrh, cassia, and other fragrant substances — excluding frankincense — and sewn up. After this preparation, the corpse was covered in natron for seventy days, the maximum period permitted for embalming. When the seventy days had passed, the body was washed and wrapped entirely in bands of fine linen, smeared underneath with gum — the substance the Egyptians used in place of glue. The wrapped body was then returned to the family, who placed it in a wooden case shaped like a human figure and stored it standing upright against the wall of a sepulchral chamber. (008-002)

The second, less expensive method involved no evisceration. Instead, cedar oil was injected into the body cavity through the anus, which was then stopped up to prevent the oil from escaping. The body was treated with natron for the prescribed number of days, after which the oil — which had dissolved the internal organs — was allowed to drain out. The natron had so acted upon the flesh that only skin and bones remained. The body was then returned to the family without further treatment. (009-006)

The third and cheapest method, reserved for the poor, consisted simply of purging the belly with a purge, treating the body with natron for seventy days, and returning it — a procedure that preserved the corpse with minimal expense. (009-006)

### Royal Burials

Royal burials reflected the monumental ambitions of Egypt's kings. The great pyramids of [[Cheops]], [[Chephren]], and Mycerinus at Giza were prodigious engineering undertakings involving tens of thousands of labourers working in rotation over decades. (012-004) (013-001) At [[Sais]], by contrast, the kings of that district were buried within the temple precinct of Athene — including [[Amasis]], whose tomb consisted of a great stone colonnade with pillars carved to imitate date-palms, double doors, and a sepulchral chamber within. (016-002)

A remarkable law from the reign of King [[Asychis]] tied burial directly to credit. Because money circulated slowly, a law permitted Egyptians to borrow money by offering as security the dead body of their father; the lender acquired a claim to the borrower's entire family sepulchral chamber. If the borrower defaulted, neither he nor any of his kin could receive burial — a penalty of immense gravity in a culture so devoted to the proper treatment of the dead. (013-003)

### Animal Burials

Sacred animals received funerary treatment no less careful than that accorded to humans. Cats, when they died, were carried to sacred buildings in the city of [[Bubastis]], where they were embalmed and buried. Dogs were buried by each people in their own city in sacred tombs. (006-005) At [[Thebes]] and around lake Moeris, where the crocodile was held most sacred, a single tame crocodile was selected, adorned with ornaments of gold and molten stone in its ears and anklets on its front feet, and after death was embalmed and buried in a sacred tomb. (007-001) At [[Mendes]], when the sacred goat died, "great mourning" fell upon the entire Mendesian district. (005-004)

### Mourning Practices

Egyptian mourning inverted Greek custom. Whereas among the Hellenes those most closely affected by a death cut their hair short, the Egyptians — who normally kept hair close-shaven — let both the hair of the head and the chin grow long during periods of mourning. (004-004) When a man of reputation died, the women of his household immediately plastered their heads and faces with mud, then left the body at home while they roamed the city beating themselves, their garments bound up by a girdle and their breasts exposed. All female relatives joined this procession of lamentation; the men beat themselves in the same manner. Only after this public display of grief was the body conveyed to the embalmers. (008-002)

At the great festival of Isis at [[Busiris]], Herodotus witnessed mass mourning on an immense scale: "all of them both men and women, very many myriads of people" beat themselves in lamentation after the sacrifice, though the historian declined to name the deity for whom they mourned, citing religious prohibition. (006-002)

### The Wooden Corpse at Banquets

Among the wealthy, a striking custom connected death to the pleasures of life. At banquets, when the feasting was finished, a servant carried around a wooden figure of a dead body in a coffin, painted and carved to resemble reality as closely as possible, measuring about one or two cubits across. Showing this to each guest in turn, the bearer said: "When thou lookest upon this, drink and be merry, for thou shalt be such as this when thou art dead." (007-005) This custom, Herodotus implies, was not morbid but philosophical — a reminder of mortality intended to enhance, not diminish, the enjoyment of life.

## Historical Context

By the 5th century BCE, Egyptian burial customs were already millennia old. The pyramids at Giza that [[herodotus|Herodotus]] describes — built by [[cheops|Cheops]] and [[chephren|Chephren]] — were over two thousand years old at the time of his visit, and the system of embalming he documents in such detail had been refined over countless generations. The tiered structure of embalming services, with options priced for different social classes, reflects a society in which proper burial was considered essential for all Egyptians, not just the wealthy. The law of [[asychis|Asychis]] linking burial rights to debt repayment underscores how profoundly the threat of denied burial weighed upon the Egyptian conscience.

## Herodotus's Commentary

[[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s account of Egyptian burial customs is remarkable for its detail and its restraint. He describes each of the three embalming methods with clinical precision, yet declines to name the originator of the most costly method "when speaking of a matter of such a kind" — a rare moment of religious scruple from a historian generally willing to discuss sacred matters openly. His treatment of Egyptian mourning practices reveals his characteristic comparative method: the Egyptians' inversion of Greek mourning customs (growing hair rather than cutting it) is presented as one of many examples of Egypt's oppositional relationship to Greek norms. The wooden corpse at banquets elicits neither condemnation nor ridicule from [[herodotus|Herodotus]], but rather a neutral description that allows the custom's philosophical intent to speak for itself.

## Significance

Egyptian burial customs illuminate several fundamental aspects of Egyptian society. The three-tier embalming system demonstrates both technological sophistication and the principle that proper treatment of the dead was a right shared across social classes, not a privilege of the elite. The mourning practices, with their public, bodily expression of grief, reveal a culture in which death was confronted openly rather than hidden away. The wooden corpse at banquets articulates a worldview in which the awareness of death was meant to intensify the appreciation of life. And the elaborate burial of sacred animals — cats, dogs, crocodiles, goats — shows that the Egyptian concern for the proper treatment of the dead extended beyond the human realm to encompass the entire sacred order.
