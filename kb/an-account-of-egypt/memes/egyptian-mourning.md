## Overview

Herodotus provides one of the earliest and most vivid ethnographic accounts of Egyptian mourning customs. His description covers the dramatic public displays of grief by both women and men, the ritual treatment of the dead body, the unique place of lamentation in Egyptian culture — including the "Song of Maneros," which he identifies as Egypt's first and only traditional song — and the integration of mourning into sacrificial ritual. (008-002) (007-005)

## Description

### Public Mourning Rituals

When a man of any standing died, Herodotus records that the response was immediate, communal, and strikingly physical. The women of the household "forthwith plaster over their heads or even their faces with mud." They would then leave the corpse within the house and go throughout the city beating themselves, with their garments bound up by a girdle and their breasts exposed. All the female relatives of the deceased joined this procession, while the men likewise beat themselves, forming a separate group. This ritualised public display of grief was the first act of Egyptian mourning, performed before the body was taken for embalming. (008-002)

### The Song of Maneros

Herodotus notes with particular interest that the Egyptians possessed only a single traditional song — the "Song of Linos," which the Egyptians called *Maneros*. This was a lament, sung in honour of the only son of the first king of Egypt, who died before his time. Herodotus observed that this lamentation song closely resembled one sung by the Greeks under the name of Linos, and also by the Phoenicians and Cypriots, each people giving it a different name. He regarded this cross-cultural diffusion of the lament as one of the most remarkable things he encountered in Egypt. (007-005)

In the entertainments of the wealthy, a reminder of mortality was ever-present. After a feast, a servant would carry around a wooden image of a corpse in a coffin, painted and carved to resemble the reality as closely as possible, measuring about a cubit or two in each direction. Showing it to each guest in turn, he would say: "When thou lookest upon this, drink and be merry, for thou shalt be such as this when thou art dead." This *memento mori* custom links Egyptian feasting directly to funerary consciousness. (007-005)

### Embalming and the Transition to Burial

After the public mourning rituals, the body was handed over to the embalmers, a distinct professional class in Egyptian society who possessed the craft of preserving the dead. Herodotus describes three tiers of embalming, differing in cost and thoroughness. The most elaborate method involved extracting the brain through the nostrils with a crooked iron tool, making an incision in the flank with a sharp Ethiopian stone to remove the internal organs, cleansing the cavity with palm wine and pounded spices, and then packing the body for seventy days in natron before wrapping it in fine linen. The middle and least expensive methods substituted less invasive techniques and simpler materials. The seventy-day embalming period formed a ritual interval between the initial mourning and the final burial, during which the body was transformed from a perishable corpse into a lasting memorial. (008-003) (008-004)

### Animal Mourning

The Egyptians' relationship with animals extended even into their mourning practices. Herodotus records that when a cat died a natural death in a household, all the inhabitants shaved off their eyebrows. When a dog died, they shaved their entire body and head. When a fire broke out, the Egyptians were more concerned with the safety of their cats than with extinguishing the flames — the cats, being creatures "fond of their young," would sometimes leap into the fire, causing "great mourning" among the Egyptians. This scale of animal-related mourning, in which bodily disfigurement paralleled that of human grief, testifies to the uniquely intimate place of animals within Egyptian domestic and religious life. (006-005)

### Mourning During Sacrifice

Mourning was not restricted to funerals but was incorporated into the most sacred sacrificial rites. During the great sacrifice to [[Isis]], after the consecrated animal had been filled with spices and offerings and the fire was kindled, all participants beat themselves in a ritual act of mourning while the offerings burned. Only when this ceremonial beating was complete did the feast begin, the community consuming what had been left unburnt. This integration of lamentation into sacrifice suggests that mourning, in Egyptian thought, was not merely a response to death but a prescribed mode of approaching the divine — grief transformed into a liturgical act. (004-006)

## Herodotus's Commentary

Herodotus was visibly impressed by the intensity and theatricality of Egyptian mourning. His detailed description of mud-plastered women and beating processions conveys a sense of cultural difference — this was grief performed at a scale and visibility unfamiliar to his Hellenic audience. Yet he also sought points of connection: the Song of Maneros, he insisted, was the same lament sung by Greeks, Phoenicians, and Cypriots, differing only in name. This claim of cross-cultural continuity transforms what might have appeared as an alien Egyptian custom into evidence of a shared Mediterranean heritage. His observation that the Egyptians possessed "only one song" — and that it was a lament — carries an implicit cultural judgment: Egyptian music, unlike Greek, was fundamentally funerary in character, its artistic expression inseparable from consciousness of mortality.

## Significance

Egyptian mourning practices reveal a culture in which death was not hidden away but made spectacularly public. From the mud-plastered processions through city streets to the wooden corpse paraded at drinking parties, the Egyptians confronted mortality openly and ritually. The integration of mourning into sacrifice — lamentation as a mode of worship — suggests that grief itself was understood as a form of piety. The cross-cultural diffusion of the Maneros/Linos lament, which Herodotus regarded as one of his most remarkable discoveries, positions Egypt as the source of a funerary tradition that radiated across the Mediterranean, shaping the mourning practices of Greeks, Phoenicians, and Cypriots alike.
