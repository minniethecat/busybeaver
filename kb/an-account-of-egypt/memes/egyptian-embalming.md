## Overview

The art of embalming was one of the most developed technical practices of ancient Egyptian civilization, and Herodotus provides the most detailed surviving Greek account of its methods. In his description, embalming was not a single uniform practice but a graded craft, with three distinct tiers offered to families according to their means. The professional embalmers, who inherited the craft through family lines, presented clients with wooden models demonstrating the available options before commencing their work. (008-002)

## The Hereditary Craft

Embalming was not an open trade but a hereditary profession, passed down within families from generation to generation. Herodotus explicitly records that "certain persons employ themselves regularly and inherit this as a craft" — a detail that places embalming among the specialized, closed professions of ancient Egypt, akin to the priesthood and the warrior class. When a corpse was conveyed to them, the embalmers displayed painted wooden models of corpses to the bereaved family, showing the three available methods in order of cost and quality. The most excellent method, Herodotus notes, was attributed to a figure whose name he considered it an act of impiety to utter in the context of death — almost certainly the god Osiris, whose mythic dismemberment and reconstitution provided the divine archetype for Egyptian mummification. (008-002)

## The Most Costly Method

The first and most excellent method of embalming proceeded through a series of meticulous steps. The embalmers began by extracting the brain through the nostrils using a crooked iron tool, partly drawn out directly and partly dissolved by drugs poured into the skull. Next, they made an incision along the side of the body with a sharp Ethiopian stone and removed the entire contents of the abdomen. After cleansing the cavity with palm-wine, they cleansed it again with pounded spices, then filled the belly with pure pounded myrrh, cassia, and other spices — frankincense alone being excluded. The incision was then sewn shut. (008-002)

Once prepared, the body was covered in natron and kept for embalming for exactly seventy days — no longer was permitted. At the end of this period, the body was washed and the whole corpse was rolled up in fine linen bands, smeared underneath with gum (which the Egyptians used generally in place of glue). The family then received the body and commissioned a wooden coffin shaped in human form, within which the embalmed corpse was enclosed. The finished mummy was stored upright against the wall of a sepulchral chamber — a practice that has preserved countless Egyptian bodies through millennia. (008-002)

## The Middle and Least Expensive Methods

For those who desired a middle way and wished to avoid great expense, a simpler method was available. Without cutting open the body or removing the bowels, the embalmers filled syringes with cedar-wood oil and injected it into the belly through the breech, stopping the drench from returning. After keeping the body for the appointed number of days, they released the oil, which had such solvent power that it brought out the bowels and interior organs in a dissolved state. The natron simultaneously dissolved the flesh, leaving only the skin and bones. The body was then returned immediately, requiring no further work. (008-002)

The third and least expensive method — used for those of fewer means — was simpler still. The embalmers cleansed the belly with a purge and kept the body in natron for seventy days, after which it was returned at once to the family for burial. This tiered system ensured that every Egyptian, regardless of wealth, could receive some form of ritual preparation for the afterlife. (008-002)

## Embalming and Egyptian Attitudes Toward Death

Herodotus's account of embalming must be understood within the broader context of Egyptian funerary customs. The Egyptians were, in his description, the most religious of all peoples, and their meticulous preparation of the dead reflected a conviction that the preservation of the body was essential to the afterlife. The exclusion of woolen garments from burial — a prohibition Herodotus specifically compares to Orphic and Bacchic mysteries and Pythagorean observances — complemented the embalming process: just as the body was purified through natron and spices, so the wrappings that enclosed it had to be of ritually pure linen, never wool. (007-005)

The elaborate mourning rituals that preceded embalming included the women of the household plastering mud over their heads and faces, binding their garments, and exposing their breasts as they went about the city beating themselves. Only after these public demonstrations of grief was the body delivered to the embalmers — except in the case of women of high rank, whose bodies were withheld for three or four days as a precaution against defilement, a practice instituted after one embalmer was caught violating the corpse of a recently deceased woman. (008-002)

## Social Precautions

Herodotus records one notable social precaution in the embalming of women. The wives of men of rank, and women of exceptional beauty or social standing, were not delivered to the embalmers immediately upon death. Instead, their bodies were kept for three or four days before being handed over. This practice arose after one embalmer was allegedly discovered violating the corpse of a recently deceased woman — an incident reported by his fellow craftsman. The delay served as a safeguard against similar abuses. (008-002)

## Significance

The Egyptian embalming system reveals a society in which death was approached with technical sophistication and graded economic access. The hereditary craft structure ensured the preservation of specialized knowledge across generations. The three-tier pricing model — from the full evisceration-and-spice treatment to the simple natron purge — made ritual preparation accessible to all social strata. Herodotus's detailed account, covering tools, materials, timelines, social protocols, and even the precautionary withholding of female corpses, stands as one of the earliest comprehensive records of a funerary craft that defined Egypt in the ancient imagination. The seventy-day natron treatment, the exclusion of frankincense, the cedar-oil dissolution method, the linen-only burial wrapping — these precise technical and ritual details together demonstrate that embalming was both a systematic body of practical knowledge and a religious act, transmitted across generations of practitioners who served as intermediaries between the living, the dead, and the divine. (008-002) (007-005)
