Prosopitis was an island in the [[Nile Delta]] described by [[Herodotus]] as the administrative centre for a distinctive religious practice — the centralised collection and reburial of sacrificial cattle bones from across all of Egypt. Herodotus records that its circuit measured nine _schoines_ (a unit of Egyptian measurement), and that it was situated within the Delta region. (004-006)

## The Island of Prosopitis in Herodotus's Account

Herodotus describes Prosopitis in the context of Egyptian sacrificial customs, specifically the treatment of cattle that had died. While female cattle were cast into the [[Nile River|Nile]], males were buried by each town in their suburbs, with one horn — or sometimes both — protruding from the earth to mark the grave. (004-006) When the bodies had rotted and the appointed time arrived, a boat was despatched from Prosopitis to every Egyptian city. The men from this boat travelled round the cities, dug up the bones of the oxen, and carried them back to be buried together in a single place. (004-006)

## The City of Atarbechis

On the island of Prosopitis stood the city of Atarbechis, which Herodotus notes was the specific point of origin for the boats that collected the cattle bones. Atarbechis also housed a holy temple of [[Aphrodite]] (likely the Egyptian goddess Hathor in Herodotus's syncretic identification). (004-006) The presence of this temple suggests that the bone-collection ritual was not merely a practical matter but a religious observance centred on the cult of this goddess.

## Administrative Significance

The Prosopitis bone-collection system reveals an organised religious infrastructure operating on a national scale. That boats were regularly despatched from a single island in the Delta to cities across the length of Egypt — from the Delta to the southern reaches of the country — implies a degree of centralised religious administration that Herodotus found noteworthy enough to record in detail. The systematic nature of this practice, with its appointed times and coordinated movements, testifies to the sophistication of Egyptian religious organisation.

## Military Context

Prosopitis also appears in Herodotus's description of the Egyptian military caste system. The island was listed among the districts of the Hermotybians, one of the two warrior classes of Egypt, whose territory included [[Busiris]], [[Sais]], [[Chemmis]], [[Papremis]], Prosopitis, and half of Natho. (015-011) At their most numerous, the Hermotybians numbered sixteen myriads (160,000), and from this district the warriors were drawn who served as the king's bodyguard and garrisoned the frontier fortresses.
