## Overview

In [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s *Account of [[egypt|Egypt]]*, [[Isis]] appears as one of the two deities worshipped by all [[egypt|Egypt]]ians without exception — alongside [[Osiris]] (whom [[herodotus|Herodotus]] identifies with [[Dionysos]]). Unlike local cults tied to specific districts, the worship of [[isis-and-osiris|Isis and Osiris]] was universal across [[egypt|Egypt]], marking them as the most significant figures in the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian pantheon as [[herodotus|Herodotus]] understood it. Isis features in the myth of [[Apollo]] and [[Artemis]] as their mother, and her great temple at [[Busiris]] hosted the second-most-important religious festival in [[egypt|Egypt]].

## Universal Worship

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] explicitly notes the exceptional status of [[Isis]] and [[Osiris]]: while different [[egypt|Egypt]]ian districts revered different gods — Thebans worshipped the [[zeus|Theban Zeus]], [[mendes|Mendesian]]s the goat-god — "not all the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians equally reverence the same gods, except only [[isis-and-osiris|Isis and Osiris]] (who they say is Dionysos), these they all reverence alike" (005-001). This observation marks one of the earliest Greek recognitions of pan-[[egypt|Egypt]]ian cults as distinct from localized worship.

The universal reverence for [[isis-and-osiris|Isis and Osiris]] reflects the centrality of the [[osiris|Osiris]] myth to [[egypt|Egypt]]ian religion: the death of [[osiris|Osiris]] at the hands of [[Typhon]] (Set), the mourning of Isis, the birth and concealment of Horus, and [[osiris|Osiris]]'s resurrection as ruler of the underworld. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] alludes to this narrative obliquely, noting the secrecy surrounding certain [[egypt|Egypt]]ian rites.

## The Festival at Busiris

The city of [[Busiris]], located in the central [[nile-delta|Delta]], housed a "very great temple of Isis" (006-002). The festival of Isis at Busiris was the second-most-zealously celebrated religious assembly in [[egypt|Egypt]], exceeded only by the festival of [[Artemis]] (Bastet) at [[Bubastis]]. After the ceremonies concluded, [[herodotus|Herodotus]] reports that the worshippers — men and women numbering in the hundreds of thousands — "beat themselves" in a ritual of mourning, though he declines to specify the object of this mourning, observing discretion regarding sacred matters (006-002).

## Mother of Apollo and Artemis

The [[egyptian-priesthood|Egyptian priests]] informed [[herodotus|Herodotus]] that [[Apollo]] and [[Artemis]] were the children of [[Dionysos]] ([[osiris|Osiris]]) and [[Isis]]. In this genealogy, [[Leto]] — rather than being their mother as in Greek myth — served as their nurse and protector, concealing the infant [[apollo|Apollo]] on the floating island of [[chemnis|Chemmis]] to shield him from [[Typhon]] (015-002). This [[egypt|Egypt]]ian variant of the divine genealogy places Isis at the center of the mythic narrative, her role as mother of the divine children paralleling her protection of Horus in native [[egypt|Egypt]]ian tradition.

## Cultic Practices

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] associates Isis with bovine cult, noting the sacrificial practices surrounding oxen. The priest examined cattle for ritual purity by inspecting hairs and the tongue — practices tied to the cult of Epaphos but associated with the worship of Isis in her capacity as cow-goddess (004-006). This conflation reflects the Greek identification of Isis with multiple deities including Demeter and Io-Epaphos.
