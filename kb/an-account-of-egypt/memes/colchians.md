## Herodotus's Account

Herodotus presents a detailed argument for the Egyptian origin of the Colchians, a people living in the region of Colchis on the eastern shore of the Black Sea, near the river Phasis.

### The Egyptian Origin Theory

During his account of the conquests of the Egyptian king Sesostris, Herodotus describes how the king marched through Asia and crossed into Europe, subduing the Scythians and Thracians [002-129]. When Sesostris reached the river Phasis, Herodotus offers two possibilities: either the king deliberately left a portion of his army as settlers, or some soldiers, wearied by the long marches, chose to remain.

### Evidence for Egyptian Descent

Herodotus marshals several pieces of evidence for the Colchians' Egyptian ancestry:

**Physical Appearance**: "The Colchians are dark-skinned and have curly hair" [002-129], though Herodotus acknowledges this alone is inconclusive since other races share these traits.

**Circumcision**: The Colchians, Egyptians, and Ethiopians are the only peoples who have practiced circumcision from the earliest times [002-129]. The Phoenicians and Syrians of Palestine admit they learned the practice from the Egyptians, and the Syrians near the Thermodon and Parthenios rivers say they learned it recently from the Colchians.

**Shared Customs**: The Colchians "alone work flax in the same fashion as the Egyptians, and the two nations are like one another in their whole manner of living and also in their language" [002-129]. The linen of Colchis was known to the Hellenes as "Sardonic" linen.

**Mutual Memory**: Herodotus reports that "the Colchians had remembrance of the Egyptians more than the Egyptians of the Colchians," and that the Egyptians believed the Colchians were descended from Sesostris's army.

## Historical Significance

Herodotus's account of the Colchians represents one of the earliest surviving examples of ethnographic argumentation — using observable customs, physical traits, and oral tradition to support a theory of shared ancestry. The passage also illustrates the cultural prestige of Egypt in the ancient Mediterranean world, where even distant peoples might claim Egyptian descent.
