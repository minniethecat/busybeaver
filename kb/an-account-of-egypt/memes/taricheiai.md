## Overview

Taricheiai was an Egyptian settlement situated at the Canobic mouth of the Nile, the westernmost branch of the river's delta. It is notable in Herodotus's account as the landing place of Alexander (Paris) when he was driven off course by contrary winds while sailing from Sparta with Helen. The location featured a temple of Heracles with the right of sanctuary for slaves. (010-001)

## Location

Taricheiai lay at the Canobic (Canobic) mouth of the Nile, which Herodotus describes as the western branch of the three principal mouths of the delta. The settlement was situated on the shore near the river-mouth, under the jurisdiction of a warden named Thonis. The location was directly accessible from the Mediterranean Sea for ships driven off course from the Aegean. (010-001)

## The Sanctuary of Heracles

Herodotus records that at Taricheiai there stood a temple of Heracles on the shore, possessing an ancient and enduring right of sanctuary. If any man's slave took refuge in this temple and received the sacred marks, it was unlawful to lay hands upon him. This custom, Herodotus notes, had continued unchanged from earliest times down to his own day. (010-001)

The sanctuary right played a critical role in the Helen episode: the attendants of Alexander, having heard of the existing custom, fled from him and took refuge as suppliants at the temple. There they accused Alexander of his wrongdoing toward Menelaos, relating the full story of Helen's abduction and the wrongs done to their master. Their accusations were made not only to the priests of the temple but also to Thonis, the warden of the river-mouth. (010-001)

## Role in the Helen Episode

The arrival of Alexander and Helen at Taricheiai set in motion a chain of events recounted in detail by the Egyptian priests. Thonis, upon hearing the accusations from the suppliants, sent a message to King Proteus at Memphis, explaining the situation and asking whether the stranger should be allowed to depart or be detained. Proteus ordered Alexander to be seized and brought before him. (010-001)

The outcome of this episode was that Proteus, after interrogating Alexander and finding him guilty of impiety toward his host Menelaos, refused to put the stranger to death but retained Helen and Alexander's wealth in safekeeping at Memphis. Alexander was ordered to depart from Egypt within three days. The priests told Herodotus that Helen thus remained in Egypt throughout the Trojan War, and that Menelaos later came to Memphis to reclaim both her and his wealth. (010-001) (011-002)
