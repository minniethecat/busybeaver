## Overview

Herodotus's itinerary through Egypt took him to many of its most important cities, and his account preserves a detailed urban geography of the country in the fifth century BCE. This list catalogues the cities he mentions, arranging them by region and recording their distinctive characteristics: their patron deities, notable monuments, festivals, and the role each played in the narrative history the priests recounted to him.

## Commentary

Herodotus's urban catalogue reflects his interests and travel constraints. He visited Memphis, Thebes, Heliopolis, and Buto in person, while his information about cities in the far south (Elephantine) and the Delta periphery came from the priests. His descriptions of Bubastis and Sais are particularly vivid, suggesting he may have attended their festivals. The city of Chemmis in the Theban nome stands out for its adoption of Hellenic customs — Herodotus singles it out as the one Egyptian city that embraced foreign practices, holding gymnastic contests in honour of Perseus that followed Hellenic rules (008-003).
