## Overview

Herodotus provides one of the earliest detailed accounts of the Egyptian diet, describing the foods that sustained a civilization whose agriculture depended entirely on the Nile. The Egyptians, he reports, ate bread made from maize, drank a wine brewed from barley (having no vines in their land), and consumed fish — some dried in the sun, others cured in brine — and birds, including quails, ducks, and small birds, either roasted or boiled. What makes Herodotus's account distinctive is his attention not only to what the Egyptians ate but to the cultural and medical theories that surrounded their food practices. (007-005)

The Egyptian diet, as Herodotus presents it, was shaped by three forces: the ecological constraints of the Nile valley (no vines, abundant fish and fowl), the religious prohibitions of sacred animal categories, and the medical conviction that food was the primary source of disease. This triple determination — environment, religion, medicine — produced a food culture that was at once practical, ritually disciplined, and theoretically sophisticated.

## Staple Foods and Drink

The foundation of the Egyptian diet was bread made from maize, formed into loaves called *kyllestis*. Because the vine did not grow in Egypt, the Egyptians brewed their wine from barley — a fact that distinguished them from the wine-drinking Greeks and other Mediterranean peoples. This barley-wine was their habitual drink, consumed daily rather than reserved for festivals. Herodotus does not comment directly on the quality of this beverage, but his matter-of-fact description suggests that it was as ordinary to the Egyptian table as grape wine was to the Greek. (007-005)

The Egyptians also consumed fish, which the Nile provided in abundance. Some fish were dried in the sun and eaten without further cooking, while others were cured in brine and preserved. Of birds, they ate quails, ducks, and small birds, often cured first and then eaten without cooking. All other birds and fish not designated as sacred were consumed either roasted or boiled. This flexible approach to food preparation — drying, curing, roasting, boiling — reflects a sophisticated food culture adapted to the rhythms of the river and the climate. (007-005)

What is notable about Herodotus's list of staples is what it omits: there is no mention of beef, pork, or other domesticated meat as everyday food. While cattle sacrifice was central to Egyptian religious practice and the meat was presumably consumed by priests and worshippers, Herodotus's silence on everyday meat consumption suggests that for most Egyptians, the diet was predominantly grain-based, supplemented by the fish and fowl that the Nile freely provided.

## The Marsh-dwellers' Diet

Herodotus notes that the Egyptians of the fen-country, while sharing most customs with their fellow countrymen, had developed distinctive food economies suited to their environment. When the Nile flooded the plains, great numbers of lilies — which the Egyptians called *lotos* — grew in the water. The marsh-dwellers would cut these with sickles, dry them in the sun, and then pound the middle portion, which resembled a poppy-head, to extract a bread-like substance. They also consumed the root of the lotos, which Herodotus describes as round and of the size of an apple, with a taste compared to a date. Another plant, the papyrus, was also eaten: its lower portion was roasted and consumed. These marsh-land innovations, Herodotus explains, were motivated by "economy in respect of food" — the need to make the most of what the environment provided. (008-005)

The marsh-dwellers' exploitation of the lotus and papyrus illustrates a broader principle of Egyptian food culture: the ability to derive sustenance from the full range of what the Nilotic environment produced. Where the grain-fed Egyptian of the cultivated plains relied on maize, the marsh-dweller turned the flood-born plants into bread-like staples — a regional adaptation that Herodotus records with evident ethnographic interest.

## Diet and Health

Herodotus offers a remarkable account of Egyptian medical theory as it related to diet. The Egyptians, he says, believed that all diseases originated in the food men consumed. To counteract this, they purged themselves for three consecutive days each month, using emetics and clysters to expel the harmful residues of their diet. This rigorous regimen, combined with the climate — which Herodotus considered remarkably stable, without seasonal change to provoke illness — made the Egyptians, in his judgment, the healthiest of all peoples after the Libyans. This passage represents one of the earliest recorded instances of a systematic theory linking diet to disease, and it illustrates how thoroughly the Egyptians had integrated food practices into their broader understanding of health and the body. (007-005)

This Egyptian theory — that disease entered the body through food and must be expelled through monthly purging — stands in contrast to the Hippocratic theory of humoral balance that would later develop in Greece. Where Greek medicine emphasized equilibrium among internal fluids, Egyptian medicine emphasized periodic cleansing of externally introduced impurities. Herodotus's account, while not a medical treatise, preserves an important witness to this alternative tradition.

## The Banquet Custom

Herodotus records a striking custom practiced at the banquets of the wealthy: after the meal, a man would carry around a wooden figure of a corpse, finely painted and carved, measuring one or two cubits in length. He would show this figure to each guest and say, "When thou lookest upon this, drink and be merry, for thou shalt be such as this when thou art dead." This *memento mori* served to remind diners of their mortality even in the midst of pleasure. It is one of the most vivid snapshots of Egyptian social life in the entire *Histories*, and it connects food and drink — the most life-sustaining of activities — to the ever-present consciousness of death. (007-005)

The banquet custom also reveals something about the Egyptian social hierarchy: this was a practice of "the rich among them," suggesting that the *memento mori* was a marker of elite dining rather than a universal custom. The wooden corpse, "made as like the reality as may be both by painting and carving," was itself a work of craftsmanship — a small sculpture whose production required the same skills employed in funerary statuary and temple decoration. In this sense, the banquet custom linked the pleasures of the table to the arts of the tomb, weaving food, craft, and mortality into a single ritual moment.

## Dietary Restrictions and Sacred Animals

A significant dimension of Egyptian diet that Herodotus addresses elsewhere is the prohibition against consuming animals designated as sacred. The Egyptians treated cats, hawks, ibises, crocodiles of Thebes, and cattle from specific precincts as inviolable. The consequences of consuming a sacred animal — whether intentionally or accidentally — could be severe, including capital punishment for the deliberate killing of certain species. These prohibitions created a dietary landscape in which the line between edible and inedible was drawn not by nutritional value or culinary preference but by religious classification. (006-005)

In consequence, the Egyptian diet was as much defined by what could *not* be eaten as by what could. The fish and fowl Herodotus enumerates as staples were, in part, those species that fell outside the sacred categories — the edible remainder after religious exclusions had been applied.

## Significance

Herodotus's account of the Egyptian diet reveals a civilization in which food was inseparable from religion, medicine, and social custom. The dietary laws regarding sacred animals determined what could and could not be eaten. The monthly purges reflected a theory of disease causation rooted in the digestive process. And the banquet custom — the wooden corpse borne among the wine cups — shows that even the act of eating was framed by the Egyptian preoccupation with mortality and the afterlife. Together, these details form a portrait not merely of what the Egyptians ate, but of how they understood the relationship between food, the body, and the divine.

## Sources

> As to their diet, it is as follows: — they eat bread, making loaves of maize, which they call *kyllestis*, and they use habitually a wine made out of barley, for vines they have not in their land. Of their fish some they dry in the sun and then eat them without cooking, others they eat cured in brine. Of birds they eat quails and ducks and small birds without cooking, after first curing them; and everything else which they have belonging to the class of birds or fishes, except such as have been set apart by them as sacred, they eat roasted or boiled.
> (007-005)

> In the entertainments of the rich among them, when they have finished eating, a man bears round a wooden figure of a dead body in a coffin... and this he shows to each of those who are drinking together, saying: "When thou lookest upon this, drink and be merry, for thou shalt be such as this when thou art dead."
> (007-005)

> Those who are settled in the fenland have the same customs for the most part as the other Egyptians... but for economy in respect of food they have invented these things besides: — when the river has become full and the plains have been flooded, there grow in the water great numbers of lilies, which the Egyptians call *lotos*; these they cut with a sickle and dry in the sun, and then they pound that which grows in the middle of the lotos.
> (008-005)
