## Overview

Bread was the staple food of ancient Egypt and carried deep religious significance in Egyptian temple life. Herodotus records that sacred bread was baked daily for the priests of Egypt, who received it among their regular provisions without having to spend their own resources. The centrality of bread to Egyptian life is further illustrated by the well-known story of Psammetichos, who identified the Phrygians as the oldest people when two children raised in isolation uttered the word _bekos_ — the Phrygian word for bread. (004-001) (001-002)

## Sacred Bread and the Priesthood

The provision of sacred bread to the Egyptian priests was an institutional arrangement of considerable scale. Herodotus reports that priests received "sacred bread baked for them" and "great quantity of flesh of oxen and geese" each day, along with wine of grapes, without consuming or spending their own property. This system of temple-sustained priesthood freed the priestly class from agricultural or commercial labour and enabled them to devote themselves fully to ritual service. The bread was presumably prepared within temple precincts according to established purity rules, reflecting the Egyptian preoccupation with ritual cleanliness. (004-001)

## Kyllestis: The Egyptian Daily Loaf

The ordinary Egyptian consumed bread made not of wheat but of maize, formed into loaves the Egyptians called _kyllestis_. Herodotus describes this as part of the daily diet of the Egyptian people — a people he considered the healthiest in the world after the Libyans, attributing their remarkable health partly to the unchanging seasons and partly to their disciplined regimen of monthly purging. Since Egypt lacked the grape-vine, the Egyptians habitually accompanied their maize bread with a wine made from barley — a substitution that suited the alluvial soil and climate of the Nile valley. (007-005)

## Bread as Military Provision

Bread served not only the priesthood but also the standing army of Egypt. The two-thousand-strong royal bodyguard — composed of a thousand Calasirians and a thousand Hermotybians, rotating yearly — received, in addition to their allotted lands, a daily allowance of five pounds weight of bread per man, alongside two pounds of beef and four half-pints of wine. This ration system reveals the Egyptian state's capacity to provision a standing military force through centrally organised distribution, with bread as the caloric foundation of soldierly sustenance. The daily provision of bread at such scale implies a well-developed royal bakery infrastructure. (016-001)

## Bread and the Psammetichos Experiment

Herodotus recounts that king Psammetichos, wishing to determine which people were the most ancient, devised an experiment in which two newborn children were raised by a shepherd in complete silence, hearing no human speech. After two years, the children spontaneously uttered the word _bekos_, stretching forth their hands. Upon inquiry, Psammetichos learned that _bekos_ was the Phrygian word for bread, leading the Egyptians to concede that the Phrygians were older than themselves. This narrative, regardless of its historicity, reveals the cultural assumption that bread — as humanity's most basic food — would be among the first words spoken. (001-002)

## Significance

Bread in ancient Egypt was more than sustenance; it was an institution. The temple bread system supported a hereditary priesthood that formed one of Egypt's seven social classes. The military bread ration provisioned the royal bodyguard and demonstrated the administrative reach of the Pharaonic state. The kyllestis loaf, made from maize and accompanied by barley wine, was the daily sustenance of a people Herodotus ranked among the healthiest known to him. And the Psammetichos story demonstrates how bread served as a cultural touchstone for thinking about human origins and linguistic identity. The sacred bread of the temples, the maize loaf of the common table, and the weighed ration of the soldier — these three forms of bread together map the social structure of ancient Egypt from priest to peasant to warrior.
