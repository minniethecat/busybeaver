## Overview

Cats occupied a uniquely intimate position among Egyptian sacred animals, living alongside humans in their houses rather than in the wild. Herodotus devotes particular attention to the Egyptian relationship with cats, recording both their peculiar behaviour during fires and the elaborate mourning rituals that followed their death. The accounts reveal a society in which the bond between human and animal was governed by religious obligation and deep emotional attachment. (006-002)

## The Domestic Life of Sacred Cats

Herodotus notes that of the animals living with humans, cats were the most numerous — and would be many more were it not for certain accidents that befell them. He describes a curious aspect of cat breeding: after females produced young, they no longer sought the males, who in turn were unable to approach them. To address this, the Egyptians would either forcibly remove or secretly take away the young and kill them — though they did not eat the dead kittens. The females, deprived of their young and desiring more, would then return to the males, for the cat, Herodotus observes, is "a creature that is fond of its young." This practice, while harsh to modern sensibilities, was framed by the Egyptians as a necessary intervention to maintain the domestic cat population. (006-002)

The cat's response to fire was, according to Herodotus, nothing short of supernatural. When a fire broke out, the Egyptians stood at intervals to watch over the cats but took no care to extinguish the flames. The cats, seemingly "divinely possessed," would slip through or leap over the bystanders and hurl themselves into the fire. These events triggered "great mourning" among the Egyptians — the loss of cats to fire was apparently a recurring tragedy in their communities. (006-002)

## Mourning and Burial Rites

The death of a cat initiated a household-wide ritual of mourning. In any house where a cat died a natural death, all the inhabitants shaved their eyebrows — a visible, bodily sign of grief. By contrast, when a dog died, the household shaved the entire body and head, indicating a hierarchy of mourning intensity among different sacred animals. The dead cats were not simply buried: they were carried away to sacred buildings in the city of Bubastis, there to be embalmed and buried in consecrated ground. This practice paralleled the treatment of other sacred animals — dogs were buried in each city's sacred tombs, shrewmice and hawks were taken to Buto, and ibises to Hermopolis. Each species had its own appointed necropolis. (006-002)

## Herodotus's Ethnographic Approach

Herodotus's account of cats in Egypt illustrates his characteristic method as an ethnographer. While he records with detail the Egyptians' rituals around cat death and their explanation of cat behaviour, he refrains from passing explicit judgment, presenting the customs as observable facts. The cat's dual role — as household companion and as sacred being whose death demanded communal mourning — offers a window into the deep integration of religious practice into Egyptian domestic life, a pattern that impressed upon Herodotus the uniqueness of Egyptian civilization among the Mediterranean cultures he documented. (006-002)
