## Overview

The Ammonians were a Libyan people who dwelt around the Oracle of Ammon (later known as the Siwa Oasis), an important oracular sanctuary of Zeus that Herodotus frequently references in his "Account of Egypt." According to Herodotus, the Ammonians were settlers descended from both Egyptians and Ethiopians, speaking a language that mixed both tongues (065). Their king Etearchos served as an informant for Greek travellers from Cyrene, and through him Herodotus received one of the most colourful accounts of African exploration — the story of the Nasamonian youths' journey across the Sahara.

## Origin and Customs

Herodotus identifies the Ammonians as colonists of mixed Egyptian and Ethiopian descent. He notes that they adopted the Egyptian practice of representing Zeus with a ram's face, following the same mythological rationale as the Thebans: that when Heracles begged to see Zeus, the god flayed a ram, held its head before him, and showed himself thus (065). The Egyptian name "Amun" for Zeus, Herodotus suggests, derives from this same tradition. The Oracle of Ammon was consulted by the people of Marea and Apis, who sought permission to eat cows' flesh on the grounds that they were Libyans, not Egyptians; the oracle refused, defining Egypt as all the land watered by the Nile (029).

## The Oracle of Ammon

The Oracle of Ammon was one of the two great prophetic seats that, according to Egyptian tradition recounted by Herodotus, were founded by women carried away from Thebes by Phoenicians — one sold into Libya who established the Oracle of Ammon, and another into Hellas who established the oracle at Dodona (081). The priestesses of Dodona told a parallel story of two black doves flying from Egyptian Thebes, one to Libya and one to their land. Herodotus rationalizes both accounts as referring to Egyptian temple servants sold into slavery, with the "dove" metaphor arising from their unintelligible barbarian speech. The oracle remained active and well-known in Herodotus's time, visited by Greeks from Cyrene.

## The Oracle's Definition of Egypt

Herodotus cites the Oracle of Ammon as a crucial authority for his own theory about the extent of Egypt. The people of Marea and Apis, dwelling in the borderlands between Egypt and Libya, considered themselves Libyans rather than Egyptians, and resented the Egyptian religious restrictions that forbade them from eating cows' flesh (002-004). They sent envoys to the Oracle of Ammon, arguing that they dwelt outside the Delta and had nothing in common with the Egyptians, and sought permission to eat beef freely. The god, however, refused them, declaring that Egypt was wherever the Nile came over and watered, and that all who dwelt below Elephantine and drank of that river were Egyptians (002-004). This oracular pronouncement confirmed for Herodotus that Egypt was not merely the Delta, as the Ionians maintained, but the entire land watered by the Nile — a definition he himself had independently reached before hearing of the oracle's reply.

## Etearchos and the Nasamonian Expedition

Herodotus relates a story he heard from men of Cyrene who visited the Oracle of Ammon and spoke with Etearchos, king of the Ammonians (051). Etearchos told them of an expedition by young Nasamonian men who journeyed across the Sahara desert, passed through a land of wild beasts and sand, and eventually reached trees bearing fruit. There they were seized by small black-skinned men who took them through great swamps to a city beside a great river flowing west to east with crocodiles — which Etearchos conjectured to be the Nile. This account, transmitted via the Ammonian king, was part of the broader ancient discourse on the unknown sources of the Nile.

## The Ram-Headed Zeus

The Thebans and the Ammonians shared a distinctive iconographic tradition: both peoples represented Zeus with the face of a ram (005-001). Herodotus explains this through a myth in which Zeus, not wishing to be seen by Heracles, flayed a ram, cut off its head, and held the head and fleece before himself before revealing himself. The Thebans therefore held rams sacred and did not sacrifice them — except on one day each year, the feast of Zeus, when they cut up and flayed a single ram, covered the image of Zeus with its skin, and then beat themselves in lamentation for the ram before burying it in a sacred tomb (005-001). The Ammonians, as settlers from both the Egyptians and Ethiopians speaking a mixed language, adopted this same custom from the Thebans. In Herodotus's opinion, the Egyptian name Amun for Zeus derived from this very ram-headed iconography.

## Cambyses and the Lost Army

The Oracle of Ammon appears in one of the most dramatic episodes of the Persian conquest. According to accounts reported by Herodotus, Cambyses, after his conquest of Egypt, dispatched an army of fifty thousand men from Thebes to attack and enslave the Ammonians and burn the Oracle of Zeus (015-013). The army reached the city of Oasis, seven days' march from Thebes across the desert, but after leaving Oasis en route to the land of the Ammonians, the entire force vanished without trace. The Ammonians themselves claimed that a violent south wind buried the Persians under mountains of sand as they were taking their midday meal. Herodotus treats this as the Ammonian account of the disaster, recording it alongside other tales of Cambyses' madness and his sacrilegious attacks on Egyptian religious institutions.

## Relations with Cyrene

The Ammonians' location in the Libyan interior placed them in contact with the Greek colony of Cyrene on the North African coast. The oracle at Ammon served as a destination for Cyrenaean travellers, who in turn relayed the Ammonian king Etearchos's accounts of Saharan exploration to Herodotus (051). This chain of transmission — from Nasamonian explorers to the Ammonian court, from the king to Cyrenaean visitors, and from Cyrenaeans to Herodotus — exemplifies the interconnected information networks linking Egyptian, Libyan, and Greek worlds in the fifth century BCE.
