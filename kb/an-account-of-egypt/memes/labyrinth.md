# Labyrinth

## Overview

The Egyptian Labyrinth, situated near the [[lake-moeris|Lake of Moeris]] and opposite the City of Crocodiles, was one of the most extraordinary monuments [[herodotus|Herodotus]] encountered in [[egypt|Egypt]]. He described it as surpassing all the great works of the [[hellenes|Hellenes]] combined, a judgment based on his personal observation. The Labyrinth was built by the twelve kings who ruled [[egypt|Egypt]] jointly, and its scale and complexity left [[herodotus|Herodotus]] struggling to convey its magnificence in words.

## Description

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] introduces the Labyrinth among [[egypt|Egypt]]'s most famous wonders: "of the pyramids and the great labyrinth; of their kings and queens and courtesans" (001-003), placing it alongside the pyramids as a defining monument.

The Labyrinth was constructed during the joint reign of twelve [[egypt|Egypt]]ian kings who "caused to be made a labyrinth, situated a little above the lake of Moiris and nearly opposite to that which is called the City of Crocodiles" (014-005). The twelve kings had agreed to rule in harmony after an oracle declared that whoever among them poured a libation with a bronze cup in the temple of [[hephaistos|Hephaistos]] would become sole king of [[egypt|Egypt]].

[[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s eyewitness account is emphatic: "This I saw myself, and I found it greater than words can say. For if one should put together and reckon up all the buildings and all the great works produced by [[hellenes|Hellenes]], they would prove to be inferior in labour and in expense to this labyrinth" (014-005). He explicitly compares it to the most famous Greek constructions — the temple at Ephesos and the temple at Samos — and declares the Labyrinth superior even to the pyramids.

The monument's physical context is tied to the [[lake-moeris|Lake of Moeris]]: "a cause for marvel even greater than this is afforded by the lake, which is called the lake of Moiris, along the side of which this labyrinth is built. The measure of its circuit is three thousand six hundred furlongs" (014-006). The proximity of these two enormous works — the labyrinth and the artificial lake — concentrated monumental grandeur at a single site.

## Historical Context

The Egyptian Labyrinth described by [[herodotus|Herodotus]] is generally identified with the mortuary temple complex of Amenemhat III at Hawara, in the Fayum region. The "twelve kings" likely correspond to rulers of the 12th Dynasty (Middle Kingdom, c. 1991–1802 BCE). [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s description, though not architecturally precise by modern standards, captures the overwhelming impression the complex made on a 5th-century BCE visitor — corridors, courts, and chambers on a scale unknown in the Greek world.

## Significance

The Labyrinth passage exemplifies [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s method and his core argument about [[egypt|Egypt]]ian cultural primacy. By declaring the Labyrinth superior to all Greek works combined, he stakes a claim that [[egypt|Egypt]]ian civilization was older, grander, and more accomplished than the Hellenic world of his own day. His insistence on autopsy ("This I saw myself") establishes his authority as an eyewitness, distinguishing his account from hearsay. The Labyrinth thus serves both as a marvel in its own right and as rhetorical evidence for [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s thesis of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian priority.
