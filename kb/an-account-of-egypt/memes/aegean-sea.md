The Aegean Sea was the maritime heartland of the Greek world, the body of water separating the Hellenic mainland from the coast of Asia Minor. In [[Herodotus]]'s account of Egypt, the Aegean appears not as a subject in its own right but as a geographical reference point in the Egyptian priests' version of the Trojan War — specifically the story of [[Alexander]] (Paris) and [[Helen of Troy|Helen]].

## In the Egyptian Account

The most significant mention of the Aegean Sea in Herodotus's Egyptian narrative occurs in the priests' account of Alexander's abduction of Helen. According to the priests of [[Memphis]], Alexander succeeded in carrying Helen away from [[Sparta]] but was prevented from reaching [[Troy]] by contrary winds that "carried him out of his course" across the Aegean into the [[Egypt]]ian Sea. (010-005) He came to shore at the Canobic mouth of the [[Nile River|Nile]], near the city of [[Taricheiai]].

This version of the story — obtained directly from Egyptian priests — contradicts the Homeric account in which Helen reached Troy and the war was fought over her return. In Herodotus's Egyptian version, Helen never reached Troy at all, being detained in Egypt by [[Proteus]] while Alexander was expelled. The Aegean thus serves as the geographical threshold separating the Greek world from the Egyptian — the sea across which winds and fate diverted the Trojan prince onto Egyptian soil.

## Herodotus's Geographical Comparisons

Herodotus also uses the Aegean as a point of comparison for Egyptian geography. Describing the annual inundation of the [[Nile]], he notes that during the flood, when the Nile covers the surrounding land, "the cities alone are seen rising above the water, resembling more nearly than anything else the islands in the Aegean Sea." (009-003) This simile — comparing Egyptian cities during the inundation to Aegean islands — is characteristic of Herodotus's method of making Egyptian phenomena comprehensible to his Greek audience through familiar geographical analogies.

## The Aegean and Egyptian-Greek Relations

The Aegean Sea was the natural maritime corridor connecting Egypt to the Greek world. Throughout Herodotus's account, the sea appears as the passageway for Greeks travelling to Egypt — whether as traders, mercenaries, or exiles — and for stories flowing in the opposite direction. The Nile Delta's proximity to Aegean shipping routes made Egypt accessible to Greek visitors from an early period, facilitating the cultural exchanges and mutual observations that Herodotus documents.
