Canobus was a city situated at the westernmost navigable mouth of the [[Nile River|Nile]], marking the northwestern extremity of the [[Nile Delta]] coastline. [[Herodotus]] identifies the Canobic mouth as one of the five navigable channels of the Nile, and the city served as a primary gateway for [[hellenes|Hellenic]] travellers and traders entering Egypt. (002-003)

## Geography and the Nile Mouths

[[Herodotus]] enumerates seven mouths of the Nile, of which only five — including the Canobic — were navigable. This was a correction to the Ionian tradition, which claimed only three mouths existed. (002-003) The Delta's coastline stretched from the watch-tower of [[Perseus]] in the east to the fish-curing houses of Pelusion in the west, with the Canobic mouth representing the westernmost navigable entry point.

## The Temple of Heracles

At the Canobic mouth stood a temple of [[Heracles]], which [[Herodotus]] notes as a place of refuge. In the Egyptian counter-narrative of the [[Trojan War]], it was to this temple that [[Alexander]] ([[Paris]]) fled with [[Helen of Troy|Helen]] after being driven off course by storms. The Egyptian king [[Proteus]], upon learning that Alexander had abducted Helen — the wife of his host [[Menelaos]] — granted Alexander three days to depart Egypt. When Alexander failed to comply, [[Proteus]] seized Helen and the treasures, banishing Alexander from Egypt. (010-005)

This sanctuary of Heracles at the Canobic mouth thus functioned as a recognised place of asylum — a role attested elsewhere in the Greek world for temples of Heracles, and one that the Egyptian priests cited in their version of the Trojan War story to [[Herodotus]].

## Role as a Maritime Gateway

As the westernmost navigable entrance to Egypt, Canobus was the natural landfall for ships arriving from the [[hellenes|Hellenic]] world, [[Phoenicia]], and the wider [[Mediterranean Sea]]. Its position made it the first point of Egyptian contact for many foreign visitors, and the presence of a major temple there suggests it was a site of considerable religious and diplomatic significance.
