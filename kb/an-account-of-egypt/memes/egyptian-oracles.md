## Overview

Herodotus records several oracular shrines in Egypt, treating them as among the most remarkable religious institutions of the country. Egyptian oracles, like their Hellenic counterparts, were consulted by kings and commoners alike on matters of war, succession, and personal fortune. Herodotus takes particular interest in tracing the Egyptian origins of Greek oracular practice, arguing that both the method and the institution spread from Egypt to Hellas.

## Commentary

Herodotus is explicit in asserting that oracular practice originated in Egypt and spread to Hellas. He recounts the Egyptian priests' story that Phoenician traders abducted two consecrated women from the temple of Zeus at Thebes — one was sold into Libya, where she founded the oracle of Zeus Ammon at Siwa; the other was taken to Dodona in Hellas, where she established the famous oak oracle. Herodotus himself visited Thebes, Dodona, and Siwa to verify these claims, and reports that the methods of divination at Thebes and Dodona were strikingly similar (006-001).

## Sources

> As regards the Oracles both that among the Hellenes and that in Libya, the Egyptians tell the following tale. The priests of the Theban Zeus told me that two women in the service of the temple had been carried away by Phoenicians, and one of them they heard had been taken to Libya and the other to the Hellenes; and these women, they said, were the first founders of the oracular seats among these peoples.
> (006-001)

> This Oracle which is in Egypt is in a great and sacred precinct of Apollo, and the name of the city is Buto; and besides this there is also an island called Chemmis, which is floating in a deep and broad lake near the temple at Buto.
