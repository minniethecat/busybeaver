## Overview

[[Heracles]] holds a dual identity in [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s *Account of [[egypt|Egypt]]*: the historian distinguishes sharply between the **Egyptian Heracles**, an ancient god who was one of the twelve great deities of the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian pantheon, and the **Hellenic Heracles**, the mortal hero and son of Amphitryon known to Greek legend. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] marshals extensive evidence — chronological, genealogical, and cultic — to argue that the name and worship of Heracles originated in [[egypt|Egypt]] and was later adopted by the [[hellenes|Greeks]], rather than the reverse. This argument is one of the most fully developed examples of [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s *interpretatio graeca* in reverse: the [[hellenes|Greeks]], he contends, borrowed the god from [[egypt|Egypt]], not [[egypt|Egypt]] from Greece.

## The Two Heracles

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] records two distinct figures sharing the name Heracles:

- The **Egyptian Heracles** is one of the twelve gods, begotten of the eight primordial gods. The [[egyptian-priesthood|Egyptian priests]] dated his existence to seventeen thousand years before the reign of [[Amasis]] — a chronology that dwarfs the Hellenic Heracles, who lived only a few generations before [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s own time (005-002).

- The **Hellenic Heracles** is the mortal hero of Greek legend, son of Amphitryon and Alcmene. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] notes that the [[hellenes|Greeks]] who worship Heracles both as an immortal Olympian god and as a mortal hero "seem to me to act most rightly" — implicitly recognizing the two distinct traditions (005-002).

The historian reports that he was unable to hear anything in [[egypt|Egypt]] about the Hellenic Heracles: the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians knew only their own ancient god of that name (005-002).

## Chronological Evidence

To establish the priority of the Egyptian Heracles, [[herodotus|Herodotus]] undertook a remarkable comparative inquiry. He traveled to [[Tyre]] in Phoenicia, where there was a temple of Heracles richly adorned with votive offerings, including two pillars — one of pure gold and the other of emerald stone that shone by night. The Tyrian priests told him that their temple was founded with the city itself, 2,300 years before his visit. He also visited [[Thasos]], where a temple of Heracles had been founded by [[phoenicians|Phoenicians]] who had sailed out in search of Europa — "full five generations of men before Heracles the son of Amphitryon was born in Hellas" (005-002). This multi-site investigation — [[egypt|Egypt]], Tyre, Thasos — was [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s method of triangulating the antiquity of the god from independent priestly traditions.

## Genealogical Argument

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] further argues that the parents of the Hellenic Heracles — Amphitryon and Alcmene — were both of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian descent, suggesting that even the Greek hero's lineage points back to [[egypt|Egypt]]. Moreover, he notes that the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians do not know the names of Poseidon or the Dioscuri, nor have they accepted these as gods — yet if the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians had borrowed Heracles from the [[hellenes|Greeks]], they would surely have preserved the names of these other Greek sea-deities, given that the [[hellenes|Greeks]] were a seafaring people (005-002). The absence of Poseidon from the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian pantheon is, for [[herodotus|Herodotus]], evidence that the borrowing went from [[egypt|Egypt]] to Greece, not the reverse.

## The Ram and the Image of Zeus

Heracles appears in one of the most vivid ritual descriptions in the *Account of [[egypt|Egypt]]*: the annual festival of [[zeus|Zeus]] ([[ammon|Amun]]) at [[Thebes]]. On a single day of the year, the Theban priests cut up and flay a ram, cover the image of [[zeus|Zeus]] with its skin, and then bring before it "another image of Heracles." All present beat themselves in lamentation for the ram, which is then buried in a sacred tomb (005-001). This ritual connects Heracles to the central Theban cult — the ram-headed [[ammon|Zeus-Ammon]] — and encodes a narrative in which Heracles desired to see [[zeus|Zeus]], who revealed himself only through the contrivance of the ram's skin and severed head.

## The False Greek Tale

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] explicitly refutes a Greek story that "is especially foolish": that Heracles came to [[egypt|Egypt]] and was led in procession by the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians to be sacrificed to [[zeus|Zeus]], but at the altar he summoned his strength and slew them all. The historian dismisses this on two grounds: first, the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians sacrifice only certain pure animals — swine, clean male oxen, calves, and geese — and it is not lawful for them to sacrifice even ordinary beasts beyond these, let alone human beings. Second, how could Heracles, "being one person only and moreover a man," slay many myriads? (005-003). The tale is, for [[herodotus|Herodotus]], evidence of Greek ignorance of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian customs rather than any authentic tradition.

## Significance

[[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s treatment of Heracles exemplifies his broader historical method: the comparison of independent priestly traditions across [[egypt|Egypt]], Phoenicia, and Greece to establish chronological priority, combined with critical scrutiny of implausible Greek legends. The argument that Heracles is fundamentally an [[egypt|Egypt]]ian god — absorbed into Greek religion as both an immortal deity and a mortal hero — is one of the most fully developed examples of [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s thesis that Greek religion owes a profound debt to [[egypt|Egypt]].
