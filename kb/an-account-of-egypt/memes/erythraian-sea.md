## Overview

The [[Erythraian Sea]] (the modern [[Red Sea]], also called the [[Arabian Gulf]] by [[Herodotus]]) was the great eastern maritime frontier of [[Egypt]]. Herodotus reports that [[Sesostris]] was the first Egyptian king to launch warships on this sea, subduing the coastal peoples until shoals prevented further navigation (009-006). Centuries later, [[Necos]] attempted to connect the [[Nile River|Nile]] to the Erythraian Sea with a canal — a project completed only under the [[Persian]] king [[Dareios]] (015-004). The Erythraian Sea thus represents the eastern limit of Egyptian naval ambition and a crucial link in the trade routes connecting Egypt to [[Arabia]], [[India]], and the lands beyond.

## Location / Geography

The Erythraian Sea is the narrow body of water separating [[Arabia]] from [[Africa]], extending from the [[Gulf of Suez]] in the north to the [[Gulf of Aden]] in the south. Herodotus correctly identifies it as the destination of the canal dug from the Nile near modern [[Ismailia]], running through the [[Wadi Tumilat]] to the Bitter Lakes and from there to the Gulf of Suez. The canal's path crossed the [[Nile Delta]]'s eastern edge and the [[Eastern Desert]] — a formidable engineering challenge that required the labour of tens of thousands of Egyptian workers.

| Human cost | 120,000 Egyptians died during its excavation |
| Interruption | An oracle warned Necos he was "working for the barbarian" |

Herodotus describes the human cost of the canal with characteristic precision: "In the cutting of this canal there perished 120,000 Egyptians" (015-004). The oracle that interrupted Necos's work — warning that he was toiling for a foreign power — proved prophetic when the Persian Dareios later completed the project, integrating Egypt's maritime infrastructure into the [[Achaemenid]] imperial system.

## Historical Significance

### Sesostris's Naval Expedition

Herodotus identifies Sesostris as the pioneer of Egyptian seafaring on the Erythraian Sea. After his land conquests across Asia, Sesostris returned to Egypt and launched warships on the Arabian Gulf, subduing the coastal tribes until he reached waters rendered impassable by shoals (009-006). This expedition marks the first recorded instance of Egyptian naval power extending beyond the Nile into open sea — an achievement that Herodotus contrasts with the limitations imposed by geography on later Egyptian kings.

### The Erythraian Sea in Persian Strategy

After Cambyses's conquest of Egypt in 525 BCE, the Erythraian Sea became part of the Persian imperial network. Dareios's completion of the Necos canal linked the Mediterranean and the Erythraian Sea, enabling Persian naval movements between the two bodies of water without circumnavigating Africa. This canal, and the Erythraian Sea it accessed, formed part of the infrastructure that sustained Persian rule over Egypt and projected Persian power toward Arabia.

### Ethnographic Dimensions

Herodotus also mentions the Erythraian Sea in connection with two ethnographic observations. First, the [[Phoenicians]] and [[Syrians of Palestine]] claimed that their ancestors originated from the Erythraian Sea region (006-007). Second, during the reign of Necos, Phoenician sailors commissioned by the pharaoh circumnavigated Africa, departing from the Erythraian Sea and returning via the [[Pillars of Heracles]] (the [[Strait of Gibraltar]]) after a voyage of more than two years (006-006). This extraordinary circumnavigation — which Herodotus reports with cautious credulity — attests to the Erythraian Sea's role as a gateway not merely to Arabia but to the broader maritime world.

## Sources

> He (Sesostris) first of all set out with ships of war from the Arabian gulf and subdued those who dwelt by the shores of the Erythraian Sea, until as he sailed he came to a sea which could no further be navigated by reason of shoals.
> (009-006)

> This man (Necos) was the first who attempted the channel leading to the Erythraian Sea, which Dareios the Persian afterwards completed. The length of this channel is a voyage of four days, and in breadth it was so dug that two triremes might be rowed through it moving side by side; and its course is from the Nile to the bitter lakes and from there to the Erythraian Sea.
> (015-004)

> The Phoenicians and the Syrians of Palestine acknowledge that they learnt the custom of circumcision from the Egyptians; but the Syrians about the river Thermodon and the river Parthenios, and the Macronians, who are their neighbours, say that they have learnt it lately from the Colchians. These are the only races of men who practise circumcision, and these evidently practise it in the same manner as the Egyptians. Of the Egyptians themselves however and the Ethiopians, I am not able to say which of them learnt it of the other, for it is clearly a custom of very great antiquity; but that the others learnt it by means of their intercourse with Egypt I think is plainly to be seen from this fact.
> (009-003)
