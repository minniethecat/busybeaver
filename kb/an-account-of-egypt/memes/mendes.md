## Overview

[[Mendes]] was a city and district of the [[Nile Delta]], notable in Herodotus's *Account of Egypt* for its distinctive religious practice: the Mendesians abstained from goats and sacrificed sheep — the exact opposite of the [[Thebes|Thebans]], who sacrificed goats and abstained from sheep. The city was the cult center of the ram-god whom Herodotus identifies with the Greek Pan, and was also the namesake of one of the Nile's seven mouths — the Mendesian mouth.

## Religious Distinctiveness

Herodotus uses Mendes as a key example of Egypt's regional religious variation. While all Egyptians worshipped [[Isis and Osiris]] without exception, other cults were local: "they who have a temple of Mendes or belong to the Mendesian district, these abstain from goats and sacrifice sheep" — a practice exactly contrary to that of the Thebans and the Theban district (005-001). The Mendesians revered Pan (the ram-god) above all other deities, and their prohibition on goat sacrifice distinguished them from most other Egyptian cult centers.

## The Mendesian Mouth

The Mendesian mouth of the [[Nile River|Nile]] was one of the five navigable distributaries through which the river emptied into the [[Mediterranean Sea]]. It was the second-easternmost of the seven mouths enumerated by Herodotus, lying between the Pelusiac mouth to the east and the Sebennytic mouth to the west (002-003). As with other major mouths, it served as a gateway for trade and travel, associated with the city whose name it bore.

## Significance

Mendes exemplifies Herodotus's attention to the regional variation of Egyptian religion. Rather than presenting Egypt as religiously uniform, he carefully records which cities and districts revered which gods and observed which dietary prohibitions. Mendes, with its reversed sacrificial practice and its devotion to Pan, serves as a counterpoint to Thebes — the two cities together illustrating that Egypt was a mosaic of local cults united only by the universal worship of Isis and Osiris.
