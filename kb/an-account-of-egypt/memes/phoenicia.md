## Overview

Phoenicia (Phenicia) was the coastal region of the Levant, home to a seafaring people whose cities — notably Tyre and Sidon — appear repeatedly in Herodotus''s account of Egypt. The Phoenicians are portrayed as traders, colonists, and intermediaries between Egypt and the Greek world. Their presence is woven through the narrative at Memphis, in tales of temple origins, in the story of Helen, and in the circumcision customs of the eastern Mediterranean. (005-005) (006-001) (010-001) (011-001)

## Phoenicians in Egyptian Narrative

The Phoenicians appear in multiple contexts throughout Herodotus''s Egyptian account. At Memphis, a community of Tyrian Phoenicians dwelled around the sacred enclosure of Proteus, living in a district known as the Camp of the Tyrians. Within this enclosure stood the temple of the "foreign Aphrodite," which Herodotus conjectured was dedicated to Helen. (010-001)

The Egyptian priests also told how Phoenicians had carried away two consecrated women from the temple of Zeus at Thebes, selling one into Libya and the other into Hellas. These women, according to the priests, became the founders of the prophetic sanctuaries at Dodona in Greece and the Oracle of Ammon in Libya. Herodotus offers his own rationalized interpretation of this tale, suggesting the women were called "doves" because of their foreign speech, and that the "black dove" indicated an Egyptian origin. (006-001)

## Trade and Cultural Exchange

The Phoenicians were key agents of cultural transmission in Herodotus''s world. He reports that the practice of circumcision was learned from the Egyptians by the Phoenicians and the Syrians of Palestine, who then transmitted it further. Those Phoenicians who had regular contact with Hellas, however, ceased to follow Egyptian custom in this matter. (005-005)

Herodotus himself traveled to Tyre to verify accounts of Heracles, visiting a richly furnished temple there. The priests of Tyre told him the temple was as old as the city itself — some two thousand three hundred years. Phoenician colonists had also established a temple of Heracles on the island of Thasos, a foundation that Herodotus dates to five generations before Heracles the son of Amphitryon was born in Greece. (005-005)

## The Sidon Connection

In discussing the story of Helen, Herodotus references Homer's knowledge that Alexander (Paris) visited Sidon during his wanderings. He quotes the Homeric verses describing the "robes many-coloured, the works of women of Sidon" that Alexander carried away. Herodotus argues that Homer's mention of Sidon proves the poet knew of Alexander's Egyptian sojourn, since Sidon is a Phoenician city in Syria, which borders upon Egypt. (011-001)
