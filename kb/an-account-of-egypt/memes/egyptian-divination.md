## Overview

Divination in ancient Egypt, as described by [[Herodotus]], was conducted through a network of oracles dedicated to various deities. Unlike in the Greek world, where divination was practiced by individual seers and prophets, the Egyptians assigned the art of prophecy not to any man but to certain of the gods. Herodotus lists oracles of Heracles, Apollo, Athene, Artemis, Ares, and Zeus, but held in highest honour among them all the [[Oracle of Buto|Oracle of Leto]] in the city of [[Buto]]. (008-001) The manner of divination varied from place to place, and oracles were intimately woven into Egyptian royal authority, legal judgment, and personal affairs.

## Description

### The Origin of Oracles

The priests of Theban Zeus related to Herodotus an account of how oracles spread from Egypt to the wider world. Two consecrated priestesses from the temple of Zeus at Thebes were carried away by Phoenician traders. One was sold into Libya, where she founded the Oracle of Ammon; the other was taken to the land of the Thesprotians in what was then called Pelasgia (later Hellas) and established the Oracle of Zeus at Dodona. (006-001)

Herodotus offered his own rationalizing version of this story: the woman sold into Hellas, being a former attendant of the sanctuary of Zeus at Thebes, naturally established a sanctuary of Zeus under an oak tree in her new home; when she learned the Hellenic tongue, she established an oracle. The dove that the Dodonaeans spoke of was, Herodotus suggested, simply the woman herself — her speech sounding to them like the twittering of a bird until she mastered their language. The dove that flew to Libya, in turn, was her sister, who commanded the Libyans to establish an Oracle of Ammon. (006-001)

### The Oracle of Buto

The most revered prophetic site in Egypt was the Oracle of Leto at [[Buto]], which Herodotus described as "the most truthful Oracle" of the Egyptians. (014-008) The temple was situated in a great city near the Sebennytic mouth of the [[Nile River|Nile]], as one sailed up from the sea. Within its precinct stood a temple-house of Leto — both great in scale and entered through a gateway ten fathoms in height. Also within Buto were temples of Apollo and Artemis. (015-001)

The oracle at Buto figured prominently in royal narratives. When the [[The Twelve Kings|twelve kings]] who jointly ruled Egypt received an oracle that whichever of them poured a libation from a bronze cup in the temple of [[Hephaistos]] would become sole ruler, [[Psammetichos]] inadvertently fulfilled the prophecy by using his bronze helmet as a libation vessel during a sacrifice. The other kings, remembering the oracle, drove him into the fen-country. Psammetichos then consulted the Oracle of Buto, which gave him the cryptic reply that "vengeance would come when men of bronze appeared from the sea." This prophecy was fulfilled when Ionian and Carian raiders, clad in bronze armor, were forced ashore by a storm — Psammetichos enlisted them as allies and reclaimed his throne. (014-005) (014-008)

### Oracles and Royal Judgment

Divination also figured in narratives of royal fate. King [[Mycerinus]], after learning from the Oracle of Buto that he had only six years to live, protested to the god, arguing that his impious predecessors Cheops and Chephren had lived long while he, a pious ruler, was to die soon. The oracle replied that this was precisely why his life was shortened: Egypt was destined to suffer evils for one hundred and fifty years, and the two previous kings had understood this, but Mycerinus had not. In response, Mycerinus devised a stratagem to prove the oracle false — he had lamps made and spent his nights feasting by lamp-light, thus turning nights into days and effectively living twelve years instead of six. (012-005)

A more skeptical relationship with oracles is recorded of King [[Amasis]], who before becoming king had been a thief. When accused of stealing, he was brought before local oracles; sometimes convicted, sometimes absolved. Upon becoming king, Amasis systematically neglected the temples of those gods whose oracles had absolved him, "considering them to be worth nothing and to possess lying Oracles," while lavishly adorning the temples of those whose oracles had convicted him, "considering them to be truly gods, and to present Oracles which did not lie." This episode reveals that even a king's personal experience of oracles could shape royal patronage and temple funding. (016-003)

### Divination by Dreams

Dreams served as an important channel of divine communication in Egyptian belief. The priests recorded that the Ethiopian king who had conquered Egypt ultimately fled the land after receiving a vision in his sleep in which a man stood beside him and counselled him to gather together all the priests of Egypt and cut every one of them in two. Interpreting this vision, the Ethiopian king understood that the gods were presenting him with a pretext for his own destruction — for the priests, as the servants of the gods, would invoke divine retribution upon him if he harmed them. Rather than obey the dream's apparent command, he chose instead to withdraw from Egypt, restoring native rule. (013-006)

### The Egyptian Claim to Oracular Primacy

Herodotus's account makes clear that the Egyptians regarded themselves as the originators of divination. The historian records that Melampus, the legendary Greek seer, "being a man of ability contrived for himself an art of divination, and having learnt from Egypt he taught the Hellenes many things." (005-005) Similarly, the Pelasgians — the pre-Hellenic inhabitants of Greece — consulted the Oracle at Dodona, which the Egyptians claimed as their daughter-foundation, when deciding whether to adopt the Egyptian-derived names of the gods. (005-006)

## Herodotus's Commentary

Herodotus's treatment of Egyptian divination reveals his characteristic method of rational critique applied to religious traditions. He neither dismisses the Egyptian claims to oracular primacy nor accepts them uncritically. Instead, he offers a demythologized account — the dove at Dodona was a foreign woman speaking an unintelligible language — while preserving the Egyptian priests' own narrative alongside it. His willingness to present both the theological account (priestesses carried away by Phoenicians) and his own rationalizing alternative (the "dove" was a human woman) exemplifies the dual perspective — ethnographic respect combined with Greek rationalism — that defines his entire Egyptian narrative. The detailed stories of oracles shaping royal destinies (Psammetichos, Mycerinus, Amasis) suggest that Herodotus recognized divination not as superstition but as a genuine mechanism of political causation in Egyptian history.

## Significance

Egyptian divination, as Herodotus presents it, was not a marginal or superstitious practice but a central institution of political and religious life. Oracles determined royal succession, foretold the duration of reigns, and shaped the distribution of royal patronage. The Egyptian claim to have exported divination to both Libya and Hellas positioned Egypt as the fountainhead of prophetic knowledge in the Mediterranean world — a claim Herodotus treated with more respect than modern readers might expect, given his own rationalizing tendencies. Dream interpretation, meanwhile, provided a channel through which even foreign rulers (the Ethiopian king) could receive messages from Egyptian gods, revealing Egypt's influence extending beyond its political borders.
