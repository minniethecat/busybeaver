## Overview

Chemnis (also called Chemmis) was a floating island in a lake near the temple of Leto at Buto. According to Egyptian tradition, it was here that the goddess Leto concealed the infant Apollo to protect him from Typhon. Herodotus reports having visited the site himself and was told by the Egyptians that the island had originally been fixed in place but was rendered floating by divine power. (015-002) He records, however, that he never personally witnessed the island moving.

## Location / Geography

The island of Chemnis was located in a broad, deep lake near the temple of Leto at Buto in the western Nile Delta. Herodotus describes it as covered with palm trees and other vegetation. The temple complex at Buto, which included the sacred enclosure of Leto, was situated near the Sebennytic mouth of the Nile, accessible by sailing upriver from the sea. (015-001)

## The Temple and Its Marvels

Within the island of Chemnis stood a great temple-house of Apollo with three altars set up inside, surrounded by many palm trees and other trees, both fruit-bearing and not. (015-001) The adjacent temple complex at Buto housed the Oracle of Leto, which Herodotus considered "worthy to be described." The temple-house of Leto itself featured a gateway of extraordinary height — ten fathoms — and contained, within the sacred enclosure, a house of Leto "made of one single stone upon the top, the cornice measuring four cubits." Herodotus declared this monolithic structure "the most marvellous" of all the sights he beheld in that temple, second only to the floating island itself. (015-001)

## The Myth of the Floating Island

The Egyptians told Herodotus that the island of Chemnis was originally fixed in place and only later became a floating island — a transformation tied directly to the myth of the divine child Apollo. (015-002) According to the priests, Leto, one of the eight primordial gods and resident of Buto where her Oracle stood, received the infant Apollo from Isis as a sacred charge and concealed him on the island to protect him from Typhon, who "came after him seeking everywhere and desiring to find the son of Osiris." (015-002) It was through this act of concealment and divine protection that the island was rendered floating.

The Egyptian tradition further identified the figures of the myth with a different genealogy than the Greek version: Apollo was equated with the Egyptian Horus, Artemis with Bubastis, and Demeter with Isis. In this Egyptian scheme, Apollo and Artemis were said to be children of Dionysos and Isis, with Leto serving as their nurse and preserver rather than their mother. (015-002) Herodotus notes that the Greek tragedian Aeschylus, son of Euphorion, drew upon this very Egyptian tradition when he represented Artemis as the daughter of Demeter — a departure from all preceding Greek poets. (015-002)

## Herodotus's Visit

Herodotus visited Chemnis personally during his travels in Egypt, inspecting the temple complex and the island. He expresses frank wonder at the claim that an island could float: "I myself did not see it either floating about or moved from its place, and I feel surprise at hearing of it, wondering if it be indeed a floating island." (015-001) This passage is characteristic of Herodotus's ethnographic method — he reports what he is told by local informants while simultaneously recording his own skepticism.
