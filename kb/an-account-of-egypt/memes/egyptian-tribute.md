## Overview

Herodotus's account of Egypt contains scattered but informative references to the economic mechanisms by which Egyptian kings extracted wealth from their territory and from conquered lands. While the Histories does not provide a systematic description of Egyptian taxation, several passages illuminate aspects of the tribute system: the redistribution of land, the revenues from fisheries, the wealth accumulated by kings, and the tribute levied on subject territories. (010-002)

## Land Distribution and Royal Revenue

One of the most important economic institutions Herodotus attributes to Egypt was the distribution of land. According to the priests, King Sesostris (identified by scholars with one of the Middle Kingdom rulers) divided the land of Egypt among all the inhabitants, giving each man an equal square portion. From these allotments the king drew his revenues, requiring each holder to pay a fixed annual rent. This system, the priests explained, was the origin of geometry in Egypt: when the Nile flood swept away portions of a man's land, surveyors were sent to measure and reassess the holding, and the tax was adjusted accordingly. (010-002)

Additional royal revenues came from natural resources. The fishery of Lake Moiris, Herodotus notes, contributed a talent of silver daily to the royal treasury during the six months when the water flowed into the lake, and twenty minae daily during the six months when it flowed out — funds that were used for the queen's personal expenses, including her perfumes and sandals. (014-007)

## Tribute from Conquered Territories

Beyond internal revenues, Egyptian kings exacted tribute from territories brought under their sway. Herodotus records that Amasis (Ahmose II) conquered Cyprus and subdued it, so that the island paid him tribute. This is the only explicit reference to external tribute in the Egyptian portion of the Histories, though the broader narrative of Egyptian military campaigns against Syria, Ethiopia, and other regions implies that tribute extraction was a standard feature of imperial conquest. (017-002)

## The Wealth of Kings

The kings' treasuries grew not only from regular tribute but also from extraordinary measures. Rhampsinitos, Herodotus relates, amassed a great wealth of silver beyond what any later king could match. He was said to have descended alive into the underworld, where he played dice with Demeter, and returned with a golden napkin as a gift from the goddess. The story, fabulous as it is, reflects the Egyptian association between royal wealth and divine favour. (011-007)

## Significance

The tribute and taxation practices glimpsed in Herodotus reveal several features of the Egyptian economic order. Land was the fundamental source of wealth, and the king's claim on it was direct and universal — every landholder owed rent to the crown. This system required an administrative apparatus capable of surveying, measuring, and reassessing land after each inundation. The tribute from conquered territories, while mentioned only briefly, suggests that Egypt's imperial reach extended its fiscal base beyond the Nile Valley. Together, these mechanisms sustained a monarchy that Herodotus regarded as fabulously wealthy.
