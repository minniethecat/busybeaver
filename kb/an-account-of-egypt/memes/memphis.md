## Overview

Memphis was the ancient capital of Egypt, situated at the strategic apex of the Nile Delta where the river divides into its branches. In Herodotus's *Account of Egypt*, Memphis appears as the political, religious, and architectural heart of the kingdom — the seat of kings from Menes onward, the site of the great temple of Hephaistos (Ptah), and the city where Herodotus himself gathered much of his information from the Egyptian priests. (009-003)

## Location / Geography

Memphis occupied a position of unique geographic and strategic importance at the meeting-point of Upper and Lower Egypt, approximately fifteen miles south of modern Cairo. According to the Egyptian priests, the city's location was established by Menes, the first king of Egypt, who diverted the Nile's course with a dam to create a dry plain for the new capital. (009-004) Herodotus describes the city as situated on the western bank of the Nile in a narrow plain, protected by the river on one side and the desert on the other. During the annual inundation, when the Nile covered the surrounding land, the cities alone appeared rising above the water, resembling — in Herodotus's memorable comparison — the islands of the Aegean Sea. (009-003)

## Historical Significance

Memphis was the traditional capital of Egypt from the unification of the Two Lands under Menes until the rise of Thebes in the New Kingdom, and it retained its religious and administrative importance throughout Egyptian history. Every major king mentioned in Herodotus's narrative is associated with Memphis in some capacity: it was here that Proteus ruled and kept his sacred enclosure, where Rhampsinitos built the western gateway of Hephaistos's temple, where Asychis constructed the largest and most beautiful temple gateway facing the sunrise, and where Psammetichos erected the southern gateway. (010-004) (011-007) (013-003) (014-008)

The temple of Hephaistos at Memphis was one of the most important religious sites in Egypt and the primary locus of the priestly tradition that Herodotus consulted. It was before this temple that Psammetichos, standing last among the twelve kings, made the fateful bronze-helmet libation that fulfilled the oracle and set him on the path to sole rule. (014-008) The temple also housed colossal statues erected by Sesostris — two of thirty cubits for himself and his wife, four of twenty cubits for his sons — which were still standing when Herodotus visited. (010-002)

## Herodotus's Description

Herodotus's description of Memphis and its monuments is woven throughout the *Account of Egypt* rather than concentrated in a single passage. He reports having personally visited the city and spoken with the priests of Hephaistos on multiple occasions. His account of the city includes the temple of Hephaistos and its statues, the sacred enclosure of Proteus, the temple of the "foreign Aphrodite" (which he conjectured to be a temple of Helen), the royal palace at Sais (near Memphis), and numerous other architectural landmarks. (010-004) (014-008)

The historian also records local customs associated with Memphis, including the annual festival of the god whom the Egyptians called "the great god of Memphis," and the presence of a Phoenician community dwelling in a quarter called the Camp of the Tyrians. (010-004)
