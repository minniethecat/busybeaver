# Delphi

Delphi is the celebrated sanctuary of Apollo in central Greece, home to the most famous oracle of the ancient Greek world.

Herodotus refers to Delphi several times in his "Account of Egypt," primarily in contexts that connect Egypt with the broader Greek world.

## Amasis and the Temple of Delphi

One of the most notable Egyptian connections to Delphi concerns Pharaoh Amasis (Ahmose II). Herodotus records that when the temple at Delphi was destroyed by fire, the Amphictyons contracted for a new temple to be built at a cost of three hundred talents, with the Delphians themselves responsible for one quarter of the payment. (251)

The Delphians travelled to various cities collecting contributions, and "they got from Egypt as much as from any place." Amasis gave them a thousand talents' weight of alum, while the Hellenes dwelling in Egypt contributed twenty pounds of silver. (251)

## Rhodopis's Dedication

Herodotus tells the story of Rhodopis, a Thracian courtesan who gained her freedom and great wealth in Egypt. With a tithe of her wealth, she commissioned a remarkable dedication for Delphi: iron spits large enough to pierce a whole ox.

These spits were still visible at Delphi in Herodotus's time, "heaped all together behind the altar which the Chians dedicated, and just opposite to the cell of the temple." (181) Herodotus uses this detail to refute the claim that Rhodopis could have afforded to build a pyramid.

## The Death of Aesop

Delphi also appears in Herodotus's narrative in connection with the death of the fable-maker Aesop. Herodotus notes that the Delphians repeatedly made proclamations, in accordance with an oracle, seeking someone to accept blood-money for Aesop's death. Eventually, the grandson of Iadmon (Aesop's former master) came forward to claim it. (179)
