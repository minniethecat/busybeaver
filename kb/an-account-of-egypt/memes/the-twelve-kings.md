## Overview

The Twelve Kings were a council of rulers who governed Egypt simultaneously after the Egyptians, freed from the reign of the priest of [[Hephaistos]], divided the land into twelve parts. Their shared rule was governed by mutual agreements and ultimately ended through a prophecy fulfilled by [[Psammetichos]].

## Description

### Division of Egypt

After the Egyptians were set free from the reign of the priest of Hephaistos, they found themselves unable to live without a king. They therefore established twelve kings, dividing all Egypt into twelve parts. These kings made intermarriages with one another and reigned together. (014-004)

### The Oracle and the Bronze Cup

The twelve kings made firm agreements that none would seek to depose the others by force or gain an advantage over one another. Their motivation was an oracle given at the beginning of their rule: whichever king poured a libation with a bronze cup in the temple of [[Hephaistos]] would rule over all Egypt. This prophecy drove them to guard their mutual agreements with great vigilance. (014-005)

### The Prophecy Fulfilled

The twelve kings continued to rule justly, but during a sacrifice at the temple of [[Hephaistos]], on the last day of a feast, the chief-priest mistakenly brought out only eleven golden cups for the twelve kings. [[Psammetichos]], standing last in order, found himself without a cup and poured his libation using his bronze helmet instead — unwittingly fulfilling the oracle. (014-008)

## Significance

The story of the Twelve Kings illustrates the Egyptian belief in oracular prophecy and the political structures that emerged in times of distributed power. The period of the Dodecarchy represents a unique episode in Egyptian governance, demonstrating how shared rule could maintain stability through formal agreements while ultimately yielding to divinely ordained kingship.

## Sources

> Being set free after the reign of the priest of Hephaistos, the Egyptians, since they could not live any time without a king, set up over them twelve kings, having divided all Egypt into twelve parts. (014-004)

> An oracle had been given to them at first when they began to exercise their rule, that he of them who should pour a libation with a bronze cup in the temple of Hephaistos, should be sole ruler of all Egypt. (014-005)

> The chief-priest, in bringing out for them the golden cups with which they had been wont to pour libations, missed his reckoning and brought eleven only for the twelve kings. Then that one of them who was standing last in order, namely Psammetichos, since he had no cup took off from his head his helmet, which was of bronze, and poured libation. (014-008)
