## Overview

Heliopolis (Egyptian: Iunu, Hebrew: On) was the principal cult centre of the sun-god Ra (Re) in ancient Egypt. Located northeast of Memphis, it was one of the oldest and most intellectually distinguished cities in Egypt. Herodotus mentions Heliopolis several times as a source of priestly knowledge, noting that the Heliopolitan priests were regarded as the most learned of all Egyptians. (002-001) (005-007)

## Location / Geography

Heliopolis was situated approximately ten miles northeast of Memphis, on the eastern bank of the Nile near the apex of the Delta. Its location placed it at the geographic and symbolic heart of the unified kingdom. The city stood on the route from Memphis to the eastern frontier at Pelusion.

## Historical Significance

Heliopolis was the intellectual centre of Egyptian religion. Its priesthood was credited with preserving the deepest knowledge of the gods, the calendar, and the history of the nation. Herodotus reports that the priests of Heliopolis were the most learned in antiquities among all the Egyptians and were consulted on matters of theology and chronology. The city was also the cult centre of the sacred bull Mnevis, one of the three great animal cults of Egypt (alongside Apis at Memphis and the Mendesian goat).

## Herodotus's Description

Herodotus obtained information from the Heliopolitan priests, whom he consulted alongside those of Memphis and Thebes. He records that the temple of the Sun at Heliopolis contained records of Egyptian chronology and was a repository of sacred learning. (002-001) (005-007) He also notes that the festival calendar, assigning each month and day to a particular deity, was a discovery of the Heliopolitan priesthood. (008-001)
