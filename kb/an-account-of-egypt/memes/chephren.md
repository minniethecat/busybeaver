## Overview

[[Chephren]] (Khafre) was an [[egypt|Egypt]]ian king of the Fourth Dynasty, the brother and successor of [[Cheops]] ([[cheops|Khufu]]). In [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s *Account of [[egypt|Egypt]]*, Chephren appears in the narrative of the pyramid-building kings — a dynasty the historian portrays as oppressive, characterized by temple closures and forced labour. His pyramid, the second of the three great monuments on the Giza plateau, is described by [[herodotus|Herodotus]] as smaller than his brother's but still a work of immense scale.

## The Pyramid

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] records that Chephren "left a pyramid, as [[cheops|Cheops]] did also; but Chephren's pyramid, as I found by measurement, is not so great as that of [[cheops|Cheops]]" (012-003). The historian's comparative attention to the dimensions of the two pyramids reflects his method of verification by autopsy — he personally measured the monuments and recorded their relative sizes.

Like his brother [[cheops|Cheops]], Chephren is depicted by [[herodotus|Herodotus]] as a tyrannical ruler. The [[egyptian-priesthood|Egyptian priests]] told him that during the reigns of [[cheops|Cheops]] and Chephren, the temples were closed and the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians were compelled to labour on the pyramids — a period of oppression said to have lasted 106 years (012-002). The [[egypt|Egypt]]ians, according to [[herodotus|Herodotus]], so detested these two kings that they were unwilling even to speak their names, referring to the pyramids as belonging to "the shepherd Philitis" who pastured his flocks on the plateau during their construction.

## The Succession

After Chephren's death, the throne passed to [[Mykerinos]] ([[mykerinos|Menkaure]]), the son of [[cheops|Cheops]] — not Chephren's own son but his nephew. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] presents [[mykerinos|Mykerinos]] as a ruler of opposite character to his uncle and father: where [[cheops|Cheops]] and Chephren were remembered as tyrants, [[mykerinos|Mykerinos]] reopened the temples, delivered justice, and was "the most righteous of all the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian kings."

## Significance

Chephren appears in [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s narrative as part of a moral diptych — the two "wicked" pyramid builders ([[cheops|Cheops]] and Chephren) contrasted with the "righteous" [[mykerinos|Mykerinos]]. The historian's moral framing of the Fourth Dynasty reflects the priestly traditions he recorded at [[memphis|Memphis]], which preserved the memory of the pyramid age as a time of national suffering and impiety. Chephren's pyramid — the enduring physical monument — stands in [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s account as a silent witness to the human cost of royal ambition.
