Horus (Egyptian Hor, identified by [[Herodotus]] as Apollo) was one of the most important deities of the Egyptian pantheon. Herodotus refers to him primarily through his Greek syncretic identification — Horus is the god whom "the Egyptians call Apollo" — and records his principal oracle and sanctuary in Egypt.

## Herodotus's Identification

Herodotus follows his consistent practice of identifying Egyptian deities with their Hellenic counterparts, equating Horus with [[Apollo]]. This identification is part of a broader system: [[Isis]] is Demeter, [[Osiris]] is Dionysos, and Horus/Apollo is the son of Isis and Osiris in the Egyptian divine family. Herodotus reports that Egyptians of the Theban district abstained from sheep and sacrificed goats, while the Mendesians did the reverse — and that these regional variations were linked to the differing local cults of the major gods. (005-001)

## The Oracle at Buto

The principal sanctuary of Horus/Apollo in Herodotus's account was at [[Buto]] in the [[Nile Delta]]. Herodotus describes it as a great and sacred precinct, notable for containing the floating island of [[Chemmis (Island)|Chemmis]] — a body of water with a deep, broad lake where the goddess [[Leto]] was said to have hidden the infant Apollo from the pursuit of Typhon. (015-001) This oracle at Buto was, according to Herodotus, one of the most revered prophetic seats in Egypt.

## Horus in Egyptian Religion

Though Herodotus's account of Horus is relatively brief — filtered as it is through the Greek lens of Apollo — the god Horus was central to Egyptian kingship and theology. The living pharaoh was identified as the earthly manifestation of Horus, and the myth of Horus's triumph over Seth (commemorated in various Egyptian rituals) was a foundational narrative of order prevailing over chaos. Herodotus's identification of Horus with Apollo captures one aspect of this complex deity — his association with light, prophecy, and youthful divine power — while necessarily simplifying the rich Egyptian theological tradition.

## Significance in Herodotus's Account

Horus/Apollo appears in Herodotus's Egyptian narrative as a member of the divine family worshipped by all Egyptians, and his oracle at Buto features in the stories of Egyptian kingship that Herodotus collected from the priests. The syncretic identification with Apollo also serves Herodotus's broader argument about the Egyptian origins of Greek religion: if Horus is Apollo under an Egyptian name, the Greek god has an Egyptian ancestry.
