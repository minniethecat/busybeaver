## Overview

Sais was a major city in the western Nile Delta and the ancestral home of the 26th (Saite) Dynasty. It is prominent in Herodotus's *Account of Egypt* as the city where the pharaohs Psammetichos, Necos, Apries, and Amasis built their greatest monuments. The temple of Athene (Egyptian: Neith) at Sais was among the most important religious sites in Egypt, and the tombs of the Saite kings — including the remarkable burial of Amasis — were located within its precincts. (016-003) (016-004)

## Location / Geography

Sais (Egyptian: Sau, modern Sa el-Hagar) was situated in the western Delta on the Rosetta branch of the Nile, at approximately the fifth nome of Lower Egypt. Herodotus places it in the Delta region and distinguishes it from Memphis, the older capital to the south. The city lay close enough to the Mediterranean to maintain contact with the Greek world — Naucratis, the Greek trading port, was founded nearby under Amasis — yet sufficiently inland to serve as a secure administrative centre for the Saite pharaohs. (016-005)

## Historical Significance

Sais rose to prominence as the seat of the 26th Dynasty, founded by Psammetichos I following the overthrow of the twelve kings. Under this dynasty, Egypt experienced a renaissance of art, architecture, and international engagement. Every Saite king contributed to the city's monuments. Psammetichos built the southern gateway of the temple of Hephaistos at Memphis but was deeply associated with Sais as his home district. (014-008) Amasis, himself of Saite origin, constructed a magnificent temple-gateway for Athene at Sais — a structure that Herodotus describes with particular admiration for its size, height, and the quality of its stone. (016-003)

The royal tombs at Sais were of special interest to Herodotus. The tomb of Amasis, situated within the sacred enclosure of Athene, was described in detail. He also records the sepulchre of Apries (whom the Hellenes called Hophra) and the tomb of Osiris, which stood behind the temple of Athene and was the site of a mystery-cult. (016-003)

## Herodotus's Description

Herodotus's description of Sais includes architectural observations (the temple-gateway of Amasis, the monolithic chamber transported from Elephantine), cultural details (the festival of lamps celebrated at Sais and throughout Egypt), and the historian's characteristically precise accounts of the royal tombs. He notes the presence of colossal statues, man-headed sphinxes, and other monuments that made the temple of Athene at Sais a site of exceptional splendour. (016-003)
