## Overview

In [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s *Account of [[egypt|Egypt]]*, the [[Ionians]] feature as a Greek people whose opinions about [[egypt|Egypt]]ian geography the historian frequently criticizes, yet who also serve as crucial intermediaries between [[egypt|Egypt]] and the Hellenic world. The Ionians appear as traders operating from [[Naucratis]], as mercenaries in [[egypt|Egypt]]ian service, and as geographers whose views on the boundaries of [[egypt|Egypt]] [[herodotus|Herodotus]] takes pains to refute.

## Ionian Opinions on Egypt

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] opens his geographical discussion of [[egypt|Egypt]] with a pointed critique of Ionian theories. The Ionians maintained that "the [[nile-delta|Delta]] alone is [[egypt|Egypt]]," defining its coastline from the watch-tower of Perseus to the fish-curing houses of [[pelusion|Pelusion]] (002-002). [[herodotus|Herodotus]] rejects this view as unsound, arguing that if the Ionian definition were correct, "neither the [[hellenes|Hellenes]] nor the Ionians themselves would know how to reckon" properly, since the [[nile-delta|Delta]] was, in his view, a gift of the river rather than the entirety of the country (002-003).

Dismissing the Ionian position, [[herodotus|Herodotus]] proposes his own definition: "[[egypt|Egypt]] is all that land which is inhabited by [[egypt|Egypt]]ians," drawing an analogy to [[Kilikia]] and other regions defined by their peoples rather than by geographical features alone (002-004). This rhetorical move — defining Ionian geography in order to refute it — structures much of [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s descriptive strategy throughout the *Account*.

## Ionian Mercenaries and Trade

The [[Ionians]] played a significant role in the military and commercial life of Saite [[egypt|Egypt]]. King [[Psammetichos]] ([[psammetichos|Psamtik I]]) employed Ionian mercenaries alongside Carians in his rise to power, granting them land in [[nile-river|the Nile]] [[nile-delta|Delta]]. These Ionian settlements became conduits for sustained Greek-[[egypt|Egypt]]ian contact.

In commerce, [[Naucratis]] — granted to Greek settlers by King [[Amasis]] — became the primary Greek emporium in [[egypt|Egypt]]. The Ionians were among the founding contributors to the Hellenion, the largest and most important Greek religious enclosure at [[naucratis|Naucratis]], alongside settlers from [[Chios]], [[Teos]], [[Phocaia]], [[Clazomenai]], [[Rhodes]], and other Greek cities (016-005).

## Ionian Knowledge and Herodotus's Method

[[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s relationship with Ionian sources is ambivalent. He acknowledges the Ionians as the primary Greek informants about [[egypt|Egypt]]ian geography, yet repeatedly positions himself as correcting their errors. This dynamic — relying on Ionian geographical knowledge while asserting his own superior judgment — is characteristic of [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s historiographical method. The Ionians function as both source and foil throughout the *Account*.
