## Overview

[[egypt|Egypt]]ian religion, as documented by [[herodotus|Herodotus]], was a complex system of polytheistic worship characterized by elaborate rituals, a powerful priesthood, sacred animal cults, and a pantheon of gods organized into distinct generational ranks. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] considered the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians "religious excessively beyond all other men" (004-005) and identified [[egypt|Egypt]] as the source from which the [[hellenes|Hellenes]] derived the names of most of their gods (005-006). The religion permeated every aspect of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian life, from daily [[ritual-purity|purification customs]] to grand annual festivals drawing hundreds of thousands of worshippers.

## Description

### Deities and the Pantheon

The [[egypt|Egypt]]ian pantheon was structured in generational tiers. The first rank consisted of the eight gods (Ogdoad), among whom were Pan (called [[mendes|Mendes]] in [[egypt|Egypt]]ian) and [[leto|Leto]]. From the eight gods were begotten the twelve gods of the second rank, including [[heracles|Heracles]], whom the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians regarded as an exceedingly ancient deity. A third rank comprised gods born of the twelve, including Dionysos. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] reports that the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians reckoned seventeen thousand years from the time of the twelve gods down to the reign of [[amasis|Amasis]], and fifteen thousand years for Dionysos (014-001). This chronology underscored the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians' belief in the immense antiquity of their religious tradition.

Not all [[egypt|Egypt]]ians worshipped the same gods universally. Regional cults prevailed: the Thebans and those of the Theban district sacrificed goats and abstained from sheep, venerating [[zeus|Zeus]] ([[ammon|Amun]]) with a ram-headed image. The [[mendes|Mendesian]]s, by contrast, abstained from goats and sacrificed sheep, revering Pan ([[mendes|Mendes]]) above all (005-001). The two deities worshipped by all [[egypt|Egypt]]ians without exception were [[isis-and-osiris|Isis and Osiris]] -- whom [[herodotus|Herodotus]] equates with [[isis-and-osiris|Demeter and Dionysos]] respectively (005-001). Other major deities included [[hephaistos|Hephaistos]] ([[hephaistos|Ptah]]), Athene, [[artemis|Artemis]] ([[bubastis|Bubastis]]), [[apollo|Apollo]] (Oros/Horus), Ares, and [[leto|Leto]].

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] identifies a striking number of Greek divine names as borrowings from [[egypt|Egypt]]. He asserts that almost all the names of the gods came to Hellas from [[egypt|Egypt]], with the exception of Poseidon (whom the [[hellenes|Hellenes]] received from the Libyans), the Dioscuroi, Hera, Hestia, Themis, the Charites, and the Nereids (005-006). According to his account, the Pelasgians originally sacrificed to unnamed gods, only later adopting the specific names they learned from [[egypt|Egypt]] and confirming them through the Oracle at Dodona.

### Temples and Sacred Enclosures

[[egypt|Egypt]]ian temples were imposing structures with grand gateways and sacred enclosures (temenoi). The temple of [[hephaistos|Hephaistos]] at [[memphis|Memphis]] featured a gateway facing west built by [[rhampsinitos|Rhampsinitos]] (011-007). The temple of [[leto|Leto]] at [[buto|Buto]], where the most revered Oracle in [[egypt|Egypt]] resided, had a gateway ten fathoms high and contained a temple-house made of a single stone (015-001). Within the sacred enclosure at [[chemnis|Chemmis]], a temple of Perseus housed an image of the hero, and the people claimed that Perseus frequently appeared there, leaving behind a sandal two cubits in length as a portent of prosperity (008-004). The [[egypt|Egypt]]ians were the first to establish rules of temple purity, forbidding sexual intercourse within temples and requiring bathing before entry after contact with women -- practices that set them apart from most other peoples of the ancient world (006-004).

### The Priesthood

The priests formed one of the seven hereditary classes of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian society, distinct from warriors, cowherds, swineherds, shopkeepers, interpreters, and boatmen (015-011). They enjoyed significant privileges, including grants of land free from taxation (016-001). The priesthood was organized with multiple priests serving each god, presided over by a chief-priest, and the office passed from father to son (004-005).

Priestly life was governed by rigorous purity codes. Priests shaved their entire bodies every other day to prevent lice or any foul thing from being upon them when ministering to the gods. They wore only linen garments and sandals made of papyrus, bathing in cold water twice daily and twice nightly. They received sacred bread, abundant portions of oxen and goose flesh, and grape wine each day, though they were forbidden fish and could not even look upon beans, which they considered unclean (004-005).

### Oracles and Divination

[[egypt|Egypt]] possessed multiple oracles, each sacred to a particular god. Oracular shrines existed for [[heracles|Heracles]], [[apollo|Apollo]], Athene, [[artemis|Artemis]], Ares, [[zeus|Zeus]], and -- held in highest honor -- [[leto|Leto]] at the city of [[buto|Buto]] (008-001). The priests of [[zeus|Theban Zeus]] recounted that the oracular tradition itself originated in [[egypt|Egypt]]: two consecrated women from the temple of [[zeus|Zeus]] at [[thebes|Thebes]] were carried away by [[phoenicians|Phoenicians]] and sold, one into Libya (where she founded the [[oracle-of-ammon|Oracle of Ammon]]) and the other into Hellas (where she established the Oracle of [[zeus|Zeus]] at Dodona) (006-001). [[herodotus|Herodotus]] notes the close resemblance between the methods of delivering oracles at [[thebes|Thebes]] and at Dodona, and asserts that the art of divination by sacrificial victims also came from [[egypt|Egypt]].

The most remarkable oracle was that of [[leto|Leto]] at [[buto|Buto]], which featured the island of [[chemnis|Chemmis]] -- said by the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians to be a floating island. According to [[egypt|Egypt]]ian tradition, [[leto|Leto]], one of the eight primordial gods, received the infant [[apollo|Apollo]] (Oros) from [[isis|Isis]] and hid him on this island to protect him from [[typhon|Typhon]], who was seeking to destroy the son of [[osiris|Osiris]] (015-002).

### Festivals and Rituals

The [[egypt|Egypt]]ians held solemn assemblies (panegyreis) not once annually but many times throughout the year. The most significant festivals included: the assembly at [[bubastis|Bubastis]] for [[artemis|Artemis]] (attracting up to seven hundred thousand men, women, and children), marked by boat processions with music and dancing, and consuming more grape wine than in the entire rest of the year; the festival at Busiris for [[isis|Isis]], where worshippers beat themselves in mourning after sacrifice; the assembly at [[sais|Sais]] for Athene, featuring the Lychnocaia (Feast of Lamps) where saucers of oil and salt were lit throughout the night; assemblies at [[heliopolis|Heliopolis]] for the Sun, at [[buto|Buto]] for [[leto|Leto]], and at Papremis for Ares (006-002). Those unable to attend the Feast of Lamps at [[sais|Sais]] lit lamps in their own homes, so that all [[egypt|Egypt]] was illuminated on that festival night (006-003).

Sacrificial practices varied by region and deity. The Thebans, who revered rams as sacred to [[ammon|Amun]], sacrificed a single ram annually on the feast of [[zeus|Zeus]], flaying it and draping its skin over the image of the god. All present in the temple then beat themselves in lamentation for the ram before burying it in a sacred tomb (005-001). Animal cults extended far beyond sacrifice: virtually all [[egypt|Egypt]]ian wild animals were considered sacred, with dedicated caretakers providing food funded by votive offerings. Killing an ibis or a hawk, even accidentally, was punishable by death (006-005).

## Historical Context

By the 5th century BCE, when [[herodotus|Herodotus]] visited [[egypt|Egypt]], the civilization's religious institutions were already millennia old. The priests at [[memphis|Memphis]], [[heliopolis|Heliopolis]], and [[thebes|Thebes]] served as [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s primary informants, transmitting an oral and written tradition that they claimed stretched back over three hundred and forty-one generations of kings and priests (013-009). [[egypt|Egypt]] was then under [[persian|Persian]] rule, yet its religious infrastructure remained intact, with temples operating and festivals observed as they had been for centuries. The [[egypt|Egypt]]ian priesthood maintained meticulous chronological records -- [[herodotus|Herodotus]] notes that "they always kept a reckoning and wrote down the years as they came" (014-001) -- making them, in his judgment, the most learned historians among all peoples (007-005).

## Herodotus's Commentary

[[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s account of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian religion is marked by a blend of ethnographic admiration and his characteristic restraint when approaching sacred matters. He repeatedly declares the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians the most religious of all peoples, emphasizing the antiquity and sophistication of their practices against Greek claims of primacy. His comparative method is evident throughout: [[heracles|Egyptian Heracles]] is not the Greek hero but an ancient god; Greek Pan is a recent invention compared to the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian [[mendes|Mendes]]; and the names of the Greek gods are, in his analysis, [[egypt|Egypt]]ian borrowings. Yet he also imposes careful boundaries on his reporting. When discussing why certain animals are held sacred, he writes: "if I should say for what reasons the sacred animals have been thus dedicated, I should fall into discourse of matters pertaining to the gods, of which I most desire not to speak; and what I have actually said touching slightly upon them, I said because I was constrained by necessity" (006-005). This reticence reflects both Greek religious sensibility and [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s awareness of the limits of his inquiry.

## Significance

[[egypt|Egypt]]ian religion, as documented by [[herodotus|Herodotus]], represents one of the earliest and most detailed outsider accounts of a non-Greek religious system in Western literature. His description provides crucial evidence for the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian origins of Greek religious nomenclature, the structure of the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian pantheon and priesthood, and the elaborate festival culture of [[nile-river|the Nile]] Valley. The account reveals significant cultural transmission from [[egypt|Egypt]] to Hellas in divine names and in ritual forms such as solemn assemblies, processions, temple purification rules, and methods of divination. For modern readers, [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s treatment of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian religion also illustrates the methodological tensions of early ethnography: the impulse to record versus the piety of silence, the comparison of foreign customs to one's own, and the reliance on priestly informants whose authority both enabled and constrained his narrative.

## Sources

> The Egyptians in agreement with their climate, which is unlike any other, and with the river, which shows a nature different from all other rivers, established for themselves manners and customs in a way opposite to other men in almost all matters... The priests of the gods in other lands wear long hair, but in Egypt they shave their heads: among other men the custom is that in mourning those whom the matter concerns most nearly have their hair cut short, but the Egyptians, when deaths occur, let their hair grow long, both that on the head and that on the chin, having before been close shaven.
> (004-004)

> They are religious excessively beyond all other men, and with regard to this they have customs as follows:--they drink from cups of bronze and rinse them out every day, and not some only do this but all: they wear garments of linen always newly washed, and this they make a special point of practice: they circumcise themselves for the sake of cleanliness, preferring to be clean rather than comely. The priests shave themselves all over their body every other day, so that no lice or any other foul thing may come to be upon them when they minister to the gods.
> (004-005)

> Not all the Egyptians equally reverence the same gods, except only Isis and Osiris (who they say is Dionysos), these they all reverence alike: but they who have a temple of Mendes or belong to the Mendesian district, these abstain from goats and sacrifice sheep.
> (005-001)

> Moreover the naming of almost all the gods has come to Hellas from Egypt: for that it has come from the Barbarians I find by inquiry is true, and I am of opinion that most probably it has come from Egypt, because, except in the case of Poseidon and the Dioscuroi... and also of Hera and Hestia and Themis and the Charites and Nereids, the Egyptians say themselves: but as for the gods whose names they profess that they do not know, these I think received their naming from the Pelasgians.
> (005-006)

> The Egyptians were the first of men who made solemn assemblies and processions and approaches to the temples, and from them the Hellenes have learnt them, and my evidence for this is that the Egyptian celebrations of these have been held from a very ancient time, whereas the Hellenic were introduced but lately.
> (006-002)
