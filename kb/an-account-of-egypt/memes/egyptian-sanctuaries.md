## Overview

Egyptian sanctuaries, as recorded by Herodotus, were sacred precincts set apart from ordinary space for the worship of specific deities. These sanctuaries varied in form — from temple enclosures housing cult images to natural sites associated with divine presence — and were governed by strict rules of ritual purity and access. (007-003)

The Egyptians were, Herodotus observes, the first people to establish the rule that men should not have intercourse within temples, nor enter temples after intercourse without first bathing. In this they differed from almost all other nations, who treated temples no differently from any other building — reasoning, as they did, that since beasts coupled freely in sacred precincts, the gods could not object to human conduct there. (006-004)

## Description

### The Temple of Perseus at Chemmis

The most architecturally distinctive sanctuary Herodotus describes is the temple of Perseus at Chemmis, a large city in the Theban district near Neapolis. The temple was of square shape and surrounded by date-palms. Its gateway, built of stone, was of very great size, and at the entrance stood two enormous stone statues. Within the enclosure stood a temple-house containing an image of Perseus. (008-003)

The people of Chemmis claimed that Perseus frequently appeared in their land and within the temple itself. A sandal worn by him, measuring two cubits in length (approximately three feet), was sometimes found, and whenever it appeared, all Egypt prospered. In honour of Perseus, the Chemmites held athletic contests in the Hellenic fashion — including the full complement of games — offering cattle, cloaks, and skins as prizes. They were the only Egyptians to do so. (008-004)

### Sanctuary of the Theban Zeus

The temple of Zeus at Thebes maintained a distinctive cult centred on the ram. The Thebans and all who belonged to the Theban district sacrificed goats but abstained entirely from sheep. Rams were held sacred — except on one day each year, the feast of Zeus, when a single ram was sacrificed. The priests cut up and flayed the ram, then covered the cult image of Zeus with its skin. They then brought forward another image, that of Heracles, and all present in the temple beat themselves in lamentation for the slain ram before burying it in a sacred tomb. (005-001)

### Sanctuary of the Ram at Mendes

Parallel to the Theban custom, the Mendesians and all who belonged to the Mendesian district abstained from goats and sacrificed sheep, holding goats sacred. This reciprocal pattern of local animal sanctity — each district consecrating a different animal — was fundamental to Egyptian religious geography. Herodotus notes that not all Egyptians revered the same gods equally, with the sole exception of Isis and Osiris (whom the Egyptians identified with Dionysos), who were universally honoured. (005-001)

### Sacred Animal Sanctuaries

Animal sanctuaries were woven into the fabric of Egyptian cities. At Bubastis, dead cats were embalmed and carried away for burial in sacred buildings. Dogs were buried by each community in their own city's sacred tombs, and ichneumons (mongoose-like animals) received the same treatment as dogs. (006-005)

At Thebes and around the lake of Moiris, crocodiles were held most sacred. Each community maintained one specially selected crocodile, trained to tameness. These creatures were adorned with ornaments of glass and gold attached to their ears, and bracelets were placed around their forefeet. They were fed with special food and, when they died, were embalmed and buried in sacred tombs. (007-001)

The river-horse (hippopotamus) was sacred exclusively in the district of Papremis. Otters, certain fish — the *lepidotos* and the eel — were held sacred to the Nile, as was the fox-goose among birds. Near Thebes, small horned serpents, harmless to men and bearing two horns growing from the head, were considered sacred to Zeus; upon their death they were buried within the temple of Zeus itself. (007-002)

### Animal Bone Depositories

The sanctuary of Aphrodite at Atarbechis, on the island of Prosopitis, served a singular ritual purpose. Throughout Egypt, the carcasses of sacrificed oxen were buried in suburban grounds until they decomposed. At the appointed time, boats from Atarbechis travelled the length of Egypt collecting the bones, which were then deposited together at this single sacred site. A holy temple of Aphrodite stood over the repository. (004-006)

### Temple Purity Regulations

Egyptian sanctuaries were governed by rigorous purity laws. Priests shaved their entire bodies every other day, wore only freshly washed linen garments and papyrus sandals, and washed in cold water twice daily and twice nightly. They were prohibited from wearing woolen garments within temple precincts, and wool was forbidden in burials — a rule that Herodotus notes is shared with the Orphic and Bacchic mysteries, which he believes are ultimately Egyptian in origin. (004-005)
