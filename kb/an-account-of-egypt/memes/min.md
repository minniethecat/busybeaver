## Overview

In [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s *Account of [[egypt|Egypt]]*, the name **Min** refers to two distinct figures: the first king of Egypt (Menes), who diverted [[nile-river|the Nile]] by embankments and founded [[Memphis]], and an [[egypt|Egypt]]ian god whom [[herodotus|Herodotus]] identifies with the Greek deity Pan. Both appear in the priestly chronology recorded at [[memphis|Memphis]], and both are central to the historian's narrative of [[egypt|Egypt]]'s earliest royal and religious foundations.

## Min the King

[[Min]] (Menes) is recorded by the priests of [[memphis|Memphis]] as the first king of Egypt. His founding acts defined the geography of the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian capital: he banked off the site of [[memphis|Memphis]] from [[nile-river|the Nile]], rerouting the river's course. Before Min, "the whole stream of the river used to flow along by the sandy mountain-range on the side of Libya"; Min formed by embankments the bend of the river about a hundred furlongs south of the city, drying the old channel and directing [[nile-river|the Nile]] through the middle between the mountains (009-004).

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] notes that even in his own time, the [[persian|Persians]] maintained careful watch over Min's bend of [[nile-river|the Nile]], repairing the bank annually — for if the river were to break through and overflow, [[memphis|Memphis]] itself would be in danger (009-004).

After damming off the land, Min founded [[memphis|Memphis]] and "established in the city the temple of [[Hephaistos]], a great work and most worthy of mention." He also dug a lake around the city communicating with the river on its north and west sides, the east being barred by [[nile-river|the Nile]] itself (009-004).

## Min the God

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] also identifies Min with the Greek god Pan — the deity of the [[mendes|Mendesian district]], who was worshipped alongside the ram-god. The [[mendes|Mendesian]]s abstained from goats and sacrificed sheep in opposition to the Theban practice (005-001). This [[egypt|Egypt]]ian Min/Pan was among the most ancient of [[egyptian-religion|Egyptian gods]], predating even the twelve-god pantheon that included [[heracles|Heracles]].

## The Chronology

After Min, the priests enumerated from a papyrus roll the names of 330 subsequent kings, spanning many generations — of whom eighteen were [[ethiopia-region|Ethiopia]]ns, one was a woman (Nitocris), and the remainder were men of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian race (009-004). The priests noted that of all these kings, "they could tell me of no great works which had been produced by them, and they said that they had no renown except only the last of them, Moiris" (009-005).

## Significance

Min the king marks the beginning of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian history in [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s chronology — the point at which the alluvial land of the [[nile-delta|Delta]] became habitable and the city of [[memphis|Memphis]] was founded as the first seat of royal power. His dual identity as both the founder-king and a deity reflects the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian tradition of deified ancestors, and his embankment of [[nile-river|the Nile]] established the pattern of hydraulic engineering that successive kings — from [[sesostris|Sesostris]] to [[necos|Necos]] — would continue.
