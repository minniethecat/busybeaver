## Overview

Herodotus describes Egyptian society as divided into seven distinct hereditary classes, each named for the occupation its members followed. This system, which he presents as a fundamental feature of Egyptian social organization, comprised the priests, the warriors, the cowherds, the swineherds, the shopkeepers, the interpreters, and the boatmen. Of these, the [[Egyptian Priesthood|priests]] and warriors were the most prominent, enjoying privileges and land grants that set them apart from the labouring classes. The rigidity of this class structure — and the political tensions it could generate — is illustrated by Herodotus's account of King [[Sethos]], whose neglect of the warrior class nearly cost him his kingdom. (015-011)

## The Seven Classes

Herodotus enumerates the seven Egyptian classes in a single concise passage. The [[Egyptian Priesthood|priests]] and warriors formed the elite, with the cowherds, shopkeepers, interpreters, and boatmen comprising the remaining five. Each class derived its name from the occupation its members pursued, and membership appears to have been hereditary — a de facto caste system in which a man followed the trade of his father. (015-011)

Notably absent from Herodotus's list are several occupations that must have existed in a civilization as complex as Egypt: scribes, sculptors, painters, goldsmiths, and merchants all go unmentioned. It is possible that Herodotus's seven classes represent broad categories into which these specialized trades were subsumed, or that his informants emphasized only the most visible social divisions. The interpreters, or dragomen, were a distinct class born of Egypt's position as a crossroads between Africa and Asia; their inclusion testifies to the practical importance of multilingual communication in a society that traded and warred with Greeks, Persians, and Nubians alike.

## The Warrior Class

The warriors, whom Herodotus calls the most significant class after the priests, were themselves divided into two groups: the Calasirians and the Hermotybians. These were not merely military units but territorial communities, each associated with specific districts (nomes) of Egypt. The Hermotybians belonged to the districts of [[Busiris]], [[Sais]], [[Chemmis]], [[Papremis]], the island of Prosopitis, and half of Natho; at their most numerous, they reached one hundred and sixty thousand men. The Calasirians were drawn from [[Thebes]], [[Bubastis]], Aphthis, Tanis, [[Mendes]], Sebennytos, and several other districts. (015-011)

The warriors were set apart from the rest of Egyptian society by a strict prohibition: none of them learned any handicraft or trade. Their lives were devoted entirely to war, and in return for their military service they received a grant of land — twelve yokes (about eight acres) of corn-land per man — a privilege bestowed by the kings of former times. This land grant system created a warrior aristocracy whose loyalty to the crown depended on the crown's continued respect for their traditional perquisites. (013-008)

## The Crisis of Sethos

The political significance of the warrior class is vividly illustrated by Herodotus's story of [[Sethos]], a priest of Hephaistos who became king. Sethos, believing he would have no need of military force, "neglected and held in no regard the warrior class of the Egyptians." He compounded the insult by confiscating the land grants — the twelve yokes of corn-land that each warrior had received as a hereditary privilege under previous kings. (013-008)

When Sanacharib, king of the Arabians and Assyrians, marched against Egypt with a great army, the warriors refused to fight. Not one of the warrior class came to Sethos's aid. Deserted by his military elite, the priest-king was forced to rely on shopkeepers, artisans, and market-men — the very labouring classes that the warriors looked down upon. Only divine intervention, in the form of field mice that gnawed through the enemy's bowstrings and shield-handles during the night, saved Egypt from conquest. (013-008)

The story, whether historical or legendary, reveals the mutual obligations that bound the warrior class to the crown: the king provided land and honour, and in return the warriors provided military service. When Sethos broke that compact, the class withdrew its support — a lesson that Herodotus's audience would have recognized as applicable to any polity dependent on a privileged military estate.

## The Labouring Classes

The five remaining classes — cowherds, swineherds, shopkeepers, interpreters, and boatmen — formed the productive base of Egyptian society. Their low status in Herodotus's account should not obscure their economic importance. The cowherds and swineherds provisioned the temples and the royal table with sacrificial animals; the shopkeepers and interpreters facilitated Egypt's busy internal and external commerce; and the boatmen controlled the Nile, the nation's principal artery of transport and communication.

Herodotus's account suggests that these classes, unlike the priests and warriors, received no special land grants or royal privileges. They were, nevertheless, indispensable to the functioning of the Egyptian state, and in the crisis of Sethos it was precisely these disdained classes — the shopkeepers, artisans, and market-men — who answered the king's call when the warriors refused.

## Significance

Herodotus's account of the seven Egyptian classes is one of the earliest surviving descriptions of a structured social hierarchy based on hereditary occupation. The system he describes — with its land-granted warrior elite, its specialized priesthood, and its clearly demarcated labouring classes — provided a model that later Greek observers would compare to the Spartan military caste and other Hellenic social experiments. The story of Sethos, meanwhile, functions as a political parable about the limits of royal power: even a king could not safely disregard the established prerogatives of a hereditary military class.
