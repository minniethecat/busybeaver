## Overview

Children appear in several distinct contexts in Herodotus's Egyptian narrative: as subjects of a royal experiment to discover the oldest nation, as participants in religious customs, and as objects of sacrificial and naming practices. While the Histories does not offer a comprehensive account of childhood in Egypt, the vignettes collectively suggest that children held a distinctive place in Egyptian ritual life and that their rearing was a matter of both royal curiosity and religious obligation. (001-008)

## The Language Experiment of Psammetichos

The most famous story involving Egyptian children in Herodotus is the experiment devised by Psammetichos to determine which nation was the oldest. The king took two newborn children of common parents and gave them to a shepherd, with strict instructions that no one should speak a word in their presence. They were to be kept in a solitary dwelling, nourished by goats, and observed to see what word they would first utter. After two years, the shepherd entered one day to find the children stretching out their hands and crying "bekos" — the Phrygian word for bread. From this, Psammetichos concluded that the Phrygians were older than the Egyptians, and the Egyptians themselves acknowledged their junior status. (001-008) (001-009)

This account, whether historical or legendary, reflects Greek curiosity about the origins of language and the antiquity of nations. It also reveals that Egyptian kings were imagined to have the authority to conduct such experiments, treating children's natural speech as an oracle of national precedence. (001-009)

## Children in Religious Customs

Children were involved in several religious observances. Herodotus records that at the festival of the goddess whom the Egyptians considered to be Artemis (Bastet), the crowds included men, women, and children — seventy myriads in total, he estimates. During this festival, the women played flutes and cymbals, and some exposed themselves to the goddess's temple as an act of devotion. (006-002)

When a child died, the Egyptians shaved their heads in mourning. Herodotus describes a specific practice: if a death occurred in a household, the women of the house would plaster their heads and faces with mud and run about the city beating themselves. But a more unusual custom concerned the heads of children: the Egyptians would shave the heads of their children — either entirely, or half, or a third — and weigh the hair against silver, giving the equivalent value to the keeper of the sacred animals. This hair-offering, apparently a votive act performed for a child's welfare or in gratitude for a recovery from illness, tied children into the economy of temple devotion. (006-005)

## Circumcision

On the matter of circumcision, Herodotus makes a pointed observation: the Egyptians circumcised their children, and he regarded them, along with the Colchians and Ethiopians, as the originators of the practice. He notes that the Phoenicians and the Syrians of Palestine acknowledged learning circumcision from the Egyptians, while other nations — including the Greeks — did not circumcise their children at all. For Herodotus, circumcision was a marker of Egyptian identity and a custom that had spread outward from Egypt to neighbouring peoples. (009-006)

## Child Sacrifice

In a darker passage, Herodotus records that King Pheros, when struck blind for an impious act, was told by an oracle that he could recover his sight by washing his eyes with the urine of a woman who had known only her own husband. After testing many women — including his own wife — and failing, he finally found a woman whose fidelity was confirmed. But before this, the priests told Herodotus of another king who, in desperation during a time of crisis, took two native children and made sacrifice of them. This rare mention of human sacrifice, attributed to an unnamed king, stood as an exception to normal Egyptian practice and a mark of extreme impiety. (011-004)

## Significance

The treatment of children in Herodotus's Egypt reveals a society in which childhood was not a private domestic matter but an intersection of royal authority, religious obligation, and ethnic identity. The Psammetichos experiment used children as vehicles for national self-knowledge; the head-shaving custom turned children's hair into offerings that circulated silver through temple economies; circumcision marked children as Egyptian from their earliest days. In each case, the child's body — its speech, its hair, its flesh — was a site where Egyptian culture inscribed itself.
