## Herodotus's References

Herodotus mentions Aphrodite in several contexts within his account of Egypt, primarily in relation to Egyptian religious geography and cross-cultural interaction.

### The Temple of Aphrodite at Atarbechis

In describing Egyptian cattle burial customs, Herodotus notes that the island of Prosopitis in the Delta contains a city named Atarbechis, "and in it there is set up a holy temple of Aphrodite" [002-061]. The bones of sacrificed oxen were transported to this island for collective burial.

### The "Foreign Aphrodite" at Memphis

At Memphis, within the enclosure of the legendary king Proteus, there stood a temple called the temple of the "foreign Aphrodite" [002-139]. Herodotus conjectures that this temple was actually dedicated to Helen, daughter of Tyndareus, noting: "the other temples of Aphrodite which there are have none of them the addition of the word 'foreign' to the name." The district around this enclosure was known as the Camp of the Tyrians and was inhabited by Phoenicians from Tyre.

### Aphrodite and the Marriage of Amasis

Herodotus recounts a story concerning Amasis, an Egyptian king, and his wife Ladike [002-255]. When Amasis found himself unable to have intercourse with Ladike — though he could with his other wives — Ladike made a vow to Aphrodite. After her vow, Amasis was able to be with her, and Ladike fulfilled her promise by sending a dedicated image to Cyrene, which Herodotus reports "is still preserved even to my own time, standing with its face turned away from the city of the Kyrenians."

## Interpretation

Herodotus's references to Aphrodite illustrate the phenomenon of _interpretatio graeca_ — the Greek practice of identifying foreign deities with their own gods. The Egyptian goddess associated with these sites was likely Hathor, whom Herodotus does not mention by name but whose cult centers correspond to locations he associates with Aphrodite.
