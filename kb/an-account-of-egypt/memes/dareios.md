## Overview

[[Dareios]] (Darius I) was the [[persian|Achaemenid]] king of Persia who appears in [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s *Account of [[egypt|Egypt]]* primarily in connection with the canal connecting the [[Nile River|Nile]] to the [[Erythraian Sea]]. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] records that the canal project was begun by the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian king [[Necos]] but was completed only under Dareios, who reigned after the [[persian|Persian]] conquest of [[egypt|Egypt]]. Dareios's role in the canal's completion is presented by [[herodotus|Herodotus]] alongside the account of [[necos|Necos]]'s abortive project and the later assertion of the [[egyptian-priesthood|Egyptian priests]] that the canal was ultimately a failed venture — navigable only after its redesign.

## The Canal to the Erythraian Sea

The canal from [[nile-river|the Nile]] to the [[erythraian-sea|Erythraian Sea]] was one of the greatest engineering projects of the ancient world. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] records that it was first begun by [[necos|Necos]], son of [[Psammetichos]], "who was the first to attempt the channel leading to the [[erythraian-sea|Erythraian Sea]]" (015-004). [[necos|Necos]]'s canal was designed to extend from a point on [[nile-river|the Nile]] near [[Bubastis]], through the [[Wadi Tumilat]], to the northern end of the [[erythraian-sea|Erythraian Sea]] (the modern Gulf of Suez). [[herodotus|Herodotus]] describes its dimensions as broad enough for two triremes to be rowed abreast, and he notes that 120,000 [[egypt|Egypt]]ians perished during its construction before [[necos|Necos]] abandoned the work, deterred by an oracle warning that he was toiling for a foreign power.

The project was later taken up by Dareios after the [[persian|Persian]] conquest. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] references Dareios's completion of the canal in passing, contrasting it with the [[egyptian-priesthood|Egyptian priests]]' claim that the waterway was ultimately redesigned to be more effective — an improvement credited not to a [[persian|Persian]] king but to a later figure. The canal represented a strategic link between the Mediterranean (via [[nile-river|the Nile]]) and the [[erythraian-sea|Erythraian Sea]], facilitating [[persian|Persian]] naval power and trade routes between [[egypt|Egypt]] and the eastern territories of the empire.

## Dareios in the Persian Context

[[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s *Account of [[egypt|Egypt]]* is embedded within his larger *Histories*, in which Dareios features prominently as the organizer and consolidator of the [[persian|Persian]] Empire after the upheavals that followed the death of [[cambyses|Cambyses]]. As the ruler who incorporated [[egypt|Egypt]] into the [[persian|Achaemenid]] administrative system, Dareios's name recurs in references to the [[persian|Persian]] regime in [[egypt|Egypt]] — the same administrative structures that [[herodotus|Herodotus]] observes in his own time, with [[persian|Persian]] garrisons stationed at [[Elephantine]], [[Daphnai]], and [[Marea]] as they had been since [[psammetichos|Psammetichos]]'s day.

## The Canals and the Delta

The canal network of the [[nile-delta|Delta]], which was expanded under successive rulers from [[Sesostris]] onwards, was furthered by Dareios's completion of the [[erythraian-sea|Erythraian Sea]] canal. This extension of the hydraulic system transformed the [[nile-delta|Delta]]'s economic geography, linking [[egypt|Egypt]]'s interior waterways to the maritime routes of the Indian Ocean. The project's lineage — begun by an [[egypt|Egypt]]ian pharaoh, interrupted by an oracle, and completed by a [[persian|Persian]] conqueror — reflects the layered history of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian-[[persian|Persian]] relations in the Late Period.

## Significance

Dareios appears in [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s [[egypt|Egypt]]ian narrative primarily as a figure of imperial completion — the ruler who finished what an [[egypt|Egypt]]ian king had begun but could not bring to fruition. His presence underscores the continuity of large-scale engineering ambition across [[egypt|Egypt]]ian and [[persian|Persian]] regimes, and the canal itself stands as one of the most ambitious hydraulic works described in the *Account of [[egypt|Egypt]]*, alongside the embankments of [[min|Min]], the lake of [[moiris|Moiris]], and the canals of [[sesostris|Sesostris]].
