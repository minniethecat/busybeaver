## Overview

The pig occupied a paradoxical position in the Egypt described by Herodotus. On the one hand, the animal served a practical agricultural function, being driven onto the fields after the Nile flood to tread seed into the moist soil. On the other, the pig was regarded as an abominable creature whose touch required immediate ritual purification, and those who tended swine were ostracised from temple worship and social marriage. This dual status — useful yet unclean — reflects the complex interplay between practical necessity and religious taboo in Egyptian society. (002-001) (005-005)

## Agricultural Function

In describing the agricultural practices of the Nile Delta, Herodotus notes a remarkably simple method of sowing that relied on swine. After the annual inundation of the river had receded, leaving the fields saturated, the Egyptian farmer sowed his seed directly onto the flooded land. He then drove swine onto the field, and the animals trod the seed into the ground by the action of their hooves. Once the seed was pressed into the soil, the farmer waited for the harvest. This swine-driven method of sowing was emblematic of the ease with which the Egyptians cultivated their land, in contrast to the ploughing and hoeing required in other regions. (002-001)

## Ritual Impurity and Social Exclusion

Despite their agricultural usefulness, swine were regarded by the Egyptians as deeply impure. Herodotus records that the pig was "accounted by the Egyptians an abominable animal." If an Egyptian accidentally touched a pig, even in passing, they were required to go immediately into the river — clothes and all — to purify themselves. Swineherds, even when they were native-born Egyptians, suffered the most severe social exclusion: they were forbidden from entering any temple in the land, and no Egyptian family would give a daughter in marriage to a swineherd or take a wife from among their families. The swineherds were thus compelled to marry exclusively among themselves, forming a hereditary underclass defined by their association with the unclean animal. (005-005)
