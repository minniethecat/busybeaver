## Overview

Egyptian religious life, as described by Herodotus, was distinguished from that of other Mediterranean peoples by its elaborate ceremonial assemblies, its strict codes of ritual purity, and the intensity of its observances. The Egyptians were, in Herodotus's estimation, "excessively careful" in matters concerning sacred rites — a judgment borne out by the detailed customs he records, from the great festivals at Bubastis and Busiris to the solemn year-round rites of lamp-lighting at Sais. (006-001) (006-002)

## The Great Solemn Assemblies

The Egyptians held their solemn assemblies not once in the year but frequently, with the greatest zeal and devotion directed toward particular cult centres. The festival at Bubastis for Artemis drew enormous crowds: men and women traveled together in crowded boats along the Nile, the women playing castanets and the men playing flutes, while the rest sang and clapped their hands. At each city they passed, the boats drew near the shore and the revelry continued, the women shouting playful insults at the women of the town. Upon reaching Bubastis, they celebrated with great sacrifices, and Herodotus reports that more wine was consumed at this festival than in all the rest of the year combined. (006-001)

The next greatest assembly was at Busiris for Isis, where after the sacrifice, a vast crowd — men and women numbering in the tens of thousands — beat their breasts in ritual lamentation. For whom they thus mourned, Herodotus declines to say, constrained by his religious scruple against speaking openly of sacred matters. At Sais, the assembly for Athene involved a strikingly beautiful custom: on a particular night, all participants lit lamps in the open air around their houses — small saucers filled with salt and oil, with a floating wick burning through the night. Those who could not attend the assembly also lit lamps in their own cities, so that on this one night, the whole of Egypt was illuminated. A sacred story, which Herodotus again does not relate, explained the origin of this custom. (006-001)

## The Temple of Bubastis

Herodotus provides a detailed architectural description of the temple of Artemis at Bubastis, which he considered the most pleasant to the eyes of all Egyptian temples — even if others were larger or more costly (013-004). The temple stood in the middle of the city, and because the city itself had been raised up by successive embankments — first under Sesostris, then under the Ethiopian king Sabacos — while the temple remained at its original level, it was possible to look down into the sacred precinct from all sides as one walked around the city. Except for its entrance, the temple was completely surrounded by water: two channels from the Nile, each a hundred feet broad and shaded with trees, flowed around it on either side. The gateway, adorned with noteworthy figures six cubits high, rose to a height of ten fathoms. Within, a stone wall carved with figures encircled a grove of very large trees planted around the temple-house containing the image of the goddess (013-004). A paved road three furlongs long and four hundred feet broad, lined with trees "reaching to heaven," led through the marketplace to the temple of Hermes.

## The Floating Island of Chemmis

Herodotus records a distinctive sacred site associated with Egyptian religious practice: the floating island of Chemmis, near the temple of Buto. The island, he writes, lay in a deep, broad lake, and the Egyptians said that it floated. Though Herodotus himself never saw it move, he faithfully transmits the local account. The story told that Leto, one of the eight primordial gods and guardian of the oracle at Buto, received the infant Apollo from Isis and concealed him on this island — which at that time was not yet floating — when Typhon came searching for the son of Osiris (015-002). The Egyptians identified Apollo as Oros (Horus), Demeter as Isis, and Artemis as Bubastis, and they held that Apollo and Artemis were children of Dionysos and Isis. Herodotus records that Aeschylus diverged from all preceding poets in representing Artemis as the daughter of Demeter, a departure that Herodotus traces directly to this Egyptian genealogy (015-002).

## Ritual Combat at Papremis

At Papremis, the festival for Ares involved an extraordinary ritual of mock combat. When the sun began to set, the majority of the priests stood at the temple entrance armed with wooden clubs, while over a thousand men fulfilling vows stood opposite them, also carrying wooden staves. Meanwhile, a small group of priests moved the god's image — housed in a small wooden shrine covered with gold — from the temple to another sacred building. The priests at the gateway attempted to prevent the image from entering, and the votaries fought on the god's behalf to force it through, striking the defenders with their staves. The ensuing fight was so violent, Herodotus writes, that many — in his opinion — died of their wounds, although the Egyptians themselves maintained that no one ever perished. The ritual was explained through a myth: Ares, raised apart from his mother, returned as a grown man seeking to visit her, and when her temple attendants barred his entry, he brought men from another city and forced his way in. The annual combat reenacted this violent homecoming. (006-001)

## Ritual Purity and Temple Conduct

The Egyptians, Herodotus records, were the first people to make it a point of religion not to have intercourse in temples, and to require bathing after sexual contact before entering a temple. He contrasts this explicitly with the practices of "almost all other men except the Egyptians and the Hellenes," who, he disapprovingly notes, behave no differently from beasts in this regard. This Egyptian insistence on ritual purity — separating the sacred from the profane — was, alongside their elaborate festival calendar, one of the defining features of their religious civilization. (006-002)
