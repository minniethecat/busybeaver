## Overview

Daphnai (also known as Daphnae) was an Egyptian frontier fortress located in the district of Pelusion, guarding the eastern approach from Arabia and Assyria. Herodotus records it as one of three major garrison posts established by Psammetichos — alongside Elephantine in the south and Marea in the west. It also appears in the narrative of Sesostris's near-fatal homecoming feast. (003-008) (010-001)

## Strategic Role

Psammetichos established Daphnai as part of a system of border garrisons protecting Egypt's three principal frontiers. The fortress at Daphnai faced toward Arabia and Assyria, covering the eastern desert approaches — the traditional invasion route into Egypt. The other garrisons were stationed at Elephantine toward Ethiopia and at Marea toward Libya, creating a comprehensive defensive perimeter. (003-008)

Herodotus observes that in his own time, the Persian administration maintained outposts in the same positions, demonstrating the enduring strategic logic of these defensive sites. The Persian garrisons at both Elephantine and Daphnai continued the system Psammetichos had established centuries earlier. (003-008)

## The Sesostris Incident

Herodotus records a dramatic episode associated with Daphnai. After Sesostris returned from his extensive military campaigns, his brother — to whom the king had entrusted the government of Egypt during his absence — invited him to a feast at Daphnai in the district of Pelusion. The brother then surrounded the building with brushwood and set it alight. Sesostris escaped only through the counsel of his wife, who advised him to lay two of his six sons across the flames as a bridge, sacrificing them to save the rest of the family. (010-001)
