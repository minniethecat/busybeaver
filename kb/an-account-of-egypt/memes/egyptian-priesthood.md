# Egyptian Priesthood

## Overview

The [[egypt|Egypt]]ian priesthood was the highest-ranking social class in ancient [[egypt|Egypt]], second only to the king in status and influence. [[herodotus|Herodotus]], who derived much of his knowledge from conversations with priests at [[Memphis]], [[Thebes]], and [[Heliopolis]] (001-003), describes them as a hereditary caste devoted to the service of the gods, enjoying substantial economic privileges and exercising considerable authority over religious and historical knowledge. The priests were the custodians of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian tradition, maintaining records of dynasties spanning thousands of years and serving as [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s primary informants for the history, geography, and customs of [[egypt|Egypt]].

## Description

According to [[herodotus|Herodotus]], [[egypt|Egypt]]ian society was divided into seven distinct classes, with the priests occupying the foremost position alongside the warriors (015-011). The priesthood was organized hierarchically: each deity had multiple priests serving under one chief-priest, and the office was hereditary -- whenever a priest died, his son was appointed to his place (004-005). The priests of [[Heliopolis]] were reputed to be the most learned in records among all [[egypt|Egypt]]ians, and [[herodotus|Herodotus]] visited them specifically to verify the accounts he had received from the priests of [[hephaistos|Hephaistos]] at [[memphis|Memphis]] (001-009). The priests maintained genealogical records of extraordinary length, with the priests of [[zeus|Zeus]] at [[thebes|Thebes]] showing [[herodotus|Herodotus]] a series of 345 colossal wooden statues, each representing a chief-priest who had succeeded his own father in an unbroken line (013-009).

## Purity and Religious Observances

The Egyptian priests observed elaborate purity regulations that set them apart from the general population. They shaved their entire bodies every other day to prevent any impurity from being upon them while ministering to the gods, and wore only freshly washed garments of linen, with sandals made of papyrus -- any other garment or footwear was forbidden (004-005). They washed themselves in cold water twice each day and twice each night, and performed religious services of an almost infinite number. The priests were also responsible for inspecting sacrificial animals: a designated priest would examine an ox for any black hairs, and if even a single one was found, the beast was deemed unclean for sacrifice (004-006). The priest would then mark approved animals by rolling a piece of papyrus around the horns, sealing it with his signet-ring; sacrificing an unsealed beast was punishable by death (004-006). The priests also abstained from eating fish and could not even endure the sight of beans, which they considered an unclean kind of pulse (004-005).

## Economic Privileges

The priesthood enjoyed substantial material benefits that freed them from ordinary labor. The priests did not consume or spend anything of their own substance; instead, sacred bread was baked for them, and each received a great quantity of oxen and goose flesh daily, along with wine of grapes (004-005). They shared with the warrior class the privilege of receiving twelve yokes of tax-exempt land -- each yoke measuring one hundred [[egypt|Egypt]]ian cubits in every direction (016-001). These economic arrangements, combined with the offerings and sacrifices brought to the temples by the general populace, sustained the priestly class at a level of comfort far above that of ordinary [[egypt|Egypt]]ians.

## The Priests as Historical Sources

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] relied heavily on the Egyptian priests as his primary sources for the history and customs of the country. He visited [[memphis|Memphis]], [[thebes|Thebes]], and [[heliopolis|Heliopolis]] specifically to cross-check the accounts of their respective priesthoods, finding that they agreed with one another on matters concerning men (001-009). From the priests he learned about the foundation of [[memphis|Memphis]] by King [[min|Min]] (009-004), the conquests of [[sesostris|Sesostris]] (009-006), the reign of [[proteus|Proteus]] and the story of Helen (010-005), and the 341 generations of chief-priests and kings who ruled [[egypt|Egypt]] before the [[persian|Persian]] period (013-009). The priests of [[zeus|Theban Zeus]] also related to him the origin of the oracles at Dodona and in Libya, which they traced to two consecrated women carried away from [[thebes|Thebes]] by Phoenician traders (006-001). When [[herodotus|Herodotus]] inquired about the Trojan War, the priests responded with knowledge they claimed to have obtained through inquiries from [[menelaos|Menelaos]] himself (011-004).

## Priest-Kings and Political Power

The [[egypt|Egypt]]ian priesthood was not merely a religious institution; priests could and did wield direct political power. The most notable example is [[sethos|Sethos]], the priest of [[hephaistos|Hephaistos]] who became king of [[egypt|Egypt]] and was said to have neglected the warrior class, even confiscating their land grants. When the Assyrian king Sanacharib invaded, the warriors refused to fight, and [[sethos|Sethos]], in desperation, prayed in the temple until a vision assured him of divine assistance -- field mice subsequently devoured the enemy's weapons, securing an [[egypt|Egypt]]ian victory (013-008). After the reign of this priest-king, the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians, unable to live without a ruler, established the rule of twelve kings who divided the land among themselves, and it was at a sacrifice in the temple of [[hephaistos|Hephaistos]] that the chief-priest inadvertently foretold the rise of [[psammetichos|Psammetichos]] by bringing out eleven golden cups instead of twelve (014-008). The priests also reported that during the 11,340 years spanning 341 generations, the sun had four times altered its course, yet nothing in [[egypt|Egypt]] -- neither its crops, its river, nor its patterns of disease and death -- had changed (013-009).

## Sources

> They are religious excessively beyond all other men, and with regard to this they have customs as follows: ... The priests shave themselves all over their body every other day, so that no lice or any other foul thing may come to be upon them when they minister to the gods; and the priests wear garments of linen only and sandals of papyrus, and any other garment they may not take nor other sandals; these wash themselves in cold water twice in a day and twice again in the night; and other religious services they perform (one may almost say) of infinite number. They enjoy also good things not a few, for they do not consume or spend anything of their own substance, but there is sacred bread baked for them and they have each great quantity of flesh of oxen and geese coming in to them each day, and also wine of grapes is given to them.
> (004-005)

> Now of the Egyptians there are seven classes, and of these one class is called that of the priests, and another that of the warriors, while the others are the cowherds, swineherds, shopkeepers, interpreters, and boatmen. This is the number of the classes of the Egyptians, and their names are given them from the occupations which they follow.
> (015-011)

> So far in the story the Egyptians and the priests were they who made the report, declaring that from the first king down to this priest of Hephaistos who reigned last, there had been three hundred and forty-one generations of men, and that in them there had been the same number of chief-priests and of kings: but three hundred generations of men are equal to ten thousand years, for a hundred years is three generations of men; and in the one-and-forty generations which remain, those I mean which were added to the three hundred, there are one thousand three hundred and forty years. Thus in the period of eleven thousand three hundred and forty years they said that there had arisen no god in human form.
> (013-009)

> I heard also other things at Memphis when I had speech with the priests of Hephaistos. Moreover I visited both Thebes and Heliopolis for this very cause, namely because I wished to know whether the priests at these places would agree in their accounts with those at Memphis; for the men of Heliopolis are said to be the most learned in records of the Egyptians.
> (001-009)
