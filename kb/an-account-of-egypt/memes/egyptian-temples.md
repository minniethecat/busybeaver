## Overview

Herodotus's account of Egypt is rich with descriptions of temples, which he calls "sacred precincts" or "temple-houses." These structures were the architectural and religious centres of Egyptian cities, housing the cult images of the gods and serving as repositories of priestly knowledge. Herodotus frequently consulted the priests at these temples for historical information, and his descriptions of temple architecture, precincts, and festivals constitute some of the earliest Greek observations of Egyptian monumental religion.

## Commentary

Herodotus's temple descriptions range from the brief to the detailed. The temple of Hephaistos at Memphis receives the most sustained attention, as it was there that Herodotus gathered much of his historical material from the priests. The temple of Artemis at Bubastis is singled out for its beauty and the scale of its festival. Herodotus also notes that the Egyptian temples were the repositories of precise astronomical and calendrical knowledge — the priests, he says, had determined the length of the solar year more accurately than the Hellenes (008-001).
