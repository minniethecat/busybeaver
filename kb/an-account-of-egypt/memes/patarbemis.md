Patarbemis was one of the most honoured courtiers in the service of King [[Apries]] during the 26th Dynasty of Egypt. His appearance in [[Herodotus]]'s account is brief but decisive: his mutilation by an enraged Apries proved to be the catalyst that turned the remaining Egyptian nobility against their king, sealing Apries's fate and ensuring the triumph of [[Amasis]]. (015-010)

## The Mission to Amasis

After a disastrous expedition against [[Cyrene]] resulted in a severe Egyptian defeat, the native troops revolted against [[Apries]], blaming him for the calamity. Apries despatched his general [[Amasis]] to persuade the rebels to disperse, but instead the rebels declared Amasis king. (015-009) When Apries learned of this betrayal, he sent Patarbemis — described by [[Herodotus]] as "a man of reputation" among the Egyptians about his own person — to summon Amasis to his presence alive. (015-010)

Upon arriving, Patarbemis found Amasis mounted on horseback. In a calculated act of defiance, Amasis lifted his leg and "behaved in an unseemly manner," bidding Patarbemis to take that gesture back to Apries. (015-010) When Patarbemis nevertheless insisted that Amasis obey the royal summons, Amasis replied that he had been preparing to do so and that Apries would have no cause for complaint — for he would come not alone, but with others.

## The Mutilation

Patarbemis, perceiving Amasis's intention from his words and his visible military preparations, departed in haste to inform [[Apries]] of the unfolding threat. But when he returned without Amasis, Apries — "paying no regard to that which he said, but being moved by violent anger" — ordered Patarbemis's ears and nose to be cut off. (015-010)

This act of cruelty was Apries's fatal mistake. The remaining Egyptians who still supported the king, when they saw "the man of most repute among them thus suffering shameful outrage, waited no longer but joined the others in revolt, and delivered themselves over to [[Amasis]]." (015-010)

## Historical Significance

The story of Patarbemis illustrates a recurring theme in [[Herodotus]]'s account of the Saite kings: the self-destructive consequences of tyrannical rage. [[Apries]], who [[Herodotus]] explicitly identifies as the most prosperous king after [[Psammetichos]], destroys himself not through military defeat but through his own inability to control his temper. The mutilation of a loyal and distinguished servant — an act that violated Egyptian norms of honour and royal conduct — alienated the king's last supporters and handed the throne to the upstart [[Amasis]]. (016-002)
