## Overview

Herodotus's "Account of Egypt" contains one of the earliest comprehensive Greek accounts of the Egyptian pantheon. Throughout his narrative he identifies Egyptian gods by their Greek equivalents — a practice he adopts for the convenience of his Hellenic audience — while recording their Egyptian cult centres, festivals, and distinctive attributes. This list catalogues the principal deities Herodotus mentions, their associated cult cities, and the notable practices he attributes to each.

## Commentary

Herodotus is careful to distinguish between the Egyptian gods and their Hellenic counterparts, noting repeatedly that the names of the gods came to Hellas from Egypt (005-006). He observes that the Egyptians were the first to establish altars, images, and temples, and that the Hellenes borrowed these practices. The Egyptian pantheon as described by Herodotus is hierarchical: the eight primeval gods (including Leto) preceded the twelve later gods, and certain deities — notably the river Nile itself — were not personified in the same way.
