## Overview

Religious processions in ancient Egypt, as recorded by Herodotus, were among the most spectacular public events in the ancient world. These festivals involved mass gatherings, music, sacrifices, and ritual performances that drew pilgrims from across the country. (006-002)

Herodotus credits the Egyptians as the originators of solemn assemblies, processions, and temple approaches — customs which, he observes, the Hellenes adopted only recently, while the Egyptians had practiced them from remote antiquity. (006-002)

## Major Festival Sites

Herodotus enumerates six principal locations where the Egyptians held their most important solemn assemblies:

1. **Bubastis** — in honour of Artemis, the most zealously attended (006-002)
2. **Busiris** — for Isis (Demeter in the Hellenic tongue), where a great temple of Isis stood in the middle of the Delta (006-002)
3. **Sais** — for Athene, site of the famous lamp festival (006-002)
4. **Heliopolis** — for the Sun (Helios), where they performed sacrifice only (006-003)
5. **Buto** — in honour of Leto, sacrifice only (006-003)
6. **Papremis** — for Ares, site of the unique mock-battle ritual (006-002)

## Description

### The Procession to Bubastis

The festival at Bubastis, dedicated to Artemis, was the largest of all Egyptian religious gatherings. Herodotus describes the journey: men and women sailed together in boats, with women playing rattles and men playing flutes, while others sang and clapped. At each riverside city they passed, they brought the boat to land — some women continued their music, others shouted jeers at the local women, some danced, and some stood up and pulled up their garments. (006-002)

Upon reaching Bubastis, they performed great sacrifices, and Herodotus notes that more wine was consumed during this festival than in all the rest of the year combined. According to native accounts, the gathering numbered as many as seven hundred thousand men and women, not counting children. (006-002)

### The Festival of Lamps at Sais

The festival of lamps at Sais, called *Lychnocaia* (the lighting of lamps), was a distinctive nocturnal observance. On the appointed night, the inhabitants kindled lamps in the open air around their houses — small saucers filled with salt and oil, with a floating wick that burned throughout the entire night. (006-002)

Even those Egyptians who could not attend the assembly at Sais observed the rite locally, lighting lamps around their own homes. Thus the illumination was not confined to Sais alone but extended over all Egypt. The reason for the sacred allotment of light and honour to this night is, Herodotus notes, told in a sacred story. (006-003)

### The Mourning Rite at Busiris

At Busiris, the festival for Isis included an intense mourning ritual. After the sacrifice, all present — both men and women, numbering many tens of thousands — beat themselves in grief. The Carians dwelling in Egypt performed this mourning even more intensely than the native Egyptians, cutting their foreheads with knives, a practice which, Herodotus observes, marked them as foreigners and not Egyptians. For whom they mourned, Herodotus declines to say, citing religious prohibition. (006-002)

### The Mock-Battle at Papremis

At Papremis, a unique ceremony honoured Ares. As the sun began to set, a small group of priests guarded the temple entrance with wooden clubs, while more than a thousand men armed with staves of wood assembled opposite them. The image of the god, housed in a small gilded wooden shrine, had been moved the day before to another sacred building. The remaining priests drew a four-wheeled wain bearing the shrine, and the priests at the gateway attempted to prevent its entry. The men under vow came to the god's assistance, striking the defenders, and a violent melee ensued. (006-003)

Herodotus reports that heads were broken and he believed many died of their wounds, though the Egyptians insisted that no one perished. The locals explained the rite's origin through a myth: the mother of Ares dwelt in the temple, and when Ares, raised apart from her, grew up and returned to visit her, the temple attendants — not recognising him — barred his entry. He brought men from another city, overpowered the attendants, and entered to see his mother. Hence the exchange of blows became an established custom at his festival. (006-003)

## Temple Purity

Herodotus also records that the Egyptians were the first to make it a religious principle not to have intercourse in temples, nor to enter temples after intercourse without first bathing. He contrasts this with nearly all other peoples (except the Hellenes), who treat temples no differently from any other place, reasoning that beasts couple in temple precincts without divine displeasure. (006-004)

## Sources

> (006-002)

> When they are coming to the city of Bubastis they do as follows: they sail men and women together, and a great multitude of each sex in every boat; and some of the women have rattles and rattle with them, while some of the men play the flute during the whole time of the voyage, and the rest, both women and men, sing and clap their hands; and when as they sail they come opposite to any city on the way they bring the boat to land, and some of the women continue to do as I have said, others cry aloud and jeer at the women in that city, some dance, and some stand up and pull up their garments.
> (006-002)
