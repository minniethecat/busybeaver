## Overview

The lotos was an aquatic lily that grew abundantly in the flooded plains of the [[Nile River]], serving as an important food source for Egyptians dwelling in the fenlands. [[Herodotus]] described its appearance, harvesting, and preparation, noting its resemblance to certain Greek plants.

## Description

### Growth and Harvesting

When the [[Nile River]] flooded the plains, great numbers of lilies grew in the water, which the Egyptians called _lotos_. The Egyptians cut these plants with a sickle and dried them in the sun. They then extracted the seed from the center of the lotos, which resembled a poppy head, and ground it into flour for baking bread. (008-005)

### The Root

The root of the lotos was also edible. Herodotus described it as round and approximately the size of an apple, with a sweet taste. The Egyptians ate the root both raw and cooked. (008-005)

### Comparison to the Kyrenian Lotos

Herodotus also noted that the thorny acacia tree, from which the Egyptians built their cargo boats, had a form very similar to the Kyrenian lotos. The gum that exuded from this acacia was collected and used by the Egyptians. (009-002)

## Significance

The lotos exemplifies how the [[Nile River]]'s annual inundation shaped Egyptian subsistence. The plant provided multiple food sources — seeds for bread and sweet roots — requiring minimal cultivation effort, which contributed to the relative ease of Egyptian agriculture that Herodotus observed. Its dual role as both food plant and cultural symbol appears throughout Egyptian art and religion.

## Sources

> When the river has become full and the plains have been flooded, there grow in the water great numbers of lilies, which the Egyptians call _lotos_; these they cut with a sickle and dry in the sun. (008-005)

> Then they pound that which grows in the middle of the lotos and which is like the head of a poppy, and they make it into loaves baked with fire. The root of this lotos also is fit for food and is fairly sweet, and is round in shape and about the size of an apple. (008-005)


### Other Nile Lilies

Beyond the lotos proper, Herodotus noted another variety of lily that grew in the [[Nile River]], distinct from the poppy-headed lotos. These lilies bore flowers resembling roses and produced fruit in a separate capsule that grew from the root beside the main plant. The fruit was shaped like a wasp's comb and contained numerous edible seeds, each about the size of an olive stone. The Egyptians ate these seeds both fresh and dried, providing yet another source of food from the Nile's bounty. (008-005)

This observation further illustrates Herodotus's interest in the diversity of Egyptian flora and the ingenuity with which the inhabitants exploited their natural environment for subsistence.
