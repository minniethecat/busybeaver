## Overview

Wine held a significant place in ancient Egyptian life, serving both as an everyday beverage and as an essential element of religious ritual. Herodotus records that wine of grapes was among the daily provisions supplied to Egyptian priests, alongside sacred bread and abundant quantities of oxen and geese. The cultivation of the grapevine and the production of wine were well-established practices by the fifth century BCE, reflecting Egypt's sophisticated agricultural and dietary traditions. (004-001)

## Wine in Religious Practice

The ritual use of wine was integral to Egyptian temple worship. During animal sacrifice, the Egyptians poured libations of wine over the altar so that it ran down upon the victim before the animal's throat was cut. This act of wine-offering was performed while calling upon the god. At the great festival of Dionysos, the Egyptians observed customs similar to those of the Hellenes, though with certain distinctive modifications. Herodotus notes that the Egyptians believed the rites of Dionysos, including the use of wine, originated in Egypt and were transmitted to the Hellenes through Melampus, who learned them from the Egyptians. (004-003) (006-002)

## The Story of the Thief and the Wine

Herodotus recounts a tale from the reign of Rhampsinitos in which wine plays a pivotal role. The king, having constructed a treasury, was robbed by a clever thief who had discovered a removable stone in the building's wall. The king, frustrated by the ongoing thefts, set a trap. When the thief and his brother were caught — one of them fatally trapped — the surviving brother used wine to intoxicate the guards, enabling him to retrieve his brother's body for burial. This story, though legendary, illustrates the everyday availability of wine and its potency in Egyptian society. (011-004)

## Significance

Wine in Herodotus's Egypt functioned on multiple levels: as a daily sustenance for the priestly class, as a ritual libation in temple sacrifice, and as a feature of popular festivals. Its integration into both the sacred and secular spheres of Egyptian life demonstrates the sophistication of Egyptian agriculture and the centrality of fermented beverages to their diet, religion, and social customs.
