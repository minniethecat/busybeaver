## Overview

The Egyptians, in Herodotus's account, were preeminent in the ancient world for their systematic attention to portents, omens, and the fortunes associated with each day of the calendar. Unlike the Greeks, who relied on inspired prophets and wandering seers, the Egyptians had developed an empirical method of divination: they observed unusual events, recorded the outcomes that followed, and consulted these records whenever a similar portent recurred. This practice was not superstition in the casual sense but a form of data collection — a rudimentary empirical science that sought to discern patterns in the apparent randomness of events. Alongside this portent-lore, the Egyptians also practiced temple incubation, seeking divine guidance through dreams received while sleeping in sacred precincts. (008-001)

## Day-Fortunes and Calendrical Divination

Herodotus reports that the Egyptians had determined which god presided over each month and each day of the year. They had elaborated this calendrical mapping into a system of natal divination: by knowing the day on which a person was born, Egyptians believed they could predict what fortunes that person would meet in life, how they would die, and what kind of character they would possess. This invention, Herodotus notes, was subsequently adopted by Greek poets — a reference, presumably, to the calendrical and astrological lore that appears in Hesiod's "Works and Days" and later Hellenistic literature. (008-001)

## The Recording of Portents

The most distinctive feature of Egyptian divination was its systematic character. When a portent occurred — an unusual natural phenomenon, a strange event in the heavens, an anomalous birth among animals or men — the Egyptians did not merely note it and speculate. They recorded it in writing, then waited to see what events followed. Once the outcome was known, they added it to the record alongside the portent. Over generations, this practice produced an empirical catalogue of portent-outcome pairs that functioned as a reference work for interpreting future signs. When a new portent resembled one previously recorded, the Egyptians consulted the archive and predicted that a similar outcome would follow. (008-001)

This method — observe, record, classify, predict — bears a formal resemblance to the procedures of empirical science, even if its underlying assumption (that portents and outcomes are causally linked) was mistaken. Herodotus, characteristically, reports the practice without endorsing or mocking it, treating it as one more datum in his catalogue of Egyptian customs.

## Divination and the Gods

Divination in Egypt was not a human art practiced by freelance seers but a gift assigned to specific deities. Each major oracle in Egypt belonged to a particular god: there were oracles of Heracles, Apollo, Athene, Artemis, Ares, and Zeus, distributed across the country. The most honoured of all was the Oracle of Leto, located in the city of Buto. The methods of divination varied from place to place — there was no single standardized technique — but the common principle was that the god, not the human practitioner, was the source of the revealed knowledge. (008-001)

## Temple Dreams: The Vision of Sethos

The most dramatic example of divine communication through dreams in Herodotus's Egyptian narrative is the story of King Sethos. When the Assyrian army under Sanacharib invaded Egypt and the warrior class refused to fight, Sethos — a priest of Hephaistos who had become king — went into the sanctuary of the temple and lamented his danger before the image of the god. As he wept, sleep came upon him, and in a dream-vision the god appeared, stood beside him, and promised that he would suffer no harm: divine helpers would be sent to defeat the enemy. Sethos, trusting the dream, marched out with a volunteer force of shopkeepers and artisans. That night, field mice swarmed through the Assyrian camp and gnawed through bowstrings, quivers, and shield-handles, leaving the enemy defenceless. This narrative illustrates the Egyptian belief that dreams received in sacred precincts could convey authentic divine messages — and that acting on such dreams could determine the fate of kingdoms. (013-008)

## Significance

Herodotus's account of Egyptian portent-lore and dream interpretation provides a window into a mode of thought that was both religious and protoscientific. The systematic recording of portents and outcomes represents one of the earliest documented attempts to predict the future through the accumulation and analysis of empirical data — a procedure that, whatever its flaws, shares the formal structure of inductive reasoning. The practice of temple incubation, meanwhile, connects Egyptian dream-beliefs to a wider Near Eastern and Mediterranean tradition of seeking divine guidance through sleep. Both practices would influence later Greek and Roman approaches to divination, augury, and oneiromancy.
