## Overview

Chemmis (also called Khemmis; modern Akhmim) was a large city in the Theban nome of Upper Egypt, notable in Herodotus's account for its temple of Perseus and for being one of the few Egyptian communities that adopted Hellenic-style athletic games. Herodotus distinguishes this city from the similarly named floating island of Chemmis near Buto in the Delta, where the goddess Leto was said to have hidden the infant Apollo.

## Location / Geography

The city of Chemmis lay in the Theban nome, in Upper Egypt, on the east bank of the Nile. Herodotus describes it as a substantial settlement in the region south of Memphis. In contrast, the floating island of Chemmis was located in the Delta near the temple of Buto (015-001). Herodotus is explicit that these are distinct places sharing a name — the city in Upper Egypt and the floating island in Lower Egypt.

## Historical Significance

Chemmis was distinguished among Egyptian cities by its unusual adoption of Hellenic customs. Herodotus records that the people of Chemmis held gymnastic games in honour of Perseus, following Hellenic practice — a striking exception to the general Egyptian refusal to adopt foreign usages (008-003). The city's temple to Perseus was a cult centre where the hero was said to appear to the inhabitants, and a sandal of Perseus, two cubits in length, was displayed as a relic. The Chemmites claimed that whenever Perseus appeared, Egypt prospered.

## Herodotus's Description

Herodotus treats the Chemmites' Perseus cult as evidence of a genealogical link between Egypt and Hellas. He notes that Perseus belonged to both Egyptian and Hellenic traditions: the Egyptians claimed him as a native of Chemmis, while the Hellenes traced his lineage through Danae to the Argive royal house. The athletic contests held in his honour — including the full range of Hellenic-style events — were, according to Herodotus, the only instance of such games being celebrated anywhere in Egypt.

## Sources

> But there is a large city named Chemmis in the Theban nome, near Neapolis. In this city there is a temple of Perseus the son of Danae, a square temple, and round it grow date-palms.
> (008-003)

> These people of Chemmis say that Perseus is wont often to appear in their land and often within the temple, and that a sandal which has been worn by him is found sometimes, being in length two cubits, and whenever this appears all Egypt prospers. This they say, and they honor Perseus after the Hellenic fashion, holding a gymnastic contest.
> (008-004)

> This Oracle which is in Egypt is in a great and sacred precinct of Apollo, and the name of the city is Buto; and besides this there is also an island called Chemmis, which is floating in a deep and broad lake near the temple at Buto.
> (015-001)
