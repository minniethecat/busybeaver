## Overview

The Nile was the great highway of ancient Egypt, and Herodotus's account provides detailed observations about the boats, navigation methods, and transport infrastructure that sustained Egyptian civilization. From the acacia-wood cargo vessels that plied the river to the canal systems that connected the Nile to the Red Sea and Lake Moiris, Herodotus documents a society whose transport technology was intimately adapted to its riverine environment. (009-002)

## Nile Riverboats

Herodotus provides a remarkably detailed description of Egyptian cargo boats, which were constructed from the thorny acacia tree. The builders cut the wood into pieces about two cubits in length and arranged them "like bricks," fastening the boat together by running a great number of long bolts through the two-cubit pieces. Cross-pieces were laid over the top, and no ribs were used for the sides. The seams were caulked from within using papyrus. A single steering-oar was passed through the bottom of the boat. The mast was made of acacia wood and the sails of papyrus. These vessels could not sail upstream without a strong following wind but were towed from the bank when moving against the current. Downstream, they were navigated using a raft made of tamarisk wood, fastened together with papyrus reeds, with a perforated stone of about two talents' weight let down ahead of the boat — the raft was carried by the stream and pulled the boat along, while the stone dragged along the river bottom to keep the raft on course. (009-002)

## Canal Systems

Beyond the natural highway of the Nile, Egyptian kings constructed extensive canal networks for both transport and irrigation. The most ambitious of these was the canal built by King Necho, son of Psammetichos, connecting the Nile to the Red Sea. According to Herodotus, Necho was the first to attempt this project, and the canal was completed later by the Persian king Darius. The canal was wide enough for two triremes to be rowed abreast, and its length represented a journey of four days. Necho is said to have lost a hundred and twenty thousand Egyptians during the digging before he abandoned the work, deterred by an oracle warning that he was laboring for the barbarian. (014-001)

Another significant canal was the one connecting the Nile to Lake Moiris. For six months of the year, water flowed from the Nile into the lake through this canal, and for the other six months it flowed back into the river. Whenever the water flowed out, it brought a talent of silver daily into the royal treasury from the fishery — an example of how transport infrastructure generated royal revenue. (014-007)

## River Navigation Challenges

Travel on the upper Nile presented formidable challenges. South of Elephantine, Herodotus reports, the country slopes steeply, and boats had to be hauled with ropes attached to both sides, "as one fastens an ox." If a rope broke, the vessel was immediately carried away by the violence of the stream. This stretch of river required a voyage of about four days, during which the Nile wound like the Maeander River, covering a distance of twelve *schoines*. Beyond this point, the river entered a level plain and flowed around an island called Tachompso. (003-008)

## Military and Imperial Transport

The Nile's transport capacity was essential to Egyptian military power. King Sesostris, Herodotus recounts, was the first Egyptian king to launch ships of war from the Arabian Gulf and subdue the peoples dwelling along the shores of the Erythraean Sea. He sailed until he reached a sea that could no longer be navigated by reason of shoals. After returning to Egypt, Sesostris took a great army and marched overland, subduing every nation in his path — a campaign that combined riverine, maritime, and overland transport. (009-006)

Forced transport of building materials was a feature of large-scale royal projects. During the reign of Cheops, Herodotus reports, some Egyptians were appointed to draw stones from the quarries in the Arabian mountains to the Nile, while others were ordered to receive the stones after they had been carried across the river in boats and draw them to the Libyan mountains. A hundred thousand men labored at a time, working in three-month shifts. (012-002)

## Significance

Herodotus's account of Egyptian transport reveals a civilization in which the river was not merely a geographical feature but the organizing principle of movement, trade, and military power. The acacia-wood cargo boat, with its brick-like construction and papyrus caulking, represents an ingenious adaptation to locally available materials. The canal systems — whether connecting the Nile to the Red Sea or to Lake Moiris — demonstrate the scale of Egyptian hydraulic engineering and its integration with royal finance. And the difficult navigation of the upper Nile, requiring ox-like towing against the current, reveals both the limits and the determination of Egyptian river travel.
