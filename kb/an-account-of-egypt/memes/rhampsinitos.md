## Overview

Rhampsinitos was an [[egypt|Egypt]]ian king who succeeded [[proteus|Proteus]] and ruled during a golden age of prosperity and orderly governance. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] presents him as a figure of immense wealth — surpassing all kings who came after him — and as the central character in one of the longest and most elaborate anecdotes in the *Account of [[egypt|Egypt]]*: the story of the clever thief who outwitted the king's treasury. He is also notable for his legendary descent to the underworld, where he played dice with the goddess Demeter. (011-007) (012-001)

## Background / Biography

Rhampsinitos received the kingdom in succession after [[proteus|Proteus]], according to what the [[egyptian-priesthood|Egyptian priests]] told [[herodotus|Herodotus]]. (011-007) He is sometimes identified by modern scholars with Ramesses II (also known as Rhampsinitus in Greek sources), though [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s account blends historical elements with folkloric motifs. His reign is placed in the 13th century BCE, corresponding approximately to the 19th Dynasty of the New Kingdom period.

As a memorial of himself, Rhampsinitos built the western gateway to the temple of [[hephaistos|Hephaistos]] and erected two colossal statues before it, each twenty-five cubits in height. The [[egypt|Egypt]]ians called the northern statue Summer and the southern one Winter, and they revered the Summer statue while doing the opposite to Winter. (011-007)

## Role in the Account

Rhampsinitos appears in [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s narrative through three main episodes:

**The Treasury and the Clever Thief.** The king amassed a great wealth of silver and had a stone treasury chamber built to store it. The builder, however, secretly designed one stone to be removable, and on his deathbed revealed the secret to his two sons. (011-007) The sons robbed the treasury repeatedly until the king set traps and captured one of them. The trapped thief instructed his brother to cut off his head to prevent identification. (011-007)

The surviving brother then recovered the body through a ruse involving wine-laden donkeys, getting the guards drunk and shaving their right cheeks in mockery. (011-008) (011-009) The king, desperate to catch the thief, placed his own daughter in a brothel to extract confessions, but the thief outwitted her by presenting a corpse's severed arm. Astonished by the thief's cunning, Rhampsinitos issued a proclamation of pardon and reward, and when the thief came forward, the king gave him his daughter in marriage. (011-009)

**Descent to the Underworld.** The [[egypt|Egypt]]ians told [[herodotus|Herodotus]] that Rhampsinitos descended alive to Hades, where he played dice with Demeter, alternately winning and losing, and returned with a golden handkerchief as a gift from the goddess. A festival celebrated by the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians in [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s own time was said to commemorate this descent. (012-001)

**Golden Age of Rule.** Until Rhampsinitos, [[egypt|Egypt]] enjoyed orderly rule and great prosperity. His successor [[cheops|Cheops]], by contrast, closed all the temples, halted sacrifices, and forced the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians into labor to build the great pyramid — inaugurating an age of oppression. (012-002)

## Herodotus's Portrayal

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] reports the stories about Rhampsinitos as accounts given to him by [[egyptian-priesthood|Egyptian priests]], but he is not wholly credulous. He explicitly states that he does not believe the part about the king placing his own daughter in a brothel. (011-009) Regarding the descent to Hades, [[herodotus|Herodotus]] is uncertain whether the festival he observed was celebrated for this reason or for some other cause. (012-001)

His portrayal of Rhampsinitos is consequently a mixture of admiration — for the king's wealth, his building projects, and the prosperity of his reign — and the kind of marvelous storytelling that characterizes much of his [[egypt|Egypt]]ian ethnography. Rhampsinitos serves as a narrative foil to the tyrannical [[cheops|Cheops]], marking the transition from a remembered golden age to an era of despotism. (012-002)

## Sources

> After Proteus, they told me, Rhampsinitos received in succession the kingdom, who left as a memorial of himself that gateway to the temple of Hephaistos which is turned towards the West, and in front of the gateway he set up two statues, in height five-and-twenty cubits, of which the one which stands on the North side is called by the Egyptians Summer and the one on the South side Winter; and to that one which they call Summer they do reverence and make offerings, while to the other which is called Winter they do the opposite of these things.
> (011-007)

> After these things they said this king went down alive to that place which by the Hellenes is called Hades, and there played at dice with Demeter, and in some throws he overcame her and in others he was overcome by her; and he came back again having as a gift from her a handkerchief of gold: and they told me that because of the going down of Rhampsinitos the Egyptians after he came back celebrated a feast, which I know of my own knowledge also that they still observe even to my time.
> (012-001)

> The thief accordingly trusting to the proclamation came to the king, and Rhampsinitos greatly marvelled at him, and gave him this daughter of his to wife, counting him to be the most knowing of all men; for as the Egyptians were distinguished from all other men, so was he from the other Egyptians.
> (011-009)

The story of the treasury and the thief occupies much of [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s 11th section (011-007 to 011-009), making it one of the longest continuous narratives about any single [[egypt|Egypt]]ian ruler in the *Account of [[egypt|Egypt]]*. The transition from Rhampsinitos's prosperous reign to the tyranny of [[cheops|Cheops]] is recorded at (012-002).
