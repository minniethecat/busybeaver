## Overview

Helen of Troy is one of the most famous figures in Greek mythology — the Spartan queen whose extraordinary beauty and alleged abduction by [[alexander-paris|Alexander (Paris)]] served as the cause of the Trojan War. In [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s *Account of [[egypt|Egypt]]*, however, the [[egyptian-priesthood|Egyptian priests]] offer a radically different version of Helen's story: they claim that she was never in [[troy|Troy]] at all but was detained in [[egypt|Egypt]] by the righteous King [[proteus|Proteus]], who kept both Helen and the stolen treasure safe until her husband [[menelaos|Menelaos]] could retrieve her. (010-005) This alternative account, which [[herodotus|Herodotus]] treats with considerable sympathy, challenges the Homeric tradition and forms one of the longest and most intriguing digressions in Book Two.

## Background / Biography

Helen was, in the Greek tradition, the daughter of [[zeus|Zeus]] (disguised as a swan) and Leda, and the wife of [[menelaos|Menelaos]], king of Sparta. [[herodotus|Herodotus]], however, is less interested in her lineage than in her movements during the Trojan War. According to the priests of [[memphis|Memphis]], when [[alexander-paris|Alexander (Paris)]] abducted Helen from Sparta and sailed for [[troy|Troy]], contrary winds drove his ship to the coast of [[egypt|Egypt]], where he landed at the Canobic mouth of [[nile-river|the Nile]] near the city of Taricheiai. (010-005)

At that location stood a temple of [[heracles|Heracles]] with a long-standing custom of granting sanctuary to runaway slaves. Alexander's own attendants, learning of this custom, fled to the temple and denounced their master, revealing the full story of his abduction of Helen and his betrayal of [[menelaos|Menelaos]]'s hospitality. (010-005) The warden of the river-mouth, [[thonis|Thonis]], sent word to [[proteus|Proteus]] at [[memphis|Memphis]], who ordered that Alexander be brought before him. When questioned by [[proteus|Proteus]], Alexander attempted to conceal Helen's whereabouts, but the suppliant attendants convicted him of falsehood. (010-005)

[[proteus|Proteus]] delivered his judgment: he would not execute Alexander — for he had a principle against killing strangers driven to his shores by winds — but he would confiscate both Helen and the stolen wealth, keeping them safe for [[menelaos|Menelaos]]. Alexander and his crew were banished from [[egypt|Egypt]] within three days. Helen thus remained in [[egypt|Egypt]], under [[proteus|Proteus]]'s protection, for the duration of the Trojan War. (010-005)

## Role in the Account

Helen functions in [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s narrative not as the romantic figure of epic poetry but as a piece of evidence in the historian's argument about the reliability of different sources. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] uses the [[egyptian-priesthood|Egyptian priests]]' account of Helen to challenge the Homeric version of the Trojan War and to advance a broader thesis about the relationship between myth and historical truth.

The key implication of the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian version is that the Trojan War was fought over a phantom: the Trojans, when besieged by the Hellenic army, truthfully replied that Helen was not in their city but in [[egypt|Egypt]]. The [[hellenes|Hellenes]], believing themselves mocked, persisted in the siege and eventually sacked [[troy|Troy]] — only to find no Helen there. (011-004) Only then did they believe the story and dispatch [[menelaos|Menelaos]] to [[egypt|Egypt]], where he was received hospitably by [[proteus|Proteus]] and recovered Helen unharmed, along with all his wealth. (011-004)

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] finds this version more historically plausible than the Homeric tradition. He argues that if Helen had truly been in [[troy|Troy]], King [[priam|Priam]] would certainly have returned her to avoid the destruction of his city. (011-005) The historian goes further, identifying a temple at [[memphis|Memphis]] dedicated to a deity the locals called the "foreign Aphrodite" as the probable temple of Helen herself — a conjecture based on the coincidence of location and tradition. (010-004)

## Herodotus's Portrayal

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] never actually describes Helen as a person in his own words, nor does he recount her appearance, character, or thoughts. She appears in the narrative only as a figure in a story told by others — first by the priests, then by the Homeric poets, and finally by [[herodotus|Herodotus]] himself as historical critic. This absence is itself significant: Helen, in the *Account of [[egypt|Egypt]]*, is not a character but a disputed fact, and [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s handling of her story reveals his method of interrogating conflicting traditions.

The historian's sympathy for the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian version is evident, and his critique of the Homeric account is precise and reasoned. He acknowledges that Homer knew of Alexander's wanderings to [[egypt|Egypt]] but chose a different narrative for poetic purposes. (010-006) This argument — that poets select and reshape facts to suit their art — is a sophisticated piece of literary criticism that anticipates modern approaches to the *Iliad* and the *Odyssey*.
