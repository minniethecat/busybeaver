## Overview

Neco (also known as Necos, Nekau, or Necho II) was a pharaoh of the 26th Dynasty of Egypt, the son and successor of Psammetichos. Herodotus records him as a builder-king who attempted to construct a canal from the Nile to the Red Sea and as a military leader who defeated the Syrians at Magdolos.

## Background / Biography

Neco was the son of Psammetichos (Psamtik I), the founder of the 26th Dynasty who reunited Egypt after Assyrian domination. Neco inherited the throne and reigned for sixteen years. (015-004) His rule fell during a period of renewed Egyptian engagement with the Near East and ambitious infrastructure projects.

## Role in the Account

Herodotus describes Neco primarily as an engineer-king. He was the first ruler to attempt the channel leading from the Nile to the Erythraian Sea (the Red Sea) — a massive work later completed by the Persian king Dareios. The canal, Herodotus reports, took a voyage of four days to traverse and was wide enough for two triremes to pass side by side rowed by oars. Water was brought into it from the Nile, and it ran from a point a little above the city of Bubastis past Patumos the Arabian city. (015-004) During its construction, one hundred and twenty thousand Egyptians perished, causing Neco to abandon the project after an oracle warned him that he was laboring for a foreign power.

On land, Neco engaged in battle with the Syrians at Magdolos (identified with Megiddo in Palestine), conquering them and afterwards taking Cadytis, a great city of Syria. (015-005) He dedicated the armor he wore during these conquests to Apollo, sending it to the Branchidai at Miletos.

Herodotus also credits Neco with dispatching a Phoenician naval expedition that circumnavigated Africa — sailing from the Red Sea around the southern cape and returning to Egypt through the Pillars of Heracles (the Strait of Gibraltar). This feat, Herodotus notes with some skepticism, included the remarkable observation that the sailors saw the sun on their right hand (to the north) as they rounded the southern part of the continent — a detail that modern scholars consider strong evidence the voyage actually occurred.

## Herodotus's Portrayal

Herodotus presents Neco as a capable but not entirely fortunate ruler. His canal project, though visionary, cost many Egyptian lives and was ultimately abandoned on religious advice. His military achievements were real but his sixteen-year reign ended in death, passing the kingdom to his son Psammis. The narrative positions Neco as a transitional figure between the great Psammetichos and the later pharaohs of the 26th Dynasty.
