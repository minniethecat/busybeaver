## Overview

In [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s *Account of [[egypt|Egypt]]*, [[Apollo]] appears primarily through the lens of *interpretatio graeca* — the Greek practice of identifying foreign deities with their own. Apollo is equated with the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian god Horus, the son of [[osiris|Osiris]] ([[Isis and Osiris]]), and his mythology is interwoven with the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian narrative of divine kingship. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] also records the dedication of spoils to Apollo by [[egypt|Egypt]]ian kings, reflecting the cultural exchange between [[egypt|Egypt]] and the Greek world.

## Apollo and the Egyptian Pantheon

The [[egyptian-priesthood|Egyptian priests]] recounted to [[herodotus|Herodotus]] a myth in which [[Apollo]] plays a central role. The goddess [[Leto]], one of the eight primordial gods dwelling at [[Buto]] where her oracle was established, "received Apollo from [[isis|Isis]] as a charge and preserved him, concealing him in the island which is said now to be a floating island, at that time when [[typhon|Typhon]] came after him seeking everywhere and desiring to find the son of [[osiris|Osiris]]" (015-002). This account maps Apollo directly onto the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian Horus — the son of [[isis-and-osiris|Isis and Osiris]] who was hidden from the destructive god Set ([[typhon|Typhon]] in Greek).

The oracle of [[Apollo]] and [[Artemis]] was, according to the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians, the offspring of [[Dionysos]] and [[Isis]] — further evidence of the syncretic identification of Greek and [[egypt|Egypt]]ian deities (015-002).

## The Oracle at Buto

The Greek god Apollo was venerated at the great oracle of [[Leto]] in the city of [[Buto]], located near the Sebennytic mouth of [[nile-river|the Nile]]. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] describes this oracle as "worthy to be described" and notes it was "sacred to [[leto|Leto]]" (015-001). This [[egypt|Egypt]]ian oracle was one of the most important religious centers mentioned in the *Account*, and its association with Apollo through the [[leto|Leto]]-Apollo-[[artemis|Artemis]] triad illustrates how thoroughly the [[hellenes|Greeks]] mapped their own religious geography onto [[egypt|Egypt]].

## Egyptian Kings and Apollo

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] records that King [[Necos]] ([[necos|Necho II]]), after his military campaigns against the [[syria|Syrians]] at Magdolos and the conquest of the great city of [[Cadytis]], "dedicated to Apollo, sending it to Branchidai of the Milesians" the dress he wore during these conquests (015-005). Branchidai, the famous sanctuary of Apollo at Didyma near Miletus, was a major oracular center in the Greek world — the dedication by an [[egypt|Egypt]]ian pharaoh demonstrates the diplomatic and cultural ties between Saite [[egypt|Egypt]] and the [[ionians|Ionian Greeks]].

King [[Amasis]] ([[amasis|Ahmose II]]) further promoted the worship of Greek deities in [[egypt|Egypt]] by granting land in [[Naucratis]] for Greek settlers to "set up altars and make sacred enclosures for their gods." The largest and most renowned of these enclosures was the Hellenion, founded jointly by several Greek cities, which included altars to Apollo among the Hellenic gods worshipped there (016-005).

## Herodotus's Comparative Method

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] mentions Apollo in the context of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian divination practices, noting that the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians "found out also to what god each month and each day belongs, and what fortunes a man will meet with who is born on any particular day" — inventions later taken up by Greek poets (008-001). This pattern of attributing the origins of Greek religious knowledge to [[egypt|Egypt]] is characteristic of [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s broader methodology.
