# Ammon

## Overview

Ammon ([[egypt|Egypt]]ian Amun) was one of the principal gods of [[egypt|Egypt]], whom the [[hellenes|Hellenes]] identified with [[zeus|Zeus]]. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] documents the [[oracle-of-ammon|Oracle of Ammon]] in the Libyan desert, the god's theriomorphic representation with a ram's head, and his role in [[egypt|Egypt]]ian theological speculation about the sources of [[nile-river|the Nile]]. The cult of Ammon had spread beyond [[egypt|Egypt]] to the Greek world, where the oracle's reputation for truthfulness was widely acknowledged.

## Description

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] invokes the authority of the [[oracle-of-ammon|Oracle of Ammon]] to support his own geographical reasoning about [[egypt|Egypt]]'s extent. He states that "the answer given by the [[oracle-of-ammon|Oracle of Ammon]] bears witness in support of my opinion that [[egypt|Egypt]] is of the extent which I declare it to be in my account; and of this answer I heard after I had formed my own opinion about [[egypt|Egypt]]" (002-004). The oracle's testimony carries weight because of its acknowledged veracity.

The oracle was also a source of geographical knowledge beyond [[egypt|Egypt]]. Men from [[cyrene|Cyrene]] told [[herodotus|Herodotus]] that "they had been to the [[oracle-of-ammon|Oracle of Ammon]], and had come to speech with Etearchos king of the Ammonians: and it happened that after speaking of other matters they fell to discourse about [[nile-river|the Nile]] and how no one knew the sources of it" (004-001). Etearchos, the king of the Ammonians, related a story of Nasamonian explorers who had traveled deep into Africa, providing one of the few accounts [[herodotus|Herodotus]] could collect about [[nile-river|the Nile]]'s upper reaches.

The iconography of Ammon was distinctive. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] records that the Thebans worshipped "[[zeus|Zeus]] with the face of a ram; and the Ammonians do so also after their example, being settlers both from the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians and from the [[ethiopia-region|Ethiopia]]ns, and using a language which is a medley of both tongues: and in my opinion it is from this god that the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians call [[zeus|Zeus]] _Amun_" (005-001). The ram-headed representation links [[egypt|Egypt]]ian Amun to the Libyan Ammonians and explains the etymology connecting Amun to the Greek [[zeus|Zeus]].

The foundation legend of the oracle connects it to the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian [[thebes|Thebes]]: "the priests of the [[zeus|Theban Zeus]] told me that two women in the service of the temple had been carried away from [[thebes|Thebes]] by [[phoenicians|Phenicians]]... the one of them, they say, bade the Libyans make an [[oracle-of-ammon|Oracle of Ammon]]; and this also is of [[zeus|Zeus]]" (006-001). Through this chain — [[thebes|Thebes]] to Phoenicia to Libya — the oracle of [[zeus|Zeus]] at Dodona and the oracle of Ammon both traced their origins to [[egypt|Egypt]]ian [[thebes|Thebes]].

## Historical Context

By the 5th century BCE, the [[oracle-of-ammon|Oracle of Ammon]] at Siwa Oasis had achieved pan-Mediterranean fame. The oracle was consulted by [[hellenes|Greeks]], [[egypt|Egypt]]ians, and Libyans alike. Its reputation would grow further in the 4th century BCE when Alexander the Great visited Siwa and was reportedly acknowledged as the son of Zeus-Ammon. [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s account captures the oracle at a period when it was already respected but had not yet reached its apogee of international prestige.

## Significance

Ammon serves a specific function in [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s [[egypt|Egypt]]ian narrative: it is evidence of [[egypt|Egypt]]'s cultural and religious influence radiating outward. The god's worship by the Ammonians (a mixed [[egypt|Egypt]]ian-[[ethiopia-region|Ethiopia]]n people), the foundation of the oracle by a Theban priestess, and the Hellenic identification of Amun with [[zeus|Zeus]] all demonstrate [[egypt|Egypt]] as a source of religious diffusion. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] uses the Ammon material to reinforce his broader thesis that Greek religion derived substantially from [[egypt|Egypt]]ian origins.
