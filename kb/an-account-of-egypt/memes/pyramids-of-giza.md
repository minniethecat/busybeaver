## Overview

The three great pyramids at Giza — built by the Fourth Dynasty pharaohs Cheops, Chephren, and Mykerinos — are among the most celebrated monuments of the ancient world. Herodotus devotes considerable attention to their construction, dimensions, and the circumstances of their builders, providing one of the earliest detailed accounts of these structures in Western literature. (012-002) (012-003) (013-001)

## Location / Geography

The pyramids of Giza are situated on the desert plateau west of the Nile, approximately ten miles southwest of central Cairo and near the ancient city of Memphis. They stand on the edge of the western desert necropolis, aligned with the cardinal directions.

## Historical Significance

Herodotus records the pyramid of Cheops (the Great Pyramid) as having taken twenty years to build, with a hundred thousand men working in three-month shifts. (012-003) The causeway for transporting stone from the quarries required ten years alone. The pyramid was built in steps, and the stones were raised with machines made of short pieces of timber. (012-003) Cheops's brother Chephren built the second pyramid, smaller than Cheops's but still immense. (012-004) Mykerinos built the third and smallest, of Ethiopian stone up to half its height, which Herodotus defends against the Greek misconception that it was built by the courtesan Rhodopis. (013-001)
