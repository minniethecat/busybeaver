## Overview

Dodona was one of the most ancient and revered oracular sanctuaries in the Greek world, located in the region of Epirus in northwestern Greece. In his "Account of Egypt," Herodotus draws a direct connection between the oracular traditions of Dodona and those of Egypt, reporting that the method of delivering oracles at Dodona closely resembled that at Thebes in Egypt. (006-001) He further records that the prophetesses of Dodona themselves traced their institution's origins to Egyptian Thebes.

## Location / Geography

Dodona was situated in a remote valley of Epirus, in the shadow of Mount Tomaros. Unlike the great oracular sites of Delphi or Delos, Dodona lay on the periphery of the Greek world — a location that made its antiquity all the more significant in Herodotus's reckoning. Its isolation and association with the Pelasgians, the pre-Hellenic inhabitants of Greece, contributed to its reputation as a primaeval sanctuary.

## Historical Significance

According to Herodotus, the Egyptian priests at Thebes told him that two priestesses had been carried away from Thebes by Phoenicians — one to Libya (where she founded the Oracle of Ammon at Siwa) and the other to Greece, where she established the oracle at Dodona. (006-001) The Dodonaean priestesses themselves confirmed this account, naming their founders as Promeneia (the eldest), Timarete, and Nicandra (the youngest). The other attendants at the temple gave supporting testimony.

## Divination Practices

The method of divination at Dodona, as described by Herodotus, involved interpreting the rustling of leaves of the sacred oak tree and the sound of bronze vessels struck by the wind. Herodotus notes the striking similarity between the oracular methods of Thebes in Egypt and those of Dodona, observing that "the ways of delivering oracles too at Thebes in Egypt and at Dodona closely resemble each other, as it happens, and also the method of divination by victims has come from Egypt." (006-001)

## The Dove Legend

Herodotus offers a rationalizing interpretation of the legend that the oracle was founded by a black dove. He reasons that the "dove" was actually an Egyptian woman — a foreigner whose speech sounded to the Pelasgians like the chattering of a bird. (006-001) When she learned to speak Greek, the story transformed into a dove that spoke with human voice. The dove's black colour, Herodotus explains, simply signified that the woman was Egyptian. This interpretation reflects Herodotus's characteristic method of demythologizing Greek traditions through ethnographic reasoning.

## Pelasgian Connection

Herodotus also connects Dodona to the religious practices of the Pelasgians, the aboriginal inhabitants of Greece. He reports learning at Dodona that the Pelasgians originally made sacrifices to unnamed gods — simply calling them "gods" (theoi) from the notion that they had "set (thentes) all things in order." (005-006) The naming and identification of the gods, Herodotus suggests, came later from Egypt, a claim central to his broader argument about the Egyptian origins of Greek religion.

## Significance

Dodona occupies a pivotal position in Herodotus's argument for the Egyptian origin of Greek religious institutions. By linking Dodona directly to Egyptian Thebes — through both Egyptian priestly testimony and Dodonaean confirmation — Herodotus weaves the oracle into his larger thesis about cultural transmission from Egypt to Greece. The Dodona passage thus serves as both ethnography and cultural genealogy, illustrating Herodotus's method of using specific sites and institutions to trace broader patterns of civilizational influence.
