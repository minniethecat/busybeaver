## Overview

Oxen occupied a central position in Egyptian religion, diet, and daily life as described by Herodotus. The male of the ox kind was considered sacred to the god Epaphos, and an elaborate system of ritual inspection governed which animals could be offered in sacrifice. At the same time, oxen provided abundant meat for the priestly class and served as a benchmark of size for describing other animals of the Nile region. (004-006) (004-005)

## Sacred Status and Ritual Inspection

The Egyptians regarded the male ox as belonging to Epaphos, and on this account they subjected every candidate for sacrifice to a meticulous examination. A priest appointed for this purpose inspected the beast both standing and lying down, searching for even a single black hair upon its hide — the presence of which rendered the animal ritually unclean and unfit for offering. The priest also examined the tongue and the tail hairs to ensure they were of the proper natural growth. Only an ox that passed every test without blemish was marked with a seal and approved for sacrifice. The penalty for offering an unsealed animal was death. (004-006)

## Oxen as Provision and Measure

Beyond their sacred role, oxen were a staple provision for the Egyptian priesthood. Herodotus records that the priests enjoyed an abundant daily allotment of "flesh of oxen and geese," along with sacred bread and wine, all supplied at no cost to themselves. The size of the ox also served as a standard of comparison: when describing the river-horse of the Nile, Herodotus notes that it is "in size as large as the largest ox," and describes the crocodile as having feet like those of an ox. The ox thus functioned both as a dietary mainstay and as a familiar point of reference for the measurement of other creatures. (004-005) (007-002)
