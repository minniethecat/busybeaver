## Overview

The Trojan War, the legendary conflict between the Achaians (Greeks) and the city of Troy (Ilion), is examined by Herodotus in his "Account of Egypt" not as myth but as a historical event subject to rational inquiry. Through the testimony of Egyptian priests at Memphis, Herodotus presents an alternative account of the war — one that challenges the Homeric version by asserting that Helen of Troy never actually reached Ilion. (011-001)

## The Egyptian Account

According to the priests of Memphis, when Alexander (Paris) abducted Helen from Sparta and set sail for Troy, adverse winds drove his ship to the Egyptian coast. There, the servants of Proteus, the king of Memphis, arrested Alexander and brought him before the king. Proteus, upon learning of Alexander's crime against his host Menelaos, condemned Alexander's actions and confiscated Helen along with all the stolen treasures, sending Alexander away empty-handed. (011-001) Helen thus remained in Egypt for the duration of the war.

## Herodotus's Rational Analysis

Herodotus endorses the Egyptian version of events through a series of rational arguments. He finds it inconceivable that Priam and the Trojans — facing the destruction of their city and the deaths of their own sons — would have refused to return Helen if they actually possessed her. (011-005) He argues that even if Priam had been initially willing, the mounting casualties would have compelled a surrender. Furthermore, he notes that the kingdom was not destined for Alexander (Paris) but for Hector, who was both older and more capable, and who would have had every reason to end his brother's destructive transgression.

## Homeric Knowledge and Suppression

Herodotus claims that Homer himself knew the Egyptian version of events but deliberately chose not to use it in his epic because it did not suit the dramatic requirements of the Iliad. This assertion — that the poet suppressed historical truth for artistic purposes — represents one of the earliest examples of literary criticism applied to the Homeric corpus. (011-005) Herodotus supports this claim by citing passages in the Iliad that he believes allude to Alexander's detour to Egypt.

## Egyptian Setting in the Odyssey

Beyond the Iliad, Herodotus draws supporting evidence from the Odyssey, noting that Menelaos is described as visiting Egypt after the war and recovering Helen from Proteus's keeping. In the Homeric account, Menelaos speaks with Proteus (identified by Herodotus as the Egyptian king, not a sea-god) and receives counsel on how to return home — details that Herodotus takes as oblique confirmation of the Egyptian tradition.

## Significance

Herodotus's treatment of the Trojan War exemplifies his rationalizing approach to Greek mythology and his reliance on Egyptian priestly authority. By preferring the Egyptian account over the Homeric, he asserts the primacy of priestly testimony over poetic tradition — a methodological stance that runs throughout the "Account of Egypt." The Trojan War narrative also serves a structural function: it establishes Egypt as both a repository of historical truth and a geographical waypoint in the transmission of Greek heroic legend. The war's connection to Egypt through Helen and Proteus transforms what might have been a purely Greek story into a cross-cultural event, reinforcing Herodotus's broader argument about the deep entanglement of Greek and Egyptian histories.
