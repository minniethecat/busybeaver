Hellas — the Greek world — serves as the implicit and explicit point of contrast throughout [[Herodotus]]'s account of Egypt. Herodotus, a Greek himself writing for a Greek audience, constantly measures Egyptian customs, geography, and history against Hellenic norms, using the familiar to illuminate the foreign and the foreign to reflect upon the familiar.

## As a Frame of Reference

Throughout his description of Egypt, [[Herodotus]] employs Hellas as the lens through which Egyptian phenomena are rendered intelligible to his audience. Egyptian deities are identified with their Greek equivalents: [[Ammon]] with [[Zeus]], [[Hathor]] with [[Aphrodite]], [[Osiris]] with [[Dionysos]], [[Horus]] with [[Apollo]]. Egyptian geography is measured against Greek landmarks: the [[Nile Delta]]'s coastline is compared to the coast of [[Ionia]], and the distance from the sea to [[Heliopolis]] is likened to that from [[Athens]] to certain Greek sanctuaries. (002-002)

## The Egyptian View of Hellas

[[Herodotus]] also records how Egyptians perceived the Greek world. The Egyptian priests told him that the Greeks were, by Egyptian standards, a young people whose historical memory extended back only a few generations — in contrast to Egypt's recorded history of more than three hundred and forty generations of kings. (002-006) The Egyptians maintained a certain disdain for Greek claims to antiquity, which [[Herodotus]] reports without fully endorsing or rejecting.

Egyptian knowledge of Hellas was filtered through maritime contact: the [[Canobus|Canobic]] mouth of the Nile, with its temple of [[Heracles]], was a primary point of entry for Greek travellers and traders. Egyptian kings from [[Psammetichos]] onward employed [[Ionians|Ionian]] and [[Carians|Carian]] mercenaries, establishing sustained contact between the two worlds.

## Herodotus's Comparative Method

[[Herodotus]]'s ethnographic method relies on systematic comparison between Hellas and Egypt. Egyptian religious practices — circumcision, dietary restrictions, animal cults — are described in terms of their similarity to or divergence from Greek customs. The Egyptian climate, the behaviour of the Nile, and the character of the Egyptian people are all evaluated against what a Greek reader would consider normal. This comparative framework is central to the intellectual architecture of the *Account of Egypt*.
