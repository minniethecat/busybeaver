## Overview

Circumcision was one of the distinctive bodily customs of the ancient Egyptians, noted by Herodotus as part of their broader pattern of doing things "in a way opposite to other men." The practice was widespread among Egyptians and, according to Herodotus, was adopted from them by several neighboring peoples.

## Description

Herodotus lists circumcision among the customs that set the Egyptians apart from other nations. He states that the Egyptians practiced circumcision for reasons of cleanliness, "thinking it better to be clean than comely." (004-004) The practice was so deeply ingrained in Egyptian identity that it served as a cultural marker: peoples who adopted circumcision were seen as having aligned themselves with Egyptian ways.

According to Herodotus, the Colchians, the Ethiopians, and the Syrians of Palestine all practiced circumcision, and the Colchians in particular were said to be of Egyptian origin because they shared this custom along with their dark skin and woolly hair. (004-004) The Phoenicians and the Syrians of Palestine themselves acknowledged learning circumcision from the Egyptians.

The practice was performed on both men and, according to some traditions, women, though Herodotus's account focuses on male circumcision. The procedure was typically carried out in youth.

## Herodotus's Commentary

Herodotus treats circumcision as a key piece of ethnographic evidence — a bodily practice that reveals cultural descent and contact. His argument that the Colchians descended from Egyptians rests partly on shared circumcision, reflecting the Greek assumption that such customs were inherited rather than independently invented. He makes no judgment on the practice itself, presenting it as a neutral custom worth recording for its comparative value.

## Significance

Circumcision illustrates Herodotus's method as an early ethnographer. Rather than dismissing foreign customs as barbaric, he used bodily practices as evidence for historical relationships between peoples. The Egyptian practice of circumcision thus serves a dual purpose in his history: it is both a distinctive feature of Egyptian life and a tool for tracing cultural diffusion across the ancient Mediterranean and Near East.
