## Overview

Animal sacrifice was the central ritual act of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian religious practice in the 5th century BCE, as observed and recorded by [[herodotus|Herodotus]] during his travels to [[memphis|Memphis]], [[thebes|Thebes]], and [[heliopolis|Heliopolis]]. The [[egypt|Egypt]]ians offered a restricted set of ritually pure animals — male oxen, calves, geese, goats, sheep, and swine — to specific deities according to regional cults and seasonal festivals. The sacrificial system married meticulous purity testing, elaborate altar ritual, and a distinctive practice of transferring imprecations onto the severed heads of victims. The entire edifice was governed by a priestly class whose authority extended to prescribing the death penalty for sacrificing an unsealed beast. (004-006)

## Description

### Which Animals Were Sacrificed

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] provides a precise, limited catalog of lawful sacrificial animals. The [[egypt|Egypt]]ians considered it "not lawful to sacrifice even beasts, except swine and the males of oxen and calves (such of them as are clean) and geese." (005-003) The female of the ox kind was categorically excluded from sacrifice, being sacred to [[isis|Isis]], "for the figure of [[isis|Isis]] is in the form of a woman with cow's horns." (004-006) Cows were revered above all other cattle, and the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians would neither kiss a Greek on the mouth nor use a Greek's knife or cooking vessels, nor taste the flesh of a clean animal cut with the knife of a Hellene. (004-006)

The pig occupied an anomalous position. Accounted "an abominable animal," it was so unclean that anyone touching one would plunge into the river fully clothed for purification. Swineherds, though native [[egypt|Egypt]]ians, were barred from all temples and could only marry among themselves. (005-005) Yet the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians sacrificed swine to the Moon and to Dionysos at the full-moon festival and then ate the flesh — though never on any subsequent day. The poor, unable to afford a live pig, shaped pigs of dough and offered these baked substitutes. (005-005) On the eve of the Dionysos festival, each [[egypt|Egypt]]ian killed a pig by cutting its throat before his own doors and then returned it to the swineherd who had sold it. (005-005)

### Purity Testing of Oxen

The selection of a sacrificial ox required an elaborate priestly examination. The examining priest inspected the animal both standing and lying on its back, drew out its tongue, and examined the hairs of its tail for natural growth. If a single black hair was found upon the beast, it was rejected as unclean. A beast that passed inspection was marked by rolling papyrus around its horns, plastered with sealing-earth, and stamped with the priest's signet ring. Sacrificing an unsealed beast was punishable by death. (004-006)

### The Ritual Procedure

The sacrificial ritual proceeded through several distinct stages. The sealed beast was led to the altar and a fire kindled. Priests poured libations of wine over the altar so that it ran down upon the victim and called upon the god. They then cut the animal's throat and severed the head from the body. (004-006)

The treatment of the severed head was a peculiarly [[egypt|Egypt]]ian feature. The priests uttered many imprecations upon it, praying that whatever evil might befall the sacrificers or the land of [[egypt|Egypt]] would fall upon the head instead. Where [[hellenes|Hellenes]] were present in the marketplace, the heads were sold to them; where no [[hellenes|Greeks]] resided, they were cast into the river. By reason of this custom, no [[egypt|Egypt]]ian ate the head of any animal. (004-006)

After flaying the body, the priests removed the lower entrails but left the upper entrails and the fat. They severed the legs, the end of the loin, the shoulders, and the neck. The body cavity was then filled with consecrated loaves, honey, raisins, figs, frankincense, myrrh, and every kind of spice, drenched in abundant oil before being offered to the fire. (004-006) The sacrifice was performed after fasting. While the offerings burned, all participants beat themselves in mourning; when this ceased, they feasted upon the unburnt portion of the sacrifice. (004-006)

### Regional Variation

[[egypt|Egypt]]ian sacrificial practice varied by region and cult. The Thebans and those of the Theban district sacrificed goats and abstained from sheep, revering the ram as sacred to [[ammon|Zeus-Ammon]]. On the annual feast of [[zeus|Zeus]], they cut up and flayed a single ram, covered the image of [[zeus|Zeus]] with its skin, and all present beat themselves in lamentation before burying the ram in a sacred tomb. (005-001) The [[mendes|Mendesian]]s practiced the reverse: they abstained from goats and sacrificed sheep, venerating Pan — called [[mendes|Mendes]] in the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian tongue — whom they counted among the eight primordial gods and represented with the face and legs of a goat. Among the goats of the [[mendes|Mendesian district]], one he-goat received extraordinary veneration, and its death occasioned public mourning throughout the entire region. (005-004) Only [[isis-and-osiris|Isis and Osiris]] were worshipped universally by all [[egypt|Egypt]]ians, transcending these regional divisions. (005-001)

## Historical Context

Animal sacrifice in 5th-century BCE [[egypt|Egypt]] was embedded in a temple economy that sustained a powerful hereditary priesthood. The priests — one of the seven classes of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian society (015-011) — received daily allocations of oxen and goose flesh, sacred bread, and grape wine, all drawn from temple stores supplied by offerings and endowed lands. (004-005) The sacrificial system was not merely a religious observance but an economic institution that redistributed meat to the priestly class and, through the burning of choice portions, maintained reciprocal relations between the human and divine realms.

The interruption of sacrifice constituted a political and cosmic crisis. When [[cheops|Cheops]] ([[cheops|Khufu]]) ascended the throne, he "shut up all the temples, and having first kept them from sacrifices there, he then bade all the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians work for him" on the construction of the [[pyramids-of-giza|Great Pyramid]]. (012-002) [[herodotus|Herodotus]] presents the cessation of sacrifice as the first and gravest of [[cheops|Cheops]]'s impieties. Conversely, the ritual of libation was tied to kingship: [[psammetichos|Psammetichos]]'s destiny as sole king of [[egypt|Egypt]] was foretold when, at a sacrifice in the temple of [[hephaistos|Hephaistos]], the chief-priest brought only eleven golden cups for the twelve kings, and [[psammetichos|Psammetichos]], standing last, poured his libation from his bronze helmet. (014-008)

## Herodotus's Commentary

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] approached [[egypt|Egypt]]ian animal sacrifice with his characteristic blend of close observation and comparative analysis. He used the sacrificial code as evidence against Greek myths he considered absurd — most notably the tale that the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians tried to sacrifice [[heracles|Heracles]]. "How should they for whom it is not lawful to sacrifice even beasts, except swine and the males of oxen and calves (such of them as are clean) and geese, how should these sacrifice human beings?" (005-003) The narrowness of the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian sacrificial canon served, in his argument, as proof that the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians could not have practiced human sacrifice.

He also identified Egyptian sacrifice as one of the channels through which Greek religion received its forms. The Pelasgians, he reports, originally sacrificed to unnamed gods, only later adopting the specific names they learned from [[egypt|Egypt]]. "From this time they sacrificed using the names of the gods, and from the Pelasgians the [[hellenes|Hellenes]] afterwards received them." (005-007) The [[egypt|Egypt]]ians were likewise "the first of men who made solemn assemblies and processions and approaches to the temples," and from them the [[hellenes|Hellenes]] learned these practices. (006-002) [[herodotus|Herodotus]] traces the very art of divination by sacrificial victims — examining entrails for omens — back to [[egypt|Egypt]]ian origins. (006-001)

Yet [[herodotus|Herodotus]] also observed boundaries in his reporting. When touching on the sacred rationale behind animal cults and sacrificial prohibitions, he wrote that he "most desired not to speak" of matters pertaining to the gods and spoke of them only "because I was constrained by necessity." (006-005) He similarly declined to relate the story explaining why swine were sacrificed to the Moon despite being abominated at all other feasts, noting that "this story I know, but it is not a seemly one for me to tell." (005-005)

## Significance

Animal sacrifice, as described by [[herodotus|Herodotus]], reveals the deep structure of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian religious life. The rigorous purity testing of each victim — the inspection of every hair, the examination of tongue and tail — reflects a theology in which the suitability of the offering determined the efficacy of the rite. The imprecation ritual transferred communal guilt onto the severed head, functioning as a mechanism of collective purification that paralleled scapegoat traditions elsewhere in the ancient Near East.

The regional diversity of sacrificial practice — goats here, sheep there; swine only to the Moon and Dionysos — illustrates the decentralized character of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian religion beneath its apparent uniformity. The annual ram sacrifice at [[thebes|Thebes]], in which the animal was mourned and buried in a sacred tomb, blurs the distinction between sacrifice and sacred animal cult, suggesting a more porous boundary between offering and deification in [[egypt|Egypt]]ian practice than in Greek. For [[herodotus|Herodotus]], the entire sacrificial system served as evidence of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian priority in religious matters: the [[hellenes|Greeks]] had learned from [[egypt|Egypt]] the forms of sacrifice, the names of the gods, and the arts of solemn assembly.
