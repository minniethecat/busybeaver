## Overview

The pyramids of Egypt are among the most famous monuments described by Herodotus in his "Account of Egypt." Located on the Libyan mountain range near Memphis, these colossal stone structures were built as royal tombs by the kings of the Fourth Dynasty. Herodotus provides detailed measurements and accounts of their construction, particularly those of Cheops, Chephren, and Mycerinus, describing them as works "greater than words can say" (207).

## Location / Geography

The pyramids stand upon a hill about one hundred feet high on the Libyan side of the Nile valley, near Memphis. Herodotus notes that the stone for their construction was quarried from the Arabian mountains to the east and transported across the river (169). During the annual Nile flood, boats could pass close by the pyramids when sailing from Naucratis to Memphis (207). The quarries themselves lay in the Arabian mountain range which stretched from the North toward the midday, and these same quarries supplied stone for the pyramids at Memphis (001-009).

## Historical Significance

According to Herodotus, the great pyramid was built by King Cheops, who "brought them to every kind of evil" by closing all the temples and compelling the Egyptians to labour on the monument (169). One hundred thousand men worked in three-month shifts for ten years to build the causeway alone, with the pyramid itself requiring twenty years (171). The Egyptians, Herodotus reports, so hated Cheops and his brother Chephren that they refused to name them, calling the pyramids instead after the shepherd Philitis who pastured flocks in the region (173). The third great pyramid was built by Mycerinus, who reopened the temples and was remembered as the most just of all Egyptian kings (173).

## Herodotus's Description

Herodotus measured the pyramids himself. The great pyramid of Cheops, he records, is square with each side measuring eight hundred feet and the height the same, built of smoothed stones none less than thirty feet in length (171). It was constructed in steps, with stones raised by machines made of short timbers. An inscription in Egyptian writing recorded the cost of radishes, onions, and leeks for the workmen at one thousand six hundred talents of silver (171). The pyramid of Chephren, which Herodotus also measured, stands forty feet lower than the great pyramid, built close beside it on the same hill, with a base course of Ethiopian stone of diverse colours (173). Herodotus judged that the pyramids and the labyrinth together surpassed all the great works of the Hellenes combined in labour and expense (207).

## The Pyramid of Mycerinus and the Rhodopis Legend

The third pyramid at Giza was built by Mycerinus, and Herodotus reports it as a square structure measuring on each side three hundred feet lacking twenty, built of Ethiopian stone up to half its height (013-001). Some Hellenes, Herodotus notes, attributed this pyramid to the courtesan Rhodopis — a claim he emphatically rejects as factually impossible. He points out that such a pyramid would have cost "innumerable thousands of talents" — far beyond the means of any courtesan — and moreover that Rhodopis lived in the reign of Amasis, generations after Mycerinus (013-001). Rhodopis was by descent a Thracian, and had been a fellow-slave of Aesop the fable-maker under Iadmon of Samos, before being brought to Egypt. This correction reveals Herodotus's method: he tests oral traditions against chronology and economic plausibility, even when the traditions circulated widely among his Greek audience.

Of this oppression there passed ten years while the causeway was made by which they drew the stones, which causeway they built, and it is a work not much less, as it appears to me, than the pyramid; for the length of it is five furlongs and the breadth ten fathoms and the height, where it is highest, eight fathoms, and it is made of stone smoothed and with figures carved upon it.
> (169)

> For the making of the pyramid itself there passed a period of twenty years; and the pyramid is square, each side measuring eight hundred feet, and the height of it is the same. It is built of stone smoothed and fitted together in the most perfect manner, not one of the stones being less than thirty feet in length. This pyramid was made after the manner of steps which some called "rows" and others "bases": and when they had first made it thus, they raised the remaining stones with machines made of short pieces of timber.
> (171)
