## Overview

Altars were central to Egyptian religious practice, serving as the focal point for animal sacrifice, votive offerings, and solemn ceremonies. Herodotus records that the Egyptians were the first people to establish formal religious assemblies, processions, and temple rituals — all of which centred upon the altar. His account also captures the intersection of Egyptian altar practice with the wider Mediterranean world through the dedications of foreign rulers. (006-002) (004-006)

## The Altar and Sacrificial Ritual

Herodotus describes a meticulous system of sacrificial inspection centred upon the altar. When an ox was brought for sacrifice to Epaphos, a priest examined it for any impurity — even a single black hair rendered the animal unfit. The priest inspected the beast both standing and lying on its back, drew out its tongue to check for "clean" signs, and examined the tail for the proper arrangement of hairs. Only after this rigorous inspection could the animal be led to the altar and sacrificed. (004-006)

The sacrifice itself followed a precise ritual. The beast was marked, and those conducting the sacrifice called upon the god. After slaughter, the head was removed and the body flayed. The head was then loaded with imprecations and, if a market was nearby, sold to Greek traders; if no Greeks were present, it was cast into the river. This ritual disposal of the head, laden with curses directed at any evils that might befall Egypt or the sacrificers themselves, was a distinctive feature of Egyptian altar practice. (004-006)

## Foreign Altars in Egypt

The reign of Amasis marked a significant development in the religious landscape of Egypt. A noted philhellene, Amasis granted the Greeks the right to establish their own altars and sacred enclosures on Egyptian soil. He gave the city of Naucratis to Greek settlers and, for those Greeks who merely visited on trading voyages, he allocated "portions of land to set up altars and make sacred enclosures for their gods." The greatest and most frequented of these was the Hellenion, a pan-Hellenic sanctuary established jointly by several Greek cities including Chios, Teos, Phocaea, and Rhodes. (016-005)

This arrangement represented a remarkable accommodation: for the first time, foreign gods could be worshipped at permanent altars within Egypt, side by side with the ancient Egyptian cults. The altar thus became a meeting point between Egyptian and Greek religious traditions — a physical marker of the cultural exchange that characterized Egypt in the Saite period. (016-005)

## Herodotus on the Greek Tale of Heracles at the Altar

Herodotus took issue with what he considered a foolish Greek story concerning Heracles. According to the Greek tale, Heracles came to Egypt, was crowned with wreaths by the Egyptians, and was led in procession to be sacrificed to Zeus at an altar — only to turn on his captors at the last moment and slay them all. Herodotus rejected this story outright, arguing that the Egyptians, far from sacrificing human beings, were so scrupulous about animal sacrifice that they would not even sacrifice goats for fear of offending the god Pan. The tale, he concluded, betrayed a fundamental ignorance of Egyptian religious customs. (005-003)
