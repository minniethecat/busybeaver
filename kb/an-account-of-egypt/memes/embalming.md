## Overview

Embalming was a central mortuary practice of ancient [[egypt|Egypt]], described in detail by [[herodotus|Herodotus]] during his travels in the 5th century BCE. The practice involved the preservation of the dead body through a multi-stage process that included the removal of internal organs, desiccation in natron, and wrapping in linen bandages. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] identifies it as a specialized craft, passed down through families, with practitioners who offered services at varying levels of cost and elaboration. (001-003) (008-002)

## Description

### The Three Methods

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] records three distinct methods of embalming, differentiated by cost and elaborateness. The embalmers presented wooden models of corpses to the bereaved family, allowing them to choose which method they desired and could afford. (008-002)

**The most costly method.** The embalmers first drew out the brain through the nostrils using a crooked iron tool, partly by extraction and partly by pouring in dissolving drugs. They then made an incision along the flank with a sharp [[ethiopia-region|Ethiopia]]n stone and removed the entire contents of the abdomen. The cavity was cleansed with palm-wine and pounded spices, then filled with pure myrrh, cassia, and other spices (but not frankincense), and sewn shut. The body was then covered in natron for seventy days -- the maximum permitted duration -- after which it was washed and wrapped entirely in fine linen bands smeared with gum. The family then commissioned a wooden case shaped like a human figure, into which the wrapped body was placed and stored upright against the wall of a sepulchral chamber. (008-002)

**The middle method.** For those wishing to avoid great expense, embalmers injected cedar oil into the belly through the breech without making an incision or removing the bowels. The body was kept for the prescribed number of days, after which the oil was released, carrying with it the dissolved internal organs. The natron simultaneously dissolved the flesh, leaving only skin and bones. The corpse was returned in this condition without further treatment. (008-002)

**The least expensive method.** Bodies of those with fewer means were prepared by cleansing the belly with a purge and then preserving the body in natron for seventy days, after which it was returned to the family for burial. (008-002)

### The Practitioners

Embalming was a hereditary occupation in [[egypt|Egypt]]. Certain persons employed themselves regularly in this craft and inherited it as a family trade. These practitioners worked in dedicated buildings to which corpses were conveyed after the initial mourning rituals. (008-002)

### Special Cases

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] notes a specific precaution regarding women of high rank and those of exceptional beauty: their bodies were not delivered to the embalmers until the third or fourth day after death. He attributes this to an incident in which an embalmer was discovered violating the corpse of a recently deceased woman, reported by a fellow craftsman. (008-002)

Individuals killed by crocodiles or drowned in [[nile-river|the Nile]] were treated with particular reverence. The people of the city where the body was cast ashore were required to embalm it in the finest manner and bury it in a sacred burial-place. No relatives or friends were permitted to touch the body; only the priests of [[nile-river|the Nile]] handled and interred it, treating the deceased as "something more than man." (008-002)

### Animal Embalming

The practice extended beyond humans to sacred animals. Cats that died were carried to sacred buildings in the city of [[bubastis|Bubastis]], embalmed, and buried there. Dogs were buried by each people in their own city in sacred tombs, and ichneumons (mongooses) were buried in the same manner. Crocodiles held sacred at [[thebes|Thebes]] and [[lake-moeris|Lake Moeris]] were treated well in life and after death embalmed and buried in sacred tombs. (006-005) (007-001) Sacred serpents at [[thebes|Thebes]] were buried upon death in the temple of [[zeus|Zeus]]. (007-004)

## Historical Context

In 5th-century BCE [[egypt|Egypt]], proper burial was of profound importance, extending beyond religious obligation into law and social standing. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] records that under King [[asychis|Asychis]], a law permitted a man to borrow money using the dead body of his father as security, with the additional provision that anyone who defaulted on such a loan would forfeit the right to burial for himself and his family. (013-003) This legal apparatus underscores how central proper entombment was to [[egypt|Egypt]]ian identity.

The expense and hierarchy of embalming methods reflected [[egypt|Egypt]]ian social stratification. The most costly method was reserved for the wealthy and high-ranking, the middle method for those of moderate means, and the simplest method for the poor. The prohibition against burying the dead in woolen garments -- observed also by Orphic and Bacchic rites and by the Pythagoreans, which [[herodotus|Herodotus]] notes are "really [[egypt|Egypt]]ian" in origin -- further illustrates the ritual precision surrounding burial. (007-005)

## Herodotus's Commentary

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] displays a characteristic reticence when discussing religiously sensitive aspects of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian burial. He declines to name the figure associated with the most costly embalming method, stating that he considers it "impiety to mention when speaking of a matter of such a kind." (008-002) He likewise withholds details of the Osirian mysteries performed at [[sais|Sais]], noting only that the burial-place associated with this unnamed god lies within the temple of Athene, "behind the house of the goddess, stretching along the whole wall of it." (016-002)

This restraint reflects [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s broader practice of respecting religious prohibitions encountered in his inquiries, even as he describes material practices in precise detail. It also signals the deep entanglement of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian embalming with theology -- specifically the cult of [[osiris|Osiris]] -- which [[herodotus|Herodotus]] knew but considered it impious to disclose.

## Significance

The embalming practices of [[egypt|Egypt]], as documented by [[herodotus|Herodotus]], represent one of the earliest and most detailed Western accounts of the technology and ritual of mummification. The systematic, multi-tiered approach reveals a society in which care for the dead was both a sacred duty and a marker of social position. The practice sheds light on [[egypt|Egypt]]ian beliefs about the afterlife, the importance of bodily preservation, and the elaborate material culture (natron, spices, linen, gum, wooden cases) devoted to mortuary ritual.

[[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s account also illuminates how [[egypt|Egypt]]ian funerary customs contrasted with Greek norms. [[egypt|Egypt]]ian mourners, for instance, let their hair grow long upon a death, having previously been close-shaven -- the opposite of Greek mourning practice. (004-004) The public self-beating rituals, with women exposing their breasts and plastering their heads and faces with mud, would have struck Greek readers as markedly foreign, underscoring [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s broader theme of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian alterity. (008-002)

These, whenever a corpse is conveyed to them, show to those who brought it wooden models of corpses made like reality by painting, and the best of the ways of embalming they say is that of him whose name I think it impiety to mention when speaking of a matter of such a kind; the second which they show is less good than this and also less expensive; and the third is the least expensive of all.
> (008-002)

> First with the crooked iron tool they draw out the brain through the nostrils, extracting it partly thus and partly by pouring in drugs; and after this with a sharp stone of Ethiopia they make a cut along the side and take out the whole contents of the belly, and when they have cleared out the cavity and cleansed it with palm-wine they cleanse it again with spices pounded up: then they fill the belly with pure myrrh pounded up and with cassia and other spices except frankincense, and sew it together again. Having so done they keep it for embalming covered up in natron for seventy days, but for a longer time than this it is not permitted to embalm it.
> (008-002)

> The wives of men of rank when they die are not given at once to be embalmed, nor such women as are very beautiful or of greater regard than others, but on the third or fourth day after their death (and not before) they are delivered to the embalmers. They do so about this matter in order that the embalmers may not abuse their women, for they say that one of them was taken once doing so to the corpse of a woman lately dead, and his fellow-craftsman gave information.
> (008-002)

> Whenever any one, either of the Egyptians themselves or of strangers, is found to have been carried off by a crocodile or brought to his death by the river itself, the people of any city by which he may have been cast up on land must embalm him and lay him out in the fairest way they can and bury him in a sacred burial-place, nor may any of his relations or friends besides touch him, but the priests of the Nile themselves handle the corpse and bury it as that of one who was something more than man.
> (008-002)

> In the entertainments of the rich among them, when they have finished eating, a man bears round a wooden figure of a dead body in a coffin, made as like the reality as may be both by painting and carving, and measuring about a cubit or two cubits each way; and this he shows to each of those who are drinking together, saying: "When thou lookest upon this, drink and be merry, for thou shalt be such as this when thou art dead."
> (007-005)
