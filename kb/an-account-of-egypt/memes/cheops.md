## Overview

Cheops ([[egypt|Egypt]]ian: Khufu) was a pharaoh of the Fourth Dynasty who reigned in the 26th century BCE. He is famous above all for building the [[pyramids-of-giza|Great Pyramid]] at Giza, one of the Seven Wonders of the ancient world. In [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s account, however, Cheops is remembered not for his architectural achievement but for his tyranny and impiety (012-002).

## Background / Biography

Cheops came to power after a period of orderly rule and prosperity under [[rhampsinitos|Rhampsinitos]], according to the [[egyptian-priesthood|Egyptian priests]] who spoke with [[herodotus|Herodotus]] (012-002). He reigned for fifty years, during which time he shut down all the temples and forbade the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians from offering sacrifices (012-002). He conscripted the entire population into forced labour for his building projects, with a hundred thousand men working in three-month shifts to construct first a causeway for transporting stone, then the pyramid itself (012-002). The causeway alone took ten years to complete, while the pyramid required twenty years (012-003). [[herodotus|Herodotus]] reports that Cheops descended to such depths of wickedness that, when short of money, he placed his own daughter in a brothel to raise funds, and she in turn demanded a stone from each visitor to build her own small pyramid (012-003).

## Role in the Account

Cheops appears in Book Two of [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s *Histories* as the archetype of the tyrannical pharaoh. His reign marks a sharp break with the preceding era of good governance, and [[herodotus|Herodotus]] uses him to illustrate how absolute power can corrupt. The description of the [[pyramids-of-giza|Great Pyramid]]'s construction is one of the most detailed architectural passages in the entire work: [[herodotus|Herodotus]] records its dimensions (eight hundred feet on each side and in height), the method of raising stones with wooden machines, and the enormous sums spent on food for the workmen (012-003). After Cheops's death, his brother [[chephren|Chephren]] succeeded him and continued the same oppressive policies, so that the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians endured one hundred and six years of misery and temple closure (012-004).

## Herodotus's Portrayal

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] portrays Cheops in an unequivocally negative light. He reports that the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians of his own day hated Cheops so intensely that they were unwilling even to speak his name, and instead called the great pyramids after Philitis, a shepherd who grazed his flocks in the area at the time (012-004). The narrator accepts the [[egyptian-priesthood|Egyptian priests]]' account of Cheops's cruelty without scepticism, though modern [[egypt|Egypt]]ology offers a more nuanced picture of Khufu's reign.
