## Overview

The veneration of sacred animals was one of the most distinctive features of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian religion as observed by [[herodotus|Herodotus]] during his travels in the 5th century BCE. From his conversations with priests at [[memphis|Memphis]], [[heliopolis|Heliopolis]], and [[thebes|Thebes]], he documented a complex system in which certain animals were held as sacred — protected from harm, ritually fed, embalmed after death, and buried in consecrated tombs — while others were treated with revulsion or considered sacred only in specific regions. (001-003)

## Description

### Which Animals Were Sacred

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] identifies a wide range of sacred animals across [[egypt|Egypt]]. Among wild creatures, he notes that all animals in [[egypt|Egypt]] — though the land does not greatly abound in them — "are one and all accounted by them sacred, some of them living with men and others not." (006-005) Specifically named sacred animals include:

- **Cattle**: Male oxen were tested for purity and consecrated to Epaphos. Cows were sacred to [[isis|Isis]] and could not be sacrificed. (004-006)
- **Crocodiles**: Sacred to those dwelling about [[thebes|Thebes]] and the lake of Moiris, but treated as enemies and even eaten by the people of [[elephantine|Elephantine]]. (007-001)
- **Cats**: Kept in households and buried at the sacred city of [[bubastis|Bubastis]] after embalming. (006-005)
- **Dogs and ichneumons**: Buried in sacred tombs within their own cities. (006-005)
- **Hawks and shrewmice**: Carried for burial to the city of [[buto|Buto]]. (006-005)
- **Ibises**: Buried at Hermopolis; honored for their role in killing winged serpents. (007-004)
- **The phoenix**: A rare sacred bird said to appear at [[heliopolis|Heliopolis]] at intervals of five hundred years to bury its father in the temple of the Sun. (007-003)
- **Sacred serpents**: Small horned serpents at [[thebes|Thebes]], harmless to men, buried in the temple of [[zeus|Zeus]]. (007-004)
- **The river-horse (hippopotamus)**: Sacred only in the district of Papremis. (007-002)
- **Otters, the lepidotos fish, the eel, and the fox-goose**: All considered sacred in [[nile-river|the Nile]]. (007-002)

### Regional Variations

A striking feature of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian animal worship was its regional character. The crocodile provides the clearest example: "for some of the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians the crocodiles are sacred animals, and for others not so, but they treat them on the contrary as enemies." (007-001) Those around [[thebes|Thebes]] and Lake Moiris selected a single tame crocodile, adorned it with gold ornaments and anklets, and buried it embalmed in sacred tombs; while the inhabitants of [[elephantine|Elephantine]] ate them without regarding them as sacred. (007-001)

The hippopotamus was sacred "in the district of Papremis, but for the other [[egypt|Egypt]]ians he is not sacred." (007-002) Similarly, different regions honored different gods and consequently different animals: "for not all the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians equally reverence the same gods, except only [[isis-and-osiris|Isis and Osiris]]." (005-001)

### How Sacred Animals Were Treated

The [[egypt|Egypt]]ians appointed both men and women to provide food for each kind of sacred beast, an office that descended from father to son. (006-005) When a person made a vow to the god to whom a particular animal belonged, they would shave the head of their child — either the whole, half, or a third — then weigh the hair against silver and give the silver's worth of fish to the animal's keeper. (006-005)

The killing of sacred animals carried severe penalties. Whosoever killed an ibis or a hawk, "whether it be with his will or against his will, must die." (006-005) For other sacred animals, the penalty for intentional killing was death; for accidental killing, whatever penalty the priests appointed. (006-005)

The treatment of cats reveals particular devotion. When a fire broke out, the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians would stand at intervals to watch the cats rather than extinguish the flames, while the cats, seeming "divinely possessed," would leap into the fire, causing great mourning. (006-005) When a cat died naturally, all dwellers in that house shaved their eyebrows; when a dog died, they shaved their whole body and head. (006-005)

Victims of crocodile attacks received extraordinary treatment: if anyone was carried off by a crocodile or drowned in [[nile-river|the Nile]], the city where the body was cast ashore was obligated to embalm it "in the fairest way they can and bury him in a sacred burial-place," handled only by the priests of [[nile-river|the Nile]], as "one who was something more than man." (008-002)

### Animals Considered Unclean

Not all animals were venerated. The pig was "accounted by the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians an abominable animal." (005-005) Anyone touching a pig would immediately immerse themselves, clothing and all, in the river. Swineherds, though native [[egypt|Egypt]]ians, were forbidden from entering temples and could only marry among themselves. (005-005) However, swine were sacrificed to the Moon and to Dionysos at the full moon festival — a practice [[herodotus|Herodotus]] found curious and for which he knew a story he deemed unseemly to relate. (005-005)

The ox, while sacred, was subject to rigorous purity testing. If the examining priest found a single black hair upon the beast, "he counts it not clean for sacrifice." (004-006) The priest checked the animal standing and lying, drew out its tongue, examined tail hairs, then sealed it with papyrus and rolling it round the horns with sealing-earth; sacrificing an unsealed beast was punishable by death. (004-006)

### Burial Practices

Sacred animals received elaborate burial rites. Cats were embalmed and carried to sacred buildings at [[bubastis|Bubastis]]; dogs and ichneumons were buried in sacred tombs in their own cities; shrewmice and hawks were taken to [[buto|Buto]]; ibises to Hermopolis; bears and wolves were buried where they were found. (006-005) The tame crocodiles of [[thebes|Thebes]] and Moiris were embalmed and buried in sacred tombs. (007-001) In the labyrinth near Lake Moiris, underground chambers served as sepulchres "of the kings who had first built this labyrinth and of the sacred crocodiles." (014-005)

Similarly, cattle that died naturally were treated with reverence: cows were cast into the river, while oxen were buried with their horns protruding to mark the spot. After decomposition, boats from the island of Prosopitis collected the bones and buried them in a single consecrated place at Atarbechis. (004-006)

## Historical Context

[[egypt|Egypt]]ian animal worship was deeply rooted in the religious landscape of the 5th century BCE. Each major deity had animal associations: Epaphos with the bull, [[isis|Isis]] with the cow, and [[zeus|Zeus]] with the horned serpents of [[thebes|Thebes]]. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] observed that the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians were "religious excessively beyond all other men" (004-005), practicing rituals far more elaborate and exacting than those of the [[hellenes|Greeks]]. The animal cults he described were not marginal superstitions but central to temple economies, regional identity, and the priestly system. His account provides one of the earliest extant Greek ethnographic records of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian zoomorphic religion, predating later Hellenistic and Roman sources by centuries.

## Herodotus's Commentary

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] approached the topic of sacred animals with a mixture of fascination and restraint. He openly acknowledged the limits of his inquiry into divine matters, writing that if he explained why sacred animals were consecrated, "I should fall into discourse of matters pertaining to the gods, of which I most desire not to speak; and what I have actually said touching slightly upon them, I said because I was constrained by necessity." (006-005) This discretion reflects both [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s Greek sensibilities and the reticence of his [[egypt|Egypt]]ian informants.

His detailed descriptions of the crocodile's natural history (007-001), the phoenix legend — which he admitted he "cannot believe" (007-003) — and the cat's behavior during fires (006-005) reveal his characteristic blend of empirical observation and reportage. He concluded his discussion of sacred animals with a formulaic closure: "Let so much suffice as has been said now concerning sacred animals." (007-004)

## Significance

The [[egypt|Egypt]]ian practice of animal veneration, as documented by [[herodotus|Herodotus]], illuminates several dimensions of life in the 5th-century BCE [[nile-river|Nile]] Valley. The regionalism of animal cults — where the same creature could be sacred in one nome and eaten in another — provides evidence for the decentralized fabric of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian religion. The elaborate provisioning, protection, and burial systems reveal institutional apparatus comparable to the mortuary cults of royalty. The Greek narrator's astonishment at practices he found alternately admirable and absurd also makes the text a valuable document for understanding early cross-cultural encounters between the Hellenic and [[egypt|Egypt]]ian worlds.
