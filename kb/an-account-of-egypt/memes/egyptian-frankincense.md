## Overview

Frankincense was an important aromatic substance in ancient Egyptian religious practice, employed in sacrifices, temple rituals, and domestic settings. Herodotus records its use in the preparation of sacrificial animals, noting that the Egyptians poured wine over the victims and then burned frankincense as part of the ritual procedure. The substance also appears in accounts of Egyptian social gatherings, suggesting that its use extended beyond the strictly liturgical realm into the domestic and convivial. (004-006)

## Ritual Use in Sacrifice

Herodotus describes a standardized Egyptian sacrificial practice in which frankincense played an integral role. After the initial preparation of the animal — which included marking it as fit for sacrifice and calling down a curse upon it — the Egyptians poured wine over the victim and then burned frankincense during the consecration. This use of aromatic smoke as a medium between the human and divine realms parallels practices in Greek religion, where frankincense and other incenses were similarly burned on altars. The universal character of this custom among the Egyptians impressed Herodotus, who notes that "all the Egyptians have the same customs equally for all their sacrifices" with respect to the pouring of wine and the use of incense. (004-006)

The smoke of burning frankincense served multiple functions. It purified the ritual space, masking the odors of slaughter and marking the occasion as sacred. It also carried prayers and invocations upward, transforming the material act of sacrifice into a communication with the divine. The aromatic quality of frankincense — it was an imported luxury, not a local product — would have added to its prestige as a temple offering.

## Domestic and Social Use

Beyond the temple precincts, frankincense appears in the account of Egyptian social gatherings. At wealthy Egyptian dinner parties, Herodotus relates that after the meal a wooden image of a corpse was carried around the room with the exhortation to "drink and take your pleasure, for such shall you be when you are dead." In such settings, aromatic substances like frankincense might have been burned to perfume the air, enhancing the convivial atmosphere while simultaneously serving as a memento mori — the sweet scent a fleeting pleasure against the certainty of death.

## Trade and Supply

Frankincense was not native to Egypt but was imported from the aromatic-producing regions of the Arabian Peninsula and the Horn of Africa — the same source regions that supplied the frankincense burned in Greek and Near Eastern temples. Egypt's position as a nexus of Mediterranean and Red Sea trade routes ensured a steady supply, and the wealth concentrated in the temple estates provided the economic means to acquire it. The substance's foreign origin, combined with its pleasant aroma and its association with the divine, made it a natural choice for use in the most solemn and costly of Egyptian ceremonies.

## Significance

The presence of frankincense in Herodotus's account of Egyptian sacrifice serves as a reminder that Egyptian religious practice, for all its distinctive features — the elaborate rules of purity, the hereditary priesthood, the monumental temple architecture — participated in a broader Mediterranean and Near Eastern ritual culture. The burning of incense, the pouring of libations, and the immolation of sacrificial victims were practices shared across the region, even as each culture invested them with its own theological meaning. Frankincense, imported from beyond Egypt's borders and burned in its temples, embodied the material connections that bound Egypt to the wider world.
