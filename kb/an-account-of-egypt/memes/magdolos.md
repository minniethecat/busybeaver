Magdolos was the site of a significant military victory by the Egyptian pharaoh [[Necos]] II (Necho II, 610–595 BCE) over the Syrians. Herodotus records the battle as one of the notable achievements of Necos's reign, alongside his canal-building and his naval exploits. (015-004) (015-005)

## The Battle of Magdolos

According to Herodotus, Necos engaged the Syrians in battle at Magdolos and defeated them. Following this victory, Necos captured [[Cadytis]], which Herodotus describes as a great city of Syria. (015-005) The location of Magdolos has been variously identified — some scholars associate it with Megiddo in northern Palestine, a site of earlier Egyptian military engagements, though the precise location remains uncertain.

## Necos's Military Achievements

The battle at Magdolos formed part of a broader pattern of Egyptian military engagement in the Levant during the Saïte period. Necos, the son of [[Psammetichos]], pursued an active foreign policy that included both naval construction (he was the builder of triremes for both the Mediterranean and the Erythraian Sea) and land campaigns. (015-004) The capture of Cadytis following Magdolos represented the northernmost extent of Necos's military campaigns on land, complementing his naval enterprises.

## Votive Dedication

As a thank-offering for his victories, including the triumph at Magdolos, Necos dedicated the ceremonial dress he had worn during the campaigns to the god [[Apollo]], sending it to the oracle at [[Branchidai]], a Milesian sanctuary. (015-005) This dedication reflects the Saïte dynasty's engagement with the wider Mediterranean world and the use of prestigious Greek sanctuaries to commemorate Egyptian royal achievements.
