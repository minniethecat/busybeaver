## Overview

The Egyptian pyramids, as described by Herodotus in the fifth century BCE, were among the most astonishing monuments he encountered in his travels. The historian devoted extensive attention to the three great pyramids at Giza — those of Cheops, Chephren, and Mycerinus — recording their dimensions, the methods and cost of their construction, and the traditions surrounding the kings who built them. He also noted other pyramids in Egypt, including those rising from the waters of lake Moeris and the pyramid attached to the labyrinth. Herodotus was deeply impressed by their sheer scale, observing that each one of them was "equal to many works of the Hellenes, great as they may be." (014-005)

## The Great Pyramid of Cheops

The largest of the three pyramids at Giza, built by King Cheops, was described by Herodotus in remarkable detail. According to the priests of Memphis, the pyramid itself took twenty years to build, and it was constructed on a square base with each side measuring eight hundred feet, the height being the same. (012-003) Before the pyramid could be raised, a ten-year period of oppression was spent constructing the causeway along which the stones were drawn — a work that Herodotus judged to be "not much less" than the pyramid itself. This causeway measured five furlongs in length, ten fathoms in breadth, and eight fathoms at its highest point, built of smoothed stone and covered with carved figures. (012-002)

The pyramid was built in the manner of steps, which some called "rows" and others "bases." Once the stepped core was complete, the remaining stones were raised using machines made of short pieces of timber — lifting each block from the ground to the first step, then from the first to the second, and so on upward. Herodotus notes two competing accounts: either as many machines were used as there were courses of steps, or a single portable machine was transferred from course to course. The highest parts of the pyramid were finished first, and the builders then worked downward, completing the lowest portions last. (012-003)

On the face of the pyramid, an inscription in Egyptian writing recorded the cost of radishes, onions, and leeks consumed by the workmen — a sum the interpreter translated as one thousand six hundred talents of silver. Herodotus, recording this detail with his characteristic credulity, wondered aloud how much more must have been spent on the iron tools, the bread, and the clothing of the laborers, as well as on the cutting and transport of the stones. (012-003)

## The Underground Chambers

Herodotus records that the pyramid of Cheops contained elaborate subterranean works. A channel was cut from the Nile, flowing through a built conduit into an underground chamber where, surrounded by water flowing around an island within, the body of Cheops himself was said to be laid. (012-004) This account, though mingled with priestly lore, reflects the genuine complexity of the pyramid's internal architecture — a feature confirmed by modern exploration.

## The Pyramids of Chephren and Mycerinus

The second great pyramid, built by Cheops's brother Chephren, followed the same general plan but was forty feet lower than its predecessor. Herodotus, having himself measured both pyramids, attested to the difference. Chephren's pyramid lacked the underground chambers and the channel from the Nile that distinguished the tomb of Cheops. For its basement, however, the first course was built of Ethiopian stone of diverse colors, giving the structure a distinctive appearance. (012-004)

The third pyramid, built by Mycerinus son of Cheops, was far smaller — a square measuring two hundred and eighty feet on each side, built of Ethiopian stone up to half its height. (013-001) Herodotus refuted a popular Greek belief that this pyramid had been built by the courtesan Rhodopis, pointing out that Rhodopis had lived many years later, in the reign of Amasis, and that her wealth — though considerable — "was not enough to suffice for the cost of such a pyramid as this." (013-001) (013-002)

Herodotus also records that the Egyptians, out of hatred for Cheops and Chephren — whom they remembered as oppressive rulers — were reluctant even to name them, preferring instead to call the pyramids after Philitis the shepherd, who had pastured his flocks in the region at the time of their construction. (012-004)

## Other Pyramids

Beyond the three great pyramids at Giza, Herodotus noted several others. At lake Moeris, two pyramids rose from the water, each fifty fathoms in height above the surface, with an equal portion submerged below. Upon each stood a colossal stone statue seated upon a chair. (014-006) Herodotus calculated that these pyramids were each a hundred fathoms high in total — equal to a furlong of six hundred feet. (014-007)

A later king, Asychis, built a pyramid of brick rather than stone. Its inscription, carved in stone, bore a defiant message: "Despise me not in comparison with the pyramids of stone, seeing that I excel them as much as Zeus excels the other gods; for with a pole they struck into the lake, and whatever of the mud attached itself to the pole, this they gathered up and made bricks, and in such manner they finished me." (013-003)

At the end of the great labyrinth, near the corner of the complex, stood yet another pyramid — forty fathoms in height, with large figures carved upon its face, connected to the labyrinth by a way built underground. (014-005)

## Stone and Construction Materials

The stone for the pyramids at Memphis was quarried from the Arabian mountain range that stretches along the eastern side of the Nile. (001-009) On the Libyan side of the river, another range — rocky and enveloped in sand — contained the pyramids themselves. (001-009) Herodotus observed that over time the very fabric of the pyramids was vulnerable: an efflorescence of salt formed upon the surface of the soil, so pervasive that "even the pyramids are being eaten away by it." (002-001)
