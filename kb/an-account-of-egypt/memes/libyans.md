# Libyans

The Libyans are the various peoples dwelling in the region to the west of Egypt, known in antiquity as Libya. Herodotus refers to Libyans frequently in his "Account of Egypt," both as neighbours of Egypt and as a point of comparison for Egyptian customs, geography, and religious practices.

## Geography and the Egyptian Border

Herodotus describes the geographical relationship between Egypt and Libya in detail. He notes that the mountain range on the side of Egypt towards Libya is rocky and enveloped in sand, containing the pyramids. (019) The space between the Arabian and Libyan mountains is plain-land, and at its narrowest point it did not seem to Herodotus to exceed two hundred furlongs across. (019)

Regarding the boundary between Egypt and Libya, Herodotus relates a dispute involving the people of Marea and Apis, who dwelt in the parts of Egypt bordering on Libya. These people considered themselves Libyans rather than Egyptians and, finding the Egyptian religious dietary restrictions burdensome, sent to the Oracle of Ammon seeking exemption. The oracle, however, declared that Egypt was all the land the Nile watered, and that Egyptians were all those who dwelt below Elephantine and drank of the river. (029)

## Poseidon and Libyan Religion

Herodotus makes the notable claim that the worship of Poseidon originated among the Libyans. He states that "no people except the Libyans have had the name of Poseidon from the first and have paid honour to this god always," and that the Hellenes learned the worship of this god from the Libyans. (075) This is in contrast to most other Greek gods, whose names Herodotus traces to Egyptian origins.

## The Oracle of Ammon

The Oracle of Ammon, located at the Siwa Oasis in the Libyan desert, plays a significant role in Herodotus's narrative. He recounts how the Ammonian king Etearchos told him a story of Nasamonian explorers — described as "a Libyan race which dwells in the Syrtis" — who journeyed into the Libyan desert and discovered a great river flowing west to east, which Etearchos conjectured to be the Nile. (051)

The founding of oracular sites in both Libya and Greece is attributed by Egyptian priests to two Theban priestesses carried away by Phoenicians, one sold into Libya and the other to the Hellenes. The Libyan oracle became the Oracle of Ammon. (081)

## Health and Customs

Herodotus considers the Libyans to be among the healthiest peoples, ranking them second only to the Egyptians. He attributes this to the stability of the seasons, noting that changes in climate are the primary cause of disease. (101)
