## Overview

The [[Nile Delta]] is the great triangular plain at the [[Mediterranean]] terminus of the [[Nile River|Nile]], formed over millennia by the river's deposits of rich alluvial silt. In [[Herodotus]]'s *Account of Egypt*, the Delta is both a geographic region and a conceptual category: the [[Egypt|Egyptians]] and the [[Ionians]] disagreed about its boundaries, and the historian devotes a substantial digression to resolving the dispute (002-001) (002-002). As the most fertile and populous region of Egypt, the Delta was home to major cities including [[Sais]], [[Bubastis]], [[Buto]], [[Naucratis]], and [[Mendes]], and its network of canals and waterways shaped Egyptian life from the earliest dynasties through the [[Persian]] period.

## Location / Geography

Herodotus describes the Delta as a region bounded on the east by the [[Pelusion|Pelusiac]] mouth of the Nile, on the west by the Canobic mouth, and on the south by the apex near modern [[Cairo]]. By his reckoning, the cultivated land between the mountains on either side was narrow in [[Upper Egypt]] — as little as two hundred furlongs across — but widened dramatically at the Delta, expanding to a coastal breadth of 3,600 stadia (002-001).

### The Five Navigable Mouths

| Mouth | Location | Associated City |
|-------|----------|----------------|
| Pelusiac | Easternmost | Pelusion |
| Mendesian | East-central | Mendes |
| Sebennytic | Central | Sebennytus |
| Saïtic | West-central | Sais |
| Canobic | Westernmost | Canobus |

Herodotus identifies seven mouths in total, but notes that only five were navigable — a correction to the Ionian tradition, which claimed only three (002-003). The Delta's coastline stretched from the watch-tower of [[Perseus]] in the east to the fish-curing houses of Pelusion in the west — though the Ionians considered this entire span to define Egypt itself, a view Herodotus disputes at length (002-002).

### The Canal Network

The canal network that crisscrossed the Delta, begun under [[Sesostris]] and expanded under later kings, transformed the region from a land accessible by horse and chariot into one navigable only by boat (010-002). Sesostris's canal-building programme was simultaneously defensive and economic: by cutting canals across the Delta, he rendered the chariot — the primary instrument of aristocratic warfare — unusable, while simultaneously facilitating irrigation and transport. Later, [[Necos]] attempted to extend this network by digging a canal from the Nile near [[Bubastis]] through the [[Wadi Tumilat]] to the [[Erythraian Sea]], a project completed only under the Persian king [[Dareios]] (015-004).

## Historical Significance

The Delta was the most densely populated and agriculturally productive region of Egypt. Its cities were centres of political, religious, and commercial life. The canal network that crisscrossed the Delta transformed the region from a land accessible by horse and chariot into one navigable only by boat (010-002). The Delta's marshy fringes also served as a refuge for fugitive kings: [[Anysis]], the blind king, hid from [[Sabacos]] for fifty years on an island in the Delta fen-country (013-006).

The Delta was also the primary point of contact between Egypt and the Mediterranean world. The city of Naucratis, granted to Greek settlers by [[Amasis]], became the exclusive port of entry for Hellenic trade (016-005). The Canobic mouth served as the gateway for travelers and invaders alike: it was here that [[Alexander (Paris)|Alexander]] came ashore with [[Helen of Troy|Helen]], here that the [[Phoenicians]] established trading posts, and here that [[Herodotus]] himself likely entered Egypt.

### Delta in Myth and Measurement

Herodotus engages extensively with the question of the Delta's geological antiquity. The Ionian theory — that the Delta was a relatively recent alluvial formation — is presented and debated by the historian, who notes that if this theory were correct, it would imply that the Egyptians were a young people who had only recently acquired habitable land (002-002). The priests of [[Memphis]] disputed this, arguing that the Delta had existed since the time of the first Egyptian king, [[Min]] (Menes), who dammed the Nile and founded Memphis on the reclaimed land (008-002).

The Delta also features in the narrative of [[Helen of Troy]]'s sojourn in Egypt. The temple of [[Heracles]] at the Canobic mouth, where Alexander's attendants sought sanctuary, was located in the Delta near [[Taricheiai]] (010-005). This episode links the Delta not only to Egyptian geography but to the great mythic-historical narratives of the Greek world.
