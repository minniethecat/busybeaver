## Overview

Herodotus of Halicarnassus (c. 484 -- c. 424 BCE) was a Greek historian whose work *Histories* is the earliest surviving example of extended historical prose in Western literature. Book Two of the *Histories*, titled *Euterpe* and commonly called *An Account of [[egypt|Egypt]]*, describes the geography, customs, religion, and history of ancient [[egypt|Egypt]] as Herodotus observed and heard about during his travels. The entire corpus of this wiki derives from his account (001-001).

## Background / Biography

Herodotus was born at Halicarnassus, a Greek city on the southwest coast of Asia [[min|Min]]or, in the early fifth century BCE (001-001). Little is known of his personal life beyond what can be inferred from his writings: he travelled extensively through Asia [[min|Min]]or, [[egypt|Egypt]], the Black Sea region, and various parts of Greece and neighbouring lands, collecting information primarily from oral sources (001-002). He eventually settled at Thurii in southern Italy, where he composed his great work (001-001). He died around 424 BCE (001-001). The *Histories* as it survives is divided into nine books named after the nine Muses, a division that may have been imposed later by Alexandrian scholars (001-002).

## Role in the Account

Herodotus is the sole narrator and observer in *An Account of [[egypt|Egypt]]*. He describes what he saw with his own eyes — such as the pyramids, the labyrinth, and the monuments at [[sais|Sais]] — and distinguishes this from what he heard from [[egyptian-priesthood|Egyptian priests]] and other informants (001-004). His narrative encompasses [[egypt|Egypt]]ian geography ([[nile-river|the Nile]]'s course and flood), religion (gods, temples, oracles, and animal worship), customs (embalming, diet, and dress), and history (the succession of kings from the earliest times down to the [[persian|Persian]] conquest). His main historical theme is the struggle between [[hellenes|Greeks]] and barbarians, culminating at the battle of Mycale in 479 BCE, but the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian book provides an extended ethnographic and historical digression (001-002).

## Herodotus's Portrayal

As the author rather than a subject of the narrative, Herodotus's portrayal must be assessed in terms of his own method and reliability. The modern scholarly assessment is nuanced: while his credulity may surprise a modern reader, he consistently distinguishes between first-hand observation, hearsay, and inference (001-004). He is candid about his own ignorance and, when confronted with conflicting versions of a story, gives both accounts. The editor G. C. Macaulay notes that Herodotus was not a mere teller of strange tales but a historian with a coherent philosophy, grounded in a belief in Fate and Nemesis as forces governing human affairs (001-005). The work has been praised for its literary quality as "the first artistic work in prose that Greek literature produced," and Herodotus himself has been called "the father of story-tellers" (001-006).

## Sources

> HERODOTUS was born at Halicarnassus, on the southwest coast of Asia Minor, in the early part of the fifth century, B. C. Of his life we know almost nothing, except that he spent much of it traveling, to collect the material for his writings, and that he finally settled down at Thurii, in southern Italy, where his great work was composed. He died in 424 B. C.
> (001-001)

> Yet Herodotus is not a mere teller of strange tales. However credulous he may appear to a modern judgment, he takes care to keep separate what he knows by his own observation from what he has merely inferred and from what he has been told. He is candid about acknowledging ignorance, and when versions differ he gives both.
> (001-004)

> It is the first artistic work in prose that Greek literature produced. This prose work, which for pure literary merit no subsequent work has surpassed, than which later generations, after using the pen for centuries, have produced no prose more easy or more readable, this was the first of histories and of literary prose.
> (001-006)
