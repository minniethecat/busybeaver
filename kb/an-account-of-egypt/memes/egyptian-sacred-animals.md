## Overview

Egyptian sacred animals formed one of the most distinctive aspects of the religious landscape that Herodotus encountered. Unlike Greek practice, where animals were primarily sacrificed to the gods, the Egyptians treated many species as themselves sacred — some dwelling with humans, others in the wild — and attached the severest penalties to their killing. Herodotus records this with characteristic ethnographic precision, noting that he would avoid discussing the theological reasons behind these dedications. (006-002)

## The Universal Sacredness of Egyptian Animals

Egypt, Herodotus observes, though bordering upon Libya, does not greatly abound in wild animals, but such as they have are "one and all accounted by them sacred, some of them living with men and others not." The Egyptians appointed hereditary caretakers — both men and women — to provide food for each kind of beast separately, the office passing from father to son. Those dwelling in the various cities made vows to the god to whom a particular animal belonged by shaving the heads of their children — either the whole, the half, or the third part — and then weighing the hair against silver. Whatever it weighed, the petitioner gave to the animal's caretaker, who cut up fish of equal value to feed the animals. (006-002)

The penalties for harming a sacred animal were severe. Anyone who killed one willfully faced death; if involuntarily, whatever penalty the priests appointed. But for the ibis or the hawk, there was no distinction: whether killed willfully or accidentally, the penalty was always death. This absolute protection underscored the centrality of animal veneration in Egyptian religious life. (006-002)

## Regional Variations in Sacred Status

The sacredness of particular animals was not uniform across Egypt. Herodotus provides the crocodile as the most vivid example. For those dwelling about Thebes and Lake Moiris, the crocodile was held most sacred: each community kept a specially selected crocodile trained to tameness, adorned with ornaments of molten stone and gold in its ears and anklets round its front feet. These creatures received appointed food and sacrificial victims and were embalmed and buried in sacred tombs after death. Yet those dwelling near Elephantine ate crocodiles, regarding them not as sacred but as food. The creatures were called not "crocodiles" but *champsai* by the Egyptians — the name "crocodile" being an Ionian term comparing them to lizards found in Greek stone walls. (007-001)

The river-horse was sacred only in the district of Papremis, not to other Egyptians. Otters, the *lepidotos* fish, and the eel were all held sacred to the Nile. Of birds, the fox-goose was considered sacred. This patchwork of regional sanctity demonstrates how Egyptian religion varied by locality, with each district preserving its own traditions regarding which creatures to venerate and which to consume. (007-002)

## Sacred Serpents and the Ibis

Herodotus records an additional category of sacred animals: the serpents of Thebes, which were "not at all harmful to men," small in size, and bore two horns growing from the top of the head (007-004). When these serpents died, they were buried in the temple of Zeus, to whom they were held sacred. More remarkably, Herodotus journeyed to a region of Arabia near the city of Buto specifically to investigate reports of winged serpents. There he saw "bones of serpents and spines in quantity so great that it is impossible to make report of the number" — heaps upon heaps of spines in varying sizes (007-004). The local account held that each spring, winged serpents flew from Arabia toward Egypt, and the ibis birds met them at the mountain pass, killing them before they could enter the country. For this service, both the Arabians and the Egyptians affirmed, the ibis came to be greatly honoured by the Egyptians (007-004). Herodotus describes two kinds of ibis: the black variety, deep black all over with legs like a crane and a very curved beak, which fought the serpents; and a second kind, white-feathered except for the bare head and throat, which crowded most commonly about men's feet.

## The Phoenix: A Mythic Sacred Bird

Among the sacred birds recorded by Herodotus, the phoenix holds a singular place, though he admits he never saw one alive — only in paintings. According to the priests of Heliopolis, the phoenix visited Egypt at intervals of five hundred years, coming regularly upon the death of its father. In the paintings it resembled an eagle in outline and size, with feathers partly of gold and partly of red. The story — which Herodotus explicitly states he cannot believe — described the phoenix conveying its father from Arabia to the temple of the Sun at Heliopolis, encased in an egg of myrrh, to be buried there. (007-003)

> (007-001)
