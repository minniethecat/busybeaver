## Overview

[[Sparta]] was a prominent Greek city-state of the Peloponnese, appearing in Herodotus's *Account of Egypt* as the homeland of [[Helen of Troy]] and [[Menelaos]], and as the starting point of [[Alexander Paris]]'s fateful journey. Herodotus invokes Sparta not to describe the city itself but as an anchor point in his revisionist account of the Trojan War — an argument that the Egyptian priests' version of events, rather than Homer's, represents the historical truth.

## Sparta in the Helen Narrative

The Egyptian priests told Herodotus that "Alexander having carried off Helen was sailing away from Sparta to his own land" (010-005). Winds drove the Trojan prince off course to the Sea of Egypt, where [[Proteus]] detained Helen while banishing Alexander. This detail — that the abduction originated at [[Sparta]] — grounds the entire alternate tradition Herodotus presents, one in which Helen never reached [[Troy]] but spent the war years in Egypt under royal protection.

Herodotus further identifies a temple within the enclosure of [[Proteus]] at [[Memphis]] as the temple of the "foreign Aphrodite," conjecturing it to be "one of Helen the daughter of Tyndareus," because of the tradition that "Helen dwelt with Proteus" (010-004). The connection between Sparta and this Egyptian sanctuary runs through Helen herself — a Spartan queen honored in a foreign land.

## Homeric Geography and the Cyprian Epic

Herodotus uses [[Sparta]] as a geographical benchmark in his argument about the authorship of the *Cyprian Epic*. He notes a contradiction: the *Cyprian Epic* claims "on the third day after leaving Sparta Alexander came to Ilion bringing with him Helen, having had a gently-blowing wind and a smooth sea," but Homer's *Iliad* states Alexander "wandered from his course when he brought her" (011-003). The priests' account, which has Alexander blown to Egypt rather than reaching [[Troy]] directly, aligns with Homer's version of a wandering journey — not with the smooth sailing of the *Cyprian Epic*. This discrepancy, Herodotus argues, proves the *Cyprian Epic* was not written by [[Homer]].

## Menelaos and the Spartan Expedition

After the fall of [[Troy]], Menelaos — the Spartan king — sailed to [[Memphis]] to recover [[Helen of Troy]], having finally believed the Trojans' claim that she was in Egypt. Herodotus records that Menelaos "received Helen unhurt, and all his own wealth besides" (011-004). However, he also reports Menelaos's dark turn: detained by contrary winds as he tried to depart, the Spartan king "took two children of natives and made sacrifice of them," an act that made him "abhorred" and forced him to flee to Libya (011-004). This grim epilogue to the Helen story underscores the Egyptian perspective Herodotus consistently favors — one in which the Greeks, even noble Spartans like Menelaos, are capable of impiety.
