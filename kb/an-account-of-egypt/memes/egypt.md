## Overview

[[Egypt]] is the central subject of Herodotus's second book of the *Histories* — a land whose marvels, customs, and history the historian records in greater detail than any other country he describes. Herodotus's Egypt is defined not by political frontiers but by the [[Nile River]]: the oracle of Ammon declared that "that land was Egypt where the Nile came over and watered, and that those were Egyptians who dwelling below the city of Elephantine drank of that river" (002-004). This hydrological definition — Egypt as the gift of the Nile — is the organizing principle of Herodotus's ethnography.

## Boundaries and the Gift of the Nile

Herodotus devotes considerable attention to the question of what, precisely, constitutes Egypt. The [[Ionians]] held that only the [[Nile Delta]] was Egypt proper, reckoning its coastline from the watch-tower of Perseus to the fish-curing houses of [[Pelusion]], and extending inland as far as the city of Kercasoros where the Nile divided (002-002). Herodotus rejects this view, arguing that if the Ionian theory were correct, the Egyptians would have had no land to inhabit in former times, since the Delta is alluvial and geologically recent — "their Delta at any rate is alluvial, and has appeared (so to speak) lately, as the Egyptians themselves say and as my opinion is" (002-002).

Instead, Herodotus proposes that Egypt is all the land inhabited by Egyptians, extending from the Cataract and the city of [[Elephantine]] southwards to the sea northwards. The Nile cuts Egypt through the midst, dividing it between Asia and Libya — making Egypt, in Greek geographical terms, a country that partakes of both continents. He further notes that the inhabitants of Marea and Apis, dwelling on the Libyan border, considered themselves Libyans rather than Egyptians and petitioned the oracle of Ammon to be exempted from Egyptian dietary restrictions — a request the god denied, affirming the river-based definition of Egyptian identity (002-004).

Herodotus understood Egypt as land gradually won from the sea by the silt deposited by the Nile over millennia. He observed shells on the mountains of Egypt, salt efflorescence eating away at the pyramids, and the black, easily crumbling soil that was "in truth mud and silt brought down from Ethiopia by the river" (002-001). He compared Egypt's alluvial formation to similar processes in the Greek world — the Acheloos River transforming the Echinades islands into mainland — but argued that none of those rivers "is worthy to be compared in volume with a single one of the mouths of the Nile" (002-001).

Four features of Egyptian geography impressed Herodotus most:

1. **The River Itself**: The Nile, with its mysterious summer flood, its unknown sources, and its seven mouths, was the defining feature of the landscape
2. **The Delta**: The triangular plain of rich alluvial soil, densely populated and crisscrossed with canals, where the most productive agriculture in the known world was practiced
3. **The Soil**: Black, easily crumbling mud utterly unlike the reddish sand of Libya or the clayey earth of Arabia and Syria
4. **The Climate**: Rainless and dependent entirely on the river for water, making Egypt uniquely vulnerable to changes in the Nile's behaviour

## The Egyptian People

The Egyptians, Herodotus writes, are "religious excessively beyond all other men" (004-005). He describes them as the healthiest of all peoples next to the Libyans, attributing this to the constancy of their climate and the absence of seasonal change. Their customs, he famously notes, are the reverse of those of other nations: women go to market and conduct trade while men stay home and weave; they knead dough with their feet but clay with their hands; they write from right to left; and their priests shave their entire bodies every third day.

Beyond these curious inversions, Herodotus presents Egypt as the source of much that the Greeks considered their own: the Egyptians were the first to establish solemn assemblies and processions, to invent the names of the twelve gods, to set up altars and statues, and to practice circumcision — from whom the [[Phoenicians]] and Syrians of Palestine later learned the custom. They were also the first to forbid sexual intercourse in temples and to require washing after contact with women before entering sacred precincts (006-004).

## The Great Cities

Egypt's principal cities punctuate Herodotus's narrative as sites of royal foundation, religious festival, and historical memory:

- **[[Memphis]]**: The ancient capital at the Delta's apex, founded by Min (Menes) after he diverted the Nile, housing the great temple of Hephaistos — the primary source of Herodotus's royal chronology
- **[[Thebes]]**: The great southern city which "in old times had the name of Egypt," its circumference measuring 6,120 furlongs
- **[[Sais]]**: The dynastic city of the western Delta, site of the temple of Athene and the tombs of the 26th Dynasty kings
- **[[Bubastis]]**: The festival city of the eastern Delta, whose annual assembly drew seven hundred thousand worshippers
- **[[Heliopolis]]**: The city of the Sun, whose priests were, along with those of Memphis and Thebes, among Herodotus's principal informants
- **[[Elephantine]]**: The frontier city at the First Cataract, marking the traditional boundary between Egypt and Ethiopia

## Egypt in the World

Herodotus situates Egypt between three continental entities — Libya to the west, Arabia to the east, and the Mediterranean to the north — with Ethiopia to the south beyond the Cataract. Egypt's position gave it a unique role in the transmission of customs and knowledge: the Egyptians taught the Greeks about religion and festival practice, while themselves receiving and adapting influences from Ethiopia and the East. The country served as both a source and a crossroads, and Herodotus's ethnography captures this dual role through its careful attention to what the Egyptians borrowed and what they originated.
