## Overview

[[Menelaos]] is the king of Sparta and the husband of [[Helen of Troy|Helen]], whose abduction by [[Alexander (Paris)|Alexander]] triggered the Trojan War. In Homeric tradition, Menelaos is a central figure in the war and in the *Odyssey*'s account of the Greek heroes' returns. [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s *Account of [[egypt|Egypt]]* presents a strikingly different version, recorded from the [[egyptian-priesthood|Egyptian priests]] of [[memphis|Memphis]]: in this alternative tradition, Menelaos never found Helen at [[troy|Troy]] because she had been detained in [[egypt|Egypt]] by King [[Proteus]] throughout the war. Menelaos's subsequent journey to [[egypt|Egypt]] to reclaim his wife, and his impious act there, form a substantial digression in the historian's narrative.

## In Herodotus's Account

### The Egyptian Priesthood's Version

The priests of [[memphis|Memphis]] claimed to have knowledge derived directly from Menelaos himself, gained through inquiries during his visit to [[egypt|Egypt]] (011-004). According to their account, the Trojans had truthfully — both with oaths and without — insisted that Helen was not in their city but in [[egypt|Egypt]], held by King [[proteus|Proteus]]. The Hellenic army, believing themselves mocked, persisted in their siege until they took the city. Finding no Helen, they finally credited the story and dispatched Menelaos himself to [[egypt|Egypt]] (011-004).

### The Recovery of Helen

Menelaos sailed to [[egypt|Egypt]] and proceeded up [[nile-river|the Nile]] to [[memphis|Memphis]], where he told the truth of the matter to [[proteus|Proteus]]. He was not only received with great hospitality and entertainment but also recovered Helen unharmed, along with all his stolen wealth (011-004). [[proteus|Proteus]], who had held both Helen and the treasure in safekeeping since expelling Alexander from [[egypt|Egypt]], returned them to Menelaos without dispute — fulfilling the expectation of hospitality that the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian king had upheld from the beginning of the affair.

### The Impious Sacrifice

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] records that Menelaos, despite the generous treatment he had received from the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians, showed himself ungrateful. When he set forth to sail away from [[egypt|Egypt]], contrary winds detained him. As this condition persisted for a long time, Menelaos "devised an impious deed": he took two native children and sacrificed them (011-004).

When this act was discovered, Menelaos became abhorred by the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians. He was pursued and forced to flee, escaping with his ships to Libya. What became of him after this, the [[egyptian-priesthood|Egyptian priests]] were unable to say — "of these things they said that they found out part by inquiries, and the rest, namely that which happened in their own land, they related from sure and certain knowledge" (011-004).

## Homeric Context

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] notes that Homer was aware of this [[egypt|Egypt]]ian variant of the Helen story but chose a different version more suitable to epic poetry. He cites lines from the *Iliad* describing Alexander's wanderings past [[sidon|Sidon]] — which [[herodotus|Herodotus]] takes as evidence that Homer knew of the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian tradition (010-006). The historian also quotes Menelaos's own words to Telemachos in the *Odyssey*: "Still the gods stayed me in [[egypt|Egypt]], to come back hither desiring, stayed me from voyaging home, since sacrifice due I performed not" (011-002), seeing in this passage an oblique acknowledgment of the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian version.

## The Quarrel Over Homer's Sources

[[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s discussion of Menelaos frames a larger historiographical argument. The historian contends that Homer, while aware of the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian variant, suppressed it — and that the *Cyprian Epic* (attributed to Stasinus rather than Homer) provides an independent witness to the tradition that Alexander reached [[egypt|Egypt]]. For [[herodotus|Herodotus]], the [[egyptian-priesthood|Egyptian priests]]' first-hand knowledge, transmitted through Menelaos's own visit, carries greater authority than the dominant Greek poetic tradition.

## Significance

Menelaos's story illuminates several themes central to [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s *Account of [[egypt|Egypt]]*. First, it showcases the historian's method of cross-referencing Greek poetic tradition with foreign sources — the [[egyptian-priesthood|Egyptian priests]]' institutional memory serving as a corrective to Homeric epic. Second, the episode of the child sacrifice casts Menelaos as morally ambiguous — a recipient of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian hospitality who repaid it with impiety — underscoring the fragility of guest-friendship in a world of competing obligations. Third, the story exemplifies [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s broader argument that [[egypt|Egypt]] preserved the truest accounts of events obscured by Greek myth-making, a thesis he advances throughout his [[egypt|Egypt]]ian ethnography.
