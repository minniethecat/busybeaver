## Overview

Ritual purity was a foundational principle of ancient [[egypt|Egypt]]ian religion, governing every aspect of interaction between the human and the divine. As recorded by [[herodotus|Herodotus]] in the 5th century BCE, the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians maintained an elaborate system of purification practices — ranging from daily bodily ablutions and garment regulations to dietary prohibitions and sacrificial protocols — all aimed at maintaining a state of cleanliness before the gods. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] presents this concern for purity as central to [[egypt|Egypt]]ian identity, describing them as "religious excessively beyond all other men" (004-005) and "excessively careful in their observances" (006-005).

## Description

### Bodily Purity

The most visible dimension of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian ritual purity was bodily cleanliness. The priests, who served as intermediaries between the people and the gods, observed the strictest regimen. They shaved their entire bodies every other day, "so that no lice or any other foul thing may come to be upon them when they minister to the gods" (004-005). They washed themselves in cold water twice each day and twice each night, maintaining a near-continuous state of physical purity. (004-005)

The general population likewise observed purification customs, though less rigorously. The [[egypt|Egypt]]ians practiced circumcision "for the sake of cleanliness, preferring to be clean rather than comely" (004-005). This distinguishes the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian practice from the Greek, framing circumcision not as a mark of ethnic identity but as a deliberate hygienic and religious measure.

### Garment Regulations

Clothing formed an essential component of ritual purity. The [[egypt|Egypt]]ians wore garments of linen "always newly washed," making frequent laundering "a special point of practice" (004-005). The priests were restricted to linen garments and papyrus sandals, forbidden from wearing any other material. (004-005)

A significant purity rule concerned wool. Woolen garments were prohibited within temple precincts and could not be used in burial. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] notes that this [[egypt|Egypt]]ian practice harmonized with the so-called Orphic and Bacchic mysteries — and with the Pythagoreans — all of which forbade burial in wool. He observes that these Greek rites were "really [[egypt|Egypt]]ian" in origin, suggesting a historical transmission of purity norms from [[egypt|Egypt]] to the Greek world. (007-005)

### Temple Purification

Entry into sacred spaces required specific purification. The [[egypt|Egypt]]ians were, according to [[herodotus|Herodotus]], the first people to prohibit sexual intercourse in temples and to require washing after sexual contact before entering a temple. He contrasts this with the practices of "almost all other men except the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians and the [[hellenes|Hellenes]]," who saw no distinction between human and animal behavior in sacred spaces. (006-004)

The daily ritual of rinsing bronze drinking cups was observed universally among the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians — "not some only do this but all" (004-005) — suggesting that basic purity practices pervaded all levels of society, not merely the priestly caste.

### Contact Pollution and Dietary Restrictions

Certain animals and substances were considered sources of ritual pollution. The pig was regarded as "an abominable animal" (005-005). If an [[egypt|Egypt]]ian accidentally touched a pig, even in passing, he was required to immerse himself in the river, garments and all, to restore his purity. Swineherds, though native [[egypt|Egypt]]ians, were excluded from temple entry and could only intermarry among themselves — a form of social pollution that accompanied their ritual uncleanness. (005-005)

Beans were considered "an unclean kind of pulse" (004-005). The priests would not even look upon them, and [[egypt|Egypt]]ians neither sowed them in their land nor consumed them. This dietary prohibition reinforced the boundary between the pure and the impure in daily life.

### Sacrificial Purity

Animals offered to the gods required rigorous inspection. Bulls were tested for ritual purity: a single black hair rendered the beast "not clean for sacrifice" (004-006). A designated priest examined the animal's tongue, tail, and entire body — both standing and lying on its back — and only upon confirming it was "clean in respect of all these things" would he seal it with papyrus and sealing-earth for sacrifice. This meticulous examination demonstrates how purity was not an abstract ideal but a physical condition subject to verification. (004-006)

### Purification Through Purging

The [[egypt|Egypt]]ians pursued internal purity as well as external cleanliness. For three consecutive days each month, they purged themselves "hunting after health with emetics and clysters" (007-005). This practice was rooted in a medical theory that all diseases originated from the food consumed, making the removal of bodily impurities essential to maintaining health. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] notes that the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians were, after the Libyans, the healthiest of all peoples — a condition he attributes to the stability of the seasons, but which the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians themselves connected to their purgative practices. (007-005)

## Historical Context

The ritual purity system [[herodotus|Herodotus]] describes reflects the highly institutionalized temple culture of Late Period [[egypt|Egypt]]. By the 5th century BCE, [[egypt|Egypt]] was under [[persian|Persian]] rule following Cambyses's conquest in 525 BCE, but the temples at [[memphis|Memphis]], [[thebes|Thebes]], and [[heliopolis|Heliopolis]] — where [[herodotus|Herodotus]] consulted priests — continued to function as centers of traditional religious life. The hereditary priesthood maintained purity regulations that had developed over millennia, with the post of chief-priest passing from father to son. (004-005)

[[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s Greek audience would have recognized certain parallels — particularly in the prohibitions shared with Orphic, Bacchic, and Pythagorean rites — but the comprehensive nature of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian purity regulations, encompassing bodily maintenance, clothing, diet, sexual conduct, temple access, and sacrificial protocol, far exceeded anything known in the Hellenic world.

## Herodotus's Commentary

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] approaches [[egypt|Egypt]]ian purity with a mixture of admiration and ethnographic curiosity. He frames the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians' concern for ritual cleanliness not as superstitious excess but as evidence of their exceptional religiosity. His description of the priests' bodily regimen — the shaving, the cold-water washing, the linen-only garments — is presented without irony, conveying a respect for the discipline involved.

At the same time, [[herodotus|Herodotus]] maintains his characteristic role as comparative observer. He notes where [[egypt|Egypt]]ian purity norms align with Greek customs — temple purification after sexual contact was observed by both [[egypt|Egypt]]ians and [[hellenes|Hellenes]], setting them apart from other peoples. (006-004) He also records where they diverge: the prohibition on wool in burial and temples, which he traces to [[egypt|Egypt]]ian influence on Orphic, Bacchic, and Pythagorean rites. (007-005) This recurring theme — that Greek religious knowledge ultimately derived from the older and more venerable [[egypt|Egypt]]ian tradition — reflects [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s broader conviction that [[egypt|Egypt]] was the source of much Hellenic religious practice.

## Significance

The [[egypt|Egypt]]ian system of ritual purity, as documented by [[herodotus|Herodotus]], illuminates how ancient [[egypt|Egypt]]ian religion operated as a total social system. Purity was not a private spiritual state but a publicly observable condition maintained through daily, monthly, and situational practices. It governed bodily presentation (shaving, circumcision, washing), material culture (linen garments, bronze cups, papyrus sandals), social organization (the segregation of swineherds, the hereditary priesthood), and relations with the divine (sacrificial inspection, temple access).

[[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s account also provides some of the earliest evidence for the transmission of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian religious norms into Greek culture. The shared prohibitions on wool and temple intercourse suggest that [[egypt|Egypt]]ian purity concepts influenced the development of Hellenic mystery cults and philosophical schools, making ritual purity a bridge between the two civilizations. The attention [[herodotus|Herodotus]] devotes to these practices — spanning multiple chapters of his account — underscores their centrality to his portrait of [[egypt|Egypt]] as a civilization defined by its piety and its disciplined approach to the sacred.

The priests shave themselves all over their body every other day, so that no lice or any other foul thing may come to be upon them when they minister to the gods; and the priests wear garments of linen only and sandals of papyrus, and any other garment they may not take nor other sandals; these wash themselves in cold water twice in a day and twice again in the night.

> The pig is accounted by the Egyptians an abominable animal; and first, if any of them in passing by touch a pig, he goes into the river and dips himself forthwith in the water together with his garments; and then too swineherds, though they may be native Egyptians, unlike all others, do not enter any of the temples in Egypt, nor is anyone willing to give his daughter in marriage to one of them or to take a wife from among them; but the swineherds both give in marriage to one another and take from one another.
> (005-005)

> For three successive days in each month they purge, hunting after health with emetics and clysters, and they think that all the diseases which exist are produced in men by the food on which they live.
