Atarbechis was a city on the island of [[Prosopitis]] in the [[Nile Delta]], notable in [[Herodotus]]'s account for its role in Egyptian cattle-burial customs and for housing a holy temple of Aphrodite. (004-006)

## The Bone-Collection Ritual

[[Herodotus]] describes a remarkable practice centred on Atarbechis. When sacrificial oxen died throughout [[egypt|Egypt]], the males were buried in the suburbs of each town, with one horn — or sometimes both — protruding from the earth to mark the grave. Female cattle, being sacred to [[isis|Isis]], were cast into the [[Nile River|Nile]]. (004-006)

After the bodies had decomposed and the appointed time arrived, a boat was despatched from the island of Prosopitis — specifically from the city of Atarbechis — to every Egyptian city. Men from this boat travelled round the cities, dug up the bones of the oxen, and carried them back to be buried together in a single place. (004-006) This systematic, centralized reburial of cattle bones across all Egyptian cities reveals an organized religious infrastructure operating at a national scale.

## The Temple of Aphrodite

Atarbechis housed a holy temple of Aphrodite — likely the Egyptian goddess [[Hathor]] in [[Herodotus]]'s syncretic identification. The presence of this temple suggests that the bone-collection ritual was not merely a practical matter of disposal but a religious observance centred on the cult of this goddess. The association of a cattle-related ritual with a goddess identified with Aphrodite/Hathor is consistent with [[Hathor]]'s bovine associations in Egyptian religion, where she was frequently depicted as a cow or a woman with cow's horns.

## Relationship to Egyptian Sacrificial Customs

The ritual originating from Atarbechis formed part of the broader Egyptian system of cattle sacrifice regulated by the priests. Male oxen were tested for ritual purity — a single black hair rendered the beast "not clean for sacrifice" (004-006) — before being sealed and offered. The prohibition on consuming the head of any sacrificial animal, and the imprecations uttered upon severed heads, were universal Egyptian practices observed alongside the bone-reburial custom originating from Atarbechis. (004-006)
