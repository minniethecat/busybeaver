## Overview

Arabia, as described by Herodotus in the "Account of Egypt," is the region immediately east of the Nile valley, characterized by a continuous mountain range running north to south toward the Erythraian Sea (Red Sea). This Arabian mountain range served as Egypt's eastern boundary and provided the stone quarries used in the construction of the pyramids. (001-009) (002-001)

## Geographic Description

Herodotus places Arabia directly adjacent to Egypt. The Arabian mountain range extends without interruption from the north toward the south and the Erythraian Sea, forming a natural barrier along Egypt's eastern flank. Within this range lie the quarries that supplied stone for the pyramids at Memphis. (001-009)

South of Egypt, a long and narrow gulf of the Erythraian Sea extends into the land of Arabia. Herodotus describes this gulf — the modern Red Sea — as a voyage of forty days by oar from its innermost point to the open sea, and half a day's breadth at its widest point, with a daily ebb and flow of tides. (002-001)

## Relationship to Egypt

Herodotus reports a territorial dispute regarding Egypt's borders with Arabia. Some accounts — which the historian himself rejects — assign portions of Egypt to both Libya and Arabia. Herodotus argues against this division on geological grounds: since the Egyptian Delta is entirely alluvial, accepting the Arabian claim would imply that Egypt had no original territory at all. (002-002)

The Arabian side of the Nile valley, according to Herodotus, produces the aromatic substances used in Egyptian embalming, including spices and perfumes derived from the region. These trade goods underscore the economic interdependence between Egypt and Arabia.

## Strategic Significance

The Persian garrisons maintained by Psammetichos were stationed at Daphnai of Pelusion toward Arabia and Assyria, indicating Arabia's strategic importance as a frontier region. Herodotus notes that in his own time, the Persian administration maintained outposts in the same positions, demonstrating the enduring military logic of Egypt's eastern defenses. (003-008)
