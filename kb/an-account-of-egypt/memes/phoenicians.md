# Phoenicians

## Overview

The Phoenicians (or Phenicians) appear in [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s account as a maritime people of the Levant with significant connections to [[egypt|Egypt]]. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] records their presence in [[egypt|Egypt]]ian territory, their role in founding oracular traditions, and their interactions with [[egypt|Egypt]]ian history from the age of [[sesostris|Sesostris]] through the reign of [[proteus|Proteus]]. They are presented as traders, colonizers, and intermediaries between [[egypt|Egypt]] and the wider Mediterranean world.

## Description

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] documents the Phoenicians in several distinct [[egypt|Egypt]]ian contexts. At [[memphis|Memphis]], during the reign of [[proteus|Proteus]], a Tyrian Phoenician community maintained a permanent settlement: "Round about this enclosure dwell Phenicians of Tyre, and this whole region is called the Camp of the Tyrians" (010-004). Within [[proteus|Proteus]]'s sacred enclosure stood a temple of the "foreign Aphrodite," which [[herodotus|Herodotus]] speculates may have belonged to Helen, connecting Phoenician settlement to the Trojan War cycle.

The Phoenicians are also credited with early Mediterranean exploration. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] records that a temple of [[heracles|Heracles]] at Thasos "had been set up by the Phenicians, who had sailed out to seek for Europa and had colonised Thasos; and these things happened full five generations of men before [[heracles|Heracles]] the son of Amphitryon was born in Hellas" (005-002). This chronology places Phoenician activity deep in the mythic past.

In the context of oracular origins, [[egyptian-priesthood|Egyptian priests]] told [[herodotus|Herodotus]] that "two women in the service of the temple had been carried away from [[thebes|Thebes]] by Phenicians, and that they had heard that one of them had been sold to go into Libya and the other to the [[hellenes|Hellenes]]; and these women, they said, were they who first founded the prophetic seats among the nations" (006-001). This account positions Phoenicians as the agents through whom [[egypt|Egypt]]ian oracular knowledge was transmitted to Libya and Greece.

## Historical Context

[[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s treatment of the Phoenicians reflects the 5th-century BCE Mediterranean, where Phoenician city-states (Tyre, [[sidon|Sidon]]) were active maritime powers. The [[persian|Persian]] Empire, which dominated both [[egypt|Egypt]] and Phoenicia during [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s lifetime, incorporated both regions into a single administrative and economic network. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] uses "Phenicians" as a collective term for the Canaanite-derived coastal peoples, including those of Tyre and [[sidon|Sidon]] (011-003).

## Significance

The Phoenicians serve a specific narrative function in [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s [[egypt|Egypt]]ian account: they are the intermediaries who transmit [[egypt|Egypt]]ian culture and religion to the Greek world. Whether through the founding of oracles, the colonization of Thasos, or the temple of [[heracles|Heracles]], Phoenician activity provides the channel by which [[egypt|Egypt]]ian religious institutions reached the [[hellenes|Hellenes]]. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] consistently uses Phoenician agency to explain how [[egypt|Egypt]]ian practices diffused across the Mediterranean, reinforcing his broader thesis of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian cultural primacy.
