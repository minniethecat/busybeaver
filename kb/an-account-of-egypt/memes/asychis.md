## Overview

Asychis was a king of [[egypt|Egypt]] who, according to the priests at [[memphis|Memphis]], succeeded [[mykerinos|Mykerinos]] to the throne (013-003). He is remembered in [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s account for two things: a remarkable law permitting money to be borrowed against the security of a father's corpse, and a brick pyramid bearing a proud inscription comparing itself favourably to the stone pyramids of earlier kings (013-003).

## Background / Biography

Asychis became king after [[mykerinos|Mykerinos]], the son of [[cheops|Cheops]], whose reign had been cut short by an oracle that gave him only six years to live (012-005) (013-003). Asychis distinguished himself as a builder, constructing a temple gateway for [[hephaistos|Hephaistos]] ([[hephaistos|Ptah]]) at [[memphis|Memphis]] that faced the sunrise. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] describes it as the most beautiful and largest of all such gateways, surpassing the others in the quantity of carved figures and architectural ornament (013-003). The chronological position of Asychis in [[egypt|Egypt]]ian history is uncertain, and he has not been securely identified with any known pharaoh from other sources.

## Role in the Account

Asychis's reign provides [[herodotus|Herodotus]] with one of the most curious legal anecdotes in the entire *Account of [[egypt|Egypt]]*. The king observed that money circulated very slowly in [[egypt|Egypt]], so he enacted a novel law allowing a borrower to offer the embalmed body of his father as collateral for a loan (013-003). An additional clause gave the lender a claim on the borrower's own future tomb, creating a powerful incentive to repay: failure to discharge the debt meant the borrower himself could not be buried in the family tomb, nor could he bury any of his deceased relatives (013-003). This law illustrates the extraordinary importance [[egypt|Egypt]]ians attached to proper burial and the integrity of the family sepulchre. Asychis also built himself a pyramid of brick rather than stone, inscribed with a boastful text challenging comparison with the great stone pyramids: it claimed to surpass them just as [[zeus|Zeus]] surpasses other gods, since it was made from mud dredged from a lake with poles and then baked into bricks (013-003).

## Herodotus's Portrayal

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] presents Asychis as an ambitious king who sought to surpass all his predecessors (013-003). The tone is neutral to appreciative — there is no moral condemnation as with [[cheops|Cheops]] nor tragic sympathy as with [[mykerinos|Mykerinos]]. The narrator seems particularly interested in the unusual law and the brick pyramid's inscription, both of which he relays without scepticism. The law about borrowing against a father's corpse is reported as a straightforward matter of fact, leaving modern readers to marvel at the centrality of burial customs in [[egypt|Egypt]]ian society.

## Sources

> After Mykerinos the priests said Asychis became king of Egypt, and he made for Hephaistos the temple gateway which is towards the sunrising, by far the most beautiful and the largest of the gateways; for while they all have figures carved upon them and innumerable ornaments of building besides, this has them very much more than the rest.
> (013-003)

> In this king's reign they told me that, as the circulation of money was very slow, a law was made for the Egyptians that a man might have that money lent to him which he needed, by offering as security the dead body of his father; and there was added moreover to this law another, namely that he who lent the money should have a claim also to the whole of the sepulchral chamber belonging to him who received it.
> (013-003)

> Despise not me in comparison with the pyramids of stone, seeing that I excel them as much as Zeus excels the other gods; for with a pole they struck into the lake, and whatever of the mud attached itself to the pole, this they gathered up and made bricks, and in such manner they finished me.
> (013-003)
