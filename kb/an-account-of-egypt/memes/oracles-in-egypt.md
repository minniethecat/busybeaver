## Overview

Oracles played a central role in Egyptian religious and political life, as Herodotus repeatedly attests throughout his account. The most significant was the oracle of Leto at Buto, a major temple complex in the Nile Delta that Herodotus considered worthy of extended description. Other oracles — such as that of Ammon in the Libyan desert and those consulted by the Ethiopians — also feature in the narrative. The Egyptians sought divine counsel through oracles for matters ranging from personal healing to royal succession, and the pronouncements of these oracles shaped the course of Egyptian history as Herodotus understood it. (015-001)

## The Oracle of Buto

The oracle of Leto at Buto was, by Herodotus's reckoning, the pre-eminent oracular shrine in Egypt. He introduces his description of it with the declaration that it is "worthy to be described," and he devotes considerable attention to its physical setting and theological significance. The oracle was housed within the temple of Leto in the great city of Buto, situated near the Sebennytic mouth of the Nile as one sailed upriver from the sea. The temple complex included a temple of Apollo and Artemis alongside the shrine of Leto, and the gateway stood ten fathoms in height — a monumental entrance that signalled the importance of the site. (015-001)

The temple of Buto was associated with the story of Leto, whom the Egyptians counted among the eight primordial gods. According to the tradition Herodotus relates, Leto had received the infant Apollo from Isis and concealed him on the floating island of Chemmis, near Buto, to protect him from Typhon. This mythological foundation linked the oracle of Buto to the broader Egyptian cosmology and to the Greek divine family that Herodotus's audience would recognize.

## Oracles and Royal Destiny

Several of the most dramatic episodes in Herodotus's Egyptian narrative turn upon oracular pronouncements. King Pheros, blinded for ten years as a punishment, received an oracle from Buto instructing him that his sight would be restored if he washed his eyes with the urine of a woman who had been faithful to her husband alone. The search for such a woman became a test of marital fidelity across the kingdom. (010-003)

Another king, Mykerinos, received an oracle from Buto foretelling that he had only six years left to live. In response, he devised a strategy of living by night as well as by day — ordering lamps lit after sunset so that the six years might become twelve, "the nights being turned into days." Herodotus records this with evident appreciation for the king's cunning, though the oracle's truth was not to be evaded. (012-005)

The story of Psammetichos's rise to sole kingship was also mediated by an oracle. When the twelve kings of Egypt were making libations, Psammetichos — standing last in order and lacking a cup — used his bronze helmet instead. The other kings, remembering an oracle that declared whichever of them should pour libation with a bronze cup would become sole ruler, stripped Psammetichos of his power and drove him into the fen-country. Yet the oracle proved inescapable: Psammetichos later returned with the aid of Ionian and Carian mercenaries and indeed became the sole king of Egypt. (014-008)

## The Oracle of Ammon

Beyond Egypt proper, the oracle of Ammon in the Libyan desert carried sufficient authority to settle matters of geography. Herodotus reports consulting its pronouncement — which he heard only at second hand — to support his claim that Egypt extended further south than most Greeks believed. He also tells of King Necos, who abandoned the digging of a canal from the Nile to the Red Sea because "the utterance of an oracle impeded him, which was to the effect that he was working for the Barbarian." This story suggests that oracles could serve as checks on royal ambition, intervening to halt projects that might benefit Egypt's enemies. (002-004)

## Significance

The oracles of Egypt, as Herodotus presents them, were not merely instruments of divination but institutions that embedded divine authority within the fabric of Egyptian governance. Kings consulted oracles before undertaking major projects; the oracles' pronouncements could legitimize or undermine a reign; and the physical grandeur of the oracle at Buto — with its ten-fathom gateway — testified to the wealth and prestige that oracular institutions commanded. For Herodotus's Greek readers, the Egyptian oracles would have resonated with their own experience of Delphi and Dodona, yet the Egyptian system was distinctive in its integration of oracle, temple, and monarchy into a single fabric of sacred kingship.
