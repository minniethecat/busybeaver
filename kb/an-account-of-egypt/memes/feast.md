## Overview

Feasting was an integral part of Egyptian religious and social life in the fifth century BCE. Herodotus describes solemn assemblies, sacrificial banquets, and royal feasts that marked major festivals and life events, where Egyptians gathered in great numbers to eat, drink, and celebrate their gods.

## Description

The largest and most celebrated feast in Egypt took place at the city of Bubastis in honor of Artemis. Herodotus reports that men and women sailed in boats to the festival — as many as seven hundred thousand persons, excluding children — and upon arrival they performed sacrifices of an innumerable multitude of cattle. (006-002) The feast was accompanied by music: some women played clappers, others played flutes, and the rest sang and clapped their hands as they journeyed along the river.

A notable feast was also celebrated in commemoration of the descent and return of King Rhampsinitos. According to the priests, Rhampsinitos went down alive to Hades, played dice with Demeter, and returned with a golden handkerchief as a gift. Herodotus knew this feast was still observed in his own time. (012-001)

Royal feasts could also be occasions of treachery. When Sesostris returned from his military campaigns, his brother invited him and his sons to a feast at Daphnai in the district of Pelusion, then piled the house with brushwood and set it on fire in an attempt to seize the throne. (010-001) At another royal feast, the twelve kings who ruled Egypt jointly gathered for sacrifice at the temple of Hephaistos. When the chief priest brought out only eleven golden cups for libation, Psammetichos — the last in line — used his bronze helmet as a drinking vessel, inadvertently fulfilling an oracle that predicted his sole rule. (014-008)

Feasts were closely tied to sacrifice: after the god's portion was offered on the altar, the remaining meat was consumed by the worshippers in a communal meal. At Papremis, the festival included a mock battle in addition to the feast.

## Historical Context

The Egyptian feast served multiple functions: religious observance, community bonding, and political display. The scale of these gatherings impressed Herodotus, who uses them to contrast Egyptian piety with Greek practice. The feast culture of Egypt also provided the narrative setting for several key political events in the historian's account, showing how shared meals could be stages for both celebration and conspiracy.

## Significance

Feasting in ancient Egypt was more than eating and drinking — it was the primary mode of public religious life. The enormous assemblies at Bubastis demonstrated the organizational capacity of Egyptian society and the centrality of the temple economy. Herodotus's detailed descriptions of Egyptian feasts serve his broader argument that many Greek religious customs, including solemn assemblies and processions, were borrowed from Egypt.
