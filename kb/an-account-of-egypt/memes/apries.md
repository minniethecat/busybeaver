## Overview

Apries ([[egypt|Egypt]]ian: Wahibre Haaibre) was a pharaoh of the 26th Dynasty who reigned in the early sixth century BCE and whose rule ended in catastrophe. He is the son of [[psammis|Psammis]] and the father of [[psammetichos|Psammetichos]] II. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] presents Apries as the most prosperous king of his era — surpassed only by his great-grandfather [[psammetichos|Psammetichos]] I — whose overconfidence in his own fortune led him to dispatch an ill-fated expedition against [[cyrene|Cyrene]] and ultimately to be overthrown by his general [[amasis|Amasis]]. (015-009)

## Background / Biography

Apries inherited the throne from his father [[psammis|Psammis]], whose reign had lasted merely six years and ended shortly after an expedition to [[ethiopia-region|Ethiopia]]. (015-009) Apries himself was a man of great wealth and military ambition. He was the king who sent an embassy to the [[oracle-of-ammon|Oracle of Ammon]] and who maintained a substantial force of foreign mercenaries — [[ionians|Ionians]] and Carians numbering thirty thousand men — stationed at [[sais|Sais]] in a great palace. (015-010) (016-002)

The turning point of his reign came when he dispatched a large army against [[cyrene|Cyrene]] in North Africa. The expedition ended in a severe defeat, and the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians — blaming Apries for the disaster — revolted against him. (015-009) Apries sent his general [[amasis|Amasis]] to persuade the rebel forces to disperse, but instead the rebels declared [[amasis|Amasis]] king. When Apries learned of this treason, he sent Patarbemis, one of his most honoured courtiers, to summon [[amasis|Amasis]] to his presence. [[amasis|Amasis]], by then openly defiant, replied with an insult — which, according to the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians, consisted of a gesture with his buttocks — and refused to come. When the mutilated Patarbemis returned to Apries, the king, in a fury, ordered his nose and ears cut off. This act of cruelty turned the remaining [[egypt|Egypt]]ians against him and they went over to [[amasis|Amasis]]. (015-010) (016-002)

## Role in the Account

Apries appears in [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s narrative as the archetype of a king whose prosperity breeds overconfidence and whose cruelty precipitates his own downfall. He represents the final chapter of the 26th Dynasty's golden age before the rise of [[amasis|Amasis]], who — despite his common birth — would bring [[egypt|Egypt]] to its greatest prosperity. (016-003) (016-004)

The battle between Apries and [[amasis|Amasis]] took place at [[momemphis|Momemphis]]. Despite his foreign mercenaries, Apries was defeated and taken alive. He was brought to the royal palace at [[sais|Sais]], and although [[amasis|Amasis]] initially treated him with leniency, the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians demanded his death. Apries was handed over to them and strangled — a fate that [[herodotus|Herodotus]] notes befell a man whose house had once been the most prosperous of all. (016-002)

## Herodotus's Portrayal

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] presents Apries with a mixture of respect for his former greatness and implicit criticism of his character. The historian explicitly identifies Apries as the most prosperous king after [[psammetichos|Psammetichos]] — a significant compliment in the context of [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s narrative. (015-009) Yet the story of Patarbemis reveals a man whose temper destroys him: by mutilating his own loyal servant, Apries alienates his remaining supporters and seals his fate. (016-002) [[herodotus|Herodotus]] offers no moralising commentary on Apries's fall, but the narrative arc — from immense prosperity to death at the hands of his own people — speaks for itself.

> (015-009)

> So Apries having heard this armed his foreign mercenaries and marched against the Egyptians: and he had about him Carian and Ionian mercenaries to the number of thirty thousand. … So when Apries leading his foreign mercenaries, and Amasis at the head of the whole body of the Egyptians, in their approach to one another had come to the city of Momemphis, they engaged in battle: and the foreign troops fought well, but being far inferior in number they were worsted.
> (016-002)

> Apries having thus been overthrown, Amasis became king, being of the district of Sais.
