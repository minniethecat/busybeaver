## Overview

Oil served multiple essential functions in ancient Egyptian life — as a cosmetic for anointing the body, as fuel for lamps, and as a substance employed in religious and funerary rites. Herodotus was particularly interested in the castor-berry oil which the Egyptians called *kiki*, and he provides a detailed account of its cultivation and production. (009-001)

## Castor Oil (*Kiki*)

The Egyptians of the marshlands obtained their anointing oil from the castor-berry plant, which they called *kiki*. Herodotus describes how the Egyptians sowed these plants along the banks of the rivers and pools, where they produced berries in great quantity "but of an evil smell." The oil was extracted by two methods: some cut the berries and pressed the oil directly, while others roasted them first and then boiled them down, collecting the oil that ran off. The resulting oil was described as fatty and no less suitable for burning than olive oil, though it emitted a disagreeable odour. (009-001)

That the Egyptians relied on castor oil rather than olive oil reflects the agricultural realities of their land. Herodotus notes explicitly that the olive tree did not grow in Egypt — a fact that distinguished it sharply from the Hellenic world, where olive oil was the universal medium for cooking, lighting, and anointing. The castor plant, which grew wild in Greece, was cultivated systematically in Egypt to fill this gap. (009-001)

## Oil in Ritual and Burial

Oil also had a place in Egyptian funerary practice. The embalming process, which Herodotus describes in meticulous detail, involved the use of various unguents and oils. When a body of a person of regard was prepared, the embalmers employed oils in the preservation of the corpse. (008-002)

Beyond burial, oils and fragrances were integral to Egyptian religious life. Herodotus mentions that the Egyptians were the first to establish "solemn assemblies and processions and approaches to the temples," and that the burning of incense and the offering of fragrant substances — myrrh, frankincense, and oils — were central to their worship. (006-002)
