# Hellenes

## Overview

The Hellenes (Greeks) appear throughout [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s [[egypt|Egypt]]ian account not as the main subject but as the perpetual point of comparison. [[herodotus|Herodotus]], writing for a Greek audience, consistently measures [[egypt|Egypt]]ian customs, knowledge, and achievements against Hellenic equivalents. The Hellenes serve as the familiar baseline against which [[egypt|Egypt]]ian difference — and often [[egypt|Egypt]]ian superiority — is demonstrated.

## Description

[[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s comparative method is visible from the earliest passages. Discussing the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian calendar, he notes that the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians divided the year into twelve months of thirty days with five intercalary days: "and they reckon to this extent more wisely than the Hellenes, as it seems to me, inasmuch as the Hellenes throw in an intercalated month every other year, to make the seasons right" (001-009). The [[egypt|Egypt]]ian system is presented as more rational than the Greek.

This pattern repeats across domains. In geography, [[herodotus|Herodotus]] compares the distance from the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian coast to [[heliopolis|Heliopolis]] with the road from Athens to Olympia, noting the "difference very small by which these roads fail of being equal in length" (001-009). In religion, he traces Greek deities to [[egypt|Egypt]]ian origins: [[heracles|Heracles]], Dionysos ([[osiris|Osiris]]), and the oracles at Dodona and [[ammon|Ammon]] all derive, in his account, from [[egypt|Egypt]]ian prototypes.

The Hellenes also appear as historical actors in [[egypt|Egypt]]. Cambyses marched against [[egypt|Egypt]] "taking with him as helpers not only other nations of which he was ruler, but also those of the Hellenes over whom he had power besides" (001-007). The Greek presence in [[egypt|Egypt]] included mercenaries, traders at [[naucratis|Naucratis]], and visitors like [[herodotus|Herodotus]] himself.

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] is candid about the limits of Greek knowledge. When the [[egyptian-priesthood|Egyptian priests]] trace 341 generations of kings, [[herodotus|Herodotus]] contrasts their record-keeping with Greek myth: "the Hellenes relate, besides many other idle tales, that [[psammetichos|Psammetichos]] cut out the tongues of certain women and then caused the children to live with these women" (001-008). He systematically prefers [[egypt|Egypt]]ian priestly accounts to Greek popular tradition.

## Historical Context

In the 5th century BCE, Greek-[[egypt|Egypt]]ian contact was extensive. [[naucratis|Naucratis]] was a Greek trading settlement in [[nile-river|the Nile]] [[nile-delta|Delta]], Greek mercenaries served [[egypt|Egypt]]ian and [[persian|Persian]] rulers, and travelers like [[herodotus|Herodotus]], Hecataeus, and Solon visited [[egypt|Egypt]]. [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s *Histories* was composed for a Greek audience that was both curious about [[egypt|Egypt]] and receptive to claims of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian cultural priority — a thesis that flattered Greek admiration for antiquity while challenging assumptions of Hellenic uniqueness.

## Significance

The Hellenes in [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s [[egypt|Egypt]]ian account function as a rhetorical device: they are the known against which the unknown is measured. By consistently finding [[egypt|Egypt]]ian customs more ancient, more rational, or more pious than Greek equivalents, [[herodotus|Herodotus]] builds his argument for [[egypt|Egypt]]ian cultural primacy. Yet the comparison also reveals the Greek intellectual framework [[herodotus|Herodotus]] brings to his inquiry — his questions about origins, causes, and comparisons are characteristically Hellenic, even when his answers challenge Hellenic assumptions.
