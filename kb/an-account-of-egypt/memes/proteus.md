## Overview

Proteus is an [[egypt|Egypt]]ian king of [[memphis|Memphis]] who appears in [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s account as a just and pious ruler. He is most notable for his role in an alternative tradition of the Trojan War story: according to [[egyptian-priesthood|Egyptian priests]], it was Proteus who received [[helen-of-troy|Helen of Troy]] in [[egypt|Egypt]] after [[alexander-paris|Paris]] (called Alexander by [[herodotus|Herodotus]]) was driven off course by contrary winds, and who detained her and the stolen wealth safely until [[menelaos|Menelaos]] could retrieve them. (010-004)

## Background / Biography

Proteus succeeded to the throne as a man of [[memphis|Memphis]], according to the [[egyptian-priesthood|Egyptian priests]] whom [[herodotus|Herodotus]] consulted. His name, [[herodotus|Herodotus]] notes, is the Hellenic rendering of his [[egypt|Egypt]]ian name. (010-004) At [[memphis|Memphis]] there existed in [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s time a sacred enclosure dedicated to Proteus, described as very fair and well ordered, situated on the north-facing side of the temple of [[hephaistos|Hephaistos]]. The area around this enclosure was inhabited by [[phoenicians|Phoenicians]] of Tyre and was known as the Camp of the Tyrians. Within the enclosure stood a temple of the "foreign Aphrodite," which [[herodotus|Herodotus]] conjectured was actually a temple dedicated to Helen, daughter of Tyndareus. (010-004)

After Proteus, the kingdom passed to [[rhampsinitos|Rhampsinitos]], who left as his memorial the western gateway of the temple of [[hephaistos|Hephaistos]]. (011-007)

## Role in the Account

Proteus occupies a pivotal role in [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s digression on the Trojan War. When [[alexander-paris|Alexander (Paris)]] abducted Helen from Sparta and sailed homeward, contrary winds drove him to the coast of [[egypt|Egypt]]. He landed at the Canobic mouth of [[nile-river|the Nile]], near Taricheiai, where a temple of [[heracles|Heracles]] stood. (010-005) The attendants of Alexander, learning of a local custom granting sanctuary to slaves who took refuge at this temple, fled there and denounced their master, revealing the whole story of the abduction to the temple priests and to [[thonis|Thonis]], the warden of that river-mouth. (010-005)

[[thonis|Thonis]] sent a message to Proteus at [[memphis|Memphis]], reporting the stranger who had committed an impious deed in Hellas. Proteus ordered that Alexander be seized and brought before him. When Alexander attempted to dissemble about Helen, the suppliants convicted him of falsehood. (010-005) Proteus then delivered his judgment: though he refrained from executing Alexander out of respect for his own principle of not killing strangers driven to his land by winds, he confiscated both Helen and the stolen wealth, keeping them safe for [[menelaos|Menelaos]], and banished Alexander and his crew from [[egypt|Egypt]] within three days. (010-005)

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] also records that when the Hellenic army besieged [[troy|Troy]] and demanded Helen's return, the Trojans truthfully replied that Helen and the wealth were in [[egypt|Egypt]]. The [[hellenes|Hellenes]], believing themselves mocked, persisted until they took the city. Finding no Helen, they finally believed the story and dispatched [[menelaos|Menelaos]] to [[egypt|Egypt]]. (011-004) [[menelaos|Menelaos]] sailed up [[nile-river|the Nile]] to [[memphis|Memphis]], where he was received hospitably by Proteus, recovered Helen unharmed along with his wealth, and received great entertainment. (011-004)

## Herodotus's Portrayal

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] presents Proteus as a model of judicial prudence and moral rectitude. The king's decision to spare Alexander's life while retaining the stolen goods demonstrates a careful balancing of clemency with justice. Proteus articulates his own ruling principle explicitly: "not to slay any of those strangers who being driven from their course by winds have come to my land." (010-005)

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] further supposes that Homer was aware of this [[egypt|Egypt]]ian variant of the Helen story but chose a different version more suitable to epic poetry. He cites lines from the Iliad describing Alexander's wanderings past [[sidon|Sidon]], which he takes as evidence that Homer knew of the [[egypt|Egypt]]ian tradition. (010-006) Significantly, [[herodotus|Herodotus]] notes that [[menelaos|Menelaos]], despite being well treated by Proteus, later showed himself ungrateful to the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians: detained by contrary winds on his departure, he sacrificed two native children, an impious act that forced him to flee to Libya. (011-004)

> After him, they said, there succeeded to the throne a man of Memphis, whose name in the tongue of the Hellenes was Proteus; for whom there is now a sacred enclosure at Memphis, very fair and well ordered, lying on that side of the temple of Hephaistos which faces the North Wind. Round about the enclosure dwell Phenicians of Tyre, and this whole region is called the Camp of the Tyrians. Within the enclosure of Proteus there is a temple called the temple of the "foreign Aphrodite," which temple I conjecture to be one of Helen the daughter of Tyndareus.
> (010-004)

> At length Proteus declared to them this sentence, saying, "Were it not that I count it a matter of great moment not to slay any of those strangers who being driven from their course by winds have come to my land hitherto, I should have taken vengeance on thee on behalf of the man of Hellas, seeing that thou, most base of men, having received from him hospitality, didst work against him a most impious deed. ... This woman indeed and the wealth which thou hast I will not allow thee to carry away, but I shall keep them safe for the Hellene who was thy host, until he come himself and desire to carry them off to his home; to thyself however and thy fellow-voyagers I proclaim that ye depart from your anchoring within three days and go from my land to some other; and if not, that ye will be dealt with as enemies."
> (010-005)

> And when they had taken the wall and did not find Helen, but heard the same tale as before, then they believed the former tale and sent Menelaos himself to Proteus. And Menelaos having come to Egypt and having sailed up to Memphis, told the truth of these matters, and not only found great entertainment, but also received Helen unhurt, and all his own wealth besides.
> (011-004)
