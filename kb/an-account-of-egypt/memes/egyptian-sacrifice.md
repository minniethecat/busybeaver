## Overview

Egyptian sacrifice was a central religious practice in ancient Egypt, governed by detailed customs that [[Herodotus]] documented during his travels. The Egyptians maintained strict rules about which animals could be sacrificed, how they were tested for purity, and which animals were considered sacred or abominable, with practices varying significantly by region. The sacrificial system integrated religious devotion, social hierarchy, and economic exchange in ways that Herodotus found both elaborate and distinctively Egyptian.

## Description

### Sacrificial Testing of Oxen

The Egyptians regarded male oxen as belonging to [[Epaphos]], and only those passing a rigorous purity test could be sacrificed. A priest appointed for this purpose examined the beast both standing and lying on its back, checking for any black hair that would render it unclean. The priest also drew out the animal's tongue to verify additional appointed signs. Once approved, the animal was sealed with the priest's signet — sacrifice of an unsealed beast carried the death penalty. (004-006)

### Regional Variations

Sacrificial customs differed by region. Those worshipping at the temple of Theban Zeus or living in the Theban district sacrificed goats and abstained from sheep. In contrast, those belonging to the Mendesian district abstained from goats and sacrificed sheep. All Egyptians universally revered [[Isis]] and [[Osiris]], regardless of regional differences. (005-001)

### The Mendesian Prohibition

The Mendesians abstained from goat sacrifice because they counted Pan among the eight gods who preceded the twelve gods. Egyptian artists depicted Pan with a goat's face and legs, similar to Greek representations, though Egyptians did not believe he actually possessed this form. (005-004)

### The Pig

The pig was considered an abominable animal by all Egyptians. Anyone accidentally touching a pig would immediately immerse themselves in the [[Nile River]] with their garments. Swineherds, even native Egyptians, were forbidden from entering any temple, and marriages with swineherds were universally avoided. This created a permanent underclass defined by association with an impure animal. (005-005)

### The Sacrificial Procedure

The appointed manner of sacrifice followed a precise ritual sequence. The sealed beast was led to the altar where the sacrifice was to be performed and a fire kindled. The officiants poured libations of wine over the altar so that it ran down upon the victim and called upon the god. They then cut the animal's throat and severed the head from the body, after which they flayed the body of the beast. (004-006)

### The Head Ritual

Upon the severed head the Egyptians made many imprecations, praying that if any evil were about to befall either those offering sacrifice or the land of Egypt in general, it might come instead upon that head. After these imprecations, those who had a marketplace with Hellenes sojourning among them for trade carried the head to the market and sold it; those who had no Hellenes among them cast it away into the river. By reason of this custom, no Egyptian would eat the head of any animal whatsoever. This ritual functioned both as an apotropaic act — transferring potential evil onto a disposable object — and as a cultural boundary marker between Egyptians and Hellenes. (004-006)

### The Great Sacrifice to Isis

For the goddess whom the Egyptians regarded as the greatest of all — to whom they celebrated the greatest feast — the sacrificial ritual was especially elaborate. After flaying the bullock and making imprecation, the priests removed the whole of its lower entrails but left the upper entrails and fat in the body. They then severed the legs, the end of the loin, the shoulders, and the neck. The remaining body was filled with consecrated loaves, honey, raisins, figs, frankincense, myrrh, and every other kind of spice, after which they poured great abundance of oil over it. The Egyptians made their sacrifice after fasting, and while the offerings were being burnt, all participants beat themselves for mourning. When the beating was finished, they set forth as a feast that which was left unburnt of the sacrifice. (004-006)

### Sacred and Prohibited Animals

The clean males of the ox kind, both full-grown animals and calves, were sacrificed by all Egyptians. Female oxen, however, could not be sacrificed, as they were sacred to [[Isis]] — whose figure was represented in the form of a woman with cow's horns, just as the Hellenes depicted Io. All Egyptians without distinction revered cows far more than any other kind of cattle. So deep was this reverence that no Egyptian man or woman would kiss a Hellene on the mouth, nor use a knife, roasting-spit, or cauldron belonging to a Hellene, nor taste the flesh of even a clean animal if it had been cut with a Hellene's knife. (004-006)

### Burial of Sacrificial Cattle

Cattle that died were buried according to a specific custom: females were cast into the river, while males were buried in the suburbs of each town, with one horn — or sometimes both — protruding to mark the place. When the bodies had rotted and the appointed time arrived, a boat came from the island of [[Prosopitis]] in the Delta to each city. From the city of Atarbechis on Prosopitis — where a holy temple of Aphrodite stood — men went abroad to dig up the bones of the oxen, gathered them, and buried them together in a single place. This systematic, centralized reburial of cattle bones across all Egyptian cities reveals an organized religious infrastructure operating at a national scale. (004-006)

## Herodotus's Commentary

Herodotus presents Egyptian sacrificial customs as evidence of a religious sophistication exceeding that of any other people. He notes with particular interest the parallels between Egyptian and Hellenic conceptions — [[Epaphos]] as the Egyptian counterpart to the Greek Epaphos, [[Isis]] represented with cow's horns like the Hellenic Io — while also emphasizing practices that had no Greek equivalent, such as the head ritual and the absolute prohibition on consuming the head of any animal. His account of the sacrificial testing of oxen, complete with the death penalty for sacrificing an unsealed beast, conveys the severity with which the Egyptians enforced ritual purity. The detail that no Egyptian would use a Hellene's knife even on a clean animal underscores Herodotus's sensitivity to the cultural boundaries that sacrifice reinforced between Egyptians and foreigners.

## Significance

Egyptian sacrificial customs reveal how religion structured daily life and social hierarchy in ancient Egypt. The detailed purity regulations, regional diversity, and social consequences of animal associations — from the absolute exclusion of swineherds from temples and marriage to the centralized reburial of cattle bones — demonstrate that sacrifice functioned as a comprehensive system governing Egyptian religious identity, rather than merely a ritual act. The sacrificial procedure itself, with its sequence of libation, slaughter, imprecation, and feasting, exemplifies the integration of the sacred and the communal that Herodotus found characteristic of Egyptian civilisation.
