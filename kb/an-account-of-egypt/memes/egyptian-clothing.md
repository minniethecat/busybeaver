## Overview

Herodotus provides one of the earliest detailed accounts of Egyptian clothing and dress customs. His observations cover the materials used (linen and wool), the number of garments worn by men and women, the special attire of priests, the ritual prohibitions surrounding wool in sacred contexts, and the dramatic changes in dress that accompanied mourning. Clothing, for Herodotus, was both a marker of Egyptian difference from other peoples and a window into Egyptian religious practices. (004-005) (007-005)

## Daily Garments

The basic Egyptian garment, as Herodotus describes it, was a linen tunic worn about the legs with fringes, which the Egyptians called *calasiris*. Over this tunic, Egyptians threw garments of white wool. In a characteristic inversion of Greek norms, Herodotus notes that Egyptian men wore two garments each while women wore but one — the opposite of what a Greek observer might expect. (007-005) (004-004)

Cleanliness of clothing was a matter of special importance to the Egyptians. Herodotus emphasizes that they "wear garments of linen always newly washed, and this they make a special point of practice." This fastidiousness about clean linen was part of a broader Egyptian concern with purity and cleanliness, which also manifested in their daily washing of bronze drinking cups and the practice of circumcision for the sake of cleanliness rather than appearance. (004-005)

## Health and Cleanliness

The Egyptian obsession with clean garments was inseparable from their broader health practices. Herodotus considered the Egyptians the healthiest people in the world after the Libyans, attributing this partly to the unchanging seasons and partly to their rigorous regimen. For three successive days each month, Egyptians purged themselves with emetics and clysters, believing that all diseases originated in the food they consumed. The daily washing of linen garments, the twice-daily washing of bronze drinking cups, and the practice of circumcision — all pursued "for the sake of cleanliness, considering it better to be clean than comely" — formed a unified cultural pattern of bodily purity that extended from what Egyptians ate, to what they drank from, to what they wore. (007-005) (004-005)

## Priestly Attire

The priests of Egypt observed stricter clothing rules than the general population. They wore garments exclusively of linen, and their linen sandals were made from the byblus plant (papyrus). Other kinds of clothing materials and footwear were not permitted to them. The priests also washed themselves in cold water twice each day and twice each night, maintaining a regimen of cleanliness that complemented their pure linen attire.

Herodotus notes that the priests shaved their entire bodies every other day — head, eyebrows, and all body hair — ensuring that no lice or any other impurity could come upon them while they ministered to the gods. This complete depilation, combined with their linen-only dress code and their frequent cold-water washings, constituted a regime of ritual purity that set the priests apart from ordinary Egyptians and from all other peoples Herodotus knew. (004-005)

## Mourning Dress

The most dramatic transformation of Egyptian clothing occurred in the context of death and mourning. When a man of any regard died, the women of the household plastered mud over their heads and faces, bound their garments up with a girdle, and exposed their breasts as they went about the city beating themselves. The men, too, bound up their garments with a girdle and beat themselves. The normal state of dress was thus reversed: garments were not removed but constricted and disordered, and the body — especially the female body — was partially exposed in ways that would have been improper in ordinary circumstances. (008-002)

In another inversion of Greek custom, Herodotus observes that while other peoples cut their hair short as a sign of mourning, the Egyptians did the opposite — letting their hair grow long, both on the head and chin, having previously been close-shaven. This practice extended even to the priests, who in ordinary life shaved their heads entirely. (004-004)

## Wool and Religious Prohibitions

The prohibition against wool in sacred contexts was one of the most distinctive features of Egyptian clothing culture. Herodotus explicitly states that "woolen garments are not taken into the temples, nor are they buried with them, for this is not permitted by religion." He connects this rule to the Orphic and Bacchic mysteries and the Pythagorean school, which imposed the same restriction, and implies an Egyptian origin for these Hellenic religious observances. This suggests that for Egyptians, linen — a plant-derived fabric — was considered ritually pure, while wool, being of animal origin, carried a form of impurity that disqualified it from sacred spaces and burial. (007-005)

## Significance

Herodotus's account of Egyptian clothing reveals a culture in which dress was deeply intertwined with religious purity, gender distinction, and the ritual marking of life transitions. The preference for linen over wool in sacred contexts points to a conceptual division between the pure (vegetal) and the impure (animal). The meticulously clean linen garments, the priests' complete bodily depilation and cold-water washings, and the monthly purging regimen all formed part of a comprehensive Egyptian philosophy of bodily purity — one that Herodotus found remarkable enough to record in detail. And the dramatic alteration of clothing in mourning — mud-smeared faces, bound garments, exposed breasts — shows how powerfully dress could be mobilized to express grief and social rupture. For the Greek historian, these customs were yet another illustration of how the Egyptians had "established for themselves manners and customs in a way opposite to other men in almost all matters." (004-004)
