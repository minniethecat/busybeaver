## Overview

Mykerinos ([[egypt|Egypt]]ian: Menkaure) was a pharaoh of the Fourth Dynasty, the son of [[cheops|Cheops]]. In [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s account he stands in stark contrast to his father: where [[cheops|Cheops]] was a tyrant who closed the temples, Mykerinos reopened them and ruled with justice. He is remembered for building the third and smallest of the great pyramids at Giza (013-001).

## Background / Biography

Mykerinos was the son of [[cheops|Cheops]] and inherited the throne after the death of his uncle [[chephren|Chephren]] (012-004). Unlike his father and uncle, who together inflicted one hundred and six years of oppression upon [[egypt|Egypt]], Mykerinos reversed their policies: he reopened the temples, allowed the people to return to their work and sacrifices, and gained a reputation as the most just king [[egypt|Egypt]] had ever known (012-004). [[herodotus|Herodotus]] reports that Mykerinos was so committed to justice that if any man complained of a court decision, the king would compensate him from his own wealth (012-004). Despite his righteous rule, Mykerinos suffered severe personal tragedies. His only daughter died, and he received an oracle from [[buto|Buto]] prophesying that he had only six years left to live (012-005).

## Role in the Account

Mykerinos serves as a counter-example to [[cheops|Cheops]] in [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s narrative, demonstrating that inherited power can be used for good as well as evil. His story is rich with memorable episodes: the elaborate burial of his daughter inside a gilded wooden cow kept at [[sais|Sais]], visible even in [[herodotus|Herodotus]]'s own day (012-004); the oracle's pronouncement that [[egypt|Egypt]] was destined to suffer for one hundred and fifty years, and that Mykerinos's early death was punishment for failing to perceive this destiny (012-005); and his defiant response of turning nights into days by lamp-light, hoping to double his remaining years and prove the oracle false (012-005). Mykerinos also built a pyramid, though much smaller than his father's, constructed of [[ethiopia-region|Ethiopia]]n stone up to half its height (013-001).

## Herodotus's Portrayal

[[herodotus|Herodotus]] presents Mykerinos as the most commendable of the pyramid-building kings, explicitly stating that the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians praised him above all earlier rulers for his justice (012-004). Yet even this virtuous king cannot escape fate. The narrator treats the oracle story with apparent credulity, and uses Mykerinos's tragedy to illustrate the inescapable workings of divine providence. [[herodotus|Herodotus]] also corrects a Greek misconception: he dismisses the tale that Mykerinos's pyramid was built by the courtesan Rhodopis, noting that she lived much later, in the reign of [[amasis|Amasis]] (013-001).

## Sources

> After him, they said, Mykerinos became king over Egypt, who was the son of Cheops; and to him his father's deeds were displeasing, and he both opened the temples and gave liberty to the people, who were ground down to the last extremity of evil, to return to their own business and to their sacrifices: also he gave decisions of their causes juster than those of all the other kings besides.
> (012-004)

> An oracle came to him from the city of Buto, saying that he was destined to live but six years more, in the seventh year to end his life: and he being indignant at it sent to the Oracle a reproach against the god, making complaint in reply that whereas his father and uncle, who had shut up the temples and had not only not remembered the gods, but also had been destroyers of men, had lived for a long time, he himself, who practised piety, was destined to end his life so soon.
> (012-005)

> This pyramid some of the Hellenes say was built by the courtesan Rhodopis, not therein speaking rightly: and besides this it is evident to me that they who speak thus do not even know who Rhodopis was, for otherwise they would not have attributed to her the building of a pyramid like this.
> (013-001)
