## Overview

Libya, in Herodotus's geographical scheme, is one of the three great divisions of the known world, alongside Asia and Europe. It designates the landmass west of Egypt — roughly corresponding to modern North Africa — and is described by the historian as a region of deserts, mountains, and distinct peoples. In his account of Egypt, Libya serves as the western boundary of the Nile valley, with its mountain range forming the natural barrier opposite the Arabian mountains to the east. (001-009)(002-001)

## Location / Geography

Herodotus describes Egypt as flanked by two mountain ranges: on the east, the Arabian mountains extending toward the Erythraian Sea, and on the west, the Libyan range, described as rocky and enveloped in sand — the range in which the pyramids were built. (001-009) This Libyan mountain range forms the natural western boundary of the Nile valley, mirroring the Arabian range to the east.

The river Nile, in Herodotus's theory of its course, flows from the west after passing Meroe, traversing Libya before turning northward into Egypt. He compares the Nile's bend to the course of the Ister (Danube), arguing that the river makes a great western sweep through the Libyan interior before reaching Egypt. (003-001)(003-008) Herodotus also proposes that the Sun's passage through the upper regions of Libya is the cause of the Nile's summer flood — the heat drawing moisture from the river — though he himself considers this theory uncertain. (003-004)

South of Egypt, the Nile valley narrows dramatically between the converging Libyan and Arabian mountains. At this narrow passage, Hercules (if indeed the river had been known to him) could not proceed further upriver, so confined was the channel. (003-008)

## Historical Significance

Libya figures prominently in Herodotus's discussion of Egypt's territorial extent and his refutation of Ionian geography. The Ionians held that only the Delta was properly Egypt, assigning the rest of Egyptian territory partly to Libya and partly to Arabia. (002-002) Herodotus vigorously rejects this view, arguing that if the Ionians were correct, then in former times — before the alluvial Delta had even formed — Egypt would have had no territory at all. (002-002)(002-003)

Instead, Herodotus proposes his own definition: Egypt is all the land inhabited by Egyptians, just as Cilicia is the land of Cilicians. The boundary between Asia and Libya, properly speaking, is nothing other than the borders of Egypt. (002-004)

Libya is also the home of the oracle of Ammon (Zeus-Ammon) at the Siwa Oasis, one of the two oracles that the Egyptians claim as the origin of all oracular divination — both those among the Hellenes and that in Libya itself. (006-001)
