## Overview

Music and dance in ancient Egypt, as described by Herodotus, were integral to religious festivals, public celebrations, and communal life. The historian records several instruments — the flute, the harp, and castanets — along with singing and rhythmic hand-clapping. Notably, he observes that certain musical customs were introduced by the Egyptians and later adopted by the Greeks, positioning Egypt as a source of Hellenic musical and ceremonial practice.

## Description

The most vivid musical scene in Herodotus's account occurs during the great festival of Artemis at Bubastis. Men and women traveled together in crowded boats down the Nile toward the city, the journey itself becoming a floating celebration. "Some of the women have castanets and make a noise with them, and some of the men play flutes during the whole journey, while the rest, both women and men, sing and clap their hands" (006-001). Whenever they passed a riverside city, they brought the boat close to the bank to continue their performance, the women shouting playfully at the women of the town.

Herodotus identifies the Egyptians as the originators of several practices later adopted by the Greeks, including solemn assemblies, religious processions, and the musical accompaniments that characterized them. He explicitly states that "the Egyptians were the first of men who made solemn assemblies and processions and approaches to the temples, and from them the Hellenes have learnt them" (006-001).

A distinctive Egyptian song was the Linos lament, a melody that Herodotus compares to the Greek Linos-song. He expresses surprise at its antiquity and widespread familiarity: "the name of Linos... the Egyptians have had from the beginning and call him Maneros" (006-002). The Egyptians, he notes, sang this particular song from the most ancient times, and it was associated with funeral rites — forming part of the broader Egyptian tradition of musical lamentation that Herodotus observed in several contexts, including the ritual mourning for the sacred ram at Thebes.

## Significance

Herodotus's account of Egyptian music is notable for its cross-cultural framing. He presents Egypt as the older, originating culture — the source from which the Greeks derived not only religious ritual but also the musical forms that accompanied it. The Linos/Maneros identification is a rare instance of Herodotus tracing a specific song across cultures, and his description of the Bubastis river-procession provides one of the earliest surviving word-pictures of mass musical celebration in the ancient world.


## The Dionysos Festival Procession

Beyond the great assembly at Bubastis, Herodotus also described a distinctive musical practice associated with the festival of [[Dionysos]]. On the eve of this festival, women carried articulated figures through the villages — puppets about a cubit in height, operated by strings, with a prominently moving phallus. A flute-player led the procession while the women followed, singing hymns in praise of the god. Herodotus noted that, apart from the absence of choral dances and the substitution of these string-operated figures for the Greek phallos, the Egyptian celebration of Dionysos closely resembled its Hellenic counterpart. (005-005)

## The Song of Linos — Egypt's Only Song

Among the customs that Herodotus found most remarkable was the Egyptian song of Linos, which he identified as being sung under the name [[Maneros]] in the Egyptian tongue. The historian expressed genuine wonder at the discovery that the same lament was sung in [[Phoenicia]], [[Cyprus]], and elsewhere, demonstrating a shared musical tradition across the eastern Mediterranean. The Egyptians told Herodotus that Maneros was the only son of the first king of Egypt, who died prematurely, and that the lament composed for him was their first and only song — a claim that the historian presents without challenge. The antiquity of this melody was beyond question: Herodotus asserted that the Egyptians had sung it "from immemorial time." (007-005)

The singularity of this claim — that a culture as musically rich as Egypt possessed only one song — suggests that Herodotus may have been recording a priestly tradition rather than a factual inventory of Egyptian musical practice. Given his own descriptions of flutes, castanets, hand-clapping, and choral singing at Bubastis and the Dionysia, the "only song" statement likely reflects a specific ritual or mythological claim about Maneros rather than a literal limitation of Egyptian musical repertoire.
