## Overview

The City of Crocodiles (later known as Crocodilopolis, modern Medinet el-Faiyum) was a prominent Egyptian city situated a little above the lake of Moeris. It was the principal cult center of the crocodile god Sobek, where a single sacred crocodile was kept tame and adorned with jewelry. Herodotus locates the great Egyptian Labyrinth as nearly opposite this city. (014-001) (008-001)

## Location and the Labyrinth

Herodotus describes the City of Crocodiles as lying near Lake Moeris, with the famous Egyptian Labyrinth situated a little above the lake and nearly opposite the city. The Labyrinth, which Herodotus visited personally, was a monumental complex of twelve covered courts with three thousand chambers — half above ground and half below — that he declared surpassed even the pyramids in its grandeur. (014-001)

The Labyrinth's underground chambers, according to the Egyptian priests, contained the sepulchres of the kings who built the complex as well as the tombs of the sacred crocodiles. The Egyptians in charge of the site were unwilling to show these subterranean chambers to Herodotus, so he could only report on them from hearsay. (014-001)

## Crocodile Cult

The City of Crocodiles was at the center of Egyptian crocodile veneration. Herodotus reports that the Egyptians were divided in their treatment of crocodiles: those dwelling about Thebes and the lake of Moeris held them to be most sacred, while those at Elephantine even ate them. At the City of Crocodiles and Thebes, each community kept one specially selected crocodile, trained to tameness, adorned with ornaments of molten stone and gold in its ears and anklets around its front feet. These sacred animals received appointed food and sacrificial offerings during their lives, and after death were embalmed and buried in sacred tombs. (008-001)

Herodotus provides a detailed natural history of the crocodile, describing its growth from an egg no larger than a goose's to a length of seventeen cubits or more, its peculiar jaw mechanism (the lower jaw is fixed while the upper moves), its sharp sight in air but blindness in water, and its symbiotic relationship with the trochilus bird, which enters the crocodile's mouth to feed on leeches. (008-001)
