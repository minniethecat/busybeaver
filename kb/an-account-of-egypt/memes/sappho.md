Sappho was the greatest lyric poet of archaic Greece, a native of Mytilene on the island of Lesbos. She appears in [[Herodotus]]'s Egyptian account not for her poetry directly but through her family connection to the story of [[Rhodopis]], the Thracian courtesan who lived in Egypt and was falsely credited with building one of the pyramids.

## Sappho and Rhodopis

The connection between Sappho and Egypt runs through Sappho's brother Charaxos. According to Herodotus, Charaxos travelled to Egypt and there fell in love with [[Rhodopis]], a courtesan of Thracian origin who had been brought to Egypt as a slave. Charaxos redeemed Rhodopis from slavery for a great sum of money and then returned to Mytilene. (013-002) Upon his return, Sappho violently rebuked him in one of her odes — publicly shaming him for his romantic and financial folly.

Herodotus records this detail as a supporting argument in his refutation of the popular Greek tradition that Rhodopis had built the third pyramid at Giza (that of [[Mycerinus]]). The fact that Sappho — a contemporary witness — treated Charaxos's expenditure on Rhodopis as a scandalous extravagance rather than a royal fortune helps establish that Rhodopis's wealth, though considerable, "was not enough to suffice for the cost of such a pyramid as this." (013-002)

## Sappho as Historical Witness

Sappho's appearance in Herodotus's Egyptian account illustrates his method of using contemporary literary sources as historical evidence. By citing Sappho's ode against Charaxos, Herodotus anchors his correction of the Rhodopis tradition in a verifiable reference point — the poetry of a known author whose work was familiar to his Greek audience. The fact that Sappho, writing in the early sixth century BCE, treated Rhodopis as a contemporary of moderate means rather than a pyramid-building queen provides chronological and economic evidence against the popular attribution.

## Significance

Sappho's brief but telling appearance in the Egyptian account demonstrates the interconnectedness of the archaic Mediterranean world. A lyric poet on Lesbos rebuking her merchant brother for his entanglement with a courtesan in [[Naucratis]] — this is the kind of concrete, personal connection across the sea that Herodotus uses to build his larger historical arguments about the relationships between Egypt and the Greek world.
