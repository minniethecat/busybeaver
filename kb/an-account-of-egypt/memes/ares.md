## Overview

Ares, the Greek god of war, appears in Herodotus's "Account of Egypt" primarily through his identification with an Egyptian deity worshipped at the city of Papremis in the Nile Delta. Herodotus describes a distinctive and violent festival held in Ares's honour at Papremis, involving ritualized combat between opposing groups of worshippers — a practice unique among Egyptian religious festivals. (006-003)

## Ares in the Egyptian Pantheon

In Herodotus's scheme of divine equivalences, Ares was the Greek name applied to an Egyptian war god worshipped at Papremis. The Egyptians held a solemn assembly (panegyris) at Papremis in Ares's honour, ranking it sixth among the major festivals Herodotus catalogues, after those for Artemis (at Bubastis), Isis (at Busiris), Athene (at Sais), Helios (at Heliopolis), and Leto (at Buto). (006-002) This placement within the hierarchy of religious assemblies indicates the significance of the martial cult in Egyptian religious life.

## The Festival at Papremis

The festival of Ares at Papremis featured a remarkable ritual that Herodotus describes in vivid detail. On the festival day, as the sun was setting, a small group of priests attended to the statue of Ares while the majority of worshippers stationed themselves at the temple entrance, armed with wooden clubs. A contingent of men — more than a thousand, according to Herodotus — then attempted to force their way into the temple, while the defenders resisted. (006-003) The resulting melee involved genuine violence; Herodotus reports that although the Egyptians claimed no one died, he personally found it hard to believe that no fatal injuries occurred.

## The Foundation Myth

The festival's origin story, as recounted by the people of Papremis, involves Ares's mother, who was said to dwell in the temple. Ares, having been raised apart from her, returned as a grown man desiring to visit his mother. The temple attendants, not recognizing him, barred his entry. In response, Ares brought armed men from another city, forced his way past the attendants, and entered the temple to see his mother. (006-003) The ritual combat of the festival thus reenacts Ares's forcible entry — a living commemoration of the god's filial devotion expressed through martial action.

## Oracles of Ares

Herodotus lists Ares among the deities to whom oracular shrines were dedicated in Egypt. The Oracle of Ares was one of seven oracles in the land, alongside those of Heracles, Apollo, Athene, Artemis, Zeus, and Leto (the most honoured, at Buto). (008-001) The existence of an oracle of Ares indicates that the god's cult at Papremis encompassed not only festival worship but also prophetic consultation — integrating the war god into the broader Egyptian oracular system.

## Significance

The worship of Ares at Papremis illustrates several patterns central to Herodotus's ethnographic project. First, it demonstrates his method of interpretatio graeca — identifying Egyptian deities by Greek names — which allowed his Greek audience to comprehend foreign religious practices. Second, the violent festival ritual provides one of the most dramatic examples of Egyptian religious custom in the "Account," showcasing Herodotus's eye for the extraordinary and his willingness to record practices that might strain credulity. Finally, the myth of Ares and his mother echoes broader Egyptian religious themes of separation, recognition, and ritual commemoration.
