## Herodotus's Account

Herodotus identifies Demeter with the Egyptian goddess Isis at multiple points in his account, and provides significant detail about Egyptian religious observances that he associates with Demeter.

### Identification with Isis

Herodotus states plainly: "Isis is in the tongue of the Hellenes Demeter" [002-083]. This identification underlies his descriptions of several Egyptian religious festivals and practices.

### The Festival of Isis at Busiris

At the city of Busiris, Herodotus describes a great festival in honor of Isis: "they beat themselves in mourning after the sacrifice, all of them both men and women, very many myriads of people" [002-083]. He notes that the Carians dwelling in Egypt participate even more intensely, cutting their foreheads with knives.

### The Mystery of Rhampsinitos

Herodotus recounts a remarkable story about King Rhampsinitos, who "went down alive to that place which by the Hellenes is called Hades, and there played at dice with Demeter" [002-167]. The king returned with a gift of a golden handkerchief from the goddess. The Egyptians, Herodotus reports, still celebrate a feast commemorating this event. During the feast, priests weave a robe in a single day, blindfold one of their number, and two wolves guide him to the temple of Demeter and back.

### Demeter as Ruler of the Underworld

According to Egyptian belief as reported by Herodotus, "Demeter and Dionysos are rulers of the world below" [002-167]. This is connected with the Egyptian doctrine of the immortality of the soul and its transmigration through creatures of land, sea, and air over a period of three thousand years.

### The Mysteries

Herodotus is notably restrained when discussing the mysteries of Demeter — the rites that the Hellenes call _thesmophoria_. He states that the daughters of Danaos brought this rite from Egypt and taught it to the women of the Pelasgians [002-245]. When the Dorians later drove out the Peloponnesians, the rite was lost everywhere except among the Arcadians who remained.
