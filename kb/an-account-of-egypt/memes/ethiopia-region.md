## Overview

Ethiopia (corresponding to the ancient kingdom of Kush, in modern Sudan) was the great southern neighbour and occasional conqueror of Egypt. In Herodotus's account, Ethiopia is both the source of the Nile's mysterious flood and the homeland of kings who twice ruled Egypt. The relationship between Egypt and Ethiopia is a recurring theme — from trade and cultural exchange to military conquest and the final expulsion of Ethiopian rule. (013-004) (013-006) (014-008)

## Location / Geography

Herodotus places Ethiopia south of Elephantine, stretching along the upper reaches of the Nile. He reports that the country extended as far as the Erythraian Sea on the east and to unknown regions on the south and west. The journey from Elephantine to the Ethiopian capital at Meroe took fifty-six days by his calculation.

## Historical Significance

Ethiopia supplied Egypt with gold, ivory, ebony, and exotic animals. More significantly, the Ethiopian king Sabacos conquered Egypt and ruled it for fifty years before a divine dream compelled his departure. (013-004) (013-006) The memory of Sabacos's rule persisted through the reign of Psammetichos — it was Sabacos who had killed Psammetichos's father Necos. (014-008)
