## Definition

In Say's *Treatise on [[political-economy|Political Economy]]*, **emigration** encompasses both the movement of persons and the transfer of capital and industry from one nation to another. Chapter XX is devoted to "temporary and permanent emigration, considered in reference to national wealth" (P03-001). Say treats emigration not as a demographic phenomenon alone but as an economic e[[demand|vent]] with complex effects on the wealth of both the sending and receiving nations.

## Explanation

### Capital Emigration

Say emphasizes that capital, like people, can emigrate: "Would you put a stop to the emigration of capital? It is not to be pre[[demand|vent]]ed" (017-025). [[capital|Capital]] flees insecurity, excessive [[tax|taxation]], and unfavourable regulatory environments. Unlike persons, capital moves silently and rapidly — a lesson that [[public-authority|government]]s repeatedly fail to learn when they attempt to control its movement through prohibitions.

### Causes of Emigration

The primary driver of emigration is the search for better economic conditions. Say notes "their strong tendency to emigration under such circumstances" (017-030) when productivity is constrained or property rights are insecure at home. High [[tax|taxes]], burdensome regulations, and political instability all contribute to what Say describes as "this emigration of industry, capital, and local advantages" (020-005). The emigrant — whether labourer, capitalist, or ad[[demand|vent]]urer — seeks a more favourable environment for productive activity.

### Temporary vs. Permanent Emigration

Say's distinction between temporary and permanent emigration is analytically significant. Temporary emigration may involve seasonal labour movements or short-term capital placements; permanent emigration entails the complete transfer of person and productive resources to a new national setting. The economic effects differ substantially: temporary emigration may bring back skills and capital, while permanent emigration represents a definitive loss to the sending nation.

### Effects on National Wealth

Say argues that the economic impact of emigration depends on circumstances. When surplus population emigrates from a country with limited opportunities, both the emigrants and those who remain may benefit. But "the emigration of industry, capital, and local advantages" (020-005) from a nation already struggling with productive decline compounds the loss. Say's analysis avoids both the mercantilist fear of population loss and the physiocratic belief that emigration is invariably harmful.

## Historical Context

Say wrote during an era of significant European emigration. The [[napoleon|Napoleon]]ic Wars had displaced populations across the continent, while the Americas offered new opportunities for settlement and investment. Say's own experience of political upheaval — he served under the Revolutionary [[public-authority|government]], the Consulate, and the Empire — gave him firsthand knowledge of how political conditions drive the movement of people and capital across borders.

## Say's Treatment

Say integrates emigration into his broader frame[[labour|work]] of production and wealth. Emigration is not a demographic curiosity but a market phenomenon: people and capital move toward conditions that reward productive activity. His analysis anticipates the concerns of later economists about capital flight, brain drain, and the economics of migration. By treating emigration as a rational economic response to differential opportunities, Say places it squarely within the domain of political economy rather than treating it as a matter of mere population statistics.
