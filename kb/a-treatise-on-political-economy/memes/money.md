# Money

## Definition

Money is a commodity that serves as a universal medium of [[commerce|exchange]] and a common measure of value. In Say's analysis, money is not a mere "sign" of value but possesses intrinsic value of its own: "the value of money is a real value, like that of any other commodity." (021-001)

Money performs its function only because it is itself a valuable good. A coin is not a token representing value — it is a piece of metal whose material value grounds its acceptability in [[commerce|exchange]].

## Explanation

### Functions of Money

1. **Medium of [[exchange|Exchange]]**: Money facilitates transactions by eliminating the need for barter. Rather than exchanging [[commodity|goods]] directly, parties [[commerce|exchange]] [[commodity|goods]] for money, then money for other [[commodity|goods]].
2. **Measure of [[value|Value]]**: Money provides a common denominator in which the values of all other commodities can be expressed. The price of a good is the quantity of money for which it [[commerce|exchange]]s.

### Qualities of Good Money

Say identifies desirable qualities for monetary material: durability, divisibility, uniformity, portability, and stability of value. Precious metals — [[precious-metals|gold and silver]] — possess these qualities to the highest degree, which is why they have universally been adopted as money. (021-002)

### Money Is Not Wealth

Say emphasizes a critical distinction: money is only one form of wealth, not wealth itself. A nation is not enriched merely by accumulating [[coin|specie]]. True national wealth consists in the abundance of producible [[commodity|goods]], not in the quantity of the circulating medium. This insight forms part of Say's critique of mercantilism.

## Say's Treatment

Say devotes an extensive chapter to the nature and uses of money (Chapter XXI), reflecting the importance of monetary questions in early 19th-century political economy. He argues against the mercantilist identification of money with wealth and against the notion that money is merely a "sign" — a con[[demand|vent]]ion without intrinsic value.

Say's translator, C.R. Prinsep, offers substantial critical notes on this chapter, observing that Say's "imperfect notion...of the origin and ingredients of value" leads to some "resulting errors in the consideration of the properties of money." (021-001)

Say observes that money derives its special status from custom, not authority:

> Custom, therefore, and not the mandate of authority, designates the specific product that shall pass exclusively as money, whether crown pieces or any other commodity whatever. The more frequent recurrence of the exchange of every individual product for the commodity, money, than for any other product, has attached particular names to this transaction ; thus, to receive money in exchange is called, selling, and to give it, buying.
>
> (021-008)
