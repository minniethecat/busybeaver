# Export Bounty

## Definition

An export bounty is a [[public-authority|government]] payment made to domestic producers or exporters for each unit of a commodity shipped abroad. It is the mirror image of an import prohibition: whereas prohibition excludes foreign [[commodity|goods]], bounty subsidizes domestic [[commodity|goods]] in foreign markets.

> …receives a bounty upon export… (017-058)

Say treats bounties as one of the principal instruments of mercantilist [[commerce|trade]] policy — a direct transfer from the public treasury to a favoured industry, justified by the mercantilist obsession with a favourable balance of [[commerce|trade]].

## Explanation

### The Mechanism of Bounties

When a [[public-authority|government]] pays a bounty on export, the exporter can sell the commodity abroad at a price below its cost of production, with the difference made up by the public treasury:

> …when a bounty is paid, not at the moment of export, but upon the production of the article… (017-059)

The foreign consumer benefits from an artificially low price; the domestic taxpayer bears the cost. The domestic producer receives the world price plus the bounty, creating an incentive to expand production beyond what the unsubsidized market would support.

### Bounties and the Public Treasury

Say analyses the arithmetic of bounties with characteristic precision:

> …in other words, if it offer a bounty of 3 fr. to the producer, the commodity which has cost 100 fr. in production is sold to the foreigner for 97 fr. The nation thus loses 3 fr. on every such transaction… (017-060)

The nation loses twice: once in the treasury payment, and again in the misallocation of capital toward an artificially profitable line of production.

### Case Study: The French Shipping Bounty

Say cites a concrete example that his French readers would recognize:

> …the marine of France, granted a bounty of 5 fr. per ton upon every ship… (017-061)

The bounty enriched shipbuilders at public expense while failing to create a genuinely competitive merchant marine. The policy's only lasting effect was a permanent claim on the treasury.

### Bounties as Agricultural Policy

Say extends his critique to agricultural bounties, citing the British bounty on wheat export:

> …The bounty on the export of wheat has, by raising the home price of that article… (017-064)

The British corn bounty raised the domestic price of grain — harming consumers — while subsidizing foreign consumers. The policy transferred wealth from British [[labouring-class|labourers]] (who paid more for bread) to foreign millers and bakers.

### The True Bounty

Say distinguishes the artificial bounty of [[public-authority|government]] from what he calls the "true bounty" of high market prices:

> …The most effectual bounty is the high price of the article… (017-104)

When a commodity commands a high price in the market, producers expand output naturally — without the distortions, treasury costs, and international frictions that accompany [[public-authority|government]] bounties. The market mechanism achieves what bounties promise but cannot deliver.

## Historical Context

Export bounties were a standard instrument of mercantilist policy throughout the se[[demand|vent]]eenth and eighteenth centuries. [[jean-baptiste-colbert|Colbert]]'s France and Walpole's Britain both made extensive use of bounties to promote favoured industries — textiles, shipping, grain, and naval stores.

[[adam-smith|Adam Smith]] devoted a lengthy chapter of *[[wealth|Wealth]] of Nations* (Book IV, Chapter V) to bounties, arguing that they forced [[commerce|trade]] into channels less advantageous than those it would naturally follow. Say adopted [[adam-smith|Smith]]'s critique but gave it a distinctive French inflection, drawing on French examples and addressing French policy debates.

The bounty system would persist well into the nineteenth century. It was not until the free-[[commerce|trade]] movement of the 1840s–1860s that bounties were substantially reduced in both Britain and France.

## Say's Treatment

Say's treatment of bounties occurs primarily in Chapter XVII, alongside his critiques of prohibition and monopoly. His analysis has several distinctive features:

**The treasury arithmetic**: Say insists on counting the full cost — the bounty payment plus the deadweight loss from capital misallocation. He treats the treasury as part of the national economy, not an external source of funds:

> …d upon in the light of a national bounty, of the amount of the whole excess of price paid by foreign consumers… (017-111)

**The natural bounty of the soil**: Say contrasts artificial bounties with nature's own:

> …as in us lies, to limit the bounty of nature, who pours forth in profusion… (017-007)

Government bounties, in his view, are feeble imitations of the abundance that free markets and natural productivity already provide.

**The privilege connection**: Say links bounties to the broader critique of privilege. A bounty is a privilege to receive public money — no different in principle from a monopoly or a sinecure, and equally destructive of the public interest.
