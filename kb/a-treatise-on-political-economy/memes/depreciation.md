## Definition

In Say's *Treatise on [[political-economy|Political Economy]]*, **depreciation** refers to the decline in the [[commerce|exchange]]able value of a currency — particularly paper money — relative to commodities and [[coin|specie]]. Say witnessed the catastrophic depreciation of the French *assignats* during the Revolution and devoted considerable analysis to its causes and consequences: "the progressive depreciation of the French assignats" (016-004) serves as his paradigm case of monetary disorder.

## Explanation

### Causes of Depreciation

Say identifies the primary cause of depreciation as excessive issuance of paper currency beyond the needs of circulation. When "the reflux of depreciation and consequent fall of money" (021-020) occurs, the value of each unit of currency declines, requiring more currency to purchase the same [[commodity|goods]]. This creates a self-reinforcing spiral: depreciation leads to further issuance, which leads to further depreciation.

### Effects on Productive Classes

Say is particularly concerned with how depreciation affects different economic actors. "The productive classes" bear a disproportionate burden during depreciation because their [[revenue|income]]s and contracts are denominated in the depreciating currency, while the prices of [[commodity|goods]] they must purchase rise (017-034). The speculative classes may profit from correctly anticipating depreciation trends, creating a perverse incentive structure that rewards speculation over production.

### Depreciation and Foreign Trade

Depreciation has complex effects on international [[commercial-industry|commerce]]. Say notes that "the temporary depreciation of the [[bank-note|paper-money]] of England" (021-050) during the Restriction Period altered [[commerce|trade]] patterns, making British exports cheaper abroad while raising the cost of imports — a phenomenon later theorized as the price-[[coin|specie]]-flow mechanism.

### Prevention and Remedy

Say argues that the surest guard against depreciation is convertibility: if paper currency is redeemable in [[coin|specie]] on demand, its value cannot fall far below that of the metal it represents. "The legal coin will suffer no depreciation" (021-098) when it is properly backed. The alternative — relying on [[public-authority|government]] decree to sustain value — is doomed to fail, as "the depreciation of the national money is a fraud" (021-051) upon those who hold it.

## Historical Context

Say's analysis of depreciation was shaped by the two great monetary catastrophes of his lifetime: the collapse of John Law's *Mississippi Company* scheme (1720) — which Say discusses as a cautionary tale — and the Revolutionary *assignat* inflation (1789–1796), which Say experienced firsthand. The depreciation of the *assignats* from their initial value to near-worthlessness demonstrated the dangers of unbacked paper currency and powerfully influenced Say's monetary thought.

## Say's Treatment

Say integrates his analysis of depreciation into his broader theory of value. Depreciation is not a mysterious affliction but a straightforward consequence of the law of supply and demand applied to the circulating medium. When the quantity of money increases faster than the demand for monetary services, each unit necessarily commands less value in [[commerce|exchange]]. This clear-headed, value-theoretic approach distinguishes Say from contemporaries who attributed depreciation to vague factors like "loss of confidence" without analyzing the underlying mechanics.
