# Government Regulations

## Definition

Government regulations are [[public-authority|state]] actions "avowedly aimed at the exertion of influence upon production." Say devotes an extensive chapter (XVII) to analyzing their effects, concluding that most such regulations diminish rather than increase national wealth. (017-001)

## Explanation

### Two Forms of Intervention

Government attempts to influence production take two forms: (1) prescribing the raising of **particular kinds of products** judged more advantageous than others; and (2) prescribing **methods of production** imagined preferable to other methods. Both are misguided because "there is no act of [[public-authority|government]] but what has some influence upon production" — and that influence is rarely beneficial. (017-001)

### Specific Regulatory Failures

Say examines four categories:

1. **Product specification regulations**: Directing which [[commodity|goods]] should be produced distorts the natural pattern of industry that would emerge from consumer preferences.
2. **[[production|Production]] method regulations**: Mandating how [[commodity|goods]] should be made pre[[demand|vent]]s [[invention|innovation]] and raises costs.
3. **Privileged trading companies**: [[monopoly|Monopoly]] grants (such as the East India Company) enrich a few at the expense of consumers and competing producers.
4. **Corn [[commerce|trade]] regulations**: Restrictions on grain [[commerce|trade]] create artificial scarcity and price volatility.

### General Principle

Say's general principle is that "the consumer is the best judge of his own [[scarcity|want]]s" and that "the interest of the producer is to produce what the consumer [[scarcity|want]]s, at the lowest possible cost." Government inter[[demand|vent]]ion that overrides these principles inevitably transfers wealth from consumers and efficient producers to privileged interests — without creating new value. (017-002)

## Say's Treatment

Say's treatment of [[public-authority|government]] regulations reflects his broader commitment to economic liberalism. While not an absolutist — he acknowledges cases where public authority's productive efforts are necessary — he is deeply skeptical of the [[public-authority|state]]'s capacity to improve upon the spontaneous order of free [[commerce|exchange]]. This places him in the French liberal tradition alongside [[turgot|Turgot]] and foreshadows the later critiques of inter[[demand|vent]]ionism by Bastiat.

Say advocates rigorous examination of the real effects of economic regulation:

> The grand object of the government should be, to protect the freedom of industry ; yet the government itself, misled by erroneous theories, has too often been the greatest disturber of the natural course of production and consumption.
>
> (017-011)
