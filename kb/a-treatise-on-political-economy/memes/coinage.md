# Coinage

## Definition

Coinage is the process by which a public authority stamps pieces of precious metal with an official mark, certifying their weight and fineness. The coin thus produced serves as a standardized medium of [[commerce|exchange]].

> …the metal, plus the charge of coinage, must be defrayed by the stat… (021-020)

Say defines [[coin|coinage]] as a service that transforms raw [[precious-metals|bullion]] into certified money. The certification eliminates the need for merchants to weigh and assay metal at each transaction, greatly reducing transaction costs.

## Explanation

### The Utility of Coinage

For Say, [[coin|coinage]] is valuable not because it confers intrinsic worth on the metal — the metal already has value — but because it provides reliable information:

> …the impression and coinage. I have merely pointed out th… (021-025)

> The coinage unquestionably adds a value t… (021-026)

The stamp certifies two things: the purity (fineness) and the weight (quantity) of the metal. Without this certification, every transaction would require costly verification.

### Coinage and Fraud Prevention

Private issuers of money — [[banking|banks]], merchants, or local authorities — may debase their issues. Public [[coin|coinage]], by contrast, offers a guarantee backed by the sovereign's reputation:

> …frauds of private issuers. The coinage unquestionably adds a value t… (021-026)

Say regards the [[public-authority|state]]'s [[coin|coinage]] monopoly as justified by the need to pre[[demand|vent]] counterfeit and debasement. The [[public-authority|government]]'s mark is harder to forge than a private one, and the [[public-authority|state]] has stronger incentives to maintain its reputation.

### Coinage Charges

The expenses of [[coin|coinage]] — labour, machinery, metal loss — must be defrayed. Say discusses who bears this cost:

> …the metal, plus the charge of coinage, must be defrayed by the stat… (021-020)

> …ceived to receive the certificate of coinage, for use, not in the characte… (021-028)

In principle, the bearer of [[precious-metals|bullion]] to the mint pays the charge. In practice, [[public-authority|government]]s often absorb part of the cost as a public service, since a well-functioning currency benefits the entire economy.

## Historical Context

Coinage is one of the oldest functions of sovereign authority, dating to ancient Lydia and the Greek city-[[public-authority|state]]s. By Say's time (early 19th century), [[coin|coinage]] had become a well-established [[public-authority|state]] monopoly across Europe. The British Royal Mint and the French Monnaie de Paris were the primary examples Say had in mind.

The historical debate over [[coin|coinage]] charges — seignorage — was particularly active in Say's era. Britain had long maintained free [[coin|coinage]] (bearing the cost from general revenue), while France charged a moderate seignorage. Say engages this debate directly, drawing on the French experience.

## Say's Treatment

Say dedicates a distinct section of Chapter XXI to [[coin|coinage]] — "Of the utility of [[coin|coinage]]; and of the charge of its execution" (P03-002). This structural decision signals that he regards [[coin|coinage]] as a conceptually distinct topic from the nature of money itself.

Say advances several specific arguments:

**Coinage adds value**: The certification premium is real economic value, not merely nominal:

> …to the precious metals by the coinage, must not be confounded with … (021-027)

This value must not be confused with the metal's commodity value; it is the value of information and trust.

**Coinage without [[public-authority|state]] action is possible but inferior**: Say notes cases where [[coin|coinage]] was undertaken by private entities:

> …ot undertaken the business of coinage themselves ; as in Hayti, and… (021-029)

These exceptions prove the rule: private [[coin|coinage]] is feasible only where the [[public-authority|state]] fails, and it generally produces inferior results.

**Debasement through re[[coin|coinage]] is disguised bankruptcy**: Say warns that sovereigns who alter the standard under guise of re[[coin|coinage]] commit a fraud on the public:

> … open, avowed bankruptcy. The coinage of money would become a matte… (021-086)

This connects [[coin|coinage]] policy to Say's broader concern with [[public-authority|government]] probity and the security of property.
