# J.C.L. Sismondi

## Biography

Jean Charles Léonard Simonde de Sismondi (1773–1842) was a Swiss historian and economist. Born in Geneva, he initially embraced [[adam-smith|Adam Smith]]'s doctrines but later became one of the first systematic critics of classical political economy, arguing that laissez-faire policies caused human suffering and economic instability.

## Major Contributions

### Critique of Classical Economics

Sismondi's *Nouveaux Principes d'Économie Politique* (1819) (007-012) challenged the classical assumption that free markets naturally produce optimal outcomes. He argued that competition leads to overproduction, unemployment, and misery for the [[labouring-class|working class]]. He was among the first to call for [[public-authority|government]] inter[[demand|vent]]ion to protect [[labour|work]]ers and regulate economic activity.

### Underconsumption Theory

Sismondi developed an early theory of underconsumption: because [[labour|work]]ers receive less than the full value of what they produce, aggregate demand falls short of aggregate supply, leading to periodic crises (011-004). This argument directly challenged [[vent|Say's Law]] and anticipated later theories of the business cycle.

## Relationship to Say

Say and Sismondi were contemporaries engaged in a fundamental debate about the nature of markets. Say insisted that general overproduction was impossible, while Sismondi documented cases of economic distress that he attributed to market failures. Their [[commerce|exchange]] represents one of the earliest systematic debates about the stability of capitalist economies — a debate that would continue through Marx, Keynes, and beyond.

Prinsep comments on Sismondi's contribution to political economy:

> The pen of Sismondi has exchanged the task of historical detail, for the kindred one of polity in the abstract ; but his work is far more commendable for eloquence and perspicuity of style, than for depth or accuracy of reasoning.
>
> (P02-004)
