# Voltaire

## Biography

François-Marie Arouet (1694–1778), known by his pen name Voltaire, was the central figure of the French Enlightenment — a prolific writer, historian, philosopher, and satirist whose [[labour|work]]s championed reason, religious toleration, and freedom of thought. His exile in England (1726–1729) gave him direct exposure to British commercial society, shaping his economic views and providing Say with comparative observations on the relative prosperity of nations under different institutional arrangements.

## Relationship to Say

Say cites Voltaire throughout the *Treatise* as a perceptive witness to economic realities, drawing on his historical and comparative observations to illustrate theoretical points.

### On Trade and Human Welfare

Say quotes Voltaire's observation on the mutual benefits of [[commercial-industry|commerce]]: "such is the lot of human affairs" (015-013) that nations prosper by exchanging what they produce abundantly for what they lack. This insight, drawn from Voltaire's broad historical reading, supports Say's central argument that free [[commerce|exchange]] enriches all parties — a principle that mercantilist policy systematically violated.

### On the Consequences of Misgovernment

Say invokes Voltaire's verdict on France's economic decline under absolutism. "All that remained to France" (017-090) after decades of war and mismanagement was the productive energy of her people — an energy that, Voltaire observed, [[public-authority|government]] seemed determined to extinguish through [[tax|taxation]] and regulation. Say uses this observation to argue that national wealth persists *despite* [[public-authority|government]], not because of it.

### On Money and Prices

Say draws on Voltaire's historical data to analyze the long-run behaviour of money and prices. Citing Voltaire's observation that "Charles V enacted that the sons of French kings" (021-070) should hold certain monetary privileges, Say traces the evolution of the French monetary system across centuries. Voltaire's data on silver values — showing that silver "was worth in the time of Charles V four times as much as in the age of Voltaire" (021-072) — provides empirical support for Say's analysis of the declining purchasing power of money over time.

### Voltaire as Economic Observer

For Say, Voltaire represents the value of enlightened observation. Though not a systematic economist, Voltaire's historical erudition and comparative perspective allowed him to perceive patterns — the impoverishing effects of war, the wealth-creating power of [[commercial-industry|commerce]], the corrosive effects of arbitrary [[public-authority|government]] — that more technically sophisticated writers sometimes missed. Say's frequent citation of Voltaire reflects his own methodological commitment to grounding economic theory in observable reality rather than abstract deduction.
