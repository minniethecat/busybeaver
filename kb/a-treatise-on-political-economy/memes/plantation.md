## Definition

A plantation, in the usage of Say and his contemporaries, is a large-scale agricultural establishment in colonial territories — particularly the West Indies — organized for the production of cash crops such as sugar, coffee, cotton, and tobacco for export to European markets. The plantation system combines land, capital, and labour (typically enslaved labour in the eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries) in an integrated productive enterprise.

> The surplus produce of a West Indian plantation is very great in relation to the capital employed. (019-009)

The plantation is distinguished from the European farm by its scale, its orientation toward export markets rather than local subsistence, and its reliance on coerced rather than free labour.

## The Economics of Plantation Production

Say analyzes plantation economics through the lens of his general theory of production. A plantation combines the three agents of production — land (the tropical soil and climate), capital (buildings, equipment, processing facilities, and the enslaved [[labouring-class|labourers]] themselves, treated as capital assets), and industry (the direction and management provided by the planter or overseer).

The distinctive economic feature of the plantation is its high ratio of output value to capital employed. Tropical soils, favourable growing conditions, and the continuous [[labour|work]]ing of enslaved labour produced what appeared to be exceptional returns on capital.

> The total produce of a sugar plantation greatly exceeds the value of the capital employed in its cultivation. (019-008)

But Say is careful to note that this apparent surplus is partly illusory. The value of the enslaved [[labouring-class|labourers]]' subsistence must be deducted from the gross produce. More fundamentally, the plantation system operates under conditions of legal monopoly — the colonial powers restricted [[commerce|trade]] to favour metropolitan merchants and [[manufacturing|manufacture]]rs.

## Monopoly and Colonial Trade

The plantation economy was embedded in a system of colonial [[commerce|trade]] restrictions. Colonial producers were typically required to sell their output exclusively to the metropolitan country and to purchase [[manufacturing|manufacture]]d [[commodity|goods]] exclusively from metropolitan suppliers.

> The monopoly of purchasing the colonial produce and of supplying the colonies with European goods was reserved to the mother country. (019-020)

This monopoly transferred wealth from colonial producers and consumers to metropolitan merchants and [[manufacturing|manufacture]]rs. The apparent prosperity of plantations reflected not genuine productive efficiency but the artificial prices created by [[commerce|trade]] restrictions. A free market in colonial produce would have yielded different and likely less spectacular returns.

## Plantation Labour

Say's treatment of plantation labour is constrained by the con[[demand|vent]]ions of his era. He does not mount a direct moral critique of slavery, but his economic analysis contains implicit criticism. The enslaved labourer has no incentive to [[labour|work]] efficiently, no opportunity to exercise initiative, and no prospect of improving his condition through skill acquisition.

The productivity of plantation labour is correspondingly low compared to free labour performing similar agricultural tasks. The planter compensates for low individual productivity through coercion and scale — driving [[labouring-class|labourers]] to [[labour|work]] longer hours under harsher conditions than free [[labour|work]]ers would accept.

The [[manufacture|manufacturing]] labour that occurs on plantations — processing sugar cane into raw sugar, for example — represents a limited form of the division of labour. But the suppression of [[labour|work]]er initiative and intelligence that slavery entails pre[[demand|vent]]s the full realization of productive potential. (021-005)

## Plantation and Metropolitan Industry

The plantation system supported European [[manufacture|manufacturing industry]] by providing [[raw-material|raw materials]] (cotton for textile mills, sugar for refineries) at artificially low prices. This subsidy to metropolitan industry came at the expense of colonial producers and enslaved [[labouring-class|labourers]].

Say's analysis of the [[colony|colonial system]] as a whole is consistently critical. The costs of maintaining [[colony|colonies]] — military garrisons, naval patrols, administrative apparatus — often exceeded the commercial benefits. The apparent prosperity of plantation [[colony|colonies]] concealed a system of cross-subsidies and forced transfers that enriched a narrow class of metropolitan merchants and planters at the expense of consumers, taxpayers, and enslaved [[labouring-class|labourers]].

> Her bane is heavy debt and taxation, aggravated by a poor-law system that paralyzes industry, while her colonial monopoly burdens her consumers and restricts her commerce. (019-018)
