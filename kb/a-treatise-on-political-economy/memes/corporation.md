# Trade Corporation

## Definition

A [[commerce|trade]] corporation is a chartered body of craftsmen or merchants granted exclusive rights to practice a particular [[commerce|trade]] within a particular jurisdiction. It combines elements of monopoly, self-regulation, and legal privilege.

> …a joint meeting of the three corporations, and signed unanimously… (017-010)

Say treats corporations as artifacts of the old regime — institutions that survive by political influence rather than economic merit, and that systematically restrain the productive forces they claim to represent.

## Explanation

### Corporations as Legal Monopolies

At the core of the corporation is an exclusive right: only members may practice the [[commerce|trade]]. This restriction limits supply and raises prices:

> …the prosperity of the corporation is mistaken for that of commerce in general… (017-067)

Say insists on a sharp distinction between the prosperity of the corporation's members and the prosperity of the [[commerce|trade]] as a whole. What benefits the corporation — restricted entry, regulated prices, controlled output — harms the consuming public.

### The Honours of Office

Corporations create internal hierarchies that reward political loyalty rather than economic efficiency:

> …the honours it has appointed to the corporations, who think to earn these honours… (017-068)

The dignities of corporate office — warden, master, alderman — are purchased with conformity to corporate interests. The system selects for political skill, not productive skill.

### Corporations and Economic Geography

Say marshals empirical evidence for his critique by comparing corporate towns with free towns:

> …places, where there are no incorporations of crafts and trades. Manchester and Birmingham… (017-072)

Manchester and Birmingham — the powerhouses of the Industrial Revolution — were unincorporated towns. Their explosive growth, in Say's analysis, was not despite the absence of corporations but because of it:

> …any other towns, subjected to corporations, have experienced a sensible decline… (017-073)

Free labour mobility, unrestricted entry into [[commerce|trade]]s, and the absence of corporate regulation made the new industrial cities engines of productivity. The corporate towns stagnated.

### The Abuse of Authority

Corporations, once established, become vehicles for official abuse:

> …The corporation, which had the control over all that concerned the trade… (017-074)

Say sees in the corporation a microcosm of [[public-authority|government]] failure: a body granted public authority that uses it for private gain. The corporation's regulatory powers — over entry, quality, price, and practice — become instruments of rent extraction.

## Historical Context

[[exchange|Trade]] corporations (guilds) were medieval institutions that survived into Say's era, though in decline. The French Revolution had formally abolished the guilds in 1791, as part of the broader attack on feudal privilege. Say's critique thus had immediate political resonance: the corporations had been legally abolished, but their spirit survived in the form of *chambres de [[commercial-industry|commerce]]*, regulated professions, and chartered trading companies.

The British experience was similar. The Statute of [[apprentice|Apprentice]]s (1563) had given corporations extensive powers over entry into [[commerce|trade]]s; these were progressively weakened through the eighteenth century and largely dismantled by the early nineteenth. [[adam-smith|Adam Smith]] had attacked corporations in *[[wealth|Wealth]] of Nations* Book I, Chapter X, arguing that they pre[[demand|vent]]ed the free circulation of labour.

Say's distinctive contribution was to link the critique of corporations to his general theory of production. Corporations were not merely unjust — they were unproductive. They misallocated labour, suppressed [[invention|innovation]], and diverted capital from its most productive employments.

## Say's Treatment

Say addresses corporations primarily in Chapter XVII, alongside his critiques of prohibition and monopoly. His treatment has several distinctive features:

**The production frame[[labour|work]]**: Say analyses corporations not as political or legal phenomena but as impediments to production. They restrict the supply of skilled labour, limit the division of labour, and pre[[demand|vent]] capital from flowing to its most productive uses.

**The corporate petition**: Say draws attention to the petitions by which corporations defend their privileges:

> …a joint meeting of the three corporations, and signed unanimously, ends with this remarkable conclusion… (017-010)

He treats corporate petitions as evidence of the self-serving logic that drives monopoly politics — the same logic he identifies in prohibitionist lobbying.

**The Manchester-Birmingham test**: Say's comparison of incorporated and unincorporated towns is an early example of what modern economics would call a natural experiment. The superior growth of the free towns provides empirical confirmation of the theoretical case against corporations.

**The [[patent]] analogy**: Say compares corporate charters to [[patent|patents]] of in[[demand|vent]]ion:

> …the charter is a kind of patent of invention, and confers an exclusive privilege… (017-090)

But whereas a [[patent]] rewards genuine in[[demand|vent]]ion for a limited term, a corporate charter rewards political connection in perpetuity. The [[patent]] analogy serves to highlight the illegitimacy of the corporation, not to justify it.
