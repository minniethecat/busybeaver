# Tax

## Definition and Purpose

A tax is a compulsory levy imposed by [[public-authority|government]] on individuals, property, or transactions. Taxes are the principal source of [[revenue|public revenue]], funding the operations of the [[public-authority|state]]. Say observes that "a tax may be indispensable to the maintenance of public order and the administration of justice" (001-005), acknowledging the legitimate functions of [[public-authority|government]] that taxation supports.

## The Incidence of Taxation

Say pays close attention to who ultimately bears the burden of a tax — its incidence. A tax on a commodity "raises the price to the consumer" (001-006), shifting the burden forward. A tax on production, by contrast, may fall partly on the producer through reduced [[profit|profits]]. The "peculiar pressure of each class of taxation" (P02-008) varies depending on the elasticity of supply and demand, the mobility of capital, and the openness of the economy.

## Productive versus Unproductive Taxation

Say distinguishes between taxes that fund productive public expenditure — such as infrastructure, education, and the administration of justice — and those that support [[productive-consumption|unproductive consumption]]. When [[public-authority|government]] devotes tax revenue to productive purposes, the tax burden is partially offset by the value created. But when taxes fund wasteful expenditure, they represent a net deduction from national wealth, withdrawing resources from productive private employment.

> For instance, a tax may be indispensable to the maintenance of public order and security, without which production may be impossible.
>
