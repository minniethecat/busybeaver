# Necessaries

## Definition

Necessaries are the [[commodity|goods]] and services indispensable to the subsistence of the human being — food, clothing, shelter, and the minimum of comfort required to sustain the labouring population. Say describes them as the baseline of all economic activity:

> It is solely by means of industry that mankind can be furnished, in any degree of abundance, with actual necessaries, and with that variety of other objects, the use of which, though not altogether indispensable, yet marks the distinction between a civilized and a savage state. ([002-001])

Necessaries form the foundation of consumption, against which all other [[commodity|goods]] are measured as "superfluities" or "luxuries." They also set a floor under [[wages|wage]]s: the [[wages|wage]] of labour cannot long fall below the cost of necessaries without reducing the labouring population.

## Explanation

### Necessaries as the Baseline of Exchange

In a developed division of labour, no individual produces the full range of [[commodity|goods]] required for personal survival. This makes [[commerce|exchange]] — and therefore [[commercial-industry|commerce]] — a necessity of civilised life:

> If a man makes, not the whole knife, but the handle of it only ... he does not produce any thing that he can turn to account; for what could he do with the handle without the blade? He cannot himself consume the smallest part of his own product, but must unavoidably exchange the whole of it for the necessaries or conveniences of life. ([021-005])

The demand for necessaries is the most stable and predictable component of aggregate consumption, since it arises from biological necessity rather than taste or fashion.

### Necessaries and the Wage-Fund

In classical political economy, the [[wages|wage]] rate is governed in part by the price of necessaries. If the price of food rises, the labourer requires higher money [[wages|wage]]s merely to maintain the same real consumption. Employers, in turn, must advance more capital in the form of [[wages|wage]]s:

> The capital of the manufacturer chiefly consists of the raw material in different stages of progress, of tools, implements, and necessaries for his workmen. ([003-003])

Say regards the [[capital|stock]] of necessaries advanced to [[labour|work]]men as a distinct component of [[capital|productive capital]]. The quantity of necessaries that a given amount of capital can command determines how many [[labouring-class|labourers]] can be employed.

### Necessaries as a Constraint on Population and Progress

[[thomas-malthus|Malthus]] and [[david-ricardo|Ricardo]] made the ratio of food supply to population central to their systems. Say, while less [[thomas-malthus|Malthus]]ian, acknowledges the constraint directly:

> The labourer's wages are limited to the bare necessaries of life, without which his agency cannot be kept up. ([013-009])

Yet Say also observes that in practice, what counts as "necessaries" varies by country, climate, and custom. The labourer in England expects wheaten bread, tea, and decent clothing; the labourer in warmer climates subsists on rice or maize. The definition of necessaries thus has both a physiological and a con[[demand|vent]]ional dimension.

## Historical Context

The concept of necessaries runs through classical political economy from Petty and Cantillon through [[adam-smith|Smith]], [[thomas-malthus|Malthus]], and [[david-ricardo|Ricardo]]. It was central to two connected debates:

1. **The [[wages|wage]]-determination debate**: whether [[wages|wage]]s tend toward bare physical subsistence (the "iron law of [[wages|wage]]s") or whether custom and bargaining power can sustain them above that level.

2. **The luxury vs. necessity distinction in [[tax|taxation]]**: classical writers often advocated taxing luxuries but exempting necessaries, on grounds of both equity (the poor spend a larger share on necessaries) and efficiency (demand for necessaries is less elastic).

Say's contribution is characteristically pragmatic. He accepts that necessaries set a lower bound but insists that industry, [[saving|capital accumulation]], and free [[commercial-industry|commerce]] can raise the general standard well above bare subsistence:

> The natives of the barren glens of Jura are in easy circumstances, because many mechanical arts are there practised. ([005-004])

## Say's Treatment

Say does not devote a separate chapter to necessaries but the concept permeates his analysis of consumption, [[wages|wage]]s, and commercial [[commerce|exchange]].

**On [[commercial-industry|commerce]] as the [[commerce|exchange]] of superfluities for necessaries**: Say reports and partly endorses the view that [[commercial-industry|commerce]] arises from regional surpluses and deficits:

> Genovesi, who lectured on political economy at Naples, defines commerce to be "the exchange of superfluities for necessaries." ([002-007])

While Say finds this definition too narrow ([[commercial-industry|commerce]] also [[commerce|exchange]]s one luxury for another), he accepts its essential truth: the most vital function of [[commerce|trade]] is to bring necessaries to where they are [[scarcity|want]]ed.

**On the moral dimension**: Say observes that the poor man's interest in protecting property rights is as strong as the rich man's, precisely because necessaries are at stake:

> The poor man, that can call nothing his own, is equally interested with the rich in upholding the inviolability of property. ([014-007])

The security of property is what allows industry to produce an abundance of necessaries — without it, even the most fertile land would go uncultivated.

**On policy**: Say warns that [[public-authority|government]] inter[[demand|vent]]ions affecting the price of necessaries — such as grain export prohibitions — produce perverse effects. High grain prices may alarm the public, but artificially suppressing them chokes off production and leads to acute scarcity (017-107).
