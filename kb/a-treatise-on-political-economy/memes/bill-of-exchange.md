## Definition

A **bill of [[commerce|exchange]]** is a written order by which one party (the drawer) directs another party (the drawee) to pay a specified sum to a third party (the payee) at a designated future date. Say discusses bills of [[commerce|exchange]] primarily in his chapters on money (Chapter XXI) and signs or representatives of money (Chapter XXII).

> A bill of exchange has no intrinsic value; it is a mere order to pay, and derives its whole value from the obligation it represents.
>
> See Ch. XXII, "Signs or Representatives of Money." (022-002)

## Explanation

### Function in Commerce

Bills of [[commerce|exchange]] serve as substitutes for money, enabling merchants to settle debts across distances without physically transporting [[coin|specie]]. Say notes: "a letter of credit or bill of [[commerce|exchange]], instead of being drawn for, and transmitted" (021-088) can facilitate transactions between remote parties.

The instrument's value is entirely derivative — it represents a claim on actual money or [[commodity|goods]] rather than possessing intrinsic worth. Say emphasizes this point: "a bill of [[commerce|exchange]] has no intrinsic value" (022-002), warning against the delusion that paper instruments create wealth independent of the underlying assets.

### International Settlement

Say discusses the role of bills of [[commerce|exchange]] in international [[commerce|trade]], where they serve to balance payments between nations. "Gold would purchase at Paris a bill of [[commerce|exchange]] on London" (022-035), illustrating how [[commerce|exchange]] rates between currencies are determined by the relative supply and demand for bills drawn on different financial centers.

The mechanism allows merchants to avoid the cost and risk of shipping precious metals across borders, substituting a paper instrument that can be negotiated in the commercial centers of Europe.

## Historical Context

Bills of [[commerce|exchange]] were among the earliest and most important financial [[invention|innovation]]s of medieval and early modern [[commercial-industry|commerce]]. By Say's time in the early 19th century, they formed the backbone of international [[commerce|trade]] finance, with London, Paris, and Amsterdam serving as the principal centers for bill negotiation.

Say's analysis of bills of [[commerce|exchange]] reflects the classical concern with distinguishing real wealth from its monetary representations. Writing in the aftermath of the French Revolutionary *assignat* inflation, Say was particularly sensitive to the dangers of confusing paper instruments with actual value.

## Say's Treatment

Say treats the bill of [[commerce|exchange]] as a representative of money — a sign that economizes on the use of [[coin|specie]] without creating new value. His analysis is cautious, emphasizing that "this is a mere delusion" (022-002) to think that bills of [[commerce|exchange]] increase national wealth. They are useful instruments of circulation, not sources of capital.

This treatment is consistent with Say's broader theory of money as a mere medium of [[commerce|exchange]], whose quantity is of secondary importance to the real factors of production and consumption.
