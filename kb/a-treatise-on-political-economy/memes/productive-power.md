# Productive Power

## Definition

> …effect of its oscillation upon productive power and activity; the limits of … ([P02-007])

Say conceives of productive power as the **capacity of productive agents** — labour, capital, and natural agents (land) — to generate useful output. It is not identical with production itself but is the underlying capability that production actualises. Productive power can be enlarged (through in[[demand|vent]]ion, [[saving|capital accumulation]], and improved organisation) or diminished (through destructive policy, insecurity of property, or misdirection of resources).

## Explanation

### The Three Sources of Productive Power

> …owe the greater part of their productive powers to works and erections the e… ([004-001])

Say identifies three primary sources:
1. **Natural agents** — land, water, climate, mineral deposits — that "wonderfully augment the productive power of man"
2. **[[capital|Capital]]** — accumulated products (tools, machinery, buildings, infrastructure) applied to further production
3. **[[labour|Labour]]** — human effort, skill, and knowledge, which Say considers the primary productive power that activates the other two

### Enlargement of Productive Power

> …e-employ it in increasing the productive powers of his farm as circumstances… ([011-003])

[[capital|Capital]] accumulation is the principal mechanism for enlarging productive power. When [[saving|savings]] are reinvested in improvements — better tools, drainage, buildings, machinery — the productive capacity of the enterprise and, in aggregate, of the nation increases. Say links this directly to his theory of capital formation and economic growth.

> …nd improvements, augments the productive powers of what already belongs to h… ([011-008])

### Productive Power vs. Accumulation

> …y, than to the enlargement of productive power. I admit, that some absurd ki… ([011-013])

Say cautions against confusing the **accumulation of products** with the **enlargement of productive power**. Some forms of expenditure — "absurd kinds of profusion" — consume capital without augmenting productive capacity. The distinction is central to his theory of economic growth: true enrichment comes not from [[hoard|hoarding]] but from the intelligent reinvestment that enlarges productive power.

### Productive Power and National Wealth

> …siderable developement of the productive powers of man, of land, and of capi… ([014-006])

The security of property rights is the institutional precondition for the development of productive power. Without secure property, the incentive to invest in long-term improvements — drainage, buildings, machinery — vanishes. Say thus links productive power to his broader political economy of institutions.

> …ut the enlargement of its own productive powers is a more important and a pr… ([015-014])

> …rrency. Every addition to the productive power of a nation is a positive goo… ([015-016])

Say argues that the enlargement of productive power is a more fundamental benefit to a nation than the acquisition of territory or the accumulation of [[coin|specie]]. Productive power is the **engine of wealth**: "every addition to the productive power of a nation is a positive good."

## Historical Context

The concept of productive power has roots in the physiocratic notion of *produit net* and [[adam-smith|Smith]]'s analysis of the division of labour as the source of "the productive powers of labour." Say systematises the concept, integrating natural agents into the frame[[labour|work]] and connecting productive power to [[saving|capital accumulation]] theory. The concept would later influence Friedrich List's theory of "productive forces" (*Produktivkräfte*) and Marx's analysis of the development of the productive forces as the motor of historical change.

## Say's Treatment

Say's treatment of productive power is original in three respects. First, he gives **natural agents** equal standing with labour and capital as sources of productive power — a departure from [[adam-smith|Smith]]'s labour-centric frame[[labour|work]]. Second, he distinguishes productive power from mere accumulation, providing an analytical basis for distinguishing productive from unproductive expenditure. Third, he makes productive power the **criterion for policy evaluation**: the question is not whether a policy is traditional or novel, but whether it enlarges or diminishes productive power.
