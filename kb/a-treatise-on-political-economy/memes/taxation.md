## Definition

Taxation is the compulsory transfer of resources from private individuals to public authority for the support of [[public-authority|government]] functions. Say distinguishes [[tax|taxation]] as a **system** — the structure of levies, their incidence across different classes of economic agents, and their aggregate effects on production and accumulation — from the individual tax payment, which is the unit of that system.

> Taxation never does, or at least never ought to, touch the national capital; it is taken from the national revenue. (006-011)

This distinction is fundamental: proper [[tax|taxation]] draws on the annual flow of [[revenue|income]] (revenue), not on the accumulated [[capital|stock]] of productive resources (capital). When [[tax|taxation]] encroaches on capital, it diminishes the nation's future productive capacity.

## The Incidence of Taxation

Say examines who ultimately bears the burden of [[tax|taxation]] — a question distinct from who nominally pays the tax. The formal incidence (the legal obligation to remit payment) and the economic incidence (the actual reduction in real [[revenue|income]]) may diverge substantially.

Import [[tax|duties]], for example, are formally paid by the merchant who imports [[commodity|goods]]. But the economic burden falls largely on consumers through higher prices, and partly on domestic producers who lose export markets when foreign nations retaliate. (017-040)

Similarly, [[tax|taxes]] on the [[necessaries|necessaries of life]] are ultimately borne by the labouring class, who must spend a larger share of their [[revenue|income]] on taxed [[commodity|goods]]. The burden is regressive in its economic effect, even if the tax code makes no distinction among classes. (020-003)

## Excess Burden and the Distortion of Production

Say identifies what modern economics terms the **excess burden** of [[tax|taxation]]: the loss of economic welfare beyond the revenue actually collected by the [[public-authority|government]]. A tax that raises one million crowns in revenue may impose a cost of one and a half million crowns on the economy, once the distortions to production, consumption, and investment decisions are accounted for.

> If the rise be occasioned by taxation or import-duty under any shape, the nation loses the whole amount of the advance of price, which is paid by consumers without reaching the treasury. (017-040)

The mechanism operates through multiple channels. High [[tax|taxes]] on [[profit|profits]] discourage productive investment, channelling capital toward tax-exempt or lower-taxed activities that may be less productive. [[tax|Tax]]es on consumption [[commodity|goods]] raise prices, reducing the real purchasing power of [[wages|wage]]s and depressing demand. Uncertainty about future tax rates discourages long-term investment commitments.

The cumulative effect of excessive [[tax|taxation]] is to drive capital and talent abroad. Say observes that emigration from Britain in his own era was substantially motivated by the burden of [[tax|taxation]] — productive individuals seeking jurisdictions where a smaller share of their earnings would be confiscated. (020-006) (011-009)

## Taxation and Capital Accumulation

The most damaging effect of [[tax|taxation]], in the analysis developed by Say, is its impact on the rate of [[saving|capital accumulation]]. [[capital|Capital]] grows through saving — the diversion of resources from [[productive-consumption|unproductive consumption]] to productive investment. Taxation reduces the pool of resources available for saving.

> It is to be feared that taxation will ultimately deprive the country of the means of maintaining a large capital. (011-017)

The mechanism is cumulative. Year after year, [[tax|taxation]] diverts resources that would otherwise have become [[capital|productive capital]]. The lost capital would have generated returns that could themselves have been saved and reinvested. The long-run cost of this forgone accumulation far exceeds the annual tax revenue collected.

## Taxation and the Right of Property

Say situates [[tax|taxation]] within the broader frame[[labour|work]] of property rights. Taxation represents a partial expropriation of private property for public purposes. The legitimacy of [[tax|taxation]] depends on the legitimacy of the [[public-authority|government]] that imposes it and the purposes to which the revenue is applied.

Where [[public-authority|government]] is representative and accountable, [[tax|taxation]] may be regarded as a voluntary contribution by citizens to the common fund. Where [[public-authority|government]] is arbitrary and unaccountable, [[tax|taxation]] becomes indistinguishable from confiscation.

> In countries under arbitrary and military sway, where taxation is mere organized spoliation, there is no security for property. (017-006)

The critical institutional safeguard is that [[tax|taxation]] must be limited to the support of genuinely public functions. When [[public-authority|government]] expands beyond its proper sphere, [[tax|taxation]] expands correspondingly, encroaching ever further on private capital and individual liberty. (014-002)

## Taxation and Trade Policy

Say analyzes the interaction between [[tax|taxation]] and [[commerce|trade]] policy. Protectionist measures — tariffs, prohibitions, bounties — are a form of [[tax|taxation]] whose burden falls on domestic consumers. The revenue collected (if any) goes to the treasury, but the price increase paid by consumers exceeds the revenue collected by the amount of the excess burden.

The complexity of the tax system itself imposes costs. Multiple overlapping levies — customs [[tax|duties]], excise [[tax|taxes]], stamp [[tax|duties]], land [[tax|taxes]] — create administrative burdens and compliance costs that add to the excess burden. Say advocates simplification: a single land tax in lieu of all other [[tax|taxation]]. (004-005)
