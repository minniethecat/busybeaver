## Definition

**Precious metals** — gold and silver — are commodities that possess high value relative to their bulk, making them uniquely suited to serve as money. Say notes that "it is long since the precious metals, that is to say, gold and silver, have been adopted as money by all nations" (021-011). However, their monetary function does not exempt them from the ordinary laws of value: "whatever brilliant or solid qualities the precious metals may possess, their value depends on their uses and their scarcity" (017-030).

## Explanation

### Dual Use as Money and Commodity

Precious metals serve two distinct economic functions. They act as the material of money, "because their value can be accurately measured and divided" (021-012). But they are also consumed in [[manufacturing|manufacture]] — for "furniture, clothes, and decoration" (012-001). The employment of precious metals in [[manufacturing|manufacture]] "makes them scarce for the purpose of money" (021-016), affecting their value in both uses.

### Value Determined by Supply and Demand

Say firmly rejects the mercantilist notion that precious metals possess an intrinsic or special value. "The value of silver and gold is subject to the same law as that of all other commodities" — they are valuable because they are useful and limited in quantity (017-030). When "the supply of the precious metals in the country be more than sufficient for the purposes to which they are applied, their value declines" (017-026). Like any other commodity, their price adjusts to equilibrate supply and demand.

### The Fallacy of Precious Metals as National Wealth

A major target of Say's critique is the confusion of precious metals with national wealth itself. He identifies the mercantilist conviction "that the precious metals are the only desirable kind of riches" (017-031) as a fundamental error. Governments that prohibit the export of [[coin|specie]] mistakenly believe that retaining gold and silver within national borders enriches the country. In reality, "the quantity of the precious metals is, in spite of all regulations, reduced to the level of the demand for them" (017-019) — no more than is needed for monetary and [[manufacture|manufacturing]] purposes will remain in circulation.

### Trade and the Automatic Adjustment Mechanism

Say develops an early version of the [[coin|specie]]-flow mechanism. If a country's supply of precious metals exceeds demand, their value falls relative to other [[commodity|goods]]. [[merchant|Merchant]]s then find it profitable to export [[coin|specie]] and import commodities: "the merchant treats the precious metals as a commodity he can dispose of to advantage abroad" (017-020). This export continues until the domestic supply is reduced to the level demanded for monetary and [[manufacture|manufacturing]] use. The process is automatic and "in spite of all regulations" (017-019).

## Historical Context

The analysis of precious metals in Say's *Treatise* must be read against the backdrop of the mercantilist system, which dominated European economic policy from the 16th through the 18th centuries. Mercantilism equated national wealth with the accumulation of gold and silver, leading to policies of export prohibition and import restriction. Say, following [[adam-smith|Adam Smith]] and the Physiocrats, systematically dismantles this doctrine. His treatment of precious metals anticipates the classical theory of international [[commerce|trade]] and the price-[[coin|specie]]-flow mechanism later formalized by David Hume and [[david-ricardo|David Ricardo]].

## Say's Treatment

Say devotes substantial attention to precious metals, particularly in Chapters XVII and XXI of the *Treatise*. His approach is twofold: first, to demonstrate that precious metals obey the same economic laws as any other commodity; second, to expose the policy errors that result from treating them as uniquely valuable. The "conviction" that "the precious metals are the only desirable kind of riches" (017-031) leads [[public-authority|government]]s to adopt restrictions that actually harm national prosperity. Say's refutation of this doctrine is one of the most sustained arguments in the *Treatise*, reflecting the central importance of the money-wealth confusion in his critique of mercantilism.
