## Definition

Manufacture is the branch of industry that transforms [[raw-material|raw materials]] into products suited for human use. It is the second of the three great branches of industry in the frame[[labour|work]] developed by Say, positioned between agriculture (which generates [[raw-material|raw materials]]) and [[commercial-industry|commerce]] (which distributes finished [[commodity|goods]]). The distinctive contribution of [[manufacturing|manufacture]] is the creation of **form-utility**: value added to matter by altering its physical form, chemical composition, or structural arrangement.

> The value communicated to a product by manufacture was no more than equivalent to the value consumed by the manufacturer during the process. (002-004)

This formulation captures the Physiocratic objection that Say [[labour|work]]s to refute. The Physiocrats held that [[manufacturing|manufacture]] merely replaced the value of inputs consumed; Say argues that [[manufacturing|manufacture]] creates genuine new value through the application of industry and skill.

## Capital in Manufacture

[[manufacturing|Manufacturing]] enterprise requires capital in distinctive forms. The capital of the [[manufacturing|manufacture]]r consists chiefly of [[raw-material|raw materials]] awaiting transformation, machinery and tools for processing, and finished [[commodity|goods]] awaiting sale. Unlike the farmer, whose capital is largely tied up in land and live[[capital|stock]], the [[manufacturing|manufacture]]r's capital is more liquid and turns over more rapidly.

> The capital of the manufacturer chiefly consists of the raw materials of his manufacture, of tools and machinery, and of the finished goods in his warehouse. (003-003)

The development of machinery dramatically increases the productivity of manufacturing capital. A steam engine driving a cotton mill processes more raw material in a day than a hundred hand-weavers could process in a week. But this machinery itself represents accumulated capital — the [[saving|savings]] of previous periods invested in productive equipment. (005-002)

## Manufacture and the Division of Labour

[[manufacturing|Manufacturing]] is the branch of industry in which the division of labour is most fully realized. [[adam-smith|Adam Smith]]'s famous example of the pin factory illustrates a principle that applies across all manufacturing: the decomposition of a complex production process into simple, specialized operations dramatically increases output per [[labour|work]]er.

Say extends [[adam-smith|Smith]]'s analysis by noting that the division of labour in [[manufacturing|manufacture]] depends on the extent of the market. A cotton [[manufacturing|manufacture]]r serving only a local market cannot profitably subdivide operations to the degree possible for a [[manufacturing|manufacture]]r serving national or international markets. (008-011)

> The master-manufacturer, under whose orders the labourers are collected, conducts the product through all its stages. (006-002)

The master-[[manufacturing|manufacture]]r plays the coordinating role — organizing the sequence of operations, ensuring quality at each stage, and managing the flow of materials from raw inputs to finished output.

## Protection and Manufacture

Say examines the historical case of [[jean-baptiste-colbert|Colbert]], who sought to develop French [[manufacturing|manufacture]]s through [[public-authority|state]] protection and subsidy. The policy appeared successful in the short term — new industries were established, production expanded — but Say argues it ultimately failed because it misallocated capital toward activities that could not sustain themselves without continued [[public-authority|state]] support.

> Colbert ruined France by his protection of manufactures. The truth is that, in every branch of industry, the talent and intelligence of the adventurer must be exerted. (002-012)

The essential requirement for successful [[manufacturing|manufacture]] is not [[public-authority|state]] protection but [[adventurer|entrepreneur]]ial talent, market access, and the free flow of capital toward its most productive uses. Protected [[manufacturing|manufacture]]s become dependent on continued protection; competitive [[manufacturing|manufacture]]s grow stronger through exposure to market discipline.

## Manufacture, Agriculture, and Commerce

The three branches of industry are interdependent. Manufacture depends on agriculture for [[raw-material|raw materials]] and on [[commercial-industry|commerce]] for distribution. [[agriculture|Agriculture]] depends on [[manufacturing|manufacture]] for tools, implements, and processed inputs. [[commerce|Commerce]] depends on both for the [[commodity|goods]] it transports and [[commerce|trade]]s.

Say emphasizes that a nation's prosperity requires all three branches to develop in balanced proportion. A nation that neglects [[manufacturing|manufacture]] in favour of agriculture, or that relies entirely on foreign [[manufacturing|manufacture]]s while exporting [[raw-material|raw materials]], foregoes the value that domestic manufacturing would add.

> The produce of its agriculture, commerce, and manufacture united. For these three great branches of industry are so intimately connected, that the prosperity of each depends on that of the others. (002-014)
