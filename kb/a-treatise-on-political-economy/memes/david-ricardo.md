# David Ricardo

## Biography

David Ricardo (1772–1823) was a British political economist, [[capital|stock]]broker, and Member of Parliament. Born in London to a Sephardic Jewish family, he made a fortune on the [[capital|stock]] [[commerce|exchange]] before turning to economic theory. His *Principles of [[political-economy|Political Economy]] and [[taxation|Taxation]]* (1817) became the dominant text of classical economics in the 19th century.

## Major Contributions

### Labour Theory of Value

Ricardo refined and systematized the labour theory of value, arguing that the [[commerce|exchange]]able value of reproducible commodities is determined by the quantity of labour required for their production (001-007). This theory became the orthodox position in classical economics and later the starting point for Marx's critique.

### Theory of Rent

Ricardo's theory of differential rent explained how the price of corn determines rent, not vice versa. [[rent|Rent]] arises from the differential fertility of land: as population grows and less fertile land is brought into [[agriculture|cultivation]], the more fertile land yields a surplus that becomes rent (017-110).

### Comparative Advantage

Ricardo demonstrated that even if one nation is more efficient than another in producing every good, both nations benefit from [[commerce|trade]] if each specializes in what it produces *relatively* more efficiently. This theory of comparative advantage remains the foundation of international [[commerce|trade]] theory.

## Relationship to Say

Say and Ricardo were contemporaries who engaged each other's [[labour|work]] extensively. They agreed on free [[commerce|trade]], sound money, and the importance of [[vent|Say's Law]] (Ricardo embraced the law of markets). However, they differed fundamentally on the theory of value: Ricardo grounded value in labour, while Say grounded it in utility. Say's translator frequently cites Ricardo — "On this subject it will be well to refer to Ricardo, on the difference between value and riches" (001-001) — and the tension between their value theories runs throughout Say's *Treatise*.

Prinsep offers a critical assessment of Ricardo's [[labour|work]]:

> Of the two most popular works of the present day in this department, that of Ricardo, besides being limited in its range, is both involved in the style, and defective in the arrangement ; and, though containing much that is calculated to instruct and enlighten, is evidently the production of a mind, that has worked its way up from practice to theory.
>
> (P02-002)
