# Profit

## Definition and Role

Profit is the remuneration received by the [[adventurer|entrepreneur]] or capitalist who undertakes productive activity. It represents the excess of the value of the product over the costs of production — including [[wages|wage]]s, [[raw-material|raw materials]], and the services of capital. As Say explains, capital is accumulated "from the profits of [[commerce|trade]] and [[manufacturing|manufacture]]" (002-006), making profit both the reward for and the source of further productive investment.

## Profit in the Production Process

In Say's frame[[labour|work]], the [[adventurer|entrepreneur]] combines the three agents of production — human industry, capital, and natural agents — and bears the risk of the [[enterprise|undertaking]]. The profit earned must be sufficient to "indemnify the ad[[demand|vent]]urer, whose profit, even when successful, is moderated by competition" (006-008). Profit is thus distinguished from the [[wages|wages of labour]] and the rent of land: it is the specific return to the function of organization and risk-bearing.

## Competition and the Rate of Profit

[[competition|Competition]] among producers tends to equalize the rate of profit across different employments of capital. When one branch of industry yields unusually high profits, capital flows into that branch until "the profit is reduced by competition to the lowest rate possible" (010-005). This equalizing tendency is central to the classical theory of markets, ensuring that capital is allocated efficiently across the economy.
