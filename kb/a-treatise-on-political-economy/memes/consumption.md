# Consumption

## Definition

Consumption is the destruction of value through use. Every act of consumption destroys the value consumed; what distinguishes types of consumption is what becomes of that value. Productive consumption reinvests value into further production; [[productive-consumption|unproductive consumption]] satisfies final [[scarcity|want]]s without generating new productive capacity. (003-002)

## Explanation

### Productive vs. Unproductive Consumption

**Productive consumption** occurs when values are consumed in the course of creating new values. The raw cotton consumed by the textile mill, the food eaten by the labourer, and the wear of machinery are all productive consumption — the value destroyed reappears in the finished product.

**Unproductive consumption** satisfies human [[scarcity|want]]s without contributing to further production. A meal eaten for pleasure, a fire[[labour|work]] display, or the services of a musician are consumed at the moment of their production and leave no durable value behind. This does not make them worthless — they satisfy genuine human [[scarcity|want]]s — but they do not augment the national capital.

### Immaterial Products

Say extends the concept of consumption to immaterial products — services consumed at the moment of their production. "The act of giving was its production, of hearing its consumption; and the consumption and production were simultaneous." (013-001) A physician's advice, a lawyer's counsel, and an actor's performance are all consumed as they are produced.

### Consumption and National Wealth

The distinction between productive and [[productive-consumption|unproductive consumption]] is critical for national prosperity. A nation that consumes its capital unproductively — through war, luxury, or [[hoard|hoarding]] — diminishes its capacity for future production. A nation that directs consumption toward productive reinvestment grows its capital and increases its wealth.

## Say's Treatment

Say's treatment of consumption departs from [[adam-smith|Smith]]'s focus on the distinction between productive and unproductive labour. For Say, what matters is not whether the labour itself is "productive" but whether the value consumed is reinvested in production or dissipated in final consumption. This shift from labour to value is characteristic of Say's utility-based frame[[labour|work]].

Say describes how immaterial products are consumed at the very moment of their production:

> The act of giving was its production, of hearing its consumption; and the consumption and production were simultaneous. Immaterial products, or values consumed at the moment of production, constitute a very important class of human values.
>
> (013-001)
