# Political Economy

## Definition

Political economy is the systematic study of how wealth is produced, distributed, and consumed within a society. Say defines its scope from the opening chapter:

> So that, in fact, there is a creation, not of matter, but of utility; and this I call production of wealth. In this sense then, the word production must be understood in political economy, and throughout the whole course of the present work. ([001-004])

The science concerns itself not with matter as such but with *value* — the utility that human industry imparts to natural materials. Political economy investigates the general laws governing this process, distinguishing itself from both statistics (which supplies examples) and politics (which concerns the organisation of authority).

## Explanation

### Scope and Object

Political economy treats of the nature and causes of the [[national-wealth|wealth of nations]]. Its object is to discover the principles by which labour, capital, and natural agents combine to create value. Say insists that the science is bounded: it does not prescribe moral [[tax|duties]], nor does it instruct rulers in [[public-authority|state]]craft — it explains the necessary relations between productive effort and its results.

> The sole use of statistics in political economy is, to supply examples and illustrations; and to furnish the ground-work for inferences as to the actual condition of mankind at different times and places. ([009-007])

### Method

Say's method is deductive and principle-based. He derives truths from observation of human behaviour — notably the universal desire to maximise satisfaction at minimum cost — and builds general laws from those truths. The validity of these laws, he argues, does not depend on local circumstances:

> The principles of political economy are eternal and immutable; but their application is subject to variation according to times, places, and the progressive knowledge of mankind. ([017-102])

He explicitly warns against building systems on "hollow foundations" and urges that the science rest on facts rather than ingenious reasoning detached from reality.

### Productive vs. Unproductive

A central distinction in Say's political economy is between productive and unproductive labour and capital. The Physiocrats had limited productive labour to agriculture and extractive industries — a position Say refutes:

> They allowed no industry to be productive, but that which procured the raw materials; as the industry of the husbandman, the fisherman, and the miner; not adverting to the distinction, that wealth consists, not in matter, but in the value of matter. ([002-004])

Say extends productiveness to [[manufacture|manufacturing]] and [[commercial-industry|commerce]], arguing that any labour that creates or augments utility is productive. He was also among the first to recognise immaterial products — services such as those of physicians, musicians, and public functionaries — as genuine economic [[commodity|goods]], though he notes they cannot be accumulated as national capital (013-003).

## Historical Context

Political economy emerged as a distinct discipline in the 18th century, shaped by the French Physiocrats (Quesnay, [[turgot|Turgot]]) and the Scottish Enlightenment (Hume, [[adam-smith|Smith]]). By Say's time, it was beginning to be taught in formal lectures:

> Genovesi, who lectured on political economy at Naples, defines commerce to be "the exchange of superfluities for necessaries." ([002-007])

> A public course of lectures on political economy, delivered at Milan in the year 1769, and since published, contains some excellent chapters on the subject of commerce. ([008-001])

Say's own *Treatise on Political Economy* (1803) was the first systematic continental European exposition. It systematised [[adam-smith|Smith]]'s insights, stripped them of remaining Physiocratic and mercantilist residues, and established the frame[[labour|work]] — production, distribution, consumption — that dominated the discipline for a century.

The rapid diffusion of political-economic knowledge in the early 19th century was one of Say's proudest observations:

> Rapid has been the progress of political economy, and so wide the diffusion of its principles, that there are few nations in the world that would not be cut off from all hope of mutual commercial relations if they ignored them. ([017-033])

## Say's Treatment

Say occupies a pivotal position in the history of political economy. He built directly on [[adam-smith|Adam Smith]] but departed in several important respects:

**On method**: Say was more systematic than [[adam-smith|Smith]]. He sought to reduce political economy to a small set of first principles and to derive all subsequent propositions from them. He criticised [[adam-smith|Smith]] for a "[[scarcity|want]] of method" and for mixing political economy with politics and moral philosophy.

**On value**: Say rejected the labour theory of value in favour of utility-based value, anticipating later subjective-value theories:

> He advanced a great step in political economy, by demonstrating wealth to consist, not in matter alone, but in the value given to matter by human industry. ([013-002])

**On immaterial products**: Say was the first major economist to treat services as genuine economic products — a position [[adam-smith|Smith]] had rejected. This alone, Say felt, corrected a fundamental error in the *[[wealth|Wealth]] of Nations* (013-002).

**On laissez-faire**: Say was a more consistent advocate of free [[commerce|trade]] and non-inter[[demand|vent]]ion than [[adam-smith|Smith]]. He saw [[public-authority|government]] regulation not as a sometimes-useful corrective but as almost invariably harmful:

> The healthy state of industry and wealth is the state of absolute liberty, in which each interest is left to take care of itself. The only useful protection authority can afford them is, that against fraud or violence. Taxes and restrictive measures never can be a benefit; they are at the best a necessary evil. ([017-053])
