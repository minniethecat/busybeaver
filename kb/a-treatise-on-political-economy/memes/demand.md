## Definition

> It is production which opens a demand for products. (015-001)

Demand, in Say's frame[[labour|work]], is not simply the desire for [[commodity|goods]] but the **effective** power to purchase — a power that originates in production. A buyer can only purchase the produce of others with the produce of their own labour, capital, or land.

Thus the total demand in an economy is bounded by, and originates from, the total value of production.

> The demand for any product is brisk in proportion to the activity of production in general. (015-018)

## Explanation

### Production as the Source of Demand

Say's central thesis on demand is that **products are paid for with products**. [[money|Money]] serves merely as an intermediary; the real purchasing power comes from having produced something of value. When a producer brings [[commodity|goods]] to market, they simultaneously create both supply and demand.

### The Role of Circulation

A [[circulation|brisk circulation]] of [[commodity|goods]] and money facilitates the matching of supply and demand across different markets and locations. When circulation is obstructed, demand falters because producers cannot readily [[commerce|exchange]] their products for others' products.

> It is a good sign when the buyer comes, without being asked for, to the place of production; it shows that the article is in demand. (015-004)

### Gluts and Partial Overproduction

Say acknowledged that individual commodities can be overproduced relative to demand, but argued that a **general glut** — an economy-wide excess of supply over demand — is impossible.

He identified two ways a commodity may exceed its total demand: "either from its having been produced too abundantly in the first instance, or from the production of other commodities having fallen short" (015-006). The remedy is to produce something else more in demand.

> A product is no sooner created, than it, from that instant, affords a market for other products to the full extent of its own value. (015-001)

## Historical Context

The debate over demand was one of the most famous in classical economics.

Say's position was formulated in response to [[thomas-malthus|Malthus]]'s and [[sismondi|Sismondi]]'s concerns about underconsumption. The debate later became known as "[[vent|Say's Law]]" and was critiqued by [[david-ricardo|Ricardo]], Mill, Marx, and Keynes in turn.

In the postwar early 19th century, many feared a permanent deficiency of demand. Say countered that these disruptions were caused by obstructions to production — tariffs, blockades, and misguided [[public-authority|government]] intervention.

## Say's Treatment

Say devotes Chapter XV ("Of the [[vent|Vent]] or Demand for Products") to demand, one of his most extensively treated concepts:

1. **Rejection of underconsumptionism**: He argues the desire for consumption is unlimited; what limits demand is the means to purchase, which comes from production.

2. **[[money|Money]] as a veil**: [[money|Money]] is only a medium of [[commerce|exchange]]. Products [[commerce|exchange]] for products. A [[scarcity|shortage]] of money may disrupt temporarily but doesn't change real demand conditions.

3. **Policy implications**: The best way to stimulate demand is to remove obstacles to production — not to subsidise consumption or erect [[commerce|trade]] barriers.
