# Wealth

## Definition

In political economy, wealth designates an indefinite quantity of objects possessing real and intrinsic value — land, metal, coin, grain, commodities of every description. When the term is extended to include securities, bills, and notes of hand, it is because these contain obligations to deliver things of inherent value. (001-001)

Wealth is proportionate to the quantum of value: "great, when the aggregate of component value is great; small, when that aggregate is small." (001-001) Wealth does NOT consist in the mere [[property|possession]] of value, which is an immaterial quality, but in the [[property|possession]] of things wherein value is vested.

## Explanation

### Wealth and Value

The relationship between wealth and value is central: "to create objects which have any kind of utility, is to create wealth; for the utility of things is the ground-[[labour|work]] of their value, and their value constitutes wealth." (001-003) [[production|Production]] creates wealth by creating utility, which grounds value.

### National Wealth

The [[political-economy|science of political economy]] is, in Say's definition, concerned with "the knowledge of the real nature of wealth...of the difficulties that must be surmounted in its attainment, of the course and order of its distribution amongst the members of society, of the uses to which it may be applied." (001-002) This tripartite frame[[labour|work]] — production, distribution, and consumption of wealth — structures the entire *Treatise*.

### Wealth vs. Money

A common error that Say critiques is the confusion of wealth with money. Metal money is only one form of wealth; the true wealth of a nation consists in the abundance of its products and the productive capacity of its industry, capital, and natural agents. Government regulations that raise prices without increasing utility do not create national wealth — they merely redistribute it.

## Say's Treatment

Say's conception of wealth is notable for its emphasis on **utility** rather than mere material accumulation. A nation is wealthy not because it has gold in its treasury but because its land, labour, and capital produce an abundance of [[commodity|goods]] that satisfy human [[scarcity|want]]s. This utility-centric view of wealth aligns with Say's broader rejection of mercantilist and Physiocratic doctrines.

Say defines political economy by its subject matter — the nature of wealth:

> The knowledge of the real nature of wealth, thus defined, of the difficulties that must be surmounted in its attainment, of the course and order of its distribution amongst the members of society, of the uses to which it may be applied, and, further, of the consequences resulting respectively from these several circumstances, constitutes that branch of science now entitled Political Economy.
>
> (001-002)
