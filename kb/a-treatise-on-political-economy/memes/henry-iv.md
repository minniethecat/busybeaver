# Henry IV of France

## Biography

Henry IV (1553–1610) was King of France from 1589 until his assassination in 1610 — the first French monarch of the House of Bourbon. Having ended the French Wars of Religion through the Edict of Nantes (1598), he devoted his reign to economic reconstruction after decades of civil conflict. His partnership with his finance minister, the Duke of Sully, produced one of the most successful episodes of economic recovery in early modern European history — a recovery that Say treats as a case study in the power of good [[public-authority|government]] to foster national prosperity.

## Relationship to Say

Say references Henry IV's reign at several points, drawing lessons about the relationship between [[public-authority|government]] policy and economic outcomes.

### Protectionism and the Cloth Trade

Say recounts how "[[manufacturing|manufacture]]rs of Tours petitioned Henry IV to prohibit the import of [[precious-metals|gold and silver]] thread" (017-044) — an early example of the protectionist pressures that [[public-authority|government]]s have always faced. Say uses this incident to illustrate his argument that producers consistently seek monopoly privileges, and that wise [[public-authority|government]] consists in resisting such demands rather than acceding to them.

### Colonial Ventures

Say notes that the French East India Company "was established in the reign of Henry IV A.D. 1604" (017-088) — one of the earliest French colonial trading [[demand|vent]]ures. The fact that this company required [[public-authority|state]] sponsorship illustrates, for Say, the inherent weakness of monopoly-based colonial [[commercial-industry|commerce]]. Private merchants, pursuing profit through competition, would have served French [[commercial-industry|commerce]] more effectively than any chartered monopoly.

### Monetary History

Several of Say's references to Henry IV concern the monetary history of France. He notes the value of silver [[coin|coinage]] "in the days of Henry IV" (021-059) and records that "in 1602, under the reign of Henry IV, the mark of fine silver" (021-071) was set at a specific value. These data points serve Say's broader analysis of the declining purchasing power of money across centuries — a trend he attributes not to any intrinsic property of precious metals but to the progressive increase in their supply from the mines of the New World.

### The Sully Partnership

Say's treatment of Henry IV is implicitly admiring precisely because the king delegated economic management to a competent minister. The economic recovery under Sully — roads built, agriculture revived, [[manufacturing|manufacture]]s encouraged — demonstrates what [[public-authority|government]] *can* achieve when it confines itself to creating conditions for private industry rather than attempting to direct it. The contrast with [[louis-xiv|Louis XIV]]'s activist but destructive reign is instructive: Henry IV's [[public-authority|government]] fostered wealth; [[louis-xiv|Louis XIV]]'s consumed it.
