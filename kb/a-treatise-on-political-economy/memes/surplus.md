## Definition

> When a man has succeeded in raising a value superior to that required for his own consumption, he has netted a surplus produce beyond his consumption. (011-001)

A **surplus** is the portion of total production that exceeds what is necessary to replace inputs consumed in the productive process and to maintain the producers themselves. It is the residual after all costs have been covered — including subsistence of [[labouring-class|labourers]] and replacement of worn-out capital.

> A surplus value had been produced; and this surplus, when employed as capital, yields a fresh profit. (011-003)

## Explanation

### Gross Produce versus Net Produce

Say distinguishes between **gross produce** (total output value) and **net produce** (surplus after replacing consumed inputs). Only net produce represents a genuine addition to national wealth.

A productive process that merely replaces its inputs — "it leaves no surplus, no net produce" (004-005) — contributes nothing to wealth, however much gross activity it generates.

### Surplus as the Engine of Growth

The economic mechanism of growth runs through surplus. A producer creates value exceeding their consumption. Say illustrates this with an example: "let us trace the disposal of this surplus of 1000 crowns to every imaginable purpose" (011-002). The surplus is saved and invested as additional [[capital|productive capital]], compounding over time.

> The sum of this surplus is all the additional wealth annually acquired by the community. (011-010)

### Surplus and the Rate of Profit

The size of the surplus relative to capital employed determines the rate of profit. Industries yielding larger surplus attract capital; those yielding little or none drain the national [[capital|stock]]. The market directs capital toward surplus-yielding employments.

## Historical Context

The surplus concept has ancient roots — in Aristotle's distinction between use-value and [[relative-value|exchange-value]], and in the Physiocratic *produit net*, which held that only agriculture yielded a genuine surplus.

[[adam-smith|Adam Smith]] generalised the concept, arguing that all sectors produced surpluses. Say follows [[adam-smith|Smith]] in generalising surplus across all productive sectors but adds greater analytical clarity on the distinction between gross and net product and the mechanism converting surplus into capital.

## Say's Treatment

Say addresses surplus primarily in Chapter XI ("Of the Formation and Multiplication of [[capital|Capital]]"):

1. **Surplus in all productive sectors**: Unlike the Physiocrats, Say insists [[manufacture|manufacturing]] and [[commercial-industry|commerce]] also generate surpluses. Any activity adding more value than it consumes is productive.

2. **The consumption decision is critical**: A surplus may be consumed productively (reinvested) or unproductively (spent on luxuries). National wealth growth depends on the proportion devoted to productive reinvestment.

3. **Immaterial products**: Say's inclusion of immaterial products raises the question of whether these yield a surplus. He argues they do — a physician's fee, after covering costs, contains a surplus.
