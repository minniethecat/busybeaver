# Competition

## Definition and Function

Competition is the rivalry among producers and [[merchant|trader]]s in the [[market|marketplace]], each seeking to sell their products or services. It is the central regulatory mechanism of classical economics, constraining prices, [[profit|profits]], and the behaviour of economic agents. "The competition of [[manufacturing|manufacture]]rs pre[[demand|vent]]s the price from rising above the natural rate" (002-005), ensuring that consumers benefit from the productive efforts of industry.

## Competition and Prices

Competition drives prices toward the cost of production. When a new technique or machine reduces production costs, "the product is thenceforward cheapened by competition to the full extent of the saving in the costs of production" (007-009). This process distributes the benefits of [[invention|technological progress]] to society at large, rather than allowing them to be captured entirely by the innovator.

## Competition and Innovation

While competition reduces individual profit margins, it also stimulates [[invention|innovation]]. Producers who can reduce their costs below those of their rivals earn temporary advantages until "competition reduces the profit to the lowest rate" (010-005). This dynamic process of [[invention|innovation]] and imitation drives continuous improvement in productive efficiency, benefiting society through lower prices and better products.
