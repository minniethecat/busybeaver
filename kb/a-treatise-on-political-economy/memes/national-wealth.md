## Definition

**National wealth** is the sum total of all values possessed by the individuals and public bodies that compose a nation. Say follows [[adam-smith|Adam Smith]] in defining national wealth as the aggregate of individual fortunes: "national wealth is made up of the aggregate of individual riches" (017-019). Crucially, national wealth is not measured by the quantity of money a nation holds but by the abundance of all useful and [[commerce|exchange]]able [[commodity|goods]] — a distinction that forms the analytical foundation of Say's critique of mercantilism.

## Explanation

### The Composition of National Wealth

National wealth encompasses "all the circumstances that, united, compose national wealth" (017-029). This includes land, buildings, machinery, live[[capital|stock]], commodities in circulation, and the skills and knowledge of the population. [[money|Money]] itself "forms an item of national wealth, in the same manner as sugar, indigo, or any other commodity" (021-023) — it is one component among many, not the measure of the whole.

### Production as the Source of Wealth

For Say, national wealth is created through productive activity. Whenever a productive operation is "performed, and an item added to national wealth" (017-007), the nation becomes richer. This applies equally to agricultural, [[manufacture|manufacturing]], and commercial production. The "productive efforts of public authority" (Chapter XVIII) also contribute to national wealth when they produce utility — roads, canals, security, and justice are all components of the national patrimony.

### Distinction from Money

A central error that Say [[labour|work]]s to correct is the confusion of national wealth with the money supply. He observes that this "notion has been extended to national wealth, which is made up of the aggregate of individual riches, not of the quantity of money" (017-019). A nation with abundant [[commodity|goods]] and a small money supply is wealthy; a nation with abundant money and scarce [[commodity|goods]] is poor. The Spanish empire, flooded with American silver, provided a historical demonstration of this principle.

### Growth and Circulation

National wealth grows through saving and productive investment. Say notes that when a nation's capital increases, "her national wealth has advanced likewise" (011-016). The "[[circulation|brisk circulation]] of money" facilitates the growth of national wealth by ensuring that [[commodity|goods]] find their markets and that production is continuous (Chapter XVI). Government regulations that obstruct circulation are "powerful checks to the progress of national wealth" (017-040).

## Historical Context

The concept of national wealth underwent a fundamental transformation in the 18th century. Mercantilist doctrine measured a nation's prosperity by its [[capital|stock]] of [[precious-metals|gold and silver]]. The Physiocrats shifted the focus to [[agriculture|agricultural production]] and the *produit net*. [[adam-smith|Adam Smith]], in *The [[wealth|Wealth]] of Nations* (1776), redefined national wealth as the [[revenue|annual produce]] of land and labour. Say extends [[adam-smith|Smith]]'s frame[[labour|work]], adding a more systematic analysis of how value — not physical matter — constitutes wealth, and how the productive contribution of [[commercial-industry|commerce]] and immaterial labour must be included in any accurate accounting of national riches.

## Say's Treatment

Say devotes sustained attention to the concept of national wealth throughout the *Treatise*. The "great subject of national wealth" (P02-004) is introduced in the translator's preface as the central theme of the [[labour|work]]. Say addresses the "effect upon national wealth, resulting from the productive efforts of public authority" (018-001) in a dedicated chapter, and the impact of [[colony|colonies]] upon "national wealth" (022-016) in another. His treatment is unified by the conviction that national wealth consists of useful values, not material objects or monetary tokens. "The sacrifice we make" in [[tax|taxes]], for example, is compensated when public authority provides security and justice that enable productive activity, thereby "adding to national wealth" (017-007) in net terms.
