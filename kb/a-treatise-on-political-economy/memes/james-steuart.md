# Sir James Steuart

## Biography

Sir James Steuart (1712–1780), also spelled Stewart, was a Scottish political economist whose major [[labour|work]] preceded [[adam-smith|Adam Smith]]'s *[[wealth|Wealth]] of Nations* by nine years. Say's translator places him in the lineage of political economy alongside [[adam-smith|Smith]]: "since the days of [[adam-smith|Smith]] and Stewart." (P02-002) He is described as "the English writer Stewart, who may be looked upon as the most methodical author that has written on political economy." (002-014)

## Major Contributions

### Principles of Political Economy

Steuart's *An Inquiry into the Principles of [[political-economy|Political Economy]]* (1767) was the first systematic treatise in English to use the term "political economy" in its title. The [[labour|work]] is distinguished by its rigorous method. Say notes that Steuart's approach required a comprehensive treatment: "a separate treatise," says Stewart, "to investigate all the artifices by which the quantity of circulation is accommodated to the demand." (021-044)

### Theory of Wealth and Production

Steuart contributed to the classical understanding of how wealth multiplies through production. Say groups him with [[turgot|Turgot]] and [[adam-smith|Smith]] as authorities who share the principle that national wealth grows in proportion to productive output: "Stewart, [[turgot|Turgot]], and [[adam-smith|Smith]], all agree in thinking, that the multiplication of wealth is in exact proportion to the increase of its production." (019-006)

## Relationship to Say

Say's engagement with Steuart is ambivalent. While he acknowledges Steuart's methodological rigour, Say's translator criticizes Steuart's consumption theory, noting that Steuart "who may be looked upon as the most methodical author that has written on political economy," nonetheless fell into the error of considering all consumption, even unproductive, as beneficial to national wealth (002-014).

Say treats Steuart as part of the foundational trio of pre-[[adam-smith|Smith]]ian political economy alongside [[turgot|Turgot]], invoking all three when establishing the fundamental principle that production — not accumulation of [[coin|specie]] or a favourable balance of [[commerce|trade]] — is the source of national opulence.
