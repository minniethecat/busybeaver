# Immaterial Products

## Definition

Immaterial products are values consumed at the very moment of their production, leaving no durable material residue. "A physician goes to visit a sick person, observes the symptoms of disease, prescribes a remedy, and takes his leave without depositing any product that the invalid or his family can transfer to a third person." (013-001) The physician's industry is nonetheless productive: the patient's life may have been saved.

## Explanation

### Production Equals Consumption

The defining characteristic of immaterial products is the simultaneity of production and consumption: "The act of giving was its production, of hearing its consumption; and the consumption and production were simultaneous." (013-001) This distinguishes services from material [[commodity|goods]], which can be stored, transported, and consumed at a later time.

### Productive Status

Say argues forcefully that immaterial products are genuinely productive. The physician, the lawyer, the musician, the actor — all satisfy real human [[scarcity|want]]s and receive payment in [[commerce|exchange]]. To classify their labour as "unproductive" (as [[adam-smith|Adam Smith]] had done) is to misunderstand the nature of value, which consists in utility, not in material durability.

### Examples

Immaterial products include: medical advice, legal counsel, musical and theatrical performances, education, religious services, public administration, and personal services of all kinds. What they share is that the value is consumed in the act of its creation — the [[scarcity|want]] ceases the moment it is satisfied.

## Say's Treatment

Say's recognition of services as productive was a significant departure from the Physiocrats (who recognized only agriculture) and from [[adam-smith|Adam Smith]] (who classified services as unproductive labour). By grounding production in utility rather than material output, Say provided a theoretical foundation for treating the service sector as genuinely wealth-creating. This insight anticipated the modern understanding of service economies.

Say describes the unique character of immaterial products:

> Immaterial products, or values consumed at the moment of production, constitute a very important class of human values. The act of giving was its production, of hearing its consumption; and the consumption and production were simultaneous.
>
> (013-001)
