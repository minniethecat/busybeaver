## Definition

**Invention** is the creation of new [[machinery|machines]], processes, or techniques that increase the productive power of [[labour|human labour]]. Say recognizes in[[demand|vent]]ion as one of the primary sources of economic progress: "the most important in[[demand|vent]]ions and discoveries are often due to the progress of society" (006-001). An in[[demand|vent]]ion is a form of immaterial capital — the application of intelligence to production — and its economic value lies in its capacity to multiply the output obtainable from a given quantity of labour and capital.

## Explanation

### Invention as a Source of Productivity

Invention directly enhances productivity by enabling the same quantity of labour to produce more. Say identifies "the in[[demand|vent]]ion of a great number of [[machinery|machines]]" as a major cause of the growth of productive power (008-003). When a new machine is introduced, "the product may be furnished at less expense" (007-005), benefiting consumers through lower prices. The "stimulus this in[[demand|vent]]ion gave to authorship" after the printing press illustrates how a single in[[demand|vent]]ion can transform an entire field of human activity (007-007).

### Patents and the Protection of Invention

Say devotes considerable attention to the institution of [[patent|patents]]. He endorses the principle that an in[[demand|vent]]or should enjoy "the exclusive advantage of his in[[demand|vent]]ion, which it is highly just and useful to secure" (007-009). Patents serve a dual purpose: they reward the in[[demand|vent]]or for the effort and ingenuity invested in discovery, and they encourage further in[[demand|vent]]ion by demonstrating that society values and protects intellectual creation. Without [[patent]] protection, "the most important in[[demand|vent]]ions and discoveries" might never be brought to commercial application.

### The Limits of Patent Protection

Say is not, however, an uncritical supporter of [[patent|patents]]. He notes that privileges are sometimes granted "for so trifling an in[[demand|vent]]ion" as to be worthless (017-079). Worse, [[monopoly|exclusive privilege]]s can obstruct the diffusion of genuinely useful innovations. A [[patent]] "confers an advantage, consisting in the exclusive right to do that which every body else is prohibited from doing" (017-090). Say warns that the [[patent]] system must balance the reward to in[[demand|vent]]ors against the public interest in the widest possible adoption of beneficial discoveries. A useless in[[demand|vent]]ion, "if it be useless, so much the worse" — the [[patent]] merely protects a dead weight on [[commercial-industry|commerce]] (017-081).

### International Diffusion of Inventions

Say observes that nations that do not originate in[[demand|vent]]ions may nevertheless benefit from them. "France has imitated the machinery and processes of England" (017-080) and in some cases has even surpassed the originator in the quality of production. The nation where an in[[demand|vent]]ion is first made is not necessarily "better off than the nation where the in[[demand|vent]]ion originated" (017-080) if others can adapt and improve upon it. This observation anticipates later theories of technological catch-up and the diffusion of innovations.

## Historical Context

Say wrote during the early decades of the Industrial Revolution, when the economic significance of in[[demand|vent]]ion was becoming unmistakable. The mechanization of textile production, the improvement of the steam engine, and the proliferation of machine tools were transforming the productive landscape of Europe. Patent systems had been established in England (1624) and France (1791), but their design and scope remained contested. Say's analysis reflects the tension between the Enlightenment commitment to the free circulation of knowledge and the recognition that in[[demand|vent]]ors require incentives to invest in discovery.

## Say's Treatment

Say's treatment of in[[demand|vent]]ion extends across several chapters of the *Treatise*, from the analysis of productive operations in Chapter VI to the discussion of [[public-authority|government]] regulation in Chapter XVII. He views in[[demand|vent]]ion as a form of productive effort that, like all productive activity, adds to national wealth. The in[[demand|vent]]or contributes immaterial value — knowledge — that multiplies the effectiveness of material capital and labour. Say's defence of [[patent|patents]] is qualified but genuine: he sees them as "highly just and useful" (007-009) while warning against their abuse. His broader point is that "in[[demand|vent]]ion in every branch of art and science" (017-047) is promoted not by [[public-authority|government]] direction but by the security of property and the freedom of enterprise.
