## Definition

In Say's *Treatise on [[political-economy|Political Economy]]*, a **bank-note** is a form of paper currency issued by a banking institution, representing a promise to pay the bearer a specified sum in metallic money ([[coin|specie]]) upon demand. Unlike [[public-authority|government]]-issued paper money, the bank-note derives its value from the credit of the issuing bank and its convertibility into coin or [[precious-metals|bullion]]. Say treats "bank-notes, or convertible paper" (P03-002) as a distinct category within the broader monetary system, devoting substantial analysis to their operation in Chapters XXI and XXII.

## Explanation

### The Mechanism of Bank-notes

Say explains that "a bank-note passes current" on "exactly the same principle" as metallic money (021-097) — by the confidence of the holder that it can be converted into [[coin|specie]] at will. The bank that issues notes must maintain sufficient reserves of coin or [[precious-metals|bullion]] to meet demands for redemption, but need not hold a one-to-one backing. This fractional-reserve principle allows bank-notes to expand the circulating medium beyond the physical [[capital|stock]] of precious metals.

### Bank-notes vs. Other Paper Instruments

Say carefully distinguishes bank-notes from other forms of commercial paper. "Bills at sight or after date, bank-notes, running credits, write-offs" (015-004) are all means of payment, but they differ in their legal character and degree of negotiability. The bank-note is the most liquid of these instruments — payable on demand and passing from hand to hand without endorsement.

### Economic Benefits

The substitution of paper for metallic currency yields significant economic advantages. Say argues that "substituting bank-notes in place of half the [[coin|specie]]" (022-017) releases precious metals for other productive uses — as raw material for industry, as capital for foreign [[commerce|trade]], or as a store of value. "The practicable issue of bank-notes procures to a nation of moderate wealth" (022-020) a more elastic and efficient circulating medium than [[coin|specie]] alone could provide.

### Risks and Limitations

Say is keenly aware of the dangers of bank-note issuance. He insists that the application of his analysis "applies exclusively to bank-notes, depending solely upon the credit of the bank" (022-015) — not to [[public-authority|government]] paper money, which lacks the discipline of convertibility. The critical question is "whether bank-notes, or paper destitute of intrinsic value" (022-016) can be sustained against the temptation of over-issue. Say's answer is that only convertibility and the discipline of the market can pre[[demand|vent]] depreciation.

## Historical Context

Say wrote at a pivotal moment in the history of banking. The [[bank-of-england|Bank of England]]'s suspension of [[coin|specie]] payments (1797–1821) had demonstrated both the possibilities and perils of paper currency. The French experience with John Law's bank (1716–1720) and the Revolutionary *assignats* provided cautionary examples of paper money's catastrophic potential. Say's analysis steers a middle course: recognizing the genuine utility of bank-notes while insisting on the safeguards of convertibility and market discipline.

## Say's Treatment

Say's treatment of bank-notes exemplifies his broader methodological commitment to analyzing monetary phenomena through the lens of value theory. A bank-note is not magical — it circulates because it represents real value, and it maintains that value only so long as the promise of convertibility remains credible. This analysis links the microeconomics of banking to the macroeconomics of the monetary system, anticipating the concerns that would later animate the [[banking|Banking]] School in its debates with the [[money|Currency]] School.
