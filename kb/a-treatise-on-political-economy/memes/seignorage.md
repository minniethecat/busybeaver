# Seignorage

## Definition

Seignorage is the charge levied by the [[public-authority|government]] upon the [[coin|coinage]] of [[precious-metals|gold and silver]], representing the difference between the face value of the coined money and the cost of the metal plus [[coinage|minting]] expenses.

> …Had England charged a seignorage upon the coinage of gold and … (017-057)

In Say's taxonomy, seignorage can be either a legitimate fee for a genuine public service (certification of weight and fineness) or a disguised tax on money-holders — depending on its magnitude.

## Explanation

### Seignorage as a Service Fee

At its most defensible, seignorage simply covers the actual costs of [[coin|coinage]] — the labour, machinery, and metal lost in the [[coinage|minting]] process:

> …the French government charges a seignorage, equal at most to defray the … (021-030)

In this moderate form, seignorage is analogous to any other fee for public service. The bearer of [[precious-metals|bullion]] pays for certification; the public gains a reliable currency.

### Seignorage as a Revenue Instrument

Governments have long treated seignorage as a source of revenue exceeding [[coinage|minting]] costs:

> …self, were simply to charge a seignorage, say of 10 per cent, upon the… (021-031)

Say is cautious about this use. A moderate seignorage that slightly exceeds costs may be acceptable, but it shades into a tax whose incidence falls unevenly on those who hold or use coined money.

### Seignorage Distinguished from Debasement

Say is careful to distinguish seignorage from the more pernicious practice of debasement. A declared seignorage is transparent; the public knows the charge. Debasement — reducing the metal content while maintaining the nominal value — is fraudulent:

> …age ; but the imposition of a seignorage would neither have promoted t… (017-058)

> … open, avowed bankruptcy. The coinage of money would become a matte… (021-086)

Say argues that England, by recoining worn gold at full weight without charge, actually subsidized money-holders at public expense. A modest seignorage, by contrast, would have been honest and fiscally sound.

## Historical Context

Seignorage has been a sovereign prerogative since ancient [[coin|coinage]] began. In medieval Europe, seignorage was a significant source of royal revenue — and frequently abused. The French monarchy was notorious for manipulating the seignorage rate as a fiscal expedient.

By Say's era, the practice varied across nations. Britain had adopted "free [[coin|coinage]]" — the Mint charged no seignorage, bearing the cost from general revenue. France maintained a moderate seignorage. Say, writing from French experience, defended the French practice as more rational than the British one, which he viewed as an implicit subsidy to gold-holders.

The debate over seignorage intersected with the larger [[bullion|Bullion]] Controversy (1810s), in which the relationship between coin, paper, and metallic value was fiercely contested.

## Say's Treatment

Say's discussion of seignorage appears primarily in Chapter XXI ("Nature and Uses of [[money|Money]]") and Chapter XVII ("[[government-regulations|Government Regulations]]").

**Seignorage is not a tax on export**: Say clarifies a conceptual confusion:

> … that, in fact, this is not a seignorage, but a duty on exportation, e… (021-036)

A true seignorage is levied at the mint, not at the border. Confusing the two leads to bad policy.

**Seignorage and the value of coin**: Say argues that a moderate seignorage does not diminish the value of coin below the value of the metal it contains, because the coin carries a certification premium:

> …e concluded, that the duty or seignorage upon coinage, which has been … (021-032)

The coin with seignorage paid circulates at a value above its metallic content — the difference being the value of the certification itself.

**Seignorage and [[precious-metals|bullion]] flows**: In international [[commerce|trade]], a high seignorage can discourage the import of [[precious-metals|bullion]] for [[coin|coinage]], affecting the domestic money supply. Say notes this effect but does not consider it a decisive objection, since the [[coin|coinage]] monopoly enables [[public-authority|government]] to manage the currency responsibly:

> …d with a moderate duty on the coinage, may acquire a currency in di… (021-033)
