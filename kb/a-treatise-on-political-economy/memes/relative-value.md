# Relative Value

## Definition

> …the confusion of positive and relative value are discussed in different pa… ([001-001])

Say defines relative value as the **ratio at which one commodity [[commerce|exchange]]s for another** — a relational property determined by market conditions, not an inherent quality of the thing itself. He distinguishes it sharply from intrinsic (positive) value, which rests on utility and cost of production. The "confusion" of these two concepts is, for Say, a persistent source of error in economic reasoning.

## Explanation

### Determination of Relative Value

> …d on the other determines the relative value of two or more products. It i… ([009-003])

Relative value is determined by the **comparative costs of production** of the commodities being [[commerce|exchange]]d. When two products embody different quantities of productive effort — labour, capital services, natural agents — their [[commerce|exchange]] ratio tends toward the ratio of their respective costs. [[supply|Supply]] and demand cause short-term deviations, but cost of production provides the gravitational centre.

### Money as Measure of Relative Value

> …ely to estimate or reckon the relative value of commodities; in other wor… ([021-058])

[[money|Money]] functions as the common measure of relative value: it expresses the [[commerce|exchange]] ratio of every commodity in a single numeraire. Say is careful to note that money is not an "invariable measure" — it is itself a commodity whose relative value fluctuates — but it is a practical instrument for reckoning commercial values.

Relative values are subject to continuous fluctuation from changes in supply, demand, and production costs. Say analyses how monetary expansion and contraction alter the relative value of money to commodities, affecting debtors and creditors asymmetrically — an analysis that feeds directly into his theory of national bankruptcy.

Say articulates the quantity-theory relationship: when the quantity of money increases relative to the volume of commodities, the relative value of money falls and prices rise. This is not a change in the intrinsic value of money (if metallic) but a change in its [[commerce|exchange]] ratio against [[commodity|goods]].

Relative value provides the frame[[labour|work]] for comparing prices across nations: differences in the relative value of [[commodity|goods]] and metals between two countries determine the direction of [[commerce|trade]] flows.

## Historical Context

The relative-value concept runs through classical political economy from [[adam-smith|Smith]] (value in [[commerce|exchange]]) through [[david-ricardo|Ricardo]] (who sought an invariable measure) to Marx (exchange-value as the form of value). Say's treatment is distinctive in grounding relative value in **utility-augmented cost of production** rather than labour alone, and in his careful application of the concept to monetary phenomena.

## Say's Treatment

Say's most original contribution regarding relative value is his **application to monetary analysis**: the intrinsic/relative distinction allows him to explain why debasement and [[bank-note|paper-money]] depreciation [[labour|work]] — they alter relative values while leaving intrinsic values (of commodities) unchanged. This analytical clarity distinguishes his treatment from contemporaries who conflated the depreciation of currency with a general rise in the value of [[commodity|goods]]. His recognition that no "invariable measure" of relative value exists anticipates later index-number theory.
