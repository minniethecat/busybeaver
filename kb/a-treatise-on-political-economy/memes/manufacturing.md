# Manufacturing

## Definition

Manufacturing is the branch of industrial activity that transforms [[raw-material|raw materials]] into finished or semi-finished products. It encompasses "all branches of [[manufacture|manufacturing industry]] — mechanical or chemical" (002-001), from textile production to metal[[labour|work]]ing to food processing. Manufacturing adds value to [[raw-material|raw materials]] by changing their form, making them fit for human use.

## The Productivity of Manufacturing

A central debate in classical economics concerned whether [[manufacture|manufacturing]] labour was productive. Some early economists, particularly the Physiocrats, argued that "[[manufacture|manufacturing]] labour is barren and unproductive" (002-005) because it merely reshapes existing matter rather than creating new substance. Say rejected this view, insisting that [[manufacture|manufacturing]] creates genuine value by imparting utility to [[raw-material|raw materials]]. The value added by the manufacturer — through skill, machinery, and organization — is as real as the value produced by the farmer.

## Manufacturing and Commerce

Manufacturing and [[commercial-industry|commerce]] are complementary: the manufacturer depends on [[commercial-industry|commerce]] to distribute [[commodity|goods]], while [[commercial-industry|commerce]] depends on [[manufacture|manufacturing]] to supply the products of [[commerce|trade]]. Say notes that the "revenue of the commercial and [[manufacture|manufacturing]] producers" (002-009) constitutes a major part of national [[revenue|income]]. A nation with a strong [[manufacture|manufacturing]] base can supply its own needs and export surplus production, enriching itself through international [[commerce|trade]].
