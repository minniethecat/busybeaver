# Utility

## Definition

Utility is the inherent fitness or capability of certain things to satisfy the various [[scarcity|want]]s of mankind. Say introduces the term to designate this quality precisely: "To this inherent fitness or capability of certain things to satisfy the various [[scarcity|want]]s of mankind, I shall take leave to affix the name of utility." (001-003)

Objects possess utility in different ways: some afford [[subsistence|sustenance]], others serve for clothing, some defend from the elements, and others gratify taste or vanity. It is universally true that "where men attribute value to any thing, it is in consideration of its useful properties: what is good for nothing they set no price upon." (001-002)

## Explanation

### Utility as the Groundwork of Value

Utility is the foundation upon which all value rests. Say [[public-authority|state]]s: "to create objects which have any kind of utility, is to create wealth; for the utility of things is the ground-[[labour|work]] of their value, and their value constitutes wealth." (001-003)

However, utility alone does not determine value. Air, water, and light have immense utility but possess no [[commerce|exchange]]able value where they are abundant, because no difficulty intervenes between the desire and its attainment. [[value|Value]] requires both utility **and** difficulty of attainment.

### Production as Creation of Utility

[[production|Production]], in Say's frame[[labour|work]], is the creation not of matter but of utility. "All that man can do is, to re-produce existing materials under another form, which may give them an utility they did not before possess, or merely enlarge one they may have before presented." (001-004) When a [[manufacturing|manufacture]]r transforms iron ore into a lock, they create utility — and thus create wealth — by giving the material a form that serves human purposes.

### Utility in Exchange

When one person sells a product to another, "he sells him the utility vested in that product: the buyer buys it only for the sake of its utility, of the use he can make of it." (001-005) The price paid should reflect the utility received. If a buyer is forced to pay more than the utility is worth to them — through monopoly or [[tax|taxation]] — they pay for value that has no existence.

## Say's Treatment

Say's emphasis on utility as the source of value is one of his most distinctive contributions to classical political economy. By grounding value in subjective utility rather than objective labour time, Say opened a path that would later be developed by the Marginal Revolution. He treats utility not as an abstract philosophical concept but as the practical measure of what production actually achieves.

Say explains that in every sale, utility itself is the object of the transaction:

> In fact, when one man sells any product to another, he sells him the utility vested in that product : the buyer buys it only for the sake of its utility, of the use he can make of it. If, by any cause whatever, the buyer is obliged to pay more than the value to himself of that utility, he pays for value that has no existence.
>
> (001-005)
