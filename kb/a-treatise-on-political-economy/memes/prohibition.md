# Prohibition

## Definition

Prohibition, in the context of [[commerce|trade]] policy, is the absolute or near-absolute ban on the importation of foreign [[commodity|goods]] — either by direct legal prohibition or by [[tax|duties]] set so high as to amount to prohibition.

> …duties amounting to prohibition… (017-052)

Say treats prohibition as the most extreme form of protectionist inter[[demand|vent]]ion, distinct from moderate tariffs in both intent and economic effect. Whereas a moderate duty raises revenue while partially restricting [[commerce|trade]], prohibition aims to extinguish foreign competition entirely.

## Explanation

### The Mechanism of Prohibition

Prohibition operates by severing the natural channel of [[commerce|exchange]] between domestic consumers and foreign producers. The consumer is compelled to purchase from a domestic source at a higher price, or to forgo the product altogether:

> …after he has once embarked his capital in a particular manufacture, to ruin him by taking off a duty, that has been laid on with a view to prohibition… (014-002)

Once prohibition has induced capital into a protected industry, the policy becomes self-reinforcing: the [[public-authority|government]] cannot withdraw the prohibition without destroying the capital it has artificially called into existence.

### Prohibition as a Weapon

Say observes that prohibitions are often wielded as instruments of economic warfare, not economic welfare:

> …interested rulers, who employ prohibition as a weapon of offence or an engine of defence… (017-032)

The language of weaponry is deliberate. Prohibition serves the interests of rulers — not the people — by creating [[monopoly|monopolies]] that can be sold, [[commerce|trade]]d, or granted as political favours.

### The Price Ceiling Paradox

Say identifies a logical contradiction in the prohibitionist argument. If prohibition is intended to elevate domestic production, it cannot succeed:

> …that a government cannot, by prohibition, elevate a product beyond its natural price… (017-036)

The protected producer gains a temporary advantage, but competition among domestic producers e[[demand|vent]]ually drives the price toward its natural level. The only lasting effect is that both nations are poorer: the prohibiting nation loses access to cheaper foreign [[commodity|goods]], and the excluded nation loses a market.

### The Ultimate Effect

Say's conclusion is unambiguous:

> …This is the utmost effect of prohibitions ; both parties are worse provided… (017-049)

Prohibition makes both sides worse off — a net destruction of wealth on both sides of the border. The domestic consumer pays more; the foreign producer sells less; the only beneficiary is the protected domestic producer, whose gain is smaller than the combined loss.

### The Petition Dynamic

Say explains why prohibitions persist despite their demonstrable harm:

> …community so importunate for prohibitions or heavy import duties ? The answer is obvious… (017-038)

Concentrated producer interests — a particular town, [[commerce|trade]], or [[corporation]] — lobby intensely for prohibition because the benefit to each is visible and concentrated. The cost is dispersed across millions of consumers, each of whom bears too small a share to justify political resistance. This asymmetry of incentives perpetuates bad policy.

## Historical Context

The prohibition debates of Say's era (the early nineteenth century) were embedded in broader controversies over mercantilism versus free [[commerce|trade]]. The British Corn Laws, the French silk prohibitions, and the Calico Acts were all contemporary examples of prohibitionist policy.

> …the eternal prohibition of the use of printed calicoes… (017-010)

Say was writing after [[adam-smith|Adam Smith]]'s *[[wealth|Wealth]] of Nations* (1776) had already exposed the fallacies of mercantilism, yet prohibitions remained politically popular. Say's distinctive contribution was to systematize the economic critique — not merely arguing that prohibition was unwise, but demonstrating through the logic of markets that it was self-defeating.

The tension between free-[[commerce|trade]] theory and protectionist practice would persist throughout the nineteenth century, culminating in the repeal of the Corn Laws (1846) and the Cobden-Chevalier Treaty (1860). Say's analysis of prohibition as a political — not economic — phenomenon anticipated the public-choice critique of protectionism by more than a century.

## Say's Treatment

Say devotes extensive attention to prohibition in Chapter XVII ("[[government-regulations|Government Regulations]]"), where he treats it as one of the principal ways [[public-authority|government]] misdirects industry. He develops several distinctive arguments:

**The consumer-is-the-nation argument**: Say insists that the interest of consumers is identical with the interest of the nation, reversing the mercantilist identification of producer interests with the public good:

> …and producers, they gain by prohibition and monopoly as much in the one character as they lose in the other… (017-042)

**The equivalence of prohibition and monopoly**: Say treats legal prohibition as simply a [[public-authority|state]]-enforced monopoly, analytically identical to private monopoly in its effects on price and quantity.

**The empirical test**: Say points to real cases — the petition of Marseilles for the prohibition of foreign straw hats (017-045), the silk prohibitions (017-055) — to show that prohibitions consistently fail to achieve their [[public-authority|state]]d goals.

**National security exception**: Say acknowledges one legitimate case for prohibition:

> …import of gunpowder, if such prohibition be necessary to set the powder mills at work… (017-051)

He concedes that military necessity may justify prohibition — but frames this as the rare exception that proves the general rule. National defence is the only ground on which prohibition can be defended.
