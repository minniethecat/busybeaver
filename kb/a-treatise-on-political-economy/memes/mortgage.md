# Mortgage

## Definition

A mortgage is a loan secured by land or real property, by which the landholder converts the value of fixed property into circulating capital. In Say's analysis, mortgages serve as a crucial financial instrument that bridges the gap between landed wealth and [[capital|productive capital]]. The landholder obtains funds "on mortgage of his land, presenting the amplest security" (022-023), using the most solid form of property as collateral for a loan that can be directed to productive ends.

## Explanation

### Mortgage as a Bridge Between Land and Capital

Say recognizes that land, while a source of wealth, is an immobile form of capital. The mortgage converts this fixed asset into circulating value that can fund industry, [[commercial-industry|commerce]], or agricultural improvement. This transformation is essential to a dynamic economy: without it, vast amounts of national wealth would remain locked in land, unavailable for productive employment.

### Mortgages and the National Capital Structure

Say groups mortgages among the financial encumbrances on national wealth alongside national funds and other instruments: "the incumbrances of those sources, mortgages, national funds, &c., &c., which are but portions of that [[property|ownership]], and to which the same observation applies." (011-006) The key observation is that the distribution of these financial claims does not affect the total national capital — transferring [[property|ownership]] does not destroy or create wealth, it merely shifts it between hands.

### The Mortgagee in the Economic Order

Say classifies the mortgagee among the various types of creditors and rentiers who derive [[revenue|income]] from financial claims: "fundholder, annuitant, mortgagee, bonded creditor, &c. &c." (021-050) These passive recipients of interest or annuity payments form a distinct class in commercial society, living on the [[revenue|income]] from their financial assets rather than from active participation in production.

## Historical Context

The mortgage has ancient origins, but it became a widespread instrument of commercial society in the eighteenth century as land markets developed and legal systems provided reliable enforcement of secured lending. For the classical economists, the mortgage epitomized the transformation of feudal landed wealth into commercial capital — a process they viewed as central to the rise of modern commercial society.

## Say's Treatment

Say's treatment of mortgages is part of his broader analysis of how different forms of property contribute to the national capital. He is careful to distinguish between the physical capital (the land itself, which is productive) and the financial claim upon it (the mortgage, which is merely a right to a portion of its revenue). The mortgage does not create new capital; it redirects existing capital to new hands, and to the extent that those new hands employ it productively, it may contribute to national enrichment.

Say is ambivalent about the multiplication of financial claims. While mortgages can mobilize capital, an excessive superstructure of debt and encumbrance — particularly when compounded by [[public-authority|government]] borrowing — can burden the productive economy. The mortgagee, like the fundholder, is a passive claimant on the produce of industry rather than an active contributor to it.
