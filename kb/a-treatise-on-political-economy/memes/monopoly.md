# Monopoly

## Definition

A monopoly is an exclusive privilege, typically granted by [[public-authority|government]], that restricts competition in a particular [[commerce|trade]] or industry. "A [[public-authority|government]] establishes a monopoly in favor of the home producer" (017-035) through import [[tax|duties]], exclusive charters, or other regulatory barriers. The defining feature of monopoly is the absence of the competitive pressures that normally constrain prices to the cost of production.

## Effects on Price and Profit

By excluding competition, monopoly enables the holder to raise prices above the level that would prevail in a free market. However, Say notes that this power has limits: if the monopoly price rises too high, it may attract competition from substitutes or smuggling, such that "the monopoly is thereby rendered nugatory" (017-036). Monopoly [[profit|profits]], where they persist, represent a transfer from consumers to the privileged producer rather than a genuine addition to national wealth.

## Costs to Society

Monopoly imposes a double burden on the public: higher prices for the monopolized good, and the misallocation of productive resources. "If the loss thrown upon consumers by the monopoly could be correctly computed, it would be found to exceed the gain to the monopolist" (017-042). Say argues that those protected by monopoly "gain by prohibition and monopoly as much in the one capacity as they lose in the other" (017-044), emphasizing that monopoly redistributes rather than creates value.
