## Overview

**The Bank of England** is the central bank of the United Kingdom, founded in 1694. In Say's *Treatise on [[political-economy|Political Economy]]*, it serves as the primary case study for analyzing paper money, inconvertible currency, and the relationship between [[banking|banks]] and [[public-authority|government]]. Say and his translator C.R. Prinsep devote extensive attention to the Bank's suspension of [[coin|specie]] payments in 1797, treating it as a pivotal experiment in monetary theory.

## History and Context

The Bank of England was established in 1694 as a private joint-[[capital|stock]] company to raise funds for the [[public-authority|government]]. By the time Say was writing (early 19th century), it had evolved into the dominant financial institution in Britain. Its notes circulated as the de facto national currency alongside [[precious-metals|gold and silver]] coin.

A critical turning point came in 1797 when, under the pressure of war expenditure, Parliament passed the Act of Restriction, making Bank of England paper inconvertible — that is, the Bank was no longer obliged to redeem its notes in [[coin|specie]] (gold or silver). This effectively transformed the Bank's notes from representative money into fiat paper money. (021-007)

Say's translator, C.R. Prinsep, observed that "the Bank of England paper at 30 millions, and the paper of the country [[banking|banks]] at as much more" together constituted a paper circulation of approximately £60 million sterling, which, despite having no intrinsic value, performed the function of money through sheer necessity in an advanced commercial nation. (021-021)

## Role in Political Economy

The Bank of England occupies a central position in Say's analysis of money and credit. Its notes illustrate the principle that money derives value not from intrinsic worth but from utility as a medium of [[commerce|exchange]]. Say argued that paper could serve as money precisely because "in a very advanced stage of society and of industry," the urgent [[scarcity|want]] of "some agent or medium of [[commerce|exchange]]" could invest even paper with real purchasing power. (021-021)

The Bank's operations also demonstrate the dangers of [[public-authority|government]] entanglement with banking institutions. Say noted that the Bank had advanced more than its entire capital to the [[public-authority|government]], which could not repay. This locked the Bank into permanent [[debt|indebtedness]]: "Not being able to obtain payment from [[public-authority|government]], it was unable to withdraw the notes in which the loan was made. From that moment its notes ceased to be convertible." (022-026)

Say also documented the financial cost of maintaining convertibility. Before the suspension, the Bank sustained "the annual loss of from 2½ to 3 per cent., upon a sum of about 850,000l., more than 20 millions of our money," due to the constant need to purchase [[bullion|gold bullion]] that was immediately melted down again by arbitrageurs. (022-021)

## Relationship to Say's Analysis

Say's treatment of the Bank of England is nuanced. He recognizes the practical necessity of paper money in a commercial nation while remaining skeptical of inconvertible paper. His analysis focuses on several key points:

**[[money|Money]] as commodity**: Say insists that money, whether gold or paper, is a commodity whose value is "determined by the same general rules, as that of all other commodities; that is to say, rises and falls in proportion to the relative demand and supply." The Bank of England paper, though "destitute of intrinsic value," was "by its mere utility, possessed of as positive value in England, as a piece of gold or silver." (021-053)

**The suspension of 1797**: Say presents the suspension as a [[public-authority|government]] decision that "constituted it the authorised, though not technically the legal, money of the nation." (021-007) His translator adds that the paper's value derived not from any promise of redemption — "That promise has been held out ever since the suspension of cash payments by the Bank in 1797, without any attempt at performance" — but from the public's need for a circulating medium. (021-019)

**Risk of [[public-authority|government]] advances**: Say is critical of the Bank's perpetual lending to [[public-authority|government]], which he identifies as "the cause of the failure of the Bank of England." (022-026) The [[public-authority|government]]'s inability to repay trapped the Bank in inconvertibility.

**Scale of circulation**: Say's quantitative analysis shows the Bank's relative size in the British economy. Before the [[bank-note|paper-money]] era, total British circulating [[coin|specie]] was estimated at no more than £47 million, or roughly 1/50th of the national capital. (003-004) This places the Bank's £30 million paper issue in macroeconomic context.
