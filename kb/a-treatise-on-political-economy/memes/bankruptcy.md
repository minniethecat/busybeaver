# Bankruptcy

## Definition

> …credit; the effect of national bankruptcy upon… ([P02-007])

Say distinguishes between two forms of bankruptcy: **private commercial bankruptcy** — the failure of an individual or firm to meet obligations — and **national bankruptcy**, which operates through monetary manipulation rather than open default. National bankruptcy, in Say's frame[[labour|work]], is effected by the sovereign's power over the currency: deteriorating the intrinsic value of coin, issuing inconvertible [[bank-note|paper-money]] beyond redemption capacity, or compelling creditors to accept depreciated instruments.

## Explanation

### National Bankruptcy by Coinage Debasement

> …republic, effected a national bankruptcy more than once, by deteriorating the intrinsic value of their coin. In the course… ([021-043])

The sovereign, holding the monopoly of [[coin|coinage]], can reduce the precious metal content of coin while maintaining its nominal face value. This operates as a **partial bankruptcy**: the [[public-authority|state]] discharges its obligations in debased currency, transferring the loss to creditors and holders of fixed money claims. Say observes that this mechanism allows [[public-authority|government]]s to default without openly acknowledging insolvency.

### Bankruptcy by Paper-Money Depreciation

Inconvertible [[bank-note|paper-money]] presents a second channel for national bankruptcy. When a [[public-authority|government]] issues paper beyond its capacity to redeem in [[coin|specie]], the paper depreciates against commodities. [[credit|Credit]]ors repaid in depreciated paper suffer a loss equivalent to the depreciation percentage — Say explicitly frames this as a bankruptcy of "80 per cent."

## Historical Context

National bankruptcy through [[coin|coinage]] debasement was a recurrent phenomenon in European monetary history. Say references the Roman republic's repeated debasements and the French experience with assignats during the Revolutionary period. The concept was central to classical political economy's concern with sound money and the limits of sovereign monetary power.

## Say's Treatment

Say's treatment is distinctive in two respects. First, he frames monetary depreciation explicitly as a **form of bankruptcy** — not merely an economic disturbance but a breach of contractual obligation. Second, he identifies the **concealment mechanism**: monetary manipulation achieves what open default cannot, precisely because the public does not immediately recognise it as bankruptcy. This insight links Say's monetary theory to his broader concern with transparency and the security of property rights.
