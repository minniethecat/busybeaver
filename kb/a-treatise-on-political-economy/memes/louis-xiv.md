# Louis XIV

## Biography

Louis XIV (1638–1715), known as the Sun King, reigned as King of France from 1643 until his death — the longest recorded reign of any European monarch. His rule epitomized absolute monarchy, characterized by the centralization of [[public-authority|state]] power, the construction of the Palace of Versailles, and a series of costly wars that sought to extend French hegemony across Europe. He revoked the Edict of Nantes in 1685, driving hundreds of thousands of Protestant Huguenots into exile — an act Say condemns for its devastating economic consequences.

## Relationship to Say

Say treats Louis XIV as the paradigmatic case of prodigal [[public-authority|government]] — a ruler whose personal extravagance and military ad[[demand|vent]]urism consumed national capital on an enormous scale while producing nothing of lasting value. "The pageantry and the wars of Louis XIV" (002-013) exemplify destructive consumption: wealth diverted from productive investment to ostentation and destruction.

Say argues that even Louis XIV's legitimate spending was economically harmful because it represented capital withdrawn from productive channels:

The forty years of warfare that marked Louis's reign created, in Say's analysis, no enduring wealth despite the vast sums expended. Say notes the paradox that France's economy persisted "in spite of the profusion" (011-015) — a testament to the resilience of private industry rather than any merit of royal policy.

### The Revocation of the Edict of Nantes

Say identifies the expulsion of the Huguenots as Louis XIV's most economically destructive act. He observes that Louis "had used his right hand to cut off his left" (020-005) — a vivid metaphor for how religious persecution destroyed the very [[manufacture|manufacturing]] and commercial classes that generated French prosperity. The exiled Protestants took their skills, capital, and industrious habits to England, Holland, and Prussia, enriching France's rivals at France's expense.

### Colonial and Commercial Misadventure

Say notes the failure of Louis XIV's colonial [[demand|vent]]ures, citing the French East India Company established "towards the close of the reign of Louis XIV" (017-088) as an example of [[public-authority|state]]-directed [[commercial-industry|commerce]] that could not compete with private enterprise. When "the forces of Louis XIV penetrated as far as Utrecht" (022-010), military success brought no lasting economic benefit — territory gained by arms was not territory made productive.

### The Fêtes of Versailles

Say singles out the court spectacles at Versailles as a particularly conspicuous form of unproductive expenditure. "The vaunted fetes of Louis XIV" (020-003) were not merely wasteful in themselves but, more seriously, established a model of aristocratic consumption that diverted capital from productive investment across the entire French elite. The cultural magnificence of Versailles was, in economic terms, a monument to the destruction of capital.

## Say's Verdict

For Say, Louis XIV is not simply a historical curiosity but a cautionary exemplar. His reign demonstrates the core principle that [[public-authority|government]] expenditure — however magnificent its appearance — draws resources away from the productive activities that alone generate national wealth. "With a view to restore the finances" (017-061), Louis's ministers multiplied [[tax|taxes]] and regulations, but the root cause — the sovereign's own consumption — was never addressed. The lesson Say draws is unambiguous: the [[national-wealth|wealth of nations]] depends on the productive employment of capital, and no monarch, however powerful, can spend his way to prosperity.
