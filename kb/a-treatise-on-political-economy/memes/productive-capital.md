# Productive Capital

## Definition

Productive capital consists of the pre-existing products without which human industry, however skilful and intelligent, could never be put in motion. These requisites are of three kinds: (1) tools and implements of the several arts; (2) products necessary for the subsistence of the industrious agent during the production process; and (3) [[raw-material|raw materials]] to be converted into finished products. (003-001)

Say defines [[capital|productive capital]] as values devoted to reproductive employment — as distinct from values consumed unproductively or hoarded in inactivity. [[capital|Capital]] is not merely money; it is the accumulated product of previous industry, applied to facilitate further production.

## Explanation

### The Three Components

1. **Tools and Implements**: The husbandman's spade and mattock, the weaver's loom, the mariner's ship — without these, labour cannot produce.
2. **[[subsistence|Subsistence]] Advances**: The [[labour|work]]er must eat and be sheltered while engaged in production. This outlay is replaced by the final product, but it must be continually advanced.
3. **[[raw-material|Raw Material]]s**: The substances that industry transforms — iron ore for the smith, cotton for the weaver, grain for the miller.

### Capital Formation

[[capital|Capital]] is formed through saving. When the value produced exceeds the value consumed, the surplus becomes new capital available for further production. "Wherever industry is active and unfettered, [[saving|savings]] are made, and more capital accumulated from the [[profit|profits]] of production." (002-006)

### Capital and Scarcity

[[capital|Capital]] is essentially the means of surmounting the difficulty of attainment. "[[capital|Capital]] may further that extension, may overcome that difficulty of attainment" — it allows producers to undertake longer and more complex productive processes that yield greater utility. (003-001)

## Say's Treatment

Say's treatment of capital is distinctive in its emphasis on capital as **productive value** rather than as a social relation. For Say, capital is a universal feature of production — present in all economic systems — and its reward (interest) is the legitimate price paid for the productive service it renders. This contrasts with later socialist critiques that would characterize capital as a source of exploitation.

Say also emphasizes the continuous **transformation** of capital: "[[capital|productive capital]], though kept during the progress of production in a continual [[public-authority|state]] of employment, and subject to perpetual change and wear, is yet ultimately reproduced in full value when the business of production is at an end." (010-001)

The value of tools, materials, and subsistence advances together constitute [[capital|productive capital]]:

> The value of all these must be found in advance by the industrious agent that works them up. The value of all these items constitute what is denominated productive capital.
>
> (003-002)
