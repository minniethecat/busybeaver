## Definition

In Say's *Treatise on [[political-economy|Political Economy]]*, **public authority** denotes the institutions of government and the state, considered from the standpoint of their economic functions and effects. Say devotes Chapters XVII–XVIII to analyzing both the proper and improper inter[[demand|vent]]ions of public authority in economic life. The public authority has a dual character: it is essential for the protection of property, yet also a persistent source of economic distortion when exceeding its legitimate sphere.

> The interference of the public authority, with regard to the details of production, can only derange the natural order.
>
> (017-065)

## Explanation

Say recognizes that public authority performs necessary economic functions. Chief among these is the protection of property rights: "when public authority is not itself a spoliator, it protects property" (014-006). Without secure property, productive industry cannot flourish. The public authority may also undertake enterprises that private ad[[demand|vent]]urers cannot profitably pursue — public [[labour|work]]s, infrastructure, and in emergencies, the [[supply|provision]] of essential [[commodity|goods]] (018-002).

Say devotes far more attention to the dangers of excessive government inter[[demand|vent]]ion. Public authority is "incompetent of itself to give value" (021-011); it cannot create wealth by decree. When governments attempt to fix prices or manipulate currency, they invariably produce the opposite of their intended effect: "the public authority is disgraced" (021-045).

Say identifies the "prodigality of public authority, war, or the poor law" (011-005) as destructive drains on national capital. Public consumption, whether for war or ostentation, consumes wealth without reproducing it.

## Historical Context

Say wrote in the aftermath of the French Revolution and the [[napoleon|Napoleon]]ic Wars — periods of massive government inter[[demand|vent]]ion in economic life, including price controls, paper money inflation, and wartime [[commerce|trade]] restrictions. His skepticism toward public authority reflects both the liberal tradition of [[adam-smith|Adam Smith]] and direct experience with revolutionary economic mismanagement. The analysis of public authority's proper limits was a central theme of classical political economy.

## Say's Treatment

Say's treatment of public authority is characteristically balanced. He is not a doctrinaire advocate of *laissez-faire* in all cases — he acknowledges necessary public functions. But his dominant theme is caution: the public authority should "intermeddle with the business of individuals" only to the limited extent required for "the security of person and property" (021-084). Say's frame[[labour|work]] distinguishes between the productive efforts of public authority and its [[productive-consumption|unproductive consumption]], mirroring his broader theory of productive versus un[[capital|productive capital]].
