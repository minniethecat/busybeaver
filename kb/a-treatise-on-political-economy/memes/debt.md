# Debt

## Definition

Debt is a financial obligation by which one party — the debtor — is bound to repay a sum to another — the creditor. In classical political economy, debt is analysed in both its private dimension ([[credit|commercial credit]]) and its public dimension (national debt), with the latter receiving particular attention from Say as a drain on [[capital|productive capital]].

> …the government, its principal debtor, must discharge its debt… (021-019)

Say treats national debt not as a neutral transfer but as a subtraction from the nation's productive fund, with consequences that extend beyond public finance into the monetary system itself.

## Explanation

### The Formation of National Debt

National debt arises when [[public-authority|government]] expenditure exceeds revenue, and the deficit is financed by borrowing rather than [[tax|taxation]]. Say traces the origin of national debt to war,奢侈宫廷, and fiscal mismanagement:

> …this that engenders national debt, internal abuse, tyranny and… (009-012)

In Say's analysis, public borrowing competes directly with private investment for the nation's scarce capital. [[money|Money]] lent to the [[public-authority|government]] is money not lent to productive enterprise.

### Debt as an Incubus on Production

Say repeatedly describes national debt as a crushing weight on the productive economy:

> …incubus of an enormous national debt, and consequent exorbitance of taxation… (015-016)

The pairing of "debt" and "[[tax|taxation]]" is deliberate. National debt requires interest payments, interest payments require [[tax|taxation]], and [[tax|taxation]] falls upon productive industry. The debt thus becomes a perpetual claim of the unproductive [[public-authority|state]] upon the productive sector.

### The Debtor-Creditor Relationship in a Monetary Economy

Say extends his analysis of debt into the monetary sphere. When [[public-authority|government]]s debase the currency, the debtor-creditor relationship is fundamentally altered:

> …extremely convenient to pay one's debts with less money than one borrowed… (021-040)

Monetary depreciation transfers wealth from creditor to debtor:

> …by which reduction, the debtor gains what the creditor loses… (021-046)

Say regards this as a concealed form of partial repudiation — a violation of the contractual foundation of credit. The integrity of money is, at bottom, the integrity of debt contracts.

### Colonial Debt

Say applies the debt analysis to [[colony|colonies]], where the burden falls particularly heavily:

> …her bane is heavy debt and taxation, aggravated by a… (019-018)

Colonies, in Say's frame[[labour|work]], suffer a double disadvantage: they export capital to the metropole through the colonial relationship, and they bear debt burdens that further drain their productive capacity.

## Historical Context

The national debt of Great Britain was the defining fiscal phenomenon of Say's era. Having grown enormously through the [[napoleon|Napoleon]]ic Wars, it stood at over £800 million by 1815 — a figure that dominated contemporary economic debate.

Say was writing in a tradition that included David Hume's famous essay "Of Public [[credit|Credit]]" (1752) and [[adam-smith|Adam Smith]]'s treatment of public debt in *[[wealth|Wealth]] of Nations* Book V. [[adam-smith|Smith]] had argued that national debt diverted capital from productive to unproductive uses; Say adopted and extended this argument within his own production-centred frame[[labour|work]].

The French experience with assignats during the Revolution — a case study in debt monetization and hyperinflation — looms in the background of Say's monetary analysis of debt. His insistence on the sanctity of debtor-creditor relations reflects a direct witnessing of monetary collapse.

## Say's Treatment

Say addresses debt across multiple chapters, reflecting its intersection with capital theory, money, and public finance:

**In the theory of capital (Chapters X–XII)**: National debt is the archetypal unproductive use of capital. [[money|Money]] lent to [[public-authority|government]] is consumed — it does not reproduce itself:

> …proportionate reduction of all debts and obligations payable in money… (021-045)

**In monetary theory (Chapter XXI)**: Debt is central to Say's analysis of money. The function of money as a standard of deferred payment means that alterations in the value of money directly affect the real burden of debt:

> …the creditor, and loss of the debtor. Have creditors, either British or French… (021-051)

**In [[commerce|trade]] policy (Chapter XVII)**: Say notes that a debtor nation has perverse incentives in [[commerce|trade]] negotiation:

> …A debtor nation has an obvious interest in depreciating its currency… (017-034)

**In colonial economics (Chapter XIX)**: Colonial debt is a specific pathology, combining the burdens of metropolitan debt with the structural disadvantages of colonial dependency.

Say's overall treatment is notable for linking the analysis of debt to his general theory of value and production. Debt is not merely a fiscal phenomenon but a claim on real resources — and its burden depends ultimately on the productive capacity of the economy that bears it.
