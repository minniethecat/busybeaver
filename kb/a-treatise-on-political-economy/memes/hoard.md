# Hoard

## Definition

A hoard is an accumulation of money — typically coin or precious metals — held idle and withdrawn from productive circulation. Unlike saving directed toward investment, hoarding sterilizes capital, removing it from the economic circuit.

> …It is not the hoarding of coin or other valuables… (011-005)

Say distinguishes hoarding sharply from productive saving: one feeds industry, the other starves it.

## Explanation

### Hoarding as Unproductive Capital

In Say's taxonomy of capital uses, hoarding is the most sterile. [[money|Money]] buried in the earth or locked in a strongbox performs no economic function:

> …ny other shape than that of a hoard of specie locked up in a stro… (011-017)

> …ey is obtained with a view to hoard or bury it, the ultimate obje… (015-002)

The hoarder withdraws value from the stream of [[commerce|exchange]]s. This damages not only the hoarder — who earns no return — but also the broader economy, which loses the productive services that the hoarded capital could have funded.

### The Transformation from Hoard to Capital

Say observes a historical progression: as civilization advances, the habit of hoarding gives way to productive investment:

> …But the moment that this hoard was found capable of yielding… (011-018)

The [[invention|discovery]] that money can earn a return — through lending, partnership, or direct enterprise — converts the hoard into capital. This conversion is, for Say, one of the defining features of commercial modernity.

### Hoards and the Money Supply

Hoarded money complicates monetary analysis. The quantity of money in circulation is not simply the total coined — much is locked away:

> …nt is here taken of the money hoarded, which, for the national in… (022-017)

> …f the quantity ; whereas, the hoards in the vaults of the bank ca… (022-009)

Say notes that bank vaults contain large hoards of [[coin|specie]] that, while physically present in the country, are economically inert. Measurements of national wealth must account for this distinction between circulating and hoarded money.

## Historical Context

The critique of hoarding is ancient — Aristotle condemned the accumulation of money for its own sake as unnatural. In the mercantile era, hoarding was seen as especially pernicious because it reduced the visible [[capital|stock]] of precious metals, which mercantilists equated with national wealth.

Say's treatment is more nuanced. He recognizes that hoarding is largely irrational in a commercial economy (since hoarded money earns no return) but acknowledges that it persists in societies with weak property rights or underdeveloped financial institutions. The transition from hoarding to investing is a mark of economic maturity.

## Say's Treatment

Say's analysis of hoarding is concentrated in Chapter XI ("Formation and Multiplication of [[capital|Capital]]") and Chapter XV ("[[vent|Vent]] or [[demand|Demand]] for Products").

In Chapter XI, hoarding is the dark counterpart to frugality. Where frugality channels saving into production, hoarding blocks it:

> …f modern days ? It is not the hoarding of coin or other valuables… (011-005)

In Chapter XV, Say considers the macroeconomic effects. A general propensity to hoard reduces aggregate demand:

> …ey is obtained with a view to hoard or bury it, the ultimate obje… (015-002)

In Chapter XVII, Say notes that [[public-authority|government]] mismanagement can provoke hoarding — when property is insecure, citizens bury their wealth rather than invest it:

> … year is not made good by the hoarded surplus of a preceding one,… (017-098)

Say thus treats hoarding as a symptom of institutional failure as much as a personal vice.
