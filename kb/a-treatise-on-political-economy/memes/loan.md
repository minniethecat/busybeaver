# Loan

## Definition

A loan is the transfer of capital from its owner (the lender) to an [[adventurer|entrepreneur]] or other agent (the borrower) for productive employment, in [[commerce|exchange]] for interest. In Say's frame[[labour|work]], loans are the essential mechanism by which idle capital is mobilized and directed toward productive uses. The loan of capital, as Say describes it, is actively sought by industry: the modern economy "every where solicited the loan of capital, and tempted the holders of it, great or small, by better terms and greater security." (011-017)

## Explanation

### Loans and Capital Mobilization

Say emphasizes that loans serve a critical function in a commercial economy: they allow capital to flow from those who possess it but lack the skill or inclination to employ it productively, to those who have [[adventurer|entrepreneur]]ial talent but insufficient capital. This process is fundamental to economic growth. Without the loan mechanism, capital would remain idle in the form of hoarded [[coin|specie]], yielding no benefit to anyone.

### The Security of Loans

The willingness to lend depends on the security of the loan. Say describes the landholder who obtains a loan by mortgaging his land, "presenting the amplest security." (022-023) This connects loans to the broader system of property rights — without secure title and legal enforcement, the loan market cannot function. The mortgage thus emerges as a key instrument for converting landed property into circulating capital.

### Loans and the Multiplication of Capital

Say traces how loans can be layered and renewed, creating a multiplying effect on the effective [[stock|capital stock]]. He describes a process where "the first loan being paid with a second" (022-026) allows capital to be continuously circulated. The notes in which loans are made become themselves instruments of circulation: "From that moment it performed the office of money until its return to the bank." (022-027)

## Historical Context

In pre-industrial economies, lending at interest was often restricted by usury laws and religious prohibitions. The classical economists, including Say, argued against such restrictions, maintaining that the free market in loans was essential for [[saving|capital accumulation]]. The emergence of banking institutions and national debt markets in the eighteenth century dramatically expanded the scale of lending and transformed it into a systematic feature of commercial society.

## Say's Treatment

Say distinguishes between productive and unproductive loans. A loan employed in production — to purchase [[raw-material|raw materials]], hire labour, or improve land — augments the national capital and generates the interest from a genuine surplus. Loans to [[public-authority|government]] for unproductive expenditure, by contrast, consume capital without reproduction. Say is particularly concerned about "excessive [[tax|taxation]], loans, over-grown establishments" (017-110) as drains on the [[capital|productive capital]] of a nation.

Say's analysis of loans is integrated with his broader theory of capital and interest. The interest paid on a loan reflects the productive power of the capital transferred; it is not an artificial imposition but a natural return to the service of providing capital for industry.
