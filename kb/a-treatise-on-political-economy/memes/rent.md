## Definition

In Say's *Treatise on [[political-economy|Political Economy]]*, **rent** denotes the annual [[surplus|net produce]] or return yielded by land and other natural agents. Say discusses rent primarily in connection with the productive powers of land (Chapter IV, "[[natural-agents|Natural Agents]]") and the establishment of property rights (Chapter XIV). Rent represents the value contributed by natural agents to production, distinct from the returns to capital and labour.

> A landed estate of 100,000l. brings an annual rent or net produce of 1000l.
>
> (004-005)

## Explanation

Say treats rent as the productive return of natural agents — land, water, mineral deposits, and other resources provided by nature rather than human industry. In his frame[[labour|work]], natural agents are one of the three factors of production alongside capital and industry (labour). Rent is their distinctive remuneration.

The magnitude of rent depends on the productive power of the natural agent and the demand for its products. Say distinguishes between the gross produce of land and its net rent, emphasizing that improvements to land made by human industry — drainage, fertilization, building — become indistinguishable from the natural productive power of the soil over time.

Say's treatment of rent is less developed than that of [[david-ricardo|David Ricardo]], who made rent theory central to his system. Say focuses more on the productive contribution of natural agents than on the distributional implications of rent.

## Historical Context

Rent theory was one of the most contested areas of classical political economy. The Physiocrats had placed land rent at the center of their system as the sole source of *produit net*. [[adam-smith|Adam Smith]] treated rent as one of the three components of price (alongside [[wages|wage]]s and profit). [[david-ricardo|David Ricardo]] developed the most systematic classical theory of rent, linking it to differences in land fertility and the margin of [[agriculture|cultivation]].

Say's contribution to rent theory was modest — he accepted the general classical frame[[labour|work]] while emphasizing the productive role of natural agents rather than the distributional consequences of rent.

## Say's Treatment

Say integrates rent into his broader theory of production. Natural agents, though provided gratuitously by nature, contribute to the value of products; their contribution is measured by rent. The establishment of the right of property makes it possible for landowners to charge rent for access to natural agents, converting what was once free into a source of [[revenue|income]].

In his discussion of [[colony|colonies]] (Chapter XIX), Say examines how abundant land and scarce labour in colonial settings affect rents and the returns to different factors of production.
