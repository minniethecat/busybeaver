# Natural Agents

## Definition

Natural agents are the powers of nature that assist in production without being themselves the product of human industry. "Independently of the aid that industry receives from capital...she avails herself of the agency and powers of a variety of agents not of her own creation, but offered spontaneously by nature." (004-001) These include land, water, sunlight, wind, gravity, chemical processes, and biological growth.

## Explanation

### Gratuitous Assistance

Natural agents are distinct from capital in that they are not created by human industry. They are "offered spontaneously by nature" — their productive power is a gift that humanity appropriates but does not [[manufacturing|manufacture]]. When a field is cultivated, "there is a process performed by the soil, the sun, and the rain" that industry and capital alone could not achieve. (004-001)

### Land as the Primary Natural Agent

Land is the most important natural agent. It provides: (1) space for all productive activities; (2) nutrients and support for agriculture; (3) mineral deposits for extraction; and (4) waterways for transport and power. The productive power of land varies enormously by location, fertility, and accessibility.

### Rent as Payment for Natural Agents

The price paid for the loan of natural agents — specifically land — is called **rent**. (005-001) [[rent|Rent]] reflects the differential productivity of land: more fertile or better-located land commands higher rent because it contributes more to production. The landholder receives this payment not for any productive effort but for allowing industry and capital to employ the natural agent they own.

## Say's Treatment

Say's treatment of natural agents reflects his three-factor theory of production: industry, capital, and natural agents are the three indispensable sources of all production. His emphasis on the **gratuitous** character of nature's contribution distinguishes natural agents from capital (which is accumulated human product) and sets the stage for the classical theory of rent later developed by [[david-ricardo|Ricardo]].

Say defines the productive agency of nature:

> This process I call the productive agency of natural agents. The term natural agents is here employed in a very extensive sense ; it being intended to include, not merely the inanimate objects and powers of nature, but likewise the laws of the physical world, and the motion of the heavenly bodies.
>
> (004-001)
