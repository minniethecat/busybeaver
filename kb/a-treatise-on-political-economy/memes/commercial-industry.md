## Definition

**Commercial industry**, or simply commerce, is the branch of productive industry concerned with the transport and distribution of [[commodity|goods]] from producers to consumers. Say defines it clearly: "it is called commercial industry, or simply commerce" — the activity that brings products "within reach" of those who need them (002-001). Commercial industry is one of Say's three fundamental types of productive activity, alongside agricultural and [[manufacture|manufacturing industry]].

> Commercial industry consists in buying a commodity where it is to be had cheapest, and selling it where it will fetch the most.
>
> (002-001)

## Explanation

Say insists that commerce is genuinely productive, contrary to earlier views that regarded merchants as unproductive intermediaries. The commercial agent adds utility by transporting [[commodity|goods]] across space — converting a product that is abundant and cheap in one location into a scarcer and more valuable commodity in another.

"All the various branches of commercial industry are agents whose agency tends to approximate the product to the consumer" (009-001). This spatial transformation creates real value, no less than the physical transformation performed by [[manufacture|manufacturing]].

Say observes that commercial industry, like [[manufacture|manufacturing]], benefits from subdivision. "The consumer will always buy cheapest, where commercial industry is the most subdivided" (008-009). He gives the example of the village grocer, whose business encompasses many branches of commerce that would each support a specialized merchant in a large city (008-008).

Like other forms of industry, commerce involves risk. "In commercial industry, the risk of experiment" can be considerable, particularly in long-distance [[commerce|trade]] where ad[[demand|vent]]urers must commit capital months in advance (006-009).

## Historical Context

Say's defense of commerce as productive industry was an important contribution to classical economics. The Physiocrats had regarded merchants as a sterile class; even [[adam-smith|Adam Smith]] sometimes wrote ambiguously about the productiveness of [[commerce|trade]]. Say's tripartite classification — agricultural, [[manufacture|manufacturing]], and commercial industry — gave commerce a secure place in the theory of production. The analysis reflects the commercial expansion of Say's era, when European [[commerce|trade]] net[[labour|work]]s extended across the Atlantic and into Asia.

## Say's Treatment

Say devotes an entire chapter (Chapter IX) to commercial industry, examining its various branches and the modes in which they contribute to production. He emphasizes that commercial industry employs capital and labour productively, generating [[revenue|income]] for ad[[demand|vent]]urers and [[wages|wage]]s for [[labour|work]]ers in the same manner as other industries. Say also discusses the distinction between internal and [[commerce|external commerce]], and the circumstances under which nations may benefit from allowing foreign ad[[demand|vent]]urers to conduct their carrying trade (009-009, 009-010).
