# Credit

## Definition

Credit is the ability to obtain [[commodity|goods]], services, or money in the present with a promise of future payment. In commercial practice, credit takes the form of "bills, orders, or letters of credit, payable elsewhere than at the place where they are given" (009-001). Credit instruments allow merchants to conduct transactions without the immediate transfer of coin, reducing the amount of money needed to sustain [[commercial-industry|commerce]].

## Credit and the Circulation of Value

Credit accelerates the circulation of value through the economy. By "the operation of credit" (P02-007), producers can obtain [[raw-material|raw materials]], [[labouring-class|labourers]] can receive advances against future [[wages|wage]]s, and merchants can [[commerce|trade]] across distances without transporting [[coin|specie]]. Credit thus lubricates the wheels of [[commercial-industry|commerce]], enabling a given [[capital|stock]] of money to support a larger volume of transactions.

## Credit and Productive Capital

Credit channels [[saving|savings]] toward productive investment. When a banker extends credit to an [[adventurer|entrepreneur]], the funds are used to purchase tools, materials, and labour — transforming credit into [[capital|productive capital]]. The "extended use of credit" (021-055) multiplies a nation's productive capacity by ensuring that idle funds are put to [[labour|work]]. However, Say cautions that credit must be based on real assets and productive prospects, not mere speculation.

> But it is still a question, whether this frugality, which Smith gives individuals credit for, be not, in the most numerous classes of society, a forced consequence of a vicious political organization.
>
> (011-013)
