# Vent

## Definition

Vent (or demand) for products is the market outlet through which [[commodity|goods]] are [[commerce|exchange]]d. Say's central insight — later known as **Say's Law** or the **Law of Markets** — is that "it is production which opens a demand for products." (015-001) Products are ultimately paid for with other products, not with money. [[money|Money]] is merely the intermediary; the real purchasing power comes from production itself.

## Explanation

### The Principle

Say's Law [[public-authority|state]]s that supply creates its own demand. When a producer brings [[commodity|goods]] to market, they do so not to accumulate money but to [[commerce|exchange]] their product for other products they desire. "The circumstance of the existence of an active [[commerce|external commerce]]...is a very powerful stimulus to internal industry." (009-004) Every act of production simultaneously creates the means and the motive for further [[commerce|exchange]].

### Against the Fear of General Glut

Say formulated this principle in opposition to the fear of "general overproduction" — the idea that an economy could produce more than it could consume. For Say, a general glut is impossible because the very act of producing [[commodity|goods]] creates the [[revenue|income]] with which to purchase other [[commodity|goods]]. Partial overproduction in specific sectors is possible, but generalized depression results from impediments to [[commerce|exchange]], not from an excess of production.

### Practical Implications

If demand originates in production, then policies that restrict production — tariffs, [[monopoly|monopolies]], excessive [[tax|taxation]] — reduce demand rather than increase it. Conversely, policies that liberate production — free [[commerce|trade]], secure property, light regulation — expand both supply and demand simultaneously.

## Say's Treatment

Say's Law is perhaps his most famous and most contested contribution. It became a cornerstone of classical economics, embraced by [[david-ricardo|Ricardo]] and James Mill, and formed the basis for the "Treasury View" in British [[taxation|fiscal policy]]. It was later the target of Keynes's critique in the *General Theory*, which argued that Say's Law fails in conditions of liquidity preference and [[demand|effective demand]] deficiency.

The chapter on [[demand|vent]] — Chapter XV — is one of the most closely read in the *Treatise*: "It is common to hear ad[[demand|vent]]urers in the different channels of industry assert, that their difficulty lies, not in the production, but in the disposal of commodities." (015-001)

Say articulates the fundamental principle that production creates its own demand:

> It is production which opens a demand for products. A product is no sooner created, than it, from that instant, affords a market for other products to the full extent of its own value.
>
> (015-001)
