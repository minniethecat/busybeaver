# Value

## Definition

Value is the quality that makes objects desirable in [[commerce|exchange]]. It arises from the combination of two ingredients: **utility** (the capacity to satisfy human [[scarcity|want]]s) and **difficulty of attainment** (the obstacles that must be surmounted to obtain the object). As Say's translator notes, "to say that an object is possessed of value, is to affirm the presence of two circumstances, utility and difficulty of attainment, the combination of which makes the object...worth some exertion, sacrifice, equivalent, or price." (001-003)

The value of a specific article remains vague and arbitrary until acknowledged by others in [[commerce|exchange]]. "The moment that other persons are willing, for the purpose of obtaining it, to give in [[commerce|exchange]] a certain quantity of other articles, likewise bearing value, the one may then be said to be worth, or to be of equal value with, the other." (001-001)

## Explanation

### Value vs. Price

The quantity of money readily parted with to obtain a thing is called its **price**. Current price at a given time and place is the price the owner is sure of obtaining. [[price|Price]] is the measure of [[commerce|exchange]]able value, but it is only a reliable index of utility "so long only as human dealings are exempt from every influence, but that of the identical utility." (001-005)

### Real vs. Relative Value

Say distinguishes between **real value** (grounded in utility) and **relative or [[commerce|exchange]]able value** (the ratio at which one good [[commerce|exchange]]s for another). Artificial obstacles — monopoly privileges, [[tax|taxes]], regulations — can raise relative value without increasing real value. Such inter[[demand|vent]]ions merely transfer wealth from consumers to privileged parties without creating new utility. (001-006)

## Say's Treatment

Say's theory of value departs from both [[adam-smith|Adam Smith]]'s labour theory and the Physiocratic emphasis on land. For Say, value is fundamentally grounded in **utility** — the subjective capacity of an object to satisfy human [[scarcity|want]]s. However, utility alone is insufficient; water and air have immense utility but no [[commerce|exchange]]able value where they are abundant. It is the **combination** of utility with scarcity or difficulty of attainment that generates value.

Say's translator C.R. Prinsep offers a critical note observing that "no author of note has yet clearly analysed or defined value; which consists of the combination of the two ingredients." (001-007) This two-factor theory — utility plus difficulty — is one of Say's most distinctive contributions.

Say grounds value in utility, departing from both [[adam-smith|Smith]]'s labour theory and Physiocratic doctrine:

> to create objects which have any kind of utility, is to create wealth ; for the utility of things is the ground-work of their value, and their value constitutes wealth.
>
> (001-003)
