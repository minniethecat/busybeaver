# Advances of Capital

## Definition

Advances of capital are the sums that an [[adventurer|entrepreneur]] lays out — in [[wages|wage]]s, [[raw-material|raw materials]], tools, and other inputs — before the productive process yields its returns. They represent the bridging function of capital between the commencement and the completion of production.

> …the common labourer generally advances some portion ; the bricklayer… (005-001)

In Say's formulation, advances are not made only by the capitalist-[[adventurer|entrepreneur]]; even the labourer advances his labour before receiving [[wages|wage]]s. The entire productive system depends on a chain of advances.

## Explanation

### The Entrepreneur's Advances

The [[adventurer|entrepreneur]]'s primary function is to assemble and advance capital. This includes:

- **[[wages|Wages]]**: Payment to [[labouring-class|labourers]] before the product is sold (005-003, 008-012)
- **Raw materials**: Purchase of inputs at the start of the production cycle
- **Tools and machinery**: The durable equipment used in production

> …kings, and make the requisite advances to double the number of workm… (005-003)

Say recognizes that the scale of advances determines the scale of production. Doubling output requires doubling the capital advanced — not merely in fixed plant but in circulating capital.

### Advances and the Production Cycle

[[production|Production]] takes time. Between the initial outlay and the final sale, capital is "locked up" in the form of [[labour|work]]-in-progress. Advances are the measure of this temporal commitment:

> …such division requires larger advances of wages, of raw materials, a… (008-012)

The more specialized the division of labour, the longer the interval between outlay and return, and therefore the larger the advances required.

### Advances and Risk

The [[adventurer|entrepreneur]] who advances capital bears the risk that the product may not sell, or may sell at a loss:

> …rofit, after repaying all his advances. If no individual capitalist … (017-024)

[[profit|Profit]], in Say's analysis, is the reward for successfully recovering advances — plus a surplus. Without the prospect of profit exceeding the value of advances, no [[adventurer|entrepreneur]] would undertake production.

## Historical Context

The concept of "advances" (avances) has deep roots in French economic thought. The Physiocrats — particularly Quesnay — distinguished between avances primitives (fixed capital) and avances annuelles (circulating capital). Say inherited this terminology and adapted it to his own value-and-production frame[[labour|work]].

In British classical economics, the equivalent term is "capital advanced," used by [[adam-smith|Smith]] and [[david-ricardo|Ricardo]]. The concept is central to the classical theory of profit: the rate of profit is the ratio of surplus to capital advanced. Say follows this tradition while giving the concept a distinctively [[adventurer|entrepreneur]]ial emphasis.

## Say's Treatment

Say's treatment of advances is dispersed across multiple chapters, reflecting the concept's pervasiveness in his system:

**In production theory (Chapters I–VII)**: Advances are the practical mechanism by which capital enters production. Every industry, from agriculture to [[manufacture|manufacturing]], operates on the advance-and-return model:

> Ill ON PRODUCTION. advances or capital have paid for the … (010-007)

**In the theory of profit (Chapter XI)**: The recovery of advances plus a surplus defines profit:

> …mnify the adventurers for the advances and risk incurred. Any thing … (017-090)

**In immaterial production (Chapter XIII)**: Even intellectual labour requires advances. A physician must advance the cost of education before earning fees; a lawyer advances time and study:

> … study can take place without advances of capital. Before the advice… (013-006)

**In commercial policy (Chapter XVII)**: Government regulations that delay the return of advances — such as prohibitions, tariffs, or inefficient tax collection — harm production by extending the advance period:

> …retted, than the sacrifice of advances and consumption, that must be… (017-007)
