## Definition

In Say's *Treatise on [[political-economy|Political Economy]]*, a **commodity** is any product that possesses [[commerce|exchange]]able value and can be brought to market. The commodity is the basic unit of [[commercial-industry|commerce]] — "the particular commodity is here only the means resorted to" for effecting [[commerce|exchange]]s (001-006). Say emphasizes that value, not the physical substance of the commodity, is the essential economic attribute: value "consists not in this or that commodity or substance, but in its value" (010-002).

## Explanation

### Commodity and Value

Say distinguishes between the physical object and its economic significance. A commodity is valuable not because of its material properties but because human industry has made it useful and [[commerce|exchange]]able. "The [[merchant|trader]] gives to the commodity, whereby he adapts to our use" (002-006) — value is imparted through the productive process.

### Exchange and Preference

Commodities circulate through a system of mutual desire: "each person desire to have the commodity of his neighbour in preference to his own" (002-008). This mutual [[scarcity|want]] drives the entire mechanism of [[commerce|exchange]], making the commodity the pivot around which commercial society turns.

### Circulation and Speculation

A commodity "depressed in value by temporary circumstances" may be withdrawn from circulation by speculators who anticipate its recovery (009-008). Say also notes the role of [[adventurer|entrepreneur]]s in introducing "a virgin commodity of foreign growth into an untried market" (006-009), expanding the range of goods available to consumers.

### Commodities as Capital

Say draws attention to the fact that commodities in circulation function as a form of capital: wheat serves as "the staple commodity of Holland, East and West India [[commerce|trade]]" (002-013), demonstrating how a single product can become the foundation of extensive commercial net[[labour|work]]s.

## Historical Context

Say's treatment of the commodity reflects the maturation of classical political economy in the early 19th century. Building on [[adam-smith|Adam Smith]]'s analysis of the division of labour and the market mechanism, Say developed a more systematic theory of how commodities acquire and transmit value. His emphasis on the commodity as a bearer of [[commerce|exchange]]able value, rather than a mere physical good, anticipates later developments in value theory.

## Say's Treatment

For Say, the commodity is the visible form in which value travels through the economic system. His analysis connects the commodity to every major theme of the *Treatise*: production (where commodities are created), [[commerce|exchange]] (where they circulate), consumption (where they are destroyed), and capital (where they are accumulated for productive use). The commodity is not merely a thing — it is value in motion.
