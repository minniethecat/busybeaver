## Definition

In Say's *Treatise on [[political-economy|Political Economy]]*, **speculation** or "the [[commerce|trade]] of speculation" is a distinct branch of [[commercial-industry|commerce]] defined by the purchase of [[commodity|goods]] with the intention of reselling them later at a higher price, profiting from temporal or spatial price differences rather than from any transformation of the commodity itself. Say defines it as "[[commercial-industry|commerce]], called the [[commerce|trade]] of speculation, which consists in the purchase of [[commodity|goods]] with a view to their resale at a future opportunity" (009-007), carefully distinguishing it from productive industry.

## Explanation

### Speculation vs. Productive Commerce

Say draws a sharp distinction between productive commercial activity and speculation. Productive [[commercial-industry|commerce]] "adds a value to the commodity, whereby he adapts to our use" (002-006); the speculator adds nothing to the commodity itself but [[profit|profits]] from market timing. "The [[commerce|trade]] of speculation, as we have before observed" (016-002), is a necessary but potentially destabilizing element of commercial society.

### The Role of the Speculator

Despite his reservations, Say acknowledges that speculation serves economic functions. Speculators "withdraw from circulation a commodity depressed in value" (009-008) when prices are low, supporting demand, and release it when prices rise. They absorb risk that other market participants wish to avoid. However, "in times of jobbing and speculation, when the sudden fluctuations of prices" (016-003) become extreme, speculation can amplify rather than dampen volatility.

### Good vs. Bad Speculation

Say distinguishes between useful and harmful speculation. "The most commendable kind of speculation" (017-094) is that which is "framed upon the principles of a thorough knowledge of the market" — speculation grounded in genuine commercial intelligence. In contrast, speculation driven by "mere matter of speculation" (004-006) without substantive knowledge of market conditions is likely to be destructive. "It is always a bad speculation to attempt raising the production" (017-004) beyond what the market can absorb.

### Speculation and Government Policy

Say warns that [[public-authority|government]] inter[[demand|vent]]ions often stimulate harmful speculation. When [[public-authority|government]]s manipulate currency, restrict [[commerce|trade]], or grant [[monopoly|monopolies]], they create artificial price differentials that speculators exploit — to the detriment of the productive classes. "The best planned and most useful speculations" (021-048) may be destroyed by monetary instability, demonstrating the interdependence of commercial and monetary systems.

## Historical Context

Say's analysis of speculation reflects his direct experience with the speculative manias of his era: John Law's Mississippi Bubble (1720), the Revolutionary *assignat* speculation, and the commercial disruptions of the [[napoleon|Napoleon]]ic Wars. His nuanced treatment — neither wholly condemning nor uncritically celebrating speculation — distinguishes him from both mercantilist defenders of speculation and physiocratic critics who dismissed all [[commercial-industry|commerce]] as sterile.

## Say's Treatment

Say integrates speculation into his tripartite classification of commercial industry. Unlike the retail and wholesale [[commerce|trade]]s, which perform genuine services of distribution, the speculative [[commerce|trade]] is analytically distinct — it [[profit|profits]] from *price changes* rather than from *value added*. This analytical separation is one of Say's original contributions to the theory of [[commercial-industry|commerce]]. He recognizes speculation as a permanent feature of commercial society while insisting that it must be constrained by sound monetary policy, open markets, and the discipline of knowledge.
