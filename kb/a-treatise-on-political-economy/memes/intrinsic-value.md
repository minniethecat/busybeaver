# Intrinsic Value

## Definition

> …things possessed of real and intrinsic value. Wealth is proportionate to t… ([001-001])

Say defines intrinsic value as the **real and inherent worth** of a thing, derived from its utility and the costs incurred in its production. This stands in contrast to **relative value** ([[commerce|exchange]] value), which is a ratio between two commodities in the market. For Say, intrinsic value is a property of the object itself; relative value is a property of the [[commerce|exchange]] relationship.

## Explanation

### Intrinsic Value and Wealth

Say grounds his definition of wealth in intrinsic value: wealth is not merely the accumulation of things but of things "possessed of real and intrinsic value." A commodity without utility — without capacity to satisfy human [[scarcity|want]]s — cannot form part of wealth, regardless of its [[commerce|exchange]] price. This connects Say's value theory directly to his theory of production and consumption.

### Production as Value Creation

> …s it to be supposed, that its intrinsic value has received no accession by … ([009-002])

[[production|Production]], in Say's frame[[labour|work]], **adds intrinsic value** to [[raw-material|raw materials]]. Each stage of industry — agriculture, [[manufacturing|manufacture]], [[commercial-industry|commerce]] — confers additional utility on the product, and this additional utility constitutes an accession of intrinsic value. The price paid at each stage reflects this accumulated value.

### Intrinsic Value of Money

The distinction between intrinsic and relative value is central to Say's monetary theory. Metallic money possesses intrinsic value as a commodity; [[bank-note|paper-money]], by contrast, is "altogether destitute of intrinsic value" and circulates solely by con[[demand|vent]]ion and limitation of quantity.

### Coinage and Intrinsic Value

> …e at a higher rate than their intrinsic value as bullion; and the dollars … ([021-033])

When coin passes at a higher rate than its intrinsic value as [[precious-metals|bullion]], the difference represents the **value added by the stamp** — the sovereign's certification of weight and fineness. This is not an increase in intrinsic value but a convenience premium. Say warns that tampering with this relationship through debasement constitutes national bankruptcy.

## Historical Context

The distinction between intrinsic and relative value was a central preoccupation of classical political economy. [[adam-smith|Adam Smith]] distinguished "value in use" from "value in [[commerce|exchange]]"; [[david-ricardo|David Ricardo]] sought an invariable measure of value. Say's contribution is his emphasis on **utility as the foundation of intrinsic value**, placing him closer to the French utility tradition (Condillac, [[turgot|Turgot]]) than to the English labour theory of value.

## Say's Treatment

Say's treatment of intrinsic value bridges two traditions. From the physiocrats and [[adam-smith|Smith]], he inherits the concern with real versus nominal magnitudes. From the French utility school, he derives the insight that value ultimately rests on the capacity to satisfy human [[scarcity|want]]s. His application of the intrinsic/relative distinction to monetary theory — analysing debasement, [[bank-note|paper-money]], and bankruptcy through this lens — is original and analytically productive.
