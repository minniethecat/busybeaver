# Agriculture

## Definition and Role

Agriculture is the cultivation of the soil to produce food, fibre, and other [[raw-material|raw materials]]. In the history of economic thought, agriculture held a privileged position: the Physiocrats argued that only agriculture generated a *produit net* (net product) beyond the costs of production. Say acknowledges this tradition but contests it, noting that "the [[profit|profits]] of [[manufacture|manufacturing industry]] are consumed in the same way as those of agriculture" (002-006), rejecting the notion that [[manufacturing|manufacture]] is sterile.

## Agriculture and National Wealth

Say argues that a nation's prosperity does not depend on the predominance of any single sector. "It matters little whether a nation derives more of its wealth from [[manufacturing|manufacture]] than from agriculture" (002-011). What matters is the total productive capacity and the efficient allocation of resources across sectors. A country that "confines her industry to agriculture, though she might be at the same time possessed of [[manufacture|manufacturing]] advantages" (002-013) may forgo substantial wealth.

## Industry, Capital, and Land

Agriculture requires the combined agency of human industry, capital, and natural agents — particularly land. The distinctive feature of agriculture is its heavy reliance on natural agents: soil fertility, climate, and biological processes. These natural agents, which "labour without remuneration" in Say's phrase, make agriculture uniquely productive in physical terms, though not exclusively so in value terms.

> Of the three branches of industry, agriculture is the one that admits division of labour in the least degree.
>
> (008-011)
