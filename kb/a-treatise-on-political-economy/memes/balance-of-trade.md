## Definition

In Say's *Treatise on [[political-economy|Political Economy]]*, the **balance of [[commerce|trade]]** is the con[[demand|vent]]ional accounting measure by which mercantilist writers attempted to determine whether a nation was gaining or losing wealth through its foreign [[commercial-industry|commerce]]. Say devotes sustained critical attention to "what is technically called, a favorable balance of [[commerce|trade]]" (017-011), arguing that the concept is fundamentally misguided and that policies aimed at achieving a favourable balance are "altogether illusory" (017-033).

## Explanation

### The Mercantilist Doctrine

The balance of [[commerce|trade]] doctrine held that a nation grows richer when it exports more than it imports, receiving the difference in [[coin|specie]] ([[precious-metals|gold and silver]]). This was the theoretical foundation of the navigation acts, tariffs, prohibitions, and colonial [[monopoly|monopolies]] that characterised European commercial policy. The *Treatise*'s table of contents flags this as a topic of central importance: "upon what is called the balance of [[commerce|trade]]" (P03-001).

### Say's Critique

Say dismantles the balance of [[commerce|trade]] doctrine on multiple grounds. First, he argues that the concept mistakes money for wealth. "If it were desirable to have the balance of [[commerce|trade]] always in our favor, yet it is impossible" because the mechanism of international [[commerce|exchange]] is self-correcting (017-028). An inflow of [[coin|specie]] from a favourable balance raises domestic prices, making exports less competitive and imports more attractive — thereby reversing the balance.

Second, the "advantages looked for from the means of a favorable balance of [[commerce|trade]], are altogether illusory" (017-033). What matters is not whether a nation exports more than it imports in monetary terms, but whether its productive resources are employed in the most valuable directions. A nation that restricts imports to achieve a favourable balance merely deprives its citizens of [[commodity|goods]] they desire.

Third, Say examines the supposed "liquidation of a favorable balance of [[commerce|trade]] by an import of [[coin|specie]]" and finds the mechanism self-defeating (017-054). The precious metals, like any other commodity, flow to where they are most valued. An attempt to accumulate them through [[commerce|trade]] surpluses merely bids up domestic prices until the surplus vanishes.

### The Real Basis of Trade

Say redirects attention from the balance of [[commerce|trade]] to the mutual benefit of [[commerce|exchange]]. The true measure of [[commerce|trade]]'s advantage is not whether money flows in or out but whether "the national merchant" acquires [[commodity|goods]] of greater value than those he parts with (022-005). Every voluntary [[commerce|exchange]] benefits both parties; to restrict [[commerce|exchange]] in pursuit of a favourable balance is to sacrifice real wealth for a bookkeeping abstraction.

## Historical Context

The balance of [[commerce|trade]] doctrine dominated European commercial policy from the 16th through the 18th centuries. The Navigation Acts, the [[colony|colonial system]], and the net[[labour|work]] of tariffs and prohibitions all rested on the assumption that a favourable balance was the object of national policy. [[adam-smith|Adam Smith]] had already subjected these doctrines to devastating critique in *The [[wealth|Wealth]] of Nations* (1776), but they retained strong political support.

Say was writing when the mercantilist system was being dismantled — the Continental System, Britain's Orders in Council, and the general disruption of [[commerce|trade]] during the [[napoleon|Napoleon]]ic Wars had demonstrated the costs of commercial restriction. Say's critique was both theoretical and practical: he showed not only that the balance of [[commerce|trade]] doctrine was conceptually flawed but that policies based upon it inflicted real harm on producers and consumers alike.

## Say's Treatment

Say's treatment of the balance of [[commerce|trade]] is one of the most sustained critical analyses in the *Treatise*. He does not simply dismiss the concept — he examines it from every angle, demonstrating its internal contradictions and its practical failures. The balance of [[commerce|trade]], he concludes, is a phantom that has haunted commercial policy for centuries; dispelling it is essential to the establishment of a rational system of international [[commercial-industry|commerce]].

Say's alternative to the balance of [[commerce|trade]] doctrine is the principle of mutual benefit through free [[commerce|exchange]]. Nations, like individuals, profit from [[commerce|trade]] not by accumulating monetary surpluses but by exchanging what they produce most efficiently for what others produce more cheaply. The proper object of policy is not a favourable balance but the removal of "every barrier to the free circulation of products" (017-049).
