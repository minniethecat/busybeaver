## Definition

The **capitalist** is the economic agent who supplies the funds — the accumulated values — that make productive enterprise possible. Say distinguishes the capitalist from both the labourer, who contributes personal exertion, and the landholder, who contributes natural agents. The capitalist's contribution is the advance of capital: "the mechanists and capitalists, who furnish the funds" (007-003) receive a share of the product in return for the productive service their capital performs.

## Explanation

### The Capitalist's Role in Production

In Say's tripartite scheme of production, the capitalist provides one of the three essential productive agencies. [[production|Production]] requires "the profit of proprietor, capitalist, and labourer" (005-001). The capitalist's specific function is to advance the funds that pay for [[raw-material|raw materials]], machinery, and the subsistence of [[labouring-class|labourers]] during the production process. Without this advance, production cannot begin.

### Capitalist and Entrepreneur Distinguished

Say does not always sharply distinguish between the capitalist and the [[adventurer|entrepreneur]] — a distinction that would become central in later classical economics. In his discussion of the English cotton industry, he refers to "the English capitalist" who "finds his account" in combining machinery with labour (005-005). At other points, however, he notes that the rewards of the capitalist are "quite distinct from" other shares of the product (021-062), recognizing that the function of capital [[supply|provision]] is analytically separable from management and risk-bearing.

### The Inactive Capitalist

Say identifies a class of "inactive capitalist" or "un[[capital|productive capital]]ist" — the rentier who lives on the interest of [[capital|accumulated wealth]] without engaging in productive direction. He observes that "the landholder, or inactive capitalist, gives an entertainment to his friends; the wealth consumed is so much value lost to the community" (015-015). This distinction between the active capitalist who directs production and the inactive capitalist who merely consumes interest anticipates the later critique of the rentier class.

### Capitalists and Property Rights

The security of the capitalist's property is essential to the functioning of the economic system. Say argues that "the property of the capitalist is violated, by prohibiting particular modes of employing capital" (014-002). Any restriction on how capital may be invested reduces the return the capitalist can expect, thereby discouraging accumulation. Even in extreme cases, Say maintains "the right of the labourer is no higher than the right of the capitalist" (014-003) — both are entitled to the full value of what they contribute to production.

## Historical Context

The figure of the capitalist emerged as a distinct economic category during the Industrial Revolution. Earlier economic thought had recognized the role of the merchant and the master-[[manufacturing|manufacture]]r but had not isolated capital-[[supply|provision]] as a distinct economic function. Say, writing in the early 19th century, captures the transitional moment when the capitalist was becoming a recognizable social type — the owner of funds who need not personally manage production to receive a return. The concept would be further developed by [[david-ricardo|Ricardo]], Marx, and the classical tradition.

## Say's Treatment

Say's treatment of the capitalist reflects his broader theory of distribution. Each productive agent — landholder, capitalist, and labourer — receives a share of the product proportional to the productive service rendered. The capitalist's reward is interest (and, when combined with [[adventurer|entrepreneur]]ial direction, profit). Say does not develop a systematic theory of exploitation or class conflict; instead, he sees the capitalist as performing a necessary productive function, without which "the mechanists and capitalists, who furnish the funds" (007-003) would have no incentive to accumulate. His later observation that "the gains, accruing to the capitalist, are quite distinct from" labour's share (021-062) opens the door to the distributional analysis that later economists would pursue.
