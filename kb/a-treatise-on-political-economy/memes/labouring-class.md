## Definition

The labouring class, in the frame[[labour|work]] developed by Say, comprises those members of society who earn their subsistence through manual labour performed for [[wages|wage]]s. They are distinguished from capitalists (who live on the returns to capital) and landowners (who live on rent), forming the third great economic class of classical political economy.

> With regard to the labouring class, the incapacity for any other than their accustomed occupation is a direct consequence of the extreme subdivision of labour. (008-016)

Say's treatment of the labouring class combines analytical rigour — examining the economic forces that determine [[wages|wage]]s, employment, and living standards — with a concern for the human welfare of those whose livelihoods depend on the sale of their labour.

## Employment and the Division of Labour

The division of labour, while increasing aggregate productivity, has ambivalent effects on the labouring class. On one hand, specialization increases the output per [[labour|work]]er, creating the potential for higher real [[wages|wage]]s. On the other hand, the extreme subdivision of tasks narrows the [[labour|work]]er's skill set, making him dependent on a single employer and vulnerable to displacement.

A [[labour|work]]er who has spent his entire career performing a single specialized operation cannot easily transfer to another occupation. When demand for his particular product declines, or when machinery renders his skill obsolete, he faces prolonged unemployment and immiseration.

> The labouring classes experience a want of work; the sources of profit are dried up. (015-019)

This vulnerability is structural, not cyclical. The very specialization that makes the [[labour|work]]er productive also makes him fragile. Say does not propose to reverse the division of labour — it is the engine of prosperity — but he recognizes its social cost.

## Machinery and the Labouring Class

The introduction of machinery generates fears of technological unemployment that Say addresses directly. Critics argued that every machine displaced [[labour|work]]ers, reducing the demand for labour and depressing [[wages|wage]]s. Say acknowledges the short-run displacement effect but argues that machinery ultimately benefits the labouring class through lower prices and expanded production.

> There is no permanent suffering to the poorer and labouring classes to be apprehended; for in the long run, machinery multiplies the products upon which their subsistence depends. (007-008)

The mechanism operates through price reduction. [[machinery|Machinery]] lowers the cost of producing [[commodity|goods]], which lowers their price, which expands the market for those [[commodity|goods]], which increases total production, which increases the demand for labour in machine-making, machine-operating, and related [[commerce|trade]]s. The displaced hand-weaver may suffer, but his children find employment in the expanding sectors.

This argument — now standard in economics — was advanced by Say as a rebuttal to the Luddite impulse to destroy machinery in defence of employment.

## Apprenticeship and the Labouring Class

Say identifies [[apprentice|apprenticeship]] restrictions as a significant burden on the labouring class. Compulsory [[apprentice|apprenticeship]] delays the age at which young [[labour|work]]ers can earn independent [[wages|wage]]s, reduces labour mobility across [[commerce|trade]]s, and restricts the supply of skilled labour to the benefit of established masters.

> Were apprentices bound out a year later, and their term of servitude shortened by a year, the condition of the labouring class would be materially improved. (017-070)

The reform of [[apprentice|apprenticeship]] — reducing the mandated term and removing the [[corporation|guild]]-enforced barriers to entry — would increase both the lifetime earnings of labouring-class [[labour|work]]ers and the efficiency of labour allocation across the economy.

## Subsistence and Wages

Say does not develop a formal subsistence [[wages|wage]] theory of the kind later associated with [[david-ricardo|Ricardo]] and Marx. But he recognizes that the [[wages|wage]]s of common labour are constrained by the cost of subsistence. When the price of necessaries rises — whether through harvest failure, [[commerce|trade]] restriction, or [[tax|taxation]] — the real [[wages|wage]] falls, and the labouring class suffers.

> Foreign grain could be sold in England at a price much below that of English grain, were it not for the exorbitance of her taxation. (017-107)

[[exchange|Trade]] restrictions on grain imports — the Corn Laws — artificially raised the price of bread, the principal item in the labouring-class budget. The burden of protection fell disproportionately on those least able to bear it.

The condition of the labouring class serves, in the analysis advanced by Say, as a barometer of economic policy. Policies that raise the price of necessaries, restrict labour mobility, or suppress [[wages|wage]]s through monopoly and privilege degrade the condition of the labouring class. Policies that expand production, reduce [[commerce|trade]] barriers, and protect property rights improve it.
