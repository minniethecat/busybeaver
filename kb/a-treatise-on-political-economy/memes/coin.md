## Definition

In Say's *Treatise on [[political-economy|Political Economy]]*, **coin** (or coinage) is metallic money — typically gold, silver, or copper — that has been stamped by [[public-authority|government]] authority to certify its weight and fineness. [[coinage|Coinage]] solves the transaction-cost problem of [[precious-metals|bullion]]: instead of weighing and assaying metal at each [[commerce|exchange]], trading parties can rely on the [[public-authority|government]]'s stamp. "For this reason, a piece of coined gold or silver, mixed with a small portion of alloy" (021-013) circulates at a known value.

## Explanation

### The Utility of Coinage

Say devotes considerable attention to "the utility of coinage; and of the charge of its execution" (P03-002). [[coinage|Coinage]] provides three essential services: it certifies weight, guarantees fineness, and facilitates counting. Without coinage, every transaction would require the tedious and uncertain process of assaying metal — a severe impediment to [[commercial-industry|commerce]].

### Coinage and Government

Governments typically monopolize the right of coinage. Say discusses the French practice: "in the standard of the silver coinage of France" (011-015), alterations by [[public-authority|government]] edict could change the nominal value of coin relative to [[precious-metals|bullion]]. He warns that [[public-authority|government]]s may abuse this power by debasing the currency — reducing the precious metal content while maintaining the nominal value.

### Seignorage

Say analyzes the practice of "seignorage upon the coinage of [[precious-metals|gold and silver]]" (017-057) — the fee charged by the mint for converting [[precious-metals|bullion]] into coin. He argues that a moderate seignorage is justified by the genuine service of certification, but excessive charges constitute a tax on [[commercial-industry|commerce]] and may drive [[precious-metals|bullion]] to foreign mints.

### Coin vs. Paper

The relationship between coin and paper money is a central theme of Say's monetary analysis. While coin derives its value primarily from its metallic content, paper money — even "bills at sight or after date, bank-notes, running credits, write-offs" (015-004) — depends on confidence in the issuer. Say argues that a well-regulated paper currency can supplement coin, but that coin remains the ultimate foundation of the monetary system.

## Historical Context

Say wrote during a period of monetary experimentation and instability. The French Revolution had seen the catastrophic collapse of the *assignats* — paper money theoretically backed by confiscated Church lands. The [[napoleon|Napoleon]]ic era brought further disruption to European monetary systems. In this context, Say's careful analysis of coinage represents a plea for monetary stability grounded in metallic value.

## Say's Treatment

Say treats coin as a particular case of his general theory of value. Coin is valuable not because the [[public-authority|government]] says so, but because it contains a commodity (precious metal) that commands value in [[commerce|exchange]]. The [[public-authority|government]]'s stamp adds informational value — certifying what the coin contains — but does not create value *ex nihilo*. This position aligns Say with the [[precious-metals|bullion]]ist camp in the great monetary debates of his era, against those who believed that [[public-authority|government]] authority alone could sustain a currency's value.
