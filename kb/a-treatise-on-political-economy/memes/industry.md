# Industry

## Definition

Industry is human agency applied to the business of production. It consists of three distinct operations: (1) the scientific study of natural laws relevant to a product; (2) the application of that knowledge to a useful purpose — the [[adventurer|entrepreneur]]ial function; and (3) the manual execution of the productive task. All three operations are productive because all "concur in the creation of a product." (007-001)

## Explanation

### The Three Operations

1. **Scientific Knowledge**: Before a lock can be made, one must understand the properties of iron, the methods of extracting and refining ore, and the techniques of shaping metal. "The first step towards the attainment of any specific product is the study of the laws and course of nature regarding that product." (006-001)

2. **Entrepreneurial Application**: Knowledge must be directed toward a useful purpose — the judgment that a particular form of metal will serve to secure a door. This is the function of the ad[[demand|vent]]urer or master-[[manufacturing|manufacture]]r.

3. **Manual Execution**: The physical labour of forging, filing, and assembling the components. Though often considered the entirety of "labour," this is only one of industry's three operations.

### Industry as a Productive Source

Industry is one of the three sources of production, alongside capital and natural agents. The price paid for the loan of industry — for the productive service it renders — is called **[[wages|wage]]s**. (005-001) Like capital and land, industry is indispensable: without human agency, neither capital nor natural agents can produce anything.

### Kinds of Industry

Say distinguishes three principal kinds of industry:
- **Agricultural industry**: the collection of natural products
- **[[manufacturing|Manufacturing]] industry**: the transformation of [[raw-material|raw materials]]
- **Commercial industry**: the transport and distribution of [[commodity|goods]]

All three concur in production by making utility available to consumers.

## Say's Treatment

Say's elevation of the [[adventurer|entrepreneur]] and the scientist to productive status alongside the manual labourer was a significant [[invention|innovation]]. It broadened the concept of productive labour beyond the physicalist conception of the Physiocrats (who saw only agriculture as productive) and anticipated later theories of [[adventurer|entrepreneur]]ship.

Say distinguishes three branches of industry:

> It is solely by means of industry that mankind can be furnished, in any degree of abundance, with actual necessaries, and with that variety of other objects, the use of which, though not altogether indispensable, yet marks the distinction between a civilized and a savage state.
>
> (002-001)
