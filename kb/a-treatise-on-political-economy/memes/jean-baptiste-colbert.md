# Jean-Baptiste Colbert

## Biography

Jean-Baptiste Colbert (1619–1683) was a French [[public-authority|state]]sman who served as Minister of Finance under King [[louis-xiv|Louis XIV]] from 1665 until his death. Born in Reims to a merchant family, he rose through the ranks of the French bureaucracy to become the architect of French economic policy during the age of [[louis-xiv|Louis XIV]].

## Major Contributions

### Colbertism and Mercantilism

Colbert's economic policy — sometimes called "Colbertism" — was a systematic form of mercantilism. Its key elements included: (1) protective tariffs to shield French [[manufacturing|manufacture]]s from foreign competition (002-012); (2) [[public-authority|state]]-sponsored [[manufacture|manufacturing]] enterprises (the *[[manufacturing|manufacture]]s royales*); (3) infrastructure investment in roads, canals, and ports (017-063); (4) the establishment of trading companies on the Dutch and English model; and (5) regulation of product quality and production methods.

### Administrative Reform

Colbert reformed France's fiscal administration, attempting to rationalize the chaotic system of tax [[agriculture|farming]] and reduce the burden of indirect [[tax|taxation]]. He also invested in the French navy and colonial expansion, viewing [[colony|colonies]] as both sources of [[raw-material|raw materials]] and captive markets for French [[manufacturing|manufacture]]s.

## Relationship to Say

Say references Colbert as an example of the misguided belief that [[public-authority|government]] regulation can improve upon free production. While acknowledging Colbert's administrative energy, Say argues that his policies — protective tariffs, [[public-authority|state]] [[monopoly|monopolies]], production regulations — enriched particular interests at the expense of consumers and the nation as a whole. Colbert represents, for Say, the mercantilist error of confusing the interests of producers with the general welfare.

Say refutes the Physiocratic claim that Colbert's policies ruined France:

> Moreover, it is not true that Colbert ruined France. On the contrary, it is a matter of fact, that under his administration, France emerged from the distress and desolation of two centuries of warfare.
>
> (002-014)


## Key Works

Colbert's economic ideas are expressed in his administrative correspondence and royal ordinances rather than theoretical treatises. His policies are documented in the *Lettres, instructions et mémoires de Colbert* (published posthumously).
