## Definition and Economic Causes

In Say's analysis, smuggling is not a spontaneous criminal impulse but a predictable and rational response to [[public-authority|government]] policies that create a gap between the legal price and the market price of [[commodity|goods]]. When customs [[tax|duties]] are set at prohibitive levels, or when the import or export of certain commodities is entirely forbidden, the profit from evading these restrictions becomes large enough to attract [[adventurer|entrepreneur]]ial activity into illicit channels. Say describes this as [[public-authority|government]] inadvertently creating "a new class of crime, that of smuggling; whereby an action, wholly innocent in itself, is converted into a criminal offence by positive enactment" (017-051).

This framing is characteristic of Say's approach to economic policy: he analyzes the incentives that laws create rather than the intentions behind them. The smuggler, in Say's account, is responding to the same profit motive that drives legitimate [[commercial-industry|commerce]] — the difference is that [[public-authority|government]] policy has placed certain [[commodity|goods]] on the wrong side of the law. The solution is not more vigorous enforcement but a reform of the [[tax|duties]] that make smuggling profitable.

## Smuggling and Trade Prohibitions

Say devotes particular attention to the relationship between [[commerce|trade]] prohibitions and smuggling. When a [[public-authority|government]] prohibits the import of a commodity that consumers desire, it does not eliminate the demand — it merely drives the [[commerce|trade]] underground. "Smuggling or contraband [[commerce|trade]], which, in all countries loaded with excessive [[tax|duties]], is carried on to an immense extent" (017-086), represents the market's resistance to artificial scarcity. The same merchants who would happily pay moderate [[tax|duties]] and [[commerce|trade]] openly become criminals when the law makes open [[commerce|trade]] impossible.

The economic consequences are severe. First, the [[public-authority|government]] loses the revenue it could have collected through moderate [[tax|duties]] — the contraband [[commerce|trade]] pays no tax. Second, the illicit [[commerce|trade]] operates without the protections of law: contracts are enforced by violence rather than courts, and capital is employed in evasion rather than production. Third, the moral boundary between lawful and unlawful [[commercial-industry|commerce]] is blurred, eroding respect for the law in general.

## Enforcement and Its Limits

Say is skeptical of the efficacy of enforcement as a remedy for smuggling. Because the [[profit|profits]] of evasion rise with the severity of the [[tax|duties]], increased enforcement only intensifies the cat-and-mouse game without addressing the underlying incentive. "As smuggling cannot be pre[[demand|vent]]ed entirely" (017-107), the sensible policy is to reduce the [[tax|duties]] to a level where the cost of evasion exceeds its benefit. When the legal price and the smuggler's price converge, the illicit [[commerce|trade]] withers for lack of profit.

Say proposes a principle that anticipates modern optimal-tax theory: the rate of duty should be set at the level that maximizes net revenue, taking into account both the direct yield of the tax and the indirect losses from smuggling, enforcement costs, and the distortion of [[commerce|trade]]. A moderate duty broadly applied yields more revenue and less social harm than a prohibitive duty that drives [[commercial-industry|commerce]] underground.

## Smuggling and Public Revenue

The fiscal dimension of smuggling is central to Say's analysis. He treats the smuggler not as a hero of free [[commerce|trade]] but as a symptom of pathological [[taxation|fiscal policy]]. The [[public-authority|government]] that imposes prohibitive [[tax|duties]] is effectively taxing its own enforcement capacity — spending resources to suppress a [[commerce|trade]] that would generate revenue if legally permitted at moderate rates. The smuggler, meanwhile, "should pay the insurance of the risk to the [[public-authority|state]]" (017-107) — that is, the risk premium that smuggling entails, which could instead be captured as [[revenue|public revenue]] through a well-designed tariff.

> Smuggling is a new class of crime, created by the legislature itself; for an action, wholly indifferent before the enactment of a prohibitory law, is thereby converted into a criminal offence.
>
> (017-051)
