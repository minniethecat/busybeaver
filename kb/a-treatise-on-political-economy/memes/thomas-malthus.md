# Thomas Malthus

## Biography

Thomas Robert Malthus (1766–1834) was an English economist, demographer, and Anglican clergyman. He served as Professor of History and [[political-economy|Political Economy]] at the East India Company College at Haileybury — the first academic chair in political economy in England.

## Major Contributions

### Population Theory

Malthus's *Essay on the Principle of [[population|Population]]* (1798) (011-004) argued that population, when unchecked, grows geometrically while food supply grows only arithmetically. The resulting pressure of population on resources leads to misery and vice unless checked by "moral restraint." This thesis profoundly influenced both economic theory and public policy, earning economics the label "the dismal science."

### Theory of Effective Demand

In his later [[labour|work]], Malthus challenged [[vent|Say's Law]], arguing that a general glut of commodities was possible if [[demand|effective demand]] was insufficient. While saving is necessary for [[saving|capital accumulation]], excessive saving could depress consumption and lead to economic stagnation. This "underconsumptionist" argument anticipated elements of Keynes's critique of classical economics.

## Relationship to Say

Say engaged Malthus's [[labour|work]] critically but respectfully. They corresponded directly (009-003) (Say published *Lettres à M. Malthus* in 1820) and debated the law of markets, with Say insisting that general overproduction was impossible and Malthus arguing that it was a real danger. Say's translator notes Malthus's positions at several points, reflecting the ongoing intellectual [[commerce|exchange]] between the French and English schools of political economy.

Prinsep evaluates Malthus's essay on political economy:

> The later Essay of Malthus is equally deficient in arrangement, as well as vague and inconclusive : and he seems so sensible himself of these failings, that he has offered it rather as a commentary than a text.
>
> (P02-002)
