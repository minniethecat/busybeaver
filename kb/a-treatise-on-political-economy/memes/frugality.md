# Frugality

## Definition

Frugality, in classical political economy, is the disposition to restrain immediate consumption in favour of saving and productive investment. It is the personal virtue that underlies [[saving|capital accumulation]] at the social level.

> …the prevalent frugality of the people at large… (011-011)

> …frugality, which Smith gives individual… (011-013)

Say treats frugality not merely as private temperance but as a collective economic force — the mechanism by which resources are diverted from present gratification toward future production.

## Explanation

### Frugality and Capital Formation

In Say's frame[[labour|work]], capital originates from saving, and saving originates from frugality. The frugal individual consumes less than their [[revenue|income]] and directs the surplus — through investment — into productive channels. Frugality is thus the behavioural foundation of economic growth.

> …consumption and frugality have increased both together… (011-014)

Say observes a seeming paradox: frugality and consumption rise together. As [[capital|productive capital]] grows through frugality, total national output increases, enabling higher absolute consumption alongside a continued habit of saving. Restraint at the individual level expands the total economic pie.

### Frugality Across Social Classes

Say extends the virtue of frugality beyond the wealthy. He notes that the lower and middle classes also practice it:

> He expresses his belief, that frugality is more generally prevalent n… (011-016)

The diffusion of frugal habits across all ranks of society contributes to the "national spirit of frugality" (022-020), which Say ranks among the most important causes of national wealth.

### Frugality as Remedial Force

When economic distress arises — whether from war, misdirected policy, or commercial crisis — Say points to frugality as the corrective:

> …which are only to be remedied by frugality, intelligence, activity, and … (015-020)

Frugality here appears as one of a quartet of virtues, alongside intelligence, activity, and (implicitly) probity, that together restore economic health.

## Historical Context

The classical economists — [[adam-smith|Smith]], [[david-ricardo|Ricardo]], [[thomas-malthus|Malthus]], and Say — all grappled with the tension between consumption and saving.

[[adam-smith|Smith]] celebrated parsimony as the immediate cause of the increase of capital. [[thomas-malthus|Malthus]] worried that excessive saving could produce a general glut. Say, while aligned with [[adam-smith|Smith]] on the productive virtue of saving, maintained that well-directed consumption was equally essential, since products are ultimately [[commerce|exchange]]d for products.

The concept of frugality in classical economics is closely tied to the broader debate over [[vent|Say's Law]] (the law of markets). If supply creates its own demand, then frugality — by expanding productive supply — cannot cause a permanent deficiency of demand. Say's position is thus an optimistic one: frugality is always beneficial in the long run.

## Say's Treatment

Say devotes substantial attention to frugality in Chapter XI ("Formation and Multiplication of [[capital|Capital]]"). He treats it as a national character trait with measurable economic consequences:

> …To the frugality exerted in the accumulation o… (017-032)

> …cumulated by her industry and frugality, and lastly, the habitual exe… (017-072)

Say distinguishes his view from [[adam-smith|Smith]]'s in one important respect: whereas [[adam-smith|Smith]] sometimes treats frugality as primarily a private virtue of the capitalist class, Say sees it as a broad social phenomenon. The "improvement in frugality" (011-017) that Say observes in his own era he attributes to the general advance of industry and intelligence, not to any single class.

> …For this improvement in frugality, we are indebted to the advances of industry… (011-017)

In Chapter XIX, Say also notes the relationship between colonization and frugality:

> …have a tendency to extend,— frugality to reduce, consumption… (019-008)

Colonies, by opening new fields for investment, encourage saving; frugality, by restraining expenditure, channels capital toward those productive outlets.
