# Colony

## Definition and Role

A colony, in classical political economy, is a territorial settlement established by a mother-country, governed under a colonial system that regulates its [[commercial-industry|commerce]] and industry. Say distinguishes between the colonial system of the ancients — where colonies were "founded by expatriated citizens, carrying with them the manners, arts, and civil institutions of the parent [[public-authority|state]]" — and the modern colonial system, which imposes exclusive commercial privileges on the mother-country (019-001).

## Colonial Commerce and Trade Restrictions

The defining economic feature of modern colonies is the restriction of [[commerce|trade]]. The mother-country compels the colony to "sell their colonial produce exclusively to its merchants" and purchase [[manufacturing|manufacture]]d [[commodity|goods]] solely from the mother-country (019-012). This monopoly, while profitable to the merchants of the ruling nation, limits the colony's access to wider markets. Say observes that such "commercial intercourse between the colony and the mother-country, in respect to all other nations, is completely interdicted" (019-011).

## Economic Growth and Capital

Colonies founded on free principles experience rapid economic growth due to the abundance of land and the high rate of return on capital. "The rapid increase of products in colonies, founded upon this plan, would be perfectly inexplicable, if one were not aware of the extraordinary power of capital in newly-settled colonies" (019-002). The fertility of uncultivated soil, combined with the industry of settlers, yields produce that "very soon exceeds the consumption of the colony" (019-003).
