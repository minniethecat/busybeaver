# Division of Labour

## Definition

Division of labour is the separation of the several operations that combine to form a branch of industry among different persons, each specializing in a single operation or a small set of tasks. These operations "are not in general undertaken or performed by the same person; for they commonly require different kinds of talent; and the labour requisite to each is enough to take up a man's whole time and attention." (008-001)

## Explanation

### Levels of Division

Division of labour operates at multiple levels:
1. **Among intellectual pursuits**: The study of nature is shared among chemists, botanists, astronomers, and other classes of philosophers
2. **Among [[manufacture|manufacturing]] [[commerce|trade]]s**: Different [[manufacturing|manufacture]]rs specialize in woollens, pottery, furniture, cottons
3. **Within a single [[commerce|trade]]**: The fabrication of a single product may be split into numerous distinct operations, each performed by a different [[labour|work]]er

### Advantages

The division of labour increases productive power through:
- Greater dexterity from repetition of a single operation
- [[saving|Saving]] of time otherwise lost in passing from one operation to another
- The in[[demand|vent]]ion of machinery suited to specialized tasks

### Disadvantages

Say identifies significant drawbacks:
1. **Intellectual narrowing**: Workers confined to a single repetitive task become "a mere adjective, without individual capacity, independence, or substantive importance" (008-004)
2. **Reduced bargaining power**: Specialized [[labour|work]]ers cannot easily change occupations and "are obliged to accept whatever terms [their] employer thinks fit to impose" (008-004)
3. **Social inequality**: The division of labour concentrates knowledge and power among masters while reducing [[labour|work]]ers to mechanical execution

## Say's Treatment

Say's treatment of division of labour follows [[adam-smith|Adam Smith]]'s famous pin-factory example but adds important qualifications. While [[adam-smith|Smith]] emphasized the productivity gains, Say draws attention to the **human costs** of extreme specialization — the degradation of [[labour|work]]ers' intellectual and social capacities.

Say also connects division of labour to **commercial industry**: the merchant who transports [[commodity|goods]] from where they are abundant to where they are scarce performs a specialized productive function, creating value by making utility available where it is [[scarcity|want]]ed. (009-001)

[[adam-smith|Adam Smith]] first demonstrated the productive power of specialization:

> The celebrated Adam Smith was the first to point out the immense increase of production, and the superior perfection of products referable to this division of labour. He has cited, among other examples, the manufacture of pins ; a manufacture which itself employs a variety of classes of workmen.
>
> (008-001)
