# Interest of Money

## Definition

The interest of money is the price paid for the use of borrowed capital, expressed as a percentage of the principal. In classical political economy, the rate of interest served as a crucial indicator of the abundance or scarcity of capital in a nation. Say, following [[turgot|Turgot]] and [[adam-smith|Smith]], treats a low rate of interest as evidence of plentiful [[saving|capital accumulation]].

> The interest of money was, on the average, lower than it had ever before been, a clear proof of the greater abundance of capital.
>
> (011-016)

## Explanation

### Interest as a Measure of Capital Abundance

Say argues that the prevailing rate of interest reflects the supply of capital relative to the demand for its productive employment. A low rate indicates that capital is abundant and its owners compete to lend it; a high rate signals scarcity. This relationship underlies Say's optimism about European economic progress in the early nineteenth century — declining interest rates across Europe demonstrated growing national wealth.

### Interest and the Capitalist

The rate of interest directly affects the distribution of [[revenue|income]] between capitalists and [[adventurer|entrepreneur]]s. Those branches of production with the most active demand yield the highest return: "yield the best interest of money to the capitalist, the largest [[profit|profits]] to the ad[[demand|vent]]urer." (015-018) The capitalist who lends funds receives interest; the [[adventurer|entrepreneur]] who employs them earns profit after paying interest.

### International Differences in Interest Rates

Say considers the argument that capital should flow from countries with low interest rates to those with high rates: "when the interest of money is lower abroad than at home, it must be advantageous to borrow there for the employment of industry at home." (017-041) However, he cautions that practical obstacles, including legal uncertainty and political risk, often pre[[demand|vent]] such arbitrage from equalizing rates internationally.

## Historical Context

In eighteenth and early nineteenth-century political economy, the rate of interest was a central topic of analysis. The Mercantilists had favoured legal restrictions on interest rates (usury laws), while the Physiocrats and classical economists generally argued for free-market determination. [[turgot|Turgot]]'s theory of interest, linking it to the productivity of capital, was particularly influential. Say endorses this classical position, treating the interest rate as a natural outcome of the supply and demand for capital, not a variable to be politically manipulated.

## Say's Treatment

Say integrates the interest of money into his broader theory of capital and production. For Say, interest is not merely a monetary phenomenon but reflects real productive conditions — the rate at which capital can be profitably employed. In [[colony|colonies]], where capital is scarce relative to land and labour, he notes that the interest of money "is also estimated far too low" (019-007) given the real returns available, suggesting that institutional factors can distort the rate away from its natural level.

Say's analysis differs from [[adam-smith|Smith]]'s in placing greater emphasis on the [[adventurer|entrepreneur]] as the active agent who borrows capital and transforms it into products, paying interest out of the resulting profit. This tripartite division — landowner (rent), capitalist (interest), [[adventurer|entrepreneur]] (profit) — is a distinctive feature of Say's economics.
