## Definition

> Commerce is in fact nothing more than the transport of goods from one place to another. (002-007)

In Say's frame[[labour|work]], transport is not merely a service ancillary to production — it is a **form of production itself**. When a [[merchant|trader]] moves [[commodity|goods]] from a place of surplus to a place of scarcity, they create value just as surely as the farmer who grows wheat.

> The trader adds to the value of a product by its transport from one place to another. (002-006)

## Explanation

### Transport as Productive Industry

Say classifies transport under **commercial industry**, one of the three great branches of human industry alongside agriculture and [[manufacturing|manufacture]]. The transporter employs capital, labour, and natural agents to effect the movement of [[commodity|goods]], participating directly in the creation of value.

This was a significant theoretical claim. Earlier economists, particularly the Physiocrats, had regarded [[commercial-industry|commerce]] as sterile. Say quotes the example of [[commodity|goods]] transported from Marseilles to Paris: "whoever executes the transport of that article adds to its value" (009-002). He insisted that the transporter's [[labour|work]] is productive because it yields a utility that consumers are willing to pay for.

### The Means of Transport

The efficiency of transport depends on infrastructure and vehicles. Roads, canals, ships, and carriages constitute the fixed capital of the transport industry. Improvements in transport infrastructure reduce costs, expand markets, and deepen the division of labour.

> The means and machinery of transport constitute a considerable portion of the national capital. (011-007)

### Transport and the Extent of the Market

[[adam-smith|Adam Smith]] argued that the division of labour is limited by the extent of the market. Transport is the mechanism that extends the market geographically. Products capable of distant transport can reach far larger markets than perishable or bulky [[commodity|goods]], shaping the pattern of international [[commercial-industry|commerce]].

> The transport of products cannot be effected without the employment of capital and labour. (009-003)

## Historical Context

In the early 19th century, transport was undergoing a revolution. Canals, improved roads, and larger sailing vessels were dramatically reducing costs. The steam engine was beginning to be applied to both water and land transport. Say wrote at the cusp of this transformation and recognised its economic significance.

The Physiocratic doctrine that only agriculture was productive had relegated [[commercial-industry|commerce]] to secondary status. Say's insistence that transport is productive was part of a broader revaluation of commercial activity that characterised classical economics.

## Say's Treatment

Say addresses transport in Chapter II and Chapter IX. His treatment is distinctive:

1. **[[value|Value]] creation through place-utility**: Say frames transport as adding value by changing the location of a product, anticipating the formal concept of place-utility.

2. **Transport as the essence of [[commercial-industry|commerce]]**: By reducing [[commercial-industry|commerce]] to transport, Say reveals it as a concrete productive activity subject to the same laws of cost, capital, and return as any other industry.

3. **Risk and the transporter**: The transporter bears risks — shipwreck, spoilage, market fluctuation — and the profit of transport must compensate for these.
