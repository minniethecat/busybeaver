## Definition

The home market is the domestic sphere of commercial [[commerce|exchange]] — the aggregate of transactions occurring within the borders of a nation where domestic products are bought and sold.

Say distinguishes the home market from **foreign markets** along several analytical dimensions: the stability of value measurement, the role in determining money prices, and the effects of [[commerce|trade]] policy on internal economic activity.

> The English labourer seldom travels beyond his own parish; and the manufacturer, in most parts of this island, must be content with the home market only. (006-007)

This observation highlights a structural feature of pre-industrial economies: the limited geographic scope of market access for most producers. The home market was, for the vast majority of economic agents, the only market.

## The Home Market as Value Anchor

Say accords the home market a privileged role in the theory of value. Domestic transactions occur within a unified institutional and monetary frame[[labour|work]], so home market prices provide a more stable measure of value than foreign market prices.

> Money is a more accurate measure of value in the home market; for the whole of it will remain in circulation, and there will be no exportation to disturb the measure. (021-056)

The home market serves as the baseline against which the value of money itself is assessed. The general price level in the home market reflects the relationship between the money supply and domestic transactions, without the complications of international [[commerce|trade]] flows.

## Trade Policy and the Home Market

Protectionist measures — prohibitions, tariffs, bounties — are typically justified as means of defending the home market for domestic producers. Say subjects these arguments to sustained criticism.

[[prohibition|Prohibition]] of foreign [[commodity|goods]] may create a temporary home market advantage for domestic producers of the prohibited article. But consumers pay higher prices, and other domestic producers are harmed as their export markets face retaliatory measures. (017-029)

> It is a powerful inducement to buy in the home market, in the expectation of making a profitable sale in the foreign; but the prohibition of foreign commodities removes this inducement. (017-029)

The home market and the foreign market are complementary, not antagonistic. A nation that restricts imports ultimately restricts its own exports, since foreign nations earning less from exports to the home market have less capacity to purchase the exports of the home nation. (017-110)

## Home Market and the Division of Labour

The extent of the home market limits the division of labour. The principle articulated by [[adam-smith|Adam Smith]] — that the division of labour is limited by the extent of the market — applies with particular force to the home market.

This limitation is most acute in rural areas and smaller nations. The English agricultural labourer, "content with the home market only" (006-007), has limited opportunity for skill specialization. Commercial centres connected to international [[commerce|trade]] routes support a finer division of labour and higher productivity.

## Government and Home Market Prices

Government inter[[demand|vent]]ion can distort home market prices in ways that misallocate resources. Say cites the case of a [[public-authority|government]] purchasing grain and reselling it "at an exorbitant rate in the home market" (018-002).

Infrastructure investments (roads, canals, ports) that expand the effective reach of the home market generate returns by enabling greater specialization and competition. Government inter[[demand|vent]]ions that contract or distort the home market — [[monopoly|monopolies]], price controls, [[commerce|trade]] restrictions — impose costs exceeding any fiscal benefits.
