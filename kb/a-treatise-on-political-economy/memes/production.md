# Production

## Definition

> Production is the creation, not of matter, but of utility. ((001-004))

In Jean-Baptiste Say's *Treatise on [[political-economy|Political Economy]]*, production is defined as the human activity that creates or augments utility in objects. It does not create matter — the mass of the globe cannot be increased or diminished by human means — but rather re-forms existing materials to give them a utility they did not previously possess. Production, in this sense, is the foundation of all wealth.

Say emphasizes that production must not be measured by the length, bulk, or weight of the product, but by the utility it presents. The value created through production is what constitutes wealth: "to create objects which have any kind of utility, is to create wealth; for the utility of things is the ground-[[labour|work]] of their value, and their value constitutes wealth." ((001-003))

## Explanation

### The Three Agents of Production

Production requires the cooperation of three distinct sources:

1. **[[industry|Industry]]** — [[labour|human labour]], skill, and knowledge applied to productive activity
2. **[[capital|Capital]]** — pre-existing products used as tools, materials, and subsistence for [[labour|work]]ers; what Say calls "[[capital|productive capital]]" ((003-001))
3. **[[natural-agents|Natural Agents]]** — the gratuitous assistance of nature, including land, water, sunlight, and physical forces

These three sources are indispensable to the creation of products, though they need not all belong to the same person. An industrious person may lend their industry to another who possesses capital and land; a capitalist may lend funds; a landholder may lease their e[[public-authority|state]]. ((005-001))

### Production vs. Price Manipulation

Say distinguishes real production from artificial price increases. When a [[public-authority|government]] grants monopoly privileges or imposes [[tax|taxes]], the resulting price rise does not constitute production:

> When authority grants to a particular class of merchants the exclusive privilege of carrying on a certain branch of trade...the price of imports is thereby raised, without any accession to their utility or intrinsic value. ((001-006))

True production requires a genuine creation or augmentation of utility: "there is no actual production of wealth, without a creation or augmentation of utility." ((001-006))

### The Three Operations of Industry

All branches of industry, whatever their object, consist of three distinct operations:

1. **Scientific study** — investigation of the laws and course of nature regarding the product
2. **Application** — the [[adventurer|entrepreneur]]ial judgment of how to apply knowledge to a useful purpose
3. **Execution** — the manual labour of fabrication

These three operations — corresponding roughly to the [[labour|work]] of the scientist, the [[adventurer|entrepreneur]], and the labourer — are all productive, each contributing to the final utility of the product. ((006-001))

## Say's Treatment

Say's theory of production is notable for its emphasis on **utility** rather than labour as the source of value. This distinguishes him from [[adam-smith|Adam Smith]], who "appears to have considered the labour expended...to be the ground[[labour|work]], as well as the measure of value." ((001-007))

For Say, what matters is not how much labour was expended, but how much utility was created. This utility-centric view places the [[adventurer|entrepreneur]] and the scientist alongside the manual [[labour|work]]er as productive agents — all three contribute to the final value of [[commodity|goods]].

Say also extends the concept of production beyond material [[commodity|goods]] to include **immaterial products** — services consumed at the moment of production, such as a physician's advice or a musician's performance. These are equally productive as they satisfy human [[scarcity|want]]s. ((013-001))

## Historical Context

Say's emphasis on utility-based production was developed in response to the Physiocrats (who considered only agriculture productive) and in dialogue with [[adam-smith|Adam Smith]]'s labour theory of value. Published in 1803 and revised through four editions, the *Treatise* systematized classical political economy around the central triad of **production, distribution, and consumption** — a frame[[labour|work]] that influenced economic thought throughout the 19th century.
