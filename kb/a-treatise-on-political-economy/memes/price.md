# Price

## Definition

Price is the quantity of money that is readily parted with to obtain a thing. "Current price, at a given time and place, is that price which the owner is sure of obtaining for a thing, if he is inclined to part with it." (001-001) Price is the market expression of value — it measures what others are willing to give in [[commerce|exchange]].

## Explanation

### Price as Measure of Value

Price serves as an index of utility, but only under conditions of free [[commerce|exchange]]. "[[exchange|Exchange]]able value, or price, is an index of the recognized utility of a thing, so long only as human dealings are exempt from every influence, but that of the identical utility; in like manner as a barometer denotes the weight of the atmosphere, only while the mercury is submitted to the exclusive action of atmospheric gravity." (001-005)

### Distortions of Price

When [[public-authority|government]] inter[[demand|vent]]ion — monopoly privileges, [[tax|taxes]], regulations — interferes with free [[commerce|exchange]], price ceases to reflect genuine utility. "When authority grants to a particular class of merchants the [[monopoly|exclusive privilege]] of carrying on a certain branch of [[commerce|trade]]...the price of imports is thereby raised, without any accession to their utility or intrinsic value." (001-006)

The current value of a taxed commodity "is composed of two ingredients, viz. 1. Its real value originating in its utility: 2. The value of the tax." (001-006) The latter component represents a transfer, not a creation, of wealth.

### Price and Production

For Say, the legitimate driver of price differences across locations is the productive activity of [[commercial-industry|commerce]]. When a merchant transports oil from Marseilles to Paris, the price difference (30 to 40 sous) reflects the utility created by making the good available where it is more [[scarcity|want]]ed. This price increment represents genuine production, not mere speculation.

## Say's Treatment

Say's analysis of price is inseparable from his theory of value. He rejects the notion that price can be arbitrarily set or that raising prices through regulation creates wealth. Only the creation of utility — through production, transport, or transformation — justifies a higher price. This principle underlies Say's critique of monopoly, protective tariffs, and other inter[[demand|vent]]ions that raise prices without creating corresponding utility.

Say defines price as the monetary measure of current value:

> The quantity of money, which is readily parted with to obtain a thing, is called its price. Current price at a given time and place, is that which the owner is sure of obtaining for a thing, provided he is willing to part with it at that time and place.
>
> (001-001)
