## Definition

In Say's *Treatise on [[political-economy|Political Economy]]*, **protectionism** denotes the array of [[public-authority|government]] inter[[demand|vent]]ions — tariffs, prohibitions, bounties, and import restrictions — by which the [[public-authority|state]] shields domestic producers from foreign competition. Say condemns these measures as economically destructive: they raise prices for consumers, perpetuate inefficient production, and redirect capital from its most productive uses. "Those who are so importunate for prohibitions or heavy import [[tax|duties]]" seek private advantage at public expense (017-038).

## Explanation

### The Mechanism of Protection

Protectionism operates by artificially raising the price of foreign [[commodity|goods]] so that domestic producers can charge higher prices without improving their methods. The consumer pays the difference. Say observes that "a [[public-authority|government]] cannot, by prohibition, elevate a product beyond its natural price" — that is, protection cannot create value, only redistribute it from consumers to protected producers (017-036). The inevitable result is a net loss to the nation: capital flows into protected industries not because they are productive, but because they are shielded.

### Prohibition as the Extreme Case

Say devotes particular attention to *prohibition* — the absolute exclusion of foreign [[commodity|goods]] — as the most harmful form of protectionism. "The extreme point" of the protective system is reached "by absolute prohibition" (017-047). He cites the French prohibition on printed calicoes as a textbook example: "it is enough for the eternal prohibition of the use of printed calicoes" that domestic [[manufacturing|manufacture]]rs petition for it, regardless of the cost to consumers (017-010). Each prohibition enriches a handful of producers at the expense of the entire consuming public.

### Duties Amounting to Prohibition

Protective [[tax|duties]] can be set so high as to be functionally equivalent to prohibition. A [[public-authority|government]] may "load it with [[tax|duties]] amounting to prohibition, after he has once embarked his capital" — trapping capital in industries that would otherwise fail (014-002). Say argues that such [[tax|duties]] compound the original error: having induced investment in an uncompetitive industry through protection, the [[public-authority|state]] must maintain or increase that protection indefinitely, creating a permanent drain on national wealth.

### Protectionism and Monopoly

Say identifies an intimate connection between protectionism and monopoly. "By means of [[tax|duties]], prohibitions, and bounties, the [[public-authority|government]] creates [[monopoly|monopolies]]" that benefit the few at the cost of the many (017-012). Protected producers "gain by prohibition and monopoly as much in the one capacity as they lose in the other" — they profit as producers from the higher prices that they suffer as consumers (017-042). This asymmetry fuels the political demand for ever more extensive protection.

### The Marseilles Straw Hat Case

Say's most vivid illustration of protectionist logic is the petition of Marseilles hat-makers for "the prohibition of the import of foreign straw hats" (017-045). The petitioners argued that foreign competition would ruin their [[commerce|trade]]. Say's rejoinder is devastating in its simplicity: if French consumers can obtain better hats more cheaply from abroad, the national interest lies in permitting that [[commerce|exchange]], not in forcing consumers to subsidise inferior domestic production.

### The Illusion of National Advantage

Protectionism's political appeal rests on the illusion that restricting imports serves the national interest. Say dismantles this claim: each nation enriches itself by producing what it can produce best and exchanging the surplus for what others produce better — the logic of comparative advantage. "For the protection of domestic agriculture might" benefit landowners, but it comes "at the expense of the consumer" and therefore of the nation as a whole (017-108).

## Historical Context

Say wrote the *Treatise* in the shadow of two protectionist systems: the mercantilism of [[jean-baptiste-colbert|Jean-Baptiste Colbert]] (1619–1683) and the Continental System of [[napoleon|Napoleon]] (1806–1814). [[jean-baptiste-colbert|Colbert]]'s protection of French [[manufacturing|manufacture]]s — "[[jean-baptiste-colbert|Colbert]] ruined France by his protection of [[manufacturing|manufacture]]s" (002-012) — had become the textbook case of protectionism's failure, yet its logic persisted into Say's own time. [[napoleon|Napoleon]]'s blockade of British [[commerce|trade]], which Say witnessed firsthand, demonstrated that protectionism could escalate into economic warfare, impoverishing both sides.

The British Corn Laws (1815–1846) provided the contemporaneous British parallel: agricultural protection that raised food prices for the industrial [[labouring-class|working class]]. Say's arguments fed into the free-[[commerce|trade]] movement that would e[[demand|vent]]ually secure the repeal of the Corn Laws and the Cobden-Chevalier Treaty of 1860, marking the high-water mark of 19th-century [[commerce|trade]] liberalisation.

## Say's Treatment

Say treats protectionism not as a legitimate policy option but as a category error — a confusion of private interest with public good. Every protectionist measure is demanded by a specific producer group that stands to gain; every such measure is paid for by the anonymous consumer who has no organised voice in the political process. "Those who impose these restrictions are usually among the severest sufferers by them" — a paradox that Say explains by the asymmetry between concentrated producer benefits and diffuse consumer costs (017-044).

What distinguishes Say's analysis is its empirical specificity. He does not offer abstract denunciations of "the protective system" but examines specific prohibitions, specific [[tax|duties]], and specific petitions — the Marseilles straw hats, the printed calicoes, the sugar monopoly — and demonstrates in each case how protection transfers wealth from the many to the few. His method is forensic: follow the money, and you will find the beneficiaries of every restriction.

Say's ultimate verdict is that "[[tax|taxes]] and restrictive measures never can be a benefit to any class of the people" (017-053). Protectionism is not merely bad economics; it is a violation of the property right of the consumer to obtain [[commodity|goods]] at the lowest possible price.
