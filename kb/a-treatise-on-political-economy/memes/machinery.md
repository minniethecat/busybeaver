## Definition

> In the employment of machinery, which wonderfully augments the productive power of man, a portion only of the annual produce is consumed in the maintenance of the labourers. (004-002)

Machinery, in Say's usage, encompasses all mechanical contrivances — from the simplest tool to the most complex engine — that multiply the productive power of [[labour|human labour]]. It is a form of fixed capital combined with natural agents and human industry.

> All machinery must be regarded as a method of compelling natural agents to work. (007-002)

## Explanation

### Machinery as a Substitute for Human Labour

The most immediate and visible effect of machinery is to perform [[labour|work]] that would otherwise require human hands. A single machine tended by one [[labour|work]]er can produce what formerly required dozens, freeing labour for other productive employments.

### The Employment Objection

Say directly addresses the charge that machinery destroys employment. He acknowledges short-term displacement but argues the long-run effect is wholly beneficial. Cheaper [[commodity|goods]] expand the market, creating new demand and new employments in other sectors.

> The ultimate effect is wholly advantageous: the product is cheapened, the demand for it increases, and its production affords employment to those very same labourers who were at first thrown out of work. (007-006)

Historical examples — from the printing press to cotton-spinning machinery — demonstrated that total employment expanded after mechanisation.

> The advantage of machinery is not limited to the bare substitution of mechanical for human power; it compels the gratuitous co-operation of natural agents. (007-010)

### Fixed Capital and Depreciation

As fixed capital, machinery is subject to gradual wear and requires replacement. This depreciation must be accounted for in the value of the products it helps create. Different kinds of machinery have different durability, affecting the cost structure of industries that employ them.

## Historical Context

The early 19th century was a period of intense mechanisation, particularly in British textiles. The Luddite movement (1811–1816) expressed the fears of hand-weavers threatened by power looms.

Say's defence of machinery placed him alongside [[adam-smith|Adam Smith]] and [[david-ricardo|David Ricardo]], who argued that [[invention|technological progress]] ultimately raised living standards for all classes. Marx later refined the argument, contending that under capitalism the benefits of machinery accrued disproportionately to capital.

## Say's Treatment

Say's treatment in Chapter VII ("Of [[labour|Labour]], Nature, and Machinery") is notable for its clarity and direct engagement with popular objections:

1. **Machinery compels nature to [[labour|work]]**: A windmill compels the wind, a watermill compels the stream. Machinery channels natural forces that would otherwise be wasted, multiplying human effort without consuming additional resources.

2. **The benefit is universal**: Consumers gain from cheaper [[commodity|goods]]; [[labour|work]]ers gain from expanded employment; capitalists gain from returns. Temporary displacement of labour is an adjustment cost, not a permanent condition.

3. **International competition**: Say notes in Chapter XVII that "English [[commodity|goods]], cheapened by machinery" (017-050) gave Britain a [[commerce|trade]] advantage. Nations refusing's mechanised industry gave it a competitive advantage. Nations refusing machinery would be unable to compete.
