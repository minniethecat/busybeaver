# Exchange

## Definition

Exchange is the act by which two parties each transfer to the other a value they possess in return for a value they desire more. The foundation of all [[commercial-industry|commerce]], [[commerce|exchange]] arises from the fact that no individual produces everything they need: "In a society ever so little advanced in civilization, no single individual produces all that is necessary to satisfy his own [[scarcity|want]]s." (021-001)

## Explanation

### The Basis of Exchange

Exchange is motivated by differences in valuation. Each party values what they receive more than what they give — otherwise [[commerce|exchange]] would not occur. This implies that [[commerce|exchange]] is mutually beneficial, creating value for both parties. The act of [[commerce|exchange]] does not destroy value but transfers it to where it is more highly valued.

### Exchange and Production

Say treats commercial activity — the transport and [[commerce|exchange]] of [[commodity|goods]] — as productive. When a merchant moves oil from Marseilles (where it is worth 30 sous) to Paris (where it is worth 40 sous), "its intrinsic value has received an accession by the transit. That value is positively augmented." (009-001) Exchange and transport are forms of production because they create utility by making [[commodity|goods]] available where and when they are [[scarcity|want]]ed.

### The Role of Money

[[money|Money]] overcomes the limitations of barter by serving as a universally acceptable medium of [[commerce|exchange]]. Rather than requiring a "double coincidence of [[scarcity|want]]s" — each party [[scarcity|want]]ing exactly what the other offers — money allows [[commerce|exchange]] to be split into two separate transactions: selling [[commodity|goods]] for money, then buying other [[commodity|goods]] with money.

## Say's Treatment

Say's treatment of [[commerce|exchange]] reflects his broader conception of production as the creation of utility. Commercial industry — the activity of buying, transporting, and selling [[commodity|goods]] — is productive because it increases the utility of [[commodity|goods]] by placing them within reach of those who desire them. This was a significant departure from Physiocratic doctrine, which considered only agriculture productive.

Say illustrates how transport creates value through the example of wine:

> When Spanish wine is bought at Paris, equal value is really given for equal value : the silver paid, and the wine received, are worth one the other ; but the wine had not the same value before its export from Alicant : its value has really increased in the hands of the trader, by the circumstance of transport, and not by the circumstance, or at the moment, of exchange.
>
> (002-009)
