# Patent of Invention

## Definition

A patent of in[[demand|vent]]ion is a legal grant of exclusive right to exploit a new in[[demand|vent]]ion for a specified period. It creates a temporary monopoly as a reward for [[invention|innovation]], trading present consumer welfare for future productive advance.

> …protected by patents of invention… (006-009)

Say treats patents as an exception to his general condemnation of monopoly — a carefully bounded privilege that, unlike [[commerce|trade]] prohibitions or corporate charters, serves a genuine public purpose.

## Explanation

### The Justification for Patents

The case for patents rests on the peculiar economics of in[[demand|vent]]ion. An idea, once disclosed, can be copied at near-zero cost. Without legal protection, the in[[demand|vent]]or cannot recover the costs of research and development:

> …while the patent remains in force, the patentee has the exclusive privilege… (017-079)

The patent solves this appropriability problem by granting a temporary exclusive right — long enough to reward the in[[demand|vent]]or, short enough to avoid permanent monopoly.

### The Consumer's Burden

Say does not ignore the cost of patents. During the patent term, the consumer pays a monopoly price:

> …during the operation of the patent, be better off than the nation at large… (017-080)

The patentee gains at the consumer's expense. The economic justification is that this temporary loss is more than compensated by the permanent gain from the in[[demand|vent]]ion itself — an in[[demand|vent]]ion that might never have been made without the patent incentive.

### The Expiration Calculus

The patent's value to society depends critically on its expiration. Say recognizes this:

> …the patent expires, and the invention falls into the public domain… (017-081)

Once the patent term ends, the in[[demand|vent]]ion becomes common property. [[competition|Competition]] drives the price down to its natural level, and consumers reap the full benefit. The patent system is thus a bargain between present and future consumers: pay more now, pay less later.

### Patents as Investment Incentive

Say situates patents within his broader theory of capital and investment. The in[[demand|vent]]or — or the capitalist who backs the in[[demand|vent]]or — must advance capital before seeing any return:

> …has been at the expense of a patent for nothing. Thus, the public gains… (017-081)

If the in[[demand|vent]]ion fails, the patent is worthless. If it succeeds, the patent monetizes the success. The system channels capital toward in[[demand|vent]]ive activity by making in[[demand|vent]]ion potentially profitable.

## Historical Context

The patent system in Say's era was still in its formative period. The English Statute of Monopolies (1624) had established the principle that patents should be limited in duration. The French patent system was formalized in 1791, during the Revolution, replacing the old regime's system of royal privileges.

Say wrote at a moment when the distinction between legitimate patents and abusive [[monopoly|monopolies]] was actively contested. The British debate over the renewal of James Watt's steam engine patent (which Parliament extended in 1775) had raised questions about the appropriate term and scope of patent protection. Say's treatment reflects this live controversy.

The broader philosophical context was the Enlightenment's revaluation of in[[demand|vent]]ion and progress. Bacon had called printing, gunpowder, and the compass the three in[[demand|vent]]ions that changed the world; the Industrial Revolution was demonstrating the economic power of in[[demand|vent]]ion on an unprecedented scale. Patents were the legal mechanism that connected the world of ideas to the world of [[commercial-industry|commerce]].

## Say's Treatment

Say addresses patents in two chapters. In Chapter VI ("Operations Common to All Branches of [[industry|Industry]]"), he notes patents as one of the ways human knowledge contributes to production:

> …protected by patents of invention. For all which… (006-009)

In Chapter XVII ("[[government-regulations|Government Regulations]]"), he develops the fuller economic analysis, treating patents as the exceptional case where [[public-authority|government]]-created monopoly may be justified:

**The charter-patent analogy**: Say draws an explicit parallel between corporate charters and patents:

> …the charter is a kind of patent of invention, and confers an exclusive privilege… (017-090)

The analogy cuts both ways. A justified patent resembles a charter that serves the public; an unjustified charter resembles a patent without an in[[demand|vent]]ion.

**The [[tax|taxation]] comparison**: Say notes that patent fees and stamp [[tax|duties]] fall on in[[demand|vent]]ive activity:

> …of the stamps on patents, of the taxes and impediments… (011-017)

He is critical of [[tax|taxes]] on patents, seeing them as a levy on [[invention|innovation]] — one of the few [[public-authority|government]] inter[[demand|vent]]ions in industry that Say regards as actively harmful rather than merely useless.

**The limits of the patent argument**: Say is careful not to extend the patent justification to other [[monopoly|monopolies]]. The unique economics of in[[demand|vent]]ion — high fixed cost, near-zero marginal cost of copying — justify patents in a way that does not generalize to [[commerce|trade]] prohibitions, corporate charters, or other [[monopoly|exclusive privilege]]s.
