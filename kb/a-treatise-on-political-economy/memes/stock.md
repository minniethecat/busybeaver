## Definition

> A portion of the values already produced and accumulated constitutes the capital on which the productive operations of a nation depend. The farming live and dead stock operates as machinery in aid of human industry. (003-002)

In Say's terminology, **[[capital|stock]]** refers to the physical inventory of [[commodity|goods]] that serves as the foundation of productive activity. It encompasses agricultural [[capital|stock]] (live[[capital|stock]], seed, implements), [[manufacture|manufacturing]] [[capital|stock]] (tools, machinery, [[raw-material|raw materials]]), and commercial [[capital|stock]] ([[commodity|merchandise]] held for sale).

Stock is the tangible form in which **[[capital|productive capital]]** is embodied and deployed.

## Explanation

### Live Stock and Dead Stock

Say adopts the agricultural distinction between **live [[capital|stock]]** (animals used in [[agriculture|farming]] — oxen, horses, sheep) and **dead [[capital|stock]]** (inanimate implements — ploughs, fences, buildings, machinery). Both are forms of fixed capital yielding their value gradually over multiple production cycles.

> The farming live and dead stock comprehends the cattle, the implements of husbandry, and the buildings employed in rural occupation. (003-003)

### Stock in Trade

In [[commercial-industry|commerce]], [[capital|stock]] refers to the inventory a merchant holds for sale. The merchant's capital is largely tied up in this [[capital|stock]], and the velocity with which it turns over determines profit. A well-[[capital|stock]]ed shop signals both capital resources and brisk demand.

> The trader carries his stock in trade upon his shoulders, or loads it in vessels. (005-001)

### Stock as National Capital

At the national level, the aggregate [[capital|stock]] of tools, machinery, buildings, live[[capital|stock]], [[raw-material|raw materials]], and [[commodity|merchandise]] constitutes the productive apparatus of the economy. Say emphasises that money is **not** productive [[capital|stock]] — it merely circulates [[commodity|goods]]. The real [[capital|stock]] of a nation includes "the outlay of money, seed, live [[capital|stock]], &c." (010-002) that must be advanced before production can begin.

> A country well stocked with intelligent merchants enjoys great advantages. (006-007)

## Historical Context

The concept of "[[capital|stock]]" was central to classical political economy. [[adam-smith|Adam Smith]] devoted attention to the distinction between fixed and circulating capital, and to the role of [[capital|stock]] in national wealth.

Say built on [[adam-smith|Smith]]'s foundation but gave the concept a more physical character — emphasising that real [[capital|stock]] is [[commodity|goods]], not money or financial claims. The agricultural origins of the term (live and dead [[capital|stock]]) reflect the economy of Say's era, when [[agriculture|farming]] was still the largest sector.

## Say's Treatment

Say's treatment of [[capital|stock]] is woven through Chapters III and XI. His contributions include:

1. **Stock as the physical form of capital**: Say insists that [[capital|productive capital]] consists of real [[commodity|goods]] — seed, tools, machinery, [[raw-material|raw materials]] — not of money. [[money|Money]] facilitates circulation but is not itself productive [[capital|stock]].

2. **The depreciation problem**: Because [[capital|stock]] (especially dead [[capital|stock]] like machinery) wears out, its value must be gradually recovered from the products it helps create. This links [[capital|stock]] to depreciation theory.

3. **Stock accumulation as growth**: Economic growth, in Say's frame[[labour|work]], is essentially the accumulation of productive [[capital|stock]] — saving from revenue to add to tools, machinery, and improvements.
