# Napoleon

## Biography

Napoleon Bonaparte (1769–1821) rose from Corsican artillery officer to Emperor of the French, dominating European affairs for over a decade. His military genius reshaped the map of Europe, but his economic policies — particularly the Continental System, a blockade designed to exclude British [[commodity|goods]] from European markets — constitute one of the most ambitious and destructive experiments in economic warfare ever attempted. Say, writing during and immediately after the Napoleonic era, treats Napoleon's policies as a living demonstration of the folly of [[public-authority|government]] interference with [[commercial-industry|commerce]].

## Relationship to Say

### The Continental System

Say's critique of Napoleon focuses on the Continental System, the blockade by which Napoleon sought to strangle British [[commercial-industry|commerce]]. Say argues that the blockade harmed France more than Britain: "France under the domination of Napoleon" (011-012) suffered from the severing of commercial ties that had formerly enriched both nations. The blockade "stripped of all resource" (017-048) French industry, cutting it off from [[raw-material|raw materials]] and colonial products.

Say's theoretical argument is that economic warfare is inherently self-destructive because [[commerce|trade]] is mutually beneficial:

> Napoleon, doubtless, occasioned much individual misery; but, as to the aggregate of national wealth, France, at his death, was poorer than when he began his career.
>
> (017-049)

### The Illusion of Plunder

Say directly challenges Napoleon's apparent belief that conquest could enrich France: "neither is at all the richer" (017-049). The wealth seized through military conquest is offset by the destruction of productive capacity and the diversion of resources from industry to warfare. Say's analysis here anticipates the modern economic understanding that war is a net destroyer of wealth, however much individual spoils may be captured.

### Imitation of Colonial Policy

Say notes that Napoleon's blockade "imitated" (019-017) the restrictive colonial policies of the old mercantile system, treating independent European nations as though they were dependencies of France. This attempt to extend colonial-style monopoly to the entire continent was, in Say's view, both economically irrational and politically unsustainable. The resistance it provoked ultimately contributed to Napoleon's downfall.

### Napoleon's Economic Legacy

Say's verdict on Napoleon is unequivocal: "Napoleon will scarcely be charged with" (017-050) any design to promote the productive industry of France. Military glory consumed the capital that should have built roads, canals, and manufactories. The lesson Say draws is that even the most brilliant conqueror cannot create wealth through force — a principle central to his entire system of political economy.
