# Raw Material

## Definition

Raw material is the physical substance that industry transforms into finished products. It constitutes one of the essential components of [[capital|productive capital]], alongside tools, implements, and the [[wages|wage]]-fund for [[labour|work]]men. Say defines it as the matter upon which human industry operates to create utility:

> The capital of the manufacturer chiefly consists of the raw material in different stages of progress, of tools, implements, and necessaries for his workmen; while that of the cultivator is vested in farming buildings, live stock, fences, and inclosures. ([003-003])

Raw material is distinct from natural agents — it has already been extracted or harvested and has entered the sphere of [[commercial-industry|commerce]], whereas natural agents (wind, rain, sunlight) are gratuitously supplied by nature.

## Explanation

### Raw Material in the Production Process

Every branch of [[manufacture|manufacturing industry]] takes some physical substance as its starting point. The cotton spinner begins with raw cotton; the iron-founder with pig iron; the carpenter with timber. These materials are themselves the products of previous industry — the cotton was grown and ginned, the iron was smelted from ore, the timber was felled and sawn. Say emphasises that wealth resides not in matter as such but in the value added at each stage:

> Wealth consists, not in matter, but in the value of matter; because matter without value is no item of wealth. ([002-004])

### Raw Material as Capital

Raw material represents a portion of the [[manufacturing|manufacture]]r's capital that is continuously tied up in the production process. The quantity required depends on the scale of operations and the rapidity of turnover:

> If, in consequence of division of labour, these same eighteen persons can be brought, as we know they can, to produce 86,400 pins, the daily supply of raw material requisite for their regular employ will be 240 ounces' weight of metal; consequently a much more considerable advance will be called for. ([008-013])

This "advance" — the capital locked in raw materials from purchase to reimbursement upon sale — is one of the principal constraints on industrial expansion. The larger and more efficient the operation, the greater the [[capital|stock]] of raw material that must be perpetually maintained (008-012).

### Domestic vs. Foreign Raw Material

A recurring policy question in Say's era was whether [[public-authority|government]]s should favour [[manufacturing|manufacture]]s using domestic raw materials over those using imported ones. Say is characteristically opposed to such inter[[demand|vent]]ion:

> A government entertains a notion, that the manufacture of a native raw material is better for the national industry, than the manufacture of a foreign raw material. It is in conformity to this notion, that we have seen instances of preference given to the woollen and linen above the cotton manufacture. By this conduct we contrive, as far as in us lies, to limit the bounty of nature, who pours forth in different climates a variety of materials adapted to our innumerable wants. ([017-007])

He argues that the true interest of industry is to use the cheapest and most suitable raw material wherever it originates, since the value of the finished product — not the origin of its inputs — determines the wealth created.

## Historical Context

The status of raw material in economic thought evolved considerably from the Physiocrats to Say. The Physiocrats held that only the extractive industries (agriculture, mining, fishing) were truly productive because they alone brought forth new matter from the earth. [[manufacture|Manufacture]]s merely reshaped existing matter and were therefore "sterile." The Physiocrats' error, Say argues, lay in their materialist conception of wealth:

> They allowed no industry to be productive, but that which procured the raw materials ... not adverting to the distinction, that wealth consists, not in matter, but in the value of matter. ([002-004])

By shifting the focus from physical matter to [[commerce|exchange]]able value, Say placed raw material, [[manufacturing|manufacture]]d [[commodity|goods]], and commercial services on a common analytical footing. Raw material is valuable not because it is "natural" but because human industry has already been exerted to procure and transport it.

## Say's Treatment

Say integrates raw material into his tripartite analysis of production (industry, capital, natural agents). Raw material is a form of *[[capital|productive capital]]* — it is consumed in the productive process and its value reappears in the finished product:

> There is nothing odious in the real picture of the accumulation of capital ... The accumulations of a society in its early stages consist for the most part of buildings, implements of husbandry, live-stock, improvements of land; those of a manufacturing people chiefly of raw materials, or such as are still in the first stage of preparation. ([011-007])

Say's key insight is that the distinction between "raw" and "[[manufacturing|manufacture]]d" is relative: what is raw material to one producer is a finished product to another. The spinner's yarn is the weaver's raw material; the weaver's cloth is the tailor's raw material. This continuum of value-addition is the essence of [[manufacturing|industrial production]].

Say also notes the importance of raw material imports for a nation's productive capacity. When a [[public-authority|government]] restricts imports of foreign raw materials, it does not merely inconvenience consumers — it starves domestic industry of its necessary inputs (017-008).
