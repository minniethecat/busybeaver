# Circulation

## Definition and Economic Function

Circulation, in Say's analysis, is the process by which commodities and money pass from hand to hand — from producer to consumer, and from consumer back to producer. It is the mechanism that "conducts the circulation and mutual interchange of all other products" (015-003). Without circulation, [[commodity|goods]] would remain idle in the hands of producers, and the incentive to produce would vanish.

## Brisk Circulation and National Wealth

A brisk circulation accelerates the creation of wealth. "By the increased activity of circulation, the subsequent advantage is, that a given quantity of the same products gives employment to a greater degree of industry" (016-001). When commodities circulate rapidly, each productive act is quickly rewarded, and capital is speedily returned to the producer to finance further production. The "facility of internal circulation, and freedom of individual agency," are essential conditions of prosperity (017-032).

## Obstacles to Circulation

Circulation is obstructed by anything that impedes the free [[commerce|exchange]] of commodities. Government regulations, [[monopoly|monopolies]], and excessive [[tax|taxation]] are primary obstacles. "An obstacle to circulation is by all means to be avoided," for it "reduces the demand for products, and consequently the reward of industry" (016-003). In extreme cases, "there is no circulation, but of such produce as cannot be dispensed with" (016-004) — the economy shrinks to bare subsistence.

## The Medium of Circulation

[[money|Money]] serves as the medium of circulation, but it is not itself the object of circulation. The quantity of money required depends on the "extent of national [[commerce|exchange]] and circulation" (017-017). An excess of money beyond what circulation requires is either exported or hoarded; a deficiency strangles [[commerce|exchange]]. The "demand for the purposes of circulation" determines the value and quantity of the circulating medium (017-026).
