## Definition

In Say's *Treatise on [[political-economy|Political Economy]]*, **free [[commerce|trade]]** is the unrestricted [[commerce|exchange]] of products between nations, unimpeded by tariffs, prohibitions, [[monopoly|monopolies]], or any other form of [[public-authority|government]] inter[[demand|vent]]ion. Say presents free [[commerce|trade]] not merely as a policy preference but as the natural extension of the principle that voluntary [[commerce|exchange]] always benefits both parties. "A perfect freedom of internal traffic, and above all, of the foreign corn [[commerce|trade]], is the only effectual [[supply|provision]] against scarcity" (017-105).

## Explanation

### The Principle of Free Exchange

Free [[commerce|trade]] is the international application of the general principle that [[commerce|exchange]] is mutually beneficial. Just as the division of labour within a nation increases productivity by allowing each producer to specialise, [[commerce|trade]] between nations allows each country to concentrate its resources where they are most productive. Say argues that "the products of Britain" should not be excluded "from the markets of the continent" by political restrictions, since such exclusion harms both British producers and continental consumers (017-049).

### Free Trade and Scarcity

Say's most powerful argument for free [[commerce|trade]] is its role in pre[[demand|vent]]ing and alleviating scarcity. During the scarcity of 1816–17, the French [[public-authority|government]] intervened in the grain [[commerce|trade]] — an inter[[demand|vent]]ion Say criticises as costly and ineffective. The alternative is clear: "a perfect freedom of internal traffic, and above all, of the foreign corn [[commerce|trade]]" allows supply to flow from regions of abundance to regions of scarcity, driven by the profit motive rather than by bureaucratic direction (017-105).

When "countries themselves experience a scarcity, and are obliged to retain their own produce," the international supply chain is disrupted (017-109). Free [[commerce|trade]] minimises such disruptions by ensuring that no nation depends exclusively on its own harvest.

### Concrete Examples

Say provides specific illustrations of free [[commerce|trade]]'s benefits. "The sugar of Cochin China might, under a free [[commerce|trade]], be sold in France at from 8 to 10 sous the pound" — far below the price maintained by the French colonial sugar monopoly (019-014). The consumer pays an artificial premium under protection, while the colonial producer enjoys an unearned advantage at public expense.

The corn [[commerce|trade]] provides a parallel case. Under "the operation of a free [[commerce|trade]]," the ratio of prices between different grains would adjust naturally to reflect their relative costs of production and transportation (017-082). Government inter[[demand|vent]]ion — bounties, tariffs, prohibitions — distorts this "natural ratio" and creates scarcity where abundance would otherwise prevail.

### Free Trade and National Prosperity

Say connects free [[commerce|trade]] to the broader theme of national prosperity. Restrictions on [[commerce|trade]] are restrictions on production, since they pre[[demand|vent]] resources from flowing to their most productive uses. A nation that embraces free [[commerce|trade]] "obtains a greater quantity of products for a less quantity of its own" — the very definition of economic gain. The consumer benefits from lower prices; the producer benefits from expanded markets; and the nation as a whole benefits from the more efficient allocation of its productive resources.

## Historical Context

Say's advocacy of free [[commerce|trade]] placed him at the centre of one of the most contentious debates of the 19th century. The British Corn Laws (1815–1846) had imposed severe restrictions on grain imports to protect domestic agriculture, raising food prices for the industrial [[labouring-class|working class]]. The French Continental System and British navigation laws had demonstrated the destructive potential of [[commerce|trade]] warfare.

Say's *Treatise* provided intellectual ammunition for the free-[[commerce|trade]] movement that would e[[demand|vent]]ually triumph with the repeal of the Corn Laws in 1846 and the Cobden-Chevalier Treaty of 1860. His arguments — that free [[commerce|trade]] pre[[demand|vent]]s scarcity, lowers prices, and promotes peace between nations — became the standard repertoire of classical liberal economics.

## Say's Treatment

Say treats free [[commerce|trade]] as the logical culmination of his entire economic system. If value is created by production, and production is maximised when resources flow freely to their most productive uses, then any barrier to [[commerce|trade]] is a barrier to the creation of value. The [[public-authority|government]] that restricts imports "perpetuates scarcity on the one hand, and consequently a glut on the other" — it protects inefficient domestic producers at the expense of consumers and of the national wealth (015-007).

What distinguishes Say's treatment is his empirical grounding. He does not content himself with abstract arguments for free [[commerce|trade]]; he examines specific cases — sugar from Cochin China, grain from the Baltic, [[manufacturing|manufacture]]d [[commodity|goods]] from Britain — and demonstrates the concrete costs of protection. Free [[commerce|trade]] is not a utopian ideal but a practical policy whose benefits can be measured in sous per pound.
