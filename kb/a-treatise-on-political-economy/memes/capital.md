# Capital

## Definition and Productive Role

Capital, in Say's system, is the accumulated stock of values devoted to productive employment. It consists of "the tools and implements of the several arts, the buildings, the machinery, the beasts of labour and of husbandry, the [[raw-material|raw materials]], the money, and whatever else is applicable to productive use" (003-002). The distinctive character of capital is that it is not consumed for immediate gratification but advanced to facilitate further production, enabling [[labour|work]]s that "the mere personal agency of man could never achieve" (003-001).

## Formation and Multiplication

Capital is formed through saving — the restraint of immediate consumption in favour of productive investment. "The more [[saving|savings]] are made, and more capital accumulated from the [[profit|profits]] of productive industry," the greater the national productive capacity (002-006). The process of multiplication is gradual: values that "in the first instance as an item of revenue, be so employed, as to become a permanent addition to capital" (011-006). Say emphasises that nations "deficient in capital labour under great disadvantage in the production of wealth" (005-004).

## Productive and Unproductive Capital

Say draws a sharp distinction between productive and unproductive capital. Productive capital is that which is actively employed in industry and yields a return; unproductive capital, by contrast, is "absorbed in [[productive-consumption|unproductive consumption]]" and "the nation loses the annual revenue of so much capital" (012-001). The classification turns on whether the capital generates new values or is simply consumed without reproduction.

## Capital and Natural Agents

The productive power of capital is often interwoven with the agency of nature. "The productive faculty of capital is often so interwoven with the productive agency of natural agents, that it is difficult to assign their respective shares" (004-001). A water-mill, for instance, combines the capital vested in the machinery with the gratuitous power of falling water.

> The value of all these must be found in advance by the industrious agent that works them up. The value of all these items constitute what is denominated productive capital.
>
> (003-002)
