# Revenue

## Definition and the Capital–Revenue Distinction

Revenue, in classical political economy, denotes the annual income or produce that individuals and the nation may consume without diminishing their productive [[capital|stock]]. Say insists on a careful "distinction between capital and revenue" (012-002): capital is the accumulated fund devoted to production, while revenue is that portion of the annual produce which may be consumed without impairing the source of future production. The nation that confuses the two "loses the annual revenue of so much capital" (012-001).

## Sources of Revenue

Revenue derives from the three great agents of production. The [[profit|profits]] of industry, the interest of capital, and the rent of land each furnish a stream of revenue to their respective proprietors. "These very charges consist the revenue of the commercial and [[manufacture|manufacturing]] classes, as the rent of land constitutes the revenue of the proprietor" (002-009). Natural agents, too, contribute: "a water-course thus appropriated to the purposes of human industry, from that time forth, forms an item of the revenues of the appropriators" (004-007).

## Revenue and Taxation

[[taxation|Taxation]] draws upon national revenue, not upon capital. "[[taxation|Taxation]] never does, or can, operate immediately upon capital, but from the national revenue" (006-011). When [[public-authority|government]] expenditure exceeds the revenue drawn from [[tax|taxation]], it must either borrow against future revenue or consume capital — both injurious to national wealth. Wise public administration "enhances rather than diminishes the revenue of the landholder" by improving the conditions of production (013-016).

## Accumulation and Saving

The portion of revenue not consumed becomes saving, and saving, when productively employed, becomes capital. "The [[saving|savings]] or accumulations of revenue, devoted to the formation of capital, are the sources of all increase of national wealth" (013-013). Thus the prosperity of a nation depends not merely on the magnitude of its revenue but on the proportion of revenue saved and productively invested.
