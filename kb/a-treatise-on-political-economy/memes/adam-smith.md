# Adam Smith

## Biography

Adam Smith (1723–1790) was a Scottish economist and moral philosopher, widely regarded as the founder of classical political economy. Born in Kirkcaldy, Scotland, he studied at the University of Glasgow and Balliol College, Oxford. He served as Professor of Moral Philosophy at Glasgow and later as tutor to the young Duke of Buccleuch, travels that brought him into contact with the French Physiocrats and other leading Enlightenment thinkers.

## Major Contributions

### The Wealth of Nations

Smith's *An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the [[wealth|Wealth]] of Nations* (1776) is the foundational text of classical economics. Its key doctrines include: the division of labour as the source of productivity growth (008-001); the labour theory of value; the distinction between productive and unproductive labour (002-007); the analysis of the "invisible hand" by which self-interest promotes the general welfare; and the case for free [[commerce|trade]] and minimal [[public-authority|government]] inter[[demand|vent]]ion.

### Theory of Moral Sentiments

Smith's earlier *Theory of Moral Sentiments* (1759) developed a theory of moral psychology based on sympathy — the capacity to imagine oneself in another's situation. This [[labour|work]], often overlooked, provides the ethical foundation for Smith's economic system, grounding commercial society in moral sentiments rather than mere self-interest.

## Relationship to Say

Say engages Smith's [[labour|work]] throughout the *Treatise*, treating him as the foremost authority while also offering significant criticisms. Say rejects Smith's labour theory of value, arguing that value rests on utility rather than labour expended. He also criticizes Smith's classification of services as "unproductive labour," insisting instead that immaterial products create genuine value. Say's translator notes: "Smith appears to have considered the labour expended in surmounting the difficulty of attainment to be the ground[[labour|work]], as well as the measure of value; and he has been followed in the first part of that opinion by [[david-ricardo|Ricardo]]." (001-007)

Say acknowledges Smith as the pioneer who systematized political economy but positions his own [[labour|work]] as correcting and extending Smith's frame[[labour|work]] — particularly by grounding value in utility and by giving proper weight to the role of the [[adventurer|entrepreneur]].

Prinsep acknowledges Smith's foundational contribution to political economy:

> Since the primaeval labours of Smith upon the Theory of National Wealth, that interesting branch of polity has undergone abundance of discussion in most of its parts.
>
> (P02-001)
