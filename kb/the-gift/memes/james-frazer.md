## Biography

James George Frazer (1 January 1854 – 7 May 1941) was a Scottish social anthropologist and classicist. Educated at Glasgow University and Trinity College, Cambridge, he spent his entire career at Cambridge, where he held the first chair of Social Anthropology in Britain. His monumental work, *The Golden Bough*, first published in two volumes in 1890 and eventually expanded to twelve volumes, was the most ambitious comparative study of myth, magic, and religion ever attempted. Frazer assembled a vast archive of ethnographic data drawn from missionaries, colonial administrators, and classical sources, creating the raw comparative material on which later French sociological theorists -- including [[emile-durkheim|Durkheim]], [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]], and Hubert -- would build their systematic theories.

## Key Works

| Year | Title | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 1890 | *The Golden Bough* (2 vols; expanded to 12 vols by 1915) | Massive comparative study of magic, myth, and religion across world cultures; introduced the magic-religion-science evolutionary sequence and the dying god archetype |
| 1910 | *Totemism and Exogamy* (4 vols) | Comprehensive survey of totemic institutions worldwide, providing data later reanalyzed by Durkheim in *The Elementary Forms of Religious Life* (1912) |

## Core Ideas

- **Sympathetic Magic** -- Frazer classified magic into two principles: *homeopathic magic* (like produces like, or imitative magic) and *contagious magic* (things once in contact continue to act on each other at a distance). [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] and Hubert later built their *General Theory of Magic* (1902) on this same data but rejected Frazer's individualistic, intellectualist explanation, arguing instead that magic is a social phenomenon grounded in collective belief and the concept of [[mana]].

- **The Evolutionary Sequence: Magic to Religion to Science** -- Frazer argued that human thought progressed through three stages: magic (manipulating immanent forces through laws of sympathy), religion (appeasing transcendent personal beings), and science (discovering the real laws of nature). This evolutionist framework was sharply critiqued by the [[emile-durkheim|Durkheim]]ian school, which insisted on the co-existence of these modes within every society rather than their sequential replacement. [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] reworked Frazer's data into a non-evolutionary framework where magic, religion, and [[gift-economy|gift exchange]] are parallel social institutions, each with its own internal logic.

- **The Dying and Reviving God** -- Frazer's most famous thesis held that a widespread myth-ritual pattern -- the sacred king who dies and is reborn, often tied to vegetation cycles -- underlies religions from ancient Near Eastern cults to European folk customs. Hubert and [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] took up this comparative material in *[[sacrifice|Sacrifice]]* (1899), but replaced Frazer's universal archetype with a structural analysis of the ritual mechanism itself: entry, consecration, destruction, and exit as the invariant schema across all sacrificial rites.

- **The Comparative Method as Data Aggregation** -- Whatever the flaws of Frazer's theoretical framework, his twelve-volume *Golden Bough* assembled an unparalleled archive of cross-cultural practices. Frazer excerpted and catalogued customs relating to magic, taboo, kingship, fertility rites, and sacrifice from thousands of sources. [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] explicitly acknowledged Frazer's massive compilations as the raw empirical material that made possible the more rigorous comparative sociology of the *[[annee-sociologique|Année Sociologique]]* group. Mauss critiqued Frazer's evolutionism but built directly on Frazer's ethnographic data in *The Gift*.

## In the Text

Frazer's influence and his role as a predecessor figure are registered across the [[marcel-mauss|Maussian]] corpus. In *Sacrifice*, Hubert and Mauss positioned their theory against Frazer's:

> "Breaking with earlier theories that sought the origin of sacrifice in a single principle (Tylor's gift-theory, Smith's totemic communion, Frazer's sympathetic magic), they proposed a unitary structural schema underlying all sacrificial rituals across cultures." (Hubert and Mauss 1899:97; cited in sacrifice.md)

In *A General Theory of Magic*, Frazer's sympathetic magic framework is treated as the leading intellectualist theory that must be overcome in favor of a sociological account grounded in collective belief and *mana* (see [[magic]]). Frazer's Cambridge chair was later held by [[meyer-fortes]], who brought Maussian exchange theory into British social anthropology.

## Influence and Legacy

Frazer's literary style and comparative breadth made him the most widely read anthropologist of his generation. He influenced not only [[bronislaw-malinowski|Malinowski]], who cited *The Golden Bough* as an inspiration, but also literary modernists such as T.S. Eliot (whose *The Waste Land* draws directly on Frazer's dying god motif) and James Joyce. Within anthropology, however, his evolutionist framework was superseded by the functionalism of [[bronislaw-malinowski|Malinowski]] and the structural-functionalism of Radcliffe-Brown, and his armchair method was replaced by participant observation. His lasting contribution is the vast archive of comparative data that he assembled, which the Durkheimian school -- particularly [[emile-durkheim|Durkheim]], [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]], and Hubert -- reanalyzed within a rigorous sociological framework, producing the theories of magic, sacrifice, classification, and gift exchange that remain central to the discipline.
