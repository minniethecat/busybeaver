## Definition

**Body techniques** (*techniques du corps*) is a concept introduced by [[marcel-mauss|Marcel Mauss]] to describe "the ways in which, from society to society, men know how to use their bodies" ([[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] 1935:70). The concept demonstrates that bodily actions typically assumed to be purely biological — walking, running, swimming, sitting, sleeping, eating, giving birth — are in fact culturally learned and vary systematically across societies and historical periods.

## Origin and Development

[[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] delivered his lecture "Techniques of the Body" to the Société de Psychologie in Paris on 17 May 1934; it was published in the *Journal de Psychologie Normale et Pathologique* in 1935. The essay exemplified [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s distinctive methodology: drawing on [[personne|person]]al observation, ethnographic comparison, and sociological analysis to illuminate a phenomenon that had been neglected by both biology and psychology.

[[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] identified four principles of [[classification]] for body techniques: (1) by sex — techniques are divided between men and women; (2) by age — techniques vary across the life course; (3) by efficiency — techniques are ranked by their effectiveness; (4) by transmission — techniques are taught in specific social contexts. He also distinguished between "prestigious imitation" (learning by copying high-status models) and explicit education.

The essay influenced the development of practice theory (Bourdieu 1972), the anthropology of the body (Lock 1993), and performance studies. It is now recognised as one of the founding texts of the anthropology of embodiment.

## Key Elements

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Cultural Variation** | Swimming strokes, walking gaits, birthing positions differ systematically across cultures |
| **Social Learning** | Body techniques are acquired through imitation and education, not biological programming |
| **Prestigious Imitation** | The child imitates successful adults whose actions carry social authority |
| **Biographical Change** | A [[personne|person]]'s body techniques change across their lifetime — from childhood games to adult work postures |
| **Classification** | Techniques vary by sex, age, efficiency, and mode of transmission |

## Theoretical Context

"Techniques of the Body" represents a bridge between [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s earlier work on [[magic]], [[sacrifice]], and the gift, and his later development of the concept of the "total man" (*l'homme total*). The essay argues that the body is simultaneously biological, psychological, and social — and that no single discipline (physiology, psychology, or sociology alone) can adequately understand it.

[[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] considered the essay part of an unfinished larger project on *[[habitus]]* and bodily practice. The concept of *[[habitus]]* appears in the essay as "the techniques and work of collective and individual practical reason" — a formulation that Bourdieu would later expand into a full theory of social practice (Bourdieu 1977:72–87).

## In the Text

[[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] on the cultural specificity of swimming:

> "We can distinguish between the different swimming techniques. The Polynesians do not swim as we do, and my generation does not swim as the present generation does. ... Formerly we were taught to dive after having learnt to swim. And when we were taught to dive, we were taught to close our eyes, then to open them in the water. ... Today the technique is the reverse. The whole of the apprenticeship begins by getting the child used to keeping its eyes open under water." (Mauss 1935:71)

On the physiological consequences of cultural variation:

> "The positions of the arms and hands during walking form a social idiosyncrasy — they are not simply the product of purely individual, almost entirely physical arrangements and mechanisms." (Mauss 1935:74)
