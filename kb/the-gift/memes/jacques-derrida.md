## Biography

Jacques Derrida (1930–2004) was a French philosopher born in El Biar, French Algeria. He taught at the École Normale Supérieure and the École des Hautes Études en Sciences Sociales in Paris, and later held visiting positions at Yale University, the University of California, Irvine, and other institutions. He is best known for developing the philosophical method of deconstruction, which he applied across an extraordinary range of topics including language, law, justice, hospitality, and the gift.

## Core Ideas

### The Aporia of the Gift

Derrida's central argument about the gift is that it is impossible — or rather, that the gift as gift is annihilated by the very conditions that make it possible. For a gift to be a true gift, it must not be recognized as such by either giver or receiver; recognition transforms the gift into a debt, an obligation, a circular exchange (Derrida 1991:12–14). This directly challenges [[marcel-mauss]]'s claim that the three obligations of giving, receiving, and reciprocating constitute the gift.

### Gift and Economy

Derrida argues that Mauss's *Essai sur le don* oscillates between two readings: the gift as a pre-economic form of exchange (gift economy as precursor to market economy) and the gift as that which exceeds any economy, any circle of return. The second reading — the gift as the aneconomic — is what Derrida develops (Derrida 1991:24–26).

### Time and the Gift

The gift requires time: "The gift is not a gift, the gift only gives to the extent it *gives time*. The difference between a gift and every other operation of pure and simple exchange is that the gift gives time" (Derrida 1991:41). This temporal dimension — the delay, the suspension of return — is what distinguishes the gift from barter or contract.

## In the Text

On the fundamental paradox:

> "For there to be a gift, there must be no reciprocity, return, exchange, countergift, or debt. If the other *gives me back* or *owes me* or has to give me back what I give him or her, there will not have been a gift."
> (Derrida 1991:12)

On Mauss and the destruction of the gift by recognition:

> "The simple intention to give, insofar as it carries the intentional meaning of the gift, suffices to make a return payment to oneself. The simple recognition of the gift as gift — as generous, as present — would be, if it is a matter here of a calculable and measurable time and object, the annulment of the gift."
> (Derrida 1991:14)

## Influence and Legacy

Derrida's *Given Time* extended gift theory from anthropology into continental philosophy, influencing debates about altruism, debt, and justice across the humanities. [[david-graeber]]'s *Debt: The First 5,000 Years* (2011) engages Derrida's gift aporia while grounding it in historical practice. Derrida's reading of [[georges-bataille]] on expenditure and [[total-prestation]] in *Writing and Difference* anticipates the later *Given Time* analysis.
