## Overview

The **Pacific Northwest Coast** is a cultural region extending along the coast of North America from Yakutat Bay in southern Alaska through British Columbia to the Columbia River in northern California. It is characterized by a temperate rainforest ecology, abundant marine resources, and a distinctive set of indigenous cultures.

In anthropology, the region is renowned for the [[potlatch]] — a ceremonial feast and gift-giving institution that [[marcel-mauss]] analyzed as one of the most elaborate forms of [[total-prestation]] and [[gift-economy|gift exchange]].

## The Potlatch Region

The Pacific Northwest is the classic locus of the [[potlatch]] institution, documented extensively by Franz Boas in the late 19th century, particularly among the Kwakwaka'wakw. The potlatch here took its most elaborate form:

- **Copper shields** — valuable objects that were broken, thrown into the sea, or given away to demonstrate wealth
- **Blankets and canoes** — mass distribution of goods to guests
- **Competitive feasting** — rivals attempting to outdo each other in generosity and waste
- **Status validation** — potlatch legitimated claims to titles, names, and hereditary rights

## In the Text

[[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] discusses the Northwest Coast potlatch as a "total service of the agonistic type":

> The system of the potlatch in the tribes of the North-West American coast is the most typical example of the total prestation of an agonistic type. Nothing in our Western civilization can give us any idea of these ceremonies.
>
> （Mauss 1966:35–36）
