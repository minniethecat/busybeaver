## Biography

Mary Douglas (25 March 1921 – 16 May 2007) was a British social anthropologist. Born Mary Tew in San Remo, Italy, and raised in England, she studied at Oxford under [[ee-evans-pritchard|E.E. Evans-Pritchard]], whose structural-functionalism deeply shaped her intellectual formation. Her fieldwork among the Lele people of the Belgian Congo (1949–1950) provided the ethnographic foundation for her later theoretical work. Douglas held academic positions at University College London, where she succeeded [[ar-radcliffe-brown|A.R. Radcliffe-Brown]], and later moved to the United States, teaching at Northwestern and Princeton. A committed Catholic, she brought an unusual sensitivity to ritual and symbol to a discipline increasingly dominated by materialist and ecological approaches. Her work systematically extended the Durkheimian tradition — and specifically Maussian insights about classification and exchange — into domains that classical anthropology had neglected: risk perception, consumer behavior, and biblical dietary laws.

## Core Ideas

- **Goods as a Communication System** — In *The World of Goods*, Douglas argued that consumption in modern capitalist societies is not fundamentally different from gift exchange in non-market societies. Goods function as markers of social relationships and as a ritual language for making visible the categories of culture. Drawing directly on Mauss, she contended that the act of purchasing, possessing, and giving goods is a "total social phenomenon" through which households construct and communicate their place in the social order.

- **Grid/Group Theory** — Douglas's grid/group framework classified societies along two axes: "grid" (the degree to which roles and categories are externally imposed) and "group" (the strength of collective boundaries). This schema allowed her to correlate social organization with cosmology, risk perception, and attitudes toward the body. The theory extends Mauss's insight that systems of classification are social in origin: how a society classifies nature and the body reflects, Douglas argued, the structure of social relations within it.

- **The Rejection of the Free Gift** — In her influential foreword to the 1990 English edition of *The Gift*, Douglas argued that Mauss's most radical claim was not about reciprocity but about the impossibility of the purely free, disinterested gift. She contended that Mauss's essay is best read as a critique of the modern ideology of the "free gift" — the notion that a gift should expect no return — and that Mauss's theory of obligatory exchange is, at root, a theory of social solidarity through material circulation.

## In the Text

Douglas wrote the foreword to the 1990 English translation of Mauss's *The Gift*, providing a vigorous defense of Mauss's relevance. She summarized the central argument:

> "Mauss's *Essay on the Gift* is not about presents exchanged at Christmas. It is a theory of human solidarity. Mauss shows that the cycle of giving, receiving, and reciprocating is the cement that holds societies together. To refuse to give, to fail to invite, to refuse to accept — these are equivalent to a declaration of war."

Douglas also connected Mauss to her own grid/group theory, arguing that different types of gift systems — agonistic potlatch, balanced reciprocity, generalized sharing — correspond to different positions on the grid/group diagram.

## Influence and Legacy

Douglas bridged the divide between classical Durkheimian anthropology and the study of modern institutions. Her grid/group theory has been applied across disciplines from biblical studies to organizational behavior, and her analysis of consumption as ritual communication influenced economic sociology and consumer culture theory. Her reading of Mauss, emphasizing the entanglement of gifts with power and hierarchy, challenged the romanticized interpretation of the gift as purely altruistic that had gained currency in some circles.
