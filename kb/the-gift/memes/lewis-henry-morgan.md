## Biography

Lewis Henry Morgan (21 November 1818 – 17 December 1881) was an American anthropologist who, alongside [[edward-tylor|E. B. Tylor]] in Britain, was the most influential cultural evolutionist of the nineteenth century. Born on a farm near Aurora, New York, Morgan studied law at Union College and practiced as a railroad attorney in Rochester. His anthropological career began unexpectedly in 1843 when he joined a young men's literary society that reorganized itself as a secret fraternal order modeled on the Iroquois League — the Grand Order of the Iroquois. To authenticate their rituals, Morgan began visiting the nearby Tonawanda Seneca reservation, and what began as a hobby became a lifelong commitment to the study of Iroquois culture. His first major work, *League of the Ho-dé-no-sau-nee, or Iroquois* (1851), was the first scientific ethnography of a Native American tribe and remains an indispensable historical record. In 1859, during a business trip to Michigan, Morgan encountered an Ojibwa community whose kinship terminology differed radically from Anglo-American patterns but resembled that of the Iroquois — the discovery that launched his monumental comparative study, *Systems of Consanguinity and Affinity of the Human Family* (1871). His masterwork, *Ancient Society* (1877), proposed a universal evolutionary sequence — savagery, barbarism, civilization — driven by technological innovation, and argued that the institutions of property, family, and government evolved in tandem with subsistence technology. Morgan's work was read and admired by Karl Marx, who took extensive notes on *Ancient Society*, and by Friedrich Engels, whose *The Origin of the Family, Private Property and the State* (1884) was subtitled "In the Light of the Researches of Lewis H. Morgan."

## Core Ideas

### The Evolution of Subsistence and Society

Morgan's central thesis was that human society evolved through three major stages — Savagery, Barbarism, and Civilization — each defined by its technological mastery of subsistence. Savagery was the age of hunting, gathering, and the discovery of fire; Barbarism began with agriculture and pottery and culminated in the smelting of iron; Civilization commenced with the invention of the phonetic alphabet and writing. Each technological advance reconfigured the institutions of kinship, property, and government. In the lower stages, property was minimal and kinship organized social life communally; as technology advanced, private property grew in importance and kinship was progressively displaced by territorial government. This materialist sequence — technology drives institutions — made Morgan the nineteenth-century precursor of [[leslie-white|Leslie White]]'s neoevolutionism and of the Marxist anthropology of the twentieth century (Morgan 1877: 3–44).

### Classificatory vs. Descriptive Kinship

Morgan's most enduring scientific contribution was the distinction between "classificatory" and "descriptive" kinship systems. In classificatory systems (exemplified by the Iroquois), a single term designates a whole class of relatives — father and father's brother are both "father"; mother and mother's sister are both "mother." In descriptive systems (Indo-European), primary kin are distinguished lexically from collateral kin. Morgan's global survey demonstrated that classificatory systems were the earlier evolutionary form, retaining traces of a prehistoric stage in which group marriage rather than pair marriage was the norm. Though Morgan's evolutionary interpretation has been abandoned, the classificatory/descriptive distinction remains a foundational concept in kinship studies, and his global survey of kinship terminologies was the largest comparative anthropological database assembled in the nineteenth century (Morgan 1871: 10–58).

### Property and the Origins of Inequality

Morgan argued that the growth of private property was the engine of social evolution, gradually dissolving communal kinship organization and giving rise to territorial states, class stratification, and hereditary inequality. In the stage of Savagery, property was negligible — a few personal implements buried with the dead. In Barbarism, property expanded to include herds, cultivated land, and houses, and inheritance rules became increasingly elaborate. In Civilization, property became the dominant force in social organization, with the state emerging as an instrument for protecting property relations. This account of property as a historically specific and destructive force resonated with Marx and Engels, who saw in Morgan's work an independent, ethnographic confirmation of their materialist conception of history. In gift theory, Morgan's account of the transition from communal to private property is the necessary prehistory of any theory of exchange: the gift, as a form of circulation that is neither pure communal sharing nor pure market commodity, occupies the transitional space between Morgan's stages of Barbarism and Civilization (Morgan 1877: 525–554).

## Influence and Legacy

Morgan's influence is paradoxical: his specific evolutionary sequences and his speculative method have been decisively rejected by later anthropology, yet his impact on the discipline — and on social thought more broadly — has been immense. Kinship studies, which he inaugurated as a systematic comparative enterprise, became one of the central domains of twentieth-century anthropology, from Rivers to Radcliffe-Brown to Lévi-Strauss. His materialist account of social evolution was taken up by Marx and Engels and became canonical in Marxist anthropology and archaeology. In American anthropology, [[leslie-white|Leslie White]]'s neoevolutionism was an explicit attempt to rehabilitate Morgan against the Boasian critique, and White's student [[marshall-sahlins|Marshall Sahlins]] — who co-edited a volume titled *Evolution and Culture* (1960) with [[elmer-service|Elman Service]] — carried Morgan's evolutionary problematic into the postwar era. In gift theory, Morgan represents the starting point from which Mauss departed: Mauss's insistence that the gift is a "total social fact" that cannot be reduced to economics or technology was a direct rebuttal of Morgan's materialist determinism. The gift, in Maussian theory, is not a stage in the evolution from communal sharing to private property but a permanent and irreducible dimension of social life — an insight that begins precisely where Morgan's evolutionism ends.
