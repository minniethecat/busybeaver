## Definition

**Habitus** (from Latin *habitus* — condition, bearing, disposition) is a concept introduced by [[marcel-mauss|Marcel Mauss]] to describe the socially acquired bodily techniques and dispositions that vary across societies. [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] defined it as the "techniques and work of collective and individual practical reason" that are transmitted through education and imitation rather than biological inheritance ([[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] 1935:75). The concept bridges the biological and the social — the body is the first and most natural instrument of man, but the ways it is used are culturally shaped.

## Origin and Development

[[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] first articulated the concept of habitus in his 1934 lecture "Techniques of the Body" (*Les techniques du corps*), published in the *Journal de Psychologie Normale et Pathologique* in 1935. He observed that basic bodily actions — walking, swimming, digging, sleeping positions — varied systematically across cultures and historical periods. The term *habitus* itself was borrowed from Aristotelian philosophy via Thomas Aquinas and French sociology ([[emile-durkheim|Durkheim]] 1895:88).

Pierre Bourdieu later substantially redeveloped the concept in *Outline of a Theory of Practice* (1972) and *Distinction* (1979), transforming habitus into a core concept of practice theory: a system of durable, transposable dispositions that generates and structures practices and representations without presupposing conscious obedience to rules (Bourdieu 1977:72).

## Key Elements

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Bodily Techniques** | The ways in which people learn to use their bodies — walking, swimming, eating — vary by society and era |
| **Social Transmission** | Habitus is acquired through education, imitation, and social conditioning, not biology |
| **Cultural Variation** | Each society has its own "body techniques" that appear natural to its members but are culturally specific |
| **Practical Reason** | Habitus operates below the level of conscious rationality — it is embodied practical knowledge |
| **Durability** | Once acquired, habitus is durable and transposable across different contexts (Bourdieu's extension) |

## Theoretical Context

[[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s concept of habitus occupies a pivotal position between [[emile-durkheim|Durkheim]]ian sociology and later practice theory. It was [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s response to the mind-body dualism in Western thought — showing that even the most "natural" bodily actions are socially patterned. The concept also connects to [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s broader theory of the "total man" (*l'homme total*), which sees the human being as simultaneously biological, psychological, and social ([[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] 1935:83).

Bourdieu's reformulation linked habitus to field (*champ*) and capital (*capital*), creating a generative theory of social practice. For Bourdieu, habitus explains how social structures become inscribed in bodies and how individual actions reproduce those structures.

## In the Text

[[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] opens "Techniques of the Body" with a striking observation:

> "A kind of revelation came to me in hospital. I was ill in New York. I wondered where I had seen girls walking as my nurses walked. I had the time to think about it. At last I realised that it was at the cinema. Returning to France, I noticed how common this gait was, especially in Paris; the girls were French and they too were walking in this way. In fact, American walking fashions had begun to arrive in France, thanks to the cinema." (Mauss 1935:70)

This anecdote illustrates the core insight of habitus: bodily techniques that seem natural are actually socially learned and historically variable. [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] goes on to analyse swimming techniques, marching styles, and even childbirth positions across cultures.
