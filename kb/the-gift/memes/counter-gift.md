## Definition

The **counter-gift** (*contre-don*) is the return gift that completes the cycle of [[gift-economy|gift exchange]] in [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]ian theory. It is the third moment of the triple [[obligation]] — after the [[obligation]] to give (*donner*) and the [[obligation]] to receive (*recevoir*) — and it has a distinctive character: the counter-gift must be different from, and ideally more valuable than, the original gift, creating what [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] called "a sort of credit and honour" that perpetuates the exchange cycle ([[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] 1925:35).

## Origin and Development

[[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] identified the counter-gift as a universal feature of [[gift-economy|gift exchange]] systems across [[melanesia|Melanesia]], Polynesia, and the [[pacific-northwest|Pacific Northwest]]. In the Trobriand [[kula-exchange|kula]], the counter-gift takes the form of *yotile* — the return of a *[[mwali-and-soulava|soulava]]* necklace for a *[[mwali-and-soulava|mwali]]* armshell, or vice versa, after an appropriate interval. In the potlatch, the counter-gift is a more lavish feast and distribution that "crushes" the rival with excess. In Māori exchange, the counter-gift is compelled by the *[[hau]]* of the original [[taonga]] ([[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] 1925:11–14).

Pierre Bourdieu later made the temporal structure of the counter-gift central to his theory of practice. In *Outline of a Theory of Practice* (1972), Bourdieu argued that the interval between gift and counter-gift is what makes the system work: if the return were immediate, it would be a payment, not a gift. The delay creates uncertainty about whether a counter-gift will come at all — and this uncertainty is what generates the social work of maintaining relationships.

## Key Elements

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Delay** | The counter-gift must not be immediate — delay distinguishes gift from payment |
| **Increase** | The counter-gift should ideally exceed the original gift, creating competitive escalation |
| **Difference** | The counter-gift must be different in kind, not identical — a necklace for an armshell |
| **Perpetuation** | The counter-gift closes one cycle and opens another — exchange is never final |
| **Social Recognition** | The counter-gift publicly acknowledges the bond between giver and recipient |

## Theoretical Context

The counter-gift is the conceptual pivot of [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s argument against utilitarian economics. If the return gift were merely the repayment of a debt, [[gift-economy|gift exchange]] would be indistinguishable from [[barter]] or credit. What makes it different is precisely the social dimension: the counter-gift is never just an economic transaction but an act of social recognition — it says "I acknowledge our relationship and I wish to continue it."

Jacques Derrida, in *Given Time* (1991), argued that the very possibility of the counter-gift undoes the gift as such: if a gift is recognized as a gift and returned, it becomes part of an economic cycle and ceases to be a pure gift. This paradox — the gift that annuls itself — has generated extensive philosophical debate.

## In the Text

[[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] on the distinctiveness of the counter-gift:

> "The gift received is not something inert. Even when the giver has abandoned it, it still possesses something of him. Through it, he has a hold over the beneficiary, just as, being its owner, he has a hold over the thief. ... The taonga and all strictly personal property possess a hau — a spiritual power. You give me one of them and I give it to a third person; he then gives me another taonga, because he is forced to do so by the hau of my gift." (Mauss 1925:12)

On the competitive dimension:

> "The counter-gift must be given with interest. ... The interest on the gift is the superiority of the giver over the recipient, which the latter must efface by a more generous counter-gift." (Mauss 1925:35, paraphrased)
