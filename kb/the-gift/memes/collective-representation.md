## Definition

**Collective representations** (*représentations collectives*) are shared ideas, beliefs, values, and categories that exist at the level of society rather than in individual minds. Introduced by [[emile-durkheim|Émile Durkheim]] in *The Rules of Sociological Method* (1895) and developed in *The Elementary Forms of Religious Life* (1912), the concept holds that certain mental contents — religious beliefs, moral norms, logical categories — are "social facts" that transcend and constrain individual thought. [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] deployed this concept throughout his work, from *[[classification|Primitive Classification]]* (1903) to *The Gift* (1925), treating [[gift-economy|gift exchange]] as a system of collective representations.

## Origin and Development

[[emile-durkheim|Durkheim]] introduced "collective representations" to distinguish sociology from psychology. Individual representations (perceptions, memories, emotions) are the domain of psychology; collective representations (religion, law, morality, categories of understanding) are the domain of sociology. The distinction was polemical: [[emile-durkheim|Durkheim]] was claiming for sociology a territory — the study of mind — that had been monopolized by philosophy and psychology.

[[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] operationalized the concept more concretely than [[emile-durkheim|Durkheim]]. In *The Gift*, the triple [[obligation]] to give, receive, and reciprocate is a collective representation: it exists in the shared understanding of members of Trobriand, Māori, and Kwakiutl societies, not merely in their individual preferences. The same is true of *mana*, *[[hau]]*, and other concepts that [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] analysed: they are collective representations that make certain forms of exchange thinkable and obligatory.

[[claude-levi-strauss|Lévi-Strauss]] later transformed the [[emile-durkheim|Durkheim]]ian concept into the structuralist notion of "unconscious structures" — collective representations without the collective subject.

## Key Elements

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Social Origin** | Collective representations are products of social interaction, not individual cognition |
| **Externality** | They exist outside and prior to any particular individual |
| **Constraint** | They impose themselves on individuals with obligatory force |
| **Sharedness** | They are common to members of a social group |
| **Symbolic Mediation** | They are expressed through symbols, rituals, and language |

## Theoretical Context

Collective representations are the epistemological foundation of [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]ian anthropology. When [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] argues that [[gift-economy|gift exchange]] is a "[[total-prestation|total social fact]]," he means that it is simultaneously economic, legal, moral, religious, and aesthetic — and this totality is possible only because the gift is a collective representation that organizes experience across these domains.

The concept bridges [[emile-durkheim|Durkheim]]ian sociology and later developments in symbolic anthropology (Geertz, Turner), structuralism ([[claude-levi-strauss|Lévi-Strauss]]), and practice theory (Bourdieu). Its influence can also be traced in the sociology of knowledge (Berger and Luckmann's *The Social Construction of Reality*, 1966) and in contemporary theories of collective intentionality (Searle, Tuomela).

## In the Text

[[emile-durkheim|Durkheim]] on the sui generis character of collective representations:

> "Collective representations are the product of an immense cooperation that extends not only through space but through time; to make them, a multitude of minds have associated, mixed, and combined their ideas and sentiments; long generations have accumulated their experience and knowledge. A special intellectual activity is therefore concentrated in them which is infinitely richer and more complex than that of the individual." (Durkheim 1912:23–24)

[[marcel-mauss|Mauss]], in *The Gift*, shows collective representations in action:

> "All these institutions express one fact alone, one social system, one precise state of mind: everything — food, women, children, property, talismans, land, labour, services, religious functions, and rank — is there for the giving, and for the returning." (Mauss 1925:14)
