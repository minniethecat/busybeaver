## Biography

**Henri Hubert** (23 June 1872 – 25 May 1927) was a French archaeologist and sociologist of the [[annee-sociologique]] school. A close friend and frequent collaborator of [[marcel-mauss]], Hubert co-authored two of the most influential works in the sociology of religion: *Essai sur la nature et la fonction du sacrifice* (1899) and *Esquisse d'une théorie générale de la magie* (1904). He also produced important archaeological studies of the Celts and maintained a parallel career at the Musée des Antiquités Nationales.

Hubert's intellectual partnership with [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] was unique in the Durkheimian circle — the two men worked so closely that their individual contributions to the co-authored texts are often difficult to disentangle. After Hubert's premature death in 1927, [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] described him as "the brother of my thought."

## Key Works

| Work | Year | Significance |

## Core Ideas

| Concept | Explanation |
|---------|-------------|
| **Sacrifice as Mediation** | Hubert and Mauss argued that sacrifice is a mechanism for establishing communication between the sacred and the profane through the destruction of a victim |
| **Sacralization and Desacralization** | The sacrificial process involves a rhythmic alternation: the sacrificer is first sacralized (set apart), then desacralized (returned to the profane world) through the rite |
| **Magic as Social Fact** | [[magic|Magic]] is not individual superstition but a socially instituted system of collective representations, governed by the concept of [[mana]] |
| **Mana as Collective Force** | The efficacy of magical rites derives from the collective belief in an impersonal sacred force ([[mana]]) — a concept that became foundational for Durkheimian sociology |

## In the Text

On the social function of sacrifice, Hubert and Mauss write:

> "Sacrifice is a religious act which, through the consecration of a victim, modifies the moral state of the sacrificer or of certain objects in which he is interested. ... The procedures of sacrifice consist in establishing a communication between the sacred world and the profane world, by means of an intermediary — the victim — which is destroyed in the course of the ceremony." (Hubert & Mauss 1899:13–15)

On the collective nature of magical belief, which they tie directly to the concept of [[mana]]:

> "It is public opinion that makes the magician and creates the power he wields. Thanks to public opinion, he knows everything, can do anything. ... Behind all magic we find the notion of mana, the idea of a force of which the magician's power is but a particular manifestation." (Hubert & Mauss 1904:135, 141)

## Influence and Legacy

Hubert's collaboration with [[marcel-mauss]] produced a body of work that bridged Durkheimian sociology and the comparative study of religion. Their theory of sacrifice as a mechanism of social mediation has influenced thinkers as diverse as [[georges-bataille]] (who radicalized the theme of destructive expenditure), René Girard (whose scapegoat theory of sacrifice develops a different line), and contemporary ritual studies.

Their analysis of [[magic]] and [[mana]] directly informed [[emile-durkheim]]'s *The Elementary Forms of Religious Life* (1912), where the concept of mana is generalized into the theory of the sacred as society's collective representation of itself. Hubert's archaeological work on the Celts, while less celebrated than his sociological writings, established a model for the interdisciplinary integration of archaeology, history, and sociology that remains relevant to the study of material culture.
