## Biography

Georges Davy (1883–1976) was a French sociologist and a prominent member of the [[annee-sociologique|Année Sociologique]] school. A student and collaborator of [[emile-durkheim|Durkheim]] and [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]], Davy specialised in the sociology of law and archaic institutions. His most important contribution to the Maussian tradition was *La Foi Jurée* (1922), a study of contractual obligation in archaic societies that [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] explicitly drew upon in *The Gift* (1925). Davy later served as dean of the Faculty of Letters at the University of Dijon and as a member of the Académie des Sciences Morales et Politiques.

## Core Ideas

**Archaic Contract**: Davy's *La Foi Jurée* (The Sworn Faith) analysed how contractual obligation emerges not from individual consent but from collective ritual — the exchange of prestations, the swearing of oaths, and the invocation of supernatural sanctions. This directly paralleled [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s argument in *The Gift* that the obligation to reciprocate is enforced by the *[[hau]]* — the spirit of the gift.

**Potlatch as Total Contract**: Building on [[franz-boas|Boas]]'s Kwakwaka'wakw ethnography, Davy analysed the [[potlatch]] as a juridical institution in which property destruction, gift-giving, and competitive feasting together constitute a binding contractual mechanism. Where modern law separates contract from ritual, Davy argued that in archaic society the two are inseparable: the contract is the feast, the gift is the oath.

**The Durkheimian Sociology of Law**: Davy extended [[emile-durkheim|Durkheim]]'s distinction between mechanical and organic solidarity into the domain of contract law. Archaic contract, he argued, reflects mechanical solidarity — obligations are collective, ritualised, and enforced by supernatural sanctions. Modern contract reflects organic solidarity — obligations are individual, written, and enforced by the state.

## In the Text

[[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] acknowledged his debt to Davy explicitly in *The Gift*:

> "M. Davy has made a study of 'the sworn faith' (*La Foi jurée*) … which has illuminated for me the entire mechanism of the gift in relation to the contract. … It was Davy who taught me the rule: that one must always return more than one receives."
> (Mauss 1925: Note to Chapter 2; paraphrase based on Mauss's introduction)

On the potlatch as contract:

> "The potlatch is not merely a feast, nor a system of exchange; it is a juridical institution of a very particular kind. The obligations it creates are not those of Roman law — they are obligations of honour, enforceable not by courts but by the entire social body, and sanctioned ultimately by belief in the supernatural power of the gift to avenge its own neglect."
> (Davy 1922:136; paraphrase)

## Influence and Legacy

Davy's *La Foi Jurée* was a direct influence on [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s formulation of the three obligations — to give, to receive, to reciprocate — that structure *The Gift*. His analysis of the [[potlatch]] as a total juridical institution anticipated later anthropological studies of law and dispute resolution in non-state societies. As a senior figure in French academic life, Davy helped maintain the Durkheimian tradition through the interwar and post-war periods.
