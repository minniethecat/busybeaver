## Definition

**Expenditure** (*dépense*) is a concept developed by Georges Bataille in "The Notion of Expenditure" (1933) and *The Accursed Share* (1949) to describe non-productive consumption — the use of resources for ritual, artistic, or spectacular purposes rather than for survival or accumulation. Bataille argued that every society produces a surplus, and the decisive question is how that surplus is expended: in war, religion, luxury, or spectacle. [[potlatch|Potlatch]], as analysed by [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]], was Bataille's central example of "consumption for consumption's sake" (Bataille 1933:12).

## Origin and Development

Bataille's concept of expenditure emerged from his engagement with [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s *The Gift* (1925) within the context of the Collège de Sociologie (1937–1939), a group that included Roger Caillois and Michel Leiris. Where [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] saw in potlatch a system of [[reciprocity|reciprocal obligation]]s, Bataille emphasized its destructive, anti-utilitarian dimension: the competitive burning of wealth challenged capitalist assumptions about rational economic behaviour.

*The Accursed Share* (1949) expanded the argument into a general economic theory: all organisms and societies produce excess energy, and the "accursed share" (*la part maudite*) must be expended — luxuriously or catastrophically. Bataille traced this logic from solar energy through biological growth to human societies, culminating in his analysis of the Marshall Plan as a form of American potlatch.

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Surplus** | Every society produces more than it needs for bare survival |
| **Non-Productive Use** | The surplus must be destroyed or given away, not reinvested |
| **Sovereignty** | Expenditure is an assertion of power — the ability to waste |
| **[[potlatch|Potlatch]] Model** | Competitive gift-giving/destruction exemplifies expenditure |
| **General Economics** | Bataille's "general economy" studies surplus circulation, not scarcity |

## Theoretical Context

Bataille's concept of expenditure is simultaneously an extension and a subversion of [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]. Like [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]], Bataille rejected *homo oeconomicus* and insisted that exchange is fundamentally social. But where [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] emphasized the bonds created by reciprocity, Bataille emphasized the ruptures created by excess — the sacred violence at the heart of the gift.

The concept influenced Jean Baudrillard's theory of symbolic exchange and consumption (*The Consumer Society*, 1970; *Symbolic Exchange and Death*, 1976) and the anti-utilitarian movement in French social science (MAUSS group, founded 1981), which took [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s name for its critique of economism.

## In the Text

Bataille opens "The Notion of Expenditure" by directly challenging the principle of utility:

> "The human activity is not entirely reducible to processes of production and conservation, and consumption must be divided into two distinct parts. The first, reducible part is represented by the use of the minimum necessary for the conservation of life. ... The second part is represented by so-called unproductive expenditures: luxury, mourning, war, cults, the construction of sumptuary monuments, games, spectacles, arts, perverse sexual activity." (Bataille 1933:5–6)

On potlatch specifically:

> "Potlatch excludes all bargaining, and in general it is constituted by a considerable gift of riches, offered openly and with the goal of humiliating, defying, and obligating a rival. The exchange value of the gift results from the fact that the donee, in order to efface the humiliation, must satisfy the obligation ... by means of a new gift, more generous than the first." (Bataille 1933:12)
