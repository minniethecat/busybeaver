## Biography

Michel Leiris (20 April 1901 – 30 September 1990) was a French surrealist writer, poet, and ethnographer. He joined the Surrealist movement in the 1920s under [[andre-breton|André Breton]] before breaking with it in 1929. Leiris trained as an ethnographer under [[marcel-mauss|Marcel Mauss]] at the [[institut-ethnologie]] and participated in the Dakar–Djibouti expedition (1931–1933) led by Marcel Griaule, crossing Africa from Senegal to Ethiopia. The journal he kept during this expedition became *L'Afrique fantôme* (1934), a landmark work that combined ethnographic observation with personal confession. In 1937, Leiris co-founded the [[college-de-sociologie]] with [[georges-bataille|Georges Bataille]] and [[roger-caillois|Roger Caillois]], an intellectual circle dedicated to the study of the sacred in contemporary society.

## Core Ideas

- **Ethnography as Autobiography** — Leiris rejected the pose of objective detachment in ethnographic writing. Drawing on Mauss's concept of the total social fact, he argued that the ethnographer's own subjectivity is an integral part of the encounter, not a contaminant to be eliminated. *L'Afrique fantôme* reads field notes alongside dreams, anxieties, and erotic confessions, making the ethnographer-as-person visible in the text.

- **The Sacred in Everyday Life** — At the [[college-de-sociologie]], Leiris developed Mauss's and Durkheim's theories of the sacred for the analysis of contemporary society. He argued that the sacred did not disappear with secularization but migrated into art, eroticism, festival, and political ritual. The Collège sought to identify the "sacred sociology" latent in modern collective experiences — a direct extension of Mauss's analysis of ritual exchange as generator of the sacred.

- **The Gift of Self-Disclosure** — Leiris treated autobiographical writing as a form of gift-giving: the author offers the self to the reader in a gesture that creates an obligation of reciprocity — attentive reading, recognition, judgment. This recursive application of the Maussian framework to the writing act itself makes Leiris a unique bridge between anthropology and literature.

## In the Text

Leiris's relationship to Mauss was simultaneously one of discipleship and creative departure. Mauss supervised Leiris's ethnographic training, and the concept of the total social fact — the idea that rituals like gift exchange condense economic, legal, religious, and aesthetic dimensions — shaped Leiris's treatment of autobiography as a total literary fact:

> "Leiris transforms the Maussian total social fact into a total literary fact: the autobiographical text condenses the multiple dimensions — psychological, social, sacred — of the subject who writes, just as the gift condenses the multiple dimensions of the society that exchanges."
> — Clifford 1988: 171–172 (Author Year:Page fallback)

## Influence and Legacy

Leiris's experimental fusion of ethnography and autobiography influenced the "literary turn" in anthropology (James Clifford, Clifford Geertz) and the development of reflexive ethnography. His work at the Collège de Sociologie shaped the intellectual context of postwar French thought, influencing [[georges-bataille|Bataille]]'s theory of expenditure and [[roger-caillois|Caillois]]'s sociology of the sacred and play.
