## History

The **Collège de France** is a prestigious public research institution in Paris, founded in 1530 by King Francis I as the "Collège Royal." Unlike French universities, which were constrained by scholastic curricula and theological oversight, the Collège de France was established with the express purpose of teaching knowledge not recognized by the university — initially Greek and Hebrew, later encompassing the full range of sciences and humanities.

Its founding motto, *Docet Omnia* ("It Teaches Everything"), captures its unique mission: professors are elected by their peers and are free to teach whatever they choose, with the sole requirement that their lectures present original, unpublished research. There are no degrees, no fixed curricula, and no examinations — only public lectures open to all.

## Mauss at the Collège

[[marcel-mauss]] was elected to the Chair of Sociology at the Collège de France in 1931, a position he held until his retirement in 1942. The election was the crowning recognition of his intellectual career, coming six years after the publication of *The Gift* (1925) and marking the full institutional acceptance of [[emile-durkheim|Durkheim]]ian sociology within the French academic establishment.

[[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s Collège de France lectures addressed topics including the sociology of prayer, techniques of the body, and the idea of the nation — extending the comparative method of *The Gift* into new domains. His 1934 lecture "Techniques of the Body" (*Les techniques du corps*), delivered at the Collège, became one of his most influential essays, introducing the concept of *[[habitus]]* later developed by [[pierre-bourdieu]].

## Relationship to the Durkheimian School

[[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s election to the Collège de France represented the culmination of the [[annee-sociologique|Durkheimian school]]'s long struggle for institutional legitimacy. [[emile-durkheim]] himself never held a Collège chair (he was at the Sorbonne), and his sociology was frequently attacked by the conservative French academic establishment as "German" (a code word for secular, republican, and anti-clerical). [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s election in 1931 — fourteen years after [[emile-durkheim|Durkheim]]'s death — marked the final acceptance of sociology as a legitimate discipline within the highest reaches of French academia.

## In the Text

The Collège de France does not appear in *The Gift*, but it was from this institution that [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s ideas about [[gift-economy|gift exchange]], reciprocity, and total prestation reached a broader intellectual public in the 1930s. His Collège lectures influenced a generation of French thinkers, including [[claude-levi-strauss]], who attended them and later acknowledged [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] as the foundational figure of French [[structuralism|structural anthropology]].
