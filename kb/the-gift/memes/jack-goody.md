## Biography

Jack Goody (27 July 1919 – 16 July 2015) was a British social anthropologist and a leading figure in the Cambridge school of kinship studies. After serving in the British Army during the Second World War — an experience that included time as a prisoner of war — he studied English at Cambridge before turning to anthropology at Oxford under [[ee-evans-pritchard|E.E. Evans-Pritchard]]. Goody conducted fieldwork among the LoDagaa of northern Ghana, which became the basis for his lifelong comparative project on kinship, inheritance, and the transmission of property across generations. He was appointed William Wyse Professor of Social Anthropology at Cambridge in 1973, succeeding his friend and colleague [[meyer-fortes|Meyer Fortes]]. Goody also worked closely with [[edmund-leach|Edmund Leach]] at Cambridge, and together they formed the core of what became known as the "Cambridge school" — a loose intellectual tradition that combined British social anthropology's ethnographic rigor with comparative-historical methods drawn from law, history, and literary studies.

## Core Ideas

- **Inheritance as Gift Exchange** — Goody's analysis of LoDagaa inheritance showed that property transmission is not a simple legal transfer but a form of intergenerational gift exchange. Mortuary rites, funeral prestations, and the distribution of the deceased's property among kin groups follow patterns that closely parallel [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s model of [[total-prestation|total prestation]]: they simultaneously discharge economic obligations, reaffirm jural relations, and express moral duties toward the dead. In *Death, Property and the Ancestors*, Goody demonstrated that inheritance among the LoDagaa operates as a cycle of gifts — from the living to the dead (through funerary offerings), from the dead to the living (through transmitted property), and laterally among the living (through redistribution at funeral feasts).

- **Diverging Devolution and Maussian Reciprocity** — Goody developed the concept of "diverging devolution" to describe inheritance systems (characteristic of Eurasia) in which property passes to children of both sexes, typically through dowry. He contrasted this with "homogeneous devolution" in sub-Saharan Africa, where property passes within the unilineage and bridewealth rather than dowry is the dominant transfer. Both forms, Goody argued, are systems of gift circulation: bridewealth is a Maussian "total prestation" that circulates persons, cattle, and obligations between lineages, while dowry constitutes a form of inter-family gift that carries status, wealth, and women between households. These material transfers are simultaneously economic, legal, and symbolic — exactly the total social facts Mauss described in [[gift-economy|The Gift]].

- **Literacy and the Transformation of Exchange** — Goody's work on literacy introduced a dimension largely absent from Mauss's original analysis: the role of writing technology in transforming how gifts and obligations are recorded and conceptualized. In literate societies, inheritance becomes codified into law, gift obligations become written contracts, and the flexibility of oral reciprocity gives way to fixed, archival forms of debt and credit. Goody argued that the shift from oral to literate modes of recording exchange fundamentally altered the nature of reciprocity, moving it from the flexible, face-to-face morality that Mauss described to impersonal, legally enforced transactions.

> Goody's comparative method placed inheritance and property transfer at the center of kinship analysis. Where Fortes had shown that kinship was a moral economy of reciprocal obligations, Goody demonstrated that the specific institutional mechanisms of property devolution — who gets what, from whom, and under what ritual conditions — determined the structure of the domestic domain across civilizations. (Goody 1976:87–112)

## In the Text

> Fortes's work established the Cambridge school of kinship studies and deeply influenced later anthropologists such as Jack Goody and Edmund Leach. The transmission of property, the obligations of inheritance, and the ritual prestations that accompany death and succession became the shared analytic vocabulary of this tradition.

## Influence and Legacy

Goody's comparative project bridged anthropology, history, and literary studies in ways that transformed all three fields. His work on literacy influenced historians of the book such as Roger Chartier and Elizabeth Eisenstein, while his comparative analysis of kinship and inheritance provided an alternative to the structuralist approaches of Claude Levi-Strauss. Within anthropology, Goody's insistence on combining ethnographic fieldwork with large-scale historical comparison remains a model of how to connect the particular and the general. His engagement with [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s gift theory — though often implicit rather than foregrounded — established inheritance and property devolution as domains in which the dynamics of gift, reciprocity, and obligation could be studied across the widest possible comparative canvas. Together with [[meyer-fortes|Fortes]] and [[edmund-leach|Leach]], Goody defined the Cambridge school's distinctive approach: ethnographically grounded, historically informed, and theoretically anchored in a Maussian understanding of social life as constituted through cycles of giving, receiving, and repaying.
