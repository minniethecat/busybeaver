## Biography

Lewis Hyde (born 1945) is an American writer, essayist, and cultural critic. He taught creative writing at Kenyon College and Harvard University, and served as the Richard L. Thomas Professor of Creative Writing at Kenyon College. His book *The Gift: Creativity and the Artist in the Modern World* (1983), later republished as *The Gift: How the Creative Spirit Transforms the World*, applied [[marcel-mauss]]'s theory of the gift to the artistic and creative process, becoming one of the most widely read works of gift theory outside academic anthropology.

## Core Ideas

### Art as Gift

Hyde's central thesis is that a work of art exists simultaneously in two economies: a *gift economy* (where it circulates, inspires, and generates new creative work) and a *market economy* (where it is bought and sold). The creative act itself operates in the gift sphere — the artist receives inspiration (a gift) and passes it on in the work. The transformation of art into commodity, while inevitable, can suffocate the gift circulation that sustains creativity (Hyde 1983:xi–xviii).

### The Gift Must Move

Drawing directly on [[marcel-mauss]]'s analysis of the Maori *hau*, Hyde argues that the essential property of a gift is that it must be passed on. A gift that is hoarded loses its vitality; a gift that circulates increases. This principle applies to ideas, stories, and artistic inspiration as much as to material objects. The artist's obligation is not to possess inspiration but to transmit it (Hyde 1983:4–9).

### Eros and the Gift

Hyde contrasts *eros*-based exchange (gift circulation, which creates attachment and transformation) with *logos*-based exchange (market transactions, which separate subject from object). The gift belongs to the sphere of eros — it bridges self and other, transforms both giver and receiver, and resists the calculation of equivalence (Hyde 1983:81–84).

## In the Text

On the movement of the gift:

> "The gift must always move. There are other forms of property that stand still, that mark a boundary or resist movement, but the gift keeps going. It is the gift that moves, and the movement of the gift is the life of the gift."
> (Hyde 1983:4)

On the artist's relationship to inspiration:

> "An essential portion of any artist's labor is not creation so much as invocation. Part of the work cannot be made, it must be received; and we cannot have this gift except, perhaps, by supplication, by courting, by creating within ourselves that 'begging bowl' to which the gift is drawn."
> (Hyde 1983:13)

## Influence and Legacy

Hyde's *The Gift* has been influential far beyond anthropology — it is widely read among artists, writers, musicians, and activists. His framing of creativity as gift circulation has resonated in debates about copyright, open-source culture, and the commodification of art. Hyde gave the Maussian gift paradigm a literary and spiritual register that academic anthropology rarely achieved, making it accessible to a broad public while remaining faithful to Mauss's core insight: that the gift is a total social fact, not merely an economic transaction. Writers including Margaret Atwood and David Foster Wallace have cited Hyde's influence, and his work shaped discussions of the cultural commons in the digital age.
