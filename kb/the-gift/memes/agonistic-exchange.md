## Definition

**Agonistic exchange** (from Greek *agon* — contest, struggle) is a form of [[gift-economy|gift exchange]] in which giving is competitive rather than cooperative: the giver seeks to overwhelm, humiliate, or defeat the recipient through excessive generosity that cannot be reciprocated. The potlatch of the Kwakiutl and other [[pacific-northwest|Northwest Coast]] peoples is the classic ethnographic example. [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] described it as "the 'war of property' — the struggle of the nobles to establish a hierarchy among themselves from which the clan as a whole benefits" ([[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] 1925:6).

## Origin and Development

[[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] identified agonistic exchange as one pole of the gift spectrum, contrasting with the more balanced, [[reciprocity|reciprocal exchange]] of the Trobriand [[kula-exchange|kula]]. In the potlatch, the competitive dimension is explicit: chiefs vie to out-give each other, distributing or destroying vast quantities of blankets, coppers, canoes, and other valuables. The goal is not to create harmonious bonds but to establish rank — "to flatten the name" of one's rival.

Franz Boas's *Kwakiutl Ethnography* (published posthumously, 1966) provided the ethnographic basis for [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s analysis, documenting how a Kwakiutl chief would "fight with property" — giving feasts, distributing wealth, and even destroying coppers to demonstrate superiority. The term "agonistic" was popularized by later anthropologists (Sahlins, Strathern) and political theorists (Mouffe, Connolly) who recognized in [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s analysis a model of competitive but rule-bound social interaction.

Ruth Benedict, in *Patterns of Culture* (1934), described the Kwakiutl as "megalomaniacal" — a characterization [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] rejected, arguing that agonistic exchange follows a logic that is social, not psychological.

## Key Elements

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Competition** | Giving is a contest — the goal is to give more than can be returned |
| **Rank** | Excessive giving establishes hierarchical superiority over rivals |
| **Destruction** | At the extreme, valuables are destroyed rather than given — the purest form of competitive [[expenditure]] |
| **Rule-Bound** | Despite its competitive nature, agonistic exchange follows strict ceremonial rules |
| **Collective Benefit** | Individual rivalry between chiefs benefits the clan through redistribution |

## Theoretical Context

Agonistic exchange reveals a dimension of gift-giving that utilitarian models cannot capture: the drive to give away, even to destroy, one's wealth. This "irrational" behaviour challenges the assumption that economic actors always seek to maximize utility. [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s analysis shows that prestige, honour, and rank are not mere by-products of economic behaviour but its primary goals in many societies.

The concept has been influential in political theory, where "agonistic pluralism" (Chantal Mouffe) draws on the idea that conflict, when channeled through rule-bound institutions, can be productive rather than destructive. It also resonates with Bataille's concept of [[expenditure]] and Nietzsche's analysis of gift-giving virtue in *Thus Spoke Zarathustra*.

## In the Text

[[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] describes the competitive logic of agonistic exchange:

> "In potlatch, one proves one's fortune by expending it, by distributing it, by humiliating others with it, by 'flattening' them under the weight of the name. ... The obligation to give is the essence of potlatch. A chief must give potlatches for himself, his son, his son-in-law, his daughter, and for the dead. He can preserve his authority over the tribe and village, even over his family, he can maintain his rank among chiefs ... only if he can prove that he is favoured by the spirits and by fortune, that he is possessed by it, and that he possesses it." (Mauss 1925:37–39)
