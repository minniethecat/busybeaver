## Definition

**Barter** is the direct exchange of goods and services without the use of money or a common medium of exchange. In classical economics, barter was conceived as the original form of exchange that preceded the invention of money (Smith 1776:24). [[marcel-mauss|Marcel Mauss]] fundamentally challenged this narrative, arguing through ethnographic evidence that "primitive" economies were never based on barter but on [[gift-economy|gift exchange]], credit, and [[reciprocity|reciprocal obligation]] ([[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] 1925:94–95).

## Origin and Development

The myth of barter as the origin of economic exchange can be traced to Adam Smith's *The Wealth of Nations* (1776), which imagined a "propensity to truck, barter, and exchange" as innate to human nature. This narrative served as the founding myth of modern economics — the idea that money emerged naturally from the inconveniences of barter.

[[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] devoted significant attention to dismantling this myth in *The Gift* (1925). Drawing on [[bronislaw-malinowski|Malinowski]]'s Trobriand ethnography, Boas's Kwakiutl studies, and extensive comparative data, [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] demonstrated that the earliest known forms of exchange were not individual barter transactions but collective [[gift-economy|gift exchange]]s governed by complex [[obligation]]s: the triple [[obligation]] to give, to receive, and to reciprocate ([[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] 1925:39–43).

Karl Polanyi later systematised this critique in *The Great Transformation* (1944) and *Trade and Market in the Early Empires* (1957), distinguishing between reciprocity, redistribution, and market exchange as distinct modes of economic integration.

## Key Elements

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Direct Exchange** | Goods trade directly for other goods without intermediary currency |
| **Non-Monetary** | No common standard of value or medium of exchange |
| **Historical Specificity** | Ethnographic evidence shows barter often occurs between strangers or enemy groups, not within communities |
| **Contrast with Gift** | Gift exchange creates ongoing social bonds; barter is a discrete, self-liquidating transaction |
| **Silent Barter** | A variant where parties leave goods at a neutral site and withdraw without face-to-face contact |

## Theoretical Context

[[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s critique of the barter myth is integral to his broader argument against utilitarian individualism. If the earliest human economies were based on [[gift-economy|gift exchange]] rather than barter, then the rational, self-interested *homo economicus* is a historical construct rather than a human universal. [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s position was further developed by the substantivist school in economic anthropology (Polanyi, Dalton, Sahlins), which argued that the economy is always embedded in social relations (Polanyi 1944:46–50).

The debate has contemporary relevance: modern experiments with complementary currencies and gift economies draw on [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s insight that exchange is fundamentally a social act, not merely an economic one.

## In the Text

[[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] directly addresses the barter question in the conclusion of *The Gift*:

> "It is our western societies which have recently made man an 'economic animal.' ... *Homo oeconomicus* is not behind us but before us, like the moral man, the man of duty, the scientific man, and the reasonable man. Man has been for a long time something else, and he has not been a machine for very long, made complicated by a calculating machine." (Mauss 1925:100–101)

This passage challenges the naturalization of market rationality by historicizing it — the economic man is not an eternal truth but a recent Western invention.
