## Definition

**Primitive classification** (*classifications primitives*) is the theory, developed by [[emile-durkheim|Émile Durkheim]] and [[marcel-mauss|Marcel Mauss]] in their 1903 essay *De quelques formes primitives de classification*, that the most fundamental categories of human understanding — space, time, genus, species — are not innate to the mind but are modelled on forms of social organization. "The classification of things reproduces the classification of men" ([[emile-durkheim|Durkheim]] and [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] 1903:82).

## Origin and Development

*Primitive Classification* (1903) was a pivotal text in the [[annee-sociologique|Durkheimian school]]'s project to establish a sociological epistemology — a theory of knowledge grounded in social facts rather than Kantian a priori categories or British empiricism. Drawing on Australian Aboriginal ethnography (Spencer and Gillen, Howitt) and limited Chinese and North American materials, [[emile-durkheim|Durkheim]] and [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] argued that the logical hierarchy of genus and species mirrors the social hierarchy of clans, phratries, and [[personne|moi]]eties.

The essay directly influenced [[claude-levi-strauss|Claude Lévi-Strauss]]'s structuralism — *The Savage Mind* (1962) and *Totemism* (1962) are extended engagements with its arguments — and Mary Douglas's *Purity and Danger* (1966) and *Natural Symbols* (1970), which developed the [[emile-durkheim|Durkheim]]ian sociology of classification into a general theory of cultural categories. Michel Foucault's *The Order of Things* (1966) engages similar questions from an archaeological rather than sociological perspective.

## Key Elements

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Social Morphology** | The structure of society (clans, [[personne|moi]]eties) provides the template for logical classification |
| **Collective Origin** | Categories are not individual inventions but collective representations |
| **Hierarchy** | Classificatory hierarchies (genus → species) mirror social hierarchies (phratry → clan) |
| **Affective Basis** | Classification is driven by social sentiment, not pure cognition |
| **Historical Variation** | Classification systems change as social organization changes |

## Theoretical Context

[[emile-durkheim|Durkheim]] and [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s argument is a radical extension of [[emile-durkheim|Durkheim]]'s earlier claim in *The Rules of Sociological Method* (1895) that social facts must be explained by other social facts. If even the most basic logical categories — which Kant had argued were a priori conditions of experience — are social in origin, then sociology claims a jurisdiction previously reserved for philosophy.

[[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] later returned to these themes in his work on [[magic]] (1902), the gift (1925), and the [[personne|person]] (1938), each time demonstrating that apparently universal categories (causality, exchange, selfhood) have culturally specific forms.

## In the Text

The essay's central thesis is stated with characteristic ambition:

> "The first logical categories were social categories; the first classes of things were classes of men into which these things were integrated. It was because men were grouped, and thought of themselves in the form of groups, that in their ideas they grouped other things, and in the beginning the two modes of grouping were merged to the point of being indistinct." (Durkheim and Mauss 1903:82–83)

And on the logical implications:

> "Every classification implies a hierarchical order for which neither the tangible world nor our mind gives us the model. ... It is society that furnished the canvas on which logical thought has worked." (Durkheim and Mauss 1903:86)
