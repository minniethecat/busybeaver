## Biography

Pierre Bourdieu (1930–2002) was a French sociologist, anthropologist, and public intellectual. Born in Denguin, a village in Béarn, southwest France, he studied philosophy at the École Normale Supérieure under Louis Althusser and Michel Foucault. After military service in Algeria (1955–1958), he conducted ethnographic fieldwork among Kabyle peasants, which became the foundation of his theoretical work. He held the chair in sociology at the Collège de France from 1982 until his death and was one of the most cited social scientists of the twentieth century.

## Core Ideas

| 概念 | 说明 |
|------|------|
| **Habitus** | Durable, transposable dispositions — structured structures predisposed to function as structuring structures |
| **Field (Champ)** | A structured social space with its own rules, stakes, and forms of capital |
| **Capital** | Economic, cultural, social, and symbolic — resources that confer power within a field |
| **Symbolic Violence** | The imposition of categories of thought upon dominated social agents who take the social order to be natural |
| **Gift and Counter-Gift** | Bourdieu's analysis of the temporal structure of gift exchange — the interval is what makes it work |

## In the Text

Bourdieu on the temporal structure of gift exchange, directly engaging Mauss:

> "The gift, if it is to be recognized as a gift, must not be returned immediately. ... The interval between gift and counter-gift is what allows the gift to be experienced as an inaugural act of generosity, without a past and without a future — and it is this self-deception, this sincere fiction, that is the very truth of the gift." (Bourdieu 1977:5–6)

On the *habitus* as Mauss's concept developed:

> "The *habitus* is precisely this immanent law, *lex insita*, laid down in each agent by his earliest upbringing. ... The word *habitus* ... is a concept that Mauss, and others after him, have used to designate the set of acquired dispositions." (Bourdieu 1977:81, 214)

## Influence and Legacy

Bourdieu's practice theory is one of the most influential frameworks in contemporary social science. His re-reading of Mauss — particularly his emphasis on the temporal interval in gift exchange — fundamentally reshaped anthropological understandings of reciprocity. The concept of *habitus* he developed from Mauss's original formulation is now standard analytical vocabulary across sociology, anthropology, education, and cultural studies.
