## Biography

Annette Weiner (1933–1997) was an American cultural anthropologist and a key figure in the re-examination of [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]ian exchange theory. She conducted her primary fieldwork in the [[trobriand-islands|Trobriand Islands]], the same ethnographic site that [[bronislaw-malinowski|Malinowski]] made famous. Her 1976 study of Trobriand women's exchange practices revealed a parallel system of value circulation — women's distribution of banana-leaf bundles and skirts — that [[bronislaw-malinowski|Malinowski]] and generations of anthropologists had overlooked.

## Core Ideas

**Inalienable Possessions**: Weiner's central theoretical contribution. She argued that the most valued objects in any society — crown jewels, sacred relics, ancestral heirlooms, intellectual property — are precisely those that must *not* be given away. This reversed the [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]ian emphasis on reciprocity by foregrounding the drive to keep.

**Keeping-While-Giving**: The paradox that even when objects circulate as gifts, the giver retains a symbolic claim. The recipient receives the object but not full ownership; the giver's identity, rank, and history remain attached to the object. This insight was already present in [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s analysis of Māori *[[taonga]]* — objects bearing the *[[hau]]* of their givers — but Weiner argued [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] failed to follow through on its implications.

**Women's Exchange**: Before *Inalienable Possessions*, Weiner had shown that Trobriand women's mortuary distributions constituted an exchange system as complex as men's [[kula-exchange|kula]]. Women produced and circulated bundles of banana leaves and fibre skirts during mortuary rituals, creating their own spheres of value, renown, and political influence.

## In the Text

Weiner's 1992 work explicitly frames her argument as a return to [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]:

> "In this book, I take up the challenge that Mauss himself posed: what power resides in the object given that causes its recipient to pay it back? … I argue that the motivation for returning a gift lies in the inalienable possessions that the giver seeks to keep. … What motivates reciprocity is its reverse — the desire to keep something back from the pressures of give and take. Something is withheld from the very beginning."
> (Weiner 1992:5–6, 40)

On Trobriand women's exchange:

> "Women's wealth … is not simply a symbolic marker of male status. Women's exchange activities constitute a separate domain of power, one in which women control the production, circulation, and regeneration of valued objects that are essential to the reproduction of matrilineal identity."
> (Weiner 1976:11)

## Influence and Legacy

Weiner's work transformed the anthropology of exchange. Her concept of inalienable possessions has been taken up in studies of cultural property, heritage, intellectual property law, and museum studies. In anthropology, her critique of [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] helped shift the field from a narrow focus on reciprocity norms toward a broader understanding of how objects mediate identity, rank, and temporal continuity across generations. Maurice Godelier's *The Enigma of the Gift* (1996) engaged directly with Weiner's argument, as did later work by David Graeber on value theory.
