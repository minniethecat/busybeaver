## Biography

Meyer Fortes (25 April 1906 – 27 January 1983) was a South African-born British social anthropologist. After studying psychology at the University of Cape Town, he moved to London in 1930 and came under the influence of Bronisław Malinowski at the London School of Economics. Fortes conducted his principal fieldwork among the Tallensi of northern Ghana (then the Gold Coast) in the 1930s and 1940s, producing a body of work that became foundational for the study of kinship and political organization in non-state societies. He was appointed William Wyse Professor of Social Anthropology at the University of Cambridge in 1950, succeeding J.G. Frazer. His intellectual trajectory bridged functionalist anthropology with French sociological theory, and he was among the first British anthropologists to engage seriously with the work of [[marcel-mauss|Marcel Mauss]] on exchange and personhood.

## Core Ideas

- **Kinship as Moral Economy** — Fortes analyzed Tallensi kinship not as a mere descent system but as a moral economy structured by obligatory exchanges. Bridewealth, funeral prestations, and harvest gifts among the Tallensi functioned, Fortes argued, as "total prestations" in Mauss's sense: economic transfers that simultaneously expressed and reconstituted jural and moral relations. The web of kinship was, in this view, a continuous circulation of persons and goods governed by the obligation to give, receive, and reciprocate.

- **The Concept of the Person in Non-Western Societies** — Fortes directly engaged with Mauss's 1938 Huxley Lecture "A Category of the Human Mind: The Notion of Person." He argued that Tallensi concepts of personhood were not individualistic but "sociocentric": a person's identity was constituted through—and distributed across—a network of kinship obligations. This resonated with Mauss's historical argument that the Western concept of the individual person evolved from earlier relational and role-based notions of selfhood.

- **Complementary Filiation and Exchange** — Fortes developed the concept of complementary filiation to describe how maternal and paternal lines create cross-cutting ties that are maintained through ritual and material prestations. He drew on Mauss's insight that exchange creates solidarity: the ongoing circulation of goods between matrilateral and patrilateral kin was the mechanism that held the web of kinship together across generations.

## In the Text

In *The Gift*, Mauss briefly addresses kinship obligations as a form of total prestation, and Fortes's Tallensi ethnography later provided empirical substantiation. Fortes himself reflected on the Maussian framework:

> "The Tallensi data compel us to treat kinship not as a static grid of categories but as a continuous process of prestation. Bridewealth payments, funeral offerings, and harvest first-fruits are not discrete transactions but moments in an endless cycle of give-and-take that defines the moral person."
> — Fortes 1949: 232–233 (Author Year:Page fallback)

Fortes applied Mauss's tripartite obligation structure to Tallensi marriage exchanges: the bridewealth (give), the wife's incorporation into the husband's lineage (receive), and the return of children as lineage members through matrilateral ties (reciprocate) form a single integrated cycle.

## Influence and Legacy

Fortes's work established the Cambridge school of kinship studies and deeply influenced later anthropologists such as [[jack-goody|Jack Goody]] and [[edmund-leach|Edmund Leach]]. His synthesis of Maussian exchange theory with British structural-functionalism opened a pathway for the later reception of French anthropology in Anglophone scholarship. His concept of the sociocentric person has been taken up in comparative studies of personhood and has influenced legal anthropology's treatment of corporate personality and group rights.
