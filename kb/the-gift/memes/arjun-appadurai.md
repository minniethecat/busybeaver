## Biography

Arjun Appadurai (born 1949) is an Indian-American anthropologist and professor at New York University and the Hertie School in Berlin. Born in Mumbai and educated at Brandeis University and the University of Chicago, Appadurai is known for his work on globalization, material culture, and the cultural dimensions of economic life. His edited volume *The Social Life of Things* (1986) opened a new paradigm in the anthropology of value and exchange.

## Core Ideas

### The Social Life of Things

Appadurai's most cited contribution to gift theory is the argument that the gift/commodity distinction is not a property of objects but of the *regimes of value* through which things move. A thing can be a commodity at one moment, a gift at another, and a sacred inalienable possession at another — it is the social context, not the object's inherent nature, that determines its status (Appadurai 1986:13–15).

### Methodological Fetishism

Appadurai proposes "methodological fetishism" — following the thing itself through its social trajectory — as a way to escape the sterile debate between [[marcel-mauss]]'s gift theory and [[karl-polanyi]]'s substantivism. By tracing how objects move between value regimes, the analyst can observe the politics of classification itself (Appadurai 1986:5).

### Commodity Candidacy

Every thing has the potential to be a commodity under certain conditions — what Appadurai calls its "commodity candidacy." The question is not whether something is a gift or a commodity, but under what conditions it enters and exits the commodity phase of its social life (Appadurai 1986:13–16).

## In the Text

On moving beyond the gift/commodity binary:

> "We can best understand the relation between gift and commodity by following the things themselves, for their meanings are inscribed in their forms, their uses, their trajectories. It is only through the analysis of these trajectories that we can interpret the human transactions and calculations that enliven things."
> (Appadurai 1986:5)

On the regimes of value:

> "The commodity is not one kind of thing rather than another, but one phase in the life of some things. The same thing may be treated as a commodity at one time and not at another. And the same thing may, at the same time, be a commodity for one person and something quite different for another."
> (Appadurai 1986:13)

## Influence and Legacy

Appadurai's *The Social Life of Things* transformed material culture studies and economic anthropology by dissolving the rigid gift/commodity binary that [[chris-gregory]] had formalized. The volume's contributors — including [[igor-kopytoff]] on the "cultural biography of things" — demonstrated that objects move between regimes of value in ways that neither Maussian gift theory nor Marxian commodity theory could fully capture. Appadurai's framework influenced subsequent work on [[gift-economy]], [[inalienable-possessions]], and the anthropology of value.
