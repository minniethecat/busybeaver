## Biography

**Marcel Mauss** (10 May 1872 – 10 February 1950) was a French sociologist and anthropologist, best known for his essay *The Gift* (*Essai sur le don*, 1925) and his influential theory of [[gift-economy|gift exchange]] and reciprocity. He was the nephew of [[emile-durkheim]] and a key member of the [[annee-sociologique|Année Sociologique]] school, which he continued after [[emile-durkheim|Durkheim]]'s death in 1917.

Mauss held the Chair of History of Religions of Non-Civilized Peoples at the [[ecole-pratique-hautes-etudes]] and was a co-founder of the [[institut-ethnologie]] at the University of Paris in 1925. In 1931 he was elected to the Chair of Sociology at the [[college-de-france]], a position he held until 1942.

## Core Ideas

### The Gift and Reciprocity

Mauss argued that in archaic societies, the exchange of gifts is governed by three [[obligation]]s — to give, to receive, and to reciprocate. This system of [[total-prestation]] creates and maintains social solidarity.

### The *hau* (Spirit of the Gift)

Drawing on Māori ethnography, Mauss proposed that the gift contains something of the giver — a spiritual essence (*[[hau]]*) that compels its return.

### Total Social Fact

The idea that certain social phenomena (like the [[potlatch]] or [[kula-exchange]]) are simultaneously economic, legal, religious, and aesthetic — they express the totality of social life.

### Comparative Method

Mauss pioneered the comparative study of social institutions across cultures, influencing [[structuralism|structural anthropology]].

## In the Text

Mauss describes the mechanism of the gift in the conclusion to his essay:

> It is by considering the whole that we have been able to detect what is essential, the living movement, the fleeting moment when society, and men, become aware of themselves and their situation in relation to others.
>
> （Mauss 1966:77–78）

## Influence and Legacy

Mauss's work on the gift economy influenced [[claude-levi-strauss]]'s structuralism, [[karl-polanyi]]'s economic anthropology, and [[pierre-bourdieu]]'s theory of practice. His concept of the gift remains foundational in economic anthropology and continues to inform debates on reciprocity, social solidarity, and the moral economy.
