## Biography

Marcel Griaule (1898–1956) was a French ethnographer trained under [[marcel-mauss]] at the [[institut-ethnologie]] in Paris. After abandoning an early career in engineering, he turned to anthropology and rose to prominence as the leader of the Dakar–Djibouti expedition (1931–1933), the most ambitious French ethnographic field mission of the interwar period. The expedition crossed Africa from Senegal to Ethiopia, collecting over 3,500 objects for the Musee d'Ethnographie du Trocadero and producing extensive documentary records. Griaule subsequently became the world's foremost authority on the Dogon people of Mali, publishing his landmark work *Dieu d'eau: Entretiens avec Ogotemmeli* (Conversations with Ogotemmeli) in 1948, which revealed the sophistication of Dogon cosmology to a Western audience for the first time.

| 1948 | *Dieu d'eau: Entretiens avec Ogotemmeli* | Groundbreaking revelation of Dogon cosmology through dialogues with the blind hunter Ogotemmeli; established the Dogon as a case of advanced indigenous philosophical system |

## Core Ideas

**Dogon Cosmology as Systematic Knowledge.** Griaule demonstrated that Dogon oral tradition encoded a fully developed cosmological system encompassing astronomy, metaphysics, and social organization—challenging Eurocentric assumptions that non-literate peoples lacked abstract, systematic thought. This aligned with the Maussian project of revealing the rationality internal to "primitive" classification.

**The Expedition as Total Ethnographic Act.** Griaule conceived the Dakar–Djibouti expedition as a direct application of [[marcel-mauss]]'s concept of the total social fact (*fait social total*). The mission was deliberately multi-disciplinary, combining linguists, musicologists, cartographers, and physical anthropologists in a single team. Just as Mauss argued that phenomena like [[total-prestation]] in the [[gift-economy]] express the entirety of social life simultaneously, Griaule organized fieldwork as a total act of ethnographic capture—artifacts, ritual performances, kinship interviews, and astronomical observations were collected in parallel under one unified research design.

**Collaborative and Generational Fieldwork.** Griaule pioneered the French tradition of long-term, team-based ethnographic engagement. He trained a cohort of researchers—most notably [[michel-leiris]], who served as secretary-archivist on the Dakar–Djibouti mission, and Germaine Dieterlen, his lifelong collaborator on Dogon studies—who extended his methods across multiple generations. This model contrasted with the solitary fieldwork style of British social anthropology and reflected the Maussian insistence that social facts require collective interpretation.

## Influence and Legacy

Griaule's work transformed Western understanding of African intellectual traditions, though his methods later attracted criticism. Posthumous re-evaluations by anthropologists such as James Clifford and Walter van Beek questioned whether Griaule had projected his own philosophical interests onto Ogotemmeli's testimony, or whether the revelations of *Dieu d'eau* represented a genuine transmission of esoteric Dogon knowledge. Nevertheless, his insistence on taking indigenous cosmology seriously as philosophy—not merely as "belief"—influenced later ethnographic writing and the interpretive turn in cultural anthropology. His institutional legacy continued through the Centre National de la Recherche Scientifique (CNRS) and the Musee de l'Homme, where he trained a new generation of Africanist ethnographers. Griaule also bridged French ethnography with the surrealist and avant-garde circles of interwar Paris; his association with figures like [[michel-leiris]] and [[georges-bataille]] placed anthropological knowledge at the center of artistic and literary modernism.
