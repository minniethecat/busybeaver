## Biography

David Graeber (1961–2020) was an American anthropologist and political activist. He taught at Yale University, Goldsmiths (University of London), and the London School of Economics. His early ethnographic fieldwork in Madagascar examined the persistence of [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]ian gift logic in rural communities. He was a central organiser of the Occupy Wall Street movement (2011), credited with the slogan "We are the 99%." Graeber's work bridged academic anthropology, political economy, and public intellectual life.

## Core Ideas

**Value as Action Made Meaningful**: In *Toward an Anthropological Theory of Value*, Graeber argued that prevailing theories — Marxist labour theory, neoclassical marginal utility, and Saussurean linguistic value — all fail to account for how value emerges in social action. Drawing on [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] and [[karl-polanyi|Polanyi]], he proposed that value is the process through which creative human action crystallises into recognised social forms. [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s gift — an act that creates both a relationship and a recognised worth — is the paradigmatic example.

**Gift vs. Debt**: In *Debt: The First 5,000 Years*, Graeber argued that the gift and the debt are two fundamentally different ways of organising obligation. [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s three obligations — to give, to receive, to reciprocate — describe a form of human relationship that debt morality systematically erases. Where gifts create ongoing social connection, debt creates quantifiable, impersonal obligation that can be settled and discharged. This argument directly challenges the economistic reading of the gift as "delayed barter."

**Baseline Communism**: Graeber proposed that everyday human society is built on a baseline of mutual aid — small acts of sharing and cooperation that precede any formal exchange logic. This "baseline communism" operates according to the principle "from each according to their abilities, to each according to their needs," and is what makes all other economic systems — including [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]ian gift exchange — possible.

## In the Text

On Mauss and value:

> "What Mauss was really describing was the process by which the value of a person — or more precisely, the value of a particular person's actions — becomes objectified, becomes something that can be seen, and therefore compared, measured, and thought about. The gift is the mechanism by which we make the value of our own actions visible to ourselves."
> (Graeber 2001:45)

On the gift-debt distinction:

> "The critical difference between a gift and a debt is that a gift creates a relationship between persons, while a debt creates a relationship between things — an obligation that can be precisely quantified and, at least in principle, cancelled. This is why [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] insisted that gifts can never truly be repaid: the relationship they create is ongoing by definition."
> (Graeber 2011:92)

## Influence and Legacy

Graeber's work brought [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]ian anthropology into contemporary political and economic debates. His reading of the gift as an alternative to debt morality influenced both academic anthropology and activist movements. His untimely death in 2020 at age 59 was mourned across academic and activist communities worldwide.
