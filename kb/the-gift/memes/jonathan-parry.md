## Biography

Jonathan Parry is a British anthropologist and professor at the London School of Economics. He conducted extensive ethnographic fieldwork in central India, studying caste, kinship, death rituals, and exchange practices in the city of Banaras (Varanasi) and the industrial town of Bhilai. His comparative work on the "Indian gift" has been foundational to debates about the universality of Maussian reciprocity.

## Core Ideas

### The Indian Gift

Parry's most influential argument is that in Hindu India, the gift (*dān*) is understood to carry the moral substance of the donor — particularly their sins and inauspiciousness. This inverts the Maussian model, where the gift carries the donor's spirit (*hau*) as a positive force binding recipient to donor. The "Indian gift" is a poisoned gift: accepting *dān* from a patron transfers spiritual danger to the recipient (Parry 1986:460).

### Maussian Reciprocity Questioned

Parry challenged the universalism of [[marcel-mauss]]'s three obligations (give/receive/reciprocate). In the Hindu context, the ideological emphasis is on giving without expectation of return — the "pure gift" — even while the Brahman recipient is ritually obligated to accept. This reveals Maussian reciprocity as culturally specific rather than universal (Parry 1986:466–468).

### Death and Exchange

In *Death in Banaras* (1994), Parry extended gift analysis to mortuary rituals, showing how funerary gifts (*śrāddha*) transform the deceased into an ancestor through cycles of giving that parallel and invert the logic of the [[kula-exchange]].

## In the Text

On the distinctive logic of the Indian gift:

> "The Indian gift — or at least the gift to a Brahman — carries the sins of the donor. It is a vessel for the transmission of inauspiciousness, of death, of evil. This seems to be the exact inverse of the Maori *hau*, which carries the 'spirit of the gift' as a positive force compelling return."
> (Parry 1986:460)

On the limits of Mauss's universalism:

> "The ideology of the pure gift — the gift that should not be reciprocated — is central to Hindu thought about *dān*. This is a category of giving that Mauss's tripartite schema of giving, receiving, and reciprocating simply cannot accommodate."
> (Parry 1986:467)

## Influence and Legacy

Parry's essay "The Gift, the Indian Gift, and the 'Indian Gift'" has been one of the most cited works in comparative gift theory. It directly influenced [[maurice-godelier]]'s *The Enigma of the Gift* (1996), which took up the question of what must be kept out of circulation. Parry's critique of Maussian universalism opened space for culturally specific gift theories, complementing [[marilyn-strathern]]'s Melanesian critique and [[annette-weiner]]'s concept of [[inalienable-possessions]].
