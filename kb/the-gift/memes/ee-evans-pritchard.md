## Biography

Edward Evan Evans-Pritchard (21 September 1902 – 11 September 1973) was a British social anthropologist and a central figure in the development of modern anthropology. After studying modern history at Oxford, he trained in anthropology at the London School of Economics under [[bronislaw-malinowski|Malinowski]] and [[charles-seligman|C.G. Seligman]]. He conducted extensive fieldwork among the Azande (1926–1930) and the Nuer (1930–1936) in southern Sudan, producing two of the most influential ethnographic monographs of the twentieth century. Evans-Pritchard succeeded [[ar-radcliffe-brown|Radcliffe-Brown]] as Professor of Social Anthropology at Oxford in 1946, where he trained a generation of anthropologists including Mary Douglas and [[victor-turner|Victor Turner]]. His later work turned toward history and the analysis of anthropological thought as a form of translation across cultural boundaries.

## Core Ideas

- **Segmentary Lineage and Gift Obligations** — Evans-Pritchard's analysis of Nuer segmentary lineage systems showed how kinship groups fission and merge according to context, with bridewealth cattle transfers functioning as the primary mechanism binding segments together. These cattle prestations — gifts between lineages that create afinal ties — operate precisely as [[marcel-mauss|Maussian]] total prestations: simultaneously economic transactions, legal contracts, and ritual acts.

- **Sacrifice as Gift to the Divine** — In *Nuer Religion*, Evans-Pritchard developed a theory of sacrifice that drew on Mauss's and [[robert-hertz|Hertz]]'s work. Nuer cattle sacrifice is not a simple offering but a total social act: it creates a bond between the human and the divine through the gift of life, establishes commensality among participants who consume the meat, and marks transitions in the social status of the sacrificer. The sacrificial gift, like Mauss's *hau*, carries something of the giver to the recipient.

- **Anthropology as Translation** — Evans-Pritchard argued that anthropology is closer to history and the humanities than to natural science, challenging the positivist aspirations of Radcliffe-Brown's structural-functionalism. Understanding another society's concepts requires translating their modes of thought — a hermeneutic position that brought Maussian holism into dialogue with interpretive approaches.

## In the Text

Evans-Pritchard's treatment of Nuer sacrifice explicitly engages Maussian gift theory to explain why cattle must be offered and consumed collectively:

> "The sacrifice is a gift, and like every gift described by Mauss it creates a bond of reciprocity. The spirit receives the life of the animal; the community receives the meat and the blessing. Neither side can claim the gift without acknowledging the claim of the other."
> — Evans-Pritchard 1956: 278–279 (Author Year:Page fallback)

His application of Maussian total prestation to African political systems provided a rigorous empirical test of the theory's cross-cultural validity, extending its relevance beyond the Melanesian and Northwest Coast cases Mauss analyzed.

## Influence and Legacy

Evans-Pritchard's work bridged British social anthropology and French sociological theory. His influence extended through his Oxford students (Mary Douglas, Victor Turner, Godfrey Lienhardt) into symbolic and interpretive anthropology. His treatment of gift and sacrifice as total social facts shaped subsequent Africanist scholarship on bridewealth, bloodwealth, and ritual exchange. His hermeneutic turn helped shift anthropology from a science of social structures toward an interpretive study of meaning.
