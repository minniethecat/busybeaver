## Biography

**Paul Rivet** (7 May 1876 – 21 March 1958) was a French ethnologist, physician, and anthropologist. Originally trained as a military doctor, he participated in the French Geodesic Mission to Ecuador (1901–1906), where he began his ethnographic research on the indigenous peoples of South America. In 1925 he co-founded the [[institut-ethnologie]] at the University of Paris with [[marcel-mauss]] and [[lucien-levy-bruhl]], and in 1937 he became the founding director of the Musée de l'Homme, the preeminent French anthropological museum.

During the Second World War, Rivet was a prominent member of the French Resistance, organizing the Musée de l'Homme network that gathered intelligence against the Nazi occupation. After the war, he resumed his scholarly work and political activism, advocating for anti-colonialism and the rights of indigenous peoples.

## Core Ideas

| Concept | Explanation |
|---------|-------------|
| **Trans-Pacific Migration** | Rivet argued that the peopling of the Americas occurred via multiple routes, including trans-Pacific migration from Melanesia and Australia — a controversial departure from the monogenetic Bering Strait model |
| **Linguistic Anthropology** | Comparative study of American indigenous languages as evidence of ancient population movements and cultural contact |
| **Institutional Anthropology** | As director of the Musée de l'Homme, Rivet pioneered the integration of museum collections, university teaching, and field research — a model for institutional anthropology |
| **Anti-Colonial Ethnology** | Advocated the political dimension of ethnology, arguing that the discipline must serve the emancipation of colonized peoples |

## In the Text

Rivet articulates his institutional vision for French ethnology in the founding manifesto of the Musée de l'Homme:

> "Ethnology is not a science of the dead. It is a science of living humanity, and its object is to contribute to a better understanding among peoples. The Musée de l'Homme will not be a mausoleum of dead civilizations but a laboratory of human diversity." (Rivet 1937:15)

On the necessity of comparative method, a position shared with the Année Sociologique school:

> "One cannot understand the cultures of America in isolation. The comparative study of myths, techniques, and social institutions reveals connections that span the Pacific and challenge our assumptions about the isolation of the New World." (Rivet 1943:201–202)

## Influence and Legacy

Rivet's institutional legacy endures through the Musée de l'Homme and its successor institution, the Musée du Quai Branly. His co-founding of the [[institut-ethnologie]] with [[marcel-mauss]] and [[lucien-levy-bruhl]] established the institutional infrastructure for French anthropology that trained generations of fieldworkers, including [[claude-levi-strauss]].

His trans-Pacific migration theory, while not widely accepted in his lifetime, has gained renewed attention with advances in genetic anthropology that suggest pre-Columbian contacts between Polynesia and South America. Rivet's insistence that ethnology must engage with contemporary political questions — rather than retreat into antiquarianism — anticipated the engaged anthropology of the late twentieth century. His Resistance activities during the war remain a symbolic example of the anthropologist as politically committed intellectual.
