## Biography

Karl Paul Polanyi (1886–1964) was a Hungarian-born economic historian, anthropologist, and social philosopher. Born in Vienna and raised in Budapest, he was active in left-wing student politics before fleeing first to Vienna after the 1919 Hungarian Revolution and then to England in 1933. He taught at workers' educational associations in England, at Bennington College in Vermont (1940–1943), and at Columbia University (1947–1953), where he directed the interdisciplinary research project that produced *Trade and Market in the Early Empires* (1957). His daughter, Kari Polanyi Levitt, is also a noted economist.

## Core Ideas

| 概念 | 说明 |
|------|------|
| **Embeddedness** | The economy is not autonomous but "embedded" in social relations — disembedding is a modern aberration |
| **Three Modes of Integration** | Reciprocity, redistribution, and market exchange are distinct ways of organizing economic life |
| **Fictitious Commodities** | Land, labour, and money are not produced for sale; treating them as commodities is a "satanic mill" |
| **Double Movement** | Market expansion provokes social counter-movements to protect society from the market |
| **Substantivism** | The economy should be studied as an instituted process of provisioning, not as a formal calculus of choice |

## In the Text

Polanyi's central thesis in *The Great Transformation*:

> "The outstanding discovery of recent historical and anthropological research is that man's economy, as a rule, is submerged in his social relationships. He does not act so as to safeguard his individual interest in the possession of material goods; he acts so as to safeguard his social standing, his social claims, his social assets. He values material goods only in so far as they serve this end." (Polanyi 1944:46)

On the concept of embeddedness that directly echoes Mauss:

> "The economic system is, in effect, a mere function of social organization. ... It is the absence of the threat of individual starvation which makes primitive society, in a sense, more humane than nineteenth-century market society, and at the same time less economic." (Polanyi 1944:49, 163–164)

## Influence and Legacy

Polanyi's substantivist position shaped the formalist-substantivist debate in economic anthropology through the 1960s and 1970s. His concept of embeddedness was revived in the 1980s by Mark Granovetter's "Economic Action and Social Structure" (1985), which launched the "new economic sociology." Polanyi's critique of market fundamentalism has gained renewed relevance since the 2008 financial crisis.

His work is directly complementary to Mauss: where Mauss demonstrated that gift exchange is governed by social obligations, Polanyi showed that even modern market economies cannot fully escape social embeddedness.
