## Biography

Alain Testart (1945–2013) was a French anthropologist and director of research at the CNRS (Centre National de la Recherche Scientifique). Trained initially as an engineer, Testart turned to anthropology and conducted fieldwork among Australian Aboriginal societies before developing a wide-ranging comparative project spanning gift exchange, slavery, burial practices, and the anthropology of law. He is best known in gift-theory circles for his sustained critical engagement with [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s *Essai sur le don*, particularly his argument that Mauss's famous "three obligations" — to give, to receive, and to reciprocate — misrepresent the logic of gift exchange by conflating analytically distinct phenomena. Testart's work exemplifies the French tradition of rigorous comparative sociology descending from [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] through [[claude-levi-strauss|Lévi-Strauss]], which he both inherited and critiqued.

## Core Ideas

### The Logical Independence of Reciprocity

Testart's most pointed critique of [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] is that the third obligation — to reciprocate — does not share the same logical status as the obligations to give and to receive. Giving and receiving are two sides of the same act; you cannot give without someone receiving, and vice versa. Reciprocation, by contrast, is a separate, subsequent act. The structural symmetry Mauss posited (give ↔ receive ↔ reciprocate) collapses when examined closely: the obligation to reciprocate is not internal to the gift but imposed by external social sanctions. This has major implications for how we understand the [[hau|Māori hau]] — Testart argues Mauss mistook a specific Melanesian/Polynesian belief for a universal mechanism (Testart 1993:15–72).

### Gift, Debt, and Slavery

Extending his critique of Mauss into the domain of servitude, Testart argued that the line between gift and debt is more ambiguous than [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] acknowledged. A gift that cannot be reciprocated becomes a debt; a debt that cannot be repaid becomes servitude. The gift, in Testart's analysis, contains a latent potential for domination — an insight that parallels [[pierre-bourdieu|Bourdieu]]'s analysis of the gift as "symbolic violence" but is grounded in Testart's own comparative framework of legal forms across societies. This makes Testart one of the few anthropologists to systematically link the anthropology of the gift with the anthropology of slavery (Testart 2004 I:95–140).

### Typology of Non-Market Transfers

Rather than treating "the gift" as a unitary category, Testart developed a typology distinguishing: (a) gifts proper (transfers without expectation of return), (b) exchanges (transfers with expectation of equivalent return), and (c) prestations (transfers that create or maintain status asymmetries). The [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]ian gift, in Testart's view, conflates all three. The kula, potlatch, and Māori *hau* each involve different combinations of these types, and no single mechanism — whether *hau*, reciprocity, or agonistic competition — can explain them all. The theory of the gift, Testart insists, must be disaggregated before it can be rebuilt (Testart 2007:33–68).

## In the Text

On Mauss's conflation of give/receive/reciprocate:

> "Donner et recevoir sont une seule et même chose vue sous deux angles différents. Mais rendre est autre chose. L'obligation de rendre n'est pas une obligation contractée en recevant; c'est une obligation imposée de l'extérieur, socialement, politiquement, juridiquement. Mauss a eu le tort de mettre sur le même plan trois 'obligations' dont deux seulement sont des faces d'un même acte, et dont la troisième relève d'un tout autre ordre de faits sociaux."
> (Testart 1993:35)
>
> "To give and to receive are one and the same thing seen from two different angles. But to reciprocate is something else. The obligation to reciprocate is not an obligation contracted in receiving; it is an obligation imposed from outside — socially, politically, juridically. Mauss erred in placing on the same plane three 'obligations' of which only two are faces of a single act, and the third belongs to an entirely different order of social facts."

On the gift as potential domination:

> "Tout don non rendu crée une dette. Toute dette non acquittée crée une dépendance. La dépendance non résolue devient servitude. Le don n'est donc pas l'opposé de la servitude; il en est parfois le commencement."
> (Testart 2004 I:112)
>
> "Every unreciprocated gift creates a debt. Every unpaid debt creates dependence. Unresolved dependence becomes servitude. The gift is therefore not the opposite of servitude; it is sometimes its beginning."

## Influence and Legacy

Within French anthropology, Testart stands as the most rigorous internal critic of the Maussian gift-theory tradition. His logical dissection of the three obligations has forced gift theorists to be more precise about what "reciprocity" means and whether it can bear the theoretical weight [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] placed on it. His typology of non-market transfers provides a more fine-grained vocabulary for describing exchange phenomena than the simple gift/commodity distinction. Beyond gift theory, Testart's comparative work on slavery and social evolution has been influential in French archaeology and legal anthropology. Despite the importance of his contributions, his work remains less known in the Anglophone world than that of other Mauss critics like [[annette-weiner]] or [[maurice-godelier]], partly because much of it has not been translated into English.
