## Biography

Elsdon Best (1856–1931) was a New Zealand ethnographer and surveyor who devoted his life to recording Māori traditional knowledge, language, and customs. Born in Tawa Flat, near Wellington, he spent his early adulthood in the New Zealand Armed Constabulary and later as a surveyor in the Urewera region, where he developed close relationships with Tūhoe Māori elders. In 1895, he began working as a quartermaster and storeman at the Urewera road works, which gave him further access to Tūhoe communities. He was appointed ethnologist at the Dominion Museum in Wellington in 1910, a position he held until his death.

## Core Ideas

| 概念 | 说明 |
|------|------|
| **Hau** | Best recorded Ranapiri's explanation that *taonga* possess a *hau* — a spiritual power that compels a return gift |
| **Salvage Ethnography** | Best saw himself as recording a vanishing culture, though Māori culture has proved resilient |
| **Māori Religion** | Documented the Io cult, *tapu*, *mana*, and other key concepts of Māori spiritual life |
| **Oral Tradition** | Best's work preserved genealogies, migration narratives, and ritual formulae that might otherwise have been lost |

## In the Text

The passage from Best's *Forest Lore of the Māori* that Mauss made the centrepiece of his theory:

> "I will now speak of the *hau*, that is to say, of the *hau* of the forest. ... The *taonga* and all strictly personal property possess a *hau* — a spiritual power. You give me one of them and I give it to a third person; he then gives me another *taonga*, because he is forced to do so by the *hau* of my gift. And I, for my part, am obliged to give this thing to you, since I must return to you what is in reality the product of the *hau* of your *taonga*." (Best 1909:439, quoted in Mauss 1925:12)

Best's own framing of the concept:

> "The *hau* of a *taonga* is its vital essence, its life principle. ... Should the gift not be returned, the *hau* will cause the death of the person who retains it — not directly, but through the medium of the *mauri*, or life force, of the gift itself." (Best 1909:440)

## Influence and Legacy

Best's ethnographic record is the indispensable bridge between Māori intellectual tradition and European social theory. Without Best's patient and respectful recording of Tamati Ranapiri's explanation, Mauss would have lacked the key evidence for his argument that gifts contain something of the giver. Best's work thus stands at the origin of one of the most important concepts in twentieth-century social science: the idea that exchange is never purely economic but always involves persons and their inalienable qualities.

Mauss himself acknowledged this debt explicitly: "The whole of this theory of the *hau* of the *taonga* ... was communicated to Mr. Elsdon Best in a remarkable document" (Mauss 1925:12).
