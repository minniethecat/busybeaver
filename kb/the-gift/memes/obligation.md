## Definition

**Obligation** in [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]ian gift theory refers to the triple imperative that governs [[gift-economy|gift exchange]] in "archaic" societies: the obligation to *give* (donner), the obligation to *receive* (recevoir), and the obligation to *reciprocate* (rendre). [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] argued that these three obligations, taken together, constitute the fundamental mechanism of social integration in societies without states or markets — "the way in which everything intermingles in the life of these societies ... is precisely how the clan, the family, and the tribe regulate their relationships" ([[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] 1925:39).

## Origin and Development

[[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] derived the triple obligation from comparative analysis of Polynesian, [[melanesia|Melanesia]]n, and [[pacific-northwest|Northwest Coast]] ethnography. The obligation to give is the essence of potlatch: to refuse to give is to lose face, to refuse to receive is to declare war, and to refuse to reciprocate is to lose status permanently ([[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] 1925:39–43).

Each obligation has distinct social consequences. The obligation to give establishes the giver's prestige; the obligation to receive acknowledges the giver's status while placing the recipient in debt; the obligation to reciprocate restores balance while perpetuating the cycle. Together, they create a system in which "things are never completely separated from the men who exchange them" ([[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] 1925:31).

[[claude-levi-strauss|Claude Lévi-Strauss]] later formalized the triple obligation as a structural principle in *The Elementary Structures of Kinship* (1949), treating reciprocity as the fundamental logic of all social exchange.

## Key Elements

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Obligation to Give** | The chief, clan, or family must give feasts, gifts, and hospitality to maintain status |
| **Obligation to Receive** | A gift cannot be refused — refusal is a declaration of hostility |
| **Obligation to Reciprocate** | A gift must be returned, with interest — failure to reciprocate means loss of face |
| **Cyclical Nature** | The triple obligation creates a perpetual cycle of exchange, not a one-time transaction |
| **Social Integration** | The cycle of obligation binds individuals, families, and clans into ongoing relationships |

## Theoretical Context

The triple obligation is [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s answer to the Hobbesian problem of social order: what holds societies together in the absence of a state? For [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]], it is not a social contract but the perpetual circulation of gifts, driven by the triple obligation, that creates and maintains social bonds. This is a fundamentally different answer from both Hobbes (coercion) and Smith (self-interest).

Later theorists developed different aspects of the obligation concept: Sahlins (1972) distinguished generalized, balanced, and negative reciprocity as points on a spectrum of social distance; Pierre Bourdieu (1972) emphasized the temporal dimension — the gap between gift and [[counter-gift]] is what makes the system work, creating ambiguity about whether a return is really a gift or a payment.

## In the Text

[[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] articulates the triple obligation in a programmatic passage:

> "In the economic and legal systems that have preceded our own, one hardly ever finds a simple exchange of goods, wealth, and products in the course of a transaction concluded between individuals. ... It is groups, not individuals, that carry on exchange, make contracts, and are bound by obligations. ... Furthermore, what they exchange is not exclusively goods and wealth, real and personal property ... but also courtesies, entertainments, ritual, military assistance, women, children, dances, and feasts." (Mauss 1925:5)

On the obligation to receive specifically:

> "To refuse to give, to fail to invite, just as to refuse to receive, is equivalent to declaring war; it is to reject the bond of alliance and commonality." (Mauss 1925:13)
