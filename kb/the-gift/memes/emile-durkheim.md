## Biography

**Émile Durkheim** (15 April 1858 – 15 November 1917) was a French sociologist, widely regarded as one of the principal founders of modern sociology alongside Max Weber and Karl Marx. Born in Épinal, Lorraine, to a Jewish family with a long line of rabbis, Durkheim broke with family tradition to pursue secular academic studies at the École Normale Supérieure in Paris. He later became the first professor of sociology in France, holding chairs at the University of Bordeaux and the Sorbonne.

Durkheim was the uncle and intellectual mentor of [[marcel-mauss]], who collaborated closely with him from the 1890s onward and eventually succeeded him as the leader of the [[annee-sociologique|Année Sociologique]] school. Durkheim's career was marked by the founding of the academic journal *[[annee-sociologique|L'Année Sociologique]]* in 1898 — a vehicle for his broader school of sociological thought. He died of a stroke in 1917, devastated by the death of his son André in World War I.

## Core Ideas

| Concept | Work | Summary |
|---------|------|---------|
| Social facts | *The Rules of Sociological Method* (1895) | Social phenomena must be studied as *things* (*choses*) — external to and coercive upon the individual |
| Mechanical and organic solidarity | *The Division of Labor in Society* (1893) | Pre-modern societies cohere through similarity (mechanical); modern societies through interdependence (organic) |
| Collective consciousness | *The Division of Labor* (1893) | The shared beliefs and moral attitudes that operate as a unifying force within society |
| Anomie | *Suicide* (1897) | A condition of normlessness that arises when social regulation breaks down |
| The sacred and the profane | *The Elementary Forms of Religious Life* (1912) | Religion is society worshipping itself; the sacred/profane distinction is the most elementary social [[classification]] |

## Intellectual Influence on Mauss

Durkheim's sociological method directly shaped [[marcel-mauss]]'s approach to the study of [[gift-economy|gift exchange]]. Durkheim insisted that social phenomena be treated as *totalities* — an idea [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] radicalized into the concept of the "[[total-prestation|total social fact]]" (*[[total-prestation|fait social total]]*) in *The Gift* (1925).

> The gift is not a free and gratuitous act but an obligatory one — it is a total social phenomenon, at once legal, economic, religious, aesthetic, and morphological.
>
> （Mauss 1966, building on Durkheim's sociological holism）

Durkheim also collaborated with [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] on *[[classification|Primitive Classification]]* (1903), a study demonstrating that systems of [[classification]] — including the very categories of human thought — derive from social structures. This work anticipated [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s later interest in how exchange systems embody and reproduce social relations.

## The Année Sociologique School

The [[annee-sociologique|Année Sociologique]] school that Durkheim founded in 1898 was more than a journal: it was a research collective that sought to apply Durkheim's sociological method across diverse domains — law, religion, economics, linguistics, and ethnology. After Durkheim's death in 1917, [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] assumed leadership of the group and continued its interdisciplinary program through the 1920s and 1930s.

> To understand the way in which a society represents itself and the world that surrounds it, we must consider the nature of the society itself and not the nature of the individual.
>
> （Durkheim 1912, *The Elementary Forms of Religious Life*）

## In the Text

Durkheim appears in *The Gift* not by name but through the methodology [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] deploys. The opening argument — that gifts appear voluntary but are in fact obligatory — is a direct application of Durkheim's concept of social facts: phenomena that appear individual and free but are socially determined.

[[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s use of comparative ethnography — juxtaposing the [[potlatch]] of the [[pacific-northwest]], the [[kula-exchange]] of the [[trobriand-islands]], and ancient Indo-European legal systems — follows Durkheim's principle that a sociological fact can only be explained by comparing its variations across different social structures.
