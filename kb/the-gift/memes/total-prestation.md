## Definition

**Total prestation** (*prestation totale*) is a concept developed by [[marcel-mauss]] to describe forms of exchange in which the objects transferred are not merely economic goods but are bound up with legal, moral, religious, and political [[obligation]]s. These exchanges involve entire social groups — clans, tribes, families — rather than individuals acting in isolation.

[[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] called this the **total social fact** (*fait social total*), meaning an event that simultaneously expresses every kind of social institution: religious, legal, moral, and economic.

## Origin and Development

[[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] introduced this concept in *The Gift* (1925) as a way to transcend the modern Western distinction between "economic" and "non-economic" spheres of life. In archaic societies, he argued, exchange is never purely material — the objects given carry the identity and honor of the giver.

The idea was influenced by [[emile-durkheim]]'s sociological holism and was further developed through analysis of the [[potlatch]] and [[kula-exchange]].

## Key Elements

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Collective participation | Exchange involves clans, tribes, and families as corporate entities |
| Multi-dimensionality | The same act is simultaneously legal, economic, religious, and aesthetic |
| Obligatory reciprocity | The three [[obligation]]s — give, receive, reciprocate — bind all participants |
| Agonistic character | In systems like [[potlatch]], total prestation becomes competitive |
| Temporal dimension | The cycle of giving-receiving-returning unfolds over time, creating enduring bonds |

## In the Text

[[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] describes the total character of exchange in the opening pages:

> What rule of legality and self-interest, in societies of a backward or archaic type, compels the gift that has been received to be obligatorily reciprocated? What power resides in the object given that causes its recipient to pay it back?
>
> （Mauss 1966:1–2）

## Theoretical Context

The concept of total prestation challenges the assumption, common in classical economics, that pre-modern exchange was simply "[[barter]]." Instead, [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] shows that exchange was always embedded in social relations — what [[karl-polanyi]] would later call the "embeddedness" of the economy.
