## Overview

**Mwali** and **Soulava** are the two categories of ceremonial valuables that circulate within the [[kula-exchange]] system of the Massim region in [[melanesia]]. Mwali are armbands fashioned from white cone shells (*Conus millepunctatus*), which travel counter-clockwise around the [[kula-exchange|Kula ring]]. Soulava are necklaces made from red *Spondylus* shell discs (known as *spondylus* or *Chama pacifica*), which travel clockwise.

These objects are not utilitarian commodities — they are *vaygu'a* (ceremonial valuables), sacred objects whose value derives from their history of previous owners, the renown of the men who possessed them, and the partnerships they encode. They are held only temporarily; the [[obligation]] to pass them on is absolute.

## The Dual Circuit

| Valuable | Material | Direction | Symbolism |
|----------|---------|-----------|-----------|
| Mwali | White cone shell armbands | Counter-clockwise | Masculine, associated with the arm |
| Soulava | Red spondylus shell necklaces | Clockwise | Feminine, associated with the neck |

[[bronislaw-malinowski]] described the circuit:

> The Kula is a form of exchange of extensive, inter-tribal character; it is carried on by communities inhabiting a wide ring of islands, which form a closed circuit. Along this route, articles of two kinds, and these two kinds only, are constantly traveling in opposite directions.
>
> （Malinowski 1922, *Argonauts of the Western Pacific*）

## Social Function

Mwali and Soulava serve functions that go far beyond their material form:

| Function | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| Partnership marker | Possession of a named valuable signals an active Kula partnership between men of rank |
| Status currency | The temporary custody of a famous valuable — even for a few hours — confers prestige |
| Historical record | Each valuable carries an oral history of its previous owners and journeys |
| Peace-making | Exchange of valuables establishes friendly relations between potentially hostile communities |
| Mortuary role | Valuables circulate intensively during funerary distributions |

## In the Text

[[marcel-mauss]] used the mwali-soulava dual circuit as the paradigmatic example of balanced reciprocity — a system in which exchange is neither competitive (unlike the [[potlatch]]) nor commercial. He emphasized that the valuables are not "money" in any economic sense; they are *total prestations* — objects that carry the [[personne|person]]hood of their givers.

> The valuables of the Kula, the *vaygu'a*, are not indifferent things. They possess individuality and personality. Each has its name, its history, and even its character.
>
> （Mauss 1966, on the personality of Kula valuables）

The counter-circulation of mwali and soulava embodies the principle of [[reciprocity]] at the structural level: the system itself is a mechanism for the perpetual creation and maintenance of social relationships, mediated by objects imbued with the identities of those who have held them.

## Relationship to the Kula

The circulation of mwali and soulava is not separate from the Kula — it *is* the Kula. The system is constituted by the movement of these two valuables through a network of named, lifelong partnerships. Secondary trade in utilitarian goods (*gimwali*) piggybacks on Kula voyages, but the [[kula-exchange|ceremonial exchange]] of mwali and soulava provides the social framework that makes all other exchange possible.
