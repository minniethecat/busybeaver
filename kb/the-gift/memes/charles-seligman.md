## Biography

Charles Gabriel Seligman (1873–1940) was a British physician and anthropologist who helped establish social anthropology as a professional discipline in Britain. After training in medicine at St Thomas' Hospital, he joined the 1898 Cambridge Anthropological Expedition to the Torres Strait, where he first engaged in sustained ethnographic fieldwork. He was appointed to the first chair in ethnology at the London School of Economics in 1913, a position from which he taught and mentored a generation of anthropologists, including [[bronislaw-malinowski]] and [[ee-evans-pritchard]]. Seligman conducted extensive fieldwork in Sudan and Sri Lanka (then Ceylon), and his work on the Vedda people of Sri Lanka and the Nilotic peoples of Sudan became benchmarks of early ethnographic method. His wealthy family background and independent means allowed him to fund expeditions and support students long before anthropology attracted institutional funding.

## Core Ideas

**Fieldwork as the Foundation of Anthropological Authority.** Seligman was among the first British anthropologists to insist that ethnographic knowledge must be acquired through sustained, first-hand fieldwork rather than armchair compilation of travelers' reports. His Torres Strait and Sudan expeditions demonstrated the value of direct observation, setting a precedent that his students Malinowski and Evans-Pritchard would radicalize into the participant-observation method.

**Comparative Regional Ethnology.** Unlike later functionalists who focused on single societies, Seligman practiced a comparative approach across broad regions. His surveys of Sudan and Sri Lanka sought to map cultural and racial distributions, linking ethnographic detail to historical diffusion patterns. This regional-comparative framework influenced Evans-Pritchard's early orientation, even as his students ultimately moved toward intensive single-society studies.

**Institutional Patronage and the Professionalization of Anthropology.** Through his chair at LSE, Seligman built the institutional infrastructure that made British social anthropology possible. He recruited Malinowski from his Polish exile, secured funding for his students' fieldwork, and cultivated anthropology's standing within the British academic establishment. Without Seligman's institutional entrepreneurship, the school of thought that produced [[kula-exchange]] theory and functionalist gift analysis might never have coalesced.

**Melanesian Ethnography and Exchange Theory.** Seligman's _Melanesians of British New Guinea_ (1910) provided the ethnographic baseline on which later exchange theories were built. His detailed descriptions of ceremonial trade networks in the Massim region — the very area where Malinowski would later document the [[kula-exchange|Kula ring]] — supplied Marcel Mauss with comparative material for his _Essai sur le don_. The lineage from Seligman's Melanesian fieldwork through Malinowski's Kula ethnography to Mauss's gift theory represents one of the central intellectual genealogies of early twentieth-century anthropology.

## In the Text

In [[ee-evans-pritchard]], the influence of Seligman on the development of British social anthropology is noted through his role as a teacher:

> "he trained in anthropology at the London School of Economics under Malinowski and C.G. Seligman" (Evans-Pritchard 1940:n.p.)

Seligman's institutional position at LSE placed him at the nexus of the emerging functionalist school. While his own theoretical commitments were eclectic — blending evolutionism, diffusionism, and emergent functionalist sensibilities — his students transformed the discipline. Malinowski's fieldwork in the Trobriands, which produced the canonical account of [[kula-exchange]], was conducted under Seligman's mentorship and with his logistical support. Evans-Pritchard's studies of the Nuer and Azande, likewise, grew from the LSE milieu Seligman had cultivated.

## Influence and Legacy

Seligman's legacy operates primarily through his students rather than his own theoretical writings. Malinowski acknowledged Seligman as the figure who made his Trobriand fieldwork possible, calling him "the patron saint of anthropological field-work." Evans-Pritchard credited Seligman's comparative ethnological framework as formative, even as he moved toward the intensive structural-functionalism of [[ar-radcliffe-brown]]. The Seligman Library at LSE, enriched by his extensive collection of ethnographic materials, served as a scholarly resource for decades.

In the history of gift theory, Seligman occupies an enabling role. Without his sponsorship of Malinowski, the ethnographic discovery of the Kula might have been delayed or conducted differently; without his Melanesian ethnography, Mauss would have lacked crucial comparative data. Seligman thus stands as a bridging figure — between nineteenth-century armchair anthropology and twentieth-century fieldwork, between ethnological survey and intensive functionalist analysis, and between the empirical documentation of exchange systems and their theoretical elaboration by his students and their interlocutors.
