## Biography

George Dalton (1926–1991) was an American economic anthropologist, the most prominent student of Karl Polanyi. He earned his Ph.D. at Columbia University and taught at Northwestern University, where he was part of the "Northwestern school" of economic anthropology alongside Paul Bohannan. Dalton edited Polanyi's posthumous works and was the primary systematizer of substantivist economic anthropology in the 1960s and 1970s. His debate with formalist economist Scott Cook in *American Anthropologist* defined the terms of the substantivist-formalist controversy.

## Core Ideas

- **Gift/Commodity Distinction** — Dalton systematized the Maussian distinction between gifts and commodities, arguing that in non-market economies, objects circulate through socially obligatory transfers (gifts) rather than price-mediated exchange (commodities). This distinction became foundational to economic anthropology.

- **Substantivist Program** — Following Polanyi, Dalton argued that formal economic theory (maximizing behavior, supply/demand) cannot explain non-market economies. Only a substantive approach — analyzing how societies provision themselves through their own institutions — is adequate.

- **Primitive Money** — Dalton showed that so-called "primitive money" (shell money, cattle, etc.) often functioned not as general-purpose currency but as special-purpose money restricted to specific social transactions — a finding consistent with Bohannan's spheres of exchange.

## In the Text

Dalton's systematization of the gift/commodity distinction explicitly traces its lineage to Mauss's *The Gift*. He argued that Mauss's key insight was that gifts are *inalienable* from the giver in a way commodities are not:

> "Mauss showed us that in primitive economies, the thing given is not alienated from the giver. It carries something of the giver's person — what the Maori call *hau*. This inalienability is what distinguishes gift exchange from market exchange."

Dalton used this Maussian framework to critique development economics, arguing that imposing market models on gift-based economies was both theoretically flawed and practically destructive.

## Influence and Legacy

Dalton's edited volume of Polanyi's essays remains the standard introduction to substantivist economic anthropology. His systematization of the gift/commodity distinction influenced later anthropologists (Gregory, Sahlins, Appadurai) who refined and critiqued the binary. The substantivist-formalist debate he shaped, though no longer central, established economic anthropology as a distinct subfield with its own theoretical vocabulary. His critique of development economics anticipated later post-development theory (Escobar, Ferguson).
