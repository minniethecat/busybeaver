## Definition

The **Kula** (sometimes called the **Kula ring**) is a system of ceremonial exchange practiced among the inhabitants of the Massim archipelago in [[papua-new-guinea|Papua New Guinea]], particularly the [[trobriand-islands]]. It was first documented in detail by [[bronislaw-malinowski]] in *Argonauts of the Western Pacific* (1922).

In the Kula, two types of shell valuables circulate in opposite directions around a ring of islands: **[[mwali-and-soulava|mwali]]** (arm-shells) travel counterclockwise, and **[[mwali-and-soulava|soulava]]** (necklaces) travel clockwise. These objects have no use value; their significance lies entirely in their history of ownership and the social relationships they embody.

## Key Features

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Directional circulation | *Mwali* moves counterclockwise, *[[mwali-and-soulava|soulava]]* moves clockwise around the ring |
| Permanent ownership | Objects are never permanently owned — each holder passes them on after a period |
| Delayed reciprocity | A gift of *[[mwali-and-soulava|mwali]]* obligates a future return gift of *[[mwali-and-soulava|soulava]]* (and vice versa), typically after months or years |
| Ceremonial exchange | Transactions are accompanied by ritual, [[magic]], and strict protocol |
| Secondary trade | Alongside the ceremonial Kula, utilitarian goods are traded (*gi[[mwali-and-soulava|mwali]]*) |

## In the Text

[[bronislaw-malinowski|Malinowski]] describes the nature of [[mwali-and-soulava|Kula valuables]]:

> The Kula articles are never used, but they are possessed for the sake of possession itself, and the ownership of them with the ensuing renown is the main source of their value.
>
> （Malinowski 1922:89）

## Kula and Gift Theory

The Kula ring provided [[marcel-mauss]] with one of his central case studies in *The Gift*. [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] used the Kula to illustrate his theory of [[total-prestation]]: the Kula is simultaneously an economic exchange, a political alliance system, a religious ritual, and a mechanism for maintaining social order across vast distances.

> The Kula is a grand system of total services, which is carried out in a noble way, apparently in a pure spirit of generosity and disinterestedness. But it is based on the strictest rules of equivalence and obligatory reciprocity.
>
> （Mauss 1966:22）
