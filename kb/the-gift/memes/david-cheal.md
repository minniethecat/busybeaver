## Biography

David Cheal is a contemporary Canadian sociologist whose work focuses on family, household economy, and gift exchange in modern societies. He taught at the University of Winnipeg, where he conducted research on the intersection of kinship obligation, material transfers, and the moral dimensions of economic life in late-capitalist contexts. His 1988 monograph *The Gift Economy* represents one of the most sustained efforts to apply the [[marcel-mauss|Maussian]] framework to contemporary Western gift practices, bridging classical anthropological theory with empirical sociology.

## Core Ideas

- **Moral Economy of Small Gifts** — Cheal argued that in capitalist societies dominated by commodity exchange, gift-giving persists as a distinct "moral economy" operating alongside the market. Small gifts between kin and friends create and maintain what he called "a universe of moral obligation" that the market cannot generate. Drawing on [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]], Cheal showed that the spirit of the gift survives even in highly commodified contexts.

- **Lifecycle Rituals and Gift Circuits** — Cheal demonstrated that modern gift-giving clusters around lifecycle transitions (birth, marriage, death) and calendrical festivals (Christmas, birthdays), mapping these onto [[arnold-van-gennep|Van Gennep]]'s rites of passage framework. Gifts at weddings and funerals function as material markers of status transition, just as [[potlatch|potlatch]] distributions mark changes in rank.

- **Gender and Gifting** — Cheal's empirical research showed that women perform the majority of gift-selection, wrapping, and ritual labor in Western households, making gift circulation a gendered practice that reinforces kinship ties. This finding connects Maussian theory to feminist sociology, revealing the gendered division of the "work of the gift."

## In the Text

Cheal directly engages Mauss's argument that the gift is a total social phenomenon, testing it against survey data from contemporary Canadian households. He finds that the three obligations (give, receive, reciprocate) remain operative but are modulated by modern norms of voluntarism and intimacy:

> "The modern gift economy is a moral economy in precisely Mauss's sense: it is governed by obligations that are simultaneously personal and social, free and constrained. The gift continues to express and create relationships, even — perhaps especially — in societies where the market appears to have colonized every other form of exchange."

## Influence and Legacy

Cheal's empirical turn in gift studies opened space for sociological investigations of contemporary exchange practices, influencing the "moral economy" tradition in economic sociology. His work is cited in debates on the persistence of non-market exchange in capitalist societies, alongside the work of [[david-graeber|Graeber]] on debt and [[lewis-hyde|Hyde]] on creativity and the gift. His emphasis on the gendered nature of gift labor has been taken up in feminist economic sociology.
