## Biography

Marshall David Sahlins (1930–2021) was an American anthropologist and one of the most influential theorists of economic anthropology in the second half of the twentieth century. He studied under Leslie White and Karl Polanyi at the University of Michigan and earned his PhD at Columbia University (1954), where he was influenced by Julian Steward and Karl Polanyi. He taught at the University of Michigan (1957–1973) and the University of Chicago (1973–2021), where he was the Charles F. Grey Distinguished Service Professor of Anthropology. His fieldwork included Polynesia (Fiji, Hawaii) and his theoretical work spanned kinship, economic anthropology, and historical anthropology.

## Key Works

| 著作 | 出版年 | 说明 |
|------|--------|------|
| *Stone Age Economics* | 1972 | Classic analysis of "primitive" economic life; introduced the spectrum of reciprocity (generalized/balanced/negative) |
| *Culture and Practical Reason* | 1976 | Critique of utilitarian and materialist theories of culture; argued that culture is not determined by practical utility |
| *Islands of History* | 1985 | Historical anthropology of Polynesian encounters with Europeans; the "structure of the conjuncture" |
| *How "Natives" Think* | 1995 | Defence of Hawaiian rationality in the face of Western misinterpretation (the Obeyesekere debate) |

## Core Ideas

| 概念 | 说明 |
|------|------|
| **Reciprocity Spectrum** | Generalized (altruistic), balanced (equal exchange), and negative (theft/cheating) — a continuum of social distance |
| **Original Affluent Society** | Hunter-gatherers were the "original affluent society" because they had limited wants, not limited means |
| **Domestic Mode of Production** | The household as the basic economic unit, with chronic underproduction as the norm |
| **Critique of Mauss on Hau** | The Māori *hau* is not a "spirit" compelling return but a principle of productivity — the return gift represents the increase of the original |
| **Structure of the Conjuncture** | Historical events are structured by cultural categories even as they transform them |

## In the Text

Sahlins's influential critique of Mauss's interpretation of the *hau*:

> "Mauss's interpretation of the *hau* as a spirit that compels a return gift has been enormously influential, but it is probably mistaken. ... What the *hau* secures is not the return of the gift but the return of the *increase* generated by the gift. ... The *hau* is a principle of productivity, not of spirit possession." (Sahlins 1972:157–160)

On the original affluent society:

> "The world's most primitive people have few possessions, but they are not poor. ... Poverty is a social status. As such it is the invention of civilization." (Sahlins 1972:37)

## Influence and Legacy

Sahlins's *Stone Age Economics* is the most important post-Maussian contribution to the anthropology of exchange. His reciprocity spectrum provided a systematic framework for comparing different forms of gift-giving, and his critique of Mauss on the *hau* sparked decades of debate about the foundation of gift theory. His concept of the "original affluent society" was central to the development of hunter-gatherer studies and has influenced ecological economics and degrowth theory.
