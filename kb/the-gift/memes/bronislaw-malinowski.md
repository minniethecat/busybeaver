## Biography

**Bronisław Kasper Malinowski** (7 April 1884 – 16 May 1942) was a Polish-British anthropologist, widely regarded as one of the founders of modern social anthropology. He pioneered the method of **participant observation** during his extended fieldwork in the [[trobriand-islands]] (1915–1918), living among the islanders and learning their language.

His ethnographic account of the [[kula-exchange]] — the [[kula-exchange|ceremonial exchange]] of shell valuables across a ring of islands — provided key empirical material for [[marcel-mauss]]'s theory of the [[gift-economy]].

He held professorships at the London School of Economics and Yale University.

## Core Ideas

- **Participant Observation**: Malinowski argued that the ethnographer must live among the people being studied, learn their language, and participate in daily life — the defining method of modern anthropology.
- **Functionalism**: Social institutions (kinship, [[magic]], exchange) exist because they fulfill basic human needs, both biological and psychological.
- **The Kula Ring**: Malinowski documented the [[kula-exchange]] as a complex system of [[kula-exchange|ceremonial exchange]] linking island communities across hundreds of miles. The *[[mwali-and-soulava|mwali]]* (arm-shells) and *[[mwali-and-soulava|soulava]]* (necklaces) circulate in opposite directions, creating enduring social bonds.
- **Gift and Reciprocity**: His data on Trobriand exchange directly influenced [[marcel-mauss]]'s *The Gift* and the theory of [[total-prestation]].

## In the Text

Malinowski describes the Kula:

> The Kula is not a surreptitious and precarious form of exchange. It is, on the contrary, rooted in myth, backed by traditional law, and surrounded with magical rites. All its main transactions are public and ceremonial, and carried out according to definite rules.
>
> （Malinowski 1922:85）

## Influence and Legacy

Malinowski's fieldwork methodology set the standard for modern ethnography. His functionalist theory, while later criticized for its ahistorical approach, was foundational for British social anthropology. His Kula ethnography remains the classic case study of [[gift-economy|gift exchange]] and ceremonial economics.
