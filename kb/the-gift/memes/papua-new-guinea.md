## Geography

**Papua New Guinea** (PNG) is a country in [[melanesia]], occupying the eastern half of the island of New Guinea — the world's second-largest island — along with numerous offshore islands including the Bismarck Archipelago, Bougainville, and the [[trobriand-islands]]. It is bordered by Indonesia to the west (the provinces of Papua and West Papua), Australia to the south across the Torres Strait, and the Solomon Islands to the east.

The country is renowned for its extraordinary geographic and cultural diversity. The interior highlands, with peaks exceeding 4,500 meters, were among the last densely populated regions on earth to be contacted by outsiders — large-scale contact occurred only in the 1930s, when Australian gold prospectors and missionaries entered the highland valleys.

## Cultural and Linguistic Diversity

Papua New Guinea is the most linguistically diverse country in the world, with over 800 indigenous languages spoken by a population of approximately 10 million. This linguistic diversity is matched by cultural diversity in kinship systems, exchange practices, political organization, and ritual life — making PNG one of the most important regions in the history of anthropology.

## Significance in The Gift

Papua New Guinea — specifically the [[trobriand-islands]] — is central to [[marcel-mauss]]'s argument in *The Gift*. The [[kula-exchange]] documented by [[bronislaw-malinowski]] among the Massim people of PNG provided [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] with his most fully described example of balanced [[kula-exchange|ceremonial exchange]] — the counterpoint to the competitive [[potlatch]] of the [[pacific-northwest]].

The Kula, with its elaborate rules governing the circulation of [[mwali-and-soulava|mwali and soulava]] across hundreds of kilometers of open ocean, demonstrated to [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] that exchange could be simultaneously economic, religious, aesthetic, and political — a [[total-prestation|total social phenomenon]]. Without the PNG ethnography, [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] would have lacked the comparative material to argue for the universality of the principles of [[reciprocity]] and [[obligation]].

> The Kula is a grand system of total services. It involves the whole community, and its rules encompass every aspect of social life — from marriage alliance to political hierarchy to magical ritual.
>
> （Mauss 1966, synthesizing Malinowski's Trobriand ethnography）
