## Biography

Roger Caillois (1913–1978) was a French intellectual whose work spanned sociology, literary criticism, and game theory. A student of Marcel Mauss and Georges Dumézil at the École Pratique des Hautes Études, he co-founded the Collège de Sociologie in 1937 with Georges Bataille and Michel Leiris. The Collège sought to revive Durkheimian sociology's engagement with the sacred in contemporary life. During WWII, Caillois lived in Argentina (1939–1944), where he founded the journal *Lettres Françaises*. He was elected to the Académie Française in 1971.

## Core Ideas

- **Sacred/Profane Dialectic** — Building on Durkheim and Mauss, Caillois theorized the sacred as a social force that alternates between periods of consolidation (order) and transgression (festival). Gift-giving, in this framework, is one modality through which the sacred circulates.

- **Festival and Expenditure** — Caillois analyzed festivals as institutionalized periods of excess that temporarily suspend the profane order. This concept extends Mauss's potlatch analysis: the festival is the temporal dimension of the gift's logic of competitive expenditure.

- **Game Classification** — In *Man, Play and Games*, Caillois proposed a fourfold classification of play: competition (agôn), chance (alea), simulation (mimicry), and vertigo (ilinx). This framework has been influential in game studies, sociology of sport, and digital media theory.

## In the Text

Caillois's *Man and the Sacred* extends Mauss's analysis of the gift into a broader theory of the sacred. The Collège de Sociologie explicitly positioned Maussian gift theory as a foundation for understanding the sacred in modernity:

> "The gift is not merely an economic fact, as Mauss demonstrated; it is the primary form through which the sacred makes itself felt in social life — an obligatory circulation that binds the individual to the collective."

Caillois emphasized that the destructive excess of the potlatch, far from being irrational, was a ritual mechanism for renewing the sacred order.

## Influence and Legacy

Caillois's work on the sacred influenced the post-structuralist turn in French thought (Bataille, Blanchot, Derrida). His game classification remains a standard reference in game studies. *Man and the Sacred* has been rediscovered by scholars of religion and ritual seeking to connect Durkheimian sociology with contemporary cultural analysis. His concept of the festival as sacred transgression has informed anthropological studies of carnival, ritual violence, and modern consumer festivals.
