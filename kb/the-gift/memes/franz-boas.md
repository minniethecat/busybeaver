## Biography

Franz Boas (1858–1942) was a German-born American anthropologist, often called the "Father of American Anthropology." Born in Minden, Westphalia, he earned a doctorate in physics from the University of Kiel (1881) before turning to geography and then anthropology. After fieldwork among the Inuit of Baffin Island (1883–1884) and the Kwakiutl of Vancouver Island (1886 onward), he settled in the United States, teaching at Clark University, the American Museum of Natural History, and Columbia University (1896–1936), where he trained the first generation of professional American anthropologists: Ruth Benedict, Margaret Mead, Edward Sapir, and Zora Neale Hurston.

## Key Works

| 著作 | 出版年 | 说明 |
|------|--------|------|
| *The Mind of Primitive Man* | 1911 | Anti-racist classic; argued that there is no biological basis for racial hierarchies in intelligence or culture |
| *Kwakiutl Ethnography* | 1966 (posth.) | Massive documentary record of Kwakiutl potlatch ceremonies; the primary source for Mauss's potlatch analysis |
| *Race, Language, and Culture* | 1940 | Collected essays spanning his career; established the four-field approach to anthropology |
| *Primitive Art* | 1927 | Analysis of Northwest Coast art, emphasizing the systematic nature of "primitive" aesthetic forms |

## Core Ideas

| 概念 | 说明 |
|------|------|
| **Cultural Relativism** | Each culture must be understood on its own terms, not judged against Western standards |
| **Historical Particularism** | Cultures develop through specific historical trajectories, not through universal evolutionary stages |
| **Four-Field Anthropology** | Integration of cultural anthropology, physical anthropology, archaeology, and linguistics |
| **Anti-Racism** | Demonstrated that race, language, and culture vary independently — no biological determinism |
| **Potlatch Documentation** | His Kwakiutl fieldwork provided the ethnographic evidence Mauss used to theorize competitive gift exchange |

## In the Text

Boas on the potlatch, the institution that became central to Maussian theory:

> "The potlatch is a great ceremonial feast at which the host distributes considerable amounts of property among his guests. ... The more he destroys and gives away, the greater his fame. ... The potlatch is the great stimulus to work and the great means of accumulating wealth among the tribes of the North Pacific coast." (Boas 1897:341–342)

On cultural relativism, summarizing a lifetime's conviction:

> "The activities of the mind exhibit an infinite variety of form among the peoples of the world. ... The behavior of man is determined not by his racial affiliation, but by the cultural tradition in which he lives." (Boas 1911:98, 208)

## Influence and Legacy

Boas's Kwakiutl ethnography was the single most important ethnographic source for Mauss's *The Gift*. Without Boas's meticulous documentation of potlatch ceremonies — the speeches, the distributions, the competitive destruction of coppers — Mauss would have had no empirical basis for his theory of agonistic exchange. Boas also established the institutional framework of American anthropology at Columbia, and his students — particularly Benedict and Mead — made anthropology a public discourse in mid-twentieth-century America.
