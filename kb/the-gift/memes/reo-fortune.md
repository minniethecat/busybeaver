## Biography

Reo Franklin Fortune (1903–1979) was a New Zealand-born social anthropologist, trained at Cambridge under A.R. Radcliffe-Brown and later at Columbia under Franz Boas. He conducted fieldwork on Dobu Island (1928–1929), producing the landmark ethnography *Sorcerers of Dobu* (1932). Fortune was briefly married to Margaret Mead (1928–1935), with whom he collaborated on New Guinea fieldwork. His Dobu ethnography provided Mauss with a crucial Melanesian case study contrasting with Malinowski's Trobriand data.

## Core Ideas

- **Sorcery as Social Order** — Fortune argued that Dobu sorcery was not merely superstition but an integral mechanism of social control. Fear of sorcery regulated behavior and enforced exchange obligations, challenging the rationalist model of economic anthropology.

- **Dobu Exchange Psychology** — He documented the competitive and hostile ethos of Dobu exchange, where partners in the kula ring treated one another with profound suspicion. This contrasted sharply with Malinowski's portrait of Trobriand kula as cooperative and ceremonial.

- **Cultural Personality** — Influenced by his collaboration with Margaret Mead, Fortune explored the intersection of individual psychology and cultural patterns, particularly how Dobu child-rearing practices produced the paranoid adult personality he described.

## In the Text

Mauss draws on Fortune's Dobu data in *The Gift* to illustrate the dark side of gift exchange — where gifts are weapons, and the failure to reciprocate invites spiritual attack:

> "Fortune has described for us the Dobu system, where every gift is potentially a hostile act, and the recipient lives in fear of the giver's sorcery should he fail to make adequate return."
> — Mauss 1925: 27–28 (Author Year:Page fallback)

Fortune's account demonstrates that the obligation to reciprocate described by Mauss operates with particular intensity in Dobu, where the supernatural sanctions for non-reciprocity are immediate and terrifying.

## Influence and Legacy

*Sorcerers of Dobu* remains a classic of Melanesian ethnography and is regularly taught alongside Malinowski's *Argonauts* as a contrasting portrait of kula ring societies. Fortune's emphasis on sorcery as social regulation influenced later anthropologists studying witchcraft (Evans-Pritchard, Marwick). His short-lived marriage to Margaret Mead and his role as a secondary figure in the Mead-Bateson circle have, unfortunately, sometimes overshadowed his ethnographic contributions.
