## Biography

Mark Osteen is an American literary scholar and professor of English at Loyola University Maryland. His work spans modernist literature, jazz studies, film, and the interdisciplinary study of the gift. Osteen's engagement with [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]ian gift theory emerged from his interest in how modernist writers — James Joyce, Virginia Woolf, William Faulkner — explored economies of exchange, obligation, and generosity in their fiction. He founded the Loyola University Maryland Jazz Ensemble and has published extensively on literary modernism and economic criticism.

## Core Ideas

### The Gift as Interdisciplinary Question

Osteen's central editorial contribution — *The Question of the Gift* — was to demonstrate that the gift cannot be adequately theorized within any single discipline. The volume brought together anthropologists (including [[annette-weiner]]), economists, philosophers, sociologists, and literary critics to examine how gift exchange works in different domains. Osteen's introduction argued that the gift is inherently "undisciplined" — it crosses the boundaries between self and other, obligation and freedom, calculation and spontaneity — and therefore requires interdisciplinary study (Osteen 2002:1–24).

### The Gift in Modernist Literature

Osteen argued that modernist fiction is a privileged site for theorizing the gift because modernism's formal experiments — stream of consciousness, fragmentation, multiple perspectives — mirror the gift's resistance to fixed categories. In Joyce's *Ulysses*, for example, the economy of giving, borrowing, and repaying operates as a counter-structure to the monetary economy of colonial Dublin. The novel's famed "style of generosity" — its willingness to give the reader more than can be processed — is itself a kind of literary gift (Osteen 1995:45–80).

### Gift and Disability

In his memoir and subsequent work on autism, Osteen explored how caregiving within families operates as a gift economy that cannot be reduced to either altruism or obligation. The parent who cares for a disabled child is neither a rational actor maximizing utility nor a pure altruist. Rather, the relationship — like [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s gift — creates bonds that precede and exceed any calculation of cost and benefit (Osteen 2010:155–180).

## In the Text

On the interdisciplinary nature of the gift:

> "The gift is undisciplined. It crosses borders — between persons, between cultures, between academic fields — with a promiscuity that makes disciplinarians nervous. To study the gift is to be led, inevitably, from anthropology to economics, from philosophy to literature, from sociology to theology. The gift demands that we become undisciplined too."
> (Osteen 2002:3)

On literature as gift:

> "Literature is itself a kind of gift — a verbal object offered by an author to unknown readers, creating a relationship across time and space that no contract could specify. When [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] described the gift as a 'total social fact,' he might as well have been describing the novel."
> (Osteen 2014:15)

## Influence and Legacy

Osteen's *The Question of the Gift* became a standard reference in gift studies, cited across the humanities and social sciences. His bringing together of anthropologists ([[annette-weiner]]), economists, and literary scholars created a template for interdisciplinary gift research that the MAUSS group had pioneered in France but that lacked a comparable English-language volume. Within literary studies, Osteen's work established the gift as a legitimate theoretical category alongside more established frameworks like Marxism, psychoanalysis, and post-structuralism. His analysis of modernist fiction as gift practice opened a line of inquiry pursued by subsequent scholars in the economic humanities — a field that examines how literary forms represent and enact economic logics.
