## Definition

**Magic** (*magie*), as theorized by [[marcel-mauss|Marcel Mauss]] and Henri Hubert in *A General Theory of Magic* (1902–1903), is a social phenomenon characterized by (1) the social position of the magician as an outsider, (2) the collective belief in *mana* as an im[[personne|person]]al force, and (3) rites that are performed in isolated, often illicit contexts, distinct from public religious ceremonies. "Magic is essentially a social phenomenon" — it exists only because a society believes in it ([[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] and Hubert 1902:120).

## Origin and Development

*A General Theory of Magic* (*Esquisse d'une théorie générale de la magie*) was published in *[[annee-sociologique|L'Année Sociologique]]* in 1902–1903, three years after *[[sacrifice|Sacrifice]]*. It applied the same [[emile-durkheim|Durkheim]]ian method — comparative analysis of ethnographic and historical data — to a phenomenon that had been treated as primitive error by Tylor and Frazer.

[[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] and Hubert argued that magic is not failed science but a coherent social institution built on three elements: the **magician** (a socially defined role, often marginal), the **magical rite** (a technical act believed to produce effects through sympathetic or contagious logic), and **collective representations** (the shared beliefs that make magic efficacious).

The concept of *mana* — borrowed from Codrington's *The [[melanesia|Melanesia]]ns* (1891) — was central to their analysis. *[[mana|Mana]]* is the im[[personne|person]]al force that makes magic work; it is simultaneously a substance, a quality, and a force, and it circulates through [[personne|person]]s, objects, and words. [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] later extended the analysis of *mana* in *The Gift* (1925), where it becomes the conceptual bridge between magic and [[gift-economy|gift exchange]].

## Key Elements

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **[[mana|Mana]]** | The im[[personne|person]]al efficacious force that animates magical acts |
| **The Magician** | A socially defined role, often marginal or liminal in status |
| **Magical Rites** | Technical acts employing laws of sympathy (like produces like) and contagion (part to whole) |
| **Collective Belief** | Magic works because society believes it works — efficacy is socially constituted |
| **Distinction from Religion** | Magic is private, illicit, and instrumental; religion is public, obligatory, and expressive |

## Theoretical Context

[[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] and Hubert's theory of magic is a key text in the [[emile-durkheim|Durkheim]]ian sociology of religion. It extends [[emile-durkheim|Durkheim]]'s sacred/profane distinction from *The Elementary Forms* (1912) — magic occupies an intermediate space, using sacred forces for profane ends — and anticipates [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s later concept of the "[[total-prestation|total social fact]]" by showing that magic, like [[gift-economy|gift exchange]], is simultaneously a religious, economic, legal, and aesthetic phenomenon.

The essay influenced [[claude-levi-strauss|Lévi-Strauss]]'s structuralist theory of magic in "The Sorcerer and His Magic" (1949) and Bourdieu's concept of symbolic power. More recently, it has been revisited in studies of ritual efficacy and performance theory.

## In the Text

[[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] and Hubert open with a definitional challenge:

> "Magic has been defined in a hundred ways. ... We propose to call magical every rite that does not form part of an organized cult — a rite that is private, secret, mysterious, and tending toward the prohibited." (Mauss and Hubert 1902:16, 24)

On *mana* as the foundation of magic:

> "*Mana* is not simply a force, a being; it is also an action, a quality, a state. ... It is the object of a belief that is both unquestioned and diffuse. ... One could say that *mana* is the very idea of magical efficacy." (Mauss and Hubert 1902:108–110)
