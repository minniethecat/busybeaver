## Biography

**Georges Bataille** (10 September 1897 – 8 July 1962) was a French philosopher, novelist, and literary critic. Trained as a paleographer at the École des Chartes, he worked as a librarian at the Bibliothèque Nationale. In the 1930s he became a central figure in French intellectual life, founding the journal *Acéphale* and the [[college-de-france|Collège de Sociologie]] with [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s student Roger Caillois and Michel Leiris, explicitly drawing on Maussian themes of the sacred, sacrifice, and collective effervescence.

After the war, Bataille published his most important theoretical works, including *La Part maudite* (1949), which extended Mauss's analysis of [[potlatch]] and gift exchange into a general economic theory of expenditure.

## Core Ideas

| Concept | Explanation |
|---------|-------------|
| **The Accursed Share** | Any system produces an excess of energy that cannot be productively absorbed; this surplus must be spent — luxuriously, destructively, or sacrificially |
| **General Economy** | As opposed to "restricted economy" (scarcity, utility), general economy studies the circulation of excess energy at the cosmic and social scale |
| **Dépense (Expenditure)** | Unproductive expenditure is not a failure of economy but its ultimate purpose — the potlatch exemplifies this principle |
| **Sovereignty** | That which refuses subordination to utility, production, or the future — a mode of being exemplified in sacrifice, art, and eroticism |
| **The Sacred** | Drawing on Durkheim and Mauss, Bataille theorized the sacred as that which is set apart through violent expenditure and transgression |

## In the Text

Bataille makes explicit his debt to Mauss in the opening of *The Accursed Share*, where he recasts the [[potlatch]] as a universal economic principle:

> "Mauss showed that the potlatch is not a simple exchange of goods but a sumptuary expenditure, a competition in giving and destroying wealth. I propose to extend this insight: the general economy is not governed by scarcity but by excess. The crucial question is not how to produce but how to expend." (Bataille 1949:67–68)

Bataille's essay on expenditure directly reworks Maussian gift theory into a philosophical anthropology of excess:

> "Human activity is not entirely reducible to processes of production and conservation. Consumption must be divided into two distinct parts: the first, reducible part is represented by the use of the minimum necessary for the conservation of life. The second part is represented by so-called unproductive expenditures: luxury, mourning, war, cults, the construction of sumptuary monuments, games, spectacles, arts, perverse sexual activity." (Bataille 1933:5–6)

## Influence and Legacy

Bataille's extension of Maussian gift theory into a general economy of excess influenced a wide range of thinkers, from the post-structuralism of Jacques Derrida and Michel Foucault to the economic anthropology of [[expenditure]] theory. The Collège de Sociologie, though short-lived (1937–1939), created a distinctive fusion of Durkheimian sociology, surrealist aesthetics, and political critique that continues to inspire studies of the sacred in modern societies.

Unlike Mauss, who emphasized the obligatory and integrative dimensions of gift exchange, Bataille foregrounded violence, transgression, and the desire for loss — revealing the dark side of the gift that Mauss had noted but not fully developed. His concept of the accursed share has been applied to phenomena as diverse as environmental economics, art theory, and the anthropology of sacrifice.
