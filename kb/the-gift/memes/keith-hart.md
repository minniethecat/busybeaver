## Biography

Keith Hart (born 1943) is a British anthropologist and emeritus professor at Goldsmiths, University of London. He studied at Cambridge before conducting fieldwork among migrants in Accra, Ghana in the 1960s.

While studying the informal income-generating activities of urban migrants, Hart coined the term "informal economy", which became one of the most influential concepts in development economics — though often in ways that diverged sharply from his original ethnographic meaning.

Hart has spent the subsequent decades developing an alternative vision of economy grounded in [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]ian anthropology, which he calls "human economy": an approach that places people, rather than abstract market forces, at the center of economic analysis.

## Core Ideas

### The Informal Economy

Hart's original concept of the informal economy was ethnographic, not econometric. Observing Accra migrants who sustained themselves through diverse entrepreneurial activities, he argued that this "informal" sector was a manifestation of human economic creativity under conditions of structural exclusion.

The term was later appropriated by development agencies and reduced to a technocratic measure, losing Hart's original emphasis on agency, networks, and the moral economy of urban life (Hart 1973:61–89).

### Money as Memory

In *The Memory Bank*, Hart argues that money is fundamentally a system of social memory — a way for communities to record who owes what to whom across time.

This is what money shares with [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]ian gifts: both are technologies for managing temporal asymmetries in social exchange. The key difference is that modern state money has been captured by an alliance of banks and governments that monopolizes credit creation (Hart 2000:1–40).

### The Human Economy

Hart's "human economy" paradigm synthesizes [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s gift theory, [[karl-polanyi|Karl Polanyi]]'s substantivism, and his own ethnographic work. A human economy, in Hart's vision, subordinates economic institutions to human needs rather than the reverse.

This requires reclaiming money as a public utility, democratizing credit creation, and recognizing the contributions of unpaid labor, household production, and gift exchange (Hart, Laville and Cattani 2010:1–20).

## In the Text

On the true nature of money:

> "Money is a memory bank — a way of keeping track of the exchanges we make with one another. The coin is a token of remembrance; the banknote is a portable record of social debt. When we strip away the mystifications of economics textbooks, money reveals itself as what [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] understood the gift to be: a mechanism for organizing social time, for binding past, present, and future in a web of mutual obligation."
> (Hart 2000:234)

On the informal economy:

> "What I saw in the streets of Nima was not the 'traditional sector' of development economics but the human economy in its most elemental form — people using their wits, their networks, their capacity for trust and reciprocity to make a life where the formal economy had none to offer."
> (Hart 1973:84)

## Influence and Legacy

Hart's coinage of "informal economy" is one of the few anthropological concepts to have entered the global policy lexicon. His later work on the human economy has been more influential within anthropology.

Hart's synthesis of [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] with monetary theory positions him as a bridge figure between the gift-theory tradition and contemporary debates about the future of money.
