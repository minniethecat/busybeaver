## Biography

Louis Dumont (1911–1998) was a French anthropologist and sociologist, a student of [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] at the [[institut-ethnologie|Institut d'Ethnologie]]. After being taken as a prisoner of war during World War II, he conducted his major fieldwork in South India (1949–1950), studying the Pramalai Kallar subcaste. His comparative analysis of hierarchy in India and individualism in the West established him as one of the most original thinkers in the French sociological tradition. He taught at Oxford University and the École des Hautes Études en Sciences Sociales (EHESS) in Paris.

## Key Works

| Work | Year | Significance |
|------|------|-------------|
| *La Tarasque* | 1951 | Study of a Provençal folklore figure; early demonstration of Dumont's structural method |
| *Une sous-caste de l'Inde du Sud* | 1957 | Ethnographic monograph on the Pramalai Kallar; established his reputation as an Indianist |
| *Homo Hierarchicus: Essai sur le système des castes* | 1966 (Eng. 1970) | Major theoretical work arguing that the Indian caste system is structured by the opposition of the pure and the impure, and that hierarchy is a form of encompassment rather than ranking |
| *Homo Aequalis I: Genèse et épanouissement de l'idéologie économique* | 1977 | Comparative study of the emergence of individualism in the West |
| *Essays on Individualism* | 1983 | Collection of essays tracing the Western ideology of the individual from early Christianity to modern economics |

## Core Ideas

**Hierarchy as Encompassment**: Dumont's most influential theoretical move was to redefine hierarchy not as a chain of command but as the "encompassment of the contrary." In the Indian caste system, the Brahmin (priest) ranks above the Kshatriya (king) not because the priest commands the king, but because the principle of purity (the priest's domain) *encompasses* the principle of power (the king's domain). The higher level includes and contextualises the lower. This is fundamentally different from Western notions of egalitarian ranking.

**Homo Hierarchicus vs. Homo Aequalis**: Dumont argued that Western social science projects its own ideology of individualism onto non-Western societies. Where modern Westerners see society as composed of autonomous individuals, traditional India sees individuals as constituted by their place in a hierarchical whole. The task of anthropology, for Dumont, is to make this contrast explicit, treating both hierarchy and individualism as *ideologies* — culturally specific systems of value — rather than as natural or universal.

**Maussian Roots**: Dumont's method of studying "ideology" (the system of ideas and values) drew directly on [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s concept of the "total social fact" and [[emile-durkheim|Durkheim]]'s concept of [[collective-representation|collective representations]]. Like [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]], Dumont insisted that classification systems — how a society divides up the world — are not secondary to social structure but constitutive of it.

## In the Text

On hierarchy:

> "To adopt a value is to introduce hierarchy. … A society is not essentially a system of equal rights but a system of ideas and values. Hierarchy is the social form in which values are arranged."
> (Dumont 1966:19–20)

On the contrast between India and the West:

> "The caste system teaches us that hierarchy is not, as we tend to think, the opposite of equality, but its complement. The modern West is not simply 'egalitarian'; it is a society in which the value of equality encompasses and subordinates the value of hierarchy. Traditional India is the inverse: a society in which the value of hierarchy encompasses and subordinates equality."
> (Dumont 1966:4; paraphrase and synthesis of Dumont's core thesis)

## Influence and Legacy

Dumont's *Homo Hierarchicus* is considered one of the most important anthropological works on India, though it has been sharply criticised for presenting an overly Brahminical, textualist view of caste that understates subaltern agency and historical change. His comparative method — contrasting "holistic" and "individualistic" ideologies — influenced the anthropology of values, the study of nationalism, and critiques of economism in the social sciences. Alongside [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] and [[claude-levi-strauss|Lévi-Strauss]], Dumont is regarded as one of the three major figures of twentieth-century French anthropology.
