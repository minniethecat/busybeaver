## Definition

**Mana** is a concept found across [[melanesia|Melanesia]]n and Polynesian societies denoting an im[[personne|person]]al, supernatural force or efficacy that can reside in [[personne|person]]s, objects, places, and natural phenomena. First systematically documented by the Anglican missionary-ethnographer Robert Henry Codrington in *The [[melanesia|Melanesia]]ns* (1891), mana is not a [[personne|person]]al spirit or god but a diffuse power — something like "what makes a chief a chief, a successful warrior successful, a productive garden productive."

In the context of [[gift-economy|gift exchange]] theory, mana is the broader Oceanic category of which the Māori [[hau]] — the "[[hau|spirit of the gift]]" that [[marcel-mauss]] made central to *The Gift* — is a specific manifestation. While the [[hau]] compels the return of a gift, mana is the general principle of efficacy that makes objects, people, and exchanges socially potent.

## Origin and Development

R.H. Codrington, who spent twenty-four years in [[melanesia|Melanesia]] (1863–1887), provided the first systematic account of mana in *The [[melanesia|Melanesia]]ns: Studies in Their Anthropology and Folk-Lore* (1891). His famous definition remains the starting point for all subsequent theorizing:

> The Melanesian mind is entirely possessed by the belief in a supernatural power or influence, called almost universally mana. This mana is not fixed in anything, and can be conveyed in almost anything.
>
> （Codrington 1891:118–119）

Codrington's account influenced a generation of comparative religion scholars, including [[emile-durkheim]], who cited mana in *The Elementary Forms of Religious Life* (1912) as an example of the im[[personne|person]]al sacred — a force that precedes and exceeds individual representations. [[marcel-mauss]] drew on mana in his *General Theory of [[magic|Magic]]* (1902, with Henri Hubert), arguing that mana is the underlying category of which [[magic]], religion, and [[gift-economy|gift exchange]] are expressions.

## Key Elements

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Im[[personne|person]]ality | Mana is not a [[personne|person]]al deity but a diffuse power — transferable, contagious, accumulable |
| Efficacy | Mana manifests in effectiveness: the chief rules, the warrior wins, the crop grows |
| Transmissibility | Mana can pass between [[personne|person]]s and objects through contact, inheritance, or exchange |
| Ambivalence | Mana can be beneficial or dangerous — sacred and polluting, depending on context |
| Social index | The distribution of mana maps the social hierarchy — those with authority have mana |

## Relationship to the Hau

The Māori concept of [[hau]] — which [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] deployed as the explanatory mechanism for gift reciprocity — is a specific, local form of the broader mana principle. Where mana is general efficacy, the [[hau]] is specifically the power that inheres in a gift-object and compels its return to the original giver. [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s use of the [[hau]] has been widely debated; [[claude-levi-strauss]] criticized [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] for taking a Māori "indigenous theory" at face value rather than recognizing the deeper structural principle of reciprocity behind it. The mana concept, as the more general category, provides the cultural logic within which the more specific [[hau]] mechanism operates.

## In the Text

[[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] does not discuss mana extensively in *The Gift*, but the concept is implicit throughout. The force that compels the return of gifts — whether called [[hau]], mana, or something else — is [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s answer to the central question of the book:

> What power resides in the object given that causes its recipient to pay it back?
>
> （Mauss 1966:2）

[[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s earlier work on mana in *A General Theory of [[magic|Magic]]* (1902) provided the theoretical foundation: mana is the "value" that circulates in systems of total prestation, just as money circulates in market economies. The difference is that mana cannot be alienated — it always carries the identity of its origin, which is why gifts must return.
