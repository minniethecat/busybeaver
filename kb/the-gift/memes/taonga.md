## Definition

**Taonga** (plural: *taonga*) is a Māori term from Aotearoa (New Zealand) that denotes treasures, heirlooms, and culturally valued possessions — both tangible (carved objects, greenstone, cloaks) and intangible (language, knowledge, ceremonies). In the context of [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]ian gift theory, taonga is significant because it embodies the *[[hau]]* (spirit, life-force) of its giver, creating an inalienable bond between the object, its original owner, and its recipient ([[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] 1925:11–14).

## Origin and Development

The concept of taonga entered anthropological theory through Elsdon Best's ethnographic work among the Māori, published in *Forest Lore of the Māori* (1909) and *The Māori* (1924). Best recorded the explanation given by the Māori sage Tamati Ranapiri regarding the *[[hau]]* of taonga — that when a gift is given, the *[[hau]]* of the giver remains in the object and compels a return gift. [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] used this account as the centrepiece of his theory of gift reciprocity:

> "The *taonga* and all strictly personal property possess a *hau* — a spiritual power. You give me one of them and I give it to a third person; he then gives me another *taonga*, because he is forced to do so by the *hau* of my gift. And I, for my part, am obliged to give this thing to you, since I must return to you what is in reality the product of the *hau* of your *taonga*." (Best 1909:439, quoted in Mauss 1925:12)

Annette Weiner later revisited taonga in *[[inalienable-possessions|Inalienable Possessions]]* (1992), arguing that [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] underemphasized the "[[inalienable-possessions|keeping-while-giving]]" dimension — that taonga are objects that must never be fully alienated, even when gifted.

## Key Elements

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Hau** | The spiritual essence or life-force that dwells in taonga, particularly in gifts |
| **Inalienability** | Taonga carry the identity of their givers and ancestors; they cannot be fully separated from their origin |
| **Reciprocal [[obligation|Obligation]]** | Receiving a taonga creates an [[obligation]] to reciprocate — the *[[hau]]* compels return |
| **Tangible/Intangible** | Taonga includes both material treasures (greenstone, carvings) and intangible heritage (language, genealogy) |
| **Ancestral Connection** | Taonga link present generations to ancestors (tūpuna) and the spiritual world |

## Theoretical Context

Taonga is central to [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s argument that gifts are never "free" — they carry the [[personne|person]]hood of the giver. The *[[hau]]* of the taonga explains why gifts must be reciprocated: the gift contains something of the giver, and keeping it without return would be spiritually dangerous. This insight forms the foundation of [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s theory of the gift as a "[[total-prestation|total social fact]]" ([[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] 1925:89).

Sahlins later critiqued [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s interpretation of the Māori material, arguing that *[[hau]]* is better understood as a principle of "productivity" rather than "spirit" — the return gift represents the increase generated by the original gift (Sahlins 1972:157–160). Weiner's concept of "[[inalienable-possessions|keeping-while-giving]]" further complicates the picture, suggesting that certain taonga are never meant to circulate freely.

## In the Text

[[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] places the taonga/[[hau]] argument at the very opening of his theoretical exposition:

> "What power resides in the object given that causes its recipient to pay it back? ... It is the *hau* which wishes to return to its birthplace, to the sanctuary of the forest and the clan, and to its owner. The *taonga*, or its *hau* — which is itself a kind of individual — constrains a series of users to return some kind of *taonga* of their own, some property or merchandise or labour." (Mauss 1925:13–14)
