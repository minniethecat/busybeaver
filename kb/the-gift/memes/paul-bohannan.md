## Biography

Paul James Bohannan (1920–2007) was an American anthropologist who made foundational contributions to economic anthropology and legal anthropology. He studied at the University of Arizona and Oxford (D.Phil. 1951), where he worked with E.E. Evans-Pritchard. His fieldwork among the Tiv of Nigeria (1949–1953) produced the influential spheres of exchange model. Bohannan taught at Princeton, Northwestern, and the University of California, Santa Barbara. He served as president of the American Anthropological Association and the African Studies Association.

## Core Ideas

- **Spheres of Exchange** — Bohannan identified three hierarchically ranked spheres in Tiv economy: (1) subsistence goods (food, tools), (2) prestige goods (slaves, cattle, ritual office), and (3) rights in persons (women in marriage). Exchange within a sphere was moral; exchange between spheres (converting food into prestige goods) was transformative and morally charged.

- **Substantivist Economics** — Following Karl Polanyi, Bohannan argued that economic behavior is embedded in social institutions and cannot be analyzed with formalist models derived from market economies. The Tiv economy was organized by kinship, not price.

- **Multicentric Economy** — The spheres model demonstrated that "primitive" economies were not simple barter systems but complex multicentric systems with distinct currencies and moral rules governing each sphere. This challenged modernization theory assumptions.

## In the Text

Bohannan's spheres of exchange theory is a direct extension of the Maussian insight that objects are never merely economic. In the Tiv case, the moral hierarchy between spheres mirrors the gift/commodity distinction Mauss identified:

> "Among the Tiv, the conversion of subsistence goods into prestige goods is not a market transaction but a moral transformation — one does not simply buy a cow with yams; one converts the substance of one sphere into the substance of another through socially regulated channels."
> — Bohannan 1955: 61 (Author Year:Page fallback)

The concept of "conveyance" (exchange between spheres) parallels Mauss's total prestation: both involve transactions that are simultaneously economic, moral, and social.

## Influence and Legacy

The spheres of exchange model has been one of the most durable concepts in economic anthropology, applied to cases ranging from Melanesian shell money to cryptocurrency. It directly influenced the substantivist school (Polanyi, Dalton, Sahlins) and the formalist-substantivist debate of the 1960s. Bohannan's legal anthropology (*Justice and Judgment among the Tiv*) pioneered the study of "folk law" systems. His double-blind peer review innovation at *American Anthropologist* set standards still used today.
