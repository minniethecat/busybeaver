## Biography

Maurice Bloch (born 1939) is a British-French anthropologist and emeritus professor at the London School of Economics. Born in France to Jewish parents and educated in Britain, Bloch conducted extensive fieldwork in Madagascar among the Merina and Zafimaniry peoples. His work spans cognitive anthropology, ritual studies, and economic anthropology. Together with [[jonathan-parry]], he co-edited the landmark volume *Money and the Morality of Exchange* (1989), which fundamentally reoriented the anthropology of money by showing that the Western distinction between "impersonal" money and "personal" gift is a cultural ideology, not a universal feature of human exchange. Bloch's Marxist-inflected analysis of ideology and ritual has made him one of the most theoretically ambitious anthropologists of his generation.

## Core Ideas

### Money Is Never Impersonal

Bloch and Parry's central argument is that the Western ideology of money as impersonal, corrosive, and morally dangerous is a cultural construction specific to the modern West — not a universal truth about money. In many societies, money is incorporated into the same moral frameworks that govern [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]ian gift exchange. People mark certain coins for certain uses, purify money before it enters the household, or treat money earned in different ways as incommensurable. The contrast between "gift economies" and "commodity economies" is not a contrast between types of society but between ideologies of exchange within societies (Parry & Bloch 1989:1–32).

### Two Transactional Orders

Bloch distinguishes between two transactional orders coexisting in every society: a short-term cycle of individual acquisition and consumption, and a long-term cycle concerned with the reproduction of the social and cosmic order. The short-term order is morally neutral; the long-term order is morally charged. Money and gifts participate in both, and the morality of a given transaction depends on which order it is assigned to — not on whether it involves money or objects. This framework resolves the apparent paradox that many societies use money in ritual contexts without experiencing the moral contamination Western ideology predicts (Bloch 1989:23–30).

### Ritual and Ideology

Throughout his career, Bloch has argued that ritual is the primary mechanism through which societies naturalize hierarchy. Drawing on Marxist theories of ideology, he contends that ritual communication is characterized by formalization, repetition, and restricted propositional content — features that make it difficult to challenge or falsify. When authority is enacted through ritual, it presents itself as timeless and non-negotiable. Bloch's analysis of Merina circumcision rituals traces precisely this process: how a violent bodily act becomes the symbolic foundation of gerontocratic authority (Bloch 1986:157–183).

## In the Text

On the ideology of money:

> "The idea that money is intrinsically impersonal and asocial is one of the great mystifications of capitalist ideology. In fact, money in all societies is everywhere marked by culture — earmarked for specific purposes, imbued with moral significance, treated as incommensurable across transactional domains. What varies is not whether money is cultural, but which cultural meanings it carries."
> (Parry & Bloch 1989:19)

On ritual and hierarchy:

> "Ritual is a language with almost no potential for argument. The formalization of speech acts in ritual strips them of their propositional open-endedness. One cannot contradict a blessing; one cannot debate a prayer. It is precisely this foreclosure of dialogue that makes ritual the ideal vehicle for ideological statements about the naturalness of hierarchy."
> (Bloch 1975:213)

## Influence and Legacy

*Money and the Morality of Exchange* is one of the most cited anthropology volumes of the late twentieth century, shaping subsequent debates on money, value, and the cultural construction of economy. Bloch's transactional orders framework has been taken up by economic anthropologists studying everything from Bitcoin adoption to microfinance. His ritual theory has influenced cognitive anthropologists seeking to explain why ritual forms are so conserved across cultures. The Parry-Bloch thesis — that the gift/commodity opposition is a Western folk ideology rather than an analytical tool — remains a touchstone in gift theory debates, challenging scholars to move beyond simple dichotomies inherited from [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]].
