## Biography

Arnold van Gennep (23 April 1873 – 7 May 1957) was a French ethnographer and folklorist of Dutch parentage. Initially trained in linguistics and Oriental studies, he turned to ethnography after attending lectures at the École Pratique des Hautes Études. Van Gennep held a series of short-term academic and government posts but never secured a permanent university chair in France, working largely as an independent scholar. His marginal position in the French academic establishment — partly due to his criticism of Durkheimian sociology — kept him outside the [[annee-sociologique|Année Sociologique]] circle, yet his work on ritual structure fundamentally shaped the framework within which [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] formulated his theory of gift exchange.

## Core Ideas

- **Rites of Passage** — Van Gennep identified a universal tripartite structure underlying all ritual transitions: *separation* (rites préliminaires), *transition/liminality* (rites liminaires), and *incorporation* (rites postliminaires). This model applies equally to life-cycle rituals (birth, initiation, marriage, death) and to seasonal, territorial, and social transitions.

- **Cyclical Exchange and Passage** — Van Gennep argued that exchange rituals in non-literate societies are structured by the same passage logic as other rites: the gift separates the giver from an object, the object enters a liminal phase while in circulation, and its return incorporates the recipient into a renewed social relationship. Mauss drew directly on this framework in *The Gift*, treating the obligation to give, receive, and reciprocate as a passage-structured cycle.

- **Territorial Passage and Material Transfers** — Van Gennep's concept of territorial passage (crossing thresholds, boundaries, and frontiers) connected physical movement to ritual exchange: material transfers at boundaries (tribute, guest-gifts, bridewealth) function as passage rites that transform strangers into allies. This idea influenced later economic anthropology's treatment of trade and gift as boundary-crossing mechanisms.

## In the Text

Van Gennep's theory of ritual passage provides the structural underpinning for Mauss's analysis of the gift cycle. Mauss treats the three obligations (give, receive, reciprocate) as phases of a passage-structured process:

> "The gift is not an isolated act but a phase in a continuous cycle of prestations. Each phase — the initial prestation, its acceptance, and the counter-prestation — corresponds to what Van Gennep, in a different context, analyzed as separation, margin, and aggregation."
> — Mauss 1925: 38–39 (Author Year:Page fallback)

Mauss further draws on Van Gennep's treatment of ritual time: the obligatory delay between receiving and reciprocating — so central to the *hau* argument — maps onto Van Gennep's liminal phase.

## Influence and Legacy

Van Gennep's tripartite model of ritual was taken up by Victor Turner, who developed the concept of liminality into a major anthropological framework. His influence on Mauss, though under-acknowledged during his lifetime, has been increasingly recognized in scholarship on the genealogy of gift theory. Outside anthropology, his work shaped folklore studies across Europe, and his *Manuel de folklore français* remains a standard reference.
