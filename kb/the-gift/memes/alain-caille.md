## Biography

Alain Caillé (born 1944) is a French sociologist and professor emeritus at Paris Nanterre University. In 1981, he co-founded MAUSS (Mouvement Anti-Utilitariste dans les Sciences Sociales) and its journal *Revue du MAUSS*, which became the central institutional vehicle for the revival of [[marcel-mauss]]'s gift theory in contemporary social science. Caillé's work systematically argues that the gift paradigm offers an alternative to both utilitarian individualism and structuralist holism.

## Core Ideas

### The Gift as Third Paradigm

Caillé argues that [[marcel-mauss]]'s *Essai sur le don* offers a "third paradigm" for social science, distinct from methodological individualism (the actor calculates interest) and methodological holism (the actor is determined by structure). In the gift paradigm, action is motivated by the desire to create and maintain social bonds — neither purely instrumental nor purely normative (Caillé 2000:11–35).

### The Cycle of Giving-Receiving-Reciprocating

Building on Mauss's three obligations, Caillé emphasizes that the gift is defined not by the absence of return but by the *uncertainty* of return. The cycle of giving, receiving, and reciprocating generates a social bond precisely because each step is voluntary and cannot be contractually enforced. This distinguishes the gift from both market exchange (enforceable by contract) and altruism (no expectation of return) (Godbout & Caillé 1994:24–28).

### Anti-Utilitarianism and Solidarity

Caillé's anti-utilitarianism does not reject all forms of interest but insists that the primary social bond is not constructed through calculation. His work with MAUSS traces how gift practices persist within capitalist economies — in family relations, friendship, blood donation, and voluntary association — and argues that these practices are the "underground" foundation of social solidarity (Caillé 1989:81–92).

## In the Text

On the gift as social bond:

> "The gift is not a thing but a social relationship. What circulates through giving, receiving, and reciprocating is not merely an object but the bond itself — a bond that no contract can substitute because it depends precisely on the voluntary and the indeterminate."
> (Godbout & Caillé 1994:22)

On the poverty of utilitarianism:

> "The utilitarian fiction — that social actors are calculating machines maximizing their interests — is not only false but destructive. It makes invisible the dense web of gift relations that sustain families, associations, friendships, and ultimately the motivation to work and to live together."
> (Caillé 2000:40–41)

## Influence and Legacy

Caillé's MAUSS group created the institutional and intellectual infrastructure for the French gift paradigm revival. The *Revue du MAUSS* has published over 50 volumes engaging economics, politics, ecology, and the arts from an anti-utilitarian perspective. Caillé's framework influenced [[maurice-godelier]]'s dialectical reading of Mauss in *The Enigma of the Gift* and has been taken up in Anglo-American sociology through the work of Frank Adloff and Ilana Silber. His insistence that the gift is the elementary form of social solidarity — not an archaic survival — has made the gift paradigm a resource for critics of neoliberal economic policy. The "convivialist" manifesto (2014), co-authored by Caillé and other MAUSS intellectuals, extended gift theory into a political program for post-neoliberal society.
