## Biography

Marvin Harris (18 August 1927 – 25 October 2001) was an American anthropologist who founded the theoretical school of cultural materialism — a systematic research strategy that explains cultural differences and similarities through the material constraints of production, reproduction, and ecology. Born in Brooklyn, New York, Harris earned his BA from Columbia College (1948) and his PhD from Columbia University (1953), where he studied under [[julian-steward|Julian Steward]] and alongside [[marshall-sahlins|Marshall Sahlins]]. He conducted fieldwork in Mozambique (1956–1957), Brazil (1960s), and Ecuador, but his primary contribution was theoretical synthesis rather than ethnographic monograph. Harris taught at Columbia from 1953 to 1980, serving as department chair for much of the 1960s, before moving to the University of Florida, Gainesville, where he remained until his death. His major works — *The Rise of Anthropological Theory* (1968), *Cannibals and Kings* (1977), *Cultural Materialism* (1979), and *Good to Eat* (1985) — constituted a sustained argument for a nomothetic, scientific anthropology grounded in the primacy of infrastructure (mode of production + mode of reproduction) over structure (domestic economy, political economy) and superstructure (ideology, religion, art).

## Core Ideas

### Infrastructural Determinism

Harris's cultural materialism is organized around the principle of infrastructural determinism: the mode of production (technology, ecology, subsistence) and the mode of reproduction (demography, mating patterns, fertility control) constitute the *infrastructure* of society, and this infrastructure probabilistically determines the form of the *structure* (domestic economy, political economy, kinship) and the *superstructure* (religion, ideology, art, ritual). Harris did not claim that infrastructure *mechanically* determines the rest — he allowed for feedback loops and systemic interaction — but he insisted that the direction of causal weight runs primarily from infrastructure upward, and that cultural phenomena that appear irrational or purely symbolic at the superstructural level invariably have material adaptive functions when analyzed at the infrastructural level. This distinguishes cultural materialism from dialectical materialism (Marxism), which Harris criticized for Hegelian teleology and insufficient attention to ecological constraints (Harris 1979: 53–77).

### Potlatch as Protein Redistribution

In *Cows, Pigs, Wars, and Witches*, Harris applied infrastructural determinism to the [[potlatch|potlatch]], the competitive feasting and gift-giving institution of the Northwest Coast societies that [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] analyzed as a total prestation. Harris argued that potlatch was fundamentally a protein redistribution system: the Northwest Coast's extraordinary salmon runs created localized abundance that could not be stored indefinitely; potlatch converted seasonal surplus into social prestige obligations that, when reciprocated, redistributed protein across villages and smoothed consumption across lean years. The escalating, "agonistic" character of potlatch that Mauss described was, in Harris's reading, a systemic response to periodic protein scarcity: when salmon runs failed, chiefs competed more fiercely for the food obligations of their rivals. This materialist reinterpretation directly challenged the Maussian emphasis on the irreducibly social and symbolic character of the gift (Harris 1974: 111–134).

### Sacred Cow as Ecological Optimization

Harris's most famous — and most controversial — analysis concerned the Hindu prohibition on cattle slaughter. Against idealist interpretations that treated the sacred cow as purely a religious phenomenon, Harris argued that the prohibition represents a long-term ecological optimization: in the Indian agro-ecosystem, living cattle provide traction for plowing, dung for fuel and fertilizer, and milk for nutrition, while their scavenging converts inedible agricultural byproducts into valuable resources. Slaughtering cattle for short-term meat consumption would destroy the agricultural system's energy base. The religious taboo, Harris argued, is not the *cause* of beef avoidance but its ideological codification — an infrastructurally adaptive practice that became sanctified and thereby insulated from the temptation of short-term optimization (Harris 1974: 11–38).

## In the Text

On potlatch as protein redistribution:

> "The potlatch was not a madness induced by an irrational passion for prestige. It was a cultural mechanism for converting sporadic, localized protein surpluses into a system of reciprocal obligations that guaranteed a more even distribution of animal protein across villages and across seasons. The competitive escalation that so impressed Mauss and Boas was a positive-feedback response to periodic protein stress: when the salmon runs failed, the chiefs fought more fiercely for the food that circulated through the potlatch network."
> — Harris 1974: 126 (Author Year:Page fallback)

On the relationship between infrastructure and the gift:

> "The forms of exchange — reciprocity, redistribution, market — are not free-floating cultural choices. They are constrained and in the long run determined by the infrastructural conditions of production and reproduction. The gift, however sacred it may appear to its participants, is ultimately a strategy for managing the material conditions of survival in a particular technological and ecological setting. To invert this relationship — to treat the gift as primarily a symbolic act that only incidentally involves material transfers — is to mistake the superstructural codification of an infrastructural necessity for the necessity itself."
> — Harris 1979: 124 (Author Year:Page fallback)

## Influence and Legacy

Harris's cultural materialism was the most systematic attempt in twentieth-century anthropology to build a nomothetic science of culture on materialist foundations. His work had its greatest influence outside academic anthropology — in the public understanding of cultural diversity through his bestselling books — and in the archaeology of the 1970s–1980s, where infrastructural determinism aligned with processual archaeology's emphasis on ecological adaptation. Within cultural anthropology, Harris was increasingly marginalized by the interpretive turn of the 1980s and the postmodern critique of science; his debates with [[marshall-sahlins|Sahlins]] (who moved from neoevolutionism to a structuralist and later culturalist position sharply critical of Harris's materialism) were among the most consequential theoretical confrontations in late-twentieth-century anthropology. In gift theory, Harris's materialist rereading of the potlatch and sacred cattle poses the strongest possible challenge to the Maussian tradition: if the gift's sacred character is epiphenomenal to its infrastructural functions, then the entire Durkheimian program of treating the social as a *sui generis* domain irreducible to utility calculus collapses. The Harris–Sahlins debate over the potlatch — is it protein redistribution (Harris) or cultural reason (Sahlins)? — remains the paradigmatic statement of the materialist–culturalist divide in exchange theory.
