## History

The **École Pratique des Hautes Études** (EPHE; "Practical School of Advanced Studies") was founded in 1868 by Victor Duruy, Minister of Public Instruction under Napoleon III, with the mission of advancing scientific research through practical training rather than lectures. Unlike the [[college-de-france]], which offered public lectures to general audiences, the EPHE was designed as a research institution where students worked directly alongside scholars in laboratories, seminars, and fieldwork.

The EPHE was organized into sections. The Fifth Section (Section des Sciences Religieuses), established in 1886, became the institutional home for the [[annee-sociologique|Durkheimian school]]'s study of religious and symbolic phenomena — a domain at the intersection of sociology, anthropology, and comparative religion.

## Role in French Anthropology

The EPHE was central to the institutionalization of French anthropology in the early twentieth century:

| Figure | Role at EPHE | Period |
|--------|-------------|--------|
| [[marcel-mauss]] | Chair, "History of Religions of Uncivilized Peoples" (Fifth Section) | 1901–1940 |
| [[emile-durkheim]] | Affiliated researcher (primary position at the Sorbonne) | — |
| [[lucien-levy-bruhl]] | Affiliated scholar; co-founder of the [[institut-ethnologie]] | — |

[[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s appointment to the EPHE in 1901 gave him an institutional platform independent of his uncle's patronage at the Sorbonne. From this position, [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] taught a broad curriculum encompassing the religions of India, ancient Greece, and "primitive" societies — a range that reflected his encyclopedic knowledge and prepared the ground for his comparative synthesis in *The Gift*.

## The Institut d'Ethnologie Connection

In 1925, the same year [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] published *The Gift*, the EPHE, the University of Paris, and the Ministry of Colonies jointly established the [[institut-ethnologie]]. [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]], [[lucien-levy-bruhl]], and [[paul-rivet]] served as the founding professors. The Institute provided the first formal professional training in ethnology in France, and its early students included [[claude-levi-strauss]], who later credited [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s EPHE lectures with shaping his structural approach.

## In the Text

The EPHE does not appear by name in *The Gift*, but it was the institutional context in which [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] developed the comparative method that structures the essay. [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s EPHE teaching traversed the full range of societies he draws upon — from ancient Indo-European law (Rome, India, Germanic tribes) to the ethnography of the [[trobriand-islands]] and the [[pacific-northwest]] — and his ability to move between these registers reflects the EPHE's interdisciplinary ideal.
