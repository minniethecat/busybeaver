## Definition

The **hau** (often translated as "spirit of the gift" or "spirit of the thing given") is a Māori concept that [[marcel-mauss]] used in *The Gift* (1925) to explain the mechanism that compels the return of gifts. According to the Māori informant Tamati Ranaipiri, as quoted by [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] through the writings of Elsdon Best, the *hau* of the forest — the spiritual power of the *[[taonga]]* (treasure) — resides in the gift-object and desires to return to its place of origin. This return is accomplished when the recipient gives a [[counter-gift]], carrying the original *hau* back to the giver.

The *hau* is thus [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s answer to the central question of the [[gift-economy]]: **Why is a gift necessarily reciprocated?** It is not self-interest or contract law that enforces the [[obligation]], but a spiritual mechanism embedded in the object itself.

## The Māori Account

[[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] quotes Ranaipiri's explanation at length (via Best):

| Step | Action | Mechanism |
|------|--------|-----------|
| 1 | A gives a *[[taonga]]* to B | The *hau* of A resides in the gift |
| 2 | B gives the *[[taonga]]* to C | C now possesses the object with A's *hau* |
| 3 | C gives a return gift to B | The return gift carries the *hau* back toward A |
| 4 | B must pass this return to A | Otherwise the *hau* will harm B — "it is the *hau* of the forest" |

This chain creates a compelling spiritual logic: the gift contains something of the giver's self, and that something must return home. A gift is never fully alienated from the giver — a radical contrast to commodity exchange, where ownership is transferred absolutely.

## Critical Reception

The *hau* passage is among the most debated in anthropological theory. [[claude-levi-strauss]], in his 1950 introduction to [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s collected works, criticized [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] for taking the Māori informant's explanation at face value — what [[claude-levi-strauss|Lévi-Strauss]] called the "indigenous theory" fallacy. For [[claude-levi-strauss|Lévi-Strauss]], the *hau* was not the cause of reciprocity but a local, post-hoc rationalization of a deeper structural principle: that exchange is the primary form of social life.

> Mauss allowed himself to be mystified by an indigenous theory. The *hau* is not the reason for exchange; it is the conscious representation that a particular society gives of an unconscious structural necessity.
>
> （Lévi-Strauss 1950, paraphrased critique）

Subsequent anthropologists have offered other interpretations. Marshall Sahlins argued that the *hau* passage reflects a specifically Māori theory of productive increase — the *hau* of the forest represents fertility, not simply spiritual [[obligation]]. Others have questioned whether [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] (and Best before him) fully understood the Māori concept in its cosmological context.

## In the Text

[[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] deploys the *hau* as his central explanatory mechanism:

> What imposes obligation in the present received and exchanged, is the fact that the thing received is not inactive. Even when it has been abandoned by the giver, it still possesses something of him. Through it the giver has a hold over the beneficiary just as, being its owner, through it he has a hold over the thief.
>
> （Mauss 1966:11–12）

The *hau* concept bridges the material and the spiritual, the individual and the collective — making it the key to [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s argument that [[gift-economy|gift exchange]] is a [[total-prestation|total social phenomenon]].
