## Biography

Ronald Godfrey Lienhardt (17 January 1921 – 9 November 1993) was a British social anthropologist and the leading successor to [[edward-evans-pritchard|Evans-Pritchard]] at the University of Oxford. Born in Bradford, Yorkshire, of Swiss-German ancestry, he studied English at Cambridge before serving in the British Army in East Africa during World War II. After the war, he studied anthropology at Oxford under Evans-Pritchard. His fieldwork among the Dinka of South Sudan (1947–1950) produced the most detailed account of Dinka religion, personhood, and ritual ever written. Lienhardt taught at Oxford from 1951 until his retirement, succeeding Evans-Pritchard as Reader in Social Anthropology. Though his published output was modest — a single ethnographic monograph and a handful of essays — *Divinity and Experience: The Religion of the Dinka* (1961) is widely regarded as one of the finest studies of religion in the anthropological canon, and his analysis of sacrifice, symbolism, and the social construction of the person directly engages [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s category of the person (*personne*).

## Core Ideas

### The Passions and the Divinities

Lienhardt showed that Dinka divinity (*nhialic*) is not a single being but a many-sided presence manifested in multiple "powers" (*jok*, *yath*) each associated with a particular human condition or affliction. These powers are not just external beings but "images of the passions" — representations of experiences that Dinka undergo: illness, desire, the mysteries of twinship, the violence of lightning. Sacrifice is the ritual means by which Dinka integrate these experiences into the moral order, transforming raw passion into structured social relationship. This analysis resonates with [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] and [[henri-hubert|Hubert]]'s theory of sacrifice (1899): Lienhardt shows that sacrifice is simultaneously a transaction with the divine and a re-structuring of the social person (Lienhardt 1961: 53–103).

### Personhood and the Self

Lienhardt's Dinka ethnography made an important contribution to the anthropology of personhood. The Dinka concept of the person is composite: each individual is a conjunction of *wei* (breath/life), *yic* (the interior self, seat of thought and emotion), and *tieŋ* (shadow/spirit). At death, *wei* departs, *tieŋ* persists as a potentially dangerous ancestral presence, and *yic* dissolves. The person is thus not a bounded individual but a temporary knot in a web of relations that includes ancestors, divinities, and living kin. This analysis directly extends [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s 1938 Huxley Lecture on the category of the person (*personne*) — showing how one African society constructs personhood through relations of exchange, sacrifice, and descent (Lienhardt 1961: 147–170).

### Symbolic Interpretation vs. Explanation

Lienhardt argued against reducing Dinka religion to either psychological projection or sociological function. Dinka ritual symbols do not "stand for" something else — they are not signs pointing to a hidden meaning — but are the very medium through which Dinka experience and act upon their world. Understanding Dinka religion requires not translation into Western categories but a patient attention to the internal logic of Dinka symbolic practice. This approach influenced later symbolic anthropologists ([[victor-turner]], [[mary-douglas]]) and the "ontological turn" of the early twenty-first century.

## In the Text

On sacrifice as the transformation of passion into order:

> "The Dinka sacrifice not to change the world but to change themselves. When a man spears a bull at a shrine, he is not bargaining with powers — he is placing his own experience, his suffering or his desire, into the framework of the moral community. The bull dies so that the man may be re-integrated, his passion cooled into relation."

On the composite person:

> "The Dinka person is not a self-enclosed unit. What a Westerner calls 'myself' is for the Dinka a confluence — of breath that comes from Divinity, of a heart that feels, of a shadow that will outlast the body. None of these is the person alone; together, in their shifting relations, they make what a Dinka means by a human being."

## Influence and Legacy

Lienhardt's *Divinity and Experience* set a standard for the anthropology of religion that has rarely been matched. Its combination of meticulous ethnographic detail with philosophical sophistication influenced [[mary-douglas]]'s symbolic analysis, [[victor-turner]]'s ritual theory, and later work on the anthropology of the self. Within gift theory, Lienhardt's analysis of Dinka sacrifice as a transaction that transforms the person — rather than a simple exchange of goods for divine favor — provides an important corrective to economistic readings of sacrifice and gift. His work on Dinka personhood directly extends the Maussian sociology of the person, showing how African concepts of the self are constructed through the same logics of exchange, obligation, and relation that Mauss identified in the gift. Lienhardt's intellectual modesty — his refusal to produce theory detached from ethnography — meant his direct influence was smaller than it deserved to be, but his monograph remains essential reading for anyone working on ritual, sacrifice, or the anthropology of personhood.
