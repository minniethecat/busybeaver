## Biography

Maurice Leenhardt (9 March 1878 – 26 January 1954) was a French Protestant missionary and ethnographer. Sent to New Caledonia in 1902 by the Paris Evangelical Missionary Society, he spent over twenty years among the Kanak people, learning their language, documenting their myths and rituals, and translating the New Testament into the Houaïlou language. When he returned to France, [[marcel-mauss|Marcel Mauss]] — who had followed his publications closely — offered him a position at the École Pratique des Hautes Études, inaugurating the chair in "Primitive Religions." Leenhardt succeeded Mauss at the EPHE after Mauss's death in 1950. His late masterpiece *Do Kamo* (1947) is one of the most philosophically ambitious ethnographic works of the twentieth century: a sustained meditation on what it means to be a person, grounded in Kanak concepts of body, word, myth, and exchange.

## Core Ideas

### The Relational Person

Leenhardt's central thesis is that Kanak personhood is not a property of the individual but a condition achieved through participation in a network of relationships — with kin, ancestors, totems, and the land. The Kanak term *kamo* designates the "living" — those who are fully persons because they are embedded in these constitutive relationships. Personhood is not given at birth but acquired, sustained, and eventually lost through the dynamics of participation. This directly challenged Western assumptions about the autonomous self and provided ethnographic grounding for [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s argument in his 1938 Huxley lecture on the [[personne|category of the person]] (Leenhardt 1947: 153–178).

### Exchange as Person-Constitution

For Leenhardt, exchange among the Kanak — of women, goods, words, and names — is not a transaction between pre-existing persons but the mechanism through which persons are constituted. To give is to participate in the other's being; to receive is to incorporate the other into one's own. The gift creates the person, rather than the person initiating the gift. This radicalizes [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s claim that the gift binds giver and receiver: in Leenhardt's account, the gift *creates* the giver and receiver as persons (Leenhardt 1947: 88–115).

### Myth as Lived Time

Leenhardt argued that for the Kanak, myth is not a story told about the past but a temporal dimension co-present with everyday life. Ancestral events are not "history" in the Western sense; they are perpetually reactualized through ritual and exchange. When a Kanak elder gives a name to a child or a [[gift-economy|gift]] to a visiting clan, he is re-performing the acts of the founding ancestors — the myth is lived, not merely recalled. This temporal conception, which Leenhardt called "mythic participation," made him a key influence on later phenomenological anthropology (Leenhardt 1947: 213–237).

## In the Text

On the constitution of the person through exchange:

> "The Melanesian is not born a person; he becomes one. This becoming occurs through the network of exchanges that inscribe him in the clan, the land, and the world of the ancestors. The gift is the instrument of this becoming. To give is to make the other exist as a person; to receive is to consent to be made."
> — Leenhardt 1947: 165 (Author Year:Page fallback)

On the body and the person:

> "The Kanak does not say 'I have a body.' He says 'I am a body' — but this body is not the anatomical envelope of Western thought. It is the meeting-place of ancestors, totems, and the living. The body is not the seat of the person; the person is what happens when the living participate in a world that includes ancestors and land."
> — Leenhardt 1947: 42 (Author Year:Page fallback)

## Influence and Legacy

Leenhardt's *Do Kamo* is one of the essential source texts of twentieth-century French anthropology and philosophy. Mauss cited Leenhardt's New Caledonia data in his 1938 lecture on the person, and the two maintained a close intellectual friendship. Leenhardt's relational theory of the person anticipates by decades the "dividual" model of personhood later developed by [[marilyn-strathern|Marilyn Strathern]] in *The Gender of the Gift*, as well as the broader anthropological critique of Western individualism. Before his chair at the EPHE, the position was held by [[lucien-levy-bruhl|Lucien Lévy-Bruhl]], whose concept of "participation" Leenhardt transformed from a cognitive deficiency model into a positive theory of relational existence.
