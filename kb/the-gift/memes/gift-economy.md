## Definition

A **gift economy** is a system of exchange in which valuables are given without an explicit agreement for immediate or future quid pro quo. Rather than market-based commodity exchange, gift economies operate through the [[obligation]]s to **give**, **receive**, and **reciprocate** — the threefold [[obligation]] identified by [[marcel-mauss]] in *The Gift* (Essai sur le don, 1925).

## Origin and Development

The concept was first systematically theorized by [[marcel-mauss]] in his 1925 essay *Essai sur le don*, translated into English as *The Gift: Forms and Functions of Exchange in Archaic Societies*. [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] drew on ethnographic data from [[bronislaw-malinowski]]'s work on the [[kula-exchange]] in the [[trobriand-islands]] and Franz Boas's documentation of the [[potlatch]] among the indigenous peoples of the [[pacific-northwest]].

[[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] argued that gift exchange is a "total social phenomenon" (*[[total-prestation|fait social total]]*) — simultaneously legal, economic, religious, aesthetic, and morphological.

## Key Elements

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| [[obligation|Obligation]] to give | The social requirement to offer gifts, establishing and maintaining relationships |
| [[obligation|Obligation]] to receive | Refusing a gift is an act of hostility or declaration of war |
| [[obligation|Obligation]] to reciprocate | The recipient must return a gift, often with interest, creating a cycle of exchange |
| The *[[hau]]* ([[hau|spirit of the gift]]) | A Māori concept [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] used to explain why gifts must be returned |
| Total prestation | Exchanges that involve entire clans, tribes, or families, not just individuals |

## In the Text

[[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] opens *The Gift* with an analysis of the apparently "voluntary" nature of gifts that are in fact obligatory:

> In Scandinavian and many other civilizations contracts are fulfilled and exchanges of goods are made by means of gifts. In theory such gifts are voluntary but in fact they are given and repaid under obligation.
>
> （Mauss 1966:1）
