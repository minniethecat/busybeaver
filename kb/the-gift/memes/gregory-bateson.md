## Biography

Gregory Bateson (9 May 1904 – 4 July 1980) was a British anthropologist, cybernetician, and systems theorist whose work bridged anthropology, psychiatry, biology, and ecology. Born in Grantchester, England, the son of the distinguished geneticist William Bateson (who coined the term "genetics"), Gregory initially followed his father into biology, earning a BA in natural sciences from Cambridge (1925). He turned to anthropology under the influence of [[alfred-haddon|A. C. Haddon]] and [[alfred-radcliffe-brown|Radcliffe-Brown]] at Cambridge, conducting fieldwork among the Baining of New Britain (1927) and the Iatmul of the Sepik River, New Guinea (1929–1930, 1932–1933), during which he also worked alongside [[margaret-mead|Margaret Mead]] and her second husband [[reo-fortune|Reo Fortune]]. Bateson's Iatmul fieldwork produced *Naven* (1936), a study of Iatmul ritual transvestism that introduced the concept of "schismogenesis" — the process by which interaction between individuals or groups produces progressive differentiation. He married Mead in 1936, and together they conducted fieldwork in Bali (1936–1938), producing *Balinese Character: A Photographic Analysis* (1942), a pioneering visual ethnography using thousands of still photographs to analyze Balinese child-rearing and cultural patterns. After the war, Bateson moved increasingly toward cybernetics and communication theory, working with the Macy Conferences on cybernetics and developing the "double bind" theory of schizophrenia (with Don Jackson, Jay Haley, and John Weakland). His late work synthesized anthropology, biology, and cybernetics in *Steps to an Ecology of Mind* (1972) and *Mind and Nature* (1979).

## Core Ideas

### Schismogenesis

Bateson's central anthropological concept, developed from Iatmul ethnography, is "schismogenesis" — progressive differentiation through interaction. He distinguished two forms: **complementary schismogenesis**, in which the actions of A and B are different but mutually reinforcing (e.g., assertion and submission, each intensifying the other), and **symmetrical schismogenesis**, in which A and B respond in kind (e.g., competitive boasting or gift-giving, each party matching and escalating the other's contributions). In gift exchange, symmetrical schismogenesis describes the competitive spiral of the potlatch and other forms of agonistic reciprocity — each gift demands a counter-gift of equal or greater value, driving progressive escalation until one party is exhausted. This provides a formal, systems-theoretic description of what [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] called the "obligation to reciprocate" and what Boas described as the competitive logic of the Kwakiutl potlatch. Schismogenesis, however, also contains the seeds of system breakdown: unrestrained escalation leads either to the destruction of one party or to the dissolution of the relationship (Bateson 1936: 175–197).

### The Double Bind

The double bind theory, Bateson's most famous contribution outside anthropology, posits that schizophrenia can arise from a communicational context in which a person is repeatedly exposed to contradictory injunctions — typically a primary negative command and a secondary command contradicting the first at a different logical level — while being prevented from escaping or commenting on the contradiction. The double bind has resonances in gift theory: Mauss's analysis of the gift as simultaneously voluntary and obligatory, disinterested and self-interested, is a form of double bind at the cultural level. The recipient of a gift is both free to refuse and obliged to accept; the giver is both generous (giving freely) and strategic (creating obligation). The gift is a "double bind that binds" — a contradiction that is not pathological but socially productive (Bateson et al. 1956).

### Ecology of Mind

Bateson's late philosophy proposed that mind is not confined within the skull but is immanent in the total circuit of organism and environment — an "ecology of mind." The unit of survival is not the individual organism or the species but the organism-in-its-environment, the pattern of information flow that connects them. This has direct implications for understanding the gift: Maussian gift exchange, as a system of obligations linking persons across time and social space, is precisely such a circuit — the gift is information (about status, alliance, obligation) as much as it is material substance. The "spirit of the gift" that Mauss identified in the Māori *hau* can be re-described in Batesonian terms as a cybernetic feedback loop: the gift goes out, and information about the relationship returns, regulating the system's equilibrium (Bateson 1972: 448–465).

## In the Text

On schismogenesis and the dynamics of interaction:

> "When we consider the behaviour of two individuals or two groups, A and B, it is convenient to classify the possible types of interaction into two categories according to whether the actions of A and B are essentially similar or dissimilar. If they are similar, so that A's action stimulates B to a similar but more intense action, we may speak of 'symmetrical schismogenesis.' If, on the other hand, the actions are essentially dissimilar but mutually appropriate — as in the case of assertion and submission — we may speak of 'complementary schismogenesis.' Both types of progressive change may lead to breakdown."

## Influence and Legacy

Bateson occupies a unique position at the intersection of anthropology and cybernetics. Within anthropology, his schismogenesis concept has been applied to the analysis of competitive gift exchange, ethnic conflict, and ritual dynamics, providing a formal language for describing the escalatory logic that Mauss and Boas identified empirically. His Balinese work with [[margaret-mead|Mead]] pioneered visual anthropology and remains a landmark of collaborative ethnography. Outside anthropology, the double bind theory shaped family therapy and the anti-psychiatry movement, and his "ecology of mind" prefigured contemporary interest in distributed cognition, biosemiotics, and ecological thinking. In gift theory, Bateson provides the missing cybernetic vocabulary for Mauss's insight that the gift is a "total" phenomenon — not just economic, religious, and juridical, but also communicational, a circuit of information that binds persons into systems. The schismogenesis model formalizes what Mauss described intuitively: the gift obliges because it is a move in an interactional game whose rules compel response, and the system stabilizes when symmetrical escalation is contained by complementary checks.
