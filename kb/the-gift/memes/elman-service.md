## Biography

Elman Rogers Service (18 May 1915 – 14 November 1996) was an American anthropologist who, alongside [[marshall-sahlins|Marshall Sahlins]] and under the mentorship of [[leslie-white|Leslie White]] and [[julian-steward|Julian Steward]], was a central figure in the neoevolutionist revival of mid-twentieth-century American anthropology. Born in Tecumseh, Michigan, Service served in the Abraham Lincoln Brigade during the Spanish Civil War before earning his BA from the University of Michigan (1941) and his PhD from Columbia University (1950), where he studied under Steward alongside Sahlins. His early fieldwork was conducted among the Havasupai of the Grand Canyon and in Paraguay. Service taught at Columbia, the University of Michigan, and the University of California, Santa Barbara, where he spent the bulk of his career. With Sahlins, he co-edited *Evolution and Culture* (1960), the defining manifesto of neoevolutionist theory. Service's own work centered on the evolution of political organization, producing the band–tribe–chiefdom–state typology that became standard in introductory anthropology for decades.

## Core Ideas

### Band–Tribe–Chiefdom–State Typology

Service's most influential contribution was a four-stage typology of political organization, each stage marked by increasing scale, integration, and institutional specialization. Bands are small, egalitarian, kin-based groups with informal leadership; tribes are larger segmentary societies integrated by pantribal sodalities (age sets, clans) but still without centralized authority; chiefdoms introduce hereditary ranking, a centralized redistributive economy, and permanent leadership offices; states add bureaucratic administration, legal monopoly of force, and class stratification. This typology, though widely criticized for its unilineal implications and its neglect of heterarchical alternatives, became the default framework for teaching political evolution in introductory anthropology courses for decades (Service 1962: 59–177).

### The Law of Evolutionary Potential

In *Evolution and Culture*, Service proposed that the more specialized and adapted a society is to its current environment, the less "evolutionary potential" it possesses — the capacity to transform into a new, more complex form. Evolutionary breakthroughs therefore tend to occur not in the most advanced centers but in peripheral or less specialized regions. This principle explained, in Service's view, why archaic states arose not in the richest river valleys but at their margins, and why Europe rather than China or the Islamic world produced the Industrial Revolution (Service 1960: 93–122).

### Integration and Redistribution

Service argued that the shift from tribe to chiefdom was driven by the need to integrate larger populations through centralized redistribution. The chief becomes the economic hub of the society, collecting surplus from producers and redistributing it in feasts, ceremonies, and public works. This redistributive function creates the institutional apparatus — accounting, storage, labor coordination — that later crystallizes into the state bureaucracy. Service's model thus placed economic exchange (redistribution) at the center of political evolution, a position that resonated with [[karl-polanyi|Polanyi]]'s substantivist typology of reciprocity, redistribution, and market exchange, and provided evolutionary depth to Polanyi's comparative framework (Service 1975: 71–102).

## In the Text

On the evolutionary relationship between economic integration and political form:

> "The chiefdom is a redistributional society with a permanent central agency of coordination. The rise of chiefdoms appears to be related to conditions in which a society must produce more than it consumes locally — either because of specialized local production or because a coordinating authority is needed to manage a diverse or varying resource base."
> — Service 1962: 144 (Author Year:Page fallback)

On the logic of the band–tribe–chiefdom–state progression:

> "The increasing complexity of organization from band to state is not merely an increase in size. It is a functional response to the problem of social integration under conditions of increasing population density, sedentarization, and economic specialization. Each stage solves the integration problem of its predecessor, but at the cost of introducing new structural tensions that the next stage must resolve."
> — Service 1975: 15 (Author Year:Page fallback)

## Influence and Legacy

Service's typology, though largely abandoned by contemporary political anthropology for its unilineal assumptions, shaped a generation of archaeological theory (through the processual archaeology of Lewis Binford and Kent Flannery) and remained the most widely cited framework for cross-cultural comparison of political systems. His collaboration with [[marshall-sahlins|Sahlins]] on *Evolution and Culture* established the theoretical vocabulary — general vs. specific evolution, the law of evolutionary potential — that structured neoevolutionist debate through the 1960s and 1970s. In gift theory, Service's emphasis on redistribution as the central economic mechanism of chiefdoms complements [[karl-polanyi|Polanyi]]'s substantivist account of how exchange forms are embedded in political institutions. Service's work, together with that of [[elder-tylor|Tylor]], [[lewis-henry-morgan|Morgan]], [[leslie-white|White]], and [[julian-steward|Steward]], forms the canonical neoevolutionist lineage that runs from nineteenth-century unilinealism to twentieth-century cultural materialism and processual archaeology.
