## Definition

**Moral economy** is the idea that economic relations are embedded in moral norms — that peasants, workers, and other economic actors make claims about what is just, fair, and legitimate, not merely about what is profitable. The term was coined by British historian E.P. Thompson in "The Moral Economy of the English Crowd in the Eighteenth Century" (1971) and extended by political scientist James C. Scott in *The Moral Economy of the Peasant* (1976). Though the term is modern, the underlying insight — that economy is always moral economy — is deeply [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]ian.

## Origin and Development

Thompson introduced the concept to explain eighteenth-century English food riots, which he argued were not irrational mob violence but disciplined expressions of a popular consensus about legitimate prices and subsistence rights. The rioters were enforcing a "moral economy" against the emerging "political economy" of the free market (Thompson 1971:78–79).

Scott extended the concept to twentieth-century Southeast Asian peasant societies, arguing that the peasant's "safety-first" principle and preference for insurance over profit were rational adaptations to subsistence precarity — not evidence of an anti-market mentality. The moral economy, Scott argued, is a system of mutual [[obligation]] between patrons and clients, rich and poor: the wealthy have an [[obligation]] to provide in times of dearth, and the poor have a right to subsistence (Scott 1976:3–7).

The concept has clear affinities with [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]ian gift theory and Polanyian substantivism, though Thompson and Scott developed it independently.

## Key Elements

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Subsistence Ethic** | The right to a minimum livelihood takes precedence over market efficiency |
| **Just Price** | Prices are judged by moral criteria of fairness, not only supply and demand |
| **Mutual [[obligation|Obligation]]** | Rich and poor are bound by mutual, though asymmetrical, [[obligation]]s |
| **Legitimacy** | Economic arrangements must be seen as legitimate to be stable |

## Theoretical Context

The moral economy concept bridges [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]ian anthropology and modern political economy. Where [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] showed that "archaic" economies were gift economies governed by norms of reciprocity, Thompson and Scott showed that even market societies contain surviving or emergent moral economies. The concept thus extends [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s anti-utilitarian argument into the analysis of modern capitalism.

The affinity with Polanyi is also significant: Polanyi's *The Great Transformation* (1944) argued that the attempt to disembed the economy from society was the central disaster of nineteenth-century liberalism. The moral economy concept shows that this disembedding was never complete — moral norms about just economic behaviour persist even under capitalism.

## In the Text

Thompson's definitional passage:

> "The moral economy of the poor ... was grounded upon a consistent traditional view of social norms and obligations, of the proper economic functions of several parties within the community. ... An outrage to these moral assumptions, quite as much as actual deprivation, was the usual occasion for direct action." (Thompson 1971:79)

Scott on the subsistence ethic:

> "The subsistence ethic ... is the belief that all members of a community have a right to a subsistence minimum. ... This is not a question of altruism but of the minimal social insurance that even the powerful must concede." (Scott 1976:5–7)
