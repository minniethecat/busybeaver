## Biography

Edward Burnett Tylor (2 October 1832 – 2 January 1917) was a British anthropologist and the first major figure of academic anthropology in Britain. Born in Camberwell, London, to a Quaker family, he was originally destined for the family brass foundry business, but illness during travels in Cuba and Mexico in 1856 redirected his path toward the study of culture. His early encounters with Mexican antiquities produced *Anahuac* (1861), but his landmark work was *Primitive Culture* (1871), which defined culture as "that complex whole which includes knowledge, belief, art, morals, law, custom, and any other capabilities and habits acquired by man as a member of society" — a definition that remains foundational in anthropology. In 1884, Tylor became the first Reader in Anthropology at Oxford University, and in 1896 he was appointed the first Professor of Anthropology — institutionalizing the discipline in British academic life. His work formed the intellectual backdrop against which the French sociological school of [[emile-durkheim|Durkheim]] and [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] reacted, and his theory of the gift as a "survival" of primitive exchange was explicitly critiqued by Mauss in *The Gift*.

## Core Ideas

### The Doctrine of Survivals

Tylor's most influential methodological concept was the "survival" — a cultural element that persists into a later stage of civilization long after the conditions that produced it have disappeared. A peasant custom, a children's game, or a proverb might be a "survival" of an earlier cultural stage, providing evidence of evolutionary sequence. [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] and [[henri-hubert|Hubert]] explicitly took Tylor's survival concept as a point of departure in their theory of sacrifice, arguing that what Tylor treated as inert fossil was in fact a living social institution. In gift theory, Tylor's survivals concept shaped the evolutionist view that gift economies represent an archaic stage superseded by market exchange — a position Mauss devoted much of *The Gift* to refuting (Tylor 1871: I, 70–72).

### Animism as the Origin of Religion

Tylor argued that religion originated in "animism" — the belief that all things, including inanimate objects, possess a soul or spirit. Primitive humans, reflecting on the difference between a dead body and a living one, inferred the existence of a separable soul; dreams and visions provided further evidence. From this minimal belief, Tylor traced an evolutionary sequence through polytheism to monotheism. This intellectualist theory — religion as primitive science, as an effort to explain the world — was directly countered by Durkheim's *The Elementary Forms of the Religious Life* (1912), which insisted that religion's fundamental reality is social, not cognitive (Tylor 1871: I, 417–428).

### Culture as a Complex Whole

Tylor's opening definition of culture in *Primitive Culture* — "that complex whole which includes knowledge, belief, art, morals, law, custom, and any other capabilities and habits acquired by man as a member of society" — was the first formal anthropological definition of its object. By including capabilities and habits "acquired" rather than inherited, Tylor broke with racial theories of mental difference and committed anthropology to the study of learned, transmitted behavior. This definition underpins the Boasian critique of racial determinism and provides the conceptual foundation for the comparative study of exchange systems across cultures (Tylor 1871: I, 1).

## Influence and Legacy

Tylor is often called the father of British anthropology. He institutionalized the discipline at Oxford — [[alfred-haddon]] and [[whr-rivers]], both Cambridge anthropologists, were direct beneficiaries of Tylor's legitimization of anthropology as a university subject. His concept of survivals, though largely abandoned, remains a methodological warning against treating living institutions as fossils; it was this very warning that animated [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s insistence on studying the gift as a "total social fact" embedded in the present rather than a relic of primitive communism. Tylor's evolutionist framework — the assumption that all societies pass through the same stages from savagery to civilization — was dismantled by [[franz-boas|Boas]] and the American historical school, but his definition of culture endures. Within gift theory, Tylor represents the evolutionist position that Mauss's *Essai sur le don* was written to transcend: the gift is neither a survival of primitive barter nor a stage in economic evolution, but a living, total institution with its own logic irreducible to market rationality.
