## Biography

Raymond Firth (1901–2002) was a New Zealand anthropologist who studied under [[bronislaw-malinowski]] at the London School of Economics and later became professor of anthropology at LSE. His early fieldwork on Tikopia (Solomon Islands) produced landmark monographs on kinship and economy. Firth's work on Maori economic organization — *Primitive Polynesian Economy* (1939) and *Economics of the New Zealand Maori* (1959) — provided detailed ethnographic documentation of gift exchange practices that enriched and complicated [[marcel-mauss]]'s theoretical framework.

## Core Ideas

### Gift as Social Organization

Firth demonstrated that in Maori society, gift exchange was not a separate "ceremonial" sphere but fully integrated with economic organization. The circulation of [[taonga]] (treasures), the obligations of *utu* (reciprocity), and the distribution of agricultural produce through feasting were the mechanisms through which labor was mobilized, rank was maintained, and alliances were secured (Firth 1959:393–419).

### Critique of Pure Gift

While acknowledging the importance of Mauss's framework, Firth was skeptical of the idea that Maori gift exchange was purely non-economic. He argued that Maori actors made careful calculations of value, timing, and return — not in a profit-maximizing sense, but in a strategic sense that Mauss's theory tended to romanticize. The gift for Firth was "reciprocity with a time lag" rather than a radical alternative to exchange (Firth 1959:412–416).

### The Hau Redux

Firth revisited the famous *hau* of the forest passage in Mauss's *Essai sur le don* — the Maori informant Tamati Ranapiri's explanation that the *hau* (spirit) of the gift compels its return. Firth's reading of the original Maori text suggested that Mauss had overinterpreted the spiritual dimension of *hau*, and that the passage was better understood as describing a system of balanced reciprocity backed by social sanctions (Firth 1959:419–421).

## In the Text

On Maori gift calculation:

> "The Maori were well aware of the credit element in gift exchange. A gift given today was an investment in social relations, and the donor kept a careful account — not in ledgers, but in the memory of the community, which was the ultimate register of obligation. To pretend otherwise is to miss the economic sophistication of the system."
> (Firth 1959:413)

On the integration of gift and economy:

> "We cannot separate the 'ceremonial' from the 'economic' in Polynesian exchange, for the same transaction may simultaneously provision a household, honor an ancestor, discharge a debt, and create a political alliance. The gift is not the opposite of the economy; it is the form through which the economy is conducted."
> (Firth 1939:356–357)

## Influence and Legacy

Firth was a key figure in the development of economic anthropology as a recognized subdiscipline. His detailed ethnographic documentation of Pacific gift exchange provided an empirical counterweight to Mauss's sweeping theoretical synthesis. His critique of the "pure gift" concept influenced later substantivist debates between formalists and substantivists in economic anthropology. Firth's students — including [[edmund-leach]] — continued his work on the intersection of exchange, kinship, and social structure. His Maori studies remain essential reading for understanding the ethnographic basis of Maussian gift theory.
