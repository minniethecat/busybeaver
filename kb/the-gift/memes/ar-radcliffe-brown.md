## Biography

Alfred Reginald Radcliffe-Brown (17 January 1881 – 24 October 1955) was a British social anthropologist and the principal architect of structural-functionalism. After studying at Cambridge under W.H.R. Rivers and A.C. Haddon, he conducted fieldwork in the Andaman Islands (1906–1908) and Western Australia (1910–1912). Radcliffe-Brown held chairs at Cape Town, Sydney, Chicago, and Oxford, building a global network of students and establishing social anthropology as a comparative science of social structures. His synthetic reading of [[emile-durkheim|Durkheim]] and [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] made him the primary conduit through which French sociological theory entered British and American anthropology.

## Core Ideas

- **Structural-Functionalism** — Radcliffe-Brown argued that social structures (kinship, ritual, law) persist because they serve functions in maintaining the social organism. He drew an analogy between social life and organic life: institutions are to society what organs are to the body. Gift exchange, in this view, functions to sustain solidarity and stabilize status relations.

- **Exchange as Social Morphology** — Applying Mauss's *The Gift* to kinship analysis, Radcliffe-Brown treated marriage prestations and ceremonial exchange as mechanisms generating and reproducing the network of relationships that constitute the social structure. Gifts do not simply express existing relationships — they *create* them.

- **Ritual and Sentiment** — In *The Andaman Islanders*, Radcliffe-Brown analyzed ritual as the collective expression and regulation of socially necessary sentiments. Exchange ceremonies, in particular, produce the sentiments (obligation, gratitude, alliance) that hold groups together, a formulation that extended Mauss's treatment of the gift's moral force.

## In the Text

Mauss's influence on Radcliffe-Brown's treatment of exchange is evident throughout his career. Radcliffe-Brown explicitly adopted the Maussian framework of total prestation to analyze Australian marriage systems:

> "Marriage is not merely a union of individuals but a system of reciprocal obligations between corporate groups, sustained by a continuous circulation of goods, services, and persons. The prestations that accompany marriage — bridewealth, counter-gifts, feasts — form a total system of exchange of the kind Mauss has described for Melanesia and the American Northwest."
> — Radcliffe-Brown 1952: 53–54 (Author Year:Page fallback)

Radcliffe-Brown's structural-functional reading of Mauss — treating gift exchange as a mechanism of structural reproduction — became the dominant interpretation in British anthropology through the 1970s.

## Influence and Legacy

Radcliffe-Brown's structural-functionalism shaped a generation of British anthropologists including [[ee-evans-pritchard|Evans-Pritchard]], Meyer Fortes, and Max Gluckman. His treatment of gift exchange as a structural mechanism was later challenged by transactionalist and processual approaches but remains foundational in kinship studies. His disciplinary-building efforts established social anthropology as a distinct academic field in Britain and the Commonwealth.
