## Biography

Julian Haynes Steward (31 January 1902 – 6 February 1972) was an American anthropologist who founded the school of cultural ecology and, alongside [[leslie-white|Leslie White]], led the neoevolutionist revival in mid-twentieth-century American anthropology. Born in Washington, D.C., Steward studied at Cornell University (BS in zoology, 1925) and the University of California, Berkeley (PhD in anthropology, 1929), where he was exposed to the ecological thinking of Carl Sauer's Berkeley geography school. His early fieldwork included archaeological surveys of the Great Basin, ethnography of the Shoshone, and applied anthropology with the Bureau of Indian Affairs. Steward taught at Columbia University from 1946 to 1952, where he influenced a generation of students including [[marshall-sahlins|Marshall Sahlins]], before moving to the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign, where he remained until his retirement. Unlike White, who pursued a universal law of cultural evolution grounded in energy capture, Steward emphasized the empirical variability of evolutionary pathways — what he called "multilinear evolution" — and insisted that the relationship between environment, technology, and social organization must be demonstrated case by case rather than deduced from a single formula.

## Core Ideas

### Cultural Ecology

Steward's central concept was that the relationship between a society's technology and its physical environment — the "culture core" — is the primary determinant of social structure. Subsistence technology, resource distribution, and environmental constraints shape the size and composition of social groups, patterns of cooperation, and systems of property and exchange. In gift theory terms, Steward's framework suggests that the form of reciprocity — generalized, balanced, or negative, in Sahlins's later systematization — is shaped at base by the ecological conditions under which a society produces its subsistence. The Shoshone, dispersed into nuclear family bands by the sparse resources of the Great Basin, could sustain only intermittent, small-scale gift exchanges; the densely settled, salmon-rich societies of the Northwest Coast could generate the massive ceremonial distributions of the potlatch (Steward 1955: 30–42).

### Multilinear Evolution

Steward challenged both the unilinear evolutionism of [[edward-tylor|Tylor]] and [[lewis-henry-morgan|Morgan]] — which assumed all societies pass through identical stages — and the Boasian particularism that had made evolutionary thinking taboo in American anthropology. His solution was "multilinear evolution": the search for regularities in cultural change, not by assuming a single universal sequence, but by comparing societies that share similar environmental and technological conditions to identify parallel developmental trajectories. This method preserved the evolutionary interest in cross-cultural regularities while respecting the empirical variability that [[franz-boas|Boas]] had insisted on. The evolution of exchange systems — from reciprocal gift to redistributive tribute to market commodity — was, in Steward's view, not a universal law but a pattern that recurred where specific ecological and demographic conditions were met (Steward 1955: 11–29).

### The Culture Core

Steward distinguished the "culture core" — those features of a society most directly related to subsistence and economic arrangements — from "secondary features" determined more by historical diffusion and cultural drift. Social organization, land tenure, and exchange systems belonged to the core; art style, mythology, and ritual idiom belonged to secondary features. This distinction gave analytical priority to economic and ecological determinants and made gift exchange — as an economic institution organizing the circulation of subsistence goods — a core phenomenon subject to ecological explanation. Sahlins's later typology of reciprocity (generalized/balanced/negative) was an attempt to specify the precise social mechanisms linking ecology (the culture core) to exchange behavior (Steward 1955: 37).

## In the Text

On the method of cultural ecology:

> "Cultural ecology differs from human and social ecology in seeking to explain the origin of particular cultural features and patterns which characterize different areas rather than to derive general principles applicable to any cultural-environmental situation. It is less concerned with the general biological and psychological processes that are common to all human beings in all environments than with the specific solutions that different societies have worked out to the particular environmental problems they face."
> — Steward 1955: 36 (Author Year:Page fallback)

On the relationship between environment and social organization:

> "The Shoshone data suggest that the possession of a simple technology and the exploitation of sparse and scattered resources impose severe limitations on the size and permanence of social groups. Where the environment provides only meager and widely dispersed food supplies, the nuclear family is the largest unit that can be supported throughout the year."
> — Steward 1938: 230 (Author Year:Page fallback)

## Influence and Legacy

Steward's cultural ecology shaped two major currents in later anthropology: ecological anthropology (Roy Rappaport, Andrew Vayda) and the materialist wing of neoevolutionism (Marvin Harris, Elman Service). His most important conduit into gift theory, however, was through his student [[marshall-sahlins|Marshall Sahlins]], who absorbed Steward's ecological determinism at Columbia and combined it with [[karl-polanyi|Polanyi]]'s substantivist economics and White's energy theory to produce the reciprocity typology that became standard in economic anthropology. The Stewardian question — how does the mode of subsistence shape the form of exchange? — underlies Sahlins's analysis of reciprocity and social distance, even though Sahlins's own answer was more nuanced than ecological determinism would allow. Steward also influenced the development of world-systems theory (through Eric Wolf, his student and co-author on *The People of Puerto Rico*) and Latin American ethnohistory. In gift theory, Steward stands for the proposition that no theory of exchange is complete that does not specify the ecological conditions under which a given form of reciprocity is viable.
