## Biography

Jacques T. Godbout (born 1939) is a Quebec sociologist and professor emeritus at the Institut national de la recherche scientifique (INRS) in Montreal. His early work focused on organizational sociology and public administration. In the 1980s, he joined [[alain-caille|Alain Caillé]]'s MAUSS group (Mouvement Anti-Utilitariste dans les Sciences Sociales), becoming a central figure in the contemporary revival of [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]ian gift theory. Godbout brought empirical sociological methods to the MAUSS project, conducting interview-based research on how gift practices operate within modern — ostensibly market-dominated — societies.

## Core Ideas

### The Gift in Modern Society

Godbout's central contribution is the demonstration that gift practices are not archaic survivals but are pervasive in modern societies. Through interviews and case studies, he documented gift dynamics in family relations, blood donation, mutual aid networks, and professional mentoring — arguing that the gift is the "underground infrastructure" of social life beneath the market surface (Godbout & Caillé 1994:31–48).

### The Gift-Stranger Relationship

Godbout identified a distinctive pattern he called "the gift to strangers": modern individuals routinely give to people they will never meet again — blood donors, organ donors, anonymous charitable givers. This form of giving, he argued, is uniquely modern and cannot be reduced to either reciprocity or altruism. It expresses a generalized desire to maintain social connection even in anonymous mass society (Godbout 2000:73–94).

### The Spirit of the Gift versus the Thing

Drawing on [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s analysis of the Māori *hau*, Godbout argued that the gift always contains something of the giver — a "spirit" that resists complete commodification. This is why objects received as gifts are treated differently from objects purchased: they carry the mark of a relationship. In modern societies this "spirit" persists even when gifts are commercially produced objects — a birthday present bought at a store still carries the giver's intention (Godbout & Caillé 1994:56–67).

## In the Text

On gift as social bond:

> "The gift is not an object but a gesture. What is given is first of all a sign of the giver's desire to create or maintain a relationship. The object is the vehicle of this sign — necessary but secondary."
> (Godbout & Caillé 1994:19)

On the pervasiveness of gift:

> "Modern society appears to be governed by the market and the state, but beneath both runs a vast river of gift relations — in families, between friends, among neighbours, within associations. Were this river to dry up, neither market nor state could function for a single day."
> (Godbout 2007:28)

## Influence and Legacy

Godbout's empirical sociology of the gift provided the MAUSS movement with its strongest evidence against the utilitarian claim that gift economies exist only at the margins of modern society. His interview methodology — asking people to describe actual gift exchanges in their daily lives — revealed a density of gift relations that economic models had rendered invisible. His work influenced [[david-graeber]]'s concept of "baseline communism" (everyday mutual aid) and [[alain-caille]]'s synthesis of anti-utilitarian social theory. Within Quebec, Godbout's work on the gift has shaped public debates about healthcare, welfare, and the limits of market-based public policy.
