## Definition

**Inalienable possessions** is a concept introduced by anthropologist Annette Weiner in *Inalienable Possessions: The Paradox of Keeping-While-Giving* (1992) to describe objects that, even when given, retain a connection to their original owner. Unlike commodities or alienable gifts, inalienable possessions carry the identity, rank, and history of their givers, and their circulation is constrained by the imperative to "keep-while-giving" (Weiner 1992:6).

## Origin and Development

Weiner developed the concept through her re-analysis of Trobriand [[kula-exchange|kula]] exchange, the same ethnographic case [[bronislaw-malinowski|Malinowski]] used and [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] theorized. In *Women of Value, Men of Renown* (1976), Weiner had already shown that women's exchange of banana-leaf bundles and skirts constituted a parallel system to men's [[kula-exchange|kula]], one that [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] and [[bronislaw-malinowski|Malinowski]] had overlooked.

*Inalienable Possessions* (1992) broadened the argument into a theoretical challenge to reciprocity theory. Weiner argued that [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]], and especially his structural-functionalist interpreters (Sahlins, [[claude-levi-strauss|Lévi-Strauss]]), overemphasized the norm of reciprocity and underemphasized the norm of inalienability — the drive to keep certain objects out of circulation entirely. The most valued objects in any society are those that must not be given: crown jewels, sacred relics, ancestral heirlooms,知识产权 (intellectual property).

The concept draws explicitly on [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s own analysis of Māori *[[taonga]]* — objects that carry the *[[hau]]* of their givers — but Weiner argues [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] failed to follow through on the implications of his own insight.

## Theoretical Context

Weiner's concept resolves a tension already present in [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]: if gifts are freely given, why do they carry such powerful [[obligation]]s to return? The answer, Weiner suggests, is that gifts are never fully alienated — the giver retains a claim, however symbolic. This insight connects [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]ian gift theory to broader questions of property, heritage, and cultural ownership.

The concept has been influential in studies of cultural property and repatriation, where indigenous claims for the return of museum objects often invoke precisely the logic of inalienable possession: these objects were never meant to be given away.

## In the Text

Weiner explicitly frames her argument as a return to [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]:

> "In this book, I take up the challenge that Mauss himself posed: what power resides in the object given that causes its recipient to pay it back? ... I argue that the motivation for returning a gift lies in the inalienable possessions that the giver seeks to keep. ... What motivates reciprocity is its reverse — the desire to keep something back from the pressures of give and take. Something is withheld from the very beginning." (Weiner 1992:5–6, 40)
