## Biography

Maurice Godelier (b. 1934) is a French anthropologist and one of the most prominent figures in the Marxist anthropology tradition. A student of [[claude-levi-strauss|Lévi-Strauss]], Godelier served as director of studies at the École des Hautes Études en Sciences Sociales (EHESS) and scientific director of the CNRS Department of Human and Social Sciences. His work bridges structural Marxism, economic anthropology, and the anthropology of kinship and religion. His 1996 book *The Enigma of the Gift* is the most sustained late-twentieth-century engagement with [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s *Essai sur le don*.

## Core Ideas

**The Enigma of the Gift**: Godelier's central question: why do some objects circulate as gifts while others are kept? Building on [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] and [[annette-weiner|Annette Weiner]], he distinguished three categories: (1) alienable goods that can be sold, (2) prestations that must be given and reciprocated, and (3) sacred objects that must *never* be given. The third category — which includes the Baruya's *kwaimatnie* sacred objects — is what Godelier argued [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s theory could not fully explain.

**Imaginary and the Symbolic**: Extending [[claude-levi-strauss|Lévi-Strauss]]'s structuralism and Lacanian psychoanalysis, Godelier argued that social relations are not merely reflected in thought but are produced through the "imaginary" — shared representations that legitimize and mask the real structures of power, production, and kinship.

**Marxist Anthropology**: Godelier sought to demonstrate that Marxist concepts of infrastructure and superstructure could be applied to non-capitalist societies without economic reductionism. He argued that kinship and religion can *function as* relations of production in societies where the economy is embedded in other institutions — an argument that parallels [[karl-polanyi|Polanyi]]'s concept of [[embeddedness]].

## In the Text

On what Mauss could not explain:

> "What Mauss's theory of the gift does not explain is why, in every society, certain objects — sacred objects, precious heirlooms, symbols of authority — are kept out of the sphere of exchange. These objects are not given, because to give them would be to give oneself, to give one's identity and the identity of the group."
> (Godelier 1996:7)

On the relationship between gift and debt:

> "The gift is not merely a mechanism for creating social bonds through reciprocity. It is also — and perhaps primarily — a mechanism for creating debts that cannot be repaid, and therefore creating enduring hierarchies."
> (Godelier 1996:12)

## Influence and Legacy

Godelier's work represents the most ambitious attempt to reconcile [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]ian gift theory with Marxist political economy. His concept of sacred objects that must not be given has been taken up in studies of cultural heritage, museum collections, and repatriation debates. As editor of the journal *L'Homme* and a leading figure at EHESS, Godelier trained generations of French and international anthropologists.
