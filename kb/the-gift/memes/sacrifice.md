## Definition

**Sacrifice** (from Latin *sacrum facere* — to make sacred) is a ritual act in which an offering is consecrated and then destroyed or consumed, establishing communication between the profane world of the sacrificer and the sacred realm of the deity. Henri Hubert and [[marcel-mauss|Marcel Mauss]], in *Sacrifice: Its Nature and Function* (1899), defined sacrifice as "a means of establishing a means of communication between the sacred and profane worlds through the mediation of a victim — that is, of a thing that is destroyed in the course of the ceremony" (Hubert and [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] 1899:97).

## Origin and Development

Hubert and [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s *Essai sur la nature et la fonction du sacrifice* (1899) was a landmark in the sociological study of religion. Breaking with earlier theories that sought the origin of sacrifice in a single principle (Tylor's gift-theory, Smith's totemic communion, Frazer's sympathetic [[magic]]), they proposed a unitary structural schema underlying all sacrificial rituals across cultures: entry into the sacred → consecration of the victim → destruction/offering → exit from the sacred.

The work was deeply [[emile-durkheim|Durkheim]]ian in orientation: sacrifice was treated as a social fact, and the sacred/profane distinction — [[emile-durkheim|Durkheim]]'s core religious category — provided the analytical framework. The essay appeared two years after [[emile-durkheim|Durkheim]]'s *Suicide* and helped build the case for treating religion sociologically rather than theologically.

Georges Bataille later radicalised the sacrificial theme in *The Accursed Share* (1949), linking sacrifice to potlatch-like [[expenditure]] and the destruction of surplus.

## Key Elements

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Sacralization** | The victim is consecrated — moved from the profane to the sacred sphere |
| **Mediation** | The victim serves as intermediary between the sacrificer (profane) and the deity (sacred) |
| **Destruction** | The victim is destroyed (killed, burnt, consumed), completing the passage to the sacred |
| **Exit Rites** | The sacrificer undergoes purification to safely return to profane life |
| **Collective Function** | Sacrifice reinforces social bonds and collective representations |

## Theoretical Context

Hubert and [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s theory of sacrifice was the first systematic comparative analysis of the institution using the sociological method. They drew primarily on Vedic Indian and ancient Hebrew sources — a textual rather than ethnographic approach that reflected the limitations of armchair anthropology at the time.

Crucially, they argued that sacrifice and [[gift-economy|gift exchange]] share a common logic: both involve renunciation and circulation, both create bonds between parties (human and divine in sacrifice; human and human in gift), and both follow a tripartite structure of [[obligation]]. This connection between sacrifice and gift would later be developed by Bataille and by the MAUSS group (Mouvement Anti-Utilitariste dans les Sciences Sociales) in the 1980s.

## In the Text

The essay opens with a programmatic statement:

> "Sacrifice is a religious act which, through the consecration of a victim, modifies the state of the moral person who accomplishes it or of certain objects with which he is concerned." (Hubert and Mauss 1899:13)

And on the unity of sacrificial schema:

> "In every sacrifice, we find the same mechanism at work: the consecration of a thing which passes from the domain of the common into the religious domain; and this consecration radiates its effects beyond the consecrated thing, acting upon the person who provides the victim." (Hubert and Mauss 1899:96–97)
