## Biography

Clifford James Geertz (23 August 1926 – 30 October 2006) was an American anthropologist and the most influential figure in the "interpretive turn" that reshaped cultural anthropology in the 1970s and 1980s. Born in San Francisco and educated at Antioch College and Harvard University (where he studied under [[talcott-parsons]]), Geertz conducted his earliest fieldwork in Java (1952–1954) as part of a multi-investigator project at MIT's Center for International Studies. His Indonesian research produced *The Religion of Java* (1960), *Agricultural Involution* (1963), and *The Social History of an Indonesian Town* (1965). In 1970 he became the first professor of social science at the Institute for Advanced Study in Princeton, where he remained for the rest of his career. Geertz's essay collection *The Interpretation of Cultures* (1973), especially its opening chapter "Thick Description: Toward an Interpretive Theory of Culture," reoriented anthropology from a science of laws to an interpretive art of reading social texts. His later work on Bali, particularly *Negara: The Theatre State in Nineteenth-Century Bali* (1980), analyzed royal ceremonial as a form of political order in which ritual display of status constituted the state itself — a model directly relevant to the [[potlatch]] and [[kula-exchange|kula]] as systems in which exchange is simultaneously economic, political, and cosmological performance.

## Core Ideas

### Thick Description

Geertz borrowed the concept of "thick description" from philosopher [[gilbert-ryle]] to distinguish the interpretive task of anthropology from mere behavioral observation. A thin description records a physical movement — a boy's eyelid contracts. A thick description places that movement within webs of significance — the boy is winking, not twitching, and winks are part of a cultural code of conspiracy, mockery, or flirtation. Ethnography, for Geertz, is always thick description: the anthropologist reads a culture's public symbols — rituals, exchanges, jokes, ceremonies — over the shoulders of those for whom they have meaning (Geertz 1973: 6–10).

### Culture as Webs of Meaning

Geertz's most cited formulation holds that "man is an animal suspended in webs of significance he himself has spun." Culture is those webs, and its analysis is not an experimental science in search of law but an interpretive one in search of meaning. This semiotic concept of culture broke with both the British structural-functionalist view (culture as institutions maintaining social order) and the structuralist view (culture as a grammar of binary oppositions). Exchange systems like the [[kula-exchange|kula]] or the [[potlatch]] are, in Geertzian terms, public texts to be interpreted — not just economic mechanisms to be explained (Geertz 1973: 5).

### The Theatre State

In *Negara*, Geertz argued that the pre-colonial Balinese state was a "theatre state" in which ceremonial display was not a means to political ends but the ends themselves. Court rituals — cremations, temple festivals, royal progresses — dramatized the king's status as the earthly embodiment of cosmic order, and this dramatization was the very substance of political authority. This model has deep resonance with Maussian gift theory: the competitive destruction of property in the potlatch, or the elaborate ceremonial exchange of valuables in the kula, can be read as theatre states in miniature — performances in which status is constituted through the very act of giving and receiving (Geertz 1980: 13–17, 121–136).

## Influence and Legacy

Geertz's interpretive anthropology transformed the discipline's self-understanding. His insistence that ethnography is a literary practice — that anthropologists "write" rather than "observe" — reframed questions of objectivity and authority. The "writing culture" debate of the 1980s, led by James Clifford and George Marcus, took Geertz's textual approach to its reflexive conclusion. Within gift theory, Geertz provides the critical insight that exchange is a vehicle of meaning before it is a mechanism of distribution: the kula shell passes from hand to hand as a sign of alliance, not merely as an item of value. Geertz's theatre state concept also illuminates the political dimension of ceremonial exchange: why do chiefs destroy wealth in the potlatch? Not for economic gain but to enact a cosmic drama in which their prestige is momentarily performed and permanently inscribed. His interpretive method challenges the purely institutional analysis of the gift to attend to what the exchange "means" to those who participate in it.
