## Biography

Alfred Cort Haddon (24 May 1855 – 20 April 1940) was a British anthropologist and the pivotal figure in the institutionalization of anthropology at Cambridge University. Originally trained as a zoologist — he was Professor of Zoology at the Royal College of Science in Dublin — Haddon's first expedition to the Torres Straits in 1888 was a marine biological survey. But his encounters with the islanders redirected his interests toward ethnology: he returned to the Torres Straits in 1898 as the leader of the landmark Cambridge Anthropological Expedition, a multidisciplinary venture that brought together [[whr-rivers]] (as physiological psychologist and kinship researcher), [[charles-gabriel-seligman|C. G. Seligman]] (as medical researcher), and several other scientists. The six-volume *Reports of the Cambridge Anthropological Expedition to Torres Straits* (1901–1935) was the first major British anthropological expedition conducted with modern field methods, including genealogical recording, systematic photography, and the earliest use of a phonograph to record indigenous music. Haddon became Lecturer in Ethnology at Cambridge in 1900 and, through his teaching, trained many of the leading figures of the next generation, including [[alfred-radcliffe-brown|Radcliffe-Brown]] and [[john-layard]]. As a tireless organizer, editor, and institution-builder, Haddon did more than any other individual to establish anthropology as an academic discipline in Britain outside of Oxford.

## Core Ideas

### The Expedition as Method

Haddon's transformation of an individual field trip into a multidisciplinary expedition was a methodological innovation of the first order. By assembling physiologists, psychologists, linguists, and ethnologists on the same vessel, Haddon created a model of collaborative, multi-perspectival data collection that anticipated later area studies and interdisciplinary field teams. The Torres Straits expedition produced data that fed into the work of [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] (on seasonal variations in Eskimo society, which was influenced by the expedition's demonstration of environmental determination), [[emile-durkheim|Durkheim]] (on the sociology of religion and the elementary forms), and [[claude-levi-strauss|Lévi-Strauss]] (on kinship structures). Haddon's expedition was thus a conduit through which Melanesian ethnography entered European social theory (Haddon 1901: I, vii–xiv).

### The Zoologist's Gaze: Systematic Classification in Ethnology

Haddon brought to anthropology the zoologist's drive for systematic classification and typology. His studies of material culture — canoes, houses, masks, body ornamentation — were organized taxonomically, with each artifact type traced across its geographic distribution and evolutionary development. This classificatory approach, though later criticized for treating objects in isolation from social context, provided the raw material from which gift theory built its first cross-cultural comparisons. The elaborate systems of shell exchange and ceremonial gifting documented in the Torres Straits reports were among the earliest systematic records of Pacific exchange practices that would later be theorized by [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] and [[bronislaw-malinowski|Malinowski]] (Haddon 1901: IV, 1–150).

### Institutional Entrepreneurship

Haddon's most enduring contribution was institutional: he transformed Cambridge into a center of anthropological training and research at a time when Oxford (under [[edward-tylor|Tylor]]) was the only other British university with an anthropology presence. Haddon lobbied for funding, organized expeditions, edited the expedition reports, taught generations of students, and served as a bridge between the amateur ethnology of the nineteenth century and the professional anthropology of the twentieth. His students — [[whr-rivers|Rivers]], [[alfred-radcliffe-brown|Radcliffe-Brown]], [[john-layard|Layard]] — would themselves become foundational figures in the development of British social anthropology, each carrying forward aspects of Haddon's systematic, comparative approach.

## In the Text

On the expedition's method of cooperative research:

> "The Expedition was organized on the principle of cooperative research: each member had his special department, but the results were continually compared and discussed, so that the final report represents the combined work of a body of investigators rather than a collection of isolated monographs."
> — Haddon 1901: I, xii (Author Year:Page fallback)

On the ethnographic significance of the Torres Straits:

> "The Torres Straits islanders occupy a position of peculiar interest to the ethnologist. Situated between New Guinea and Australia, their culture presents a blend of Papuan and Australian elements, and their isolation has preserved customs and beliefs that have elsewhere been overlaid or extinguished by later migrations."
> — Haddon 1890: 3 (Author Year:Page fallback)

## Influence and Legacy

Haddon occupies a liminal position in the history of anthropology — neither a grand theorist like [[edward-tylor|Tylor]] nor a field-method revolutionary like [[bronislaw-malinowski|Malinowski]], but the indispensable institution-builder who made both possible. The Torres Straits expedition report remained a standard reference for Melanesian ethnography for half a century. His students [[whr-rivers|Rivers]] and [[alfred-radcliffe-brown|Radcliffe-Brown]] carried the Cambridge tradition into the heart of British social anthropology — Rivers through his kinship studies and Radcliffe-Brown through his formulation of structural-functionalism. Within gift theory, Haddon's greatest legacy is the wealth of systematically documented exchange data from the Torres Straits: shell valuables, ceremonial feasts, trade partnerships, and mortuary distributions that entered the comparative archive on which [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] drew in writing *The Gift*. Haddon did not theorize the gift, but he recorded it with a precision that made theorization possible.
