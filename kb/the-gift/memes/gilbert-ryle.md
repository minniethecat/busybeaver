## Biography

Gilbert Ryle (19 August 1900 – 6 October 1976) was a British philosopher who, as Waynflete Professor of Metaphysical Philosophy at Oxford (1945–1968) and editor of the journal *Mind* (1947–1971), shaped the direction of post-war Anglophone philosophy. Born in Brighton, England, to a medical family, Ryle read Classics and then Philosophy at The Queen's College, Oxford — the institution he would remain associated with for his entire career. He was a central figure in the "ordinary language philosophy" movement associated with Oxford in the 1940s and 1950s, alongside J.L. Austin and P.F. Strawson, which held that many traditional philosophical problems arise from "category mistakes" — misuses of language that treat concepts of one logical type as if they belonged to another. Ryle's principal target was the Cartesian mind-body dualism he called the "ghost in the machine," which he dismantled in *The Concept of Mind* (1949). Though Ryle never wrote on anthropology, his concept of "thick description" — a brief remark about the layered intentionality required to describe actions — was famously appropriated by [[clifford-geertz|Clifford Geertz]] as the methodological foundation of interpretive anthropology, making Ryle an unlikely but significant figure in anthropological theory.

## Core Ideas

### Category Mistakes

Ryle's central methodological innovation was the concept of the "category mistake": the error of treating a concept belonging to one logical type as if it belonged to another. The classic example is the visitor to Oxford who, having been shown the colleges, libraries, and administrative buildings, asks "But where is the University?" — failing to understand that "the University" is not another building alongside the others but the way all those buildings are organized. Descartes's mind-body dualism, Ryle argued, commits a category mistake of exactly this kind: it treats "the mind" as an additional entity alongside the body, operating in a parallel but invisible causal realm, when mental predicates are in fact ways of describing complex behavioral dispositions — what a person does, could do, and would do under various circumstances (Ryle 1949: 15–23).

### Knowing-How and Knowing-That

Ryle distinguished between "knowing-how" (practical competence, skill, the ability to perform) and "knowing-that" (propositional knowledge, the ability to state facts). He argued against the "intellectualist legend" that knowing-how is always reducible to knowing-that — that intelligent action is always the application of prior theoretical knowledge. A chef knows how to season a sauce without being able to articulate the rules; a chess player knows how to develop pieces without consulting a theory of chess. For anthropology, this distinction resonates with [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s concept of *techniques du corps* — embodied skills transmitted through practice and imitation rather than explicit instruction. The gift, as a *technique* of social relation, is something people know how to do before they can state the rules of reciprocity (Ryle 1949: 27–61).

### Thick Description

In a brief essay written late in his career, Ryle introduced the contrast between "thin" and "thick" description to illustrate the layered intentionality of human action. A thin description of a boy's eyelid contracting might read: "His eyelid contracted." But a thick description must distinguish between a twitch (involuntary), a wink (conspiratorial signal to an accomplice), a parody of a wink (performed for an audience), and a rehearsal of a parody (practiced alone). Each layer of description adds a new level of intentionality — the wink is about something, the parody is about the wink, the rehearsal is about the parody — and failure to specify the correct level is a category mistake. [[clifford-geertz|Geertz]] appropriated this passage to argue that ethnography is "thick description": the task of the anthropologist is not to record behaviors but to specify the structures of signification — what actions mean to those who perform them — which requires attending to the multiple nested layers of intentionality that distinguish a twitch from a wink (Ryle 1968: 5–7).

## In the Text

On the distinction between thin and thick description:

> "A single contraction of the eyelids may be an involuntary twitch, a conspiratorial signal to an accomplice, a parody of the conspiratorial wink, or the rehearsal of the parody by a young actor before his mirror. The thin description 'his right eyelid contracted' applies to all four. The thick description 'he winked conspiratorially' applies only to the second. The difference between the twitch and the wink is vast, but it is not a difference in the observable muscular events. It is a difference in the meaning of the action."

## Influence and Legacy

Ryle's direct philosophical influence has waned as ordinary-language philosophy gave way to post-positivist and naturalist approaches in Anglophone philosophy. His indirect influence, however, is immense: his demolition of Cartesian dualism helped clear the ground for the cognitive science revolution that began in the 1960s, and his knowing-how / knowing-that distinction has been revived in contemporary epistemology and philosophy of mind (Jason Stanley, Stephen Stich). For anthropology, Ryle's most important legacy is via [[clifford-geertz|Geertz]]'s appropriation of "thick description" as the master metaphor for interpretive ethnography. Geertz's essay "Thick Description: Toward an Interpretive Theory of Culture" (1973) explicitly credits Ryle's wink-twitch distinction as the methodological foundation for reading culture as a "text" — a stratified hierarchy of meaningful structures. This Ryle-Geertz lineage makes ordinary-language philosophy an unlikely ancestor of the "interpretive turn" that reshaped anthropology in the 1970s and 1980s, influencing [[james-clifford|James Clifford]]'s reflexive critique of ethnographic writing and the broader "Writing Culture" movement.
