## Biography

Nancy Munn (1931–2020) was an American cultural anthropologist and professor at the University of Chicago. She conducted fieldwork on Gawa Island in the Massim region of Papua New Guinea, the same cultural area where [[bronislaw-malinowski]] had documented the [[kula-exchange]] half a century earlier. Her monograph *The Fame of Gawa* (1986) is widely regarded as one of the most important theoretical contributions to the anthropology of value, extending and transforming the Maussian analysis of gift exchange.

## Core Ideas

### Value as Transformative Action

Munn's central contribution to gift theory is the argument that value is not a static property of objects but an ongoing process of *transformation*. On Gawa, value is created when actors convert one form of exchange into another — for example, turning garden produce into hospitality, hospitality into [[kula-exchange]] shells, and shells into fame (which travels across islands). Each transformation extends the actor's spatiotemporal influence, and it is this extension that constitutes value (Munn 1986:3–11).

### Spatiotemporal Extension

For Munn, the value of an action or object is measured by its capacity to extend the actor's influence across space and time. A Gawan who gives generously to visitors creates an obligation that reaches across the sea and persists into the future; this extension is what Gawan people call *fame*. Fame, not material accumulation, is the highest form of value in this system — a reworking of [[marcel-mauss]]'s insight that the gift creates persons (Munn 1986:105–111).

### Negative Value and Witchcraft

Munn also theorizes negative value: actions that contract rather than extend the actor. Witchcraft beliefs on Gawa represent the contraction of social relations through hidden, antisocial consumption. Gossip about witchcraft is the community's way of monitoring and sanctioning those who refuse to participate in the positive cycle of giving (Munn 1986:215–225).

## In the Text

On the creation of value through hospitality:

> "By feeding visitors and thus controlling consumption, actors can transform a merely physiological event into the intersubjective production of a more extensive spacetime of social relations. The act of giving food is an act of creating value — not because food is valuable, but because giving converts a private act into a public relation."
> (Munn 1986:49–50)

On fame and the self:

> "Fame is the name that travels. It is the self-in-the-other, the trace of the actor's positive influence that extends across the sea and persists in memory. A Gawan with fame has a self that is larger than a body, spread across the paths of Kula."
> (Munn 1986:105)

## Influence and Legacy

Munn's *The Fame of Gawa* transformed the anthropology of value by shifting analysis from the *objects* exchanged to the *processes* by which exchange generates value. Her framework has been taken up by anthropologists working on [[gift-economy]], ritual exchange, and material culture. The concept of "spatiotemporal extension" — value as the widening of the actor's social reach — has been applied beyond Melanesia to studies of branding, reputation in digital networks, and celebrity culture. Munn's analysis of the relationship between hospitality, gift exchange, and witchcraft on Gawa enriches the understanding of [[kula-exchange]] beyond Malinowski's classic account.
