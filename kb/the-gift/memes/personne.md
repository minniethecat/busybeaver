## Definition

**Personne** (French: person) is a concept developed by [[marcel-mauss|Marcel Mauss]] in his 1938 Huxley Memorial Lecture "A Category of the Human Mind: The Notion of Person, the Notion of Self" (*Une catégorie de l'esprit humain: la notion de personne, celle de "moi"*). [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] argued that the idea of the individual self as a unified, autonomous, morally responsible agent is not a natural human universal but a historically specific social category that evolved from tribal role-masks through Roman legal personality to Christian moral personhood and finally modern individualism ([[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] 1938:1–2).

## Origin and Development

[[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] delivered the lecture in London in 1938, near the end of his active academic career. The essay traces the evolution of the concept of the person through several civilizational stages:

1. **Pueblo and [[pacific-northwest|Northwest Coast]]**: The person as a ritual role, identified with a clan mask and a name that is reincarnated across generations
2. **Rome**: The *persona* as a legal category — originally a mask worn by actors, then a juridical status
3. **Christianity**: The person as a moral entity with a soul, capable of sin and salvation
4. **Modernity**: The person as an individual consciousness (*moi*), the psychological self of Descartes and Kant

The lecture is notable for its historical sweep, its comparative method, and its argument that even the most intimate sense of selfhood is a social institution.

## Theoretical Context

The essay extends the [[emile-durkheim|Durkheim]]ian programme of demonstrating the social origin of fundamental categories to the most intimate of all: the self. Just as *[[classification|Primitive Classification]]* (1903) had argued that logical categories are modelled on social organization, this lecture argued that the category of the person is itself a social institution with a traceable history.

[[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s argument has been influential in anthropology (Louis Dumont's *Homo Hierarchicus*, 1966; Clifford Geertz's "From the Native's Point of View," 1974), philosophy (Charles Taylor's *Sources of the Self*, 1989), and sociology (Anthony Giddens's *Modernity and Self-Identity*, 1991). Michel Foucault's later work on "technologies of the self" (1982) engages similar questions from a different methodological angle.

## In the Text

[[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] states his thesis at the outset:

> "My subject is a category of the human mind: the notion of person, the notion of self. ... It is plain, particularly to us, that there has never existed a human being who has not been aware, not only of his body, but also at the same time of his individuality, both spiritual and physical. ... But I must tell you at once that I do not think that this notion has existed since all eternity ... It is slow work, requiring almost a century to elaborate even a few concepts." (Mauss 1938:1–3)

On the Roman contribution:

> "It is the Romans who made of the word *persona* the sense in which it has come down to us. ... The *persona* becomes synonymous with the true nature of the individual. ... But this sense is still far from the notion of the self as a sacred and psychological entity." (Mauss 1938:14–15)
