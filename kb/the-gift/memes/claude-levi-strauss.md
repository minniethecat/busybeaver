## Biography

**Claude Lévi-Strauss** (28 November 1908 – 30 October 2009) was a French anthropologist and ethnologist, widely regarded as the father of [[structuralism|structural anthropology]]. Born in Brussels to French parents, he studied law and philosophy at the University of Paris before turning to ethnology. He was among the early students trained at the [[institut-ethnologie]], where he came under the influence of [[marcel-mauss]]'s work — an influence he later described as a "revelation."

Lévi-Strauss conducted his seminal fieldwork among the Nambikwara, Bororo, and other indigenous peoples of Brazil between 1935 and 1939. Forced to flee Vichy France during World War II, he spent the war years in New York, where his encounters with American anthropology and structural linguistics (particularly Roman Jakobson) shaped the theoretical framework he would later systematize.

## Core Ideas

| Concept | Work | Summary |
|---------|------|---------|
| Elementary structures of kinship | *The Elementary Structures of Kinship* (1949) | Kinship systems are systems of [[reciprocity|reciprocal exchange]] — men exchange women between groups |
| Structural anthropology | *Structural Anthropology* (1958) | Cultural phenomena can be analyzed as systems of binary oppositions, modeled on structural linguistics |
| The raw and the cooked | *The Raw and the Cooked* (1964) | Mythological systems transform nature into culture through a "culinary triangle" (raw/cooked/rotten) |
| The savage mind | *The Savage Mind* (1962) | "Primitive" thought is not inferior to scientific thought but operates through a different logic — *bricolage* |
| Incest taboo as exchange | *Elementary Structures* (1949) | The prohibition of incest is the positive rule that compels exogamy — it is the founding act of culture |

## Influence of Mauss

Lévi-Strauss considered [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s *The Gift* a foundational text but also a deeply frustrating one. In his introduction to the 1950 collection *Sociologie et Anthropologie* (which gathered [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s major essays), Lévi-Strauss praised [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] for revealing that exchange — not production — is the primary social fact. But he criticized [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] for stopping short of structural analysis — for taking the Māori informant's explanation of the [[hau]] at face value rather than identifying the underlying structure.

> Mauss stopped at the threshold of the structural revolution. He provided the data for a structural analysis, but he did not carry it out himself.
>
> （Lévi-Strauss 1950, Introduction to the Work of Marcel Mauss）

For Lévi-Strauss, reciprocity was not a moral [[obligation]] enforced by the [[hau|spirit of the gift]] but a formal property of exchange systems — a structural principle that could be modeled mathematically. This re-reading transformed [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s ethnographically rich but theoretically ambiguous concept into a cornerstone of structuralist social theory.

## In the Text

Lévi-Strauss does not appear in *The Gift* itself (he was a teenager when it was published), but his structuralist interpretation of reciprocity has shaped all subsequent readings. His own research on kinship — demonstrating that marriage rules across societies encode a universal logic of [[reciprocity|reciprocal exchange]] between groups — can be read as an extension of [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s insight that exchange is the fundamental social act.
