## Biography

**Lucien Lévy-Bruhl** (10 April 1857 – 13 March 1939) was a French philosopher and anthropologist, best known for his studies of "primitive mentality" and his theory of pre-logical thought. A professor at the Sorbonne from 1899, he was a close colleague of [[emile-durkheim]] and [[marcel-mauss]] and an active participant in the [[annee-sociologique]] circle. In 1917 he was instrumental in establishing the [[institut-ethnologie]] at the University of Paris alongside [[marcel-mauss]] and [[paul-rivet]].

Lévy-Bruhl published a series of influential works on the nature of collective representations in non-Western societies, arguing that "primitive" thought was governed by a logic of participation rather than the law of contradiction. His later writings increasingly acknowledged the coexistence of both modes of thought in all human societies.

## Key Works

| Work | Year | Significance |
|------|------|-------------|
| *Les fonctions mentales dans les sociétés inférieures* | 1910 | First systematic exposition of the pre-logical mentality thesis |
| *La mentalité primitive* | 1922 | Developed the concept of participation mystique and collective representations |
| *L'âme primitive* | 1927 | Study of the conception of the soul in non-Western societies |
| *Le surnaturel et la nature dans la mentalité primitive* | 1931 | Analysis of the perception of causality and the supernatural |
| *Les carnets* (posthumous notebooks) | 1949 | Revealed his late-career revisions and self-criticism of the primitive mentality concept |

## Core Ideas

| Concept | Explanation |
|---------|-------------|
| **Pre-logical Mentality** | "Primitive" thought is indifferent to the law of contradiction; it accepts mystical participation between beings and objects as natural |
| **Law of Participation** | In collective representations, things can be simultaneously themselves and something else — a sacred object is both wood and a spirit |
| **Mystical Experience** | "Primitive" perception is oriented toward invisible forces rather than natural causality; every event has a supernatural dimension |
| **Collective Representations** | Mental categories are socially determined, not individually constructed — a position shared with Durkheim's sociology of knowledge |
| **Late Self-Revision** | In his posthumous *Carnets*, Lévy-Bruhl abandoned the rigid distinction between "primitive" and "civilized" mentality |

## In the Text

Lévy-Bruhl articulates his central thesis on the law of participation in *La mentalité primitive*:

> "For the primitive mentality, being is perceived in a way that is undifferentiated. The object is not separated from the mystical forces and properties that emanate from it, and these forces and properties are felt as immediately present in the representation itself." (Lévy-Bruhl 1922:91–92)

On the collective nature of representations, which Mauss built upon in his study of [[collective-representation]]:

> "Collective representations are social facts in their own right. They impose themselves on the individual with an irresistible force and precede the individual's own mental experience." (Lévy-Bruhl 1910:7–8)

## Influence and Legacy

Lévy-Bruhl's work profoundly influenced the Durkheimian sociology of knowledge, providing the ethnographic material upon which [[emile-durkheim]] and [[marcel-mauss]] built their theory of [[classification]] and [[collective-representation]] in *Primitive Classification* (1903). His concept of participation mystique was taken up by [[claude-levi-strauss]] in his structuralist reworking of totemism, and his late self-criticism in the *Carnets* — acknowledging that participation and logical thought coexist in all societies — anticipated later phenomenological anthropology.

Critics including [[bronislaw-malinowski]] and E.E. Evans-Pritchard rejected the "pre-logical" label as ethnocentric, but recognized Lévy-Bruhl's insistence that non-Western thought systems possess their own internal coherence as a crucial advance over Victorian evolutionism. His archive of ethnographic data, drawn from missionary and colonial reports, remains a valuable (if problematic) source for the study of collective representations in the early twentieth-century French school.
