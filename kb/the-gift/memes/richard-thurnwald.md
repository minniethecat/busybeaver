## Biography

Richard Thurnwald (1869–1954) was a German ethnologist and sociologist, trained in law before turning to anthropology under the influence of Carl F. von den Steinen. He conducted extensive fieldwork in the Bismarck Archipelago, the Solomon Islands, and New Guinea between 1906 and 1915. His legal training shaped his focus on the normative frameworks underlying exchange systems. Thurnwald held professorships at the University of Berlin and later at the Freie Universität Berlin, and founded the journal *Sociologus*.

## Core Ideas

- **Reciprocity (Gegenseitigkeit)** — Thurnwald was the first to propose reciprocity as a foundational principle of social organization, preceding both Malinowski's and Mauss's formulations. He identified reciprocity not as mere exchange but as a moral obligation structuring kin and inter-group relations.

- **Normative Exchange** — Drawing on his legal background, Thurnwald analyzed exchange as governed by implicit social norms and sanctions. He distinguished between symmetrical reciprocity (between equals) and asymmetrical reciprocity (between hierarchical groups), a distinction Mauss developed further.

- **Functionalist Social Systems** — He viewed societies as integrated functional systems where economic, legal, and kinship institutions are mutually reinforcing, anticipating key themes of British social anthropology.

## In the Text

Thurnwald appears in Mauss's *The Gift* as a key ethnographic source for Melanesian exchange systems. Mauss cites Thurnwald's Bánaro data on reciprocal gift-giving obligations between kin groups:

> "Thurnwald has given us a good description of the system of reciprocal gifts among the Bánaro, where each gift obligates a return of equivalent value, not immediately but within a socially prescribed interval."
> — Mauss 1925: 30–31 (Author Year:Page fallback)

Mauss draws on Thurnwald's concept of reciprocity to argue that gift exchange is a total social phenomenon binding economic, legal, and moral obligations into a single system.

## Influence and Legacy

Thurnwald's concept of reciprocity directly influenced Mauss's *The Gift* and, through Mauss, the entire French sociological tradition (Bataille, Caillois, Lévi-Strauss). Karl Polanyi drew on Thurnwald's economic anthropology in developing the substantivist school. His functionalist approach anticipated aspects of Malinowski's and Radcliffe-Brown's frameworks. The journal *Sociologus*, which he founded in 1951, remains a respected venue for comparative social research.
