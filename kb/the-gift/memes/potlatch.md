## Definition

The **potlatch** (from the Chinook Jargon *patshatl*, "to give") is a ceremonial institution practiced by the indigenous peoples of the [[pacific-northwest]] Coast — including the Kwakwaka'wakw (Kwakiutl), Haida, Tlingit, Tsimshian, and Coast Salish. It is a feast at which hosts distribute gifts and sometimes destroy property to validate or enhance their social rank.

As analyzed by [[marcel-mauss]] in *The Gift*, the potlatch represents an extreme, agonistic form of [[total-prestation]], where the [[obligation]] to reciprocate may involve returning with interest — or even destroying wealth to outdo a rival.

## Historical Context

Potlatch ceremonies were documented by anthropologist Franz Boas in the late 19th century among the Kwakwaka'wakw of British Columbia. The Canadian government banned the potlatch from 1884 to 1951 under the Indian Act, considering it wasteful and antithetical to Christian values. The ban was lifted after sustained indigenous resistance and legal challenges.

## Key Features

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Wealth distribution | Hosts give away blankets, coppers, canoes, food, and other valuables |
| Competitive giving | Rivals attempt to out-give each other, establishing hierarchy through generosity |
| Property destruction | At extreme potlatches, copper shields and other valuables are broken or thrown into the sea |
| Status validation | Potlatch legitimates inheritance, marriage, naming, and other life-cycle events |
| Witnessing | Guests serve as witnesses to the host's claims of rank and title |

## In the Text

[[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] describes the agonistic logic of the potlatch:

> The obligation to reciprocate worthily is imperative. One loses face forever if one does not reciprocate, or if one does not carry out destruction of equivalent value.
>
> （Mauss 1966:41）

## Theoretical Significance

The potlatch is a key case in economic anthropology because it challenges the assumption that economic behavior is universally aimed at maximizing material utility. Here, wealth is accumulated in order to be given away — **generosity, not accumulation, confers status**.

[[marcel-mauss]] saw the potlatch as a form of [[total-prestation]] in its most extreme, competitive mode. The institution also influenced [[georges-bataille]]'s concept of [[expenditure]] (*dépense*) and the theory of the [[gift-economy]].
