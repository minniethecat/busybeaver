## Definition

**Structuralism** in anthropology is a theoretical and methodological approach, most closely associated with [[claude-levi-strauss]], that seeks to uncover the unconscious, universal structures of the human mind that underlie the diversity of cultural expressions. Drawing on structural linguistics (Ferdinand de Saussure, Roman Jakobson), structural anthropology analyzes myths, kinship systems, [[classification]], and exchange not as collections of individual elements but as systems of relations governed by formal rules — above all, the rule of reciprocity.

In the context of [[gift-economy|gift exchange]] theory, structuralism provided the most influential reinterpretation of [[marcel-mauss]]'s *The Gift*. [[claude-levi-strauss|Lévi-Strauss]] argued that [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] had been too empiricist — that the Māori [[hau]] was not the *cause* of reciprocity but its local, symbolic expression. The real cause, for [[claude-levi-strauss|Lévi-Strauss]], was the unconscious structural principle that exchange — of women, goods, and words — is the fundamental form of social life.

## Origin and Development

Structuralism emerged from [[claude-levi-strauss|Lévi-Strauss]]'s encounter with structural linguistics during his wartime exile in New York (1941–1945), where he studied with Roman Jakobson. In *The Elementary Structures of Kinship* (1949), [[claude-levi-strauss|Lévi-Strauss]] applied the linguistic model to kinship, arguing that the incest taboo is a structural rule — the negative face of a positive principle: the [[obligation]] to exchange women between groups.

[[claude-levi-strauss|Lévi-Strauss]]'s 1950 essay "Introduction to the Work of [[marcel-mauss|Marcel Mauss]]" — originally the preface to a collection of [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s essays — became the founding document of structuralist gift theory. In it, [[claude-levi-strauss|Lévi-Strauss]] both celebrated [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] as the "father of French ethnology" and critiqued him for failing to grasp the full structural implications of his own discoveries:

> Mauss allowed himself to be mystified by an indigenous theory. The hau is not the reason for exchange; it is the form in which a particular society represents an unconscious structural necessity.
>
> （Lévi-Strauss 1950, "Introduction à l'œuvre de Marcel Mauss"）

## Key Elements

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Unconscious structures | Cultural phenomena are surface expressions of deep, universal mental structures |
| Binary opposition | The mind organizes experience through pairs of opposites (raw/cooked, nature/culture) |
| Reciprocity as fundamental | Exchange — of women, goods, signs — is the elementary form of social life |
| The symbolic order | Social life is organized through systems of symbols, not through individual intentions |
| Synchronic analysis | Focus on relations within a system rather than historical development |

## The Structuralist Critique of Mauss

[[claude-levi-strauss|Lévi-Strauss]]'s 1950 essay made three arguments against [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s interpretation of the gift:

1. **The "indigenous theory" fallacy**: [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] took his Māori informants' explanation (the [[hau]]) as the scientific explanation, rather than treating it as one cultural representation among others of a deeper structural reality.

2. **Exchange before the gift**: [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] began with the gift and analyzed it as total prestation. [[claude-levi-strauss|Lévi-Strauss]] argued that exchange itself — of which the gift is one form — is the primary social fact. Marriage rules (incest prohibition as the [[obligation]] to give women to other groups) are logically prior to [[gift-economy|gift exchange]].

3. **The unconscious as cause**: The three [[obligation]]s [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] identified (to give, to receive, to reciprocate) are not conscious norms but unconscious structural imperatives. Reciprocity operates beneath awareness.

Later anthropological theorists, including [[pierre-bourdieu]] and Marshall Sahlins, responded that [[claude-levi-strauss|Lévi-Strauss]] had overcorrected — that the temporal dimension of the gift (the delay between giving and returning) is irreducible to a structural formula.

## In the Text

Structuralism reshaped how *The Gift* is read. Before [[claude-levi-strauss|Lévi-Strauss]], the text was treated as ethnography: a description of archaic exchange. After [[claude-levi-strauss|Lévi-Strauss]], it became a theoretical treatise on the foundations of social life. The question shifted from "What did [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] discover about Polynesian [[gift-economy|gift exchange]]?" to "What does [[gift-economy|gift exchange]] reveal about the elementary structures of human sociality?"

This structuralist reading influenced [[karl-polanyi]]'s substantivist economics, [[pierre-bourdieu]]'s theory of practice, and Jacques Derrida's deconstructive reading of the gift as an impossibility (a "pure" gift would annihilate itself as gift).
