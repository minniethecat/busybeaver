## Biography

Marilyn Strathern (born 1941) is a British anthropologist and former William Wyse Professor of Social Anthropology at the University of Cambridge. She conducted extensive fieldwork in Papua New Guinea, particularly in the Mount Hagen area, and later became a leading theorist of feminist anthropology and the anthropology of kinship, property, and exchange.

## Core Ideas

### The Gender of the Gift

Strathern's central contribution is the argument that [[marcel-mauss]]'s *Essai sur le don* implicitly relies on a Western conception of the person as a bounded individual, a model that does not map onto Melanesian personhood. In Melanesia, persons are "dividual" — composed of the relations that constitute them — and objects given are not alienated from the giver but carry part of the giver's person.

### Dividual Personhood

The "dividual" (Strathern 1988:13) contrasts with the Western "individual": Melanesian persons are constituted through the multiple social relations they embody. A gift is therefore not a transfer between separate individuals but a revelation of the relational composition of both parties.

### Gender and Exchange

Strathern demonstrates that gift exchange in Melanesia is gendered in ways that classic [[gift-economy]] theory ignored. Male and female are not fixed biological categories but positions within exchange relations — women's gifts and men's gifts operate in different registers of social reproduction.

## In the Text

Strathern's work reframes the Maussian [[total-prestation]] by showing that what Mauss took as a universal form of exchange is culturally specific:

> "The Western view that an object is a thing, and that a person is a subject, cannot be taken for granted. In Melanesia, persons are frequently constructed as the plural and composite site of the relationships that produced them. Objects, likewise, are not mere things but are revealed as persons in another form."
> (Strathern 1988:13–14)

On the relationship between gift and commodity:

> "It matters not whether we call a transaction a gift or a commodity exchange. What matters is what the transactors construe themselves to be doing, and what kinds of persons they make of one another in the process."
> (Strathern 1988:145)

## Influence and Legacy

Strathern's *The Gender of the Gift* reshaped economic anthropology by challenging the gift/commodity binary that had structured the field since [[karl-polanyi]] and [[chris-gregory]]. Her work influenced [[david-graeber]]'s rethinking of value and debt, and opened Melanesian ethnography to feminist analysis. The concept of the "dividual" has been taken up across anthropology, science and technology studies, and legal theory.
