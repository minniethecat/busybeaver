## Definition

**Embeddedness** is the principle, articulated by economic historian [[karl-polanyi]] in *The Great Transformation* (1944), that in non-market societies the economy is not an autonomous sphere governed by supply and demand but is submerged in social relations. Economic activities — production, exchange, distribution — are organized through kinship, religion, politics, and gift [[obligation]]s rather than through price-setting markets.

The concept directly complements [[marcel-mauss]]'s analysis of the [[gift-economy]]. Where [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] demonstrated that pre-modern exchange is a "total social phenomenon" (*[[total-prestation|fait social total]]*), Polanyi provided the theoretical vocabulary: the economy is *embedded* in society rather than disembedded as a separate, self-regulating market.

## Origin and Development

Polanyi developed the concept of embeddedness in *The Great Transformation* (1944) as part of his critique of classical political economy. Against Adam Smith's assumption of a natural "propensity to truck, [[barter]], and exchange," Polanyi argued that market economies are a historical anomaly. For most of human history, the economy was embedded in social institutions:

> The outstanding discovery of recent historical and anthropological research is that man's economy, as a rule, is submerged in his social relationships. He does not act so as to safeguard his individual interest in the possession of material goods; he acts so as to safeguard his social standing, his social claims, his social assets.
>
> （Polanyi 1944:46）

The concept was later refined by economic sociologist Mark Granovetter (1985), who argued that even modern market economies remain partially embedded in social networks — a position he called "weak embeddedness" as opposed to Polanyi's "strong embeddedness" thesis.

## Key Elements

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Social integration | The economy is organized through non-economic institutions: kinship, religion, politics |
| Three modes of integration | Polanyi identified reciprocity, redistribution, and market exchange as the three modes |
| Reciprocity | Exchange between symmetrical groups (e.g., Kula partners, kinship groups) |
| Redistribution | Goods flow to a center and are reallocated (e.g., chiefly tribute systems) |
| Market exchange | Price-making markets — dominant only in the modern era |

## Relationship to Mauss

Polanyi's framework systematizes what [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] demonstrated ethnographically. [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] showed that [[kula-exchange]] and [[potlatch]] were not "primitive [[barter]]" but systems in which material exchange expressed and reproduced social relationships. Polanyi's three modes of integration — reciprocity, redistribution, and market exchange — directly map onto [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s examples:

| Polanyi Mode | [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]ian Example |
|-------------|-----------------|
| Reciprocity | [[kula-exchange]], balanced [[gift-economy|gift exchange]] |
| Redistribution | [[potlatch]] (goods collected by chief, redistributed) |
| Market exchange | Modern commodity economy (contrasted with gift) |

The concept of embeddedness also illuminates [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s political project. *The Gift* ends with a call for modern societies to recover the morality of [[gift-economy|gift exchange]] — in Polanyi's terms, a call to *re-embed* the economy in social relations.

## In the Text

Polanyi does not appear in *The Gift* (published 1925, predating *The Great Transformation* by nineteen years), but [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s argument anticipates Polanyi's. [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s insistence that the gift is never "free" but always obligatory, that exchange creates and perpetuates social bonds, and that pre-modern peoples were not "economic animals" are all restatements of what Polanyi would later formalize as embeddedness.

> It is only our Western societies that quite recently turned man into an "economic animal." Homo economicus is not behind us, but lies ahead.
>
> （Mauss 1966:74）
