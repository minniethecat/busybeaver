## Definition

In economic anthropology, **reciprocity** refers to the moral principle of mutual [[obligation]] that structures [[gift-economy|gift exchange]] in non-market societies. It is the norm that a gift must be repaid — not necessarily immediately or in equivalent form, but in a manner that sustains the social relationship between giver and receiver. [[marcel-mauss]], in *The Gift* (1925), identified reciprocity as one of three interlocking [[obligation]]s: the [[obligation]] to **give**, the [[obligation]] to **receive**, and the [[obligation]] to **reciprocate**.

Unlike market exchange, where the transaction extinguishes the relationship between buyer and seller, reciprocal exchange *creates and perpetuates* social bonds. The delay between receipt and return — characteristic of [[kula-exchange]] and the [[potlatch]] — transforms a material transaction into a durable social tie.

## The Three Obligations

| [[obligation|Obligation]] | Description | Example |
|------------|-------------|---------|
| To give | The social requirement to initiate exchange; refusing to give signals hostility or inferiority | A Trobriand chief initiating a *Kula* voyage |
| To receive | Acceptance is mandatory; refusal to receive is a declaration of war | Refusing a [[potlatch]] gift signals enmity in [[pacific-northwest|Northwest Coast]] societies |
| To reciprocate | The return gift, often with interest; failure to reciprocate results in loss of status | *Urigubu* in the [[trobriand-islands|Trobriands]] — a man must provide yams to his sister's household |

## Origin and Development

[[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] derived the principle of reciprocity from comparative ethnographic analysis. He observed that across the [[trobriand-islands]], the [[pacific-northwest]], and ancient Indo-European legal systems, seemingly voluntary gift-giving followed a universal grammar of [[obligation]].

The concept was later systematized by [[karl-polanyi]], who distinguished reciprocity (exchange between social equals within symmetrical groups) from redistribution (centralized collection and reallocation) and market exchange. [[claude-levi-strauss]] further elevated reciprocity to a structural principle, arguing that the incest taboo is fundamentally a rule of reciprocity — men exchange women between groups, creating the elementary structures of kinship.

## In the Text

[[marcel-mauss|Mauss]] opens *The Gift* by observing the paradoxical character of [[gift-economy|gift exchange]]:

> In Scandinavian and many other civilizations contracts are fulfilled and exchanges of goods are made by means of gifts. In theory such gifts are voluntary but in fact they are given and repaid under obligation.
>
> （Mauss 1966:1）

The [[hau]] — the Māori concept of the "[[hau|spirit of the gift]]" — provides [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]'s most famous argument for why reciprocity is obligatory. The *[[hau]]* of the giver resides in the gift-object and compels its return to the original giver. Māori informant Tamati Ranaipiri's explanation, quoted by [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]], became one of the most debated passages in anthropological theory.

## Theoretical Context

Reciprocity is the animating principle of [[total-prestation]] — the system in which entire clans, tribes, and families exchange not just goods but courtesies, entertainments, ritual, military assistance, women, children, dances, and feasts. In the [[gift-economy]], reciprocity replaces the price mechanism: value is not measured in currency but in the strength of the social relationships created and maintained through exchange.

> The Kula is a grand system of total services, which is carried out in a noble way, apparently in a pure spirit of generosity and disinterestedness. But it is based on the strictest rules of equivalence and obligatory reciprocity.
>
> （Mauss 1966, on the Kula ring）
