## Biography

Igor Kopytoff (1930–2013) was a Russian-born American anthropologist and professor at the University of Pennsylvania. Trained in the Africanist tradition, he conducted fieldwork among the Suku of the Democratic Republic of Congo. Kopytoff is best known for his seminal essay "The Cultural Biography of Things: Commoditization as Process," published in [[arjun-appadurai|Arjun Appadurai]]'s edited volume *The Social Life of Things* (1986), which became one of the most cited works in material culture studies and economic anthropology. His essay bridged [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]ian gift theory with the study of commodities, arguing that the gift/commodity distinction is not a binary but a continuum that every object traverses over its lifetime.

## Core Ideas

### Commoditization as Process, Not Essence

Kopytoff's most influential argument is that "commodity" is not an inherent property of an object but a phase in its social life. Any thing can be commoditized (treated as exchangeable for other things of equivalent value) or singularized (treated as unique and non-exchangeable) depending on the social context. A wedding ring, a piece of land, or even a human being can move between commodity and singularized states. This processual view dissolves the sharp dichotomy between [[marcel-mauss|Mauss]]ian gifts and market commodities, showing that the same object can be both at different moments (Kopytoff 1986:64–73).

### The Cultural Biography

Kopytoff proposed that objects, like persons, have "cultural biographies" — life histories that reveal the society's underlying classifications, values, and contradictions. By tracing an object's biography from production through exchange to consumption and disposal, anthropologists can map the cultural categories that organize a society. This method has been widely adopted across disciplines, from archaeology to design studies, and has shaped how scholars think about [[annette-weiner|inalienable possessions]] and heirloom objects that resist commoditization.

### Singularization and the Sack of Commodities

Every society maintains a boundary between the commoditized and the singularized, but this boundary is constantly contested and renegotiated. Slavery, for Kopytoff, represents the extreme case: the commoditization of what every society most insists on singularizing — the human person. His analysis of slavery as "the process of commoditizing a human being, and then singularizing them again" provides a powerful framework for understanding the limits of exchangeability (Kopytoff 1986:84–89).

## In the Text

On the gift/commodity distinction as a process:

> "Commoditization is best looked upon as a process of becoming rather than as an all-or-none state of being. Its expansion takes place in a culture's management of the contradictory pulls of commoditization and singularization. ... There is a perennial moral struggle in every society between the two opposing tendencies. In a complex society, the same thing may be treated as a commodity at one time and not at another — and as a commodity by one person and not by another."
> (Kopytoff 1986:73)

On singularizing slaves:

> "When a slave is acquired, he or she is singularized — given a new social identity, incorporated into a kinship group, rehumanized. The process of commoditization that made the person a slave in the first place is systematically reversed. In this sense, the slave's biography recapitulates the moral drama of commoditization and singularization that all objects undergo."
> (Kopytoff 1986:87)

## Influence and Legacy

Kopytoff's cultural biography approach has been enormously influential well beyond anthropology. Historians, archaeologists, and material culture scholars have adopted his framework to trace how objects accumulate meaning over time. The essay directly shaped subsequent work in the anthropology of value, including [[david-graeber|David Graeber]]'s *Toward an Anthropological Theory of Value* (2001), which builds on Kopytoff's dissolution of the gift/commodity binary. [[arjun-appadurai]]'s introduction to *The Social Life of Things* explicitly credits Kopytoff for the volume's central methodological innovation: that things, like people, have social lives that can be studied ethnographically.
