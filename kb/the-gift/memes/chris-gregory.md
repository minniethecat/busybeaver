## Biography

Chris Gregory (Christopher A. Gregory) is a British economic anthropologist based at the Australian National University and later the University of Manchester. He conducted fieldwork in Papua New Guinea and India, focusing on the political economy of agrarian change and the comparative analysis of exchange systems. His 1982 book *Gifts and Commodities* established the core theoretical framework for distinguishing gift economies from commodity economies.

## Core Ideas

### The Gift/Commodity Distinction

Building on [[karl-polanyi]]'s substantive economics and [[marcel-mauss]]'s gift theory, Gregory formalized the distinction: gift exchange establishes qualitative relationships between persons, while commodity exchange establishes quantitative relationships between things (Gregory 1982:41). Gifts are inalienable from the giver; commodities are alienable.

### Consumption and Production

Gregory argued that in gift economies, "consumption" is a form of social reproduction — the "consumption" of gifts reproduces kinship and social relations — whereas in capitalist economies, consumption reproduces labor power. This linked Maussian [[gift-economy]] theory to Marxist political economy.

### The Indian Gift

Gregory's later work on India (Gregory 1997) explored how the gift/commodity distinction plays out in contexts where gift and commodity forms coexist and hybridize, complicating the clean analytical binary of his earlier work.

## In the Text

Gregory's formulation of the gift/commodity distinction has been one of the most cited passages in economic anthropology:

> "Commodity exchange is an exchange of alienable objects between people who are in a state of reciprocal independence that establishes a quantitative relationship between the objects exchanged. Gift exchange is an exchange of inalienable objects between people who are in a state of reciprocal dependence that establishes a qualitative relationship between the transactors."
> (Gregory 1982:41)

On the relationship to Mauss:

> "Mauss's great achievement was to show that in primitive and archaic societies the exchange of gifts is the 'total' social fact. My more modest aim is to show that the exchange of commodities is the 'partial' social fact of capitalist economies — partial because it obscures the class relations that underlie the appearance of free exchange."
> (Gregory 1982:19)

## Influence and Legacy

Gregory's *Gifts and Commodities* became a touchstone in economic anthropology, setting the terms of debate for decades. [[marilyn-strathern]]'s *The Gender of the Gift* (1988) engaged critically with Gregory's framework, arguing that Melanesian personhood complicates the clean gift/commodity binary. [[david-graeber]] built on Gregory's work while pushing toward a more nuanced theory of value. [[james-carrier]]'s *Gifts and Commodities* (1995) offered an ethnographic counterpoint from Melanesia.
