# Greek Philosophy

Greek philosophy is the tradition of systematic thought that emerged in ancient Greece from the sixth century BCE onwards, encompassing figures such as Socrates, Plato, and Aristotle. In *The Story of Utopias*, Lewis Mumford treats Greek philosophy as the foundational intellectual resource for the Western utopian tradition — the original method of rational inquiry into the nature of the good society. Mumford explicitly contrasts the Greek philosophical approach with the Jewish prophetic tradition: "The Old Testament used to call such people prophets. They raised strange cities of their hearts' delight, which should be based exclusively upon righteousness and piety. But the greatest of all their prophets the Jews killed to make a Roman holiday. The Greeks knew such wise men as philosophers. They allowed them great freedom and rejoiced in the mathematical precision with which their intellectual leaders mapped out those theoretical roads which were to lead mankind from chaos to an ordered state of society" (P01-053).

## The Two Traditions: Prophecy vs. Philosophy

Mumford's opening chapter establishes a fundamental dichotomy that structures his entire intellectual history. The Jewish prophetic tradition produced visions of ideal commonwealths grounded in divine authority and moral righteousness. The Greek philosophical tradition, by contrast, grounded its utopias in reason, dialogue, and systematic inquiry. This distinction is not merely historical but methodological: "We, on the other hand, have our engines and we have our railroad trains and we have our factories and we cannot get rid of these iron servants without destroying the very basis of our civilization" (P01-049). The Greek method — analytical, skeptical, willing to follow an argument wherever it leads — is, for Mumford, the model for how modern civilization might think its way beyond its own idolum.

Mumford's preference for the Greek approach is not uncritical. He observes that Greek philosophy was itself a product of specific historical conditions: the Greek city-state, with its face-to-face political community, its common gods and gymnasia, and its integration of intellectual and civic life. "To the Greek of Plato's time, the commonwealth was something he actively shared with his fellow citizens. It was a definite parcel of land whose limits he could probably see from any convenient hilltop" (P02-228). This embodied, geographically-bounded conception of community is one that modern industrial civilization, with its vast scale and abstract social relations, cannot simply recover. The challenge Mumford sets for himself — and for his readers — is to translate the Greek philosophical method into terms appropriate for the twentieth century.

## Plato and the Philosophical Method

Plato's *Republic* — "the greatest of all the Greek utopias" — exemplifies the philosophical approach to ideal commonwealths. Mumford traces how Plato's method was shaped by historical crisis: "Plato's *Republic* dates from the period of social disintegration which followed the Peloponnesian War; and some of its mordant courage is probably derived from the hopelessness of conditions that came under Plato's eye" (P01-125). The *Republic* was not an academic exercise but a response to the collapse of Athenian society — a pattern Mumford sees repeated across the history of utopian thought: "This discussion of ideal commonwealths gets its form and its color from the time in which it is written" (P01-125).

### The Problem of Social Cohesion

Mumford notes that "in The Republic, Plato is chiefly concerned with what will hold the ideal city together" (P02-001). The central question is not merely what a perfect state would look like but what principle of cohesion prevents social disintegration. Plato's answer — the rule of the philosopher-kings, the class structure of guardians, auxiliaries, and producers — provides a model against which Mumford measures subsequent utopias.

This concern with cohesion is what distinguishes Plato's utopia from mere fantasy. "People are not the members of a community because they live under the same system of political government or dwell in the same country. They become genuine citizens to the extent that they share certain institutions and ways of life with similarly educated people" (P02-270). Plato's insight — that citizenship requires shared institutions, not merely shared territory — remains one of the most durable contributions of Greek philosophy to utopian thought.

### The Physical Foundations

Mumford emphasizes that Plato grounded his ideal city in material realities. "In describing his ideal community Plato, like a trained workman, begins with his physical foundations. So far from putting his utopia in a mythical island of Avilion, where falls not hail nor rain nor any snow, it is plain that Plato was referring repeatedly to the soil in which Athens was planted, and to the economic life which grew out of that soil" (P02-224). This attention to the material basis of the good life — soil, climate, economic production — sets Plato apart from the purely literary utopians who would follow him.

### The Question of Moderation

Mumford identifies a second central Platonic principle: the discipline of desire. "Should we moderate our wants or should we increase production? Plato had no difficulty in answering this question. He held that a reasonable man would moderate his wants" (P02-258). This principle — that the good life is achieved through disciplined limitation, not endless accumulation — would become a touchstone for Mumford's critique of industrial civilization. "Wealth and poverty, said Plato, are the two causes of deterioration in the arts" (P02-258). The Platonic ideal of moderation stands in deliberate opposition to what Mumford would later identify as the central pathology of the industrial age: the belief that happiness is achieved through increased production and consumption rather than through the cultivation of inner capacities.

Mumford's gloss on this principle is worth quoting at length for the light it throws on his own philosophical commitments:

> Plato pictured a community living a sane, continent, athletic, clear-eyed life; a community that would be always, so to say, within bounds. There is a horror of laxity and easy living in his Republic. His society was stripped for action. The fragrance that permeates his picture of the good life is not the heavy fragrance of rose-petals and incense falling upon languorous couches: it is the fragrance of the morning grass, and the scent of crushed mint or marjoram beneath the feet. (P02-260)

### Plato's Class Structure and Its Modern Parallels

Mumford extends the comparison to modern utopian schemes with characteristic irony: "Let us note what an improvement the three classes of Coketown are upon the three classes in Plato's Republic" (P10-050). The Coketown class structure — capitalists, managers, and workers — replicates Plato's tripartite division but strips it of its philosophical rationale, retaining hierarchy while discarding the commitment to wisdom and justice.

Crucially, Mumford insists that Plato's class division was not a hereditary caste system but a meritocracy: "Aristotle criticizes Plato in terms of a more simple system of democracy; but Plato did not mean to institute a fixed order; within his Republic the Napoleonic motto — *la carrière est ouverte aux talents* — was the guiding principle" (P02-294). Plato's guardians were selected through a lifelong process of education and testing, not through birth. The modern parallel, Mumford implies, is not the hereditary aristocracy of the Country House but something closer to the competitive examinations of the civil service — imperfect, but predicated on the possibility that talent might be identified and cultivated regardless of origin.

### Beyond the Republic: Laws, Statesman, Critias

The *Laws* and *Critias* further develop Plato's utopian thinking. In the *Critias*, Plato "attempted to show his Republic in action" (P02-043), depicting the auxiliaries dwelling "within a single enclosure" with "common meals and common temples of their own" (P02-043). Mumford sees here an early model of intentional community that would echo through centuries of utopian experimentation, from the Benedictine monastery to the Owenite village.

Mumford explicitly justifies his focus on Plato: "When we look at the utopias that Phaleas and Hippodamus and Aristotle have left us, and compare them with the Republic of Plato, the differences between them melt into insignificance and their likenesses are apparent. It is for this reason that I shall confine our examination of the Greek utopia to that which Plato set forth in *The Republic*, and qualified and broadened in *The Laws*, 'The Statesman,' and 'Critias'" (P02-214).

## Aristotle and the Scope of Greek Utopian Thought

Mumford acknowledges the breadth of Greek utopian speculation beyond Plato. "Aristotle tells us about an ideal state designed by one, Phaleas, who believed like Mr. Bernard Shaw in a complete equality of property; and from Aristotle, too, we learn of another utopia which was described by the great architect, city planner, and sociologist — Hippodamus" (P02-004). The Greek tradition was rich with competing visions of the ideal commonwealth, of which Plato's *Republic* is only the most influential survivor.

Hippodamus receives particular attention from Mumford, not only as an ancient utopian but as a personal inspiration: "If it adds at all to our sense of reality in going through utopia, let me confess that it is ultimately through the inspiration and example of another Hippodamus — Patrick Geddes, the town planner for Jerusalem and many other cities — that this book about utopias came to be written" (P02-212). The chain of intellectual inheritance — from Hippodamus to Aristotle to Geddes to Mumford — illustrates the living continuity of the Greek philosophical tradition in modern urban and social thought.

## The Two Branches of Knowledge

Mumford identifies in Aristotle's practice a lasting cleavage in Western thought. Aristotle divided his writing "into the exoteric and esoteric groups, into the popular and the scientific" — the origin of "two separate branches of literature, two different ways of taking account of the world, two disparate methods of approaching its problems" (P12-1359). The first branch — "that of the philosophers, the prophets, the poets, and the plain people" — is the lineage of utopian thought, interested in "the community" and holding that "nothing human was foreign to it." The second — specialized, technical, esoteric — becomes the lineage of modern science.

This division is not merely descriptive for Mumford; it is diagnostic. The hypertrophy of the second branch — specialized, technical knowledge pursued without reference to the good life or the community — is, in his view, one of the principal deformations of modern civilization. The utopian tradition, by keeping alive the first branch, performs an essential civilizational function: it insists that knowledge must ultimately serve the community and contribute to the good life. The Greek philosophical method — which refused to separate ethics from epistemology, the question of how to live from the question of what to know — is the original model for this integration.

## The Rediscovery of Greek Thought

Mumford emphasizes that the Renaissance rediscovery of Greek texts was a necessary condition for modern utopian thought. More's Raphael Hythloday "has abandoned Aristotle, whom the schoolmen had butchered and had made pemmican of, and through his conquest of Greek he has come into possession of that new learning which stems back to Plato; and his brain is teeming with the criticisms and suggestions of a strange, pagan philosophy" (P01-377). The recovery of Greek philosophy — Plato especially, liberated from medieval scholasticism — opened the intellectual space in which More's *Utopia* and its successors could be conceived.

Mumford notes the transformative encounter between Greek thought and New World exploration: "Conceive of the world of ideas which Greek literature had just opened up coming headlong against the new lands which the magnetic compass had given men the courage to explore, and utopia, as a fresh conception of the good life, becomes a throbbing possibility" (P01-377). This convergence — ancient Greek rationalism meeting the geographical expansion of European consciousness — created the conditions for utopia as a modern literary and philosophical genre.

## Greek Philosophy as Critical Method

Mumford closes his study with a return to the Greek philosophical method as a model for contemporary thought. "We travel through utopia only in order to get beyond utopia: if we leave the domains of history when we enter the gates of Plato's Republic, we do so in order to reenter more effectively the dusty midday traffic of the contemporary world" (P01-034). The purpose of studying Greek utopias is not to retreat into an idealized past but to acquire the philosophical tools necessary for diagnosing and transforming the present.

The final chapter returns to the theme of the two branches of knowledge, noting that "with the petrification of Greek thought that followed the collapse of the Alexandrian school, the second branch was slow in coming into its own" (P12-009). The challenge Mumford poses — and which he sees Greek philosophy as modeling — is to reunite the two branches: to bring specialized technical knowledge back into the service of the community and the good life. In this sense, Greek philosophy is not merely the historical origin point of the utopian tradition but its normative ideal: the integration of reason, ethics, and civic life that modern civilization must recover if it is to produce utopias adequate to its own possibilities.
