# Ideal Commonwealth

**Ideal Commonwealth** is the term [[lewis-mumford|Lewis Mumford]] uses to describe the classic literary utopias — rationally designed communities, typically city-regions, that embody a complete vision of the good life. In *[[the-story-of-utopias|The Story of Utopias]]*, the ideal commonwealth serves as the primary object of historical survey, from [[plato|Plato]]'s *[[republic-plato|Republic]]* through [[sir-thomas-more|Sir Thomas More]]'s *[[utopia-more|Utopia]]* to Campanella's *City of the Sun*.

## Definition and Scope

Mumford introduces ideal commonwealths in the opening chapter as the central subject of his inquiry:

> "It is the separate reality of utopia that we are going to explore in the course of this book — Utopia as a world by itself, divided into ideal commonwealths, with all its communities clustered into proud cities, aiming bravely at the good life" (001-123).

The ideal commonwealth is distinguished from the private [[utopia-of-escape]] and the practical [[utopia-of-reconstruction]]: it is a comprehensive intellectual exercise — an attempt to design an entire society according to rational principles.

## Historical Context

Mumford emphasizes that the form and content of each ideal commonwealth reflects its historical moment:

> "This discussion of ideal commonwealths gets its form and its color from the time in which it is written. Plato's *Republic* dates from the period of social disintegration which followed the Peloponnesian War; and some of its mordant courage is probably derived from the hopelessness of conditions that came under Plato's eye" (001-125).

The principle holds across the tradition: More's *Utopia* bridged the medieval and Renaissance worlds (001-125), and later ideal commonwealths responded to the upheavals of their own eras.

## The City-Region Model

Geographically, the ideal commonwealth took the form of a city-region — a dense urban center surrounded by sufficient agricultural land:

> "Geographically speaking, then, the ideal commonwealth was a city-region; that is, a city which was surrounded by enough land to supply the greater part of the food needed by the inhabitants; and placed convenient to the sea" (004-230).

Mumford traces this model to the geography of ancient Greece, where mountain-to-sea terrain compressed diverse occupations — woodcutters, herdsmen, cultivators, fishers, and traders — into a small area, providing the material basis for an integrated community (004-234).

## The Tension Between Family and Commonwealth

A recurring theme in the ideal commonwealth tradition is the conflict between private household loyalties and the public good:

> "Each home, each small circle of relatives and friends, tends to be a miniature utopia; there is a limited community of goods, a tendency to adjust one's actions to the welfare of the little whole, and a habit of banding together against the world at large. But the good, contrary to the proverb, is frequently the enemy of the better; and the little utopia of the family is the enemy — indeed the principal enemy — of the beloved community" (004-322).

Both Plato and Jesus recognized this tension, commanding their followers to abandon family ties for the sake of a higher common purpose. Plato's guardians were required to live in common barracks and hold no private property, so that their loyalty to the whole community would not be divided by private interests (004-324–326).

## Critiques and Evolution

Mumford does not treat ideal commonwealths uncritically. He notes that even More's *Utopia* — one of the most celebrated entries in the tradition — contains serious flaws, particularly in its treatment of crime and war (004-449). Similarly, he observes that Campanella's *City of the Sun* borrows so heavily from Plato and More that "very little indeed remains" of original contribution (006-613).

By the book's final section, Mumford distinguishes the classic ideal commonwealths from a broader category of "collective utopias or social myths" — the [[idolum|idola]] that have actually shaped mass behavior:

> "Up to the present the idola which have exercised the most considerable influence upon the actual life of the community are such as have been partly expressed in a hundred works and never perhaps fully expressed in one" (010-1029).

## The Enduring Value

In his concluding assessment, Mumford argues that the ideal commonwealth tradition remains vital because these works demonstrate how ideas and ideals can shape material reality:

> "It has been in the pictures of ideal commonwealths such as Plato's that the 'ideal' and the 'actual' have met" (012-1479).

The weakness of the utopian method was the assumption that any single person's vision could be directly realized in society; its strength lay in recognizing that human beliefs about the future are themselves forces that condition that future (012-1478).
