# Robert Owen

**Robert Owen** (1771-1858) is the Welsh industrialist and social reformer whose model community at New Lanark and advocacy for cooperative production made him one of the most visible — and, in [[lewis-mumford|Lewis Mumford]]'s view, most personally frustrating — figures of early nineteenth-century utopianism. His name is "usually associated with utopianism," though Mumford treats his work as belonging "more to the 'real' world than to the idola of utopia" (006-008).

## New Lanark and Industrial Reform

Owen's fame rests on his transformation of New Lanark, a cotton mill village in Scotland, into a model industrial community. But Mumford's treatment is notably ambivalent:

> "Let us grant him good intentions, organizing ability, and moral fervor: without doubt he is a noble figure, even when his attitude is strained and his tone strident" (006-008).

The series of essays Owen wrote on love and marriage are, Mumford notes, "marked by fine sympathy and common sense; and it is to be regretted that they are not as widely known as his plans for a new moral world" (006-008).

## The Limits of Owen's Vision

Despite his practical achievements, Mumford delivers a devastating personal assessment:

> "Robert Owen, one of the most sanguine advocates of popular education, was himself a living example of the need for a different kind of discipline than his narrow and homiletic mind, with its childish interpretation of religious belief and its equally childish rationalism, was capable of framing" (012-074).

Mumford argues that Owen's personality undermined his ideas:

> "No one ever frustrated so many good ideas, from the plan of garden cities down to the project for cooperative production than this same Owen, whose bumptiousness, arrogance, and conceit were bound to provoke reactions in other people which would have defeated the plans of Omnipotence itself" (012-074).

## The Odor of the Poorhouse

In a sharp contrast with [[sir-thomas-more|Thomas More]]'s Utopia, Mumford notes the qualitative difference between the visions:

> "The smell of the barracks or the poorhouse, which we should find later in Robert Owen's common halls, does not intrude for an instant" in More's description of the Utopian evening meal (004-039).

This sensory judgment captures Mumford's broader critique: where More imagined a cultivated community, Owen's schemes carried the institutional bleakness of the early industrial age.

## The Conditions Owen Faced

Mumford contextualizes Owen's work in the brutal reality of early industrialization:

> "I do not speak too harshly of the early industrial age; it is impossible to speak too harshly. Take the trouble to read Robert Owen's 'Essay on the Formation of Character' (Manchester: 1837) and learn what conditions were like in a model factory run by an enlightened employer: it is a picture of unmitigated brutality" (006-068).

## Owen's Legacy

In Mumford's analysis, Owen's socialism was "partly fulfilled in the cooperative movement," while the Marxian alternative "rests upon very shaky foundations." Yet the critical weakness of all partisan movements, Owen's included, is that "they have not altered the contents of the modern social order, even when they have altered the method of distribution" (012-052). Owen's contribution to the [[good-life|good life]] tradition remains ambiguous — he fought against industrial brutality but could not imagine beyond its institutional forms.
