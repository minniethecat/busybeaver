# Johann Valentin Andreae

Johann Valentin Andreae (1586-1654) was a German Lutheran pastor, social reformer, and humanist scholar whose utopia *Christianopolis* (1619) represents one of the most forward-looking visions of the early modern period. Mumford ranks Andreae above his better-known contemporaries Francis Bacon and Tommaso Campanella, declaring that "Bacon with his positively nauseating foppishness about details in dress and his superstitious regard for forms and ceremonials, and Campanella, the lonely monk whose *City of the Sun* seems a marriage of Plato's Republic and the Court of Montezuma," are "quite second-rate in comparison" (0381-498).

Andreae lived and worked across the intellectual centers of the German-speaking world — Herrenburg, Tübingen, Strasbourg, Heidelberg, Frankfurt, Geneva — and corresponded with learned men abroad, including Samuel Hartlib in England and John Amos Comenius (0381-500). *Christianopolis* thus emerges from a dense network of early modern intellectual exchange, synthesizing Renaissance humanism, Lutheran piety, and an emerging scientific worldview into a vision that, in Mumford's assessment, "seems to leap three hundred years ahead of his time and environment" (0381-492).

## Christianopolis: The City

Christianopolis is "a square whose side is 700 feet, well fortified with four towers and a wall," looking "towards the four quarters of the earth" (0381-506). The city houses about four hundred citizens "in religious faith and peace of the highest order." Buildings are made of burnt stone with fireproof walls between them, and "fresh air and ventilation are provided throughout" (0381-506). The island beyond the city serves agriculture and workshops.

## Republic of Workers

If Plato's Republic was modeled on the military camp and More's Utopia on the patriarchal family, "in Christianopolis, the workshop and the worker set the lines upon which the community is developed" (0381-510). Mumford frames the distinction precisely: "If Utopia exhibits the communism of the family, Christianopolis presents the communism of the guild" (0381-510). The community is a "republic of workers, living in equality, desiring peace, and renouncing riches" (0381-510).

## Industrial Zoning

Andreae divides Christianopolis into three industrial quarters, anticipating modern zoning practice by centuries. The first quarter handles agriculture and animal husbandry; the second contains mills, bakeshops, meat-shops, and light manufacturing; the third is devoted to metallurgy and industries requiring constant fire — glass, brick, and earthenware (0381-512, 0381-514, 0381-516). Mumford notes that "the separation of the city into zones, the distinction between 'heavy' industries and 'light' industries, the grouping of similar industrial establishments, the provision of an agricultural zone adjacent to the city — in all this our garden cities are but belated reproductions of Christianopolis" (0381-516).

## Science Applied to Industry

A distinctive feature of Christianopolis is its systematic application of scientific knowledge to production. Workers are "not driven to a work with which they are unfamiliar, like pack-animals to their task, but they have been trained before in an accurate knowledge of scientific matters" (0381-518). The governing principle is that "unless you analyze matter by experiment, unless you improve the deficiencies of knowledge by more capable instruments, you are worthless" (0381-518). This commitment to what we would now call applied research — "the dependence of industrial improvement upon deliberate scientific research" — may be "a new discovery for the practical man, but it is an old story in Utopia" (0381-518).

## Commerce Without Profit

Commerce in Christianopolis does not exist for individual gain. Trade is conducted by "those selected to attend to them," and its aim is "not to gain money but to increase the variety of things at the disposal of the local community" (0381-536). Andreae envisions a world where "we may see the peculiar production of each land, and so communicate with each other that we may seem to have the advantages of the universe in one place, as it were" (0381-536).

## Education and the Laboratory

Christianopolis's educational system centers on laboratories, museums, and workshops rather than lecture halls. A Drug Supply House develops pharmacy scientifically; an anatomy theater teaches the operations of life; a Natural Science laboratory functions as what we would now call a Museum of Natural History (0381-572, 0381-574). Mumford draws a striking parallel to modern experimental education: "If anyone thinks that Johann Andreae's prescription for a teaching staff is an impossible one, let him visit the Peterborough School, and examine its records and achievements" (0381-566).

## Family and Domestic Life

The family in Christianopolis consists of isolated couples with, at most, four to six people — a departure from the extended patriarchal household of More's Utopia (0381-542). Houses are built to maximize air and sunlight, with a forty-foot street frontage and covered walkways. Marriage requires no dowry, no professional anxieties, and no housing shortage, since "all houses are owned by the city and are granted and assigned to individuals for their use" (0381-542). Domestic labor is shared between husband and wife, and public kitchens provide cooked meals on demand.
