The Cooperative Movement emerged in nineteenth-century Britain as one of the most enduring practical legacies of utopian socialism. Rooted in the ideas of Robert Owen and his followers, the movement sought to establish cooperative enterprises — both consumer and producer cooperatives — as alternatives to the competitive capitalist economy. Mumford observes that while Owen's broader utopian vision was often dismissed, "Owen's kind of socialism has been partly fulfilled in the cooperative movement" (011-013).

The movement drew on a vision of economic association that Mumford traces through the utopian tradition, from Fourier's phalanx model — where in commercial exchange "the phalanx acts as a unit" (006-020) — to Theodor Hertzka's Freeland, which Mumford describes as "a cooperative commonwealth in which the State acts as an interested party in the production and distribution of goods" (007-018). This cooperative commonwealth model represented a middle path between pure laissez-faire individualism and full state socialism.

By the early twentieth century, the cooperative movement had achieved substantial practical success. Mumford acknowledges that "consumer's cooperation alone has in England measurably lightened the physical burden of existence for a great many people" (011-013). The movement's strength lay in its concrete, incremental approach to economic reform, building alternative institutions rather than merely agitating for political change.

However, Mumford also offers a critical assessment of the movement's limitations. Its weakness, he argues, "consists in the fact that they have not altered the contents of the modern social order, even when they have altered the method of distribution" (011-013). The cooperative movement, for all its practical achievements, remained within the existing economic framework rather than transforming the deeper structures of industrial society — a critique that reflected Mumford's broader argument that genuine utopian transformation required more than institutional reform alone.

The cooperative ideal also suffered from the limitations of its chief advocate. Mumford notes pointedly that "no one ever frustrated so many good ideas, from the plan of garden cities down to the project for cooperative production than this same Owen, whose bumptiousness, arrogance, and conceit were bound to provoke reactions in other people" (011-023). This observation underscores Mumford's recurring theme that the success of utopian projects depends as much on the character of their proponents as on the soundness of their plans.
