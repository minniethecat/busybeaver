# Karl Marx

**Karl Marx** (1818-1883) is the German philosopher, economist, and revolutionary whose materialist analysis of history and capitalism stands as the most influential "partial utopia" of the nineteenth century. [[lewis-mumford|Lewis Mumford]] treats Marx not as a utopian in the classic sense but as a key figure in the modern shift toward instrumental thinking about society.

## Marx and the Idola

Mumford groups Marx with the "practical men" whose behavior was patterned by the dominant idola of the nineteenth century, in a section of the book devoted to the partial utopias that distorted the full humanist vision:

> "Those who were inside this new order — the Gradgrinds and Bounderbys whom Dickens pictures in *Hard Times* — sought to realize their utopia of the Iron Age on earth. When we are through with the genuine utopians we shall examine the idola by which all the 'practical' men of the nineteenth century, Marx as well as Macaulay, patterned their behavior" (006-070).

## The Economic Man

For Mumford, Marx's key limitation was his reduction of the human problem to economics:

> "Out of the better quality [of the Economic Man conception], Karl Marx painted the picture of the faithful laborer in Coketown whose masters swindled him out of the 'surplus value' he produced" (012-042).

This Economic Man — whether the exploited worker of Marx or the beneficent capitalist of Ricardo — was a caricature. Mumford argues that the notion that "the only fundamental problem in the modern world was the labor problem" grew directly from these conceptions (012-042).

But his critique is even-handed:

> "Is it any less absurd when we confess that most of the movements which were founded upon this assumption were actuated by generous and humane motives, and that Francis Place, the tailor of Charing Cross, who believed in a radical application of laissez faire principles, was just as sincere a believer in the common weal as Karl Marx, who predicted a dictatorship of the proletariat?" (012-046).

## Utopian vs. Scientific Socialism

Mumford engages directly with the Engels-Marx distinction between "utopian" and "scientific" socialism:

> "In his *Socialism: Utopian and Scientific*, Friedrich Engels made a plea for a realistic method of thought, which limited itself to a here and now, as against what he derided as the utopian method, the attempt on the part of a single thinker to give a detailed picture of the society of the future" (012-052).

Mumford's verdict on this distinction is pointed and even-handed: "if the utopian socialism of [[robert-owen|Owen]] has been ineffective, the realistic socialism of Marx has been equally ineffective" (012-052).

## The Power of the Dream

Despite his critique, Mumford grants Marx's project an essential quality — its power as a vision that moved people:

> "However crude the Marxian analysis of society may have been, it at least had the merit of presenting a great dream — the dream of a titanic struggle between the possessors and the dispossessed in which every worker had a definite part to play" (012-062).

Without such dreams, Mumford warns, "the advances in social science will be just as disorderly and fusty as the applications of physical science have been in our material affairs" (012-062). The utopian imagination, even in its partial forms, provides the motivating vision that purely instrumental analysis cannot supply.

## The Missing Scale of Values

Mumford's fundamental objection to Marx and the entire nineteenth-century reform tradition is that they addressed distribution without addressing content:

> "If a great many of these programs have had the notion that industrial machinery, under socialism or guildism or cooperation was to be used for the common benefit, what was lacking was any common notion as to what the common benefit was" (012-046).

This critique links Marx back to the core question of the utopian tradition: not who owns the means of production, but what constitutes the [[good-life|good life]] toward which all means should be directed.
