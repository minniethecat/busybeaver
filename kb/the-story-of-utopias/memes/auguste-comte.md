Auguste Comte (1798-1857) was a French philosopher who founded the discipline of sociology and the philosophical system of positivism. In *The Story of Utopias*, Mumford invokes Comte at two decisive moments: first, in tracing the lineage of social classification that runs from Plato through More to H. G. Wells, and second, in articulating a crucial distinction between temporal and spiritual community — a distinction that lies at the heart of Mumford's critique of the National State.

## Life and Works

Comte was born in Montpellier and studied at the École Polytechnique in Paris before serving as secretary to Henri de Saint-Simon, from whom he absorbed many of the ideas that would later become systematized in his own work. His major work, *Cours de Philosophie Positive* (1830-1842), laid out his theory of the three stages of human intellectual development — theological, metaphysical, and positive — and established sociology as the culminating science in the hierarchy of knowledge. His later work, *Système de Politique Positive* (1851-1854), extended positivism into a comprehensive social doctrine, including a "Religion of Humanity" that would replace traditional religious worship.

## The Classification of Social Types

In his analysis of H. G. Wells's *A Modern Utopia*, Mumford notes that Wells's four-class division of the population — the kinetic, the poietic, the base, and the dull — "follows in general the lines laid down by Comte — chiefs, people, intellectuals, and emotionals" (1004). Mumford traces this impulse toward social classification through a long genealogy:

> This division of classes is a very ancient one. In that old Indian script, the *Bhagavad Gita*, we find that the population is divided into Brahmans, Kshatriyas, Vaisryas, and Sudras, and that their duties are "determined by the modes that prevail in their separate natures."
> (1004)

The recurrence of this fourfold structure — from ancient India through Plato's guardians/auxiliaries/producers, More's Philarchs/people/priests/scholars, Comte's positivist classification, and Wells's kinetic/poietic/base/dull — suggests, for Mumford, a persistent utopian instinct to organize society according to functional rather than hereditary distinctions. Comte's contribution was to give this instinct a "scientific" formulation grounded in his positivist philosophy.

## Temporal and Spiritual Community

Mumford's most significant use of Comte appears in his critique of nationalism in Chapter X. Arguing that the National State is simultaneously too narrow and too broad — too narrow for spiritual community, too broad for genuine social bonds — Mumford invokes Comte's distinction:

> The temporal community, as Auguste Comte finely pointed out, is local, restricted, and multiform; this is its essential nature and limitation. The spiritual community is universal.
> (1211)

This distinction becomes foundational to Mumford's entire eutopian argument. The error of the National State — what Mumford calls "a great cultural misdemeanor" (1211) — is precisely its attempt to reverse Comte's insight: to make the spiritual community restricted (national literature, national science, national church) and the temporal community universal (imperialism). Comte's formulation provides the philosophical anchor for Mumford's insistence that genuine community must be built at the regional and local scale, while cultural and spiritual life must draw freely from the whole of human inheritance.

## Place in the Utopian Tradition

Comte is not himself a utopian in Mumford's strict sense — he did not produce "complete pictures of an ideal commonwealth" (1524). Rather, he belongs to the class of systematic thinkers whose ideas provide the intellectual materials out of which utopian visions are constructed. His positivist philosophy, with its emphasis on scientific method, social classification, and the religion of humanity, influenced a wide range of nineteenth- and twentieth-century social movements.

The specific legacy that Mumford preserves is Comte's insight into the *scale* of community — the recognition that different kinds of human association operate at different levels and that the confusion of these levels produces "idola" that distort social life. This insight resonates throughout Mumford's later work on regionalism and the city.

## Mumford's Assessment

Mumford's treatment of Comte is brief but respectful. The phrase "as Auguste Comte finely pointed out" (1211) signals genuine intellectual debt: Comte is cited not as an adversary or a curiosity but as a source of genuine insight. The limitation is implicit: Comte's positivism, with its elaborate hierarchy of sciences and its quasi-religious apparatus, represents precisely the kind of systematic rigidity that Mumford's pragmatic, pluralist eutopianism seeks to avoid. Comte provided a crucial distinction; he did not provide a livable community.
