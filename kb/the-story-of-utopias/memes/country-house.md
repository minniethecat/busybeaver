# Country House (idolum)

The Country House is the first of three great "idola" that Lewis Mumford identifies in *The Story of Utopias* as the dominant utopian visions that shaped the modern age. Together with [[coketown|Coketown]] and the [[megalopolis|Megalopolis]], it constitutes one of the "partial utopias" that have actually been realized in the modern world — not as literary fantasies but as lived social forms that have "made the world over in their image" (II-1021).

Mumford is explicit about the methodological challenge of treating these idola: "In selecting these idola — The Country House, Coketown, the Megalopolis — I have been forced to gauge their strength and test their quality very largely by their actual results in the workaday world, and it is a little hard to purify them from the various institutions, old and new, in which they are mixt. Yet with all this taint of actuality, these idola are scarcely as credible as the Republic" (II-1037). The Country House is simultaneously a utopian vision and a material reality — and it is precisely this doubleness that makes it, for Mumford, more consequential than any purely literary utopia.

> To understand the utopia of the Country House we must jump back a few centuries in history. (II-1037)

## Historical Origins: From Castle and Monastery to Country House

Mumford locates the Country House's emergence at the hinge between the medieval and modern worlds. Two great institutions of the Middle Age — the castle and the monastery — were simultaneously abandoned as living forms: "Men ceased to build castles to protect themselves against physical dangers; and they left off entering monasteries in order to fortify their souls for the Hereafter. Both the spiritual and the temporal life began to shift to a new institution, the Country House" (II-1041).

This transformation was driven by two converging changes. First, a material transformation in the physical life of Western Europe — the end of the conditions that made fortification necessary and monastic withdrawal attractive. Second, a cultural transformation brought about by the Renaissance rediscovery of classical antiquity. "Digging about the ruins of Rome and other cities, the men of the late Middle Age discovered the remains of a great and opulent civilization; and exploring the manuscripts and printed books which were getting into general circulation, they found themselves face to face with strange conceptions of life, with habits of refinement, ease, and sensuous luxury which the hard life of the camp and the castle had never really permitted" (II-1049).

The architectural signature of this shift is revealing. Mumford notes that "the colleges at Oxford which date from the Renaissance can scarcely be distinguished in architectural detail from the palaces which the aristocracy were building in the same period; while our banks and our political edifices to this day bear almost universally the stamp of that Roman and Grecian litter which men discovered on the outskirts of the medieval city" (II-1049). The Country House was not merely a residential form; it became the template for the institutional architecture of modern civilization.

## The Abbey of Thélème: The Illustrative Utopia

Mumford identifies Rabelais's Abbey of Thélème (from *Gargantua*) as the exemplary illustration of the Country House ideal — "one of the few downright serious passages in his great work" (IV-1053). In Rabelais's vision, the Abbey inverts every feature of the medieval monastery: it is to have no walls, to be situated in open country rather than wilderness, to admit both men and women, and to substitute liberty for the monastic vows.

The physical description is lavish: "every member is to be furnished with a generous apartment, consisting of a principal room, a withdrawing room, a handsome closet, a wardrobe, and an oratory; and the house itself is to contain not merely libraries in every language, but fair and spacious galleries of paintings" (IV-1055). Beyond the house lie a tiltyard, riding court, theatre, swimming pool, gardens, orchards, parks with venison, and an archery range. Surrounding the estate is "a row of houses to the extent of half a league, very neat and cleanly, wherein dwelt the goldsmiths, lapidaries, jewellers, embroiderers, tailors, gold drawers, velvet weavers, tapestry makers, and upholsterers" — all "furnished with matter and stuff from the hands of Lord Nausiclete, who every year brought them seven ships from the Perlas and Cannibal Islands, laden with ingots of gold, with raw silk, with pearls and precious stones" (IV-1057).

The liberty of Thélème is complete: "such a liberty as one enjoys at a Country House to this day, under the care of a tactful hostess; for everyone does nothing except follow his own free will and pleasure" (III-1113). The inmates may "rise out of his bed whenever he thinks good, and eating, drinking, and laboring when he has a mind to it." The single rule — *Fay ce que vouldras* (Do what thou wilt) — is the anti-monastic principle par excellence.

Mumford's judgment is clear: "When we turn our attention from Rabelais' conceit of an anti-monastic order, we discover that he has given us an excellent picture of the Country House, and of what I shall take the liberty of calling Country House culture" (IV-1141).

## The Culture of the Country House

### Possession Without Work

Mumford's analysis of Country House culture centers on the relationship between possession and production. "In the Country House possession is based upon privilege and not upon work. The title to land which was historically obtained for the most part through force and fraud is the economic foundation of the Country House existence" (V-1141). The corollary is systematic dispossession: "In order to keep the artisans and laborers who surround the Country House at their work, it is necessary to keep them from having access to the land on their own account, provision always being made that the usufruct of the land shall go to the owner and not to the worker" (V-1077).

This separation of ownership from labor creates a society organized around passive enjoyment rather than active creation. The Country House ideal, Mumford argues, "is that of a completely functionless existence; or at best, an existence in which all the functions that properly belong to a civilized man shall be carried on by functionaries" (V-1077). Since this ideal is biologically impossible — human beings are constitutively active creatures — the resulting void must be filled with play and sport, imitations of activities that once had "a vital use."

### The Arts as Acquisition

In the Country House, literature and the fine arts undoubtedly flourish — but "they flourish as the objects of appreciation rather than as the active, creative elements in the community's life; they flourish particularly in the fashion that Plato looked upon as a corrupting influence in the community" (V-1079). The governing aesthetic faculty is not creativity but "taste" — "a certain capacity to discriminate among sensory stimuli, a capacity which is essentially just as hospitable to a decomposing cheese as to the very staff of life" (V-1079).

Mumford develops this critique through a concrete architectural analysis: Country House architecture is almost universally derivative — bastard Greek or Roman in England, Tudor pastiche in America. "From feature to feature everything is derivative; everything, in the last analysis, has either been stolen or purchased from the original makers; and what has not been stolen or purchased has been basely copied" (V-1081). "The insatiability of the Country House to possess art is only equalled by its inability to create it" (V-1083).

### The Dissociation From Community

A crucial structural feature of the Country House is its separation from the surrounding community. Mumford contrasts this with the medieval order, in which "the lowest workman in the guild shared the institutions of his masters: the churches, the guild pageants, and the morality plays were all part and parcel of the same culture" (V-1087). The Country House shattered this integration: "Culture came to mean not a participation in the creative activities of one's own community, but the acquisition of the products of other communities" (V-1089).

The consequence is a double impoverishment. Popular institutions, "deprived of their contacts with the general world of culture, languished away." Meanwhile, each Country House was forced to "obtain for its limited circle all the elements that were necessary to the good life in a whole community such as Plato described" (V-1089) — an impossible project that could only result in the manic accumulation of goods, styles, and experiences.

## Opposition to Plato's Civic Ideal

Mumford positions the Country House in deliberate opposition to the Greek philosophical tradition: "Country House institutions are the precise opposite of everything that Plato looked upon as desirable in a good community" (V-1073). Where Plato's Republic sought the happiness of the whole community, the Country House is "concerned not with the happiness of the whole community but with the felicity of the governors." Where Plato insisted on moderation and the discipline of desire, the Country House pursues limitless acquisition. Where Plato grounded citizenship in shared institutions and active participation, the Country House substitutes passive enjoyment and the accumulation of private goods.

This opposition is not merely antiquarian; it captures the fundamental choice that Mumford sees confronting modern civilization. Do we organize society around the cultivation of active, participating citizens — the Platonic model — or around the provision of goods to passive consumers — the Country House model? The two utopias, the ancient and the modern, represent competing answers to the question of what the good life consists of.

## The Admitted Virtues

Mumford is careful to acknowledge what is valid in the Country House ideal. "Enjoyment is a necessary element in achievement, and by its regard for the decent graces of life, for such things as an ease in manners and a fine flow of conversation and the clash of wits and a sensitiveness to beautiful things, the Country House was by all odds a humanizing influence" (VI-1093). It insisted "that all our pragmatic activities must be realized in things that are worth having or doing for themselves." It established "more permanent standards — standards which were common to the arts of Greece and China — than those which were looked upon as sufficient in the local region."

Ruskin and Samuel Butler, Mumford notes, "were possibly right when they insisted that the perfect gentleman was a finer product than the perfect peasant or artisan: he is a finer product because he is essentially more alive" (VI-1093). The Country House, at its best, embodied a genuine human ideal — one whose appeal explains its remarkable durability across centuries and classes.

## The Fatal Weakness

The fatal weakness of Country House culture is its separation of enjoyment from achievement: "The Country House did not see that enjoyment rested upon achievement, and was indeed inseparable from achievement" (VII-1095). The craftsman deprived of the capacity to enjoy the fine arts lost the ability to create them; the connoisseur for whom enjoyment "came too easily, with a mere snap of the fingers," was condemned to an endless chase after novelty. "Hence the succession of styles by which Country House decoration has become a thing for mockery: Chinese in one age, Indian in another, Persian in the next, with Egyptian, Middle African, and heaven knows what else destined to follow in due order. There is nothing to settle to, because there is no task to be done, and no problem to work out" (VII-1095).

## The Country House–Coketown Relationship

A central insight of Mumford's analysis is the symbiotic — and ultimately generative — relationship between the Country House and Coketown. The Country House's "passionate demand for physical goods" directly gives rise to Coketown as its industrial servant (VII-1109). The commercial ventures that the Country House promoted — "under its Drakes and Raleighs, ventures which were needed to bring them 'Ships from the Perlas and Cannibal Islands'" — shifted the emphasis from workmanship to sale, from production to acquisition (VII-1103).

Mumford traces the economic logic precisely: the Country House ideal "is to exaggerate the demand for goods, and to cause an enormously wasteful duplication of the apparatus of consumption" (VII-1105). If happiness is acquired through obtaining things, and a man with five houses is five times as fortunate as a man with one, "there is really no limit to the business of getting and spending." The ideal that began in the Renaissance villa culminates in "six inadequate rooms at the end of nowhere in a Philadelphia suburb" (VII-1105).

The weekend compromise — "Coketown for the workaday week, the Country House for the weekend" — represents the uneasy settlement between these two competing idola in modern life (VIII-1131). The Country House created the demand; Coketown supplied it; and the modern individual is caught between them, working in one idolum to fund a fragmentary participation in the other.

## The Country House in Literature

Mumford notes the prominence of the Country House as a literary theme, citing Boccaccio's *Decameron*, Pope's "Rape of the Lock," Meredith's *The Egoist*, Wells's *Tono-Bungay*, Shaw's *Heartbreak House*, Mallock's *The New Republic*, and Chekhov's *The Cherry Orchard*. "Whether Mr. W. H. Mallock holds the pattern of Country House culture up to us in *The New Republic* or Anton Chekhov penetrates its aimlessness and futility in *The Cherry Orchard*, The Country House is one of the recurrent themes of literature" (IV-1067).

A particularly interesting counter-example appears in [[w-h-hudson|W. H. Hudson]]'s *[[a-crystal-age|A Crystal Age]]*, where the Country House is transformed from a weekend retreat into a permanent home — a self-sufficient community where "the itch for getting and spending has disappeared" and "the life of dignified leisure is a life of work; in short, the life of the artist" (I-950). This utopian transmutation of the Country House form suggests that the architectural and social type might be redeemed if its economic basis — dispossession, passive ownership, limitless acquisition — were overturned.
