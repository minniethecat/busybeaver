## Overview

John Galsworthy (1867-1933) was an English novelist and playwright, best known for *The Forsyte Saga* and for winning the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1932. His work examined the tensions between class, property, and human relationships in Edwardian England. Mumford references Galsworthy's play *Strife* in *The Story of Utopias* as an illustration of the conflict between institutional roles and personal ties.

## Role in The Story of Utopias

Mumford invokes Galsworthy's dramatic portrait of a trade union leader to illustrate the predicament of the individual caught between collective responsibility and private life:

> The picture of a trade union leader which Mr. John Galsworthy portrays in Strife, whose power to act firmly in behalf of his group is sapped by the demands made by family ties, could be matched in a thousand places. (002-051)

## The Conflict Between Institution and Individual

Mumford's use of Galsworthy is strategically placed in Chapter II, where he is laying out the fundamental tensions that utopian thought must address. The trade union leader in *Strife* (1909) embodies the problem of divided loyalty: he is accountable to his fellow workers and must bargain fiercely on their behalf, yet his personal relationships and family obligations pull him in other directions, draining his will to fight.

This conflict is not unique to trade unionism. Mumford suggests it "could be matched in a thousand places" — in every institution, in every social role, the demands of the collective and the needs of the individual come into tension. The utopian project, as Mumford conceives it, must find ways to reconcile these competing claims rather than simply choosing one over the other.

## Significance

Galsworthy represents the literary dimension of Mumford's argument. *The Story of Utopias* draws not only on philosophy and political theory but also on the evidence of imaginative literature, where novelists and playwrights dramatize the lived experience of social contradictions. Galsworthy's acute observation of the human costs of class conflict provides empirical content for Mumford's theoretical analysis, grounding abstract discussion of "the individual and society" in concrete dramatic situations.
