# Tommaso Campanella

**Tommaso Campanella** (1568-1639) is the Italian Dominican friar and philosopher whose *City of the Sun* (*Civitas Solis*, written c. 1602, published 1623) is, in [[lewis-mumford|Lewis Mumford]]'s assessment, "a marriage of [[plato|Plato]]'s Republic and the Court of Montezuma" (004-098). Together with [[francis-bacon|Francis Bacon]], Campanella stands at the threshold between the classical utopia of ends and the modern utopia of means.

## The City of the Sun

Mumford introduces Campanella's utopia as a "picture puzzle put together out of fragments from Plato and More" (006-013). The city itself is an exotic apparition:

> "The outward appearance of this country is a little strange — the city with its seven rings named after the seven planets, and its four gates that lead to the four quarters of the earth, and the hill that is topped by a grand temple, and the walls covered with laws and alphabets and paintings of natural phenomena" (006-011).

The city is governed by three rulers — **Power**, **Wisdom**, and **Love** — assisted by learned doctors with titles like Astrologus, Cosmographus, and Arithmeticus.

## Mechanical Invention

Despite his secondhand framework, Campanella contributed one genuinely original element: an anticipation of mechanical power:

> "The people of the City of the Sun have wagons that are driven by the wind, and boats 'which go over the waters without rowers or the force of the wind, but by a marvelous contrivance'" (006-015).

There is here, Mumford notes, "a very clear anticipation of the mechanical improvements which began to multiply so rapidly in the eighteenth century" (006-015). The Grand Master of the narrative exclaims prophetically of "the coming age, that has in it more history within a hundred years than all the world had in four thousand years before!" (006-015).

With mechanical arts fully developed, labor in the City of the Sun is dignified — there is no slavery, and work is limited to four hours per day:

> "They are rich because they want nothing; poor because they possess nothing; and consequently they are not slaves to circumstances, but circumstances serve them" (006-015).

## Community of Property and Family

Campanella follows Plato in abolishing private property and the individual family — a choice Mumford traces directly to biography:

> "More and Andreae are married men, and they stand for the individual family. Plato and Campanella were bachelors, and they proposed that men should live like monks or soldiers" (005-008).

Campanella's analysis of the connection between private property, the household, and the commonwealth is remarkably penetrating — he sees that "the separate household is the true root of private property, and that as long as people have their own wives and children, self-interest, in however refined a form, will be the dominant note" (006-017).

## Mumford's Verdict

Mumford is notably cool toward Campanella. Together with Bacon, he ranks them as second-rate compared to Johann Valentin Andreae:

> "The two other utopians who wrote in the same half century as Andreae — Francis Bacon and Tommaso Campanella — are quite second-rate in comparison" (004-098).

When the borrowed elements are subtracted — community of property from Plato, communal dining from More, demonstration-based science from Andreae — "very little indeed remains" (006-013) that is Campanella's own.

## The Entrance to the Utopia of Means

Yet Campanella's historical significance is genuine:

> "Campanella with his dream of powerful mechanical inventions, in which he had been anticipated by Leonardo, and Bacon with his sketch of scientific institutes — with these two utopians we stand at the entrance to the utopia of means" (006-039).

Like Bacon, Campanella assumed the ends of the good life without re-examining them, focusing instead on expanding man's material powers. The result, Mumford warns, is that "we in the Western World live in an inventor's paradise" with "more knowledge and more power than Bacon or Campanella could possibly have dreamed of" — yet we face again the fundamental question: "what are men to do with their knowledge and power?" (006-039).
