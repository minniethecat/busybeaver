## Biography

Émile Durkheim (1858-1917) was a French sociologist who is widely regarded as one of the founding figures of modern sociology, alongside Karl Marx and Max Weber. Born in Épinal, France, to a family of rabbis, Durkheim established sociology as a rigorous academic discipline in France, founding the first European department of sociology at the University of Bordeaux in 1895 and later holding a chair at the Sorbonne.

His major works include *The Division of Labour in Society* (1893), *The Rules of Sociological Method* (1895), *Suicide* (1897), and *The Elementary Forms of Religious Life* (1912). Across these works, Durkheim developed a systematic approach to understanding how social structures shape individual behavior and consciousness — an approach that directly informs Mumford's analysis of the social dimensions of utopian thought.

## Collective Representations

Durkheim's most significant concept for the framework of *The Story of Utopias* is that of **collective representations** (*représentations collectives*) — shared ideas, beliefs, and values that emerge from social interaction and come to constitute the mental environment of a society. Mumford directly engages with this concept in his discussion of *idola* (social myths):

> The sociologists who follow Émile Durkheim have called a certain part of these idola collective representations but they are wrong, I believe, when they limit these "representations" to savage or ignorant groups for they are an important part of every civilized person's luggage. (1035)

Mumford's critique here is revealing: while he acknowledges the Durkheimian concept as a useful tool for understanding how shared ideas shape social reality, he rejects the anthropological limitation of the concept to pre-modern societies. For Mumford, the civilized world is equally governed by collective representations — the "idola" of nationalism, industrialism, and the Country House ideal — that function as powerful social myths requiring critical examination.

This engagement with Durkheim locates *The Story of Utopias* within a broader sociological tradition concerned with the relationship between collective beliefs and social organization. Utopias, in Mumford's framework, are not merely literary fantasies but function as alternative collective representations that challenge the dominant social myths of an age.

## The French Sociological School

Mumford acknowledges the broader French sociological tradition that followed Durkheim in the bibliographic appendix to *The Story of Utopias*:

> The importance of our social myths and our collective representations has been noted by a whole school of French sociologists who follow Émile Durkheim; and the dynamic force of ideas has been treated by Alfred Fouillée. (1528)

This reference places Mumford's own project — the critical analysis of social myths and the constructive proposal of utopian alternatives — within a recognizable intellectual lineage. The Durkheimian tradition provided Mumford with a vocabulary for discussing how ideas (including utopian ideas) function as social forces rather than merely as individual fantasies.

## Relationship to Mumford's Utopian Framework

Durkheim's influence on *The Story of Utopias* operates at a methodological rather than substantive level. Mumford does not adopt Durkheim's specific sociological doctrines but rather uses the concept of collective representations to articulate his own theory of *idola*:

| Durkheim's Concept | Mumford's Adaptation |
|--------------------|---------------------|
| *Représentations collectives* | *Idola* — social myths shared by a community |
| Social facts as external constraints | Utopias as forces of reconstruction |
| Primitive classification systems | Modern social myths (the Country House, Coketown, the National State) |

Mumford's key innovation is to extend the Durkheimian framework from the analysis of past societies to the critique of present ones — and, crucially, to offer utopian thinking as a method for generating new, more adequate collective representations. Where Durkheim was content to describe how collective beliefs function, Mumford insists on evaluating them and proposing alternatives.

The connection between Durkheim's sociology and Mumford's utopianism lies in the recognition that social transformation requires the reconstruction of collective mental life — not merely changes in institutions or technology. Utopias, in this light, are exercises in the deliberate re-imagination of collective representations.
