# John Dewey

John Dewey (1859–1952) was an American philosopher, psychologist, and educational reformer associated with pragmatism and functional psychology. In *The Story of Utopias*, Lewis Mumford twice invokes Dewey's ideas — once at the very beginning of his argument and again in his concluding chapter — to frame utopian thinking as a legitimate mode of practical inquiry rather than idle fantasy.

## Key Ideas in Mumford's Account

Mumford deploys Dewey's thought in two distinct contexts that bookend his entire survey.

### The Pragmatic Theory of Inquiry (Chapter I)

At the opening of the book, Mumford uses a Dewey illustration to distinguish between escapist fantasy and constructive invention. He writes:

> "Speaking more generally, running away does not always mean a physical escape, nor does an 'attack' necessarily mean doing something practical 'on the spot.' Let us use Dr. John Dewey's illustration and suppose that a man is denied intercourse with his friends at a distance. One kind of reaction is for him to 'imagine' meeting his friends, and going through, in fantasy, a whole ritual of meeting, repartee, and discussion. The other kind of reaction, as Dr. Dewey says, is to see what conditions must be met in order to cement distant friends, and then invent the telephone." (001-017)

This illustration is the conceptual hinge on which Mumford's entire argument turns. The same desire — to connect with distant friends — can produce either fantasy (the daydream of meeting) or invention (the telephone). The distinction between these two modes — which Mumford labels **utopia of escape** and **utopia of reconstruction** — organizes his reading of every utopia from Plato to Wells.

Mumford adds a third possibility: "The so-called extrovert, the type of man who has no need for utopias, will satisfy his desire by talking to the nearest human being." (001-017). This third figure — the person who merely accommodates to whatever is nearest — represents, for Mumford, the genuinely limited response, since "from the very weakness and inconstancy of his aims, [he] is incapable of contributing anything but 'good nature' to the good life of the community" (001-017).

### Belief as a Condition of Realization (Chapter XII)

In the concluding chapter, Mumford returns to Dewey to defend the utopian tradition against its pragmatic critics. He writes:

> "Where the critics of the utopian method were, I believe, wrong was in holding that the business of projecting prouder worlds was a futile and footling pastime. These anti-utopian critics overlooked the fact that one of the main factors that condition any future are the attitudes and beliefs which people have in relation to that future — that, as Mr. John Dewey would say, in any judgment of practice one's belief in a hypothesis is one of the things that affect its realization." (012-060)

This pragmatist principle — that expectations shape outcomes — provides Mumford's final justification for the utopian enterprise. If our beliefs about what is possible condition what becomes possible, then the act of imagining a better world is itself a practical contribution to bringing it about. Utopia is not a blueprint to be followed literally; it is a hypothesis that, by being entertained, alters the conditions of its own test.

## The Intellectual Framework

Dewey's pragmatism gives Mumford a philosophical language for what earlier utopian thinkers did by instinct. The model is:

| Concept | Dewey's term | Mumford's application |
|---------|-------------|----------------------|
| Desire | Impulse toward an absent good | The longing for a better community |
| Fantasy | Imagination without analysis | Utopia of escape |
| Inquiry | Analysis of conditions + invention | Utopia of reconstruction |
| Verification | Testing the hypothesis in practice | Building experimental communities |

Mumford's survey of classic utopias can be read as an extended case study of each cell in this table — showing, through the history of utopian thought, what happens when the utopian impulse remains at the level of fantasy and what becomes possible when it is disciplined by inquiry.

## Place in the Utopian Tradition

Dewey himself did not write utopias, but his pragmatist philosophy provides the intellectual framework Mumford uses to rehabilitate utopian thinking as a serious social method. By treating ideals as hypotheses to be tested in practice, Dewey's instrumentalism bridges the gap between the classic utopian imagination and the practical reconstruction of communities that Mumford advocates. The recurrence of Dewey's name at both the first and last chapters of the book is not incidental: it signals that pragmatism is the philosophical backbone of Mumford's entire project.
