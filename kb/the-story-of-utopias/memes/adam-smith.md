# Adam Smith

**Adam Smith** (1723-1790) is the Scottish economist and moral philosopher whose *The Wealth of Nations* (1776) provided the intellectual foundation for classical economics and, in Mumford's account, stands at the head of the 19th-century lineage of economic thinkers whose "semi-scientific investigations" transformed utopian thought from political reform into economic analysis (011-005).

## The Wealth of Nations and Its Legacy

Smith's *The Wealth of Nations* articulated the principles of free markets, the division of labor, and the "invisible hand" — ideas that would shape not only economic theory but also the practical utopias of the industrial age. Mumford places Smith at the origin of a chain of influence:

> "In the nineteenth century the main accent was economic, and a number of movements arose which could be traced back to the semi-scientific investigations of Adam Smith, Ricardo, Proudhon, Malthus, Marx, and perhaps half a dozen other thinkers of outstanding importance, among whom we should perhaps include such latter day figures as Mill, Spencer, and Henry George" (011-005).

Smith thus stands as the initiator of a tradition that runs through Ricardo, Malthus, and [[karl-marx|Marx]] — a tradition that, for better and worse, shifted the center of gravity in social thought away from the comprehensive visions of [[plato|Plato]] and [[sir-thomas-more|More]] and toward specialized economic analysis.

## Smith and Individualist Utopia

A more specific engagement with Smith's legacy appears in Mumford's discussion of Theodor Hertzka's *Freeland* (1890). Hertzka, an Austrian economist, sought to create an individualist utopia on a cooperative foundation:

> "Hertzka was filled with sympathy and admiration for the doctrines that Adam Smith set forth in *The Wealth of Nations*; and he desired to realize a society in which the maximum amount of individual freedom and initiative would prevail, especially in industrial enterprises" (007-018).

Yet Hertzka's attempt to operationalize Smith's doctrines produced a paradox Mumford finds instructive:

> "In order to ensure freedom it is impossible to practice laissez faire; for the effect of laissez faire is to permit accidental aggregations of wealth and power to threaten the freedom that less fortunate individuals seek to enjoy" (007-018).

This paradox — that genuine freedom requires the active restraint of the forces Smith's own doctrines unleashed — is characteristic of the dilemma Mumford diagnoses across the 19th-century utopias. The "individualist Utopia on a social foundation" (007-018) that Hertzka attempted is in some sense the logical endpoint of Smith's project, and its impossibility reveals the inadequacy of purely economic frameworks for thinking about the good society.

## Smith in the Eutopian Frame

Mumford's placement of Smith is strategic. By tracing the economic turn in utopian thought to Smith's investigations, he establishes that the problem is not with economic analysis as such but with its tendency to dominate and displace other modes of valuation. Smith is not a villain in *The Story of Utopias* — he is a point of origin for a tradition that, in Mumford's assessment, produced powerful analytical tools at the cost of losing the holistic vision of the earlier utopians.

| Thinker | Contribution | Mumford's Assessment |
|---------|-------------|---------------------|
| Adam Smith | Market economics, invisible hand | Foundation of economic turn |
| Ricardo | Labor theory of value | Deepened economic analysis |
| Malthus | Population theory | Narrowed frame to scarcity |
| Marx | Historical materialism | Most comprehensive critique |
| Mill | Liberty, utilitarianism | Reform within existing order |

The challenge Mumford poses to Smith's inheritors is whether economic analysis can be re-integrated into a broader vision of [[good-life|the good life]] — a vision that attends to values, purposes, and the quality of human experience, not merely to the mechanisms of production and exchange.
