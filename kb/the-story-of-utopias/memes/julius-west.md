# Julius West

**Julius West** is the fictional protagonist of [[edward-bellamy|Edward Bellamy]]'s utopian novel *Looking Backward* (1888). A wealthy young man of late-nineteenth-century Boston, West serves as the narrative vehicle through which Bellamy critiques industrial capitalism and imagines a cooperative, state-managed society of the year 2000. [[lewis-mumford|Lewis Mumford]] examines West's journey as both a literary device and a revealing artifact of the utopian imagination's limits, treating Bellamy's vision as a "high-powered engine of repression" whose release valves remain hidden (008-037).

## The Device of Hibernation

Bellamy introduces West through a framing device that Mumford describes in detail:

> "Julius West is a person whom our Shawmut historian invents, to bridge the gap between the two eras, Julius West, a young man of wealth, sensitive to the ignominy of his position, and feeling that, as a 'rich man living among the poor, an educated man among the uneducated,' he 'was like one living in isolation among a jealous and alien race'" (008-025).

To overcome his insomnia, West sleeps in a vaulted room in the foundations of his house and is put into a trance by a hypnotist. Through what Mumford calls "a dramatic oversight," West hibernates for 113 years and awakens among strange faces in the year 2000. The love affair West begins in the old world is carried forward in the new through a descendant of the woman he meant to marry — a narrative convenience that Mumford treats as a necessary concession to the romance genre (008-025).

## Witness to the Great Trust

West's role in the novel is primarily that of an observer. His host, Dr. Leete, explains how American industrial concentration reached its logical conclusion:

> "In the United States of 1887 the growing organization of labor and the aggregation of capital into trusts were the two chief economic factors: Dr. Leete, Julius West's host, pictures how this aggregation and combination were continued until, by a mere shift of gears, 'the epoch of trusts had ended in The Great Trust'" (008-028).

The transition, as Bellamy presents it through Dr. Leete, was peaceful: public opinion had been prepared, the great corporations had trained everyone into acceptance of large-scale organization, and the final merger of all industry into a single national corporation "occurred without a jar." Every citizen became by virtue of citizenship an employee of the government (008-028).

## Mumford's Critique: The Missing Safety-Valve

Mumford is not persuaded by Bellamy's frictionless vision. Following West through the modern shop where goods are ordered by sample from a central warehouse, he observes an almost complete absence of personal contact:

> "This externalism, this impersonality, seems to characterize the whole scene. We follow Julius West and his new love, Edith, into a modern shop, where everything is displayed by sample, and an order for goods is sent to a central warehouse, and along with undoubted economies of space and time, we note that there is an almost complete absence of personal contacts or relationships: more than ever the worker has become a cog in the machine" (008-037).

Mumford argues that Bellamy has "invented a high-powered engine of repression" without showing the compensatory institutions — the "safety-valve" — that such a disciplined society would require. West, as the reader's surrogate, experiences the utopia's surfaces but never its hidden costs. In Mumford's judgment, Bellamy's twenty-first century would "be all that a modern city is, exaggerated" (008-037).

## Place in the Utopian Tradition

Julius West belongs to a lineage of utopian protagonists — alongside [[raphael-hythloday|Raphael Hythloday]] in [[sir-thomas-more|Thomas More]]'s *Utopia* — who function as narrative bridges between the reader's familiar world and an imagined alternative. Where Hythloday is a seasoned traveller reporting on a distant island, West is a time-traveller by accident, a man of his own era confronting its future. Both figures allow the author to defamiliarize contemporary institutions by juxtaposing them against a radically different social order.
