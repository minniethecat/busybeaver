# Idolum

**Idolum** (plural: *idola*) is [[lewis-mumford|Lewis Mumford]]'s term for the inner world of ideas — the subjective mental environment comprising philosophies, fantasies, rationalizations, projections, images, and opinions through which human beings pattern their behavior and make sense of the external world (001-009). The concept is central to *[[the-story-of-utopias|The Story of Utopias]]* (1922) and underlies his fundamental distinction between [[utopia-of-escape|utopias of escape]] and [[utopia-of-reconstruction|utopias of reconstruction]].

## Definition

Mumford introduces the term in Chapter I, coining it from the Latin *idolum* (image, form, apparition). He uses it to designate what philosophers call the subjective world and what theologians might call the spiritual world — encompassing "all the philosophies, fantasies, rationalizations, projections, images, and opinions in terms of which people pattern their behavior" (001-009). Crucially, Mumford insists that the idolum "has contours of its own which are quite independent of the material environment," meaning it operates according to its own internal logic rather than simply reflecting physical reality (001-009).

The idolum's power derives not from its correspondence to external facts but from the human willingness to regulate actions in its terms:

> "An idea is a solid fact, a theory is a solid fact, a superstition is a solid fact as long as people continue to regulate their actions in terms of the idea, theory, or superstition" (001-011).

He illustrates this with the historical belief that the world was flat — while factually false, it "was once upon a time more important than the 'fact' that it was round," keeping medieval sailors from venturing beyond sight of land "as effectively as would a string of gunboats or floating mines" (001-011).

## Functions

The idolum serves two fundamental purposes in human life, which Mumford identifies as the basis for his classification of utopian thought:

### Escape

The idolum functions as a substitute for the external world — "a sort of house of refuge to which we flee when our contacts with 'hard facts' become too complicated to carry through or too rough to face" (001-012). This escapist function seeks "an immediate release from the difficulties or frustrations of our lot" and leaves the external world unchanged (001-012).

### Reconstruction

The idolum also serves as the mechanism by which "the facts of the everyday world are brought together and assorted and sifted, and a new sort of reality is projected back again upon the external world" (001-012). This reconstructive function "attempts to provide a condition for our release in the future" — it seeks to change the world rather than merely escape it (001-012).

## Role in Utopian Theory

These two functions of the idolum give rise to Mumford's central classification:

| Function | Utopian Type | Orientation | Goal |
|----------|-------------|-------------|------|
| Escape | [[utopia-of-escape|Utopia of Escape]] | Backward-looking | Immediate release, world unchanged |
| Reconstruction | [[utopia-of-reconstruction|Utopia of Reconstruction]] | Forward-looking | Change the world, build new conditions |

> "The genuine alternative ... is not between living in reality and dreaming in utopia, but between an aimless utopia of escape and a purposive utopia of reconstruction" (001-013).

In a world "so full of frustrations," humans inevitably spend "a good part of our mental lives in utopia" — the question is only whether that utopian thinking is productive or merely compensatory (001-013).

## Historical Idola

Throughout *The Story of Utopias*, Mumford traces how successive historical idola have shaped civilization:

- **The idolum of the [[country-house-idolum|Country House]]**: The dominant cultural pattern that emerged from the Renaissance, instituting a dissociation between the ruling class and the broader community, and giving rise to the Acquisitive Society (010-021).

- **The idolum of [[coketown-idolum|Coketown]]**: The industrial age's contribution — an environment devoted "solely to the production of material goods," which is "no sort of environment for a good community" yet contains elements of relevance to the practical reconstruction of society (010-056).

- **The idolum of the [[national-state-idolum|National State]]**: The "social myth" that serves as "connecting tissue" between the Country House and Coketown, perpetuated through national education, advertising, and the cultural dominance of Megalopolis (010-060).

## Reconstruction of the Idolum

In his concluding vision, Mumford argues that genuine utopian reconstruction must address "a reconstruction of both the physical world and the idolum" (001-027). The utopian's task is distinguished from that of the mere inventor or industrialist precisely by this dual concern: it is not enough to change the material environment; the world of ideas must also be transformed. A good idolum, Mumford insists, "cannot be founded on the basis of a bad one" (012-072). This principle underlies the contributions of thinkers like [[plato|Plato]], whose *[[republic-plato|Republic]]* was the first systematic attempt to reconstruct the idolum, and [[sir-thomas-more|Sir Thomas More]], whose *[[utopia-more|Utopia]]* built a bridge between the medieval and modern idola.
