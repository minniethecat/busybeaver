# Pierre-Joseph Proudhon

## Introduction

Pierre-Joseph Proudhon (1809–1865) was a French philosopher, economist, and political theorist, best known for his declaration that "property is theft" (*Qu'est-ce que la propriété?*, 1840). In *The Story of Utopias*, [[lewis-mumford|Lewis Mumford]] places Proudhon within the lineage of nineteenth-century economic thinkers — alongside [[adam-smith|Adam Smith]], [[david-ricardo|Ricardo]], [[thomas-malthus|Malthus]], and [[karl-marx|Marx]] — whose work reshaped the direction of utopian thought.

## Proudhon in Mumford's Account

Mumford references Proudhon in Chapter XI as part of a broad survey of the intellectual currents that fed into utopian social thought in the nineteenth century. Where the early utopians had constructed comprehensive visions of ideal commonwealths, the nineteenth century saw the emergence of specialized economic analysis — a development Mumford records with a certain ambivalence:

> "In the nineteenth century the main accent was economic, and a number of movements arose which could be traced back to the semi-scientific investigations of Adam Smith, Ricardo, Proudhon, Malthus, Marx, and perhaps half a dozen other thinkers of outstanding importance" (011-005).

## Mutualism and the Critique of Property

Proudhon's distinctive contribution was the theory of **mutualism** — a form of economic organization based on reciprocity, free association, and the abolition of interest and rent. Unlike the communitarian utopians who proposed collective ownership, Proudhon envisioned a society of independent producers exchanging goods and services through a network of mutual banks and cooperative associations. His critique of property went beyond simple egalitarianism: he distinguished between property as a form of domination (which he condemned) and possession as a form of use-right (which he defended), a subtlety that set him apart from both state socialists and laissez-faire liberals.

## Tensions in the Utopian Tradition

Proudhon's place in Mumford's genealogy highlights a productive tension in utopian thought. His rejection of centralized authority anticipated the anarchist critique of state socialism, yet his detailed attention to economic mechanisms — credit, exchange, labor value — aligned him with the "semi-scientific" turn that Mumford both acknowledges and subtly critiques. For Mumford, the shift from comprehensive utopian vision to specialized economic analysis represented a narrowing of imaginative scope, even as it brought new rigor to the study of social conditions.

## Influence on Later Movements

Proudhon's ideas influenced a wide range of subsequent movements, from French syndicalism and [[georges-sorel|Georges Sorel]]'s revolutionary syndicalism to the [[fabian-society|Fabian]] tradition of gradual social reform in Britain. His emphasis on voluntary association and decentralized economic organization also resonated with the cooperative movement and with later experiments in [[guild-socialism|guild socialism]]. Mumford's own preference for regional planning and decentralized communities — what he called the [[regional-survey|regional survey]] method — shares certain elective affinities with Proudhon's mutualist vision, even if Mumford's intellectual lineage runs more directly through [[patrick-geddes|Patrick Geddes]] than through the French anarchist tradition.
