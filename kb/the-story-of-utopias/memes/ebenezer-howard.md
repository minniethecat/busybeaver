# Ebenezer Howard

**Ebenezer Howard** (1850-1928) was an English urban planner whose *Garden Cities of Tomorrow* (first published as *Tomorrow* in 1898) Lewis Mumford celebrates as "unique among utopian books in that its eutopia has been realized" (utopias appendix). The first Garden City at Letchworth, England, demonstrated that utopian principles could be translated into actual built environments.

## The Garden City

Howard's contribution is precisely the practical dimension that earlier utopian thinkers lacked. From Howard, "we can learn the importance of converting the idolum of eutopia into plans and layouts and detailed projections, such as a townplanner might utilize; and we may suspect that a eutopia which cannot be converted into such specific plans will continue, as the saying is, to remain up in the air" (012-068).

## Place in the Utopian Tradition

Mumford positions Howard in the lineage of reconstructive utopians that runs from Plato through More, Andreae, and the nineteenth-century socialists to the modern period. Each figure contributes a distinct element: Plato contributes the emphasis on education and breeding; More, the principle of shared possessions; Andreae, the integration of science and humanities; the nineteenth-century utopians, the recognition that ideals require economic foundations; and Howard, the demonstration that ideals can be converted into actual plans.

## Impact

The Garden City movement under Howard's influence began to challenge the Coketown idolum. Mumford notes that "the Coketown idolum has been disintegrating a little during the last two decades, under the influence of the garden cities movement" (010-045).
