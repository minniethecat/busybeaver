## Definition

A **social myth** (or **collective utopia**) is a shared [[idolum]]—an ideal projection held not by a single utopian author but diffused across an entire community. Unlike the classic literary utopias of [[plato]] or [[sir-thomas-more]], the social myth is "partly expressed in a hundred works and never perhaps fully expressed in one" (010-1029). It functions as the "ideal content of the existing order of things"—a collectively held picture of the good life that, by being "consciously formulated and worked out in thought," tends to "perpetuate and perfect that order" (010-1031).

Mumford introduces the concept at the conclusion of his survey of the classic utopias, signaling a transition from literary utopias to an examination of "certain social myths and partial utopias that have played an important part in the affairs of the Western World during the last few centuries" (001-193). The concept draws on the French sociological tradition—particularly [[emile-durkheim]]'s notion of collective representations and [[georges-sorel]]'s analysis of political myths—but Mumford extends it beyond "savage or ignorant groups" to insist that such idola are "an important part of every civilized person's luggage" (010-1035).

## Core Characteristics

Mumford identifies several defining traits of the social myth:

**Diffuse authorship.** Unlike a literary utopia authored by a single thinker, the social myth emerges organically from the shared mental life of a community. It is "a sort of diffused environment or atmosphere, which differs in 'chemical content' and in extension with each individual" (010-1035). No single text contains it; rather, it permeates institutions, customs, and everyday assumptions.

**Reality-shaping power.** The social myth is not idle fantasy. Mumford insists that "these social myths have been potent; they have given a pattern to our lives" (010-1215). They operate on the same plane as material facts: "An idea is a solid fact, a theory is a solid fact, a superstition is a solid fact as long as people continue to regulate their actions in terms of the idea, theory, or superstition" (001-141).

**Moral ambivalence.** Social myths "cannot be characterized as either good or bad: their nature is defined by their capacity to help men to react creatively upon their environment and to develop a humane life" (010-1033). Even "quite base and stupid people are frequently governed by ideals; indeed, it is the ideals that are in many cases responsible for their baseness and stupidity" (010-1033).

**Dual classification.** As with literary utopias, social myths can be divided into "myths of escape and myths of reconstruction" (010-1031). The myth of political freedom, for example, may serve as "an excellent refuge for disturbed consciences" when institutions fail—an escape function—while also providing a template for reforming those same institutions.

## Mumford's Three Modern Social Myths

In the latter half of *The Story of Utopias*, Mumford examines three dominant social myths of the modern period:

1. **The Country House.** The [[country-house-idolum]]—an aristocratic ideal of leisure, landed estate, and cultivated withdrawal formed during the Renaissance—"was built up during the Renaissance" and persisted as a powerful attractor for the middle classes (010-1159).

2. **Coketown.** The [[coketown-idolum]]—the industrial utopia of production, efficiency, and material accumulation—"was formed in the early part of the nineteenth century" and became the dominant pattern for urban industrial life (010-1159).

3. **The National State.** The [[national-state-idolum]] serves as the "connecting tissue" between the Country House and Coketown—"the social myth, the collective utopia, of the National State" (010-1159). It reconciles the aristocratic desire for distinction with the industrial drive for production by channeling both into national competition.

These three myths, Mumford argues, are not merely ideas on paper. They "have been potent; they have given a pattern to our lives; and they are the source of a great many evils that threaten, like stinking weeds, to choke the good life in our communities" (010-1215).

## The Path Beyond Social Myths

Mumford's critique of social myths is not a call to abandon myth-making altogether. He acknowledges that "we cannot put aside old myths without creating new ones" and that "the nearest we can get to rationality is not to efface our myths but to attempt to infuse them with right reason, and to alter them or exchange them for other myths when they appear to work badly" (012-1487).

The first step, he argues, is "to ignore all the fake utopias and social myths that have proved either so sterile or so disastrous during the last few centuries" (012-1485). This clearing of the ground opens space for what Mumford calls [[eutopia]]—the good place, a reconstructed idolum infused with the insights of the arts and sciences rather than the narrow imperatives of industrial capitalism or national competition.

The utopian tradition, properly understood, offers an alternative order of social myth: "In turning away from the social myths that hamper us, we do not jump blindly into a blankness: we rather ally ourselves with a different order of social myth which has always been vivified and enriched by the arts and sciences" (012-1489).
