# Giovanni Boccaccio

## Introduction

Giovanni Boccaccio (1313–1375) was an Italian writer, poet, and humanist, best known for the *Decameron* (c. 1353), a collection of one hundred tales told by a group of young Florentines who have fled the city to escape the Black Death. In *The Story of Utopias*, [[lewis-mumford|Lewis Mumford]] identifies Boccaccio's framing narrative as a foundational expression of **Country House culture** — the leisured retreat into a cultivated private world that Mumford treats as one of the enduring idols of Western civilization.

## The Decameron in Mumford's Analysis

In Chapter X, Mumford traces the literary lineage of the Country House as a cultural form, moving from Boccaccio's *Decameron* through [[alexander-pope|Alexander Pope]]'s "Rape of the Lock," [[george-meredith|George Meredith]]'s *The Egoist*, [[h-g-wells|H. G. Wells]]'s *Tono-Bungay*, [[bernard-shaw|Bernard Shaw]]'s *Heartbreak House*, to [[anton-chekhov|Anton Chekhov]]'s *The Cherry Orchard*:

> "We see pretty much the same outlines in the introduction to Boccaccio's *Decameron*; it is elaborately described in terms of that most complete of Country Houses, Hampton Court, in Pope's 'Rape of the Lock'; it is vividly pictured by Meredith in his portrait of *The Egoist*" (010-020).

For Mumford, the *Decameron* is not merely a literary precursor to Country House culture — it is the **archetypal instance**: a group of well-born young people retreating from a crumbling world to tell stories in a garden, creating a self-contained universe of refinement and pleasure while the city they left behind is ravaged by plague.

## The Country House as Utopian Surrogate

Mumford's engagement with Boccaccio is not primarily literary-historical but analytical. The Country House represents what Mumford calls the [[country-house-idolum|Country House idolum]] — an ideal of the good life centered on private cultivation, aesthetic refinement, and withdrawal from public responsibility. This idolum, Mumford argues, "has imposed its standards and its practices with such complete success upon the greater part of European civilization" (010-021) that it has shaped everything from architecture to economic consumption patterns.

The *Decameron* is significant because it presents the Country House ideal in its purest, most unselfconscious form. The brigata of storytellers in Boccaccio's frame narrative are not utopian planners — they have no program for remaking society, no vision of a reconstructed environment. They have simply found a way to live beautifully in the interstices of a world collapsing around them.

## Place in the Utopian Tradition

This is precisely what makes the *Decameron* — and Boccaccio's Country House culture more broadly — both fascinating and suspect in Mumford's eyes. The Country House represents the **[[utopia-of-escape|utopia of escape]]** in one of its most alluring forms: not the crude fantasy of a golden age or a distant paradise, but the achievable, cultivated life of the private estate. Yet for Mumford, unlike the **[[utopia-of-reconstruction|utopia of reconstruction]]**, the Country House offers no path toward the transformation of the whole — it can never be universalized, because its elegance depends on the existence of the conditions it flees.
