## Overview

The modern metropolis occupies a central place in Lewis Mumford's analysis of urban civilization in *The Story of Utopias*. It represents the dominant urban form of the industrial and post-industrial age — a vast, complex city that serves as the theater for the competing idola of the Country House, Coketown, and the National State [1045]. Mumford distinguishes the modern metropolis from both the classical Greek polis (an organic, human-scaled city-state) and from his concept of the Megalopolis (a parasitic, formless urban sprawl that has lost the qualities of genuine urban life).

## Historical Emergence

Mumford traces the emergence of the modern metropolis to the breakdown of the medieval order. Up to the fourteenth century in Western Europe, "the little fortified town, or the unfortified town that lay beneath the protection of a garrison on a hill, was the only other social unit" beyond the peasant's village and the Church [1045]. To have dreamed of "huge metropolises and far-flung armies and food brought from the ends of the earth" would have seemed wilder in those days than anything More pictured in his *Utopia* [1045]. The modern metropolis emerged as a new scale of human settlement, made possible by industrial technology, colonial expansion, and the concentration of economic and political power.

## The Metropolis and the Idola

The metropolis serves as the physical container and arena within which Mumford's three modern idola — the Country House, Coketown, and the Megalopolis — compete and coexist. The Country House ideal persists even "when quarters are taken up in the midst of the metropolis" [1107], where it attempts to compensate for its divorce from community through an abundance of physical goods. Coketown's factories and workers' quarters form the industrial zones of the metropolis. And the metropolis contains within itself the seeds of the Megalopolis — the tendency toward unlimited, formless expansion that ultimately destroys the qualities that make city life valuable.

## Culture and Education in the Metropolis

Mumford provides a notably satirical portrait of education in the metropolis, where the young citizen's early life "is spent in acquiring the tools by which paper may be used" [1187]. Subjects like literature, science, and gymnastics are added to the curriculum "on paper" — a bureaucratic formalism that substitutes documentation for genuine experience. The metropolitan student may "know the atomic formula of clay without ever having seen it in the raw earth" and "go through the masterpieces of poetic literature without having experienced a single emotion" adequate to appreciate them [1187].

## Beyond the Metropolis

Mumford's analysis points toward the need to transcend the metropolis as a form. His critique anticipates later developments in regional planning, garden cities, and the search for human-scaled urban environments. The question that the metropolis poses — whether urban civilization can serve genuine human needs or must inevitably degenerate into the parasitic Megalopolis — is one of the central concerns of his larger body of work on cities and civilization.
