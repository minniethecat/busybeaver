## Introduction

*The Time Machine* is a science fiction novella by H.G. Wells (1866-1946), first published in 1895. It is Wells's earliest work of utopian fiction and introduced the concept of purposeful time travel to modern literature. The story follows an unnamed Time Traveller who journeys to the year 802,701 AD, where he discovers a seemingly idyllic world inhabited by the gentle, childlike Eloi — only to learn that beneath the surface, the brutish Morlocks maintain the machinery that sustains civilization, and prey upon the Eloi in darkness.

[[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] identifies *The Time Machine* as Wells's earliest utopian excursion: "Mr. H.G. Wells, it is true, has made more than one excursion into an imaginary commonwealth: *The Time Machine* is his earliest and *The World Set Free* may possibly be considered as his latest" (010-023). For [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]], the novella represents a crucial stage in Wells's development as a utopian thinker — one that would culminate in *[[a-modern-utopia|A Modern Utopia]]*.

## Content and Key Ideas

The Time Traveller's journey to the far future reveals a world where humanity has diverged into two distinct species: the Eloi, who live a carefree existence on the surface, and the Morlocks, who toil underground, tending the machines that make the Eloi's leisure possible. The Eloi are beautiful but intellectually feeble; the Morlocks are physically repulsive but possess the practical intelligence needed to maintain industrial society.

The novella's central allegory concerns the ultimate consequence of class division: the leisured class (the Eloi) has so thoroughly separated itself from the working class (the Morlocks) that the two have become different species, locked in a predatory relationship. The Morlocks, once the oppressed workers, have become the masters in a hidden, underground economy — breeding the Eloi as livestock.

## Mumford's Assessment

[[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] reads *The Time Machine* through the lens of his [[country-house-idolum|Country House]] / [[coketown-idolum|Coketown]] dialectic. He writes:

> Mr. Wells in *The Time Machine* has given a picture of Coketown which is perhaps a little exuberant in some of its details — the picture of a happy and careless Country House population, living on the surface of the earth, amid all the graces of a jolly weekend, and that of the factory population, the Morlocks, living in the bowels of the earth and performing the necessary industrial functions. (011-049)

For [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]], the Eloi represent the [[country-house-idolum|Country House]] ideal taken to its extreme — a leisured class so detached from productive labor that it has lost all capacity for self-sufficiency. The Morlocks represent [[coketown-idolum|Coketown]] — the industrial working class — pushed underground, invisible, yet indispensable. The horror of Wells's vision lies in its recognition that the two classes cannot be separated: the leisure of one depends upon the labor of the other.

[[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] judges this presentation "a little exaggerated" (011-049), but it serves his argument that the separation of enjoyment from achievement — the defining feature of [[country-house-idolum|Country House]] culture — produces a deformed and ultimately unsustainable civilization.

## Place in the Utopian Tradition

*The Time Machine* occupies a distinctive position in the utopian tradition. Unlike the positive utopias of [[sir-thomas-more|More]] or Bellamy, it is a [[kakotopia|dystopia]]n warning — a vision of what happens when the tendencies of the present are allowed to run their course unchecked. Wells transforms the class divisions of Victorian England into a biological allegory, using the device of time travel to make the future consequences of present injustices visible and urgent.

Within [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]]'s taxonomy, *The Time Machine* functions as a "utopia of reconstruction" in negative — it critiques the existing order not by describing a better alternative but by projecting the existing order's logic to its horrifying conclusion. The novella paved the way for Wells's later, more constructive utopian works, particularly *[[a-modern-utopia|A Modern Utopia]]* (1905), which [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] calls "a fine and lucid product of the imagination" (010-023).
