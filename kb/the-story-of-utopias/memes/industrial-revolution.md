## Mumford's Treatment

The Industrial Revolution occupies a pivotal position in Lewis Mumford's account of utopian thought in *The Story of Utopias*. Rather than treating it as a discrete historical event, Mumford views it as the material and spiritual rupture that both produced and was interpreted by the modern utopian imagination. The term serves not merely as a period marker but as shorthand for the complex of technological, economic, and social transformations that created [[coketown-idolum|Coketown]]—the degraded industrial environment against which nineteenth-century utopianism defined itself.

## The Pre-Industrial Baseline

Mumford contrasts the industrial order with an earlier, more integrated pattern of rural life that the Industrial Revolution destroyed:

> Before the Industrial Revolution upset the balance of social power, there were little villages in England where, on a limited scale and to no very grand purpose, a quiet and placid and fairly jolly existence must have been the rule of things. These villages were those in which the land was either held in freehold by small proprietors, or where there still remained for the use of each inhabitant certain common pastures and wastes. (747-747)

This pre-industrial life, memorialized in the work of W. H. Hudson and William Cobbett, served as a touchstone for utopian critics who saw in the old agrarian order a model of human-scale community that industrialism had obliterated.

## The Enclosure Connection

Mumford traces the Industrial Revolution not to technological invention alone but to the prior enclosure of common lands, a process that created both the labor supply and the social dislocation that mechanized production required:

> When the medieval order broke down the great proprietors began to seize this common land; and during the eighteenth century, under the incentive of big-scale scientific agriculture, the seizure went on at a merry pace. The peasant without land was forced to migrate to the new towns, as the Hammonds have pictured in their graphic work on the Town Laborer; and the labor of the peasant and his family fed the machines which the Watts and Arkwrights were developing in the eighteenth century. Industrial progress and social poverty went hand in hand. (749-749)

This linkage between agricultural dispossession and industrial exploitation is central to Mumford's analysis: the Industrial Revolution was not an autonomous technological development but a reorganization of social power facilitated by legal and economic changes in land tenure.

## Coketown as Product

The physical and social environment produced by the Industrial Revolution was Coketown—Mumford's generic term for the factory-dominated industrial city, drawn from Dickens's *Hard Times*. In Coketown, all the degradations of the new order were concentrated:

> The period before the Industrial Revolution seemed in comparison a real utopia; and the key to this utopia was the land. (749-749)

Mumford's assessment of what industrial society offered its members is uncompromising:

> The incredible thing about the industrial revolution, indeed, is not that there were a few riots here and there against the use of machinery, but that the industrial population has not been in a state of continual insurrection, and that the industrial towns have not been looted and razed again and again. (1248-1248)

## The Country House and Industrial Consumption

Mumford identifies an unexpected relationship between the [[country-house-idolum|Country House]] pattern and the direction taken by industrialization:

> This renaissance idolum of the Country House, then, is powerful and complete: I know no other pattern which has imposed its standards and its practices with such complete success upon the greater part of European civilization. While the Country House was in the beginning an aristocratic institution, it has penetrated now to every stratum of society; and although we may not immediately see the connection, it is responsible, I believe, for the particular go and direction which the industrial revolution has taken. The Country House standards of consumption are responsible for our Acquisitive Society. (1069-1069)

This insight connects the cultural and economic dimensions of industrialization: the patterns of consumption established by the aristocratic Country House provided the demand structure that shaped industrial production.

## Science and Industry

Mumford emphasizes that the Industrial Revolution was not merely a change in production methods but the practical realization of theoretical advances in the physical sciences—a demonstration of the power of the [[world-of-ideas]] over the material world:

> If the essential relationship between the world of ideas and the world of action were ever in doubt, the industrial revolution, especially in its later phases, would be a final demonstration; for beneath the ostensible skyscrapers, subways, factories, telephone lines, and sewers of the modern industrial city lie the immaterial foundations of western physical science. (1363-1363)

## The Utopian Response

The Industrial Revolution was the condition to which virtually all nineteenth-century utopian thought responded. From [[robert-owen]]'s model factories to [[charles-fourier]]'s phalansteries, from [[karl-marx]]'s revolutionary program to [[edward-bellamy]]'s nationalized industry, the utopian imagination was shaped by the imperative to either reform, transcend, or overthrow the industrial order. Mumford observes:

> All that was common to these partisan utopias was a desire to get rid of positive evils such as overwork or starvation or irregularity of employment. (1248-1248)
