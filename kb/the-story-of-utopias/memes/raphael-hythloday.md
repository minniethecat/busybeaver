# Raphael Hythloday

**Raphael Hythloday** is the fictional Portuguese scholar-sailor who narrates the description of the island commonwealth in [[sir-thomas-more|Sir Thomas More]]'s *[[utopia-more|Utopia]]* (1516). For [[lewis-mumford|Lewis Mumford]], Hythloday is the "very emblem" of the disoriented Renaissance intellectual — a figure stranded between a decaying medieval order and the fresh possibilities opened by Greek learning and geographical discovery.

## The Renaissance Intellectual

Mumford presents Hythloday as the product of converging intellectual revolutions. He has abandoned the scholastic Aristotle and "through his conquest of Greek he has come into possession of that new learning which stems back to [[plato|Plato]]" (003-006). His mind teems with "the criticisms and suggestions of a strange, pagan philosophy." At the same time, he is a traveller who has sailed with Amerigo Vespucci, ready to tell "of a strange land on the other side of the world, where, as Sterne said of France, 'they do things better'" (003-006).

> "Conceive of the world of ideas which Greek literature had just opened up coming headlong against the new lands which the magnetic compass had given men the courage to explore, and utopia, as a fresh conception of the good life, becomes a throbbing possibility" (003-006).

## Social Critic

Hythloday's function in More's dialogue is to deliver a devastating critique of early sixteenth-century European society — a critique that Mumford finds startlingly contemporary:

> "Everything that Raphael brings up against the government of England in the Introduction to *Utopia* could be brought with almost equal force, I believe, against the very country which is to serve as a standard" (004-013).

Hythloday catalogues the injustices of his age: the enclosure of land for sheep pasture, the dispossession of peasants, unemployed soldiers turned to theft, extravagant luxury alongside desperate poverty, and a legal system that hangs thieves rather than addressing the conditions that produce them. Mumford observes that "people complain that the laws are not strict enough or that they are not enforced; and everyone stubbornly refuses to look at the matter through Raphael Hythloday's eyes and to see that the robbery and violence which are abroad are not a cause of bad times but a result of them" (003-009).

## Refusal of Power

When More's friend Peter Giles suggests that a man of Hythloday's talents should enter royal service, Hythloday refuses. His reasoning establishes a tension that runs through the entire utopian tradition — the relationship between intellectual critique and political power:

| Position | Argument | Implication |
|----------|---------|-------------|
| Giles (pragmatic) | A man of talent should advise the king | Reform from within is possible |
| Hythloday (utopian) | "He does not wish to be enslaved" (003-010) | The court corrupts; only root-and-branch change suffices |
| Mumford's reading | Hythloday is "the very emblem of some of our best spirits today" (003-011) | The intellectual's alienation is structural, not personal |

Hythloday's radical conclusion is that "as long as there is any property, and while money is the standard of all other things, I cannot think that a nation can be governed either justly or happily" (003-011). The only remedy is to follow "the practices of the Utopians."

## Mumford's Assessment

For Mumford, Hythloday embodies both the power and the limitation of the utopian stance. His comprehensive critique reveals the systemic character of social ills — they cannot be fixed one by one. But his retreat to the position of pure description ("he who knows one of their towns knows all of them" [004-006]) also reveals the utopian's characteristic difficulty: how to move from the ideal back to the actual.

Mumford himself echoes Hythloday's predicament. Writing in 1922, he finds that "rack and ruin have gone too far to admit of any sort of repair except that which proceeds from the bottom up" (003-011). The utopian intellectual, then and now, confronts the same question: whether to enter the compromised institutions of power or to hold out for comprehensive reconstruction.
