The Spencean Society, formally known as the Society of Spencean Philanthropists, was a radical political organization that emerged from the ideas of Thomas Spence (1750-1814), a self-educated London stationer who developed one of the most distinctive land-reform utopias of the early nineteenth century. Mumford identifies Spence's utopia, Spensonia, alongside Theodor Hertzka's Freeland, as one of "two, in particular, in which the common possession of land is the foundation of every other institution" (007-005).

Thomas Spence belonged to a remarkable generation of working-class intellectuals. "The early part of the nineteenth century is remarkable for the fact that men of common stock, usually self-educated, began to apply their wits to improving the conditions of the class to which they belonged; and in particular there was in London a peasant named William Cobbett, a tailor named Francis Place, and a stationer named Thomas Spence" (007-006). From his shop in High Holborn, Spence published pamphlets of "rough philosophy" called *Pig's Meat*, and in 1795 issued *A Description of Spensonia*, followed in 1801 by *The Constitution of Spensonia* (007-007).

Spence's utopia was grounded in the parish as the fundamental unit of community — "a compact portion of the country, designedly not too large that it may the more easily be managed by the inhabitants with respect to its revenues and the police" (007-009). His vision was characterized by "a rough, homespun quality," rooted in the experience of English village life where common lands had been preserved against enclosure (007-012).

At the core of Spence's thought lay a conviction shared with "Plato and all the other genuine utopians; namely, that in Thoreau's words less is accomplished by the thousands who are hacking at the branches of evil than by one who is striking at the root" (007-013). His followers, organized as the Spencean Philanthropists, carried this radical land-reform program into the turbulent politics of post-Napoleonic Britain, agitating for the common ownership of land and the establishment of parish-based democratic communities.
