# Syndicalism

## Definition

Syndicalism is a revolutionary labor movement that emerged in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, advocating direct worker control of industry and the means of production through trade unions (syndicats), with the general strike as its primary instrument of political action. In Lewis Mumford's *The Story of Utopias*, syndicalism appears as one of the many programs and projections that competed to answer the social problems of the industrial age.

## Syndicalism in Mumford's Catalog

Mumford groups syndicalism within a broad catalog of responses to the "labor problem" — the question of who should control industry and who should profit from its advances:

> It is enough to see here the common element in capitalism, copartnery, State Socialism, Guild Socialism, Cooperation, Communism, Syndicalism, the One Big Union, Trades Unionism, and the like; whether these movements represent actual facts, like capitalism, copartnery, or trades unionism, or whether they are simply projections, like Syndicalism and the One Big Union. (IX-1244)

Mumford's distinction is significant: he classifies syndicalism as a "projection" — a movement whose vision of industrial reorganization remained largely aspirational rather than institutionally realized. This places syndicalism within the utopian mode of thought, even though its proponents would have considered themselves practical revolutionaries rather than dreamers.

## Nineteenth-Century Utopian Affiliations

In explaining his selection of nineteenth-century utopias for extended treatment, Mumford acknowledges that his criteria shifted in that period: he chose utopias "on the basis of their association with temporal movements like state socialism, the single tax, and syndicalism" rather than purely on their literary completeness as ideal commonwealths (XII-1524). This reveals that for Mumford, syndicalism represents one of the bridges between the classic utopian literary tradition and the practical social movements of the industrial era — a channel through which utopian energy flowed into real-world political action.

## Beyond Industrial Machinery

Mumford's treatment of syndicalism, however, contains an implicit critique shared with all the programs in his catalog. His point is that the nineteenth century's obsessive focus on industrial ownership and control missed the deeper question:

> If a great many of these programs have had the notion that industrial machinery, under socialism or guildism or cooperation was to be used for the common benefit, what was lacking was any common notion as to what the common benefit was. (IX-1246)

Syndicalism, like the other movements Mumford catalogs, addressed the means of production but not the ends of life — a critique that runs throughout *The Story of Utopias* and points toward Mumford's own vision of eutopia as a reconstruction of the entire human environment, not merely its industrial arrangements.

## Syndicalism and the Utopian Form

The classification of syndicalism as a "projection" rather than an "actual fact" is methodologically revealing. It demonstrates Mumford's expansive definition of the utopian: a utopia, in his framework, need not be a literary description of an ideal commonwealth; it can be a program, a movement, or a social theory that projects a fundamentally different arrangement of human relations. This capacious understanding runs through Mumford's entire work — from his treatment of [[plato]]'s *Republic* as a utopia of the educated classes to his discussion of the [[country-house]] as an embodied utopia of the landed gentry. Syndicalism earns its place in *The Story of Utopias* not because it produced detailed architectural plans for the ideal factory but because it insisted, against the evidence of the present, that a fundamentally different industrial order was possible.

The utopian dimension of syndicalism was not lost on its contemporaries. [[georges-sorel]], the French theorist of revolutionary syndicalism, explicitly described the general strike as a "social myth" — an image capable of mobilizing action precisely because it fired the imagination, not because it corresponded to existing realities. Mumford's own utopian method shares this emphasis on the power of images and ideals to reshape human conduct, even as he distinguishes his own eutopian project from the revolutionary violence that syndicalist theory sometimes endorsed.
