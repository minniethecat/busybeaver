# Utopia of Reconstruction

The **utopia of reconstruction** is Lewis Mumford's term for the second fundamental type of utopian thinking, characterized by its orientation toward transforming the external world rather than merely escaping from it. It represents the reconstructive function of the idolum and serves as the foundation for Mumford's vision of genuine social progress.

## Definition

Mumford introduces the concept in Chapter I of *The Story of Utopias* as the productive counterpart to the utopia of escape. The utopia of reconstruction "is what its name implies: a vision of a reconstituted environment which is better adapted to the nature and aims of the human beings who dwell within it than the actual one; and not merely better adapted to their actual nature, but better fitted to their possible developments" (001-026).

Where the utopia of escape "leads backward into the utopian's ego, the second leads outward — outward into the world" (001-026). This directional distinction is fundamental: one turns inward toward fantasy, the other outward toward action.

## Scope of Reconstruction

Mumford is careful to define reconstruction broadly. He means "not merely a physical thing" but "a new set of habits, a fresh scale of values, a different net of relationships and institutions, and possibly — for almost all utopias emphasize the factor of breeding — an alteration of the physical and mental characteristics of the people chosen, through education, biological selection, and so forth" (001-027).

Crucially, "the reconstructed environment which all the genuine utopians seek to contrive is a reconstruction of both the physical world and the idolum" (001-027). This dual concern — transforming both material conditions and the world of ideas — is what distinguishes the utopian from "the practical inventor and the industrialist" (001-027).

## Intellectual Heritage

Mumford places the utopia of reconstruction within a long intellectual tradition. He quotes Anatole France's affirmation:

> "Without the Utopians of other times, men would still live in caves, miserable and naked. It was Utopians who traced the lines of the first city. … Out of generous dreams come beneficial realities. Utopia is the principle of all progress, and the essay into a better future" (001-027).

The classic utopias — Plato's Republic, More's Utopia, Andreae's Christianopolis, and the nineteenth-century utopian socialists — are all, in Mumford's framework, exercises in reconstruction, however incomplete.

## Eutopia

Mumford draws on Patrick Geddes's observation that Thomas More's "Utopia" is a pun on both *Outopia* (no-place) and *Eutopia* (the good place) (012-003). The utopia of reconstruction, properly understood, is **eutopia** — not an impossible no-place but a good place that can be built.

"The choice is not between eutopia and the world as it is, but between eutopia and nothing — or rather, nothingness" (012-004). This existential urgency drives Mumford's insistence that utopian thinking is not a luxury but a necessity for civilization's survival.

## Practical Dimensions

The utopia of reconstruction must be grounded in concrete reality. Mumford argues that authentic eutopian thinking requires:

- **Regional survey**: "We must return to the real world, and face it, and survey it in its complicated totality. Our castles-in-air must have their foundations in solid ground" (012-030).
- **Institutional awareness**: "Every institution has a momentum of its own," and the eutopian must account for "the transition from one set of institutions to another" (012-062).
- **Specific plans**: From Ebenezer Howard's Garden Cities, we learn "the importance of converting the idolum of eutopia into plans and layouts and detailed projections, such as a townplanner might utilize" (012-068).
