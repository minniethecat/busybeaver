## Biography

Gerrard Winstanley (1609-1676) was an English reformer, political philosopher, and activist during the period of the English Civil War and the Commonwealth. Originally a cloth merchant in London, Winstanley was ruined by the economic disruption of the Civil War and retreated to the countryside in Surrey, where he worked as a hired laborer. This experience of dispossession and rural poverty shaped his radical vision for the reconstruction of English society.

In April 1649, Winstanley and a small group of followers began cultivating common land on St. George's Hill in Surrey, declaring that the earth was "a common treasury for all." This community, known as the **Diggers** (or True Levellers), was one of the most radical expressions of the egalitarian ferment unleashed by the Civil War. Though the Digger colonies were soon suppressed by local landowners and the authorities, Winstanley continued to write and publish his ideas until the Restoration.

## *The Law of Freedom* — A Minor Utopia

Winstanley's major theoretical work, *The Law of Freedom in a Platform* (1652), is what Mumford calls a "minor utopia." Dedicated to Oliver Cromwell, the work proposed a comprehensive reorganization of English society based on the common ownership of land and the abolition of private property, wage labor, and the buying and selling of goods. Lewis Mumford situates Winstanley within the tradition of agrarian utopianism that recognized land as the foundation of civil society:

> The importance of land in the constitution of civil society was emphasized by the Diggers of Cromwell's time; one of them, Gerard Winstanley, wrote a minor utopia to prove that the land should be held in common; and this view was reinforced — without the communism — in a purely political utopia called *Oceana* by Sir James Harrington, who lived during the same period. (751)

Mumford's pairing of Winstanley and Harrington is revealing. Both recognized land as the material basis of political power — Harrington advocating for a regulated distribution that would balance power among classes, Winstanley arguing for outright common ownership. Together, they represent the two poles of seventeenth-century English thinking about the relationship between property and polity.

## The Digger Vision

The Digger program, as articulated in Winstanley's pamphlets, went beyond land reform to encompass a vision of social and spiritual transformation:

| Aspect | Winstanley's Position |
|--------|----------------------|
| Land ownership | All land held in common; private property abolished |
| Labor | All able-bodied persons to work; no wage labor |
| Government | Annual elections; all men over 20 to vote and hold office |
| Education | Universal education for all children |
| Religion | Rejection of a professional clergy; direct spiritual experience |
| Trade | Abolition of buying and selling; goods distributed from common storehouses |

Winstanley's utopia was unusual for its time in deriving from a fusion of radical Christian mysticism and practical agrarian experience. Where More's *Utopia* was the product of a humanist scholar writing in Latin for an elite audience, Winstanley wrote in plain English for the common people, and his utopia was meant to be realized immediately — not on a distant island but on the commons of Surrey.

## Relationship to Land-Centered Utopianism

Mumford places Winstanley at a pivotal point in the history of land-centered utopian thought. In the medieval period, the commons had been the material foundation of peasant independence. The enclosure of common land — which Mumford, following the Hammonds, describes as a seizure that "went on at a merry pace" during the eighteenth century — drove peasants into the new industrial towns, where their labor fed the machines of the Industrial Revolution.

In this context, "the period before the Industrial Revolution seemed in comparison a real utopia; and the key to this utopia was the land" (751). Winstanley gave this intuition its most radical theoretical expression: the land, as a common treasury, was the foundation of freedom. Without access to land, the common person was reduced to dependence and wage slavery.

The legacy of this insight runs through the utopian tradition — from Harrington's *Oceana* to Thomas Spence's *Spensonia*, from the land reform movements of the nineteenth century to the Garden City movement of Ebenezer Howard. In each case, the organization of land tenure is recognized as foundational to the organization of social life.
