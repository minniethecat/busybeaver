# Aristotle

**Aristotle** (384–322 BC) is the Greek philosopher whose empirical approach to politics, ethics, and natural science provides an essential counterpoint and complement to the utopian tradition Mumford traces. Unlike [[plato|Plato]], his teacher, Aristotle's surviving utopian fragments are scant — but his framework for thinking about the [[good-life|good life]] and community runs throughout *The Story of Utopias*.

## The Good Life Defined

Mumford repeatedly returns to Aristotle's formulation as a touchstone:

> "Men come together, says Aristotle, to live; they remain together in order to live the good life" (012-055).

This definition anchors Mumford's entire critical project. The determination of the good life is, he argues, "the only check and balance that we can have upon Coketown" (012-055). Aristotle's insight that human community is not merely instrumental — not just for survival but for flourishing — becomes the standard against which every subsequent utopia is measured.

## The Greek Utopian Context

Mumford notes that Aristotle's own utopian contributions must be understood within a broader Greek tradition:

> "Aristotle tells us about an ideal state designed by one, Phaleas, who believed like Mr. Bernard Shaw in a complete equality of property; and from Aristotle, too, we learn of another utopia which was described by the great architect, city planner, and sociologist — Hippodamus" (001-012).

From Aristotle we also learn the names and outlines of utopian thinkers — **Phaleas of Chalcedon** and **Hippodamus of Miletus** — who would otherwise be entirely lost to history.

## Plato's Classes and the Aristotelian Critique

Mumford notes that "Aristotle criticizes Plato in terms of a more simple system of democracy; but Plato did not mean to institute a fixed order" (002-056). The disagreement between the two philosophers is not as sharp as it appears: both recognized that human beings have different capacities, and both sought a social order that would allow each to find their proper place.

However, Mumford emphasizes that Plato's Republic, not Aristotle's Politics, is the cornerstone of the utopian tradition:

> "When we look at the utopias that Phaleas and Hippodamus and Aristotle have left us, and compare them with the Republic of Plato, the differences between them melt into insignificance and their likenesses are apparent" (001-014).

## The Parting of Knowledge

In Mumford's analysis, Aristotle plays a second, historically decisive role: the separation of specialized knowledge from general human wisdom:

> "When Aristotle divided his writing into the exoteric and esoteric groups, into the popular and the scientific, he definitely recognized the existence of two separate branches of literature, two different ways of taking account of the world" (012-059).

This division, Mumford argues, initiated a process by which "the separate sciences increasingly isolate themselves from the general body of knowledge," a process that reached completion in the twentieth century, when "philosophy, once the compendium of the sciences, has disappeared except as a sort of impalpable, viscous residue" (012-057).

## Aristotle and the Limits of Means

In the concluding movement of the book, Mumford returns to Aristotle's analysis of slavery — and its prophetic quality:

> "Although Aristotle, for instance, predicted that slavery would come to an end on the condition that the shuttle should weave by itself and the lyre play without human hand, no one in the Greek community of his time saw very much likelihood of improvement through mechanical inventions" (012-030).

This passage illuminates the relationship between Aristotle and [[francis-bacon|Bacon]]: Aristotle could conceive of the material means but could not envision their realization; Bacon envisioned the means but lost sight of the ends. The full utopian project, for Mumford, requires both.
