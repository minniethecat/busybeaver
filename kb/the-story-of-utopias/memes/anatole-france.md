# Anatole France

## Introduction

Anatole France (1844-1924) was a French poet, journalist, and novelist, awarded the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1921. A central figure of the French literary establishment in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, France was known for works such as *Le Crime de Sylvestre Bonnard* (1881), *Thaïs* (1890), and *L'Île des Pingouins* (1908). In *The Story of Utopias*, Lewis Mumford quotes France in the opening chapter to establish the foundational role of utopian thinking in human progress.

## France in Mumford's Account

In Chapter I, as Mumford develops his distinction between "utopias of escape" and "utopias of reconstruction," he enlists France's words to affirm the transformative power of utopian vision:

> As Anatole France says: "Without the Utopians of other times, men would still live in caves, miserable and naked. It was Utopians who traced the lines of the first city. … Out of generous dreams come beneficial realities. Utopia is the principle of all progress, and the essay into a better future." (001-027)

This passage anchors Mumford's central thesis: that utopian thinking is not idle fantasy but the essential precondition of social progress. France's formulation — that utopia is the "principle of all progress" — provides Mumford with a rhetorical foundation for his entire survey of utopian thought. The quote is strategically placed early in the book to disarm the common objection that utopian speculation is impractical or escapist.

Mumford broadens France's defense by arguing that all material improvements — "Every attempt that has been made to domesticate animals, cultivate plants, dredge rivers, dig ditches, and in modern times, apply the energy of the sun to mechanical instruments" — are, in their origin, acts of utopian imagination made real (001-027). The reconstructed environment that utopians envision is a reconstruction of both "the physical world and the idolum" — the inner world of ideas, values, and habits.

## France's Skeptical Humanism

France's own worldview was marked by a gentle skepticism and a deep humanism, qualities that align him with the utopian tradition Mumford champions. A Dreyfusard who defended Alfred Dreyfus and a lifelong critic of institutional authority, France embodied the Enlightenment conviction that reason and sympathy could gradually reshape social life. His novel *The White Stone* (1905) itself contains a utopian vision of a future socialist commonwealth, demonstrating that his defense of utopianism was not merely rhetorical but a theme he explored in his own literary work. For Mumford, France's authority as both a major literary figure and a defender of the utopian imagination makes his testimony especially valuable at the outset of the book's argument.
