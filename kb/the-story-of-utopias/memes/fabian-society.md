# Fabian Society

The **Fabian Society** is a British socialist organization founded in 1884, named after the Roman general Fabius Maximus Cunctator, whose strategy of gradual attrition rather than decisive battle the Society adopted as its political metaphor. The Fabians advocated gradual, parliamentary reform rather than revolutionary transformation, pursuing what [[lewis-mumford|Lewis Mumford]], through his discussion of [[bernard-shaw|George Bernard Shaw]] — the Society's most publicly visible member — treats as a "method" approach linking utopian ideals to practical politics.

## In Mumford's Account

Mumford does not discuss the Fabian Society directly, but his treatment of Bernard Shaw places Fabian ideas at two distinct points in the utopian tradition. Early in *The Story of Utopias*, Mumford identifies Shaw as a modern descendant of the classical egalitarian tradition:

> "Aristotle tells us about an ideal state designed by one, Phaleas, who believed like Mr. Bernard Shaw in a complete equality of property" [212-PN].

This brief genealogy connects the Fabian program — the collective ownership of the means of production, achieved through democratic legislation — to one of the oldest propositions in utopian thought: that economic equality is the necessary foundation of the good community. Significantly, Mumford places Phaleas and Hippodamus alongside Plato as the founding figures of the utopian tradition, noting that "when we look at the utopias that Phaleas and Hippodamus and Aristotle have left us, and compare them with the Republic of Plato, the differences between them melt into insignificance and their likenesses are apparent" [214-PN]. By linking Shaw to Phaleas, Mumford situates Fabian thought within the core utopian lineage rather than treating it as a modern deviation.

Shaw appears a second time in Mumford's anatomy of Country House culture, alongside [[h-g-wells|H. G. Wells]] and [[anton-chekhov|Anton Chekhov]], as one of the literary witnesses to the decay of the aristocratic order that utopia must overcome. Mumford writes that "whether Mr. W. H. Mallock holds the pattern of Country House culture up to us in *The New Republic* or Anton Chekhov penetrates its aimlessness and futility in *The Cherry Orchard*, The Country House is one of the recurrent themes of literature" [1067-PN], and he names Shaw's *Heartbreak House* (1919) alongside works by Boccaccio, Pope, Meredith, and Wells as key texts in this literary tradition. Shaw's portrayal of cultured impotence among a leisure class drifting toward catastrophe — Ellie Dunn, Captain Shotover, and the Hushabye family waiting for the bombs to fall — exemplifies for Mumford the critical function that Fabian sensibilities brought to bear on English society.

## Fabian Method and Utopian Tradition

The Fabian approach to social transformation differed markedly from the grand systematizers of the nineteenth-century utopian tradition. Where [[charles-fourier|Charles Fourier]] prescribed precise architectural and social blueprints, and [[edward-bellamy|Edward Bellamy]] projected a comprehensively reorganized future, the Fabians — Shaw, [[sydney-webb|Sidney Webb]], Beatrice Webb, and Graham Wallas among them — embedded their utopianism in policy pamphlets, statistical surveys, and platform speeches. Their incrementalism represented a methodological innovation within the broader utopian tradition: the conviction that the path from the actual to the ideal could be paved with ordinary legislative acts.

Mumford's brief but pointed references to Shaw suggest an appreciation for this approach. By linking Shaw to Phaleas — the most practically minded of the ancient Greek utopian thinkers — Mumford implicitly recognizes Fabianism as a legitimate expression of the utopian impulse, one that seeks not to escape the world but to reconstruct it through its own political institutions. At the same time, Mumford's placement of Shaw within the Country House tradition hints at a Fabian limitation: the Society's membership was drawn predominantly from the educated middle class, and its sensibility remained, in certain respects, that of the very order it sought to reform.

## The Fabian Legacy

The Fabian Society's influence extended far beyond its modest membership. It was instrumental in the founding of the London School of Economics (1895), the establishment of the *New Statesman* (1913), and the intellectual formation of the British Labour Party. Fabian ideas about the minimum wage, the welfare state, and the nationalization of key industries shaped the policy architecture of twentieth-century Britain. In the context of Mumford's framework, Fabianism represents both the strengths and limitations of the gradualist approach: its concrete policy achievements were substantial, yet its vision remained — in Mumford's terms — confined to the "compartment of politics and economics" rather than reaching toward a comprehensive reconstruction of community life.
