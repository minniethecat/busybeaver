# Thorstein Veblen

**Thorstein Veblen** (1857-1929) is the American economist and sociologist whose *The Theory of the Leisure Class* (1899) provides [[lewis-mumford|Lewis Mumford]] with a critical vocabulary for analyzing the cultural degradation produced by the Country House [[idolum]]. Veblen's concepts of "conspicuous consumption," "conspicuous waste," and "pecuniary culture" run through Mumford's diagnosis of how Western civilization substituted the Goods Life for the [[good-life]].

## The Theory of the Leisure Class

Mumford draws on Veblen's masterwork at several critical junctures. In discussing [[h-g-wells|H.G. Wells]]'s utopian economics, Mumford notes that the modern system of capital accumulation generates "the waste and leakage that arises in present-day society through what Mr. Thorstein Veblen calls the conspicuous waste — the futile expenditures — of the leisured classes" (008-015). This is not merely a descriptive borrowing; Veblen's analysis underpins Mumford's central argument that the existing economic order systematically diverts productive capacity toward wasteful display.

> "The good life, as I have said elsewhere, was the Goods Life: it could be purchased" (010-015).

## The Country House Idolum

Veblen's influence is most evident in Mumford's chapter on the Country House, which Mumford himself suggests "might well be prefaced by Mr. Thorstein Veblen's *Theory of the Leisure Class*, a satire which seems to me unique in scholarship and originality" (013-012). Mumford traces how the Country House — the architectural and cultural expression of a rentier class — reshaped every domain of life:

| Domain | Medieval Ideal | Country House Ideal | Veblen's Concept |
|--------|---------------|-------------------|------------------|
| Industry | Craftsmanship for use | Production for sale | Pecuniary interests displace industrial interests |
| Art | Integrated craft tradition | "Stylicism" and antique imitation | Conspicuous consumption of "taste" |
| Architecture | Castle and monastery (defense/salvation) | Palace and country house (display) | Vicarious leisure made visible |
| Work | Getting a living from one's work | Getting money to escape work | The leisure class ideal |

Mumford recounts a furniture manufacturer who "seemed as though he had been reading Thorstein Veblen's *Theory of the Leisure Class*" (010-013) — the craftsman's innate knowledge of sound design had been subordinated to the market's demand for conspicuous "style."

## Science and Idle Curiosity

Mumford also draws on Veblen's *The Place of Science in Civilization* to argue that "science arises out of idle curiosity" and that "science, studied and advanced for its own sake, is surely one of the great playthings of the race" (012-028). This apparently digressive reference serves a strategic purpose: Veblen's account of science as play — as the pursuit of knowledge for its own intrinsic satisfaction — models the integration of work and [[good-life|the good life]] that Mumford's [[eutopia]] aims to achieve.

The Veblenian insight is that a civilization that subordinates all activity to pecuniary calculation not only wastes resources on conspicuous display but also starves the very sources of innovation — idle curiosity, craft pride, the joy of pure perception — on which genuine progress depends.
