A **collective utopia** or **social myth** is, in [[lewis-mumford|Lewis Mumford]]'s analysis, an [[idolum]] that exercises "considerable influence upon the actual life of the community" without having been fully expressed in any single work (009-1029). These collective frameworks distinguish themselves from the classic literary utopias — which Mumford sometimes calls "utopias of the study" — by their power to shape everyday behavior, institutions, and political life.

## Definition

Mumford introduces the concept in Chapter IX, defining collective utopias as those idola "which have been partly expressed in a hundred works and never perhaps fully expressed in one" (009-1029). He notes the connection to [[georges-sorel|Georges Sorel]]'s concept of the social myth, writing that "in practice it is sometimes rather hard to tell where the Utopia leaves off and the social myth begins" (009-1029). The two most powerful collective utopias Mumford identifies in modern society are **[[coketown|Coketown]]** — the industrial utopia of efficiency and production — and the **[[country-house|Country House]]** — the aristocratic utopia of leisure and land ownership.

## The National State as Social Myth

The collective utopia of the **[[national-state|National State]]** functions, in Mumford's framework, as a connecting tissue between Coketown and the Country House: "in order that each might be realized in our daily life, it was necessary that some connecting tissue be manufactured to keep them together. This tissue was the social myth, the collective utopia, of the National State" (011-1159). The National State's power derives precisely from its character as a collective rather than individual utopia — it is a shared [[idolum]] embedded in institutions, education, and everyday assumptions.

## Partisan Utopias

In contrast to comprehensive utopian visions, the nineteenth century saw the rise of **partisan utopias** — reform movements each "strenuously bent on realizing its private and partisan utopia" (011-1236). These included [[socialist-movement|socialism]], the [[cooperative-movement|cooperative movement]], the [[trade-unionism|trade unions]], and the [[single-tax]] movement. Mumford observes that these partisan utopias, despite achieving specific gains like consumer cooperation, ultimately proved too narrow: "for lack of any definite and coherent scheme of values, [they] crumble away as soon as they meet the opposition of such powerful collective utopias as Coketown or the Country House" (011-1252).

> "These utopias were so concerned to alter the shell of the community's institutions that they neglected to pay attention to the habits of the creature itself — or its habitat." (011-1286)

## Mumford's Critique

Mumford identifies the fatal weakness of the partisan utopias as **externalism** — the assumption that changing institutions alone would create a good society. The collective utopias of Coketown and the Country House proved more powerful precisely because they operated at the level of the [[idolum]], shaping not just laws but desires, values, and the very image of the good life. The only force capable of challenging these entrenched collective utopias, Mumford suggests, is a comprehensive vision that addresses both material and mental reconstruction — what he would later call **eutopia**.
