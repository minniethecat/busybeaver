## Introduction

"The Country House" is not a published literary work but a **critical concept** developed by [[lewis-mumford|Lewis Mumford]] in *The Story of [[utopia-more|Utopia]]s* (1922). In Chapter X, [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] identifies the [[country-house-idolum|Country House]] as one of the principal **idola** ([[national-state-idolum|social myth]]s) of the modern age — a utopian pattern that, along with [[coketown-idolum|Coketown]] and the [[megalopolis|Megalopolis]], has profoundly shaped [[julius-west|West]]ern civilization. [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] describes how "the [[country-house-idolum|Country House]] and [[coketown-idolum|Coketown]] became the utopias of the Modern Age; and how they made the world over in their image" (010-002).

## Content and Analysis

[[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] traces the **historical origins** of the [[country-house-idolum|Country House]] to the Renaissance, when European elites "ceased to build castles to protect themselves against physical dangers; and they left off entering monasteries in order to fortify their souls for the Hereafter" (010-003). Both spiritual and temporal life shifted to this new institution, whose architectural stamp can still be seen in Oxford colleges, banks, and political edifices built in the Roman and Grecian style.

The **physical and cultural characteristics** of the [[country-house-idolum|Country House]] are vividly illustrated through François Rabelais's *Gargantua*, specifically the Abbey of Thélème, where "everyone does nothing except follow his own free will and pleasure" (010-004). [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] notes that the [[country-house-idolum|Country House]] is one of the "recurrent themes of literature," appearing in Boccaccio's *Decameron*, Pope's "Rape of the Lock," Meredith's *The Egoist*, Wells's *Tono-Bungay*, [[bernard-shaw|Shaw]]'s *Heartbreak House*, and Chekhov's *The Cherry Orchard* (010-005).

[[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] argues that the [[country-house-idolum|Country House]] is "the precise opposite of everything that [[plato|Plato]] looked upon as desirable in a good community" (010-006). It is concerned "not with the happiness of the whole community but with the felicity of the governors," and its chief principles are **possession and passive enjoyment** (010-007). Possession is based upon "privilege and not upon work," with land title "historically obtained for the most part through force and fraud" (010-008).

The Country House ideal is that of a **"completely functionless existence"** — or at best, an existence in which "all the functions that properly belong to a civilized man shall be carried on by functionaries" (010-008). In literature and the fine arts, a "gourmandizing habit of mind" prevails, emphasizing "taste" — "a certain capacity to discriminate among sensory stimuli" — rather than creative participation (010-009).

Architecturally, the [[country-house-idolum|Country House]] is **derivative**: everything "has either been stolen or purchased from the original makers; and what has not been stolen or purchased has been basely copied" (010-010). [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] observes that "the insatiability of the [[country-house-idolum|Country House]] to possess art is only equalled by its inability to create it" (010-011).

## Mumford's Assessment

[[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] acknowledges the **valid contributions** of the [[country-house-idolum|Country House]]: its regard for "the decent graces of life," ease in manners, "fine flow of conversation and the clash of wits and a sensitiveness to beautiful things" (010-012). Even by emphasizing appreciation, the [[country-house-idolum|Country House]] called attention to permanent standards common across civilizations.

However, its **fatal weakness** is that "enjoyment rested upon achievement, and was indeed inseparable from achievement" — yet the [[country-house-idolum|Country House]] "strove to put achievement in one compartment and enjoyment in another" (010-013). The result was that craftsmen who no longer enjoyed fine arts could no longer create them, while masters of the [[country-house-idolum|Country House]] found enjoyment "came too easily," leading to a succession of decorative styles — Chinese, Indian, Persian, Egyptian — with "nothing to settle to, because there is no task to be done" (010-013).

The **economic outcome** is "to exaggerate the demand for goods, and to cause an enormously wasteful duplication of the apparatus of consumption" (010-014). If happiness is acquired through obtaining comforts and luxuries, "there is really no limit to the business of getting and spending" (010-014).

## Place in Utopian Tradition

The Country House is not a classic literary utopia but a **[[national-state-idolum|social myth]]** — one of the "[[social-myth|collective utopia]]s" that [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] distinguishes from the formal utopias of [[plato|Plato]], [[sir-thomas-more|More]], and Andreae. It represents an [[world-of-ideas|idolum]] that has "penetrated now to every stratum of society" and is "responsible for the particular go and direction which the industrial revolution has taken" (010-015). [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] identifies the [[country-house-idolum|Country House]]'s standards of consumption as the root cause of the "[[acquisitive-society|Acquisitive Society]]."

In [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]]'s framework, the [[country-house-idolum|Country House]], [[coketown-idolum|Coketown]], and the [[national-state-idolum|National State]] form an interconnected triad of destructive [[national-state-idolum|social myth]]s that must be dismantled before genuine utopian reconstruction can begin.
