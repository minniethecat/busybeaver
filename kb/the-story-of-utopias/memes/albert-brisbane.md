# Albert Brisbane

Albert Brisbane (1809–1890) was an American social theorist and the principal popularizer of Charles Fourier's utopian socialist ideas in the United States. In *The Story of Utopias*, Lewis Mumford draws on Brisbane's interpretation of Fourier to elucidate the French utopian's distinctive psychological theory — the idea that social institutions should channel human passions rather than suppress them.

## Brisbane in Mumford's Account

Mumford invokes Brisbane in Chapter VI, quoting Brisbane's introduction to Fourier's philosophy to explain the relationship between human passions and social design. He writes:

> "Fourier takes these passions as 'given'; his utopia is not designed to 'effect any change in our passions … their direction will be changed without changing their nature.' As Brisbane says in his Introduction to Fourier's philosophy, social institutions are to these passional forces what machinery is to material forces. A good community, according to Fourier, is one which will bring all these passions into play, in their complex actions and interactions." (006-013)

This passage is central to Mumford's reading of Fourier. Unlike utopians who imagine perfected human beings, Fourier starts with passions as they actually are — competitive, ambitious, sensual, curious — and asks what kind of community would organize these forces productively rather than suppress them. Brisbane's mechanical metaphor — institutions as machinery for passions — captures this engineering approach to social design with striking clarity.

## Fourierism in America

After studying with Fourier in Paris, Brisbane returned to the United States and published *The Social Destiny of Man* (1840), which adapted Fourier's complex system for an American audience. Fourier's "phalanxes" — self-sufficient cooperative communities of approximately 1,620 people, organized around the principle of attractive labor — found fertile ground in a nation already experimenting with intentional communities, from the Shakers to Brook Farm.

Brisbane's advocacy inspired the creation of numerous Fourierist communities across the United States during the 1840s, including the North American Phalanx in New Jersey and Brook Farm's brief but consequential conversion to Fourierist principles in Massachusetts. Mumford notes Brisbane's *General Introduction to the Social Sciences* in his bibliography, alongside the standard French editions of Fourier and Julia Franklin's English *Selections from the Works of Fourier*.

## The Machinery Metaphor

Brisbane's formulation — that "social institutions are to these passional forces what machinery is to material forces" — contains in miniature the Fourierist wager. The metaphor implies:

| If... | Then... |
|-------|---------|
| Passions are material forces (like steam or water power) | They cannot be eliminated, only channeled |
| Social institutions are machinery | They can be designed well or badly |
| A good machine harnesses energy productively | A good community channels passions toward harmony |
| A bad machine wastes or destroys energy | A bad community suppresses passions, creating misery |

This framework distinguishes Fourier from both the moralistic tradition (which would reform passions) and the libertarian tradition (which would simply release them). The Fourierist community is an engineering problem: given the repertoire of human passions, design the institutional machinery that will convert them into productive and pleasurable activity.

## Place in the Utopian Tradition

Brisbane stands at the intersection of European utopian theory and American communal practice. His role as a translator and popularizer demonstrates how utopian ideas travel across national boundaries and adapt to new cultural contexts — a pattern Mumford traces throughout *The Story of Utopias*. The Fourierist phalanxes Brisbane promoted represent the nineteenth-century conviction that systematic social redesign, rather than piecemeal reform or moral exhortation, offered the path to a better world. And Brisbane's mechanical metaphor, quoted by Mumford, captures the spirit of an era that saw social science and industrial engineering as twin instruments of progress.
