David Ricardo (1772–1823) was a British political economist whose theories of value, rent, and comparative advantage profoundly influenced 19th-century economic thought. In [[lewis-mumford]]'s *[[the-story-of-utopias]]*, Ricardo appears as a key figure in the intellectual lineage that utopian thinkers sought to transcend.

## In the Utopian Critique

Mumford places Ricardo within a sequence of economic thinkers whose "semi-scientific investigations" spurred utopian and socialist responses:

> in the nineteenth century the main accent was economic, and a number of movements arose which could be traced back to the semi-scientific investigations of Adam Smith, Ricardo, Proudhon, Malthus, Marx, and perhaps half a dozen other thinkers of outstanding importance [1234-004]

Ricardo's importance for the utopian tradition lies not primarily in his own doctrines but in the reactions they provoked. His iron law of wages — the proposition that wages inevitably tend toward subsistence level — was precisely the kind of pseudo-scientific pessimism that utopian thought existed to refute. If wages were held down not by the laws of economics but by the arrangements of society, then a reorganized society could provide abundance for all.

## The Economic Man and Coketown

Ricardo's abstract model of economic behavior contributed to what Mumford critiques as the "Economic Man" — a reductive figure whose values helped produce the industrial nightmare of [[coketown]]:

> out of the worse quality classical economists like Ricardo painted an equally entrancing picture of the beneficent capitalist, through whose foresight, organizing ability, and boldness business could be conducted on a scale a simpler age had scarcely dreamed of [1242-005]

Mumford further argues that Ricardo's economic vision, adopted throughout industrial society, shaped the physical and moral landscape of the 19th century:

> It was rather because everyone within these horrid centers accepted the same values and pursued the same ends — as they were projected by economists like Ricardo, industrialists like Stephenson, and lyric poets like Samuel Smiles — that the plans of the jerrybuilder and the engineer expressed to perfection the brutality and social disharmony of the community [1475-006]

## Ricardo and the Utopian Response

The utopian tradition's response to Ricardo took two forms. The first, represented by the [[owenite-movement|Owenite]] and [[fourierist-movement|Fourierist]] experiments, was practical: communities like [[new-lanark]] and the American [[phalanx|phalansteries]] demonstrated that shared ownership and cooperative production could improve living standards in ways that Ricardo's "laws" declared impossible. The second, represented by [[karl-marx]] and the [[socialist-movement]], was theoretical: a wholesale critique of political economy as ideology rather than science.

Mumford's own position synthesizes both responses. He accepts the utopian critique of classical economics but insists that the critique must go further — beyond the question of industrial ownership to the larger question of the [[good-life]]. The true weakness of Ricardo, in Mumford's framework, is not that his economic theories were wrong (though many were) but that his vision of human purpose was so impoverished.
