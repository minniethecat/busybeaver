## Definition

In [[the-story-of-utopias]], the **Social Contract** tradition represents a pivotal shift in Western political thought that helped transform utopian speculation into practical reform. Mumford traces how philosophers "devoted themselves to preparing fairly minute plans and specifications for the social order" (1234-1234), beginning with Rousseau, Beccaria, Bentham, Jefferson, Godwin, and the eighteenth-century reformers. While Mumford chose not to treat Hobbes' *Leviathan* directly — it "excluded important essays in politics like Hobbes' *Leviathan* and Harrington's *Oceana*" (1524-1524) — the contractarian framework permeates his discussion of how modern social thought moved from picturing ideal cities to designing constitutional orders.

The social contract tradition bridges two eras: it retains the utopian impulse to imagine a better society, but channels it into the language of rights, constitutions, and institutional design — the language that would dominate nineteenth-century reform movements.

## Core Characteristics

**Society as artifact.** Contract theory treats the social order not as a natural given but as a human construction that can be redesigned. Mumford identifies this as a double-edged development: on one hand, it unleashed enormous creative energy for reform; on the other, it led to the error of believing "that human nature was unsocial and obstreperous only because the church, the state, or the institution of property perverted every human impulse" (1268-1268).

**The myth of consent.** Mumford analyzes the social contract as a form of "social myth" — one that, "by being consciously formulated and worked out in thought, tend to perpetuate and perfect that order" (1031-1031). The American revolution's "myth of political freedom" (1031-1031) is a prime example: it served simultaneously as a genuine ideal and as "an excellent refuge for disturbed consciences" (1031-1031) when government actions violated its own professed principles.

**From pictures to principles.** Mumford's own criterion for selecting utopias was that they "had been embodied in complete pictures of an ideal commonwealth" (1524-1524). The social contract tradition moved away from picture-making toward principle-stating — a transformation that made political thought more practically effective but, Mumford implies, less imaginatively rich.

## Historical Context in Mumford's Narrative

The social contract emerges in Mumford's story at a transitional moment. After the Renaissance utopias of [[sir-thomas-more|More]] and [[francis-bacon|Bacon]], and before the industrial-era designs of Fourier and Bellamy, there is a period when the utopian impulse goes underground, surfacing instead in political philosophy. Mumford notes how "Rousseau, writing about the corrupting influence of the arts and sciences, and Chateaubriand, seeking the noble savage in the American wilderness" (656-656) "struck the dominating note of the eighteenth century" (656-656).

The contract tradition culminates in what Mumford describes as the nineteenth-century error: reformers "looked upon human institutions as altogether external to men" and "could conceive of changing the institutions without changing the habits and redirecting the impulses of the people by whom and for whom they had been created" (1268-1268). This critique sets up Mumford's own position — that genuine utopia requires transformation of both institutions and human character, both external conditions and internal dispositions.

## Relation to Other Concepts

The social contract tradition intersects with several key Mumfordian concepts:

- **[[idolum]]** — contract theory can itself become an idolum when its "myth of political freedom" is used to mask oppression
- **[[utopia-of-reconstruction]]** — the contract tradition is primarily reconstructive rather than escapist
- **[[national-state-idolum|National State]]** — contract theory helped legitimize the nation-state that Mumford critiques
- **[[will-to-utopia]]** — the deeper impulse that contract theory channels into constitutional form
- **[[utopian-socialism]]** — the nineteenth-century extension of contractarian thinking into the economic sphere

Mumford's own position, developed in the concluding chapters, suggests that neither contract theory alone nor utopian picture-making alone is sufficient. What is needed is a synthesis: institutions designed with the rigor of the contract tradition, animated by the imaginative richness of the classic utopias, and grounded — as Mumford insists — in real regions, real communities, and the full complexity of human nature.
