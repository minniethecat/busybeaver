# Spensonia

*Spensonia* is a land-reform utopia by the London stationer and radical pamphleteer [[thomas-spence|Thomas Spence]] (1750-1814). First published in 1795 as *A Description of Spensonia* and expanded in 1801 as *The Constitution of Spensonia: A Country in Fairyland Situated Between [[utopia-more|Utopia]] and Oceana; Brought from Thence by Captain Swallow*, it belongs to a tradition of self-educated working-class radicalism that [[lewis-mumford|Lewis Mumford]] singles out for its "rough, homespun quality" (007-012). Along with [[freeland|Hertzka's *Freeland*]], [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] identifies it as one of the two modern utopias in which "the common possession of land is the foundation of every other institution" (007-005).

## Historical Context

Spence wrote from a shop in High Holborn, London, where he published little pamphlets of "rough philosophy" under the title *Pig's Meat* (007-007). He belonged to the same moment of working-class intellectual ferment that produced [[william-cobbett|William Cobbett]] and [[francis-place|Francis Place]] — "men of common stock, usually self-educated, [who] began to apply their wits to improving the conditions of the class to which they belonged" (007-006). Spence wrote in the thick of the agitation for parliamentary reform that would dominate nineteenth-century British politics — the Chartist movement, parliamentary socialism, and the long campaign for universal suffrage. His utopia is a rebuke to that entire tradition: he saw that political reform without economic restructuring was superficial.

## Summary of the Work

### The Parable of the Ship

Spensonia begins with a parable: a father builds a ship for his sons to trade, decreeing that the profits be shared in common. The ship is wrecked upon an island, and the sons quickly realize that:

> "If they did not apply the Marine Constitution given them by their father to their landed property, they would soon experience inexpressible inconveniences. They therefore declared the property of the island to be the property of them all collectively, in the same manner as the ship had been, and that they ought to share the profits thereof in the same way. The island they named Spensonia, after the ship which their father had given them." (007-008)

This parable encapsulates Spence's central argument: the principles of common ownership that seem natural at sea — where the ship is everyone's home and livelihood — should apply equally on land.

### Parish Democracy

Spensonia is organized around the parish as the fundamental political and economic unit:

> "A parish, to begin with, is a 'compact portion of the country, designedly not too large that it may the more easily be managed by the inhabitants with respect to its revenues and police.'" (007-010)

Each parish controls its own land and revenues. A National Assembly composed of delegates from all parishes "takes care of the national concerns, and defrays the expenses of the state and matters of common utility, by a pound rate from each parish without any other tax" (007-008). Individuals or families who wish to occupy land pay rent to the parish — "according to its value" — and this rent constitutes the public revenue. To preserve the memory of their common rights, the Spensonians "never fail to share at rent-time, an equal dividend, though ever so small, and though the public demands should be ever so urgent" (007-008).

### The Two Guardian Angels

Spence places his utopia under the protection of two "guardian angels" — Voting by Ballot and the Universal Use of Arms — which [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] observes with characteristic irony: "two angels which look less formidable and potent in the twentieth century than they did in the first decade of the nineteenth, when the first had still to be tried, and when the second was not complicated by the invention of machine guns and poison gases" (007-012).

## Mumford's Assessment

[[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] values Spence not for literary elegance but for philosophical clarity:

> "At the bottom of Spence's Utopia, however, lies the conviction which he shares with Plato and all the other genuine utopians; namely, that in Thoreau's words less is accomplished by the thousands who are hacking at the branches of evil than by one who is striking at the root." (007-013)

Spence saw clearly what the parliamentary reformers did not: that the root of social inequality was land ownership, and that no amount of political tinkering — votes, charters, or parliamentary majorities — could remedy economic dependence. [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] invokes [[henry-david-thoreau|Thoreau]]'s image of the axe at the root to position Spence in the genuine utopian tradition alongside [[republic-plato|Plato]] and [[utopia-more|More]], however rough-hewn his expression.

[[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] concedes the utopia's limitations — it needs "a visit to the English villages of the New Forest or the Chiltern Hills, where some of the common lands have been kept, to see what a rural utopia would be like" (007-012) — but treats it as more philosophically serious than many more elaborate utopias. He pointedly contrasts Spence and [[charles-fourier|Fourier]] with the bulk of nineteenth-century utopian writing, which he dismisses as "vast reticulations of steel and red tape" that "keep on adding inventions to the present one" (007-037). Spence, by contrast, addressed the fundamental question.

## Place in the Utopian Tradition

In [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]]'s genealogy, Spensonia represents the purest expression of the land-based utopian tradition — more radical than the [[garden-city-organization|Garden City movement]], more philosophically coherent than the parliamentary reformism it opposed. It stands as a bridge between the agrarian radicalism of the English Civil War (the Diggers, [[gerard-winstanley|Gerrard Winstanley]]) and the comprehensive economic utopias of the late nineteenth century. Where [[freeland|Hertzka]] would later extend the principle of common ownership from land to industrial capital, Spence remained focused on the land — because, for an eighteenth-century London radical, the land was still where the root of inequality lay.
