## Georges Sorel

Georges Sorel (1847-1922) was a French philosopher and social theorist whose *Reflections on Violence* (1908) introduced the concept of the "social myth" as a dynamic force in political life. Mumford draws directly on Sorel's framework to develop his own theory of "idola" — the collective mental representations that shape social reality — and to distinguish between classic literary utopias and the broader category of social myths (0381-1029).

### The Social Myth

For Sorel, a social myth is not a description of future events but an expression of collective will: it mobilizes action in the present by projecting an idealized image of social transformation. The general strike, in Sorel's analysis, functions as the central myth of syndicalism — not as a practical blueprint but as a unifying vision that gives coherence and emotional force to working-class struggle. Mumford adopts this insight, noting that "the idola which have exercised the most considerable influence upon the actual life of the community are such as have been partly expressed in a hundred works and never perhaps fully expressed in one" (0381-1029). These "collective utopias or social myths" operate differently from the classic literary utopia: where the classic utopia is a finished blueprint, the social myth is an active, evolving force in historical consciousness.

### Utopia and Myth

Mumford proposes a typological relationship between the classic utopia and the social myth, suggesting that the latter "approaches very closely to the classic utopia" and can be divided, similarly, into "myths of escape and myths of reconstruction" (0381-1031). The myth of political freedom formulated by the American revolutionaries, for example, "frequently serves as an excellent refuge for disturbed consciences" — functioning as a utopia of escape — while simultaneously motivating reconstructive political action. This dual character of the social myth — simultaneously escapist and reconstructive — mirrors the fundamental division Mumford identifies within the utopian tradition itself.

### Syndicalism and Violence

Sorel's philosophy of syndicalism, with its emphasis on direct action and the transformative power of violence, presented a radical alternative to both parliamentary socialism and state-centered Marxism. The myth of the general strike, in his view, could achieve what rational argument and electoral politics could not: the moral and spiritual unification of the working class. While Mumford's own politics differ markedly from Sorel's, the analytical tool of the social myth proves indispensable to *The Story of Utopias*, particularly in the later chapters where Mumford examines the collective representations (Coketown, the Country House, Broadway) that dominate modern civilization.

### Influence on Mumford

Sorel's influence on *The Story of Utopias* extends beyond the explicit citation in Chapter X. The very structure of the book — which moves from survey of classic literary utopias to analysis of collective social myths to the projection of a reconstructive "eutopia" — follows the trajectory that Sorel's concept implies: from individual fantasy to collective myth to transformative action. Mumford acknowledges his debt to the broader French sociological tradition (Durkheim, Fouillée) while singling out Sorel's *Reflections on Violence* for its accessible treatment of "the place of myths and ideals" (0381-1528).
