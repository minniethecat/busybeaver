## Definition

The **utopian method** is the practice of deliberately projecting a comprehensive ideal commonwealth as a means of social critique and reconstruction. Mumford uses the term to describe what the classic utopian writers—from [[plato]] through [[sir-thomas-more]] to [[edward-bellamy]]—were doing: not idle daydreaming but a disciplined exercise in "giving a detailed picture of the society of the future" (009-1252).

Mumford distinguishes two dimensions of the utopian method. The first is the **utopian vision**—the imaginative grasp of what the good life might look like. The second is the method proper—the structural principles by which that vision is translated into a concrete picture of institutions, practices, and relationships. Having "ransacked the literature of ideal commonwealths for examples of the utopian vision and the utopian method" (010-1025), Mumford concludes that while individual visions differ, the method itself exhibits recurring features across centuries and cultures.

## The Engels–Marx Critique

The term "utopian method" enters Mumford's vocabulary largely through the critique mounted by [[friedrich-engels]] and [[karl-marx]]. In *Socialism: Utopian and Scientific*, Engels "made a plea for a realistic method of thought, which limited itself to a here and now, as against what he derided as the utopian method, the attempt on the part of a single thinker to give a detailed picture of the society of the future" (009-1252).

Mumford offers a nuanced rebuttal. He points out that "if the utopian socialism of Owen has been ineffective, the realistic socialism of Marx has been equally ineffective" (009-1252). The cooperative movement has realized parts of [[robert-owen]]'s vision, while the dictatorship of the proletariat "rests upon very shaky foundations, and such success as it has had is due perhaps as much to Marx's literary picture of what it would be like as to anything else" (009-1252).

Mumford's deeper argument is that the critics of the utopian method "overlooked the fact that one of the main factors that condition any future are the attitudes and beliefs which people have in relation to that future—that, as Mr. [[john-dewey]] would say, in any judgment of practice one's belief in a hypothesis is one of the things that affect its realization" (012-1477). The utopian method, far from being irrelevant to practical change, is one of its essential preconditions.

## The Method in Practice

Mumford identifies concrete examples of the utopian method operating successfully outside the literary tradition:

**The Peterborough School.** Discussing [[johann-andreae]]'s *Christianopolis* and its vision of education, Mumford points to a real-world analogue: "a summer school in the hills of New Hampshire, where the children govern themselves in the classroom, where there is no punishment except temporary exclusion from the group, and where, above all, each instructor is chosen because of his creative practice in the subject which he teaches" (003-566). He insists: "It is not the creation of this utopian method that is difficult; for the thing has already been done: what we need is its extension" (003-566).

**Patrick Geddes and the Regional Survey.** Mumford identifies [[patrick-geddes]] as "the outstanding exponent of the Eutopian method both in thought and in practical activity" [Bib-1530]. Geddes's [[regional-survey]] method—the integrated study of a region's geography, work, and culture—provides "this common tissue of definite, verifiable, localized knowledge" that the utopian method requires to move from paper to practice (009-1403).

**From paper to practice.** The chief use of the classic utopias, Mumford argues, "is to suggest that the same methods which are used by the utopian thinkers to project an ideal community on paper may be employed, in a practical way, to develop a better community on earth" (012-1477). The weakness of the classic utopians was not their method but their assumption "that the dreams and projects of any single man might be realized in society at large" (012-1477). The corrective is not to abandon the method but to embed it in regional knowledge and collective practice.

## Contrast with the Partisan Method

The utopian method stands in direct contrast to the [[partisan-utopia|partisan method]] of the nineteenth-century reform movements. Where the partisan method seizes on a single dimension of social life—economic distribution, legal rights, political machinery—the utopian method attempts to imagine the whole: work and play, production and reproduction, soil and spirit, in their interconnections.

The partisan programs failed, Mumford argues, precisely because they lacked what the utopian method provides: a comprehensive picture of the good life against which partial reforms can be measured and coordinated. Singly, "not one of these utopias would create a happy community; while if all of these partisanisms could be realized the result could scarcely be anything else than discord—such a discord as now exists and every day becomes more raucous" (009-1326).
