# Christianopolis

*Christianopolis* (1619), originally titled *Reipublicae Christianopolitanae Descriptio*, is a utopian work by the German Humanist scholar, preacher, and social reformer [[johann-andreae|Johann Valentin Andreae]] (1586-1654). [[lewis-mumford|Lewis Mumford]] devotes all of Chapter IV of *The Story of [[utopia-more|Utopia]]s* to this work, judging it the finest of the three early seventeenth-century utopias that also include [[tommaso-campanella|Campanella]]'s *[[city-of-the-sun|City of the Sun]]* and [[francis-bacon|Bacon]]'s *[[new-atlantis|New Atlantis]]*.

## Historical Context

Andreae wrote in the wake of the Renaissance recovery of classical learning. He had lived and studied in Herrenburg, Tübingen, Strasbourg, Heidelberg, Frankfurt, Geneva, and other centers of European intellectual life, and corresponded with learned men abroad including [[samuel-hartlib|Samuel Hartlib]] and [[john-amos-comenius|John Amos Comenius]] (004-004). The work emerged from the same moment that produced [[francis-bacon|Bacon]]'s scientific institutes and [[tommaso-campanella|Campanella]]'s astrological city — but [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] insists that Andreae surpasses both contemporaries.

## Summary of the Work

The narrator, like Andreae himself a traveler and scholar, is shipwrecked on the shore of an island dominated by the city of Christianopolis. After being examined as to "his ideas of life and morals, his person, and his culture," he is admitted to the community (004-004).

### Physical Layout

The city is a model of rational design:

> "Its shape is a square whose side is 700 feet, well fortified with four towers and a wall.… It looks therefore towards the four quarters of the earth. Of buildings there are two rows, or if you count the seat of the government and the storehouses, four; there is only one public street, and only one marketplace, but this one is of a very high order." (004-006)

At the center stands a circular temple, a hundred feet in diameter. All buildings are three stories, connected by public balconies. Fire safety is ensured by building with burnt stone and separating structures with fireproof walls. Approximately four hundred citizens live "in religious faith and peace of the highest order" (004-006).

### Industrial Organization

[[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] identifies Christianopolis's defining characteristic as the centrality of labor:

> "In Christianopolis, the workshop and the worker set the lines upon which the community is developed; and whatever else this society may be, it is a 'republic of workers, living in equality, desiring peace, and renouncing riches.'" (004-007)

Where [[republic-plato|Plato]]'s [[republic-plato|Republic]] modeled itself on the military camp and [[utopia-more|More]]'s [[utopia-more|Utopia]] on the family farmstead, Christianopolis is organized around the guild workshop. [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] calls this "the communism of the guild" as opposed to [[utopia-more|Utopia]]'s "communism of the family" (004-007).

The city is divided into three industrial zones (004-008):
- **Agriculture and animal husbandry**, with guildhalls where citizens "act on sacred as well as civil matters"
- **Light industry**: mills, bakeries, meat-shops, paper manufacturing, saw mills, and common kitchens and wash houses
- **Heavy industry**: metallurgy, glass, brick, and earthenware — industries "which require constant fire"

[[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] notes that in this zoning scheme "these seventeenth century [[utopia-more|Utopia]]ns have anticipated the best practice that has been worked out today, after a century of disorderly building" (004-010). The separation into zones, the distinction between heavy and light industry, and the provision of an agricultural zone adjacent to the city all prefigure the modern [[garden-city-organization|garden city]].

### Science and Work

A distinctive feature of Christianopolis is the conscious application of science to industrial processes:

> "Here in truth you see a testing of nature herself. The men are not driven to a work with which they are unfamiliar, like pack-animals to their task, but they have been trained before in an accurate knowledge of scientific matters." (004-011)

Artisans are educated: "their artisans are almost entirely educated men" on the principle that "neither the substance of letters is such, nor yet the difficulty of work, that one man, if given enough time, cannot master both" (004-014). Work is conducted collectively — the whole city functions as "one single workshop, but of all different sorts and crafts" (004-015) — and if the supply of material is sufficient, "the workmen are permitted to indulge and give free play to their inventive genius" (004-015).

## Mumford's Assessment

[[lewis-mumford|Mumford]]'s admiration for Andreae is striking. He contrasts Andreae's seriousness with [[francis-bacon|Bacon]]'s "nauseating foppishness about details in dress" and finds [[tommaso-campanella|Campanella]]'s *[[city-of-the-sun|City of the Sun]]* to be "a sort of picture puzzle put together out of fragments from [[plato|Plato]] and [[sir-thomas-more|More]]" (004-003). Andreae alone among the three "fastens itself upon essentials" (004-003).

The governing philosophy of Christianopolis is summarized in Andreae's maxim: "To be wise and to work are not incompatible, if there is moderation" (004-012). [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] sees this as the core of a genuine artisan democracy in which "the creative rather than the possessive impulses are uppermost" (004-017). There is no money, no private wealth — "no one can be superior to the other in the amount of riches owned, since the advantage is rather one of power and genius, and the highest respect that of morals and piety" (004-015).

[[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] traces the influence of Christianopolis on [[john-amos-comenius|Comenius]] and through him on later educational reformers. He concludes that Andreae "has reached the innermost shrine of Christianopolis" (004-044) — a society where religion, science, work, and art are integrated rather than compartmentalized.

## Place in the Utopian Tradition

*Christianopolis* stands at a pivotal juncture in the utopian tradition. It inherits the [[good-life|moral seriousness]] of [[republic-plato|Plato]] and [[utopia-more|More]] while anticipating the scientific and [[industrialism|industrial utopia]]s of later centuries. Unlike [[francis-bacon|Bacon]], who separated scientific research from the broader question of human purposes, Andreae integrates laboratory, workshop, temple, and household into a single vision.

[[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] positions Andreae alongside [[francis-bacon|Bacon]] and [[tommaso-campanella|Campanella]] as the three utopian writers who "stand at the entrance to the utopia of means" (005-015) — the point at which utopian thought shifted from asking what ends men should pursue to asking what instruments could expand human possibility. But Andreae alone among them never lost sight of the question of ends.
