# Freeland

*Freeland: A Social Anticipation* (1889) is an economic utopia by the Austrian economist [[theodor-hertzka|Theodor Hertzka]] (1845-1924), later condensed into the narrative *A Visit to Freeland, or the New [[paradise-regained-concept|Paradise Regained]]*. The work describes a cooperative commonwealth established in the Kenya Highlands of East Africa, founded on the common ownership of land and the social provision of capital. [[lewis-mumford|Lewis Mumford]] treats it as the pivotal transition between purely land-based utopias and those in which "land and capital and all the machinery of production belongs to a national state" (007-015).

## Historical Context

Hertzka wrote in the late 1880s, at a moment when socialist agitation was intensifying across Europe. Unlike revolutionary socialists, however, Hertzka proposed not the overthrow of existing institutions but "turning over a new leaf in the Kenya Highlands of Africa" (007-017). His books generated a "whirlwind of agitation": a magazine was founded, societies organized in cities across Europe and America, and a definite attempt made to colonize a section of Africa selected by Hertzka. The colonization effort failed, [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] notes dryly, "as a result of the obtuseness and international jealousy of various colonial officials" (007-017). [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] speculates that the movement's only practical effect may have been "to turn the thoughts of certain Zionists, like Israel Zangwill, from establishing Zion in Jerusalem to building it up again in some more suitable region in the heart of Africa" (007-017).

## Summary of the Work

### The Five Fundamental Laws

Freeland is governed by five fundamental laws, the first and most important being that "Every inhabitant has an equal right to the common land and to the means of production which are furnished by the state" (007-020, 007-021). The remaining laws provide for the support of women and children, the elderly, and those unfit to work (007-022).

### The Economic Mechanism

The visitor to Freeland first encounters the Central Statistical Office, which maintains records of available occupations and pay rates. "Every inhabitant of Freeland has the right to become a member of any business he pleases" (007-023). In practice, large companies dominate: they operate factories, provide restaurant service, build houses, and even supply domestic service.

The system's defining innovation is the social provision of capital. Capital is collected through a yearly tax — obviating the need for individual savings — and distributed without interest to companies that apply for it:

> "The community pays for the plant through the added charge which is laid on consumers; the credit advanced is cancelled out through production. This arrangement does away with the standing charge for capital which is maintained under present day production for profit even after the original capital has been paid off in dividends; and above all, it does away with the practice of capitalizing increased returns in such a way as to enlarge the amount of the standing charge for capital." (007-026)

[[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] observes that Hertzka recognizes what many reformers miss: "in an industrial society, access to machinery is just as important as access to the land" (007-026).

### Life in Edendale

Freeland's principal city, Edendale, features an administrative palace, the Central Bank, a university, public libraries, theaters, concert halls, and extensive parks (007-035). Workers earn credits at the Central Bank and are free to join multiple companies. The visitor, an engineer, finds a railway equipment plant "run under the general direction of its own elected officers" (007-027).

## Mumford's Assessment

[[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] offers a measured judgment. He acknowledges the work's analytical sharpness — Hertzka's grasp of how capital accumulation distorts industrial society is clear — but finds the utopia limited in scope:

> "A visit to Freeland teaches us little about the arts of social life or the constitution of a good society. What we can learn is one of the methods by which — on hypothesis anyway — the industrial mechanism might be controlled." (007-019)

The "chief good that Freeland seems to offer is freedom in industrial enterprise" (007-033). [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] notes that there are genuine differences from capitalist society — "the slum proletariat has been abolished; everyone has access to the land and to the instruments of production" (007-036) — but the underlying pattern of industrial life remains familiar: large companies, wage differentials, centralized banking.

[[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] groups Freeland with the "utopias of means" that proliferated in the nineteenth century. Hertzka's innovation was to recognize that land reform alone was insufficient in an [[industrialism|industrial age]] — access to capital and machinery must be socialized alongside access to land. But the work ultimately exemplifies what [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] calls "the Nightmare of the Age of Machinery" (007-037): a utopia that perfects the industrial mechanism without adequately reimagining the human purposes it should serve.

## Place in the Utopian Tradition

*Freeland* marks a transition point in [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]]'s genealogy. It shares with [[spensonia|Spensonia]] the premise that common land is the foundation of just institutions (007-005), but extends the principle to industrial capital. In this it anticipates the fully socialized economies of later utopias like [[looking-backward|Bellamy's *Looking Backward*]]. [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] positions Freeland between the agrarian radicalism of [[thomas-spence|Spence]] and the comprehensive state socialism of Bellamy — a halfway house that recognized the industrial dimension of the land question but could not quite imagine a society organized around anything beyond efficient production.
