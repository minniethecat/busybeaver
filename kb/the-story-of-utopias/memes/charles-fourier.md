Charles Fourier (1772-1837) was a French philosopher and utopian socialist whose elaborate system of "passional attraction" and communal living — the phalanx or *phalanstère* — marked a decisive turn in utopian thought from the classical model of merely restraining human nature toward designing institutions that channel it. Among the Associationists, Mumford ranks him as "the most influential utopian" (005-009), a figure who bridged the rationalist utopias of the Enlightenment and the socialist experiments of the nineteenth century.

## Life and Background

Fourier was a commercial traveller from Besançon whose modest personal fortune was lost in the French Revolution. His hopes for founding a real eutopia were "blasted by the July revolution of 1830" (005-009). He spent his working life moving between trading houses, deliberately transferring "from one line of goods to another in order to increase the area of the territory he covered and learn more of the workings of society" (005-009). This peripatetic commercial life gave his writings a "wealth of concrete detail" that set them apart from more abstract utopian schemes.

Mumford describes Fourier as "a prolific and incoherent writer, and his Utopia, if the truth be told, exists as *disjecta membra* rather than as a single work" (005-009). Nevertheless, Mumford makes an exception to his usual criterion that a utopia must exist as a single self-contained work, because "in every other respect he has a claim upon our attention" (005-009).

## The Theory of Passions

Fourier's signal innovation was to base his utopia not on the modification of human nature but on its systematic understanding and liberation — a move Mumford identifies as his decisive break with the classical utopian tradition:

> Fourier differs largely from the early utopians in that he is concerned first of all not with modifying human nature but with finding out what it actually is. His utopia is to be based upon an understanding of man's actual physical and mental makeup, and its institutions are to be such as will permit man's original nature to function freely.
> (005-009)

The motive force of Fourier's community is **attraction**; the engine that drives its institutions is **"the passions"** — a list of twelve fundamental tendencies that, in Mumford's observation, "corresponds roughly with the modern psychologist's list of instincts" (005-009). Social institutions, Fourier held, are to the passional forces "what machinery is to material forces" (005-009). A good community is one that brings all the passions into complex play rather than suppressing them — a sharp departure from the ascetic discipline of More's *Utopia* or the rational constraint of Plato's *Republic*.

Mumford identifies Fourier's ideal as **harmony**, grounded in a threefold human destiny:

> Man has a threefold destiny; namely, "an industrial destiny, to harmonize the material world; a social destiny, to harmonize the passional or moral world; and an intellectual destiny, to discover the laws of universal order and harmony."
> (005-009)

What was wrong with modern civilized societies, Fourier argued, was their incompleteness: they created "social dissonance" by forcing individuals to do in isolation what a complete community could do cooperatively.

## The Phalanx

The institutional vehicle for Fourier's theory was the *phalanx* — an experimental association of 1,500 to 1,600 persons occupying a diversified agricultural domain of roughly one square league. The site could not be arbitrary; Fourier insisted on a valley with varied terrain:

> "A flat country, such as Antwerp, Leipzig, Orleans, would be totally unsuitable … owing to the uniformity of land surface. It will therefore be necessary to select a diversified region, like the surroundings of Lausanne, or at the very least, a fine valley, provided with a stream of water and a forest, like the valley of Brussel or of Halle."
> (005-009)

Mumford notes this as a return to the valley section principle: "Fourier, too, goes back to the valley section" (005-009). The phalanx was to be primarily agricultural — this, Mumford says, is "perhaps the great distinction between Fourier and later Utopians" (005-009), who would increasingly turn to industrial mechanization as the material base of utopia. Yet all the arts were to be practiced within the phalanstery, since "otherwise the association would be incomplete" (005-009).

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Population | 1,500–1,600 persons |
| Land | ≥1 square league, diversified terrain with valley, stream, forest |
| Economy | Primarily agricultural (horticulture, arboriculture) |
| Production | Large-scale where possible; extreme division of labor |
| Monotony remedy | Rotating tasks and occupations |
| Exchange | Phalanx as unit; direct trade with other phalanxes (no middleman) |

The economic organization balanced private and collective interests. Public kitchens replaced individual housekeeping; children were trained from an early age in cooking. Every member was guaranteed a minimum of food, clothing, lodging, and amusements "without respect to work" (005-009). At the same time, private property was sanctioned, and each member drew from the common store a dividend proportional to stock held. Profit-sharing replaced the pure wage system — a balance "between private self-seeking and the maintenance of the public good" (005-009).

## Treatment of Women

Fourier understood that communal organization entailed a radical reconstruction of gender relations. By abolishing the individual household, the phalanx "gives a new freedom to women" (005-009). Fourier saw monogamic proprietorship as incompatible with women's free choice of mates. In the phalanx, women "are not intellectual nonentities; and since they no longer preside over the individual home, they help run the whole community" (005-009). Common nurseries, common schools, and informal education of children followed from this emancipation.

## Mumford's Assessment

Mumford treats Fourier with genuine intellectual respect, not as a crank but as a thinker who grasped a principle that the rationalist utopians had missed: that institutions must work *with* human nature rather than against it. The phalanx "gives play to all their activities, and … by erecting common institutions, will do away with the waste arising in the individual's attempts to do for himself all the things which would be done by a complete community" (005-009).

Fourier's achievement was to shift the center of gravity of utopian thought from moral exhortation to institutional design grounded in psychology — a shift that would influence the entire Associationist tradition and, indirectly, the cooperative movement of the nineteenth century. His weakness, as Mumford implies, was an obsessive specificity that verged on the comical: "minute plans and tables" (005-009) that transformed a genuinely liberatory vision into a rigid blueprint.

## Place in the Utopian Tradition

Fourier occupies a pivotal position between the classical utopias of ideal cities and the nineteenth-century socialist utopias of Owen, Saint-Simon, and Cabet. His phalanx was a conscious attempt to solve the problem that Plato had posed — how to harmonize individual passion with collective order — but by means that Plato would never have countenanced: the liberation rather than the suppression of desire.

Mumford pairs Fourier with [[robert-owen]] (though treating Owen as more "real-world" than utopian) and contrasts his agricultural orientation with the industrial utopias that followed. Fourier's influence extends to the [[garden-city-movement]] through the shared emphasis on limited scale, agricultural surroundings, and balanced community life.
