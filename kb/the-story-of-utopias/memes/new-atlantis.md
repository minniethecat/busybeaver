## Introduction

*New Atlantis* is an unfinished utopian fragment by [[francis-bacon|Francis Bacon]], published posthumously in 1627. The story describes the discovery by European sailors of a previously unknown island in the South Pacific, Bensalem, whose society is organized around Salomon's House — a state-sponsored college of scientific research and technological invention. The work is less a complete utopia than a "hint" (005-014), but its vision of organized science as the engine of social improvement has proved enormously influential.

[[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] is sharply critical of [[francis-bacon|Bacon]]'s literary abilities while acknowledging the importance of his vision. He notes that *New Atlantis* "is not a utopia in the sense that I have explained our principle of selection in the preface to the bibliography. It is only a fragment, and not very good as fragments go" (005-010). Yet the fragment's central insight — that systematic scientific research could transform the conditions of human life — was prophetic.

## Summary of the Work

The narrative is told by one of a group of European sailors whose ship is driven off course into the unknown Pacific. They encounter the island of Bensalem, whose inhabitants — unlike [[ideal-commonwealths|More's Utopia]]ns — are Christian, having received the Gospel through a miraculous transmission. The strangers are quarantined, instructed in the language, and eventually granted an audience with the Father of Salomon's House, who explains the island's central institution.

Salomon's House, "the noblest foundation that ever was upon the earth," is a research institute whose fellows undertake systematic investigation of nature: they conduct experiments in "caves" and "towers," study diseases, develop new foods and materials, and pursue what [[francis-bacon|Bacon]] elsewhere called "the relief of man's estate." The House sends out secret expeditions ("Merchants of Light") to gather knowledge from other nations while keeping Bensalem's own existence concealed.

## Mumford's Assessment

[[lewis-mumford|Mumford]]'s treatment of *New Atlantis* mixes contempt for its execution with respect for its core idea. He finds [[francis-bacon|Bacon]] "incredibly childish and incoherent" as a writer: "he gives such a description of Salomon's House as a six-year-old schoolboy might give of a visit to the Rockefeller Foundation" (005-014). He also mocks [[francis-bacon|Bacon]]'s preoccupation with ceremonial dress: "When [[francis-bacon|Bacon]] talks about science, he talks like a court costumer who is in the habit of describing the stage properties for a masque; and it is hard to tell whether he is more interested in the experiments performed by the scientists of the New Atlantis or the sort of clothes they wear while engaged in them" (004-003).

Yet beneath these "maladroit interpretations," [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] recognizes [[francis-bacon|Bacon]]'s prescience:

> Beneath these maladroit interpretations, however, we see that Bacon had a grasp on some of the fundamentals of scientific research, and of the part that science might play in the "relief of man's estate." It is nothing more than a hint, this New Atlantis; but a word to the wise is enough; and as we look about the modern world we see that, in its material affairs at any rate, the great scientific institutes and foundations — the United States Bureau of Standards, for one — play a part not a little like that of the College of the Six Days' Works. (005-014)

This is the paradox of [[francis-bacon|Bacon]]'s utopia: a poorly written fragment that nonetheless anticipated the institutional structure of modern science. [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] acknowledges the fragment's historical significance while denying it the status of a true utopia in his sense — it deals with the *means* of the [[good-life|good life]] (science, technology, organization) without adequately imagining the *ends*.

## Place in the Utopian Tradition

*New Atlantis* stands at the threshold between Renaissance humanist utopianism and the modern technological utopia. Its vision of organized research — "Merchants of Light" gathering knowledge globally, systematic experimentation, state funding of science — prefigured the institutional structure of modern science by three centuries. The fragment influenced the founders of the Royal Society and has been regularly invoked in defenses of publicly funded research.

[[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] positions [[francis-bacon|Bacon]] alongside [[johann-andreae|Johann Andreae]] (author of *[[christianopolis|Christianopolis]]*) and [[tommaso-campanella|Tommaso Campanella]] (author of *[[city-of-the-sun|City of the Sun]]*) as the three utopian writers of the early seventeenth century, judging Andreae the finest of the three. In [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]]'s genealogy, [[francis-bacon|Bacon]]'s significance lies less in the literary quality of his utopia than in his recognition — however inelegantly expressed — that organized knowledge could become an instrument of human betterment.
