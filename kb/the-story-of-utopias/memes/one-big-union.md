# One Big Union

The "One Big Union" was a revolutionary labor concept that envisioned all workers united in a single, industry-wide organization capable of overthrowing capitalism through a general strike and reorganizing society along industrial lines. Most prominently associated with the Industrial Workers of the World (IWW), founded in Chicago in 1905, the One Big Union represented the syndicalist wing of the labor movement, rejecting craft-based trade unionism in favor of industrial unionism and direct action.

## In Mumford's Analysis

Mumford situates the One Big Union within a sweeping catalog of the partisan movements that emerged from nineteenth-century industrial society:

> "Our business here is not to examine the various programs that were offered during the last century in answer to these problems; merely to catalog them with the barest explanation of their purpose would be an imposing task, were it not for the fact that it has been neatly done for us by Mr. Savel Zimand. It is enough to see here the common element in capitalism, copartnery, State Socialism, Guild Socialism, Cooperation, Communism, Syndicalism, the One Big Union, Trades Unionism, and the like; whether these movements represent actual facts, like capitalism, copartnery, or trades unionism, or whether they are simply projections, like Syndicalism and the One Big Union" (008-021).

This passage contains a distinction fundamental to Mumford's analysis. He separates these movements into two categories: those that represent "actual facts" — real, functioning institutions with tangible existence in the world — and those that are "simply projections" — utopian aspirations cast in organizational form. The One Big Union, Mumford insists, belongs to the latter category.

## The Part and the Whole: Fetishism of the Partisan

Mumford's classification of the One Big Union as a "projection" is not dismissive but diagnostic. The problem with such partisan utopias, he argues, is not that they lack vision but that their vision is partial:

> "For all the activities that men engage in we have separate words. This is a great misfortune; for in using these words we tend to believe that each action takes place in a separate compartment. Instead of beginning with a whole man interacting in a whole community, we are likely to consider only a partial man in a partial community, and by a mental sleight of hand, before we know it, we have let the part stand for the whole" (008-022).

The One Big Union, for all its revolutionary ambition, exemplifies this partiality. By focusing exclusively on the economic organization of labor, it treats workers solely as producers and the industrial system as the totality of social life. This reduction of the "whole man" to the "economic man" is, Mumford argues, the foundational error from which all partisan utopianism proceeds.

Mumford deepens this argument with a psychological analogy that is among the most striking passages in *The Story of Utopias*:

> "I mean that the utopia of the partisan is, psychologically speaking, a fetish; that is to say, it is an attempt to substitute the part for the whole, and to pour into the part all the emotional content that belongs to the whole. When a man gets hold of a lady's handkerchief or garter, and behaves towards that object with as much intensity and interest as he would towards its flesh and blood owner, the handkerchief or garter is said to be a fetish. I hazard the judgment that Socialism, Prohibition, Proportional Representation, and the various other abstract 'isms' are the fetishes of the partisan: they are attempts to make some particular instrument or function of the community stand for the whole" (008-030).

This passage is characteristically Mumfordian in its method: a psychological concept (fetishism) is borrowed from clinical discourse and redeployed as a tool of social criticism. The One Big Union, like the other partisan "isms" Mumford catalogues, takes one dimension of human life — economic production — and invests it with the total emotional and intellectual energy that properly belongs to the project of reconstructing the entire community. "It is easier indeed; but it is fatal; for the result of this fetishism is perhaps that the girl remains unmated, and the society fails to undergo any fundamental change" (008-030).

## The Utopian Dimension

Mumford's treatment of the One Big Union reveals a recurring pattern in his analysis of social movements. Utopian aspirations do not only appear in literary form — the classic utopias of [[sir-thomas-more|More]], [[francis-bacon|Bacon]], and [[edward-bellamy|Bellamy]]. They also crystallize in organizational forms: the One Big Union is, in this sense, a utopia of solidarity, a vision of total working-class unity projected onto the institutional template of the labor union. The gap Mumford identifies between the "projection" and the "actual fact" is the same gap that separates all utopias from existing reality — the distance between what is and what might be.

## Historical Significance

The One Big Union concept, as embodied by the IWW, played a significant role in early twentieth-century labor struggles, particularly in the mining, lumber, and agricultural sectors of the American West. While the IWW never achieved its revolutionary ambitions, its organizing model and direct-action tactics influenced subsequent labor movements worldwide. For Mumford, the One Big Union serves as an illustration of how utopian aspirations can be channeled into organizational forms — and of the gap that always remains between the projection and the reality.
