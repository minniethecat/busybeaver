# A Crystal Age

**A Crystal Age** (1887) is W. H. Hudson's utopian romance, one of the three "[[utopia-of-escape|utopias of escape]]" that [[lewis-mumford|Lewis Mumford]] examines in Chapter Nine of *The Story of [[utopia-more|Utopia]]s*. Hudson (1841-1922), better known as the author of *Green Mansions*, was a naturalist with a deep sympathy for rural life. His utopia returns unconsciously to the model of [[sir-thomas-more|Thomas More]], where the household rather than the city is the ultimate unit of social life.

[[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] identifies a pattern of unconscious revival among the nineteenth-century utopians: "Mr. W. H. Hudson returns upon [[sir-thomas-more|More]]; and in *A Crystal Age* the farmstead and the family is the ultimate unit of social life" (009-003). Unlike the [[industrialism|industrial utopia]]s of Bellamy or the craft-centered vision of [[william-morris|William Morris]], Hudson's utopia is built around the great [[country-house-idolum|Country House]] and a radical reorganization of biological and domestic life.

## The Country House as Social Unit

As the traveller's vision clears, "he finds himself received in a great [[country-house-idolum|Country House]], which is inhabited by a large group of men and women who till the land and perform the simple operations of weaving and stonecutting and the like" (009-004). These houses are not weekend retreats but permanent homes, each carrying traditions back thousands of years:

> All over the world, one gathers, these great country houses dot the landscape. Each of them is no weekend center of social life but a permanent home; indeed their permanence is almost past believing; for in each house traditions are carried back thousands of years. (009-004)

The great cities and their complicated metropolitan customs have been "wiped away, as one might wipe away mold." The world has been stabilized; "the itch for getting and spending has disappeared" (009-004). The household is governed by a housefather who administers laws and customs; housemates work together, eat together, play together, and listen to music together.

## The Beehive Model

The secret of the Crystal Age's stability is "the secret of the beehive: a queen bee" (009-006). Hudson's society has solved the difficulties of human mating by appointing one woman in every house to be the housemother, whose capital duty is to carry on the family:

> The Crystallites have done away with the difficulties of mating by appointing one woman, in every house, to be the housemother ... The wish of a housemother is a command; the word of the housemother is law. (009-006)

For all except the housemother, sex is a matter of purely physical appearance. The Crystallites are "content with a vegetable love" — a state that proves tragic for the traveller, who falls in love with a woman who cannot reciprocate. When he turns to the housemother for consolation, she gives him a phial of liquid that ends his life: "The social life of the household is not to be wrecked by the storms and stresses of the individual's passions. The engines of life are no longer dangerous: the fuel has been taken away! A 'chill moonlight felicity' is all that remains" (009-008).

## Domestication and Selection

[[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] interprets the Crystal Age as an exploration of biological domestication. He suggests that "there are times when one may look upon the whole adventure of civilized life as a sort of Odyssey of domestication; and in this mood the Crystal Age marks a terminus upon that particular aspect of the adventure" (009-009). Against the objection that this utopia requires changing human nature, [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] offers a modern biological rebuttal:

> There is no apparent scientific reason why certain elements in human nature should not be selected and brought to the front, or why certain others should not be reduced in importance and eliminated. (009-009)

Between individual households and common marriages, Hudson's beehive utopia represents a third alternative that, [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] suggests, "possibly remains to be explored" (009-010).

## Place in the Utopian Tradition

In [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]]'s framework, *A Crystal Age* belongs to the "[[utopia-of-escape|utopias of escape]]" — works that provide imaginative alternatives to industrial civilization rather than blueprints for its reform. It returns to the household-based model of [[sir-thomas-more|More]]'s *[[utopia-more|Utopia]]* while introducing a radical biological dimension absent from earlier utopias. [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] values it for opening the mind to possibilities of human self-domestication that most [[utopian-method|utopian thinking]] ignores.
