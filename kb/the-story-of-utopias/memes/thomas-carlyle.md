# Thomas Carlyle

## Introduction

Thomas Carlyle (1795-1881) was a Scottish historian, essayist, and social critic whose work profoundly influenced Victorian thought. In *The Story of Utopias*, Lewis Mumford twice turns to Carlyle's sharp-edged social commentary to illuminate the persistent failures of industrial civilization and the challenge of building a better society from flawed human material.

| Period | Role | Key Works |
|--------|------|-----------|

## Carlyle in Mumford's Account

Mumford's engagement with Carlyle is double-edged. He admires Carlyle's penetrating critique of nineteenth-century society but resists his pessimism about human nature. In a passage that draws together Carlyle's skepticism and Mumford's own qualified optimism, Mumford writes:

> Unfortunately, we are still in the same ditch that Carlyle mordantly pointed out in his essay on *Characteristics*: Given a world of knaves, we are trying by various cunning devices to produce an honesty from their united action. I do not share Carlyle's contempt for human nature in the raw, but he is quite right, I believe, in making fun of the superficiality of our partisan utopias. (011-029)

Immediately after this passage, Mumford explains why Carlyle's critique matters to utopian thought: "These utopias were so concerned to alter the shell of the community's institutions that they neglected to pay attention to the habits of the creature itself — or its habitat" (011-029). This distinction between the "shell" of institutions and the "habits of the creature" is central to Mumford's own methodology throughout *The Story of Utopias*: he consistently evaluates utopian schemes not by their architectural elegance alone but by their engagement with the full complexity of human nature.

## The Stomach as Metaphor

Mumford also records Walt Whitman's remark that "behind the tally of Carlyle's work and genius stood the stomach, and gave a sort of casting vote" (011-026). This visceral image appears in a key methodological passage where Mumford elaborates the idea that human biology and temperament can override even the most carefully designed social programs:

> Between our programs, our utopias, and their fulfillment there has usually dropped a thick veil of personalities; and were the plan itself the collaborate product of the best minds of the race… it would still have to take its chances with the wild asses of the devil that human weakness, apathy, greed, lust for power, might release. (011-026)

Whitman's comment about Carlyle's stomach thus becomes, in Mumford's hands, a general principle: "behind the tally of its theoretic background and its concrete programs stand human beings — hale and sick, neurotic and stable, well-intentioned and malicious — and give the casting vote" (011-026). This observation situates Carlyle's social criticism within the realm of material and bodily needs, connecting him to Mumford's broader argument that any viable utopia must reckon with the organic realities of human existence.

## Place in the Utopian Tradition

Carlyle occupies an ambivalent position in the utopian genealogy. He was a fierce critic of the industrial order and the "cash nexus" that replaced traditional social bonds, yet his proposed remedies tended toward authoritarian hero-worship rather than democratic reconstruction. For Mumford, Carlyle represents both the diagnostic power and the prescriptive limitations of the Romantic critique of industrialism. The Carlyle who "mordantly pointed out" the gap between institutional machinery and moral outcomes (011-029) is indispensable to utopian thought; the Carlyle whose "contempt for human nature in the raw" (011-029) Mumford explicitly disavows represents a temptation the utopian tradition must resist.
