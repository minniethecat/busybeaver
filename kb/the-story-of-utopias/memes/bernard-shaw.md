# Bernard Shaw

**George Bernard Shaw** (1856-1950) is the Irish playwright, critic, and Fabian socialist whose egalitarian convictions and literary dissection of English society place him, in [[lewis-mumford|Lewis Mumford]]'s account, at two distinct points in the utopian tradition: as an intellectual descendant of the ancient Greek egalitarian Phaleas, and as a sharp-eyed anatomist of the Country House culture that utopia must overcome.

## The Egalitarian Impulse

Mumford invokes Shaw early in *The Story of Utopias*, in his survey of the classical sources of utopian thought. Discussing the fragmentary record of ancient Greek utopian schemes, he notes:

> "Aristotle tells us about an ideal state designed by one, Phaleas, who believed like Mr. Bernard Shaw in a complete equality of property; and from Aristotle, too, we learn of another utopia which was described by the great architect, city planner, and sociologist — Hippodamus" (002-005).

This brief but telling comparison places Shaw in a lineage stretching back to the fourth century BCE. Phaleas of Chalcedon, known only through Aristotle's *Politics*, argued that equality of property would eliminate civil strife — a proposition that Shaw, as a leading voice of the Fabian Society, would advance in his own time through advocacy for the collective ownership of the means of production. Mumford's analogy, drawn in passing, links the Fabian program to one of the oldest questions in utopian thought: whether economic equality is the necessary foundation of the good community.

## Anatomist of the Country House

Shaw appears a second time in Mumford's analysis as a literary witness to the decay of Country House culture — the aristocratic order that, in Mumford's framework, utopia must transcend. Surveying the literary tradition that runs from Boccaccio's *Decameron* through Pope's "Rape of the Lock" to the modern novel, Mumford writes:

> "We see pretty much the same outlines in the introduction to Boccaccio's *Decameron*; it is elaborately described in terms of that most complete of Country Houses, Hampton Court, in Pope's 'Rape of the Lock'; it is vividly pictured by Meredith in his portrait of *The Egoist*; and it is analyzed in Mr. H. G. Wells' cruel description of Bladesover in *Tono-Bungay*, as well as by Mr. Bernard Shaw in *Heartbreak House*" (010-021).

Shaw's *Heartbreak House* (1919), subtitled "A Fantasia in the Russian Manner on English Themes," portrays the cultured impotence of a leisure class drifting toward catastrophe — a theme that resonated with the First World War's exposure of ruling-class failure. For Mumford, Shaw's diagnosis of Country House aimlessness complements [[h-g-wells|H. G. Wells]]'s portrait of Bladesover and [[anton-chekhov|Chekhov]]'s *The Cherry Orchard* as evidence that the old order, however elegant its surfaces, had lost its claim to moral authority.

## Fabianism and the Utopian Method

Although Mumford's references to Shaw are brief, they point toward a larger relationship between Fabian socialism and the utopian tradition. The Fabian Society, which Shaw joined in 1884, pursued gradual, parliamentary reform rather than revolutionary transformation — a "method" approach that connected utopian ends to political means. This placed Shaw and his fellow Fabians (including H. G. Wells, though the two would later clash) in a different relationship to utopia than the grand systematizers like [[charles-fourier|Fourier]] or [[edward-bellamy|Bellamy]]: their utopianism was incremental, embedded in policy pamphlets and platform speeches rather than in visions of transformed cities.

Shaw's intellectual range — playwright, music critic, political pamphleteer, and debater — made him one of the most publicly visible advocates for the proposition that a good society required not only new institutions but new sensibilities. His critique of property relations, his dissection of class culture, and his unrelenting confidence in rational improvement mark him as a figure in whom the utopian impulse took a distinctively modern, polemical form.
