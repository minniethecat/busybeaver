Herbert George Wells (1866-1946) was a British novelist, historian, and social prophet whose contributions to the utopian tradition span from the dystopian romance of *The Time Machine* (1895) to the systematic social criticism of *A Modern Utopia* (1905). Mumford treats Wells as the culminating figure of the modern utopian tradition — the writer who "sums up and clarifies the utopias of the past, and brings them into contact with the world of the present" (Ch III). Where [[edward-bellamy]] unconsciously reproduced the structures of the [[national-state-idolum]], Wells, in Mumford's reading, achieved a critical distance that makes *A Modern Utopia* "the quintessential utopia" (Ch III).

## The Wellsian Method: Entry by Hypothesis

Mumford identifies a distinctive method in Wells's approach to the imaginary commonwealth. Unlike the shipwrecks and hibernations that had become the conventional narrative devices of utopian fiction, Wells enters his utopia through a pure act of thought:

> He conceives of a modern man, a little thickset and protuberant, seated at a desk and brooding over the possibilities of man's future; and gradually this image comes to life and defines his views, and his voice rises into narrative in something like the fashion of a lecturer, throwing from time to time his illustrations of a New World upon the screen.
> (Ch III)

This "entry by hypothesis" — "without any other subterfuge than an act of the imagination" (Ch III) — is a self-conscious break with the narrative conventions Mumford had traced from More through Bellamy. It signals the maturity of the genre: the utopia no longer needs to disguise itself as a traveler's tale.

Wells is accompanied in his explorations by a "sentimental botanist" — a deliberately comic figure "who is sick with a love affair and is maudlin about dogs" (Ch III) and who repeatedly disrupts the sober examination of utopian institutions with petty earthly complications. This device of the irritable companion allows Wells to maintain both critical distance and self-irony.

## *A Modern Utopia*: The World State

Wells's utopia is conceived on a planetary scale. It is "a world community; it is a single civilization whose net of monorails and posts, whose identification bureaus, whose rules of law and order are the same in England as in Switzerland; and presumably the same in Asia and Africa as in Europe" (Ch III). Crucially, it exists on an Earth identical to ours — "it has the same oceans and continents, the same rivers and minor landmasses, the same animals and plants; yes, even the same people, so that each one of us has his utopian counterpart" (Ch III). The utopian world is located "beyond Sirius" and its history is parallel to ours, except that "it had a critical turn for the better at a not too remote period" (Ch III).

> Machinery plays an important part, and the absence of menial service is conspicuous from the very first contacts in which our travellers get the hospitality of an inn, and find that interior decoration has verged towards the style of the modern lunchroom and subway station, so that the whole room can be redded, after use, by the guest himself. There is no harking back to the past in industry, in architecture, or in the mode of living. All that machinery has to offer has been accepted and humanized: there is a cleanliness, an absence of squalor and confusion, in this world-community, which indicates that utopia has not been purchased by evasion.
> (Ch III)

Mumford contrasts Wells's price for order favorably with Bellamy's. Land and natural resources belong to the community; transportation and communication are centrally administered; but "there are a good many minor affairs which are still undertaken by private individuals and companies" (Ch III). Farms are worked by cooperative associations of tenant farmers. The system is socialist in its major enterprises but leaves room for private initiative at the periphery.

### The Four Classes

Wells divides the population of his utopia into four functional classes that Mumford traces back through Comte to More and ultimately to the *Bhagavad Gita*:

| Class | Role | Traditional Parallel |
|-------|------|---------------------|
| **Kinetic** (active/passive) | Administrators, managers, officials | More's Philarchs, Comte's chiefs |
| **Poietic** | Creative intellectuals, artists, scientists | More's scholars, Comte's intellectuals |
| **Base** | Residual population, limited capability | Sudras |
| **Dull** | Criminals, habitual drunkards (exported to islands) | Untouchables |

### The Samurai

The most distinctive institution of *A Modern Utopia* is the order of Samurai — a voluntary elite selected by "rigorous mental and physical tests" after age twenty-five. Mumford describes them as:

> They live a simple life. They are under strict moral discipline, and follow a minute regimentation of dress and minor details of conduct. They cannot marry out of their class. Once a year they are sent out into the forests, the mountains, or the waste places to shift for themselves; they go "bookless and weaponless, without pen or paper, or money"; and they come back again with a new hardness and fineness and fortification of spirit.
> (Ch III)

Mumford compares the Samurai to the Jesuit Order — "such an organization as might have been evolved at the time of the Reformation had the Order of Jesuits been able to effect a dictatorship of Christendom" (Ch III) — and notes, without disparagement, that "these guardians of *A Modern Utopia* are plausible historic characters" (Ch III). All important economic and political enterprises, as well as professions like medicine, are in Samurai hands.

The Samurai represent Wells's solution to the Platonic problem of governance: like [[plato]]'s guardians, they are a specially trained and disciplined class; unlike Plato's, they are selected in adulthood after a period of permitted folly, and their asceticism is periodically renewed through solitary wilderness retreats.

### The Registration State

One feature of Wells's utopia draws Mumford's gentle mockery: the universal registration system in which every individual is filed with "name, numeral, fingerprint, changes of residence and changes in life" in "a huge central filing office" (Ch III). Mumford attributes this to "Mr. Wells' temperamental regard for tidiness — tidiness on a planetary scale — the tagging and labelling of a well-conducted shop" (Ch III).

## The Critical Achievement

What distinguishes *A Modern Utopia*, for Mumford, is its intellectual honesty. Where other utopias "assume that a change has come over the population; that it has been diminished; that the blind, the lame, and the deaf have been cured; that the mean sensual man has been converted," there is "a minimum of these assumptions" in Wells (Ch III). The book is "above all other things an accounting and a criticism; and so it forms a fitting prelude to the remainder of this book" (Ch III) — the remainder being Mumford's own critical project.

Mumford also acknowledges Wells's broader literary contribution beyond *A Modern Utopia*: *The Time Machine* provides a "picture of Coketown which is perhaps a little exuberant in some of its details — the picture of a happy and careless Country House population, living on the surface of the earth … and that of the factory population, the Morlocks, living in the bowels of the earth" (Ch VI). This bifurcation of humanity into Eloi and Morlocks — the [[country-house-idolum]] and [[coketown-idolum]] made literal — is, for Mumford, one of the most penetrating images of industrial civilization ever produced.

## Place in the Utopian Tradition

Mumford positions Wells as the writer who brings the utopian tradition to self-consciousness. After the long arc from [[plato]] through [[sir-thomas-more]], [[francis-bacon]], [[charles-fourier]], and [[edward-bellamy]], Wells steps back and surveys the entire enterprise critically. His *A Modern Utopia* is less a blueprint than a commentary on blueprints — it "brings together, compares, and criticizes important points that all the other utopias have raised; and it does all this with a deftness and a turn of humor" (Ch III).

Yet Mumford does not uncritically endorse Wells. The registration mania, the planetary-scale tidiness, the elaborate classification of human beings into kinetic/poietic/base/dull — these are symptoms of what Mumford would later diagnose as the [[megalopolis]] mentality: the impulse to manage human life through paper systems. Wells advances beyond Bellamy but does not fully escape the gravitational pull of the [[national-state-idolum]].
