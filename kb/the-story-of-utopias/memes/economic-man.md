In *[[the-story-of-utopias]]*, [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] uses the figure of "Economic Man" to criticize the anthropology implicit in classical economics — a creature reduced to instincts of acquisition and construction, stripped of every human dimension beyond labor and saving. The concept appears in Mumford's critique of [[one-big-union|the One Big Union]] and other partisan utopianisms that adopt this reductionist model.

## In Mumford's Analysis

Mumford describes Economic Man with characteristic irony:

> "The economists seem to have made the error first by talking of a creature whom they called the Economic Man, a creature who had no instincts but those of construction and acquisition, no habits but of working and saving, and no other ultimate purpose than to become such a captain of industry as would make him a candidate for the biographic sketches of Mr. [[samuel-smiles|Samuel Smiles]], and his present successors in the newspapers and popular magazines" (009-1240).

This is not an isolated rhetorical flourish. The critique of Economic Man runs through Mumford's entire argument about the inadequacy of partial social philosophies.

## Reduction of the Whole Person

The central problem with Economic Man, for Mumford, is the reduction of the whole person to a single economic function. The abstraction is not merely inaccurate — it is actively harmful when it becomes the basis for social organization. Movements that organize society around Economic Man "treat workers solely as producers and the industrial system as the totality of social life" (011-041), ignoring the full range of human interests: love, friendship, art, science, play, and contemplation.

## Relation to Partisanism

Economic Man exemplifies [[partisanism]] in Mumford's scheme: it takes one dimension of human activity and inflates it into the whole. By defining human beings exclusively through economic categories, it creates the same kind of fragmentation that Mumford diagnoses in religious sectarianism, nationalism, and class politics. The result, in every case, is "a segment of a personality" rather than a "whole man or woman" (011-044).

## The Utopian Response

The utopian tradition, as Mumford reads it, has always resisted the reduction to Economic Man. From [[sir-thomas-more|More]]'s *[[utopia-more|Utopia]]* onward, utopian thinkers have insisted that the [[good-life|good life]] includes but transcends the economic. The utopian method aims at a comprehensive picture of human flourishing — "the things that unite human beings as human beings" (011-044) — rather than maximizing a single variable.

## In Later Utopian Thought

The critique of Economic Man became a recurring theme in utopian literature and social criticism. [[john-ruskin|Ruskin]]'s assault on the "economic man" of political economy, [[william-morris|Morris]]'s vision of creative labor in *[[news-from-nowhere|News from Nowhere]]*, and the regionalist economists around [[patrick-geddes|Geddes]] all developed variations on the argument that Mumford distills. The concept remains central to understanding why utopian thought so persistently challenges the premises of market-based social order.
