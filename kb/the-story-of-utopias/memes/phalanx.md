## Overview

The phalanx (French: *phalanstère*) is the fundamental architectural and social unit in Charles Fourier's utopian system. It is a self-contained cooperative community designed to house approximately 1,500 to 1,600 persons, combining residential, industrial, agricultural, and cultural functions within a single integrated structure [690]. Fourier conceived the phalanx as the building block of a new social order based on "passional attraction" rather than economic compulsion.

## Site and Setting

Fourier insisted that the phalanx must be situated on diversified terrain. As Mumford reports: "A flat country, such as Antwerp, Leipzig, Orleans, would be totally unsuitable … owing to the uniformity of land surface. It will therefore be necessary to select a diversified region, like the surroundings of Lausanne, or at the very least, a fine valley, provided with a stream of water and a forest, like the valley of Brussel or of Halle" [686]. The domain would be laid out in fields, orchards, and vineyards, with an intensive development in horticulture and arboriculture supplying the colony's needs. The main economic occupation of the phalanx would be agricultural—a great distinction between Fourier and later utopians [688].

## The Physical Structure

Mumford describes the phalanx as "a palace complete in all its appointments serving as the residence of the associates" [690]. The edifice is organized into three wings corresponding to three domains. The first wing houses workshops and halls of industry. The second wing contains the library, scientific collections, museums, and artists' studios. The central wing, devoted to social life, features banquet halls, reception rooms, and grand salons. At one end stands a Temple of the Material Harmonies for singing, music, poetry, dancing, gymnastics, and painting; at the other, a Temple of Unityism for celebrating "man's unity with the universe" [690]. An observatory with telegraph and signal tower crowns the summit, enabling "communication with other phalanxes" [690].

## Social Organization

Fourier's phalanx represents a radical departure from both the individualistic [[country-house|Country House]] and the industrial [[coketown]]. Rather than organizing society around the factory or the manor, Fourier centered community life on the principle of association. The phalanx was designed to harmonize what Fourier identified as the twelve fundamental human passions, channeling them into productive and creative work rather than repressing them as existing civilization did.

The phalanx guarantees every member a minimum of food, clothing, lodging, and amusements without respect to work; at the same time, private property is sanctioned, and each member extracts from the common store a dividend in proportion to the stock he holds in the association. A system of profit sharing replaces the pure wage system, creating "a sort of balance between private self-seeking and the maintenance of the public good" [692].

## Women's Emancipation

By abolishing the individual household, the phalanx gives a new freedom to women. Fourier did not see how it was possible to maintain the system of monogamic proprietorship once women had a free choice of mates. Mumford notes that the women of the phalanx "are not intellectual nonentities; and since they no longer preside over the individual home, they help run the whole community" [696]. This emancipation extended to common nurseries, common schools, and the informal education of children.

## The Industrial Army

One of the most remarkable characteristics of Fourier's utopia, Mumford observes, is its "utilization of a moral equivalent for war, long before Professor William James invented the phrase" [698]. Fourier envisioned the phalanx assembling productive armies rather than destructive ones:

> There is a fine passage in which Fourier pictures an industrial army of golden youths and maidens, "instead of devastating thirty provinces in a campaign, these armies will have spanned thirty rivers with bridges, re-wooded thirty barren mountains, dug thirty trenches for irrigation, and drained thirty marshes." It is for lack of such industrial armies, says Fourier, that civilization is unable to produce anything great. [698]

## Historical Significance

The phalanx concept influenced a wide range of nineteenth-century utopian communities and experiments, particularly in France and the United States. [[albert-brisbane|Albert Brisbane]], Fourier's American advocate, promoted the phalanx model through his column in the *New York Tribune*, leading to the establishment of several Fourierist communities including [[brook-farm|Brook Farm]]. The *familistère* of the great steel works of Godin at Guise, in France, is another direct result of Fourier's inspiration. Mumford credits Fourier as "the first man who had a plan for colonizing the wilderness of industrial barbarism that existed at the beginning of the nineteenth century, and redeeming that wilderness to civilization" [704]. While none of these experiments achieved lasting success, the phalanx idea contributed to later developments in cooperative housing, [[garden-city-movement|garden city planning]], and intentional community design.
