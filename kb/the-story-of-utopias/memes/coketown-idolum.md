# Coketown Idolum

The **Coketown Idolum** is Lewis Mumford's term for the industrial age's dominant cultural pattern — the set of values, institutions, and behaviors organized around mass production and mechanical consumption. It is the "Frankenstein which had been created by the Country House" (010-045), representing the productive engine that feeds the Country House's insatiable demand.

## Origins

Coketown emerged as a direct consequence of the Country House idolum. "The Country House's passionate demand for physical goods has given rise to another institution, Coketown; and it is the idolum of Coketown, the industrial age's contribution to the Country House, that we have now to consider" (010-038).

Unlike the Country House, which was an aristocratic pattern that permeated all social strata, Coketown was specifically the environment of production — the world of factories, mines, and working-class dwellings. In its "pristine state, Coketown is not a complete community" (010-047). It existed to produce goods, not to sustain a full human life.

## Core Characteristics

The Coketown idolum operates according to a logic of endless production. "The aim of production in Coketown is naturally more production, and it is only by making things sufficiently shoddy to go to pieces quickly, or by changing the fashion sufficiently often, that the machinery of Coketown can for the most part be kept running" (010-054).

This production imperative has a corresponding consumption imperative: "The rage and fury of Coketown's production has to be balanced off by an equal rage and fury of consumption — continence would be fatal" (010-054). Nothing in Coketown is designed to last: "Coketown makes china to be broken, clothes to be worn out, and houses to be torn down" (010-054).

## Work in Coketown

Mumford draws on Samuel Butler's *Erewhon* to characterize labor under the Coketown idolum: "work in Coketown ... is in the main simply attendance upon machinery" (010-047). The human worker is disciplined to serve the machine, and education's purpose becomes "to discipline the younger generation to the machine, and to show them the necessity of doing nothing which would interfere with the continued activity of the machine" (010-047).

## The Human Cost

The Coketown idolum satisfies "only certain elements in the human makeup," and when the "igneous instincts of the workers ... break through the solidified layer of habit which the school and the factory have produced," the suppressed human energies "flow either into the Country House or into that other simulacrum of the civic life, Broadway" (010-047). The choice between aristocratic consumption and commercial entertainment is no choice at all — both are escape mechanisms from the poverty of Coketown's spiritual life.

## Critique and Value

Mumford is careful not to dismiss everything in Coketown. "There is a certain solid reality in Coketown that remains when all its pretensions and idiocies have been incinerated" (010-056). The industrial capacity to "melt steel and build roads and perform certain essential industrial operations" has genuine value — Mumford had already acknowledged this in his discussion of Andreae's Christianopolis, where science and industry serve the community.

However, "an environment that is devoted solely to the production of material goods is obviously no sort of environment for a good community, for life is more than a matter of finding what we shall eat and wherewithal we shall be clothed" (010-056). The task is not to destroy Coketown but to subordinate it to the demands of the good life.

## The Good Life as Check

"The determination of the good life is the only check and balance that we can have upon Coketown; and it is perhaps because we have been so little concerned with it that the practical effect of the Coketown idolum has been so devastating" (010-059). Mumford quotes George Santayana: "Invention and organization, which ought to have increased leisure by producing the necessaries of life with little labor, have only increased the population, degraded labor, and diffused luxury" (010-059).
