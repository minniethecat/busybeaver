# Regional Survey

The **Regional Survey** is the methodological foundation of Lewis Mumford's eutopian project — the systematic study of a region's actual conditions that provides the factual basis for genuine social reconstruction. The concept is derived from the work of Patrick Geddes and represents Mumford's insistence that utopian thinking must be grounded in concrete reality.

## Definition and Purpose

Regional survey is "the bridge by which the specialist whose face is turned towards the library and the laboratory, and the active worker in the field, whose face is turned towards the city and region in which he lives, may come into contact; and out of this contact our plans and our eutopias may be founded on such a permanent foundation of facts as the scientist can build for us" (012-030).

This is "the first step out of the present impasse: we must return to the real world, and face it, and survey it in its complicated totality. Our castles-in-air must have their foundations in solid ground" (012-030).

## Reality vs. Idolum

A central feature of regional survey is its capacity to cut through false idola. "In looking at the community through the Regional Survey, the investigator is dealing with a real thing and not with an arbitrary idolum" (012-028). The survey reveals actual conditions — economic, geographic, social, cultural — that exist regardless of the myths propagated by the National State or other partial idola.

"In so far as the local community has certain elements in common with similar regions in other countries, or has absorbed elements from other civilizations, these things will be given their full value, instead of being disregarded because they weaken the identity of the local community with that precious myth, the National State" (012-028).

## Method and Presentation

The data gathered through regional survey "may be plotted on a map, graphically presented in a chart, or photographed" (012-028). Mumford cites the museum at Saffron Walden, England, and the Outlook Tower in Edinburgh as exemplary institutions where "one could begin at the point where one was standing and work outwards, in thought, to embrace the whole wide world" (012-028).

Knowledge presented in this fashion "is available so that whoever runs may read; it has every feature, therefore, of popular science as it is purveyed in the cheap newspaper and magazine, whilst it remains real science" (012-028).

## Geddes's Contribution

Mumford identifies Patrick Geddes as "the outstanding exponent of the Eutopian method both in thought and in practical activity" (013-005). Geddes's works — particularly *City Development* (1904) and *Town-Planning Towards City Development* (1918) — are described as "mines from which all sorts of precious thoughts can be quarried" (013-005).

## Role in Eutopian Method

Regional survey is the first of three integrated dimensions of the eutopian method, alongside the integration of science and art and the development of specific plans. Without it, "all our day to day improvements have been wasteful sallies into eutopia, proceeding without order, without a sufficient equipment, and without any general plan" (012-015).
