# Industrialism

**Industrialism** is a key critical concept in [[lewis-mumford|Lewis Mumford]]'s *[[the-story-of-utopias|The Story of Utopias]]*, denoting not merely the technology of the Industrial Revolution but a comprehensive social order that reshaped utopian thought itself by subordinating questions of human value to problems of mechanical and economic organization.

## The Soil of Nineteenth-Century Utopias

Mumford presents industrialism as the transformative historical force that generated a new wave of utopian writing:

> "How something happened in the eighteenth century which made men 'furiously to think,' and how a whole group of utopias sprang out of the upturned soil of industrialism" (006-650).

The industrial age marks a decisive break in the utopian tradition. Between the seventeenth century and the nineteenth, the ideal commonwealth tradition faded into romance and escape literature (006-654). Industrialism revived utopian speculation — but on fundamentally altered terms.

## The Critique of Instrumentalism

Mumford's central charge against the industrial utopias is that they confuse means with ends:

> "These industrial utopias are no longer concerned with values but with means; they are all instrumentalist. I doubt whether an intelligent peasant in India or China would get out of the whole batch of these utopias a single idea which would have any bearing on the life that he has experienced — so little of human significance remains when the problems of mechanical and political organization have been disposed of!" (006-670).

This instrumentalism represents a profound narrowing of the utopian imagination. Where [[plato|Plato]] and [[sir-thomas-more|Sir Thomas More]] had attempted to picture the whole life of man, the industrial utopians — even those who criticized industrialism — "tend to magnify the importance of the industrial order ... and in doing this they lose sight of the whole life of man" (006-670).

## Coketown as Industrial Idolum

The concrete form of industrialism in Mumford's analysis is the [[coketown-idolum|Coketown idolum]], the factory-centered city that reduces all institutions to instruments of production. Drawing on Dickens's *Hard Times*, Mumford describes a world where "the jail looked like the town hall, and the town hall like the infirmary — and all of them looked like the factory" (008-1119).

The factory became "the only social unit" (008-1119), and the city's physical layout — mathematically correct grids with no regard for topography, vista, or community — reflected a deeper spiritual poverty.

## Mummford's Qualified Defense

Mumford does not reject industrialism wholesale. He recognizes genuine achievements:

> "Up to a certain point, then, using mechanical power rather than human power is good; so is large-scale production, so is the division of labor and division of operation; so is rapid transportation; so is the accurate methodology of the engineer" (008-1151).

The problem is not industrial technology itself but the assumption that its goods are absolute. Coketown "made the horrid mistake of believing that all these things were good in themselves" (008-1151). The remedy is the [[good-life|good life]] — the Aristotelian criterion that asks of every institution and technique whether it serves human flourishing:

> "Up to a certain point, industrialism is good, especially in its modern, neotechnic, electrical phase: Coketown, on the other hand, believes that there is no limit to the usefulness of industrialism" (008-1152).

## Industrial Utopias of Reconstruction vs. Escape

The industrial utopias of reconstruction (Bellamy, Hertzka, Buckingham) shared "a deadly sameness of purpose and a depressing singleness of interest" — they saw social reconstruction as "a simple problem of industrial reorganization" (009-944). By contrast, the utopias of escape, particularly [[william-morris|William Morris]]'s *News from Nowhere*, preserved a connection to human creativity and dignity:

> "He knows that the chief dignity of man lies not in what he consumes but in what he creates, and that the Manchester ideal is — devastatingly consumptive" (009-944).
