## Introduction

*Reflections on Violence* (*Réflexions sur la violence*) is a book by French philosopher and syndicalist theorist [[george-sorel|Georges Sorel]] (1847-1922), first published in 1908. The work argues that political movements are driven not by rational calculation but by "[[national-state-idolum|social myth]]s" — powerful, emotionally charged narratives that inspire collective action. Sorel's prime example was the myth of the general strike, which he saw as the driving force behind revolutionary syndicalism.

[[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] cites Sorel's work in his discussion of [[national-state-idolum|social myth]]s and [[social-myth|collective utopia]]s, writing that "there is a considerable literature that relates to these myths in French, one of the best known works being M. George Sorel's *Reflections on Violence*" (011-005). For [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]], Sorel's concept of the [[national-state-idolum|social myth]] provides a bridge between the classic literary utopia and the collective belief systems that actually shape history.

## Content and Key Ideas

Sorel argues that the most powerful forces in political life are not rational arguments or material interests but myths — compelling narratives about the future that mobilize collective action. The general strike serves as his central example: whether or not such a strike ever actually occurs, the myth of the general strike gives workers a sense of purpose, solidarity, and historical direction.

Sorel distinguishes between myths (which inspire action) and utopias (which describe ideal states). [[utopia-more|Utopia]]s, in his view, are intellectual constructions that describe a finished, perfected society. Myths, by contrast, are not descriptions but calls to action — they organize the present rather than depict the future. This distinction would prove influential for [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]]'s own analysis of the relationship between utopian thought and social change.

## Mumford's Assessment

[[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] draws on Sorel to develop his concept of "idola" — the collective [[national-state-idolum|social myth]]s that shape civilization. He writes:

> Up to the present the idola which have exercised the most considerable influence upon the actual life of the community are such as have been partly expressed in a hundred works and never perhaps fully expressed in one. In order to distinguish these idola from those that have occupied us till now, we should perhaps call them collective utopias or social myths. There is a considerable literature that relates to these myths in French, one of the best known works being M. George Sorel's *Reflections on Violence*; and in practice it is sometimes rather hard to tell where the Utopia leaves off and the social myth begins. (011-005)

This passage is significant for [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]]'s overall argument. He recognizes that the boundary between the literary utopia and the [[national-state-idolum|social myth]] is porous — both are attempts to give form to collective aspirations and ideals. The utopia of the book and the myth that moves the crowd are different expressions of the same fundamental human drive: the will-to-utopia.

[[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] places Sorel's work alongside [[thorstein-veblen|Thorstein Veblen]]'s *[[theory-of-the-leisure-class|Theory of the Leisure Class]]* and Benjamin Kidd's *Science of Power* as key texts for understanding the role of myths and ideals in social life (013-004). However, [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] differentiates his own project from Sorel's: "The type of myth that concerns us here is not the pure action myth which M. Sorel has analyzed; we are rather interested in those myths which are, as it were, the ideal content of the existing order of things" (011-007).

## Place in the Utopian Tradition

Sorel's *Reflections on Violence* is not a utopia in the literary sense — it describes no ideal commonwealth. Yet it is essential to the story of utopias because it theorizes the mechanism by which shared visions of the future shape human action in the present. For [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]], understanding this mechanism is crucial: it explains why utopias matter, and why the study of utopias is not an antiquarian pastime but an engagement with the forces that drive history.

Sorel's distinction between myth and utopia also provides [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] with a critical tool. The classic utopias [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] examines — from [[plato|Plato]] to Wells — are intellectual constructions, but they also function as [[national-state-idolum|social myth]]s when they capture the imagination of communities and inspire action. The story of utopias is, in part, the story of how literary visions become collective myths, and how collective myths shape the world we actually inhabit.
