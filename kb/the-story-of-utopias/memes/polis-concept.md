## Definition

The **Polis** was the ancient Greek city-state — a self-governing urban community surrounded by enough agricultural land to sustain its population. In [[the-story-of-utopias]], Mumford identifies the polis as the original setting in which the utopian imagination first took shape: "Before the great empires of Rome and Macedonia began to spread their camps through the length and breadth of the Mediterranean world, there was a time when the vision of an ideal city seems to have been uppermost in the minds of a good many men" (202-208). Unlike the modern nation-state, which spans vast territories and contains millions of citizens who never meet, the polis was "a definite parcel of land whose limits he could probably see from any convenient hilltop" (228-228), where citizens shared "common gods to worship, common theaters and gymnasia, and a multitude of common interests that could be satisfied only by their working together, playing together, thinking together" (228-228).

## Core Characteristics

The polis was characterized by three defining features:

**Human scale.** The community was limited to a size where all citizens could participate directly in public life. "Plato said that 'the city may increase to any size which is consistent with its unity; that is the limit'" (268-268). Mumford notes approvingly that Plato anticipated "by more than two thousand years the verdict of modern town planners like Mr. Raymond Unwin" (268-268) in recognizing that beyond a certain population, the shared life of citizenship becomes impossible.

**Geographic self-containment.** The ideal polis occupied what geographers call a "valley section" — a city "surrounded by enough land to supply the greater part of the food needed by the inhabitants; and placed convenient to the sea" (230-230). This compressed "as many different kinds of agricultural and industrial life as one could single out in going down the Hudson valley from the Adirondack Mountains to New York Harbor" (234-234).

**Shared commonwealth.** To the Greek citizen, "the commonwealth was something he actively shared with his fellow citizens" (228-228). This stood in stark contrast to the modern state, where a citizen's relationship to distant territories is purely economic — Mumford observes that "there is scarcely any conceivable way in which a Dutchman in Rotterdam possesses the Island of Java" (226-226) except through abstract financial interests.

## Historical Context in Mumford's Narrative

Mumford situates the polis in a specific historical moment: the period between 600 and 300 BC, which he describes as "city-building years for the parent cities of Greece" (210-210). The city of Miletus alone "is supposed to have begotten some three hundred cities" (210-210). This era of colonization and civic experimentation created conditions analogous to eighteenth-century America, where "the wide expanse of unsettled territory caused the people to think of building a civilization in which the errors and vices and superstitions of the old world might be left behind" (208-208).

The polis provided the physical and conceptual foundation for [[plato]]'s *Republic*, which Mumford treats as the archetypal utopia. Mumford argues that Plato's ideal city was not a fantasy but a deliberate re-imagination of Athenian reality: "it is an ideal Sparta and an ideal Athens that Plato has in mind" (218-218). The Republic's insistence on moderation — "a mode of life which was neither impoverished nor luxurious" (256-256) — derived directly from the polis's bounded geography and economy: "Plato saw clearly that an ideal community must have a common physical standard of living; and that boundless wealth or unlimited desires and gratifications had nothing to do with a good standard" (254-254).

## Relation to Other Concepts

The polis stands as the **positive original** against which Mumford's later critical concepts gain meaning. The [[idolum]] of the [[national-state-idolum|National State]] and its instrument, the [[megalopolis]], represent the degradation of the polis ideal into something parasitic and abstract. Where the polis enabled "direct action, direct intercourse, direct association" (1191-1191), the modern Megalopolis reduces all life to paper transactions. Mumford's concept of [[eutopia]] — the good place that is also a possible place — represents an attempt to recover, at modern scale, the polis's integration of physical environment, civic participation, and human proportion.

The polis also connects to Mumford's [[regional-survey]] methodology: just as the polis was rooted in its valley section, so the good modern community must begin with a thorough understanding of its regional geography, resources, and limits.
