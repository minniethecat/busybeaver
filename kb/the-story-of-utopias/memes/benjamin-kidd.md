## Introduction

Benjamin Kidd (1858-1916) was a British sociologist and civil servant whose works on social evolution achieved wide popularity at the turn of the twentieth century. His major books, *Social Evolution* (1894) and *The Science of Power* (1918, posthumous), argued that religious sanctions and collective ideals — rather than rational self-interest — were the driving forces of social progress. Mumford cites Kidd alongside Georges Sorel as a theorist who recognized the power of myths and ideals in shaping social behavior.

Mumford's reference to Kidd is brief but strategically placed — in the concluding acknowledgments of *The Story of Utopias*, where he recommends: "There is a fairly popular discussion of the place of myths and ideals in the George Sorel's *Reflections on Violence*, and Benjamin Kidd's *Science of Power* (especially Chapter V.)" (012-061).

## The Science of Power

Kidd's *The Science of Power*, published posthumously in 1918, attempted to synthesize evolutionary biology, sociology, and psychology into a comprehensive theory of social change. Chapter V, which Mumford specifically recommends, develops Kidd's argument that emotional and religious motivations — what he called "the emotion of the ideal" — are more powerful drivers of human behavior than rational calculation of self-interest.

This argument aligns closely with Mumford's own analysis of "idola" and "social myths." Kidd maintained that civilization depends on collective emotional commitments to ideals that transcend individual advantage — an insight that Mumford applies to both the destructive myths of nationalism and industrialism, and the constructive potential of genuine utopian thinking.
