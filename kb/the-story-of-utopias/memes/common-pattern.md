# Common Pattern

The **Common Pattern** is Lewis Mumford's concept of the shared cultural framework — the set of common idola, values, and aspirations — that enables a community to produce coherent, beautiful, and life-enhancing environments. It is the cultural precondition for successful eutopian reconstruction.

## Definition

Mumford develops the concept in his concluding chapter by contrasting the aesthetic coherence of medieval cities with the chaos of modern industrial towns. Medieval civilization succeeded in creating beautiful urban environments not through legislation or centralized planning, but because "everyone within these ... centers accepted the same values and pursued the same ends" (012-059).

The absence of such a common pattern in the modern world explains the failure of piecemeal reforms. "Without the common background of eutopian idola, all our efforts at rehabilitation — the new architecture, the garden city movement, the electrification of industry, the organization of great industrial guilds ... without these common idola, I say, all our practical efforts are spotty and inconsecutive and incomplete" (012-059).

## Mechanism

The common pattern operates through shared idola — collectively held ideas, values, and images that guide action without requiring explicit coordination. The same process that produced Coketown — "everyone within these horrid centers accepted the same values and pursued the same ends — as they were projected by economists like Ricardo, industrialists like Stephenson, and lyric poets like Samuel Smiles" — can, "when our world of ideas is transformed, give us something better than Coketown" (012-059).

## Cultural Prerequisite

Mumford's argument implies that physical and institutional reforms are necessary but insufficient. The deeper task is cultural: transforming the idola that people carry in their heads so that better physical environments naturally follow from better shared values. This is the insight that distinguishes Mumford's eutopianism from both technocratic planning and purely moral exhortation.
