# Will-to-Utopia

The **will-to-utopia** is Lewis Mumford's term for the persistent human impulse toward utopian thinking — an innate drive that survives the failure or suppression of any particular utopian form. It represents the anthropological constant underlying all utopian expression.

## Definition

Mumford introduces the concept in Chapter III while discussing the apparent disappearance of utopian literature after the classical period. "If the idea of utopia loses its practical hold during this period, the will-to-utopia remains; and the rise of the monastic system and the attempts of the great popes from Hildebrand onward to establish a universal empire under the shield of the church show that, as always, there was a breach between the ideas which people carried in their heads and the things which actual circumstances and going institutions compelled them to do" (003-004).

## Distinction from Specific Utopias

The will-to-utopia is distinct from any particular utopian content. It is the underlying motivational force that drives humans to imagine better worlds, even when the specific forms those imaginations take are constrained or channeled by historical circumstances. When one utopian form collapses — as the Kingdom of Heaven "ceased to hold men's allegiance when they discovered other channels and other possibilities" — the will-to-utopia finds new expression (003-004).

## The Will-to-Eutopia

In the concluding chapter, Mumford transforms the concept into the **will-to-eutopia** — a more directed and purposive form of the impulse, oriented specifically toward achievable reconstruction rather than escapist fantasy. "Our choice is not between eutopia and the world as it is, but between eutopia and nothing — or rather, nothingness. Other civilizations have proved inimical to the good life and have failed and past away; and there is nothing but our own will-to-eutopia to prevent us from following them" (012-004).

## Psychological Basis

The will-to-utopia is rooted in Mumford's understanding of the idolum. Because humans inevitably live in "two worlds — the world within and the world without" (001-009), the drive to shape the inner world into coherent patterns is as fundamental as the drive to satisfy physical needs. The question is not whether we will have utopian thinking, but whether that thinking will take the form of "an aimless utopia of escape" or "a purposive utopia of reconstruction" (001-013).

## Historical Persistence

Throughout *The Story of Utopias*, Mumford demonstrates the will-to-utopia's persistence across historical epochs: in Plato's Republic, in the monastic movements of the Middle Ages, in the Renaissance utopias, in the nineteenth-century socialist experiments, and in the garden city movement of the twentieth century. Each represents a different expression of the same underlying drive.
