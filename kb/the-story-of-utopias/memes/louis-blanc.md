## Overview

Louis Blanc (1811-1882) was a French politician, journalist, and socialist theorist whose proposal for state-organized "National Workshops" (Ateliers Nationaux) made him a central figure in the revolutionary ferment of 1848. Mumford discusses Blanc as an example of utopian ideas translated into practical, if short-lived, institutional experiments.

## Role in The Story of Utopias

Mumford situates Blanc's National Workshops within the context of Étienne Cabet's utopian socialism and the broader momentum of social reform in mid-nineteenth-century France:

> To understand why the Journey to Icaria, as we may call it, should have been one of the best sellers among workingmen in 1845, and to see why Louis Blanc should have attempted to set up an organization of National Workshops in 1848, one must realize the historic momentum of Napoleon's dictatorship. (008-004)

This passage links Blanc's practical reforms with the wider utopian impulse that Mumford traces through the book. Blanc's "organization of National Workshops" represented an attempt to give institutional form to the ideal of the right to work — a principle that connected utopian speculation to direct economic intervention by the state.

## The National Workshops

Blanc's central idea, elaborated in *L'Organisation du travail* (1839), was that the state should establish cooperative workshops where workers could own and manage production collectively. Blanc argued that competition was the source of poverty and degradation, and that only by replacing competitive markets with cooperative production could workers achieve genuine freedom.

After the February Revolution of 1848, Blanc served in the provisional government and secured the creation of National Workshops. However, the workshops established by the government bore little resemblance to Blanc's vision — they were essentially unemployment relief programs of unskilled public works, and their rapid closure in June 1848 triggered the bloody June Days uprising.

## Significance

Blanc illustrates a recurring pattern in Mumford's account: the difficulty of translating utopian ideals into functioning institutions. His National Workshops were an attempt to bridge the gap between utopian vision and political reality, but they foundered on the resistance of existing power structures and the gap between theoretical design and practical implementation.
