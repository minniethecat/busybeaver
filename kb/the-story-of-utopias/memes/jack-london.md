# Jack London

## Introduction

Jack London (1876-1916) was a prolific American novelist, journalist, and social activist, best known for adventure novels such as *The Call of the Wild* (1903) and *The Sea-Wolf* (1904), as well as dystopian works including *The Iron Heel* (1908). In *The Story of Utopias*, Lewis Mumford mentions London in Chapter VIII, grouping him with other popular American writers whose work shaped the imaginative horizons of the rising generation.

## London in Mumford's Account

In his critique of the state of American art and its relation to the community, Mumford writes:

> Because of the limited horizons of the American artist, therefore, the rising generation aspires after the things that Messrs. Jack London, Rupert Hughes, Robert Chambers, and heaven knows who else have thought good and fine; the younger generation talks like the heroes and heroines of a melodrama by Mr. Samuel Shipman, when they do not attain the higher level of comic cuts; the younger generation thrills to the type of beauty which Mr. Penryhn Stanlaws sets before its gaze. (012-048)

Mumford places London within a broader diagnosis of American cultural mediocrity. The argument is that when genuine artists fail to engage with the community, the public turns to lesser figures — "artlings" — who idealize the crude and the commercial. London appears here not as a target of direct condemnation but as a symptom: his popularity, for Mumford, reflects the vacuum left by the withdrawal of serious artists from public life. The antidote, Mumford suggests, is not better popular entertainment but the re-engagement of genuine artists — like Eugene O'Neill or William Butler Yeats — with the task of providing the community with worthy imaginative patterns.

## The Iron Heel and Utopian Themes

London's *The Iron Heel* (1908) is among the earliest modern dystopian novels, depicting a future American oligarchy crushed by a socialist revolution. Though Mumford does not discuss the novel directly, its fusion of political critique with speculative narrative places London within the broader utopian-dystopian literary tradition that *The Story of Utopias* surveys. London's socialist convictions — he was an active member of the Socialist Party of America — align him with the utopian impulse toward social reconstruction, even as his naturalist literary method and grim depictions of class conflict distinguish him from the more optimistic utopian writers Mumford discusses elsewhere.

## London and the American Utopian Tradition

London occupies a distinctive position in the American utopian literary tradition. Unlike [[edward-bellamy]], whose *[[looking-backward]]* (1888) presented a peaceful, rationally organized socialist future, London's *The Iron Heel* depicted a violent revolutionary path. This divergence reflects a broader tension within American utopian thought between gradual reform and revolutionary transformation — a tension that also characterized the debates between the [[fabian-society]] and more militant socialist organizations. London's naturalist aesthetic, with its emphasis on harsh environmental and economic forces, represented a literary counterpart to the [[socialist-movement]]'s insistence that capitalism could not be reformed from within.

Mumford's own utopianism, developed in later works like *[[the-culture-of-cities]]*, occupies a middle ground: he shares London's critique of industrial capitalism but insists, like Bellamy, that reconstruction can be achieved through rational planning rather than violent upheaval.
