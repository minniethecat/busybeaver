# National State

## Definition

In Lewis Mumford's *The Story of Utopias*, the **National State** is a modern "social myth" — a collective utopia that functions as an idolum. Unlike the [[country-house|Country House]] (rooted in aristocratic privilege) or [[coketown]] (driven by industrial production), the National State is what Mumford calls the "connecting tissue" that binds these separate worlds together into a coherent political framework. It is not a natural fact but a fabrication — a projection that must be continually *willed* into existence.

> In fact, it is only by the persistent projection of this utopia for the last three or four hundred years that its existence has become credible; for all the minute descriptions which the political historian gives to the National State, its origins and its institutions and its people, read a good deal like that fine story which Hans Andersen told about the king who walked the streets naked because two rascally tailors had persuaded him that they had woven and cut up for him a beautiful outfit of clothes. (XI-1161)

## The Connecting Tissue

The National State serves a crucial structural function in Mumford's system of modern idola. While the Country House pursues aristocratic leisure and Coketown pursues industrial production, these two worlds would remain disconnected without a unifying framework:

> By what means can the Country House keep Coketown working for it? The idolum of the Country House, which was built up during the Renaissance, and the idolum of Coketown, which was formed in the early part of the nineteenth century, are obviously two separate worlds; and in order that each might be realized in our daily life, it was necessary that some connecting tissue be manufactured to keep them together. This tissue was the social myth, the collective utopia, of the National State. (VII-1159)

## Against Geographic Reality

A key element of Mumford's critique is that the National State bears "only the most casual relation to the facts of geography" (XI-1171). The geographer and anthropologist see natural regions defined by soil, climate, vegetation, and historic tradition — but the National State overrides these organic boundaries:

> Wherever it suits the purposes of the Guardians of the State, the facts are ignored, and an artificial relation is *willed* into existence. The human communities which the regional sociologist recognizes do not always coincide with those which the statesman wishes to incorporate as "national territory," and when this conflict occurs, the idea rather than the reality triumphs, if necessary by brute force. (XI-1171)

This tension between utopian projection and geographic reality is central to Mumford's understanding of the National State as an idolum — it substitutes an abstract, territorially-bounded community for the concrete relationships of regional life that had sustained civilization for centuries before the rise of nationalism.

## The Scale Problem

Mumford contrasts the National State's vast scale with the classical utopian tradition. [[plato|Plato]] limited his Republic to 5,040 citizens — "about the number that can be conveniently addressed by a single orator" — because he understood that "when you increase the number of people in a community you decrease the number of things that they can share in common" (I-268). The modern National State, with its millions of citizens, reverses this logic entirely: it creates a community so vast that genuine civic participation becomes impossible, replaced by the abstract bond of national identity and the threat of external enemies.

## Cabet's Icaria: The National State as Utopia

Mumford traces the National State ideal through the utopian tradition itself, most notably in [[etienne-cabet|Étienne Cabet's]] *Voyage en Icarie* (1840). Cabet's Icaria, Mumford argues, "consciously or unconsciously idealized the Napoleonic tradition" — it was "a national state, with all its pomp and dignity and splendor, and not a squalid collection of huts in the midst of a dreary prairie" (VI-851). Cabet's vision of a "National State, abundantly organized for war, and remaining on that footing in the midst of its peacetime activities" (VI-883) reveals how deeply the national-state form had penetrated utopian imagination by the mid-nineteenth century.

## Mumford's Rejection

Mumford's verdict is unambiguous. In his concluding vision of eutopia, he declares:

> Finally, if we are to give eutopia a local habitation it will not be founded upon the National State, for the National State is a myth which sane people will no more sacrifice their lives to than they would hand their children into the furnace of some tribal Moloch; and a good idolum cannot be founded on the basis of a bad one. (XII-1505)

For Mumford, the National State is not merely incomplete as a framework for human flourishing — it is actively destructive, a "bad idolum" that channels the genuine human impulse toward community into the deadly competitions of nation-states.
