# Marquesas Islands

The Marquesas Islands are a group of volcanic islands in French Polynesia, in the South Pacific Ocean. In *The Story of Utopias*, Lewis Mumford cites them as a limit case for utopian thought — a society that, in its pre-contact state, had no need for utopias at all.

## The Marquesan Exception

Mumford introduces the Marquesas in the context of qualifying his claim that all people and communities need utopias. He acknowledges an important exception:

> The savages of the Marquesas whom Hermann Melville described seem to have had such a jolly and complete adjustment to their environment that, except for the raids of hostile tribes — and this turned out to be chiefly sport which only whetted their appetites for the feast that followed — everything needed for a good life at the South Sea level could be obtained by direct attack. The Marquesans had no need to dream of a happier existence; they had only to grab it. (P01-151)

## Utopian Implications

The Marquesan example serves a precise function in Mumford's argument. It demonstrates that the need for utopia is not a universal feature of human existence but a response to specific conditions — particularly to environments that frustrate direct satisfaction of human needs. Mumford distinguishes between the "aimless utopia of escape" and the "purposive utopia of reconstruction," and the Marquesans represent a third possibility: a life so complete in itself that neither escape nor reconstruction is called for (P01-149).

However, Mumford does not hold up the Marquesans as an ideal. He immediately notes that such completeness is fragile and narrow: "People who will not venture out into the open sea pay the penalty of never having looked into the bright eyes of danger; and at best they know but half of life" (P01-153). The Marquesan model, however enviable in its adjustment, lacks the "complete circle" of a fully realized human existence (P01-153).
