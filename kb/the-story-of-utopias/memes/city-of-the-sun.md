# City of the Sun

*The City of the Sun* (*La Città del Sole*, 1602; published as *Civitas Solis Poetica: Idea Reipublicae Philosophiae*, 1637) is a utopian dialogue by the Calabrian Dominican friar [[tommaso-campanella|Tommaso Campanella]] (1568-1639). [[lewis-mumford|Lewis Mumford]] treats it briefly in Chapter V of *The Story of [[utopia-more|Utopia]]s*, judging it largely derivative of [[republic-plato|Plato]] and [[utopia-more|More]] while acknowledging two "significant passages" that mark genuine contributions to the utopian tradition (005-004).

## Historical Context

[[tommaso-campanella|Campanella]] wrote while imprisoned by the Spanish Inquisition for his role in a Calabrian conspiracy against Spanish rule. His utopia "existed in manuscript before Andreae wrote his *[[christianopolis|Christianopolis]]*" (005-002), making it roughly contemporary with [[christianopolis|Andreae's work]] and [[new-atlantis|Bacon's fragment]]. Unlike Andreae's sober Humanism or [[francis-bacon|Bacon]]'s institutional vision, [[tommaso-campanella|Campanella]]'s utopia reflects the mind of a "lonely monk" — exotic, astrological, and shaped by monastic ideals of communal life.

## Summary of the Work

The narrative is framed as a dialogue: a Genoese sea-captain, guest of a Grand Master of the Knights Hospitaller, describes a great country under the equator dominated by the City of the Sun. The city is built in seven concentric rings named after the seven planets, with four gates opening to the four quarters of the earth. At its summit stands a grand temple, and the walls are covered with laws, alphabets, and paintings of natural phenomena — a visual encyclopedia for public education (005-002).

The city is ruled by a priestly hierarchy headed by three princes — Power, Wisdom, and Love — assisted by learned doctors with titles like Astrologus, Cosmographus, and Arithmeticus. [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] calls it "an apparition such as never yet was seen on land or sea" (005-002).

As in [[republic-plato|Plato's Republic]], there is complete community of property, community of wives, and equality of the sexes. The younger serve the elders, and science is imparted by visual demonstration — a practice [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] notes is also found in [[christianopolis|Christianopolis]] (005-003).

## Mumford's Assessment

[[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] is candid about the work's limitations:

> "After we have become familiar with the outward color and form of the landscape, we discover, alas! that it is not a foreign country we are exploring, but a sort of picture puzzle put together out of fragments from Plato and More." (005-003)

When one subtracts what [[plato|Plato]] and [[sir-thomas-more|More]] contributed, "very little indeed remains" (005-003). [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] ranks [[tommaso-campanella|Campanella]] below Andreae among the three early seventeenth-century utopians, describing him as "quite second-rate in comparison" with Andreae's "fine intelligence and candor" (004-003).

Nevertheless, [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] identifies two points on which [[tommaso-campanella|Campanella]]'s observation is "remarkably keen" (005-004):

### Anticipation of Mechanical Invention

[[tommaso-campanella|Campanella]] imagines wagons driven by wind and boats "which go over the waters without rowers or the force of the wind, but by a marvelous contrivance" (005-004). A character exclaims: "Oh, if you knew what our astrologers say of the coming age, that has in it more history within a hundred years than all the world had in four thousand years before! Of the wonderful invention of printing and guns, and the use of the magnet…" (005-004).

With mechanical arts fully developed, labor is dignified and reduced: not more than four hours of work per day:

> "They are rich because they want nothing; poor because they possess nothing; and consequently they are not slaves to circumstances, but circumstances serve them." (005-004)

### The Problem of Private Property and the Household

[[tommaso-campanella|Campanella]]'s second insight is his analysis of how private property and the private household threaten the commonwealth. He argues that "property is formed among us and maintained by having our own separate houses, and our own wives and children" (005-005). From this diagnosis he concludes — as [[plato|Plato]] did — that all must be held in common.

[[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] notes a biographical pattern: "[[sir-thomas-more|More]] and Andreae are married men, and they stand for the individual family. [[plato|Plato]] and [[tommaso-campanella|Campanella]] were bachelors, and they proposed that men should live like monks or soldiers" (005-008).

## Place in the Utopian Tradition

[[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] places [[tommaso-campanella|Campanella]] alongside [[francis-bacon|Bacon]] at "the entrance to the utopia of means" (005-015) — the historical turn from utopias that asked what ends men should pursue to utopias that sought to perfect the instruments of human life. [[tommaso-campanella|Campanella]]'s mechanical anticipations, like [[francis-bacon|Bacon]]'s scientific institutes, reflect the Renaissance's dawning recognition that human power over nature could be systematically expanded.

But [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]]'s ultimate judgment is historical rather than literary. He groups *City of the Sun* with those utopias that, however colorful, did not substantially advance the tradition's central problem: how to reconcile individual family life with the common good. That problem, which [[tommaso-campanella|Campanella]] "loyally follows [[plato|Plato]]" in solving through abolition of the private household (005-008), would remain unresolved in the utopian tradition — and [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] suggests that the answer may lie not in choosing between [[plato|Plato]]'s monasticism and [[sir-thomas-more|More]]'s familism, but in finding forms of community that accommodate both.
