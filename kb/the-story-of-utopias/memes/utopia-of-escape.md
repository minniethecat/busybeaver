# Utopia of Escape

The **utopia of escape** is one of the two fundamental types of utopian thinking identified by Lewis Mumford in *The Story of Utopias* (1922). It corresponds to the escapist function of the idolum — the inner world of ideas — and represents the human tendency to flee from difficult realities into imaginary worlds of perfection rather than working to transform actual conditions.

## Definition

Mumford defines the utopia of escape in opposition to the utopia of reconstruction. While both arise from the idolum's two functions, the utopia of escape "leaves the external world the way it is" (001-012). It seeks "an immediate release from the difficulties or frustrations of our lot" without attempting to change the conditions that produce those frustrations (001-012).

In the utopia of escape, Mumford writes, "we build impossible castles in the air" — in contrast to the utopia of reconstruction, where "we consult a surveyor and an architect and a mason and proceed to build a house which meets our essential needs" (001-012).

## Function

The utopia of escape serves as a psychological refuge. Mumford describes it vividly:

> "In the midst of the clanking machinery of a paper factory I have come across a moving picture actress's portrait, stuck upon an inoperative part of the machine; and it was not hard to reconstruct the private utopia of the man who minded the levers, or to picture the world into which he had fled from the roar and throb and muck of the machinery about him" (001-019).

This function is not inherently pathological. "For the most part," Mumford acknowledges, "this is an idle dream, and if we do not grow out of it, we must at any rate thrust other conditions into it; but for a good many of us, idleness without a dream is the only alternative" (001-021). Much of art and literature, he argues, has grown from such "fantasies of bliss and perfection" (001-021).

## Dangers

Despite its psychological value, the utopia of escape poses a significant danger when it becomes a permanent residence. Mumford warns that "once we have weathered the storm, it is dangerous to remain in the utopia of escape; for it is an enchanted island, and to remain there is to lose one's capacity for dealing with things as they are" (001-022).

The danger is twofold: the utopia of escape is "too easy" and "too blankly perfect — there is nothing to sharpen your teeth upon" (001-022). Mumford insists that human vitality requires more substance: "Our daily diet must have more roughage in it than these daydreams will give us if we are not to become debilitated" (001-022).

## Historical Manifestations

Mumford identifies the Christian Kingdom of Heaven as the paradigmatic utopia of escape in Western history. After utopia "dropped out of literature" in the post-classical period, "the utopia of the first fifteen hundred years after Christ is transplanted to the sky, and called the Kingdom of Heaven. It is distinctly a utopia of escape" (003-003).

In this conception, "the world as men find it is full of sin and trouble. Nothing can be done about it except to repent of the sin and find refuge from the trouble in the life after the grave" (003-003). Happiness is deferred to "the ultimate compensation" rather than pursued through action in the present world (003-003). This model, Mumford notes, "ceased to hold men's allegiance when they discovered other channels and other possibilities" (003-004).

## Relation to Idolum

The utopia of escape corresponds to the first function of the idolum: "the pseudo-environment or idolum is a substitute for the external world; it is a sort of house of refuge to which we flee when our contacts with 'hard facts' become too complicated to carry through or too rough to face" (001-012).

The genuine human alternative, Mumford argues, is not whether to have utopian thinking at all — for "men are so constituted that only by a deliberate discipline ... can one or the other world be abolished from consciousness" — but rather "between an aimless utopia of escape and a purposive utopia of reconstruction" (001-013).
