# Renaissance

The Renaissance was a period of European cultural, artistic, and intellectual revival spanning roughly the fourteenth to seventeenth centuries. In *The Story of Utopias*, Lewis Mumford treats the Renaissance as a pivotal moment in the history of utopian thought — a period that simultaneously opened new human possibilities and generated new forms of social idolatry. For Mumford, the Renaissance is not merely a backdrop to More's *Utopia* but the very condition of its possibility.

## More's Utopia as Renaissance Bridge

Mumford identifies the Renaissance as the specific historical context that made More's *Utopia* possible. He writes that "*Utopia* was the bridge by which More sought to span the gap between the old order of the Middle Age, and the new interests and institutions of the Renaissance" (P01-125). The collision of two forces — the rediscovery of Greek thought and the exploration of new continents — created the intellectual conditions for utopian speculation.

Mumford captures this convergence vividly: "Conceive of the world of ideas which Greek literature had just opened up coming headlong against the new lands which the magnetic compass had given men the courage to explore, and utopia, as a fresh conception of the good life, becomes a throbbing possibility" (P01-377). Raphael Hythloday, the narrator of More's *Utopia*, embodies this double inheritance: "learned in Greek" yet a world traveler who "has gone adventuring for other continents with Americus Vesputius" (P01-377).

Hythloday "has abandoned Aristotle, whom the schoolmen had butchered and had made pemmican of, and through his conquest of Greek he has come into possession of that new learning which stems back to Plato; and his brain is teeming with the criticisms and suggestions of a strange, pagan philosophy" (P01-377). For Mumford, this liberation from scholasticism — the recovery of Greek philosophy unmediated by medieval commentary — is the essential intellectual precondition of utopian thought.

## The Shift to Means

Mumford charts a critical shift within the Renaissance from utopias of ends to utopias of means. With Campanella's *City of the Sun* and Bacon's *New Atlantis*, "we stand at the entrance to the utopia of means; that is to say, the place in which all that materially contributes to the good life has been perfected" (P01-639). While the earlier utopias "were concerned to establish the things which men should aim for in life," the later Renaissance utopias "took these aims for granted and discussed how man's scope of action might be broadened" (P01-639).

This shift, Mumford argues, is epochal. The utopia of means — concerned with tools, techniques, material conditions — becomes the dominant mode of modern utopian thought, culminating in the industrial utopias of the nineteenth century. Mumford's critique of the Coketown idolum and the "utopia of means" running unchecked traces its genealogy back to this Renaissance turning point.

## The Country House Idolum

The Renaissance also gave rise to what Mumford calls the idolum of the Country House — a pattern of aristocratic consumption that, he argues, shaped the entire course of Western industrial civilization. He describes "this renaissance idolum of the Country House" as "powerful and complete," noting that "I know no other pattern which has imposed its standards and its practices with such complete success upon the greater part of European civilization" (P01-1069).

The architecture of the Country House — "that bastard Greek or Roman which we call Renaissance architecture" (P01-1081) — became the physical template for power, while its standards of consumption drove what Mumford calls "our Acquisitive Society" (P01-1069). The Country House represents for Mumford the dark side of the Renaissance legacy: the translation of classical ideals into aristocratic display that would ultimately fuel the material excesses of industrial capitalism.

Mumford's critique of the Country House idolum reveals his dialectical treatment of the Renaissance. The same period that recovered Greek philosophy and made utopian speculation possible also crystallized patterns of power and consumption that produced new forms of social distortion. The Renaissance is, in Mumford's analysis, simultaneously liberating and entrapping — a judgment that foreshadows his ambivalent treatment of modern technology throughout his career.

## The Renaissance and the Two Utopias

The Renaissance occupies a pivotal position in Mumford's larger argument about the "two utopias" — the utopia of reconstruction and the utopia of escape. The Country House represents the utopia of escape: a private paradise for the few, built on exclusion and display. But the same Renaissance intellectual resources — Greek philosophy, geographic discovery, the critical method — also made possible the utopia of reconstruction: the systematic project of reimagining community life that runs from More through the nineteenth-century utopians to Mumford's own Regional Survey movement.
