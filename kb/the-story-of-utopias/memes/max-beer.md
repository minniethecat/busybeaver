Max Beer (1864–1943) was an Austrian-born Marxist historian and journalist, best known for his *History of British Socialism* (1919), a work extensively cited by [[lewis-mumford]] in *[[the-story-of-utopias]]*.

## Bacon, More, and the Two Paths to Utopia

Mumford draws on Beer's analysis to articulate a central tension in utopian thought — the divide between scientific-technological and moral-religious approaches to social transformation:

> Max Beer, in his *History of British Socialism*, points out that Bacon looked for the happiness of mankind chiefly in the application of science and industry. But by now it is plain that if this alone were sufficient, we could all live in heaven tomorrow. Beer points out that More, on the other hand, looked to social reform and religious ethics to transform society; and it is equally plain that if the souls of men could be transformed without altering their material and institutional activities, Christianity, Mohammedanism, and Buddhism might have created an earthly paradise almost any time this last two thousand years. [1349-007]

Mumford endorses Beer's conclusion that these two conceptions — [[francis-bacon]]'s scientific utopianism and [[sir-thomas-more]]'s ethical utopianism — must be combined for genuine social reconstruction. "The truth is, as Beer sees, that these two conceptions are still at war with each other: idealism and science continue to function in separate compartments; and yet 'the happiness of man on earth' depends upon their combination" (1349-007).

## The Idolum and Reconstruction

Beer's analysis serves a specific function in Mumford's argument about the [[idolum]] — the world of ideas that shapes social reality. The separation of science and ethics into "separate compartments" is itself a symptom of the fragmented modern idolum. For Mumford, Beer's thesis confirms that "the first step in reconstruction is to make over our inner world, and to give our knowledge and our projections a new foundation" (1349-007). The problem of realizing community potential is "not simply a matter of economics or eugenics or ethics as the various specialist thinkers and their political followers have emphasized" (1349-007) — it requires the integration that Beer identifies as lacking.

## Scholarly Contribution

Beer's work is acknowledged as an authoritative source on the connection between utopian literature and the socialist tradition. Mumford recommends it as essential reading:

> Max Beer's *History of British Socialism* has an excellent discussion of the relation of the utopians to socialism. [1526-008]

In the same bibliographic note, Mumford places Beer alongside other foundational scholars of utopian thought, including Kautsky and Moritz Kaufmann, situating Beer's work within a broader European tradition of socialist historiography that traced the intellectual lineage from More through the nineteenth-century reformers to contemporary socialism.
