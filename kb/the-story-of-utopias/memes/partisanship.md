# Partisanship

**Partisanship** is [[lewis-mumford|Lewis Mumford]]'s term for the ideological narrowness that characterizes modern reform movements — the tendency to seize on a single aspect of social life while ignoring the total human situation. In *[[the-story-of-utopias|The Story of Utopias]]*, it represents the antithesis of genuine utopian thinking and the primary obstacle to achieving the [[good-life|good life]].

## Definition and Critique

Mumford identifies partisanship as the central weakness of modern reform:

> "There is a final criticism of the partial utopias: our one-sided reforms have had this fatal defect — they are one-sided. This partisanship was expressed by their relation to the facts upon which their programs were based, and in their attitude towards the people who were to be affected by them" (012-1302).

The partisan does not seek understanding but rhetorical victory: "The mood of partisanship has been that of a lawyer who is getting up an argument and is looking for such facts as will bolster up his case. That mood is inimical to free and intelligent thought: its object is rhetorical triumph" (012-1304).

## Two Weaknesses

Mumford identifies two fundamental flaws in partisanship:

**First**, the partisan neglects the total situation. Socialists concentrated so intensely on ownership and profits that they remained "blinded ... to the specific problems of organization, distribution, and control" (012-1306). The partisan's haste to reach solutions means they "neglect to make a complete tally of the facts" (012-1306).

**Second**, partisanship "breaks the community into vertical divisions, and promotes fictitious antagonisms and kinships which run against the horizontal affiliations and loyalties of a man's life" (012-1312). Drawing on the Irish play *Mixed Marriage*, Mumford illustrates how sectarian identities destroy the ordinary bonds of neighborliness — the shared pleasures of friendship, courtship, and work that bind communities horizontally.

## Partisanship vs. Utopianism

Mumford draws a sharp contrast between the partisan and the utopian temperaments:

> "There are any number of interests in a well-wrought life which lie altogether beyond these categories, and it is the chief misdemeanor of partisanism, as opposed to utopianism, that it tends to slight these general interests, and either bring them into the service of the 'ism' or urge that they be neglected in devotion to the 'cause'" (012-1314).

The utopian sees society as a whole; the partisan sees only a segment. This is why Mumford's survey of the classic [[ideal-commonwealth|ideal commonwealths]] — from [[plato|Plato]] through [[sir-thomas-more|More]] — is framed as an antidote to the partial, one-sided utopias of the modern era.

## Varieties of Partisan Utopias

Mumford catalogues the fragmentary character of modern partisan visions:

> "One of these partisan utopias issues from a bundle of red-tape; everything is filed and ticketed and labelled there ... Another is a mechanical contraption ... A third utopia of the partisan calls human beings, with all their color and thickness, 'individuals,' and makes the good life a matter of legal relationships" (012-1326).

Singly, none of these would create a happy community; together, they produce only discord:

> "While if all of these partisanisms could be realized the result could scarcely be anything else than discord — such a discord as now exists and every day becomes more raucous" (012-1326).

## The Human Cost

The ultimate cost of partisanship is personal impoverishment. The nationalist who insists on being "a hundred percent American" becomes "something less than half a man" (012-1320). By fastening attention on a single segment of the world, "the partisan creates a segment of a personality" (012-1320). Mumford's alternative — the whole man, the complete human being — requires resisting the temptation to reduce identity to any single category.

## The Irish Counter-Example

Mumford finds hope in Ireland's cooperative movement, led by Sir Horace Plunkett and [[patrick-geddes|Æ]]:

> "The horizontal interests which bind men together as farmers and members of a local community are successfully emphasized to the exclusion of irrelevant vertical differences ... this single organization has done more to promote the good life in Ireland than any other institution" (012-1316).

This example — together with the Irish literary revival — demonstrates that communities can transcend partisan divisions when they organize around shared concrete interests rather than abstract ideological loyalties.
