## Overview

Coketown is the second of three great "idola" that Lewis Mumford identifies in *The Story of Utopias* as the dominant utopian visions of the modern age, alongside the [[country-house|Country House]] and the [[megalopolis]] [X-1021]. It represents the quintessential industrial town of the nineteenth century, devoted entirely to the production of material goods, and is Mumford's term for what he analyzes as the paleotechnic industrial environment.

> The Country House's passionate demand for physical goods has given rise to another institution, Coketown; and it is the idolum of Coketown, the industrial age's contribution to the Country House, that we have now to consider. [VII-1109]

## What Coketown Replaced

Before Coketown, Mumford observes, the center of every important European city consisted of a marketplace, shadowed over by a Cathedral, a Market Court, and a Guildhall, frequently with an adjacent university. The various quarters of the city were subordinated to these central institutions, and the work carried on within the city's walls was concretely realized in the local community [VIII-1117]. Coketown swept this organic urban form away, replacing it with a city laid out by an engineer with "mathematical correctness and with a complete disregard for the amenities" [VIII-1121].

> Coketown, as Dickens sees it, is the quintessence of the industrial age. It is perhaps one of the few idola of the modern world which has no parallel in any earlier civilization that we have been able to explore. [VIII-1117]

## Physical Characteristics

Mumford describes Coketown as a city where hills are levelled, swamps are filled, and the urban plan is dictated solely by the needs of the factory. The factory itself is "the center of Coketown's social life" and the only institution providing any communal function [VIII-1123].

> The center of Coketown's activity was the mill, set at first in the open country near falling water, and then as coal was applied to steam engines, removed to areas more accessible to the coal fields. The factory became the new urban unit. [VIII-1119]

> The factory is the center of Coketown's social life; and it is here that the greater part of the population spend their days. At its purest, that is to say, during the first half of the nineteenth century, and in a great many centers to this day, the factory is the only institution that provides anything like a social life. [VIII-1123]

## Economic Logic

The aim of production in Coketown is, according to Mumford, "naturally more production" [IX-1145]. This creates a cycle of shoddy goods and planned obsolescence—machinery can only be kept running "by making things sufficiently shoddy to go to pieces quickly, or by changing the fashion sufficiently often" [IX-1145]. Mumford notes that the status of every family in Coketown "can be told by the size of its rubbish heap" [IX-1147].

> The aim of production in Coketown is naturally more production, and it is only by making things sufficiently shoddy to go to pieces quickly, or by changing the fashion sufficiently often, that the machinery of Coketown can for the most part be kept running. The rage and fury of Coketown's production has to be balanced off by an equal rage and fury of consumption—continence would be fatal. [IX-1145]

## Quantitative Standards

All the standards of Coketown are of a quantitative kind: "so many score of machines, so many tons of gewgaws, so many miles of piping, so many dollars of profit" [IX-1127]. Work whose standards are qualitative—the work of scholars, artists, and scientists—is either frozen out of the community by deliberate ostracism, or hitched to the machine. The only enjoyment available to those inured to the Coketown routine is mechanical achievement [IX-1127].

## Class Structure

Coketown organizes society into three classes corresponding to three grades of goods: necessities (working classes), comforts (middle classes), and luxuries (governing classes) [VIII-1133]. Mumford observes that this tripartite division represents an ironic "improvement" upon the three classes in Plato's *Republic*, but notes that "the custom of limiting the earnings of the working classes to the margin of subsistence is singularly effective in keeping them occupied with the business of production" [VIII-1135].

## The Dickens Connection

Mumford explicitly references Charles Dickens's *Hard Times* as the definitive literary representation of Coketown, calling it "the sharp picture of industrialism which Charles Dickens presents" [VIII-1115]. The fictional Coketown of Dickens becomes, in Mumford's analysis, the archetype for an entire mode of civilization. [[jack-london|Jack London]] and [[george-bernard-shaw|George Bernard Shaw]] would later contribute their own literary critiques of the industrial environment Mumford anatomizes.
