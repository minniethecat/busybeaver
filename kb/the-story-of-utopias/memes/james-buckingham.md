# James Buckingham

**James Silk Buckingham** (1786-1855) is the British traveller, journalist, and social reformer whose proposal for a model town called Victoria is treated by [[lewis-mumford|Lewis Mumford]] as a transitional figure — the point at which utopian thought passes "from a pre-scientific method of thinking to one which sacrifices the artistic imagination to a realistic grasp of the facts" (006-027). Writing at the height of the Industrial Revolution, Buckingham gave utopian form to middle-class aspirations for order, sanitation, and respectability.

## Victoria: The Bourgeois Utopia

Mumford introduces Buckingham in the context of the neglected utopias of the mid-nineteenth century:

> "One of the neglected utopias of the mid-nineteenth century is that of James Buckingham" (006-027).

Buckingham, Mumford observes, was "one of those erratic men of affairs which the fertile soil of British individualism produces, and which hard British common sense persistently ignores." Like [[robert-owen|Robert Owen]], Buckingham knew industrial and commercial affairs from the inside. He travelled widely and wrote on various matters with "that copious, amateurish dogmatism and spirit which marks him, perhaps, as the philistine counterpart of [[john-ruskin|John Ruskin]]" (006-028). Mumford's judgment is pointed: where Owen's utopias expressed the ideals of the artisan, Buckingham's Victoria "represents the ideal of the bourgeoisie" — "the ideal aspect of that Coketown which in a later chapter we shall attempt to describe" (006-028).

## The Model Town Association

Buckingham's plan, as Mumford details it, was organized around a joint-stock company: the Model Town Association. The town of Victoria was to be approximately one mile square, limited to 10,000 inhabitants, surrounded by 10,000 acres of farmland. All land, houses, factories, and materials would be the property of the company, held for the benefit of shareholders. Inhabitants had to be shareholders investing at least twenty pounds and had to subscribe to an extensive set of regulations — permitting freedom of worship and prohibiting child labor, but also banning liquor, drugs, and tobacco.

The plan specified housing standards in remarkable detail: each workingman was to have at least one entire room; married couples without children received two rooms; families with children were allocated at least three rooms. Yet Mumford is unsparing in his assessment of the vision's imaginative poverty: "Buckingham's society is not based upon a thoroughgoing criticism of human institutions: the ends for which this society exists are doubtless those which were held good and proper by the Macaulays and the Martineaus."

## From Chimera to Garden City

Despite its limitations, Buckingham's utopia achieved what more imaginative visions could not: practical influence. Mumford traces the direct lineage:

> "Buckingham thought that, given a successful model town, the rest of England might in time be colonized by the surplus population, and thus the old centers of black industry would be wiped out. Nor was Buckingham altogether deceived. His utopia was a limited one, but out of his limitations has come success" (006-037).

In 1848, Buckingham's Victoria was a chimera. By 1898, [[ebenezer-howard|Ebenezer Howard]] had reconstructed it in *Tomorrow* (later retitled *Garden Cities of To-Morrow*), and from Howard's work came the garden cities of Letchworth and Welwyn — and through them, an entire movement in town planning. Buckingham thus occupies a unique position: a utopian whose concrete specifications, however pedestrian, proved capable of translation into actual communities.

## The Shell-Builder

In Mumford's typology of utopian thinkers, Buckingham belongs to a specific and limited category:

> "If utopia begins with [[plato|Plato]]'s glorious dream of an organic community, the image of the just man made perfect, it cannot end with Buckingham's invention of a shell. Nevertheless, through the nineteenth century the superficial utopians, the shell-builders, are dominant" (012-069).

What Buckingham gained in practicality, he lost in critical imagination. He took contemporary values for granted and sought merely to realize them completely and in orderly fashion. Yet Mumford credits Buckingham and [[ebenezer-howard|Ebenezer Howard]] with demonstrating "the importance of converting the idolum of eutopia into plans and layouts and detailed projections, such as a townplanner might utilize" (012-069). A utopia that cannot be converted into specific plans, Mumford suggests, will remain "up in the air."
