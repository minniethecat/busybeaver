New Harmony was a utopian community established in 1825 by the British industrialist and social reformer [[robert-owen|Robert Owen]] on the banks of the Wabash River in Indiana. Owen purchased the existing town from the Harmony Society, a German religious communal group, and sought to transform it into a model community based on principles of cooperation, education, and social equality.

## Origins and Vision

Robert Owen had already achieved international fame for his reforms at the New Lanark cotton mills in Scotland, where he demonstrated that improved working conditions, education, and reasonable hours could coexist with commercial success. New Harmony represented the next logical step: a community organized from the ground up on Owen's principles. Owen envisioned New Harmony as the prototype for a worldwide network of cooperative "Villages of Unity and Mutual Cooperation" that would replace the competitive, individualistic basis of existing society.

## The Experiment in Practice

In January 1826, the *New Harmony Gazette* announced the community's formal constitution, declaring that all members would "be considered as one family, and no one can be employed by and receive wages from another." The community attracted an eclectic group of scientists, educators, artists, and reformers, including the geologist William Maclure, the zoologist Thomas Say, and the educator Marie Duclos Fretageot.

However, the experiment quickly encountered difficulties. The community struggled with internal disagreements over governance, tensions between Owen's authoritative vision and the members' democratic aspirations, and the practical challenges of coordinating the labor of a diverse and largely urban membership unaccustomed to agricultural work. Owen himself was frequently absent, lecturing and promoting his ideas elsewhere, leaving the community without consistent leadership.

## Collapse and Legacy

By 1827, the communitarian phase of New Harmony had effectively collapsed. Owen divided the land into smaller parcels that individual families could work independently. The experiment had lasted less than two years in its full communal form.

Despite its brief existence, New Harmony left a significant legacy. The community demonstrated both the appeal and the difficulty of translating utopian ideals into lived practice. In *The Story of Utopias*, [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] discusses New Harmony within the broader context of nineteenth-century experimental communities that grappled with the practical challenge of separating from the surrounding society while remaining economically viable.
