# William Blake

## Introduction

William Blake (1757–1827) was an English poet, painter, and printmaker of the Romantic era, best known for works including *Songs of Innocence and Experience*, *The Marriage of Heaven and Hell*, and the prophetic books. Though primarily remembered as a literary and visual artist, Blake appears in Lewis Mumford's *The Story of Utopias* as a source of political wisdom: Mumford invokes Blake's dictum about the tyranny of uniform law to support his argument that genuine eutopia must respect the diversity of local communities.

## Blake in Mumford's Eutopia

In the concluding chapter of *The Story of Utopias*, as Mumford sketches the outlines of his own vision of eutopia, he draws on Blake to argue against the imposition of uniform legislation across diverse communities:

> We shall not attempt to legislate for all these communities at one stroke; for we shall respect William Blake's dictum that one law for the lion and the ox is tyranny. (XII-1507)

This citation places Blake in the distinguished company of thinkers Mumford draws upon for his concluding vision — alongside Patrick Geddes and the regionalist tradition. For Mumford, Blake's aphorism encapsulated the principle that genuine community life cannot be imposed from above by a central authority but must grow organically from local conditions.

## The Artist and Community

Mumford also references Blake in a broader discussion of the artist's relationship to community, quoting W. B. Yeats's recollection of how Blake's early poems, alongside Spenser's *The Fairy Queen* and Wordsworth's *Lyrical Ballads*, were invoked by defenders of artistic freedom against the demand that art must conform to the style of its own day (XII-1459). In this context, Blake represents for Mumford the artist whose work transcends temporal fashion and speaks to enduring human concerns — a model for the kind of creative spirit that any genuine eutopia must nurture.

## Significance in the Utopian Tradition

Blake himself was a creator of visionary and mythic worlds — his prophetic books depict cosmic struggles between forces of imagination and reason, freedom and oppression. Though Mumford does not discuss Blake's own mythic system in detail, Blake's presence in the final chapter of *The Story of Utopias* is significant: it suggests that for Mumford, the utopian tradition draws not only from formal utopian writers like [[plato|Plato]] and [[sir-thomas-more|Thomas More]] but also from poets and artists whose work articulates fundamental truths about human community and the conditions of the good life.
