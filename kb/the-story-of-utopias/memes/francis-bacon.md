# Francis Bacon

**Francis Bacon** (1561-1626) is the English philosopher and statesman whose fragment *New Atlantis* (1627) sketched the first systematic vision of a society organized around organized scientific research. [[lewis-mumford|Lewis Mumford]] treats Bacon as a pivotal but flawed figure: the thinker who grasped the transformative power of science while remaining "incredibly childish and incoherent" in his literary execution (006-037).

## New Atlantis and Salomon's House

Bacon's *New Atlantis* is, Mumford argues, "not a utopia in the sense that I have explained our principle of selection" — it is only a fragment, and would "drop out altogether from our survey were it not for the hugely overrated reputation that Bacon has as a philosopher of natural science — indeed, as *the* philosopher after [[aristotle|Aristotle]]" (006-029).

The core of Bacon's commonwealth is **Salomon's House**, also called the College of the Six Days' Works, described as "the noblest foundation that ever was upon earth, and the lantern of the kingdom" (006-031). Its purpose is the "knowledge of the causes and secret motions of things; and the enlarging of the bounds of human empire, to the effecting of all things possible" (006-033).

## Mumford's Assessment

Mumford is blunt about Bacon's literary defects:

> "In telling all this, as in the rest of his *New Atlantis*, Bacon is incredibly childish and incoherent: he gives such a description of Salomon's House as a six-year-old schoolboy might give of a visit to the Rockefeller Foundation" (006-037).

Beneath these "maladroit interpretations," however, Mumford acknowledges that Bacon "had a grasp on some of the fundamentals of scientific research, and of the part that science might play in the 'relief of man's estate'" (006-037). Bacon's greater ideas were largely "anticipated and more amply expressed by Andreae" (006-031), but his sketch of scientific institutes resonated through subsequent centuries.

## The Turn to Means

Bacon, together with [[tommaso-campanella|Tommaso Campanella]], marks a crucial turning point in utopian thought:

> "With these two utopians we stand at the entrance to the utopia of means; that is to say, the place in which all that materially contributes to the good life has been perfected" (006-039).

The earlier utopias — those of [[plato|Plato]], [[sir-thomas-more|More]], and Andreae — were concerned to establish what men should aim for in life. Bacon and Campanella took these aims for granted and discussed how man's scope of action might be broadened. Mumford sees this shift as consequential for the entire modern world: "As a result of our preoccupation with the means, we in the Western World live in an inventor's paradise" — but face again "the riddle that Plato, More, and Andreae sought to answer: what are men to do with their knowledge and power?" (006-039).

## The Two Conceptions at War

Mumford returns to Bacon in his concluding analysis, citing Max Beer's *History of British Socialism*:

> "Bacon looked for the happiness of mankind chiefly in the application of science and industry. But by now it is plain that if this alone were sufficient, we could all live in heaven tomorrow. Beer points out that More, on the other hand, looked to social reform and religious ethics to transform society; and it is equally plain that if the souls of men could be transformed without altering their material and institutional activities, Christianity, Mohammedanism, and Buddhism might have created an earthly paradise almost any time this last two thousand years" (012-068).

The truth, Mumford concludes, is that "these two conceptions are still at war with each other: idealism and science continue to function in separate compartments; and yet 'the happiness of man on earth' depends upon their combination" (012-068).
