# Patrick Geddes

**Patrick Geddes** (1854-1932) was a Scottish biologist, sociologist, and town planner whose work provided the methodological foundation for Lewis Mumford's eutopian project. Mumford describes him as "the outstanding exponent of the Eutopian method both in thought and in practical activity" (013-005).

## The Eutopian Method

Geddes's key insight, which Mumford adopts and extends, is that Thomas More's "Utopia" was a pun on both *Outopia* (no-place) and *Eutopia* (the good place) (012-003). This etymological observation becomes the basis for Mumford's entire distinction between escapist and reconstructive utopianism.

Geddes's own works — *City Development* (1904) and *Town-Planning Towards City Development: A Report to the Durbar of Indore* (1918) — are described as "mines from which all sorts of precious thoughts can be quarried" (013-005).

## Regional Survey

The regional survey method, which Mumford presents as the essential first step in eutopian reconstruction, derives directly from Geddes. The Outlook Tower in Edinburgh, where "one could begin at the point where one was standing and work outwards, in thought, to embrace the whole wide world" (012-028), was Geddes's creation.

Mumford notes that Geddes's work "exemplifies concretely a good part of what I have sought to explain and define in not altogether adequate prose" (013-005).

## Influence on Mumford

Geddes stands behind Mumford's insistence that utopian thinking must be grounded in concrete reality — in the actual conditions of specific regions, understood through systematic survey. This is the bridge between the specialist in the library and the active worker in the field that makes eutopia possible.
