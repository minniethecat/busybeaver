# Oscar Wilde

**Oscar Wilde** (1854-1900) is the Irish poet, playwright, and essayist whose "The Soul of Man Under Socialism" (1891) represents a distinctive current in the late nineteenth-century utopian tradition: the fusion of radical aesthetic [[idolum|individualism]] with a socialist critique of property and poverty. Though Wilde appears only briefly in [[lewis-mumford|Lewis Mumford]]'s *The Story of Utopias*, his essay articulates a vision of the [[good-life]] that challenges both the industrial [[idolum]] of Coketown and the regimented utopias of the [[edward-bellamy|Bellamy]] school.

## The Soul of Man Under Socialism

Wilde's central utopian argument is paradoxical: socialism is valuable not because it would make everyone equal, but because it would make everyone free — free to develop as individuals. The abolition of private property, in Wilde's vision, is not an end in itself but the precondition for the flowering of personality. This aligns with Mumford's own insistence that the [[good-life]] cannot be reduced to material sufficiency; the question "what are men to do with their knowledge and power?" (005-015) must be answered in cultural and spiritual terms, not merely economic ones.

Wilde's utopia is fundamentally aesthetic: he imagines a world in which the elimination of poverty and mechanized labor liberates human beings for the cultivation of beauty, thought, and self-realization. This stands in contrast to the more administrative utopias of the nineteenth century, where, as Mumford notes, "the utopia of means" came to eclipse the older concern with human ends (005-015).

## Individualism and the Machine

Wilde's relationship to [[industrialism]] was characteristically ambivalent. He celebrated the machine precisely because he believed it could liberate humanity from drudgery:

| Wilde's Position | Conventional Socialist Position | Mumford's Framework |
|-----------------|--------------------------------|---------------------|
| Machinery should serve individualism | Machinery should serve collective equality | The machine is a means; the [[idolum]] determines its ends |
| Socialism enables the exceptional individual | Socialism serves the common good | The good community must serve both the norm and the exception |
| Art is the highest human activity | Labor is the source of value | The [[good-life]] integrates work and culture |

Mumford's own analysis of [[industrialism]] echoes Wilde's concern: the machines "whose output was so great that all men might be clothed" became "for the vast majority of people who possessed neither capital nor land, nothing short of instruments of torture" (005-022). The problem was never the machine but the [[idolum]] that directed it.

## Art, Propaganda, and Community

Wilde's famous declaration that "all art is quite useless" finds an unexpected echo in Mumford's concluding chapter, where he argues that "pure art is inevitably propaganda" — meaning that genuine art "is meant to be propagated" and "in so far as it fails to impregnate the community in which it exists with its ideas and images, its claims are spurious" (012-044). Both Wilde and Mumford reject the reduction of art to either private therapy or public relations; both insist that art must transform the shared [[idolum]] through which a community understands itself.

Wilde's utopian contribution lies in his insistence that the ultimate purpose of social reconstruction is not comfort or efficiency but the release of human creativity — a vision that resonates with Mumford's own conclusion that the "fundamental problem of eutopian reconstruction" requires transforming both the material environment and the inner world of ideas (012-025).
