## Introduction

François Rabelais (c. 1483–1553) was a French Renaissance writer, physician, and humanist, best known for his comic masterpiece *Gargantua and Pantagruel* — a sprawling satire of medieval scholasticism, monasticism, and received authority. For Mumford, Rabelais occupies a distinctive position in the history of utopian thought: not as a formal utopian, but as the creator of the Abbey of Thélème — a vision of aristocratic liberty that Mumford identifies as the prototype of Country House culture, one of the central "idola" that his book critiques.

Mumford introduces Rabelais at the beginning of his chapter on the Country House:

> perhaps instead of examining the contemporary Country House it will be well to go back to its beginnings, and see how it was pictured in all its encrusted splendor at the first movement of the Renaissance — in the setting which François Rabelais, in one of the few downright serious passages in his great work, *Gargantua*, sought to provide for the good life. (010-027)

## The Abbey of Thélème

The Abbey of Thélème appears in *Gargantua* as an anti-monastery — a community organized around freedom rather than rule, abundance rather than austerity. Where traditional abbeys were walled, Thélème has no walls. Where monks took vows of poverty, chastity, and obedience, the Thélèmites may be honorably married, may be rich, and may live at liberty. The Abbey is situated on the banks of the Loire in open country, arranged in a hexagonal plan with six towers. Its facilities include libraries in every language, painting galleries, a tiltyard, a theatre, a swimming pool, gardens, orchards, tennis courts, and an archery range.

Mumford quotes Rabelais's description of the residents' splendid costumes, the goldsmiths and jewelers who supply them, and the seven ships that arrive yearly "laden with ingots of gold, with raw silk, with pearls and precious stones" (010-030). The governing principle is expressed in the Abbey's single rule:

> "Do what you please." (010-031)

## Mumford's Analysis

Mumford reads the Abbey of Thélème as the urtext of Country House culture — an ideal of aristocratic consumption that has, he argues, penetrated every stratum of modern society:

> When we turn our attention from Rabelais' conceit of an anti-monastic order, we discover that he has given us an excellent picture of the Country House, and of what I shall take the liberty of calling Country House culture. (010-029)

The Abbey of Thélème, in Mumford's analysis, represents a fateful shift in Western cultural ideals: the good life ceased to mean active participation in the creative work of one's own community and came instead to mean the private accumulation of pleasures, goods, and refinements. This "idolum" — the ideal image that patterns behavior — has, Mumford argues, driven the acquisitive character of modern industrial society. "Our Country House will not merely be a house: there will be a chapel, an art gallery, a theater, a gymnasium, as François Rabelais imagined" (011-010).

Mumford's treatment of Rabelais is dialectical: he admires the vitality and scope of the Thélème vision while identifying its limitations as a social model. The Abbey is a utopia of a single class — the wealthy, the beautiful, the well-born — and its liberty depends on the exclusion of everyone else.

## Place in Mumford's Argument

Rabelais functions in *The Story of Utopias* as a hinge figure. His Abbey of Thélème bridges the classical utopias (Plato, More) and the modern "idola" that Mumford critiques. The Abbey is not, strictly speaking, a utopia — it is designed for a privileged few, not a whole society — but it crystallizes an ideal of the good life that has exerted enormous influence on Western culture.

Mumford credits Van Wyck Brooks for putting him "on the trail of Rabelais' Abbey of Thélème" (012-058), and acknowledges that a German scholar, Herr Blucher, anticipated him in recognizing Thélème as a key to Renaissance culture. In treating Rabelais as a utopian thinker, Mumford expands the boundaries of the utopian tradition to include literary works that, while not presenting complete ideal commonwealths, nevertheless articulate powerful visions of how life should be lived.
