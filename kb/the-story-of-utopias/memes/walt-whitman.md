# Walt Whitman

## Introduction

Walt Whitman (1819-1892) was an American poet, essayist, and journalist, best known for his collection *Leaves of Grass* (first published 1855, expanded throughout his life), which celebrated democracy, the body, and the American landscape in an innovative free verse style. In *The Story of Utopias*, Lewis Mumford references Whitman twice — as an exemplar of a naturalistic approach to life and as a sharp observer of human nature's role in social movements.

## Whitman in Mumford's Account

Mumford's first reference to Whitman appears in Chapter I, in a discussion of the two worlds in which human beings live — the physical environment and the inner world of ideas, or "idolum":

> If the world in which men live were the world as it is known to the physical geographer, we should have a pretty simple time of it. We might follow Whitman's advice, and live as the animals, and stop whining for all time about our sins and imperfections. (001-008)

Mumford invokes Whitman's embrace of the natural world to mark the limit of a purely physical understanding of human existence. For Mumford, the distinctively human challenge is that we inhabit both the outer world of geography and climate and the inner world of ideas, values, and fantasies — the *idolum*. Whitman's advice to "live as the animals" represents a tempting simplification, but one that cannot account for the transformative power of human consciousness.

Mumford's second reference draws on Whitman's psychological insight into the role of personality in social change:

> Walt Whitman said of Carlyle that behind the tally of his work and genius stood the stomach, and gave a sort of casting vote. So one may say of every social movement, that behind the tally of its theoretic background and its concrete programs stand human beings — hale and sick, neurotic and stable, well-intentioned and malicious — and give the casting vote. (011-026)

This passage occurs in Mumford's reflections on why utopian programs so often fail. The gap between theory and practice, he argues, is not merely a matter of flawed plans but of flawed people. Whitman's remark about Carlyle — that physical constitution and temperament exercise a decisive influence even on the work of genius — is extended by Mumford into a general principle: every social movement is at the mercy of the human beings who carry it forward.

## Whitman's Democratic Vision

Whitman's poetry articulates a distinctly American utopianism — a vision of democratic comradeship, bodily joy, and spiritual expansiveness that resonates with the utopian impulse to imagine a transformed human community. Though Mumford does not discuss *Leaves of Grass* directly, Whitman's presence in *The Story of Utopias* — alongside figures like Emerson, Thoreau, and William James — reflects Mumford's interest in the American contribution to the utopian tradition, which often takes the form of cultural and spiritual renewal rather than systematic social planning.
