## Introduction

Alfred Jules Émile Fouillée (1838-1912) was a French philosopher and sociologist known for his theory of *idées-forces* — the concept that ideas possess an inherent force or momentum that drives them toward realization in the world. In *The Story of Utopias*, Lewis Mumford invokes Fouillée to support his argument that utopian ideas are not passive fantasies but active forces in social transformation.

## Fouillée in Mumford's Account

Mumford draws on Fouillée's work in two significant contexts. In Chapter II, he references Fouillée alongside Alfred Zimmern in a discussion of how the Greek conception of the good life shaped classical utopian thought (002-006). More directly, in Chapter XIII, Mumford cites Fouillée's theory of the dynamic force of ideas as part of the intellectual foundation for his own argument that social myths and collective representations exercise real power in human affairs (013-004).

In Mumford's concluding acknowledgments, he situates Fouillée within the broader intellectual tradition that informed his work:

> The importance of our social myths and our collective representations has been noted by a whole school of French sociologists who follow Émile Durkheim; and the dynamic force of ideas has been treated by Alfred Fouillée. On both these topics there is a whole literature; and it would give a sense of false simplicity to single out any particular essay. [XIII-1528]

## The Theory of Idées-Forces

Fouillée's central philosophical contribution was the concept of *idées-forces* — the notion that ideas are not mere mental representations but contain within themselves an inherent tendency toward actualization. An idea of justice, for Fouillée, is not simply a thought about justice; it is a force pressing toward the creation of just institutions. This concept resonated deeply with Mumford's thesis that utopian visions, far from being idle dreams, function as practical hypotheses that reshape social reality.

The theory of *idées-forces* bridges the gap between idealism and materialism that characterized much nineteenth-century philosophy. By arguing that ideas are themselves causal forces, Fouillée challenged both the mechanistic determinism of positivism and the detached speculation of pure idealism, offering a dynamic model of the relationship between thought and action that prefigured later developments in the sociology of knowledge.

## Place in the Utopian Tradition

Fouillée belongs to the French sociological tradition — alongside [[emile-durkheim|Émile Durkheim]] and the school of collective representations — that Mumford mobilizes to defend the utopian enterprise against its critics. By theorizing the causal efficacy of ideas, Fouillée provides philosophical warrant for Mumford's central claim: that the way people imagine the good life has concrete consequences for how they build their communities. In this sense, Fouillée's philosophy is foundational to the entire utopian project Mumford outlines. Mumford places Fouillée's work alongside that of [[georges-sorel|Georges Sorel]] and [[benjamin-kidd|Benjamin Kidd]] as key contributions to understanding the dynamic relationship between social ideals and social change (XIII-1528).
