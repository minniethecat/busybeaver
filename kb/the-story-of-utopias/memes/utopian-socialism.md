## Definition

In [[the-story-of-utopias]], **Utopian Socialism** refers to the tradition of nineteenth-century thinkers who designed ideal communities as alternatives to the degradations of industrial capitalism, before Marx and Engels gave socialism its "scientific" turn. Mumford examines this tradition through the work of [[charles-fourier]], [[robert-owen]], [[etienne-cabet]], and [[edward-bellamy]], among others. These thinkers shared a conviction that social reorganization could be achieved through the demonstration of model communities rather than class struggle — a conviction that Marx would later dismiss as "utopian" in the pejorative sense.

Mumford's treatment of this tradition is nuanced: he recognizes the genuine human insight in Fourier's psychology, respects Owen's moral seriousness, yet is unsparing in his critique of Cabet's *Icaria* and Bellamy's *Looking Backward* for substituting bureaucratic machinery for the substance of the good life.

## Core Characteristics

**The model community as method.** The utopian socialists believed that demonstrating a working alternative would persuade others to adopt it. "Fourier died without persuading anyone to give a trial to his scheme of association; and yet his work was not without its practical influence" (704-704). The Brook Farm experiment in America and Godin's *familistère* at Guise in France were direct results of Fourier's inspiration.

**Design from first principles.** Unlike the classical utopians who began with human nature, the utopian socialists began with institutional design. Mumford praises Fourier precisely because he is "concerned first of all not with modifying human nature but with finding out what it actually is" (678-678). Fourier's utopia "is to be based upon an understanding of man's actual physical and mental makeup, and its institutions are to be such as will permit man's original nature to function freely" (678-678).

**The drift toward bureaucracy.** Mumford's central critique is that, in practice, utopian socialist designs substituted one form of domination for another. In Cabet's Icaria, the State is "the sole proprietor and director of industry" (873-873), and "the uniformity that irks us in modern life... is extended to the last degree" (875-875). Mumford concludes: "Icaria is essentially not an ideal but an idealization" (889-889). The means of achieving utopia had become the end, and the end had become something indistinguishable from the nineteenth-century national state.

## Key Figures in Mumford's Analysis

**Charles Fourier (1772-1837).** Mumford calls him "the most influential utopian" among the Associationists (676-676). Fourier's phalanstery — a community of 1,500 to 1,600 persons on a square league of diversified land (686-686) — was designed to harmonize human passions rather than suppress them. Mumford finds in Fourier "the first man who had a plan for colonizing the wilderness of industrial barbarism that existed at the beginning of the nineteenth century, and redeeming that wilderness to civilization" (704-704).

**Robert Owen (1771-1858).** Mumford treats Owen briefly, noting that "his work belongs more to the 'real' world than to the idola of utopia" (708-708). Owen's projects for model industrial towns "have more of the flavor of a poor colony than that of a productive human society" (708-708). Yet Mumford grants him "good intentions, organizing ability, and moral fervor" (708-708).

**Étienne Cabet (1788-1856).** The *Journey to Icaria* represents, for Mumford, the culmination of the Napoleonic bureaucratic imagination applied to utopia. Everything in Icaria is "arranged" and "attended to" by committees — food, clothing, education, industry (865-865). The system essentially extends military organization into all of peacetime life: "Napoleon's conception of a nation in arms is dominant; only now it is a nation in overalls" (875-875).

**Edward Bellamy (1850-1898).** *Looking Backward* gave Cabet's vision "a fresh outline" (843-843) and became enormously influential in late nineteenth-century America. Mumford treats Bellamy as the endpoint of a tradition that progressively substituted machinery for human purpose.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Utopian socialism occupies a critical position in Mumford's framework:

- **[[eutopia]]** — the utopian socialists attempted to build eutopias but, Mumford argues, too often produced mere efficiency-machines
- **[[coketown-idolum|Coketown]]** — the industrial reality against which the utopian socialists reacted
- **[[national-state-idolum|National State]]** — the political form that Cabet and Bellamy unwittingly reproduced in their utopian designs
- **[[idolum]]** — the utopian socialists' weakness was creating new idola (the perfectly administered society) rather than escaping the logic of idolum altogether
- **[[will-to-utopia]]** — the utopian socialists exemplify this drive, even when their specific designs fail
