*Human Geography in Western Europe* (1919) is a work by the British geographer and anthropologist Herbert John Fleure (1877–1969), published in the *Making of the Future* series edited by [[patrick-geddes|Patrick Geddes]] and [[victor-branford|Victor Branford]]. [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] recommends it, alongside C. B. Fawcett's *The Provinces of England*, as "an able exposition of the regionalist movement and of the fundamental realities upon which this movement is based" (013-005).

## In Mumford's Endorsement

Mumford places Fleure's book in a select list of works illustrating the "general method and outlook" of *The Story of Utopias*. He writes:

> "As a loose illustration of the general method and outlook embodied in this book I refer to the *Making of the Future Series*, edited by Messrs. Patrick Geddes and Victor Branford... There is an able exposition of the regionalist movement and of the fundamental realities upon which this movement is based in two books published in that series; namely, Professor Fleure's *Human Geography in Western Europe* and C. B. Fawcett's *The Provinces of England*" (013-005).

## The Geddes Connection

Fleure was a close associate of [[patrick-geddes|Patrick Geddes]], the Scottish biologist, sociologist, and town planner whom Mumford regarded as "the outstanding exponent of the Eutopian method both in thought and in practical activity" (013-005). The *Making of the Future* series, in which Fleure's book appeared, was the publishing vehicle for the regionalist movement that Geddes and Branford led. Fleure's human geography provided the empirical foundation for Geddes's regional survey method, which Mumford saw as the practical embodiment of utopian thinking.

## Regionalism and Utopian Method

The regionalist movement that Fleure's work supported was, for Mumford, not merely an academic discipline but a practical application of the utopian method. By studying the concrete realities of place — soil, climate, settlement patterns, economic life — the regional survey method grounded utopian aspiration in the particularities of actual communities. This connection between geography and utopia is characteristic of the Geddesian tradition: the good life must be realized in specific places, by specific communities, working with the materials their region provides.

## Fleure's Intellectual Contribution

Fleure's work bridged physical anthropology, geography, and regional planning. His emphasis on the long-term relationship between human communities and their environments provided the historical depth that Geddes's "valley section" model required. Through *Human Geography in Western Europe*, Fleure helped establish the intellectual framework for a form of planning that treated regions as living wholes rather than administrative subdivisions.
