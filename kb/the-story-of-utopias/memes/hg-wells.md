## Introduction

Herbert George Wells (1866-1946) was an English novelist, historian, and social commentator, best known as a pioneer of science fiction. Among utopian scholars, Wells holds a distinctive position as the most prolific utopian writer of the modern period, producing works that span the spectrum from dystopian warning (*The Time Machine*, 1895) to constructive utopian blueprint (*A Modern Utopia*, 1905). Mumford engages with Wells throughout *The Story of Utopias*, treating him as the twentieth century's most significant contributor to the utopian tradition.

Mumford acknowledges Wells's range: "Mr. H.G. Wells, it is true, has made more than one excursion into an imaginary commonwealth: *The Time Machine* is his earliest and *The World Set Free* may possibly be considered as his latest" (010-023). For Mumford, Wells's utopian career represents the evolution of the genre from literary fantasy toward practical social planning.

## Utopian Works

Wells's utopian fiction spans three decades and multiple modes. *The Time Machine* (1895) is a dystopian allegory — what Mumford calls "a picture of Coketown which is perhaps a little exuberant in some of its details" (011-049). The novel projects Victorian class divisions into a far future where humanity has split into two species: the leisured Eloi and the subterranean Morlocks.

*A Modern Utopia* (1905) represents Wells's most ambitious constructive utopian effort. Mumford praises it as "a fine and lucid product of the imagination" (010-023), though he criticizes Wells's four-class social system (kinetic, poietic, base, dull) as a modernization of ancient caste divisions traceable to the *Bhagavad Gita*. *When the Sleeper Awakens* (1899) and *The World Set Free* (1914) complete Wells's major utopian cycle.

Mumford also draws on Wells's non-utopian fiction. He cites Wells's "cruel description of Bladesover in *Tono-Bungay*" (010-029) as a key literary portrait of Country House culture, alongside works by Meredith, Shaw, and Chekhov.
