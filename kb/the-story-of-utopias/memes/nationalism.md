## Overview

In Lewis Mumford's analysis of modern utopian forms, nationalism and the National State represent a powerful collective idolum that channels utopian aspirations into territorial, political, and cultural identity. Unlike the [[country-house|Country House]] (rooted in aristocratic privilege) or [[coketown]] (driven by industrial production), the National State functions as what Mumford terms a "national utopia" — a vision of collective good life organized around the sovereign nation.

> The chief concern of the national utopia is the support of the central government, for the government is the guardian of territory and privilege. [XI-1179]

## The Fabricated Utopia

Mumford argues that the National State is not a natural fact but a collective projection — a social myth that must be continually *willed* into existence. He compares it to Hans Christian Andersen's tale of the emperor's new clothes:

> In fact, it is only by the persistent projection of this utopia for the last three or four hundred years that its existence has become credible; for all the minute descriptions which the political historian gives to the National State, its origins and its institutions and its people, read a good deal like that fine story which Hans Andersen told about the king who walked the streets naked because two rascally tailors had persuaded him that they had woven and cut up for him a beautiful outfit of clothes. [XI-1161]

This insight places nationalism within Mumford's broader framework of "idola" — collective representations that function as utopian visions, shaping the world in their image just as surely as any literary utopia.

## The National State and Geography

The National State, Mumford observes, has "only the most casual relation to the facts of geography" [XI-1171]. Where the facts of geography, trade, and human settlement conflict with the demands of national territory, the utopia overrides the reality:

> Wherever it suits the purposes of the Guardians of the State, the facts are ignored, and an artificial relation is *willed* into existence. The human communities which the regional sociologist recognizes do not always coincide with those which the statesman wishes to incorporate as "national territory," and when this conflict occurs, the idea rather than the reality triumphs, if necessary by brute force. [XI-1171]

This tension between utopian projection and geographic reality is central to Mumford's critique. The National State substitutes an abstract, territorially-bounded community for the concrete relationships of regional life that had sustained European civilization for centuries before the rise of nationalism.

## The Connecting Tissue

The National State serves a crucial function in Mumford's system of modern idola: it is the "connecting tissue" that binds together the otherwise separate worlds of the Country House and Coketown [XI-1159]. While the Country House pursues aristocratic leisure and Coketown pursues industrial production, the National State provides the political framework that protects the privileges of both while claiming to serve the common good of the entire national community.

## Relationship to Other Idola

The National State functions in a complementary and often competitive relationship with Mumford's other modern idola. It provides the political framework that protects the privileges of the Country House and the markets of Coketown, while simultaneously claiming a higher moral purpose — the common good of the national community. This tension between the particular interests served by the state and its universalist claims is a recurring theme in Mumford's analysis.

## The Scale Problem

Mumford contrasts the National State with the classical utopian tradition, particularly [[plato|Plato]]'s insistence on limiting the size of the ideal community. Plato limited his *Republic* to 5,040 citizens — "about the number that can be conveniently addressed by a single orator" — because he understood that "when you increase the number of people in a community you decrease the number of things that they can share in common" [I-268]. The modern National State, with its millions of citizens, represents the opposite pole: a community so vast that genuine civic participation becomes impossible, replaced by the abstract bond of national identity.

## Mumford's Critique

Mumford's treatment of nationalism as a utopian form is fundamentally critical. He sees the national utopia as a dangerous misdirection of genuine utopian impulses — the human need for community, belonging, and shared purpose — into the service of territorial expansion, economic competition, and military power. The national utopia substitutes the abstract bond of citizenship for the concrete relationships of community life, and it channels the creative energies that might build genuine eutopias into the destructive competitions between nation-states.
