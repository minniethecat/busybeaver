**Land reform** was a central theme in nineteenth-century utopian and social reform movements. [[lewis-mumford|Lewis Mumford]] identifies the land question as fundamental to understanding how the breakdown of the medieval order and the enclosure of common land created the conditions for both industrial progress and social poverty (006-749).

## The Enclosure Crisis

Mumford describes how, when the medieval order broke down, "the great proprietors began to seize this common land; and during the eighteenth century, under the incentive of big-scale scientific agriculture, the seizure went on at a merry pace" (006-749). The peasant without land was forced to migrate to the new industrial towns, and "the labor of the peasant and his family fed the machines which the Watts and Arkwrights were developing" (006-749). In the popular imagination, "the period before the Industrial Revolution seemed in comparison a real utopia; and the key to this utopia was the land" (006-749).

## Thomas Spence and Spensonia

[[thomas-spence|Thomas Spence]] (1750–1814) was among the earliest and most radical advocates of land nationalization. His utopia, Spensonia, began from the premise that the land, like a ship at sea, should be "the property of them all collectively" (006-761). Land was parceled out for occupation with rent paid to the public treasury, which was then divided equally among all citizens as a social dividend. Mumford includes Spence's scheme alongside the land-based utopias that marked a transition toward full economic collectivism.

## Freeland and Theodor Hertzka

[[theodor-hertzka|Theodor Hertzka]]'s *Freeland* (1890) represented a later stage in land-reform utopianism, marking the transition "between the utopia in which the land alone is held by the community and that in which land and capital and all the machinery of production belongs to a national state" (006-777). In Freeland, access to both land and capital was guaranteed: any association could "get land and capital on demand" (006-824), and individual families retained their own houses and gardens of a thousand square meters.

## Henry George and the Single Tax

Mumford groups [[henry-george|Henry George]] (1839–1897) among the "half a dozen other thinkers of outstanding importance" in the economic tradition alongside [[david-ricardo|Ricardo]], [[pierre-joseph-proudhon|Proudhon]], and [[karl-marx|Marx]] (011-1234). George's single-tax proposal — to tax land values as the primary source of public revenue — was one of the "temporal movements" Mumford used as criterion for selecting his nineteenth-century utopias (009-1524).

> "The peasant without land was forced to migrate to the new towns... Industrial progress and social poverty went hand in hand. The period before the Industrial Revolution seemed in comparison a real utopia; and the key to this utopia was the land." (006-749)
