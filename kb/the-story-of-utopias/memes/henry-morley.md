Henry Morley (1822–1894) was an English scholar, editor, and literary historian whose anthology *[[ideal-commonwealths|Ideal Commonwealths]]* (1886) provided [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] with a principal source for the classic utopian texts surveyed in *[[the-story-of-utopias]]*.

## *Ideal Commonwealths*

Morley's anthology brought together five foundational utopian texts in a single volume published by George Routledge in London: Plutarch's *Lycurgus*, [[sir-thomas-more|More]]'s *[[utopia-more|Utopia]]*, [[francis-bacon|Bacon]]'s *[[new-atlantis|New Atlantis]]*, [[tommaso-campanella|Campanella]]'s *[[city-of-the-sun|City of the Sun]]*, and a fragment of Joseph Hall's *Mundus Alter et Idem*. Morley supplied an introduction that framed these works as a coherent literary tradition.

## Influence on Mumford

Mumford relied on Morley's anthology throughout *The Story of Utopias*. His bibliography directs readers to *Ideal Commonwealths* for the texts of More (014-010), Bacon (014-012), and Campanella (014-013). More profoundly, Morley's organizing concept — the "ideal commonwealth" — became Mumford's own central category. Mumford opens his study by declaring it an exploration of "Utopia as a world by itself, divided into ideal commonwealths, with all its communities clustered into proud cities, aiming bravely at the good life" (001-003).

Mumford explicitly acknowledges his debt to Morley's framework, writing that he "set out to treat such plans for the improvement of the human community as had been embodied in complete pictures of an ideal commonwealth" (013-002). The anthology thus shaped not only Mumford's source materials but the very definition of the genre he sought to survey.

## The Victorian Context

Morley's anthology was a product of the Victorian era's growing interest in systematic social reform through literary imagination. By collecting the classic utopian texts together, Morley suggested that utopian writing constituted a tradition worthy of serious study — a suggestion Mumford would fulfill on a far larger scale nearly four decades later. The anthology also reflects the late nineteenth-century publishing practice of making classic texts accessible to a broad reading public through affordable collected editions.
