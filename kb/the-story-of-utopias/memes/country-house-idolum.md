# Country House Idolum

The **Country House Idolum** is one of the major historical idola identified by Lewis Mumford in *The Story of Utopias*. It represents the dominant cultural pattern that emerged from the Renaissance, characterized by the dissociation of the ruling class from the broader community and an emphasis on acquisitive consumption that would ultimately shape modern industrial civilization.

## Emergence

Mumford traces the idolum of the Country House to the cultural transformation of the late Middle Ages. As men "ceased to build castles to protect themselves against physical dangers" and "left off entering monasteries in order to fortify their souls for the Hereafter," both "the spiritual and the temporal life began to shift to a new institution, the Country House" (010-013).

Architecturally, this shift was visible in the colleges at Oxford, which "date from the Renaissance" and "can scarcely be distinguished in architectural detail from the palaces which the aristocracy were building in the same period" (010-013). The Country House pattern was so pervasive that "our banks and our political edifices to this day bear almost universally the stamp of that Roman and Grecian litter which men discovered on the outskirts of the medieval city" (010-013).

## Cultural Dissociation

The defining characteristic of the Country House idolum was its dissociation from the surrounding community. In medieval conditions, "differences of rank and wealth did not make a very great difference between the life of the lord in his castle, and his retainers" — the lord "shared most of the common man's disabilities" (010-029). In cities, "the lowest workman in the guild shared the institutions of his masters" (010-029).

The Country House changed this fundamentally. "Culture came to mean not a participation in the creative activities of one's own community, but the acquisition of the products of other communities" (010-030). This split transformed popular institutions, which "were deprived of their contacts with the general world of culture, and languished away" (010-030).

## The Acquisitive Society

Mumford identifies the Country House as the engine of modern consumerism: "The Country House standards of consumption are responsible for our Acquisitive Society" (010-021). Each separate Country House "was forced to obtain for its limited circle all the elements that were necessary to the good life in a whole community such as Plato described" (010-030). This created an insatiable demand that would drive the industrial revolution.

The Country House pattern is not limited to grand estates. "It does not matter very much whether the Country House is an estate on Long Island or a cottage in Montclair; whether it is a house in Golder's Green or a family manor in Devonshire: these are essentially affairs of scale, and the underlying identity is plain enough" (010-037).

## Modern Persistence

More than ever, Mumford observes, "the Country House today tries to make up by an abundance of physical goods for all that has been lost through its divorce from the underlying community; more than ever it attempts to be self-sufficient within the limits of suburbia" (010-037). Technologies like "the automobile, the phonograph, and the radiotelephone have only served to increase this self-sufficiency" and "deepened the elements of acquisitiveness and passive, uncreative, mechanical enjoyment" (010-037).

## Consequences

The Country House's "passionate demand for physical goods has given rise to another institution, Coketown" — the industrial city devoted to mass production (010-038). Together, the Country House and Coketown form a linked system, bound by the social myth of the National State, that Mumford sees as one of the primary obstacles to genuine eutopian reconstruction.

The idolum of the Country House "is powerful and complete: I know no other pattern which has imposed its standards and its practices with such complete success upon the greater part of European civilization" (010-021).
