## Introduction

Josiah Royce (1855-1916) was an American objective idealist philosopher and the leading figure of the philosophical school known as American idealism or absolute idealism. In *The Story of Utopias*, Lewis Mumford draws on Royce's conception of the "Beloved Community" as a philosophical touchstone for the utopian ideal of communal integration and shared purpose.

## Royce in Mumford's Account

Mumford evokes Royce's thought in Chapter I, where he diagnoses the fragmented spiritual condition of modern humanity and argues that the problem underlying utopian reconstruction is fundamentally a human problem:

> Our physical reconstructions however have been limited; they have touched chiefly the surfaces of things. The result is that people live in a modern physical environment and carry in their minds an odd assortment of spiritual relics from almost every other age, from that of the primitive, taboo-ridden savage, to the energetic Victorian disciples of Gradgrind and Bounderby. As Mr. Hendrik van Loon pithily says: "A human being with the mind of a sixteenth century tradesman driving a 1921 Rolls-Royce is still a human being with the mind of a sixteenth century tradesman." The problem is fundamentally a human problem. The more completely man is in control of physical nature, the more urgently we must ask ourselves what under the heavens is to move and guide and keep in hand the controller. [I-179]

It is in response to this diagnosis that Royce's philosophy of loyalty and community — the idea that shared ideals can unify the fragmented modern self — becomes acutely relevant to Mumford's utopian argument.

## The Beloved Community

Royce's most enduring contribution to social philosophy is the concept of the "Beloved Community" — a community united by shared loyalty to a common cause, in which individual fulfillment and collective purpose are reconciled. For Royce, the highest moral ideal is "loyalty to loyalty" — the cultivation of communities in which loyalty itself is the binding value. This vision resonated with Mumford's argument that utopian thinking is fundamentally a search for communal integration and shared purpose in an age of spiritual fragmentation.

Mumford's invocation of Royce reflects the broader thesis of *The Story of Utopias*: that human beings have constructed ever more powerful control over physical nature while neglecting the inner world of ideals and purposes. The Beloved Community, in this reading, is not merely a philosophical abstraction but a practical necessity — the spiritual reconstruction that must accompany, and ultimately guide, the material reconstruction of the modern world.

## Place in the Utopian Tradition

Royce did not write a literary utopia, but his philosophical system is deeply utopian in its aspiration to reconcile individual freedom with communal belonging. Mumford's invocation of Royce reflects the broader theme that utopianism draws on idealist philosophy's conviction that human beings can transcend their present limitations through shared ideals. The Beloved Community serves as a bridge between the classic utopian commonwealth — from [[plato|Plato]]'s *Republic* to [[sir-thomas-more|More]]'s *Utopia* — and the modern democratic ideal of an inclusive, participatory social order. Royce's philosophy, like Fouillée's theory of *idées-forces*, provides the intellectual foundation for Mumford's central claim that the way people imagine community has concrete consequences for how they build it.
