## Overview

Hendrik Willem van Loon (1882-1944) was a Dutch-American historian, journalist, and author known for making history accessible to general readers. His work is cited by Lewis Mumford in *The Story of Utopias* as a voice of cultural critique.

## Role in The Story of Utopias

Mumford invokes van Loon's concise critique of modern civilization's cultural lag. As Mumford records in Chapter I:

> As Mr. Hendrik van Loon pithily says: "A human being with the mind of a sixteenth century tradesman driving a 1921 Rolls-Royce is still a human being with the mind of a sixteenth century tradesman." The problem is fundamentally a human problem. (001-028)

Van Loon's observation captures a central theme of Mumford's analysis: the gap between technological progress and the human capacity to direct it toward humane ends. Modern civilization has produced remarkable machines, but the mental and moral equipment of its members has not kept pace.

## Context and Significance

In the preface to the book, Mumford thanks van Loon directly, noting: "Mr. Hendrik van Loon's friendly interest calls for a departing beam of gratitude" (P02-002). This personal acknowledgment suggests that van Loon's work and perspective influenced Mumford's thinking during the writing of *The Story of Utopias*.

Van Loon's appeal lay in his ability to distill broad historical observations into vivid epigrams, bridging academic scholarship and public discourse. His most famous work, *The Story of Mankind* (1921), won the first Newbery Medal and exemplified his approach of presenting history as a grand narrative.
