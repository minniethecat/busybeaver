In *[[the-story-of-utopias]]*, [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] coins "partisanism" as a critical term for the fragmenting logic of single-issue political movements. He defines it in direct opposition to utopianism: where utopianism seeks a comprehensive vision of the [[good-life|good life]], partisanism fixates on one dimension of human existence — nationality, class, religion — and subordinates all others to it.

## Mumford's Critique

Mumford identifies two fundamental weaknesses of partisanism. The first is its tendency to segment human personality. As he writes:

> "Whether our partisanism consists in being first and foremost an American or first and foremost a Theosophist, it tends to limit the world with which we may have commerce and so impoverishes the personality. The person who insists upon being a hundred percent American has by that very emphasis become something less than half a man" (011-044).

The second weakness is that partisanism "breaks the community into vertical divisions, and promotes fictitious antagonisms and kinships which run against the horizontal affiliations and loyalties of a man's life" (011-040). In place of the natural bonds of friendship, work, and shared locality, partisanism substitutes artificial categories that pit neighbor against neighbor.

## Illustration: *Mixed Marriage*

Mumford illustrates the second weakness through [[john-ervine|St. John Ervine]]'s play *Mixed Marriage*, set in a sectarian Ulster community where "religions served as an excuse to keep people from being friendly and decent to their neighbors" (011-040). He generalizes the pattern: "Now for Catholic and Protestant in Mr. St. John Ervine's play one may substitute Democrat and Republican, White Guard and Red Guard, Socialist and Financier, Prohibitionist and anti-Prohibitionist and the results will be just as deplorably the same" (011-041).

## Partisanism and the National State

A key target of Mumford's critique is [[national-state|nationalism]] as a form of partisanism. He argues that the National State co-opts the general goods of culture, science, and art by labeling them as national resources: "The partisans of the State talked about American science as opposed to German science, of Italian art as opposed to French art" (011-041). This tactic of appropriation, Mumford argues, makes culture itself a weapon of division rather than a shared human inheritance.

## The Utopian Alternative

Against partisanism, Mumford sets the utopian method, which insists on the whole person and the whole community. "At long last, the things that unite human beings as human beings, the social inheritance that enables them to realize their stature as human beings, are more important than any particular element that the partisan may lay hold of" (011-044). The utopian vision refuses to reduce the person to a single identity and insists that the [[good-life|good life]] encompasses all dimensions of human flourishing.

## Partisan Utopias

Mumford extends the critique to movements that wear a utopian face but operate by partisan logic. The [[one-big-union|One Big Union]] exemplifies this: "By focusing exclusively on the economic organization of labor, it treats workers solely as producers and the industrial system as the totality of social life" (011-041). Such movements, he argues, "while if all of these partisanisms could be realized the result could scarcely be anything else than discord" (009-1326).
