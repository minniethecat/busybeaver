# Henry George

Henry George (1839–1897) was an American political economist and journalist, best known for his book *Progress and Poverty* (1879), which argued that land value taxation could solve social inequality. In *The Story of Utopias*, Mumford situates George within the nineteenth-century wave of economic reform movements that redirected utopian thought from political blueprints toward economic reconstruction.

## George in Mumford's Account

Mumford places Henry George in a genealogy of economic thinkers who shifted the focus of reform from political institutions to economic relations. He writes:

> "The Renaissance, as we have seen, changed all this. Presently a school of philosophers followed on the heels of the utopians who devoted themselves to preparing fairly minute plans and specifications for the social order. In the beginning, these plans were devoted to politics and criminal reform, like those of Rousseau, Beccaria, Bentham, Jefferson, Godwin, and the eighteenth century reformers generally; in the nineteenth century the main accent was economic, and a number of movements arose which could be traced back to the semi-scientific investigations of Adam Smith, Ricardo, Proudhon, Malthus, Marx, and perhaps half a dozen other thinkers of outstanding importance, among whom we should perhaps include such latter day figures as Mill, Spencer, and Henry George." (011-005)

Mumford's placement of George at the end of this lineage is significant. George represents the moment when economic analysis — the "semi-scientific investigations" of classical political economy — becomes a vehicle for utopian aspiration. The single-tax movement was not merely a fiscal proposal but a vision of social transformation achieved through economic mechanism.

## The Single Tax and Utopian Economics

George's central argument was that land, unlike labor or capital, is a gift of nature whose value belongs to the community as a whole. His proposed "single tax" on land values would capture this unearned increment for public benefit, eliminating the need for other taxes while discouraging land speculation. The logic was elegantly simple: because the value of land derives from community growth and public investment rather than individual effort, the community has a rightful claim to that value.

This idea resonated deeply with utopian reformers who saw land monopoly as a root cause of poverty. If wealth inequality could be traced to a single structural cause — the private appropriation of land rent — then a single structural remedy could address it. The single tax thus appealed to the utopian temperament: it offered a systemic solution rather than piecemeal charity.

## George and the Broader Reform Tradition

Mumford's genealogy in Chapter XI traces a specific historical arc:

| Period | Emphasis | Key Figures |
|--------|----------|------------|
| 18th century | Political and criminal reform | Rousseau, Beccaria, Bentham, Jefferson, Godwin |
| 19th century | Economic reconstruction | Smith, Ricardo, Proudhon, Malthus, Marx |
| Late 19th century | Systematic economic reform | Mill, Spencer, Henry George |

This trajectory — from politics to economics, from rights to mechanisms — reflects a deepening understanding of what utopian change requires. Mumford's inclusion of George in this company signals that the single-tax movement, however specific its proposal, belongs to the broader utopian tradition of systematic social redesign.

## Place in the Utopian Tradition

While George did not produce a literary utopia, his economic program shares the utopian conviction that institutional redesign can produce a fundamentally better society. His influence extended to Fabian socialists, land reformers, and progressive-era thinkers who sought to bridge individualist and collectivist visions. Mumford's treatment of George reflects the broader argument of *The Story of Utopias*: that utopianism is not confined to fictional commonwealths but emerges wherever people envision and advocate for a radically reorganized social order. George's single tax is, in this sense, as much a utopian project as Fourier's phalanxes or More's island — a hypothesis about how human beings might live differently if the institutions governing land were remade.
