## Overview

Eutopian (from Greek *eu-topos*, "good place") is Mumford's term for a genuine, realizable utopia — as opposed to a mere fantasy, escape, or partial idolum. The concept is central to Mumford's argument that utopian thinking is not futile daydreaming but an essential practical activity: "Our choice is not between eutopia and the world as it is, but between eutopia and nothing — or rather, nothingness" (093-XI).

## Etymology and More's Pun

Mumford traces the term to Thomas More's original wordplay. As Mumford explains via Patrick Geddes: "Sir Thomas More was an inveterate punster, and Utopia is a mock-name for either *Outopia*, which means no-place, or *Eutopia* — the good place" (093-XI). The distinction between "no-place" and "good place" is fundamental to Mumford's reading of the utopian tradition. The classic utopias have been criticized as merely outopian — imaginary projections with no grounding in real environments — but their deeper purpose, Mumford argues, is eutopian: to provide a vision of the good life toward which practical efforts can be directed.

## Eutopia vs. Idolum

Mumford contrasts genuine eutopias with [[idolum\|idola]] — the partial, inadequate myths that actually pattern modern behavior. Idola like [[coketown]], the [[country-house]], and the [[national-state]] function as "fake utopias" that shape conduct without providing a coherent vision of the good life. A eutopia, by contrast, must integrate science and art, knowledge and dreams, the physical environment and the mental framework. Mumford writes: "If this dissipation of Western Civilization is to cease, the first step in reconstruction is to make over our inner world, and to give our knowledge and our projections a new foundation" (093-XI).

## The Will-to-Eutopia

Mumford posits a "will-to-eutopia" as a fundamental human drive — one that has historically countered civilizational decline. He argues that "other civilizations have proved inimical to the good life and have failed and passed away; and there is nothing but our own will-to-eutopia to prevent us from following them" (093-XI). This will requires not merely technical knowledge or moral ideals but their integration: "the happiness of man on earth depends upon their combination" (093-XI), echoing Max Beer's observation that Bacon's scientific optimism and More's social idealism remain "still at war with each other."

## The Regional Survey as Foundation

Mumford identifies the Regional Survey — a comprehensive, localized study of a region's geography, history, economy, and culture — as "the first step out of the present impasse." Eutopian reconstruction requires "a permanent foundation of facts" grounded in real environments. He writes: "Regional survey, then, is the bridge by which the specialist whose face is turned towards the library and the laboratory, and the active worker in the field... may come into contact; and out of this contact our plans and our eutopias may be founded" (093-XI). The slogan: "Our castles-in-air must have their foundations in solid ground."
