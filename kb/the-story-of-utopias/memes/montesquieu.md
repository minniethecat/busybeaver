## Overview

Charles-Louis de Secondat, Baron de La Brède et de Montesquieu (1689-1755), commonly known as Montesquieu, was a French political philosopher during the Enlightenment. His works *The Spirit of Law* and *Persian Letters* made foundational contributions to political theory, the sociology of law, and the separation of powers. Mumford cites Montesquieu as a pioneer in the early development of the social sciences.

## Role in The Story of Utopias

Mumford places Montesquieu in the genealogy of the social sciences, linking the utopian impulse to the systematic study of society:

> The development of what are called the social sciences was dimly outlined in Bacon's Novum Organon; but it was not till the eighteenth century, with Quesnay and Montesquieu, that the movement gained any real headway, and down to this day a large part of what is called science in Economics, Politics, and Sociology is only disguised literature — work in which the jargon of science is accepted as a substitute for the scientific method of arriving at factual truth, and in which the effort to mold conduct overwhelms the attempt to reach correct conclusions. (012-013)

## Science and Utopia

For Mumford, Montesquieu represents a pivotal moment in intellectual history: the transition from utopian speculation as purely imaginative literature to utopian thinking allied with empirical investigation. Montesquieu's comparative study of laws and institutions across different societies was a genuine attempt to understand social reality rather than merely project ideals onto it.

Yet Mumford's citation also carries a critical edge. By pairing Montesquieu with the observation that social science "down to this day" has often remained "disguised literature," Mumford suggests that the utopian impulse — the effort to "mold conduct" — has frequently overwhelmed the scientific aspiration to "arrive at correct conclusions."

This tension between normative vision and empirical observation is central to Mumford's own methodology in *The Story of Utopias*. He seeks to evaluate utopian thought critically — understanding its social functions and psychological roots — without either dismissing it as fantasy or accepting it uncritically as science.

## Significance

Montesquieu's appearance in Mumford's narrative serves to anchor the utopian tradition in the broader history of systematic social thought. Utopias are not merely dreams; they are early, imperfect attempts to reason about alternative social arrangements — attempts that, with Montesquieu and his successors, would gradually develop into the modern social sciences.
