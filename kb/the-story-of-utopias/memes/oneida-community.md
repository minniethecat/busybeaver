## Introduction

The Oneida Community was a religious utopian commune founded by John Humphrey Noyes (1811-1886) in 1848 at Oneida, New York. Practicing what Noyes called "Bible Communism," the community held all property in common and developed distinctive social institutions — most notably "complex marriage" (a system in which every man was considered married to every woman), mutual criticism sessions, and communal child-rearing. The community endured for over three decades, making it one of the longest-lived utopian experiments in American history, before reorganizing as a joint-stock corporation (the Oneida Community Ltd., known today for silverware production) in 1881.

[[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] mentions the Oneida Community in passing, using it as a comparative benchmark for assessing the scope of other utopian projects — specifically, as an example of a community that was "utopian" only in a partial and limited sense:

> With Owen I must also dismiss John Ruskin, who began in the last quarter of the nineteenth century to develop plans for a "Guild of St. George." This guild was to form a little island of honest labor and sound education in the midst of the turbid sea of industrialism; but it did not embrace the whole of society, and it was utopian only in the sense that the Oneida Community, let us say, was utopian. (007-005)

This single sentence, brief as it is, positions Oneida within [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]]'s critical framework with considerable precision. To say something is "utopian only in the sense that the Oneida Community ... was utopian" is to draw a line between comprehensive utopian thought — the tradition of [[plato|Plato]], [[sir-thomas-more|More]], and [[edward-bellamy|Bellamy]], who envisioned the reorganization of an entire society — and the more modest tradition of intentional communities that carve out exceptional spaces within the existing order.

## Beliefs and Practices

The Oneida Community was rooted in Perfectionism — the theological doctrine that human beings could achieve spiritual perfection in this life, not merely in the hereafter. Noyes believed that the second coming of Christ had already occurred in 70 AD, and that the task of believers was to establish the kingdom of heaven on earth.

Key social innovations included:

- **Complex marriage**: Every adult member was considered spiritually married to every other adult member of the opposite sex, with sexual relationships regulated by the community's leadership. This was intended to eliminate possessiveness and jealousy.
- **Mutual criticism**: Members were regularly subjected to group criticism sessions, designed to root out personality flaws and behaviors that threatened community harmony.
- **Communal child-rearing**: Children were raised collectively, with parents and non-parents alike sharing responsibilities.
- **Economic communalism**: All property was held in common, and the community operated diverse industries including farming, manufacturing, and silverware production.

## Mumford's Assessment: The Partial Utopia

[[lewis-mumford|Mumford]]'s brief reference to Oneida is revealing in its framing. He mentions it not as a model to emulate but as a **limit case** — a community that was "utopian" in a partial sense, defining the boundary between what counts as utopian thought and what falls short. For [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]], a true utopia must embrace the whole of society: it is not enough to carve out "a little island of honest labor" (007-005).

This judgment aligns with [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]]'s recurring theme: genuine [[utopian-method|utopian thinking]] must address society in its totality, not merely create enclaves of virtue within a corrupt larger order. The "[[utopia-of-reconstruction|utopia of escape]]" — the desire to withdraw from the world rather than transform it — is, for [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]], a persistent temptation that utopian thinkers must resist. Oneida, like [[guild-of-st-george|Ruskin's Guild]], exemplifies this temptation: a well-organized, long-lasting community that chose separation over reconstruction.

The irony of Oneida's trajectory is worth noting. The community endured longer than almost any other American utopian experiment of the period, and its economic successor — Oneida Limited — became a successful commercial enterprise. In a sense, Oneida achieved a kind of practical success that eluded more ambitious utopian projects. But this success came at the cost of the comprehensive vision that [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] considers essential to genuine utopianism. Oneida survived by ceasing to be utopian.

## Place in Utopian History

The Oneida Community belongs to the tradition of religious utopian communities that flourished in nineteenth-century America. Unlike the Fourierist experiments such as [[brook-farm]], which were based on secular socialist principles, Oneida was fundamentally a religious enterprise. Its longevity — over three decades — distinguishes it from most utopian community experiments, which typically collapsed within a few years. Yet this longevity came through economic adaptation and eventual secularization, raising the question of whether the community's "success" was achieved at the expense of its utopian character.

Within [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]]'s taxonomy, Oneida exemplifies the [[utopia-of-reconstruction|utopia of escape]] in its communal form: a withdrawal from the broader society rather than an attempt to reconstruct it. This distinguishes it from the "[[utopia-of-reconstruction|utopias of reconstruction]]" — the grand designs of [[plato|Plato]], [[sir-thomas-more|More]], and Bellamy — that [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] treats as the central thread of the utopian tradition. Oneida is, in [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]]'s implicit classification, utopian *adjacent* — a community experiment that shares some of the impulses of utopian thought without achieving its comprehensive scope.
