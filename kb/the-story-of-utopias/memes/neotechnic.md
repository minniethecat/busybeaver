## Overview

The Neotechnic is Mumford's term for the modern phase of industrial civilization characterized by electricity, scientific methodology, and precision engineering — as distinguished from the paleotechnic phase of coal, steam, and brute force. The term appears in his analysis of industrialism's potential, where he argues that "up to a certain point, industrialism is good, especially in its modern, neotechnic, electrical phase" (077-X). The neotechnic represents the technological dimension of what Mumford calls the possibility of a genuine [[eutopian|eutopia]].

## Neotechnic vs. Paleotechnic

Mumford's periodization of industrial history distinguishes two phases. The paleotechnic phase — the "iron age" of coal, steam, and heavy machinery — produced the environmental devastation and human degradation epitomized by [[coketown]]. The neotechnic phase, by contrast, uses electricity, light metals, and scientific precision to achieve industrial production with less brute force and environmental damage. Mumford sees in the neotechnic the technical means for a better civilization — but only if guided by "eutopian idola" rather than the [[gradgrind-idolum|Gradgrind]] mentality that drove the paleotechnic era.

## Up to a Certain Point

Central to Mumford's assessment of the neotechnic is the qualifying phrase "up to a certain point." He writes: "Up to a certain point, then, using mechanical power rather than human power is good; so is large-scale production, so is the division of labor and division of operation; so is rapid transportation; so is the accurate methodology of the engineer" (077-X). The question is: what point? Mumford's answer is: "up to the point at which the cultivation of a humane life in a community of humane people becomes difficult or impossible." The neotechnic phase provides the tools, but not the values, for eutopian reconstruction.

## The Coketown Trap

The danger Mumford identifies is that the neotechnic phase can perpetuate "the horrid mistake" of Coketown — "believing that all these things were good in themselves" (077-X). Without a reorientation toward human values, even the most advanced neotechnic civilization merely produces a more efficient version of the same industrial idolum. Mumford invokes George Santayana's observation that "invention and organization, which ought to have increased leisure by producing the necessaries of life with little labor, have only increased the population, degraded labor, and diffused luxury" (077-X).

## Relation to Eutopian Reconstruction

For Mumford, the neotechnic phase provides the material conditions for eutopia but does not itself constitute one. The task of eutopian reconstruction is to redirect neotechnic capabilities — electrical power, scientific knowledge, precision engineering — toward the cultivation of the good life, rather than the mere accumulation of goods. This requires the integration of science and art, knowledge and values, that Mumford argues has been the missing element in all modern reform movements.
