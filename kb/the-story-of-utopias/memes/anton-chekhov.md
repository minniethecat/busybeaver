# Anton Chekhov

Anton Pavlovich Chekhov (1860–1904) was a Russian playwright and master of the modern short story. In *The Story of Utopias*, Lewis Mumford invokes Chekhov alongside a constellation of literary figures — Boccaccio, Pope, Meredith, Wells, and Shaw — to illustrate the Country House as one of the great recurrent themes of Western literature.

## Chekhov in Mumford's Account

Mumford places Chekhov within a literary survey that traces the Country House tradition across centuries and national cultures. He writes:

> "When we turn our attention from Rabelais' conceit of an anti-monastic order, we discover that he has given us an excellent picture of the Country House, and of what I shall take the liberty of calling Country House culture. We see pretty much the same outlines in the introduction to Boccaccio's *Decameron*; it is elaborately described in terms of that most complete of Country Houses, Hampton Court, in Pope's 'Rape of the Lock'; it is vividly pictured by Meredith in his portrait of *The Egoist*; and it is analyzed in Mr. H.G. Wells' cruel description of Bladesover in *Tono-Bungay*, as well as by Mr. Bernard Shaw in *Heartbreak House*. Whether Mr. W.H. Mallock holds the pattern of Country House culture up to us in *The New Republic* or Anton Chekhov penetrates its aimlessness and futility in *The Cherry Orchard*, The Country House is one of the recurrent themes of literature." (010-020)

Chekhov's role in this survey is distinctive: where earlier writers celebrate or satirize the Country House, Chekhov "penetrates its aimlessness and futility" — capturing a moment when the leisured estate is no longer a viable social ideal but a site of paralysis and loss.

## The Cherry Orchard and Country House Culture

*The Cherry Orchard* (1904), Chekhov's final play, is the work Mumford singles out. Set on a Russian estate with its famous cherry orchard, the play traces the inability of its aristocratic owners to prevent the estate's sale and the orchard's destruction. The central tension — between the nostalgic attachment to the Country House and the economic forces that render it unsustainable — makes it a natural reference point for Mumford's analysis of Country House culture as an ideal in decay.

Chekhov's other major plays — *The Seagull* (1896), *Uncle Vanya* (1897), and *Three Sisters* (1901) — are all set on provincial estates and explore the psychological terrain of characters trapped between inherited privilege and encroaching obsolescence. The country estate in Chekhov is never a utopia; it is, rather, a space where the gap between aspiration and reality becomes painfully visible.

## Place in the Utopian Tradition

Chekhov did not write utopias. His contribution to Mumford's argument is as a diagnostician of the Country House ideal — showing that even before the political revolutions of the twentieth century, the leisured estate was already hollowed out from within by aimlessness and futility. Where utopians project perfected communities onto the future, Chekhov's characters confront the reality that the idealized life of the country house is already slipping away. This tension — between the dream of escape and the inevitability of change — is, Mumford suggests, central to understanding the Country House as a half-conscious utopia that could never fully realize its promise.

## The Country House Survey

Mumford's literary survey in Chapter X traces the Country House motif across five centuries of European literature:

| Period | Author | Work | Treatment of Country House |
|--------|--------|------|---------------------------|
| 14th c. | Boccaccio | *Decameron* | Aristocratic refuge from plague |
| 16th c. | Rabelais | *Gargantua and Pantagruel* | Anti-monastic vision of Theleme |
| 18th c. | Pope | *The Rape of the Lock* | Hampton Court as social theater |
| 19th c. | Meredith | *The Egoist* | Country house as psychological arena |
| 20th c. | Chekhov | *The Cherry Orchard* | Aimlessness and futility of estate life |
