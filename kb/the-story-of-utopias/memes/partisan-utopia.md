## Definition

A **partisan utopia** is Mumford's term for the partial, single-issue utopian programs advanced by nineteenth-century reform movements. Unlike the comprehensive literary utopias of [[plato]] or [[sir-thomas-more]], which sought to imagine a complete and coherent commonwealth, the partisan utopia focuses on one dimension of social life—economic distribution, legal rights, political representation—while neglecting the broader ecology of human existence.

Mumford surveys "a multitude of partisan organizations and movements, each of which is strenuously bent on realizing its private and partisan utopia" (009-1236). His critique is not that these movements were wrong to reject the degradations of industrial society—"to reject what industrial society had to offer its members in the filthy factory districts and wretched slums of Coketown was obviously to reject barbarism and degradation of the worst sort" (009-1248)—but that their visions were too narrow to succeed on their own terms.

## Three Forms of Partisan Utopia

Mumford identifies three characteristic patterns among the partisan utopias:

**The bureaucratic utopia.** "One of these partisan utopias issues from a bundle of red-tape; everything is filed and ticketed and labelled there; and anything in life that cannot be treated in this fashion does not exist" (009-1326). This is the utopia of purely administrative reform—efficient, rationalized, and blind to the qualitative dimensions of human life.

**The mechanical utopia.** "Another is a mechanical contraption; somehow it seems to litter little mechanical contraptions; and its aim is, it would seem, to do away with vegetation and reproduction, so that everything under the sun might be performed with the sterile accuracy of the machine" (009-1326). Mumford has in mind here [[edward-bellamy]]'s *Looking Backward*, which he elsewhere describes as a "hideous cog-and-wheel utopia" (009-1286).

**The legalistic utopia.** "A third utopia of the partisan calls human beings, with all their color and thickness, 'individuals,' and makes the good life a matter of legal relationships without any regard to their necessities in time and space; such a utopia could almost be carried in one's pocket, so much is it a matter of verbal statement" (009-1326).

## The Core Weaknesses

Mumford identifies two structural flaws common to all partisan utopias:

**Externalism.** "There was still another weakness that characterized all the partisan utopias of the nineteenth century. That weakness was their externalism" (009-1266). The partisan utopias focused on altering institutions—laws, property relations, machinery of distribution—while neglecting the inner life. Mumford invokes Carlyle's critique: "Given a world of knaves, we are trying by various cunning devices to produce an honesty from their united action" (009-1286).

**Lack of a coherent scheme of values.** The partisan utopias "crumble away as soon as they meet the opposition of such powerful collective utopias as Coketown or the Country House" because they lack "any definite and coherent scheme of values" (009-1252). A movement organized solely around economic redistribution cannot withstand the gravitational pull of the [[country-house-idolum]]'s promise of genteel withdrawal, or the [[coketown-idolum]]'s promise of material abundance.

**Absence of regional knowledge.** The partisan utopias lacked what Mumford calls "this common tissue of definite, verifiable, localized knowledge"—the kind of integrated understanding of a region's soil, work, and play that the [[regional-survey]] method provides. Lacking it, the partisan programs were "one-sided and ignorant and abstract—devising paper programs for the reconstruction of a paper world" (009-1403).

## The Utopian Method Debate

Mumford engages critically with [[friedrich-engels]]'s distinction between "utopian" and "scientific" socialism. Engels, in *Socialism: Utopian and Scientific*, had "made a plea for a realistic method of thought, which limited itself to a here and now, as against what he derided as the utopian method, the attempt on the part of a single thinker to give a detailed picture of the society of the future" (009-1252).

Mumford turns this critique on its head: "if the utopian socialism of Owen has been ineffective, the realistic socialism of Marx has been equally ineffective" (009-1252). The cooperative movement has partially fulfilled Owen's vision, while "the dictatorship of the proletariat rests upon very shaky foundations, and such success as it has had is due perhaps as much to Marx's literary picture of what it would be like as to anything else" (009-1252). The "realistic" method, in other words, succeeded only to the extent that it was secretly utopian—providing a compelling [[idolum]] around which people could organize their hopes and actions.
