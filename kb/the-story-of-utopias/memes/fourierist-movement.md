The Fourierist Movement, also known as Fourierism, was a utopian socialist movement inspired by the theories of [[charles-fourier|Charles Fourier]] (1772–1837). Fourier proposed a comprehensive system of social reorganization based on his theory of human passions, organized around self-contained communities called phalanxes. The movement that grew from his ideas spread from France to the United States and beyond, reaching its peak of influence during the 1840s.

## Fourier's Theory of Passions

At the core of Fourierism lay the conviction that human passions — far from being sinful impulses requiring suppression — represented the raw material from which a harmonious society could be constructed. Fourier identified twelve fundamental passions and argued that a properly designed community would channel these drives into productive and pleasurable activity rather than repressing them.

> Fourier differs largely from the early utopians in that he is concerned first of all not with modifying human nature but with finding out what it actually is. His utopia is to be based upon an understanding of man's actual physical and mental makeup, and its institutions are to be such as will permit man's original nature to function freely. (VI-678)

This framework distinguishes Fourierism from both the moralistic tradition (which would reform passions) and the libertarian tradition (which would simply release them). The Fourierist project is essentially an engineering problem: given the repertoire of human passions, design the institutional machinery that will convert them into productive and pleasurable activity. As [[albert-brisbane|Albert Brisbane]], Fourier's principal American advocate, summarized: "social institutions are to these passional forces what machinery is to material forces" (VI-680).

## The Phalanx Model

The organizational unit of Fourierism was the **phalanx** — a self-contained community of approximately 1,500 to 1,600 persons occupying a domain of at least a square league. Fourier insisted on diversity of terrain as a precondition for success, following the valley section principle:

> A flat country, such as Antwerp, Leipzig, Orleans, would be totally unsuitable … owing to the uniformity of land surface. It will therefore be necessary to select a diversified region, like the surroundings of Lausanne, or at the very least, a fine valley, provided with a stream of water and a forest, like the valley of Brussel or of Halle. (VI-686)

At the physical center of the phalanx stood the **phalanstery** — a vast edifice housing all the collective functions of the community. Mumford describes it as "a palace complete in all its appointments" (VI-690), with three wings corresponding to the Material (workshops and halls of industry), the Social (banquet halls and reception rooms), and the Intellectual (library, scientific collections, artists' studios). A Temple of Material Harmonies and a Temple of Unityism anchored the structure at either end, while an observatory with telegraph and signal tower crowned the summit — anticipating the networked communities of a later age (VI-690).

## Spread to America

Fourierist ideas reached the United States primarily through Albert Brisbane, whose *Social Destiny of Man* (1840) adapted Fourier's thought for an American audience. Brisbane's *General Introduction to the Social Sciences* was listed in Mumford's bibliography. His advocacy inspired the creation of numerous Fourierist communities across the United States during the 1840s, including the North American Phalanx in New Jersey and the conversion of [[brook-farm|Brook Farm]] to Fourierist principles in 1844.

Mumford notes that Brook Farm was "a fumbling attempt to plant a phalanstery without paying any attention to the conditions which Fourier would have rigorously imposed" (VI-704), underscoring the gap between Fourier's detailed specifications and the improvisational reality of American Fourierist communities.

## Practical Outcomes

The most successful practical outcome of Fourier's ideas was the [[familistere-de-guise|Familistère de Guise]], founded by Jean-Baptiste André Godin at Guise, France, in 1859. Mumford presents it as evidence that Fourier's ideas bore genuine practical fruit — not through slavish replication of Fourierist doctrine, but through intelligent adaptation to actual conditions. Godin's industrial cooperative survived and thrived long after the American Fourierist phalanxes had dissolved, demonstrating that Fourier's principles could be translated into durable institutions when adapted to concrete economic circumstances rather than pursued as doctrinal purity.

## Mumford's Assessment

In *The Story of Utopias*, [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] treats Fourier as one of the most original minds in the utopian tradition. His assessment cuts two ways: Fourier's phalanx model represents "the first plan for colonizing the wilderness of industrial barbarism that existed at the beginning of the nineteenth century, and redeeming that wilderness to civilization" (VI-704), yet Fourierism also exemplifies the nineteenth-century tendency to turn utopia into machinery — "the means has become the end, and the genuine problem of ends has been forgotten" (VI-834).

Mumford ultimately classes Fourier among the distinguished exceptions to this mechanical drift, alongside [[thomas-spence|Thomas Spence]], precisely because Fourier's theory of passions grounded his utopia in human nature rather than in institutional blueprints alone. The Fourierist movement, at its best, represented the conviction that systematic social redesign, rather than piecemeal reform or moral exhortation, offered the path to a better world.
