## Introduction

*Ideal Commonwealths* is an anthology of classic utopian texts compiled and introduced by the English scholar Henry Morley (1822-1894), published by George Routledge in London in 1886. The volume collects Plutarch's *Lycurgus*, [[sir-thomas-more|More]]'s *[[utopia-more|Utopia]]*, [[francis-bacon|Bacon]]'s *[[new-atlantis|New Atlantis]]*, [[tommaso-campanella|Campanella]]'s *[[city-of-the-sun|City of the Sun]]*, and a fragment of Joseph Hall's *Mundus Alter et Idem*. It was one of the first modern anthologies to present the utopian tradition as a coherent literary and philosophical genre.

[[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] cites Morley's anthology throughout *The Story of [[utopia-more|Utopia]]s* as his principal source for the classic utopian texts. In his bibliography, he directs readers to it for [[sir-thomas-more|More]]'s *[[utopia-more|Utopia]]*, [[francis-bacon|Bacon]]'s *[[new-atlantis|New Atlantis]]*, and [[tommaso-campanella|Campanella]]'s *[[city-of-the-sun|City of the Sun]]* (014-010, 014-012, 014-013). [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] uses the term "[[ideal-commonwealth|ideal commonwealths]]" as his central organizing concept, opening the book with the declaration: "It is the separate reality of utopia that we are going to explore in the course of this book — [[utopia-more|Utopia]] as a world by itself, divided into [[ideal-commonwealth|ideal commonwealths]], with all its communities clustered into proud cities, aiming bravely at the [[good-life|good life]]" (001-003). Morley's anthology serves as both source material and methodological model for [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]]'s survey.

## Summary and Significance

Morley's collection brings together five foundational texts spanning from ancient Greece to the early seventeenth century. Each text represents a distinct mode of utopian speculation:

- *Lycurgus* (Plutarch): The legendary Spartan lawgiver — a historical rather than fictional utopia, showing how the ideal of an ordered commonwealth was projected onto a real or legendary past (002-006).
- *[[utopia-more|Utopia]]* ([[sir-thomas-more|More]]): The founding text of the genre, setting the template for the imaginary island commonwealth.
- *[[new-atlantis|New Atlantis]]* ([[francis-bacon|Bacon]]): A fragment focused on the scientific organization of society, centered on Salomon's House.
- *[[city-of-the-sun|City of the Sun]]* ([[tommaso-campanella|Campanella]]): A theocratic utopia governed by astrological and philosophical principles.
- *Mundus Alter et Idem* (Hall): A satirical antipodean fantasy, included in fragment.

## Mumford's Assessment

[[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] does not analyze *Ideal Commonwealths* directly — it is a source, not a subject of analysis — but his use of the phrase "[[ideal-commonwealth|ideal commonwealths]]" as his central category reveals Morley's influence. The term recurs throughout the book:

> This discussion of ideal commonwealths gets its form and its color from the time in which it is written. (001-004)

> Geographically speaking, then, the ideal commonwealth was a city-region. (002-011)

> Now that we have ransacked the literature of ideal commonwealths for examples of the utopian vision and the utopian method, there remains another class of utopias. (010-002)

[[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] explicitly defines his subject in terms derived from Morley's framework: "I set out to treat such plans for the improvement of the human community as had been embodied in complete pictures of an ideal commonwealth" (013-002). The anthology thus shaped the very definition of the genre that [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] inherited and expanded.

## Place in the Utopian Tradition

*Ideal Commonwealths* played a key role in establishing the utopian canon for English-language readers in the late nineteenth century. It provided the textual foundation upon which [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]]'s own survey built, and its selection of texts — from Lycurgus to [[tommaso-campanella|Campanella]] — effectively defined the lineage of utopian writing that [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] would trace and critique.

The anthology also represents the Victorian era's growing interest in systematic social reform through literary imagination. By collecting these texts together, Morley suggested that utopian writing constituted a tradition worthy of serious study — a suggestion [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] would fulfill in *The Story of [[utopia-more|Utopia]]s*.
