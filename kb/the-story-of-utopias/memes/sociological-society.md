# Sociological Society of Great Britain

The **Sociological Society of Great Britain** was founded in 1903 as the institutional vehicle for the distinctive approach to sociology advanced by [[patrick-geddes|Patrick Geddes]] and his collaborators, including [[victor-branford|Victor Branford]], who served as the Society's organizing secretary. In [[lewis-mumford|Lewis Mumford]]'s *The Story of Utopias*, the Society appears in the acknowledgments as a concrete influence on the book's intellectual formation.

## Mumford's Acknowledgment

In the preface to *The Story of Utopias*, Mumford records a specific debt:

> "To Messrs. Victor Branford and Alexander Farquharson of the Sociological Society of Great Britain I am indebted for many pertinent suggestions" (P02-002).

This acknowledgment, placed alongside Mumford's broader tribute to Geddes — "the general background of ideas has been heavily colored by my contacts with Professor Patrick Geddes" (P02-001) — situates the Sociological Society within the network of influences that shaped Mumford's method.

## The Geddesian Approach

The Society promoted a distinctive intellectual program that distinguished it from more abstract and theoretical forms of academic sociology. The Geddesian approach, as institutionalized through the Society, emphasized:

- **Regional survey** — the systematic mapping of geographical, historical, economic, and social data within a defined territory as the necessary foundation for civic action
- **Civic renewal** — the application of survey findings to practical projects of urban and regional improvement
- **Integration of disciplines** — the conviction that sociology must draw upon geography, history, biology, and the arts, resisting the specialization that, in Geddes's view, fragmented understanding

This program resonated deeply with the method Mumford deploys in *The Story of Utopias*: approaching utopian thought not as a sequence of abstract proposals but as expressions of specific historical and geographical conditions, to be evaluated by their relationship to the actual possibilities of a given time and place.

## The Geddes-Mumford Lineage: From Hippodamus to Regional Survey

Mumford positions Geddes within a lineage of thinker-practitioners stretching back to antiquity. In a passage that illuminates the intellectual genealogy of his own work, Mumford draws a direct parallel between the ancient city planner and the modern sociologist:

> "Hippodamus was one of the first city planners known to history, and he achieved fame in the ancient world by designing cities on the somewhat monotonous checkerboard design we know so well in America. He realized, apparently, that a city was something more than a collection of houses, streets, markets, and temples; and so, whilst he was putting the physical town to rights, he concerned himself with the more basic problem of the social order. If it adds at all to our sense of reality in going through utopia, let me confess that it is ultimately through the inspiration and example of another Hippodamus — Patrick Geddes, the town planner for Jerusalem and many other cities — that this book about utopias came to be written" (002-010).

This passage reveals a double genealogy. Hippodamus of Miletus, the fifth-century BCE architect who designed the port of Piraeus, understood that the physical form of a city could not be separated from its social constitution — an insight that, Mumford suggests, was recovered and deepened by Geddes in the early twentieth century. The Sociological Society was the institutional vehicle through which this recovered insight was transmitted to a generation of planners, sociologists, and social thinkers — Mumford among them.

Mumford further elaborates the specific methodology that Geddes advanced through the Society:

> "The aim of the Regional Survey is to take a geographic region and explore it in every aspect. It differs from the social survey with which we are acquainted in America in that it is not chiefly a survey of evils; it is, rather, a survey of the existing conditions in all their aspects; and it emphasizes to a much greater extent than the social survey the natural characteristics of the environment" (014-002).

The distinction is crucial to Mumford's own project. The Regional Survey, as Geddes conceived it, was not a catalog of deficiencies — the standard mode of nineteenth-century reform — but a comprehensive synthesis of environmental, historical, and social data. This method, institutionalized through the Sociological Society, directly informs the structure of *The Story of Utopias*, which treats each utopia not as an abstract proposal but as an expression of the specific environmental and historical conditions from which it emerged.

## Influence on Mumford's Method

The Sociological Society's emphasis on survey-before-plan — the principle that adequate reconstruction depends upon adequate understanding of existing conditions — informs Mumford's central distinction between the "utopia of reconstruction" and the "utopia of escape." The former, which Mumford champions, emerges from a realistic appraisal of the world as it is; the latter, which he criticizes, substitutes wish for knowledge. This methodological commitment, traceable through Geddes and the Society, is one of the organizing principles of Mumford's entire treatment.

Mumford's intellectual debt to the Society and to Geddes is not merely a matter of personal acknowledgment. The very structure of *The Story of Utopias* — its movement from historical survey to synthetic analysis, its insistence on evaluating each utopia against the actual conditions of its time and place, its refusal to treat ideas in abstraction from their geographical and institutional settings — embodies the Geddesian method that the Sociological Society was founded to advance. In this sense, the Society's influence on Mumford extends beyond "pertinent suggestions" into the methodological foundations of the work.
