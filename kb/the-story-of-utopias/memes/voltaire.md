## Biography

Voltaire (François-Marie Arouet, 1694–1778) was a French Enlightenment writer, historian, and philosopher. One of the most prolific and influential intellectuals of the eighteenth century, Voltaire was known for his wit, his criticism of religious intolerance and dogmatism, and his advocacy for freedom of speech and the separation of church and state. His vast literary output included plays, poems, novels, essays, historical works, and an enormous correspondence.

His most famous work, *Candide, ou l'Optimisme* (1759), is a satirical novella that follows the misadventures of the naive young Candide as he travels the world and encounters war, natural disaster, religious persecution, and human cruelty — all while his tutor Pangloss insists that this is "the best of all possible worlds." The work is both a philosophical critique of Leibnizian optimism and a meditation on how to live meaningfully in a flawed world.

## The Utopian Context: Retreat and Cultivation

Mumford situates Voltaire within a broader eighteenth-century retreat from systematic utopian construction. In the wake of the grand institutional utopias of More, Andreae, Campanella, and Bacon, the eighteenth century — an age Mumford characterizes as "painfully artificial and 'arranged'" — turned away from comprehensive social blueprints toward ideals of individual withdrawal and cultivation. Voltaire, alongside Montaigne and the archetype of Robinson Crusoe, exemplifies this turn:

> One remembers the way in which Montaigne spent his declining years; one remembers Voltaire; and one sees how deeply the ideal of Robinson Crusoe — a cultivated Robinson Crusoe, surrounded with books and beyond the reach of any king and court — colored the deepest aspirations of this period. (656)

This ideal of the cultivated retreat — the gentleman-philosopher withdrawing to his country estate to tend his garden and his library — represented a significant shift in utopian thought. The utopia was no longer a city to be built or an institution to be founded, but a personal mode of life to be cultivated. This inward turn would, in Mumford's view, persist until the industrial and social transformations of the nineteenth century made systematic utopian construction urgent once again.

## "Let Us Cultivate Our Garden" — The Eutopian Motto

Voltaire's most significant appearance in *The Story of Utopias* comes in the closing pages, where Mumford enlists the final words of *Candide* — "Il faut cultiver notre jardin" — as the summation of his own eutopian philosophy:

> The chief business of eutopians was summed up by Voltaire in the final injunction of *Candide*: Let us cultivate our garden. The aim of the real eutopian is the culture of his environment, most distinctly not the culture, and above all not the exploitation, of some other person's environment. (1515)

This passage is crucial to understanding Mumford's distinction between **utopia** (an idealized nowhere, a comprehensive blueprint for a perfect society) and **eutopia** (a reachable good place, grounded in the cultivation of one's actual environment). By invoking Voltaire's conclusion to *Candide*, Mumford aligns his project with a tradition of practical, grounded idealism — the work of improving the world begins not with grand schemes but with the immediate cultivation of one's own garden, whether literal or metaphorical.

Mumford elaborates: a genuine pattern for the eutopian life, once planted in a particular locality, may spread as organically as Coketown duplicated itself throughout the Western World. The reconstruction of personal *idola* — the mental environment — is the first step, and it can begin "wherever we are, without further ado" (1515).

## From Utopia to Eutopia

Voltaire's role in Mumford's argument illustrates a key transition in utopian thought:

| Era | Utopian Mode | Representative Figure |
|-----|-------------|----------------------|
| Renaissance / Baroque | Institutional design (More, Andreae, Campanella, Bacon) | City as utopian blueprint |
| Eighteenth Century | Cultivated withdrawal (Montaigne, Voltaire, Rousseau) | Garden as personal retreat |
| Nineteenth Century | Systematic reconstruction (Owen, Fourier, Bellamy) | Community as social experiment |
| Twentieth Century (Mumford) | Eutopian cultivation (Voltaire's garden) | Environment as ongoing project |

In Mumford's reading, Voltaire's quietist conclusion — tend your own garden, do not exploit the gardens of others — is not a counsel of resignation but a principle of constructive action. It rejects both the passivity of Panglossian optimism and the hubris of comprehensive social blueprints, advocating instead for the patient, localized work of environmental improvement that Mumford calls eutopia.
