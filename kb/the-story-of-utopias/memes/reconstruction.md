**Reconstruction** (or the *utopia of reconstruction*) is one of the two fundamental types of utopia identified by [[lewis-mumford|Lewis Mumford]] in *The Story of Utopias*, contrasted with the [[idolum|utopia of escape]]. While the utopia of escape leaves the external world unchanged, the utopia of reconstruction "seeks to change it so that one may have intercourse with it on one's own terms" (001-145).

## Mumford's Definition

Mumford introduces the distinction between the two utopian modes in Chapter I. The utopia of reconstruction is "a vision of a reconstituted environment which is better adapted to the nature and aims of the human beings who dwell within it than the actual one; and not merely better adapted to their actual nature, but better fitted to their possible developments" (001-175). He emphasizes that a reconstructed environment encompasses not merely physical improvements but "a new set of habits, a fresh scale of values, a different net of relationships and institutions" (001-177).

> "In one we build impossible castles in the air; in the other we consult a surveyor and an architect and a mason and proceed to build a house which meets our essential needs; as well as houses made of stone and mortar are capable of meeting them." (001-145)

## Reconstruction vs. Escape

The genuine alternative for most people, Mumford argues, is "between an aimless utopia of escape and a purposive utopia of reconstruction" (001-149). The utopia of reconstruction leads outward into the world, while the utopia of escape leads backward into the utopian's ego. This distinction is central to Mumford's entire analytical framework, allowing him to classify and evaluate utopian thought from [[republic-plato|Plato]]'s [[republic-plato|Republic]] to the [[socialist-movement|socialist movements]] of the nineteenth century.

## The Comprehensive Vision

A key virtue of the utopia of reconstruction, according to Mumford, is its comprehensive scope: "It not merely pictures a whole world, but it faces every part of it at the same time" (001-183). Unlike the compartmentalized thinking of the practical man and the idealist, the reconstruction utopia treats material and mental reconstruction as "the faces of a single thing" (001-181). This integration of the physical environment and the [[idolum]] — the inner world of ideas — is what distinguishes the genuine utopian from the mere inventor or industrialist.

## Nineteenth-Century Limitations

Mumford observes that the utopias of reconstruction in the nineteenth century "had a deadly sameness of purpose and a depressing singleness of interest; and although they saw society whole, they saw the problem of reconstructing society as a simple problem of industrial reorganization" (009-944). This limitation would later be addressed by thinkers like [[patrick-geddes|Patrick Geddes]], whose Regional Survey approach provided the concrete, verifiable, localized knowledge that Mumford believed all "partisan utopias and reconstruction programs have lacked" (012-1403).
