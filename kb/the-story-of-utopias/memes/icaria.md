## Overview

Icaria is the fictional utopian country depicted in Étienne Cabet's 1840 novel *Voyage en Icarie* (Journey to Icaria). It represents a highly centralized, state-managed communist society divided into one hundred nearly equal provinces, each further subdivided into ten communes. Mumford characterizes Icaria as "essentially not an ideal but an idealization" — a rationalized vision of Napoleonic bureaucracy extended into every domain of life (062-VIII).

## National Organization

Icaria is divided into one hundred provinces of almost equal extent and population, each containing ten communes. The provincial capital sits at the center of each province, while each communal city forms the center of its commune. Mumford notes that "the elegance and precision of the decimal system has overlaid the facts of geography," comparing the arrangement to the French Revolution's arbitrary division of France into departments (062-VIII).

## The Capital City: Icara

The capital city Icara is a reconstructed Paris built on a straightened river, its banks enclosed in two straight walls and deepened to receive ocean vessels. The city is almost circular, divided into two equal parts by the river, with the civic center on a circular island planted with trees. The city contains sixty communes of nearly equal size, each with a school, hospital, temple, shops, public places, and monuments. Streets are laid out as fifty avenues parallel to the river and fifty perpendicular to it — a geometric scheme Mumford finds logically implausible for a circular city (062-VIII).

## Daily Life and State Control

Life in Icaria is comprehensively administered by the State. A committee of scientists regulates the food served to all citizens. Work is set at seven hours in summer and six in winter, with all products of industry deposited in public stores. Even clothing is prescribed by a committee on clothes — "every Icarian's dress is a uniform, even as every Icarian is an official of the State" (062-VIII). Education follows a standardized national curriculum from age five to eighteen, and professional opportunities are limited to industries recognized and sanctioned by the State, with an annual list published of the number of workers needed in each profession.

## Critique

Mumford is sharply critical of Icaria's totalizing bureaucracy, describing it as "a National State, abundantly organized for war, and remaining on that footing in the midst of its peacetime activities" (062-VIII). He observes that the political system of popular assemblies is merely "a shadow of that dictatorial power which was first wielded by Icar and was in turn transmitted to the committees and bureaus" — what he calls an epiphenomenon. Mumford notes that "there was more of Cabet than of Marx perhaps in embryonic Soviet Russia," drawing a direct line between Icarian ideology and twentieth-century state communism (062-VIII).

## Historical Significance

Despite its shortcomings, Cabet's *Voyage en Icarie* became "one of the best sellers among workingmen in 1845" (062-VIII). Under the influence of Robert Owen, Cabet eventually led a small band of communist pioneers to establish a real-world Icarian settlement in the American frontier. Mumford calls this "an ironic twist of circumstance," since the fictional Icaria "was a national state, with all its pomp and dignity and splendor, and not a squalid collection of huts in the midst of a dreary prairie" (062-VIII). Cabet died in America, and the Icarian communities eventually dissolved, though the idea of Icaria — a centralized, rationally administered utopia — persisted as an influential model in the history of utopian thought.
