James Silk Buckingham's Model Town Association was a proposal for a planned industrial community, outlined in the mid-nineteenth century as a response to the chaotic conditions of early industrial urbanism. Mumford describes Buckingham as "one of those erratic men of affairs which the fertile soil of British individualism produces" — a widely traveled reformer whose copious writings addressed the defects of industrial society (006-026, 006-027).

Buckingham's proposal called for "a model town association, with a limited liability, for the purpose of building a new town called Victoria" (006-033). The town was to incorporate "every improvement in position, plan, drainage, ventilation, architecture, supply of water, light, and every other elegance and convenience," with a population limited to about ten thousand inhabitants within an area of roughly one square mile (006-033). The plan included common laundries, kitchens, refectories, and nurseries, with free medical advice provided to all residents (006-034).

The broader context of Buckingham's proposal was the proliferation of reform associations in early Victorian Britain. As Mumford notes, the nineteenth century "was thriving with associations," including "Temperance Societies, Tract Societies, Home Missions," and numerous philanthropic bodies — of which Buckingham's model town scheme was among the most ambitious attempts to address urban conditions through comprehensive planning (006-028).

Though Buckingham's specific vision was never realized in his lifetime, Mumford traces a direct line from his proposal to later achievements: "Buckingham thought that, given a successful model town, the rest of England might in time be colonized by the surplus population... In 1848 this utopia was a chimera; in 1898 Ebenezer Howard's Garden City at Letchworth began to give it form" (006-036). Buckingham's limited but practical utopianism thus served as a crucial bridge between the speculative utopias of the early nineteenth century and the concrete town-planning movements of the twentieth.
