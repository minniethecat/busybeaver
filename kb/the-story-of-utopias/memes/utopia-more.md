# Utopia

**Utopia** (1516) is [[sir-thomas-more|Sir Thomas More]]'s seminal work that gave the utopian tradition its name. [[lewis-mumford|Lewis Mumford]] treats it as the pivotal text that revived the [[plato|Platonic]] tradition after nearly two thousand years and first articulated the relationship between the [[idolum]] and material community.

## Historical Context

[[sir-thomas-more|More]]'s *Utopia* was "the bridge by which he sought to span the gap between the old order of the Middle Age, and the new interests and institutions of the Renaissance" (001-004). It emerged from the collision of the New Learning — the recovery of Greek philosophy — and the discovery of the New World.

> "More's narrator, Raphael Hythloday, is a Portuguese scholar, learned in Greek, who has left his family possessions with his kinsmen and has gone adventuring for other continents with Americus Vesputius" (003-006).

Through [[raphael-hythloday|Hythloday]], "no institution is too fantastic but that it might exist — on the other side of the world" (003-006).

## The Bridge Between Worlds

[[sir-thomas-more|More]] occupies a unique position in [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]]'s genealogy. Unlike [[plato|Plato]], who was a bachelor proposing that men should "live like monks or soldiers" (005-008), [[sir-thomas-more|More]] was a married man who "defended the individual family" (005-008). Unlike later scientific utopians, [[sir-thomas-more|More]] "looked to social reform and religious ethics to transform society" (012-005) rather than to technological innovation alone.

> "Sir Thomas More makes us aware of the fact that a community becomes a community to the extent that it has shared possessions, and he suggests that the local group might develop such a common life as the old colleges of Oxford have enjoyed" (012-068).

His vision of shared possessions as the foundation of community would echo through later experiments — from the [[owenite-communities|Owenite communities]] to the [[garden-city-organization|Garden City Association]] — each attempting in its own way to realize [[sir-thomas-more|More]]'s insight that the [[good-life|good life]] requires common purpose embodied in common institutions.
