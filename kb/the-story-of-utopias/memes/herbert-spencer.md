# Herbert Spencer

## Introduction

Herbert Spencer (1820-1903) was a British philosopher, biologist, and sociologist whose theory of social evolution dominated late nineteenth-century thought. In *The Story of Utopias*, Lewis Mumford references Spencer twice — first to contrast him with the holistic thinking of Patrick Geddes, and second to situate him among the major nineteenth-century intellects whose ideas shaped the utopian tradition's assumptions about social change.

| Period | Role | Key Works / Context |
|--------|------|---------------------|
| 1855 | *Principles of Psychology* | Early evolutionary framework |
| 1860s–1890s | *System of Synthetic Philosophy* | Unified biology, psychology, sociology, ethics |
| 1903 | Death | Evolutionary sociology already in decline |

## Spencer in Mumford's Account

Mumford's first mention of Spencer comes in the context of his admiration for Patrick Geddes, the Scottish biologist and town planner, to whom Mumford attributes the original inspiration for *The Story of Utopias*. Contrasting Geddes's integrative, organismic approach with the narrower evolutionary frameworks of his contemporaries, Mumford observes:

> In many ways the distance between Geddes and Aristotle or Hippodamus seems much less than that which separates Geddes and Herbert Spencer. (002-004)

This striking comparison places Spencer — despite his Victorian eminence — on the far side of a philosophical divide, representing a mode of thought that Mumford finds less adequate to the utopian task. The passage is among the earliest in the book; it appears in Mumford's introductory chapter, where he establishes the lineage of "ideal states" from antiquity to the present. By juxtaposing Geddes and Spencer, Mumford signals that his own intellectual debts run toward the holistic, organismic tradition of Geddes rather than the mechanical evolutionism of Spencer.

In a later passage surveying the intellectual foundations of utopian planning, Mumford groups Spencer among "such latter day figures as Mill, Spencer, and Henry George" (011-005) who contributed to the "semi-scientific investigations" that began with Adam Smith and Malthus and provided the analytical framework for nineteenth-century social reform movements. The full passage traces a genealogy from Enlightenment political reform through nineteenth-century economic analysis:

> In the beginning, these plans were devoted to politics and criminal reform, like those of Rousseau, Beccaria, Bentham, Jefferson, Godwin, and the eighteenth century reformers generally; in the nineteenth century the main accent was economic, and a number of movements arose which could be traced back to the semi-scientific investigations of Adam Smith, Ricardo, Proudhon, Malthus, Marx, and perhaps half a dozen other thinkers of outstanding importance, among whom we should perhaps include such latter day figures as Mill, Spencer, and Henry George. (011-005)

## Spencer's Evolutionary Philosophy

Spencer coined the phrase "survival of the fittest" and applied evolutionary principles to society decades before Darwin published *On the Origin of Species*. His vast *System of Synthetic Philosophy* attempted to unify biology, psychology, sociology, and ethics under a single evolutionary law. While Mumford's utopian sensibility inclines toward cooperation over competition, Spencer's emphasis on organic development and the interdependence of social institutions resonates, at least formally, with the organismic utopianism Mumford endorses. The tension is instructive: Spencer's systematic ambition to trace "evolution" across every domain of human life parallels the utopian impulse toward comprehensive social design, even as his individualist conclusions diverge sharply from utopian communitarianism.

## Place in the Utopian Tradition

Spencer's relationship to the utopian tradition is complex. His individualism and laissez-faire politics place him at odds with the communitarian impulse of most utopian schemes, yet his evolutionary framework — the idea that societies develop through stages toward greater complexity and integration — provided a vocabulary that progressive and utopian thinkers would adapt to their own purposes. Mumford's ambivalent treatment of Spencer, acknowledging his intellectual stature while questioning his philosophical adequacy, captures this tension. By placing Spencer among the "semi-scientific investigations" that fed into utopian planning (011-005), Mumford implicitly recognizes that even thinkers hostile to utopianism contributed essential conceptual tools to the tradition's development.
