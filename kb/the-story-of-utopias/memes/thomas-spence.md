## Thomas Spence

Thomas Spence (1750-1814) was an English radical, bookseller, and utopian writer whose vision of a land-based commonwealth represents one of the few genuinely working-class utopias of the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries. A stationer by trade, Spence ran a shop in High Holborn, London, from which he published his pamphlets and agitated for fundamental social transformation (0381-757).

### Life and Context

Spence belonged to a generation of self-educated working men who "began to apply their wits to improving the conditions of the class to which they belonged" (0381-757). Alongside figures like William Cobbett and Francis Place, Spence devoted his spare hours to political philosophy and reform schemes. He wrote during the thick of the agitation for parliamentary reform, a movement that would define much of nineteenth-century British politics — from Chartism to parliamentary socialism. Unlike many of his contemporaries, Spence saw these political reforms as superficial: "Spence saw the futility of these superficial demands" (0381-771).

### Spensonia

Spence's utopian writings center on *A Description of Spensonia* (1795) and *The Constitution of Spensonia: A Country in Fairyland Situated Between Utopia and Oceana; Brought from Thence by Captain Swallow* (1801) (0381-759). He also published a series of pamphlets titled *Pig's Meat*, which carried his "rough philosophy" to a popular audience.

The utopia of Spensonia is built on the foundation of common land ownership. Mumford identifies Spensonia as one of "two, in particular, in which the common possession of land is the foundation of every other institution" (0381-753), the other being Hertzka's Freeland. Spence proposed a return to an environment that "had once been, in its fashion, complete" (0381-759), drawing on the English village tradition of common lands.

### The Guardian Angels

Spence's constitution rested on two institutional safeguards, which Mumford calls his "guardian angels" — Voting by Ballot and the Universal Use of Arms (0381-769). These mechanisms were intended to preserve the equality of the commonwealth against those who would undermine it. Mumford notes that these angels "look less formidable and potent in the twentieth century than they did in the first decade of the nineteenth, when the first had still to be tried, and when the second was not complicated by the invention of machine guns and poison gases" (0381-769).

### Philosophy

At the foundation of Spence's utopian thought lies a conviction he shares with Plato and "all the other genuine utopians": that, in Thoreau's words, "less is accomplished by the thousands who are hacking at the branches of evil than by one who is striking at the root" (0381-771). This radical approach distinguishes Spence from the more incremental reformers of his era and aligns him with the deeper utopian tradition.

### Legacy

Mumford describes a "rough, homespun quality" in Spence's utopia, suggesting that one needs to visit "the English villages of the New Forest or the Chiltern Hills, where some of the common lands have been kept, to see what a rural utopia would be like if it could keep itself free from invaders" (0381-769). Spence is listed in Mumford's bibliography as one of the utopian writers deserving of restoration to their proper place, alongside Fourier (0381-1549). Mumford explicitly groups Spence with Fourier as one of the "more distinguished" nineteenth-century utopias that genuinely "dream of a renovated world" rather than merely adding inventions to the present one (0381-834).
