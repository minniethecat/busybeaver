# Acquisitive Society

The **Acquisitive Society** is Lewis Mumford's term for the economic and social order produced by the Country House idolum — a civilization organized around passive consumption and the accumulation of goods rather than creative participation in community life.

## Origin

Mumford directly attributes the Acquisitive Society to the Country House cultural pattern: "The Country House standards of consumption are responsible for our Acquisitive Society" (010-021). Because the Country House dissociated its inhabitants from the productive and creative life of the broader community, consumption became the primary mode of engagement with the world.

## Mechanism

The mechanism is one of compensatory accumulation. Each Country House "was forced to obtain for its limited circle all the elements that were necessary to the good life in a whole community such as Plato described" (010-030). What had once been distributed across an entire community — art, music, learning, craftsmanship, recreation — now had to be purchased and imported by each isolated household.

This pattern, Mumford insists, is not limited to grand estates. Whether "an estate on Long Island or a cottage in Montclair," the underlying logic is the same (010-037). Technologies of modern convenience have "only served to increase this self-sufficiency" and "deepened the elements of acquisitiveness and passive, uncreative, mechanical enjoyment" (010-037).

## Cultural Consequences

The shift from participation to acquisition transformed the meaning of culture itself. "Culture came to mean not a participation in the creative activities of one's own community, but the acquisition of the products of other communities" (010-030). This redefinition severed the connection between cultural life and the everyday experience of ordinary people, turning culture into a commodity.

Popular institutions "were deprived of their contacts with the general world of culture, and languished away; or they were transformed, as the public schools of England were transformed into restricted upper class institutions" (010-030).

## The Production-Consumption Cycle

The Acquisitive Society creates a self-reinforcing cycle with Coketown. The Country House demands goods, Coketown produces them, and the resulting abundance reinforces the pattern of passive consumption. "The rage and fury of Coketown's production has to be balanced off by an equal rage and fury of consumption — continence would be fatal" (010-054).
