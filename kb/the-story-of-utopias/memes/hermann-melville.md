# Herman Melville

## Introduction

Herman Melville (1819–1891) was an American novelist, short story writer, and poet, best known for *Moby-Dick* (1851). In *The Story of Utopias*, [[lewis-mumford|Lewis Mumford]] draws on Melville's early travel narrative *Typee* (1846) to illustrate a crucial point about the conditions under which the utopian imagination arises — or does not.

## Melville's Marquesans in Mumford's Argument

Mumford invokes Melville in the opening chapter to make a foundational distinction between communities that need utopias and those that do not. Writing of the [[marquesas|Marquesas Islands]], Mumford observes:

> "The savages of the Marquesas whom Hermann Melville described seem to have had such a jolly and complete adjustment to their environment that, except for the raids of hostile tribes — and this turned out to be chiefly sport which only whetted their appetites for the feast that followed — everything needed for a good life at the South Sea level could be obtained by direct attack. The Marquesans had no need to dream of a happier existence; they had only to grab it." (001-014)

This passage serves as the negative case in Mumford's argument about the nature of utopian thought. If utopia is a response to incompleteness — a "reconstructed environment" that compensates for the inadequacies of the given world — then a community that perceives no gap between desire and reality generates no utopia. The Marquesans, as Melville portrayed them, lived in what Mumford calls "a jolly and complete adjustment," a state of satisfied wholeness that obviates the need for imaginative reconstruction.

## The Utopian Implications

Melville's Marquesas thus function as a kind of natural control experiment in Mumford's theory: they demonstrate that the utopian impulse is not a universal constant of human nature but a contingent response to specific conditions — namely, the experience of social disintegration, frustrated desire, or a perceived gap between the world as it is and the world as it should be. This insight underlies Mumford's central distinction between the [[utopia-of-escape|utopia of escape]] (the compensatory fantasy of those who cannot remake their world) and the [[utopia-of-reconstruction|utopia of reconstruction]] (the practical vision of those who can and must).

## Melville's Place in the Utopian Tradition

Though Melville himself was not a utopian writer, his ethnographic portrayal of the Marquesas in *Typee* enters Mumford's argument as a kind of limit-case — the community at the far end of the spectrum from the restless, imaginative utopian builder. Where figures like [[plato|Plato]], [[sir-thomas-more|Thomas More]], and [[edward-bellamy|Edward Bellamy]] represent the drive to reconstruct the social environment, Melville's Marquesans represent what life might look like when reconstruction is unnecessary — a possibility that Mumford presents not as an ideal to be imitated but as an observation that clarifies, by contrast, the nature of the utopian enterprise itself.
