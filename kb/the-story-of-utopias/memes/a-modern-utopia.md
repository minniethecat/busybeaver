## Introduction

*A Modern Utopia* is a utopian novel by [[h-g-wells|H.G. Wells]], published in 1905. Unlike earlier utopian fictions that accessed the ideal world through shipwrecks or magical sleep, Wells enters his utopia "by hypothesis; that is, without any other subterfuge than an act of the imagination" (009-023). The novel describes an alternate Earth — located beyond Sirius — identical to ours in geography but different in social organization, where a global world-state is governed by a voluntary ascetic order, the Samurai.

[[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] treats *A Modern Utopia* as the culminating work of the utopian tradition up to his time. It is, he writes, "the quintessential utopia, for it is written with a free and critical gesture, and with a succinct familiarity towards the more important books that came before it" (009-022). Wells's novel marks a synthesis: it brings together the critical intelligence missing from Bellamy and the vivid fantasy absent from the administrative utopias.

## Summary of the Work

The novel's frame is reflexive: Wells imagines a "modern man, a little thickset and protuberant, seated at a desk and brooding over the possibilities of man's future," whose vision gradually solidifies into narrative (009-023). This narrator, accompanied by a sentimental botanist perpetually distracted by romantic complications, explores a utopian world first glimpsed in an Alpine pass.

Wells's utopia is a planetary state — "a single civilization whose net of monorails and posts, whose identification bureaus, whose rules of law and order are the same in England as in Switzerland" (009-025). It embraces modern machinery without apology: "All that machinery has to offer has been accepted and humanized: there is a cleanliness, an absence of squalor and confusion, in this world-community, which indicates that utopia has not been purchased by evasion" (009-025).

The society is structured around four classes — the kinetic, the poietic, the base, and the dull (009-027) — but the central institution is the Samurai, a voluntary order of ascetic guardians who serve as the society's ruling class. These Samurai "are as necessary to the social organization of A Modern Utopia as the research laboratories, which are provided by charter with each factory, are necessary to its industrial organization" (009-028). They represent Wells's modernized version of [[plato|Plato]]'s Guardians.

## Mumford's Assessment

[[lewis-mumford|Mumford]]'s appraisal of *A Modern Utopia* is predominantly favorable, positioning it as a breakthrough after the mechanistic utopias of the nineteenth century:

> A Modern Utopia brings together, compares, and criticizes important points that all the other utopias have raised; and it does all this with a deftness and a turn of humor that speaks for Mr. Wells at his best. Above all, A Modern Utopia strikes a new note, the note of reality, the note of the daily world from which we endeavor in vain to escape. (009-030)

[[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] particularly values the book's honesty about human nature. Unlike earlier utopias that assumed a transformed populace, Wells's world "has the same oceans and continents, the same rivers and minor landmasses, the same animals and plants; yes, even the same people, so that each one of us has his utopian counterpart" (009-024). There is "a minimum of these assumptions in A Modern Utopia" — no miraculous conversion of "the mean sensual man" (009-030).

In the larger architecture of [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]]'s argument, Wells's novel is situated alongside [[news-from-nowhere|William Morris's *News from Nowhere*]] and [[a-crystal-age|W.H. Hudson's *A Crystal Age*]] as a return to classic utopian models: "In [[news-from-nowhere|News from Nowhere]] the city of workers, such as Andreae dreamed of, comes again into being; and in A Modern Utopia, with its order of Samurai, we are ruled once more by a highly disciplined class of [[plato|Plato]]nic guardians" (009-003). Each of these three works recovers, without conscious imitation, an element of the utopian tradition that the nineteenth-century reconstructors had lost.

## Place in the Utopian Tradition

*A Modern Utopia* represents a pivotal moment in utopian literature: the point at which the genre shed its nineteenth-century obsession with machinery and reconnected with the classical tradition of the ideal commonwealth. Wells's Samurai are explicit descendants of [[plato|Plato]]'s Guardians, his world-state extends [[sir-thomas-more|More]]'s island to planetary scale, and his critical engagement with predecessors like Bellamy and [[william-morris|Morris]] makes the novel a self-conscious summation of the tradition.

For [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]], the book's greatest contribution is its realism — its insistence that utopia must reckon with the same people, the same passions, and the same problems as the actual world. This is the note that would inform [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]]'s own [[utopia-of-reconstruction|reconstructive utopia]]nism in his later work.
