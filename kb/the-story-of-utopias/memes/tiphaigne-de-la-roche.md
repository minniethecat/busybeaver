## Introduction

Charles-François Tiphaigne de la Roche (1722-1774) was a French physician and writer of speculative fiction during the Enlightenment. His novel *Giphantia* (1760) — the title an anagram of his name — describes a voyage to an imaginary land and contains remarkably prescient ideas about technology and society. Mumford includes Tiphaigne in his survey of nineteenth-century utopian literature, treating *Giphantia* as part of the French utopian tradition that extends from Rabelais through Fourier to Cabet.

## *Giphantia* and Its Significance

*Giphantia* belongs to the tradition of imaginary voyage literature that flourished in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries. The narrator travels to a distant land where he encounters advanced technologies and social arrangements that reflect critically on European society. Among Tiphaigne's prescient inventions is a description of what appears to anticipate photography — a method of fixing images on a specially prepared canvas — decades before the actual invention of the daguerreotype.

Mumford's engagement with Tiphaigne is brief but appreciative. Within the context of *The Story of Utopias*, *Giphantia* represents the period between the Renaissance utopias (More, Andreae, Campanella) and the great outpouring of nineteenth-century utopian fiction — a transitional work that keeps the genre alive during the Enlightenment.

## The Parable of Sophia

Mumford draws particular attention to a parable embedded within *Giphantia*, using it to diagnose the temper of the eighteenth century. In the tale, Sophia — the incarnation of Wisdom — rejects the offers of a spendthrift, a merchant, a soldier, and a student, and instead "accepts the suit of a diffident fellow who had retired in solitude to the country, to spend his days like a cultivated gentleman" (005-023).

For Mumford, this parable crystallizes the dominant eighteenth-century ideal: the retreat from society into cultivated solitude. He connects it to "the way in which Montaigne spent his declining years" and to Voltaire's retreat to Ferney, observing that "the ideal of Robinson Crusoe — a cultivated Robinson Crusoe, surrounded with books and beyond the reach of any king and court — colored the deepest aspirations of this period" (005-023). Rousseau's critique of the arts and sciences and Chateaubriand's search for the noble savage represent variations on this same theme.

Mumford's conclusion is pointed: "In a society that was already painfully artificial and 'arranged' the institutes of Lycurgus and Utopus must have seemed as repressive as those of Louis XIV. So almost two centuries pass before we find any fresh regions to explore in Utopia" (005-023). Tiphaigne's *Giphantia*, in this analysis, marks both the persistence and the transformation of the utopian impulse during a period when systematic social reconstruction had fallen out of favor.

## Mumford's Assessment

In his bibliographic appendix, Mumford characterizes *Giphantia* as "a pithy little satire which I include in this list of utopias out of the courtesy that is due to good literature" (1526-029). The description is characteristically droll — Mumford clearly valued the work's literary quality even while acknowledging its limited scope as a program for social reconstruction. This places *Giphantia* firmly in the tradition of [[utopia-of-escape]] rather than [[reconstruction-utopia]], a distinction central to Mumford's classificatory scheme.
