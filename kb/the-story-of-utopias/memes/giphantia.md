# Giphantia

*Giphantia: or, a View of What Has Passed, What Is Now Passing, and What Will Pass, in the World* (1760) is a satirical work by the French writer [[tiphaigne-de-la-roche|Tiphaigne de la Roche]] (1722-1774). [[lewis-mumford|Lewis Mumford]] includes it in his survey "out of the courtesy that is due to good literature" (bibliography), treating it not as a full utopia in the classic sense but as a revealing symptom of what happened to the utopian imagination in the eighteenth century.

## Historical Context

[[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] identifies a striking gap in the utopian tradition "between the seventeenth century and the nineteenth" (006-002). After the burst of Renaissance utopianism that produced [[utopia-more|More]], [[christianopolis|Andreae]], [[city-of-the-sun|Campanella]], and [[new-atlantis|Bacon]], nearly two centuries pass without a major addition to the utopian canon. *Giphantia* is the clue to understanding why.

The eighteenth century, [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] argues, turned away from the reconstruction of institutions and toward the cultivation of the individual. The ideal was no longer the just city but the wise man — the gentleman-scholar who had retired from public life to his library and garden:

> "One remembers the way in which Montaigne spent his declining years; one remembers Voltaire; and one sees how deeply the ideal of Robinson Crusoe — a cultivated Robinson Crusoe, surrounded with books and beyond the reach of any king and court — colored the deepest aspirations of this period." (006-003)

In a society that was "already painfully artificial and 'arranged,'" [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] observes, "the institutes of Lycurgus and Utopus must have seemed as repressive as those of Louis XIV" (006-003). The [[will-to-utopia|utopian impulse]] did not disappear — it turned inward.

## Summary of the Work

*Giphantia* is structured around a parable: Sophia, the incarnation of Wisdom, is courted by a spendthrift, a merchant, a soldier, and a student. She rejects them all and instead accepts the suit of "a diffident fellow who had retired in solitude to the country, to spend his days like a cultivated gentleman" (006-003). The choice is emblematic: Wisdom does not dwell in courts, markets, battlefields, or universities — but in the reflective solitude of the private man.

The work is subtitled "a View of What Has Passed, What Is Now Passing, and What Will Pass," suggesting a survey of past, present, and future — but [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]]'s treatment focuses entirely on the parable, as if the rest of the work is less important than what the parable reveals about its age.

## Mumford's Assessment

[[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] does not treat *Giphantia* as a major utopia — it receives only a paragraph of discussion in the main text and a brief, affectionate mention in the bibliography as a "pithy little satire." Its significance is diagnostic rather than substantive. It marks the moment when the utopian tradition, having produced its great Renaissance statements, temporarily exhausted its capacity to imagine reconstructed communities.

The figures [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] invokes to frame this moment — Montaigne, [[voltaire|Voltaire]], [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]], Chateaubriand — define the eighteenth century's characteristic posture: skeptical of institutions, inward-turning, seeking the [[good-life|good life]] in private cultivation rather than public reconstruction. [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]], "writing about the corrupting influence of the arts and sciences, and Chateaubriand, seeking the noble savage in the American wilderness and finding him in his own bosom — these men struck the dominating note of the eighteenth century" (006-003).

## Place in the Utopian Tradition

*Giphantia* stands at the low-water mark of the institutional utopian tradition — the point where the utopia of reconstruction gave way to the utopia of escape. [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]]'s inclusion of it serves a structural purpose in his narrative: it marks the gap that the Industrial Revolution would soon fill with a new wave of systematic [[utopian-method|utopian thinking]], from [[charles-fourier|Fourier]] to [[looking-backward|Bellamy]]. The eighteenth century's retreat into private wisdom was, in [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]]'s account, a necessary interlude — a period of individual cultivation that prepared the ground for the next great cycle of institutional imagination. Tiphaigne's modest parable is its epitaph.
