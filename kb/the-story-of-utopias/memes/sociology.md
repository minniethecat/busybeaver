# Sociology

Sociology is the systematic study of society, social relationships, and institutions. In *The Story of Utopias*, Lewis Mumford engages with sociology both as a necessary tool for understanding community life and as a discipline he finds frequently inadequate to the task. Mumford's treatment of sociology reflects his broader argument that specialized knowledge, when disconnected from a synthetic vision of the good life, becomes an intellectual dead end.

## Mumford's Engagement

Mumford acknowledges his debt to sociological thinkers throughout the book. In his acknowledgments, he notes being "indebted for many pertinent suggestions" to Victor Branford and Alexander Farquharson of the Sociological Society of Great Britain (P02-002). He locates the roots of modern sociology in the intellectual ferment of the early modern period, tracing the social sciences to Francis Bacon's *Novum Organum* and crediting Quesnay and Montesquieu with giving the movement "real headway" in the eighteenth century (P12-013).

Mumford's engagement with sociology is not merely academic; it is personal and institutional. Through figures like Branford and Patrick Geddes, he was connected to the Regional Survey movement and the Outlook Tower in Edinburgh — described as "the world's first sociological laboratory" (P12-025). This tradition of applied, place-based sociology provided Mumford with a model for what the discipline could become: a grounded inquiry into the actual conditions of community life.

## Critique of Sociology

Despite his engagement with the sociological tradition, Mumford is sharply critical of the state of the discipline in his time. His critique operates on several levels.

### The Problem of Method

Mumford argues that "a large part of what is called science in Economics, Politics, and Sociology is only disguised literature — work in which the jargon of science is accepted as a substitute for the scientific method" (P12-013). He observes that "among the economists and sociologists there has been a persistent dribble of discussion as to whether or not their subjects entitled them to the august designation of scientists" (P12-013). For Mumford, the pretense of scientific rigor masks an intellectual failure to engage with the complexity of human social life.

### Institutional Marginalization

Mumford notes the low status of sociology within the scientific establishment, observing that the British Association permitted sociology "to enter as a subclassification of Anthropology" (P12-014). For Mumford, this marginalization reflects a deeper problem: "the nearer the investigator gets to man, the more easily he is overwhelmed with the complexity of his subject; and the more tempted he is to adopt the swift and easy partisan methods of the novelist, the poet, the prophet" (P12-014).

### Fragmentation and Intellectual Weakness

Mumford goes further, diagnosing "the weakness of contemporary sociological thought, with its diabetic flatulence of special sociologies, and its lack of any general agreement as to the field which is to be cultivated" (P12-023). The proliferation of sub-disciplines — what he calls "special sociologies" — signals not vitality but intellectual disintegration. The discipline has, in Mumford's view, lost sight of its original synthetic purpose.

## The Regional Survey Alternative

Against the fragmentation of academic sociology, Mumford champions the Regional Survey movement associated with Patrick Geddes and Victor Branford. The Regional Survey aimed "to take a geographic region and explore it in every aspect," attempting "a local synthesis of all the specialist 'knowledges'" (P12-026). Unlike the abstract generalities of academic sociology, "in looking at the community through the Regional Survey, the investigator is dealing with a real thing and not with an arbitrary idolum" (P12-028).

Mumford presents the Regional Survey as a bridge between specialized knowledge and practical community action: "the knowledge embodied in the Regional Survey has a coherence and pithiness which no isolated study of science can possibly possess" (P12-029). It is "the bridge by which the specialist whose face is turned towards the library and the laboratory, and the active worker in the field, whose face is turned towards the city and the country-side, can come into communication and co-operate" (P12-030).

## The French School and Collective Representations

Mumford also engages with the French sociological tradition of Émile Durkheim. He notes that "the importance of our social myths and our collective representations has been noted by a whole school of French sociologists who follow Émile Durkheim; and the dynamic force of ideas has been treated by Alfred Fouillée" (P13-004). However, Mumford takes issue with the Durkheimian tendency to limit "collective representations" to "savage or ignorant groups," arguing that they are "an important part of every civilized person's luggage" (P10-007). This critique reflects Mumford's insistence that the sociological analysis of collective mental frameworks — what he calls *idola* — applies as much to modern civilization as to pre-modern societies.

## Role in Utopian Thought

For Mumford, genuine sociology — what he calls "sociological insight into the causes and conditions of war and peace" — is "a needed corrective to the crudities of applied physical science," without which "the mere increase of scientific knowledge... may be highly inimical to the practice of the good life in the community" (P12-021). He sees the sociologist's task as surveying "existing conditions" to discover "facts and relations which remain to be described" (P12-023).

At its best, sociology for Mumford converges with the utopian project itself: the disciplined study of how communities actually live, as the necessary groundwork for imagining how they might live better. The Regional Survey tradition, with its commitment to synthetic understanding of particular places, represents "the sort of community-study which alone can supply the facts for remodeling the utopian method" (P12-071).
