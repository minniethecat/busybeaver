# William Morris

**William Morris** (1834-1896) was an English craftsman, designer, poet, and socialist whose utopian romance *News from Nowhere* (1890) presents a vision of a post-industrial England restored to beauty, craftsmanship, and communal life. Mumford treats Morris's work as one of the "utopias of escape" in the nineteenth century that nonetheless contains a powerful critique of industrial civilization.

## News from Nowhere

Mumford describes Morris as "a craftsman who knew what the English town was like before it had been blighted by industrialism; and with both of these men [Morris and W. H. Hudson] we feel close to the essential life of man and the essential occupations" (009-003). In *News from Nowhere*, "the city of workers, such as Andreae dreamed of, comes again into being" (009-003).

## Critique of Machinery

Morris's most provocative contribution is his questioning of technological necessity. Mumford notes that Morris "conceived that men in the future might discard many complicated machines because they could live more happily, aye, work more happily without them" (010-059). While Mumford does not fully endorse this anti-industrial position, he treats it as a serious challenge: "Whether indeed a good part of modern organization and machinery could be scrapped is perhaps a debatable question: but the possibility of scrapping it is at least conceivable once we become more interested in the actual result of industrialism upon the life and happiness of the people" (010-059).

## Place in the Utopian Tradition

Morris represents the aesthetic and craft-oriented pole of utopian thought. Like Ruskin before him, he insisted that the quality of daily work and the beauty of the material environment are not luxuries but essential components of the good life. His vision serves as a counterweight to the purely technological utopias of writers like Bellamy and Wells.
