Jean-Jacques Rousseau (1712-1778) was a Genevan philosopher, writer, and composer whose ideas about human nature, the corrupting influence of civilization, and the social contract profoundly shaped the intellectual landscape of the eighteenth century. In *The Story of Utopias*, Mumford positions Rousseau as a figure who "struck the dominating note of the eighteenth century" (656), emblematic of a period when utopian thought turned away from comprehensive institutional blueprints toward a more introspective critique of society itself.

## Life and Works

Rousseau was born in Geneva and spent much of his life in France, where he became a central figure of the Enlightenment — and one of its most formidable critics. His major works include *Discourse on the Arts and Sciences* (1750), *Discourse on Inequality* (1755), *The Social Contract* (1762), and *Émile* (1762). His writing on education, politics, and the arts placed him in direct opposition to the rationalist optimism of many of his contemporaries, arguing instead that the progress of civilization had corrupted rather than improved human beings.

## The Critique of Civilization

Mumford identifies Rousseau's critique of the arts and sciences as central to the mood of the eighteenth century. Writing about "the corrupting influence of the arts and sciences" (656), Rousseau rejected the prevailing Enlightenment assumption that intellectual progress necessarily led to moral improvement. Mumford places him alongside Chateaubriand, who "sought the noble savage in the American wilderness and found him in his own bosom" (656). Together, they articulated a deep dissatisfaction with an "already painfully artificial and 'arranged'" (656) society — a mood that, in Mumford's account, explains why the comprehensive utopian programs of the Renaissance gave way to a long hiatus in the utopian tradition.

## Human Nature and Institutions

Mumford groups Rousseau with Bentham, Godwin, Fourier, and Owen as thinkers who shared "an underlying consensus in their belief in human nature" (1268). For Rousseau and those who followed him, human beings were not intrinsically corrupt; rather:

> They looked upon human institutions as altogether external to men; these were so many straitjackets that cunning rulers had thrown over the community to make sane and kindly people behave like madmen; and they could conceive of changing the institutions without changing the habits and redirecting the impulses of the people by whom and for whom they had been created.
> (1268)

This conviction — that institutional reform alone could liberate an essentially good human nature — marks Rousseau as a foundational figure in what Mumford calls the nineteenth century's "opposite kind of error and absurdity" (1268): the belief that institutional change, by itself, would be sufficient to transform society.

## Place in the Utopian Tradition

Mumford situates Rousseau within a broader school of philosophers who "followed on the heels of the utopians" and "devoted themselves to preparing fairly minute plans and specifications for the social order" (1234). In the eighteenth century, these plans were primarily concerned with "politics and criminal reform" (1234), and Rousseau stands alongside Beccaria, Bentham, Jefferson, and Godwin as a representative of this reformist impulse.

Though Rousseau did not write a full utopia in the classical sense — he produced no complete picture of an ideal commonwealth — his ideas provided the philosophical foundation upon which later utopian thinkers would build. His insistence that society rather than human nature was the source of corruption became a defining assumption of nineteenth-century utopian socialism.

## Mumford's Assessment

Mumford treats Rousseau with historical respect but philosophical distance. He recognizes Rousseau's centrality to the eighteenth-century turn away from Renaissance-style institutional utopianism, but he also identifies the limitation of Rousseau's approach: the assumption that institutional change could proceed without "changing the habits and redirecting the impulses of the people" (1268). This critique is consistent with Mumford's broader argument that genuine eutopia requires the simultaneous reconstruction of ideas (*idola*), institutions, and human purposes — a project that goes deeper than either institutional reform or the liberation of natural goodness alone.
