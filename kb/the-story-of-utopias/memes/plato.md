# Plato

**Plato** (c. 428–348 BCE) is the ancient Greek philosopher whose *[[republic-plato|Republic]]* [[lewis-mumford|Lewis Mumford]] treats as the founding work of the Western utopian tradition. Throughout *[[the-story-of-utopias|The Story of Utopias]]*, Plato appears as both the origin point and recurring standard against which all subsequent utopian thinkers — from [[sir-thomas-more|Sir Thomas More]] to [[ebenezer-howard|Ebenezer Howard]] — are measured.

## The Republic

Mumford locates the *Republic* in its historical moment: it "dates from the period of social disintegration which followed the Peloponnesian War; and some of its mordant courage is probably derived from the hopelessness of conditions that came under Plato's eye" (001-004). This historical grounding is significant — for Mumford, authentic utopian thinking always arises from engagement with real conditions, not from abstract speculation.

> "Plato's glorious dream of an organic community, the image of the just man made perfect" (006-037).

The *Republic* is a vision of the whole community — not merely a political or economic blueprint but "a complete picture of the good life, a picture so vivid that it took possession of men's minds and never quite let go" (012-068).

## Key Contributions

Mumford identifies several enduring Platonic contributions to the utopian tradition:

| Contribution | Core Idea | Enduring Value |
|-------------|-----------|----------------|
| Birth and education | Character formation through systematic training | "the enormous importance of birth and education ... must play in the good community" (012-068) |
| Guardian class | Ruling class holding property in common, freed from family ties | Anticipates the tension between private interest and public duty |
| Organic community | Vision of society as an integrated whole, not competing parts | The "common pattern" that the [[good-life|good life]] requires |

## The Problem of Transition

Mumford identifies a critical limitation in Plato's framework:

> "Plato's Republic, for example, was a fairly attractive place; but one wonders in what Greek city in the Fourth Century BC the transition could have taken place" (012-062).

This problem — how to move from the actual world to the ideal one — was "overlooked" by Plato and remains "the critical problem for the eutopian" (012-062). Later figures in the tradition, particularly [[sir-thomas-more|More]] and the builders of the [[garden-city-organization|Garden City movement]], would grapple with this same challenge: how does one cross from the [[idolum]] of the existing world into a reconstructed one?

## Influence

Mumford traces Plato's influence through the entire utopian tradition:

- The Renaissance utopians returned to Plato through the recovery of Greek learning. More's Raphael Hythloday "through his conquest of Greek ... has come into possession of that new learning which stems back to Plato" (003-006).
- The nineteenth-century utopians, though focusing on industrial means rather than human ends, still echo Platonic structures — Wells's Samurai in *A Modern Utopia* are "a highly disciplined class of Platonic guardians" (009-003).
- Plato's vision of education as character formation resonates in Mumford's own emphasis on the [[regional-survey]] as a tool for reconstructing the [[idolum]].

## Mumford's Assessment

Mumford treats Plato with deep respect but not uncritical reverence. The *Republic* is the necessary starting point, but the utopian tradition must build upon and go beyond it. The task that Plato began — "what are men to do with their knowledge and power?" — remains the fundamental question, more urgent in the industrial age than ever before (005-015).
