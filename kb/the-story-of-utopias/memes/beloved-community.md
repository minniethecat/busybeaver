## Definition

The **beloved community** is Mumford's term for the ideal of a fully integrated, organic human community—a society in which the good life is realized not for isolated individuals or privileged classes but for the whole. The phrase recurs at critical junctures in *The Story of Utopias*, marking the deepest aspiration that animates the utopian tradition.

The concept has theological roots—it appears in the writings of the American philosopher [[josiah-royce]] and later in the work of [[martin-luther-king-jr]]—but Mumford deploys it in a distinctly secular and historical sense. For him, the beloved community is the seed that "Plato had sought to implant in men's minds" and that "springs up again, after a fallow period of almost two thousand years" with the rediscovery of [[greek-philosophy|Greek thought]] and the opening of the New World (002-397).

## The Family as Obstacle

Mumford opens his discussion of the beloved community with a provocative claim: "the good, contrary to the proverb, is frequently the enemy of the better; and the little utopia of the family is the enemy—indeed the principal enemy—of the beloved community" (002-322).

Each home, each small circle of relatives and friends, "tends to be a miniature utopia; there is a limited community of goods, a tendency to adjust one's actions to the welfare of the little whole, and a habit of banding together against the world at large" (002-322). But this very intimacy becomes an obstacle to wider solidarity. Mumford cites [[john-galsworthy]]'s play *Strife*, in which a trade union leader's "power to act firmly in behalf of his group is sapped by the demands made by family ties" (002-322).

This insight links Mumford to a radical tradition within utopian thought. "Jesus commanded his followers to leave their families and abandon their worldly goods; and [[plato]], in order to preserve his ideal commonwealth, laid down a similar rule. For those who as guardians were to apply the science of government to public affairs, a private life, private duties, private interests, were all to be left behind" (002-322). The beloved community demands a loyalty that transcends the domestic circle.

## The Beloved Community and Eutopia

In Mumford's concluding chapter, the beloved community becomes the goal toward which the entire analysis has been moving. The [[utopian-method]]—the deliberate projection of ideal commonwealths—is justified precisely because it keeps alive the aspiration toward the beloved community:

> The chief use of the classic utopias that we have surveyed is to suggest that the same methods which are used by the utopian thinkers to project an ideal community on paper may be employed, in a practical way, to develop a better community on earth. (012-1477)

He issues a warning drawn from the historical record: "From the bitter frustration of Fourier, Cabet, Hertzka, and even [[john-ruskin]] those who are in search of the beloved community may well take a warning" (012-1477). The error of these utopian practitioners was the assumption "that the dreams and projects of any single man might be realized in society at large" (012-1477). The beloved community cannot be imposed by a solitary genius; it must be grown through the shared cultivation of [[eutopia|eutopian]] idola.

## The Artist and the False Beloved Community

Mumford offers a cautionary note about the power of art to sanctify the existing order under the guise of the beloved community. Writing of American popular culture, he observes that "most of these poor wretches have never been educated in the humanist sense to any degree—since they know no other environment than New York or Los Angeles or Gopher Prairie, since they are acquainted with the achievements of no other age than their own, they have devoted themselves wholeheartedly to idealizing a great many of the things that are crude or ugly or stupid in their beloved community" (011-1447).

The result is a perversion of the concept: the "beloved community" becomes whatever community one happens to belong to, its flaws transfigured by sentiment rather than transformed by critical vision. The genuine beloved community, by contrast, requires the critical distance that the utopian tradition provides—the capacity to measure one's actual community against an ideal standard.
