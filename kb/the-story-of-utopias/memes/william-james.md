# William James

## Introduction

William James (1842-1910) was an American philosopher and psychologist, a founder of pragmatism and of the first American psychology laboratory. In *The Story of Utopias*, Lewis Mumford references James twice — once for his theory of emotional expression, and once for his influential concept of "the moral equivalent of war," which Mumford identifies as an essential principle in the construction of utopian communities.

|--------|-------------|------------------------------|

## James in Mumford's Account

Mumford's first reference to James appears in his discussion of Plato's utopian psychology. Defending Plato's apparently repressive doctrine — that citizens should not passively experience emotions without translating them into action — Mumford enlists James as an unexpected ally:

> William James used to teach that no one should passively experience an emotion at a concert or a play without trying to express that emotion actively as soon as he could make the opportunity. (002-061)

This parallel between Platonic psychology and Jamesian pragmatism underscores Mumford's argument that utopian thought, far from being divorced from reality, is deeply engaged with the practical dynamics of human feeling. Mumford introduces the reference while acknowledging that Plato's restrictions on art and music "sounds foreign" to modern readers, yet he finds "more than a grain of sense in it" — a judgment he supports by appealing to James's psychological authority (002-061).

The second and more consequential reference concerns the organization of utopian life. Describing one Fourierist community, Mumford notes "perhaps one of the most remarkable characteristics of this utopia is its utilization of a moral equivalent for war, long before Professor William James invented the phrase" (006-022). The context is Fourier's vision of "industrial armies" — not destructive but constructive:

> There is a fine passage in which Fourier pictures an industrial army of golden youths and maidens, "instead of devastating thirty provinces in a campaign, these armies will have spanned thirty rivers with bridges, re-wooded thirty barren mountains, dug thirty trenches for irrigation, and drained thirty marshes." It is for lack of such industrial armies, says Fourier, that civilization is unable to produce anything great. (006-022)

For Mumford, this demonstrates that the best utopian thinking anticipated insights that formal philosophy would only later articulate.

## The Moral Equivalent of War

James's "moral equivalent of war" envisions conscripting young people into a peacetime army tasked with public works, environmental restoration, and social service — harnessing the "strenuous mood" that war evokes for purposes of collective betterment. Mumford's recognition of this concept as an operative principle in utopian communities reveals his conviction that the best utopian thought does not deny human aggressiveness but finds creative outlets for it. Fourier's "industrial armies," in this reading, are not a naive evasion of human conflict but a sophisticated channeling of martial energies into productive ends — precisely what James would later theorize.

## Place in the Utopian Tradition

William James was not a utopian writer himself, but his pragmatist philosophy — with its emphasis on the real-world consequences of ideas — supplied tools that utopian thinkers have employed. His "moral equivalent of war" has entered the lexicon of social reform as a model for mobilizing collective energy toward shared goals, and Mumford's deployment of the concept in *The Story of Utopias* confirms its relevance to the utopian imagination. The convergence between James's psychology of active emotional expression and Plato's ancient insight — that emotions demand outward realization — exemplifies the deep continuity Mumford traces across the utopian tradition.
