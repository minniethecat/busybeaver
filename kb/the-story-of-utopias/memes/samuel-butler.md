## Samuel Butler

Samuel Butler (1835-1902) was an English novelist, essayist, and critic whose satirical utopia *Erewhon* (1872) stands as one of the most original critiques of Victorian industrial society. Mumford invokes Butler at two critical junctures in *The Story of Utopias*, both times as a diagnostician of the deeper spiritual costs of machine civilization.

### The Perfect Gentleman

In his analysis of the Country House ideal, Mumford references Butler alongside John Ruskin in arguing that "the perfect gentleman was a finer product than the perfect peasant or artisan: he is a finer product because he is essentially more alive" (0381-1093). This defense of the contemplative, aesthetic values embodied in the Country House tradition acknowledges a partial truth: that the capacity for appreciation and the cultivation of sensibility represent genuine human achievements, not mere class privilege.

### Erewhon and the Machine

*Erewhon* (an anagram of "Nowhere") depicts a society that has made the conscious decision to destroy all machinery, fearing that machines would evolve to dominate their human creators. Mumford draws on Butler's central insight when describing life in Coketown, the industrial city-idolum: "work in Coketown, as Samuel Butler fearfully predicted in *Erewhon*, is in the main simply attendance upon machinery" (0381-1129). This phrase distills Butler's prescient warning — that industrialization, far from liberating human beings, would reduce them to mere tenders of the mechanical apparatus they had created.

### The Human Cost of Industrialism

Butler's critique in *Erewhon* operates through inversion: by presenting a society that fears and bans machines, he forces readers to confront their own unthinking embrace of industrial technology. The "Book of the Machines" section of *Erewhon* develops a sustained argument that consciousness might arise in machines just as it has in animals, and that humanity is unwittingly creating its own successors. Mumford's invocation of Butler within the Coketown chapter connects this fictional warning to the actual conditions of industrial capitalism, where the machine, rather than serving human purposes, increasingly dictates the rhythm and meaning of life.

### Literary Significance

Unlike most utopian writers in Mumford's survey, Butler approached the ideal commonwealth through the lens of satire rather than earnest prescription. *Erewhon* belongs to a tradition of literary utopianism that includes Jonathan Swift and, later, Aldous Huxley, where the imagined society serves primarily as a critical mirror held up to the author's own world. Butler's method is closer to the utopia of escape than the utopia of reconstruction, yet its critical power arguably exceeds that of many more programmatic utopian schemes.
