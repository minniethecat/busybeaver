Edward Bellamy (1850-1898) was an American author and social reformer whose utopian romance *Looking Backward: 2000–1887* (1888) sold over a million copies and inspired a短暂 but intense Nationalist political movement in the United States. Mumford treats Bellamy as the representative figure of the nineteenth-century "utopia of reconstruction" — a tradition that placed industrial reorganization at the center of social renewal while, in Mumford's judgment, neglecting the deeper human problem of ends.

## Life and the Concord School

Bellamy was born in Chicopee Falls, Massachusetts, and came of intellectual age within the orbit of the New England transcendentalists. Mumford situates him explicitly within this tradition:

> Looking backward into the future: that was the paradox by which a young New England romancer, Edward Bellamy, concerned like Thoreau and Emerson and the rest of the great Concord school with the well-being of his community, descended from literature to sociology; and stirred the minds of thousands of people in America in much the same fashion that Theodor Hertzka, writing at the same time, stirred his European contemporaries.
> (Ch III)

The trajectory from literature to sociology is central to Mumford's understanding of Bellamy. He "began to romanticize about reality" and then, during the decade following *Looking Backward*'s publication, "devoted himself to realizing his romance" (Ch III). A subsequent work, *Equality* (1897), elaborated the institutional details with greater specificity — "just as if the popularity of his first work committed him to take up seriously the tasks of the economist and the statesman" (Ch III).

## *Looking Backward* and the Industrial Army

The novel's protagonist, Julius West, is a wealthy young Bostonian afflicted by insomnia — and by a guilty awareness that, as "a rich man living among the poor, an educated man among the uneducated," he "was like one living in isolation among a jealous and alien race" (Ch V). Put into a hypnotic sleep in a vaulted chamber in 1887, West awakens in the year 2000 to find America transformed into a single, nationalized industrial order.

The key institutional innovation is the **industrial army** — a universal labor organization in which every citizen serves from age 21 to 45, organized hierarchically like a military force:

> If Plato cavalierly disposes of the labor problem of the Republic by permitting things to remain pretty much as they were, Bellamy makes the solution of labor organization and the distribution of wealth the key to every other institution in his utopia.
> (Ch V)

The President of the United States serves as general of this industrial army, elected by alumni — men over 45 who have been mustered out of service. Mumford identifies this as **gerontocracy**, or government by the aged, and notes its conservative implications: "when we recollect that the hardships of military service look rather mild and pleasant to the man who has been mustered out, I doubt if the youngsters in the industrial army would stand much chance of having their lot improved" (Ch V). Worker organizing would be mutiny; criticism of the administration would be treason.

|---------|-----------------|---------------------|
| Labor | Universal industrial army, ages 21–45 | Eliminates worker agency; organizing = mutiny |
| Governance | President elected by alumni (gerontocracy) | Conservative bias against reform |
| Distribution | Complete economic equality | Achieved through regimentation, not freedom |
| Crime | Economic motive eliminated → crime disappears | Naïve about non-economic sources of conflict |
| Recreation | State-organized sports by guild rivalry | "Bread and circuses" — work and play both external to the self |

## The Problem of Ends

Mumford's most sustained criticism of Bellamy targets the substitution of mechanical means for human ends. In Bellamy's utopia, "these utopias are all machinery: the means has become the end, and the genuine problem of ends has been forgotten" (Ch III). The elaborate systems — pneumatic tubes delivering goods, telephone concerts, centralized dining halls — are, Mumford argues, "so much rubbish — idiotic rubbish" when they are not "consonant with humanized purposes" (Ch V):

> There is no escaping the problem of ends and the problem of ends, if I may be permitted a pun, belongs at the beginning. Subordinate to humanized ends, machinery and organization — yes, complicated machinery and organization — have undoubtedly a useful contribution to make towards a good community; unsubordinated, or subordinated only to the engineer's conceptions of an efficient industrial equipment and personnel, the most innocent machine may be as humanly devastating as a Lewis gun.
> (Ch V)

The impersonal character of Bellamy's utopia — the "almost complete absence of personal contacts or relationships" (Ch V), the worker who "has become a cog in the machine," dealing with "a thin, barren, abstract world of paper notations" — leads Mumford to predict that "there must be occasion in this new age for stimulants and socialities beside which the roller coasters of Coney Island and the promiscuities of a modern dance hall would be insipid things" (Ch V).

## What Remains

Despite these criticisms, Mumford acknowledges the genuine moral passion that animated Bellamy:

> What remains in *Looking Backward* is the honest passion that inspired the man; the play of generous impulses; the insistence that there is no fun for an ordinarily imaginative person in dining with Dives whilst Lazarus hangs around the table. Bellamy wanted everyone to be equally educated, so that everyone might be his companion; he wanted everyone to be decently fed and sheltered; he wanted to take his share in the dirty work and to see that accidents of wealth did not keep other people from taking theirs.
> (Ch V)

The fundamental problem, as Mumford diagnoses it, is a **breach between Bellamy's conception of the good life and the structure he erected to shelter it** — "due, I believe, to an overemphasis of the part that wholesale mechanical organization, directed by a handful of people, would play in such a reconstruction" (Ch V). Bellamy overestimated the good that modern society contained and "was more than fair to the present order of things when he made the future so closely in its image" (Ch V).

## Place in the Utopian Tradition

Bellamy represents for Mumford the culmination — and the暴露 of the limits — of the nineteenth-century utopia of reconstruction. His work is compared unfavorably with the "utopias of escape" represented by [[william-morris]], whose *News from Nowhere* was written partly as a rejoinder to *Looking Backward*. Where Bellamy centralized and mechanized, Morris decentralized and re-humanized labor.

Mumford also connects Bellamy to the broader critique of [[coketown-idolum]] and [[megalopolis]], arguing that Bellamy's utopia unconsciously reproduces the very patterns of the National State idolum — standardized, impersonal, organized around paper transactions — that it purports to transcend. The "hideous cog-and-wheel utopia of Edward Bellamy" (Ch XII) is, in the end, less an alternative to the industrial order than its idealized reflection.
