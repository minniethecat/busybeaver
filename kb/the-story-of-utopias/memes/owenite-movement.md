The **Owenite movement** was a utopian socialist movement of the early nineteenth century, inspired by the Welsh industrialist and social reformer [[robert-owen|Robert Owen]] (1771–1858). [[lewis-mumford|Lewis Mumford]] treats Owen with brevity in *The Story of Utopias*, noting that while Owen is "usually associated with utopianism," his work "belongs more to the 'real' world than to the idola of utopia" (006-708).

## Mumford's Assessment

Mumford grants Owen "good intentions, organizing ability, and moral fervor" and acknowledges him as "a noble figure, even when his attitude is strained and his tone strident" (006-708). However, Mumford passes over Owen's model communities with "the briefest mention" because his projects for industrial towns had "more of the flavor of a poor colony than that of a productive human society" (006-708). Mumford's decision to give "short shrift to Owen" was deliberate — he sought to restore attention to comparatively neglected figures like [[charles-fourier|Charles Fourier]] and [[thomas-spence|Thomas Spence]] (009-1524).

## The Cooperative Legacy

Despite Mumford's limited treatment, he acknowledges that the Owenite vision achieved partial realization: "Owen's kind of socialism has been partly fulfilled in the [[cooperative-movement|cooperative movement]]" (011-1252). The movement's emphasis on cooperative production, distribution, and community-based reform influenced subsequent generations of British labor and cooperative activists.

## Experimental Communities

The Owenite movement's most visible legacy was its experimental communities. The most famous of these was **[[new-lanark|New Lanark]]** in Scotland, where Owen transformed a cotton mill settlement into a model industrial community with progressive education, housing, and labor conditions. In America, Owen's followers established **[[new-harmony|New Harmony]]** in Indiana (1825–1827), an ambitious attempt to create a fully cooperative society on the American frontier. While both communities ultimately dissolved, they demonstrated practical alternatives to the [[coketown|Coketown]] model of industrial organization.

> "He is a noble figure, even when his attitude is strained and his tone strident. The series of essays he wrote on love and marriage are marked by fine sympathy and common sense." (006-708)

## Mumford's Critique

Mumford groups Owen with [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]], [[jeremy-bentham|Bentham]], and other nineteenth-century reformers who shared "an underlying consensus in their belief in human nature" (011-1268). The weakness of this approach, Mumford argues, was that these thinkers "looked upon human institutions as altogether external to men" and "could conceive of changing the institutions without changing the habits and redirecting the impulses of the people" (011-1268). This externalism was, in Mumford's view, the fatal limitation of all the partisan utopias — including Owenism.
