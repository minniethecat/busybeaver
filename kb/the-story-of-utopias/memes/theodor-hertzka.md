## Theodor Hertzka

Theodor Hertzka (1845-1924) was an Austrian economist and utopian writer whose work *Freeland: A Social Anticipation* (1889, German original; English translation 1891) stirred a significant international movement in the 1890s. His utopia represents a distinctive attempt to reconcile individual freedom in industry with collective ownership of land and capital (0381-779).

### Freeland

*Freeland: A Social Anticipation* sets forth Hertzka's economic doctrines in detail, while its companion volume *A Visit to Freeland, or the New Paradise Regained* (1894) dramatizes the commonwealth in action (0381-779). Mumford characterizes Freeland as "an individualist Utopia on a social foundation" (0381-783). Hertzka admired the doctrines of Adam Smith and sought to realize a society in which individual freedom and initiative would flourish, particularly in industrial enterprise.

### The Paradox of Freedom

Hertzka's thinking leads to a central paradox: "in order to ensure freedom it is impossible to practice laissez faire; for the effect of laissez faire is to permit accidental aggregations of wealth and power to threaten the freedom that less fortunate individuals seek to enjoy" (0381-783). Freeland is therefore a cooperative commonwealth in which the State acts as an interested party in production and distribution, differing from socialism "in name" but arriving at similar conclusions in practice (0381-783).

### Five Fundamental Laws

Freeland operates under five fundamental laws, the first of which is the most important: every inhabitant has the right to become a member of any business they choose, with managers deciding only on the manner of employment, not on membership itself (0381-789, 0381-795). This right of occupational access is complemented by a system in which capital is collected through taxation and distributed without interest to companies that apply for it (0381-801).

### The Central Statistical Office

A distinctive institution in Freeland is the Central Statistical Office, which keeps records of available occupations, pay rates, and economic data. This office enables the rational coordination of production and consumption: "The social use of capital to advance production, rather than to provide fixed incomes for a rentier class, is recognized in Freeland" (0381-801). Risk of business failure is minimized by complete knowledge of probable demand and supply (0381-824).

### Life in Edendale

Every family in Freeland dwells in its own house surrounded by a garden of a thousand square meters. These houses are private property, but the principle governing land use is that "the land must be put in everyone's hands to do with what he chooses" (0381-824). Edendale, the principal city, boasts an administrative palace, the Central Bank, a University, an Academy of Arts, three public libraries, four theaters, and extensive sanitation infrastructure (0381-830).

### Historical Movement and Failure

The publication of Freeland generated "a whirlwind of agitation": a magazine was founded, societies were organized across Europe and America, and an attempt was made to colonize a section of Africa selected by Hertzka. This effort "met with speedy failure as a result of the obtuseness and international jealousy of various colonial officials" (0381-781). Mumford speculates that one practical effect of the Freeland movement was to turn the thoughts of certain Zionists, like Israel Zangwill, "from establishing Zion in Jerusalem to building it up again in some more suitable region in the heart of Africa" (0381-781).

### Critique

Mumford offers a measured critique of Hertzka's utopia. While acknowledging its progressive mechanical innovations, he finds that "people seem to live the same sort of life here as they do in a 'modern' European or American city" (0381-830). The utopia abolishes the slum proletariat and universalizes middle-class existence, but this is the peculiar limitation of nineteenth-century utopianism: "they do not so much criticize the goods of their times as demand more of them!" (0381-832). Alongside Buckingham, Hertzka "wish[es] to extend middle class values throughout society — comfort and security and a plenitude of soap and sanitation" (0381-832).
