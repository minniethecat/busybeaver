## Étienne Cabet

Étienne Cabet (1788-1856) was a French utopian socialist whose *Voyage en Icarie* (1840) became one of the most popular utopian works among European workingmen in the 1840s. His Icarian vision, blending Napoleonic centralism with communist ideals, directly influenced the National Workshops experiment of 1848 and later provided a template that Edward Bellamy would refresh in *Looking Backward* (0381-843, 0381-851).

### Life and Historical Context

Cabet was born in the year preceding the convening of the National Assembly in 1788 and died under the Empire of Napoleon III (0381-847). His most impressionable years were, as Mumford notes, "drenched with the flamboyant light of the Napoleonic conquests and the Napoleonic tradition" (0381-849). The spectacle of a nationalized church and education system extending to the smallest commune through a vast bureaucracy gave solidity to his utopian dreams — a solidity reinforced rather than weakened by Napoleon's personal downfall (0381-849).

### The Napoleonic Template

Mumford identifies the Napoleonic model as the key to understanding Icaria: "Cabet consciously or unconsciously idealized the Napoleonic tradition; and in Icaria he consummated it" (0381-851). This distinguishes Cabet's utopia from those of his contemporaries — where Fourier envisioned decentralized phalanxes and Owen promoted voluntary cooperative communities, Cabet imagined a centralized nation-state with all the "pomp and dignity and splendor" that Napoleon's France had embodied.

### The Irony of Icarian Practice

Cabet's utopian narrative ends in a striking historical irony. Inspired by Robert Owen's example, Cabet led a small band of communist pioneers to the swamps of Missouri to establish an actual Icarian colony. Mumford notes the tragic gap between vision and reality: "his Icaria was a national state, with all its pomp and dignity and splendor, and not a squalid collection of huts in the midst of a dreary prairie" (0381-851). Cabet died in America, "as much perhaps from an outraged sense of dignity as from any physical disease" (0381-851).

### Icaria: The Planned Nation

Icaria is a country divided into one hundred nearly equal provinces, each subdivided into ten communes (0381-857). Mumford observes that "the elegance and precision of the decimal system has overlaid the facts of geography," recalling the French Revolution's division of France into arbitrary administrative departments that "upset those ancient regional groupings which corresponded, roughly, with the natural units of soil, climate, population, and historic continuity" (0381-857).

### Icara: The Ideal City

At the center of Icaria stands Icara, a reconstructed Paris on a reconstructed Seine — "almost circular, cut into two equal parts by a river whose banks have been straightened and enclosed in two straight walls" (0381-859). A circular island, formed by the river's division into two arms, houses the civic center with a palace, terraced garden, and a colossal statue atop a vast column. The city is divided into sixty communes, each with its own school, hospital, temple, and public monuments (0381-861).

Mumford's urbanist eye catches the geometric impossibility of Cabet's plan: "fifty avenues parallel to the river and fifty perpendicular to it. How it is possible to reconcile this street plan with a circular city I have no notion; and Cabet apparently did not take the trouble to cast his verbal specifications into a definite picture or plan" (0381-861).

### Urban Amenities and Hygiene

The Icarian villages display a notable "preoccupation with hygienic conveniences and sanitary regulations" (0381-863). Dust collectors of special model, glass-covered sidewalks, covered omnibus stations, and well-paved and lighted streets characterize the built environment. Factories and warehouses are situated on railway lines and canals, while half the streets are closed to all traffic except dogcarts — a detail that reveals the persistent ambition of nineteenth-century utopias to order every aspect of urban life (0381-863).

### The Icarian Citizen: Life Under State Regulation

Mumford's most penetrating critique of Cabet's vision lies in his depiction of daily life in Icaria. The Icarian citizen rises at six for a breakfast whose content has been determined not by personal preference but by a "committee of scientists" (0381-871). Work occupies seven hours in summer and six in winter, with all products deposited in public stores. Every aspect of existence — food, clothing, labor, even courtship — falls under state regulation:

> When he is through with his work, our Icarian possibly changes his clothes. Exactly what clothes are necessary, and what are permissible has already been prescribed by a committee on clothes; which comes to saying that every Icarian's dress is a uniform, even as every Icarian is an official of the State. Eating, working, dressing, sleeping — there is no getting away from State regulations. (0381-875)

This totalizing uniformity, Mumford observes, represents "Napoleon's conception of a nation in arms," extended to civilian life — "only now it is a nation in overalls" (0381-875). The Icarian citizen is not merely employed by the State but is, in every meaningful sense, a functionary of it.

### The Political System: Democracy as Epiphenomenon

Cabet describes an elaborate system of communal assemblies, provincial representatives, and a national legislature of two thousand deputies. Yet Mumford identifies a fundamental emptiness in this political architecture:

> The only thing resembling public opinion is the collective opinion of these assemblies. The newspapers are published by the government, one for the nation, one for the province, and one for the commune; and they are devoted solely to the presentation of news, divorced from opinion. For this kind of political system, and for all the power that it might presume to wield, there is a word in philosophy which has no substitute — epiphenomenon. (0381-887)

The real power in Icaria resides not in its representative bodies but in the sixteen-member national executive and its network of specialized committees — on food, industry, education, and clothing — which together constitute a bureaucratic dictatorship inherited from the mythical founder Icar (0381-885). Mumford's verdict is severe: "Icaria is essentially not an ideal but an idealization," more a reflection of the Napoleonic state than a genuine alternative to it (0381-889).

### Legacy: From Icaria to Bellamy

Mumford traces a direct line from Cabet to Edward Bellamy: "nothing came of his utopia until Edward Bellamy gave it a fresh outline in *Looking Backward*" (0381-851). Both Cabet and Bellamy share a vision of the nation-state as the proper vehicle for utopian transformation, with centralized planning and bureaucratic rationality as the instruments of social perfection. Cabet's dream, Mumford concludes, gives us "a hint of what machinery might bring us to if the industrial organization were nationalized" (0381-843).
