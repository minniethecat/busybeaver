# Christianopolis (Utopian Model)

**Christianopolis** is Johann Valentin Andreae's seventeenth-century utopian vision, which Lewis Mumford celebrates in *The Story of Utopias* as a model for integrating scientific inquiry, practical craftsmanship, and spiritual life into a unified community. It represents one of the classic utopias of reconstruction.

## Mumford's Assessment

Mumford positions Christianopolis as an important contribution to the eutopian tradition. From Andreae's vision, "we are reminded that the daily life and work of the community must be infused with the spirit of science, and that an acute practical intelligence such as we find today among the engineers need not be divorced from the practice of the humanities" (012-068).

This integration is precisely what Mumford finds lacking in modern civilization, where the "idolum of science and the idolum of art were not to be cemented together in a single personality" and "henceforth, in fact, the dehumanization of art and science begins" (012-036).

## Science and the Good Life

Unlike the purely technocratic visions that would follow, Christianopolis embeds scientific inquiry within a comprehensive vision of the good life. The scientific spirit is not an end in itself but a means of enriching community life. Mumford had earlier acknowledged that Christianopolis's industrial dimension — its workshops and laboratories — represented a legitimate element of eutopia: "There is no need to dismiss the good that lies inside of industrialism because it does not embrace the good that lies beyond it" (010-056).

## Place in the Utopian Tradition

Mumford places Christianopolis in the lineage of reconstructive utopias that runs from Plato's Republic through More's Utopia to the modern garden city movement. Each contributes a distinct element: from Plato, the emphasis on birth and education; from More, the principle of shared possessions; from Christianopolis, the integration of science and humanities; from the nineteenth-century utopians, the recognition that ideals require economic support; and from Howard, the conversion of ideals into concrete plans (012-068).
