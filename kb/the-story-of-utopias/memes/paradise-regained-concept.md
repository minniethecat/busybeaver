## Definition

In [[the-story-of-utopias]], **Paradise Regained** refers to the utopian impulse conceived as the restoration of a lost ideal state — an attempt to recover what humanity once possessed or believes it once possessed. Mumford identifies this impulse as one of the two fundamental utopian drives, alongside the drive toward reconstruction. While Mumford never uses the phrase "Paradise Regained" as a technical term, the concept pervades his analysis: utopias of escape look backward to a lost perfection, while utopias of reconstruction look forward. The concept draws its name from Theodor Hertzka's utopian narrative *A Visit to Freeland, or the New Paradise Regained* (779-779), which Mumford discusses as an attempt "to picture his freeman's commonwealth in action" (779-779).

## Core Characteristics

**Retrospective orientation.** The paradise-regained impulse treats utopia not as an invention but as a recovery. It assumes that what is good was once possessed and has been lost. Mumford observes how "there is scarcely any other piece of writing which even hints at utopia except as utopia may refer to a dim golden age in the past when all men were virtuous and happy" (367-367). This backward-looking orientation appears across cultures and eras, from the myth of the Garden of Eden to classical accounts of a lost Golden Age.

**Mythical foundation.** Unlike utopias of reconstruction, which attempt rational planning, paradise-regained utopias depend on myth and memory. They draw their power not from blueprints but from a shared cultural narrative of loss and the longing for restoration. Mumford explicitly connects this to the psychological depth of utopian yearning — the sense that "our utopias are just as human and warm and jolly as the world out of which they are born" (191-191).

**Tension with reconstruction.** Mumford sees this retrospective impulse as both a source of utopian energy and a limitation. The danger is that the longing for restoration can become escapist — what he calls utopia of escape — rather than constructive. The healthy utopian imagination must transform the memory of paradise into a plan for the future: "we travel through utopia only in order to get beyond utopia" (193-193).

## Historical Context

Mumford traces the paradise-regained impulse through the entire utopian tradition. He finds it in the classical world — "Plutarch's *Life of Lycurgus* looks back into a mythical past" (367-367) — and in Christian thought, where Augustine's *City of God* substitutes a heavenly city for an earthly one. The Renaissance humanists attempted to recover the lost wisdom of antiquity, while nineteenth-century socialists like Hertzka gave the impulse a modern, economic form.

## Relation to Other Concepts

The paradise-regained concept illuminates the fundamental distinction in Mumford's framework between two modes of utopian imagination:

- **[[utopia-of-escape]]** — when the restorative impulse becomes purely escapist, abandoning the real world for a fantasy
- **[[utopia-of-reconstruction]]** — when the memory of paradise is transformed into a practical plan for rebuilding the world
- **[[will-to-utopia]]** — the deeper human drive that fuels both the retrospective and constructive utopian impulses
- **[[eutopia]]** — Mumford's synthesis, which incorporates the wisdom of the past without being imprisoned by it

The concept also resonates with Mumford's critique of the [[idolum]] — particularly the [[country-house-idolum|Country House idolum]], which represents a degraded form of the paradise-regained impulse, substituting consumer fantasy for genuine restoration of community and place.
