## Origin and Inspiration

The Icarian movement originated in the visionary writings of [[etienne-cabet]], a French political theorist whose utopian novel *Voyage en Icarie* (1840) depicted an ideal communist society governed by a rational, centralized state. Cabet's vision, as Lewis Mumford observes, was explicitly modeled on the Napoleonic tradition:

> How Étienne Cabet dreamed of a new Napoleon called Icar, and a new France called Icaria; and how his utopia, with that which Edward Bellamy shows us in *Looking Backward*, gives us a hint of what machinery might bring us to if the industrial organization were nationalized. (843-843)

The novel became an unexpected bestseller among French workingmen in the 1840s, its popularity fueled by the disillusionment and economic hardship of the July Monarchy. Mumford notes that to understand this appeal "one must realize the historic momentum of Napoleon's dictatorship. Cabet consciously or unconsciously idealized the Napoleonic tradition; and in Icaria he consummated it" (851-851).

## Icaria as a Planned Society

Cabet's Icaria is a meticulously planned nation of one hundred provinces, each divided into ten communes. The capital, Icara, is a reconstructed Paris built on a straightened Seine—a circular city traversed by fifty avenues parallel to the river and fifty perpendicular to it, laid out with the precision of the decimal system imposed upon the facts of geography (857-861).

The organization of daily life in Icaria reflects Cabet's commitment to comprehensive state planning. Mumford describes the typical Icarian's regimented day:

| Aspect of Life | Icarian Arrangement |
|---------------|-------------------|
| Breakfast | Served at 6 a.m. in restaurant or factory; menu determined by committee of scientists |
| Work | Seven hours summer, six hours winter; all products deposited in public stores |
| Employer | The State, sole proprietor of all instruments of production |
| Clothing | Prescribed by committee on clothes; effectively a uniform |
| Marriage | Permitted at twenty (men) and eighteen (women); six-month courtship |
| Education | Domestic to age five, then combined with state instruction to seventeen/eighteen |
| Retirement | Sixty-five for men, fifty for women |

Mumford's summary is pointed:

> Eating, working, dressing, sleeping—there is no getting away from State regulations. The uniformity that irks us in modern life and that makes people who have some remnant of free initiative in their makeup chafe in the civil service, to say nothing of the army, is extended to the last degree in Icaria. Napoleon's conception of a nation in arms is dominant; only now it is a nation in overalls. (875-875)

## Political Structure

The political system of Icaria consists of a national assembly of two thousand deputies drawn from the thousand communes, with a national executive of sixteen members each heading a specialized department. However, Mumford identifies the effective concentration of power not in the representative bodies but in the committees and bureaus that control production, education, and consumption:

> For this kind of political system, and for all the power that it might presume to wield, there is a word in philosophy which has no substitute—epiphenomenon. The popular system of representation in Icaria is but a shadow of that dictatorial power which was first wielded by Icar and was in turn transmitted to the committees and bureaus. (887-887)

## American Colonies

The transition from literary utopia to practical experiment came when Cabet, inspired by [[robert-owen]]'s example, led a group of followers to establish Icarian colonies in the United States. The reality proved starkly different from the metropolitan splendor of the fictional Icara:

> That Cabet's futile will-to-power should have led him, under the inspiration of Owen, to the swamps of Missouri as the leader of a little band of communist pioneers is an ironic twist of circumstance: his Icaria was a national state, with all its pomp and dignity and splendor, and not a squalid collection of huts in the midst of a dreary prairie. Cabet died in America, as much perhaps from an outraged sense of dignity as from any physical disease, and nothing came of his utopia until Edward Bellamy gave it a fresh outline in *Looking Backward*. (851-851)

## Critical Assessment

Mumford is careful to distinguish between utopia as ideal and Icaria as idealization. He finds Icaria significant not as a model to emulate but as a revealing case of how the utopian imagination, when captured by the [[national-state-idolum]], produces not liberation but regimentation:

> Icaria is essentially not an ideal but an idealization; and it is in order to keep the two from being confused that I have emphasized its little weaknesses. What is good in Icaria is what is good in the institution of an army; what is bad is what is bad in the execution of a war. (889-889)

This critique places Icaria within Mumford's broader distinction between the [[utopia-of-reconstruction]] and the [[utopia-of-escape]]: Cabet's vision, for all its superficial appeal, ultimately reproduces rather than transcends the authoritarian structures it claims to remedy.

The Icarian movement nonetheless holds a distinctive place in utopian history. As Mumford acknowledges early in *The Story of Utopias*:

> The Icarians who lived only in the mind of Étienne Cabet, or the Freelanders who dwelt within the imagination of a dry little Austrian economist, have had more influence upon the lives of our contemporaries than the Etruscan people who once dwelt in Italy, although the Etruscans belong to what we call the real world, and the Freelanders and Icarians inhabited—Nowhere. (187-187)
