# William Godwin

## Introduction

William Godwin (1756-1836) was a British political philosopher and novelist, best known for *An Enquiry Concerning Political Justice* (1793), a foundational text of philosophical anarchism. In *The Story of Utopias*, Lewis Mumford mentions Godwin twice, placing him among the key eighteenth-century reformers whose work established the intellectual groundwork for the utopian tradition.

## Godwin in Mumford's Account

Mumford first introduces Godwin in his survey of the evolution of utopian and reformist thought across centuries. Tracing how the Renaissance transformed social speculation from religious otherworldliness to worldly planning, Mumford writes:

> In the beginning, these plans were devoted to politics and criminal reform, like those of Rousseau, Beccaria, Bentham, Jefferson, Godwin, and the eighteenth century reformers generally; in the nineteenth century the main accent was economic, and a number of movements arose which could be traced back to the semi-scientific investigations of Adam Smith, Ricardo, Proudhon, Malthus, Marx, and perhaps half a dozen other thinkers of outstanding importance, among whom we should perhaps include such latter day figures as Mill, Spencer, and Henry George. (011-005)

Here Godwin is situated squarely within the Enlightenment project of rational social reconstruction — a tradition that Mumford regards as a precursor to the more fully elaborated utopian schemes of the nineteenth century. The passage delineates a historical shift from political and legal reform (the eighteenth-century mode) to economic and scientific analysis (the nineteenth-century mode), with Godwin positioned as a representative of the earlier, more philosophically radical phase.

Mumford returns to Godwin in a later passage that emphasizes the underlying consensus among utopian thinkers despite their surface disagreements:

> Men like Rousseau, Bentham, Godwin, Fourier, and Owen might be miles apart from one another in their criticism of society, but there was an underlying consensus in their belief in human nature. They looked upon human institutions as altogether external to men; these were so many straitjackets that cunning rulers had thrown over the community to make sane and kindly people behave like madmen; and they could conceive of changing the institutions without changing the habits and redirecting the impulses of the people by whom and for whom they had been created. (011-020)

This observation — that utopian thinkers share a fundamental optimism about human potential, however different their specific programs — is central to Mumford's understanding of the utopian tradition. Yet Mumford's own position is more nuanced: he criticizes this Enlightenment consensus for its shallow psychology, arguing that institutional change without corresponding inner transformation is insufficient. This is precisely the point at which Mumford enlists Emerson's contrary insight — that "it was stupid to expect any real or permanent change from any social program which was unable to regenerate or convert the people who are to engineer it and carry it through" (011-028) — as a corrective to the Godwinian tradition.

## Political Justice and Its Influence

Godwin's *Political Justice* argued that government, law, and property are corrupting institutions that should be gradually dismantled in favor of small, self-governing communities governed by rational persuasion. His faith in human perfectibility and the power of reason to overcome prejudice influenced a generation of radicals, including his son-in-law Percy Bysshe Shelley and, indirectly, the early socialist and anarchist movements. Godwin's conviction that social institutions should be judged by their effects on human happiness anticipates the pragmatic criterion that Mumford himself employs throughout *The Story of Utopias*, even as Mumford's more psychologically sophisticated account of human nature leads him to qualify the Enlightenment's unbounded confidence in rational reform.

## Place in the Utopian Tradition

Godwin represents the rationalist, Enlightenment pole of utopian thought — the belief that clear thinking about human nature and social arrangements can yield principles capable of transforming society. For Mumford, Godwin's presence alongside Rousseau, Bentham, and Owen in the intellectual genealogy of utopianism testifies to the breadth and diversity of the tradition. At the same time, Mumford's critique of the Enlightenment assumption that institutions alone determine human behavior — that "they could conceive of changing the institutions without changing the habits and redirecting the impulses of the people" (011-020) — marks the limit of Godwinian rationalism within Mumford's larger argument for a more integrated, psychologically realistic utopianism.
