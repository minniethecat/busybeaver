St. John Greer Ervine (1883–1971) was an Irish playwright, novelist, and drama critic. His play *Mixed Marriage* receives extended analysis in [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]]'s *[[the-story-of-utopias]]*, where it serves as the principal illustration of [[partisanism]]'s second weakness — its tendency to fracture communities along vertical divisions.

## *Mixed Marriage* in Mumford's Analysis

*Mixed Marriage* dramatizes a love affair between a young Catholic woman and a young Protestant man in sectarian Ulster. Mumford uses the play to show how partisan identities — in this case, religious sectarianism — override the "horizontal affiliations" of daily life:

> "This tendency was nicely illustrated in a play by Mr. St. John Ervine, called *Mixed Marriage*, which dealt with the love affair of a young girl and a young man who were separated by the religions that had been handed down by their parents. In Mr. Ervine's wretched little Ulster community, these religions served as an excuse to keep people from being friendly and decent to their neighbors" (011-040).

## The Generalization

Mumford does not limit the play's lesson to Ulster sectarianism. He immediately generalizes:

> "Now for Catholic and Protestant in Mr. St. John Ervine's play one may substitute Democrat and Republican, White Guard and Red Guard, Socialist and Financier, Prohibitionist and anti-Prohibitionist and the results will be just as deplorably the same" (011-041).

In Mumford's reading, Ervine's play reveals a universal pattern: whenever a single identity — religious, political, national — is elevated above the "horizontal interests" of mating, friendship, and neighborly life, the result is "mischievous" to human flourishing.

## The Horizontal Against the Vertical

Mumford contrasts the "vertical divisions" created by partisan identities with the "horizontal affiliations" that cut across them: "mating, and making friends with those who have common interests and sentiments, and mixing freely within the whole community, are highly important horizontal interests; they tend to unite people in a common bond which is fundamental for the reason that these interests and activities are essentially human" (011-040). Ervine's play, in this reading, is a case study in what happens when the vertical overrides the horizontal.

## Literary Career

Beyond *Mixed Marriage*, Ervine had a substantial career in the Irish Literary Revival and later as drama critic for *The Observer*. His plays often explored the tensions between individual desire and communal identity in Irish society. His work at the Abbey Theatre in Dublin placed him among the major figures of early twentieth-century Irish drama.
