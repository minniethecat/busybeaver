# Sir Thomas More

**Sir Thomas More** (1478-1535) is the English statesman and author whose *[[utopia-more|Utopia]]* (1516) gave the utopian tradition its name and established its modern form. [[lewis-mumford|Lewis Mumford]] treats More as the pivotal figure who revived the [[plato|Platonic]] tradition after a "fallow period of almost two thousand years" (003-015), and as the thinker who first grasped the centrality of shared possessions to genuine community.

## Utopia

More's *Utopia* was, Mumford argues, "the bridge by which he sought to span the gap between the old order of the Middle Age, and the new interests and institutions of the Renaissance" (001-004). It arose from the collision of two worlds: the recovery of Greek philosophy and the discovery of the New World.

> "More's narrator, Raphael Hythloday, is a Portuguese scholar, learned in Greek, who has left his family possessions with his kinsmen and has gone adventuring for other continents with Americus Vesputius" (003-006).

Through Hythloday, "no institution is too fantastic but that it might exist — on the other side of the world" (003-006). Mumford, following [[patrick-geddes|Patrick Geddes]], notes that More was "an inveterate punster" whose title plays on both *Outopia* (no-place) and *[[eutopia|Eutopia]]* (the good place) (012-003). This linguistic doubling encapsulates the central tension of the utopian tradition.

## Key Contributions

| Contribution | Description | Mumford's Assessment |
|-------------|-------------|---------------------|
| Shared possessions | Community based on common ownership | "a community becomes a community to the extent that it has shared possessions" (012-068) |
| Moral-spiritual approach | Social reform through ethics and religion | More "looked to social reform and religious ethics to transform society" (012-005) |
| Defense of family | Unlike Plato and Campanella | More "defended the individual family" as a married man (005-008) |

## Mumford's Assessment

More stands as the bridge figure between classical and modern utopian thought. His married status shaped his vision — unlike [[plato|Plato]] and [[tommaso-campanella|Campanella]], who "were bachelors, and they proposed that men should live like monks or soldiers," More defended the individual family as the foundation of social life (005-008). His contribution to the concept of the [[good-life|good life]] is his insistence that material arrangements — shared possessions, just institutions — are not ends in themselves but means to human flourishing.

More's work is treated not as a finished system but as one of the essential contributions to the accumulating eutopian tradition, alongside the practical experiments of figures like [[ebenezer-howard|Ebenezer Howard]] and the [[garden-city-organization|Garden City Association]].

> "More, on the other hand, looked to social reform and religious ethics to transform society; Bacon, to organized science and the mechanical arts. The truth is, both are necessary" (012-005).
