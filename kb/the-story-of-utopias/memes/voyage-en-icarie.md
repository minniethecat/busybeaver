# Voyage en Icarie

**Voyage en Icarie** (English: *Voyage to Icaria*) is [[etienne-cabet|Étienne Cabet]]'s utopian novel, first published in 1840 under the title *Voyage et aventures de lord William Carisdall en Icarie* and reissued in a more popular edition in 1845. It was one of the best-selling utopian works among workingmen in the 1840s and inspired a real-world communitarian movement that established Icarian colonies in the United States.

[[lewis-mumford|Lewis Mumford]] devotes Chapter Seven of *The Story of [[utopia-more|Utopia]]s* to Cabet, examining how the Napoleonic tradition shaped Icaria into a "[[national-state-idolum|National State]], abundantly organized for war, and remaining on that footing in the midst of its peacetime activities" (007-011). For [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]], Icaria represents the utopia as idealization rather than ideal — the transformation of utopian aspiration into bureaucratic machinery.

## The Napoleonic Foundation

[[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] emphasizes that Cabet's vision cannot be understood apart from the Napoleonic context. Cabet's most impressionable years were "drenched with the flamboyant light of the Napoleonic conquests and the Napoleonic tradition which remained as an afterglow when the conquests themselves had fallen below the horizon" (007-003). The spectacle of a nationalized church, nationalized education, and a vast bureaucratic apparatus reaching into every commune gave solidity to Cabet's dreams.

> Cabet consciously or unconsciously idealized the Napoleonic tradition; and in Icaria he consummated it. (007-003)

This Napoleonic inheritance explains, for [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]], both the popular appeal of the work and its fundamental limitation: "Icaria is essentially not an ideal but an idealization; and it is in order to keep the two from being confused that I have emphasized its little weaknesses" (007-015).

## The City and Its Organization

Cabet describes Icaria's capital in elaborate detail. The city is divided into quarters; Icara has sixty communes of almost equal size. In each quarter is a school, a hospital, a temple, shops, public places, and monuments:

> The streets are straight and wide, the city being traversed by fifty avenues parallel to the river and fifty perpendicular to it. (007-008)

[[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] notes a revealing inconsistency: "How it is possible to reconcile this street plan with a circular city I have no notion; and Cabet apparently did not take the trouble to cast his verbal specifications into a definite picture or plan" (007-008). Each block has fifteen houses on each side, with a public building in the middle; between the rows of houses are gardens which the inhabitants tend with pride, much like those of [[ideal-commonwealths|More's Utopia]].

## The National State as Utopia

Cabet describes Icarian institutions "in the minutest fashion, down to the noiseless window with which each Icarian's house is equipped" (007-011). What emerges is a society where national importance is the sole criterion of value:

> What is not of national importance, in this scheme of things, is of no importance; and the people who decide what is or is not of national importance are the officeholders — in the capital. (007-011)

[[lewis-mumford|Mumford]]'s judgment is sharp: "What is good in Icaria is what is good in the institution of an army; what is bad is what is bad in the execution of a war. If the [[good-life|good life]] could be perpetrated by a junta of busybodies, as [[plato|Plato]] would call them, Icaria would be a model community" (007-015).

## Historical Significance

Despite his criticisms, [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] acknowledges Icaria's influence. The work was a bestseller among French workingmen and inspired [[louis-blanc|Louis Blanc]]'s ill-fated National Workshops of 1848. Cabet himself led a group of followers to America to establish an Icarian colony — an ironic outcome for a vision conceived on a national scale:

> That Cabet's futile will-to-power should have led him, under the inspiration of Owen, to the swamps of Missouri as the leader of a little band of communist pioneers is an ironic twist of circumstance: his Icaria was a national state, with all its pomp and dignity and splendor, and not a squalid collection of huts in the midst of a dreary prairie. (007-003)

[[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] even suggests a provocative parallel: "in the early days of the second Russian revolution it came near to being a fait accompli — there was more of Cabet than of [[karl-marx|Marx]] perhaps in embryonic Soviet Russia!" (007-015).

## Place in the Utopian Tradition

In [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]]'s framework, *Voyage en Icarie* is a transitional work, bridging the gap between the classic utopias and the nineteenth-century [[utopia-of-reconstruction|utopias of reconstruction]]. It shares with [[looking-backward|Looking Backward]] the vision of a nationally organized industrial society, but its Napoleonic foundation gives it a distinct character — utopia as the perfection of state machinery rather than the transformation of human purposes. [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] places it alongside the [[partisanship|partisan utopia]]s that mistake institutional rearrangement for genuine social reconstruction.
