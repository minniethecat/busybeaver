**John Amos Comenius** (1592–1670), born Jan Amos Komenský, was a Czech philosopher, educator, and theologian of the Moravian Reformation. In *The Story of Utopias*, [[lewis-mumford|Lewis Mumford]] discusses Comenius in the context of the early seventeenth-century utopian tradition, alongside [[johann-andreae|Johann Valentin Andreae]], the author of *[[christianopolis|Christianopolis]]* (004-500).

## The Labyrinth of the World

Comenius published *The Labyrinth of the World and the Paradise of the Heart* in 1623. [[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] describes it as a work that "combined the outlooks of science and art in a remarkable synthesis" (004-500). The first part is a survey of the actual world as Comenius found it, while the second depicts the transition to the heavenly world of Christian religion. Mumford notes that the idea behind the *Labyrinth* was the same that inspired [[johann-andreae|Andreae]] (004-500).

> "When John Amos Comenius wrote his remarkable little book called *The Labyrinth of the World and the Paradise of the Heart* in 1623, he combined the outlooks of science and art in a remarkable synthesis." (004-500)

## Intellectual Network

Comenius corresponded with learned figures across Europe, including [[samuel-hartlib|Samuel Hartlib]] in England (004-500). This placed him within the same transnational network of Reformation-era intellectuals that produced [[johann-andreae|Andreae]]'s *[[christianopolis|Christianopolis]]* (1619), [[francis-bacon|Bacon]]'s *[[new-atlantis|New Atlantis]]*, and [[tommaso-campanella|Campanella]]'s *[[city-of-the-sun|City of the Sun]]*.

## Mumford's Assessment

Mumford judges that Comenius's work would "take a high place in the history of utopian thought" were it not for "the complete otherworldliness of this theological utopia" (004-500). Unlike the more this-worldly utopias of reconstruction such as [[christianopolis|Christianopolis]], the *Labyrinth* ultimately retreats into a heavenly paradise rather than attempting to transform the earthly city. This reflects the broader tension in the seventeenth century between the Renaissance ideal of "fully energized human beings, able to span life in all its manifestations" and the continued pull of theological otherworldliness (004-500).
