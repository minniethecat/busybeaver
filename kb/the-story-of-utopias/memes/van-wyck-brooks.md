## Introduction

Van Wyck Brooks (1886-1963) was an American literary critic, biographer, and cultural historian. His work on American literature and his personal encouragement played a catalytic role in the creation of [[lewis-mumford|Lewis Mumford]]'s *[[the-story-of-utopias|The Story of Utopias]]*. Brooks's independent scholarly leads also shaped the book's treatment of Renaissance utopian thought, particularly the connection between [[rabelais]] and the utopian tradition.

## Role in *The Story of Utopias*

### Catalytic Encouragement

Mumford acknowledged Brooks as the direct impetus behind the book's composition. In the Acknowledgments to *The Story of Utopias*, Mumford wrote:

> "The actual work of composition was started by a suggestion from Mr. Van Wyck Brooks; and without Mr. Brooks' encouragement I should perhaps never have begun or carried through the task."
> — [100]

This personal debt places Brooks at the origin point of the entire study — without his suggestion, Mumford's decade-long outline might never have become the published work.

### The Rabelais Connection

Beyond general encouragement, Brooks provided a specific and valuable scholarly lead. Mumford credited Brooks with independently identifying [[rabelais|Rabelais]]'s *Abbey of Thélème* as a significant utopian text:

> "While Mr. Van Wyck Brooks put me independently on the trail of Rabelais' Abbey of Thélème I must acknowledge, with as much grace as possible, that Herr Blueher anticipated me in grasping this clue to Renaissance culture; and if any credit is due, he deserves it."
> — [1526]

This lead connected the utopian tradition to Renaissance humanism through one of its most imaginative literary expressions.

## Intellectual Context

### The Early Twentieth-Century American Cultural Revival

Brooks was a central figure in the American literary revival of the 1910s and 1920s. His 1915 book *America's Coming-of-Age* argued for a mature American literary culture that could bridge the divide between highbrow and lowbrow traditions — a theme that resonated with Mumford's own project of connecting intellectual history to practical social reconstruction.

### Relationship to Mumford's Circle

Brooks moved in the same New York intellectual circles as Mumford, which included figures such as [[patrick-geddes|Patrick Geddes]] (whose regionalist ideas heavily influenced Mumford), [[thorstein-veblen|Thorstein Veblen]], and the contributors to *The Freeman* magazine. Mumford's Acknowledgments connect Brooks to this broader network of thinkers who shaped the book's intellectual framework.

## Legacy in Utopian Studies

Brooks's contribution to utopian studies is mediated through his influence on Mumford. By encouraging the composition of *The Story of Utopias* and pointing to the Rabelais connection, Brooks helped shape what became one of the foundational English-language surveys of utopian thought. His role exemplifies the collaborative nature of intellectual production — where a critic's encouragement and a well-timed scholarly lead can catalyze a major work.
