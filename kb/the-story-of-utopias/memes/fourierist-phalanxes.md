The Fourierist Phalanxes were intentional communities founded on the principles of Charles Fourier's theory of "association," which Mumford classifies as the first major group of industrial utopias — "the Associationists" (006-010). Fourier envisioned communities organized around the "phalanx," a self-sufficient agrarian-industrial unit designed to harmonize human passions with productive labor.

Fourier's model phalanx was to consist of "a company of 1,500 or 1,600 persons, owning a good stretch of land comprising at least a square league" (006-016). The community would be centered on a vast communal building — the phalanstery — containing workshops, living quarters, and social halls. The main economic occupation was agricultural rather than industrial, which Mumford identifies as "perhaps the great distinction between Fourier and the other utopians of his time" (006-017).

Fourier's design incorporated measures to combat the monotony of industrial labor, suggesting "that the monotony be corrected by having recourse to changing tasks and occupations from time to time" (006-020). In commercial exchange, the phalanx was to act as a single economic unit, achieving the efficiencies of large-scale production while preserving individual choice and variety.

## The Phalanstery as Architectural Utopia

The principle of association found concrete embodiment in a vast central edifice: "a palace complete in all its appointments serving as the residence of the associates" (006-018). This phalanstery was organized into three wings corresponding to the Material, the Social, and the Intellectual domains. Workshops and halls of industry occupied one wing; libraries, scientific collections, museums, and artists' studios another; and banquet halls, reception rooms, and grand salons the central Social wing. At opposite ends stood a Temple of the Material Harmonies — "devoted to singing, music, poetry, dancing, gymnastics, painting, and so forth" — and a Temple of Unityism, "to celebrate with appropriate rites man's unity with the universe" (006-018). An observatory with telegraph and signal tower crowned the summit, enabling communication with other phalanxes.

Mumford emphasizes that the phalanstery's design was not merely architectural but social: it abolished the individual household, "gives a new freedom to women" (006-023), and replaced private kitchens with public ones where children were trained in cooking from an early age. Yet Fourier did not abolish private life entirely — "it is possible to dine in solitude as well as in company" (006-019), and private property was sanctioned, with each member receiving dividends proportional to their stock holdings alongside a guaranteed minimum of food, clothing, and lodging.

## Industrial Armies and the Moral Equivalent of War

One of Fourier's most remarkable innovations, Mumford observes, was "the utilization of a moral equivalent for war, long before Professor William James invented the phrase" (006-024). Fourier envisioned the phalanx assembling productive armies that would transform the landscape instead of devastating it:

> "Instead of devastating thirty provinces in a campaign, these armies will have spanned thirty rivers with bridges, re-wooded thirty barren mountains, dug thirty trenches for irrigation, and drained thirty marshes." [006-024]

This vision of collective labor directed toward constructive rather than destructive ends was, for Mumford, one of Fourier's most enduring contributions to utopian thought.

## Historical Experiments

Although "Fourier died without persuading anyone to give a trial to his scheme of association," his ideas nonetheless exerted considerable practical influence. "The Brook Farm experiment in America was a fumbling attempt to plant a phalanstery," and the *familistère* of the great steel works of Godin at Guise, in France, represented a more successful industrial adaptation of Fourierist principles (006-025). Numerous phalanxes were established across the United States during the 1840s, most notably the North American Phalanx in New Jersey, which operated from 1843 to 1856.

Mumford credits Fourier as "the first man who had a plan for colonizing the wilderness of industrial barbarism that existed at the beginning of the nineteenth century, and redeeming that wilderness to civilization" (006-025). While the actual phalanxes fell short of Fourier's elaborate specifications, they demonstrated the practical appeal of associationist ideas and influenced subsequent cooperative movements.
