## Introduction

Georges Eugène Sorel (1847-1922) was a French philosopher and social theorist whose work on myth, violence, and social change influenced movements across the political spectrum — from revolutionary syndicalism to fascism. For Mumford, Sorel provides a crucial analytical category: the concept of "social myth," the collectively held images and narratives that, like the classic literary utopias, pattern the actual behavior of societies.

Mumford acknowledges Sorel's importance directly: "There is a fairly popular discussion of the place of myths and ideals in the George Sorel's *Reflections on Violence*, and Benjamin Kidd's *Science of Power* (especially Chapter V.)" (012-061). Throughout *The Story of Utopias*, Mumford applies Sorel's insight to the "idola" — the Country House, Coketown, the National State — that have shaped modern civilization.

## Social Myth and Utopia

Sorel's key contribution to utopian studies is the concept of the "social myth" — a collectively held image of the future that, regardless of its factual accuracy or likelihood of realization, motivates group action in the present. For Sorel, the general strike functioned as the central myth of revolutionary syndicalism: workers acted as if the general strike were imminent, and this belief shaped their behavior in ways that made revolutionary change possible.

Mumford adapts and extends this concept. Where Sorel focused on the myths of proletarian revolution, Mumford identifies myths operating throughout society — the Country House ideal of aristocratic consumption, Coketown's vision of limitless industrial production, the National State's image of sovereign political unity. Mumford calls these "collective utopias or social myths" (008-085) and treats them as the hidden utopias that have exerted more influence on actual behavior than the classic literary utopias of More, Bacon, or Campanella.

> We must think of our idola as a sort of diffused environment or atmosphere, which differs in "chemical content" and in extension with each individual. Some of these idola have so uniformly taken possession of men's minds in a particular age that they are as much a part of the environment a baby is born into as the furniture of his house. (008-087)
