James Harrington (1611-1677) was an English political philosopher of the Commonwealth period whose major work, *The Commonwealth of Oceana* (1656), proposed a republican constitution grounded in the redistribution of landed property. In *The Story of Utopias*, Mumford treats Harrington as a transitional figure: significant enough to merit discussion in his chapter on land-based utopias, yet ultimately excluded from the main line of the utopian tradition because *Oceana* was, in Mumford's judgment, an "important essay in politics" (1524) rather than a complete picture of an ideal commonwealth.

## Life and Works

Harrington was born into a landed gentry family and served as a gentleman of the bedchamber to Charles I, with whom he remained on close terms throughout the king's imprisonment. After Charles's execution, Harrington turned to political theory, producing *Oceana* as a constitutional blueprint for a republican England. The book, dedicated to Oliver Cromwell, was initially seized by the authorities but published after intervention by Cromwell himself. Harrington's later years were marked by imprisonment and declining health.

## Land and Republican Government

Mumford locates Harrington within a tradition of political thought that recognized land as the foundation of civil society. In his chapter on land-based utopias, Mumford writes:

> The importance of land in the constitution of civil society was emphasized by the Diggers of Cromwell's time; one of them, Gerard Winstanley, wrote a minor utopia to prove that the land should be held in common; and this view was reinforced — without the communism — in a purely political utopia called *Oceana* by Sir James Harrington, who lived during the same period. Harrington advocated such a distribution of land that the landed gentry should be the leaders, and the commonalty should have the preponderance of power.
> (751)

Harrington's central argument was that political power follows property, and that the distribution of land determines the form of government. An agrarian law limiting the amount of land any individual could hold would, in Harrington's view, prevent the concentration of wealth that led to tyranny and ensure that sovereignty rested with a broad class of independent freeholders rather than a monarch or oligarchy. Unlike Winstanley's communist vision, however, Harrington's *Oceana* preserved private property and class distinctions — it was a reform of the existing order rather than a radical transformation of it.

This qualified, structurally conservative approach is precisely what separates Harrington from the utopian tradition proper. Harrington sought to repair the English constitution; he did not seek to build a new world from first principles.

## Mumford's Exclusion

In the bibliographical note that closes *The Story of Utopias*, Mumford explains the criterion that led him to exclude *Oceana* from his main survey:

> I set out to treat such plans for the improvement of the human community as had been embodied in complete pictures of an ideal commonwealth: this excluded important essays in politics like Hobbes' *Leviathan* and Harrington's *Oceana*; and it ruled out any treatment of abstract idealisms which, however important, did not exemplify the essential utopian method.
> (1524)

This exclusion is itself revealing of Mumford's taxonomy of utopian thought. For Mumford, a genuine utopia must present a *complete picture* — an integrated vision of an entire way of life, not merely a constitutional mechanism or a political reform. *Oceana*, for all its influence on eighteenth- and nineteenth-century republican thought, remained a work of political science rather than utopian imagination. Harrington's concern was the machinery of government; the utopian's concern is the whole of life.

## Place in the Utopian Tradition

Harrington's indirect influence on the utopian tradition is nonetheless significant. His agrarian law anticipated the land-reform themes that would become central to nineteenth-century utopian schemes, from Thomas Spence's *Spensonia* to Henry George's single-tax movement. The fundamental insight — that political liberty requires economic independence, and that economic independence is impossible without access to land — runs as a continuous thread through the land-based utopian tradition that Mumford traces in Chapter VII.

Moreover, *Oceana* influenced the American founders — John Adams cited Harrington extensively — and through them shaped the constitutional architecture of the United States. If Mumford's standard is the "influence on thought and life" (1524) that a utopian work has exercised, Harrington's indirect influence may be greater than that of many works Mumford does include.
