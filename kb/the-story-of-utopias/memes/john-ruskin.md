# John Ruskin

**John Ruskin** (1819-1900) is the English art critic, social thinker, and philanthropist whose plans for a "Guild of St. George" — intended as "a little island of honest labor and sound education in the midst of the turbid sea of industrialism" — earn him a place, alongside [[robert-owen|Robert Owen]], among the practical utopians Mumford examines and ultimately dismisses as limited in scope (006-025).

## The Guild of St. George

In the last quarter of the nineteenth century, Ruskin developed plans for the Guild of St. George, a community that would cultivate honest labor and sound education apart from the degradations of industrial capitalism. Mumford describes the Guild with measured sympathy:

> "With Owen I must also dismiss John Ruskin, who began in the last quarter of the nineteenth century to develop plans for a 'Guild of St. George.' This guild was to form a little island of honest labor and sound education in the midst of the turbid sea of industrialism; but it did not embrace the whole of society, and it was utopian only in the sense that the Oneida Community, let us say, was utopian. While they are full of pregnant suggestions, the plans for the Guild are as fragmentary as the *New Atlantis*" (006-025).

The critique is twofold: first, the Guild was separatist rather than reconstructive — it withdrew from society rather than transforming it; second, its plans remained incomplete, more a sketch than a working blueprint.

## Mumford's Assessment

Mumford's engagement with Ruskin spans multiple registers. In the context of describing Buckingham's utopia, he notes that Buckingham's proposal lacked the critical depth Ruskin brought to social thought:

> "It is true that in this proposal of Buckingham's there is none of Fourier's brilliant intuitions of a true social order, and none of Ruskin's critical inquiry into what composed a good life: Buckingham took contemporary values for granted" (006-032).

The phrase "Ruskin's critical inquiry into what composed a good life" is revealing. It suggests that Mumford recognized in Ruskin a kindred concern — the insistence that any social order must be judged by the quality of life it enables, not merely by its efficiency or productivity. This aligns Ruskin, however imperfectly, with the tradition of [[good-life|the good life]] that Mumford traces from [[plato|Plato]] through [[sir-thomas-more|More]].

Elsewhere, Mumford pairs Ruskin with Samuel Butler in defending the ideal of the cultivated person:

> "Ruskin and Samuel Butler were possibly right when they insisted that the perfect gentleman was a finer product than the perfect peasant or artisan: he is a finer product because he is essentially more alive" (010-031).

## The Frustration of the Partial Utopian

In his concluding chapter, Mumford returns to Ruskin as an example of the utopian whose dreams outran the capacity of a single individual to realize them:

> "From the bitter frustration of Fourier, Cabet, Hertzka, and even John Ruskin those who are in search of the beloved community may well take a warning" (012-060).

This "bitter frustration" is diagnostic: Ruskin's Guild, like [[charles-fourier|Fourier's]] phalansteries or Cabet's Icaria, failed because it assumed that "the dreams and projects of any single man might be realized in society at large" (012-060). The eutopian alternative Mumford proposes — regional survey, community-scale reconstruction, education as the foundation of change — is in part a response to the Ruskinian impasse.

## Ruskin as Educator

In a more appreciative register, Mumford invokes Ruskin's example as an educator. Describing the ideal of a teaching staff composed of creative practitioners, he notes:

> "Faraday's classic lectures on the physics of the candle, and Ruskin's addresses to a young ladies' boarding school on the function of literature — such things might be multiplied" (004-031).

This Ruskin — the passionate amateur sharing knowledge out of love rather than professional obligation — exemplifies what Mumford sees as the latent humanist potential present in society, waiting only to be organized and extended.
