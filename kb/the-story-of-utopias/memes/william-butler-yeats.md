## Introduction

William Butler Yeats (1865-1939) was an Irish poet, dramatist, and central figure in the Irish Literary Revival. In *[[the-story-of-utopias|The Story of Utopias]]*, [[lewis-mumford|Lewis Mumford]] cited Yeats as a living exemplar of the [[eutopia|eutopian]] method — an artist whose work demonstrated how creative practice, rooted in local community and cultural tradition, could contribute to the concrete reconstruction of the [[good-life|good life]].

## Yeats as Eutopian Model

### The Artist and the Community

In the closing chapter of *The Story of Utopias*, Mumford turned to Yeats to illustrate what the artist's proper role in social reconstruction might look like:

> "For examples of what the artist might be, and what his proper relation to the community might be when he was mature enough to recognize it and discipline himself to it, let us look at Mr. William Butler Yeats or Æ. There are doubtless a good many other examples that might be offered in Europe; but these are particularly good; for the reason that with Æ one can see in his *The National Being* how the conceptions of art enter into the tissues of all his plans for renovating life in the Irish Countryside. In the work of these artists and their fellows we have a clue to one of the most promising attempts to establish a concrete eutopia which shall rise out of the real facts of the everyday environment and, at the same time, turn upon them and mold them creatively a little nearer the heart's desire."
> — [1455]

This passage is significant because it positions Yeats not as a utopian writer in the conventional sense, but as a practitioner of the eutopian method — someone who works from the actual conditions of a specific place and culture to reshape them toward a more humane form.

### Yeats's Own Words on Art and Community

Mumford then quoted Yeats himself, from an account published in *The Dial*, to illustrate the artist's philosophy of creative engagement with tradition and community:

> "We had in Ireland imaginative stories, which the uneducated classes knew and even sang, and might we not make those stories current among the educated classes, rediscovering, for the work's sake, what I have called 'the applied arts of literature,' the association of literature, that is, with music, speech, and dance; and at last, it might be, so deepen the political passion of the nation that all, artist and poet, craftsman and day laborer, would accept a common design. Perhaps even these images, once created and associated with river and mountain, might move of themselves and with some powerful, even turbulent life, like those painted horses that trample the rice-fields of Japan."
> — [1455]

Mumford recommended this account "to all the forlorn revolutionaries and reformers who wonder why the dry bones of their doctrines remain dry bones, instead of knitting themselves together and becoming alive" [1455]. Yeats's vision of a "common design" that unites artist and day-laborer was, for Mumford, precisely what abstract ideological utopianism lacked.

## The Irish Cultural Revival as Nonpartisan Reconstruction

### Horizontal vs. Vertical Organization

Yeats's Dublin literary circle also served as Mumford's case study in nonpartisan cultural organization. In Chapter VIII, Mumford contrasted the divisive "vertical" loyalties of political parties with the "horizontal" bonds of shared cultural life:

> "The equally nonpartisan literary association which grew up in Dublin under the leadership of Æ, William Butler Yeats, Lady Gregory, and the rest of that fine and glorious crew" — [1316]

Mumford argued that this literary association had "done more to promote the good life in Ireland than any other institution" except possibly the agricultural cooperative movement led by Sir Horace Plunkett and Æ [1316]. The key was that both organizations emphasized what people shared as members of a local community rather than what divided them as partisans of competing ideologies.

### The Lesson for Utopian Practice

The Irish example, with Yeats at its cultural center, demonstrated Mumford's core thesis: that cultural and cooperative institutions which grow organically from a specific region and its traditions are more effective at improving lived conditions than abstract ideological schemes imposed from above. This is the essence of the eutopian method — rebuilding the world from the ground up, using the materials of actual community life.

## Legacy in Utopian Thought

Yeats's inclusion in *The Story of Utopias* is notable because he was not a writer of utopian fictions or political programs. Mumford valued him instead as a practitioner — someone whose creative work demonstrated, in concrete practice, how art could serve as a vehicle for community reconstruction. This reframing of the artist's social role is consistent with Mumford's broader argument that eutopia must be built from "the real facts of the everyday environment," not imposed as a blueprint. Yeats, the poet who dreamed of images "associated with river and mountain" that might connect all members of a society in "a common design," represented for Mumford the living possibility of art in the service of the good life.
