## Definition

In [[the-story-of-utopias]], the **Megalopolis** is Mumford's term for the oversized, parasitic great city that functions as the "chief instrument of the National State" (1183-1183). It is one of the three great idola Mumford analyzes alongside the [[country-house-idolum|Country House]] and [[coketown-idolum|Coketown]]: "In selecting these idola — The Country House, Coketown, the Megalopolis — I have been forced to gauge their strength and test their quality very largely by their actual results in the workaday world" (1037-1037). Unlike a genuine city that serves its region, the Megalopolis extracts resources from its hinterland while imposing a paper-based uniformity upon its inhabitants.

## Core Characteristics

**The rule of paper.** Mumford's most penetrating critique of the Megalopolis centers on its substitution of paper transactions for direct experience. "The ultimate aim of the Megalopolis is to conduct the whole of human life and intercourse through the medium of paper" (1185-1185). Education in Megalopolis exemplifies this: "it is indeed possible for a Megalopolitan student to know the atomic formula of clay without ever having seen it in the raw earth, to handle pine wood in the workshop without having walked through a pine forest" (1187-1187). Everything is mediated: "the notion of direct action, direct intercourse, direct association, is a foreign one to Megalopolis" (1191-1191).

**Cultural uniformity.** Through the combined agencies of "national education" and "national advertising," the Megalopolis persuades its inhabitants "that the good life is that which is lived, on paper, in the capital city" (1193-1193). The result is a radical homogenization: "Whereas the inhabitants of the national utopia may originally have been as diverse as the trees in a forest, they tend to become, under the influence of education and propaganda, as similar as telegraph poles along a road" (1197-1197).

**Parasitic relationship to region.** The Megalopolis does not produce its own sustenance but ensures supply "by its traffic in Coketown's multifarious goods and by its command over certain kinds of paper known as mortgages or securities" (1193-1193). "[T]he actual world of machinery is at present, it seems fair to say, a parasite upon this body of knowledge, and it would speedily starve to death if the host were annihilated" (1363-1363), Mumford writes — and the Megalopolis is the spatial embodiment of this parasitic relationship.

## Historical Context

Mumford positions the Megalopolis as the product of a specific historical development: the transformation of the [[polis-concept|Greek polis]] into something unrecognizably vast and abstract. Where the polis was bounded by its valley section and limited to a population capable of face-to-face citizenship, the Megalopolis grows without limit. Its citizens "will no longer go to London for coals, as the people in the provinces have in a sense been doing this last century and more" (1509-1509) — a reference to the centralization that erodes local autonomy.

The Megalopolis is the spatial instrument through which the [[national-state-idolum|National State idolum]] is "perpetually willed into existence" (1183-1183). Together with Coketown's industrial production and the Country House's consumer fantasy, it forms a complete system of [[idolum|idola]] that Mumford regards as a "paper purgatory" mediating between "the producer's hell" and "the consumer's Heaven" (1197-1197).

## Relation to Other Concepts

The Megalopolis is structurally linked to Mumford's broader critique of industrial civilization:

- **[[coketown-idolum|Coketown]]** produces the goods the Megalopolis distributes and consumes
- **[[country-house-idolum|Country House]]** is the consumer's paradise the Megalopolitan ultimately aspires to reach
- **[[national-state-idolum|National State]]** is the political idolum whose "chief instrument" is the Megalopolis
- **[[polis-concept|Polis]]** is the original, human-scale city that the Megalopolis has devoured and replaced
- **[[eutopia]]** and **[[regional-survey]]** represent Mumford's alternative — communities rooted in their actual regions, not in paper abstractions

Mumford's critique of the Megalopolis also anticipates his later urban theory, where the city must be understood not as a mere concentration of buildings and infrastructure but as an organ of human culture — healthy when limited in scale, parasitic when overgrown.
