# John Humphrey Noyes

## Introduction

John Humphrey Noyes (1811–1886) was an American utopian socialist, religious leader, and the founder of the [[oneida-community|Oneida Community]]. A Vermont-born theologian who developed the doctrine of Perfectionism — the belief that humans could achieve sinless perfection in this life — Noyes transformed a small Bible study group into one of the most durable and distinctive utopian experiments in American history.

## Noyes in Mumford's Analysis

[[lewis-mumford|Mumford]] mentions Noyes's Oneida Community in *The Story of Utopias* as a benchmark case of what he terms the [[partisan-utopia|partial utopia]] — a community that is "utopian only in a partial and limited sense" (007-005). For Mumford, the Oneida Community, like [[john-ruskin|John Ruskin]]'s [[guild-of-st-george|Guild of St. George]], represented a retreat from the full challenge of social reconstruction: a "little island of honest labor and sound education in the midst of the turbid sea of industrialism" rather than a genuine attempt to transform society as a whole.

## Bible Communism and Perfectionism

Noyes's theological system, which he called "Bible Communism," was rooted in the conviction that the Second Coming of Christ had already occurred in 70 CE and that believers were now called to live without sin — "perfect" in the present age. This doctrine had radical social implications: if Christians were already living in the Kingdom of God, then conventional social arrangements — private property, exclusive marriage — were relics of a fallen order. Noyes implemented this vision through three distinctive institutions at Oneida: **complex marriage** (in which every man was considered married to every woman), **mutual criticism** (a form of group therapy in which members received candid public evaluations), and **communal property** (all material goods held in common).

## The Oneida Experiment

Founded in 1848 at Oneida, New York, the community grew to approximately 300 members at its peak. It supported itself through manufacturing and agriculture, developing notable expertise in silverware production — the commercial successor to the community, Oneida Ltd., would become a major American manufacturer. Noyes led the community through three decades of continuous existence, a remarkable duration for a utopian settlement. The experiment ended in 1881 when external pressure, internal dissent over Noyes's leadership succession, and legal threats over complex marriage forced the community to reorganize as a joint-stock corporation.

## Legacy

Noyes represents a recurring tension in the utopian tradition between comprehensive social reconstruction and the creation of exemplary communities that demonstrate an ideal in microcosm. Mumford's critique — that Oneida was "utopian only in a partial and limited sense" — reflects his conviction that genuine utopianism must address the whole of society, not merely carve out an enclave. Yet Noyes's achievement in sustaining a community that put radical ideals into daily practice for over thirty years remains a significant chapter in American utopian history and a test case for the viability of communal living.
