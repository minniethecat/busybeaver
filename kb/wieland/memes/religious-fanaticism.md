# Religious Fanaticism

Religious fanaticism in *[[theodore-wieland|Wieland]]* is the conviction that one has received a direct, personal command from the Deity — a command whose divine origin places it beyond all human moral scrutiny. Shared across two generations of the [[theodore-wieland|Wieland]] family, it transforms [[Wieland-Sr|the father]]'s solitary piety into death by [[Spontaneous-Combustion|spontaneous combustion]] and [[Theodore-Wieland|the son]]'s sincere devotion into the [[Murder-of-Catharine|murder of his wife and children]]. Where [[Carwin]]'s [[Ventriloquism|ventriloquism]] provides the sensory trigger, religious fanaticism provides the interpretive framework that makes catastrophe not only possible but, in [[theodore-wieland|Theodore]]'s mind, obligatory.

## Definition

Religious fanaticism in *[[theodore-wieland|Wieland]]* is the conviction that one has received a direct, personal command from the Deity — a command whose divine origin places it beyond all human moral scrutiny. This conviction, shared across two generations of the [[theodore-wieland|Wieland]] family, transforms sincere piety into a force that destroys every natural bond: husband from wife, father from children, man from community. [[clara-wieland|Clara Wieland]] opens her narrative by declaring that her story "will exemplify the force of early impressions, and show the immeasurable evils that flow from an erroneous or imperfect discipline" (001-001). That discipline, rooted in the Camissard tradition of solitary worship and unmediated divine communication, proves to be the seed of catastrophe.

## Manifestation in Wieland

### The First Generation: Wieland Sr.

The elder [[theodore-wieland|Wieland]]'s fanaticism originates in his chance encounter with a Camissard text during his youth as a discontented London apprentice. The book "contained an exposition of the doctrine of the sect of Camissards, and an historical account of its origin" (001-010). His mind, described as "in a state peculiarly fitted for the reception of devotional sentiments" (001-010), rapidly constructs an idiosyncratic creed from scripture, reading it "through a medium which the writings of the Camissard apostle had suggested" (001-011).

The elder [[theodore-wieland|Wieland]]'s fanaticism carries distinct hallmarks that will prove hereditary: solitary worship, the primacy of duty, and the belief in personal divine commands. He builds a "temple of his Deity" on a rocky precipice overlooking the [[schuylkill-river|Schuylkill]] — "Twice in twenty-four hours he repaired hither, unaccompanied by any human being" (001-019). His religion is not merely private but anti-communal: "According to him devotion was not only a silent office, but must be performed alone" (001-017).

Crucially, the elder [[theodore-wieland|Wieland]]'s fanaticism is not merely a psychological affliction but a moral framework that reshapes his entire being. "The empire of religious duty extended itself to his looks, gestures, and phrases" (001-012). Even his neighbors, who "might call him a fanatic and a dreamer," could not deny their "veneration to his invincible candour and invariable integrity" (001-020) — a paradox central to Brown's treatment: the fanatic is not a hypocrite but someone whose sincerity makes him more dangerous.

His end is both the fulfillment and the punishment of his fanaticism. Having failed to obey an unspecified divine command, he is consumed by the belief that "his peace of mind was flown, in consequence of deviation from his duty. A command had been laid upon him, which he had delayed to perform" (001-021). He predicts that "the kind of death that awaited him was strange and terrible" (001-022). His death by apparent spontaneous combustion in the temple leaves unresolved whether it was "the penalty of disobedience" or "merely the irregular expansion of the fluid that imparts warmth to our heart" (002-020).

### The Second Generation: Theodore Wieland

Though [[theodore-wieland|Theodore]] and [[clara-wieland|Clara]] were raised "modelled by no religious standard" (003-008), free from their father's dogmatic indoctrination, the inheritance of temperament proves inescapable. [[clara-wieland|Clara]] observes "an obvious resemblance between him and my father, in their conceptions of the importance of certain topics" (003-011). [[theodore-wieland|Theodore]] is drawn to "moral necessity, and calvinistic inspiration" (003-017), while his friend [[henry-pleyel|Pleyel]] champions "intellectual liberty."

[[theodore-wieland|Theodore]]'s religious fanaticism emerges fully in his trial confession (Chapter 19), the novel's central set piece. He begins by affirming that "God is the object of my supreme passion" and that he has "burnt with ardour to approve my faith and my obedience" (019-007). His yearning for direct divine communication is explicit: "O! that I might be admitted to thy presence; that mine were the supreme delight of knowing thy will, and of performing it! The blissful privilege of direct communication with thee, and of listening to the audible enunciation of thy pleasure!" (019-015).

When that communication arrives — a vision of light and a voice commanding, "In proof of thy faith, render me thy wife. This is the victim I choose" (019-023) — [[theodore-wieland|Theodore]]'s religious psychology transforms the command into an inescapable moral imperative. The murder is framed entirely in the language of religious duty: "I brought thee hither to fulfil a divine command. I am appointed thy destroyer, and destroy thee I must" (019-059). After killing her, he exults: "It is done! My sacred duty is fulfilled! To that I have sacrificed, O my God! thy last and best gift, my wife!" (019-064).

Even facing the death sentence, [[theodore-wieland|Theodore]] refuses guilt. His final statement is a manifesto of fanatical certainty: "My deed was enjoined by heaven; ... obedience was the test of perfect virtue, and the extinction of selfishness and error" (020-009). [[clara-wieland|Clara]] recognizes that his fanaticism has placed him beyond human judgment: "none but a command from heaven could have swayed his will" (020-039).

> "'I brought thee hither to fulfil a divine command. I am appointed thy destroyer, and destroy thee I must.' Saying this I seized her wrists." (019-059)

## Related Characters

- **[[Theodore-Wieland]]** — The novel's tragic protagonist, whose inherited religious temperament makes him vulnerable to [[carwin|Carwin]]'s ventriloquism. His fanatical conviction that God commands the murder of his family transforms him from virtuous husband into killer.
- **[[Wieland-Sr]]** — [[theodore-wieland|Theodore]]'s father, whose conversion to Camissard doctrine establishes the pattern of solitary worship, obsessive duty, and belief in personal divine commands.
- **[[Carwin]]** — The biloquist whose voice-throwing provides the sensory trigger. [[carwin|Carwin]] insists he "prompted none to slay" (022-035), yet his reckless experiments demonstrate how easily a prepared religious psychology can be weaponized.
- **[[Henry-Pleyel]]** — The rationalist foil to [[theodore-wieland|Theodore]]'s religious enthusiasm. The tragedy proves that reason offers no immunity against fanaticism.
- **[[Clara-Wieland]]** — The narrator, who must grapple with whether her brother's fanaticism was madness, supernatural intervention, or the product of [[carwin|Carwin]]'s imposture on inherited predisposition.

## Literary Context

Religious fanaticism in *[[theodore-wieland|Wieland]]* operates at the intersection of three distinct cultural currents in late-eighteenth-century thought.

**The Legacy of Enthusiasm.** Brown draws on a long tradition of Protestant anxiety about "enthusiasm" — the claim to direct divine inspiration outside the mediation of scripture and church authority. The Camissards (French Prophets) were a real millenarian Protestant sect from the Cevennes whose ecstatic prophecies and claims of divine possession caused controversy across Europe. By rooting the elder [[theodore-wieland|Wieland]]'s faith in this specific tradition, Brown gives his fictional fanaticism a historically grounded genealogy.

**[[enlightenment-rationalism|Enlightenment Rationalism]] and Its Limits.** The novel stages a competition between competing epistemologies: [[theodore-wieland|Theodore]]'s faith in direct revelation, [[henry-pleyel|Pleyel]]'s Lockean empiricism, [[clara-wieland|Clara]]'s skeptical reason, and the medical materialism of [[cambridge|Mr. Cambridge]]. None fully explains the events. Brown's refusal to resolve the question — divine command? madness? imposture? — reflects a deeper Enlightenment anxiety about the limits of reason to contain religious passion.

**[[gothic-fiction|Gothic Fiction]] and the Psychology of Terror.** Unlike Radcliffe's [[gothic-fiction|Gothic novel]]s where supernatural appearances are invariably explained, Brown's religious fanaticism is Protestant, domestic, and psychological. The horror emanates not from medieval abbeys but from a Pennsylvania farmhouse and the mind of a virtuous man convinced he is doing God's will. This domestication of Gothic terror makes *[[theodore-wieland|Wieland]]* a foundational text of the American psychological Gothic tradition.

## Historical Background

The Camissard connection is not incidental. The Camisards were Huguenot rebels who fought against Louis XIV's religious persecution in the Cevennes region of France (1702-1704). Their movement was characterized by prophetic ecstasies: children and peasants would fall into trances, speak in tongues, and deliver divine messages. After defeat, some prophets traveled to England as the "French Prophets," attracting followers seeking more immediate religious experience.

The elder [[theodore-wieland|Wieland]]'s conversion through an Albigensian/Camissard book is historically plausible: such texts circulated in London during the mid-eighteenth century. The Camissard emphasis on direct divine communication and willingness to sacrifice all earthly ties for divine commands flows directly into the [[theodore-wieland|Wieland]] family's tragic inheritance.
