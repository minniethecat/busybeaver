# The Voices Begin

## Summary

The first mysterious voices are heard at [[mettingen|Mettingen]], initiating the novel's campaign of sensory deception. The voices — which appear to emanate from absent persons or empty spaces — are heard by multiple witnesses on separate occasions, lending them a terrifying credibility. [[theodore-wieland|Wieland]] hears what he believes is his wife [[catharine-wieland|Catharine]]'s voice calling from the hill, though she is elsewhere. [[henry-pleyel|Pleyel]] hears a voice that mimics his absent beloved. [[clara-wieland|Clara]] herself experiences voices in her chamber. Only the novel's conclusion reveals that all these voices were produced by [[carwin|Carwin]]'s ventriloquism, a natural skill pushed to deceptive extremes.

## Context

The voices emerge in the aftermath of the elder [[theodore-wieland|Wieland]]'s mysterious death, during a period when the [[theodore-wieland|Wieland]] and [[henry-pleyel|Pleyel]] families have established a routine of Enlightenment sociability — evening conversations at the temple, musical performances, intellectual discourse. The voices shatter this rational domesticity, introducing an element that resists empirical explanation and undermines the characters' confidence in their own senses.

## Key Participants

- **[[Clara-Wieland]]** — Hears voices in her chamber; the first-person narrator who records the events
- **[[Theodore-Wieland]]** — Hears what he believes is [[catharine-wieland|Catharine]]'s voice where she cannot be
- **[[Henry-Pleyel]]** — Hears a voice mimicking his absent beloved; his rationalism is tested
- **[[Carwin]]** — The ventriloquist producing the voices, though his role is not yet known

## Narrative Account

The first voice is heard by [[theodore-wieland|Wieland]], who reports hearing his wife [[catharine-wieland|Catharine]] calling from the hillside while she was known to be elsewhere:

> "Still the voice was Catharine's, and still it proceeded from that quarter." (004-023)

> "Perhaps a voice had been heard; but Wieland's imagination had supplied the rest." (004-026)

[[henry-pleyel|Pleyel]], the rationalist, experiences the same phenomenon and is forced to acknowledge that sensory evidence contradicts reason:

> "This is the selfsame voice which I formerly heard; you are convinced that this is not my fancy." (005-019)

## Consequences

The voices create an atmosphere of radical uncertainty that pervades the rest of the novel. Each character responds according to their disposition: [[theodore-wieland|Theodore]] moves toward supernatural explanation, [[henry-pleyel|Pleyel]] toward frustrated skepticism, [[clara-wieland|Clara]] toward psychological terror. The voices prepare the ground for [[theodore-wieland|Theodore]]'s eventual reception of what he believes to be a divine command to murder his family — when the greatest deception speaks in the voice of God, the tragedy is complete.
