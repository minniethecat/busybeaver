# Wieland Sr.

## Biography

Wieland Sr. was born in [[leipzig|Saxony]] to a noble but impoverished family. His father, a younger son, married the daughter of a [[hamburg|Hamburg]] merchant and was disowned by his relations. After the early deaths of both parents, Wieland Sr. was apprenticed to a London trader, where he spent seven years in "laborious and mechanical" service (001-011). During his apprenticeship, he discovered a volume of Camissard (French Protestant) writings that transformed his life. His conversion was total: "The empire of religious duty extended itself to his looks, gestures, and phrases" (001-012). He emigrated to Pennsylvania, purchased a farm on the [[schuylkill-river|Schuylkill River]], and built the cliffside temple where he performed solitary devotions twice daily.

## Role in the Narrative

Wieland Sr. appears only in [[clara-wieland|Clara]]'s retrospective account of the family history, but his presence haunts the entire novel. His religious fanaticism, his temple on the cliff, and his mysterious death by spontaneous combustion create the psychological and spatial template for his son [[theodore-wieland|Theodore]]'s tragedy. The elder [[theodore-wieland|Wieland]]'s conviction that "a command had been laid upon him, which he had delayed to perform" (001-021) prefigures [[theodore-wieland|Theodore]]'s belief in the divine command to murder. The father's "strange and terrible" death (001-022) opens the novel as a Gothic spectacle; the son's homicidal fanaticism closes it as a psychological horror.

## Key Relationships

- **[[Theodore-Wieland]]** — His son, who inherits his religious intensity and transforms it into murderous action
- **[[Clara-Wieland]]** — His daughter, who records his story and traces its consequences

## Significance

Wieland Sr. establishes the novel's thesis about the transmission of religious pathology across generations. His Camissard faith, with its emphasis on private revelation and solitary worship, isolates religious experience from community, tradition, and reason. This isolation is not merely personal — it is heritable, passed from father to son as a disposition toward extreme states. The novel's two-generation structure is thus its deepest argument: that the errors of the fathers are visited upon the children, not through supernatural curse but through the ordinary mechanisms of inheritance, education, and example.
