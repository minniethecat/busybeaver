# Philadelphia

## Location

Philadelphia, the largest city in late-eighteenth-century America and the temporary capital of the new republic, lies within a few miles of the [[theodore-wieland|Wieland]] estate. The elder [[theodore-wieland|Wieland]] "embarked for Philadelphia" upon arriving from England (001-015) and purchased his farm "on [[schuylkill-river|Schuylkill]], within a few miles of the city." This proximity ensures that urban civilization is always within reach — and always contrasted with the rural isolation of [[mettingen|Mettingen]].

## Significance in the Narrative

Philadelphia represents the world of Enlightenment order against which the Gothic events at [[mettingen|Mettingen]] are measured. When the elder [[theodore-wieland|Wieland]] seeks to convert Native Americans, he returns from his missionary journeys to Philadelphia; when [[clara-wieland|Clara]] is driven from the estate after the murders, she flees to the city for safety; when [[henry-pleyel|Pleyel]] requires an audience for his European travels, Philadelphia provides it.

The city's rationality stands in implicit contrast to the rural irrationality of the [[schuylkill-river|Schuylkill]] valley. If [[mettingen|Mettingen]] is where the senses are deceived and religious fanaticism destroys families, Philadelphia is where reason, commerce, and civil society supposedly prevail. Yet Brown complicates this opposition: the novel's events cannot be contained by urban rationality any more than they can be explained by rural superstition.

## Description in the Text

> "Purchasing a farm on Schuylkill, within a few miles of the city, set himself down to the cultivation of it." (001-015)

## Historical Basis

Philadelphia in the 1790s was the political and cultural capital of the early American republic. Brown himself lived there and was part of its literary circles. The city's proximity to the novel's rural setting is geographically accurate — the [[schuylkill-river|Schuylkill River]] flows into the Delaware at Philadelphia, and farms within a few miles of the city were common. Brown's use of a real American city rather than a fictionalized or European setting was part of his project to create a distinctly [[gothic-fiction|American Gothic]].
