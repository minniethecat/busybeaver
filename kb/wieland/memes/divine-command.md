# Divine Command

## Definition

Divine command in *[[theodore-wieland|Wieland]]* refers to the belief, held by [[theodore-wieland|Theodore Wieland]], that God has directly and personally instructed him to murder his wife [[catharine-wieland|Catharine]] and their children. This is not a general divine directive or scriptural principle but a specific, immediate command directed to a particular individual at a particular moment. The concept originates with the elder [[theodore-wieland|Wieland]], who believed "a command had been laid upon him, which he had delayed to perform" (001-021). [[theodore-wieland|Theodore]] inherits this theological framework and, when [[carwin|Carwin]]'s ventriloquism produces a voice that seems to originate from nowhere, interprets it as the voice of God issuing the ultimate test of obedience.

## Manifestation in Wieland

The novel traces the divine command from its paternal source to its catastrophic fulfillment. [[wieland-sr|Wieland Sr.]]'s Camissard-influenced faith taught that God communicates directly with believers, bypassing church, clergy, and scripture. This private revelation model is the theological inheritance [[theodore-wieland|Theodore]] receives. [[clara-wieland|Clara]] records that her father felt his peace of mind "was flown, in consequence of deviation from his duty" and that the "command had been laid upon him" (001-021).

[[theodore-wieland|Theodore]]'s divine command arrives in graduated stages. First comes a general divine communication: a mysterious voice and light in the temple that he interprets as supernatural affirmation. Then the specific, terrible command: "I am appointed thy destroyer, and destroy thee I will" (019-059) becomes his own voice turned against his family. [[theodore-wieland|Theodore]]'s conviction that "none but a command from heaven could have swayed my will" (020-039) demonstrates his complete subordination of moral judgment to perceived divine authority. Crucially, his response is not horror at having killed but ecstasy at having obeyed: "I thank thee, my Father, for thy patience."

## Key Passages

> "A command had been laid upon him, which he had delayed to perform. He felt as if a certain period of hesitation and reluctance had been allowed him, but that this period was passed." (001-021)

> "None but a command from heaven could have swayed my will; and, of such a command, the testimony of my senses was the only proper evidence." (020-039)

> "I am appointed thy destroyer, and destroy thee I will." (019-059)

## Related Characters

- **[[Theodore-Wieland]]** — The primary recipient of the "divine command," who murders his family in religious obedience
- **[[Carwin]]** — The ventriloquist whose voice manipulates [[theodore-wieland|Theodore]] into believing he hears God, raising the question of whether divine command is ever more than human deception

## Literary Context

The divine command motif in *[[theodore-wieland|Wieland]]* engages with the biblical story of Abraham and Isaac (Genesis 22), in which God commands the patriarch to sacrifice his son. But Brown's version is a dark inversion: where the biblical story ends with angelic intervention and a substitute ram, *[[theodore-wieland|Wieland]]* offers no reprieve. [[theodore-wieland|Theodore]] kills not only his wife (his "Isaac" figures) but all his children, and there is no voice from heaven to stay his hand. The novel thus asks whether the Abrahamic model of faith — obedience without question — is inherently murderous when applied outside biblical narrative. This radical critique of private revelation placed Brown at the center of late-Enlightenment debates about religious enthusiasm, anticipating the nineteenth-century secularization of Gothic horror.
