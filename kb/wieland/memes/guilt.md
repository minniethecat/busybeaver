# Guilt

## Definition

In Charles Brockden Brown's *[[theodore-wieland|Wieland]]; or The Transformation* (1798), guilt operates as a multi-layered concept encompassing personal remorse, legal culpability, and religious transgression. Unlike the simple moral calculus of contemporaneous sentimental fiction, guilt in *[[theodore-wieland|Wieland]]* is unstable: characters are uncertain whether their guilt originates in their own will, in divine commandment, or in the manipulation of others. This ambiguity places guilt at the center of the novel's interrogation of Enlightenment rationality and religious fanaticism.

## Manifestation in Wieland

Guilt manifests in three distinct but interconnected patterns throughout the novel:

**Paternal Guilt.** The elder [[theodore-wieland|Wieland]]'s religious melancholy is rooted in a guilt-ridden fear of having failed in his duty to God. His solitary temple, nightly vigils, and eventual death by spontaneous combustion all stem from an unarticulated sense of transgression that prefigures his son's more catastrophic guilt.

**Filial Guilt.** [[theodore-wieland|Theodore Wieland]] inherits his father's guilt-haunted temperament. After hearing what he believes to be a divine voice commanding the murder of his wife and children, [[theodore-wieland|Theodore]] initially experiences guilt as a temptation to be overcome — but after the act, guilt transforms into a conviction of righteousness. His inability to recognize his own guilt becomes the novel's most terrifying psychological portrait.

**Criminal Guilt.** [[carwin|Carwin]], the biloquist whose voice-throwing triggers [[theodore-wieland|Theodore]]'s delusion, acknowledges "the blackest and most sordid guilt" in deceiving the [[theodore-wieland|Wieland]] family. Unlike [[theodore-wieland|Theodore]], [[carwin|Carwin]] possesses full awareness of his culpability, and his confession in the novel's final chapters represents the only instance where guilt leads to accountability rather than destruction.

## Related Characters

- **[[Theodore-Wieland]]** — The novel's central figure of guilt, whose religious delusion transforms murder into an act of obedience
- **[[Carwin]]** — The biloquist whose admitted "sordid guilt" sets the tragedy in motion, and whose confession offers the only path to accountability
- **[[Catharine-Wieland]]** — The innocent victim whose murder at her husband's hands is the novel's most horrific expression of misplaced guilt
- **[[Clara-Wieland]]** — The narrator who struggles to reconcile her brother's guilt with her love for him, and who herself faces the threat of becoming his next victim

## Literary Context

Brown's treatment of guilt in *[[theodore-wieland|Wieland]]* departs significantly from the conventions of eighteenth-century Gothic and sentimental fiction. In Ann Radcliffe's novels, guilt is typically a temporary state resolved by the restoration of virtue and the punishment of villains. In *[[theodore-wieland|Wieland]]*, guilt is irresolvable: [[theodore-wieland|Theodore]] dies without ever acknowledging his culpability, [[carwin|Carwin]]'s confession provides no legal remedy, and [[clara-wieland|Clara]] is left to narrate a story in which guilt cannot be satisfactorily assigned.

The novel's exploration of guilt also engages with contemporary philosophical debates. Brown was influenced by William Godwin's *Caleb Williams* (1794), in which guilt and innocence are functions of social power rather than objective moral truth. The question that haunts *[[theodore-wieland|Wieland]]* — whether a person who sincerely believes they are obeying God can be guilty of murder — anticipates the insanity defense in modern criminal law and reflects Enlightenment anxieties about the reliability of individual conscience.
