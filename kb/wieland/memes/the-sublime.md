# The Sublime

## Definition

The sublime, as theorized by Edmund Burke in *A Philosophical Enquiry into the Origin of Our Ideas of the Sublime and Beautiful* (1757), is the aesthetic experience produced by objects or scenes that are vast, powerful, obscure, or terrifying — anything that exceeds the mind's capacity for easy comprehension and thereby produces a mingled sensation of awe and terror. In *[[theodore-wieland|Wieland]]*, the sublime operates primarily through landscape, transforming the Pennsylvania countryside into a site of aesthetic and psychological intensity equivalent to the European Gothic settings Brown's readers would have expected.

## Manifestation in Wieland

The novel's most concentrated sublime set-piece is the description of the elder [[theodore-wieland|Wieland]]'s temple: "At the distance of three hundred yards from his house, on the top of a rock whose sides were steep, rugged, and encumbered with dwarf cedars and stony asperities, he built what to a common eye would have seemed a summerhouse. The eastern verge of this precipice was sixty feet above the river which flowed at its foot. The view before it consisted of a transparent current, fluctuating and rippling in a rocky channel, and bounded by a rising scene of cornfields and orchards" (001-018).

This scene combines the classic elements of Burkean sublimity: height, ruggedness, obscurity, and the potential for danger. The temple itself — "without seat, table, or ornament of any kind" (001-019) — strips the scene of beautiful or domestic comforts, leaving only the overwhelming confrontation between the solitary worshipper and the infinite sky above the precipice. The sublime here is explicitly linked to religious experience: the elder [[theodore-wieland|Wieland]]'s solitary devotions require the overwhelming scale of natural grandeur.

The sublime also manifests in [[theodore-wieland|Theodore]]'s description of his own mental state: "his soul was tranquil and sublime" (025-021). This internalization of sublimity — transforming it from landscape feature to psychological condition — is characteristically Brownian, turning an aesthetic category into a description of extreme mental states. The novel thus suggests that the sublime is not merely an experience of nature but a condition of the mind pushed beyond its ordinary limits.

## Literary Context

Brown's use of the sublime places *[[theodore-wieland|Wieland]]* within the mainstream of late-eighteenth-century aesthetic discourse while also signaling its Americanness. Burke had argued that sublime terror requires distance — we must feel safe to enjoy the thrill of danger — and Brown employs the [[schuylkill-river|Schuylkill]] landscape to provide this distance while keeping the terror close to home. Unlike the European Gothic's medieval castles and Alpine peaks, the American sublime operates in cornfields, rivers, and farmhouses, suggesting that the new republic's very landscape could produce experiences as intense as any Old World setting. This domestication of sublimity would influence later American writers from Cooper to Melville, who similarly found Gothic amplitude in native landscapes.
