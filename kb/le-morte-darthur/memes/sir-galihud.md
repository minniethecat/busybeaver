# Sir Galihud

Sir Galihud was a knight of [[Sir Launcelot]]'s extended kindred, belonging to the network of cousins, nephews, and kinsmen who formed Launcelot's faction at the Round Table. Though a minor figure in the vast Arthurian tapestry, his appearances in the tournament catalogues and his inclusion among the ten Grail knights mark him as a knight of genuine standing within Launcelot's circle of blood and allegiance.

## Of Launcelot's Blood

Galihud appears in the roll of Launcelot's kindred who rode to the great tournament of the Castle of Maidens: "Then came Sir Launcelot du Lake with his brethren, nephews, and cousins, as Sir Lionel, Sir Ector de Maris, Sir Bors de Ganis, and Sir Galihodin, Sir Galihud, and many more of Sir Launcelot's blood" (154-007). His pairing with [[Sir Galihodin]] — presumably a brother or close kinsman — places Galihud among the recognised kin of the greatest knight in the world. To be named in this company was to be part of the most formidable martial network in Arthur's realm.

## The Tournament of Surluse

At the great tournament of Surluse, Galihud attempted to avenge his kinsman Galihodin, who had been unhorsed by [[Sir Gareth]]. Malory records: "Then Sir Galihud gat a spear to avenge his brother, and in the same wise Sir Gareth served him" (155-032). The outcome — Galihud joining his brother on the ground — speaks less to his weakness than to Gareth's extraordinary prowess. The attempt at vengeance, however, demonstrates the kinship loyalty that bound Launcelot's kin together; when one fell, another rose to take up the quarrel.

## The Grail Quest

Galihud's most significant distinction is his inclusion among the ten knights who departed from [[King Arthur]]'s court at the outset of the Grail Quest. The catalogue records: "First was Sir Launcelot, Sir Ector de Maris, Sir Bors de Ganis, and Bleoberis, and Sir Blamore de Ganis, and Lucan the Butler, Sir Uwaine, Sir Galihud Lionel, and Galiodin" (238-044). That Galihud was chosen as one of ten from the entire Round Table — a company that included Launcelot himself, [[Sir Bors de Ganis]], and [[Sir Ector de Maris]] — is a testament to his standing. The Grail Quest was the supreme spiritual and chivalric test of the age; to be numbered among its first departures was an honour reserved for knights of proven worth.
