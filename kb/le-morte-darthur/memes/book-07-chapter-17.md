[144-003]And then thus they fought till it was past noon, and never would stint, till at

[144-004]the last they lacked wind both; and then they stood wagging and scattering,

[144-005]panting, blowing and bleeding, that all that beheld them for the most part wept

[144-006]for [[mercy|pity]]. So when they had rested them a while they yede to battle again,

[144-007]tracing, racing, foining as two boars. And at some time they took their run as

[144-008]it had been two rams, and hurtled together that sometime they fell grovelling

[144-009]to the earth: and at some time they were so amazed that either took

[144-010]other’s sword instead of his own.

[144-011]Thus they endured till evensong time, that there was none that beheld them

[144-012]might know whether was like to win the battle; and their armour was so forhewn

[144-013]that men might see their naked sides; and in other places they were naked, but

[144-014]ever the naked places they did defend. And the Red Knight was a wily knight of

[144-015][[battle|war]], and his wily fighting taught Sir [[sir-gareth|Beaumains]] to be wise; but he abought it

[144-016]full sore or he did espy his fighting.

[144-017]And thus by assent of them both they granted either other to rest; and so they

[144-018]set them down upon two mole-hills there beside the fighting place, and either

[144-019]of them unlaced his helm, and took the cold wind; for either of their pages was

[144-020]fast by them, to come when they called to unlace their harness and to set them

[144-021]on again at their commandment. And then when Sir Beaumains’ helm was off,

[144-022]he looked up to the window, and there he saw the fair lady Dame Lionesse, and

[144-023]she made him such countenance that his heart waxed light and jolly; and

[144-024]therewith he bade the Red Knight of the Red Launds make him ready, and let us

[144-025]do the battle to the utterance. I will well, said the knight, and then they

[144-026]laced up their helms, and their pages avoided, and they stepped together and

[144-027]fought freshly; but the Red Knight of the Red Launds awaited him, and at an

[144-028]overthwart smote him within the hand, that his sword fell out of his hand; and

[144-029]yet he gave him another buffet upon the helm that he fell grovelling to the

[144-030]earth, and the Red Knight fell over him, for to hold him down.

[144-031]Then cried the maiden Linet on high: O Sir Beaumains, where is thy courage

[144-032]become? Alas, my lady my sister beholdeth thee, and she sobbeth and weepeth,

[144-033]that maketh mine heart heavy. When Sir Beaumains heard her say so, he abraid up

[144-034]with a great might and gat him upon his feet, and lightly he leapt to his sword

[144-035]and gripped it in his hand, and doubled his pace unto the Red Knight, and there

[144-036]they fought a new battle together. But Sir Beaumains then doubled his strokes,

[144-037]and smote so thick that he smote the sword out of his hand, and then he smote

[144-038]him upon the helm that he fell to the earth, and Sir Beaumains fell upon him,

[144-039]and unlaced his helm to have slain him; and then he yielded him and asked

[144-040]mercy, and said with a loud voice: O noble knight, I yield me to thy mercy.

[144-041]Then Sir Beaumains bethought him upon the knights that he had made to be hanged

[144-042]shamefully, and then he said: I may not with my [[honour|worship]] save thy life, for the

[144-043]shameful deaths that thou hast caused many full good knights to die. Sir, said

[144-044]the Red Knight of the Red Launds, hold your hand and ye shall know the causes

[144-045]why I put them to so shameful a death. Say on, said Sir Beaumains. Sir, I loved

[144-046]once a lady, a fair damosel, and she had her brother slain; and she said it was

[144-047][[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot du Lake]], or else [[sir-gawaine|Sir Gawaine]]; and she prayed me as that I loved

[144-048]her heartily, that I would make her a promise by the faith of my knighthood,

[144-049]for to labour daily in arms unto I met with one of them; and all that I might

[144-050]overcome I should put them unto a villainous death; and this is the cause that

[144-051]I have put all these knights to death, and so I ensured her to do all the

[144-052]villainy unto [[king-arthur|King Arthur]]’s knights, and that I should take vengeance

[144-053]upon all these knights. And, sir, now I will thee tell that every day my

[144-054]strength increaseth till noon, and all this time have I seven men’s

[144-055]strength.
