[226-001]came to a lodging where they must joust with two knights.

[226-002]Then [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]] and Sir Dinadan rode forth their way till they came to the

[226-003]shepherds and to the herdmen, and there they asked them if they knew any

[226-004]lodging or harbour there nigh hand. Forsooth, sirs, said the herdmen, hereby is

[226-005]good lodging in a castle; but there is such a custom that there shall no knight

[226-006]be harboured but if he joust with two knights, and if he be but one knight he

[226-007]must joust with two. And as ye be therein soon shall ye be matched. There is

[226-008]shrewd harbour, said Sir Dinadan; lodge where ye will, for I will not lodge

[226-009]there. Fie for shame, said Sir Tristram, are ye not a knight of the Table

[226-010]Round? wherefore ye may not with your [[honour|worship]] refuse your lodging. Not so, said

[226-011]the herdmen, for an ye be beaten and have the worse ye shall not be lodged

[226-012]there, and if ye beat them ye shall be well harboured. Ah, said Sir Dinadan,

[226-013]they are two sure knights. Then Sir Dinadan would not lodge there in no manner

[226-014]but as Sir Tristram required him of his knighthood; and so they rode thither.

[226-015]And to make short tale, Sir Tristram and Sir Dinadan smote them down both, and

[226-016]so they entered into the castle and had good cheer as they could think or

[226-017]devise.

[226-018]And when they were unarmed, and thought to be merry and in good rest, there

[226-019]came in at the gates [[sir-palomides|Sir Palomides]] and Sir Gaheris, requiring to have the

[226-020]custom of the castle. What array is this? said Sir Dinadan, I would have my

[226-021]rest. That may not be, said Sir Tristram; now must we needs defend the custom

[226-022]of this castle, insomuch as we have the better of the lords of this castle, and

[226-023]therefore, said Sir Tristram, needs must ye make you ready. In the

[226-024]devil’s name, said Sir Dinadan, came I into your [[fellowship|company]]. And so they

[226-025]made them ready; and Sir Gaheris encountered with Sir Tristram, and Sir Gaheris

[226-026]had a fall; and Sir Palomides encountered with Sir Dinadan, and Sir Dinadan had

[226-027]a fall: then was it fall for fall. So then must they [[battle|fight]] on foot. That would

[226-028]not Sir Dinadan, for he was so sore bruised of the fall that Sir Palomides gave

[226-029]him. Then Sir Tristram unlaced Sir Dinadan’s helm, and prayed him to help

[226-030]him. I will not, said Sir Dinadan, for I am sore wounded of the thirty knights

[226-031]that we had but late ago to do withal. But ye fare, said Sir Dinadan unto Sir

[226-032]Tristram, as a madman and as a man that is out of his mind that would cast

[226-033]himself away, and I may curse the time that ever I saw you, for in all the

[226-034]world are not two such knights that be so wood as is [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]] and ye Sir

[226-035]Tristram; for once I fell in the fellowship of Sir Launcelot as I have done now

[226-036]with you, and he set me a work that a quarter of a year I kept my bed. Jesu

[226-037]defend me, said Sir Dinadan, from such two knights, and specially from your

[226-038]fellowship. Then, said Sir Tristram, I will fight with them both. Then Sir

[226-039]Tristram bade them come forth both, for I will fight with you. Then Sir

[226-040]Palomides and Sir Gaheris dressed them, and smote at them both. Then Dinadan

[226-041]smote at Sir Gaheris a stroke or two, and turned from him. Nay, said Sir

[226-042]Palomides, it is too much shame for us two knights to fight with one. And then

[226-043]he did bid Sir Gaheris stand aside with that knight that hath no list to fight.

[226-044]Then they rode together and fought long, and at the last Sir Tristram doubled

[226-045]his strokes, and drove Sir Palomides aback more than three strides. And then by

[226-046]one assent Sir Gaheris and Sir Dinadan went betwixt them, and departed them

[226-047]in-sunder. And then by assent of Sir Tristram they would have lodged together.

[226-048]But Sir Dinadan would not lodge in that castle. And then he cursed the time

[226-049]that ever he came in their fellowship, and so he took his horse, and his

[226-050]harness, and departed.

[226-051]Then Sir Tristram prayed the lords of that castle to lend him a man to bring

[226-052]him to a lodging, and so they did, and overtook Sir Dinadan, and rode to their

[226-053]lodging two mile thence with a good man in a priory, and there they were well

[226-054]at ease. And that same night [[sir-bors|Sir Bors]] and Sir Bleoberis, and Sir Ector and Sir

[226-055]Driant, abode still in the same place thereas Sir Tristram fought with the

[226-056]thirty knights; and there they met with Sir Launcelot the same night, and had

[226-057]made promise to lodge with Sir Colgrevance the same night.
