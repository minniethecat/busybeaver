# Sir Dinant

Sir Dinant was a knight who appears in the latter books of [[Le Morte d'Arthur]], associated with the court of [[King Mark]] of Cornwall. His encounter with a giant in a forest clearing and his subsequent conversation with the king reveal the world of danger and intrigue that surrounded the tale of [[Sir Tristram]] and [[La Beale Isoud]].

## The Giant of the Forest

While riding through a forest, Sir Dinant came upon a fearsome giant. The giant, seeing the armed knight approach, prepared to defend its domain. Sir Dinant did not flee but stood his ground, prepared to fight the monstrous creature that dwarfed even the strongest of men. This brief episode places Dinant among those knights who faced the supernatural perils that haunted the wild places of Britain (222-032).

Before the combat could begin in earnest, another knight intervened. He rode swiftly to Sir Dinant and seized him by the arm, pulling him away from the giant. Whether this was an act of rescue or a strategic withdrawal is left ambiguous, but the episode reveals that Sir Dinant was willing to face even a giant in single combat — a mark of the courage expected of any knight in [[King Arthur]]'s world (222-036).

## The Fair Fountain

Later, Sir Dinant found himself at a fair fountain in the land of Cornwall, a place known to many knights as a trysting spot and a source of adventure. There he encountered [[King Mark]], the lord of Cornwall, who questioned him about the whereabouts of [[Sir Tristram]]. Dinant answered truthfully, directing the king to the fountain where Tristram could be found — a small but pivotal piece of information in the tangled web of pursuit and rivalry that defined the latter part of Tristram's story (223-006).

## In the Shadow of Greater Knights

Sir Dinant's role in the narrative is characteristic of the minor knights who populate Malory's world: they serve as witnesses, messengers, and occasional combatants whose brief appearances advance the stories of the greater champions. Yet each such knight carried a name, a history, and a measure of renown within his own circle. Dinant's willingness to face a giant and his presence at the court of [[King Mark]] suggest a knight of some standing in Cornwall.
