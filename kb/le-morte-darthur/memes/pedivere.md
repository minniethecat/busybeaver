# Pedivere

Pedivere's story is one of the most striking moral parables in Malory's narrative — a knight whose crime of jealous murder was transformed, through the justice of [[Sir Launcelot]] and the mercy of the Pope, into a life of penitence and holiness. His tale appears in the episode of Sir Launcelot's adventures after the healing of [[Sir Urre]], a brief but vividly rendered arc that illustrates the redemptive arc possible within the Arthurian code of honour.

## The Crime of Jealousy

Sir Launcelot encountered Pedivere in a forest, where he discovered the knight bearing the dead body of his wife across his saddle, her severed head hanging from his neck. Pedivere had killed her in a fit of jealous rage, convinced of her infidelity — a charge she denied with her dying breath. When Launcelot demanded that Pedivere fight him, the knight refused, saying "Nay, sir, said Pedivere, that will I never" (125-033). Launcelot's response was a sentence more terrible than death: Pedivere must carry the body and head to [[Queen Guenever]] at Winchester, never resting until he had delivered his gruesome burden to the queen. When asked his name, he replied "Sir, my name is Pedivere," to which Launcelot answered, "In a shameful hour wert thou born" (125-037).

## The Penance

Pedivere obeyed Launcelot's command. He "departed with the dead lady and the head, and found the queen with King Arthur at Winchester, and there he told all the truth" (125-039). Queen Guenever, hearing the tale, pronounced it "an horrible deed and a shameful, and a great rebuke unto Sir Launcelot" — for Launcelot had spared the killer's life. The queen imposed a further penance: Pedivere must journey to Rome and submit himself to the Pope's judgment. This he did, bearing his dead wife's body across the breadth of Christendom.

## The Holy Hermit

In Rome, the Pope absolved Pedivere and commanded him to return to Guenever. His wife was buried in Rome by the Pope's own commandment — an extraordinary honour for a murdered woman, conferred by the highest spiritual authority in Christendom. In the narrative's final word on Pedivere: "after this Sir Pedivere fell to great goodness, and was an holy man and an hermit" (125-050). The murderer of jealousy became a man of God, his transformation from sinner to saint completed through the layered justice of Launcelot, Guenever, and the Pope — each imposing not vengeance but a pathway to redemption.

## The Justice of Penance

Pedivere's arc follows a pattern repeated throughout Malory's work: the knight who falls into grave sin and is redeemed not through combat but through submission and suffering. Launcelot's sentence — to carry the physical evidence of his crime before the queen — is a form of public shaming, but one designed to produce repentance rather than destruction. The Pope's mercy completes the arc, transforming a story that begins with domestic murder into one that ends with sainthood. The episode serves as a quiet but forceful argument that even the worst sins can be remitted through genuine penitence, a theological principle that runs beneath the surface of the chivalric narrative.
