# King Rience

King Rience of North Wales, also king of Ireland and many isles, is the most memorably insolent antagonist of [[King Arthur]]'s early reign. Having "discomfited and overcome eleven kings, and everych of them did him homage" (029-005), Rience compels each defeated monarch to surrender his beard — "clean flayed off, as much as there was" (029-007) — and has "purfled a mantle with kings' beards" (029-008). One place on the garment remains empty, and Rience sends a messenger to demand Arthur's beard to fill it. This symbolic challenge, combining grotesque humiliation with territorial aggression, marks the moment when Arthur's kingship passes from domestic consolidation to confrontation with an external rival of genuine power.

## The Beard Mantle

Rience's messenger delivers what Arthur himself calls "the most villainous and lewdest message that ever man heard sent unto a king" (029-012). The demand is stark: Arthur's beard, flayed from his face, or Rience "would enter into his lands, and burn and slay, and never leave till he have the head and the beard" (029-010). Arthur's reply carries the cold confidence of a young king whose authority is hardening: "I owe him none homage, nor none of mine elders; but or it be long to, he shall do me homage on both his knees, or else he shall lose his head, by the faith of my body" (029-015). He adds a sardonic observation: "thou mayest see my beard is full young yet to make a purfle of it" (029-014). When a knight named Naram informs Arthur that Rience is "a passing good man of his body, as few be living, and a passing proud man" (029-021), Arthur merely replies that he "shall ordain for him in short time" (029-024).

## Capture by Balin and Balan

Rience's military campaign is formidable. He raises a great host, and tidings come to Arthur "how that the King Rience of North Wales had reared a great number of people" (032-006). [[Sir Balin]], having been banished from Arthur's court for beheading the [[Lady of the Lake]], sees Rience's defeat as his path to restoration: "I will hie me, in all the haste that I may, to meet with King Rience and destroy him, either else to die therefore; and if it may hap me to win him, then will King Arthur be my good and gracious lord" (034-038).

The capture is accomplished through [[Merlin]]'s counsel. Merlin guides Balin and his brother [[Sir Balan]] to the castle where Rience lies, and "by the counsel of Merlin, took King Rience and brought him to King Arthur" (040-001). The operation is a night raid; Rience's men "would have slain him had he not yielded" (040-014) unto Sir Balan. When Arthur confronts his captive, Rience acknowledges the reversal with grim brevity: "I came hither by an hard adventure" (040-026). Arthur's question — "Who won you?" — and its implicit answer seal Rience's fate: defeated not by the king he insulted but by the knight that king had banished.

## Aftermath

Rience's capture brings immediate consequences. His brother, Nero, raises a host to avenge him and "will set on you or noon with a great host" (040-036), leading to the great battle at Castle Terrabil. Rience himself is delivered to Arthur's court as a prisoner, his beard intact but his sovereignty extinguished. The episode crystalizes a pattern that defines Arthur's early wars: insult answered by campaign, campaign concluded by a single decisive capture that converts an enemy king into a courtly prisoner. Rience's beard-mantle — that grotesque garment of eleven royal beards — passes into memory as the emblem of a defeated world of petty kings, swept aside by the consolidating force of Arthur's rule.
