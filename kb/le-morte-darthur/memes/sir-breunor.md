# Sir Breunor

Sir Breunor was the lord of the Castle Pluere, also called the Weeping Castle. He maintained a foul custom by which any passing knight who brought a lady must fight him, with the loser's lady put to death. Sir Tristram de Liones ended this wicked practice by slaying Breunor in single combat. He is distinct from Sir Breunor le Noire, the young knight later known as La Cote Male Taile.

## The Custom of the Weeping Castle

The Castle Pluere was governed by a cruel custom: whosoever rode by that castle and brought any lady must needs fight with the lord, that hight Breunor (187-039). If Breunor won the field, the stranger knight and his lady were both put to death, whatsoever they were. If the stranger knight won, then Breunor and his lady should both die (187-040). This custom had been used many winters and had brought about the destruction of many good knights and ladies.

When Sir Tristram and La Beale Isoud arrived at the castle to repose themselves, they were taken prisoners under this custom (187-042).

## The Judgment of the Ladies

Sir Breunor came forth with his lady in his hand, muffled, and asked Sir Tristram where his lady was. The custom demanded that the ladies be compared: if Tristram's lady proved fairer than Breunor's, Breunor's lady would lose her head; if Breunor's lady was fairer, Isoud would die (188-028). Tristram declared the custom foul and horrible, saying he would rather lose his own head than his lady's (188-034).

Tristram showed La Beale Isoud, turning her thrice about with his naked sword in his hand. When Breunor beheld her, he thought he had never seen a fairer lady, and dreaded his own lady's head should be off (188-041). All the people present gave judgment that La Beale Isoud was the fairer lady. Breunor himself admitted the truth of it, though it repented him sore (188-049).

Sir Tristram took Breunor's lady from him and with an awk stroke smote off her head clean, citing the long-time wicked custom that had destroyed many good knights and ladies (188-057).

## The Combat with Sir Tristram

Enraged, Breunor mounted his horse to win Tristram's lady. They came together as it had been the thunder, and Sir Tristram smote Breunor clean from his horse (189-001). Breunor rose up lightly and ran after Tristram, who had lost his own horse when Breunor thrust it through both shoulders (189-004).

They fought mightily as two noble knights — for Breunor was a proved knight who had been the death of many good knights, such that it was pity he had so long endured (189-011). They battled nigh two hours, and either were wounded sore. At the last, Breunor rashed upon Tristram and took him in his arms, trusting much in his strength (189-015). But Tristram, called the strongest and highest knight of the world, thrust Breunor down grovelling, unlaced his helm, and struck off his head (189-019).

After Breunor's death, all those who belonged to the castle came to Tristram and did him homage and fealty, praying him to abide there to fordo that foul custom (189-024).

## Distinction from Breunor le Noire

This Sir Breunor, the lord of Castle Pluere, is a different figure from Sir Breunor le Noire, also called La Cote Male Taile, who came to Arthur's court as a young man seeking knighthood. The latter Breunor was made a Knight of the Round Table, wedded the damosel Maledisant, and proved a passing noble knight (212-013). The Breunor of Castle Pluere was slain by Tristram long before those events.
