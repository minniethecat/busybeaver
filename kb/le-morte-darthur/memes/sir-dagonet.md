# Sir Dagonet

Sir Dagonet was [[King Arthur]]'s fool, the comic figure of the Arthurian court. Though dubbed a knight for the amusement of the court, Dagonet's ineptitude in arms and his succession of humiliating misadventures provided a running counterpoint to the high chivalric seriousness of the [[Round Table]] fellowship.

## The Fool of the Court

Dagonet held the office of King Arthur's fool, a recognised position in the royal household that permitted him a licence of speech and behaviour denied to other courtiers. The chronicle refers to him consistently as "Sir Dagonet, King Arthur's fool" (206-003), a title that paradoxically elevates him to knighthood while denying him any real martial standing. In the hierarchical world of [[Le Morte d'Arthur]], Dagonet occupied a unique status: both within and outside the chivalric order.

## The Mock Knighting by Sir Kay

[[Sir Kay]] the Seneschal, known for his sharp tongue, took particular pleasure in arranging Dagonet's martial humiliations. "Then Sir Kay ordained Sir Dagonet, King Arthur's fool, to follow Sir La Cote Male Taile" (206-003), setting the fool up for a confrontation with a genuine knight. Sir Kay ensured "that Sir Dagonet was horsed and armed" (206-004), dressing the fool in knightly equipment as a cruel jest.

The inevitable outcome was swift: "Sir La Cote Male Taile smote Sir Dagonet over his horse's croup" (206-007), unhorsing the fool with a single blow. The episode exemplified the sharp division in the Arthurian world between authentic knighthood and its mockery.

## The Ducking by Sir Tristram

Dagonet's most memorable humiliation came at the hands of [[Sir Tristram]]. The chronicle records how Tristram "first soused Sir Dagonet in that well" (221-007), throwing the fool headlong into a well. This "sousing" became a byword for Dagonet's characteristic mishaps. When the fool's squires came to his rescue, "he ran thither and gat Sir Dagonet by the head" (221-043), hauling the sodden knight from the water.

These episodes of comic violence were not merely cruel — they served a narrative function. Against the high tragedy of [[Sir Launcelot]]'s forbidden love or [[Sir Balin]]'s fatal blow, Dagonet's pratfalls reminded the audience that the Arthurian world also contained laughter.

## The Warning to King Mark

Despite his comic role, Dagonet possessed a fool's privileged insight. When [[King Mark]] of Cornwall approached, Dagonet warned him with surprising clarity: "Therefore, beware, King Mark, that thou come not" (221-049) into the presence of certain knights. This moment revealed that beneath the fool's motley there lay a sharp perception — the traditional wisdom of the court fool who sees what others overlook.

## The Fool's Paradox

Sir Dagonet occupies a unique position in [[Le Morte d'Arthur]] — a knight who is no knight, a fool who sometimes speaks wisdom. His presence reminds us that Malory's chronicle, for all its high chivalric idealism, never loses touch with the comic and the human. Dagonet is the shadow-side of chivalry: the mockery that defines, by inversion, what true knighthood means.
