# Cornwall

**Cornwall** is a [[realm|kingdom]] in the far southwest of Britain, one of the [[king|sovereign]] regions that compose the [[king-arthur|Arthur]]ian political [[realm|land]]scape alongside Wales, Scot[[realm|land]], Ire[[realm|land]], and the North. In Malory's *Le Morte d'[[king-arthur|Arthur]]*, Cornwall is the domain of [[King Mark]] and the home[[realm|land]] of [[Sir Tristram]], and it serves as the stage for some of the most extended narrative sequences in the ent[[wrath|ire]] work.

## Political and Geographical Position

Cornwall first enters the narrative through the figure of the [[duke|Duke]] of Cornwall, whose wife Igraine draws the fatal des[[wrath|ire]] of [[king|King]] Uther [[dragon|Pendragon]]:

> There was a mighty duke in Cornwall that held war against him long time, and the duke was called the Duke of Tintagil. (004-004)

After Uther's death and [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s rise, Cornwall is integrated into the larger [[king-arthur|Arthur]]ian realm, though it retains its own [[kinsman|kin]]g and a measure of autonomy. The marches of Cornwall are fortified alongside those of Wales and the North as part of [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s consolidation of the [[realm|kingdom]] (021-036).

## The Truage Conflict

Cornwall's defining political crisis is the truage — a tribute demanded by [[king-arthur|the King]] of Ire[[realm|land]] that Cornwall has paid for many years. When [[king|King]] Anguish of Ire[[realm|land]] sends Sir Marhaus to collect it, the young [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]] steps for[[battle|war]]d to defend Cornwall's [[worship|honour]] in single [[battle|combat]]:

> Sir Marhaus, I pray you go into Cornwall for my sake, and do battle for our truage that of right we demand. (167-014)

The [[battle|combat]] between [[sir-tristram|Tristram]] and Marhaus is the catalyst for [[sir-tristram|Tristram]]'s ent[[wrath|ire]] career as a knight and for the intertwined destinies of Cornwall and Ire[[realm|land]]. The men of Cornwall rally behind their champion:

> Then they of Cornwall let make cries in every place, that what knight would fight for to deliver them of the truage of Ireland, that he should fare the better all the days of his life. (167-032)

## Cornwall Under King Mark

[[king-mark|King Mark]]'s rule over Cornwall is a study in weakness and [[treason|treachery]]. Though he owes his throne to [[sir-tristram|Tristram]]'s victories, Mark repeatedly betrays his nephew, earning the contempt of the [[round-table|Round Table]]. Cornwall under Mark becomes a place from which the hero must repeatedly flee, a [[realm|kingdom]] whose [[king|sovereign]] is unworthy of the knights who defend it:

> Thus Sir Tristram endured in Cornwall until he was big and strong, and he was well cherished by the king and by the barons. (166-022)

The irony of "endured" speaks volumes: Cornwall is home, but it is also a place to be suffered.

## Role in the Arthurian World

Cornwall is one of the five great di[[dream|vision]]s of [[king-arthur|Arthur]]ian Britain, and its knights appear in the great musters of the realm:

> In Cornwall and in the west were two kings, and in Wales two kings, and in the North many kings. (164-009)

At [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s great feast, the knights of Cornwall answer the call alongside the chivalry of Wales, Scot[[realm|land]], and Ire[[realm|land]] (153-076). Cornwall is thus neither a marginal province nor a mere backdrop — it is a [[realm|kingdom]] whose internal drama (the t[[wrath|rage]]dy of [[sir-tristram|Tristram]] and Mark) runs parallel to and eventually intersects with the central [[king-arthur|Arthur]]ian t[[wrath|rage]]dy.
