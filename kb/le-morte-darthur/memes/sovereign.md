**Sovereign** in [[Le Morte d'Arthur]] names the highest form of political authority—the [[baron|lord]] to whom all others owe [[fealty]] and from whom all legitimate power descends. [[King Arthur]] is the [[king|sovereign]] [[baron|lord]] of Eng[[realm|land]], and his claim to sovereignty extends beyond his borders to encompass an imperial [[dream|vision]] of rule. But sovereignty in Malory is not exclusively political: in the language of courtly [[love]], the beloved lady is the knight's "[[king|sovereign]] lady," exercising a personal dominion that mirrors and sometimes challenges the political sovereignty of [[kinsman|kin]]gs.

## The Sovereign Lord

[[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s sovereignty is declared from the moment of his coronation. His [[baron|lord]]s and counsellors address him as "our [[kinsman|kin]]g and [[king|sovereign]] [[baron|lord]]," a formula that combines regnal title with the feudal claim to paramount authority:

> this is my counsel, that our king and sovereign lord send unto the (013-024)

The phrase "[[king|sovereign]] [[baron|lord]]" is not merely [[honour|honor]]ific. It asserts a specific feudal relationship: all other [[baron|lord]]s hold their [[realm|land]]s and titles from the [[king|sovereign]], and all oaths of fealty ultimately terminate in him. When [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s authority is challenged—by rebellious [[kinsman|kin]]gs in the early books, by [[Sir Launcelot]] in the later ones—the challenge is framed as a repudiation of sovereignty itself.

## Imperial Sovereignty

[[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s sovereignty asp[[wrath|ire]]s beyond Eng[[realm|land]]. After his victories over the rebel [[kinsman|kin]]gs, he claims an imperial dignity that places him among the great rulers of the age. The language of imperial sovereignty runs through the Morte:

> I pretend to have and occupy the sovereignty of the empire (097-021)

This is Lucius the Roman emperor spea[[kinsman|kin]]g, but his claim mirrors [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s own pretensions. The medieval conception of [[king|kingship]] as a hierarchy of [[baron|lord]]ships culminating in a universal [[king|sovereign]] shapes the political imagination of the ent[[wrath|ire]] work. [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s [[Round Table]] is itself a microcosm of universal sovereignty—a gathering of [[kinsman|kin]]gs and knights from many [[realm|land]]s under one paramount [[baron|lord]].

## The Sovereign Lady

Alongside political sovereignty, Malory develops a parallel language of personal sovereignty in the realm of love. The knight who loves chooses a lady as his "[[king|sovereign]] lady," owing her a devotion that mimics the feudal bond:

> And so he chose her for his sovereign lady, and never to love other but her (087-003)

The language is explicitly feudal—the knight "chooses" his lady as a vassal might choose a [[baron|lord]], and the bond is exclusive and lifelong. Sir [[Sir Tristram|Tristram]]'s devotion to La Beale Isoud, Sir [[Sir Launcelot|Launcelot]]'s to [[Queen Guenever]]—these are relationships of personal sovereignty that rival and ultimately undermine political sovereignty:

> dead, for my sovereign lady gave thee to me, and evil have I kept thee (057-047)

When [[sir-launcelot|Launcelot]] must choose between his [[king|sovereign]] [[baron|lord]] [[king-arthur|Arthur]] and his [[king|sovereign]] lady [[queen-guenever|Guenever]], the contradiction between the two forms of sovereignty becomes catastrophic for the [[realm|kingdom]].

## Sovereignty and the Statutes

The tension between [[king|sovereign]] will and established law is a recurring concern. The [[king|sovereign]] is the source of law, yet is expected to govern according to "the statutes and decrees":

> sovereign, withholdest and retainest contrary to the statutes and decrees made (096-014)

This is the language of baronial opposition—the claim that the [[king|sovereign]] has exceeded his lawful authority. In the later books of the Morte, as [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s sovereignty fractures under the pressure of the [[sir-launcelot|Launcelot]]-[[queen-guenever|Guenever]] affair and the rebellion of Sir [[Sir Mordred|Mordred]], this tension between [[king|sovereign]] prerogative and legal constraint becomes the engine of t[[wrath|rage]]dy. The [[king|sovereign]] who makes the law is also bound by it, and when he breaks it, the ent[[wrath|ire]] edifice of order begins to crumble.
