# Miracle

The **[[marvel|miracle]]** in [[Le Morte d'Arthur]] is the visible sign of divine intervention in the human world — the event that cannot be explained by natural causes and that therefore attests to [[God]]'s active presence in the narrative. Malory deploys [[marvel|miracle]]s sparingly but at moments of the highest narrative significance: the election of a [[king]], the proof of a [[knight]]'s sanctity, the vindication of a cause.

## The Miracle of the Sword

The paradigmatic [[marvel|miracle]] in Malory's work is the sword in the stone, by which [[King Arthur]] is elected [[kinsman|kin]]g:

> with the sword that ye had by miracle, till that ye receive your right (012-016)

The sword is "had by [[marvel|miracle]]" — not won, not inherited, but given by divine agency. This miraculous origin sets [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s [[kingship]] apart from ordinary succession. [[king-arthur|Arthur]] is not [[kinsman|kin]]g because he is Uther's son (though he is) but because God has chosen him, and the [[marvel|miracle]] of the sword is the public proof of that election.

The word "[[marvel|miracle]]" here carries its full theological weight. It is not a marvel or a [[marvel|wonder]] — words Malory uses for the merely extraordinary — but a *[[marvel|miracle]]*, an event that can only be explained by divine action. The distinction matters: marvels belong to the world of [[enchantment]] and [[adventure]]; [[marvel|miracle]]s belong to the world of [[providence]] and [[grace]].

## Miracle as Coronation

The [[marvel|miracle]] of [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s [[king|kingship]] is explicitly framed as a divine act of installation:

> as he was come to be king of all England by adventure and by miracle (008-008)

"By adventure and by [[marvel|miracle]]" — the pairing is instructive. [[Adventure]] is the ordinary mechanism of chivalric advancement; [[marvel|miracle]] is its divine counterpart. [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s path to the throne is both a human story (the adventure of the unknown boy who draws the sword) and a divine story (the [[marvel|miracle]] by which God installs His chosen [[kinsman|kin]]g). The two narratives do not compete; they reinforce each other.

## Miracles and Sanctity

Malory also deploys [[marvel|miracle]] to attest to personal sanctity. When a [[knight]] or holy figure performs or receives a miraculous intervention, it confirms their special relationship with God:

> she did all that she might, and so by miracle of Our Lord she was saved (164-025)

The phrase "by [[marvel|miracle]] of Our Lord" attributes the event d[[wrath|ire]]ctly to divine agency rather than to any human power. The [[marvel|miracle]] certifies the subject's [[worship|worthiness]] — God intervenes for those He favours — and it functions as a narrative endorsement of the character's moral standing.

## Miracle and Marvel

Malory distinguishes between [[marvel|miracle]] and [[marvel]], though the boundary can blur. A marvel is an extraordinary event that may or may not have a divine origin — the [[dragon]] in the sky, the strange [[beast]], the mysterious ship. A [[marvel|miracle]] is explicitly attributed to God. The difference is not in the event's strangeness but in its source:

| Feature | Miracle | [[marvel|Marvel]] |
|---------|---------|--------|
| Source | God / divine agency | Unknown / natural / [[enchantment|magic]]al |
| Function | Attests to sanctity or election | Drives adventure / quest |
| Narrative register | Theological | Chivalric-romance |
| Example | [[sword|Sword]] in the stone | The [[questing-beast|Questing Beast]] |

## Miracle's Narrative Function

Miracle in Malory is the ultimate narrative authority. When a [[marvel|miracle]] occurs, all doubt is resolved — the event carries divine [[battle|war]]rant. This is why [[marvel|miracle]]s cluster at moments of crisis in the legitimacy of [[king|kingship]] or the sanctity of a knight. The [[marvel|miracle]] closes the argument: God has spoken, and human judgment must yield.
