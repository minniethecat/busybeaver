# Sir Sadok

Sir Sadok was a knight of the Arthurian world who appears in the later books of [[Le Morte d'Arthur]] as a companion of [[Sir Tristram]]. His most notable recorded deed is an encounter with [[Sir Petipase]] at the Tournament of the Castle of Maidens, where he was discomfited in the lists.

## The Companion of Sir Tristram

The chronicle first names Sadok in connection with the great knight of Cornwall. In a roll-call of companions, the text mentions "the Seneschal, and Sir Sadok; but this Sir Tristram was not at that time knight" (153-077). This brief notice places Sadok in [[Sir Tristram]]'s world — a knight of the Cornish circle, present in the company of Tristram's associates before the hero himself had achieved his full stature.

The association with Tristram is significant. Tristram's circle included many knights who fought in his tournaments and followed his banner, and Sadok's presence among them suggests a knight of sufficient standing to move in the orbit of one of the greatest warriors of the age.

## The Tournament of the Castle of Maidens

Sadok's moment of greatest narrative prominence comes at the Tournament of the Castle of Maidens, one of the grand chivalric gatherings of the latter books. The chronicle records his entry: "Then came in Sir Tristram, Sir Sadok, and" others (155-012), listing him among the party that took the field under Tristram's leadership.

To ride into a tournament in the company of Sir Tristram was itself a mark of distinction. The Castle of Maidens tournament drew knights from across Britain, and Sadok's place in Tristram's party placed him at the centre of the action.

## The Encounter with Sir Petipase

In the lists, Sadok met [[Sir Petipase]] of Winchelsea. The chronicle records the result with characteristic terseness: "Sir Sadok encountered with Sir Petipase, and there Sir Sadok was discomfited" (155-015). The lance of Petipase proved the stronger, and Sadok joined the ranks of knights who took their falls in the tournament's opening exchanges.

"Discomfited" — unhorsed, defeated, driven from the field — was the fate of many knights in Malory's tournament narratives. That Sadok fell to Petipase rather than to a more famous opponent places him in the middle rank of Arthurian warriors: a knight who could ride with Tristram but could not always prevail in the lists.

## The Knight in the Company

Sir Sadok exemplifies the knights who give the Arthurian world its depth. He is not a hero whose deeds fill chapters, nor a villain whose treachery drives the plot. He is a knight of the company — one who rides with the great, takes his turn in the lists, and receives from the chronicle the only gift it can bestow on the minor: a name, a single deed, and the honour of having been there. In the crowded world of [[Le Morte d'Arthur]], where hundreds of knights cross the stage, Sadok's brief appearances remind us that the [[Round Table]] was not only its champions but also its company — the knights who filled the ranks, broke their spears, and lent their presence to the great gatherings that defined the age of Arthur.
