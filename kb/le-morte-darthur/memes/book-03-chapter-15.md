[065-002]And therewith they departed, and came there as the head of the lady lay with a

[065-003]fair yellow hair that grieved King Pellinore passingly sore when he looked on

[065-004]it, for much he cast his heart on the visage. And so by noon they came to

[065-005][[camelot|Camelot]]; and the king and the queen were passing fain of his coming to the

[065-006]court. And there he was made to swear upon the Four Evangelists, to tell the

[065-007]truth of his quest from the one to the other. Ah! Sir Pellinore, said Queen

[065-008][[queen-guenever|Guenever]], ye were greatly to blame that ye saved not this lady’s life.

[065-009]Madam, said Pellinore, ye were greatly to blame an ye would not save your own

[065-010]life an ye might, but, save your pleasure, I was so furious in my quest that I

[065-011]would not abide, and that repenteth me, and shall the days of my life. Truly,

[065-012]said [[merlin|Merlin]], ye ought sore to repent it, for that lady was your own daughter

[065-013]begotten on the lady of the Rule, and that knight that was dead was her love,

[065-014]and should have wedded her, and he was a right good knight of a young man, and

[065-015]would have proved a good man, and to this court was he coming, and his name was

[065-016]Sir Miles of the Launds, and a knight came behind him and slew him with a

[065-017]spear, and his name is Loraine le Savage, a false knight and a coward; and she

[065-018]for great sorrow and dole slew herself with his sword, and her name was Eleine.

[065-019]And because ye would not abide and help her, ye shall see your best friend fail

[065-020]you when ye be in the greatest distress that ever ye were or shall be. And that

[065-021]penance God hath ordained you for that deed, that he that ye shall most trust

[065-022]to of any man alive, he shall leave you there ye shall be slain. Me

[065-023]forthinketh, said King Pellinore, that this shall me betide, but God may fordo

[065-024]well destiny.

[065-025]Thus, when the quest was done of the white hart, the which followed Sir

[065-026][[sir-gawaine|Gawaine]]; and the quest of the brachet, followed of Sir Tor, Pellinore’s

[065-027]son; and the quest of the lady that the knight took away, the which King

[065-028]Pellinore at that time followed; then the king stablished all his knights, and

[065-029]them that were of lands not rich he gave them lands, and charged them never to

[065-030]do outrageousity nor murder, and always to flee treason; also, by no means to

[065-031]be cruel, but to give mercy unto him that asketh mercy, upon pain of forfeiture

[065-032]of their [[honour|worship]] and lordship of [[king-arthur|King Arthur]] for evermore; and always to do

[065-033]ladies, damosels, and gentlewomen succour, upon pain of death. Also, that no

[065-034]man take no battles in a wrongful quarrel for no law, nor for no world’s

[065-035]goods. Unto this were all the knights sworn of the [[round-table|Table Round]], both old and

[065-036]young. And every year were they sworn at the high feast of Pentecost.
