# Lordship

**Lordship** in [[Le Morte d'Arthur]] is the feudal bond that binds knight to [[kinsman|kin]]g, vassal to [[sovereign]], and man to master. It is the fun[[lady|dame]]ntal political relationship in Malory's world — a reciprocal obligation of protection in exchange for service, of [[worship]] in exchange for [[fealty]]. When a knight speaks of "my good [[baron|lord]]ship," he names the bond that defines his place in the chivalric order.

## The Reciprocal Bond

Lordship is never one-sided. The [[baron|lord]] owes protection and [[honour]]; the vassal owes service and counsel. This reciprocity is explicit in the language Malory's characters use:

> I thank you of your good lordship that I have had with you here (175-018)

The phrase "good [[baron|lord]]ship" names not merely the legal relationship but the quality with which the [[baron|lord]] has discharged his obligations. A [[baron|lord]] who fails to protect his men forfeits his claim to their service; a vassal who fails to serve forfeits his claim to protection. The bond is conditional on both sides, and its breach is the material of [[treason]].

## Lordship and Territory

The word "[[baron|lord]]ship" in Malory also denotes the territory over which a [[baron|lord]] rules:

> the estates and commons of that lordship were there ready to behold their new lord (188-026)

A [[baron|lord]]ship is a concrete political unit — [[realm|land]], people, and obligation bound together. When [[King Arthur]] demands that the Roman emperor "acknowledge him for his [[baron|lord]]" (164-012), he is claiming [[baron|lord]]ship not as a personal tie but as a territorial [[sovereign|sovereignty]] encompassing all of Britain and beyond:

> lordships unto Rome (164-012)

The plural "[[baron|lord]]ships" reflects the composite nature of medieval [[sovereign|sovereignty]]: a [[kinsman|kin]]g holds many [[baron|lord]]ships, each with its own customs and obligations, knit together by the person of the [[kinsman|kin]]g.

## Lordship as Worship

The bond of [[baron|lord]]ship is inseparable from the concept of [[worship]] — the public [[honour]] that accrues to both [[baron|lord]] and vassal through faithful service:

> of their worship and lordship of King Arthur for evermore (065-032)

Here "[[honour|worship]] and [[baron|lord]]ship" are pa[[wrath|ire]]d as inseparable goods. To acknowledge [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s [[baron|lord]]ship is to participate in his [[honour|worship]]; to rebel against his [[baron|lord]]ship is to diminish one's own [[honour|worship]]. The political and the moral are not distinct categories — [[baron|lord]]ship is an ethical condition as much as a legal one.

## The Language of Lordship

Malory's characters invoke [[baron|lord]]ship in three distinct registers:

> that would please your lordship, and to-morrow ye shall have your desire (040-020)

The courteous address — "your [[baron|lord]]ship" — acknowledges the [[baron|lord]]'s authority while framing a request within the bond's reciprocal expectations. The speaker defers to the [[baron|lord]]'s pleasure, but the deferral itself is a claim: the [[baron|lord]] who is pleased ought to grant the request.

> have my love and my lordship in the uttermost wise that may be (161-020)

Here [[baron|lord]]ship is offered as a gift alongside love. The phrase captures the dual nature of the bond — personal affection ("my love") and political obligation ("my [[baron|lord]]ship") in a single breath.

> for the good lordship ye showed me in Ireland, and for the good lordship your father showed me (184-041)

Lordship is heritable and cumulative. The "good [[baron|lord]]ship" of a father creates obligations that extend to the son, and the memory of past [[baron|lord]]ship shapes present [[fealty|allegiance]]. This is the temporal depth of the feudal world — bonds that cross generations.

## Lordship's Narrative Function

Lordship is the gravity that holds Malory's political universe together. Every alliance, every rebellion, every transfer of [[fealty|allegiance]] is a story about [[baron|lord]]ship. The [[Round Table]] itself is an extension of [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s [[baron|lord]]ship — a [[fellowship|company]] of knights bound to the [[kinsman|kin]]g not merely by oath but by the reciprocal obligations that [[baron|lord]]ship entails. When [[baron|lord]]ship fails — when a [[baron|lord]] proves unworthy or a vassal unfaithful — the ent[[wrath|ire]] political order trembles.
