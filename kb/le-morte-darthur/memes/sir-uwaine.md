# Sir Uwaine

Sir Uwaine is the son of [[King Uriens]] of Gore and [[Morgan le Fay]], and the nephew of [[King Arthur]] through his mother. He is most remembered for one decisive act: preventing his mother from murdering his sleeping father — an intervention that reveals both the darkness within Arthur's own family and Uwaine's instinct for justice over blood loyalty. As a companion of his cousin [[Sir Gawaine]], he rides quest alongside [[Sir Marhaus]] and participates in the three-damosel adventure at the fountain of Arroy, proving himself a knight of sound judgment if modest martial prowess.

## The Saving of King Uriens

Uwaine's defining moment occurs while Morgan le Fay believes King Arthur to be dead. Seeing her husband King Uriens lying asleep, Morgan calls for a maiden to "fetch me my lord's sword, for I saw never better time to slay him than now" (079-004). The terrified maiden instead wakes Sir Uwaine, warning him: "Arise, and wait on my lady your mother, for she will slay the king your father sleeping in his bed" (079-010).

Uwaine's response is swift and composed: "Well, go on your way, and let me deal" (079-011). As Morgan lifts the sword to strike, "Sir Uwaine leapt unto his mother, and caught her by the hand, and said, Ah, fiend, what wilt thou do? An thou wert not my mother, with this sword I should smite off thy head" (079-015). His words carry the weight of a man caught between filial duty and moral clarity: "men saith that Merlin was begotten of a devil, but I may say an earthly devil bare me" (079-018). Morgan begs for mercy, blaming a devil's temptation, and Uwaine grants her pardon on the condition that she "never be about to do such deeds" (079-022) — a covenant of silence that preserves his mother's honor while securing his father's life.

## Banishment from Court

Despite Uwaine's discretion, Morgan's broader treachery — the theft of Excalibur's scabbard — is soon discovered. When [[King Arthur]] pursues Morgan, Uwaine's loyalty to his mother places him in an impossible position. Arthur, learning that Uwaine rode with Morgan, declares: "as for your son, Sir Uwaine, I love him passing well, but as for you I love not, for ye and your son Uwaine were of accord for to destroy me" (082-018). Uwaine is put out of Arthur's court alongside his mother, though the king's words suggest personal affection beneath the political necessity.

## The Joust with Sir Marhaus

Riding quest with Sir Gawaine, Uwaine encounters [[Sir Marhaus]], a formidable knight of the Round Table. When Gawaine insists on testing Marhaus despite Uwaine's caution that "he is a passing good knight as any is living; I would not by my will that any of us were matched with him" (084-010), Uwaine volunteers to joust first, reasoning: "I am more weaker than ye, and if he smite me down then may ye revenge me" (084-013). The joust confirms his self-assessment: "Sir Uwaine smote Sir Marhaus that his spear brast in pieces on the shield, and Sir Marhaus smote him so sore that horse and man he bare to the earth, and hurt Sir Uwaine on the left side" (084-015). Though defeated, Uwaine's willingness to test the unknown knight before his stronger cousin demonstrates a tactical humility rare among Arthur's knights. After the combat, all three — Uwaine, Gawaine, and Marhaus — become sworn brethren and sojourn together at Marhaus's priory.

## The Three Damosels of Arroy

In the forest of Arroy, the three companion knights come upon a fountain where three damosels sit: one of sixty winters with white hair, one of thirty, and one of fifteen. The damosels propose that each knight choose one as guide, with a vow to return to the fountain after a twelvemonth. Uwaine, describing himself as "the youngest and most weakest of you both" (085-002), deliberately chooses the eldest damosel: "for she hath seen much, and can best help me when I have need, for I have most need of help of you both" (085-004). His choice reflects the same practical wisdom that marked his handling of Morgan: an understanding that brute strength is not always the surest path through peril. Uwaine takes the western way with his aged guide, his subsequent adventures left untold in the surviving narrative.

## A Knight of Judgment

Sir Uwaine occupies a distinctive niche among Arthur's knights. He is not among the six who overmatch Gawaine — the chronicle pointedly records that [[Sir Launcelot]], [[Sir Tristram]], [[Sir Bors de Ganis]], [[Sir Percivale]], [[Sir Pelleas]], and Sir Marhaus "had the better of Sir Gawaine" (084-057), and Uwaine is absent from that list. Yet what he lacks in martial supremacy he supplies in judgment: the restraint to spare his murderous mother, the wisdom to choose the aged guide over the fairest damosel, the honest self-appraisal that sends him into combat knowing he may lose. In a court of heroes defined by their capacity for violence and passion, Uwaine's defining virtue is an uncommon prudence.
