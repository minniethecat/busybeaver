# King Anguish

**King Anguish** is the King of Ireland, father of [[La Beale Isoud]], and a central figure in the early chapters of [[Sir Tristram]]'s story. His court in Ireland serves as the stage for Tristram's first great deeds: defeating [[Sir Marhaus]] to free Cornwall from truage, and later winning the tournament disguised as Tramtrist. Anguish also appears at [[King Arthur]]'s court when summoned to answer accusations, where Tristram champions his cause.

## Role in Cornwall's Truage

King Anguish held a claim of truage (tribute) over Cornwall, a dominion of [[King Mark]]. When the tribute went unpaid, Anguish sent Sir Marhaus — his brother-in-law and champion — to enforce it. The messenger delivered the demand bluntly: "tell King Anguish, and tell him we will pay him" (167-006). When Anguish received Cornwall's defiant reply, he understood that the matter would be resolved by combat (167-010). Marhaus was the knight who rode to Cornwall to fight the champion of King Mark on Anguish's behalf, a battle that would draw Tristram into the great cycle of love, honour, and tragedy.

## The Tournament and Tramtrist

King Anguish proclaimed a great jousts and tournament in Ireland, an event that drew knights from across the land (172-016). Among the competitors was a young knight calling himself **Tramtrist** — in truth Sir Tristram, concealing his identity because he had slain Sir Marhaus in the truage duel. Tristram's prowess in the field caught Anguish's attention, and the King came to him personally to inquire about his lineage and purpose (172-044). Unaware that Tramtrist was the slayer of Marhaus, Anguish received him with honour at his court.

## Family and the Discovery of Marhaus's Death

The peace of Anguish's household was broken when La Beale Isoud's mother — Anguish's queen — discovered that Tramtrist was Tristram, the knight who had killed her brother Marhaus. She "ran to the King Anguish, her father" in anguish over the revelation (174-026). Anguish, hearing the accusation, demanded at once: "Who is that, and where is he?" (174-029). The discovery placed Tristram in mortal danger within Anguish's own hall, a turning point that tested the bonds of hospitality and honour.

## Summons to Arthur's Court

Later in the narrative, King Anguish was summoned to appear before [[King Arthur]]'s court to face accusations (183-003, 184-013). He arrived at Camelot to meet his accusers (183-010) and was deeply troubled by the charges: "So when King Anguish understood his accusing he was abashed" (183-020). Word of his plight reached Tristram through Gouvernail (184-001). Tristram, whose loyalty Anguish had earned in Ireland, intervened on the King's behalf — Gouvernail went to Anguish and saluted him in Tristram's name (184-020).

The dispute was ultimately resolved when "the two brethren were accorded with King Anguish" (186-036), and Anguish, grateful for Tristram's aid, swore to reward him: "So God me help, I will for your sake" (186-022). With the matter settled, "King Anguish and Sir Tristram took their leave" and departed together (186-041), cementing the bond between the King of Ireland and the knight of Cornwall.
