# Counsel

**Counsel** in Malory's "Le Morte d'[[king-arthur|Arthur]]" is the [[custom|practice]] of [[quest|seeking]] and giving advice that structures political and personal decision-ma[[kinsman|kin]]g throughout the narrative. Good counsel sustains the realm; bad or rejected counsel precipitates catastrophe. The giving of counsel is tied to specific institutional forms — the [[kinsman|kin]]g's council of [[baron|barons]], the private counsel of [[merlin|Merlin]], the advice of hermits and [[maiden|damsel]]s — but its fun[[lady|dame]]ntal function is constant: to align human action with wisdom beyond individual judgment.

## Counsel and Kingship

The obligation to seek and heed counsel is a defining feature of good [[king|kingship]] in Malory. [[King Arthur]] is praised when he rules "by the counsel of [[merlin|Merlin]]" (052-007) and imperiled when he acts without it. The young [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s establishment of his reign proceeds through repeated acts of counsel-ta[[kinsman|kin]]g:

> "the Archbishop, by the advice of Merlin, sent for all the lords and gentlemen of arms" (008-009)

The pattern recurs throughout [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s reign: the [[kinsman|kin]]g assembles his [[baron|barons]], seeks their advice, and acts upon it. When counsel is bypassed — as in [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s hasty judgment of [[Guenever]] or his prosecution of [[battle|war]] against [[Sir Launcelot]] — the consequences are catastrophic.

## The Counsel of Merlin

[[merlin|Merlin]] serves as the archetypal counsellor, providing advice that is both politically shrewd and prophetically informed. His counsel shapes the major decisions of [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s early reign: the [[battle|war]] against the rebel [[kinsman|kin]]gs, the marriage to [[queen-guenever|Guenever]], the establishment of the [[Round Table]]:

> "by Merlin's advice there were sent fore-riders to skim the country" (016-002)

[[merlin|Merlin]]'s counsel is distinctive in being both freely given and unerringly correct — a combination that makes his eventual disappearance from the narrative a structural turning point. After [[merlin|Merlin]] departs, [[king-arthur|Arthur]] must rely on fallible human counsel, and the quality of political decision-ma[[kinsman|kin]]g visibly declines.

## The Baronial Council

The baronial council is the institutional form of counsel in Malory's political world. Major decisions — [[battle|war]], peace, marriage, judgment — are made "in council" with the assembled [[baron|lord]]s:

> "the king let call his barons to council" (013-003)

The council functions as a check on royal will: the [[kinsman|kin]]g "asked counsel at them all" (013-005) and is expected to follow their collective advice. When [[baron|barons]] "could no counsel give" (013-006), [[merlin|Merlin]] steps in with [[marvel|supernatural]] wisdom, but the institutional norm is deliberation among equals. The council at Rome (096-022) extends this principle to imperial governance.

## Counsel Rejected

The rejection of good counsel is one of Malory's most persistent tragic patterns. Characters who refuse advice — whether from pride, passion, or folly — invariably suffer the [[battle|war]]ned-of consequences. Sir Balin ignores [[merlin|Merlin]]'s counsel and precipitates the Dolorous Stroke. [[King Mark]] calls his council "and asked advice" (195-027) only to reject it in favour of [[treason|treachery]]. The pattern is so consistent that it functions as a narrative law: counsel spurned is disaster earned.

## Counsel from Unexpected Sources

Not all counsel in Malory comes from [[baron|lord]]s and sages. [[damosel|Damsel]]s give counsel that knights ignore at their peril ("Do by my counsel" — 057-016). [[hermit|Hermit]]s provide spiritual counsel to questing knights. Even opponents offer advice that proves true. The diversity of counsel's sources reflects a world in which wisdom is distributed across the social order and cannot be monopolized by rank:

> "Sir, she said, that me repenteth, but an ye will be ruled by my counsel" (112-004)

The knight who refuses counsel because of its source — dismissing a [[maiden|damsel]]'s [[battle|war]]ning or a hermit's admonition — learns through suffering what deliberation would have taught without cost.

## Counsel's Narrative Function

Counsel scenes serve as narrative hinges: moments of deliberation where the available futures are laid out and a choice is made. They provide the narrative with its moments of moral clarity, where characters explicitly weigh consequences. The increasing scarcity of good counsel as the work progresses — [[merlin|Merlin]] gone, the [[round-table|Round Table]] fractured, [[baron|barons]] divided by faction — mirrors the progressive darkening of the political world. The t[[wrath|rage]]dy of "Le Morte d'[[king-arthur|Arthur]]" is in part a t[[wrath|rage]]dy of counsel: when the institutions of good advice collapse, disaster becomes inevitable.
