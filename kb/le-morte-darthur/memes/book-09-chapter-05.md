[208-001]and heard of La Cote Male Taile, and how he followed after him, and how La Cote

[208-002]Male Taile was prisoner.

[208-003]That when he was come to [[camelot|the court of King Arthur]], then heard he tell of the

[208-004]young knight La Cote Male Taile, how he slew the lion, and how he took upon him

[208-005]the adventure of the black shield, the which was named at that time the

[208-006]hardiest adventure of the world. So God me save, said [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]] unto many

[208-007]of his fellows, it was shame to all the noble knights to suffer such a young

[208-008]knight to take such adventure upon him for his destruction; for I will that ye

[208-009]wit, said Sir Launcelot, that that damosel Maledisant hath borne that shield

[208-010]many a day for to seek the most proved knights, and that was she that Breuse

[208-011]Saunce Pité took that shield from her, and after [[sir-tristram|Tristram]] de Liones rescued

[208-012]that shield from him and gave it to the damosel again, a little afore that time

[208-013]that Sir Tristram fought with my nephew Sir Blamore de Ganis, for a quarrel

[208-014]that was betwixt [[king-arthur|the King]] of Ireland and him. Then many knights were sorry that

[208-015]Sir La Cote Male Taile was gone forth to that adventure. Truly, said Sir

[208-016]Launcelot, I cast me to ride after him. And within seven days Sir Launcelot

[208-017]overtook La Cote Male Taile, and then he saluted him and the damosel

[208-018]Maledisant. And when [[mordred|Sir Mordred]] saw Sir Launcelot, then he left their

[208-019]fellowship; and so Sir Launcelot rode with them all a day, and ever that

[208-020]damosel rebuked La Cote Male Taile; and then Sir Launcelot answered for him,

[208-021]then she left off, and rebuked Sir Launcelot.

[208-022]So this meantime Sir Tristram sent by a damosel a letter unto Sir Launcelot,

[208-023]excusing him of the wedding of Isoud la Blanche Mains; and said in the letter,

[208-024]as he was a true knight he had never ado fleshly with Isoud la Blanche Mains;

[208-025]and passing courteously and gentily Sir Tristram wrote unto Sir Launcelot, ever

[208-026]beseeching him to be his good friend and unto La Beale Isoud of Cornwall, and

[208-027]that Sir Launcelot would excuse him if that ever he saw her. And within short

[208-028]time by the grace of God, said Sir Tristram, that he would speak with La Beale

[208-029]Isoud, and with him right hastily. Then Sir Launcelot departed from the damosel

[208-030]and from Sir La Cote Male Taile, for to oversee that letter, and to write

[208-031]another letter unto Sir Tristram de Liones.

[208-032]And in the meanwhile La Cote Male Taile rode with the damosel until they came

[208-033]to a castle that hight Pendragon; and there were six knights stood afore him,

[208-034]and one of them proffered to joust with La Cote Male Taile. And there La Cote

[208-035]Male Taile smote him over his horse’s croup. And then the five knights

[208-036]set upon him all at once with their spears, and there they smote La Cote Male

[208-037]Taile down, horse and man. And then they alighted suddenly, and set their hands

[208-038]upon him all at once, and took him prisoner, and so led him unto the castle and

[208-039]kept him as prisoner.

[208-040]And on the morn Sir Launcelot arose, and delivered the damosel with letters

[208-041]unto Sir Tristram, and then he took his way after La Cote Male Taile; and by

[208-042]the way upon a bridge there was a knight proffered Sir Launcelot to joust, and

[208-043]Sir Launcelot smote him down, and then they fought upon foot a noble battle

[208-044]together, and a mighty; and at the last Sir Launcelot smote him down grovelling

[208-045]upon his hands and his knees. And then that knight yielded him, and Sir

[208-046]Launcelot received him fair. Sir, said the knight, I require thee tell me your

[208-047]name, for much my heart giveth unto you. Nay, said Sir Launcelot, as at this

[208-048]time I will not tell you my name, unless then that ye tell me your name.

[208-049]Certainly, said the knight, my name is Sir Nerovens, that was made knight of my

[208-050]lord Sir Launcelot du Lake. Ah, Nerovens de Lile, said Sir Launcelot, I am

[208-051]right glad that ye are proved a good knight, for now wit ye well my name is Sir

[208-052]Launcelot du Lake. Alas, said Sir Nerovens de Lile, what have I done! And

[208-053]therewithal flatling he fell to his feet, and would have kissed them, but Sir

[208-054]Launcelot would not let him; and then either made great joy of other. And then

[208-055]Sir Nerovens told Sir Launcelot that he should not go by the Castle of

[208-056]Pendragon: For there is a lord, a mighty knight, and many knights with him, and

[208-057]this night I heard say that they took a knight prisoner yesterday that rode

[208-058]with a damosel, and they say he is a Knight of the [[round-table|Round Table]].
