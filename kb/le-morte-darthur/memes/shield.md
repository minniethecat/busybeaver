The **shield** in Malory's [[Le Morte d'Arthur]] is at once a piece of battle[[battle|field]] equipment, a surface for heraldic display, and a narrative device for the concealment and revelation of identity. In [[battle|combat]], shields are broken and remade; in the language of [[chivalry]], a shield's device announces its bearer's [[blood|lineage]] and [[fealty|allegiance]]; and in the logic of romance narrative, a blank or borrowed shield allows a knight to move unrecognised through a world that knows him by his arms. The shield is thus inseparable from questions of [[honour]], [[identity]], and the public performance of knighthood.

## The Shield in Combat

In the mounted [[battle|combat]] that dominates Malory's battle scenes, the shield is the knight's primary defence. The couched [[spear]] strikes the shield first, and the quality of a knight's shield—and his skill in using it—can determine survival:

> Then they dressed their shields, and began to come together (014-004)

The phrase "dressed their shields"—positioning the shield for the charge—is one of Malory's [[banner|standard]] formulae for the onset of single [[battle|combat]]. Shields fall to pieces under the force of [[spear|lances]] (014-007), are cast up in desperate defence (019-018), and become so bloodied that a knight is rendered unrecognisable (019-031). The shield both protects and, when shattered, signals the ferocity of the encounter.

## The Shield as Heraldic Canvas

A [[knight]]'s shield displays his device—the visual signature of his identity and [[blood|lineage]]. When [[Sir Griflet|Griflet]] encounters a shield hanging on a tree before a pavilion, it serves as a territorial marker and a challenge:

> on a tree a shield of divers colours and a great spear (025-017)

To strike the shield is to issue a challenge, as Griflet does with the butt of his spear (025-018). The shield-knight who emerges demands to know why his shield has been struck down (025-020), treating the act as a personal affront. The shield, even when separated from its bearer, represents the knight himself.

## The Concealed Shield

Just as a shield can proclaim identity, its absence or concealment can obscure it. A knight who [[battle|fight]]s with a blank shield, or who borrows another's shield, moves through the narrative incognito—his [[prowess]] visible but his name hidden. This device drives much of the recognition-and-revelation dynamic that structures Malory's romance episodes. The moment when a concealed shield is replaced by the knight's true arms is the moment when identity is restored and narrative tension resolves.

## The Shield and the Body

The shield is so closely identified with the knight that it becomes an extension of his body. When a spear passes "through the shield and the left side" (025-027), the shield's failure is the body's wound. The blood that covers a shield in battle (019-031) is both the knight's and his enemies'—the shield becomes a record of the [[battle|fight]], a material [[testimony]] to the violence it has witnessed and withstood.
