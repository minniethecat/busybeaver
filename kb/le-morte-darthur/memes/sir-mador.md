# Sir Mador de la Porte

Sir Mador de la Porte was a knight of [[King Arthur]]'s [[Round Table]] who appears in the tournament narratives of [[Le Morte d'Arthur]]. His most notable moment comes in an encounter with [[Sir Launcelot]], in which he took a heavy fall that the chronicle records with vivid brevity.

## A Knight of the Round Table

Sir Mador belonged to the fellowship of the [[Round Table]], one of the many knights who populated King Arthur's court and took the field in its tournaments. The chronicle lists him among Arthur's company: "Sir Mador de la Porte, and Sir Mordred and Sir Gahalantine" (114-022), placing him in the company of well-known knights. His epithet "de la Porte" — of the Gate — suggests either a place of origin or a family name that distinguished him from other Madors.

## The Tournament Encounter

The critical moment of Mador's recorded career occurred during a tournament in which [[Sir Launcelot]] fought on the opposing side. Launcelot, as was his custom, proved an unstoppable force on the field. When Mador rode against him, "said Sir Mador de la Porte, therefore have here once at him" (115-007) — a challenge delivered with the directness of a knight confident in his strength.

The outcome was swift and decisive. "Sir Mador hath a sore fall" (115-010), the chronicle records. Launcelot, "ware of him, and gat a great spear in his hand" (115-010), met Mador with the full force of his unmatched prowess. The "sore fall" — a phrase that carries both physical impact and a hint of narrative sympathy — marked Mador as one of the many knights who discovered, at the point of Launcelot's lance, the distance between ordinary valour and the terrifying excellence of the greatest knight in the world.

## At the Tournament of Surluse

The chronicle places Mador at another great tournament, listed alongside [[Sir Mordred]]: "Mador de la Porte and Sir Mordred, for they were at that same tournament" (126-029). His appearance across multiple tournament narratives suggests a knight whose name was sufficiently established in the Arthurian corpus to be invoked alongside the more famous figures of the court — a member of the chivalric rank and file whose presence added weight to the roll-call of combatants.

## The Measure of a Knight

Sir Mador de la Porte is a minor figure in the Arthurian chronicle, but his presence serves a function common to many such knights: he provides scale. The roll-call of Round Table knights gains its weight from names like Mador's — warriors who filled the lists, took their falls, and contributed to the texture of Arthur's court without commanding centre stage. His "sore fall" at Launcelot's hands is the kind of detail that gives the [[Round Table]] its human dimension: for every [[Sir Tristram]] or [[Sir Gawaine]] who could match Launcelot blow for blow, there were a dozen Madors who learned, in a single shattering instant, the true measure of the distance between them.
