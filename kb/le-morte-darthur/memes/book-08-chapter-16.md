[179-001]knights of the [[round-table|Round Table]].

[179-002]Then within a while [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]] saw them afore him, two likely knights. Sir,

[179-003]said Gouvernail unto his master, Sir, I would counsel you not to have ado with

[179-004]them, for they be two proved knights of [[king-arthur|Arthur]]’s court. As for that, said

[179-005]Sir Tristram, have ye no doubt but I will have ado with them to increase my

[179-006][[honour|worship]], for it is many day sithen I did any deeds of arms. Do as ye list, said

[179-007]Gouvernail. And therewithal anon Sir Tristram asked them from whence they came,

[179-008]and whither they would, and what they did in those marches. Sir Sagramore

[179-009]looked upon Sir Tristram, and had scorn of his words, and asked him again, Fair

[179-010]knight, be ye a knight of Cornwall? Whereby ask ye it? said Sir Tristram. For

[179-011]it is seldom seen, said Sir Sagramore, that ye Cornish knights be valiant men

[179-012]of arms; for within these two hours there met us one of your Cornish knights,

[179-013]and great words he spake, and anon with little might he was laid to the earth.

[179-014]And, as I trow, said Sir Sagramore, ye shall have the same handsel that he had.

[179-015]Fair lords, said Sir Tristram, it may so happen that I may better withstand

[179-016]than he did, and whether ye will or nill I will have ado with you, because he

[179-017]was my cousin that ye beat. And therefore here do your best, and wit ye well

[179-018]but if ye quit you the better here upon this ground, one knight of Cornwall

[179-019]shall beat you both.

[179-020]When Sir Dodinas le Savage heard him say so he gat a spear in his hand, and

[179-021]said, Sir knight, keep well thyself: And then they departed and came together

[179-022]as it had been thunder. And Sir Dodinas’ spear brast in-sunder, but Sir

[179-023]Tristram smote him with a more might, that he smote him clean over the

[179-024]horse-croup, that nigh he had broken his neck. When Sir Sagramore saw his

[179-025]fellow have such a fall he marvelled what knight he might be. And he dressed

[179-026]his spear with all his might, and Sir Tristram against him, and they came

[179-027]together as the thunder, and there Sir Tristram smote Sir Sagramore a strong

[179-028]buffet, that he bare his horse and him to the earth, and in the falling he

[179-029]brake his thigh.

[179-030]When this was done Sir Tristram asked them: Fair knights, will ye any more? Be

[179-031]there no bigger knights in [[camelot|the court of King Arthur]]? it is to you shame to say

[179-032]of us knights of Cornwall dishonour, for it may happen a Cornish knight may

[179-033]match you. That is truth, said Sir Sagramore, that have we well proved; but I

[179-034]require thee, said Sir Sagramore, tell us your right name, by the faith and

[179-035]troth that ye owe to the high order of knighthood. Ye charge me with a great

[179-036]thing, said Sir Tristram, and sithen ye list to wit it, ye shall know and

[179-037]understand that my name is Sir Tristram de Liones, King Meliodas’ son,

[179-038]and nephew unto [[king-mark|King Mark]]. Then were they two knights fain that they had met

[179-039]with Tristram, and so they prayed him to abide in their fellowship. Nay, said

[179-040]Sir Tristram, for I must have ado with one of your fellows, his name is Sir

[179-041]Bleoberis de Ganis. God speed you well, said Sir Sagramore and Dodinas. Sir

[179-042]Tristram departed and rode onward on his way. And then was he ware before him

[179-043]in a valley where rode Sir Bleoberis, with Sir Segwarides’ lady, that

[179-044]rode behind his squire upon a palfrey.
