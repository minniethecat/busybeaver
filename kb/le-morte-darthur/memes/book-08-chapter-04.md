[167-002]Then it befell that King Anguish of Ireland sent unto [[king-mark|King Mark of Cornwall]] for

[167-003]his truage, that Cornwall had paid many winters. And all that time King Mark

[167-004]was behind of the truage for seven years. And King Mark and his barons gave

[167-005]unto the messenger of Ireland these words and answer, that they would none pay;

[167-006]and bade the messenger go unto his King Anguish, and tell him we will pay him

[167-007]no truage, but tell your lord, an he will always have truage of us of Cornwall,

[167-008]bid him send a trusty knight of his land, that will fight for his right, and we

[167-009]shall find another for to defend our right. With this answer the messengers

[167-010]departed into Ireland. And when King Anguish understood the answer of the

[167-011]messengers he was wonderly wroth. And then he called unto him Sir Marhaus, the

[167-012]good knight, that was nobly proved, and a Knight of the [[round-table|Table Round]]. And this

[167-013]Marhaus was brother unto the queen of Ireland. Then the king said thus: Fair

[167-014]brother, Sir Marhaus, I pray you go into Cornwall for my sake, and do battle

[167-015]for our truage that of right we ought to have; and whatsomever ye spend ye

[167-016]shall have sufficiently, more than ye shall need. Sir, said Marhaus, wit ye

[167-017]well that I shall not be loath to do battle in the right of you and your land

[167-018]with the best knight of the Table Round; for I know them, for the most part,

[167-019]what be their deeds; and for to advance my deeds and to increase my [[honour|worship]] I

[167-020]will right gladly go unto this journey for our right.

[167-021]So in all haste there was made purveyance for Sir Marhaus, and he had all

[167-022]things that to him needed; and so he departed out of Ireland, and arrived up in

[167-023]Cornwall even fast by the Castle of Tintagil. And when King Mark understood

[167-024]that he was there arrived to fight for Ireland, then made King Mark great

[167-025]sorrow when he understood that the good and noble knight Sir Marhaus was come.

[167-026]For they knew no knight that durst have ado with him. For at that time Sir

[167-027]Marhaus was called one of the famousest and renowned knights of the world. And

[167-028]thus Sir Marhaus abode in the sea, and every day he sent unto King Mark for to

[167-029]pay the truage that was behind of seven year, other else to find a knight to

[167-030]fight with him for the truage. This manner of message Sir Marhaus sent daily

[167-031]unto King Mark.

[167-032]Then they of Cornwall let make cries in every place, that what knight would

[167-033]fight for to save the truage of Cornwall, he should be rewarded so that he

[167-034]should fare the better, term of his life. Then some of the barons said to King

[167-035]Mark, and counselled him to send to [[camelot|the court of King Arthur]] for to seek Sir

[167-036][[sir-launcelot|Launcelot du Lake]], that was that time named for the marvelloust knight of all

[167-037]the world. Then there were some other barons that counselled the king not to do

[167-038]so, and said that it was labour in vain, because Sir Marhaus was a knight of

[167-039]the Round Table, therefore any of them will be loath to have ado with other,

[167-040]but if it were any knight at his own request would fight disguised and unknown.

[167-041]So the king and all his barons assented that it was no bote to seek any knight

[167-042]of the Round Table. This mean while came the language and the noise unto King

[167-043]Meliodas, how that Sir Marhaus abode battle fast by Tintagil, and how King Mark

[167-044]could find no manner knight to fight for him. When young [[sir-tristram|Tristram]] heard of this

[167-045]he was wroth, and sore ashamed that there durst no knight in Cornwall have ado

[167-046]with Sir Marhaus of Ireland.
