[198-003]So when they were departed, Gouvernail, and Sir Lambegus, and Sir Sentraille de

[198-004]Lushon, that were Sir Tristram’s men, sought their master. When they

[198-005]heard he was escaped then they were passing glad; and on the rocks they found

[198-006]him, and with towels they pulled him up. And then Sir Tristram asked them where

[198-007]was La Beale Isoud, for he weened she had been had away of Andred’s

[198-008]people. Sir, said Gouvernail, she is put in a lazar-cote. Alas, said Sir

[198-009]Tristram, this is a full ungoodly place for such a fair lady, and if I may she

[198-010]shall not be long there. And so he took his men and went thereas was La Beale

[198-011]Isoud, and fetched her away, and brought her into a forest to a fair manor, and

[198-012]Sir Tristram there abode with her. So the good knight bade his men go from him:

[198-013]For at this time I may not help you. So they departed all save Gouvernail. And

[198-014]so upon a day Sir Tristram yede into the forest for to disport him, and then it

[198-015]happened that there he fell sleep; and there came a man that Sir Tristram

[198-016]aforehand had slain his brother, and when this man had found him he shot him

[198-017]through the shoulder with an arrow, and Sir Tristram leapt up and killed that

[198-018]man. And in the meantime it was told [[king-mark|King Mark]] how Sir Tristram and La Beale

[198-019]Isoud were in that same manor, and as soon as ever he might thither he came

[198-020]with many knights to slay Sir Tristram. And when he came there he found him

[198-021]gone; and there he took La Beale Isoud home with him, and kept her strait that

[198-022]by no means never she might wit nor send unto Tristram, nor he unto her. And

[198-023]then when Sir Tristram came toward the old manor he found the track of many

[198-024]horses, and thereby he wist his lady was gone. And then Sir Tristram took great

[198-025]sorrow, and endured with great pain long time, for the arrow that he was hurt

[198-026]withal was envenomed.

[198-027]Then by the mean of La Beale Isoud she told a lady that was cousin unto Dame

[198-028]Bragwaine, and she came to Sir Tristram, and told him that he might not be

[198-029]whole by no means. For thy lady, La Beale Isoud, may not help thee, therefore

[198-030]she biddeth you haste into Brittany to King Howel, and there ye shall find his

[198-031]daughter, Isoud la Blanche Mains, and she shall help thee. Then Sir Tristram

[198-032]and Gouvernail gat them shipping, and so sailed into Brittany. And when King

[198-033]Howel wist that it was Sir Tristram he was full glad of him. Sir, he said, I am

[198-034]come into this country to have help of your daughter, for it is told me that

[198-035]there is none other may heal me but she; and so within a while she healed him.
