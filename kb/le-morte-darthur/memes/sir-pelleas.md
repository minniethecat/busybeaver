# Sir Pelleas

Sir Pelleas was a knight of the [[Round Table]] whose story is one of the most poignant love tragedies in [[Le Morte d'Arthur]]. His unrequited devotion to the lady Ettard, the betrayal he suffered at the hands of [[Sir Gawaine]], and his eventual deliverance by the Lady of the Lake form a narrative of love, treachery, and supernatural intervention.

## The Love of Ettard

Sir Pelleas loved a lady named Ettard with an intensity that consumed him entirely. He was known to many as the knight who loved Ettard above all else (086-049), and his reputation as the finest knight she had ever encountered was well established: there was never a man that Sir Pelleas met but he overcame in combat for her sake (086-056). Yet despite his prowess and devotion, Ettard scorned him, and Pelleas made great moan out of measure for her rejection (087-023).

His standing among the fellowship of [[King Arthur]]'s knights was high; he ranked alongside [[Sir Percivale]] and [[Sir Marhaus]] among the notable knights of the court (084-058).

## The Betrayal of Sir Gawaine

Desperate in his unrequited love, Sir Pelleas confided in Sir Gawaine, telling him his name and his plight (087-038). Sir Gawaine swore his troth to Sir Pelleas to be true and to help him win the love of Ettard (088-002). But Gawaine betrayed that oath. Taking Pelleas's armour and pretending to have slain him, Gawaine gained access to Ettard's company and lay with her while Pelleas was away (088-029).

When Sir Pelleas discovered them sleeping together, he chose the path of honour: rather than slay them both in their sleep, he laid his naked sword across their throats and departed. This act of restraint—the act of a knight who loved truly and chose mercy over vengeance—defines Pelleas's character at its most noble.

## Rescue by the Lady of the Lake

The [[Lady of the Lake]] (Nimue) interceded in Pelleas's tragedy. Finding him in despair after Gawaine's betrayal, she took pity on him and worked her enchantment to make Ettard fall in love with Pelleas—only for Ettard to die of sorrow when she realised what had been done. The Lady of the Lake then took Sir Pelleas as her own, and they loved together for the rest of their days, with Nimue providing the devotion that Ettard had always withheld.

## The Pattern of Love and Betrayal

Sir Pelleas's tale mirrors several of the great love triangles in Arthurian legend. His story shares thematic ground with the tragedy of [[Sir Tristram]] and [[La Beale Isoud]], and with the fatal love of [[Sir Launcelot]] for [[Queen Guenever]]. Pelleas stands apart, however, in that his story ends not in death but in supernatural rescue—the Lady of the Lake replaces false love with true, and the knight who had been deceived finds lasting happiness. His narrative offers a rare moment of grace in Malory's chronicle of love's sorrows.
