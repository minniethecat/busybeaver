[231-001][[sir-palomides|Palomides]] and overthrew him, and after he was assailed with twelve

[231-002]knights.

[231-003]Then Sir Palomides sent unto [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]] a squire, and required him of

[231-004][[tournament|jousts]]. Fair fellow, said Sir Launcelot, tell me thy lord’s name. Sir,

[231-005]said the squire, my lord’s name is Sir Palomides, the good knight. In

[231-006]good hour, said Sir Launcelot, for there is no knight that I saw this seven

[231-007]years that I had liefer ado withal than with him. And so either knights made

[231-008]them ready with two great spears. Nay, said Sir Dinadan, ye shall see that Sir

[231-009]Palomides will quit him right well. It may be so, said [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]], but I

[231-010]undertake that knight with the shield of Cornwall shall give him a fall. I

[231-011]believe it not, said Sir Dinadan. Right so they spurred their horses and

[231-012]feutred their spears, and either hit other, and Sir Palomides brake a spear

[231-013]upon Sir Launcelot, and he sat and moved not; but Sir Launcelot smote him so

[231-014]lightly that he made his horse to avoid the saddle, and the stroke brake his

[231-015]shield and the hauberk, and had he not fallen he had been slain. How now, said

[231-016]Sir Tristram, I wist well by the manner of their riding both that Sir Palomides

[231-017]should have a fall.

[231-018]Right so Sir Launcelot rode his way, and rode to a well to drink and to repose

[231-019]him, and they of Northgalis espied him whither he rode; and then there followed

[231-020]him twelve knights for to have mischieved him, for this cause that upon the

[231-021]morn at the tournament of the Castle of Maidens that he should not win the

[231-022]victory. So they came upon Sir Launcelot suddenly, and unnethe he might put

[231-023]upon him his helm and take his horse, but they were in hands with him; and then

[231-024]Sir Launcelot gat his spear, and rode through them, and there he slew a knight

[231-025]and brake a spear in his body. Then he drew his sword and smote upon the right

[231-026]hand and upon the left hand, so that within a few strokes he had slain other

[231-027]three knights, and the remnant that abode he wounded them sore all that did

[231-028]abide. Thus Sir Launcelot escaped from his enemies of North Wales, and then Sir

[231-029]Launcelot rode his way till a friend, and lodged him till on the morn; for he

[231-030]would not the first day have ado in the tournament because of his great labour.

[231-031]And on the first day he was with [[king-arthur|King Arthur]] thereas he was set on high upon a

[231-032]scaffold to discern who was best worthy of his deeds. So Sir Launcelot was with

[231-033]King Arthur, and jousted not the first day.
