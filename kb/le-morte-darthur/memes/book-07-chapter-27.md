[154-003]And then there came with [[king-arthur|King Arthur]] [[sir-gawaine|Sir Gawaine]], Agravaine, Gaheris, his

[154-004]brethren. And then his nephews Sir Uwaine le Blanchemains, and Sir Aglovale,

[154-005]Sir Tor, [[sir-percivale|Sir Percivale]] de Galis, and Sir Lamorak de Galis. Then came Sir

[154-006][[sir-launcelot|Launcelot du Lake]] with his brethren, nephews, and cousins, as Sir Lionel, Sir

[154-007]Ector de Maris, [[sir-bors|Sir Bors de Ganis]], and Sir Galihodin, Sir Galihud, and many

[154-008]more of Sir Launcelot’s blood, and Sir Dinadan, Sir La Cote Male Taile,

[154-009]his brother, a good knight, and Sir Sagramore, a good knight; and all the most

[154-010]part of the [[round-table|Round Table]]. Also there came with King Arthur these knights, the

[154-011]King of Ireland, King Agwisance, and the King of Scotland, King Carados and

[154-012]King Uriens of the land of Gore, and King Bagdemagus and his son Sir

[154-013]Meliaganus, and Sir Galahault the noble prince. All these kings, princes, and

[154-014]earls, barons, and other noble knights, as Sir Brandiles, Sir Uwaine les

[154-015]Avoutres, and [[sir-kay|Sir Kay]], Sir Bedivere, Sir Meliot de Logres, Sir Petipase of

[154-016]Winchelsea, Sir Godelake: all these came with King Arthur, and more that cannot

[154-017]be rehearsed.

[154-018]Now leave we of these kings and knights, and let us speak of the great array

[154-019]that was made within the castle and about the castle for both parties. The Lady

[154-020]Dame Lionesse ordained great array upon her part for her noble knights, for all

[154-021]manner of lodging and victual that came by land and by water, that there lacked

[154-022]nothing for her party, nor for the other, but there was plenty to be had for

[154-023]gold and silver for King Arthur and his knights. And then there came the

[154-024]harbingers from King Arthur for to harbour him, and his kings, dukes, earls,

[154-025]barons, and knights. And then [[sir-gareth|Sir Gareth]] prayed Dame Lionesse and the Red

[154-026]Knight of the Red Launds, and Sir Persant and his brother, and Sir Gringamore,

[154-027]that in no wise there should none of them tell not his name, and make no more

[154-028]of him than of the least knight that there was, For, he said, I will not be

[154-029]known of neither more nor less, neither at the beginning neither at the ending.

[154-030]Then Dame Lionesse said unto Sir Gareth: Sir, I will lend you a ring, but I

[154-031]would pray you as you love me heartily let me have it again when the tournament

[154-032]is done, for that ring increaseth my beauty much more than it is of himself.

[154-033]And the virtue of my ring is that, that is green it will turn to red, and that

[154-034]is red it will turn in likeness to green, and that is blue it will turn to

[154-035]likeness of white, and that is white it will turn in likeness to blue, and so

[154-036]it will do of all manner of colours. Also who that beareth my ring shall lose

[154-037]no blood, and for great love I will give you this ring. Gramercy, said Sir

[154-038]Gareth, mine own lady, for this ring is passing meet for me, for it will turn

[154-039]all manner of likeness that I am in, and that shall cause me that I shall not

[154-040]be known. Then Sir Gringamore gave Sir Gareth a bay courser that was a passing

[154-041]good horse; also he gave him good armour and sure, and a noble sword that

[154-042]sometime Sir Gringamore’s father won upon an heathen tyrant. And so thus

[154-043]every knight made him ready to that tournament. And King Arthur was come two

[154-044]days to-fore the Assumption of our Lady. And there was all manner of royalty of

[154-045]all minstrelsy that might be found. Also there came [[queen-guenever|Queen Guenever]] and the

[154-046]Queen of Orkney, Sir Gareth’s mother.

[154-047]And upon the Assumption Day, when mass and matins were done, there were heralds

[154-048]with trumpets commanded to blow to the [[battle|field]]. And so there came out Sir

[154-049]Epinogrus, the king’s son of Northumberland, from the castle, and there

[154-050]encountered with him Sir Sagramore le Desirous, and either of them brake their

[154-051]spears to their hands. And then came in Sir Palamides out of the castle, and

[154-052]there encountered with him Gawaine, and either of them smote other so hard that

[154-053]both the good knights and their horses fell to the earth. And then knights of

[154-054]either party rescued their knights. And then came in Sir Safere and Sir

[154-055]Segwarides, brethren to Sir Palamides; and there encountered Sir Agravaine with

[154-056]Sir Safere and Sir Gaheris encountered with Sir Segwarides. So Sir Safere smote

[154-057]down Agravaine, Sir Gawaine’s brother; and Sir Segwarides, Sir

[154-058]Safere’s brother. And Sir Malegrine, a knight of the castle, encountered

[154-059]with Sir Uwaine le Blanchemains, and there Sir Uwaine gave Sir Malegrine a

[154-060]fall, that he had almost broke his neck.
