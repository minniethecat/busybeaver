**Marriage** in [[Le Morte d'Arthur]] is far more than a private bond between man and wife. It is a political instrument, a dynastic engine, and a narrative hinge upon which the fates of [[realm|kingdom]]s turn. From the marriage of [[King Arthur]] to [[Queen Guenever]]—which brings the [[Round Table]] into being (053-007)—to the wedding of [[Sir Gareth]] and Dame Lionesse, marriage ceremonies in Malory frame the great moments of the [[king-arthur|Arthur]]ian world. Yet marriage is also a source of t[[wrath|rage]]dy: divided loyalties, adulterous [[love]], and the incompatible claims of political alliance and personal des[[wrath|ire]] run through the Morte like fissures in a foundation.

## The Marriage of King Arthur

The marriage of [[king-arthur|Arthur]] to [[queen-guenever|Guenever]] is the central dynastic union of the Morte. It cements [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s [[king|kingship]] and inaugurates the age of the [[round-table|Round Table]]:

> the king let ordain for the marriage and the coronation in the most honourable (053-007)

The pairing of "marriage and coronation" is significant—[[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s wedding is simultaneously a rite of personal union and an act of state. The [[Round Table]] itself, with its hundred knights, comes to [[king-arthur|Arthur]] as [[queen-guenever|Guenever]]'s dowry, binding the institution of chivalry to the royal marriage from its inception. Yet this founding union carries within it the seed of destruction: [[queen-guenever|Guenever]]'s love for [[Sir Launcelot]] will ultimately divide the [[round-table|Round Table]] and bring down the [[realm|kingdom]].

## Marriage as Political Alliance

Throughout the Morte, marriage functions as a tool of diplomacy and alliance-building. [[king|King]]s give daughters in marriage to secure peace; knights marry into [[baron|lord]]ship and [[realm|land]]. The marriage of [[Sir Gareth]] to Dame Lionesse at the [[castle|Castle]] Perilous consolidates his transformation from unknown kitchen knight to [[realm|land]]ed [[baron|lord]]:

> made the wedding betwixt Sir Gareth and the Lady Lionesse with great solemnity (162-007)

The weddings of [[sir-gareth|Gareth]]'s brothers—Gaheris to Dame Linet, Agravaine to Dame Laurel—are recorded in the same breath (162-061–062), as if marriage were a form of re[[battle|war]]d and settlement distributed alongside [[worship|honour]]s and titles. Marriage in this mode is less about love than about the orderly distribution of persons and properties within the feudal system.

## The Wedded Man's Obligation

Malory registers a tension between marriage and the errant life of the knight. To be "a wedded man" is to incur obligations that compete with the freedom of [[quest]] and adventure:

> but for to be a wedded man, I think it not; for then I must (118-034)

The knight who marries must "couch his spear" and attend to his household and [[blood|lineage]]—duties that pull him away from the fellowship of the [[round-table|Round Table]] and the life of errantry. This tension is structural: the [[king-arthur|Arthur]]ian world celebrates both the solitary knight-errant and the married [[baron|lord]], but the two roles are difficult to reconcile within a single life.

## Marriage and Adultery

The shadow side of marriage in the Morte is adultery—not merely as moral failing but as a structural consequence of dynastic marriage itself. When [[King Mark]] marries La Beale Isoud for political reasons, and [[Sir Tristram]] loves her, the marriage becomes a trap from which both love and [[worship|honour]] must escape:

> they were wedded, and solemnly held their marriage. And so when they were abed (199-023)

This is [[sir-tristram|Tristram]]'s marriage to Isoud la Blanche Mains—a union contracted in despair at losing La Beale Isoud, and one that generates only misery. Malory's treatment of marriage and adultery is not simply moralistic; it exposes the inherent instability of a system that uses marriage to secure political ends while the heart obeys its own imperatives.

## Marriage as Narrative Frame

Marriages in the Morte are almost always public spectacles—feasts, tournaments, gatherings of the court. The wedding functions as a narrative framing device: it assembles the characters, creates occasions for [[oath]] and [[joust]], and marks transitions between narrative arcs. The marriage feast of [[king-arthur|Arthur]] and [[queen-guenever|Guenever]] (058-047) and the wedding feast of [[sir-gareth|Gareth]] (162-044) are both occasions at which the social order is performed and reaffirmed. When the Morte ends in civil [[battle|war]] and the destruction of the [[round-table|Round Table]], it is in part the collapse of the web of marriages and alliances that marriage had woven.
