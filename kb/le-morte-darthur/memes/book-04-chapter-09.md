[075-001]Accolon.

[075-002]And then they dressed them on both parties of the [[battle|field]], and let their horses

[075-003]run so fast that either smote other in the midst of the shield with their

[075-004]spear-heads, that both horse and man went to the earth; and then they started

[075-005]up both, and pulled out their swords. The meanwhile that they were thus at the

[075-006]battle, came the Damosel of the Lake into the field, that put [[merlin|Merlin]] under the

[075-007]stone; and she came thither for love of [[king-arthur|King Arthur]], for she knew how [[morgan-le-fay|Morgan]] le

[075-008]Fay had so ordained that King Arthur should have been slain that day, and

[075-009]therefore she came to save his life. And so they went eagerly to the battle,

[075-010]and gave many great strokes, but always Arthur’s sword bit not like

[075-011]Accolon’s sword; but for the most part, every stroke that Accolon gave he

[075-012]wounded sore Arthur, that it was marvel he stood, and always his blood fell

[075-013]from him fast.

[075-014]When Arthur beheld the ground so sore be-bled he was dismayed, and then he

[075-015]deemed treason that his sword was changed; for his sword bit not steel as it

[075-016]was wont to do, therefore he dreaded him sore to be dead, for ever him seemed

[075-017]that the sword in Accolon’s hand was [[sword-in-the-stone|Excalibur]], for at every stroke that

[075-018]Accolon struck he drew blood on Arthur. Now, knight, said Accolon unto Arthur,

[075-019]keep thee well from me; but Arthur answered not again, and gave him such a

[075-020]buffet on the helm that it made him to stoop, nigh falling down to the earth.

[075-021]Then Sir Accolon withdrew him a little, and came on with Excalibur on high, and

[075-022]smote Sir Arthur such a buffet that he fell nigh to the earth. Then were they

[075-023]wroth both, and gave each other many sore strokes, but always Sir Arthur lost

[075-024]so much blood that it was marvel he stood on his feet, but he was so full of

[075-025]knighthood that knightly he endured the pain. And Sir Accolon lost not a deal

[075-026]of blood, therefore he waxed passing light, and Sir Arthur was passing feeble,

[075-027]and weened verily to have died; but for all that he made countenance as though

[075-028]he might endure, and held Accolon as short as he might. But Accolon was so bold

[075-029]because of Excalibur that he waxed passing hardy. But all men that beheld him

[075-030]said they saw never knight fight so well as Arthur did considering the blood

[075-031]that he bled. So was all the people sorry for him, but the two brethren would

[075-032]not accord. Then always they fought together as fierce knights, and Sir Arthur

[075-033]withdrew him a little for to rest him, and Sir Accolon called him to battle and

[075-034]said, It is no time for me to suffer thee to rest. And therewith he came

[075-035]fiercely upon Arthur, and Sir Arthur was wroth for the blood that he had lost,

[075-036]and smote Accolon on high upon the helm, so mightily, that he made him nigh to

[075-037]fall to the earth; and therewith Arthur’s sword brast at the cross, and

[075-038]fell in the grass among the blood, and the pommel and the sure handles he held

[075-039]in his hands. When Sir Arthur saw that, he was in great fear to die, but always

[075-040]he held up his shield and lost no ground, nor bated no cheer.
