[026-001]and asked truage for this land of [[king-arthur|Arthur]], and how Arthur fought with a

[026-002]knight.

[026-003]When the knight saw him lie so on the ground, he alighted, and was passing

[026-004]heavy, for he weened he had slain him, and then he unlaced his helm and gat him

[026-005]wind, and so with the truncheon he set him on his horse, and so betook him to

[026-006]God, and said he had a mighty heart, and if he might live he would prove a

[026-007]passing good knight. And so Sir Griflet rode to the court, where great dole was

[026-008]made for him. But through good leeches he was healed and saved. Right so came

[026-009]into the court twelve knights, and were aged men, and they came from the

[026-010]Emperor of Rome, and they asked of Arthur truage for this realm, other else the

[026-011]emperor would destroy him and his land. Well, said King Arthur, ye are

[026-012]messengers, therefore ye may say what ye will, other else ye should die

[026-013]therefore. But this is mine answer: I owe the emperor no truage, nor none will

[026-014]I hold him, but on a fair [[battle|field]] I shall give him my truage that shall be with a

[026-015]sharp spear, or else with a sharp sword, and that shall not be long, by my

[026-016]father’s soul, Uther Pendragon. And therewith the messengers departed

[026-017]passingly wroth, and King Arthur as wroth, for in evil time came they then; for

[026-018]the king was passingly wroth for the hurt of Sir Griflet. And so he commanded a

[026-019]privy man of his chamber that or it be day his best horse and armour, with all

[026-020]that longeth unto his person, be without the city or to-morrow day. Right so or

[026-021]to-morrow day he met with his man and his horse, and so mounted up and dressed

[026-022]his shield and took his spear, and bade his chamberlain tarry there till he

[026-023]came again. And so Arthur rode a soft pace till it was day, and then was he

[026-024]ware of three churls chasing [[merlin|Merlin]], and would have slain him. Then the king

[026-025]rode unto them, and bade them: Flee, churls! then were they afeard when they

[026-026]saw a knight, and fled. O Merlin, said Arthur, here hadst thou been slain for

[026-027]all thy crafts had I not been. Nay, said Merlin, not so, for I could save

[026-028]myself an I would; and thou art more near thy death than I am, for thou goest

[026-029]to the deathward, an God be not thy friend.

[026-030]So as they went thus talking they came to the fountain, and the rich pavilion

[026-031]there by it. Then King Arthur was ware where sat a knight armed in a chair. Sir

[026-032]knight, said Arthur, for what cause abidest thou here, that there may no knight

[026-033]ride this way but if he joust with thee? said the king. I rede thee leave that

[026-034]custom, said Arthur. This custom, said the knight, have I used and will use

[026-035]maugre who saith nay, and who is grieved with my custom let him amend it that

[026-036]will. I will amend it, said Arthur. I shall defend thee, said the knight. Anon

[026-037]he took his horse and dressed his shield and took a spear, and they met so hard

[026-038]either in other’s shields, that all to-shivered their spears. Therewith

[026-039]anon Arthur pulled out his sword. Nay, not so, said the knight; it is fairer,

[026-040]said the knight, that we twain run more together with sharp spears. I will

[026-041]well, said Arthur, an I had any more spears. I have enow, said the knight; so

[026-042]there came a squire and brought two good spears, and Arthur chose one and he

[026-043]another; so they spurred their horses and came together with all their mights,

[026-044]that either brake their spears to their hands. Then Arthur set hand on his

[026-045]sword. Nay, said the knight, ye shall do better, ye are a passing good jouster

[026-046]as ever I met withal, and once for the love of the high order of knighthood let

[026-047]us joust once again. I assent me, said Arthur. Anon there were brought two

[026-048]great spears, and every knight gat a spear, and therewith they ran together

[026-049]that Arthur’s spear all to-shivered. But the other knight hit him so hard

[026-050]in midst of the shield, that horse and man fell to the earth, and therewith

[026-051]Arthur was eager, and pulled out his sword, and said, I will assay thee, sir

[026-052]knight, on foot, for I have lost the honour on horseback. I will be on

[026-053]horseback, said the knight. Then was Arthur wroth, and dressed his shield

[026-054]toward him with his sword drawn. When the knight saw that, he alighted, for him

[026-055]thought no [[honour|worship]] to have a knight at such avail, he to be on horseback and he

[026-056]on foot, and so he alighted and dressed his shield unto Arthur. And there began

[026-057]a strong battle with many great strokes, and so hewed with their swords that

[026-058]the cantels flew in the fields, and much blood they bled both, that all the

[026-059]place there as they fought was overbled with blood, and thus they fought long

[026-060]and rested them, and then they went to the battle again, and so hurtled

[026-061]together like two rams that either fell to the earth. So at the last they smote

[026-062]together that both their swords met even together. But the sword of the knight

[026-063]smote King Arthur’s sword in two pieces, wherefore he was heavy. Then

[026-064]said the knight unto Arthur, Thou art in my daunger whether me list to save

[026-065]thee or slay thee, and but thou yield thee as overcome and recreant, thou shalt

[026-066]die. As for death, said King Arthur, welcome be it when it cometh, but to yield

[026-067]me unto thee as recreant I had liefer die than to be so shamed. And therewithal

[026-068]the king leapt unto Pellinore, and took him by the middle and threw him down,

[026-069]and raced off his helm. When the knight felt that he was adread, for he was a

[026-070]passing big man of might, and anon he brought Arthur under him, and raced off

[026-071]his helm and would have smitten off his head.
