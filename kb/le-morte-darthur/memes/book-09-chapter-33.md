[236-001][[sir-tristram|Tristram]], and how after Sir Tristram smote down [[sir-palomides|Sir Palomides]].

[236-002]Then this cry was so large that [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]] heard it. And then he gat a great

[236-003]spear in his hand and came towards the cry. Then Sir Launcelot cried: The

[236-004]Knight with the Black Shield, make thee ready to joust with me. When Sir

[236-005]Tristram heard him say so he gat his spear in his hand, and either abashed down

[236-006]their heads, and came together as thunder; and Sir Tristram’s spear brake

[236-007]in pieces, and Sir Launcelot by malfortune struck Sir Tristram on the side a

[236-008]deep wound nigh to the death; but yet Sir Tristram avoided not his saddle, and

[236-009]so the spear brake. Therewithal Sir Tristram that was wounded gat out his

[236-010]sword, and he rushed to Sir Launcelot, and gave him three great strokes upon

[236-011]the helm that the fire sprang thereout, and Sir Launcelot abashed his head

[236-012]lowly toward his saddle-bow. And therewithal Sir Tristram departed from the

[236-013][[battle|field]], for he felt him so wounded that he weened he should have died; and Sir

[236-014]Dinadan espied him and followed him into the forest. Then Sir Launcelot abode

[236-015]and did many [[marvel|marvellous]] deeds.

[236-016]So when Sir Tristram was departed by the forest’s side he alighted, and

[236-017]unlaced his harness and freshed his wound; then weened Sir Dinadan that he

[236-018]should have died. Nay, nay, said Sir Tristram, Dinadan never dread thee, for I

[236-019]am heart-whole, and of this wound I shall soon be whole, by the mercy of God.

[236-020]By that Sir Dinadan was ware where came Palomides riding straight upon them.

[236-021]And then Sir Tristram was ware that Sir Palomides came to have destroyed him.

[236-022]And so Sir Dinadan gave him warning, and said: Sir Tristram, my lord, ye are so

[236-023]sore wounded that ye may not have ado with him, therefore I will ride against

[236-024]him and do to him what I may, and if I be slain ye may pray for my soul; and in

[236-025]the meanwhile ye may withdraw you and go into the castle, or in the forest,

[236-026]that he shall not meet with you. Sir Tristram smiled and said: I thank you, Sir

[236-027]Dinadan, of your good will, but ye shall wit that I am able to handle him. And

[236-028]then anon hastily he armed him, and took his horse, and a great spear in his

[236-029]hand, and said to Sir Dinadan: Adieu; and rode toward Sir Palomides a soft

[236-030]pace. Then when Sir Palomides saw that, he made countenance to amend his horse,

[236-031]but he did it for this cause, for he abode Sir Gaheris that came after him. And

[236-032]when he was come he rode toward Sir Tristram. Then Sir Tristram sent unto Sir

[236-033]Palomides, and required him to joust with him; and if he smote down Sir

[236-034]Palomides he would do no more to him; and if it so happened that Sir Palomides

[236-035]smote down Sir Tristram, he bade him do his utterance. So they were accorded.

[236-036]Then they met together, and Sir Tristram smote down Sir Palomides that he had a

[236-037]grievous fall, so that he lay still as he had been dead. And then Sir Tristram

[236-038]ran upon Sir Gaheris, and he would not have jousted; but whether he would or

[236-039]not Sir Tristram smote him over his horse’s croup, that he lay still as

[236-040]though he had been dead. And then Sir Tristram rode his way and left Sir

[236-041]Persides’ squire within the pavilions, and Sir Tristram and Sir Dinadan

[236-042]rode to an old knight’s place to lodge them. And that old knight had five

[236-043]sons at the tournament, for whom he prayed God heartily for their coming home.

[236-044]And so, as the French book saith, they came home all five well beaten.

[236-045]And when Sir Tristram departed into the forest Sir Launcelot held alway the

[236-046]stour like hard, as a man araged that took no heed to himself, and wit ye well

[236-047]there was many a noble knight against him. And when [[king-arthur|King Arthur]] saw Sir

[236-048]Launcelot do so marvellous deeds of arms he then armed him, and took his horse

[236-049]and his armour, and rode into the field to help Sir Launcelot; and so many

[236-050]knights came in with King Arthur. And to make short tale in conclusion, the

[236-051]King of Northgalis and the King of the Hundred Knights were put to the worse;

[236-052]and because Sir Launcelot abode and was the last in the field the prize was

[236-053]given him. But Sir Launcelot would neither for king, queen, nor knight, have

[236-054]the prize, but where the cry was cried through the field: Sir Launcelot, Sir

[236-055]Launcelot hath won the field this day, Sir Launcelot let make another cry

[236-056]contrary: Sir Tristram hath won the field, for he began first, and last he hath

[236-057]endured, and so hath he done the first day, the second, and the third day.
