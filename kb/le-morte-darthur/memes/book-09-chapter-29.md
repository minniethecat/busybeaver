[232-001]first day of the tournament, and there he had the prize.

[232-002]Now turn we unto [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]] de Liones, that commanded Gouvernail, his

[232-003]servant, to ordain him a black shield with none other remembrance therein. And

[232-004]so Sir Persides and Sir Tristram departed from their host Sir Pellounes, and

[232-005]they rode early toward the tournament, and then they drew them to King

[232-006]Carados’ side, of Scotland; and anon knights began the [[battle|field]] what of King

[232-007]Northgalis’ part, and what of King Carados’ part, and there began

[232-008]great party. Then there was hurling and rashing. Right so came in Sir Persides

[232-009]and Sir Tristram and so they did fare that they put [[king-arthur|the King]] of Northgalis

[232-010]aback. Then came in Sir Bleoberis de Ganis and Sir Gaheris with them of

[232-011]Northgalis, and then was Sir Persides smitten down and almost slain, for more

[232-012]than forty horse men went over him. For Sir Bleoberis did great deeds of arms,

[232-013]and Sir Gaheris failed him not. When Sir Tristram beheld them, and saw them do

[232-014]such deeds of arms, he marvelled what they were. Also Sir Tristram thought

[232-015]shame that Sir Persides was so done to; and then he gat a great spear in his

[232-016]hand, and then he rode to Sir Gaheris and smote him down from his horse. And

[232-017]then was Sir Bleoberis wroth, and gat a spear and rode against Sir Tristram in

[232-018]great ire; and there Sir Tristram met with him, and smote Sir Bleoberis from

[232-019]his horse. So then the King with the Hundred Knights was wroth, and he horsed

[232-020]Sir Bleoberis and Sir Gaheris again, and there began a great medley; and ever

[232-021]Sir Tristram held them passing short, and ever Sir Bleoberis was passing busy

[232-022]upon Sir Tristram; and there came Sir Dinadan against Sir Tristram, and Sir

[232-023]Tristram gave him such a buffet that he swooned in his saddle. Then anon Sir

[232-024]Dinadan came to Sir Tristram and said: Sir, I know thee better than thou

[232-025]weenest; but here I promise thee my troth I will never come against thee more,

[232-026]for I promise thee that sword of thine shall never come on mine helm.

[232-027]With that came Sir Bleoberis, and Sir Tristram gave him such a buffet that down

[232-028]he laid his head; and then he caught him so sore by the helm that he pulled him

[232-029]under his horse’s feet. And then King Arthur blew to lodging. Then Sir

[232-030]Tristram departed to his pavilion, and Sir Dinadan rode with him; and Sir

[232-031]Persides and King Arthur then, and the kings upon both parties, marvelled what

[232-032]knight that was with the black shield. Many said their advice, and some knew

[232-033]him for Sir Tristram, and held their peace and would nought say. So that first

[232-034]day King Arthur, and all the kings and lords that were judges, gave Sir

[232-035]Tristram the prize; howbeit they knew him not, but named him the Knight with

[232-036]the Black Shield.
