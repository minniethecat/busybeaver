# Wound

The **wound** is a central motif in [[Le Morte d'Arthur]], functioning as both a physical consequence of [[chivalry|chivalric]] [[battle|combat]] and a narrative device that drives quests, tests character, and reveals the limits of mortal [[prowess]]. From the battle[[battle|field]] to the [[Sangreal]] quest, wounds mark the boundary between life and death, human frailty and divine intervention.

## Wounds in Combat

Knights in Malory's world sustain wounds in almost every significant encounter. The ritualised violence of [[joust]] and battle produces a continuous stream of injury, described with matter-of-fact frequency:

> and made leeches search their wounds, and sorrowed greatly (021-027)

The severest wounds serve as marks of exceptional [[battle|combat]]. Sir [[Sir Launcelot|Launcelot]] and [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]] each bear wounds that testify to their willingness to endure harm:

> smitten other seven great wounds, so that the least of them might have been the death of the mightiest [[giant]] in this world. (049-020)

Wounds are not merely incidental to [[battle|combat]] but are its expected outcome, and the capacity to [[battle|fight]] while wounded distinguishes the greatest knights from the merely competent.

## Wound as Narrative Device

Malory uses wounds to structure narrative arcs. A wound may precipitate a quest, force a knight into temporary retreat, or bring unlikely characters into contact. When Sir [[Sir Uwaine|Uwaine]] is wounded, his need for healing leads him to the [[damosel]] who becomes central to his adventure:

> brethren wounded Sir Uwaine passing grievous (093-014)

The wounded knight encountered by the roadside is a recurring figure, a catalyst for the [[knight-errant|errant knight]]'s next adventure:

> Then came there a wounded knight unto the king (069-010)
> there sat a damosel sit by a well, and a wounded knight in her arms (062-006)

In both cases, the wound is less a personal affliction than a mechanism for advancing the narrative. The wounded body becomes a sign that demands interpretation and action from those who encounter it.

## Healing and Leeches

The work of healing falls to leeches, hermits, and occasionally to [[enchantment|magic]]al or sacred means. Leeches—the medieval term for physicians—appear regularly after battles:

> and made leeches search their wounds (021-027)
> fetch leeches and search his wounds and Accolon's both (078-041)

The [[hermit]] serves as a healer in the [[forest|wilderness]], combining physical care with spiritual counsel:

> the hermit searched all his wounds and gave him good salves (028-003)

Healing is never instantaneous. Knights convalesce for days or weeks—a period that often proves as narratively significant as [[battle|combat]] itself, providing space for reflection, [[penance|confession]], and the forging of new bonds.

## The Sangreal and Miraculous Healing

The [[Sangreal]] quest introduces a [[marvel|supernatural]] dimension to healing. Certain wounds resist all earthly medicine and can only be cured by the Grail or its agents:

> the quest of the Sangreal... healed him (047-006)

Galahad's arrival and the Grail's power represent the ultimate answer to the problem of the wound—a healing that transcends the skill of leeches and the [[hardiness|endurance]] of knights. This miraculous healing redefines the wound from a mark of martial [[worship|honour]] to a sign of spiritual need.

## Wound's Narrative Function

In Malory's work, the wound operates on three registers simultaneously.

It is a **martial reality**—the expected cost of the knightly vocation. It is a **narrative hinge**—the event that red[[wrath|ire]]cts a knight's path and opens new adventures. And it is a **spiritual sign**—the bodily mark that points beyond itself to the need for healing that only [[grace]] can provide.

The wound thus unites the three great themes of the Morte: [[battle|combat]], quest, and redemption.
