[045-003]Then they rode three or four days and never met with adventure, and by hap they

[045-004]were lodged with a gentle man that was a rich man and well at ease. And as they

[045-005]sat at their supper Balin overheard one complain grievously by him in a chair.

[045-006]What is this noise? said Balin. Forsooth, said his host, I will tell you. I was

[045-007]but late at a [[tournament|jousting]], and there I jousted with a knight that is brother unto

[045-008]King Pellam, and twice smote I him down, and then he promised to quit me on my

[045-009]best friend; and so he wounded my son, that cannot be whole till I have of that

[045-010]knight’s blood, and he rideth alway invisible; but I know not his name.

[045-011]Ah! said Balin, I know that knight, his name is Garlon, he hath slain two

[045-012]knights of mine in the same manner, therefore I had liefer meet with that

[045-013]knight than all the gold in this realm, for the despite he hath done me. Well,

[045-014]said his host, I shall tell you, King Pellam of Listeneise hath made do cry in

[045-015]all this country a great feast that shall be within these twenty days, and no

[045-016]knight may come there but if he bring his wife with him, or his paramour; and

[045-017]that knight, your enemy and mine, ye shall see that day. Then I behote you,

[045-018]said Balin, part of his blood to heal your son withal. We will be forward

[045-019]to-morn, said his host. So on the morn they rode all three toward Pellam, and

[045-020]they had fifteen days’ journey or they came thither; and that same day

[045-021]began the great feast. And so they alighted and stabled their horses, and went

[045-022]into the castle; but Balin’s host might not be let in because he had no

[045-023]lady. Then Balin was well received and brought unto a chamber and unarmed him;

[045-024]and there were brought him robes to his pleasure, and would have had Balin

[045-025]leave his sword behind him. Nay, said Balin, that do I not, for it is the

[045-026]custom of my country a knight always to keep his weapon with him, and that

[045-027]custom will I keep, or else I will depart as I came. Then they gave him leave

[045-028]to wear his sword, and so he went unto the castle, and was set among knights of

[045-029][[honour|worship]], and his lady afore him.

[045-030]Soon Balin asked a knight, Is there not a knight in this court whose name is

[045-031]Garlon? Yonder he goeth, said a knight, he with the black face; he is the

[045-032]marvellest knight that is now living, for he destroyeth many good knights, for

[045-033]he goeth invisible. Ah well, said Balin, is that he? Then Balin advised him

[045-034]long: If I slay him here I shall not escape, and if I leave him now,

[045-035]peradventure I shall never meet with him again at such a steven, and much harm

[045-036]he will do an he live. Therewith this Garlon espied that this Balin beheld him,

[045-037]and then he came and smote Balin on the face with the back of his hand, and

[045-038]said, Knight, why beholdest me so? for shame therefore, eat thy meat and do

[045-039]that thou came for. Thou sayest sooth, said Balin, this is not the first

[045-040]despite that thou hast done me, and therefore I will do what I came for, and

[045-041]rose up fiercely and clave his head to the shoulders. Give me the truncheon,

[045-042]said Balin to his lady, wherewith he slew your knight. Anon she gave it him,

[045-043]for alway she bare the truncheon with her. And therewith Balin smote him

[045-044]through the body, and said openly, With that truncheon thou hast slain a good

[045-045]knight, and now it sticketh in thy body. And then Balin called unto him his

[045-046]host, saying, Now may ye fetch blood enough to heal your son withal.
