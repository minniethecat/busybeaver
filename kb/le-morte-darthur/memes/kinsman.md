# Kinsman

In the world of *Le Morte d'[[king-arthur|Arthur]]*, the **kinsman** is the irreducible unit of loyalty. Before a [[knight]] swears [[fealty]] to his [[baron|lord]], before he takes his seat at the [[Round Table]], before he owes anything to anyone, he owes everything to his kin. The kinsman bond is blood — prior to choice, immune to argument, and binding even when every other obligation has frayed. This is precisely why its violation is the most terrible event Malory records.

## The Kinsman as Protector

When kinship functions as it should, the kinsman is the natural guardian of his kin. In the dispute over a lady abducted from [[King Arthur]]'s court, one knight claims the right to intervene precisely because of blood:

> the other said he would have the rule of her, by cause he was her kinsman, and would lead her to her kin. (062-021)

The claim is presented as self-evidently valid. To be a kinsman is to have "the rule" of a kinswoman — not ownership but responsibility, the right and duty to guide her to safety and to her own "kin." The language is precise: "this lady is my kinswoman nigh, mine aunt's daughter" (062-033). The specificity of "mine aunt's daughter" traces the exact degree of relationship, as if the legal force of the claim depends on its genealogical precision. In a world without police or courts, the kinsman is the only law between a woman and her abductor.

## The Kinsman as Betrayer

If the kinsman as protector is the ideal, the kinsman as betrayer is Malory's most concentrated image of evil. When [[Sir Tristram]] is ambushed in bed with [[La Beale Isoud]], bound, and led to a [[chapel]] for execution, his captor is not a str[[wrath|anger]] but his own blood:

> O Andred, Andred, said Sir Tristram, thou shouldst be my kinsman, and now thou art to me full unfriendly. (197-050)

The repetition of the name — "O Andred, Andred" — is Malory at his most rhetorically intense. [[sir-tristram|Tristram]] does not say "thou art my kinsman" but "thou shouldst be" — the conditional verb registering that the bond has been severed by Andred's own act. A kinsman who acts as an enemy is a thing that should not exist, a category error in the moral order. [[sir-tristram|Tristram]] adds that "an there were no more but thou and I, thou wouldst not put me to death" (197-050), exposing Andred's co[[battle|war]]dice as well as his [[treason|treachery]]: the kinsman who betrays must bring twelve knights with him, because alone he could not face the man whose blood he shares.

The Andred episode is the dark double of the kinsman-as-protector motif. Where the kinsman in Chapter 62 defends a kinswoman from abduction, Andred delivers his cousin to death. The structural symmetry is exact, and it makes the point: kinship is the strongest bond in the chivalric world, which means its violation leaves the deepest wound.

## Kinship and the Round Table

The [[Round Table]] is an attempt to create an artificial kinship among knights who are not related by blood. When [[Sir Gawaine]] declares that "Whoso banisheth my cousin-germain shall banish me" (082-021), he is asserting that the bond of blood takes precedence over the bond of fellowship. The [[round-table|Round Table]]'s t[[wrath|rage]]dy is that it never fully succeeds in replacing kinship with comradeship. When the final crisis comes — the exposure of [[Queen Guenever]]'s adultery with [[Sir Launcelot]] — [[sir-gawaine|Gawaine]]'s loyalty to his kin (his brothers, killed by [[sir-launcelot|Launcelot]]) overrides his loyalty to the [[round-table|Round Table]] and to [[King Arthur]] himself. The kinsman bond that should preserve order becomes the instrument of its destruction.

## The Kinsman's Narrative Function

The kinsman in Malory is a switch that can flip two ways. When the kinsman [[worship|honour]]s his bond, he is the foundation of social order — the protector who needs no contract because the obligation is in the blood. When the kinsman betrays his bond, he becomes the most d[[wrath|anger]]ous figure in the narrative, because his [[treason|treachery]] comes from inside the circle of trust. Almost every major catastrophe in *Le Morte d'[[king-arthur|Arthur]]* has a broken kinship bond somewhere in its origin: [[Mordred]] is [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s son-and-nephew, Andred is [[sir-tristram|Tristram]]'s cousin, the Orkney brothers' feud with [[Sir Lamorak]] is a matter of avenging a father. The kinsman is the source of both the deepest loyalty and the deadliest [[revenge]].
