[017-001]fought against [[king-arthur|Arthur]] and his host, and many great feats of the [[battle|war]].

[017-002]Then King Arthur and King Ban and King [[sir-bors|Bors]], with their good and trusty

[017-003]knights, set on them so fiercely that they made them overthrow their pavilions

[017-004]on their heads, but the eleven kings, by manly [[courage|prowess]] of arms, took a fair

[017-005]champaign, but there was slain that morrowtide ten thousand good men’s

[017-006]bodies. And so they had afore them a strong passage, yet were they fifty

[017-007]thousand of hardy men. Then it drew toward day. Now shall ye do by mine advice,

[017-008]said [[merlin|Merlin]] unto the three kings: I would that King Ban and King Bors, with

[017-009]their fellowship of ten thousand men, were put in a wood here beside, in an

[017-010]ambushment, and keep them privy, and that they be laid or the light of the day

[017-011]come, and that they stir not till ye and your knights have fought with them

[017-012]long. And when it is daylight, dress your battle even afore them and the

[017-013]passage, that they may see all your host, for then will they be the more hardy,

[017-014]when they see you but about twenty thousand men, and cause them to be the

[017-015]gladder to suffer you and your host to come over the passage. All the three

[017-016]kings and the whole barons said that Merlin said passingly well, and it was

[017-017]done anon as Merlin had devised. So on the morn, when either host saw other,

[017-018]the host of the north was well comforted. Then to Ulfius and Brastias were

[017-019]delivered three thousand men of arms, and they set on them fiercely in the

[017-020]passage, and slew on the right hand and on the left hand that it was [[marvel|wonder]] to

[017-021]tell.

[017-022]When that the eleven kings saw that there was so few a fellowship did such

[017-023]deeds of arms, they were ashamed and set on them again fiercely; and there was

[017-024]Sir Ulfius’s horse slain under him, but he did marvellously well on foot.

[017-025]But the Duke Eustace of Cambenet and King Clariance of Northumberland, were

[017-026]alway grievous on Ulfius. Then Brastias saw his fellow fared so withal he smote

[017-027]the duke with a spear, that horse and man fell down. That saw King Clariance

[017-028]and returned unto Brastias, and either smote other so that horse and man went

[017-029]to the earth, and so they lay long astonied, and their horses’ knees

[017-030]brast to the hard bone. Then came [[sir-kay|Sir Kay]] the seneschal with six fellows with

[017-031]him, and did passing well. With that came the eleven kings, and there was

[017-032]Griflet put to the earth, horse and man, and Lucas the butler, horse and man,

[017-033]by King Brandegoris, and King Idres, and King Agwisance. Then waxed the medley

[017-034]passing hard on both parties. When Sir Kay saw Griflet on foot, he rode on King

[017-035]Nentres and smote him down, and led his horse unto Sir Griflet, and horsed him

[017-036]again. Also Sir Kay with the same spear smote down King Lot, and hurt him

[017-037]passing sore. That saw the King with the Hundred Knights, and ran unto Sir Kay

[017-038]and smote him down, and took his horse, and gave him King Lot, whereof he said

[017-039][[mercy|gramercy]]. When Sir Griflet saw Sir Kay and Lucas the butler on foot, he took a

[017-040]sharp spear, great and square, and rode to Pinel, a good man of arms, and smote

[017-041]horse and man down, and then he took his horse, and gave him unto Sir Kay. Then

[017-042]King Lot saw King Nentres on foot, he ran unto Melot de la Roche, and smote him

[017-043]down, horse and man, and gave King Nentres the horse, and horsed him again.

[017-044]Also the King of the Hundred Knights saw King Idres on foot; then he ran unto

[017-045]Gwiniart de Bloi, and smote him down, horse and man, and gave King Idres the

[017-046]horse, and horsed him again; and King Lot smote down Clariance de la Forest

[017-047]Savage, and gave the horse unto Duke Eustace. And so when they had horsed the

[017-048]kings again they drew them, all eleven kings, together, and said they would be

[017-049]revenged of the damage that they had taken that day. The meanwhile came in Sir

[017-050]Ector with an eager countenance, and found Ulfius and Brastias on foot, in

[017-051]great peril of death, that were foul defoiled under horse-feet.

[017-052]Then Arthur as a lion, ran unto King Cradelment of North Wales, and smote him

[017-053]through the left side, that the horse and the king fell down; and then he took

[017-054]the horse by the rein, and led him unto Ulfius, and said, Have this horse, mine

[017-055]old friend, for great need hast thou of horse. Gramercy, said Ulfius. Then Sir

[017-056]Arthur did so marvellously in arms, that all men had wonder. When the King with

[017-057]the Hundred Knights saw King Cradelment on foot, he ran unto Sir Ector, that

[017-058]was well horsed, Sir Kay’s father, and smote horse and man down, and gave

[017-059]the horse unto the king, and horsed him again. And when King Arthur saw the

[017-060]king ride on Sir Ector’s horse, he was wroth and with his sword he smote

[017-061]the king on the helm, that a quarter of the helm and shield fell down, and so

[017-062]the sword carved down unto the horse’s neck, and so the king and the

[017-063]horse fell down to the ground. Then Sir Kay came unto Sir Morganore, seneschal

[017-064]with the King of the Hundred Knights, and smote him down, horse and man, and

[017-065]led the horse unto his father, Sir Ector; then Sir Ector ran unto a knight,

[017-066]hight Lardans, and smote horse and man down, and led the horse unto Sir

[017-067]Brastias, that great need had of an horse, and was greatly defoiled. When

[017-068]Brastias beheld Lucas the butler, that lay like a dead man under the

[017-069]horses’ feet, and ever Sir Griflet did marvellously for to rescue him,

[017-070]and there were always fourteen knights on Sir Lucas; then Brastias smote one of

[017-071]them on the helm, that it went to the teeth, and he rode to another and smote

[017-072]him, that the arm flew into the field. Then he went to the third and smote him

[017-073]on the shoulder, that shoulder and arm flew in the field. And when Griflet saw

[017-074]rescues, he smote a knight on the temples, that head and helm went to the

[017-075]earth, and Griflet took the horse of that knight, and led him unto Sir Lucas,

[017-076]and bade him mount upon the horse and revenge his hurts. For Brastias had slain

[017-077]a knight to-fore and horsed Griflet.
