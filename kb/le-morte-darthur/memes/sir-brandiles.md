# Sir Brandiles

Sir Brandiles was a Knight of the [[Round Table]] who appears across several books of [[Le Morte d'Arthur]] as a loyal companion and capable tournament fighter. Though not among the first rank of Arthurian heroes, his consistent presence at councils, tournaments, and adventures marks him as a reliable member of [[King Arthur]]'s fellowship.

## A Knight of the Round Table

Brandiles was counted among the established knights of the Round Table, appearing alongside other notable figures at key gatherings. He was listed with "other noble knights, as Sir Brandiles, Sir Uwaine les" (154-014), placing him in the company of [[Sir Uwaine]], son of [[King Uriens]]. His association with [[Sir Kay]] the Seneschal is also recorded, as the two rode together on at least one occasion (155-011).

When Queen Guenever held a dinner for select knights of the Round Table, Brandiles was among those invited, alongside Kay the Seneschal and others (212-003). This inclusion suggests he was considered a knight of good standing, worthy of the queen's table.

## Encounters with Sir Tristram

Brandiles's most notable narrative moments involve [[Sir Tristram]]. During one adventure, Tristram and his companions came upon a castle where "they found Sir Brandiles" (217-016). Brandiles immediately "proffered to joust with Sir Tristram" (217-022), showing the customary chivalric eagerness to test one's skill against renowned opponents.

After the joust, Brandiles came to Sir Kay and offered further combat, but Kay declined, saying they had already tested each other enough. Brandiles accepted this with good grace: "In good time, said Sir Brandiles, and well" (217-035), displaying the courtesy expected of a Round Table knight. This brief exchange reveals a man who sought honour through combat but accepted the customs of fellowship.

## Later Service

Brandiles continued to serve the Round Table through its later years. When [[Sir Launcelot]] departed from court after the breaking of the fellowship, Brandiles was among the knights who missed him, listed alongside "Sir Launcelot, Brandiles, and many other" (218-009). This final mention places him among the loyalists who remained at court even as the great tragedy of the Round Table unfolded.

## The Rank and File of Camelot

Sir Brandiles represents the broader fellowship of the [[Round Table]] — knights who were not the greatest champions but whose steady presence constituted the strength of [[King Arthur]]'s court. His willingness to joust, his courtesy in defeat or stalemate, and his loyalty through the fellowship's dissolution embody the ordinary virtues that sustained Camelot. Without knights like Brandiles, the Round Table would have been a collection of heroes rather than a true fellowship.
