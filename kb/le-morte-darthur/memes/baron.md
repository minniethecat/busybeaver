# Baron

The **baron** in [[Le Morte d'Arthur]] is the foundational unit of the political order — the noble lord whose counsel, armed support, and assent constitute the practical basis of [[kingship]]. When [[King Arthur]] must make a decision of weight, he "let call his barons to [[counsel|council]]" (013-003). The baron is not a silent subject but an active participant in governance, whose voice the [[kinsman|kin]]g ignores at his peril.

## The Barons as Royal Council

The barons are the [[kinsman|kin]]g's natural counsellors. Before any great underta[[kinsman|kin]]g — [[battle|war]], marriage, treaty — the [[kinsman|kin]]g summons them:

> Merlin, the king let call his barons to council, for Merlin had told... (013-003)

Their assent is not a formality. When the barons "by one assent prayed" (005-032) for a course of action, the [[kinsman|kin]]g is under the strongest obligation to heed them. The barons collectively embody the wisdom of the realm, and a [[kinsman|kin]]g who rules without their counsel is a tyrant, not a [[king|sovereign]]:

> Therefore, by the advice of his barons, he took him to a strong tower (011-022)

## Barons as Military Power

Each baron commands his own knights and holds his own castles. When the [[kinsman|kin]]g goes to [[battle|war]], he does so with the barons' levies, not merely his own household. The barons' military weight means that their support or defection can decide a campaign:

> All the barons said they would pray him and... (013-010)

A [[kinsman|kin]]g who loses the confidence of his barons loses his army. The baron's dual role — counsellor in peacetime, captain in [[battle|war]] — makes him indispensable to the feudal machinery of the [[king-arthur|Arthur]]ian world.

## Barons and the Succession Crisis

The barons are at their most consequential when [[kingship]] itself is in question. After [[Uther Pendragon]]'s death, the realm falls into uncertainty:

> all the barons made great sorrow, and asked... (007-014)

> So on the morn all the barons with Merlin... (007-017)

It is the barons who gather to resolve the succession, and the barons who ultimately accept — or resist — [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s claim. [[merlin|Merlin]] knows that he must persuade the barons first, because without them, even the sword in the stone cannot make a [[kinsman|kin]]g. The barons convene on high feast days to seek divine guidance:

> on Twelfth-day all the barons came thither, and to... (009-027)

> till Candlemas and then all the barons should meet there again; but... (009-032)

## Barons as Tournament Patrons

Even in peacetime, the barons' role as patrons of [[chivalry]] is essential. They organise the [[joust|jousts]] and tournaments at which young knights prove themselves:

> New Year's Day the barons let make a jousts and a tournament (008-031)

> when the service was done, the barons rode unto... (008-035)

The baron who holds a great tournament enhances his own [[worship|honour]] while providing the arena in which the next generation of [[knighthood]] is tested and recognised.

## The Baron's Narrative Function

The baron in Malory stands between the [[kinsman|kin]]g and the knight. He is both subject and lord, commanded by the crown but commanding his own men. The tension in this position — loyalty to the [[kinsman|kin]]g versus loyalty to his own house — is one of the deep structural conflicts of the [[king-arthur|Arthur]]ian world. When the barons are united behind the crown, [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s realm is invincible; when they are divided, the [[Round Table]] itself begins to crack.
