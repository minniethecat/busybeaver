[008-001]wonders and marvels of a sword taken out of a stone by the said [[king-arthur|Arthur]].

[008-002]Then stood the realm in great jeopardy long while, for every lord that was

[008-003]mighty of men made him strong, and many weened to have been king. Then [[merlin|Merlin]]

[008-004]went to the Archbishop of Canterbury, and counselled him for to send for all

[008-005]the lords of the realm, and all the gentlemen of arms, that they should to

[008-006]London come by Christmas, upon pain of cursing; and for this cause, that Jesus,

[008-007]that was born on that night, that he would of his great mercy show some

[008-008][[marvel|miracle]], as he was come to be king of mankind, for to show some miracle who

[008-009]should be rightwise king of this realm. So the Archbishop, by the advice of

[008-010]Merlin, sent for all the lords and gentlemen of arms that they should come by

[008-011]Christmas even unto London. And many of them made them clean of their life,

[008-012]that their prayer might be the more acceptable unto God. So in the greatest

[008-013]church of London, whether it were Paul’s or not the French book maketh no

[008-014]mention, all the estates were long or day in the church for to pray. And when

[008-015]matins and the first mass was done, there was seen in the churchyard, against

[008-016]the high altar, a great stone four square, like unto a marble stone; and in

[008-017]midst thereof was like an anvil of steel a foot on high, and therein stuck a

[008-018]fair sword naked by the point, and letters there were written in gold about the

[008-019]sword that said thus:—Whoso pulleth out this sword of this stone and

[008-020]anvil, is rightwise king born of all England. Then the people marvelled, and

[008-021]told it to the Archbishop.

[008-022]I command, said the Archbishop, that ye keep you within your church and pray

[008-023]unto God still, that no man touch the sword till the high mass be all done. So

[008-024]when all masses were done all the lords went to behold the stone and the sword.

[008-025]And when they saw the scripture some assayed, such as would have been king. But

[008-026]none might stir the sword nor move it. He is not here, said the Archbishop,

[008-027]that shall achieve the sword, but doubt not God will make him known. But this

[008-028]is my counsel, said the Archbishop, that we let purvey ten knights, men of good

[008-029]fame, and they to keep this sword. So it was ordained, and then there was made

[008-030]a cry, that every man should assay that would, for to win the sword. And upon

[008-031]New Year’s Day the barons let make a [[tournament|jousts]] and a tournament, that all

[008-032]knights that would joust or tourney there might play, and all this was ordained

[008-033]for to keep the lords together and the commons, for the Archbishop trusted that

[008-034]God would make him known that should win the sword.

[008-035]So upon New Year’s Day, when the service was done, the barons rode unto

[008-036]the [[battle|field]], some to joust and some to tourney, and so it happened that Sir

[008-037]Ector, that had great livelihood about London, rode unto the jousts, and with

[008-038]him rode [[sir-kay|Sir Kay]] his son, and young Arthur that was his nourished brother; and

[008-039]Sir Kay was made knight at All Hallowmass afore. So as they rode to the

[008-040]jousts-ward, Sir Kay lost his sword, for he had left it at his father’s

[008-041]lodging, and so he prayed young Arthur for to ride for his sword. I will well,

[008-042]said Arthur, and rode fast after the sword, and when he came home, the lady and

[008-043]all were out to see the jousting. Then was Arthur wroth, and said to himself, I

[008-044]will ride to the churchyard, and take the sword with me that sticketh in the

[008-045]stone, for my brother Sir Kay shall not be without a sword this day. So when he

[008-046]came to the churchyard, Sir Arthur alighted and tied his horse to the stile,

[008-047]and so he went to the tent, and found no knights there, for they were at the

[008-048]jousting. And so he handled the sword by the handles, and lightly and fiercely

[008-049]pulled it out of the stone, and took his horse and rode his way until he came

[008-050]to his brother Sir Kay, and delivered him the sword. And as soon as Sir Kay saw

[008-051]the sword, he wist well it was the sword of the stone, and so he rode to his

[008-052]father Sir Ector, and said: Sir, lo here is the sword of the stone, wherefore I

[008-053]must be king of this land. When Sir Ector beheld the sword, he returned again

[008-054]and came to the church, and there they alighted all three, and went into the

[008-055]church. And anon he made Sir Kay swear upon a book how he came to that sword.

[008-056]Sir, said Sir Kay, by my brother Arthur, for he brought it to me. How gat ye

[008-057]this sword? said Sir Ector to Arthur. Sir, I will tell you. When I came home

[008-058]for my brother’s sword, I found nobody at home to deliver me his sword;

[008-059]and so I thought my brother Sir Kay should not be swordless, and so I came

[008-060]hither eagerly and pulled it out of the stone without any pain. Found ye any

[008-061]knights about this sword? said Sir Ector. Nay, said Arthur. Now, said Sir Ector

[008-062]to Arthur, I understand ye must be king of this land. Wherefore I, said Arthur,

[008-063]and for what cause? Sir, said Ector, for God will have it so; for there should

[008-064]never man have drawn out this sword, but he that shall be rightwise king of

[008-065]this land. Now let me see whether ye can put the sword there as it was, and

[008-066]pull it out again. That is no mastery, said Arthur, and so he put it in the

[008-067]stone; wherewithal Sir Ector assayed to pull out the sword and failed.
