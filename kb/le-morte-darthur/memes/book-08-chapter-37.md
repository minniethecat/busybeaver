[200-001][[sir-tristram|Tristram]] how he was defamed in [[camelot|the court of King Arthur]], and of Sir

[200-002]Lamorak.

[200-003]Then departed Sir Suppinabiles unto Brittany again, and there he found Sir

[200-004]Tristram, and told him that he had been in [[king-arthur|King Arthur]]’s court. Then said

[200-005]Sir Tristram: Heard ye anything of me? So God me help, said Sir Suppinabiles,

[200-006]there I heard [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]] speak of you great shame, and that ye be a false

[200-007]knight to your lady and he bade me do you to wit that he will be your mortal

[200-008]enemy in every place where he may meet you. That me repenteth, said Tristram,

[200-009]for of all knights I loved to be in his fellowship. So Sir Tristram made great

[200-010]moan and was ashamed that noble knights should defame him for the sake of his

[200-011]lady. And in this meanwhile La Beale Isoud made a letter unto [[queen-guenever|Queen Guenever]],

[200-012]complaining her of the untruth of Sir Tristram, and how he had wedded the

[200-013]king’s daughter of Brittany. Queen Guenever sent her another letter, and

[200-014]bade her be of good cheer, for she should have joy after sorrow, for Sir

[200-015]Tristram was so noble a knight called, that by crafts of sorcery ladies would

[200-016]make such noble men to wed them. But in the end, Queen Guenever said, it shall

[200-017]be thus, that he shall hate her, and love you better than ever he did to-fore.

[200-018]So leave we Sir Tristram in Brittany, and speak we of Sir Lamorak de Galis,

[200-019]that as he sailed his ship fell on a rock and perished all, save Sir Lamorak

[200-020]and his squire; and there he swam mightily, and fishers of the Isle of Servage

[200-021]took him up, and his squire was drowned, and the shipmen had great labour to

[200-022]save Sir Lamorak’s life, for all the comfort that they could do.

[200-023]And the lord of that isle, hight Sir Nabon le Noire, a great mighty giant. And

[200-024]this Sir Nabon hated all the knights of King Arthur’s, and in no wise he

[200-025]would do them favour. And these fishers told Sir Lamorak all the guise of Sir

[200-026]Nabon; how there came never knight of King Arthur’s but he destroyed him.

[200-027]And at the last battle that he did was slain Sir Nanowne le Petite, the which

[200-028]he put to a shameful death in despite of King Arthur, for he was drawn

[200-029]limb-meal. That forthinketh me, said Sir Lamorak, for that knight’s

[200-030]death, for he was my cousin; and if I were at mine ease as well as ever I was,

[200-031]I would revenge his death. Peace, said the fishers, and make here no words, for

[200-032]or ever ye depart from hence Sir Nabon must know that ye have been here, or

[200-033]else we should die for your sake. So that I be whole, said Lamorak, of my

[200-034]disease that I have taken in the sea, I will that ye tell him that I am a

[200-035]knight of King Arthur’s, for I was never afeard to reneye my lord.
