[113-001]in his leman’s bed, and how [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]] fought with the knight.

[113-002]Then within an hour there came the knight to whom the pavilion ought, and he

[113-003]weened that his leman had lain in that bed, and so he laid him down beside Sir

[113-004]Launcelot, and took him in his arms and began to kiss him. And when Sir

[113-005]Launcelot felt a rough beard kissing him, he started out of the bed lightly,

[113-006]and the other knight after him, and either of them gat their swords in their

[113-007]hands, and out at the pavilion door went the knight of the pavilion, and Sir

[113-008]Launcelot followed him, and there by a little slake Sir Launcelot wounded him

[113-009]sore, nigh unto the death. And then he yielded him unto Sir Launcelot, and so

[113-010]he granted him, so that he would tell him why he came into the bed. Sir, said

[113-011]the knight, the pavilion is mine own, and there this night I had assigned my

[113-012]lady to have slept with me, and now I am likely to die of this wound. That me

[113-013]repenteth, said Launcelot, of your hurt, but I was adread of treason, for I was

[113-014]late beguiled, and therefore come on your way into your pavilion and take your

[113-015]rest, and as I suppose I shall staunch your blood. And so they went both into

[113-016]the pavilion, and anon Sir Launcelot staunched his blood.

[113-017]Therewithal came the knight’s lady, that was a passing fair lady, and

[113-018]when she espied that her lord Belleus was sore wounded, she cried out on Sir

[113-019]Launcelot, and made great dole out of measure. Peace, my lady and my love, said

[113-020]Belleus, for this knight is a good man, and a knight adventurous, and there he

[113-021]told her all the cause how he was wounded; And when that I yielded me unto him,

[113-022]he left me goodly and hath staunched my blood. Sir, said the lady, I require

[113-023]thee tell me what knight ye be, and what is your name? Fair lady, he said, my

[113-024]name is Sir Launcelot du Lake. So me thought ever by your speech, said the

[113-025]lady, for I have seen you oft or this, and I know you better than ye ween. But

[113-026]now an ye would promise me of your courtesy, for the harms that ye have done to

[113-027]me and my Lord Belleus, that when he cometh unto [[king-arthur|Arthur]]’s court for to

[113-028]cause him to be made knight of the [[round-table|Round Table]], for he is a passing good man of

[113-029]arms, and a mighty lord of lands of many out isles.

[113-030]Fair lady, said Sir Launcelot, let him come unto the court the next high feast,

[113-031]and look that ye come with him, and I shall do my power, an ye prove you

[113-032]doughty of your hands, that ye shall have your desire. So thus within a while,

[113-033]as they thus talked, the night passed, and the day shone, and then Sir

[113-034]Launcelot armed him, and took his horse, and they taught him to the Abbey, and

[113-035]thither he rode within the space of two hours.
