# Sir Perimones

Sir Perimones, known as the Red Knight, is one of three knightly brothers — the others being Sir Pertolepe (the Green Knight) and [[Sir Persant of Inde]] (the Blue Knight) — who were overcome by Sir Gareth of Orkney during his quest to rescue the [[Lady Lionesse|Lady Lionesse of the Castle Perilous]]. After his defeat, Perimones became one of Sir Gareth's most devoted followers.

## The Brotherhood of Colours

Sir Perimones belonged to a remarkable fraternity of knights, each associated with a colour. The Green Knight was Sir Pertolepe, the Red Knight was Sir Perimones, and the Blue Knight was Sir Persant of Inde, the most powerful of the three (150-036). Together they commanded a substantial armed following, with Perimones himself leading three score knights. The brothers held lands and castles in the region surrounding the Castle Perilous, and their collective submission to [[Sir Gareth]] marked one of the young knight's most significant early victories.

## Defeat by Sir Gareth

Perimones was overcome by Sir Gareth during the same campaign in which Gareth defeated all three brothers successively. Sir Persant of Inde acknowledged the full scope of Gareth's triumph when he said: "Also ye won my brother the Red Knight, Sir Perimones" (139-026). Following his defeat, Perimones yielded himself and his three score knights to King Arthur, alongside his brothers (155-023).

The three brothers later told King Arthur how they had been overcome by a knight called Beaumains — the kitchen nickname under which Gareth had concealed his true identity — accompanied by a damosel. King Arthur marvelled at the knight's prowess, not yet knowing that Beaumains was his own nephew Gareth (150-036).

## The Tournament and Loyal Service

At a great tournament, Sir Perimones demonstrated his continued prowess by encountering [[Sir Ector de Maris]], brother of Sir Launcelot. The two knights smote each other so hard that both horses and riders fell to the earth (155-023). This passage of arms was marked by the heralds as among the notable deeds of the day.

Perimones's loyalty to Sir Gareth extended beyond martial submission. When Gareth held his great wedding feast after marrying the Lady Lionesse, Perimones came forward with three score knights and did homage and fealty to Gareth, binding himself and all his knights to hold of Gareth for evermore. He then made a personal request: that he might serve as Gareth's chief butler at the high feast (162-018). Gareth granted this office gladly, and the role of butler — a position of trust and honour in a noble household — signified the complete transformation of Perimones from adversary to devoted retainer.
