# Sir Balin

**Sir Balin**, called **Balin le Savage**, is the tragic [[knight]] of Malory's Second Book — a figure whose [[prowess]] is inseparable from disaster. Caxton's preface introduces "Balin the noble knight" (002-105), and the book that follows traces his path from obscure adventurer to the doomed slayer of his own brother [[sir-balan|Balan]].

## The Sword Adventure

Balin's story begins at [[king-arthur|King Arthur's]] court, where a [[damosel|maiden]] arrives bearing a [[sword]] that only "a passing good man of his hands and of his deeds, and without any [[treason|treachery]]" can draw. "The name of this knight was called Balin" (033-004), and by his [[virtue]] and [[worthiness]] he succeeds where all the [[round-table|Round Table]] knights have failed. When the damosel demands the sword back, Balin refuses: "for this sword will I keep" (033-030), a declaration that sets in motion his tragic fate.

## The Dolorous Stroke

Balin's most consequential act is the Dolorous Stroke — the grievous blow he strikes against King Pellam with the [[spear|Lance of Longinus]]. The preface records that the Second Book "containeth the dolorous death and departing out of this world" (002-105) of those touched by Balin's tragedy. His actions wound the [[realm|land]] itself, creating the Waste Land that only [[sir-galahad|Galahad]] can heal generations later, and his story becomes the cautionary tale of [[prowess]] without wisdom.

## The Battle of Brothers

The tragedy culminates when Balin meets his brother [[sir-balan|Balan]] in unrecognized combat. "Balin le Savage, and Balan, his brother" (021-023) — the two fight to mutual destruction, each failing to know the other until it is too late. Balin's death marks the end of the Second Book and establishes the pattern of [[tragedy]] through misrecognition that echoes through the later books of the *Morte*.
