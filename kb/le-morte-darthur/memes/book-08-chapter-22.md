[185-001]Anguish and overcame his adversary, and how his adversary would never yield

[185-002]him.

[185-003]Now make your answer that your champion is ready, for I shall die in your

[185-004]quarrel rather than to be recreant. I have no doubt of you, said the king,

[185-005]that, an ye should have ado with [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot du Lake]]— Sir, said Sir

[185-006][[sir-tristram|Tristram]], as for Sir Launcelot, he is called the noblest knight of the world,

[185-007]and wit ye well that the knights of his blood are noble men, and dread shame;

[185-008]and as for Bleoberis, brother unto Sir Blamore, I have done battle with him,

[185-009]therefore upon my head it is no shame to call him a good knight. It is noised,

[185-010]said the king, that Blamore is the hardier knight. Sir, as for that let him be,

[185-011]he shall never be refused, an as he were the best knight that now beareth

[185-012]shield or spear.

[185-013]So King Anguish departed unto King Carados and the kings that were that time as

[185-014]judges, and told them that he had found his champion ready. Then by the

[185-015]commandment of the kings Sir Blamore de Ganis and Sir Tristram were sent for to

[185-016]hear the charge. And when they were come before the judges there were many

[185-017]kings and knights beheld Sir Tristram, and much speech they had of him because

[185-018]that he slew Sir Marhaus, the good knight, and because he for-jousted Sir

[185-019]Palamides the good knight. So when they had taken their charge they withdrew

[185-020]them to make them ready to do battle.

[185-021]Then said Sir Bleoberis unto his brother, Sir Blamore: Fair dear brother,

[185-022]remember of what kin we be come of, and what a man is Sir Launcelot du Lake,

[185-023]neither farther nor nearer but brother’s children, and there was never

[185-024]none of our kin that ever was shamed in battle; and rather suffer death,

[185-025]brother, than to be shamed. Brother, said Blamore, have ye no doubt of me, for

[185-026]I shall never shame none of my blood; howbeit I am sure that yonder knight is

[185-027]called a passing good knight as of his time one of the world, yet shall I never

[185-028]yield me, nor say the loath word: well may he happen to smite me down with his

[185-029]great might of chivalry, but rather shall he slay me than I shall yield me as

[185-030]recreant. God speed you well, said Sir Bleoberis, for ye shall find him the

[185-031]mightiest knight that ever ye had ado withal, for I know him, for I have had

[185-032]ado with him. God me speed, said Sir Blamore de Ganis; and therewith he took

[185-033]his horse at the one end of the lists, and Sir Tristram at the other end of the

[185-034]lists, and so they feutred their spears and came together as it had been

[185-035]thunder; and there Sir Tristram through great might smote down Sir Blamore and

[185-036]his horse to the earth. Then anon Sir Blamore avoided his horse and pulled out

[185-037]his sword and threw his shield afore him, and bade Sir Tristram alight: For

[185-038]though an horse hath failed me, I trust to God the earth will not fail me. And

[185-039]then Sir Tristram alighted, and dressed him unto battle; and there they lashed

[185-040]together strongly as racing and tracing, foining and dashing, many sad strokes,

[185-041]that the kings and knights had great [[marvel|wonder]] that they might stand; for ever

[185-042]they fought like wood men, so that there was never knights seen [[battle|fight]] more

[185-043]fiercely than they did; for Sir Blamore was so hasty that he would have no

[185-044]rest, that all men wondered that they had breath to stand on their feet; and

[185-045]all the place was bloody that they fought in. And at the last, Sir Tristram

[185-046]smote Sir Blamore such a buffet upon the helm that he there fell down upon his

[185-047]side, and Sir Tristram stood and beheld him.
