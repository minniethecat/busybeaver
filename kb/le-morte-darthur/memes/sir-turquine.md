# Sir Turquine

Sir Turquine was one of the most formidable villainous knights in [[Le Morte d'Arthur]], a keeper of a private prison filled with defeated Knights of the [[Round Table]], and the strongest opponent [[Sir Launcelot]] ever faced in combat. His deep hatred of Launcelot, born of his brother's death, drove him to capture and imprison any Round Table knight he could defeat, making him one of the most dangerous obstacles to the fellowship of [[King Arthur]]'s court until his death at Launcelot's hands.

## The Prison of the Tour de la Fontaine

Sir Turquine established himself at a stronghold where he held many captive knights. When [[Sir Ector]] de Maris came searching for the missing Sir Launcelot, he encountered Turquine's custom: any passing knight who beat upon a basin would be challenged to combat. Ector fought and was defeated, becoming yet another prisoner (110-018). The prison already held many knights of the Round Table, including [[Gaheris]], whom Turquine led as a prisoner (115-001).

The scope of Turquine's enterprise was vast. When Launcelot finally defeated him and liberated the prison, all the knights that Sir Turquine had imprisoned came home (126-008), a testament to the scale of his depredations against Arthur's fellowship.

## The Hatred of Sir Launcelot

Sir Turquine's enmity toward Sir Launcelot was the defining passion of his career. As one damosel explained to Launcelot, "His name is Sir Turquine. And, as I understand, he hateth" Launcelot above all other knights (115-048). When Launcelot himself finally confronted Turquine and asked the reason for this hatred, Turquine revealed its source: his brother Sir Carados had been slain by Launcelot (116-020).

Sir Carados of the Dolorous Tower was Turquine's brother (153-075), and his death at Launcelot's hands transformed Turquine into a relentless hunter of Round Table knights. He made no distinction among them — any knight of Arthur's fellowship was a target for his vengeance.

## The Great Combat with Sir Launcelot

The climactic battle between Turquine and Launcelot was one of the longest and most evenly matched duels in Malory's narrative. When Launcelot arrived to rescue the prisoners, Turquine greeted him with grim delight: "Now fellow, said Sir Turquine, hold thy hand a while" (116-012), clearly recognising in his opponent a worthy challenge.

The fight was brutal and extended. Sir Turquine gave Sir Launcelot many wounds (116-038), pressing the greatest knight of the Round Table harder than almost any other adversary. Yet Launcelot's endurance proved decisive: "Then at the last Sir Turquine waxed faint" (117-002), and Launcelot slew him.

After Turquine's death, a damosel observed the justice of his fate: "for like as Sir Turquine watched to destroy knights" (118-020), so had he been destroyed. His prison was thrown open, and the liberated knights returned to the fellowship of the Round Table.

## Strength and Reputation

Sir Turquine's martial prowess was spoken of with unusual respect even by his enemies. Launcelot himself declared that "Sir Turquine was the strongest knight that" he ever encountered (126-012), a remarkable admission from the greatest knight in Christendom. This assessment carried particular weight given the number of renowned knights Turquine had defeated: [[Sir Ector]] de Maris, [[Gaheris]], Sir Lionel, and many others.

His reputation was such that even the approach of knights seeking combat against him was a matter of grave concern. He also fought with [[Sir Lamorak]] (155-008), proving that his strength extended beyond his encounters with Launcelot's kin to include other great knights of the age.

## The Nemesis of the Fellowship

Sir Turquine stands as a dark mirror to the chivalric ideal. Where a knight of the Round Table fought for honour, love, and the defence of the weak, Turquine fought for vengeance — and he was strong enough to make that vengeance a genuine threat to Arthur's fellowship. His prison represented a systematic assault on the bonds of [[knighthood]], capturing the companions of the Round Table one by one.

Yet Turquine was no coward or mere brigand. He faced Launcelot in open combat and fought to the death, giving the greatest knight of the age a battle he never forgot. In his hatred, his strength, and his final defeat, Turquine embodied the darker forces that the Round Table existed to oppose — and the cost of that opposition for both sides.
