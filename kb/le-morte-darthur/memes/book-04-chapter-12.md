[078-001]and delivered the twenty knights, and how Sir Accolon died.

[078-002]Then all the people fell down on their knees and cried [[king-arthur|King Arthur]] mercy. [[mercy|Mercy]]

[078-003]shall ye have, said Arthur: here may ye see what adventures befall ofttime of

[078-004]errant knights, how that I have fought with a knight of mine own unto my great

[078-005]damage and his both. But, sirs, because I am sore hurt, and he both, and I had

[078-006]great need of a little rest, ye shall understand the opinion betwixt you two

[078-007]brethren: As to thee, Sir Damas, for whom I have been champion and won the

[078-008][[battle|field]] of this knight, yet will I judge because ye, Sir Damas, are called an

[078-009]orgulous knight, and full of villainy, and not worth of [[courage|prowess]] your deeds,

[078-010]therefore I will that ye give unto your brother all the whole manor with the

[078-011]appurtenance, under this form, that Sir Ontzlake hold the manor of you, and

[078-012]yearly to give you a palfrey to ride upon, for that will become you better to

[078-013]ride on than upon a courser. Also I charge thee, Sir Damas, upon pain of death,

[078-014]that thou never distress no knights errant that ride on their adventure. And

[078-015]also that thou restore these twenty knights that thou hast long kept prisoners,

[078-016]of all their harness, that they be content for; and if any of them come to my

[078-017]court and complain of thee, by my head thou shalt die therefore. Also, Sir

[078-018]Ontzlake, as to you, because ye are named a good knight, and full of prowess,

[078-019]and true and gentle in all your deeds, this shall be your charge I will give

[078-020]you, that in all goodly haste ye come unto me and my court, and ye shall be a

[078-021]knight of mine, and if your deeds be thereafter I shall so prefer you, by the

[078-022]grace of God, that ye shall in short time be in ease for to live as

[078-023]worshipfully as your brother Sir Damas. God thank your largeness of your

[078-024]goodness and of your bounty, I shall be from henceforward at all times at your

[078-025]commandment; for, sir, said Sir Ontzlake, as God would, as I was hurt but late

[078-026]with an adventurous knight through both my thighs, that grieved me sore, and

[078-027]else had I done this battle with you. God would, said Arthur, it had been so,

[078-028]for then had not I been hurt as I am. I shall tell you the cause why: for I had

[078-029]not been hurt as I am, had it not been mine own sword, that was stolen from me

[078-030]by treason; and this battle was ordained aforehand to have slain me, and so it

[078-031]was brought to the purpose by false treason, and by false enchantment. Alas,

[078-032]said Sir Ontzlake, that is great pity that ever so noble a man as ye are of

[078-033]your deeds and prowess, that any man or woman might find in their hearts to

[078-034]work any treason against you. I shall reward them, said Arthur, in short time,

[078-035]by the grace of God. Now, tell me, said Arthur, how far am I from [[camelot|Camelot]]? Sir,

[078-036]ye are two days’ journey therefrom. I would fain be at some place of

[078-037][[honour|worship]], said Sir Arthur, that I might rest me. Sir, said Sir Ontzlake, hereby

[078-038]is a rich abbey of your elders’ foundation, of nuns, but three miles

[078-039]hence. So the king took his leave of all the people, and mounted upon

[078-040]horseback, and Sir Accolon with him. And when they were come to the abbey, he

[078-041]let fetch leeches and search his wounds and Accolon’s both; but Sir

[078-042]Accolon died within four days, for he had bled so much blood that he might not

[078-043]live, but King Arthur was well recovered. So when Accolon was dead he let send

[078-044]him on an horse-bier with six knights unto Camelot, and said: Bear him to my

[078-045]sister [[morgan-le-fay|Morgan le Fay]], and say that I send her him to a present, and tell her I

[078-046]have my sword [[sword-in-the-stone|Excalibur]] and the scabbard; so they departed with the body.
