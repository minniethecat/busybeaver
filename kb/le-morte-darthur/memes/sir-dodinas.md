# Sir Dodinas le Savage

Sir Dodinas le Savage was a [[Knight of the Round Table]] whose name recurs across the chronicle in the great assemblies of knights — in tournaments, in the lists of Arthur's companions, and in the fellowship of those who rode together in peace and war. His epithet "le Savage" sets him apart from the many other knights named Dodinas, marking him as a figure of wild or fierce renown.

## Among the Round Table Fellowship

Dodinas appears consistently in the rolls of Arthur's knights. He is listed among the company that included "le Desirous, Sir Osanna, Sir Dodinas le Savage, and Sir Felot" (091-050), placing him in the fellowship of named knights of the court. In another enumeration he rides "agramore le Desirous, and Sir Dodinas le" (155-034) Savage, paired with the knight whose epithet — "le Desirous" — suggests a complementary quality to Dodinas's own "le Savage."

The chronicle later numbers him among the Round Table's great gathering: "Agravaine, Bagdemagus, Kay, Dodinas le Savage" (172-039), listing him alongside knights of the first rank. And again: "other hight Dodinas le Savage" (178-045), his name recurring as one the chronicler deemed worth recording among the many who served Arthur.

## In Tournament and Combat

Dodinas's most vivid appearance comes in the lists, where he faced the great knights of the age. When the chronicle records that "When Sir Dodinas le Savage heard him say so he" (179-020) responded to a challenge, the knight's fighting spirit is made plain. The impact of his blow was such that "it had been thunder. And Sir Dodinas' spear brast in-sunder, but" (179-022) his opponent withstood the shock. The shattering of his spear — and the thunderous sound it made — speaks to the force with which Dodinas rode.

He fought alongside Sir Sagramore: "well, said Sir Sagramore and Dodinas. Sir" (179-041), the two knights acting in concert, their names linked in the record as companions in arms.

## The Company of Launcelot

In a later passage, Dodinas is found in the company of the greatest knight of the age: "Launcelot, and Sir Dodinas le Savage took their horses" (237-015), riding out with [[Sir Launcelot]] himself. To be named alongside Launcelot in the chronicle is a mark of standing — Dodinas was no obscure knight but one whose company was recorded when the great names gathered.

## The Savage Knight

The chronicle names many knights, but few carry an epithet as evocative as "le Savage." It hints at a fierceness in combat — the same fierceness that made his spear-strike sound like thunder — or perhaps a wildness of spirit that set him apart from the courtly knights of Camelot. Whatever its origin, the name Dodinas le Savage recurs through the chronicle as one of the reliable presences of the Round Table, a knight who rode when called and struck with force when he met his opponent in the lists.
