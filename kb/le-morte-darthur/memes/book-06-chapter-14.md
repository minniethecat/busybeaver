[122-001]into a castle, where he found a dead knight, and how he after was required of a

[122-002]damosel to heal her brother.

[122-003]Now leave we there and speak of [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]] that rode a great while in a deep

[122-004]forest, where he saw a black brachet, [[quest|seeking]] in manner as it had been in the

[122-005]feute of an hurt deer. And therewith he rode after the brachet, and he saw lie

[122-006]on the ground a large feute of blood. And then Sir Launcelot rode after. And

[122-007]ever the brachet looked behind her, and so she went through a great marsh, and

[122-008]ever Sir Launcelot followed. And then was he ware of an old manor, and thither

[122-009]ran the brachet, and so over the bridge. So Sir Launcelot rode over that bridge

[122-010]that was old and feeble; and when he came in midst of a great hall, there he

[122-011]saw lie a dead knight that was a seemly man, and that brachet licked his

[122-012]wounds. And therewithal came out a lady weeping and wringing her hands; and

[122-013]then she said, O knight, too much sorrow hast thou brought me. Why say ye so?

[122-014]said Sir Launcelot, I did never this knight no harm, for hither by feute of

[122-015]blood this brachet brought me; and therefore, fair lady, be not displeased with

[122-016]me, for I am full sore aggrieved of your grievance. Truly, sir, she said, I

[122-017]trow it be not ye that hath slain my husband, for he that did that deed is sore

[122-018]wounded, and he is never likely to recover, that shall I ensure him. What was

[122-019]your husband’s name? said Sir Launcelot. Sir, said she, his name was

[122-020]called Sir Gilbert the Bastard, one of the best knights of the world, and he

[122-021]that hath slain him I know not his name. Now God send you better comfort, said

[122-022]Sir Launcelot; and so he departed and went into the forest again, and there he

[122-023]met with a damosel, the which knew him well, and she said aloud, Well be ye

[122-024]found, my lord; and now I require thee, on thy knighthood, help my brother that

[122-025]is sore wounded, and never stinteth bleeding; for this day he fought with Sir

[122-026]Gilbert the Bastard and slew him in plain battle, and there was my brother sore

[122-027]wounded, and there is a lady a sorceress that dwelleth in a castle here beside,

[122-028]and this day she told me my brother’s wounds should never be whole till I

[122-029]could find a knight that would go into the Chapel Perilous, and there he should

[122-030]find a sword and a bloody cloth that the wounded knight was lapped in, and a

[122-031]piece of that cloth and sword should heal my brother’s wounds, so that

[122-032]his wounds were searched with the sword and the cloth. This is a [[marvel|marvellous]]

[122-033]thing, said Sir Launcelot, but what is your brother’s name? Sir, she

[122-034]said, his name was Sir Meliot de Logres. That me repenteth, said Sir Launcelot,

[122-035]for he is a fellow of the [[round-table|Table Round]], and to his help I will do my power.

[122-036]Then, sir, said she, follow even this highway, and it will bring you unto the

[122-037]Chapel Perilous; and here I shall abide till God send you here again, and, but

[122-038]you speed, I know no knight living that may achieve that adventure.
