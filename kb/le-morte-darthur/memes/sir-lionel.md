# Sir Lionel

Sir Lionel was the son of [[King Bors]] of Gaul, the brother of [[Sir Bors|Sir Bors de Ganis]], and cousin to [[Sir Launcelot]]. He was a [[Knight of the Round Table]] who fought in the Roman War alongside [[Sir Gawaine]] and his brother Bors, and later participated in the Quest of the [[Sangreal|Holy Grail]].

## Kinsman of Launcelot

Lionel belonged to the lineage of King Bors of Gaul, making him first cousin to Sir Launcelot. The chronicles record him among Arthur's "nephews and cousins, as Sir Lionel" (154-006), confirming his place within the innermost circle of the royal kinship. His brother, Sir Bors de Ganis, was one of the three knights who achieved the Holy Grail, and their bond was one of the defining relationships of Lionel's story.

## The Roman War Campaign

When King Arthur led his host against the Emperor Lucius of Rome, Lionel served in the vanguard. Arthur "called Sir Gawaine, Sir Bors, Sir Lionel, and Sir Bedivere" (101-002) to his council, entrusting them with the most critical commands. Lionel and [[Sir Bedivere]] were deployed together — "left in a bushment Sir Lionel and Sir Bedivere" (101-010) — lying in ambush against the Roman forces. The chronicle records that "Sir Lionel and Sir Bedivere were hoving" (101-022) during the battle, positioning themselves for the decisive stroke that broke the Roman advance.

## The Quest of the Holy Grail

Lionel's role in the Grail Quest reveals both his knightly valour and the perils that attend the sacred search. As the knights departed from the court, the chronicle tells "how Sir Lionel left him sleeping and was" (109-001) separated from his brother Bors. Overcome by "great lust to sleep," Lionel "espied a great apple-tree" (109-022) and rested beneath its branches, laying his sword under his head.

While Lionel slept, danger approached. A knight came upon him and "smote Sir Lionel so hard that horse and man he" (109-042) struck him down, capturing him. His brother Bors later "saw his brother's shield, Sir Lionel" (110-015), and knew that Lionel had been taken.

## The Bond of Brotherhood

The relationship between Lionel and Bors forms one of the Grail Quest's most human episodes. After Lionel was captured, "how his brother Sir Lionel was departed from him he wist" (114-016) — Bors knew not where his brother had been taken, and the uncertainty weighed upon him throughout the quest. Even as Bors pursued the highest spiritual calling, the thought of "Sir Lionel, that went from him" (115-032) haunted his steps.

Later in the chronicle, Lionel appears again among the great company of knights, riding beside "Sir Ector de Maris and Sir Lionel" (117-023) in tournament, and fighting alongside his kinsmen. His name is invoked in prayer — "Sir Lionel and Ector de Maris he prayeth" (117-037) — as one whose fellowship was valued by his companions.

## The Kinsman's Fate

Sir Lionel stands at the intersection of the Arthurian world's greatest lineages and its most sacred quests. As brother to one of the Grail achievers and cousin to the greatest knight of the age, Lionel was never far from the centre of the chronicle's most significant events. Yet his capture during the Grail Quest and the anguish it caused Bors reminds us that even the noblest knights were vulnerable — that the path of chivalry was marked as much by loss and separation as by triumph.
