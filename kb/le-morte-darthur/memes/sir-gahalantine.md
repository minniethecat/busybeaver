# Sir Gahalantine

Sir Gahalantine is a [[Knight of the Round Table]] who appears in the chronicle as a formidable tournament combatant. He fought alongside [[Sir Mador de la Porte]] and [[Sir Mordred]] at the tournament before the Castle of the Hard Rock, and later served as a witness to the great deeds of [[Sir Launcelot]].

## The Tournament at the Castle of the Hard Rock

Sir Gahalantine distinguished himself at a notable tournament where he fought on the side of the [[King of Northgalis]] against the knights of [[King Arthur]]. Together with Sir Mador de la Porte and Sir Mordred, he formed a trio so effective that, as the King of Northgalis reported to Sir Launcelot, "against them three I nor my knights might bear no strength" (114-022). This testimony placed Gahalantine among the elite warriors of the tournament circuit.

## Combat with Sir Launcelot

When Sir Launcelot entered the tournament, he confronted Sir Gahalantine directly in a passage of arms described with characteristic vigour. Gahalantine came against Launcelot "with a great spear," and the two knights drove at each other with such force that both spears shattered to their hands. They then drew swords and exchanged many grim strokes (115-015).

The duel ended when the enraged Sir Launcelot struck Gahalantine such a blow on the helm that "his nose brast out on blood, and ears and mouth both, and therewith his head hung low" (115-019). Gahalantine's horse carried him from the field and he fell to the earth, defeated by the greatest knight of the age.

## Witness to Launcelot's Deeds

Sir Gahalantine later appeared as a key witness to Sir Launcelot's exploits between the King of Northgalis and [[King Bagdemagus]]. When the truth of these deeds — including how Launcelot had been imprisoned by four sorceress queens and delivered by King Bagdemagus's daughter — was recounted at court, Gahalantine was among those who could attest to them personally, having been present at the same tournament (126-028). His testimony, alongside that of Sir Mador de la Porte and Sir Mordred, lent credibility to the accounts of Launcelot's adventures.
