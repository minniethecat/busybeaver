[186-003]Then when Sir Blamore might speak, he said thus: Sir Tristram de Liones, I

[186-004]require thee, as thou art a noble knight, and the best knight that ever I

[186-005]found, that thou wilt slay me out, for I would not live to be made lord of all

[186-006]the earth, for I have liefer die with [[honour|worship]] than live with shame; and needs,

[186-007]Sir Tristram, thou must slay me, or else thou shalt never win the [[battle|field]], for I

[186-008]will never say the loath word. And therefore if thou dare slay me, slay me, I

[186-009]require thee. When Sir Tristram heard him say so knightly, he wist not what to

[186-010]do with him; he remembering him of both parties, of what blood he was come, and

[186-011]for [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]]’s sake he would be loath to slay him; and in the other

[186-012]party in no wise he might not choose, but that he must make him to say the

[186-013]loath word, or else to slay him.

[186-014]Then Sir Tristram stert aback, and went to the kings that were judges, and

[186-015]there he kneeled down to-fore them, and besought them for their worships, and

[186-016]for [[king-arthur|King Arthur]]’s and Sir Launcelot’s sake, that they would take

[186-017]this matter in their hands. For, my fair lords, said Sir Tristram, it were

[186-018]shame and [[mercy|pity]] that this noble knight that yonder lieth should be slain; for ye

[186-019]hear well, shamed will he not be, and I pray to God that he never be slain nor

[186-020]shamed for me. And as for the king for whom I fight for, I shall require him,

[186-021]as I am his true champion and true knight in this field, that he will have

[186-022]mercy upon this good knight. So God me help, said King Anguish, I will for your

[186-023]sake; Sir Tristram, be ruled as ye will have me, for I know you for my true

[186-024]knight; and therefore I will heartily pray the kings that be here as judges to

[186-025]take it in their hands. And the kings that were judges called Sir Bleoberis to

[186-026]them, and asked him his advice. My lords, said Bleoberis, though my brother be

[186-027]beaten, and hath the worse through might of arms, I dare say, though Sir

[186-028]Tristram hath beaten his body he hath not beaten his heart, and I thank God he

[186-029]is not shamed this day; and rather than he should be shamed I require you, said

[186-030]Bleoberis, let Sir Tristram slay him out. It shall not be so, said the kings,

[186-031]for his part adversary, both the king and the champion, have pity of Sir

[186-032]Blamore’s knighthood. My lords, said Bleoberis, I will right well as ye

[186-033]will.

[186-034]Then the kings called the King of Ireland, and found him goodly and treatable.

[186-035]And then, by all their advices, Sir Tristram and Sir Bleoberis took up Sir

[186-036]Blamore, and the two brethren were accorded with King Anguish, and kissed and

[186-037]made friends for ever. And then Sir Blamore and Sir Tristram kissed together,

[186-038]and there they made their oaths that they would never none of them two brethren

[186-039]fight with Sir Tristram, and Sir Tristram made the same oath. And for that

[186-040]gentle battle all the blood of Sir Launcelot loved Sir Tristram for ever.

[186-041]Then King Anguish and Sir Tristram took their leave, and sailed into Ireland

[186-042]with great noblesse and joy. So when they were in Ireland the king let make it

[186-043]known throughout all the land how and in what manner Sir Tristram had done for

[186-044]him. Then the queen and all that there were made the most of him that they

[186-045]might. But the joy that La Beale Isoud made of Sir Tristram there might no

[186-046]tongue tell, for of all men earthly she loved him most.
