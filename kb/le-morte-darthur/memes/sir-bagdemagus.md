# Sir Bagdemagus

Sir Bagdemagus was a knight of the [[Round Table]] and a king in his own right whose story in [[Le Morte d'Arthur]] is defined by wounded pride, a quest for renown, and the determination to earn his place among the greatest knights of [[King Arthur]]'s fellowship.

## Displeasure at the Round Table

Bagdemagus first enters the chronicle in a moment of slighted honour. When the Round Table sieges—the seats reserved for the worthiest knights—were being assigned, Sir Bagdemagus was passed over in favour of [[Sir Tor]]. The chronicle records that Bagdemagus was "wonderly wroth" at this judgment and departed from the court in anger, swearing a solemn oath: "That shall I never return unto the court till men speak of me great worship" (071-022). His departure is a declaration of intent—he would not accept a diminished place among the fellowship.

## The Quest for Renown

Riding out alone to seek adventures, Bagdemagus encountered many trials that tested his prowess and chivalry. He did many adventures that proved his worth (071-032), and in time his name grew in stature across the realm. The knight who had left the court in wounded pride returned with the honour he had sought, his place among the Round Table's worthiest no longer in question.

## The Tournament of King Bagdemagus

Bagdemagus also appears as a tournament lord, leading his own party in the lists. His daughter's tournament became the site of a great contest of arms, with Bagdemagus himself entering the field to lead his knights (114-037). Though his party was at first driven back—"King Bagdemagus' party was far set aback" (114-041)—the day ultimately belonged to him: "the gree was given to King Bagdemagus" (115-027), confirming his standing as both king and knight.

## Royal Lineage

As both a king and a knight of the Round Table, Bagdemagus occupied a unique station in Arthur's world. His daughter is mentioned in connection with significant events (114-001), and his castle served as a gathering place for knights and courtiers (115-029). His dual identity as sovereign and Round Table companion placed him among the distinguished few who ruled their own lands while serving Arthur's greater fellowship.

## A Knight's Pride and A Knight's Honour

Bagdemagus's story is a meditation on the tension between pride and honour in the chivalric code. His anger at being passed over is understandable, yet he channels it not into bitterness but into a quest for genuine achievement. By earning his renown through deeds rather than demanding it as a right, Bagdemagus embodies the chivalric ideal in its truest form: worth must be proven, not claimed. His tale stands as a counterpoint to the many knights who sought honour through lineage or favour rather than through their own exertions.
