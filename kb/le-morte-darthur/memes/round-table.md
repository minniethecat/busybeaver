# Round Table

The **Round Table** is the central institution of [[king-arthur|King Arthur]]'s court in Thomas Malory's *Le Morte d'[[king-arthur|Arthur]]*. It is both a physical object — a circular table at which [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s knights assemble — and a symbolic fellowship representing the ideals of chivalric equality and mutual obligation.

## Symbol and Institution

The circular form of the table eliminates hierarchy among those seated: no knight occupies a "head" position, and all are equal in the fellowship. Caxton's preface notes among [[king-arthur|Arthur]]ian relics "at Winchester the Round Table" (002-051), treating the table itself as a tangible artifact alongside [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s other possessions.

The knights of the Round Table are collectively invoked as the measure of chivalric excellence. When a damosel seeks a champion, "all the [[baron|barons]] of the Round Table that were there at that time assayed all by row, but there might none speed" (032-038), showing the table as the pool from which the worthiest knights are drawn. Even in failure, the knights of the Round Table are the [[banner|standard]] against which all others are measured.

## The Fellowship

Membership in the Round Table fellowship is the highest [[honour|honor]] a knight can achieve. [[king-arthur|Arthur]] "bade them sit still" at the Round Table (056-006), and its members include the most [[honour|renown]]ed figures of the cycle — [[sir-kay|Sir Kay the Seneschal]] is listed among those of "the Round Table" (070-031). The fellowship represents a collective identity that transcends individual [[fealty|allegiance]].

## Tensions Within the Fellowship

The ideal of the Round Table is tested throughout Malory's narrative. Some "knights of the Round Table said that Balin did not this adventure all only by might, but by witchcraft" (033-053), revealing the internal jealousies and suspicions that undermine the fellowship's unity. This tension between the ideal and its human realities is a central theme of the Morte.
