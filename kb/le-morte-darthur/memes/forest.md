The **forest** in Malory's [[Le Morte d'Arthur]] is far more than a geographic feature. It is the primary setting of knightly [[adventure]]—the space where a [[knight]] leaves the known world of court and castle and enters a realm governed by different rules. In the forest, identities are concealed and revealed, hermits offer [[counsel]], madmen wander, and the [[marvel|supernatural]] intrudes into the mundane. The forest is the narrative engine of romance: to enter it is to relinquish control and to submit to whatever the forest chooses to offer.

## The Forest as Adventure Space

When a knight rides into the forest, he crosses a threshold. The court is a space of social order, of known relationships and predictable encounters; the forest is the opposite:

> And so they rode throughout a forest (059-031)

This simple formula—"they rode throughout a forest"—is one of Malory's most consequential narrative transitions. It signals that the governed world has been left behind and that whatever happens next will be shaped by chance, by [[miracle|providence]], or by the forest's own inscrutable logic. In the forest, a knight may encounter a [[maiden|damsel]] with a [[quest]], a pavilion with a challenging knight, or a [[hermit]] with spiritual guidance. The forest contains all possibilities.

## The Forest of Bedegraine

Some forests in Malory are named and function almost as characters in their own right. The forest of Bedegraine shelters an army of ten thousand (014-049), and its vastness enables the strategic deceptions of the early campaigns. Forests serve as mustering grounds, places of concealment for armies, and—as at the "castles that stand in the forest" (020-055)—the interface between wild space and fortified settlement. The named forest is a known quantity within the unknown, a [[realm|land]]mark in the uncharted.

## The Forest as Spiritual Landscape

The forest is also the domain of the [[hermit]] and the holy man. Knights in quest of the [[Sangreal]] pass through forests to reach the chapels and hermitages where spiritual truth is revealed. The forest strips away the distractions of courtly life and exposes the knight to the essentials of his [[faith]] and his [[sin]]. It is in the forest that [[Sir Launcelot|Launcelot]] receives the most searching spiritual examination of his life, and it is in the forest that knights who have lost their way—both literally and spiritually—find the path restored through grace.

## The Forest and Madness

The forest is also the space of madness. Knights who lose their reason—as [[sir-launcelot|Launcelot]] does after being banished by [[Queen Guenever]]—flee to the forest and live as wild men, stripped of [[arms|armour]], speech, and identity. The forest thus accommodates the full spectrum of human experience, from the highest spiritual illumination to the most abject loss of self. It is the space where the norms of [[chivalry]] are suspended and where the knight confronts what he is without the structures that define him.
