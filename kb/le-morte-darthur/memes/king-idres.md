# King Idres

King Idres was one of the rebel kings who opposed [[King Arthur]] in the early wars of consolidation following Arthur's coronation. He fought alongside [[King Lot]], [[King Nentres]], King Brandegoris, and King Agwisance in the great coalition that challenged Arthur's right to the throne. His story illustrates the pattern of resistance and submission that defined the first phase of Arthur's reign.

## The Rebellion Against Arthur

When the young [[King Arthur]] was crowned, many of the established kings of Britain refused to accept a boy king of uncertain lineage. Idres was among the first to swear opposition. At the great feast where the rebel kings gathered, "there swore King Idres" (015-019) to bring his forces against Arthur alongside the other dissenting monarchs.

The coalition fielded a formidable army. Alongside [[King Lot]] of Orkney, [[King Nentres]] of Garlot, King Brandegoris, and King Agwisance of Ireland, Idres mustered his men and marched against the new king. Together they represented the old guard of British kingship — established rulers who saw Arthur as an upstart backed by the wizard [[Merlin]] rather than by right of blood.

## The Battle and Its Reversals

The war went badly for the rebels. At the first great battle, "King Brandegoris, and King Idres, and King Agwisance" found their forces pressed hard (017-033). Idres fought on foot after losing his horse — a dangerous position for a commander — and was nearly taken. In a striking moment of battlefield solidarity, the King of the Hundred Knights "saw King Idres on foot; then he ran unto him" and gave Idres "horse and man" (017-044 to 017-045), remounting him at personal risk.

This rescue proved crucial, allowing Idres to continue fighting through the day. But the tide turned inexorably against the rebels. Despite their numbers — "King Lot, King Nentres, King Brandegoris, King Idres, King Uriens, and King Agwisance" with eleven kings in total (018-012) — they were overwhelmed by Arthur's forces and forced from the field.

## Defeat and Submission

The final engagement brought disaster for the rebel cause. Two of Arthur's knights "met with King Idres" on the field (018-033), and the slaughter of his men was so severe that "King Idres was near at hand to be taken" (018-035). The coalition shattered, and Arthur pressed his advantage relentlessly. Merlin himself counselled Arthur to pursue them "into their own countries" (019-071), ensuring the rebellion could not reform.

In the aftermath, Arthur dealt with the defeated kings according to his characteristic policy of mercy to those who submitted. "King Idres and King Brandegoris" were among those who survived the war and made their peace (019-071). Idres was "put in the City of Nauntes in Brittany" (021-037) — effectively exiled or garrisoned in a distant holding, removed from the centre of British power but permitted to live and retain a measure of his dignity.

## The Cost of Resistance

King Idres embodies the fate of those who resisted the Arthurian order. His story serves a structural function in [[Le Morte d'Arthur]]: the rebel kings demonstrate that Arthur's kingship was not peacefully inherited but fought for and won against determined opposition. Idres's trajectory — from defiant oath-swearing, through desperate battle, to final submission and exile to Brittany — traces the arc of the old order giving way to the new. His presence in the narrative, alongside his fellow rebels, establishes that the [[Round Table]] was built upon the ruins of an older, more fragmented Britain, and that Arthur's peace was achieved through war before it was sustained through chivalry.
