# King Agwisance

King Agwisance of Ireland was one of the great enemy kings who opposed King Arthur in the wars of his early reign. He brought five thousand men of arms from Ireland and fought alongside Kings Lot, Idres, and Brandegoris against Arthur's forces. His presence at the major battles of the early books marks him as one of the principal adversaries Arthur had to overcome to secure his throne.

## The Alliance Against Arthur

In the early days of Arthur's reign, many kings and lords rose against the young king, refusing to recognize his sovereignty. King Agwisance of Ireland swore to bring five thousand men of arms to the cause of the rebel kings (015-022). He joined the great host that assembled under the leadership of King Lot of Orkney, King Idres of Cornwall, King Brandegoris, and the Duke of Cambenet.

At the great battle before Castle Terrabil, King Agwisance stood among the rebel kings, including King Brandegoris, King Idres, and King Agwisance, and the medley of battle waxed passing hard (017-033).

## The Slaying of Sir Lucas

During the battle, Sir Lucas the Butler saw King Agwisance, who had late slain Sir Moris de la Roche (018-001). Sir Lucas, enraged by the death of his fellow knight, engaged King Agwisance fiercely. With the help of Sir Kay the Seneschal, Sir Lucas overcame and slew King Agwisance's forces (018-013).

## The Defeat and Oath

King Agwisance was with the remaining rebel kings — King Idres and the Duke of Cambenet — when they were pressed hard by Arthur's knights (018-026). When they saw the tide of battle turning definitively against them, King Agwisance and the others swore they would never fight against Arthur again, and yielded themselves unto the king's grace (019-073).

## Later Years

Despite his earlier opposition, King Agwisance appears later in the chronicle in a different light. At the great tournament where Sir Gareth won honour against the knights of the Round Table, King Agwisance of Ireland was present. When he saw Sir Gareth fare so mightily against all comers, he encountered the unknown knight himself — and was smitten down by Sir Gareth's hand (155-036, 155-040).
