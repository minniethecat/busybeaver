[168-001][[battle|fight]] for the truage of Cornwall, and how he was made knight.

[168-002]Therewithal [[sir-tristram|Tristram]] went unto his father, King Meliodas, and asked him counsel

[168-003]what was best to do for to recover Cornwall from truage. For, as meseemeth,

[168-004]said Sir Tristram, it were shame that Sir Marhaus, the queen’s brother of

[168-005]Ireland, should go away unless that he were foughten withal. As for that, said

[168-006]King Meliodas, wit you well, son Tristram, that Sir Marhaus is called one of

[168-007]the best knights of the world, and Knight of the [[round-table|Table Round]]; and therefore I

[168-008]know no knight in this country that is able to match with him. Alas, said Sir

[168-009]Tristram, that I am not made knight; and if Sir Marhaus should thus depart into

[168-010]Ireland, God let me never have [[honour|worship]]: an I were made knight I should match

[168-011]him. And sir, said Tristram, I pray you give me leave to ride to [[king-mark|King Mark]];

[168-012]and, so ye be not displeased, of King Mark will I be made knight. I will well,

[168-013]said King Meliodas, that ye be ruled as your courage will rule you. Then Sir

[168-014]Tristram thanked his father much. And then he made him ready to ride into

[168-015]Cornwall.

[168-016]In the meanwhile there came a messenger with letters of love from King Faramon

[168-017]of France’s daughter unto Sir Tristram, that were full piteous letters,

[168-018]and in them were written many complaints of love; but Sir Tristram had no joy

[168-019]of her letters nor regard unto her. Also she sent him a little brachet that was

[168-020]passing fair. But when the king’s daughter understood that Sir Tristram

[168-021]would not love her, as the book saith, she died for sorrow. And then the same

[168-022]squire that brought the letter and the brachet came again unto Sir Tristram, as

[168-023]after ye shall hear in the tale.

[168-024]So this young Sir Tristram rode unto his eme, King Mark of Cornwall. And when

[168-025]he came there he heard say that there would no knight fight with Sir Marhaus.

[168-026]Then yede Sir Tristram unto his eme and said: Sir, if ye will give me the order

[168-027]of knighthood, I will do battle with Sir Marhaus. What are ye, said the king,

[168-028]and from whence be ye come? Sir, said Tristram, I come from King Meliodas that

[168-029]wedded your sister, and a gentleman wit ye well I am. King Mark beheld Sir

[168-030]Tristram and saw that he was but a young man of age, but he was passingly well

[168-031]made and big. Fair sir, said the king, what is your name, and where were ye

[168-032]born? Sir, said he again, my name is Tristram, and in the country of Liones was

[168-033]I born. Ye say well, said the king; and if ye will do this battle I shall make

[168-034]you knight. Therefore I come to you, said Sir Tristram, and for none other

[168-035]cause. But then King Mark made him knight. And therewithal, anon as he had made

[168-036]him knight, he sent a messenger unto Sir Marhaus with letters that said that he

[168-037]had found a young knight ready for to take the battle to the uttermost. It may

[168-038]well be, said Sir Marhaus; but tell King Mark I will not fight with no knight

[168-039]but he be of blood royal, that is to say, other king’s son, other

[168-040]queen’s son, born of a prince or princess.

[168-041]When King Mark understood that, he sent for Sir Tristram de Liones and told him

[168-042]what was the answer of Sir Marhaus. Then said Sir Tristram: Sithen that he

[168-043]saith so, let him wit that I am come of father side and mother side of as noble

[168-044]blood as he is: for, sir, now shall ye know that I am King Meliodas’ son,

[168-045]born of your own sister, Dame Elizabeth, that died in the forest in the birth

[168-046]of me. O Jesu, said King Mark, ye are welcome fair nephew to me. Then in all

[168-047]the haste the king let horse Sir Tristram, and armed him in the best manner

[168-048]that might be had or gotten for gold or silver. And then King Mark sent unto

[168-049]Sir Marhaus, and did him to wit that a better born man than he was himself

[168-050]should fight with him, and his name is Sir Tristram de Liones, gotten of King

[168-051]Meliodas, and born of King Mark’s sister. Then was Sir Marhaus glad and

[168-052]blithe that he should fight with such a gentleman. And so by the assent of King

[168-053]Mark and of Sir Marhaus they let ordain that they should fight within an island

[168-054]nigh Sir Marhaus’ ships; and so was Sir Tristram put into a vessel both

[168-055]his horse and he, and all that to him longed both for his body and for his

[168-056]horse. Sir Tristram lacked nothing. And when King Mark and his barons of

[168-057]Cornwall beheld how young Sir Tristram departed with such a carriage to fight

[168-058]for the right of Cornwall, there was neither man nor woman of worship but they

[168-059]wept to see and understand so young a knight to jeopardy himself for their

[168-060]right.
