# King Uriens

King Uriens was the ruler of the land of Gore, the husband of [[Morgan le Fay]], and one of the kings of early Britain whose relationship with [[King Arthur]] evolved from rebellion to alliance. His marriage to Morgan le Fay placed him at the centre of one of Arthurian legend's most dangerous domestic betrayals.

## The Early Wars

Uriens first appears among the host of kings who opposed Arthur's accession. He came to the feast of Arthur's coronation with four hundred knights (011-005), an impressive retinue that reflected his power among the regional rulers. During the great rebellion, he fought alongside King Brandegoris, King Idres, and other rebel kings against Arthur's forces (018-012), and was counted among the opposition at the great battle where "King Uriens and King Carados" faced Arthur, Ban, and Bors (019-071).

After the rebellion was crushed, Uriens became one of Arthur's allies. Arthur's army "refreshed them within King Uriens's city" during a later campaign (021-026), suggesting that he had made peace with the crown and integrated into the Arthurian polity. He later attended tournaments with "a noble company of knights" (042-003), maintaining his dignity as one of the great lords of Britain.

## Morgan le Fay and Betrayal

Uriens's marriage to Morgan le Fay, Arthur's half-sister, was the defining personal relationship of his life — and nearly the cause of his death. Morgan, "King Uriens's wife" (077-006), harboured ambitions far exceeding her station as queen of Gore. She "would slay her husband King Uriens lightly" (077-011), plotting his murder so that she might be free of him.

The moment of crisis came when Morgan "espied King Uriens lay in his bed sleeping" (079-003) and approached with a sword, preparing to strike the fatal blow. Only her son [[Sir Uwaine]]'s sudden intervention saved Uriens's life. Uwaine confronted his mother, who could not bring herself to slay her own son, and the plot was exposed.

## Arthur's Judgment

When Arthur learned of the plot against Uriens, he confronted Morgan directly with the evidence: "he told her to-forehand, and said unto King Uriens, My sister, your wife, would have slain you" (082-013). Arthur offered Uriens the justice he saw fit, but the king's response was remarkably measured: "I deem not greatly that ye be in fault" (082-016), accepting that his wife's treachery was not Arthur's responsibility.

This episode demonstrated Uriens's political wisdom. Rather than demanding vengeance against Arthur's own sister, he accepted the situation with dignity, preserving his alliance with the crown while dealing privately with his wife's treachery. Morgan fled the court after her plot was exposed, and Uriens remained a loyal vassal.

## Later Appearances

Uriens continued to appear in the Arthurian world as a respected elder king. He participated in the great tournaments of the middle books, where [[Sir Gareth]] "in the same wise served King Uriens of the land of Gore" (155-043) in the lists. His son, Sir Uwaine, became one of the most valiant Knights of the [[Round Table]], and Uriens himself was counted among the lords who attended the great feast at [[Camelot]] (042-025).

His presence at Arthur's court, alongside King Brandegoris and other former rebels, represented the successful consolidation of the Arthurian realm — a kingdom built from once-warring regional lords into a unified fellowship.

## Survival Amid Treachery

King Uriens represents a figure who survived the treacherous currents of Arthurian politics through a combination of dignity, restraint, and loyal alliance. Married to one of the most dangerous women in the legend, he escaped assassination by a hair's breadth and chose the path of measured reconciliation rather than escalated vengeance. His son Uwaine's heroism and his own continued presence at court mark him as a successful survivor in a world where many kings fell to sword or betrayal.
