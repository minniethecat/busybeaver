[123-001]Chapel Perilous and gat there of a dead corpse a piece of the cloth and a

[123-002]sword.

[123-003]Right so [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]] departed, and when he came unto the Chapel Perilous he

[123-004]alighted down, and tied his horse unto a little gate. And as soon as he was

[123-005]within the churchyard he saw on the front of the chapel many fair rich shields

[123-006]turned up-so-down, and many of the shields Sir Launcelot had seen knights bear

[123-007]beforehand. With that he saw by him there stand a thirty great knights, more by

[123-008]a yard than any man that ever he had seen, and all those grinned and gnashed at

[123-009]Sir Launcelot. And when he saw their countenance he dreaded him sore, and so

[123-010]put his shield afore him, and took his sword ready in his hand ready unto

[123-011]battle, and they were all armed in black harness ready with their shields and

[123-012]their swords drawn. And when Sir Launcelot would have gone throughout them,

[123-013]they scattered on every side of him, and gave him the way, and therewith he

[123-014]waxed all bold, and entered into the chapel, and then he saw no light but a dim

[123-015]lamp burning, and then was he ware of a corpse hilled with a cloth of silk.

[123-016]Then Sir Launcelot stooped down, and cut a piece away of that cloth, and then

[123-017]it fared under him as the earth had quaked a little; therewithal he feared. And

[123-018]then he saw a fair sword lie by the dead knight, and that he gat in his hand

[123-019]and hied him out of the chapel.

[123-020]Anon as ever he was in the chapel yard all the knights spake to him with a

[123-021]grimly voice, and said, Knight, Sir Launcelot, lay that sword from thee or else

[123-022]thou shalt die. Whether that I live or die, said Sir Launcelot, with no great

[123-023]word get ye it again, therefore [[battle|fight]] for it an ye list. Then right so he

[123-024]passed throughout them, and beyond the chapel yard there met him a fair

[123-025]damosel, and said, Sir Launcelot, leave that sword behind thee, or thou wilt

[123-026]die for it. I leave it not, said Sir Launcelot, for no treaties. No, said she,

[123-027]an thou didst leave that sword, [[queen-guenever|Queen Guenever]] should thou never see. Then were

[123-028]I a fool an I would leave this sword, said Launcelot. Now, gentle knight, said

[123-029]the damosel, I require thee to kiss me but once. Nay, said Sir Launcelot, that

[123-030]God me forbid. Well, sir, said she, an thou hadst kissed me thy life days had

[123-031]been done, but now, alas, she said, I have lost all my labour, for I ordained

[123-032]this chapel for thy sake, and for [[sir-gawaine|Sir Gawaine]]. And once I had Sir Gawaine

[123-033]within me, and at that time he fought with that knight that lieth there dead in

[123-034]yonder chapel, Sir Gilbert the Bastard; and at that time he smote the left hand

[123-035]off of Sir Gilbert the Bastard. And, Sir Launcelot, now I tell thee, I have

[123-036]loved thee this seven year, but there may no woman have thy love but Queen

[123-037]Guenever. But sithen I may not rejoice thee to have thy body alive, I had kept

[123-038]no more joy in this world but to have thy body dead. Then would I have balmed

[123-039]it and served it, and so have kept it my life days, and daily I should have

[123-040]clipped thee, and kissed thee, in despite of Queen Guenever. Ye say well, said

[123-041]Sir Launcelot, Jesu preserve me from your subtle crafts. And therewithal he

[123-042]took his horse and so departed from her. And as the book saith, when Sir

[123-043]Launcelot was departed she took such sorrow that she died within a fourteen

[123-044]night, and her name was Hellawes the sorceress, Lady of the Castle Nigramous.

[123-045]Anon Sir Launcelot met with the damosel, Sir Meliot’s sister. And when

[123-046]she saw him she clapped her hands, and wept for joy. And then they rode unto a

[123-047]castle thereby where lay Sir Meliot. And anon as Sir Launcelot saw him he knew

[123-048]him, but he was passing pale, as the earth, for bleeding. When Sir Meliot saw

[123-049]Sir Launcelot he kneeled upon his knees and cried on high: O lord Sir

[123-050]Launcelot, help me! Anon Sir Launcelot leapt unto him and touched his wounds

[123-051]with Sir Gilbert’s sword. And then he wiped his wounds with a part of the

[123-052]bloody cloth that Sir Gilbert was wrapped in, and anon an wholer man in his

[123-053]life was he never. And then there was great joy between them, and they made Sir

[123-054]Launcelot all the cheer that they might, and so on the morn Sir Launcelot took

[123-055]his leave, and bade Sir Meliot hie him to the court of my lord [[king-arthur|Arthur]], for it

[123-056]draweth nigh to the Feast of Pentecost, and there by the grace of God ye shall

[123-057]find me. And therewith they departed.
