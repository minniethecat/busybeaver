# Adventure

**Adventure** is the narrative pulse of Thomas Malory's *Le Morte d'[[king-arthur|Arthur]]*. It is the knight's encounter with the unexpected — a sudden meeting in a forest, a challenge at a bridge, a damosel's plea for aid. Adventures are at once random occurrences and providentially arranged tests, and a knight's response to adventure defines his character.

## The Fabric of Arthurian Narrative

Caxton's preface promises "many [[marvel|wonder]]ful histories and adventures" (002-101), and the word recurs throughout his summary of the book's contents. Book VI treats "of [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot du Lake]], and of many marvellous adventures" (002-111), while a later book covers "[[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]], and other marvellous adventures" (002-117). Adventure is not decorative but structural — it is the primary mode through which the [[king-arthur|Arthur]]ian world is experienced and narrated.

## Chance and Providence

Adventures in Malory often begin by chance. Ulfius "by adventure met [[merlin|Merlin]] in a beggar's array" (004-039), a meeting that seems accidental but proves providential. This tension between randomness and fate is essential to the adventure form: knights ride out into the forest trusting that worthy adventures will find them, and they do. The world of the Morte is one where the [[marvel|supernatural]] and the mundane intersect at unexpected moments.

## Adventure as Narrative Engine

The adventure serves as a self-contained narrative unit within the larger cycle: a knight departs, encounters a challenge, overcomes it (or fails), and returns — often changed. These episodes accumulate into the larger arcs of the Morte. As Malory notes, the tale continues "as it telleth in the book of adventures following" (021-048), positioning the adventure sequence as the basic building block of the ent[[wrath|ire]] work.
