# Sir Lamorak

**Sir Lamorak de Galis** is one of the foremost [[knight|knights]] of the [[round-table|Round Table]], counted among the great names of Arthurian [[chivalry]] alongside [[sir-launcelot|Launcelot]], [[sir-tristram|Tristram]], and [[sir-gawaine|Gawaine]]. He is the brother of [[sir-percivale|Sir Percivale de Galis]], and his [[prowess]] in [[joust|jousting]] and [[battle|combat]] earns him a place in Malory's catalogue of heroes.

## Marvellous Deeds of Arms

Lamorak's reputation rests on his deeds in the tournament field. "There did Sir Lamorak marvellous deeds of arms" (156-002) — the narrative repeatedly singles him out for his martial excellence. In the great tournament at the Castle of Maidens, "there jousted Sir Lamorak de Galis, for he overthrew thirty knights" (162-045), a performance that places him in the highest rank of chivalric achievement. His name is invoked as a benchmark: knights pass through perilous passages only if they are "Sir Tristram, or the good knight Sir Lamorak" (136-030).

## Among the Greatest Knights

Lamorak belongs to the inner circle of Arthurian heroes. The narrative groups him with "Sir Launcelot, Sir Tristram, Sir Lamorak, or Sir Gawaine" (141-049) — the four [[champion|champions]] whose names alone suffice to identify the highest tier of knighthood. He and his brother "Sir Percivale de Galis, and Sir Lamorak de Galis" (154-005) represent the house of King Pellinore, a [[lineage]] of great knights whose deeds and rivalries thread through the later books of the *Morte*.

## Feud and Tragedy

Lamorak's [[prowess]] does not shield him from the politics of the [[round-table|Round Table]]. The sons of [[king-lot|King Lot]] — Gawaine and his brothers — bear a feud against the house of Pellinore, and Lamorak's [[love]] affair with [[morgawse|Queen Morgawse]], Gawaine's mother, seals his doom. His death at the hands of Gawaine and his brothers is one of the darkest episodes in the Round Table's internal history — a murder driven not by [[battle|combat]] but by private vengeance, marking the decay of [[chivalry]] into blood feud.
