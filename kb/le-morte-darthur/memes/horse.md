The **horse** is the indispensable companion of the [[knight]] in Malory's [[Le Morte d'Arthur]], without which the mounted [[battle|combat]] that defines [[chivalry]] is impossible. A knight [[battle|fight]]s from horseback, travels on horseback, and is identified—in part—by the quality and condition of his mount. To lose one's horse is to be stripped of mobility, of martial advantage, and of visible status; to gain a horse is to be restored to the condition of knighthood. The horse is at once a living weapon, a marker of rank, and a narrative engine whose loss and recovery drive episodes across the romance.

## The Horse in Battle

In the [[battle|war]]fare of Malory's world, the horse is a [[battle|combat]] multiplier. [[king-arthur|King Arthur]] [[battle|fight]]s "always on horseback" (012-020), and the mounted knight has an overwhelming advantage over foot soldiers. Yet the horse is also vulnerable—"horse and man fell to the earth" is one of Malory's most recurrent phrases (014-007, 014-018), mar[[kinsman|kin]]g the moment when a [[spear|lance]]-strike unseats both mount and rider:

> his horse was slain underneath him (012-025)

The death of a horse beneath its rider leaves the knight exposed, and the act of "horsing him again" (014-010)—providing a fresh mount—is an act of comradeship that can turn the tide of an individual [[battle|combat]].

## Losing and Gaining Horses

The loss and acquisition of horses forms a recurring narrative rhythm. A knight unhorsed must either [[battle|fight]] on foot or withdraw; a knight who captures an opponent's horse gains a valuable prize. Horses are spoils of [[battle|war]], gifts of [[fealty]], and tokens of [[courtesy]]. When "there were seven hundred" knights "ready on horseback" (013-080), the number of mounted men is the measure of a [[baron|lord]]'s military power. The horse is currency as well as companion.

## The Horse-Litter

Malory's world admits one significant exception to the knight's dependence on the riding horse: the horse-litter, a conveyance for the wounded and the dying. [[Uther Pendragon|King Uther]], too ill to ride, is carried "in an horse-litter with a great host" into his final battle (007-008). The horse-litter is a paradox—a horse that carries without riding, a mount for those who can no longer mount—and its appearance signals the approach of death or the limits of bodily capacity. It is the form in which a [[kinsman|kin]]g may still lead his army when his body has failed.

## The Horse as Status Marker

The quality of a knight's horse reflects his standing. A great warhorse—a destrier—is a [[baron|lord]]ly possession, while a lesser mount marks a knight of lesser means. The gift of a fine horse is an act of [[honour]]; the seizure of a horse is an act of domination. When knights ride "well horsed and well armed" (013-033), the pairing of horse and [[arms|harness]] is the baseline condition of knightly readiness. A knight without a horse is a knight incomplete.
