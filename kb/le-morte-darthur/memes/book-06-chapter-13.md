[121-001]four knights of the [[round-table|Round Table]] and overthrew them.

[121-002]So [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]] rode into a deep forest, and thereby in a slade, he saw four

[121-003]knights hoving under an oak, and they were of [[king-arthur|Arthur]]’s court, one was Sir

[121-004]Sagramour le Desirous, and Ector de Maris, and [[sir-gawaine|Sir Gawaine]], and Sir Uwaine.

[121-005]Anon as these four knights had espied Sir Launcelot, they weened by his arms it

[121-006]had been [[sir-kay|Sir Kay]]. Now by my faith, said Sir Sagramour, I will prove Sir

[121-007]Kay’s might, and gat his spear in his hand, and came toward Sir

[121-008]Launcelot. Therewith Sir Launcelot was ware and knew him well, and feutred his

[121-009]spear against him, and smote Sir Sagramour so sore that horse and man fell both

[121-010]to the earth. Lo, my fellows, said he, yonder ye may see what a buffet he hath;

[121-011]that knight is much bigger than ever was Sir Kay. Now shall ye see what I may

[121-012]do to him. So Sir Ector gat his spear in his hand and walloped toward Sir

[121-013]Launcelot, and Sir Launcelot smote him through the shield and shoulder, that

[121-014]man and horse went to the earth, and ever his spear held.

[121-015]By my faith, said Sir Uwaine, yonder is a strong knight, and I am sure he hath

[121-016]slain Sir Kay; and I see by his great strength it will be hard to match him.

[121-017]And therewithal, Sir Uwaine gat his spear in his hand and rode toward Sir

[121-018]Launcelot, and Sir Launcelot knew him well, and so he met him on the plain, and

[121-019]gave him such a buffet that he was astonied, that long he wist not where he

[121-020]was. Now see I well, said Sir Gawaine, I must encounter with that knight. Then

[121-021]he dressed his shield and gat a good spear in his hand, and Sir Launcelot knew

[121-022]him well; and then they let run their horses with all their mights, and either

[121-023]knight smote other in midst of the shield. But Sir Gawaine’s spear

[121-024]to-brast, and Sir Launcelot charged so sore upon him that his horse reversed

[121-025]up-so-down. And much sorrow had Sir Gawaine to avoid his horse, and so Sir

[121-026]Launcelot passed on a pace and smiled, and said, God give him joy that this

[121-027]spear made, for there came never a better in my hand.

[121-028]Then the four knights went each one to other and comforted each other. What say

[121-029]ye by this guest? said Sir Gawaine, that one spear hath felled us all four. We

[121-030]commend him unto the devil, they said all, for he is a man of great might. Ye

[121-031]may well say it, said Sir Gawaine, that he is a man of might, for I dare lay my

[121-032]head it is Sir Launcelot, I know it by his riding. Let him go, said Sir

[121-033]Gawaine, for when we come to the court then shall we wit; and then had they

[121-034]much sorrow to get their horses again.
