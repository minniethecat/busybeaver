[131-003]But there went many after to behold how well he was horsed and trapped in cloth

[131-004]of gold, but he had neither shield nor spear. Then Sir Kay said all open in the

[131-005]hall, I will ride after my boy in the kitchen, to wit whether he will know me

[131-006]for his better. Said Sir Launcelot and [[sir-gawaine|Sir Gawaine]], Yet abide at home. So Sir

[131-007]Kay made him ready and took his horse and his spear, and rode after him. And

[131-008]right as [[sir-gareth|Beaumains]] overtook the damosel, right so came Sir Kay and said,

[131-009]Beaumains, what, sir, know ye not me? Then he turned his horse, and knew it was

[131-010]Sir Kay, that had done him all the despite as ye have heard afore. Yea, said

[131-011]Beaumains, I know you for an ungentle knight of the court, and therefore beware

[131-012]of me. Therewith Sir Kay put his spear in the rest, and ran straight upon him;

[131-013]and Beaumains came as fast upon him with his sword in his hand, and so he put

[131-014]away his spear with his sword, and with a foin thrust him through the side,

[131-015]that Sir Kay fell down as he had been dead; and he alighted down and took Sir

[131-016]Kay’s shield and his spear, and stert upon his own horse and rode his

[131-017]way.

[131-018]All that saw Sir Launcelot, and so did the damosel. And then he bade his dwarf

[131-019]stert upon Sir Kay’s horse, and so he did. By that Sir Launcelot was

[131-020]come, then he proffered Sir Launcelot to joust; and either made them ready, and

[131-021]they came together so fiercely that either bare down other to the earth, and

[131-022]sore were they bruised. Then Sir Launcelot arose and helped him from his horse.

[131-023]And then Beaumains threw his shield from him, and proffered to [[battle|fight]] with Sir

[131-024]Launcelot on foot; and so they rushed together like boars, tracing, rasing, and

[131-025]foining to the mountenance of an hour; and Sir Launcelot felt him so big that

[131-026]he marvelled of his strength, for he fought more liker a giant than a knight,

[131-027]and that his fighting was durable and passing perilous. For Sir Launcelot had

[131-028]so much ado with him that he dreaded himself to be shamed, and said, Beaumains,

[131-029]fight not so sore, your quarrel and mine is not so great but we may leave off.

[131-030]Truly that is truth, said Beaumains, but it doth me good to feel your might,

[131-031]and yet, my lord, I showed not the utterance.
