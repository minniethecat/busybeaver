# Sir Gilbert the Bastard

Sir Gilbert the Bastard was a knight renowned as one of the best knights of the world. He was slain in plain battle, and his body was afterwards kept in a chapel used as a snare by the sorceress Hellawes, the Lady of the Castle Nigramous.

## Reputation

Sir Gilbert was held in high esteem as a warrior. His widow described him as one of the best knights of the world, a testament to his prowess (122-020). The circumstances of his death placed his body at the centre of an enchanted trap laid for Sir Launcelot and Sir Gawaine.

## Death in Battle

Sir Gilbert was slain in plain battle by a knight whose identity is not recorded (122-026). His widow, grieving, encountered Sir Launcelot in the forest and told him of her husband's death. The knight who fought Sir Gilbert — the brother of a damosel who sought Launcelot's aid — was sore wounded in the same combat and never stinted bleeding.

## The Chapel of Hellawes

Sir Gilbert's body was laid in a chapel that served as a trap set by Hellawes the sorceress, the Lady of the Castle Nigramous. She had ordained this chapel for Sir Launcelot's sake and for Sir Gawaine's (123-034). She told Launcelot that she had once had Sir Gawaine within the chapel, and at that time Gawaine had fought with the dead knight — Sir Gilbert the Bastard — and smote the left hand off him (123-035).

Hellawes confessed she had loved Launcelot for seven years, and knowing she could never have him alive, she desired to have his body dead, so that she might have balmed it and kept it all her life days. When Launcelot departed untouched by her crafts, Hellawes took such sorrow that she died within a fortnight.

## The Healing of Sir Meliot

After Sir Launcelot escaped the sorceress, he met the damosel Sir Meliot's sister, who led him to her wounded brother. Launcelot touched Meliot's wounds with Sir Gilbert's sword, and then wiped his wounds with a part of the bloody cloth that Sir Gilbert was wrapped in (123-051). Through this touch, Sir Meliot became an wholer man than ever he was in his life (123-052). Thus Sir Gilbert's relics served as instruments of healing, resolving the very wounds his slayer had received.
