[171-001]arrived in Ireland died of the stroke that [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]] had given him, and how

[171-002]Tristram was hurt.

[171-003]Anon Sir Marhaus and his fellowship departed into Ireland. And as soon as he

[171-004]came to the king, his brother, he let search his wounds. And when his head was

[171-005]searched a piece of Sir Tristram’s sword was found therein, and might

[171-006]never be had out of his head for no surgeons, and so he died of Sir

[171-007]Tristram’s sword; and that piece of the sword the queen, his sister, kept

[171-008]it for ever with her, for she thought to be revenged an she might.

[171-009]Now turn we again unto Sir Tristram, that was sore wounded, and full sore bled

[171-010]that he might not within a little while, when he had taken cold, unnethe stir

[171-011]him of his limbs. And then he set him down softly upon a little hill, and bled

[171-012]fast. Then anon came Gouvernail, his man, with his vessel; and the king and his

[171-013]barons came with procession against him. And when he was come unto the land,

[171-014][[king-mark|King Mark]] took him in his arms, and the king and Sir Dinas, the seneschal, led

[171-015]Sir Tristram into the castle of Tintagil. And then was he searched in the best

[171-016]manner, and laid in his bed. And when King Mark saw his wounds he wept

[171-017]heartily, and so did all his lords. So God me help, said King Mark, I would not

[171-018]for all my lands that my nephew died. So Sir Tristram lay there a month and

[171-019]more, and ever he was like to die of that stroke that Sir Marhaus smote him

[171-020]first with the spear. For, as the French book saith, the spear’s head was

[171-021]envenomed, that Sir Tristram might not be whole. Then was King Mark and all his

[171-022]barons passing heavy, for they deemed none other but that Sir Tristram should

[171-023]not recover. Then the king let send after all manner of leeches and surgeons,

[171-024]both unto men and women, and there was none that would behote him the life.

[171-025]Then came there a lady that was a right wise lady, and she said plainly unto

[171-026]King Mark, and to Sir Tristram, and to all his barons, that he should never be

[171-027]whole but if Sir Tristram went in the same country that the venom came from,

[171-028]and in that country should he be holpen or else never. Thus said the lady unto

[171-029]the king.

[171-030]When King Mark understood that, he let purvey for Sir Tristram a fair vessel,

[171-031]well victualled, and therein was put Sir Tristram, and Gouvernail with him, and

[171-032]Sir Tristram took his harp with him, and so he was put into the sea to sail

[171-033]into Ireland; and so by good fortune he arrived up in Ireland, even fast by a

[171-034]castle where the king and the queen was; and at his arrival he sat and harped

[171-035]in his bed a merry lay, such one heard they never none in Ireland before that

[171-036]time.

[171-037]And when it was told the king and the queen of such a knight that was such an

[171-038]harper, anon the king sent for him, and let search his wounds, and then asked

[171-039]him his name. Then he answered, I am of the country of Liones, and my name is

[171-040]Tramtrist, that thus was wounded in a battle as I fought for a lady’s

[171-041]right. So God me help, said King Anguish, ye shall have all the help in this

[171-042]land that ye may have here; but I let you wit, in Cornwall I had a great loss

[171-043]as ever had king, for there I lost the best knight of the world; his name was

[171-044]Marhaus, a full noble knight, and Knight of the [[round-table|Table Round]]; and there he told

[171-045]Sir Tristram wherefore Sir Marhaus was slain. Sir Tristram made semblant as he

[171-046]had been sorry, and better knew he how it was than the king.
