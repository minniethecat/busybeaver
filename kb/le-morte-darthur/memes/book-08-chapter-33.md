[196-001]thirty knights, and [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]] at the request of [[king-mark|King Mark]] smote his horse

[196-002]down.

[196-003]The king and the queen made their pavilions and their tents in that forest

[196-004]beside a river, and there was daily hunting and [[tournament|jousting]], for there were ever

[196-005]thirty knights ready to joust unto all them that came in at that time. And

[196-006]there by fortune came Sir Lamorak de Galis and Sir Driant; and there Sir Driant

[196-007]jousted right well, but at the last he had a fall. Then Sir Lamorak proffered

[196-008]to joust. And when he began he fared so with the thirty knights that there was

[196-009]not one of them but that he gave him a fall, and some of them were sore hurt. I

[196-010]marvel, said King Mark, what knight he is that doth such deeds of arms. Sir,

[196-011]said Sir Tristram, I know him well for a noble knight as few now be living, and

[196-012]his name is Sir Lamorak de Galis. It were great shame, said the king, that he

[196-013]should go thus away, unless that some of you meet with him better. Sir, said

[196-014]Sir Tristram, meseemeth it were no [[honour|worship]] for a noble man to have ado with

[196-015]him: and for because at this time he hath done over much for any mean knight

[196-016]living, therefore, as meseemeth, it were great shame and villainy to tempt him

[196-017]any more at this time, insomuch as he and his horse are weary both; for the

[196-018]deeds of arms that he hath done this day, an they be well considered, it were

[196-019]enough for [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot du Lake]]. As for that, said King Mark, I require you,

[196-020]as ye love me and my lady the queen, La Beale Isoud, take your arms and joust

[196-021]with Sir Lamorak de Galis. Sir, said Sir Tristram, ye bid me do a thing that is

[196-022]against knighthood, and well I can deem that I shall give him a fall, for it is

[196-023]no mastery, for my horse and I be fresh both, and so is not his horse and he;

[196-024]and wit ye well that he will take it for great unkindness, for ever one good

[196-025]knight is loath to take another at disadvantage; but because I will not

[196-026]displease you, as ye require me so will I do, and obey your commandment.

[196-027]And so Sir Tristram armed him and took his horse, and put him forth, and there

[196-028]Sir Lamorak met him mightily, and what with the might of his own spear, and of

[196-029]Sir Tristram’s spear, Sir Lamorak’s horse fell to the earth, and he

[196-030]sitting in the saddle. Then anon as lightly as he might he avoided the saddle

[196-031]and his horse, and put his shield afore him and drew his sword. And then he

[196-032]bade Sir Tristram: Alight, thou knight, an thou durst. Nay, said Sir Tristram,

[196-033]I will no more have ado with thee, for I have done to thee over much unto my

[196-034]dishonour and to thy worship. As for that, said Sir Lamorak, I can thee no

[196-035]thank; since thou hast for-jousted me on horseback I require thee and I beseech

[196-036]thee, an thou be Sir Tristram, [[battle|fight]] with me on foot. I will not so, said Sir

[196-037]Tristram; and wit ye well my name is Sir Tristram de Liones, and well I know ye

[196-038]be Sir Lamorak de Galis, and this that I have done to you was against my will,

[196-039]but I was required thereto; but to say that I will do at your request as at

[196-040]this time, I will have no more ado with you, for me shameth of that I have

[196-041]done. As for the shame, said Sir Lamorak, on thy part or on mine, bear thou it

[196-042]an thou wilt, for though a mare’s son hath failed me, now a queen’s

[196-043]son shall not fail thee; and therefore, an thou be such a knight as men call

[196-044]thee, I require thee, alight, and fight with me. Sir Lamorak, said Sir

[196-045]Tristram, I understand your heart is great, and cause why ye have, to say thee

[196-046]sooth; for it would grieve me an any knight should keep him fresh and then to

[196-047]strike down a weary knight, for that knight nor horse was never formed that

[196-048]alway might stand or endure. And therefore, said Sir Tristram, I will not have

[196-049]ado with you, for me forthinketh of that I have done. As for that, said Sir

[196-050]Lamorak, I shall quit you, an ever I see my time.
