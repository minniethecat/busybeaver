# Duke

The **duke** in [[Le Morte d'Arthur]] occupies the highest tier of the [[baron|noble]] hierarchy below the crown itself. Where a [[baron]] holds a castle or a manor, a duke holds a territory — a [[Cornwall]], a [[Tintagil]] — and commands forces that can challenge a [[kinsman|kin]]g in open [[battle|war]]. The duke is both the [[kinsman|kin]]g's greatest asset and his most d[[wrath|anger]]ous subject, for a rebellious duke can [[battle|field]] an army that rivals the royal host.

## The Duke of Cornwall and the Founding Crisis

The ent[[wrath|ire]] [[king-arthur|Arthur]]ian story is set in motion by a duke. The "mighty duke in [[cornwall|Cornwall]] that held [[battle|war]] against" (004-004) [[Uther Pendragon]] is no petty rebel — he wages sustained [[battle|war]] against the crown:

> the duke was called the Duke of Tintagil (004-005)

Uther must treat with this duke as near-equal rather than subject: "Uther sent for this duke, charging him to bring his wife" (004-006). The duke comes, but under compulsion, and his wife [[Igraine]] comes with him:

> So when the duke and his wife were come unto the... (004-008)

The Duke of [[cornwall|Cornwall]]'s [[challenge|defiance]] of Uther — his refusal to surrender his wife, his flight from court, his death in battle — is the original sin of the [[king-arthur|Arthur]]ian dynasty, the act of resistance that leads, through [[Merlin]]'s intervention, to [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s conception.

## The Duke as Territorial Power

A duke is defined by territory. The Duke of [[cornwall|Cornwall]] rules not a single castle but a whole region; when he goes to [[battle|war]], he does so from his own [[realm|land]]s, with his own [[baron|lord]]s. When "the duke had this [[battle|war]]ning, anon he went" (004-027) to fortify his castles, he acts as a [[king|sovereign]] within his own domain. The [[kinsman|kin]]g's authority over a duke is conditional — enforceable only if the [[kinsman|kin]]g can bring superior force to bear:

> When the duke her husband, and said, I suppose... (004-012)

The conversation in the ducal household reveals that the duke's wife herself weighs the political calculus: resist the [[kinsman|kin]]g, or submit? The duke's decision to resist is the decision of a man who believes his territorial power can match the crown's.

## Dukes and the Arthurian Lineage

The ducal title runs through the [[king-arthur|Arthur]]ian narrative as a marker of proximity to the throne. Duke Joshua is named among the worthies "whom the first was duke Joshua which brought the children" (002-016), anchoring the [[king-arthur|Arthur]]ian world in a longer history of [[baron|noble]] rule. A duke may be the father of a [[kinsman|kin]]g, the brother of a [[kinsman|kin]]g, or the rival of a [[kinsman|kin]]g — but he is never far from the centre of power.

## Duke and Baron: The Hierarchy of Nobility

The distinction between duke and [[baron]] is practical as well as titular. A baron gives counsel in the [[kinsman|kin]]g's hall; a duke gives it from his own. A baron's rebellion is a disturbance; a duke's rebellion is a civil [[battle|war]]. The duke's knights are "knight of the duke's" (005-017) before they are the [[kinsman|kin]]g's — a primary loyalty that the [[Round Table]]'s fellowship is designed to transcend but can never ent[[wrath|ire]]ly erase:

> Sir Jordanus, a knight of the duke's. But wait... (005-018)

## The Duke's Narrative Function

The duke in Malory is the figure who tests the limits of [[kingship]]. Can a [[kinsman|kin]]g command a duke? Under what circumstances may a duke refuse? The Duke of [[cornwall|Cornwall]]'s resistance to Uther raises the question at the heart of the [[king-arthur|Arthur]]ian political order: does the crown bind by right, or only by force? [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s answer — that right and force must be one — is the founding principle of his reign, and the rebellion of later dukes and [[kinsman|kin]]gs against him is the measure of how far that principle can stretch before it breaks.
