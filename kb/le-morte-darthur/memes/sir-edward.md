# Sir Edward of the Red Castle

Sir Edward of the Red Castle was a knight who appears in the early tournament passages of the Arthurian chronicle, remembered for a single but vivid encounter in which he and his companion Sir Hue tested their spears against [[Sir Uwaine]], one of the knights of the [[Round Table]].

## The Knight of the Red Castle

Sir Edward's identity was bound to his lordship. The chronicle names him as the "knight hight Sir Edward of the Red Castle" (092-010), and his companion as "Sir Hue of the Red Castle" (092-010). The shared holding of the Red Castle marked them as brothers-in-arms, perhaps kin, certainly comrades whose fates ran together in the tournament field.

The Red Castle itself is not elaborated in the chronicle — it is a heraldic signpost rather than a developed location — but the title sufficed to establish Sir Edward's standing: he was a knight of place and property, not a landless adventurer.

## The Encounter with Sir Uwaine

Sir Edward's moment on the tournament stage came when he and Sir Hue faced [[Sir Uwaine]] in a passage of arms. The chronicle records the sequence with precision: "Sir Edward and Sir Hue brake their spears upon Sir Uwaine" (093-007), suggesting a coordinated assault — two knights charging together against a single champion of the Round Table.

The initial impact was absorbed, but [[Sir Uwaine]]'s counter-stroke was decisive: "Sir Uwaine smote Sir Edward that he fell over his horse" (093-008). The chronicle emphasises the force of the blow: "at the last Sir Uwaine smote Sir Edward upon the helm such a stroke" (093-016) that the Red Castle knight could not recover.

## The Pattern of the Tournament Knight

Sir Edward's brief appearance follows a pattern common in Malory's tournament chronicles. Knights of regional standing — lords of castles, commanders of local retinues — appear in the lists to test themselves against the champions of the [[Round Table]]. Most are unhorsed. Their defeat is not humiliation but measurement: the chronicle records their names so that the reader knows they were there, that they dared, that the Round Table's pre-eminence was earned against real opposition.

In this, Sir Edward of the Red Castle serves the same narrative function as dozens of his peers. His presence makes the victory of [[Sir Uwaine]] meaningful; his name, preserved across the centuries, is the chronicler's acknowledgement that even the defeated knight belonged to the great story.

## The Named Defeated

Sir Edward of the Red Castle belongs to that great company of knights whose primary contribution to the Arthurian chronicle is to have ridden, to have fought, and to have fallen. He was not a villain to be condemned nor a champion to be celebrated, but a knight of substance whose defeat at the hands of a Round Table champion contributed one more thread to the tapestry of chivalric record. The chronicler named him, and in naming him, granted him the only immortality a knight of the Red Castle could hope for: to be remembered as one who dared the field against [[Sir Uwaine]].
