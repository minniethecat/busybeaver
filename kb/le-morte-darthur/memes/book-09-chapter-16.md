[219-001]Isoud, and how Kehydius began to love Beale Isoud, and of a letter that

[219-002][[sir-tristram|Tristram]] found.

[219-003]And then at a day set Sir Tristram and Sir Lamorak met at the well; and then

[219-004]they took Kehydius at the forester’s house, and so they rode with him to

[219-005]the ship where they left Dame Bragwaine and Gouvernail, and so they sailed into

[219-006]Cornwall all wholly together. And by assent and information of Dame Bragwaine

[219-007]when they were landed they rode unto Sir Dinas, the Seneschal, a trusty friend

[219-008]of Sir Tristram’s. And so Dame Bragwaine and Sir Dinas rode to the court

[219-009]of [[king-mark|King Mark]], and told the queen, La Beale Isoud, that Sir Tristram was nigh

[219-010]her in that country. Then for very pure joy La Beale Isoud swooned; and when

[219-011]she might speak she said: Gentle knight Seneschal, help that I might speak with

[219-012]him, outher my heart will brast. Then Sir Dinas and Dame Bragwaine brought Sir

[219-013]Tristram and Kehydius privily unto the court, unto a chamber whereas La Beale

[219-014]Isoud had assigned it; and to tell the joys that were betwixt La Beale Isoud

[219-015]and Sir Tristram, there is no tongue can tell it, nor heart think it, nor pen

[219-016]write it. And as the French book maketh mention, at the first time that ever

[219-017]Sir Kehydius saw La Beale Isoud he was so enamoured upon her that for very pure

[219-018]love he might never withdraw it. And at the last, as ye shall hear or the book

[219-019]be ended, Sir Kehydius died for the love of La Beale Isoud. And then privily he

[219-020]wrote unto her letters and ballads of the most goodliest that were used in

[219-021]those days. And when La Beale Isoud understood his letters she had [[mercy|pity]] of his

[219-022]complaint, and unavised she wrote another letter to comfort him withal.

[219-023]And Sir Tristram was all this while in a turret at the commandment of La Beale

[219-024]Isoud, and when she might she came unto Sir Tristram. So on a day King Mark

[219-025]played at the chess under a chamber window; and at that time Sir Tristram and

[219-026]Sir Kehydius were within the chamber over King Mark, and as it mishapped Sir

[219-027]Tristram found the letter that Kehydius sent unto La Beale Isoud, also he had

[219-028]found the letter that she wrote unto Kehydius, and at that same time La Beale

[219-029]Isoud was in the same chamber. Then Sir Tristram came unto La Beale Isoud and

[219-030]said: Madam, here is a letter that was sent unto you, and here is the letter

[219-031]that ye sent unto him that sent you that letter. Alas, Madam, the good love

[219-032]that I have loved you; and many lands and riches have I forsaken for your love,

[219-033]and now ye are a traitress to me, the which doth me great pain. But as for

[219-034]thee, Sir Kehydius, I brought thee out of Brittany into this country, and thy

[219-035]father, King Howel, I won his lands, howbeit I wedded thy sister Isoud la

[219-036]Blanche Mains for the goodness she did unto me. And yet, as I am true knight,

[219-037]she is a clean maiden for me; but wit thou well, Sir Kehydius, for this

[219-038]falsehood and treason thou hast done me, I will revenge it upon thee. And

[219-039]therewithal Sir Tristram drew out his sword and said: Sir Kehydius, keep thee,

[219-040]and then La Beale Isoud swooned to the earth. And when Sir Kehydius saw Sir

[219-041]Tristram come upon him he saw none other boot, but leapt out at a bay-window

[219-042]even over the head where sat King Mark playing at the chess. And when the king

[219-043]saw one come hurling over his head he said: Fellow, what art thou, and what is

[219-044]the cause thou leapest out at that window? My lord the king, said Kehydius, it

[219-045]fortuned me that I was asleep in the window above your head, and as I slept I

[219-046]slumbered, and so I fell down. And thus Sir Kehydius excused him.
