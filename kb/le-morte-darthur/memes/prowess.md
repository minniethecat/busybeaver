# Prowess

**Prowess**  is the knightly ideal of martial excellence — the combination of physical strength, trained skill in arms, and demonstrated capacity to prevail in [[battle|combat]] that defines a knight's worth in Malory's "Le Morte d'[[king-arthur|Arthur]]." In William Caxton's preface, [[courage|prowess]] stands first among the knightly [[virtue|virtues]], described as the practical complement to [[Chivalry]]'s moral code. Throughout the work, a knight without [[courage|prowess]] cannot be "[[honour|worship]]ful" — [[courage|prowess]] is the material foundation upon which all other knightly [[virtue|virtues]] rest.

## Prowess as Physical and Martial Excellence

At its most literal, [[courage|prowess]] means the ability to [[battle|fight]] and win. Malory's knights are measured first by their performance in the lists and on the battle[[battle|field]]: how many opponents they unhorse, how long they endure, and whether they emerge victorious. The narrative repeatedly evaluates knights by this [[banner|standard]]:

> "through the noble prowess of himself and his knights of the Round Table" (010-026)

Prowess is tested in three primary arenas: the tournament (formal, rule-bound [[battle|combat]]), the battle[[battle|field]] (massed [[battle|war]]fare between armies), and the adventure (individual encounters with unknown knights or [[marvel|supernatural]] foes). Each tests a different dimension: tournaments test technical skill, battle tests [[hardiness|endurance]] and leadership, and adventures test cou[[wrath|rage]] under unpredictable conditions.

## Prowess as Narrative Identifier

A knight's [[courage|prowess]] is often the first and most important thing the text tells us about him. When a new character is introduced, his [[courage|prowess]] is stated before his [[blood|lineage]], his [[fealty|allegiance]], or his moral character. This prioritization reflects the text's value system:

> "a passing good knight of prowess and a full true man" (035-015)

The phrase "knight of [[courage|prowess]]" functions as the strongest positive epithet Malory can bestow. It signals that this knight is not merely competent but exceptional — worthy of the reader's attention, likely to play a significant role in the narrative, and d[[wrath|anger]]ous to his opponents.

## The Measure of Prowess: Comparison and Rank

Prowess in Malory is inherently comparative. Knights are not simply "of [[courage|prowess]]" in isolation; they are "of more [[courage|prowess]]" or "of such [[courage|prowess]]" relative to others. The text constantly ranks knights against one another, creating a hierarchy of martial worth that mirrors and sometimes conflicts with the hierarchy of social rank:

> "a man of more worship than thou wotest of" (027-004)
> "they shall have no fellow of prowess and of good" (027-016)

This comparative logic drives much of the narrative tension: knights challenge str[[wrath|anger]]s to test whether their [[courage|prowess]] exceeds their own, and defeats are significant precisely because they reorder the invisible ran[[kinsman|kin]]g that governs knightly [[honour|reputation]].

## Prowess Beyond Physical Strength

While [[courage|prowess]] is grounded in physical capacity, Malory also recognizes a deeper, almost mystical dimension. The greatest knights possess a [[courage|prowess]] that seems to transcend mere training — it is described as a quality innate to their being, recognized even before it is demonstrated:

> "he should be hard and full of prowess, and withal he should be the best knight of the world" (036-004)

This prophetic dimension of [[courage|prowess]] links it to the work's [[marvel|supernatural]] elements: [[Merlin]]'s prophecies, the [[Sangreal]] quest's selection of the worthy, and the destined superiority of knights like [[Sir Launcelot]] and [[Sir Galahad]].

## Prowess's Narrative Function

Prowess is the narrative's primary engine of action. Knights ride out to prove their [[courage|prowess]]; they encounter other knights to test it; they return to court to have it acknowledged. The pursuit of [[courage|prowess]] generates the episodic structure of the romance — each adventure is an opportunity to demonstrate, increase, or lose [[courage|prowess]]. This relentless focus on martial excellence also contains the seeds of the work's t[[wrath|rage]]dy: the obsession with proving [[courage|prowess]] leads to fatal duels between friends, the destruction of [[kinsman|kin]]ship bonds, and ultimately the impossibility of sustaining the [[Round Table]] as a fellowship of competitive equals.
