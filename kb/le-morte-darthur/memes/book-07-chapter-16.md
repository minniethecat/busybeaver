[143-001]and of their talking, and how they began their battle.

[143-002]Sir, said the damosel Linet unto Sir [[sir-gareth|Beaumains]], look ye be glad and light, for

[143-003]yonder is your deadly enemy, and at yonder window is my lady, my sister, Dame

[143-004]Lionesse. Where? said Beaumains. Yonder, said the damosel, and pointed with her

[143-005]finger. That is truth, said Beaumains. She beseemeth afar the fairest lady that

[143-006]ever I looked upon; and truly, he said, I ask no better quarrel than now for to

[143-007]do battle, for truly she shall be my lady, and for her I will [[battle|fight]]. And ever

[143-008]he looked up to the window with glad countenance, and the Lady Lionesse made

[143-009]curtsey to him down to the earth, with holding up both their hands.

[143-010]With that the Red Knight of the Red Launds called to Sir Beaumains, Leave, sir

[143-011]knight, thy looking, and behold me, I counsel thee; for I warn thee well she is

[143-012]my lady, and for her I have done many strong battles. If thou have so done,

[143-013]said Beaumains, meseemeth it was but waste labour, for she loveth none of thy

[143-014]fellowship, and thou to love that loveth not thee is but great folly. For an I

[143-015]understood that she were not glad of my coming, I would be advised or I did

[143-016]battle for her. But I understand by the besieging of this castle she may

[143-017]forbear thy fellowship. And therefore wit thou well, thou Red Knight of the Red

[143-018]Launds, I love her, and will rescue her, or else to die. Sayst thou that? said

[143-019]the Red Knight, meseemeth thou ought of reason to be ware by yonder knights

[143-020]that thou sawest hang upon yonder trees. Fie for shame, said Beaumains, that

[143-021]ever thou shouldest say or do so evil, for in that thou shamest thyself and

[143-022]knighthood, and thou mayst be sure there will no lady love thee that knoweth

[143-023]thy wicked customs. And now thou weenest that the sight of these hanged knights

[143-024]should fear me. Nay truly, not so; that shameful sight causeth me to have

[143-025]courage and [[courage|hardiness]] against thee, more than I would have had against thee an

[143-026]thou wert a well-ruled knight. Make thee ready, said the Red Knight of the Red

[143-027]Launds, and talk no longer with me.

[143-028]Then Sir Beaumains bade the damosel go from him; and then they put their spears

[143-029]in their rests, and came together with all their might that they had both, and

[143-030]either smote other in midst of their shields that the paitrelles, surcingles,

[143-031]and cruppers brast, and fell to the earth both, and the reins of their bridles

[143-032]in their hands; and so they lay a great while sore astonied, that all that were

[143-033]in the castle and in the siege weened their necks had been broken; and then

[143-034]many a stranger and other said the strange knight was a big man, and a noble

[143-035]jouster, for or now we saw never no knight match the Red Knight of the Red

[143-036]Launds: thus they said, both within the castle and without. Then lightly they

[143-037]avoided their horses and put their shields afore them, and drew their swords

[143-038]and ran together like two fierce lions, and either gave other such buffets upon

[143-039]their helms that they reeled backward both two strides; and then they recovered

[143-040]both, and hewed great pieces off their harness and their shields that a great

[143-041]part fell into the fields.
