[132-002]damosel.

[132-003]In God’s name, said Sir Launcelot, for I promise you, by the faith of my

[132-004]body, I had as much to do as I might to save myself from you unshamed, and

[132-005]therefore have ye no doubt of none earthly knight. Hope ye so that I may any

[132-006]while stand a proved knight? said [[sir-gareth|Beaumains]]. Yea, said Launcelot, do as ye have

[132-007]done, and I shall be your warrant. Then, I pray you, said Beaumains, give me

[132-008]the order of knighthood. Then must ye tell me your name, said Launcelot, and of

[132-009]what kin ye be born. Sir, so that ye will not discover me I shall, said

[132-010]Beaumains. Nay, said Sir Launcelot, and that I promise you by the faith of my

[132-011]body, until it be openly known. Then, sir, he said, my name is Gareth, and

[132-012]brother unto [[sir-gawaine|Sir Gawaine]] of father and mother. Ah, sir, said Sir Launcelot, I

[132-013]am more gladder of you than I was; for ever me thought ye should be of great

[132-014]blood, and that ye came not to the court neither for meat nor for drink. And

[132-015]then Sir Launcelot gave him the order of knighthood, and then Sir Gareth prayed

[132-016]him for to depart and let him go.

[132-017]So Sir Launcelot departed from him and came to [[sir-kay|Sir Kay]], and made him to be

[132-018]borne home upon his shield, and so he was healed hard with the life; and all

[132-019]men scorned Sir Kay, and in especial Sir Gawaine and Sir Launcelot said it was

[132-020]not his part to rebuke no young man, for full little knew he of what birth he

[132-021]is come, and for what cause he came to this court; and so we leave Sir Kay and

[132-022]turn we unto Beaumains.

[132-023]When he had overtaken the damosel, anon she said, What dost thou here? thou

[132-024]stinkest all of the kitchen, thy clothes be bawdy of the grease and tallow that

[132-025]thou gainest in [[king-arthur|King Arthur]]’s kitchen; weenest thou, said she, that I

[132-026]allow thee, for yonder knight that thou killest. Nay truly, for thou slewest

[132-027]him unhappily and cowardly; therefore turn again, bawdy kitchen page, I know

[132-028]thee well, for Sir Kay named thee Beaumains. What art thou but a lusk and a

[132-029]turner of broaches and a ladle-washer? Damosel, said Beaumains, say to me what

[132-030]ye will, I will not go from you whatsomever ye say, for I have undertaken to

[132-031]King Arthur for to achieve your adventure, and so shall I finish it to the end,

[132-032]either I shall die therefore. Fie on thee, kitchen knave, wilt thou finish mine

[132-033]adventure? thou shalt anon be met withal, that thou wouldest not for all the

[132-034]broth that ever thou suppest once look him in the face. I shall assay, said

[132-035]Beaumains.

[132-036]So thus as they rode in the wood, there came a man flying all that ever he

[132-037]might. Whither wilt thou? said Beaumains. O lord, he said, help me, for here by

[132-038]in a slade are six thieves that have taken my lord and bound him, so I am

[132-039]afeard lest they will slay him. Bring me thither, said Beaumains. And so they

[132-040]rode together until they came thereas was the knight bounden; and then he rode

[132-041]unto them, and struck one unto the death, and then another, and at the third

[132-042]stroke he slew the third thief, and then the other three fled. And he rode

[132-043]after them, and he overtook them; and then those three thieves turned again and

[132-044]assailed Beaumains hard, but at the last he slew them, and returned and unbound

[132-045]the knight. And the knight thanked him, and prayed him to ride with him to his

[132-046]castle there a little beside, and he should worshipfully reward him for his

[132-047]good deeds. Sir, said Beaumains, I will no reward have: I was this day made

[132-048]knight of noble Sir Launcelot, and therefore I will no reward have, but God

[132-049]reward me. And also I must follow this damosel.

[132-050]And when he came nigh her she bade him ride from her, For thou smellest all of

[132-051]the kitchen: weenest thou that I have joy of thee, for all this deed that thou

[132-052]hast done is but mishapped thee: but thou shalt see a sight shall make thee

[132-053]turn again, and that lightly. Then the same knight which was rescued of the

[132-054]thieves rode after that damosel, and prayed her to lodge with him all that

[132-055]night. And because it was near night the damosel rode with him to his castle,

[132-056]and there they had great cheer, and at supper the knight sat Sir Beaumains

[132-057]afore the damosel. Fie, fie, said she, Sir knight, ye are uncourteous to set a

[132-058]kitchen page afore me; him beseemeth better to stick a swine than to sit afore

[132-059]a damosel of high parage. Then the knight was ashamed at her words, and took

[132-060]him up, and set him at a sideboard, and set himself afore him, and so all that

[132-061]night they had good cheer and merry rest.
