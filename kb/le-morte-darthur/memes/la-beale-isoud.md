# La Beale Isoud

**La Beale Isoud** is the Irish [[lady|princess]] whose [[love]] for [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]] forms one of the great [[romance|romances]] of the Arthurian world. Her name — "the Fair Isoud" — marks her as the narrative's supreme figure of beauty and desire, and the potion-bound [[love]] between her and Tristram drives events across many books of Malory's *Morte*.

## Healer and Beloved

Isoud first appears as the healer of the wounded Tristram, who comes to Ireland "for to be healed of his" (172-001) wound received in combat. She recognises his quality beneath his disguise: "La Beale Isoud came unto Sir Tramtrist" (172-020), and her care restores him to strength. When she discovers his true identity — that he is the slayer of her uncle, Sir Marhaus — the revelation tests the bond between them, and the [[love]] that grows in its aftermath defies [[fealty|loyalty]], [[kin|kinship]], and political obligation.

## The Love Potion

The tragedy of Isoud's story turns on the love potion she drinks with Tristram on the voyage from Ireland to Cornwall, where she is to wed [[king-mark|King Mark]]. The potion binds them in a [[love]] that is both irresistible and illicit — she becomes the wife of one king and the beloved of another. "Then had La Beale Isoud great suspicion unto Tramtrist" (173-007) when she learns the truth, but the bond proves stronger than [[wrath|anger]] or duty. Her story with Tristram alternates between reunion and separation, joy and [[sorrow]], as the lovers navigate the impossible triangle with King Mark.

## The Queen's Rivalry

Isoud's presence at the court of Cornwall draws the [[envy|jealousy]] of other ladies, "namely with La Beale Isoud" (174-004) herself acknowledged as the fairest. The narrative positions her as the counterpart to [[queen-guenever|Queen Guenever]] — both are great queens whose loves define their stories, and both are caught between public duty and private desire. Where Guenever's love for Launcelot destroys a [[realm|kingdom]], Isoud's love for Tristram is a more intimate [[tragedy]], the undoing of two lovers and the king who holds them both.
