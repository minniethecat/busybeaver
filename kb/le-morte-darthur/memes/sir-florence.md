# Sir Florence

Sir Florence was a [[Knight of the Round Table]] who served as a commander in [[King Arthur]]'s Roman War campaign, leading a contingent of one hundred knights against the forces of the Emperor Lucius. His command in the field and his role in the vanguard place him among the notable military captains of Arthur's host.

## Called to Command

When King Arthur prepared to cross the sea and confront the Roman Empire, he summoned Florence to a position of command. The chronicle records that "on a time, the king called Sir Florence, a knight, and said to" (104-008) him, entrusting him with the leadership of a fighting force. Florence was no mere retainer but a captain in his own right, chosen by the king to bear the weight of command in the coming campaign.

## The Vanguard at the Ford

Florence's most significant military action came at a contested river crossing. The chronicle records Arthur's order: "Sir Florence shall abide" (105-072) at a strategic position while the main host advanced. Florence was tasked with holding the ground — a command that required both personal courage and the ability to inspire his men in the face of the enemy.

When the Romans pressed forward, "Sir Florence called to him Sir Floridas" (106-002), a knight under his command, rallying his forces. The engagement was fierce and at one point a knight "came to Sir Florence and asked him why he fled" (106-009) — a question that reveals the chaos of battle and the scrutiny under which commanders operated. Florence's response was to fight on: "Sir Florence with his hundred knights" (106-009) held the field, their discipline preventing a Roman breakthrough.

## The Captain's Hundred

The chronicle emphasises that Florence led "his hundred knights" (106-009) — a substantial force in the context of Arthurian warfare. A commander of one hundred knights was no minor captain; he was entrusted with a significant portion of Arthur's host, capable of independent action on the battlefield. The number also suggests that Florence possessed both the martial reputation to attract followers and the organisational skill to command them.

After the battle, "Sir Florence and Sir Gawaine harboured" (106-035) together, the captain sharing quarters with the king's own nephew. This fellowship with [[Sir Gawaine]] — the foremost knight of the vanguard — confirms Florence's standing among the highest ranks of Arthur's war council.

## A Knight of the Field

Unlike many of his Round Table peers, Florence's story is told entirely on the battlefield. He does not appear in quest romances or tournament lists; his claim to remembrance rests on his conduct in war. In this, Florence represents the martial foundation of the Round Table — the company of knights whose primary service to the crown was rendered not in individual adventure but in the command of men and the discipline of the line.
