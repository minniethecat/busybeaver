# Sir Lucan

Sir Lucan, known as Sir Lucan the Butler, was a [[Knight of the Round Table]] and an officer of [[King Arthur]]'s royal household. His most notable appearance in the chronicle recounts his combat with [[Sir Tristram|Sir Tristram de Lyonesse]], a chance encounter that tested his valour against one of the greatest knights of the age.

## The Butler of the Round Table

Lucan held the office of butler in Arthur's court, a position of honour and trust. The chronicle introduces him with his full title: "my name is Sir Lucan, the butler, a Knight of the Round Table" (239-005), establishing him as a knight whose courtly office was matched by his membership in the Round Table fellowship. The butler oversaw the royal household's provisioning and service, a role that placed him at the centre of the court's daily life.

## The Encounter with Sir Tristram

Lucan's most memorable episode begins when "Sir Lucan the butler, that by fortune" (239-002) rode abroad and chanced upon an adventure. He met a knight in the field and "smote down" (239-010) his opponent in the first course, displaying the martial skill expected of a Round Table knight. But the victory was short-lived, for the knight he had unhorsed proved to be none other than Sir Tristram de Lyonesse.

"Sir Lucan rode after him, and asked after" (239-012) the fallen knight, not knowing his identity. Tristram, unhorsed but far from defeated, pursued him. The chronicle records that "Tristram overtook Sir Lucan, and bade him turn" (239-019), challenging him to face a remount.

## The Wound and Its Consequences

The second encounter went ill for Lucan. "Tristram hurt Sir Lucan passing sore and gave him a fall" (239-020), wounding him grievously. Yet Tristram, ever the "gentle knight," showed mercy to his fallen opponent. The chronicle records that "when he saw Sir Lucan so hurt he called Sir" (239-021) for aid, ensuring that the wounded butler received care.

The encounter left Lucan gravely injured. Other knights who "heard that Sir Tristram had hurt Sir Lucan he would have" (239-028) ridden in pursuit — "ridden after Sir Lucan for to have slain him" (239-029) — but Tristram restrained them. An "horse litter" was prepared, and they "brought Sir Lucan to the" (239-030) safety of the court, where he recovered from his wounds.

## Duty and Valour

Lucan's story illustrates the double life of the Round Table knight: by day a household officer, overseeing the buttery and the provisioning of the court; by adventure, a warrior who rode abroad to test his steel. His wounding at Tristram's hands was no shame — to fall to the greatest knight of the age, in a fair encounter, was the common fate of many worthies.
