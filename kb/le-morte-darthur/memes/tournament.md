# Tournament

**Tournaments** are a recurring institution in Thomas Malory's *Le Morte d'[[king-arthur|Arthur]]*, functioning as organized displays of martial [[courage|prowess]] where knights compete for [[honour|honor]], prizes, and [[honour|renown]]. They serve both as social gatherings that affirm chivalric values and as narrative mechanisms that introduce characters, resolve conflicts, and advance the plot.

## Form and Function

Tournaments are typically organized by [[baron|noble]] patrons. Malory records that on "New Year's Day the [[baron|barons]] let make a [[joust|jousts]] and a tournament, that all knights that would joust or tourney there might play" (008-031). These events unite [[baron|lord]]s and commons, as "all this was ordained for to keep the [[baron|lord]]s together and the commons" (008-031). The tournament is thus both a martial exercise and a political instrument.

## Prizes and Prestige

Victory in tournament carries substantial re[[battle|war]]ds. Sir Marhaus at "a great tournament that the [[lady|Lady]] de Vawse had cried" performed so nobly that he "had sometime down forty knights, and so the circlet of gold was re[[battle|war]]ded him" (091-016). At another tournament, Sir Uwaine "smote down thirty knights, therefore was given him the prize, and that was a gerfalcon, and a white [[horse|steed]] trapped with cloth of gold" (092-003). These tangible re[[battle|war]]ds reinforce the tournament's role as a proving ground for knightly worth.

## Reputation and Identity

Performance in tournaments defines a knight's public identity. [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot du Lake]] is [[honour|renown]]ed because "in all tournaments and [[joust|jousts]] and deeds of arms" (109-008) he excels above all others. The tournament circuit is where knights earn the [[honour|reputation]] that precedes them into battle and quest. Conversely, some knights avoid tournaments: "at any [[joust|jousts]] or any tournament, she would not suffer him" (094-031), showing how attendance at tournaments is a marker of participation in the chivalric community.

## Narrative Function

Beyond their social role, tournaments in Malory often serve specific narrative purposes — introducing a new champion, revealing hidden identities, or setting the stage for a larger conflict. The tournament is a controlled space of violence that mirrors and foreshadows the destructive battles that ultimately destroy [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s [[realm|kingdom]].
