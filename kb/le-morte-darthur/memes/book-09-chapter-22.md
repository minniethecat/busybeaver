[225-001][[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]] against thirty knights, and how [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]] fought with

[225-002]them.

[225-003]And at the next landing, fast by the sea, there met with Sir Tristram and with

[225-004]Sir Dinadan, Sir Ector de Maris and [[sir-bors|Sir Bors de Ganis]]; and there Sir Ector

[225-005]jousted with Sir Dinadan, and he smote him and his horse down. And then Sir

[225-006]Tristram would have jousted with Sir Bors, and Sir Bors said that he would not

[225-007]joust with no Cornish knights, for they are not called men of [[honour|worship]]; and all

[225-008]this was done upon a bridge. And with this came Sir Bleoberis and Sir Driant,

[225-009]and Sir Bleoberis proffered to joust with Sir Tristram, and there Sir Tristram

[225-010]smote down Sir Bleoberis. Then said Sir Bors de Ganis: I wist never Cornish

[225-011]knight of so great valour nor so valiant as that knight that beareth the

[225-012]trappings embroidered with crowns. And then Sir Tristram and Sir Dinadan

[225-013]departed from them into a forest, and there met them a damosel that came for

[225-014]the love of Sir Launcelot to seek after some noble knights of King

[225-015][[king-arthur|Arthur]]’s court for to rescue Sir Launcelot. And so Sir Launcelot was

[225-016]ordained, for-by the treason of Queen [[morgan-le-fay|Morgan le Fay]] to have slain Sir

[225-017]Launcelot, and for that cause she ordained thirty knights to lie in await for

[225-018]Sir Launcelot, and this damosel knew this treason. And for this cause the

[225-019]damosel came for to seek noble knights to help Sir Launcelot. For that night,

[225-020]or the day after, Sir Launcelot should come where these thirty knights were.

[225-021]And so this damosel met with Sir Bors and Sir Ector and with Sir Driant, and

[225-022]there she told them all four of the treason of Morgan le Fay; and then they

[225-023]promised her that they would be nigh where Sir Launcelot should meet with the

[225-024]thirty knights. And if so be they set upon him we will do rescues as we can.

[225-025]So the damosel departed, and by adventure the damosel met with Sir Tristram and

[225-026]with Sir Dinadan, and there the damosel told them all the treason that was

[225-027]ordained for Sir Launcelot. Fair damosel, said Sir Tristram, bring me to that

[225-028]same place where they should meet with Sir Launcelot. Then said Sir Dinadan:

[225-029]What will ye do? it is not for us to [[battle|fight]] with thirty knights, and wit you

[225-030]well I will not thereof; as to match one knight two or three is enough an they

[225-031]be men, but for to match fifteen knights that will I never undertake. Fie for

[225-032]shame, said Sir Tristram, do but your part. Nay, said Sir Dinadan, I will not

[225-033]thereof but if ye will lend me your shield, for ye bear a shield of Cornwall;

[225-034]and for the cowardice that is named to the knights of Cornwall, by your shields

[225-035]ye be ever forborne. Nay, said Sir Tristram, I will not depart from my shield

[225-036]for her sake that gave it me. But one thing, said Sir Tristram, I promise thee,

[225-037]Sir Dinadan, but if thou wilt promise me to abide with me, here I shall slay

[225-038]thee, for I desire no more of thee but answer one knight. And if thy heart will

[225-039]not serve thee, stand by and look upon me and them. Sir, said Sir Dinadan, I

[225-040]promise you to look upon and to do what I may to save myself, but I would I had

[225-041]not met with you.

[225-042]So then anon these thirty knights came fast by these four knights, and they

[225-043]were ware of them, and either of other. And so these thirty knights let them

[225-044]pass, for this cause, that they would not wrath them, if case be that they had

[225-045]ado with Sir Launcelot; and the four knights let them pass to this intent, that

[225-046]they would see and behold what they would do with Sir Launcelot. And so the

[225-047]thirty knights passed on and came by Sir Tristram and by Sir Dinadan, and then

[225-048]Sir Tristram cried on high: Lo, here is a knight against you for the love of

[225-049]Sir Launcelot. And there he slew two with one spear and ten with his sword. And

[225-050]then came in Sir Dinadan and he did passing well, and so of the thirty knights

[225-051]there went but ten away, and they fled. All this battle saw Sir Bors de Ganis

[225-052]and his three fellows, and then they saw well it was the same knight that

[225-053]jousted with them at the bridge; then they took their horses and rode unto Sir

[225-054]Tristram, and praised him and thanked him of his good deeds, and they all

[225-055]desired Sir Tristram to go with them to their lodging; and he said: Nay, he

[225-056]would not go to no lodging. Then they all four knights prayed him to tell them

[225-057]his name. Fair lords, said Sir Tristram, as at this time I will not tell you my

[225-058]name.
