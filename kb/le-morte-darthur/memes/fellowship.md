# Fellowship

**Fellowship** in Thomas Malory's *Le Morte d'[[king-arthur|Arthur]]* denotes the bond of mutual loyalty, shared purpose, and collective identity that binds knights together in common cause. It is the social glue of the chivalric world, operating at scales from a handful of companions to the ent[[wrath|ire]] [[round-table|Round Table]].

## Fellowship in Battle

Fellowship is most vividly realized in [[battle|combat]]. Malory describes how knights "found there a passing fair fellowship, and well beseen" (015-003), evo[[kinsman|kin]]g the visual splendor of a company of armed knights. In battle, "their fellowship of ten thousand men" (017-009) acts as a collective force. When "there was so few a fellowship did such deeds of arms" (017-022), the small number magnifies the glory — a few knights bound in fellowship can overcome superior numbers.

## The Fragility of Fellowship

Fellowship in Malory is perpetually under threat. [[battle|Battle]]s fragment companies: knights are separated, captured, or killed. More insidiously, fellowship is corroded from within by [[envy|jealousy]], ambition, and conflicting loyalties. The [[round-table|Round Table]] fellowship — the greatest ever assembled — is ultimately destroyed not by external enemies but by internal [[treason|betrayal]]s. The very bonds that make fellowship powerful also make it vulnerable.

## Fellowship as Ideal

Despite its fragility, fellowship remains the highest social ideal in Malory's world. Knights "rescued themselves and their fellowship [[marvel|marvellous]]ly" (018-040), and the phrase "his fellowship" recurs as a marker of identity and belonging. To be without fellowship is to be alone in a d[[wrath|anger]]ous world — the condition Malory himself may have known as a [[prison|prisoner]] composing his great work.
