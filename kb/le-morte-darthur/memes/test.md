# Test

**Tests** in Malory's "Le Morte d'[[king-arthur|Arthur]]" are narrative mechanisms by which knightly identity and virtue are publicly proven. Unlike modern conceptions of testing as assessment, Malorian tests are existential trials — a knight who fails a test loses not merely standing but identity itself. Tests appear in multiple forms: the drawn sword that proves [[king|kingship]], the disguised knight whose identity must be revealed through [[battle|combat]], the perilous adventure that distinguishes the worthy from the unworthy, and the moral test of mercy or loyalty. Across these forms, the test serves a single function: to make visible what is otherwise invisible about a knight's inner worth.

## The Sword in the Stone as Public Test

The archetypal test in the [[king-arthur|Arthur]]ian narrative is the sword in the stone, which publicly proves [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s right to [[king|kingship]]. The test is democratic in form — all may assay it — but selective in result: only the true [[kinsman|kin]]g can succeed:

> "every man should assay that would, for to win the sword" (008-030)

The repeated public assays at [[feast-of-pentecost|Pentecost]] create a narrative of collective witness: [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s [[king|kingship]] is not merely claimed but publicly proven before the assembled [[baron|lord]]s. When [[king-arthur|Arthur]] himself draws the sword, the act is described as proof ("I prove him he is no son of my [[baron|lord]] the duke" — 011-031), lin[[kinsman|kin]]g the test to the revelation of true [[blood|lineage]] and identity.

## Combat as Test of Worth

The most frequent form of test in Malory is armed [[battle|combat]], where a knight's [[courage|prowess]] proves his inner quality. Knights constantly "assay" one another through [[joust|jousts]] and single [[battle|combat]], and the outcome is read as a verdict on character:

> "who that proved him the best knight" (086-052)

The test-by-[[battle|combat]] logic extends to judicial procedure: when a lady's virtue is questioned, a knight may "prove it on his body" (024-015) through trial by [[battle|combat]], ma[[kinsman|kin]]g the test simultaneously martial, moral, and legal. A knight who "would have proved a good man" (065-015) is one whose potential was demonstrated through martial action.

## Tests of Identity and Disguise

Many of Malory's most significant tests involve concealed or mistaken identity. Knights [[battle|fight]] in borrowed [[arms|armour]], under assumed names, or in disguise, and the test reveals who they truly are — not merely their name but their quality:

> "because of his armour and shield I am sure I shall know him" (119-085)

The disguised knight must be tested in [[battle|combat]] before his identity can be known; identity is not self-declared but publicly proven through deeds. [[Sir Launcelot]] [[battle|fight]]ing in [[Sir Kay]]'s [[arms|armour]], [[Sir Gareth]] serving anonymously in the kitchen, and [[Sir Tristram]] appearing incognito at tournaments all embody this pattern: the test strips away [[honour|reputation]] and rank, leaving only the naked proof of action.

## The Perilous Adventure as Spiritual Test

Beyond martial tests, Malory presents adventures that function as spiritual or moral trials. The [[siege|Siege]] Perilous at the [[Round Table]] is a test that kills the unworthy who assay it, reserving its seat for the chosen knight. The quest of the [[Sangreal]] is itself a prolonged test of spiritual purity:

> "all his knights have assayed it and none can speed" (032-027)

These spiritual tests discriminate not by strength but by virtue: the strongest knight may fail where the purest succeeds. [[Sir Galahad]]'s success where [[Sir Launcelot]] fails marks the transition from a chivalry of [[courage|prowess]] to a chivalry of grace.

## Mercy as Moral Test

A recurring moral test in Malory involves the choice between mercy and [[revenge|vengeance]]. When a defeated opponent cries for mercy, the victorious knight faces a test of character that reveals his inner disposition:

> "a knight without mercy is dishonoured" (058-008)

[[Sir Gawaine]]'s early failure to grant mercy and his subsequent oath never to refuse it (058-043) turns the act of mercy into a permanent test of knighthood. A knight who fails the mercy test — who slays a yielding opponent — forfeits not [[honour|worship]] but [[worship|honour]] itself.

## Test's Narrative Function

Tests structure the episodic rhythm of "Le Morte d'[[king-arthur|Arthur]]" by providing continuous occasions for the public demonstration of knightly quality. Each test — whether a joust, a perilous adventure, or a moral choice — functions as a miniature drama of proving. The cumulative effect is a world in which identity is never stable but must be constantly re-proven through action. This restlessness drives the narrative for[[battle|war]]d and creates the tragic arc of the whole: the [[Round Table]]'s dissolution occurs when the tests of loyalty, truth, and virtue can no longer be passed.
