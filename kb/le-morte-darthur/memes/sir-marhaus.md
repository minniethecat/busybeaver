# Sir Marhaus

Sir Marhaus was a knight of the [[Round Table]] and the son of the King of [[Ireland]], known throughout [[Le Morte d'Arthur]] for his formidable size, his chivalrous courtesy, and his prowess in single combat. He ranked among the notable knights of [[King Arthur]]'s court, counted alongside [[Sir Percivale]] and [[Sir Pelleas]] in the fellowship of worthies.

## The Champion from Ireland

Marhaus was revealed to be of royal lineage: "his name is Marhaus, the king's son of Ireland" (083-011). His heritage placed him among the highest-born knights of the Round Table, and his reputation preceded him wherever he rode. When twelve damsels complained of a wrong, it was Sir Marhaus who answered their call for justice, coming to meet them with the bearing of a champion (083-001). The sight of him riding on a great horse caused the twelve damsels to flee in fear, mistaking him for the very knight who had wronged them (083-019).

## Combat with Sir Uwaine

One of Sir Marhaus's most fully recorded encounters was his combat with [[Sir Uwaine]]. The two knights met in fierce single combat, each proving the other's worth. Sir Uwaine smote Marhaus with such force that his spear broke to pieces, but Sir Marhaus returned the blow with even greater power, smiting Uwaine so sore that he was unhorsed (084-016). Yet when Uwaine continued on foot, Marhaus showed his chivalry: seeing his opponent dismounted, he himself alighted from his horse to meet him on equal terms, saying, "I have well felt that ye are a passing good knight" (084-039).

This act of courtesy—refusing to fight a dismounted opponent from horseback—defines Marhaus's character as a knight who valued fair combat above easy victory. He was "bigger and bigger" in stature than most knights (084-038), yet he used his physical advantage with restraint and honour.

## Among the Fellowship

Marhaus was named among the Round Table's worthiest, listed with [[Sir Percivale]], [[Sir Pelleas]], and other knights of renown (084-058). His presence in these lists attests to his standing in the court, and his reputation as both a fierce warrior and a chivalrous opponent made him respected among friend and foe alike.

## The Irish Knight

As the son of the King of Ireland, Sir Marhaus represents the wider reach of Arthur's fellowship beyond the shores of [[Logres]] and [[Cornwall]]. The presence of an Irish prince among the Round Table's worthies demonstrates that the chivalric code and the ideals of [[knighthood]] transcended national boundaries in Malory's world. Marhaus embodied the union of royal blood, martial prowess, and chivalrous courtesy that defined the Round Table at its finest.
