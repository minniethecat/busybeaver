[169-001]Island for to furnish the battle with Sir Marhaus.

[169-002]So to shorten this tale, when [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]] was arrived within the island he

[169-003]looked to the farther side, and there he saw at an anchor six ships nigh to the

[169-004]land; and under the shadow of the ships upon the land, there hoved the noble

[169-005]knight, Sir Marhaus of Ireland. Then Sir Tristram commanded his servant

[169-006]Gouvernail to bring his horse to the land, and dress his harness at all manner

[169-007]of rights. And then when he had so done he mounted upon his horse; and when he

[169-008]was in his saddle well apparelled, and his shield dressed upon his shoulder,

[169-009]Tristram asked Gouvernail, Where is this knight that I shall have ado withal?

[169-010]Sir, said Gouvernail, see ye him not? I weened ye had seen him; yonder he

[169-011]hoveth under the umbre of his ships on horseback, with his spear in his hand

[169-012]and his shield upon his shoulder. That is truth, said the noble knight, Sir

[169-013]Tristram, now I see him well enough.

[169-014]Then he commanded his servant Gouvernail to go to his vessel again: And commend

[169-015]me unto mine eme [[king-mark|King Mark]], and pray him, if that I be slain in this battle,

[169-016]for to inter my body as him seemed best; and as for me, let him wit that I will

[169-017]never yield me for cowardice; and if I be slain and flee not, then they have

[169-018]lost no truage for me; and if so be that I flee or yield me as recreant, bid

[169-019]mine eme never bury me in Christian burials. And upon thy life, said Sir

[169-020]Tristram to Gouvernail, come thou not nigh this island till that thou see me

[169-021]overcome or slain, or else that I win yonder knight. So either departed from

[169-022]other sore weeping.
