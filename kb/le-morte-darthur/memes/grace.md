# Grace

**Grace** in Malory's "Le Morte d'[[king-arthur|Arthur]]" is the unearned divine favour that underpins the ent[[wrath|ire]] chivalric order. [[king|King]]ship, victory in [[battle|combat]], the achievement of adventures, and ultimately salvation are all attributed to grace rather than to human merit alone. While [[Worship]] is earned through deeds and [[Prowess]] is demonstrated in [[battle|combat]], grace comes from God and cannot be compelled — it marks the limit of human agency in a world otherwise governed by martial self-assertion.

## Grace and Kingship

[[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s [[king|kingship]] is repeatedly attributed to grace rather than to conquest or [[blood|lineage]]. He is "chosen [[kinsman|kin]]g by adventure and by grace" (052-003), ma[[kinsman|kin]]g grace the ultimate source of political legitimacy. This divine sanction distinguishes [[king-arthur|Arthur]] from the rebel [[kinsman|kin]]gs who oppose him: their claims are based on blood and force; his is based on grace. The sword in the stone, the counsel of [[merlin|Merlin]], and the establishment of the [[Round Table]] all flow from this initial grant of divine favour.

## Grace in Combat and Adventure

Knights repeatedly invoke grace as the source of their martial success. Before [[battle|combat]], they express hope that "by the grace of God" they shall prevail:

> "by the grace of God, that ye shall in short time be whole and sound" (078-022)

The formula "by the grace of God" appears throughout the text as a ritual acknowledgement that victory comes not from skill alone but from divine assistance. When a knight achieves a seemingly impossible feat, the narrator attributes it to grace rather than to the knight's own power. This theological framing places the ent[[wrath|ire]] chivalric enterprise under divine super[[dream|vision]].

## Grace and the Sangreal

The quest of the [[Sangreal]] intensifies the role of grace to its maximum. The Grail appears not to the strongest knight but to the one who receives God's grace:

> "by the grace of God, I shall deliver you clearly" (112-015)

[[Sir Galahad]]'s achievement of the Grail is framed ent[[wrath|ire]]ly in terms of grace: he succeeds not because he is the best [[battle|fight]]er but because he is the recipient of divine election. [[Sir Launcelot]]'s failure, conversely, marks the limit of what [[courage|prowess]] can achieve without grace:

> "Thou gettest none other grace" (124-030)

This moment — where [[sir-launcelot|Launcelot]] is told he will receive no grace — crystallizes the work's central theological tension between human effort and divine favour.

## Human Mediation of Grace

Grace in Malory flows not only d[[wrath|ire]]ctly from God but through human intermediaries. Knights "beseech" one another for grace, [[kinsman|kin]]gs grant grace to supplicants, and [[lady|ladies]] receive knights into their grace:

> "and put him in her grace. And so she received him upon such covenant" (145-028)

This human-to-human grace mirrors the divine pattern: it is unearned, freely given or withheld, and creates bonds of obligation between giver and receiver. The feudal language of "putting oneself in another's grace" reflects a social order built on the same asymmetrical generosity that characterizes divine-human relations.

## Grace against the Tragic Arc

The frequency of grace-language in "Le Morte d'[[king-arthur|Arthur]]" creates a theological counter-current to the work's tragic trajectory. Even as the [[Round Table]] disintegrates through sin, [[treason|betrayal]], and civil [[battle|war]], characters continue to invoke grace. The persistent hope that "by the grace of God" things will be restored makes the final catastrophe more devastating: grace is available but not seized. The t[[wrath|rage]]dy of the work is not the absence of grace but the human failure to align with it.
