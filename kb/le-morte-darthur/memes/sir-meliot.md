# Sir Meliot

Sir Meliot de Logres was a [[Knight of the Round Table]] whose life was saved through the intervention of [[Sir Launcelot]]. His story — of a knight brought to the brink of death by a mysterious wound, and healed through an act of chivalric courage — stands as one of the most vivid miracles in [[Le Morte d'Arthur]].

## The Knight of Logres

Meliot's identity was bound to his kingdom. "Sir, my name is Sir Meliot of Logurs" (063-020), he declared when first encountered, and the chronicle afterwards refers to him consistently as "Sir Meliot de Logres" (122-034). His sister, a damosel who sought Sir Launcelot's aid, identified him with the same territorial designation (123-045), linking Meliot to the Arthurian geography of Logres — a name that itself came to stand for the realm of Britain.

## The Mortal Wound

Meliot had suffered a grievous wound that no physician could heal. When Sir Launcelot came to the castle where Meliot lay, he found the knight "upon the earth, for bleeding" (123-048). Meliot's wound was no ordinary injury — it resisted all conventional treatment, and his sister had been told that only a sword and a cloth from the [[Chapel Perilous]] could restore him.

The damosel, "Sir Meliot's sister" (123-045), had travelled far to find a knight brave enough to undertake the perilous quest. When she met Sir Launcelot, she was "passing glad" (123-045), recognising in him the champion who could save her brother.

## The Healing by Sir Launcelot

The cure required Sir Launcelot to enter the Chapel Perilous, a place of sorcery and terror from which few knights returned. Launcelot succeeded where others would have failed, obtaining the sword and the bloody cloth that held the power to close Meliot's wound. "When Sir Meliot saw Sir Launcelot" (123-048) returning with the instruments of his healing, he understood that his deliverance was at hand.

After Launcelot touched the wound with the sacred cloth, Meliot was restored. Launcelot "bade Sir Meliot hie him to the court of my lord King Arthur" (123-055), sending the healed knight to bear witness to the miracle at the Round Table. The chronicle later confirms that "Sir Meliot de Logres came home, and told the king and the queen" (126-023) of the wonders Launcelot had performed.

## The Knight of the Round Table

Once restored, Sir Meliot took his place among the fellowship of the Round Table. He appears in the roll of knights alongside "Sir Kay, Sir Bedivere, Sir Meliot de Logres, Sir Petipase of Winchelsea" (154-015), listed among the named companions who attended the great feasts and councils of the court. His presence in this company confirmed that his healing had been complete, and that he had earned his place among the proven champions of [[King Arthur]]'s realm.

## The Miracle of Chivalric Medicine

Sir Meliot's story belongs to a distinctive pattern in Arthurian narrative: the knight whose physical healing requires not merely a physician's skill but a champion's courage. His wound, the perilous quest for its cure, and his restoration through Launcelot's valour together form a parable of the Round Table's highest ideal — that true knighthood serves not only to destroy but to heal.
