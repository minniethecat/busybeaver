# Sir La Cote Male Taile

Sir La Cote Male Taile — the Knight of the Ill-Fitting Coat — was a young knight who came to [[King Arthur]]'s court in his father's hacked and bloody coat, endured [[Sir Kay]]'s mockery, and proved his worth through a determined quest. His story is one of the most complete chivalric coming-of-age narratives in [[Le Morte d'Arthur]]: a tale of humiliation transformed into honour through perseverance in arms.

## The Arrival at Court

The young knight arrived at King Arthur's court wearing his dead father's coat of arms — a garment still bearing the rents and bloodstains of the combat that had killed its original wearer. [[Sir Kay]] the Seneschal, whose sharp tongue spared no stranger, seized upon the coat's condition: "in mockage ye shall be called La Cote Male Taile, that is as much to say as the evil-shapen coat" (204-007). The name stuck; the court knew him thereafter by Kay's cruel invention.

The coat was not merely ill-fitting but sacred. The young knight wore it as a memorial, vowing not to replace it until he had avenged his father's death. What Kay saw as comic poverty was, in truth, a badge of filial devotion.

## The Quest Begins

A damosel arrived at court bearing a black shield and seeking a knight to undertake a dangerous adventure. When no established knight stepped forward, La Cote Male Taile offered himself. "Fair damosel, I will take the adventure" (205-026), he declared, and despite the damosel's open contempt for his ragged appearance, he set forth.

The damosel mocked him at every turn. When he was unhorsed by [[Sir Bleoberis]], she cried "Fie on thee, thou coward" (206-008). When he struggled, she reminded him that his very name was an insult. Yet La Cote Male Taile bore her abuse with a patience that revealed the quality beneath the ill-fitting coat.

## The Castle Orgulous

The young knight's defining trial came at the Castle Orgulous, where he faced a hundred knights in succession. "La Cote Male Taile rode after him into the Castle Orgulous, and there La Cote Male Taile slew him" (206-044), the chronicle records of his victory over the castle's lord. Against overwhelming odds, the mocked knight proved that his courage far exceeded his appearance.

Even in victory, he remained humble. "When I am so hewn I will ask you for no succour" (205-033), he had told the scornful damosel — and hewn he was, but he asked no succour. His perseverance transformed his narrative from one of comic humiliation to one of genuine chivalric achievement.

## Recognition and Knighthood

The news of La Cote Male Taile's deeds spread through the Arthurian world. "The king heard of La Cote Male Taile, and how he followed after his quest" (208-001), the chronicle records. [[Sir Launcelot]] himself took notice, and when the damosel continued her mockery, Launcelot rebuked her: the young knight had earned his place among worthy men of arms.

The narrative of La Cote Male Taile culminates in the transformation of mockery into honour. The ill-fitting coat, once a symbol of humiliation, became the badge of a proven knight of the [[Round Table]] — a testament to the truth that in Arthur's court, worth was won by deeds, not bestowed by birth or appearance.

## The Coat's Meaning

Sir La Cote Male Taile's story is a meditation on the relationship between appearance and worth. In a chivalric world obsessed with heraldry, lineage, and the outward signs of honour, the young knight in his father's hacked coat was a living paradox: the worst-dressed knight in Arthur's court, and one of the most determined. His narrative affirms what the [[Round Table]] at its best always stood for — that knighthood was proven in the field, not in the wardrobe.
