# Sin

**Sin** in Malory's "Le Morte d'[[king-arthur|Arthur]]" is the violation of both divine law and the chivalric code, operating as the dark engine of narrative consequence. Unlike abstract theological sin, Malorian sin is enacted through specific deeds — adultery, murder, oath-brea[[kinsman|kin]]g, [[kinsman|kin]]strife — whose effects ripple out[[battle|war]]d through the social order.

The Caxton preface establishes the moral frame explicitly: the work exists so readers may "do after the good and leave the evil" (002-085), ma[[kinsman|kin]]g sin and virtue the work's fun[[lady|dame]]ntal thematic opposition.

## Sin and Virtue in the Moral Frame

Caxton's preface establishes the didactic purpose of the work through the sin-virtue binary:

> "cowardice, murder, hate, virtue, and sin. Do after the good and leave the evil" (002-085)

The preface urges readers to "exercise and follow virtue" (002-090) rather than sin, positioning the ent[[wrath|ire]] [[king-arthur|Arthur]]iad as a moral exemplar. This framing means that every sinful act in the narrative carries implicit moral weight — the text does not merely recount sin but judges it.

## The Sin of Adultery

The most consequential sin in "Le Morte d'[[king-arthur|Arthur]]" is the adultery between [[Sir Launcelot]] and Queen [[Guenever]]. Though the narrator often treats the affair with discretion, its effects are catastrophic: it destroys the fellowship of the [[Round Table]], drives the civil [[battle|war]] that ends [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s reign, and prevents [[sir-launcelot|Launcelot]] from achieving the [[Sangreal]].

The sin is both private (a violation of loyalty to [[King Arthur]]) and public (a fissure in the political order):

> "God hath ordained you for that penance" (065-021)

The theme of penance for sin runs throughout, particularly in the [[sangreal|Sangreal]] quest where sinful knights fail where pure knights succeed.

## Repentance and Its Failures

Malory's characters frequently express [[penance|repentance]] ("me [[repentance|repent]]eth") for their actions, but the [[penance|repentance]] is often shallow or transient. [[Sir Balin]]'s repeated exclamations of [[penance|repentance]] ("me [[repentance|repent]]eth" — 037-013, 038-010) do not alter his course of destruction. [[Sir Gawaine]] [[repentance|repent]]s his refusal of mercy only when confronted with its consequences:

> "ye ought sore to repent it, for that lady was your own mother" (065-012)

The narrative pattern is consistent: [[penance|repentance]] is expressed but rarely transformative. True penance — lasting change — is the exception rather than the rule, contributing to the work's tragic trajectory.

## Sin and the Sangreal Quest

The quest of the [[Sangreal]] makes sin the explicit criterion of success or failure. Knights who are "clean of sin" may achieve the Grail; those stained by sin cannot. [[Sir Galahad]] succeeds because of his purity; [[Sir Launcelot]] fails despite his [[courage|prowess]] because of his adultery. The quest transforms sin from a background moral condition to the active determinant of narrative outcome:

> "this shall I give you in penance, make ye as good shift as ye may" (125-043)

The [[sangreal|Sangreal]] episodes thus function as a divine judgment on the chivalric world, exposing the sin that underlies its surface of [[honour|worship]] and [[courage|prowess]].

## Sin's Narrative Function

Sin serves as the structural counterpoint to [[Chivalry]] in Malory's work. Where chivalry seeks to order human conduct through codes of [[worship|honour]] and [[honour|worship]], sin represents the irrepressible human impulses — des[[wrath|ire]], pride, [[revenge|vengeance]] — that undo that order.

The t[[wrath|rage]]dy of "Le Morte d'[[king-arthur|Arthur]]" is not that sin exists but that it is inseparable from the [[virtue|virtues]] that make the chivalric world glorious: the same passion that makes [[sir-launcelot|Launcelot]] the greatest knight also makes him an adulterer, and the same loyalty that binds the [[round-table|Round Table]] together also fuels the blood feuds that destroy it.
