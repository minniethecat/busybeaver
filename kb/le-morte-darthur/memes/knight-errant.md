# Knight-Errant

The **knight-errant** is the engine of the [[king-arthur|Arthur]]ian world. Without him, there is no [[adventure]], no [[quest]], no chance encounter at the [[forest]]'s edge that tips a life into legend. In Malory's *Le Morte d'[[king-arthur|Arthur]]*, the knight-errant is defined by movement: he rides out from [[Camelot]] alone or with a single companion, his identity unannounced, his purpose improvised from whatever the road delivers. The term appears in several forms — "knight errant," "knight-errant," "errant-knight" — but the function is constant: to seek, to test, and to be tested.

## The King's Protection

The errant knight enjoys a special status under [[King Arthur]]'s law. After his battle with [[Sir Accolon]], [[king-arthur|Arthur]] delivers a ruling that makes the protection of errant knights a royal charge:

> here may ye see what adventures befall ofttime of errant knights, how that I have fought with a knight of mine own unto my great damage and his both. (078-004)

[[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s own wounding in a [[battle|fight]] arranged by [[treason]] becomes the occasion for legislating the safety of wandering knights. He charges [[Sir Damas]] "upon pain of death, that thou never distress no knights errant that ride on their adventure" (078-014). The errant knight, in this formulation, is sacrosanct — his mobility is the circulatory system of the realm, and to block it is a capital offence. This protection extends to the material: Damas must also restore the twenty knights he has imprisoned "of all their [[arms|harness]]" (078-015), recognizing that an errant knight stripped of his arms is not merely inconvenienced but erased.

## Recognition by Arms and Harness

A knight-errant is known by what he carries. The arms and [[arms|armour]] are not merely equipment but identity:

> see that he is a knight-errant. And so he armed him and horsed him (137-010)

The circular logic is deliberate: one becomes a knight-errant by arming oneself as one. Elsewhere, a knight is recognized as "knight-errant by your arms and [[arms|harness]]" (213-025), confirming that the visual language of [[chivalry]] — the [[shield]], the [[helm]], the [[spear]] — functions as a passport. The errant knight who rides unidentified is still legible to those who know how to read the signs.

## Errantry as Social Obligation

The role of knight-errant is not purely voluntary. Communities expect knights to ride, and knights expect communities to receive them. When [[Sir Lamorak]] encounters two knights awaiting an ambush, he offers: "if ye be knights-errant that will joust, lo I am ready" (215-033). The conditional is revealing: to be a knight-errant is to be assumed willing to [[joust]] at any moment. Refusal would be a denial of the very category.

Conversely, the errant knight imposes duties on those he encounters. A location is described as "convenient for an errant-knight" (149-021), suggesting that the [[realm|land]]scape of [[Logres]] is organised, at least in part, around the needs of wandering [[battle|war]]riors. Certain places are better suited to hosting them; certain [[baron|lord]]s are known to be generous or stingy; the errant knight's passage through the world is a continuous negotiation between his freedom of movement and the hospitality of str[[wrath|anger]]s.

## The Errant Knight and the Damosel

The knight-errant rarely rides ent[[wrath|ire]]ly alone. The archetypal pairing is with the [[damosel]], as in: "how he brought him a knight errant and a damosel that would be led" (090-014). The damosel is not merely a companion but often the narrator of the quest — the one who knows where they are going and why. The errant knight provides the arms; the damosel provides the d[[wrath|ire]]ction. Together they form the smallest possible unit of chivalric narrative: one who acts, and one who witnesses.

## The Errant Knight's Narrative Function

In Malory's structure, the knight-errant is the solution to the problem of how to generate story. The [[Round Table]] at peace produces no narrative; only when a knight rides out from court does something happen. The errant knight is thus a narrative device as much as a social role: he carries the reader from episode to episode, stitching together what would otherwise be a collection of disconnected [[battle]] scenes. His wandering creates the geography of the [[king-arthur|Arthur]]ian world — every [[castle]], [[chapel]], and crossroads is mapped by the knight who passes through it. And because he rides alone, his encounters are private and unverifiable, which is precisely what gives Malory's narration its characteristic texture of rumour, report, and disputed [[honour]].
