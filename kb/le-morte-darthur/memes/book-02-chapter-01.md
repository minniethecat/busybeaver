[032-002]After the death of Uther Pendragon reigned [[king-arthur|Arthur]] his son, the which had great

[032-003][[battle|war]] in his days for to get all England into his hand. For there were many kings

[032-004]within the realm of England, and in Wales, Scotland, and Cornwall. So it befell

[032-005]on a time when King Arthur was at London, there came a knight and told the king

[032-006]tidings how that the King Rience of North Wales had reared a great number of

[032-007]people, and were entered into the land, and burnt and slew the king’s

[032-008]true liege people. If this be true, said Arthur, it were great shame unto mine

[032-009]estate but that he were mightily withstood. It is truth, said the knight, for I

[032-010]saw the host myself. Well, said the king, let make a cry, that all the lords,

[032-011]knights, and gentlemen of arms, should draw unto a castle called [[camelot|Camelot]] in

[032-012]those days, and there the king would let make a council-general and a great

[032-013][[tournament|jousts]].

[032-014]So when the king was come thither with all his baronage, and lodged as they

[032-015]seemed best, there was come a damosel the which was sent on message from the

[032-016]great lady Lile of Avelion. And when she came before King Arthur, she told from

[032-017]whom she came, and how she was sent on message unto him for these causes. Then

[032-018]she let her mantle fall that was richly furred; and then was she girt with a

[032-019]noble sword whereof the king had marvel, and said, Damosel, for what cause are

[032-020]ye girt with that sword? it beseemeth you not. Now shall I tell you, said the

[032-021]damosel; this sword that I am girt withal doth me great sorrow and cumbrance,

[032-022]for I may not be delivered of this sword but by a knight, but he must be a

[032-023]passing good man of his hands and of his deeds, and without villainy or

[032-024][[treason|treachery]], and without treason. And if I may find such a knight that hath all

[032-025]these virtues, he may draw out this sword out of the sheath, for I have been at

[032-026]King Rience’s it was told me there were passing good knights, and he and

[032-027]all his knights have assayed it and none can speed. This is a great marvel,

[032-028]said Arthur, if this be sooth; I will myself assay to draw out the sword, not

[032-029]presuming upon myself that I am the best knight, but that I will begin to draw

[032-030]at your sword in giving example to all the barons that they shall assay everych

[032-031]one after other when I have assayed it. Then Arthur took the sword by the

[032-032]sheath and by the girdle and pulled at it eagerly, but the sword would not out.

[032-033]Sir, said the damosel, you need not to pull half so hard, for he that shall

[032-034]pull it out shall do it with little might. Ye say well, said Arthur; now assay

[032-035]ye all my barons; but beware ye be not defiled with shame, treachery, nor

[032-036]guile. Then it will not avail, said the damosel, for he must be a clean knight

[032-037]without villainy, and of a gentle strain of father side and mother side. Most

[032-038]of all the barons of the [[round-table|Round Table]] that were there at that time assayed all

[032-039]by row, but there might none speed; wherefore the damosel made great sorrow out

[032-040]of measure, and said, Alas! I weened in this court had been the best knights

[032-041]without treachery or treason. By my faith, said Arthur, here are good knights,

[032-042]as I deem, as any be in the world, but their grace is not to help you,

[032-043]wherefore I am displeased.
