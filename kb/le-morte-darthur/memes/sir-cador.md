# Sir Cador of Cornwall

Sir Cador of Cornwall was a knight of King Arthur's Round Table. He served as a chieftain in Arthur's Roman campaign and was the father of Sir Constantine, who after Arthur's death was crowned king of the realm.

## Counsellor of War

When King Arthur received the Roman emperor's demand for truage, he called all his lords and knights of the Round Table to counsel. Sir Cador of Cornwall spoke first, welcoming the challenge: "This message liketh me well, for we have many days rested us and have been idle, and now I hope ye shall make sharp war on the Romans, where I doubt not we shall get honour" (096-032). Arthur acknowledged that the matter pleased Cador well, though the demand grieved the king sorely, for he would never pay truage to Rome.

## Conveyor of the Roman Ambassadors

After Arthur delivered his defiant answer to the Roman ambassadors, he commanded his treasurer to give them great and large gifts, and to pay all their dispenses. The king assigned Sir Cador to convey them out of the land (097-029). Cador escorted the ambassadors to Sandwich, where they took their shipping and passed forth by Flanders, Alemaine, the mountains, and all Italy until they came unto Lucius.

## Chieftain of the Roman Campaign

When Arthur's army crossed the sea, Sir Cador served as one of the chieftains and governors. After the first victories, Arthur sent the prisoners into Paris under the guard of Sir Launcelot and Sir Cador (101-054). Later, as Launcelot and Sir Cador conveyed prisoners through a wood, they discovered a Roman ambush of sixty thousand men. They fought with them manly, and slew and detrenched many of the Romans (102-006).

During the campaign's later stages, the king commanded Sir Cador to take the rearward, with certain knights of the Round Table, while Sir Launcelot, Sir Bors, Sir Kay, Sir Marrok, and Sir Marhaus attended upon Arthur's person (103-014). This dispersal of the host ensured the enemies could not escape.

## Father of a King

Sir Cador was the father of Sir Constantine of Cornwall. When Arthur prepared for his Roman expedition, he ordained two governors of the realm: Sir Baudwin of Britain and Sir Constantine, son to Sir Cador of Cornwall, which after the death of Arthur was king of this realm (098-009). Through his son, Cador's lineage ascended to the throne of Britain itself.
