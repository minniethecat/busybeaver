# Sir Carados of the Dolorous Tower

Sir Carados of the Dolorous Tower was a noble knight of Scotland, brother of Sir Turquine. He was counted among the worthy knights of his time and appeared at great tournaments, though his life was ultimately cut short by Sir Launcelot du Lake.

## The Knight of the Dolorous Tower

Sir Carados took his name and seat from the dolorous tower, a stronghold he shared with his brother Sir Turquine. The brothers were counted among the notable knights of their day, and Sir Carados was regarded as a noble knight of Scotland (153-074). He and his brother Sir Turquine fought together in many encounters, their bond as brothers extending to the field of arms (155-007).

## Host to Sir Gawaine

When Sir Gawaine wandered in search of adventures, he lodged with Sir Carados as his host. It was from Sir Carados's household that Gawaine set out on the morn into the forest, where he encountered Sir Pelleas making great moan out of measure over his unrequited love for Ettard (087-022). This brief role as host places Sir Carados among the company of knights who offered hospitality to the knights of Arthur's court.

## At the Castle of Maidens Tournament

Sir Carados appeared at the great tournament of the Castle of Maidens, where the knights of the castle jousted against King Arthur's knights. There came in Sir Carados of the Dolorous Tower and Sir Turquine, knights of the castle, and encountered with them Sir Percivale de Galis and Sir Lamorak de Galis, that were two brethren (155-004). Sir Percivale encountered with Sir Carados, and either brake their spears unto their hands (155-007). Sir Turquine and Sir Lamorak likewise smote down each other's horses, and either parties rescued the other and horsed them again.

## Death at Sir Launcelot's Hands

Sir Carados's life ended in combat with Sir Launcelot du Lake. Years later, when Sir Launcelot encountered Sir Turquine, the latter revealed the source of his bitter hatred for Launcelot: "he slew my brother, Sir Carados, at the dolorous tower, that was one of the best knights alive" (116-021). For Sir Launcelot's sake, Sir Turquine had slain a hundred good knights and maimed as many more, all in vengeance for his brother's death. Sir Carados's death thus became the catalyst for one of the longest vendettas in Arthurian history.

On another occasion, Sir Launcelot met with Sir Carados himself. Launcelot bade Carados lay down the knight he was carrying and fight with him instead (191-013). Carados refused, and at the last Sir Launcelot smote Sir Carados, ending the encounter (191-020).

## Distinction from King Carados

Sir Carados of the Dolorous Tower should not be confused with King Carados of Scotland, who was one of the rebel kings that fought against Arthur in the early wars and later appeared at tournaments as an ally of Arthur. The two are distinct figures in the narrative.
