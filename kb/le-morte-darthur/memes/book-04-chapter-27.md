[093-002]So was there sikerness made on both parties that no treason should be wrought

[093-003]on neither party; so then the knights departed and made them ready, and that

[093-004]night Sir Uwaine had great cheer. And on the morn he arose early and heard

[093-005]mass, and brake his fast, and so he rode unto the plain without the gates,

[093-006]where hoved the two brethren abiding him. So they rode together passing sore,

[093-007]that Sir Edward and Sir Hue brake their spears upon Sir Uwaine. And Sir Uwaine

[093-008]smote Sir Edward that he fell over his horse and yet his spear brast not. And

[093-009]then he spurred his horse and came upon Sir Hue and overthrew him, but they

[093-010]soon recovered and dressed their shields and drew their swords and bade Sir

[093-011]Uwaine alight and do his battle to the uttermost. Then Sir Uwaine devoided his

[093-012]horse suddenly, and put his shield afore him and drew his sword, and so they

[093-013]dressed together, and either gave other such strokes, and there these two

[093-014]brethren wounded Sir Uwaine passing grievously that the Lady of the Rock weened

[093-015]he should have died. And thus they fought together five hours as men raged out

[093-016]of reason. And at the last Sir Uwaine smote Sir Edward upon the helm such a

[093-017]stroke that his sword carved unto his canel bone, and then Sir Hue abated his

[093-018]courage, but Sir Uwaine pressed fast to have slain him. That saw Sir Hue: he

[093-019]kneeled down and yielded him to Sir Uwaine. And he of his gentleness received

[093-020]his sword, and took him by the hand, and went into the castle together. Then

[093-021]the Lady of the Rock was passing glad, and the other brother made great sorrow

[093-022]for his brother’s death. Then the lady was restored of all her lands, and

[093-023]Sir Hue was commanded to be at [[camelot|the court of King Arthur]] at the next feast of

[093-024]Pentecost. So Sir Uwaine dwelt with the lady nigh half a year, for it was long

[093-025]or he might be whole of his great hurts. And so when it drew nigh the term-day

[093-026]that [[sir-gawaine|Sir Gawaine]], Sir Marhaus, and Sir Uwaine should meet at the cross-way,

[093-027]then every knight drew him thither to hold his promise that they had made; and

[093-028]Sir Marhaus and Sir Uwaine brought their damosels with them, but Sir Gawaine

[093-029]had lost his damosel, as it is afore rehearsed.
