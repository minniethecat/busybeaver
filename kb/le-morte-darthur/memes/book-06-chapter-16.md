[124-001]a lady recovered a falcon, by which he was deceived.

[124-002]And so [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]] rode through many strange countries, over marshes and

[124-003]valleys, till by fortune he came to a fair castle, and as he passed beyond the

[124-004]castle him thought he heard two bells ring. And then was he ware of a falcon

[124-005]came flying over his head toward an high elm, and long lunes about her feet,

[124-006]and as she flew unto the elm to take her perch the lunes over-cast about a

[124-007]bough. And when she would have taken her flight she hung by the legs fast; and

[124-008]Sir Launcelot saw how she hung, and beheld the fair falcon perigot, and he was

[124-009]sorry for her.

[124-010]The meanwhile came a lady out of the castle and cried on high: O Launcelot,

[124-011]Launcelot, as thou art flower of all knights, help me to get my hawk, for an my

[124-012]hawk be lost my lord will destroy me; for I kept the hawk and she slipped from

[124-013]me, and if my lord my husband wit it he is so hasty that he will slay me. What

[124-014]is your lord’s name? said Sir Launcelot. Sir, she said, his name is Sir

[124-015]Phelot, a knight that longeth unto [[king-arthur|the King]] of Northgalis. Well, fair lady,

[124-016]since that ye know my name, and require me of knighthood to help you, I will do

[124-017]what I may to get your hawk, and yet God knoweth I am an ill climber, and the

[124-018]tree is passing high, and few boughs to help me withal. And therewith Sir

[124-019]Launcelot alighted, and tied his horse to the same tree, and prayed the lady to

[124-020]unarm him. And so when he was unarmed, he put off all his clothes unto his

[124-021]shirt and breech, and with might and force he clomb up to the falcon, and tied

[124-022]the lines to a great rotten boyshe, and threw the hawk down and it withal.

[124-023]Anon the lady gat the hawk in her hand; and therewithal came out Sir Phelot out

[124-024]of the groves suddenly, that was her husband, all armed and with his naked

[124-025]sword in his hand, and said: O knight Launcelot, now have I found thee as I

[124-026]would, and stood at the bole of the tree to slay him. Ah, lady, said Sir

[124-027]Launcelot, why have ye betrayed me? She hath done, said Sir Phelot, but as I

[124-028]commanded her, and therefore there nis none other boot but thine hour is come

[124-029]that thou must die. That were shame unto thee, said Sir Launcelot, thou an

[124-030]armed knight to slay a naked man by treason. Thou gettest none other grace,

[124-031]said Sir Phelot, and therefore help thyself an thou canst. Truly, said Sir

[124-032]Launcelot, that shall be thy shame, but since thou wilt do none other, take

[124-033]mine harness with thee, and hang my sword upon a bough that I may get it, and

[124-034]then do thy best to slay me an thou canst. Nay, nay, said Sir Phelot, for I

[124-035]know thee better than thou weenest, therefore thou gettest no weapon, an I may

[124-036]keep you therefrom. Alas, said Sir Launcelot, that ever a knight should die

[124-037]weaponless. And therewith he waited above him and under him, and over his head

[124-038]he saw a rownsepyk, a big bough leafless, and therewith he brake it off by the

[124-039]body. And then he came lower and awaited how his own horse stood, and suddenly

[124-040]he leapt on the further side of the horse, fro-ward the knight. And then Sir

[124-041]Phelot lashed at him eagerly, weening to have slain him. But Sir Launcelot put

[124-042]away the stroke with the rownsepyk, and therewith he smote him on the one side

[124-043]of the head, that he fell down in a swoon to the ground. So then Sir Launcelot

[124-044]took his sword out of his hand, and struck his neck from the body. Then cried

[124-045]the lady, Alas! why hast thou slain my husband? I am not causer, said Sir

[124-046]Launcelot, for with falsehood ye would have had slain me with treason, and now

[124-047]it is fallen on you both. And then she swooned as though she would die. And

[124-048]therewithal Sir Launcelot gat all his armour as well as he might, and put it

[124-049]upon him for dread of more resort, for he dreaded that the knight’s

[124-050]castle was so nigh. And so, as soon as he might, he took his horse and

[124-051]departed, and thanked God that he had escaped that adventure.
