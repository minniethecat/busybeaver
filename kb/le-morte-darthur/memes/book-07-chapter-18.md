[145-001]how [[sir-gareth|Beaumains]] made him to go unto [[king-arthur|King Arthur]]’s court, and to cry Sir

[145-002][[sir-launcelot|Launcelot]] mercy.

[145-003]Then came there many earls, and barons, and noble knights, and prayed that

[145-004]knight to save his life, and take him to your prisoner. And all they fell upon

[145-005]their knees, and prayed him of mercy, and that he would save his life; and,

[145-006]Sir, they all said, it were fairer of him to take homage and fealty, and let

[145-007]him hold his lands of you than for to slay him; by his death ye shall have none

[145-008]advantage, and his misdeeds that be done may not be undone; and therefore he

[145-009]shall make amends to all parties, and we all will become your men and do you

[145-010]homage and fealty. Fair lords, said Beaumains, wit you well I am full loath to

[145-011]slay this knight, nevertheless he hath done passing ill and shamefully; but

[145-012]insomuch all that he did was at a lady’s request I blame him the less;

[145-013]and so for your sake I will release him that he shall have his life upon this

[145-014]covenant, that he go within the castle, and yield him there to the lady, and if

[145-015]she will forgive and quit him, I will well; with this he make her amends of all

[145-016]the trespass he hath done against her and her lands. And also, when that is

[145-017]done, that ye go unto [[camelot|the court of King Arthur]], and there that ye ask Sir

[145-018]Launcelot mercy, and [[sir-gawaine|Sir Gawaine]], for the evil will ye have had against them.

[145-019]Sir, said the Red Knight of the Red Launds, all this will I do as ye command,

[145-020]and siker assurance and borrows ye shall have. And so then when the assurance

[145-021]was made, he made his homage and fealty, and all those earls and barons with

[145-022]him.

[145-023]And then the maiden Linet came to Sir Beaumains, and unarmed him and searched

[145-024]his wounds, and stinted his blood, and in likewise she did to the Red Knight of

[145-025]the Red Launds. And there they sojourned ten days in their tents; and the Red

[145-026]Knight made his lords and servants to do all the pleasure that they might unto

[145-027]Sir Beaumains. And so within a while the Red Knight of the Red Launds yede unto

[145-028]the castle, and put him in her grace. And so she received him upon sufficient

[145-029]surety, so all her hurts were well restored of all that she could complain. And

[145-030]then he departed unto the court of King Arthur, and there openly the Red Knight

[145-031]of the Red Launds put him in the mercy of Sir Launcelot and Sir Gawaine, and

[145-032]there he told openly how he was overcome and by whom, and also he told all the

[145-033]battles from the beginning unto the ending. Jesu mercy, said King Arthur and

[145-034]Sir Gawaine, we marvel much of what blood he is come, for he is a noble knight.

[145-035]Have ye no marvel, said Sir Launcelot, for ye shall right well wit that he is

[145-036]come of a full noble blood; and as for his might and [[courage|hardiness]], there be but

[145-037]few now living that is so mighty as he is, and so noble of prowess. It seemeth

[145-038]by you, said King Arthur, that ye know his name, and from whence he is come,

[145-039]and of what blood he is. I suppose I do so, said Launcelot, or else I would not

[145-040]have given him the order of knighthood; but he gave me such charge at that time

[145-041]that I should never discover him until he required me, or else it be known

[145-042]openly by some other.
