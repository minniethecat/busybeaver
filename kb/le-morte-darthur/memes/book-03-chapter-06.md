[057-001]again the hart, and how two brethren fought each against other for the

[057-002]hart.

[057-003][[sir-gawaine|Sir Gawaine]] rode more than a pace, and Gaheris his brother that rode with him

[057-004]instead of a squire to do him service. So as they rode they saw two knights

[057-005][[battle|fight]] on horseback passing sore, so Sir Gawaine and his brother rode betwixt

[057-006]them, and asked them for what cause they fought so. The one knight answered and

[057-007]said, We fight for a simple matter, for we two be two brethren born and

[057-008]begotten of one man and of one woman. Alas, said Sir Gawaine, why do ye so?

[057-009]Sir, said the elder, there came a white hart this way this day, and many hounds

[057-010]chased him, and a white brachet was alway next him, and we understood it was

[057-011]adventure made for the high feast of [[king-arthur|King Arthur]], and therefore I would have

[057-012]gone after to have won me [[honour|worship]]; and here my younger brother said he would go

[057-013]after the hart, for he was better knight than I: and for this cause we fell at

[057-014]debate, and so we thought to prove which of us both was better knight. This is

[057-015]a simple cause, said Sir Gawaine; uncouth men ye should debate withal, and not

[057-016]brother with brother; therefore but if you will do by my counsel I will have

[057-017]ado with you, that is ye shall yield you unto me, and that ye go unto King

[057-018]Arthur and yield you unto his grace. Sir knight, said the two brethren, we are

[057-019]forfoughten and much blood have we lost through our wilfulness, and therefore

[057-020]we would be loath to have ado with you. Then do as I will have you, said Sir

[057-021]Gawaine. We will agree to fulfil your will; but by whom shall we say that we be

[057-022]thither sent? Ye may say, By the knight that followeth the quest of the hart

[057-023]that was white. Now what is your name? said Gawaine. Sorlouse of the Forest,

[057-024]said the elder. And my name is, said the younger, Brian of the Forest. And so

[057-025]they departed and went to the king’s court, and Sir Gawaine on his quest.

[057-026]And as Gawaine followed the hart by the cry of the hounds, even afore him there

[057-027]was a great river, and the hart swam over; and as Sir Gawaine would follow

[057-028]after, there stood a knight over the other side, and said, Sir knight, come not

[057-029]over after this hart but if thou wilt joust with me. I will not fail as for

[057-030]that, said Sir Gawaine, to follow the quest that I am in, and so made his horse

[057-031]to swim over the water. And anon they gat their spears and ran together full

[057-032]hard; but Sir Gawaine smote him off his horse, and then he turned his horse and

[057-033]bade him yield him. Nay, said the knight, not so, though thou have the better

[057-034]of me on horseback. I pray thee, valiant knight, alight afoot, and match we

[057-035]together with swords. What is your name? said Sir Gawaine. Allardin of the

[057-036]Isles, said the other. Then either dressed their shields and smote together,

[057-037]but Sir Gawaine smote him so hard through the helm that it went to the brains,

[057-038]and the knight fell down dead. Ah! said Gaheris, that was a mighty stroke of a

[057-039]young knight.

## CHAPTER VIIHow the hart was chased into a castle
and there slain, and how Sir Gawaine slew a lady.

[057-040]Then Gawaine and Gaheris rode more than a pace after the white hart, and let

[057-041]slip at the hart three couple of greyhounds, and so they chased the hart into a

[057-042]castle, and in the chief place of the castle they slew the hart; Sir Gawaine

[057-043]and Gaheris followed after. Right so there came a knight out of a chamber with

[057-044]a sword drawn in his hand and slew two of the greyhounds, even in the sight of

[057-045]Sir Gawaine, and the remnant he chased them with his sword out of the castle.

[057-046]And when he came again, he said, O my white hart, me repenteth that thou art

[057-047]dead, for my sovereign lady gave thee to me, and evil have I kept thee, and thy

[057-048]death shall be dear bought an I live. And anon he went into his chamber and

[057-049]armed him, and came out fiercely, and there met he with Sir Gawaine. Why have

[057-050]ye slain my hounds? said Sir Gawaine, for they did but their kind, and liefer I

[057-051]had ye had wroken your anger upon me than upon a dumb beast. Thou sayest truth,

[057-052]said the knight, I have avenged me on thy hounds, and so I will on thee or thou

[057-053]go. Then Sir Gawaine alighted afoot and dressed his shield, and struck together

[057-054]mightily, and clave their shields, and stoned their helms, and brake their

[057-055]hauberks that the blood ran down to their feet.

[057-056]At the last Sir Gawaine smote the knight so hard that he fell to the earth, and

[057-057]then he cried mercy, and yielded him, and besought him as he was a knight and

[057-058]gentleman, to save his life. Thou shalt die, said Sir Gawaine, for slaying of

[057-059]my hounds. I will make amends, said the knight, unto my power. Sir Gawaine

[057-060]would no mercy have, but unlaced his helm to have stricken off his head. Right

[057-061]so came his lady out of a chamber and fell over him, and so he smote off her

[057-062]head by misadventure. Alas, said Gaheris, that is foully and shamefully done,

[057-063]that shame shall never from you; also ye should give mercy unto them that ask

[057-064]mercy, for a knight without mercy is without worship. Sir Gawaine was so

[057-065]stonied of the death of this fair lady that he wist not what he did, and said

[057-066]unto the knight, Arise, I will give thee mercy. Nay, nay, said the knight, I

[057-067]take no force of mercy now, for thou hast slain my love and my lady that I

[057-068]loved best of all earthly things. Me sore repenteth it, said Sir Gawaine, for I

[057-069]thought to strike unto thee; but now thou shalt go unto King Arthur and tell

[057-070]him of thine adventures, and how thou art overcome by the knight that went in

[057-071]the quest of the white hart. I take no force, said the knight, whether I live

[057-072]or I die; but so for dread of death he swore to go unto King Arthur, and he

[057-073]made him to bear one greyhound before him on his horse, and another behind him.

[057-074]What is your name? said Sir Gawaine, or we depart. My name is, said the knight,

[057-075]Ablamar of the Marsh. So he departed toward [[camelot|Camelot]].
