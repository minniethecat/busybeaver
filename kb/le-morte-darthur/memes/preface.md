[002-001]After that I had accomplished and finished divers histories, as well of

[002-002]contemplation as of other historial and worldly acts of great conquerors and

[002-003]princes, and also certain books of ensamples and doctrine, many noble and

[002-004]divers gentlemen of this realm of England came and demanded me many and oft

[002-005]times, wherefore that I have not do made and imprint the noble history of the

[002-006]Saint Greal, and of the most renowned Christian king, first and chief of the

[002-007]three best Christian, and worthy, [[king-arthur|King Arthur]], which ought most to be

[002-008]remembered among us Englishmen to-fore all other Christian kings; for it is

[002-009]notoyrly known through the universal world, that there be nine worthy and the

[002-010]best that ever were, that is to wit, three Paynims, three Jews, and three

[002-011]Christian men. As for the Paynims, they were to-fore the Incarnation of Christ,

[002-012]which were named, the first Hector of Troy, of whom the history is comen both

[002-013]in ballad and in prose, the second Alexander the Great, and the third Julius

[002-014]Caesar, Emperor of Rome, of whom the histories be well known and had. And as

[002-015]for the three Jews, which also were to-fore the incarnation of our Lord, of

[002-016]whom the first was duke Joshua which brought the children of Israel into the

[002-017]land of behest, the second David king of Jerusalem, and the third Judas

[002-018]Machabeus, of these three the Bible rehearseth all their noble histories and

[002-019]acts. And since the said Incarnation have been three noble Christian men,

[002-020]stalled and admitted through the universal world into the number of the nine

[002-021]best and worthy. Of whom was first the noble Arthur, whose noble acts I purpose

[002-022]to write in this present book here following. The second was Charlemain, or

[002-023]Charles the Great, of whom the history is had in many places, both in French

[002-024]and in English. And the third and last was Godfrey of Boloine, of whose acts

[002-025]and life I made a book unto the excellent prince and king of noble memory, King

[002-026]Edward the Fourth.

[002-027]The said noble gentlemen instantly required me to imprint the history of the

[002-028]said noble king and conqueror King Arthur, and of his knights, with the history

[002-029]of the Saint Greal, and of the death and ending of the said Arthur; affirming

[002-030]that I ought rather to imprint his acts and noble feats, than of Godfrey of

[002-031]Boloine, or any of the other eight, considering that he was a man born within

[002-032]this realm, and king and emperor of the same: and that there be in French

[002-033]divers and many noble volumes of his acts, and also of his knights. To whom I

[002-034]answered that divers men hold opinion that there was no such Arthur, and that

[002-035]all such books as been made of him be feigned and fables, because that some

[002-036]chronicles make of him no mention, nor remember him nothing, nor of his

[002-037]knights. Whereto they answered, and one in special said, that in him that

[002-038]should say or think that there was never such a king called Arthur might well

[002-039]be aretted great folly and blindness. For he said that there were many

[002-040]evidences of the contrary. First ye may see his sepulchre in the monastery of

[002-041]Glastonbury. And also in Policronicon, in the fifth book the sixth chapter, and

[002-042]in the seventh book the twenty-third chapter, where his body was buried, and

[002-043]after found, and translated into the said monastery. Ye shall see also in the

[002-044]history of Bochas, in his book De Casu Principum, part of his noble acts, and

[002-045]also of his fall. Also Galfridus in his British book recounteth his life: and

[002-046]in divers places of England many remembrances be yet of him, and shall remain

[002-047]perpetually, and also of his knights. First in the abbey of Westminster, at St.

[002-048]Edward’s shrine, remaineth the print of his seal in red wax closed in

[002-049]beryl, in which is written, Patricius Arthurus Britannie, Gallie, Germanie,

[002-050]Dacie, Imperator. Item in the castle of Dover ye may see [[sir-gawaine|Gawaine]]’s skull,

[002-051]and Cradok’s mantle: at Winchester the [[round-table|Round Table]]: in other places

[002-052][[sir-launcelot|Launcelot]]’s sword and many other things. Then all these things

[002-053]considered, there can no man reasonably gainsay but there was a king of this

[002-054]land named Arthur. For in all places, Christian and heathen, he is reputed and

[002-055]taken for one of the nine worthy, and the first of the three Christian men. And

[002-056]also, he is more spoken of beyond the sea, more books made of his noble acts,

[002-057]than there be in England, as well in Dutch, Italian, Spanish, and Greekish, as

[002-058]in French. And yet of record remain in witness of him in Wales, in the town of

[002-059][[camelot|Camelot]], the great stones and the [[marvel|marvellous]] works of iron lying under the

[002-060]ground, and royal vaults, which divers now living have seen. Wherefore it is a

[002-061]marvel why he is no more renowned in his own country, save only it accordeth to

[002-062]the Word of God, which saith that no man is accepted for a prophet in his own

[002-063]country.

[002-064]Then all these things aforesaid alleged, I could not well deny but that there

[002-065]was such a noble king named Arthur, and reputed one of the nine worthy, and

[002-066]first and chief of the Christian men. And many noble volumes be made of him and

[002-067]of his noble knights in French, which I have seen and read beyond the sea,

[002-068]which be not had in our maternal tongue. But in Welsh be many and also in

[002-069]French, and some in English but nowhere nigh all. Wherefore, such as have late

[002-070]been drawn out briefly into English I have after the simple conning that God

[002-071]hath sent to me, under the favour and correction of all noble lords and

[002-072]gentlemen, enprised to imprint a book of the noble histories of the said King

[002-073]Arthur, and of certain of his knights, after a copy unto me delivered, which

[002-074]copy Sir Thomas Malorye did take out of certain books of French, and reduced it

[002-075]into English. And I, according to my copy, have done set it in imprint, to the

[002-076]intent that noble men may see and learn the noble acts of chivalry, the gentle

[002-077]and virtuous deeds that some knights used in those days, by which they came to

[002-078]honour, and how they that were vicious were punished and oft put to shame and

[002-079]rebuke; humbly beseeching all noble lords and ladies, with all other estates of

[002-080]what estate or degree they been of, that shall see and read in this said book

[002-081]and work, that they take the good and honest acts in their remembrance, and to

[002-082]follow the same. Wherein they shall find many joyous and pleasant histories,

[002-083]and noble and renowned acts of humanity, gentleness, and chivalry. For herein

[002-084]may be seen noble chivalry, courtesy, humanity, friendliness, [[courage|hardiness]], love,

[002-085]friendship, cowardice, murder, hate, virtue, and sin. Do after the good and

[002-086]leave the evil, and it shall bring you to good fame and [[honour|renown]]. And for to pass

[002-087]the time this book shall be pleasant to read in, but for to give faith and

[002-088][[faith|belief]] that all is true that is contained herein, ye be at your liberty: but

[002-089]all is written for our doctrine, and for to beware that we fall not to vice nor

[002-090]sin, but to exercise and follow virtue, by which we may come and attain to good

[002-091]fame and renown in this life, and after this short and transitory life to come

[002-092]unto everlasting bliss in heaven; the which He grant us that reigneth in

[002-093]heaven, the blessed Trinity. Amen.

[002-094]Then to proceed forth in this said book, which I direct unto all noble princes,

[002-095]lords and ladies, gentlemen or gentlewomen, that desire to read or hear read of

[002-096]the noble and joyous history of the great conqueror and excellent king, King

[002-097]Arthur, sometime king of this noble realm, then called Britain; I, William

[002-098]Caxton, simple person, present this book following, which I have enprised to

[002-099]imprint: and treateth of the noble acts, feats of arms of chivalry, prowess,

[002-100]hardiness, humanity, love, courtesy, and very gentleness, with many wonderful

[002-101]histories and adventures. And for to understand briefly the content of this

[002-102]volume, I have divided it into XXI Books, and every book chaptered, as

[002-103]hereafter shall by God’s grace follow. The First Book shall treat how

[002-104]Uther Pendragon gat the noble conqueror King Arthur, and containeth xxviii

[002-105]chapters. The Second Book treateth of Balin the noble knight, and containeth

[002-106]xix chapters. The Third Book treateth of the marriage of King Arthur to Queen

[002-107][[queen-guenever|Guenever]], with other matters, and containeth xv chapters. The Fourth Book, how

[002-108][[merlin|Merlin]] was assotted, and of [[battle|war]] made to King Arthur, and containeth xxix

[002-109]chapters. The Fifth Book treateth of the conquest of Lucius the emperor, and

[002-110]containeth xii chapters. The Sixth Book treateth of Sir Launcelot and Sir

[002-111]Lionel, and marvellous adventures, and containeth xviii chapters. The Seventh

[002-112]Book treateth of a noble knight called [[sir-gareth|Sir Gareth]], and named by [[sir-kay|Sir Kay]]

[002-113]Beaumains, and containeth xxxvi chapters. The Eighth Book treateth of the birth

[002-114]of [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]] the noble knight, and of his acts, and containeth xli chapters.

[002-115]The Ninth Book treateth of a knight named by Sir Kay Le Cote Male Taille, and

[002-116]also of Sir Tristram, and containeth xliv chapters. The Tenth Book treateth of

[002-117]Sir Tristram, and other marvellous adventures, and containeth lxxxviii

[002-118]chapters. The Eleventh Book treateth of Sir Launcelot and Sir Galahad, and

[002-119]containeth xiv chapters. The Twelfth Book treateth of Sir Launcelot and his

[002-120]madness, and containeth xiv chapters. The Thirteenth Book treateth how Galahad

[002-121]came first to king Arthur’s court, and the quest how the Sangreal was

[002-122]begun, and containeth xx chapters. The Fourteenth Book treateth of the quest of

[002-123]the Sangreal, and containeth x chapters. The Fifteenth Book treateth of Sir

[002-124]Launcelot, and containeth vi chapters. The Sixteenth Book treateth of [[sir-bors|Sir Bors]]

[002-125]and Sir Lionel his brother, and containeth xvii chapters. The Seventeenth Book

[002-126]treateth of the Sangreal, and containeth xxiii chapters. The Eighteenth Book

[002-127]treateth of Sir Launcelot and the queen, and containeth xxv chapters. The

[002-128]Nineteenth Book treateth of Queen Guenever and Launcelot, and containeth xiii

[002-129]chapters. The Twentieth Book treateth of the piteous death of Arthur, and

[002-130]containeth xxii chapters. The Twenty-first Book treateth of his last departing,

[002-131]and how Sir Launcelot came to revenge his death, and containeth xiii chapters.

[002-132]The sum is twenty-one books, which contain the sum of five hundred and seven

[002-133]chapters, as more plainly shall follow hereafter.
