[242-001][[sir-palomides|Palomides]], and Sir Dinadan had been long in prison they were delivered.

[242-002]Now leave we off this tale, and speak we of Sir Dinas that had within the

[242-003]castle a paramour, and she loved another knight better than him. And so when

[242-004]Sir Dinas went out a-hunting she slipped down by a towel, and took with her two

[242-005]brachets, and so she yede to the knight that she loved, and he her again. And

[242-006]when Sir Dinas came home and missed his paramour and his brachets, then was he

[242-007]the more wrother for his brachets than for the lady. So then he rode after the

[242-008]knight that had his paramour, and bade him turn and joust. So Sir Dinas smote

[242-009]him down, that with the fall he brake his leg and his arm. And then his lady

[242-010]and paramour cried Sir Dinas mercy, and said she would love him better than

[242-011]ever she did. Nay, said Sir Dinas, I shall never trust them that once betrayed

[242-012]me, and therefore, as ye have begun, so end, for I will never meddle with you.

[242-013]And so Sir Dinas departed, and took his brachets with him, and so rode to his

[242-014]castle.

[242-015]Now will we turn unto [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]], that was right heavy that he could never

[242-016]hear no tidings of [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]], for all this while he was in prison with Sir

[242-017]Darras, Palomides, and Dinadan. Then Dame Bragwaine took her leave to go into

[242-018]Cornwall, and Sir Launcelot, [[sir-kay|Sir Kay]], and Sir Gaheris rode to seek Sir Tristram

[242-019]in the country of Surluse.

[242-020]Now speaketh this tale of Sir Tristram and of his two fellows, for every day

[242-021]Sir Palomides brawled and said language against Sir Tristram. I marvel, said

[242-022]Sir Dinadan, of thee, Sir Palomides, an thou haddest Sir Tristram here thou

[242-023]wouldst do him no harm; for an a wolf and a sheep were together in a prison the

[242-024]wolf would suffer the sheep to be in peace. And wit thou well, said Sir

[242-025]Dinadan, this same is Sir Tristram at a word, and now must thou do thy best

[242-026]with him, and let see now if ye can skift it with your hands. Then was Sir

[242-027]Palomides abashed and said little. Sir Palomides, then said Sir Tristram, I

[242-028]have heard much of your maugre against me, but I will not meddle with you as at

[242-029]this time by my will, because I dread the lord of this place that hath us in

[242-030]governance; for an I dread him not more than I do thee, soon it should be

[242-031]skift: so they peaced themself. Right so came in a damosel and said: Knights,

[242-032]be of good cheer, for ye are sure of your lives, and that I heard say my lord,

[242-033]Sir Darras. Then were they glad all three, for daily they weened they should

[242-034]have died.

[242-035]Then soon after this Sir Tristram fell sick that he weened to have died; then

[242-036]Sir Dinadan wept, and so did Sir Palomides under them both making great sorrow.

[242-037]So a damosel came in to them and found them mourning. Then she went unto Sir

[242-038]Darras, and told him how that mighty knight that bare the black shield was

[242-039]likely to die. That shall not be, said Sir Darras, for God defend when knights

[242-040]come to me for succour that I should suffer them to die within my prison.

[242-041]Therefore, said Sir Darras to the damosel, fetch that knight and his fellows

[242-042]afore me. And then anon Sir Darras saw Sir Tristram brought afore him. He said:

[242-043]Sir knight, me repenteth of thy sickness for thou art called a full noble

[242-044]knight, and so it seemeth by thee; and wit ye well it shall never be said that

[242-045]Sir Darras shall destroy such a noble knight as thou art in prison, howbeit

[242-046]that thou hast slain three of my sons whereby I was greatly aggrieved. But now

[242-047]shalt thou go and thy fellows, and your harness and horses have been fair and

[242-048]clean kept, and ye shall go where it liketh you, upon this covenant, that thou,

[242-049]knight, wilt promise me to be good friend to my sons two that be now alive, and

[242-050]also that thou tell me thy name. Sir, said he, as for me my name is Sir

[242-051]Tristram de Liones, and in Cornwall was I born, and nephew I am unto [[king-mark|King Mark]].

[242-052]And as for the death of your sons I might not do withal, for an they had been

[242-053]the next kin that I have I might have done none otherwise. And if I had slain

[242-054]them by treason or [[treason|treachery]] I had been worthy to have died. All this I

[242-055]consider, said Sir Darras, that all that ye did was by force of knighthood, and

[242-056]that was the cause I would not put you to death. But sith ye be Sir Tristram,

[242-057]the good knight, I pray you heartily to be my good friend and to my sons. Sir,

[242-058]said Sir Tristram, I promise you by the faith of my body, ever while I live I

[242-059]will do you service, for ye have done to us but as a natural knight ought to

[242-060]do. Then Sir Tristram reposed him there till that he was amended of his

[242-061]sickness; and when he was big and strong they took their leave, and every

[242-062]knight took their horses, and so departed and rode together till they came to a

[242-063]cross way. Now fellows, said Sir Tristram, here will we depart in sundry ways.

[242-064]And because Sir Dinadan had the first adventure of him I will begin.

## CHAPTER XL.How Sir Dinadan rescued a lady from
Sir Breuse Saunce Pité, and how Sir Tristram received a shield of [[morgan-le-fay|Morgan]] le

[242-065]Fay.

[242-066]So as Sir Dinadan rode by a well he found a lady making great dole. What aileth

[242-067]you? said Sir Dinadan. Sir knight, said the lady, I am the wofullest lady of

[242-068]the world, for within these five days here came a knight called Sir Breuse

[242-069]Saunce Pité, and he slew mine own brother, and ever since he hath kept me at

[242-070]his own will, and of all men in the world I hate him most; and therefore I

[242-071]require you of knighthood to avenge me, for he will not tarry, but be here

[242-072]anon. Let him come, said Sir Dinadan, and because of honour of all women I will

[242-073]do my part. With this came Sir Breuse, and when he saw a knight with his lady

[242-074]he was wood wroth. And then he said: Sir knight, keep thee from me. So they

[242-075]hurtled together as thunder, and either smote other passing sore, but Sir

[242-076]Dinadan put him through the shoulder a grievous wound, and or ever Sir Dinadan

[242-077]might turn him Sir Breuse was gone and fled. Then the lady prayed him to bring

[242-078]her to a castle there beside but four mile thence; and so Sir Dinadan brought

[242-079]her there, and she was welcome, for the lord of that castle was her uncle; and

[242-080]so Sir Dinadan rode his way upon his adventure.

[242-081]Now turn we this tale unto Sir Tristram, that by adventure he came to a castle

[242-082]to ask lodging, wherein was Queen Morgan le Fay; and so when Sir Tristram was

[242-083]let into that castle he had good cheer all that night. And upon the morn when

[242-084]he would have departed the queen said: Wit ye well ye shall not depart lightly,

[242-085]for ye are here as a prisoner. Jesu defend! said Sir Tristram, for I was but

[242-086]late a prisoner. Fair knight, said the queen, ye shall abide with me till that

[242-087]I wit what ye are and from whence ye come. And ever the queen would set Sir

[242-088]Tristram on her own side, and her paramour on the other side. And ever Queen

[242-089]Morgan would behold Sir Tristram, and thereat the knight was jealous, and was

[242-090]in will suddenly to have run upon Sir Tristram with a sword, but he left it for

[242-091]shame. Then the queen said to Sir Tristram: Tell me thy name, and I shall

[242-092]suffer you to depart when ye will. Upon that covenant I tell you my name is Sir

[242-093]Tristram de Liones. Ah, said Morgan le Fay, an I had wist that, thou shouldst

[242-094]not have departed so soon as thou shalt. But sithen I have made a promise I

[242-095]will hold it, with that thou wilt promise me to bear upon thee a shield that I

[242-096]shall deliver thee, unto the castle of the Hard Rock, where [[king-arthur|King Arthur]] had

[242-097]cried a great tournament, and there I pray you that ye will be, and to do for

[242-098]me as much deeds of arms as ye may do. For at the Castle of Maidens, Sir

[242-099]Tristram, ye did [[marvel|marvellous]] deeds of arms as ever I heard knight do. Madam,

[242-100]said Sir Tristram, let me see the shield that I shall bear. Then the shield was

[242-101]brought forth, and the [[battle|field]] was goldish, with a king and a queen therein

[242-102]painted, and a knight standing above them, [one foot] upon the king’s

[242-103]head, and the other upon the queen’s. Madam, said Sir Tristram, this is a

[242-104]fair shield and a mighty; but what signifieth this king and this queen, and the

[242-105]knight standing upon both their heads? I shall tell you, said Morgan le Fay, it

[242-106]signifieth King Arthur and [[queen-guenever|Queen Guenever]], and a knight who holdeth them both

[242-107]in bondage and in servage. Who is that knight? said Sir Tristram. That shall ye

[242-108]not wit as at this time, said the queen. But as the French book saith, Queen

[242-109]Morgan loved Sir Launcelot best, and ever she desired him, and he would never

[242-110]love her nor do nothing at her request, and therefore she held many knights

[242-111]together for to have taken him by strength. And because she deemed that Sir

[242-112]Launcelot loved Queen Guenever paramour, and she him again, therefore Queen

[242-113]Morgan le Fay ordained that shield to put Sir Launcelot to a rebuke, to that

[242-114]intent that King Arthur might understand the love between them. Then Sir

[242-115]Tristram took that shield and promised her to bear it at the tournament at the

[242-116]Castle of the Hard Rock. But Sir Tristram knew not that that shield was

[242-117]ordained against Sir Launcelot, but afterward he knew it.

## CHAPTER XLI.How Sir Tristram took with him the
shield, and also how he slew the paramour of Morgan le Fay.

[242-118]So then Sir Tristram took his leave of the queen, and took the shield with him.

[242-119]Then came the knight that held Queen Morgan le Fay, his name was Sir Hemison,

[242-120]and he made him ready to follow Sir Tristram. Fair friend, said Morgan, ride

[242-121]not after that knight, for ye shall not win no [[honour|worship]] of him. Fie on him,

[242-122]coward, said Sir Hemison, for I wist never good knight come out of Cornwall but

[242-123]if it were Sir Tristram de Liones. What an that be he? said she. Nay, nay, said

[242-124]he, he is with La Beale Isoud, and this is but a daffish knight. Alas, my fair

[242-125]friend, ye shall find him the best knight that ever ye met withal, for I know

[242-126]him better than ye do. For your sake, said Sir Hemison, I shall slay him. Ah,

[242-127]fair friend, said the queen, me repenteth that ye will follow that knight, for

[242-128]I fear me sore of your again coming. With this this knight rode his way wood

[242-129]wroth, and he rode after Sir Tristram as fast as he had been chased with

[242-130]knights. When Sir Tristram heard a knight come after him so fast he returned

[242-131]about, and saw a knight coming against him. And when he came nigh to Sir

[242-132]Tristram he cried on high: Sir knight, keep thee from me. Then they rushed

[242-133]together as it had been thunder, and Sir Hemison brised his spear upon Sir

[242-134]Tristram, but his harness was so good that he might not hurt him. And Sir

[242-135]Tristram smote him harder, and bare him through the body, and he fell over his

[242-136]horse’s croup. Then Sir Tristram turned to have done more with his sword,

[242-137]but he saw so much blood go from him that him seemed he was likely to die, and

[242-138]so he departed from him and came to a fair manor to an old knight, and there

[242-139]Sir Tristram lodged.

## CHAPTER XLII.How Morgan le Fay buried her
paramour, and how Sir Tristram praised Sir Launcelot and his kin.

[242-140]Now leave to speak of Sir Tristram, and speak we of the knight that was wounded

[242-141]to the death. Then his varlet alighted, and took off his helm, and then he

[242-142]asked his lord whether there were any life in him. There is in me life, said

[242-143]the knight, but it is but little; and therefore leap thou up behind me when

[242-144]thou hast holpen me up, and hold me fast that I fall not, and bring me to Queen

[242-145]Morgan le Fay; for deep draughts of death draw to my heart that I may not live,

[242-146]for I would fain speak with her or I died: for else my soul will be in great

[242-147]peril an I die. For[thwith] with great pain his varlet brought him to the

[242-148]castle, and there Sir Hemison fell down dead. When Morgan le Fay saw him dead

[242-149]she made great sorrow out of reason; and then she let despoil him unto his

[242-150]shirt, and so she let him put into a tomb. And about the tomb she let write:

[242-151]Here lieth Sir Hemison, slain by the hands of Sir Tristram de Liones.

[242-152]Now turn we unto Sir Tristram, that asked the knight his host if he saw late

[242-153]any knights adventurous. Sir, he said, the last night here lodged with me Ector

[242-154]de Maris and a damosel with him, and that damosel told me that he was one of

[242-155]the best knights of the world. That is not so, said Sir Tristram, for I know

[242-156]four better knights of his own blood, and the first is Sir Launcelot du Lake,

[242-157]call him the best knight, and [[sir-bors|Sir Bors de Ganis]], Sir Bleoberis, Sir Blamore de

[242-158]Ganis, and Sir Gaheris. Nay, said his host, [[sir-gawaine|Sir Gawaine]] is a better knight than

[242-159]he. That is not so, said Sir Tristram, for I have met with them both, and I

[242-160]felt Sir Gaheris for the better knight, and Sir Lamorak I call him as good as

[242-161]any of them except Sir Launcelot. Why name ye not Sir Tristram? said his host,

[242-162]for I account him as good as any of them. I know not Sir Tristram, said

[242-163]Tristram. Thus they talked and bourded as long as them list, and then went to

[242-164]rest. And on the morn Sir Tristram departed, and took his leave of his host,

[242-165]and rode toward the Roche Dure, and none adventure had Sir Tristram but that;

[242-166]and so he rested not till he came to the castle, where he saw five hundred

[242-167]tents.

## CHAPTER XLIII.How Sir Tristram at a tournament
bare the shield that Morgan le Fay delivered to him.

[242-168]Then the King of Scots and the King of Ireland held against King Arthur’s

[242-169]knights, and there began a great medley. So came in Sir Tristram and did

[242-170]marvellous deeds of arms, for there he smote down many knights. And ever he was

[242-171]afore King Arthur with that shield. And when King Arthur saw that shield he

[242-172]marvelled greatly in what intent it was made; but Queen Guenever deemed as it

[242-173]was, wherefore she was heavy. Then was there a damosel of Queen Morgan in a

[242-174]chamber by King Arthur, and when she heard King Arthur speak of that shield,

[242-175]then she spake openly unto King Arthur. Sir King, wit ye well this shield was

[242-176]ordained for you, to warn you of your shame and dishonour, and that longeth to

[242-177]you and your queen. And then anon that damosel picked her away privily, that no

[242-178]man wist where she was become. Then was King Arthur sad and wroth, and asked

[242-179]from whence came that damosel. There was not one that knew her nor wist where

[242-180]she was become. Then Queen Guenever called to her Sir Ector de Maris, and there

[242-181]she made her complaint to him, and said: I wot well this shield was made by

[242-182]Morgan le Fay in despite of me and of Sir Launcelot, wherefore I dread me sore

[242-183]lest I should be destroyed. And ever the king beheld Sir Tristram, that did so

[242-184]marvellous deeds of arms that he wondered sore what knight he might be, and

[242-185]well he wist it was not Sir Launcelot. And it was told him that Sir Tristram

[242-186]was in Petit Britain with Isoud la Blanche Mains, for he deemed, an he had been

[242-187]in the realm of Logris, Sir Launcelot or some of his fellows that were in the

[242-188]quest of Sir Tristram that they should have found him or that time. So King

[242-189]Arthur had marvel what knight he might be. And ever Sir Arthur’s eye was

[242-190]on that shield. All that espied the queen, and that made her sore afeard.

[242-191]Then ever Sir Tristram smote down knights wonderly to behold, what upon the

[242-192]right hand and upon the left hand, that unnethe no knight might withstand him.

[242-193]And the King of Scots and the King of Ireland began to withdraw them. When

[242-194]Arthur espied that, he thought that that knight with the strange shield should

[242-195]not escape him. Then he called unto him Sir Uwaine le Blanche Mains, and bade

[242-196]him arm him and make him ready. So anon King Arthur and Sir Uwaine dressed them

[242-197]before Sir Tristram, and required him to tell them where he had that shield.

[242-198]Sir, he said, I had it of Queen Morgan le Fay, sister unto King Arthur.
