[215-001]Questing Beast, and smote down [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]] and Sir Lamorak with one

[215-002]spear.

[215-003]And this meanwhile there came [[sir-palomides|Sir Palomides]], the good knight, following the

[215-004]Questing Beast that had in shape a head like a serpent’s head, and a body

[215-005]like a leopard, buttocks like a lion, and footed like an hart; and in his body

[215-006]there was such a noise as it had been the noise of thirty couple of hounds

[215-007]questing, and such a noise that beast made wheresomever he went; and this beast

[215-008]ever more Sir Palomides followed, for it was called his quest. And right so as

[215-009]he followed this beast it came by Sir Tristram, and soon after came Palomides.

[215-010]And to brief this matter he smote down Sir Tristram and Sir Lamorak both with

[215-011]one spear; and so he departed after the beast Galtisant, that was called the

[215-012]Questing Beast; wherefore these two knights were passing wroth that Sir

[215-013]Palomides would not [[battle|fight]] on foot with them. Here men may understand that be of

[215-014][[honour|worship]], that he was never formed that all times might stand, but sometime he

[215-015]was put to the worse by mal-fortune; and at sometime the worse knight put the

[215-016]better knight to a rebuke.

[215-017]Then Sir Tristram and Sir Lamorak gat Sir Kehydius upon a shield betwixt them

[215-018]both, and led him to a forester’s lodge, and there they gave him in

[215-019]charge to keep him well, and with him they abode three days. Then the two

[215-020]knights took their horses and at the cross they departed. And then said Sir

[215-021]Tristram to Sir Lamorak: I require you if ye hap to meet with Sir Palomides,

[215-022]say him that he shall find me at the same well where I met him, and there I,

[215-023]Sir Tristram, shall prove whether he be better knight than I. And so either

[215-024]departed from other a sundry way, and Sir Tristram rode nigh thereas was Sir

[215-025]Kehydius; and Sir Lamorak rode until he came to a chapel, and there he put his

[215-026]horse unto pasture. And anon there came Sir Meliagaunce, that was King

[215-027]Bagdemagus’ son, and he there put his horse to pasture, and was not ware

[215-028]of Sir Lamorak; and then this knight Sir Meliagaunce made his moan of the love

[215-029]that he had to [[queen-guenever|Queen Guenever]], and there he made a woful complaint. All this

[215-030]heard Sir Lamorak, and on the morn Sir Lamorak took his horse and rode unto the

[215-031]forest, and there he met with two knights hoving under the wood-shaw. Fair

[215-032]knights, said Sir Lamorak, what do ye hoving here and watching? and if ye be

[215-033]knights-errant that will joust, lo I am ready. Nay, sir knight, they said, not

[215-034]so, we abide not here to joust with you, but we lie here in await of a knight

[215-035]that slew our brother. What knight was that, said Sir Lamorak, that you would

[215-036]fain meet withal? Sir, they said, it is [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]] that slew our brother,

[215-037]and if ever we may meet with him he shall not escape, but we shall slay him. Ye

[215-038]take upon you a great charge, said Sir Lamorak, for Sir Launcelot is a noble

[215-039]proved knight. As for that we doubt not, for there nis none of us but we are

[215-040]good enough for him. I will not believe that, said Sir Lamorak, for I heard

[215-041]never yet of no knight the days of my life but Sir Launcelot was too big for

[215-042]him.
