[165-001]had ordained poison for to have poisoned [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]].

[165-002]Then certain of them would have slain the child, because they would have been

[165-003]lords of the country of Liones. But then through the fair speech of the

[165-004]gentlewoman, and by the means that she made, the most part of the barons would

[165-005]not assent thereto. And then they let carry home the dead queen, and much dole

[165-006]was made for her.

[165-007]Then this meanwhile [[merlin|Merlin]] delivered King Meliodas out of prison on the morn

[165-008]after his queen was dead. And so when the king was come home the most part of

[165-009]the barons made great joy. But the sorrow that the king made for his queen that

[165-010]might no tongue tell. So then the king let inter her richly, and after he let

[165-011]christen his child as his wife had commanded afore her death. And then he let

[165-012]call him Tristram, the sorrowful born child. Then [[king-arthur|the King]] Meliodas endured

[165-013]seven years without a wife, and all this time Tristram was nourished well. Then

[165-014]it befell that King Meliodas wedded King Howell’s daughter of Brittany,

[165-015]and anon she had children of King Meliodas: then was she heavy and wroth that

[165-016]her children should not rejoice the country of Liones, wherefore this queen

[165-017]ordained for to poison young Tristram. So she let poison be put in a piece of

[165-018]silver in the chamber whereas Tristram and her children were together, unto

[165-019]that intent that when Tristram were thirsty he should drink that drink. And so

[165-020]it fell upon a day, the queen’s son, as he was in that chamber, espied

[165-021]the piece with poison, and he weened it had been good drink, and because the

[165-022]child was thirsty he took the piece with poison and drank freely; and

[165-023]therewithal suddenly the child brast and was dead.

[165-024]When the queen of Meliodas wist of the death of her son, wit ye well that she

[165-025]was heavy. But yet the king understood nothing of her treason. Notwithstanding

[165-026]the queen would not leave this, but eft she let ordain more poison, and put it

[165-027]in a piece. And by fortune King Meliodas, her husband, found the piece with

[165-028]wine where was the poison, and he that was much thirsty took the piece for to

[165-029]drink thereout. And as he would have drunken thereof the queen espied him, and

[165-030]then she ran unto him, and pulled the piece from him suddenly. The king

[165-031]marvelled why she did so, and remembered him how her son was suddenly slain

[165-032]with poison. And then he took her by the hand, and said: Thou false traitress,

[165-033]thou shalt tell me what manner of drink this is, or else I shall slay thee. And

[165-034]therewith he pulled out his sword, and sware a great oath that he should slay

[165-035]her but if she told him truth. Ah! mercy, my lord, said she, and I shall tell

[165-036]you all. And then she told him why she would have slain Tristram, because her

[165-037]children should rejoice his land. Well, said King Meliodas, and therefore shall

[165-038]ye have the law. And so she was condemned by the assent of the barons to be

[165-039]burnt; and then was there made a great fire, and right as she was at the fire

[165-040]to take her execution, young Tristram kneeled afore King Meliodas, and besought

[165-041]him to give him a boon. I will well, said the king again. Then said young

[165-042]Tristram, Give me the life of thy queen, my stepmother. That is unrightfully

[165-043]asked, said King Meliodas, for thou ought of right to hate her, for she would

[165-044]have slain thee with that poison an she might have had her will; and for thy

[165-045]sake most is my cause that she should die.

[165-046]Sir, said Tristram, as for that, I beseech you of your mercy that you will

[165-047]forgive it her, and as for my part, God forgive it her, and I do; and so much

[165-048]it liked your highness to grant me my boon, for God’s love I require you

[165-049]hold your promise. Sithen it is so, said the king, I will that ye have her

[165-050]life. Then, said the king, I give her to you, and go ye to the fire and take

[165-051]her, and do with her what ye will. So Sir Tristram went to the fire, and by the

[165-052]commandment of the king delivered her from the death. But after that King

[165-053]Meliodas would never have ado with her, as at bed and board. But by the good

[165-054]means of young Tristram he made the king and her accorded. But then the king

[165-055]would not suffer young Tristram to abide no longer in his court.
