[206-001]Dagonet the king’s fool, and of the rebuke that he had of the

[206-002]damosel.

[206-003]Then [[sir-kay|Sir Kay]] ordained Sir Dagonet, [[king-arthur|King Arthur]]’s fool, to follow after La

[206-004]Cote Male Taile; and there Sir Kay ordained that Sir Dagonet was horsed and

[206-005]armed, and bade him follow La Cote Male Taile and proffer him to joust, and so

[206-006]he did; and when he saw La Cote Male Taile, he cried and bade him make him

[206-007]ready to joust. So Sir La Cote Male Taile smote Sir Dagonet over his

[206-008]horse’s croup. Then the damosel mocked La Cote Male Taile, and said: Fie

[206-009]for shame! now art thou shamed in Arthur’s court, when they send a fool

[206-010]to have ado with thee, and specially at thy first [[tournament|jousts]]; thus she rode long,

[206-011]and chid. And within a while there came Sir Bleoberis, the good knight, and

[206-012]there he jousted with La Cote Male Taile, and there Sir Bleoberis smote him so

[206-013]sore, that horse and all fell to the earth. Then La Cote Male Taile arose up

[206-014]lightly, and dressed his shield, and drew his sword, and would have done battle

[206-015]to the utterance, for he was wood wroth. Not so, said Sir Bleoberis de Ganis,

[206-016]as at this time I will not [[battle|fight]] upon foot. Then the damosel Maledisant rebuked

[206-017]him in the foulest manner, and bade him: Turn again, coward. Ah, damosel, he

[206-018]said, I pray you of mercy to missay me no more, my grief is enough though ye

[206-019]give me no more; I call myself never the worse knight when a mare’s son

[206-020]faileth me, and also I count me never the worse knight for a fall of Sir

[206-021]Bleoberis.

[206-022]So thus he rode with her two days; and by fortune there came [[sir-palomides|Sir Palomides]] and

[206-023]encountered with him, and he in the same wise served him as did Bleoberis

[206-024]to-forehand. What dost thou here in my fellowship? said the damosel Maledisant,

[206-025]thou canst not sit no knight, nor withstand him one buffet, but if it were Sir

[206-026]Dagonet. Ah, fair damosel, I am not the worse to take a fall of Sir Palomides,

[206-027]and yet great disworship have I none, for neither Bleoberis nor yet Palomides

[206-028]would not fight with me on foot. As for that, said the damosel, wit thou well

[206-029]they have disdain and scorn to light off their horses to fight with such a lewd

[206-030]knight as thou art. So in the meanwhile there came [[mordred|Sir Mordred]], Sir

[206-031][[sir-gawaine|Gawaine]]’s brother, and so he fell in the fellowship with the damosel

[206-032]Maledisant. And then they came afore the Castle Orgulous, and there was such a

[206-033]custom that there might no knight come by that castle but either he must joust

[206-034]or be prisoner, or at the least to lose his horse and his harness. And there

[206-035]came out two knights against them, and Sir Mordred jousted with the foremost,

[206-036]and that knight of the castle smote Sir Mordred down off his horse. And then La

[206-037]Cote Male Taile jousted with that other, and either of them smote other down,

[206-038]horse and all, to the earth. And when they avoided their horses, then either of

[206-039]them took other’s horses. And then La Cote Male Taile rode unto that

[206-040]knight that smote down Sir Mordred, and jousted with him. And there Sir La Cote

[206-041]Male Taile hurt and wounded him passing sore, and put him from his horse as he

[206-042]had been dead. So he turned unto him that met him afore, and he took the flight

[206-043]towards the castle, and Sir La Cote Male Taile rode after him into the Castle

[206-044]Orgulous, and there La Cote Male Taile slew him.
