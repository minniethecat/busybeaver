# Sir Gingalin

Sir Gingalin is the son of [[Sir Gawaine]] and a knight who appears in the latter part of the chronicle. His brief but vivid appearance at the Castle of Tintagil places him in direct confrontation with [[Sir Tristram]], in an encounter witnessed by [[King Mark]].

## Son of Sir Gawaine

Gingalin is identified in the chronicle first and foremost by his lineage: "Gingalin, Sir Gawaine's son" (220-009). As the son of one of the most prominent [[Knights of the Round Table]], Gingalin belonged to the Orkney kinship network that dominated much of the Arthurian chivalric landscape. His father Sir Gawaine was King Arthur's nephew and one of the foremost knights of the realm, though Gingalin himself appears only in a single episode.

## Encounter at Tintagil

Gingalin's sole recorded deed occurred at the gate of the Castle of Tintagil. He encountered Sir Tristram, who was riding forth from the castle armed only with a sword. Gingalin put his spear in its rest and charged — but Tristram, even without a lance, gave him such a buffet upon the helm that he fell from his saddle, and Tristram's sword carved asunder his horse's neck (220-009).

The defeat was total. Gingalin lay wounded while Sir Tristram rode away into the forest, and the entire episode was observed by King Mark, who had been watching from concealment (220-016).

## Aftermath

King Mark sent a squire to attend the injured Gingalin and commanded him to come before the king. When Mark learned the identity of the wounded knight and welcomed him, giving him a horse, he questioned Gingalin about the knight who had struck him down. Gingalin could only answer that he knew not who the knight was, but noted tellingly: "Well I wot that he sigheth and maketh great dole" (220-017) — a detail that spoke to Sir Tristram's sorrowful state and confirmed to Mark that the mysterious knight was indeed Tristram.
