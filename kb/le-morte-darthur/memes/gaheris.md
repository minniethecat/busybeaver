# Gaheris

**Gaheris** is one of the Orkney brothers in Thomas Malory's *Le Morte d'[[king-arthur|Arthur]]* — the third son of [[king-lot|King Lot]] and Morgawse, and brother to [[sir-gawaine|Sir Gawaine]], Agravaine, and [[sir-gareth|Sir Gareth]]. Though less prominent than Gawaine or Gareth in the narrative, Gaheris appears repeatedly as a companion in arms, sharing both his brothers' triumphs and their defeats.

## The Orkney Brother

Gaheris belongs to the house of Orkney through his mother Morgawse, wife of King Lot (022-005). He enters the Arthurian court alongside his brothers; the chronicles count "Gawaine, Agravaine, Gaheris, and Gareth" among the knights present at major assemblies (042-003). The four brothers are frequently named together, their collective identity as Lot's sons shaping the politics of [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s court. When [[sir-gawaine|Gawaine]] and his kin withdraw from court or act in concert, Gaheris stands among them (154-003).

## Adventures Alongside Gawaine

Gaheris rides most often in the company of his eldest brother [[sir-gawaine|Gawaine]]. When Gawaine sets out in pursuit of a knight who has offended the court, Gaheris follows beside him: "Gawaine and Gaheris rode more than a pace after the knight" (057-040). In battle, Gaheris proves himself a capable fighter — when Gawaine faces a powerful opponent, "Gaheris smote him again sore" (058-012), fighting side by side with his brother.

Their shared adventures do not always end in victory. Gaheris and Gawaine are both overcome in one engagement, taken prisoner together, and "made great dole" at their capture (058-017–018). Gaheris's recognition of disaster is immediate and honest: when a knight falls "by misadventure," Gaheris laments, "that is foully and shamefully done" (057-062), displaying a moral sensitivity that marks him among the Orkney sons.

## Captivity and Deliverance by Launcelot

Gaheris is captured by [[sir-turquine|Sir Turquine]], a formidable knight who takes many prisoners of the Round Table. When [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]] defeats Turquine, he "was ware that it was Sir Gaheris, Gawaine's brother, a knight of the Table Round" among the captives (115-062). Launcelot bids Gaheris to "deliver all the prisoners," and Gaheris takes up the keys, throwing open the prison gates and freeing "many knights and many ladies" (117-029–033).

In a gesture of humility, Gaheris refuses credit for the liberation: "Not so, it was Launcelot that delivered us" (117-034). This moment captures Gaheris's character — loyal, competent, but without the proud ambition that drives his brother Gawaine or the dark resentment that consumes Agravaine.
