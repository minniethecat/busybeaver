[195-001]Isoud home, and of the debate of [[king-mark|King Mark]] and [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]].

[195-002]And so Sir Palamides departed with great heaviness. And Sir Tristram took the

[195-003]queen and brought her again to King Mark, and then was there made great joy of

[195-004]her home-coming. Who was cherished but Sir Tristram! Then Sir Tristram let

[195-005]fetch Sir Lambegus, his knight, from the forester’s house, and it was

[195-006]long or he was whole, but at the last he was well recovered. Thus they lived

[195-007]with joy and play a long while. But ever Sir Andred, that was nigh cousin to

[195-008]Sir Tristram, lay in a watch to wait betwixt Sir Tristram and La Beale Isoud,

[195-009]for to take them and slander them. So upon a day Sir Tristram talked with La

[195-010]Beale Isoud in a window, and that espied Sir Andred, and told it to [[king-arthur|the King]].

[195-011]Then King Mark took a sword in his hand and came to Sir Tristram, and called

[195-012]him false traitor, and would have stricken him. But Sir Tristram was nigh him,

[195-013]and ran under his sword, and took it out of his hand. And then the King cried:

[195-014]Where are my knights and my men? I charge you slay this traitor. But at that

[195-015]time there was not one would move for his words. When Sir Tristram saw that

[195-016]there was not one would be against him, he shook the sword to the king, and

[195-017]made countenance as though he would have stricken him. And then King Mark fled,

[195-018]and Sir Tristram followed him, and smote upon him five or six strokes flatling

[195-019]on the neck, that he made him to fall upon the nose. And then Sir Tristram yede

[195-020]his way and armed him, and took his horse and his man, and so he rode into that

[195-021]forest.

[195-022]And there upon a day Sir Tristram met with two brethren that were knights with

[195-023]King Mark, and there he struck off the head of the one, and wounded the other

[195-024]to the death; and he made him to bear his brother’s head in his helm unto

[195-025]the king, and thirty more there he wounded. And when that knight came before

[195-026]the king to say his message, he there died afore the king and the queen. Then

[195-027]King Mark called his council unto him, and asked advice of his barons what was

[195-028]best to do with Sir Tristram. Sir, said the barons, in especial Sir Dinas, the

[195-029]Seneschal, Sir, we will give you counsel for to send for Sir Tristram, for we

[195-030]will that ye wit many men will hold with Sir Tristram an he were hard bestead.

[195-031]And sir, said Sir Dinas, ye shall understand that Sir Tristram is called

[195-032]peerless and makeless of any Christian knight, and of his might and [[courage|hardiness]]

[195-033]we knew none so good a knight, but if it be [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot du Lake]]. And if he

[195-034]depart from your court and go to King Arthur’s court, wit ye well he will

[195-035]get him such friends there that he will not set by your malice. And therefore,

[195-036]sir, I counsel you to take him to your grace. I will well, said the king, that

[195-037]he be sent for, that we may be friends. Then the barons sent for Sir Tristram

[195-038]under a safe conduct. And so when Sir Tristram came to the king he was welcome,

[195-039]and no rehearsal was made, and there was game and play. And then the king and

[195-040]the queen went a-hunting, and Sir Tristram.
