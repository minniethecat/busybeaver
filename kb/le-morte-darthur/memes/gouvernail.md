# Gouvernail

Gouvernail was the loyal servant, governor, and companion of Sir Tristram de Liones. Appointed to guide Tristram from his youth, Gouvernail accompanied the noble knight through his education in France, his greatest battles, and his most perilous adventures.

## Governor of Young Tristram

When Tristram was a young boy, a gentleman well learned and taught was ordained to govern him. His name was Gouvernail, and he was sent with young Tristram into France to learn the language, nurture, and deeds of arms (166-001). Tristram remained in France more than seven years under Gouvernail's care, and when he could well speak the language and had learned all that he might in that country, he came home to his father, King Meliodas (166-004). It was during this time that Tristram also learned to be a harper passing all others.

## The Battle with Sir Marhaus

When Tristram fought the knight of Ireland, Sir Marhaus, Gouvernail attended him with steadfast loyalty. Tristram commanded Gouvernail to bring his horse to the land and dress his harness at all manner of rights (169-006). As Tristram prepared for battle, he asked Gouvernail where the enemy knight was. Gouvernail pointed him out, hoving under the shadow of his ships on horseback with his spear in hand (169-009).

Before the combat, Tristram gave Gouvernail solemn instructions: he commanded him to go back to the vessel and commend him to his uncle King Mark. If Tristram were slain, Gouvernail was to ask the king to inter his body as seemed best. Tristram also charged Gouvernail upon his life not to come near the island until he saw his master overcome or slain, or else victorious (169-014).

## Guardian in Wounds and Exile

After the battle with Marhaus, Tristram was sore wounded. Gouvernail came with his vessel to retrieve his master, and the king and his barons came with procession to receive him (171-012). When Tristram later needed to seek healing in Ireland, Gouvernail sailed with him in a fair vessel well victualled. Tristram took his harp, and by good fortune they arrived in Ireland fast by the castle where the king and queen dwelled (171-031).

While Tristram bathed in Ireland, Gouvernail and Hebes attended upon him. It was during this attendance that the queen and La Beale Isoud discovered Tristram's broken sword, revealing his identity as the slayer of Sir Marhaus (174-006).

## Counsellor in Arms

Gouvernail did not merely serve — he also counselled. When Tristram sought to fight two proved knights of Arthur's court, Gouvernail advised against it, saying they were two proved knights and counseled his master not to have ado with them (171-012). Tristram, eager to increase his worship, chose to fight regardless. Gouvernail accepted his master's decision, saying simply, "Do as ye list."

Later, Gouvernail served as Tristram's eyes and ears at tournaments, telling him of King Anguish of Ireland's arrival and his great distress (169-020).
