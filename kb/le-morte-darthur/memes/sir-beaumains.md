# Sir Beaumains

Sir Beaumains, which means "Fair-hands," was the name given to [[Sir Gareth]] by [[Sir Kay]] when the young knight first arrived at [[King Arthur]]'s court. Arriving in disguise and refusing to reveal his true identity, Gareth served a year in the kitchens under this mocking epithet before proving himself one of the worthiest knights of the [[Round Table]]. The tale of Beaumains is among the most complete coming-of-age narratives in [[Le Morte d'Arthur]], tracing the journey of an unknown youth to a proven champion.

## Arrival at Court

Gareth came to King Arthur's court at [[Camelot]] on the feast of [[Feast of Pentecost|Pentecost]], presenting himself not as a nobleman but as a supplicant seeking three gifts. He asked only for sustenance for a year and the right to a quest thereafter. It was Sir Kay, the seneschal, who mocked the unknown youth and gave him the name Beaumains — "Fair-hands" — a sarcastic comment on the young man's hands, which Kay claimed were too fair for a labourer but fit only for a ladle-washer (128-050). Kay set Beaumains to work in the kitchens, where the youth endured the scorn of the court in silence.

## The Quest of the Damosel

After his year of service, a damosel came to court seeking a champion to rescue her lady from a besieging knight. Beaumains claimed the quest, revealing that he had already been knighted in secret by [[Sir Launcelot]] during his year of waiting. The damosel was furious that Arthur had sent her a kitchen boy, and throughout their journey she mocked Beaumains relentlessly. "What art thou but a lusk and a ladle-washer?" she taunted him as they rode (132-028). Beaumains endured her scorn with patience, replying only, "Say to me what ye will" (132-029).

## The Proving

Despite the damosel's contempt, Beaumains proved himself at every encounter. When she demanded he turn back, he declared, "I will not turn again" (133-006). He fought and defeated knight after knight along the road — the Black Knight, the Green Knight, the Red Knight of the Red Launds, and the perilous Sir Persant of Inde. In combat Beaumains fought with terrifying power: he threw his shield from him and struck with both hands, caring not whether he lived or died (131-023). When asked why he fought so fiercely, he answered simply, "It doth me good to feel your might" (131-030).

## The True Name

When Beaumains finally encountered Sir Launcelot in the field, the greatest knight of the Round Table tested the unknown warrior and recognised his quality. Launcelot asked him whether he stood as a proved knight, and Beaumains replied in the affirmative, asking Launcelot to give him the order of knighthood formally (132-006). Launcelot instead revealed that he had already knighted him, and at last Beaumains's true identity was made known — he was Gareth, youngest son of King Lot and [[Morgawse]], and brother to [[Sir Gawaine]]. His year of humility and his patient endurance of mockery had transformed a prince into a knight of proven worth.
