[119-001]and made a castle free.

[119-002]Anon withal came there upon him two great giants, well armed all save the

[119-003]heads, with two horrible clubs in their hands. [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]] put his shield

[119-004]afore him and put the stroke away of the one giant, and with his sword he clave

[119-005]his head asunder. When his fellow saw that, he ran away as he were wood, for

[119-006]fear of the horrible strokes, and Launcelot after him with all his might, and

[119-007]smote him on the shoulder, and clave him to the navel. Then Sir Launcelot went

[119-008]into the hall, and there came afore him three score ladies and damosels, and

[119-009]all kneeled unto him, and thanked God and him of their deliverance; For sir,

[119-010]said they, the most party of us have been here this seven year their prisoners,

[119-011]and we have worked all manner of silk works for our meat, and we are all great

[119-012]gentlewomen born; and blessed be the time, knight, that ever thou be born, for

[119-013]thou hast done the most [[honour|worship]] that ever did knight in this world, that will

[119-014]we bear record, and we all pray you to tell us your name, that we may tell our

[119-015]friends who delivered us out of prison. Fair damosel, he said, my name is Sir

[119-016]Launcelot du Lake. Ah, sir, said they all, well mayest thou be he, for else

[119-017]save yourself, as we deemed, there might never knight have the better of these

[119-018]two giants; for many fair knights have assayed it, and here have ended, and

[119-019]many times have we wished after you, and these two giants dread never knight

[119-020]but you. Now may ye say, said Sir Launcelot, unto your friends how and who hath

[119-021]delivered you, and greet them all from me, and if that I come in any of your

[119-022]marches, show me such cheer as ye have cause, and what treasure that there in

[119-023]this castle is I give it you for a reward for your grievance, and the lord that

[119-024]is owner of this castle I would he received it as is right. Fair sir, said

[119-025]they, the name of this castle is Tintagil, and a duke ought it sometime that

[119-026]had wedded fair Igraine, and after wedded her Uther Pendragon, and gat on her

[119-027][[king-arthur|Arthur]]. Well, said Sir Launcelot, I understand to whom this castle longeth; and

[119-028]so he departed from them, and betaught them unto God.

[119-029]And then he mounted upon his horse, and rode into many strange and wild

[119-030]countries, and through many waters and valleys, and evil was he lodged. And at

[119-031]the last by fortune him happened, against a night, to come to a fair

[119-032]courtelage, and therein he found an old gentlewoman that lodged him with good

[119-033]will, and there he had good cheer for him and his horse. And when time was, his

[119-034]host brought him into a fair garret, over the gate, to his bed. There Sir

[119-035]Launcelot unarmed him, and set his harness by him, and went to bed, and anon he

[119-036]fell asleep. So, soon after, there came one on horseback, and knocked at the

[119-037]gate in great haste, and when Sir Launcelot heard this, he arose up and looked

[119-038]out at the window, and saw by the moonlight three knights came riding after

[119-039]that one man, and all three lashed on him at once with swords, and that one

[119-040]knight turned on them knightly again, and defended him. Truly, said Sir

[119-041]Launcelot, yonder one knight shall I help, for it were shame for me to see

[119-042]three knights on one, and if he be slain I am partner of his death; and

[119-043]therewith he took his harness, and went out at a window by a sheet down to the

[119-044]four knights, and then Sir Launcelot said on high, Turn you knights unto me,

[119-045]and leave your fighting with that knight. And then they all three left [[sir-kay|Sir Kay]],

[119-046]and turned unto Sir Launcelot, and there began great battle, for they alighted

[119-047]all three, and struck many great strokes at Sir Launcelot, and assailed him on

[119-048]every side. Then Sir Kay dressed him for to have holpen Sir Launcelot. Nay,

[119-049]sir, said he, I will none of your help; therefore as ye will have my help, let

[119-050]me alone with them. Sir Kay, for the pleasure of the knight, suffered him for

[119-051]to do his will, and so stood aside. And then anon within six strokes, Sir

[119-052]Launcelot had stricken them to the earth.

[119-053]And then they all three cried: Sir knight, we yield us unto you as a man of

[119-054]might makeless. As to that, said Sir Launcelot, I will not take your yielding

[119-055]unto me. But so that ye will yield you unto Sir Kay the Seneschal, on that

[119-056]covenant I will save your lives, and else not. Fair knight, said they, that

[119-057]were we loath to do; for as for Sir Kay, we chased him hither, and had overcome

[119-058]him had not ye been, therefore to yield us unto him it were no reason. Well, as

[119-059]to that, said Launcelot, advise you well, for ye may choose whether ye will die

[119-060]or live, for an ye be yolden it shall be unto Sir Kay. Fair knight, then they

[119-061]said, in saving of our lives we will do as thou commandest us. Then shall ye,

[119-062]said Sir Launcelot, on Whitsunday next coming, go unto the court of King

[119-063]Arthur, and there shall ye yield you unto [[queen-guenever|Queen Guenever]], and put you all three

[119-064]in her grace and mercy, and say that Sir Kay sent you thither to be her

[119-065]prisoners. Sir, they said, it shall be done by the faith of our bodies, an we

[119-066]be living, and there they swore every knight upon his sword. And so Sir

[119-067]Launcelot suffered them so to depart. And then Sir Launcelot knocked at the

[119-068]gate with the pommel of his sword, and with that came his host, and in they

[119-069]entered Sir Kay and he. Sir, said his host, I weened ye had been in your bed.

[119-070]So I was, said Sir Launcelot, but I rose and leapt out at my window for to help

[119-071]an old fellow of mine. And so when they came nigh the light, Sir Kay knew well

[119-072]that it was Sir Launcelot, and therewith he kneeled down and thanked him of all

[119-073]his kindness that he had holpen him twice from the death. Sir, he said, I have

[119-074]nothing done but that me ought for to do, and ye are welcome, and here shall ye

[119-075]repose you and take your rest.

[119-076]So when Sir Kay was unarmed, he asked after meat; so there was meat fetched

[119-077]him, and he ate strongly. And when he had supped they went to their beds and

[119-078]were lodged together in one bed. On the morn Sir Launcelot arose early, and

[119-079]left Sir Kay sleeping, and Sir Launcelot took Sir Kay’s armour and his

[119-080]shield, and armed him, and so he went to the stable, and took his horse, and

[119-081]took his leave of his host, and so he departed. Then soon after arose Sir Kay

[119-082]and missed Sir Launcelot. And then he espied that he had his armour and his

[119-083]horse. Now by my faith I know well that he will grieve some of the court of

[119-084]King Arthur; for on him knights will be bold, and deem that it is I, and that

[119-085]will beguile them. And because of his armour and shield I am sure I shall ride

[119-086]in peace. And then soon after departed Sir Kay and thanked his host.
