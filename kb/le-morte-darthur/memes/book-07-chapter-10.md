[137-001]Knight, jousted and fought against [[sir-gareth|Beaumains]], and how Beaumains overcame

[137-002]him.

[137-003]So within a while they saw a tower as white as any snow, well matchecold all

[137-004]about, and double dyked. And over the tower gate there hung a fifty shields of

[137-005]divers colours, and under that tower there was a fair meadow. And therein were

[137-006]many knights and squires to behold, scaffolds and pavilions; for there upon the

[137-007]morn should be a great tournament: and the lord of the tower was in his castle

[137-008]and looked out at a window, and saw a damosel, a dwarf, and a knight armed at

[137-009]all points. So God me help, said the lord, with that knight will I joust, for I

[137-010]see that he is a knight-errant. And so he armed him and horsed him hastily. And

[137-011]when he was on horseback with his shield and his spear, it was all red, both

[137-012]his horse and his harness, and all that to him longeth. And when that he came

[137-013]nigh him he weened it had been his brother the Black Knight; and then he cried

[137-014]aloud, Brother, what do ye in these marches? Nay, nay, said the damosel, it is

[137-015]not he; this is but a kitchen knave that was brought up for alms in King

[137-016][[king-arthur|Arthur]]’s court. Nevertheless, said the Red Knight, I will speak with him

[137-017]or he depart. Ah, said the damosel, this knave hath killed thy brother, and Sir

[137-018][[sir-kay|Kay]] named him Beaumains, and this horse and this harness was thy

[137-019]brother’s, the Black Knight. Also I saw thy brother the Green Knight

[137-020]overcome of his hands. Now may ye be revenged upon him, for I may never be quit

[137-021]of him.

[137-022]With this either knights departed in sunder, and they came together with all

[137-023]their might, and either of their horses fell to the earth, and they avoided

[137-024]their horses, and put their shields afore them and drew their swords, and

[137-025]either gave other sad strokes, now here, now there, rasing, tracing, foining,

[137-026]and hurling like two boars, the space of two hours. And then she cried on high

[137-027]to the Red Knight, Alas, thou noble Red Knight, think what [[honour|worship]] hath

[137-028]followed thee, let never a kitchen knave endure thee so long as he doth. Then

[137-029]the Red Knight waxed wroth and doubled his strokes, and hurt Beaumains wonderly

[137-030]sore, that the blood ran down to the ground, that it was [[marvel|wonder]] to see that

[137-031]strong battle. Yet at the last Sir Beaumains struck him to the earth, and as he

[137-032]would have slain the Red Knight, he cried mercy, saying, Noble knight, slay me

[137-033]not, and I shall yield me to thee with fifty knights with me that be at my

[137-034]commandment. And I forgive thee all the despite that thou hast done to me, and

[137-035]the death of my brother the Black Knight. All this availeth not, said

[137-036]Beaumains, but if my damosel pray me to save thy life. And therewith he made

[137-037]semblant to strike off his head. Let be, thou Beaumains, slay him not, for he

[137-038]is a noble knight, and not so hardy, upon thine head, but thou save him.

[137-039]Then Beaumains bade the Red Knight, Stand up, and thank the damosel now of thy

[137-040]life. Then the Red Knight prayed him to see his castle, and to be there all

[137-041]night. So the damosel then granted him, and there they had merry cheer. But

[137-042]always the damosel spake many foul words unto Beaumains, whereof the Red Knight

[137-043]had great marvel; and all that night the Red Knight made three score knights to

[137-044]watch Beaumains, that he should have no shame nor villainy. And upon the morn

[137-045]they heard mass and dined, and the Red Knight came before Beaumains with his

[137-046]three score knights, and there he proffered him his homage and fealty at all

[137-047]times, he and his knights to do him service. I thank you, said Beaumains, but

[137-048]this ye shall grant me: when I call upon you, to come afore my lord King

[137-049]Arthur, and yield you unto him to be his knights. Sir, said the Red Knight, I

[137-050]will be ready, and my fellowship, at your summons. So Sir Beaumains departed

[137-051]and the damosel, and ever she rode chiding him in the foulest manner.
