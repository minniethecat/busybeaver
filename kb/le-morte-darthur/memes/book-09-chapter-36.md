[239-001]and Sir Dinadan were taken and put in prison.

[239-002]Now will we speak of Sir Lucan the butler, that by fortune he came riding to

[239-003]the same place thereas was [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]], and in he came in none other intent

[239-004]but to ask harbour. Then the porter asked what was his name. Tell your lord

[239-005]that my name is Sir Lucan, the butler, a Knight of the [[round-table|Round Table]]. So the

[239-006]porter went unto Sir Darras, lord of the place, and told him who was there to

[239-007]ask harbour. Nay, nay, said Sir Daname, that was nephew to Sir Darras, say him

[239-008]that he shall not be lodged here, but let him wit that I, Sir Daname, will meet

[239-009]with him anon, and bid him make him ready. So Sir Daname came forth on

[239-010]horseback, and there they met together with spears, and Sir Lucan smote down

[239-011]Sir Daname over his horse’s croup, and then he fled into that place, and

[239-012]Sir Lucan rode after him, and asked after him many times.

[239-013]Then Sir Dinadan said to Sir Tristram: It is shame to see the lord’s

[239-014]cousin of this place defoiled. Abide, said Sir Tristram, and I shall redress

[239-015]it. And in the meanwhile Sir Dinadan was on horseback, and he jousted with

[239-016]Lucan the butler, and there Sir Lucan smote Dinadan through the thick of the

[239-017]thigh, and so he rode his way; and Sir Tristram was wroth that Sir Dinadan was

[239-018]hurt, and followed after, and thought to avenge him; and within a while he

[239-019]overtook Sir Lucan, and bade him turn; and so they met together so that Sir

[239-020]Tristram hurt Sir Lucan passing sore and gave him a fall. With that came Sir

[239-021]Uwaine, a gentle knight, and when he saw Sir Lucan so hurt he called Sir

[239-022]Tristram to joust with him. Fair knight, said Sir Tristram, tell me your name I

[239-023]require you. Sir knight, wit ye well my name is Sir Uwaine le Fise de Roy

[239-024]Ureine. Ah, said Sir Tristram, by my will I would not have ado with you at no

[239-025]time. Ye shall not so, said Sir Uwaine, but ye shall have ado with me. And then

[239-026]Sir Tristram saw none other bote, but rode against him, and overthrew Sir

[239-027]Uwaine and hurt him in the side, and so he departed unto his lodging again. And

[239-028]when Sir Dinadan understood that Sir Tristram had hurt Sir Lucan he would have

[239-029]ridden after Sir Lucan for to have slain him, but Sir Tristram would not suffer

[239-030]him. Then Sir Uwaine let ordain an horse litter, and brought Sir Lucan to the

[239-031]abbey of Ganis, and the castle thereby hight the Castle of Ganis, of the which

[239-032]Sir Bleoberis was lord. And at that castle [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]] promised all his

[239-033]fellows to meet in the quest of Sir Tristram.

[239-034]So when Sir Tristram was come to his lodging there came a damosel that told Sir

[239-035]Darras that three of his sons were slain at that tournament, and two grievously

[239-036]wounded that they were never like to help themself. And all this was done by a

[239-037]noble knight that bare the black shield, and that was he that bare the prize.

[239-038]Then came there one and told Sir Darras that the same knight was within, him

[239-039]that bare the black shield. Then Sir Darras yede unto Sir Tristram’s

[239-040]chamber, and there he found his shield and showed it to the damosel. Ah sir,

[239-041]said the damosel, that same is he that slew your three sons. Then without any

[239-042]tarrying Sir Darras put Sir Tristram, and [[sir-palomides|Sir Palomides]], and Sir Dinadan,

[239-043]within a strong prison, and there Sir Tristram was like to have died of great

[239-044]sickness; and every day Sir Palomides would reprove Sir Tristram of old hate

[239-045]betwixt them. And ever Sir Tristram spake fair and said little. But when Sir

[239-046]Palomides saw the falling of sickness of Sir Tristram, then was he heavy for

[239-047]him, and comforted him in all the best wise he could. And as the French book

[239-048]saith, there came forty knights to Sir Darras that were of his own kin, and

[239-049]they would have slain Sir Tristram and his two fellows, but Sir Darras would

[239-050]not suffer that, but kept them in prison, and meat and drink they had. So Sir

[239-051]Tristram endured there great pain, for sickness had undertaken him, and that is

[239-052]the greatest pain a prisoner may have. For all the while a prisoner may have

[239-053]his health of body he may endure under the mercy of God and in hope of good

[239-054]deliverance; but when sickness toucheth a prisoner’s body, then may a

[239-055]prisoner say all wealth is him bereft, and then he hath cause to wail and to

[239-056]weep. Right so did Sir Tristram when sickness had undertaken him, for then he

[239-057]took such sorrow that he had almost slain himself.
