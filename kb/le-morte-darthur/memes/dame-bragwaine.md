# Dame Bragwaine

**Dame Bragwaine** is the devoted maiden and confidante of [[la-beale-isoud|La Beale Isoud]] in Thomas Malory's *Le Morte d'[[king-arthur|Arthur]]*. More than a servant, Bragwaine is the guardian of Isoud's secrets, the courier of her messages, and — through the accident of the love potion — one of the unwitting architects of the great romance between [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]] and Isoud.

## Keeper of the Love Drink

Bragwaine's most consequential moment occurs before she speaks a single line in the narrative. When Isoud is sent from Ireland to marry [[king-mark|King Mark]] of Cornwall, Isoud's mother entrusts to Bragwaine a drink of [[love]]: "The queen, Isoud's mother, gave to her and Dame Bragwaine, her gentlewoman, a drink" (187-017). The potion is intended for Isoud and King Mark on their wedding night — a charm to ensure [[love]] in a political [[marriage]].

But "by fortune the drink was given unto Dame Bragwaine, and unto Gouvernail" (187-022). The mistake is fateful: Tristram and Isoud drink instead, and "that drink was the beginning of the great [[love]] between Sir Tristram and La Beale Isoud that never departed all the days of their life" (187-027). Bragwaine, though blameless in the error, becomes the keeper of the secret — the one who knows that the great love of Tristram and Isoud began with an accident born of her own hands.

## Loyalty to Isoud

Bragwaine's [[fealty|loyalty]] to her lady is absolute, tested repeatedly by the dangers of the Cornish court. When enemies seek "to destroy Dame Bragwaine, that was maiden and lady unto La Beale Isoud," [[sir-palomides|Sir Palamides]] intervenes: "there he delivered her from death" (192-008, 192-011). Bragwaine survives these threats through the protection of knights who recognize her worth, but her survival is never guaranteed — she lives always in the shadow of the secret she keeps.

When Isoud and Tristram's love is discovered, Bragwaine becomes the essential courier between the separated lovers. "La Beale Isoud sent to him by her maiden Bragwaine as piteous letters as could be thought and made" (213-004). Bragwaine crosses hostile territory bearing these messages, her loyalty to Isoud overriding all concern for her own safety.

## Messenger and Guardian

Throughout the later books, Bragwaine's role deepens. She rides with Tristram and Isoud on their journey to Joyous Gard (213-010), accompanies Sir Dinas to court (219-008), and even serves as an intermediary with [[queen-guenever|Queen Guenever]] at Camelot (234-026). When Isoud despairs, believing Tristram lost or faithless, "ever she said unto Dame Bragwaine: Meseemeth I should have seen him" (223-026) — Bragwaine is the constant witness to Isoud's [[love]] and [[sorrow]].

In a remarkable encounter late in the narrative, Bragwaine meets [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]] by chance: "by adventure he met with Dame Bragwaine that was sent from her lady La Beale Isoud" (238-048). When Launcelot asks why she flees, she answers, "I flee for dread of my life" (238-051) — a reminder that her service to Isoud places her perpetually in danger. Launcelot returns her safely to her lady, and she continues her service to the end.
