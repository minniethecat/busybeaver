# Prophecy

**Prophecy** is a driving [[marvel|supernatural]] force in Thomas Malory's *Le Morte d'[[king-arthur|Arthur]]*. Delivered primarily through the figure of [[merlin|Merlin]], prophecies shape the decisions of [[kinsman|kin]]gs, foretell the rise and fall of [[realm|kingdom]]s, and create an atmosphere of inescapable fate that pervades the [[king-arthur|Arthur]]ian cycle.

## Merlin as Prophet

[[merlin|Merlin]] is the principal vehicle of prophecy in Malory's work. After a great battle, "[[merlin|Merlin]] told unto [[king-arthur|King Arthur]] of the prophecy that there should be a great battle beside Salisbury, and [[mordred|Mordred]] his own son should be against him" (042-029). This prophecy establishes the tragic horizon of the ent[[wrath|ire]] narrative — [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s [[realm|kingdom]] is fated to end in civil [[battle|war]] with his own son. [[merlin|Merlin]]'s prophecies are not merely predictions but active forces that shape events, as when "he knew well that an [[king|King]] Lot had been with his body there at the first battle, [[king-arthur|King Arthur]] had been slain" (041-020).

## Prophecy as Narrative Engine

Malory integrates prophecy into the narrative structure itself. The tale "of the prophecy of [[merlin|Merlin]], and how Balin should give the dolorous stroke" (042-001) shows how prophecy is woven into chapter headings and announced as a narrative promise. The reader, like the characters, knows what is fated and watches it unfold.

## Ambiguity and Deception

Not all prophecies in Malory are genuine. A false prophet "with a tale of prophecy" (041-007) mocks and deceives, and one character complains that "this faiter with his prophecy hath mocked me" (041-020). The possibility of false prophecy introduces ambiguity — characters must discern between true foreknowledge and deception, a judgment that often proves fatal when wrong.
