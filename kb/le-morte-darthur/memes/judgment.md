**Judgment** in Malory's [[Le Morte d'Arthur]] operates on multiple registers: it is the legal decision of a [[kinsman|kin]]g or court, the outcome of [[test|trial]] by [[battle|combat]] in which God is understood to render verdict, and the moral discernment that distinguishes the wise ruler from the mere wielder of power. In a world without codified law, judgment is the mechanism by which justice becomes manifest—through the spoken word of the [[sovereign]], through the outcome of the [[joust]], or through what Malory calls "the righteous judgment of God."

## The King's Judgment

As [[king|sovereign]], [[King Arthur]] holds the power of judgment over his subjects. When a knight is accused or a dispute arises, it is to the [[kinsman|kin]]g that the parties turn for resolution:

> the duke shall abide my judgment (107-018)

The [[kinsman|kin]]g's judgment is final and binding, but its legitimacy depends on the [[kinsman|kin]]g's own righteousness. A [[kinsman|kin]]g who judges unjustly—as [[King Mark]] does—undermines the very order he is meant to uphold. Judgment, in this sense, is both the [[king|sovereign]]'s greatest power and his greatest vulnerability: every judgment rendered is a test of the judge's own [[worship|worthiness]].

## Trial by Combat

When human judgment is insufficient—when guilt cannot be determined by testimony or evidence—the matter is referred to God through [[test|trial]] by [[battle|combat]]. The [[battle|combat]]ants [[battle|fight]], and the victor is understood to have been vindicated by divine will:

> That is the righteous judgment of God (088-084)

This form of judgment assumes that God intervenes d[[wrath|ire]]ctly in the outcome of [[battle|combat]], ensuring that the right prevails. It is the ultimate fusion of [[prowess]] and [[faith]]: the knight who [[battle|fight]]s in a just cause may trust that his skill is augmented by divine assistance. Yet Malory is not naive about this mechanism—the [[test|trial]] by [[battle|combat]] can be manipulated, and innocent parties may fall when God's purposes are inscrutable.

## Judgment and the Queen

The most consequential judgment in the Morte falls upon [[Queen Guenever]] when she is accused of [[treason]]. Her [[test|trial]] by [[battle|combat]]—with Sir [[Sir Launcelot|Launcelot]] as her [[champion]]—is a judgment not only of her guilt but of the ent[[wrath|ire]] moral order of the [[round-table|Round Table]]. When [[sir-launcelot|Launcelot]] prevails in [[battle|combat]], the judgment of God appears to vindicate the queen, but the political consequences of the [[test|trial]] set in motion the events that will destroy [[Camelot]]. Judgment, in this ultimate instance, delivers a true verdict that nonetheless produces catastrophe.

## Divine Judgment and Human Justice

Throughout the Morte, Malory maintains a tension between human and divine judgment. Human courts may err; [[kinsman|kin]]gs may judge unjustly; [[test|trial]]s by [[battle|combat]] may produce outcomes whose meaning is ambiguous. But behind all human judgment stands the judgment of God, which—though often delayed—is never absent. The fall of the [[round-table|Round Table]] is itself a judgment, rendered not by any human court but by the logic of events that Malory presents as providential.
