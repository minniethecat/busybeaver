[155-001]battle.

[155-002]Then Sir Brian de les Isles and Grummore Grummursum, knights of the castle,

[155-003]encountered with Sir Aglovale, and Sir Tor smote down Sir Grummore Grummursum

[155-004]to the earth. Then came in Sir Carados of the dolorous tower, and Sir Turquine,

[155-005]knights of the castle; and there encountered with them [[sir-percivale|Sir Percivale]] de Galis

[155-006]and Sir Lamorak de Galis, that were two brethren. And there encountered Sir

[155-007]Percivale with Sir Carados, and either brake their spears unto their hands, and

[155-008]then Sir Turquine with Sir Lamorak, and either of them smote down other’s

[155-009]horse and all to the earth, and either parties rescued other, and horsed them

[155-010]again. And Sir Arnold and Sir Gauter, knights of the castle, encountered with

[155-011]Sir Brandiles and [[sir-kay|Sir Kay]], and these four knights encountered mightily, and

[155-012]brake their spears to their hands. Then came in [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]], Sir Sadok, and

[155-013]Sir Dinas, knights of the castle, and there encountered Sir Tristram with Sir

[155-014]Bedivere, and there Sir Bedivere was smitten to the earth both horse and man.

[155-015]And Sir Sadok encountered with Sir Petipase, and there Sir Sadok was

[155-016]overthrown. And there Uwaine les Avoutres smote down Sir Dinas, the Seneschal.

[155-017]Then came in Sir Persant of Inde, a knight of the castle, and there encountered

[155-018]with him [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot du Lake]], and there he smote Sir Persant, horse and man,

[155-019]to the earth. Then came Sir Pertolepe from the castle, and there encountered

[155-020]with him Sir Lionel, and there Sir Pertolepe, the Green Knight, smote down Sir

[155-021]Lionel, brother to Sir Launcelot. All this was marked by noble heralds, who

[155-022]bare him best, and their names.

[155-023]And then came into the [[battle|field]] Sir Perimones, the Red Knight, Sir Persant’s

[155-024]brother, that was a knight of the castle, and he encountered with Sir Ector de

[155-025]Maris, and either smote other so hard that both their horses and they fell to

[155-026]the earth. And then came in the Red Knight of the Red Launds, and [[sir-gareth|Sir Gareth]],

[155-027]from the castle, and there encountered with them [[sir-bors|Sir Bors de Ganis]] and Sir

[155-028]Bleoberis, and there the Red Knight and Sir Bors [either] smote other so hard

[155-029]that their spears brast, and their horses fell grovelling to the earth. Then

[155-030]Sir Bleoberis brake his spear upon Sir Gareth, but of that stroke Sir Bleoberis

[155-031]fell to the earth. When Sir Galihodin saw that he bade Sir Gareth keep him, and

[155-032]Sir Gareth smote him to the earth. Then Sir Galihud gat a spear to avenge his

[155-033]brother, and in the same wise Sir Gareth served him, and Sir Dinadan and his

[155-034]brother, La Cote Male Taile, and Sir Sagramore le Desirous, and Sir Dodinas le

[155-035]Savage. All these he bare down with one spear.

[155-036]When King Agwisance of Ireland saw Sir Gareth fare so, he marvelled what he

[155-037]might be that one time seemed green, and another time, at his again coming, he

[155-038]seemed blue. And thus at every course that he rode to and fro he changed his

[155-039]colour, so that there might neither king nor knight have ready cognisance of

[155-040]him. Then Sir Agwisance, [[king-arthur|the King]] of Ireland, encountered with Sir Gareth, and

[155-041]there Sir Gareth smote him from his horse, saddle and all. And then came King

[155-042]Carados of Scotland, and Sir Gareth smote him down horse and man. And in the

[155-043]same wise he served King Uriens of the land of Gore. And then came in Sir

[155-044]Bagdemagus, and Sir Gareth smote him down, horse and man, to the earth. And

[155-045]Bagdemagus’ son, Meliganus, brake a spear upon Sir Gareth mightily and

[155-046]knightly. And then Sir Galahault, the noble prince, cried on high: Knight with

[155-047]the many colours, well hast thou jousted; now make thee ready that I may joust

[155-048]with thee. Sir Gareth heard him, and he gat a great spear, and so they

[155-049]encountered together, and there the prince brake his spear; but Sir Gareth

[155-050]smote him upon the left side of the helm that he reeled here and there, and he

[155-051]had fallen down had not his men recovered him.

[155-052]So God me help, said King Arthur, that same knight with the many colours is a

[155-053]good knight. Wherefore the king called unto him Sir Launcelot, and prayed him

[155-054]to encounter with that knight. Sir, said Launcelot, I may well find in my heart

[155-055]for to forbear him as at this time, for he hath had travail enough this day;

[155-056]and when a good knight doth so well upon some day, it is no good knight’s

[155-057]part to let him of his [[honour|worship]], and namely, when he seeth a knight hath done so

[155-058]great labour; for peradventure, said Sir Launcelot, his quarrel is here this

[155-059]day, and peradventure he is best beloved with this lady of all that be here;

[155-060]for I see well he paineth him and enforceth him to do great deeds, and

[155-061]therefore, said Sir Launcelot, as for me, this day he shall have the honour;

[155-062]though it lay in my power to put him from it I would not.
