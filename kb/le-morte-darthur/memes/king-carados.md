# King Carados

King Carados, also known as Carados of Scotland and Sir Carados of the Dolorous Tower, was a figure of formidable martial prowess in [[Le Morte d'Arthur]] whose loyalties shifted across the narrative. He appears both among the rebel kings who opposed [[King Arthur]]'s accession and as a powerful tournament knight and gaoler of the Round Table's greatest champions. His strength in arms was such that even [[Sir Launcelot]] was pressed to overcome him.

## The Rebel King

In the early days of Arthur's reign, Carados numbered among the kings who refused to accept the young sovereign's authority. He arrived at the great feast with five hundred knights and joined his fellow kings in their defiance (011-011). At the battle of Bedegraine, Carados and the other rebel kings set on Arthur fiercely, seeking to overthrow him by force of arms (012-023). Despite swearing later to bring five thousand men to Arthur's service (015-024), Carados remained a figure whose allegiance could not be taken for granted.

## The Warrior in Battle

Carados was a fearsome combatant on the field. He encountered King Bors of Gaul in the press of battle and was smitten to the earth by the force of the older knight's blow (018-059). His knights rescued him mightily by force of arms, dragging him from beneath the enemy's spears, for the rebels valued him too highly to let him fall (018-060). Even in defeat, Carados displayed the resilience of a battle-hardened warrior, meeting King Brandegore and others on foot when his horse was slain and the ground became too treacherous to hold (018-021).

## The Knight of the Dolorous Tower

In later books, Carados appears in a more ominous guise as Sir Carados of the Dolorous Tower, a fearsome knight who imprisoned many of Arthur's champions. He was known as a "noble knight" of formidable skill who struck down opponents in the tournament field (153-074). At one great tournament, Sir Carados of the Dolorous Tower entered the lists and fought with terrible efficacy (155-004). Sir Percivale broke his spear against Carados, and the two knights battled to a standstill, neither yielding ground to the other (155-007).

## The Duel with Sir Launcelot

Carados's most notorious act was the abduction of [[Sir Gawaine]]. Carados, described as "the mighty king," was made like a giant and bore Gawaine away across his horse's neck, so powerful was his grip (191-004). When Sir Launcelot intercepted him, he commanded Carados to lay down the knight and fight. Carados refused with contempt, declaring he would serve Launcelot in the same manner (191-014). The ensuing combat was fierce, but at last Sir Launcelot smote Carados from his horse, took him by the collar, and pulled him to the ground, securing Gawaine's release (191-020). The great prison of the Dolorous Tower was found to contain many knights whom Carados had captured, including King Carados of Scotland himself — a reminder that even the mightiest gaoler could become a prisoner (211-041).
