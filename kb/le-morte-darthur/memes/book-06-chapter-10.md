[118-001]and slew a knight that distressed all ladies and also a villain that kept a

[118-002]bridge.

[118-003]Now turn we unto [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]], that rode with the damosel in a fair highway.

[118-004]Sir, said the damosel, here by this way haunteth a knight that distressed all

[118-005]ladies and gentlewomen, and at the least he robbeth them or lieth by them.

[118-006]What, said Sir Launcelot, is he a thief and a knight and a ravisher of women?

[118-007]he doth shame unto the order of knighthood, and contrary unto his oath; it is

[118-008][[mercy|pity]] that he liveth. But, fair damosel, ye shall ride on afore, yourself, and I

[118-009]will keep myself in covert, and if that he trouble you or distress you I shall

[118-010]be your rescue and learn him to be ruled as a knight.

[118-011]So the maid rode on by the way a soft ambling pace, and within a while came out

[118-012]that knight on horseback out of the wood, and his page with him, and there he

[118-013]put the damosel from her horse, and then she cried. With that came Launcelot as

[118-014]fast as he might till he came to that knight, saying, O thou false knight and

[118-015]traitor unto knighthood, who did learn thee to distress ladies and gentlewomen?

[118-016]When the knight saw Sir Launcelot thus rebuking him he answered not, but drew

[118-017]his sword and rode unto Sir Launcelot, and Sir Launcelot threw his spear from

[118-018]him, and drew out his sword, and struck him such a buffet on the helmet that he

[118-019]clave his head and neck unto the throat. Now hast thou thy payment that long

[118-020]thou hast deserved! That is truth, said the damosel, for like as Sir Turquine

[118-021]watched to destroy knights, so did this knight attend to destroy and distress

[118-022]ladies, damosels, and gentlewomen, and his name was Sir Peris de Forest Savage.

[118-023]Now, damosel, said Sir Launcelot, will ye any more service of me? Nay, sir, she

[118-024]said, at this time, but almighty Jesu preserve you wheresomever ye ride or go,

[118-025]for the curteist knight thou art, and meekest unto all ladies and gentlewomen,

[118-026]that now liveth. But one thing, sir knight, methinketh ye lack, ye that are a

[118-027]knight wifeless, that he will not love some maiden or gentlewoman, for I could

[118-028]never hear say that ever ye loved any of no manner degree, and that is great

[118-029]pity; but it is noised that ye love [[queen-guenever|Queen Guenever]], and that she hath ordained

[118-030]by enchantment that ye shall never love none other but her, nor none other

[118-031]damosel nor lady shall rejoice you; wherefore many in this land, of high estate

[118-032]and low, make great sorrow.

[118-033]Fair damosel, said Sir Launcelot, I may not warn people to speak of me what it

[118-034]pleaseth them; but for to be a wedded man, I think it not; for then I must

[118-035]couch with her, and leave arms and tournaments, battles, and adventures; and as

[118-036]for to say for to take my pleasaunce with paramours, that will I refuse in

[118-037]principal for dread of God; for knights that be adventurous or lecherous shall

[118-038]not be happy nor fortunate unto the wars, for other they shall be overcome with

[118-039]a simpler knight than they be themselves, other else they shall by unhap and

[118-040]their cursedness slay better men than they be themselves. And so who that useth

[118-041]paramours shall be unhappy, and all thing is unhappy that is about them.

[118-042]And so Sir Launcelot and she departed. And then he rode in a deep forest two

[118-043]days and more, and had strait lodging. So on the third day he rode over a long

[118-044]bridge, and there stert upon him suddenly a passing foul churl, and he smote

[118-045]his horse on the nose that he turned about, and asked him why he rode over that

[118-046]bridge without his licence. Why should I not ride this way? said Sir Launcelot,

[118-047]I may not ride beside. Thou shalt not choose, said the churl, and lashed at him

[118-048]with a great club shod with iron. Then Sir Launcelot drew his sword and put the

[118-049]stroke aback, and clave his head unto the paps. At the end of the bridge was a

[118-050]fair village, and all the people, men and women, cried on Sir Launcelot, and

[118-051]said, A worse deed didst thou never for thyself, for thou hast slain the chief

[118-052]porter of our castle. Sir Launcelot let them say what they would, and straight

[118-053]he went into the castle; and when he came into the castle he alighted, and tied

[118-054]his horse to a ring on the wall and there he saw a fair green court, and

[118-055]thither he dressed him, for there him thought was a fair place to [[battle|fight]] in. So

[118-056]he looked about, and saw much people in doors and windows that said, Fair

[118-057]knight, thou art unhappy.
