# Homage

**Homage**  in Malory's "Le Morte d'[[king-arthur|Arthur]]" is the foundational political ritual of the feudal world — a formal ceremony in which a vassal kneels before his [[baron|lord]], places his hands between the [[baron|lord]]'s hands, and swears an oath of loyalty and service in exchange for protection and [[realm|land]]. The act of "doing homage" establishes the hierarchical bonds that structure [[king-arthur|Arthur]]ian political life, and the refusal of homage is tantamount to a declaration of [[battle|war]]. The ceremony sits at the intersection of personal [[Fealty]] and public obligation, translating individual loyalty into the architecture of political order.

## The Ceremony of Homage

The act of doing homage is described with ritual specificity throughout the text. A vassal "kneels down" and "does homage" — the physical posture of submission enacting the political relationship it creates:

> "kneeled down, and did him homage with his sword" (135-050)
> "everych of them did him homage" (029-006)

The homage ceremony is public by design: it must be witnessed to be valid, and the presence of witnesses transforms a private agreement into a publicly enforceable obligation. The ceremony's power lies in this publicity — a [[baron|lord]] who has received homage can call upon the witnessing community to enforce the vassal's obligations.

## Homage as Political Consolidation

The early books of "Le Morte d'[[king-arthur|Arthur]]" are structured around [[King Arthur]]'s campaign to receive homage from the [[baron|lord]]s of Britain. After each major battle, defeated [[kinsman|kin]]gs and [[baron|lord]]s come to "do him homage" — these ceremonies mark the progressive consolidation of [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s [[sovereign|sovereignty]]:

> "she came thither for to do homage, as other lords did after the great battle" (020-070)
> "they come to King Arthur for to do him homage" (053-014)

The refusal to do homage is the primary form of political resistance in the [[king-arthur|Arthur]]ian world. When [[King Rience]] refuses to do homage to [[king-arthur|Arthur]], or when [[king-arthur|Arthur]] himself debates whether to do homage to [[Emperor Lucius]], the stakes are not merely symbolic — the refusal or acceptance of homage determines the ent[[wrath|ire]] structure of political authority:

> "I owe him none homage, nor none of mine elders" (029-015)
> "he should do me homage on both his knees, or else he should lose his head" (029-019)

## Homage and Fealty

Homage is distinct from but closely related to [[Fealty]]. Homage is the physical ceremony — the kneeling, the clasped hands, the public declaration. [[fealty|Fealty]] is the oath of loyalty that often accompanies but is not identical to homage. A vassal may swear fealty without doing homage (as when pledging loyalty to a temporary ally), and homage may be performed without a separate fealty oath (as when the physical ceremony is itself deemed sufficient). The text sometimes combines them:

> "yield to him homage and fealty" (107-038)
> "proffered him his homage and fealty at all times" (137-046)

This pairing reflects the dual nature of the feudal bond: homage is the physical and public dimension, fealty is the verbal and moral dimension.

## The Binding Force of Homage

Once performed, homage creates obligations that are nearly impossible to dissolve legitimately. A vassal who has done homage and later rebels is not merely a political opponent but an oathbreaker — a far more serious charge in the chivalric value system. This binding quality is what gives homage its political utility: it converts temporary military advantage into permanent political obligation.

The t[[wrath|rage]]dy of the work's final books is in part a t[[wrath|rage]]dy of broken homage. The [[baron|lord]]s who once did homage to [[King Arthur]] turn against him; the knights who once knelt before him take up arms against each other. The dissolution of the [[Round Table]] is the dissolution of every homage bond that constituted the [[king-arthur|Arthur]]ian polity.

## Homage's Narrative Function

Homage serves as the narrative's mechanism for mar[[kinsman|kin]]g political transitions. Each ceremony of homage signals a shift in the ba[[spear|lance]] of power — a defeated [[baron|lord]] becomes a subject, a contested [[kinsman|kin]]g becomes a [[king|sovereign]], a rebel's submission becomes a restoration of order. The accumulation of homage ceremonies across the early books charts [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s rise from disputed claimant to undisputed [[kinsman|kin]]g, just as the repudiation of homage in the later books charts the collapse of his authority. Homage is thus the ritual punctuation of [[king-arthur|Arthur]]ian political history.
