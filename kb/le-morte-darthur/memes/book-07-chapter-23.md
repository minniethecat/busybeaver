[150-001]next night and was beheaded again, and how at the feast of Pentecost all the

[150-002]knights that [[sir-gareth|Sir Gareth]] had overcome came and yielded them to [[king-arthur|King Arthur]].

[150-003]Right as she promised she came; and she was not so soon in his bed but she

[150-004]espied an armed knight coming toward the bed: therewithal she warned Sir

[150-005]Gareth, and lightly through the good help of Dame Lionesse he was armed; and

[150-006]they hurtled together with great ire and malice all about the hall; and there

[150-007]was great light as it had been the number of twenty torches both before and

[150-008]behind, so that Sir Gareth strained him, so that his old wound brast again

[150-009]a-bleeding; but he was hot and courageous and took no keep, but with his great

[150-010]force he struck down that knight, and voided his helm, and struck off his head.

[150-011]Then he hewed the head in an hundred pieces. And when he had done so he took up

[150-012]all those pieces, and threw them out at a window into the ditches of the

[150-013]castle; and by this done he was so faint that unnethes he might stand for

[150-014]bleeding. And by when he was almost unarmed he fell in a deadly swoon on the

[150-015]floor; and then Dame Lionesse cried so that Sir Gringamore heard; and when he

[150-016]came and found Sir Gareth in that plight he made great sorrow; and there he

[150-017]awaked Sir Gareth, and gave him a drink that relieved him wonderly well; but

[150-018]the sorrow that Dame Lionesse made there may no tongue tell, for she so fared

[150-019]with herself as she would have died.

[150-020]Right so came this damosel Linet before them all, and she had fetched all the

[150-021]gobbets of the head that Sir Gareth had thrown out at a window, and there she

[150-022]anointed them as she had done to-fore, and set them together again. Well,

[150-023]damosel Linet, said Sir Gareth, I have not deserved all this despite that ye do

[150-024]unto me. Sir knight, she said, I have nothing done but I will avow, and all

[150-025]that I have done shall be to your [[honour|worship]], and to us all. And then was Sir

[150-026]Gareth staunched of his bleeding. But the leeches said that there was no man

[150-027]that bare the life should heal him throughout of his wound but if they healed

[150-028]him that caused that stroke by enchantment.

[150-029]So leave we Sir Gareth there with Sir Gringamore and his sisters, and turn we

[150-030]unto King Arthur, that at the next feast of Pentecost held his feast; and there

[150-031]came the Green Knight with fifty knights, and yielded them all unto King

[150-032]Arthur. And so there came the Red Knight his brother, and yielded him to King

[150-033]Arthur, and three score knights with him. Also there came the Blue Knight,

[150-034]brother to them, with an hundred knights, and yielded them unto King Arthur;

[150-035]and the Green Knight’s name was Pertolepe, and the Red Knight’s

[150-036]name was Perimones, and the Blue Knight’s name was Sir Persant of Inde.

[150-037]These three brethren told King Arthur how they were overcome by a knight that a

[150-038]damosel had with her, and called him Beaumains. Jesu, said the king, I marvel

[150-039]what knight he is, and of what lineage he is come. He was with me a

[150-040]twelvemonth, and poorly and shamefully he was fostered, and [[sir-kay|Sir Kay]] in scorn

[150-041]named him Beaumains. So right as the king stood so talking with these three

[150-042]brethren, there came [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot du Lake]], and told the king that there was

[150-043]come a goodly lord with six hundred knights with him.

[150-044]Then the king went out of [[camelot|Carlion]], for there was the feast, and there came to

[150-045]him this lord, and saluted the king in a goodly manner. What will ye, said King

[150-046]Arthur, and what is your [[quest|errand]]? Sir, he said, my name is the Red Knight of the

[150-047]Red Launds, but my name is Sir Ironside; and sir, wit ye well, here I am sent

[150-048]to you of a knight that is called Beaumains, for he won me in plain battle hand

[150-049]for hand, and so did never no knight but he, that ever had the better of me

[150-050]this thirty winter; the which commanded to yield me to you at your will. Ye are

[150-051]welcome, said the king, for ye have been long a great foe to me and my court,

[150-052]and now I trust to God I shall so entreat you that ye shall be my friend. Sir,

[150-053]both I and these five hundred knights shall always be at your summons to do you

[150-054]service as may lie in our powers. Jesu mercy, said King Arthur, I am much

[150-055]beholden unto that knight that hath put so his body in devoir to worship me and

[150-056]my court. And as to thee, Ironside, that art called the Red Knight of the Red

[150-057]Launds, thou art called a perilous knight; and if thou wilt hold of me I shall

[150-058]worship thee and make thee knight of the [[round-table|Table Round]]; but then thou must be no

[150-059]more a murderer. Sir, as to that, I have promised unto Sir Beaumains never more

[150-060]to use such customs, for all the shameful customs that I used I did at the

[150-061]request of a lady that I loved; and therefore I must go unto Sir Launcelot, and

[150-062]unto [[sir-gawaine|Sir Gawaine]], and ask them forgiveness of the evil will I had unto them;

[150-063]for all that I put to death was all only for the love of Sir Launcelot and of

[150-064]Sir Gawaine. They be here now, said the king, afore thee, now may ye say to

[150-065]them what ye will. And then he kneeled down unto Sir Launcelot, and to Sir

[150-066]Gawaine, and prayed them of forgiveness of his enmity that ever he had against

[150-067]them.
