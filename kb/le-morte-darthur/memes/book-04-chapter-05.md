[071-001][[round-table|Round Table]], and how Bagdemagus was displeased.

[071-002]Now, said King Pellinore, I shall put to you two knights, and ye shall choose

[071-003]which is most worthy, that is Sir Bagdemagus, and Sir Tor, my son. But because

[071-004]Sir Tor is my son I may not praise him, but else, an he were not my son, I

[071-005]durst say that of his age there is not in this land a better knight than he is,

[071-006]nor of better conditions and loath to do any wrong, and loath to take any

[071-007]wrong. By my head, said [[king-arthur|Arthur]], he is a passing good knight as any ye spake of

[071-008]this day, that wot I well, said the king; for I have seen him proved, but he

[071-009]saith little and he doth much more, for I know none in all this court an he

[071-010]were as well born on his mother’s side as he is on your side, that is

[071-011]like him of [[courage|prowess]] and of might: and therefore I will have him at this time,

[071-012]and leave Sir Bagdemagus till another time. So when they were so chosen by the

[071-013]assent of all the barons, so were there found in their sieges every

[071-014]knights’ names that here are rehearsed, and so were they set in their

[071-015]sieges; whereof Sir Bagdemagus was wonderly wroth, that Sir Tor was advanced

[071-016]afore him, and therefore suddenly he departed from the court, and took his

[071-017]squire with him, and rode long in a forest till they came to a cross, and there

[071-018]alighted and said his prayers devoutly. The meanwhile his squire found written

[071-019]upon the cross, that Bagdemagus should never return unto the court again, till

[071-020]he had won a knight’s body of the Round Table, body for body. So, sir,

[071-021]said the squire, here I find writing of you, therefore I rede you return again

[071-022]to the court. That shall I never, said Bagdemagus, till men speak of me great

[071-023][[honour|worship]], and that I be worthy to be a knight of the Round Table. And so he rode

[071-024]forth, and there by the way he found a branch of an holy herb that was the sign

[071-025]of the Sangreal, and no knight found such tokens but he were a good liver.

[071-026]So, as Sir Bagdemagus rode to see many adventures, it happed him to come to the

[071-027]rock whereas the [[lady-of-the-lake|Lady of the Lake]] had put [[merlin|Merlin]] under the stone, and there he

[071-028]heard him make great dole; whereof Sir Bagdemagus would have holpen him, and

[071-029]went unto the great stone, and it was so heavy that an hundred men might not

[071-030]lift it up. When Merlin wist he was there, he bade leave his labour, for all

[071-031]was in vain, for he might never be holpen but by her that put him there. And so

[071-032]Bagdemagus departed and did many adventures, and proved after a full good

[071-033]knight, and came again to the court and was made knight of the Round Table. So

[071-034]on the morn there fell new tidings and other adventures.
