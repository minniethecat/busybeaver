# Sir Segwarides

**Sir Segwarides** is a knight of Cornwall and one of three Saracen brothers — alongside [[Sir Palamides]] and Sir Safere — who appears primarily in the Tristram cycle of Thomas Malory's *Le Morte d'[[King Arthur]]*. Though a warrior of acknowledged prowess, Segwarides' narrative importance derives less from his own deeds than from his entanglement with [[Sir Tristram]] over the [[love]] of his wife — a domestic tragedy that echoes the larger triangles of the Morte.

## The Saracen Brethren

Segwarides belongs to a notable fraternal triad: himself, [[Sir Palamides]], and Sir Safere — "Segwarides, brethren to Sir Palamides" (154-055). The three brothers share Saracen lineage and a connection to the court of Cornwall. While Palamides achieves renown as one of the greatest knights of the Round Table, Segwarides remains a secondary figure, yet his brotherhood with Palamides places him among the significant martial families of the Arthurian world: "Sir Segwarides, Sir Palamides, Sir Safere — brethren" (154-057).

## The Wife and the Lover

Segwarides' wife — "a fair lady, and a passing good woman" — becomes the unwitting centre of a conflict between her husband and [[Sir Tristram]]. The lady "loved Sir Tristram" (176-013), and Tristram reciprocated this [[love]]. The affair is consummated through a clandestine meeting: "the steven was set betwixt Segwarides' wife and Sir Tristram" (176-028). What distinguishes this episode from the grand romance of Tristram and [[La Beale Isoud]] is its sordid domesticity: Segwarides is not a villainous rival but a wronged husband, and Tristram's conduct here sits uneasily alongside his chivalric reputation.

## The Confrontation

When Segwarides discovers the truth, he confronts his wife directly: "Ah, false traitress, said Segwarides, where is he become?" (177-018). Armed with the knowledge, he rides against [[Sir Tristram]]: "therewithal Segwarides smote Sir Tristram with a spear" (177-024). He declares that honour leaves no room for retreat: "Nay, said Segwarides, that shall not be, for either thou shalt die or I" (177-028).

Yet for all his righteous fury, Segwarides is outmatched. Tristram, "the mightiest knight of the body he smote Sir Segwarides that he fell to the earth" (177-030). The Saracen knight is wounded and his men carry him from the field, leaving Tristram the victor (177-033). Later, Segwarides "durst not" renew the quarrel — a prudent but unheroic retreat from a contest he cannot win (177-041).

## The Abduction of His Lady

Segwarides' humiliation deepens when [[Sir Bleoberis]] "did choose Sir Segwarides' wife, and took her" (178-011) during a courtly game of choosing. Segwarides, still recovering from his wounds, can do nothing as Bleoberis rides away with his lady. The news reaches him through a squire: "Sir Segwarides was beaten sore and wounded to the death" (178-030).

The lady herself later declares that she was abandoned, reproaching her husband: "I suffered my lord Segwarides ride after me; but until the time he met with you he rescued me not" (181-006). The reunion at last comes when Tristram brings her to "the abbey where my lord Sir Segwarides lieth" (181-020). Seeing his lady restored, Segwarides is "pleased right well" (181-036), and what might have become a fatal grudge is instead "forgiven and forgotten" (177-041) — at least on Segwarides' part.

## Later Encounters

Segwarides reappears later in the narrative in a quieter register: "by a well he saw Segwarides and a damosel" (201-007). The two knights exchange greetings: "Sir, said Segwarides, I know you for a knight of the Round Table" (201-008). This brief, courteous encounter suggests a knight who has made his peace — no longer defined by the injuries of his marriage, but simply as one knight among many in the Arthurian landscape.
