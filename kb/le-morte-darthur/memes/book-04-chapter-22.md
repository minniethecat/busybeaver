[088-001]Ettard, and how Sir Pelleas found them sleeping.

[088-002]And therewith [[sir-gawaine|Sir Gawaine]] plight his troth unto Sir Pelleas to be true and

[088-003]faithful unto him; so each one plight their troth to other, and so they changed

[088-004]horses and harness, and Sir Gawaine departed, and came to the castle whereas

[088-005]stood the pavilions of this lady without the gate. And as soon as Ettard had

[088-006]espied Sir Gawaine she fled in toward the castle. Sir Gawaine spake on high,

[088-007]and bade her abide, for he was not Sir Pelleas; I am another knight that have

[088-008]slain Sir Pelleas. Do off your helm, said the Lady Ettard, that I may see your

[088-009]visage. And so when she saw that it was not Sir Pelleas, she bade him alight

[088-010]and led him unto her castle, and asked him faithfully whether he had slain Sir

[088-011]Pelleas. And he said her yea, and told her his name was Sir Gawaine of the

[088-012]court of [[king-arthur|King Arthur]], and his sister’s son. Truly, said she, that is

[088-013]great [[mercy|pity]], for he was a passing good knight of his body, but of all men alive

[088-014]I hated him most, for I could never be quit of him; and for ye have slain him I

[088-015]shall be your woman, and to do anything that might please you. So she made Sir

[088-016]Gawaine good cheer. Then Sir Gawaine said that he loved a lady and by no means

[088-017]she would love him. She is to blame, said Ettard, an she will not love you, for

[088-018]ye that be so well born a man, and such a man of [[courage|prowess]], there is no lady in

[088-019]the world too good for you. Will ye, said Sir Gawaine, promise me to do all

[088-020]that ye may, by the faith of your body, to get me the love of my lady? Yea,

[088-021]sir, said she, and that I promise you by the faith of my body. Now, said Sir

[088-022]Gawaine, it is yourself that I love so well, therefore I pray you hold your

[088-023]promise. I may not choose, said the Lady Ettard, but if I should be forsworn;

[088-024]and so she granted him to fulfil all his desire.

[088-025]So it was then in the month of May that she and Sir Gawaine went out of the

[088-026]castle and supped in a pavilion, and there was made a bed, and there Sir

[088-027]Gawaine and the Lady Ettard went to bed together, and in another pavilion she

[088-028]laid her damosels, and in the third pavilion she laid part of her knights, for

[088-029]then she had no dread of Sir Pelleas. And there Sir Gawaine lay with her in

[088-030]that pavilion two days and two nights. And on the third day, in the morning

[088-031]early, Sir Pelleas armed him, for he had never slept since Sir Gawaine departed

[088-032]from him; for Sir Gawaine had promised him by the faith of his body, to come to

[088-033]him unto his pavilion by that priory within the space of a day and a night.

[088-034]Then Sir Pelleas mounted upon horseback, and came to the pavilions that stood

[088-035]without the castle, and found in the first pavilion three knights in three

[088-036]beds, and three squires lying at their feet. Then went he to the second

[088-037]pavilion and found four gentlewomen lying in four beds. And then he yede to the

[088-038]third pavilion and found Sir Gawaine lying in bed with his Lady Ettard, and

[088-039]either clipping other in arms, and when he saw that his heart well-nigh brast

[088-040]for sorrow, and said: Alas! that ever a knight should be found so false; and

[088-041]then he took his horse and might not abide no longer for pure sorrow. And when

[088-042]he had ridden nigh half a mile he turned again and thought to slay them both;

[088-043]and when he saw them both so lie sleeping fast, unnethe he might hold him on

[088-044]horseback for sorrow, and said thus to himself, Though this knight be never so

[088-045]false, I will never slay him sleeping, for I will never destroy the high order

[088-046]of knighthood; and therewith he departed again. And or he had ridden half a

[088-047]mile he returned again, and thought then to slay them both, making the greatest

[088-048]sorrow that ever man made. And when he came to the pavilions, he tied his horse

[088-049]unto a tree, and pulled out his sword naked in his hand, and went to them

[088-050]thereas they lay, and yet he thought it were shame to slay them sleeping, and

[088-051]laid the naked sword overthwart both their throats, and so took his horse and

[088-052]rode his way.

[088-053]And when Sir Pelleas came to his pavilions he told his knights and his squires

[088-054]how he had sped, and said thus to them, For your true and good service ye have

[088-055]done me I shall give you all my goods, for I will go unto my bed and never

[088-056]arise until I am dead. And when that I am dead I charge you that ye take the

[088-057]heart out of my body and bear it her betwixt two silver dishes, and tell her

[088-058]how I saw her lie with the false knight Sir Gawaine. Right so Sir Pelleas

[088-059]unarmed himself, and went unto his bed making [[marvel|marvellous]] dole and sorrow.

[088-060]When Sir Gawaine and Ettard awoke of their sleep, and found the naked sword

[088-061]overthwart their throats, then she knew well it was Sir Pelleas’ sword.

[088-062]Alas! said she to Sir Gawaine, ye have betrayed me and Sir Pelleas both, for ye

[088-063]told me ye had slain him, and now I know well it is not so, he is alive. And if

[088-064]Sir Pelleas had been as uncourteous to you as ye have been to him ye had been a

[088-065]dead knight; but ye have deceived me and betrayed me falsely, that all ladies

[088-066]and damosels may beware by you and me. And therewith Sir Gawaine made him

[088-067]ready, and went into the forest. So it happed then that the Damosel of the

[088-068]Lake, [[lady-of-the-lake|Nimue]], met with a knight of Sir Pelleas, that went on his foot in the

[088-069]forest making great dole, and she asked him the cause. And so the woful knight

[088-070]told her how his master and lord was betrayed through a knight and lady, and

[088-071]how he will never arise out of his bed till he be dead. Bring me to him, said

[088-072]she anon, and I will warrant his life he shall not die for love, and she that

[088-073]hath caused him so to love, she shall be in as evil plight as he is or it be

[088-074]long to, for it is no joy of such a proud lady that will have no mercy of such

[088-075]a valiant knight. Anon that knight brought her unto him, and when she saw him

[088-076]lie in his bed, she thought she saw never so likely a knight; and therewith she

[088-077]threw an enchantment upon him, and he fell asleep. And therewhile she rode unto

[088-078]the Lady Ettard, and charged no man to awake him till she came again. So within

[088-079]two hours she brought the Lady Ettard thither, and both ladies found him

[088-080]asleep: Lo, said the Damosel of the Lake, ye ought to be ashamed for to murder

[088-081]such a knight. And therewith she threw such an enchantment upon her that she

[088-082]loved him sore, that well-nigh she was out of her mind. O Lord Jesu, said the

[088-083]Lady Ettard, how is it befallen unto me that I love now him that I have most

[088-084]hated of any man alive? That is the righteous judgment of God, said the

[088-085]damosel. And then anon Sir Pelleas awaked and looked upon Ettard; and when he

[088-086]saw her he knew her, and then he hated her more than any woman alive, and said:

[088-087]Away, traitress, come never in my sight. And when she heard him say so, she

[088-088]wept and made great sorrow out of measure.
