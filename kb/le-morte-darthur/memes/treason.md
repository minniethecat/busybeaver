# Treason

**Treason** is the capital sin of the chivalric world in Thomas Malory's *Le Morte d'[[king-arthur|Arthur]]*. It is the violation of sworn loyalty — to [[kinsman|kin]]g, to [[kinsman|kin]], to fellowship — and it is the force that ultimately destroys [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s [[realm|kingdom]]. Where chivalry builds, treason corrodes.

## Treason as the Ultimate Crime

Treason pervades the Morte from its earliest chapters. The narrative opens with "[[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s mother, of treason; and how a knight came and demanded" (024-001), establishing treason as a foundational concern. The knights of the [[round-table|Round Table]] seek a champion "without treachery or treason" (032-041), and the ideal knight is one "most of [[honour|worship]] without treason" (033-028). Treason is the negation of everything the [[round-table|Round Table]] represents.

## The Oath Against Treason

The [[feast-of-pentecost|Pentecost]]al [[oath|Oath]] — the closest thing to a formal chivalric code in Malory — explicitly commands knights "always to flee treason" (065-030), alongside prohibitions against murder and cruelty. Treason is listed as something to be fled, not merely avoided — the language of flight suggesting its insidious and pervasive nature.

## The Cry of Betrayal

The most dramatic invocation of treason comes at the moment of the [[round-table|Round Table]]'s dissolution. When the affair of [[sir-launcelot|Launcelot]] and [[queen-guenever|Guenever]] is exposed, "many cried, Treason, treason! Alas, said [[king-arthur|King Arthur]], we been all destroyed" (069-008). The repetition of the word captures the moment when private betrayal becomes public catastrophe. [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s response — "we been all destroyed" — confirms that treason against the [[kinsman|kin]]g is treason against the ent[[wrath|ire]] social order.
