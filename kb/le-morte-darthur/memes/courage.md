# Courage

**Courage** is among the most frequently invoked [[virtue|virtues]] in Thomas Malory's *Le Morte d'[[king-arthur|Arthur]]*. It is primarily displayed in battle but also tested in moral choices, where characters must act despite fear, uncertainty, or conflicting loyalties.

## Battlefield Courage

The most visible form of cou[[wrath|rage]] in Malory is martial bravery. Knights "dressed their [[shield|shields]] and [[arms|harness]], and they were so cou[[wrath|rage]]ous that many knights shook and bevered for eagerness" (018-009). This physical cou[[wrath|rage]] is contagious — the sight of cou[[wrath|rage]]ous [[battle|fight]]ers insp[[wrath|ire]]s others. [[king-arthur|King Arthur]] acknowledges his knights' "good cou[[wrath|rage]]" (013-007) as essential to the realm's defense.

Courage can tip the ba[[spear|lance]] of battle. Of a formidable opponent, Malory writes: "he is so cou[[wrath|rage]]ous of himself that he is come to the [[battle|field]] with little people" (068-029), showing how individual cou[[wrath|rage]] can compensate for numerical disadvantage. [[king-arthur|King Arthur]] "understood their cou[[wrath|rage]]s and good wills he thanked them" (097-015), recognizing cou[[wrath|rage]] as both an individual and collective resource.

## The Limits of Courage

Malory does not present cou[[wrath|rage]] as an unqualified good. One knight is [[battle|war]]ned: "Your cou[[wrath|rage]] had near-hand destroyed you" (102-030), acknowledging that cou[[wrath|rage]] unchecked by wisdom becomes recklessness. The tension between cou[[wrath|rage]]ous action and prudent restraint is a recurring theme, particularly in the later books where the [[round-table|Round Table]]'s martial ethos leads to civil [[battle|war]].

## Courage and Moral Testing

Beyond the battle[[battle|field]], cou[[wrath|rage]] in Malory encompasses the fortitude to resist temptation and endure suffering. The Grail quest tests not only martial cou[[wrath|rage]] but spiritual cou[[wrath|rage]] — the willingness to confess sins, accept failure, and persist in a quest one may be unworthy to complete. This interior dimension of cou[[wrath|rage]] distinguishes Malory's treatment from mere celebration of physical valor.
