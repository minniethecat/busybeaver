[224-002]Then the queen departed, but the brachet would not from him; and therewithal

[224-003]came [[king-mark|King Mark]], and the brachet set upon him, and bayed at them all. There

[224-004]withal Sir Andred spake and said: Sir, this is Sir Tristram, I see by the

[224-005]brachet. Nay, said the king, I cannot suppose that. Then the king asked him

[224-006]upon his faith what he was, and what was his name. So God me help, said he, my

[224-007]name is Sir Tristram de Liones; now do by me what ye list. Ah, said King Mark,

[224-008]me repenteth of your recovery. And then he let call his barons to judge Sir

[224-009]Tristram to the death. Then many of his barons would not assent thereto, and in

[224-010]especial Sir Dinas, the Seneschal, and Sir Fergus. And so by the advice of them

[224-011]all Sir Tristram was banished out of the country for ten year, and thereupon he

[224-012]took his oath upon a book before the king and his barons. And so he was made to

[224-013]depart out of the country of Cornwall; and there were many barons brought him

[224-014]unto his ship, of the which some were his friends and some his foes. And in the

[224-015]meanwhile there came a knight of [[king-arthur|King Arthur]]’s, his name was Dinadan, and

[224-016]his coming was for to seek after Sir Tristram; then they showed him where he

[224-017]was armed at all points going to the ship. Now fair knight, said Sir Dinadan,

[224-018]or ye pass this court that ye will joust with me I require thee. With a good

[224-019]will, said Sir Tristram, an these lords will give me leave. Then the barons

[224-020]granted thereto, and so they ran together, and there Sir Tristram gave Sir

[224-021]Dinadan a fall. And then he prayed Sir Tristram to give him leave to go in his

[224-022]fellowship. Ye shall be right welcome, said then Sir Tristram.

[224-023]And so they took their horses and rode to their ships together, and when Sir

[224-024]Tristram was in the sea he said: Greet well King Mark and all mine enemies, and

[224-025]say them I will come again when I may; and well am I rewarded for the fighting

[224-026]with Sir Marhaus, and delivered all this country from servage; and well am I

[224-027]rewarded for the fetching and costs of Queen Isoud out of Ireland, and the

[224-028]danger that I was in first and last, and by the way coming home what danger I

[224-029]had to bring again Queen Isoud from the Castle Pluere; and well am I rewarded

[224-030]when I fought with Sir Bleoberis for Sir Segwarides’ wife; and well am I

[224-031]rewarded when I fought with Sir Blamore de Ganis for King Anguish, father unto

[224-032]La Beale Isoud; and well am I rewarded when I smote down the good knight, Sir

[224-033]Lamorak de Galis, at King Mark’s request; and well am I rewarded when I

[224-034]fought with the King with the Hundred Knights, and the King of Northgalis, and

[224-035]both these would have put his land in servage, and by me they were put to a

[224-036]rebuke; and well am I rewarded for the slaying of Tauleas, the mighty giant,

[224-037]and many other deeds have I done for him, and now have I my warison. And tell

[224-038]King Mark that many noble knights of the [[round-table|Table Round]] have spared the barons of

[224-039]this country for my sake. Also am I not well rewarded when I fought with the

[224-040]good knight [[sir-palomides|Sir Palomides]] and rescued Queen Isoud from him; and at that time

[224-041]King Mark said afore all his barons I should have been better rewarded. And

[224-042]forthwithal he took the sea.
