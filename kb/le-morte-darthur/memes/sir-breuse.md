# Sir Breuse Saunce Pité

Sir Breuse Saunce Pité was one of the most notorious villains of the Arthurian chronicle — a knight whose very name ("Without Pity") proclaimed his nature. Unlike the tragic antagonists of [[Le Morte d'Arthur]] such as [[Sir Mordred]] or the noble adversaries like [[Sir Palomides]], Breuse was defined by a singular quality: cowardice. He preyed upon the defenceless but fled the moment any competent knight turned to face him, earning the contempt of the entire [[Round Table]] fellowship.

## The Coward-Knight

Breuse Saunce Pité's defining characteristic was his refusal to face any opponent who might defeat him. When he encountered [[Sir Gawaine]] defending a lady, "there Sir Breuse overthrew Sir Gawaine" (228-034) — but the lady's cry for help brought [[Sir Tristram]] riding to the rescue, and Breuse immediately fled. The pattern repeated throughout his career: attack the vulnerable, retreat from the strong.

This cowardice was not merely personal failing but a violation of the chivalric code that bound the Arthurian world. A knight was expected to stand and fight; Breuse's habit of flight marked him as outside the order of true knighthood.

## Encounters with Sir Tristram

Breuse's most significant encounters came against [[Sir Tristram]] of Lyonesse, the greatest hunter and one of the finest knights of the Round Table. The chronicle records how "Sir Breuse Saunce Pité" had taken a naked sword from a lady, and Sir Tristram intervened (210-009). On another occasion, when Tristram "followed after him," Breuse fled rather than face the Cornish champion (228-038).

These encounters established a clear hierarchy: Breuse could terrorise the weak, but against a true knight of prowess, he had no recourse but his horse's speed. Malory's narrative uses Breuse as a foil — the anti-knight whose presence defines, by inversion, what true chivalry requires.

## The Flight from Sir Launcelot

The most revealing episode in Breuse's career came when he encountered [[Sir Launcelot]] du Lake. Breuse had cornered a lady and threatened to slay her, crying "Hold you nigh me" (238-052) — but the moment he saw Sir Launcelot's shield, his courage vanished entirely. "When Sir Breuse Saunce Pité saw Sir Launcelot's shield he knew it well, and fled" (238-055).

The chronicle notes that "Breuse was so well horsed that when he came to the stable door he lighted, and ran into the stable and took the first horse he found, and so he went his way" (238-058). This detail — Breuse abandoning not only the fight but even his own horse in panic — crystallised his reputation. [[Sir Launcelot]] pursued, but Breuse's instinct for self-preservation proved too quick.

## The Tournament Adventure

In his final major appearance, Breuse came upon a tournament field where a lone knight held the ground against all comers. "With this came Sir Breuse, and when he saw a knight with two swords, then he would not joust for dread" (242-073). Only the lady's reproach drove him to reluctant action — and even then, "or ever the lady might turn him Sir Breuse was gone and fled" (242-077).

The episode demonstrated that Breuse was incapable of reform. Even when circumstance offered him the chance to behave honourably, his nature reasserted itself instantly.

## The Anti-Knight

Sir Breuse Saunce Pité serves a distinct function in Malory's chronicle. He is the shadow against which true chivalry is measured — the knight who possesses arms, horse, and title but none of the moral substance that makes a knight. His cowardice is not a tragic flaw but a defining essence; he exists in the narrative to demonstrate what knighthood is not, and in doing so, he sharpens the reader's understanding of what it means to be a true knight of the [[Round Table]].
