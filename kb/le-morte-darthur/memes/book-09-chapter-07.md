[210-002]So then they took their horses and rode forth a pace after [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]]. And

[210-003]within two mile they overtook him, and saluted him, and thanked him, and the

[210-004]damosel cried Sir Launcelot mercy of her evil deed and saying: For now I know

[210-005]the flower of all knighthood is departed even between [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]] and you. For

[210-006]God knoweth, said the damosel, that I have sought you my lord, Sir Launcelot,

[210-007]and Sir Tristram long, and now I thank God I have met with you; and once at

[210-008][[camelot|Camelot]] I met with Sir Tristram, and there he rescued this black shield with

[210-009]the white hand holding a naked sword that Sir Breuse Saunce Pité had taken from

[210-010]me. Now, fair damosel, said Sir Launcelot, who told you my name? Sir, said she,

[210-011]there came a damosel from a knight that ye fought withal at the bridge, and she

[210-012]told me your name was Sir Launcelot du Lake. Blame have she then, said Sir

[210-013]Launcelot, but her lord, Sir Nerovens, hath told her. But, damosel, said Sir

[210-014]Launcelot, upon this covenant I will ride with you, so that ye will not rebuke

[210-015]this knight Sir La Cote Male Taile no more; for he is a good knight, and I

[210-016]doubt not he shall prove a noble knight, and for his sake and [[mercy|pity]] that he

[210-017]should not be destroyed I followed him to succour him in this great need. Ah,

[210-018]Jesu thank you, said the damosel, for now I will say unto you and to him both,

[210-019]I rebuked him never for no hate that I hated him, but for great love that I had

[210-020]to him. For ever I supposed that he had been too young and too tender to take

[210-021]upon him these adventures. And therefore by my will I would have driven him

[210-022]away for jealousy that I had of his life, for it may be no young knight’s

[210-023]deed that shall enchieve this adventure to the end. Pardieu, said Sir

[210-024]Launcelot, it is well said, and where ye are called the Damosel Maledisant I

[210-025]will call you the Damosel Bienpensant.

[210-026]And so they rode forth a great while unto they came to the border of the

[210-027]country of Surluse, and there they found a fair village with a strong bridge

[210-028]like a fortress. And when Sir Launcelot and they were at the bridge there stert

[210-029]forth afore them of gentlemen and yeomen many, that said: Fair lords, ye may

[210-030]not pass this bridge and this fortress because of that black shield that I see

[210-031]one of you bear, and therefore there shall not pass but one of you at once;

[210-032]therefore choose you which of you shall enter within this bridge first. Then

[210-033]Sir Launcelot proffered himself first to enter within this bridge. Sir, said La

[210-034]Cote Male Taile, I beseech you let me enter within this fortress, and if I may

[210-035]speed well I will send for you, and if it happened that I be slain, there it

[210-036]goeth. And if so be that I am a prisoner taken, then may ye rescue me. I am

[210-037]loath, said Sir Launcelot, to let you pass this passage. Sir, said La Cote Male

[210-038]Taile, I pray you let me put my body in this adventure. Now go your way, said

[210-039]Sir Launcelot, and Jesu be your speed.

[210-040]So he entered, and anon there met with him two brethren, the one hight Sir

[210-041]Plaine de Force, and the other hight Sir Plaine de Amours. And anon they met

[210-042]with Sir La Cote Male Taile; and first La Cote Male Taile smote down Plaine de

[210-043]Force, and after he smote down Plaine de Amours; and then they dressed them to

[210-044]their shields and swords, and bade La Cote Male Taile alight, and so he did;

[210-045]and there was dashing and foining with swords, and so they began to assail full

[210-046]hard La Cote Male Taile, and many great wounds they gave him upon his head, and

[210-047]upon his breast, and upon his shoulders. And as he might ever among he gave sad

[210-048]strokes again. And then the two brethren traced and traversed for to be of both

[210-049]hands of Sir La Cote Male Taile, but he by fine force and knightly [[courage|prowess]] gat

[210-050]them afore him. And then when he felt himself so wounded, then he doubled his

[210-051]strokes, and gave them so many wounds that he felled them to the earth, and

[210-052]would have slain them had they not yielded them. And right so Sir La Cote Male

[210-053]Taile took the best horse that there was of them three, and so rode forth his

[210-054]way to the other fortress and bridge; and there he met with the third brother

[210-055]whose name was Sir Plenorius, a full noble knight, and there they jousted

[210-056]together, and either smote other down, horse and man, to the earth. And then

[210-057]they avoided their horses, and dressed their shields, and drew their swords,

[210-058]and gave many sad strokes, and one while the one knight was afore on the

[210-059]bridge, and another while the other. And thus they fought two hours and more,

[210-060]and never rested. And ever Sir Launcelot and the damosel beheld them. Alas,

[210-061]said the damosel, my knight fighteth passing sore and over long. Now may ye

[210-062]see, said Sir Launcelot, that he is a noble knight, for to consider his first

[210-063]battle, and his grievous wounds; and even forthwithal so wounded as he is, it

[210-064]is marvel that he may endure this long battle with that good knight.
