# Sir Ector

Sir Ector was the foster father of [[King Arthur]] and the biological father of [[Sir Kay]]. A loyal lord in the early days of Britain, Ector's role in nurturing the young Arthur — unknowingly raising the future king — places him among the foundational figures of the Arthurian story. In later books, his son Sir Ector de Maris appears as a Knight of the [[Round Table]] and kinsman of [[Sir Launcelot]].

## The Fostering of Arthur

When [[Merlin]] orchestrated the secret fostering of the infant Arthur, Sir Ector was the trusted lord chosen for the task. Merlin "let send for the lord, Sir Ector" (006-021), and when Ector arrived he was granted great rewards by the king (006-027). The newborn Arthur was delivered to Ector, who "made an holy man to christen him, and named him Arthur" (006-032). Sir Ector's wife nourished the child with her own breasts, raising him alongside their natural son Kay (006-032).

This fostering arrangement, devised by Merlin, kept Arthur's true parentage — as son of [[Uther Pendragon]] and [[Igraine]] — hidden from the world until the moment of revelation at the sword in the stone.

## The Sword in the Stone

Ector played a pivotal role in the revelation of Arthur's identity. When the young Arthur pulled the sword from the stone, he brought it to Sir Ector and Sir Kay. Ector beheld the sword and immediately understood its significance, returning to the churchyard to verify the miracle (008-052). He asked Arthur to describe how he obtained the sword and whether any other knights had been present (008-057), and when he heard that Arthur alone had pulled it, he assayed to pull it himself but failed (008-067).

The moment of truth came when Ector told Kay to assay, and Kay pulled the sword easily from the stone (009-002) — but only because Arthur had already freed it. Finally, "Sir Ector told him all, how he was betaken to him for to nourish him" (009-008), revealing that Arthur was not his natural son but a king's child placed in his care.

## Arthur's Grief and Ector's Loyalty

When Arthur learned that Sir Ector was not his true father, he "made great dole" (009-011). Yet Ector's devotion was unwavering. He knelt before the boy who was now king, and when Arthur feared Ector would abandon him, asking only that Ector remain as his foster father, Ector replied: "I will ask no more of you but that ye will make my son, your foster brother, Sir Kay, seneschal of all your lands" (009-017). This single request — the advancement of his natural son — became the origin of Sir Kay's high office at court.

Ector continued to serve Arthur faithfully, attending the Archbishop of Canterbury's council (013-081) and bearing witness in later years to the truth of Arthur's upbringing: "Sir Ector bare witness how he nourished him" (024-027).

## Sir Ector de Maris

In the later books of [[Le Morte d'Arthur]], a different Sir Ector de Maris appears — the son of King Ban's brother and a cousin of Sir Launcelot. When this Ector learned that Sir Launcelot had gone mad with grief, he set out to find him: "When Sir Ector de Maris wist that Sir Launcelot was passed out of his mind" (110-002). He searched through forests and foreign lands, asking a forester for news of any mad knight (110-005).

This Ector came upon the Tour de la Fontaine where Sir Turquine held many knights prisoner. He beat upon the basin as custom demanded, fought Turquine, and was defeated and taken prisoner (110-018). When he saw Sir Launcelot's horse recognised in Turquine's stable, he cried "Alas, brother, said Sir Ector, where is my brother Sir Launcelot?" (110-033), revealing his kinship with the greatest knight of the Round Table.

## The Foster Father

Sir Ector represents one of the most significant supporting roles in the Arthurian origin story. Without his faithful care, the infant Arthur would not have survived to fulfil his destiny. Ector's character embodies the virtues of loyalty, discretion, and paternal devotion — raising a child not his own as if he were, and then releasing him to his greater fate without resentment. His single request on behalf of his natural son Kay shows both a father's love and a restraint that speaks to his nobility of character.
