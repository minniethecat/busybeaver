**Christening**—the sacrament of baptism—marks the entry into Christian identity in Malory's [[Le Morte d'Arthur]]. In a world where being "christened" is synonymous with being civilised, the rite of baptism serves as a threshold between the pagan and the Christian, the outsider and the member of the [[chivalry|chivalric]] community. To be christened is to be brought within the order of [[grace]] that governs [[king-arthur|Arthur]]ian knighthood.

## Christening and Identity

For Malory's knights, to be christened is fun[[lady|dame]]ntal to identity. The phrase "any [[kinsman|kin]]g or prince that is christened" (161-011) makes explicit what is everywhere assumed: the political and social order is a Christian order, and those outside it—pagans, Saracens—exist beyond the boundary of legitimate rule. Christening is not merely a personal spiritual act but a civic one, the rite that makes a person recognisable within the chivalric world.

## Christening as Narrative Threshold

The act of christening often marks a turning point in a character's story. When the infant [[Sir Tristram|Tristram]] is christened, his name itself encodes his sorrowful destiny:

> when he is christened let call him Tristram, that is as much to say as a sorrowful birth (164-039)

Here christening and naming are one act, and the name given at the font carries prophetic weight. Similarly, when a pagan knight converts and receives baptism, the christening is both a spiritual transformation and a narrative one—the point at which the character becomes legible within the Christian framework of the romance.

## Christening and Conversion

Several episodes in the Morte involve the christening of those born outside the faith. When a pagan knight or lady embraces Christianity, the baptism is often performed immediately, by a [[hermit]] or priest:

> Then the king let him anon be christened, and did do call him Sir Priamus (107-008)

The speed of the rite—"anon"—emphasises its urgency. To delay christening is to risk dying unchristened, a fate that Malory's characters regard with genuine horror. The soul's eternal condition hangs on the timely administration of the sacrament.

## The Christened World

Malory's narrative assumes a world in which christening is the norm and the unchristened are the exception—a foreign element to be converted, resisted, or pitied. This assumption shapes the ent[[wrath|ire]] moral geography of the Morte. The [[Sangreal]] quest, as the ultimate expression of Christian knighthood, is only accessible to the christened. The unchristened knight, however valiant, stands outside the order of [[grace]] that makes the quest intelligible. Christening is thus not merely a ritual but the precondition for participation in the deepest meanings of the [[king-arthur|Arthur]]ian world.
