# Queen Guenever

**Queen Guenever** is the wife of [[king-arthur|King Arthur]] and the beloved of [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]] in Thomas Malory's *Le Morte d'[[king-arthur|Arthur]]*. She occupies the emotional and symbolic center of the [[king-arthur|Arthur]]ian t[[wrath|rage]]dy — the queen whose love insp[[wrath|ire]]s the greatest knight and whose divided loyalties help destroy the [[realm|kingdom]].

## Queen and Consort

Guenever appears throughout the narrative as [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s consort and the lady of the court. When "the [[kinsman|kin]]g came to Queen Guenever, and said, [[lady|Lady]], make you ready" (068-017), she is the partner in royal ceremony. [[king-arthur|Arthur]] "had laid him to rest with his Queen Guenever" (069-005), and the domestic image of the royal couple underscores the normalcy that will be shattered. Guenever is not merely a passive figure — she exercises authority, as when a damosel "went unto Queen Guenever, and asked her leave to ride" (080-009), and the Queen responds with practical counsel: "Ye may abide, said Queen Guenever, till your brother the [[kinsman|kin]]g come" (080-010).

## Launcelot's Love

The "Nineteenth Book treateth of Queen Guenever and [[sir-launcelot|Launcelot]]" (002-128), devoting an ent[[wrath|ire]] book to their relationship. Guenever's love for [[sir-launcelot|Launcelot]] is the great fact of the Morte's second half — it motivates [[sir-launcelot|Launcelot]]'s deeds, tests his loyalty to [[king-arthur|Arthur]], and ultimately precipitates civil [[battle|war]]. Malory treats the love with sympathy if not approval, recognizing its power even as he records its consequences.

## Judgment and Penance

After the exposure of the affair, Guenever faces judgment for treason. Her fate becomes the central question around which the final t[[wrath|rage]]dy revolves. In Malory's conclusion, Guenever survives [[king-arthur|Arthur]] and ends her days in a nunnery — a penitential ret[[wrath|ire]]ment that mirrors [[sir-launcelot|Launcelot]]'s own turn to the religious life. The queen who presided over the [[round-table|Round Table]]'s golden age becomes the penitent who witnesses its aftermath.
