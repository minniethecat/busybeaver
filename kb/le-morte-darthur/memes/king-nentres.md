# King Nentres

King Nentres was the ruler of the land of Garlot and the husband of Elaine, one of [[King Arthur]]'s three half-sisters. His position at the intersection of Arthur's family and his political opponents made him a significant figure in the early consolidation of Arthur's realm. He fought against Arthur in the rebellion of the eleven kings, yet his marriage to Arthur's sister foreshadowed his eventual integration into the Arthurian order.

## Lineage and Marriage

Nentres's marriage to Elaine, daughter of [[Igraine]] and the Duke of [[Cornwall]], placed him at the heart of Arthurian kinship. Elaine was one of three daughters born to Igraine before her marriage to [[Uther Pendragon]] — the others being Margawse (who married [[King Lot]] of Orkney) and [[Morgan le Fay]] (who was sent to a nunnery and became a great sorceress). Thus "King Nentres of the land of Garlot wedded" (005-043) into the family that would produce Arthur himself.

Through this marriage, Nentres became uncle-by-marriage to [[Sir Gawaine]] and his brothers — Gawaine being the son of King Lot and Margawse. This web of kinship through the female line bound the rebel kings to Arthur even as they opposed him politically, creating the complex dynamic of family loyalty versus royal authority that runs throughout [[Le Morte d'Arthur]].

## The Rebellion

Despite his family connection to Arthur, Nentres joined the coalition of kings who rejected the young sovereign. At Arthur's coronation feast, "there came to that feast King Nentres of Garlot, with" his retinue (011-006), but this was not an act of homage — it was a display of power by a king who did not yet accept Arthur's authority. Soon after, "there swore King Nentres to bring five thousand men of" arms against Arthur (015-023), committing substantial military resources to the rebellion.

In the great battle that followed, Nentres fought in the thick of the action. He was unhorsed in combat: an opposing knight "smote him down" (017-035). [[King Lot]] himself "saw King Nentres on foot" and, in a display of the rebel kings' mutual loyalty, "ran unto" his ally and "gave King Nentres the horse, and horsed him again" (017-042 to 017-043). This moment of battlefield solidarity — one king rescuing another — illustrates the bonds that held the rebel coalition together.

After the defeat, Nentres remained defiant. "Quoth King Nentres; so said the King of the Hundred Knights" (019-070), as the surviving rebels regrouped and assessed their position. But the war was lost, and Arthur's forces drove the kings back into their own territories.

## Submission and Integration

The eventual fate of King Nentres illustrates Arthur's policy of reconciliation. Rather than destroying his defeated enemies, Arthur brought them into his realm as allies. Nentres, bound to Arthur by marriage to Elaine, was in a position to make an honourable peace. The chronicle records that he brought his forces — "King Nentres of Garlot, with four thousand" men (021-039) — now to serve Arthur rather than oppose him.

His later career is obscure in Malory's text, but his family's integration into the Arthurian world continued across generations.

## Kinship and Kingship

King Nentres occupies a liminal position in the early pages of [[Le Morte d'Arthur]] — a king who was both family and foe to Arthur. His marriage to Elaine made him part of the Pendragon kinship network, yet his participation in the rebellion of the eleven kings placed him squarely in opposition to the new order. This duality was resolved through the characteristic Arthurian pattern: defeat in battle followed by reconciliation through kinship. Nentres's eventual service to Arthur, and his integration into the Arthurian world, demonstrate the integrative power of Arthur's rule — a kingship that transformed rebels into relatives and foes into followers.
