[197-001][[king-mark|King Mark]] in despite of [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]], and how Sir Tristram was driven into a

[197-002]chapel.

[197-003]So he departed from him with Sir Driant, and by the way they met with a knight

[197-004]that was sent from [[morgan-le-fay|Morgan le Fay]] unto [[king-arthur|King Arthur]]; and this knight had a fair

[197-005]horn harnessed with gold, and the horn had such a virtue that there might no

[197-006]lady nor gentlewoman drink of that horn but if she were true to her husband,

[197-007]and if she were false she should spill all the drink, and if she were true to

[197-008]her lord she might drink peaceable. And because of [[queen-guenever|the Queen]] Guenever, and in

[197-009]the despite of [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]], this horn was sent unto King Arthur; and by force

[197-010]Sir Lamorak made that knight to tell all the cause why he bare that horn. Now

[197-011]shalt thou bear this horn, said Lamorak, unto King Mark, or else choose thou to

[197-012]die for it; for I tell thee plainly, in despite and reproof of Sir Tristram

[197-013]thou shalt bear that horn unto King Mark, his uncle, and say thou to him that I

[197-014]sent it him for to assay his lady, and if she be true to him he shall prove

[197-015]her. So the knight went his way unto King Mark, and brought him that rich horn,

[197-016]and said that Sir Lamorak sent it him, and thereto he told him the virtue of

[197-017]that horn. Then the king made Queen Isoud to drink thereof, and an hundred

[197-018]ladies, and there were but four ladies of all those that drank clean. Alas,

[197-019]said King Mark, this is a great despite, and sware a great oath that she should

[197-020]be burnt and the other ladies.

[197-021]Then the barons gathered them together, and said plainly they would not have

[197-022]those ladies burnt for an horn made by sorcery, that came from as false a

[197-023]sorceress and witch as then was living. For that horn did never good, but

[197-024]caused strife and debate, and always in her days she had been an enemy to all

[197-025]true lovers. So there were many knights made their avow, an ever they met with

[197-026]Morgan le Fay, that they would show her short courtesy. Also Sir Tristram was

[197-027]passing wroth that Sir Lamorak sent that horn unto King Mark, for well he knew

[197-028]that it was done in the despite of him. And therefore he thought to quite Sir

[197-029]Lamorak.

[197-030]Then Sir Tristram used daily and nightly to go to Queen Isoud when he might,

[197-031]and ever Sir Andred his cousin watched him night and day for to take him with

[197-032]La Beale Isoud. And so upon a night Sir Andred espied the hour and the time

[197-033]when Sir Tristram went to his lady. Then Sir Andred gat unto him twelve

[197-034]knights, and at midnight he set upon Sir Tristram secretly and suddenly and

[197-035]there Sir Tristram was taken naked abed with La Beale Isoud, and then was he

[197-036]bound hand and foot, and so was he kept until day. And then by the assent of

[197-037]King Mark, and of Sir Andred, and of some of the barons, Sir Tristram was led

[197-038]unto a chapel that stood upon the sea rocks, there for to take his judgment:

[197-039]and so he was led bounden with forty knights. And when Sir Tristram saw that

[197-040]there was none other boot but needs that he must die, then said he: Fair lords,

[197-041]remember what I have done for the country of Cornwall, and in what jeopardy I

[197-042]have been in for the weal of you all; for when I fought for the truage of

[197-043]Cornwall with Sir Marhaus, the good knight, I was promised for to be better

[197-044]rewarded, when ye all refused to take the battle; therefore, as ye be good

[197-045]gentle knights, see me not thus shamefully to die, for it is shame to all

[197-046]knighthood thus to see me die; for I dare say, said Sir Tristram, that I never

[197-047]met with no knight but I was as good as he, or better. Fie upon thee, said Sir

[197-048]Andred, false traitor that thou art, with thine avaunting; for all thy boast

[197-049]thou shalt die this day. O Andred, Andred, said Sir Tristram, thou shouldst be

[197-050]my kinsman, and now thou art to me full unfriendly, but an there were no more

[197-051]but thou and I, thou wouldst not put me to death. No! said Sir Andred, and

[197-052]therewith he drew his sword, and would have slain him.

[197-053]When Sir Tristram saw him make such countenance he looked upon both his hands

[197-054]that were fast bounden unto two knights, and suddenly he pulled them both to

[197-055]him, and unwrast his hands, and then he leapt unto his cousin, Sir Andred, and

[197-056]wrested his sword out of his hands; then he smote Sir Andred that he fell to

[197-057]the earth, and so Sir Tristram fought till that he had killed ten knights. So

[197-058]then Sir Tristram gat the chapel and kept it mightily. Then the cry was great,

[197-059]and the people drew fast unto Sir Andred, mo than an hundred. When Sir Tristram

[197-060]saw the people draw unto him, he remembered he was naked, and sperd fast the

[197-061]chapel door, and brake the bars of a window, and so he leapt out and fell upon

[197-062]the crags in the sea. And so at that time Sir Andred nor none of his fellows

[197-063]might get to him, at that time.
