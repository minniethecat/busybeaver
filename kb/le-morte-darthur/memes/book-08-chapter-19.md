[182-001]La Beale Isoud toward Ireland, and how by fortune he arrived into England.

[182-002]Then when this was done [[king-mark|King Mark]] cast always in his heart how he might destroy

[182-003][[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]]. And then he imagined in himself to send Sir Tristram into Ireland

[182-004]for La Beale Isoud. For Sir Tristram had so praised her beauty and her goodness

[182-005]that King Mark said that he would wed her, whereupon he prayed Sir Tristram to

[182-006]take his way into Ireland for him on message. And all this was done to the

[182-007]intent to slay Sir Tristram. Notwithstanding, Sir Tristram would not refuse the

[182-008]message for no danger nor peril that might fall, for the pleasure of his uncle,

[182-009]but to go he made him ready in the most goodliest wise that might be devised.

[182-010]For Sir Tristram took with him the most goodliest knights that he might find in

[182-011]the court; and they were arrayed, after the guise that was then used, in the

[182-012]goodliest manner. So Sir Tristram departed and took the sea with all his

[182-013]fellowship. And anon, as he was in the broad sea a tempest took him and his

[182-014]fellowship, and drove them back into the coast of England; and there they

[182-015]arrived fast by [[camelot|Camelot]], and full fain they were to take the land.

[182-016]And when they were landed Sir Tristram set up his pavilion upon the land of

[182-017]Camelot, and there he let hang his shield upon the pavilion. And that same day

[182-018]came two knights of [[king-arthur|King Arthur]]’s, that one was Sir Ector de Maris, and

[182-019]Sir Morganor. And they touched the shield, and bade him come out of the

[182-020]pavilion for to joust, an he would joust. Ye shall be answered, said Sir

[182-021]Tristram, an ye will tarry a little while. So he made him ready, and first he

[182-022]smote down Sir Ector de Maris, and after he smote down Sir Morganor, all with

[182-023]one spear, and sore bruised them. And when they lay upon the earth they asked

[182-024]Sir Tristram what he was, and of what country he was knight. Fair lords, said

[182-025]Sir Tristram, wit ye well that I am of Cornwall. Alas, said Sir Ector, now am I

[182-026]ashamed that ever any Cornish knight should overcome me. And then for despite

[182-027]Sir Ector put off his armour from him, and went on foot, and would not ride.
