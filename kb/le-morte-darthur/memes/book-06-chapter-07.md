[115-002]With that came [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot du Lake]], and he thrust in with his spear in the

[115-003]thickest of the press, and there he smote down with one spear five knights, and

[115-004]of four of them he brake their backs. And in that throng he smote down [[king-arthur|the King]]

[115-005]of Northgalis, and brake his thigh in that fall. All this doing of Sir

[115-006]Launcelot saw the three knights of Arthur’s. Yonder is a shrewd guest,

[115-007]said Sir Mador de la Porte, therefore have here once at him. So they

[115-008]encountered, and Sir Launcelot bare him down horse and man, so that his

[115-009]shoulder went out of lith. Now befalleth it to me to joust, said [[mordred|Mordred]], for

[115-010]Sir Mador hath a sore fall. Sir Launcelot was ware of him, and gat a great

[115-011]spear in his hand, and met him, and Sir Mordred brake a spear upon him, and Sir

[115-012]Launcelot gave him such a buffet that the arson of his saddle brake, and so he

[115-013]flew over his horse’s tail, that his helm butted into the earth a foot

[115-014]and more, that nigh his neck was broken, and there he lay long in a swoon.

[115-015]Then came in Sir Gahalantine with a great spear and Launcelot against him, with

[115-016]all their strength that they might drive, that both their spears to-brast even

[115-017]to their hands, and then they flang out with their swords and gave many a grim

[115-018]stroke. Then was Sir Launcelot wroth out of measure, and then he smote Sir

[115-019]Gahalantine on the helm that his nose brast out on blood, and ears and mouth

[115-020]both, and therewith his head hung low. And therewith his horse ran away with

[115-021]him, and he fell down to the earth. Anon therewithal Sir Launcelot gat a great

[115-022]spear in his hand, and or ever that great spear brake, he bare down to the

[115-023]earth sixteen knights, some horse and man, and some the man and not the horse,

[115-024]and there was none but that he hit surely, he bare none arms that day. And then

[115-025]he gat another great spear, and smote down twelve knights, and the most part of

[115-026]them never throve after. And then the knights of the King of Northgalis would

[115-027]joust no more. And there the gree was given to King Bagdemagus.

[115-028]So either party departed unto his own place, and Sir Launcelot rode forth with

[115-029]King Bagdemagus unto his castle, and there he had passing good cheer both with

[115-030]the king and with his daughter, and they proffered him great gifts. And on the

[115-031]morn he took his leave, and told the king that he would go and seek his brother

[115-032]Sir Lionel, that went from him when that he slept, so he took his horse, and

[115-033]betaught them all to God. And there he said unto the king’s daughter, If

[115-034]ye have need any time of my service I pray you let me have knowledge, and I

[115-035]shall not fail you as I am true knight. And so Sir Launcelot departed, and by

[115-036]adventure he came into the same forest there he was taken sleeping. And in the

[115-037]midst of a highway he met a damosel riding on a white palfrey, and there either

[115-038]saluted other. Fair damosel, said Sir Launcelot, know ye in this country any

[115-039]adventures? Sir knight, said that damosel, here are adventures near hand, an

[115-040]thou durst prove them. Why should I not prove adventures? said Sir Launcelot

[115-041]for that cause come I hither. Well, said she, thou seemest well to be a good

[115-042]knight, and if thou dare meet with a good knight, I shall bring thee where is

[115-043]the best knight, and the mightiest that ever thou found, so thou wilt tell me

[115-044]what is thy name, and what knight thou art. Damosel, as for to tell thee my

[115-045]name I take no great force; truly my name is Sir Launcelot du Lake. Sir, thou

[115-046]beseemest well, here be adventures by that fall for thee, for hereby dwelleth a

[115-047]knight that will not be overmatched for no man I know but ye overmatch him, and

[115-048]his name is Sir Turquine. And, as I understand, he hath in his prison, of

[115-049]Arthur’s court, good knights three score and four, that he hath won with

[115-050]his own hands. But when ye have done that journey ye shall promise me as ye are

[115-051]a true knight for to go with me, and to help me and other damosels that are

[115-052]distressed daily with a false knight. All your intent, damosel, and desire I

[115-053]will fulfil, so ye will bring me unto this knight. Now, fair knight, come on

[115-054]your way; and so she brought him unto the ford and the tree where hung the

[115-055]basin.

[115-056]So Sir Launcelot let his horse drink, and then he beat on the basin with the

[115-057]butt of his spear so hard with all his might till the bottom fell out, and long

[115-058]he did so, but he saw nothing. Then he rode endlong the gates of that manor

[115-059]nigh half-an-hour. And then was he ware of a great knight that drove an horse

[115-060]afore him, and overthwart the horse there lay an armed knight bound. And ever

[115-061]as they came near and near, Sir Launcelot thought he should know him. Then Sir

[115-062]Launcelot was ware that it was Sir Gaheris, [[sir-gawaine|Gawaine]]’s brother, a knight

[115-063]of the [[round-table|Table Round]]. Now, fair damosel, said Sir Launcelot, I see yonder cometh

[115-064]a knight fast bounden that is a fellow of mine, and brother he is unto Sir

[115-065]Gawaine. And at the first beginning I promise you, by the leave of God, to

[115-066]rescue that knight; but if his master sit better in the saddle I shall deliver

[115-067]all the prisoners that he hath out of danger, for I am sure he hath two

[115-068]brethren of mine prisoners with him. By that time that either had seen other,

[115-069]they gripped their spears unto them. Now, fair knight, said Sir Launcelot, put

[115-070]that wounded knight off the horse, and let him rest awhile, and let us two

[115-071]prove our strengths; for as it is informed me, thou doest and hast done great

[115-072]despite and shame unto knights of the Round Table, and therefore now defend

[115-073]thee. An thou be of the Table Round, said Turquine, I defy thee and all thy

[115-074]fellowship. That is overmuch said, said Sir Launcelot.
