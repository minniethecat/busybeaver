[128-001][[king-arthur|Arthur]]’s Court and demanded three petitions of King Arthur.

[128-002]When Arthur held his [[round-table|Round Table]] most plenour, it fortuned that he commanded

[128-003]that the high feast of Pentecost should be holden at a city and a castle, the

[128-004]which in those days was called Kynke Kenadonne, upon the sands that marched

[128-005]nigh Wales. So ever the king had a custom that at the feast of Pentecost in

[128-006]especial, afore other feasts in the year, he would not go that day to meat

[128-007]until he had heard or seen of a great marvel. And for that custom all manner of

[128-008]strange adventures came before Arthur as at that feast before all other feasts.

[128-009]And so [[sir-gawaine|Sir Gawaine]], a little to-fore noon of the day of Pentecost, espied at a

[128-010]window three men upon horseback, and a dwarf on foot, and so the three men

[128-011]alighted, and the dwarf kept their horses, and one of the three men was higher

[128-012]than the other twain by a foot and an half. Then Sir Gawaine went unto the king

[128-013]and said, Sir, go to your meat, for here at the hand come strange adventures.

[128-014]So Arthur went unto his meat with many other kings. And there were all the

[128-015]knights of the Round Table, [save] only those that were prisoners or slain at a

[128-016]recounter. Then at the high feast evermore they should be fulfilled the whole

[128-017]number of an hundred and fifty, for then was the Round Table fully complished.

[128-018]Right so came into the hall two men well beseen and richly, and upon their

[128-019]shoulders there leaned the goodliest young man and the fairest that ever they

[128-020]all saw, and he was large and long, and broad in the shoulders, and well

[128-021]visaged, and the fairest and the largest handed that ever man saw, but he fared

[128-022]as though he might not go nor bear himself but if he leaned upon their

[128-023]shoulders. Anon as Arthur saw him there was made peace and room, and right so

[128-024]they yede with him unto the high dais, without saying of any words. Then this

[128-025]much young man pulled him aback, and easily stretched up straight, saying, King

[128-026]Arthur, God you bless and all your fair fellowship, and in especial the

[128-027]fellowship of the Table Round. And for this cause I am come hither, to pray you

[128-028]and require you to give me three gifts, and they shall not be unreasonably

[128-029]asked, but that ye may worshipfully and honourably grant them me, and to you no

[128-030]great hurt nor loss. And the first don and gift I will ask now, and the other

[128-031]two gifts I will ask this day twelvemonth, wheresomever ye hold your high

[128-032]feast. Now ask, said Arthur, and ye shall have your asking.

[128-033]Now, sir, this is my petition for this feast, that ye will give me meat and

[128-034]drink sufficiently for this twelvemonth, and at that day I will ask mine other

[128-035]two gifts.

[128-036]My fair son, said Arthur, ask better, I counsel thee, for this is but a simple

[128-037]asking; for my heart giveth me to thee greatly, that thou art come of men of

[128-038][[honour|worship]], and greatly my conceit faileth me but thou shalt prove a man of right

[128-039]great worship. Sir, he said, thereof be as it be may, I have asked that I will

[128-040]ask. Well, said the king, ye shall have meat and drink enough; I never defended

[128-041]that none, neither my friend nor my foe. But what is thy name I would wit? I

[128-042]cannot tell you, said he. That is marvel, said the king, that thou knowest not

[128-043]thy name, and thou art the goodliest young man that ever I saw. Then the king

[128-044]betook him to [[sir-kay|Sir Kay]] the steward, and charged him that he should give him of

[128-045]all manner of meats and drinks of the best, and also that he had all manner of

[128-046]finding as though he were a lord’s son. That shall little need, said Sir

[128-047]Kay, to do such cost upon him; for I dare undertake he is a villain born, and

[128-048]never will make man, for an he had come of gentlemen he would have asked of you

[128-049]horse and armour, but such as he is, so he asketh. And sithen he hath no name,

[128-050]I shall give him a name that shall be [[sir-gareth|Beaumains]], that is Fair-hands, and into

[128-051]the kitchen I shall bring him, and there he shall have fat brose every day,

[128-052]that he shall be as fat by the twelvemonths’ end as a pork hog. Right so

[128-053]the two men departed and beleft him to Sir Kay, that scorned him and mocked

[128-054]him.
