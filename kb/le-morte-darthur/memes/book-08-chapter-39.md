[202-002]In the meantime there came word that Sir Nabon had made a cry that all the

[202-003]people of that isle should be at his castle the fifth day after. And the same

[202-004]day the son of Nabon should be made knight, and all the knights of that valley

[202-005]and thereabout should be there to joust, and all those of the realm of Logris

[202-006]should be there to joust with them of North Wales: and thither came five

[202-007]hundred knights, and they of the country brought thither Sir Lamorak, and Sir

[202-008][[sir-tristram|Tristram]], and Sir Kehydius, and Sir Segwarides, for they durst none otherwise

[202-009]do; and then Sir Nabon lent Sir Lamorak horse and armour at Sir Lamorak’s

[202-010]desire, and Sir Lamorak jousted and did such deeds of arms that Nabon and all

[202-011]the people said there was never knight that ever they saw do such deeds of

[202-012]arms; for, as the French book saith, he for-jousted all that were there, for

[202-013]the most part of five hundred knights, that none abode him in his saddle.

[202-014]Then Sir Nabon proffered to play with him his play: For I saw never no knight

[202-015]do so much upon a day. I will well, said Sir Lamorak, play as I may, but I am

[202-016]weary and sore bruised. And there either gat a spear, but Nabon would not

[202-017]encounter with Sir Lamorak, but smote his horse in the forehead, and so slew

[202-018]him; and then Sir Lamorak yede on foot, and turned his shield and drew his

[202-019]sword, and there began strong battle on foot. But Sir Lamorak was so sore

[202-020]bruised and short breathed, that he traced and traversed somewhat aback. Fair

[202-021]fellow, said Sir Nabon, hold thy hand and I shall show thee more courtesy than

[202-022]ever I showed knight, because I have seen this day thy noble knighthood, and

[202-023]therefore stand thou by, and I will wit whether any of thy fellows will have

[202-024]ado with me. Then when Sir Tristram heard that, he stepped forth and said:

[202-025]Nabon, lend me horse and sure armour, and I will have ado with thee. Well,

[202-026]fellow, said Sir Nabon, go thou to yonder pavilion, and arm thee of the best

[202-027]thou findest there, and I shall play a [[marvel|marvellous]] play with thee. Then said Sir

[202-028]Tristram: Look ye play well, or else peradventure I shall learn you a new play.

[202-029]That is well said, fellow, said Sir Nabon. So when Sir Tristram was armed as

[202-030]him liked best, and well shielded and sworded, he dressed to him on foot; for

[202-031]well he knew that Sir Nabon would not abide a stroke with a spear, therefore he

[202-032]would slay all knights’ horses. Now, fair fellow, Sir Nabon, let us play.

[202-033]So then they fought long on foot, tracing and traversing, smiting and foining

[202-034]long without any rest. At the last Sir Nabon prayed him to tell him his name.

[202-035]Sir Nabon, I tell thee my name is Sir Tristram de Liones, a knight of Cornwall

[202-036]under [[king-mark|King Mark]]. Thou art welcome, said Sir Nabon, for of all knights I have

[202-037]most desired to [[battle|fight]] with thee or with [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]].

[202-038]So then they went eagerly together, and Sir Tristram slew Sir Nabon, and so

[202-039]forthwith he leapt to his son, and struck off his head; and then all the

[202-040]country said they would hold of Sir Tristram. Nay, said Sir Tristram, I will

[202-041]not so; here is a worshipful knight, Sir Lamorak de Galis, that for me he shall

[202-042]be lord of this country, for he hath done here great deeds of arms. Nay, said

[202-043]Sir Lamorak, I will not be lord of this country, for I have not deserved it as

[202-044]well as ye, therefore give ye it where ye will, for I will none have. Well,

[202-045]said Sir Tristram, since ye nor I will not have it, let us give it to him that

[202-046]hath not so well deserved it. Do as ye list, said Segwarides, for the gift is

[202-047]yours, for I will none have an I had deserved it. So was it given to

[202-048]Segwarides, whereof he thanked them; and so was he lord, and worshipfully he

[202-049]did govern it. And then Sir Segwarides delivered all prisoners, and set good

[202-050]governance in that valley; and so he returned into Cornwall, and told King Mark

[202-051]and La Beale Isoud how Sir Tristram had advanced him to the Isle of Servage,

[202-052]and there he proclaimed in all Cornwall of all the adventures of these two

[202-053]knights, so was it openly known. But full woe was La Beale Isoud when she heard

[202-054]tell that Sir Tristram was wedded to Isoud la Blanche Mains.

## CHAPTER XL.How Sir Lamorak departed from Sir
Tristram, and how he met with Sir Frol, and after with Sir Launcelot.

[202-055]So turn we unto Sir Lamorak, that rode toward [[king-arthur|Arthur]]’s court, and Sir

[202-056]Tristram’s wife and Kehydius took a vessel and sailed into Brittany, unto

[202-057]King Howel, where he was welcome. And when he heard of these adventures they

[202-058]marvelled of his noble deeds. Now turn we unto Sir Lamorak, that when he was

[202-059]departed from Sir Tristram he rode out of the forest, till he came to an

[202-060]hermitage. When the hermit saw him, he asked him from whence he came. Sir, said

[202-061]Sir Lamorak, I come from this valley. Sir, said the hermit: thereof I marvel.

[202-062]For this twenty winter I saw never no knight pass this country but he was

[202-063]either slain or villainously wounded, or pass as a poor prisoner. Those ill

[202-064]customs, said Sir Lamorak, are fordone, for Sir Tristram slew your lord, Sir

[202-065]Nabon, and his son. Then was the hermit glad, and all his brethren, for he said

[202-066]there was never such a tyrant among Christian men. And therefore, said the

[202-067]hermit, this valley and franchise we will hold of Sir Tristram.

[202-068]So on the morrow Sir Lamorak departed; and as he rode he saw four knights fight

[202-069]against one, and that one knight defended him well, but at the last the four

[202-070]knights had him down. And then Sir Lamorak went betwixt them, and asked them

[202-071]why they would slay that one knight, and said it was shame, four against one.

[202-072]Thou shalt well wit, said the four knights, that he is false. That is your

[202-073]tale, said Sir Lamorak, and when I hear him also speak, I will say as ye say.

[202-074]Then said Lamorak: Ah, knight, can ye not excuse you, but that ye are a false

[202-075]knight. Sir, said he, yet can I excuse me both with my word and with my hands,

[202-076]that I will make good upon one of the best of them, my body to his body. Then

[202-077]spake they all at once: We will not jeopardy our bodies as for thee. But wit

[202-078]thou well, they said, an King Arthur were here himself, it should not lie in

[202-079]his power to save his life. That is too much said, said Sir Lamorak, but many

[202-080]speak behind a man more than they will say to his face; and because of your

[202-081]words ye shall understand that I am one of the simplest of King Arthur’s

[202-082]court; in the [[honour|worship]] of my lord now do your best, and in despite of you I

[202-083]shall rescue him. And then they lashed all at once to Sir Lamorak, but anon at

[202-084]two strokes Sir Lamorak had slain two of them, and then the other two fled. So

[202-085]then Sir Lamorak turned again to that knight, and asked him his name. Sir, he

[202-086]said, my name is Sir Frol of the Out Isles. Then he rode with Sir Lamorak and

[202-087]bare him [[fellowship|company]].

[202-088]And as they rode by the way they saw a seemly knight riding against them, and

[202-089]all in white. Ah, said Frol, yonder knight jousted late with me and smote me

[202-090]down, therefore I will joust with him. Ye shall not do so, said Sir Lamorak, by

[202-091]my counsel, an ye will tell me your quarrel, whether ye jousted at his request,

[202-092]or he at yours. Nay, said Sir Frol, I jousted with him at my request. Sir, said

[202-093]Lamorak, then will I counsel you deal no more with him, for meseemeth by his

[202-094]countenance he should be a noble knight, and no japer; for methinketh he should

[202-095]be of the [[round-table|Table Round]]. Therefore I will not spare, said Sir Frol. And then he

[202-096]cried and said: Sir knight, make thee ready to joust. That needeth not, said

[202-097]the White Knight, for I have no lust to joust with thee; but yet they feutred

[202-098]their spears, and the White Knight overthrew Sir Frol, and then he rode his way

[202-099]a soft pace. Then Sir Lamorak rode after him, and prayed him to tell him his

[202-100]name: For meseemeth ye should be of the fellowship of the Round Table. Upon a

[202-101]covenant, said he, I will tell you my name, so that ye will not discover my

[202-102]name, and also that ye will tell me yours. Then, said he, my name is Sir

[202-103]Lamorak de Galis. And my name is Sir Launcelot du Lake. Then they put up their

[202-104]swords, and kissed heartily together, and either made great joy of other. Sir,

[202-105]said Sir Lamorak, an it please you I will do you service. God defend, said

[202-106]Launcelot, that any of so noble a blood as ye be should do me service. Then he

[202-107]said: More, I am in a quest that I must do myself alone. Now God speed you,

[202-108]said Sir Lamorak, and so they departed. Then Sir Lamorak came to Sir Frol and

[202-109]horsed him again. What knight is that? said Sir Frol. Sir, he said, it is not

[202-110]for you to know, nor it is no point of my charge. Ye are the more uncourteous,

[202-111]said Sir Frol, and therefore I will depart from you. Ye may do as ye list, said

[202-112]Sir Lamorak, and yet by my company ye have saved the fairest flower of your

[202-113]garland; so they departed.

## CHAPTER XLI.How Sir Lamorak slew Sir Frol, and
of the courteous fighting with Sir Belliance his brother.

[202-114]Then within two or three days Sir Lamorak found a knight at a well sleeping,

[202-115]and his lady sat with him and waked. Right so came [[sir-gawaine|Sir Gawaine]] and took the

[202-116]knight’s lady, and set her up behind his squire. So Sir Lamorak rode

[202-117]after Sir Gawaine, and said: Sir Gawaine, turn again. And then said Sir

[202-118]Gawaine: What will ye do with me? for I am nephew unto King Arthur. Sir, said

[202-119]he, for that cause I will spare you, else that lady should abide with me, or

[202-120]else ye should joust with me. Then Sir Gawaine turned him and ran to him that

[202-121]ought the lady, with his spear, but the knight with pure might smote down Sir

[202-122]Gawaine, and took his lady with him. All this Sir Lamorak saw, and said to

[202-123]himself: But I revenge my fellow he will say of me dishonour in King

[202-124]Arthur’s court. Then Sir Lamorak returned and proffered that knight to

[202-125]joust. Sir, said he, I am ready. And there they came together with all their

[202-126]might, and there Sir Lamorak smote the knight through both sides that he fell

[202-127]to the earth dead.

[202-128]Then that lady rode to that knight’s brother that hight Belliance le

[202-129]Orgulus, that dwelt fast thereby, and then she told him how his brother was

[202-130]slain. Alas, said he, I will be revenged. And so he horsed him, and armed him,

[202-131]and within a while he overtook Sir Lamorak, and bade him: Turn and leave that

[202-132]lady, for thou and I must play a new play; for thou hast slain my brother Sir

[202-133]Frol, that was a better knight than ever wert thou. It might well be, said Sir

[202-134]Lamorak, but this day in the field I was found the better. So they rode

[202-135]together, and unhorsed other, and turned their shields, and drew their swords,

[202-136]and fought mightily as noble knights proved, by the space of two hours. So then

[202-137]Sir Belliance prayed him to tell him his name. Sir, said he, my name is Sir

[202-138]Lamorak de Galis. Ah, said Sir Belliance, thou art the man in the world that I

[202-139]most hate, for I slew my sons for thy sake, where I saved thy life, and now

[202-140]thou hast slain my brother Sir Frol. Alas, how should I be accorded with thee;

[202-141]therefore defend thee, for thou shalt die, there is none other remedy. Alas,

[202-142]said Sir Lamorak, full well me ought to know you, for ye are the man that most

[202-143]have done for me. And therewithal Sir Lamorak kneeled down, and besought him of

[202-144]grace. Arise, said Sir Belliance, or else thereas thou kneelest I shall slay

[202-145]thee. That shall not need, said Sir Lamorak, for I will yield me unto you, not

[202-146]for fear of you, nor for your strength, but your goodness maketh me full loath

[202-147]to have ado with you; wherefore I require you for God’s sake, and for the

[202-148]honour of knighthood, forgive me all that I have offended unto you. Alas, said

[202-149]Belliance, leave thy kneeling, or else I shall slay thee without mercy.

[202-150]Then they yede again unto battle, and either wounded other, that all the ground

[202-151]was bloody thereas they fought. And at the last Belliance withdrew him aback

[202-152]and set him down softly upon a little hill, for he was so faint for bleeding

[202-153]that he might not stand. Then Sir Lamorak threw his shield upon his back, and

[202-154]asked him what cheer. Well, said Sir Belliance. Ah, Sir, yet shall I show you

[202-155]favour in your mal-ease. Ah, Knight Sir Belliance, said Sir Lamorak, thou art a

[202-156]fool, for an I had had thee at such advantage as thou hast done me, I should

[202-157]slay thee; but thy gentleness is so good and so large, that I must needs

[202-158]forgive thee mine evil will. And then Sir Lamorak kneeled down, and unlaced

[202-159]first his umberere, and then his own, and then either kissed other with weeping

[202-160]tears. Then Sir Lamorak led Sir Belliance to an abbey fast by, and there Sir

[202-161]Lamorak would not depart from Belliance till he was whole. And then they sware

[202-162]together that none of them should never fight against other. So Sir Lamorak

[202-163]departed and went to [[camelot|the court of King Arthur]].
