# King Clariance

King Clariance of Northumberland was one of the rebel kings who challenged [[King Arthur]]'s right to the throne in the wars that followed Arthur's coronation. He swore to bring three thousand men of arms against the young king and fought alongside [[King Lot]], [[King Nentres]], and the other dissenting monarchs in the great battles that decided the fate of Britain.

## The Oath of Rebellion

When the kings of Britain gathered to oppose the newly crowned Arthur, Clariance was among the most committed. The chronicle records that "sware King Clariance of Northumberland he would bring three thousand men of arms" (015-013) — a pledge of substantial force that marked him as one of the rebellion's principal supporters. Northumberland was a northern kingdom of considerable strength, and its commitment lent weight to the rebel cause.

The coalition that Clariance joined included some of the most powerful lords in Britain: [[King Lot]] of Orkney, [[King Nentres]] of Garlot, King Brandegoris, and King Agwisance of Ireland. Together they represented the established order, kings who had ruled their territories before Arthur's rise and who refused to accept a boy king elevated by the wizard [[Merlin]].

## The Day of Battle

The rebel army met Arthur's forces in a great engagement that would determine the shape of British kingship. "The Duke Eustace of Cambenet and King Clariance of Northumberland" fought together on the field (017-025), their forces arrayed against the new king's loyalists. The fighting was fierce and the outcome uncertain.

In the chaos, Clariance witnessed a moment of crisis for his ally. When the Duke of Cambenet was struck down — "horse and man fell down" — "that saw King Clariance" (017-027), and the sight of his comrade's fall drove him to press harder into the fray. But the tide turned against the rebels, and Clariance himself faced overwhelming force.

## Between Two Armies

The chronicle contains an intriguing ambiguity in its naming: at one point, "King Lot smote down Clariance de la Forest" (017-046). Whether this "Clariance de la Forest" is the same King Clariance of Northumberland — struck down by his own ally in the confusion of battle — or a different knight bearing the same name is a question the text does not resolve. If the former, it suggests the chaos of the rebel coalition, where allies might fell allies in the fog of war. If the latter, it hints at a network of Clarances across the narrative, a name that echoes through the early books of [[Le Morte d'Arthur]].

## Aftermath and Submission

When the rebel cause collapsed, Clariance was among those who made their peace with Arthur. Alongside King Cradelment and the Duke of Cambenet, "the same said King Clariance" (019-072) — that is, he joined the other defeated kings in accepting Arthur's rule. Like [[King Idres]] and [[King Nentres]], Clariance survived the wars into which he had thrown his kingdom and his honour, and learned to live under the sovereignty of the king he had sworn to destroy.

## The Old Guard

King Clariance of Northumberland belongs to the gallery of rebel kings whose resistance gave Arthur's early reign its military texture. He and his fellow rebels serve an essential narrative purpose: they demonstrate that Arthur's kingship was not peacefully received but fought for blade by blade, that the [[Round Table]] arose from the ashes of an older Britain, and that the peace of Camelot was purchased with the blood of men like Clariance — kings who gambled their crowns on the old order and lost. His three thousand promised men, his field shared with Cambenet, and his final submission trace the trajectory of a world in transition, where the price of loyalty was sometimes everything.
