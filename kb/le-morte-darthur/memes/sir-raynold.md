# Sir Raynold

Sir Raynold was one of three brother-knights whose woodland encounter with [[Sir Launcelot]] du Lake forms a compact episode of combat, recognition, and knightly courtesy in the Arthurian chronicle. His brief appearance illustrates the bonds of brotherhood that structured the world of chivalric adventure.

## The Three Brothers

Sir Raynold rode in company with his two brothers, forming a fellowship of blood that the chronicle presents as a cohesive fighting unit. When the brothers encountered a lone knight in the forest, the eldest brother challenged first and was swiftly overthrown. The second brother met the same fate. Sir Raynold, as the third, was the last to ride.

The chronicle records his defiance: "Whether it be so or no, said Sir Raynold, the third brother" (120-021). His words — half challenge, half acknowledgment of what had already befallen his kin — captured the predicament of the youngest brother who must avenge the fallen or share their fate.

## The Combat in the Wood

When Sir Raynold took his turn against the stranger knight — who was [[Sir Launcelot]] du Lake in disguise — the combat was fierce: "he smote a sore stroke unto Sir Raynold, that he fell down to the earth" (120-038). The chronicle emphasises the force of the blow, and Raynold's resilience: "With that Sir Raynold began to rise" (120-040), struggling to his feet even after the devastating stroke.

[[Sir Launcelot]], recognising valour when he saw it, offered recognition rather than further punishment. "Sir Raynold, and also I know thou art a good knight" (120-043), Launcelot declared. The response was gracious: "Gramercy, said Sir Raynold, as for your goodness; and I thank you" (120-044). The encounter ended not in enmity but in mutual acknowledgment.

## Brotherly Fidelity

Sir Raynold's defining trait, beyond his strength, was his fidelity to his brothers. When the first brother fell, "arise, and help we our brother Sir Raynold" (120-035), the second brother cried — though in the confusion of combat, it was Raynold who needed rescue. And when the fighting ended, the three brothers rose together, their bond intact despite defeat.

This brotherly solidarity reflects a deeper value in the Arthurian world. Kinship was the primary bond beneath the chivalric code; before a knight owed loyalty to the [[Round Table]], he owed it to his blood. Sir Raynold and his brothers exemplify that older, more primal allegiance.

## The Third Brother

Sir Raynold's place in the chronicle is defined by his position in the sibling sequence: the third to fight, the third to fall, and the one whose resilience drew acknowledgment from the greatest knight of the age. He represents the anonymous valour of the secondary knights — those whose names appear once in the record, preserved not by great deeds but by a single moment of combat in which they proved themselves worthy of [[Sir Launcelot]]'s respect. In a world of legendary champions, Sir Raynold reminds the reader that the chivalric order rested on the courage of countless such brothers, each prepared to rise and fight again.
