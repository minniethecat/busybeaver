# Sir Sagramore le Desirous

Sir Sagramore le Desirous was a knight of the [[Round Table]] whose epithet — "le Desirous," the Desirous — marked him with a quality of eager ardour. He appeared throughout [[Le Morte d'Arthur]] as a steadfast companion of the fellowship, participating in tournaments, quests, and the defence of the Arthurian order. Though never rising to the first rank of knights alongside [[Sir Launcelot]] or [[Sir Tristram]], Sagramore was a reliable presence whose name recurs across the chronicle's span.

## The Round Table Knight

Sagramore was counted among the named knights of the Round Table, listed alongside "Sir Dodinas le Savage" and "Sir Osanna" in the roll of the fellowship (091-050). His consistent presence at tournaments and in the company of greater knights established him as a member in good standing of the chivalric order. The chronicle treats him not as a singular hero but as part of the fabric of Arthurian knighthood — the background of competent warriors against whom the greatest knights are measured.

## Tournaments and Combats

Sagramore appeared regularly in the tournament field. The chronicle records how he "encountered with him" another knight, "and either of them brake their spears unto their hands" (154-050) — the classic image of equal combat between two capable adversaries. On another occasion, he rode in company with "La Cote Male Taile, and Sir Sagramore le Desirous, and Sir Dodinas le Savage" (155-034), part of a group of knights bound together in the adventures of the chronicle.

These tournament appearances, though individually minor, collectively established Sagramore as a knight who answered the call when the fellowship gathered — a quality no less essential to the Round Table than the singular prowess of its champions.

## The Encounter with Sir Tristram

Sagramore's most dramatic moment came in confrontation with [[Sir Tristram]]. The chronicle records how "Sir Sagramore le Desirous rode after Sir Tristram" (227-032), seeking to prove himself against the great Cornish knight. The outcome was swift and decisive: "there Sir Tristram smote down Sir Sagramore le Desirous from his horse" (227-033).

This defeat was no disgrace. To be unhorsed by Sir Tristram was a fate shared by dozens of knights, many of them greater than Sagramore. The episode served the narrative's recurring purpose: demonstrating the extraordinary prowess of its central heroes through their dominance over capable but merely mortal knights like Sagramore.

## Companion and Witness

Sagramore's role in the chronicle was often that of witness and companion. He rode with [[Sir Gawaine]], [[Sir Uwaine]], and [[Sir Ector de Maris]] (126-005), part of the network of Round Table knights who populated the adventures of the chronicle. His presence at key moments anchored the narrative in the broader fellowship, reminding the reader that the Round Table was not merely a collection of singular heroes but a living community of knights.

## The Desirous Knight

The epithet "le Desirous" suggests an eagerness, an ardour — perhaps the quality of a knight who always sought the next adventure, the next tournament, the next chance to prove himself. If Sagramore never achieved the legendary status of a [[Sir Launcelot]], he nevertheless embodied a different kind of chivalric virtue: the steady, unceasing readiness to serve that sustained the [[Round Table]] through its long years of glory.
