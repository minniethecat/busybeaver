[213-001][[sir-tristram|Tristram]] by her maid Bragwaine, and of divers adventures of Sir Tristram.

[213-002]Now leave we here Sir La Cote Male Taile, and turn we unto Sir Tristram de

[213-003]Liones that was in Brittany. When La Beale Isoud understood that he was wedded

[213-004]she sent to him by her maiden Bragwaine as piteous letters as could be thought

[213-005]and made, and her conclusion was that, an it pleased Sir Tristram, that he

[213-006]would come to her court, and bring with him Isoud la Blanche Mains, and they

[213-007]should be kept as well as she herself. Then Sir Tristram called unto him Sir

[213-008]Kehydius, and asked him whether he would go with him into Cornwall secretly. He

[213-009]answered him that he was ready at all times. And then he let ordain privily a

[213-010]little vessel, and therein they went, Sir Tristram, Kehydius, Dame Bragwaine,

[213-011]and Gouvernail, Sir Tristram’s squire. So when they were in the sea a

[213-012]contrarious wind blew them on the coasts of North Wales, nigh the Castle

[213-013]Perilous. Then said Sir Tristram: Here shall ye abide me these ten days, and

[213-014]Gouvernail, my squire, with you. And if so be I come not again by that day take

[213-015]the next way into Cornwall; for in this forest are many strange adventures, as

[213-016]I have heard say, and some of them I cast me to prove or I depart. And when I

[213-017]may I shall hie me after you.

[213-018]Then Sir Tristram and Kehydius took their horses and departed from their

[213-019]fellowship. And so they rode within that forest a mile and more; and at the

[213-020]last Sir Tristram saw afore him a likely knight, armed, sitting by a well, and

[213-021]a strong mighty horse passing nigh him tied to an oak, and a man hoving and

[213-022]riding by him leading an horse laden with spears. And this knight that sat at

[213-023]the well seemed by his countenance to be passing heavy. Then Sir Tristram rode

[213-024]near him and said: Fair knight, why sit ye so drooping? ye seem to be a

[213-025]knight-errant by your arms and harness, and therefore dress you to joust with

[213-026]one of us, or with both. Therewithal that knight made no words, but took his

[213-027]shield and buckled it about his neck, and lightly he took his horse and leapt

[213-028]upon him. And then he took a great spear of his squire, and departed his way a

[213-029]furlong. Sir Kehydius asked leave of Sir Tristram to joust first. Do your best,

[213-030]said Sir Tristram. So they met together, and there Sir Kehydius had a fall, and

[213-031]was sore wounded on high above the paps. Then Sir Tristram said: Knight, that

[213-032]is well jousted, now make you ready unto me. I am ready, said the knight. And

[213-033]then that knight took a greater spear in his hand, and encountered with Sir

[213-034]Tristram, and there by great force that knight smote down Sir Tristram from his

[213-035]horse and had a great fall. Then Sir Tristram was sore ashamed, and lightly he

[213-036]avoided his horse, and put his shield afore his shoulder, and drew his sword.

[213-037]And then Sir Tristram required that knight of his knighthood to alight upon

[213-038]foot and [[battle|fight]] with him. I will well, said the knight; and so he alighted upon

[213-039]foot, and avoided his horse, and cast his shield upon his shoulder, and drew

[213-040]his sword, and there they fought a long battle together full nigh two hours.

[213-041]Then Sir Tristram said: Fair knight, hold thine hand, and tell me of whence

[213-042]thou art, and what is thy name. As for that, said the knight, I will be avised;

[213-043]but an thou wilt tell me thy name peradventure I will tell thee mine.
