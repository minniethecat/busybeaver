# Sir Lambegus

Sir Lambegus was a knight who served [[Sir Tristram|Sir Tristram de Liones]], counted among the loyal companions of the great Cornish knight. His name appears in the latter books of the chronicle, where he rode in arms, received wounds, and was numbered among Tristram's household alongside [[Gouvernail]] and Sir Sentraille de Lushon.

## In the Service of Tristram

Lambegus is first identified as "a knight of Sir Tristram" (193-024), placing him within Tristram's retinue. When a lady came to King Arthur's court seeking aid, Lambegus "rode after to rescue her" (193-001), pursuing the cause with the urgency befitting a knight of Tristram's household. He spoke directly to the king, who addressed him by name: "as I live, Sir Lambegus, I shall deserve it" (193-027), acknowledging the knight's service. Armed and mounted, "Sir Lambegus armed him, and rode after" (193-028) the quest with determination.

When asked his identity, Lambegus declared plainly: "my name is Sir Lambegus" (193-031), expressing repentance for some turn of events. His exchanges with other knights reveal a man who spoke his mind — when warned of a coming encounter, "said Lambegus, but when thou meetest with" (193-033) a formidable opponent, he did not shrink from the challenge.

## The Wound from Palamides

The most consequential event in Lambegus's recorded story is his combat with [[Sir Palamides]], the Saracen knight who was both rival and sometime-ally to Sir Tristram. The chronicle records that "Sir Palamides gave Sir Lambegus such a wound that he fell down" (193-036), striking him with such force that he was unhorsed and gravely injured.

The aftermath was severe: "Lambegus almost wounded to the death" (194-010), lying in peril of his life. Yet "on horseback, for well I wot Lambegus" (194-006) suggests he recovered sufficient strength to ride again. The wounding at Palamides's hand marks Lambegus as one of the many knights who bore the scars of the great rivalries that drove the latter books of the chronicle.

## Recovery and Return

After his wounding, Tristram did not abandon his knight. The chronicle records that Tristram sent to "fetch Sir Lambegus, his knight, from the forest" (195-005), bringing him out of the wild where he had been left after the combat. This act of retrieval speaks to the bond between lord and knight — Tristram would not leave his man to perish in the woodland.

In time, Lambegus recovered and rejoined his company. The chronicle numbers him among those who "departed, Gouvernail, and Sir Lambegus, and Sir Sentraille de Lushon" (198-003), riding out together as Tristram's trusted household. His presence alongside Gouvernail, Tristram's long-serving governor and companion, confirms Lambegus's standing as a knight of note within the Tristram circle.

## The Household Knight

Sir Lambegus belongs to that class of Arthurian knights who are not themselves protagonists but whose presence gives depth and texture to the world of the chronicle. He rode for Tristram, spoke before kings, received wounds from the great, and was fetched from the forest by his lord. His story, brief as it is in the record, embodies the loyalty and peril that defined the life of a household knight in the age of Arthur.
