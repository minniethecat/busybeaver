[233-001][[king-arthur|King Arthur]]’s party because he saw [[sir-palomides|Sir Palomides]] on that party.

[233-002]Then upon the morn Sir Palomides returned from the King of Northgalis, and rode

[233-003]to King Arthur’s side, where was King Carados, and the King of Ireland,

[233-004]and [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]]’s kin, and [[sir-gawaine|Sir Gawaine]]’s kin. So Sir Palomides

[233-005]sent the damosel unto [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]] that he sent to seek him when he was out of

[233-006]his mind in the forest, and this damosel asked Sir Tristram what he was and

[233-007]what was his name?

[233-008]As for that, said Sir Tristram, tell Sir Palomides ye shall not wit as at this

[233-009]time unto the time I have broken two spears upon him. But let him wit thus

[233-010]much, said Sir Tristram, that I am the same knight that he smote down in over

[233-011]evening at the tournament; and tell him plainly on what party that Sir

[233-012]Palomides be I will be of the contrary party.

[233-013]Sir, said the damosel, ye shall understand that Sir Palomides will be on King

[233-014]Arthur’s side, where the most noble knights of the world be. In the name

[233-015]of God, said Sir Tristram, then will I be with the King of Northgalis, because

[233-016]Sir Palomides will be on King Arthur’s side, and else I would not but for

[233-017]his sake. So when King Arthur was come they blew unto the [[battle|field]]; and then there

[233-018]began a great party, and so King Carados jousted with the King of the Hundred

[233-019]Knights, and there King Carados had a fall: then was there hurling and rushing,

[233-020]and right so came in knights of King Arthur’s, and they bare aback the

[233-021]King of Northgalis’ knights.

[233-022]Then Sir Tristram came in, and began so roughly and so bigly that there was

[233-023]none might withstand him, and thus Sir Tristram dured long. And at the last Sir

[233-024]Tristram fell among the fellowship of King Ban, and there fell upon him Sir

[233-025][[sir-bors|Bors]] de Ganis, and Sir Ector de Maris, and Sir Blamore de Ganis, and many other

[233-026]knights. And then Sir Tristram smote on the right hand and on the left hand,

[233-027]that all lords and ladies spake of his noble deeds. But at the last Sir

[233-028]Tristram should have had the worse had not the King with the Hundred Knights

[233-029]been. And then he came with his fellowship and rescued Sir Tristram, and

[233-030]brought him away from those knights that bare the shields of Cornwall. And then

[233-031]Sir Tristram saw another fellowship by themself, and there were a forty knights

[233-032]together, and [[sir-kay|Sir Kay]], the Seneschal, was their governor. Then Sir Tristram

[233-033]rode in amongst them, and there he smote down Sir Kay from his horse; and there

[233-034]he fared among those knights like a greyhound among conies.

[233-035]Then Sir Launcelot found a knight that was sore wounded upon the head. Sir,

[233-036]said Sir Launcelot, who wounded you so sore? Sir, he said, a knight that

[233-037]beareth a black shield, and I may curse the time that ever I met with him, for

[233-038]he is a devil and no man. So Sir Launcelot departed from him and thought to

[233-039]meet with Sir Tristram, and so he rode with his sword drawn in his hand to seek

[233-040]Sir Tristram; and then he espied him how he hurled here and there, and at every

[233-041]stroke Sir Tristram wellnigh smote down a knight. O mercy Jesu! said the king,

[233-042]sith the times I bare arms saw I never no knight do so [[marvel|marvellous]] deeds of

[233-043]arms. And if I should set upon this knight, said Sir Launcelot to himself, I

[233-044]did shame to myself, and therewithal Sir Launcelot put up his sword. And then

[233-045]the King with the Hundred Knights and an hundred more of North Wales set upon

[233-046]the twenty of Sir Launcelot’s kin: and they twenty knights held them ever

[233-047]together as wild swine, and none would fail other. And so when Sir Tristram

[233-048]beheld the noblesse of these twenty knights he marvelled of their good deeds,

[233-049]for he saw by their fare and by their rule that they had liefer die than avoid

[233-050]the field. Now Jesu, said Sir Tristram, well may he be valiant and full of

[233-051][[courage|prowess]] that hath such a sort of noble knights unto his kin, and full like is

[233-052]he to be a noble man that is their leader and governor. He meant it by Sir

[233-053]Launcelot du Lake. So when Sir Tristram had beholden them long he thought shame

[233-054]to see two hundred knights battering upon twenty knights. Then Sir Tristram

[233-055]rode unto the King with the Hundred Knights and said: Sir, leave your fighting

[233-056]with those twenty knights, for ye win no [[honour|worship]] of them, ye be so many and

[233-057]they so few; and wit ye well they will not out of the field I see by their

[233-058]cheer and countenance; and worship get ye none an ye slay them. Therefore leave

[233-059]your fighting with them, for I to increase my worship I will ride to the twenty

[233-060]knights and help them with all my might and power. Nay, said the King with the

[233-061]Hundred Knights, ye shall not do so; now I see your courage and courtesy I will

[233-062]withdraw my knights for your pleasure, for evermore a good knight will favour

[233-063]another, and like will draw to like.
