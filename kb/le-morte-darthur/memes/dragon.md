The **dragon** in Malory's [[Le Morte d'Arthur]] appears less as a literal monster than as a symbol—etched into the royal name Pendragon, conjured in prophetic [[dream|dreams]], and invoked as an emblem of [[king|sovereign]] power. Where the [[giant]] is flesh and iron, the dragon is [[dream|vision]] and meaning, operating in the register of [[prophecy]] and heraldry rather than d[[wrath|ire]]ct [[battle|combat]].

## The Pendragon Title

The name "Pendragon" (from Welsh *pen* "head" and *dragon* "dragon") is borne by [[King Arthur|Arthur]]'s father, [[Uther Pendragon]], and by [[king-arthur|Arthur]] himself in some [[custom|tradition]]s. In Malory it functions as a mark of royal legitimacy:

> Uther Pendragon gat the noble conqueror King Arthur (002-104)

The dragon is not a personal emblem but a dynastic one—the sign under which Britain's greatest [[kinsman|kin]]gs rule. When [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s parentage is revealed, the Pendragon name confirms his right to the throne:

> Uther Pendragon was thy father, and begat thee on Igraine (023-017)

The title thus carries the weight of both martial conquest and legitimate succession, lin[[kinsman|kin]]g the [[king-arthur|Arthur]]ian line to the dragon-[[banner|standard]] of Roman and Celtic [[king|kingship]].

## The Dragon Dream

The most sustained dragon-imagery in the Morte appears in [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s prophetic dream on the eve of battle. A dreadful dragon rises from the west, battling a boar from the east:

> him seemed that a dreadful dragon did drown much of his people (099-003)

The dream is later interpreted by a philosopher:

> the dragon that thou dreamedst of betokeneth thyself (099-021)

The dragon in this [[dream|vision]] represents [[king-arthur|Arthur]] himself—terrifying to his enemies, [[king|sovereign]] in his domain. The boar represents a tyrant (the Roman Emperor Lucius), and the dragon's victory prefigures [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s triumph. This episode fuses the heraldic dragon of the Pendragon name with the prophetic [[custom|tradition]] of royal dream-[[dream|vision]]s.

## The Dragon and the Supernatural

Dragons in Malory belong to the same category as [[griffins]] and serpents—creatures that exist at the boundary between the natural world and the realm of [[enchantment]]:

> griffins and serpents, and him thought they burnt him (022-013)

Unlike the giant, whose slaying is a concrete act of heroism, the dragon is rarely encountered as a physical foe. It operates in the symbolic order: a creature seen in [[dream|dreams]], invoked in names, or carved on [[shield|shields]]. Its power lies not in its bodily strength but in what it signifies—[[sovereign|sovereignty]], terror, and the uncanny.

## The Dragon's Narrative Function

The dragon in Malory is a **sign of [[sovereign|sovereignty]]**—the Pendragon name confers legitimacy on [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s [[king|kingship]]. It is a **vehicle of prophecy**—[[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s dragon-dream foretells the outcome of his Roman campaign. And it is a **marker of the [[marvel|supernatural]]**—a creature whose presence signals that events have moved beyond the merely martial into a realm where [[grace]] and destiny govern outcomes. In a work dominated by the clash of [[sword|swords]], the dragon reminds us that power also resides in symbols.
