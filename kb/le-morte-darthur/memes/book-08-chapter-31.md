[194-001]Palamides, and how he found him and fought with him, and by the means of Isoud

[194-002]the battle ceased.

[194-003]Now turn we unto [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]], that when he was come home and wist La Beale

[194-004]Isoud was gone with Sir Palamides, wit ye well he was wroth out of measure.

[194-005]Alas, said Sir Tristram, I am this day shamed. Then he cried to Gouvernail his

[194-006]man: Haste thee that I were armed and on horseback, for well I wot Lambegus

[194-007]hath no might nor strength to withstand Sir Palamides: alas that I have not

[194-008]been in his stead! So anon as he was armed and horsed Sir Tristram and

[194-009]Gouvernail rode after into the forest, and within a while he found his knight

[194-010]Lambegus almost wounded to the death; and Sir Tristram bare him to a forester,

[194-011]and charged him to keep him well. And then he rode forth, and there he found

[194-012]Sir Adtherp sore wounded, and he told him how the queen would have drowned

[194-013]herself had he not been, and how for her sake and love he had taken upon him to

[194-014]do battle with Sir Palamides. Where is my lady? said Sir Tristram. Sir, said

[194-015]the knight, she is sure enough within my castle, an she can hold her within it.

[194-016]Gramercy, said Sir Tristram, of thy great goodness. And so he rode till he came

[194-017]nigh to that castle; and then Sir Tristram saw where Sir Palamides sat at the

[194-018]gate sleeping, and his horse pastured fast afore him. Now go thou, Gouvernail,

[194-019]said Sir Tristram, and bid him awake, and make him ready. So Gouvernail rode

[194-020]unto him and said: Sir Palamides, arise, and take to thee thine harness. But he

[194-021]was in such a study he heard not what Gouvernail said. So Gouvernail came again

[194-022]and told Sir Tristram he slept, or else he was mad. Go thou again, said Sir

[194-023]Tristram, and bid him arise, and tell him that I am here, his mortal foe. So

[194-024]Gouvernail rode again and put upon him the butt of his spear, and said: Sir

[194-025]Palamides, make thee ready, for wit ye well Sir Tristram hoveth yonder, and

[194-026]sendeth thee word he is thy mortal foe. And therewithal Sir Palamides arose

[194-027]stilly, without words, and gat his horse, and saddled him and bridled him, and

[194-028]lightly he leapt upon, and gat his spear in his hand, and either feutred their

[194-029]spears and hurtled fast together; and there Tristram smote down Sir Palamides

[194-030]over his horse’s tail. Then lightly Sir Palamides put his shield afore

[194-031]him and drew his sword. And there began strong battle on both parts, for both

[194-032]they fought for the love of one lady, and ever she lay on the walls and beheld

[194-033]them how they fought out of measure, and either were wounded passing sore, but

[194-034]Palamides was much sorer wounded. Thus they fought tracing and traversing more

[194-035]than two hours, that well-nigh for dole and sorrow La Beale Isoud swooned.

[194-036]Alas, she said, that one I loved and yet do, and the other I love not, yet it

[194-037]were great [[mercy|pity]] that I should see Sir Palamides slain; for well I know by that

[194-038]time the end be done Sir Palamides is but a dead knight: because he is not

[194-039]christened I would be loath that he should die a Saracen. And therewithal she

[194-040]came down and besought Sir Tristram to [[battle|fight]] no more. Ah, madam, said he, what

[194-041]mean you, will ye have me shamed? Well ye know I will be ruled by you. I will

[194-042]not your dishonour, said La Beale Isoud, but I would that ye would for my sake

[194-043]spare this unhappy Saracen Palamides. Madam, said Sir Tristram, I will leave

[194-044]fighting at this time for your sake. Then she said to Sir Palamides: This shall

[194-045]be your charge, that thou shalt go out of this country while I am therein. I

[194-046]will obey your commandment, said Sir Palamides, the which is sore against my

[194-047]will. Then take thy way, said La Beale Isoud, unto [[camelot|the court of King Arthur]],

[194-048]and there recommend me unto [[queen-guenever|Queen Guenever]], and tell her that I send her word

[194-049]that there be within this land but four lovers, that is, [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot du Lake]]

[194-050]and Queen Guenever, and Sir Tristram de Liones and Queen Isoud.
