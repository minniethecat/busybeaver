# Mercy

**Mercy** is a persistent moral imperative in Thomas Malory's *Le Morte d'[[king-arthur|Arthur]]*. Knights and [[kinsman|kin]]gs are repeatedly called upon to show mercy to the defeated, the penitent, and the helpless. Yet mercy in Malory is as often denied as granted, and its absence drives the cycle of [[revenge|vengeance]] that ultimately consumes [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s [[realm|kingdom]].

## The Cry for Mercy

The plea for mercy echoes throughout the Morte. After [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s coronation, "rich and poor cried [[king-arthur|Arthur]] mercy because they had delayed him" (010-008), and the Archbishop prays "that he would of his great mercy show some [[marvel|miracle]]" (008-007). Mercy is invoked as both a personal virtue and a divine attribute — the [[kinsman|kin]]g who shows mercy mirrors God's mercy, and the [[kinsman|kin]]g who denies it invites divine judgment.

## Gramercy: Gratitude as Mercy

The archaic form "gramercy" (from *grand merci*, great thanks) appears frequently in Malory as an expression of gratitude. Knights say "Gramercy" when receiving a gift or aid (017-039, 017-055, 023-006), etymologically lin[[kinsman|kin]]g gratitude to mercy. To receive a benefit is to receive mercy, and the language of chivalric courtesy is saturated with this recognition.

## The Denial of Mercy

Mercy's power is most visible in its absence. [[battle|Battle]]s are fought "without mercy" (021-031), and the refusal to show quarter perpetuates the cycle of violence. The t[[wrath|rage]]dy of the Morte is in part a t[[wrath|rage]]dy of denied mercy — between [[king-arthur|Arthur]] and [[sir-launcelot|Launcelot]], between [[sir-gawaine|Gawaine]] and [[sir-launcelot|Launcelot]], between [[king-arthur|Arthur]] and [[mordred|Mordred]]. Had mercy been shown at any of these critical junctures, the [[realm|kingdom]] might have been saved.
