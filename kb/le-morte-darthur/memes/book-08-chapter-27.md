[190-001][[sir-tristram|Tristram]], and how Sir Tristram yielded him and promised to fellowship with

[190-002][[sir-launcelot|Launcelot]].

[190-003]Then came Sir Galahad, and [[king-arthur|the King]] with the Hundred Knights with him; and this

[190-004]Sir Galahad proffered to [[battle|fight]] with Sir Tristram hand for hand. And so they

[190-005]made them ready to go unto battle on horseback with great courage. Then Sir

[190-006]Galahad and Sir Tristram met together so hard that either bare other down,

[190-007]horse and all, to the earth. And then they avoided their horses as noble

[190-008]knights, and dressed their shields, and drew their swords with ire and rancour,

[190-009]and they lashed together many sad strokes, and one while striking, another

[190-010]while foining, tracing and traversing as noble knights; thus they fought long,

[190-011]near half a day, and either were sore wounded. At the last Sir Tristram waxed

[190-012]light and big, and doubled his strokes, and drove Sir Galahad aback on the one

[190-013]side and on the other, so that he was like to have been slain.

[190-014]With that came the King with the Hundred Knights, and all that fellowship went

[190-015]fiercely upon Sir Tristram. When Sir Tristram saw them coming upon him, then he

[190-016]wist well he might not endure. Then as a wise knight of war, he said to Sir

[190-017]Galahad, the haut prince: Sir, ye show to me no knighthood, for to suffer all

[190-018]your men to have ado with me all at once; and as meseemeth ye be a noble knight

[190-019]of your hands it is great shame to you. So God me help, said Sir Galahad, there

[190-020]is none other way but thou must yield thee to me, other else to die, said Sir

[190-021]Galahad to Sir Tristram. I will rather yield me to you than die for that is

[190-022]more for the might of your men than of your hands. And therewithal Sir Tristram

[190-023]took his own sword by the point, and put the pommel in the hand of Sir Galahad.

[190-024]Therewithal came the King with the Hundred Knights, and hard began to assail

[190-025]Sir Tristram. Let be, said Sir Galahad, be ye not so hardy to touch him, for I

[190-026]have given this knight his life. That is your shame, said the King with the

[190-027]Hundred Knights; hath he not slain your father and your mother? As for that,

[190-028]said Sir Galahad, I may not wite him greatly, for my father had him in prison,

[190-029]and enforced him to do battle with him; and my father had such a custom that

[190-030]was a shameful custom, that what knight came there to ask harbour his lady must

[190-031]needs die but if she were fairer than my mother; and if my father overcame that

[190-032]knight he must needs die. This was a shameful custom and usage, a knight for

[190-033]his harbour-asking to have such harbourage. And for this custom I would never

[190-034]draw about him. So God me help, said the King, this was a shameful custom.

[190-035]Truly, said Sir Galahad, so seemed me; and meseemed it had been great [[mercy|pity]] that

[190-036]this knight should have been slain, for I dare say he is the noblest man that

[190-037]beareth life, but if it were Sir Launcelot du Lake. Now, fair knight, said Sir

[190-038]Galahad, I require thee tell me thy name, and of whence thou art, and whither

[190-039]thou wilt. Sir, he said, my name is Sir Tristram de Liones, and from [[king-mark|King Mark]]

[190-040]of Cornwall I was sent on message unto King Anguish of Ireland, for to fetch

[190-041]his daughter to be his wife, and here she is ready to go with me into Cornwall,

[190-042]and her name is La Beale Isoud. And, Sir Tristram, said Sir Galahad, the haut

[190-043]prince, well be ye found in these marches, and so ye will promise me to go unto

[190-044]Sir Launcelot du Lake, and accompany with him, ye shall go where ye will, and

[190-045]your fair lady with you; and I shall promise you never in all my days shall

[190-046]such customs be used in this castle as have been used. Sir, said Sir Tristram,

[190-047]now I let you wit, so God me help, I weened ye had been Sir Launcelot du Lake

[190-048]when I saw you first, and therefore I dread you the more; and sir, I promise

[190-049]you, said Sir Tristram, as soon as I may I will see Sir Launcelot and in

[190-050]fellowship me with him; for of all the knights of the world I most desire his

[190-051]fellowship.
