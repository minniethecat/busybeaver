# Sir Dinas the Seneschal

Sir Dinas the Seneschal was one of the most steadfast figures in the court of [[King Mark]] of Cornwall — a loyal officer whose fidelity to his king was matched only by his friendship toward the true heroes of the Tristan cycle. Where [[King Mark]] himself wavered between gratitude and murderous jealousy, Sir Dinas remained constant: a voice of reason, a protector of the innocent, and a bridge between the troubled king and his greatest knight.

## The Seneschal of Cornwall

Sir Dinas held the office of seneschal in [[King Mark]]'s court, a position of high trust that placed him at the centre of Cornish political life. The chronicle repeatedly names him as "Sir Dinas, the Seneschal" (171-014), a title that defined his role: steward of the royal household, administrator of justice, and the king's right hand in matters of state.

His office placed him in a delicate position when the king's jealousy of [[Sir Tristram]] threatened to tear the court apart. Unlike the flatterers who fed Mark's suspicions, Sir Dinas consistently urged restraint and honour.

## The Trusted Friend

When [[Sir Tristram]] returned from exile and landed on the Cornish coast, it was to Sir Dinas that the party rode: "they were landed they rode unto Sir Dinas, the Seneschal, a trusty friend of Sir Tristram's" (219-007). This description — "trusty friend" — is no small epithet in Malory's chronicle, where betrayal and shifting allegiance are the ordinary currency of court life.

Sir Dinas's friendship extended beyond [[Sir Tristram]] to those the knight loved. When Dame Bragwaine needed escort to court, "Sir Dinas and Dame Bragwaine rode to the court" (219-008), and at a moment of emotional crisis, "Dame Bragwaine and Sir Dinas brought Sir Tristram" (219-012) to safety. His role as protector was both practical and emotional: he was the steady hand when those around him trembled.

## The Voice of Counsel

When [[King Mark]]'s barons debated how to deal with [[Sir Tristram]], Sir Dinas spoke with the authority of both office and wisdom. "Sir, said Sir Dinas, ye shall understand that Sir Tristram" (195-031) — his interventions in council marked him as a figure of independent judgment rather than mere royal instrument.

The barons respected him: "said the barons, in especial Sir Dinas" (195-028), his voice carrying special weight among the Cornish nobility. He was the conscience of the court, reminding [[King Mark]] of his obligations when passion threatened to overwhelm policy.

## The Tournament Knight

Though his fame rested on office and counsel rather than feats of arms, Sir Dinas did not shrink from the field. When [[King Mark]] called upon him to joust, "Sir Dinas made him ready, and anon they encountered together with great spears" (240-034). The outcome was honest if unglamorous: "Sir Dinas was overthrown, horse and man" (240-035) by Sir Uwaine.

His willingness to ride, even knowing his limitations, demonstrated the completeness of his knighthood. The seneschal who administered the king's justice was also the knight who took the field when duty called.

## The Conscience of Cornwall

Sir Dinas the Seneschal represents a type essential to the Arthurian chronicle: the good counsellor in a troubled court. Where [[King Mark]] embodied the flawed sovereign whose passions corrupted his rule, Sir Dinas stood for the possibility of steadfast service — a knight whose loyalty to king, friend, and justice were not forced into contradiction but held in careful balance. He is the administrator of honour, a figure whose quiet constancy makes the world of [[Le Morte d'Arthur]] habitable amid the storms of passion and betrayal.
