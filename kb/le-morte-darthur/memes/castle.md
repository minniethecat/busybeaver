# Castle

The **castle** is the defining architectural presence of Malory's [[king-arthur|Arthur]]ian world. It serves simultaneously as a military fortification, the seat of [[lordship]] and [[sovereign|sovereignty]], a place of [[prison|imprisonment]], and a narrative threshold where knights encounter tests and adventures. From the [[siege]] of Terrabil to the [[custom]] of the castle encountered by [[knight-errant|errant knight]]s, the castle structures both the geography and the narrative logic of [[Le Morte d'Arthur]].

## Castle as Fortification and Seat of War

Castles in Malory are first and foremost instruments of [[battle|war]]. They are defended, besieged, and captured throughout the narrative, and their possession determines the ba[[spear|lance]] of power between [[kinsman|kin]]gs and [[baron|lord]]s. The early books establish this pattern with the castles of the [[Duke of Cornwall]]:

> strong castles of his, of the which the one hight Terrabil... and himself he put in the castle of Terrabil, the which had many issues and posterns (004-028–030)

[[king|King]] Uther's siege of Terrabil is one of the first military actions described in the work:

> laid a siege about the castle of Terrabil. And there he pight many pavilions (004-032)

Throughout the narrative, the ta[[kinsman|kin]]g or holding of a castle signals shifts in political fortune. When [[King Arthur]] consolidates power, his [[baron|lord]]s "garnish the castles, for dread of [[king|King]] Claudas" (014-040), ma[[kinsman|kin]]g the castle the visible index of territorial control.

## Castle as Seat of Lordship

The castle is not only a military structure but the centre of [[baron|lord]]ly authority. It is where [[baron|lord]]s hold court, receive guests, and exercise [[fealty]]. The castle of Tintagil is the seat of the [[Duke of Cornwall]], and it is there that the central dynastic events of [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s conception unfold:

> Igraine in the castle of Tintagil; and ye shall be like the duke her husband (005-016)

When Uther comes to Tintagil by [[merlin|Merlin]]'s artifice, the castle's walls become both barrier and site of deception:

> or ever the king came at the castle of Tintagil (005-025)

The castle as seat anchors the feudal geography of the narrative, lin[[kinsman|kin]]g each [[baron|lord]] to a specific stronghold that defines his domain and his military capacity. The loss of a castle is the loss of [[baron|lord]]ship itself.

## Siege Warfare and the Castle

The [[siege]] is the primary form of large-scale military action in Malory, and every siege is d[[wrath|ire]]cted at a castle. The siege of Bedegraine illustrates the scale of such operations:

> eleven kings in their ways laid a siege unto the castle of Bedegraine; and so they drew a great host (015-028)

The castle of Bedegraine was "holden of [[king-arthur|King Arthur]]" (015-030), and its defence becomes a test of [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s capacity to command loyalty across his realm. The castle under siege concentrates narrative attention — the defenders within, the besiegers without, and the [[knight-errant|errant knight]] who may arrive to break the stalemate.

## The Custom of the Castle

Beyond its military function, the castle appears as a narrative device — a place where [[knight-errant|errant knight]]s encounter binding customs or tests. The "custom of the castle" is a recurring motif that transforms the castle from a military structure into a moral or judicial theatre:

> for the custom of the castle was such; who that rode by that castle must other joust or be prisoner (187-038)

This custom transforms the castle from a passive setting into an active agent in [[adventure|knightly adventure]]. The knight who approaches a castle cannot simply pass by; he must en[[challenge|gage]] with its demands, and the outcome of that en[[challenge|gage]]ment determines the shape of his [[adventure]]. In a later encounter:

> custom of the castle. What array is this? said Sir Launcelot (226-020)

The question signals the knight's recognition that he has entered a space governed by rules beyond ordinary chivalric conduct.

## The Castle's Narrative Function

The castle in Malory operates on three registers. It is a **military reality** — the hard fact of fortification that governs [[battle|war]]fare and territorial control. It is a **feudal centre** — the seat from which [[baron|lord]]ship emanates and around which loyalty gathers. And it is a **narrative threshold** — the liminal space where the [[knight-errant|errant knight]]'s journey pauses, turns, or transforms.

The castle thus embodies the fusion of power, place, and story that defines the [[king-arthur|Arthur]]ian world. Every castle is at once a building, a jurisdiction, and a stage for the [[test|testing]] of [[knighthood]].
