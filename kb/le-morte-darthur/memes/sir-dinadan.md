# Sir Dinadan

Sir Dinadan was a Knight of the [[Round Table]] known for his sharp wit, pragmatic temperament, and close companionship with [[Sir Tristram]]. Distinguished from the more solemn figures of Arthurian romance, Dinadan brought humour and self-preserving realism to the chivalric world. He was of [[Sir Launcelot]]'s blood (154-008) and served alongside [[Sir Gareth]] in the great tournaments of [[Cornwall]].

## Companion of Sir Tristram

Dinadan's most significant narrative role was as Sir Tristram's loyal companion through a long series of adventures. The two rode together through forests and hostile lands, encountering opposing knights and mysterious damsels. When they came upon a well-harboured lodge that other knights warned against, Dinadan's characteristic caution emerged: "shrewd harbour, said Sir Dinadan; lodge where ye will, for I will lodge in none other place" (226-008). Together they smote down two sure knights who challenged them (226-015), and Dinadan proved himself a capable fighter despite his reluctance for unnecessary combat.

Their partnership continued through multiple tournament circuits, and Dinadan rode with Tristram to his pavilion after each day's fighting (232-030). He also rode alongside Gouvernail, Tristram's servant (234-005), forming part of a small travelling company that moved through the Arthurian landscape.

## The Reluctant Knight

Dinadan was notable for his honest reluctance to fight when the odds were unfavourable. When confronted with a challenge, he declared "Nay, said Sir Dinadan, I will not" (225-032), preferring discretion to reckless valour. His pragmatic philosophy was memorably expressed when he exclaimed "in the devil's name, said Sir Dinadan, came I into your company" (226-024), lamenting the constant danger that followed Tristram.

When a damosel demanded he fight two knights for her, Dinadan's reply was characteristically blunt: "defend me, said Sir Dinadan, from such two knights" (226-037). Yet when pressed into combat, he performed admirably — at one tournament he "did passing well" (225-050), proving that his reluctance masked genuine martial capability.

## The Humorist Knight

Dinadan's wit set him apart in a genre dominated by earnest heroism. He made "japes" and humorous observations that lightened the tone of the narrative. When he saw Sir Tristram in a moment of vulnerability, he remarked: "here may a man prove, be a man never so good a knight, he shall have a fall" (230-026). When warned about a fearsome opponent, he maintained his scepticism: "I believe it not, said Sir Dinadan" (231-011).

His humour served a narrative function, providing moments of levity and a foil to the relentless seriousness of knights like Sir Launcelot and Sir Tristram. Dinadan spoke aloud what more courtly knights might only think, making him one of Malory's most recognisably human characters.

## Tournaments and Combat

Despite his protests, Dinadan was a frequent presence on the tournament field. He jousted against formidable opponents including [[Sir Palomides]], who smote him and his horse both to the earth (225-005). At a later encounter, "Sir Palomides encountered with Sir Dinadan, and Sir Dinadan had the worse" (226-026), leaving him so sorely bruised that Tristram had to unlace his helm and urge him to seek help (226-029).

Dinadan also served as peacemaker when needed. He and [[Sir Gaheris]] went between Tristram and Palomides to part them from further combat (226-046), showing that his value extended beyond martial prowess to the diplomatic skills needed among high-spirited knights. He was eventually overtaken by adversaries and left at a priory to recover from his wounds (227-008), a testament to the physical toll of his adventures.

## The Voice of Common Sense

In the world of [[Le Morte d'Arthur]], where knights routinely charged toward mortal danger for honour, Dinadan embodied a different kind of courage: the courage to say no. His narrative arc suggests that the [[Round Table]] needed not only those who would fight to the death but also those who could see when a fight was unwise. Dinadan represented the voice of common sense and self-preservation in a culture that otherwise celebrated glorious death. His enduring presence across many chapters testifies to Malory's skill in creating characters whose humanity transcends the conventions of romance.
