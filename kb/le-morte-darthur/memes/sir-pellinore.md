# Sir Pellinore

**Sir Pellinore** (also called King Pellinore) is one of the early knights of [[king-arthur|King Arthur]]'s court in Thomas Malory's *Le Morte d'[[king-arthur|Arthur]]*. He is the pursuer of the [[questing-beast|Questing Beast]], the father of two notable knights — [[sir-percivale|Sir Percivale]] and Sir Tor — and the central figure in a cycle of [[revenge|vengeance]] that shapes the destiny of the Orkney house.

## The Pursuit of the Questing Beast

Pellinore is bound to a singular and endless task: the pursuit of the [[questing-beast|Questing Beast]], a creature whose strange form and terrible cry embody a mystery that no knight can resolve. Merlin declares that Pellinore "must bring again the Questing Beast" (056-026), and his identity as a knight is inseparable from this pursuit. The Questing Beast chase is both Pellinore's distinction and his burden — a symbol of the futile, consuming nature of some knightly quests.

## King Pellinore at Court

Pellinore first appears in the narrative in combat with [[king-arthur|King Arthur]] himself. In their initial encounter, Pellinore unhorses Arthur, and the king "leapt unto Pellinore, and took him by the middle" in a fierce struggle (026-068). Merlin intervenes, casting "an enchantment on King Pellinore and made him to sleep" (027-001), saving Arthur's life. This episode establishes Pellinore as one of the few knights who can match the king in battle.

Pellinore's place at court is complex: he is a "good man of arms" (041-041) who serves Arthur loyally, yet his history carries a shadow. Merlin prophesies that Pellinore "shall have two sons that shall be passing good men" (027-015) — a prophecy fulfilled in [[sir-percivale|Sir Percivale]], the Grail knight, and Sir Tor, the cowherd's son made knight at Arthur's court (055-004).

## The Killing of King Lot and Its Aftermath

The defining act of Pellinore's life — and the one that seals his fate — is the slaying of King Lot of Orkney in battle (044-017). This death plants a seed of [[revenge|vengeance]] in Lot's sons, particularly [[sir-gawaine|Sir Gawaine]]. Years later, when Gawaine has been "made knight," he slays King Pellinore in revenge for his father's death (041-048), even as Pellinore was fighting on Arthur's side.

The irony runs deep: Pellinore is killed by a fellow knight of the Round Table, in an act of kin-vengeance that Gawaine himself will later condemn in others. Pellinore's death becomes one of the early fractures in the Round Table's unity, a precursor to the greater tragedy of the Morte.
