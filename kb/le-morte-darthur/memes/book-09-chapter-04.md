[207-001]an hundred knights, and how he escaped by the mean of a lady.

[207-002]And anon there came an hundred knights about him and assailed him; and when he

[207-003]saw his horse should be slain he alighted and voided his horse, and put the

[207-004]bridle under his feet, and so put him out of the gate. And when he had so done

[207-005]he hurled in among them, and dressed his back unto a lady’s chamber-wall,

[207-006]thinking himself that he had liefer die there with [[honour|worship]] than to abide the

[207-007]rebukes of the damosel Maledisant. And in the meantime as he stood and fought,

[207-008]that lady whose was the chamber went out slily at her postern, and without the

[207-009]gates she found La Cote Male Taile’s horse, and lightly she gat him by

[207-010]the bridle, and tied him to the postern. And then she went unto her chamber

[207-011]slily again for to behold how that one knight fought against an hundred

[207-012]knights. And when she had beheld him long she went to a window behind his back,

[207-013]and said: Thou knight, thou fightest wonderly well, but for all that at the

[207-014]last thou must needs die, but, an thou canst through thy mighty [[courage|prowess]], win

[207-015]unto yonder postern, for there have I fastened thy horse to abide thee: but wit

[207-016]thou well thou must think on thy worship, and think not to die, for thou mayst

[207-017]not win unto that postern without thou do nobly and mightily. When La Cote Male

[207-018]Taile heard her say so he gripped his sword in his hands, and put his shield

[207-019]fair afore him, and through the thickest press he thrulled through them. And

[207-020]when he came to the postern he found there ready four knights, and at two the

[207-021]first strokes he slew two of the knights, and the other fled; and so he won his

[207-022]horse and rode from them. And all as it was it was rehearsed in King

[207-023][[king-arthur|Arthur]]’s court, how he slew twelve knights within the Castle Orgulous;

[207-024]and so he rode on his way.

[207-025]And in the meanwhile the damosel said to [[mordred|Sir Mordred]]: I ween my foolish knight

[207-026]be either slain or taken prisoner: then were they ware where he came riding.

[207-027]And when he was come unto them he told all how he had sped and escaped in

[207-028]despite of them all: And some of the best of them will tell no tales. Thou

[207-029]liest falsely, said the damosel, that dare I make good, but as a fool and a

[207-030]dastard to all knighthood they have let thee pass. That may ye prove, said La

[207-031]Cote Male Taile. With that she sent a courier of hers, that rode alway with

[207-032]her, for to know the truth of this deed; and so he rode thither lightly, and

[207-033]asked how and in what manner that La Cote Male Taile was escaped out of the

[207-034]castle. Then all the knights cursed him, and said that he was a fiend and no

[207-035]man: For he hath slain here twelve of our best knights, and we weened unto this

[207-036]day that it had been too much for [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot du Lake]] or for [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]] de

[207-037]Liones. And in despite of us all he is departed from us and maugre our heads.

[207-038]With this answer the courier departed and came to Maledisant his lady, and told

[207-039]her all how Sir La Cote Male Taile had sped at the Castle Orgulous. Then she

[207-040]smote down her head, and said little. By my head, said Sir Mordred to the

[207-041]damosel, ye are greatly to blame so to rebuke him, for I warn you plainly he is

[207-042]a good knight, and I doubt not but he shall prove a noble knight; but as yet he

[207-043]may not yet sit sure on horseback, for he that shall be a good horseman it must

[207-044]come of usage and exercise. But when he cometh to the strokes of his sword he

[207-045]is then noble and mighty, and that saw Sir Bleoberis and [[sir-palomides|Sir Palomides]], for wit

[207-046]ye well they are wily men of arms, and anon they know when they see a young

[207-047]knight by his riding, how they are sure to give him a fall from his horse or a

[207-048]great buffet. But for the most part they will not light on foot with young

[207-049]knights, for they are wight and strongly armed. For in likewise Sir Launcelot

[207-050]du Lake, when he was first made knight, he was often put to the worse upon

[207-051]horseback, but ever upon foot he recovered his renown, and slew and defoiled

[207-052]many knights of the [[round-table|Round Table]]. And therefore the rebukes that Sir Launcelot

[207-053]did unto many knights causeth them that be men of prowess to beware; for often

[207-054]I have seen the old proved knights rebuked and slain by them that were but

[207-055]young beginners. Thus they rode sure talking by the way together.

[207-056]Here leave we off a while of this tale, and speak we of Sir Launcelot du Lake.
