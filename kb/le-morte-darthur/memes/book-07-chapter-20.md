[147-002]Then Sir [[sir-gareth|Beaumains]] put on his helm anon, and buckled his shield, and took his

[147-003]horse, and rode after him all that ever he might ride through marshes, and

[147-004]fields, and great dales, that many times his horse and he plunged over the head

[147-005]in deep mires, for he knew not the way, but took the gainest way in that

[147-006]woodness, that many times he was like to perish. And at the last him happened

[147-007]to come to a fair green way, and there he met with a poor man of the country,

[147-008]whom he saluted and asked him whether he met not with a knight upon a black

[147-009]horse and all black harness, a little dwarf sitting behind him with heavy

[147-010]cheer. Sir, said the poor man, here by me came Sir Gringamore the knight, with

[147-011]such a dwarf mourning as ye say; and therefore I rede you not follow him, for

[147-012]he is one of the periloust knights of the world, and his castle is here nigh

[147-013]hand but two mile; therefore we advise you ride not after Sir Gringamore, but

[147-014]if ye owe him good will.

[147-015]So leave we Sir Beaumains riding toward the castle, and speak we of Sir

[147-016]Gringamore and the dwarf. Anon as the dwarf was come to the castle, Dame

[147-017]Lionesse and Dame Linet her sister, asked the dwarf where was his master born,

[147-018]and of what lineage he was come. And but if thou tell me, said Dame Lionesse,

[147-019]thou shalt never escape this castle, but ever here to be prisoner. As for that,

[147-020]said the dwarf, I fear not greatly to tell his name and of what kin he is come.

[147-021]Wit you well he is a king’s son, and his mother is sister to [[king-arthur|King Arthur]],

[147-022]and he is brother to the good knight [[sir-gawaine|Sir Gawaine]], and his name is Sir Gareth of

[147-023]Orkney. And now I have told you his right name, I pray you, fair lady, let me

[147-024]go to my lord again, for he will never out of this country until that he have

[147-025]me again. And if he be angry he will do much harm or that he be stint, and work

[147-026]you wrack in this country. As for that threatening, said Sir Gringamore, be it

[147-027]as it be may, we will go to dinner. And so they washed and went to meat, and

[147-028]made them merry and well at ease, and because the Lady Lionesse of the castle

[147-029]was there, they made great joy. Truly, madam, said Linet unto her sister, well

[147-030]may he be a king’s son, for he hath many good tatches on him, for he is

[147-031]courteous and mild, and the most suffering man that ever I met withal. For I

[147-032]dare say there was never gentlewoman reviled man in so foul a manner as I have

[147-033]rebuked him; and at all times he gave me goodly and meek answers again.

[147-034]And as they sat thus talking, there came Sir Gareth in at the gate with an

[147-035]angry countenance, and his sword drawn in his hand, and cried aloud that all

[147-036]the castle might hear it, saying: Thou traitor, Sir Gringamore, deliver me my

[147-037]dwarf again, or by the faith that I owe to the order of knighthood, I shall do

[147-038]thee all the harm that I can. Then Sir Gringamore looked out at a window and

[147-039]said, Sir Gareth of Orkney, leave thy boasting words, for thou gettest not thy

[147-040]dwarf again. Thou coward knight, said Sir Gareth, bring him with thee, and come

[147-041]and do battle with me, and win him and take him. So will I do, said Sir

[147-042]Gringamore, an me list, but for all thy great words thou gettest him not. Ah!

[147-043]fair brother, said Dame Lionesse, I would he had his dwarf again, for I would

[147-044]he were not wroth, for now he hath told me all my desire I keep no more of the

[147-045]dwarf. And also, brother, he hath done much for me, and delivered me from the

[147-046]Red Knight of the Red Launds, and therefore, brother, I owe him my service

[147-047]afore all knights living. And wit ye well that I love him before all other, and

[147-048]full fain I would speak with him. But in nowise I would that he wist what I

[147-049]were, but that I were another strange lady.

[147-050]Well, said Sir Gringamore, sithen I know now your will, I will obey now unto

[147-051]him. And right therewithal he went down unto Sir Gareth, and said: Sir, I cry

[147-052]you mercy, and all that I have misdone I will amend it at your will. And

[147-053]therefore I pray you that ye would alight, and take such cheer as I can make

[147-054]you in this castle. Shall I have my dwarf? said Sir Gareth. Yea, sir, and all

[147-055]the pleasaunce that I can make you, for as soon as your dwarf told me what ye

[147-056]were and of what blood ye are come, and what noble deeds ye have done in these

[147-057]marches, then I repented of my deeds. And then Sir Gareth alighted, and there

[147-058]came his dwarf and took his horse. O my fellow, said Sir Gareth, I have had

[147-059]many adventures for thy sake. And so Sir Gringamore took him by the hand and

[147-060]led him into the hall where his own wife was.
