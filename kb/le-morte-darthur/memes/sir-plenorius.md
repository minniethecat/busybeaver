# Sir Plenorius

Sir Plenorius was a noble knight who held a tower and lands in [[Logres]]. His story, told in the Tale of Sir La Cote Male Tale, follows a familiar Arthurian pattern: a proud independent knight is defeated by a greater champion, yields, and is ultimately welcomed into the fellowship of the [[Round Table]]. Plenorius is distinguished by the grace with which he accepted defeat and the honour he showed in his subsequent service.

## The Knight of the Tower

Plenorius was introduced as a knight "whose name was Sir Plenorius, a full noble knight" (210-055). He held a tower with prisoners, as was common among the independent knights of the Arthurian landscape — knights who were not villains but who maintained their own domains and customs until brought into the larger order of [[King Arthur]]'s realm.

When Sir Launcelot, travelling incognito as the champion of Sir La Cote Male Tale, encountered Plenorius, a battle ensued that would change Plenorius's fate. After hard combat, Sir Launcelot "sent him grovelling down" (211-034), and Plenorius yielded. His yielding was remarkable for its completeness: he gave not only his person but "his tower, and all his livelihood" (211-034), surrendering his entire worldly estate to the victor.

## A Curious Encounter

After his defeat, Plenorius displayed a disarmingly human curiosity. Finding himself in the company of Sir Launcelot, who had not revealed his true identity, Plenorius sensed that this was no ordinary knight. He asked directly about the nature of his conqueror: "Why, said Plenorius, is there another manner knight than I am?" (211-013). When told there was indeed a greater knight, he pressed further: "What is his name? said Plenorius. Ye shall not know for me, said Sir Launcelot" (211-015).

This exchange reveals Plenorius as a man of honest self-assessment. Rather than nursing wounded pride, he sought to understand the nature of the force that had overcome him — a response more philosophical than many defeated knights in Malory's work.

## Launcelot's Generosity

The encounter between Plenorius and [[Sir Launcelot]] reached its most remarkable moment after the surrender. Though Plenorius had yielded everything, Launcelot declined to take his livelihood: "I will not have Sir Plenorius' livelihood" (211-045). Instead, Launcelot undertook to recommend him for the Round Table: "Sir Plenorius, I will undertake, said Sir Launcelot" (211-050) to have him made a Knight of the Round Table "at the next feast of Pentecost" (211-052).

This act of generosity — defeating a knight decisively and then raising him up — exemplifies the Round Table's ideal of converting adversaries into allies. Plenorius, for his part, accepted this elevation with the same grace he had shown in defeat.

## Service at the Round Table

Plenorius proved worthy of Launcelot's recommendation. At a subsequent tournament, "there was Sir Plenorius and Sir La Cote Male Tale" (212-012), and the chronicle records that "Sir Plenorius proved a noble knight and full" (212-018). His "brethren were ever knights of the Round Table" (212-020), suggesting a family tradition of service to the crown.

Through his defeat, yielding, and subsequent elevation, Plenorius embodies the Arthurian ideal of redemption through combat — the belief that a worthy opponent, once overcome, could become a worthy ally.

## The Grace of Defeat

Sir Plenorius represents a type rarely celebrated but essential to the Arthurian world: the knight who loses well. His defeat by [[Sir Launcelot]] became not a humiliation but a transformation — from an independent lord of a single tower to a Knight of the [[Round Table]], bound to the greatest fellowship in romance. In a genre where defeat often means death or exile, Plenorius's story offers a gentler vision: that yielding to a greater power, when done with honour, can be the beginning rather than the end of a knight's story.
