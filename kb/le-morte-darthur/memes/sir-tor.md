# Sir Tor

Sir Tor is the son of [[King Pellinore]] and a knight of the [[Round Table]] whose reputation rests not on spectacular deeds but on a quality rarer among Arthur's company: steady, unboastful competence. When his father presents him alongside [[Sir Bagdemagus]] for a seat at the Round Table, [[King Arthur]] himself delivers the judgment that defines Tor's character: "he saith little and he doth much more" (071-009). His principal recorded adventure — the quest of the white brachet — demonstrates the methodical efficiency and tempered ferocity that make him, in his father's careful estimation, a knight "of better conditions and loath to do any wrong, and loath to take any wrong" (071-006).

## The White Brachet Quest

Tor's most fully chronicled exploit begins at [[King Arthur]]'s wedding feast to [[Gwenyvere]], when a white brachet (a small hunting hound) enters the hall pursued by a knight who seizes it and rides away. Arthur assigns the quest to recover the brachet to Sir Tor — his first major commission as a newly made knight.

Riding in pursuit, Tor encounters a dwarf who bars his passage and demands he joust with two pavilion knights before proceeding. Though Tor protests that "I am in a quest that I must needs follow" (059-008), the dwarf blows his horn and summons the first challenger. Tor unhorses him in a single pass: "Tor bare him from his horse" (059-011). A second knight charges "with great raundon" and strikes Tor's shield so hard that "his spear all to-shivered," but Tor's answering stroke drives through the knight's shield and "went through the cost of the knight" (059-017). Both defeated knights — Sir Felot of Langduk and Sir Petipase of Winchelsea — yield and are sent as prisoners to King Arthur's court (059-021). The dwarf, impressed, asks to enter Tor's service, declaring "I will serve no more recreant knights" (059-029), and guides him onward.

At the white pavilion, Tor finds the brachet with a sleeping lady. He "took her by force and took her to the dwarf" (060-007), fulfilling the material object of his quest. The lady warns that he shall not go far unchallenged, and the next day a knight named Abelleus overtakes them, demanding the brachet's return. The ensuing combat is Tor's most severe test: "they smote through the shields, that the cantels fell off both parties" and "the hot blood ran to the earth" (060-028). But "Sir Tor espied that the other knight fainted, and then he sued fast upon him, and doubled his strokes, and gart him go to the earth" (060-031). When Abelleus refuses to yield without the brachet, Tor stands firm: "it was my quest to bring again thy brachet, thee, or both" (060-035). He returns to Camelot victorious, the brachet recovered and prisoners sent to swell Arthur's court.

## The Round Table Seat

When a vacancy opens at the Round Table, King Pellinore presents two candidates: his son Sir Tor and the more celebrated Sir Bagdemagus. Pellinore is scrupulous in his paternal restraint: "because Sir Tor is my son I may not praise him, but else, an he were not my son, I durst say that of his age there is not in this land a better knight than he is, nor of better conditions" (071-004). Arthur, who has observed Tor's performance on the brachet quest, confirms the judgment with a phrase that becomes Tor's epitaph: "he saith little and he doth much more, for I know none in all this court an he were as well born on his mother's side as he is on your side, that is like him of prowess and of might" (071-009). Tor is seated at the Round Table, and Sir Bagdemagus — "wonderly wroth" at being passed over — departs the court in anger, setting in motion his own long arc of knightly proving (071-015).
