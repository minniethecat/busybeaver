# Sir Constantine

Sir Constantine of Cornwall is a knight of the [[Round Table]] and the son of [[Sir Cador]] of Cornwall. He occupies a singular position in Arthurian history as the man [[King Arthur]] publicly designated as his heir before departing for the Roman campaign, and who ultimately succeeded Arthur as king of Britain after the final battle.

## Heir to the Realm

As King Arthur prepared his great expedition against [[Emperor Lucius]] of Rome, he convened a parliament at York to organize both his army and the governance of the realm during his absence. In a deliberate act of succession planning, Arthur appointed two governors: [[Sir Baudwin]] of Britain, charged with counsel, and Sir Constantine, son of Sir Cador of Cornwall (098-008). Arthur explicitly tied Constantine to the royal bloodline, describing him as next of his blood and therefore the rightful successor should Arthur perish abroad.

The gravity of this appointment was underscored by the public nature of the declaration. Before all his assembled lords, Arthur resigned the rule of the realm and the care of [[Queen Guenever]] to Baudwin and Constantine, a gesture that notably angered [[Sir Launcelot]] (098-010). Arthur then mounted his horse and proclaimed before the assembled host: "If I die in this journey I will that Sir Constantine be mine heir and king crowned of this realm as next of my blood" (098-017).

## The Roman Campaign

During the Roman campaign, Constantine remained in Britain as co-governor with Sir Baudwin, charged with maintaining the kingdom's stability while Arthur and the greater part of the chivalry fought on the continent. This role kept Constantine distant from the battlefield glory that defined many of his contemporaries, yet it placed him at the centre of political authority at a critical juncture.

Constantine's appointment came at a moment of considerable tension at court. Sir Launcelot's displeasure at the arrangement — attributed in the chronicle to his concern over [[Sir Tristram]] being left with [[King Mark]] — hinted at the fractures that would later widen into civil war (098-011).

## Kingship After Arthur

Upon King Arthur's death at the Battle of Salisbury, Constantine succeeded to the throne as Arthur had ordained. The chronicle presents this succession as the fulfillment of Arthur's public will at York, making Constantine the legitimate successor in an era marked by contested claims and fractured loyalties. His reign bridged the end of the Arthurian age proper and the uncertain aftermath that followed the dissolution of the Round Table fellowship.

## The Country of Constantine

A geographic footnote links Constantine's name to a region beside Brittany, where a marvellous giant terrorised the countryside during Arthur's Roman expedition (098-CHV). When news of this giant reached Arthur's army, the king himself undertook to destroy the monster in single combat, a deed that became one of the celebrated episodes of the continental campaign. The coincidence of names between the knight and the country is noted in the chronicle without further elaboration.
