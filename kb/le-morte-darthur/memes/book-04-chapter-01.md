[067-003]So after these quests of [[sir-gawaine|Sir Gawaine]], Sir Tor, and King Pellinore, it fell so

[067-004]that [[merlin|Merlin]] fell in a dotage on the damosel that King Pellinore brought to

[067-005]court, and she was one of the damosels of the lake, that hight [[lady-of-the-lake|Nimue]]. But

[067-006]Merlin would let her have no rest, but always he would be with her. And ever

[067-007]she made Merlin good cheer till she had learned of him all manner thing that

[067-008]she desired; and he was assotted upon her, that he might not be from her. So on

[067-009]a time he told [[king-arthur|King Arthur]] that he should not dure long, but for all his crafts

[067-010]he should be put in the earth quick. And so he told the king many things that

[067-011]should befall, but always he warned the king to keep well his sword and the

[067-012]scabbard, for he told him how the sword and the scabbard should be stolen by a

[067-013]woman from him that he most trusted. Also he told King Arthur that he should

[067-014]miss him,—Yet had ye liefer than all your lands to have me again. Ah,

[067-015]said the king, since ye know of your adventure, purvey for it, and put away by

[067-016]your crafts that misadventure. Nay, said Merlin, it will not be; so he departed

[067-017]from the king. And within a while the Damosel of the Lake departed, and Merlin

[067-018]went with her evermore wheresomever she went. And ofttimes Merlin would have

[067-019]had her privily away by his subtle crafts; then she made him to swear that he

[067-020]should never do none enchantment upon her if he would have his will. And so he

[067-021]sware; so she and Merlin went over the sea unto the land of Benwick, whereas

[067-022]King Ban was king that had great [[battle|war]] against King Claudas, and there Merlin

[067-023]spake with King Ban’s wife, a fair lady and a good, and her name was

[067-024]Elaine, and there he saw young [[sir-launcelot|Launcelot]]. There the queen made great sorrow for

[067-025]the mortal war that King Claudas made on her lord and on her lands. Take none

[067-026]heaviness, said Merlin, for this same child within this twenty year shall

[067-027]revenge you on King Claudas, that all Christendom shall speak of it; and this

[067-028]same child shall be the most man of [[honour|worship]] of the world, and his first name is

[067-029]Galahad, that know I well, said Merlin, and since ye have confirmed him

[067-030]Launcelot. That is truth, said the queen, his first name was Galahad. O Merlin,

[067-031]said the queen, shall I live to see my son such a man of [[courage|prowess]]? Yea, lady, on

[067-032]my peril ye shall see it, and live many winters after.

[067-033]And so, soon after, the lady and Merlin departed, and by the way Merlin showed

[067-034]her many wonders, and came into Cornwall. And always Merlin lay about the lady

[067-035]to have her maidenhood, and she was ever passing weary of him, and fain would

[067-036]have been delivered of him, for she was afeard of him because he was a

[067-037]devil’s son, and she could not beskift him by no mean. And so on a time

[067-038]it happed that Merlin showed to her in a rock whereas was a great [[marvel|wonder]], and

[067-039]wrought by enchantment, that went under a great stone. So by her subtle working

[067-040]she made Merlin to go under that stone to let her wit of the marvels there; but

[067-041]she wrought so there for him that he came never out for all the craft he could

[067-042]do. And so she departed and left Merlin.
