# Sir Priamus

Sir Priamus was a noble Saracen knight who encountered [[Sir Gawaine]] during [[King Arthur]]'s wars against the Romans, converted to Christianity, and was rewarded with the duchy of Lorraine and a seat at the [[Round Table]]. His story is one of the most fully realised conversion narratives in [[Le Morte d'Arthur]], tracing a complete arc from enemy warrior to Christian duke and honoured companion.

## The Encounter with Sir Gawaine

The meeting between Priamus and Gawaine began in blood. In the aftermath of a skirmish during the Roman campaign, Gawaine happened upon a knight who declared himself: "my name is Priamus, and a great prince is my father" (105-022). The two fought fiercely, and "then Sir Priamus and Sir Gawaine alighted, and let their horses graze" (105-061) as the combat exhausted them both, each man recognising in the other a worthy opponent.

What began as enmity quickly turned to respect. Priamus, though a Saracen, displayed all the courtly graces of a Christian knight, and Gawaine saw in him a nobility that transcended the boundaries of faith and nation.

## The Vial of the Four Waters

The most remarkable detail of the Priamus episode is the healing object he carried. "Priamus took from his page a vial full of the four waters that came out of Paradise" (105-063) — a relic of extraordinary power that could heal wounds and restore strength. This vial marked Priamus as a figure touched by grace even before his formal conversion, as though the waters of Paradise themselves foreshadowed the baptism that awaited him.

The vial's origin — the four rivers of Eden — placed Priamus within a cosmic geography that linked the Arthurian world to biblical history. That a Saracen knight should bear such a relic suggested a divine purpose at work in his encounter with Gawaine, a providence that would lead him from the armies of Rome to the fellowship of the Round Table.

## Christening and Transformation

After the Roman campaign concluded, Priamus chose to accept baptism. Arthur himself "did call him his first name Priamus, and made him a duke and knight of the Table Round" (107-009). The king honoured the name Priamus had borne as a Saracen — the same name the chronicle first recorded — rather than imposing a new Christian name upon him. This retention of identity through conversion speaks to the particular respect Arthur held for this former enemy.

The transformation was complete: a Saracen prince became a Christian duke, an enemy warrior became a knight of the Round Table. Priamus embodied the Arthurian ideal that nobility of character transcended origin, and that the fellowship of the Round Table could absorb even those who had once fought against it.

## The Duke of Lorraine

As his reward for service, Arthur granted "to Sir Priamus the duchy of Lorraine" (107-057). This was no small gift — Lorraine was a territory of consequence, and the grant elevated Priamus from a foreign knight to a lord with lands and responsibilities within Arthur's empire. Priamus "thanked him, and said he would serve him" (107-057), pledging his fealty in the language of feudal obligation.

The duchy represented the material confirmation of Priamus's spiritual transformation. As Christendom had claimed his soul, so Arthur claimed his loyalty; the two conversions, sacred and secular, were woven together in the tapestry of his new life.

## The Saracen at the Round Table

Sir Priamus occupies a distinctive place in the Arthurian world. He is a Saracen knight whose conversion is not forced but chosen, whose nobility is recognised before his baptism, and whose service is rewarded with the highest honours Arthur can bestow. In a chronicle where Saracens most often appear as enemies to be slain, Priamus stands as an exception — proof that the [[Round Table]] could embrace those from beyond Christendom who proved themselves worthy by the shared code of arms and honour. His vial of Paradise waters, his duke's coronet, and his seat at the Table together map the journey from pagan prince to Christian knight in a single, elegant arc.
