# Banner

The **banner** in [[Le Morte d'Arthur]] is the knight's public face — a coloured [[battle|field]] bearing his [[arms]] that declares his identity before he speaks a word. In the chaos of battle, the banner is the rallying point that holds an army together; in the [[tournament]], it marks the arrival of a known challenger; and when a banner is lowered or captured, it signals a defeat more final than any wound. The banner is the material form of [[honour]] itself.

## The Banner in Battle

When armies meet, the banner is the first thing seen and the last thing yielded. The display of banners marks the moment when skirmishing becomes pitched battle:

> advanced banners and smote together and all they... (041-030)

A commander "displays" his banner to signal that the army is formed for battle and ready to en[[challenge|gage]]. [[King Arthur]] himself is described at the moment of supreme crisis:

> Arthur was embattled and his banner displayed; and he was beset round... (103-019)

To be "beset round" with banner displayed is the defining image of a [[kinsman|kin]]g at [[battle|war]] — surrounded but visible, the banner holding the army's centre.

## The Banner as Heraldic Identity

Every knight of standing bears arms, and those arms are displayed upon his banner. The banner is the visual shorthand for the knight himself. After battle, the [[battle|field]] is read by what banners remain standing:

> their shields with their arms and banners (103-064)

The banner links the anonymous [[arms|armour]]ed figure to a specific name, [[blood|lineage]], and [[honour|reputation]]. In a world where identity is deliberately concealed — as when knights ride incognito — the absence of a banner or the display of a borrowed banner is itself a meaningful signal.

## The Banner on City Walls

The banner is not only a battle[[battle|field]] device but a political proclamation. When a city is taken, the victor sets his banner upon the walls:

> set upon the walls the king's banner. Then came the king (107-030)

The [[kinsman|kin]]g sees his own banner from afar and knows the city is his:

> and saw the city and his banner on the walls, by which he knew... (107-031)

The banner on the walls is the simplest and most definitive declaration of [[baron|lord]]ship. No herald need cry it; the banner speaks for itself.

## The Black Banner

Not all banners proclaim triumph. [[Sir Tristram]] encounters a ship bearing a sombre device:

> thereon hung a black banner, and on the other... (133-032)

The black banner signals grief, [[shame|disgrace]], or death — a visual language understood by every character in the work. A banner's colour and charge are not decorative but communicative, part of a [[heraldry|heraldic]] grammar that every knight learns from childhood.

## The Banner's Narrative Function

The banner in Malory functions as a narrative shortcut. A banner glimpsed on the horizon announces who approaches before the rider is in sight. A banner struck down signals the fall of its bearer. A banner raised on a [[castle|tower]] tells the whole story of a siege's outcome in a single image. In a work where so much turns on who is who, the banner is the answer to the question before it is asked.
