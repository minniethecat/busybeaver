# Dame Nyneve

Dame Nyneve, also called Nimue, is one of the damosels of the lake and the figure who becomes the [[Lady of the Lake]] after the death of her predecessor. Introduced to [[King Arthur]]'s court by [[King Pellinore]], she endures the unwanted advances of [[Merlin]], learns all his crafts of enchantment, and ultimately imprisons him under a stone — an act that removes the kingdom's greatest prophetic counselor but establishes her as an independent magical power. She later uses her arts to rescue the knight [[Sir Pelleas]] from love-madness and to save King Arthur from assassination in the Forest Perilous, proving herself one of the realm's most capable protectors.

## The Damosel of the Lake

Nyneve first appears at King Arthur's court after the quests of [[Sir Gawaine]], [[Sir Tor]], and King Pellinore. She is brought to court by King Pellinore and is identified as "one of the damosels of the lake, that hight Nimue" (067-005). Her connection to the lake places her within the enchanted sisterhood that earlier produced the first Lady of the Lake — the figure who bestowed [[Excalibur]] upon Arthur and was later beheaded by [[Sir Balin]] in the king's own hall. Unlike her predecessor, whose death brought shame to Arthur's court (034-042), Nyneve navigates the dangerous intersection of feminine enchantment and masculine chivalry with lasting success.

## Merlin's Downfall

Upon seeing Nyneve at court, Merlin falls into a consuming infatuation. "Merlin would let her have no rest, but always he would be with her" (067-006). The great enchanter, who has orchestrated Arthur's rise and guided the kingdom through prophecy and craft, becomes utterly bound to her presence. Nyneve, however, is not a passive object of his desire. She makes him "good cheer till she had learned of him all manner thing that she desired" (067-007), systematically extracting his magical knowledge while enduring his pursuit.

Merlin himself foresees his fate. He tells King Arthur "he should not dure long, but for all his crafts he should be put in the earth quick" (067-009), and warns Arthur to guard Excalibur and its scabbard, for "the sword and the scabbard should be stolen by a woman from him that he most trusted" (067-013). Despite this foreknowledge, Merlin cannot resist his own doom.

The end comes in Cornwall. After crossing the sea to Benwick — where Merlin prophesies the greatness of the infant [[Sir Launcelot]] (067-026) — the pair travel through Cornwall. Nyneve, "ever passing weary of him" and afraid of Merlin "because he was a devil's son" (067-036), seizes her opportunity when Merlin shows her a great wonder "wrought by enchantment, that went under a great stone" (067-039). "By her subtle working she made Merlin to go under that stone to let her wit of the marvels there; but she wrought so there for him that he came never out for all the craft he could do" (067-040). She departs, leaving Merlin entombed alive beneath the rock, where [[Sir Bagdemagus]] later hears him "make great dole" but cannot free him — for "he might never be holpen but by her that put him there" (071-031).

## The Rescue of Sir Pelleas

Nyneve next appears in the tragedy of [[Sir Pelleas]] and the Lady Ettard. After [[Sir Gawaine]] betrays Pelleas by lying with Ettard — the very lady he swore to win for Pelleas — the heartbroken knight retreats to his bed, vowing never to rise until death. The Damosel of the Lake encounters one of Pelleas's knights wandering the forest "making great dole" (088-069) and, learning of the betrayal, declares: "I will warrant his life he shall not die for love, and she that hath caused him so to love, she shall be in as evil plight as he is" (088-072).

Nyneve's intervention is precise and morally symmetrical. She casts an enchantment upon the sleeping Pelleas, then brings Ettard to him and throws "such an enchantment upon her that she loved him sore, that well-nigh she was out of her mind" (088-081). The reversal is complete: Ettard, who once "could never be quit of him" (088-014), now loves the man she most hated, while Pelleas, upon waking, hates Ettard "more than any woman alive" (088-086). Nyneve frames this as divine justice: "That is the righteous judgment of God" (088-084). Pelleas and Nyneve depart together, and she becomes his companion thereafter.

## Guardian of King Arthur

Nyneve's most decisive intervention comes late in Arthur's reign, in the Forest Perilous of North Wales. The sorceress Lady Annowre, having failed to seduce Arthur, conspires to have him slain. The Lady of the Lake, "that was alway friendly to King Arthur" (218-016), "understood by her subtle crafts that King Arthur was like to be destroyed" (218-017). Knowing that Arthur would perish that day without the aid of either Sir Launcelot or [[Sir Tristram]], she rides through the forest until she finds Tristram.

Her warning is urgent: "this same day, and within these two hours, shall be done the foulest deed that ever was done in this land" (218-024). Tristram follows her to a castle where Arthur fights alone against two knights while Annowre holds Excalibur, prepared to strike off the king's head. Sir Tristram slays both knights, and as Annowre attempts to flee, "the Lady of the Lake cried to King Arthur: Let not that false lady escape" (218-040). Arthur overtakes Annowre and strikes off her head, and "the Lady of the Lake took up her head and hung it up by the hair of her saddle-bow" (218-042) — a grim trophy that marks her complete transition from the maiden who once fled Merlin's advances to an assertive agent in the realm's most dangerous affairs.

## The Succession of Enchantment

Nyneve's story traces a distinctive arc in the [[Le Morte d'Arthur]] narrative: from hunted maiden to independent enchantress to guardian of the realm. Where Merlin's magic serves prophecy and manipulation — guiding kings, arranging conceptions, engineering outcomes — Nyneve's enchantment operates through just reversal. She turns Merlin's own lust into his undoing, Ettard's cruelty into its mirror, and Annowre's treachery into her execution. Her epithet "the Lady of the Lake" links her to the enchanted sisterhood that gave Arthur his sword, but her career outlasts both her predecessor and Merlin himself, making her one of the few magical figures in the Arthurian world who survives and thrives through cunning rather than combat.
