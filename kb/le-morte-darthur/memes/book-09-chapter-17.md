[220-001]Tintagil, and how he sorrowed and was so long in a forest till he was out of

[220-002]his mind.

[220-003]Then [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]] dread sore lest he were discovered unto the king that he was

[220-004]there; wherefore he drew him to the strength of the Tower, and armed him in

[220-005]such armour as he had for to [[battle|fight]] with them that would withstand him. And so

[220-006]when Sir Tristram saw there was no resistance against him he sent Gouvernail

[220-007]for his horse and his spear, and knightly he rode forth out of the castle

[220-008]openly, that was called the Castle of Tintagil. And even at gate he met with

[220-009]Gingalin, [[sir-gawaine|Sir Gawaine]]’s son. And anon Sir Gingalin put his spear in his

[220-010]rest, and ran upon Sir Tristram and brake his spear; and Sir Tristram at that

[220-011]time had but a sword, and gave him such a buffet upon the helm that he fell

[220-012]down from his saddle, and his sword slid adown, and carved asunder his

[220-013]horse’s neck. And so Sir Tristram rode his way into the forest, and all

[220-014]this doing saw [[king-mark|King Mark]]. And then he sent a squire unto the hurt knight, and

[220-015]commanded him to come to him, and so he did. And when King Mark wist that it

[220-016]was Sir Gingalin he welcomed him and gave him an horse, and asked him what

[220-017]knight it was that had encountered with him. Sir, said Gingalin, I wot not what

[220-018]knight he was, but well I wot that he sigheth and maketh great dole.

[220-019]Then Sir Tristram within a while met with a knight of his own, that hight Sir

[220-020]Fergus. And when he had met with him he made great sorrow, insomuch that he

[220-021]fell down off his horse in a swoon, and in such sorrow he was in three days and

[220-022]three nights. Then at the last Sir Tristram sent unto the court by Sir Fergus,

[220-023]for to spere what tidings. And so as he rode by the way he met with a damosel

[220-024]that came from [[sir-palomides|Sir Palomides]], to know and seek how Sir Tristram did. Then Sir

[220-025]Fergus told her how he was almost out of his mind. Alas, said the damosel,

[220-026]where shall I find him? In such a place, said Sir Fergus. Then Sir Fergus found

[220-027]Queen Isoud sick in her bed, making the greatest dole that ever any earthly

[220-028]woman made. And when the damosel found Sir Tristram she made great dole because

[220-029]she might not amend him, for the more she made of him the more was his pain.

[220-030]And at the last Sir Tristram took his horse and rode away from her. And then

[220-031]was it three days or that she could find him, and then she brought him meat and

[220-032]drink, but he would none; and then another time Sir Tristram escaped away from

[220-033]the damosel, and it happed him to ride by the same castle where Sir Palomides

[220-034]and Sir Tristram did battle when La Beale Isoud departed them. And there by

[220-035]fortune the damosel met with Sir Tristram again, making the greatest dole that

[220-036]ever earthly creature made; and she yede to the lady of that castle and told

[220-037]her of the misadventure of Sir Tristram. Alas, said the lady of that castle,

[220-038]where is my lord, Sir Tristram? Right here by your castle, said the damosel. In

[220-039]good time, said the lady, is he so nigh me; he shall have meat and drink of the

[220-040]best; and an harp I have of his whereupon he taught me, for of goodly harping

[220-041]he beareth the prize in the world. So this lady and damosel brought him meat

[220-042]and drink, but he ate little thereof. Then upon a night he put his horse from

[220-043]him, and then he unlaced his armour, and then Sir Tristram would go into the

[220-044]wilderness, and brast down the trees and boughs; and otherwhile when he found

[220-045]the harp that the lady sent him, then would he harp, and play thereupon and

[220-046]weep together. And sometime when Sir Tristram was in the wood that the lady

[220-047]wist not where he was, then would she sit her down and play upon that harp:

[220-048]then would Sir Tristram come to that harp, and hearken thereto, and sometime he

[220-049]would harp himself. Thus he there endured a quarter of a year. Then at the last

[220-050]he ran his way, and she wist not where he was become. And then was he naked and

[220-051]waxed lean and poor of flesh; and so he fell in the fellowship of herdmen and

[220-052]shepherds, and daily they would give him some of their meat and drink. And when

[220-053]he did any shrewd deed they would beat him with rods, and so they clipped him

[220-054]with shears and made him like a fool.
