# Crown

The **crown** in [[Le Morte d'Arthur]] is more than a ceremonial ornament — it is the visible embodiment of [[kingship]], the object that transforms a man into a [[kinsman|kin]]g. When a ruler is "crowned," he ceases to be a mere [[baron|lord]] or [[battle|war]][[baron|lord]] and becomes the anointed [[king|sovereign]] of a [[realm]]. The crown carries with it duties of justice and protection, and to forfeit the crown is to forfeit not merely power but the blessing that legitimises rule.

## The Crown and Legitimate Rule

The crown distinguishes rightful [[king|kingship]] from rule by force. [[King Arthur]] does not simply seize power; he is "crowned [[kinsman|kin]]g of this [[realm|land]]" (068-009) by acclamation and divine sanction. The crowning is the act that completes and ratifies the sword in the stone's revelation:

> he was crowned king of this land. Now shall... (068-009)

To wear the crown is to accept a sacred charge. [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s coronation binds him to his people and his people to him. The crown is the contract made visible.

## Coronation and Sacred Authority

Crowning in Malory invariably has a religious dimension. [[king-arthur|Arthur]] plans "to crown him emperor with chrism as it" (107-048) — the use of chrism, the holy oil of [[christen|baptism]] and confirmation, makes explicit that [[king|kingship]] is a sacramental state. The emperor is to be crowned not merely by popular will but:

> crowned emperor by the pope's hand (107-053)

The pope's hand confers a legitimacy that no amount of military victory can equal. The crown thus links [[sovereign|sovereignty]] to the divine order, ma[[kinsman|kin]]g rebellion against the crowned [[kinsman|kin]]g not merely treason but sin.

## The Crown as Burden

To hold the crown is to bear its weight. When a [[kinsman|kin]]g is urged to[[battle|war]]d [[battle|war]], the crown restrains as much as it empowers. [[king-arthur|Arthur]] speaks of the d[[wrath|anger]] that the crown's authority may be undermined by hasty action, acknowledging the peril "of my crown to be causer to withdraw your" (161-018) support. The crown demands that its wearer place the good of the [[realm]] above personal [[wrath]] or [[vengeance]].

## The Crown as Heraldic Device

Beyond its political significance, the crown appears as a visual emblem throughout Malory. Knights ride into battle with "trappings embroidered with crowns" (225-012), and [[Sir Tristram]] bears the crown device as a mark of his royal [[blood|lineage]]. The crown in [[heraldry]] declares that its bearer is no ordinary knight but one of royal blood, entitled to deference even in the democracy of the tournament [[battle|field]].

## The Crown's Narrative Function

The crown in Malory is the object around which the ent[[wrath|ire]] political order revolves. [[King Arthur]]'s claim to the crown is the premise of the work; the defence of that claim drives the early books; the eventual collapse of that claim in the final books is the t[[wrath|rage]]dy. The crown passes, but the ideal of [[king|kingship]] it represents — justice under God, power bound by oath — endures as the [[banner|standard]] against which every ruler in the work is measured.
