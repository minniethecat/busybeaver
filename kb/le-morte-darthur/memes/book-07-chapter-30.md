[157-001]heralds, and how he escaped out of the [[battle|field]].

[157-002]But before he was in so many colours, and now he is but in one colour; that is

[157-003]yellow. Now go, said [[king-arthur|King Arthur]] unto divers heralds, and ride about him, and

[157-004]espy what manner knight he is, for I have spered of many knights this day that

[157-005]be upon his party, and all say they know him not.

[157-006]And so an herald rode nigh [[sir-gareth|Gareth]] as he could; and there he saw written about

[157-007]his helm in gold, This helm is Sir Gareth of Orkney.

[157-008]Then the herald cried as he were wood, and many heralds with him:—This is

[157-009]Sir Gareth of Orkney in the yellow arms; that by all kings and knights of

[157-010]Arthur’s beheld him and awaited; and then they pressed all to behold him,

[157-011]and ever the heralds cried: This is Sir Gareth of Orkney, King Lot’s son.

[157-012]And when Sir Gareth espied that he was discovered, then he doubled his strokes,

[157-013]and smote down Sir Sagramore, and his brother [[sir-gawaine|Sir Gawaine]].

[157-014]O brother, said Sir Gawaine, I weened ye would not have stricken me.

[157-015]So when he heard him say so he thrang here and there, and so with great pain he

[157-016]gat out of the press, and there he met with his dwarf. O boy, said Sir Gareth,

[157-017]thou hast beguiled me foul this day that thou kept my ring; give it me anon

[157-018]again, that I may hide my body withal; and so he took it him. And then they all

[157-019]wist not where he was become; and Sir Gawaine had in manner espied where Sir

[157-020]Gareth rode, and then he rode after with all his might. That espied Sir Gareth,

[157-021]and rode lightly into the forest, that Sir Gawaine wist not where he was

[157-022]become. And when Sir Gareth wist that Sir Gawaine was passed, he asked the

[157-023]dwarf of best counsel. Sir, said the dwarf, meseemeth it were best, now that ye

[157-024]are escaped from spying, that ye send my lady Dame Lionesse her ring. It is

[157-025]well advised, said Sir Gareth; now have it here and bear it to her, and say

[157-026]that I recommend me unto her good grace, and say her I will come when I may,

[157-027]and I pray her to be true and faithful to me as I will be to her. Sir, said the

[157-028]dwarf, it shall be done as ye command: and so he rode his way, and did his

[157-029][[quest|errand]] unto the lady. Then she said, Where is my knight, Sir Gareth? Madam,

[157-030]said the dwarf, he bade me say that he would not be long from you. And so

[157-031]lightly the dwarf came again unto Sir Gareth, that would full fain have had a

[157-032]lodging, for he had need to be reposed. And then fell there a thunder and a

[157-033]rain, as heaven and earth should go together. And Sir Gareth was not a little

[157-034]weary, for of all that day he had but little rest, neither his horse nor he. So

[157-035]this Sir Gareth rode so long in that forest until the night came. And ever it

[157-036]lightened and thundered, as it had been wood. At the last by fortune he came to

[157-037]a castle, and there he heard the waits upon the walls.
