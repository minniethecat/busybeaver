[175-001]king and La Beale Isoud out of Ireland for to come into Cornwall.

[175-002]Sir, said [[sir-tristram|Tristram]], now I shall tell you all the truth: my father’s name

[175-003]is Sir Meliodas, King of Liones, and my mother hight Elizabeth, that was sister

[175-004]unto [[king-mark|King Mark of Cornwall]]; and my mother died of me in the forest, and because

[175-005]thereof she commanded, or she died, that when I were christened they should

[175-006]christen me Tristram; and because I would not be known in this country I turned

[175-007]my name and let me call Tramtrist; and for the truage of Cornwall I fought for

[175-008]my eme’s sake, and for the right of Cornwall that ye had posseded many

[175-009]years. And wit ye well, said Tristram unto the king, I did the battle for the

[175-010]love of mine uncle, King Mark, and for the love of the country of Cornwall, and

[175-011]for to increase mine honour; for that same day that I fought with Sir Marhaus I

[175-012]was made knight, and never or then did I battle with no knight, and from me he

[175-013]went alive, and left his shield and his sword behind.

[175-014]So God me help, said the king, I may not say but ye did as a knight should, and

[175-015]it was your part to do for your quarrel, and to increase your [[honour|worship]] as a

[175-016]knight should; howbeit I may not maintain you in this country with my worship,

[175-017]unless that I should displease my barons, and my wife and her kin. Sir, said

[175-018]Tristram, I thank you of your good lordship that I have had with you here, and

[175-019]the great goodness my lady, your daughter, hath shewed me, and therefore, said

[175-020]Sir Tristram, it may so happen that ye shall win more by my life than by my

[175-021]death, for in the parts of England it may happen I may do you service at some

[175-022]season, that ye shall be glad that ever ye shewed me your good lordship. With

[175-023]more I promise you as I am true knight, that in all places I shall be my lady

[175-024]your daughter’s servant and knight in right and in wrong, and I shall

[175-025]never fail her, to do as much as a knight may do. Also I beseech your good

[175-026]grace that I may take my leave at my lady, your daughter, and at all the barons

[175-027]and knights. I will well, said the king.

[175-028]Then Sir Tristram went unto La Beale Isoud and took his leave of her. And then

[175-029]he told her all, what he was, and how he had changed his name because he would

[175-030]not be known, and how a lady told him that he should never be whole till he

[175-031]came into this country where the poison was made, wherethrough I was near my

[175-032]death had not your ladyship been. O gentle knight, said La Beale Isoud, full

[175-033]woe am I of thy departing, for I saw never man that I owed so good will to. And

[175-034]therewithal she wept heartily. Madam, said Sir Tristram, ye shall understand

[175-035]that my name is Sir Tristram de Liones, gotten of King Meliodas, and born of

[175-036]his queen. And I promise you faithfully that I shall be all the days of my life

[175-037]your knight. Gramercy, said La Beale Isoud, and I promise you there-against

[175-038]that I shall not be married this seven years but by your assent; and to whom

[175-039]that ye will I shall be married to him will I have, and he will have me if ye

[175-040]will consent.

[175-041]And then Sir Tristram gave her a ring, and she gave him another; and therewith

[175-042]he departed from her, leaving her making great dole and lamentation; and he

[175-043]straight went unto the court among all the barons, and there he took his leave

[175-044]at most and least, and openly he said among them all: Fair lords, now it is so

[175-045]that I must depart: if there be any man here that I have offended unto, or that

[175-046]any man be with me grieved, let complain him here afore me or that ever I

[175-047]depart, and I shall amend it unto my power. And if there be any that will

[175-048]proffer me wrong, or say of me wrong or shame behind my back, say it now or

[175-049]never, and here is my body to make it good, body against body. And all they

[175-050]stood still, there was not one that would say one word; yet were there some

[175-051]knights that were of the queen’s blood, and of Sir Marhaus’ blood,

[175-052]but they would not meddle with him.
