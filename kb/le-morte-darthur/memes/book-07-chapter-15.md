[142-001]the siege; and came to a sycamore tree, and there [[sir-gareth|Beaumains]] blew a horn, and

[142-002]then the Knight of the Red Launds came to [[battle|fight]] with him.

[142-003]Now leave we the knight and the dwarf, and speak we of Beaumains, that all

[142-004]night lay in the hermitage; and upon the morn he and the damosel Linet heard

[142-005]their mass and brake their fast. And then they took their horses and rode

[142-006]throughout a fair forest; and then they came to a plain, and saw where were

[142-007]many pavilions and tents, and a fair castle, and there was much smoke and great

[142-008]noise; and when they came near the siege Sir Beaumains espied upon great trees,

[142-009]as he rode, how there hung full goodly armed knights by the neck, and their

[142-010]shields about their necks with their swords, and gilt spurs upon their heels,

[142-011]and so there hung nigh a forty knights shamefully with full rich arms.

[142-012]Then Sir Beaumains abated his countenance and said, What meaneth this? Fair

[142-013]sir, said the damosel, abate not your cheer for all this sight, for ye must

[142-014]courage yourself, or else ye be all shent, for all these knights came hither to

[142-015]this siege to rescue my sister Dame Lionesse, and when the Red Knight of the

[142-016]Red Launds had overcome them, he put them to this shameful death without mercy

[142-017]and [[mercy|pity]]. And in the same wise he will serve you but if you quit you the

[142-018]better.

[142-019]Now Jesu defend me, said Beaumains, from such a villainous death and shenship

[142-020]of arms. For rather than I should so be faren withal, I would rather be slain

[142-021]manly in plain battle. So were ye better, said the damosel; for trust not, in

[142-022]him is no courtesy, but all goeth to the death or shameful murder, and that is

[142-023]pity, for he is a full likely man, well made of body, and a full noble knight

[142-024]of [[courage|prowess]], and a lord of great lands and possessions. Truly, said Beaumains,

[142-025]he may well be a good knight, but he useth shameful customs, and it is marvel

[142-026]that he endureth so long that none of the noble knights of my lord

[142-027][[king-arthur|Arthur]]’s have not dealt with him.

[142-028]And then they rode to the dykes, and saw them double dyked with full warlike

[142-029]walls; and there were lodged many great lords nigh the walls; and there was

[142-030]great noise of minstrelsy; and the sea beat upon the one side of the walls,

[142-031]where were many ships and mariners’ noise with “hale and

[142-032]how.” And also there was fast by a sycamore tree, and there hung an horn,

[142-033]the greatest that ever they saw, of an elephant’s bone; and this Knight

[142-034]of the Red Launds had hanged it up there, that if there came any errant-knight,

[142-035]he must blow that horn, and then will he make him ready and come to him to do

[142-036]battle. But, sir, I pray you, said the damosel Linet, blow ye not the horn till

[142-037]it be high noon, for now it is about prime, and now increaseth his might, that

[142-038]as men say he hath seven men’s strength. Ah, fie for shame, fair damosel,

[142-039]say ye never so more to me; for, an he were as good a knight as ever was, I

[142-040]shall never fail him in his most might, for either I will win [[honour|worship]]

[142-041]worshipfully, or die knightly in the field. And therewith he spurred his horse

[142-042]straight to the sycamore tree, and blew so the horn eagerly that all the siege

[142-043]and the castle rang thereof. And then there leapt out knights out of their

[142-044]tents and pavilions, and they within the castle looked over the walls and out

[142-045]at windows.

[142-046]Then the Red Knight of the Red Launds armed him hastily, and two barons set on

[142-047]his spurs upon his heels, and all was blood red, his armour, spear and shield.

[142-048]And an earl buckled his helm upon his head, and then they brought him a red

[142-049]spear and a red steed, and so he rode into a little vale under the castle, that

[142-050]all that were in the castle and at the siege might behold the battle.
