# Sir Launcelot

**Sir Launcelot du Lake** is the supreme knight of Thomas Malory's *Le Morte d'[[king-arthur|Arthur]]* — the [[battle|war]]rior without equal, the flower of [[Chivalry]], and the tragic lover whose divided loyalties bring down [[King Arthur]]'s [[realm|kingdom]].

Caxton's preface devotes more books to Launcelot than to any other single figure: "The Sixth Book treateth of Sir Launcelot" (002-110), "The Eleventh Book treateth of Sir Launcelot and Sir Galahad" (002-118), "The Twelfth Book treateth of Sir Launcelot and his madness" (002-119).

He embodies both the highest aspirations and the deepest contradictions of the chivalric ideal. His arc — from tournament triumph through spiritual crisis to penitent death — is the moral spine of the ent[[wrath|ire]] [[Round Table]] cycle.

## The Greatest Knight

Malory establishes Launcelot's supremacy through prophecy, [[honour|reputation]], and the testimony of other knights. The sword in the stone at [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s coronation carries an inscription naming the best knight:

> The best knight of the world, and that shall be Sir Launcelot or else Sir Galahad his son. (050-011)

That the prophecy names both father and son — Launcelot the greatest earthly knight, Galahad the knight of pure spirit — encapsulates the central tension of Launcelot's character. He is simultaneously the pinnacle of what mortal chivalry can achieve and the proof that mortal chivalry falls short of [[grace|divine grace]].

His ran[[kinsman|kin]]g among the three supreme knights — "Sir Launcelot de Lake, [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]] de Liones, and Sir Lamorak de Galis" (140-027) — is never contested, but his pre-eminence is a burden as much as a crown.

## Love and Tragedy

Launcelot's defining relationship is with [[Queen Guenever]]. The "Nineteenth Book treateth of [[queen-guenever|Queen Guenever]] and Launcelot" (002-127), and their love is the engine of his greatest deeds — performed in [[queen-guenever|Guenever]]'s name and for her [[worship|honour]] — and of his ultimate t[[wrath|rage]]dy.

Where [[Sir Tristram]]'s parallel love-t[[wrath|rage]]dy plays out at the margins of [[camelot|Arthur's court]], Launcelot's occupies its centre. His divided loyalty — to [[king-arthur|Arthur]] his [[kinsman|kin]]g and [[queen-guenever|Guenever]] his love — cannot be resolved within the feudal framework.

When the affair is exposed, the chivalric order fractures: [[kinsman|kin]] against [[kinsman|kin]], knight against knight, until [[king-arthur|Arthur]] and [[mordred|Mordred]] meet at Salisbury and the fellowship is broken forever.

## The Sangreal and Spiritual Failure

The [[Sangreal]] quest is Launcelot's decisive spiritual crisis. Despite being the greatest earthly knight, he cannot achieve the Holy Grail. His sin with [[queen-guenever|Guenever]] bars the way, and the quest that his son [[Sir Galahad]] completes becomes for Launcelot a revelation of his own insufficiency:

> As it telleth after in the Sangreal, that Sir Launcelot began to be estranged from the queen, and to withdraw from her company, and to eschew the fellowship of ladies. (044-034)

This reformation proves temporary. The t[[wrath|rage]]dy of Launcelot is not that he is ignorant of the good — he sees the [[sangreal|Sangreal]], he knows what purity demands — but that he cannot sustain it. The passionate intensity that makes him peerless in arms is inseparable from the passion that binds him to [[queen-guenever|Guenever]].

## The Penitent End

After [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s death, Launcelot's story curves to[[battle|war]]d [[repentance|penitence]]. "How Sir Launcelot came to revenge his death" (002-131) marks the transition: the knight who could not achieve the [[sangreal|Sangreal]] becomes a hermit.

The greatest of the [[round-table|Round Table]]'s knights dies not on the battle[[battle|field]] but on his knees — a death that is both defeat and redemption.
