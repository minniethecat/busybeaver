[217-001]and after of the shame spoken of the knights of Cornwall, and how they

[217-002]jousted.

[217-003]Now leave we of this tale, and speak we of [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]], that as he rode he met

[217-004]with [[sir-kay|Sir Kay]], the Seneschal; and there Sir Kay asked Sir Tristram of what

[217-005]country he was. He answered that he was of the country of Cornwall. It may well

[217-006]be, said Sir Kay, for yet heard I never that ever good knight came out of

[217-007]Cornwall. That is evil spoken, said Sir Tristram, but an it please you to tell

[217-008]me your name I require you. Sir, wit ye well, said Sir Kay, that my name is Sir

[217-009]Kay, the Seneschal. Is that your name? said Sir Tristram, now wit ye well that

[217-010]ye are named the shamefullest knight of your tongue that now is living; howbeit

[217-011]ye are called a good knight, but ye are called unfortunate, and passing

[217-012]overthwart of your tongue. And thus they rode together till they came to a

[217-013]bridge. And there was a knight would not let them pass till one of them jousted

[217-014]with him; and so that knight jousted with Sir Kay, and there that knight gave

[217-015]Sir Kay a fall: his name was Sir Tor, Sir Lamorak’s half-brother. And

[217-016]then they two rode to their lodging, and there they found Sir Brandiles, and

[217-017]Sir Tor came thither anon after. And as they sat at supper these four knights,

[217-018]three of them spake all shame by Cornish knights. Sir Tristram heard all that

[217-019]they said and he said but little, but he thought the more, but at that time he

[217-020]discovered not his name.

[217-021]Upon the morn Sir Tristram took his horse and abode them upon their way. And

[217-022]there Sir Brandiles proffered to joust with Sir Tristram, and Sir Tristram

[217-023]smote him down, horse and all, to the earth. Then Sir Tor le Fise de Vayshoure

[217-024]encountered with Sir Tristram and there Sir Tristram smote him down, and then

[217-025]he rode his way, and Sir Kay followed him, but he would not of his fellowship.

[217-026]Then Sir Brandiles came to Sir Kay and said: I would wit fain what is that

[217-027]knight’s name. Come on with me, said Sir Kay, and we shall pray him to

[217-028]tell us his name. So they rode together till they came nigh him, and then they

[217-029]were ware where he sat by a well, and had put off his helm to drink at the

[217-030]well. And when he saw them come he laced on his helm lightly, and took his

[217-031]horse, and proffered them to joust. Nay, said Sir Brandiles, we jousted late

[217-032]enough with you, we come not in that intent. But for this we come to require

[217-033]you of knighthood to tell us your name. My fair knights, sithen that is your

[217-034]desire, and to please you, ye shall wit that my name is Sir Tristram de Liones,

[217-035]nephew unto [[king-mark|King Mark of Cornwall]]. In good time, said Sir Brandiles, and well

[217-036]be ye found, and wit ye well that we be right glad that we have found you, and

[217-037]we be of a fellowship that would be right glad of your [[fellowship|company]]. For ye are the

[217-038]knight in the world that the noble fellowship of the [[round-table|Round Table]] most desireth

[217-039]to have the company of. God thank them, said Sir Tristram, of their great

[217-040]goodness, but as yet I feel well that I am unable to be of their fellowship,

[217-041]for I was never yet of such deeds of worthiness to be in the company of such a

[217-042]fellowship. Ah, said Sir Kay, an ye be Sir Tristram de Liones, ye are the man

[217-043]called now most of [[courage|prowess]] except [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot du Lake]]; for he beareth not the

[217-044]life, Christian nor heathen, that can find such another knight, to speak of his

[217-045]prowess, and of his hands, and his truth withal. For yet could there never

[217-046]creature say of him dishonour and make it good. Thus they talked a great while,

[217-047]and then they departed either from other such ways as them seemed best.
