[081-002]Then said [[morgan-le-fay|Morgan]], Saw ye Arthur, my brother? Yea, said her knights, right well,

[081-003]and that ye should have found an we might have stirred from one stead, for by

[081-005]said Morgan. Anon after as she rode she met a knight leading another knight on

[081-006]his horse before him, bound hand and foot, blindfold, to have drowned him in a

[081-007]fountain. When she saw this knight so bound, she asked him, What will ye do

[081-008]with that knight? Lady, said he, I will drown him. For what cause? she asked.

[081-009]For I found him with my wife, and she shall have the same death anon. That were

[081-010][[mercy|pity]], said Morgan le Fay. Now, what say ye, knight, is it truth that he saith

[081-011]of you? she said to the knight that should be drowned. Nay truly, madam, he

[081-012]saith not right on me. Of whence be ye, said Morgan le Fay, and of what

[081-013]country? I am of [[camelot|the court of King Arthur]], and my name is Manassen, cousin unto

[081-014]Accolon of Gaul. Ye say well, said she, and for the love of him ye shall be

[081-015]delivered, and ye shall have your adversary in the same case ye be in. So

[081-016]Manassen was loosed and the other knight bound. And anon Manassen unarmed him,

[081-017]and armed himself in his harness, and so mounted on horseback, and the knight

[081-018]afore him, and so threw him into the fountain and drowned him. And then he rode

[081-019]unto Morgan again, and asked if she would anything unto King Arthur. Tell him

[081-020]that I rescued thee, not for the love of him but for the love of Accolon, and

[081-021]tell him I fear him not while I can make me and them that be with me in

[081-022]likeness of stones; and let him wit I can do much more when I see my time. And

[081-023]so she departed into the country of Gore, and there was she richly received,

[081-024]and made her castles and towns passing strong, for always she dreaded much King

[081-025]Arthur.

[081-026]When the king had well rested him at the abbey, he rode unto Camelot, and found

[081-027]his queen and his barons right glad of his coming. And when they heard of his

[081-028]strange adventures as is afore rehearsed, then all had marvel of the falsehood

[081-029]of Morgan le Fay; many knights wished her burnt. Then came Manassen to court

[081-030]and told the king of his adventure. Well, said the king, she is a kind sister;

[081-031]I shall so be avenged on her an I live, that all Christendom shall speak of it.

[081-032]So on the morn there came a damosel from Morgan to the king, and she brought

[081-033]with her the richest mantle that ever was seen in that court, for it was set as

[081-034]full of precious stones as one might stand by another, and there were the

[081-035]richest stones that ever the king saw. And the damosel said, Your sister

[081-036]sendeth you this mantle, and desireth that ye should take this gift of her; and

[081-037]in what thing she hath offended you, she will amend it at your own pleasure.

[081-038]When the king beheld this mantle it pleased him much, but he said but little.
