[125-002]So [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]] rode many wild ways, throughout marches and many wild ways.

[125-003]And as he rode in a valley he saw a knight chasing a lady, with a naked sword,

[125-004]to have slain her. And by fortune as this knight should have slain this lady,

[125-005]she cried on Sir Launcelot and prayed him to rescue her. When Sir Launcelot saw

[125-006]that mischief, he took his horse and rode between them, saying, Knight, fie for

[125-007]shame, why wilt thou slay this lady? thou dost shame unto thee and all knights.

[125-008]What hast thou to do betwixt me and my wife? said the knight. I will slay her

[125-009]maugre thy head. That shall ye not, said Sir Launcelot, for rather we two will

[125-010]have ado together. Sir Launcelot, said the knight, thou dost not thy part, for

[125-011]this lady hath betrayed me. It is not so, said the lady, truly he saith wrong

[125-012]on me. And for because I love and cherish my cousin germain, he is jealous

[125-013]betwixt him and me; and as I shall answer to God there was never sin betwixt

[125-014]us. But, sir, said the lady, as thou art called the worshipfullest knight of

[125-015]the world, I require thee of true knighthood, keep me and save me. For

[125-016]whatsomever ye say he will slay me, for he is without mercy. Have ye no doubt,

[125-017]said Launcelot, it shall not lie in his power. Sir, said the knight, in your

[125-018]sight I will be ruled as ye will have me. And so Sir Launcelot rode on the one

[125-019]side and she on the other: he had not ridden but a while, but the knight bade

[125-020]Sir Launcelot turn him and look behind him, and said, Sir, yonder come men of

[125-021]arms after us riding. And so Sir Launcelot turned him and thought no treason,

[125-022]and therewith was the knight and the lady on one side, and suddenly he swapped

[125-023]off his lady’s head.

[125-024]And when Sir Launcelot had espied him what he had done, he said, and called

[125-025]him, Traitor, thou hast shamed me for ever. And suddenly Sir Launcelot alighted

[125-026]off his horse, and pulled out his sword to slay him, and therewithal he fell

[125-027]flat to the earth, and gripped Sir Launcelot by the thighs, and cried mercy.

[125-028]Fie on thee, said Sir Launcelot, thou shameful knight, thou mayest have no

[125-029]mercy, and therefore arise and [[battle|fight]] with me. Nay, said the knight, I will

[125-030]never arise till ye grant me mercy. Now will I proffer thee fair, said

[125-031]Launcelot, I will unarm me unto my shirt, and I will have nothing upon me but

[125-032]my shirt, and my sword and my hand. And if thou canst slay me, quit be thou for

[125-033]ever. Nay, sir, said Pedivere, that will I never. Well, said Sir Launcelot,

[125-034]take this lady and the head, and bear it upon thee, and here shalt thou swear

[125-035]upon my sword, to bear it always upon thy back, and never to rest till thou

[125-036]come to [[queen-guenever|Queen Guenever]]. Sir, said he, that will I do, by the faith of my body.

[125-037]Now, said Launcelot, tell me what is your name? Sir, my name is Pedivere. In a

[125-038]shameful hour wert thou born, said Launcelot.

[125-039]So Pedivere departed with the dead lady and the head, and found the queen with

[125-040][[king-arthur|King Arthur]] at Winchester, and there he told all the truth. Sir knight, said

[125-041]the queen, this is an horrible deed and a shameful, and a great rebuke unto Sir

[125-042]Launcelot; but notwithstanding his [[honour|worship]] is not known in many divers

[125-043]countries; but this shall I give you in penance, make ye as good shift as ye

[125-044]can, ye shall bear this lady with you on horseback unto the Pope of Rome, and

[125-045]of him receive your penance for your foul deeds; and ye shall never rest one

[125-046]night whereas ye do another; an ye go to any bed the dead body shall lie with

[125-047]you. This oath there he made, and so departed. And as it telleth in the French

[125-048]book, when he came to Rome, the Pope bade him go again unto Queen Guenever, and

[125-049]in Rome was his lady buried by the Pope’s commandment. And after this Sir

[125-050]Pedivere fell to great goodness, and was an holy man and an hermit.
