# Faith

**Faith** in Thomas Malory's *Le Morte d'[[king-arthur|Arthur]]* operates on multiple levels: religious faith in God and His [[miracle|providence]], feudal faith between [[baron|lord]] and vassal, and personal faith as trust[[worship|worthiness]] and fidelity. These meanings intertwine throughout the narrative, each reinforcing and complicating the others.

## Religious Faith

Caxton's preface states that the book is meant "for to give faith and belief" (002-087), positioning the ent[[wrath|ire]] work within a framework of religious instruction. Knights swear by their faith — "by the faith she owed him" (006-003) and "by the faith of my body" (006-006) — treating faith as a sacred bond that guarantees truth. [[king-arthur|King Arthur]] himself swears "by the faith of my body" (009-019), demonstrating that faith is the foundation upon which knightly speech and promise rest.

## Faith as Feudal Bond

Faith in Malory is also the political bond between [[kinsman|kin]]g and subject. Those who are "faithful" (006-019) earn re[[battle|war]]ds and trust. Knights are welcomed "in the faithfullest manner" (013-055), and faithlessness — the brea[[kinsman|kin]]g of sworn loyalty — is the path to treason and destruction. The feudal system of the [[king-arthur|Arthur]]ian world is held together by a network of faith-oaths, and when those oaths break, the [[realm|kingdom]] shatters.

## The Crisis of Faith

The Grail quest represents the ultimate test of faith in Malory. Knights who have excelled in martial faith find themselves spiritually unworthy, and the greatest knight of the [[round-table|Round Table]] — [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]] — cannot achieve the Grail because his earthly faith (to [[queen-guenever|Guenever]]) conflicts with his heavenly faith (to God). This crisis of competing faiths is central to the t[[wrath|rage]]dy of the Morte.
