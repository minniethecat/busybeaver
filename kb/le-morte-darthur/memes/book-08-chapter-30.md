[193-001]and how Lambegus rode after to rescue her, and of the escape of Isoud.

[193-002]Sir, said Palamides, I promised your Queen Isoud to bring again Dame Bragwaine

[193-003]that she had lost, upon this covenant, that she should grant me a boon that I

[193-004]would ask, and without grudging, outher advisement, she granted me. What say

[193-005]ye, my lady? said the king. It is as he saith, so God me help, said the queen;

[193-006]to say thee sooth I promised him his asking for love and joy that I had to see

[193-007]her. Well, Madam, said the king, and if ye were hasty to grant him what boon he

[193-008]would ask, I will well that ye perform your promise. Then, said Palamides, I

[193-009]will that ye wit that I will have your queen to lead her and govern her whereas

[193-010]me list. Therewith the king stood still, and bethought him of [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]], and

[193-011]deemed that he would rescue her. And then hastily the king answered: Take her

[193-012]with the adventures that shall fall of it, for as I suppose thou wilt not enjoy

[193-013]her no while. As for that, said Palamides, I dare right well abide the

[193-014]adventure. And so, to make short tale, Sir Palamides took her by the hand and

[193-015]said: Madam, grudge not to go with me, for I desire nothing but your own

[193-016]promise. As for that, said the queen, I fear not greatly to go with thee,

[193-017]howbeit thou hast me at advantage upon my promise, for I doubt not I shall be

[193-018]worshipfully rescued from thee. As for that, said Sir Palamides, be it as it be

[193-019]may. So Queen Isoud was set behind Palamides, and rode his way.

[193-020]Anon the king sent after Sir Tristram, but in no wise he could be found, for he

[193-021]was in the forest a-hunting; for that was always his custom, but if he used

[193-022]arms, to chase and to hunt in the forests. Alas, said the king, now I am shamed

[193-023]for ever, that by mine own assent my lady and my queen shall be devoured. Then

[193-024]came forth a knight, his name was Lambegus, and he was a knight of Sir

[193-025]Tristram. My lord, said this knight, sith ye have trust in my lord, Sir

[193-026]Tristram, wit ye well for his sake I will ride after your queen and rescue her,

[193-027]or else I shall be beaten. Gramercy, said the king, as I live, Sir Lambegus, I

[193-028]shall deserve it. And then Sir Lambegus armed him, and rode after as fast as he

[193-029]might. And then within a while he overtook Sir Palamides. And then Sir

[193-030]Palamides left the queen. What art thou, said Palamides, art thou Tristram?

[193-031]Nay, he said, I am his servant, and my name is Sir Lambegus. That me repenteth,

[193-032]said Palamides. I had liefer thou hadst been Sir Tristram. I believe you well,

[193-033]said Lambegus, but when thou meetest with Sir Tristram thou shalt have thy

[193-034]hands full. And then they hurtled together and all to-brast their spears, and

[193-035]then they pulled out their swords, and hewed on helms and hauberks. At the last

[193-036]Sir Palamides gave Sir Lambegus such a wound that he fell down like a dead

[193-037]knight to the earth.

[193-038]Then he looked after La Beale Isoud, and then she was gone he nist where. Wit

[193-039]ye well Sir Palamides was never so heavy. So the queen ran into the forest, and

[193-040]there she found a well, and therein she had thought to have drowned herself.

[193-041]And as good fortune would, there came a knight to her that had a castle

[193-042]thereby, his name was Sir Adtherp. And when he found the queen in that mischief

[193-043]he rescued her, and brought her to his castle. And when he wist what she was he

[193-044]armed him, and took his horse, and said he would be avenged upon Palamides; and

[193-045]so he rode on till he met with him, and there Sir Palamides wounded him sore,

[193-046]and by force he made him to tell him the cause why he did battle with him, and

[193-047]how he had led the queen unto his castle. Now bring me there, said Palamides,

[193-048]or thou shalt die of my hands. Sir, said Sir Adtherp, I am so wounded I may not

[193-049]follow, but ride you this way and it shall bring you into my castle, and there

[193-050]within is the queen. Then Sir Palamides rode still till he came to the castle.

[193-051]And at a window La Beale Isoud saw Sir Palamides; then she made the gates to be

[193-052]shut strongly. And when he saw he might not come within the castle, he put off

[193-053]his bridle and his saddle, and put his horse to pasture, and set himself down

[193-054]at the gate like a man that was out of his wit that recked not of himself.
