The **joust** is the defining form of individual knightly [[battle|combat]] in [[Le Morte d'Arthur]], a ritual encounter between two mounted knights that tests [[prowess]], [[honour]], and the right to passage. More than mere sport, the joust functions as a narrative engine—a mechanism for introducing knights, establishing hierarchy, and resolving disputes without the chaos of full battle.

## The Joust as Ritual Combat

The joust follows a fixed form: two knights ride against each other with [[spear|lances]], each [[quest|seeking]] to unhorse the other. The encounter may be a single pass or a series, and defeat means loss of horse, arms, and sometimes liberty. In Malory's world, the joust is not optional—knights encounter challenges at bridges, fords, and forest passes where jousting is an enforced condition of passage:

> this man pass this way but he must joust (048-025)
> pass this way, but if thou joust (059-005)

The ubiquity of such challenges creates a [[realm|land]]scape in which every journey is potentially a sequence of jousts, and every unknown knight a test of one's own [[worship|worthiness]].

## The Joust at Festivals and Tournaments

Beyond the roadside challenge, the joust flourishes at great feasts and [[tournament|tournaments]], where it serves as public spectacle and theatre of [[worship|honour]]:

> the barons let make a jousts and a tournament, that all knights that would joust or tourney there might play (008-031)

At such gatherings the joust becomes a competition for [[honour|renown]], with prizes a[[battle|war]]ded to the most successful [[battle|combat]]ant:

> Sir Pelleas had the prize (094-017)

The tournament joust allows knights to establish [[honour|reputation]] before the assembled court, and the judgment of [[lady|ladies]] at the jousts—who may a[[battle|war]]d the prize—introduces the [[love|courtly]] dimension to martial performance.

## The Joust as Test of Identity

Many of the Morte's most significant encounters unfold as jousts between knights who do not recognise one another. The anonymity of the closed helm makes every joust a potential confrontation between [[kinsman|kin]], friend, or liege [[baron|lord]]:

> I will joust with you. That is me loath, said the king (025-023)

The tension between the joust's formal demand for [[battle|combat]] and its potential for tragic misrecognition runs throughout the work, culminating in moments where a withheld name or a disguised shield turns the ritual encounter into something closer to [[treason]].

## The Joust and Knightly Prowess

Excellence in jousting is the most visible mark of [[prowess]]. Sir [[Sir Launcelot|Launcelot]] and Sir [[Sir Tristram|Tristram]] are defined in large part by their dominance in the lists:

> in all tournaments and jousts and deeds of arms, both for life and death (109-008)

Yet the joust is not the whole of knighthood. Malory distinguishes between the joust as a contained test of skill and the deeper [[test|trial]] of the [[battle|battlefield]], where cou[[wrath|rage]] faces death without the safety of sporting convention. The knight who excels only at jousting has not yet proven himself.

## The Joust's Narrative Function

In the architecture of Malory's romance, the joust is a narrative building block. It introduces the unknown knight, establishes his quality in a single stroke, and red[[wrath|ire]]cts the narrative to[[battle|war]]d the consequences of victory or defeat. A knight unhorsed may become a [[prison|prisoner]], a companion, or a messenger. A knight victorious may be drawn into the affairs of the castle whose champion he has felled. The joust is the smallest complete unit of knightly narrative—a story in miniature that, multiplied across the work, forms the rhythm of chivalric adventure itself.
