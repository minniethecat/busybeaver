[230-001][[sir-palomides|Sir Palomides]], and how [[sir-launcelot|Launcelot]] overthrew two knights.

[230-002]Then they saw much people of the country salute Sir Palomides. And within a

[230-003]while after there came a squire of the castle, that told Sir Pellounes that was

[230-004]lord of that castle, that a knight with a black shield had smitten down

[230-005]thirteen knights. Fair brother, said [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]] unto Sir Persides, let us

[230-006]cast upon us cloaks, and let us go see the play. Not so, said Sir Persides, we

[230-007]will not go like knaves thither, but we will ride like men and good knights to

[230-008]withstand our enemies. So they armed them, and took their horses and great

[230-009]spears, and thither they went thereas many knights assayed themself before the

[230-010]tournament. And anon Sir Palomides saw Sir Persides, and then he sent a squire

[230-011]unto him and said: Go thou to the yonder knight with the green shield and

[230-012]therein a lion of gold, and say him I require him to joust with me, and tell

[230-013]him that my name is Sir Palomides. When Sir Persides understood that request of

[230-014]Sir Palomides, he made him ready, and there anon they met together, but Sir

[230-015]Persides had a fall. Then Sir Tristram dressed him to be revenged upon Sir

[230-016]Palomides, and that saw Sir Palomides that was ready and so was not Sir

[230-017]Tristram, and took him at an advantage and smote him over his horse’s

[230-018]tail when he had no spear in his rest. Then stert up Sir Tristram and took his

[230-019]horse lightly, and was wroth out of measure, and sore ashamed of that fall.

[230-020]Then Sir Tristram sent unto Sir Palomides by Gouvernail, and prayed him to

[230-021]joust with him at his request. Nay, said Sir Palomides, as at this time I will

[230-022]not joust with that knight, for I know him better than he weeneth. And if he be

[230-023]wroth he may right it to-morn at the Castle of Maidens, where he may see me and

[230-024]many other knights.

[230-025]With that came Sir Dinadan, and when he saw Sir Tristram wroth he list not to

[230-026]jape. Lo, said Sir Dinadan, here may a man prove, be a man never so good yet

[230-027]may he have a fall, and he was never so wise but he might be overseen, and he

[230-028]rideth well that never fell. So Sir Tristram was passing wroth, and said to Sir

[230-029]Persides and to Sir Dinadan: I will revenge me. Right so as they stood talking

[230-030]there, there came by Sir Tristram a likely knight riding passing soberly and

[230-031]heavily with a black shield. What knight is that? said Sir Tristram unto Sir

[230-032]Persides. I know him well, said Sir Persides, for his name is Sir Briant of

[230-033]North Wales; so he passed on among other knights of North Wales. And there came

[230-034]in Sir Launcelot du Lake with a shield of the arms of Cornwall, and he sent a

[230-035]squire unto Sir Briant, and required him to joust with him. Well, said Sir

[230-036]Briant, sithen I am required to joust I will do what I may; and there Sir

[230-037]Launcelot smote down Sir Briant from his horse a great fall. And then Sir

[230-038]Tristram marvelled what knight he was that bare the shield of Cornwall.

[230-039]Whatsoever he be, said Sir Dinadan, I warrant you he is of King Ban’s

[230-040]blood, the which be knights of the most noble [[courage|prowess]] in the world, for to

[230-041]account so many for so many. Then there came two knights of Northgalis, that

[230-042]one hight Hew de la Montaine, and the other Sir Madok de la Montaine, and they

[230-043]challenged Sir Launcelot foot-hot. Sir Launcelot not refusing them but made him

[230-044]ready, with one spear he smote them down both over their horses’ croups;

[230-045]and so Sir Launcelot rode his way. By the good lord, said Sir Tristram, he is a

[230-046]good knight that beareth the shield of Cornwall, and meseemeth he rideth in the

[230-047]best manner that ever I saw knight ride.

[230-048]Then [[king-arthur|the King]] of Northgalis rode unto Sir Palomides and prayed him heartily for

[230-049]his sake to joust with that knight that hath done us of Northgalis despite.

[230-050]Sir, said Sir Palomides, I am full loath to have ado with that knight, and

[230-051]cause why is, for as to-morn the great tournament shall be; and therefore I

[230-052]will keep myself fresh by my will. Nay, said the King of Northgalis, I pray you

[230-053]require him of [[tournament|jousts]]. Sir, said Sir Palomides, I will joust at your request,

[230-054]and require that knight to joust with me, and often I have seen a man have a

[230-055]fall at his own request.
