# Blood

In [[Le Morte d'Arthur]], **blood** is the master-metaphor for identity. It binds [[knight]] to lineage, determines political [[fealty|allegiance]], and provides the invisible logic by which characters recognise one another across estrangement and disguise. A knight is known by his blood — and by the blood he sheds.

## Noble Blood and Social Identity

Throughout Malory's narrative, [[baron|noble]] blood is the prerequisite for [[chivalry|chivalric]] virtue. Those of low [[lineage|birth]] cannot, by definition, be knights; those of high [[lineage|birth]] cannot help but show their quality, however obscure their circumstances:

> he is come of a noble blood and of king's lineage (136-009)

> howbeit he be, he is come of a noble blood (139-048)

> he is come of a full noble blood; and as for his might and hardiness, there be but few now living that is so mighty (145-036)

The refrain "come of a [[baron|noble]] blood" is Malory's shorthand for innate worth. The body itself testifies to lineage — a knight of [[baron|noble]] blood [[battle|fight]]s better, endures more, and reveals his quality in the test of arms. This is not meritocracy but its opposite: worth is inherited, not earned.

## The Stigma of Low Blood

The corollary of [[baron|noble]] blood's prestige is the contempt visited upon those of low [[lineage|birth]]. [[King Arthur]] himself is scorned as:

> a boy of no high blood born (009-026)

And when the young [[king-arthur|Arthur]] sends messengers to the rebel [[kinsman|kin]]gs, they dismiss him:

> that beardless boy that was come of low blood (011-016)

These insults, voiced by the rebel [[kinsman|kin]]gs, are the ultimate denial of legitimacy. In the [[king-arthur|Arthur]]ian world, to be of low blood is to be unfit to rule — and the narrative's vindication of [[king-arthur|Arthur]] rests on the revelation that he is, in truth, of the highest blood in the [[realm|land]].

## Bloodshed and Combat

Blood is not only inherited but spilled. Malory's battle scenes are drenched in it:

> their horses went in blood up to the fetlocks (020-016)

> much blood they bled both, that all the place thereas they fought was overbled with blood (026-059)

The blood shed in [[battle|combat]] is the visible sign of knightly worth — the more blood a knight loses, the greater his [[hardiness]]. Yet bloodshed also carries the taint of violence: the blood that proves a knight's cou[[wrath|rage]] is also the blood that cries out for [[revenge|vengeance]]. The line between [[worship|honour]]able [[battle|combat]] and [[feud]] is drawn in blood.

## The Healing Blood of the Grail

Blood is also the medium of healing and sacrifice in the [[Sangreal]] episodes:

> she had a dish of silver full of blood (044-027)

The blood that appears in the Grail quest transforms bodily substance into spiritual sacrifice:

> she shall bleed of her blood in a dish of silver (044-029)

> her blood helped not the lady (044-035)

The blood that heals and the blood that kills are two sides of the same coin in Malory's symbolic economy — both flow from the body, and both mark the boundary between the mortal world and the divine.
