[048-003]And when Garnish beheld her so lying, for pure sorrow his mouth and nose burst

[048-004]out a-bleeding, and with his sword he smote off both their heads, and then he

[048-005]made sorrow out of measure, and said, O Balin, much sorrow hast thou brought

[048-006]unto me, for hadst thou not shewed me that sight I should have passed my

[048-007]sorrow. Forsooth, said Balin, I did it to this intent that it should better thy

[048-008]courage, and that ye might see and know her falsehood, and to cause you to

[048-009]leave love of such a lady; God knoweth I did none other but as I would ye did

[048-010]to me. Alas, said Garnish, now is my sorrow double that I may not endure, now

[048-011]have I slain that I most loved in all my life; and therewith suddenly he rove

[048-012]himself on his own sword unto the hilts. When Balin saw that, he dressed him

[048-013]thenceward, lest folk would say he had slain them; and so he rode forth, and

[048-014]within three days he came by a cross, and thereon were letters of gold written,

[048-015]that said, It is not for no knight alone to ride toward this castle. Then saw

[048-016]he an old hoar gentleman coming toward him, that said, Balin le Savage, thou

[048-017]passest thy bounds to come this way, therefore turn again and it will avail

[048-018]thee. And he vanished away anon; and so he heard an horn blow as it had been

[048-019]the death of a beast. That blast, said Balin, is blown for me, for I am the

[048-020]prize and yet am I not dead. Anon withal he saw an hundred ladies and many

[048-021]knights, that welcomed him with fair semblant, and made him passing good cheer

[048-022]unto his sight, and led him into the castle, and there was dancing and

[048-023]minstrelsy and all manner of joy. Then the chief lady of the castle said,

[048-024]Knight with the Two Swords, ye must have ado and joust with a knight hereby

[048-025]that keepeth an island, for there may no man pass this way but he must joust or

[048-026]he pass. That is an unhappy custom, said Balin, that a knight may not pass this

[048-027]way but if he joust. Ye shall not have ado but with one knight, said the lady.

[048-028]Well, said Balin, since I shall thereto I am ready, but travelling men are oft

[048-029]weary and their horses too, but though my horse be weary my heart is not weary,

[048-030]I would be fain there my death should be. Sir, said a knight to Balin,

[048-031]methinketh your shield is not good, I will lend you a bigger. Thereof I pray

[048-032]you. And so he took the shield that was unknown and left his own, and so rode

[048-033]unto the island, and put him and his horse in a great boat; and when he came on

[048-034]the other side he met with a damosel, and she said, O knight Balin, why have ye

[048-035]left your own shield? alas ye have put yourself in great danger, for by your

[048-036]shield ye should have been known; it is great [[mercy|pity]] of you as ever was of

[048-037]knight, for of thy [[courage|prowess]] and hardiness thou hast no fellow living. Me

[048-038]repenteth, said Balin, that ever I came within this country, but I may not turn

[048-039]now again for shame, and what adventure shall fall to me, be it life or death,

[048-040]I will take the adventure that shall come to me. And then he looked on his

[048-041]armour, and understood he was well armed, and therewith blessed him and mounted

[048-042]upon his horse.
