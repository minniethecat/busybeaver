# Sir Balan

Sir Balan was a knight of [[King Arthur]]'s court and the younger brother of [[Sir Balin|Balin le Savage]]. His story, recorded in [[Le Morte d'Arthur]], is one of the most tragic episodes in Arthurian legend: two brothers who fought together against the enemies of the realm, only to slay one another in unrecognised combat.

## Brother of Balin le Savage

Balan was bound to Balin by ties of blood and battle. Together they were known as marvellous good knights, worthy of renown throughout the kingdom (021-023). When Balin met with his brother Balan after long separation, the two wept for joy and pity at their reunion (037-018). Their bond was absolute, and they agreed to take on adventures together, united against the foes of the Round Table (037-027).

## The Two Swords

In the war against [[King Lot]] of Orkney, Balan and Balin proved themselves indispensable to Arthur's cause. Together, the brothers put King Lot and his host to great rebuke (041-032), and their valour earned them the admiration of the court. Even King Arthur himself, when lamenting his embattled position, cried out for Balin and Balan and [[Sir Pellinore|Pellinore]] (042-016), naming them among the knights most essential to his realm's survival.

## The Tragedy of Mutual Slaying

The bond that had made Balan and Balin invincible in battle became the instrument of their destruction. In a tragic turn, the two brothers met in combat without recognising one another, neither knowing they fought their own blood. Balan smote Balin first, nearly felling him (049-012), and Balin in turn dealt his brother a grievous blow that well-nigh had felled Balan (049-014). The battle raged until Balan, the younger brother, withdrew in exhaustion, bloodied and dying (049-024).

In the final moments, as Balan lay mortally wounded, he revealed his identity: "My name is Balan, brother unto the good knight Balin" (049-026). The horror of recognition came too late. Balin wept over his dying brother, and [[Merlin]] came and placed both brothers in the same tomb, uniting them in death as they had been united in life.

## The Pattern of Fraternal Tragedy

Balan's death at his own brother's hand is one of the earliest and most powerful statements of the tragic vision that runs through [[Le Morte d'Arthur]]. The mutual slaying of Balin and Balan prefigures the larger collapse of the Round Table fellowship, where bonds of love and loyalty become the very instruments of destruction. Balan's story is a warning: in a world governed by fate and mischance, even the purest bonds of blood can turn to sorrow.
