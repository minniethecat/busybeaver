# Siege

**Siege**  [[battle|war]]fare in Malory's "Le Morte d'[[king-arthur|Arthur]]" is the principal form of large-scale military action, appearing at critical junctures from the opening conflict between [[King Uther]] and the [[duke|Duke]] of Tintagil to the final tragic siege of [[Joyous Gard]] and the destruction of the [[Round Table]]. Unlike the single [[battle|combat]] that defines individual knightly identity, siege [[battle|war]]fare represents collective action — the marshaling of armies, the [[test|testing]] of alliances, and the imposition of strategic will upon defended positions that cannot be carried by [[courage|prowess]] alone.

## The Mechanics of Siege

Malory's sieges follow a recognizable pattern: an army invests a castle or walled city, cutting off supply and reinforcement while mounting assaults against the defenses. The besieged defend from within, occasionally sallying forth for skirmishes that allow individual knights to demonstrate [[Prowess]]:

> "laid a siege about the castle of Terrabil" (004-032)
> "the king rode from the siege of Terrabil" (005-022)

The narrative treats the siege as a persistent state — once "laid," it becomes the backdrop against which other actions (individual [[battle|combat]]s, diplomatic missions, [[marvel|supernatural]] interventions) unfold. The siege is rarely described in tactical detail; its narrative function is to establish a condition of pressure that forces decisions and reveals character.

## Siege as Political Instrument

Sieges in Malory are not merely military operations but political declarations. To lay siege to a castle is to assert a claim — to [[baron|lord]]ship, to justice, or to [[revenge|vengeance]] — that cannot be resolved by diplomacy. The recurrent sieges in the early books of the work establish the political geography of [[king-arthur|Arthur]]ian Britain, defining which [[baron|lord]]s hold which [[castle|stronghold]]s and whose claims are contested:

> "they laid a siege unto the castle of Bedegraine" (015-028)
> "have laid siege at the castle Wandesborow" (020-035)

The siege of [[Bedegraine]] and the siege of Wandesborow mark the two major campaigns through which [[King Arthur]] consolidates his rule, transforming from a contested young [[kinsman|kin]]g into the undisputed [[king|sovereign]] of Britain.

## Siege as Moral Test

The siege also functions as a moral test for the knights involved. The besieger must ba[[spear|lance]] the demands of military necessity against the code of [[Chivalry]]: a siege conducted with unnecessary cruelty or against non[[battle|combat]]ants draws narrative condemnation. The besieged, meanwhile, must decide when continued resistance becomes futile and whether surrender is [[honour|honor]]able or co[[battle|war]]dly:

> "King Rience lieth at a siege" (037-030)

The siege brings into focus the tension between the collective logic of [[battle|war]]fare and the individualistic code of knightly [[honour|honor]]. A knight may win [[Worship]] through individual feats during a siege, but the siege itself is won or lost through patience, logistics, and numbers — qualities that have little to do with individual [[courage|prowess]].

## The Siege Perilous

The word "siege" carries a second, distinct meaning in Malory's text. The Siege Perilous (from Old French *siège*, "seat") is the empty seat at the [[Round Table]], reserved by [[Merlin]] for the knight who will achieve the [[Sangreal]]:

> "their sieges blessed by the Bishop of Canterbury" (053-001)
> "Merlin found in every sieges letters of gold" (053-016)
> "two sieges were void" (053-017)

This dual meaning enriches the concept: just as a military siege surrounds and tests a castle, the Siege Perilous surrounds and tests the unworthy — those who presume to sit in it perish, while only [[Sir Galahad]], the perfect knight, can occupy it safely. The convergence of military siege and sacred seat in a single term reflects the work's broader fusion of chivalric and religious meaning.

## Siege's Narrative Function

Siege [[battle|war]]fare structures the large-scale conflict that frames the work's episodes of individual adventure. The sieges of the early books establish [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s dominion; the siege of [[Joyous Gard]] in the work's tragic final movement marks the rupture between [[Sir Launcelot]] and [[Sir Gawaine]]; and the siege of [[Benwick]] carries the conflict to its bitter conclusion. The siege thus serves as both the foundation and the destruction of the [[king-arthur|Arthur]]ian polity — the military form through which order is first imposed and later shattered.
