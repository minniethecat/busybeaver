# Worship

**Worship**  in Malory's "Le Morte d'[[king-arthur|Arthur]]" is the central measure of a knight's worth — a compound of [[honour|honor]], [[honour|reputation]], martial [[courage|prowess]], and [[baron|noble]] [[blood|lineage]] that must be publicly demonstrated and acknowledged. Unlike the modern religious sense, Malorian [[honour|worship]] is thoroughly secular and social: it is earned through deeds of arms before witnesses, lost through shameful conduct, and preserved through constant vigi[[spear|lance]]. The pursuit of [[honour|worship]] drives nearly every named knight's actions, from [[King Arthur]]'s establishment of the [[Round Table]] to the tragic rivalry between [[Sir Launcelot]] and [[Sir Gawaine]].

## Worship as Public Reputation

Worship in Malory is never private — it exists only insofar as it is seen and spoken of by others. When a knight performs a great deed, it must be witnessed and reported to the court; otherwise it is as if it never happened. The phrase "a knight of [[honour|worship]]" recurs throughout the text as a formulaic designation of rank and regard, applied to those who have proven themselves in open [[battle|combat]] or [[baron|noble]] service:

> "the one hight Lionses, lord of the town of Payarne, and Sir Phariance a worshipful knight" (013-050)

A knight of [[honour|worship]] is distinguished not merely by his skill but by his consistent demonstration of it before credible witnesses. The loss of [[honour|worship]], conversely, comes from actions that tarnish this public standing — fleeing from battle, brea[[kinsman|kin]]g an oath, or being defeated in dis[[honour|honor]]able circumstances.

## Worship and Deeds of Arms

The primary mechanism for acquiring [[honour|worship]] is the performance of "deeds of arms" — feats of martial skill performed in tournament, battle, or single [[battle|combat]]. Sir Thomas Malory describes knights as [[quest|seeking]] [[honour|worship]] through these feats, and the narrative repeatedly measures a knight's worth by the number and quality of his martial achievements:

> "great worship; and if they were longing unto the court of King Arthur they should win such worship that all the world should speak of it" (019-054)

The connection between [[honour|worship]] and action is absolute: a knight who does not actively seek and win [[honour|worship]] through [[battle|combat]] ceases to be regarded as a knight of worth. This creates the restless, questing energy that drives the narrative — knights must constantly ride out, seek adventures, and prove themselves anew.

## Worship and Noble Lineage

While [[honour|worship]] is earned through deeds, it is also intimately tied to blood and [[blood|lineage]]. The phrase "come of [[honour|worship]]" appears in the text to describe those of [[baron|noble]] [[lineage|birth]], suggesting that [[honour|worship]] inheres partly in one's [[lineage|ancestry]] and cannot be fully achieved by those of base origin:

> "thou art come of worship, and therefore thou shalt have my love and my lordship" (010-026)

This tension between earned merit and inherited status runs through the ent[[wrath|ire]] work: knights like [[Sir Tor]] and [[Sir Gareth]] must prove themselves despite uncertain or concealed [[blood|lineage]], while the greatest knights arise from established [[baron|noble]] houses.

## Shame as the Counterpoint of Worship

The concept of [[honour|worship]] is defined as much by its opposite — shame — as by its positive content. A knight's constant fear is to "lose his [[honour|worship]]" through co[[battle|war]]dice, dis[[honour|honor]], or defeat. The narrator repeatedly notes that certain actions would bring "no [[honour|worship]]" to a knight, and characters frequently invoke the threat of shame to motivate action:

> "King Arthur thought no worship to have a knight at such avail" (026-055)

This shame-[[honour|worship]] binary forms the moral spine of chivalric conduct in Malory's world. A knight cannot simply refrain from evil; he must actively pursue [[honour|worship]] to avoid the default state of shame.

## Worship's Narrative Function

Worship serves as the narrative's engine of motivation. Knights undertake perilous quests not for material re[[battle|war]]d but for the increase of their [[honour|worship]]. This pursuit creates both the episodic structure of adventure and the tragic arc of the whole work: the [[Round Table]] is founded on the collective pursuit of [[honour|worship]], and its dissolution follows the systematic destruction of [[honour|worship]] through [[treason|betrayal]], adultery, and [[kinsman|kin]]strife. The concept binds together the work's celebration of chivalry and its elegiac recognition of chivalry's fragility.
