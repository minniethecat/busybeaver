[102-003]Now turn we to the Emperor of Rome, which espied that these prisoners should be

[102-004]sent to Paris, and anon he sent to lie in a bushment certain knights and

[102-005]princes with sixty thousand men, for to rescue his knights and lords that were

[102-006]prisoners. And so on the morn as [[sir-launcelot|Launcelot]] and Sir Cador, chieftains and

[102-007]governors of all them that conveyed the prisoners, as they should pass through

[102-008]a wood, Sir Launcelot sent certain knights to espy if any were in the woods to

[102-009]let them. And when the said knights came into the wood, anon they espied and

[102-010]saw the great embushment, and returned and told Sir Launcelot that there lay in

[102-011]await for them three score thousand Romans. And then Sir Launcelot with such

[102-012]knights as he had, and men of [[battle|war]] to the number of ten thousand, put them in

[102-013]array, and met with them and fought with them manly, and slew and detrenched

[102-014]many of the Romans, and slew many knights and admirals of the party of the

[102-015]Romans and Saracens; there was slain the king of Lyly and three great lords,

[102-016]Aladuke, Herawd, and Heringdale. But Sir Launcelot fought so nobly that no man

[102-017]might endure a stroke of his hand, but where he came he showed his [[courage|prowess]] and

[102-018]might, for he slew down right on every side; and the Romans and Saracens fled

[102-019]from him as the sheep from the wolf or from the lion, and put them, all that

[102-020]abode alive, to flight.

[102-021]And so long they fought that tidings came to [[king-arthur|King Arthur]], and anon he graithed

[102-022]him and came to the battle, and saw his knights how they had vanquished the

[102-023]battle, he embraced them knight by knight in his arms, and said, Ye be worthy

[102-024]to wield all your honour and [[honour|worship]]; there was never king save myself that had

[102-025]so noble knights. Sir, said Cador, there was none of us failed other, but of

[102-026]the prowess and manhood of Sir Launcelot were more than [[marvel|wonder]] to tell, and

[102-027]also of his cousins which did that day many noble feats of war. And also Sir

[102-028]Cador told who of his knights were slain, as Sir Berel, and other Sir Moris and

[102-029]Sir Maurel, two good knights. Then the king wept, and dried his eyes with a

[102-030]kerchief, and said, Your courage had near-hand destroyed you, for though ye had

[102-031]returned again, ye had lost no worship; for I call it folly, knights to abide

[102-032]when they be overmatched. Nay, said Launcelot and the other, for once shamed

[102-033]may never be recovered.
