[076-001]fought with brake, and how he recovered of Accolon his own sword [[sword-in-the-stone|Excalibur]], and

[076-002]overcame his enemy.

[076-003]Then Sir Accolon began with words of treason, and said, Knight, thou art

[076-004]overcome, and mayst not endure, and also thou art weaponless, and thou hast

[076-005]lost much of thy blood, and I am full loath to slay thee, therefore yield thee

[076-006]to me as recreant. Nay, said Sir [[king-arthur|Arthur]], I may not so, for I have promised to

[076-007]do the battle to the uttermost by the faith of my body, while me lasteth the

[076-008]life, and therefore I had liefer to die with honour than to live with shame;

[076-009]and if it were possible for me to die an hundred times, I had liefer to die so

[076-010]oft than yield me to thee; for though I lack weapon, I shall lack no [[honour|worship]],

[076-011]and if thou slay me weaponless that shall be thy shame. Well, said Accolon, as

[076-012]for the shame I will not spare, now keep thee from me, for thou art but a dead

[076-013]man. And therewith Accolon gave him such a stroke that he fell nigh to the

[076-014]earth, and would have had Arthur to have cried him mercy. But Sir Arthur

[076-015]pressed unto Accolon with his shield, and gave him with the pommel in his hand

[076-016]such a buffet that he went three strides aback.

[076-017]When the Damosel of the Lake beheld Arthur, how full of [[courage|prowess]] his body was,

[076-018]and the false treason that was wrought for him to have had him slain, she had

[076-019]great [[mercy|pity]] that so good a knight and such a man of worship should so be

[076-020]destroyed. And at the next stroke Sir Accolon struck him such a stroke that by

[076-021]the damosel’s enchantment the sword Excalibur fell out of Accolon’s

[076-022]hand to the earth. And therewithal Sir Arthur lightly leapt to it, and gat it

[076-023]in his hand, and forthwithal he knew that it was his sword Excalibur, and said,

[076-024]Thou hast been from me all too long, and much damage hast thou done me; and

[076-025]therewith he espied the scabbard hanging by his side, and suddenly he sterte to

[076-026]him and pulled the scabbard from him, and threw it from him as far as he might

[076-027]throw it. O knight, said Arthur, this day hast thou done me great damage with

[076-028]this sword; now are ye come unto your death, for I shall not warrant you but ye

[076-029]shall as well be rewarded with this sword, or ever we depart, as thou hast

[076-030]rewarded me; for much pain have ye made me to endure, and much blood have I

[076-031]lost. And therewith Sir Arthur rushed on him with all his might and pulled him

[076-032]to the earth, and then rushed off his helm, and gave him such a buffet on the

[076-033]head that the blood came out at his ears, his nose, and his mouth. Now will I

[076-034]slay thee, said Arthur. Slay me ye may well, said Accolon, an it please you,

[076-035]for ye are the best knight that ever I found, and I see well that God is with

[076-036]you. But for I promised to do this battle, said Accolon, to the uttermost, and

[076-037]never to be recreant while I lived, therefore shall I never yield me with my

[076-038]mouth, but God do with my body what he will. Then Sir Arthur remembered him,

[076-039]and thought he should have seen this knight. Now tell me, said Arthur, or I

[076-040]will slay thee, of what country art thou, and of what court? Sir Knight, said

[076-041]Sir Accolon, I am of [[camelot|the court of King Arthur]], and my name is Accolon of Gaul.

[076-042]Then was Arthur more dismayed than he was beforehand; for then he remembered

[076-043]him of his sister [[morgan-le-fay|Morgan le Fay]], and of the enchantment of the ship. O sir

[076-044]knight, said he, I pray you tell me who gave you this sword, and by whom ye had

[076-045]it.
