[216-001]Meliagaunce, and fought together for the beauty of Dame [[queen-guenever|Guenever]].

[216-002]Right so as they stood talking thus Sir Lamorak was ware how [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]] came

[216-003]riding straight toward them; then Sir Lamorak saluted him, and he him again.

[216-004]And then Sir Lamorak asked Sir Launcelot if there were anything that he might

[216-005]do for him in these marches. Nay, said Sir Launcelot, not at this time I thank

[216-006]you. Then either departed from other, and Sir Lamorak rode again thereas he

[216-007]left the two knights, and then he found them hid in the leaved wood. Fie on

[216-008]you, said Sir Lamorak, false cowards, [[mercy|pity]] and shame it is that any of you

[216-009]should take the high order of knighthood. So Sir Lamorak departed from them,

[216-010]and within a while he met with Sir Meliagaunce. And then Sir Lamorak asked him

[216-011]why he loved Queen Guenever as he did: For I was not far from you when ye made

[216-012]your complaint by the chapel. Did ye so? said Sir Meliagaunce, then will I

[216-013]abide by it: I love Queen Guenever, what will ye with it? I will prove and make

[216-014]good that she is the fairest lady and most of beauty in the world. As to that,

[216-015]said Sir Lamorak, I say nay thereto, for Queen Morgawse of Orkney, mother to

[216-016][[sir-gawaine|Sir Gawaine]], and his mother is the fairest queen and lady that beareth the

[216-017]life. That is not so, said Sir Meliagaunce, and that will I prove with my hands

[216-018]upon thy body. Will ye so? said Sir Lamorak, and in a better quarrel keep I not

[216-019]to [[battle|fight]]. Then they departed either from other in great wrath. And then they

[216-020]came riding together as it had been thunder, and either smote other so sore

[216-021]that their horses fell backward to the earth. And then they avoided their

[216-022]horses, and dressed their shields, and drew their swords. And then they hurtled

[216-023]together as wild boars, and thus they fought a great while. For Meliagaunce was

[216-024]a good man and of great might, but Sir Lamorak was hard big for him, and put

[216-025]him always aback, but either had wounded other sore.

[216-026]And as they stood thus fighting, by fortune came Sir Launcelot and Sir

[216-027]Bleoberis riding. And then Sir Launcelot rode betwixt them, and asked them for

[216-028]what cause they fought so together: And ye are both knights of [[king-arthur|King Arthur]]!

[216-029]Sir, said Meliagaunce, I shall tell you for what cause we do this battle. I

[216-030]praised my lady, Queen Guenever, and said she was the fairest lady of the

[216-031]world, and Sir Lamorak said nay thereto, for he said Queen Morgawse of Orkney

[216-032]was fairer than she and more of beauty. Ah, Sir Lamorak, why sayest thou so? it

[216-033]is not thy part to dispraise thy princess that thou art under her obeissance,

[216-034]and we all. And therewith he alighted on foot, and said: For this quarrel, make

[216-035]thee ready, for I will prove upon thee that Queen Guenever is the fairest lady

[216-036]and most of bounty in the world. Sir, said Sir Lamorak, I am loath to have ado

[216-037]with you in this quarrel, for every man thinketh his own lady fairest; and

[216-038]though I praise the lady that I love most ye should not be wroth; for though my

[216-039]lady, Queen Guenever, be fairest in your eye, wit ye well Queen Morgawse of

[216-040]Orkney is fairest in mine eye, and so every knight thinketh his own lady

[216-041]fairest; and wit ye well, sir, ye are the man in the world except [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]]

[216-042]that I am most loathest to have ado withal, but, an ye will needs fight with me

[216-043]I shall endure you as long as I may. Then spake Sir Bleoberis and said: My lord

[216-044]Sir Launcelot, I wist you never so misadvised as ye are now, for Sir Lamorak

[216-045]sayeth you but reason and knightly; for I warn you I have a lady, and

[216-046]methinketh that she is the fairest lady of the world. Were this a great reason

[216-047]that ye should be wroth with me for such language? And well ye wot, that Sir

[216-048]Lamorak is as noble a knight as I know, and he hath ought you and us ever good

[216-049]will, and therefore I pray you be good friends. Then Sir Launcelot said unto

[216-050]Sir Lamorak. I pray you forgive me mine evil will, and if I was misadvised I

[216-051]will amend it. Sir, said Sir Lamorak, the amends is soon made betwixt you and

[216-052]me. And so Sir Launcelot and Sir Bleoberis departed, and Sir Meliagaunce and

[216-053]Sir Lamorak took their horses, and either departed from other.

[216-054]And within a while came King Arthur, and met with Sir Lamorak, and jousted with

[216-055]him; and there he smote down Sir Lamorak, and wounded him sore with a spear,

[216-056]and so he rode from him; wherefore Sir Lamorak was wroth that he would not

[216-057]fight with him on foot, howbeit that Sir Lamorak knew not King Arthur.
