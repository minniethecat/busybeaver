# Custom

**Custom**  in Malory's "Le Morte d'[[king-arthur|Arthur]]" is the unwritten law of the realm — a binding force of tradition and established practice that governs conduct more powerfully than royal decree. Customs determine who may pass a bridge, who must [[battle|fight]] whom, how feasts are conducted, and what obligations a host owes a guest. Unlike the explicit code of [[Chivalry]] or the formal oath of [[Knighthood]], custom operates beneath the surface of conscious choice, shaping the [[realm|land]]scape of [[adventure|knightly adventure]] and often trapping knights in obligations they did not choose.

## The Custom of the Castle

The most prominent and narratively significant form of custom in Malory is the "custom of the castle" — a local rule governing passage, hospitality, or [[battle|combat]] that binds all who enter a [[baron|lord]]'s domain. These customs are ancient, impersonal, and absolute: they apply regardless of the visitor's rank, virtue, or intentions:

> "the custom of this castle is, there shall no knight pass this way but he shall joust with one of our knights" (044-025)

The custom of the castle creates the archetypal adventure encounter: a knight arrives at a castle, bridge, or ford and is informed that local custom requ[[wrath|ire]]s him to [[battle|fight]] before he may pass. Whether he [[battle|fight]]s, and how, becomes a test of both his [[Prowess]] and his character. Some customs are [[honour|honor]]able tests of strength; others are mere predation disguised as tradition.

## Malign Customs and Their Correction

Not all customs are [[honour|honor]]able. Malory's text distinguishes between legitimate customs — those that serve a reasonable purpose and treat all comers fairly — and "ill customs" or "shameful customs" that exploit travelers, oppress the weak, or serve private cruelty under the guise of tradition:

> "destroy that castle for ill customs" (049-037)
> "he useth shameful customs, and it is marvel that he endureth so long" (142-025)

The correction of malign customs is a key function of the knight-errant. Knights like [[Sir Balin]] and [[Sir Launcelot]] encounter castles where unjust customs prevail, and their destruction of these customs — often by killing the [[baron|lord]] who enforces them — constitutes a form of itinerant justice. This pattern positions the knight as an agent of moral order who can override entrenched local practice when it conflicts with the higher code of [[Knighthood]].

## Royal Customs and the Feast of Pentecost

Custom also operates at the royal level, structuring the rhythm of courtly life. [[King Arthur]]'s most famous custom — refusing to eat at the [[feast-of-pentecost|Feast of Pentecost]] until a [[miracle|marvel]] or adventure has presented itself — drives the episodic structure of the narrative:

> "the king had a custom that at the feast of Pentecost, he would not go to his meat until he had heard or seen some great marvel" (128-005)

This royal custom is generative rather than restrictive: far from trapping those who encounter it, it creates the conditions for adventure, drawing knights and [[miracle|marvel]]s to the court at the appointed time. It demonstrates that custom, when wielded by a wise ruler, can serve the values of [[Chivalry]] rather than undermine them.

## Custom as Moral Ambiguity

The most sophisticated treatment of custom in Malory occurs when the text refuses to resolve whether a custom is just or unjust. When [[King Arthur]] responds to complaints about his customs, his answer is both a defense and an evasion:

> "This custom, said Arthur. This custom, said the king, I will defend" (026-034)
> "who is grieved with my custom let him amend it that will" (026-035)

This ambivalence reflects a deeper tension in the chivalric world: customs provide stability and continuity, but they can also perpetuate injustice. The knight's task is not to abolish custom but to discern — through the exercise of [[Worship]] and conscience — which customs serve [[honour|honor]] and which must be overturned.

## Custom's Narrative Function

Custom functions in the narrative as a mechanism for generating obligatory encounters. By establishing that "the custom of this place" requ[[wrath|ire]]s a certain action, Malory creates situations in which knights must act — [[battle|fight]], yield, speak, or defy — regardless of their personal inclination. Custom thus serves as the impersonal trigger of adventure, the tradition that propels knights into the tests that reveal their character and advance the plot. It is, along with [[Quest]] and [[Adventure]], one of the primary structural forces that shape the episodic romance narrative.
