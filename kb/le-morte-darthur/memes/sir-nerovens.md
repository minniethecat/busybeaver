# Sir Nerovens de Lile

Sir Nerovens de Lile was a knight whose story is defined by his relationship to [[Sir Launcelot|Sir Launcelot du Lake]] — made a knight by Launcelot's own hand, he later opposed his lord in arms, only to be reconciled through the grace of the greatest knight of the age. His tale is one of the chronicle's most compact dramas of loyalty, error, and forgiveness.

## Made Knight by Launcelot

Nerovens's identity is inseparable from the man who gave him his knighthood. He declared himself plainly: "my name is Sir Nerovens, that was made knight of my lord Sir Launcelot du Lake" (208-049). The bond between the knight who confers the accolade and the one who receives it was sacred in the Arthurian world, and Nerovens acknowledged this debt in his very introduction.

Launcelot recognised him at once: "Ah, Nerovens de Lile, said Sir Launcelot" (208-050), addressing him by his full name and his territorial designation. The recognition carried weight — Launcelot knew the knight he had made, and the encounter that followed would test the bond between them.

## The Error of Opposition

Despite the sacred bond of knighthood, Nerovens found himself in opposition to Launcelot. The chronicle records his anguish upon realising his fault: "Alas, said Sir Nerovens de Lile, what have I done!" (208-052), a cry of remorse that speaks to the gravity of raising arms against the lord who had knighted him. The exclamation is one of the chronicle's most direct expressions of knightly regret.

Nerovens then confessed his situation: "Sir Nerovens told Sir Launcelot that he should" (208-055) make plain the circumstances that had led him to this breach of fealty. The chronicle preserves the moment of accounting between the lord who had given knighthood and the knight who had strayed.

## The Intercession of the Lady

While Nerovens contended with his error, a lady of his household became involved. The chronicle records that "the meanwhile Sir Nerovens, that Sir Launcelot had fought" (209-028) remained in the background as events unfolded. His wife was aware of the situation: "Launcelot, but her lord, Sir Nerovens, hath told her. But, damosel" (210-013), the lady acting as intermediary between the two knights.

## Reconciliation

The story of Nerovens ends not in tragedy but in restoration. The chronicle records with simplicity that "then Sir Launcelot sent for Nerovens that he made once knight, and" (212-009) summoned him back into fellowship.

Launcelot did not abandon the knight he had made — the bond forged in the accolade proved stronger than the error of opposition. Nerovens de Lile, who had cried out "what have I done," was received again by the lord who had made him.

## The Knight Who Remembered

Sir Nerovens de Lile's story is a testament to the gravity of the knightly bond in the Arthurian world. To be made a knight by Launcelot was an honour; to oppose him was a breach that demanded remorse.

To be sent for again — to be called back by the lord who had once conferred the accolade — was grace. In the compact arc of Nerovens's tale, the chronicle distills the essential drama of loyalty, error, and the possibility of return.
