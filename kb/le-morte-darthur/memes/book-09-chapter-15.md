[218-001]Forest Perilous, and how [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]] saved his life.

[218-002]Now shall ye hear what was the cause that [[king-arthur|King Arthur]] came into the Forest

[218-003]Perilous, that was in North Wales, by the means of a lady. Her name was

[218-004]Annowre, and this lady came to King Arthur at Cardiff; and she by fair promise

[218-005]and fair behests made King Arthur to ride with her into that Forest Perilous;

[218-006]and she was a great sorceress; and many days she had loved King Arthur, and

[218-007]because she would have him to lie by her she came into that country. So when

[218-008]the king was gone with her many of his knights followed after King Arthur when

[218-009]they missed him, as [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]], Brandiles, and many other; and when she had

[218-010]brought him to her tower she desired him to lie by her; and then the king

[218-011]remembered him of his lady, and would not lie by her for no craft that she

[218-012]could do. Then every day she would make him ride into that forest with his own

[218-013]knights, to the intent to have had King Arthur slain. For when this Lady

[218-014]Annowre saw that she might not have him at her will, then she laboured by false

[218-015]means to have destroyed King Arthur, and slain.

[218-016]Then the [[lady-of-the-lake|Lady of the Lake]] that was alway friendly to King Arthur, she

[218-017]understood by her subtle crafts that King Arthur was like to be destroyed. And

[218-018]therefore this Lady of the Lake, that hight Nimue, came into that forest to

[218-019]seek after Sir Launcelot du Lake or Sir Tristram for to help King Arthur; foras

[218-020]that same day this Lady of the Lake knew well that King Arthur should be slain,

[218-021]unless that he had help of one of these two knights. And thus she rode up and

[218-022]down till she met with Sir Tristram, and anon as she saw him she knew him. O my

[218-023]lord Sir Tristram, she said, well be ye met, and blessed be the time that I

[218-024]have met with you; for this same day, and within these two hours, shall be done

[218-025]the foulest deed that ever was done in this land. O fair damosel, said Sir

[218-026]Tristram, may I amend it. Come on with me, she said, and that in all the haste

[218-027]ye may, for ye shall see the most worshipfullest knight of the world hard

[218-028]bestead. Then said Sir Tristram: I am ready to help such a noble man. He is

[218-029]neither better nor worse, said the Lady of the Lake, but the noble King Arthur

[218-030]himself. God defend, said Sir Tristram, that ever he should be in such

[218-031]distress. Then they rode together a great pace, until they came to a little

[218-032]turret or castle; and underneath that castle they saw a knight standing upon

[218-033]foot fighting with two knights; and so Sir Tristram beheld them, and at the

[218-034]last the two knights smote down the one knight, and that one of them unlaced

[218-035]his helm to have slain him. And the Lady Annowre gat King Arthur’s sword

[218-036]in her hand to have stricken off his head. And therewithal came Sir Tristram

[218-037]with all his might, crying: Traitress, traitress, leave that. And anon there

[218-038]Sir Tristram smote the one of the knights through the body that he fell dead;

[218-039]and then he rashed to the other and smote his back asunder; and in the

[218-040]meanwhile the Lady of the Lake cried to King Arthur: Let not that false lady

[218-041]escape. Then King Arthur overtook her, and with the same sword he smote off her

[218-042]head, and the Lady of the Lake took up her head and hung it up by the hair of

[218-043]her saddle-bow. And then Sir Tristram horsed King Arthur and rode forth with

[218-044]him, but he charged the Lady of the Lake not to discover his name as at that

[218-045]time.

[218-046]When the king was horsed he thanked heartily Sir Tristram, and desired to wit

[218-047]his name; but he would not tell him, but that he was a poor knight adventurous;

[218-048]and so he bare King Arthur fellowship till he met with some of his knights. And

[218-049]within a while he met with Sir Ector de Maris, and he knew not King Arthur nor

[218-050]Sir Tristram, and he desired to joust with one of them. Then Sir Tristram rode

[218-051]unto Sir Ector, and smote him from his horse. And when he had done so he came

[218-052]again to the king and said: My lord, yonder is one of your knights, he may bare

[218-053]you fellowship, and another day that deed that I have done for you I trust to

[218-054]God ye shall understand that I would do you service. Alas, said King Arthur,

[218-055]let me wit what ye are? Not at this time, said Sir Tristram. So he departed and

[218-056]left King Arthur and Sir Ector together.
