[174-001][[sir-tristram|Tristram]] had slain her brother Sir Marhaus by his sword, and in what jeopardy

[174-002]he was.

[174-003]Thus was Sir Tramtrist long there well cherished with the king and the queen,

[174-004]and namely with La Beale Isoud. So upon a day the queen and La Beale Isoud made

[174-005]a bain for Sir Tramtrist. And when he was in his bain the queen and Isoud, her

[174-006]daughter, roamed up and down in the chamber; and therewhiles Gouvernail and

[174-007]Hebes attended upon Tramtrist, and the queen beheld his sword thereas it lay

[174-008]upon his bed. And then by unhap the queen drew out his sword and beheld it a

[174-009]long while, and both they thought it a passing fair sword; but within a foot

[174-010]and an half of the point there was a great piece thereof out-broken of the

[174-011]edge. And when the queen espied that gap in the sword, she remembered her of a

[174-012]piece of a sword that was found in the brain-pan of Sir Marhaus, the good

[174-013]knight that was her brother. Alas then, said she unto her daughter, La Beale

[174-014]Isoud, this is the same traitor knight that slew my brother, thine eme. When

[174-015]Isoud heard her say so she was passing sore abashed, for passing well she loved

[174-016]Tramtrist, and full well she knew the cruelness of her mother the queen.

[174-017]Anon therewithal the queen went unto her own chamber, and sought her coffer,

[174-018]and there she took out the piece or the sword that was pulled out of Sir

[174-019]Marhaus’ head after that he was dead. And then she ran with that piece of

[174-020]iron to the sword that lay upon the bed. And when she put that piece of steel

[174-021]and iron unto the sword, it was as meet as it might be when it was new broken.

[174-022]And then the queen gripped that sword in her hand fiercely, and with all her

[174-023]might she ran straight upon Tramtrist where he sat in his bain, and there she

[174-024]had rived him through had not Sir Hebes gotten her in his arms, and pulled the

[174-025]sword from her, and else she had thrust him through.

[174-026]Then when she was let of her evil will she ran to [[king-arthur|the King]] Anguish, her

[174-027]husband, and said on her knees: O my lord, here have ye in your house that

[174-028]traitor knight that slew my brother and your servant, that noble knight, Sir

[174-029]Marhaus. Who is that, said King Anguish, and where is he? Sir, she said, it is

[174-030]Sir Tramtrist, the same knight that my daughter healed. Alas, said the king,

[174-031]therefore am I right heavy, for he is a full noble knight as ever I saw in

[174-032][[battle|field]]. But I charge you, said the king to the queen, that ye have not ado with

[174-033]that knight, but let me deal with him.

[174-034]Then the king went into the chamber unto Sir Tramtrist, and then was he gone

[174-035]unto his chamber, and the king found him all ready armed to mount upon his

[174-036]horse. When the king saw him all ready armed to go unto horseback, the king

[174-037]said: Nay, Tramtrist, it will not avail to compare thee against me; but thus

[174-038]much I shall do for my [[honour|worship]] and for thy love; in so much as thou art within

[174-039]my court it were no worship for me to slay thee: therefore upon this condition

[174-040]I will give thee leave for to depart from this court in safety, so thou wilt

[174-041]tell me who was thy father, and what is thy name, and if thou slew Sir Marhaus,

[174-042]my brother.
