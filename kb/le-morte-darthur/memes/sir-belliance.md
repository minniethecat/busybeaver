# Sir Belliance

**Sir Belliance** — also called Belliance le Angre — is a knight of the [[Round Table]] in Thomas Malory's *Le Morte d'[[King Arthur]]*, remembered primarily for his encounter with his brother [[Sir Lamorak|Sir Lamorak de Galis]]. Their meeting, which passes from combat to recognition to tender care, forms one of the most delicately rendered portraits of fraternal [[love]] in the Morte.

## The Courteous Combat

Belliance first appears as an unnamed opponent engaged in "courteous fighting with Sir Belliance his brother" — meaning [[Sir Lamorak]] (202-113). What begins as a passage of arms between two knights who do not recognise each other soon takes an unexpected turn. When Belliance learns his opponent's identity — "Sir Lamorak de Galis" — his response is immediate and heartfelt: "Ah, said Sir Belliance, thou art the man in the world that I most love" (202-138).

## Recognition and Reverence

Rather than press the combat, Belliance falls to his knees: "Sir Belliance prayed him to tell him his name" and upon hearing it, knelt in veneration of the knight he most admired (202-137–138). [[Sir Lamorak]], moved by this display, responds with corresponding [[humility]]: "Arise, said Sir Belliance, or else thereas thou kneelest I shall kneel" (202-144). The scene becomes a mutual genuflection — two great knights, brothers in blood and in [[chivalry]], each refusing to stand above the other.

Lamorak at last insists: "Belliance, leave thy kneeling, or else I shall arise and fight with thee" (202-149). And so they fight — but their combat is an expression of [[honour]] rather than enmity, a testing of prowess between equals bound by [[love]].

## The Wound and the Care

The duel concludes when "at the last Belliance withdrew him aback and fell flatling to the earth" (202-151). Lamorak, having bested his brother, does not exult. He asks after Belliance's condition with genuine concern: "Ah, Knight Sir Belliance, said Sir Lamorak, thou art a passing good knight" (202-155). Learning that Belliance is sorely hurt, Lamorak "led Sir Belliance to an abbey fast by" (202-160) and there remained with him — "Lamorak would not depart from Belliance till he was whole" (202-161).

This final image — one brother wounded, the other refusing to leave his bedside until he is healed — distinguishes the Belliance-Lamorak relationship from the many fraternal conflicts that darken the Morte. Where brothers like Sir Damas and Sir Ontzlake tear each other apart, Belliance and Lamorak model a [[kinsman|kinship]] that is both martial and tender.

## A Light in the Morte

Belliance's role in the narrative is brief but thematically significant. In a text dominated by brother-betrayals — [[Mordred]] against Arthur, [[Sir Andred]] against [[Sir Tristram]], Sir Damas against his brother — Belliance offers a counter-example. His story asserts that the bond between brothers need not be a site of destruction; it can also be a source of healing.
