# Sir Andred

**Sir Andred** is the cousin of [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]] and a knight of Cornwall in Thomas Malory's *Le Morte d'[[king-arthur|Arthur]]*. He functions as the shadow to Tristram's light — a kinsman who, instead of offering [[fealty|loyalty]], becomes Tristram's most persistent betrayer. Where Tristram embodies chivalric virtue, Andred embodies the destructive power of [[envy]] within the bonds of [[kinsman|kinship]].

## The Spy in Cornwall

Andred serves [[king-mark|King Mark]] of Cornwall as a watcher and informant against Tristram. His role is established early: "ever Sir Andred, that was nigh cousin to Sir Tristram, lay in a window to watch" (195-007). From this hidden vantage, Andred observes Tristram's meetings with [[la-beale-isoud|La Beale Isoud]] and reports what he sees to [[king-mark|King Mark]]: "that espied Sir Andred, and told it to the King" (195-010). His watching is constant; Malory repeats that "ever Sir Andred his cousin watched him night and day" (197-031), making Andred the embodiment of surveillance in a court poisoned by jealousy.

## The Night of Betrayal

The most consequential act of Andred's treachery occurs when he orchestrates Tristram's capture. Having "espied the hour and the time when Sir Tristram went to his lady," Andred "gat unto him twelve knights armed" and lay in wait (197-032–033). He ambushes Tristram unarmed and alone, binding him and bringing him before King Mark as a prisoner.

Tristram's response to this betrayal is remarkable in its restraint. He appeals to kinship: "O Andred, Andred, said Sir Tristram, think on our kinship" (197-049). Andred's cold reply — "No, I will not spare you" (197-051) — marks the absolute perversion of the [[kinsman|kin]] bond. Tristram does escape, seizing a sword and smiting Andred to the ground (197-055–056), but Andred survives this encounter, protected by the hundred men who rally to his side (197-059).

## The Traitor's End

Andred survives his early betrayals and continues to scheme against Tristram throughout the narrative. He falsely accuses Tristram before King Mark (222-002), claims lordship over lands that are not his (222-006), and "would have had Sir Tristram's lands" for himself (222-007). His motivations crystallize into a single driving force: [[envy]] of Tristram's prowess, lands, and love.

In the final conflicts of the Morte, Andred meets his end at the hands of knights loyal to the Round Table. He is struck down by Sir Uwaine (240-028) and later smitten from his horse by Gaheris (241-029) — the instrument of Tristram's destruction finally destroyed by those who serve a higher code of [[chivalry]].
