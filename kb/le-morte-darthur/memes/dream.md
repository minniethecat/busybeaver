The **dream** in Malory's [[Le Morte d'Arthur]] is never merely a dream. It is a vehicle of [[prophecy]], a conduit for divine [[battle|war]]ning, and a narrative device that reveals to the reader what the characters themselves may only dimly understand. When [[King Arthur]] dreams, the [[realm|kingdom]] itself is being addressed—the dream is a royal genre, and its interpretation is a matter of state.

## The Royal Dream

Dreams in the Morte are overwhelmingly the province of [[kinsman|kin]]gs. [[king-arthur|Arthur]] dreams repeatedly, and each dream arrives on the eve of a significant turning point. Before his fateful battle, he dreams a [[marvel|marvellous]] vision:

> Then the king dreamed a marvellous dream whereof he was sore afraid (022-010)

The royal dream is both a privilege and a burden. It grants access to knowledge that ordinary perception cannot reach, but that knowledge is coded in symbols—[[dragon|dragons]], [[griffins]], serpents, boars—that requ[[wrath|ire]] interpretation. The [[kinsman|kin]]g who dreams without understanding is more anxious than the [[kinsman|kin]]g who does not dream at all.

## The Dream and Its Interpreter

Every significant dream in Malory is followed by an act of interpretation. The dreamer seeks out a philosopher, a [[hermit]], or a holy man who can decode the symbols:

> Sir, said the philosopher, the dragon that thou dreamedst of betokeneth thyself (099-021)

The interpreter renders the dream's enigmatic imagery into actionable knowledge, transforming private anxiety into public strategy. The dismissal of dream-interpreters is a mark of folly—[[king|King]] Lot mocks them and dies for his scepticism:

> afeared of a dream-reader? said King Lot. With that there came a knight (012-012)

To ignore the dream is to ignore the divine will that speaks through it, and Malory's narrative consistently punishes those who do so.

## Dreams as Narrative Foreshadowing

For the reader, the interpreted dream functions as a promise—a preview of events whose fulfilment the narrative will eventually deliver. When [[king-arthur|Arthur]] dreams of a dragon defeating a boar, the philosopher's interpretation that the dragon is [[king-arthur|Arthur]] and the boar is a tyrant sets an expectation that the subsequent battle will confirm. The dream thus operates as a form of structural irony: the reader knows what is to come because the dream has already told them, and watches as characters move to[[battle|war]]d a destiny they may resist but cannot escape.

## Dream and Divine Communication

In the world of the Morte, the dream is one of the primary channels through which the divine communicates with mortals. Unlike the [[Sangreal]] vision—available only to the pure—the dream can come to any [[kinsman|kin]]g, regardless of his spiritual state. It is a form of [[grace]] that operates independently of the dreamer's [[worship|worthiness]], a reminder that even flawed rulers remain within the divine gaze. The t[[wrath|rage]]dy of [[king-arthur|Arthur]] is not that he fails to receive [[battle|war]]nings but that the [[battle|war]]nings, once received and interpreted, cannot always be heeded.
