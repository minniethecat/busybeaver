[237-001]given to [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]], and Sir Launcelot gave it to [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]].

[237-002]Then all the estates and degrees high and low said of Sir Launcelot great

[237-003][[honour|worship]], for the honour that he did unto Sir Tristram; and for that honour

[237-004]doing to Sir Tristram he was at that time more praised and renowned than an he

[237-005]had overthrown five hundred knights; and all the people wholly for this

[237-006]gentleness, first the estates both high and low, and after the commonalty cried

[237-007]at once: Sir Launcelot hath won the [[battle|field]] whosoever say nay. Then was Sir

[237-008]Launcelot wroth and ashamed, and so therewithal he rode to [[king-arthur|King Arthur]]. Alas,

[237-009]said the king, we are all dismayed that Sir Tristram is thus departed from us.

[237-010]By God, said King Arthur, he is one of the noblest knights that ever I saw hold

[237-011]spear or sword in hand, and the most courteoust knight in his fighting; for

[237-012]full hard I saw him, said King Arthur, when he smote [[sir-palomides|Sir Palomides]] upon the

[237-013]helm thrice, that he abashed his helm with his strokes, and also he said: Here

[237-014]is a stroke for Sir Tristram, and thus thrice he said. Then King Arthur, Sir

[237-015]Launcelot, and Sir Dodinas le Savage took their horses to seek Sir Tristram,

[237-016]and by the means of Sir Persides he had told King Arthur where Sir Tristram was

[237-017]in his pavilion. But when they came there, Sir Tristram and Sir Dinadan were

[237-018]gone.

[237-019]Then King Arthur and Sir Launcelot were heavy, and returned again to the Castle

[237-020]of Maidens making great dole for the hurt of Sir Tristram, and his sudden

[237-021]departing. So God me help, said King Arthur, I am more heavy that I cannot meet

[237-022]with him than for all the hurts that all my knights have had at the tournament.

[237-023]Right so came Sir Gaheris and told King Arthur how Sir Tristram had smitten

[237-024]down Sir Palomides, and it was at Sir Palomides’ own request. Alas, said

[237-025]King Arthur, that was great dishonour to Sir Palomides, inasmuch as Sir

[237-026]Tristram was sore wounded, and now may we all, kings, and knights, and men of

[237-027]worship, say that Sir Tristram may be called a noble knight, and one of the

[237-028]best knights that ever I saw the days of my life. For I will that ye all, kings

[237-029]and knights, know, said King Arthur, that I never saw knight do so marvellously

[237-030]as he hath done these three days; for he was the first that began and that

[237-031]longest held on, save this last day. And though he was hurt, it was a manly

[237-032]adventure of two noble knights, and when two noble men encounter needs must the

[237-033]one have the worse, like as God will suffer at that time. As for me, said Sir

[237-034]Launcelot, for all the lands that ever my father left me I would not have hurt

[237-035]Sir Tristram an I had known him at that time; that I hurt him was for I saw not

[237-036]his shield. For an I had seen his black shield, I would not have meddled with

[237-037]him for many causes; for late he did as much for me as ever did knight, and

[237-038]that is well known that he had ado with thirty knights, and no help save Sir

[237-039]Dinadan. And one thing shall I promise, said Sir Launcelot, Sir Palomides shall

[237-040]repent it as in his unkindly dealing for to follow that noble knight that I by

[237-041]mishap hurted thus. Sir Launcelot said all the worship that might be said by

[237-042]Sir Tristram. Then King Arthur made a great feast to all that would come. And

[237-043]thus we let pass King Arthur, and a little we will turn unto Sir Palomides,

[237-044]that after he had a fall of Sir Tristram, he was nigh-hand araged out of his

[237-045]wit for despite of Sir Tristram. And so he followed him by adventure. And as he

[237-046]came by a river, in his woodness he would have made his horse to have leapt

[237-047]over; and the horse failed footing and fell in the river, wherefore Sir

[237-048]Palomides was adread lest he should have been drowned; and then he avoided his

[237-049]horse, and swam to the land, and let his horse go down by adventure.
