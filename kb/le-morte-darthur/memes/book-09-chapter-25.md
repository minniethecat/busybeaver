[228-001]rode to have foughten with the thirty knights, but they durst not come

[228-002]out.

[228-003]Sir, said [[sir-gawaine|Sir Gawaine]], will ye stand with me, and we will see the malice of

[228-004]these thirty knights. Sir, said [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]], go ye to them, an it please you,

[228-005]and ye shall see I will not fail you, for it is not long ago since I and a

[228-006]fellow met with thirty knights of that queen’s fellowship; and God speed

[228-007]us so that we may win [[honour|worship]]. So then Sir Gawaine and Sir Tristram rode toward

[228-008]the castle where [[morgan-le-fay|Morgan le Fay]] was, and ever Sir Gawaine deemed well that he

[228-009]was Sir Tristram de Liones, because he heard that two knights had slain and

[228-010]beaten thirty knights. And when they came afore the castle Sir Gawaine spake on

[228-011]high and said: Queen Morgan le Fay, send out your knights that ye have laid in

[228-012]a watch for [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]] and for Sir Tristram. Now, said Sir Gawaine, I know

[228-013]your false treason, and through all places where that I ride men shall know of

[228-014]your false treason; and now let see, said Sir Gawaine, whether ye dare come out

[228-015]of your castle, ye thirty knights. Then the queen spake and all the thirty

[228-016]knights at once, and said: Sir Gawaine, full well wottest thou what thou dost

[228-017]and sayest; for by God we know thee passing well, but all that thou speakest

[228-018]and dost, thou sayest it upon pride of that good knight that is there with

[228-019]thee. For there be some of us that know full well the hands of that knight over

[228-020]all well. And wit thou well, Sir Gawaine, it is more for his sake than for

[228-021]thine that we will not come out of this castle. For wit ye well, Sir Gawaine,

[228-022]the knight that beareth the arms of Cornwall, we know him and what he is.

[228-023]Then Sir Gawaine and Sir Tristram departed and rode on their ways a day or two

[228-024]together; and there by adventure, they met with [[sir-kay|Sir Kay]] and Sir Sagramore le

[228-025]Desirous. And then they were glad of Sir Gawaine, and he of them, but they wist

[228-026]not what he was with the shield of Cornwall, but by deeming. And thus they rode

[228-027]together a day or two. And then they were ware of Sir Breuse Saunce Pité

[228-028]chasing a lady for to have slain her, for he had slain her paramour afore. Hold

[228-029]you all still, said Sir Gawaine, and show none of you forth, and ye shall see

[228-030]me reward yonder false knight; for an he espy you he is so well horsed that he

[228-031]will escape away. And then Sir Gawaine rode betwixt Sir Breuse and the lady,

[228-032]and said: False knight, leave her, and have ado with me. When Sir Breuse saw no

[228-033]more but Sir Gawaine he feutred his spear, and Sir Gawaine against him; and

[228-034]there Sir Breuse overthrew Sir Gawaine, and then he rode over him, and

[228-035]overthwart him twenty times to have destroyed him; and when Sir Tristram saw

[228-036]him do so villainous a deed, he hurled out against him. And when Sir Breuse saw

[228-037]him with the shield of Cornwall he knew him well that it was Sir Tristram, and

[228-038]then he fled, and Sir Tristram followed after him; and Sir Breuse Saunce Pité

[228-039]was so horsed that he went his way quite, and Sir Tristram followed him long,

[228-040]for he would fain have been avenged upon him. And so when he had long chased

[228-041]him, he saw a fair well, and thither he rode to repose him, and tied his horse

[228-042]till a tree.
