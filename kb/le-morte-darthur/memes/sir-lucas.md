# Sir Lucas

Sir Lucas, also called Sir Lucas the Butler, was an officer of [[King Arthur]]'s royal household who served as butler of the court. A man of noble birth — he was a duke before entering Arthur's service — Lucas embodied the ideal of the high-born retainer who combined courtly office with martial prowess.

## The Butler of the Court

Lucas held the office of butler in the royal household, one of the great offices of state in Arthur's court. The chronicle names him as "Sir Lucas the butler, that was Duke" (013-076), recording both his courtly function and his ducal rank. In the early days of Arthur's reign, the butler was not merely a domestic servant but a position of honour and authority, entrusted to a man of proven nobility. Lucas the butler "served in the hall" (013-076) alongside [[Sir Kay]] the seneschal, the two together forming the backbone of the royal household's administration.

## The Battle Against the Eleven Kings

When the eleven kings rose against the young King Arthur, Lucas fought in the defence of the realm. The chronicle records that "there were always fourteen knights on Sir Lucas" (017-070), testifying to the ferocity with which the enemy pressed against him. In the melee, Brastias, one of Arthur's loyal knights, "smote one of them down" (017-070) who had been attacking Lucas.

The chronicle describes how Brastias "led him unto Sir Lucas" (017-075) — the defeated knight was brought before Lucas as a prisoner, an acknowledgement of his authority on the field. This episode reveals Lucas not merely as a household officer but as a fighting knight who endured the hottest press of battle and commanded the respect of Arthur's warriors.

## The Duke in Service

Lucas's transition from duke to butler reflects the broader pattern of Arthur's court, where men of high birth and territorial power submitted themselves to the king's service. The office of butler, like that of seneschal, was one of the mechanisms by which Arthur transformed the old feudal nobility into the officers of a unified realm. Lucas's ducal past gave weight to his courtly present, and his willingness to serve in a domestic office spoke to the magnetic authority of Arthur's kingship.

## A Pillar of the Early Court

Sir Lucas the Butler belongs to the founding generation of Arthur's household — those knights and officers who established the court's order in the turbulent years after Arthur's coronation. Though his appearances in the chronicle are brief, his dual identity as duke and butler embodies the transformation that Arthur's reign wrought: the fusion of martial nobility with courtly service that made the Round Table possible.
