# Revenge

**Revenge**  in Malory's "Le Morte d'[[king-arthur|Arthur]]" is one of the most powerful and destructive forces in the narrative — a primary driver of conflict that operates in persistent tension with the chivalric ideals of [[Mercy]] and forgiveness. The pursuit of revenge generates some of the work's most memorable episodes, from [[Sir Balin]]'s tragic quest for the invisible knight to [[Sir Gawaine]]'s implacable pursuit of [[Sir Launcelot]] after the death of his brothers. Revenge is simultaneously recognized as a natural and even [[honour|honor]]able response to [[wound|injury]], and condemned as the force that ultimately destroys the [[Round Table]].

## The Mechanism of Revenge

Revenge in Malory follows a recognizable pattern: an [[wound|injury]] or killing occurs, the aggrieved party (typically a [[kinsman|kin]]sman) swears to "revenge" the wrong, and this oath sets in motion a chain of violence that often extends far beyond the original parties. The mechanism is self-perpetuating because each act of revenge creates new grievances that demand further revenge:

> "how Sir Launcelot came to revenge his death" (002-131)
> "revenged of the damage that they had done" (017-049)

The language of revenge permeates the work at every level, from individual [[battle|combat]]s to massed battles. Lords seek to "revenge" themselves on their enemies, knights swear to "revenge" their masters' deaths, and ent[[wrath|ire]] armies take the [[battle|field]] to "revenge" past defeats.

## Revenge as Kinship Obligation

The most binding form of revenge in Malory is [[kinsman|kin]]ship revenge — the obligation of a [[kinsman|kin]]sman to avenge the death of his relative. This obligation is presented not as a choice but as a duty, and a knight who fails to pursue it forfeits his [[Worship]]:

> "some knight may revenge my master's death" (024-035)
> "wherefore Sir Gawaine revenged the death of his father" (041-047)

The [[kinsman|kin]]ship revenge obligation creates the tragic architecture of the work's final books. When [[Sir Launcelot]] accidentally kills [[Sir Gareth]] and [[Sir Gaheris]] while rescuing [[Queen Guenever]], he creates a revenge obligation that [[Sir Gawaine]] cannot refuse, even though [[sir-launcelot|Launcelot]] is his former closest friend. The collision between the duty of revenge and the bond of friendship is the central t[[wrath|rage]]dy of the [[king-arthur|Arthur]]ian story.

## Revenge versus Mercy

The chivalric code's demand for [[Mercy]] creates an explicit tension with the revenge imperative. A knight who shows mercy to a defeated opponent may be acting chivalrously, but if that opponent has killed his [[kinsman|kin]]sman, mercy may also be a [[treason|betrayal]] of [[kinsman|kin]]ship duty:

> "revenge him of his enemies" (021-007)
> "he would soon revenge us" (021-033)

This tension is never resolved in Malory's work. The code of [[Knighthood]] demands both mercy and vengeance, and the impossibility of satisfying both requ[[wrath|ire]]ments simultaneously is one of the structural contradictions that ultimately destroys the chivalric world.

## Revenge's Narrative Function

Revenge is the narrative's most reliable generator of sustained conflict. While the episodic adventure structure produces one-off [[battle|combat]]s that conclude with a winner and a reconciliation, revenge narratives extend across multiple episodes and often across ent[[wrath|ire]] books. The revenge plot creates continuity — it links otherwise discrete adventures into causal chains, transforming random encounters into installments of an ongoing feud. In this sense, revenge is what makes Malory's episodic romance cohere as a unified t[[wrath|rage]]dy: the accumulated weight of unavenged deaths and unsatisfied vengeance claims eventually overwhelms the chivalric system's capacity for resolution.
