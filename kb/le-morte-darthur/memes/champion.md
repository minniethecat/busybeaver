The **champion** in Malory's [[Le Morte d'Arthur]] is a knight who [[battle|fight]]s not for himself but for another—a lady accused of [[treason]], a [[baron|lord]] whose cause requ[[wrath|ire]]s vindication, or a [[queen]] whose life hangs on the outcome of [[test|trial]] by [[battle|combat]]. To serve as champion is to place one's [[prowess]] at the disposal of [[justice]], to become the instrument through which divine [[judgment]] is made manifest.

## The Champion in Trial by Combat

The champion's primary role is in [[test|trial]] by [[battle|combat]], where he represents a party who cannot or will not [[battle|fight]] personally. When a [[damosel]] or queen is accused, it is the champion who answers the charge with his body:

> for whom I have been champion and won the field (078-007)

The champion's victory is understood as proof of the represented party's innocence—not because the champion is necessarily in the right but because God, it is believed, will not permit an unjust champion to prevail. The champion thus bears a double burden: he must [[battle|fight]] well, and he must [[battle|fight]] for a cause that God will support.

## The Champion as Volunteer

To offer oneself as champion is among the highest expressions of knightly [[honour]]. When Sir [[Sir Launcelot|Launcelot]] volunteers to champion [[Queen Guenever]] in her [[test|trial]], he does so knowing that he [[battle|fight]]s against knights who were once his companions:

> I would have been her champion (178-026)

The champion who volunteers for a d[[wrath|anger]]ous or unpopular cause demonstrates a [[courage]] that transcends mere martial skill. He risks not only his life but his standing in the chivalric community, placing loyalty to an individual above his place in the broader order.

## The Champion's Conditions

Not every request for championship is accepted. Knights may decline to serve as champion when the cause is unjust, when the opponent is unknown, or when prior obligations prevent it:

> to be your champion? Nay, fair knight (134-007)

The champion's consent is a moral act—to [[battle|fight]] for a cause is to endorse it, and the knight who champions an unworthy party shares in that party's [[shame|disgrace]]. The refusal to serve as champion is therefore as significant as the acceptance, a line that even the most valiant knight must sometimes draw.

## The Champion Beyond the Court

The term "champion" in Malory also carries a broader sense, referring to any formidable [[battle|fight]]er who stands as the representative of a group or cause. [[giant|Giant]]s, pagan knights, and enemy commanders are described as "champions" of their people:

> lady, giant or champion, I take no force so that I may have ado (158-012)

In this [[custom|usage]], "champion" is almost synonymous with "formidable opponent"—a recognition that the role of representing another in [[battle|combat]] exists across the boundaries of faith and nation. The champion is a universal figure in the world of the romance, appearing wherever justice must be decided by [[battle|combat]].
