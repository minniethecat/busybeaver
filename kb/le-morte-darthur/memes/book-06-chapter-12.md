[120-001][[sir-kay|Sir Kay]]’s harness, and how he smote down a knight.

[120-002]Now turn we unto [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]] that had ridden long in a great forest, and at

[120-003]the last he came into a low country, full of fair rivers and meadows. And afore

[120-004]him he saw a long bridge, and three pavilions stood thereon, of silk and sendal

[120-005]of divers hue. And without the pavilions hung three white shields on truncheons

[120-006]of spears, and great long spears stood upright by the pavilions, and at every

[120-007]pavilion’s door stood three fresh squires, and so Sir Launcelot passed by

[120-008]them and spake no word. When he was passed the three knights said them that it

[120-009]was the proud Kay; He weeneth no knight so good as he, and the contrary is

[120-010]ofttime proved. By my faith, said one of the knights, his name was Sir Gaunter,

[120-011]I will ride after him and assay him for all his pride, and ye may behold how

[120-012]that I speed. So this knight, Sir Gaunter, armed him, and hung his shield upon

[120-013]his shoulder, and mounted upon a great horse, and gat his spear in his hand,

[120-014]and walloped after Sir Launcelot. And when he came nigh him, he cried, Abide,

[120-015]thou proud knight Sir Kay, for thou shalt not pass quit. So Sir Launcelot

[120-016]turned him, and either feutred their spears, and came together with all their

[120-017]mights, and Sir Gaunter’s spear brake, but Sir Launcelot smote him down

[120-018]horse and man. And when Sir Gaunter was at the earth his brethren said each one

[120-019]to other, Yonder knight is not Sir Kay, for he is bigger than he. I dare lay my

[120-020]head, said Sir Gilmere, yonder knight hath slain Sir Kay and hath taken his

[120-021]horse and his harness. Whether it be so or no, said Sir Raynold, the third

[120-022]brother, let us now go mount upon our horses and rescue our brother Sir

[120-023]Gaunter, upon pain of death. We all shall have work enough to match that

[120-024]knight, for ever meseemeth by his person it is Sir Launcelot, or [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]],

[120-025]or Sir Pelleas, the good knight.

[120-026]Then anon they took their horses and overtook Sir Launcelot, and Sir Gilmere

[120-027]put forth his spear, and ran to Sir Launcelot, and Sir Launcelot smote him down

[120-028]that he lay in a swoon. Sir knight, said Sir Raynold, thou art a strong man,

[120-029]and as I suppose thou hast slain my two brethren, for the which raseth my heart

[120-030]sore against thee, and if I might with my [[honour|worship]] I would not have ado with

[120-031]you, but needs I must take part as they do, and therefore, knight, he said,

[120-032]keep thyself. And so they hurtled together with all their mights, and all

[120-033]to-shivered both their spears. And then they drew their swords and lashed

[120-034]together eagerly. Anon therewith arose Sir Gaunter, and came unto his brother

[120-035]Sir Gilmere, and bade him, Arise, and help we our brother Sir Raynold, that

[120-036]yonder marvellously matched yonder good knight. Therewithal, they leapt on

[120-037]their horses and hurtled unto Sir Launcelot.

[120-038]And when he saw them come he smote a sore stroke unto Sir Raynold, that he fell

[120-039]off his horse to the ground, and then he struck to the other two brethren, and

[120-040]at two strokes he struck them down to the earth. With that Sir Raynold began to

[120-041]start up with his head all bloody, and came straight unto Sir Launcelot. Now

[120-042]let be, said Sir Launcelot, I was not far from thee when thou wert made knight,

[120-043]Sir Raynold, and also I know thou art a good knight, and loath I were to slay

[120-044]thee. Gramercy, said Sir Raynold, as for your goodness; and I dare say as for

[120-045]me and my brethren, we will not be loath to yield us unto you, with that we

[120-046]knew your name, for well we know ye are not Sir Kay. As for that be it as it be

[120-047]may, for ye shall yield you unto dame [[queen-guenever|Guenever]], and look that ye be with her on

[120-048]Whitsunday, and yield you unto her as prisoners, and say that Sir Kay sent you

[120-049]unto her. Then they swore it should be done, and so passed forth Sir Launcelot,

[120-050]and each one of the brethren holp other as well as they might.
