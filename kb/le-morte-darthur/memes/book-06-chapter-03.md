[111-001]sleeping, and how by enchantment he was taken and led into a castle.

[111-002]Now leave we these knights prisoners, and speak we of [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot du Lake]]

[111-003]that lieth under the apple-tree sleeping. Even about the noon there came by him

[111-004]four queens of great estate; and, for the heat should not annoy them, there

[111-005]rode four knights about them, and bare a cloth of green silk on four spears,

[111-006]betwixt them and the sun, and the queens rode on four white mules. Thus as they

[111-007]rode they heard by them a great horse grimly neigh, then were they ware of a

[111-008]sleeping knight, that lay all armed under an apple-tree; anon as these queens

[111-009]looked on his face, they knew it was Sir Launcelot. Then they began for to

[111-010]strive for that knight, everych one said they would have him to her love. We

[111-011]shall not strive, said [[morgan-le-fay|Morgan le Fay]], that was [[king-arthur|King Arthur]]’s sister, I

[111-012]shall put an enchantment upon him that he shall not awake in six hours, and

[111-013]then I will lead him away unto my castle, and when he is surely within my hold,

[111-014]I shall take the enchantment from him, and then let him choose which of us he

[111-015]will have unto paramour.

[111-016]So this enchantment was cast upon Sir Launcelot, and then they laid him upon

[111-017]his shield, and bare him so on horseback betwixt two knights, and brought him

[111-018]unto the castle Chariot, and there they laid him in a chamber cold, and at

[111-019]night they sent unto him a fair damosel with his supper ready dight. By that

[111-020]the enchantment was past, and when she came she saluted him, and asked him what

[111-021]cheer. I cannot say, fair damosel, said Sir Launcelot, for I wot not how I came

[111-022]into this castle but it be by an enchantment. Sir, said she, ye must make good

[111-023]cheer, and if ye be such a knight as it is said ye be, I shall tell you more

[111-024]to-morn by prime of the day. Gramercy, fair damosel, said Sir Launcelot, of

[111-025]your good will I require you. And so she departed. And there he lay all that

[111-026]night without comfort of anybody. And on the morn early came these four queens,

[111-027]passingly well beseen, all they bidding him good morn, and he them again.

[111-028]Sir knight, the four queens said, thou must understand thou art our prisoner,

[111-029]and we here know thee well that thou art Sir Launcelot du Lake, King

[111-030]Ban’s son, and because we understand your worthiness, that thou art the

[111-031]noblest knight living, and as we know well there can no lady have thy love but

[111-032]one, and that is [[queen-guenever|Queen Guenever]], and now thou shalt lose her for ever, and she

[111-033]thee, and therefore thee behoveth now to choose one of us four. I am the Queen

[111-034]Morgan le Fay, queen of the land of Gore, and here is the queen of Northgalis,

[111-035]and the queen of Eastland, and the queen of the Out Isles; now choose one of us

[111-036]which thou wilt have to thy paramour, for thou mayest not choose or else in

[111-037]this prison to die. This is an hard case, said Sir Launcelot, that either I

[111-038]must die or else choose one of you, yet had I liefer to die in this prison with

[111-039][[honour|worship]], than to have one of you to my paramour maugre my head. And therefore

[111-040]ye be answered, I will none of you, for ye be false enchantresses, and as for

[111-041]my lady, Dame Guenever, were I at my liberty as I was, I would prove it on you

[111-042]or on yours, that she is the truest lady unto her lord living. Well, said the

[111-043]queens, is this your answer, that ye will refuse us. Yea, on my life, said Sir

[111-044]Launcelot, refused ye be of me. So they departed and left him there alone that

[111-045]made great sorrow.
