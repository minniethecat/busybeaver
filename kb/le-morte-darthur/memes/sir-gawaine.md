# Sir Gawaine

**Sir Gawaine** is [[king-arthur|King Arthur]]'s nephew and one of the principal knights of the [[round-table|Round Table]] in Thomas Malory's *Le Morte d'[[king-arthur|Arthur]]*. He embodies the competing claims of loyalty — to [[kinsman|kin]]g, to [[kinsman|kin]], and to the fellowship of knights — and his descent from [[baron|noble]] champion to vengeful antagonist is one of the central t[[wrath|rage]]dies of the later books.

## The Avenger of Kin

Gawaine is defined above all by loyalty to blood. After "the death of [[king|King]] Lot, wherefore Sir Gawaine revenged the death of his father" (041-047), the pattern of [[kinsman|kin]]-[[revenge|vengeance]] is established. A prophecy written in "letters of gold" foretells "how Sir Gawaine shall revenge his father's death" (044-016), and this destiny shapes his ent[[wrath|ire]] career. Gawaine's identity is inseparable from his family obligations — he is [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s nephew, Lot's son, and the brother of [[sir-gareth|Gareth]], Gaheris, and Agravaine.

## Knight of the Round Table

Gawaine is consistently among the foremost knights of [[camelot|Arthur's court]]. A prophecy states that the knight [[king-arthur|Arthur]] loves best "shall be Sir Gawaine" (050-013), and at the [[round-table|Round Table]] "sat Sir Gawaine in great [[honour|worship]]" (055-013). When a quest is proposed, [[merlin|Merlin]] says "let call Sir Gawaine" (056-023), and "Sir Gawaine had the first request" (056-029) — he is the knight who steps for[[battle|war]]d first, whose eagerness for adventure is both a strength and a liability.

## The Feud with Launcelot

Gawaine's t[[wrath|rage]]dy is his feud with [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]]. When [[sir-launcelot|Launcelot]] accidentally kills [[sir-gareth|Gareth]] (Gawaine's brother) during the rescue of [[queen-guenever|Guenever]], Gawaine's loyalty to [[kinsman|kin]] transforms into implacable hatred. He pursues [[sir-launcelot|Launcelot]] even as [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s [[realm|kingdom]] crumbles, and his refusal to accept [[sir-launcelot|Launcelot]]'s offers of peace d[[wrath|ire]]ctly contributes to the final catastrophe. Gawaine — once the flower of chivalry — dies from [[wound|wounds]] received in single [[battle|combat]] with [[sir-launcelot|Launcelot]], a death that epitomizes the self-destruction of the [[round-table|Round Table]].
