# Sir Accolon

**Sir Accolon of Gaul** is the knight who becomes the instrument of [[morgan-le-fay|Morgan le Fay]]'s most direct attempt to destroy [[king-arthur|King Arthur]] in Thomas Malory's *Le Morte d'[[king-arthur|Arthur]]*. Wielding the stolen sword [[excalibur|Excalibur]], Accolon fights Arthur in a duel that nearly kills the king — and reveals the depth of Morgan's [[treason|treachery]].

## The Plot of Morgan le Fay

Morgan le Fay's hatred for her brother [[king-arthur|Arthur]] drives her to orchestrate an elaborate scheme. She brings Accolon, her lover, into her confidence and arranges for Arthur and Accolon to be lodged in separate chambers of the same enchanted castle (072-003, 072-030). There, she causes Accolon to receive the true Excalibur while Arthur receives a counterfeit. The dwarf who delivers the sword to Accolon tells him he comes from "Queen Morgan le Fay" and that the sword is Excalibur itself (074-010, 074-017). Accolon "took him in his arms" (074-019), accepting both the sword and the mission.

## The Duel for Excalibur

The battle between Arthur and Accolon is one of the most desperate combats in the Morte. Accolon wields Excalibur, whose blade "drew blood on Arthur" at every stroke (075-018), while Arthur fights with a false sword that wounds Accolon but does not cut as Excalibur does. Accolon fights with the confidence of a man who believes he holds an unbeatable weapon: "Sir Accolon lost not a deal of blood" while Arthur grows steadily weaker (075-025).

When Arthur's false sword breaks, Accolon "called him to battle" with the confidence of certain victory (075-033). But the Lady of the Lake intervenes, causing Excalibur to fall from Accolon's grasp. Arthur seizes the true sword, and the battle turns instantly: "at the next stroke Sir Accolon struck him such a stroke that he fell to the earth" — but now it is Arthur who holds Excalibur, and Accolon who receives the mortal blow (076-020).

## Death and Confession

Dying, Accolon confesses the full scope of Morgan's treason. He "bethought him, and said, Woe worth the time that ever I saw you" (077-003), acknowledging that he was used as a pawn. His confession reveals Morgan's ultimate aim: not merely Arthur's death, but the replacement of Arthur with Accolon as king, with Morgan as his queen. Arthur, recognizing Accolon's manipulated role, treats him with a measure of mercy, and Accolon dies having exposed the [[treason|treachery]] that might otherwise have remained hidden.
