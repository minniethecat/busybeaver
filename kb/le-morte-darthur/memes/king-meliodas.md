# King Meliodas

**King Meliodas** is the King of Liones and the father of [[Sir Tristram]], one of the greatest knights of Arthurian legend. His reign is marked by personal tragedy, imprisonment, a troubled second marriage, and ultimately the pride of seeing his son rise to renown.

## Biography

King Meliodas ruled the land of Liones. He married the sister of [[King Mark]] of Cornwall, and from this union [[Sir Tristram]] was born (164-013). Before Tristram's birth, a lady of the court who had long loved King Meliodas without reciprocation (164-016) plotted against him. She arranged for him to be taken prisoner while he was out hunting (164-017), and it was during his captivity that his wife died in childbirth, naming the infant Tristram before she passed (164-039).

King Meliodas was eventually delivered from prison through the intervention of [[Merlin]] (165-007). Upon returning to Liones, he endured great sorrow over the loss of his first wife (165-012). He later wedded the daughter of [[King Howell]] of Brittany (165-014), but this second marriage brought fresh difficulties: his new wife was "heavy and wroth" about the young Tristram's presence (165-015), and she attempted to poison the boy. By fortune, King Meliodas discovered the scheme (165-027) and, after a confrontation, was reunited with his son (165-037).

## Relationship with Tristram

When Tristram grew older, King Meliodas told him of his true lineage, declaring "thou ought of right" to know (165-043). The bond between father and son was strong; Tristram later returned to his "father, King Meliodas, again" after adventures abroad (166-008), and the King "had great joy of Sir Tristram" upon each reunion (166-023).

When Tristram sought counsel about going to Cornwall, King Meliodas advised him with paternal wisdom: "wit you well, son Tristram" (168-006), and urged him to "be ruled as your counsel" (168-013). Tristram carried his father's name with pride, declaring "I am King Meliodas' son" when revealing his identity (168-044).

## Legacy

King Meliodas is remembered less for his own deeds than for his role as the father of [[Sir Tristram]]. His story is one of endurance through treachery, imprisonment, and domestic discord, yet he consistently acted with honor and paternal devotion. The deliverance by [[Merlin]] connects his fate to the broader Arthurian world, and his counsel to Tristram reveals a wise and measured ruler who valued prudence over reckless valor.
