[240-001]good [[honour|renown]] of [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]]. Some of [[king-arthur|King Arthur]]’s knights jousted with

[240-002]knights of Cornwall.

[240-003]Now will we speak, and leave Sir Tristram, [[sir-palomides|Sir Palomides]], and Sir Dinadan in

[240-004]prison, and speak we of other knights that sought after Sir Tristram many

[240-005]divers parts of this land. And some yede into Cornwall; and by adventure Sir

[240-006]Gaheris, nephew unto King Arthur, came unto [[king-mark|King Mark]], and there he was well

[240-007]received and sat at King Mark’s own table and ate of his own mess. Then

[240-008]King Mark asked Sir Gaheris what tidings there were in the realm of Logris.

[240-009]Sir, said Sir Gaheris, the king reigneth as a noble knight; and now but late

[240-010]there was a great [[tournament|jousts]] and tournament as ever I saw any in the realm of

[240-011]Logris, and the most noble knights were at that jousts. But there was one

[240-012]knight that did marvellously three days, and he bare a black shield, and of all

[240-013]knights that ever I saw he proved the best knight. Then, said King Mark, that

[240-014]was [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]], or Sir Palomides the paynim. Not so, said Sir Gheris, for

[240-015]both Sir Launcelot and Sir Palomides were on the contrary party against the

[240-016]Knight with the Black Shield. Then was it Sir Tristram, said the king. Yea,

[240-017]said Sir Gaheris. And therewithal the king smote down his head, and in his

[240-018]heart he feared sore that Sir Tristram should get him such worship in the realm

[240-019]of Logris wherethrough that he himself should not be able to withstand him.

[240-020]Thus Sir Gaheris had great cheer with King Mark, and with Queen La Beale Isoud,

[240-021]the which was glad of Sir Gaheris’ words; for well she wist by his deeds

[240-022]and manners that it was Sir Tristram. And then the king made a feast royal, and

[240-023]to that feast came Sir Uwaine le Fise de Roy Ureine, and some called him Uwaine

[240-024]le Blanchemains. And this Sir Uwaine challenged all the knights of Cornwall.

[240-025]Then was the king wood wroth that he had no knights to answer him. Then Sir

[240-026]Andred, nephew unto King Mark, leapt up and said: I will encounter with Sir

[240-027]Uwaine. Then he yede and armed him and horsed him in the best manner. And there

[240-028]Sir Uwaine met with Sir Andred, and smote him down that he swooned on the

[240-029]earth. Then was King Mark sorry and wroth out of measure that he had no knight

[240-030]to revenge his nephew, Sir Andred.

[240-031]So the king called unto him Sir Dinas, the Seneschal, and prayed him for his

[240-032]sake to take upon him to joust with Sir Uwaine. Sir, said Sir Dinas, I am full

[240-033]loath to have ado with any knight of the [[round-table|Round Table]]. Yet, said the king, for

[240-034]my love take upon thee to joust. So Sir Dinas made him ready, and anon they

[240-035]encountered together with great spears, but Sir Dinas was overthrown, horse and

[240-036]man, a great fall. Who was wroth but King Mark! Alas, he said, have I no knight

[240-037]that will encounter with yonder knight? Sir, said Sir Gaheris, for your sake I

[240-038]will joust. So Sir Gaheris made him ready, and when he was armed he rode into

[240-039]the [[battle|field]]. And when Sir Uwaine saw Sir Gaheris’ shield he rode to him and

[240-040]said: Sir, ye do not your part. For, sir, the first time ye were made Knight of

[240-041]the Round Table ye sware that ye should not have ado with your fellowship

[240-042]wittingly. And pardie, Sir Gaheris, ye knew me well enough by my shield, and so

[240-043]do I know you by your shield, and though ye would break your oath I would not

[240-044]break mine; for there is not one here, nor ye, that shall think I am afeard of

[240-045]you, but I durst right well have ado with you, and yet we be sisters’

[240-046]sons. Then was Sir Gaheris ashamed, and so therewithal every knight went their

[240-047]way, and Sir Uwaine rode into the country.

[240-048]Then King Mark armed him, and took his horse and his spear, with a squire with

[240-049]him. And then he rode afore Sir Uwaine, and suddenly at a gap he ran upon him

[240-050]as he that was not ware of him, and there he smote him almost through the body,

[240-051]and there left him. So within a while there came [[sir-kay|Sir Kay]] and found Sir Uwaine,

[240-052]and asked him how he was hurt. I wot not, said Sir Uwaine, why nor wherefore,

[240-053]but by treason I am sure I gat this hurt; for here came a knight suddenly upon

[240-054]me or that I was ware, and suddenly hurt me. Then there was come Sir Andred to

[240-055]seek King Mark. Thou traitor knight, said Sir Kay, an I wist it were thou that

[240-056]thus traitorly hast hurt this noble knight thou shouldst never pass my hands.

[240-057]Sir, said Sir Andred, I did never hurt him, and that I will report me to

[240-058]himself. Fie on you false knight, said Sir Kay, for ye of Cornwall are nought

[240-059]worth. So Sir Kay made carry Sir Uwaine to the Abbey of the Black Cross, and

[240-060]there he was healed. And then Sir Gaheris took his leave of King Mark, but or

[240-061]he departed he said: Sir king, ye did a foul shame unto you and your court,

[240-062]when ye banished Sir Tristram out of this country, for ye needed not to have

[240-063]doubted no knight an he had been here. And so he departed.
