# Sir Hebes le Renoumes

Sir Hebes, called Hebes le Renoumes, was the loyal squire of [[Sir Tristram]] who was raised to knighthood by Tristram's own hand. His story is inextricably bound to Tristram's sojourn in Ireland under the assumed name "Tramtrist," during which Hebes served as the keeper of his master's most dangerous secret — his true identity as the slayer of Sir Marhaus, the brother of the Queen of Ireland. In this role, Hebes became not merely a servant but a guardian of Tristram's very life.

## The Guardian of a Secret

When Tristram arrived in Ireland to be healed of a poisoned wound, he concealed his identity, calling himself Tramtrist. He entrusted his secret to Hebes alone: "Sir Tristram ran unto his squire, whose name was Hebes le Renoumes, and prayed him heartily in no wise to tell his name. Sir, said Hebes, I will not discover your name but if ye command me" (172-050, 172-051). This compact of loyalty established Hebes as Tristram's most trusted attendant, a man on whose discretion a knight's life depended.

## Knighthood and Deeds of Arms

Tristram rewarded Hebes's service with the highest honour a squire could receive: "Sir Tristram made Hebes a knight, and caused him to put himself forth, and did right well that day. So after Sir Hebes held him with Sir Tristram" (173-022, 173-023). The knighting was not merely ceremonial — Hebes immediately proved himself in the lists, fighting "right well" in the same tournament where Tristram himself had defeated Sir Palamides. The bond between the two knights was thereafter one of companionship in arms as much as service.

## The Rescue in the Bath

Hebes's defining moment came when the Queen of Ireland, examining Tristram's sword while he bathed, recognised the notch in the blade that matched the fragment found in her brother Marhaus's skull. Realising that Tramtrist was Marhaus's killer, "the queen gripped that sword in her hand fiercely, and with all her might she ran straight upon Tramtrist where he sat in his bain, and there she had rived him through had not Sir Hebes gotten her in his arms, and pulled the sword from her, and else she had thrust him through" (174-024). In that instant, Hebes saved Tristram's life by physical intervention, wrestling the sword from a queen in a murderous rage. Without Hebes's quick action, the greatest love-story of Arthurian romance — Tristram and [[La Beale Isoud]] — would have ended in a bath in Ireland.

## A Life in Service

After the Ireland episode, Hebes "held him with Sir Tristram," continuing in his service as knight and companion. His trajectory — from squire to knight, from keeper of secrets to saviour of his lord — embodies the ideal of loyal service that the Arthurian code celebrated. Hebes le Renoumes earned his epithet through deeds that required not only courage but unwavering fidelity.
