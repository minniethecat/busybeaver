**Courtesy** is one of the foundational [[virtue|virtues]] of [[chivalry]] in Malory's [[Le Morte d'Arthur]], a code of refined speech and conduct that distinguishes the true knight from the mere [[battle|war]]rior. Where [[prowess]] governs what a knight can do and [[honour]] governs what he must do, courtesy governs how he does it—the manner, the tone, the gesture that transforms force into nobility.

## Courtesy in the Chivalric Catalogue

In the preface to the Morte, Caxton places courtesy among the cardinal knightly [[virtue|virtues]]:

> noble chivalry, courtesy, humanity, friendliness, hardiness, love, courtesy, and very gentleness (002-100)

Courtesy appears alongside [[gentleness]], humanity, and love—grouped not with the martial [[virtue|virtues]] but with the social and moral qualities that define the knight's conduct in peacetime. It is what makes the knight welcome at court, trusted by [[lady|ladies]], and respected by equals.

## Courtesy as Social Currency

In [[custom|practice]], courtesy functions as a form of social currency. A knight who speaks with courtesy can request favours, negotiate passage, and defuse conflicts without resorting to [[battle|combat]]:

> Damosel, I pray you of your courtesy, suffer me as this day to do you service (033-012)

The [[challenge|appeal]] to courtesy establishes a mutual obligation: the person addressed is reminded of their own obligation to be courteous in return. This reciprocal structure makes courtesy a powerful tool for navigating the social [[realm|land]]scape of the romance, where str[[wrath|anger]]s meet constantly and must quickly establish the terms of their interaction.

## Courtesy and Gentleness

Malory frequently pairs courtesy with [[gentleness]], suggesting that the two [[virtue|virtues]] are inseparable:

> of his great gentleness and courtesy (129-020)
> of your gentleness ye teach me courtesy (084-027)

[[gentleness|Gentleness]]—nobility of character and [[lineage|birth]]—provides the foundation upon which courtesy is built. The gentle knight is naturally courteous; the discourteous knight reveals by his manner a defect of gentleness. Together, the pa[[wrath|ire]]d [[virtue|virtues]] define the ideal of knightly conduct that the Morte holds up for admiration.

## The Discourteous Knight

The absence of courtesy is a reliable marker of villainy or spiritual decline. Knights who mock, threaten, or speak coarsely reveal themselves as unworthy regardless of their martial skill. Sir [[Sir Kay|Kay]]'s discourtesy is his defining flaw, while the courtesy of Sir [[Sir Launcelot|Launcelot]]—even to his enemies—marks him as the flower of knighthood. In Malory's moral economy, courtesy is not an optional refinement but a diagnostic of character.
