# Igraine

**Igraine** — "a passing fair lady" (004-007) — is the duchess who becomes the mother of [[King Arthur]] and, through her daughters, the ancestress of the entire Orkney dynasty. Her story opens *Le Morte d'[[King Arthur|Arthur]]* and carries within it the founding paradox of Arthurian kingship: the king is begotten through [[enchantment]] and [[deception]], yet the queen who bears him is blameless.

## The Duchess of Cornwall

Igraine first appears as the wife of the Duke of Cornwall, "a passing fair lady and a passing wise, and her name was called Igraine" (004-007). King Uther Pendragon, seeing her at a feast, is seized by desire: "for great love of fair Igraine the king Uther fell sick" (004-034). His [[love]] is neither courtly nor restrained — it is a consuming passion that threatens the peace of the realm even as the Saxons press upon the borders.

The Duke, perceiving Uther's intent, withdraws with Igraine from the court without leave. He places Igraine in the castle of Tintagil for safety while he himself garrisons the castle of Terrabil (004-029). Uther pursues them with war, and the Duke is slain in the ensuing conflict.

## The Night at Tintagil

What follows is the founding act of Arthurian history — and it is accomplished through [[magic]]. [[Merlin]], serving Uther's desire, works an [[enchantment]]: "this night ye shall lie by Igraine ye shall get a child on her" (005-011). Through Merlin's art, Uther is transformed into the likeness of the dead Duke, and in that guise he enters Tintagil: "King Uther lay with Igraine more than three hours after the death of the duke" (005-026). Igraine, believing herself to be with her husband, conceives [[King Arthur|Arthur]].

The moral complexity of this episode is acute. Igraine is deceived — she acts in good faith, believing the man in her chamber to be her lawful husband. When she later learns that the Duke had died before Uther came to her, she is placed in an impossible position: the child she carries was fathered by a man she did not consent to bed.

## Queen of England

After the Duke's death, Uther makes Igraine his queen. The chroniclers advise: "my lady Igraine is a passing fair lady; it were great joy and bliss to the realm to make her our queen" (005-037). Igraine accepts, "and so they were married with great solemnity" (005-033). She bears the weight of this new role with dignity, giving birth to Arthur and raising him — at least until Merlin takes the child away to be fostered in secret.

Her sorrow when Uther dies is deep and public: "the queen, fair Igraine, made great sorrow, and all the barons" (007-024). She has lost two husbands in rapid succession, and her son has been taken from her by the designs of [[Merlin]].

## The Mother Acknowledged

Igraine reappears in the narrative when Arthur's legitimacy is challenged. His opponents argue that "he was no son of the king, born in wedlock, gotten on Igraine, the duke's wife" (011-028), questioning both his paternity and the legitimacy of Uther's union with Igraine. The defence counters that Arthur was conceived after the Duke's death and that "days after King Uther wedded Igraine; and therefore I prove him he is the rightful king" (011-031).

In a later and more intimate scene, Arthur confronts Igraine directly about his parentage. [[Merlin]] brings him to her, and Arthur demands to know the truth: "thy father, and begat thee on Igraine. That is false, said Arthur" (023-017). Igraine, faced with her son's doubt, must confirm the truth of his birth — a difficult reckoning between mother and child. Yet Arthur receives her with [[honour]]: "he welcomed Igraine in the best manner" (023-043), affirming the bond that no political questioning can sever.

## Legacy

Igraine's legacy extends far beyond the birth of [[King Arthur]]. Through her first marriage to the Duke of Cornwall, she is the mother of three daughters: [[Morgan le Fay]], [[Morgawse|Queen Morgawse]] of Orkney, and Elaine. From these daughters spring [[Sir Gawaine]], [[Sir Gareth]], [[Mordred]], [[Gaheris]], Sir Agravaine, and others — the entire Orkney clan that will both defend and destroy the Round Table. Igraine is thus the matriarch of the two dynasties whose intertwined fates structure the entire Morte.
