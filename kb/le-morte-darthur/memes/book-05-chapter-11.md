[106-002]Then Sir Florence called to him Sir Floridas, with an hundred knights, and

[106-003]drove forth the herd of beasts. Then followed him seven hundred men of arms;

[106-004]and Sir Ferant of Spain on a fair steed came springing out of the woods, and

[106-005]came to Sir Florence and asked him why he fled. Then Sir Florence took his

[106-006]spear and rode against him, and smote him in the forehead and brake his neck

[106-007]bone. Then all the other were moved, and thought to avenge the death of Sir

[106-008]Ferant, and smote in among them, and there was great [[battle|fight]], and many slain and

[106-009]laid down to ground, and Sir Florence with his hundred knights alway kept the

[106-010]stale, and fought manly.

[106-011]Then when Priamus the good knight perceived the great fight, he went to Sir

[106-012][[sir-gawaine|Gawaine]], and bade him that he should go and succour his fellowship, which were

[106-013]sore bestead with their enemies. Sir, grieve you not, said Sir Gawaine, for

[106-014]their gree shall be theirs. I shall not once move my horse to them ward, but if

[106-015]I see more than there be; for they be strong enough to match them.

[106-016]And with that he saw an earl called Sir Ethelwold and the duke of Dutchmen,

[106-017]came leaping out of a wood with many thousands, and Priamus’ knights, and

[106-018]came straight unto the battle. Then Sir Gawaine comforted his knights, and bade

[106-019]them not to be abashed, for all shall be ours. Then they began to wallop and

[106-020]met with their enemies, there were men slain and overthrown on every side. Then

[106-021]thrust in among them the knights of the [[round-table|Table Round]], and smote down to the

[106-022]earth all them that withstood them, in so much that they made them to recoil

[106-023]and flee. By God, said Sir Gawaine, this gladdeth my heart, for now be they

[106-024]less in number by twenty thousand. Then entered into the battle Jubance a

[106-025]giant, and fought and slew down right, and distressed many of our knights,

[106-026]among whom was slain Sir Gherard, a knight of Wales. Then our knights took

[106-027]heart to them, and slew many Saracens. And then came in Sir Priamus with his

[106-028]pennon, and rode with the knights of the Round Table, and fought so manfully

[106-029]that many of their enemies lost their lives. And there Sir Priamus slew the

[106-030]Marquis of Moises land, and Sir Gawaine with his fellows so quit them that they

[106-031]had the field, but in that stour was Sir Chestelaine, a child and ward of Sir

[106-032]Gawaine slain, wherefore was much sorrow made, and his death was soon avenged.

[106-033]Thus was the battle ended, and many lords of Lombardy and Saracens left dead in

[106-034]the field.

[106-035]Then Sir Florence and Sir Gawaine harboured surely their people, and took great

[106-036]plenty of bestial, of gold and silver, and great treasure and riches, and

[106-037]returned unto [[king-arthur|King Arthur]], which lay still at the siege. And when they came to

[106-038]the king they presented their prisoners and recounted their adventures, and how

[106-039]they had vanquished their enemies.
