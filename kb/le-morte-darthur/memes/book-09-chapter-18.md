[221-001]well, and how [[sir-palomides|Palomides]] sent a damosel to seek [[sir-tristram|Tristram]], and how Palomides met

[221-002]with [[king-mark|King Mark]].

[221-003]And upon a day Dagonet, [[king-arthur|King Arthur]]’s fool, came into Cornwall with two

[221-004]squires with him; and as they rode through that forest they came by a fair well

[221-005]where Sir Tristram was wont to be; and the weather was hot, and they alighted

[221-006]to drink of that well, and in the meanwhile their horses brake loose. Right so

[221-007]Sir Tristram came unto them, and first he soused Sir Dagonet in that well, and

[221-008]after his squires, and thereat laughed the shepherds; and forthwithal he ran

[221-009]after their horses and brought them again one by one, and right so, wet as they

[221-010]were, he made them leap up and ride their ways. Thus Sir Tristram endured there

[221-011]an half year naked, and would never come in town nor village. The meanwhile the

[221-012]damosel that Sir Palomides sent to seek Sir Tristram, she yede unto Sir

[221-013]Palomides and told him all the mischief that Sir Tristram endured. Alas, said

[221-014]Sir Palomides, it is great [[mercy|pity]] that ever so noble a knight should be so

[221-015]mischieved for the love of a lady; but nevertheless, I will go and seek him,

[221-016]and comfort him an I may. Then a little before that time La Beale Isoud had

[221-017]commanded Sir Kehydius out of the country of Cornwall. So Sir Kehydius departed

[221-018]with a dolorous heart, and by adventure he met with Sir Palomides, and they

[221-019]enfellowshipped together; and either complained to other of their hot love that

[221-020]they loved La Beale Isoud. Now let us, said Sir Palomides, seek Sir Tristram,

[221-021]that loved her as well as we, and let us prove whether we may recover him. So

[221-022]they rode into that forest, and three days and three nights they would never

[221-023]take their lodging, but ever sought Sir Tristram.

[221-024]And upon a time, by adventure, they met with King Mark that was ridden from his

[221-025]men all alone. When they saw him Sir Palomides knew him, but Sir Kehydius knew

[221-026]him not. Ah, false king, said Sir Palomides, it is pity thou hast thy life, for

[221-027]thou art a destroyer of all worshipful knights, and by thy mischief and thy

[221-028]vengeance thou hast destroyed that most noble knight, Sir Tristram de Liones.

[221-029]And therefore defend thee, said Sir Palomides, for thou shalt die this day.

[221-030]That were shame, said King Mark, for ye two are armed and I am unarmed. As for

[221-031]that, said Sir Palomides, I shall find a remedy therefore; here is a knight

[221-032]with me, and thou shalt have his harness. Nay, said King Mark, I will not have

[221-033]ado with you, for cause have ye none to me; for all the misease that Sir

[221-034]Tristram hath was for a letter that he found; for as to me I did to him no

[221-035]displeasure, and God knoweth I am full sorry for his disease and malady. So

[221-036]when the king had thus excused him they were friends, and King Mark would have

[221-037]had them unto Tintagil; but Sir Palomides would not, but turned unto the realm

[221-038]of Logris, and Sir Kehydius said that he would go into Brittany.

[221-039]Now turn we unto Sir Dagonet again, that when he and his squires were upon

[221-040]horseback he deemed that the shepherds had sent that fool to array them so,

[221-041]because that they laughed at them, and so they rode unto the keepers of beasts

[221-042]and all to-beat them. Sir Tristram saw them beat that were wont to give him

[221-043]meat and drink, then he ran thither and gat Sir Dagonet by the head, and gave

[221-044]him such a fall to the earth that he bruised him sore so that he lay still. And

[221-045]then he wrast his sword out of his hand, and therewith he ran to one of his

[221-046]squires and smote off his head, and the other fled. And so Sir Tristram took

[221-047]his way with that sword in his hand, running as he had been wild wood. Then Sir

[221-048]Dagonet rode to King Mark and told him how he had sped in that forest. And

[221-049]therefore, said Sir Dagonet, beware, King Mark, that thou come not about that

[221-050]well in the forest, for there is a fool naked, and that fool and I fool met

[221-051]together, and he had almost slain me. Ah, said King Mark, that is Sir Matto le

[221-052]Breune, that fell out of his wit because he lost his lady; for when Sir Gaheris

[221-053]smote down Sir Matto and won his lady of him, never since was he in his mind,

[221-054]and that was pity, for he was a good knight.
