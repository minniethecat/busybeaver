[222-001]was dead, and how La Beale Isoud would have slain herself.

[222-002]Then Sir Andred, that was cousin unto [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]], made a lady that was his

[222-003]paramour to say and to noise it that she was with Sir Tristram or ever he died.

[222-004]And this tale she brought unto [[king-mark|King Mark]]’s court, that she buried him by

[222-005]a well, and that or he died he besought King Mark to make his cousin, Sir

[222-006]Andred, king of the country of Liones, of the which Sir Tristram was lord of.

[222-007]All this did Sir Andred because he would have had Sir Tristram’s lands.

[222-008]And when King Mark heard tell that Sir Tristram was dead he wept and made great

[222-009]dole. But when Queen Isoud heard of these tidings she made such sorrow that she

[222-010]was nigh out of her mind; and so upon a day she thought to slay herself and

[222-011]never to live after Sir Tristram’s death. And so upon a day La Beale

[222-012]Isoud gat a sword privily and bare it to her garden, and there she pight the

[222-013]sword through a plum tree up to the hilt, so that it stuck fast, and it stood

[222-014]breast high. And as she would have run upon the sword and to have slain herself

[222-015]all this espied King Mark, how she kneeled down and said: Sweet Lord Jesu, have

[222-016]mercy upon me, for I may not live after the death of Sir Tristram de Liones,

[222-017]for he was my first love and he shall be the last. And with these words came

[222-018]King Mark and took her in his arms, and then he took up the sword, and bare her

[222-019]away with him into a tower; and there he made her to be kept, and watched her

[222-020]surely, and after that she lay long sick, nigh at the point of death.

[222-021]This meanwhile ran Sir Tristram naked in the forest with the sword in his hand,

[222-022]and so he came to an hermitage, and there he laid him down and slept; and in

[222-023]the meanwhile the hermit stole away his sword, and laid meat down by him. Thus

[222-024]was he kept there ten days; and at the last he departed and came to the herdmen

[222-025]again. And there was a giant in that country that hight Tauleas, and for fear

[222-026]of Sir Tristram more than seven year he durst never much go at large, but for

[222-027]the most part he kept him in a sure castle of his own; and so this Tauleas

[222-028]heard tell that Sir Tristram was dead, by the noise of the court of King Mark.

[222-029]Then this Tauleas went daily at large. And so he happed upon a day he came to

[222-030]the herdmen wandering and langering, and there he set him down to rest among

[222-031]them. The meanwhile there came a knight of Cornwall that led a lady with him,

[222-032]and his name was Sir Dinant; and when the giant saw him he went from the

[222-033]herdmen and hid him under a tree, and so the knight came to that well, and

[222-034]there he alighted to repose him. And as soon as he was from his horse this

[222-035]giant Tauleas came betwixt this knight and his horse, and took the horse and

[222-036]leapt upon him. So forthwith he rode unto Sir Dinant and took him by the

[222-037]collar, and pulled him afore him upon his horse, and there would have stricken

[222-038]off his head. Then the herdmen said unto Sir Tristram: Help yonder knight. Help

[222-039]ye him, said Sir Tristram. We dare not, said the herdmen. Then Sir Tristram was

[222-040]ware of the sword of the knight thereas it lay; and so thither he ran and took

[222-041]up the sword and struck off Sir Tauleas’ head, and so he yede his way to

[222-042]the herdmen.
