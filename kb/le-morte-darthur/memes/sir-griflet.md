# Sir Griflet

Sir Griflet, son of Cardol, holds a singular distinction among the knights of the [[Round Table]]: he is one of the very first men whom [[King Arthur]] ever dubbed knight. A squire of Arthur's own age who "besought the king for all his service that he had done him to give the order of knighthood" (024-038), Griflet's knighthood is granted against Arthur's initial reluctance — the king judges him "full young and tender of age" (025-002) — and with [[Merlin]]'s ambivalent endorsement: "it were great pity to lose Griflet, for he will be a passing good man when he is of age, abiding with you the term of his life" (025-004). That prophecy shapes Griflet's career: a knight whose value lies not in single-combat supremacy but in lifelong, unbroken service through all the wars of Arthur's rise.

## The First Joust

Griflet's knighthood is immediately tested. As Arthur's gift in return for the accolade, he demands that Griflet promise to joust once with the mysterious knight at the forest fountain — and then return directly, "without making any more debate" (025-013). The knight proves to be of far greater prowess than the newly made Sir Griflet. He warns Griflet: "ye are but young, and late made knight, and your might is nothing to mine" (025-022). Griflet insists on the combat regardless, and the result is decisive: "Griflet's spear all to-shivered; and there withal he smote Griflet through the shield and the left side, and brake the spear that the truncheon stuck in his body, that horse and knight fell down" (025-026). Griflet is carried back to court gravely wounded, fulfilling Merlin's warning about the peril of premature adventure. Yet the tale's outcome — Griflet survives to serve Arthur "the term of his life" — proves both Merlin's foreknowledge and the young knight's resilience.

## The Battle of Bedegraine

Griflet distinguishes himself in the great battle against the eleven kings at Bedegraine. He is "the first that met with a knight, one Ladinas, and they met so eagerly that all men had wonder; and they so fought that their shields fell to pieces, and horse and man fell to the earth" (014-005). So fiercely do they fight that "all men weened they had been dead" (014-009). [[Sir Lucas]] the butler rehorses Griflet, and the two of them "did marvellous deeds of arms with many bachelors" (014-010).

When [[Sir Kay]] is unhorsed by Sir Placidas, "Sir Griflet was wroth, and met with Sir Placidas so hard, that horse and man fell to the earth" (014-017). At the day's end, the three kings — Arthur, Ban, and Bors — "gave the prize unto Sir Kay, and to Lucas the butler, and unto Sir Griflet" (014-024), marking him as one of the three most valiant performers in a battle involving tens of thousands.

Throughout the wider campaign, Griflet fights with unflagging energy. When the rival kings press hard in the main engagement, Griflet is among those unhorsed, yet [[Sir Kay]] himself rehorses him (017-034). Griflet repays the debt: seeing Kay and Lucas on foot, he "did marvellously for to rescue" them (017-069), and later captures an enemy knight's horse to remount Lucas (017-075). His presence in the chronicle is that of a knight perpetually in the thickest press, constantly unhorsed and constantly remounted, never the greatest champion but always among the indispensable.

## A Squire's Rise

Griflet's origin as a squire — "Griflet le Fise de Dieu" among the company listed at Arthur's court (020-007) — gives his knighthood a symbolic weight beyond his personal deeds. He represents the first generation of Arthur's own making: men raised to the order not by birthright or distant renown but by the young king's own hand. His name appears among the earliest roll calls of Arthur's knights (013-077), and his survival through the ferocious early campaigns secures his place in the foundation story of the Round Table. Merlin's prophecy — that Griflet will abide with Arthur "the term of his life" (025-005) — proves true in the most literal sense: a squire who asked for knighthood and received it, then never left his king's side.
