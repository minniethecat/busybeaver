# Chivalry

**Chivalry** is the moral and social code governing the knights of [[king-arthur|Arthur]]ian legend, as depicted in Thomas Malory's *Le Morte d'[[king-arthur|Arthur]]*. It encompasses martial [[courage|prowess]], courtly manners, religious devotion, and feudal loyalty — a composite ideal that Caxton's preface describes as "[[baron|noble]] chivalry, courtesy, humanity, friendliness, [[courage|hardiness]], love, friendship, co[[battle|war]]dice, murder, hate, virtue, and sin" (002-084).

## The Chivalric Ideal in Malory

William Caxton's preface presents *Le Morte d'[[king-arthur|Arthur]]* as a mirror of chivalry, intended "that [[baron|noble]] men may see and learn the [[baron|noble]] acts of chivalry, the gentle and virtuous deeds that some knights used in those days" (002-076). The work is explicitly framed as a didactic text, teaching "[[baron|noble]] and [[honour|renown]]ed acts of humanity, gentleness, and chivalry" (002-083) through the examples of [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s knights.

Caxton emphasizes that the book "treateth of the [[baron|noble]] acts, feats of arms of chivalry, [[courage|prowess]], [[courage|hardiness]], humanity, love, courtesy, and very gentleness, with many [[marvel|wonder]]ful histories and adventures" (002-099), positioning chivalry as the unifying theme of Malory's ent[[wrath|ire]] cycle.

## Chivalry as Martial Excellence

Throughout the work, chivalry is closely associated with battle[[battle|field]] [[courage|prowess]]. The phrase "the flower of chivalry" recurs as an epithet for the finest knights. One knight declares of [[king-arthur|King Arthur]]'s court: "there is the flower of chivalry" (064-007), while another confirms that "Sir [[king-arthur|Arthur]] hath the flower of chivalry of the world with him" (068-026). Knights are praised "for their [[baron|noble]] cheer of chivalry, for the hardiest [[battle|fight]]ers that ever they heard or saw" (020-003).

## Tensions and Contradictions

Malory's text does not present a single, stable definition of chivalry. The narrative records both "[[baron|noble]] chivalry" (002-084) and its [[treason|betrayal]]s — acts of "co[[battle|war]]dice, murder, hate" that Caxton lists alongside the [[virtue|virtues]]. This duality is central to the t[[wrath|rage]]dy of the [[round-table|Round Table]], where the chivalric code itself becomes a source of conflict between competing obligations to [[kinsman|kin]]g, [[kinsman|kin]], and love.
