# Sir Persides de Bloise

Sir Persides de Bloise was a knight of the [[Round Table]] and a close companion of [[Sir Tristram]] of Lyonesse. His most significant contribution to the Arthurian chronicle was his role in identifying [[Sir Palomides]] to King Arthur's court — an act of witness that brought the Saracen knight into the fellowship of the Round Table. Persides exemplified a quieter chivalric virtue: the loyal friend whose service to the order took the form of recognition, introduction, and steadfast companionship.

## Companion to Sir Tristram

Persides appears in the chronicle principally in the company of Sir Tristram. The narrative records how "Sir Tristram and Sir Persides came to their lodging both at once" (229-027), the two knights sharing quarters and companionship during the tournament season. Their friendship was built on mutual recognition: "each welcomed other" (229-029), and when Persides learned that his companion was Sir Tristram himself, the bond between them deepened.

Persides was more than a passive friend. When Tristram proposed that they go to watch a tournament, Persides counselled caution: "Not so, let us not go thither as at this time" (230-006). His was the voice of prudence beside Tristram's readiness for action — a complementary virtue in the fellowship of knights.

## The Identification of Sir Palomides

Persides's most consequential act in the chronicle was the identification of Sir Palomides. When King Arthur's court sought to know the identity of a mysterious and formidable knight who had been defeating all comers, Persides provided the answer. "I know him well, said Sir Persides, he is one of the best knights living" (229-042). He named the knight as "Sir Palomides, that is yet unchristened" (229-044), revealing both the Saracen's identity and his religious status.

This recognition had far-reaching consequences. By naming Palomides publicly, Persides opened the door for the Saracen's eventual integration into the fellowship of the Round Table. His act was one of generous recognition — the good knight who sees the worth of another and speaks it aloud.

## The Tournament Wound

Persides's career was not without cost. In a later tournament, "then was Sir Persides smitten down and almost slain" (232-011), wounded grievously in the press of combat. The chronicle records his injury without dramatic emphasis — a reminder that even the loyal companions of greater knights bore the real physical cost of the chivalric life.

The wound also revealed the depth of Tristram's regard. When Tristram heard of Persides's injury, he felt "great shame that Sir Persides was so done to" (232-015), and his anger on his friend's behalf testified to the bonds of affection that held the Round Table together.

## Service to the Crown

Persides served King Arthur's court in other ways as well. The chronicle notes that "by the means of Sir Persides he had told King Arthur where that Sir Palomides was" (237-016), making him a conduit of intelligence between the scattered knights of the fellowship. In a world where knights wandered far from court on their individual quests, figures like Persides — who knew the whereabouts and identities of distant warriors — performed an essential function in knitting the Round Table together.

## The Loyal Witness

Sir Persides de Bloise embodies a species of chivalric virtue less celebrated than the prowess of a [[Sir Launcelot]] or the endurance of a [[Sir Tristram]], but no less necessary to the life of the [[Round Table]]. He was the friend who recognised worth in others, the companion who counselled prudence, and the witness whose word brought scattered knights into the fellowship. In the intricate web of loyalties that sustained Arthur's court, men like Persides were the threads that held the pattern together.
