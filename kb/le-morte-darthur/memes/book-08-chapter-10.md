[173-003]Then [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]] asked him what he did in those countries. Sir, he said, I

[173-004]came hither with [[sir-gawaine|Sir Gawaine]] for to be made knight, and if it please you, of

[173-005]your hands that I may be made knight. Await upon me as to-morn secretly, and in

[173-006]the [[battle|field]] I shall make you a knight.

[173-007]Then had La Beale Isoud great suspicion unto Tramtrist, that he was some man of

[173-008][[honour|worship]] proved, and therewith she comforted herself, and cast more love unto

[173-009]him than she had done to-fore. And so on the morn Sir Palamides made him ready

[173-010]to come into the field as he did the first day. And there he smote down the

[173-011]King with the Hundred Knights, and [[king-arthur|the King]] of Scots. Then had La Beale Isoud

[173-012]ordained and well arrayed Sir Tristram in white horse and harness. And right so

[173-013]she let put him out at a privy postern, and so he came into the field as it had

[173-014]been a bright angel. And anon Sir Palamides espied him, and therewith he

[173-015]feutred a spear unto Sir Tramtrist, and he again unto him. And there Sir

[173-016]Tristram smote down Sir Palamides unto the earth. And then there was a great

[173-017]noise of people: some said Sir Palamides had a fall, some said the Knight with

[173-018]the Black Shield had a fall. And wit you well La Beale Isoud was passing glad.

[173-019]And then Sir Gawaine and his fellows nine had marvel what knight it might be

[173-020]that had smitten down Sir Palamides. Then would there none joust with

[173-021]Tramtrist, but all that there were forsook him, most and least. Then Sir

[173-022]Tristram made Hebes a knight, and caused him to put himself forth, and did

[173-023]right well that day. So after Sir Hebes held him with Sir Tristram.

[173-024]And when Sir Palamides had received this fall, wit ye well that he was sore

[173-025]ashamed, and as privily as he might he withdrew him out of the field. All that

[173-026]espied Sir Tristram, and lightly he rode after Sir Palamides and overtook him,

[173-027]and bade him turn, for better he would assay him or ever he departed. Then Sir

[173-028]Palamides turned him, and either lashed at other with their swords. But at the

[173-029]first stroke Sir Tristram smote down Palamides, and gave him such a stroke upon

[173-030]the head that he fell to the earth. So then Tristram bade yield him, and do his

[173-031]commandment, or else he would slay him. When Sir Palamides beheld his

[173-032]countenance, he dread his buffets so, that he granted all his askings. Well

[173-033]said, said Sir Tristram, this shall be your charge. First, upon pain of your

[173-034]life that ye forsake my lady La Beale Isoud, and in no manner wise that ye draw

[173-035]not to her. Also this twelvemonth and a day that ye bear none armour nor none

[173-036]harness of war. Now promise me this, or here shalt thou die. Alas, said

[173-037]Palamides, for ever am I ashamed. Then he sware as Sir Tristram had commanded

[173-038]him. Then for despite and anger Sir Palamides cut off his harness, and threw

[173-039]them away.

[173-040]And so Sir Tristram turned again to the castle where was La Beale Isoud; and by

[173-041]the way he met with a damosel that asked after [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]], that won the

[173-042]Dolorous Guard worshipfully; and this damosel asked Sir Tristram what he was.

[173-043]For it was told her that it was he that smote down Sir Palamides, by whom the

[173-044]ten knights of King Arthur’s were smitten down. Then the damosel prayed

[173-045]Sir Tristram to tell her what he was, and whether that he were Sir Launcelot du

[173-046]Lake, for she deemed that there was no knight in the world might do such deeds

[173-047]of arms but if it were Launcelot. Fair damosel, said Sir Tristram, wit ye well

[173-048]that I am not Sir Launcelot, for I was never of such [[courage|prowess]], but in God is all

[173-049]that he may make me as good a knight as the good knight Sir Launcelot. Now,

[173-050]gentle knight, said she, put up thy visor; and when she beheld his visage she

[173-051]thought she saw never a better man’s visage, nor a better faring knight.

[173-052]And then when the damosel knew certainly that he was not Sir Launcelot, then

[173-053]she took her leave, and departed from him. And then Sir Tristram rode privily

[173-054]unto the postern, where kept him La Beale Isoud, and there she made him good

[173-055]cheer, and thanked God of his good speed. So anon, within a while the king and

[173-056]the queen understood that it was Tramtrist that smote down Sir Palamides; then

[173-057]was he much made of, more than he was before.
