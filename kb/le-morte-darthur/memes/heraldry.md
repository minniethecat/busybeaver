# Heraldry

**Heraldry** in [[Le Morte d'Arthur]] is the visual language of chivalric identity — the system of arms, [[shield|shields]], [[banner|banners]], and devices by which knights recognise one another in battle and at [[tournament]]. Though Malory rarely describes coats of arms in formal blazon, the narrative depends upon heraldic recognition: knights identify friends and foes by the devices they bear, and the disguised knight who changes his [[shield]] exploits the system from within.

## Shields and Arms

The [[shield]] is the primary surface of heraldic display. Knights bear arms upon their [[shield|shields]] that declare their identity, their [[blood|lineage]], and their [[fealty|allegiance]]:

> their shields with their arms and banners (103-064)

The phrase "their arms" refers both to [[arms|weapons]] and to the heraldic devices painted upon [[shield|shields]]. To bear arms is to claim a place within the chivalric order — a visual assertion of [[lordship]] and [[lineage]] that precedes any spoken introduction.

The knight who rides with a plain or borrowed shield is deliberately effacing his identity. This concealment is a narrative device of the first importance in Malory: the unknown knight at the tournament, the disguised champion who reveals himself only after victory, the brother who [[battle|fight]]s brother because neither recognises the other's arms.

## Banners and Military Display

The [[banner]] is heraldry at scale — the device of a [[kinsman|kin]]g or great [[baron|lord]] displayed as a rallying point for troops:

> Arthur was embattled and his banner displayed (103-019)

The display of the [[kinsman|kin]]g's banner signals the formal commencement of battle. Its capture or display upon enemy walls is the sign of victory or defeat:

> set upon the walls the king's banner (107-030)

When the [[kinsman|kin]]g "saw the city and his banner on the walls, by which he knew it was won" (107-031), the banner functions as a message across distance — heraldry as military communication.

In the great battle scenes, "advanced banners and smote together and all their troops" (041-030) marks the moment of en[[challenge|gage]]ment. The banner is the visual anchor around which the chaos of battle organises itself.

## The Black Banner

Malory deploys heraldic imagery for symbolic as well as practical effect. The black banner is a sign of mourning, menace, or [[marvel|supernatural]] agency:

> thereon hung a black banner (133-032)

The black banner appears at moments of narrative darkness — death, [[treason]], or otherworldly intervention. Its colour is legible to every knight who sees it, part of a shared heraldic vocabulary that needs no gloss.

## Recognition and Misrecognition

The narrative engine of Malory's work depends upon heraldic recognition and its deliberate frustration. A knight sees a shield and knows its bearer; conversely, a knight who changes his shield becomes, for narrative purposes, a different person:

> Sir Launcelot had taken upon him another armour and another shield (implied throughout the tournament sequences)

This capacity to manipulate identity through arms is essential to the chivalric adventure. The [[joust]] and the [[tournament]] are not only tests of [[prowess]] but theatres of identity, in which the heraldic sign may confirm or conceal the person beneath.

## Heraldry's Narrative Function

Heraldry in Malory is the system that makes visual sense of the chivalric world. It turns anonymous [[arms|armour]]ed figures into members of named houses, and it enables the misrecognition plots — brothers [[battle|fight]]ing brothers, lovers [[joust|jousting]] against lovers — that structure many of the work's most memorable episodes.

The heraldic device is never merely decorative. It is a claim staked in paint and metal: this is who I am, this is whom I serve, this is the [[honour]] I bear. To strike a man's shield is to strike his identity.
