[184-001]from a knight, and how Gouvernail told him of King Anguish.

[184-002]The knight turned his horse and made him ready to [[battle|fight]]. And then [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]]

[184-003]smote him with a sword such a buffet that he tumbled to the earth. And then he

[184-004]yielded him unto Sir Tristram. Then come thy way, said Sir Tristram, and bring

[184-005]the child to the lady again. So he took his horse meekly and rode with Sir

[184-006]Tristram; and then by the way Sir Tristram asked him his name. Then he said, My

[184-007]name is Breuse Saunce Pité. So when he had delivered that child to the lady, he

[184-008]said: Sir, as in this the child is well remedied. Then Sir Tristram let him go

[184-009]again that sore repented him after, for he was a great foe unto many good

[184-010]knights of [[king-arthur|King Arthur]]’s court.

[184-011]Then when Sir Tristram was in his pavilion Gouvernail, his man, came and told

[184-012]him how that King Anguish of Ireland was come thither, and he was put in great

[184-013]distress; and there Gouvernail told Sir Tristram how King Anguish was summoned

[184-014]and appealed of murder. So God me help, said Sir Tristram, these be the best

[184-015]tidings that ever came to me this seven years, for now shall the King of

[184-016]Ireland have need of my help; for I daresay there is no knight in this country

[184-017]that is not of Arthur’s court dare do battle with Sir Blamore de Ganis;

[184-018]and for to win the love of the King of Ireland I will take the battle upon me;

[184-019]and therefore Gouvernail bring me, I charge thee, to the king.

[184-020]Then Gouvernail went unto King Anguish of Ireland, and saluted him fair. The

[184-021]king welcomed him and asked him what he would. Sir, said Gouvernail, here is a

[184-022]knight near hand that desireth to speak with you: he bade me say he would do

[184-023]you service. What knight is he? said the king. Sir, said he, it is Sir Tristram

[184-024]de Liones, that for your good grace that ye showed him in your lands will

[184-025]reward you in this country. Come on, fellow, said the king, with me anon and

[184-026]show me unto Sir Tristram. So the king took a little hackney and but few

[184-027]fellowship with him, until he came unto Sir Tristram’s pavilion. And when

[184-028]Sir Tristram saw the king he ran unto him and would have holden his stirrup.

[184-029]But the king leapt from his horse lightly, and either halsed other in their

[184-030]arms. My gracious lord, said Sir Tristram, [[mercy|gramercy]] of your great goodnesses

[184-031]showed unto me in your marches and lands: and at that time I promised you to do

[184-032]you service an ever it lay in my power. And, gentle knight, said the king unto

[184-033]Sir Tristram, now have I great need of you, never had I so great need of no

[184-034]knight’s help. How so, my good lord? said Sir Tristram. I shall tell you,

[184-035]said the king: I am summoned and appealed from my country for the death of a

[184-036]knight that was kin unto the good knight [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]]; wherefore Sir Blamore

[184-037]de Ganis, brother to Sir Bleoberis hath appealed me to fight with him, outher

[184-038]to find a knight in my stead. And well I wot, said the king, these that are

[184-039]come of King Ban’s blood, as Sir Launcelot and these other, are passing

[184-040]good knights, and hard men for to win in battle as any that I know now living.

[184-041]Sir, said Sir Tristram, for the good lordship ye showed me in Ireland, and for

[184-042]my lady your daughter’s sake, La Beale Isoud, I will take the battle for

[184-043]you upon this condition that ye shall grant me two things: that one is that ye

[184-044]shall swear to me that ye are in the right, that ye were never consenting to

[184-045]the knight’s death; Sir, then said Sir Tristram, when that I have done

[184-046]this battle, if God give me grace that I speed, that ye shall give me a reward,

[184-047]what thing reasonable that I will ask of you. So God me help, said the king, ye

[184-048]shall have whatsomever ye will ask. It is well said, said Sir Tristram.
