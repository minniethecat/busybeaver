[223-003]Then the knight took up the giant’s head and bare it with him unto King

[223-004]Mark, and told him what adventure betid him in the forest, and how a naked man

[223-005]rescued him from the grimly giant, Tauleas. Where had ye this adventure? said

[223-006][[king-mark|King Mark]]. Forsooth, said Sir Dinant, at the fair fountain in your forest where

[223-007]many adventurous knights meet, and there is the mad man. Well, said King Mark,

[223-008]I will see that wild man. So within a day or two King Mark commanded his

[223-009]knights and his hunters that they should be ready on the morn for to hunt, and

[223-010]so upon the morn he went unto that forest. And when the king came to that well

[223-011]he found there lying by that well a fair naked man, and a sword by him. Then

[223-012]King Mark blew and straked, and therewith his knights came to him; and then the

[223-013]king commanded his knights to: Take that naked man with fairness, and bring him

[223-014]to my castle. So they did softly and fair, and cast mantles upon [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]],

[223-015]and so led him unto Tintagil; and there they bathed him, and washed him, and

[223-016]gave him hot suppings till they had brought him well to his remembrance; but

[223-017]all this while there was no creature that knew Sir Tristram, nor what man he

[223-018]was.

[223-019]So it fell upon a day that the queen, La Beale Isoud, heard of such a man, that

[223-020]ran naked in the forest, and how the king had brought him home to the court.

[223-021]Then La Beale Isoud called unto her Dame Bragwaine and said: Come on with me,

[223-022]for we will go see this man that my lord brought from the forest the last day.

[223-023]So they passed forth, and spered where was the sick man. And then a squire told

[223-024]the queen that he was in the garden taking his rest, and reposing him against

[223-025]the sun. So when the queen looked upon Sir Tristram she was not remembered of

[223-026]him. But ever she said unto Dame Bragwaine: Meseemeth I should have seen him

[223-027]heretofore in many places. But as soon as Sir Tristram saw her he knew her well

[223-028]enough. And then he turned away his visage and wept.

[223-029]Then the queen had always a little brachet with her that Sir Tristram gave her

[223-030]the first time that ever she came into Cornwall, and never would that brachet

[223-031]depart from her but if Sir Tristram was nigh thereas was La Beale Isoud; and

[223-032]this brachet was sent from the king’s daughter of France unto Sir

[223-033]Tristram for great love. And anon as this little brachet felt a savour of Sir

[223-034]Tristram, she leapt upon him and licked his lears and his ears, and then she

[223-035]whined and quested, and she smelled at his feet and at his hands, and on all

[223-036]parts of his body that she might come to. Ah, my lady, said Dame Bragwaine unto

[223-037]La Beale Isoud, alas, alas, said she, I see it is mine own lord, Sir Tristram.

[223-038]And thereupon Isoud fell down in a swoon, and so lay a great while. And when

[223-039]she might speak she said: My lord Sir Tristram, blessed be God ye have your

[223-040]life, and now I am sure ye shall be discovered by this little brachet, for she

[223-041]will never leave you. And also I am sure as soon as my lord, King Mark, do know

[223-042]you he will banish you out of the country of Cornwall, or else he will destroy

[223-043]you; for God’s sake, mine own lord, grant King Mark his will, and then

[223-044]draw you unto [[camelot|the court of King Arthur]], for there are ye beloved, and ever when

[223-045]I may I shall send unto you; and when ye list ye may come to me, and at all

[223-046]times early and late I will be at your commandment, to live as poor a life as

[223-047]ever did queen or lady. O Madam, said Sir Tristram, go from me, for mickle

[223-048]anger and danger have I escaped for your love.
