# Sir Damas

**Sir Damas** appears in the early books of Thomas Malory's *Le Morte d'[[King Arthur]]* as a figure of fraternal treachery — "the falsest knight that now liveth" (073-006). His story forms one of King Arthur's earliest tests of [[justice]]: a dispute between two brothers that reveals the young king's capacity to judge between right and wrong, even when one of the parties has rendered him unwilling service.

## The Brothers' War

Sir Damas and his younger brother, Sir Ontzlake, were lords of a [[castle]] and its surrounding lands. The elder Damas, "the falsest knight that now liveth, and the greatest destroyer of all people" (073-012), sought to dispossess his brother entirely. When Ontzlake refused to surrender his share, Damas "proffereth Sir Damas to fight for the livelihood" — demanding trial by combat to settle the matter (073-015).

But Damas' reputation was so foul that "there was never knight would fight for him" (073-016). No warrior in the land would stake his [[honour]] on the cause of a man so widely despised. Meanwhile, Ontzlake, the righteous brother, found a champion willing to fight for him — though that champion lay wounded and could not take the field.

## The Unwilling Champion

By chance, [[King Arthur]] himself, wandering alone in the forest after a [[hunt]], was captured and brought to Sir Damas' castle. There he was presented with a cruel choice: "he should fight for Sir Damas against his brother, or else to die in prison" (073-042). Arthur, unrecognised by his captors, agreed to fight — a decision that placed the King of all England in the service of a traitor, sworn "unto Arthur" himself without either party knowing the irony (073-044).

## Arthur's Judgment

After the combat — in which Arthur, fighting unwillingly for Damas, acquitted himself nobly — the king revealed himself and delivered his [[judgment]]. He addressed the brothers directly: "As to thee, Sir Damas, for whom I have been champion" (078-007). His verdict was severe and unambiguous: "I will judge because ye, Sir Damas, are called an horrible traitor" (078-008).

Arthur stripped Damas of his lordship and commanded: "I charge thee, Sir Damas, upon pain of death, that thou give thy brother Sir Ontzlake the whole manor" (078-013). All that Damas had sought to steal was taken from him and given to the brother he had wronged. Yet Arthur tempered [[justice]] with mercy: Ontzlake was commanded to "worshipfully as your brother Sir Damas" provide for the fallen knight (078-023), ensuring that even a false knight should not starve.

## The Pattern of the False Brother

Sir Damas is among the earliest villains confronted by [[King Arthur]] in his reign. His crime — the perversion of [[kinsman|kinship]] — establishes a pattern that recurs throughout the Morte: the brother who turns against brother, the [[kinsman]] who becomes a destroyer. Damas stands at the head of a lineage of treacherous kin that extends through figures like [[Sir Andred]] and [[Mordred]], his story serving as a first test of Arthur's capacity to discern truth from falsehood and to deliver [[justice]] that is at once firm and merciful.
