[110-001][[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]], and how he was taken by Sir Turquine.

[110-002]When Sir Ector de Maris wist that Sir Launcelot was passed out of the court to

[110-003]seek adventures, he was wroth with himself, and made him ready to seek Sir

[110-004]Launcelot, and as he had ridden long in a great forest he met with a man was

[110-005]like a forester. Fair fellow, said Sir Ector, knowest thou in this country any

[110-006]adventures that be here nigh hand? Sir, said the forester, this country know I

[110-007]well, and hereby, within this mile, is a strong manor, and well dyked, and by

[110-008]that manor, on the left hand, there is a fair ford for horses to drink of, and

[110-009]over that ford there groweth a fair tree, and thereon hang many fair shields

[110-010]that wielded sometime good knights, and at the hole of the tree hangeth a basin

[110-011]of copper and latten, and strike upon that basin with the butt of thy spear

[110-012]thrice, and soon after thou shalt hear new tidings, and else hast thou the

[110-013]fairest grace that many a year had ever knight that passed through this forest.

[110-014]Gramercy, said Sir Ector, and departed and came to the tree, and saw many fair

[110-015]shields. And among them he saw his brother’s shield, Sir Lionel, and many

[110-016]more that he knew that were his fellows of the [[round-table|Round Table]], the which grieved

[110-017]his heart, and promised to revenge his brother.

[110-018]Then anon Sir Ector beat on the basin as he were wood, and then he gave his

[110-019]horse drink at the ford, and there came a knight behind him and bade him come

[110-020]out of the water and make him ready; and Sir Ector anon turned him shortly, and

[110-021]in feuter cast his spear, and smote the other knight a great buffet that his

[110-022]horse turned twice about. This was well done, said the strong knight, and

[110-023]knightly thou hast stricken me; and therewith he rushed his horse on Sir Ector,

[110-024]and cleight him under his right arm, and bare him clean out of the saddle, and

[110-025]rode with him away into his own hall, and threw him down in midst of the floor.

[110-026]The name of this knight was Sir Turquine. Then he said unto Sir Ector, For thou

[110-027]hast done this day more unto me than any knight did these twelve years, now

[110-028]will I grant thee thy life, so thou wilt be sworn to be my prisoner all thy

[110-029]life days. Nay, said Sir Ector, that will I never promise thee, but that I will

[110-030]do mine advantage. That me repenteth, said Sir Turquine. And then he gart to

[110-031]unarm him, and beat him with thorns all naked, and sithen put him down in a

[110-032]deep dungeon, where he knew many of his fellows. But when Sir Ector saw Sir

[110-033]Lionel, then made he great sorrow. Alas, brother, said Sir Ector, where is my

[110-034]brother Sir Launcelot? Fair brother, I left him asleep when that I from him

[110-035]yode, under an apple-tree, and what is become of him I cannot tell you. Alas,

[110-036]said the knights, but Sir Launcelot help us we may never be delivered, for we

[110-037]know now no knight that is able to match our master Turquine.
