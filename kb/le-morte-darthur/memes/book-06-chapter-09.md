[117-001][[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]] bade Sir Gaheris deliver all the prisoners.

[117-002]Then at the last Sir Turquine waxed faint, and gave somewhat aback, and bare

[117-003]his shield low for weariness. That espied Sir Launcelot, and leapt upon him

[117-004]fiercely and gat him by the beaver of his helmet, and plucked him down on his

[117-005]knees, and anon he raced off his helm, and smote his neck in sunder. And when

[117-006]Sir Launcelot had done this, he yode unto the damosel and said, Damosel, I am

[117-007]ready to go with you where ye will have me, but I have no horse. Fair sir, said

[117-008]she, take this wounded knight’s horse and send him into this manor, and

[117-009]command him to deliver all the prisoners. So Sir Launcelot went unto Gaheris,

[117-010]and prayed him not to be aggrieved for to lend him his horse. Nay, fair lord,

[117-011]said Gaheris, I will that ye take my horse at your own commandment, for ye have

[117-012]both saved me and my horse, and this day I say ye are the best knight in the

[117-013]world, for ye have slain this day in my sight the mightiest man and the best

[117-014]knight except you that ever I saw, and, fair sir, said Gaheris, I pray you tell

[117-015]me your name. Sir, my name is Sir Launcelot du Lake, that ought to help you of

[117-016]right for [[king-arthur|King Arthur]]’s sake, and in especial for my lord Sir

[117-017][[sir-gawaine|Gawaine]]’s sake, your own dear brother; and when that ye come within

[117-018]yonder manor, I am sure ye shall find there many knights of the [[round-table|Round Table]],

[117-019]for I have seen many of their shields that I know on yonder tree. There is

[117-020][[sir-kay|Kay]]’s shield, and Sir Brandel’s shield, and Sir Marhaus’

[117-021]shield, and Sir Galind’s shield, and Sir Brian de Listnois’ shield,

[117-022]and Sir Aliduke’s shield, with many more that I am not now advised of,

[117-023]and also my two brethren’s shields, Sir Ector de Maris and Sir Lionel;

[117-024]wherefore I pray you greet them all from me, and say that I bid them take such

[117-025]stuff there as they find, and that in any wise my brethren go unto the court

[117-026]and abide me there till that I come, for by the feast of Pentecost I cast me to

[117-027]be there, for as at this time I must ride with this damosel for to save my

[117-028]promise.

[117-029]And so he departed from Gaheris, and Gaheris yede in to the manor, and there he

[117-030]found a yeoman porter keeping there many keys. Anon withal Sir Gaheris threw

[117-031]the porter unto the ground and took the keys from him, and hastily he opened

[117-032]the prison door, and there he let out all the prisoners, and every man loosed

[117-033]other of their bonds. And when they saw Sir Gaheris, all they thanked him, for

[117-034]they weened that he was wounded. Not so, said Gaheris, it was Launcelot that

[117-035]slew him worshipfully with his own hands. I saw it with mine own eyes. And he

[117-036]greeteth you all well, and prayeth you to haste you to the court; and as unto

[117-037]Sir Lionel and Ector de Maris he prayeth you to abide him at the court. That

[117-038]shall we not do, says his brethren, we will find him an we may live. So shall

[117-039]I, said Sir Kay, find him or I come at the court, as I am true knight.

[117-040]Then all those knights sought the house thereas the armour was, and then they

[117-041]armed them, and every knight found his own horse, and all that ever longed unto

[117-042]him. And when this was done, there came a forester with four horses laden with

[117-043]fat venison. Anon, Sir Kay said, Here is good meat for us for one meal, for we

[117-044]had not many a day no good repast. And so that venison was roasted, baken, and

[117-045]sodden, and so after supper some abode there all night, but Sir Lionel and

[117-046]Ector de Maris and Sir Kay rode after Sir Launcelot to find him if they might.
