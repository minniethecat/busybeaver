[234-002]Then [[king-arthur|the King]] with the Hundred Knights withdrew his knights. And all this

[234-003]while, and long to-fore, [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]] had watched upon [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]] with a

[234-004]very purpose to have fellowshipped with him. And then suddenly Sir Tristram,

[234-005]Sir Dinadan, and Gouvernail, his man, rode their way into the forest, that no

[234-006]man perceived where they went. So then King Arthur blew unto lodging, and gave

[234-007]the King of Northgalis the prize because Sir Tristram was upon his side. Then

[234-008]Sir Launcelot rode here and there, so wood as lion that fauted his fill,

[234-009]because he had lost Sir Tristram, and so he returned unto King Arthur. And then

[234-010]in all the [[battle|field]] was a noise that with the wind it might be heard two mile

[234-011]thence, how the lords and ladies cried: The Knight with the Black Shield hath

[234-012]won the field. Alas, said King Arthur, where is that knight become? It is shame

[234-013]to all those in the field so to let him escape away from you; but with

[234-014]gentleness and courtesy ye might have brought him unto me to the Castle of

[234-015]Maidens. Then the noble King Arthur went unto his knights and comforted them in

[234-016]the best wise that he could, and said: My fair fellows, be not dismayed,

[234-017]howbeit ye have lost the field this day. And many were hurt and sore wounded,

[234-018]and many were whole. My fellows, said King Arthur, look that ye be of good

[234-019]cheer, for to-morn I will be in the field with you and revenge you of your

[234-020]enemies. So that night King Arthur and his knights reposed themself.

[234-021]The damosel that came from La Beale Isoud unto Sir Tristram, all the while the

[234-022]tournament was a-doing she was with [[queen-guenever|Queen Guenever]], and ever the queen asked

[234-023]her for what cause she came into that country. Madam, she answered, I come for

[234-024]none other cause but from my lady La Beale Isoud to wit of your welfare. For in

[234-025]no wise she would not tell the queen that she came for Sir Tristram’s

[234-026]sake. So this lady, Dame Bragwaine, took her leave of Queen Guenever, and she

[234-027]rode after Sir Tristram. And as she rode through the forest she heard a great

[234-028]cry; then she commanded her squire to go into the forest to wit what was that

[234-029]noise. And so he came to a well, and there he found a knight bounden till a

[234-030]tree crying as he had been wood, and his horse and his harness standing by him.

[234-031]And when he espied that squire, therewith he abraid and brake himself loose,

[234-032]and took his sword in his hand, and ran to have slain the squire. Then he took

[234-033]his horse and fled all that ever he might unto Dame Bragwaine, and told her of

[234-034]his adventure. Then she rode unto Sir Tristram’s pavilion, and told Sir

[234-035]Tristram what adventure she had found in the forest. Alas, said Sir Tristram,

[234-036]upon my head there is some good knight at mischief.

[234-037]Then Sir Tristram took his horse and his sword and rode thither, and there he

[234-038]heard how the knight complained unto himself and said: I, woful knight Sir

[234-039][[sir-palomides|Palomides]], what misadventure befalleth me, that thus am defoiled with falsehood

[234-040]and treason, through [[sir-bors|Sir Bors]] and Sir Ector. Alas, he said, why live I so long!

[234-041]And then he gat his sword in his hands, and made many strange signs and tokens;

[234-042]and so through his raging he threw his sword into that fountain. Then Sir

[234-043]Palomides wailed and wrang his hands. And at the last for pure sorrow he ran

[234-044]into that fountain, over his belly, and sought after his sword. Then Sir

[234-045]Tristram saw that, and ran upon Sir Palomides, and held him in his arms fast.

[234-046]What art thou, said Palomides, that holdeth me so? I am a man of this forest

[234-047]that would thee none harm. Alas, said Sir Palomides, I may never win [[honour|worship]]

[234-048]where Sir Tristram is; for ever where he is an I be there, then get I no

[234-049]worship; and if he be away for the most part I have the gree, unless that Sir

[234-050]Launcelot be there or Sir Lamorak. Then Sir Palomides said: Once in Ireland Sir

[234-051]Tristram put me to the worse, and another time in Cornwall, and in other places

[234-052]in this land. What would ye do, said Sir Tristram, an ye had Sir Tristram? I

[234-053]would fight with him, said Sir Palomides, and ease my heart upon him; and yet,

[234-054]to say thee sooth, Sir Tristram is the gentlest knight in this world living.

[234-055]What will ye do, said Sir Tristram, will ye go with me to your lodging? Nay,

[234-056]said he, I will go to the King with the Hundred Knights, for he rescued me from

[234-057]Sir Bors de Ganis and Sir Ector and else had I been slain traitorly. Sir

[234-058]Tristram said him such kind words that Sir Palomides went with him to his

[234-059]lodging. Then Gouvernail went to-fore, and charged Dame Bragwaine to go out of

[234-060]the way to her lodging. And bid ye Sir Persides that he make him no quarrels.

[234-061]And so they rode together till they came to Sir Tristram’s pavilion, and

[234-062]there Sir Palomides had all the cheer that might be had all that night. But in

[234-063]no wise Sir Palomides might not know what was Sir Tristram; and so after supper

[234-064]they yede to rest, and Sir Tristram for great travail slept till it was day.

[234-065]And Sir Palomides might not sleep for anguish; and in the dawning of the day he

[234-066]took his horse privily, and rode his way unto Sir Gaheris and unto Sir

[234-067]Sagramore le Desirous, where they were in their pavilions; for they three were

[234-068]fellows at the beginning of the tournament. And then upon the morn the king

[234-069]blew unto the tournament upon the third day.
