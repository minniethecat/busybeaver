[141-001]word from her sister how she had brought a knight to [[battle|fight]] for her, and what

[141-002]battles he had achieved.

[141-003]So the book saith that the lady that was besieged had word of her

[141-004]sister’s coming by the dwarf, and a knight with her, and how he had

[141-005]passed all the perilous passages. What manner a man is he? said the lady. He is

[141-006]a noble knight, truly, madam, said the dwarf, and but a young man, but he is as

[141-007]likely a man as ever ye saw any. What is he? said the damosel, and of what kin

[141-008]is he come, and of whom was he made knight? Madam, said the dwarf, he is the

[141-009]king’s son of Orkney, but his name I will not tell you as at this time;

[141-010]but wit ye well, of [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]] was he made knight, for of none other would

[141-011]he be made knight, and [[sir-kay|Sir Kay]] named him [[sir-gareth|Beaumains]]. How escaped he, said the

[141-012]lady, from the brethren of Persant? Madam, he said, as a noble knight should.

[141-013]First, he slew two brethren at a passage of a water. Ah! said she, they were

[141-014]good knights, but they were murderers, the one hight Gherard le Breuse, and the

[141-015]other knight hight Sir Arnold le Breuse. Then, madam, he recountered with the

[141-016]Black Knight, and slew him in plain battle, and so he took his horse and his

[141-017]armour and fought with the Green Knight and won him in plain battle, and in

[141-018]like wise he served the Red Knight, and after in the same wise he served the

[141-019]Blue Knight and won him in plain battle. Then, said the lady, he hath overcome

[141-020]Sir Persant of Inde, one of the noblest knights of the world, and the dwarf

[141-021]said, He hath won all the four brethren and slain the Black Knight, and yet he

[141-022]did more to-fore: he overthrew Sir Kay and left him nigh dead upon the ground;

[141-023]also he did a great battle with Sir Launcelot, and there they departed on even

[141-024]hands: and then Sir Launcelot made him knight.

[141-025]Dwarf, said the lady, I am glad of these tidings, therefore go thou in an

[141-026]hermitage of mine hereby, and there shalt thou bear with thee of my wine in two

[141-027]flagons of silver, they are of two gallons, and also two cast of bread with fat

[141-028]venison baked, and dainty fowls; and a cup of gold here I deliver thee, that is

[141-029]rich and precious; and bear all this to mine hermitage, and put it in the

[141-030]hermit’s hands. And sithen go thou unto my sister and greet her well, and

[141-031]commend me unto that gentle knight, and pray him to eat and to drink and make

[141-032]him strong, and say ye him I thank him of his courtesy and goodness, that he

[141-033]would take upon him such labour for me that never did him bounty nor courtesy.

[141-034]Also pray him that he be of good heart and courage, for he shall meet with a

[141-035]full noble knight, but he is neither of bounty, courtesy, nor gentleness; for

[141-036]he attendeth unto nothing but to murder, and that is the cause I cannot praise

[141-037]him nor love him.

[141-038]So this dwarf departed, and came to Sir Persant, where he found the damosel

[141-039]Linet and Sir Beaumains, and there he told them all as ye have heard; and then

[141-040]they took their leave, but Sir Persant took an ambling hackney and conveyed

[141-041]them on their ways, and then beleft them to God; and so within a little while

[141-042]they came to that hermitage, and there they drank the wine, and ate the venison

[141-043]and the fowls baken. And so when they had repasted them well, the dwarf

[141-044]returned again with his vessel unto the castle again; and there met with him

[141-045]the Red Knight of the Red Launds, and asked him from whence that he came, and

[141-046]where he had been. Sir, said the dwarf, I have been with my lady’s sister

[141-047]of this castle, and she hath been at [[king-arthur|King Arthur]]’s court, and brought a

[141-048]knight with her. Then I account her travail but lost; for though she had

[141-049]brought with her Sir Launcelot, [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]], Sir Lamorak, or [[sir-gawaine|Sir Gawaine]], I

[141-050]would think myself good enough for them all.

[141-051]It may well be, said the dwarf, but this knight hath passed all the perilous

[141-052]passages, and slain the Black Knight and other two more, and won the Green

[141-053]Knight, the Red Knight, and the Blue Knight. Then is he one of these four that

[141-054]I have afore rehearsed. He is none of those, said the dwarf, but he is a

[141-055]king’s son. What is his name? said the Red Knight of the Red Launds. That

[141-056]will I not tell you, said the dwarf, but Sir Kay upon scorn named him

[141-057]Beaumains. I care not, said the knight, what knight so ever he be, for I shall

[141-058]soon deliver him. And if I ever match him he shall have a shameful death as

[141-059]many other have had. That were [[mercy|pity]], said the dwarf, and it is marvel that ye

[141-060]make such shameful war upon noble knights.
