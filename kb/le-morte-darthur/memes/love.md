# Love

**Love** is, alongside [[courage|prowess]] in arms, the primary motive force of Thomas Malory's *Le Morte d'[[king-arthur|Arthur]]*. It is simultaneously celebrated as a wellspring of knightly greatness and indicted as the passion that destroys the [[Round Table]].

Malory never resolves this duality — and the refusal to resolve it is the source of the Morte's enduring moral complexity. Caxton's preface names "love" among the [[virtue|virtues]] the book teaches (002-084), yet the same preface also records the "discourse on true love" (001-053) that anatomises love's destructive power.

The tension between love-as-virtue and love-as-vice runs through every major narrative strand: [[King Arthur]]'s conception, [[Sir Launcelot]]'s t[[wrath|rage]]dy, [[Sir Tristram]]'s exile, and the [[Sangreal]] quest's spiritual [[judgment|judgement]].

## Love as Chivalric Inspiration

Malory presents love as a legitimate and powerful source of knightly [[courage|prowess]]. The narrator observes that "the [[kinsman|kin]]g liked and loved this lady well" (004-009). Uther [[dragon|Pendragon]]'s passion for Igraine — driven by "pure [[wrath|anger]] and for great love of fair Igraine" (004-034) — is cosmically generative: it produces [[king-arthur|Arthur]], the [[kinsman|kin]]g around whom the ent[[wrath|ire]] cycle revolves.

The great knights of the [[round-table|Round Table]] are all defined by love. [[sir-launcelot|Launcelot]]'s devotion to [[Queen Guenever]] insp[[wrath|ire]]s his greatest victories; [[sir-tristram|Tristram]]'s passion for La Beale Isoud fuels his tournament triumphs. The ideal Malory inherits from the courtly [[custom|tradition]] holds that a knight without a lady is a knight without a reason to excel.

## Love's Destructive Power

Yet the same force that insp[[wrath|ire]]s greatness also destroys. The love between [[sir-launcelot|Launcelot]] and [[queen-guenever|Guenever]] is the most consequential example: it prevents [[sir-launcelot|Launcelot]] from achieving the [[Sangreal]], triggers the exposure of their affair, and ignites the civil [[battle|war]] that ends [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s reign.

[[sir-tristram|Tristram]]'s love for Isoud — his uncle [[King Mark]]'s wife — follows the same trajectory: greatness abroad, t[[wrath|rage]]dy at home, and a death that is intimate rather than heroic.

Caxton's preface itemises the [[virtue|virtues]] the Morte teaches: "[[courage|hardiness]], humanity, love, courtesy, and very gentleness" (002-100). Love's inclusion in this catalogue — alongside [[courage|hardiness]] and courtesy — is remarkable precisely because love is also the book's great destroyer.

## The Judgement of the Sangreal

The [[Sangreal]] quest provides Malory's most explicit [[judgment|judgement]] upon earthly love. The knights who achieve the Grail — [[Sir Galahad]], [[Sir Percival]], [[sir-bors|Sir Bors]] — are distinguished not by their [[courage|prowess]] but by their purity.

Galahad is "a clean maiden in will and in deed" (071-025); [[sir-launcelot|Launcelot]], for all his greatness, cannot approach the mystery. His love for [[queen-guenever|Guenever]], however [[baron|noble]] in chivalric terms, is adulterous in spiritual terms.

This [[judgment|judgement]] is not, however, Malory's last word. After the [[sangreal|Sangreal]] quest, [[sir-launcelot|Launcelot]] returns to [[queen-guenever|Guenever]]; the claims of earthly love reassert themselves against the claims of spiritual purity.

Malory does not condemn this return so much as record it. In that recording lies the Morte's deepest insight: that love cannot be renounced any more than it can be redeemed.
