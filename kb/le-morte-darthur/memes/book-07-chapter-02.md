[129-001]were wroth because [[sir-kay|Sir Kay]] mocked [[sir-gareth|Beaumains]], and of a damosel which desired a

[129-002]knight to [[battle|fight]] for a lady.

[129-003]Thereat was [[sir-gawaine|Sir Gawaine]] wroth, and in especial [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]] bade Sir Kay leave

[129-004]his mocking, for I dare lay my head he shall prove a man of great [[honour|worship]]. Let

[129-005]be said Sir Kay, it may not be by no reason, for as he is, so he hath asked.

[129-006]Beware, said Sir Launcelot, so ye gave the good knight Brewnor, Sir

[129-007]Dinadan’s brother, a name, and ye called him La Cote Male Taile, and that

[129-008]turned you to anger afterward. As for that, said Sir Kay, this shall never

[129-009]prove none such. For Sir Brewnor desired ever worship, and this desireth bread

[129-010]and drink and broth; upon pain of my life he was fostered up in some abbey,

[129-011]and, howsomever it was, they failed meat and drink, and so hither he is come

[129-012]for his sustenance.

[129-013]And so Sir Kay bade get him a place, and sit down to meat; so Beaumains went to

[129-014]the hall door, and set him down among boys and lads, and there he ate sadly.

[129-015]And then Sir Launcelot after meat bade him come to his chamber, and there he

[129-016]should have meat and drink enough. And so did Sir Gawaine: but he refused them

[129-017]all; he would do none other but as Sir Kay commanded him, for no proffer. But

[129-018]as touching Sir Gawaine, he had reason to proffer him lodging, meat, and drink,

[129-019]for that proffer came of his blood, for he was nearer kin to him than he wist.

[129-020]But that as Sir Launcelot did was of his great gentleness and courtesy.

[129-021]So thus he was put into the kitchen, and lay nightly as the boys of the kitchen

[129-022]did. And so he endured all that twelvemonth, and never displeased man nor

[129-023]child, but always he was meek and mild. But ever when that he saw any [[tournament|jousting]]

[129-024]of knights, that would he see an he might. And ever Sir Launcelot would give

[129-025]him gold to spend, and clothes, and so did Sir Gawaine, and where there were

[129-026]any masteries done, thereat would he be, and there might none cast bar nor

[129-027]stone to him by two yards. Then would Sir Kay say, How liketh you my boy of the

[129-028]kitchen? So it passed on till the feast of Whitsuntide. And at that time the

[129-029]king held it at [[camelot|Carlion]] in the most royallest wise that might be, like as he

[129-030]did yearly. But the king would no meat eat upon the Whitsunday, until he heard

[129-031]some adventures. Then came there a squire to the king and said, Sir, ye may go

[129-032]to your meat, for here cometh a damosel with some strange adventures. Then was

[129-033]the king glad and sat him down.

[129-034]Right so there came a damosel into the hall and saluted the king, and prayed

[129-035]him of succour. For whom? said the king, what is the adventure?

[129-036]Sir, she said, I have a lady of great worship and renown, and she is besieged

[129-037]with a tyrant, so that she may not out of her castle; and because here are

[129-038]called the noblest knights of the world, I come to you to pray you of succour.

[129-039]What hight your lady, and where dwelleth she, and who is she, and what is his

[129-040]name that hath besieged her? Sir king, she said, as for my lady’s name

[129-041]that shall not ye know for me as at this time, but I let you wit she is a lady

[129-042]of great worship and of great lands; and as for the tyrant that besiegeth her

[129-043]and destroyeth her lands, he is called the Red Knight of the Red Launds. I know

[129-044]him not, said the king. Sir, said Sir Gawaine, I know him well, for he is one

[129-045]of the perilloust knights of the world; men say that he hath seven men’s

[129-046]strength, and from him I escaped once full hard with my life. Fair damosel,

[129-047]said the king, there be knights here would do their power for to rescue your

[129-048]lady, but because you will not tell her name, nor where she dwelleth, therefore

[129-049]none of my knights that here be now shall go with you by my will. Then must I

[129-050]speak further, said the damosel.
