[227-001][[sir-kay|Kay]] and Sir Sagramore le Desirous, and how [[sir-gawaine|Sir Gawaine]] turned [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]] from

[227-002][[morgan-le-fay|Morgan le Fay]].

[227-003]But anon as the noble knight, [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]], heard of the shield of Cornwall,

[227-004]then wist he well that it was Sir Tristram that fought with his enemies. And

[227-005]then Sir Launcelot praised Sir Tristram, and called him the man of most [[honour|worship]]

[227-006]in the world. So there was a knight in that priory that hight Pellinore, and he

[227-007]desired to wit the name of Sir Tristram, but in no wise he could not; and so

[227-008]Sir Tristram departed and left Sir Dinadan in the priory, for he was so weary

[227-009]and so sore bruised that he might not ride. Then this knight, Sir Pellinore,

[227-010]said to Sir Dinadan: Sithen that ye will not tell me that knight’s name I

[227-011]will ride after him and make him to tell me his name, or he shall die

[227-012]therefore. Beware, sir knight, said Sir Dinadan, for an ye follow him ye shall

[227-013]repent it. So that knight, Sir Pellinore, rode after Sir Tristram and required

[227-014]him of [[tournament|jousts]]. Then Sir Tristram smote him down and wounded him through the

[227-015]shoulder, and so he passed on his way. And on the next day following Sir

[227-016]Tristram met with pursuivants, and they told him that there was made a great

[227-017]cry of tournament between King Carados of Scotland and [[king-arthur|the King]] of North Wales,

[227-018]and either should joust against other at the Castle of Maidens; and these

[227-019]pursuivants sought all the country after the good knights, and in especial King

[227-020]Carados let make [[quest|seeking]] for Sir Launcelot du Lake, and the King of Northgalis

[227-021]let seek after Sir Tristram de Liones. And at that time Sir Tristram thought to

[227-022]be at that jousts; and so by adventure they met with Sir Kay, the Seneschal,

[227-023]and Sir Sagramore le Desirous; and Sir Kay required Sir Tristram to joust, and

[227-024]Sir Tristram in a manner refused him, because he would not be hurt nor bruised

[227-025]against the great jousts that should be before the Castle of Maidens, and

[227-026]therefore thought to repose him and to rest him. And alway Sir Kay cried: Sir

[227-027]knight of Cornwall, joust with me, or else yield thee to me as recreant. When

[227-028]Sir Tristram heard him say so he turned to him, and then Sir Kay refused him

[227-029]and turned his back. Then Sir Tristram said: As I find thee I shall take thee.

[227-030]Then Sir Kay turned with evil will, and Sir Tristram smote Sir Kay down, and so

[227-031]he rode forth.

[227-032]Then Sir Sagramore le Desirous rode after Sir Tristram, and made him to joust

[227-033]with him, and there Sir Tristram smote down Sir Sagramore le Desirous from his

[227-034]horse, and rode his way; and the same day he met with a damosel that told him

[227-035]that he should win great worship of a knight adventurous that did much harm in

[227-036]all that country. When Sir Tristram heard her say so, he was glad to go with

[227-037]her to win worship. So Sir Tristram rode with that damosel a six mile, and then

[227-038]met him Sir Gawaine, and therewithal Sir Gawaine knew the damosel, that she was

[227-039]a damosel of Queen Morgan le Fay. Then Sir Gawaine understood that she led that

[227-040]knight to some mischief. Fair knight, said Sir Gawaine, whither ride you now

[227-041]with that damosel? Sir, said Sir Tristram, I wot not whither I shall ride but

[227-042]as the damosel will lead me. Sir, said Sir Gawaine, ye shall not ride with her,

[227-043]for she and her lady did never good, but ill. And then Sir Gawaine pulled out

[227-044]his sword and said: Damosel, but if thou tell me anon for what cause thou

[227-045]leadest this knight with thee thou shalt die for it right anon: I know all your

[227-046]lady’s treason, and yours. [[mercy|Mercy]], Sir Gawaine, she said, and if ye will

[227-047]save my life I will tell you. Say on, said Sir Gawaine, and thou shalt have thy

[227-048]life. Sir, she said, Queen Morgan le Fay, my lady, hath ordained a thirty

[227-049]ladies to seek and espy after Sir Launcelot or Sir Tristram, and by the trains

[227-050]of these ladies, who that may first meet any of these two knights they should

[227-051]turn them unto Morgan le Fay’s castle, saying that they should do deeds

[227-052]of worship; and if any of the two knights came there, there be thirty knights

[227-053]lying and watching in a tower to wait upon Sir Launcelot or upon Sir Tristram.

[227-054]Fie for shame, said Sir Gawaine, that ever such false treason should be wrought

[227-055]or used in a queen, and a king’s sister, and a king and queen’s

[227-056]daughter.
