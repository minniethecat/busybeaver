[112-001]the mean of a damosel.

[112-002]Right so at the noon came the damosel unto him with his dinner, and asked him

[112-003]what cheer. Truly, fair damosel, said [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]], in my life days never so

[112-004]ill. Sir, she said, that me repenteth, but an ye will be ruled by me, I shall

[112-005]help you out of this distress, and ye shall have no shame nor villainy, so that

[112-006]ye hold me a promise. Fair damosel, I will grant you, and sore I am of these

[112-007]queen-sorceresses afeard, for they have destroyed many a good knight. Sir, said

[112-008]she, that is sooth, and for the [[honour|renown]] and bounty that they hear of you they

[112-009]would have your love, and Sir, they say, your name is Sir Launcelot du Lake,

[112-010]the flower of knights, and they be passing wroth with you that ye have refused

[112-011]them. But Sir, an ye would promise me to help my father on Tuesday next coming,

[112-012]that hath made a tournament betwixt him and [[king-arthur|the King]] of Northgalis—for

[112-013]the last Tuesday past my father lost the [[battle|field]] through three knights of

[112-014]Arthur’s court—an ye will be there on Tuesday next coming, and help

[112-015]my father, to-morn or prime, by the grace of God, I shall deliver you clean.

[112-016]Fair maiden, said Sir Launcelot, tell me what is your father’s name, and

[112-017]then shall I give you an answer. Sir knight, she said, my father is King

[112-018]Bagdemagus, that was foul rebuked at the last tournament. I know your father

[112-019]well, said Sir Launcelot, for a noble king and a good knight, and by the faith

[112-020]of my body, ye shall have my body ready to do your father and you service at

[112-021]that day. Sir, she said, [[mercy|gramercy]], and to-morn await ye be ready betimes and I

[112-022]shall be she that shall deliver you and take you your armour and your horse,

[112-023]shield and spear, and hereby within this ten mile, is an abbey of white monks,

[112-024]there I pray you that ye me abide, and thither shall I bring my father unto

[112-025]you. All this shall be done, said Sir Launcelot as I am true knight.

[112-026]And so she departed, and came on the morn early, and found him ready; then she

[112-027]brought him out of twelve locks, and brought him unto his armour, and when he

[112-028]was clean armed, she brought him until his own horse, and lightly he saddled

[112-029]him and took a great spear in his hand and so rode forth, and said, Fair

[112-030]damosel, I shall not fail you, by the grace of God. And so he rode into a great

[112-031]forest all that day, and never could find no highway and so the night fell on

[112-032]him, and then was he ware in a slade, of a pavilion of red sendal. By my faith,

[112-033]said Sir Launcelot, in that pavilion will I lodge all this night, and so there

[112-034]he alighted down, and tied his horse to the pavilion, and there he unarmed him,

[112-035]and there he found a bed, and laid him therein and fell asleep sadly.
