[201-001]arrived in Wales, and how he met there with Sir Lamorak.

[201-002]Now turn we unto [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]], that upon a day he took a little barget, and his

[201-003]wife Isoud la Blanche Mains, with Sir Kehydius her brother, to play them in the

[201-004]coasts. And when they were from the land, there was a wind drove them in to the

[201-005]coast of Wales upon this Isle of Servage, whereas was Sir Lamorak, and there

[201-006]the barget all to-rove; and there Dame Isoud was hurt; and as well as they

[201-007]might they gat into the forest, and there by a well he saw Segwarides and a

[201-008]damosel. And then either saluted other. Sir, said Segwarides, I know you for

[201-009]Sir Tristram de Liones, the man in the world that I have most cause to hate,

[201-010]because ye departed the love between me and my wife; but as for that, said Sir

[201-011]Segwarides, I will never hate a noble knight for a light lady; and therefore, I

[201-012]pray you, be my friend, and I will be yours unto my power; for wit ye well ye

[201-013]are hard bestead in this valley, and we shall have enough to do either of us to

[201-014]succour other. And then Sir Segwarides brought Sir Tristram to a lady thereby

[201-015]that was born in Cornwall, and she told him all the perils of that valley, and

[201-016]how there came never knight there but he were taken prisoner or slain. Wit you

[201-017]well, fair lady, said Sir Tristram, that I slew Sir Marhaus and delivered

[201-018]Cornwall from the truage of Ireland, and I am he that delivered [[king-arthur|the King]] of

[201-019]Ireland from Sir Blamore de Ganis, and I am he that beat Sir Palamides; and wit

[201-020]ye well I am Sir Tristram de Liones, that by the grace of God shall deliver

[201-021]this woful Isle of Servage. So Sir Tristram was well eased.

[201-022]Then one told him there was a knight of King Arthur’s that was wrecked on

[201-023]the rocks. What is his name? said Sir Tristram. We wot not, said the fishers,

[201-024]but he keepeth it no counsel but that he is a knight of King Arthur’s,

[201-025]and by the mighty lord of this isle he setteth nought. I pray you, said Sir

[201-026]Tristram, an ye may, bring him hither that I may see him, and if he be any of

[201-027]the knights of Arthur’s I shall know him. Then the lady prayed the

[201-028]fishers to bring him to her place. So on the morrow they brought him thither in

[201-029]a fisher’s raiment; and as soon as Sir Tristram saw him he smiled upon

[201-030]him and knew him well, but he knew not Sir Tristram. Fair sir, said Sir

[201-031]Tristram, meseemeth by your cheer ye have been diseased but late, and also

[201-032]methinketh I should know you heretofore. I will well, said Sir Lamorak, that ye

[201-033]have seen me and met with me. Fair sir, said Sir Tristram, tell me your name.

[201-034]Upon a covenant I will tell you, said Sir Lamorak, that is, that ye will tell

[201-035]me whether ye be lord of this island or no, that is called Nabon le Noire.

[201-036]Forsooth, said Sir Tristram, I am not he, nor I hold not of him; I am his foe

[201-037]as well as ye be, and so shall I be found or I depart out of this isle. Well,

[201-038]said Sir Lamorak, since ye have said so largely unto me, my name is Sir Lamorak

[201-039]de Galis, son unto King Pellinore. Forsooth, I trow well, said Sir Tristram,

[201-040]for an ye said other I know the contrary. What are ye, said Sir Lamorak, that

[201-041]knoweth me? I am Sir Tristram de Liones. Ah, sir, remember ye not of the fall

[201-042]ye did give me once, and after ye refused me to [[battle|fight]] on foot. That was not for

[201-043]fear I had of you, said Sir Tristram, but me shamed at that time to have more

[201-044]ado with you, for meseemed ye had enough; but, Sir Lamorak, for my kindness

[201-045]many ladies ye put to a reproof when ye sent the horn from [[morgan-le-fay|Morgan le Fay]] to

[201-046][[king-mark|King Mark]], whereas ye did this in despite of me. Well, said he, an it were to

[201-047]do again, so would I do, for I had liefer strife and debate fell in King

[201-048]Mark’s court rather than Arthur’s court, for the honour of both

[201-049]courts be not alike. As to that, said Sir Tristram, I know well; but that that

[201-050]was done it was for despite of me, but all your malice, I thank God, hurt not

[201-051]greatly. Therefore, said Sir Tristram, ye shall leave all your malice, and so

[201-052]will I, and let us assay how we may win [[honour|worship]] between you and me upon this

[201-053]giant Sir Nabon le Noire that is lord of this island, to destroy him. Sir, said

[201-054]Sir Lamorak, now I understand your knighthood, it may not be false that all men

[201-055]say, for of your bounty, noblesse, and worship, of all knights ye are peerless,

[201-056]and for your courtesy and gentleness I showed you ungentleness, and that now me

[201-057]repenteth.
