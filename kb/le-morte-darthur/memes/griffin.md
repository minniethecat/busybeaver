# Griffin

The **griffin** — the legendary beast with the body of a lion and the head and wings of an eagle — appears in Malory's *Le Morte d'[[king-arthur|Arthur]]* in two distinct registers: as a heraldic device on a [[knight]]'s [[shield]], and as a creature of apocalyptic [[dream]]. In both cases, the griffin signals something foreign — either a knight from beyond the familiar geography of [[Logres]], or a d[[wrath|anger]] that has crossed into the [[realm|land]] from outside.

## The Griffin in Heraldry

The sole heraldic appearance of the griffin occurs during [[King Arthur]]'s Roman campaign, when [[Sir Gawaine]] encounters an armed knight in Tuscany:

> The knight bare in his shield three griffins of gold, in sable carbuncle, the chief of silver. (104-021)

The [[heraldry|blazon]] is precise: three gold griffins on a sable (black) [[battle|field]] with a silver chief. This level of heraldic detail is unusual in Malory, who rarely describes shield devices at such length. The knight carrying these arms is a Tuscan, and the griffin — a beast associated in medieval bestiaries with strength and vigi[[spear|lance]] — marks him as a worthy opponent. [[sir-gawaine|Gawaine]] immediately challenges him, and the ensuing [[joust]] is hard-fought. The griffin here functions as a visual shorthand for foreign [[courage|prowess]]: a knight from beyond the mountains who bears a creature that exists nowhere in nature.

## The Griffin in Prophetic Dream

The griffin's other appearance is darker and more consequential. After [[King Arthur]] has lain with his sister [[Morgawse]] and begotten [[Mordred]] — though he does not yet know her identity — he [[dream|dreams]] a terrifying [[dream|vision]]:

> Him thought there was come into this land griffins and serpents, and him thought they burnt and slew all the people in the land, and then him thought he fought with them, and they did him passing great harm, and wounded him full sore, but at the last he slew them. (022-013)

The griffins are pa[[wrath|ire]]d with [[serpent]]s as invaders from outside the realm, burning and slaying. The dream is transparently prophetic: the beasts that [[king-arthur|Arthur]] fathers upon his own sister will one day destroy his [[realm|kingdom]]. [[king-arthur|Arthur]] [[battle|fight]]s them and prevails in the dream, but not before they have done "passing great harm" and wounded him "full sore" — a precise anticipation of the [[Battle of Camlann]], where [[king-arthur|Arthur]] kills [[mordred|Mordred]] but receives his death-wound in the act. In the dream-logic, the griffin is the shape that [[treason]] takes before it has a name.

## Heraldry and Nightmare

The griffin's double life in Malory — as a heraldic emblem of foreign knighthood and as a dream-creature of destruction — reflects a medieval ambivalence about hybrid beasts. The griffin is [[baron|noble]] enough to be borne on a shield, terrible enough to burn a country. It is fitting that the beast appears only twice: once on the eve of the [[Round Table]]'s founding ([[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s dream occurs early in his reign), and once during its furthest imperial reach (the Roman campaign). Between these two sightings, the griffin brackets the arc of [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s power — first as a [[battle|war]]ning, then as an ornament.
