# Wrath

**Wrath** in [[Le Morte d'Arthur]] is the most dangerous of knightly passions — a sudden, consuming [[anger]] that drives [[knights]] to acts of violence they would not commit in cold blood. Malory treats wrath not as a private emotion but as a narrative force: the turning point of battles, the cause of fatal misunderstandings, and the first step on the path to[[battle|war]]d [[treason]] and tragedy.

## Wrath as Combat Catalyst

The most frequent deployment of wrath in Malory is as the immediate trigger of [[battle|combat]]. When a knight is shamed, challenged, or th[[battle|war]]ted, wrath converts the slight into action:

> Wherefore they departed with wrath, and each party made ready for battle (012-007)

The syntax is revealing: "they departed with wrath" — wrath is not something they *feel* but something they *depart with*, a companion that rides with them from the hall to the [[battle|field]]. Wrath is a state that a knight enters, a condition that persists until it is discharged in violence.

> heaved up his sword, and in his wrath smote him (027-006)

The phrase "in his wrath" marks the moment when emotion becomes action. The sword is heaved up *in wrath* — the wrath is not a commentary on the act but its efficient cause. Malory does not moralise; he narrates the causal chain: slight → wrath → sword → death.

## Wrath and Mass Violence

When wrath is collective rather than individual, it produces the mass violence that threatens to destroy the chivalric order:

> Then in great wrath they departed with their horses, and encountered together (134-030)

The "great wrath" of two armed parties transforms a dispute into a battle. Neither side can withdraw without loss of [[worship]], so wrath locks both parties into escalation. This is the structural danger of wrath in a [[honour]] culture: the emotion is public, the response is mandatory, and the costs are borne by everyone.

## Wrath as Self-Knowledge

Malory's characters sometimes reflect on their wrath with startling clarity. The knight who has wreaked destruction in anger can look back and recognise the pattern:

> the more ye angered me, and my wrath I wreaked upon them that I had no quarrel with (138-054)

This is a moment of moral self-knowledge rare in the genre. The speaker acknowledges that his wrath was misdirected — "I wreaked upon them that I had no quarrel with." Wrath is blind; it strikes the nearest target, not the guilty one. The [[penance|confession]] is not quite [[repentance]] — no [[penance]] follows — but it is the recognition of a moral pattern that the narrative itself does not judge.

## The Restraint of Wrath

Against the destructive pattern of wrath, Malory also records the deliberate restraint of anger:

> they would not wrath them, if case be that they had power to choose (225-044)

To "not wrath them" — to refuse the emotion that would otherwise be expected — is presented as a deliberate moral choice. The knight who can choose not to be wrathful exercises a [[virtue]] higher than mere [[prowess]]. This restraint is rare in Malory, but its rarity is precisely what makes it significant: the knight who masters his wrath masters himself.

## Wrath's Narrative Function

Wrath in Malory is the hinge between the chivalric and the tragic. The same [[prowess]] that makes a knight glorious in battle makes him dangerous in anger. Wrath is [[courage|prowess]] without judgment — the sword drawn before the mind can intervene. The great tragedies of the [[king-arthur|Arthur]]ian world — the death of [[Sir Gareth]] at [[Sir Launcelot]]'s hand, the final battle at [[Salisbury Plain]] — are stories about wrath that could not be recalled in time.
