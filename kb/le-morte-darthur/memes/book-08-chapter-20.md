[183-001]summoned to come to [[king-arthur|King Arthur]]’s court for treason.

[183-002]Then it fell that Sir Bleoberis and Sir Blamore de Ganis, that were brethren,

[183-003]they had summoned the King Anguish of Ireland for to come to Arthur’s

[183-004]court upon pain of forfeiture of King Arthur’s good grace. And if the

[183-005]King of Ireland came not in, at the day assigned and set, the king should lose

[183-006]his lands. So it happened that at the day assigned, King Arthur neither Sir

[183-007][[sir-launcelot|Launcelot]] might not be there for to give the judgment, for King Arthur was with

[183-008]Sir Launcelot at the Castle Joyous Garde. And so King Arthur assigned King

[183-009]Carados and the King of Scots to be there that day as judges. So when the kings

[183-010]were at [[camelot|Camelot]] King Anguish of Ireland was come to know his accusers. Then was

[183-011]there Sir Blamore de Ganis, and appealed the King of Ireland of treason, that

[183-012]he had slain a cousin of his in his court in Ireland by treason. The king was

[183-013]sore abashed of his accusation, for-why he was come at the summons of King

[183-014]Arthur, and or he came at Camelot he wist not wherefore he was sent after. And

[183-015]when the king heard Sir Blamore say his will, he understood well there was none

[183-016]other remedy but for to answer him knightly; for the custom was such in those

[183-017]days, that an any man were appealed of any treason or murder he should [[battle|fight]]

[183-018]body for body, or else to find another knight for him. And all manner of

[183-019]murders in those days were called treason.

[183-020]So when King Anguish understood his accusing he was passing heavy, for he knew

[183-021]Sir Blamore de Ganis that he was a noble knight, and of noble knights come.

[183-022]Then the King of Ireland was simply purveyed of his answer; therefore the

[183-023]judges gave him respite by the third day to give his answer. So the king

[183-024]departed unto his lodging. The meanwhile there came a lady by Sir

[183-025][[sir-tristram|Tristram]]’s pavilion making great dole. What aileth you, said Sir

[183-026]Tristram, that ye make such dole? Ah, fair knight, said the lady, I am ashamed

[183-027]unless that some good knight help me; for a great lady of [[honour|worship]] sent by me a

[183-028]fair child and a rich, unto Sir Launcelot du Lake, and hereby there met with me

[183-029]a knight, and threw me down from my palfrey, and took away the child from me.

[183-030]Well, my lady, said Sir Tristram, and for my lord Sir Launcelot’s sake I

[183-031]shall get you that child again, or else I shall be beaten for it. And so Sir

[183-032]Tristram took his horse, and asked the lady which way the knight rode; and then

[183-033]she told him. And he rode after him, and within a while he overtook that

[183-034]knight. And then Sir Tristram bade him turn and give again the child.
