[116-001]fought together.

[116-002]And then they put their spears in the rests, and came together with their

[116-003]horses as fast as they might run, and either smote other in midst of their

[116-004]shields, that both their horses’ backs brast under them, and the knights

[116-005]were both stonied. And as soon as they might avoid their horses, they took

[116-006]their shields afore them, and drew out their swords, and came together eagerly,

[116-007]and either gave other many strong strokes, for there might neither shields nor

[116-008]harness hold their strokes. And so within a while they had both grimly wounds,

[116-009]and bled passing grievously. Thus they fared two hours or more trasing and

[116-010]rasing either other, where they might hit any bare place.

[116-011]Then at the last they were breathless both, and stood leaning on their swords.

[116-012]Now fellow, said Sir Turquine, hold thy hand a while, and tell me what I shall

[116-013]ask thee. Say on. Then Turquine said, Thou art the biggest man that ever I met

[116-014]withal, and the best breathed, and like one knight that I hate above all other

[116-015]knights; so be it that thou be not he I will lightly accord with thee, and for

[116-016]thy love I will deliver all the prisoners that I have, that is three score and

[116-017]four, so thou wilt tell me thy name. And thou and I we will be fellows

[116-018]together, and never to fail thee while that I live. It is well said, said Sir

[116-019][[sir-launcelot|Launcelot]], but sithen it is so that I may have thy friendship, what knight is

[116-020]he that thou so hatest above all other? Faithfully, said Sir Turquine, his name

[116-021]is Sir Launcelot du Lake, for he slew my brother, Sir Carados, at the dolorous

[116-022]tower, that was one of the best knights alive; and therefore him I except of

[116-023]all knights, for may I once meet with him, the one of us shall make an end of

[116-024]other, I make mine avow. And for Sir Launcelot’s sake I have slain an

[116-025]hundred good knights, and as many I have maimed all utterly that they might

[116-026]never after help themselves, and many have died in prison, and yet have I three

[116-027]score and four, and all shall be delivered so thou wilt tell me thy name, so be

[116-028]it that thou be not Sir Launcelot.

[116-029]Now, see I well, said Sir Launcelot, that such a man I might be, I might have

[116-030]peace, and such a man I might be, that there should be [[battle|war]] mortal betwixt us.

[116-031]And now, sir knight, at thy request I will that thou wit and know that I am

[116-032]Launcelot du Lake, King Ban’s son of Benwick, and very knight of the

[116-033][[round-table|Table Round]]. And now I defy thee, and do thy best. Ah, said Turquine,

[116-034]Launcelot, thou art unto me most welcome that ever was knight, for we shall

[116-035]never depart till the one of us be dead. Then they hurtled together as two wild

[116-036]bulls rushing and lashing with their shields and swords, that sometime they

[116-037]fell both over their noses. Thus they fought still two hours and more, and

[116-038]never would have rest, and Sir Turquine gave Sir Launcelot many wounds that all

[116-039]the ground thereas they fought was all bespeckled with blood.
