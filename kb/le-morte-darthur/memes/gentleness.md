**Gentleness** in Malory's [[Le Morte d'Arthur]] is a quality of character that combines [[baron|noble]] [[lineage|birth]] with [[baron|noble]] conduct—the inner disposition from which out[[battle|war]]d [[courtesy]] and [[honour]] spring. It is the word Malory reaches for when he wishes to praise not a knight's strength but his soul, not his victories but his [[worship|worthiness]]. In the chivalric catalogue of the preface, gentleness stands as the summary virtue, the quality that comprehends all others.

## Gentleness in the Moral Vocabulary

Caxton's preface places gentleness at the heart of the chivalric ideal:

> herein may be seen noble chivalry, courtesy, humanity, friendliness, hardiness, love, courtesy, and very gentleness, with many wonderful histories and adventures (002-100)

The phrase "very gentleness"—true gentleness—suggests a quality that admits of counterfeits. A knight may perform courtesy without possessing gentleness, but the performance will eventually ring hollow. Gentleness is the substance beneath the form.

## Gentleness and Birth

In its medieval sense, "gentleness" carries the meaning of [[baron|noble]] [[lineage|birth]]—the quality of being *gentil*, of belonging to the gentry. Malory exploits this doubleness: the gentle knight is both well-born and well-natured. When a knight of unknown origin displays gentleness, it functions as proof of [[baron|noble]] [[blood|lineage]] yet to be revealed—as in the case of [[Sir Gareth|Gareth]], whose gentleness of conduct while disguised as a kitchen boy confirms his royal blood:

> neither of bounty, courtesy, nor gentleness (141-035)

The absence of gentleness in a character who *should* possess it by [[lineage|birth]] becomes a mark of moral failure—a gentility that has degenerated into mere rank.

## Gentleness and Courtesy

Gentleness and [[courtesy]] are pa[[wrath|ire]]d so consistently in Malory that they form a virtual compound:

> of his great gentleness and courtesy (129-020)

If courtesy is the out[[battle|war]]d expression, gentleness is the inner condition. The knight who possesses gentleness will naturally be courteous, merciful, and generous. The knight who lacks gentleness may simulate these qualities but will fail when tested—the simulation cannot survive the pressures of [[battle|combat]], [[love]], or [[faith]].

## Gentleness in Action

Gentleness manifests in the knight's treatment of the weak, the vanquished, and the unknown. To receive a str[[wrath|anger]] with gentleness, to spare a defeated opponent, to speak gently to a [[damosel]] in distress—these are not strategic choices but the spontaneous expressions of a gentle nature:

> And he of his gentleness received them well (093-019)

The word "received" here is key: gentleness governs the most basic social act, the welcoming of another. A knight whose first impulse is gentleness will navigate the moral [[realm|land]]scape of the romance without needing to consult a code, because the code has become his nature.
