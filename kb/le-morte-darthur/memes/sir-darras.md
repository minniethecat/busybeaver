# Sir Darras

Sir Darras was an old knight and the lord of a strong castle. He appears late in Sir Tristram's adventures when he imprisoned Tristram and his two companions after learning that three of his sons had been slain by knights bearing their arms. Upon discovering that Sir Tristram was among his prisoners, Sir Darras ultimately released them, showing mercy where others might have sought vengeance.

## The Imprisonment of the Three Knights

Sir Tristram, Sir Palomides, and Sir Dinadan came to repose themselves at a strong castle with the old knight Sir Darras (238-027). The porter went unto Sir Darras and told him who had arrived. Presently there came one who told Sir Darras that three of his sons had been slain at a tournament, and that the same knight who bore the black shield was among the guests (239-035, 239-038).

Without delay, Sir Darras went unto Sir Tristram's chamber and there found the black shield (239-039). Without any tarrying, Sir Darras put Sir Tristram, Sir Palomides, and Sir Dinadan into a deep prison (239-042).

## The Kin's Demand for Vengeance

There came forty knights to Sir Darras that were of his own kin, and they would have slain Sir Tristram and his two fellows outright, but Sir Darras would not suffer it (239-048, 239-049). He held back the vengeance of his kinsmen, choosing to keep the knights imprisoned rather than put them to death.

## The Release of Sir Tristram

Some time thereafter, a damosel of Sir Darras's household learned that Sir Tristram lay in the prison, wounded and sorrowful (242-037, 242-038). When she brought word to Sir Darras of the identity of the wounded knight — that it was indeed Sir Tristram de Liones — Sir Darras declared that God defended when knights came to succour and those that were hurt should not die (242-039).

Sir Darras commanded the damosel to fetch that knight before him, and when Sir Tristram was brought forth, Sir Darras saw the noble knight and considered that all that had been done was by force of arms and not by treason (242-041, 242-055). He released Sir Tristram, Sir Palomides, and Sir Dinadan. Then were they glad all three, and they took their leave of Sir Darras, who had shown them great mercy (242-033).
