[176-001]hurted each other for the love of a knight’s wife.

[176-002]So [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]] departed, and took the sea, and with good wind he arrived up at

[176-003]Tintagil in Cornwall; and when [[king-mark|King Mark]] was whole in his prosperity there came

[176-004]tidings that Sir Tristram was arrived, and whole of his wounds: thereof was

[176-005]King Mark passing glad, and so were all the barons; and when he saw his time he

[176-006]rode unto his father, King Meliodas, and there he had all the cheer that the

[176-007]king and the queen could make him. And then largely King Meliodas and his queen

[176-008]departed of their lands and goods to Sir Tristram.

[176-009]Then by the license of King Meliodas, his father, he returned again unto the

[176-010]court of King Mark, and there he lived in great joy long time, until at the

[176-011]last there befell a jealousy and an unkindness betwixt King Mark and Sir

[176-012]Tristram, for they loved both one lady. And she was an earl’s wife that

[176-013]hight Sir Segwarides. And this lady loved Sir Tristram passingly well. And he

[176-014]loved her again, for she was a passing fair lady, and that espied Sir Tristram

[176-015]well. Then King Mark understood that and was jealous, for King Mark loved her

[176-016]passingly well.

[176-017]So it fell upon a day this lady sent a dwarf unto Sir Tristram, and bade him,

[176-018]as he loved her, that he would be with her the night next following. Also she

[176-019]charged you that ye come not to her but if ye be well armed, for her lover was

[176-020]called a good knight. Sir Tristram answered to the dwarf: Recommend me unto my

[176-021]lady, and tell her I will not fail but I will be with her the term that she

[176-022]hath set me. And with this answer the dwarf departed. And King Mark espied that

[176-023]the dwarf was with Sir Tristram upon message from Segwarides’ wife; then

[176-024]King Mark sent for the dwarf, and when he was come he made the dwarf by force

[176-025]to tell him all, why and wherefore that he came on message from Sir Tristram.

[176-026]Now, said King Mark, go where thou wilt, and upon pain of death that thou say

[176-027]no word that thou spakest with me; so the dwarf departed from the king.

[176-028]And that same night that the steven was set betwixt Segwarides’ wife and

[176-029]Sir Tristram, King Mark armed him, and made him ready, and took two knights of

[176-030]his counsel with him; and so he rode afore for to abide by the way for to wait

[176-031]upon Sir Tristram. And as Sir Tristram came riding upon his way with his spear

[176-032]in his hand, King Mark came hurtling upon him with his two knights suddenly.

[176-033]And all three smote him with their spears, and King Mark hurt Sir Tristram on

[176-034]the breast right sore. And then Sir Tristram feutred his spear, and smote his

[176-035]uncle, King Mark, so sore, that he rashed him to the earth, and bruised him

[176-036]that he lay still in a swoon, and long it was or ever he might wield himself.

[176-037]And then he ran to the one knight, and eft to the other, and smote them to the

[176-038]cold earth, that they lay still. And therewithal Sir Tristram rode forth sore

[176-039]wounded to the lady, and found her abiding him at a postern.
