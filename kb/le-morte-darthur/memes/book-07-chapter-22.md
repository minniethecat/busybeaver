[149-005]And then she let fetch to-fore him Linet, the damosel that had ridden with him

[149-006]many wildsome ways. Then was Sir Gareth more gladder than he was to-fore. And

[149-007]then they troth-plight each other to love, and never to fail whiles their life

[149-008]lasteth. And so they burnt both in love, that they were accorded to abate their

[149-009]lusts secretly. And there Dame Lionesse counselled Sir Gareth to sleep in none

[149-010]other place but in the hall. And there she promised him to come to his bed a

[149-011]little afore midnight.

[149-012]This counsel was not so privily kept but it was understood; for they were but

[149-013]young both, and tender of age, and had not used none such crafts to-fore.

[149-014]Wherefore the damosel Linet was a little displeased, and she thought her sister

[149-015]Dame Lionesse was a little over-hasty, that she might not abide the time of her

[149-016]marriage; and for saving their [[honour|worship]], she thought to abate their hot lusts.

[149-017]And so she let ordain by her subtle crafts that they had not their intents

[149-018]neither with other, as in their delights, until they were married. And so it

[149-019]passed on. At-after supper was made clean avoidance, that every lord and lady

[149-020]should go unto his rest. But Sir Gareth said plainly he would go no farther

[149-021]than the hall, for in such places, he said, was convenient for an errant-knight

[149-022]to take his rest in; and so there were ordained great couches, and thereon

[149-023]feather beds, and there laid him down to sleep; and within a while came Dame

[149-024]Lionesse, wrapped in a mantle furred with ermine, and laid her down beside Sir

[149-025]Gareth. And therewithal he began to kiss her. And then he looked afore him, and

[149-026]there he apperceived and saw come an armed knight, with many lights about him;

[149-027]and this knight had a long gisarm in his hand, and made grim countenance to

[149-028]smite him. When Sir Gareth saw him come in that wise, he leapt out of his bed,

[149-029]and gat in his hand his sword, and leapt straight toward that knight. And when

[149-030]the knight saw Sir Gareth come so fiercely upon him, he smote him with a foin

[149-031]through the thick of the thigh that the wound was a shaftmon broad and had cut

[149-032]a-two many veins and sinews. And therewithal Sir Gareth smote him upon the helm

[149-033]such a buffet that he fell grovelling; and then he leapt over him and unlaced

[149-034]his helm, and smote off his head from the body. And then he bled so fast that

[149-035]he might not stand, but so he laid him down upon his bed, and there he swooned

[149-036]and lay as he had been dead.

[149-037]Then Dame Lionesse cried aloud, that her brother Sir Gringamore heard, and came

[149-038]down. And when he saw Sir Gareth so shamefully wounded he was sore displeased,

[149-039]and said: I am shamed that this noble knight is thus honoured. Sir, said Sir

[149-040]Gringamore, how may this be, that ye be here, and this noble knight wounded?

[149-041]Brother, she said, I can not tell you, for it was not done by me, nor by mine

[149-042]assent. For he is my lord and I am his, and he must be mine husband; therefore,

[149-043]my brother, I will that ye wit I shame me not to be with him, nor to do him all

[149-044]the pleasure that I can. Sister, said Sir Gringamore, and I will that ye wit

[149-045]it, and Sir Gareth both, that it was never done by me, nor by my assent that

[149-046]this unhappy deed was done. And there they staunched his bleeding as well as

[149-047]they might, and great sorrow made Sir Gringamore and Dame Lionesse.

[149-048]And forthwithal came Dame Linet, and took up the head in the sight of them all,

[149-049]and anointed it with an ointment thereas it was smitten off; and in the same

[149-050]wise she did to the other part thereas the head stuck, and then she set it

[149-051]together, and it stuck as fast as ever it did. And the knight arose lightly up,

[149-052]and the damosel Linet put him in her chamber. All this saw Sir Gringamore and

[149-053]Dame Lionesse, and so did Sir Gareth; and well he espied that it was the

[149-054]damosel Linet, that rode with him through the perilous passages. Ah well,

[149-055]damosel, said Sir Gareth, I weened ye would not have done as ye have done. My

[149-056]lord Gareth, said Linet, all that I have done I will avow, and all that I have

[149-057]done shall be for your honour and worship, and to us all. And so within a while

[149-058]Sir Gareth was nigh whole, and waxed light and jocund, and sang, danced, and

[149-059]gamed; and he and Dame Lionesse were so hot in burning love that they made

[149-060]their covenant at the tenth night after, that she should come to his bed. And

[149-061]because he was wounded afore, he laid his armour and his sword nigh his

[149-062]bed’s side.
