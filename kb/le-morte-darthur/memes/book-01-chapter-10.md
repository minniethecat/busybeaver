[013-001]send for King Ban and King [[sir-bors|Bors]], and of their counsel taken for the [[battle|war]].

[013-002]So after the feast and journey, [[king-arthur|King Arthur]] drew him unto London, and so by the

[013-003]counsel of [[merlin|Merlin]], the king let call his barons to council, for Merlin had told

[013-004]the king that the six kings that made war upon him would in all haste be awroke

[013-005]on him and on his lands. Wherefore the king asked counsel at them all. They

[013-006]could no counsel give, but said they were big enough. Ye say well, said Arthur;

[013-007]I thank you for your good courage, but will ye all that loveth me speak with

[013-008]Merlin? ye know well that he hath done much for me, and he knoweth many things,

[013-009]and when he is afore you, I would that ye prayed him heartily of his best

[013-010]advice. All the barons said they would pray him and desire him. So Merlin was

[013-011]sent for, and fair desired of all the barons to give them best counsel. I shall

[013-012]say you, said Merlin, I warn you all, your enemies are passing strong for you,

[013-013]and they are good men of arms as be alive, and by this time they have gotten to

[013-014]them four kings more, and a mighty duke; and unless that our king have more

[013-015]chivalry with him than he may make within the bounds of his own realm, an he

[013-016]fight with them in battle, he shall be overcome and slain. What were best to do

[013-017]in this cause? said all the barons. I shall tell you, said Merlin, mine advice;

[013-018]there are two brethren beyond the sea, and they be kings both, and [[marvel|marvellous]]

[013-019]good men of their hands; and that one hight King Ban of Benwick, and that other

[013-020]hight King Bors of Gaul, that is France. And on these two kings warreth a

[013-021]mighty man of men, the King Claudas, and striveth with them for a castle, and

[013-022]great war is betwixt them. But this Claudas is so mighty of goods whereof he

[013-023]getteth good knights, that he putteth these two kings most part to the worse;

[013-024]wherefore this is my counsel, that our king and sovereign lord send unto the

[013-025]kings Ban and Bors by two trusty knights with letters well devised, that an

[013-026]they will come and see King Arthur and his court, and so help him in his wars,

[013-027]that he will be sworn unto them to help them in their wars against King

[013-028]Claudas. Now, what say ye unto this counsel? said Merlin. This is well

[013-029]counselled, said the king and all the barons.

[013-030]Right so in all haste there were ordained to go two knights on the message unto

[013-031]the two kings. So were there made letters in the pleasant wise according unto

[013-032]King Arthur’s desire. Ulfius and Brastias were made the messengers, and

[013-033]so rode forth well horsed and well armed and as the guise was that time, and so

[013-034]passed the sea and rode toward the city of Benwick. And there besides were

[013-035]eight knights that espied them, and at a strait passage they met with Ulfius

[013-036]and Brastias, and would have taken them prisoners; so they prayed them that

[013-037]they might pass, for they were messengers unto King Ban and Bors sent from King

[013-038]Arthur. Therefore, said the eight knights, ye shall die or be prisoners, for we

[013-039]be knights of King Claudas. And therewith two of them dressed their spears, and

[013-040]Ulfius and Brastias dressed their spears, and ran together with great raundon.

[013-041]And Claudas’ knights brake their spears, and theirs to-held and bare the

[013-042]two knights out of their saddles to the earth, and so left them lying, and rode

[013-043]their ways. And the other six knights rode afore to a passage to meet with them

[013-044]again, and so Ulfius and Brastias smote other two down, and so passed on their

[013-045]ways. And at the fourth passage there met two for two, and both were laid unto

[013-046]the earth; so there was none of the eight knights but he was sore hurt or

[013-047]bruised. And when they come to Benwick it fortuned there were both kings, Ban

[013-048]and Bors.

[013-049]And when it was told the kings that there were come messengers, there were sent

[013-050]unto them two knights of [[honour|worship]], the one hight Lionses, lord of the country of

[013-051]Payarne, and Sir Phariance a worshipful knight. Anon they asked from whence

[013-052]they came, and they said from King Arthur, king of England; so they took them

[013-053]in their arms and made great joy each of other. But anon, as the two kings wist

[013-054]they were messengers of Arthur’s, there was made no tarrying, but

[013-055]forthwith they spake with the knights, and welcomed them in the faithfullest

[013-056]wise, and said they were most welcome unto them before all the kings living;

[013-057]and therewith they kissed the letters and delivered them. And when Ban and Bors

[013-058]understood the letters, then they were more welcome than they were before. And

[013-059]after the haste of the letters they gave them this answer, that they would

[013-060]fulfil the desire of King Arthur’s writing, and Ulfius and Brastias,

[013-061]tarry there as long as they would, they should have such cheer as might be made

[013-062]them in those marches. Then Ulfius and Brastias told the kings of the adventure

[013-063]at their passages of the eight knights. Ha! ah! said Ban and Bors, they were my

[013-064]good friends. I would I had wist of them; they should not have escaped so. So

[013-065]Ulfius and Brastias had good cheer and great gifts, as much as they might bear

[013-066]away; and had their answer by mouth and by writing, that those two kings would

[013-067]come unto Arthur in all the haste that they might. So the two knights rode on

[013-068]afore, and passed the sea, and came to their lord, and told him how they had

[013-069]sped, whereof King Arthur was passing glad. At what time suppose ye the two

[013-070]kings will be here? Sir, said they, afore All Hallowmass. Then the king let

[013-071]purvey for a great feast, and let cry a great [[tournament|jousts]]. And by All Hallowmass the

[013-072]two kings were come over the sea with three hundred knights well arrayed both

[013-073]for the peace and for the war. And King Arthur met with them ten mile out of

[013-074]London, and there was great joy as could be thought or made. And on All

[013-075]Hallowmass at the great feast, sat in the hall the three kings, and [[sir-kay|Sir Kay]]

[013-076]seneschal served in the hall, and Sir Lucas the butler, that was Duke

[013-077]Corneus’ son, and Sir Griflet, that was the son of Cardol, these three

[013-078]knights had the rule of all the service that served the kings. And anon, as

[013-079]they had washen and risen, all knights that would joust made them ready; by

[013-080]then they were ready on horseback there were seven hundred knights. And Arthur,

[013-081]Ban, and Bors, with the Archbishop of Canterbury, and Sir Ector, Kay’s

[013-082]father, they were in a place covered with cloth of gold like an hall, with

[013-083]ladies and gentlewomen, for to behold who did best, and thereon to give

[013-084]judgment.
