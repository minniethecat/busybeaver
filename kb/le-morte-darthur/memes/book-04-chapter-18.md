[084-001][[sir-gawaine|Gawaine]] and Sir Uwaine, and overthrew them both.

[084-002]And then Sir Marhaus rode unto his shield, and saw how it was defouled, and

[084-003]said, Of this despite I am a part avenged, but for her love that gave me this

[084-004]white shield I shall wear thee, and hang mine where thou wast; and so he hanged

[084-005]it about his neck. Then he rode straight unto Sir Gawaine and to Sir Uwaine,

[084-006]and asked them what they did there? They answered him that they came from King

[084-007][[king-arthur|Arthur]]’s court to see adventures. Well, said Sir Marhaus, here am I

[084-008]ready, an adventurous knight that will fulfil any adventure that ye will

[084-009]desire; and so departed from them, to fetch his range. Let him go, said Sir

[084-010]Uwaine unto Sir Gawaine, for he is a passing good knight as any is living; I

[084-011]would not by my will that any of us were matched with him. Nay, said Sir

[084-012]Gawaine, not so, it were shame to us were he not assayed, were he never so good

[084-013]a knight. Well, said Sir Uwaine, I will assay him afore you, for I am more

[084-014]weaker than ye, and if he smite me down then may ye revenge me. So these two

[084-015]knights came together with great raundon, that Sir Uwaine smote Sir Marhaus

[084-016]that his spear brast in pieces on the shield, and Sir Marhaus smote him so sore

[084-017]that horse and man he bare to the earth, and hurt Sir Uwaine on the left side.

[084-018]Then Sir Marhaus turned his horse and rode toward Gawaine with his spear, and

[084-019]when Sir Gawaine saw that he dressed his shield, and they aventred their

[084-020]spears, and they came together with all the might of their horses, that either

[084-021]knight smote other so hard in midst of their shields, but Sir Gawaine’s

[084-022]spear brake, but Sir Marhaus’ spear held; and therewith Sir Gawaine and

[084-023]his horse rushed down to the earth. And lightly Sir Gawaine rose on his feet,

[084-024]and pulled out his sword, and dressed him toward Sir Marhaus on foot, and Sir

[084-025]Marhaus saw that, and pulled out his sword and began to come to Sir Gawaine on

[084-026]horseback. Sir knight, said Sir Gawaine, alight on foot, or else I will slay

[084-027]thy horse. Gramercy, said Sir Marhaus, of your gentleness ye teach me courtesy,

[084-028]for it is not for one knight to be on foot, and the other on horseback. And

[084-029]therewith Sir Marhaus set his spear against a tree and alighted and tied his

[084-030]horse to a tree, and dressed his shield, and either came unto other eagerly,

[084-031]and smote together with their swords that their shields flew in cantels, and

[084-032]they bruised their helms and their hauberks, and wounded either other. But Sir

[084-033]Gawaine from it passed nine of the clock waxed ever stronger and stronger, for

[084-034]then it came to the hour of noon, and thrice his might was increased. All this

[084-035]espied Sir Marhaus and had great [[marvel|wonder]] how his might increased, and so they

[084-036]wounded other passing sore. And then when it was past noon, and when it drew

[084-037]toward evensong, Sir Gawaine’s strength feebled, and waxed passing faint

[084-038]that unnethes he might dure any longer, and Sir Marhaus was then bigger and

[084-039]bigger. Sir knight, said Sir Marhaus, I have well felt that ye are a passing

[084-040]good knight and a marvellous man of might as ever I felt any, while it lasteth,

[084-041]and our quarrels are not great, and therefore it were [[mercy|pity]] to do you hurt, for

[084-042]I feel ye are passing feeble. Ah, said Sir Gawaine, gentle knight, ye say the

[084-043]word that I should say. And therewith they took off their helms, and either

[084-044]kissed other, and there they swore together either to love other as brethren.

[084-045]And Sir Marhaus prayed Sir Gawaine to lodge with him that night. And so they

[084-046]took their horses, and rode toward Sir Marhaus’ house. And as they rode

[084-047]by the way, Sir knight, said Sir Gawaine, I have marvel that so valiant a man

[084-048]as ye be love no ladies nor damosels. Sir, said Sir Marhaus, they name me

[084-049]wrongfully those that give me that name, but well I wot it be the damosels of

[084-050]the turret that so name me, and other such as they be. Now shall I tell you for

[084-051]what cause I hate them: for they be sorceresses and enchanters many of them,

[084-052]and be a knight never so good of his body and full of [[courage|prowess]] as man may be,

[084-053]they will make him a stark coward to have the better of him, and this is the

[084-054]principal cause that I hate them; and to all good ladies and gentlewomen I owe

[084-055]my service as a knight ought to do.

[084-056]As the book rehearseth in French, there were many knights that overmatched Sir

[084-057]Gawaine, for all the thrice might that he had: [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]] de Lake, Sir

[084-058][[sir-tristram|Tristram]], [[sir-bors|Sir Bors de Ganis]], [[sir-percivale|Sir Percivale]], Sir Pelleas, and Sir Marhaus, these

[084-059]six knights had the better of Sir Gawaine. Then within a little while they came

[084-060]to Sir Marhaus’ place, which was in a little priory, and there they

[084-061]alighted, and ladies and damosels unarmed them, and hastily looked to their

[084-062]hurts, for they were all three hurt. And so they had all three good lodging

[084-063]with Sir Marhaus, and good cheer; for when he wist that they were King

[084-064]Arthur’s sister’s sons he made them all the cheer that lay in his

[084-065]power, and so they sojourned there a sennight, and were well eased of their

[084-066]wounds, and at the last departed. Now, said Sir Marhaus, we will not depart so

[084-067]lightly, for I will bring you through the forest; and rode day by day well a

[084-068]seven days or they found any adventure. At the last they came into a great

[084-069]forest, that was named the country and forest of Arroy, and the country of

[084-070]strange adventures. In this country, said Sir Marhaus, came never knight since

[084-071]it was christened but he found strange adventures; and so they rode, and came

[084-072]into a deep valley full of stones, and thereby they saw a fair stream of water;

[084-073]above thereby was the head of the stream a fair fountain, and three damosels

[084-074]sitting thereby. And then they rode to them, and either saluted other, and the

[084-075]eldest had a garland of gold about her head, and she was three score winter of

[084-076]age or more, and her hair was white under the garland. The second damosel was

[084-077]of thirty winter of age, with a circlet of gold about her head. The third

[084-078]damosel was but fifteen year of age, and a garland of flowers about her head.

[084-079]When these knights had so beheld them, they asked them the cause why they sat

[084-080]at that fountain? We be here, said the damosels, for this cause: if we may see

[084-081]any errant knights, to teach them unto strange adventures; and ye be three

[084-082]knights that seek adventures, and we be three damosels, and therefore each one

[084-083]of you must choose one of us; and when ye have done so we will lead you unto

[084-084]three highways, and there each of you shall choose a way and his damosel with

[084-085]him. And this day twelvemonth ye must meet here again, and God send you your

[084-086]lives, and thereto ye must plight your troth. This is well said, said Sir

[084-087]Marhaus.
