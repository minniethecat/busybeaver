[138-001]rebukes of the damosel, and he suffered it patiently.

[138-002]Damosel, said [[sir-gareth|Beaumains]], ye are uncourteous so to rebuke me as ye do, for

[138-003]meseemeth I have done you good service, and ever ye threaten me I shall be

[138-004]beaten with knights that we meet, but ever for all your boast they lie in the

[138-005]dust or in the mire, and therefore I pray you rebuke me no more; and when ye

[138-006]see me beaten or yielden as recreant, then may ye bid me go from you

[138-007]shamefully; but first I let you wit I will not depart from you, for I were

[138-008]worse than a fool an I would depart from you all the while that I win [[honour|worship]].

[138-009]Well, said she, right soon there shall meet a knight shall pay thee all thy

[138-010]wages, for he is the most man of worship of the world, except [[king-arthur|King Arthur]]. I

[138-011]will well, said Beaumains, the more he is of worship, the more shall be my

[138-012]worship to have ado with him.

[138-013]Then anon they were ware where was afore them a city rich and fair. And betwixt

[138-014]them and the city a mile and an half there was a fair meadow that seemed new

[138-015]mown, and therein were many pavilions fair to behold. Lo, said the damosel,

[138-016]yonder is a lord that owneth yonder city, and his custom is, when the weather

[138-017]is fair, to lie in this meadow to joust and [[tournament|tourney]]. And ever there be about

[138-018]him five hundred knights and gentlemen of arms, and there be all manner of

[138-019]games that any gentleman can devise. That goodly lord, said Beaumains, would I

[138-020]fain see. Thou shalt see him time enough, said the damosel, and so as she rode

[138-021]near she espied the pavilion where he was. Lo, said she, seest thou yonder

[138-022]pavilion that is all of the colour of Inde, and all manner of thing that there

[138-023]is about, men and women, and horses trapped, shields and spears were all of the

[138-024]colour of Inde, and his name is Sir Persant of Inde, the most lordliest knight

[138-025]that ever thou lookedst on. It may well be, said Beaumains, but be he never so

[138-026]stout a knight, in this [[battle|field]] I shall abide till that I see him under his

[138-027]shield. Ah, fool, said she, thou wert better flee betimes. Why, said Beaumains,

[138-028]an he be such a knight as ye make him, he will not set upon me with all his

[138-029]men, or with his five hundred knights. For an there come no more but one at

[138-030]once, I shall him not fail whilst my life lasteth. Fie, fie, said the damosel,

[138-031]that ever such a stinking knave should blow such a boast. Damosel, he said, ye

[138-032]are to blame so to rebuke me, for I had liefer do five battles than so to be

[138-033]rebuked, let him come and then let him do his worst.

[138-034]Sir, she said, I marvel what thou art and of what kin thou art come; boldly

[138-035]thou speakest, and boldly thou hast done, that have I seen; therefore I pray

[138-036]thee save thyself an thou mayest, for thy horse and thou have had great

[138-037]travail, and I dread we dwell over long from the siege, for it is but hence

[138-038]seven mile, and all perilous passages we are passed save all only this passage;

[138-039]and here I dread me sore lest ye shall catch some hurt, therefore I would ye

[138-040]were hence, that ye were not bruised nor hurt with this strong knight. But I

[138-041]let you wit that Sir Persant of Inde is nothing of might nor strength unto the

[138-042]knight that laid the siege about my lady. As for that, said Sir Beaumains, be

[138-043]it as it be may. For sithen I am come so nigh this knight I will prove his

[138-044]might or I depart from him, and else I shall be shamed an I now withdraw me

[138-045]from him. And therefore, damosel, have ye no doubt by the grace of God I shall

[138-046]so deal with this knight that within two hours after noon I shall deliver him.

[138-047]And then shall we come to the siege by daylight. O Jesu, marvel have I, said

[138-048]the damosel, what manner a man ye be, for it may never be otherwise but that ye

[138-049]be come of a noble blood, for so foul nor shamefully did never woman rule a

[138-050]knight as I have done you, and ever courteously ye have suffered me, and that

[138-051]came never but of a gentle blood.

[138-052]Damosel, said Beaumains, a knight may little do that may not suffer a damosel,

[138-053]for whatsomever ye said unto me I took none heed to your words, for the more ye

[138-054]said the more ye angered me, and my wrath I wreaked upon them that I had ado

[138-055]withal. And therefore all the missaying that ye missaid me furthered me in my

[138-056]battle, and caused me to think to show and prove myself at the end what I was;

[138-057]for peradventure though I had meat in King Arthur’s kitchen, yet I might

[138-058]have had meat enough in other places, but all that I did it for to prove and

[138-059]assay my friends, and that shall be known another day; and whether that I be a

[138-060]gentleman born or none, I let you wit, fair damosel, I have done you

[138-061]gentleman’s service, and peradventure better service yet will I do or I

[138-062]depart from you. Alas, she said, fair Beaumains, forgive me all that I have

[138-063]missaid or done against thee. With all my heart, said he, I forgive it you, for

[138-064]ye did nothing but as ye should do, for all your evil words pleased me; and

[138-065]damosel, said Beaumains, since it liketh you to say thus fair unto me, wit ye

[138-066]well it gladdeth my heart greatly, and now meseemeth there is no knight living

[138-067]but I am able enough for him.
