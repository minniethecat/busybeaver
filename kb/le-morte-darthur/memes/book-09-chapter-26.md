[229-001][[sir-tristram|Tristram]] sleeping by a well, and how she delivered letters to him from La Beale

[229-002]Isoud.

[229-003]And then he pulled off his helm and washed his visage and his hands, and so he

[229-004]fell asleep. In the meanwhile came a damosel that had sought Sir Tristram many

[229-005]ways and days within this land. And when she came to the well she looked upon

[229-006]him, and had forgotten him as in remembrance of Sir Tristram, but by his horse

[229-007]she knew him, that hight Passe-Brewel that had been Sir Tristram’s horse

[229-008]many years. For when he was mad in the forest Sir Fergus kept him. So this

[229-009]lady, Dame Bragwaine, abode still till he was awake. So when she saw him wake

[229-010]she saluted him, and he her again, for either knew other of old acquaintance;

[229-011]then she told him how she had sought him long and broad, and there she told him

[229-012]how she had letters from Queen La Beale Isoud. Then anon Sir Tristram read

[229-013]them, and wit ye well he was glad, for therein was many a piteous complaint.

[229-014]Then Sir Tristram said: Lady Bragwaine, ye shall ride with me till that

[229-015]tournament be done at the Castle of Maidens, and then shall bear letters and

[229-016]tidings with you. And then Sir Tristram took his horse and sought lodging, and

[229-017]there he met with a good ancient knight and prayed him to lodge with him. Right

[229-018]so came Gouvernail unto Sir Tristram, that was glad of that lady. So this old

[229-019]knight’s name was Sir Pellounes, and he told of the great tournament that

[229-020]should be at the Castle of Maidens. And there [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]] and thirty-two

[229-021]knights of his blood had ordained shields of Cornwall. And right so there came

[229-022]one unto Sir Pellounes, and told him that Sir Persides de Bloise was come home;

[229-023]then that knight held up his hands and thanked God of his coming home. And

[229-024]there Sir Pellounes told Sir Tristram that in two years he had not seen his

[229-025]son, Sir Persides. Sir, said Sir Tristram, I know your son well enough for a

[229-026]good knight.

[229-027]So on a time Sir Tristram and Sir Persides came to their lodging both at once,

[229-028]and so they unarmed them, and put upon them their clothing. And then these two

[229-029]knights each welcomed other. And when Sir Persides understood that Sir Tristram

[229-030]was of Cornwall, he said he was once in Cornwall: And there I jousted afore

[229-031][[king-mark|King Mark]]; and so it happed me at that time to overthrow ten knights, and then

[229-032]came to me Sir Tristram de Liones and overthrew me, and took my lady away from

[229-033]me, and that shall I never forget, but I shall remember me an ever I see my

[229-034]time. Ah, said Sir Tristram, now I understand that ye hate Sir Tristram. What

[229-035]deem ye, ween ye that Sir Tristram is not able to withstand your malice? Yes,

[229-036]said Sir Persides, I know well that Sir Tristram is a noble knight and a much

[229-037]better knight than I, yet shall I not owe him my good will. Right as they stood

[229-038]thus talking at a bay-window of that castle, they saw many knights riding to

[229-039]and fro toward the tournament. And then was Sir Tristram ware of a likely

[229-040]knight riding upon a great black horse, and a black-covered shield. What knight

[229-041]is that, said Sir Tristram, with the black horse and the black shield? he

[229-042]seemeth a good knight. I know him well, said Sir Persides, he is one of the

[229-043]best knights of the world. Then is it Sir Launcelot, said Tristram. Nay, said

[229-044]Sir Persides, it is [[sir-palomides|Sir Palomides]], that is yet unchristened.
