[159-001]knight that held within his castle thirty ladies, and how he slew him.

[159-002]So when they saw that they might not overcome him, they rode from him, and took

[159-003]their counsel to slay his horse; and so they came in upon [[sir-gareth|Sir Gareth]], and with

[159-004]spears they slew his horse, and then they assailed him hard. But when he was on

[159-005]foot, there was none that he fought but he gave him such a buffet that he did

[159-006]never recover. So he slew them by one and one till they were but four, and

[159-007]there they fled; and Sir Gareth took a good horse that was one of theirs, and

[159-008]rode his way.

[159-009]Then he rode a great pace till that he came to a castle, and there he heard

[159-010]much mourning of ladies and gentlewomen. So there came by him a page. What

[159-011]noise is this, said Sir Gareth, that I hear within this castle? Sir knight,

[159-012]said the page, here be within this castle thirty ladies, and all they be

[159-013]widows; for here is a knight that waiteth daily upon this castle, and his name

[159-014]is the Brown Knight without Pity, and he is the periloust knight that now

[159-015]liveth; and therefore sir, said the page, I rede you flee. Nay, said Sir

[159-016]Gareth, I will not flee though thou be afeard of him. And then the page saw

[159-017]where came the Brown Knight: Lo, said the page, yonder he cometh. Let me deal

[159-018]with him, said Sir Gareth. And when either of other had a sight they let their

[159-019]horses run, and the Brown Knight brake his spear, and Sir Gareth smote him

[159-020]throughout the body, that he overthrew him to the ground stark dead. So Sir

[159-021]Gareth rode into the castle, and prayed the ladies that he might repose him.

[159-022]Alas, said the ladies, ye may not be lodged here. Make him good cheer, said the

[159-023]page, for this knight hath slain your enemy. Then they all made him good cheer

[159-024]as lay in their power. But wit ye well they made him good cheer, for they might

[159-025]none otherwise do, for they were but poor.

[159-026]And so on the morn he went to mass, and there he saw the thirty ladies kneel,

[159-027]and lay grovelling upon divers tombs, making great dole and sorrow. Then Sir

[159-028]Gareth wist well that in the tombs lay their lords. Fair ladies, said Sir

[159-029]Gareth, ye must at the next feast of Pentecost be at [[camelot|the court of King Arthur]],

[159-030]and say that I, Sir Gareth, sent you thither. We shall do this, said the

[159-031]ladies. So he departed, and by fortune he came to a mountain, and there he

[159-032]found a goodly knight that bade him, Abide sir knight, and joust with me. What

[159-033]are ye? said Sir Gareth. My name is, said he, the Duke de la Rowse. Ah sir, ye

[159-034]are the same knight that I lodged once in your castle; and there I made promise

[159-035]unto your lady that I should yield me unto you. Ah, said the duke, art thou

[159-036]that proud knight that profferest to [[battle|fight]] with my knights; therefore make thee

[159-037]ready, for I will have ado with you. So they let their horses run, and there

[159-038]Sir Gareth smote the duke down from his horse. But the duke lightly avoided his

[159-039]horse, and dressed his shield and drew his sword, and bade Sir Gareth alight

[159-040]and fight with him. So he did alight, and they did great battle together more

[159-041]than an hour, and either hurt other full sore. At the last Sir Gareth gat the

[159-042]duke to the earth, and would have slain him, and then he yield him to him. Then

[159-043]must ye go, said Sir Gareth, unto Sir [[king-arthur|Arthur]] my lord at the next feast, and say

[159-044]that I, Sir Gareth of Orkney, sent you unto him. It shall be done, said the

[159-045]duke, and I will do to you homage and fealty with an hundred knights with me;

[159-046]and all the days of my life to do you service where ye will command me.
