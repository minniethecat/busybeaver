# Sir Blamore

Sir Blamore de Ganis was a [[Knight of the Round Table]], the brother of [[Sir Bleoberis|Sir Bleoberis de Ganis]], and cousin to [[Sir Launcelot]]. Together with his brother Bleoberis, he served as a champion of the court of [[King Arthur]] and fought in several notable judicial combats and tournaments.

## Brother of Bleoberis de Ganis

Blamore was bound by blood and knighthood to his brother Bleoberis. The chronicles record that "Sir Bleoberis and Sir Blamore de Ganis, that were brethren" (183-002) fought and adventured together, sharing the same lineage that connected them to the house of Sir Launcelot himself. Their kinship defined Blamore's place in the Arthurian world: he was never a solitary knight but one whose identity rested on the bonds of family honour.

## The Appeal Against King Anguish

Blamore's most notable appearance in the chronicle comes when he appealed [[King Anguish]] of Ireland before King Arthur's court. He "appealed the King of Ireland" (183-011) on a charge of treason, standing before the assembled Round Table to demand justice. When the king heard "Sir Blamore say his will, he understood what he meant" (183-015), and the matter was set for trial by combat.

The court recognised that "Sir Blamore de Ganis was a noble knight" (183-021), and few dared stand against him. The chronicler notes the general sentiment: there was "no knight in the king's court dare do battle with Sir Blamore de Ganis" (184-017), save for the greatest champions.

## The Combat with Sir Tristram

When no knight of King Anguish's party would face Blamore, [[Sir Tristram]] was summoned to fight in the king's stead. Blamore and Tristram met in the lists, where Tristram "through great might smote down Sir Blamore" (185-035) from his horse. "Then anon Sir Blamore avoided his horse and pulled out his sword" (185-036), continuing the fight on foot with fierce determination.

The combat was closely fought. "Sir Blamore was so hasty that he would have no rest" (185-043), pressing Tristram relentlessly until Tristram struck "Sir Blamore such a buffet upon the helm" (185-046) that ended the contest. In defeat, Blamore accepted the outcome with knightly dignity, and the matter was resolved at the commandment of the kings (185-015).

## The Kinsman of Launcelot

Blamore's kinship to Sir Launcelot placed him within the most formidable lineage of the Arthurian age. When Sir Bleoberis spoke of his brother, the text names them together: "Bleoberis, brother unto Sir Blamore" (185-008), and Bleoberis appealed to "his brother, Sir Blamore: Fair dear brother" (185-021), testifying to the deep affection between them. This brotherhood echoed the larger pattern of fraternal loyalty — like that of [[Sir Balin]] and [[Sir Balan]] — that runs through [[Le Morte d'Arthur]].

## The Honour of a Champion

Blamore de Ganis embodied the role of the Round Table champion: a knight whose martial prowess served the apparatus of justice. He stands as a secondary but significant figure in the Arthurian tapestry — a reminder that the fellowship of the Round Table was built not only on the singular deeds of its greatest knights but on the collective honour of men like Blamore, who risked their lives to uphold the law of the court.
