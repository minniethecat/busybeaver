# King Arthur

**King Arthur** is the central figure of Thomas Malory's *Le Morte d'Arthur* — the "[[baron|noble]] [[kinsman|kin]]g and conqueror" (002-028) around whom the ent[[wrath|ire]] Arthurian cycle revolves. His reign encompasses the founding of the [[round-table|Round Table]], the unification of Britain, and the flowering of chivalric culture, but ends in civil [[battle|war]] and personal t[[wrath|rage]]dy.

## Rise to Kingship

Arthur's origins are both mysterious and providential. Caxton's preface recounts how "Uther [[dragon|Pendragon]] gat the [[baron|noble]] conqueror King Arthur" (002-104), and the early books trace Arthur's path from unknown youth to acknowledged [[king|sovereign]]. His pulling of the sword from the stone proves his divine election, and "King Arthur made the [[realm|land]]s to be given again" (010-017) to dispossessed [[baron|lord]]s, establishing his reign on principles of justice and restoration.

## The Round Table King

Arthur's defining achievement is the creation of [[round-table|the Round Table fellowship]]. The preface describes him as "the best Christian, and worthy, King Arthur, which ought most to be remembered" (002-007), positioning him as both a secular and spiritual exemplar. Under his rule, the knights of the [[round-table|Round Table]] undertake quests, [[battle|fight]] battles, and establish a [[banner|standard]] of chivalric conduct that defines the age. The narrative of "the marriage of King Arthur to [[queen-guenever|Queen Guenever]]" (002-106) marks the union of personal and political fulfillment.

## Tragedy and Fall

Arthur's greatness is inseparable from his t[[wrath|rage]]dy. Wars are "made to King Arthur" (002-108) by rivals and rebels, and the later books record the progressive unraveling of his [[realm|kingdom]]. The [[treason|betrayal]] of [[sir-launcelot|Launcelot]] and [[queen-guenever|Guenever]], the machinations of [[mordred|Mordred]], and the final battle at Salisbury bring Arthur's reign to its destined end. The [[kinsman|kin]]g who united Britain dies leaving it fragmented — a t[[wrath|rage]]dy that Malory presents as both personal and cosmic.
