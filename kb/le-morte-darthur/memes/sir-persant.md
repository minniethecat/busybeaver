# Sir Persant

Sir Persant of Inde was one of the four colour-coded brother knights who guarded the approaches to the [[Castle Perilous]] and the lady [[Dame Lioness]]. As the Knight of Indigo, he was the most lordly of the four brothers, and his defeat by [[Sir Beaumains]] (Sir Gareth) marked the penultimate challenge before the young knight faced the Red Knight of the Red Launds.

## The Knight of Indigo

The chronicle introduces Persant with deliberate ceremony: "his name is Sir Persant of Inde, the most lordliest knight that ever thou knewest" (138-024). This superlative — "most lordliest" — distinguished Persant from his three brothers, all of whom guarded the passages to Dame Lioness. Each brother was associated with a colour: the Black Knight, the Green Knight, the Red Knight, and Sir Persant of Inde — the indigo knight whose refinement set him apart.

Those who knew the brothers warned Beaumains that "Sir Persant of Inde is nothing of might nor strength" compared to the Red Knight who guarded the final pass (138-041). Yet this warning spoke less to Persant's weakness than to the extraordinary power of his elder brother; Persant himself was a formidable opponent.

## The Combat with Sir Beaumains

When [[Sir Beaumains]] (the incognito [[Sir Gareth]]) approached Persant's pavilion, the knight of Indigo "had espied them as they came" (139-002) and prepared for battle. The messenger whom Beaumains sent returned "unto Sir Persant and told him all his answer" (139-005), confirming that the young knight would not yield passage without combat.

The fight was decisive. "Beaumains smote Sir Persant above upon the helm, that he fell grovelling to the earth" (139-017), and "then Sir Persant yielded him and asked him mercy" (139-020). In defeat, Persant displayed the courtesy that marked the truest knights: he immediately offered hospitality to the man who had conquered him.

## The Lordly Host

Persant's hospitality was extraordinary. The victor and his company "went unto Sir Persant's pavilion" (139-029) where they "ate spices, and afterward Sir Persant made him great cheer" (139-030). The defeated knight became the gracious host, transforming the site of combat into a place of welcome.

That night, Persant sent his own daughter to Beaumains's bedchamber. "Sir Persant had a lady, a fair daughter" (139-032), and the damosel came at her father's command. When asked her purpose, she answered: "Sir, I am Sir Persant's daughter, that by the commandment of my father am come hither" (139-040). The episode revealed the complex codes of hospitality and honour that governed knightly households — codes that Beaumains navigated with the purity that marked him as a true son of the Round Table.

## The Prophecy of the Red Knight

After the night's events, Persant provided Beaumains with crucial intelligence about the final challenge. "Now shall I tell you, said Sir Persant, this Red Knight" (140-013) — and he proceeded to describe his elder brother, the most fearsome of the four, who had held the Lady Lioness captive for two years. Persant's testimony prepared Beaumains for the ordeal that would prove his worth and reveal his true identity as Sir Gareth of [[Orkney]].

## The Courtesy of the Conquered

Sir Persant of Inde exemplifies a distinctive Arthurian type: the knight whose nobility is most fully revealed in defeat. Where lesser men grow bitter in loss, Persant transformed submission into hospitality, enmity into alliance. His pavilion became a place of healing and counsel, and his testimony armed Beaumains for the ultimate trial. In this, Persant demonstrated that the chivalric virtues — courtesy, generosity, truth-telling — could flourish even on the losing side of the lance.
