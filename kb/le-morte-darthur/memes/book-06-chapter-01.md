[109-001]departed from the court, and how Sir Lionel left him sleeping and was

[109-002]taken.

[109-003]Soon after that [[king-arthur|King Arthur]] was come from Rome into England, then all the

[109-004]knights of the [[round-table|Table Round]] resorted unto the king, and made many [[tournament|jousts]] and

[109-005]tournaments, and some there were that were but knights, which increased so in

[109-006]arms and [[honour|worship]] that they passed all their fellows in [[courage|prowess]] and noble deeds,

[109-007]and that was well proved on many; but in especial it was proved on Sir

[109-008][[sir-launcelot|Launcelot du Lake]], for in all tournaments and jousts and deeds of arms, both

[109-009]for life and death, he passed all other knights, and at no time he was never

[109-010]overcome but if it were by treason or enchantment; so Sir Launcelot increased

[109-011]so marvellously in worship, and in honour, therefore is he the first knight

[109-012]that the French book maketh mention of after King Arthur came from Rome.

[109-013]Wherefore [[queen-guenever|Queen Guenever]] had him in great favour above all other knights, and

[109-014]in certain he loved the queen again above all other ladies and damosels of his

[109-015]life, and for her he did many deeds of arms, and saved her from the fire

[109-016]through his noble chivalry.

[109-017]Thus Sir Launcelot rested him long with play and game. And then he thought

[109-018]himself to prove himself in strange adventures, then he bade his nephew, Sir

[109-019]Lionel, for to make him ready; for we two will seek adventures. So they mounted

[109-020]on their horses, armed at all rights, and rode into a deep forest and so into a

[109-021]deep plain. And then the weather was hot about noon, and Sir Launcelot had

[109-022]great lust to sleep. Then Sir Lionel espied a great apple-tree that stood by an

[109-023]hedge, and said, Brother, yonder is a fair shadow, there may we rest us [and]

[109-024]our horses. It is well said, fair brother, said Sir Launcelot, for this eight

[109-025]year I was not so sleepy as I am now; and so they there alighted and tied their

[109-026]horses unto sundry trees, and so Sir Launcelot laid him down under an

[109-027]appletree, and his helm he laid under his head. And Sir Lionel waked while he

[109-028]slept. So Sir Launcelot was asleep passing fast.

[109-029]And in the meanwhile there came three knights riding, as fast fleeing as ever

[109-030]they might ride. And there followed them three but one knight. And when Sir

[109-031]Lionel saw him, him thought he saw never so great a knight, nor so well faring

[109-032]a man, neither so well apparelled unto all rights. So within a while this

[109-033]strong knight had overtaken one of these knights, and there he smote him to the

[109-034]cold earth that he lay still. And then he rode unto the second knight, and

[109-035]smote him so that man and horse fell down. And then straight to the third

[109-036]knight he rode, and smote him behind his horse’s arse a spear length. And

[109-037]then he alighted down and reined his horse on the bridle, and bound all the

[109-038]three knights fast with the reins of their own bridles. When Sir Lionel saw him

[109-039]do thus, he thought to assay him, and made him ready, and stilly and privily he

[109-040]took his horse, and thought not for to awake Sir Launcelot. And when he was

[109-041]mounted upon his horse, he overtook this strong knight, and bade him turn, and

[109-042]the other smote Sir Lionel so hard that horse and man he bare to the earth, and

[109-043]so he alighted down and bound him fast, and threw him overthwart his own horse,

[109-044]and so he served them all four, and rode with them away to his own castle. And

[109-045]when he came there he gart unarm them, and beat them with thorns all naked, and

[109-046]after put them in a deep prison where were many more knights, that made great

[109-047]dolour.
