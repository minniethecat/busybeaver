**Virtue** in [[Le Morte d'Arthur]] operates on multiple registers: it is a moral quality to be cultivated, a [[marvel|supernatural]] property inhering in objects and persons, and a criterion by which knights are judged worthy or unworthy. In Caxton's preface, virtue and [[sin]] are presented as the two poles between which the reader must choose (002-085). Throughout the Morte, the possession of virtue—or its absence—determines a knight's fate more surely than [[prowess]] or [[lineage]].

## Virtue as Moral Choice

Caxton's preface frames the ent[[wrath|ire]] work as a lesson in moral discernment, urging the reader to follow virtue and reject sin:

> Do after the good and leave the evil, and it shall bring you to good fame and renomee. And for to pass the time this book shall be pleasant to read in; but for to give faith and believe that all is true that is contained herein, ye be at your liberty. But all is written for our doctrine, and for to beware that we fall not to vice nor sin, but to exercise and follow virtue, by which we may come and attain to good fame and name in this life, and after this short and transitory life to come unto everlasting bliss in heaven. (002-085–090)

This is the moral frame of the ent[[wrath|ire]] work. Virtue is not merely a knightly attribute but the path to salvation—a medieval Christian conception that places chivalric conduct within a cosmic moral order.

## The Virtue of the Sword

The sword-in-the-stone episode establishes virtue as a quality that can be tested and demonstrated. Only the knight who possesses the requisite virtues may draw the sword:

> for to find a man of such virtue to draw it out of the scabbard (032-001)

The Archbishop of Canterbury prays that God will reveal a man of sufficient virtue to claim the sword—and by implication, the throne. The test is not of strength but of moral desert. When [[King Arthur]] draws the sword, it is not his martial [[prowess]] that qualifies him but his possession of the virtues that [[king|kingship]] requ[[wrath|ire]]s—a point underscored when the assembled [[baron|lord]]s refuse to accept him until repeated demonstrations silence their objections.

## Magical Virtue

In Malory's world, virtue is not only a moral quality but a property that objects can possess. Rings, horns, and precious stones are described as having "virtue"—an efficacy or power that exceeds their material nature:

> beset with stones of virtue to the value of a thousand pound (153-025)

> the virtue of my ring is that, that is green it will turn to red (154-033)

> the horn had such a virtue that there might no (197-005)

This sense of "virtue" as inherent power—closer to the Latin *virtus* than to modern moral virtue—pervades the Morte's [[marvel|supernatural]] economy. A lady's ring, a [[enchantment|magic]] horn, a stone in a crown: each possesses a specific operative power. The moral and the [[enchantment|magic]]al meanings of virtue are linked by the idea of *efficacy*—virtue is that which produces results, whether in the soul or in the world.

## Virtue and Worthiness

The virtues requ[[wrath|ire]]d of a knight are not abstract but demonstrated in action. When the Archbishop declares that only one who possesses "these virtues" may draw the sword (032-025), he implies a catalogue of specific qualities—[[hardiness]], [[courtesy]], [[gentleness]], [[faith]]—that together constitute knightly [[worship|worthiness]]. Virtue in this sense is composite and cumulative; no single quality suffices. The knight who excels in [[courage|prowess]] but lacks [[mercy]], or who possesses cou[[wrath|rage]] but not [[humility]], is not truly virtuous and will fail the tests that the [[king-arthur|Arthur]]ian world sets for him.
