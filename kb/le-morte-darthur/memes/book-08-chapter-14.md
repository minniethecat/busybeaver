[177-001]and how her husband fought with [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]].

[177-002]And there she welcomed him fair, and either halsed other in arms, and so she

[177-003]let put up his horse in the best wise, and then she unarmed him. And so they

[177-004]supped lightly, and went to bed with great joy and pleasaunce; and so in his

[177-005]raging he took no keep of his green wound that [[king-mark|King Mark]] had given him. And so

[177-006]Sir Tristram be-bled both the over sheet and the nether, and pillows, and head

[177-007]sheet. And within a while there came one afore, that warned her that her lord

[177-008]was near-hand within a bow-draught. So she made Sir Tristram to arise, and so

[177-009]he armed him, and took his horse, and so departed. By then was come Segwarides,

[177-010]her lord, and when he found her bed troubled and broken, and went near and

[177-011]beheld it by candle light, then he saw that there had lain a wounded knight.

[177-012]Ah, false traitress, then he said, why hast thou betrayed me? And therewithal

[177-013]he swang out a sword, and said: But if thou tell me who hath been here, here

[177-014]thou shalt die. Ah, my lord, mercy, said the lady, and held up her hands,

[177-015]saying: Slay me not, and I shall tell you all who hath been here. Tell anon,

[177-016]said Segwarides, to me all the truth. Anon for dread she said: Here was Sir

[177-017]Tristram with me, and by the way as he came to me ward, he was sore wounded.

[177-018]Ah, false traitress, said Segwarides, where is he become? Sir, she said, he is

[177-019]armed, and departed on horseback, not yet hence half a mile. Ye say well, said

[177-020]Segwarides.

[177-021]Then he armed him lightly, and gat his horse, and rode after Sir Tristram that

[177-022]rode straightway unto Tintagil. And within a while he overtook Sir Tristram,

[177-023]and then he bade him, Turn, false traitor knight. And Sir Tristram anon turned

[177-024]him against him. And therewithal Segwarides smote Sir Tristram with a spear

[177-025]that it all to-brast; and then he swang out his sword and smote fast at Sir

[177-026]Tristram. Sir knight, said Sir Tristram, I counsel you that ye smite no more,

[177-027]howbeit for the wrongs that I have done you I will forbear you as long as I

[177-028]may. Nay, said Segwarides, that shall not be, for either thou shalt die or I.

[177-029]Then Sir Tristram drew out his sword, and hurtled his horse unto him fiercely,

[177-030]and through the waist of the body he smote Sir Segwarides that he fell to the

[177-031]earth in a swoon. And so Sir Tristram departed and left him there. And so he

[177-032]rode unto Tintagil and took his lodging secretly, for he would not be known

[177-033]that he was hurt. Also Sir Segwarides’ men rode after their master, whom

[177-034]they found lying in the [[battle|field]] sore wounded, and brought him home on his shield,

[177-035]and there he lay long or that he were whole, but at the last he recovered. Also

[177-036]King Mark would not be aknown of that Sir Tristram and he had met that night.

[177-037]And as for Sir Tristram, he knew not that King Mark had met with him. And so

[177-038]the king askance came to Sir Tristram, to comfort him as he lay sick in his

[177-039]bed. But as long as King Mark lived he loved never Sir Tristram after that;

[177-040]though there was fair speech, love was there none. And thus it passed many

[177-041]weeks and days, and all was forgiven and forgotten; for Sir Segwarides durst

[177-042]not have ado with Sir Tristram, because of his noble [[courage|prowess]], and also because

[177-043]he was nephew unto King Mark; therefore he let it overslip: for he that hath a

[177-044]privy hurt is loath to have a shame outward.
