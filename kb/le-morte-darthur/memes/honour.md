# Honour

**Honour** is the currency of the chivalric world in Thomas Malory's *Le Morte d'[[king-arthur|Arthur]]*. It is simultaneously an internal quality — a knight's inherent [[worship|worthiness]] — and an external reputation that must be continually earned, defended, and publicly acknowledged. The pursuit and preservation of [[worship|honour]] drives much of the narrative action.

## Honour as Public Standing

Caxton's preface presents the book as teaching readers to follow "[[worship|honour]]" and avoid the path of those "that were vicious" (002-078). Honour is something that can be lost: a character fears being "[[shame|dishonour]]ed" (004-013) and another declares "I have lost the [[worship|honour]] on horseback" (026-052). The public nature of [[worship|honour]] means that shame is its constant shadow — to be [[shame|dishonour]]ed is to be socially unmade.

## Honour as Motivation

Knights act for [[worship|honour]] above all else. [[merlin|Merlin]] assures that fulfilling a des[[wrath|ire]] "shall be his [[worship|honour]] and profit" (004-043), lin[[kinsman|kin]]g personal benefit to honorable conduct. When knights offer service, they bring "great [[worship|honour]]" (036-010) to their [[baron|lord]]. The phrase "I should be [[shame|dishonour]]ed" (024-017) is sufficient to motivate desperate action — the threat to [[worship|honour]] is as compelling as any physical d[[wrath|anger]].

## The Tragedy of Honour

Honour's dark side is its inflexibility. Knights must defend their [[worship|honour]] even when doing so leads to destruction. The [[round-table|Round Table]] fractures not because its knights lack [[worship|honour]] but because competing claims of [[worship|honour]] — to [[kinsman|kin]]g, to [[kinsman|kin]], to lover — cannot all be satisfied. Honour, which should unite the chivalric world, becomes the instrument of its dissolution.
