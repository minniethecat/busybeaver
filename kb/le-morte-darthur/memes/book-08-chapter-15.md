[178-001]fairest lady in [[king-mark|King Mark]]’s court, whom he took away, and how he was

[178-002]fought with.

[178-003]Then it befell upon a day that the good knight Bleoberis de Ganis, brother to

[178-004]Blamore de Ganis, and nigh cousin unto the good knight [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot du Lake]],

[178-005]this Bleoberis came unto the court of King Mark, and there he asked of King

[178-006]Mark a boon, to give him what gift that he would ask in his court. When the

[178-007]king heard him ask so, he marvelled of his asking, but because he was a knight

[178-008]of the [[round-table|Round Table]], and of a great [[honour|renown]], King Mark granted him his whole

[178-009]asking. Then, said Sir Bleoberis, I will have the fairest lady in your court

[178-010]that me list to choose. I may not say nay, said King Mark; now choose at your

[178-011]adventure. And so Sir Bleoberis did choose Sir Segwarides’ wife, and took

[178-012]her by the hand, and so went his way with her; and so he took his horse and

[178-013]gart set her behind his squire, and rode upon his way.

[178-014]When Sir Segwarides heard tell that his lady was gone with a knight of King

[178-015][[king-arthur|Arthur]]’s court, then he armed him and rode after that knight for to

[178-016]rescue his lady. So when Bleoberis was gone with this lady, King Mark and all

[178-017]the court was wroth that she was away. Then were there certain ladies that knew

[178-018]that there were great love between [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]] and her, and also that lady

[178-019]loved Sir Tristram above all other knights. Then there was one lady that

[178-020]rebuked Sir Tristram in the horriblest wise, and called him coward knight, that

[178-021]he would for shame of his knighthood see a lady so shamefully be taken away

[178-022]from his uncle’s court. But she meant that either of them had loved other

[178-023]with entire heart. But Sir Tristram answered her thus: Fair lady, it is not my

[178-024]part to have ado in such matters while her lord and husband is present here;

[178-025]and if it had been that her lord had not been here in this court, then for the

[178-026]worship of this court peradventure I would have been her champion, and if so be

[178-027]Sir Segwarides speed not well, it may happen that I will speak with that good

[178-028]knight or ever he pass from this country.

[178-029]Then within a while came one of Sir Segwarides’ squires, and told in the

[178-030]court that Sir Segwarides was beaten sore and wounded to the point of death; as

[178-031]he would have rescued his lady Sir Bleoberis overthrew him and sore hath

[178-032]wounded him. Then was King Mark heavy thereof, and all the court. When Sir

[178-033]Tristram heard of this he was ashamed and sore grieved; and then was he soon

[178-034]armed and on horseback, and Gouvernail, his servant, bare his shield and spear.

[178-035]And so as Sir Tristram rode fast he met with Sir Andred his cousin, that by the

[178-036]commandment of King Mark was sent to bring forth, an ever it lay in his power,

[178-037]two knights of Arthur’s court, that rode by the country to seek their

[178-038]adventures. When Sir Tristram saw Sir Andred he asked him what tidings. So God

[178-039]me help, said Sir Andred, there was never worse with me, for here by the

[178-040]commandment of King Mark I was sent to fetch two knights of King Arthur’s

[178-041]court, and that one beat me and wounded me, and set nought by my message. Fair

[178-042]cousin, said Sir Tristram, ride on your way, and if I may meet them it may

[178-043]happen I shall revenge you. So Sir Andred rode into Cornwall, and Sir Tristram

[178-044]rode after the two knights, the which one hight Sagramore le Desirous, and the

[178-045]other hight Dodinas le Savage.
