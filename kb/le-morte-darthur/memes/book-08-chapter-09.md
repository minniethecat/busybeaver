[172-002]Then the king for great favour made Tramtrist to be put in his daughter’s

[172-003]ward and keeping, because she was a noble surgeon. And when she had searched

[172-004]him she found in the bottom of his wound that therein was poison, and so she

[172-005]healed him within a while; and therefore Tramtrist cast great love to La Beale

[172-006]Isoud, for she was at that time the fairest maid and lady of the world. And

[172-007]there Tramtrist learned her to harp, and she began to have a great fantasy unto

[172-008]him. And at that time Sir Palamides, the Saracen, was in that country, and well

[172-009]cherished with the king and the queen. And every day Sir Palamides drew unto La

[172-010]Beale Isoud and proffered her many gifts, for he loved her passingly well. All

[172-011]that espied Tramtrist, and full well knew he Sir Palamides for a noble knight

[172-012]and a mighty man. And wit you well Sir Tramtrist had great despite at Sir

[172-013]Palamides, for La Beale Isoud told Tramtrist that Palamides was in will to be

[172-014]christened for her sake. Thus was there great envy betwixt Tramtrist and Sir

[172-015]Palamides.

[172-016]Then it befell that King Anguish let cry a great [[tournament|jousts]] and a great tournament

[172-017]for a lady that was called the Lady of the Launds, and she was nigh cousin unto

[172-018]the king. And what man won her, three days after he should wed her and have all

[172-019]her lands. This cry was made in England, Wales, Scotland, and also in France

[172-020]and in Brittany. It befell upon a day La Beale Isoud came unto Sir Tramtrist,

[172-021]and told him of this tournament. He answered and said: Fair lady, I am but a

[172-022]feeble knight, and but late I had been dead had not your good ladyship been.

[172-023]Now, fair lady, what would ye I should do in this matter? well ye wot, my lady,

[172-024]that I may not joust. Ah, Tramtrist, said La Beale Isoud, why will ye not have

[172-025]ado at that tournament? well I wot Sir Palamides shall be there, and to do what

[172-026]he may; and therefore Tramtrist, I pray you for to be there, for else Sir

[172-027]Palamides is like to win the degree. Madam, said Tramtrist, as for that, it may

[172-028]be so, for he is a proved knight, and I am but a young knight and late made;

[172-029]and the first battle that I did it mishapped me to be sore wounded as ye see.

[172-030]But an I wist ye would be my better lady, at that tournament I will be, so that

[172-031]ye will keep my counsel and let no creature have knowledge that I shall joust

[172-032]but yourself, and such as ye will to keep your counsel, my poor person shall I

[172-033]jeopard there for your sake, that, peradventure, Sir Palamides shall know when

[172-034]that I come. Thereto, said La Beale Isoud, do your best, and as I can, said La

[172-035]Beale Isoud, I shall purvey horse and armour for you at my device. As ye will

[172-036]so be it, said Sir Tramtrist, I will be at your commandment.

[172-037]So at the day of jousts there came Sir Palamides with a black shield, and he

[172-038]overthrew many knights, that all the people had marvel of him. For he put to

[172-039]the worse [[sir-gawaine|Sir Gawaine]], Gaheris, Agravaine, Bagdemagus, [[sir-kay|Kay]], Dodinas le Savage,

[172-040]Sagramore le Desirous, Gumret le Petit, and Griflet le Fise de Dieu. All these

[172-041]the first day Sir Palamides struck down to the earth. And then all manner of

[172-042]knights were adread of Sir Palamides, and many called him the Knight with the

[172-043]Black Shield. So that day Sir Palamides had great [[honour|worship]].

[172-044]Then came King Anguish unto Tramtrist, and asked him why he would not joust.

[172-045]Sir, he said, I was but late hurt, and as yet I dare not adventure me. Then

[172-046]came there the same squire that was sent from the king’s daughter of

[172-047]France unto [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]]. And when he had espied Sir Tristram he fell flat to

[172-048]his feet. All that espied La Beale Isoud, what courtesy the squire made unto

[172-049]Sir Tristram. And therewithal suddenly Sir Tristram ran unto his squire, whose

[172-050]name was Hebes le Renoumes, and prayed him heartily in no wise to tell his

[172-051]name. Sir, said Hebes, I will not discover your name but if ye command me.
