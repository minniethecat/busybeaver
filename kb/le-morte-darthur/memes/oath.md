# Oath

The **oath** is the formal mechanism through which loyalty is constituted in Thomas Malory's *Le Morte d'[[king-arthur|Arthur]]*. Spoken before witnesses and often invo[[kinsman|kin]]g God, the oath transforms personal intention into binding obligation. The ent[[wrath|ire]] feudal and chivalric order of [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s world rests upon a network of oaths, and the brea[[kinsman|kin]]g of oaths is the mechanism through which that order collapses.

## The Making of Oaths

Oaths in Malory are public performative acts. When [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s allies pledge their support, "then they made an oath. The first that began the oath was the [[duke|Duke]] of Cambenet" (015-009), the formality of the scene emphasizing the gravity of the commitment. Each [[baron|lord]] swears in turn, creating a chain of mutual obligation that binds the realm together.

## Loath and Oath

The word "loath" (reluctant, unwilling) appears frequently in proximity to oath-ma[[kinsman|kin]]g in Malory. Knights say "that is me loath" (025-023) when challenged, and "else would I have been loath" (036-028) when explaining their actions. The hesitation encoded in "loath" acknowledges the weight of the oath — a knight does not break his word lightly, and the reluctance to act against one's sworn bond is a mark of [[honour|honor]]. When a character says "that were me loath to do" (043-020), the phrase carries the moral gravity of the ent[[wrath|ire]] oath-bound system.

## The Pentecostal Oath

The most important oath in Malory is the [[feast-of-pentecost|Pentecost]]al Oath, sworn annually by the knights of the [[round-table|Round Table]]. It binds them to specific ethical commitments — to flee treason, to show mercy, to defend [[lady|ladies]] — and constitutes the formal code of chivalric conduct. This oath transforms the [[round-table|Round Table]] from a collection of individual [[battle|war]]riors into a moral community, and its renewal each year acknowledges that oaths must be continually reaffirmed.
