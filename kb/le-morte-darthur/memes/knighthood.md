# Knighthood

**Knighthood**  in Malory's "Le Morte d'[[king-arthur|Arthur]]" is both a social institution and a moral order — a sacred [[fellowship|brotherhood]] conferred through ritual dubbing that binds its members to a code of [[honour|honor]], mercy, loyalty, and service. The "high order of knighthood" is repeatedly invoked as the [[banner|standard]] against which every knight's conduct is measured, and its violation constitutes the gravest form of [[treason|betrayal]] in the [[king-arthur|Arthur]]ian world. The order transcends individual knights, nations, and even life itself, creating bonds of obligation that persist across enmity and death.

## The Conferral of Knighthood

Knighthood is granted through a formal ceremony — the "dubbing" or "giving the order of knighthood" — typically performed by a [[kinsman|kin]]g or a knight of established [[honour|worship]]. The ritual is both a recognition of proven worth and an induction into an order with binding obligations:

> "the king did do give him the order of knighthood" (024-039)

The conferral carries weight precisely because it is not freely given: it must be earned through demonstrated [[courage|prowess]], and the giver must themselves possess sufficient [[honour|worship]] to confer it validly. This creates chains of knighthood that link the greatest knights back to [[King Arthur]] himself, forming a [[blood|lineage]] of [[honour|honor]] parallel to the [[blood|lineage]] of blood.

## The Oath and Obligations

Upon receiving knighthood, each knight swears an oath that defines the moral boundaries of his conduct. The oath is not only to the [[kinsman|kin]]g but to the order itself — a higher [[fealty|allegiance]] that sometimes brings knights into conflict with their earthly [[baron|lord]]s:

> "the oath that they made; and therewith they all arose" (092-015)

The oath's core obligations include: to show mercy to defeated opponents, to aid those who ask in the name of knighthood, to protect the defenseless, and never to commit treason. A knight who violates these terms commits "shame unto the order of knighthood" and becomes a "traitor unto knighthood" — a far graver charge than mere treason against a [[kinsman|kin]]g:

> "shame unto the order of knighthood, and contrary unto his oath" (118-007)
> "traitor unto knighthood, who did learn thee to distress ladies and gentlewomen?" (118-015)

## Knighthood as a Bond of Mutual Obligation

The most distinctive feature of Malorian knighthood is its function as a bond of mutual obligation between knights. A knight can "requ[[wrath|ire]]" another "of knighthood" or "of true knighthood" to perform a service, and this [[challenge|appeal]] carries a force that overrides personal enmity or competing loyalties:

> "I require thee of thy knighthood, keep me and save me" (125-015)
> "now I require thee, on thy knighthood, help my brother" (122-024)

This obligation creates a network of reciprocal duties that is both the strength and the t[[wrath|rage]]dy of the [[king-arthur|Arthur]]ian world. When the bonds of knighthood are [[honour|honor]]ed, they enable acts of extraordinary nobility across lines of [[fealty|allegiance]]. When they are broken — as in the fatal [[battle|war]] between [[Sir Launcelot]] and [[Sir Gawaine]] — they render the ent[[wrath|ire]] order unsustainable.

## The Mercy Clause

Among the most emphasized obligations of knighthood is the duty of mercy. A knight must spare a defeated opponent who asks for quarter, and failure to do so is a d[[wrath|ire]]ct violation of the knightly oath:

> "a knight without mercy is dishonoured" (058-008; cf. knighthood context)

This mercy obligation creates recurring dramatic tension: a knight who has every personal reason to kill an opponent — [[revenge|vengeance]], self-preservation, or strategic advantage — must stay his hand if the opponent invokes the code. The refusal of mercy by characters like [[Sir Gawaine]] in his pursuit of [[Sir Launcelot]] marks the point at which the order of knighthood has broken down irretrievably.

## Knighthood's Narrative Function

Knighthood as an institution provides the moral framework against which the narrative measures every knight's actions. Each [[battle|combat]], each quest, and each act of mercy or cruelty is judged not merely by its outcome but by its conformity to the order's code. The [[Round Table]] represents the fullest institutional expression of this code, and its dissolution signifies the collapse of knighthood itself as a viable ideal. The t[[wrath|rage]]dy of the work is that the code of knighthood contains within itself the contradictions — between the demands of [[honour|honor]] and mercy, between loyalty to [[kinsman|kin]] and loyalty to the order — that ultimately destroy it.
