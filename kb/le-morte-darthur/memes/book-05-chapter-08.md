[103-003]Now leave we King Arthur and his noble knights which had won the [[battle|field]], and had

[103-004]brought their prisoners to Paris, and speak we of a senator which escaped from

[103-005]the battle, and came to Lucius the emperor, and said to him, Sir emperor, I

[103-006]advise thee for to withdraw thee; what dost thou here? thou shalt win nothing

[103-007]in these marches but great strokes out of all measure, for this day one of

[103-008]Arthur’s knights was worth in the battle an hundred of ours. Fie on thee,

[103-009]said Lucius, thou speakest cowardly; for thy words grieve me more than all the

[103-010]loss that I had this day. And anon he sent forth a king, which hight Sir

[103-011]Leomie, with a great army, and bade him hie him fast to-fore, and he would

[103-012]follow hastily after. King Arthur was warned privily, and sent his people to

[103-013]Sessoine, and took up the towns and castles from the Romans. Then the king

[103-014]commanded Sir Cador to take the rearward, and to take with him certain knights

[103-015]of the [[round-table|Round Table]], and [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]], [[sir-bors|Sir Bors]], [[sir-kay|Sir Kay]], Sir Marrok, with Sir

[103-016]Marhaus, shall await on our person. Thus the King Arthur disperpled his host in

[103-017]divers parties, to the end that his enemies should not escape.

[103-018]When the emperor was entered into the vale of Sessoine, he might see where King

[103-019]Arthur was embattled and his banner displayed; and he was beset round about

[103-020]with his enemies, that needs he must fight or yield him, for he might not flee,

[103-021]but said openly unto the Romans, Sirs, I admonish you that this day ye fight

[103-022]and acquit you as men, and remember how Rome domineth and is chief and head

[103-023]over all the earth and universal world, and suffer not these Britons this day

[103-024]to abide against us; and therewith he did command his trumpets to blow the

[103-025]bloody sounds, in such wise that the ground trembled and dindled.

[103-026]Then the battles approached and shoved and shouted on both sides, and great

[103-027]strokes were smitten on both sides, many men overthrown, hurt, and slain; and

[103-028]great valiances, prowesses and appertices of war were that day showed, which

[103-029]were over long to recount the noble feats of every man, for they should contain

[103-030]an whole volume. But in especial, King Arthur rode in the battle exhorting his

[103-031]knights to do well, and himself did as nobly with his hands as was possible a

[103-032]man to do; he drew out [[sword-in-the-stone|Excalibur]] his sword, and awaited ever whereas the Romans

[103-033]were thickest and most grieved his people, and anon he addressed him on that

[103-034]part, and hew and slew down right, and rescued his people; and he slew a great

[103-035]giant named Galapas, which was a man of an huge quantity and height, he shorted

[103-036]him and smote off both his legs by the knees, saying, Now art thou better of a

[103-037]size to deal with than thou were, and after smote off his head. There Sir

[103-038][[sir-gawaine|Gawaine]] fought nobly and slew three admirals in that battle. And so did all the

[103-039]knights of the Round Table. Thus the battle between King Arthur and Lucius the

[103-040]Emperor endured long. Lucius had on his side many Saracens which were slain.

[103-041]And thus the battle was great, and oftsides that one party was at a fordeal and

[103-042]anon at an afterdeal, which endured so long till at the last King Arthur espied

[103-043]where Lucius the Emperor fought, and did [[marvel|wonder]] with his own hands. And anon he

[103-044]rode to him. And either smote other fiercely, and at last Lucius smote Arthur

[103-045]thwart the visage, and gave him a large wound. And when King Arthur felt

[103-046]himself hurt, anon he smote him again with Excalibur that it cleft his head,

[103-047]from the summit of his head, and stinted not till it came to his breast. And

[103-048]then the emperor fell down dead and there ended his life.

[103-049]And when it was known that the emperor was slain, anon all the Romans with all

[103-050]their host put them to flight, and King Arthur with all his knights followed

[103-051]the chase, and slew down right all them that they might attain. And thus was

[103-052]the victory given to King Arthur, and the triumph; and there were slain on the

[103-053]part of Lucius more than an hundred thousand. And after King Arthur did do

[103-054]ransack the dead bodies, and did do bury them that were slain of his retinue,

[103-055]every man according to the estate and degree that he was of. And them that were

[103-056]hurt he let the surgeons do search their hurts and wounds, and commanded to

[103-057]spare no salves nor medicines till they were whole.

[103-058]Then the king rode straight to the place where the Emperor Lucius lay dead, and

[103-059]with him he found slain the Soudan of Syria, the King of Egypt and of Ethiopia,

[103-060]which were two noble kings, with seventeen other kings of divers regions, and

[103-061]also sixty senators of Rome, all noble men, whom the king did do balm and gum

[103-062]with many good gums aromatic, and after did do cere them in sixty fold of cered

[103-063]cloth of sendal, and laid them in chests of lead, because they should not chafe

[103-064]nor savour, and upon all these bodies their shields with their arms and banners

[103-065]were set, to the end they should be known of what country they were. And after

[103-066]he found three senators which were alive, to whom he said, For to save your

[103-067]lives I will that ye take these dead bodies, and carry them with you unto great

[103-068]Rome, and present them to the Potestate on my behalf, shewing him my letters,

[103-069]and tell them that I in my person shall hastily be at Rome. And I suppose the

[103-070]Romans shall beware how they shall demand any tribute of me. And I command you

[103-071]to say when ye shall come to Rome, to the Potestate and all the Council and

[103-072]Senate, that I send to them these dead bodies for the tribute that they have

[103-073]demanded. And if they be not content with these, I shall pay more at my coming,

[103-074]for other tribute owe I none, nor none other will I pay. And methinketh this

[103-075]sufficeth for Britain, Ireland and all Almaine with Germany. And furthermore, I

[103-076]charge you to say to them, that I command them upon pain of their heads never

[103-077]to demand tribute nor tax of me nor of my lands. Then with this charge and

[103-078]commandment, the three senators aforesaid departed with all the said dead

[103-079]bodies, laying the body of Lucius in a car covered with the arms of the Empire

[103-080]all alone; and after alway two bodies of kings in a chariot, and then the

[103-081]bodies of the senators after them, and so went toward Rome, and showed their

[103-082]legation and message to the Potestate and Senate, recounting the battle done in

[103-083]France, and how the field was lost and much people and innumerable slain.

[103-084]Wherefore they advised them in no wise to move no more war against that noble

[103-085]conqueror Arthur, for his might and [[courage|prowess]] is most to be doubted, seen the

[103-086]noble kings and great multitude of knights of the Round Table, to whom none

[103-087]earthly prince may compare.
