# Enchantment

**Enchantment**  in Malory's "Le Morte d'[[king-arthur|Arthur]]" is the principal form of [[marvel|supernatural]] intervention in the chivalric world — a magical art that can alter perception, control the will, transform appearances, and shape the course of destiny. Unlike the divine [[marvel|miracle]]s that attend the [[Sangreal]] quest, enchantment is morally ambivalent: it may serve good or ill ends, be wielded by virtuous or villainous figures, and its effects on those subjected to it range from benign protection to catastrophic destruction.

## The Practitioners of Enchantment

Enchantment in Malory is the province of a small number of named figures, each with a distinct relationship to the art. [[Merlin]] is the supreme enchanter, using his powers primarily in service of [[King Arthur]]'s destiny — casting protective spells, engineering strategic deceptions, and shaping events to[[battle|war]]d prophesied outcomes:

> "Merlin cast an enchantment to the duke of Tintagil" (027-006)

[[Morgan le Fay]] represents enchantment's darker dimension, employing magic for personal [[revenge|vengeance]], political manipulation, and the destruction of her enemies:

> "by enchantment and sorcery she hath been the destroyer of many good knights" (034-024)

Other enchanters — [[Dame Nyneve]], [[Sir Palomides]]'s unnamed enchantress, and the various sorceresses who populate the adventure [[realm|land]]scape — fill out a spectrum from protective to predatory magic.

## The Mechanics of Enchantment

Malory's enchantments typically operate through two mechanisms: illusion (altering what a victim perceives) and compulsion (altering what a victim does). Illusion enchantments create false appearances — a scabbard that resembles [[Excalibur]]'s, a knight who appears to be someone else, a castle that vanishes:

> "made another scabbard like it by enchantment" (042-027)

Compulsion enchantments override the victim's will, causing them to act against their own des[[wrath|ire]]s — to sleep, to [[battle|fight]], to fall in love, or to remain in a place against their will:

> "threw an enchantment on King Pellinore and made him sleep" (027-001)

## Enchantment and Agency

A recurring narrative concern in Malory's treatment of enchantment is its effect on knightly agency. When a knight acts under enchantment, is he morally responsible for his actions? The text generally answers yes — enchantment may explain conduct but does not excuse it from narrative judgment. This treatment preserves the chivalric world's emphasis on personal responsibility even in the presence of forces that seem to undermine free will.

The tension is most acute in the case of [[Sir Launcelot]]'s conception of [[Sir Galahad]], which occurs while [[sir-launcelot|Launcelot]] is under an enchantment that makes him believe he is with [[Queen Guenever]]. The text treats this as a morally significant act despite the enchantment — [[sir-launcelot|Launcelot]]'s willingness to betray [[king-arthur|Arthur]] is real even if the specific circumstances are illusory.

## Enchantment versus Divine Power

The work maintains a clear hierarchy between enchantment and divine power. Enchantment, however powerful, is ultimately subordinate to the will of God. The [[Sangreal]] quest demonstrates this subordination repeatedly: enchantments that have held for centuries dissolve at the approach of [[Sir Galahad]], whose purity renders him immune to magical deception. This hierarchy reflects the work's broader theological framework, in which chivalry's highest form is not magical but sacred.

## Enchantment's Narrative Function

Enchantment serves as Malory's mechanism for introducing the [[miracle|marvel]]ous into an otherwise realistic narrative of [[battle|combat]] and politics. It enables plot developments that would be impossible within the logic of ordinary causation — disguises that cannot be penetrated, sleeps that cannot be broken, transformations that alter the very terms of identity. More importantly, enchantment externalizes internal states: a knight's moral confusion becomes a magical fog; a lady's des[[wrath|ire]] becomes a binding spell. This externalization makes the invisible dynamics of character and motivation visible as narrative action.
