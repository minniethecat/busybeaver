[156-001]Then when this was done there was drawing of swords, and then there began a

[156-002]sore tournament. And there did Sir Lamorak [[marvel|marvellous]] deeds of arms; and

[156-003]betwixt Sir Lamorak and Sir Ironside, that was the Red Knight of the Red

[156-004]Launds, there was strong battle; and betwixt Sir Palamides and Bleoberis there

[156-005]was a strong battle; and [[sir-gawaine|Sir Gawaine]] and [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]] met, and there Sir

[156-006]Gawaine had the worse, for he pulled Sir Gawaine from his horse, and there he

[156-007]was long upon foot, and defouled. Then came in [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]], and he smote Sir

[156-008]Turquine, and he him; and then came Sir Carados his brother, and both at once

[156-009]they assailed him, and he as the most noblest knight of the world worshipfully

[156-010]fought with them both, that all men wondered of the noblesse of Sir Launcelot.

[156-011]And then came in [[sir-gareth|Sir Gareth]], and knew that it was Sir Launcelot that fought

[156-012]with the two perilous knights. And then Sir Gareth came with his good horse and

[156-013]hurtled them in-sunder, and no stroke would he smite to Sir Launcelot. That

[156-014]espied Sir Launcelot, and deemed it should be the good knight Sir Gareth: and

[156-015]then Sir Gareth rode here and there, and smote on the right hand and on the

[156-016]left hand, and all the folk might well espy where that he rode. And by fortune

[156-017]he met with his brother Sir Gawaine, and there he put Sir Gawaine to the worse,

[156-018]for he put off his helm, and so he served five or six knights of the Round

[156-019]Table, that all men said he put him in the most pain, and best he did his

[156-020]devoir. For when Sir Tristram beheld him how he first jousted and after fought

[156-021]so well with a sword, then he rode unto Sir Ironside and to Sir Persant of

[156-022]Inde, and asked them, by their faith, What manner a knight is yonder knight

[156-023]that seemeth in so many divers colours? Truly, meseemeth, said Tristram, that

[156-024]he putteth himself in great pain, for he never ceaseth. Wot ye not what he is?

[156-025]said Sir Ironside. No, said Sir Tristram. Then shall ye know that this is he

[156-026]that loveth the lady of the castle, and she him again; and this is he that won

[156-027]me when I besieged the lady of this castle, and this is he that won Sir Persant

[156-028]of Inde, and his three brethren. What is his name, said Sir Tristram, and of

[156-029]what blood is he come? He was called in [[camelot|the court of King Arthur]], Beaumains,

[156-030]but his right name is Sir Gareth of Orkney, brother to Sir Gawaine. By my head,

[156-031]said Sir Tristram, he is a good knight, and a big man of arms, and if he be

[156-032]young he shall prove a full noble knight. He is but a child, they all said, and

[156-033]of Sir Launcelot he was made knight. Therefore he is mickle the better, said

[156-034]Tristram. And then Sir Tristram, Sir Ironside, Sir Persant, and his brother,

[156-035]rode together for to help Sir Gareth; and then there were given many strong

[156-036]strokes.

[156-037]And then Sir Gareth rode out on the one side to amend his helm; and then said

[156-038]his dwarf: Take me your ring, that ye lose it not while that ye drink. And so

[156-039]when he had drunk he gat on his helm, and eagerly took his horse and rode into

[156-040]the [[battle|field]], and left his ring with his dwarf; and the dwarf was glad the ring

[156-041]was from him, for then he wist well he should be known. And then when Sir

[156-042]Gareth was in the field all folks saw him well and plainly that he was in

[156-043]yellow colours; and there he rased off helms and pulled down knights, that King

[156-044][[king-arthur|Arthur]] had marvel what knight he was, for the king saw by his hair that it was

[156-045]the same knight.
