# King Leodegrance

King Leodegrance was the ruler of Cameliard, the father of [[Queen Guenever]], and one of [[King Arthur]]'s earliest allies. His most enduring contribution to the Arthurian world was the gift of the [[Round Table]] itself, bestowed upon Arthur as a wedding present when the young king married Leodegrance's daughter.

## The King of Cameliard

Leodegrance held the kingdom of Cameliard, a realm in North Wales that bordered the territories of Arthur's early campaigns. The chronicle records that "North Wales made great war on King Leodegrance of Cameliard" (020-074), testifying to the embattled position of his kingdom. Surrounded by hostile powers, Leodegrance turned to the young King Arthur for protection — an alliance that would reshape the political landscape of Britain.

## The Rescue by Arthur's Champions

When Leodegrance's kingdom was overrun by enemies, Arthur's knights came to his aid. "[[King Bors]] rescued King Leodegrance" (021-001) in the campaign of Cameliard, and "there rescued King Leodegrance, and slew there much people" (021-004), turning back the forces that threatened his realm. The three kings — Arthur, [[King Ban]], and King Bors — received "great cheer of King Leodegrance, that thanked them of their great goodness" (021-006), cementing a bond of gratitude and loyalty.

The rescue established Leodegrance as a steadfast ally. While other kings rebelled against Arthur's authority, Leodegrance remained loyal. His gratitude was not merely personal but dynastic: the alliance he forged with Arthur would culminate in the marriage that defined the Arthurian age.

## The Father of Queen Guenever

Leodegrance's daughter Guenever was the most consequential marriage in Arthurian legend. When Arthur's counsellors urged him to take a wife, the king declared his choice: Leodegrance's "daughter. That is to me, said King Leodegrance, the best news" (052-027). The king of Cameliard welcomed the match with joy, recognising in it the culmination of the alliance he had long cultivated.

Leodegrance had no son to inherit his kingdom, and he saw in Arthur not only a son-in-law but a protector who would secure Cameliard's future. His daughter's elevation to the queenship of Britain was the highest honour a provincial king could achieve.

## The Gift of the Round Table

Leodegrance's most famous contribution to Arthurian lore was his wedding gift. The Round Table — that great symbol of chivalric equality and fellowship — had originally belonged to [[Uther Pendragon]], Arthur's father. Upon Uther's death, the table passed to Leodegrance, who held it in trust.

When Arthur married Guenever, Leodegrance bestowed the table upon the young king. This gift was more than a piece of furniture; it was the physical embodiment of the chivalric order that would define Arthur's reign. Every knight who later sat at the Round Table owed his place, in a sense, to the generosity of King Leodegrance of Cameliard.

## The Gift That Shaped an Age

King Leodegrance is a figure defined by gifts — first his daughter, then the Round Table — to the king who would define his age. His story is that of the loyal ally whose contributions, made without fanfare, proved more enduring than the battlefield triumphs of more famous kings. The Round Table that he bestowed became the very symbol of Arthurian chivalry, and the daughter he gave in marriage became the queen whose love story would bring that chivalry to its tragic end.
