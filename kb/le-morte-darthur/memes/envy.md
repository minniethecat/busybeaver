# Envy

**Envy** in [[Le Morte d'Arthur]] is among the most corrosive of all passions — a private resentment that, once [[kinsman|kin]]dled between [[knights]], spreads out[[battle|war]]d into [[feud]], [[treason|betrayal]], and the unravelling of the [[Round Table]]'s fellowship. Unlike [[wrath]], which is hot and visible, envy operates in whispers and sidelong g[[spear|lances]], poisoning relationships before its victims know they are marked.

## Envy Between Knights

The competitive structure of [[chivalry]] makes envy almost inevitable. When one knight wins greater [[honour|worship]] at a [[tournament]] or receives a higher place at the [[Round Table]], those he displaces feel the sting:

> Thus was there great envy betwixt Tramtrist and Sir... (172-014)

The naming of envy as "great" signals that this is not a minor irritation but a force capable of driving the plot. The envy between [[Sir Tristram]] (disguised as Tramtrist) and his rival is the seed from which later violence grows. When [[courage|prowess]] is the measure of a man, another's [[courage|prowess]] is a standing [[shame|reproach]].

## Envy and Kinship Betrayed

Envy is at its most destructive when it strikes within a family. [[Sir Gawaine]]'s brother [[Gaheris]] is moved by envy as much as by [[worship|honour]]:

> envy and told Gaheris his brother... (055-014)

That envy flows between brothers — the closest bond in the chivalric world after the oath of [[fealty]] — reveals how profoundly the emotion can corrupt even the most sacred ties. When a brother envies a brother, the household itself is no longer safe.

## Envy as Political Poison

At court, envy transforms into faction. The knights who resent another's favour with the [[kinsman|kin]]g do not always act openly:

> they ordained for hate and envy for... (192-007)

"Hate and envy" are pa[[wrath|ire]]d as the twin motivations for conspiracy. Where hate supplies the energy, envy supplies the pretext: the envious man convinces himself that the object of his envy does not deserve what he has, and from there it is a short step to plotting his downfall. The political history of the [[king-arthur|Arthur]]ian court is, in significant part, a history of envy transmuted into action.

## Envy and the Grail Quest

The [[Sangreal]] quest, for all its spiritual elevation, does not escape the shadow of envy. Knights who are not chosen to achieve the Grail must contend with the knowledge that another has been found worthier. The Grail tests not only purity but the capacity to rejoice in another's grace — and those who fail that test fail through envy of the chosen.

## Envy's Narrative Function

Envy in Malory is the counter-force to fellowship. If [[love]] and [[honour]] bind the [[king-arthur|Arthur]]ian world together, envy is what pulls it apart. Every act of envy — a whispered s[[realm|land]]er, a challenged place, a withheld recognition — is a small cut in the fabric of the [[round-table|Round Table]]. Cumulatively, these cuts weaken the whole until it cannot withstand the larger blows of [[treason]] and [[battle|war]]. Envy does not kill [[king-arthur|King Arthur]]; but it leaves him with fewer to defend him when the killing comes.
