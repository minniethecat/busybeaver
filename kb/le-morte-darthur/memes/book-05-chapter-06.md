[101-001]other to Lucius, and how they were assailed and escaped with [[honour|worship]].

[101-002]Then the king did do call [[sir-gawaine|Sir Gawaine]], [[sir-bors|Sir Bors]], Sir Lionel, and Sir Bedivere,

[101-003]and commanded them to go straight to Sir Lucius, and say ye to him that hastily

[101-004]he remove out of my land; and if he will not, bid him make him ready to battle

[101-005]and not distress the poor people. Then anon these noble knights dressed them to

[101-006]horseback, and when they came to the green wood, they saw many pavilions set in

[101-007]a meadow, of silk of divers colours, beside a river, and the emperor’s

[101-008]pavilion was in the middle with an eagle displayed above. To the which tent our

[101-009]knights rode toward, and ordained Sir Gawaine and Sir Bors to do the message,

[101-010]and left in a bushment Sir Lionel and Sir Bedivere. And then Sir Gawaine and

[101-011]Sir Bors did their message, and commanded Lucius, in [[king-arthur|Arthur]]’s name to

[101-012]avoid his land, or shortly to address him to battle. To whom Lucius answered

[101-013]and said, Ye shall return to your lord, and say ye to him that I shall subdue

[101-014]him and all his lands. Then Sir Gawaine was wroth and said, I had liefer than

[101-015]all France [[battle|fight]] against thee; and so had I, said Sir Bors, liefer than all

[101-016]Brittany or Burgoyne.

[101-017]Then a knight named Sir Gainus, nigh cousin to the emperor, said, Lo, how these

[101-018]Britons be full of pride and boast, and they brag as though they bare up all

[101-019]the world. Then Sir Gawaine was sore grieved with these words, and pulled out

[101-020]his sword and smote off his head. And therewith turned their horses and rode

[101-021]over waters and through woods till they came to their bushment, whereas Sir

[101-022]Lionel and Sir Bedivere were hoving. The Romans followed fast after, on

[101-023]horseback and on foot, over a champaign unto a wood; then Sir Bors turned his

[101-024]horse and saw a knight come fast on, whom he smote through the body with a

[101-025]spear that he fell dead down to the earth; then came Caliburn one of the

[101-026]strongest of Pavie, and smote down many of Arthur’s knights. And when Sir

[101-027]Bors saw him do so much harm, he addressed toward him, and smote him through

[101-028]the breast, that he fell down dead to the earth. Then Sir Feldenak thought to

[101-029]revenge the death of Gainus upon Sir Gawaine, but Sir Gawaine was ware thereof,

[101-030]and smote him on the head, which stroke stinted not till it came to his breast.

[101-031]And then he returned and came to his fellows in the bushment. And there was a

[101-032]recounter, for the bushment brake on the Romans, and slew and hew down the

[101-033]Romans, and forced the Romans to flee and return, whom the noble knights chased

[101-034]unto their tents.

[101-035]Then the Romans gathered more people, and also footmen came on, and there was a

[101-036]new battle, and so much people that Sir Bors and Sir Berel were taken. But when

[101-037]Sir Gawaine saw that, he took with him Sir Idrus the good knight, and said he

[101-038]would never see King Arthur but if he rescued them, and pulled out Galatine his

[101-039]good sword, and followed them that led those two knights away; and he smote him

[101-040]that led Sir Bors, and took Sir Bors from him and delivered him to his fellows.

[101-041]And Sir Idrus in likewise rescued Sir Berel. Then began the battle to be great,

[101-042]that our knights were in great jeopardy, wherefore Sir Gawaine sent to King

[101-043]Arthur for succour, and that he hie him, for I am sore wounded, and that our

[101-044]prisoners may pay goods out of number. And the messenger came to the king and

[101-045]told him his message. And anon the king did do assemble his army, but anon, or

[101-046]he departed the prisoners were come, and Sir Gawaine and his fellows gat the

[101-047]field and put the Romans to flight, and after returned and came with their

[101-048]fellowship in such wise that no man of worship was lost of them, save that Sir

[101-049]Gawaine was sore hurt. Then the king did do ransack his wounds and comforted

[101-050]him. And thus was the beginning of the first journey of the Britons and Romans,

[101-051]and there were slain of the Romans more than ten thousand, and great joy and

[101-052]mirth was made that night in the host of King Arthur. And on the morn he sent

[101-053]all the prisoners into Paris under the guard of [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]], with many

[101-054]knights, and of Sir Cador.
