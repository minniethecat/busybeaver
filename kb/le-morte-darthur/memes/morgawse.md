# Queen Morgawse

**Queen Morgawse** of Orkney is a figure of dynastic consequence in Thomas Malory's *Le Morte d'[[King Arthur]]*. As the half-sister of [[King Arthur]] and the mother of five sons — [[Sir Gawaine]], [[Sir Gareth]], [[Gaheris]], Sir Agravaine, and [[Mordred]] — she stands at the crossroads of the Arthurian bloodline, her body the channel through which the royal house of [[Camelot]] and the martial house of Orkney are joined. Though she appears directly in only a handful of scenes, the web of kinship she anchors shapes the destiny of the Round Table.

## Queen of Orkney

Morgawse is introduced in her majesty: "there came in the Queen of Orkney, with ladies and damosels" (152-003), entering the Arthurian court with the full dignity of her station. She is the daughter of [[Igraine]] and the Duke of Cornwall, making her [[King Arthur]]'s half-sister through their mother. Her husband, King Lot of Orkney, is one of the great lords of the realm, and their marriage produces the most formidable fraternal bloc in Arthurian [[chivalry]].

## Mother of Five Knights

Morgawse's principal role in the narrative is as the mother of the Orkney brothers. When [[Sir Gareth]] comes to court in disguise, his true identity is traced through his mother: "Queen of Orkney, Sir Gareth's mother" (154-046). Her maternal recognition of Gareth carries emotional weight: "with that came his mother, the Queen of Orkney, Dame Morgawse, and when she saw Sir Gareth she swooned" (160-044–045). This swoon — a mother overcome at the sight of her son, now a proven knight — is one of the few moments of unguarded maternal feeling in the Morte.

Her relationship with King Arthur is marked by the formal [[courtesy]] of court: "Sir, said the Queen of Orkney unto King Arthur her brother" (152-029). In calling him both "Sir" and "brother," Morgawse navigates the twin registers of fealty and [[kinsman|kinship]] that define the Orkney clan's position — bound to Arthur by blood, yet always a distinct and potent political force.

## The Shadow of Mordred

Morgawse's legacy is darkened by the circumstances of [[Mordred]]'s birth. In the Morte, Arthur fathers Mordred upon Morgawse without knowing she is his half-sister — a union that produces both a son and, ultimately, the destruction of the kingdom. When accusations fly against the Orkney clan, Morgawse's name is invoked as a point of defence: "I say nay thereto, for Queen Morgawse of Orkney, mother to Sir Gawaine" (216-015). Her sons, particularly [[Sir Gawaine]], defend her [[honour]] fiercely: "he said Queen Morgawse of Orkney was the causer of no such thing" (216-031).

Yet the shadow persists. When a knight speaks ill of the Orkney bloodline, the response is swift and violent: "wit ye well Queen Morgawse of Orkney was causer of none such" — and the speaker is struck down (216-039). Morgawse's name becomes a flashpoint, a sign under which battles are fought and loyalties tested.

## The Dynastic Anchor

Morgawse's significance in the Morte is structural rather than dramatic. She does not ride into [[battle]], cast [[enchantment]], or deliver [[counsel]]. Her power lies in what she connects: the royal line of [[Igraine]] to the martial vigour of Orkney, the love of a mother to the deeds of her sons, the [[honour]] of a queen to the doom that her youngest child brings upon the kingdom. She is the still point around which the Orkney tragedy turns.
