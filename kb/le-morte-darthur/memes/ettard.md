# Ettard

The Lady Ettard was a noblewoman of [[King Arthur]]'s Britain whose story is one of the most poignant romantic tragedies in [[Le Morte d'Arthur]]. Beloved desperately by the knight [[Sir Pelleas]], she scorned his devotion with cold disdain. Through the enchantment of the [[Damosel of the Lake]], she was made to fall in love with Pelleas only after betraying him — a revelation that drove her to die of grief. Her tale explores the dark intersections of love, pride, and magical compulsion in the Arthurian world.

## The Devotion of Sir Pelleas

The knight Sir Pelleas loved Ettard with an all-consuming passion. Her name was known throughout the country, and Pelleas, upon seeing her, was utterly overcome (086-050). He worshipped her from afar, and when he came before her, he gave her a golden circlet as a token of his devotion (086-059). Yet Ettard treated his love with contempt, refusing to acknowledge him and scorning every gesture of his service (088-017). Pelleas endured her rejection without resentment, remaining steadfast in his love despite her cruelty.

## The Treachery of Sir Gawaine

[[Sir Gawaine]], chancing upon the lovelorn Pelleas, offered to intercede on his behalf. Gawaine went to Ettard and persuaded her that Pelleas was dead to her; he then asked her to remove his helm that he might see her face. When she did so, Gawaine deceived her, presenting himself as a worthier suitor (088-008). That night, Gawaine and the Lady Ettard went to bed together, betraying Pelleas utterly (088-027). When Pelleas found Gawaine lying in bed with his Lady Ettard, he laid his naked sword across their throats and departed — a gesture of both vengeance and mercy (088-038).

## The Enchantment and Awakening

The [[Damosel of the Lake]], [[Dame Nyneve]], intervened in the tragedy. She cast a spell upon Ettard, causing her to fall desperately in love with the very knight she had spurned. When Ettard awoke and was brought by the damosel to where Pelleas lay sleeping, she beheld him and was filled with love and remorse (088-085). Ettard lamented her fate, crying out how it had befallen her that she now loved the man she had so cruelly rejected (088-083). But Pelleas, waking and looking upon Ettard, felt only revulsion for the woman who had scorned him — the enchantment had freed him from his love.

## Death of Sorrow

The reversal of affections proved fatal for the Lady Ettard. Pelleas, now liberated from his passion, declared that Ettard had done him great wrong and that he would have no more to do with her (089-004). Ettard, consumed by the unrequited love that the enchantment had kindled in her breast, could not endure the pain of rejection. The Lady Ettard died of sorrow, a victim of her own former cruelty made unbearable by the magical compulsion that had turned her heart against her (089-010).
