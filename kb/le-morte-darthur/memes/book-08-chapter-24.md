[187-001]Isoud for [[king-mark|King Mark]], and how [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]] and Isoud drank the love drink.

[187-002]Then upon a day King Anguish asked Sir Tristram why he asked not his boon, for

[187-003]whatsomever he had promised him he should have it without fail. Sir, said Sir

[187-004]Tristram, now is it time; this is all that I will desire, that ye will give me

[187-005]La Beale Isoud, your daughter, not for myself, but for mine uncle, King Mark,

[187-006]that shall have her to wife, for so have I promised him. Alas, said the king, I

[187-007]had liefer than all the land that I have ye would wed her yourself. Sir, an I

[187-008]did then I were shamed for ever in this world, and false of my promise.

[187-009]Therefore, said Sir I Tristram, I pray you hold your promise that ye promised

[187-010]me; for this is my desire, that ye will give me La Beale Isoud to go with me

[187-011]into Cornwall for to be wedded to King Mark, mine uncle. As for that, said King

[187-012]Anguish, ye shall have her with you to do with her what it please you; that is

[187-013]for to say if that ye list to wed her yourself, that is me liefest, and if ye

[187-014]will give her unto King Mark, your uncle, that is in your choice. So, to make

[187-015]short conclusion, La Beale Isoud was made ready to go with Sir Tristram, and

[187-016]Dame Bragwaine went with her for her chief gentlewoman, with many other.

[187-017]Then the queen, Isoud’s mother, gave to her and Dame Bragwaine, her

[187-018]daughter’s gentlewoman, and unto Gouvernail, a drink, and charged them

[187-019]that what day King Mark should wed, that same day they should give him that

[187-020]drink, so that King Mark should drink to La Beale Isoud, and then, said the

[187-021]queen, I undertake either shall love other the days of their life. So this

[187-022]drink was given unto Dame Bragwaine, and unto Gouvernail. And then anon Sir

[187-023]Tristram took the sea, and La Beale Isoud; and when they were in their cabin,

[187-024]it happed so that they were thirsty, and they saw a little flasket of gold

[187-025]stand by them, and it seemed by the colour and the taste that it was noble

[187-026]wine. Then Sir Tristram took the flasket in his hand, and said, Madam Isoud,

[187-027]here is the best drink that ever ye drank, that Dame Bragwaine, your maiden,

[187-028]and Gouvernail, my servant, have kept for themselves. Then they laughed and

[187-029]made good cheer, and either drank to other freely, and they thought never drink

[187-030]that ever they drank to other was so sweet nor so good. But by that their drink

[187-031]was in their bodies, they loved either other so well that never their love

[187-032]departed for weal neither for woe. And thus it happed the love first betwixt

[187-033]Sir Tristram and La Beale Isoud, the which love never departed the days of

[187-034]their life.

[187-035]So then they sailed till by fortune they came nigh a castle that hight Pluere,

[187-036]and thereby arrived for to repose them, weening to them to have had good

[187-037]harbourage. But anon as Sir Tristram was within the castle they were taken

[187-038]prisoners; for the custom of the castle was such; who that rode by that castle

[187-039]and brought any lady, he must needs [[battle|fight]] with the lord, that hight Breunor.

[187-040]And if it were so that Breunor won the field, then should the knight stranger

[187-041]and his lady be put to death, what that ever they were; and if it were so that

[187-042]the strange knight won the field of Sir Breunor, then should he die and his

[187-043]lady both. This custom was used many winters, for it was called the Castle

[187-044]Pluere, that is to say the Weeping Castle.
