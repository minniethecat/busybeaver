[062-004]Then King Pellinore armed him and mounted upon his horse, and rode more than a

[062-005]pace after the lady that the knight led away. And as he rode in a forest, he

[062-006]saw in a valley a damosel sit by a well, and a wounded knight in her arms, and

[062-007]Pellinore saluted her. And when she was ware of him, she cried overloud, Help

[062-008]me, knight; for Christ’s sake, King Pellinore. And he would not tarry, he

[062-009]was so eager in his quest, and ever she cried an hundred times after help. When

[062-010]she saw he would not abide, she prayed unto God to send him as much need of

[062-011]help as she had, and that he might feel it or he died. So, as the book telleth,

[062-012]the knight there died that there was wounded, wherefore the lady for pure

[062-013]sorrow slew herself with his sword. As King Pellinore rode in that valley he

[062-014]met with a poor man, a labourer. Sawest thou not, said Pellinore, a knight

[062-015]riding and leading away a lady? Yea, said the man, I saw that knight, and the

[062-016]lady that made great dole; and yonder beneath in a valley there shall ye see

[062-017]two pavilions, and one of the knights of the pavilions challenged that lady of

[062-018]that knight, and said she was his cousin near, wherefore he should lead her no

[062-019]farther. And so they waged battle in that quarrel, the one said he would have

[062-020]her by force, and the other said he would have the rule of her, by cause he was

[062-021]her kinsman, and would lead her to her kin. For this quarrel he left them

[062-022]fighting. And if ye will ride a pace ye shall find them fighting, and the lady

[062-023]was beleft with the two squires in the pavilions. God thank thee, said King

[062-024]Pellinore.

[062-025]Then he rode a wallop till he had a sight of the two pavilions, and the two

[062-026]knights fighting. Anon he rode unto the pavilions, and saw the lady that was

[062-027]his quest, and said, Fair lady, ye must go with me unto the court of King

[062-028][[king-arthur|Arthur]]. Sir knight, said the two squires that were with her, yonder are two

[062-029]knights that [[battle|fight]] for this lady, go thither and depart them, and be agreed

[062-030]with them, and then may ye have her at your pleasure. Ye say well, said King

[062-031]Pellinore. And anon he rode betwixt them, and departed them, and asked them the

[062-032]causes why that they fought? Sir knight, said the one, I shall tell you, this

[062-033]lady is my kinswoman nigh, mine aunt’s daughter, and when I heard her

[062-034]complain that she was with him maugre her head, I waged battle to fight with

[062-035]him. Sir knight, said the other, whose name was Hontzlake of Wentland, and this

[062-036]lady I gat by my [[courage|prowess]] of arms this day at Arthur’s court. That is

[062-037]untruly said, said King Pellinore, for ye came in suddenly there as we were at

[062-038]the high feast, and took away this lady or any man might make him ready; and

[062-039]therefore it was my quest to bring her again and you both, or else the one of

[062-040]us to abide in the field; therefore the lady shall go with me, or I will die

[062-041]for it, for I have promised it King Arthur. And therefore fight ye no more, for

[062-042]none of you shall have no part of her at this time; and if ye list to fight for

[062-043]her, fight with me, and I will defend her. Well, said the knights, make you

[062-044]ready, and we shall assail you with all our power. And as King Pellinore would

[062-045]have put his horse from them, Sir Hontzlake rove his horse through with a

[062-046]sword, and said: Now art thou on foot as well as we are. When King Pellinore

[062-047]espied that his horse was slain, lightly he leapt from his horse and pulled out

[062-048]his sword, and put his shield afore him, and said, Knight, keep well thy head,

[062-049]for thou shalt have a buffet for the slaying of my horse. So King Pellinore

[062-050]gave him such a stroke upon the helm that he clave the head down to the chin,

[062-051]that he fell to the earth dead.
