# Sir Frol

Sir Frol, called Sir Frol of the Out Isles, was a knight who encountered Sir Lamorak de Galis and Sir Launcelot on the road to Arthur's court. Impetuous and proud, he was eventually slain in combat by Sir Lamorak, leading his brother Sir Belliance to seek revenge.

## Meeting Sir Lamorak

As Sir Lamorak rode toward Arthur's court, he came upon Sir Frol beset by four attackers. Lamorak rode to his rescue, slaying two of them at two strokes and driving the other two to flight (202-086). Sir Frol thanked him and gave his name: Sir Frol of the Out Isles. He then rode with Lamorak and bore him company.

## The Joust with the White Knight

As they rode together, they saw a seemly knight riding against them, all in white. Sir Frol declared that this knight had jousted with him late and smote him down, and he meant to joust with him again (202-089). Sir Lamorak counselled against it, observing by the knight's countenance that he should be a noble knight of the Table Round. He asked Frol whether the earlier joust was at his request or the White Knight's.

Sir Frol admitted he had jousted at his own request. Lamorak advised him to deal no more with such a knight, but Frol would not spare (202-095). He cried out a challenge, and though the White Knight had no lust to joust, they feutred their spears. The White Knight overthrew Sir Frol and rode away at a soft pace (202-098).

The White Knight proved to be Sir Launcelot, who revealed his name to Lamorak upon a covenant of secrecy (202-108).

## Parting from Lamorak

After Lamorak helped Frol to horse again, Frol demanded to know the White Knight's name. Lamorak refused, saying it was not for Frol to know and no point of his charge (202-109). Frol called him the more uncourteous and declared he would depart from Lamorak. Lamorak let him go, saying that by his company Frol had saved the fairest flower of his garland — his life would have been forfeit had he jousted again with Launcelot (202-111).

## Death and Vengeance

Within two or three days, Sir Lamorak encountered a knight sleeping at a well. The two fought, and Lamorak slew the knight, who proved to be Sir Frol (202-133). Shortly after, Frol's brother Sir Belliance overtook Lamorak, crying that Lamorak had slain his brother Frol, a better knight than Lamorak ever was (202-140). The two fought mightily for two hours before Belliance learned Lamorak's name and, though he hated Lamorak for other grievances, they were eventually reconciled.
