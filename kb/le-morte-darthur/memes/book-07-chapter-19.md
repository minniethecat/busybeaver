[146-001]when he came to the castle the gates were closed against him, and of the words

[146-002]that the lady said to him.

[146-003]Now turn we unto Sir [[sir-gareth|Beaumains]] that desired of Linet that he might see her

[146-004]sister, his lady. Sir, she said, I would fain ye saw her. Then Sir Beaumains

[146-005]all armed him, and took his horse and his spear, and rode straight unto the

[146-006]castle. And when he came to the gate he found there many men armed, and pulled

[146-007]up the drawbridge and drew the port close.

[146-008]Then marvelled he why they would not suffer him to enter. And then he looked up

[146-009]to the window; and there he saw the fair Lionesse that said on high: Go thy

[146-010]way, Sir Beaumains, for as yet thou shalt not have wholly my love, unto the

[146-011]time that thou be called one of the number of the worthy knights. And therefore

[146-012]go labour in [[honour|worship]] this twelvemonth, and then thou shalt hear new tidings.

[146-013]Alas, fair lady, said Beaumains, I have not deserved that ye should show me

[146-014]this strangeness, and I had weened that I should have right good cheer with

[146-015]you, and unto my power I have deserved thank, and well I am sure I have bought

[146-016]your love with part of the best blood within my body. Fair courteous knight,

[146-017]said Dame Lionesse, be not displeased nor over-hasty; for wit you well your

[146-018]great travail nor good love shall not be lost, for I consider your great

[146-019]travail and labour, your bounty and your goodness as me ought to do. And

[146-020]therefore go on your way, and look that ye be of good comfort, for all shall be

[146-021]for your worship and for the best, and perdy a twelvemonth will soon be done,

[146-022]and trust me, fair knight, I shall be true to you, and never to betray you, but

[146-023]to my death I shall love you and none other. And therewithal she turned her

[146-024]from the window, and Sir Beaumains rode awayward from the castle, making great

[146-025]dole, and so he rode here and there and wist not where he rode, till it was

[146-026]dark night. And then it happened him to come to a poor man’s house, and

[146-027]there he was harboured all that night.

[146-028]But Sir Beaumains had no rest, but wallowed and writhed for the love of the

[146-029]lady of the castle. And so upon the morrow he took his horse and rode until

[146-030]underne, and then he came to a broad water, and thereby was a great lodge, and

[146-031]there he alighted to sleep and laid his head upon the shield, and betook his

[146-032]horse to the dwarf, and commanded him to watch all night.

[146-033]Now turn we to the lady of the same castle, that thought much upon Beaumains,

[146-034]and then she called unto her Sir Gringamore her brother, and prayed him in all

[146-035]manner, as he loved her heartily, that he would ride after Sir Beaumains: And

[146-036]ever have ye wait upon him till ye may find him sleeping, for I am sure in his

[146-037]heaviness he will alight down in some place, and lie him down to sleep; and

[146-038]therefore have ye your wait upon him, and in the priviest manner ye can, take

[146-039]his dwarf, and go ye your way with him as fast as ever ye may or Sir Beaumains

[146-040]awake. For my sister Linet telleth me that he can tell of what kindred he is

[146-041]come, and what is his right name. And the meanwhile I and my sister will ride

[146-042]unto your castle to await when ye bring with you the dwarf. And then when ye

[146-043]have brought him unto your castle, I will have him in examination myself. Unto

[146-044]the time that I know what is his right name, and of what kindred he is come,

[146-045]shall I never be merry at my heart. Sister, said Sir Gringamore, all this shall

[146-046]be done after your intent.

[146-047]And so he rode all the other day and the night till that he found Sir Beaumains

[146-048]lying by a water, and his head upon his shield, for to sleep. And then when he

[146-049]saw Sir Beaumains fast asleep, he came stilly stalking behind the dwarf, and

[146-050]plucked him fast under his arm, and so he rode away with him as fast as ever he

[146-051]might unto his own castle. And this Sir Gringamore’s arms were all black,

[146-052]and that to him longeth. But ever as he rode with the dwarf toward his castle,

[146-053]he cried unto his lord and prayed him of help. And therewith awoke Sir

[146-054]Beaumains, and up he leapt lightly, and saw where Sir Gringamore rode his way

[146-055]with the dwarf, and so Sir Gringamore rode out of his sight.
