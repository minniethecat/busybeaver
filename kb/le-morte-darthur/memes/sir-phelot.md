# Sir Phelot

Sir Phelot was a knight associated with the woodland and forest, whose recorded story centres upon an ambush laid for [[Sir Launcelot|Sir Launcelot du Lake]]. The chronicle presents him as a knight of the wild places — one who knew the forest paths and used them to his advantage against the greatest knight of the age.

## The Knight of the Forest

Phelot is introduced as "Phelot, a knight that longeth unto the" (124-015) forest, a knight whose domain was not the court or the castle but the woodland. This association with the wild marks him as one of those knights who dwelt beyond the ordered world of Camelot, governed by the laws of the hunt and the ambush rather than the round table.

When the moment of action came, "therewithal came out Sir Phelot out" (124-023) of hiding, springing upon his quarry from the cover of the trees. The suddenness of his appearance — bursting from concealment — reveals the tactics of the forest knight.

## The Ambush of Launcelot

Phelot's most significant act was his ambush of Sir Launcelot. The chronicle records a tense exchange between the two. When Launcelot questioned the nature of the trap, Phelot's responses were curt and unyielding: "She hath done, said Sir Phelot, but as I" (124-027) deflecting inquiry with the hard economy of a man who had come to fight, not to parley.

Launcelot, finding himself at disadvantage, sought terms. But Phelot refused any quarter: "said Sir Phelot, and therefore help thyself" (124-031), offering no mercy. When Launcelot pressed again, Phelot was adamant: "Nay, nay, said Sir Phelot, for I" (124-034) would not relent. The forest knight had the greatest warrior of the age at a disadvantage, and he meant to press it.

## The Combat

The fight that followed was fierce. "Phelot lashed at him eagerly" (124-041), the chronicle says, the word "eagerly" capturing the hunger with which the forest knight attacked. Yet the outcome — whatever it was — did not elevate Phelot to the company of the great. He appears in this single episode and then vanishes from the record, a knight of one encounter, defined entirely by his ambush of Launcelot.

## The Knight of One Encounter

The Arthurian chronicle is populated by many such knights — figures who flash across the narrative in a single episode and then are gone. Sir Phelot is among them: a knight of the forest who knew the paths of ambush, who struck at the greatest knight of the age with eagerness, and who thereafter passes from the record. His name may appear in an earlier list alongside Dodinas le Savage — "Sir Felot" (091-050) — suggesting a wider place in the Round Table fellowship, but his recorded deeds belong to a single woodland encounter that tested even Sir Launcelot.
