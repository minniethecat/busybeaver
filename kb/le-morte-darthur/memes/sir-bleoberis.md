# Sir Bleoberis

Sir Bleoberis de Ganis was a Knight of the [[Round Table]] and a cousin of [[Sir Launcelot]], part of the extensive kinship network that surrounded the greatest knight of Arthur's court. His most notable adventure involved the taking of Sir Segwarides's lady, a episode that entangled him with [[Sir Tristram]] and illustrated the complex chivalric codes of love and honour in [[Le Morte d'Arthur]].

## The Adventure of Sir Segwarides's Lady

Bleoberis's defining narrative episode began at [[King Arthur]]'s court when he declared: "I will have the fairest lady" (178-009), asserting an ancient custom of the court that permitted a knight to claim any lady and depart unchallenged. He chose Sir Segwarides's wife, provoking a confrontation. When Segwarides attempted to rescue his lady, "Sir Bleoberis overthrew him and sore hath wounded him" (178-031), demonstrating the martial prowess that made him a formidable opponent.

[[Sir Tristram]] later encountered Bleoberis riding through a valley with Segwarides's lady (179-043), leading to a confrontation between the two knights. When Tristram caught up and fought him, "Sir Bleoberis de Ganis stert aback" (180-021) — yet Bleoberis acquitted himself well, identifying himself honourably: "Wit ye well that my name is Sir Bleoberis de Ganis" (180-033).

## The Contest with Sir Tristram

The episode brought Bleoberis into direct conflict with Sir Tristram, who challenged the custom by which Bleoberis had taken the lady. Their combat ended in a negotiated resolution: Bleoberis agreed to return the lady to Tristram and ride to [[Tintagil]] (181-031), while Tristram granted him honourable terms. This outcome preserved Bleoberis's dignity while restoring the lady to her husband — a compromise that reflected the nuanced chivalric diplomacy of Malory's world.

Bleoberis later reflected on this encounter when Tristram recalled "when I fought with Sir Bleoberis for Sir Segwarides's wife" (224-030), showing how the episode resonated through the narrative as a touchstone of honour-based conflict resolution.

## Kinsman of Launcelot

Bleoberis's identity was deeply shaped by his kinship with Sir Launcelot. As a member of the de Ganis family, he was brother to Sir Blamore de Ganis (184-037) and cousin to Launcelot himself. When Sir Mador de la Porte appealed to fight Sir Blamore, Bleoberis warned his brother: "God speed you well, said Sir Bleoberis, for ye shall find him the best knight that ever ye fought with" (185-030), showing both fraternal concern and honest assessment of opponents.

During Launcelot's confrontation with [[Sir Meliagaunce]], Bleoberis was among the loyal kinsmen who sought their lord: "Then spake Sir Bleoberis and said: My lord Sir Launcelot, we have sought you long" (216-043). He and Launcelot departed together (216-052), underscoring his role in the Launcelot faction that would become so significant in the later tragedy of the Round Table.

## Tournament Deeds

Bleoberis was an active tournament knight. He "brake his spear upon Sir Gareth" (155-030) in one encounter. At another tournament, he and [[Sir Palomides]] witnessed the noble and mighty deeds of a knight who turned out to be Sir Launcelot in disguise (207-045). He also appeared at the bridge where he offered to joust with [[Sir Tristram]], only to be promptly smote down: "Sir Bleoberis proffered to joust... and Sir Tristram smote down Sir Bleoberis" (225-010).

With Sir Driant and Sir Bors, he formed part of the de Ganis contingent at tournaments (225-008), representing the family network that ran through the Round Table's fighting strength.

## Honour and Kinship

Bleoberis embodied the virtues and complexities of the extended Launcelot kin network. His actions — taking another knight's lady by claimed custom, then yielding honourably to Tristram — illustrate the tensions between ancient prerogative and emerging chivalric standards. His loyalty to Launcelot, shared with his brother Sir Blamore, made him part of the faction whose internal solidarity would prove both a strength and, in the final tragedy, a source of civil war.
