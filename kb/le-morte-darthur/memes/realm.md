# Realm

The realm in Malory's *Le Morte d'[[king-arthur|Arthur]]* is the fun[[lady|dame]]ntal unit of political organization and the primary object of [[kinsman|kin]]gly responsibility. It is not merely a territory bounded by geography but a social body composed of [[baron|lord]]s, [[baron|barons]], knights, and commons, whose welfare depends on legitimate rule. The realm stands in jeopardy when the throne is contested, suffers damage from knightly misadventure, and flourishes when governed by a "rightwise [[kinsman|kin]]g." Malory's [[custom|usage]] encompasses both the singular realm of England (or "Britain" in its ancient designation) and the plurality of realms — Ireland, Scotland, Benwick, and others — that constitute the political geography of the [[king-arthur|Arthur]]ian world.

## The Realm and Legitimate Kingship

The realm's condition is d[[wrath|ire]]ctly tied to the legitimacy of its ruler. When Uther [[dragon|Pendragon]] dies, "then stood the realm in great jeopardy long while" (008-002), for "every knight that was of any strength" asp[[wrath|ire]]d to the throne. The "[[baron|lord]]s of the realm, and all the gentlemen of arms" (008-005) must be assembled to resolve the succession crisis. The Archbishop, acting "by the [[counsel|advice]] of" the [[baron|lord]]s, seeks divine intervention to identify the "rightwise [[kinsman|kin]]g of this realm" (008-009). This episode establishes the realm's vulnerability to interregnum: without a recognized [[king|sovereign]], the realm descends into fragmentation and private [[battle|war]]. The shame of misrule extends to the ent[[wrath|ire]] political body — it is "shame unto them all and the realm, to be overgoverned with a" weak or illegitimate ruler (009-025).

## The Realm as a Plurality of Kingdoms

[[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s dominion extends across multiple realms. In his [[battle|war]] against the eleven [[kinsman|kin]]gs, [[king-arthur|Arthur]] holds "Ireland, and Scotland, and more realms than I will now rehearse" (011-034). The "realm of Benwick" (014-003) — the kingdom of [[king|King]] Ban across the Channel — supplies three hundred knights to [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s cause, demonstrating how realms function as military-political units within the broader [[king-arthur|Arthur]]ian hegemony. The [[king|King]] of "a hundred knights" challenges [[king-arthur|Arthur]] for "truage for this realm" (026-010), treating the realm as an entity from which tribute can be exacted. These plural realms create the federated structure of [[king-arthur|Arthur]]ian power, distinct from the singular national conception of later periods.

## The Realm's Vulnerability to Knightly Action

Individual knightly conduct can have consequences for the ent[[wrath|ire]] realm. A knight is [[battle|war]]ned that "thou puttest this realm in the greatest damage that ever" any knight did (027-003), lin[[kinsman|kin]]g personal martial action to collective political harm. The spectre of "the destruction of all this realm" (027-019) hangs over episodes of internal conflict, suggesting that the realm's integrity is never guaranteed but must be continually maintained through the restraint and loyalty of its armed nobility. [[king-arthur|Arthur]] himself swears that he will hold a certain course "so it apair not my realm" (054-010), articulating the [[kinsman|kin]]g's duty to avoid actions that diminish the realm's strength or [[honour|honor]].

## The Barons and the Realm

The "[[baron|barons]] of your realm" (024-011) constitute the realm's political constituency. When "many [[baron|lord]]s and [[baron|barons]] of this realm were displeased, for their children were" lost (030-012), their dissatisfaction threatens the realm's stability. The [[baron|barons]]' knowledge — or ignorance — of the [[kinsman|kin]]g's [[blood|lineage]] is politically consequential: "the most part of your [[baron|barons]] of your realm knew never whose son ye were" (024-011). The realm thus depends on a triangle of relationships: [[kinsman|kin]]g, [[baron|barons]], and commons, each bound by reciprocal obligations of counsel, loyalty, and protection.

## The Realm's Narrative Function

The realm serves as the constant stakes against which all political action is measured. When a [[kinsman|kin]]g dies, the realm is in jeopardy; when a knight acts rashly, the realm is damaged; when a ruler is "rightwise," the realm is secure. Malory uses the realm as a barometer of political health, compressing complex questions of legitimacy, consent, and obligation into a single word that carries both territorial and moral weight. The shift from "Britain" to "England" — "sometime [[kinsman|kin]]g of this [[baron|noble]] realm, then called Britain" (002-097) — further layers the concept with historical consciousness, positioning the [[king-arthur|Arthur]]ian moment as a transition point in the nation's self-understanding.
