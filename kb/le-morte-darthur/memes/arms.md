# Arms

**Arms** in Malory's "Le Morte d'[[king-arthur|Arthur]]" encompass the full apparatus of knightly [[battle|combat]]: weapons, armour, [[shield|shields]], and the heraldic devices that mark identity on the battle[[battle|field]]. Arms are never mere tools in the narrative — they are extensions of knightly identity, instruments of [[honour|worship]], and symbols of status. A knight is defined as much by his arms as by his deeds; to lose one's arms is to lose one's visible knighthood, and to bear another's arms is to assume another's identity.

## Arms and Knightly Identity

The phrase "men of arms" recurs throughout Malory as a formula for designating knights and their military retinues. An army is measured not in abstract numbers but in "men of arms on horseback," ma[[kinsman|kin]]g arms the unit of martial identity:

> "his whole host was of clean men of arms on horseback fifty thousand" (015-025)

To be a "good man of arms" (017-040) is to be a knight of proven worth. The equipment of knighthood — horse, armour, sword, shield, spear — constitutes the visible sign of the estate, and the narrative frequently marks transitions in status through the bestowal or acquisition of arms.

## Deeds of Arms as the Currency of Worship

The performance of "deeds of arms" is the primary mechanism by which knights acqu[[wrath|ire]] [[Worship]]. The phrase appears throughout the text as a measure of martial achievement:

> "great deeds of arms, that all men had wonder" (017-056)

A knight who does "[[marvel|marvellous]] deeds of arms" (014-010) earns [[honour|renown]] that spreads through the court and beyond. The connection is absolute: [[honour|worship]] cannot be acqu[[wrath|ire]]d except through deeds of arms performed before witnesses. The tournament, the battle, and the single [[battle|combat]] are all stages on which deeds of arms are enacted and recorded.

## Armour as Protection and Disguise

Armour in Malory serves a dual function: it protects the body in [[battle|combat]], but it also conceals identity. The disguised knight in borrowed armour is one of the work's most persistent motifs:

> "Sir Launcelot took Sir Kay's armour and his shield" (119-079)

When [[Sir Launcelot]] [[battle|fight]]s in [[Sir Kay]]'s armour, he is misrecognized by friend and foe alike, demonstrating that armour functions as identity in a world where faces are hidden behind helms. A knight "in [[sir-kay|Kay]]'s armour" (126-006) is read as [[sir-kay|Kay]] until his deeds prove otherwise. The donning and doffing of armour marks the transition between the private man and the public knight.

## The Shield as Heraldic Text

The shield is the most symbolically charged piece of knightly equipment. It bears the device that declares identity, and stri[[kinsman|kin]]g a shield is an act of challenge:

> "Griflet smote on the shield with the butt of his spear, that the shield fell down to the ground" (025-019)

When [[shield|shields]] are shattered in [[battle|combat]] ("their [[shield|shields]] fell to pieces" — 014-007), identity itself is fractured, requiring the [[battle|combat]]ants to "right their [[shield|shields]]" (019-075) and resume their public selves. The shield's heraldic function makes it the primary site where arms meet identity.

## Weapons and the Ethics of Combat

Malory's treatment of weapons reveals an ethical dimension to arms. The knight who [[battle|fight]]s a weaponless opponent commits an act of shame:

> "if thou slay me weaponless that shall be thy shame" (076-011)

A knight is expected to keep his weapon with him always (045-026), and the loss of a weapon in [[battle|combat]] creates a moment of vulnerability that tests both the disarmed knight and his opponent. The "grim weapon" that [[Sir Balin]] seizes in his final [[battle|combat]] (046-007) becomes the instrument of tragic destiny.

## Arms in the Narrative Economy

Arms circulate through the narrative as gifts, spoils, and tokens. [[king-arthur|King Arthur]] gives armour and sword to newly made knights (061-037); defeated knights yield their arms to the victor. The "horse and armour" that a knight requ[[wrath|ire]]s to continue his adventures (048-041, 073-035) create a recurring motif of disarmed knights [[quest|seeking]] re-equipment. This circulation of arms binds the knightly economy together, ensuring that martial identity is never a fixed possession but a resource constantly won, lost, and re-won.
