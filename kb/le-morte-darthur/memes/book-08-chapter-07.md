[170-001]Marhaus and achieved his battle, and how Sir Marhaus fled to his ship.

[170-002]And then Sir Marhaus avised [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]], and said thus: Young knight, Sir

[170-003]Tristram, what dost thou here? me sore repenteth of thy courage, for wit thou

[170-004]well I have been assayed, and the best knights of this land have been assayed

[170-005]of my hand; and also I have matched with the best knights of the world, and

[170-006]therefore by my counsel return again unto thy vessel. And fair knight, and

[170-007]well-proved knight, said Sir Tristram, thou shalt well wit I may not forsake

[170-008]thee in this quarrel, for I am for thy sake made knight. And thou shalt well

[170-009]wit that I am a king’s son born, and gotten upon a queen; and such

[170-010]promise I have made at my uncle’s request and mine own [[quest|seeking]], that I

[170-011]shall [[battle|fight]] with thee unto the uttermost, and deliver Cornwall from the old

[170-012]truage. And also wit thou well, Sir Marhaus, that this is the greatest cause

[170-013]that thou couragest me to have ado with thee, for thou art called one of the

[170-014]most renowned knights of the world, and because of that noise and fame that

[170-015]thou hast thou givest me courage to have ado with thee, for never yet was I

[170-016]proved with good knight; and sithen I took the order of knighthood this day, I

[170-017]am well pleased that I may have ado with so good a knight as thou art. And now

[170-018]wit thou well, Sir Marhaus, that I cast me to get [[honour|worship]] on thy body; and if

[170-019]that I be not proved, I trust to God that I shall be worshipfully proved upon

[170-020]thy body, and to deliver the country of Cornwall for ever from all manner of

[170-021]truage from Ireland for ever.

[170-022]When Sir Marhaus had heard him say what he would, he said then thus again: Fair

[170-023]knight, sithen it is so that thou castest to win worship of me, I let thee wit

[170-024]worship may thou none lose by me if thou mayest stand me three strokes; for I

[170-025]let thee wit for my noble deeds, proved and seen, [[king-arthur|King Arthur]] made me Knight of

[170-026]the [[round-table|Table Round]].

[170-027]Then they began to feutre their spears, and they met so fiercely together that

[170-028]they smote either other down, both horse and all. But Sir Marhaus smote Sir

[170-029]Tristram a great wound in the side with his spear, and then they avoided their

[170-030]horses, and pulled out their swords, and threw their shields afore them. And

[170-031]then they lashed together as men that were wild and courageous. And when they

[170-032]had stricken so together long, then they left their strokes, and foined at

[170-033]their breaths and visors; and when they saw that that might not prevail them,

[170-034]then they hurtled together like rams to bear either other down. Thus they

[170-035]fought still more than half a day, and either were wounded passing sore, that

[170-036]the blood ran down freshly from them upon the ground. By then Sir Tristram

[170-037]waxed more fresher than Sir Marhaus, and better winded and bigger; and with a

[170-038]mighty stroke he smote Sir Marhaus upon the helm such a buffet that it went

[170-039]through his helm, and through the coif of steel, and through the brain-pan, and

[170-040]the sword stuck so fast in the helm and in his brain-pan that Sir Tristram

[170-041]pulled thrice at his sword or ever he might pull it out from his head; and

[170-042]there Marhaus fell down on his knees, the edge of Tristram’s sword left

[170-043]in his brain-pan. And suddenly Sir Marhaus rose grovelling, and threw his sword

[170-044]and his shield from him, and so ran to his ships and fled his way, and Sir

[170-045]Tristram had ever his shield and his sword.

[170-046]And when Sir Tristram saw Sir Marhaus withdraw him, he said: Ah! Sir Knight of

[170-047]the Round Table, why withdrawest thou thee? thou dost thyself and thy kin great

[170-048]shame, for I am but a young knight, or now I was never proved, and rather than

[170-049]I should withdraw me from thee, I had rather be hewn in an hundred pieces. Sir

[170-050]Marhaus answered no word but yede his way sore groaning. Well, Sir Knight, said

[170-051]Sir Tristram, I promise thee thy sword and thy shield shall be mine; and thy

[170-052]shield shall I wear in all places where I ride on mine adventures, and in the

[170-053]sight of King Arthur and all the Round Table.
