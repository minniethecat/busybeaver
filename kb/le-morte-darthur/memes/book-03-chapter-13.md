[063-002]And then he turned him to the other knight, that was sore wounded. But when he

[063-003]saw the other’s buffet, he would not [[battle|fight]], but kneeled down and said,

[063-004]Take my cousin the lady with you at your request, and I require you, as ye be a

[063-005]true knight, put her to no shame nor villainy. What, said King Pellinore, will

[063-006]ye not fight for her? No, sir, said the knight, I will not fight with such a

[063-007]knight of [[courage|prowess]] as ye be. Well, said Pellinore, ye say well; I promise you

[063-008]she shall have no villainy by me, as I am true knight; but now me lacketh an

[063-009]horse, said Pellinore, but I will have Hontzlake’s horse. Ye shall not

[063-010]need, said the knight, for I shall give you such an horse as shall please you,

[063-011]so that you will lodge with me, for it is near night. I will well, said King

[063-012]Pellinore, abide with you all night. And there he had with him right good

[063-013]cheer, and fared of the best with passing good wine, and had merry rest that

[063-014]night. And on the morn he heard a mass and dined; and then was brought him a

[063-015]fair bay courser, and King Pellinore’s saddle set upon him. Now, what

[063-016]shall I call you? said the knight, inasmuch as ye have my cousin at your desire

[063-017]of your quest. Sir, I shall tell you, my name is King Pellinore of the Isles

[063-018]and knight of the [[round-table|Table Round]]. Now I am glad, said the knight, that such a

[063-019]noble man shall have the rule of my cousin. Now, what is your name? said

[063-020]Pellinore, I pray you tell me. Sir, my name is Sir Meliot of Logurs, and this

[063-021]lady my cousin hight [[lady-of-the-lake|Nimue]], and the knight that was in the other pavilion is my

[063-022]sworn brother, a passing good knight, and his name is Brian of the Isles, and

[063-023]he is full loath to do wrong, and full loath to fight with any man, but if he

[063-024]be sore sought on, so that for shame he may not leave it. It is marvel, said

[063-025]Pellinore, that he will not have ado with me. Sir, he will not have ado with no

[063-026]man but if it be at his request. Bring him to the court, said Pellinore, one of

[063-027]these days. Sir, we will come together. And ye shall be welcome, said

[063-028]Pellinore, to the court of King Arthur, and greatly allowed for your coming.

[063-029]And so he departed with the lady, and brought her to Camelot.

[063-030]So as they rode in a valley it was full of stones, and there the lady’s

[063-031]horse stumbled and threw her down, that her arm was sore bruised and near she

[063-032]swooned for pain. Alas! sir, said the lady, mine arm is out of lithe,

[063-033]wherethrough I must needs rest me. Ye shall well, said King Pellinore. And so

[063-034]he alighted under a fair tree where was fair grass, and he put his horse

[063-035]thereto, and so laid him under the tree and slept till it was nigh night. And

[063-036]when he awoke he would have ridden. Sir, said the lady, it is so dark that ye

[063-037]may as well ride backward as forward. So they abode still and made there their

[063-038]lodging. Then Sir Pellinore put off his armour; then a little afore midnight

[063-039]they heard the trotting of an horse. Be ye still, said King Pellinore, for we

[063-040]shall hear of some adventure.
