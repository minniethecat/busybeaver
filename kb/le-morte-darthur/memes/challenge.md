# Challenge

The **challenge** is the fun[[lady|dame]]ntal speech act of [[chivalry]] in [[Le Morte d'Arthur]]. To challenge is to invoke the code of [[honour]] in its most d[[wrath|ire]]ct form: one [[knight]] calls upon another to prove his worth in [[combat]]. The challenge structures the narrative of Malory's work as surely as the [[feast]] structures its social world — every book turns on challenges issued, accepted, and resolved.

## The Form of the Challenge

A challenge in Malory follows a recognisable ritual form. The challenger declares his identity (or withholds it), states the grievance or the claim, and names the terms:

> Sir Uwaine challenged all the knights of Cornwall (240-024)

The challenge may be delivered in person, by messenger, or by symbolic token — a glove thrown down, a horn sounded, a shield hung from a tree. Whatever the medium, the challenge is a demand that cannot be refused without loss of [[honour]]. To be challenged is to be placed under obligation.

## Challenge by Pavilion

One of the most distinctive forms of challenge in [[Le Morte d'Arthur]] is the challenge by pavilion. A knight pitches a pavilion by a well or at a ford and challenges all who pass:

> the knights of the pavilions challenged that lady of Sir Launcelot (062-017)

The pavilion-challenge is a standing invitation to [[battle|combat]] — a claim upon a piece of territory that must be answered by any knight who would pass. It is the spatial form of the challenge, turning geography into a theatre of [[prowess]].

## The Challenge as Narrative Engine

The challenge serves Malory as a narrative device of great economy. A single challenge can generate an ent[[wrath|ire]] episode: a strange knight arrives at court, issues a challenge, a champion rises to meet it, and the [[battle|combat]] that follows reveals identity, tests virtue, and advances the plot. The challenge is the hinge on which the door of [[adventure]] swings open:

> challenged Sir Launcelot foot-hot (230-043)

The adverb "foot-hot" — meaning immediately, without pause — captures the urgency that the challenge imposes on narrative time. Once a challenge is issued, the story cannot proceed until it is answered.

## Appeal and Accusation

The challenge shades into the legal form of the appeal when the grievance is a crime rather than a point of [[worship|honour]]. Malory's glossary records the equivalence:

> Appealed, challenged, accused (242-226)

To appeal a knight of treason is to challenge him to mortal [[battle|combat]]; the judicial duel is the challenge in its most consequential form. In this sense, every challenge carries within it the possibility of death — it is never merely a game, never merely a [[tournament]] exercise.

## The Refused Challenge

A challenge refused is [[shame|dishonour]] absolute. The knight who will not answer a challenge forfeits his place in the chivalric order. This is why challenges in Malory are so seldom refused: the cost of refusal is social death, which for a knight is worse than physical death. The challenge system thus functions as a compulsory mechanism — it guarantees that disputes are settled by [[battle|combat]] rather than by evasion, and that [[prowess]] rather than [[counsel]] is the final arbiter of truth.
