**Lineage** is the hidden architecture of the [[king-arthur|Arthur]]ian world. In [[Le Morte d'Arthur]], who a knight descends from often matters more than what he does. Lineage determines a knight's place in the feudal order, his claim to [[honour]], and his eligibility for the highest [[quest]]s. It can be a source of pride, a burden of expectation, or a secret that, once revealed, transforms a character's identity and fate. Malory returns repeatedly to the question—who are you, and of what blood?—as the key that unlocks a knight's significance.

## The Question of Lineage

Throughout the Morte, characters are interrogated about their [[blood|lineage]]. To know a knight's descent is to know his worth:

> what knight he is, and of what lineage he is come (150-039)

> of what lineage he was come. And but if thou tell me (147-018)

Dame Lionesse's demand that the unknown knight declare his [[blood|lineage]] is typical. In the feudal imagination, identity is genealogical: a knight without a known [[blood|lineage]] is a knight without a name, without a place, without a claim. The unknown knight—[[sir-gareth|Beaumains]], [[Sir Gareth]] in disguise—must eventually reveal his descent before he can be fully integrated into the social order. Lineage is not merely ancestry; it is the password that admits a knight to full recognition.

## The Proof of Lineage

Lineage is not simply claimed but proven—in [[battle|combat]], in conduct, and in the revelation of birth:

> prove at the end that he is come of a noble blood and of king's lineage (136-009)

The word "prove" is telling. Lineage manifests itself in action: a knight of [[baron|noble]] blood will [[battle|fight]] nobly, speak nobly, bear himself nobly. Malory makes the connection between blood and behaviour explicit—[[baron|noble]] descent produces [[baron|noble]] conduct, and [[baron|noble]] conduct testifies to [[baron|noble]] descent. The proof runs in both d[[wrath|ire]]ctions, creating a self-reinforcing system in which birth and behaviour confirm each other.

## Lineage and Political Legitimacy

Lineage is the foundation of political legitimacy. A [[kinsman|kin]]g rules because of his descent; a knight holds [[realm|land]] because of his ancestors. When Sir [[Sir Palomides|Palomides]] declares:

> Maccabaeus were of our lineage. I am right inheritor of Alexandria and Africa (105-025)

he is ma[[kinsman|kin]]g a political claim grounded in genealogy. His [[blood|lineage]] gives him title to [[realm|kingdom]]s, even [[realm|kingdom]]s he has never seen. The claim may be distant, even fantastical, but the logic is the same that undergirds [[king-arthur|Arthur]]'s own claim to the throne: descent from [[Uther Pendragon]] through [[Igraine]], a [[blood|lineage]] that the [[sword]] in the stone ultimately confirms.

## Lineage and the Hidden Heir

The hidden heir—the [[baron|noble]] child raised in ignorance of his [[blood|lineage]]—is one of Malory's most potent narrative devices. [[king-arthur|Arthur]] himself is the archetype: raised by Sir Ector, una[[battle|war]]e of his royal blood, revealed as [[kinsman|kin]]g only when he draws the sword. The revelation of [[blood|lineage]] is a moment of narrative transformation, turning the unknown into the known, the common into the royal:

> more high lineage than thou, and that will I prove on thy body (134-029)

The challenge to prove superior [[blood|lineage]] through [[battle|combat]] is a motif that recurs throughout the Morte. Lineage is not merely a matter of records and genealogies; it is a quality that manifests in the body, in strength, in the capacity to prevail in [[joust]] and battle.

## The Burden of Lineage

If [[blood|lineage]] confers [[worship|honour]], it also imposes obligation. A knight of high [[blood|lineage]] must live up to his ancestry, and failure to do so is a double [[shame|disgrace]]—a personal failure and a stain upon the family name. The t[[wrath|rage]]dy of the Orkney brothers—[[Sir Gawaine]], Sir Agravaine, Sir Gaheris, [[Sir Gareth]]—is in part a t[[wrath|rage]]dy of [[blood|lineage]]: the sons of [[king|King]] Lot and Queen Margawse, bound by blood to avenge their father's death, caught in a cycle of [[revenge]] that [[blood|lineage]] demands and that destroys them all.
