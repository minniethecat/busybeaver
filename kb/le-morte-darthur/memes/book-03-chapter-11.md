[061-001]how he lost his head at the request of a lady.

[061-002]With that came a damosel riding on a palfrey as fast as she might drive, and

[061-003]cried with a loud voice unto Sir Tor. What will ye with me? said Sir Tor. I

[061-004]beseech thee, said the damosel, for [[king-arthur|King Arthur]]’s love, give me a gift; I

[061-005]require thee, gentle knight, as thou art a gentleman. Now, said Tor, ask a gift

[061-006]and I will give it you. Gramercy, said the damosel; now I ask the head of the

[061-007]false knight Abelleus, for he is the most outrageous knight that liveth, and

[061-008]the greatest murderer. I am loath, said Sir Tor, of that gift I have given you;

[061-009]let him make amends in that he hath trespassed unto you. Now, said the damosel,

[061-010]he may not, for he slew mine own brother before mine own eyes, that was a

[061-011]better knight than he, an he had had grace; and I kneeled half an hour afore

[061-012]him in the mire for to save my brother’s life, that had done him no

[061-013]damage, but fought with him by adventure of arms, and so for all that I could

[061-014]do he struck off his head; wherefore I require thee, as thou art a true knight,

[061-015]to give me my gift, or else I shall shame thee in all [[camelot|the court of King Arthur]];

[061-016]for he is the falsest knight living, and a great destroyer of good knights.

[061-017]Then when Abelleus heard this, he was more afeard, and yielded him and asked

[061-018]mercy. I may not now, said Sir Tor, but if I should be found false of my

[061-019]promise; for while I would have taken you to mercy ye would none ask, but if ye

[061-020]had the brachet again, that was my quest. And therewith he took off his helm,

[061-021]and he arose and fled, and Sir Tor after him, and smote off his head quite.

[061-022]Now sir, said the damosel, it is near night; I pray you come and lodge with me

[061-023]here at my place, it is here fast by. I will well, said Sir Tor, for his horse

[061-024]and he had fared evil since they departed from Camelot, and so he rode with

[061-025]her, and had passing good cheer with her; and she had a passing fair old knight

[061-026]to her husband that made him passing good cheer, and well eased both his horse

[061-027]and him. And on the morn he heard his mass, and brake his fast, and took his

[061-028]leave of the knight and of the lady, that besought him to tell them his name.

[061-029]Truly, he said, my name is Sir Tor that was late made knight, and this was the

[061-030]first quest of arms that ever I did, to bring again that this knight Abelleus

[061-031]took away from King Arthur’s court. O fair knight, said the lady and her

[061-032]husband, an ye come here in our marches, come and see our poor lodging, and it

[061-033]shall be always at your commandment. So Sir Tor departed and came to Camelot on

[061-034]the third day by noon, and the king and the queen and all the court was passing

[061-035]fain of his coming, and made great joy that he was come again; for he went from

[061-036]the court with little succour, but as King Pellinore his father gave him an old

[061-037]courser, and King Arthur gave him armour and a sword, and else had he none

[061-038]other succour, but rode so forth himself alone. And then the king and the queen

[061-039]by [[merlin|Merlin]]’s advice made him to swear to tell of his adventures, and so he

[061-040]told and made proofs of his deeds as it is afore rehearsed, wherefore the king

[061-041]and the queen made great joy. Nay, nay, said Merlin, these be but japes to that

[061-042]he shall do; for he shall prove a noble knight of [[courage|prowess]], as good as any is

[061-043]living, and gentle and courteous, and of good tatches, and passing true of his

[061-044]promise, and never shall outrage. Wherethrough Merlin’s words King Arthur

[061-045]gave him an earldom of lands that fell unto him. And here endeth the quest of

[061-046]Sir Tor, King Pellinore’s son.
