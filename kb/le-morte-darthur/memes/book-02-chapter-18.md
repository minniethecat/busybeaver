[049-001]Balan, and how each of them slew other unknown, till they were wounded to

[049-002]death.

[049-003]Then afore him he saw come riding out of a castle a knight, and his horse

[049-004]trapped all red, and himself in the same colour. When this knight in the red

[049-005]beheld Balin, him thought it should be his brother Balin by cause of his two

[049-006]swords, but by cause he knew not his shield he deemed it was not he. And so

[049-007]they aventryd their spears and came marvellously fast together, and they smote

[049-008]each other in the shields, but their spears and their course were so big that

[049-009]it bare down horse and man, that they lay both in a swoon. But Balin was

[049-010]bruised sore with the fall of his horse, for he was weary of travel. And Balan

[049-011]was the first that rose on foot and drew his sword, and went toward Balin, and

[049-012]he arose and went against him; but Balan smote Balin first, and he put up his

[049-013]shield and smote him through the shield and tamed his helm. Then Balin smote

[049-014]him again with that unhappy sword, and well-nigh had felled his brother Balan,

[049-015]and so they fought there together till their breaths failed. Then Balin looked

[049-016]up to the castle and saw the towers stand full of ladies. So they went unto

[049-017]battle again, and wounded everych other dolefully, and then they breathed

[049-018]ofttimes, and so went unto battle that all the place there as they fought was

[049-019]blood red. And at that time there was none of them both but they had either

[049-020]smitten other seven great wounds, so that the least of them might have been the

[049-021]death of the mightiest giant in this world.

[049-022]Then they went to battle again so marvellously that doubt it was to hear of

[049-023]that battle for the great blood-shedding, and their hauberks unnailed that

[049-024]naked they were on every side. At last Balan the younger brother withdrew him a

[049-025]little and laid him down. Then said Balin le Savage, What knight art thou? for

[049-026]or now I found never no knight that matched me. My name is, said he, Balan,

[049-027]brother unto the good knight, Balin. Alas, said Balin, that ever I should see

[049-028]this day, and therewith he fell backward in a swoon. Then Balan yede on all

[049-029]four feet and hands, and put off the helm off his brother, and might not know

[049-030]him by the visage it was so ful hewn and bled; but when he awoke he said, O

[049-031]Balan, my brother, thou hast slain me and I thee, wherefore all the wide world

[049-032]shall speak of us both. Alas, said Balan, that ever I saw this day, that

[049-033]through mishap I might not know you, for I espied well your two swords, but by

[049-034]cause ye had another shield I deemed ye had been another knight. Alas, said

[049-035]Balin, all that made an unhappy knight in the castle, for he caused me to leave

[049-036]my own shield to our both’s destruction, and if I might live I would

[049-037]destroy that castle for ill customs. That were well done, said Balan, for I had

[049-038]never grace to depart from them since that I came hither, for here it happed me

[049-039]to slay a knight that kept this island, and since might I never depart, and no

[049-040]more should ye, brother, an ye might have slain me as ye have, and escaped

[049-041]yourself with the life.

[049-042]Right so came the lady of the tower with four knights and six ladies and six

[049-043]yeomen unto them, and there she heard how they made their moan either to other,

[049-044]and said, We came both out of one tomb, that is to say one mother’s

[049-045]belly, and so shall we lie both in one pit. So Balan prayed the lady of her

[049-046]gentleness, for his true service, that she would bury them both in that same

[049-047]place there the battle was done. And she granted them, with weeping, it should

[049-048]be done richly in the best manner. Now, will ye send for a priest, that we may

[049-049]receive our sacrament, and receive the blessed body of our Lord Jesus Christ?

[049-050]Yea, said the lady, it shall be done; and so she sent for a priest and gave

[049-051]them their rights. Now, said Balin, when we are buried in one tomb, and the

[049-052]mention made over us how two brethren slew each other, there will never good

[049-053]knight, nor good man, see our tomb but they will pray for our souls. And so all

[049-054]the ladies and gentlewomen wept for [[mercy|pity]]. Then anon Balan died, but Balin died

[049-055]not till the midnight after, and so were they buried both, and the lady let

[049-056]make a mention of Balan how he was there slain by his brother’s hands,

[049-057]but she knew not Balin’s name.
