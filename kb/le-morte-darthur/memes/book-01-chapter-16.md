[019-001]By then came into the [[battle|field]] King Ban as fierce as a lion, with bands of green

[019-002]and thereupon gold. Ha! a! said King Lot, we must be discomfited, for yonder I

[019-003]see the most valiant knight of the world, and the man of the most [[honour|renown]], for

[019-004]such two brethren as is King Ban and King [[sir-bors|Bors]] are not living, wherefore we

[019-005]must needs void or die; and but if we avoid manly and wisely there is but

[019-006]death. When King Ban came into the battle, he came in so fiercely that the

[019-007]strokes redounded again from the wood and the water; wherefore King Lot wept

[019-008]for [[mercy|pity]] and dole that he saw so many good knights take their end. But through

[019-009]the great force of King Ban they made both the northern battles that were

[019-010]departed hurtled together for great dread; and the three kings and their

[019-011]knights slew on ever, that it was pity on to behold that multitude of the

[019-012]people that fled. But King Lot, and King of the Hundred Knights, and King

[019-013]Morganore gathered the people together passing knightly, and did great [[courage|prowess]]

[019-014]of arms, and held the battle all that day, like hard.

[019-015]When [[king-arthur|the King]] of the Hundred Knights beheld the great damage that King Ban did,

[019-016]he thrust unto him with his horse, and smote him on high upon the helm, a great

[019-017]stroke, and astonied him sore. Then King Ban was wroth with him, and followed

[019-018]on him fiercely; the other saw that, and cast up his shield, and spurred his

[019-019]horse forward, but the stroke of King Ban fell down and carved a cantel off the

[019-020]shield, and the sword slid down by the hauberk behind his back, and cut through

[019-021]the trapping of steel and the horse even in two pieces, that the sword felt the

[019-022]earth. Then the King of the Hundred Knights voided the horse lightly, and with

[019-023]his sword he broached the horse of King Ban through and through. With that King

[019-024]Ban voided lightly from the dead horse, and then King Ban smote at the other so

[019-025]eagerly, and smote him on the helm that he fell to the earth. Also in that ire

[019-026]he felled King Morganore, and there was great slaughter of good knights and

[019-027]much people. By then came into the press King Arthur, and found King Ban

[019-028]standing among dead men and dead horses, fighting on foot as a wood lion, that

[019-029]there came none nigh him, as far as he might reach with his sword, but he

[019-030]caught a grievous buffet; whereof King Arthur had great pity. And Arthur was so

[019-031]bloody, that by his shield there might no man know him, for all was blood and

[019-032]brains on his sword. And as Arthur looked by him he saw a knight that was

[019-033]passingly well horsed, and therewith Sir Arthur ran to him, and smote him on

[019-034]the helm, that his sword went unto his teeth, and the knight sank down to the

[019-035]earth dead, and anon Arthur took the horse by the rein, and led him unto King

[019-036]Ban, and said, Fair brother, have this horse, for he have great myster thereof,

[019-037]and me repenteth sore of your great damage. It shall be soon revenged, said

[019-038]King Ban, for I trust in God mine ure is not such but some of them may sore

[019-039]repent this. I will well, said Arthur, for I see your deeds full actual;

[019-040]nevertheless, I might not come at you at that time.

[019-041]But when King Ban was mounted on horseback, then there began new battle, the

[019-042]which was sore and hard, and passing great slaughter. And so through great

[019-043]force King Arthur, King Ban, and King Bors made their knights a little to

[019-044]withdraw them. But alway the eleven kings with their chivalry never turned

[019-045]back; and so withdrew them to a little wood, and so over a little river, and

[019-046]there they rested them, for on the night they might have no rest on the field.

[019-047]And then the eleven kings and knights put them on a heap all together, as men

[019-048]adread and out of all comfort. But there was no man might pass them, they held

[019-049]them so hard together both behind and before, that King Arthur had marvel of

[019-050]their deeds of arms, and was passing wroth. Ah, Sir Arthur, said King Ban and

[019-051]King Bors, blame them not, for they do as good men ought to do. For by my

[019-052]faith, said King Ban, they are the best fighting men, and knights of most

[019-053]prowess, that ever I saw or heard speak of, and those eleven kings are men of

[019-054]great worship; and if they were longing unto you there were no king under the

[019-055]heaven had such eleven knights, and of such worship. I may not love them, said

[019-056]Arthur, they would destroy me. That wot we well, said King Ban and King Bors,

[019-057]for they are your mortal enemies, and that hath been proved aforehand; and this

[019-058]day they have done their part, and that is great pity of their wilfulness.

[019-059]Then all the eleven kings drew them together, and then said King Lot, Lords, ye

[019-060]must other ways than ye do, or else the great loss is behind; ye may see what

[019-061]people we have lost, and what good men we lose, because we wait always on these

[019-062]foot-men, and ever in saving of one of the foot-men we lose ten horsemen for

[019-063]him; therefore this is mine advice, let us put our foot-men from us, for it is

[019-064]near night, for the noble Arthur will not tarry on the footmen, for they may

[019-065]save themselves, the wood is near hand. And when we horsemen be together, look

[019-066]every each of you kings let make such ordinance that none break upon pain of

[019-067]death. And who that seeth any man dress him to flee, lightly that he be slain,

[019-068]for it is better that we slay a coward, than through a coward all we to be

[019-069]slain. How say ye? said King Lot, answer me all ye kings. It is well said,

[019-070]quoth King Nentres; so said the King of the Hundred Knights; the same said the

[019-071]King Carados, and King Uriens; so did King Idres and King Brandegoris; and so

[019-072]did King Cradelment, and the Duke of Cambenet; the same said King Clariance and

[019-073]King Agwisance, and sware they would never fail other, neither for life nor for

[019-074]death. And whoso that fled, but did as they did, should be slain. Then they

[019-075]amended their harness, and righted their shields, and took new spears and set

[019-076]them on their thighs, and stood still as it had been a plump of wood.
