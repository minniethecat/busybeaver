[238-001]where [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]] was, and of the quest that [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]] and ten knights

[238-002]made for Sir Tristram.

[238-003]And when he came to the land he took off his harness, and sat roaring and

[238-004]crying as a man out of his mind. Right so came a damosel even by [[sir-palomides|Sir Palomides]],

[238-005]that was sent from [[sir-gawaine|Sir Gawaine]] and his brother unto [[mordred|Sir Mordred]], that lay sick

[238-006]in the same place with that old knight where Sir Tristram was. For, as the

[238-007]French book saith, Sir Persides hurt so Sir Mordred a ten days afore; and had

[238-008]it not been for the love of Sir Gawaine and his brother, Sir Persides had slain

[238-009]Sir Mordred. And so this damosel came by Sir Palomides, and she and he had

[238-010]language together, the which pleased neither of them; and so the damosel rode

[238-011]her ways till she came to the old knight’s place, and there she told that

[238-012]old knight how she met with the woodest knight by adventure that ever she met

[238-013]withal. What bare he in his shield? said Sir Tristram. It was indented with

[238-014]white and black, said the damosel. Ah, said Sir Tristram, that was Sir

[238-015]Palomides, the good knight. For well I know him, said Sir Tristram, for one of

[238-016]the best knights living in this realm. Then that old knight took a little

[238-017]hackney, and rode for Sir Palomides, and brought him unto his own manor; and

[238-018]full well knew Sir Tristram Sir Palomides, but he said but little, for at that

[238-019]time Sir Tristram was walking upon his feet, and well amended of his hurts; and

[238-020]always when Sir Palomides saw Sir Tristram he would behold him full

[238-021]marvellously, and ever him seemed that he had seen him. Then would he say unto

[238-022]Sir Dinadan: An ever I may meet with Sir Tristram he shall not escape mine

[238-023]hands. I marvel, said Sir Dinadan, that ye boast behind Sir Tristram, for it is

[238-024]but late that he was in your hands, and ye in his hands; why would ye not hold

[238-025]him when ye had him? for I saw myself twice or thrice that ye gat but little

[238-026][[honour|worship]] of Sir Tristram. Then was Sir Palomides ashamed. So leave we them a

[238-027]little while in the old castle with the old knight Sir Darras.

[238-028]Now shall we speak of [[king-arthur|King Arthur]], that said to Sir Launcelot: Had not ye been

[238-029]we had not lost Sir Tristram, for he was here daily unto the time ye met with

[238-030]him, and in an evil time, said Arthur, ye encountered with him. My lord Arthur,

[238-031]said Launcelot, ye put upon me that I should be cause of his departition; God

[238-032]knoweth it was against my will. But when men be hot in deeds of arms oft they

[238-033]hurt their friends as well as their foes. And my lord, said Sir Launcelot, ye

[238-034]shall understand that Sir Tristram is a man that I am loath to offend, for he

[238-035]hath done for me more than ever I did for him as yet. But then Sir Launcelot

[238-036]made bring forth a book: and then Sir Launcelot said: Here we are ten knights

[238-037]that will swear upon a book never to rest one night where we rest another this

[238-038]twelvemonth until that we find Sir Tristram. And as for me, said Sir Launcelot,

[238-039]I promise you upon this book that an I may meet with him, either with fairness

[238-040]or foulness I shall bring him to this court, or else I shall die therefore. And

[238-041]the names of these ten knights that had undertaken this quest were these

[238-042]following: First was Sir Launcelot, Sir Ector de Maris, [[sir-bors|Sir Bors de Ganis]], and

[238-043]Bleoberis, and Sir Blamore de Ganis, and Lucan the Butler, Sir Uwaine, Sir

[238-044]Galihud Lionel, and Galiodin. So these ten noble knights departed from the

[238-045]court of King Arthur, and so they rode upon their quest together until they

[238-046]came to a cross where departed four ways, and there departed the fellowship in

[238-047]four to seek Sir Tristram.

[238-048]And as Sir Launcelot rode by adventure he met with Dame Bragwaine that was sent

[238-049]into that country to seek Sir Tristram, and she fled as fast as her palfrey

[238-050]might go. So Sir Launcelot met with her and asked her why she fled. Ah, fair

[238-051]knight, said Dame Bragwaine, I flee for dread of my life, for here followeth me

[238-052]Sir Breuse Saunce Pité to slay me. Hold you nigh me, said Sir Launcelot. Then

[238-053]when Sir Launcelot saw Sir Breuse Saunce Pité, Sir Launcelot cried unto him,

[238-054]and said: False knight, destroyer of ladies and damosels, now thy last days be

[238-055]come. When Sir Breuse Saunce Pité saw Sir Launcelot’s shield he knew it

[238-056]well, for at that time he bare not the arms of Cornwall, but he bare his own

[238-057]shield. And then Sir Breuse fled, and Sir Launcelot followed after him. But Sir

[238-058]Breuse was so well horsed that when him list to flee he might well flee, and

[238-059]also abide when him list. And then Sir Launcelot returned unto Dame Bragwaine,

[238-060]and she thanked him of his great labour.
