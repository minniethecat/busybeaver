[001-001]The Morte D’[[king-arthur|Arthur]] was finished, as the epilogue tells us, in the ninth

[001-002]year of Edward IV., i.e. between March 4, 1469 and the same date in 1470. It is

[001-003]thus, fitly enough, the last important English book written before the

[001-004]introduction of printing into this country, and since no manuscript of it has

[001-005]come down to us it is also the first English classic for our knowledge of which

[001-006]we are entirely dependent on a printed text. Caxton’s story of how the

[001-007]book was brought to him and he was induced to print it may be read farther on

[001-008]in his own preface. From this we learn also that he was not only the printer of

[001-009]the book, but to some extent its editor also, dividing Malory’s work into

[001-010]twenty-one books, splitting up the books into chapters, by no means skilfully,

[001-011]and supplying the “Rubrish” or chapter-headings. It may be added

[001-012]that Caxton’s preface contains, moreover, a brief criticism which, on the

[001-013]points on which it touches, is still the soundest and most sympathetic that has

[001-014]been written.

[001-015]Caxton finished his edition the last day of July 1485, some fifteen or sixteen

[001-016]years after Malory wrote his epilogue. It is clear that the author was then

[001-017]dead, or the printer would not have acted as a clumsy editor to the book, and

[001-018]recent discoveries (if bibliography may, for the moment, enlarge its bounds to

[001-019]mention such matters) have revealed with tolerable certainty when Malory died

[001-020]and who he was. In letters to The Athenaeum in July 1896 Mr. T. Williams

[001-021]pointed out that the name of a Sir Thomas Malorie occurred among those of a

[001-022]number of other Lancastrians excluded from a general pardon granted by Edward

[001-023]IV. in 1468, and that a William Mallerye was mentioned in the same year as

[001-024]taking part in a Lancastrian rising. In September 1897, again, in another

[001-025]letter to the same paper, Mr. A. T. Martin reported the finding of the will of

[001-026]a Thomas Malory of Papworth, a hundred partly in Cambridgeshire, partly in

[001-027]Hunts. This will was made on September 16, 1469, and as it was proved the 27th

[001-028]of the next month the testator must have been in immediate expectation of

[001-029]death. It contains the most careful provision for the education and starting in

[001-030]life of a family of three daughters and seven sons, of whom the youngest seems

[001-031]to have been still an infant. We cannot say with certainty that this Thomas

[001-032]Malory, whose last thoughts were so busy for his children, was our author, or

[001-033]that the Lancastrian knight discovered by Mr. Williams was identical with

[001-034]either or both, but such evidence as the Morte D’Arthur offers favours

[001-035]such a [[faith|belief]]. There is not only the epilogue with its petition, “pray

[001-036]for me while I am alive that God send me good deliverance and when I am dead

[001-037]pray you all for my soul,” but this very request is foreshadowed at the

[001-038]end of chap. 37 of Book ix. in the touching passage, surely inspired by

[001-039]personal experience, as to the sickness “that is the greatest pain a

[001-040]prisoner may have”; and the reflections on English fickleness in the

[001-041]first chapter of Book xxi., though the Wars of the Roses might have inspired

[001-042]them in any one, come most naturally from an author who was a Lancastrian

[001-043]knight.

[001-044]If the Morte D’Arthur was really written in prison and by a prisoner

[001-045]distressed by ill-health as well as by lack of liberty, surely no task was ever

[001-046]better devised to while away weary hours. Leaving abundant scope for

[001-047]originality in selection, modification, and arrangement, as a compilation and

[001-048]translation it had in it that mechanical element which adds the touch of

[001-049]restfulness to literary work. No original, it is said, has yet been found for

[001-050]Book vii., and it is possible that none will ever be forthcoming for chap. 20

[001-051]of Book xviii., which describes the arrival of the body of the Fair Maiden of

[001-052]Astolat at Arthur’s court, or vii for chap. 25 of the same book, with its

[001-053]discourse on [[love|true love]]; but the great bulk of the work has been traced chapter

[001-054]by chapter to the “[[merlin|Merlin]]” of Robert de Borron and his successors

[001-055](Bks. i.-iv.), the English metrical romance La Morte Arthur of the Thornton

[001-056]manuscript (Bk. v.), the French romances of [[sir-tristram|Tristan]] (Bks. viii.-x.) and of

[001-057][[sir-launcelot|Launcelot]] (Bks. vi., xi.-xix.), and lastly to the English prose Morte Arthur of

[001-058]Harley MS. 2252 (Bks. xviii., xx., xxi.). As to Malory’s choice of his

[001-059]authorities critics have not failed to point out that now and again he gives a

[001-060]worse version where a better has come down to us, and if he had been able to

[001-061]order a complete set of Arthurian manuscripts from his bookseller, no doubt he

[001-062]would have done even better than he did! But of the skill, approaching to

[001-063]original genius, with which he used the books from which he worked there is

[001-064]little dispute.

[001-065]Malory died leaving his work obviously unrevised, and in this condition it was

[001-066]brought to Caxton, who prepared it for the press with his usual enthusiasm in

[001-067]the cause of good literature, and also, it must be added, with his usual

[001-068]carelessness. New chapters are sometimes made to begin in the middle of a

[001-069]sentence, and in addition to simple misprints there are numerous passages in

[001-070]which it is impossible to believe that we have the text as Malory intended it

[001-071]to stand. After Caxton’s edition Malory’s manuscript must have

[001-072]disappeared, and subsequent editions are differentiated only by the degree of

[001-073]closeness with which they follow the first. Editions appeared printed by Wynkyn

[001-074]de Worde in 1498 and 1529, by William Copland in 1559, by Thomas East about

[001-075]1585, and by Thomas Stansby in 1634, each printer apparently taking the text of

[001-076]his immediate predecessor and reproducing it with modifications.

[001-077]Stansby’s edition served for reprints in 1816 and 1856 (the latter edited

[001-078]by Thomas Wright); but in 1817 an edition supervised by Robert Southey went

[001-079]back to Caxton’s text, though to a copy (only two are extant, and only

[001-080]one perfect!) in which eleven leaves were supplied from Wynkyn de Worde’s

[001-081]reprint. In 1868 Sir Edward Strachey produced for the present publishers a

[001-082]reprint of Southey’s text in modern spelling, with the substitution of

[001-083]current words for those now obsolete, and the softening of a handful of

[001-084]passages likely, he thought, to prevent the book being placed in the hands of

[001-085]boys. In 1889 a boon was conferred on scholars by the publication of Dr. H.

[001-086]Oskar Sommer’s page-for-page reprint of Caxton’s text, with an

[001-087]elaborate discussion of Malory’s sources. Dr. Sommer’s edition was

[001-088]used by Sir E. Strachey to revise his Globe text, and in 1897 Mr. Israel

[001-089]Gollancz produced for the “Temple Classics” a very pretty edition

[001-090]in which Sir Edward Strachey’s principles of modernisation in spelling

[001-091]and punctuation were adopted, but with the restoration of obsolete words and

[001-092]omitted phrases. As to the present edition, Sir Edward Strachey altered with so

[001-093]sparing a hand that on many pages differences between his version and that here

[001-094]printed will be looked for in vain; but the most anxious care has been taken to

[001-095]produce a text modernised as to its spelling, but in other respects in accurate

[001-096]accordance with Caxton’s text, as represented by Dr Sommer’s

[001-097]reprint. Obvious misprints have been silently corrected, but in a few cases

[001-098]notes show where emendations have been introduced from Wynkyn de

[001-099]Worde—not that Wynkyn had any more right to emend Caxton than we, but

[001-100]because even a printer’s conjecture gains a little sanctity after four

[001-101]centuries. The restoration of obsolete words has necessitated a much fuller

[001-102]glossary, and the index of names has therefore been separated from it and

[001-103]enlarged. In its present form the index is the work of Mr. Henry Littlehales.

[001-104]A. W. POLLARD.
