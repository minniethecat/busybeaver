[241-001]how Sir Gaheris smote him down and Andred his cousin.

[241-002]Then there came [[sir-kay|Sir Kay]], the Seneschal, unto [[king-mark|King Mark]], and there he had good

[241-003]cheer showing outward. Now, fair lords, said he, will ye prove any adventure in

[241-004]the forest of Morris, in the which I know well is as hard an adventure as I

[241-005]know any. Sir, said Sir Kay, I will prove it. And Sir Gaheris said he would be

[241-006]avised for King Mark was ever full of treason: and therewithal Sir Gaheris

[241-007]departed and rode his way. And by the same way that Sir Kay should ride he laid

[241-008]him down to rest, charging his squire to wait upon Sir Kay; And warn me when he

[241-009]cometh. So within a while Sir Kay came riding that way, and then Sir Gaheris

[241-010]took his horse and met him, and said: Sir Kay, ye are not wise to ride at the

[241-011]request of King Mark, for he dealeth all with treason. Then said Sir Kay: I

[241-012]require you let us prove this adventure. I shall not fail you, said Sir

[241-013]Gaheris. And so they rode that time till a lake that was that time called the

[241-014]Perilous Lake, and there they abode under the shaw of the wood.

[241-015]The meanwhile King Mark within the castle of Tintagil avoided all his barons,

[241-016]and all other save such as were privy with him were avoided out of his chamber.

[241-017]And then he let call his nephew Sir Andred, and bade arm him and horse him

[241-018]lightly; and by that time it was midnight. And so King Mark was armed in black,

[241-019]horse and all; and so at a privy postern they two issued out with their varlets

[241-020]with them, and rode till they came to that lake. Then Sir Kay espied them

[241-021]first, and gat his spear, and proffered to joust. And King Mark rode against

[241-022]him, and smote each other full hard, for the moon shone as the bright day. And

[241-023]there at that [[tournament|jousts]] Sir Kay’s horse fell down, for his horse was not so

[241-024]big as the king’s horse, and Sir Kay’s horse bruised him full sore.

[241-025]Then Sir Gaheris was wroth that Sir Kay had a fall. Then he cried: Knight, sit

[241-026]thou fast in thy saddle, for I will revenge my fellow. Then King Mark was

[241-027]afeard of Sir Gaheris, and so with evil will King Mark rode against him, and

[241-028]Sir Gaheris gave him such a stroke that he fell down. So then forthwithal Sir

[241-029]Gaheris ran unto Sir Andred and smote him from his horse quite, that his helm

[241-030]smote in the earth, and nigh had broken his neck. And therewithal Sir Gaheris

[241-031]alighted, and gat up Sir Kay. And then they yode both on foot to them, and bade

[241-032]them yield them, and tell their names outher they should die. Then with great

[241-033]pain Sir Andred spake first, and said: It is King Mark of Cornwall, therefore

[241-034]be ye ware what ye do, and I am Sir Andred, his cousin. Fie on you both, said

[241-035]Sir Gaheris, for a false traitor, and false treason hast thou wrought and he

[241-036]both, under the feigned cheer that ye made us! it were [[mercy|pity]], said Sir Gaheris,

[241-037]that thou shouldst live any longer. Save my life, said King Mark, and I will

[241-038]make amends; and consider that I am a king anointed. It were the more shame,

[241-039]said Sir Gaheris, to save thy life; thou art a king anointed with cream, and

[241-040]therefore thou shouldst hold with all men of [[honour|worship]]; and therefore thou art

[241-041]worthy to die. With that he lashed at King Mark without saying any more, and

[241-042]covered him with his shield and defended him as he might. And then Sir Kay

[241-043]lashed at Sir Andred, and therewithal King Mark yielded him unto Sir Gaheris.

[241-044]And then he kneeled adown, and made his oath upon the cross of the sword, that

[241-045]never while he lived he would be against errant-knights. And also he sware to

[241-046]be good friend unto [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]] if ever he came into Cornwall.

[241-047]By then Sir Andred was on the earth, and Sir Kay would have slain him. Let be,

[241-048]said Sir Gaheris, slay him not I pray you. It were pity, said Sir Kay, that he

[241-049]should live any longer, for this is nigh cousin unto Sir Tristram, and ever he

[241-050]hath been a traitor unto him, and by him he was exiled out of Cornwall, and

[241-051]therefore I will slay him, said Sir Kay. Ye shall not, said Sir Gaheris; sithen

[241-052]I have given the king his life, I pray you give him his life. And therewithal

[241-053]Sir Kay let him go. And so Sir Kay and Sir Gaheris rode their way unto Dinas,

[241-054]the Seneschal, for because they heard say that he loved well Sir Tristram. So

[241-055]they reposed them there, and soon after they rode unto the realm of Logris. And

[241-056]so within a little while they met with [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]] that always had Dame

[241-057]Bragwaine with him, to that intent he weened to have met the sooner with Sir

[241-058]Tristram; and Sir Launcelot asked what tidings in Cornwall, and whether they

[241-059]heard of Sir Tristram or not. Sir Kay and Sir Gaheris answered and said, that

[241-060]they heard not of him. Then they told Sir Launcelot word by word of their

[241-061]adventure. Then Sir Launcelot smiled and said: Hard it is to take out of the

[241-062]flesh that is bred in the bone; and so made them merry together.
