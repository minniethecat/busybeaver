[199-001]King Howel of Brittany, and slew his adversary in the [[battle|field]].

[199-002]There was an earl that hight Grip, and this earl made great war upon the king,

[199-003]and put the king to the worse, and besieged him. And on a time Sir Kehydius,

[199-004]that was son to King Howel, as he issued out he was sore wounded, nigh to the

[199-005]death. Then Gouvernail went to the king and said: Sir, I counsel you to desire

[199-006]my lord, [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]], as in your need to help you. I will do by your counsel,

[199-007]said the king. And so he yede unto Sir Tristram, and prayed him in his wars to

[199-008]help him: For my son, Sir Kehydius, may not go into the field. Sir, said Sir

[199-009]Tristram, I will go to the field and do what I may. Then Sir Tristram issued

[199-010]out of the town with such fellowship as he might make, and did such deeds that

[199-011]all Brittany spake of him. And then, at the last, by great might and force, he

[199-012]slew the Earl Grip with his own hands, and more than an hundred knights he slew

[199-013]that day. And then Sir Tristram was received worshipfully with procession. Then

[199-014]King Howel embraced him in his arms, and said: Sir Tristram, all my kingdom I

[199-015]will resign to thee. God defend, said Sir Tristram, for I am beholden unto you

[199-016]for your daughter’s sake to do for you.

[199-017]Then by the great means of King Howel and Kehydius his son, by great proffers,

[199-018]there grew great love betwixt Isoud and Sir Tristram, for that lady was both

[199-019]good and fair, and a woman of noble blood and fame. And for because Sir

[199-020]Tristram had such cheer and riches, and all other pleasaunce that he had,

[199-021]almost he had forsaken La Beale Isoud. And so upon a time Sir Tristram agreed

[199-022]to wed Isoud la Blanche Mains. And at the last they were wedded, and solemnly

[199-023]held their marriage. And so when they were abed both Sir Tristram remembered

[199-024]him of his old lady La Beale Isoud. And then he took such a thought suddenly

[199-025]that he was all dismayed, and other cheer made he none but with clipping and

[199-026]kissing; as for other fleshly lusts Sir Tristram never thought nor had ado with

[199-027]her: such mention maketh the French book; also it maketh mention that the lady

[199-028]weened there had been no pleasure but kissing and clipping. And in the meantime

[199-029]there was a knight in Brittany, his name was Suppinabiles, and he came over the

[199-030]sea into England, and then he came into [[camelot|the court of King Arthur]], and there he

[199-031]met with [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot du Lake]], and told him of the marriage of Sir Tristram.

[199-032]Then said Sir Launcelot: Fie upon him, untrue knight to his lady, that so noble

[199-033]a knight as Sir Tristram is should be found to his first lady false, La Beale

[199-034]Isoud, Queen of Cornwall; but say ye him this, said Sir Launcelot, that of all

[199-035]knights in the world I loved him most, and had most joy of him, and all was for

[199-036]his noble deeds; and let him wit the love between him and me is done for ever,

[199-037]and that I give him warning from this day forth as his mortal enemy.
