[180-001]Bleoberis for a lady, and how the lady was put to choice to whom she would

[180-002]go.

[180-003]Then [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]] rode more than a pace until that he had overtaken him. Then

[180-004]spake Sir Tristram: Abide, he said, Knight of [[king-arthur|Arthur]]’s court, bring again

[180-005]that lady, or deliver her to me. I will do neither, said Bleoberis, for I dread

[180-006]no Cornish knight so sore that me list to deliver her. Why, said Sir Tristram,

[180-007]may not a Cornish knight do as well as another knight? this same day two

[180-008]knights of your court within this three mile met with me, and or ever we

[180-009]departed they found a Cornish knight good enough for them both. What were their

[180-010]names? said Bleoberis. They told me, said Sir Tristram, that the one of them

[180-011]hight Sir Sagramore le Desirous, and the other hight Dodinas le Savage. Ah,

[180-012]said Sir Bleoberis, have ye met with them? so God me help, they were two good

[180-013]knights and men of great [[honour|worship]], and if ye have beat them both ye must needs

[180-014]be a good knight; but if it so be ye have beat them both, yet shall ye not fear

[180-015]me, but ye shall beat me or ever ye have this lady. Then defend you, said Sir

[180-016]Tristram. So they departed and came together like thunder, and either bare

[180-017]other down, horse and all, to the earth.

[180-018]Then they avoided their horses, and lashed together eagerly with swords, and

[180-019]mightily, now tracing and traversing on the right hand and on the left hand

[180-020]more than two hours. And sometime they rushed together with such a might that

[180-021]they lay both grovelling on the ground. Then Sir Bleoberis de Ganis stert

[180-022]aback, and said thus: Now, gentle good knight, a while hold your hands, and let

[180-023]us speak together. Say what ye will, said Tristram, and I will answer you. Sir,

[180-024]said Bleoberis, I would wit of whence ye be, and of whom ye be come, and what

[180-025]is your name? So God me help, said Sir Tristram, I fear not to tell you my

[180-026]name. Wit ye well I am King Meliodas’ son, and my mother is King

[180-027]Mark’s sister, and my name is Sir Tristram de Liones, and [[king-mark|King Mark]] is

[180-028]mine uncle. Truly, said Bleoberis, I am right glad of you, for ye are he that

[180-029]slew Marhaus the knight, hand for hand in an island, for the truage of

[180-030]Cornwall; also ye overcame Sir Palamides the good knight, at a tournament in an

[180-031]island, where ye beat [[sir-gawaine|Sir Gawaine]] and his nine fellows. So God me help, said

[180-032]Sir Tristram, wit ye well that I am the same knight; now I have told you my

[180-033]name, tell me yours with good will. Wit ye well that my name is Sir Bleoberis

[180-034]de Ganis, and my brother hight Sir Blamore de Ganis, that is called a good

[180-035]knight, and we be sister’s children unto my lord [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot du Lake]],

[180-036]that we call one of the best knights of the world. That is truth, said Sir

[180-037]Tristram, Sir Launcelot is called peerless of courtesy and of knighthood; and

[180-038]for his sake, said Sir Tristram, I will not with my good will [[battle|fight]] no more

[180-039]with you, for the great love I have to Sir Launcelot du Lake. In [[faith|good faith]],

[180-040]said Bleoberis, as for me I will be loath to fight with you; but sithen ye

[180-041]follow me here to have this lady, I shall proffer you kindness, courtesy, and

[180-042]gentleness right here upon this ground. This lady shall be betwixt us both, and

[180-043]to whom that she will go, let him have her in peace. I will well, said

[180-044]Tristram, for, as I deem, she will leave you and come to me. Ye shall prove it

[180-045]anon, said Bleoberis.
