# Sir Gilmere

Sir Gilmere was a knight who crossed paths with [[Sir Launcelot]] during the adventures recounted in the Book of Sir Tristram. His encounter with Launcelot reveals the perilous world of knight-errantry, where even seasoned warriors could fall to a single stroke from a greater champion.

## Encounter in the Forest

Sir Gilmere came upon a scene of violence in the forest, where a knight lay slain. He identified the body and declared to his companions the identity of the fallen warrior, recognising the handiwork of a formidable opponent. The suddenness of the death underscored the ever-present danger of the forest paths where knights rode in search of adventure (120-020).

## The Pursuit of Sir Launcelot

Together with his companions, Sir Gilmere rode in pursuit of the knight responsible for the slaying. They overtook [[Sir Launcelot]], whose reputation as the greatest knight in the world was already well established. Gilmere's willingness to pursue such a renowned warrior speaks to his courage and sense of knightly duty, even when facing an opponent of overwhelming prowess (120-026).

## Wounded and Spared

When the confrontation came, Sir Gilmere was struck down by Launcelot. Yet Launcelot, who rarely killed a fellow knight of [[King Arthur]]'s realm if it could be avoided, bade Gilmere to arise and helped him to his feet. The mercy shown to Gilmere illustrates Launcelot's code of honour — to defeat but not to destroy, to humble but not to annihilate (120-035).

## A Knight Among Many

Sir Gilmere belongs to the company of lesser-known knights whose encounters with the great champions illuminate the chivalric world of [[Le Morte d'Arthur]]. His brief appearance in the narrative serves to demonstrate both the lethal efficiency of [[Sir Launcelot]] in combat and the courtesy that tempered that efficiency.
