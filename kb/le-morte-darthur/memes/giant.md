The **giant** is the supreme monstrous antagonist in Malory's [[Le Morte d'Arthur]], a being of immense size and strength whose defeat represents the ultimate test of knightly [[prowess]]. Unlike the human opponent who [[battle|fight]]s within the codes of [[chivalry]], the giant stands outside those codes—a creature of brute force, tyranny, and unnatural appetite whose destruction is an unqualified good. To slay a giant is to perform the most legible form of heroism available to the [[king-arthur|Arthur]]ian knight.

## The Giant as Tyrant

Giants in Malory are defined not merely by their size but by their relationship to [[realm|land]] and people. They are usurpers who seize territory, plunder villages, and hold knights [[prison|prisoner]]:

> there was a giant fast by him that destroyed all his lands (091-026)

The giant's castle contains the evidence of his tyranny—captive knights freed only when the hero prevails:

> there he delivered many knights out of the giant's prison (091-042)

The giant is thus not only a physical monster but a political one, representing the collapse of rightful [[baron|lord]]ship into mere force. His defeat restores order to the [[realm|land]].

## Combat with Giants

Fighting a giant requ[[wrath|ire]]s a different approach from the [[joust]] or knightly duel. Giants wield clubs and iron [[arms|weapons]], not [[sword|swords]] and [[spear|lances]], and they [[battle|fight]] with cunning as well as force:

> the giant took an iron club in his hand... the giant was a wily fighter (091-033, 091-035)

The knight must adapt, using terrain and agility against an opponent who cannot be matched in strength alone:

> with stones the knight gave the giant many sore knocks (091-040)

Victory over a giant is never a matter of refined technique but of cou[[wrath|rage]], [[hardiness|endurance]], and the willingness to [[battle|fight]] on unfavourable terms. It is the most elemental form of knightly [[battle|combat]].

## The Giant as Measure of Prowess

To be compared to a giant is, in Malory, the highest compliment that can be paid to a knight's martial capacity:

> for he fought more liker a giant than a knight (131-026)

This comparison—applied to [[sir-gareth|Gareth]] in the height of his battle-[[wrath|fury]]—captures the ambivalent status of giant-strength in the chivalric imagination. It is admirable in its power but unsettling in its implications, suggesting a violence that strains against the civilising norms of knighthood. The knight who [[battle|fight]]s like a giant is fearsome; the knight who becomes like a giant risks losing the very [[honour]] that distinguishes him from the monster he [[battle|fight]]s.
