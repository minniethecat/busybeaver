[191-001]Carados bearing away [[sir-gawaine|Sir Gawaine]], and of the rescue of Sir Gawaine.

[191-002]And then [[sir-tristram|Sir Tristram]] took his leave when he saw his time, and took the sea.

[191-003]And in the meanwhile word came unto [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]] and to Sir Tristram that Sir

[191-004]Carados, the mighty king, that was made like a giant, fought with Sir Gawaine,

[191-005]and gave him such strokes that he swooned in his saddle, and after that he took

[191-006]him by the collar and pulled him out of his saddle, and fast bound him to the

[191-007]saddle-bow, and so rode his way with him toward his castle. And as he rode, by

[191-008]fortune Sir Launcelot met with Sir Carados, and anon he knew Sir Gawaine that

[191-009]lay bound after him. Ah, said Sir Launcelot unto Sir Gawaine, how stands it

[191-010]with you? Never so hard, said Sir Gawaine, unless that ye help me, for so God

[191-011]me help, without ye rescue me I know no knight that may, but outher you or Sir

[191-012]Tristram. Wherefore Sir Launcelot was heavy of Sir Gawaine’s words. And

[191-013]then Sir Launcelot bade Sir Carados: Lay down that knight and [[battle|fight]] with me.

[191-014]Thou art but a fool, said Sir Carados, for I will serve you in the same wise.

[191-015]As for that, said Sir Launcelot, spare me not, for I warn thee I will not spare

[191-016]thee. And then he bound Sir Gawaine hand and foot, and so threw him to the

[191-017]ground. And then he gat his spear of his squire, and departed from Sir

[191-018]Launcelot to fetch his course. And so either met with other, and brake their

[191-019]spears to their hands; and then they pulled out swords, and hurtled together on

[191-020]horseback more than an hour. And at the last Sir Launcelot smote Sir Carados

[191-021]such a buffet upon the helm that it pierced his brain-pan. So then Sir

[191-022]Launcelot took Sir Carados by the collar and pulled him under his horse’s

[191-023]feet, and then he alighted and pulled off his helm and struck off his head. And

[191-024]then Sir Launcelot unbound Sir Gawaine. So this same tale was told to Sir

[191-025]Galahad and to Sir Tristram:—here may ye hear the nobleness that

[191-026]followeth Sir Launcelot. Alas, said Sir Tristram, an I had not this message in

[191-027]hand with this fair lady, truly I would never stint or I had found Sir

[191-028]Launcelot. Then Sir Tristram and La Beale Isoud went to the sea and came into

[191-029]Cornwall, and there all the barons met them.
