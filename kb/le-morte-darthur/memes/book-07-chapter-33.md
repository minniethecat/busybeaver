[160-002]Linet.

[160-003]So the duke departed, and [[sir-gareth|Sir Gareth]] stood there alone; and there he saw an

[160-004]armed knight coming toward him. Then Sir Gareth took the duke’s shield,

[160-005]and mounted upon horseback, and so without biding they ran together as it had

[160-006]been the thunder. And there that knight hurt Sir Gareth under the side with his

[160-007]spear. And then they alighted and drew their swords, and gave great strokes

[160-008]that the blood trailed to the ground. And so they fought two hours.

[160-009]At the last there came the damosel Linet, that some men called the damosel

[160-010]Savage, and she came riding upon an ambling mule; and there she cried all on

[160-011]high, [[sir-gawaine|Sir Gawaine]], Sir Gawaine, leave thy fighting with thy brother Sir Gareth.

[160-012]And when he heard her say so he threw away his shield and his sword, and ran to

[160-013]Sir Gareth, and took him in his arms, and sithen kneeled down and asked him

[160-014]mercy.

[160-015]What are ye, said Sir Gareth, that right now were so strong and so mighty, and

[160-016]now so suddenly yield you to me? O Gareth, I am your brother Sir Gawaine, that

[160-017]for your sake have had great sorrow and labour.

[160-018]Then Sir Gareth unlaced his helm, and kneeled down to him, and asked him mercy.

[160-019]Then they rose both, and embraced either other in their arms, and wept a great

[160-020]while or they might speak, and either of them gave other the prize of the

[160-021]battle. And there were many kind words between them.

[160-022]Alas, my fair brother, said Sir Gawaine, perdy I owe of right to [[honour|worship]] you an

[160-023]ye were not my brother, for ye have worshipped [[king-arthur|King Arthur]] and all his court,

[160-024]for ye have sent me more worshipful knights this twelvemonth than six the best

[160-025]of the [[round-table|Round Table]] have done, except [[sir-launcelot|Sir Launcelot]].

[160-026]Then came the damosel Savage that was the Lady Linet, that rode with Sir Gareth

[160-027]so long, and there she did staunch Sir Gareth’s wounds and Sir

[160-028]Gawaine’s. Now what will ye do? said the damosel Savage; meseemeth that

[160-029]it were well done that Arthur had witting of you both, for your horses are so

[160-030]bruised that they may not bear. Now, fair damosel, said Sir Gawaine, I pray you

[160-031]ride unto my lord mine uncle, King Arthur, and tell him what adventure is to me

[160-032]betid here, and I suppose he will not tarry long. Then she took her mule, and

[160-033]lightly she came to King Arthur that was but two mile thence. And when she had

[160-034]told him tidings the king bade get him a palfrey. And when he was upon his back

[160-035]he bade the lords and ladies come after, who that would; and there was saddling

[160-036]and bridling of queens’ horses and princes’ horses, and well was

[160-037]him that soonest might be ready.

[160-038]So when the king came thereas they were, he saw Sir Gawaine and Sir Gareth sit

[160-039]upon a little hill-side, and then the king avoided his horse. And when he came

[160-040]nigh Sir Gareth he would have spoken but he might not; and therewith he sank

[160-041]down in a swoon for gladness. And so they stert unto their uncle, and required

[160-042]him of his good grace to be of good comfort. Wit ye well the king made great

[160-043]joy, and many a piteous complaint he made to Sir Gareth, and ever he wept as he

[160-044]had been a child. With that came his mother, [[queen-guenever|the Queen]] of Orkney, Dame

[160-045]Morgawse, and when she saw Sir Gareth readily in the visage she might not weep,

[160-046]but suddenly fell down in a swoon, and lay there a great while like as she had

[160-047]been dead. And then Sir Gareth recomforted his mother in such wise that she

[160-048]recovered and made good cheer. Then the king commanded that all manner of

[160-049]knights that were under his obeissance should make their lodging right there

[160-050]for the love of his nephews. And so it was done, and all manner of purveyance

[160-051]purveyed, that there lacked nothing that might be gotten of tame nor wild for

[160-052]gold or silver. And then by the means of the damosel Savage Sir Gawaine and Sir

[160-053]Gareth were healed of their wounds; and there they sojourned eight days.

[160-054]Then said King Arthur unto the damosel Savage: I marvel that your sister, Dame

[160-055]Lionesse, cometh not here to me, and in especial that she cometh not to visit

[160-056]her knight, my nephew Sir Gareth, that hath had so much travail for her love.

[160-057]My lord, said the damosel Linet, ye must of your good grace hold her excused,

[160-058]for she knoweth not that my lord, Sir Gareth, is here. Go then for her, said

[160-059]King Arthur, that we may be appointed what is best to be done, according to the

[160-060]pleasure of my nephew. Sir, said the damosel, that shall be done, and so she

[160-061]rode unto her sister. And as lightly as she might she made her ready; and she

[160-062]came on the morn with her brother Sir Gringamore, and with her forty knights.

[160-063]And so when she was come she had all the cheer that might be done, both of the

[160-064]king, and of many other kings and queens.
