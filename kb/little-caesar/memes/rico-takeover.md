Rico Bandello's seizure of control from Sam Vettori is the novel's pivotal power transition — the moment ambition overtakes loyalty and a new era of gangster leadership begins.

## Prelude

Before the takeover, the Vettori gang operates under Sam Vettori's cautious leadership. Vettori is the old-school boss: he delegates to specialists, avoids unnecessary violence, and respects the Big Boy's rules. But his lieutenants, especially Rico, chafe under this conservatism. Rico "hated Joe" Massara [002-014] and viewed Vettori's caution as weakness.

The Courriere holdup [009-011] provides the catalyst. During the operation, Rico takes command, directing Otero and overriding Vettori's authority on the scene. Vettori's desperate plea — "for the love of God, no gunwork" [009-015] — marks him as the old generation, afraid of the violence his business requires.

## The Confrontation

After the holdup, Rico confronts Vettori directly. His declaration — "I been taking orders too long. We're done" [011-063] — is not a negotiation but a fait accompli. Vettori, recognizing his own weakness, does not resist. The takeover is complete not through violence but through the sheer force of Rico's will and Vettori's recognition that he cannot match it.

## Aftermath

Rico immediately restructures the gang's operations, absorbing Little Arnie's gambling territory and expanding aggressively. Vettori fades into irrelevance, a ghost of the old order who serves as a warning of what happens to those who cannot keep up. The gang is now Rico's instrument — leaner, more violent, and more ambitious than anything Vettori could have imagined.

## Significance

Rico's takeover represents the novel's central generational argument: the old guard's caution cannot survive in a world that rewards ruthlessness. Vettori's fall is not tragic but inevitable — the weak yielding to the strong in a system that recognizes no other principle of succession.
