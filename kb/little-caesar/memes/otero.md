# Otero

**Otero**, called **"The Greek,"** is a gunman in [[vettori-gang|Sam Vettori's Chicago gang]] and one of the crew's trusted specialists. A dark-complexioned immigrant with a volatile temperament, he serves as a trigger man during the [[courriere-holdup]] and represents the professional criminal class that populates Burnett's gangster underworld.

## Role in the Novel

Otero functions as a member of Vettori's inner circle — a specialist gunman whose ethnic otherness (marked by his nickname "The Greek") sets him apart from the Italian core of the gang. He participates in the pivotal Courriere holdup, handles firearms alongside [[rico-bandello|Rico Bandello]], and his eventual fate serves as an early warning signal of the gang's disintegration under Rico's ambition. His disappearance from the narrative after the early chapters mirrors the dissolution of the old Vettori order. [002-002] [002-055]

## Biography

Otero first appears at a round table in the back room of the [[palermo-club]], playing stud poker alongside Tony Passa and Rico Bandello under a green-shaded lamp that makes his "dark face looked livid and cavernous" [002-002]. He wins the hand that night, and Vettori soon assigns him, together with Rico, to handle the firearms ("rods") for the Courriere holdup [002-055].

> "Rico and Otero," said Vettori, looking at each in turn, "will handle the rods. Yeah?" [002-055]

During the heist planning, Otero displays his characteristic vehemence — at one point "broke in vehemently, surprising them" [002-063] — and afterward claps his hands in celebration at the success [002-082]. Rico slaps him on the back in a rare moment of camaraderie [002-084].

Otero's private life is glimpsed through his relationship with a woman nicknamed "Seal Skin," whom he visits after the holdup [002-093]. In a later chapter, he lies in bed with Seal Skin, smoking a twenty-five cent cigar and singing softly to himself as snow falls past the windows [004-001] [004-011]. This domestic scene — tender and almost peaceful — is one of the novel's few depictions of a gangster at rest, and it stands in stark contrast to the violence that defines his professional life.

> Otero lay looking out the window at the electric sign across the Street. ... Snow was falling past the windows. Otero lay smoking a big twenty-five cent cigar and singing softly to himself. He always sang when he was with Seal Skin. [004-001] [004-011]

## Key Scenes

**The card game** — Otero wins a stud poker hand under the green lamp, his dark face illuminated in the back room of Palermo Club. This scene establishes him as a competent, self-possessed professional among the gang's core members. [002-002] [002-016] [002-020]

**The holdup assignment** — Vettori designates Otero to handle firearms alongside Rico, underscoring Otero's trusted status as a specialist whose technical skill is valued above personal loyalty or ethnic solidarity. [002-055–056]

**The domestic interlude** — In one of the novel's rare quiet moments, Otero lies in bed with Seal Skin, cigar in hand, singing softly as snow falls. This scene humanizes the gunman and suggests a private world beyond the gang's violent enterprise. [004-001] [004-011]

## Significance

Otero embodies the professional criminal specialist — technically skilled, ethnically marked as an outsider ("The Greek"), and ultimately disposable. His role in the Courriere holdup is essential but unglamorous; he handles the tools of violence without claiming the leadership or ambition that defines Rico. His quiet domestic scene with Seal Skin provides one of the novel's few glimpses of gangster interiority — a man who, away from the card table and the heist, finds contentment in a cigar and a woman's company. That Otero vanishes from the narrative without fanfare reinforces Burnett's vision of the underworld as a machine that consumes its parts without ceremony.
