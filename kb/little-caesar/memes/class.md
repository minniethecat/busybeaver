Class is the invisible structure that determines every character's place in the world of *Little Caesar* — and the force that gangster ambition futilely attempts to overcome.

## Definition

Though never named as such, class consciousness pervades *Little Caesar*. The gangsters are immigrants and children of immigrants, locked out of legitimate paths to wealth and status. Their criminal careers are not just economic strategies but class rebellions — attempts to seize by force what the social order denies them by birth. Yet the novel is clear-eyed about the limits of this rebellion: no amount of money makes Rico the equal of the men on the Gold Coast.

## In the Novel

- **Little Italy vs. the Gold Coast** — The novel's geography is a map of class: Little Italy (working-class immigrant enclave), the North Side (established criminal wealth), the Gold Coast (legitimate old money). Rico's territorial conquests trace an attempt to climb this hierarchy, culminating in his fascination with the Gold Coast residents who regard him as beneath notice.

- **The respectable front** — Pete Montana's "respectable-looking" appearance [036-038] represents the gangster's class aspiration: to pass as legitimate. His quiet dress, avoidance of jewelry, and cane are props in a performance of class belonging that the Big Boy can revoke at will.

- **Rico's raw ambition** — Rico "loved but three things: himself, his hair and his gun" [007-002]. He has no cultural capital, no family connections, no legitimate path upward. His gun is his class mobility — crude, violent, and ultimately self-defeating.

- **Joe Massara's escape** — Joe attempts to exit the gangster class through show business, a parallel illegitimate path. His dancing career offers a different kind of respectability — still marginal, still precarious, but not violent. The novel treats both paths as gambles against a stacked deck.

## Thematic Function

Burnett, writing in 1929 on the eve of the Depression, captures the profound class anxiety of his era. The gangsters of *Little Caesar* are not moral monsters but economic actors — men who have chosen the only path they see toward the American promise of wealth and status. That the path destroys them is not a moral judgment but a sociological observation: the system that excludes them also punishes their attempts to force entry.
