# Rico's Apartment

The door is found unlocked ([029-001]) — a detail that says everything about the precariousness of gangster domesticity. Rico's apartment is where he retreats to read newspapers, to plan, to seethe. It is one of the few spaces in Burnett's novel that belongs to Rico alone, and it is correspondingly vulnerable.

## Overview

Unlike the public spaces of the Palermo Club or the back rooms of Little Italy, Rico's apartment is a private interior where the performance of gangster authority pauses. Here he "bought all the papers he could find and went up to his room to read them" ([015-001]) — the obsessive tracking of his own press coverage revealing a hunger for recognition that the streets alone cannot satisfy. The apartment is sparse, functional, a place of temporary refuge rather than domestic comfort.

## Significance in the Story

The apartment's unlocked door ([029-001]) captures the central irony of Rico's situation: as his power grows, his vulnerability increases. The same ambition that drives him out of Little Italy also ensures he can never truly be safe anywhere. The domestic space that should offer sanctuary instead becomes another front — a place where enemies could be waiting, where the gangster code follows him home.

Rico's relationship to his apartment reveals his fundamental solitude. Unlike the married Vettori with his domestic complications, Rico moves through rooms alone, the space around him defined by absence — no wife, no family, only the newspapers that tell him who he is supposed to be.

## Key Scenes at This Location

After the Courriere holdup and the killing of Tony Passa, "Rico bought all the papers he could find and went up to his room to read them" ([015-001]). This moment of private obsession — the gangster alone with his press coverage — is one of the novel's most psychologically revealing scenes. Rico needs to see himself reflected in print, needs the public validation that even a successful robbery cannot provide.

Rico "found the door of his apartment unlocked" ([029-001]) after his ascension to gang leadership. The detail is chilling in its ambiguity — was someone there, or is this simply the new normal for a man whose enemies multiply with every promotion?

The gang's banquet "in one of Sam Vettori's big back rooms" ([026-001]) contrasts with Rico's private apartment: Vettori's spaces are communal and performative; Rico's are solitary and anxious.

## Real-World Context

A gang leader's apartment in 1920s Chicago would likely have been a modest flat in a tenement or a room in a boarding house — not the luxurious suite of later gangster mythology. Burnett's portrayal of Rico's apartment as sparse and functional aligns with the historical reality of Prohibition-era gangsters who often maintained humble domestic arrangements even as their criminal wealth accumulated, partly from habit and partly from the practical need to avoid attention.
