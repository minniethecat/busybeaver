Pete Montana's syndicate, the dominant organized crime operation controlling Chicago's North Side during the Prohibition era, led by Pietro Fontano ("Pete Montana") with ex-prizefighter Ritz Colonna as his lieutenant.

## Overview

The Montana syndicate is the largest and most established criminal organization in W.R. Burnett's *Little Caesar* — a vast North Side empire built on narcotics trafficking, the alcohol racket, gambling, and vice. Unlike the scrappy Vettori gang of Little Italy or the precarious Little Arnie operation in the neutral zone, Montana's organization represents the entrenched old guard: wealthy, politically connected, and "magnificently indifferent" to the territorial squabbles of smaller gangs. At the novel's outset, Pete Montana is the most powerful gang leader in Chicago, but his position is increasingly threatened by Rico Bandello's meteoric rise — a challenge Montana attempts to defuse through negotiation rather than violence, revealing the fear beneath his respectable facade.

## Territory & Operations

The syndicate's territory encompasses the entire North Side of Chicago — the wealthiest and most lucrative criminal domain in the city. Montana previously controlled the neutral zone (later occupied by Little Arnie) during the Kips Berger era but allowed it to decline through neglect, a decision he later regrets as Rico begins consolidating it [036-058].

**Revenue streams**:

- **Narcotics** — The syndicate's financial foundation. The Big Boy reveals Montana "peddles them" on an industrial scale: "He built that big house of his on 'em" [038-044]. Federal authorities can be mobilized against him on narcotics charges at any time, giving the Big Boy leverage.
- **Alcohol racket** — Montana controls North Side bootlegging and uses access to the alcohol trade as a bargaining chip, offering Rico a share if they "get along" [036-079].
- **Gambling and vice** — A network of gambling joints, brothels, and vice dens across the North Side, protected by political connections.
- **Protection** — Montana "could get all the protection I wanted" from political and police sources, the hallmark of an established syndicate [036-060].

Montana's operational style is corporate rather than confrontational. He delegates heavily, avoids direct violence, and relies on his political connections and the Big Boy's mediation to maintain his position — a strategy that leaves him vulnerable to hungrier, more aggressive rivals.

## Key Events

- **The Rico summit (c. Chapter VII-VIII)** — Montana, accompanied by Ritz Colonna, visits Rico at Little Arnie's former gambling joint to negotiate. Recognizing Rico as a threat, Montana attempts to buy peace by ceding "Kiketown" territory and dangling access to the alcohol racket. Rico sees through the gambit: "So this was the great Pete Montana. A guy that couldn't turn over in bed without getting plastered all over the front page. All that softie stuff was a front. Pete Montana was scared" [036-066]. The meeting ends with a handshake but no genuine resolution [036-030]–[036-079].
- **The Big Boy's restructuring** — At a dinner meeting, the Big Boy informs Rico that "Pete Montana's through" [038-044]. The syndicate is to be dismantled from above: the police "Old Man" will pressure Montana to leave town, and the Big Boy will turn Federal narcotics agents onto him if necessary. Ritz Colonna and other lieutenants are expected to "make a rush" when Montana falls, triggering a violent succession struggle [038-042].
- **Montana's exile (implied)** — The Big Boy's plan succeeds. Montana's organization is dissolved, and he is forced out of Chicago. Rico's subsequent reflection on his own downfall is haunted by thoughts of "Montana and Little Arnie and the Big Boy" — the three power figures whose fates foreshadow his own [048-002].

## Significance

The Montana syndicate represents the old guard of organized crime in *Little Caesar* — wealthy, complacent, and politically entrenched, but fatally softened by success. Pete Montana's fate is a mirror held up to Rico Bandello: both men rise through violence and cunning, both believe themselves untouchable, and both are ultimately discarded by the same system that elevated them. Montana's downfall — orchestrated not by rival gangsters but by the Big Boy, the shadowy power broker who transcends individual organizations — reveals the true hierarchy of the novel's criminal ecosystem. The gang leaders are interchangeable; the system endures.

Rico recognizes this too late. In his final exile, he thinks "about Montana and Little Arnie and the Big Boy" [048-002] — the three figures whose trajectories map the life cycle of a gangster: rise, peak, and fall. Montana's story is the cautionary tale Rico failed to heed.
