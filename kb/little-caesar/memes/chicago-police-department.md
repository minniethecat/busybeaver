# Chicago Police Department

The **Chicago Police Department** functions in *Little Caesar* as the institutional antagonist — the law enforcement apparatus that pursues, investigates, and ultimately destroys the novel's gangster protagonists. It appears not as a unified character but as a diffuse presence: individual detectives, patrolmen, and investigators whose collective pressure shapes every gangster's calculations.

## Overview

Burnett depicts the police not as heroes but as a structural force — part of the same urban ecosystem as the criminals they pursue. Detectives like [[flaherty|Flaherty]] and [[courtney|Courtney]] are competent professionals, neither corrupt nor crusading. They do their jobs, follow leads, apply pressure, and wait for the gangsters to make mistakes. The novel's police work is methodical rather than dramatic — surveillance, interrogation, informants, the slow accumulation of evidence — and its effectiveness lies precisely in this methodical quality.

## Role in the Novel

The police presence in *Little Caesar* is pervasive rather than focal. Officers appear at key moments: the investigation after the Casa Alvarado robbery, Joe Massara's arrest and interrogation, the stakeouts and surveillance that gradually close the net around Rico. But they are rarely named and never developed as characters. This is deliberate: the police are the environment, the condition of the gangster's existence, not a parallel drama.

## Key Personnel

- Sergeant [[flaherty|Flaherty]] — homicide investigator who pursues the Vettori gang
- [[courtney|Courtney]] — police captain involved in the gang investigation
- Various unnamed patrolmen, detectives, and desk officers

## Significance

The Chicago Police Department in Burnett's novel represents the institutional counterweight to organized crime — the force that makes the gangster's life one of constant vigilance and eventual destruction. The novel is clear-eyed about police limitations (they cannot prevent crime, only react to it) but also about police effectiveness: patience, procedure, and the gangster's own mistakes will eventually deliver every criminal to justice. The police are not exciting — and in this, Burnett suggests, lies their power.
