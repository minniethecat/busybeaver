# Loyalty

**Loyalty** is the most invoked and most violated value in *Little Caesar* — the gangster's cardinal virtue, endlessly demanded and almost never delivered. Burnett's novel is, among other things, a systematic demolition of the idea that criminal organizations are held together by anything other than mutual self-interest and the threat of violence.

## In the Novel

Every major character in the novel demands loyalty and betrays it. Sam Vettori demands it from Rico and is overthrown; Rico demands it from Joe Massara and is informed upon; the Big Boy demands it from his subordinates and dispenses it to no one. The gangster's code — "stand by your partners," "never rat," "the gang comes first" — is invoked constantly but honored only when convenient.

The novel's key insight is that loyalty in the gangster world is not a bond but a currency: it is given in expectation of return and withdrawn when the return fails. Vettori's loyalty to Rico is contingent on Rico's usefulness; Joe's loyalty to the gang dissolves the moment his own life is threatened; the Big Boy's loyalty is purely instrumental, a tool for maintaining the system. No one in the novel is loyal for its own sake — because loyalty for its own sake is suicide in a world without institutions to enforce it.

## The Paradox of Gangster Loyalty

Burnett identifies the fundamental paradox of criminal loyalty: the gang demands absolute fidelity but provides no institutional structure to guarantee it. The state can enforce contracts; the Church can enforce morality; the family can enforce obligation. The gang can only enforce through violence — and violence, as the novel demonstrates, is a poor guarantor of anything. The moment violence fails (Joe is arrested and Rico cannot reach him), loyalty evaporates. The gangster's bond is revealed as nothing more than a mutual protection racket, and when the protection stops, so does the bond.
