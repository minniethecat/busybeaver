# Willoughby

Mr. Willoughby. A millionaire who moves easily through The Bronze Peacock's exclusive atmosphere — one of Joe Massara's connections in Chicago's legitimate high society, and a marker of the porous boundary between the world of wealth and the world of crime.

## Role in the Novel

Willoughby represents the world that Joe Massara aspires to join — and that Rico can never enter. His presence at The Bronze Peacock shows that the gangster economy and legitimate society are not separate spheres but overlapping ones, connected by shared spaces, shared tastes, and the money that flows between them. ([030-033])

## Biography

Willoughby is introduced by Joe Massara with casual familiarity: "This boy here is Mr. Willoughby, the millionaire." Willoughby's response — "Why bring that up?" — suggests a man who is self-aware enough to be embarrassed by the label, but wealthy enough to bear it anyway. He shakes hands "strenuously," a physical enthusiasm that contrasts with the gangsters' guarded postures. ([030-033], [030-034])

He offers to take Olga next door while Joe talks business with Rico — a gentlemanly gesture that inadvertently facilitates the conversation that will seal Joe's fate. Willoughby's social ease is both charming and naive; he cannot see the violence beneath the surface of the interactions he enables. ([030-040])

When the police close in at The Bronze Peacock, Willoughby tries to comfort Olga and offers to get his lawyer — the reflexes of a man for whom money and connections have always solved problems. But in Burnett's world, the law is not a service to be purchased, and Willoughby's wealth cannot protect Joe from the consequences of his past. ([040-076], [040-086])

## Significance

Willoughby is the novel's most direct representation of the "legitimate" world that the gangsters orbit but can never truly join. His money comes from inheritance or business, not from robbery; his lawyers solve his problems, not his guns. But the novel insists on proximity — Willoughby and Rico breathe the same air at The Bronze Peacock, share the same women's company, and move through the same Chicago night. The boundary between their worlds is thinner than either man would like to admit.
