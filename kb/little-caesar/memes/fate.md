The arc of every gangster in *Little Caesar* bends toward destruction — Burnett presents rise and fall not as possibility but as inevitability.

## Definition

Fate in the novel is not supernatural but structural. The same qualities that enable a gangster's rise — ruthlessness, ambition, the willingness to use violence — guarantee his fall. The system that elevates Rico also destroys him, just as it destroyed Montana, Arnie, and Vettori before him. There is no escape because the mechanism of success and the mechanism of destruction are the same.

## In the Novel

The novel's structure mirrors this fatalism. It opens with Rico ascending and closes with him dying on a foreign street. Between these poles, every victory plants a seed of eventual defeat: the takeover of Vettori's gang creates enemies, the destruction of Arnie attracts attention, the betrayal of Joe Massara produces the informant who will destroy him.

Rico's final thoughts are haunted by "Montana and Little Arnie and the Big Boy" [048-002] — the three figures whose trajectories traced the arc he now completes. He understood their fates intellectually but believed himself exempt. The novel's final argument is that no one is exempt.

## Thematic Function

Burnett's fatalism distinguishes *Little Caesar* from moralistic crime fiction. Rico is not punished for his sins — he is consumed by a system whose logic he mastered but could not transcend. The novel is less a tragedy than a documentary of inevitability.
