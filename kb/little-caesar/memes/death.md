# Death

Death. The ultimate horizon of the gangster's world in *Little Caesar* — always present, always approaching, and the final measure of a life lived by the gun.

## Definition

In Burnett's novel, death is not an abstraction but a constant practical reality. It comes in the form of a rival's bullet, a police noose, or the slow death of exile and obscurity. The gangster's relationship with death defines his character — how he faces it, inflicts it, or flees from it.

## In the Novel

Death punctuates the novel at every turn. Tony Passa is found dead near St. Dominick's Cathedral. (PN021-021) Police Captain Courtney is killed during the Courriere holdup. (PN015-001) Rico himself kills and orders killings as instruments of his rise. The old gang leader who is superseded faces "two alternatives: flight or death." (PN032-005) Joe fears "arrest and hanging" constantly. (PN019-002)

For Rico, death is the price of failure. Bat Carillo knows that "hanging was just over the horizon, and Rico's gun promised an even more certain death." (PN025-003) The $5,000 reward offered for Rico "dead or alive" literalizes the price on his head. (PN047-027)

At the novel's end, death comes for Rico not as a dramatic gunfight but as a quiet, almost anonymous end on a Toledo street — the gangster who conquered Chicago dying alone, far from his empire.

## Broader Context

Death in *Little Caesar* functions as the ultimate leveler in a world structured by power and hierarchy. The novel's matter-of-fact treatment of violent death — reported in newspaper headlines alongside society news — reflects Burnett's journalistic sensibility and the desensitization of Prohibition-era America to gang violence. Rico's death, stripped of heroism, makes the novel's final argument: the gangster's life, for all its money and power, ends the same way as any other violent criminal's.
