# St. Dominick's Cathedral

**St. Dominick's Cathedral** is a Catholic church in Chicago referenced in *Little Caesar* — one of the institutional landmarks of the Italian-American community that forms the ethnic background of the novel's gangster world. It represents the religious and cultural traditions that the novel's criminals have largely abandoned but cannot entirely escape.

## Overview

St. Dominick's appears in the novel as part of the geographic and cultural landscape of Chicago's Italian neighborhoods — a physical presence that anchors the community's religious life. For the novel's gangsters, the Church is an institution they no longer belong to but whose rituals and moral authority continue to shape their world, however distantly. [SCN28 R18]

## Significance in the Story

The cathedral is mentioned in connection with the Italian immigrant community — the world of weddings, funerals, and festivals that runs parallel to the gangster's world of stickups and card rooms. It represents the "old country" values that the novel's second-generation gangster characters have rejected but that continue to provide the cultural frame within which they operate. Rico Bandello, who can't even speak Italian, is nevertheless defined by the ethnic community whose church dominates its neighborhood.

## Real-World Context

Catholic churches were central institutions in Chicago's immigrant neighborhoods during the early twentieth century, functioning not only as places of worship but as community centers, social-service providers, and anchors of ethnic identity. A cathedral named for St. Dominic would be consistent with the Italian-American religious landscape, though the specific name may be a Burnett invention or composite.
