# The Beer Racket

The **beer racket** — the illegal manufacture and distribution of beer during Prohibition — is the unstated but essential economic foundation of the gangster world depicted in *Little Caesar*. While the novel focuses on stickups and territorial disputes, the steady income that sustains gangs like [[vettori-gang|Sam Vettori's]] comes from the beer trade, the most profitable sector of Prohibition-era organized crime.

## Definition

"Beer racket" refers to the system of illegal breweries, distribution networks, and retail sales (through speakeasies, nightclubs, and restaurants) that supplied alcohol to a thirsty public during Prohibition. Unlike hard liquor, which was often smuggled from Canada or produced in small stills, beer required industrial-scale brewing operations and extensive distribution — making it a capital-intensive business controlled by organized gangs rather than individual bootleggers.

## In the Novel

Burnett's characters rarely discuss the economics of beer directly — the business is too ordinary, too much a part of daily life, to require exposition. But the evidence is everywhere: the whiskey at the banquet, the drinks at the Bronze Peacock, the crowded cabaret at Sansotta's on New Year's Eve. Every glass of beer and whiskey consumed in the novel is illegal, and the revenue from its sale funds the gangs, corrupts the police, and creates the fortunes that men kill for.

The gang's territorial disputes — over the North Side, the South Side, the "neutral zones" — are fundamentally about the right to sell beer in those territories. When Rico talks about "muscling in," he is talking about taking over someone else's beer route. The violence that defines the novel is ultimately about distribution rights.

## Broader Context

The beer racket was the backbone of Chicago organized crime during Prohibition. Unlike the spectacular stickups and shootings that made newspaper headlines, beer-running was a steady, predictable business — trucks delivering kegs to hundreds of speakeasies, each paying regular protection money. The Capone organization, Burnett's real-world model, built its empire on beer distribution before expanding into other rackets. The novel's gangsters, for all their talk of "jobs" and "scores," are ultimately beer salesmen with guns.
