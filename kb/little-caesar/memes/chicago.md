# Chicago

Chicago. The primary setting of W.R. Burnett's *Little Caesar*, depicting the city's Prohibition-era underworld of gangsters, gambling joints, speakeasies, and territorial warfare. The novel's Chicago is a landscape of opportunity and danger — where ambitious men like Rico Bandello rise from nothing and fall just as fast.

## Overview

The Chicago of *Little Caesar* is not the tourist city of the lakefront and the Loop but the ethnic neighborhoods, gambling dens, and back rooms where organized crime flourished during Prohibition. Burnett's Chicago is divided into territories — the North Side, South Side, Little Italy — each controlled by competing gangs. It is a city where a man with a gun and ambition can build an empire, and where the police, the press, and the politicians are all part of the game. (PN021-003)

## Significance in the Story

Chicago functions as both setting and character in *Little Caesar*. The city's geography of power — the North Side gambling joints, the Little Italy neighborhood, the downtown hotels, the police stations — maps directly onto Rico's rise and fall. Chicago's newspapers report on gang killings with the same dispassion as society events. Its police department wavers between corruption and genuine enforcement. And its ethnic neighborhoods provide both the manpower for the gangs and the communities that shelter them. (PN027-023)

The city is also a symbol of escape and aspiration. Rico dreams of leaving "this Chicago muck" for the clean air of Florida. Chicago Red (Bill Hackett) carries the city's name as his identity. And when Rico finally flees Chicago, his fall accelerates — away from the city, he loses his power base and his sense of who he is. (PN040-013, PN042-043)

## Key Scenes at This Location

**Rico Arrives** — Rico Bandello and Joe Massara arrive in Chicago from Youngstown. The city represents everything they want: power, money, and a new life. (Early chapters)

**Gang Warfare** — The Vettori gang's territory on the city's near West Side becomes the staging ground for Rico's rise. Drive-by shootings, bombings, and intimidation mark the gang wars. (PN025-001)

**The Big Boy's Domain** — The Big Boy operates from a position of supreme authority in the city, suggesting a shadow government above the official one. (PN023-001)

**The Flight** — Rico's desperate flight from Chicago, with a $7,000 reward offered by the City of Chicago for his capture, marks the beginning of his end. (PN047-027)

## Real-World Context

Burnett's Chicago is based on the real Prohibition-era city that produced Al Capone, Johnny Torrio, and the organized crime empires of the 1920s. The novel's geographic details — Halsted Street, Chicago Avenue, the North Side, Little Italy — are real locations. The gang wars, police corruption, and newspaper sensationalism reflect historical reality. Burnett, a Chicago native who worked as a night clerk in a hotel frequented by gangsters, drew from direct observation. The novel captures a specific moment in American urban history when Prohibition turned ordinary criminals into wealthy, powerful figures who could corrupt entire city systems.
