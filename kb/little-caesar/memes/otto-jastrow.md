# Otto Jastrow

Jastrow, the famous Little Italy cop. A Chicago police officer with a reputation for being "on the square" — honest and respected even by the gangsters he policed.

## Role in the Novel

Jastrow appears during a critical moment when Rico Bandello has been shot and lies wounded on the street in Little Italy. As the "famous Little Italy cop," Jastrow represents the thin line between law and the underworld in Burnett's Chicago — a figure who commands respect from both sides. (PN033-053)

## Character Profile

- **Full Name**: Jastrow (first name Otto presumed, used in some references)
- **Aliases**: The famous Little Italy cop
- **Affiliation**: Chicago Police Department
- **Role**: Beat cop, Little Italy district
- **First Appearance**: Chapter 33 (PN033-053)

## Biography

Jastrow pushes through a crowd that has gathered around the wounded Rico Bandello, arriving almost simultaneously with Joe Massara. When he sees Rico bleeding, he wryly observes: "Well, somebody finally put one in you, did they, Rico?"The remark is characteristic — Jastrow knows the gangsters by name and treats violence as an occupational inevitability. (PN033-054)

Jastrow proceeds to take charge of the chaotic scene with professional detachment. He takes out his notebook and writes in it while bantering with the gangsters. When Otero and Killer Pepi carry Rico, Jastrow goes ahead to clear the crowd, using his nightstick — though "the sight of it was enough, the crowd made a path." (PN033-065, PN033-070)

When the crowd becomes unruly outside, Jastrow shouts: "You birds quit your damn yelling and get in off the sidewalk." He represents an older style of urban policing — familiar, direct, and maintaining order through personal authority rather than procedure. (PN033-084)

## Key Scenes

**After the Shooting** — Jastrow arrives at the scene where Rico has been shot. His casual familiarity with the gangsters and dry humor establish the blurred lines between cops and criminals in Burnett's Chicago:

> "Well," said Jastrow, "somebody finally put one in you, did they, Rico?" (PN033-054)

> "Hello, boys," said Jastrow, looking up from his little book. "Your boss got nudged by a hunk of lead." (PN033-066)

## Significance

Jastrow represents a type — the neighborhood cop who knows everyone, commands respect through personal presence rather than institutional authority, and navigates the moral ambiguity of policing a community where crime is embedded in daily life. "They all liked Jastrow, who had the reputation of being on the square" — the highest compliment in a world where most cops could be bought. (PN033-085)

Jastrow's brief but memorable appearance illustrates how deeply organized crime was woven into the fabric of 1920s Chicago. The police didn't wage war on gangsters so much as manage them — a reality Burnett captures with documentary precision.
