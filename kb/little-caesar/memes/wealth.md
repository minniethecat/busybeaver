Wealth in *Little Caesar* is not merely money — it is the visible evidence of having become "somebody," the gangster's only legitimate claim to respect.

## Definition

For Rico Bandello, wealth is aspirational and almost mystical. He does not want money for what it buys but for what it *means*: arrival, dominance, escape from the obscurity of Little Italy. His "instinctive respect for wealth" reflects a recognition that "money was power" [030-035] — the two are interchangeable, and together they constitute the only ladder out of the immigrant ghetto.

## In the Novel

- **Rico's upward gaze** — Rico studies the wealthy with the intensity of an anthropologist. He imagines the Gold Coast residents looking down on him, and he burns to reverse the hierarchy. His ambition is class ambition dressed in gangster clothes.

- **Pete Montana's house** — The Big Boy notes that Montana "built that big house of his" on narcotics money [038-044]. The house is the symbol of criminal success — and its vulnerability to seizure by federal agents represents the fragility of ill-gotten wealth.

- **The banquet** — Rico's banquet at the Little Arnie club is a display of wealth-as-power, a ritual of arrival that declares him the equal of the men he once envied [033-085].

- **The futility of it all** — Wealth does not save Rico. By the novel's end he is hiding in a boarding house, his empire dismantled. The fortune he accumulated is meaningless against the forces that destroy him.

## Thematic Function

Burnett uses wealth to expose the gangster's fundamental contradiction: the pursuit of money promises respectability, but the means of acquiring it guarantee exclusion from respectable society. Rico can accumulate a fortune but can never be the gentleman he imagines. His wealth is simultaneously his greatest achievement and the proof of his irredeemable otherness.
