# Kiketown

**Kiketown** is a Chicago neighborhood mentioned in *Little Caesar's* geographic vocabulary — a term from the city's early twentieth-century ethnic landscape that functions in the novel as a territorial boundary marker, one of the many neighborhood names that defined where one gang's territory ended and another's began.

## Overview

The name "Kiketown" reflects the blunt ethnic mapping of early twentieth-century Chicago, where neighborhoods were designated by the dominant immigrant group — Italian, Jewish, Polish, Irish, Greek — and these designations carried real weight in the geography of organized crime. In Burnett's novel, such neighborhood names are not merely descriptive but operational: they define territory, mark boundaries, and signal which gang controls which streets.

## Significance in the Story

Kiketown is mentioned as a boundary area — a place whose ethnic character defines it as neutral or contested ground in the novel's territorial disputes. Like [[little-italy|Little Italy]] or the [[north-side|North Side]], it is less a specific location than a marker in the novel's symbolic geography of ethnic belonging and criminal jurisdiction.

## Real-World Context

Chicago's early twentieth-century neighborhoods were strongly identified by ethnicity, with boundaries that were both social and spatial. Jewish neighborhoods on the city's West Side (centered on Maxwell Street) were adjacent to Italian, Greek, and Eastern European enclaves, and the borders between them were often the borders between different criminal organizations' territories. Burnett's use of such names reflects the real spatial organization of Chicago's ethnic gangster world.
