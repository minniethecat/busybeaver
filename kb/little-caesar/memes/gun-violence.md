The gun is the central object in *Little Caesar* — the tool that makes the gangster's world possible and the symbol that defines his identity.

## Definition

Guns in Burnett's novel are not merely weapons but extensions of character. Rico's "big automatic" [009-011], Otero's "riot gun" [009-011], the "hooded machine-gun" [009-036] on the police Packard — each weapon defines its bearer's role and status. The novel's world is saturated with firearms, and the ability to use them decisively is the fundamental gangster competence.

## In the Novel

- **Rico's gun as identity** — Rico "loved but three things: himself, his hair and his gun. He took excellent care of all three" [007-002]. The gun is not separate from Rico but constitutive of him — the source of his power, his pride, and ultimately his destruction.

- **The Courriere holdup** — The novel's first major gun operation establishes the rhythm: weapons readied, targets threatened, violence always imminent. Rico pressing his gun against the manager's back [009-107] is the novel's iconic image of power reduced to its simplest form.

- **Gun as equalizer and threat** — Guns make every interaction potentially fatal. Flaherty carries a reputation for violence — "that boy used his rod and argued afterwards" [040-051] — and this reputation is a form of power indistinguishable from the gangsters'.

- **The final gun** — Rico dies by the gun, completing the circle. The weapon that built his empire ends his life. There is no irony in this — just the working-out of a logic established from the novel's first pages.
