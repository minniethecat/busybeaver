# Gold Coast

The **Gold Coast** — Chicago's wealthy lakefront district — functions in *Little Caesar* as a symbol of everything the gangster world cannot have: legitimate wealth, social respectability, and permanent status. It is the world that [[rico-bandello|Rico Bandello]] both despises and desperately craves.

## Overview

Chicago's Gold Coast, centered on North Lake Shore Drive, was in the 1920s one of America's wealthiest neighborhoods — the home of industrialists, department-store magnates, and old-money families whose fortunes predated Prohibition and would survive its repeal. For the gangsters of Burnett's novel, it represents an unbridgeable distance: they can take money from the city's commerce but can never enter its society.

## Significance in the Story

Rico's relationship with the Gold Coast is pure resentment. He imagines the people in "the Big Boy's wonderful apartment" — not on the Gold Coast proper but in the same orbit of legitimate luxury — and compares it bitterly to his own circumstances: "The damn dressed-up softies. Got everything in the world and never had to turn a hand for it." The line captures the class resentment that fuels Rico's ambition: he wants not just money but recognition, not just power but admission to a world that will never admit him. [047-012]

## Key Themes

The Gold Coast embodies the novel's central irony of gangster ambition: the gangster can accumulate wealth but cannot convert it into status. The Big Boy's apartment, luxurious as it is, is still a gangster's apartment — not a Gold Coast address. The distance between the gangster world and legitimate society is not economic but social, and no amount of money can close it.

## Real-World Context

Chicago's Gold Coast developed in the late nineteenth century as the city's elite residential district, anchored by the Palmers, McCormicks, and other industrial and mercantile families. Its architecture — mansions, luxury apartments, and exclusive clubs — defined a standard of wealth and social prestige that the Prohibition-era gangsters, for all their cash, could never access. The neighborhood remains one of America's wealthiest urban districts.
