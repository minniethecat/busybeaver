# Halsted Street

The novel begins with Sam Vettori "staring into Halsted Street" ([002-001]), and the image recurs at a critical negotiation: "Vettori sat down by the window and stared into Halsted Street" ([002-022]). This Chicago thoroughfare becomes the silent witness to Vettori's decline — the world outside the window he can no longer command.

## Overview

Halsted Street is never described in architectural or geographic detail; Burnett uses it purely as dramatic space. It is what Vettori looks at when he should be paying attention to Rico's insubordination. The street's repetition — bookending the chapter's negotiation scene — creates a visual rhyme that marks Vettori as a man already half-absent from his own gang, his gaze drawn to the anonymous life of the street rather than the power struggle happening inches away.

## Significance in the Story

The opening image of Vettori staring into Halsted Street establishes the novel's visual language: power is something you watch, something that moves past you if you're not quick enough. Vettori's two turns at the window ([002-001], [002-022]) bracket the negotiation where Otero and Rico outmaneuver him. The street is where normal life continues — pedestrians, traffic, the lawful city — while inside the room, the gangster code plays out its implacable logic.

## Key Scenes at This Location

The novel's first sentence — "Sam Vettori sat staring into Halsted Street" ([002-001]) — is one of the most efficient character introductions in American crime fiction. Before a word of dialogue, we know Vettori is a man who watches rather than acts, a leader whose attention is elsewhere. The second Halsted Street moment confirms the pattern: even when directly challenged, Vettori's instinct is to turn toward the window and disengage.

## Real-World Context

Halsted Street is one of Chicago's longest and most historically significant north-south thoroughfares, running from the South Side through the Near West Side (including the edge of Little Italy) and far into the North Side. In the 1920s it was a bustling commercial strip, lined with shops, saloons, and tenements — exactly the kind of street where a gang leader might keep a front-operation office with a window onto the passing life of the city.
