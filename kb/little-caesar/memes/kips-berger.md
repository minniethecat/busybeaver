# Kips Berger

Kips Berger. A New York gambler and former associate of Little Arnie who sold his Chicago gambling joint to Arnie for practically nothing, unwittingly enabling Arnie's rise in the Chicago underworld.

## Role in the Novel

Kips Berger is a background figure whose single consequential action — selling his gambling joint — sets the stage for Little Arnie's territorial expansion in Chicago. He represents the transient nature of underworld success: once a player, he goes broke and exits the scene, making way for the next operator ([035-006]).

## Character Profile

- **Full Name**: Kips Berger
- **Aliases**: None recorded
- **Affiliation**: Independent (formerly Little Arnie's associate in New York)
- **Role**: Gambling joint owner (former)
- **First Appearance**: Mentioned in backstory, Part IV ([035-006])

## Biography

Kips Berger was originally from New York, where he was one of Little Arnie's pals. He relocated to Chicago at some point and established a large gambling joint in a strategically valuable neutral zone — territory touching Little Italy on the south and Pete Montana's vast domain on the north ([035-006]).

By the time Arnie arrived in Chicago about five years before the novel's main events, Berger had gone broke. He sold his gambling joint to Arnie "for practically nothing" ([035-006]). Arnie, arriving from New York with a small stake and a ruined reputation, bought the joint and prospered, using its neutral-zone location to his advantage.

Pete Montana later dismissed the territory's earlier years dismissively: "It wasn't worth nothing when Kips Berger had it" ([036-???]). Berger thus serves as a cautionary footnote — the operator who failed where Arnie (however temporarily) succeeded, before Arnie himself was destroyed by Rico.

## Key Scenes

**The Sale** ([035-006]): In the narrative's account of Little Arnie's origins, the sale of Kips Berger's gambling joint is cited as the pivotal transaction that gave Arnie his Chicago foothold. Berger, "formerly of New York and once one of Arnie's pals, had gone broke and was willing to sell out his big gambling joint for practically nothing."

## Significance

Kips Berger is emblematic of the turnover in Burnett's gangster world: operators rise, go broke, and are replaced. His failed venture creates the opening for Arnie, whose subsequent rise and fall forms one of the novel's central arcs. Berger's ghost haunts the territory — a reminder that in the gangland economy, yesterday's boss is tomorrow's footnote.
