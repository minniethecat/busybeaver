# Joe Massara

Joe Massara is a key figure in W.R. Burnett's *Little Caesar* — a stylish young gang member whose desire to leave the criminal underworld for a legitimate career as a dancer sets him on a collision course with Rico Bandello. Unlike the hardened gangsters around him, Joe is vain about his appearance, in love with his dancer girlfriend Olga, and increasingly unwilling to risk his life for the gang.

## Role in the Novel

Joe Massara serves as Rico Bandello's foil. Where Rico is consumed by ambition and contempt for weakness, Joe represents the possibility of escape from the criminal life. His determination to quit "the racket" and pursue dancing with Olga forces Rico into a dilemma: the gangster code demands that Joe be killed for disloyalty, but Rico's personal feelings — and perhaps a buried envy of the life Joe wants — make him hesitate. This hesitation is one of the few cracks in Rico's armor, and it proves fatal. [030-067]

## Character Profile

- **Full Name**: Joe Massara
- **Aliases**: Joe
- **Affiliation**: Sam Vettori's gang (formerly)
- **Role**: Inside man (cases jobs, drives getaway car), dancer
- **First Appearance**: Chapter I, arriving late to the gang's card game [002-026]

## Biography

Joe Massara enters the novel as the gang's "front man" — the member who cases targets, plans the inside work, and drives the getaway car. He is sharply dressed, with manicured nails and a diamond ring given to him by Olga Stassoff, the dancer who is his girlfriend [002-031]. The other gang members view him with a mix of respect and resentment: Rico openly despises Joe's "softness" [002-006], but acknowledges his usefulness.

Joe's role in the Courriere Holdup is critical — he cases the location and provides the inside information. But the violence of the botched robbery shakes him. Afterward, Joe begins to distance himself from the gang, spending more time dancing at the Palermo Club and less time in Vettori's back room.

The novel's central conflict crystallizes when Joe tells Rico directly that he is leaving "the racket" [030-067]. Rico threatens him, and for much of the novel's second half, Joe lives under the shadow of Rico's vengeance. His escape is only possible because Rico's empire collapses before he can act on his threat.

## Key Scenes

Joe's arrival at Vettori's back room establishes his character immediately — he is late, well-dressed, and unapologetic:

> "Sorry," said Joe, "the bridge was up." [002-029]

His skill as an inside man is on display during the Courriere planning session, where he provides detailed intelligence on the target:

> "Sure," said Joe, "it's an up and up place." [002-036]

But it is his confrontation with Rico that defines his arc. When Rico demands to know why he would quit the racket, Joe's answer — that he has had enough — is the novel's quietest act of rebellion:

> "Listen, Rico, I got enough of the racket." [030-067]

## Significance

Joe Massara represents what Rico Bandello can never have: the ability to choose a different life. Burnett uses Joe to expose the emptiness of the gangster code — for all Rico's power, he is trapped in a world he cannot leave, while Joe, for all his "softness," has something worth escaping for. Joe's survival is not a triumph of courage over fear, but of simple human desire over the inhuman logic of the gangster world.
