# Olga Stassoff

Olga Stassoff. A dancer at DeVoss's Bronze Peacock nightclub, Joe Massara's girlfriend, and an unwitting catalyst in the conflict between Joe's desire for a legitimate life and Rico Bandello's grip on his former partner.

## Role in the Novel

Olga represents the "straight life" that pulls Joe Massara away from the gang — and by extension, the romantic entanglement that Rico sees as a threat to his control over Joe. Her relationship with Joe creates the central tension of the novel's third act, as Rico demands Joe participate in robberies while Joe tries to protect his new life with Olga. ([006-006], [039-020])

## Character Profile

- **Full Name**: Olga Stassoff
- **Aliases**: "that little hunky dancer" (derogatory, from gang members)
- **Affiliation**: The Bronze Peacock nightclub
- **Role**: Professional dancer
- **First Appearance**: Mentioned in Part I, first appears in Part II ([006-006])

## Biography

Olga is introduced through Joe Massara's association — he wears a diamond ring she gave him, a symbol of their relationship and his drift toward legitimate society. She is a professional performer at DeVoss's establishment, The Bronze Peacock, where Joe also works. ([002-031])

Her relationship with Joe is passionate but strained by the shadow of his criminal past. When Joe erupts at her teasing, she immediately chases after him: "I was just kidding, Joe. Honest I didn't mean it." The volatility of their dynamic reflects the precariousness of Joe's position between two worlds. ([006-009], [006-011])

As Rico tightens his grip on Joe, Olga becomes an implicit target — the gang views her as the force "that has got him down" and is "going straight." Her presence represents everything the gangster code rejects: domesticity, legitimate work, emotional attachment outside the gang. ([026-086])

## Key Scenes

Olga's dressing-room exchange with Joe captures their dynamic:

> "If you're singing for me," said Joe, "you can stop any time." Olga turned. "I was just kidding, Joe," said Olga, "honest I didn't mean it." ([006-006], [006-011])

Her worried phone call when Joe is jailed:

> "Want me to come down and see you, Joe?" asked Olga. ([040-096])

## Significance

In Burnett's deterministic universe, Olga represents the possibility of escape — a life beyond the gang, built on love and legitimate work rather than violence and loyalty. But the novel is clear-eyed about the cost of such escape: every character who reaches for it must pay a price. Olga's relationship with Joe is the emotional heart of the novel's third act, and her very existence as a "straight" influence makes her a threat to Rico's worldview.
