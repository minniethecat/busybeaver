Identity in *Little Caesar* is something the gangster must build from scratch — and something the system inevitably dismantles.

## Definition

Rico Bandello has no inherited identity. He is not a Vettori by blood, not a Montana by wealth, not an Arnie by established reputation. His identity is entirely self-made: the gunman from Little Italy who became "Rico," the name that commands fear and respect. But this constructed identity is fragile — it exists only as long as his power holds, and when the power collapses, the identity collapses with it.

## In the Novel

- **Rico's self-invention** — Rico "loved but three things: himself, his hair and his gun" [007-002]. This trinity defines his self-conception: vanity, violence, and ego. There is nothing else — no family, no faith, no community loyalty. Rico is what he has made himself, and nothing more.

- **The name as identity** — Characters are known by nicknames and aliases: "Rico" (not Cesare Bandello), "Pete Montana" (not Pietro Fontano), "The Greek" (Otero). These names are performances — gangster identities adopted like costumes. Pete Montana's "respectable-looking" facade [036-038] is a carefully maintained fiction that collapses the moment the Big Boy withdraws support.

- **Joe Massara's split identity** — Joe attempts to maintain two selves: the gangster and the dancer. This split is unsustainable. The novel argues that in the gangster world, you are either wholly in or wholly out — there is no middle ground, and Joe's attempt to occupy it makes him the most dangerous figure in Rico's orbit.

- **The dissolution of self** — Rico in exile is no longer "Rico" but a fugitive, stripped of his empire, his reputation, and eventually his life. The identity he spent his career building evaporates, leaving only the man who "loved but three things" — and lost all three.

## Thematic Function

Burnett anticipates existentialist themes in his treatment of identity. Rico is not born a gangster — he chooses it, builds it, and is ultimately destroyed by his own creation. The novel suggests that in a world without inherited meaning, self-invention is both necessary and fatal. You can become anyone — but no one can stay anyone for long.
