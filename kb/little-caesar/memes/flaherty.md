# Flaherty

Sergeant Flaherty. A plainclothes detective with the Chicago police force, persistently investigating Sam Vettori's gang and later Rico Bandello's rise in the Chicago underworld.

## Role in the Novel

Flaherty serves as the primary law-enforcement antagonist throughout the novel. He represents the pressure of institutional justice against the gangsters' world — always watching, always closing in. His pursuit of Rico spans the entire narrative arc, from the early Vettori gang days to Rico's final downfall. ([011-009], [043-009])

## Character Profile

- **Full Name**: Flaherty (first name not given in the novel)
- **Aliases**: Sergeant Flaherty
- **Affiliation**: Chicago Police Department
- **Role**: Plainclothes detective
- **First Appearance**: Part II, investigating Sam Vettori ([011-009])

## Biography

Flaherty first appears questioning Sam Vettori about gang activities, showing his methodical, persistent approach to police work. He and his plainclothes men operate with a mix of intimidation and strategy — rather than making hasty arrests, they gather intelligence and apply pressure at calculated moments. ([011-020], [023-031])

When Flaherty corners Rico in a hallway, he makes it clear he could arrest him but chooses not to — part of a deliberate game of cat and mouse designed to keep Rico off balance. "This ain't a pinch," he tells Rico. "It ought to be, but it ain't." This moment defines Flaherty's tactical approach: patience over speed, leverage over force. ([023-035], [023-036])

Flaherty's investigation extends through the novel's later stages. He interrogates DeVoss, tracks Rico's movements through the Big Boy's organization, and ultimately closes the net around Rico as his empire crumbles. ([040-055], [043-009])

## Key Scenes

Flaherty's hallway encounter with Rico is a defining moment of applied pressure:

> "Well," said Flaherty, "just to put your mind at rest. I'll tell you, this ain't a pinch. It ought to be, but it ain't. Now will you take a walk … ?" ([023-036])

His appearance at Rico's hideout late in the novel signals the closing of the trap:

> "Yeah," said Flaherty, "the Big Boy sent me down. I want to have a talk with him." ([043-009])

## Significance

Flaherty embodies the institutional force of law in Burnett's gangster world — not a heroic crusader but a professional operator working within a corrupt system. His persistence throughout the novel creates a constant undercurrent of tension, reminding the reader (and Rico) that every rise in the underworld carries the seeds of its own destruction. Unlike the gangsters who operate on loyalty and violence, Flaherty operates on information and patience — a different, ultimately more durable form of power.
