Rico Bandello's flight from Chicago is the novel's final act — the collapse of an empire reduced to one man running for his life.

## Prelude

Rico's position had been deteriorating. Little Arnie was eliminated but at the cost of increased police attention. The Big Boy's restructuring of the criminal hierarchy removed Pete Montana and destabilized the old order. Rico's former Allies were now rivals or corpses.

The fatal blow came from the one person Rico had spared: Joe Massara. Arrested and facing pressure from Detective Flaherty, Joe "squealed" [043-046], providing the police with everything they needed to move against Rico.

## The Flight

Olga brings the news to Rico: "the bulls're after you. Joe squealed. You ought to plugged that softie, Rico. You ought to plugged him" [043-046]. Her words are both warning and judgment — Rico's mercy toward Joe was his fatal weakness.

Rico goes into hiding at Ma Magdalena's boarding house, but he cannot stay. Chicago has become a cage — every face is a potential informant, every street corner a potential ambush. He flees the city entirely, heading for another town where he hopes his name and face are unknown.

## Aftermath

Rico's flight does not save him. In the new city, he is recognized, reported, and cornered by police. His death on an anonymous street — far from Little Italy, far from the empire he built — is the novel's final verdict on the gangster life. You can conquer a city but you cannot escape yourself.

## Significance

Rico's flight transforms him from gangster to fugitive — a reduction that mirrors the novel's larger argument about the gangster's trajectory. The man who took territory, absorbed gangs, and commanded loyalty is reduced to a hunted animal, alone and afraid. Burnett's genius is to make this reduction feel not like tragedy but like arithmetic: the sum of Rico's choices, finally reconciled.
