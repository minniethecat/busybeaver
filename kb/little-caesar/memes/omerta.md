# Omertà

**Omertà** — the Sicilian code of silence — is the unspoken foundation of the gangster's moral universe in *Little Caesar*. Never named directly in the novel, it is nonetheless the principle that governs every character's relationship to authority, the unwritten law that makes [[snitching|informing]] the one unforgivable crime.

## Definition

Omertà (from the Italian *umiltà*, "humility," through Sicilian dialect) is the traditional code of the Sicilian Mafia requiring absolute silence about criminal activities before authorities. It forbids cooperation with police, courts, or any state institution, and prescribes that disputes be settled within the criminal community through its own mechanisms of justice. Violation of omertà is punished by death.

## In the Novel

Burnett never uses the word "omertà" — his characters are second-generation Americans who speak neither Italian nor Sicilian — but the code's logic pervades every scene. The novel's central conflict — Rico's accusation that Joe Massara has "turned yellow" and cooperated with police — is an omertà conflict translated into American idiom. The gangster's world is governed not by law but by a parallel system of obligations and prohibitions, and the most fundamental prohibition is: do not talk to the police.

When Joe chooses cooperation over silence, he is violating not just a tactical arrangement but a sacred prohibition. Rico's fury is not strategic calculation — it is genuinely moral outrage, the anger of a true believer whose faith has been betrayed.

## The American Transformation

Burnett's innovation is to show omertà under pressure from American conditions. The old-country code assumes a stable community with shared values; Chicago in the 1920s is nothing of the sort. The code persists but is fraying — Scabby the informer operates openly, Joe Massara chooses survival over silence, and even the Big Boy's system of "fixing" things represents a commodification of what was once a sacred obligation. The novel documents not omertà itself but its American dissolution.
