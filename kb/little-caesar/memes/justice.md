Justice in *Little Caesar* is not a principle but a function of power — the powerful are never held to account, and the only law that matters is the law of survival.

## Definition

Burnett's novel depicts a Chicago in which formal justice has collapsed. The police are bought, the courts are circumvented, and the only consequences that matter are the ones gangsters impose on each other. In this environment, justice is privatized: each gang enforces its own code, and the ultimate arbiter is the gun.

## In the Novel

- **The Big Boy's fixing** — The clearest statement of the novel's view of justice: "The Big Boy can't fix murder. He can fix anything but murder" [002-062]. The justice system is a menu of services available for purchase, with murder as the one item that exceeds the fixer's budget.

- **Tony Passa's killing** — Passa is murdered, and the police do nothing meaningful. The killing is treated as a professional hazard, not a crime requiring justice. The newspaper reports it, the gang mutters about it, and the world moves on.

- **Rico's execution of traitors** — When Rico kills those who betray him, he is enacting gangster justice — swift, personal, and final. This private justice is more efficient and more terrifying than anything the state can offer.

- **Flaherty's moral ambiguity** — Detective Flaherty pursues Rico not out of commitment to justice but out of professional pride and personal animus. He is a "boy that used his rod and argued afterwards" [040-051] — as violent as the men he hunts. The line between lawman and gangster blurs into irrelevance.

## Thematic Function

By presenting a world without functioning justice, Burnett forces the reader to confront the gangster on his own terms. Rico is not a villain operating against a just system — he is a rational actor in a system that has abandoned justice as a meaningful category. The novel's realism depends on this moral vacuum: without it, Rico would be a simple criminal; with it, he is a product of his environment.
