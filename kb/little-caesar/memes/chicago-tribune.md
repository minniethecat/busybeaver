# Chicago Tribune

The **Chicago Tribune** appears in *Little Caesar* as the representative of the newspaper industry whose crime reporting is both the gangster's necessary medium of fame and his ultimate enemy — the institution that publishes the photographs, the wanted posters, and the front-page stories that define the public dimension of the criminal career.

## Overview

Burnett uses newspapers throughout the novel as a narrative device and thematic element. The Tribune — Chicago's dominant morning paper in the 1920s — is the implied publisher of the crime stories that track Rico's rise and fall. The novel's awareness of newspaper coverage reflects the real historical moment: the 1920s were the golden age of Chicago crime journalism, and the city's newspapers competed fiercely for gangster scoops.

## Role in the Novel

The Tribune's most significant presence is at the banquet for Rico, when newspaper photographers arrive to take a "flashlight" picture for a Sunday feature on Little Italy. The Big Boy's warning — that the photograph is "a bad play" [027-030] — proves prophetic. The newspaper that celebrates Rico as a community figure also publishes the wanted poster that identifies him as a murderer. The media machine that creates the gangster's celebrity is the same machine that destroys him.

## Significance

The Tribune represents the novel's implicit argument about the relationship between crime and publicity: the gangster needs the newspaper to achieve the recognition he craves, but recognition in a democratic society means visibility to the police as well as to the public. The Big Boy, who refuses to be photographed and never appears in the papers, represents the older, wiser model of criminal anonymity. Rico, who craves the limelight, represents the modern gangster-as-celebrity — and the novel makes clear which model survives.
