# The Bronze Peacock

An establishment of such exclusivity that even the mention of its name locates a character on the social ladder. When DeVoss appraises Rico, the Bronze Peacock is the yardstick: Rico is "out of his element in an atmosphere as exclusive as that of The Bronze Peacock" ([030-001]). He can pass — "sartorially Rico could pass at The Bronze Peacock" — but "there was something vulgar and predatory about him" ([030-001]) that no tailoring can conceal.

## Overview

The Bronze Peacock is never entered in the novel's action; it exists purely as a reference point, a measuring stick for class. Its name alone — ornate, self-consciously elegant — signals the aspirational world of 1920s nightlife where gangsters mingled with politicians and society figures. That Rico is judged against it without ever being shown inside it reinforces his position: close enough to be measured, distant enough to be found wanting.

## Significance in the Story

The passage about the Bronze Peacock is one of the novel's most compressed statements of theme. In two sentences, Burnett delivers the central tragedy of Rico's ambition: he can acquire the clothes, the money, the mannerisms, but he cannot acquire the thing that matters — belonging. The "vulgar and predatory" quality that made him "the best gunman in Little Italy" is precisely what disqualifies him from the Bronze Peacock. Success in the gangster world is self-limiting; the very traits that enable the rise prevent the arrival.

## Key Scenes at This Location

The Bronze Peacock is never a setting for action — its power comes from being unattainable. DeVoss's appraisal of Rico ([030-001]) uses the club as shorthand for a world Rico cannot enter, making the judgment more damning for being purely social rather than criminal. Rico has conquered Little Italy, but the Bronze Peacock remains as distant as ever.

## Real-World Context

Exclusive nightclubs like the Green Mill and the Cotton Club defined 1920s Chicago and New York nightlife, serving as neutral ground where gangsters, politicians, and celebrities mixed under an unspoken truce. The Bronze Peacock is Burnett's fictional composite of these establishments — places where the boundaries between legitimate and illegitimate society blurred, but never entirely dissolved. The judgment that Rico can "pass" in dress but not in essence captures a real social dynamic of the Prohibition era.
