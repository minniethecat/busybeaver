# Tony Passa

Tony Passa. A young, rosy-cheeked gang member barely twenty years old — enthusiastic, impulsive, and ultimately disposable — whose murder becomes one of the novel's early turning points, bringing police attention down on [[vettori-gang|Sam Vettori's gang]] and accelerating the instability that allows [[rico-bandello|Rico Bandello]] to take control.

## Role in the Novel

Tony is introduced in the novel's opening scene as one of the card players in Vettori's back room, "robust and rosy, scarcely twenty years old," who "watched each turn of cards intently, shouting with joy when his luck was good, cursing when it was bad" [001-006]. His youth and enthusiasm mark him as the gang's future — or so it seems. Vettori assigns him to procure the getaway car for the Courriere holdup, a task he accepts with a flourish, though "his hands shook a little." [001-018]

- **Full Name**: Tony Passa

- **First Appearance**: Part I, playing stud poker in Vettori's back room [001-006]

## Biography

Tony is the youngest member of Vettori's inner circle — enthusiastic, eager to prove himself, but visibly nervous when real violence is in the offing. He is assigned to drive the getaway car for the Casa Alvarado robbery, a role that places him at the center of the gang's most ambitious operation. His death is referenced obliquely — the novel does not linger on it — but its consequences ripple through the narrative as police investigations intensify and the gang's internal cohesion begins to fracture. ## Key Scenes

**The card game** — Tony plays stud poker with Otero and Rico, his emotional transparency — shouting when winning, cursing when losing — establishing him as the gang's youngest and least guarded member. [001-006]

**The assignment** — Vettori orders Tony to get a "big, fast car" for the holdup; Tony's hands shake as he lights a cigarette, revealing the fear beneath his bravado. [001-018]

## Significance

Tony Passa functions as an innocence figure — not moral innocence (he is a willing participant in criminal violence) but experiential innocence, the raw material of gang life before it has been hardened by betrayal and loss. His death is the novel's first major casualty within Vettori's inner circle, and it marks the beginning of the end for the old gang structure. In Burnett's world, the young and enthusiastic are not protected by their youth — they are simply the first to fall.
