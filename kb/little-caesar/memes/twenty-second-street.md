# Twenty-Second Street

**Twenty-Second Street** is one of the many Chicago street names that populate *Little Caesar's* geographic texture — a coordinate in the novel's careful mapping of the city's gangster geography, where every street can be a boundary, a territory, or a route.

## Overview

Twenty-Second Street appears as part of Burnett's documentary approach to Chicago geography, where specific street names create the novel's sense of being anchored in a real, mappable city. In the 1920s, this corridor served as a key east-west artery on the city's near South Side, connecting industrial and residential neighborhoods.

## Significance in the Story

Like other street names in the novel, Twenty-Second Street functions as a geographic marker rather than a narrative setting — it defines where things happen rather than being the thing that happens. But this very specificity is part of Burnett's technique: the novel's gangster world is not set in an abstract "Chicago" but in a city of named streets, each with its own character and associations. The cumulative effect is a sense of total geographic immersion.

## Real-World Context

Chicago's numbered street grid, with State and Madison as the zero points, was one of the most systematic in any American city. Burnett's use of numbered streets like Twenty-Second reflects the real precision of Chicago's urban geography, where every address encodes its location in a citywide coordinate system — a form of spatial legibility that gangsters and police alike could exploit.
