# South Side

A vast expanse of Chicago that functions in Burnett's novel as a safety valve for the underworld's displaced. When Bat Carillo's gang "demolished" Jew Mike's joint, Mike "fled to the South Side, where he opened a couple of vice-joints" ([035-008]). When Rico's former associates scatter, rumor places them on the South Side — a territory large enough to absorb the casualties of gang warfare and obscure enough to keep their fates uncertain.

## Overview

The South Side appears in the novel only as rumor and refuge. Unlike Little Italy or the North Side, it is never described directly — it is the place people go when they can no longer stay where they are. Jew Mike's South Side vice-joints ([035-008]) suggest an underworld infrastructure that mirrors but never intersects with the novel's primary geography, a parallel Chicago where the game continues under different management.

## Significance in the Story

The South Side's role as a destination for the displaced reveals the novel's implicit map of Chicago gangland: Little Italy is the contested center, the North Side is the prize, and the South Side is the safety valve. When Rico's story concludes, "there was a rumour that he had got in bad with a South Side gang over there and had left to keep from getting bumped off" ([050-056]) — the South Side becomes the obligatory postscript to a gangster's rise and fall, the place where stories end in obscurity rather than headlines.

## Key Scenes at This Location

Jew Mike's flight to the South Side after his joint is demolished ([035-008]) illustrates the territorial logic of Chicago's underworld: violence in one neighborhood creates refugees who reconstitute their operations elsewhere. The South Side's size and anonymity make it the natural destination for the displaced.

The rumor that Rico "had got in bad with a South Side gang" ([050-056]) closes the circle on his career — the man who conquered Little Italy and dreamed of the North Side ends as fugitive gossip from the city's southern reaches.

## Real-World Context

Chicago's South Side in the 1920s was a vast, diverse region encompassing everything from the Black Belt to the stockyards to middle-class neighborhoods. The real South Side was home to its own political machines and criminal organizations, including portions of Al Capone's territory. Burnett's use of the South Side as a place of exile and obscurity reflects the way the city's sheer size made it possible for gangsters to disappear and resurface indefinitely.
