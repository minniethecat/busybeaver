# Rico Bandello

Known on the streets as **"Little Caesar,"** full name **Cesare Bandello**. The ruthless protagonist of Burnett's novel, Rico rises from a small-time hood in Sam Vettori's gang to a powerful crime boss in Chicago's underworld — and ultimately falls when his ambition outruns his luck.

## Role in the Novel

Rico is the novel's central character — every major plot event revolves around his ambition, his violence, and his eventual undoing. He is introduced in the opening scene as Vettori's lieutenant and gradually eclipses his boss, taking over the gang and expanding into the North Side. His arc is a classic rise-and-fall gangster narrative: from obscurity to power to a lonely death on a Chicago street.

## Character Profile

- **Full Name**: Cesare Bandello
- **Aliases**: Rico, Little Caesar
- **Affiliation**: Sam Vettori's gang (formerly); independent North Side organization
- **Role**: Gunman → lieutenant → gang leader → crime boss
- **First Appearance**: Chapter I, playing cards in Vettori's back room [002-001]

## Biography

Rico Bandello begins as Sam Vettori's trusted lieutenant, described as "the best gunman in Little Italy" [003-002] — a man defined by his simple devotion to "himself, his hair and his gun" [007-002]. When Vettori puts him in charge of the Courriere holdup, warning "for the love of God, no gunwork" [009-015], Rico leads the job with cold precision, his big automatic intimidating the crowd [009-078]. The job's aftermath — Scabby's rebuke that "The Big Boy can't fix murder" [002-062] — establishes the pattern that defines his career: Rico does what others cannot, and the consequences never catch up to him until they do.

After the Courriere holdup goes wrong, Rico seizes the moment. He takes control from the wavering Vettori, eliminates threats (including his old friend Otero), and builds his own organization on the North Side. At his peak, Rico commands fear and respect across Chicago. But his inability to trust anyone — and his contempt for "softness" — isolates him. When the police close in and his allies fall away, Rico flees the city only to meet an unglamorous end, shot dead on a street in Hammond by a patrolman who didn't even know who he was.

## Key Scenes

Rico's defining moment comes early, when he confronts Vettori after a botched job:

> "You're the boss, Sam, but I'm telling you this: you're going to ruin us all if you don't get some guts. You're letting this outfit go to pieces." [002-015]

Another pivotal scene is Rico's final confrontation with Joe Massara, where Rico's twisted code of loyalty is on full display:

> "You ain't dumb, Joe. You mean to tell me you're gonna quit the racket. Why, boy, you ain't seen nothing yet. In a couple of weeks I'm gonna take over Little Arnie's joint." [030-070]

## Significance

Rico Bandello is Burnett's study of the gangster as pure ambition. Unlike later cinematic gangsters (including Edward G. Robinson's iconic portrayal in the 1931 film), the novel's Rico is not glamorous or sympathetic. He is small, vain about his appearance, and driven by a pathological need for respect. His tragedy is not that he is caught — it is that his worldview, in which "softness" is the only sin, makes it impossible for him to build anything lasting. He dies alone, unnamed, on an empty street.
