# Power

Power. The central currency of W.R. Burnett's *Little Caesar* — pursued with relentless ambition, exercised through violence and intimidation, and always precarious.

## Definition

In the novel's gangster world, power is primarily the ability to command obedience through fear, control territory, and accumulate wealth. Money is power: "Money was power." (PN030-035) But power is also inherently unstable — it must be constantly defended against rivals, and the qualities that enable its acquisition (ruthlessness, decisiveness) are precisely what erode over time.

## In the Novel

Rico Bandello's entire arc is a study in the acquisition, exercise, and loss of power. He rises by being more decisive and ruthless than Sam Vettori, whose "growing inability to make decisions had lost him his power, but it had also saved his life." (PN032-005) Rico's conquest of Little Arnie's territory demonstrates power as zero-sum: Arnie's fall is "the signal for a series of minor tumbles." (PN035-008)

But power in *Little Caesar* carries a paradox. The higher Rico rises, the more vulnerable he becomes. His isolation increases. Old allies become threats. And when he falls, the fall is absolute — from the heights of Chicago's underworld to a lonely death on a Toledo street.

At the novel's climax, Rico addresses "a vague power which he felt to be stronger than himself" — the first acknowledgment that his own power has limits. (PN051-065)

## Broader Context

Burnett's treatment of power reflects the Hobbesian nature of organized crime: a war of all against all, where authority rests on nothing but force and fear. Unlike legitimate power structures that derive authority from law or tradition, gangster power is purely personal — it dies with the man. This is Rico's tragedy: he builds an empire that cannot outlast him.
