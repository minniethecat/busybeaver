# State Street

**State Street** — "that great street" in Chicago's civic mythology — appears in *Little Caesar* as one of the coordinates of the city's legitimate geography, the commercial thoroughfare whose department stores, theaters, and office buildings represent the world of respectable commerce that exists alongside — but entirely separate from — the gangster world of back rooms and cabarets.

## Overview

State Street in the 1920s was Chicago's primary retail and entertainment corridor, anchored by department stores like Marshall Field's and Carson Pirie Scott, theaters like the Chicago, and office buildings housing the city's commercial and professional classes. It was the public face of Chicago — prosperous, respectable, and entirely indifferent to the gangster violence that filled the city's newspapers.

## Significance in the Story

State Street functions in Burnett's novel as a boundary marker — the line between the legitimate city and the gangster's Chicago. When characters cross State Street, they cross between worlds. The street represents a respectability that gangsters can observe, envy, and occasionally exploit (as customers in its stores or patrons of its theaters) but can never truly inhabit. It is the geographic expression of the social exclusion that fuels Rico's resentment.

## Real-World Context

Chicago's State Street was, along with Michigan Avenue, one of the two great commercial streets of the early twentieth-century American city. Frank Sinatra would later immortalize it as "that great street" — but in 1929, when Burnett published *Little Caesar*, it was simply the center of a city whose gangster problem was international news. The contrast between State Street's prosperity and the gang violence occurring blocks away was one of the defining contradictions of Prohibition-era Chicago.
