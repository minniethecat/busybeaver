# Little Arnie

Little Arnie. A North Side gambling boss who runs the biggest gambling joint on the North Side of Chicago. He becomes Rico Bandello's primary rival and the first major target of Rico's takeover after the Vettori gang rises to power.

## Role in the Novel

Little Arnie serves as Rico's first significant independent adversary after Rico consolidates power within the Vettori gang. Arnie represents the old guard of Chicago's underworld — established, comfortable, and slipping — whom the ambitious and ruthless Rico systematically displaces. Their conflict demonstrates Rico's method: muscle in, demand a split, then take over entirely when the target resists. (PN026-053)

## Biography

Little Arnie ran the biggest gambling joint on the North Side and had been a fixture of Chicago's underworld for years. He came to Chicago from New York about five years before the events of the novel. He bought his gambling joint from Kips Berger, also formerly of New York and once one of Arnie's pals, who had gone broke and was willing to sell for practically nothing. Arnie prospered — he was "cute enough to see his advantage." (PN035-006)

For a long time Blondy Belle had been Little Arnie's woman. But Arnie had been slipping for the last year or so, and Rico's sudden rise accelerated his decline. (PN035-007)

When Rico began his takeover, Arnie made advances to him, securing Rico's protection for a thirty percent split. But things looked too easy and Arnie began to double-cross Rico, refusing to pay the full cut and spreading loose talk about him. (PN028-046, PN035-007)

Rico confronted Arnie directly in his own gambling joint on the North Side. In a tense scene, Rico told him "you're through," demanded he leave town, and took control of his operation. When Arnie threatened to go to Mr. Flaherty — a police contact — Rico called his bluff: "If you go to see Mr. Flaherty you better have an alibi because he might ask you where you were on the night of the Courriere holdup." (PN034-057, PN034-079)

Arnie fled Chicago. Several of his henchmen followed him into exile. Joseph Pavlovsky, his doorman who had driven the getaway car in the attempted hit on Rico, went to Hammond and opened a speakeasy with Arnie's money. Arnie's gang was smashed and the Little Italians took over territory they hadn't controlled since the days of Monk De Angelo. (PN035-004, PN035-005)

Arnie's fall was described as "the signal for a series of minor tumbles" — with the fall of his three lieutenants, the last vestiges of his rule vanished. (PN035-008)

## Key Scenes

**The Confrontation at Arnie's Joint** — Rico, Otero, and Killer Pepi confront Arnie in his own office. Rico tells Arnie he's through and orders him to leave town. The scene crackles with tension as Arnie's Detroit gunmen sit impotently by:

> "Arnie," said Rico, "you're through. You been slipping for a year. You're all washed up." (PN034-057)

**Arnie's Bluff Called** — When Arnie threatens to go to the police, Rico turns the tables by reminding him of his complicity in the Courriere holdup:

> "If you go to see Mr. Flaherty you better have an alibi because he might ask you where you were on the night of the Courriere holdup." (PN034-079)

**Arnie's Exile** — After being driven out, several of Arnie's men follow him. Pavlovsky opens a speakeasy in Hammond. Arnie's territory is absorbed. (PN035-004–035-006)

## Significance

Little Arnie represents the complacent old order that Rico's ruthless ambition destroys. Arnie had grown comfortable in his position and allowed his operation to slip — a fatal weakness in Burnett's Darwinian gangster world. His fall illustrates the novel's central theme: in the criminal underworld, power is never secure. The established boss who fails to stay sharp will be replaced by a hungrier rival. Arnie's decline and exile prefigure Rico's own eventual fall, making him both a victim of Rico's rise and a foreshadowing of Rico's fate.
