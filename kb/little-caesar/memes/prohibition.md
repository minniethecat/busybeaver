# Prohibition

**Prohibition** — the Eighteenth Amendment and the Volstead Act — is the unnamed but omnipresent economic engine of *Little Caesar*. Never directly mentioned by name in the novel's dialogue, it is nonetheless the structural condition that makes the entire gangster world possible: the illegal liquor trade that funds the gangs, corrupts the police, and creates the fortunes men like [[rico-bandello|Rico Bandello]] kill and die for.

## Definition

Prohibition was the constitutional ban on the manufacture, sale, and transportation of intoxicating liquors in the United States, in effect from January 1920 until its repeal in December 1933. Rather than eliminating alcohol consumption, it created a vast black market that transformed small-time urban gangs into sophisticated criminal enterprises. The gangsters of *Little Caesar* — Sam Vettori, Little Arnie, Pete Montana, the Big Boy — are all creatures of this economic moment.

## In the Novel

Burnett never has his characters say "Prohibition" — they talk about "the rackets," about beer-running, about territories and "the big money." But the novel's entire economy is Prohibition's shadow economy. The Palermo Club serves illegal liquor; the gangs fight over territory not for abstract honor but for the right to sell beer and whiskey in it; the Big Boy's political power derives from his ability to "fix" the legal consequences of an industry that does not legally exist.

The novel's physical details — the whiskey at the banquet, the bottles on the tables, the nightclub crowds drinking openly — are all markers of an illegal commerce so normalized that no one in Burnett's Chicago even bothers to mention that it is illegal. Prohibition is so pervasive as to be invisible. [026-001]

## Broader Context

*Little Caesar* was published in 1929, the year of the St. Valentine's Day Massacre — the real-world peak of Chicago's Prohibition gang wars. Burnett's novel was among the first to treat the gangster not as a melodramatic villain but as a product of economic conditions: the gangster is not born but made, and Prohibition is the factory. When the novel ends with Rico dead in a Hammond gutter, the implicit question is not "why did he fall?" but "what system made his rise possible in the first place?"
