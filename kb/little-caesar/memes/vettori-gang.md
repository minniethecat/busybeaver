# Vettori Gang

The **Vettori Gang** is the criminal organization at the center of W.R. Burnett's *Little Caesar* — the vehicle through which [[rico-bandello|Rico Bandello]] rises from lieutenant to boss, and the first casualty of the ambition that ultimately destroys him. Initially led by [[Sam Vettori]], the gang operates out of [[Chicago]] from a back room in the [[palermo-club]], specializing in robbery and protection. Over the course of the novel, leadership passes from the cautious, old-school Vettori to the aggressive Rico Bandello, transforming the gang's culture, scale, and fate.

## History

At the novel's opening, the Vettori Gang is a small-to-medium Chicago outfit led by Sam Vettori, an old-school Italian gangster who values stability over expansion. Vettori prides himself on his organizational philosophy: each man a specialist, each role discrete [003-002]. The gang's core members gather around a round table under a green-shaded lamp at the Palermo Club, playing stud poker between jobs [002-002].

> He congratulated himself on his subordinates. Each man a specialist. Yes, yes! That was the way to do. None of this hit or miss stuff for Sam Vettori. [003-002]

The gang's defining crisis comes after the [[courriere-holdup]] goes wrong. Vettori's indecisiveness in the aftermath — his instinct to lay low and wait — creates a power vacuum that Rico fills with characteristic speed. Vettori, increasingly isolated, turns to drink: by Part III he is drinking whisky instead of his customary wine, his face "puffy and his eyes swollen" [017-001].

## Leadership Transition

The transition from Vettori to Rico is not a coup in the traditional sense — Rico does not kill Vettori or openly challenge him. Instead, he demonstrates Vettori's weakness through action: where Vettori hesitates, Rico decides; where Vettori counsels caution, Rico executes. Gang members drift toward competence, and Vettori, recognizing the shift, makes no serious attempt to resist. By the novel's middle, the gang operates under Rico's command, with Vettori reduced to a spectral presence who occupies the old headquarters but commands nothing.

The transition is brokered, in part, by [[big-boy\|Big Boy]], who visits Vettori to issue commands that apply to the entire crew — Rico included — signaling that the underworld's institutional authority now deals directly with Rico [017-005].

## Under Rico

Under Rico's leadership, the gang expands aggressively into the North Side, absorbing [[little-arnies-gang|Little Arnie]]'s territory and operations. Rico runs the organization with cold efficiency — rewarding results, punishing failure, demanding absolute loyalty. But his inability to trust anyone means the gang never develops the institutional depth to survive without him. When Rico falls, the organization collapses instantly: no succession plan, no loyal lieutenants, no institutional memory — just a power structure that was always a one-man show.

## Significance

The Vettori Gang's trajectory — from stable neighborhood crew to Rico's vehicle of ambition to total dissolution — maps the novel's central argument about power. Vettori's old-school model (specialists, consensus, patience) is inefficient but sustainable; Rico's model (ruthlessness, speed, personal terror) is spectacularly effective in the short term but contains the seeds of its own destruction. Burnett does not sentimentalize Vettori's approach — it is cautious to the point of paralysis — but he shows that Rico's alternative, however dynamic, leaves nothing behind when the man at the center falls.
