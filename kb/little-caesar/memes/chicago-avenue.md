# Chicago Avenue

A major east-west artery on Chicago's Near North Side, appearing in Burnett's novel as shorthand for police authority. When Rico snarls at a trailing detective as "a Chicago Avenue bull" ([036-014]), the street name functions as metonym — the avenue where the police station sits, and by extension the entire apparatus of law enforcement that circles Rico but never quite catches him until the end.

## Overview

Chicago Avenue in the novel is less a described location than a symbolic marker. The "Chicago Avenue bull" insult ([036-014]) captures Rico's contempt for the police while naming the specific station house that serves as their base of operations. When detectives search for the missing Joe Massara, they are directed from station to station: "they must have him at the Chicago Avenue station. And out at Chicago Avenue they sent 'em some place else" ([042-030]) — the avenue becomes part of the bureaucratic machinery that shields Rico's world even as it investigates it.

## Significance in the Story

The Chicago Avenue police station is where the law's pursuit of Rico is coordinated and, paradoxically, where it stalls. The sequence of detectives being sent "some place else" from Chicago Avenue ([042-030]) reveals both the limited reach of 1920s policing and the underground network that protects gangsters from coordinated investigation. The station becomes a symbol of the law's proximity without its effectiveness — police are close enough to be insulted in the street but unable to penetrate the ethnic loyalties of Little Italy.

## Key Scenes at This Location

Rico's outburst — "I'm sick of you trailing me like a Chicago Avenue bull. I ain't gonna drop in my tracks" ([036-014]) — is one of the novel's most revealing moments. The insult locates police authority on a specific street while Rico asserts his own territorial knowledge: he knows which bulls work which streets, and he knows how to evade them.

The fruitless search for Joe Massara, with detectives bounced from one precinct to another starting at the Chicago Avenue station ([042-030]), illustrates the jurisdictional fragmentation that real Chicago gangs exploited. The police know something is wrong, but the system's compartmentalization prevents them from connecting the dots.

## Real-World Context

Chicago Avenue was and remains a major commercial thoroughfare. In the 1920s it ran through several distinct neighborhoods, and the police stationhouse would have been a familiar landmark. The reference to a "Chicago Avenue bull" uses the street name the way Chicagoans actually spoke — precincts and stations were identified by their streets, and a cop's beat was inseparable from the avenue he walked.
