# Michigan Boulevard

The wide lakefront avenue that bookends Rico Bandello's career. Early in the novel, after a successful job, he and his accomplices "came back to Michigan Boulevard by a wide detour" ([009-049]) — the boulevard as triumphal return. In the novel's closing pages, "Rico, with the Big Boy's cigar still between his teeth, lay in the taxi and stared out at the tangle of traffic on Michigan Boulevard" ([038-048]) — the boulevard as deathbed view, the city's indifferent motion continuing as he fades.

## Overview

Michigan Boulevard appears only twice, but its placement is structural. The early reference marks Rico's confidence — the wide detour suggesting caution mixed with satisfaction, the boulevard as destination worth reaching. The final reference reverses the meaning: Rico is no longer returning in triumph but being carried, no longer navigating the city but passively watching it through a taxi window. The "tangle of traffic" ([038-048]) is Chicago in its perpetual motion, utterly indifferent to the dying gangster in its midst.

## Significance in the Story

The Michigan Boulevard bookend is one of Burnett's most controlled pieces of narrative architecture. The boulevard's two appearances compress the entire arc of Rico's story: arrival and departure, ambition and consequence, the city as prize and the city as witness. The "cigar still between his teeth" ([038-048]) — a prop of gangster confidence — becomes grotesque in the context of approaching death, a performance maintained for an audience that has already stopped paying attention.

## Key Scenes at This Location

The return "to Michigan Boulevard by a wide detour" ([009-049]) after a job captures the rhythm of gangster life — the wide detour is practical caution but also a small pleasure, the satisfaction of a route well chosen by a driver who knows his city.

Rico's death scene, staring "out at the tangle of traffic on Michigan Boulevard" ([038-048]), is the novel's final image of its protagonist alive. The city that Rico tried to conquer — its streets, its neighborhoods, its boulevards — does not notice his passing. The traffic tangles and untangles itself; Chicago goes on.

## Real-World Context

Michigan Avenue (called "Boulevard" in the novel, reflecting period usage) is Chicago's most famous street, running along the lakefront past Grant Park. In the 1920s it was already the city's grand avenue, lined with hotels, clubs, and the newly built Michigan Avenue Bridge. Burnett's choice of Michigan Boulevard for Rico's final view is deliberate: it is Chicago's most public, most monumental street, and against its scale a single dying gangster is both framed and diminished.
