# Murder

Murder. In *Little Caesar*, the ultimate crime and the gangster's primary instrument — the line that separates the fixable from the unforgivable, the professional from the doomed.

## Definition

Murder in Burnett's novel is both a practical tool of the gangster's trade and a moral threshold. It is distinguished from other crimes by its finality and its special status in the underworld's calculus: "The Big Boy can't fix murder. He can fix anything but murder." (PN002-062)

## In the Novel

The murder of Police Captain Courtney during the Courriere holdup is the novel's pivotal crime — it transforms a robbery into a capital case and sets the full force of the law against the Vettori gang. Rico's wanted poster reads "Wanted for murder: Cesare Bandello, known as Rico." (PN047-027)

Tony Passa's murder, found near St. Dominick's Cathedral, is reported with the chilling headline "Another Gang Killing." (PN021-020) The casual enumeration of killings — the newspaper's matter-of-fact tone — reflects Burnett's documentary approach to violence.

The novel distinguishes between professional killings (ordered, purposeful) and the chaos of murder that brings heat. Rico understands this calculus — he uses violence strategically — but the Courtney killing, whether intentional or not, marks him permanently and sets the stage for his downfall.

## Broader Context

The special status of murder in the gangster's moral economy reflects a real distinction in organized crime: professional criminals understood that killings attracted police attention in ways that gambling, bootlegging, and bribery did not. Burnett's line — "The Big Boy can't fix murder" — captures the limits of corruption in even the most corrupt system. Murder was the threshold at which the machinery of the state, however compromised, still had to act.
