# Fear

Fear. A constant psychological undercurrent in W.R. Burnett's *Little Caesar* — the force that holds the gangster hierarchy together, drives characters toward desperate acts, and ultimately isolates even the most powerful figures.

## Definition

Fear in *Little Caesar* operates on multiple levels: the fear of rivals that keeps gangsters armed and vigilant, the fear of arrest and hanging that haunts even successful criminals, the fear of women that makes men vulnerable, and the primal fear of death that Rico himself cannot escape. It is both a tool of control and a symptom of the precariousness of criminal life.

## In the Novel

Rico's power derives largely from the fear he inspires. "Rico had always inspired fear." (PN023-001) This fear is strategic — it keeps subordinates in line and rivals at bay. But fear also operates on Rico himself. His flight from Chicago is driven by fear of capture. In Toledo, "Rico feared them both" — referring to his landlady Ma Magdalena and her son Young Chiggi. (PN051-003)

Joe Massara is paralyzed by fear of both Vettori and Rico: "Joe feared Vettori and Rico equally, but something told him that Rico would win." (PN010-040) Seal Skin stays awake "for fear Otero would do some crazy thing like shooting out the window." (PN014-002) And at the novel's climax, Rico — the man who inspired fear in everyone — "feared them both" and, for the first time, "addressed a vague power which he felt to be stronger than himself." (PN051-003, PN051-065)

## Broader Context

Fear is the dark twin of ambition in Burnett's gangster world. The same ruthless drive that propels Rico to power creates the terror of losing it. The novel suggests that fear is the true cost of criminal success — a psychological tax that no amount of money or power can eliminate. This theme connects *Little Caesar* to the broader tradition of American naturalism, where characters are driven by forces beyond their control and the pursuit of success leads inevitably to isolation and destruction.
