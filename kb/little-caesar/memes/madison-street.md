# Madison Street

**Madison Street** is Chicago's fundamental geographic dividing line — the east-west axis from which all street addresses are numbered, and the boundary between the city's North and South Sides. In *Little Caesar*, streets like Madison are part of the novel's precise urban geography, where every location carries social and territorial meaning.

## Overview

Madison Street in the 1920s was a bustling commercial strip running west from the Loop through the city's near West Side. As the zero-point for Chicago's street numbering system (intersecting with State Street at "0 North / 0 South, 0 East / 0 West"), it was the city's most important geographic referent — the line that divided North from South, and by extension, one set of gang territories from another.

## Significance in the Story

Madison Street is part of the geographic vocabulary that Burnett uses to ground his novel in a real, navigable Chicago. The gangsters' territorial divisions — [[north-side|North Side]] versus [[south-side|South Side]] — are defined by Madison Street, whether the characters are conscious of it or not. The street is not dramatized but is always present as an organizing principle of the novel's spatial imagination.

## Real-World Context

Chicago's grid system, with State and Madison as the zero-points, was established in 1909 and remains in use today. The North/South division at Madison Street is not merely geographic but deeply social: for much of Chicago's history, "North Side" and "South Side" carried distinct class and ethnic connotations that map onto the novel's gangster geography with remarkable precision.
