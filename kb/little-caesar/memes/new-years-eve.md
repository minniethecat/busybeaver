# New Year's Eve at Sansotta's

The **New Year's Eve at Sansotta's cabaret** is the final turning point in [[rico-bandello|Rico Bandello's]] career as a free man — the night when his volatile temper destroys his last safe harbor, alienates his final ally, and sets him on the path to the Hammond streets where he will be killed.

## Summary

On New Year's Eve, Rico dresses up and goes down to Sansotta's crowded cabaret. Unable to find a seat, he retreats to Sansotta's private office, where a blonde woman engages him in conversation. A fat Italian man — later revealed to be Paolo, the local political boss — takes offense at Rico's attention to the woman ("That bird there tried to grab my girl") and confronts him. Rico, characteristically, reaches for his gun. [057-048]

## Consequences

Rico's expulsion from Sansotta's is the novel's decisive break — the moment when the last underworld figure willing to shelter him withdraws his protection. Sansotta's calculation is purely practical: Paolo can close down his establishment, and no fugitive's friendship is worth that risk. Rico is left on the street, a gangster without a gang, a fugitive without a refuge.

## Significance

The New Year's Eve scene is the novel in miniature: Rico's pride and temper destroy what his cunning has built. The confrontation with Paolo — a political boss who, in Chicago, Rico would have cultivated rather than threatened — demonstrates how far Rico has fallen from the strategic thinking of his rise. The gangster who once planned the Casa Alvarado job with meticulous care now draws a gun on a ward-heeler over a woman whose name he doesn't even know. The arc from calculation to impulse is complete.
