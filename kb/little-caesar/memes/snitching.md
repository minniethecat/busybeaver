# Snitching

**Snitching** — informing to the police or authorities — is the ultimate transgression in the moral universe of *Little Caesar*. More than murder, more than betrayal of a friend, the act of "turning soft" and cooperating with the law represents the one unforgivable crime in the gangster's code. It is the specific charge that [[rico-bandello|Rico Bandello]] levels against [[joe-massara|Joe Massara]], and it becomes the justification for every act of violence that follows.

## Definition

In Burnett's gangster lexicon, a "snitch" or "softie" is a criminal who cooperates with law enforcement — providing information, testifying in court, or otherwise breaking the code of silence (omertà in the Italian tradition) that binds the underworld. The term carries an irreducible moral weight: a snitch is not merely a criminal who made a tactical error but a man who has violated the fundamental terms of gangster identity.

## In the Novel

Snitching is the novel's central moral crisis. When Joe Massara is arrested and decides to cooperate with the police rather than face Rico's threats, Rico frames the decision not as self-preservation but as moral failure: Joe has "turned yellow," he has "gone soft." The gangster's code cannot accommodate the possibility that cooperation with the law might be rational — it must be weakness.

Scabby, the novel's professional informer, complicates the picture. He provides information to both sides — to Vettori about robbery targets, to the police about gang activities — and survives by remaining useful to everyone. Scabby demonstrates that snitching is not a binary moral state but a spectrum of strategic behavior, and that survival in the underworld often depends on knowing what to tell whom.

## Broader Context

The prohibition against informing is one of the oldest and most deeply held norms of organized crime, rooted in the Sicilian tradition of omertà — the code of silence that forbids cooperation with authorities under any circumstances. Burnett's novel was among the first in American literature to treat this code not as exotic folklore but as a functional social system with its own logic, contradictions, and human costs.
