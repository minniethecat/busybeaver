The friendship between Rico Bandello and Joe Massara is the one relationship in *Little Caesar* that transcends business — and its destruction is the novel's deepest wound.

## Definition

Friendship in the gangster world is a contradiction in terms. The code demands loyalty, but the business rewards betrayal. Rico and Joe's relationship tests this contradiction: they are childhood friends who have taken different paths through the criminal world, and their divergent trajectories create the tension that drives the novel toward its tragic climax.

## In the Novel

- **The opening scene** — Rico and Joe are presented as a pair from the start: "Rico and Joe felt queer alone together. They were silent" [002-110]. Even in friendship, they cannot communicate. Rico "hated Joe" [002-014] — not because Joe has wronged him but because Joe is "soft," a weakness Rico recognizes and despises.

- **Joe's desertion** — Joe's ambition is to leave the gangster life for show business. His dancing career represents a parallel escape route that Rico cannot understand and resents. When Joe drifts away from the gang, Rico interprets it as betrayal before any actual betrayal occurs.

- **The squeal** — Joe's ultimate betrayal: he informs on Rico to save himself. Olga's bitter assessment — "Joe squealed. You ought to plugged that softie, Rico" [043-046] — is the novel's judgment on Rico's fatal mistake. His one gesture of mercy (or weakness) toward his only friend is what destroys him.

## Thematic Function

The Rico-Joe friendship is the novel's only sustained human relationship, and its failure is Burnett's final comment on the gangster life. The system does not permit friendship — it permits only alliances. Rico's inability to fully embrace this truth (he spares Joe when he should kill him) is his only human moment and his fatal error.
