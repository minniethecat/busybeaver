A man's name — his reputation — is the foundation of criminal power in *Little Caesar*, more valuable than money and more carefully guarded than territory.

## Definition

In Burnett's gangster world, honor is not chivalric virtue but a practical asset: the reputation for being "on the square" (trustworthy in criminal dealings) and dangerous to cross. This reputation is earned through demonstrated competence and ruthlessness, maintained through constant vigilance, and lost instantly through weakness or betrayal. Honor is both personal (individual reputation) and collective (ethnic pride, gang prestige).

## In the Novel

Reputation functions as a character's most revealing trait:

- **Rico's obsession with standing** — Rico's career is a pursuit of reputation above all else. He studies the newspaper coverage of Pete Montana with envy, measuring himself against the established gang lords. His instinctive "respect for wealth" stems from recognizing that "money was power" [030-035] — and power is the ultimate reputation.

- **Pete Montana's respectable facade** — Montana is "a big, solemn, respectable-looking Italian" who "dressed very quietly, wore no jewellery" [036-038]. His reputation is built on narcotics profits rather than street violence, allowing him to present as a businessman rather than a thug. But this respectability proves fragile — the Big Boy dismantles it with a single order.

- **Little Arnie's ruined name** — Arnie came to Chicago because "his reputation had got so bad in New York that no one would do business with him" [035-006]. His inability to maintain a trustworthy reputation follows him west, and his subsequent double-crossing of Rico merely confirms the pattern. A criminal without a reputation is a criminal without a future.

- **Otto Jastrow's square dealing** — Jastrow is notable precisely because "they all liked Jastrow, who had the reputation of being on the square" [033-085]. In a world of constant treachery, a reputation for honesty — even criminal honesty — is remarkable and commercially valuable.

- **Rico's posthumous influence** — Even after his death, Rico's reputation persists: "under Rico's guidance Chiggi's gang prospered. Chicago Red, impressed by Rico's reputation, carried out his orders and never argued" [051-001]. A great gangster's name outlasts him.

## Thematic Function

Reputation in *Little Caesar* is the gangster's only inheritance. Money can be seized, territory conquered, life ended — but a name endures. Rico's final thoughts circle back to the gang lords who came before him, their fates a measure of his own. The novel argues that reputation is both the gangster's greatest achievement and his deepest trap: the drive to be "somebody" makes every man a target for the next ambitious nobody.
