# Ambition

**Ambition** is the engine of *Little Caesar* — the force that drives [[rico-bandello|Rico Bandello]] from small-time stickup man to Chicago's most feared gangster, and the quality that, unchecked by prudence or loyalty, destroys him. Burnett treats ambition not as a moral failing but as a natural force, as indifferent to human cost as gravity.

## In the Novel

Rico's ambition is established in the novel's opening scene: while the other gangsters play cards for small stakes, "Rico always played to win." The line is both character sketch and thematic statement — winning is not incidental to Rico's identity but constitutive of it. He cannot play for the pleasure of the game; he must dominate, must rise, must be recognized. [001-006]

This ambition is what distinguishes Rico from every other character in the novel. Sam Vettori is content to run his gang and collect his profits; the Big Boy is content to control the system from above, invisible and secure; Joe Massara is content to dance and be loved. Only Rico wants everything — and the novel's argument is that "wanting everything" is a terminal condition.

## Ambition's Trajectory

The novel traces ambition's life cycle in three phases: rise (Rico's ascent from lieutenant to gang leader, characterized by cunning and controlled violence), peak (the banquet, the newspaper photograph, the Big Boy's recognition), and excess (the reckless consolidation, the murder of Courtney, the flight to Hammond). Each phase demonstrates ambition's internal logic: it cannot stop. The ambitious man must continue to rise, and when there is nowhere left to rise, he must fall.

## Ambition vs. the System

The novel's sharpest insight is that ambition is incompatible with the gangster system itself. The Big Boy survives because he does not want to be seen; Rico dies because he does. The gangster hierarchy, for all its violence, is fundamentally conservative — it exists to manage and contain ambition, not to reward it. Rico's tragedy is not that he is a bad gangster but that he is too good at being a gangster: his ambition exceeds the system's capacity to accommodate it.
