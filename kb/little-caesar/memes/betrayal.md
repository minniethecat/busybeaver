The constant threat of betrayal — by informants, rivals, and former allies — structures the moral and practical reality of gangster life in *Little Caesar*.

## Definition

In Burnett's criminal underworld, betrayal is not a moral failing but a structural inevitability. The gangster economy runs on loyalty, yet every alliance is provisional, every subordinate a potential threat. The novel presents betrayal as a spectrum: from the professional informant (Scabby, "passing as a police informer" while working for Vettori [003-015]) to the panicked squealer (Joe Massara, who gives Rico up to save himself [043-046]), to the treacherous business partner (Little Arnie, who "double-crosses" Rico by hiring Detroit gunmen [026-079]–[026-080]).

## In the Novel

Betrayal appears in nearly every chapter and drives the major plot turns:

- **Vettori's paranoia** — Sam Vettori keeps Scabby on his payroll precisely because Scabby poses as a police informer, allowing Vettori to monitor what the police know. But even this professional arrangement breeds anxiety, as Scabby's dual role makes him a vector for betrayal from either side [003-015].

- **Rico's takeover** — Rico betrays Vettori by seizing operational control during the Courriere holdup. What begins as "no bad blood" [002-035] becomes Rico declaring "I been taking orders too long. We're done" [011-063]. The betrayal is not violent — Vettori is too weak to resist — but it represents the novel's model: the strong displace the weak, and loyalty is only as durable as power.

- **Little Arnie's double-cross** — Fearing Rico's expansion, Arnie hires Detroit gunmen to kill him. Rico's response is cold pragmatism: "He'll only double-cross me once" [026-080]. Arnie's betrayal accelerates his downfall, as Rico uses it to justify destroying him entirely and absorbing his territory [034-059]–[034-061], [036-043].

- **Joe Massara's squeal** — The novel's climactic betrayal: Joe, Rico's childhood friend, betrays him to the police. Olga's bitter assessment — "the bulls're after you. Joe squealed. You ought to plugged that softie, Rico" [043-046] — frames it as Rico's fatal mistake, his uncharacteristic mercy toward the man who would destroy him.

## Thematic Function

Betrayal in *Little Caesar* is not a sin but a business risk. Characters assess it with the same cold calculus they apply to territory and revenue. Rico "hated Joe" [002-014] not because Joe betrayed him but because he *knew Joe would* and failed to act. The novel suggests that in a system built on violence and illegal profit, betrayal is the predictable outcome — not a deviation from the code but its logical endpoint. The gangster who forgets this, as Rico does with Joe, seals his own fate.
