# Stickup

The **stickup** is the fundamental unit of criminal action in *Little Caesar* — the armed robbery on which Sam Vettori's gang is built, around which the novel's plot turns, and through which Burnett explores the economics, psychology, and ultimate futility of gangster violence.

## Definition

In Burnett's underworld, a "stickup" is an armed robbery — typically of a business, a cashier's office, or a gambling establishment — executed by a small crew with guns, a getaway car, and inside information. The term carries no connotation of drama or spectacle; it is work, planned and discussed with the matter-of-factness of any commercial operation. Vettori plans the Casa Alvarado job the way a foreman might plan a factory shift: assigning roles, checking the route, managing personnel. [001-027]

## In the Novel

The novel's first major set piece — the Casa Alvarado robbery — is a stickup described in procedural detail: the inside man (Joe Massara), the gunmen (Rico and Otero), the driver (Tony), the timing (midnight on New Year's Eve when "all the whistles'll be tooting"), and the primary target (the cashier). The planning session in Vettori's back room, complete with a hand-drawn map, is the novel's most sustained scene of collective action, and it establishes the stickup as the gang's reason for being. [001-027]

But the stickup is also the novel's moral pivot. Scabby objects to the Courriere holdup, warning that "the Big Boy'd raise hell" and that changing times have "even got the Big Boy scared" — because newspapers now cover crime, and the stickup that once was safe is now dangerous. The novel tracks the obsolescence of the simple armed robbery as organized crime professionalizes: by the end, the stickup is a relic, and Rico, the master stickup man, is a relic with it. [003-023]

## Broader Context

The stickup in *Little Caesar* represents a transitional phase in American organized crime — the moment when simple armed robbery was giving way to the more sophisticated, less violent model of the "racket" (ongoing protection and bootlegging operations). Rico is the supreme practitioner of a dying art; his tragedy is not just personal but historical. The future belongs to the Big Boy and his kind, men who "fix" things rather than steal them.
