Political and police corruption is the invisible infrastructure upon which organized crime is built in *Little Caesar*.

## Definition

Corruption in the novel operates as a system, not a scandal. Police officers, politicians, and officials are purchased as routinely as supplies. The "Big Boy" serves as the central broker of corruption, mediating between gang leaders and the authorities they own. This system makes the gangster economy possible — without corrupt protection, illegal enterprises could not operate at scale.

## In the Novel

- **The Big Boy's fixing power** — The Big Boy can "fix anything but murder" [002-062], a distinction Vettori makes clear to Rico early on. The limits of corruption define the boundaries of permissible gangster action: robbery, vice, and narcotics are protected; murder brings unpredictable consequences that even the fixer cannot control.

- **Pete Montana's political protection** — Montana "could get all the protection I wanted" from political and police sources [036-060]. His downfall is orchestrated by turning this same protection against him: the police "Old Man" is instructed to pressure Montana out of town, and Federal narcotics agents are mobilized as leverage [038-044]. Corruption is a weapon wielded by those higher in the hierarchy.

- **Scabby's dual role** — The informer Scabby embodies institutionalized corruption: "Passing as a police informer, he was in reality a member of Vettori's gang" [003-015]. The police-informer system is so thoroughly corrupted that the gang has a man on the inside of the inside.

## Thematic Function

Burnett presents corruption not as moral decay but as economic rationality. The gangster who cannot purchase protection is a dead gangster. The system is self-reinforcing: corruption enables crime, crime generates profits, profits fuel more corruption. Rico's tragedy is that he never fully understands this — he believes personal dominance can substitute for institutional relationships, and he is wrong.
