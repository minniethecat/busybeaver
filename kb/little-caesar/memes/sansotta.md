# Sansotta

**Sansotta** is a minor but structurally crucial figure in the final section of W.R. Burnett's *Little Caesar* — the last underworld ally who shelters [[rico-bandello|Rico Bandello]] after his flight from Chicago, and the man whose eventual rejection of Rico precipitates the novel's climactic final movement.

## Role in the Novel

Sansotta operates a cabaret and card room in Hammond, Indiana, functioning as a neighborhood-level gang boss — smaller than Chicago's [[big-boy|Big Boy]] or [[vettori-gang|Sam Vettori]], but established enough to provide safe harbor to a fugitive. His brief arc mirrors the novel's larger theme: even among criminals, reputation and prudence ultimately outweigh personal loyalty. When Rico's volatile temper endangers Sansotta's relationship with local political protection, Sansotta casts him out without hesitation. [047-002] [057-056]

## Character Profile

- **Full Name**: Never given (known only as Sansotta)
- **Aliases**: None
- **Affiliation**: Independent Hammond gang; formerly associated with various Chicago crews (implies shared history with Rico)
- **Role**: Cabaret owner, card-room operator, local underworld fixer
- **First Appearance**: Part VII, when Rico arrives at Sansotta's back door [047-002]

## Biography

Sansotta first appears when Rico, newly escaped from Chicago, arrives at the back door of his establishment and asks to see him. The doorman's suspicion evaporates when Rico uses the name "Cesare," suggesting a history between the two men — Sansotta knows Rico from before his Chicago rise. [047-002]

Sansotta greets Rico as a peer, showing him the wanted handbill and expressing a certain admiration: "You sure went up fast over in the big burg." He offers Rico shelter and, for a time, the arrangement works — Rico plays cards with Sansotta's crew, stays out of sight during the day, and the Hammond gang provides cover. [047-013] [047-017]

The arrangement collapses on New Year's Eve when Rico, drunk and resentful, pulls a gun on Paolo — the local political boss — in Sansotta's own cabaret. Sansotta tries to smooth things over, but Paul refuses to stay, and Sansotta's patience finally breaks:

> "Goddamn you, Cesare, why don't you be more careful? That guy is Paolo, the political boss. He can close me up tomorrow if he wants to." [057-053]

Sansotta delivers the verdict without sentiment: "Cesare, you got to be moving. I can't have you hanging around here no more. It's too dangerous." Rico protests — "Giving me the go-by, huh?" — but Sansotta is immovable. His practical calculus is the same one that has governed every underworld relationship in the novel: self-preservation trumps loyalty. [057-056]

## Key Scenes

**The arrival** — Rico, shaved and disguised, arrives at Sansotta's back door. The password "Cesare" earns him entry. Sansotta greets him with the wanted poster and a mixture of respect and caution. [047-002]

**The card games** — During the days of Rico's hiding, Sansotta and his crew provide the only social contact he has. The refrain "Well, Crabby Louis, it's your shot" recurs like a ritual, marking the slow erosion of Rico's patience. [047-017]

**The ejection** — After Rico draws on Paolo, Sansotta's face "darkens" and he makes his decision. The scene is a compressed replay of every betrayal in the novel: the gangster's code provides no safety net when self-interest is at stake. [057-056]

## Significance

Sansotta is the last underworld figure to offer Rico hospitality, and his rejection completes the pattern of isolation that defines the novel's final movement. He is not a villain — he is a realist, operating by the same calculus that Rico himself has always championed. His ejection of Rico is not betrayal but consistency: the gangster world runs on power, and Rico, stripped of his Chicago operation, has none left. Sansotta's cabaret is the last way station before the street, the fog, and the police bullets.
