# Sam Vettori

Sam Vettori. The head of the Vettori gang — a big man, "fat as a hog," with a dark, oily complexion and an aquiline face. Once the undisputed power in Chicago's Little Italy underworld, he watches his authority crumble as his lieutenant Rico Bandello systematically eclipses him.

## Role in the Novel

Sam Vettori is the novel's embodiment of the gangster life cycle: rise, plateau, decline. When the novel opens, he is already past his prime — drinking heavily, losing the loyalty of his men, and unable to stop Rico's ascent. His trajectory from feared boss to irrelevant bystander is the cautionary tale that Rico refuses to read, making Vettori both a character and a prophecy. ([002-001], [017-001])

## Biography

Sam Vettori enters the narrative in the novel's second paragraph, sitting and staring into Halsted Street — a posture of passivity that defines his arc. He is physically imposing but internally eroded, a boss who commands on paper but controls nothing in practice. ([002-001])

His organization is described with professional pride — "Each man a specialist. None of this hit or miss stuff for Sam Vettori" — but the pride is retrospective. Rico, his best gunman, is already too big for the organization. Carillo and Killer Pepi, once loyal, have shifted allegiance. Vettori is a king being stripped of his court, one retainer at a time. ([003-002])

His decline accelerates after the Courriere holdup. He drinks whisky instead of his usual wine, his face grows puffy, his eyes swell. When he confronts Rico directly, Killer Pepi's face is "stony" — a silent declaration of which way the loyalty has shifted. Vettori "began to sweat" — the physical manifestation of fear replacing authority. ([017-001], [025-005])

But Burnett gives Vettori one moment of resurgence. When Scabby brings news, "there was a flash of the old Sam Vettori. He got up and took Scabby by the arm." The old instincts — decisive, physical, commanding — surface briefly before receding. Vettori's tragedy is not that he lost power but that he can still remember what power felt like. ([042-035])

## Key Scenes

Vettori's introduction establishes his physical and symbolic position:

> Sam Vettori sat staring into Halsted Street. He was a big man, fat as a hog, with a dark, oily complexion, kinky black hair and a fat, aquiline face. In repose he had an air of lethargy. ([002-001])

The confrontation with Rico is the novel's pivotal power transfer:

> A new Rico appeared, followed by Otero, Carillo, and Killer Pepi. … Vettori looked him over and winked at Killer Pepi, but Killer Pepi's face was stony. … Vettori began to sweat. ([025-004], [025-005])

## Significance

Sam Vettori is what Rico Bandello will become. His function in the novel is to demonstrate the iron determinism of Burnett's gangster world: every king is deposed, every empire falls, and the men who wield power today will be staring out a window tomorrow, waiting for the inevitable knock. Vettori's physical decline — the whisky, the puffy face, the sweating — is the body's confession of what the mind cannot accept: that the game is already over.
