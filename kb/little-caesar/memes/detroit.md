# Detroit

**Detroit** appears in *Little Caesar* as a satellite node in the Midwest's Prohibition-era criminal geography — a city whose gangsters periodically enter Chicago's orbit as hired muscle, rival operators, or potential allies. It is less fully realized than Chicago or Hammond in the novel's landscape, but its presence establishes the regional scale of the organized crime Burnett depicts.

## Overview

Detroit in the 1920s was a booming industrial city whose criminal underworld — fueled by Prohibition profits and proximity to the Canadian border for liquor smuggling — was among the most active in the Midwest. Burnett uses Detroit sparingly, mentioning it primarily as the origin point for outside gunmen and as a place where Chicago gangsters might expand. [038-009]

## Significance in the Story

Detroit enters the novel most concretely when Rico, consolidating his power, encounters resistance from "toughs" from Detroit who have been brought into Chicago as muscle for a rival operation. The incident is brief but revealing: outside talent is always available in the gangster economy, and a leader's authority must be asserted against threats from any quarter. Rico's treatment of the Detroit men is characteristically decisive. [038-009]

The city also appears in connection with [[pete-montana|Pete Montana]], the North Side crime boss — though Montana's operations are centered in Chicago, the Detroit-Chicago axis is understood as part of the larger criminal network within which all these figures operate.

## Real-World Context

Detroit in the Prohibition era was a major hub for bootlegging, benefiting from its position on the Detroit River across from Windsor, Ontario — a primary conduit for Canadian liquor entering the United States. The city's gangster scene, dominated by the Purple Gang (primarily Jewish bootleggers), was distinct from Chicago's Italian-dominated crews, but the two cities' underworlds regularly intersected. Burnett's passing references to Detroit reflect this real-world geography of crime.
