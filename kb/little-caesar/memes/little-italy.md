# Little Italy

The Italian immigrant enclave on Chicago's Near West Side, serving as the power base for Sam Vettori's gang and the proving ground for Rico Bandello's ruthless ambition. A world of tenement flats, back-room banquets, and whispered rumors — where reputation is currency and abdication is impossible without flight.

## Overview

Little Italy in Burnett's novel is not the tourist-friendly neighborhood of restaurateurs but a closed ethnic enclave governed by gang hierarchies. Rico Bandello is introduced as "the best gunman in Little Italy" ([003-002]), establishing the neighborhood as the measure of criminal talent. The district operates by its own code: "In Little Italy there is no such thing as abdication unless it is accompanied by flight" ([032-005]) — a truth that seals Sam Vettori's fate when Rico usurps his leadership. The neighborhood's social fabric is woven from "wise boys" who watch and judge, creating the pressure cooker that drives Rico's ambition.

## Significance in the Story

Little Italy is the novel's geographic and moral center. The gang gathers in back rooms of its tenements; Blondy Belle is celebrated as "the swellest woman in Little Italy" ([026-044]); and even law enforcement takes its cues from the neighborhood, embodied in Jastrow, "the famous Little Italy cop" ([033-053]). When Rico launches his banquet circuit — "This Sunday we want Little Italy" ([027-023]) — it marks his bid for public legitimacy within the very community that knows his origins.

After Sam Vettori's fall, "there were many rumours in Little Italy about the passing of Sam Vettori" ([023-001]), the full truth "never known outside the gang." The neighborhood functions as both audience and jury, its collective memory shaping the reputations that determine who rises and who falls.

## Key Scenes at This Location

The gang's banquet for Rico in "one of Sam Vettori's big back rooms" marks a pivotal transition of power within the neighborhood. The gathering of the "wise boys" who "thought that Rico's sensational rise was a fluke" ([032-001]) shows Little Italy as the arena where reputations are contested and either validated or destroyed.

The gambling joint that "touched Little Italy on the south" sits in a "neutral zone" bordering Pete Montana's "vast territory" to the north ([035-006]) — a literal borderland where the neighborhood's influence meets the larger Chicago underworld.

## Real-World Context

The Near West Side's Little Italy was a genuine Chicago immigrant community in the 1920s, centered on Taylor Street. Burnett's portrayal captures its insularity and the way Prohibition-era gangsterism intertwined with ethnic neighborhood identity. The novel's Little Italy is a composite — real enough to ground the story in Chicago geography, fictional enough to serve as a closed stage for the gangster tragedy.

The geographical detail that the gambling joint touches "Little Italy on the south and the vast territory that Pete Montana controlled on the north" ([035-006]) maps the neighborhood's position relative to the North Side gang territories, anchoring the fictional gang rivalry in Chicago's actual urban geography.
