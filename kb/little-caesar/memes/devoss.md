# DeVoss

Mr. DeVoss. The manager of The Bronze Peacock, an exclusive Chicago nightclub that serves as a crossroads between the legitimate entertainment world and the criminal underworld. A "square guy" in the gangsters' assessment — honest in his dealings, respected by both sides.

## Role in the Novel

DeVoss operates at the permeable boundary between straight society and the gangster milieu. The Bronze Peacock is where Joe Massara dances, where Olga performs, and where gangsters like Rico come to be seen. DeVoss navigates this world with careful professionalism — he pays what he owes, asks few questions, and maintains relationships with men on both sides of the law. ([006-016], [030-001])

## Biography

DeVoss first appears offering Joe Massara a break — the Stranskys have broken their contract, and DeVoss puts Joe on in their place. The transaction is straightforward and professional, establishing DeVoss as a man who runs his business cleanly. He even passes along Olga's bribe money to Joe with a chuckle, showing a wry awareness of the romantic dynamics around him. ([006-017], [006-027])

The gangsters respect DeVoss precisely because he is not one of them. The Big Boy praises him directly: "There's a square guy, Rico. DeVoss is a square guy all right. Never have to nudge him for dough." In a world built on extortion and threat, a man who pays willingly and on time stands out as exceptional. ([026-076])

When Rico comes to The Bronze Peacock, DeVoss sizes him up with a professional's eye — "positive that he was out of his element in an atmosphere as exclusive as that of The Bronze Peacock." The judgment is sharp but not hostile; DeVoss is a reader of men, a skill essential to his survival in a business that serves both society and the underworld. ([030-001])

In the novel's climactic scenes, DeVoss finds himself caught in the crossfire — questioned by Flaherty, watching his establishment become a crime scene. His pallor in that moment ("DeVoss got pale") reveals the cost of proximity to violence for a man who chose the square life. ([040-088], [040-091])

## Significance

DeVoss represents the legitimate world's uneasy coexistence with organized crime — not a collaborator, not a crusader, but a businessman who has learned to operate in the gray space between them. In Burnett's Chicago, men like DeVoss are the connective tissue between the underworld and society: they don't commit crimes, but they don't report them either. His presence in the novel reminds us that the gangster economy depends on a vast network of "square guys" who look the other way.
