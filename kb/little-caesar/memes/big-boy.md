# Big Boy

**Big Boy** is the unseen power at the apex of Chicago's criminal hierarchy in W.R. Burnett's *Little Caesar*. Never given a proper name — his moniker alone conveys his stature — he operates as the ultimate fixer and arbiter of the underworld, the man to whom even [[vettori-gang|Sam Vettori]] must answer. His appearances are brief but decisive: each visit to Vettori's headquarters reshapes the trajectory of the gang and, by extension, the novel's plot.

## Role in the Novel

Big Boy functions as a structural representation of organized crime's hierarchy — the layer above the street-level gangster that controls political connections, fixes legal problems, and enforces the unwritten rules of the underworld. When he speaks, Vettori listens; when he issues a command, even [[rico-bandello|Rico Bandello]] — whose defining trait is defiance of authority — complies. Big Boy's power is not physical but systemic: he can "fix" anything except murder, and this limitation defines the boundaries within which the gang operates. [002-062] [017-005]

## Character Profile

- **Full Name**: Never revealed (known only as "Big Boy")
- **Aliases**: Big Boy
- **Affiliation**: Chicago underworld (above individual gangs)
- **Role**: Crime boss, political fixer, underworld authority
- **First Appearance**: Referenced in Part I, Chapter I [002-062]; physically appears in Part III, Chapter III [017-002]

## Biography

Big Boy is first invoked as an unseen authority early in the novel, when Vettori's men discuss the limits of his power. Scabby warns Rico that "The Big Boy can't fix murder. He can fix anything but murder" [002-062], establishing the cardinal rule that governs the gang's operations: gunplay attracts attention that even the highest-level fixer cannot suppress.

> "That's your trouble, Rico. The Big Boy can't fix murder. He can fix anything but murder. Get that. You're too quick with the lead." [002-062]

The shadow of Big Boy hangs over the Courriere holdup planning. Scabby objects to the job, warning that "The Big Boy'd raise hell if he knew what was up" [003-023], and notes that changing times have "even got the Big Boy scared" due to newspaper crime coverage [003-026]. Vettori, fearful of panic spreading among his men, orders Scabby: "Don't spill nothing about the Big Boy" [003-028].

Big Boy's sole physical appearance occurs in Part III, when he visits Vettori, who has been drinking heavily and sleeping poorly [017-001]. Big Boy arrives as an authority figure — shaking his head slowly [017-002] — and delivers a clear directive: the gang must cease all operations. His orders to Rico are specific and absolute:

> "OK," said the Big Boy. "I think you're the goods, Rico. But don't get nervous with that gat of yours, or they'll put a necktie on you. Get this. No more stickups. No more jobs. Just lay low, all of you." [017-005]

His physical presence is imposing: he stands "leaning his huge hairy paws on the table" [017-006], laughs loudly [017-008], and when he leaves, "slammed the door" and walked "as heavily as a squad of police" [017-013]. Despite this display of raw physicality, Big Boy's true power remains his ability to control the gang's behavior from a distance through his network of fixers and informants. Later, Rico boasts that he is "OK with the Big Boy" [025-039], using the boss's implicit approval as a credential.

## Key Scenes

**The fixer invoked** — Before the Courriere holdup, Scabby invokes Big Boy's authority to warn against violence, defining the underworld's unwritten constitution: anything can be fixed except murder. This scene establishes the novel's moral and practical economy. [002-062]

**The visit** — Big Boy's sole physical appearance is a masterclass in underworld authority. He arrives, shakes his head, issues commands, laughs, and departs — each gesture carrying the weight of institutional power. His orders ("No more stickups. No more jobs. Just lay low") reshape the gang's entire operational posture. [017-001–013]

## Significance

Big Boy represents the institutional layer of organized crime — the political and economic infrastructure that makes street-level gangsterism possible. He is not a man of action but a man of connections, and his limitation ("can't fix murder") defines the boundary between sustainable criminal enterprise and self-destructive violence. Burnett uses Big Boy to illustrate the hierarchy that Rico ultimately fails to respect: the fixer who controls the system from above, invisible to the police and the press, while the gunman who seeks personal glory inevitably falls. Big Boy survives precisely because he never reaches for the crown that Rico so desperately wants.
