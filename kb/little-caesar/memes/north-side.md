# North Side

The affluent territory north of Little Italy, controlled by Pete Montana — a "wise bird" who had it "tied up" ([029-030]). For Rico Bandello, the North Side represents the next rung on the ladder: not just money but legitimacy, the kind of power that comes with territory and political connections rather than just a quick trigger finger.

## Overview

The North Side in Burnett's Chicago is the domain of established power. Pete Montana runs it with connections and cunning rather than street-level violence — "a wise bird" whose control is absolute ([029-030]). The territory houses the "biggest gambling joint" run by Little Arnie ([026-053]) and generates the "graft" that Rico hungers to control. Unlike Little Italy's insular world of tenement back rooms, the North Side represents the metropolitan stage where a gangster becomes a figure of public note, appearing in newspaper items: "Mr. Arnold Worch, of the North Side, has just left for Detroit" ([035-002]).

## Significance in the Story

The North Side serves as the novel's symbol of aspiration and the limits of Rico's reach. After deposing Sam Vettori, Rico immediately thinks of "how he could muscle in on the North Side graft" ([029-030]), only to find that Pete Montana's control is not easily challenged. The territorial boundary between Little Italy and the North Side is not merely geographic — it separates the neighborhood tough from the citywide power broker, a line Rico can never truly cross.

A stolen car incident on the North Side ([011-035]) shows the territory as a zone of different rules and different consequences, where crime is handled with description-matching and police procedure rather than the direct violence of Little Italy.

## Key Scenes at This Location

Little Arnie's gambling joint on the North Side represents the kind of operation Rico envies — large-scale, established, a real business rather than a back-room affair. That Arnie "had been slipping for a year or more" ([026-053]) reveals the territory's volatility even under Montana's control, hinting at the instability that makes Rico's ambition plausible.

Rico's frustrated realization that Pete Montana "had the North Side tied up" ([029-030]) is a turning point — the moment he understands that shooting his way to the top in Little Italy does not translate to conquering the city's wider underworld, where connections and political cover matter more than a fast gun.

## Real-World Context

Chicago's North Side in the 1920s was associated with more affluent neighborhoods like the Gold Coast and Lincoln Park, contrasting sharply with the immigrant tenements of the Near West Side. The North Side gang of Dean O'Banion and later Bugs Moran was a real Prohibition-era power, making Burnett's fictional "Pete Montana" a recognizable type — the established boss whose control depends on political connections rather than street violence.
