# Police

The police. In *Little Caesar*, law enforcement exists as a complex, often compromised institution — part adversary, part tool, and part indifferent backdrop to the gangsters' real drama of power and survival.

## Definition

The police in Burnett's Chicago are not a monolithic force for justice but a varied cast of characters: beat cops like Jastrow who know the gangsters by name and are "on the square," detectives like Flaherty who navigate the gray zones between law and politics, and rank-and-file officers who serve as little more than traffic control for gang violence. The police car, the station house, and the newspaper crime beat form an institutional triangle that contains rather than eliminates organized crime.

## In the Novel

Police presence runs throughout the novel in multiple registers. Police Captain Courtney is killed during the Courriere holdup, triggering a manhunt that puts pressure on the Vettori gang. (PN015-001) The newspapers sensationalize police failures and gang killings with equal enthusiasm. (PN021-020–021-021)

Yet the police are also objects of contempt and manipulation. Ottavio Vettori declares "the police were a bunch of bums." (PN026-001) Rico uses the threat of police involvement as leverage — he reminds Little Arnie that going to the police means answering questions about the Courriere holdup. (PN034-079) The Big Boy "can fix anything but murder" — suggesting the police are largely purchasable for lesser crimes. (PN002-062)

Joe Massara's arrest and interrogation shows the police as both threatening and limited. They arrest Joe to pressure him into informing on Rico, but Joe's fear of Rico outweighs his fear of the police. The Crime Commission represents a new, more systematic form of law enforcement pressure — one that disturbs even the Big Boy. (PN041-013)

The $7,000 reward offered by the City of Chicago for Rico's capture — "dead or alive" — transforms the police pursuit into a hunt that ultimately corners Rico in his final stand. (PN047-027)

## Broader Context

The depiction of police in *Little Caesar* reflects the historical reality of 1920s law enforcement: understaffed, politically compromised, and struggling to adapt to the scale of Prohibition-era organized crime. The novel captures a moment before the FBI and federal law enforcement systematically targeted gangsters — when policing was local, personal, and often corrupt. Burnett's nuanced portrayal — showing honest cops like Jastrow alongside the system's failures — gives the novel its documentary texture and separates it from simpler gangster morality tales.
