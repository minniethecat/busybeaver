# Red Gus

Red Gus. A notoriously tough Chicago gangster whose execution by hanging becomes a haunting memory for Joe Massara during his night in jail following the Courriere holdup investigation.

## Role in the Novel

Red Gus never appears directly in the novel — he is already dead by the time of the story. His significance is as a cautionary figure: a gangster of legendary toughness who nevertheless met his end at the hands of the law. Joe Massara's sudden memory of Red Gus during his jail cell night underscores the ever-present threat of execution that hangs over every character in the novel's underworld ([041-065]).

## Character Profile

- **Full Name**: Unknown (known only as "Red Gus")
- **Aliases**: Old Gus
- **Affiliation**: Unknown (Chicago gangland)
- **Role**: Gangster, executed criminal
- **First Appearance**: Referenced posthumously ([030-074])

## Biography

Red Gus was active in Chicago's underworld prior to the events of the novel. The first reference to him comes in a conversation about informants: "Didn't Humpy get soft on Red Gus and turn State's?" ([030-074]). Someone named Humpy turned state's evidence against Gus, leading to his arrest and conviction.

Gus was hanged for his crimes. His execution became legendary among Chicago gangsters for his extraordinary toughness: "Yeah, they sure put the rope on old Gus, and there wasn't a tougher guy in the world. Yeah, he was so tough he didn't die right away and kept kicking. Cops fainted and all that stuff" ([041-065]).

The memory of Red Gus surfaces for Joe Massara in a moment of crisis. Lying in his jail cell after being identified in the Courriere holdup lineup, unable to sleep, Joe suddenly thinks of Gus. He paces his cell, haunted by the image of a man too tough to die cleanly — and by the realisation that he may face the same fate ([041-065]).

## Key Scenes

**In the Jail Cell** ([041-065]): Unable to sleep after being identified by the peroxide blonde, Joe Massara suddenly remembers Red Gus. He paces his cell, thinking about the hanging — a man so tough he didn't die right away, cops fainting at the sight. The memory crystallises Joe's fear: this is what the law does to gangsters, and he may be next.

## Significance

Red Gus represents the ultimate fate that awaits the novel's gangsters — not a glorious death in a shootout but a calculated execution by the state. His legendary toughness, far from saving him, only made his death more grotesque. For Joe Massara, the memory of Gus is a turning point: it sharpens his fear and may contribute to his eventual decision to leave the gangster life. Gus thus serves as the novel's memento mori — a reminder that even the toughest men end up on the gallows.
