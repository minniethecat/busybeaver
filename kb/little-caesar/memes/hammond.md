# Hammond

**Hammond, Indiana** is the most significant location outside Chicago in *Little Caesar* — the industrial border city where [[rico-bandello|Rico Bandello]] flees after his gang's collapse, where he hides at [[sansotta|Sansotta's]] cabaret, where he briefly takes over [[young-chiggi|Chiggi's]] small-time operation, and where he is finally cornered and killed by police.

## Overview

Hammond sits just across the Illinois-Indiana state line, an industrial city in the Calumet Region whose proximity to Chicago — close enough to reach but far enough to hide — made it a natural refuge for Chicago gangsters on the run. In Burnett's geography, Hammond is the "outside" to Chicago's "inside": smaller, rougher, less organized, a place where a fugitive can disappear into a card room or a cabaret but can never truly rebuild the power he left behind. [046-001]

## Significance in the Story

Hammond functions as the novel's final stage — the city where Rico's arc completes its downward trajectory. After escaping Chicago in a stolen car, Rico drives into a field on Hammond's outskirts, removes his license plates, and walks into town disguised and alone. The city that first appeared as a distant reference now becomes his entire world. [046-001]

Within Hammond, three key locations anchor Rico's final days: Sansotta's cabaret and card room, where Rico initially hides; the streets and back alleys where he walks with increasing desperation; and the foggy neighborhood where the police finally track him down. Each location is smaller, darker, and more claustrophobic than the last — the geography itself mirrors Rico's diminishing prospects. [057-066]

## Key Scenes at This Location

**The arrival** — Rico reaches Hammond's outskirts at nightfall, drives into a field, and strips his car of identifying plates — the fugitive's ritual of disappearance. [046-001]

**Sansotta's back room** — The cramped, card-smoke-filled rooms where Rico spends his days, playing cards with small-time crooks and nursing his resentment. [047-017]

**The final stand** — In a foggy Hammond street, Rico makes his last stand against the police — the novel's climactic scene, compressed into a few terse paragraphs. [057-066]

## Real-World Context

Hammond, Indiana, was a real Prohibition-era nexus of industrial labor and criminal activity. Founded as a meatpacking and railroad town, it grew rapidly in the early twentieth century and, like many Calumet Region cities, developed its own underworld ecology. Its position on the state line made it strategically valuable for criminals crossing jurisdictions, and its working-class character matched Burnett's vision of a rough, unglamorous gangster refuge.
