# Organized Crime

Organized crime. The shadow economy and parallel society depicted in W.R. Burnett's *Little Caesar* — a hierarchical, territorial system of illegal enterprises that mirrors legitimate business in its structure while operating outside the law.

## Definition

Organized crime in *Little Caesar* encompasses the network of gangs, rackets, and criminal enterprises that constitute Chicago's underworld during Prohibition. It is characterized by territorial division, hierarchical leadership (from street-level gunmen to the Big Boy at the apex), revenue diversification (gambling, bootlegging, protection), and a system of rules and codes that govern behavior within and between organizations.

## In the Novel

The novel charts the full architecture of organized crime in 1920s Chicago. At the bottom are the street-level operators — gunmen, bombers, and hooligans like Bat Carillo's crew who specialize in "strong arm stuff and intimidation; they threw bombs; they smashed up barrooms and vice-joints operated by rival gangs." (PN025-001)

In the middle are the gang chiefs — Sam Vettori, Little Arnie, Pete Montana — each controlling a territory and a portfolio of criminal enterprises. They pay tribute upward and demand loyalty downward.

At the top is the Big Boy, the shadowy figure who "can fix anything but murder" and whose word is law across the entire Chicago underworld. (PN002-062) The Big Boy's apartment, with its "big pictures of the old time guys in their gold frames," serves as a kind of criminal museum — a lineage of power that predates and will outlast any individual gangster. (PN048-004)

The novel shows organized crime as a living ecosystem. When Arnie falls, his territory is immediately absorbed. When Rico rises, the entire structure adjusts. It is a world of constant churn, governed by violence and ambition, but held together by the shared understanding that chaos is bad for business.

## Broader Context

*Little Caesar* was one of the first American novels to depict organized crime not as a moral aberration but as a functioning alternative economy. Burnett's portrayal influenced the gangster film cycle of the early 1930s and established the template for how American popular culture would understand organized crime for decades to come. The novel's emphasis on structure, hierarchy, and the business logic of crime anticipated later sociological studies of organized crime as a rational enterprise.
