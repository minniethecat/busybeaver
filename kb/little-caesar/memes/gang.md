# Gang

A gang. In W.R. Burnett's *Little Caesar*, the fundamental organizational unit of Chicago's criminal underworld — a hierarchical structure bound by loyalty, territory, and the threat of violence.

## Definition

In the world of *Little Caesar*, a gang is not merely a collection of criminals but a structured organization with a clear chain of command (chief, lieutenants, gunmen, hooligans), defined territorial holdings, and diversified revenue streams including gambling, bootlegging, and protection rackets. Membership is for life — "once you were in, you were in." (PN013-003)

## In the Novel

The novel traces Rico Bandello's trajectory through multiple gang structures. He begins as a member of the Vettori gang, rises to become its de facto leader, then absorbs Little Arnie's North Side operation and eventually achieves a position where he deals directly with the Big Boy — the apex of Chicago's gang hierarchy. (PN026-001, PN035-005)

Burnett depicts gang life with documentary precision. The Vettori gang maintains safe houses, back rooms for meetings, and a network of informants. Gang members have specialized roles — from the "hooligans" who smash up rival operations to the gunmen who carry out hits. The gang throws a banquet for Rico's promotion, showing how criminal organizations mirror legitimate social structures. (PN025-001, PN026-001)

Gang warfare is a constant backdrop. Territory changes hands through violence. When Arnie falls, "Arnie's gang was smashed and the Little Italians took over a territory they hadn't controlled since the days of Monk De Angelo." The collapse of one gang creates opportunity for others. (PN035-005)

The novel also depicts the psychological dimension of gang membership. Joe Massara wants out but knows he cannot simply quit — "you couldn't quit a gang." Old gang leaders who are superseded face "two alternatives: flight or death." (PN013-003, PN032-005)

## Broader Context

Burnett's depiction of gang structure in *Little Caesar* reflected the real organizational sophistication of Prohibition-era crime syndicates. The novel captured a transitional moment — when street gangs evolved into quasi-corporate enterprises with diversified revenue, political connections, and territorial monopolies. The ethnic composition of the gangs (Italian, Irish, Jewish) also reflected historical reality. *Little Caesar* helped establish the gangster genre's vocabulary, influencing everything from Warner Bros. films to later crime fiction's depiction of organized crime as a shadow society with its own rules, hierarchies, and codes.
