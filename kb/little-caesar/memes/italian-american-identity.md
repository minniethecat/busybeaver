The gangster world of *Little Caesar* is inseparable from Italian-American immigrant experience — the novel's violence and ambition are rooted in ethnic marginalization and the hunger for belonging.

## Definition

Italian-American identity in the novel is not a theme discussed by characters but the water they swim in. Every major figure is Italian, every gang operates within ethnic boundaries, and the characters' speech, food, religion, and social rituals are drawn from the immigrant communities of 1920s Chicago. The novel depicts a world in which Italian immigrants, barred from legitimate paths to success, have built a parallel economy — and a parallel social hierarchy — through organized crime.

## In the Novel

- **Little Italy as home base** — The Vettori gang operates out of Little Italy, a closed ethnic enclave where "Three Italians were playing cards at a table" [005-002] at Sicily Pete's restaurant, and the rhythms of immigrant life provide cover for criminal operations.

- **Ethnic slurs and otherness** — Characters casually use ethnic slurs: "Some of them dumb Irish" [003-011], "The Greek" for Otero. These labels mark the city as a patchwork of ethnic territories, each with its own hierarchy and rules. Rico's rise involves crossing these boundaries — taking territory that was not "his" by ethnicity.

- **Sicily Pete's restaurant** — Pete's establishment functions as an ethnic clubhouse where gangsters eat Italian food, play cards, and conduct business. When Pete declares "That is no drink for an Italian" [012-029] about gin, he is policing ethnic boundaries from within, insisting on a distinct Italian identity even in vice.

- **The church** — Father McConagha's relationship with Tony Passa's family [013-028] shows the Catholic Church as an ethnic institution that bridges the legitimate and criminal worlds, hearing confessions it cannot act upon.

## Thematic Function

Burnett's treatment of ethnicity is ethnographic rather than polemical. He does not argue that Italians are criminal, but he shows how immigrant exclusion produces criminal economies. The gang is a response to blocked opportunity — an ethnic institution as much as a criminal one. The novel's realism on this point was controversial in 1929 and remains one of its most significant contributions to American literature.
