The speakeasy was the fundamental economic unit of Prohibition-era organized crime — the retail outlet for illegal alcohol that transformed local gangs into criminal empires.

## Overview

Speakeasies were clandestine bars and nightclubs that operated in defiance of the Eighteenth Amendment (1920–1933). They ranged from basement dives to elegant supper clubs, united by their illegality and their dependence on organized crime for alcohol supply. In Chicago, the speakeasy economy was controlled by gangs who smuggled, manufactured, or hijacked liquor and distributed it through networks of protected establishments.

## In the Novel

Burnett's gangsters move naturally through a world of drinking establishments that are at once social spaces and crime scenes:

- **The Palermo Club** — The novel's central gathering place, where the Vettori gang plays cards upstairs while a "jazz band began to play and a saxophone sent vibrations" [002-046] below. It is simultaneously a legitimate nightclub, a gang headquarters, and a site of criminal planning.

- **Sicily Pete's Restaurant** — A more modest establishment where "Three Italians were playing cards at a table" [005-002] and Pete serves wine to his countrymen. It functions as an ethnic clubhouse as much as a commercial enterprise, the kind of neighborhood institution that provided cover for criminal activity.

- **Hammond speakeasies** — The Indiana border town of Hammond appears as a satellite of Chicago's vice economy, where Joseph Pavlovsky operates a speakeasy away from the city's more heavily policed districts.

## The Racket Connection

The speakeasy economy was inseparable from the broader racket system. Gangsters did not merely supply alcohol — they provided "protection" (from themselves, primarily), enforced territorial monopolies, and used speakeasies as fronts for gambling, prostitution, and money laundering. The [[the-beer-racket|beer racket]] that figures in the novel was the wholesale side of this retail infrastructure.

## Significance

The speakeasy represents the material foundation of the gangster's world. Without Prohibition, there is no bootlegging; without bootlegging, there is no mass criminal wealth; without that wealth, the gangster remains a neighborhood thug rather than a "big shot" in the newspapers. The novel's gangster economy rests on the simple fact that millions of Americans wanted to drink and were willing to fund organized crime to do so.
