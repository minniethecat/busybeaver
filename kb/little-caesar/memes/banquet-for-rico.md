# Banquet for Rico

The **Banquet for Rico** is the central celebratory set piece of W.R. Burnett's *Little Caesar* — a lavish gangland party thrown in [[rico-bandello|Rico Bandello]]'s honor at the midpoint of the novel that marks the apex of his rise and, in its excess and recklessness, the first signs of his coming fall. The banquet brings together nearly every major character in the novel's underworld, and its single act of vanity — allowing newspaper photographers to take his picture — becomes the thread that will eventually help the police identify and hunt him down.

## Summary

The gang gives the banquet for Rico in one of [[vettori-gang|Sam Vettori]]'s "big back rooms" [026-001]. The setting is elaborate and self-consciously ceremonial: a fifteen-foot table covered with a fine white cloth, red, green, and white streamers hanging from the chandeliers, Italian and American flags crossed along the walls [026-001]. The event is attended by the full roster of the gang's "notables": [[killer-pepi|Killer Pepi]], Ottavio Vettori, Joe Sansone, [[blackie-avezzano|Blackie Avezzano]], Kid Bean, and their female companions, along with [[otero|Otero]], [[blondy-belle|Blondy Belle]], and the [[big-boy|Big Boy]].

The banquet unfolds in three acts: first, the boisterous meal with whiskey and crude jokes [026-003]–[026-020]; then Rico's speech and the toasts [027-001]–[027-008]; and finally the arrival of newspaper photographers, which provokes the evening's one moment of genuine tension [027-009]–[027-034].

## Timeline

1. **The Meal**: Waiters bring whiskey; crude banter and Ottavio's goat impressions dominate the table; Joe Sansone corrects "appetizer" to "apéritif" [026-001]–[026-020]
2. **Rico's Speech**: Rico thanks the crew in a short, self-deprecating speech; applause lasts "over a minute" [027-001]–[027-003]; toasts are drunk; Pepi and Kid Bean briefly quarrel [027-005]–[027-008]
3. **The Photograph**: Newspaper men arrive to take a "flashlight" for a Sunday feature on Little Italy [027-023]; Rico agrees to pose; the Big Boy refuses to appear [027-025] and warns Rico it was "a bad play" [027-030]; Rico dismisses the warning [027-033]
4. **Departure**: The banquet ends; Rico helps a drunk Blondy Belle down the stairs; Flaherty observes from the club [027-035]

## Consequences

The banquet is the novel's hinge. It is Rico's greatest public moment — the gang formally acknowledges him as leader, the newspapers treat him as a community figure — but it is also the moment his pride begins to exceed his caution. The Big Boy's warning about the photograph [027-030] proves prophetic: being publicly identified makes Rico traceable. The banquet scene also demonstrates the gang's internal chaos — the quarrels, the drunkenness, the inability to maintain dignity even at their own celebration — foreshadowing the disintegration that follows.

## Significance

Structurally, the Banquet for Rico occupies the same position in *Little Caesar* that the wedding feast occupies in *The Godfather*: a moment of celebration that simultaneously displays the gang's power and its vulnerabilities. Burnett renders the scene with satirical precision — the Italian flags, the self-conscious "apéritif" correction, the goat impressions — creating a portrait of gangsters who crave respectability but cannot escape their own coarseness. The Big Boy's refusal to be photographed is the novel in miniature: the old guard understands what the young aspirant does not, that visibility is the gangster's greatest enemy.
