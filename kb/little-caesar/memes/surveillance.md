Every character in *Little Caesar* operates under the knowledge that they are being watched — the gangster world is a panopticon in which observation is the prelude to violence or arrest.

## Definition

Surveillance in the novel takes multiple forms: police surveillance (detectives tracking gangsters), internal surveillance (informants within gangs), rival surveillance (gangs watching each other's movements), and media surveillance (newspapers reporting on criminal activity). The cumulative effect is a pervasive atmosphere of exposure and vulnerability — no action is private, no conversation secure.

## In the Novel

- **Scabby the informer** — The most explicit embodiment of surveillance: "Passing as a police informer, he was in reality a member of Vettori's gang" [003-015]. Scabby's dual role means the gang is simultaneously watching the police and being watched by them. The informer system is so thoroughly corrupted that surveillance flows in all directions.

- **The police Packard** — The "big Packard with a hooded machine-gun in the back seat" carrying plainclothes men [009-036] is mobile surveillance — the police presence that can appear anywhere, at any time, turning routine criminal operations into life-or-death encounters.

- **Vettori's paranoia** — Sam Vettori lives in a state of constant vigilance, fearing both the police and his own lieutenants. His repeated warnings against "gunwork" reflect the knowledge that violence attracts attention, and attention leads to destruction.

- **Newspaper surveillance** — The press functions as a parallel surveillance system, making private criminal acts public knowledge. The gangsters read the papers to track each other; the police read them for leads; the public reads them for entertainment. Everyone is watching everyone.

## Thematic Function

Surveillance is the atmospheric condition of the gangster world — the reason conversations are whispered, meetings are brief, and trust is impossible. Burnett uses it to create the novel's distinctive tone of pervasive dread. The gangster is never safe, never private, never offstage. He is always being watched, and the watcher is always armed.
