# Crime

Crime. The profession and the world of *Little Caesar* — organized, hierarchical, territorial, and ultimately self-destructive.

## Definition

In Burnett's Chicago, crime is not individual law-breaking but an organized system with its own economy, social structure, and moral code. It encompasses gambling, bootlegging, protection rackets, robbery, and murder — diversified revenue streams managed through gang structures and protected by a combination of violence, corruption, and territorial control.

## In the Novel

The novel's characters inhabit crime as a total world. Joe Massara's desire to leave it for legitimate life as a dancer is treated as a dangerous fantasy. The Crime Commission represents an external threat — a new, more systematic approach to law enforcement that disturbs even the Big Boy: "It's that goddamn Crime Commission business." (PN041-013)

Crime in the novel has its own ironic commentary. The theatre crowd dismisses gangster drama: "They play that crime stuff off the boards." (PN003-026) The line captures the distance between the romanticized gangster of popular entertainment and the grim reality Burnett depicts.

The novel traces the full arc of a criminal career — from Rico's early stickups in Youngstown to his reign over Chicago's North Side to his final flight and death. The trajectory suggests that crime, however lucrative in the short term, is ultimately a dead end.

## Broader Context

*Little Caesar* was published in 1929, at the height of Prohibition and the peak of organized crime's power in American cities. Burnett's depiction of crime as a rational, structured enterprise — rather than a moral failing — was groundbreaking. The novel influenced the gangster film genre and helped establish the template for how American culture would understand organized crime: as a shadow society operating parallel to legitimate institutions.
