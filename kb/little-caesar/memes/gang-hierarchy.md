# Gang Hierarchy

The **gang hierarchy** in *Little Caesar* is the invisible architecture of the criminal world — a clearly defined ladder of power, respect, and survival that every character must climb or be crushed by. Burnett maps this hierarchy with documentary precision, from the neighborhood stickup men at the bottom to the political fixers at the apex.

## Definition

The novel's gangster hierarchy has four distinct tiers:

1. **The Fixers** ([[big-boy|Big Boy]]): Men who operate above the level of street crime, controlling political connections, "fixing" legal problems, and issuing commands that bind even the most powerful gang bosses. They are never photographed and never named.

2. **The Gang Bosses** ([[vettori-gang|Sam Vettori]], [[pete-montana|Pete Montana]]): Leaders of individual criminal organizations who control territory, command crews, and manage relationships with the fixers above and the soldiers below.

3. **The Crew Leaders / Lieutenants** ([[rico-bandello|Rico Bandello]], [[otero|Otero]]): The operational layer — men who plan and execute jobs, command respect through violence, and compete among themselves for advancement.

4. **The Soldiers** ([[bat-carillo|Bat Carillo]], [[kid-bean|Kid Bean]], [[tony-passa|Tony Passa]]): The rank-and-file gang members who drive cars, carry guns, and take orders. Disposable by definition.

## In the Novel

The entire plot of *Little Caesar* is a study in hierarchy — specifically, Rico Bandello's determination to climb it and the system's resistance to his ambition. Rico's trajectory — from lieutenant to gang leader to independent operator to fugitive — traces a complete arc through the hierarchy, and his fall is as much about violating its unwritten rules as about any specific tactical error.

The hierarchy is enforced through a combination of violence (the gun), economics (the racket), and social ritual (the banquet, the deference, the "Mr." that men use for their superiors). It is not a meritocracy — the Big Boy did not rise through superior gunplay — but neither is it entirely closed. Rico's rise proves that ambition and violence can carry a man up the ladder; his fall proves that they cannot keep him there.

## Broader Context

The gang hierarchy Burnett depicts reflects the real organizational structure of Chicago's Prohibition-era criminal syndicates. The Capone organization's division into crews, each with a defined territory and a clear chain of command, was the model for the novel's fictional gangs. Burnett's innovation was to treat this structure not as exotic background but as a social system with its own logic, constraints, and tragic inevitability.
