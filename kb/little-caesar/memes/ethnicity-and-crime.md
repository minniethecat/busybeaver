# Ethnicity and Crime

The relationship between **ethnicity and crime** in *Little Caesar* is one of the novel's deepest and most uncomfortable themes — the way immigrant identity, social exclusion, and the American dream of mobility combine to produce the gangster as a social type. Burnett's characters are overwhelmingly Italian-American, and their ethnicity is neither incidental nor decorative: it is the structural condition of their criminal careers.

## In the Novel

Burnett handles ethnicity with documentary precision and minimal editorializing. Every major gangster is Italian: Sam Vettori, Rico Bandello (born Cesare Bandello), Otero ("the Greek" — the exception that proves the rule), Joe Massara, Little Arnie. The gang's social world — the banquets with Italian flags, the backroom card games, the deference to "the old country" — is saturated with ethnic markers. Yet Rico himself cannot speak Italian, and his ambition is explicitly framed as an escape from ethnic limitation into a modern, American identity. [032-005]

The novel's ethnic geography is equally precise: the Italian [[little-italy|Little Italy]], the Jewish "Kiketown," the Irish police, the "bohunk" (Slavic) nightclub manager. Each character's ethnicity determines their place in the novel's social hierarchy, and the gangster's rise is fundamentally a story about transcending — or attempting to transcend — the limitations that ethnicity imposes.

## The Assimilation Narrative

Rico's tragedy is, in part, an assimilation tragedy. He is the immigrant's son who rejects the old-country identity (the language, the church, the community) in favor of a purely American ambition — money, power, recognition — but discovers that the only path available to him leads through the very ethnic criminal world he despises. He cannot escape being Italian, but he cannot be Italian on the old terms. He is trapped between identities, and the novel suggests that this trap is the immigrant's condition.

## Broader Context

The association between Italian ethnicity and organized crime in American culture was, by 1929, already becoming a stereotype. Burnett's achievement was to treat it not as sensationalism but as sociology: the gangster is not a monster but a product of specific historical conditions — immigration, poverty, social exclusion, and the Prohibition-era opportunity structure. The novel's ethnic realism was among the qualities that made it, in the judgment of later critics, the first serious American gangster novel.
