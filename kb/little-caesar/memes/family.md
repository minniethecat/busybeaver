The traditional family is conspicuously absent from *Little Caesar* — the gang has replaced it as the primary social structure, with fatal consequences for everyone involved.

## Definition

Family in the novel is a void around which the action organizes itself. Rico has no visible family. Joe Massara's family appears only as a distant concern. The gang operates as a substitute family — Vettori as patriarch, Rico as ambitious son, the crew as siblings — but this substitute lacks the binding force of blood. Gang loyalty is contingent on power; family loyalty, in theory, is not. The novel demonstrates what happens when the former replaces the latter.

## In the Novel

- **Rico's family vacuum** — We never meet Rico's parents, siblings, wife, or children. He exists without origin or lineage, a self-created man whose only attachments are professional. This vacuum is not incidental — it is the condition of his mobility. Family would anchor him; the gang demands he float free.

- **The gang as family** — The Vettori crew operates with the rituals of an Italian family: shared meals at Sicily Pete's restaurant [005-002], card games, mutual obligations. But this family is a business, and when Rico judges Vettori weak, filial loyalty dissolves instantly.

- **Tony Passa's mother** — Tony's death introduces the novel's only glimpse of real family grief. His mother's relationship with Father McConagha [013-028] suggests a world of family piety that the gangsters have abandoned but not entirely escaped.

- **Ma Magdalena** — The boarding-house keeper who shelters Rico is the closest the novel comes to a maternal figure, but she is transactional, not familial. She hides Rico because he pays, not because she loves him.

## Thematic Function

Burnett's erasure of family from the gangster world is a deliberate structural choice. The gang is a family stripped of love — loyalty without affection, obligation without care. This substitution is what makes the gangster's rise possible (no dependents to protect) and his fall inevitable (no one to catch him). The novel's bleakness is rooted in this absence: these men live and die in a world without mothers.
