# Pete Montana

Pietro "Pete" Montana. The crime boss controlling Chicago's North Side, considered one of the city's most powerful gangsters before his fall.

## Role in the Novel

Pete Montana is the established power on Chicago's North Side — a figure of such dominance that other gang leaders do not challenge him. He serves as both a foil to Rico Bandello's ambition and a marker of Rico's meteoric rise: when Rico negotiates with Montana as an equal, it signals his ascent; when the Big Boy later declares Montana "through," it marks the old order's collapse ([002-062]).

## Character Profile

- **Full Name**: Pietro Fontano
- **Aliases**: Pete Montana
- **Affiliation**: North Side gang (independent)
- **Role**: Crime boss, territory controller
- **First Appearance**: Chapter 2 ([002-062])

## Biography

Pete Montana is introduced as the entrenched power of the North Side, described as "a wise bird" who "had the North Side tied up" ([002-062]). Unlike flashier gangsters, he presents himself with quiet dignity — "a big, solemn, respectable-looking Italian" dressed quietly, wearing no jewellery and carrying a cane ([044-001]).

His lieutenant is Ritz Colonna, a former prizefighter. Montana's gang dominates the vast territory north of Little Italy, maintaining a policy of "magnificent indifference" toward the smaller operators on his borders ([026-040]).

Montana's pivotal scene comes when he visits Rico to negotiate territory. He cedes "Kiketown" to Rico, acknowledging the younger man's effectiveness while attempting to bind him through diplomacy rather than war ([044-001]–[045-001]). Rico sees through the gesture, recognizing that "Pete Montana was scared" ([046-003]).

Montana's downfall is revealed at a dinner with the Big Boy, who tells Rico bluntly: "Pete Montana's through" ([060-010]). The once-great boss becomes a symbol of the gangster life cycle — rise, dominance, and inevitable decline.

## Key Scenes

**Territory negotiation with Rico** ([044-001]–[046-009]): Montana arrives at Rico's room with Ritz Colonna, measuring up the younger gangster. Rico finds him "fat and puffy, like Sam Vettori" and realizes Montana's diplomatic approach masks fear.

> "Mopping up, ain't you, Rico?" asked Montana, who kept his eyes lowered. ([044-009])

> "by rights that territory's mine, get the idea? I could get all the protection I wanted, but I don't muscle in on no right guy, see? Kiketown's yours, Rico." ([045-004])

**The Big Boy's verdict** ([060-010]): At a dinner with Rico, the Big Boy casually announces Montana's end, demonstrating the ruthlessness of gangland hierarchy.

## Significance

Pete Montana embodies the established order that Rico disrupts. His physical description — "big, solemn, respectable-looking" — contrasts sharply with Rico's small, intense presence, yet Rico dominates their encounter. Montana's arc from "the great Pete Montana" to "through" illustrates the novel's central theme: power in the gangster world is provisional, and yesterday's king is tomorrow's casualty.
