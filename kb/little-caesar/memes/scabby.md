# Scabby

Scabby. A double agent who poses as a police informer while secretly feeding information to Sam Vettori's gang. Dark, undersized, with a blotched face and a permanent melancholy expression — he never smiles, and his treachery runs to the bone.

## Role in the Novel

Scabby is the novel's embodiment of underworld rot — a man so thoroughly corrupt that he sells information to both sides and betrays everyone eventually. He provides Vettori's gang with intelligence on robbery targets, serves as a go-between in Rico's later schemes, and in the final act stands revealed as the ultimate betrayer who would "sell his own mother out for a split on seven grand." ([003-015], [051-046])

## Biography

Scabby first appears in Sam Vettori's office, described with Burnett's characteristic precision: dark and undersized, with a heavy, sullen, blotched face. He passes as a police informer but is in reality a member of Vettori's organization — a double role that gives him access to information on both sides of the law. ([003-015])

He provides the gang with intelligence on robbery targets, notably feeding them information about a safe that "a baby could crack." But Scabby is cautious by nature — when Vettori gets too ambitious, Scabby warns him that times have changed: "Things ain't what they used to be, Sam. It's getting dangerous. They've even got the Big Boy scared." His caution is not morality but survival instinct. ([002-049], [003-026])

In the novel's final chapters, Scabby reappears as the agent of Rico's doom. When Rico realizes Scabby is involved in his capture, his reaction is one of pure resentment: "Good God, wasn't that a break! Scabby hated him and Scabby would sell his own mother out for a split on seven grand." The judgment is final and devastating — Scabby is, in the end, defined entirely by his capacity for betrayal. ([051-045], [051-046])

## Key Scenes

Scabby's first appearance establishes his physical and moral character:

> Scabby was dark and undersized with a heavy, sullen, blotched face. Passing as a police informer, he was in reality a member of Sam Vettori's gang. "Everything's jake," said Scabby, taking off his hat and revealing a shining bald head. ([003-015], [003-017])

His warning to Vettori reveals the cautious coward beneath the informer's facade:

> "I've had my say. But things ain't what they used to be, Sam. It's getting dangerous. They've even got the Big Boy scared." ([003-026])

Rico's final judgment seals Scabby's legacy:

> Scabby hated him and Scabby would sell his own mother out for a split on seven grand. ([051-046])

## Significance

Scabby represents the moral basement of Burnett's gangster world — a creature so debased that even other criminals hold him in contempt. His function in the narrative is to demonstrate that loyalty, the gang's supposed highest value, is ultimately a fiction sustained by self-interest. When the money is right, Scabby will sell anyone — and the novel implies that, in this world, everyone has their price.
