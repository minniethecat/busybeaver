# Bat Carillo

Bat Carillo. A former light-heavyweight boxer who once led his own small crew, now employed as the bouncer at the [[palermo-club|Palermo Club]] — a vivid illustration of what happens to gangsters who fail to rise, and a living reminder of the hierarchy [[rico-bandello|Rico Bandello]] has transcended.

## Role in the Novel

Bat Carillo appears briefly but memorably: he is the man who puts his head in the door to announce visitors, the bouncer who physically embodies the gang's decline from its earlier, more violent days. His presence establishes the pecking order of Vettori's world — even a former crew leader ends up working the door if he lacks the ambition or luck to climb higher. [001-029] [025-004]

## Character Profile

- **Full Name**: Bat Carillo
- **Aliases**: Bat
- **Affiliation**: Sam Vettori's gang (formerly independent crew leader)
- **Role**: Bouncer at the Palermo Club
- **First Appearance**: Part I, announcing Joe Massara's arrival at Vettori's office [001-029]

## Biography

For three or four years, Bat Carillo had been the leader of one of the small gangs absorbed into Vettori's larger operation. Unlike Rico, who rose by ruthless ambition, Carillo never capitalized on his position and was gradually displaced — a process the novel handles with characteristic economy, reducing a former crew leader to a man who "put his head in the door" to announce visitors. [001-029] [025-004]

His presence at the banquet for Rico is telling: he is among the attendees but entirely marginal, a face in the crowd rather than a participant in the evening's drama. The novel's hierarchy is absolute — those who fail to climb are not destroyed but demoted, kept around as object lessons in the cost of mediocrity. [025-004]

## Key Scenes

**The bouncer's entrance** — The band stops playing and Carillo puts his head in the door to announce a visitor, in the novel's first depiction of the Palermo Club's underworld ecosystem. [001-029]

**The banquet** — Carillo is among the gang members celebrating Rico's rise, his presence a silent reminder of the gangster's alternative: rise or be reduced. [025-004]

## Significance

Bat Carillo is a cautionary figure in Burnett's gangster taxonomy. He is not evil, not incompetent — simply ordinary, a man who once had a crew and now opens doors for the men who surpassed him. In a novel obsessed with the mechanics of rise and fall, Carillo represents the static state: the gangster who neither climbs nor crashes, but simply drifts downward into irrelevance.
