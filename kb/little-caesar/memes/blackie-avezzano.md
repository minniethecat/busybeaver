# Blackie Avezzano

Blackie Avezzano. The manager of Sam Vettori's garage — small, bowlegged, and so dark-complexioned he was "taken for a mulatto many times." He handles money and logistics for Vettori's operation with a quiet, deliberate manner, his broken English a mark of his immigrant roots.

## Role in the Novel

Blackie operates in the gang's logistical infrastructure — running the garage that serves as a front for criminal operations, handling cash, and serving as a trusted functionary in Vettori's declining organization. He is not a gunman but an administrator, and his presence in the narrative shows the breadth of the gang's operations beyond its violent core. ([018-008])

## Character Profile

- **Full Name**: Blackie Avezzano
- **Aliases**: Blackie
- **Affiliation**: Sam Vettori's gang
- **Role**: Garage manager, money handler
- **First Appearance**: Part II, reporting to Vettori and Rico ([018-003])

## Biography

Blackie Avezzano is introduced through Bat Carillo: "Blackie wants to see you." He enters Vettori's room and shuts the door behind him — a careful, deliberate man who takes his time collecting his thoughts before speaking. Burnett describes him with characteristic precision: small, bowlegged, dark enough to have been mistaken for a mulatto. ([018-003], [018-008])

His English is limited — "Blackie couldn't speak very good English" — but Vettori prefers English, forcing Blackie to do the best he can. The linguistic compromise reflects the gang's assimilation into American life and the compromises required of its immigrant members. ([018-013])

At Rico's gang banquet, Blackie sits at the foot of the table — his position in the seating hierarchy reflecting his rank. When the drinking gets heavy, "Blackie Avezzano fell under the table and stayed there, lying on his face." The image is both comic and pathetic — a small man overwhelmed by the excess of the gangster life he serves. ([026-093], [027-005])

Later, Vettori notes that "Blackie's got all the jack" — a testament to his role as the gang's money man, trusted to hold the cash while the gunmen go broke between jobs. ([042-009])

## Key Scenes

Blackie's introduction captures his quiet, deliberate nature:

> Blackie Avezzano, who managed Sam's garage, came in and shut the door behind him. He was small and bowlegged, and he was so dark that he had been taken for a mulatto many times. … Blackie sat down at the table and seemed to be making an effort to collect his thoughts. ([018-008], [018-011])

At the banquet:

> Blackie Avezzano fell under the table and stayed there, lying on his face. ([027-005])

## Significance

Blackie represents the invisible infrastructure of organized crime — the men who keep the books, manage the garages, hold the money, and never make headlines. His broken English and physical smallness mark him as an immigrant ground down by the American machine, yet his position as the gang's money handler gives him a quiet, steady importance that the flashier gunmen lack. In Burnett's world, men like Blackie are the foundation on which Vettori — and later Rico — build their empires.
