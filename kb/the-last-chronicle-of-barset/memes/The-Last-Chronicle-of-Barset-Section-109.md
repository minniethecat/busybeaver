[109-003]CHAPTER LVI.

[109-004]THE ARCHDEACON GOES TO FRAMLEY.

[109-005][Illustration]

[109-006]By some of those unseen telegraphic wires which carry news about the

[109-007]country and make no charge for the conveyance, Archdeacon Grantly

[109-008]heard that his son the major was at Framley. Now in that itself there

[109-009]would have been nothing singular. There had been for years much

[109-010]intimacy between the Lufton family and the Grantly family,--so much

[109-011]that an alliance between the two houses had once been planned, the

[109-012]elders having considered it expedient that the young lord should

[109-013]marry that Griselda who had since mounted higher in the world even

[109-014]than the elders had then projected for her. There had come no such

[109-015]alliance; but the intimacy had not ceased, and there was nothing in

[109-016]itself surprising in the fact that Major Grantly should be staying at

[109-017]Framley Court. But the archdeacon, when he heard the news, bethought

[109-018]him at once of Grace Crawley. Could it be possible that his old

[109-019]friend Lady Lufton,--Lady Lufton whom he had known and trusted all

[109-020]his life, whom he had ever regarded as a pillar of the church in

[109-021]Barsetshire,--should now be untrue to him in a matter so closely

[109-022]affecting his interests? Men when they are worried by fears and

[109-023]teased by adverse circumstances become suspicious of those on whom

[109-024]suspicion should never rest. It was hardly possible, the archdeacon

[109-025]thought, that Lady Lufton should treat him so unworthily,--but the

[109-026]circumstances were strong against his friend. Lady Lufton had induced

[109-027]Miss Crawley to go to Framley, much against his advice, at a time

[109-028]when such a visit seemed to him to be very improper; and it now

[109-029]appeared that his son was to be there at the same time,--a fact of

[109-030]which Lady Lufton had made no mention to him whatever. Why had not

[109-031]Lady Lufton told him that Henry Grantly was coming to Framley Court?

[109-032]The reader, whose interest in the matter will be less keen than

[109-033]was the archdeacon's, will know very well why Lady Lufton had said

[109-034]nothing about the major's visit. The reader will remember that Lady

[109-035]Lufton, when she saw the archdeacon, was as ignorant as to the

[109-036]intended visit as was the archdeacon himself. But the archdeacon was

[109-037]uneasy, troubled, and suspicious;--and he suspected his old friend

[109-038]unworthily.

[109-039]He spoke to his wife about it within a very few hours of the arrival

[109-040]of the tidings by those invisible wires. He had already told her that

[109-041]Miss Crawley was to go to Framley parsonage, and that he thought that

[109-042]Mrs. Robarts was wrong to receive her at such a time. "It is only

[109-043]intended for good-nature," Mrs. Grantly had said. "It is misplaced

[109-044]good-nature at the present moment," the archdeacon had replied. Mrs.

[109-045]Grantly had not thought it worth her while to undertake at the moment

[109-046]any strong defence of the Framley people. She knew well how odious

[109-047]was the name of Crawley in her husband's ears, and she felt that the

[109-048]less that was said at present about the Crawleys the better for the

[109-049]peace of the rectory at Plumstead. She had therefore allowed the

[109-050]expression of his disapproval to pass unchallenged. But now he came

[109-051]upon her with a more bitter grievance, and she was obliged to argue

[109-052]the matter with him.

[109-053]"What do you think?" said he; "Henry is at Framley."

[109-054]"He can hardly be staying there," said Mrs. Grantly, "because I know

[109-055]that he is so very busy at home." The business at home of which the

[109-056]major's mother was speaking was his projected moving from Cosby

[109-057]Lodge, a subject which was also very odious to the archdeacon. He did

[109-058]not wish his son to move from Cosby Lodge. He could not endure the

[109-059]idea that his son should be known throughout the county to be giving

[109-060]up a residence because he could not afford to keep it. The archdeacon

[109-061]could have afforded to keep up two Cosby Lodges for his son, and

[109-062]would have been well pleased to do so, if only his son would not

[109-063]misbehave against him so shamefully! He could not bear that his

[109-064]son should be punished, openly, before the eyes of all Barsetshire.

[109-065]Indeed he did not wish that his son should be punished at all. He

[109-066]simply desired that his son should recognize his father's power to

[109-067]inflict punishment. It would be henbane to Archdeacon Grantly to have

[109-068]a poor son,--a son living at Pau,--among Frenchmen!--because he could

[109-069]not afford to live in England. Why had the archdeacon been careful of

[109-070]his money, adding house to house and field to field? He himself was

[109-071]contented,--so he told himself,--to die as he had lived in a country

[109-072]parsonage, working with the collar round his neck up to the day of

[109-073]his death, if God would allow him so to do. He was ambitious of

[109-074]no grandeur for himself. So he would tell himself,--being partly

[109-075]oblivious of certain episodes in his own life. All his wealth had

[109-076]been got together for his children. He desired that his sons should

[109-077]be fitting brothers for their August sister. And now the son who was

[109-078]nearest to him, whom he was bent upon making a squire in his own

[109-079]county, wanted to marry the daughter of a man who had stolen twenty

[109-080]pounds, and when objection was made to so discreditable a connexion,

[109-081]replied by packing up all his things and saying that he would go and

[109-082]live--at Pau! The archdeacon therefore did not like to hear of his

[109-083]son being very busy at home.

[109-084]"I don't know whether he's busy or not," said the archdeacon, "but I

[109-085]tell you he is staying at Framley."

[109-086]"From whom have you heard it?"

[109-087]"What matter does that make if it is so? I heard it from Flurry."

[109-088]"Flurry may have been mistaken," said Mrs. Grantly.

[109-089]"It is not at all likely. Those people always know about such things.

[109-090]He heard it from the Framley keeper. I don't doubt but it's true, and

[109-091]I think that it's a great shame."

[109-092]"A great shame that Henry should be at Framley! He has been there two

[109-093]or three times every year since he has lived in the county."

[109-094]"It is a great shame that he should be had over there just at the

[109-095]time when that girl is there also. It is impossible to believe that

[109-096]such a thing is an accident."

[109-097]"But, archdeacon, you do not mean to say that you think that Lady

[109-098]Lufton has arranged it?"

[109-099]"I don't know who has arranged it. Somebody has arranged it. If it is

[109-100]Robarts, that is almost worse. One could forgive a woman in such a

[109-101]matter better than one could a man."

[109-102]"Psha!" Mrs. Grantly's temper was never bitter, but at this moment it

[109-103]was not sweetened by her husband's very uncivil reference to her sex.

[109-104]"The whole idea is nonsense, and you should get it out of your head."

[109-105]"Am I to get it out of my head that Henry wants to make this girl his

[109-106]wife, and that the two are at this moment at Framley together?" In

[109-107]this the archdeacon was wrong as to his facts. Major Grantly had left

[109-108]Framley on the previous day, having stayed there only one night.

[109-109]"It is coming to that that one can trust no one--no one--literally

[109-110]no one." Mrs. Grantly perfectly understood that the archdeacon,

[109-111]in the agony of the moment, intended to exclude even herself from

[109-112]his confidence by that "no one;" but to this she was indifferent,

[109-113]understanding accurately when his words should be accepted as

[109-114]expressing his thoughts, and when they should be supposed to express

[109-115]only his anger.

[109-116]"The probability is that no one at Lufton knew anything about Henry's

[109-117]partiality for Miss Crawley," said Mrs. Grantly.

[109-118]"I tell you I think they are both at Framley together."

[109-119]"And I tell you that if they are, which I doubt, they are there

[109-120]simply by an accident. Besides, what does it matter? If they choose

[109-121]to marry each other, you and I cannot prevent them. They don't want

[109-122]any assistance from Lady Lufton, or anybody else. They have simply

[109-123]got to make up their own minds, and then no one can hinder them."

[109-124]"And, therefore, you would like to see them brought together?"

[109-125]"I say nothing about that, archdeacon; but I do say that we must take

[109-126]these things as they come. What can we do? Henry may go and stay with

[109-127]Lady Lufton if he pleases. You and I cannot prevent him."

[109-128]After this the archdeacon walked away, and would not argue the matter

[109-129]any further with his wife at that moment. He knew very well that he

[109-130]could not get the better of her, and was apt at such moments to think

[109-131]that she took an unfair advantage of him by keeping her temper. But

[109-132]he could not get out of his head the idea that perhaps on this very

[109-133]day things were being arranged between his son and Grace Crawley at

[109-134]Framley; and he resolved that he himself would go over and see what

[109-135]might be done. He would, at any rate, tell all his trouble to Lady

[109-136]Lufton, and beg his old friend to assist him. He could not think that

[109-137]such a one as he had always known Lady Lufton to be would approve of

[109-138]a marriage between Henry Grantly and Grace Crawley. At any rate, he

[109-139]would learn the truth. He had once been told that Grace Crawley had

[109-140]herself refused to marry his son, feeling that she would do wrong to

[109-141]inflict so great an injury upon any gentleman. He had not believed

[109-142]in so great a virtue. He could not believe in it now,--now, when he

[109-143]heard that Miss Crawley and his son were staying together in the

[109-144]same parish. Somebody must be doing him an injury. It could hardly

[109-145]be chance. But his presence at Framley might even yet have a good

[109-146]effect, and he would at least learn the truth. So he had himself

[109-147]driven to Barchester, and from Barchester he took post-horses to

[109-148]Framley.

[109-149]As he came near to the village, he grew to be somewhat ashamed of

[109-150]himself, or, at least, nervous as to the mode in which he would

[109-151]proceed. The driver, turning round to him, had suggested that he

[109-152]supposed he was to drive to "My lady's." This injustice to Lord

[109-153]Lufton, to whom the house belonged, and with whom his mother lived

[109-154]as a guest, was very common in the county; for old Lady Lufton had

[109-155]lived at Framley Court through her son's long minority, and had kept

[109-156]the house there till his marriage; and even since his marriage she

[109-157]had been recognized as its presiding genius. It certainly was not

[109-158]the fault of old Lady Lufton, as she always spoke of everything as

[109-159]belonging either to her son or to her daughter-in-law. The archdeacon

[109-160]had been in doubt whether he would go to the Court or to the

[109-161]parsonage. Could he have done exactly as he wished, he would have

[109-162]left the chaise and walked to the parsonage, so as to reach it

[109-163]without the noise and fuss incidental to a postilion's arrival. But

[109-164]that was impossible. He could not drop into Framley as though he had

[109-165]come from the clouds, and, therefore, he told the man to do as he

[109-166]had suggested. "To my lady's?" said the postilion. The archdeacon

[109-167]assented, and the man, with loud cracks of his whip, and with a

[109-168]spasmodic gallop along the short avenue, took the archdeacon up to

[109-169]the door of Lord Lufton's house. He asked for Lord Lufton first,

[109-170]putting on his pleasantest smile, so that the servant should not

[109-171]suspect the purpose, of which he was somewhat ashamed. Was Lord

[109-172]Lufton at home? Lord Lufton was not at home. Lord Lufton had gone up

[109-173]to London that morning, intending to return the day after to-morrow;

[109-174]but both my ladies were at home. So the archdeacon was shown into the

[109-175]room where both my ladies were sitting,--and with them he found Mrs.

[109-176]Robarts. Any one who had become acquainted with the habits of the

[109-177]Framley ladies would have known that this might very probably be the

[109-178]case. The archdeacon himself was as well aware as any one of the

[109-179]modes of life at Framley. The lord's wife was the parson's sister,

[109-180]and the parson's wife had from her infancy been the petted friend of

[109-181]the old lady. Of course they all lived very much together. Of course

[109-182]Mrs. Robarts was as much at home in the drawing-room of Framley Court

[109-183]as she was in her own drawing-room at the parsonage. Nevertheless,

[109-184]the archdeacon thought himself to be hardly used when he found that

[109-185]Mrs. Robarts was at the house.
