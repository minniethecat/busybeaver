[128-001]"Really I don't know what to do as to getting upon him again," said

[128-002]Mr. Thumble.

[128-003]"If you will allow him to progress slowly," said Mr. Crawley, "he

[128-004]will probably travel with the greater safety."

[128-005]"I don't know what you call slow, Mr. Crawley. I was ever so much

[128-006]over two hours coming here from Barchester. He stumbled almost at

[128-007]every step."

[128-008]"Did he fall while you were on him?" asked the major.

[128-009]"Indeed he did, sir. You never saw such a thing, Major Grantly. Look

[128-010]here." Then Mr. Thumble, turning round, showed that the rear portion

[128-011]of his clothes had not escaped without injury.

[128-012]"It was well he was not going fast, or you would have come on to your

[128-013]head," said Grantly.

[128-014]"It was a mercy," said Thumble. "But, sir, as it was, I came to the

[128-015]ground with much violence. It was on Spigglewick Hill, where the road

[128-016]is covered with loose stones. I see, sir, you have a gig and horse

[128-017]here, with a servant. Perhaps, as the circumstances are so very

[128-018]peculiar,--" Then Mr. Thumble stopped, and looked up into the major's

[128-019]face with imploring eyes. But the major had no tenderness for such

[128-020]sufferings. "I'm sorry to say that I am going quite the other way,"

[128-021]he said. "I am returning to Silverbridge."

[128-022]Mr. Thumble hesitated, and then made a renewed request. "If you would

[128-023]not mind taking me to Silverbridge, I could get home from thence by

[128-024]railway; and perhaps you would allow your servant to take the horse

[128-025]to Barchester."

[128-026]Major Grantly was for a moment dumfounded. "The request is most

[128-027]unreasonable, sir," said Mr. Crawley.

[128-028]"That is as Major Grantly pleases to look at it," said Mr. Thumble.

[128-029]"I am sorry to say that it is quite out of my power," said the major.

[128-030]"You can surely walk, leading the beast, if you fear to mount him,"

[128-031]said Mr. Crawley.

[Illustration: Mrs. Proudie's Emissary.]

[128-032]"I shall do as I please about that," said Mr. Thumble. "And, Mr.

[128-033]Crawley, if you will have the kindness to leave things in the parish

[128-034]just as they are,--just as they are, I will be obliged to you. It is

[128-035]the bishop's wish that you should touch nothing." Mr. Thumble was by

[128-036]this time on the step, and Mr. Crawley instantly slammed the door.

[128-037]"The gentleman is a clergyman from Barchester," said Mr. Crawley,

[128-038]modestly folding his hands upon his breast, "whom the bishop has

[128-039]sent over here to take upon himself temporarily the services of the

[128-040]church, and, as it appears, the duties also of the parish. I refrain

[128-041]from animadverting upon his lordship's choice."

[128-042]"And are you leaving Hogglestock?"

[128-043]"When I have found a shelter for my wife and children I shall do so;

[128-044]nay, peradventure, I must do so before any such shelter can be found.

[128-045]I shall proceed in that matter as I am bid. I am one who can regard

[128-046]myself as no longer possessing the privilege of free action in

[128-047]anything. But while I have a room at your service, permit me to ask

[128-048]you to enter it." Then Mr. Crawley motioned him in with his hand, and

[128-049]Major Grantly found himself in the presence of Mrs. Crawley and her

[128-050]younger daughter.

[128-051]He looked at them both for a moment, and could trace much of the

[128-052]lines of that face which he loved so well. But the troubles of life

[128-053]had almost robbed the elder lady of her beauty; and with the younger,

[128-054]the awkward thinness of the last years of feminine childhood had not

[128-055]yet given place to the fulfilment of feminine grace. But the likeness

[128-056]in each was quite enough to make him feel that he ought to be at home

[128-057]in that room. He thought that he could love the woman as his mother,

[128-058]and the girl as his sister. He found it very difficult to begin any

[128-059]conversation in their presence, and yet it seemed to be his duty to

[128-060]begin. Mr. Crawley had marshalled him into the room, and having done

[128-061]so, stood aside near the door. Mrs. Crawley had received him very

[128-062]graciously, and having done so, seemed to be ashamed of her own

[128-063]hospitality. Poor Jane had shrunk back into a distant corner, near

[128-064]the open standing desk at which she was accustomed to read Greek

[128-065]to her father, and, of course, could not be expected to speak. If

[128-066]Major Grantly could have found himself alone with any one of the

[128-067]three,--nay, if he could have been there with any two, he could have

[128-068]opened his budget at once; but, before all the family, he felt the

[128-069]difficulty of his situation. "Mrs. Crawley," said he, "I have been

[128-070]most anxious to make your acquaintance, and I trust you will excuse

[128-071]the liberty I have taken in calling."

[128-072]"I feel grateful to you, as I am sure does also my husband." So much

[128-073]she said, and then felt angry with herself for saying so much. Was

[128-074]she not expressing her strong hope that he might stand fast by her

[128-075]child, whereby the whole Crawley family would gain so much,--and the

[128-076]Grantly family lose much, in the same proportion?

[128-077]"Sir," said Mr. Crawley, "I owe you thanks, still unexpressed, in

[128-078]that you came forward, together with Mr. Robarts of Framley, to

[128-079]satisfy the not unnatural requisition of the magistrates before whom

[128-080]I was called upon to appear in the early winter. I know not why any

[128-081]one should have ventured into such jeopardy on my account."

[128-082]"There was no jeopardy, Mr. Crawley. Any one in the county would have

[128-083]done it."

[128-084]"I know not that; nor can I see that there was no jeopardy. I trust

[128-085]that I may assure you that there is no danger;--none, I mean, to you.

[128-086]The danger to myself and those belonging to me is, alas, very urgent.

[128-087]The facts of my position are pressing close upon me. Methinks I

[128-088]suffer more from the visit of the gentleman who has just departed

[128-089]from me than from anything that has yet happened to me. And yet he is

[128-090]in his right;--he is altogether in his right."

[128-091]"No, papa; he is not," said Jane, from her standing ground near the

[128-092]upright desk.

[128-093]"My dear," said her father, "you should be silent on such a subject.

[128-094]It is a matter hard to be understood in all its bearings,--even by

[128-095]those who are most conversant with them. But as to this we need not

[128-096]trouble Major Grantly."

[128-097]After that there was silence among them, and for a while it seemed as

[128-098]though there could be no approach to the subject on which Grantly had

[128-099]come thither to express himself. Mrs. Crawley, in her despair, said

[128-100]something about the weather; and the major, trying to draw near the

[128-101]special subject, became bold enough to remark "that he had had the

[128-102]pleasure of seeing Miss Crawley at Framley." "Mrs. Robarts has been

[128-103]very kind," said Mrs. Crawley, "very kind indeed. You can understand,

[128-104]Major Grantly, that this must be a very sad house for any young

[128-105]person." "I don't think it is at all sad," said Jane, still standing

[128-106]in the corner by the upright desk.

[128-107]Then Major Grantly rose from his seat and walked across to the girl

[128-108]and took her hand. "You are so like your sister," said he. "Your

[128-109]sister is a great friend of mine. She has often spoken to me of you.

[128-110]I hope we shall be friends some day." But Jane could make no answer

[128-111]to this, though she had been able to vindicate the general character

[128-112]of the house while she was left in her corner by herself. "I wonder

[128-113]whether you would be angry with me," continued the major, "if I told

[128-114]you that I wanted to speak a word to your father and mother alone?"

[128-115]To this Jane made no reply, but was out of the room almost before

[128-116]the words had reached the ears of her father and mother. Though

[128-117]she was only sixteen, and had as yet read nothing but Latin and

[128-118]Greek,--unless we are to count the twelve books of Euclid and Wood's

[128-119]Algebra, and sundry smaller exercises of the same description,--she

[128-120]understood, as well as any one then present, the reason why her

[128-121]absence was required.

[128-122]As she closed the door the major paused for a moment, expecting, or

[128-123]perhaps hoping, that the father or the mother would say a word. But

[128-124]neither of them had a word to say. They sat silent, and as though

[128-125]conscience-stricken. Here was a rich man come, of whom they had heard

[128-126]that he might probably wish to wed their daughter. It was manifest

[128-127]enough to both of them that no man could marry into their family

[128-128]without subjecting himself to a heavy portion of that reproach and

[128-129]disgrace which was attached to them. But how was it possible that

[128-130]they should not care more for their daughter,--for their own flesh

[128-131]and blood, than for the incidental welfare of this rich man? As

[128-132]regarded the man himself they had heard everything that was good.

[128-133]Such a marriage was like the opening of paradise to their child. "Nil

[128-134]conscire sibi," said the father to himself, as he buckled on his

[128-135]armour for the fight.

[128-136]When he had waited for a moment or two the major began. "Mrs.

[128-137]Crawley," he said, addressing himself to the mother, "I do not quite

[128-138]know how far you may be aware that I,--that I have for some time

[128-139]been,--been acquainted with your eldest daughter."

[128-140]"I have heard from her that she is acquainted with you," said Mrs.

[128-141]Crawley, almost panting with anxiety.

[128-142]"I may as well make a clean breast of it at once," said the major,

[128-143]smiling, "and say outright that I have come here to request your

[128-144]permission and her father's to ask her to be my wife." Then he was

[128-145]silent, and for a few moments neither Mr. nor Mrs. Crawley replied

[128-146]to him. She looked at her husband, and he gazed at the fire, and the

[128-147]smile died away from the major's face, as he watched the solemnity

[128-148]of them both. There was something almost forbidding in the peculiar

[128-149]gravity of Mr. Crawley's countenance when, as at present, something

[128-150]operated within him to cause him to express dissent from any

[128-151]proposition that was made to him. "I do not know how far this may be

[128-152]altogether new to you, Mrs. Crawley," said the major, waiting for a

[128-153]reply.

[128-154]"It is not new to us," said Mrs. Crawley.

[128-155]"May I hope, then, that you will not disapprove?"

[128-156]"Sir," said Mr. Crawley, "I am so placed by the untoward

[128-157]circumstances of my life that I can hardly claim to exercise over my

[128-158]own daughter that authority which should belong to a parent."

[128-159]"My dear, do not say that," exclaimed Mrs. Crawley.

[128-160]"But I do say it. Within three weeks of this time I may be a

[128-161]prisoner, subject to the criminal laws of my country. At this moment

[128-162]I am without the power of earning bread for myself, or for my wife,

[128-163]or for my children. Major Grantly, you have even now seen the

[128-164]departure of the gentleman who has been sent here to take my place in

[128-165]this parish. I am, as it were, an outlaw here, and entitled neither

[128-166]to obedience nor respect from those who under other circumstances

[128-167]would be bound to give me both."

[128-168]"Major Grantly," said the poor woman, "no husband or father in the

[128-169]county is more closely obeyed or more thoroughly respected and

[128-170]loved."

[128-171]"I am sure of it," said the major.

[128-172]"All this, however, matters nothing," continued Mr. Crawley, "and all

[128-173]speech on such homely matters would amount to an impertinence before

[128-174]you, sir, were it not that you have hinted at a purpose of connecting

[128-175]yourself at some future time with this unfortunate family."

[128-176]"I meant to be plain-spoken, Mr. Crawley."

[128-177]"I did not mean to insinuate, sir, that there was aught of reticence

[128-178]in your words, so contrived that you might fall back upon the

[128-179]vagueness of your expression for protection, should you hereafter see

[128-180]fit to change your purpose. I should have wronged you much by such a

[128-181]suggestion. I rather was minded to make known to you that I,--or, I

[128-182]should rather say, we," and Mr. Crawley pointed to his wife,--"shall

[128-183]not accept your plainness of speech as betokening aught beyond a

[128-184]conceived idea in furtherance of which you have thought it expedient

[128-185]to make certain inquiries."
