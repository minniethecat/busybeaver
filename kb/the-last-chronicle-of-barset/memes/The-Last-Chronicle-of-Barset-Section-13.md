[013-001]"You think she would refuse me?"

[013-002]"I do not think that she would accept you now. She would feel,--I am

[013-003]sure she would feel, that these hours of her father's sorrow are not

[013-004]hours in which love should be either offered or accepted. You shall,

[013-005]however, see her if you please."

[013-006]The major allowed himself a moment for thought; and as he thought he

[013-007]sighed. Grace Crawley became more beautiful in his eyes than ever,

[013-008]was endowed by these words from Miss Prettyman with new charms and

[013-009]brighter virtues than he had seen before. Let come what might he

[013-010]would ask her to be his wife on some future day, if he did not so ask

[013-011]her now. For the present, perhaps, he had better be guided by Miss

[013-012]Prettyman. "Then I will not see her," he said.

[013-013]"I think that will be the wiser course."

[013-014]"Of course you knew before this that I--loved her?"

[013-015]"I thought so, Major Grantly."

[013-016]"And that I intended to ask her to be my wife?"

[013-017]"Well; since you put the question to me so plainly, I must confess

[013-018]that as Grace's friend I should not quite have let things go on as

[013-019]they have gone,--though I am not at all disposed to interfere with

[013-020]any girl whom I believe to be pure and good as I know her to be,--but

[013-021]still I should hardly have been justified in letting things go as

[013-022]they have gone, if I had not believed that such was your purpose."

[013-023]"I wanted to set myself right with you, Miss Prettyman."

[013-024]"You are right with me,--quite right;" and she got up and gave him

[013-025]her hand. "You are a fine, noble-hearted gentleman, and I hope that

[013-026]our Grace may live to be your happy wife, and the mother of your

[013-027]darling child, and the mother of other children. I do not see how a

[013-028]woman could have a happier lot in life."

[013-029]"And will you give Grace my love?"

[013-030]"I will tell her at any rate that you have been here, and that

[013-031]you have inquired after her with the greatest kindness. She will

[013-032]understand what that means without any word of love."

[013-033]"Can I do anything for her,--or for her father; I mean in the way

[013-034]of--money? I don't mind mentioning it to you, Miss Prettyman."

[013-035]"I will tell her that you are ready to do it, if anything can be

[013-036]done. For myself I feel no doubt that the mystery will be cleared up

[013-037]at last; and then, if you will come here, we shall be so glad to see

[013-038]you.--I shall, at least."

[013-039]Then the major went, and Miss Prettyman herself actually descended

[013-040]with him into the hall, and bade him farewell most affectionately

[013-041]before her sister and two of the maids who came out to open the door.

[013-042]Miss Anne Prettyman, when she saw the great friendship with which

[013-043]the major was dismissed, could not contain herself, but asked most

[013-044]impudent questions, in a whisper indeed, but in such a whisper that

[013-045]any sharp-eared maid-servant could hear and understand them. "Is

[013-046]it settled," she asked when her sister had ascended only the first

[013-047]flight of stairs;--"has he popped?" The look with which the elder

[013-048]sister punished and dismayed the younger, I would not have borne for

[013-049]twenty pounds. She simply looked, and said nothing, but passed on.

[013-050]When she had regained her room she rang the bell, and desired the

[013-051]servant to ask Miss Crawley to be good enough to step to her. Poor

[013-052]Miss Anne retired discomforted into the solitude of one of the lower

[013-053]rooms, and sat for some minutes all alone, recovering from the shock

[013-054]of her sister's anger. "At any rate, he hasn't popped," she said to

[013-055]herself, as she made her way back to the school.

[013-056]After that Miss Prettyman and Miss Crawley were closeted together for

[013-057]about an hour. What passed between them need not be repeated here

[013-058]word for word; but it may be understood that Miss Prettyman said no

[013-059]more than she ought to have said, and that Grace understood all that

[013-060]she ought to have understood.

[013-061]"No man ever behaved with more considerate friendship, or more like a

[013-062]gentleman," said Miss Prettyman.

[013-063]"I am sure he is very good, and I am so glad he did not ask to see

[013-064]me," said Grace. Then Grace went away, and Miss Prettyman sat awhile

[013-065]in thought, considering what she had done, not without some stings of

[013-066]conscience.

[013-067]Major Grantly, as he walked home, was not altogether satisfied with

[013-068]himself, though he gave himself credit for some diplomacy which I

[013-069]do not think he deserved. He felt that Miss Prettyman and the world

[013-070]in general, should the world in general ever hear anything about

[013-071]it, would give him credit for having behaved well; and that he had

[013-072]obtained this credit without committing himself to the necessity

[013-073]of marrying the daughter of a thief, should things turn out badly

[013-074]in regard to the father. But,--and this but robbed him of all the

[013-075]pleasure which comes from real success,--but he had not treated Grace

[013-076]Crawley with the perfect generosity which love owes, and he was in

[013-077]some degree ashamed of himself. He felt, however, that he might

[013-078]probably have Grace, should he choose to ask for her when this

[013-079]trouble should have passed by. "And I will," he said to himself, as

[013-080]he entered the gate of his own paddock, and saw his child in her

[013-081]perambulator before the nurse. "And I will ask her, sooner or later,

[013-082]let things go as they may." Then he took the perambulator under his

[013-083]own charge for half-an-hour, to the satisfaction of the nurse, of the

[013-084]child, and of himself.

[013-085]CHAPTER VIII.

[013-086]MR. CRAWLEY IS TAKEN TO SILVERBRIDGE.

[013-087]It had become necessary on the Monday morning that Mrs. Crawley

[013-088]should obtain from her husband an undertaking that he would present

[013-089]himself before the magistrates at Silverbridge on the Thursday. She

[013-090]had been made to understand that the magistrates were sinning against

[013-091]the strict rule of the law in not issuing a warrant at once for Mr.

[013-092]Crawley's apprehension; and that they were so sinning at the instance

[013-093]of Mr. Walker,--at whose instance they would have committed almost

[013-094]any sin practicable by a board of English magistrates, so great was

[013-095]their faith in him; and she knew that she was bound to answer her

[013-096]engagement. She had also another task to perform--that, namely,

[013-097]of persuading him to employ an attorney for his defence; and she

[013-098]was prepared with the name of an attorney, one Mr. Mason, also of

[013-099]Silverbridge, who had been recommended to her by Mr. Walker. But when

[013-100]she came to the performance of these two tasks on the Monday morning,

[013-101]she found that she was unable to accomplish either of them. Mr.

[013-102]Crawley first declared that he would have nothing to do with any

[013-103]attorney. As to that he seemed to have made up his mind beforehand,

[013-104]and she saw at once that she had no hope of shaking him. But when she

[013-105]found that he was equally obstinate in the other matter, and that he

[013-106]declared that he would not go before the magistrates unless he were

[013-107]made to do so,--unless the policemen came and fetched him, then she

[013-108]almost sank beneath the burden of her troubles, and for a while was

[013-109]disposed to let things go as they would. How could she strive to bear

[013-110]a load that was so manifestly too heavy for her shoulders?

[013-111]On the Sunday the poor man had exerted himself to get through his

[013-112]Sunday duties, and he had succeeded. He had succeeded so well that

[013-113]his wife had thought that things might yet come right with him, that

[013-114]he would remember, before it was too late, the true history of that

[013-115]unhappy bit of paper, and that he was rising above that half madness

[013-116]which for months past had afflicted him. On the Sunday evening, when

[013-117]he was tired with his work, she thought it best to say nothing to him

[013-118]about the magistrates and the business of Thursday. But on the Monday

[013-119]morning she commenced her task, feeling that she owed it to Mr.

[013-120]Walker to lose no more time. He was very decided in his manners and

[013-121]made her understand that he would employ no lawyer on his own behalf.

[013-122]"Why should I want a lawyer? I have done nothing wrong," he said.

[013-123]Then she tried to make him understand that many who may have done

[013-124]nothing wrong require a lawyer's aid. "And who is to pay him?" he

[013-125]asked. To this she replied, unfortunately, that there would be no

[013-126]need of thinking of that at once. "And I am to get further into

[013-127]debt!" he said. "I am to put myself right before the world by

[013-128]incurring debts which I know I can never pay? When it has been a

[013-129]question of food for the children I have been weak, but I will not be

[013-130]weak in such a matter as this. I will have no lawyer." She did not

[013-131]regard this denial on his part as very material, though she would

[013-132]fain have followed Mr. Walker's advice had she been able; but when,

[013-133]later in the day, he declared that the police should fetch him, then

[013-134]her spirit gave way. Early in the morning he had seemed to assent to

[013-135]the expediency of going into Silverbridge on the Thursday, and it was

[013-136]not till after he had worked himself into a rage about the proposed

[013-137]attorney, that he utterly refused to make the journey. During the

[013-138]whole day, however, his state was such as almost to break his wife's

[013-139]heart. He would do nothing. He would not go to the school, nor even

[013-140]stir beyond the house-door. He would not open a book. He would not

[013-141]eat, nor would he even sit at table or say the accustomed grace

[013-142]when the scanty mid-day meal was placed upon the table. "Nothing is

[013-143]blessed to me," he said, when his wife pressed him to say the words

[013-144]for their child's sake. "Shall I say that I thank God when my heart

[013-145]is thankless? Shall I serve my child by a lie?" Then for hours he

[013-146]sat in the same position, in the old arm-chair, hanging over the

[013-147]fire speechless, sleepless, thinking ever, as she well knew, of the

[013-148]injustice of the world. She hardly dared to speak to him, so great

[013-149]was the bitterness of his words when he was goaded to reply. At last,

[013-150]late in the evening, feeling that it would be her duty to send in to

[013-151]Mr. Walker early on the following morning, she laid her hand gently

[013-152]on his shoulder and asked him for his promise. "I may tell Mr. Walker

[013-153]that you will be there on Thursday?"

[013-154]"No," he said, shouting at her. "No. I will have no such message

[013-155]sent." She started back, trembling. Not that she was accustomed to

[013-156]tremble at his ways, or to show that she feared him in his paroxysms,

[013-157]but that his voice had been louder than she had before known it. "I

[013-158]will hold no intercourse with them at Silverbridge in this matter. Do

[013-159]you hear me, Mary?"

[013-160]"I hear you, Josiah; but I must keep my word to Mr. Walker. I

[013-161]promised that I would send to him."

[013-162]"Tell him, then, that I will not stir a foot out of this house on

[013-163]Thursday, of my own accord. On Thursday I shall be here; and here I

[013-164]will remain all day,--unless they take me hence by force."

[013-165]"But, Josiah--"

[013-166]"Will you obey me, or I shall walk into Silverbridge myself and tell

[013-167]the man that I will not come to him." Then he arose from his chair

[013-168]and stretched forth his hand to his hat as though he were going forth

[013-169]immediately, on his way to Silverbridge. The night was now pitch

[013-170]dark, and the rain was falling, and abroad he would encounter all the

[013-171]severity of the pitiless winter. Still it might have been better that

[013-172]he should have gone. The exercise and the fresh air, even the wet and

[013-173]the mud, would have served to bring back his mind to reason. But his

[013-174]wife thought of the misery of the journey, of his scanty clothing, of

[013-175]his worn boots, of the need there was to preserve the raiment which

[013-176]he wore; and she remembered that he was fasting,--that he had eaten

[013-177]nothing since the morning, and that he was not fit to be alone. She

[013-178]stopped him, therefore, before he could reach the door.
