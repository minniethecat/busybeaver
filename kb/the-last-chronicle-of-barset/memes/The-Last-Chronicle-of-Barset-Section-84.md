[084-001]"Butterwell," said Crosbie, "I'm eternally obliged to you. It's

[084-002]hardly too much to say that you've saved me from ruin."

[084-003]"Of course I was joking about interest," said Butterwell. "Five

[084-004]per cent. is the proper thing. You'd better let me have a little

[084-005]acknowledgment. I'll give you the first half to-morrow."

[084-006]They were genuine tears which filled Crosbie's eyes, as he seized

[084-007]hold of the senior's hands. "Butterwell," he said, "what am I to say

[084-008]to you?"

[084-009]"Nothing at all,--nothing at all."

[084-010]"Your kindness makes me feel that I ought not to have come to you."

[084-011]"Oh, nonsense. By-the-by, would you mind telling Thompson to bring

[084-012]those papers to me which I gave him yesterday? I promised Optimist I

[084-013]would read them before three, and it's past two now." So saying he

[084-014]sat himself down at his table, and Crosbie felt that he was bound to

[084-015]leave the room.

[084-016]Mr. Butterwell, when he was left alone, did not read the papers which

[084-017]Thompson brought him; but sat, instead, thinking of his five hundred

[084-018]pounds. "Just put them down," he said to Thompson. So the papers were

[084-019]put down, and there they lay all that day and all the next. Then

[084-020]Thompson took them away again, and it is to be hoped that somebody

[084-021]read them. Five hundred pounds! It was a large sum of money, and

[084-022]Crosbie was a man for whom Mr. Butterwell in truth felt no very

[084-023]strong affection. "Of course he must have it now," he said to

[084-024]himself. "But where should I be if anything happened to him?" And

[084-025]then he remembered that Mrs. Butterwell especially disliked Mr.

[084-026]Crosbie,--disliked him because she knew that he snubbed her husband.

[084-027]"But it's hard to refuse, when one man has known another for more

[084-028]than ten years." Then he comforted himself somewhat with the

[084-029]reflection, that Crosbie would no doubt make himself more pleasant

[084-030]for the future than he had done lately, and with a second reflection,

[084-031]that Crosbie's life was a good life,--and with a third, as to his own

[084-032]great goodness, in assisting a brother officer. Nevertheless, as he

[084-033]sat looking out of the omnibus-window, on his journey home to Putney,

[084-034]he was not altogether comfortable in his mind. Mrs. Butterwell was a

[084-035]very prudent woman.

[084-036]But Crosbie was very comfortable in his mind on that afternoon. He

[084-037]had hardly dared to hope for success, but he had been successful. He

[084-038]had not even thought of Butterwell as a possible fountain of supply,

[084-039]till his mind had been brought back to the affairs of his office,

[084-040]by the voice of Sir Raffle Buffle at the corner of the street. The

[084-041]idea that his bill would be dishonoured, and that tidings of his

[084-042]insolvency would be conveyed to the Commissioners at his Board,

[084-043]had been dreadful to him. The way in which he had been treated by

[084-044]Musselboro and Dobbs Broughton had made him hate City men, and what

[084-045]he supposed to be City ways. Now there had come to him a relief which

[084-046]suddenly made everything feel light. He could almost think of Mr.

[084-047]Mortimer Gazebee without disgust. Perhaps after all there might be

[084-048]some happiness yet in store for him. Might it not be possible that

[084-049]Lily would yet accept him in spite of the chilling letter,--the

[084-050]freezing letter which he had received from Lily's mother? Of one

[084-051]thing he was quite certain. If ever he had an opportunity of pleading

[084-052]his own cause with her, he certainly would tell her everything

[084-053]respecting his own money difficulties.

[084-054]In that last resolve I think we may say that he was right. If Lily

[084-055]would ever listen to him again at all, she certainly would not be

[084-056]deterred from marrying him by his own story of his debts.

[084-057]CHAPTER XLV.

[084-058]LILY DALE GOES TO LONDON.

[084-059]One morning towards the end of March the squire rapped at the window

[084-060]of the drawing-room of the Small House, in which Mrs. Dale and her

[084-061]daughter were sitting. He had a letter in his hand, and both Lily and

[084-062]her mother knew that he had come down to speak about the contents of

[084-063]the letter. It was always a sign of good-humour on the squire's part,

[084-064]this rapping at the window. When it became necessary to him in his

[084-065]gloomy moods to see his sister-in-law, he would write a note to her,

[084-066]and she would go across to him at the Great House. At other times,

[084-067]if, as Lily would say, he was just then neither sweet nor bitter, he

[084-068]would go round to the front door and knock, and be admitted after the

[084-069]manner of ordinary people; but when he was minded to make himself

[084-070]thoroughly pleasant he would come and rap at the drawing-room window,

[084-071]as he was doing now.

[084-072]"I'll let you in, uncle; wait a moment," said Lily, as she unbolted

[084-073]the window which opened out upon the lawn. "It's dreadfully cold, so

[084-074]come in as fast as you can."

[084-075]"It's not cold at all," said the squire. "It's more like spring than

[084-076]any morning we've had yet. I've been sitting without a fire."

[084-077]"You won't catch us without one for the next two months; will he,

[084-078]mamma? You have got a letter, uncle. Is it for us to see?"

[084-079]"Well,--yes; I've brought it down to show you. Mary, what do you

[084-080]think is going to happen?"

[084-081]A terrible idea occurred to Mrs. Dale at that moment, but she was

[084-082]much too wise to give it expression. Could it be possible that the

[084-083]squire was going to make a fool of himself and get married? "I am

[084-084]very bad at guessing," said Mrs. Dale. "You had better tell us."

[084-085]"Bernard is going to be married," said Lily.

[084-086]"How did you know?" said the squire.

[084-087]"I didn't know. I only guessed."

[084-088]"Then you've guessed right," said the squire, a little annoyed at

[084-089]having his news thus taken out of his mouth.

[084-090]"I am so glad," said Mrs. Dale; "and I know from your manner that you

[084-091]like the match."

[084-092]"Well,--yes. I don't know the young lady, but I think that upon the

[084-093]whole I do like it. It's quite time, you know, that he got married."

[084-094]"He's not thirty yet," said Mrs. Dale.

[084-095]"He will be, in a month or two."

[084-096]"And who is it, uncle?"

[084-097]"Well;--as you're so good at guessing, I suppose you can guess that?"

[084-098]"It's not that Miss Partridge he used to talk about?"

[084-099]"No; it's not Miss Partridge,--I'm glad to say. I don't believe that

[084-100]the Partridges have a shilling among them."

[084-101]"Then I suppose it's an heiress?" said Mrs. Dale.

[084-102]"No; not an heiress; but she will have some money of her own. And she

[084-103]has connexions in Barsetshire, which makes it pleasant."

[084-104]"Connexions in Barsetshire! Who can it be?" said Lily.

[084-105]"Her name is Emily Dunstable," said the squire, "and she is the

[084-106]niece of that Miss Dunstable who married Dr. Thorne and who lives at

[084-107]Chaldicotes."

[084-108]"She was the woman who had millions upon millions," said Lily, "all

[084-109]got by selling ointment."

[084-110]"Never mind how it was got," said the squire, angrily. "Miss

[084-111]Dunstable married most respectably, and has always made a most

[084-112]excellent use of her money."

[084-113]"And will Bernard's wife have all her fortune?" asked Lily.

[084-114]"She will have twenty thousand pounds the day she marries, and I

[084-115]suppose that will be all."

[084-116]"And quite enough, too," said Mrs. Dale.

[084-117]"It seems that old Dr. Dunstable, as he was called, who, as Lily

[084-118]says, sold the ointment, quarrelled with his son or with his son's

[084-119]widow, and left nothing either to her or her child. The mother is

[084-120]dead, and the aunt, Dr. Thorne's wife, has always provided for the

[084-121]child. That's how it is, and Bernard is going to marry her. They are

[084-122]to be married at Chaldicotes in May."

[084-123]"I am delighted to hear it," said Mrs. Dale.

[084-124]"I've known Dr. Thorne for the last forty years;" and the squire now

[084-125]spoke in a low melancholy tone. "I've written to him to say that the

[084-126]young people shall have the old place up there to themselves if they

[084-127]like it."

[084-128]"What! and turn you out?" said Mrs. Dale.

[084-129]"That would not matter," said the squire.

[084-130]"You'd have to come and live with us," said Lily, taking him by the

[084-131]hand.

[084-132]"It doesn't matter much now where I live," said the squire.

[084-133]"Bernard will never consent to that," said Mrs. Dale.

[084-134]"I wonder whether she'll ask me to be a bridesmaid?" said Lily. "They

[084-135]say that Chaldicotes is such a pretty place, and I should see all

[084-136]the Barsetshire people that I've been hearing about from Grace. Poor

[084-137]Grace! I know that the Grantlys and the Thornes are very intimate.

[084-138]Fancy Bernard having twenty thousand pounds from the making of

[084-139]ointment!"

[084-140]"What does it matter to you where it comes from?" said the squire,

[084-141]half in anger.

[084-142]"Not in the least; only it sounds so odd. I do hope she's a nice

[084-143]girl."

[084-144]Then the squire produced a photograph of Emily Dunstable which his

[084-145]nephew had sent to him, and they all pronounced her to be very

[084-146]pretty, to be very much like a lady, and to be very good-humoured.

[084-147]The squire was evidently pleased with the match, and therefore the

[084-148]ladies were pleased also. Bernard Dale was the heir to the estate,

[084-149]and his marriage was of course a matter of moment; and as on such

[084-150]properties as that of Allington money is always wanted, the squire

[084-151]may be forgiven for the great importance which he attached to the

[084-152]young lady's fortune. "Bernard could hardly have married prudently

[084-153]without any money," he said,--"unless he had chosen to wait till I

[084-154]am gone."

[Illustration: They pronounced her to be very much like a Lady.]

[084-155]"And then he would have been too old to marry at all," said Lily.

[084-156]But the squire's budget of news had not yet been emptied. He told

[084-157]them soon afterwards that he himself had been summoned up to London.

[084-158]Bernard had written to him, begging him to come and see the young

[084-159]lady; and the family lawyer had written also, saying that his

[084-160]presence in town would be very desirable. "It is very troublesome,

[084-161]of course; but I shall go," said the squire. "It will do you all the

[084-162]good in the world," said Mrs. Dale; "and of course you ought to know

[084-163]her personally before the marriage." And then the squire made a clean

[084-164]breast of it and declared his full purpose. "I was thinking that,

[084-165]perhaps, Lily would not object to go up to London with me."

[084-166]"Oh, uncle Christopher, I should so like it," said Lily.

[084-167]"If your mamma does not object."

[084-168]"Mamma never objects to anything. I should like to see her objecting

[084-169]to that!" And Lily shook her head at her mother.

[084-170]"Bernard says that Miss Dunstable particularly wants to see you."

[084-171]"Does she, indeed? And I particularly want to see Miss Dunstable.

[084-172]How nice! Mamma, I don't think I've ever been in London since I wore

[084-173]short frocks. Do you remember taking us to the pantomime? Only think

[084-174]how many years ago that is. I'm quite sure it's time that Bernard

[084-175]should get married. Uncle, I hope you're prepared to take me to the

[084-176]play."

[084-177]"We must see about that!"

[084-178]"And the opera, and Madame Tussaud, and the Horticultural Gardens,

[084-179]and the new conjuror who makes a woman lie upon nothing. The idea

[084-180]of my going to London! And then I suppose I shall be one of the

[084-181]bridesmaids. I declare a new vista of life is opening out to me!

[084-182]Mamma, you mustn't be dull while I'm away. It won't be very long,

[084-183]I suppose, uncle?"

[084-184]"[[About]] a month, probably," said the squire.

[084-185]"Oh, mamma; what will you do?"

[084-186]"Never mind me, Lily."

[084-187]"You must get Bell and the children to come. But I cannot imagine

[084-188]living away from home a month. I was never away from home a month in

[084-189]my life."
