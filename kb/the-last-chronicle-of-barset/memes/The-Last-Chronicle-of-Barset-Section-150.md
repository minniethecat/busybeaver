[150-001]But during that one day she kept her promise to John Eames by going

[150-002]to Lily Dale. Mrs. Arabin had become very fond of Johnny, and felt

[150-003]that he deserved the prize which he had been so long trying to win.

[150-004]The reader, perhaps, may not agree with Mrs. Arabin. The reader, who

[150-005]may have caught a closer insight into Johnny's character than Mrs.

[150-006]Arabin had obtained, may, perhaps, think that a young man who could

[150-007]amuse himself with Miss Demolines was unworthy of Lily Dale. If so, I

[150-008]may declare for myself that I and the reader are not in accord about

[150-009]John Eames. It is hard to measure worth and worthlessness in such

[150-010]matters, as there is no standard for such measurement. My old friend

[150-011]John was certainly no hero,--was very unheroic in many phases of his

[150-012]life; but then, if all the girls are to wait for heroes, I fear that

[150-013]the difficulties in the way of matrimonial arrangements, great as

[150-014]they are at present, will be very seriously enhanced. Johnny was not

[150-015]ecstatic, nor heroic, nor transcendental, nor very beautiful in his

[150-016]manliness; he was not a man to break his heart for love, or to have

[150-017]his story written in an epic; but he was an affectionate, kindly,

[150-018]honest young man; and I think most girls might have done worse than

[150-019]take him. Whether he was wise to ask assistance in his love-making so

[150-020]often as he had done, that may be another question.

[150-021]Mrs. Arabin was intimately acquainted with Mrs. Thorne, and therefore

[150-022]there was nothing odd in her going to Mrs. Thorne's house. Mrs.

[150-023]Thorne was very glad to see her, and told her all the Barsetshire

[150-024]news,--much more than Mrs. Arabin would have learned in a week at the

[150-025]deanery; for Mrs. Thorne had a marvellous gift of picking up news.

[150-026]She had already heard the whole story of Mr. Soames's cheque, and

[150-027]expressed her conviction that the least that could be done in amends

[150-028]to Mr. Crawley was to make him a bishop. "And you see the palace is

[150-029]vacant," said Mrs. Thorne.

[150-030]"The palace vacant!" said Mrs. Arabin.

[150-031]"It is just as good. Now that Mrs. Proudie has gone I don't suppose

[150-032]the poor bishop will count for much. I can assure you, Mrs. Arabin, I

[150-033]felt that poor woman's death so much! She used to regard me as one

[150-034]of the staunchest of the Proudieites! She once whispered to me such

[150-035]a delightfully wicked story about the dean and the archdeacon. When

[150-036]I told her that they were my particular friends, she put on a look

[150-037]of horror. But I don't think she believed me." Then Emily Dunstable

[150-038]entered the room, and with her came Lily Dale. Mrs. Arabin had never

[150-039]before seen Lily, and of course they were introduced. "I am sorry to

[150-040]say Miss Dale is going home to Allington to-morrow," said Emily. "But

[150-041]she is coming to Chaldicotes in May," said Mrs. Thorne. "Of course,

[150-042]Mrs. Arabin, you know what gala doings we are going to have in May?"

[150-043]Then there were various civil little speeches made on each side, and

[150-044]Mrs. Arabin expressed a wish that she might meet Miss Dale again in

[150-045]Barsetshire. But all this did not bring her at all nearer to her

[150-046]object.

[150-047]"I particularly wish to say a word to Miss Dale,--here to-day, if she

[150-048]will allow me," said Mrs. Arabin.

[150-049]"I'm sure she will,--twenty words; won't you, Lily?" said Mrs.

[150-050]Thorne, preparing to leave the room. Then Mrs. Arabin apologized,

[150-051]and Mrs. Thorne, bustling up, said that it did not signify, and

[150-052]Lily, remaining quite still on the sofa, wondered what it was all

[150-053]about,--and in two minutes Lily and Mrs. Arabin were alone together.

[150-054]Lily had just time to surmise that Mrs. Arabin's visit must have some

[150-055]reference to Mr. Crosbie,--remembering that Crosbie had married his

[150-056]wife out of Barsetshire, and forgetting altogether that Mrs. Arabin

[150-057]had been just brought home from Italy by John Eames.

[150-058]"I am afraid, Miss Dale, you will think me very impertinent," said

[150-059]Mrs. Arabin.

[150-060]"I am sure I shall not think that," said Lily.

[150-061]"I believe you knew, before Mr. Eames started, that he was going to

[150-062]Italy to find me and my husband?" said Mrs. Arabin. Then Lily put Mr.

[150-063]Crosbie altogether out of her head, and became aware that he was not

[150-064]to be the subject of the coming conversation. She was almost sorry

[150-065]that it was so. There was no doubt in her mind as to what she would

[150-066]have said to any one who might have taken up Crosbie's cause. On

[150-067]that matter she could now have given a very decisive answer in a

[150-068]few words. But on that other matter she was much more in doubt. She

[150-069]remembered, however, every word of the note she had received from M.

[150-070]D. She remembered also the words of John's note to that young woman.

[150-071]And her heart was still hard against him. "Yes," she said; "Mr. Eames

[150-072]came here one night and told us why he was going. I was very glad

[150-073]that he was going, because I thought it was right."

[150-074]"You know, of course, how successful he has been? It was I who gave

[150-075]the cheque to Mr. Crawley."

[150-076]"So Mrs. Thorne has heard. Dr. Thorne has written to tell her the

[150-077]whole story."

[150-078]"And now I've come to look for Mr. Eames's reward."

[150-079]"His reward, Mrs. Arabin?"

[150-080]"Yes; or rather to plead for him. You will not, I hope, be angry

[150-081]with him because he has told me much of his history while we were

[150-082]travelling home alone together."

[150-083]"Oh, no," said Lily, smiling. "How could he have chosen a better

[150-084]friend in whom to trust?"

[150-085]"He could certainly have chosen none who would take his part more

[150-086]sincerely. He is so good and so amiable! He is so pleasant in his

[150-087]ways, and so fitted to make a woman happy! And then, Miss Dale, he is

[150-088]also so devoted!"

[150-089]"He is an old friend of ours, Mrs. Arabin."

[150-090]"So he has told me."

[150-091]"And we all of us love him dearly. Mamma is very much attached to

[150-092]him."

[150-093]"Unless he flatters himself, there is no one belonging to you who

[150-094]would not wish that he should be nearer and dearer still."

[150-095]"It may be so. I do not say that it is not so. Mamma and my uncle are

[150-096]both fond of him."

[150-097]"And does not that go a long way?" said Mrs. Arabin.

[150-098]"It ought not to do so," said Lily. "It ought not to go any way at

[150-099]all."

[150-100]"Ought it not? It seems to me that I could never have brought myself

[150-101]to marry any one whom my old friends had not liked."

[150-102]"Ah! that is another thing."

[150-103]"But is it not a recommendation to a man that he has been so

[150-104]successful with your friends as to make them all feel that you might

[150-105]trust yourself to him with perfect safety?" To this Lily made no

[150-106]answer, and Mrs. Arabin went on to plead her friend's cause with all

[150-107]the eloquence she could use, insisting on all his virtues, his good

[150-108]temper, his kindness, his constancy,--and not forgetting the fact

[150-109]that the world was inclined to use him very well. Still Lily made no

[150-110]answer. She had promised Mrs. Arabin that she would not regard her

[150-111]interference as impertinent, and therefore she refrained from any

[150-112]word that might seem to show offence. Nor did she feel offence. It

[150-113]was something gained by John Eames in Lily's estimation that he

[150-114]should have such a friend as Mrs. Arabin to take an interest in his

[150-115]welfare. But there was a self-dependence, perhaps one may call it an

[150-116]obstinacy about Lily Dale, which made her determined that she would

[150-117]not be driven hither or thither by any pressure from without. Why

[150-118]had John Eames, at the very moment when he should have been doing

[150-119]his best to drive from her breast the memory of past follies,--when

[150-120]he would have striven to do so had he really been earnest in his

[150-121]suit,--why at such a moment had he allowed himself to correspond in

[150-122]terms of affection with such a woman as this M. D.? While Mrs. Arabin

[150-123]was pleading for John Eames, Lily was repeating to herself certain

[150-124]words which John had written to the woman--"Ever and always yours

[150-125]unalterably." Such were not the exact words, but such was the form in

[150-126]which Lily, dishonestly, chose to repeat them to herself. And why was

[150-127]it so with her? In the old days she would have forgiven Crosbie any

[150-128]offence at a word or a look,--any possible letter to any M. D., let

[150-129]her have been ever so abominable! Nay,--had she not even forgiven him

[150-130]the offence of deserting herself altogether on behalf of a woman as

[150-131]detestable as could be any M. D. of Johnny's choosing;--a woman whose

[150-132]only recommendation had been her title? And yet she would not forgive

[150-133]John Eames, though the evidence against him was of so flimsy a

[150-134]nature,--but rather strove to turn the flimsiness of that evidence

[150-135]into strength! Why was it so? Unheroic as he might be, John Eames was

[150-136]surely a better man and a bigger man than Adolphus Crosbie. It was

[150-137]simply this;--she had fallen in love with the one, and had never

[150-138]fallen in love with the other! She had fallen in love with the one

[150-139]man, though in her simple way she had made a struggle against such

[150-140]feeling; and she had not come to love the other man, though she had

[150-141]told herself that it would be well that she should do so if it were

[150-142]possible. Again and again she had half declared to herself that she

[150-143]would take him as her husband and leave the love to come afterwards;

[150-144]but when the moment came for doing so, she could not do it.

[150-145]"May I not say a word of comfort to him?" said Mrs. Arabin.

[150-146]"He will be very comfortable without any such word," said Lily,

[150-147]laughing.

[150-148]"But he is not comfortable; of that you may be very sure." "Yours

[150-149]ever and unalterably, J. E.," said Lily to herself. "You do not doubt

[150-150]his affection?" continued Mrs. Arabin.

[150-151]"I neither doubt it nor credit it."

[150-152]"Then I think you wrong him. And the reason why I have ventured to

[150-153]come to you is that you may know the impression which he has made

[150-154]upon one who was but the other day a stranger to him. I am sure that

[150-155]he loves you."

[150-156]"I think he is light of heart."

[150-157]"Oh, no, Miss Dale."

[150-158]"And how am I to become his wife unless I love him well enough

[150-159]myself? Mrs. Arabin, I have made up my mind about it. I shall never

[150-160]become any man's wife. Mamma and I are all in all together, and

[150-161]we shall remain together." As soon as these words were out of her

[150-162]mouth, she hated herself for having spoken them. There was a maudlin,

[150-163]missish, namby-mamby sentimentality about them which disgusted her.

[150-164]She specially desired to be straightforward, resolute of purpose,

[150-165]honest-spoken, and free from all touch of affectation. And yet she

[150-166]had excused herself from marrying John Eames after the fashion of a

[150-167]sick schoolgirl. "It is no good talking about it any more," she said,

[150-168]getting up from her chair quickly.

[150-169]"You are not angry with me;--or at any rate you will forgive me?"

[150-170]"I'm quite sure you have meant to be very good, and I am not a bit

[150-171]angry."

[150-172]"And you will see him before you go?"

[150-173]"Oh, yes; that is if he likes to come to-day, or early to-morrow.

[150-174]I go home to-morrow. I cannot refuse him, because he is such an old

[150-175]friend,--almost like a brother. But it is of no use, Mrs. Arabin."

[150-176]Then Mrs. Arabin kissed her and left her, telling her that Mr. Eames

[150-177]would come to her that afternoon at half-past five. Lily promised

[150-178]that she would be at home to receive him.
