[062-001]"She'll take it very kindly, your reverence. But while you are here,

[062-002]sir, there's just a word I'd like to say. I didn't happen to catch

[062-003]Mr. Henry when he was here the other day."

[062-004]"Never mind Mr. Henry; what is it you have to say?"

[Illustration: "Never mind Mr. Henry."]

[062-005]"I do think, I do indeed, sir, that Mr. Thorne's man ain't dealing

[062-006]fairly along of the foxes. I wouldn't say a word about it, only that

[062-007]Mr. Henry is so particular."

[062-008]"What about the foxes? What is he doing with the foxes?"

[062-009]"Well, sir, he's a trapping on 'em. He is, indeed, your reverence. I

[062-010]wouldn't speak if I warn't well nigh mortial sure."

[062-011]Now the archdeacon had never been a hunting man, though in his early

[062-012]days many a clergyman had been in the habit of hunting without

[062-013]losing his clerical character by doing so; but he had lived all

[062-014]his life among gentlemen in a hunting county, and had his own very

[062-015]strong ideas about the trapping of foxes. Foxes first, and pheasants

[062-016]afterwards, had always been the rule with him as to any land of which

[062-017]he himself had had the management. And no man understood better than

[062-018]he did how to deal with keepers as to this matter of fox-preserving,

[062-019]or knew better that keepers will in truth obey not the words of their

[062-020]employers, but their sympathies. "Wish them to have foxes, and pay

[062-021]them, and they will have them," Mr. Sowerby of Chaldicotes used to

[062-022]say, and he in his day was reckoned to be the best preserver of foxes

[062-023]in Barsetshire. "Tell them to have them, and don't wish it, and pay

[062-024]them well, and you won't have a fox to interfere with your game.

[062-025]I don't care what a man says to me, I can read it all like a book

[062-026]when I see his covers drawn." That was what poor Mr. Sowerby of

[062-027]Chaldicotes used to say, and the archdeacon had heard him say it a

[062-028]score of times, and had learned the lesson. But now his heart was not

[062-029]with the foxes,--and especially not with the foxes on behalf of his

[062-030]son Henry. "I can't have any meddling with Mr. Thorne," he said; "I

[062-031]can't, and I won't."

[062-032]"But I don't suppose it can be Mr. Thorne's order, your reverence;

[062-033]and Mr. Henry is so particular."

[062-034]"Of course it isn't Mr. Thorne's order. Mr. Thorne has been a hunting

[062-035]man all his life."

[062-036]"But he have guv' up now, your reverence. He ain't a hunted these two

[062-037]years."

[062-038]"I'm sure he wouldn't have the foxes trapped."

[062-039]"Not if he knowed it, he wouldn't, your reverence. A gentleman of

[062-040]the likes of him, who's been a hunting over fifty year, wouldn't do

[062-041]the likes of that; but the foxes is trapped, and Mr. Henry 'll be a

[062-042]putting it on me if I don't speak out. They is Plumstead foxes, too;

[062-043]and a vixen was trapped just across the field yonder, in Goshall

[062-044]Springs, no later than yesterday morning." Flurry was now thoroughly

[062-045]in earnest; and, indeed, the trapping of a vixen in February is a

[062-046]serious thing.

[062-047]"Goshall Springs don't belong to me," said the archdeacon.

[062-048]"No, your reverence; they're on the Ullathorne property. But a word

[062-049]from your reverence would do it. Mr. Henry thinks more of the foxes

[062-050]than anything. The last word he told me was that it would break his

[062-051]heart if he saw the coppices drawn blank."

[062-052]"Then he must break his heart." The words were pronounced, but the

[062-053]archdeacon had so much command over himself as to speak them in such

[062-054]a voice that the man should not hear them. But it was incumbent

[062-055]on him to say something that the man should hear. "I will have no

[062-056]meddling in the matter, Flurry. Whether there are foxes or whether

[062-057]there are not, is matter of no great moment. I will not have a word

[062-058]said to annoy Mr. Thorne." Then he rode away, back through the wood

[062-059]and out on to the road, and the horse walked with him leisurely on,

[062-060]whither the archdeacon hardly knew,--for he was thinking, thinking,

[062-061]thinking. "Well;--if that ain't the darn'dest thing that ever was,"

[062-062]said Flurry; "but I'll tell the squire about Thorne's man,--darned if

[062-063]I don't." Now "the squire" was young Squire Gresham, the master of

[062-064]the East Barsetshire hounds.

[062-065]But the archdeacon went on thinking, thinking, thinking. He could

[062-066]have heard nothing of his son to stir him more in his favour than

[062-067]this strong evidence of his partiality for foxes. I do not mean it

[062-068]to be understood that the archdeacon regarded foxes as better than

[062-069]active charity, or a contented mind, or a meek spirit, or than

[062-070]self-denying temperance. No doubt all these virtues did hold in his

[062-071]mind their proper places, altogether beyond contamination of foxes.

[062-072]But he had prided himself on thinking that his son should be a

[062-073]country gentleman, and, probably nothing doubting as to the major's

[062-074]active charity and other virtues, was delighted to receive evidence

[062-075]of those tastes which he had ever wished to encourage in his son's

[062-076]character. Or rather, such evidence would have delighted him at any

[062-077]other time than the present. Now it only added more gall to his cup.

[062-078]"Why should he teach himself to care for such things, when he has not

[062-079]the spirit to enjoy them," said the archdeacon to himself. "He is a

[062-080]fool,--a fool. A man that has been married once, to go crazy after a

[062-081]little girl, that has hardly a dress to her back, and who never was

[062-082]in a drawing-room in her life! Charles is the eldest, and he shall be

[062-083]the eldest. It will be better to keep it together. It is the way in

[062-084]which the country has become what it is." He was out nearly all day,

[062-085]and did not see his wife till dinner-time. Her father, Mr. Harding,

[062-086]was still with them, but had breakfasted in his own room. Not a word,

[062-087]therefore, was said about Henry Grantly between the father and mother

[062-088]on that evening.

[062-089]Mrs. Grantly was determined that, unless provoked, she would say

[062-090]nothing to him till the following morning. He should sleep upon his

[062-091]wrath before she spoke to him again. And he was equally unwilling to

[062-092]recur to the subject. Had she permitted it, the next morning would

[062-093]have passed away, and no word would have been spoken. But this

[062-094]would not have suited her. She had his orders to write, and she had

[062-095]undertaken to obey these orders,--with the delay of one day. Were

[062-096]she not to write at all,--or in writing to send no message from the

[062-097]father, there would be cause for further anger. And yet this, I

[062-098]think, was what the archdeacon wished.

[062-099]"Archdeacon," she said, "I shall write to Henry to-day."

[062-100]"Very well."

[062-101]"And what am I to say from you?"

[062-102]"I told you yesterday what are my intentions."

[062-103]"I am not asking about that now. We hope there will be years and

[062-104]years to come, in which you may change them, and shape them as you

[062-105]will. What shall I tell him now from you?"

[062-106]"I have nothing to say to him,--nothing; not a word. He knows what

[062-107]he has to expect from me, for I have told him. He is acting with his

[062-108]eyes open, and so am I. If he marries Miss Crawley, he must live on

[062-109]his own means. I told him that myself so plainly, that he can want

[062-110]no further intimation." Then Mrs. Grantly knew that she was absolved

[062-111]from the burden of yesterday's message, and she plumed herself on the

[062-112]prudence of her conduct. On the same morning the archdeacon wrote the

[062-113]following note:--

[062-114]DEAR THORNE,--

[062-115]My man tells me that foxes have been trapped on Darvell's

[062-116]farm, just outside the coppices. I know nothing of it

[062-117]myself, but I am sure you'll look to it.

[062-118]Yours always,

[062-119]T. GRANTLY.

[062-120]CHAPTER XXXIV.

[062-121]MRS. PROUDIE SENDS FOR HER LAWYER.

[062-122][Illustration]

[062-123]There was great dismay in Barchester Palace after the visit paid

[062-124]to the bishop and Mrs. Proudie by that terrible clerical offender,

[062-125]Mr. Crawley. It will be remembered, perhaps, how he had defied the

[062-126]bishop with spoken words, and how he had defied the bishop's wife by

[062-127]speaking no words to her. For the moment, no doubt, Mr. Crawley had

[062-128]the best of it. Mrs. Proudie acknowledged to herself that this was

[062-129]the case; but as she was a woman who had never yet succumbed to an

[062-130]enemy, who had never,--if on such an occasion I may be allowed to use

[062-131]a schoolboy's slang,--taken a licking from any one, it was not likely

[062-132]that Mr. Crawley would be long allowed to enjoy his triumph in peace.

[062-133]It would be odd if all the weight of the palace would not be able to

[062-134]silence a wretch of a perpetual curate who had already been committed

[062-135]to take his trial for thieving;--and Mrs. Proudie was determined

[062-136]that all the weight of the palace should be used. As for the bishop,

[062-137]though he was not as angry as his wife, he was quite as unhappy, and

[062-138]therefore quite as hostile to Mr. Crawley; and was fully conscious

[062-139]that there could be no peace for him now until Mr. Crawley should

[062-140]be crushed. If only the assizes would come at once, and get him

[062-141]condemned out of the way, what a blessed thing it would be! But

[062-142]unluckily it still wanted three months to the assizes, and during

[062-143]those three months Mr. Crawley would be at large and subject only to

[062-144]episcopal authority. During that time he could not be silenced by the

[062-145]arm of the civil law. His wife was not long in expressing her opinion

[062-146]after Mr. Crawley had left the palace. "You must proceed against him

[062-147]in the Court of Arches,--and that at once," said Mrs. Proudie. "You

[062-148]can do that, of course? I know that it will be expensive. Of course

[062-149]it will be expensive. I suppose it may cost us some hundreds of

[062-150]pounds; but duty is duty, my lord, and in such a case as this your

[062-151]duty as a bishop is paramount."

[062-152]The poor bishop knew that it was useless to explain to her the

[062-153]various mistakes which she made,--which she was ever making,--as to

[062-154]the extent of his powers and the modes of procedure which were open

[062-155]to him. When he would do so she would only rail at him for being

[062-156]lukewarm in his office, poor in spirit, and afraid of dealing roundly

[062-157]with those below him. On the present occasion he did say a word, but

[062-158]she would not even hear him to the end. "Don't tell me about rural

[062-159]deans, as if I didn't know. The rural dean has nothing to do with

[062-160]such a case. The man has been committed for trial. Send for Mr.

[062-161]Chadwick at once, and let steps be taken before you are an hour

[062-162]older."

[062-163]"But, my dear, Mr. Chadwick can do nothing."

[062-164]"Then I will see Mr. Chadwick." And in her anger she did sit down and

[062-165]write a note to Mr. Chadwick, begging him to come over to her at the

[062-166]palace.

[062-167]Mr. Chadwick was a lawyer, living in Barchester, who earned his bread

[062-168]from ecclesiastical business. His father, and his uncle, and his

[062-169]grandfather and granduncles, had all been concerned in the affairs

[062-170]of the diocese of Barchester. His uncle had been bailiff to the

[062-171]episcopal estates, or steward as he had been called, in Bishop

[062-172]Grantly's time, and still contrived to draw his income in some shape

[062-173]from the property of the see. The nephew had also been the legal

[062-174]assistant of the bishop in his latter days, and had been continued in

[062-175]that position by Bishop Proudie, not from love, but from expediency.

[062-176]Mr. John Chadwick was one of those gentlemen, two or three of

[062-177]whom are to be seen in connection with every see,--who seem to be

[062-178]hybrids--half-lay, half-cleric. They dress like clergymen, and affect

[062-179]that mixture of clerical solemnity and clerical waggishness which

[062-180]is generally to be found among minor canons and vicar chorals of a

[062-181]cathedral. They live, or at least have their offices, half in the

[062-182]Close and half out of it,--dwelling as it were just on the borders

[062-183]of holy orders. They always wear white neck-handkerchiefs and black

[062-184]gloves; and would be altogether clerical in their appearance, were

[062-185]it not that as regards the outward man they impinge somewhat on the

[062-186]characteristics of the undertaker. They savour of the church, but

[062-187]the savour is of the church's exterior. Any stranger thrown into

[062-188]chance contact with one of them would, from instinct, begin to talk

[062-189]of things ecclesiastical without any reference to things theological

[062-190]or things religious. They are always most worthy men, much respected

[062-191]in the society of the Close, and I never heard of one of them

[062-192]whose wife was not comfortable or whose children were left without

[062-193]provision.
