[002-001]"And how did it end?"

[002-002]"I asked her to go in to you, but she declined. She said you could do

[002-003]nothing for her."

[002-004]"And does she think her husband guilty?"

[002-005]"No, indeed. She think him guilty! Nothing on earth,--or from heaven

[002-006]either, as I take it, would make her suppose it to be possible. She

[002-007]came to me simply to tell me how good he was."

[002-008]"I love her for that," said Mrs. Walker.

[002-009]"So did I. But what is the good of loving her? Thank you, dearest.

[002-010]I'll get your slippers for you some day, perhaps."

[002-011]The whole county was astir in this matter of this alleged guilt of

[002-012]the Reverend Josiah Crawley,--the whole county, almost as keenly as

[002-013]the family of Mr. Walker, of Silverbridge. The crime laid to his

[002-014]charge was the theft of a cheque for twenty pounds, which he was said

[002-015]to have stolen out of a pocket-book left or dropped in his house, and

[002-016]to have passed as money into the hands of one Fletcher, a butcher

[002-017]of Silverbridge, to whom he was indebted. Mr. Crawley was in those

[002-018]days the perpetual curate of Hogglestock, a parish in the northern

[002-019]extremity of East Barsetshire; a man known by all who knew anything

[002-020]of him to be very poor,--an unhappy, moody, disappointed man, upon

[002-021]whom the troubles of the world always seemed to come with a double

[002-022]weight. But he had ever been respected as a clergyman, since his

[002-023]old friend Mr. Arabin, the dean of Barchester, had given him the

[002-024]small incumbency which he now held. Though moody, unhappy, and

[002-025]disappointed, he was a hard-working, conscientious pastor among

[002-026]the poor people with whom his lot was cast; for in the parish of

[002-027]Hogglestock there resided only a few farmers higher in degree than

[002-028]field labourers, brickmakers, and such like. Mr. Crawley had now

[002-029]passed some ten years of his life at Hogglestock; and during those

[002-030]years he had worked very hard to do his duty, struggling to teach the

[002-031]people around him perhaps too much of the mystery, but something also

[002-032]of the comfort, of religion. That he had become popular in his parish

[002-033]cannot be said of him. He was not a man to make himself popular in

[002-034]any position. I have said that he was moody and disappointed. He was

[002-035]even worse than this; he was morose, sometimes almost to insanity.

[002-036]There had been days in which even his wife had found it impossible

[002-037]to deal with him otherwise than as with an acknowledged lunatic. And

[002-038]this was known among the farmers, who talked about their clergyman

[002-039]among themselves as though he were a madman. But among the very poor,

[002-040]among the brickmakers of Hoggle End,--a lawless, drunken, terribly

[002-041]rough lot of humanity,--he was held in high respect; for they knew

[002-042]that he lived hardly, as they lived; that he worked hard, as they

[002-043]worked; and that the outside world was hard to him, as it was to

[002-044]them; and there had been an apparent sincerity of godliness about the

[002-045]man, and a manifest struggle to do his duty in spite of the world's

[002-046]ill-usage, which had won its way even with the rough; so that Mr.

[002-047]Crawley's name had stood high with many in his parish, in spite of

[002-048]the unfortunate peculiarity of his disposition. This was the man who

[002-049]was now accused of stealing a cheque for twenty pounds.

[002-050]But before the circumstances of the alleged theft are stated, a word

[002-051]or two must be said as to Mr. Crawley's family. It is declared that a

[002-052]good wife is a crown to her husband, but Mrs. Crawley had been much

[002-053]more than a crown to him. As had regarded all the inner life of the

[002-054]man,--all that portion of his life which had not been passed in the

[002-055]pulpit or in pastoral teaching,--she had been crown, throne, and

[002-056]sceptre all in one. That she had endured with him and on his behalf

[002-057]the miseries of poverty, and the troubles of a life which had known

[002-058]no smiles, is perhaps not to be alleged as much to her honour.

[002-059]She had joined herself to him for better or worse, and it was her

[002-060]manifest duty to bear such things; wives always have to bear them,

[002-061]knowing when they marry that they must take their chance. Mr. Crawley

[002-062]might have been a bishop, and Mrs. Crawley, when she married him,

[002-063]perhaps thought it probable that such would be his fortune. Instead

[002-064]of that he was now, just as he was approaching his fiftieth year, a

[002-065]perpetual curate, with an income of one hundred and thirty pounds

[002-066]per annum,--and a family. That had been Mrs. Crawley's luck in life,

[002-067]and of course she bore it. But she had also done much more than

[002-068]this. She had striven hard to be contented, or, rather, to appear

[002-069]to be contented, when he had been most wretched and most moody.

[002-070]She had struggled to conceal from him her own conviction as to his

[002-071]half-insanity, treating him at the same time with the respect due

[002-072]to an honoured father of a family, and with the careful measured

[002-073]indulgence fit for a sick and wayward child. In all the terrible

[002-074]troubles of their life her courage had been higher than his. The

[002-075]metal of which she was made had been tempered to a steel which was

[002-076]very rare and fine, but the rareness and fineness of which he had

[002-077]failed to appreciate. He had often told her that she was without

[002-078]pride, because she had stooped to receive from others, on his behalf

[002-079]and on behalf of her children, things which were very needful, but

[002-080]which she could not buy. He had told her that she was a beggar, and

[002-081]that it was better to starve than to beg. She had borne the rebuke

[002-082]without a word in reply, and had then begged again for him, and had

[002-083]endured the starvation herself. Nothing in their poverty had, for

[002-084]years past, been a shame to her; but every accident of their poverty

[002-085]was still, and ever had been, a living disgrace to him.

[Illustration: Mr. and Mrs. Crawley.]

[002-086]They had had many children, and three were still alive. Of the

[002-087]eldest, Grace Crawley, we shall hear much in the coming story. She

[002-088]was at this time nineteen years old, and there were those who said

[002-089]that, in spite of her poverty, her shabby outward apparel, and a

[002-090]certain thin, unfledged, unrounded form of person, a want of fulness

[002-091]in the lines of her figure, she was the prettiest girl in that part

[002-092]of the world. She was living now at a school in Silverbridge, where

[002-093]for the last year she had been a teacher; and there were many in

[002-094]Silverbridge who declared that very bright prospects were opening to

[002-095]her,--that young Major Grantly of Cosby Lodge, who, though a widower

[002-096]with a young child, was the cynosure of all female eyes in and

[002-097]round Silverbridge, had found beauty in her thin face, and that

[002-098]Grace Crawley's fortune was made in the teeth, as it were, of the

[002-099]prevailing ill-fortune of her family. Bob Crawley, who was two years

[002-100]younger, was now at Marlbro' School, from whence it was intended that

[002-101]he should proceed to Cambridge, and be educated there at the expense

[002-102]of his godfather, Dean Arabin. In this also the world saw a stroke

[002-103]of good luck. But then nothing was lucky to Mr. Crawley. Bob, indeed,

[002-104]who had done very well at school, might do well at Cambridge,--might

[002-105]do great things there. But Mr. Crawley would almost have preferred

[002-106]that the boy should work in the fields, than that he should be

[002-107]educated in a manner so manifestly eleemosynary. And then his

[002-108]clothes! How was he to be provided with clothes fit either for school

[002-109]or for college? But the dean and Mrs. Crawley between them managed

[002-110]this, leaving Mr. Crawley very much in the dark, as Mrs. Crawley was

[002-111]in the habit of leaving him. Then there was a younger daughter, Jane,

[002-112]still at home, who passed her life between her mother's work-table

[002-113]and her father's Greek, mending linen and learning to scan

[002-114]iambics,--for Mr. Crawley in his early days had been a ripe scholar.

[002-115]And now there had come upon them all this terribly-crushing disaster.

[002-116]That poor Mr. Crawley had gradually got himself into a mess of debt

[002-117]at Silverbridge, from which he was quite unable to extricate himself,

[002-118]was generally known by all the world both of Silverbridge and

[002-119]Hogglestock. To a great many it was known that Dean Arabin had

[002-120]paid money for him, very much contrary to his own consent, and

[002-121]that he had quarrelled, or attempted to quarrel, with the dean in

[002-122]consequence,--had so attempted, although the money had in part passed

[002-123]through his own hands. There had been one creditor, Fletcher, the

[002-124]butcher of Silverbridge, who had of late been specially hard upon

[002-125]poor Crawley. This man, who had not been without good nature in his

[002-126]dealings, had heard stories of the dean's good-will and such like,

[002-127]and had loudly expressed his opinion that the perpetual curate of

[002-128]Hogglestock would show a higher pride in allowing himself to be

[002-129]indebted to a rich brother clergyman, than in remaining under thrall

[002-130]to a butcher. And thus a rumour had grown up. And then the butcher

[002-131]had written repeated letters to the bishop,--to Bishop Proudie of

[002-132]Barchester, who had at first caused his chaplain to answer them,

[002-133]and had told Mr. Crawley somewhat roundly what was his opinion of a

[002-134]clergyman who eat meat and did not pay for it. But nothing that the

[002-135]bishop could say or do enabled Mr. Crawley to pay the butcher. It was

[002-136]very grievous to such a man as Mr. Crawley to receive these letters

[002-137]from such a man as Bishop Proudie; but the letters came, and made

[002-138]festering wounds, but then there was an end of them. And at last

[002-139]there had come forth from the butcher's shop a threat that if the

[002-140]money were not paid by a certain date, printed bills should be posted

[002-141]about the county. All who heard of this in Silverbridge were very

[002-142]angry with Mr. Fletcher, for no one there had ever known a tradesman

[002-143]to take such a step before; but Fletcher swore that he would

[002-144]persevere, and defended himself by showing that six or seven months

[002-145]since, in the spring of the year, Mr. Crawley had been paying money

[002-146]in Silverbridge, but had paid none to him,--to him who had been not

[002-147]only his earliest, but his most enduring creditor. "He got money

[002-148]from the dean in March," said Mr. Fletcher to Mr. Walker, "and he

[002-149]paid twelve pounds ten to Green, and seventeen pounds to Grobury,

[002-150]the baker." It was that seventeen pounds to Grobury, the baker, for

[002-151]flour, which made the butcher so fixedly determined to smite the

[002-152]poor clergyman hip and thigh. "And he paid money to Hall, and to Mrs.

[002-153]Holt, and to a deal more; but he never came near my shop. If he had

[002-154]even shown himself, I would not have said so much about it." And then

[002-155]a day before the date named, Mrs. Crawley had come to Silverbridge,

[002-156]and had paid the butcher twenty pounds in four five-pound notes. So

[002-157]far Fletcher the butcher had been successful.

[002-158]Some six weeks after this, inquiry began to be made as to a certain

[002-159]cheque for twenty pounds drawn by Lord Lufton on his bankers in

[002-160]London, which cheque had been lost early in the spring by Mr. Soames,

[002-161]Lord Lufton's man of business in Barsetshire, together with a

[002-162]pocket-book in which it had been folded. This pocket-book Soames had

[002-163]believed himself to have left at Mr. Crawley's house, and had gone

[002-164]so far, even at the time of the loss, as to express his absolute

[002-165]conviction that he had so left it. He was in the habit of paying a

[002-166]rentcharge to Mr. Crawley on behalf of Lord Lufton, amounting to

[002-167]twenty pounds four shillings, every half-year. Lord Lufton held

[002-168]the large tithes of Hogglestock, and paid annually a sum of forty

[002-169]pounds eight shillings to the incumbent. This amount was, as a rule,

[002-170]remitted punctually by Mr. Soames through the post. On the occasion

[002-171]now spoken of, he had had some reason for visiting Hogglestock, and

[002-172]had paid the money personally to Mr. Crawley. Of so much there was

[002-173]no doubt. But he had paid it by a cheque drawn by himself on his

[002-174]own bankers at Barchester, and that cheque had been cashed in the

[002-175]ordinary way on the next morning. On returning to his own house in

[002-176]Barchester he had missed his pocket-book, and had written to Mr.

[002-177]Crawley to make inquiry. There had been no money in it, beyond the

[002-178]cheque drawn by Lord Lufton for twenty pounds. Mr. Crawley had

[002-179]answered this letter by another, saying that no pocket-book had been

[002-180]found in his house. All this had happened in March.
