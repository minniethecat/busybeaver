[119-001]And as for John Eames,--in those bitter moments of her reflection she

[119-002]almost wished the same in regard to him. If he would only cease to

[119-003]be her lover, he might be very well; but he was not very well to her

[119-004]as long as his pretensions were dinned into her ear by everybody who

[119-005]knew her. And then she told herself that John would have had a better

[119-006]chance if he had been content to plead for himself. In this, I think,

[119-007]she was hard upon her lover. He had pleaded for himself as well as he

[119-008]knew how, and as often as the occasion had been given to him. It had

[119-009]hardly been his fault that his case had been taken in hand by other

[119-010]advocates. He had given no commission to Mrs. Thorne to plead for

[119-011]him.

[119-012]Poor Johnny. He had stood in much better favour before the lady had

[119-013]presented her compliments to Miss L. D. It was that odious letter,

[119-014]and the thoughts which it had forced upon Lily's mind, which were now

[119-015]most inimical to his interests. Whether Lily loved him or not, she

[119-016]did not love him well enough not to be jealous of him. Had any such

[119-017]letter reached her respecting Crosbie in the happy days of her young

[119-018]love, she would simply have laughed at it. It would have been nothing

[119-019]to her. But now she was sore and unhappy, and any trifle was powerful

[119-020]enough to irritate her. "Is Miss L. D. engaged to marry Mr. J. E.?"

[119-021]"No," said Lily, out loud. "Lily Dale is not engaged to marry John

[119-022]Eames, and never will be so engaged." She was almost tempted to sit

[119-023]down and write the required answer to Miss M. D. Though the letter

[119-024]had been destroyed, she well remembered the number of the post-office

[119-025]in the Edgware Road. Poor John Eames!

[119-026]That evening she told Emily Dunstable that she thought she would like

[119-027]to return to Allington before the day that had been appointed for

[119-028]her. "But why," said Emily, "should you be worse than your word?"

[119-029]"I daresay it will seem silly, but the fact is I am homesick. I'm not

[119-030]accustomed to be away from mamma for so long."

[119-031]"I hope it is not what occurred to-day at the picture-gallery."

[119-032]"I won't deny that it is that in part."

[119-033]"That was a strange accident, you know, that might never occur

[119-034]again."

[119-035]"It has occurred twice already, Emily."

[119-036]"I don't call the affair in the Park anything. Anybody may see

[119-037]anybody else in the Park, of course. He was not brought so near you

[119-038]that he could annoy you there. You ought certainly to wait till Mr.

[119-039]Eames has come back from Italy."

[119-040]Then Lily declared that she must and would go back to Allington on

[119-041]the next Monday, and she actually did write a letter to her mother

[119-042]that night to say that such was her intention. But on the morrow her

[119-043]heart was less sore, and the letter was not sent.

[119-044]CHAPTER LX.

[119-045]THE END OF JAEL AND SISERA.

[119-046][Illustration]

[119-047]There was to be one more sitting for the picture, as the reader will

[119-048]remember, and the day for that sitting had arrived. Conway Dalrymple

[119-049]had in the meantime called at Mrs. Van Siever's house, hoping that he

[119-050]might be able to see Clara, and make his offer to her there. But he

[119-051]had failed in his attempt to reach her. He had found it impossible

[119-052]to say all that he had to say in the painting-room, during the very

[119-053]short intervals which Mrs. Broughton left to him. A man should be

[119-054]allowed to be alone more than fifteen minutes with a young lady on

[119-055]the occasion in which he offers to her his hand and his heart; but

[119-056]hitherto he had never had more than fifteen minutes at his command;

[119-057]and then there had been the turban! He had also in the meantime

[119-058]called on Mrs. Broughton, with the intention of explaining to her

[119-059]that if she really intended to favour his views in respect to

[119-060]Miss Van Siever, she ought to give him a little more liberty for

[119-061]expressing himself. On this occasion he had seen his friend, but had

[119-062]not been able to go as minutely as he had wished into the matter that

[119-063]was so important to himself. Mrs. Broughton had found it necessary

[119-064]during this meeting to talk almost exclusively about herself and her

[119-065]own affairs. "Conway," she had said, directly she saw him, "I am so

[119-066]glad you have come. I think I should have gone mad if I had not seen

[119-067]some one who cares for me." This was early in the morning, not much

[119-068]after eleven, and Mrs. Broughton, hearing first his knock at the

[119-069]door, and then his voice, had met him in the hall and taken him into

[119-070]the dining-room.

[119-071]"Is anything the matter?" he asked.

[119-072]"Oh, Conway!"

[119-073]"What is it? Has anything gone wrong with Dobbs?"

[119-074]"Everything has gone wrong with him. He is ruined."

[119-075]"Heaven and earth! What do you mean?"

[119-076]"Simply what I say. But you must not speak a word of it. I do not

[119-077]know it from himself."

[119-078]"How do you know it?"

[119-079]"Wait a moment. Sit down there, will you?--and I will sit by you. No,

[119-080]Conway; do not take my hand. It is not right. There;--so. Yesterday

[119-081]Mrs. Van Siever was here. I need not tell you all that she said to

[119-082]me, even if I could. She was very harsh and cruel, saying all manner

[119-083]of things about Dobbs. How can I help it, if he drinks? I have not

[119-084]encouraged him. And as for expensive living, I have been as ignorant

[119-085]as a child. I have never asked for anything. When we were married

[119-086]somebody told me how much we should have to spend. It was either two

[119-087]thousand, or three thousand, or four thousand, or something like

[119-088]that. You know, Conway, how ignorant I am about money;--that I am

[119-089]like a child. Is it not true?" She waited for an answer and Dalrymple

[119-090]was obliged to acknowledge that it was true. And yet he had known

[119-091]the times in which his dear friend had been very sharp in her memory

[119-092]with reference to a few pounds. "And now she says that Dobbs owes her

[119-093]money which he cannot pay her, and that everything must be sold. She

[119-094]says that Musselboro must have the business, and that Dobbs must

[119-095]shift for himself elsewhere."

[119-096]"Do you believe that she has the power to decide that things shall go

[119-097]this way or that,--as she pleases?"

[119-098]"How am I to know? She says so, and she says it is because he drinks.

[119-099]He does drink. That at least is true; but how can I help it? Oh,

[119-100]Conway, what am I to do? Dobbs did not come home at all last night,

[119-101]but sent for his things,--saying that he must stay in the City. What

[119-102]am I to do if they come and take the house, and sell the furniture,

[119-103]and turn me out into the street?" Then the poor creature began to cry

[119-104]in earnest, and Dalrymple had to console her as best he might. "How

[119-105]I wish I had known you first," she said. To this Dalrymple was able

[119-106]to make no direct answer. He was wise enough to know that a direct

[119-107]answer might possibly lead him into terrible trouble. He was by no

[119-108]means anxious to find himself "protecting" Mrs. Dobbs Broughton from

[119-109]the ruin which her husband had brought upon her.

[119-110]Before he left her she had told him a long story, partly of matters

[119-111]of which he had known something before, and partly made up of

[119-112]that which she had heard from the old woman. It was settled, Mrs.

[119-113]Broughton said, that Mr. Musselboro was to marry Clara Van Siever.

[119-114]But it appeared, as far as Dalrymple could learn, that this was a

[119-115]settlement made simply between Mrs. Van Siever and Musselboro. Clara,

[119-116]as he thought, was not a girl likely to fall into such a settlement

[119-117]without having an opinion of her own. Musselboro was to have the

[119-118]business, and Dobbs Broughton was to be "sold up," and then look for

[119-119]employment in the City. From her husband the wife had not heard a

[119-120]word on this matter, and the above story was simply what had been

[119-121]told to Mrs. Broughton by Mrs. Van Siever. "For myself it seems that

[119-122]there can be but one fate," said Mrs. Broughton. Dalrymple, in his

[119-123]tenderest voice, asked what that one fate must be. "Never mind," said

[119-124]Mrs. Broughton. "There are some things which one cannot tell even

[119-125]to such a friend as you." He was sitting near her and had all but

[119-126]got his arm behind her waist. He was, however, able to be prudent.

[119-127]"Maria," he said, getting up on his feet, "if it should really come

[119-128]about that you should want anything, you will send to me. You will

[119-129]promise me that, at any rate?" She rubbed a tear from her eye and

[119-130]said that she did not know. "There are moments in which a man must

[119-131]speak plainly," said Conway Dalrymple;--"in which it would be unmanly

[119-132]not to do so, however prosaic it may seem. I need hardly tell you

[119-133]that my purse shall be yours if you want it." But just at that moment

[119-134]she did not want his purse, nor must it be supposed that she wanted

[119-135]to run away with him and to leave her husband to fight the battle

[119-136]alone with Mrs. Van Siever. The truth was that she did not know

[119-137]what she wanted, over and beyond an assurance from Conway Dalrymple

[119-138]that she was the most ill-used, the most interesting, and the most

[119-139]beautiful woman ever heard of, either in history or romance. Had he

[119-140]proposed to her to pack up a bundle and go off with him in a cab

[119-141]to the London, Chatham, and Dover railway station, en route for

[119-142]Boulogne, I do not for a moment think that she would have packed up

[119-143]her bundle. She would have received intense gratification from the

[119-144]offer,--so much so that she would have been almost consoled for her

[119-145]husband's ruin; but she would have scolded her lover, and would have

[119-146]explained to him the great iniquity of which he was guilty.

[119-147]It was clear to him that at this present time he could not make any

[119-148]special terms with her as to Clara Van Siever. At such a moment as

[119-149]this he could hardly ask her to keep out of the way, in order that

[119-150]he might have his opportunity. But when he suggested that probably

[119-151]it might be better, in the present emergency, to give up the idea

[119-152]of any further sitting in her room, and proposed to send for his

[119-153]canvas, colour-box, and easel, she told him that, as far as she was

[119-154]concerned, he was welcome to have that one other sitting for which

[119-155]they had all bargained. "You had better come to-morrow, as we had

[119-156]agreed," she said; "and unless I shall have been turned out into the

[119-157]street by the creditors, you may have the room as you did before. And

[119-158]you must remember, Conway, that though Mrs. Van says that Musselboro

[119-159]is to have Clara, it doesn't follow that Clara should give way." When

[119-160]we consider everything, we must acknowledge that this was, at any

[119-161]rate, good-natured. Then there was a tender parting, with many tears,

[119-162]and Conway Dalrymple escaped from the house.

[119-163]He did not for a moment doubt the truth of the story which Mrs.

[119-164]Broughton had told, as far, at least, as it referred to the ruin of

[119-165]Dobbs Broughton. He had heard something of this before, and for some

[119-166]weeks had expected that a crash was coming. Broughton's rise had been

[119-167]very sudden, and Dalrymple had never regarded his friend as firmly

[119-168]placed in the commercial world. Dobbs was one of those men who seem

[119-169]born to surprise the world by a spurt of prosperity, and might,

[119-170]perhaps, have had a second spurt, or even a third, could he have kept

[119-171]himself from drinking in the morning. But Dalrymple, though he was

[119-172]hardly astonished by the story, as it regarded Broughton, was put out

[119-173]by that part of it which had reference to Musselboro. He had known

[119-174]that Musselboro had been introduced to Broughton by Mrs. Van Siever,

[119-175]but, nevertheless, he had regarded the man as being no more than

[119-176]Broughton's clerk. And now he was told that Musselboro was to marry

[119-177]Clara Van Siever, and have all Mrs. Van Siever's money. He resolved,

[119-178]at last, that he would run his risk about the money, and take Clara

[119-179]either with or without it, if she would have him. And as for that

[119-180]difficulty in asking her, if Mrs. Broughton would give him no

[119-181]opportunity of putting the question behind her back, he would put it

[119-182]before her face. He had not much leisure for consideration on these

[119-183]points, as the next day was the day for the last sitting.
