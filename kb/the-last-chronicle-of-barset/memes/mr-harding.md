## Character

Mr. Harding, the former precentor of Barchester Cathedral, is the enduring moral heart of Trollope's Barsetshire series. Now in old age, he appears in *The Last Chronicle of Barset* as a figure of gentle wisdom, the grandfather of Major Grantly through his daughter Eleanor, and a man whose simplicity of faith and kindness of heart serve as the novel's quiet ethical standard. "Old Mr. Harding" is long retired from his cathedral duties, and his conversations — particularly with the Archdeacon and with Mr. Toogood — provide the novel with moments of reflective calm amidst the storm of the Crawley scandal.

## Biography

Mr. Harding's life spans the entire Barsetshire series, from his central role in *The Warden* through to this final chronicle. In the present novel, he appears chiefly as the beloved grandfather of Major Grantly and as a friend and counselor. His most significant scenes occur in conversation with his son-in-law, Archdeacon Grantly, where he gently challenges the Archdeacon's worldly values: "It has lasted my time," said Mr. Harding, "and I'm" content with what he has had (040-171). Later, he receives a mysterious visit from Mr. Toogood, the London lawyer investigating the Crawley case, who comes to Barchester seeking background information. "Altogether as a friend, Mr. Harding," Toogood explains (079-005), and the old clergyman receives him with characteristic courtesy.

## Significance

Mr. Harding represents the moral continuity of the Barsetshire world. Having been the hero of *The Warden* — where he resigned the wardenship of Hiram's Hospital rather than profit from what he considered an unjust arrangement — he now appears as the series draws to a close, his quiet virtue undimmed by age. His conversations with the Archdeacon about wealth, preferment, and the proper aims of a clergyman's life contrast two visions of the Church: the worldly ambition that the Archdeacon represents, and the gentle pastoral ideal that Mr. Harding embodies. "Sometimes I sit and wonder," he tells the Archdeacon, "whether your" priorities align with what truly matters (041-016). His willingness to receive Toogood as "altogether a friend" reflects the openness of spirit that has made him, across six novels, the conscience of Barsetshire.
