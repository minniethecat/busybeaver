[125-001]He had heard of Lady Lufton's offer to his wife. The offers of the

[125-002]Lady Luftons of the world had been sorely distressing to his spirit,

[125-003]since it had first come to pass that such offers had reached him in

[125-004]consequence of his poverty. But now there was something almost of

[125-005]relief to him in the thought that the Lady Luftons would, after some

[125-006]fashion, save his wife and children from starvation;--would save his

[125-007]wife from the poorhouse, and enable his children to have a start in

[125-008]the world. For one of his children a brilliant marriage might be

[125-009]provided,--if only he himself were out of the way. How could he take

[125-010]himself out of the way? It had been whispered to him that he might be

[125-011]imprisoned for two months,--or for two years. Would it not be a grand

[125-012]thing if the judge would condemn him to be imprisoned for life? Was

[125-013]there ever a man whose existence was so purposeless, so useless, so

[125-014]deleterious, as his own? And yet he knew Hebrew well, whereas the

[125-015]dean knew but very little Hebrew. He could make Greek iambics, and

[125-016]doubted whether the bishop knew the difference between an iambus

[125-017]and a trochee. He could disport himself with trigonometry, feeling

[125-018]confident that Dr. Tempest had forgotten his way over the asses'

[125-019]bridge. He knew "Lycidas" by heart; and as for Thumble, he felt

[125-020]quite sure that Thumble was incompetent of understanding a single

[125-021]allusion in that divine poem. Nevertheless, though all this wealth of

[125-022]acquirement was his, it would be better for himself, better for those

[125-023]who belonged to him, better for the world at large, that he should be

[125-024]put an end to. A sentence of penal servitude for life, without any

[125-025]trial, would be of all things the most desirable. Then there would be

[125-026]ample room for the practice of that virtue which Hoggett had taught

[125-027]him.

[125-028]When he returned home the Hoggethan doctrine prevailed, and he

[125-029]prepared to copy his letter. But before he commenced his task, he

[125-030]sat down with his youngest daughter, and read,--or made her read

[125-031]to him,--a passage out of a Greek poem, in which are described the

[125-032]troubles and agonies of a blind giant. No giant would have been

[125-033]more powerful,--only that he was blind, and could not see to avenge

[125-034]himself on those who had injured him. "The same story is always

[125-035]coming up," he said, stopping the girl in her reading. "We have it in

[125-036]various versions, because it is so true to life.

[125-037]Ask for this great deliverer now, and find him

[125-038]Eyeless in Gaza, at the mill with slaves.

[125-039]It is the same story. Great power reduced to impotence, great glory

[125-040]to misery, by the hand of Fate,--Necessity, as the Greeks called

[125-041]her; the goddess that will not be shunned! At the mill with slaves!

[125-042]People, when they read it, do not appreciate the horror of the

[125-043]picture. Go on, my dear. It may be a question whether Polyphemus

[125-044]had mind enough to suffer; but, from the description of his power,

[125-045]I should think that he had. 'At the mill with slaves!' Can any

[125-046]picture be more dreadful than that? Go on, my dear. Of course you

[125-047]remember Milton's Samson Agonistes. Agonistes indeed!" His wife was

[125-048]sitting stitching at the other side of the room; but she heard his

[125-049]words,--heard and understood them; and before Jane could again get

[125-050]herself into the swing of the Greek verse, she was over at her

[125-051]husband's side, with her arms round his neck. "My love!" she said.

[125-052]"My love!"

[125-053]He turned to her, and smiled as he spoke to her. "These are old

[125-054]thoughts with me. Polyphemus and Belisarius, and Samson and Milton,

[125-055]have always been pets of mine. The mind of the strong blind creature

[125-056]must be so sensible of the injury that has been done to him! The

[125-057]impotency, combined with his strength, or rather the impotency

[125-058]with the memory of former strength and former aspirations, is so

[125-059]essentially tragic!"

[125-060]She looked into his eyes as he spoke, and there was something of

[125-061]the flash of old days, when the world was young to them, and when

[125-062]he would tell her of his hopes, and repeat to her long passages

[125-063]of poetry, and would criticize for her advantage the works of old

[125-064]writers. "Thank God," she said, "that you are not blind. It may yet

[125-065]be all right with you."

[125-066]"Yes,--it may be," he said.

[125-067]"And you shall not be at the mill with slaves."

[125-068]"Or, at any rate, not eyeless in Gaza, if the Lord is good to me.

[125-069]Come, Jane, we will go on." Then he took up the passage himself, and

[125-070]read it on with clear, sonorous voice, every now and then explaining

[125-071]some passage or expressing his own ideas upon it, as though he were

[125-072]really happy with his poetry.

[125-073]It was late in the evening before he got out his small stock of best

[125-074]letter-paper, and sat down to work at his letter. He first addressed

[125-075]himself to the bishop; and what he wrote to the bishop was as

[125-076]follows:--

[125-077]Hogglestock Parsonage, April llth, 186--.

[125-078]MY LORD BISHOP,

[125-079]I have been in communication with Dr. Tempest, of

[125-080]Silverbridge, from whom I have learned that your lordship

[125-081]has been pleased to appoint a commission of inquiry,--of

[125-082]which commission he is the chairman,--with reference to

[125-083]the proceedings which it may be necessary that you should

[125-084]take, as bishop of this diocese, after my forthcoming

[125-085]trial at the approaching Barchester assizes. My lord, I

[125-086]think it right to inform you, partly with a view to the

[125-087]comfort of the gentlemen named on that commission, and

[125-088]partly with the purport of giving you that information

[125-089]which I think that a bishop should possess in regard to

[125-090]the clerical affairs of his own diocese, that I have by

[125-091]this post resigned my preferment at Hogglestock into the

[125-092]hands of the Dean of Barchester, by whom it was given

[125-093]to me. In these circumstances, it will, I suppose, be

[125-094]unnecessary for you to continue the commission which you

[125-095]have set in force; but as to that, your lordship will, of

[125-096]course, be the only judge.

[125-097]I have the honour to be, my Lord Bishop,

[125-098]Your most obedient and very humble servant,

[125-099]JOSIAH CRAWLEY,

[125-100]Perpetual Curate of Hogglestock

[125-101]The Right Reverend

[125-102]The Bishop of Barchester,

[125-103]&c. &c. &c.

[125-104]The Palace, Barchester.

[125-105]But the letter which was of real importance,--which was intended to

[125-106]say something,--was that to the dean, and that also shall be given to

[125-107]the reader. Mr. Crawley had been for a while in doubt how he should

[125-108]address his old friend in commencing this letter, understanding that

[125-109]its tone throughout must, in a great degree, be made conformable with

[125-110]its first words. He would fain, in his pride, have begun "Sir." The

[125-111]question was between that and "My dear Arabin." It had once between

[125-112]them always been "Dear Frank" and "Dear Joe;" but the occasions for

[125-113]"Dear Frank" and "Dear Joe" between them had long been past. Crawley

[125-114]would have been very angry had he now been called Joe by the dean,

[125-115]and would have bitten his tongue out before he would have called the

[125-116]dean Frank. His better nature, however, now prevailed, and he began

[125-117]his letter, and completed it as follows:--

[125-118]MY DEAR ARABIN,

[125-119]Circumstances, of which you have probably heard something,

[125-120]compel me to write to you, as I fear, at some length. I am

[125-121]sorry that the trouble of such a letter should be forced

[125-122]upon you during your holidays;--

[125-123]Mr. Crawley, as he wrote this, did not forget to remind himself that

[125-124]he never had any holidays;

[125-125]--but I think you will admit, if you will bear with me to

[125-126]the end, that I have no alternative.

[125-127]I have been accused of stealing a cheque for twenty

[125-128]pounds, which cheque was drawn by my Lord Lufton on his

[125-129]London bankers, and was lost out of his pocket by Mr.

[125-130]Soames, his lordship's agent, and was so lost, as Mr.

[125-131]Soames states,--not with an absolute assertion,--during a

[125-132]visit which he made to my parsonage here at Hogglestock.

[125-133]Of the fact that I paid the cheque to a tradesman in

[125-134]Silverbridge there is no doubt. When questioned about it,

[125-135]I first gave an answer which was so manifestly incorrect

[125-136]that it has seemed odd to me that I should not have had

[125-137]credit for a mistake from those who must have seen that

[125-138]detection was so evident. The blunder was undoubtedly

[125-139]stupid, and it now bears heavy on me. I then, as I have

[125-140]learned, made another error,--of which I am aware that you

[125-141]have been informed. I said that the cheque had come to me

[125-142]from you, and in saying so, I thought that it had formed

[125-143]a portion of that alms which your open-handed benevolence

[125-144]bestowed upon me when I attended on you, not long before

[125-145]your departure, in your library. I have striven to

[125-146]remember the facts. It may be,--nay, it probably is the

[125-147]case,--that such struggles to catch some accurate glimpse

[125-148]of bygone things do not trouble you. Your mind is, no

[125-149]doubt, clearer and stronger than mine, having been kept

[125-150]to its proper tune by greater and fitter work. With me,

[125-151]memory is all but gone, and the power of thinking is on

[125-152]the wane! I struggled to remember, and I thought that

[125-153]the cheque had been in the envelope which you handed to

[125-154]me,--and I said so. I have since learned, from tidings

[125-155]received, as I am told, direct from yourself, that I was

[125-156]as wrong in the second statement as I had been in the

[125-157]first. The double blunder has, of course, been very heavy

[125-158]on me.

[125-159]I was taken before the magistrates at Silverbridge, and

[125-160]was by them committed to stand my trial at the assizes to

[125-161]be holden in Barchester on the 28th of this month. Without

[125-162]doubt, the magistrates had no alternative but to commit

[125-163]me, and I am indebted to them that they have allowed me my

[125-164]present liberty upon bail. That my sufferings in all this

[125-165]should have been grievous, you will understand. But on

[125-166]that head I should not touch, were it not that I am bound

[125-167]to explain to you that my troubles in reference to this

[125-168]parish of Hogglestock, to which I was appointed by you,

[125-169]have not been the slightest of those sufferings. I felt at

[125-170]first, believing then that the world around me would think

[125-171]it unlikely that such a one as I had wilfully stolen a sum

[125-172]of money, that it was my duty to maintain myself in my

[125-173]church. I did so maintain myself against an attack made

[125-174]upon me by the bishop, who sent over to Hogglestock one

[125-175]Mr. Thumble, a gentleman doubtless in holy orders, though

[125-176]I know nothing and can learn nothing of the place of his

[125-177]cure, to dispossess me of my pulpit and to remove me from

[125-178]my ministrations among my people. To Mr. Thumble I turned

[125-179]a deaf ear, and would not let him so much as open his

[125-180]mouth inside the porch of my church. Up to this time I

[125-181]myself have read the services, and have preached to the

[125-182]people, and have continued, as best I could, my visits to

[125-183]the poor and my labours in the school, though I know,--no

[125-184]one knows as well,--how unfitted I am for such work by the

[125-185]grief which has fallen upon me.

[125-186]Then the bishop sent for me, and I thought it becoming on

[125-187]my part to go to him. I presented myself to his lordship

[125-188]at his palace, and was minded to be much governed in my

[125-189]conduct by what he might say to me, remembering that I am

[125-190]bound to respect the office, even though I may not approve

[125-191]the man; and I humbled myself before his lordship, waiting

[125-192]patiently for any directions which he in his discretion

[125-193]might think it proper to bestow on me. But there arose up

[125-194]between us that very pestilent woman, his wife,--to his

[125-195]dismay, seemingly, as much as to mine,--and she would

[125-196]let there be place for no speech but her own. If there

[125-197]be aught clear to me in ecclesiastical matters, it is

[125-198]this,--that no authority can be delegated to a female. The

[125-199]special laws of this and of some other countries do allow

[125-200]that women shall sit upon the temporal thrones of the

[125-201]earth, but on the lowest step of the throne of the Church

[125-202]no woman has been allowed to sit as bearing authority,

[125-203]the romantic tale of the woman Pope notwithstanding.

[125-204]Thereupon, I left the palace in wrath, feeling myself

[125-205]aggrieved that a woman should have attempted to dictate

[125-206]to me, and finding it hopeless to get a clear instruction

[125-207]from his lordship,--the woman taking up the word whenever

[125-208]I put a question to my lord the bishop. Nothing,

[125-209]therefore, came of that interview but fruitless labour to

[125-210]myself, and anger, of which I have since been ashamed.
