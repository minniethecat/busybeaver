[056-001]After a while she was able to think of his conduct, and she believed

[056-002]that she ought to be very angry with him. He had taken her roughly in

[056-003]his arms, and had insulted her. He had forced a kiss from her. She

[056-004]had felt his arms warm and close and strong about her, and had not

[056-005]known whether she was in paradise or in purgatory. She was very angry

[056-006]with him. She would send back his letter to him without reading

[056-007]it,--without opening it, if that might be possible. He had done that

[056-008]to her which nothing could justify. But yet,--yet,--yet how dearly

[056-009]she loved him! Was he not a prince of men? He had behaved badly, of

[056-010]course; but had any man ever behaved so badly before in so divine a

[056-011]way? Was it not a thousand pities that she should be driven to deny

[056-012]anything to a lover who so richly deserved everything that could be

[056-013]given to him? He had kissed her hand as he let her go, and now, not

[056-014]knowing what she did, she kissed the spot on which she had felt his

[056-015]lips. His arm had been round her waist, and the old frock which she

[056-016]wore should be kept by her for ever, because it had been so graced.

[056-017]What was she now to say to Lily and to Lily's mother? Of one thing

[056-018]there was no doubt. She would never tell them of her lover's wicked

[056-019]audacity. That was a secret never to be imparted to any ears. She

[056-020]would keep her resentment to herself, and not ask the protection of

[056-021]any vicarious wrath. He could never so sin again, that was certain;

[056-022]and she would keep all knowledge and memory of the sin for her own

[056-023]purposes. But how could it be that such a man as that, one so good

[056-024]though so sinful, so glorious though so great a trespasser, should

[056-025]have come to such a girl as her and have asked for her love? Then she

[056-026]thought of her father's poverty and the misery of her own condition,

[056-027]and declared to herself that it was very wonderful.

[056-028]Lily was the first to enter the room, and she, before she did so,

[056-029]learned from the servant that Major Grantly had left the house. "I

[056-030]heard the door, miss, and then I saw the top of his hat out of the

[056-031]pantry window." Armed with this certain information Lily entered the

[056-032]drawing-room, and found Grace in the act of rising from the sofa.

[056-033]"Am I disturbing you?" said Lily.

[056-034]"No; not at all. I am glad you have come. Kiss me, and be good to

[056-035]me." And she twined her arms about Lily and embraced her.

[056-036]"Am I not always good to you, you simpleton? Has he been good?"

[056-037]"I don't know what you mean?"

[056-038]"And have you been good to him?"

[056-039]"As good as I knew how, Lily."

[056-040]"And where is he?"

[056-041]"He has gone away. I shall never see him any more, Lily."

[056-042]Then she hid her face upon her friend's shoulder and broke forth

[056-043]again into hysterical tears.

[056-044]"But tell me, Grace, what he said;--that is, if you mean to tell me!"

[056-045]"I will tell you everything;--that is, everything I can." And Grace

[056-046]blushed as she thought of the one secret which she certainly would

[056-047]not tell.

[056-048]"Has he,--has he done what I said he would do? Come, speak out

[056-049]boldly. Has he asked you to be his wife?"

[056-050]"Yes," said Grace, barely whispering the word.

[056-051]"And you have accepted him?"

[056-052]"No, Lily, I have not. Indeed, I have not. I did not know how to

[056-053]speak, because I was surprised;--and he, of course, could say what

[056-054]he liked. But I told him as well as I could, that I would not marry

[056-055]him."

[056-056]"And why;--did you tell him why?"

[056-057]"Yes; because of papa!"

[056-058]"Then, if he is the man I take him to be, that answer will go for

[056-059]nothing. Of course he knew all that before he came here. He did not

[056-060]think you were an heiress with forty thousand pounds. If he is in

[056-061]earnest, that will go for nothing. And I think he is in earnest."

[056-062]"And so was I in earnest."

[056-063]"Well, Grace;--we shall see."

[056-064]"I suppose I may have a will of my own, Lily."

[056-065]"Do not be so sure of that. Women are not allowed to have wills of

[056-066]their own on all occasions. Some man comes in a girl's way, and she

[056-067]gets to be fond of him, just because he does come in her way. Well;

[056-068]when that has taken place, she has no alternative but to be taken if

[056-069]he chooses to take her; or to be left, if he chooses to leave her."

[056-070]"Lily, don't say that."

[056-071]"But I do say it. A man may assure himself that he will find for

[056-072]himself a wife who shall be learned, or beautiful, or six feet

[056-073]high, if he wishes it, or who has red hair, or red eyes, or red

[056-074]cheeks,--just what he pleases; and he may go about till he finds it,

[056-075]as you can go about and match your worsteds. You are a fool if you

[056-076]buy a colour you don't want. But we can never match our worsteds

[056-077]for that other piece of work, but are obliged to take any colour

[056-078]that comes,--and, therefore, it is that we make such a jumble of it!

[056-079]Here's mamma. We must not be philosophical before her. Mamma, Major

[056-080]Grantly has--skedaddled."

[056-081]"Oh, Lily, what a word!"

[056-082]"But, oh, mamma, what a thing! Fancy his going away and not saying a

[056-083]word to anybody!"

[056-084]"If he had anything to say to Grace, I suppose he said it."

[056-085]"He asked her to marry him, of course. We none of us had any doubt

[056-086]about that. He swore to her that she and none but she should be his

[056-087]wife,--and all that kind of thing. But he seems to have done it in

[056-088]the most prosaic way;--and now he has gone away without saying a word

[056-089]to any of us. I shall never speak to him again,--unless Grace asks

[056-090]me."

[056-091]"Grace, my dear, may I congratulate you?" said Mrs. Dale.

[056-092]Grace did not answer, as Lily was too quick for her. "Oh, she has

[056-093]refused him, of course. But Major Grantly is a man of too much sense

[056-094]to expect that he should succeed the first time. Let me see; this is

[056-095]the fourteenth. These clocks run fourteen days, and, therefore, you

[056-096]may expect him again about the twenty-eighth. For myself, I think you

[056-097]are giving him an immense deal of unnecessary trouble, and that if he

[056-098]left you in the lurch it would only serve you right; but you have the

[056-099]world with you, I'm told. A girl is supposed to tell a man two fibs

[056-100]before she may tell him one truth."

[056-101]"I told him no fib, Lily. I told him that I would not marry him, and

[056-102]I will not."

[056-103]"But why not, dear Grace?" said Mrs. Dale.

[056-104]"Because the people say that papa is a thief!" Having said this,

[056-105]Grace walked slowly out of the room, and neither Mrs. Dale nor Lily

[056-106]attempted to follow her.

[056-107]"She's as good as gold," said Lily, when the door was closed.

[056-108]"And he;--what of him?"

[056-109]"I think he is good, too; but she has told me nothing yet of what

[056-110]he has said to her. He must be good, or he would not have come down

[056-111]here after her. But I don't wonder at his coming, because she is so

[056-112]beautiful! Once or twice as we were walking back to-day, I thought

[056-113]her face was the most lovely that I had ever seen. And did you see

[056-114]her just now, as she spoke of her father?"

[056-115]"Oh, yes;--I saw her."

[056-116]"Think what she will be in two or three years' time, when she becomes

[056-117]a woman. She talks French, and Italian, and Hebrew for anything that

[056-118]I know; and she is perfectly beautiful. I never saw a more lovely

[056-119]figure;--and she has spirit enough for a goddess. I don't think that

[056-120]Major Grantly is such a fool after all."

[056-121]"I never took him for a fool."

[056-122]"I have no doubt all his own people do;--or they will, when they hear

[056-123]of it. But, mamma, she will grow to be big enough to walk atop of all

[056-124]the Lady Hartletops in England. It will all come right at last."

[056-125]"You think it will?"

[056-126]"Oh, yes. Why should it not? If he is worth having, it will;--and

[056-127]I think he is worth having. He must wait till this horrid trial is

[056-128]over. It is clear to me that Grace thinks that her father will be

[056-129]convicted."

[056-130]"But he cannot have taken the money."

[056-131]"I think he took it, and I think it wasn't his. But I don't think he

[056-132]stole it. I don't know whether you can understand the difference."

[056-133]"I am afraid a jury won't understand it."

[056-134]"A jury of men will not. I wish they could put you and me on it,

[056-135]mamma. I would take my best boots and eat them down to the heels, for

[056-136]Grace's sake, and for Major Grantly's. What a good-looking man he

[056-137]is!"

[056-138]"Yes, he is."

[056-139]"And so like a gentleman! I'll tell you what, mamma; we won't say

[056-140]anything to her about him for the present. Her heart will be so full

[056-141]she will be driven to talk, and we can comfort her better in that

[056-142]way." The mother and daughter agreed to act upon these tactics, and

[056-143]nothing more was said to Grace about her lover on that evening.

[056-144]Major Grantly walked from Mrs. Dale's house to the inn and ordered

[056-145]his gig, and drove himself out of Allington, almost without

[056-146]remembering where he was or whither he was going. He was thinking

[056-147]solely of what had just occurred, and of what, on his part, should

[056-148]follow as the result of that meeting. Half at least of the noble

[056-149]deeds done in this world are due to emulation, rather than to the

[056-150]native nobility of the actors. A young man leads a forlorn hope

[056-151]because another young man has offered to do so. Jones in the

[056-152]hunting-field rides at an impracticable fence because he is told that

[056-153]Smith took it three years ago. And Walker puts his name down for ten

[056-154]guineas at a charitable dinner, when he hears Thompson's read out for

[056-155]five. And in this case the generosity and self-denial shown by Grace

[056-156]warmed and cherished similar virtues within her lover's breast. Some

[056-157]few weeks ago Major Grantly had been in doubt as to what his duty

[056-158]required of him in reference to Grace Crawley; but he had no doubt

[056-159]whatsoever now. In the fervour of his admiration he would have gone

[056-160]straight to the archdeacon, had it been possible, and have told him

[056-161]what he had done and what he intended to do. Nothing now should

[056-162]stop him;--no consideration, that is, either as regarded money or

[056-163]position. He had pledged himself solemnly, and he was very glad that

[056-164]he had pledged himself. He would write to Grace and explain to her

[056-165]that he trusted altogether in her father's honour and innocence, but

[056-166]that no consideration as to that ought to influence either him or her

[056-167]in any way. If, independently of her father, she could bring herself

[056-168]to come to him and be his wife, she was bound to do so now, let the

[056-169]position of her father be what it might. And thus, as he drove his

[056-170]gig back towards Guestwick, he composed a very pretty letter to the

[056-171]lady of his love.

[056-172]And as he went, at the corner of the lane which led from the main

[056-173]road up to Guestwick cottage, he again came upon John Eames, who

[056-174]was also returning to Guestwick. There had been a few words spoken

[056-175]between Lady Julia and Johnny respecting Major Grantly after the

[056-176]girls had left the cottage, and Johnny had been persuaded that the

[056-177]strange visitor to Allington could have no connection with his

[056-178]arch-enemy. "And why has he gone to Allington?" John demanded,

[056-179]somewhat sternly, of his hostess.
