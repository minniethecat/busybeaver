[114-001]"Well!" said Lady Lufton, stopping him in the passage,--"have you

[114-002]seen her?"

[114-003]"Yes; I have seen her."

[114-004]"Well?"

[114-005]"She is a good girl,--a very good girl. I am in a great hurry, and

[114-006]hardly know how to tell you more now."

[114-007]"You say that she is a good girl?"

[114-008]"I say that she is a very good girl. An angel could not have behaved

[114-009]better. I will tell you all some day, Lady Lufton, but I can hardly

[114-010]tell you now."

[114-011]When the archdeacon was gone old Lady Lufton confided to young Lady

[114-012]Lufton her very strong opinion that many months would not be gone by

[114-013]before Grace Crawley would be the mistress of Cosby Lodge. "It will

[114-014]be great promotion," said the old lady, with a little toss of her

[114-015]head.

[114-016]When Grace was interrogated afterwards by Mrs. Robarts as to what

[114-017]had passed between her and the archdeacon she had very little to

[114-018]say as to the interview. "No, he did not scold me," she replied to

[114-019]an inquiry from her friend. "But he spoke about your engagement?"

[114-020]said Mrs. Robarts. "There is no engagement," said Grace. "But I

[114-021]suppose you acknowledged, my dear, that a future engagement is

[114-022]quite possible?" "I told him, Mrs. Robarts," Grace answered, after

[114-023]hesitating for a moment, "that I would never marry his son as long

[114-024]as papa was suspected by any one in the world of being a thief. And

[114-025]I will keep my word." But she said nothing to Mrs. Robarts of the

[114-026]pledge which the archdeacon had made to her.

[114-027]CHAPTER LVIII.

[114-028]THE CROSS-GRAINEDNESS OF MEN.

[114-029][Illustration]

[114-030]By the time that the archdeacon reached Plumstead his enthusiasm in

[114-031]favour of Grace Crawley had somewhat cooled itself; and the language

[114-032]which from time to time he prepared for conveying his impressions to

[114-033]his wife, became less fervid as he approached his home. There was his

[114-034]pledge, and by that he would abide;--and so much he would make both

[114-035]his wife and his son understand. But any idea which he might have

[114-036]entertained for a moment of extending the promise he had given and

[114-037]relaxing that given to him was gone before he saw his own chimneys.

[114-038]Indeed, I fear he had by that time begun to feel that the only

[114-039]salvation now open to him must come from the jury's verdict. If the

[114-040]jury should declare Mr. Crawley to be guilty, then--; he would not

[114-041]say even to himself that in such case all would be right, but he did

[114-042]feel that much as he might regret the fate of the poor Crawleys, and

[114-043]of the girl whom in his warmth he had declared to be almost an angel,

[114-044]nevertheless to him personally such a verdict would bring consolatory

[114-045]comfort.

[114-046]"I have seen Miss Crawley," he said to his wife, as soon as he had

[114-047]closed the door of his study, before he had been two minutes out of

[114-048]the chaise. He had determined that he would dash at the subject at

[114-049]once, and he thus carried his resolution into effect.

[114-050]"You have seen Grace Crawley?"

[114-051]"Yes; I went up to the parsonage and called upon her. Lady Lufton

[114-052]advised me to do so."

[114-053]"And Henry?"

[114-054]"Oh, Henry has gone. He was only there one night. I suppose he saw

[114-055]her, but I am not sure."

[114-056]"Would not Miss Crawley tell you?"

[114-057]"I forgot to ask her." Mrs. Grantly, at hearing this, expressed her

[114-058]surprise by opening wide her eyes. He had gone all the way over to

[114-059]Framley on purpose to look after his son, and learn what were his

[114-060]doings, and when there he had forgotten to ask the person who could

[114-061]have given him better information than any one else! "But it does not

[114-062]signify," continued the archdeacon; "she said enough to me to make

[114-063]that of no importance."

[114-064]"And what did she say?"

[114-065]"She said that she would never consent to marry Henry as long as

[114-066]there was any suspicion abroad as to her father's guilt."

[114-067]"And you believe her promise?"

[114-068]"Certainly I do; I do not doubt it in the least. I put implicit

[114-069]confidence in her. And I have promised her that if her father is

[114-070]acquitted,--I will withdraw my opposition."

[114-071]"No!"

[114-072]"But I have. And you would have done the same had you been there."

[114-073]"I doubt that, my dear. I am not so impulsive as you are."

[114-074]"You could not have helped yourself. You would have felt yourself

[114-075]obliged to be equally generous with her. She came up to me and she

[114-076]put her hand upon me--" "Psha!" said Mrs. Grantly. "But she did, my

[114-077]dear; and then she said, 'I promise you that I will not become your

[114-078]son's wife while people think that papa stole this money.' What else

[114-079]could I do?"

[114-080]"And is she pretty?"

[114-081]"Very pretty; very beautiful."

[114-082]"And like a lady?"

[114-083]"Quite like a lady. There is no mistake about that."

[114-084]"And she behaved well?"

[114-085]"Admirably," said the archdeacon, who was in a measure compelled

[114-086]to justify the generosity into which he had been betrayed by his

[114-087]feelings.

[114-088]"Then she is a paragon," said Mrs. Grantly.

[114-089]"I don't know what you may call a paragon, my dear. I say that she is

[114-090]a lady, and that she is extremely good-looking, and that she behaved

[114-091]very well. I cannot say less in her favour. I am sure you would not

[114-092]say less yourself, if you had been present."

[114-093]"She must be a wonderful young woman."

[114-094]"I don't know anything about her being wonderful."

[114-095]"She must be wonderful when she has succeeded both with the son and

[114-096]with the father."

[114-097]"I wish you had been there instead of me," said the archdeacon,

[114-098]angrily. Mrs. Grantly very probably wished so also, feeling that in

[114-099]that case a more serene mode of business would have been adopted.

[114-100]How keenly susceptible the archdeacon still was to the influences of

[114-101]feminine charms, no one knew better than Mrs. Grantly, and whenever

[114-102]she became aware that he had been in this way seduced from the wisdom

[114-103]of his cooler judgment she always felt something akin to indignation

[114-104]against the seducer. As for her husband, she probably told herself at

[114-105]such moments that he was an old goose. "If you had been there, and

[114-106]Henry with you, you would have made a great deal worse job of it than

[114-107]I have done," said the archdeacon.

[114-108]"I don't say you have made a bad job of it, my dear," said Mrs.

[114-109]Grantly. "But it's past eight, and you must be terribly in want of

[114-110]your dinner. Had you not better go up and dress?"

[114-111]In the evening the plan of the future campaign was arranged between

[114-112]them. The archdeacon would not write to his son at all. In passing

[114-113]through Barchester he had abandoned his idea of despatching a note

[114-114]from the hotel, feeling that such a note as would be required was

[114-115]not easily written in a hurry. Mrs. Grantly would now write to her

[114-116]son, telling him that circumstances had changed, that it would be

[114-117]altogether unnecessary for him to sell his furniture, and begging

[114-118]him to come over and see his father without a day's delay. She wrote

[114-119]her letter that night, and read to the archdeacon all that she had

[114-120]written,--with the exception of the postscript:--"You may be quite

[114-121]sure that there will be no unpleasantness with your father." That was

[114-122]the postscript which was not communicated to the archdeacon.

[114-123]On the third day after that Henry Grantly did come over to Plumstead.

[114-124]His mother in her letter to him had not explained how it had come to

[114-125]pass that the sale of his furniture would be unnecessary. His father

[114-126]had given him to understand distinctly that his income would be

[114-127]withdrawn from him unless he would express his intention of giving

[114-128]up Miss Crawley; and it had been admitted among them all that Cosby

[114-129]Lodge must be abandoned if this were done. He certainly would not

[114-130]give up Grace Crawley. Sooner than that, he would give up every

[114-131]stick in his possession, and go and live in New Zealand if it were

[114-132]necessary. Not only had Grace's conduct to him made him thus firm,

[114-133]but the natural bent of his own disposition had tended that way also.

[114-134]His father had attempted to dictate to him, and sooner than submit to

[114-135]that he would sell the coat off his back. Had his father confined his

[114-136]opposition to advice, and had Miss Crawley been less firm in her view

[114-137]of her duty, the major might have been less firm also. But things

[114-138]had so gone that he was determined to be fixed as granite. If others

[114-139]would not be moved from their resolves, neither would he. Such being

[114-140]the state of his mind, he could not understand why he was thus

[114-141]summoned to Plumstead. He had already written over to Pau about his

[114-142]house, and it was well that he should, at any rate, see his mother

[114-143]before he started. He was willing, therefore, to go to Plumstead, but

[114-144]he took no steps as to the withdrawal of those auctioneer's bills to

[114-145]which the archdeacon so strongly objected. When he drove into the

[114-146]rectory yard, his father was standing there before him. "Henry," he

[114-147]said, "I am very glad to see you. I am very much obliged to you for

[114-148]coming." Then Henry got out of his cart and shook hands with his

[114-149]father, and the archdeacon began to talk about the weather. "Your

[114-150]mother has gone into Barchester to see your grandfather," said the

[114-151]archdeacon. "If you are not tired, we might as well take a walk.

[114-152]I want to go up as far as Flurry's cottage." The major of course

[114-153]declared that he was not at all tired, and that he should be

[114-154]delighted of all things to go up and see old Flurry, and thus they

[114-155]started. Young Grantly had not even been into the house before he

[114-156]left the yard with his father. Of course, he was thinking of the

[114-157]coming sale at Cosby Lodge, and of his future life at Pau, and of his

[114-158]injured position in the world. There would be no longer any occasion

[114-159]for him to be solicitous as to the Plumstead foxes. Of course these

[114-160]things were in his mind; but he could not begin to speak of them till

[114-161]his father did so. "I'm afraid your grandfather is not very strong,"

[114-162]said the archdeacon, shaking his head. "I fear he won't be with us

[114-163]very long."

[114-164]"Is it so bad as that, sir?"

[114-165]"Well, you know, he is an old man, Henry; and he was always somewhat

[114-166]old for his age. He will be eighty, if he lives two years longer,

[114-167]I think. But he'll never reach eighty;--never. You must go and see

[114-168]him before you go back home; you must indeed." The major, of course,

[114-169]promised that he would see his grandfather, and the archdeacon told

[114-170]his son how nearly the old man had fallen in the passage between the

[114-171]cathedral and the deanery. In this way they had nearly made their way

[114-172]up to the gamekeeper's cottage without a word of reference to any

[114-173]subject that touched upon the matter of which each of them was of

[114-174]course thinking. Whether the major intended to remain at home or to

[114-175]live at Pau, the subject of Mr. Harding's health was a natural topic

[114-176]for conversation between him and his father; but when his father

[114-177]stopped suddenly, and began to tell him how a fox had been trapped on

[114-178]Darvell's farm,--"and of course it was a Plumstead fox,--there can be

[114-179]no doubt that Flurry is right about that;"--when the archdeacon spoke

[114-180]of this iniquity with much warmth, and told his son how he had at

[114-181]once written off to Mr. Thorne of Ullathorne, and how Mr. Thorne

[114-182]had declared that he didn't believe a word of it, and how Flurry

[114-183]had produced the pad of the fox, with the marks of the trap on the

[114-184]skin,--then the son began to feel that the ground was becoming very

[114-185]warm, and that he could not go on much longer without rushing into

[114-186]details about Grace Crawley. "I've no more doubt that it was one of

[114-187]our foxes than that I stand here," said the archdeacon.
