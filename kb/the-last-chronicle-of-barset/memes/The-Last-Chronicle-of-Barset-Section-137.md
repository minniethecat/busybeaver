[137-001]Mrs. Proudie had died on the Tuesday,--that having been the day of

[137-002]Mr. Thumble's visit to Hogglestock,--and Mr. Robarts had gone over to

[137-003]Silverbridge, in answer to Dr. Tempest's invitation, on the Thursday.

[137-004]He had not, therefore, the command of much time, it being his express

[137-005]object to prevent the appearance of Mr. Thumble at Hogglestock

[137-006]on the next Sunday. He had gone to Silverbridge by railway, and

[137-007]had, therefore, been obliged to postpone his visit to Mr. Crawley

[137-008]till the next day; but early on the Friday morning he rode over

[137-009]to Hogglestock. That he did not arrive there with a broken-knee'd

[137-010]horse, the reader may be quite sure. In all matters of that sort, Mr.

[137-011]Robarts was ever above reproach. He rode a good horse, and drove a

[137-012]neat gig, and was always well dressed. On this account Mr. Crawley,

[137-013]though he really liked Mr. Robarts, and was thankful to him for many

[137-014]kindnesses, could never bear his presence with perfect equanimity.

[137-015]Robarts was no scholar, was not a great preacher, had obtained no

[137-016]celebrity as a churchman,--had, in fact, done nothing to merit great

[137-017]reward; and yet everything had been given to him with an abundant

[137-018]hand. Within the last twelvemonth his wife had inherited Mr. Crawley

[137-019]did not care to know how many thousand pounds. And yet Mr. Robarts

[137-020]had won all that he possessed by being a clergyman. Was it possible

[137-021]that Mr. Crawley should regard such a man with equanimity? Robarts

[137-022]rode over with a groom behind him,--really taking the groom because

[137-023]he knew that Mr. Crawley would have no one to hold his horse for

[137-024]him;--and the groom was the source of great offence. He came upon Mr.

[137-025]Crawley standing at the school door, and stopping at once, jumped off

[137-026]his nag. There was something in the way in which he sprang out of the

[137-027]saddle and threw the reins to the man, which was not clerical in Mr.

[137-028]Crawley's eyes. No man could be so quick in the matter of a horse

[137-029]who spent as many hours with the poor and with the children as

[137-030]should be spent by a parish clergyman. It might be probable that Mr.

[137-031]Robarts had never stolen twenty pounds,--might never be accused of

[137-032]so disgraceful a crime,--but, nevertheless, Mr. Crawley had his own

[137-033]ideas, and made his own comparisons.

[137-034]"Crawley," said Robarts, "I am so glad to find you at home."

[137-035]"I am generally to be found in the parish," said the perpetual curate

[137-036]of Hogglestock.

[137-037]"I know you are," said Robarts, who knew the man well, and cared

[137-038]nothing for his friend's peculiarities when he felt his own withers

[137-039]to be unwrung. "But you might have been down at Hoggle End with the

[137-040]brickmakers, and then I should have had to go after you."

[137-041]"I should have grieved--," began Crawley; but Robarts interrupted him

[137-042]at once.

[137-043]"Let us go for a walk, and I'll leave the man with the horses. I've

[137-044]something special to say to you, and I can say it better out here

[137-045]than in the house. Grace is quite well, and sends her love. She is

[137-046]growing to look so beautiful!"

[137-047]"I hope she may grow in grace with God," said Mr. Crawley.

[137-048]"She's as good a girl as I ever knew. By-the-by, you had Henry

[137-049]Grantly over here the other day?"

[137-050]"Major Grantly, whom I cannot name without expressing my esteem for

[137-051]him, did do us the honour of calling upon us not very long since. If

[137-052]it be with reference to him that you have taken this trouble--"

[137-053]"No, no; not at all. I'll allow him and the ladies to fight out that

[137-054]battle. I've not the least doubt in the world how that will go. When

[137-055]I'm told that she made a complete conquest of the archdeacon, there

[137-056]cannot be a doubt about that."

[137-057]"A conquest of the archdeacon!"

[137-058]But Mr. Robarts did not wish to have to explain anything further

[137-059]about the archdeacon. "Were you not terribly shocked, Crawley," he

[137-060]asked, "when you heard of the death of Mrs. Proudie?"

[137-061]"It was sudden and very awful," said Mr. Crawley. "Such deaths are

[137-062]always shocking. Not more so, perhaps, as regards the wife of a

[137-063]bishop, than with any other woman."

[137-064]"Only we happened to know her."

[137-065]"No doubt the finite and meagre nature of our feelings does prevent

[137-066]us from extending our sympathies to those whom we have not seen

[137-067]in the flesh. It should not be so, and would not with one who had

[137-068]nurtured his heart with proper care. And we are prone to permit an

[137-069]evil worse than that to canker our regards and to foster and to mar

[137-070]our solicitudes. Those who are high in station strike us more by

[137-071]their joys and sorrows than do the poor and lowly. Were some young

[137-072]duke's wife, wedded but the other day, to die, all England would put

[137-073]on some show of mourning,--nay, would feel some true gleam of pity;

[137-074]but nobody cares for the widowed brickmaker seated with his starving

[137-075]infant on his cold hearth."

[137-076]"Of course we hear more of the big people," said Robarts.

[137-077]"Ay; and think more of them. But do not suppose, sir, that I complain

[137-078]of this man or that woman because his sympathies, or hers, run out of

[137-079]that course which my reason tells me they should hold. The man with

[137-080]whom it would not be so would simply be a god among men. It is in

[137-081]his perfection as a man that we recognize the divinity of Christ. It

[137-082]is in the imperfection of men that we recognize our necessity for a

[137-083]Christ. Yes, sir, the death of the poor lady at Barchester was very

[137-084]sudden. I hope that my lord the bishop bears with becoming fortitude

[137-085]the heavy misfortune. They say that he was a man much beholden to his

[137-086]wife,--prone to lean upon her in his goings out and comings in. For

[137-087]such a man such a loss is more dreadful perhaps than for another."

[137-088]"They say she led him a terrible life, you know."

[137-089]"I am not prone, sir, to believe much of what I hear about the

[137-090]domesticities of other men, knowing how little any other man can know

[137-091]of my own. And I have, methinks, observed a proneness in the world to

[137-092]ridicule that dependence on a woman which every married man should

[137-093]acknowledge in regard to the wife of his bosom, if he can trust her

[137-094]as well as love her. When I hear jocose proverbs spoken as to men,

[137-095]such as that in this house the gray mare is the better horse, or that

[137-096]in that house the wife wears that garment which is supposed to denote

[137-097]virile command, knowing that the joke is easy, and that meekness in

[137-098]a man is more truly noble than a habit of stern authority, I do not

[137-099]allow them to go far with me in influencing my judgment."

[137-100]So spoke Mr. Crawley, who never permitted the slightest interference

[137-101]with his own word in his own family, and who had himself been a

[137-102]witness of one of those scenes between the bishop and his wife in

[137-103]which the poor bishop had been so cruelly misused. But to Mr. Crawley

[137-104]the thing which he himself had seen under such circumstances was as

[137-105]sacred as though it had come to him under the seal of confession. In

[137-106]speaking of the bishop and Mrs. Proudie,--nay, as far as was possible

[137-107]in thinking of them,--he was bound to speak and to think as though he

[137-108]had not witnessed that scene in the palace study.

[137-109]"I don't suppose that there is much doubt about her real character,"

[137-110]said Robarts. "But you and I need not discuss that."

[137-111]"By no means. Such discussion would be both useless and unseemly."

[137-112]"And just at present there is something else that I specially want

[137-113]to say to you. Indeed, I went to Silverbridge on the same subject

[137-114]yesterday, and have come here expressly to have a little conversation

[137-115]with you."

[137-116]"If it be about affairs of mine, Mr. Robarts, I am indeed troubled in

[137-117]spirit that so great labour should have fallen upon you."

[137-118]"Never mind my labour. Indeed your saying that is a nuisance to me,

[137-119]because I hoped that by this time you would have understood that I

[137-120]regard you as a friend, and that I think nothing any trouble that

[137-121]I do for a friend. Your position just now is so peculiar that it

[137-122]requires a great deal of care."

[137-123]"No care can be of any avail to me."

[137-124]"There I disagree with you. You must excuse me, but I do; and so does

[137-125]Dr. Tempest. We think that you have been a little too much in a hurry

[137-126]since he communicated to you the result of our first meeting."

[137-127]"As how, sir?"

[137-128]"It is, perhaps, hardly worth while for us to go into the whole

[137-129]question; but that man, Thumble, must not come here on next Sunday."

[137-130]"I cannot say, Mr. Robarts, that the Reverend Mr. Thumble has

[137-131]recommended himself to me strongly either by his outward symbols of

[137-132]manhood or by such manifestation of his inward mental gifts as I have

[137-133]succeeded in obtaining. But my knowledge of him has been so slight,

[137-134]and has been acquired in a manner so likely to bias me prejudicially

[137-135]against him, that I am inclined to think my opinion should go for

[137-136]nothing. It is, however, the fact that the bishop has nominated him

[137-137]to this duty; and that, as I have myself simply notified my desire

[137-138]to be relieved from the care of the parish, on account of certain

[137-139]unfitness of my own, I am the last man who should interfere with the

[137-140]bishop in the choice of my temporary successor."

[137-141]"It was her choice, not his."

[137-142]"Excuse me, Mr. Robarts, but I cannot allow that assertion to pass

[137-143]unquestioned. I must say that I have adequate cause for believing

[137-144]that he came here by his lordship's authority."

[137-145]"No doubt he did. Will you just listen to me for a moment? Ever since

[137-146]this unfortunate affair of the cheque became known, Mrs. Proudie has

[137-147]been anxious to get you out of this parish. She was a violent woman,

[137-148]and chose to take this matter up violently. Pray hear me out before

[137-149]you interrupt me. There would have been no commission at all but for

[137-150]her."

[137-151]"The commission is right and proper and just," said Mr. Crawley, who

[137-152]could not keep himself silent.

[137-153]"Very well. Let it be so. But Mr. Thumble's coming over here is not

[137-154]proper or right; and you may be sure the bishop does not wish it."

[137-155]"Let him send any other clergyman whom he may think more fitting,"

[137-156]said Mr. Crawley.

[137-157]"But we do not want him to send anybody."

[137-158]"Somebody must be sent, Mr. Robarts."

[137-159]"No, not so. Let me go over and see Thumble and Snapper,--Snapper,

[137-160]you know, is the domestic chaplain; and all that you need do is to go

[137-161]on with your services on Sunday. If necessary, I will see the bishop.

[137-162]I think you may be sure that I can manage it. If not, I will come

[137-163]back to you." Mr. Robarts paused for an answer, but it seemed for

[137-164]awhile that all Mr. Crawley's impatient desire to speak was over. He

[137-165]walked on silently along the lane by his visitor's side, and when,

[137-166]after some five or six minutes, Robarts stood still in the road, Mr.

[137-167]Crawley even then said nothing. "It cannot be but that you should be

[137-168]anxious to keep the income of the parish for your wife and children,"

[137-169]said Mark Robarts.

[137-170]"Of course, I am anxious for my wife and children," Crawley answered.

[137-171]"Then let me do as I say. Why should you throw away a chance, even if

[137-172]it be a bad one? But here the chance is all in your favour. Let me

[137-173]manage it for you at Barchester."
