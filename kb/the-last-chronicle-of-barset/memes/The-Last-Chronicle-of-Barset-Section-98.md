[098-001]"I do trust you."

[098-002]"As a friend, I mean;--as a real friend. If it should be the case,

[098-003]Mrs. Crawley, that a jury should give a verdict against your

[098-004]husband,--what will you do then? Perhaps I ought not to suppose that

[098-005]it is possible."

[098-006]"Of course we know that is possible," said Mrs. Crawley. Her voice

[098-007]was stern, and there was in it a tone almost of offence. As she spoke

[098-008]she did not look at her visitor, but sat with her face averted and

[098-009]her arms akimbo on the table.

[098-010]"Yes;--it is possible," said Lady Lufton. "I suppose there is not one

[098-011]in the county who does not truly wish that it may not be so. But it

[098-012]is right to be prepared for all alternatives. In such case have you

[098-013]thought what you will do?"

[098-014]"I do not know what they would do to him," said she.

[098-015]"I suppose that for some time he would be--"

[098-016]"Put in prison," said Mrs. Crawley, speaking very quickly, bringing

[098-017]out the words with a sharp eagerness that was quite unusual to her.

[098-018]"They will send him to gaol. Is it not so, Lady Lufton?"

[098-019]"I suppose it would be so; not for long I should hope; but I presume

[098-020]that such would be the sentence for some short period."

[098-021]"And I might not go with him?"

[098-022]"No; that would be impossible."

[098-023]"And the house, and the living; would they let him have them again

[098-024]when he came out?"

[098-025]"Ah; that I cannot say. That will depend much, probably, on what

[098-026]these clergymen will report. I hope he will not put himself in

[098-027]opposition to them."

[098-028]"I do not know. I cannot say. It is probable that he may do so. It

[098-029]is not easy for a man so injured as he has been, and one at the same

[098-030]time so great in intelligence, to submit himself gently to such

[098-031]inquiries. When ill is being done to himself or others he is very

[098-032]prone to oppose it."

[098-033]"But these gentlemen do not wish to do him ill, Mrs. Crawley."

[098-034]"I cannot say. I do not know. When I think of it I see that there is

[098-035]nothing but ruin on every side. What is the use of talking of it? Do

[098-036]not be angry, Lady Lufton, if I say that it is of no use."

[098-037]"But I desire to be of use,--of real use. If it should be the case,

[098-038]Mrs. Crawley, that your husband should be--detained at Barchester--"

[098-039]"You mean imprisoned, Lady Lufton."

[098-040]"Yes, I mean imprisoned. If it should be so, then do you bring

[098-041]yourself and your children,--all of them,--over to Framley, and I

[098-042]will find a home for you while he is lost to you."

[098-043]"Oh, Lady Lufton; I could not do that."

[098-044]"Yes, you can. You have not heard me yet. It would not be a comfort

[098-045]to you in such a home as that to sit at table with people who are

[098-046]partly strangers to you. But there is a cottage nearly adjoining to

[098-047]the house, which you shall have all to yourself. The bailiff lived

[098-048]in it once, and others have lived in it who belong to the place; but

[098-049]it is empty now and it shall be made comfortable." The tears were

[098-050]now running down Mrs. Crawley's face, so that she could not answer a

[098-051]word. "Of course it is my son's property, and not mine, but he has

[098-052]commissioned me to say that it is most heartily at your service. He

[098-053]begs that in such case you will occupy it. And I beg the same. And

[098-054]your old friend Lucy has desired me also to ask you in her name."

[098-055]"Lady Lufton, I could not do that," said Mrs. Crawley through her

[098-056]tears.

[098-057]"You must think better of it, my dear. I do not scruple to advise

[098-058]you, because I am older than you, and have experience of the world."

[098-059]This, I think, taken in the ordinary sense of the words, was a boast

[098-060]on the part of Lady Lufton, for which but little true pretence

[098-061]existed. Lady Lufton's experience of the world at large was not

[098-062]perhaps extensive. Nevertheless she knew what one woman might offer

[098-063]to another, and what one woman might receive from another. "You would

[098-064]be better over with me, my dear, than you could be elsewhere. You

[098-065]will not misunderstand me if I say that, under such circumstances, it

[098-066]would do your husband good that you and your children should be under

[098-067]our protection during his period of temporary seclusion. We stand

[098-068]well in the county. Perhaps I ought not to say so, but I do not know

[098-069]how otherwise to explain myself; and when it is known, by the bishop

[098-070]and others, that you have come to us during that sad time, it will be

[098-071]understood that we think well of Mr. Crawley, in spite of anything

[098-072]that a jury may say of him. Do you see that, my dear? And we do think

[098-073]well of him. I have known of your husband for many years, though I

[098-074]have not personally had the pleasure of much acquaintance with him.

[098-075]He was over at Framley once at my request, and I had great occasion

[098-076]then to respect him. I do respect him; and I shall feel grateful to

[098-077]him if he will allow you to put yourself and your children under my

[098-078]wing, as being an old woman, should this misfortune fall upon him.

[098-079]We hope that it will not fall upon him; but it is always well to be

[098-080]provided for the worst."

[098-081]In this way Lady Lufton at last made her speech and opened out the

[098-082]proposal with which she had come laden to Hogglestock. While she was

[098-083]speaking Mrs. Crawley's shoulder was still turned to her; but the

[098-084]speaker could see that the quick tears were pouring themselves down

[098-085]the cheeks of the woman whom she addressed. There was a downright

[098-086]honesty of thorough-going well-wishing charity about the proposition

[098-087]which overcame Mrs. Crawley altogether. She did not feel for a

[098-088]moment that it would be possible for her to go to Framley in such

[098-089]circumstances as those which had been suggested. As she thought of it

[098-090]all at the present moment, it seemed to her that her only appropriate

[098-091]home during the terrible period which was coming upon her, would

[098-092]be under the walls of the prison in which her husband would be

[098-093]incarcerated. But she fully appreciated the kindness which had

[098-094]suggested a measure, which, if carried into execution, would make

[098-095]the outside world feel that her husband was respected in the county,

[098-096]despite the degradation to which he was subjected. She felt all this,

[098-097]but her heart was too full to speak.

[098-098]"Say that it shall be so, my dear," continued Lady Lufton. "Just give

[098-099]me one nod of assent, and the cottage shall be ready for you should

[098-100]it so chance that you should require it."

[098-101]But Mrs. Crawley did not give the nod of assent. With her face still

[098-102]averted, while the tears were still running down her cheeks, she

[098-103]muttered but a word or two. "I could not do that, Lady Lufton; I

[098-104]could not do that."

[098-105]"You know at any rate what my wishes are, and as you become calmer

[098-106]you will think of it. There is quite time enough, and I am speaking

[098-107]of an alternative which may never happen. My dear friend Mrs.

[098-108]Robarts, who is now with your daughter, wishes Miss Crawley to go

[098-109]over to Framley Parsonage while this inquiry among the clergymen is

[098-110]going on. They all say it is the most ridiculous thing in all the

[098-111]world,--this inquiry. But the bishop you know is so silly! We all

[098-112]think that if Miss Crawley would go for a week or so to Framley

[098-113]Parsonage, that it will show how happy we all are to receive her. It

[098-114]should be while Mr. Robarts is employed in his part of the work. What

[098-115]do you say, Mrs. Crawley? We at Framley are all clearly of opinion

[098-116]that it will be best that it should be known that the people in

[098-117]the county uphold your husband. Miss Crawley would be back, you

[098-118]know, before the trial comes on. I hope you will let her come, Mrs.

[098-119]Crawley?"

[098-120]But even to this proposition Mrs. Crawley could give no assent,

[098-121]though she expressed no direct dissent. As regarded her own feelings,

[098-122]she would much have preferred to have been left to live through her

[098-123]misery alone; but she could not but appreciate the kindness which

[098-124]endeavoured to throw over her and hers in their trouble the ægis of

[098-125]first-rate county respectability. She was saved from the necessity

[098-126]of giving a direct answer to this suggestion by the return of Mrs.

[098-127]Robarts and Grace herself. The door was opened slowly, and they crept

[098-128]into the room as though they were aware that their presence would be

[098-129]hardly welcomed.

[098-130]"Is the carriage there, Fanny?" said Lady Lufton. "It is almost time

[098-131]for us to think of returning home."

[098-132]Mrs. Robarts said that the carriage was standing within twenty yards

[098-133]of the door.

[098-134]"Then I think we will make a start," said Lady Lufton. "Have you

[098-135]succeeded in persuading Miss Crawley to come over to Framley in

[098-136]April?"

[098-137]Mrs. Robarts made no answer to this, but looked at Grace; and Grace

[098-138]looked down upon the ground.

[098-139]"I have spoken to Mrs. Crawley," said Lady Lufton, "and they will

[098-140]think of it." Then the two ladies took their leave, and walked out to

[098-141]their carriage.

[098-142]"What does she say about your plan?" Mrs. Robarts asked.

[098-143]"She is too broken-hearted to say anything," Lady Lufton answered.

[098-144]"Should it happen that he is convicted, we must come over and take

[098-145]her. She will have no power then to resist us in anything."

[098-147]MRS. DOBBS BROUGHTON PILES HER FAGOTS.

[098-149]The picture still progressed up in Mrs. Dobbs Broughton's room, and

[098-150]the secret was still kept, or supposed to be kept. Miss Van Siever

[098-151]was, at any rate, certain that her mother had heard nothing of it,

[098-152]and Mrs. Broughton reported from day to day that her husband had not

[098-153]as yet interfered. Nevertheless, there was in these days a great

[098-154]gloom upon the Dobbs Broughton household, so much so that Conway

[098-155]Dalrymple had more than once suggested to Mrs. Broughton that the

[098-156]work should be discontinued. But the mistress of the house would not

[098-157]consent to this. In answer to these offers, she was wont to declare

[098-158]in somewhat mysterious language, that any misery coming upon herself

[098-159]was matter of moment to nobody,--hardly even to herself, as she was

[098-160]quite prepared to encounter moral and social death without delay, if

[098-161]not an absolute physical demise; as to which latter alternative, she

[098-162]seemed to think that even that might not be so far distant as some

[098-163]people chose to believe. What was the cause of the gloom over the

[098-164]house neither Conway Dalrymple nor Miss Van Siever understood, and

[098-165]to speak the truth Mrs. Broughton did not quite understand the cause

[098-166]herself. She knew well enough, no doubt, that her husband came home

[098-167]always sullen, and sometimes tipsy, and that things were not going

[098-168]well in the City. She had never understood much about the City, being

[098-169]satisfied with an assurance that had come to her in early days from

[098-170]her friends, that there was a mine of wealth in Hook Court, from

[098-171]whence would always come for her use, house and furniture, a carriage

[098-172]and horses, dresses and jewels, which latter, if not quite real,

[098-173]should be manufactured of the best sham substitute known. Soon after

[098-174]her brilliant marriage with Mr. Dobbs Broughton, she had discovered

[098-175]that the carriage and horses, and the sham jewels, did not lift her

[098-176]so completely into a terrestrial paradise as she had taught herself

[098-177]to expect that they would do. Her brilliant drawing-room, with Dobbs

[098-178]Broughton for a companion, was not an elysium. But though she had

[098-179]found out early in her married life that something was still wanting

[098-180]to her, she had by no means confessed to herself that the carriage

[098-181]and horses and sham jewels were bad, and it can hardly be said that

[098-182]she had repented. She had endeavoured to patch up matters with a

[098-183]little romance, and then had fallen upon Conway Dalrymple,--meaning

[098-184]no harm. Indeed, love with her, as it never could have meant much

[098-185]good, was not likely to mean much harm. That somebody should pretend

[098-186]to love her, to which pretence she might reply by a pretence of

[098-187]friendship,--this was the little excitement which she craved, and by

[098-188]which she had once flattered herself that something of an elysium

[098-189]might yet be created for her. Mr. Dobbs Broughton had unreasonably

[098-190]expressed a dislike to this innocent amusement,--very unreasonably,

[098-191]knowing, as he ought to have known, that he himself did so very

[098-192]little towards providing the necessary elysium by any qualities of

[098-193]his own. For a few weeks this interference from her husband had

[098-194]enhanced the amusement, giving an additional excitement to the game.

[098-195]She felt herself to be a woman misunderstood and ill-used; and to

[098-196]some women there is nothing so charming as a little mild ill-usage,

[098-197]which does not interfere with their creature comforts, with their

[098-198]clothes, or their carriage, or their sham jewels; but suffices to

[098-199]afford them the indulgence of a grievance. Of late, however, Mr.

[098-200]Dobbs Broughton had become a little too rough in his language, and

[098-201]things had gone uncomfortably. She suspected that Conway Dalrymple

[098-202]was not the only cause of all this. She had an idea that Mr.

[098-203]Musselboro and Mrs. Van Siever had it in their power to make

[098-204]themselves unpleasant, and that they were exercising this power. Of

[098-205]his business in the City her husband never spoke to her, nor she to

[098-206]him. Her own fortune had been very small, some couple of thousand

[098-207]pounds or so, and she conceived that she had no pretext on which she

[098-208]could, unasked, interrogate him about his money. She had no knowledge

[098-209]that marriage of itself had given her the right to such interference;

[098-210]and had such knowledge been hers she would have had no desire to

[098-211]interfere. She hoped that the carriage and sham jewels would be

[098-212]continued to her; but she did not know how to frame any question on

[098-213]the subject. Touching the other difficulty,--the Conway Dalrymple

[098-214]difficulty,--she had her ideas. The tenderness of her friendship had

[098-215]been trodden upon and outraged by the rough foot of an overbearing

[098-216]husband, and she was ill-used. She would obey. It was becoming to her

[098-217]as a wife that she should submit. She would give up Conway Dalrymple,

[098-218]and would induce him,--in spite of his violent attachment to

[098-219]herself,--to take a wife. She herself would choose a wife for him.

[098-220]She herself would, with suicidal hands, destroy the romance of her

[098-221]own life, since an overbearing, brutal husband demanded that it

[098-222]should be destroyed. She would sacrifice her own feelings, and do all

[098-223]in her power to bring Conway Dalrymple and Clara Van Siever together.

[098-224]If, after that, some poet did not immortalize her friendship in

[098-225]Byronic verse, she certainly would not get her due. Perhaps Conway

[098-226]Dalrymple would himself become a poet in order that this might be

[098-227]done properly. For it must be understood that, though she expected

[098-228]Conway Dalrymple to marry, she expected also that he should be

[098-229]Byronically wretched after his marriage on account of his love for

[098-230]herself.
