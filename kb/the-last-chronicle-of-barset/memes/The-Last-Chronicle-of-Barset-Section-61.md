[061-001]"And what is to be done?"

[061-002]"He will write to the dean."

[061-003]"I am glad of that."

[061-004]"And he will be at Barchester."

[061-005]"Thank God for that."

[061-006]"But not as my lawyer."

[061-007]"Nevertheless, I thank God that some one will be there who will know

[061-008]how to give you assistance and advice."

[061-009]CHAPTER XXXIII.

[061-010]THE PLUMSTEAD FOXES.

[061-011]The letters had been brought into the breakfast-parlour at Plumstead

[061-012]Rectory one morning, and the archdeacon had inspected them all, and

[061-013]then thrown over to his wife her share of the spoil,--as was the

[061-014]custom of the house. As to most of Mrs. Grantly's letters, he never

[061-015]made any further inquiry. To letters from her sister, the dean's

[061-016]wife, he was profoundly indifferent, and rarely made any inquiry

[061-017]as to those which were directed in writing with which he was not

[061-018]familiar. But there were others as to which, as Mrs. Grantly knew, he

[061-019]would be sure to ask her questions if she did not show them. No note

[061-020]ever reached her from Lady Hartletop as to which he was not curious,

[061-021]and yet Lady Hartletop's notes very seldom contained much that was

[061-022]of interest. Now, on this morning, there came a letter which, as

[061-023]a matter of course, Mrs. Grantly read at breakfast, and which,

[061-024]she knew, would not be allowed to disappear without inquiry. Nor,

[061-025]indeed, did she wish to keep the letter from her husband. It was

[061-026]too important to be so treated. But she would have been glad to

[061-027]gain time to think in what spirit she would discuss the contents

[061-028]of the letter,--if only such time might be allowed to her. But the

[061-029]archdeacon would allow her no time. "What does Henry say, my dear?"

[061-030]he asked, before the breakfast things had been taken away.

[061-031]"What does he say? Well; he says--. I'll give you his letter to read

[061-032]by-and-by."

[061-033]"And why not now?"

[061-034]"I thought I'd read it again myself, first."

[061-035]"But if you have read it, I suppose you know what's in it?"

[061-036]"Not very clearly, as yet. However, there it is." She knew very well

[061-037]that when she had once been asked for it, no peace would be allowed

[061-038]to her till he had seen it. And, alas! there was not much probability

[061-039]of peace in the house for some time after he should see it.

[061-040]The archdeacon read the three or four first lines in silence,--and

[061-041]then he burst out. "He has, has he? Then, by heavens--"

[061-042]"Stop, dearest; stop," said his wife, rising from her chair and

[061-043]coming over to him; "do not say words which you will surely repent."

[061-044]"I will say words which shall make him repent. He shall never have

[061-045]from me a son's portion."

[061-046]"Do not make threats in anger. Do not! You know that it is wrong. If

[061-047]he has offended you, say nothing about it,--even to yourself,--as to

[061-048]threatened punishments, till you can judge of the offence in cool

[061-049]blood."

[061-050]"I am cool," said the archdeacon.

[061-051]"No, my dear; no; you are angry. And you have not even read his

[061-052]letter through."

[061-053]"I will read his letter."

[061-054]"You will see that the marriage is not imminent. It may be that even

[061-055]yet it will never take place. The young lady has refused him."

[061-056]"Psha!"

[061-057]"You will see that she has done so. He tells us so himself. And she

[061-058]has behaved very properly."

[061-059]"Why has she refused him?"

[061-060]"There can be no doubt about the reason. She feels that, with this

[061-061]charge hanging over her father, she is not in a position to become

[061-062]the wife of any gentleman. You cannot but respect her for that."

[061-063]Then the archdeacon finished his son's letter, uttering sundry

[061-064]interjections and ejaculations as he did so.

[061-065]"Of course; I knew it. I understood it all," he said at last. "I've

[061-066]nothing to do with the girl. I don't care whether she be good or

[061-067]bad."

[061-068]"Oh, my dear!"

[061-069]"I care not at all,--with reference to my own concerns. Of course

[061-070]I would wish that the daughter of a neighbouring clergyman,--that

[061-071]the daughter of any neighbour,--that the daughter of any one

[061-072]whatsoever,--should be good rather than bad. But as regards Henry and

[061-073]me, and our mutual relation, her goodness can make no difference. Let

[061-074]her be another Grizel, and still such a marriage must estrange him

[061-075]from me, and me from him."

[061-076]"But she has refused him."

[061-077]"Yes; and what does he say?--that he has told her that he will not

[061-078]accept her refusal. Of course we know what it all means. The girl

[061-079]I am not judging. The girl I will not judge. But my own son, to

[061-080]whom I have ever done a father's duty with a father's affectionate

[061-081]indulgence,--him I will judge. I have warned him, and he declares

[061-082]himself to be careless of my warning. I shall take no notice of this

[061-083]letter. I shall neither write to him about it, or speak to him about

[061-084]it. But I charge you to write to him, and tell him that if he does

[061-085]this thing he shall not have a child's portion from me. It is not

[061-086]that I will shorten that which would have been his; but he shall

[061-087]have--nothing!" Then, having spoken these words with a solemnity

[061-088]which for the moment silenced his wife, he got up and left the

[061-089]room. He left the room and closed the door, but, before he had gone

[061-090]half the length of the hall towards his own study, he returned and

[061-091]addressed his wife again. "You understand my instructions, I hope?"

[061-092]"What instructions?"

[061-093]"That you write to Henry and tell him what I say."

[061-094]"I will speak again to you about it by-and-by."

[061-095]"I will speak no more about it,--not a word more. Let there be not a

[061-096]word more said, but oblige me by doing as I ask you."

[061-097]Then he was again about to leave the room, but she stopped him. "Wait

[061-098]a moment, my dear."

[061-099]"Why should I wait?"

[061-100]"That you may listen to me. Surely you will do that, when I ask you.

[061-101]I will write to Henry, of course, if you bid me; and I will give him

[061-102]your message, whatever it may be; but not to-day, my dear."

[061-103]"Why not to-day?"

[061-104]"Because the sun shall go down upon your wrath before I become its

[061-105]messenger. If you choose to write to-day yourself, I cannot help it.

[061-106]I cannot hinder you. If I am to write to him on your behalf I will

[061-107]take my instructions from you to-morrow morning. When to-morrow

[061-108]morning comes you will not be angry with me because of the delay."

[061-109]The archdeacon was by no means satisfied; but he knew his wife too

[061-110]well, and himself too well, and the world too well, to insist on the

[061-111]immediate gratification of his passion. Over his bosom's mistress

[061-112]he did exercise a certain marital control,--which was, for instance,

[061-113]quite sufficiently fixed to enable him to look down with thorough

[061-114]contempt on such a one as Bishop Proudie; but he was not a despot who

[061-115]could exact a passive obedience to every fantasy. His wife would not

[061-116]have written the letter for him on that day, and he knew very well

[061-117]that she would not do so. He knew also that she was right;--and yet

[061-118]he regretted his want of power. His anger at the present moment was

[061-119]very hot,--so hot that he wished to wreak it. He knew that it would

[061-120]cool before the morrow;--and, no doubt, knew also theoretically, that

[061-121]it would be most fitting that it should cool. But not the less was

[061-122]it a matter of regret to him that so much good hot anger should

[061-123]be wasted, and that he could not have his will of his disobedient

[061-124]son while it lasted. He might, no doubt, have written himself,

[061-125]but to have done so would not have suited him. Even in his anger

[061-126]he could not have written to his son without using the ordinary

[061-127]terms of affection, and in his anger he could not bring himself to

[061-128]use those terms. "You will find that I shall be of the same mind

[061-129]to-morrow,--exactly," he said to his wife. "I have resolved about it

[061-130]long since; and it is not likely that I shall change in a day." Then

[061-131]he went out, about his parish, intending to continue to think of his

[061-132]son's iniquity, so that he might keep his anger hot,--red hot. Then

[061-133]he remembered that the evening would come, and that he would say his

[061-134]prayers; and he shook his head in regret,--in a regret of which he

[061-135]was only half conscious, though it was very keen, and which he did

[061-136]not attempt to analyze,--as he reflected that his rage would hardly

[061-137]be able to survive that ordeal. How common with us it is to repine

[061-138]that the devil is not stronger over us than he is.

[061-139]The archdeacon, who was a very wealthy man, had purchased a property

[061-140]in Plumstead, contiguous to the glebe-land, and had thus come to

[061-141]exercise in the parish the double duty of rector and squire. And

[061-142]of this estate in Barsetshire, which extended beyond the confines

[061-143]of Plumstead into the neighbouring parish of Eiderdown, and which

[061-144]comprised also an outlying farm in the parish of Stogpingum,--Stoke

[061-145]Pinguium would have been the proper name had not barbarous Saxon

[061-146]tongues clipped it of its proper proportions,--he had always intended

[061-147]that his son Henry should enjoy the inheritance. There was other

[061-148]property, both in land and in money, for his elder son, and other

[061-149]again for the maintenance of his wife,--for the archdeacon's father

[061-150]had been for many years Bishop of Barchester, and such a bishopric

[061-151]as that of Barchester had been in those days was worth money. Of

[061-152]his intention in this respect he had never spoken in plain language

[061-153]to either of his sons; but the major had for the last year or two

[061-154]enjoyed the shooting of the Barsetshire covers, giving what orders

[061-155]he pleased about the game; and the father had encouraged him to

[061-156]take something like the management of the property into his hands.

[061-157]There might be some fifteen hundred acres of it altogether, and the

[061-158]archdeacon had rejoiced over it with his wife scores of times, saying

[061-159]that there was many a squire in the county whose elder son would

[061-160]never find himself half so well placed as would his own younger son.

[061-161]Now there was a string of narrow woods called Plumstead Coppices

[061-162]which ran from a point near the church right across the parish,

[061-163]dividing the archdeacon's land from the Ullathorne estate, and these

[061-164]coppices, or belts of woodland, belonged to the archdeacon. On the

[061-165]morning of which we are speaking, the archdeacon, mounted on his cob,

[061-166]still thinking of his son's iniquity and of his own fixed resolve to

[061-167]punish him as he had said that he would punish him, opened with his

[061-168]whip a woodland gate, from which a green muddy lane led through the

[061-169]trees up to the house of his gamekeeper. The man's wife was ill, and

[061-170]in his ordinary way of business the archdeacon was about to call and

[061-171]ask after her health. At the door of the cottage he found the man,

[061-172]who was woodman as well as gamekeeper, and was responsible for fences

[061-173]and faggots, as well as for foxes and pheasants' eggs.

[061-174]"How's Martha, Flurry?" said the archdeacon.

[061-175]"Thanking your reverence, she be a deal improved since the mistress

[061-176]was here,--last Tuesday it was, I think."

[061-177]"I'm glad of that. It was only rheumatism, I suppose?"

[061-178]"Just a tich of fever with it, your reverence, the doctor said."

[061-179]"Tell her I was asking after it. I won't mind getting down to-day, as

[061-180]I am rather busy. She has had what she wanted from the house?"

[061-181]"The mistress has been very good in that way. She always is, God

[061-182]bless her!"

[061-183]"Good-day to you, Flurry. I'll ask Mr. Sims to come and read to her

[061-184]a bit this afternoon, or to-morrow morning." The archdeacon kept two

[061-185]curates, and Mr. Sims was one of them.
