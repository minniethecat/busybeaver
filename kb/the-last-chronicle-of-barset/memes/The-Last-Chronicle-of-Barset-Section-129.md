[129-001]"I don't quite follow you," said the major. "But what I want you to

[129-002]do is to give me your consent to visit your daughter; and I want Mrs.

[129-003]Crawley to write to Grace and tell her that it's all right." Mrs.

[129-004]Crawley was quite sure that it was all right, and was ready to sit

[129-005]down and write the letter that moment, if her husband would permit

[129-006]her to do so.

[129-007]"I am sorry that I have not been explicit," said Mr. Crawley, "but I

[129-008]will endeavour to make myself more plainly intelligible. My daughter,

[129-009]sir, is so circumstanced in reference to her father, that I, as her

[129-010]father and as a gentleman, cannot encourage any man to make a tender

[129-011]to her of his hand."

[129-012]"But I have made up my mind about all that."

[129-013]"And I, sir, have made up mine. I dare not tell my girl that I think

[129-014]she will do well to place her hand in yours. A lady, when she does

[129-015]that, should feel at least that her hand is clean."

[129-016]"It is the cleanest and the sweetest and the fairest hand in

[129-017]Barsetshire," said the major. Mrs. Crawley could not restrain

[129-018]herself, but running up to him, took his hand in hers and kissed it.

[129-019]"There is unfortunately a stain, which is vicarial," began

[129-020]Mr. Crawley, sustaining up to that point his voice with Roman

[129-021]fortitude,--with a fortitude which would have been Roman had it not

[129-022]at that moment broken down under the pressure of human feeling. He

[129-023]could keep it up no longer, but continued his speech with broken

[129-024]sobs, and with a voice altogether changed in its tone,--rapid now,

[129-025]whereas it had before been slow,--natural, whereas it had hitherto

[129-026]been affected,--human, whereas it had hitherto been Roman. "Major

[129-027]Grantly," he said, "I am sore beset; but what can I say to you? My

[129-028]darling is as pure as the light of day,--only that she is soiled with

[129-029]my impurity. She is fit to grace the house of the best gentleman in

[129-030]England, had I not made her unfit."

[129-031]"She shall grace mine," said the major. "By God, she

[129-032]shall!--to-morrow, if she'll have me." Mrs. Crawley, who was standing

[129-033]beside him, again raised his hand and kissed it.

[129-034]"It may not be so. As I began by saying,--or rather strove to say,

[129-035]for I have been overtaken by weakness, and cannot speak my mind,--I

[129-036]cannot claim authority over my child as would another man. How can I

[129-037]exercise authority from between a prison's bars?"

[129-038]"She would obey your slightest wish," said Mrs. Crawley.

[129-039]"I could express no wish," said he. "But I know my girl, and I am

[129-040]sure that she will not consent to take infamy with her into the house

[129-041]of the man who loves her."

[129-042]"There will be no infamy," said the major. "Infamy! I tell you that

[129-043]I shall be proud of the connexion."

[129-044]"You, sir, are generous in your prosperity. We will strive to be

[129-045]at least just in our adversity. My wife and children are to be

[129-046]pitied,--because of the husband and the father."

[129-047]"No!" said Mrs. Crawley. "I will not hear that said without denying

[129-048]it."

[129-049]"But they must take their lot as it has been given to them,"

[129-050]continued he. "Such a position in life as that which you have

[129-051]proposed to bestow upon my child would be to her, as regards

[129-052]human affairs, great elevation. And from what I have heard,--I

[129-053]may be permitted to add also from what I now learn by personal

[129-054]experience,--such a marriage would be laden with fair promise of

[129-055]future happiness. But if you ask my mind, I think that my child is

[129-056]not free to make it. You, sir, have many relatives, who are not in

[129-057]love, as you are, all of whom would be affected by the stain of my

[129-058]disgrace. You have a daughter, to whom all your solicitude is due.

[129-059]No one should go to your house as your second wife who cannot feel

[129-060]that she will serve your child. My daughter would feel that she was

[129-061]bringing an injury upon the babe. I cannot bid her do this,--and I

[129-062]will not. Nor do I believe that she would do so if I bade her." Then

[129-063]he turned his chair round, and sat with his face to the wall, wiping

[129-064]away the tears with a tattered handkerchief.

[129-065]Mrs. Crawley led the major away to the further window, and there

[129-066]stood looking up into his face. It need hardly be said that they also

[129-067]were crying. Whose eyes could have been dry after such a scene,--upon

[129-068]hearing such words? "You had better go," said Mrs. Crawley. "I know

[129-069]him so well. You had better go."

[129-070]"Mrs. Crawley," he said, whispering to her, "if I ever desert her,

[129-071]may all that I love desert me! But you will help me?"

[129-072]"You would want no help, were it not for this trouble."

[129-073]"But you will help me?"

[129-074]Then she paused a moment. "I can do nothing," she said, "but what he

[129-075]bids me."

[129-076]"You will trust me, at any rate?" said the major.

[129-077]"I do trust you," she replied. Then he went without saying a word

[129-078]further to Mr. Crawley. As soon as he was gone, the wife went over to

[129-079]her husband, and put her arm gently round his neck as he was sitting.

[129-080]For a while the husband took no notice of his wife's caress, but sat

[129-081]motionless, with his face still turned to the wall. Then she spoke

[129-082]to him a word or two, telling him that their visitor was gone. "My

[129-083]child!" he said. "My poor child! my darling! She has found grace in

[129-084]this man's sight; but even of that has her father robbed her! The

[129-085]Lord has visited upon the children the sins of the father, and will

[129-086]do so to the third and fourth generation."

[129-087]CHAPTER LXIV.

[129-088]THE TRAGEDY IN HOOK COURT.

[129-089][Illustration]

[129-090]Conway Dalrymple had hurried out of the room in Mrs. Broughton's

[129-091]house in which he had been painting Jael and Sisera, thinking that

[129-092]it would be better to meet an angry and perhaps tipsy husband on

[129-093]the stairs, than it would be either to wait for him till he should

[129-094]make his way into his wife's room, or to hide away from him with

[129-095]the view of escaping altogether from so disagreeable an encounter.

[129-096]He had no fear of the man. He did not think that there would be any

[129-097]violence,--nor, as regarded himself, did he much care if there was

[129-098]to be violence. But he felt that he was bound, as far as it might

[129-099]be possible, to screen the poor woman from the ill effects of her

[129-100]husband's temper and condition. He was, therefore, prepared to stop

[129-101]Broughton on the stairs, and to use some force in arresting him on

[129-102]his way, should he find the man to be really intoxicated. But he

[129-103]had not descended above a stair or two before he was aware that

[129-104]the man below him, whose step had been heard in the hall, was

[129-105]not intoxicated, and that he was not Dobbs Broughton. It was Mr.

[129-106]Musselboro.

[129-107]"It is you, is it?" said Conway. "I thought it was Broughton." Then

[129-108]he looked into the man's face and saw that he was ashy pale. All that

[129-109]appearance of low-bred jauntiness which used to belong to him seemed

[129-110]to have been washed out of him. His hair had forgotten to curl,

[129-111]his gloves had been thrown aside, and even his trinkets were out

[129-112]of sight. "What has happened?" said Conway. "What is the matter?

[129-113]Something is wrong." Then it occurred to him that Musselboro had been

[129-114]sent to the house to tell the wife of the husband's ruin.

[129-115]"The servant told me that I should find you upstairs," said

[129-116]Musselboro.

[129-117]"Yes; I have been painting here. For some time past I have been doing

[129-118]a picture of Miss Van Siever. Mrs. Van Siever has been here to-day."

[129-119]Conway thought that this information would produce some strong effect

[129-120]on Clara's proposed husband; but he did not seem to regard the matter

[129-121]of the picture nor the mention of Miss Van Siever's name.

[129-122]"She knows nothing of it?" said he. "She doesn't know yet?"

[129-123]"Know what?" asked Conway. "She knows that her husband has lost

[129-124]money."

[129-125]"Dobbs has--destroyed himself."

[129-126]"What!"

[129-127]"Blew his brains out this morning just inside the entrance at Hook

[129-128]Court. The horror of drink was on him, and he stood just in the

[129-129]pathway and shot himself. Bangles was standing at the top of their

[129-130]vaults and saw him do it. I don't think Bangles will ever be a man

[129-131]again. O Lord! I shall never get over it myself. The body was there

[129-132]when I went in." Then Musselboro sank back against the wall of the

[129-133]staircase, and stared at Dalrymple as though he still saw before him

[129-134]the terrible sight of which he had just spoken.

[129-135]Dalrymple seated himself on the stairs and strove to bring his mind

[129-136]to bear on the tale which he had just heard. What was he to do, and

[129-137]how was that poor woman upstairs to be informed? "You came here

[129-138]intending to tell her," he said, in a whisper. He feared every moment

[129-139]that Mrs. Broughton would appear on the stairs, and learn from a word

[129-140]or two what had happened without any hint to prepare her for the

[129-141]catastrophe.

[129-142]"I thought you would be here. I knew you were doing the picture. He

[129-143]knew it. He'd had a letter to say so,--one of those anonymous ones."

[129-144]"But that didn't influence him?"

[129-145]"I don't think it was that," said Musselboro. "He meant to have had

[129-146]it out with her; but it wasn't that as brought this about. Perhaps

[129-147]you didn't know that he was clean ruined?"

[129-148]"She had told me."

[129-149]"Then she knew it?"

[129-150]"Oh, yes; she knew that. Mrs. Van Siever had told her. Poor creature!

[129-151]How are we to break this to her?"

[129-152]"You and she are very thick," said Musselboro. "I suppose you'll do

[129-153]it best." By this time they were in the drawing-room, and the door

[129-154]was closed. Dalrymple had put his hand on the other man's arm, and

[129-155]had led him downstairs, out of reach of hearing from the room above.

[129-156]"You'll tell her,--won't you?" said Musselboro. Then Dalrymple tried

[129-157]to think what loving female friend there was who could break the news

[129-158]to the unfortunate woman. He knew of the Van Sievers, and he knew

[129-159]of the Demolines, and he almost knew that there was no other woman

[129-160]within reach whom he was entitled to regard as closely connected with

[129-161]Mrs. Broughton. He was well aware that the anonymous letter of which

[129-162]Musselboro had just spoken had come from Miss Demolines, and he could

[129-163]not go there for sympathy and assistance. Nor could he apply to Mrs.

[129-164]Van Siever after what had passed this morning. To Clara Van Siever

[129-165]he would have applied, but that it was impossible he should reach

[129-166]Clara except through her mother. "I suppose I had better go to her,"

[129-167]he said, after a while. And then he went, leaving Musselboro in the

[129-168]drawing-room. "I'm so bad with it," said Musselboro, "that I really

[129-169]don't know how I shall ever go up that court again."

[129-170]Conway Dalrymple made his way up the stairs with very slow steps,

[129-171]and as he did so he could not but think seriously of the nature of

[129-172]his friendship with this woman, and could not but condemn himself

[129-173]heartily for the folly and iniquity of his own conduct. Scores of

[129-174]times he had professed his love to her with half-expressed words,

[129-175]intended to mean nothing, as he said to himself when he tried to

[129-176]excuse himself, but enough to turn her head, even if they did not

[129-177]reach her heart. Now, this woman was a widow, and it came to be his

[129-178]duty to tell her that she was so. What if she should claim from

[129-179]him now the love which he had so often proffered to her! It was

[129-180]not that he feared that she would claim anything from him at this

[129-181]moment,--neither now, nor to-morrow, nor the next day,--but the agony

[129-182]of the present meeting would produce others in which there would be

[129-183]some tenderness mixed with the agony; and so from one meeting to

[129-184]another the thing would progress. Dalrymple knew well enough how such

[129-185]things might progress. But in this danger before him, it was not of

[129-186]himself that he was thinking, but of her. How could he assist her at

[129-187]such a time without doing her more injury than benefit? And, if he

[129-188]did not assist her, who would do so? He knew her to be heartless; but

[129-189]even heartless people have hearts which can be touched and almost

[129-190]broken by certain sorrows. Her heart would not be broken by her

[129-191]husband's death, but it would become very sore if she were utterly

[129-192]neglected. He was now at the door, with his hand on the lock, and was

[129-193]wondering why she should remain so long within without making herself

[129-194]heard. Then he opened it, and found her seated in a lounging-chair,

[129-195]with her back to the door, and he could see that she had a volume of

[129-196]a novel in her hand. He understood it all. She was pretending to be

[129-197]indifferent to her husband's return. He walked up to her, thinking

[129-198]that she would recognize his step; but she made no sign of turning

[129-199]towards him. He saw the motion of her hair over the back of the chair

[129-200]as she affected to make herself luxuriously comfortable. She was

[129-201]striving to let her husband see that she cared nothing for him, or

[129-202]for his condition, or for his jealousy, if he were jealous,--or even

[129-203]for his ruin. "Mrs. Broughton," he said, when he was close to her.

[129-204]Then she jumped up quickly, and turned round, facing him. "Where is

[129-205]Dobbs?" she said. "Where is Broughton?"
