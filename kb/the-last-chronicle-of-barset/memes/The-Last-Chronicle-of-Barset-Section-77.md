[077-001]"What has the bishop ordered, papa?"

[077-002]"Nothing at all. It is she who does it. He has given no opinion about

[077-003]it. Of course not. He has none to give. It is the woman. You go and

[077-004]tell her from me that in such a matter I will not obey the word of

[077-005]any woman living. Go at once, when I tell you."

[077-006]Then she knew that her father's mind was wandering, and she knelt

[077-007]down by the bedside, still holding his hand.

[077-008]"Grace," he said.

[077-009]"Yes, papa, I am here."

[077-010]"Why do you not do what I tell you?" And he sat upright in his bed.

[077-011]"I suppose you are afraid of the woman?"

[077-012]"I should be afraid of her, dear papa."

[077-013]"I was not afraid of her. When she spoke to me, I would have nothing

[077-014]to say to her;--not a word; not a word;--not a word." As he said this

[077-015]he waved his hands about. "But as for him,--if it must be, it must. I

[077-016]know I'm not fit for it. Of course I am not. Who is? But what has he

[077-017]ever done that he should be a dean? I beat him at everything; almost

[077-018]at everything. He got the Newdegate, and that was about all. Upon my

[077-019]word I think that was all."

[077-020]"But Dr. Arabin loves you truly, dear papa."

[077-021]"Love me! psha! Does he ever come here to tea, as he used to do? No!

[077-022]I remember buttering toast for him down on my knees before the fire,

[077-023]because he liked it,--and keeping all the cream for him. He should

[077-024]have had my heart's blood if he wanted it. But now;--look at his

[077-025]books, Grace. It's the outside of them he cares about. They are all

[077-026]gilt, but I doubt if he ever reads. As for her,--I will not allow

[077-027]any woman to tell me my duty. No;--by my Maker; not even your mother,

[077-028]who is the best of women. And as for her, with her little husband

[077-029]dangling at her apron-strings, as a call-whistle to be blown into

[077-030]when she pleases,--that she should dare to teach me my duty! No! The

[077-031]men in the jury-box may decide it how they will. If they can believe

[077-032]a plain story, let them! If not,--let them do as they please. I am

[077-033]ready to bear it all."

[077-034]"Dear papa, you are tired. Will you not try to sleep?"

[077-035]"Tell Mrs. Proudie what I say; and as for Arabin's money, I took

[077-036]it. I know I took it. What would you have had me do? Shall I--see

[077-037]them--all--starve?" Then he fell back upon his bed and did sleep.

[077-038]The next day he was better, and insisted upon getting out of bed,

[077-039]and on sitting in his old arm-chair over the fire. And the Greek

[077-040]books were again had out; and Grace, not at all unwillingly, was

[077-041]put through her facings. "If you don't take care, my dear," he said,

[077-042]"Jane will beat you yet. She understands the force of the verbs

[077-043]better than you do."

[077-044]"I am very glad that she is doing so well, papa. I am sure I shall

[077-045]not begrudge her her superiority."

[077-046]"Ah, but you should begrudge it her!" Jane was sitting by at the

[077-047]time, and the two sisters were holding each other by the hand.

[077-048]"Always to be best;--always to be in advance of others. That should

[077-049]be your motto."

[077-050]"But we can't both be best, papa," said Jane.

[077-051]"You can both strive to be best. But Grace has the better voice. I

[077-052]remember when I knew the whole of the Antigone by heart. You girls

[077-053]should see which can learn it first."

[077-054]"It would take such a long time," said Jane.

[077-055]"You are young, and what can you do better with your leisure hours?

[077-056]Fie, Jane! I did not expect that from you. When I was learning it I

[077-057]had eight or nine pupils, and read an hour a day with each of them.

[077-058]But I think that nobody works now as they used to work then. Where is

[077-059]your mamma? Tell her I think I could get out as far as Mrs. Cox's, if

[077-060]she would help me to dress." Soon after this he was in bed again, and

[077-061]his head was wandering; but still they knew that he was better than

[077-062]he had been.

[077-063]"You are more of a comfort to your papa than I can be," said Mrs.

[077-064]Crawley to her eldest daughter that night as they sat together, when

[077-065]everybody else was in bed.

[077-066]"Do not say that, mamma. Papa does not think so."

[077-067]"I cannot read Greek plays to him as you can do. I can only nurse him

[077-068]in his illness and endeavour to do my duty. Do you know, Grace, that

[077-069]I am beginning to fear that he half doubts me?"

[077-070]"Oh, mamma!"

[077-071]"That he half doubts me, and is half afraid of me. He does not think

[077-072]as he used to do, that I am altogether, heart and soul, on his side.

[077-073]I can see it in his eye as he watches me. He thinks that I am tired

[077-074]of him,--tired of his sufferings, tired of his poverty, tired of the

[077-075]evil which men say of him. I am not sure but what he thinks that I

[077-076]suspect him."

[077-077]"Of what, mamma?"

[077-078]"Of general unfitness for the work he has to do. The feeling is not

[077-079]strong as yet, but I fear that he will teach himself to think that

[077-080]he has an enemy at his hearth,--not a friend. It will be the saddest

[077-081]mistake he ever made."

[077-082]"He told me to-day that you were the best of women. Those were his

[077-083]very words."

[077-084]"Were they, my dear? I am glad at least that he should say so to you.

[077-085]He has been better since you came;--a great deal better. For one day

[077-086]I was frightened; but I am sorry now that I sent for you."

[077-087]"I am so glad, mamma; so very glad."

[077-088]"You were happy there,--and comfortable. And if they were glad to

[077-089]have you, why should I have brought you away?"

[077-090]"But I was not happy;--even though they were very good to me. How

[077-091]could I be happy there when I was thinking of you and papa and Jane

[077-092]here at home? Whatever there is here, I would sooner share it with

[077-093]you than be anywhere else,--while this trouble lasts."

[077-094]"My darling!--it is a great comfort to see you again."

[077-095]"Only that I knew that one less in the house would be a saving to you

[077-096]I should not have gone. When there is unhappiness, people should stay

[077-097]together;--shouldn't they, mamma?" They were sitting quite close to

[077-098]each other, on an old sofa in a small upstairs room, from which a

[077-099]door opened into the larger chamber in which Mr. Crawley was lying.

[077-100]It had been arranged between them that on this night Mrs. Crawley

[077-101]should remain with her husband, and that Grace should go to her bed.

[077-102]It was now past one o'clock, but she was still there, clinging to her

[077-103]mother's side, with her mother's arm drawn round her. "Mamma," she

[077-104]said, when they had both been silent for some ten minutes, "I have

[077-105]got something to tell you."

[Illustration: "Mamma, I've got something to tell you."]

[077-106]"To-night?"

[077-107]"Yes, mamma; to-night, if you will let me."

[077-108]"But you promised that you would go to bed. You were up all last

[077-109]night."

[077-110]"I am not sleepy, mamma."

[077-111]"Of course you shall tell me what you please, dearest. Is it a

[077-112]secret? Is it something I am not to repeat?"

[077-113]"You must say how that ought to be, mamma. I shall not tell it to any

[077-114]one else."

[077-115]"Well, dear?"

[077-116]"Sit comfortably, mamma;--there; like that, and let me have your

[077-117]hand. It's a terrible story to have to tell."

[077-118]"A terrible story, Grace?"

[077-119]"I mean that you must not draw away from me. I shall want to feel

[077-120]that you are quite close to me. Mamma, while I was at Allington,

[077-121]Major Grantly came there."

[077-122]"Did he, my dear?"

[077-123]"Yes, mamma."

[077-124]"Did he know them before?"

[077-125]"No, mamma; not at the Small House. But he came there--to see me. He

[077-126]asked me--to be his wife. Don't move, mamma."

[077-127]"My darling child! I won't move, dearest. Well; and what did you

[077-128]say to him? God bless him, at any rate. May God bless him, because

[077-129]he has seen with a true eye, and felt with a noble instinct. It is

[077-130]something, Grace, to have been wooed by such a man at such a time."

[077-131]"Mamma, it did make me feel proud; it did."

[077-132]"You had known him well before,--of course? I knew that you and he

[077-133]were friends, Grace."

[077-134]"Yes, we were friends. I always liked him. I used not to know what to

[077-135]think about him. Miss Anne Prettyman told me that it would be so; and

[077-136]once before I thought so myself."

[077-137]"And had you made up your mind what to say to him?"

[077-138]"Yes, I had then. But I did not say it."

[077-139]"Did not say what you had made up your mind to say?"

[077-140]"That was before all this had happened to papa."

[077-141]"I understand you, dearest."

[077-142]"When Miss Anne Prettyman told me that I should be ready with my

[077-143]answer, and when I saw that Miss Prettyman herself used to let him

[077-144]come to the house and seemed to wish that I should see him when he

[077-145]came, and when he once was--so very gentle and kind, and when he said

[077-146]that he wanted me to love Edith,-- Oh, mamma!"

[077-147]"Yes, darling, I know. Of course you loved him."

[077-148]"Yes, mamma. And I do love him. How could one not love him?"

[077-149]"I love him,--for loving you."

[077-150]"But, mamma, one is bound not to do a harm to any one that one loves.

[077-151]So when he came to Allington I told him that I could not be his

[077-152]wife."

[077-153]"Did you, my dear?"

[077-154]"Yes; I did. Was I not right? Ought I to go to him to bring a

[077-155]disgrace upon all the family, just because he is so good that he asks

[077-156]me? Shall I injure him because he wants to do me a service?"

[077-157]"If he loves you, Grace, the service he will require will be your

[077-158]love in return."

[077-159]"That is all very well, mamma,--in books; but I do not believe it in

[077-160]reality. Being in love is very nice, and in poetry they make it out

[077-161]to be everything. But I do not think I should make Major Grantly

[077-162]happy if when I became his wife his own father and mother would not

[077-163]see him. I know I should be so wretched, myself, that I could not

[077-164]live."

[077-165]"But would it be so?"

[077-166]"Yes;--I think it would. And the archdeacon is very rich, and can

[077-167]leave all his money away from Major Grantly if he pleases. Think what

[077-168]I should feel if I were the cause of Edith losing her fortune!"

[077-169]"But why do you suppose these terrible things?"

[077-170]"I have a reason for supposing them. This must be a secret. Miss Anne

[077-171]Prettyman wrote to me."

[077-172]"I wish Miss Anne Prettyman's hand had been in the fire."

[077-173]"No, mamma; no; she was right. Would not I have wished, do you think,

[077-174]to have learned all the truth about the matter before I answered him?

[077-175]Besides, it made no difference. I could have made no other answer

[077-176]while papa is under such a terrible ban. It is no time for us to

[077-177]think of being in love. We have got to love each other. Isn't it

[077-178]so, mamma?" The mother did not answer in words, but slipping down

[077-179]on her knees before her child threw her arms round her girl's body

[077-180]in a close embrace. "Dear mamma; dearest mamma; this is what I

[077-181]wanted;--that you should love me!"

[077-182]"Love you, my angel!"

[077-183]"And trust me;--and that we should understand each other, and stand

[077-184]close by each other. We can do so much to comfort one another;--but

[077-185]we cannot comfort other people."
