## Overview

Hogglestock is a poor rural parish in the fictional county of Barsetshire, serving as the ecclesiastical living of the Reverend Josiah Crawley. The parish is characterised by its poverty — Crawley's meagre stipend is insufficient to support his family, and the living itself offers little prospect of advancement. The physical and economic conditions of Hogglestock form the backdrop to Crawley's moral struggles and his family's hardships (002-061–002-066).

## Significance

Hogglestock represents the economic realities of the Victorian church hierarchy. Unlike the comfortable livings of Plumstead or Framley, Hogglestock embodies the precarious existence of the lower clergy. The contrast between the wealth of Barchester's cathedral close and the poverty of Hogglestock's parsonage is one of Trollope's sharpest commentaries on ecclesiastical inequality (002-110–002-116). The parish's remote and humble character isolates Crawley from potential allies, intensifying the drama of his false accusation.
