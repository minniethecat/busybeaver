[132-001]"At any rate you would hardly choose to remain here," said Clara.

[132-002]"But I hope she'll let me have my own things. A great many of them

[132-003]are altogether my own. I know there's a law that a woman may have her

[132-004]own things, even though her husband has,--done what poor Dobbs did.

[132-005]And I think she was hard upon me about the mourning. They never do

[132-006]mind giving credit for such things as that, and though there is a

[132-007]bill due to Mrs. Morell now, she has had a deal of Dobbs's money."

[132-008]Clara promised her that she should have mourning to her heart's

[132-009]content. "I will see to that myself," she said.

[132-010]Presently there was a knock at the door, and the discreet

[132-011]head-servant beckoned Clara out of the room. "You are not going

[132-012]away," said Mrs. Broughton. Clara promised her that she would not

[132-013]go without coming back again. "He will be here soon, I suppose, and

[132-014]perhaps you had better see him; though, for the matter of that,

[132-015]perhaps you had better not, because he is so much cut up about poor

[132-016]Dobbs." The servant had come up to tell Clara that the "he" in

[132-017]question was at the present moment waiting for her below stairs.

[132-018]The first words which passed between Dalrymple and Clara had

[132-019]reference to the widow. He told her what he had learned in the

[132-020]City,--that Broughton's property had never been great, and that

[132-021]his personal liabilities at the time of his death were supposed to

[132-022]be small. But he had fallen lately altogether into the hands of

[132-023]Musselboro, who, though penniless himself in the way of capital,

[132-024]was backed by the money of Mrs. Van Siever. There was no doubt that

[132-025]Broughton had destroyed himself in the manner told by Musselboro,

[132-026]but the opinion in the City was that he had done so rather through

[132-027]the effects of drink than because of his losses. As to the widow,

[132-028]Dalrymple thought that Mrs. Van Siever, or nominally, perhaps,

[132-029]Musselboro, might be induced to settle an annuity on her, if she

[132-030]would give up everything quietly. "I doubt whether your mother is

[132-031]not responsible for everything Broughton owed when he died,--for

[132-032]everything, that is, in the way of business; and if so, Mrs.

[132-033]Broughton will certainly have a claim upon the estate." It occurred

[132-034]to Dalrymple once or twice that he was talking to Clara about Mrs.

[132-035]Van Siever as though he and Clara were more closely bound together

[132-036]than were Clara and her mother; but Clara seemed to take this in good

[132-037]part, and was as solicitous as was he himself in the matter of Mrs.

[132-038]Broughton's interest.

[132-039]Then the discreet head-servant knocked and told them that Mrs.

[132-040]Broughton was very anxious to see Mr. Dalrymple, but that Miss

[132-041]Van Siever was on no account to go away. She was up, and in her

[132-042]dressing-gown, and had gone into the sitting-room. "I will come

[132-043]directly," said Dalrymple, and the discreet head-servant retired.

[132-044]"Clara," said Conway, "I do not know when I may have another chance

[132-045]of asking for an answer to my question. You heard my question?"

[132-046]"Yes, I heard it."

[132-047]"And will you answer it?"

[132-048]"If you wish it, I will."

[132-049]"Of course I wish it. You understood what I said upon the doorstep

[132-050]yesterday?"

[132-051]"I don't think much of that; men say those things so often. What you

[132-052]said before was serious, I suppose?"

[132-053]"Serious! Heavens! do you think that I am joking?"

[132-054]"Mamma wants me to marry Mr. Musselboro."

[132-055]"He is a vulgar brute. It would be impossible."

[132-056]"It is impossible; but mamma is very obstinate. I have no fortune of

[132-057]my own,--not a shilling. She told me to-day that she would turn me

[132-058]into the street. She forbade me to come here, thinking I should meet

[132-059]you; but I came, because I had promised Mrs. Broughton. I am sure

[132-060]that she will never give me one shilling."

[132-061]Dalrymple paused for a moment. It was certainly true that he had

[132-062]regarded Clara Van Siever as an heiress, and had at first been

[132-063]attracted to her because he thought it expedient to marry an heiress.

[132-064]But there had since come something beyond that, and there was perhaps

[132-065]less of regret than most men would have felt as he gave up his golden

[132-066]hopes. He took her into his arms and kissed her, and called her his

[132-067]own. "Now we understand each other," he said.

[132-068]"If you wish it to be so."

[132-069]"I do wish it."

[132-070]"And I shall tell my mother to-day that I am engaged to you,--unless

[132-071]she refuses to see me. Go to Mrs. Broughton now. I feel that we are

[132-072]almost cruel to be thinking of ourselves in this house at such a

[132-073]time." Upon this Dalrymple went, and Clara Van Siever was left to

[132-074]her reflections. She had never before had a lover. She had never had

[132-075]even a friend whom she loved and trusted. Her life had been passed at

[132-076]school till she was nearly twenty, and since then she had been vainly

[132-077]endeavouring to accommodate herself and her feelings to her mother.

[132-078]Now she was about to throw herself into the absolute power of a man

[132-079]who was nearly a stranger to her! But she did love him, as she had

[132-080]never loved any one else;--and then, on the other side, there was Mr.

[132-081]Musselboro!

[132-082]Dalrymple was upstairs for an hour, and Clara did not see him again

[132-083]before he left the house. It was clear to her, from Mrs. Broughton's

[132-084]first words, that Conway had told her what had passed. "Of course

[132-085]I shall never see anything more of either of you now?" said Mrs.

[132-086]Broughton.

[132-087]"I should say that probably you will see a great deal of us both."

[132-088]"There are some people," said Mrs. Broughton, "who can do well for

[132-089]their friends, but can never do well for themselves. I am one of

[132-090]them. I saw at once how great a thing it would be for both of you to

[132-091]bring you two together,--especially for you, Clara; and therefore I

[132-092]did it. I may say that I never had it out of my mind for months past.

[132-093]Poor Dobbs misunderstood what I was doing. God knows how far that may

[132-094]have brought about what has happened."

[132-095]"Oh, Mrs. Broughton!"

[132-096]"Of course he could not be blind to one thing;--nor was I. I mention

[132-097]it now because it is right, but I shall never, never allude to it

[132-098]again. Of course he saw, and I saw, that Conway--was attached to

[132-099]me. Poor Conway meant no harm. I was aware of that. But there was

[132-100]the terrible fact. I knew at once that the only cure for him was a

[132-101]marriage with some girl that he could respect. Admiring you as I do,

[132-102]I immediately resolved on bringing you two together. My dear, I have

[132-103]been successful, and I heartily trust that you may be happier than

[132-104]Maria Broughton."

[132-105]Miss Van Siever knew the woman, understood all the facts, and pitying

[132-106]the condition of the wretched creature, bore all this without a word

[132-107]of rebuke. She scorned to put out her strength against one who was in

[132-108]truth so weak.

[132-109]CHAPTER LXVI.

[132-110]REQUIESCAT IN PACE.

[132-111]Things were very gloomy at the palace. It has been already said that

[132-112]for many days after Dr. Tempest's visit to Barchester the intercourse

[132-113]between the bishop and Mrs. Proudie had not been of a pleasant

[132-114]nature. He had become so silent, so sullen, and so solitary in his

[132-115]ways, that even her courage had been almost cowed, and for a while

[132-116]she had condescended to use gentler measures, with the hope that

[132-117]she might thus bring her lord round to his usual state of active

[132-118]submission; or perhaps, if we strive to do her full justice, we may

[132-119]say of her that her effort was made conscientiously, with the idea of

[132-120]inducing him to do his duty with proper activity. For she was a woman

[132-121]not without a conscience, and by no means indifferent to the real

[132-122]service which her husband, as bishop of the diocese, was bound to

[132-123]render to the affairs of the Church around her. Of her own struggles

[132-124]after personal dominion she was herself unconscious; and no doubt

[132-125]they gave her, when recognized and acknowledged by herself, many

[132-126]stabs to her inner self, of which no single being in the world knew

[132-127]anything. And now, as after a while she failed in producing any

[132-128]amelioration in the bishop's mood, her temper also gave way, and

[132-129]things were becoming very gloomy and very unpleasant.

[132-130]The bishop and his wife were at present alone in the palace. Their

[132-131]married daughter and her husband had left them, and their unmarried

[132-132]daughter was also away. How far the bishop's mood may have produced

[132-133]this solitude in the vast house I will not say. Probably Mrs.

[132-134]Proudie's state of mind may have prevented her from having other

[132-135]guests in the place of those who were gone. She felt herself to be

[132-136]almost disgraced in the eyes of all those around her by her husband's

[132-137]long absence from the common rooms of the house and by his dogged

[132-138]silence at meals. It was better, she thought, that they two should be

[132-139]alone in the palace.

[132-140]Her own efforts to bring him back to something like life, to some

[132-141]activity of mind if not of body, were made constantly; and when she

[132-142]failed, as she did fail day after day, she would go slowly to her own

[132-143]room, and lock her door, and look back in her solitude at all the

[132-144]days of her life. She had agonies in these minutes of which no one

[132-145]near her knew anything. She would seize with her arm the part of

[132-146]the bed near which she would stand, and hold by it, grasping it, as

[132-147]though she were afraid to fall; and then, when it was at the worst

[132-148]with her, she would go to her closet,--a closet that no eyes ever saw

[132-149]unlocked but her own,--and fill for herself and swallow some draught;

[132-150]and then she would sit down with the Bible before her, and read it

[132-151]sedulously. She spent hours every day with her Bible before her,

[132-152]repeating to herself whole chapters, which she almost knew by heart.

[132-153]It cannot be said that she was a bad woman, though she had in her

[132-154]time done an indescribable amount of evil. She had endeavoured to

[132-155]do good, failing partly by ignorance and partly from the effects of

[132-156]an unbridled, ambitious temper. And now, even amidst her keenest

[132-157]sufferings, her ambition was by no means dead. She still longed to

[132-158]rule the diocese by means of her husband,--but was made to pause and

[132-159]hesitate by the unwonted mood that had fallen upon him. Before this,

[132-160]on more than one occasion, and on one very memorable occasion, he

[132-161]had endeavoured to combat her. He had fought with her, striving to

[132-162]put her down. He had failed, and given up the hope of any escape for

[132-163]himself in that direction. On those occasions her courage had never

[132-164]quailed for a moment. While he openly struggled to be master, she

[132-165]could openly struggle to be mistress,--and could enjoy the struggle.

[132-166]But nothing like this moodiness had ever come upon him before.

[132-167]She had yielded to it for many days, striving to coax him by little

[132-168]softnesses of which she herself had been ashamed as she practised

[132-169]them. They had served her nothing, and at last she determined that

[132-170]something else must be done. If only for his sake, to keep some life

[132-171]in him, something else must be done. Were he to continue as he was

[132-172]now, he must give up his diocese, or, at any rate, declare himself

[132-173]too ill to keep the working of it in his own hands. How she hated

[132-174]Mr. Crawley for all the sorrow that he had brought upon her and her

[132-175]house!
