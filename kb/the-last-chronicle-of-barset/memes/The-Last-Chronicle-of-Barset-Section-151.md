[151-001]"Won't you ride with us for the last time?" said Emily Dunstable when

[151-002]Lily gave notice that she would not want the horse on that afternoon.

[151-003]"No; not to-day."

[151-004]"You'll never have another opportunity of riding with Emily

[151-005]Dunstable," said the bride elect;--"at least I hope not."

[151-006]"Even under those circumstances I must refuse, though I would give a

[151-007]guinea to be with you. John Eames is coming here to say good-by."

[151-008]"Oh; then indeed you must not come with us. Lily, what will you say

[151-009]to him?"

[151-010]"Nothing."

[151-011]"Oh, Lily, think of it."

[151-012]"I have thought of it. I have thought of nothing else. I am tired of

[151-013]thinking of it. It is not good to think of anything so much. What

[151-014]does it matter?"

[151-015]"It is very good to have some one to love one better than all the

[151-016]world besides."

[151-017]"I have some one," said Lily, thinking of her mother, but not caring

[151-018]to descend again to the mawkish weakness of talking about her.

[151-019]"Yes; but some one to be always with you, to do everything for you,

[151-020]to be your very own."

[151-021]"It is all very well for you," said Lily, "and I think that Bernard

[151-022]is the luckiest fellow in the world; but it will not do for me. I

[151-023]know in what college I'll take my degree, and I wish they'd let me

[151-024]write the letters after my name as the men do."

[151-025]"What letters, Lily?"

[151-026]"O.M., for Old Maid. I don't see why it shouldn't be as good as B.A.

[151-027]for Bachelor of Arts. It would mean a great deal more."

[151-028]CHAPTER LXXVII.

[151-029]THE SHATTERED TREE.

[151-030]When Mrs. Arabin saw Johnny in the middle of that day, she could

[151-031]hardly give him much encouragement. And yet she felt by no means sure

[151-032]that he might not succeed even yet. Lily had been very positive in

[151-033]her answers, and yet there had been something, either in her words

[151-034]or in the tone of her voice, which had made Mrs. Arabin feel that

[151-035]even Lily was not quite sure of herself. There was still room for

[151-036]relenting. Nothing, however, had been said which could justify her in

[151-037]bidding John Eames simply "to go in and win." "I think he is light of

[151-038]heart," Lily had said. Those were the words which, of all that had

[151-039]been spoken, most impressed themselves on Mrs. Arabin's memory. She

[151-040]would not repeat them to her friend, but she would graft upon them

[151-041]such advice as she had to give him.

[151-042]And this she did, telling him that she thought that perhaps Lily

[151-043]doubted his actual earnestness. "I would marry her this moment," said

[151-044]Johnny. But that was not enough, as Mrs. Arabin knew, to prove his

[151-045]earnestness. Many men, fickle as weathercocks, are ready to marry

[151-046]at the moment,--are ready to marry at the moment, because they are

[151-047]fickle, and think so little about it. "But she hears, perhaps, of

[151-048]your liking other people," said Mrs. Arabin. "I don't care a straw

[151-049]for any other person," said Johnny. "I wonder whether if I was to

[151-050]shut myself up in a cage for six months, it would do any good?" "If

[151-051]she had the keeping of the cage, perhaps it might," said Mrs. Arabin.

[151-052]She had nothing more to say to him on that subject, but to tell him

[151-053]that Miss Dale would expect him that afternoon at half-past five. "I

[151-054]told her that you would come to wish her good-by, and she promised to

[151-055]see you."

[151-056]"I wish she'd say she wouldn't see me. Then there would be some

[151-057]chance," said Johnny.

[151-058]Between him and Mrs. Arabin the parting was very affectionate. She

[151-059]told him how thankful she was for his kindness in coming to her,

[151-060]and how grateful she would ever be,--and the dean also,--for his

[151-061]attention to her. "Remember, Mr. Eames, that you will always be most

[151-062]welcome at the deanery of Barchester. And I do hope that before long

[151-063]you may be there with your wife." And so they parted.

[151-064]He left her at about two, and went to Mr. Toogood's office in Bedford

[151-065]Row. He found his uncle, and the two went out to lunch together in

[151-066]Holborn. Between them there was no word said about Lily Dale, and

[151-067]John was glad to have some other subject in his mind for half an

[151-068]hour. Toogood was full of his triumph about Mr. Crawley and of his

[151-069]successes in Barsetshire. He gave John a long account of his visit

[151-070]to Plumstead, and expressed his opinion that if all clergymen were

[151-071]like the archdeacon there would not be so much room for Dissenters.

[151-072]"I've seen a good many parsons in my time," said Toogood; "but I

[151-073]don't think I ever saw such a one as him. You know he is a clergyman

[151-074]somehow, and he never lets you forget it; but that's about all. Most

[151-075]of 'em are never contented without choking you with their white

[151-076]cravats all the time you're with 'em. As for Crawley himself," Mr.

[151-077]Toogood continued, "he's not like anybody else that ever was born,

[151-078]saint or sinner, parson or layman. I never heard of such a man in all

[151-079]my experience. Though he knew where he got the cheque as well as I

[151-080]know it now, he wouldn't say so, because the dean had said it wasn't

[151-081]so. Somebody ought to write a book about it,--indeed they ought."

[151-082]Then he told the whole story of Dan Stringer, and how he had found

[151-083]Dan out, looking at the top of Dan's hat through the little aperture

[151-084]in the wall of the inn parlour. "When I saw the twitch in his hat,

[151-085]John, I knew he had handled the cheque himself. I don't mean to say

[151-086]that I'm sharper than another man, and I don't think so; but I do

[151-087]mean to say that when you are in any difficulty of that sort, you

[151-088]ought to go to a lawyer. It's his business, and a man does what is

[151-089]his business with patience and perseverance. It's a pity, though,

[151-090]that that scoundrel should get off." Then Eames gave his uncle an

[151-091]account of his Italian trip, to and fro, and was congratulated also

[151-092]upon his success. John's great triumph lay in the fact that he had

[151-093]been only two nights in bed, and that he would not have so far

[151-094]condescended on those occasions but for the feminine weakness of his

[151-095]fellow-traveller. "We shan't forget it all in a hurry,--shall we,

[151-096]John?" said Mr. Toogood, in a pleasant voice, as they parted at the

[151-097]door of the luncheon-house in Holborn. Toogood was returning to his

[151-098]office, and John Eames was to prepare himself for his last attempt.

[151-099]He went home to his lodgings, intending at first to change his

[151-100]dress,--to make himself smart for the work before him,--but after

[151-101]standing for a moment or two leaning on the chest of drawers in his

[151-102]bed-room, he gave up this idea. "After all that's come and gone," he

[151-103]said to himself, "if I cannot win her as I am now, I cannot win her

[151-104]at all." And then he swore to himself a solemn oath, resolving that

[151-105]he would repeat the purport of it to Lily herself,--that this should

[151-106]be the last attempt. "What's the use of it? Everybody ridicules me.

[151-107]And I am ridiculous. I am an ass. It's all very well wanting to be

[151-108]prime minister; but if you can't be prime minister, you must do

[151-109]without being prime minister." Then he attempted to sing the old

[151-110]song--"Shall I, sighing in despair, die because a woman's fair? If

[151-111]she be not fair for me, what care I how fair she be?" But he did

[151-112]care, and he told himself that the song did him no good. As it was

[151-113]not time for him as yet to go to Lily, he threw himself on the sofa,

[151-114]and strove to read a book. Then all the weary nights of his journey

[151-115]prevailed over him, and he fell asleep.

[151-116]When he awoke it wanted a quarter to six. He sprang up, and rushing

[151-117]out, jumped into a cab. "Berkeley Square,--as hard as you can go," he

[151-118]said. "Number --." He thought of Rosalind, and her counsels to lovers

[151-119]as to the keeping of time, and reflected that in such an emergency as

[151-120]his, he might really have ruined himself by that unfortunate slumber.

[151-121]When he got to Mrs. Thorne's door he knocked hurriedly, and bustled

[151-122]up to the drawing-room as though everything depended on his saving a

[151-123]minute. "I'm afraid I'm ever so much behind my time," he said.

[151-124]"It does not matter in the least," said Lily. "As Mrs. Arabin said

[151-125]that perhaps you might call, I would not be out of the way. I

[151-126]supposed that Sir Raffle was keeping you and that you wouldn't come."

[151-127]"Sir Raffle was not keeping me. I fell asleep. That is the truth of

[151-128]it."

[151-129]"I am so sorry that you should have been disturbed!"

[151-130]"Do not laugh at me, Lily,--to-day. I had been travelling a good

[151-131]deal, and I suppose I was tired."

[151-132]"I won't laugh at you," she said, and of a sudden her eyes became

[151-133]full of tears,--she did not know why. But there they were, and she

[151-134]was ashamed to put up her handkerchief, and she could not bring

[151-135]herself to turn away her face, and she had no resource but that he

[151-136]should see them.

[151-137]"Lily!" he said.

[151-138]"What a paladin you have been, John, rushing all about Europe on your

[151-139]friend's behalf!"

[151-140]"Don't talk about that."

[151-141]"And such a successful paladin too! Why am I not to talk about it? I

[151-142]am going home to-morrow, and I mean to talk about nothing else for a

[151-143]week. I am so very, very, very glad that you have saved your cousin."

[151-144]Then she did put up her handkerchief, making believe that her tears

[151-145]had been due to Mr. Crawley. But John Eames knew better than that.

[151-146]"Lily," he said, "I've come for the last time. It sounds as though I

[151-147]meant to threaten you; but you won't take it in that way. I think you

[151-148]will know what I mean. I have come for the last time--to ask you to

[151-149]be my wife." She had got up to greet him when he entered, and they

[151-150]were both still standing. She did not answer him at once, but turning

[151-151]away from him walked towards the window. "You knew why I was coming

[151-152]to-day, Lily?"

[151-153]"Mrs. Arabin told me. I could not be away when you were coming, but

[151-154]perhaps it would have been better."

[151-155]"Is it so? Must it be so? Must you say that to me, Lily? Think of it

[151-156]for a moment, dear."

[151-157]"I have thought of it."

[151-158]"One word from you, yes or no, spoken now is to be everything to me

[151-159]for always. Lily, cannot you say yes?" She did not answer him, but

[151-160]walked further away from him to another window. "Try to say yes. Look

[151-161]round at me with one look that may only half mean it;--that may tell

[151-162]me that it shall not positively be no for ever." I think that she

[151-163]almost tried to turn her face to him; but be that as it may, she kept

[151-164]her eyes steadily fixed upon the window-pane. "Lily," he said, "it is

[151-165]not that you are hard-hearted,--perhaps not altogether that you do

[151-166]not like me. I think that you believe things against me that are not

[151-167]true." As she heard this she moved her foot angrily upon the carpet.

[151-168]She had almost forgotten M. D., but now he had reminded her of the

[151-169]note. She assured herself that she had never believed anything

[151-170]against him except on evidence that was incontrovertible. But she

[151-171]was not going to speak to him on such a matter as that! It would

[151-172]not become her to accuse him. "Mrs. Arabin tells me that you doubt

[151-173]whether I am in earnest," he said.
