[025-001]"Oh, very well. I will tell his lordship."

[025-002]"I will pray you to do so."

[025-003]"And his lordship, should he so decide, will arm me with such power

[025-004]on my next coming as will enable me to carry out his lordship's

[025-005]wishes."

[025-006]"His lordship will abide by the law, as will you also." In speaking

[025-007]these last words he stood with the door in his hand, and Mr. Thumble,

[025-008]not knowing how to increase or even to maintain his firmness, thought

[025-009]it best to pass out, and mount his grey pony and ride away.

[025-010]"The poor man thought that you were laughing at him when you called

[025-011]him an angel of the church," said Mrs. Crawley, coming up to him and

[025-012]smiling on him.

[025-013]"Had I told him he was simply a messenger, he would have taken it

[025-014]worse;--poor fool! When they have rid themselves of me they may put

[025-015]him here, in my church; but not yet,--not yet. Where is Jane? Tell

[025-016]her that I am ready to commence the Seven against Thebes with her."

[025-017]Then Jane was immediately sent for out of the school, and the Seven

[025-018]against Thebes was commenced with great energy. Often during the next

[025-019]hour and a half Mrs. Crawley from the kitchen would hear him reading

[025-020]out, or rather saying by rote, with sonorous, rolling voice, great

[025-021]passages from some chorus, and she was very thankful to the bishop

[025-022]who had sent over to them a message and a messenger which had been

[025-023]so salutary in their effect upon her husband. "In truth an angel of

[025-024]the church," she said to herself as she chopped up the onions for

[025-025]the mutton-broth; and ever afterwards she regarded Mr. Thumble as an

[025-026]"angel."

[025-027]CHAPTER XIV.

[025-028]MAJOR GRANTLY CONSULTS A FRIEND.

[025-029]Grace Crawley passed through Silverbridge on her way to Allington on

[025-030]the Monday, and on the Tuesday morning Major Grantly received a very

[025-031]short note from Miss Prettyman, telling him that she had done so.

[025-032]"Dear Sir,--I think you will be glad to learn that our friend Miss

[025-033]Crawley went from us yesterday on a visit to her friend, Miss Dale,

[025-034]at Allington.--Yours truly, Annabella Prettyman." The note said no

[025-035]more than that. Major Grantly was glad to get it, obtaining from it

[025-036]that satisfaction which a man always feels when he is presumed to

[025-037]be concerned in the affairs of the lady with whom he is in love.

[025-038]And he regarded Miss Prettyman with favourable eyes,--as a discreet

[025-039]and friendly woman. Nevertheless, he was not altogether happy. The

[025-040]very fact that Miss Prettyman should write to him on such a subject

[025-041]made him feel that he was bound to Grace Crawley. He knew enough

[025-042]of himself to be sure that he could not give her up without making

[025-043]himself miserable. And yet, as regarded her father, things were going

[025-044]from bad to worse. Everybody now said that the evidence was so strong

[025-045]against Mr. Crawley as to leave hardly a doubt of his guilt. Even

[025-046]the ladies in Silverbridge were beginning to give up his cause,

[025-047]acknowledging that the money could not have come rightfully into his

[025-048]hands, and excusing him on the plea of partial insanity. "He has

[025-049]picked it up and put it by for months, and then thought that it was

[025-050]his own." The ladies of Silverbridge could find nothing better to

[025-051]say for him than that; and when young Mr. Walker remarked that such

[025-052]little mistakes were the customary causes of men being taken to

[025-053]prison, the ladies of Silverbridge did not know how to answer him.

[025-054]It had come to be their opinion that Mr. Crawley was affected with a

[025-055]partial lunacy, which ought to be forgiven in one to whom the world

[025-056]had been so cruel; and when young Mr. Walker endeavoured to explain

[025-057]to them that a man must be sane altogether or mad altogether, and

[025-058]that Mr. Crawley must, if sane, be locked up as a thief, and if mad,

[025-059]locked up as a madman, they sighed, and were convinced that until the

[025-060]world should have been improved by a new infusion of romance, and a

[025-061]stronger feeling of poetic justice, Mr. John Walker was right.

[025-062]And the result of this general opinion made its way out to Major

[025-063]Grantly, and made its way, also, to the archdeacon at Plumstead. As

[025-064]to the major, in giving him his due, it must be explained that the

[025-065]more certain he became of the father's guilt, the more certain also

[025-066]he became of the daughter's merits. It was very hard. The whole thing

[025-067]was cruelly hard. It was cruelly hard upon him that he should be

[025-068]brought into this trouble, and be forced to take upon himself the

[025-069]armour of a knight-errant for the redress of the wrong on the part

[025-070]of the young lady. But when alone in his house, or with his child,

[025-071]he declared to himself that he would do so. It might well be that

[025-072]he could not live in Barsetshire after he had married Mr. Crawley's

[025-073]daughter. He had inherited from his father enough of that longing

[025-074]for ascendancy among those around him to make him feel that in

[025-075]such circumstances he would be wretched. But he would be made more

[025-076]wretched by the self-knowledge that he had behaved badly to the

[025-077]girl he loved; and the world beyond Barsetshire was open to him. He

[025-078]would take her with him to Canada, to New Zealand, or to some other

[025-079]far-away country, and there begin his life again. Should his father

[025-080]choose to punish him for so doing by disinheriting him, they would be

[025-081]poor enough; but, in his present frame of mind, the major was able to

[025-082]regard such poverty as honourable and not altogether disagreeable.

[025-083]He had been out shooting all day at Chaldicotes, with Dr. Thorne and

[025-084]a party who were staying in the house there, and had been talking

[025-085]about Mr. Crawley, first with one man and then with another. Lord

[025-086]Lufton had been there, and young Gresham from Greshamsbury, and Mr.

[025-087]Robarts the clergyman, and news had come among them of the attempt

[025-088]made by the bishop to stop Mr. Crawley from preaching. Mr. Robarts

[025-089]had been of opinion that Mr. Crawley should have given way; and Lord

[025-090]Lufton, who shared his mother's intense dislike of everything that

[025-091]came from the palace, had sworn that he was right to resist. The

[025-092]sympathy of the whole party had been with Mr. Crawley; but they had

[025-093]all agreed that he had stolen the money.

[025-094]"I fear he'll have to give way to the bishop at last," Lord Lufton

[025-095]had said.

[025-096]"And what on earth will become of his children?" said the doctor.

[025-097]"Think of the fate of that pretty girl; for she is a very pretty

[025-098]girl. It will be ruin to her. No man will allow himself to fall

[025-099]in love with her when her father shall have been found guilty of

[025-100]stealing a cheque for twenty pounds."

[025-101]"We must do something for the whole family," said the lord. "I say,

[025-102]Thorne, you haven't half the game here that there used to be in poor

[025-103]old Sowerby's time."

[025-104]"Haven't I?" said the doctor. "You see Sowerby had been at it all his

[025-105]days, and never did anything else. I only began late in life."

[025-106]The major had intended to stay and dine at Chaldicotes, but when

[025-107]he heard what was said about Grace, his heart became sad, and he

[025-108]made some excuse as to his child, and returned home. Dr. Thorne had

[025-109]declared that no man could allow himself to fall in love with her.

[025-110]But what if a man had fallen in love with her beforehand? What if a

[025-111]man had not only fallen in love, but spoken of his love? Had he been

[025-112]alone with the doctor, he would, I think, have told him the whole of

[025-113]his trouble; for in all the county there was no man whom he would

[025-114]sooner have trusted with his secret. This Dr. Thorne was known far

[025-115]and wide for his soft heart, his open hand, and his well-sustained

[025-116]indifference to the world's opinions on most of those social matters

[025-117]with which the world meddles; and therefore the words which he had

[025-118]spoken had more weight with Major Grantly than they would have had

[025-119]from other lips. As he drove home he almost made up his mind that he

[025-120]would consult Dr. Thorne upon the matter. There were many younger men

[025-121]with whom he was very intimate,--Frank Gresham, for instance, and

[025-122]Lord Lufton himself; but this was an affair which he hardly knew how

[025-123]to discuss with a young man. To Dr. Thorne he thought that he could

[025-124]bring himself to tell the whole story.

[025-125]In the evening there came to him a messenger from Plumstead, with a

[025-126]letter from his father and some present for the child. He knew at

[025-127]once that the present had been thus sent as an excuse for the letter.

[025-128]His father might have written by the post, of course; but that would

[025-129]have given to his letter a certain air and tone which he had not

[025-130]wished it to bear. After some message from the major's mother, and

[025-131]some allusion to Edith, the archdeacon struck off upon the matter

[025-132]that was near his heart.

[025-133]"I fear it is all up with that unfortunate man at Hogglestock," he

[025-134]said. "From what I hear of the evidence which came out before the

[025-135]magistrates, there can, I think, be no doubt as to his guilt. Have

[025-136]you heard that the bishop sent over on the following day to stop him

[025-137]from preaching? He did so, and sent again on the Sunday. But Crawley

[025-138]would not give way, and so far I respect the man; for, as a matter

[025-139]of course, whatever the bishop did, or attempted to do, he would do

[025-140]with an extreme of bad taste, probably with gross ignorance as to his

[025-141]own duty and as to the duty of the man under him. I am told that on

[025-142]the first day Crawley turned out of his house the messenger sent to

[025-143]him,--some stray clergyman whom Mrs. Proudie keeps about the house;

[025-144]and that on the Sunday the stairs to the reading-desk and pulpit

[025-145]were occupied by a lot of brickmakers, among whom the parson from

[025-146]Barchester did not venture to attempt to make his way, although he

[025-147]was fortified by the presence of one of the cathedral vergers and by

[025-148]one of the palace footmen. I can hardly believe about the verger and

[025-149]the footman. As for the rest, I have no doubt it is all true. I pity

[025-150]Crawley from my heart. Poor, unfortunate man! The general opinion

[025-151]seems to be that he is not in truth responsible for what he has done.

[025-152]As for his victory over the bishop, nothing on earth could be better.

[025-153]"Your mother particularly wishes you to come over to us before the

[025-154]end of the week, and to bring Edith. Your grandfather will be here,

[025-155]and he is becoming so infirm that he will never come to us for

[025-156]another Christmas. Of course you will stay over the new year."

[025-157]Though the letter was full of Mr. Crawley and his affairs there was

[025-158]not a word in it about Grace. This, however, was quite natural. Major

[025-159]Grantly perfectly well understood his father's anxiety to carry his

[025-160]point without seeming to allude to the disagreeable subject. "My

[025-161]father is very clever," he said to himself, "very clever. But he

[025-162]isn't so clever but one can see how clever he is."

[025-163]On the next day he went into Silverbridge, intending to call on Miss

[025-164]Prettyman. He had not quite made up his mind what he would say to

[025-165]Miss Prettyman; nor was he called upon to do so, as he never got as

[025-166]far as that lady's house. While walking up the High Street he saw

[025-167]Mrs. Thorne in her carriage, and, as a matter of course, he stopped

[025-168]to speak to her. He knew Mrs. Thorne quite as intimately as he did

[025-169]her husband, and liked her quite as well. "Major Grantly," she said,

[025-170]speaking out loud to him, half across the street; "I was very angry

[025-171]with you yesterday. Why did you not come up to dinner? We had a room

[025-172]ready for you and everything."
