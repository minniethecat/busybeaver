[162-001]"A visitor,--what visitor?"

[162-002]"Grace's own friend,--Henry Grantly."

[162-003]"Grace, come here, that I may kiss you and bless you," he said, very

[162-004]solemnly. "It would seem that the world is going to be very good to

[162-005]you."

[162-006]"Papa, you must read this letter first."

[162-007]"Before I kiss my own darling?" Then she knelt at his feet. "I

[162-008]see," he said, taking the letter; "it is from your lover's father.

[162-009]Peradventure he signifies his consent, which would be surely needful

[162-010]before such a marriage would be seemly."

[Illustration: "Peradventure he signifies his Consent."]

[162-011]"It isn't about me, papa, at all."

[162-012]"Not about you? If so, that would be most unpromising. But, in any

[162-013]case, you are my best darling." Then he kissed her and blessed her,

[162-014]and slowly opened the letter. His wife had now come close to him, and

[162-015]was standing over him, touching him, so that she also could read the

[162-016]archdeacon's letter. Grace, who was still in front of him, could see

[162-017]the working of his face as he read it; but even she could not tell

[162-018]whether he was gratified, or offended, or dismayed. When he had got

[162-019]as far as the first offer of the presentation, he ceased reading for

[162-020]a while, and looked round about the room as though lost in thought.

[162-021]"Let me see what further he writes to me," he then said; and after

[162-022]that he continued the letter slowly to the end. "Nay, my child, you

[162-023]were in error in saying that he wrote not about you. 'Tis in writing

[162-024]of you he has put some real heart into his words. He writes as though

[162-025]his home would be welcome to you."

[162-026]"And does he not make St. Ewolds welcome to you, papa?"

[162-027]"He makes me welcome to accept it,--if I may use the word after the

[162-028]ordinary and somewhat faulty parlance of mankind."

[162-029]"And you will accept it,--of course?"

[162-030]"I know not that, my dear. The acceptance of a cure of souls is

[162-031]a thing not to be decided on in a moment,--as is the colour of a

[162-032]garment or the shape of a toy. Nor would I condescend to take this

[162-033]thing from the archdeacon's hands, if I thought that he bestowed it

[162-034]simply that the father of his daughter-in-law might no longer be

[162-035]accounted poor."

[162-036]"Does he say that, papa?"

[162-037]"He gives it as a collateral reason, basing his offer first on the

[162-038]kindly expressed judgment of one who is now no more. Then he refers

[162-039]to the friendship of the dean. If he believed that the judgment of

[162-040]his late father-in-law in so weighty a matter were the best to be

[162-041]relied upon of all that were at his command, then he would have

[162-042]done well to trust to it. But in such case he should have bolstered

[162-043]up a good ground for action with no collateral supports which

[162-044]are weak,--and worse than weak. However, it shall have my best

[162-045]consideration, whereunto I hope that wisdom will be given me where

[162-046]only such wisdom can be had."

[162-047]"Josiah," said his wife to him, when they were alone, "you will not

[162-048]refuse it?"

[162-049]"Not willingly,--not if it may be accepted. Alas! you need not urge

[162-050]me, when the temptation is so strong!"

[162-051]CHAPTER LXXXIII.

[162-052]MR. CRAWLEY IS CONQUERED.

[162-053]It was more than a week before the archdeacon received a reply from

[162-054]Mr. Crawley, during which time the dean had been over at Hogglestock

[162-055]more than once, as had also Mrs. Arabin and Lady Lufton the

[162-056]younger,--and there had been letters written without end, and the

[162-057]archdeacon had been nearly beside himself. "A man who pretends to

[162-058]conscientious scruples of that kind is not fit to have a parish," he

[162-059]had said to his wife. His wife understood what he meant, and I trust

[162-060]that the reader may also understand it. In the ordinary cutting

[162-061]of blocks a very fine razor is not an appropriate instrument. The

[162-062]archdeacon, moreover, loved the temporalities of the Church as

[162-063]temporalities. The Church was beautiful to him because one man by

[162-064]interest might have a thousand a year, while another man equally

[162-065]good, but without interest, could only have a hundred. And he liked

[162-066]the men who had the interest a great deal better than the men who

[162-067]had it not. He had been willing to admit this poor perpetual curate,

[162-068]who had so long been kept out in the cold, within the pleasant

[162-069]circle which was warm with ecclesiastical good things, and the man

[162-070]hesitated,--because of scruples, as the dean told him! "I always

[162-071]button up my pocket when I hear of scruples," the archdeacon said.

[162-072]But at last Mr. Crawley condescended to accept St. Ewolds. "Reverend

[162-073]and dear Sir," he said in his letter.

[162-074]For the personal benevolence of the offer made to me in

[162-075]your letter of the ---- instant, I beg to tender you my

[162-076]most grateful thanks; as also for your generous kindness

[162-077]to me, in telling me of the high praise bestowed upon me

[162-078]by a gentleman who is now no more,--whose character I

[162-079]have esteemed and whose good opinion I value. There is,

[162-080]methinks, something inexpressibly dear to me in the

[162-081]recorded praise of the dead. For the further instance

[162-082]of the friendship of the Dean of Barchester, I am also

[162-083]thankful.

[162-084]Since the receipt of your letter I have doubted much as to

[162-085]my fitness for the work you have proposed to entrust to

[162-086]me,--not from any feeling that the parish of St. Ewolds

[162-087]may be beyond my intellectual power, but because the

[162-088]latter circumstances of my life have been of a nature so

[162-089]strange and perplexing, that they have left me somewhat

[162-090]in doubt as to my own aptitude for going about among men

[162-091]without giving offence and becoming a stumbling-block.

[162-092]Nevertheless, reverend and dear sir, if after this

[162-093]confession on my part of a certain faulty demeanour with

[162-094]which I know well that I am afflicted, you are still

[162-095]willing to put the parish into my hands, I will accept the

[162-096]charge,--instigated to do so by the advice of all whom I

[162-097]have consulted on the subject; and in thus accepting it,

[162-098]I hereby pledge myself to vacate it at a month's warning,

[162-099]should I be called upon by you to do so at any period

[162-100]within the next two years. Should I be so far successful

[162-101]during those twenty-four months as to have satisfied both

[162-102]yourself and myself, I may then perhaps venture to regard

[162-103]the preferment as my own in perpetuity for life.

[162-104]I have the honour to be, reverend and dear sir,

[162-105]Your most humble and faithful servant,

[162-106]JOSIAH CRAWLEY.

[162-107]"Psha!" said the archdeacon, who professed that he did not at all

[162-108]like the letter. "I wonder what he would say if I sent him a month's

[162-109]notice at next Michaelmas?"

[162-110]"I'm sure he would go," said Mrs. Grantly.

[162-111]"The more fool he," said the archdeacon.

[162-112]At this time Grace was at the parsonage in a seventh heaven of

[162-113]happiness. The archdeacon was never rough to her, nor did he make any

[162-114]of his harsh remarks about her father in her presence. Before her St.

[162-115]Ewolds was spoken of as the home that was to belong to the Crawleys

[162-116]for the next twenty years. Mrs. Grantly was very loving with her,

[162-117]lavishing upon her pretty presents, and words that were prettier than

[162-118]the presents. Grace's life had hitherto been so destitute of those

[162-119]prettinesses and softnesses, which can hardly be had without money

[162-120]though money alone will not purchase them, that it seemed to her now

[162-121]that the heavens rained graciousness upon her. It was not that the

[162-122]archdeacon's watch, or her lover's chain, or Mrs. Grantly's locket,

[162-123]or the little toy from Italy which Mrs. Arabin brought to her from

[162-124]the treasures of the deanery, filled her heart with undue exultation.

[162-125]It was not that she revelled in her new delights of silver and gold

[162-126]and shining gems: but that the silver and gold and shining gems were

[162-127]constant indications to her that things had changed, not only for

[162-128]her, but for her father and mother, and brother and sister. She felt

[162-129]now more sure than ever that she could not have enjoyed her love had

[162-130]she accepted her lover while the disgrace of the accusation against

[162-131]her father remained. But now,--having waited till that had passed

[162-132]away, everything was a new happiness to her.

[162-133]At last it was settled that Mr. and Mrs. Crawley were to come to

[162-134]Plumstead,--and they came. It would be too long to tell now how

[162-135]gradually had come about that changed state of things which made such

[162-136]a visit possible. Mr. Crawley had at first declared that such a thing

[162-137]was quite out of the question. If St. Ewolds was to depend upon it

[162-138]St. Ewolds must be given up. And I think that it would have been

[162-139]impossible for him to go direct from Hogglestock to Plumstead. But it

[162-140]fell out after this wise.

[162-141]Mr. Harding's curate at St. Ewolds was nominated to Hogglestock, and

[162-142]the dean urged upon his friend Crawley the expediency of giving up

[162-143]the house as quickly as he could do so. Gradually at this time Mr.

[162-144]Crawley had been forced into a certain amount of intimacy with the

[162-145]haunts of men. He had been twice or thrice at Barchester, and had

[162-146]lunched with the dean. He had been at Framley for an hour or two,

[162-147]and had been forced into some communication with old Mr. Thorne,

[162-148]the squire of his new parish. The end of this had been that he had

[162-149]at last consented to transfer himself and wife and daughter to the

[162-150]deanery for a fortnight. He had preached one farewell sermon at

[162-151]Hogglestock,--not, as he told his audience, as their pastor, which

[162-152]he had ceased to be now for some two or three months,--but as their

[162-153]old and loving friend, to whom the use of his former pulpit had been

[162-154]lent, that he might express himself thus among them for the last

[162-155]time. His sermon was very short, and was preached without book or

[162-156]notes,--but he never once paused for a word or halted in the string

[162-157]or rhythm of his discourse. The dean was there and declared to him

[162-158]afterwards that he had not given him credit for such powers of

[162-159]utterance. "Any man can utter out of a full heart," Crawley had

[162-160]answered. "In this trumpery affair about myself, my heart is full! If

[162-161]we could only have our hearts full in other matters, our utterances

[162-162]thereanent would receive more attention." To all of which the dean

[162-163]made no reply.

[162-164]On the day after this the Crawleys took their final departure from

[162-165]Hogglestock, all the brickmakers from Hoggle End having assembled

[162-166]on the occasion, with a purse containing seventeen pounds seven

[162-167]shillings and sixpence, which they insisted on presenting to Mr.

[162-168]Crawley, and as to which there was a little difficulty. And at the

[162-169]deanery they remained for a fortnight. How Mrs. Crawley, under the

[162-170]guidance of Mrs. Arabin, had there so far trenched upon the revenues

[162-171]of St. Ewolds as to provide for her husband and herself raiment

[162-172]fitting for the worldly splendour of Plumstead, need not here be

[162-173]told in detail. Suffice to say, the raiment was forthcoming, and Mr.

[162-174]Crawley found himself to be the perplexed possessor of a black dress

[162-175]coat, in addition to the long frock, coming nearly to his feet, which

[162-176]was provided for his daily wear. Touching this garment, there had

[162-177]been some discussion between the dean and the new vicar. The dean

[162-178]had desired that it should be curtailed in length. The vicar had

[162-179]remonstrated,--but still with something of the weakness of compliance

[162-180]in his eye. Then the dean had persisted. "Surely the price of

[162-181]the cloth wanted to perfect the comeliness of the garment cannot

[162-182]be much," said the vicar, almost woefully. After that, the dean

[162-183]relented, and the comeliness of the coat was made perfect. The new

[162-184]black long frock, I think Mr. Crawley liked; but the dress coat, with

[162-185]the suit complete, perplexed him sorely.
