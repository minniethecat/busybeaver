[140-001]John had found an opportunity of talking to a young lady in the

[140-002]breakfast-room, and had told her of his deeds. "I only left London on

[140-003]Tuesday night, and I have come here taking Venice on the road."

[140-004]"Then you have travelled fast," said the young lady.

[140-005]"I haven't seen a bed, of course," said John.

[140-006]The young lady immediately afterwards told her father. "I suppose he

[140-007]must be one of those Foreign Office messengers," said the young lady.

[140-008]"Anything but that," said the gentleman. "People never talk about

[140-009]their own trades. He's probably a clerk with a fortnight's leave of

[140-010]absence, seeing how many towns he can do in the time. It's the usual

[140-011]way of travelling now-a-days. When I was young and there were no

[140-012]railways, I remember going from Paris to Vienna without sleeping."

[140-013]Luckily for his present happiness, John did not hear this.

[140-014]He was still fast asleep when a servant came to him from Mrs. Arabin

[140-015]to say that she would see him at once. "Yes, yes; I'm quite ready to

[140-016]go on," said Johnny, jumping up, and thinking of the journey to Rome.

[140-017]But there was no journey to Rome before him. Mrs. Arabin was almost

[140-018]in the next room, and there he found her.

[140-019]The reader will understand that they had never met before, and

[140-020]hitherto knew nothing of each other. Mrs. Arabin had never heard the

[140-021]name of John Eames till John's card was put into her hands, and would

[140-022]not have known his business with her had he not written those few

[140-023]words upon it. "You have come about Mr. Crawley?" she said to him,

[140-024]eagerly. "I have heard from my father that somebody was coming."

[140-025]"Yes, Mrs. Arabin; as hard as I could travel. I had expected to find

[140-026]you at Venice."

[140-027]"Have you been at Venice?"

[140-028]"I have just arrived from Venice. They told me at Paris I should find

[140-029]you there. However, that does not matter, as I have found you here. I

[140-030]wonder whether you can help us?"

[140-031]"Do you know Mr. Crawley? Are you a friend of his?"

[140-032]"I never saw him in my life; but he married my cousin."

[140-033]"I gave him the cheque, you know," said Mrs. Arabin.

[140-034]"What!" exclaimed Eames, literally almost knocked backwards by the

[140-035]easiness of the words which contained a solution for so terrible a

[140-036]difficulty. The Crawley case had assumed such magnitude, and the

[140-037]troubles of the Crawley family had been so terrible, that it seemed

[140-038]to him to be almost sacrilegious that words so simply uttered should

[140-039]suffice to cure everything. He had hardly hoped,--had at least barely

[140-040]hoped,--that Mrs. Arabin might be able to suggest something which

[140-041]would put them all on a track towards discovery of the truth. But he

[140-042]found that she had the clue in her hand, and that the clue was one

[140-043]which required no further delicacy of investigation. There would be

[140-044]nothing more to unravel; no journey to Jerusalem would be necessary!

[140-045]"Yes," said Mrs. Arabin, "I gave it to him. They have been writing

[140-046]to my husband about it, and never wrote to me; and till I received a

[140-047]letter about it from my father, and another from my sister, at Venice

[140-048]the day before yesterday, I knew nothing of the particulars of Mr.

[140-049]Crawley's trouble."

[140-050]"Had you not heard that he had been taken before the magistrates?"

[140-051]"No; not so much even as that. I had seen in 'Galignani' something

[140-052]about a clergyman, but I did not know what clergyman; and I heard

[140-053]that there was something wrong about Mr. Crawley's money, but there

[140-054]has always been something wrong about money with poor Mr. Crawley;

[140-055]and as I knew that my husband had been written to also, I did not

[140-056]interfere, further than to ask the particulars. My letters have

[140-057]followed me about, and I only learned at Venice, just before I came

[140-058]here, what was the nature of the case."

[140-059]"And did you do anything?"

[140-060]"I telegraphed at once to Mr. Toogood, who I understand is acting as

[140-061]Mr. Crawley's solicitor. My sister sent me his address."

[140-062]"He is my uncle."

[140-063]"I telegraphed to him, telling him that I had given Mr. Crawley the

[140-064]cheque, and then I wrote to Archdeacon Grantly giving him the whole

[140-065]history. I was obliged to come here before I could return home, but

[140-066]I intended to start this evening."

[140-067]"And what is the whole history?" asked John Eames.

[140-068]The history of the gift of the cheque was very simple. It has been

[140-069]told how Mr. Crawley in his dire distress had called upon his old

[140-070]friend at the deanery asking for pecuniary assistance. This he had

[140-071]done with so much reluctance that his spirit had given way while

[140-072]he was waiting in the dean's library, and he had wished to depart

[140-073]without accepting what the dean was quite willing to bestow upon

[140-074]him. From this cause it had come to pass there had been no time for

[140-075]explanatory words, even between the dean and his wife,--from whose

[140-076]private funds had in truth come the money which had been given to

[140-077]Mr. Crawley. For the private wealth of the family belonged to Mrs.

[140-078]Arabin, and not to the dean; and was left entirely in Mrs. Arabin's

[140-079]hands, to be disposed of as she might please. Previously to Mr.

[140-080]Crawley's arrival at the deanery this matter had been discussed

[140-081]between the dean and his wife, and it had been agreed between them

[140-082]that a sum of fifty pounds should be given. It should be given by

[140-083]Mrs. Arabin, but it was thought that the gift would come with more

[140-084]comfort to the recipient from the hands of his old friend than from

[140-085]those of his wife. There had been much discussion between them as to

[140-086]the mode in which this might be done with least offence to the man's

[140-087]feelings,--for they knew Mr. Crawley and his peculiarities well. At

[140-088]last it was agreed that the notes should be put into an envelope,

[140-089]which envelope the dean should have ready with him. But when the

[140-090]moment came the dean did not have the envelope ready, and was obliged

[140-091]to leave the room to seek his wife. And Mrs. Arabin explained to

[140-092]John Eames that even she had not had it ready, and had been forced

[140-093]to go to her own desk to fetch it. Then, at the last moment, with

[140-094]the desire of increasing the good to be done to people who were so

[140-095]terribly in want, she put the cheque for twenty pounds, which was in

[140-096]her possession as money of her own, along with the notes, and in this

[140-097]way the cheque had been given by the dean to Mr. Crawley. "I shall

[140-098]never forgive myself for not telling the dean," she said. "Had I done

[140-099]that all this trouble would have been saved!"

[140-100]"But where did you get the cheque?" Eames asked with natural

[140-101]curiosity.

[140-102]"Exactly," said Mrs. Arabin. "I have got to show now that I did not

[140-103]steal it,--have I not? Mr. Soames will indict me now. And, indeed, I

[140-104]have had some trouble to refresh my memory as to all the particulars,

[140-105]for you see it is more than a year past." But Mrs. Arabin's mind

[140-106]was clearer on such matters than Mr. Crawley's, and she was able to

[140-107]explain that she had taken the cheque as part of the rent due to

[140-108]her from the landlord of "The Dragon of Wantly," which inn was her

[140-109]property, having been the property of her first husband. For some

[140-110]years past there had been a difficulty about the rent, things not

[140-111]having gone at "The Dragon of Wantly" as smoothly as they had used to

[140-112]go. At one time the money had been paid half-yearly by the landlord's

[140-113]cheque on the bank at Barchester. For the last year-and-a-half this

[140-114]had not been done, and the money had come into Mrs. Arabin's hands

[140-115]at irregular periods and in irregular sums. There was at this moment

[140-116]rent due for twelve months, and Mrs. Arabin expressed her doubt

[140-117]whether she would get it on her return to Barchester. On the occasion

[140-118]to which she was now alluding, the money had been paid into her own

[140-119]hands, in the deanery breakfast-parlour, by a man she knew very

[140-120]well,--not the landlord himself, but one bearing the landlord's name,

[140-121]whom she believed to be the landlord's brother, or at least his

[140-122]cousin. The man in question was named Daniel Stringer, and he had

[140-123]been employed in "The Dragon of Wantly," as a sort of clerk or

[140-124]managing man, as long as she had known it. The rent had been paid

[140-125]to her by Daniel Stringer quite as often as by Daniel's brother or

[140-126]cousin, John Stringer, who was, in truth, the landlord of the hotel.

[140-127]When questioned by John respecting the persons employed at the

[140-128]inn, she said that she did believe that there had been rumours of

[140-129]something wrong. The house had been in the hands of the Stringers for

[140-130]many years,--before the property had been purchased by her husband's

[140-131]father,--and therefore there had been an unwillingness to move them;

[140-132]but gradually, so she said, there had come upon her and her husband a

[140-133]feeling that the house must be put into other hands. "But did you say

[140-134]nothing about the cheque?" John asked. "Yes, I said a good deal about

[140-135]it. I asked why a cheque of Mr. Soames's was brought to me, instead

[140-136]of being taken to the bank for money; and Stringer explained to me

[140-137]that they were not very fond of going to the bank, as they owed

[140-138]money there, but that I could pay it into my account. Only I kept my

[140-139]account at the other bank."

[140-140]"You might have paid it in there?" said Johnny.

[140-141]"I suppose I might, but I didn't. I gave it to poor Mr. Crawley

[140-142]instead,--like a fool, as I know now that I was. And so I have

[140-143]brought all this trouble on him and on her; and now I must rush home,

[140-144]without waiting for the dean, as fast as the trains will carry me."

[140-145]Eames offered to accompany her, and this offer was accepted. "It is

[140-146]hard upon you, though," she said; "you will see nothing of Florence.

[140-147]Three hours in Venice, and six in Florence, and no hours at all

[140-148]anywhere else, will be a hard fate to you on your first trip to

[140-149]Italy." But Johnny said "Excelsior" to himself once more, and thought

[140-150]of Lily Dale, who was still in London, hoping that she might hear of

[140-151]his exertions; and he felt, perhaps, also, that it would be pleasant

[140-152]to return with a dean's wife, and never hesitated. Nor would it do,

[140-153]he thought, for him to be absent in the excitement caused by the news

[140-154]of Mr. Crawley's innocence and injuries. "I don't care a bit about

[140-155]that," he said. "Of course, I should like to see Florence, and, of

[140-156]course, I should like to go to bed; but I will live in hopes that I

[140-157]may do both some day." And so there grew to be a friendship between

[140-158]him and Mrs. Arabin even before they had started.

[140-159]He was driven once through Florence; he saw the Venus de' Medici, and

[140-160]he saw the Seggiola; he looked up from the side of the Duomo to the

[140-161]top of the Campanile, and he walked round the back of the cathedral

[140-162]itself; he tried to inspect the doors of the Baptistery, and declared

[140-163]that the "David" was very fine. Then he went back to the hotel, dined

[140-164]with Mrs. Arabin, and started for England.

[140-165]The dean was to have joined his wife at Venice, and then they were

[140-166]to have returned together, coming round by Florence. Mrs. Arabin had

[140-167]not, therefore, taken her things away from Florence when she left

[140-168]it, and had been obliged to return to pick them up on her journey

[140-169]homewards. He,--the dean,--had been delayed in his Eastern travels.

[140-170]Neither Syria nor Constantinople had got themselves done as quickly

[140-171]as he had expected, and he had, consequently, twice written to his

[140-172]wife, begging her to pardon the transgression of his absence for even

[140-173]yet a few days longer. "Everything, therefore," as Mrs. Arabin said,

[140-174]"has conspired to perpetuate this mystery, which a word from me would

[140-175]have solved. I owe more to Mr. Crawley than I can ever pay him."
