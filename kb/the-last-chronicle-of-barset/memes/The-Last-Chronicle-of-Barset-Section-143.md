[143-001]Mr. Walker, after breakfast, had himself driven up in his open

[143-002]carriage to Cosby Lodge, and, as he entered the gates, observed that

[143-003]the auctioneer's bills as to the sale had been pulled down. The

[143-004]Mr. Walkers of the world know everything, and our Mr. Walker had

[143-005]quite understood that the major was leaving Cosby Lodge because

[143-006]of some misunderstanding with his father. The exact nature of the

[143-007]misunderstanding he did not know, even though he was Mr. Walker, but

[143-008]had little doubt that it referred in some way to Grace Crawley. If

[143-009]the archdeacon's objection to Grace arose from the imputation against

[143-010]the father, that objection would now be removed, but the abolition of

[143-011]the posters could not as yet have been owing to any such cause as

[143-012]that. Mr. Walker found the major at the gate of the farmyard attached

[143-013]to Cosby Lodge, and perceived that at that very moment he was engaged

[143-014]in superintending the abolition of sundry other auctioneer's bills

[143-015]from sundry other posts. "What is all this about?" said Mr. Walker,

[143-016]greeting the major. "Is there to be no sale after all?"

[Illustration: "No sale after all?"]

[143-017]"It has been postponed," said the major.

[143-018]"Postponed for good, I hope? Bill to be read again this day six

[143-019]months!" said Mr. Walker.

[143-020]"I rather think not. But circumstances have induced me to have it put

[143-021]off."

[143-022]Mr. Walker had got out of the carriage and had taken Major Grantly

[143-023]aside. "Just come a little further," he said; "I've something special

[143-024]to tell you. News reached me last night which will clear Mr. Crawley

[143-025]altogether. We know now where he got the cheque."

[143-026]"You don't tell me so!"

[143-027]"Yes, I do. And though the news has reached us in such a way that we

[143-028]cannot act upon it till it's confirmed, I do not in the least doubt

[143-029]it."

[143-030]"And how did he get it?"

[143-031]"You cannot guess?"

[143-032]"Not in the least," said the major; "unless, after all, Soames gave

[143-033]it to him."

[143-034]"Soames did not give it to him, but Mrs. Arabin did."

[143-035]"Mrs. Arabin?"

[143-036]"Yes, Mrs. Arabin."

[143-037]"Not the dean?"

[143-038]"No, not the dean. What we know is this, that your aunt has

[143-039]telegraphed to Crawley's cousin, Toogood, to say that she gave

[143-040]Crawley that cheque, and that she has written to your father about it

[143-041]at length. We do not like to tell Crawley till that letter has been

[143-042]received. It is so easy, you know, to misunderstand a telegram, and

[143-043]the wrong copying of a word may make such a mistake!"

[143-044]"When was it received?"

[143-045]"Toogood received it in London only yesterday morning. Your father

[143-046]will not get his letter, as I calculate, till the day after

[143-047]to-morrow. But, perhaps, you had better go over and see him, and

[143-048]prepare him for it. Toogood has gone to Barchester this morning."

[143-049]To this proposition Grantly made no immediate answer. He could not

[143-050]but remember the terms on which he had left his father; and though

[143-051]he had, most unwillingly, pulled down the auctioneer's bills, in

[143-052]compliance with his mother's last prayer to him,--and, indeed,

[143-053]had angrily told the auctioneer to send him in his bill when the

[143-054]auctioneer had demurred to these proceedings,--nevertheless he was

[143-055]hardly prepared to discuss the matter of Mr. Crawley with his father

[143-056]in pleasant words,--in words which should be full of rejoicing. It

[143-057]was a great thing for him, Henry Grantly, that Mr. Crawley should

[143-058]be innocent, and he did rejoice; but he had intended his father to

[143-059]understand that he meant to persevere, whether Mr. Crawley were

[143-060]innocent or guilty, and thus he would now lose an opportunity for

[143-061]exhibiting his obstinacy,--an opportunity which had not been without

[143-062]a charm for him. He must console himself as best he might with the

[143-063]returning prospect of assured prosperity, and with his renewed hopes

[143-064]as to the Plumstead foxes! "We think, major, that when the time

[143-065]comes you ought to be the bearer of the news to Hogglestock," said

[143-066]Mr. Walker. Then the major did undertake to convey the news to

[143-067]Hogglestock, but he made no promise as to going over to Plumstead.

[143-068]CHAPTER LXXII.

[143-069]MR. TOOGOOD AT "THE DRAGON OF WANTLY."

[143-070]In accordance with his arrangement with Mr. Walker, Mr. Toogood went

[143-071]over to Barchester early in the morning and put himself up at "The

[143-072]Dragon of Wantly." He now knew the following facts: that Mr. Soames,

[143-073]when he lost his cheque, had had with him one of the servants from

[143-074]that inn,--that the man who had been with Mr. Soames had gone to New

[143-075]Zealand,--that the cheque had found its way into the hands of Mrs.

[143-076]Arabin, and that Mrs. Arabin was the owner of the inn in question.

[143-077]So much he believed to be within his knowledge, and if his knowledge

[143-078]should prove to be correct, his work would be done as far as Mr.

[143-079]Crawley was concerned. If Mr. Crawley had not stolen the cheque, and

[143-080]if that could be proved, it would be a question of no great moment to

[143-081]Mr. Toogood who had stolen it. But he was a sportsman in his own line

[143-082]who liked to account for his own fox. As he was down at Barchester,

[143-083]he thought that he might as well learn how the cheque had got into

[143-084]Mrs. Arabin's hands. No doubt that for her own personal possession of

[143-085]it she would be able to account on her return. Probably such account

[143-086]would be given in her first letter home. But it might be well that

[143-087]he should be prepared with any small circumstantial details which he

[143-088]might be able to pick up at the inn.

[143-089]He reached Barchester before breakfast, and in ordering his tea and

[143-090]toast, reminded the old waiter with the dirty towel of his former

[143-091]acquaintance with him. "I remember you, sir," said the old waiter.

[143-092]"I remember you very well. You was asking questions about the cheque

[143-093]which Mr. Soames lost afore Christmas." Mr. Toogood certainly had

[143-094]asked one question on the subject. He had inquired whether a certain

[143-095]man who had gone to New Zealand had been the post-boy who accompanied

[143-096]Mr. Soames when the cheque was lost; and the waiter had professed to

[143-097]know nothing about Mr. Soames or the cheque. He now perceived at once

[143-098]that the gist of the question had remained on the old man's mind, and

[143-099]that he was recognized as being in some way connected with the lost

[143-100]money.

[143-101]"Did I? Ah, yes; I think I did. And I think you told me that he was

[143-102]the man?"

[143-103]"No, sir; I never told you that."

[143-104]"Then you told me that he wasn't."

[143-105]"Nor I didn't tell you that neither," said the waiter angrily.

[143-106]"Then what the devil did you tell me?" To this further question the

[143-107]waiter sulkily declined to give any answer, and soon afterwards left

[143-108]the room. Toogood, as soon as he had done his breakfast, rang the

[143-109]bell, and the same man appeared. "Will you tell Mr. Stringer that I

[143-110]should be glad to see him if he's disengaged," said Mr. Toogood. "I

[143-111]know he's bad with the gout, and therefore if he'll allow me, I'll go

[143-112]to him instead of his coming to me." Mr. Stringer was the landlord

[143-113]of the inn. The waiter hesitated a moment, and then declared that

[143-114]to the best of his belief his master was not down. He would go and

[143-115]see. Toogood, however, would not wait for that; but rising quickly

[143-116]and passing the waiter, crossed the hall from the coffee-room, and

[143-117]entered what was called the bar. The bar was a small room connected

[143-118]with the hall by a large open window, at which orders for rooms were

[143-119]given and cash was paid, and glasses of beer were consumed,--and a

[143-120]good deal of miscellaneous conversation was carried on. The barmaid

[143-121]was here at the window, and there was also, in a corner of the room,

[143-122]a man at a desk with a red nose. Toogood knew that the man at the

[143-123]desk with the red nose was Mr. Stringer's clerk. So much he had

[143-124]learned in his former rummaging about the inn. And he also remembered

[143-125]at this moment that he had observed the man with the red nose

[143-126]standing under a narrow archway in the close as he was coming out of

[143-127]the deanery, on the occasion of his visit to Mr. Harding. It had not

[143-128]occurred to him then that the man with the red nose was watching him,

[143-129]but it did occur to him now that the man with the red nose had been

[143-130]there, under the arch, with the express purpose of watching him on

[143-131]that occasion. Mr. Toogood passed quickly through the bar into an

[143-132]inner parlour, in which was sitting Mr. Stringer, the landlord,

[143-133]propped among his cushions. Toogood, as he had entered the hotel, had

[143-134]seen Mr. Stringer so placed, through the two doors, which at that

[143-135]moment had both happened to be open. He knew therefore that his old

[143-136]friend the waiter had not been quite true to him in suggesting that

[143-137]his master was not as yet down. As Toogood cast a glance of his eye

[143-138]on the man with the red nose, he told himself the old story of the

[143-139]apparition under the archway.

[143-140]"Mr. Stringer," said Mr. Toogood to the landlord, "I hope I'm not

[143-141]intruding."

[143-142]"O dear, no, sir," said the forlorn man. "Nobody ever intrudes coming

[143-143]in here. I'm always happy to see gentlemen,--only, mostly, I'm so bad

[143-144]with the gout."

[143-145]"Have you got a sharp touch of it just now, Mr. Stringer?"

[143-146]"Not just to-day, sir. I've been a little easier since Saturday. The

[143-147]worst of this burst is over. But Lord bless you, sir, it don't leave

[143-148]me,--not for a fortnight at a time, now; it don't. And it ain't what

[143-149]I drink, nor it ain't what I eat."

[143-150]"Constitutional, I suppose?" said Toogood.

[143-151]"Look here, sir;" and Mr. Stringer shewed his visitor the chalk

[143-152]stones in all his knuckles. "They say I'm all a mass of chalk. I

[143-153]sometimes think they'll break me up to mark the scores behind my own

[143-154]door with." And Mr. Stringer laughed at his own wit.

[143-155]Mr. Toogood laughed too. He laughed loud and cheerily. And then he

[143-156]asked a sudden question, keeping his eye as he did so upon a little

[143-157]square open window, which communicated between the landlord's private

[143-158]room and the bar. Through this small aperture he could see as he

[143-159]stood a portion of the hat worn by the man with the red nose. Since

[143-160]he had been in the room with the landlord, the man with the red nose

[143-161]had moved his head twice, on each occasion drawing himself closer

[143-162]into his corner; but Mr. Toogood, by moving also, had still contrived

[143-163]to keep a morsel of the hat in sight. He laughed cheerily at the

[143-164]landlord's joke, and then he asked a sudden question,--looking well

[143-165]at the morsel of the hat as he did so. "Mr. Stringer," said he,

[143-166]"how do you pay your rent, and to whom do you pay it?" There was

[143-167]immediately a jerk in the hat, and then it disappeared. Toogood,

[143-168]stepping to the open door, saw that the red-nosed clerk had taken his

[143-169]hat off and was very busy at his accounts.

[143-170]"How do I pay my rent?" said Mr. Stringer, the landlord. "Well, sir,

[143-171]since this cursed gout has been so bad, it's hard enough to pay it at

[143-172]all sometimes. You ain't sent here to look for it, sir, are you?"

[143-173]"Not I," said Toogood. "It was only a chance question." He felt

[143-174]that he had nothing more to do with Mr. Stringer, the landlord. Mr.

[143-175]Stringer, the landlord, knew nothing about Mr. Soames's cheque.

[143-176]"What's the name of your clerk?" said he.

[143-177]"The name of my clerk?" said Mr. Stringer. "Why do you want to know

[143-178]the name of my clerk?"
