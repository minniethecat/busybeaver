[118-001]Lily was seated between Mrs. Thorne and Mrs. Smith, and Siph Dunn had

[118-002]been standing immediately opposite to them. Fowler Pratt, who had

[118-003]been drawn into the circle against his will, was now standing close

[118-004]to Dunn, almost between him and Lily,--and Crosbie was standing

[118-005]within two yards of Lily, on the other side of Dunn. Emily and

[118-006]Bernard had gone behind Pratt and Crosbie to Mrs. Thorne's side

[118-007]before they had recognized the two men;--and in this way Lily was

[118-008]completely surrounded. Mrs. Thorne, who, in spite of her eager,

[118-009]impetuous ways, was as thoughtful of others as any woman could be, as

[118-010]soon as she heard Crosbie's name understood it all, and knew that it

[118-011]would be well that she should withdraw Lily from her plight. Crosbie,

[118-012]in his attempt to talk to Mrs. Smith, had smiled and simpered,--and

[118-013]had then felt that to smile and simper before Lily Dale, with a

[118-014]pretended indifference to her presence, was false on his part, and

[118-015]would seem to be mean. He would have avoided Lily for both their

[118-016]sakes, had it been possible; but it was no longer possible, and he

[118-017]could not keep his eyes from her face. Hardly knowing what he did, he

[118-018]bowed to her, lifted his hat, and uttered some word of greeting.

[118-019]Lily, from the moment that she had perceived his presence, had looked

[118-020]straight before her, with something almost of fierceness in her eyes.

[118-021]Both Pratt and Siph Dunn had observed her narrowly. It had seemed

[118-022]as though Crosbie had been altogether outside the ken of her eyes,

[118-023]or the notice of her ears, and yet she had seen every motion of his

[118-024]body, and had heard every word which had fallen from his lips. Now,

[118-025]when he saluted her, she turned her face full upon him, and bowed to

[118-026]him. Then she rose from her seat, and made her way, between Siph Dunn

[118-027]and Pratt, out of the circle. The blood had mounted to her face and

[118-028]suffused it all, and her whole manner was such that it could escape

[118-029]the observation of none who stood there. Even Mrs. Harold Smith had

[118-030]seen it, and had read the story. As soon as she was on her feet,

[118-031]Bernard had dropped Emily's hand, and offered his arm to his cousin.

[118-032]"Lily," he had said out loud, "you had better let me take you away.

[118-033]It is a misfortune that you have been subjected to the insult of

[118-034]such a greeting." Bernard and Crosbie had been early friends, and

[118-035]Bernard had been the unfortunate means of bringing Crosbie and Lily

[118-036]together. Up to this day, Bernard had never had his revenge for the

[118-037]ill-treatment which his cousin had received. Some morsel of that

[118-038]revenge came to him now. Lily almost hated her cousin for what he

[118-039]said; but she took his arm, and walked with him from the room.

[118-040]It must be acknowledged in excuse for Bernard Dale, and as an

[118-041]apology for the apparent indiscretion of his words, that all the

[118-042]circumstances of the meeting had become apparent to every one there.

[118-043]The misfortune of the encounter had become too plain to admit of its

[118-044]being hidden under any of the ordinary veils of society. Crosbie's

[118-045]salutation had been made before the eyes of them all, and in the

[118-046]midst of absolute silence, and Lily had risen with so queen-like

[118-047]a demeanour, and had moved with so stately a step, that it was

[118-048]impossible that any one concerned should pretend to ignore the facts

[118-049]of the scene that had occurred. Crosbie was still standing close to

[118-050]Mrs. Harold Smith, Mrs. Thorne had risen from her seat, and the words

[118-051]which Bernard Dale had uttered were still sounding in the ears of

[118-052]them all. "Shall I see after the carriage?" said Siph Dunn. "Do,"

[118-053]said Mrs. Thorne; "or, stay a moment; the carriage will of course be

[118-054]there, and we will go together. Good-morning, Mr. Pratt. I expect

[118-055]that, at any rate, you will send me your card by post." Then they all

[118-056]passed on, and Crosbie and Fowler Pratt were left among the pictures.

[118-057]"I think you will agree with me now that you had better give her up,"

[118-058]said Fowler Pratt.

[118-059]"I will never give her up," said Crosbie, "till I shall hear that she

[118-060]has married some one else."

[118-061]"You may take my word for it, that she will never marry you after

[118-062]what has just now occurred."

[118-063]"Very likely not; but still the attempt, even the idea of the

[118-064]attempt, will be a comfort to me. I shall be endeavouring to do that

[118-065]which I ought to have done."

[118-066]"What you have got to think of, I should suppose, is her

[118-067]comfort,--not your own."

[118-068]Crosbie stood for a while silent, looking at a portrait which was

[118-069]hung just within the doorway of a smaller room into which they had

[118-070]passed, as though his attention were entirely riveted by the picture.

[118-071]But he was thinking of the picture not at all, and did not even know

[118-072]what kind of painting was on the canvas before him.

[118-073]"Pratt," he said at last, "you are always hard to me."

[118-074]"I will say nothing more to you on the subject, if you wish me to be

[118-075]silent."

[118-076]"I do wish you to be silent about that."

[118-077]"That shall be enough," said Pratt.

[118-078]"You do not quite understand me. You do not know how thoroughly I

[118-079]have repented of the evil that I have done, or how far I would go to

[118-080]make retribution, if retribution were possible!"

[118-081]Fowler Pratt, having been told to hold his tongue as regarded that

[118-082]subject, made no reply to this, and began to talk about the pictures.

[118-083]Lily, leaning on her cousin's arm, was out in the courtyard in front

[118-084]of the house before Mrs. Thorne or Siph Dunn. It was but for a

[118-085]minute, but still there was a minute in which Bernard felt that he

[118-086]ought to say a word to her.

[118-087]"I hope you are not angry with me, Lily, for having spoken."

[118-088]"I wish, of course, that you had not spoken; but I am not angry. I

[118-089]have no right to be angry. I made the misfortune for myself. Do not

[118-090]say anything more about it, dear Bernard;--that is all."

[118-091]They had walked to the picture-gallery; but, by agreement, two

[118-092]carriages had come to take them away,--Mrs. Thorne's and Mrs. Harold

[118-093]Smith's. Mrs. Thorne easily managed to send Emily Dunstable and

[118-094]Bernard away with her friend, and to tell Siph Dunn that he must

[118-095]manage for himself. In this way it was contrived that no one but Mrs.

[118-096]Thorne should be with Lily Dale.

[118-097]"My dear," said Mrs. Thorne, "it seemed to me that you were a little

[118-098]put out, and so I thought it best to send them all away."

[118-099]"It was very kind."

[118-100]"He ought to have passed on and not to have stood an instant when he

[118-101]saw you," said Mrs. Thorne, with indignation. "There are moments when

[118-102]it is a man's duty simply to vanish, to melt into the air, or to sink

[118-103]into the ground,--in which he is bound to overcome the difficulties

[118-104]of such sudden self-removal, or must ever after be accounted poor and

[118-105]mean."

[118-106]"I did not want him to vanish;--if only he had not spoken to me."

[118-107]"He should have vanished. A man is sometimes bound in honour to do

[118-108]so, even when he himself has done nothing wrong;--when the sin has

[118-109]been all with the woman. Her femininity has still a right to expect

[118-110]that so much shall be done in its behalf. But when the sin has been

[118-111]all his own, as it was in this case,--and such damning sin too,--"

[118-112]"Pray do not go on, Mrs. Thorne."

[118-113]"He ought to go out and hang himself simply for having allowed

[118-114]himself to be seen. I thought Bernard behaved very well, and I shall

[118-115]tell him so."

[118-116]"I wish you could manage to forget it all, and say no word more about

[118-117]it."

[118-118]"I won't trouble you with it, my dear; I will promise you that. But,

[118-119]Lily, I can hardly understand you. This man who must have been and

[118-120]must ever be a brute,--"

[118-121]"Mrs. Thorne, you promised me this instant that you would not talk of

[118-122]him."

[118-123]"After this I will not; but you must let me have my way now for one

[118-124]moment. I have so often longed to speak to you, but have not done so

[118-125]from fear of offending you. Now the matter has come up by chance,

[118-126]and it was impossible that what has occurred should pass by without

[118-127]a word. I cannot conceive why the memory of that bad man should be

[118-128]allowed to destroy your whole life."

[118-129]"My life is not destroyed. My life is anything but destroyed. It is a

[118-130]very happy life."

[118-131]"But, my dear, if all that I hear is true, there is a most estimable

[118-132]young man, whom everybody likes, and particularly all your own

[118-133]family, and whom you like very much yourself; and you will have

[118-134]nothing to say to him, though his constancy is like the constancy of

[118-135]an old Paladin,--and all because of this wretch who just now came in

[118-136]your way."

[118-137]"Mrs. Thorne, it is impossible to explain it all."

[118-138]"I do not want you to explain it all. Of course I would not ask any

[118-139]young woman to marry a man whom she did not love. Such marriages are

[118-140]abominable to me. But I think that a young woman ought to get married

[118-141]if the thing fairly comes in her way, and if her friends approve, and

[118-142]if she is fond of the man who is fond of her. It may be that some

[118-143]memory of what has gone before is allowed to stand in your way, and

[118-144]that it should not be so allowed. It sometimes happens that a morbid

[118-145]sentiment will destroy a life. Excuse me, then, Lily, if I say too

[118-146]much to you in my hope that you may not suffer after this fashion."

[118-147]"I know how kind you are, Mrs. Thorne."

[118-148]"Here we are at home, and perhaps you would like to go in. I have

[118-149]some calls which I must make." Then the conversation was ended, and

[118-150]Lily was alone.

[118-151]As if she had not thought of it all before! As if there was anything

[118-152]new in this counsel which Mrs. Thorne had given her! She had received

[118-153]the same advice from her mother, from her sister, from her uncle, and

[118-154]from Lady Julia, till she was sick of it. How had it come to pass

[118-155]that matters which with others are so private, should with her have

[118-156]become the public property of so large a circle? Any other girl would

[118-157]receive advice on such a subject from her mother alone, and there the

[118-158]secret would rest. But her secret had been published, as it were, by

[118-159]the town-crier in the High Street! Everybody knew that she had been

[118-160]jilted by Adolphus Crosbie, and that it was intended that she should

[118-161]be consoled by John Eames. And people seemed to think that they had

[118-162]a right to rebuke her if she expressed an unwillingness to carry out

[118-163]this intention which the public had so kindly arranged for her.

[118-164]Morbid sentiment! Why should she be accused of morbid sentiment

[118-165]because she was unable to transfer her affections to the man who

[118-166]had been fixed on as her future husband by the large circle of

[118-167]acquaintance who had interested themselves in her affairs? There was

[118-168]nothing morbid in either her desires or her regrets. So she assured

[118-169]herself, with something very like anger at the accusation made

[118-170]against her. She had been contented, and was contented, to live at

[118-171]home as her mother lived, asking for no excitement beyond that given

[118-172]by the daily routine of her duties. There could be nothing morbid in

[118-173]that. She would go back to Allington as soon as might be, and have

[118-174]done with this London life, which only made her wretched. This seeing

[118-175]of Crosbie had been terrible to her. She did not tell herself that

[118-176]his image had been shattered. Her idea was that all her misery had

[118-177]come from the untowardness of the meeting. But there was the fact

[118-178]that she had seen the man and heard his voice, and that the seeing

[118-179]him and hearing him had made her miserable. She certainly desired

[118-180]that it might never be her lot either to see him or to hear him

[118-181]again.
