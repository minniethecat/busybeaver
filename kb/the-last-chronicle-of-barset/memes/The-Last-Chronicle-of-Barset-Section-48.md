[048-001]"One would perhaps let that pass without asking her opinion at all."

[048-002]"She has acknowledged it to me, twenty times. But he is very

[048-003]good-natured, and lets her do pretty nearly anything that she likes.

[048-004]I only hope she won't trespass on his good-nature. I do, indeed."

[048-005]"You mean, spend too much money?"

[048-006]"No; I didn't mean that exactly. Of course she ought to be moderate,

[048-007]and I hope she is. To that kind of fevered existence profuse

[048-008]expenditure is perhaps necessary. But I was thinking of something

[048-009]else. I fear she is a little giddy."

[048-010]"Dear me! I should have thought she was too--too--too--"

[048-011]"You mean too old for anything of that kind. Maria Broughton must be

[048-012]thirty-three if she's a day."

[048-013]"That would make you just twenty-five," said Johnny, feeling

[048-014]perfectly sure as he said so that the lady whom he was addressing was

[048-015]at any rate past thirty!

[048-016]"Never mind my age, Mr. Eames; whether I am twenty-five, or a

[048-017]hundred-and-five, has nothing to do with poor Maria Clutterbuck. But

[048-018]now I'll tell you why I mention all this to you. You must have seen

[048-019]how foolish she is about your friend Mr. Dalrymple?"

[048-020]"Upon my word, I haven't."

[048-021]"Nonsense, Mr. Eames; you have. If she were your wife, would you like

[048-022]her to call a man Conway? Of course you would not. I don't mean to

[048-023]say that there's anything in it. I know Maria's principles too well

[048-024]to suspect that. It's merely because she's flighty and fevered."

[048-025]"That fevered existence accounts for it all," said Johnny.

[048-026]"No doubt it does," said Miss Demolines, with a nod of her head,

[048-027]which was intended to show that she was willing to give her friend

[048-028]the full benefit of any excuse which could be offered for her. "But

[048-029]don't you think you could do something, Mr. Eames?"

[048-030]"I do something?"

[048-031]"Yes, you. You and Mr. Dalrymple are such friends! If you were just

[048-032]to point out to him you know--"

[048-033]"Point out what? Tell him that he oughtn't to be called Conway?

[048-034]Because, after all, I suppose that's the worst of it. If you mean to

[048-035]say that Dalrymple is in love with Mrs. Broughton, you never made a

[048-036]greater mistake in your life."

[048-037]"Oh, no; not in love. That would be terrible, you know." And Miss

[048-038]Demolines shook her head sadly. "But there may be so much mischief

[048-039]done without anything of that kind! Thoughtlessness, you know, Mr.

[048-040]Eames,--pure thoughtlessness! Think of what I have said, and if

[048-041]you can speak a word to your friend, do. And now I want to ask you

[048-042]something else. I'm so glad you are come, because circumstances have

[048-043]seemed to make it necessary that you and I should know each other.

[048-044]We may be of so much use if we put our heads together." Johnny bowed

[048-045]when he heard this, but made no immediate reply. "Have you heard

[048-046]anything about a certain picture that is being planned?" Johnny did

[048-047]not wish to answer this question, but Miss Demolines paused so long,

[048-048]and looked so earnestly into his face, that he found himself forced

[048-049]to say something.

[048-050]"What picture?"

[048-051]"A certain picture that is--, or, perhaps, that is not to be, painted

[048-052]by Mr. Dalrymple?"

[048-053]"I hear so much about Dalrymple's pictures! You don't mean the

[048-054]portrait of Lady Glencora Palliser? That is nearly finished, and will

[048-055]be in the Exhibition this year."

[048-056]"I don't mean that at all. I mean a picture that has not yet been

[048-057]begun."

[048-058]"A portrait, I suppose?"

[048-059]"As to that I cannot quite say. It is at any rate to be a likeness.

[048-060]I am sure you have heard of it. Come, Mr. Eames; it would be better

[048-061]that we should be candid with each other. You remember Miss Van

[048-062]Siever, of course?"

[048-063]"I remember that she dined at the Broughtons'."

[048-064]"And you have heard of Jael, I suppose, and Sisera?"

[048-065]"Yes; in a general way,--in the Bible."

[048-066]"And now will you tell me whether you have not heard the names of

[048-067]Jael and Miss Van Siever coupled together? I see you know all about

[048-068]it."

[048-069]"I have heard of it, certainly."

[048-070]"Of course you have. So have I, as you perceive. Now, Mr.

[048-071]Eames,"--and Miss Demolines' voice became tremulously eager as she

[048-072]addressed him,--"it is your duty, and it is my duty, to take care

[048-073]that that picture shall never be painted."

[048-074]"But why should it not be painted?"

[048-075]"You don't know Miss Van Siever, yet."

[048-076]"Not in the least."

[048-077]"Nor Mrs. Van Siever."

[048-078]"I never spoke a word to her."

[048-079]"I do. I know them both,--well." There was something almost grandly

[048-080]tragic in Miss Demolines' voice as she thus spoke. "Yes, Mr. Eames,

[048-081]I know them well. If that scheme be continued, it will work terrible

[048-082]mischief. You and I must prevent it."

[048-083]"But I don't see what harm it will do."

[048-084]"Think of Conway Dalrymple passing so many hours in Maria's

[048-085]sitting-room upstairs! The picture is to be painted there, you know."

[048-086]"But Miss Van Siever will be present. Won't that make it all right?

[048-087]What is there wrong about Miss Van Siever?"

[048-088]"I won't deny that Clara Van Siever has a certain beauty of her own.

[048-089]To me she is certainly the most unattractive woman that I ever came

[048-090]near. She is simply repulsive!" Hereupon Miss Demolines held up her

[048-091]hand as though she were banishing Miss Van Siever for ever from

[048-092]her sight, and shuddered slightly. "Men think her handsome, and

[048-093]she is handsome. But she is false, covetous, malicious, cruel, and

[048-094]dishonest."

[048-095]"What a fiend in petticoats!"

[048-096]"You may say that, Mr. Eames. And then her mother! Her mother is

[048-097]not so bad. Her mother is very different. But the mother is an

[048-098]odious woman, too. It was an evil day for Maria Clutterbuck when

[048-099]she first saw either the mother or the daughter. I tell you that in

[048-100]confidence."

[048-101]"But what can I do?" said Johnny, who began to be startled and almost

[048-102]interested by the eagerness of the woman.

[048-103]"I'll tell you what you can do. Don't let your friend go to Mr.

[048-104]Broughton's house to paint the picture. If he does do it, there will

[048-105]mischief come of it. Of course you can prevent him."

[048-106]"I should not think of trying to prevent him unless I knew why."

[048-107]"She's a nasty proud minx, and it would set her up ever so high,--to

[048-108]think that she was being painted by Mr. Dalrymple! But that isn't the

[048-109]reason. Maria would get into terrible trouble about it, and there

[048-110]would be no end of mischief. I must not tell you more now, and if you

[048-111]do not believe me, I cannot help it. Surely, Mr. Eames, my word may

[048-112]be taken as going for something? And when I ask you to help me in

[048-113]this, I do expect that you will not refuse me." By this time Miss

[048-114]Demolines was sitting close to him, and had more than once put

[048-115]her hand upon his arm in the energy of her eloquence. Then as he

[048-116]remembered that he had never seen Miss Demolines till the other

[048-117]day, or Miss Van Siever, or even Mrs. Dobbs Broughton, he bethought

[048-118]himself that it was all very droll. Nevertheless he had no objection

[048-119]to Miss Demolines putting her hand upon his arm.

[048-120]"I never like to interfere in anything that does not seem to be my

[048-121]own business," said Johnny.

[048-122]"Is not your friend's business your own business? What does

[048-123]friendship mean if it is not so? And when I tell you that it is my

[048-124]business, mine of right, does that go for nothing with you? I thought

[048-125]I might depend upon you, Mr. Eames; I did indeed." Then again she put

[048-126]her hand upon his arm, and as he looked into her eyes he began to

[048-127]think that after all she was good-looking in a certain way. At any

[048-128]rate she had fine eyes, and there was something picturesque about the

[048-129]entanglement of her hair. "Think of it, and then come back and talk

[048-130]to me again," said Miss Demolines.

[048-131]"But I am going out of town to-morrow."

[048-132]"For how long?"

[048-133]"For ten days."

[048-134]"Nothing can be done during that time. Clara Van Siever is going

[048-135]away in a day, and will not be back for three weeks. I happen to

[048-136]know that; so we have plenty of time for working. It would be very

[048-137]desirable that she should never even hear of it; but that cannot be

[048-138]hoped, as Maria has such a tongue! Couldn't you see Mr. Dalrymple

[048-139]to-night?"

[048-140]"Well, no; I don't think I could."

[048-141]"Mind, at least, that you come to me as soon as ever you return."

[048-142]Before he got out of the house, which he did after a most

[048-143]affectionate farewell, Johnny felt himself compelled to promise that

[048-144]he would come to Miss Demolines again as soon as he got back to town;

[048-145]and as the door was closed behind him by the boy in buttons, he made

[048-146]up his mind that he certainly would call as soon as he returned to

[048-147]London. "It's as good as a play," he said to himself. Not that he

[048-148]cared in the least for Miss Demolines, or that he would take any

[048-149]steps with the intention of preventing the painting of the picture.

[048-150]Miss Demolines had some battle to fight, and he would leave her to

[048-151]fight it with her own weapons. If his friend chose to paint a picture

[048-152]of Jael, and take Miss Van Siever as a model, it was no business of

[048-153]his. Nevertheless he would certainly go and see Miss Demolines again,

[048-154]because, as he said, she was as good as a play.

[048-155]CHAPTER XXVI.

[048-156]THE PICTURE.

[048-157]On that same afternoon Conway Dalrymple rolled up his sketch of

[048-158]Jael and Sisera, put it into his pocket, dressed himself with some

[048-159]considerable care, putting on a velvet coat which he was in the habit

[048-160]of wearing out of doors when he did not intend to wander beyond

[048-161]Kensington Gardens and the neighbourhood and which was supposed to

[048-162]become him well, yellow gloves, and a certain Spanish hat of which he

[048-163]was fond, and slowly sauntered across to the house of his friend Mrs.

[048-164]Dobbs Broughton. When the door was opened to him he did not ask if

[048-165]the lady were at home, but muttering some word to the servant, made

[048-166]his way through the hall, upstairs, to a certain small sitting-room

[048-167]looking to the north, which was much used by the mistress of the

[048-168]house. It was quite clear that Conway Dalrymple had arranged his

[048-169]visit beforehand, and that he was expected. He opened the door

[048-170]without knocking, and, though the servant had followed him, he

[048-171]entered without being announced. "I'm afraid I'm late," he said, as

[048-172]he gave his hand to Mrs. Broughton; "but for the life I could not get

[048-173]away sooner."

[048-174]"You are quite in time," said the lady, "for any good that you are

[048-175]likely to do."

[048-176]"What does that mean?"

[048-177]"It means this, my friend, that you had better give the idea up. I

[048-178]have been thinking of it all day, and I do not approve of it."

[048-179]"What nonsense!"

[048-180]"Of course you will say so, Conway. I have observed of late that

[048-181]whatever I say to you is called nonsense. I suppose it is the new

[048-182]fashion that gentlemen should so express themselves, but I am not

[048-183]quite sure that I like it."

[048-184]"You know what I mean. I am very anxious about this picture, and I

[048-185]shall be much disappointed if it cannot be done now. It was you put

[048-186]it into my head first."

[048-187]"I regret it very much, I can assure you; but it will not be generous

[048-188]in you to urge that against me."

[048-189]"But why shouldn't it succeed?"

[048-190]"There are many reasons,--some personal to myself."

[048-191]"I do not know what they can be. You hinted at something which I only

[048-192]took as having been said in joke."
