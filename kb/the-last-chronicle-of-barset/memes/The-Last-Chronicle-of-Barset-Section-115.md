[115-001]"It doesn't matter where the fox was bred. It shouldn't have been

[115-002]trapped," said the major.

[115-003]"Of course not," said the archdeacon, indignantly. I wonder whether

[115-004]he would have been so keen had a Romanist priest come into his

[115-005]parish, and turned one of his Protestants into a Papist?

[115-006]Then Flurry came up, and produced the identical pad out of his

[115-007]pocket. "I don't suppose it was intended," said the major, looking

[115-008]at the interesting relic with scrutinizing eyes. "I suppose it was

[115-009]caught in a rabbit-trap,--eh, Flurry?"

[115-010]"I don't see what right a man has with traps at all, when gentlemen

[115-011]is particular about their foxes," said Flurry. "Of course they'd call

[115-012]it rabbits."

[115-013]"I never liked that man on Darvell's farm," said the archdeacon.

[115-014]"Nor I either," said Flurry. "No farmer ought to be on that land

[115-015]who don't have a horse of his own. And if I war Squire Thorne, I

[115-016]wouldn't have no farmer there who didn't keep no horse. When a farmer

[115-017]has a horse of his own, and follies the hounds, there ain't no

[115-018]rabbit-traps;--never. How does that come about, Mr. Henry? Rabbits! I

[115-019]know very well what rabbits is!"

[115-020]Mr. Henry shook his head, and turned away, and the archdeacon

[115-021]followed him. There was an hypocrisy about this pretended care for

[115-022]the foxes which displeased the major. He could not, of course, tell

[115-023]his father that the foxes were no longer anything to him; but yet

[115-024]he must make it understood that such was his conviction. His mother

[115-025]had written to him, saying that the sale of furniture need not take

[115-026]place. It might be all very well for his mother to say that, or for

[115-027]his father; but, after what had taken place, he could consent to

[115-028]remain in England on no other understanding than that his income

[115-029]should be made permanent to him. Such permanence must not be any

[115-030]longer dependent on his father's caprice. In these days he had come

[115-031]to be somewhat in love with poverty and Pau, and had been feeding on

[115-032]the luxury of his grievance. There is, perhaps, nothing so pleasant

[115-033]as the preparation for self-sacrifice. To give up Cosby Lodge and

[115-034]the foxes, to marry a penniless wife, and go and live at Pau on six

[115-035]or seven hundred a year, seemed just now to Major Grantly to be a

[115-036]fine thing, and he did not intend to abandon this fine thing without

[115-037]receiving a very clear reason for doing so. "I can't quite understand

[115-038]Thorne," said the archdeacon. "He used to be so particular about the

[115-039]foxes, and I don't suppose that a country gentleman will change his

[115-040]ideas because he has given up hunting himself."

[115-041]"Mr. Thorne never thought much of Flurry," said Henry Grantly, with

[115-042]his mind intent upon Pau and his grievance.

[115-043]"He might take my word at any rate," said the archdeacon.

[115-044]It was a known fact that the archdeacon's solicitude about the

[115-045]Plumstead covers was wholly on behalf of his son the major. The major

[115-046]himself knew this thoroughly, and felt that his father's present

[115-047]special anxiety was intended as a corroboration of the tidings

[115-048]conveyed in his mother's letter. Every word so uttered was meant

[115-049]to have reference to his son's future residence in the country.

[115-050]"Father," he said, turning round shortly, and standing before the

[115-051]archdeacon in the pathway, "I think you are quite right about the

[115-052]covers. I feel sure that every gentleman who preserves a fox does

[115-053]good to the country. I am sorry that I shall not have a closer

[115-054]interest in the matter myself."

[115-055]"Why shouldn't you have a closer interest in it?" said the

[115-056]archdeacon.

[115-057]"Because I shall be living abroad."

[115-058]"You got your mother's letter?"

[115-059]"Yes; I got my mother's letter."

[115-060]"Did she not tell you that you can stay where you are?"

[115-061]"Yes, she said so. But, to tell you the truth, sir, I do not like the

[115-062]risk of living beyond my assured income." "But if I justify it?"

[115-063]"I do not wish to complain, sir, but you have made me understand that

[115-064]you can, and that in certain circumstances you will, at a moment,

[115-065]withdraw what you give me. Since this was said to me, I have felt

[115-066]myself to be unsafe in such a house as Cosby Lodge."

[115-067]The archdeacon did not know how to explain. He had intended that

[115-068]the real explanation should be given by Mrs. Grantly, and had been

[115-069]anxious to return to his old relations with his son without any exact

[115-070]terms on his own part. But his son was, as he thought, awkward, and

[115-071]would drive him to some speech that was unnecessary. "You need not be

[115-072]unsafe there at all," he said, half angrily.

[115-073]"I must be unsafe if I am not sure of my income."

[115-074]"Your income is not in any danger. But you had better speak to your

[115-075]mother about it. For myself, I think I may say that I have never yet

[115-076]behaved to any of you with harshness. A son should, at any rate,

[115-077]not be offended because a father thinks that he is entitled to some

[115-078]consideration for what he does."

[115-079]"There are some points on which a son cannot give way even to his

[115-080]father, sir."

[115-081]"You had better speak to your mother, Henry. She will explain to you

[115-082]what has taken place. Look at that plantation. You don't remember it,

[115-083]but every tree there was planted since you were born. I bought that

[115-084]farm from old Mr. Thorne, when he was purchasing St. Ewold's Downs,

[115-085]and it was the first bit of land I ever had of my own."

[115-086]"That is not in Plumstead, I think?"

[115-087]"No: this is Plumstead, where we stand, but that's in Eiderdown. The

[115-088]parishes run in and out here. I never bought any other land as cheap

[115-089]as I bought that."

[115-090]"And did old Thorne make a good purchase at St. Ewold's?"

[115-091]"Yes, I fancy he did. It gave him the whole of the parish, which was

[115-092]a great thing. It is astonishing how land has risen in value since

[115-093]that, and yet rents are not so very much higher. They who buy land

[115-094]now can't have above two-and-a-half for their money."

[115-095]"I wonder people are so fond of land," said the major.

[115-096]"It is a comfortable feeling to know that you stand on your own

[115-097]ground. Land is about the only thing that can't fly away. And then,

[115-098]you see, land gives so much more than the rent. It gives position and

[115-099]influence and political power, to say nothing about the game. We'll

[115-100]go back now. I daresay your mother will be at home by this time."

[115-101]The archdeacon was striving to teach a great lesson to his son when

[115-102]he thus spoke of the pleasure which a man feels when he stands upon

[115-103]his own ground. He was bidding his son to understand how great was

[115-104]the position of an heir to a landed property, and how small the

[115-105]position of a man depending on what Dr. Grantly himself would have

[115-106]called a scratch income,--an income made up of a few odds and ends,

[115-107]a share or two in this company and a share or two in that, a slight

[115-108]venture in foreign stocks, a small mortgage and such like convenient

[115-109]but uninfluential driblets. A man, no doubt, may live at Pau on

[115-110]driblets; may pay his way and drink his bottle of cheap wine, and

[115-111]enjoy life after a fashion while reading Galignani and looking at the

[115-112]mountains. But,--as it seemed to the archdeacon,--when there was a

[115-113]choice between this kind of thing, and fox-covers at Plumstead, and a

[115-114]seat among the magistrates of Barsetshire, and an establishment full

[115-115]of horses, beeves, swine, carriages, and hayricks, a man brought up

[115-116]as his son had been brought up ought not to be very long in choosing.

[115-117]It never entered into the archdeacon's mind that he was tempting his

[115-118]son; but Henry Grantly felt that he was having the good things of the

[115-119]world shown to him, and that he was being told that they should be

[115-120]his--for a consideration.

[115-121]The major, in his present mood, looked at the matter from his own

[115-122]point of view, and determined that the consideration was too high.

[115-123]He was pledged not to give up Grace Crawley, and he would not yield

[115-124]on that point, though he might be tempted by all the fox-covers in

[115-125]Barsetshire. At this moment he did not know how far his father was

[115-126]prepared to yield, or how far it was expected that he should yield

[115-127]himself. He was told that he had to speak to his mother. He would

[115-128]speak to his mother, but, in the meantime, he could not bring himself

[115-129]to make a comfortable answer to his father's eloquent praise of

[115-130]landed property. He could not allow himself to be enthusiastic on the

[115-131]matter till he knew what was expected of him if he chose to submit to

[115-132]be made a British squire. At present Galignani and the mountains had

[115-133]their charms for him. There was, therefore, but little conversation

[115-134]between the father and the son as they walked back to the rectory.

[115-135]Late that night the major heard the whole story from his mother.

[115-136]Gradually, and as though unintentionally, Mrs. Grantly told him all

[115-137]she knew of the archdeacon's visit to Framley. Mrs. Grantly was quite

[115-138]as anxious as was her husband to keep her son at home, and therefore

[115-139]she omitted in her story those little sneers against Grace which she

[115-140]herself had been tempted to make by the archdeacon's fervour in the

[115-141]girl's favour. The major said as little as was possible while he was

[115-142]being told of his father's adventure, and expressed neither anger

[115-143]nor satisfaction till he had been made thoroughly to understand that

[115-144]Grace had pledged herself not to marry him as long as any suspicion

[115-145]should rest upon her father's name.

[115-146]"Your father is quite satisfied with her," said Mrs. Grantly. "He

[115-147]thinks that she is behaving very well."

[115-148]"My father had no right to exact such a pledge."

[115-149]"But she made it of her own accord. She was the first to speak about

[115-150]Mr. Crawley's supposed guilt. Your father never mentioned it."

[115-151]"He must have led to it; and I think he had no right to do so. He had

[115-152]no right to go to her at all."

[115-153]"Now don't be foolish, Henry."

[115-154]"I don't see that I am foolish."

[115-155]"Yes, you are. A man is foolish if he won't take what he wants

[115-156]without asking exactly how he is to come by it. That your father

[115-157]should be anxious is the most natural thing in the world. You know

[115-158]how high he has always held his own head, and how much he thinks

[115-159]about the characters and position of clergymen. It is not surprising

[115-160]that he should dislike the idea of such a marriage."

[115-161]"Grace Crawley would disgrace no family," said the lover.

[115-162]"That's all very well for you to say, and I'll take your word that it

[115-163]is so;--that is as far as the young lady goes herself. And there's

[115-164]your father almost as much in love with her as you are. I don't know

[115-165]what you would have?"

[115-166]"I would be left alone."

[115-167]"But what harm has been done you? From what you yourself have told

[115-168]me, I know that Miss Crawley has said the same thing to you that she

[115-169]has said to your father. You can't but admire her for the feeling."

[115-170]"I admire her for everything."

[115-171]"Very well. We don't say anything against that."

[115-172]"And I don't mean to give her up."

[115-173]"Very well again. Let us hope that Mr. Crawley will be acquitted, and

[115-174]then all will be right. Your father never goes back from his promise.

[115-175]He is always better than his word. You'll find that if Mr. Crawley

[115-176]is acquitted, or if he escapes in any way, your father will only be

[115-177]happy of an excuse to make much of the young lady. You should not be

[115-178]hard on him, Henry. Don't you see that it is his one great desire to

[115-179]keep you near to him? The sight of those odious bills nearly broke

[115-180]his heart."
