[097-001]"Mark says that the bishop could hardly do anything else," rejoined

[097-002]Mrs. Robarts.

[097-003]"I daresay not, my dear. I suppose the bishop has somebody near him

[097-004]to tell him what he may do, and what he may not do. It would be

[097-005]terrible to think of, if it were not so. But yet, when I hear that

[097-006]he has named such men as Mr. Thumble and Mr. Quiverful, I cannot but

[097-007]feel that the whole diocese is disgraced."

[097-008]"Oh, Lady Lufton, that is such a strong word," said Mrs. Robarts.

[097-009]"It may be strong, but it is not the less true," said Lady Lufton.

[097-010]And from talking on the subject of the Crawleys, Lady Lufton soon

[097-011]advanced, first to a desire for some action, and then to acting.

[097-012]"I think, my dear, I will go over and see Mrs. Crawley," said Lady

[097-013]Lufton the elder to Lady Lufton the younger. Lady Lufton the younger

[097-014]had nothing to urge against this; but she did not offer to accompany

[097-015]the elder lady. I attempted to explain in the early part of this

[097-016]story that there still existed a certain understanding between Mrs.

[097-017]Crawley and Lord Lufton's wife, and that kindnesses occasionally

[097-018]passed from Framley Court to Hogglestock Parsonage; but on this

[097-019]occasion young Lady Lufton,--the Lucy Robarts who had once passed

[097-020]certain days of her life with the Crawleys at Hogglestock,--did not

[097-021]choose to accompany her mother-in-law; and therefore Mrs. Robarts was

[097-022]invited to do so. "I think it may comfort her to know that she has

[097-023]our sympathy," the elder woman said to the younger as they made their

[097-024]journey together.

[097-025]When the carriage stopped before the little wicket-gate, from whence

[097-026]a path led through a ragged garden from the road to Mr. Crawley's

[097-027]house, Lady Lufton hardly knew how to proceed. The servant came to

[097-028]the door of the carriage, and asked for her orders. "H--m--m, ha,

[097-029]yes; I think I'll send in my card;--and say that I hope Mrs. Crawley

[097-030]will be able to see me. Won't that be best; eh, Fanny?" Fanny,

[097-031]otherwise Mrs. Robarts, said that she thought that would be best; and

[097-032]the card and message were carried in.

[097-033]It was happily the case that Mr. Crawley was not at home. Mr. Crawley

[097-034]was away at Hoggle End, reading to the brickmakers, or turning the

[097-035]mangles of their wives, or teaching them theology, or politics, or

[097-036]history, after his fashion. In these days he spent, perhaps, the

[097-037]happiest hours of his life down at Hoggle End. I say that his absence

[097-038]was a happy chance, because, had he been at home, he would certainly

[097-039]have said something, or done something, to offend Lady Lufton. He

[097-040]would either have refused to see her, or when seeing her he would

[097-041]have bade her hold her peace and not interfere with matters which did

[097-042]not concern her, or,--more probable still,--he would have sat still

[097-043]and sullen, and have spoken not at all. But he was away, and Mrs.

[097-044]Crawley sent out word by the servant that she would be most proud to

[097-045]see her ladyship, if her ladyship would be pleased to alight. Her

[097-046]ladyship did alight, and walked into the parsonage, followed by Mrs.

[097-047]Robarts.

[097-048]Grace was with her mother. Indeed Jane had been there also when the

[097-049]message was brought in, but she fled into back regions, overcome by

[097-050]shame as to her frock. Grace, I think, would have fled too, had she

[097-051]not been bound in honour to support her mother. Lady Lufton, as she

[097-052]entered, was very gracious, struggling with all the power of her

[097-053]womanhood so to carry herself that there should be no outwardly

[097-054]visible sign of her rank or her wealth,--but not altogether

[097-055]succeeding. Mrs. Robarts, on her first entrance, said only a word or

[097-056]two of greeting to Mrs. Crawley, and kissed Grace, whom she had known

[097-057]intimately in early years. "Lady Lufton," said Mrs. Crawley, "I am

[097-058]afraid this is a very poor place for you to come to; but you have

[097-059]known that of old, and therefore I need hardly apologize."

[097-060]"Sometimes I like poor places best," said Lady Lufton. Then there was

[097-061]a pause, after which Lady Lufton addressed herself to Grace, seeking

[097-062]some subject for immediate conversation. "You have been down at

[097-063]Allington, my dear, have you not?" Grace, in a whisper, said that she

[097-064]had. "Staying with the Dales, I believe? I know the Dales well by

[097-065]name, and I have always heard that they are charming people."

[097-066]"I like them very much," said Grace. And then there was another

[097-067]pause.

[097-068]"I hope your husband is pretty well, Mrs. Crawley?" said Lady Lufton.

[097-069]"He is pretty well,--not quite strong. I daresay you know, Lady

[097-070]Lufton, that he has things to vex him?" Mrs. Crawley felt that it was

[097-071]the need of the moment that the only possible subject of conversation

[097-072]in that house should be introduced; and therefore she brought it in

[097-073]at once, not loving the subject, but being strongly conscious of the

[097-074]necessity. Lady Lufton meant to be good-natured, and therefore Mrs.

[097-075]Crawley would do all in her power to make Lady Lufton's mission easy

[097-076]to her.

[097-077]"Indeed yes," said her ladyship; "we do know that."

[097-078]"We feel so much for you and Mr. Crawley," said Mrs. Robarts; "and

[097-079]are so sure that your sufferings are unmerited." This was not

[097-080]discreet on the part of Mrs. Robarts, as she was the wife of one

[097-081]of the clergymen who had been selected to form the commission of

[097-082]inquiry; and so Lady Lufton told her on their way home.

[097-083]"You are very kind," said Mrs. Crawley. "We must only bear it with

[097-084]such fortitude as God will give us. We are told that He tempers the

[097-085]wind to the shorn lamb."

[097-086]"And so He does, my dear," said the old lady, very solemnly. "So He

[097-087]does. Surely you have felt that it is so?"

[097-088]"I struggle not to complain," said Mrs. Crawley.

[097-089]"I know that you struggle bravely. I hear of you, and I admire you

[097-090]for it, and I love you." It was still the old lady who was speaking,

[097-091]and now she had at last been roused out of her difficulty as to

[097-092]words, and had risen from her chair, and was standing before Mrs.

[097-093]Crawley. "It is because you do not complain, because you are so great

[097-094]and so good, because your character is so high, and your spirit so

[097-095]firm, that I could not resist the temptation of coming to you. Mrs.

[097-096]Crawley, if you will let me be your friend, I shall be proud of your

[097-097]friendship."

[097-098]"Your ladyship is too good," said Mrs. Crawley.

[097-099]"Do not talk to me after that fashion," said Lady Lufton. "If you do

[097-100]I shall be disappointed, and feel myself thrown back. You know what

[097-101]I mean." She paused for an answer; but Mrs. Crawley had no answer to

[097-102]make. She simply shook her head, not knowing why she did so. But we

[097-103]may know. We can understand that she had felt that the friendship

[097-104]offered to her by Lady Lufton was an impossibility. She had decided

[097-105]within her own breast that it was so, though she did not know that

[097-106]she had come to such decision. "I wish you to take me at my word,

[097-107]Mrs. Crawley," continued Lady Lufton. "What can we do for you? We

[097-108]know that you are distressed."

[097-109]"Yes,--we are distressed."

[097-110]"And we know how cruel circumstances have been to you. Will you not

[097-111]forgive me for being plain?"

[097-112]"I have nothing to forgive," said Mrs. Crawley.

[097-113]"Lady Lufton means," said Mrs. Robarts, "that in asking you to talk

[097-114]openly to her of your affairs, she wishes you to remember that-- I

[097-115]think you know what we mean," said Mrs. Robarts, knowing very well

[097-116]herself what she did mean, but not knowing at all how to express

[097-117]herself.

[097-118]"Lady Lufton is very kind," said Mrs. Crawley, "and so are you, Mrs.

[097-119]Robarts. I know how good you both are, and for how much it behoves

[097-120]me to be grateful." These words were very cold, and the voice in

[097-121]which they were spoken was very cold. They made Lady Lufton feel that

[097-122]it was beyond her power to proceed with the work of her mission in

[097-123]its intended spirit. It is ever so much easier to proffer kindness

[097-124]graciously than to receive it with grace. Lady Lufton had intended

[097-125]to say, "Let us be women together;--women bound by humanity, and not

[097-126]separated by rank, and let us open our hearts freely. Let us see how

[097-127]we may be of comfort to each other." And could she have succeeded in

[097-128]this, she would have spread out her little plans of succour with so

[097-129]loving a hand that she would have conquered the woman before her. But

[097-130]the suffering spirit cannot descend from its dignity of reticence. It

[097-131]has a nobility of its own, made sacred by many tears, by the flowing

[097-132]of streams of blood from unseen wounds, which cannot descend from its

[097-133]daïs to receive pity and kindness. A consciousness of undeserved woe

[097-134]produces a grandeur of its own, with which the high-souled sufferer

[097-135]will not easily part. Baskets full of eggs, pounds of eleemosynary

[097-136]butter, quarters of given pork, even second-hand clothing from the

[097-137]wardrobe of some richer sister,--even money, unsophisticated money,

[097-138]she could accept. She had learned to know that it was a portion

[097-139]of her allotted misery to take such things,--for the sake of her

[097-140]children and her husband,--and to be thankful for them. She did take

[097-141]them, and was thankful; and in the taking she submitted herself to

[097-142]the rod of cruel circumstances; but she could not even yet bring

[097-143]herself to accept spoken pity from a stranger, and to kiss the

[097-144]speaker.

[097-145]"Can we not do something to help you?" said Mrs. Robarts. She would

[097-146]not have spoken but that she perceived that Lady Lufton had completed

[097-147]her appeal, and that Mrs. Crawley did not seem prepared to answer it.

[097-148]"You have done much to help us," said Mrs. Crawley. "The things you

[097-149]have sent to us have been very serviceable."

[097-150]"But we mean something more than that," said Lady Lufton.

[097-151]"I do not know what there is more," said Mrs. Crawley. "A bit to eat

[097-152]and something to wear;--that seems to be all that we have to care for

[097-153]now."

[097-154]"But we were afraid that this coming trial must cause you so much

[097-155]anxiety."

[097-156]"Of course it causes anxiety;--but what can we do? It must be so. It

[097-157]cannot be put off, or avoided. We have made up our minds to it now,

[097-158]and almost wish that it would come quicker. If it were once over I

[097-159]think that he would be better whatever the result might be."

[097-160]Then there was another lull in the conversation, and Lady Lufton

[097-161]began to be afraid that her visit would be a failure. She thought

[097-162]that perhaps she might get on better if Grace were not in the room,

[097-163]and she turned over in her mind various schemes for sending her away.

[097-164]And perhaps her task would be easier if Mrs. Robarts also could be

[097-165]banished for a time. "Fanny, my dear," she said at last, boldly, "I

[097-166]know you have a little plan to arrange with Miss Crawley. Perhaps you

[097-167]will be more likely to be successful if you can take a turn with her

[097-168]alone." There was not much subtlety in her ladyship's scheme; but it

[097-169]answered the proposed purpose, and the two elder ladies were soon

[097-170]left face to face, so that Lady Lufton had a fair pretext for making

[097-171]another attempt. "Dear Mrs. Crawley," she said, "I do so long to say

[097-172]a word to you, but I fear that I may be thought to interfere."

[097-173]"Oh, no, Lady Lufton; I have no feeling of that kind."

[097-174]"I have asked your daughter and Mrs. Robarts to go out because I can

[097-175]speak more easily to you alone. I wish I could teach you to trust

[097-176]me."
