[032-001]"No authority in your own diocese!"

[032-002]"None to silence a man merely by my own judgment. I thought, and

[032-003]still think, that it was for this gentleman's own interest, as well

[032-004]as for the credit of the Church, that some provision should be made

[032-005]for his duties during his present,--present--difficulties."

[032-006]"Difficulties indeed! Everybody knows that the man has been a thief."

[032-007]"No, my dear; I do not know it."

[032-008]"You never know anything, bishop."

[032-009]"I mean to say that I do not know it officially. Of course I have

[032-010]heard the sad story; and, though I hope it may not be the--"

[032-011]"There is no doubt about its truth. All the world knows it. He has

[032-012]stolen twenty pounds, and yet he is to be allowed to desecrate the

[032-013]Church, and imperil the souls of the people!" The bishop got up from

[032-014]his chair and began to walk backwards and forwards through the room

[032-015]with short quick steps. "It only wants five days to Christmas Day,"

[032-016]continued Mrs. Proudie, "and something must be done at once. I say

[032-017]nothing as to the propriety or impropriety of his being out on bail,

[032-018]as it is no affair of ours. When I heard that he had been bailed by a

[032-019]beneficed clergyman of this diocese, of course I knew where to look

[032-020]for the man who would act with so much impropriety. Of course I was

[032-021]not surprised when I found that that person belonged to Framley.

[032-022]But, as I have said before, that is no business of ours. I hope, Mr.

[032-023]Thumble, that the bishop will never be found interfering with the

[032-024]ordinary laws of the land. I am very sure that he will never do so by

[032-025]my advice. But when there comes a question of inhibiting a clergyman

[032-026]who has committed himself as this clergyman unfortunately has done,

[032-027]then I say that that clergyman ought to be inhibited." The bishop

[032-028]walked up and down the room throughout the whole of this speech, but

[032-029]gradually his steps became quicker, and his turns became shorter.

[032-030]"And now here is Christmas Day upon us, and what is to be done?" With

[032-031]these words Mrs. Proudie finished her speech.

[032-032]"Mr. Thumble," said the bishop, "perhaps you had better now retire.

[032-033]I am very sorry that you should have had so thankless and so

[032-034]disagreeable a task."

[032-035]"Why should Mr. Thumble retire?" asked Mrs. Proudie.

[032-036]"I think it better," said the bishop. "Mr. Thumble, good night." Then

[032-037]Mr. Thumble did retire, and Mrs. Proudie stood forth in her full

[032-038]panoply of armour, silent and awful, with her helmet erect, and

[032-039]vouchsafed no recognition whatever of the parting salutation with

[032-040]which Mr. Thumble greeted her. "My dear, the truth is, you do not

[032-041]understand the matter," said the bishop as soon as the door was

[032-042]closed. "You do not know how limited is my power."

[032-043]"Bishop, I understand it a great deal better than some people; and I

[032-044]understand also what is due to myself and the manner in which I ought

[032-045]to be treated by you in the presence of the subordinate clergy of the

[032-046]diocese. I shall not, however, remain here to be insulted either in

[032-047]the presence or in the absence of any one." Then the conquered amazon

[032-048]collected together the weapons which she had laid upon the table, and

[032-049]took her departure with majestic step, and not without the clang of

[032-050]arms. The bishop, when he was left alone, enjoyed for a few moments

[032-051]the triumph of his victory.

[032-052]But then he was left so very much alone! When he looked round about

[032-053]him upon his solitude after the departure of his wife, and remembered

[032-054]that he should not see her again till he should encounter her on

[032-055]ground that was all her own, he regretted his own success, and was

[032-056]tempted to follow her and to apologize. He was unable to do anything

[032-057]alone. He would not even know how to get his tea, as the very

[032-058]servants would ask questions, if he were to do so unaccustomed

[032-059]a thing as to order it to be brought up to him in his solitude.

[032-060]They would tell him that Mrs. Proudie was having tea in her little

[032-061]sitting-room upstairs, or else that the things were laid in the

[032-062]drawing-room. He did wander forth to the latter apartment, hoping

[032-063]that he might find his wife there; but the drawing-room was dark

[032-064]and deserted, and so he wandered back again. It was a grand thing

[032-065]certainly to have triumphed over his wife, and there was a crumb

[032-066]of comfort in the thought that he had vindicated himself before Mr.

[032-067]Thumble; but the general result was not comforting, and he knew from

[032-068]of old how short-lived his triumph would be.

[032-069]But wretched as he was during that evening he did employ himself with

[032-070]some energy. After much thought he resolved that he would again write

[032-071]to Mr. Crawley, and summon him to appear at the palace. In doing this

[032-072]he would at any rate be doing something. There would be action. And

[032-073]though Mr. Crawley would, as he thought, decline to obey the order,

[032-074]something would be gained even by that disobedience. So he wrote

[032-075]his summons,--sitting very comfortless and all alone on that Sunday

[032-076]evening,--dating his letter, however, for the following day:--

[032-077]Palace, December 20, 186--.

[032-078]REVEREND SIR,

[032-079]I have just heard from Mr. Thumble that you have declined

[032-080]to accede to the advice which I thought it my duty to

[032-081]tender to you as the bishop who has been set over you by

[032-082]the Church, and that you yesterday insisted on what you

[032-083]believed to be your right, to administer the services in

[032-084]the parish church of Hogglestock. This has occasioned me

[032-085]the deepest regret. It is, I think, unavailing that I

[032-086]should further write to you my mind upon the subject, as

[032-087]I possess such strong evidence that my written word will

[032-088]not be respected by you. I have, therefore, no alternative

[032-089]now but to invite you to come to me here; and this I do,

[032-090]hoping that I may induce you to listen to that authority

[032-091]which I cannot but suppose you acknowledge to be vested in

[032-092]the office which I hold.

[032-093]I shall be glad to see you on to-morrow, Tuesday, as near

[032-094]the hour of two as you can make it convenient to yourself

[032-095]to be here, and I will take care to order that refreshment

[032-096]shall be provided for yourself and your horse.

[032-097]I am, Reverend Sir,

[032-098]&c. &c. &c.,

[032-099]THOS. BARNUM.

[032-100]"My dear," he said, when he did again encounter his wife that night,

[032-101]"I have written to Mr. Crawley, and I thought I might as well bring

[032-102]up the copy of my letter."

[032-103]"I wash my hands of the whole affair," said Mrs. Proudie--"of the

[032-104]whole affair!"

[032-105]"But you will look at the letter?"

[032-106]"Certainly not. Why should I look at the letter? My word goes for

[032-107]nothing. I have done what I could, but in vain. Now let us see how

[032-108]you will manage it yourself."

[032-109]The bishop did not pass a comfortable night; but in the morning

[032-110]his wife did read his letter, and after that things went a little

[032-111]smoother with him. She was pleased to say that, considering all

[032-112]things; seeing, as she could not help seeing, that the matter

[032-113]had been dreadfully mismanaged, and that great weakness had been

[032-114]displayed;--seeing that these faults had already been committed,

[032-115]perhaps no better step could now be taken than that proposed in the

[032-116]letter.

[032-117]"I suppose he will not come," said the bishop.

[032-118]"I think he will," said Mrs. Proudie, "and I trust that we may be

[032-119]able to convince him that obedience will be his best course. He will

[032-120]be more humble-minded here than at Hogglestock." In saying this the

[032-121]lady showed some knowledge of the general nature of clergymen and of

[032-122]the world at large. She understood how much louder a cock can crow in

[032-123]its own farmyard than elsewhere, and knew that episcopal authority,

[032-124]backed by all the solemn awe of palatial grandeur, goes much further

[032-125]than it will do when sent under the folds of an ordinary envelope.

[032-126]But though she understood ordinary human nature, it may be that she

[032-127]did not understand Mr. Crawley's nature.

[032-128]But she was at any rate right in her idea as to Mr. Crawley's

[032-129]immediate reply. The palace groom who rode over to Hogglestock

[032-130]returned with an immediate answer.

[032-131]"MY LORD"--said Mr. Crawley.

[032-132]I will obey your lordship's summons, and, unless

[032-133]impediments should arise, I will wait upon your lordship

[032-134]at the hour you name to-morrow. I will not trespass on

[032-135]your hospitality. For myself, I rarely break bread in any

[032-136]house but my own; and as to the horse, I have none.

[032-137]I have the honour to be,

[032-138]My lord, &c. &c.,

[032-139]JOSIAH CRAWLEY.

[032-140]"Of course I shall go," he had said to his wife as soon as he had had

[032-141]time to read the letter, and make known to her the contents. "I shall

[032-142]go if it be possible for me to get there. I think that I am bound to

[032-143]comply with the bishop's wishes in so much as that."

[032-144]"But how will you get there, Josiah?"

[032-145]"I will walk,--with the Lord's aid."

[032-146]Now Hogglestock was fifteen miles from Barchester, and Mr. Crawley

[032-147]was, as his wife well knew, by no means fitted in his present state

[032-148]for great physical exertion. But from the tone in which he had

[032-149]replied to her, she well knew that it would not avail for her to

[032-150]remonstrate at the moment. He had walked more than thirty miles in a

[032-151]day since they had been living at Hogglestock, and she did not doubt

[032-152]but that it might be possible for him to do it again. Any scheme,

[032-153]which she might be able to devise for saving him from so terrible

[032-154]a journey in the middle of winter, must be pondered over silently,

[032-155]and brought to bear, if not slyly, at least deftly, and without

[032-156]discussion. She made no reply therefore when he declared that on

[032-157]the following day he would walk to Barchester and back,--with the

[032-158]Lord's aid; nor did she see, or ask to see the note which he sent

[032-159]to the bishop. When the messenger was gone, Mr. Crawley was all

[032-160]alert, looking forward with evident glee to his encounter with the

[032-161]bishop,--snorting like a racehorse at the expected triumph of the

[032-162]coming struggle. And he read much Greek with Jane on that afternoon,

[032-163]pouring into her young ears, almost with joyous rapture, his

[032-164]appreciation of the glory and the pathos and the humanity, as also of

[032-165]the awful tragedy, of the story of Oedipus. His very soul was on fire

[032-166]at the idea of clutching the weak bishop in his hand, and crushing

[032-167]him with his strong grasp.

[032-168]In the afternoon Mrs. Crawley slipped out to a neighbouring farmer's

[032-169]wife, and returned in an hour's time with a little story which she

[032-170]did not tell with any appearance of eager satisfaction. She had

[032-171]learned well what were the little tricks necessary to the carrying

[032-172]of such a matter as that which she had now in hand. Mr. Mangle, the

[032-173]farmer, as it happened, was going to-morrow morning in his tax-cart

[032-174]as far as Framley Mill, and would be delighted if Mr. Crawley

[032-175]would take a seat. He must remain at Framley the best part of

[032-176]the afternoon, and hoped that Mr. Crawley would take a seat back

[032-177]again. Now Framley Mill was only half a mile off the direct road to

[032-178]Barchester, and was almost half way from Hogglestock parsonage to the

[032-179]city. This would, at any rate, bring the walk within a practicable

[032-180]distance. Mr. Crawley was instantly placed upon his guard, like an

[032-181]animal that sees the bait and suspects the trap. Had he been told

[032-182]that farmer Mangle was going all the way to Barchester, nothing would

[032-183]have induced him to get into the cart. He would have felt sure that

[032-184]farmer Mangle had been persuaded to pity him in his poverty and his

[032-185]strait, and he would sooner have started to walk to London than have

[032-186]put a foot upon the step of the cart. But this lift half way did look

[032-187]to him as though it were really fortuitous. His wife could hardly

[032-188]have been cunning enough to persuade the farmer to go to Framley,

[032-189]conscious that the trap would have been suspected had the bait been

[032-190]made more full. But I fear,--I fear the dear good woman had been thus

[032-191]cunning,--had understood how far the trap might be baited, and had

[032-192]thus succeeded in catching her prey.
