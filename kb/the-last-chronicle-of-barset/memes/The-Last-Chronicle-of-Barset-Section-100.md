[100-001]"Age does not go by years," said Mrs. Dobbs Broughton. "We all know

[100-002]that. 'His hair was grey, but not with years.' Look here, Conway,"

[100-003]and she moved back her tresses from off her temples to show him that

[100-004]there were gray hairs behind. He did not see them; and had they been

[100-005]very visible she might not perhaps have been so ready to exhibit

[100-006]them. "No one can say that length of years has blanched them. I have

[100-007]no secrets from you about my age. One should not be grey before one

[100-008]has reached thirty."

[100-009]"I did not see a changed hair."

[100-010]"'Twas the fault of your eyes, then, for there are plenty of them.

[100-011]And what is it has made them grey?"

[100-012]"They say that hot rooms will do it."

[100-013]"Hot rooms! No, Conway, it does not come from heated atmosphere. It

[100-014]comes from a cold heart, a chilled heart, a frozen heart, a heart

[100-015]that is all ice." She was getting out of the cloud into the heat now,

[100-016]and he could only hope that Miss Van Siever would come soon. "The

[100-017]world is beginning with you, Conway, and yet you are as old as I am.

[100-018]It is ending with me, and yet I am as young as you are. But I do not

[100-019]know why I talk of all this. It is simply folly,--utter folly. I

[100-020]had not meant to speak of myself; but I did wish to say a few words

[100-021]to you of your own future. I suppose I may still speak to you as a

[100-022]friend?"

[100-023]"I hope you will always do that."

[100-024]"Nay,--I will make no such promise. That I will always have a

[100-025]friend's feeling for you, a friend's interest in your welfare, a

[100-026]friend's triumph in your success,--that I will promise. But friendly

[100-027]words, Conway, are sometimes misunderstood."

[100-028]"Never by me," said he.

[100-029]"No, not by you,--certainly not by you. I did not mean that. I did

[100-030]not expect that you should misinterpret them." Then she laughed

[100-031]hysterically,--a little low, gurgling, hysterical laugh; and after

[100-032]that she wiped her eyes, and then she smiled, and then she put her

[100-033]hand very gently upon his shoulder. "Thank God, Conway, we are quite

[100-034]safe there,--are we not?"

[100-035]He had made a blunder, and it was necessary that he should correct

[100-036]it. His watch was lying in the trough of his easel, and he looked at

[100-037]it and wondered why Miss Van Siever was not there. He had tripped,

[100-038]and he must make a little struggle and recover his step. "As I said

[100-039]before, it shall never be misunderstood by me. I have never been vain

[100-040]enough to suppose for a moment that there was any other feeling,--not

[100-041]for a moment. You women can be so careful, while we men are always

[100-042]off our guard! A man loves because he cannot help it; but a woman has

[100-043]been careful, and answers him--with friendship. Perhaps I am wrong to

[100-044]say that I never thought of winning anything more; but I never think

[100-045]of winning more now." Why the mischief didn't Miss Van Siever come!

[100-046]In another five minutes, despite himself, he would be on his knees,

[100-047]making a mock declaration, and she would be pouring forth the vial of

[100-048]her mock wrath, or giving him mock counsel as to the restraint of his

[100-049]passion. He had gone through it all before, and was tired of it; but

[100-050]for his life he did not know how to help himself.

[100-051]"Conway," said she, gravely, "how dare you address me in such

[100-052]language?"

[100-053]"Of course it is very wrong; I know that."

[100-054]"I'm not speaking of myself, now. I have learned to think so little

[100-055]of myself, as even to be indifferent to the feeling of the injury you

[100-056]are doing me. My life is a blank, and I almost think that nothing

[100-057]can hurt me further. I have not heart left enough to break; no, not

[100-058]enough to be broken. It is not of myself that I am thinking, when I

[100-059]ask you how you dare to address me in such language. Do you not know

[100-060]that it is an injury to another?"

[100-061]"To what other?" asked Conway Dalrymple, whose mind was becoming

[100-062]rather confused, and who was not quite sure whether the other one was

[100-063]Mr. Dobbs Broughton, or somebody else.

[100-064]"To that poor girl who is coming here now, who is devoted to you, and

[100-065]to whom, I do not doubt, you have uttered words which ought to have

[100-066]made it impossible for you to speak to me as you spoke not a moment

[100-067]since."

[100-068]Things were becoming very grave and difficult. They would have been

[100-069]very grave, indeed, had not some god saved him by sending Miss Van

[100-070]Siever to his rescue at this moment. He was beginning to think what

[100-071]he would say in answer to the accusation now made, when his eager ear

[100-072]caught the sound of her step upon the stairs; and before the pause

[100-073]in the conversation which the circumstances admitted had given place

[100-074]to the necessity for further speech, Miss Van Siever had knocked at

[100-075]the door and had entered the room. He was rejoiced, and I think that

[100-076]Mrs. Broughton did not regret the interference. It is always well

[100-077]that these little dangerous scenes should be brought to sudden ends.

[100-078]The last details of such romances, if drawn out to their natural

[100-079]conclusions, are apt to be uncomfortable, if not dull. She did not

[100-080]want him to go down on his knees, knowing that the getting up again

[100-081]is always awkward.

[100-082]"Clara, I began to think you were never coming," said Mrs. Broughton,

[100-083]with her sweetest smile.

[100-084]"I began to think so myself also," said Clara. "And I believe this

[100-085]must be the last sitting, or, at any rate, the last but one."

[100-086]"Is anything the matter at home?" said Mrs. Broughton, clasping her

[100-087]hands together.

[100-088]"Nothing very much; mamma asked me a question or two this morning,

[100-089]and I said I was coming here. Had she asked me why, I should have

[100-090]told her."

[100-091]"But what did she ask? What did she say?"

[100-092]"She does not always make herself very intelligible. She complains

[100-093]without telling you what she complains of. But she muttered something

[100-094]about artists which was not complimentary, and I suppose, therefore,

[100-095]that she has a suspicion. She stayed ever so late this morning,

[100-096]and we left the house together. She will ask some direct question

[100-097]to-night, or before long, and then there will be an end of it."

[100-098]"Let us make the best of our time then," said Dalrymple; and the

[100-099]sitting was arranged; Miss Van Siever went down on her knees with her

[100-100]hammer in her hand, and the work began. Mrs. Broughton had twisted a

[100-101]turban round Clara's head, as she always did on these occasions, and

[100-102]assisted to arrange the drapery. She used to tell herself as she did

[100-103]so, that she was like Isaac, piling the fagots for her own sacrifice.

[100-104]Only Isaac had piled them in ignorance, and she piled them conscious

[100-105]of the sacrificial flames. And Isaac had been saved; whereas it was

[100-106]impossible that the catching of any ram in any thicket could save

[100-107]her. But, nevertheless, she arranged the drapery with all her skill,

[100-108]piling the fagots ever so high for her own pyre. In the meantime

[100-109]Conway Dalrymple painted away, thinking more of his picture than he

[100-110]did of one woman or of the other.

[Illustration: Mrs. Dobbs Broughton piles her Fagots.]

[100-111]After a while, when Mrs. Broughton had piled the fagots as high as

[100-112]she could pile them, she got up from her seat and prepared to leave

[100-113]the room. Much of the piling consisted, of course, in her own absence

[100-114]during a portion of these sittings. "Conway," she said, as she went,

[100-115]"if this is to be the last sitting, or the last but one, you should

[100-116]make the most of it." Then she threw upon him a very peculiar glance

[100-117]over the head of the kneeling Jael, and withdrew. Jael, who in those

[100-118]moments would be thinking more of the fatigue of her position than

[100-119]of anything else, did not at all take home to herself the peculiar

[100-120]meaning of her friend's words. Conway Dalrymple understood them

[100-121]thoroughly, and thought that he might as well take the advice given

[100-122]to him. He had made up his mind to propose to Miss Van Siever, and

[100-123]why should he not do so now? He went on with his brush for a couple

[100-124]of minutes without saying a word, working as well as he could work,

[100-125]and then resolved that he would at once begin the other task. "Miss

[100-126]Van Siever," he said, "I'm afraid you are tired?"

[100-127]"Not more than usually tired. It is fatiguing to be slaying Sisera by

[100-128]the hour together. I do get to hate this block." The block was the

[100-129]dummy by which the form of Sisera was supposed to be typified.

[100-130]"Another sitting will about finish it," said he, "so that you need

[100-131]not positively distress yourself now. Will you rest yourself for a

[100-132]minute or two?" He had already perceived that the attitude in which

[100-133]Clara was posed before him was not one in which an offer of marriage

[100-134]could be received and replied to with advantage.

[100-135]"Thank you, I am not tired yet," said Clara, not changing the fixed

[100-136]glance of national wrath with which she regarded her wooden Sisera as

[100-137]she held her hammer on high.

[100-138]"But I am. There; we will rest for a moment." Dalrymple was aware

[100-139]that Mrs. Dobbs Broughton, though she was very assiduous in piling

[100-140]her fagots, never piled them for long together. If he did not make

[100-141]haste she would be back upon them before he could get his word

[100-142]spoken. When he put down his brush, and got up from his chair, and

[100-143]stretched out his arm as a man does when he ceases for a moment from

[100-144]his work, Clara of course got up also, and seated herself. She was

[100-145]used to her turban and her drapery, and therefore thought not of it

[100-146]at all; and he also was used to it, seeing her in it two or three

[100-147]times a week; but now that he intended to accomplish a special

[100-148]purpose, the turban and the drapery seemed to be in the way. "I do so

[100-149]hope you will like the picture," he said, as he was thinking of this.

[100-150]"I don't think I shall. But you will understand that it is natural

[100-151]that a girl should not like herself in such a portraiture as that."

[100-152]"I don't know why. I can understand that you specially should not

[100-153]like the picture; but I think that most women in London in your place

[100-154]would at any rate say that they did."

[100-155]"Are you angry with me?"

[100-156]"What; for telling the truth? No, indeed." He was standing opposite

[100-157]to his easel, looking at the canvas, shifting his head about so as to

[100-158]change the lights, and observing critically this blemish and that;

[100-159]and yet he was all the while thinking how he had best carry out his

[100-160]purpose. "It will have been a prosperous picture to me," he said at

[100-161]last, "if it leads to the success of which I am ambitious."

[100-162]"I am told that all you do is successful now,--merely because you do

[100-163]it. That is the worst of success."

[100-164]"What is the worst of success?"

[100-165]"That when won by merit it leads to further success, for the gaining

[100-166]of which no merit is necessary."

[100-167]"I hope it may be so in my case. If it is not I shall have a very

[100-168]poor chance. Clara, I think you must know that I am not talking about

[100-169]my pictures."

[100-170]"I thought you were."

[100-171]"Indeed I am not. As for success in my profession, far as I am from

[100-172]thinking I merit it, I feel tolerably certain that I shall obtain

[100-173]it."
