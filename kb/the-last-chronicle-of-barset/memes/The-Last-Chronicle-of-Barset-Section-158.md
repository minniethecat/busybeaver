[158-001]"I must know more of this, sir, before you leave the house," said

[158-002]Lady Demolines. He saw that between them both there might probably

[158-003]be a very bad quarter of an hour in store for him; but he swore to

[158-004]himself that no union of dragon and tigress should extract from him

[158-005]a word that could be taken as a promise of marriage.

[158-006]The old woman was now kneeling by the head of the sofa, and Johnny

[158-007]was standing close by her side. Suddenly Madalina opened her

[158-008]eyes,--opened them very wide and gazed around her. Then slowly she

[158-009]raised herself on the sofa, and turned her face first upon her mother

[158-010]and then upon Johnny. "You here, mamma!" she said.

[158-011]"Dearest one, I am near you. Be not afraid," said her ladyship.

[158-012]"Afraid! Why should I be afraid? John! My own John! Mamma, he is my

[158-013]own." And she put out her arms to him, as though calling to him to

[158-014]come to her. Things were now very bad with John Eames,--so bad that

[158-015]he would have given a considerable lump out of Lord De Guest's legacy

[158-016]to be able to escape at once into the street. The power of a woman,

[158-017]when she chooses to use it recklessly, is, for the moment, almost

[158-018]unbounded.

[158-019]"I hope you find yourself a little better," said John, struggling to

[158-020]speak, as though he were not utterly crushed by the occasion.

[158-021]Lady Demolines slowly raised herself from her knees, helping herself

[158-022]with her hands against the shoulder of the sofa,--for though still

[158-023]very clever, she was old and stiff,--and then offered both her hands

[158-024]to Johnny. Johnny cautiously took one of them, finding himself unable

[158-025]to decline them both. "My son!" she exclaimed; and before he knew

[158-026]where he was the old woman had succeeded in kissing his nose and his

[158-027]whiskers. "My son!" she said again.

[158-028]Now the time had come for facing the dragon and the tigress in their

[158-029]wrath. If they were to be faced at all, the time for facing them had

[158-030]certainly arrived. I fear that John's heart sank low in his bosom

[158-031]at that moment. "I don't quite understand," he said, almost in a

[158-032]whisper. Madalina put out one arm towards him, and the fingers

[158-033]trembled. Her lips were opened, and the white row of interior ivory

[158-034]might be seen plainly; but at the present conjuncture of affairs

[158-035]she spoke not a word. She spoke not a word; but her arm remained

[158-036]stretched out towards him, and her fingers did not cease to tremble.

[158-037]"You do not understand!" said Lady Demolines, drawing herself back,

[158-038]and looking, in her short open cloak, like a knight who has donned

[158-039]his cuirass, but has forgotten to put on his leg-gear. And she shook

[158-040]the bright ribbons of her cap, as a knight in his wrath shakes the

[158-041]crest of his helmet. "You do not understand, Mr. Eames! What is it,

[158-042]sir, that you do not understand?"

[158-043]"There is some misconception, I mean," said Johnny.

[158-044]"Mother!" said Madalina, turning her eyes from her recreant lover to

[158-045]her tender parent; trembling all over, but still keeping her hand

[158-046]extended. "Mother!"

[158-047]"My darling! But leave him to me, dearest. Compose yourself."

[158-048]"'Twas the word that he said--this moment; before he pressed me to

[158-049]his heart."

[158-050]"I thought you were fainting," said Johnny.

[158-051]"Sir!" And Lady Demolines, as she spoke, shook her crest, and glared

[158-052]at him, and almost flew at him in her armour.

[158-053]"It may be that nature has given way with me, and that I have been in

[158-054]a dream," said Madalina.

[158-055]"That which mine eyes saw was no dream," said Lady Demolines. "Mr.

[158-056]Eames, I have given to you the sweetest name that can fall from an

[158-057]old woman's lips. I have called you my son."

[158-058]"Yes, you did, I know. But, as I said before, there is some mistake.

[158-059]I know how proud I ought to be, and how happy, and all that kind of

[158-060]thing. But--" Then there came a screech from Madalina, which would

[158-061]have awakened the dead, had there been any dead in that house. The

[158-062]page and the cook, however, took no notice of it, whether they were

[158-063]awakened or not. And having screeched, Madalina stood erect upon the

[158-064]floor, and she also glared upon her recreant lover. The dragon and

[158-065]the tiger were there before him now, and he knew that it behoved him

[158-066]to look to himself. As he had a battle to fight, might it not be best

[158-067]to put a bold face upon it? "The truth is," said he, "that I don't

[158-068]understand this kind of thing at all."

[158-069]"Not understand it, sir?" said the dragon.

[158-070]"Leave him to me, mother," said the tigress, shaking her head again,

[158-071]but with a kind of shake differing from that which she had used

[158-072]before. "This is my business, and I'll have it out for myself. If he

[158-073]thinks I'm going to put up with his nonsense he's mistaken. I've been

[158-074]straightforward and above board with you, Mr. Eames, and I expect to

[158-075]be treated in the same way in return. Do you mean to tell my mother

[158-076]that you deny that we are engaged?"

[158-077]"Well; yes; I do. I'm very sorry, you know, if I seem to be

[158-078]uncivil--"

[158-079]"It's because I've no brother," said the tigress. "He thinks that

[158-080]I have no man near me to protect me. But he shall find that I can

[158-081]protect myself. John Eames, why are you treating me like this?"

[158-082]"I shall consult my cousin the serjeant to-morrow," said the dragon.

[158-083]"In the meantime he must remain in this house. I shall not allow the

[158-084]front door to be unlocked for him."

[158-085]This, I think, was the bitterest moment of all to Johnny. To be

[158-086]confined all night in Lady Demolines' drawing-room would, of itself,

[158-087]be an intolerable nuisance. And then the absurdity of the thing,

[158-088]and the story that would go abroad! And what should he say to the

[158-089]dragon's cousin the serjeant, if the serjeant should be brought upon

[158-090]the field before he was able to escape from it? He did not know what

[158-091]a serjeant might not do to him in such circumstances. There was one

[158-092]thing no serjeant should do, and no dragon! Between them all they

[158-093]should never force him to marry the tigress. At this moment Johnny

[158-094]heard a tramp along the pavement, and he rushed to the window. Before

[158-095]the dragon or even the tigress could arrest him, he had thrown up

[158-096]the sash, and had appealed in his difficulty to the guardian of

[158-097]the night. "I say, old fellow," said Johnny, "don't you stir from

[158-098]that till I tell you." The policeman turned his bull's-eye upon the

[158-099]window, and stood perfectly motionless. "Now, if you please, I'll say

[158-100]good-night," said Johnny. But, as he spoke, he still held the open

[158-101]window in his hand.

[158-102]"What means this violence in my house?" said the dragon.

[158-103]"Mamma, you had better let him go," said the tigress. "We shall know

[158-104]where to find him."

[158-105]"You will certainly be able to find me," said Johnny.

[158-106]"Go," said the dragon, shaking her crest,--shaking all her armour at

[158-107]him, "dastard, go!"

[158-108]"Policeman," shouted Johnny, while he still held the open window in

[158-109]his hand, "mind you don't stir till I come out." The bull's-eye was

[158-110]shifted a little, but the policeman spoke never a word.

[158-111]"I wish you good-night, Lady Demolines," said Johnny. "Good-night,

[158-112]Miss Demolines." Then he left the window and made a run for the door.

[158-113]But the dragon was there before him.

[158-114]"Let him go, mamma," said the tigress as she closed the window. "We

[158-115]shall only have a rumpus."

[158-116]"That will be all," said Johnny. "There isn't the slightest use in

[158-117]your trying to keep me here."

[158-118]"And are we never to see you again?" said the tigress, almost

[158-119]languishing again with one eye.

[158-120]"Well; no. What would be the use? No man likes to be shut in, you

[158-121]know."

[158-122]"Go then," said the tigress; "but if you think that this is to be the

[158-123]end of it, you'll find yourself wonderfully mistaken. You poor false,

[158-124]drivelling creature! Lily Dale won't touch you with a pair of tongs.

[158-125]It's no use your going to her."

[158-126]"Go away, sir, this moment, and don't contaminate my room an instant

[158-127]longer by your presence," said the dragon, who had observed through

[158-128]the window that the bull's-eye was still in full force before the

[158-129]house. Then John Eames withdrew, and descending into the hall made

[158-130]his way in the dark to the front door. For aught he knew there might

[158-131]still be treachery in regard to the lock; but his heart was comforted

[158-132]as he heard the footfall of the policeman on the door-step. With much

[158-133]fumbling he succeeded at last in turning the key and drawing the

[158-134]bolt, and then he found himself at liberty in the street. Before he

[158-135]even spoke a word to the policeman he went out into the road and

[158-136]looked up at the window. He could just see the figure of the dragon's

[158-137]helmet as she was closing the shutters. It was the last he ever saw

[158-138]of Lady Demolines or of her daughter.

[158-139]"What was it all about?" said the policeman.

[158-140]"I don't know that I can just tell you," said Johnny, searching in

[158-141]his pocket-book for half a sovereign which he tendered to the man.

[158-142]"There was a little difficulty, and I'm obliged to you for waiting."

[158-143]"There ain't nothing wrong?" said the man suspiciously, hesitating

[158-144]for a moment before he accepted the coin.

[158-145]"Nothing on earth. I'll wait with you, while you have the house

[158-146]opened and inquire, if you wish it. The truth is somebody inside

[158-147]refused to have the door opened, and I didn't want to stay there all

[158-148]night."

[158-149]"They're a rummy couple, if what I hear is true."

[158-150]"They are a rummy couple," said Johnny.

[158-151]"I suppose it's all right," said the policeman, taking the money. And

[158-152]then John walked off home by himself, turning in his mind all the

[158-153]circumstances of his connection with Miss Demolines. Taking his own

[158-154]conduct as a whole, he was rather proud of it; but he acknowledged to

[158-155]himself that it would be well that he should keep himself free from

[158-156]the society of Madalinas for the future.

[158-157]CHAPTER LXXXI.

[158-158]BARCHESTER CLOISTERS.

[158-159]On the morning of the Sunday after the dean's return Mr. Harding was

[158-160]lying in his bed, and Posy was sitting on the bed beside him. It was

[158-161]manifest to all now that he became feebler and feebler from day to

[158-162]day, and that he would never leave his bed again. Even the archdeacon

[158-163]had shaken his head, and had acknowledged to his wife that the last

[158-164]day for her father was near at hand. It would very soon be necessary

[158-165]that he should select another vicar for St. Ewolds.

[158-166]"Grandpa won't play cat's-cradle," said Posy, as Mrs. Arabin entered

[158-167]the room.

[158-168]"No, darling,--not this morning," said the old man. He himself knew

[158-169]well enough that he would never play cat's-cradle again. Even that

[158-170]was over for him now.

[158-171]"She teases you, papa," said Mrs. Arabin.

[158-172]"No, indeed," said he. "Posy never teases me;" and he slowly moved

[158-173]his withered hand down outside the bed, so as to hold the child by

[158-174]her frock. "Let her stay with me, my dear."

[158-175]"Dr. Filgrave is downstairs, papa. You will see him, if he comes up?"

[158-176]Now Dr. Filgrave was the leading physician of Barchester, and nobody

[158-177]of note in the city,--or for the matter of that in the eastern

[158-178]division of the county,--was allowed to start upon the last great

[158-179]journey without some assistance from him as the hour of going drew

[158-180]nigh. I do not know that he had much reputation for prolonging life,

[158-181]but he was supposed to add a grace to the hour of departure. Mr.

[158-182]Harding had expressed no wish to see the doctor,--had rather declared

[158-183]his conviction that Dr. Filgrave could be of no possible service to

[158-184]him. But he was not a man to persevere in his objection in opposition

[158-185]to the wishes of the friends around him; and as soon as the

[158-186]archdeacon had spoken a word on the subject he assented.
