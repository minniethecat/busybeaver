[019-001]"It's not for me to like or dislike. It's a great thing having your

[019-002]mother in the parish."

[019-003]"That's all very well; and of course she'll do as she likes. She may

[019-004]ask whom she pleases here, and I shan't interfere. It's the same as

[019-005]though it was her own house. But I shall take Lucy to Lufton." Now

[019-006]Lord Lufton had been building his house at Lufton for the last seven

[019-007]years, and it was not yet finished,--or nearly finished, if all that

[019-008]his wife and mother said was true. And if they could have their

[019-009]way, it never would be finished. And so, in order that Lord Lufton

[019-010]might not be actually driven away by the turmoils of ecclesiastical

[019-011]contest, the younger Lady Lufton would endeavour to moderate both the

[019-012]wrath and the zeal of the elder one, and would struggle against the

[019-013]coming clergymen. On this day, however, three sat at the board at

[019-014]Framley, and Lady Lufton, in her justification to her son, swore that

[019-015]the invitation had been given by her daughter-in-law. "You know, my

[019-016]dear," the dowager said to Lord Lufton, "something must be done for

[019-017]these poor Crawleys; and as the dean is away, Lucy wants to speak to

[019-018]the archdeacon about them."

[019-019]"And the archdeacon could not subscribe his ten-pound note without

[019-020]having Mr. Champion to back him?"

[019-021]"My dear Ludovic, you do put it in such a way."

[019-022]"Never mind, mother. I've no special dislike to Champion; only as you

[019-023]are not paid five thousand a year for your trouble, it is rather hard

[019-024]that you should have to do all the work of opposition bishop in the

[019-025]diocese."

[019-026]It was felt by them all,--including Lord Lufton himself, who became

[019-027]so interested in the matter as to forgive the black coats before the

[019-028]evening was over,--that this matter of Mr. Crawley's committal was

[019-029]very serious, and demanded the full energies of their party. It was

[019-030]known to them all that the feeling at the palace was inimical to Mr.

[019-031]Crawley. "That she-Beelzebub hates him for his poverty, and because

[019-032]Arabin brought him into the diocese," said the archdeacon, permitting

[019-033]himself to use very strong language in his allusion to the bishop's

[019-034]wife. It must be recorded on his behalf that he used the phrase in

[019-035]the presence only of the gentlemen of the party. I think he might

[019-036]have whispered the word into the ear of his confidential friend old

[019-037]Lady Lufton, and perhaps have given no offence; but he would not have

[019-038]ventured to use such words aloud in the presence of ladies.

[019-039]"You forget, archdeacon," said Dr. Thorne, laughing, "that the

[019-040]she-Beelzebub is my wife's particular friend."

[019-041]"Not a bit of it," said the archdeacon. "Your wife knows better than

[019-042]that. You tell her what I call her, and if she complains of the name,

[019-043]I'll unsay it." It may therefore be supposed that Dr. Thorne, and

[019-044]Mrs. Thorne, and the archdeacon, knew each other intimately, and

[019-045]understood each other's feelings on these matters.

[019-046]It was quite true that the palace party was inimical to Mr. Crawley.

[019-047]Mr. Crawley undoubtedly was poor, and had not been so submissive to

[019-048]episcopal authority as it behoves any clergyman to be whose loaves

[019-049]and fishes are scanty. He had raised his back more than once against

[019-050]orders emanating from the palace in a manner that had made the hairs

[019-051]on the head of the bishop's wife to stand almost on end, and had

[019-052]taken as much upon himself as though his living had been worth twelve

[019-053]hundred a year. Mrs. Proudie, almost as energetic in her language as

[019-054]the archdeacon, had called him a beggarly perpetual curate. "We must

[019-055]have perpetual curates, my dear," the bishop had said. "They should

[019-056]know their places then. But what can you expect of a creature from

[019-057]the deanery? All that ought to be altered. The dean should have no

[019-058]patronage in the diocese. No dean should have any patronage. It is

[019-059]an abuse from the beginning to the end. Dean Arabin, if he had any

[019-060]conscience, would be doing the duty at Hogglestock himself." How the

[019-061]bishop strove to teach his wife, with mildest words, what really

[019-062]ought to be a dean's duty, and how the wife rejoined by teaching her

[019-063]husband, not in the mildest words, what ought to be a bishop's duty,

[019-064]we will not further inquire here. The fact that such dialogues took

[019-065]place at the palace is recorded simply to show that the palatial

[019-066]feeling in Barchester ran counter to Mr. Crawley.

[019-067]And this was cause enough, if no other cause existed, for partiality

[019-068]to Mr. Crawley at Framley Court. But, as has been partly explained,

[019-069]there existed, if possible, even stronger ground than this for

[019-070]adherence to the Crawley cause. The younger Lady Lufton had known

[019-071]the Crawleys intimately, and the elder Lady Lufton had reckoned them

[019-072]among the neighbouring clerical families of her acquaintance. Both

[019-073]these ladies were therefore staunch in their defence of Mr. Crawley.

[019-074]The archdeacon himself had his own reasons,--reasons which for the

[019-075]present he kept altogether within his own bosom,--for wishing that

[019-076]Mr. Crawley had never entered the diocese. Whether the perpetual

[019-077]curate should or should not be declared to be a thief, it would be

[019-078]terrible to him to have to call the child of that perpetual curate

[019-079]his daughter-in-law. But not the less on this occasion was he true to

[019-080]his order, true to his side in the diocese, true to his hatred of the

[019-081]palace.

[019-082]"I don't believe it for a moment," he said, as he took his place on

[019-083]the rug before the fire in the drawing-room when the gentlemen came

[019-084]in from their wine. The ladies understood at once what it was that

[019-085]he couldn't believe. Mr. Crawley had for the moment so usurped the

[019-086]county that nobody thought of talking of anything else.

[019-087]"How is it, then," said Mrs. Thorne, "that Lord Lufton, and my

[019-088]husband, and the other wiseacres at Silverbridge, have committed him

[019-089]for trial?"

[019-090]"Because we were told to do so by the lawyer," said Dr. Thorne.

[019-091]"Ladies will never understand that magistrates must act in accordance

[019-092]with the law," said Lord Lufton.

[019-093]"But you all say he's not guilty," said Mrs. Robarts.

[019-094]"The fact is, that the magistrates cannot try the question," said

[019-095]the archdeacon; "they only hear the primary evidence. In this case

[019-096]I don't believe Crawley would ever have been committed if he had

[019-097]employed an attorney, instead of speaking for himself."

[019-098]"Why didn't somebody make him have an attorney?" said Lady Lufton.

[019-099]"I don't think any attorney in the world could have spoken for him

[019-100]better than he spoke for himself," said Dr. Thorne.

[019-101]"And yet you committed him," said his wife. "What can we do for him?

[019-102]Can't we pay the bail, and send him off to America?"

[019-103]"A jury will never find him guilty," said Lord Lufton.

[019-104]"And what is the truth of it?" asked the younger Lady Lufton.

[019-105]Then the whole matter was discussed again, and it was settled among

[019-106]them all that Mr. Crawley had undoubtedly appropriated the cheque

[019-107]through temporary obliquity of judgment,--obliquity of judgment and

[019-108]forgetfulness as to the source from whence the cheque had come to

[019-109]him. "He has picked it up about the house, and then has thought that

[019-110]it was his own," said Lord Lufton. Had they come to the conclusion

[019-111]that such an appropriation of money had been made by one of the

[019-112]clergy of the palace, by one of the Proudeian party, they would

[019-113]doubtless have been very loud and very bitter as to the iniquity

[019-114]of the offender. They would have said much as to the weakness of

[019-115]the bishop and the wickedness of the bishop's wife, and would have

[019-116]declared the appropriator to have been as very a thief as ever

[019-117]picked a pocket or opened a till;--but they were unanimous in their

[019-118]acquittal of Mr. Crawley. It had not been his intention, they said,

[019-119]to be a thief, and a man should be judged only by his intention. It

[019-120]must now be their object to induce a Barchester jury to look at the

[019-121]matter in the same light.

[019-122]"When they come to understand how the land lies," said the

[019-123]archdeacon, "they will be all right. There's not a tradesman in the

[019-124]city who does not hate that woman as though she were--"

[019-125]"Archdeacon," said his wife, cautioning him to repress his energy.

[019-126]"Their bills are all paid by this new chaplain they've got, and he is

[019-127]made to claim discount on every leg of mutton," said the archdeacon.

[019-128]Arguing from which fact,--or from which assertion, he came to the

[019-129]conclusion that no Barchester jury would find Mr. Crawley guilty.

[019-130]But it was agreed on all sides that it would not be well to trust to

[019-131]the unassisted friendship of the Barchester tradesmen. Mr. Crawley

[019-132]must be provided with legal assistance, and this must be furnished

[019-133]to him whether he should be willing or unwilling to receive it. That

[019-134]there would be a difficulty was acknowledged. Mr. Crawley was known

[019-135]to be a man not easy of persuasion, with a will of his own, with

[019-136]a great energy of obstinacy on points which he chose to take up

[019-137]as being of importance to his calling, or to his own professional

[019-138]status. He had pleaded his own cause before the magistrates, and it

[019-139]might be that he would insist on doing the same thing before the

[019-140]judge. At last Mr. Robarts, the clergyman of Framley, was deputed

[019-141]from the knot of Crawleian advocates assembled in Lady Lufton's

[019-142]drawing-room, to undertake the duty of seeing Mr. Crawley, and of

[019-143]explaining to him that his proper defence was regarded as a matter

[019-144]appertaining to the clergy and gentry generally of that part of the

[019-145]country, and that for the sake of the clergy and gentry the defence

[019-146]must of course be properly conducted. In such circumstances the

[019-147]expense of the defence would of course be borne by the clergy and

[019-148]gentry concerned. It was thought that Mr. Robarts could put the

[019-149]matter to Mr. Crawley with such a mixture of the strength of manly

[019-150]friendship and the softness of clerical persuasion, as to overcome

[019-151]the recognized difficulties of the task.

[019-152]CHAPTER XI.

[019-153]THE BISHOP SENDS HIS INHIBITION.

[019-154]Tidings of Mr. Crawley's fate reached the palace at Barchester on the

[019-155]afternoon of the day on which the magistrates had committed him. All

[019-156]such tidings travel very quickly, conveyed by imperceptible wires,

[019-157]and distributed by indefatigable message boys whom Rumour seems to

[019-158]supply for the purpose. Barchester is twenty miles from Silverbridge

[019-159]by road, and more than forty by railway. I doubt whether any one was

[019-160]commissioned to send the news along the actual telegraph, and yet

[019-161]Mrs. Proudie knew it before four o'clock. But she did not know it

[019-162]quite accurately. "Bishop," she said, standing at her husband's study

[019-163]door. "They have committed that man to gaol. There was no help for

[019-164]them unless they had forsworn themselves."

[019-165]"Not forsworn themselves, my dear," said the bishop, striving, as was

[019-166]usual with him, by some meek and ineffectual word to teach his wife

[019-167]that she was occasionally led by her energy into error. He never

[019-168]persisted in the lessons when he found, as was usual, that they were

[019-169]taken amiss.

[019-170]"I say forsworn themselves!" said Mrs. Proudie; "and now what do

[019-171]you mean to do? This is Thursday, and of course the man must not be

[019-172]allowed to desecrate the church of Hogglestock by performing the

[019-173]Sunday services."

[019-174]"If he has been committed, my dear, and is in prison,--"

[019-175]"I said nothing about prison, bishop."

[019-176]"Gaol, my dear."

[019-177]"I say they have committed him to gaol. So my informant tells me.

[019-178]But of course all the Plumstead and Framley set will move heaven and

[019-179]earth to get him out, so that he may be there as a disgrace to the

[019-180]diocese. I wonder how the dean will feel when he hears of it! I do,

[019-181]indeed. For the dean, though he is an idle, useless man, with no

[019-182]church principles, and no real piety, still he has a conscience. I

[019-183]think he has a conscience."
