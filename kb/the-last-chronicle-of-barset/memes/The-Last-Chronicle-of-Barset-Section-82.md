[082-001]"But you may believe me in the way of business," continued

[082-002]Musselboro, "when I tell you that it really does not suit us to renew

[082-003]the bill. We're pressed ourselves, and we must press others."

[082-004]"And who will do it for me?" said Crosbie, almost in despair.

[082-005]"There are Burton and Bangles there, the wine-merchants down in the

[082-006]yard; perhaps they may accommodate you. It's all in their line; but

[082-007]I'm told they charge uncommon dear."

[082-008]"I don't know Messrs. Burton and Bangles," said Crosbie.

[082-009]"That needn't stand in your way. You tell them where you come from,

[082-010]and they'll make inquiry. If they think it's about right, they'll

[082-011]give you the money; and if they don't, they won't."

[082-012]Mr. Crosbie then left the office without exchanging another word with

[082-013]Dobbs Broughton, and went down into Hook Court. As he descended the

[082-014]stairs he turned over in his mind the propriety of going to Messrs.

[082-015]Burton and Bangles with the view of relieving himself from his

[082-016]present difficulty. He knew that it was ruinous. Dealings even with

[082-017]such men as Dobbs Broughton and Musselboro, whom he presumed to be

[082-018]milder in their greed than Burton and Bangles, were, all of them,

[082-019]steps on the road to ruin. But what was he to do? If his bill were

[082-020]dishonoured, the fact would certainly become known at his office, and

[082-021]he might even ultimately be arrested. In the doorway at the bottom

[082-022]of the stairs he stood for some moments, looking over at Burton and

[082-023]Bangles', and he did not at all like the aspect of the establishment.

[082-024]Inside the office he could see a man standing with a cigar in his

[082-025]mouth, very resplendent with a new hat,--with a hat remarkable

[082-026]for the bold upward curve of its rim, and this man was copiously

[082-027]decorated with a chain and seals hanging about widely over his

[082-028]waistcoat. He was leaning with his back against the counter, and was

[082-029]talking to some one on the other side of it. There was something in

[082-030]the man's look and manner that was utterly repulsive to Crosbie. He

[082-031]was more vulgar to the eye even than Musselboro, and his voice, which

[082-032]Crosbie could hear as he stood in the other doorway, was almost as

[082-033]detestable as that of Dobbs Broughton in his drunkenness. Crosbie

[082-034]did not doubt that this was either Burton or Bangles, and that the

[082-035]man standing inside was either Bangles or Burton. He could not

[082-036]bring himself to accost these men and tell them of his necessities,

[082-037]and propose to them that they should relieve him. In spite of what

[082-038]Musselboro had just said to him, he could not believe it possible

[082-039]that he should succeed, were he to do so without some introduction.

[082-040]So he left Hook Court and went out into the lane, hearing as he went

[082-041]the loud voice of the man with the turned-up hat and the chain.

[082-042]But what was he to do? At the outset of his pecuniary troubles, when

[082-043]he first found it necessary to litigate some question with the De

[082-044]Courcy people, and withstand the web which Mortimer Gazebee wove so

[082-045]assiduously, his own attorney had introduced him to Dobbs Broughton,

[082-046]and the assistance which he had needed had come to him, at any rate,

[082-047]without trouble. He did not especially like Mr. Broughton; and when

[082-048]Mr. Broughton first invited him to come and eat a little bit of

[082-049]dinner, he had told himself with painful remorse that in his early

[082-050]days he had been accustomed to eat his little bits of dinner with

[082-051]people of a different kind. But there had been nothing really painful

[082-052]in this. Since his marriage with a daughter of the De Courcys,--by

[082-053]which marriage he had intended to climb to the highest pinnacle of

[082-054]social eating and drinking,--he had gradually found himself to be

[082-055]falling in the scale of such matters, and could bring himself to

[082-056]dine with a Dobbs Broughton without any violent pain. But now he had

[082-057]fallen so low that Dobbs Broughton had insulted him, and he was in

[082-058]such distress that he did not know where to turn for ten pounds. Mr.

[082-059]Gazebee had beaten him at litigation, and his own lawyer had advised

[082-060]him that it would be foolish to try the matter further. In his

[082-061]marriage with the noble daughter of the De Courcys he had allowed the

[082-062]framers of the De Courcy settlement to tie him up in such a way that

[082-063]now, even when chance had done so much for him in freeing him from

[082-064]his wife, he was still bound to the De Courcy faction. Money had been

[082-065]paid away,--on his behalf, as alleged by Mr. Gazebee,--like running

[082-066]water; money for furniture, money for the lease of a house, money

[082-067]when he had been separated from his wife, money while she was living

[082-068]abroad. It had seemed to him that he had been made to pay for the

[082-069]entire support of the female moiety of the De Courcy family which had

[082-070]settled itself at Baden-Baden, from the day, and in some respects

[082-071]from before the day, on which his wife had joined that moiety. He

[082-072]had done all in his power to struggle against these payments, but

[082-073]every such struggle had only cost him more money. Mr. Gazebee had

[082-074]written to him the civilest notes; but every note seemed to cost him

[082-075]money,--every word of each note seemed to find its way into some

[082-076]bill. His wife had died and her body had been brought back, with all

[082-077]the pomp befitting the body of an earl's daughter, that it might be

[082-078]laid with the old De Courcy dust,--at his expense. The embalming of

[082-079]her dear remains had cost a wondrous sum, and was a terrible blow

[082-080]upon him. All these items were showered upon him by Mr. Gazebee

[082-081]with the most courteously worded demands for settlement as soon as

[082-082]convenient. And then, when he applied that Lady Alexandrina's small

[082-083]fortune should be made over to him,--according to a certain agreement

[082-084]under which he had made over all his possessions to his wife, should

[082-085]she have survived him,--Mr. Gazebee expressed a mild opinion that he

[082-086]was wrong in his law, and blandly recommended an amicable lawsuit.

[082-087]The amicable lawsuit was carried on. His own lawyer seemed to

[082-088]throw him over. Mr. Gazebee was successful in everything. No money

[082-089]came to him. Money was demanded from him on old scores and on new

[082-090]scores,--and all that he received to console him for what he had lost

[082-091]was a mourning ring with his wife's hair,--for which, with sundry

[082-092]other mourning rings, he had to pay,--and an introduction to Mr.

[082-093]Dobbs Broughton. To Mr. Dobbs Broughton he owed five hundred pounds;

[082-094]and as regarded a bill for the one-half of that sum which was due

[082-095]to-morrow, Mr. Dobbs Broughton had refused to grant him renewal for a

[082-096]single month!

[082-097]I know no more uncomfortable walking than that which falls to the lot

[082-098]of men who go into the City to look for money, and who find none. Of

[082-099]all the lost steps trodden by men, surely the steps lost after that

[082-100]fashion are the most melancholy. It is not only that they are so

[082-101]vain, but that they are accompanied by so killing a sense of shame!

[082-102]To wait about in dingy rooms, which look on to bare walls, and are

[082-103]approached through some Hook Court; or to keep appointments at a low

[082-104]coffee-house, to which trystings the money-lender will not trouble

[082-105]himself to come unless it pleases him; to be civil, almost suppliant,

[082-106]to a cunning knave whom the borrower loathes; to be refused thrice,

[082-107]and then cheated with his eyes open on the fourth attempt; to submit

[082-108]himself to vulgarity of the foulest kind, and to have to seem to like

[082-109]it; to be badgered, reviled, and at last accused of want of honesty

[082-110]by the most fraudulent of mankind; and at the same time to be clearly

[082-111]conscious of the ruin that is coming,--this is the fate of him who

[082-112]goes into the City to find money, not knowing where it is to be

[082-113]found!

[082-114]Crosbie went along the lane into Lombard Street, and then he stood

[082-115]still for a moment to think. Though he knew a good deal of affairs in

[082-116]general, he did not quite know what would happen to him if his bill

[082-117]should be dishonoured. That somebody would bring it to him noted,

[082-118]and require him instantly to put his hand into his pocket and bring

[082-119]out the amount of the bill, plus the amount of certain expenses, he

[082-120]thought that he did know. And he knew that were he in trade he would

[082-121]become a bankrupt; and he was well aware that such an occurrence

[082-122]would prove him to be insolvent. But he did not know what his

[082-123]creditors would immediately have the power of doing. That the fact of

[082-124]the bill having been dishonoured would reach the Board under which

[082-125]he served,--and, therefore, also the fact that he had had recourse

[082-126]to such bill transactions,--this alone was enough to fill him with

[082-127]dismay. In early life he had carried his head so high, he had been so

[082-128]much more than a mere Government clerk, that the idea of the coming

[082-129]disgrace almost killed him. Would it not be well that he should put

[082-130]an end to himself, and thus escape? What was there in the world now

[082-131]for which it was worth his while to live? Lily, whom he had once

[082-132]gained, and by that gain had placed himself high in all hopes of

[082-133]happiness and riches,--whom he had then thrown away from him, and who

[082-134]had again seemed to be almost within his reach,--Lily had so refused

[082-135]him that he knew not how to approach her with a further prayer. And,

[082-136]had she not refused him, how could he have told her of his load of

[082-137]debt? As he stood at the corner where the lane runs into Lombard

[082-138]Street, he came for a while to think almost more of Lily than of his

[082-139]rejected bill. Then, as he thought of both his misfortunes together,

[082-140]he asked himself whether a pistol would not conveniently put an end

[082-141]to them together.

[082-142]At that moment a loud, harsh voice greeted his ear. "Hallo, Crosbie,

[082-143]what brings you so far east? One does not often see you in the City."

[082-144]It was the voice of Sir Raffle Buffle, which in former days had been

[082-145]very odious to Crosbie's ears;--for Sir Raffle Buffle had once been

[082-146]the presiding genius of the office to which Crosbie still belonged.

[082-147]"No, indeed, not very often," said Crosbie, smiling. Who can tell,

[082-148]who has not felt it, the pain that goes to the forcing of such

[082-149]smiles? But Sir Raffle was not an acutely observant person, and did

[082-150]not see that anything was wrong.

[082-151]"I suppose you're doing a little business?" said Sir Raffle. "If a

[082-152]man has kept a trifle of money by him, this certainly is the time for

[082-153]turning it. You have always been wide awake about such things."

[082-154]"No, indeed," said Crosbie. If he could only make up his mind that he

[082-155]would shoot himself, would it not be a pleasant thing to inflict some

[082-156]condign punishment on this odious man before he left the world? But

[082-157]Crosbie knew that he was not going to shoot himself, and he knew also

[082-158]that he had no power of inflicting condign punishment on Sir Raffle

[082-159]Buffle. He could only hate the man, and curse him inwardly.

[082-160]"Ah, ha!" said Sir Raffle. "You wouldn't be here unless you knew

[082-161]where a good thing is to be picked up. But I must be off. I'm on the

[082-162]Rocky Mountain Canal Company Directory. I'm not above taking my two

[082-163]guineas a day. Good-by, my boy. Remember me to old Optimist." And so

[082-164]Sir Raffle passed on, leaving Crosbie still standing at the corner of

[082-165]the lane.
