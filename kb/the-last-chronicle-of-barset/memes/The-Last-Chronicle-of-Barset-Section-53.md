[053-001]Major Grantly went back to the inn and saw his horse fed, and smoked

[053-002]a cigar, and then, finding that it was still only just one o'clock,

[053-003]he started for a walk. He was careful not to go out of Allington by

[053-004]the road he had entered it, as he had no wish to encounter Grace and

[053-005]her friend on their return into the village; so he crossed a little

[053-006]brook which runs at the bottom of the hill on which the chief street

[053-007]of Allington is built, and turned into a field-path to the left as

[053-008]soon as he had got beyond the houses. Not knowing the geography of

[053-009]the place he did not understand that by taking that path he was

[053-010]making his way back to the squire's house; but it was so; and after

[053-011]sauntering on for about a mile and crossing back again over the

[053-012]stream, of which he took no notice, he found himself leaning across a

[053-013]gate, and looking into a paddock on the other side of which was the

[053-014]high wall of a gentleman's garden. To avoid this he went on a little

[053-015]further and found himself on a farm road, and before he could retrace

[053-016]his steps so as not to be seen, he met a gentleman whom he presumed

[053-017]to be the owner of the house. It was the squire surveying his home

[053-018]farm, as was his daily custom; but Major Grantly had not perceived

[053-019]that the house must of necessity be Allington House, having been

[053-020]aware that he had passed the entrance to the place, as he entered

[053-021]the village on the other side. "I'm afraid I'm intruding," he said,

[053-022]lifting his hat. "I came up the path yonder, not knowing that it

[053-023]would lead me so close to a gentleman's house."

[053-024]"There is a right of way through the fields on to the Guestwick

[053-025]road," said the squire, "and therefore you are not trespassing in any

[053-026]sense; but we are not particular about such things down here, and you

[053-027]would be very welcome if there were no right of way. If you are a

[053-028]stranger, perhaps you would like to see the outside of the old house.

[053-029]People think it picturesque."

[053-030]Then Major Grantly became aware that this must be the squire, and

[053-031]he was annoyed with himself for his own awkwardness in having thus

[053-032]come upon the house. He would have wished to keep himself altogether

[053-033]unseen if it had been possible,--and especially unseen by this old

[053-034]gentleman, to whom, now that he had met him, he was almost bound to

[053-035]introduce himself. But he was not absolutely bound to do so, and he

[053-036]determined that he would still keep his peace. Even if the squire

[053-037]should afterwards hear of his having been there, what would it

[053-038]matter? But to proclaim himself at the present moment would be

[053-039]disagreeable to him. He permitted the squire, however, to lead him

[053-040]to the front of the house, and in a few moments was standing on the

[053-041]terrace hearing an account of the architecture of the mansion.

[Illustration: Squire Dale and Major Grantly.]

[053-042]"You can see the date still in the brickwork of one of the

[053-043]chimneys,--that is, if your eyes are very good you can see it,--1617.

[053-044]It was completed in that year, and very little has been done to it

[053-045]since. We think the chimneys are pretty."

[053-046]"They are very pretty," said the major. "Indeed, the house altogether

[053-047]is as graceful as it can be."

[053-048]"Those trees are old, too," said the squire, pointing to two cedars

[053-049]which stood at the side of the house. "They say they are older than

[053-050]the house, but I don't feel sure of it. There was a mansion here

[053-051]before, very nearly, though not quite, on the same spot."

[053-052]"Your own ancestors were living here before that, I suppose?" said

[053-053]Grantly, meaning to be civil.

[053-054]"Well, yes; two or three hundred years before it, I suppose. If you

[053-055]don't mind coming down to the churchyard, you'll get an excellent

[053-056]view of the house;--by far the best that there is. By-the-by, would

[053-057]you like to step in and take a glass of wine?"

[053-058]"I'm very much obliged," said the major, "but indeed I'd rather not."

[053-059]Then he followed the squire down to the churchyard, and was shown the

[053-060]church as well as the view of the house, and the vicarage, and a view

[053-061]over to Allington woods from the vicarage gate, of which the squire

[053-062]was very fond, and in this way he was taken back on to the Guestwick

[053-063]side of the village, and even down on to the road by which he had

[053-064]entered it, without in the least knowing where he was. He looked at

[053-065]his watch and saw that it was past two. "I'm very much obliged to

[053-066]you, sir," he said, again taking off his hat to the squire, "and if

[053-067]I shall not be intruding I'll make my way back to the village."

[053-068]"What village?" said the squire.

[053-069]"To Allington," said Grantly.

[053-070]"This is Allington," said the squire; and as he spoke, Lily Dale and

[053-071]Grace Crawley turned a corner from the Guestwick road and came close

[053-072]upon them. "Well, girls, I did not expect to see you," said the

[053-073]squire; "your mamma told me you wouldn't be back till it was nearly

[053-074]dark, Lily."

[053-075]"We have come back earlier than we intended," said Lily. She of

[053-076]course had seen the stranger with her uncle, and knowing the ways of

[053-077]the squire in such matters had expected to be introduced to him. But

[053-078]the reader will be aware that no introduction was possible. It never

[053-079]occurred to Lily that this man could be the Major Grantly of whom she

[053-080]and Grace had been talking during the whole length of the walk home.

[053-081]But Grace and her lover had of course known each other at once, and

[053-082]Grantly, though he was abashed and almost dismayed by the meeting, of

[053-083]course came forward and gave his hand to his friend. Grace in taking

[053-084]it did not utter a word.

[053-085]"Perhaps I ought to have introduced myself to you as Major Grantly?"

[053-086]said he, turning to the squire.

[053-087]"Major Grantly! Dear me! I had no idea that you were expected in

[053-088]these parts."

[053-089]"I have come without being expected."

[053-090]"You are very welcome, I'm sure. I hope your father is well? I used

[053-091]to know him some years ago, and I daresay he has not forgotten me."

[053-092]Then, while the girls stood by in silence, and while Grantly was

[053-093]endeavouring to escape, the squire invited him very warmly to send

[053-094]his portmanteau up to the house. "We'll have the ladies up from the

[053-095]house below, and make it as little dull for you as possible." But

[053-096]this would not have suited Grantly,--at any rate would not suit him

[053-097]till he should know what answer he was to have. He excused himself

[053-098]therefore, pleading a positive necessity to be at Guestwick that

[053-099]evening, and then, explaining that he had already seen Mrs. Dale, he

[053-100]expressed his intention of going back to the Small House in company

[053-101]with the ladies, if they would allow him. The squire, who did not as

[053-102]yet quite understand it all, bade him a formal adieu, and Lily led

[053-103]the way home down behind the churchyard wall and through the bottom

[053-104]of the gardens belonging to the Great House. She of course knew now

[053-105]who the stranger was, and did all in her power to relieve Grace of

[053-106]her embarrassment. Grace had hitherto not spoken a single word since

[053-107]she had seen her lover, nor did she say a word to him in their walk

[053-108]to the house. And, in truth, he was not much more communicative than

[053-109]Grace. Lily did all the talking, and with wonderful female skill

[053-110]contrived to have some words ready for use till they all found

[053-111]themselves together in Mrs. Dale's drawing-room. "I have caught a

[053-112]major, mamma, and landed him," said Lily laughing, "but I'm afraid,

[053-113]from what I hear, that you had caught him first."

[053-114]CHAPTER XXIX.

[053-115]MISS LILY DALE'S LOGIC.

[053-116]Lady Julia De Guest always lunched at one exactly, and it was not

[053-117]much past twelve when John Eames made his appearance at the cottage.

[053-118]He was of course told to stay, and of course said that he would stay.

[053-119]It had been his purpose to lunch with Lady Julia; but then he had

[053-120]not expected to find Lily Dale at the cottage. Lily herself would

[053-121]have been quite at her ease, protected by Lady Julia, and somewhat

[053-122]protected also by her own powers of fence, had it not been that Grace

[053-123]was there also. But Grace Crawley, from the moment that she had heard

[053-124]the description of the gentleman who looked out of the window with

[053-125]his glass in his eye, had by no means been at her ease. Lily saw at

[053-126]once that she could not be brought to join in any conversation, and

[053-127]both John and Lady Julia, in their ignorance of the matter in hand,

[053-128]made matters worse.

[053-129]"So that was Major Grantly?" said John. "I have heard of him before,

[053-130]I think. He is a son of the old archdeacon, is he not?"

[053-131]"I don't know about old archdeacon," said Lady Julia. "The archdeacon

[053-132]is the son of the old bishop, whom I remember very well. And it is

[053-133]not so very long since the bishop died, either."

[053-134]"I wonder what he's doing at Allington?" said Johnny.

[053-135]"I think he knows my uncle," said Lily.

[053-136]"But he's going to call on your mother," he said. Then Johnny

[053-137]remembered that the major had said something as to knowing Miss

[053-138]Crawley, and for the moment he was silent.

[053-139]"I remember when they talked of making the son a bishop also," said

[053-140]Lady Julia.

[053-141]"What;--this same man who is now a major?" said Johnny.

[053-142]"No, you goose. He is not the son; he is the grandson. They were

[053-143]going to make the archdeacon a bishop, and I remember hearing that

[053-144]he was terribly disappointed. He is getting to be an old man now,

[053-145]I suppose; and yet, dear me, how well I remember his father."

[053-146]"He didn't look like a bishop's son," said Johnny.

[053-147]"How does a bishop's son look?" Lily asked.

[053-148]"I suppose he ought to have some sort of clerical tinge about him;

[053-149]but this fellow had nothing of that kind."

[053-150]"But then this fellow, as you call him," said Lily, "is only the son

[053-151]of an archdeacon."

[053-152]"That accounts for it, I suppose," said Johnny.

[053-153]But during all this time Grace did not say a word, and Lily perceived

[053-154]it. Then she bethought herself as to what she had better do. Grace,

[053-155]she knew, could not be comfortable where she was. Nor, indeed, was

[053-156]it probable that Grace would be very comfortable in returning home.

[053-157]There could not be much ease for Grace till the coming meeting

[053-158]between her and Major Grantly should be over. But it would be better

[053-159]that Grace should go back to Allington at once; and better also,

[053-160]perhaps, for Major Grantly that it should be so. "Lady Julia," she

[053-161]said, "I don't think we'll mind stopping for lunch to-day."

[053-162]"Nonsense, my dear; you promised."

[053-163]"I think we must break our promise; I do indeed. You mustn't be

[053-164]angry with us." And Lily looked at Lady Julia, as though there were

[053-165]something which Lady Julia ought to understand, which she, Lily,

[053-166]could not quite explain. I fear that Lily was false, and intended her

[053-167]old friend to believe that she was running away because John Eames

[053-168]had come there.

[053-169]"But you will be famished," said Lady Julia.

[053-170]"We shall live through it," said Lily.

[053-171]"It is out of the question that I should let you walk all the way

[053-172]here from Allington and all the way back without taking something."

[053-173]"We shall just be home in time for lunch if we go now," said Lily.

[053-174]"Will not that be best, Grace?"
