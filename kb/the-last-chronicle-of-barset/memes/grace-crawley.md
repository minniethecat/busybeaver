## Character

Grace Crawley is the eldest daughter of the Reverend Josiah Crawley, and Trollope announces early that "we shall hear much in the course of this story of Grace Crawley" (002-087). She is a young woman of quiet dignity and moral courage, caught between the disgrace of her father's cheque-forgery accusation and the steadfast love of [[major-grantly|Major Henry Grantly]]. Her situation — impoverished, socially compromised, yet beloved by a gentleman of means — drives the novel's central romantic and ethical conflicts. Her character is tested by the cruelty of village gossip: some declared that "Grace Crawley was little better than a nobody" (003-148), while others, like Major Grantly, saw in her a worth that transcended her family's scandal.

## Biography

Grace Crawley grows up in the impoverished parish of Hogglestock, where her father's brilliant scholarship has done nothing to relieve the family's poverty. Her mother, Mary Crawley, is a devoted wife who has endured much. Grace's fortune, such as it is, "was made in the teeth of poverty" (002-098). She becomes a pupil-teacher under Miss Prettyman, a position that provides her with some independence and brings her into contact with a wider social world. It is through this connection that she meets Major Grantly, and their attachment becomes the subject of intense speculation in Barsetshire society. When Major Grantly's father, the Archdeacon, learns of the attachment, his reaction is swift and damning: Grace Crawley, "who had no money, and no position" (005-034), is deemed an unacceptable match. The chapter "Grace Crawley at Home" (001-008) and her introduction to Squire Dale (001-059) mark key moments in her social navigation of the scandal.

## Significance

Grace Crawley is the moral centre of *The Last Chronicle of Barset*. Unlike the novel's more powerful figures — the Archdeacon, the Bishop, Lady Lufton — she possesses no social authority, no wealth, and no institutional protection. Her only resources are her integrity and the loyalty of those who believe in her. Through Grace, Trollope explores the vulnerability of women to social judgment and the courage required to maintain self-respect when one's family is publicly disgraced. Her eventual vindication — through marriage to Major Grantly and the clearing of her father's name — represents Trollope's faith that personal worth can overcome social prejudice, though not without cost.
