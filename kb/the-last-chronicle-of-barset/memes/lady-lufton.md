## Character

Lady Lufton is the dowager of Framley Court and one of the most powerful social forces in Barsetshire. As Trollope writes, "There were two Lady Luftons,—mother-in-law and daughter-in-law" (008-058) — and it is the elder, the dowager, who commands the county's deference. She is a woman of strong opinions, fierce loyalties, and an unshakeable belief in her right to direct the lives of those within her orbit. Her influence extends from the drawing-room to the parish church: "Dear old Lady Lufton had done so, and had been greatly praised for doing so" (005-059).

## Biography

Lady Lufton's life has been spent at Framley Court, the estate that anchors the social world of eastern Barsetshire. Widowed, she has managed the Framley estate and its dependencies with a firm hand, exercising a maternal authority over tenants, clergy, and gentry alike. When the young Lady Lufton — Lucy Robarts, sister of the Framley vicar — marries her son the present Lord Lufton, the dowager finds herself navigating the delicate transition from sole authority to dowager eminence. She is "equally sure" of her own judgment in all matters (008-077), a conviction that brings her into conflict with the [[mrs-proudie|Bishop's wife]] and at times with her own family.

## Social Influence

Lady Lufton's power is wielded through a network of personal connections rather than formal office. In the ecclesiastical politics of Barsetshire, her influence over clerical appointments and parish affairs rivals that of the bishop himself. Her proposition to Major Grantly — the subject of an entire chapter, "Lady Lufton's Proposition" (001-017) — exemplifies her method: personal intervention in the lives of those she considers within her sphere of responsibility. Unlike [[mrs-proudie|Mrs. Proudie]], whose power is exercised through her husband's office, Lady Lufton's authority is indigenous to the county, rooted in land and lineage rather than ecclesiastical hierarchy.

## Significance

Lady Lufton embodies the Victorian ideal of the great lady — a figure whose social authority is at once patriarchal and maternal. Her significance in *The Last Chronicle of Barset* lies in her role as mediator between the gentry establishment and the personal dramas that threaten to destabilise it. By lending her support to Major Grantly's pursuit of Grace Crawley despite the scandal surrounding the Crawley family, she demonstrates that the gentry's code of honour can accommodate charity and flexibility. She represents the best of the old order: conservative in instinct but capable of generosity when personal relationships demand it.
