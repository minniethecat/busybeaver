[121-001]"I do not expect to be paid for it at all," said Dalrymple.

[121-002]"And who is it to belong to?"

[121-003]"It belongs to me at present."

[121-004]"Then, sir, it mustn't belong to you any longer. It won't do for you

[121-005]to have a picture of my girl to hang up in your painting-room for all

[121-006]your friends to come and make their jokes about, nor yet to make a

[121-007]show of it in any of your exhibitions. My daughter has been a fool,

[121-008]and I can't help it. If you'll tell me what's the cost, I'll pay you;

[121-009]then I'll have the picture home, and I'll treat it as it deserves."

[121-010]Dalrymple thought for a moment about his picture and about Mrs. Van

[121-011]Siever. What had he better do? He wanted to behave well, and he felt

[121-012]that the old woman had something of justice on her side. "Madam," he

[121-013]said, "I will not sell this picture; but it shall be destroyed, if

[121-014]you wish it."

[121-015]"I certainly do wish it, but I won't trust to you. If it's not sent

[121-016]to my house at once you'll hear from me through my lawyers."

[121-017]Then Dalrymple deliberately opened his penknife and slit the canvas

[121-018]across, through the middle of the picture each way. Clara, as she

[121-019]saw him do it, felt that in truth she loved him. "There, Mrs. Van

[121-020]Siever," he said; "now you can take the bits home with you in your

[121-021]basket if you wish it." At this moment, as the rent canvas fell and

[121-022]fluttered upon the stretcher, there came a loud voice of lamentation

[121-023]from the sofa, a groan of despair and a shriek of wrath. "Very fine

[121-024]indeed," said Mrs. Van Siever. "When ladies faint they always ought

[121-025]to have their eyes about them. I see that Mrs. Broughton understands

[121-026]that."

[121-027]"Take her away, Conway--for God's sake take her away," said Mrs.

[121-028]Broughton.

[121-029]"I shall take myself away very shortly," said Mrs. Van Siever, "so

[121-030]you needn't trouble Mr. Conway about that. Not but what I thought the

[121-031]gentleman's name was Mr. something else."

[121-032]"My name is Conway Dalrymple," said the artist.

[121-033]"Then I suppose you must be her brother, or her cousin, or something

[121-034]of that sort?" said Mrs. Van Siever.

[121-035]"Take her away," screamed Mrs. Dobbs Broughton.

[121-036]"Wait a moment, madam. As you've chopped up your handiwork there, Mr.

[121-037]Conway Dalrymple, and as I suppose my daughter has been more to blame

[121-038]than anybody else--"

[121-039]"She has not been to blame at all," said Dalrymple.

[121-040]"That's my affair, and not yours," said Mrs. Van Siever, very

[121-041]sharply. "But as you've been at all this trouble, and have now

[121-042]chopped it up, I don't mind paying you for your time and paints; only

[121-043]I shall be glad to know how much it will come to?"

[121-044]"There will be nothing to pay, Mrs. Van Siever."

[121-045]"How long has he been at it, Clara?"

[121-046]"Mamma, indeed you had better not say anything about paying him."

[121-047]"I shall say whatever I please, miss. Will ten pounds do it, sir?"

[121-048]"If you choose to buy the picture, the price will be seven hundred

[121-049]and fifty," said Dalrymple, with a smile, pointing to the fragments.

[121-050]"Seven hundred and fifty pounds?" said the old woman.

[121-051]"But I strongly advise you not to make the purchase," said Dalrymple.

[121-052]"Seven hundred and fifty pounds! I certainly shall not give you seven

[121-053]hundred and fifty pounds, sir."

[121-054]"I certainly think you could invest your money better, Mrs. Van

[121-055]Siever. But if the thing is to be sold at all, that is my price. I've

[121-056]thought that there was some justice in your demand that it should be

[121-057]destroyed,--and therefore I have destroyed it."

[121-058]Mrs. Van Siever had been standing on the same spot ever since she had

[121-059]entered the room, and now she turned round to leave the room.

[121-060]"If you have any demand to make, I beg that you will send in your

[121-061]account for work done to Mr. Musselboro. He is my man of business.

[121-062]Clara, are you ready to come home? The cab is waiting at the

[121-063]door,--at sixpence the quarter of an hour, if you will be pleased to

[121-064]remember."

[121-065]"Mrs. Broughton," said Clara, thoughtful of her raiment, and

[121-066]remembering that it might not be well that she should return home,

[121-067]even in a cab, dressed as Jael; "if you will allow me, I will go into

[121-068]your room for a minute or two."

[121-069]"Certainly, Clara," said Mrs. Broughton, preparing to accompany her.

[121-070]"But before you go, Mrs. Broughton," said Mrs. Van Siever, "it may be

[121-071]as well that I should tell you that my daughter is going to become

[121-072]the wife of Mr. Musselboro. It may simplify matters that you should

[121-073]know this." And Mrs. Van Siever, as she spoke, looked hard at Conway

[121-074]Dalrymple.

[121-075]"Mamma!" exclaimed Clara.

[121-076]"My dear," said Mrs. Van Siever, "you had better change your dress

[121-077]and come away with me."

[121-078]"Not till I have protested against what you have said, mamma."

[121-079]"You had better leave your protesting alone, I can tell you."

[121-080]"Mrs. Broughton," continued Clara, "I must beg you to understand that

[121-081]mamma has not the slightest right in the world to tell you what she

[121-082]just now said about me. Nothing on earth would induce me to become

[121-083]the wife of Mr. Broughton's partner."

[121-084]There was something which made Clara unwilling even to name the man

[121-085]whom her mother had publicly proposed as her future husband.

[121-086]"He isn't Mr. Broughton's partner," said Mrs. Van Siever. "Mr.

[121-087]Broughton has not got a partner. Mr. Musselboro is the head of the

[121-088]firm. And as to your marrying him, of course, I can't make you."

[121-089]"No, mamma; you cannot."

[121-090]"Mrs. Broughton understands that, no doubt;--and so, probably, does

[121-091]Mr. Dalrymple. I only tell them what are my ideas. If you choose to

[121-092]marry the sweep at the crossing, I can't help it. Only I don't see

[121-093]what good you would do the sweep, when he would have to sweep for

[121-094]himself and you too. At any rate, I suppose you mean to go home with

[121-095]me now?" Then Mrs. Broughton and Clara left the room, and Mrs. Van

[121-096]Siever was left with Conway Dalrymple. "Mr. Dalrymple," said Mrs.

[121-097]Van Siever, "do not deceive yourself. What I told you just now will

[121-098]certainly come to pass."

[121-099]"It seems to me that that must depend on the young lady," said

[121-100]Dalrymple.

[121-101]"I'll tell you what certainly will not depend on the young lady,"

[121-102]said Mrs. Van Siever, "and that is whether the man who marries her

[121-103]will have more with her than the clothes she stands up in. You will

[121-104]understand that argument, I suppose?"

[121-105]"I'm not quite sure that I do," said Dalrymple.

[121-106]"Then you'd better try to understand it. Good-morning, sir. I'm sorry

[121-107]you've had to slit your picture." Then she curtseyed low, and walked

[121-108]out on to the landing-place. "Clara," she cried, "I'm waiting for

[121-109]you--sixpence a quarter of an hour,--remember that." In a minute or

[121-110]two Clara came out to her, and then Mrs. Van Siever and Miss Van

[121-111]Siever took their departure.

[121-112]"Oh, Conway, what am I to do? what am I to do?" said Mrs. Dobbs

[121-113]Broughton. Dalrymple stood perplexed for a few minutes, and could not

[121-114]tell her what she was to do. She was in such a position that it was

[121-115]very hard to tell her what to do. "Do you believe, Conway, that he is

[121-116]really ruined?"

[121-117]"What am I to say? How am I to know?"

[121-118]"I see that you believe it," said the wretched woman.

[121-119]"I cannot but believe that there is something of truth in what this

[121-120]woman says. Why else should she come here with such a story?" Then

[121-121]there was a pause, during which Mrs. Broughton was burying her face

[121-122]on the arm of the sofa. "I'll tell you what I'll do," continued he.

[121-123]"I'll go into the City, and make inquiry. It can hardly be but what

[121-124]I shall learn the truth there."

[121-125]Then there was another pause, at the end of which Mrs. Broughton got

[121-126]up from the sofa.

[121-127]"Tell me," said she;--"what do you mean to do about that girl?"

[121-128]"You heard me ask her to be my wife?"

[121-129]"I did. I did!"

[121-130]"Is it not what you intended?"

[121-131]"Do not ask me. My mind is bewildered. My brain is on fire! Oh,

[121-132]Conway!"

[121-133]"Shall I go into the City as I proposed?" said Dalrymple, who felt

[121-134]that he might at any rate improve the position of circumstances by

[121-135]leaving the house.

[121-136]"Yes;--yes; go into the City! Go anywhere. Go. But stay! Oh, Conway!"

[121-137]There was a sudden change in her voice as she spoke. "Hark,--there he

[121-138]is, as sure as life." Then Conway listened, and heard a footstep on

[121-139]the stairs, as to which he had then but little doubt that it was the

[121-140]footstep of Dobbs Broughton. "O heavens! he is tipsy!" exclaimed Mrs.

[121-141]Broughton; "and what shall we do?" Then Dalrymple took her hand and

[121-142]pressed it, and left the room, so that he might meet the husband on

[121-143]the stairs. In the one moment that he had for reflection he thought

[121-144]it was better that there should be no concealment.

[121-145]CHAPTER LXI.

[121-146]"IT'S DOGGED AS DOES IT."

[121-147]In accordance with the resolution to which the clerical commission

[121-148]had come on the first day of their sitting, Dr. Tempest wrote the

[121-149]following letter to Mr. Crawley:--

[121-150]Rectory, Silverbridge, April 9, 186--.

[121-151]DEAR SIR,--

[121-152]I have been given to understand that you have been

[121-153]informed that the Bishop of Barchester has appointed a

[121-154]commission of clergymen of the diocese to make inquiry

[121-155]respecting certain accusations which, to the great regret

[121-156]of us all, have been made against you, in respect to a

[121-157]cheque for twenty pounds which was passed by you to a

[121-158]tradesman in this town. The clergymen appointed to form

[121-159]this commission are Mr. Oriel, the rector of Greshamsbury,

[121-160]Mr. Robarts, the vicar of Framley, Mr. Quiverful, the

[121-161]warden of Hiram's Hospital at Barchester, Mr. Thumble, a

[121-162]clergyman established in that city, and myself. We held

[121-163]our first meeting on last Monday, and I now write to you

[121-164]in compliance with a resolution to which we then came.

[121-165]Before taking any other steps we thought it best to ask

[121-166]you to attend us here on next Monday, at two o'clock, and

[121-167]I beg that you will accept this letter as an invitation to

[121-168]that effect.

[121-169]We are, of course, aware that you are about to stand your

[121-170]trial at the next assizes for the offence in question. I

[121-171]beg you to understand that I do not express any opinion as

[121-172]to your guilt. But I think it right to point out to you

[121-173]that in the event of a jury finding an adverse verdict,

[121-174]the bishop might be placed in great difficulty unless he

[121-175]were fortified with the opinion of a commission formed

[121-176]from your fellow clerical labourers in the diocese. Should

[121-177]such adverse verdict unfortunately be given, the bishop

[121-178]would hardly be justified in allowing a clergyman placed

[121-179]as you then would be placed, to return to his cure after

[121-180]the expiration of such punishment as the judge might

[121-181]award, without a further decision from an ecclesiastical

[121-182]court. This decision he could only obtain by proceeding

[121-183]against you under the Act in reference to clerical

[121-184]offences, which empowers him as bishop of the diocese to

[121-185]bring you before the Court of Arches,--unless you would

[121-186]think well to submit yourself entirely to his judgment.

[121-187]You will, I think, understand what I mean. The judge at

[121-188]assizes might find it his duty to imprison a clergyman

[121-189]for a month,--regarding that clergyman simply as he would

[121-190]regard any other person found guilty by a jury and thus

[121-191]made subject to his judgment,--and might do this for an

[121-192]offence which the ecclesiastical judge would find himself

[121-193]obliged to visit with the severer sentence of prolonged

[121-194]suspension, or even with deprivation.
