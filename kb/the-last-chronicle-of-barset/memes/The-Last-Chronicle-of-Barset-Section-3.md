[003-001]In October, Mrs. Crawley paid the twenty pounds to Fletcher, the

[003-002]butcher, and in November Lord Lufton's cheque was traced back through

[003-003]the Barchester bank to Mr. Crawley's hands. A brickmaker of Hoggle

[003-004]End, much favoured by Mr. Crawley, had asked for change over the

[003-005]counter of this Barchester bank,--not, as will be understood,

[003-006]the bank on which the cheque was drawn--and had received it. The

[003-007]accommodation had been refused to the man at first, but when he

[003-008]presented the cheque the second day, bearing Mr. Crawley's name on

[003-009]the back of it, together with a note from Mr. Crawley himself, the

[003-010]money had been given for it; and the identical notes so paid had been

[003-011]given to Fletcher, the butcher, on the next day by Mrs. Crawley. When

[003-012]inquiry was made, Mr. Crawley stated that the cheque had been paid

[003-013]to him by Mr. Soames, on behalf of the rentcharge due to him by Lord

[003-014]Lufton. But the error of this statement was at once made manifest.

[003-015]There was the cheque, signed by Mr. Soames himself, for the exact

[003-016]amount,--twenty pounds four shillings. As he himself declared, he had

[003-017]never in his life paid money on behalf of Lord Lufton by a cheque

[003-018]drawn by his lordship. The cheque given by Lord Lufton, and which had

[003-019]been lost, had been a private matter between them. His lordship had

[003-020]simply wanted change in his pocket, and his agent had given it to

[003-021]him. Mr. Crawley was speedily shown to be altogether wrong in the

[003-022]statement made to account for possession of the cheque.

[003-023]Then he became very moody and would say nothing further. But his

[003-024]wife, who had known nothing of his first statement when made, came

[003-025]forward and declared that she believed the cheque for twenty pounds

[003-026]to be a part of a present given by Dean Arabin to her husband in

[003-027]April last. There had been, she said, great heartburnings about

[003-028]this gift, and she had hardly dared to speak to her husband on the

[003-029]subject. An execution had been threatened in the house by Grobury,

[003-030]the baker, of which the dean had heard. Then there had been some

[003-031]scenes at the deanery between her husband and the dean and Mrs.

[003-032]Arabin, as to which she had subsequently heard much from Mrs. Arabin.

[003-033]Mrs. Arabin had told her that money had been given,--and at last

[003-034]taken. Indeed, so much had been very apparent, as bills had been paid

[003-035]to the amount of at least fifty pounds. When the threat made by the

[003-036]butcher had reached her husband's ears, the effect upon him had been

[003-037]very grievous. All this was the story told by Mrs. Crawley to Mr.

[003-038]Walker, the lawyer, when he was pushing his inquiries. She, poor

[003-039]woman, at any rate told all that she knew. Her husband had told her

[003-040]one morning, when the butcher's threat was weighing heavily on his

[003-041]mind, speaking to her in such a humour that she found it impossible

[003-042]to cross-question him, that he had still money left, though it was

[003-043]money which he had hoped that he would not be driven to use; and he

[003-044]had given her the four five-pound notes, and had told her to go to

[003-045]Silverbridge and satisfy the man who was so eager for his money. She

[003-046]had done so, and had felt no doubt that the money so forthcoming had

[003-047]been given by the dean. That was the story as told by Mrs. Crawley.

[003-048]But how could she explain her husband's statement as to the cheque,

[003-049]which had been shown to be altogether false? All this passed between

[003-050]Mr. Walker and Mrs. Crawley, and the lawyer was very gentle with her.

[003-051]In the first stages of the inquiry he had simply desired to learn the

[003-052]truth, and place the clergyman above suspicion. Latterly, being bound

[003-053]as he was to follow the matter up officially, he would not have seen

[003-054]Mrs. Crawley, had he been able to escape that lady's importunity.

[003-055]"Mr. Walker," she had said, at last, "you do not know my husband.

[003-056]No one knows him but I. It is hard to have to tell you of all our

[003-057]troubles." "If I can lessen them, trust me that I will do so," said

[003-058]the lawyer. "No one, I think, can lessen them in this world," said

[003-059]the lady. "The truth is, sir, that my husband often knows not what

[003-060]he says. When he declared that the money had been paid to him by Mr.

[003-061]Soames, most certainly he thought so. There are times when in his

[003-062]misery he knows not what he says,--when he forgets everything."

[003-063]Up to this period Mr. Walker had not suspected Mr. Crawley of

[003-064]anything dishonest, nor did he suspect him as yet. The poor man had

[003-065]probably received the money from the dean, and had told the lie about

[003-066]it, not choosing to own that he had taken money from his rich friend,

[003-067]and thinking that there would be no further inquiry. He had been very

[003-068]foolish, and that would be the end of it. Mr. Soames was by no means

[003-069]so good-natured in his belief. "How should my pocket-book have got

[003-070]into Dean Arabin's hands?" said Mr. Soames, almost triumphantly. "And

[003-071]then I felt sure at the time that I had left it at Crawley's house!"

[003-072]Mr. Walker wrote a letter to the dean, who at that moment was in

[003-073]Florence, on his way to Rome, from whence he was going on to the Holy

[003-074]Land. There came back a letter from Mr. Arabin, saying that on the

[003-075]17th of March he had given to Mr. Crawley a sum of fifty pounds, and

[003-076]that the payment had been made with five Bank of England notes of

[003-077]ten pounds each, which had been handed by him to his friend in the

[003-078]library at the deanery. The letter was very short, and may, perhaps,

[003-079]be described as having been almost curt. Mr. Walker, in his anxiety

[003-080]to do the best he could for Mr. Crawley, had simply asked a question

[003-081]as to the nature of the transaction between the two gentlemen, saying

[003-082]that no doubt the dean's answer would clear up a little mystery which

[003-083]existed at present respecting a cheque for twenty pounds. The dean

[003-084]in answer simply stated the fact as it has been given above; but

[003-085]he wrote to Mr. Crawley begging to know what was in truth this new

[003-086]difficulty, and offering any assistance in his power. He explained

[003-087]all the circumstances of the money, as he remembered them. The sum

[003-088]advanced had certainly consisted of fifty pounds, and there had

[003-089]certainly been five Bank of England notes. He had put the notes

[003-090]into an envelope, which he had not closed, but had addressed to Mr.

[003-091]Crawley, and had placed this envelope in his friend's hands. He went

[003-092]on to say that Mrs. Arabin would have written, but that she was in

[003-093]Paris with her son. Mrs. Arabin was to remain in Paris during his

[003-094]absence in the Holy Land, and meet him in Italy on his return. As

[003-095]she was so much nearer at hand, the dean expressed a hope that Mrs.

[003-096]Crawley would apply to her if there was any trouble.

[003-097]The letter to Mr. Walker was conclusive as to the dean's money. Mr.

[003-098]Crawley had not received Lord Lufton's cheque from the dean. Then

[003-099]whence had he received it? The poor wife was left by the lawyer to

[003-100]obtain further information from her husband. Ah, who can tell how

[003-101]terrible were the scenes between that poor pair of wretches, as the

[003-102]wife endeavoured to learn the truth from her miserable, half-maddened

[003-103]husband! That her husband had been honest throughout, she had not

[003-104]any shadow of doubt. She did not doubt that to her at least he

[003-105]endeavoured to tell the truth, as far as his poor racked imperfect

[003-106]memory would allow him to remember what was true and what was not

[003-107]true. The upshot of it all was that the husband declared that he

[003-108]still believed that the money had come to him from the dean. He had

[003-109]kept it by him, not wishing to use it if he could help it. He had

[003-110]forgotten it,--so he said at times,--having understood from Arabin

[003-111]that he was to have fifty pounds, and having received more. If it had

[003-112]not come to him from the dean, then it had been sent to him by the

[003-113]Prince of Evil for his utter undoing; and there were times in which

[003-114]he seemed to think that such had been the manner in which the fatal

[003-115]cheque had reached him. In all that he said he was terribly confused,

[003-116]contradictory, unintelligible,--speaking almost as a madman might

[003-117]speak,--ending always by declaring that the cruelty of the world had

[003-118]been too much for him, that the waters were meeting over his head,

[003-119]and praying for God's mercy to remove him from the world. It need

[003-120]hardly be said that his poor wife in these days had a burden on her

[003-121]shoulders that was more than enough to crush any woman.

[003-122]She at last acknowledged to Mr. Walker that she could not account

[003-123]for the twenty pounds. She herself would write again to the dean

[003-124]about it, but she hardly hoped for any further assistance there. "The

[003-125]dean's answer is very plain," said Mr. Walker. "He says that he gave

[003-126]Mr. Crawley five ten-pound notes, and those five notes we have traced

[003-127]to Mr. Crawley's hands." Then Mrs. Crawley could say nothing further

[003-128]beyond making protestations of her husband's innocence.

[003-129]CHAPTER II.

[003-130]BY HEAVENS HE HAD BETTER NOT!

[003-131]I must ask the reader to make the acquaintance of Major Grantly of

[003-132]Cosby Lodge, before he is introduced to the family of Mr. Crawley, at

[003-133]their parsonage in Hogglestock. It has been said that Major Grantly

[003-134]had thrown a favourable eye on Grace Crawley,--by which report

[003-135]occasion was given to all men and women in those parts to hint that

[003-136]the Crawleys, with all their piety and humility, were very cunning,

[003-137]and that one of the Grantlys was,--to say the least of it,--very

[003-138]soft, admitted as it was throughout the county of Barsetshire,

[003-139]that there was no family therein more widely awake to the affairs

[003-140]generally of this world and the next combined, than the family of

[003-141]which Archdeacon Grantly was the respected head and patriarch. Mrs.

[003-142]Walker, the most good-natured woman in Silverbridge, had acknowledged

[003-143]to her daughter that she could not understand it,--that she could not

[003-144]see anything at all in Grace Crawley. Mr. Walker had shrugged his

[003-145]shoulders and expressed a confident belief that Major Grantly had

[003-146]not a shilling of his own beyond his half-pay and his late wife's

[003-147]fortune, which was only six thousand pounds. Others, who were

[003-148]ill-natured, had declared that Grace Crawley was little better than a

[003-149]beggar, and that she could not possibly have acquired the manners of

[003-150]a gentlewoman. Fletcher the butcher had wondered whether the major

[003-151]would pay his future father-in-law's debts; and Dr. Tempest, the

[003-152]old rector of Silverbridge, whose four daughters were all as yet

[003-153]unmarried, had turned up his old nose, and had hinted that half-pay

[003-154]majors did not get caught in marriage so easily as that.

[003-155]Such and such like had been the expressions of the opinion of

[003-156]men and women in Silverbridge. But the matter had been discussed

[003-157]further afield than at Silverbridge, and had been allowed to intrude

[003-158]itself as a most unwelcome subject into the family conclave of the

[003-159]archdeacon's rectory. To those who have not as yet learned the fact

[003-160]from the public character and well-appreciated reputation of the

[003-161]man, let it be known that Archdeacon Grantly was at this time, as

[003-162]he had been for many years previously, Archdeacon of Barchester and

[003-163]Rector of Plumstead Episcopi. A rich and prosperous man he had ever

[003-164]been,--though he also had had his sore troubles, as we all have,--his

[003-165]having arisen chiefly from want of that higher ecclesiastical

[003-166]promotion which his soul had coveted, and for which the whole tenour

[003-167]of his life had especially fitted him. Now, in his green old age, he

[003-168]had ceased to covet, but had not ceased to repine. He had ceased to

[003-169]covet aught for himself, but still coveted much for his children; and

[003-170]for him such a marriage as this which was now suggested for his son

[003-171]was encompassed almost with the bitterness of death. "I think it

[003-172]would kill me," he had said to his wife; "by heavens, I think it

[003-173]would be my death!"
