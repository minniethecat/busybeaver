## Character

Mr. Musselboro is a City man of shady reputation whose most distinctive feature — his enormous whiskers — makes him instantly recognizable and faintly absurd. "If I don't see the whiskers," a character remarks of him, "I shall know there's a Peer" (044-134). He is a business associate of Mr. Dobbs Broughton, involved in the financial speculations that sustain the Broughtons' fashionable lifestyle, and his role in the novel is that of the predator who profits from another man's ruin. Trollope sketches him with economical disdain — he is not a fully realized villain but a type of the grasping City operator whose fortunes rise as the speculating gentry's fall.

## Biography

Musselboro is a partner or associate in Dobbs Broughton's financial ventures, though the exact nature of their dealings remains deliberately obscure — a murkiness that Trollope uses to suggest the opacity of City finance. He appears in the novel's London scenes, hovering at the edges of Mrs. Dobbs Broughton's social circle and conducting his business with a quiet, unshowy competence that contrasts with Dobbs Broughton's more flamboyant speculations. When Dobbs Broughton's financial house collapses, Musselboro is positioned to pick up the pieces — his survival and probable profit from the wreckage illustrate the Darwinian nature of Victorian commercial life. "Mr. Musselboro was in the room" during key business discussions, always present, always calculating (044-135).

## Significance

Mr. Musselboro represents the commercial underside of the London world that Trollope's Barsetshire characters must navigate. His whiskers — that comic physical detail — mark him as a type: the vulgar but effective City man whose competence in financial matters outstrips the gentlemanly amateurs he displaces. In the novel's moral economy, Musselboro is a survivor, not a villain — he operates within the rules of commercial life as they exist, not as gentility might wish them to be. His presence in the novel, and the Broughtons' fall, serve as a cautionary counterpoint to the landed stability represented by Framley Court and the Barsetshire clergy.
