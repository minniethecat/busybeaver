[108-001]"Upon my word we do," said Lady Lufton.

[108-002]I am induced to think that Mr. Robarts was not quite confident of the

[108-003]expediency of what he was doing by the way in which he mentioned to

[108-004]Mr. Oriel the fact of Miss Crawley's presence at the parsonage as he

[108-005]drove that gentleman home in his gig. They had been talking about Mr.

[108-006]Crawley when he suddenly turned himself round, so that he could look

[108-007]at his companion, and said, "Miss Crawley is staying with us at the

[108-008]parsonage at the present moment."

[108-009]"What! Mr. Crawley's daughter?" said Mr. Oriel, showing plainly by

[108-010]his voice that the tidings had much surprised him.

[108-011]"Yes; Mr. Crawley's daughter."

[108-012]"Oh, indeed. I did not know that you were on those terms with the

[108-013]family."

[108-014]"We have known them for the last seven or eight years," said Mark;

[108-015]"and though I should be giving you a false notion if I were to say

[108-016]that I myself have known them intimately,--for Crawley is a man

[108-017]whom it is quite impossible to know intimately,--yet the womankind

[108-018]at Framley have known them. My sister stayed with them over at

[108-019]Hogglestock for some time."

[108-020]"What; Lady Lufton?"

[108-021]"Yes; my sister Lucy. It was just before her marriage. There was a

[108-022]lot of trouble, and the Crawleys were all ill, and she went to nurse

[108-023]them. And then the old lady took them up, and altogether there came

[108-024]to be a sort of feeling that they were to be regarded as friends.

[108-025]They are always in trouble, and now in this special trouble the women

[108-026]between them have thought it best to have the girl over at Framley.

[108-027]Of course I had a kind of feeling about this commission; but as I

[108-028]knew that it would make no difference with me I did not think it

[108-029]necessary to put my veto upon the visit." Mr. Oriel said nothing

[108-030]further, but Mark Robarts was aware that Mr. Oriel did not quite

[108-031]approve of the visit.

[108-032]That morning old Lady Lufton herself had come across to the parsonage

[108-033]with the express view of bidding all the parsonage party to come

[108-034]across to the hall to dine. "You can tell Mr. Oriel, Fanny, with

[108-035]Lucy's compliments, how delighted she will be to see him." Old Lady

[108-036]Lufton always spoke of her daughter-in-law as the mistress of the

[108-037]house. "If you think he is particular, you know, we will send a note

[108-038]across." Mrs. Robarts said that she supposed Mr. Oriel would not be

[108-039]particular, but, looking at Grace, made some faint excuse. "You must

[108-040]come, my dear," said Lady Lufton. "Lucy wishes it particularly." Mrs.

[108-041]Robarts did not know how to say that she would not come; and so the

[108-042]matter stood,--when Mrs. Robarts was called upon to leave the room

[108-043]for a moment, and Lady Lufton and Grace were left alone.

[108-044]"Dear Lady Lufton," said Grace, getting up suddenly from her chair;

[108-045]"will you do me a favour,--a great favour?" She spoke with an energy

[108-046]which quite surprised the old lady, and caused her almost to start

[108-047]from her seat.

[108-048]"I don't like making promises," said Lady Lufton; "but anything I can

[108-049]do with propriety I will."

[108-050]"You can do this. Pray let me stay here to-day. You don't understand

[108-051]how I feel about going out while papa is in this way. I know how

[108-052]kind and how good you all are; and when dear Mrs. Robarts asked me

[108-053]here, and mamma said that I had better come, I could not refuse. But

[108-054]indeed, indeed, I had rather not go out to a dinner-party."

[108-055]"It is not a party, my dear girl," said Lady Lufton, with the kindest

[108-056]voice which she knew how to assume. "And you must remember that my

[108-057]daughter-in-law regards you as so very old a friend! You remember, of

[108-058]course, when she was staying over at Hogglestock?"

[108-059]"Indeed I do. I remember it well."

[108-060]"And therefore you should not regard it as going out. There will be

[108-061]nobody there but ourselves and the people from this house."

[108-062]"But it will be going out, Lady Lufton; and I do hope you will let

[108-063]me stay here. You cannot think how I feel it. Of course I cannot go

[108-064]without something like dressing, and--and--and-- In poor papa's state

[108-065]I feel that I ought not to do anything that looks like gaiety. I

[108-066]ought never to forget it;--not for a moment."

[108-067]There was a tear in Lady Lufton's eye as she said,--"My dear, you

[108-068]shan't come. You and Fanny shall stop and dine here by yourselves.

[108-069]The gentlemen shall come."

[108-070]"Do let Mrs. Robarts go, please," said Grace.

[108-071]"I won't do anything of the kind," said Lady Lufton. Then, when Mrs.

[108-072]Robarts returned to the room, her ladyship explained it all in two

[108-073]words. "Whilst you have been away, my dear, Grace has begged off, and

[108-074]therefore we have decided that Mr. Oriel and Mr. Robarts shall come

[108-075]without you."

[108-076]"I am so sorry, Mrs. Robarts," said Grace.

[108-077]"Pooh, pooh," said Lady Lufton. "Fanny and I have known each other

[108-078]quite long enough not to stand on any compliments,--haven't we, my

[108-079]dear? I must get home now, as all the morning has gone by. Fanny my

[108-080]dear, I want to speak to you." Then she expressed her opinion of

[108-081]Grace Crawley as she walked across the parsonage garden with Mrs.

[108-082]Robarts. "She is a very nice girl, and a very good girl, I am sure;

[108-083]and she shows excellent feeling. Whatever happens we must take care

[108-084]of her. And, Fanny, have you observed how handsome she is?"

[108-085]"We think her very pretty."

[108-086]"She is more than pretty when she has a little fire in her eyes. She

[108-087]is downright handsome,--or will be when she fills out a little. I

[108-088]tell you what, my dear; she'll make havoc with somebody yet; you see

[108-089]if she doesn't. By--by. Tell the two gentlemen to be up by seven

[108-090]punctually." And then Lady Lufton went home.

[108-091]Grace so contrived that Mr. Oriel came and went without seeing

[108-092]her. There was a separate nursery breakfast at the parsonage,

[108-093]and by special permission Grace was allowed to have her tea and

[108-094]bread-and-butter on the next morning with the children. "I thought

[108-095]you told me Miss Crawley was here," said Mr. Oriel, as the two

[108-096]clergymen stood waiting for the gig that was to take the visitor away

[108-097]to Barchester.

[108-098]"So she is," said Robarts; "but she likes to hide herself, because of

[108-099]her father's trouble. You can't blame her."

[108-100]"No, indeed," said Mr. Oriel.

[108-101]"Poor girl. If you knew her you would not only pity her, but like

[108-102]her."

[108-103]"Is she,--what you call--?"

[108-104]"You mean, is she a lady?"

[108-105]"Of course she is by birth, and all that," said Mr. Oriel,

[108-106]apologizing for his inquiry.

[108-107]"I don't think there is another girl in the county so well educated,"

[108-108]said Mr. Robarts.

[108-109]"Indeed! I had no idea of that."

[108-110]"And we think her a great beauty. As for manners, I never saw a girl

[108-111]with a prettier way of her own."

[108-112]"Dear me," said Mr. Oriel. "I wish she had come down to breakfast."

[108-113]It will have been perceived that old Lady Lufton had heard nothing

[108-114]of Major Grantly's offence; that she had no knowledge that Grace had

[108-115]already made havoc, as she had called it,--had, in truth, made very

[108-116]sad havoc, at Plumstead. She did not, therefore, think much about it

[108-117]when her son told her upon her return home from the parsonage on that

[108-118]afternoon that Major Grantly had come over from Cosby Lodge, and that

[108-119]he was going to dine and sleep at Framley Court. Some slight idea of

[108-120]thankfulness came across her mind that she had not betrayed Grace

[108-121]Crawley into a meeting with a stranger. "I asked him to come some day

[108-122]before we went up to town," said his lordship; "and I am glad he has

[108-123]come to-day, as two clergymen to one's self are, at any rate, one too

[108-124]many." So Major Grantly dined and slept at the Court.

[108-125]But Mrs. Robarts was in a great flurry when she was told of this by

[108-126]her husband on his return from the dinner. Mrs. Crawley had found

[108-127]an opportunity of telling the story of Major Grantly's love to Mrs.

[108-128]Robarts before she had sent her daughter to Framley, knowing that the

[108-129]families were intimate, and thinking it right that there should be

[108-130]some precaution.

[108-131]"I wonder whether he will come up here," Mrs. Robarts had said.

[108-132]"Probably not," said the vicar. "He said he was going home early."

[108-133]"I hope he will not come--for Grace's sake," said Mrs. Robarts. She

[108-134]hesitated whether she should tell her husband. She always did tell

[108-135]him everything. But on this occasion she thought she had no right to

[108-136]do so, and she kept the secret. "Don't do anything to bring him up,

[108-137]dear."

[108-138]"You needn't be afraid. He won't come," said the vicar. On the

[108-139]following morning, as soon as Mr. Oriel was gone, Mr. Robarts went

[108-140]out,--about his parish he would probably have called it; but in half

[108-141]an hour he might have been seen strolling about the Court stable-yard

[108-142]with Lord Lufton. "Where is Grantly?" asked the vicar. "I don't know

[108-143]where he is," said his lordship. "He has sloped off somewhere." The

[108-144]major had sloped off to the parsonage, well knowing in what nest his

[108-145]dove was lying hid; and he and the vicar had passed each other. The

[108-146]major had gone out at the front gate, and the vicar had gone in at

[108-147]the stable entrance.

[108-148]The two clergymen had hardly taken their departure when Major Grantly

[108-149]knocked at the parsonage door. He had come so early that Mrs. Robarts

[108-150]had taken no precautions,--even had there been any precautions which

[108-151]she would have thought it right to take. Grace was in the act of

[108-152]coming down the stairs, not having heard the knock at the door,

[108-153]and thus she found her lover in the hall. He had asked, of course,

[108-154]for Mrs. Robarts, and thus they two entered the drawing-room

[108-155]together. They had not had time to speak when the servant opened the

[108-156]drawing-room door to announce the visitor. There had been no word

[108-157]spoken between Mrs. Robarts and Grace about Major Grantly, but the

[108-158]mother had told the daughter of what she had said to Mrs. Robarts.

[108-159]"Grace," said the major, "I am so glad to find you!" Then he turned

[108-160]to Mrs. Robarts with his open hand. "You won't take it uncivil of me

[108-161]if I say that my visit is not entirely to yourself? I think I may

[108-162]take upon myself to say that I and Miss Crawley are old friends. May

[108-163]I not?"

[108-164]Grace could not answer a word. "Mrs. Crawley told me that you

[108-165]had known her at Silverbridge," said Mrs. Robarts, driven to say

[108-166]something, but feeling that she was blundering.

[108-167]"I came over to Framley yesterday because I heard that she was here.

[108-168]Am I wrong to come up here to see her?"

[108-169]"I think she must answer that for herself, Major Grantly."

[108-170]"Am I wrong, Grace?" Grace thought that he was the finest gentleman

[108-171]and the noblest lover that had ever shown his devotion to a woman,

[108-172]and was stirred by a mighty resolve that if it ever should be in her

[108-173]power to reward him after any fashion, she would pour out the reward

[108-174]with a very full hand indeed. But what was she to say on the present

[108-175]moment? "Am I wrong, Grace?" he said, repeating his question with so

[108-176]much emphasis, that she was positively driven to answer it.

[108-177]"I do not think you are wrong at all. How can I say you are wrong

[108-178]when you are so good? If I could be your servant I would serve you.

[108-179]But I can be nothing to you, because of papa's disgrace. Dear Mrs.

[108-180]Robarts, I cannot stay. You must answer him for me." And having thus

[108-181]made her speech she escaped from the room.
