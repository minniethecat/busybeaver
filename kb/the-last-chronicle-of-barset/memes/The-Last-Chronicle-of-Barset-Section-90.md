[090-001]Not a word was spoken that night at the palace about Mr. Crawley;

[090-002]and when that obnoxious guest from Plumstead was gone, Mrs. Proudie

[090-003]resumed her good-humour towards Dr. Tempest. So intent was she on

[090-004]conciliating him that she refrained even from abusing the archdeacon,

[090-005]whom she knew to have been intimate for very many years with the

[090-006]rector of Silverbridge. In her accustomed moods she would have broken

[090-007]forth in loud anger, caring nothing for old friendships; but at

[090-008]present she was thoughtful of the morrow, and desirous that Dr.

[090-009]Tempest should, if possible, meet her in a friendly humour when the

[090-010]great discussion as to Hogglestock should be opened between them. But

[090-011]Dr. Tempest understood her bearing, and as he pulled on his nightcap

[090-012]made certain resolutions of his own as to the morrow's proceedings.

[090-013]"I don't suppose she will dare to interfere," he had said to his

[090-014]wife; "but if she does, I shall certainly tell the bishop that I

[090-015]cannot speak on the subject in her presence."

[090-016]At breakfast on the following morning there was no one present but

[090-017]the bishop, Mrs. Proudie, and Dr. Tempest. Very little was said at

[090-018]the meal. Mr. Crawley's name was not mentioned, but there seemed to

[090-019]be a general feeling among them that there was a task hanging over

[090-020]them which prevented any general conversation. The eggs were eaten

[090-021]and the coffee was drunk, but the eggs and the coffee disappeared

[090-022]almost in silence. When these ceremonies had been altogether

[090-023]completed, and it was clearly necessary that something further should

[090-024]be done, the bishop spoke: "Dr. Tempest," he said, "perhaps you will

[090-025]join me in my study at eleven. We can then say a few words to each

[090-026]other about the unfortunate matter on which I shall have to trouble

[090-027]you." Dr. Tempest said he would be punctual to his appointment, and

[090-028]then the bishop withdrew, muttering something as to the necessity

[090-029]of looking at his letters. Dr. Tempest took a newspaper in his hand,

[090-030]which had been brought in by a servant, but Mrs. Proudie did not

[090-031]allow him to read it. "Dr. Tempest," she said, "this is a matter of

[090-032]most vital importance. I am quite sure that you feel that it is so."

[090-033]"What matter, madam?" said the doctor.

[090-034]"This terrible affair of Mr. Crawley's. If something be not done the

[090-035]whole diocese will be disgraced." Then she waited for an answer, but

[090-036]receiving none she was obliged to continue. "Of the poor man's guilt

[090-037]there can, I fear, be no doubt." Then there was another pause, but

[090-038]still the doctor made no answer. "And if he be guilty," said Mrs.

[090-039]Proudie, resolving that she would ask a question that must bring

[090-040]forth some reply, "can any experienced clergyman think that he can be

[090-041]fit to preach from the pulpit of a parish church? I am sure that you

[090-042]must agree with me, Dr. Tempest? Consider the souls of the people!"

[090-043]"Mrs. Proudie," said he, "I think that we had better not discuss the

[090-044]matter."

[090-045]"Not discuss it?"

[090-046]"I think that we had better not do so. If I understand the bishop

[090-047]aright, he wishes that I should take some step in the matter."

[090-048]"Of course he does."

[090-049]"And therefore I must decline to make it a matter of common

[090-050]conversation."

[090-051]"Common conversation, Dr. Tempest! I should be the last person in the

[090-052]world to make it a matter of common conversation. I regard this as by

[090-053]no means a common conversation. God forbid that it should be a common

[090-054]conversation. I am speaking now very seriously with reference to the

[090-055]interests of the Church, which I think will be endangered by having

[090-056]among her active servants a man who has been guilty of so base a

[090-057]crime as theft. Think of it, Dr. Tempest. Theft! Stealing money!

[090-058]Appropriating to his own use a cheque for twenty pounds which did

[090-059]not belong to him! And then telling such terrible falsehoods about

[090-060]it! Can anything be worse, anything more scandalous, anything more

[090-061]dangerous? Indeed, Dr. Tempest, I do not regard this as any common

[090-062]conversation." The whole of this speech was not made at once,

[090-063]fluently, or without a break. From stop to stop Mrs. Proudie paused,

[090-064]waiting for her companion's words; but as he would not speak she was

[090-065]obliged to continue. "I am sure that you cannot but agree with me,

[090-066]Dr. Tempest?" she said.

[090-067]"I am quite sure that I shall not discuss it with you," said the

[090-068]doctor, very brusquely.

[090-069]"And why not? Are you not here to discuss it?"

[090-070]"Not with you, Mrs. Proudie. You must excuse me for saying so, but I

[090-071]am not here to discuss any such matter with you. Were I to do so, I

[090-072]should be guilty of a very great impropriety."

[090-073]"All these things are in common between me and the bishop," said Mrs.

[090-074]Proudie, with an air that was intended to be dignified, but which

[090-075]nevertheless displayed her rising anger.

[090-076]"As to that I know nothing, but they cannot be in common between you

[090-077]and me. It grieves me much that I should have to speak to you in

[090-078]such a strain, but my duty allows me no alternative. I think, if you

[090-079]will permit me, I will take a turn round the garden before I keep my

[090-080]appointment with his lordship." And so saying he escaped from the

[090-081]lady without hearing her further remonstrance.

[090-082]It still wanted nearly an hour to the time named by the bishop, and

[090-083]Dr. Tempest used it in preparing for his withdrawal from the palace

[090-084]as soon as his interview with the bishop should be over. After what

[090-085]had passed he thought that he would be justified in taking his

[090-086]departure without bidding adieu formally to Mrs. Proudie. He would

[090-087]say a word or two, explaining his haste, to the bishop; and then,

[090-088]if he could get out of the house at once, it might be that he would

[090-089]never see Mrs. Proudie again. He was rather proud of his success

[090-090]in their late battle, but he felt that, having been so completely

[090-091]victorious, it would be foolish in him to risk his laurels in the

[090-092]chance of another encounter. He would say not a word of what had

[090-093]happened to the bishop, and he thought it probable that neither

[090-094]would Mrs. Proudie speak of it,--at any rate till after he was gone.

[090-095]Generals who are beaten out of the field are not quick to talk of

[090-096]their own repulses. He, indeed, had not beaten Mrs. Proudie out of

[090-097]the field. He had, in fact, himself run away. But he had left his

[090-098]foe silenced; and with such a foe, and in such a contest, that was

[090-099]everything. He put up his portmanteau, therefore, and prepared for

[090-100]his final retreat. Then he rang his bell and desired the servant

[090-101]to show him to the bishop's study. The servant did so, and when he

[090-102]entered the room the first thing he saw was Mrs. Proudie sitting

[090-103]in an arm-chair near the window. The bishop was also in the room,

[090-104]sitting with his arms upon the writing-table, and his head upon his

[090-105]hands. It was very evident that Mrs. Proudie did not consider herself

[090-106]to have been beaten, and that she was prepared to fight another

[090-107]battle. "Will you sit down, Dr. Tempest?" she said, motioning him

[090-108]with her hand to a chair opposite to that occupied by the bishop. Dr.

[090-109]Tempest sat down. He felt that at the moment he had nothing else to

[090-110]do, and that he must restrain any remonstrance that he might make

[090-111]till Mr. Crawley's name should be mentioned. He was almost lost in

[090-112]admiration of the woman. He had left her, as he thought, utterly

[090-113]vanquished and prostrated by his determined but uncourteous usage

[090-114]of her; and here she was, present again upon the field of battle as

[090-115]though she had never been even wounded. He could see that there had

[090-116]been words between her and the bishop, and that she had carried a

[090-117]point on which the bishop had been very anxious to have his own way.

[090-118]He could perceive at once that the bishop had begged her to absent

[090-119]herself and was greatly chagrined that he should not have prevailed

[090-120]with her. There she was,--and as Dr. Tempest was resolved that he

[090-121]would neither give advice nor receive instructions respecting Mr.

[090-122]Crawley in her presence, he could only draw upon his courage and

[090-123]his strategy for the coming warfare. For a few moments no one said

[090-124]a word. The bishop felt that if Dr. Tempest would only begin, the

[090-125]work on hand might be got through, even in his wife's presence. Mrs.

[090-126]Proudie was aware that her husband should begin. If he would do so,

[090-127]and if Dr. Tempest would listen and then reply, she might gradually

[090-128]make her way into the conversation; and if her words were once

[090-129]accepted then she could say all that she desired to say; then she

[090-130]could play her part and become somebody in the episcopal work. When

[090-131]once she should have been allowed liberty of speech, the enemy would

[090-132]be powerless to stop her. But all this Dr. Tempest understood quite

[090-133]as well as she understood it, and had they waited till night he would

[090-134]not have been the first to mention Mr. Crawley's name.

[090-135]The bishop sighed aloud. The sigh might be taken as expressing grief

[090-136]over the sin of the erring brother whose conduct they were then to

[090-137]discuss, and was not amiss. But when the sigh with its attendant

[090-138]murmurs had passed away it was necessary that some initiative step

[090-139]should be taken. "Dr. Tempest," said the bishop, "what are we to do

[090-140]about this poor stiff-necked gentleman?" Still Dr. Tempest did not

[090-141]speak. "There is no clergyman in the diocese," continued the bishop,

[090-142]"in whose prudence and wisdom I have more confidence than in yours.

[090-143]And I know, too, that you are by no means disposed to severity where

[090-144]severe measures are not necessary. What ought we to do? If he has

[090-145]been guilty, he should not surely return to his pulpit after the

[090-146]expiration of such punishment as the law of his country may award to

[090-147]him."

[090-148]Dr. Tempest looked at Mrs. Proudie, thinking that she might perhaps

[090-149]say a word now; but Mrs. Proudie knew her part better and was silent.

[090-150]Angry as she was, she contrived to hold her peace. Let the debate

[090-151]once begin and she would be able to creep into it, and then to lead

[090-152]it,--and so she would hold her own. But she had met a foe as wary

[090-153]as herself. "My lord," said the doctor, "it will perhaps be well

[090-154]that you should communicate your wishes to me in writing. If it be

[090-155]possible for me to comply with them I will do so."

[090-156]"Yes;--exactly; no doubt;--but I thought that perhaps we might better

[090-157]understand each other if we had a few words of quiet conversation

[090-158]upon the subject. I believe you know the steps that I have--"

[090-159]But here the bishop was interrupted. Dr. Tempest rose from his chair,

[090-160]and advancing to the table put both his hands upon it. "My lord," he

[090-161]said, "I feel myself compelled to say that which I would very much

[090-162]rather leave unsaid, were it possible. I feel the difficulty, and

[090-163]I may say delicacy, of my position; but I should be untrue to my

[090-164]conscience and to my feeling of what is right in such matters, if I

[090-165]were to take any part in a discussion on this matter in the presence

[090-166]of--a lady."

[090-167]"Dr. Tempest, what is your objection?" said Mrs. Proudie, rising from

[090-168]her chair, and coming also to the table, so that from thence she

[090-169]might confront her opponent; and as she stood opposite to Dr. Tempest

[090-170]she also put both her hands upon the table.

[090-171]"My dear, perhaps you will leave us for a few moments," said the

[090-172]bishop. Poor bishop! Poor weak bishop! As the words came from his

[090-173]mouth he knew that they would be spoken in vain, and that, if so, it

[090-174]would have been better for him to have left them unspoken.
