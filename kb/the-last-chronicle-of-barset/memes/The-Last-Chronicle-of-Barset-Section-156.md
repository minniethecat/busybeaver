[156-001]"Mrs. Arabin is too kind," said Mrs. Crawley, looking across at her

[156-002]husband.

[156-003]"We should like it of all things," said the dean, with perhaps more

[156-004]of good nature than of truth. "Of course you must have been knocked

[156-005]about a good deal."

[156-006]"Indeed we have," said Mrs. Crawley.

[156-007]"And till you are somewhat settled again, I think that the change of

[156-008]scene would be good for all of you. Come, Crawley, I'll talk to you

[156-009]every evening about Jerusalem for as long as you please;--and then

[156-010]there will perhaps come back to us something of the pleasantness

[156-011]of old days." As she heard this Mrs. Crawley's eyes became full of

[156-012]tears, and she could not altogether hide them. What she had endured

[156-013]during the last four months had almost broken her spirit. The burden

[156-014]had at last been too heavy for her strength. "You cannot fancy,

[156-015]Crawley, how often I have thought of the old days and wished that

[156-016]they might return. I have found it very hard to get an opportunity of

[156-017]saying so much to you; but I will say it now."

[156-018]"It may hardly be as you say," said Crawley, grimly.

[156-019]"You mean that the old days can never be brought back?"

[156-020]"Assuredly they cannot. But it was not that that I meant. It may not

[156-021]be that I and mine should transfer ourselves to your roof and sojourn

[156-022]there."

[156-023]"Why should you not?"

[156-024]"The reasons are many, and on the face of things. The reason,

[156-025]perhaps, the most on the face is to be found in my wife's gown, and

[156-026]in my coat." This Mr. Crawley said very gravely, looking neither to

[156-027]the right nor to the left, nor at the face of any of them, nor at his

[156-028]own garment, nor at hers, but straight before him; and when he had

[156-029]so spoken he said not a word further,--not going on to dilate on his

[156-030]poverty as the dean expected that he would do.

[156-031]"At such a time such reasons should stand for nothing," said the

[156-032]dean.

[156-033]"And why not now as they always do, and always must till the power

[156-034]of tailors shall have waned, and the daughters of Eve shall toil and

[156-035]spin no more? Like to like is true, and should be held to be true, of

[156-036]all societies and of all compacts for co-operation and mutual living.

[156-037]Here, where, if I may venture to say so, you and I are like to

[156-038]like;--for the new gloss of your coat,"--the dean, as it happened,

[156-039]had on at the moment a very old coat, his oldest coat, selected

[156-040]perhaps with some view to this special visit,--"does not obtrude

[156-041]itself in my household, as would the threadbare texture of mine in

[156-042]yours;--I can open my mouth to you and converse with you at my ease;

[156-043]you are now to me that Frank Arabin who has so often comforted me

[156-044]and so often confuted me; whom I may perhaps on an occasion have

[156-045]confuted--and perhaps have comforted. But were I sitting with you in

[156-046]your library in Barchester, my threadbare coat would be too much for

[156-047]me. I should be silent, if not sullen. I should feel the weight of

[156-048]all my poverty, and the greater weight of all your wealth. For my

[156-049]children, let them go. I have come to know that they will be better

[156-050]away from me."

[156-051]"Papa!" said Jane.

[156-052]"Papa does not mean it," said Grace, coming up to him and standing

[156-053]close to him.

[156-054]There was silence amongst them for a few moments, and then the master

[156-055]of the house shook himself,--literally shook himself, till he had

[156-056]shaken off the cloud. He had taken Grace by the hand, and thrusting

[156-057]out the other arm had got it round Jane's waist. "When a man has

[156-058]girls, Arabin," he said, "as you have, but not big girls yet like

[156-059]Grace here, of course he knows that they will fly away."

[156-060]"I shall not fly away," said Jane.

[156-061]"I don't know what papa means," said Grace.

[156-062]Upon the whole the dean thought it the pleasantest visit he had ever

[156-063]made to Hogglestock, and when he got home he told his wife that he

[156-064]believed that the accusation made against Mr. Crawley had done him

[156-065]good. "I could not say a word in private to her," he said, "but I

[156-066]did promise that you would go and see her." On the very next day Mrs.

[156-067]Arabin went over, and I think that the visit was a comfort to Mrs.

[156-068]Crawley.

[156-069]CHAPTER LXXX.

[156-070]MISS DEMOLINES DESIRES TO BECOME A FINGER-POST.

[156-071]John Eames had passed Mrs. Thorne in the hall of her own house almost

[156-072]without noticing her as he took his departure from Lily Dale. She had

[156-073]told him as plainly as words could speak that she could not bring

[156-074]herself to be his wife,--and he had believed her. He had sworn to

[156-075]himself that if he did not succeed now he would never ask her again.

[156-076]"It would be foolish and unmanly to do so," he said to himself as he

[156-077]rushed along the street towards his club. No! That romance was over.

[156-078]At last there had come an end to it! "It has taken a good bit out of

[156-079]me," he said, arresting his steps suddenly that he might stand still

[156-080]and think of it all. "By George, yes! A man doesn't go through that

[156-081]kind of thing without losing some of the caloric. I couldn't do it

[156-082]again if an angel came in my way." He went to his club, and tried to

[156-083]be jolly. He ordered a good dinner, and got some man to come and dine

[156-084]with him. For an hour or so he held himself up, and did appear to be

[156-085]jolly. But as he walked home at night, and gave himself time to think

[156-086]over what had taken place with deliberation, he stopped in the gloom

[156-087]of a deserted street and leaning against the rails burst into tears.

[156-088]He had really loved her and she was never to be his. He had wanted

[156-089]her,--and it is so painful a thing to miss what you want when you

[156-090]have done your very best to obtain it! To struggle in vain always

[156-091]hurts the pride; but the wound made by the vain struggle for a woman

[156-092]is sorer than any other wound so made. He gnashed his teeth, and

[156-093]struck the iron railings with his stick;--and then he hurried home,

[156-094]swearing that he would never give another thought to Lily Dale. In

[156-095]the dead of the night, thinking of it still, he asked himself whether

[156-096]it would not be a fine thing to wait another ten years, and then go

[156-097]to her again. In such a way would he not make himself immortal as a

[156-098]lover beyond any Jacob or any Leander?

[156-099]The next day he went to his office and was very grave. When Sir

[156-100]Raffle complimented him on being back before his time, he simply said

[156-101]that when he had accomplished that for which he had gone, he had, of

[156-102]course, come back. Sir Raffle could not get a word out from him about

[156-103]Mr. Crawley. He was very grave, and intent upon his work. Indeed

[156-104]he was so serious that he quite afflicted Sir Raffle,--whose mock

[156-105]activity felt itself to be confounded by the official zeal of his

[156-106]private secretary. During the whole of that day Johnny was resolving

[156-107]that there could be no cure for his malady but hard work. He would

[156-108]not only work hard at the office if he remained there, but he would

[156-109]take to heavy reading. He rather thought that he would go deep into

[156-110]Greek and do a translation, or take up the exact sciences and make

[156-111]a name for himself that way. But as he had enough for the life of a

[156-112]secluded literary man without his salary, he rather thought that he

[156-113]would give up his office altogether. He had a mutton chop at home

[156-114]that evening, and spent his time in endeavouring to read out loud to

[156-115]himself certain passages from the Iliad;--for he had bought a Homer

[156-116]as he returned from his office. At nine o'clock he went, half-price,

[156-117]to the Strand Theatre. How he met there his old friend Boulger and

[156-118]went afterwards to "The Cock" and had a supper need not here be told

[156-119]with more accurate detail.

[156-120]On the evening of the next day he was bound by his appointment to go

[156-121]to Porchester Terrace. In the moments of his enthusiasm about Homer

[156-122]he had declared to himself that he would never go near Miss Demolines

[156-123]again. Why should he? All that kind of thing was nothing to him

[156-124]now. He would simply send her his compliments and say that he was

[156-125]prevented by business from keeping his engagement. She, of course,

[156-126]would go on writing to him for a time, but he would simply leave

[156-127]her letters unanswered, and the thing, of course, would come to an

[156-128]end at last. He afterwards said something to Boulger about Miss

[156-129]Demolines,--but that was during the jollity of their supper,--and he

[156-130]then declared that he would follow out that little game. "I don't see

[156-131]why a fellow isn't to amuse himself, eh, Boulger, old boy?" Boulger

[156-132]winked and grinned, and said that some amusements were dangerous. "I

[156-133]don't think that there is any danger there," said Johnny. "I don't

[156-134]believe she is thinking of that kind of thing herself;--not with me

[156-135]at least. What she likes is the pretence of a mystery; and as it is

[156-136]amusing I don't see why a fellow shouldn't indulge her." But that

[156-137]determination was pronounced after two mutton chops at "The Cock,"

[156-138]between one and two o'clock in the morning. On the next day he was

[156-139]cooler and wiser. Greek he thought might be tedious as he discovered

[156-140]that he would have to begin again from the very alphabet. He would

[156-141]therefore abandon that idea. Greek was not the thing for him, but

[156-142]he would take up the sanitary condition of the poor in London. A

[156-143]fellow could be of some use in that way. In the meantime he would

[156-144]keep his appointment with Miss Demolines, simply because it was an

[156-145]appointment. A gentleman should always keep his word to a lady!

[156-146]He did keep his appointment with Miss Demolines, and was with her

[156-147]almost precisely at the hour she had named. She received him with a

[156-148]mysterious tranquillity which almost perplexed him. He remembered,

[156-149]however, that the way to enjoy the society of Miss Demolines was to

[156-150]take her in all her moods with perfect seriousness, and was therefore

[156-151]very tranquil himself. On the present occasion she did not rise as he

[156-152]entered the room, and hardly spoke as she tendered to him the tips

[156-153]of her fingers to be touched. As she said almost nothing, he said

[156-154]nothing at all, but sank into a chair and stretched his legs out

[156-155]comfortably before him. It had been always understood between them

[156-156]that she was to bear the burden of the conversation.

[156-157]"You'll have a cup of tea?" she said.

[156-158]"Yes;--if you do." Then the page brought the tea, and John Eames

[156-159]amused himself with swallowing three slices of very thin bread and

[156-160]butter.

[156-161]"None for me,--thanks," said Madalina. "I rarely eat after dinner,

[156-162]and not often much then. I fancy that I should best like a world in

[156-163]which there was no eating."

[156-164]"A good dinner is a very good thing," said John. And then there was

[156-165]again silence. He was aware that some great secret was to be told to

[156-166]him during this evening, but he was much too discreet to show any

[156-167]curiosity upon that subject. He sipped his tea to the end, and then,

[156-168]having got up to put his cup down, stood on the rug with his back to

[156-169]the fire. "Have you been out to-day?" he asked.

[156-170]"Indeed I have."
