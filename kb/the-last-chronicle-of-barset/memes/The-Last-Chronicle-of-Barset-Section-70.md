[070-001]"You don't mean to tell me it's six miles!" And she tendered a florin

[070-002]to the man.

[070-003]"Can't take that, ma'am," said the cabman.

[070-004]"Can't take it! But you must take it. Broughton, just get a

[070-005]policeman, will you?" Dobbs Broughton satisfied the driver out of his

[070-006]own pocket, and the cab was driven away. "What did you give him?"

[070-007]said Mrs. Van Siever.

[070-008]"Just another sixpence. There never is a policeman anywhere about

[070-009]here."

[070-010]"It'll be out of your own pocket, then," said Mrs. Van. "But you're

[070-011]not going away?"

[070-012]"I must be at Capel Court by half-past twelve;--I must, indeed. If it

[070-013]wasn't real business, I'd stay."

[070-014]"I told Musselboro I should be here."

[070-015]"He's up there, and he knows all about the business just as well as

[070-016]I do. When I found that I couldn't stay for you, I went through the

[070-017]account with him, and it's all settled. Good morning. I'll see you at

[070-018]the West End in a day or two." Then he made his way out into Lombard

[070-019]Street, and Mrs. Van Siever picked her steps across the yard, and

[070-020]mounted the stairs, and made her way into the room in which Mr.

[070-021]Musselboro was sitting.

[070-022]"Somebody's been smoking, Gus," she said, almost as soon as she had

[070-023]entered the room.

[070-024]"That's nothing new here," he replied, as he got up from his chair.

[070-025]"There's no good being done when men sit and smoke over their work.

[070-026]Is it you, or he, or both of you?"

[070-027]"Well;--it was Broughton was smoking just now. I don't smoke of a

[070-028]morning myself."

[070-029]"What made him get up and run away when I came?"

[070-030]"How can I tell, Mrs. Van Siever," said Musselboro, laughing. "If he

[070-031]did run away when you came, I suppose it was because he didn't want

[070-032]to see you."

[070-033]"And why shouldn't he want to see me? Gus, I expect the truth from

[070-034]you. How are things going on here?" To this question Mr. Musselboro

[070-035]made no immediate answer; but tilted himself back in his chair and

[070-036]took his hat off, and put his thumbs into the arm-holes of his

[070-037]waistcoat, and looked his patroness full in the face. "Gus," she

[070-038]said again, "I do expect the truth from you. How are things going on

[070-039]here?"

[070-040]"There'd be a good business,--if he'd only keep things together."

[070-041]"But he's idle. Isn't he idle?"

[070-042]"Confoundedly idle," said Musselboro.

[070-043]"And he drinks;--don't he drink in the day?"

[070-044]"Like the mischief,--some days. But that isn't the worst of it."

[070-045]"And what is the worst of it?"

[070-046]"Newmarket;--that's the rock he's going to pieces on."

[070-047]"You don't mean to say he takes the money out of the business

[070-048]for that?" And Mrs. Van Siever's face, as she asked the question,

[070-049]expressed almost a tragic horror. "If I thought that I wouldn't give

[070-050]him an hour's mercy."

[070-051]"When a man bets he doesn't well know what money he uses. I can't say

[070-052]that he takes money that is not his own. Situated as I am, I don't

[070-053]know what is his own and what isn't. If your money was in my name I

[070-054]could keep a hand on it;--but as it is not I can do nothing. I can

[070-055]see that what is put out is put out fairly well; and when I think of

[070-056]it, Mrs. Van Siever, it is quite wonderful that we've lost so little.

[070-057]It has been next to nothing. That has been my doing;--and that's

[070-058]about all that I can do."

[070-059]"You must know whether he has used my money for his own purposes or

[070-060]not."

[070-061]"If you ask me, I think he has," said Mr. Musselboro.

[070-062]"Then I'll go into it, and I'll find it out, and if it is so, as

[070-063]sure as my name's Van Siever, I'll sew him up." Having uttered which

[070-064]terrible threat, the old woman drew a chair to the table and seated

[070-065]herself fairly down, as though she were determined to go through all

[070-066]the books of the office before she quitted that room. Mrs. Van Siever

[070-067]in her present habiliments was not a thing so terrible to look at as

[070-068]she had been in her wiggeries at Mrs. Dobbs Broughton's dinner-table.

[070-069]Her curls were laid aside altogether, and she wore simply a front

[070-070]beneath her close bonnet,--and a very old front, too, which was not

[070-071]loudly offensive because it told no lies. Her eyes were as bright,

[070-072]and her little wizen face was as sharp, as ever; but the wizen face

[070-073]and the bright eyes were not so much amiss as seen together with

[070-074]the old dark brown silk dress which she now wore, as they had been

[070-075]with the wiggeries and the evening finery. Even now, in her morning

[070-076]costume, in her work-a-day business dress, as we may call it, she

[070-077]looked to be very old,--so old that nobody could guess her age.

[070-078]People attempting to guess would say that she must be at least over

[070-079]eighty. And yet she was wiry, and strong, and nimble. It was not

[070-080]because she was feeble that she was thought to be so old. They who so

[070-081]judged of her were led to their opinion by the extreme thinness of

[070-082]her face, and by the brightness of her eyes, joined to the depth of

[070-083]the hollows in which they lay, and the red margin by which they were

[070-084]surrounded. It was not really the fact that Mrs. Van Siever was so

[070-085]very aged, for she had still some years to live before she would

[070-086]reach eighty, but that she was such a weird old woman, so small, so

[070-087]ghastly, and so ugly! "I'll sew him up, if he's been robbing me," she

[070-088]said. "I will, indeed." And she stretched out her hand to grab at the

[070-089]ledger which Musselboro had been using.

[070-090]"You won't understand anything from that," said he, pushing the book

[070-091]over to her.

[070-092]"You can explain it to me."

[070-093]"That's all straight sailing, that is."

[070-094]"And where does he keep the figures that ain't straight sailing?

[070-095]That's the book I want to see."

[070-096]"There is no such book."

[070-097]"Look here, Gus,--if I find you deceiving me I'll throw you overboard

[070-098]as sure as I'm a living woman. I will indeed. I'll have no mercy.

[070-099]I've stuck to you, and made a man of you, and I expect you to stick

[070-100]to me."

[070-101]"Not much of a man," said Musselboro, with a touch of scorn in his

[070-102]voice.

[070-103]"You've never had a shilling yet but what I gave you."

[070-104]"Yes; I have. I've had what I've worked for,--and worked confounded

[070-105]hard too."

[070-106]"Look here, Musselboro; if you're going to throw me over, just tell

[070-107]me so, and let us begin fair."

[070-108]"I'm not going to throw you over. I've always been on the square with

[070-109]you. Why don't you trust me out and out, and then I could do a deal

[070-110]better for you. You ask me now about your money. I don't know about

[070-111]your money, Mrs. Van Siever. How am I to know anything about your

[070-112]money, Mrs. Van Siever? You don't give me any power of keeping a

[070-113]hand upon Dobbs Broughton. I suppose you have security from Dobbs

[070-114]Broughton, but I don't know what security you have, Mrs. Van Siever.

[070-115]He owes you now £915 16_s._ 2_d._ on last year's account!"

[070-116]"Why doesn't he give me a cheque for the money?"

[070-117]"He says he can't spare it. You may have £500, and the rest when he

[070-118]can give it you. Or he'll give you his note-of-hand at fourteen days

[070-119]for the whole."

[070-120]"Bother his note-of-hand. Why should I take his note-of-hand?"

[070-121]"Do as you like, Mrs. Van Siever."

[070-122]"It's the interest on my own money. Why don't he give it me? I

[070-123]suppose he has had it."

[070-124]"You must ask him that, Mrs. Van Siever. You're in partnership with

[070-125]him, and he can tell you. Nobody else knows anything about it. If you

[070-126]were in partnership with me, then of course I could tell you. But

[070-127]you're not. You've never trusted me, Mrs. Van Siever."

[070-128]The lady remained there closeted with Mr. Musselboro for an hour

[070-129]after that, and did, I think, at length learn something more as to

[070-130]the details of her partner's business, than her faithful servant Mr.

[070-131]Musselboro had at first found himself able to give to her. And at

[070-132]last they came to friendly and confidential terms, in the midst

[070-133]of which the personal welfare of Mr. Dobbs Broughton was, I fear,

[070-134]somewhat forgotten. Not that Mr. Musselboro palpably and plainly

[070-135]threw his friend overboard. He took his friend's part,--alleging

[070-136]excuses for him, and pleading some facts. "Of course, you know, a man

[070-137]like that is fond of pleasure, Mrs. Van Siever. He's been at it more

[070-138]or less all his life. I don't suppose he ever missed a Derby or an

[070-139]Oaks, or the cup at Ascot, or the Goodwood in his life." "He'll have

[070-140]to miss them before long, I'm thinking," said Mrs. Van Siever. "And

[070-141]as to not cashing up, you must remember, Mrs. Van Siever, that ten

[070-142]per cent. won't come in quite as regularly as four or five. When you

[070-143]go for high interest, there must be hitches here and there. There

[070-144]must, indeed, Mrs. Van Siever." "I know all about it," said Mrs. Van

[070-145]Siever. "If he gave it me as soon as he got it himself, I shouldn't

[070-146]complain. Never mind. He's only got to give me my little bit of money

[070-147]out of the business, and then he and I will be all square. You come

[070-148]and see Clara this evening, Gus."

[070-149]Then Mr. Musselboro put Mrs. Van Siever into another cab, and

[070-150]went out upon 'Change,--hanging about the Bank, and standing in

[070-151]Threadneedle Street, talking to other men just like himself. When he

[070-152]saw Dobbs Broughton he told that gentleman that Mrs. Van Siever had

[070-153]been in her tantrums, but that he had managed to pacify her before

[070-154]she left Hook Court. "I'm to take her the cheque for the five hundred

[070-155]to-night," he said.

[070-156]CHAPTER XXXVIII.

[070-157]JAEL.

[070-158]On the first of March, Conway Dalrymple's easel was put up in Mrs.

[070-159]Dobbs Broughton's boudoir upstairs, the canvas was placed upon it

[070-160]on which the outlines of Jael and Sisera had been already drawn,

[070-161]and Mrs. Broughton and Clara Van Siever and Conway Dalrymple were

[070-162]assembled with the view of steady art-work. But before we see how

[070-163]they began their work together, we will go back for a moment to John

[070-164]Eames on his return to his London lodgings. The first thing every

[070-165]man does when he returns home after an absence, is to look at his

[070-166]letters, and John Eames looked at his. There were not very many.

[070-167]There was a note marked immediate, from Sir Raffle Buffle, in which

[070-168]Sir R. had scrawled in four lines a notification that he should be

[070-169]driven to an extremity of inconvenience if Eames were not at his post

[070-170]at half-past nine on the following morning. "I think I see myself

[070-171]there at that hour," said John. There was a notification of a house

[070-172]dinner, which he was asked to join, at his club, and a card for an

[070-173]evening gathering at Lady Glencora Palliser's,--procured for him by

[070-174]his friend Conway,--and an invitation to dinner at the house of his

[070-175]uncle, Mr. Toogood; and there was a scented note in the handwriting

[070-176]of a lady, which he did not recognize. "My nearest and dearest

[070-177]friend, M. D. M.," he said, as he opened the note and looked at the

[070-178]signature. Then he read the letter from Miss Demolines.

[070-179]MY DEAR MR. EAMES,

[070-180]Pray come to me at once. I know that you are to be back

[070-181]to-morrow. Do not lose an hour if you can help it. I shall

[070-182]be at home at half-past five. I fear what you know of has

[070-183]been begun. But it certainly shall not go on. In one way

[070-184]or another it must be prevented. I won't say another word

[070-185]till I see you, but pray come at once.
