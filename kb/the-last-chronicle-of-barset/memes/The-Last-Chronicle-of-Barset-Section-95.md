[095-001]In these latter days of which I am now speaking he would never draw

[095-002]the instrument out of its case. Indeed he was aware that it was too

[095-003]heavy for him to handle without assistance. But he would open the

[095-004]prison door, and gaze upon the thing that he loved, and he would

[095-005]pass his fingers among the broad strings, and ever and anon he would

[095-006]produce from one of them a low, melancholy, almost unearthly sound.

[095-007]And then he would pause, never daring to produce two such notes in

[095-008]succession,--one close upon the other. And these last sad moans of

[095-009]the old fiddle were now known through the household. They were the

[095-010]ghosts of the melody of days long past. He imagined that his visits

[095-011]to the box were unsuspected,--that none knew of the folly of his old

[095-012]fingers which could not keep themselves from touching the wires; but

[095-013]the voice of the violoncello had been recognized by the servants

[095-014]and by his daughter, and when that low wail was heard through the

[095-015]house,--like the last dying note of a dirge,--they would all know

[095-016]that Mr. Harding was visiting his ancient friend.

[095-017]When the dean and Mrs. Arabin had first talked of going abroad for a

[095-018]long visit, it had been understood that Mr. Harding should pass the

[095-019]period of their absence with his other daughter at Plumstead; but

[095-020]when the time came he begged of Mrs. Arabin to be allowed to remain

[095-021]in his old rooms. "Of course I shall go backwards and forwards," he

[095-022]had said. "There is nothing I like so much as a change now and then."

[095-023]The result had been that he had gone once to Plumstead during the

[095-024]dean's absence. When he had thus remonstrated, begging to be allowed

[095-025]to remain in Barchester, Mrs. Arabin had declared her intention of

[095-026]giving up her tour. In telling her father of this she had not said

[095-027]that her altered purpose had arisen from her disinclination to

[095-028]leave him alone;--but he had perceived that it was so, and had then

[095-029]consented to be taken over to Plumstead. There was nothing, he said,

[095-030]which he would like so much as going over to Plumstead for four or

[095-031]five months. It had ended in his having his own way altogether. The

[095-032]Arabins had gone upon their tour, and he was left in possession of

[095-033]the deanery. "I should not like to die out of Barchester," he said to

[095-034]himself in excuse to himself for his disinclination to sojourn long

[095-035]under the archdeacon's roof. But, in truth, the archdeacon, who loved

[095-036]him well and who, after a fashion, had always been good to him,--who

[095-037]had always spoken of the connexion which had bound the two families

[095-038]together as the great blessing of his life,--was too rough in his

[095-039]greetings for the old man. Mr. Harding had ever mixed something of

[095-040]fear with his warm affection for his elder son-in-law, and now in

[095-041]these closing hours of his life he could not avoid a certain amount

[095-042]of shrinking from that loud voice,--a certain inaptitude to be quite

[095-043]at ease in that commanding presence. The dean, his second son-in-law,

[095-044]had been a modern friend in comparison with the archdeacon; but the

[095-045]dean was more gentle with him; and then the dean's wife had ever been

[095-046]the dearest to him of human beings. It may be a doubt whether one

[095-047]of the dean's children was not now almost more dear, and whether in

[095-048]these days he did not have more free communication with that little

[095-049]girl than with any other human being. Her name was Susan, but he

[095-050]had always called her Posy, having himself invented for her that

[095-051]soubriquet. When it had been proposed to him to pass the winter and

[095-052]spring at Plumstead, the suggestion had been made alluring by a

[095-053]promise that Posy also should be taken to Mrs. Grantly's house.

[095-054]But he, as we have seen, had remained at the deanery, and Posy had

[095-055]remained with him.

[095-056]Posy was now five years old, and could talk well, and had her own

[095-057]ideas of things. Posy's eyes,--hers, and no others besides her

[095-058]own,--were allowed to see the inhabitant of the big black case; and

[095-059]now that the deanery was so nearly deserted, Posy's fingers had

[095-060]touched the strings, and had produced an infantine moan. "Grandpa,

[095-061]let me do it again." Twang! It was not, however, in truth, a twang,

[095-062]but a sound as of a prolonged dull, almost deadly, hum-m-m-m-m! On

[095-063]this occasion the moan was not entirely infantine,--Posy's fingers

[095-064]having been something too strong,--and the case was closed and

[095-065]locked, and grandpapa shook his head.

[095-066]"But Mrs. Baxter won't be angry," said Posy. Mrs. Baxter was the

[095-067]housekeeper in the deanery, and had Mr. Harding under her especial

[095-068]charge.

[095-069]"No, my darling; Mrs. Baxter will not be angry, but we mustn't

[095-070]disturb the house."

[095-071]"No," said Posy, with much of important awe in her tone; "we

[095-072]mustn't disturb the house; must we, grandpapa?" And so she gave

[095-073]in her adhesion to the closing of the case. But Posy could play

[095-074]cat's-cradle, and as cat's-cradle did not disturb the house at all,

[095-075]there was a good deal of cat's-cradle played in these days. Posy's

[095-076]fingers were so soft and pretty, so small and deft, that the dear old

[095-077]man delighted in taking the strings from them, and in having them

[095-078]taken from his own by those tender little digits.

[095-079]On the afternoon after the conversation respecting Grace Crawley

[095-080]which is recorded in the early part of this chapter, a messenger

[095-081]from Barchester went over to Plumstead, and a part of his mission

[095-082]consisted of a note from Mrs. Baxter to Mrs. Grantly, beginning,

[095-083]"Honoured Madam," and informing Mrs. Grantly, among other things,

[095-084]that her "respected papa," as Mrs. Baxter called him, was not quite

[095-085]so well as usual; not that Mrs. Baxter thought there was much the

[095-086]matter. Mr. Harding had been to the cathedral service, as was usual

[095-087]with him, but had come home leaning on a lady's arm, who had thought

[095-088]it well to stay with him at the door till it had been opened for him.

[095-089]After that "Miss Posy" had found him asleep, and had been unable,--or

[095-090]if not unable, unwilling, to wake him. "Miss Posy" had come down to

[095-091]Mrs. Baxter somewhat in a fright, and hence this letter had been

[095-092]written. Mrs. Baxter thought that there was nothing "to fright" Mrs.

[095-093]Grantly, and she wasn't sure that she should have written at all only

[095-094]that Dick was bound to go over to Plumstead with the wool; but as

[095-095]Dick was going, Mrs. Baxter thought it proper to send her duty, and

[095-096]to say that to her humble way of thinking perhaps it might be best

[095-097]that Mr. Harding shouldn't go alone to the cathedral every morning.

[095-098]"If the dear reverend gentleman was to get a tumble, ma'am," said the

[095-099]letter, "it would be awkward." Then Mrs. Grantly remembered that she

[095-100]had left her father almost without a greeting on the previous day,

[095-101]and she resolved that she would go over very early on the following

[095-102]morning,--so early that she would be at the deanery before her father

[095-103]should have gone to the cathedral.

[095-104]"He ought to have come over here, and not stayed there by himself,"

[095-105]said the archdeacon, when his wife told him of her intention.

[095-106]"It is too late to think of that now, my dear; and one can

[095-107]understand, I think, that he should not like leaving the cathedral as

[095-108]long as he can attend it. The truth is he does not like being out of

[095-109]Barchester."

[095-110]"He would be much better here," said the archdeacon. "Of course you

[095-111]can have the carriage and go over. We can breakfast at eight; and if

[095-112]you can bring him back with you, do. I should tell him that he ought

[095-113]to come." Mrs. Grantly made no answer to this, knowing very well that

[095-114]she could not bring herself to go beyond the gentlest persuasion with

[095-115]her father, and on the next morning she was at the deanery by ten

[095-116]o'clock. Half-past ten was the hour at which the service began. Mrs.

[095-117]Baxter contrived to meet her before she saw her father, and begged

[095-118]her not to let it be known that any special tidings of Mr. Harding's

[095-119]failing strength had been sent from the deanery to Plumstead. "And

[095-120]how is my father?" asked Mrs. Grantly. "Well, then, ma'am," said

[095-121]Baxter, "in one sense he's finely. He took a morsel of early lamb

[095-122]to his dinner yesterday, and relished it ever so well,--only he

[095-123]gave Miss Posy the best part of it. And then he sat with Miss Posy

[095-124]quite happy for an hour or so. And then he slept in his chair; and

[095-125]you know, ma'am, we never wakes him. And after that old Skulpit

[095-126]toddled up from the hospital,"--this was Hiram's Hospital, of which

[095-127]establishment, in the city of Barchester, Mr. Harding had once been

[095-128]the warden and kind master, as has been told in former chronicles

[095-129]of the city,--"and your papa has said, ma'am, you know, that he is

[095-130]always to see any of the old men when they come up. And Skulpit is

[095-131]sly, and no better than he should be, and got money from your father,

[095-132]ma'am, I know. And then he had just a drop of tea, and after that I

[095-133]took him his glass of port wine with my own hands. And it touched me,

[095-134]ma'am, so it did, when he said, 'Oh, Mrs. Baxter, how good you are;

[095-135]you know well what it is I like.' And then he went to bed. I listened

[095-136]hard,--not from idle cur'osity, ma'am, as you, who know me, will

[095-137]believe, but just because it's becoming to know what he's about, as

[095-138]there might be an accident, you know, ma'am." "You are very good,

[095-139]Mrs. Baxter, very good." "Thank ye, ma'am, for saying so. And so I

[095-140]listened hard; but he didn't go to his music, poor gentleman; and

[095-141]I think he had a quiet night. He doesn't sleep much at nights, poor

[095-142]gentleman, but he's very quiet; leastwise he was last night." This

[095-143]was the bulletin which Mrs. Baxter gave to Mrs. Grantly on that

[095-144]morning before Mrs. Grantly saw her father.

[095-145]She found him preparing himself for his visit to the cathedral. Some

[095-146]year or two,--but no more,--before the date of which we are speaking,

[095-147]he had still taken some small part in the service; and while he had

[095-148]done so he had of course worn his surplice. Living so close to the

[095-149]cathedral,--so close that he could almost walk out of the house into

[095-150]the transept,--he had kept his surplice in his own room, and had gone

[095-151]down in his vestment. It had been a bitter day to him when he had

[095-152]first found himself constrained to abandon the white garment which

[095-153]he loved. He had encountered some failure in the performance of the

[095-154]slight clerical task allotted to him, and the dean had tenderly

[095-155]advised him to desist. He did not utter one word of remonstrance.

[095-156]"It will perhaps be better," the dean had said. "Yes,--it will be

[095-157]better," Mr. Harding had replied. "Few have had accorded to them

[095-158]the high privilege of serving their Master in His house for so many

[095-159]years,--though few more humbly, or with lower gifts." But on the

[095-160]following morning, and for nearly a week afterwards, he had been

[095-161]unable to face the minor canon and the vergers, and the old women

[095-162]who knew him so well, in his ordinary black garments. At last he

[095-163]went down with the dean, and occupied a stall close to the dean's

[095-164]seat,--far away from that in which he had sat for so many years,--and

[095-165]in this seat he had said his prayers ever since that day. And now his

[095-166]surplices were washed and ironed and folded and put away; but there

[095-167]were moments in which he would stealthily visit them, as he also

[095-168]stealthily visited his friend in the black wooden case. This was very

[095-169]melancholy, and the sadness of it was felt by all those who lived

[095-170]with him; but he never alluded himself to any of those bereavements

[095-171]which age brought upon him. Whatever might be his regrets, he kept

[095-172]them ever within his own breast.
