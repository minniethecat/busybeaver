[088-001]"What difference does that make?"

[088-002]"A fellow wants to get a little sleep, you know."

[088-003]"Go then;--go and get your sleep. What a sleepy-headed generation it

[088-004]is." Johnny longed to ask her whether the last generation was less

[088-005]sleepy-headed, and whether the gentleman with two thousand a year

[088-006]had sat up talking all night before he pressed his foot for the last

[088-007]time on his native soil; but he did not dare. As he said to himself

[088-008]afterwards, "It would not do to bring the Bayswater romance too

[088-009]suddenly to its termination!" "But before you go," she continued,

[088-010]"I must say the word to you about that picture. Did you speak to Mr.

[088-011]Dalrymple?"

[088-012]"I did not. I have been so busy with different things that I have not

[088-013]seen him."

[088-014]"And now you are going?"

[088-015]"Well,--to tell the truth, I think I shall see him to-night, in spite

[088-016]of my being so sleepy-headed. I wrote him a line that I would look in

[088-017]and smoke a cigar with him if he chanced to be at home!"

[088-018]"And that is why you want to go. A gentleman cannot live without his

[088-019]cigar now."

[088-020]"It is especially at your bidding that I am going to see him."

[088-021]"Go, then,--and make your friend understand that if he continues this

[088-022]picture of his, he will bring himself to great trouble, and will

[088-023]probably ruin the woman for whom he professes, I presume, to feel

[088-024]something like friendship. You may tell him that Mrs. Van Siever has

[088-025]already heard of it."

[088-026]"Who told her?" demanded Johnny.

[088-027]"Never mind. You need not look at me like that. It was not I. Do you

[088-028]suppose that secrets can be kept when so many people know them? Every

[088-029]servant in Maria's house knows all about it."

[088-030]"As for that, I don't suppose Mrs. Broughton makes any great secret

[088-031]of it."

[088-032]"Do you think she has told Mr. Broughton? I am sure she has not. I

[088-033]may say I know she has not. Maria Clutterbuck is infatuated. There is

[088-034]no other excuse to be made for her."

[088-035]"Good-by," said Johnny, hurriedly.

[088-036]"And you really are going?"

[088-037]"Well,--yes. I suppose so."

[088-038]"Go then. I have nothing more to say to you."

[088-039]"I shall come and call directly I return," said Johnny.

[088-040]"You may do as you please about that, sir."

[088-041]"Do you mean that you won't be glad to see me again?"

[088-042]"I am not going to flatter you, Mr. Eames. Mamma will be well by that

[088-043]time, I hope, and I do not mind telling you that you are a favourite

[088-044]with her." Johnny thought that this was particularly kind, as he had

[088-045]seen so very little of the old lady. "If you choose to call upon

[088-046]her," said Madalina, "of course she will be glad to see you."

[088-047]"But I was speaking of yourself, you know?" and Johnny permitted

[088-048]himself for a moment to look tenderly at her.

[088-049]"Then from myself pray understand that I will say nothing to flatter

[088-050]your self-love."

[088-051]"I thought you would be kinder just when I was going away."

[088-052]"I think I have been quite kind enough. As you observed yourself just

[088-053]now, it is nearly eleven o'clock, and I must ask you to go away. Bon

[088-054]voyage, and a happy return to you."

[088-055]"And you will be glad to see me when I am back? Tell me that you will

[088-056]be glad to see me."

[088-057]"I will tell you nothing of the kind. Mr. Eames, if you do, I will be

[088-058]very angry with you." And then he went.

[088-059]On his way back to his own lodgings he did call on Conway Dalrymple,

[088-060]and in spite of his need for early rising, sat smoking with the

[088-061]artist for an hour. "If you don't take care, young man," said his

[088-062]friend, "you will find yourself in a scrape with your Madalina."

[088-063]"What sort of a scrape?"

[088-064]"As you walk away from Porchester Terrace some fine day, you will

[088-065]have to congratulate yourself on having made a successful overture

[088-066]towards matrimony."

[088-067]"You don't think I am such a fool as that comes to?"

[088-068]"Other men as wise as you have done the same sort of thing. Miss

[088-069]Demolines is very clever, and I daresay you find it amusing."

[088-070]"It isn't so much that she's clever, and I can hardly say that it is

[088-071]amusing. One gets awfully tired of it, you know. But a fellow must

[088-072]have something to do, and that is as good as anything else."

[088-073]"I suppose you have not heard that one young man levanted last year

[088-074]to save himself from a breach of promise case?"

[088-075]"I wonder whether he had any money in Indian securities?"

[088-076]"What makes you ask that?"

[088-077]"Nothing particular."

[088-078]"Whatever little he had he chose to save, and I think I heard that he

[088-079]went to Canada. His name was Shorter; and they say that, on the eve

[088-080]of his going, Madalina sent him word that she had no objection to the

[088-081]colonies, and that, under the pressing emergency of his expatriation,

[088-082]she was willing to become Mrs. Shorter with more expedition than

[088-083]usually attends fashionable weddings. Shorter, however, escaped, and

[088-084]has never been seen back again."

[088-085]Eames declared that he did not believe a word of it. Nevertheless, as

[088-086]he walked home he came to the conclusion that Mr. Shorter must have

[088-087]been the handsome gentleman with Indian securities, to whom "no" had

[088-088]been said once too often.

[088-089]While sitting with Conway Dalrymple, he had forgotten to say a word

[088-090]about Jael and Sisera.

[088-091]CHAPTER XLVII.

[088-092]DR. TEMPEST AT THE PALACE.

[088-093][Illustration]

[088-094]Intimation had been sent from the palace to Dr. Tempest of

[088-095]Silverbridge of the bishop's intention that a commission should

[088-096]be held by him, as rural dean, with other neighbouring clergymen,

[088-097]as assessors with him, that inquiry might be made on the part of

[088-098]the Church into the question of Mr. Crawley's guilt. It must be

[088-099]understood that by this time the opinion had become very general that

[088-100]Mr. Crawley had been guilty,--that he had found the cheque in his

[088-101]house, and that he had, after holding it for many months, succumbed

[088-102]to temptation, and applied it to his own purposes. But various

[088-103]excuses were made for him by those who so believed. In the first

[088-104]place it was felt by all who really knew anything of the man's

[088-105]character, that the very fact of his committing such a crime proved

[088-106]him to be hardly responsible for his actions. He must have known, had

[088-107]not all judgment in such matters been taken from him, that the cheque

[088-108]would certainly be traced back to his hands. No attempt had been made

[088-109]in the disposing of it to dispose of it in such a way that the trace

[088-110]should be obliterated. He had simply given it to a neighbour with a

[088-111]direction to have it cashed, and had written his own name on the back

[088-112]of it. And therefore, though there could be no doubt as to the theft

[088-113]in the mind of those who supposed that he had found the cheque in his

[088-114]own house, yet the guilt of the theft seemed to be almost annihilated

[088-115]by the folly of the thief. And then his poverty, and his struggles,

[088-116]and the sufferings of his wife, were remembered; and stories were

[088-117]told from mouth to mouth of his industry in his profession, of

[088-118]his great zeal among those brickmakers of Hoggle End, of acts of

[088-119]charity done by him which startled the people of the district into

[088-120]admiration;--how he had worked with his own hands for the sick poor

[088-121]to whom he could not give relief in money, turning a woman's mangle

[088-122]for a couple of hours, and carrying a boy's load along the lanes. Dr.

[088-123]Tempest and others declared that he had derogated from the dignity

[088-124]of his position as an English parish clergyman by such acts; but,

[088-125]nevertheless, the stories of these deeds acted strongly on the minds

[088-126]of both men and women, creating an admiration for Mr. Crawley which

[088-127]was much stronger than the condemnation of his guilt.

[088-128]Even Mrs. Walker and her daughter, and the Miss Prettymans, had so

[088-129]far given way that they had ceased to asseverate their belief in

[088-130]Mr. Crawley's innocence. They contented themselves now with simply

[088-131]expressing a hope that he would be acquitted by a jury, and that when

[088-132]he should be so acquitted the thing might be allowed to rest. If he

[088-133]had sinned, no doubt he had repented. And then there were serious

[088-134]debates whether he might not have stolen the money without much sin,

[088-135]being mad or half-mad,--touched with madness when he took it; and

[088-136]whether he might not, in spite of such temporary touch of madness,

[088-137]be well fitted for his parish duties. Sorrow had afflicted him

[088-138]grievously; but that sorrow, though it had incapacitated him for the

[088-139]management of his own affairs, had not rendered him unfit for the

[088-140]ministrations of his parish. Such were the arguments now used in

[088-141]his favour by the women around him; and the men were not keen to

[088-142]contradict them. The wish that he should be acquitted and allowed to

[088-143]remain in his parsonage was very general.

[088-144]When therefore it became known that the bishop had decided to put on

[088-145]foot another investigation, with the view of bringing Mr. Crawley's

[088-146]conduct under ecclesiastical condemnation, almost everybody accused

[088-147]the bishop of persecution. The world of the diocese declared that

[088-148]Mrs. Proudie was at work, and that the bishop himself was no better

[088-149]than a puppet. It was in vain that certain clear-headed men among the

[088-150]clergy, of whom Dr. Tempest himself was one, pointed out that the

[088-151]bishop after all might perhaps be right;--that if Mr. Crawley were

[088-152]guilty, and if he should be found to have been so by a jury, it might

[088-153]be absolutely necessary that an ecclesiastical court should take some

[088-154]cognizance of the crime beyond that taken by the civil law. "The

[088-155]jury," said Dr. Tempest, discussing the case with Mr. Robarts and

[088-156]other clerical neighbours,--"the jury may probably find him guilty

[088-157]and recommend him to mercy. The judge will have heard his character,

[088-158]and will have been made acquainted with his manner of life, and will

[088-159]deal as lightly with the case as the law will allow him. For aught

[088-160]I know he may be imprisoned for a month. I wish it might be for

[088-161]no more than a day,--or an hour. But when he comes out from his

[088-162]month's imprisonment,--how then? Surely it should be a case for

[088-163]ecclesiastical inquiry, whether a clergyman who has committed a theft

[088-164]should be allowed to go into his pulpit directly he comes out of

[088-165]prison?" But the answer to this was that Mr. Crawley always had been

[088-166]a good clergyman, was a good clergyman at this moment, and would be a

[088-167]good clergyman when he did come out of prison.

[088-168]But Dr. Tempest, though he had argued in this way, was by no means

[088-169]eager for the commencement of the commission over which he was to

[088-170]be called upon to preside. In spite of such arguments as the above,

[088-171]which came from the man's head when his head was brought to bear

[088-172]upon the matter, there was a thorough desire within his heart to

[088-173]oppose the bishop. He had no strong sympathy with Mr. Crawley, as

[088-174]had others. He would have had Mr. Crawley silenced without regret,

[088-175]presuming Mr. Crawley to have been guilty. But he had a much stronger

[088-176]feeling with regard to the bishop. Had there been any question of

[088-177]silencing the bishop,--could it have been possible to take any steps

[088-178]in that direction,--he would have been very active. It may therefore

[088-179]be understood that in spite of his defence of the bishop's present

[088-180]proceedings as to the commission, he was anxious that the bishop

[088-181]should fail, and anxious to put impediments in the bishop's way,

[088-182]should it appear to him that he could do so with justice. Dr. Tempest

[088-183]was well known among his parishioners to be hard and unsympathetic,

[088-184]some said unfeeling also, and cruel; but it was admitted by those who

[088-185]disliked him the most that he was both practical and just, and that

[088-186]he cared for the welfare of many, though he was rarely touched by the

[088-187]misery of one. Such was the man who was rector of Silverbridge and

[088-188]rural dean in the district, and who was now called upon by the bishop

[088-189]to assist him in making further inquiry as to this wretched cheque

[088-190]for twenty pounds.
