[037-001]Mr. Robarts knew that Mr. Winthrop was a fool, and that he could get

[037-002]no useful advice from him. So he suggested that he would take his gig

[037-003]down to the inn, and call back again before long. "You'll find that

[037-004]Walker knows no more than I do about it," said Mr. Winthrop, "but

[037-005]of course he'll be glad to see you if he happens to come in." So Mr.

[037-006]Robarts went to the inn, put up his horse, and then, as he sauntered

[037-007]back up the street, met Mr. Walker coming out of the private door of

[037-008]his house.

[037-009]"I've been at home all the morning," he said, "but I've had a stiff

[037-010]job of work on hand, and told them to say in the office that I was

[037-011]not in. Seen Winthrop, have you? I don't suppose he did know that

[037-012]I was here. The clerks often know more than the partners. [[About]] Mr.

[037-013]Crawley is it? Come into my dining-room, Mr. Robarts, where we shall

[037-014]be alone. Yes;--it is a bad case; a very bad case. The pity is that

[037-015]anybody should ever have said anything about it. Lord bless me, if

[037-016]I'd been Soames I'd have let him have the twenty pounds. Lord Lufton

[037-017]would never have allowed Soames to lose it."

[037-018]"But Soames wanted to find out the truth."

[037-019]"Yes;--that was just it. Soames couldn't bear to think that he should

[037-020]be left in the dark, and then, when the poor man said that Soames had

[037-021]paid the cheque to him in the way of business,--it was not odd that

[037-022]Soames' back should have been up, was it? But, Mr. Robarts, I should

[037-023]have thought a deal about it before I should have brought such a man

[037-024]as Mr. Crawley before a bench of magistrates on that charge."

[037-025]"But between you and me, Mr. Walker, did he steal the money?"

[037-026]"Well, Mr. Robarts, you know how I'm placed."

[037-027]"Mr. Crawley is my friend, and of course I want to assist him. I was

[037-028]under a great obligation to Mr. Crawley once, and I wish to befriend

[037-029]him, whether he took the money or not. But I could act so much better

[037-030]if I felt sure one way or the other."

[037-031]"If you ask me, I think he did take it."

[037-032]"What!--stole it?"

[037-033]"I think he knew it was not his own when he took it. You see I don't

[037-034]think he meant to use it when he took it. He perhaps had some queer

[037-035]idea that Soames had been hard on him, or his lordship, and that the

[037-036]money was fairly his due. Then he kept the cheque by him till he was

[037-037]absolutely badgered out of his life by the butcher up the street

[037-038]there. That was about the long and the short of it, Mr. Robarts."

[037-039]"I suppose so. And now what had he better do?"

[037-040]"Well; if you ask me,-- He is in very bad health, isn't he?"

[037-041]"No; I should say not. He walked to Barchester and back the other

[037-042]day."

[037-043]"Did he? But he's very queer, isn't he?"

[037-044]"Very odd-mannered indeed."

[037-045]"And does and says all manner of odd things?"

[037-046]"I think you'd find the bishop would say so after that interview."

[037-047]"Well; if it would do any good, you might have the bishop examined."

[037-048]"Examined for what, Mr. Walker?"

[037-049]"If you could show, you know, that Crawley has got a bee in his

[037-050]bonnet; that the mens sana is not there, in short;--I think you might

[037-051]manage to have the trial postponed."

[037-052]"But then somebody must take charge of his living."

[037-053]"You parsons could manage that among you;--you and the dean and the

[037-054]archdeacon. The archdeacon has always got half-a-dozen curates about

[037-055]somewhere. And then,--after the assizes, Mr. Crawley might come to

[037-056]his senses; and I think,--mind it's only an idea,--but I think the

[037-057]committal might be quashed. It would have been temporary insanity,

[037-058]and, though mind I don't give my word for it, I think he might go on

[037-059]and keep his living. I think so, Mr. Robarts."

[037-060]"That has never occurred to me."

[037-061]"No;--I daresay not. You see the difficulty is this. He's so

[037-062]stiff-necked,--will do nothing himself. Well, that will do for one

[037-063]proof of temporary insanity. The real truth is, Mr. Robarts, he is as

[037-064]mad as a hatter."

[037-065]"Upon my word I've often thought so."

[037-066]"And you wouldn't mind saying so in evidence,--would you? Well, you

[037-067]see, there is no helping such a man in any other way. He won't even

[037-068]employ a lawyer to defend him."

[037-069]"That was what I had come to you about."

[037-070]"I'm told he won't. Now a man must be mad who won't employ a lawyer

[037-071]when he wants one. You see, the point we should gain would be

[037-072]this,--if we tried to get him through as being a little touched in

[037-073]the upper story,--whatever we could do for him, we could do against

[037-074]his own will. The more he opposed us the stronger our case would be.

[037-075]He would swear he was not mad at all, and we should say that that was

[037-076]the greatest sign of his madness. But when I say we, of course I mean

[037-077]you. I must not appear in it."

[037-078]"I wish you could, Mr. Walker."

[037-079]"Of course I can't; but that won't make any difference."

[037-080]"I suppose he must have a lawyer?"

[037-081]"Yes, he must have a lawyer;--or rather his friends must."

[037-082]"And who should employ him, ostensibly?"

[037-083]"Ah;--there's the difficulty. His wife wouldn't do it, I suppose? She

[037-084]couldn't do him a better turn."

[037-085]"He would never forgive her. And she would never consent to act

[037-086]against him."

[037-087]"Could you interfere?"

[037-088]"If necessary, I will;--but I hardly know him well enough."

[037-089]"Has he no father or mother, or uncles or aunts? He must have

[037-090]somebody belonging to him," said Mr. Walker.

[037-091]Then it occurred to Mr. Robarts that Dean Arabin would be the proper

[037-092]person to interfere. Dean Arabin and Mr. Crawley had been intimate

[037-093]friends in early life, and Dean Arabin knew more of him than did any

[037-094]man, at least in those parts. All this Mr. Robarts explained to Mr.

[037-095]Walker, and Mr. Walker agreed with him that the services of Dean

[037-096]Arabin should if possible be obtained. Mr. Robarts would at once

[037-097]write to Dean Arabin and explain at length all the circumstances of

[037-098]the case. "The worst of it is, he will hardly be home in time," said

[037-099]Mr. Walker. "Perhaps he would come a little sooner if you were to

[037-100]press it?"

[037-101]"But we could act in his name in his absence, I suppose?--of course

[037-102]with his authority?"

[037-103]"I wish he could be here a month before the assizes, Mr. Robarts. It

[037-104]would be better."

[037-105]"And in the meantime shall I say anything to Mr. Crawley, myself,

[037-106]about employing a lawyer?"

[037-107]"I think I would. If he turns upon you, as like enough he may, and

[037-108]abuses you, that will help us in one way. If he should consent, and

[037-109]perhaps he may, that would help us in the other way. I'm told he's

[037-110]been over and upset the whole coach at the palace."

[037-111]"I shouldn't think the bishop got much out of him," said the parson.

[037-112]"I don't like Crawley the less for speaking his mind free to the

[037-113]bishop," said the attorney, laughing. "And he'll speak it free to you

[037-114]too, Mr. Robarts."

[037-115]"He won't break any of my bones. Tell me, Mr. Walker, what lawyer

[037-116]shall I name to him?"

[037-117]"You can't have a better man than Mr. Mason, up the street there."

[037-118]"Winthrop proposed Borleys at Barchester."

[037-119]"No, no, no. Borleys and Bonstock are capital people to push a fellow

[037-120]through on a charge of horse-stealing, or to squeeze a man for a

[037-121]little money; but they are not the people for Mr. Crawley in such a

[037-122]case as this. Mason is a better man; and then Mason and I know each

[037-123]other." In saying which Mr. Walker winked.

[037-124]There was then a discussion between them whether Mr. Robarts should

[037-125]go at once to Mr. Mason; but it was decided at last that he should

[037-126]see Mr. Crawley and also write to the dean before he did so. The dean

[037-127]might wish to employ his own lawyer, and if so the double expense

[037-128]should be avoided. "Always remember, Mr. Robarts, that when you go

[037-129]into an attorney's office door, you will have to pay for it, first or

[037-130]last. In here, you see, the dingy old mahogany, bare as it is, makes

[037-131]you safe. Or else it's the salt-cellar, which will not allow itself

[037-132]to be polluted by six-and-eightpenny considerations. But there is the

[037-133]other kind of tax to be paid. You must go up and see Mrs. Walker, or

[037-134]you won't have her help in this matter."

[037-135]Mr. Walker returned to his work, either to some private den within

[037-136]his house, or to his office, and Mr. Robarts was taken upstairs to

[037-137]the drawing-room. There he found Mrs. Walker and her daughter, and

[037-138]Miss Anne Prettyman, who had just looked in, full of the story of

[037-139]Mr. Crawley's walk to Barchester. Mr. Thumble had seen one of Dr.

[037-140]Tempest's curates, and had told the whole story--he, Mr. Thumble,

[037-141]having heard Mrs. Proudie's version of what had occurred, and having,

[037-142]of course, drawn his own deductions from her premises. And it seemed

[037-143]that Mr. Crawley had been watched as he passed through the close

[037-144]out of Barchester. A minor canon had seen him, and had declared

[037-145]that he was going at the rate of a hunt, swinging his arms on high

[037-146]and speaking very loud, though,--as the minor canon said with

[037-147]regret,--the words were hardly audible. But there had been no doubt

[037-148]as to the man. Mr. Crawley's old hat, and short rusty cloak, and

[037-149]dirty boots, had been duly observed and chronicled by the minor

[037-150]canon; and Mr. Thumble had been enabled to put together a not

[037-151]altogether false picture of what had occurred. As soon as the

[037-152]greetings between Mr. Robarts and the ladies had been made, Miss

[037-153]Anne Prettyman broke out again, just where she had left off when Mr.

[037-154]Robarts came in. "They say that Mrs. Proudie declared that she will

[037-155]have him sent to Botany Bay!"

[037-156]"Luckily Mrs. Proudie won't have much to do in the matter," said Miss

[037-157]Walker, who ranged herself, as to church matters, in ranks altogether

[037-158]opposed to those commanded by Mrs. Proudie.

[037-159]"She will have nothing to do with it, my dear," said Mrs. Walker;

[037-160]"and I daresay Mrs. Proudie was not foolish enough to say anything of

[037-161]the kind."

[037-162]"Mamma, she would be fool enough to say anything. Would she not, Mr.

[037-163]Robarts?"

[037-164]"You forget, Miss Walker, that Mrs. Proudie is in authority over me."

[037-165]"So she is, for the matter of that," said the young lady; "but I know

[037-166]very well what you all think of her, and say of her too, at Framley.

[037-167]Your friend, Lady Lufton, loves her dearly. I wish I could have been

[037-168]hidden behind a curtain in the palace, to hear what Mr. Crawley said

[037-169]to her."

[037-170]"Mr. Smillie declares," said Miss Anne Prettyman, "that the bishop

[037-171]has been ill ever since. Mr. Smillie went over to his mother's at

[037-172]Barchester for Christmas, and took part of the cathedral duty, and

[037-173]we had Mr. Spooner over here in his place. So Mr. Smillie of course

[037-174]heard all about it. Only fancy, poor Mr. Crawley walking all the way

[037-175]from Hogglestock to Barchester and back;--and I am told he hardly had

[037-176]a shoe to his foot! Is it not a shame, Mr. Robarts?"

[037-177]"I don't think it was quite so bad as you say, Miss Prettyman; but,

[037-178]upon the whole, I do think it is a shame. But what can we do?"

[037-179]"I suppose there are tithes at Hogglestock. Why are they not given up

[037-180]to the church, as they ought to be?"

[037-181]"My dear Miss Prettyman, that is a very large subject, and I am

[037-182]afraid it cannot be settled in time to relieve our poor friend

[037-183]from his distress." Then Mr. Robarts escaped from the ladies in

[037-184]Mr. Walker's house, who, as it seemed to him, were touching upon

[037-185]dangerous ground, and went back to the yard of the George Inn for

[037-186]his gig,--the George and Vulture it was properly called, and was

[037-187]the house in which the magistrates had sat when they committed Mr.

[037-188]Crawley for trial.
