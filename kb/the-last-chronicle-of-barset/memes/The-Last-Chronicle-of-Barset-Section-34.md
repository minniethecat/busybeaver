[034-001]Then the bishop rose from his chair to speak, intending to take up

[034-002]a position on the rug. But as he did so Mr. Crawley, who had seated

[034-003]himself on an intimation that he was expected to sit down, rose also,

[034-004]and the bishop found that he would thus lose his expected vantage.

[034-005]"Will you not be seated, Mr. Crawley?" said the bishop. Mr. Crawley

[034-006]smiled, but stood his ground. Then the bishop returned to his

[034-007]arm-chair, and Mr. Crawley also sat down again. "Mr. Crawley,"

[034-008]began the bishop, "this matter which came the other day before the

[034-009]magistrates at Silverbridge has been a most unfortunate affair. It

[034-010]has given me, I can assure you, the most sincere pain."

[034-011]Mr. Crawley had made up his mind how far the bishop should be allowed

[034-012]to go without a rebuke. He had told himself that it would only be

[034-013]natural, and would not be unbecoming, that the bishop should allude

[034-014]to the meeting of the magistrates and to the alleged theft, and that

[034-015]therefore such allusion should be endured with patient humility.

[034-016]And, moreover, the more rope he gave the bishop, the more likely the

[034-017]bishop would be to entangle himself. It certainly was Mr. Crawley's

[034-018]wish that the bishop should entangle himself. He, therefore, replied

[034-019]very meekly, "It has been most unfortunate, my lord."

[034-020]"I have felt for Mrs. Crawley very deeply," said Mrs. Proudie. Mr.

[034-021]Crawley had now made up his mind that as long as it was possible he

[034-022]would ignore the presence of Mrs. Proudie altogether; and, therefore,

[034-023]he made no sign that he had heard the latter remark.

[034-024]"It has been most unfortunate," continued the bishop. "I have never

[034-025]before had a clergyman in my diocese placed in so distressing a

[034-026]position."

[034-027]"That is a matter of opinion, my lord," said Mr. Crawley, who at that

[034-028]moment thought of a crisis which had come in the life of another

[034-029]clergyman in the diocese of Barchester, with the circumstances of

[034-030]which he had by chance been made acquainted.

[034-031]"Exactly," said the bishop. "And I am expressing my opinion." Mr.

[034-032]Crawley, who understood fighting, did not think that the time had

[034-033]yet come for striking a blow, so he simply bowed again. "A most

[034-034]unfortunate position, Mr. Crawley," continued the bishop. "Far be it

[034-035]from me to express an opinion upon the matter, which will have to

[034-036]come before a jury of your countrymen. It is enough for me to know

[034-037]that the magistrates assembled at Silverbridge, gentlemen to whom no

[034-038]doubt you must be known, as most of them live in your neighbourhood,

[034-039]have heard evidence upon the subject--"

[034-040]"Most convincing evidence," said Mrs. Proudie, interrupting her

[034-041]husband. Mr. Crawley's black brow became a little blacker as he heard

[034-042]the word, but still he ignored the woman. He not only did not speak,

[034-043]but did not turn his eye upon her.

[034-044]"They have heard the evidence on the subject," continued the bishop,

[034-045]"and they have thought it proper to refer the decision as to your

[034-046]innocence or your guilt to a jury of your countrymen."

[034-047]"And they were right," said Mr. Crawley.

[034-048]"Very possibly. I don't deny it. Probably," said the bishop, whose

[034-049]eloquence was somewhat disturbed by Mr. Crawley's ready acquiescence.

[034-050]"Of course they were right," said Mrs. Proudie.

[034-051]"At any rate it is so," said the bishop. "You are in the position of

[034-052]a man amenable to the criminal laws of the land."

[034-053]"There are no criminal laws, my lord," said Mr. Crawley; "but to such

[034-054]laws as there are we are all amenable,--your lordship and I alike."

[034-055]"But you are so in a very particular way. I do not wish to remind

[034-056]you what might be your condition now, but for the interposition of

[034-057]private friends."

[034-058]"I should be in the condition of a man not guilty before the

[034-059]law;--guiltless, as far as the law goes,--but kept in durance, not

[034-060]for faults of his own, but because otherwise, by reason of laches in

[034-061]the police, his presence at the assizes might not be ensured. In such

[034-062]a position a man's reputation is made to hang for awhile on the trust

[034-063]which some friends or neighbours may have in it. I do not say that

[034-064]the test is a good one."

[034-065]"You would have been put in prison, Mr. Crawley, because the

[034-066]magistrates were of opinion that you had taken Mr. Soames's cheque,"

[034-067]said Mrs. Proudie. On this occasion he did look at her. He turned one

[034-068]glance upon her from under his eyebrows, but he did not speak.

[034-069]"With all that I have nothing to do," said the bishop.

[034-070]"Nothing whatever, my lord," said Mr. Crawley.

[034-071]"But, bishop, I think that you have," said Mrs. Proudie. "The

[034-072]judgment formed by the magistrates as to the conduct of one of your

[034-073]clergymen makes it imperative upon you to act in the matter."

[034-074]"Yes, my dear, yes; I am coming to that. What Mrs. Proudie says is

[034-075]perfectly true. I have been constrained most unwillingly to take

[034-076]action in this matter. It is undoubtedly the fact that you must at

[034-077]the next assizes surrender yourself at the court-house yonder, to be

[034-078]tried for this offence against the laws."

[034-079]"That is true. If I be alive, my lord, and have strength sufficient,

[034-080]I shall be there."

[034-081]"You must be there," said Mrs. Proudie. "The police will look to

[034-082]that, Mr. Crawley." She was becoming very angry in that the man would

[034-083]not answer her a word. On this occasion again he did not even look at

[034-084]her.

[034-085]"Yes; you will be there," said the bishop. "Now that is, to say the

[034-086]least of it, an unseemly position for a beneficed clergyman."

[034-087]"You said before, my lord, that it was an unfortunate position, and

[034-088]the word, methinks, was better chosen."

[034-089]"It is very unseemly, very unseemly indeed," said Mrs. Proudie;

[034-090]"nothing could possibly be more unseemly. The bishop might very

[034-091]properly have used a much stronger word."

[034-092]"Under these circumstances," continued the bishop, "looking to the

[034-093]welfare of your parish, to the welfare of the diocese, and allow me

[034-094]to say, Mr. Crawley, to the welfare of yourself also--"

[034-095]"And especially to the souls of the people," said Mrs. Proudie.

[034-096]The bishop shook his head. It is hard to be impressively eloquent

[034-097]when one is interrupted at every best turned period, even by a

[034-098]supporting voice. "Yes;--and looking of course to the religious

[034-099]interests of your people, Mr. Crawley, I came to the conclusion

[034-100]that it would be expedient that you should cease your ministrations

[034-101]for awhile." The bishop paused, and Mr. Crawley bowed his head.

[034-102]"I, therefore, sent over to you a gentleman with whom I am well

[034-103]acquainted, Mr. Thumble, with a letter from myself, in which I

[034-104]endeavoured to impress upon you, without the use of any severe

[034-105]language, what my convictions were."

[034-106]"Severe words are often the best mercy," said Mrs. Proudie. Mr.

[034-107]Crawley had raised his hand, with his finger out, preparatory to

[034-108]answering the bishop. But as Mrs. Proudie had spoken he dropped his

[034-109]finger and was silent.

[034-110]"Mr. Thumble brought me back your written reply," continued the

[034-111]bishop, "by which I was grieved to find that you were not willing to

[034-112]submit yourself to my counsel in the matter."

[034-113]"I was most unwilling, my lord. Submission to authority is at times a

[034-114]duty;--and at times opposition to authority is a duty also."

[034-115]"Opposition to just authority cannot be a duty, Mr. Crawley."

[034-116]"Opposition to usurped authority is an imperative duty," said Mr.

[034-117]Crawley.

[034-118]"And who is to be the judge?" demanded Mrs. Proudie. Then there was

[034-119]silence for a while; when, as Mr. Crawley made no reply, the lady

[034-120]repeated her question. "Will you be pleased to answer my question,

[034-121]sir? Who, in such a case, is to be the judge?" But Mr. Crawley did

[034-122]not please to answer her question. "The man is obstinate," said Mrs.

[034-123]Proudie.

[034-124]"I had better proceed," said the bishop. "Mr. Thumble brought me back

[034-125]your reply, which grieved me greatly."

[034-126]"It was contumacious and indecent," said Mrs. Proudie.

[034-127]The bishop again shook his head and looked so unutterably miserable

[034-128]that a smile came across Mr. Crawley's face. After all, others

[034-129]besides himself had their troubles and trials. Mrs. Proudie saw

[034-130]and understood the smile, and became more angry than ever. She drew

[034-131]her chair close to the table, and began to fidget with her fingers

[034-132]among the papers. She had never before encountered a clergyman so

[034-133]contumacious, so indecent, so unreverend,--so upsetting. She had had

[034-134]to do with men difficult to manage;--the archdeacon for instance;

[034-135]but the archdeacon had never been so impertinent to her as this man.

[034-136]She had quarrelled once openly with a chaplain of her husband's, a

[034-137]clergyman whom she herself had introduced to her husband, and who had

[034-138]treated her very badly;--but not so badly, not with such unscrupulous

[034-139]violence, as she was now encountering from this ill-clothed beggarly

[034-140]man, this perpetual curate, with his dirty broken boots, this already

[034-141]half-convicted thief! Such was her idea of Mr. Crawley's conduct to

[034-142]her, while she was fingering the papers,--simply because Mr. Crawley

[034-143]would not speak to her.

[034-144]"I forget where I was," said the bishop. "Oh. Mr. Thumble came back,

[034-145]and I received your letter;--of course I received it. And I was

[034-146]surprised to learn from that, that in spite of what had occurred at

[034-147]Silverbridge, you were still anxious to continue the usual Sunday

[034-148]ministrations in your church."

[034-149]"I was determined that I would do my duty at Hogglestock, as long as

[034-150]I might be left there to do it," said Mr. Crawley.

[034-151]"Duty!" said Mrs. Proudie.

[034-152]"Just a moment, my dear," said the bishop. "When Sunday came, I had

[034-153]no alternative but to send Mr. Thumble over again to Hogglestock. It

[034-154]occurred to us,--to me and Mrs. Proudie,--"

[034-155]"I will tell Mr. Crawley just now what has occurred to me," said Mrs.

[034-156]Proudie.

[034-157]"Yes;--just so. And I am sure that he will take it in good part. It

[034-158]occurred to me, Mr. Crawley, that your first letter might have been

[034-159]written in haste."

[034-160]"It was written in haste, my lord; your messenger was waiting."

[034-161]"Yes;--just so. Well; so I sent him again, hoping that he might be

[034-162]accepted as a messenger of peace. It was a most disagreeable mission

[034-163]for any gentleman, Mr. Crawley."

[034-164]"Most disagreeable, my lord."

[034-165]"And you refused him permission to obey the instructions which I had

[034-166]given him! You would not let him read from your desk, or preach from

[034-167]your pulpit."

[034-168]"Had I been Mr. Thumble," said Mrs. Proudie, "I would have read from

[034-169]that desk and I would have preached from that pulpit."

[034-170]Mr. Crawley waited a moment, thinking that the bishop might perhaps

[034-171]speak again; but as he did not, but sat expectant as though he had

[034-172]finished his discourse, and now expected a reply, Mr. Crawley got up

[034-173]from his seat and drew near to the table. "My lord," he began, "it

[034-174]has all been just as you have said. I did answer your first letter in

[034-175]haste."

[034-176]"The more shame for you," said Mrs. Proudie.

[034-177]"And therefore, for aught I know, my letter to your lordship may be

[034-178]so worded as to need some apology."

[034-179]"Of course it needs an apology," said Mrs. Proudie.

[034-180]"But for the matter of it, my lord, no apology can be made, nor is

[034-181]any needed. I did refuse to your messenger permission to perform the

[034-182]services of my church, and if you send twenty more, I shall refuse

[034-183]them all,--till the time may come when it will be your lordship's

[034-184]duty, in accordance with the laws of the Church,--as borne out and

[034-185]backed by the laws of the land, to provide during my constrained

[034-186]absence for the spiritual wants of those poor people at Hogglestock."

[034-187]"Poor people, indeed," said Mrs. Proudie. "Poor wretches!"

[034-188]"And, my lord, it may well be, that it shall soon be your lordship's

[034-189]duty to take due and legal steps for depriving me of my benefice at

[034-190]Hogglestock;--nay, probably, for silencing me altogether as to the

[034-191]exercise of my sacred profession!"
