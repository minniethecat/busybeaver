[107-001]"But Mr. Crawley has not shown himself to be unfit," said Robarts.

[107-002]"That is begging the question, Robarts," said the doctor.

[107-003]"Just so," said Mr. Thumble. Then Mr. Robarts gave a look at Mr.

[107-004]Thumble, and Mr. Thumble retired into his shoes.

[107-005]"That is the question as to which we are called upon to advise the

[107-006]bishop," continued Dr. Tempest. "And I must say that I think the

[107-007]bishop is right. If he were to allow the matter to pass by without

[107-008]notice,--that is to say, in the event of Mr. Crawley being pronounced

[107-009]to be guilty by a jury,--he would, I think, neglect his duty. Now,

[107-010]I have been informed that the bishop has recommended Mr. Crawley to

[107-011]desist from his duties till the trial be over, and that Mr. Crawley

[107-012]has declined to take the bishop's advice."

[107-013]"That is true," said Mr. Thumble. "He altogether disregarded the

[107-014]bishop."

[107-015]"I cannot say that I think he was wrong," said Dr. Tempest.

[107-016]"I think he was quite right," said Mr. Robarts.

[107-017]"A bishop in almost all cases is entitled to the obedience of his

[107-018]clergy," said Mr. Oriel.

[107-019]"I must say that I agree with you, sir," said Mr. Thumble.

[107-020]"The income is not large, and I suppose that it would have gone with

[107-021]the duties," said Mr. Quiverful. "It is very hard for a man with a

[107-022]family to live when his income has been stopped."

[107-023]"Be that as it may," continued the doctor, "the bishop feels that it

[107-024]may be his duty to oppose the return of Mr. Crawley to his pulpit,

[107-025]and that he can oppose it in no other way than by proceeding against

[107-026]Mr. Crawley under the Clerical Offences Act. I propose, therefore,

[107-027]that we should invite Mr. Crawley to attend here--"

[107-028]"Mr. Crawley is not coming here to-day, then?" said Mr. Robarts.

[107-029]"I thought it useless to ask for his attendance until we had settled

[107-030]on our course of action," said Dr. Tempest. "If we are all agreed,

[107-031]I will beg him to come here on this day week, when we will meet

[107-032]again. And we will then ask him whether he will submit himself to the

[107-033]bishop's decision, in the event of the jury finding him guilty. If

[107-034]he should decline to do so, we can only then form our opinion as to

[107-035]what will be the bishop's duty by reference to the facts as they are

[107-036]elicited at the trial. If Mr. Crawley should choose to make to us

[107-037]any statement as to his own case, of course we shall be willing to

[107-038]receive it. That is my idea of what had better be done; and now, if

[107-039]any gentleman has any other proposition to make, of course we shall

[107-040]be pleased to hear him." Dr. Tempest, as he said this, looked round

[107-041]upon his companions, as though his pleasure, under the circumstances

[107-042]suggested by himself, would be very doubtful.

[107-043]"I don't suppose we can do anything better," said Mr. Robarts. "I

[107-044]think it a pity, however, that any steps should have been taken by

[107-045]the bishop before the trial."

[107-046]"The bishop has been placed in a very delicate position," said Mr.

[107-047]Thumble, pleading for his patron.

[107-048]"I don't know the meaning of the word 'delicate,'" said Robarts.

[107-049]"I think his duty was very clear, to avoid interference whilst the

[107-050]matter is, so to say, before the judge."

[107-051]"Nobody has anything else to propose?" said Dr. Tempest. "Then I will

[107-052]write to Mr. Crawley, and you, gentlemen, will perhaps do me the

[107-053]honour of meeting me here at one o'clock on this day week." Then the

[107-054]meeting was over, and the four clergymen having shaken hands with Dr.

[107-055]Tempest in the hall, all promised that they would return on that day

[107-056]week. So far, Dr. Tempest had carried his point exactly as he might

[107-057]have done had the four gentlemen been represented by the chairs on

[107-058]which they had sat.

[107-059]"I shan't come again, all the same, unless I know where I'm to get my

[107-060]expenses," said Mr. Quiverful, as he got into the gig.

[107-061]"I shall come," said Mr. Thumble, "because I think it a duty. Of

[107-062]course it is a hardship." Mr. Thumble liked the idea of being joined

[107-063]with such men as Dr. Tempest, and Mr. Oriel, and Mr. Robarts, and

[107-064]would any day have paid the expense of a gig from Barchester to

[107-065]Silverbridge out of his own pocket, for the sake of sitting with such

[107-066]benchfellows on any clerical inquiry.

[107-067]"One's first duty is to one's own wife and family," said Mr.

[107-068]Quiverful.

[107-069]"Well, yes; in a way, of course, that is quite true, Mr. Quiverful;

[107-070]and when we know how very inadequate are the incomes of the working

[107-071]clergy, we cannot but feel ourselves to be, if I may so say, put

[107-072]upon, when we have to defray the expenses incidental to special

[107-073]duties out of our own pockets. I think, you know,--I don't mind

[107-074]saying this to you,--that the palace should have provided us with

[107-075]a chaise and pair." This was ungrateful on the part of Mr. Thumble,

[107-076]who had been permitted to ride miles upon miles to various outlying

[107-077]clerical duties upon the bishop's worn-out cob. "You see," continued

[107-078]Mr. Thumble, "you and I go specially to represent the palace, and the

[107-079]palace ought to remember that. I think there ought to have been a

[107-080]chaise and pair; I do indeed."

[107-081]"I don't care much what the conveyance is," said Mr. Quiverful; "but

[107-082]I certainly shall pay nothing more out of my own pocket;--certainly I

[107-083]shall not."

[107-084]"The result will be that the palace will be thrown over if they

[107-085]don't take care," said Mr. Thumble. "Tempest, however, seems to be

[107-086]pretty steady. Tempest, I think, is steady. You see he is getting

[107-087]tired of parish work, and would like to go into the close. That's

[107-088]what he is looking out for. Did you ever see such a fellow as that

[107-089]Robarts,--just look at him;--quite indecent, wasn't he? He thinks he

[107-090]can have his own way in everything, just because his sister married a

[107-091]lord. I do hate to see all that meanness."

[107-092]Mark Robarts and Caleb Oriel left Silverbridge in another gig by the

[107-093]same road, and soon passed their brethren, as Mr. Robarts was in the

[107-094]habit of driving a large, quick-stepping horse. The last remarks were

[107-095]being made as the dust from the vicar of Framley's wheels saluted the

[107-096]faces of the two slower clergymen. Mr. Oriel had promised to dine and

[107-097]sleep at Framley, and therefore returned in Mr. Robarts' gig.

[107-098]"Quite unnecessary, all this fuss; don't you think so?" said Mr.

[107-099]Robarts.

[107-100]"I am not quite sure," said Mr. Oriel. "I can understand that the

[107-101]bishop may have found a difficulty."

[107-102]"The bishop, indeed! The bishop doesn't care two straws about it.

[107-103]It's Mrs. Proudie! She has put her finger on the poor man's neck

[107-104]because he has not put his neck beneath her feet; and now she thinks

[107-105]she can crush him,--as she would crush you or me, if it were in

[107-106]her power. That's about the long and the short of the bishop's

[107-107]solicitude."

[107-108]"You are very hard on him," said Mr. Oriel.

[107-109]"I know him;--and am not at all hard on him. She is hard upon him if

[107-110]you like. Tempest is fair. He is very fair, and as long as no one

[107-111]meddles with him he won't do amiss. I can't hold my tongue always,

[107-112]but I often know that it is better that I should."

[107-113]Dr. Tempest said not a word to any one on the subject, not even in

[107-114]his own defence. And yet he was sorely tempted. On the very day of

[107-115]the meeting he dined at Mr. Walker's in Silverbridge, and there

[107-116]submitted to be talked at by all the ladies and most of the gentlemen

[107-117]present, without saying a word in his own defence. And yet a word or

[107-118]two would have been so easy and so conclusive.

[107-119]"Oh, Dr. Tempest," said Mary Walker, "I am so sorry that you have

[107-120]joined the bishop."

[107-121]"Are you, my dear?" said he. "It is generally thought well that a

[107-122]parish clergyman should agree with his bishop."

[107-123]"But you know, Dr. Tempest, that you don't agree with your bishop

[107-124]generally."

[107-125]"Then it is the more fortunate that I shall be able to agree with him

[107-126]on this occasion."

[107-127]Major Grantly was present at the dinner, and ventured to ask the

[107-128]doctor in the course of the evening what he thought would be done.

[107-129]"I should not venture to ask such a question, Dr. Tempest," he said,

[107-130]"unless I had the strongest possible reason to justify my anxiety."

[107-131]"I don't know that I can tell you anything, Major Grantly," said the

[107-132]doctor. "We did not even see Mr. Crawley to-day. But the real truth

[107-133]is that he must stand or fall as the jury shall find him guilty or

[107-134]not guilty. It would be the same in any profession. Could a captain

[107-135]in the army hold up his head in his regiment after he had been tried

[107-136]and found guilty of stealing twenty pounds?"

[107-137]"I don't think he could," said the major.

[107-138]"Neither can a clergyman," said the doctor. "The bishop can neither

[107-139]make him nor mar him. It is the jury that must do it."

[107-140]CHAPTER LV.

[107-141]FRAMLEY PARSONAGE.

[107-142]At this time Grace Crawley was at Framley Parsonage. Old Lady

[107-143]Lufton's strategy had been quite intelligible, but some people said

[107-144]that in point of etiquette and judgment and moral conduct, it was

[107-145]indefensible. Her vicar, Mr. Robarts, had been selected to be one

[107-146]of the clergymen who was to sit in ecclesiastical judgment upon Mr.

[107-147]Crawley, and while he was so sitting Mr. Crawley's daughter was

[107-148]staying in Mr. Robarts' house as a visitor with his wife! It might

[107-149]be that there was no harm in this. Lady Lufton, when the apparent

[107-150]impropriety was pointed out to her by no less a person than

[107-151]Archdeacon Grantly, ridiculed the idea. "My dear archdeacon," Lady

[107-152]Lufton had said, "we all know the bishop to be such a fool and the

[107-153]bishop's wife to be such a knave, that we cannot allow ourselves to

[107-154]be governed in this matter by ordinary rules. Do you not think that

[107-155]it is expedient to show how utterly we disregard his judgment and

[107-156]her malice?" The archdeacon had hesitated much before he spoke to

[107-157]Lady Lufton, whether he should address himself to her or to Mr.

[107-158]Robarts,--or indeed to Mrs. Robarts. But he had become aware that

[107-159]the proposition as to the visit had originated with Lady Lufton, and

[107-160]he had therefore decided on speaking to her. He had not condescended

[107-161]to say a word as to his son, nor would he so condescend. Nor could

[107-162]he go from Lady Lufton to Mr. Robarts, having once failed with her

[107-163]ladyship. Indeed, in giving him his due, we must acknowledge that

[107-164]his disapprobation of Lady Lufton's strategy arose rather from his

[107-165]true conviction as to its impropriety, than from any fear lest

[107-166]this attention paid to Miss Crawley should tend to bring about

[107-167]her marriage with his son. By this time he hated the very name of

[107-168]Crawley. He hated it the more because in hating it he had to put

[107-169]himself for the time on the same side with Mrs. Proudie. But for all

[107-170]that he would not condescend to any unworthy mode of fighting. He

[107-171]thought it wrong that the young lady should be invited to Framley

[107-172]Parsonage at this moment, and he said so to the person who had, as he

[107-173]thought, in truth, given the invitation; but he would not allow his

[107-174]own personal motives to induce him to carry on the argument with Lady

[107-175]Lufton. "The bishop is a fool," he said, "and the bishop's wife is

[107-176]a knave. Nevertheless I would not have had the young lady over to

[107-177]Framley at this moment. If, however, you think it right and Robarts

[107-178]thinks it right, there is an end of it."
