[017-001]It was at last decided that Grace should go to her friend at

[017-002]Allington, and to Allington she went. She returned home for a day or

[017-003]two, and was persuaded by her mother to accept the invitation that

[017-004]had been given her. At Hogglestock, while she was there, new troubles

[017-005]came up, of which something shall shortly be told; but they were

[017-006]troubles in which Grace could give no assistance to her mother, and

[017-007]which, indeed, though they were in truth troubles, as will be seen,

[017-008]were so far beneficent that they stirred her father up to a certain

[017-009]action which was in itself salutary. "I think it will be better that

[017-010]you should be away, dearest," said the mother, who now, for the first

[017-011]time, heard plainly all that poor Grace had to tell about Major

[017-012]Grantly;--Grace having, heretofore, barely spoken, in most ambiguous

[017-013]words, of Major Grantly as a gentleman whom she had met at Framley,

[017-014]and whom she had described as being "very nice."

[017-015]In old days, long ago, Lucy Robarts, the present Lady Lufton, sister

[017-016]of the Rev. Mark Robarts, the parson of Framley, had sojourned

[017-017]for a while under Mr. Crawley's roof at Hogglestock. Peculiar

[017-018]circumstances, which need not, perhaps, be told here, had given

[017-019]occasion for this visit. She had then resolved,--for her future

[017-020]destiny had been known to her before she left Mrs. Crawley's

[017-021]house,--that she would in coming days do much to befriend the family

[017-022]of her friend; but the doing of much had been very difficult. And the

[017-023]doing of anything had come to be very difficult through a certain

[017-024]indiscretion on Lord Lufton's part. Lord Lufton had offered

[017-025]assistance, pecuniary assistance, to Mr. Crawley, which Mr. Crawley

[017-026]had rejected with outspoken anger. What was Lord Lufton to him that

[017-027]his lordship should dare to come to him with his paltry money in his

[017-028]hand? But after a while, Lady Lufton, exercising some cunning in the

[017-029]operations of her friendship, had persuaded her sister-in-law at the

[017-030]Framley parsonage to have Grace Crawley over there as a visitor,--and

[017-031]there she had been during the summer holidays previous to the

[017-032]commencement of our story. And there, at Framley, she had become

[017-033]acquainted with Major Grantly, who was staying with Lord Lufton at

[017-034]Framley Court. She had then said something to her mother about Major

[017-035]Grantly, something ambiguous, something about his being "very nice,"

[017-036]and the mother had thought how great was the pity that her daughter,

[017-037]who was "nice" too in her estimation, should have so few of those

[017-038]adjuncts to assist her which come from full pockets. She had thought

[017-039]no more about it then; but now she felt herself constrained to think

[017-040]more. "I don't quite understand why he should have come to Miss

[017-041]Prettyman on Monday," said Grace, "because he hardly knows her at

[017-042]all."

[017-043]"I suppose it was on business," said Mrs. Crawley.

[017-044]"No, mamma, it was not on business."

[017-045]"How can you tell, dear?"

[017-046]"Because Miss Prettyman said it was,--it was--to ask after me. Oh,

[017-047]mamma, I must tell you. I know he did like me."

[017-048]"Did he ever say so to you, dearest?"

[017-049]"Yes, mamma."

[017-050]"And what did you tell him?"

[017-051]"I told him nothing, mamma."

[017-052]"And did he ask to see you on Monday?"

[017-053]"No, mamma; I don't think he did. I think he understood it all too

[017-054]well, for I could not have spoken to him then."

[017-055]Mrs. Crawley pursued the cross-examination no further, but made up

[017-056]her mind that it would be better that her girl should be away from

[017-057]her wretched home during this period of her life. If it were written

[017-058]in the book of fate that one of her children should be exempted from

[017-059]the series of misfortunes which seemed to fall, one after another,

[017-060]almost as a matter of course, upon her husband, upon her, and upon

[017-061]her family; if so great good fortune were in store for her Grace as

[017-062]such a marriage as this which seemed to be so nearly offered to her,

[017-063]it might probably be well that Grace should be as little at home as

[017-064]possible. Mrs. Crawley had heard nothing but good of Major Grantly;

[017-065]but she knew that the Grantlys were proud rich people,--who lived

[017-066]with their heads high up in the county,--and it could hardly be that

[017-067]a son of the archdeacon would like to take his bride direct from

[017-068]Hogglestock parsonage.

[017-069]It was settled that Grace should go to Allington as soon as a letter

[017-070]could be received from Miss Dale in return to Grace's note, and on

[017-071]the third morning after her arrival at home she started. None but

[017-072]they who have themselves been poor gentry,--gentry so poor as not to

[017-073]know how to raise a shilling,--can understand the peculiar bitterness

[017-074]of the trials which such poverty produces. The poverty of the normal

[017-075]poor does not approach it; or, rather, the pangs arising from such

[017-076]poverty are altogether of a different sort. To be hungry and have no

[017-077]food, to be cold and have no fuel, to be threatened with distraint

[017-078]for one's few chairs and tables, and with the loss of the roof over

[017-079]one's head,--all these miseries, which, if they do not positively

[017-080]reach, are so frequently near to reaching the normal poor, are, no

[017-081]doubt, the severest of the trials to which humanity is subjected.

[017-082]They threaten life,--or, if not life, then liberty,--reducing the

[017-083]abject one to a choice between captivity and starvation. By hook or

[017-084]crook, the poor gentleman or poor lady,--let the one or the other

[017-085]be ever so poor,--does not often come to the last extremity of the

[017-086]workhouse. There are such cases, but they are exceptional. Mrs.

[017-087]Crawley, through all her sufferings, had never yet found her cupboard

[017-088]to be absolutely bare, or the bread-pan to be actually empty. But

[017-089]there are pangs to which, at the time, starvation itself would seem

[017-090]to be preferable. The angry eyes of unpaid tradesmen, savage with

[017-091]an anger which one knows to be justifiable; the taunt of the poor

[017-092]servant who wants her wages; the gradual relinquishment of habits

[017-093]which the soft nurture of earlier, kinder years had made second

[017-094]nature; the wan cheeks of the wife whose malady demands wine; the

[017-095]rags of the husband whose outward occupations demand decency; the

[017-096]neglected children, who are learning not to be the children of

[017-097]gentlefolk; and, worse than all, the alms and doles of half-generous

[017-098]friends, the waning pride, the pride that will not wane, the growing

[017-099]doubt whether it be not better to bow the head, and acknowledge to

[017-100]all the world that nothing of the pride of station is left,--that the

[017-101]hand is open to receive and ready to touch the cap, that the fall

[017-102]from the upper to the lower level has been accomplished,--these are

[017-103]the pangs of poverty which drive the Crawleys of the world to the

[017-104]frequent entertaining of that idea of the bare bodkin. It was settled

[017-105]that Grace should go to Allington;--but how about her clothes? And

[017-106]then, whence was to come the price of her journey?

[017-107]"I don't think they'll mind about my being shabby at Allington. They

[017-108]live very quietly there."

[017-109]"But you say that Miss Dale is so very nice in all her ways."

[017-110]"Lily is very nice, mamma; but I shan't mind her so much as her

[017-111]mother, because she knows it all. I have told her everything."

[017-112]"But you have given me all your money, dearest."

[017-113]"Miss Prettyman told me I was to come to her," said Grace, who had

[017-114]already taken some small sum from the schoolmistress, which at once

[017-115]had gone into her mother's pocket, and into household purposes. "She

[017-116]said I should be sure to go to Allington, and that of course I should

[017-117]go to her, as I must pass through Silverbridge."

[017-118]"I hope papa will not ask about it," said Mrs. Crawley. Luckily papa

[017-119]did not ask about it, being at the moment occupied much with other

[017-120]thoughts and other troubles, and Grace was allowed to return by

[017-121]Silverbridge, and to take what was needed from Miss Prettyman. Who

[017-122]can tell of the mending and patching, of the weary wearing midnight

[017-123]hours of needlework which were accomplished before the poor girl

[017-124]went, so that she might not reach her friend's house in actual rags?

[017-125]And when the work was ended, what was there to show for it? I do not

[017-126]think that the idea of the bare bodkin, as regarded herself, ever

[017-127]flitted across Mrs. Crawley's brain,--she being one of those who are

[017-128]very strong to endure; but it must have occurred to her very often

[017-129]that the repose of the grave is sweet, and that there cometh after

[017-130]death a levelling and making even of things, which would at last cure

[017-131]all her evils.

[017-132]Grace no doubt looked forward to a levelling and making even of

[017-133]things,--or perhaps even to something more prosperous than that,

[017-134]which should come to her relief on this side of the grave. She could

[017-135]not but have high hopes in regard to her future destiny. Although,

[017-136]as has been said, she understood no more than she ought to have

[017-137]understood from Miss Prettyman's account of the conversation with

[017-138]Major Grantly, still, innocent as she was, she had understood much.

[017-139]She knew that the man loved her, and she knew also that she loved the

[017-140]man. She thoroughly comprehended that the present could be to her no

[017-141]time for listening to speeches of love, or for giving kind answers;

[017-142]but still I think that she did look for relief on this side of the

[017-143]grave.

[017-144]"Tut, tut," said Miss Prettyman as Grace in vain tried to conceal her

[017-145]tears up in the private sanctum. "You ought to know me by this time,

[017-146]and to have learned that I can understand things." The tears had

[017-147]flown in return not only for the five gold sovereigns which Miss

[017-148]Prettyman had pressed into her hand, but on account of the prettiest,

[017-149]soft, grey merino frock that ever charmed a girl's eye. "I should

[017-150]like to know how many girls I have given dresses to, when they have

[017-151]been going out visiting. Law, my dear; they take them, many of them,

[017-152]from us old maids, almost as if we were only paying our debts in

[017-153]giving them." And then Miss Anne gave her a cloth cloak, very warm,

[017-154]with pretty buttons and gimp trimmings,--just such a cloak as any

[017-155]girl might like to wear who thought that she would be seen out

[017-156]walking by her Major Grantly on a Christmas morning. Grace Crawley

[017-157]did not expect to be seen out walking by her Major Grantly, but

[017-158]nevertheless she liked the cloak. By the power of her practical will,

[017-159]and by her true sympathy, the elder Miss Prettyman had for a while

[017-160]conquered the annoyance which, on Grace's part, was attached to the

[017-161]receiving of gifts, by the consciousness of her poverty; and when

[017-162]Miss Anne, with some pride in the tone of her voice, expressed a

[017-163]hope that Grace would think the cloak pretty, Grace put her arms

[017-164]pleasantly round her friend's neck, and declared that it was very

[017-165]pretty,--the prettiest cloak in all the world!

[017-166]Grace was met at the Guestwick railway-station by her friend Lilian

[017-167]Dale, and was driven over to Allington in a pony carriage belonging

[017-168]to Lilian's uncle, the squire of the parish. I think she will be

[017-169]excused in having put on her new cloak, not so much because of the

[017-170]cold as with a view of making the best of herself before Mrs. Dale.

[017-171]And yet she knew that Mrs. Dale would know all the circumstances of

[017-172]her poverty, and was very glad that it should be so. "I am so glad

[017-173]that you have come, dear," said Lily. "It will be such a comfort."

[017-174]"I am sure you are very good," said Grace.

[017-175]"And mamma is so glad. From the moment that we both talked ourselves

[017-176]into eagerness about it,--while I was writing my letter, you know, we

[017-177]resolved that it must be so."
