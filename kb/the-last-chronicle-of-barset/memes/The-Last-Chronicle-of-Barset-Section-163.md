[163-001]With his new coats, and something, also, of new manners, he and his

[163-002]wife went over to Plumstead, leaving Jane at the deanery with Mrs.

[163-003]Arabin. The dean also went to Plumstead. They arrived there not much

[163-004]before dinner, and as Grace was there before them the first moments

[163-005]were not so bad. Before Mr. Crawley had had time to feel himself lost

[163-006]in the drawing-room, he was summoned away to prepare himself for

[163-007]dinner,--for dinner, and for the coat, which at the deanery he had

[163-008]been allowed to leave unworn. "I would with all my heart that I might

[163-009]retire to rest," he said to his wife, when the ceremony had been

[163-010]perfected.

[163-011]"Do not say so. Go down and take your place with them, and speak your

[163-012]mind with them,--as you so well know how. Who among them can do it so

[163-013]well?"

[163-014]"I have been told," said Mr. Crawley, "that you shall take a cock

[163-015]which is lord of the farmyard,--the cock of all that walk,--and when

[163-016]you have daubed his feathers with mud, he shall be thrashed by every

[163-017]dunghill coward. I say not that I was ever the cock of the walk, but

[163-018]I know that they have daubed my feathers." Then he went down among

[163-019]the other poultry into the farmyard.

[163-020]At dinner he was very silent, answering, however, with a sort of

[163-021]graceful stateliness any word that Mrs. Grantly addressed to him. Mr.

[163-022]Thorne, from Ullathorne, was there also to meet his new vicar, as was

[163-023]also Mr. Thorne's very old sister, Miss Monica Thorne. And Lady Anne

[163-024]Grantly was there,--she having come with the expressed intention that

[163-025]the wives of the two brothers should know each other,--but with a

[163-026]warmer desire, I think, of seeing Mr. Crawley, of whom the clerical

[163-027]world had been talking much since some notice of the accusation

[163-028]against him had become general. There were, therefore, ten or twelve

[163-029]at the dinner-table, and Mr. Crawley had not made one at such a board

[163-030]certainly since his marriage. All went fairly smooth with him till

[163-031]the ladies left the room; for though Lady Anne, who sat at his left

[163-032]hand, had perplexed him somewhat with clerical questions, he had

[163-033]found that he was not called upon for much more than monosyllabic

[163-034]responses. But in his heart he feared the archdeacon, and he felt

[163-035]that when the ladies were gone the archdeacon would not leave him

[163-036]alone in his silence.

[163-037]As soon as the door was closed, the first subject mooted was that of

[163-038]the Plumstead fox, which had been so basely murdered on Mr. Thorne's

[163-039]ground. Mr. Thorne had confessed the iniquity, had dismissed the

[163-040]murderous keeper, and all was serene. But the greater on that account

[163-041]was the feasibility of discussing the question, and the archdeacon

[163-042]had a good deal to say about it. Then Mr. Thorne turned to the new

[163-043]vicar, and asked him whether foxes abounded in Hogglestock. Had he

[163-044]been asked as to the rats or the moles, he would have known more

[163-045]about it.

[163-046]"Indeed, sir, I know not whether or no there be any foxes in the

[163-047]parish of Hogglestock. I do not remember me that I ever saw one. It

[163-048]is an animal whose habits I have not watched."

[163-049]"There is an earth at Hoggle Bushes," said the major; "and I never

[163-050]knew it without a litter."

[163-051]"I think I know the domestic whereabouts of every fox in Plumstead,"

[163-052]said the archdeacon, with an ill-natured intention of astonishing Mr.

[163-053]Crawley.

[163-054]"Of foxes with two legs our friend is speaking, without doubt," said

[163-055]the vicar of St. Ewolds, with an attempt at grim pleasantry.

[163-056]"Of them we have none at Plumstead. No,--I was speaking of the dear

[163-057]old fellow with the brush. Pass the bottle, Mr. Crawley. Won't you

[163-058]fill your glass?" Mr. Crawley passed the bottle, but would not fill

[163-059]his glass. Then the dean, looking up slily, saw the vexation written

[163-060]in the archdeacon's face. The parson whom the archdeacon feared most

[163-061]of all parsons was the parson who wouldn't fill his glass.

[163-062]Then the subject was changed. "I'm told that the bishop has at last

[163-063]made his reappearance on his throne," said the archdeacon.

[163-064]"He was in the cathedral last Sunday," said the dean.

[163-065]"Does he ever mean to preach again?"

[163-066]"He never did preach very often," said the dean.

[163-067]"A great deal too often, from all that people say," said the

[163-068]archdeacon. "I never heard him myself, and never shall, I dare say.

[163-069]You have heard him, Mr. Crawley?"

[163-070]"I have never had that good fortune, Mr. Archdeacon. But living as I

[163-071]shall now do, so near to the city, I may perhaps be enabled to attend

[163-072]the cathedral service on some holyday of the Church, which may not

[163-073]require prayers in my own rural parish. I think that the clergy of

[163-074]the diocese should be acquainted with the opinions, and with the

[163-075]voice, and with the very manner and words of their bishop. As things

[163-076]are now done, this is not possible. I could wish that there were

[163-077]occasions on which a bishop might assemble his clergy, and preach to

[163-078]them sermons adapted to their use."

[163-079]"What do you call a bishop's charge, then?"

[163-080]"It is usually in the printed form that I have received it," said Mr.

[163-081]Crawley.

[163-082]"I think we have quite enough of that kind of thing," said the

[163-083]archdeacon.

[163-084]"He is a man whose conversation is not pleasing to me," Mr. Crawley

[163-085]said to his wife that night.

[163-086]"Do not judge of him too quickly, Josiah," his wife said. "There

[163-087]is so much of good in him! He is kind, and generous, and I think

[163-088]affectionate."

[163-089]"But he is of the earth, earthy. When you and the other ladies had

[163-090]retired, the conversation at first fell on the habits and value

[163-091]of--foxes. I have been informed that in these parts the fox is

[163-092]greatly prized, as without a fox to run before the dogs, that

[163-093]scampering over the country which is called hunting, and which

[163-094]delights by the quickness and perhaps by the peril of the exercise,

[163-095]is not relished by the riders. Of the wisdom or taste herein

[163-096]displayed by the hunters of the day I say nothing. But it seemed to

[163-097]me that in talking of foxes Dr. Grantly was master of his subject.

[163-098]Thence the topic glided to the duties of a bishop and to questions

[163-099]of preaching, as to which Dr. Grantly was not slow in offering his

[163-100]opinion. But I thought that I would rather have heard him talk about

[163-101]the foxes for a week together." She said nothing more to him, knowing

[163-102]well how useless it was to attempt to turn him by any argument.

[163-103]To her thinking the kindness of the archdeacon to them personally

[163-104]demanded some indulgence in the expression, and even in the

[163-105]formation, of an opinion, respecting his clerical peculiarities.

[163-106]On the next day, however, Mr. Crawley, having been summoned by the

[163-107]archdeacon into the library for a little private conversation, found

[163-108]that he got on better with him. How the archdeacon conquered him may

[163-109]perhaps be best described by a further narration of what Mr. Crawley

[163-110]said to his wife. "I told him that in regard to money matters, as he

[163-111]called them, I had nothing to say. I only trusted that his son was

[163-112]aware that my daughter had no money, and never would have any. 'My

[163-113]dear Crawley,' the archdeacon said,--for of late there seems to have

[163-114]grown up in the world a habit of greater familiarity than that which

[163-115]I think did prevail when last I moved much among men;--'my dear

[163-116]Crawley, I have enough for both.' 'I would we stood on more equal

[163-117]grounds,' I said. Then as he answered me, he rose from his chair. 'We

[163-118]stand,' said he, 'on the only perfect level on which such men can

[163-119]meet each other. We are both gentlemen.' 'Sir,' I said, rising also,

[163-120]'from the bottom of my heart I agree with you. I could not have

[163-121]spoken such words; but coming from you who are rich to me who am

[163-122]poor, they are honourable to the one and comfortable to the other.'"

[163-123]"And after that?"

[163-124]"He took down from the shelves a volume of sermons which his father

[163-125]published many years ago, and presented it to me. I have it now under

[163-126]my arm. It hath the old bishop's manuscript notes, which I will study

[163-127]carefully." And thus the archdeacon had hit his bird on both wings.

[163-128]CHAPTER LXXXIV.

[163-129]CONCLUSION.

[163-130]It now only remains for me to gather together a few loose strings,

[163-131]and tie them together in a knot, so that my work may not become

[163-132]untwisted. Early in July, Henry Grantly and Grace Crawley were

[163-133]married in the parish church of Plumstead,--a great impropriety, as

[163-134]to which neither Archdeacon Grantly nor Mr. Crawley could be got to

[163-135]assent for a long time, but which was at last carried, not simply by

[163-136]a union of Mrs. Grantly and Mrs. Crawley, nor even by the assistance

[163-137]of Mrs. Arabin, but by the strong intervention of old Lady Lufton

[163-138]herself. "Of course Miss Crawley ought to be married from St. Ewolds

[163-139]vicarage; but when the furniture has only half been got in, how is

[163-140]it possible?" When Lady Lufton thus spoke, the archdeacon gave way,

[163-141]and Mr. Crawley hadn't a leg to stand upon. Henry Grantly had not an

[163-142]opinion upon the matter. He told his father that he expected that

[163-143]they would marry him among them, and that that would be enough for

[163-144]him. As for Grace, nobody even thought of asking her; and I doubt

[163-145]whether she would have heard anything about the contest, had not

[163-146]some tidings of it reached her from her lover. Married they were at

[163-147]Plumstead,--and the breakfast was given with all that luxuriance of

[163-148]plenty which was so dear to the archdeacon's mind. Mr. Crawley was

[163-149]the officiating priest. With his hands dropping before him, folded

[163-150]humbly, he told the archdeacon,--when that Plumstead question had

[163-151]been finally settled in opposition to his wishes,--that he would fain

[163-152]himself perform the ceremony by which his dearest daughter would

[163-153]be bound to her marriage duties. "And who else should?" said the

[163-154]archdeacon. Mr. Crawley muttered that he had not known how far his

[163-155]reverend brother might have been willing to waive his rights. But

[163-156]the archdeacon, who was in high good humour,--having just bestowed

[163-157]a little pony carriage on his new daughter-in-law,--only laughed

[163-158]at him; and, if the rumour which was handed about the families be

[163-159]true, the archdeacon, before the interview was over, had poked Mr.

[163-160]Crawley in the ribs. Mr. Crawley married them; but the archdeacon

[163-161]assisted,--and the dean gave away the bride. The Rev. Charles Grantly

[163-162]was there also; and as there was, as a matter of course, a cloud of

[163-163]curates floating in the distance, Henry Grantly was perhaps to be

[163-164]excused for declaring to his wife, when the pair had escaped, that

[163-165]surely no couple had ever been so tightly buckled since marriage had

[163-166]first become a Church ceremony.

[163-167]Soon after that, Mr. and Mrs. Crawley became quiet at St. Ewolds,

[163-168]and, as I think, contented. Her happiness began very quickly. Though

[163-169]she had been greatly broken by her troubles, the first sight she had

[163-170]of her husband in his new long frock-coat went far to restore her,

[163-171]and while he was declaring himself to be a cock so daubed with mud as

[163-172]to be incapable of crowing, she was congratulating herself on seeing

[163-173]her husband once more clothed as became his position. And they were

[163-174]lucky, too, as regarded the squire's house; for Mr. Thorne was old,

[163-175]and quiet, and old-fashioned; and Miss Thorne was older, and though

[163-176]she was not exactly quiet, she was very old-fashioned indeed. So that

[163-177]there grew to be a pleasant friendship between Miss Thorne and Mrs.

[163-178]Crawley.

[163-179]Johnny Eames, when last I heard of him, was still a bachelor, and, as

[163-180]I think, likely to remain so. At last he had utterly thrown over Sir

[163-181]Raffle Buffle, declaring to his friends that the special duties of

[163-182]private secretaryship were not exactly to his taste. "You get so sick

[163-183]at the thirteenth private note," he said, "that you find yourself

[163-184]unable to carry on the humbug any farther." But he did not leave his

[163-185]office. "I'm the head of a room, you know," he told Lady Julia De

[163-186]Guest; "and there's nothing to trouble me,--and a fellow, you know,

[163-187]ought to have something to do." Lady Julia told him, with a great

[163-188]deal of energy, that she would never forgive him if he gave up his

[163-189]office. After that eventful night when he escaped ignominiously from

[163-190]the house of Lady Demolines under the protection of the policeman's

[163-191]lantern, he did hear more than once from Porchester Terrace, and

[163-192]from allies employed by the enemy who was there resident. "My cousin,

[163-193]the serjeant," proved to be a myth. Johnny found out all about that

[163-194]Serjeant Runter, who was distantly connected, indeed, with the late

[163-195]husband of Lady Demolines, but had always persistently declined to

[163-196]have any intercourse whatever with her ladyship. For the serjeant was

[163-197]a rising man, and Lady Demolines was not exactly progressing in the

[163-198]world. Johnny heard nothing from the serjeant; but from Madalina he

[163-199]got letter after letter. In the first she asked him not to think

[163-200]too much of the little joke that had occurred. In her second she

[163-201]described the vehemence of her love. In her third the bitterness

[163-202]of her wrath. Her fourth she simply invited him to come and

[163-203]dine in Porchester Terrace. Her fifth was the outpouring of

[163-204]injured innocence. And then came letters from an attorney. Johnny

[163-205]answered not a word to any of them, and gradually the letters were

[163-206]discontinued. Within six months of the receipt of the last, he was

[163-207]delighted by reading among the marriages in the newspapers a notice

[163-208]that Peter Bangles, Esq., of the firm of Burton and Bangles, wine

[163-209]merchants, of Hook Court, had been united to Madalina, daughter of

[163-210]the late Sir Confucius Demolines, at the church of Peter the Martyr.

[163-211]"Most appropriate," said Johnny, as he read the notice to Conway

[163-212]Dalrymple, who was then back from his wedding tour; "for most

[163-213]assuredly there will be now another Peter the Martyr."
