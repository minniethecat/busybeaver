[103-001]"I am afraid he will; I am afraid he will," said Sir Raffle; "and I

[103-002]fear, my dear Miss Dale, that I must go further than that. I fear I

[103-003]must express an opinion that he is guilty."

[103-004]"Nothing will ever make me think so," said Lily.

[103-005]"Ladies are always tender-hearted," said Sir Raffle, "and especially

[103-006]young ladies,--and especially pretty young ladies. I do not wonder

[103-007]that such should be your opinion. But you see, Miss Dale, a man of

[103-008]business has to look at these things in a business light. What I want

[103-009]to know is, where did he get the cheque? He is bound to be explicit

[103-010]in answering that before anybody can acquit him."

[103-011]"That is just what Mr. Eames has gone abroad to learn."

[103-012]"It is very well for Eames to go abroad,--though, upon my word, I

[103-013]don't know whether I should not have given him different advice if

[103-014]I had known how much I was to be tormented by his absence. The thing

[103-015]couldn't have happened at a more unfortunate time;--the Ministry

[103-016]going out, and everything. But, as I was saying, it is all very well

[103-017]for him to do what he can. He is related to them, and is bound to

[103-018]save the honour of his relations if it be possible. I like him for

[103-019]going. I always liked him. As I said to my friend De Guest, 'That

[103-020]young man will make his way.' And I rather fancy that the chance word

[103-021]which I spoke then to my valued old friend was not thrown away in

[103-022]Eames's favour. But, my dear Miss Dale, where did Mr. Crawley get

[103-023]that cheque? That's what I want to know. If you can tell me that,

[103-024]then I can tell you whether or no he will be acquitted."

[103-025]Lily did not feel a strong prepossession in favour of Sir Raffle, in

[103-026]spite of his praise of John Eames. The harsh voice of the man annoyed

[103-027]her, and his egotism offended her. When, much later in the evening,

[103-028]his character came on for discussion between herself and Mrs. Thorne

[103-029]and Emily Dunstable, she had not a word to say in his favour. But

[103-030]still she had been pleased to meet him, because he was the man with

[103-031]whom Johnny's life was most specially concerned. I think that a

[103-032]portion of her dislike to him arose from the fact that in continuing

[103-033]the conversation he did not revert to his private secretary, but

[103-034]preferred to regale her with stories of his own doings in wonderful

[103-035]cases which had partaken of interest similar to that which now

[103-036]attached itself to Mr. Crawley's case. He had known a man who had

[103-037]stolen a hundred pounds, and had never been found out; and another

[103-038]man who had been arrested for stealing two-and-sixpence which was

[103-039]found afterwards sticking to a bit of butter at the bottom of a

[103-040]plate. Mrs. Thorne had heard all this, and had answered him, "Dear

[103-041]me, Sir Raffle," she had said, "what a great many thieves you have

[103-042]had among your acquaintance!" This had rather disconcerted him, and

[103-043]then there had been no more talking about Mr. Crawley.

[103-044]It had been arranged on this morning that Mr. Dale should return to

[103-045]Allington and leave Lily with Mrs. Thorne. Some special need of his

[103-046]presence at home, real or assumed, had arisen, and he had declared

[103-047]that he must shorten his stay in London by about half the intended

[103-048]period. The need would not have been so pressing, probably, had he

[103-049]not felt that Lily would be more comfortable with Mrs. Thorne than

[103-050]in his lodgings in Sackville Street. Lily had at first declared that

[103-051]she would return with him, but everybody had protested against this.

[103-052]Emily Dunstable had protested against it very stoutly; Mrs. Dale

[103-053]herself had protested against it by letter; and Mrs. Thorne's protest

[103-054]had been quite imperious in its nature. "Indeed, my dear, you'll do

[103-055]nothing of the kind. I'm sure your mother wouldn't wish it. I look

[103-056]upon it as quite essential that you and Emily should learn to know

[103-057]each other." "But we do know each other; don't we, Emily?" said Lily.

[103-058]"Not quite well yet," said Emily. Then Lily had laughed, and so the

[103-059]matter was settled. And now, on this present occasion, Mr. Dale was

[103-060]at Mrs. Thorne's house for the last time. His conscience had been

[103-061]perplexed about Lily's horse, and if anything was to be said it must

[103-062]be said now. The subject was very disagreeable to him, and he was

[103-063]angry with Bernard because Bernard had declined to manage it for him

[103-064]after his own fashion. But he had told himself so often that anything

[103-065]was better than a pecuniary obligation, that he was determined to

[103-066]speak his mind to Mrs. Thorne, and to beg her to allow him to have

[103-067]his way. So he waited till the Harold Smiths were gone, and Sir

[103-068]Raffle Buffle, and then, when Lily was apart with Emily,--for Bernard

[103-069]Dale had left them,--he found himself at last alone with Mrs. Thorne.

[103-070]"I can't be too much obliged to you," he said, "for your kindness to

[103-071]my girl."

[103-072]"Oh, laws, that's nothing," said Mrs. Thorne. "We look on her as one

[103-073]of us now."

[103-074]"I'm sure she is grateful,--very grateful; and so am I. She and

[103-075]Bernard have been brought up so much together that it is very

[103-076]desirable that she should be not unknown to Bernard's wife."

[103-077]"Exactly,--that's just what I mean. Blood's thicker than water; isn't

[103-078]it? Emily's child, if she has one, will be Lily's cousin."

[103-079]"Her first-cousin once removed," said the squire, who was accurate

[103-080]in these matters. Then he drew himself up in his seat and compressed

[103-081]his lips together, and prepared himself for his task. It was very

[103-082]disagreeable. Nothing, he thought, could be more disagreeable. "I

[103-083]have a little thing to speak about," he said at last, "which I hope

[103-084]will not offend you."

[103-085]"[[About]] Lily?"

[103-086]"Yes; about Lily."

[103-087]"I'm not very easily offended, and I don't know how I could possibly

[103-088]be offended about her."

[103-089]"I'm an old-fashioned man, Mrs. Thorne, and don't know much about the

[103-090]ways of the world. I have always been down in the country, and maybe

[103-091]I have prejudices. You won't refuse to humour one of them, I hope?"

[103-092]"You're beginning to frighten me, Mr. Dale; what is it?"

[103-093]"About Lily's horse."

[103-094]"Lily's horse! What about her horse? I hope he's not vicious?"

[103-095]"She is riding every day with your niece," said the squire, thinking

[103-096]it best to stick to his own point.

[103-097]"It will do her all the good in the world," said Mrs. Thorne.

[103-098]"Very likely. I don't doubt it. I do not in the least disapprove her

[103-099]riding. But--"

[103-100]"But what, Mr. Dale?"

[103-101]"I should be so much obliged if I might be allowed to pay the

[103-102]livery-stable keeper's bill."

[103-103]"Oh, laws a' mercy."

[103-104]"I daresay it may sound odd, but as I have a fancy about it, I'm sure

[103-105]you'll gratify me."

[103-106]"Of course I will. I'll remember it. I'll make it all right with

[103-107]Bernard. Bernard and I have no end of accounts,--or shall have before

[103-108]long,--and we'll make an item of it. Then you can arrange with

[103-109]Bernard afterwards."

[103-110]Mr. Dale as he got up to go away felt that he was beaten, but he did

[103-111]not know how to carry the battle any further on that occasion. He

[103-112]could not take out his purse and put down the cost of the horse on

[103-113]the table. "I will then speak to my nephew about it," he said, very

[103-114]gravely, as he went away. And he did speak to his nephew about it,

[103-115]and even wrote to him more than once. But it was all to no purpose.

[103-116]Mr. Potts could not be induced to give a separate bill, and,--so said

[103-117]Bernard,--swore at last that he would furnish no account to anybody

[103-118]for horses that went to Mrs. Thorne's door except to Mrs. Thorne

[103-119]herself.

[103-120]That night Lily took leave of her uncle and remained at Mrs. Thorne's

[103-121]house. As things were now arranged she would, no doubt, be in London

[103-122]when John Eames returned. If he should find her in town--and she told

[103-123]herself that if she was in town he certainly would find her,--he

[103-124]would, doubtless, repeat to her the offer he had so often made

[103-125]before. She never ventured to tell herself that she doubted as to

[103-126]the answer to be made to him. The two letters were written in the

[103-127]book, and must remain there. But she felt that she would have had

[103-128]more courage for persistency down at Allington than she would be able

[103-129]to summon to her assistance up in London. She knew she would be weak,

[103-130]should she be found by him alone in Mrs. Thorne's drawing-room. It

[103-131]would be better for her to make some excuse and go home. She was

[103-132]resolved that she would not become his wife. She could not extricate

[103-133]herself from the dominion of a feeling which she believed to be love

[103-134]for another man. She had given a solemn promise both to her mother

[103-135]and to John Eames that she would not marry that other man; but in

[103-136]doing so she had made a solemn promise to herself that she would

[103-137]not marry John Eames. She had sworn it and would keep her oath. And

[103-138]yet she regretted it! In writing home to her mother the next day,

[103-139]she told Mrs. Dale that all the world was speaking well of John

[103-140]Eames,--that John had won for himself a reputation of his own, and

[103-141]was known far and wide to be a noble fellow. She could not keep

[103-142]herself from praising John Eames, though she knew that such praise

[103-143]might, and would, be used against her at some future time. "Though

[103-144]I cannot love him I will give him his due," she said to herself.

[103-145]"I wish you would make up your mind to have an 'it' for yourself,"

[103-146]Emily Dunstable said to her again that night; "a nice 'it,' so that I

[103-147]could make a friend, perhaps a brother, of him."

[103-148]"I shall never have an 'it,' if I live to be a hundred," said Lily

[103-149]Dale.

[103-150]CHAPTER LIII.

[103-151]ROTTEN ROW.

[103-152][Illustration]

[103-153]Lily had heard nothing as to the difficulty about her horse, and

[103-154]could therefore enjoy her exercise without the drawback of feeling

[103-155]that her uncle was subjected to an annoyance. She was in the habit of

[103-156]going out every day with Bernard and Emily Dunstable, and their party

[103-157]was generally joined by others who would meet them at Mrs. Thorne's

[103-158]house. For Mrs. Thorne was a very hospitable woman, and there

[103-159]were many who liked well enough to go to her house. Late in the

[103-160]afternoon there would be a great congregation of horses before the

[103-161]door,--sometimes as many as a dozen; and then the cavalcade would go

[103-162]off into the Park, and there it would become scattered. As neither

[103-163]Bernard nor Miss Dunstable were unconscionable lovers, Lily in these

[103-164]scatterings did not often find herself neglected or lost. Her cousin

[103-165]would generally remain with her, and as in those days she had no "it"

[103-166]of her own she was well pleased that he should do so.

[103-167]But it so happened that on a certain afternoon she found herself

[103-168]riding in Rotten Row alone with a certain stout gentleman whom she

[103-169]constantly met at Mrs. Thorne's house. His name was Onesiphorus Dunn,

[103-170]and he was usually called Siph by his intimate friends. It had seemed

[103-171]to Lily that everybody was an intimate friend of Mr. Dunn's, and she

[103-172]was in daily fear lest she should make a mistake and call him Siph

[103-173]herself. Had she done so it would not have mattered in the least. Mr.

[103-174]Dunn, had he observed it at all, would neither have been flattered

[103-175]nor angry. A great many young ladies about London did call him Siph,

[103-176]and to him it was quite natural that they should do so. He was

[103-177]an Irishman, living on the best of everything in the world, with

[103-178]apparently no fortune of his own, and certainly never earning

[103-179]anything. Everybody liked him, and it was admitted on all sides that

[103-180]there was no safer friend in the world, either for young ladies or

[103-181]young men, than Mr. Onesiphorus Dunn. He did not borrow money, and he

[103-182]did not encroach. He did like being asked out to dinner, and he did

[103-183]think that they to whom he gave the light of his countenance in town

[103-184]owed him the return of a week's run in the country. He neither shot,

[103-185]nor hunted nor fished, nor read, and yet he was never in the way in

[103-186]any house. He did play billiards, and whist, and croquet--very badly.

[103-187]He was a good judge of wine, and would occasionally condescend to

[103-188]look after the bottling of it on behalf of some very intimate friend.

[103-189]He was a great friend of Mrs. Thorne's, with whom he always spent ten

[103-190]days in the autumn at Chaldicotes.
