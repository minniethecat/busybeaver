[123-001]He pitied himself with a commiseration that was sickly in spite

[123-002]of its truth. It was the fault of the man that he was imbued too

[123-003]strongly with self-consciousness. He could do a great thing or two.

[123-004]He could keep up his courage in positions which would wash all

[123-005]courage out of most men. He could tell the truth though truth should

[123-006]ruin him. He could sacrifice all that he had to duty. He could do

[123-007]justice though the heaven should fall. But he could not forget to pay

[123-008]a tribute to himself for the greatness of his own actions; nor, when

[123-009]accepting with an effort of meekness the small payment made by the

[123-010]world to him, in return for his great works, could he forget the

[123-011]great payments made to others for small work. It was not sufficient

[123-012]for him to remember that he knew Hebrew, but he must remember also

[123-013]that the dean did not.

[123-014]Nevertheless, as he sat there under the rain, he made up his mind

[123-015]with a clearness that certainly had in it nothing of that muddiness

[123-016]of mind of which he had often accused himself. Indeed, the intellect

[123-017]of this man was essentially clear. It was simply his memory that

[123-018]would play him tricks,--his memory as to things which at the moment

[123-019]were not important to him. The fact that the dean had given him money

[123-020]was very important, and he remembered it well. But the amount of the

[123-021]money, and its form, at a moment in which he had flattered himself

[123-022]that he might have strength to leave it unused, had not been

[123-023]important to him. Now, he resolved that he would go to Dr. Tempest,

[123-024]and that he would tell Dr. Tempest that there was no occasion for

[123-025]any further inquiry. He would submit to the bishop, let the bishop's

[123-026]decision be what it might. Things were different since the day on

[123-027]which he had refused Mr. Thumble admission to his pulpit. At that

[123-028]time people believed him to be innocent, and he so believed of

[123-029]himself. Now, people believed him to be guilty, and it could not

[123-030]be right that a man held in such slight esteem should exercise the

[123-031]functions of a parish priest, let his own opinion of himself be what

[123-032]it might. He would submit himself, and go anywhere,--to the galleys

[123-033]or the workhouse, if they wished it. As for his wife and children,

[123-034]they would, he said to himself, be better without him than with him.

[123-035]The world would never be so hard to a woman or to children as it had

[123-036]been to him.

[123-037]He was sitting saturated with rain,--saturated also with

[123-038]thinking,--and quite unobservant of anything around him, when he

[123-039]was accosted by an old man from Hoggle End, with whom he was well

[123-040]acquainted. "Thee be wat, Master Crawley," said the old man.

[123-041]"Wet!" said Crawley, recalled suddenly back to the realities of life.

[123-042]"Well,--yes. I am wet. That's because it's raining."

[123-043]"Thee be teeming o' wat. Hadn't thee better go whome?"

[123-044]"And are not you wet also?" said Mr. Crawley, looking at the old man,

[123-045]who had been at work in the brickfield, and who was soaked with mire,

[123-046]and from whom there seemed to come a steam of muddy mist.

[123-047]"Is it me, yer reverence? I'm wat in course. The loikes of us is

[123-048]always wat,--that is barring the insides of us. It comes to us

[123-049]natural to have the rheumatics. How is one of us to help hisself

[123-050]against having on 'em? But there ain't no call for the loikes of you

[123-051]to have the rheumatics."

[123-052]"My friend," said Crawley, who was now standing on the road,--and

[123-053]as he spoke he put out his arm and took the brickmaker by the hand,

[123-054]"there is a worse complaint than rheumatism,--there is, indeed."

[123-055]"There's what they calls the collerer," said Giles Hoggett, looking

[123-056]up into Mr. Crawley's face. "That ain't a got a hold of yer?"

[123-057]"Ay, and worse than the cholera. A man is killed all over when he is

[123-058]struck in his pride;--and yet he lives."

[123-059]"Maybe that's bad enough too," said Giles, with his hand still held

[123-060]by the other.

[123-061]"It is bad enough," said Mr. Crawley, striking his breast with his

[123-062]left hand. "It is bad enough."

[123-063]"Tell 'ee what, Master Crawley;--and yer reverence mustn't think as

[123-064]I means to be preaching; there ain't nowt a man can't bear if he'll

[123-065]only be dogged. You go whome, Master Crawley, and think o' that,

[123-066]and maybe it'll do ye a good yet. It's dogged as does it. It ain't

[123-067]thinking about it." Then Giles Hoggett withdrew his hand from the

[123-068]clergyman's, and walked away towards his home at Hoggle End. Mr.

[123-069]Crawley also turned homewards, and as he made his way through the

[123-070]lanes, he repeated to himself Giles Hoggett's words. "It's dogged as

[123-071]does it. It's not thinking about it."

[Illustration: "It's dogged as does it."]

[123-072]He did not say a word to his wife on that afternoon about Dr.

[123-073]Tempest; and she was so much taken up with his outward condition

[123-074]when he returned, as almost to have forgotten the letter. He allowed

[123-075]himself, but barely allowed himself, to be made dry, and then for

[123-076]the remainder of the day applied himself to learn the lesson which

[123-077]Hoggett had endeavoured to teach him. But the learning of it was not

[123-078]easy, and hardly became more easy when he had worked the problem out

[123-079]in his own mind, and discovered that the brickmaker's doggedness

[123-080]simply meant self-abnegation;--that a man should force himself to

[123-081]endure anything that might be sent upon him, not only without outward

[123-082]grumbling, but also without grumbling inwardly.

[123-083]Early on the next morning, he told his wife that he was going into

[123-084]Silverbridge. "It is that letter,--the letter which I got yesterday

[123-085]that calls me," he said. And then he handed her the letter as to

[123-086]which he had refused to speak to her on the preceding day.

[123-087]"But this speaks of your going next Monday, Josiah," said Mrs.

[123-088]Crawley.

[123-089]"I find it to be more suitable that I should go to-day," said he.

[123-090]"Some duty I do owe in this matter, both to the bishop, and to Dr.

[123-091]Tempest, who, after a fashion, is, as regards my present business,

[123-092]the bishop's representative. But I do not perceive that I owe it as a

[123-093]duty to either to obey implicitly their injunctions, and I will not

[123-094]submit myself to the cross-questionings of the man Thumble. As I am

[123-095]purposed at present I shall express my willingness to give up the

[123-096]parish."

[123-097]"Give up the parish altogether?"

[123-098]"Yes, altogether." As he spoke he clasped both his hands together,

[123-099]and having held them for a moment on high, allowed them to fall thus

[123-100]clasped before him. "I cannot give it up in part; I cannot abandon

[123-101]the duties and reserve the honorarium. Nor would I if I could."

[123-102]"I did not mean that, Josiah. But pray think of it before you speak."

[123-103]"I have thought of it, and I will think of it. Farewell, my dear."

[123-104]Then he came up to her and kissed her, and started on his journey on

[123-105]foot to Silverbridge.

[123-106]It was about noon when he reached Silverbridge, and he was told that

[123-107]Doctor Tempest was at home. The servant asked him for a card. "I have

[123-108]no card," said Mr. Crawley, "but I will write my name for your behoof

[123-109]if your master's hospitality will allow me paper and pencil." The

[123-110]name was written, and as Crawley waited in the drawing-room he spent

[123-111]his time in hating Dr. Tempest because the door had been opened by

[123-112]a man-servant dressed in black. Had the man been in livery he would

[123-113]have hated Dr. Tempest all the same. And he would have hated him a

[123-114]little had the door been opened even by a smart maid.

[123-115]"Your letter came to hand yesterday morning, Dr. Tempest," said Mr.

[123-116]Crawley, still standing, though the doctor had pointed to a chair for

[123-117]him after shaking hands with him; "and having given yesterday to the

[123-118]consideration of it, with what judgment I have been able to exercise,

[123-119]I have felt it to be incumbent upon me to wait upon you without

[123-120]further delay, as by doing so I may perhaps assist your views and

[123-121]save labour to those gentlemen who are joined with you in this

[123-122]commission of which you have spoken. To some of them it may possibly

[123-123]be troublesome that they should be brought together here on next

[123-124]Monday."

[123-125]Dr. Tempest had been looking at him during this speech, and could

[123-126]see by his shoes and trowsers that he had walked from Hogglestock to

[123-127]Silverbridge. "Mr. Crawley, will you not sit down?" said he, and then

[123-128]he rang his bell. Mr. Crawley sat down, not on the chair indicated,

[123-129]but on one further removed and at the other side of the table. When

[123-130]the servant came,--the objectionable butler in black clothes that

[123-131]were so much smarter than Mr. Crawley's own,--his master's orders

[123-132]were communicated without any audible word, and the man returned with

[123-133]a decanter and wine-glasses.

[123-134]"After your walk, Mr. Crawley," said Dr. Tempest, getting up from his

[123-135]seat to pour out the wine.

[123-136]"None, I thank you."

[123-137]"Pray let me persuade you. I know the length of the miles so well."

[123-138]"I will take none, if you please, sir," said Mr. Crawley.

[123-139]"Now, Mr. Crawley," said Dr. Tempest, "do let me speak to you as a

[123-140]friend. You have walked eight miles, and are going to talk to me on a

[123-141]subject which is of vital importance to yourself. I won't discuss it

[123-142]unless you'll take a glass of wine and a biscuit."

[123-143]"Dr. Tempest!"

[123-144]"I'm quite in earnest. I won't. If you do as I ask you, you shall

[123-145]talk to me till dinner-time, if you like it. There. Now you may

[123-146]begin."

[123-147]Mr. Crawley did eat the biscuit and did drink the wine, and as he did

[123-148]so, he acknowledged to himself that Dr. Tempest was right. He felt

[123-149]that the wine made him stronger to speak. "I hardly know why you have

[123-150]preferred to-day to next Monday," said Dr. Tempest; "but if anything

[123-151]can be done by your presence here to-day, your time shall not be

[123-152]thrown away."

[123-153]"I have preferred to-day to Monday," said Crawley, "partly because I

[123-154]would sooner talk to one man than to five."

[123-155]"There is something in that, certainly," said Dr. Tempest.

[123-156]"And as I have made up my mind as to the course of action which it

[123-157]is my duty to take in the matter to which your letter of the 9th of

[123-158]this month refers, there can be no reason why I should postpone the

[123-159]declaration of my purpose. Dr. Tempest, I have determined to resign

[123-160]my preferment at Hogglestock, and shall write to-day to the Dean of

[123-161]Barchester, who is the patron, acquainting him of my purpose."

[123-162]"You mean in the event--in the event--"

[123-163]"I mean, sir, to do this without reference to any event that is

[123-164]future. The bishop, Dr. Tempest, when I shall have been proved to be

[123-165]a thief, shall have no trouble either in causing my suspension or

[123-166]my deprivation. The name and fame of a parish clergyman should be

[123-167]unstained. Mine have become foul with infamy. I will not wait to be

[123-168]deprived by any court, by any bishop, or by any commission. I will

[123-169]bow my head to that public opinion which has reached me, and I will

[123-170]deprive myself."

[123-171]He had got up from his chair, and was standing as he pronounced the

[123-172]final sentence against himself. Dr. Tempest still remained seated in

[123-173]his chair, looking at him, and for a few moments there was silence.

[123-174]"You must not do that, Mr. Crawley," Dr. Tempest said at last.

[123-175]"But I shall do it."

[123-176]"Then the dean must not take your resignation. Speaking to you

[123-177]frankly, I tell you that there is no prevailing opinion as to the

[123-178]verdict which the jury may give."
