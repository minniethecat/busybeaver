[020-001]"I'm sure he has, my dear."

[020-002]"Well;--let us hope so. And if he has a conscience, what must be his

[020-003]feelings when he hears that this creature whom he brought into the

[020-004]diocese has been committed to gaol along with common felons."

[020-005]"Not with felons, my dear; at least, I should think not."

[020-006]"I say with common felons! A downright robbery of twenty pounds,

[020-007]just as though he had broken into the bank! And so he did, with

[020-008]sly artifice, which is worse in such hands than a crowbar. And now

[020-009]what are we to do? Here is Thursday, and something must be done

[020-010]before Sunday for the souls of those poor benighted creatures at

[020-011]Hogglestock." Mrs. Proudie was ready for the battle, and was even

[020-012]now sniffing the blood afar-off. "I believe it's a hundred and

[020-013]thirty pounds a year," she said, before the bishop had collected his

[020-014]thoughts sufficiently for a reply.

[020-015]"I think we must find out, first of all, whether he is really to be

[020-016]shut up in prison," said the bishop.

[020-017]"And suppose he is not to be shut up. Suppose they have been weak,

[020-018]or untrue to their duty--and from what we know of the magistrates of

[020-019]Barsetshire, there is too much reason to suppose that they will have

[020-020]been so; suppose they have let him out, is he to go about like a

[020-021]roaring lion--among the souls of the people?"

[020-022]The bishop shook in his shoes. When Mrs. Proudie began to talk of the

[020-023]souls of the people he always shook in his shoes. She had an eloquent

[020-024]way of raising her voice over the word souls that was qualified

[020-025]to make any ordinary man shake in his shoes. The bishop was a

[020-026]conscientious man, and well knew that poor Mr. Crawley, even though

[020-027]he might have become a thief under terrible temptation, would

[020-028]not roar at Hogglestock to the injury of any man's soul. He was

[020-029]aware that this poor clergyman had done his duty laboriously and

[020-030]efficiently, and he was also aware that though he might have been

[020-031]committed by the magistrates, and then let out upon bail, he should

[020-032]not be regarded now, in these days before his trial, as a convicted

[020-033]thief. But to explain all this to Mrs. Proudie was beyond his power.

[020-034]He knew well that she would not hear a word in mitigation of Mr.

[020-035]Crawley's presumed offence. Mr. Crawley belonged to the other party,

[020-036]and Mrs. Proudie was a thorough-going partisan. I know a man,--an

[020-037]excellent fellow, who, being himself a strong politician, constantly

[020-038]expresses a belief that all politicians opposed to him are thieves,

[020-039]child-murderers, parricides, lovers of incest, demons upon the

[020-040]earth. He is a strong partisan, but not, I think, so strong as Mrs.

[020-041]Proudie. He says that he believes all evil of his opponents; but she

[020-042]really believed the evil. The archdeacon had called Mrs. Proudie a

[020-043]she-Beelzebub; but that was a simple ebullition of mortal hatred. He

[020-044]believed her to be simply a vulgar, interfering, brazen-faced virago.

[020-045]Mrs. Proudie in truth believed that the archdeacon was an actual

[020-046]emanation from Satan, sent to those parts to devour souls,--as she

[020-047]would call it,--and that she herself was an emanation of another

[020-048]sort, sent from another source expressly to Barchester, to prevent

[020-049]such devouring, as far as it might possibly be prevented by a mortal

[020-050]agency. The bishop knew it all,--understood it all. He regarded the

[020-051]archdeacon as a clergyman belonging to a party opposed to his party,

[020-052]and he disliked the man. He knew that from his first coming into the

[020-053]diocese he had been encountered with enmity by the archdeacon and

[020-054]the archdeacon's friends. If left to himself he could feel and to a

[020-055]certain extent could resent such enmity. But he had no faith in his

[020-056]wife's doctrine of emanations. He had no faith in many things which

[020-057]she believed religiously;--and yet what could he do? If he attempted

[020-058]to explain, she would stop him before he had got through the first

[020-059]half of his first sentence.

[020-060]"If he is out on bail--," commenced the bishop.

[020-061]"Of course he will be out on bail."

[020-062]"Then I think he should feel--"

[020-063]"Feel! such men never feel! What feeling can one expect from a

[020-064]convicted thief?"

[020-065]"Not convicted as yet, my dear," said the bishop.

[020-066]"A convicted thief," repeated Mrs. Proudie; and she vociferated the

[020-067]words in such a tone that the bishop resolved that he would for the

[020-068]future let the word convicted pass without notice. After all she was

[020-069]only using the phrase in a peculiar sense given to it by herself.

[Illustration: "A convicted thief," repeated Mrs. Proudie.]

[020-070]"It won't be proper, certainly, that he should do the services,"

[020-071]suggested the bishop.

[020-072]"Proper! It would be a scandal to the whole diocese. How could he

[020-073]raise his head as he pronounced the eighth commandment? That must be

[020-074]at least prevented."

[020-075]The bishop, who was seated, fretted himself in his chair, moving

[020-076]about with little movements. He knew that there was a misery coming

[020-077]upon him; and, as far as he could see, it might become a great

[020-078]misery,--a huge blistering sore upon him. When miseries came to him,

[020-079]as they did not unfrequently, he would unconsciously endeavour to

[020-080]fathom them and weigh them, and then, with some gallantry, resolve to

[020-081]bear them, if he could find that their depth and weight were not too

[020-082]great for his powers of endurance. He would let the cold wind whistle

[020-083]by him, putting up the collar of his coat, and would encounter the

[020-084]winter weather without complaint. And he would be patient under the

[020-085]hot sun, knowing well that tranquillity is best for those who have to

[020-086]bear tropical heat. But when the storm threatened to knock him off

[020-087]his legs, when the earth beneath him became too hot for his poor

[020-088]tender feet,--what could he do then? There had been with him such

[020-089]periods of misery, during which he had wailed inwardly and had

[020-090]confessed to himself that the wife of his bosom was too much for him.

[020-091]Now the storm seemed to be coming very roughly. It would be demanded

[020-092]of him that he should exercise certain episcopal authority which he

[020-093]knew did not belong to him. Now, episcopal authority admits of being

[020-094]stretched or contracted according to the character of the bishop who

[020-095]uses it. It is not always easy for a bishop himself to know what he

[020-096]may do, and what he may not do. He may certainly give advice to any

[020-097]clergyman in his diocese, and he may give it in such form that it

[020-098]will have in it something of authority. Such advice coming from a

[020-099]dominant bishop to a clergyman with a submissive mind, has in it very

[020-100]much of authority. But Bishop Proudie knew that Mr. Crawley was not a

[020-101]clergyman with a submissive mind, and he feared that he himself, as

[020-102]regarded from Mr. Crawley's point of view, was not a dominant bishop.

[020-103]And yet he could only act by advice. "I will write to him," said

[020-104]the bishop, "and will explain to him that as he is circumstanced he

[020-105]should not appear in the reading desk."

[020-106]"Of course he must not appear in the reading desk. That scandal must

[020-107]at any rate be inhibited." Now the bishop did not at all like the use

[020-108]of the word inhibited, understanding well that Mrs. Proudie intended

[020-109]it to be understood as implying some episcopal command against which

[020-110]there should be no appeal;--but he let it pass.

[020-111]"I will write to him, my dear, to-night."

[020-112]"And Mr. Thumble can go over with the letter the first thing in the

[020-113]morning."

[020-114]"Will not the post be better?"

[020-115]"No, bishop; certainly not."

[020-116]"He would get it sooner, if I write to-night, my dear."

[020-117]"In either case he will get it to-morrow morning. An hour or two will

[020-118]not signify, and if Mr. Thumble takes it himself we shall know how it

[020-119]is received. It will be well that Thumble should be there in person

[020-120]as he will want to look for lodgings in the parish."

[020-121]"But, my dear--"

[020-122]"Well, bishop?"

[020-123]"[[About]] lodgings? I hardly think that Mr. Thumble, if we decide that

[020-124]Mr. Thumble shall undertake the duty--"

[020-125]"We have decided that Mr. Thumble should undertake the duty. That is

[020-126]decided."

[020-127]"But I do not think he should trouble himself to look for lodgings at

[020-128]Hogglestock. He can go over on the Sundays."

[020-129]"And who is to do the parish work? Would you have that man, a

[020-130]convicted thief, to look after the schools, and visit the sick, and

[020-131]perhaps attend the dying?"

[020-132]"There will be a great difficulty; there will indeed," said the

[020-133]bishop, becoming very unhappy, and feeling that he was driven by

[020-134]circumstances either to assert his own knowledge or teach his wife

[020-135]something of the law with reference to his position as a bishop. "Who

[020-136]is to pay Mr. Thumble?"

[020-137]"The income of the parish must be sequestrated, and he must be paid

[020-138]out of that. Of course he must have the income while he does the

[020-139]work."

[020-140]"But, my dear, I cannot sequestrate the man's income."

[020-141]"I don't believe it, bishop. If the bishop cannot sequestrate, who

[020-142]can? But you are always timid in exercising the authority put into

[020-143]your hands for wise purposes. Not sequestrate the income of a man

[020-144]who has been proved to be a thief! You leave that to us, and we

[020-145]will manage it." The "us" here named comprised Mrs. Proudie and the

[020-146]bishop's managing chaplain.

[020-147]Then the bishop was left alone for an hour to write the letter which

[020-148]Mr. Thumble was to carry over to Mr. Crawley,--and after a while

[020-149]he did write it. Before he commenced the task, however, he sat for

[020-150]some moments in his arm-chair close by the fire-side, asking himself

[020-151]whether it might not be possible for him to overcome his enemy in

[020-152]this matter. How would it go with him suppose he were to leave the

[020-153]letter unwritten, and send in a message by his chaplain to Mrs.

[020-154]Proudie, saying that as Mr. Crawley was out on bail, the parish might

[020-155]be left for the present without episcopal interference? She could not

[020-156]make him interfere. She could not force him to write the letter. So,

[020-157]at least, he said to himself. But as he said it, he almost thought

[020-158]that she could do these things. In the last thirty years, or more,

[020-159]she had ever contrived by some power latent in her to have her will

[020-160]effected. But what would happen if now, even now, he were to rebel?

[020-161]That he would personally become very uncomfortable, he was well

[020-162]aware, but he thought that he could bear that. The food would become

[020-163]bad,--mere ashes between his teeth, the daily modicum of wine would

[020-164]lose its flavour, the chimneys would all smoke, the wind would come

[020-165]from the east, and the servants would not answer the bell. Little

[020-166]miseries of that kind would crowd upon him. He had arrived at a time

[020-167]of life in which such miseries make such men very miserable; but yet

[020-168]he thought that he could endure them. And what other wretchedness

[020-169]would come to him? She would scold him,--frightfully, loudly,

[020-170]scornfully, and worse than all, continually. But of this he had so

[020-171]much habitually, that anything added might be borne also;--if only he

[020-172]could be sure that the scoldings should go on in private, that the

[020-173]world of the palace should not be allowed to hear the revilings to

[020-174]which he would be subjected. But to be scolded publicly was the great

[020-175]evil which he dreaded beyond all evils. He was well aware that the

[020-176]palace would know his misfortune, that it was known, and freely

[020-177]discussed by all, from the examining chaplain down to the palace

[020-178]boot-boy;--nay, that it was known to all the diocese; but yet he

[020-179]could smile upon those around him, and look as though he held his

[020-180]own like other men,--unless when open violence was displayed. But

[020-181]when that voice was heard aloud along the corridors of the palace,

[020-182]and when he was summoned imperiously by the woman, calling for her

[020-183]bishop, so that all Barchester heard it, and when he was compelled

[020-184]to creep forth from his study, at the sound of that summons, with

[020-185]distressed face, and shaking hands, and short hurrying steps,--a

[020-186]being to be pitied even by a deacon,--not venturing to assume

[020-187]an air of masterdom should he chance to meet a housemaid on the

[020-188]stairs,--then, at such moments as that, he would feel that any

[020-189]submission was better than the misery which he suffered. And he well

[020-190]knew that should he now rebel, the whole house would be in a turmoil.

[020-191]He would be bishoped here, and bishoped there, before the eyes of all

[020-192]palatial men and women, till life would be a burden to him. So he got

[020-193]up from his seat over the fire, and went to his desk and wrote the

[020-194]letter. The letter was as follows:--
