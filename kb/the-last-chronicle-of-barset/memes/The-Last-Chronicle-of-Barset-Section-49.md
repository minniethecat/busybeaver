[049-001]"If you mean about Miss Van Siever and yourself, I was quite in

[049-002]earnest, Conway. I do not think you could do better, and I should be

[049-003]glad to see it of all things. Nothing would please me more than to

[049-004]bring Miss Van Siever and you together."

[049-005]"And nothing would please me less."

[049-006]"But why so?"

[049-007]"Because,--because--. I can do nothing but tell you the truth,

[049-008]carina; because my heart is not free to present itself at Miss Van

[049-009]Siever's feet."

[049-010]"It ought to be free, Conway, and you must make it free. It will

[049-011]be well that you should be married, and well for others besides

[049-012]yourself. I tell you so as your friend, and you have no truer friend.

[049-013]Sit where you are, if you please. You can say anything you have to

[049-014]say without stalking about the room."

[049-015]"I was not going to stalk,--as you call it."

[049-016]"You will be safer and quieter while you are sitting. I heard a knock

[049-017]at the door, and I do not doubt that it is Clara. She said she would

[049-018]be here."

[049-019]"And you have told her of the picture?"

[049-020]"Yes; I have told her. She said that it would be impossible, and that

[049-021]her mother would not allow it. Here she is." Then Miss Van Siever

[049-022]was shown into the room, and Dalrymple perceived that she was a girl

[049-023]the peculiarity of whose complexion bore daylight better even than

[049-024]candlelight. There was something in her countenance which seemed to

[049-025]declare that she could bear any light to which it might be subjected

[049-026]without flinching from it. And her bonnet, which was very plain,

[049-027]and her simple brown morning gown, suited her well. She was one who

[049-028]required none of the circumstances of studied dress to carry off

[049-029]aught in her own appearance. She could look her best when other women

[049-030]look their worst, and could dare to be seen at all times. Dalrymple,

[049-031]with an artist's eye, saw this at once, and immediately confessed to

[049-032]himself that there was something great about her. He could not deny

[049-033]her beauty. But there was ever present to him that look of hardness

[049-034]which had struck him when he first saw her. He could not but fancy

[049-035]that though at times she might be playful, and allow the fur of

[049-036]her coat to be stroked with good-humour,--she would be a dangerous

[049-037]plaything, using her claws unpleasantly when the good-humour should

[049-038]have passed away. But not the less was she beautiful, and--beyond

[049-039]that and better than that, for his purpose,--she was picturesque.

[049-040]"Clara," said Mrs. Broughton, "here is this mad painter, and he says

[049-041]that he will have you on his canvas, either with your will or without

[049-042]it."

[049-043]"Even if he could do that, I am sure he would not," said Miss Van

[049-044]Siever.

[049-045]"To prove to you that I can, I think I need only show you the

[049-046]sketch," said Dalrymple, taking the drawing out of his pocket. "As

[049-047]regards the face, I know it so well by heart already, that I feel

[049-048]certain I could produce a likeness without even a sitting. What do

[049-049]you think of it, Mrs. Broughton?"

[Illustration: "What do you think of it, Mrs. Broughton?"]

[049-050]"It is clever," said she, looking at it with all that enthusiasm

[049-051]which women are able to throw into their eyes on such occasions;

[049-052]"very clever. The subject would just suit her. I have never doubted

[049-053]that."

[049-054]"Eames says that it is confused," said the artist.

[049-055]"I don't see that at all," said Mrs. Broughton.

[049-056]"Of course a sketch must be rough. This one has been rubbed about and

[049-057]altered,--but I think there is something in it."

[049-058]"An immense deal," said Mrs. Broughton. "Don't you think so, Clara?"

[049-059]"I am not a judge."

[049-060]"But you can see the woman's fixed purpose; and her stealthiness as

[049-061]well;--and the man sleeps like a log. What is that dim outline?"

[049-062]"Nothing in particular," said Dalrymple. But the dim outline was

[049-063]intended to represent Mrs. Van Siever.

[049-064]"It is very good,--unquestionably good," said Mrs. Dobbs Broughton.

[049-065]"I do not for a moment doubt that you would make a great picture of

[049-066]it. It is just the subject for you, Conway; so much imagination, and

[049-067]yet such a scope for portraiture. It would be full of action, and yet

[049-068]such perfect repose. And the lights and shadows would be exactly in

[049-069]your line. I can see at a glance how you would manage the light in

[049-070]the tent, and bring it down just on the nail. And then the pose of

[049-071]the woman would be so good, so much strength, and yet such grace!

[049-072]You should have the bowl he drank the milk out of, so as to tell

[049-073]the whole story. No painter living tells a story so well as you do,

[049-074]Conway." Conway Dalrymple knew that the woman was talking nonsense to

[049-075]him, and yet he liked it, and liked her for talking it.

[049-076]"But Mr. Dalrymple can paint his Sisera without making me a Jael,"

[049-077]said Miss Van Siever.

[049-078]"Of course he can," said Mrs. Broughton.

[049-079]"But I never will," said the artist. "I conceived the subject as

[049-080]connected with you, and I will never disjoin the two ideas."

[049-081]"I think it no compliment, I can assure you," said Miss Van Siever.

[049-082]"And none was intended. But you may observe that artists in all ages

[049-083]have sought for higher types of models in painting women who have

[049-084]been violent or criminal, than have sufficed for them in their

[049-085]portraitures of gentleness and virtue. Look at all the Judiths, and

[049-086]the Lucretias, and the Charlotte Cordays; how much finer the women

[049-087]are than the Madonnas and the Saint Cecilias."

[049-088]"After that, Clara, you need not scruple to be a Jael," said Mrs.

[049-089]Broughton.

[049-090]"But I do scruple,--very much; so strongly that I know I never shall

[049-091]do it. In the first place I don't know why Mr. Dalrymple wants it."

[049-092]"Want it!" said Conway. "I want to paint a striking picture."

[049-093]"But you can do that without putting me into it."

[049-094]"No;--not this picture. And why should you object? It is the

[049-095]commonest thing in the world for ladies to sit to artists in that

[049-096]manner."

[049-097]"People would know it."

[049-098]"Nobody would know it, so that you need care about it. What would

[049-099]it matter if everybody knew it? We are not proposing anything

[049-100]improper;--are we, Mrs. Broughton?"

[049-101]"She shall not be pressed if she does not like it," said Mrs.

[049-102]Broughton. "You know I told you before Clara came in, that I was

[049-103]afraid it could not be done."

[049-104]"And I don't like it," said Miss Van Siever, with some little

[049-105]hesitation in her voice.

[049-106]"I don't see anything improper in it, if you mean that," said Mrs.

[049-107]Broughton.

[049-108]"But, mamma!"

[049-109]"Well, yes; that is the difficulty, no doubt. The only question is,

[049-110]whether your mother is not so very singular, as to make it impossible

[049-111]that you should comply with her in everything."

[049-112]"I am afraid that I do not comply with her in very much," said Miss

[049-113]Van Siever in her gentlest voice.

[049-114]"Oh, Clara!"

[049-115]"You drive me to say so, as otherwise I should be a hypocrite. Of

[049-116]course I ought not to have said it before Mr. Dalrymple."

[049-117]"You and Mr. Dalrymple will understand all about that, I daresay,

[049-118]before the picture is finished," said Mrs. Broughton.

[049-119]It did not take much persuasion on the part of Conway Dalrymple to

[049-120]get the consent of the younger lady to be painted, or of the elder to

[049-121]allow the sitting to go on in her room. When the question of easels

[049-122]and other apparatus came to be considered Mrs. Broughton was rather

[049-123]flustered, and again declared with energy that the whole thing

[049-124]must fall to the ground; but a few more words from the painter

[049-125]restored her, and at last the arrangements were made. As Mrs. Dobbs

[049-126]Broughton's dear friend, Madalina Demolines had said, Mrs. Dobbs

[049-127]Broughton liked a fevered existence. "What will Dobbs say?" she

[049-128]exclaimed more than once. And it was decided at last that Dobbs

[049-129]should know nothing about it as long as it could be kept from him.

[049-130]"Of course he shall be told at last," said his wife. "I wouldn't keep

[049-131]anything from the dear fellow for all the world. But if he knew it at

[049-132]first it would be sure to get through Musselboro to your mother."

[049-133]"I certainly shall beg that Mr. Broughton may not be taken into

[049-134]confidence if Mr. Musselboro is to follow," said Clara. "And it must

[049-135]be understood that I must cease to sit immediately, whatever may be

[049-136]the inconvenience, should mamma speak to me about it."

[049-137]This stipulation was made and conceded, and then Miss Van Siever went

[049-138]away, leaving the artist with Mrs. Dobbs Broughton. "And now, if you

[049-139]please, Conway, you had better go too," said the lady, as soon as

[049-140]there had been time for Miss Van Siever to get downstairs and out of

[049-141]the hall-door.

[049-142]"Of course you are in a hurry to get rid of me."

[049-143]"Yes, I am."

[049-144]"A little while ago I improperly said that some suggestion of yours

[049-145]was nonsense and you rebuked me for my blunt incivility. Might not I

[049-146]rebuke you now with equal justice?"

[049-147]"Do so, if you will;--but leave me. I tell you, Conway, that in these

[049-148]matters you must either be guided by me, or you and I must cease to

[049-149]see each other. It does not do that you should remain here with me

[049-150]longer than the time usually allowed for a morning call. Clara has

[049-151]come and gone, and you also must go. I am sorry to disturb you, for

[049-152]you seem to be so very comfortable in that chair."

[049-153]"I am comfortable,--and I can look at you. Come;--there can be no

[049-154]harm in saying that, if I say nothing else. Well;--there, now I am

[049-155]gone." Whereupon he got up from his arm-chair.

[049-156]"But you are not gone while you stand there."

[049-157]"And you would really wish me to marry that girl?"

[049-158]"I do,--if you can love her."

[049-159]"And what about her love?"

[049-160]"You must win it, of course. She is to be won, like any other woman.

[049-161]The fruit won't fall into your mouth merely because you open your

[049-162]lips. You must climb the tree."

[049-163]"Still climbing trees in the Hesperides," said Conway. "Love does

[049-164]that, you know; but it is hard to climb the trees without the love.

[049-165]It seems to me that I have done my climbing,--have clomb as high as

[049-166]I knew how, and that the boughs are breaking with me, and that I am

[049-167]likely to get a fall. Do you understand me?"

[049-168]"I would rather not understand you."

[049-169]"That is no answer to my question. Do you understand that at this

[049-170]moment I am getting a fall which will break every bone in my skin and

[049-171]put any other climbing out of the question as far as I am concerned?

[049-172]Do you understand that?"

[049-173]"No; I do not," said Mrs. Broughton, in a tremulous voice.

[049-174]"Then I'll go and make love at once to Clara Van Siever. There's

[049-175]enough of pluck left in me to ask her to marry me, and I suppose I

[049-176]could manage to go through the ceremony if she accepted me."

[049-177]"But I want you to love her," said Mrs. Dobbs Broughton.

[049-178]"I daresay I should love her well enough after a bit;--that is, if

[049-179]she didn't break my head or comb my hair. I suppose there will be no

[049-180]objection to my saying that you sent me when I ask her?"

[049-181]"Conway, you will of course not mention my name to her. I have

[049-182]suggested to you a marriage which I think would tend to make you

[049-183]happy, and would give you a stability in life which you want. It is

[049-184]perhaps better that I should be explicit at once. As an unmarried

[049-185]man I cannot continue to know you. You have said words of late which

[049-186]have driven me to this conclusion. I have thought about it much,--too

[049-187]much, perhaps, and I know that I am right. Miss Van Siever has beauty

[049-188]and wealth and intellect, and I think that she would appreciate the

[049-189]love of such a man as you are. Now go." And Mrs. Dobbs Broughton,

[049-190]standing upright, pointed to the door. Conway Dalrymple slowly took

[049-191]his Spanish hat from off the marble slab on which he had laid it, and

[049-192]left the room without saying a word. The interview had been quite

[049-193]long enough, and there was nothing else which he knew how to say with

[049-194]effect.
