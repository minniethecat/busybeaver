[059-001]By this time Mr. Crawley was almost beside himself, and was

[059-002]altogether at a loss how to bring in the matter on which he wished

[059-003]to speak. He had expected to find a man who in the hurry of London

[059-004]business might perhaps just manage to spare him five minutes,--who

[059-005]would grapple instantly with the subject that was to be discussed

[059-006]between them, would speak to him half-a-dozen hard words of wisdom,

[059-007]and would then dismiss him and turn on the instant to other matters

[059-008]of important business;--but here was an easy familiar fellow, who

[059-009]seemed to have nothing on earth to do, and who at this first meeting

[059-010]had taken advantage of a distant family connexion to tell him

[059-011]everything about the affairs of his own household. And then how

[059-012]peculiar were the domestic traits which he told! What was Mr. Crawley

[059-013]to say to a man who had taught his own children to call their mother

[059-014]Thais? Of Thais Mr. Crawley did know something, and he forgot to

[059-015]remember that perhaps Mr. Toogood knew less. He felt it, however, to

[059-016]be very difficult to submit the details of his case to a gentleman

[059-017]who talked in such a strain about his own wife and children.

[059-018]But something must be done. Mr. Crawley, in his present frame of

[059-019]mind, could not sit and talk about Thais all day. "Sir," he said,

[059-020]"the picture of your home is very pleasant, and I presume that plenty

[059-021]abounds there."

[059-022]"Well, you know, pretty toll-loll for that. With twelve of 'em, Mr.

[059-023]Crawley, I needn't tell you they are not all going to have castles

[059-024]and parks of their own, unless they can get 'em off their own bats.

[059-025]But I pay upwards of a hundred a year each for my eldest three boys'

[059-026]schooling, and I've been paying eighty for the girls. Put that and

[059-027]that together and see what it comes to. Educate, educate, educate;

[059-028]that's my word."

[059-029]"No better word can be spoken, sir."

[059-030]"I don't think there's a girl in Tavistock Square that can beat

[059-031]Polly,--she's the eldest, called after her mother, you know;--that

[059-032]can beat her at the piano. And Lucy has read Lord Byron and Tom Moore

[059-033]all through, every word of 'em. By Jove, I believe she knows most of

[059-034]Tom Moore by heart. And the young uns are coming on just as well."

[059-035]"Perhaps, sir, as your time is, no doubt, precious--"

[059-036]"Just at this time of the day we don't care so much about it, Mr.

[059-037]Crawley; and one doesn't catch a new cousin every day, you know."

[059-038]"However, if you will allow me,--"

[059-039]"We'll tackle to? Very well; so be it. Now, Mr. Crawley, let me hear

[059-040]what it is that I can do for you." Of a sudden, as Mr. Toogood spoke

[059-041]these last words, the whole tone of his voice seemed to change, and

[059-042]even the position of his body became so much altered as to indicate a

[059-043]different kind of man. "You just tell your story in your own way, and

[059-044]I won't interrupt you till you've done. That's always the best."

[059-045]"I must first crave your attention to an unfortunate preliminary,"

[059-046]said Mr. Crawley.

[059-047]"And what is that?"

[059-048]"I come before you in formâ pauperis." Here Mr. Crawley paused and

[059-049]stood up before the attorney with his hands crossed one upon the

[059-050]other, bending low, as though calling attention to the poorness of

[059-051]his raiment. "I know that I have no justification for my conduct. I

[059-052]have nothing of reason to offer why I should trespass upon your time.

[059-053]I am a poor man, and cannot pay you for your services."

[059-054]"Oh, bother!" said Mr. Toogood, jumping up out of his chair.

[059-055]"I do not know whether your charity will grant me that which I ask--"

[059-056]"Don't let's have any more of this," said the attorney. "We none of

[059-057]us like this kind of thing at all. If I can be of any service to you,

[059-058]you're as welcome to it as flowers in May; and as for billing my

[059-059]first-cousin, which your wife is, I should as soon think of sending

[059-060]in an account to my own."

[059-061]"But, Mr. Toogood,--"

[059-062]"Do you go on now with your story; I'll put the rest all right."

[059-063]"I was bound to be explicit, Mr. Toogood."

[059-064]"Very well; now you have been explicit with a vengeance, and you may

[059-065]heave a-head. Let's hear the story, and if I can help you I will.

[059-066]When I've said that, you may be sure I mean it. I've heard something

[059-067]of it before; but let me hear it all from you."

[059-068]Then Mr. Crawley began and told the story. Mr. Toogood was actually

[059-069]true to his promise and let the narrator go on with his narrative

[059-070]without interruption. When Mr. Crawley came to his own statement that

[059-071]the cheque had been paid to him by Mr. Soames, and went on to say

[059-072]that that statement had been false,--"I told him that, but I told

[059-073]him so wrongly," and then paused, thinking that the lawyer would ask

[059-074]some question, Mr. Toogood simply said, "Go on; go on. I'll come back

[059-075]to all that when you've done." And he merely nodded his head when

[059-076]Mr. Crawley spoke of his second statement, that the money had come

[059-077]from the dean. "We had been bound together by close ties of early

[059-078]familiarity," said Mr. Crawley, "and in former years our estates in

[059-079]life were the same. But he has prospered and I have failed. And when

[059-080]creditors were importunate, I consented to accept relief in money

[059-081]which had previously been often offered. And I must acknowledge,

[059-082]Mr. Toogood, while saying this, that I have known,--have known with

[059-083]heartfelt agony,--that at former times my wife has taken that from

[059-084]my friend Mr. Arabin, with hand half-hidden from me, which I have

[059-085]refused. Whether it be better to eat--the bread of charity,--or

[059-086]not to eat bread at all, I, for myself, have no doubt," he said;

[059-087]"but when the want strikes one's wife and children, and the charity

[059-088]strikes only oneself, then there is a doubt." When he spoke thus, Mr.

[059-089]Toogood got up, and thrusting his hands into his waistcoat pockets

[059-090]walked about the room, exclaiming, "By George, by George, by George!"

[059-091]But he still let the man go on with his story, and heard him out at

[059-092]last to the end.

[059-093]"And they committed you for trial at the next Barchester assizes?"

[059-094]said the lawyer.

[059-095]"They did."

[059-096]"And you employed no lawyer before the magistrates?"

[059-097]"None;--I refused to employ any one."

[059-098]"You were wrong there, Mr. Crawley. I must be allowed to say that you

[059-099]were wrong there."

[059-100]"I may possibly have been so from your point of view, Mr. Toogood;

[059-101]but permit me to explain. I--"

[059-102]"It's no good explaining now. Of course you must employ a lawyer for

[059-103]your defence,--an attorney who will put the case into the hands of

[059-104]counsel."

[059-105]"But that I cannot do, Mr. Toogood."

[059-106]"You must do it. If you don't do it, your friends should do it for

[059-107]you. If you don't do it, everybody will say you're mad. There isn't

[059-108]a single solicitor you could find within half a mile of you at this

[059-109]moment who wouldn't give you the same advice,--not a single man,

[059-110]either, who has got a head on his shoulders worth a turnip."

[059-111]When Mr. Crawley was told that madness would be laid to his charge if

[059-112]he did not do as he was bid, his face became very black, and assumed

[059-113]something of that look of determined obstinacy which it had worn when

[059-114]he was standing in the presence of the bishop and Mrs. Proudie. "It

[059-115]may be so," he said. "It may be as you say, Mr. Toogood. But these

[059-116]neighbours of yours, as to whose collected wisdom you speak with so

[059-117]much certainty, would hardly recommend me to indulge in a luxury for

[059-118]which I have no means of paying."

[059-119]"Who thinks about paying under such circumstances as these?"

[059-120]"I do, Mr. Toogood."

[059-121]"The wretchedest costermonger that comes to grief has a barrister in

[059-122]a wig and gown to give him his chance of escape."

[059-123]"But I am not a costermonger, Mr. Toogood,--though more wretched

[059-124]perhaps than any costermonger now in existence. It is my lot to have

[059-125]to endure the sufferings of poverty, and at the same time not to

[059-126]be exempt from those feelings of honour to which poverty is seldom

[059-127]subject. I cannot afford to call in legal assistance for which I

[059-128]cannot pay,--and I will not do it."

[059-129]"I'll carry the case through for you. It certainly is not just my

[059-130]line of business,--but I'll see it carried through for you."

[059-131]"Out of your own pocket?"

[059-132]"Never mind; when I say I'll do a thing, I'll do it."

[059-133]"No, Mr. Toogood; this thing you can not do. But do not suppose I am

[059-134]the less grateful."

[059-135]"What is it I can do then? Why do you come to me if you won't take my

[059-136]advice?"

[059-137]After this the conversation went on for a considerable time without

[059-138]touching on any point which need be brought palpably before the

[059-139]reader's eye. The attorney continued to beg the clergyman to have his

[059-140]case managed in the usual way, and went so far as to tell him that

[059-141]he would be ill-treating his wife and family if he continued to be

[059-142]obstinate. But the clergyman was not shaken from his resolve, and was

[059-143]at last able to ask Mr. Toogood what he had better do,--how he had

[059-144]better attempt to defend himself,--on the understanding that no legal

[059-145]aid was to be employed. When this question was at last asked in such

[059-146]a way as to demand an answer, Mr. Toogood sat for a moment or two in

[059-147]silence. He felt that an answer was not only demanded, but almost

[059-148]enforced; and yet there might be much difficulty in giving it.

[059-149]"Mr. Toogood," said Mr. Crawley, seeing the attorney's hesitation, "I

[059-150]declare to you before God, that my only object will be to enable the

[059-151]jury to know about this sad matter all that I know myself. If I could

[059-152]open my breast to them I should be satisfied. But then a prisoner can

[059-153]say nothing; and what he does say is ever accounted false."

[059-154]"That is why you should have legal assistance."

[059-155]"We had already come to a conclusion on that matter, as I thought,"

[059-156]said Mr. Crawley.

[059-157]Mr. Toogood paused for another moment or two, and then dashed at his

[059-158]answer; or rather, dashed at a counter question. "Mr. Crawley, where

[059-159]did you get the cheque? You must pardon me, you know; or, if you wish

[059-160]it, I will not press the question. But so much hangs on that, you

[059-161]know."

[059-162]"Every thing would hang on it,--if I only knew."

[059-163]"You mean that you forget?"

[059-164]"Absolutely; totally. I wish, Mr. Toogood, I could explain to you the

[059-165]toilsome perseverance with which I have cudgelled my poor brains,

[059-166]endeavouring to extract from them some scintilla of memory that would

[059-167]aid me."

[059-168]"Could you have picked it up in the house?"

[059-169]"No;--no; that I did not do. Dull as I am, I know so much. It was

[059-170]mine of right, from whatever source it came to me. I know myself as

[059-171]no one else can know me, in spite of the wise man's motto. Had I

[059-172]picked up a cheque in my house, or on the road, I should not have

[059-173]slept till I had taken steps to restore it to the seeming owner. So

[059-174]much I can say. But, otherwise, I am in such matters so shandy-pated,

[059-175]that I can trust myself to be sure of nothing. I thought;--I

[059-176]certainly thought--"

[059-177]"You thought what?"

[059-178]"I thought that it had been given to me by my friend the dean. I

[059-179]remember well that I was in his library at Barchester, and I was

[059-180]somewhat provoked in spirit. There were lying on the floor hundreds

[059-181]of volumes, all glittering with gold, and reeking with new leather

[059-182]from the binders. He asked me to look at his toys. Why should I look

[059-183]at them? There was a time, but the other day it seemed, when he had

[059-184]been glad to borrow from me such treasures as I had. And it seemed to

[059-185]me that he was heartless in showing me these things. Well; I need not

[059-186]trouble you with all that."
