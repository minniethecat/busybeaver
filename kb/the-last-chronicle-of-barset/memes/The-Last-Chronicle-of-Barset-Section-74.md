[074-001]"Upon my word I do not see the harm of the picture," said he.

[074-002]"You do not?"

[074-003]"Indeed, no. Why should not Dalrymple paint Miss Van Siever as well

[074-004]as any other lady? It is his special business to paint ladies."

[074-005]"Look here, Mr. Eames.--" And now Miss Demolines, as she spoke, drew

[074-006]her own seat closer to that of her companion and pushed away the

[074-007]little table. "Do you suppose that Conway Dalrymple, in the usual

[074-008]way of his business, paints pictures of young ladies, of which their

[074-009]mothers know nothing? Do you suppose that he paints them in ladies'

[074-010]rooms without their husbands' knowledge? And in the common way of his

[074-011]business does he not expect to be paid for his pictures?"

[074-012]"But what is all that to you and me, Miss Demolines?"

[074-013]"Is the welfare of your friend nothing to you? Would you like to see

[074-014]him become the victim of the artifice of such a girl as Clara Van

[074-015]Siever?"

[074-016]"Upon my word I think he is very well able to take care of himself."

[074-017]"And would you wish to see that poor creature's domestic hearth

[074-018]ruined and broken up?"

[074-019]"Which poor creature?"

[074-020]"Dobbs Broughton, to be sure."

[074-021]"I can't pretend that I care very much for Dobbs Broughton," said

[074-022]John Eames; "and you see I know so little about his domestic hearth."

[074-023]"Oh, Mr. Eames!"

[074-024]"Besides, her principles will pull her through. You told me yourself

[074-025]that Mrs. Broughton has high principles."

[074-026]"God forbid that I should say a word against Maria Clutterbuck," said

[074-027]Miss Demolines, fervently. "Maria Clutterbuck was my early friend,

[074-028]and though words have been spoken which never should have been

[074-029]spoken, and though things have been done which never should have been

[074-030]even dreamed of, still I will not desert Maria Clutterbuck in her

[074-031]hour of need. No, never!"

[074-032]"I'm sure you're what one may call a trump to your friends, Miss

[074-033]Demolines."

[074-034]"I have always endeavoured to be so, and always shall. You will find

[074-035]me so;--that is if you and I ever become intimate enough to feel that

[074-036]sort of friendship."

[074-037]"There's nothing on earth I should like better," said Johnny. As

[074-038]soon as the words were out of his mouth he felt ashamed of himself.

[074-039]He knew that he did not in truth desire the friendship of Miss

[074-040]Demolines, and that any friendship with such a one would mean

[074-041]something different from friendship,--something that would be an

[074-042]injury to Lily Dale. A week had hardly passed since he had sworn a

[074-043]life's constancy to Lily Dale,--had sworn it, not to her only, but to

[074-044]himself; and now he was giving way to a flirtation with this woman,

[074-045]not because he liked it himself, but because he was too weak to keep

[074-046]out of it.

[074-047]"If that is true--," said Miss Demolines.

[074-048]"Oh, yes; it's quite true," said Johnny.

[074-049]"Then you must earn my friendship by doing what I ask of you. That

[074-050]picture must not be painted. You must tell Conway Dalrymple as his

[074-051]friend that he must cease to carry on such an intrigue in another

[074-052]man's house."

[074-053]"You would hardly call painting a picture an intrigue; would you?"

[074-054]"Certainly I would when it's kept a secret from the husband by the

[074-055]wife,--and from the mother by the daughter. If it cannot be stopped

[074-056]in any other way, I must tell Mrs. Van Siever;--I must, indeed. I

[074-057]have such an abhorrence of the old woman, that I could not bring

[074-058]myself to speak to her,--but I should write to her. That's what I

[074-059]should do."

[074-060]"But what's the reason? You might as well tell me the real reason."

[074-061]Had Miss Demolines been christened Mary, or Fanny, or Jane, I think

[074-062]that John Eames would now have called her by either of those names;

[074-063]but Madalina was such a mouthful that he could not bring himself to

[074-064]use it at once. He had heard that among her intimates she was called

[074-065]Maddy. He had an idea that he had heard Dalrymple in old times talk

[074-066]of her as Maddy Mullins, and just at this moment the idea was not

[074-067]pleasant to him; at any rate he could not call her Maddy as yet. "How

[074-068]am I to help you," he said, "unless I know all about it?"

[074-069]"I hate that girl like poison!" said Miss Demolines, confidentially,

[074-070]drawing herself very near to Johnny as she spoke.

[074-071]"But what has she done?"

[074-072]"What has she done? I can't tell you what she has done. I could not

[074-073]demean myself by repeating it. Of course we all know what she wants.

[074-074]She wants to catch Conway Dalrymple. That's as plain as anything can

[074-075]be. Not that I care about that."

[074-076]"Of course not," said Johnny.

[074-077]"Not in the least. It's nothing to me. I have known Mr. Dalrymple,

[074-078]no doubt, for a year or two, and I should be sorry to see a young

[074-079]man who has his good points sacrificed in that sort of way. But it

[074-080]is mere acquaintance between Mr. Dalrymple and me, and of course I

[074-081]cannot interfere."

[074-082]"She'll have a lot of money, you know."

[074-083]"He thinks so; does he? I suppose that is what Maria has told him.

[074-084]Oh, Mr. Eames, you don't know the meanness of women; you don't,

[074-085]indeed. Men are so much more noble."

[074-086]"Are they, do you think?"

[074-087]"Than some women. I see women doing things that really disgust

[074-088]me; I do, indeed;--things that I wouldn't do myself, were it ever

[074-089]so;--striving to catch men in every possible way, and for such

[074-090]purposes! I wouldn't have believed it of Maria Clutterbuck. I

[074-091]wouldn't indeed. However, I will never say a word against her,

[074-092]because she has been my friend. Nothing shall ever induce me."

[074-093]John Eames before he left Porchester Terrace, had at last succeeded

[074-094]in calling his fair friend Madalina, and had promised that he would

[074-095]endeavour to open the artist's eyes to the folly of painting his

[074-096]picture in Broughton's house without Broughton's knowledge.

[074-097]CHAPTER XL.

[074-098]MR. TOOGOOD'S IDEAS ABOUT SOCIETY.

[074-099]A day or two after the interview which was described in the last

[074-100]chapter John Eames dined with his uncle Mr. Thomas Toogood, in

[074-101]Tavistock Square. He was in the habit of doing this about once a

[074-102]month, and was a great favourite both with his cousins and with their

[074-103]mother. Mr. Toogood did not give dinner-parties; always begging

[074-104]those whom he asked to enjoy his hospitality, to take pot luck, and

[074-105]telling young men whom he could treat with familiarity,--such as his

[074-106]nephew,--that if they wanted to be regaled à la Russe they must not

[074-107]come to number 75, Tavistock Square. "A leg of mutton and trimmings;

[074-108]that will be about the outside of it," he would say; but he would add

[074-109]in a whisper,--"and a glass of port such as you don't get every day

[074-110]of your life." Polly and Lucy Toogood were pretty girls, and merry

[074-111]withal, and certain young men were well contented to accept the

[074-112]attorney's invitations,--whether attracted by the promised leg of

[074-113]mutton, or the port wine, or the young ladies, I will not attempt to

[074-114]say. But it had so happened that one young man, a clerk from John

[074-115]Eames' office, had partaken so often of the pot luck and port wine

[074-116]that Polly Toogood had conquered him by her charms, and he was now a

[074-117]slave, waiting an appropriate time for matrimonial sacrifice. William

[074-118]Summerkin was the young man's name; and as it was known that Mr.

[074-119]Summerkin was to inherit a fortune amounting to five thousand pounds

[074-120]from his maiden aunt, it was considered that Polly Toogood was not

[074-121]doing amiss. "I'll give you three hundred pounds, my boy, just to put

[074-122]a few sheets on the beds," said Toogood the father, "and when the old

[074-123]birds are both dead she'll have a thousand pounds out of the nest.

[074-124]That's the extent of Polly's fortune;--so now you know." Summerkin

[074-125]was, however, quite contented to have his own money settled on his

[074-126]darling Polly, and the whole thing was looked at with pleasant and

[074-127]propitious eyes by the Toogood connection.

[074-128]When John Eames entered the drawing-room Summerkin and Polly were

[074-129]already there. Summerkin blushed up to his eyes, of course, but Polly

[074-130]sat as demurely as though she had been accustomed to having lovers

[074-131]all her life. "Mamma will be down almost immediately, John," said

[074-132]Polly as soon as the first greetings were over, "and papa has come

[074-133]in, I know."

[074-134]"Summerkin," said Johnny, "I'm afraid you left the office before four

[074-135]o'clock."

[074-136]"No, I did not," said Summerkin. "I deny it."

[074-137]"Polly," said her cousin, "you should keep him in better order. He

[074-138]will certainly come to grief if he goes on like this. I suppose you

[074-139]could do without him for half an hour."

[074-140]"I don't want him, I can assure you," said Polly.

[074-141]"I have only been here just five minutes," said Summerkin, "and I

[074-142]came because Mrs. Toogood asked me to do a commission."

[074-143]"That's civil to you, Polly," said John.

[074-144]"It's quite as civil as I wish him to be," said Polly. "And as for

[074-145]you, John, everybody knows that you're a goose, and that you always

[074-146]were a goose. Isn't he always doing foolish things at the office,

[074-147]William?" But as John Eames was rather a great man at the Income-tax

[074-148]Office, Summerkin would not fall into his sweetheart's joke on this

[074-149]subject, finding it easier and perhaps safer to twiddle the bodkins

[074-150]in Polly's work-basket. Then Toogood and Mrs. Toogood entered the

[074-151]room together, and the lovers were able to be alone again during the

[074-152]general greeting with which Johnny was welcomed.

[074-153]"You don't know the Silverbridge people,--do you?" asked Mr. Toogood.

[074-154]Eames said that he did not. He had been at Silverbridge more than

[074-155]once, but did not know very much of the Silverbridgians. "Because

[074-156]Walker is coming to dine here. Walker is the leading man in

[074-157]Silverbridge."

[074-158]"And what is Walker;--besides being leading man in Silverbridge?"

[074-159]"He's a lawyer. Walker and Winthrop. Everybody knows Walker in

[074-160]Barsetshire. I've been down at Barchester since I saw you."

[074-161]"Have you indeed?" said Johnny.

[074-162]"And I'll tell you what I've been about. You know Mr. Crawley; don't

[074-163]you?"

[074-164]"The Hogglestock clergyman that has come to grief? I don't know him

[074-165]personally. He's a sort of cousin by marriage, you know."

[074-166]"Of course he is," said Mr. Toogood. "His wife is my first-cousin,

[074-167]and your mother's first-cousin. He came here to me the other day;--or

[074-168]rather to the shop. I had never seen the man before in my life, and a

[074-169]very queer fellow he is too. He came to me about this trouble of his,

[074-170]and of course I must do what I can for him. I got myself introduced

[074-171]to Walker, who has the management of the prosecution, and I asked him

[074-172]to come here and dine to-day."

[074-173]"And what sort of fellow did you find Crawley, uncle Tom?"

[074-174]"Such a queer fish;--so unlike anybody else in the world!"

[074-175]"But I suppose he did take the money?" said Johnny.

[074-176]"I don't know what to say about it. I don't indeed. If he took it he

[074-177]didn't mean to steal it. I'm as sure that man didn't mean to steal

[074-178]twenty pounds as I ever could be of anything. Perhaps I shall get

[074-179]something about it out of Walker after dinner." Then Mr. Walker

[074-180]entered the room. "This is very kind of you, Mr. Walker; very indeed.

[074-181]I take it quite as a compliment, your coming in in this sort of way.

[074-182]It's just pot luck, you know, and nothing else." Mr. Walker of course

[074-183]assured his host that he was delighted. "Just a leg of mutton and a

[074-184]bottle of old port, Mr. Walker," continued Toogood. "We never get

[074-185]beyond that in the way of dinner-giving; do we, Maria?"

[074-186]But Maria was at this moment descanting on the good luck of the

[074-187]family to her nephew,--and on one special piece of good luck which

[074-188]had just occurred. Mr. Summerkin's maiden aunt had declared her

[074-189]intention of giving up the fortune to the young people at once. She

[074-190]had enough to live upon, she said, and would therefore make two

[074-191]lovers happy. "And they're to be married on the first of May," said

[074-192]Lucy,--that Lucy of whom her father had boasted to Mr. Crawley that

[074-193]she knew Byron by heart,--"and won't that be jolly? Mamma is going

[074-194]out to look for a house for them to-morrow. Fancy Polly with a house

[074-195]of her own! Won't it be stunning? I wish you were going to be married

[074-196]too, Johnny."
