## Character

Lily Dale is the heroine of the Allington branch of the Barsetshire chronicles and appears in *The Last Chronicle of Barset* as Grace Crawley's dearest friend and confidante in London. She is a young woman of independent means and strong will, known throughout the county for her resolution never to marry — a vow taken after Adolphus Crosbie jilted her for Lady Alexandrina de Courcy. Trollope describes her as possessing a charm that is "half divine" (029-101). Her London drawing-room becomes the setting for much of the novel's romantic plotting, and her loyalty to Grace Crawley during the cheque-forgery scandal demonstrates the strength of her character.

## Biography

Lily Dale lives at the Small House at Allington with her mother, Mary Dale. Her uncle, the Squire of Allington, is Christopher Dale. Her romantic history is the defining fact of her present life: years before the events of *The Last Chronicle*, she was engaged to Adolphus Crosbie, a rising man in London, who abandoned her for a more advantageous match. "Lily Dale had declared" that she would never marry, a resolution that has become the central fact of her social identity (026-094). In the present novel, she takes Grace Crawley into her London circle, providing a refuge from the scandal engulfing the Crawley family. She appears in the chapter "Lily Dale Goes to London" (001-012), and her correspondence and conversation drive much of the emotional tension between Johnny Eames and the Crawley-Grantly plot.

## Significance

Lily Dale represents Trollope's most sustained examination of a woman's right to determine her own romantic destiny. Her refusal to marry — despite the earnest and repeated proposals of Johnny Eames — is neither punished nor reversed by the narrative, making her one of Victorian fiction's most unconventional heroines. In *The Last Chronicle of Barset*, her role is that of the loyal friend and emotional anchor: by standing by Grace Crawley when society might have shunned her, Lily demonstrates that personal integrity matters more than social reputation. Her presence connects the London and Barsetshire worlds, bridging the urban sophistication of the Eames-Toogood circle with the rural gentry world of the Grantlys.
