[046-001]At first he found it hard to talk to her. She answered him, and

[046-002]not with monosyllables. But she answered him without sympathy, or

[046-003]apparent pleasure in talking. Now the young artist was in the habit

[046-004]of being flattered by ladies, and expected to have his small talk

[046-005]made very easy for him. He liked to give himself little airs, and

[046-006]was not generally disposed to labour very hard at the task of making

[046-007]himself agreeable.

[046-008]"Were you ever painted yet?" he asked her after they had both been

[046-009]sitting silent for two or three minutes.

[046-010]"Was I ever--ever painted? In what way?"

[046-011]"I don't mean rouged, or enamelled, or got up by Madame Rachel; but

[046-012]have you ever had your portrait taken?"

[046-013]"I have been photographed,--of course."

[046-014]"That's why I asked you if you had been painted,--so as to make some

[046-015]little distinction between the two. I am a painter by profession, and

[046-016]do portraits."

[046-017]"So Mrs. Broughton told me."

[046-018]"I am not asking for a job, you know."

[046-019]"I am quite sure of that."

[046-020]"But I should have thought you would have been sure to have sat to

[046-021]somebody."

[046-022]"I never did. I never thought of doing so. One does those things at

[046-023]the instigation of one's intimate friends,--fathers, mothers, uncles,

[046-024]and aunts, and the like."

[046-025]"Or husbands, perhaps,--or lovers?"

[046-026]"Well, yes; my intimate friend is my mother, and she would never

[046-027]dream of such a thing. She hates pictures."

[046-028]"Hates pictures!"

[046-029]"And especially portraits. And I'm afraid, Mr. Dalrymple, she hates

[046-030]artists."

[046-031]"Good heavens; how cruel! I suppose there is some story attached

[046-032]to it. There has been some fatal likeness,--some terrible

[046-033]picture,--something in her early days?"

[046-034]"Nothing of the kind, Mr. Dalrymple. It is merely the fact that her

[046-035]sympathies are with ugly things, rather than with pretty things. I

[046-036]think she loves the mahogany dinner-table better than anything else

[046-037]in the house; and she likes to have everything dark, and plain, and

[046-038]solid."

[046-039]"And good?"

[046-040]"Good of its kind, certainly."

[046-041]"If everybody was like your mother, how would the artists live?"

[046-042]"There would be none."

[046-043]"And the world, you think, would be none the poorer?"

[046-044]"I did not speak of myself. I think the world would be very much

[046-045]the poorer. I am very fond of the ancient masters, though I do not

[046-046]suppose that I understand them."

[046-047]"They are easier understood than the modern, I can tell you. Perhaps

[046-048]you don't care for modern pictures?"

[046-049]"Not in comparison, certainly. If that is uncivil, you have brought

[046-050]it on yourself. But I do not in truth mean anything derogatory to the

[046-051]painters of the day. When their pictures are old, they,--that is the

[046-052]good ones among them,--will be nice also."

[046-053]"Pictures are like wine, and want age, you think?"

[046-054]"Yes, and statues too, and buildings above all things. The colours

[046-055]of new paintings are so glaring, and the faces are so bright and

[046-056]self-conscious, that they look to me when I go to the exhibition like

[046-057]coloured prints in a child's new picture-book. It is the same thing

[046-058]with buildings. One sees all the points, and nothing is left to the

[046-059]imagination."

[046-060]"I find I have come across a real critic."

[046-061]"I hope, at any rate, I am not a sham one;" and Miss Van Siever as

[046-062]she said this looked very savage.

[046-063]"I shouldn't take you to be a sham in anything."

[046-064]"Ah, that would be saying a great deal for myself. Who can undertake

[046-065]to say that he is not a sham in anything?"

[046-066]As she said this the ladies were getting up. So Miss Van Siever also

[046-067]got up, and left Mr. Conway Dalrymple to consider whether he could

[046-068]say or could think of himself that he was not a sham in anything.

[046-069]As regarded Miss Clara Van Siever, he began to think that he should

[046-070]not object to paint her portrait, even though there might be no

[046-071]sugar-plum. He would certainly do it as Jael; and he would, if he

[046-072]dared, insert dimly in the background some idea of the face of

[046-073]the mother, half-appearing, half-vanishing, as the spirit of the

[046-074]sacrifice. He was composing his picture, while Mr. Dobbs Broughton

[046-075]was arranging himself and his bottles.

[046-076]"Musselboro," he said, "I'll come up between you and Crosbie. Mr.

[046-077]Eames, though I run away from you, the claret shall remain; or,

[046-078]rather, it shall flow backwards and forwards as rapidly as you will."

[046-079]"I'll keep it moving," said Johnny.

[046-080]"Do; there's a good fellow. It's a nice glass of wine, isn't it? Old

[046-081]Ramsby, who keeps as good a stock of stuff as any wine-merchant in

[046-082]London, gave me a hint, three or four years ago, that he'd a lot of

[046-083]tidy Bordeaux. It's '41, you know. He had ninety dozen, and I took it

[046-084]all."

[046-085]"What was the figure, Broughton?" said Crosbie, asking the question

[046-086]which he knew was expected.

[046-087]"Well, I only gave one hundred and four for it then; it's worth a

[046-088]hundred and twenty now. I wouldn't sell a bottle of it for any money.

[046-089]Come, Dalrymple, pass it round; but fill your glass first."

[046-090]"Thank you, no; I don't like it. I'll drink sherry."

[046-091]"Don't like it!" said Dobbs Broughton.

[046-092]"It's strange, isn't it? but I don't."

[046-093]"I thought you particularly told me to drink his claret?" said Johnny

[046-094]to his friend afterwards.

[046-095]"So I did," said Conway; "and wonderfully good wine it is. But I make

[046-096]it a rule never to eat or drink anything in a man's house when he

[046-097]praises it himself and tells me the price of it."

[046-098]"And I make it a rule never to cut the nose off my own face," said

[046-099]Johnny.

[046-100]Before they went, Johnny Eames had been specially invited to call

[046-101]on Lady Demolines, and had said that he would do so. "We live in

[046-102]Porchester Gardens," said Miss Demolines. "Upon my word, I believe

[046-103]that the farther London stretches in that direction, the farther

[046-104]mamma will go. She thinks the air so much better. I know it's a long

[046-105]way."

[046-106]"Distance is nothing to me," said Johnny; "I can always set off over

[046-107]night."

[046-108]Conway Dalrymple did not get invited to call on Mrs. Van Siever, but

[046-109]before he left the house he did say a word or two more to his friend

[046-110]Mrs. Broughton as to Clara Van Siever. "She is a fine young woman,"

[046-111]he said; "she is indeed."

[046-112]"You have found it out, have you?"

[046-113]"Yes, I have found it out. I do not doubt that some day she'll

[046-114]murder her husband or her mother, or startle the world by some

[046-115]newly-invented crime; but that only makes her the more interesting."

[046-116]"And when you add to that all the old woman's money," said Mrs. Dobbs

[046-117]Broughton, "you think that she might do?"

[046-118]"For a picture, certainly. I'm speaking of her simply as a model.

[046-119]Could we not manage it? Get her once here, without her mother knowing

[046-120]it, or Broughton, or any one. I've got the subject,--Jael and Sisera,

[046-121]you know. I should like to put Musselboro in as Sisera, with the nail

[046-122]half driven in." Mrs. Dobbs Broughton declared that the scheme was a

[046-123]great deal too wicked for her participation, but at last she promised

[046-124]to think of it.

[046-125]"You might as well come up and have a cigar," Dalrymple said, as he

[046-126]and his friend left Mr. Broughton's house. Johnny said that he would

[046-127]go up and have a cigar or two. "And now tell me what you think of

[046-128]Mrs. Dobbs Broughton and her set," said Conway.

[046-129]"Well; I'll tell you what I think of them. I think they stink of

[046-130]money, as the people say; but I'm not sure that they've got any all

[046-131]the same."

[046-132]"I should suppose he makes a large income."

[046-133]"Very likely, and perhaps spends more than he makes. A good deal of

[046-134]it looked to me like make-believe. There's no doubt about the claret,

[046-135]but the champagne was execrable. A man is a criminal to have such

[046-136]stuff handed round to his guests. And there isn't the ring of real

[046-137]gold about the house."

[046-138]"I hate the ring of the gold, as you call it," said the artist.

[046-139]"So do I,--I hate it like poison; but if it is there, I like it to be

[046-140]true. There is a sort of persons going now,--and one meets them out

[046-141]here and there every day of one's life,--who are downright Brummagem

[046-142]to the ear and to the touch and to the sight, and we recognize them

[046-143]as such at the very first moment. My honoured lord and master, Sir

[046-144]Raffle, is one such. There is no mistaking him. Clap him down upon

[046-145]the counter, and he rings dull and untrue at once. Pardon me, my

[046-146]dear Conway, if I say the same of your excellent friend Mr. Dobbs

[046-147]Broughton."

[046-148]"I think you go a little too far, but I don't deny it. What you mean

[046-149]is, that he's not a gentleman."

[046-150]"I mean a great deal more than that. Bless you, when you come to talk

[046-151]of a gentleman, who is to define the word? How do I know whether or

[046-152]no I'm a gentleman myself? When I used to be in Burton Crescent, I

[046-153]was hardly a gentleman then,--sitting at the same table with Mrs.

[046-154]Roper and the Lupexes;--do you remember them, and the lovely Amelia?"

[046-155]"I suppose you were a gentleman, then, as well as now."

[046-156]"You, if you had been painting duchesses then, with a studio in

[046-157]Kensington Gardens, would not have said so, if you had happened to

[046-158]come across me. I can't define a gentleman, even in my own mind;--but

[046-159]I can define the sort of man with whom I think I can live

[046-160]pleasantly."

[046-161]"And poor Dobbs doesn't come within the line?"

[046-162]"N--o, not quite; a very nice fellow, I'm quite sure, and I'm very

[046-163]much obliged to you for taking me there."

[046-164]"I never will take you to any house again. And what did you think of

[046-165]his wife?"

[046-166]"That's a horse of another colour altogether. A pretty woman with

[046-167]such a figure as hers has got a right to be anything she pleases.

[046-168]I see you are a great favourite."

[046-169]"No, I'm not;--not especially. I do like her. She wants to make up a

[046-170]match between me and that Miss Van Siever. Miss Van is to have gold

[046-171]by the ingot, and jewels by the bushel, and a hatful of bank shares,

[046-172]and a whole mine in Cornwall, for her fortune."

[046-173]"And is very handsome into the bargain."

[046-174]"Yes; she's handsome."

[046-175]"So is her mother," said Johnny. "If you take the daughter, I'll

[046-176]take the mother, and see if I can't do you out of a mine or two.

[046-177]Good-night, old fellow. I'm only joking about old Dobbs. I'll go and

[046-178]dine there again to-morrow, if you like it."

[046-179]CHAPTER XXV.

[046-180]MISS MADALINA DEMOLINES.

[046-181][Illustration]

[046-182]"I don't think you care two straws about her," Conway Dalrymple

[046-183]said to his friend John Eames, two days after the dinner-party at

[046-184]Mrs. Dobbs Broughton's. The painter was at work in his studio, and

[046-185]the private secretary from the Income-tax Office, who was no doubt

[046-186]engaged on some special mission to the West End on the part of Sir

[046-187]Raffle Buffle, was sitting in a lounging-chair and smoking a cigar.

[046-188]"Because I don't go about with my stockings cross-gartered, and do

[046-189]that kind of business?"

[046-190]"Well, yes; because you don't do that kind of business, more or

[046-191]less."

[046-192]"It isn't in my line, my dear fellow. I know what you mean, very

[046-193]well. I daresay, artistically speaking,--"

[046-194]"Don't be an ass, Johnny."

[046-195]"Well then, poetically, or romantically, if you like that better,-- I

[046-196]daresay that poetically or romantically I am deficient. I eat my

[046-197]dinner very well, and I don't suppose I ought to do that; and, if

[046-198]you'll believe me, I find myself laughing sometimes."

[046-199]"I never knew a man who laughed so much. You're always laughing."
