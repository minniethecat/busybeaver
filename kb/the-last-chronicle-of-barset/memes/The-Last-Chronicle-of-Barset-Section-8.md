[008-001]After that, after those church services were over, he sank again and

[008-002]never roused himself till the dreaded day had come.

[008-003]CHAPTER V.

[008-004]WHAT THE WORLD THOUGHT ABOUT IT.

[008-005]Opinion in Silverbridge, at Barchester, and throughout the county,

[008-006]was very much divided as to the guilt or innocence of Mr. Crawley. Up

[008-007]to the time of Mrs. Crawley's visit to Silverbridge, the affair had

[008-008]not been much discussed. To give Mr. Soames his due, he had been by

[008-009]no means anxious to press the matter against the clergyman; but he

[008-010]had been forced to go on with it. While the first cheque was missing,

[008-011]Lord Lufton had sent him a second cheque for the money, and the loss

[008-012]had thus fallen upon his lordship. The cheque had of course been

[008-013]traced, and inquiry had of course been made as to Mr. Crawley's

[008-014]possession of it. When that gentleman declared that he had received

[008-015]it from Mr. Soames, Mr. Soames had been forced to contradict and to

[008-016]resent such an assertion. When Mr. Crawley had afterwards said that

[008-017]the money had come to him from the dean, and when the dean had shown

[008-018]that this also was untrue, Mr. Soames, confident as he was that he

[008-019]had dropped the pocket-book at Mr. Crawley's house, could not but

[008-020]continue the investigation. He had done so with as much silence as

[008-021]the nature of the work admitted. But by the day of the magistrates'

[008-022]meeting at Silverbridge the subject had become common through the

[008-023]county, and men's minds were very much divided.

[008-024]All Hogglestock believed their parson to be innocent; but then all

[008-025]Hogglestock believed him to be mad. At Silverbridge the tradesmen

[008-026]with whom he had dealt, and to whom he had owed, and still owed,

[008-027]money, all declared him to be innocent. They knew something of the

[008-028]man personally, and could not believe him to be a thief. All the

[008-029]ladies in Silverbridge, too, were sure of his innocence. It was to

[008-030]them impossible that such a man should have stolen twenty pounds.

[008-031]"My dear," said the eldest Miss Prettyman to poor Grace Crawley, "in

[008-032]England, where the laws are good, no gentleman is ever made out to

[008-033]be guilty when he is innocent; and your papa, of course, is innocent.

[008-034]Therefore you should not trouble yourself." "It will break papa's

[008-035]heart," Grace had said, and she did trouble herself. But the

[008-036]gentlemen in Silverbridge were made of sterner stuff, and believed

[008-037]the man to be guilty, clergyman and gentleman though he was. Mr.

[008-038]Walker, who among the lights in Silverbridge was the leading light,

[008-039]would not speak a word upon the subject to anybody; and then

[008-040]everybody, who was anybody, knew that Mr. Walker was convinced of the

[008-041]man's guilt. Had Mr. Walker believed him to be innocent, his tongue

[008-042]would have been ready enough. John Walker, who was in the habit of

[008-043]laughing at his father's good nature, had no doubt upon the subject.

[008-044]Mr. Winthrop, Mr. Walker's partner, shook his head. People did not

[008-045]think much of Mr. Winthrop, excepting certain unmarried ladies; for

[008-046]Mr. Winthrop was a bachelor, and had plenty of money. People did not

[008-047]think much of Mr. Winthrop; but still on this subject he might know

[008-048]something, and when he shook his head he manifestly intended to

[008-049]indicate guilt. And Dr. Tempest, the rector of Silverbridge, did

[008-050]not hesitate to declare his belief in the guilt of the incumbent

[008-051]of Hogglestock. No man reverences a clergyman, as a clergyman, so

[008-052]slightly as a brother clergyman. To Dr. Tempest it appeared to be

[008-053]neither very strange nor very terrible that Mr. Crawley should have

[008-054]stolen twenty pounds. "What is a man to do," he said, "when he

[008-055]sees his children starving? He should not have married on such a

[008-056]preferment as that." Mr. Crawley had married, however, long before he

[008-057]got the living of Hogglestock.

[008-058]There were two Lady Luftons,--mother-in-law and daughter-in-law,--who

[008-059]at this time were living together at Framley Hall, Lord Lufton's seat

[008-060]in the county of Barset, and they were both thoroughly convinced

[008-061]of Mr. Crawley's innocence. The elder lady had lived much among

[008-062]clergymen, and could hardly, I think, by any means have been brought

[008-063]to believe in the guilt of any man who had taken upon himself the

[008-064]orders of the Church of England. She had also known Mr. Crawley

[008-065]personally for some years, and was one of those who could not admit

[008-066]to herself that any one was vile who had been near to herself. She

[008-067]believed intensely in the wickedness of the outside world, of the

[008-068]world which was far away from herself, and of which she never saw

[008-069]anything; but they who were near to her, and who had even become dear

[008-070]to her, or who even had been respected by her, were made, as it were,

[008-071]saints in her imagination. They were brought into the inner circle,

[008-072]and could hardly be expelled. She was an old woman who thought all

[008-073]evil of those she did not know, and all good of those whom she did

[008-074]know; and as she did know Mr. Crawley, she was quite sure he had not

[008-075]stolen Mr. Soames's twenty pounds. She did know Mr. Soames also; and

[008-076]thus there was a mystery for the unravelling of which she was very

[008-077]anxious. And the young Lady Lufton was equally sure, and perhaps with

[008-078]better reason for such certainty. She had, in truth, known more of

[008-079]Mr. Crawley personally, than had any one in the county, unless it was

[008-080]the dean. The younger Lady Lufton, the present Lord Lufton's wife,

[008-081]had sojourned at one time in Mr. Crawley's house, amidst the Crawley

[008-082]poverty, living as they lived, and nursing Mrs. Crawley through an

[008-083]illness which had well nigh been fatal to her; and the younger Lady

[008-084]Lufton believed in Mr. Crawley,--as Mr. Crawley also believed in her.

[008-085]"It is quite impossible, my dear," the old woman said to her

[008-086]daughter-in-law.

[008-087]"Quite impossible, my lady." The dowager was always called "my

[008-088]lady," both by her own daughter and by her son's wife, except in the

[008-089]presence of their children, when she was addressed as "grandmamma."

[008-090]"Think how well I knew him. It's no use talking of evidence. No

[008-091]evidence would make me believe it."

[008-092]"Nor me; and I think it a great shame that such a report should be

[008-093]spread about."

[008-094]"I suppose Mr. Soames could not help himself?" said the younger lady,

[008-095]who was not herself very fond of Mr. Soames.

[008-096]"Ludovic says that he has only done what he was obliged to do." The

[008-097]Ludovic spoken of was Lord Lufton.

[008-098]This took place in the morning, but in the evening the affair was

[008-099]again discussed at Framley Hall. Indeed, for some days, there was

[008-100]hardly any other subject held to be worthy of discussion in the

[008-101]county. Mr. Robarts, the clergyman of the parish and the brother of

[008-102]the younger Lady Lufton, was dining at the hall with his wife, and

[008-103]the three ladies had together expressed their perfect conviction of

[008-104]the falseness of the accusation. But when Lord Lufton and Mr. Robarts

[008-105]were together after the ladies had left them there was much less of

[008-106]this certainty expressed. "By Jove," said Lord Lufton, "I don't know

[008-107]what to think of it. I wish with all my heart that Soames had said

[008-108]nothing about it, and that the cheque had passed without remark."

[008-109]"That was impossible. When the banker sent to Soames, he was obliged

[008-110]to take the matter up."

[008-111]"Of course he was. But I'm sorry that it was so. For the life of me I

[008-112]can't conceive how the cheque got into Crawley's hands."

[008-113]"I imagine that it had been lying in the house, and that Crawley had

[008-114]come to think that it was his own."

[008-115]"But, my dear Mark," said Lord Lufton, "excuse me if I say that

[008-116]that's nonsense. What do we do when a poor man has come to think that

[008-117]another man's property is his own? We send him to prison for making

[008-118]the mistake."

[008-119]"I hope they won't send Crawley to prison."

[008-120]"I hope so too; but what is a jury to do?"

[008-121]"You think it will go to a jury, then?"

[008-122]"I do," said Lord Lufton. "I don't see how the magistrates can save

[008-123]themselves from committing him. It is one of those cases in which

[008-124]every one concerned would wish to drop it if it were only possible.

[008-125]But it is not possible. On the evidence, as one sees it at present,

[008-126]one is bound to say that it is a case for a jury."

[008-127]"I believe that he is mad," said the brother parson.

[008-128]"He always was, as far as I could learn," said the lord. "I never

[008-129]knew him, myself. You do, I think?"

[008-130]"Oh, yes. I know him." And the vicar of Framley became silent and

[008-131]thoughtful as the memory of a certain interview between himself and

[008-132]Mr. Crawley came back upon his mind. At that time the waters had

[008-133]nearly closed over his head and Mr. Crawley had given him some

[008-134]assistance. When the gentlemen had again found the ladies, they kept

[008-135]their own doubts to themselves; for at Framley Hall, as at present

[008-136]tenanted, female voices and female influences predominated over those

[008-137]which came from the other sex.

[008-138]At Barchester, the cathedral city of the county in which the Crawleys

[008-139]lived, opinion was violently against Mr. Crawley. In the city Mrs.

[008-140]Proudie, the wife of the bishop, was the leader of opinion in

[008-141]general, and she was very strong in her belief of the man's guilt.

[008-142]She had known much of clergymen all her life, as it behoved a

[008-143]bishop's wife to do, and she had none of that mingled weakness and

[008-144]ignorance which taught so many ladies in Barsetshire to suppose that

[008-145]an ordained clergyman could not become a thief. She hated old Lady

[008-146]Lufton with all her heart, and old Lady Lufton hated her as warmly.

[008-147]Mrs. Proudie would say frequently that Lady Lufton was a conceited

[008-148]old idiot, and Lady Lufton would declare as frequently that

[008-149]Mrs. Proudie was a vulgar virago. It was known at the palace in

[008-150]Barchester, that kindness had been shown to the Crawleys by the

[008-151]family at Framley Hall, and this alone would have been sufficient to

[008-152]make Mrs. Proudie believe that Mr. Crawley could have been guilty of

[008-153]any crime. And as Mrs. Proudie believed, so did the bishop believe.

[008-154]"It is a terrible disgrace to the diocese," said the bishop, shaking

[008-155]his head, and patting his apron as he sat by his study fire.

[008-156]"Fiddlestick!" said Mrs. Proudie.

[008-157]"But, my dear,--a beneficed clergyman!"

[008-158]"You must get rid of him; that's all. You must be firm whether he be

[008-159]acquitted or convicted."

[008-160]"But if he be acquitted, I cannot get rid of him, my dear."

[008-161]"Yes, you can, if you are firm. And you must be firm. Is it not true

[008-162]that he has been disgracefully involved in debt ever since he has

[008-163]been there; that you have been pestered by letters from unfortunate

[008-164]tradesmen who cannot get their money from him?"

[008-165]"That is true, my dear, certainly."

[008-166]"And is that kind of thing to go on? He cannot come to the palace as

[008-167]all clergymen should do, because he has got no clothes to come in. I

[008-168]saw him once about the lanes, and I never set my eyes on such an

[008-169]object in my life! I would not believe that the man was a clergyman

[008-170]till John told me. He is a disgrace to the diocese, and he must

[008-171]be got rid of. I feel sure of his guilt, and I hope he will be

[008-172]convicted. One is bound to hope that a guilty man should be

[008-173]convicted. But if he escape conviction, you must sequestrate the

[008-174]living because of the debts. The income is enough to get an excellent

[008-175]curate. It would just do for Thumble." To all of which the bishop

[008-176]made no further reply, but simply nodded his head and patted his

[008-177]apron. He knew that he could not do exactly what his wife required of

[008-178]him; but if it should so turn out that poor Crawley was found to be

[008-179]guilty, then the matter would be comparatively easy.
