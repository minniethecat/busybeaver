[099-001]But there was certainly something wrong over and beyond the Dalrymple

[099-002]difficulty. The servants were not as civil as they used to be, and

[099-003]her husband, when she suggested to him a little dinner-party, snubbed

[099-004]her most unmercifully. The giving of dinner-parties had been his

[099-005]glory, and she had made the suggestion simply with the view of

[099-006]pleasing him. "If the world were going round the wrong way, a woman

[099-007]would still want a party," he had said, sneering at her. "It was

[099-008]of you I was thinking, Dobbs," she replied; "not of myself. I care

[099-009]little for such gatherings." After that she retired to her own room

[099-010]with a romantic tear in each eye, and told herself that, had chance

[099-011]thrown Conway Dalrymple into her way before she had seen Dobbs

[099-012]Broughton, she would have been the happiest woman in the world. She

[099-013]sat for a while looking into vacancy, and thinking that it would be

[099-014]very nice to break her heart. How should she set about it? Should

[099-015]she take to her bed and grow thin? She would begin by eating no

[099-016]dinner for ever so many days together. At lunch her husband was never

[099-017]present, and therefore the broken heart could be displayed at dinner

[099-018]without much positive suffering. In the meantime she would implore

[099-019]Conway Dalrymple to get himself married with as little delay as

[099-020]possible, and she would lay upon him her positive order to restrain

[099-021]himself from any word of affection addressed to herself. She, at any

[099-022]rate, would be pure, high-minded, and self-sacrificing,--although

[099-023]romantic and poetic also, as was her nature.

[099-024]The picture was progressing, and so also, as it had come about, was

[099-025]the love-affair between the artist and his model. Conway Dalrymple

[099-026]had begun to think that he might, after all, do worse than make Clara

[099-027]Van Siever his wife. Clara Van Siever was handsome, and undoubtedly

[099-028]clever, and Clara Van Siever's mother was certainly rich. And, in

[099-029]addition to this, the young lady herself began to like the man into

[099-030]whose society she was thrown. The affair seemed to flourish, and Mrs.

[099-031]Dobbs Broughton should have been delighted. She told Clara, with a

[099-032]very serious air, that she was delighted, bidding Clara, at the same

[099-033]time, to be very cautious, as men were so fickle, and as Conway,

[099-034]though the best fellow in the world, was not, perhaps, altogether

[099-035]free from that common vice of men. Indeed, it might have been

[099-036]surmised, from a word or two which Mrs. Broughton allowed to escape,

[099-037]that she considered poor Conway to be more than ordinarily afflicted

[099-038]in that way. Miss Van Siever at first only pouted, and said that

[099-039]there was nothing in it. "There is something in it, my dear,

[099-040]certainly," said Mrs. Dobbs Broughton; "and there can be no earthly

[099-041]reason why there should not be a great deal in it." "There is nothing

[099-042]in it," said Miss Van Siever, impetuously; "and if you will continue

[099-043]to speak of Mr. Dalrymple in that way, I must give up the picture."

[099-044]"As for that," said Mrs. Broughton, "I conceive that we are both of

[099-045]us bound to the young man now, seeing that he has given so much time

[099-046]to the work." "I am not bound to him at all," said Miss Van Siever.

[099-047]Mrs. Broughton also told Conway Dalrymple that she was

[099-048]delighted,--oh, so much delighted! He had obtained permission to come

[099-049]in one morning before the time of sitting, so that he might work at

[099-050]his canvas independently of his model. As was his custom, he made his

[099-051]own way upstairs and commenced his work alone,--having been expressly

[099-052]told by Mrs. Broughton that she would not come to him till she

[099-053]brought Clara with her. But she did go up to the room in which the

[099-054]artist was painting, without waiting for Miss Van Siever. Indeed, she

[099-055]was at this time so anxious as to the future welfare of her two young

[099-056]friends that she could not restrain herself from speaking either to

[099-057]the one or to the other, whenever any opportunity for such speech

[099-058]came round. To have left Conway Dalrymple at work upstairs without

[099-059]going to him was impossible to her. So she went, and then took the

[099-060]opportunity of expressing to her friend her ideas as to his past and

[099-061]future conduct.

[099-062]"Yes, it is very good; very good, indeed," she said, standing before

[099-063]the easel, and looking at the half-completed work. "I do not know

[099-064]that you ever did anything better."

[099-065]"I never can tell myself till a picture is finished whether it is

[099-066]going to be good or not," said Dalrymple, thinking really of his

[099-067]picture and of nothing else.

[099-068]"I am sure this will be good," she said, "and I suppose it is because

[099-069]you have thrown so much heart into it. It is not mere industry that

[099-070]will produce good work, nor yet skill, nor even genius: more than

[099-071]this is required. The heart of the artist must be thrust with all its

[099-072]gushing tides into the performance." By this time he knew all the

[099-073]tones of her voice and their various meanings, and immediately became

[099-074]aware that at the present moment she was intent upon something beyond

[099-075]the picture. She was preparing for a little scene, and was going to

[099-076]give him some advice. He understood it all, but as he was really

[099-077]desirous of working at his canvas, and was rather averse to having a

[099-078]scene at that moment, he made a little attempt to disconcert her. "It

[099-079]is the heart that gives success," she said, while he was considering

[099-080]how he might best put an extinguisher upon her romance for the

[099-081]occasion.

[099-082]"Not at all, Mrs. Broughton; success depends on elbow-grease."

[099-083]"On what, Conway?"

[099-084]"On elbow-grease,--hard work, that is,--and I must work hard now if

[099-085]I mean to take advantage of to-day's sitting. The truth is, I don't

[099-086]give enough hours of work to it." And he leaned upon his stick, and

[099-087]daubed away briskly at the background, and then stood for a moment

[099-088]looking at his canvas with his head a little on one side, as though

[099-089]he could not withdraw his attention for a moment from the thing he

[099-090]was doing.

[099-091]"You mean to say, Conway, that you would rather that I should not

[099-092]speak to you."

[099-093]"Oh, no, Mrs. Broughton, I did not mean that at all."

[099-094]"I won't interrupt you at your work. What I have to say is perhaps of

[099-095]no great moment. Indeed, words between you and me never can have much

[099-096]importance now. Can they, Conway?"

[099-097]"I don't see that at all," said he, still working away with his

[099-098]brush.

[099-099]"Do you not? I do. They should never amount to more,--they can never

[099-100]amount to more than the common, ordinary courtesies of life; what I

[099-101]call the greetings and good-byings of conversation." She said this in

[099-102]a low, melancholy tone of voice, not intending to be in any degree

[099-103]jocose. "How seldom is it that conversation between ordinary friends

[099-104]goes beyond that."

[099-105]"Don't you think it does?" said Conway, stepping back and taking

[099-106]another look at his picture. "I find myself talking to all manner of

[099-107]people about all manner of things."

[099-108]"You are different from me. I cannot talk to all manner of people."

[099-109]"Politics, you know, and art, and a little scandal, and the wars,

[099-110]with a dozen other things, make talking easy enough, I think. I grant

[099-111]you this, that it is very often a great bore. Hardly a day passes

[099-112]that I don't wish to cut out somebody's tongue."

[099-113]"Do you wish to cut out my tongue, Conway?"

[099-114]He began to perceive that she was determined to talk about herself,

[099-115]and that there was no remedy. He dreaded it, not because he did not

[099-116]like the woman, but from a conviction that she was going to make some

[099-117]comparison between herself and Clara Van Siever. In his ordinary

[099-118]humour he liked a little pretence at romance, and was rather good

[099-119]at that sort of love-making which in truth means anything but love.

[099-120]But just now he was really thinking of matrimony, and had on this

[099-121]very morning acknowledged to himself that he had become sufficiently

[099-122]attached to Clara Van Siever to justify him in asking her to be his

[099-123]wife. In his present mood he was not anxious for one of those tilts

[099-124]with blunted swords and half-severed lances in the lists of Cupid

[099-125]of which Mrs. Dobbs Broughton was so fond. Nevertheless, if she

[099-126]insisted that he should now descend into the arena and go through

[099-127]the paraphernalia of a mock tournament, he must obey her. It is the

[099-128]hardship of men that when called upon by women for romance, they are

[099-129]bound to be romantic, whether the opportunity serves them or does

[099-130]not. A man must produce romance, or at least submit to it, when duly

[099-131]summoned, even though he should have a sore-throat or a headache. He

[099-132]is a brute if he decline such an encounter,--and feels that, should

[099-133]he so decline persistently, he will ever after be treated as a brute.

[099-134]There are many Potiphar's wives who never dream of any mischief, and

[099-135]Josephs who are very anxious to escape, though they are asked to

[099-136]return only whisper for whisper. Mrs. Dobbs Broughton had asked him

[099-137]whether he wished that her tongue should be cut out, and he had of

[099-138]course replied that her words had always been a joy to him,--never

[099-139]a trouble. It occurred to him as he made his little speech that it

[099-140]would only have served her right if he had answered her quite in

[099-141]another strain; but she was a woman, and was young and pretty, and

[099-142]was entitled to flattery. "They have always been a joy to me," he

[099-143]said, repeating his last words as he strove to continue his work.

[099-144]"A deadly joy," she replied, not quite knowing what she herself

[099-145]meant. "A deadly joy, Conway. I wish with all my heart that we had

[099-146]never known each other."

[099-147]"I do not. I will never wish away the happiness of my life, even

[099-148]should it be followed by misery."

[099-149]"You are a man, and if trouble comes upon you, you can bear it on

[099-150]your own shoulders. A woman suffers more, just because another's

[099-151]shoulders may have to bear the burden."

[099-152]"When she has got a husband, you mean?"

[099-153]"Yes,--when she has a husband."

[099-154]"It's the same with a man when he has a wife." Hitherto the

[099-155]conversation had had so much of milk-and-water in its composition,

[099-156]that Dalrymple found himself able to keep it up and go on with his

[099-157]background at the same time. If she could only be kept in the same

[099-158]dim cloud of sentiment, if the hot rays of the sun of romance could

[099-159]be kept from breaking through the mist till Miss Van Siever should

[099-160]come, it might still be well. He had known her to wander about within

[099-161]the clouds for an hour together, without being able to find her way

[099-162]into the light. "It's all the same with a man when he has got a

[099-163]wife," he said. "Of course one has to suffer for two, when one, so to

[099-164]say, is two."

[099-165]"And what happens when one has to suffer for three?" she asked.

[099-166]"You mean when a woman has children?"

[099-167]"I mean nothing of the kind, Conway; and you must know that I do not,

[099-168]unless your feelings are indeed blunted. But worldly success has, I

[099-169]suppose, blunted them."

[099-170]"I rather fancy not," he said. "I think they are pretty nearly as

[099-171]sharp as ever."

[099-172]"I know mine are. Oh, how I wish I could rid myself of them! But it

[099-173]cannot be done. Age will not blunt them,--I am sure of that," said

[099-174]Mrs. Broughton. "I wish it would."

[099-175]He had determined not to talk about herself if the subject could be

[099-176]in any way avoided; but now he felt that he was driven up into a

[099-177]corner;--now he was forced to speak to her of her own personality.

[099-178]"You have no experience yet as to that. How can you say what age will

[099-179]do?"
