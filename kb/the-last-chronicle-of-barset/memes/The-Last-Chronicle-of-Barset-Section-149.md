[149-001]"I do not know," he answered, frowning at her. He always frowned at

[149-002]her when she spoke to him of Miss Dale.

[149-003]"I do not in the least care for your frowns," she said playfully,

[149-004]putting up her hands to smooth his brows. "I think I know you

[149-005]intimately enough to name your goddess to you."

[149-006]"She isn't my goddess."

[149-007]"A very cold goddess, I should think, from what I hear. I wish to ask

[149-008]you for a promise respecting her."

[149-009]"What promise?"

[149-010]"Will you grant it me?"

[149-011]"How can I tell till I hear?"

[149-012]"You must promise me not to speak of me to her when you see her."

[149-013]"But why must I promise that?"

[149-014]"Promise me."

[149-015]"Not unless you tell me why." Johnny had already assured himself that

[149-016]nothing could be more improbable than that he should mention the name

[149-017]of Miss Demolines to Lily Dale.

[149-018]"Very well, sir. Then you may go. And I must say that unless you can

[149-019]comply with so slight a request as that, I shall not care to see you

[149-020]here again. Mr. Eames, why should you want to speak evil of me to

[149-021]Miss Dale?"

[149-022]"I do not want to speak evil of you."

[149-023]"I know that you could not speak of me to her without at least

[149-024]ridicule. Come, promise me. You shall come here on Thursday evening,

[149-025]and I will tell you why I have asked you."

[149-026]"Tell me now."

[149-027]She hesitated a moment, and then shook her head. "No. I cannot tell

[149-028]you now. My heart is still bleeding with the memory of that poor

[149-029]man's fate. I will not tell you now. And yet it is now that you must

[149-030]give me the promise. Will you not trust me so far as that?"

[149-031]"I will not speak of you to Miss Dale."

[149-032]"There is my own friend! And now, John, mind you are here at

[149-033]half-past eight on Thursday. Punctually at half-past eight. There is

[149-034]a thing I have to tell you, which I will tell you then if you will

[149-035]come. I had thought to have told you to-day."

[149-036]"And why not now?"

[149-037]"I cannot. My feelings are too many for me. I should never go through

[149-038]with it after all that has passed between us about poor Broughton.

[149-039]I should break down; indeed I should. Go now, for I am tired." Then,

[149-040]having probably taken a momentary advantage of that more potent

[149-041]attraction to which we have before alluded, he left the room very

[149-042]suddenly.

[149-043]He left the room very suddenly because Madalina's movements had been

[149-044]so sudden, and her words so full of impulse. He had become aware

[149-045]that in this little game which he was playing in Porchester Terrace

[149-046]everything ought to be done after some unaccustomed and special

[149-047]fashion. So,--having clasped Madalina for one moment in his arms,--he

[149-048]made a rush at the room door, and was out on the landing in a second.

[149-049]He was a little too quick for old Lady Demolines, the skirt of whose

[149-050]night-dress,--as it seemed to Johnny,--he saw whisking away, in at

[149-051]another door. It was nothing, however, to him if old Lady Demolines,

[149-052]who was always too ill to be seen, chose to roam about her own house

[149-053]in her night-dress.

[149-054]When he found himself alone in the street, his mind reverted to Dobbs

[149-055]Broughton and the fate of the wretched man, and he sauntered slowly

[149-056]down Palace Gardens, that he might look at the house in which he had

[149-057]dined with a man who had destroyed himself by his own hands. He stood

[149-058]for a moment looking up at the windows, in which there was now no

[149-059]light, thinking of the poor woman whom he had seen in the midst of

[149-060]luxury, and who was now left a widow in such miserable circumstances!

[149-061]As for the suggestion that his friend Conway would marry her, he did

[149-062]not believe it for a moment. He knew too well what the suggestions of

[149-063]his Madalina were worth, and the motives from which they sprung. But

[149-064]he thought it might be true that Mrs. Van Siever had absorbed all

[149-065]there was of property, and possibly, also, that Musselboro was to

[149-066]marry her daughter. At any rate, he would go to Dalrymple's rooms,

[149-067]and if he could find him, would learn the truth. He knew enough of

[149-068]Dalrymple's ways of life, and of the ways of his friend's chambers

[149-069]and studio, to care nothing for the lateness of the hour, and in a

[149-070]very few minutes he was sitting in Dalrymple's arm-chair. He found

[149-071]Siph Dunn there, smoking in unperturbed tranquillity, and as long as

[149-072]that lasted he could ask no questions about Mrs. Broughton. He told

[149-073]them, therefore, of his adventures abroad, and of Crawley's escape.

[149-074]But at last, having finished his third pipe, Siph Dunn took his

[149-075]leave.

[149-076]"Tell me," said John, as soon as Dunn had closed the door, "what is

[149-077]this I hear about Dobbs Broughton?"

[149-078]"He has blown his brains out. That is all."

[149-079]"How terribly shocking!"

[149-080]"Yes; it shocked us all at first. We are used to it now."

[149-081]"And the business?"

[149-082]"That had gone to the dogs. They say at least that his share of it

[149-083]had done so."

[149-084]"And he was ruined?"

[149-085]"They say so. That is, Musselboro says so, and Mrs. Van Siever."

[149-086]"And what do you say, Conway?"

[149-087]"The less I say the better. I have my hopes,--only you're such a

[149-088]talkative fellow, one can't trust you."

[149-089]"I never told any secret of yours, old fellow."

[149-090]"Well;--the fact is, I have an idea that something may be saved for

[149-091]the poor woman. I think that they are wronging her. Of course all

[149-092]I can do is to put the matter into a lawyer's hands, and pay the

[149-093]lawyer's bill. So I went to your cousin, and he has taken the case

[149-094]up. I hope he won't ruin me."

[149-095]"Then I suppose you are quarrelling with Mrs. Van?"

[149-096]"That doesn't matter. She has quarrelled with me."

[149-097]"And what about Jael, Conway? They tell me that Jael is going to

[149-098]become Mrs. Musselboro."

[149-099]"Who has told you that?"

[149-100]"A bird."

[149-101]"Yes; I know who the bird is. I don't think that Jael will become

[149-102]Mrs. Musselboro. I don't think that Jael would become Mrs.

[149-103]Musselboro, if Jael were the only woman, and Musselboro the only

[149-104]man in London. To tell you a little bit of secret, Johnny, I think

[149-105]that Jael will become the wife of one Conway Dalrymple. That is my

[149-106]opinion; and as far as I can judge, it is the opinion of Jael also."

[149-107]"But not the opinion of Mrs. Van. The bird told me another thing,

[149-108]Conway."

[149-109]"What was the other thing?"

[149-110]"The bird hinted that all this would end in your marrying the widow

[149-111]of that poor wretch who destroyed himself."

[149-112]"Johnny, my boy," said the artist, after a moment's silence, "if I

[149-113]give you a bit of advice, will you profit by it?"

[149-114]"I'll try, if it's not disagreeable."

[149-115]"Whether you profit by it, or whether you do not, keep it to

[149-116]yourself. I know the bird better than you do, and I strongly caution

[149-117]you to beware of the bird. The bird is a bird of prey, and altogether

[149-118]an unclean bird. The bird wants a mate and doesn't much care how she

[149-119]finds one. And the bird wants money, and doesn't much care how she

[149-120]gets it. The bird is a decidedly bad bird, and not at all fit to take

[149-121]the place of domestic hen in a decent farmyard. In plain English,

[149-122]Johnny, you'll find some day, if you go over too often to Porchester

[149-123]Terrace, either that you are going to marry the bird, or else that

[149-124]you are employing your cousin Toogood for your defence in an action

[149-125]for breach of promise, brought against you by that venerable old

[149-126]bird, the bird's mamma."

[149-127]"If it's to be either, it will be the latter," said Johnny as he took

[149-128]up his hat to go away.

[149-129]CHAPTER LXXVI.

[149-130]I THINK HE IS LIGHT OF HEART.

[149-131][Illustration]

[149-132]Mrs. Arabin remained one day in town. Mr. Toogood, in spite of his

[149-133]asseveration that he would not budge from Barchester till he had seen

[149-134]Mr. Crawley through all his troubles, did run up to London as soon

[149-135]as the news reached him that John Eames had returned. He came up and

[149-136]took Mrs. Arabin's deposition, which he sent down to Mr. Walker. It

[149-137]might still be necessary, Mrs. Arabin was told, that she should go

[149-138]into court, and there state on oath that she had given the cheque to

[149-139]Mr. Crawley; but Mr. Walker was of opinion that the circumstances

[149-140]would enable the judge to call upon the grand jury not to find a true

[149-141]bill against Mr. Crawley, and that the whole affair, as far as Mr.

[149-142]Crawley was concerned, would thus be brought to an end. Toogood was

[149-143]still very anxious to place Dan Stringer in the dock, but Mr. Walker

[149-144]declared that they would fail if they made the attempt. Dan had been

[149-145]examined before the magistrates at Barchester, and had persisted

[149-146]in his statement that he had heard nothing about Mr. Crawley and

[149-147]the cheque. This he said in the teeth of the words which had fallen

[149-148]from him unawares in the presence of Mr. Toogood. But they could not

[149-149]punish him for a lie,--not even for such a lie as that! He was not

[149-150]upon oath, and they could not make him responsible to the law because

[149-151]he had held his tongue upon a matter as to which it was manifest

[149-152]to them all that he had known the whole history during the entire

[149-153]period of Mr. Crawley's persecution. They could only call upon him to

[149-154]account for his possession of the cheque, and this he did by saying

[149-155]it had been paid to him by Jem Scuttle, who received all moneys

[149-156]appertaining to the hotel stables, and accounted for them once a

[149-157]week. Jem Scuttle had simply told him that he had taken the cheque

[149-158]from Mr. Soames, and Jem had since gone to New Zealand. It was

[149-159]quite true that Jem's departure had followed suspiciously close upon

[149-160]the payment of the rent to Mrs. Arabin, and that Jem had been in

[149-161]close amity with Dan Stringer up to the moment of his departure.

[149-162]That Dan Stringer had not become honestly possessed of the cheque,

[149-163]everybody knew; but, nevertheless, the magistrates were of opinion,

[149-164]Mr. Walker coinciding with them, that there was no evidence against

[149-165]him sufficient to secure a conviction. The story, however, of Mr.

[149-166]Crawley's injuries was so well known in Barchester, and the feeling

[149-167]against the man who had permitted him to be thus injured was

[149-168]so strong, that Dan Stringer did not altogether escape without

[149-169]punishment. Some rough spirits in Barchester called one night at "The

[149-170]Dragon of Wantly," and begged that Mr. Dan Stringer would be kind

[149-171]enough to come out and take a walk with them that evening; and when

[149-172]it was intimated to them that Dan Stringer had not just then any

[149-173]desire for such exercise, they requested to be allowed to go into the

[149-174]back parlour and make an evening with Dan Stringer in that recess.

[149-175]There was a terrible row at "The Dragon of Wantly" that night, and

[149-176]Dan with difficulty was rescued by the police. On the following

[149-177]morning he was smuggled out of Barchester by an early train, and has

[149-178]never more been seen in that city. Rumours of him, however, were soon

[149-179]heard, from which it appeared that he had made himself acquainted

[149-180]with the casual ward of more than one workhouse in London. His cousin

[149-181]John left the inn almost immediately,--as, indeed, he must have done

[149-182]had there been no question of Mr. Soames's cheque,--and then there

[149-183]was nothing more heard of the Stringers in Barchester.

[149-184]Mrs. Arabin remained in town one day, and would have remained longer,

[149-185]waiting for her husband, had not a letter from her sister impressed

[149-186]upon her that it might be as well that she should be with their

[149-187]father as soon as possible. "I don't mean to make you think that

[149-188]there is any immediate danger," Mrs. Grantly said, "and, indeed, we

[149-189]cannot say that he is ill; but it seems that the extremity of old age

[149-190]has come upon him almost suddenly, and that he is as weak as a child.

[149-191]His only delight is with the children, especially with Posy, whose

[149-192]gravity in her management of him is wonderful. He has not left his

[149-193]room now for more than a week, and he eats very little. It may be

[149-194]that he will live yet for years; but I should be deceiving you if I

[149-195]did not let you know that both the archdeacon and I think that the

[149-196]time of his departure from us is near at hand." After reading this

[149-197]letter, Mrs. Arabin could not wait in town for her husband, even

[149-198]though he was expected in two days, and though she had been told that

[149-199]her presence at Barchester was not immediately required on behalf of

[149-200]Mr. Crawley.
