[144-001]"Does he ever pay your rent for you?"

[144-002]"Well, yes; he does, at times. He pays it into the bank for the

[144-003]lady as owns the house. Is there any reason for your asking these

[144-004]questions, sir? It isn't usual, you know, for a stranger, sir."

[144-005]Toogood during the whole of this time was standing with his eye

[144-006]upon the red-nosed man, and the red-nosed man could not move. The

[144-007]red-nosed man heard all the questions and the landlord's answers, and

[144-008]could not even pretend that he did not hear them. "I am my cousin's

[144-009]clerk," said he, putting on his hat, and coming up to Mr. Toogood

[144-010]with a swagger. "My name is Dan Stringer, and I'm Mr. John Stringer's

[144-011]cousin. I've lived with Mr. John Stringer for twelve year and more,

[144-012]and I'm a'most as well known in Barchester as himself. Have you

[144-013]anything to say to me, sir?"

[144-014]"Well, yes; I have," said Toogood.

[144-015]"I believe you're one of them attorneys from London?" said Mr. Dan

[144-016]Stringer.

[144-017]"That's true. I am an attorney from London."

[144-018]"I hope there's nothing wrong?" said the gouty man, trying to get off

[144-019]his chair, but not succeeding. "If there is anything wronger than

[144-020]usual, Dan, do tell me. Is there anything wrong, sir?" and the

[144-021]landlord appealed piteously to Mr. Toogood.

[144-022]"Never you mind, John," said Dan. "You keep yourself quiet, and don't

[144-023]answer none of his questions. He's one of them low sort, he is. I

[144-024]know him. I knowed him for what he is directly I saw him. Ferreting

[144-025]about,--that's his game; to see if there's anything to be got."

[144-026]"But what is he ferreting here for?" said Mr. John Stringer.

[144-027]"I'm ferreting for Mr. Soames's cheque for twenty pounds," said Mr.

[144-028]Toogood.

[144-029]"That's the cheque that the parson stole," said Dan Stringer. "He's

[144-030]to be tried for it at the 'sizes."

[144-031]"You've heard about Mr. Soames and his cheque, and about Mr. Crawley,

[144-032]I daresay?" said Toogood.

[144-033]"I've heard a deal about them," said the landlord.

[144-034]"And so, I daresay, have you?" said Toogood, turning to Dan Stringer.

[144-035]But Dan Stringer did not seem inclined to carry on the conversation

[144-036]any further. When he was hardly pressed, he declared that he just had

[144-037]heard that there was some parson in trouble about a sum of money; but

[144-038]that he knew no more about it than that. He didn't know whether it

[144-039]was a cheque or a note that the parson had taken, and had never been

[144-040]sufficiently interested in the matter to make any inquiry.

[144-041]"But you've just said that Mr. Soames's cheque was the cheque the

[144-042]parson stole," said the astonished landlord, turning with open eyes

[144-043]upon his cousin.

[144-044]"You be blowed," said Dan Stringer, the clerk, to Mr. John Stringer,

[144-045]the landlord; and then walked out of the room back to the bar.

[144-046]"I understand nothing about it,--nothing at all," said the gouty man.

[144-047]"I understand pretty nearly all about it," said Mr. Toogood,

[144-048]following the red-nosed clerk. There was no necessity that he should

[144-049]trouble the landlord any further. He left the room, and went through

[144-050]the bar, and as he passed out along the hall, he found Dan Stringer

[144-051]with his hat on talking to the waiter. The waiter immediately pulled

[144-052]himself up, and adjusted his dirty napkin under his arm, after the

[144-053]fashion of waiters, and showed that he intended to be civil to the

[144-054]customers of the house. But he of the red nose cocked his hat, and

[144-055]looked with insolence at Mr. Toogood, and defied him. "There's

[144-056]nothing I do hate so much as them low-bred Old Bailey attorneys,"

[144-057]said Mr. Dan Stringer to the waiter, in a voice intended to reach Mr.

[144-058]Toogood's ears. Then Mr. Toogood told himself that Dan Stringer was

[144-059]not the thief himself, and that it might be very difficult to prove

[144-060]that Dan had even been the receiver of stolen goods. He had, however,

[144-061]no doubt in his own mind but that such was the case.

[144-062]He first went to the police office, and there explained his business.

[144-063]Nobody at the police office pretended to forget Mr. Soames's cheque,

[144-064]or Mr. Crawley's position. The constable went so far as to swear that

[144-065]there wasn't a man, woman, or child in all Barchester who was not

[144-066]talking of Mr. Crawley at that very moment. Then Mr. Toogood went

[144-067]with the constable to the private house of the mayor, and had a

[144-068]little conversation with the mayor. "Not guilty!" said the mayor,

[144-069]with incredulity, when he first heard the news about Crawley. But

[144-070]when he heard Mr. Toogood's story, or as much of it as it was

[144-071]necessary that he should hear, he yielded reluctantly. "Dear, dear!"

[144-072]he said. "I'd have bet anything 'twas he who stole it." And after

[144-073]that the mayor was quite sad. Only let us think what a comfortable

[144-074]excitement it would create throughout England if it was surmised that

[144-075]an archbishop had forged a deed; and how much England would lose when

[144-076]it was discovered that the archbishop was innocent! As the archbishop

[144-077]and his forgery would be to England, so was Mr. Crawley and the

[144-078]cheque for twenty pounds to Barchester and its mayor. Nevertheless,

[144-079]the mayor promised his assistance to Mr. Toogood.

[144-080]Mr. Toogood, still neglecting his red-nosed friend, went next to the

[144-081]deanery, hoping that he might again see Mr. Harding. Mr. Harding

[144-082]was, he was told, too ill to be seen. Mr. Harding, Mrs. Baxter said,

[144-083]could never be seen now by strangers, nor yet by friends, unless they

[144-084]were very old friends. "There's been a deal of change since you were

[144-085]here last, sir. I remember your coming, sir. You were talking to Mr.

[144-086]Harding about the poor clergyman as is to be tried." He did not stop

[144-087]to tell Mrs. Baxter the whole story of Mr. Crawley's innocence; but

[144-088]having learned that a message had been received to say that Mrs.

[144-089]Arabin would be home on the next Tuesday,--this being Friday,--he

[144-090]took his leave of Mrs. Baxter. His next visit was to Mr. Soames, who

[144-091]lived three miles out in the country.

[144-092]He found it very difficult to convince Mr. Soames. Mr. Soames was

[144-093]more staunch in his belief of Mr. Crawley's guilt than any one whom

[144-094]Toogood had yet encountered. "I never took the cheque out of his

[144-095]house," said Mr. Soames. "But you have not stated that on oath," said

[144-096]Mr. Toogood. "No," rejoined the other; "and I never will. I can't

[144-097]swear to it; but yet I'm sure of it." He acknowledged that he had

[144-098]been driven by a man named Scuttle, and that Scuttle might have

[144-099]picked up the cheque, if it had been dropped in the gig. But the

[144-100]cheque had not been dropped in the gig. The cheque had been dropped

[144-101]in Mr. Crawley's house. "Why did he say then that I paid it to him?"

[144-102]said Mr. Soames, when Mr. Toogood spoke confidently of Crawley's

[144-103]innocence. "Ah, why indeed?" answered Toogood. "If he had not been

[144-104]fool enough to do that, we should have been saved all this trouble.

[144-105]All the same, he did not steal your money, Mr. Soames; and Jem

[144-106]Scuttle did steal it. Unfortunately, Jem Scuttle is in New Zealand by

[144-107]this time." "Of course, it is possible," said Mr. Soames, as he bowed

[144-108]Mr. Toogood out. Mr. Soames did not like Mr. Toogood.

[144-109]That evening a gentleman with a red nose asked at the Barchester

[144-110]station for a second-class ticket for London by the up night-mail

[144-111]train. He was well known at the station, and the station-master made

[144-112]some little inquiry. "All the way to London to-night, Mr. Stringer?"

[144-113]he said.

[144-114]"Yes,--all the way," said the red-nosed man, sulkily.

[144-115]"I don't think you'd better go to London to-night, Mr. Stringer,"

[144-116]said a tall man, stepping out of the door of the booking-office. "I

[144-117]think you'd better come back with me to Barchester. I do indeed."

[144-118]There was some little argument on the occasion; but the stranger, who

[144-119]was a detective policeman, carried his point, and Mr. Dan Stringer

[144-120]did return to Barchester.

[144-121]CHAPTER LXXIII.

[144-122]THERE IS COMFORT AT PLUMSTEAD.

[144-123][Illustration]

[144-124]Henry Grantly had written the following short letter to Mrs. Grantly

[144-125]when he made up his mind to pull down the auctioneer's bills.

[144-126]DEAR MOTHER,--

[144-127]I have postponed the sale, not liking to refuse you

[144-128]anything. As far as I can see, I shall still be forced to

[144-129]leave Cosby Lodge, as I certainly shall do all I can to

[144-130]make Grace Crawley my wife. I say this that there may be

[144-131]no misunderstanding with my father. The auctioneer has

[144-132]promised to have the bills removed.

[144-133]Your affectionate son,

[144-134]HENRY GRANTLY.

[144-135]This had been written by the major on the Friday before Mr. Walker

[144-136]had brought up to him the tidings of Mr. Toogood and Mrs. Arabin's

[144-137]solution of the Crawley difficulty; but it did not reach Plumstead

[144-138]till the following morning. Mrs. Grantly immediately took the good

[144-139]news about the sale to her husband,--not of course showing him the

[144-140]letter, being far too wise for that, and giving him credit for being

[144-141]too wise to ask for it. "Henry has arranged with the auctioneer," she

[144-142]said joyfully; "and the bills have been all pulled down."

[144-143]"How do you know?"

[144-144]"I've just heard from him. He has told me so. Come, my dear, let me

[144-145]have the pleasure of hearing you say that things shall be pleasant

[144-146]again between you and him. He has yielded."

[144-147]"I don't see much yielding in it."

[144-148]"He has done what you wanted. What more can he do?"

[144-149]"I want him to come over here, and take an interest in things, and

[144-150]not treat me as though I were nobody." Within an hour of this the

[144-151]major had arrived at Plumstead, laden with the story of Mrs. Arabin

[144-152]and the cheque, and of Mr. Crawley's innocence,--laden not only

[144-153]with such tidings as he had received from Mr. Walker, but also with

[144-154]further details, which he had received from Mr. Toogood. For he

[144-155]had come through Barchester, and had seen Mr. Toogood on his way.

[144-156]This was on the Saturday morning, and he had breakfasted with Mr.

[144-157]Toogood at "The Dragon of Wantly." Mr. Toogood had told him of his

[144-158]suspicions,--how the red-nosed man had been stopped, and had been

[144-159]summoned as a witness for Mr. Crawley's trial,--and how he was now

[144-160]under the surveillance of the police. Grantly had not cared very much

[144-161]about the red-nosed man, confining his present solicitude to the

[144-162]question whether Grace Crawley's father would certainly be shown to

[144-163]have been innocent of the theft. "There's not a doubt about it,

[144-164]major," said Mr. Toogood; "not a doubt on earth. But we'd better be a

[144-165]little quiet till your aunt comes home,--just a little quiet. She'll

[144-166]be here in a day or two, and I won't budge till she comes." In spite

[144-167]of his desire for quiescence Mr. Toogood consented to a revelation

[144-168]being at once made to the archdeacon and Mrs. Grantly. "And I'll tell

[144-169]you what, major; as soon as ever Mrs. Arabin is here, and has given

[144-170]us her own word to act on, you and I will go over to Hogglestock and

[144-171]astonish them. I should like to go myself, because, you see, Mrs.

[144-172]Crawley is my cousin, and we have taken a little trouble about this

[144-173]matter." To this the major assented; but he altogether declined to

[144-174]assist in Mr. Toogood's speculations respecting the unfortunate Dan

[144-175]Stringer. It was agreed between them that for the present no visit

[144-176]should be made to the palace, as it was thought that Mr. Thumble had

[144-177]better be allowed to do the Hogglestock duties on the next Sunday.

[144-178]As matters went, however, Mr. Thumble did not do so. He had paid his

[144-179]last visit to Hogglestock.

[144-180]It may be as well to explain here that the unfortunate Mr. Snapper

[144-181]was constrained to go out to Hogglestock on the Sunday which was now

[144-182]approaching,--which fell out as follows. It might be all very well

[144-183]for Mr. Toogood to arrange that he would not tell this person or that

[144-184]person of the news which he had brought down from London; but as he

[144-185]had told various people in Silverbridge, as he had told Mr. Soames,

[144-186]and as he had told the police at Barchester, of course the tale found

[144-187]its way to the palace. Mr. Thumble heard it, and having come by this

[144-188]time thoroughly to hate Hogglestock and all that belonged to it, he

[144-189]pleaded to Mr. Snapper that this report afforded ample reason why he

[144-190]need not again visit that detestable parish. Mr. Snapper did not see

[144-191]it in the same light. "You may be sure Mr. Crawley will not get into

[144-192]the pulpit after his resignation, Mr. Thumble," said he.
