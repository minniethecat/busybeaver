[111-001]"But is he engaged to her?"

[111-002]"He says that she has refused him. But of course that means nothing."

[111-003]Again the archdeacon's position was very like Lady Lufton's position,

[111-004]as it had existed before her son's marriage. In that case also the

[111-005]young lady, who was now Lady Lufton's own daughter and dearest

[111-006]friend, had refused the lover who proposed to her, although the

[111-007]marriage was so much to her advantage,--loving him, too, the while,

[111-008]with her whole heart, as it was natural to suppose that Grace Crawley

[111-009]might so love her lover. The more she thought of the similarity of

[111-010]the stories, the stronger were her sympathies on the side of poor

[111-011]Grace. Nevertheless, she would comfort her old friend if she knew

[111-012]how; and of course she could not but admit to herself that the match

[111-013]was one which must be a cause of real sorrow to him. "I don't know

[111-014]why her refusal should mean nothing," said Lady Lufton.

[111-015]"Of course a girl refuses at first,--a girl, I mean, in such

[111-016]circumstances as hers. She can't but feel that more is offered to

[111-017]her than she ought to take, and that she is bound to go through the

[111-018]ceremony of declining. But my anger is not with her, Lady Lufton."

[111-019]"I do not see how it can be."

[111-020]"No; it is not with her. If she becomes his wife I trust that I may

[111-021]never see her."

[111-022]"Oh, Dr. Grantly!"

[111-023]"I do; I do. How can it be otherwise with me? But I shall have no

[111-024]quarrel with her. With him I must quarrel."

[111-025]"I do not see why," said Lady Lufton.

[111-026]"You do not? Does he not set me at defiance?"

[111-027]"At his age surely a son has a right to marry as he pleases."

[111-028]"If he took her out of the streets, then it would be the same?" said

[111-029]the archdeacon with bitter anger.

[111-030]"No;--for such a one would herself be bad."

[111-031]"Or if she were the daughter of a huxter out of the city?"

[111-032]"No again;--for in that case her want of education would probably

[111-033]unfit her for your society."

[111-034]"Her father's disgrace, then, should be a matter of indifference to

[111-035]me, Lady Lufton?"

[111-036]"I did not say so. In the first place, her father is not

[111-037]disgraced,--not as yet; and we do not know whether he may ever be

[111-038]disgraced. You will hardly be disposed to say that persecution from

[111-039]the palace disgraces a clergyman in Barsetshire."

[111-040]"All the same, I believe that the man was guilty," said the

[111-041]archdeacon.

[111-042]"Wait and see, my friend, before you condemn him altogether. But, be

[111-043]that as it may, I acknowledge that the marriage is one which must

[111-044]naturally be distasteful to you."

[111-045]"Oh, Lady Lufton! if you only knew! If you only knew!"

[111-046]"I do know; and I feel for you. But I think that your son has a

[111-047]right to expect that you should not show the same repugnance to such

[111-048]a marriage as this as you would have had a right to show had he

[111-049]suggested to himself such a wife as those at which you just now

[111-050]hinted. Of course you can advise him, and make him understand your

[111-051]feelings; but I cannot think you will be justified in quarrelling

[111-052]with him, or in changing your views towards him as regards money,

[111-053]seeing that Miss Crawley is an educated lady, who has done nothing to

[111-054]forfeit your respect." A heavy cloud came upon the archdeacon's brow

[111-055]as he heard these words, but he did not make any immediate answer.

[111-056]"Of course, my friend," continued Lady Lufton, "I should not have

[111-057]ventured to say so much to you, had you not come to me, as it were,

[111-058]for my opinion."

[111-059]"I came here because I thought Henry was here," said the archdeacon.

[111-060]"If I have said too much I beg your pardon."

[111-061]"No; you have not said too much. It is not that. You and I are such

[111-062]old friends that either may say almost anything to the other."

[111-063]"Yes;--just so. And therefore I have ventured to speak my mind," said

[111-064]Lady Lufton.

[111-065]"Of course;--and I am obliged to you. But, Lady Lufton, you do not

[111-066]understand yet how this hits me. Everything in life that I have done,

[111-067]I have done for my children. I am wealthy, but I have not used my

[111-068]wealth for myself, because I have desired that they should be able

[111-069]to hold their heads high in the world. All my ambition has been for

[111-070]them, and all the pleasure which I have anticipated for myself in

[111-071]my old age is that which I have hoped to receive from their credit.

[111-072]As for Henry, he might have had anything he wanted from me in the

[111-073]way of money. He expressed a wish, a few months since, to go into

[111-074]Parliament, and I promised to help him as far as ever I could go. I

[111-075]have kept up the game altogether for him. He, the younger son of a

[111-076]working parish parson, has had everything that could be given to the

[111-077]eldest son of a country gentleman,--more than is given to the eldest

[111-078]son of many a peer. I have hoped that he would marry again, but I

[111-079]have never cared that he should marry for money. I have been willing

[111-080]to do anything for him myself. But, Lady Lufton, a father does feel

[111-081]that he should have some return for all this. No one can imagine

[111-082]that Henry ever supposed that a bride from that wretched place at

[111-083]Hogglestock could be welcomed among us. He knew that he would break

[111-084]our hearts, and he did not care for it. That is what I feel. Of

[111-085]course he has the power to do as he likes;--and of course I have the

[111-086]power to do as I like also with what is my own."

[111-087]Lady Lufton was a very good woman, devoted to her duties,

[111-088]affectionate and just to those about her, truly religious, and

[111-089]charitable from her nature; but I doubt whether the thorough

[111-090]worldliness of the archdeacon's appeal struck her as it will strike

[111-091]the reader. People are so much more worldly in practice than they

[111-092]are in theory, so much keener after their own gratification in

[111-093]detail than they are in the abstract, that the narrative of many an

[111-094]adventure would shock us, though the same adventure would not shock

[111-095]us in the action. One girl tells another how she has changed her mind

[111-096]in love; and the friend sympathizes with the friend, and perhaps

[111-097]applauds. Had the story been told in print, the friend who had

[111-098]listened with equanimity would have read of such vacillation with

[111-099]indignation. She who vacillated herself would have hated her own

[111-100]performance when brought before her judgment as a matter in which

[111-101]she had no personal interest. Very fine things are written every day

[111-102]about honesty and truth, and men read them with a sort of external

[111-103]conviction that a man, if he be anything of a man at all, is of

[111-104]course honest and true. But when the internal convictions are

[111-105]brought out between two or three who are personally interested

[111-106]together,--between two or three who feel that their little gathering

[111-107]is, so to say, "tiled,"--those internal convictions differ very much

[111-108]from the external convictions. This man, in his confidences, asserts

[111-109]broadly that he does not mean to be thrown over, and that man has a

[111-110]project for throwing over somebody else; and the intention of each is

[111-111]that scruples are not to stand in the way of his success. The "Ruat

[111-112]coelum, fiat justitia," was said, no doubt, from an outside balcony

[111-113]to a crowd, and the speaker knew that he was talking buncombe. The

[111-114]"Rem, si possis recte, si non, quocunque modo," was whispered into

[111-115]the ear in a club smoking-room, and the whisperer intended that his

[111-116]words should prevail.

[111-117]Lady Lufton had often heard her friend the archdeacon preach, and

[111-118]she knew well the high tone which he could take as to the necessity

[111-119]of trusting to our hopes for the future for all our true happiness;

[111-120]and yet she sympathized with him when he told her that he was

[111-121]broken-hearted because his son would take a step which might possibly

[111-122]interfere with his worldly prosperity. Had the archdeacon been

[111-123]preaching about matrimony, he would have recommended young men, in

[111-124]taking wives to themselves, especially to look for young women who

[111-125]feared the Lord. But in talking about his own son's wife, no word

[111-126]as to her eligibility or non-eligibility in this respect escaped

[111-127]his lips. Had he talked on the subject till nightfall no such word

[111-128]would have been spoken. Had any friend of his own, man or woman,

[111-129]in discussing such a matter with him and asking his advice upon

[111-130]it, alluded to the fear of the Lord, the allusion would have been

[111-131]distasteful to him and would have smacked to his palate of hypocrisy.

[111-132]Lady Lufton, who understood as well as any woman what it was to be

[111-133]"tiled" with a friend, took all this in good part. The archdeacon

[111-134]had spoken out of his heart what was in his heart. One of his

[111-135]children had married a marquis. Another might probably become

[111-136]a bishop,--perhaps an archbishop. The third might be a county

[111-137]squire,--high among county squires. But he could only so become

[111-138]by walking warily;--and now he was bent on marrying the penniless

[111-139]daughter of an impoverished half-mad country curate, who was about to

[111-140]be tried for stealing twenty pounds! Lady Lufton, in spite of all her

[111-141]arguments, could not refuse her sympathy to her old friend.

[111-142]"After all, from what you say, I suppose they are not engaged."

[111-143]"I do not know," said the archdeacon. "I cannot tell!"

[111-144]"And what do you wish me to do?"

[111-145]"Oh,--nothing. I came over, as I said before, because I thought

[111-146]he was here. I think it right, before he has absolutely committed

[111-147]himself, to take every means in my power to make him understand that

[111-148]I shall withdraw from him all pecuniary assistance,--now and for the

[111-149]future."

[111-150]"My friend, that threat seems to me to be so terrible."

[111-151]"It is the only power I have left to me."

[111-152]"But you, who are so affectionate by nature, would never adhere to

[111-153]it."

[111-154]"I will try. I will do my best to be firm. I will at once put

[111-155]everything beyond my control after my death." The archdeacon, as

[111-156]he uttered these terrible words,--words which were awful to Lady

[111-157]Lufton's ears,--resolved that he would endeavour to nurse his own

[111-158]wrath; but, at the same time, almost hated himself for his own

[111-159]pusillanimity, because he feared that his wrath would die away before

[111-160]he should have availed himself of its heat.

[111-161]"I would do nothing rash of that kind," said Lady Lufton. "Your

[111-162]object is to prevent the marriage,--not to punish him for it when

[111-163]once he has made it."

[111-164]"He is not to have his own way in everything, Lady Lufton."

[111-165]"But you should first try to prevent it."

[111-166]"What can I do to prevent it?"

[111-167]Lady Lufton paused for a couple of minutes before she replied. She

[111-168]had a scheme in her head, but it seemed to her to savour of cruelty.

[111-169]And yet at present it was her chief duty to assist her old friend,

[111-170]if any assistance could be given. There could hardly be a doubt that

[111-171]such a marriage as this, of which they were speaking, was in itself

[111-172]an evil. In her case, the case of her son, there had been no question

[111-173]of a trial, of money stolen, of aught that was in truth disgraceful.

[111-174]"I think if I were you, Dr. Grantly," she said, "that I would see the

[111-175]young lady while I was here."

[111-176]"See her myself?" said the archdeacon. The idea of seeing Grace

[111-177]Crawley himself had, up to this moment, never entered his head.

[111-178]"I think I would do so."
