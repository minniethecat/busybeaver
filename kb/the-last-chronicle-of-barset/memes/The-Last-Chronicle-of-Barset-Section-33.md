[033-001]On the following morning he consented to get into farmer Mangle's

[033-002]cart, and was driven as far as Framley Mill. "I wouldn't think

[033-003]nowt, your reverence, of running you over into Barchester,--that I

[033-004]wouldn't. The powny is so mortial good," said farmer Mangle in his

[033-005]foolish good-nature.

[Illustration: Farmer Mangle and Mr. Crawley.]

[033-006]"And how about your business here?" said Mr. Crawley. The farmer

[033-007]scratched his head, remembering all Mrs. Crawley's injunctions, and

[033-008]awkwardly acknowledged that to be sure his own business with the

[033-009]miller was very pressing. Then Mr. Crawley descended, terribly

[033-010]suspicious, and went on his journey.

[033-011]"Anyways, your reverence will call for me coming back?" said farmer

[033-012]Mangle. But Mr. Crawley would make no promise. He bade the farmer not

[033-013]wait for him. If they chanced to meet together on the road he might

[033-014]get up again. If the man really had business at Framley, how could

[033-015]he have offered to go on to Barchester? Were they deceiving him? The

[033-016]wife of his bosom had deceived him in such matters before now. But

[033-017]his trouble in this respect was soon dissipated by the pride of his

[033-018]anticipated triumph over the bishop. He took great glory from the

[033-019]thought that he would go before the bishop with dirty boots,--with

[033-020]boots necessarily dirty,--with rusty pantaloons, that he would be hot

[033-021]and mud-stained with his walk, hungry, and an object to be wondered

[033-022]at by all who should see him, because of the misfortunes which had

[033-023]been unworthily heaped upon his head; whereas the bishop would be

[033-024]sleek and clean and well-fed,--pretty with all the prettinesses that

[033-025]are becoming to a bishop's outward man. And he, Mr. Crawley, would be

[033-026]humble, whereas the bishop would be very proud. And the bishop would

[033-027]be in his own arm-chair,--the cock in his own farmyard, while he, Mr.

[033-028]Crawley, would be seated afar off, in the cold extremity of the room,

[033-029]with nothing of outward circumstances to assist him,--a man called

[033-030]thither to undergo censure. And yet he would take the bishop in his

[033-031]grasp and crush him,--crush him,--crush him! As he thought of this

[033-032]he walked quickly through the mud, and put out his long arm and his

[033-033]great hand, far before him out into the air, and, there and then, he

[033-034]crushed the bishop in his imagination. Yes, indeed! He thought it

[033-035]very doubtful whether the bishop would ever send for him a second

[033-036]time. As all this passed through his mind, he forgot his wife's

[033-037]cunning, and farmer Mangle's sin, and for the moment he was happy.

[033-038]As he turned a corner round by Lord Lufton's park paling, who should

[033-039]he meet but his old friend Mr. Robarts, the parson of Framley,--the

[033-040]parson who had committed the sin of being bail for him,--the sin,

[033-041]that is, according to Mrs. Proudie's view of the matter. He was

[033-042]walking with his hand still stretched out,--still crushing the

[033-043]bishop, when Mr. Robarts was close upon him.

[033-044]"What, Crawley! upon my word I am very glad to see you; you are

[033-045]coming up to me, of course?"

[033-046]"Thank you, Mr. Robarts; no, not to-day. The bishop has summoned me

[033-047]to his presence, and I am on my road to Barchester."

[033-048]"But how are you going?"

[033-049]"I shall walk."

[033-050]"Walk to Barchester. Impossible!"

[033-051]"I hope not quite impossible, Mr. Robarts. I trust I shall get as

[033-052]far before two o'clock; but to do so I must be on my road." Then

[033-053]he showed signs of a desire to go on upon his way without further

[033-054]parley.

[033-055]"But, Crawley, do let me send you over. There is the horse and gig

[033-056]doing nothing."

[033-057]"Thank you, Mr. Robarts; no. I should prefer the walk to-day."

[033-058]"And you have walked from Hogglestock?"

[033-059]"No;--not so. A neighbour coming hither, who happened to have

[033-060]business at your mill,--he brought me so far in his cart. The walk

[033-061]home will be nothing,--nothing. I shall enjoy it. Good morning, Mr.

[033-062]Robarts."

[033-063]But Mr. Robarts thought of the dirty road, and of the bishop's

[033-064]presence, and of his own ideas of what would be becoming for a

[033-065]clergyman,--and persevered. "You will find the lanes so very muddy;

[033-066]and our bishop, you know, is apt to notice such things. Do be

[033-067]persuaded."

[033-068]"Notice what things?" demanded Mr. Crawley, in an indignant tone.

[033-069]"He, or perhaps she rather, will say how dirty your shoes were when

[033-070]you came to the palace."

[033-071]"If he, or she, can find nothing unclean about me but my shoes,

[033-072]let them say their worst. I shall be very indifferent. I have long

[033-073]ceased, Mr. Robarts, to care much what any man or woman may say

[033-074]about my shoes. Good morning." Then he stalked on, clutching and

[033-075]crushing in his hand the bishop, and the bishop's wife, and the whole

[033-076]diocese,--and all the Church of England. Dirty shoes, indeed! Whose

[033-077]was the fault that there were in the church so many feet soiled by

[033-078]unmerited poverty, and so many hands soiled by undeserved wealth? If

[033-079]the bishop did not like his shoes, let the bishop dare to tell him

[033-080]so! So he walked on through the thick of the mud, by no means picking

[033-081]his way.

[033-082]He walked fast, and he found himself in the close half an hour before

[033-083]the time named by the bishop. But on no account would he have rung

[033-084]the palace bell one minute before two o'clock. So he walked up and

[033-085]down under the towers of the cathedral, and cooled himself, and

[033-086]looked up at the pleasant plate-glass in the windows of the house of

[033-087]his friend the dean, and told himself how, in their college days, he

[033-088]and the dean had been quite equal,--quite equal, except that by the

[033-089]voices of all qualified judges in the university, he, Mr. Crawley,

[033-090]had been acknowledged to be the riper scholar. And now the Mr. Arabin

[033-091]of those days was Dean of Barchester,--travelling abroad luxuriously

[033-092]at this moment for his delight, while he, Crawley, was perpetual

[033-093]curate at Hogglestock, and had now walked into Barchester at the

[033-094]command of the bishop, because he was suspected of having stolen

[033-095]twenty pounds! When he had fully imbued his mind with the injustice

[033-096]of all this, his time was up, and he walked boldly to the bishop's

[033-097]gate, and boldly rang the bishop's bell.

[033-098]CHAPTER XVIII.

[033-099]THE BISHOP OF BARCHESTER IS CRUSHED.

[033-100]Who inquires why it is that a little greased flour rubbed in among

[033-101]the hair on a footman's head,--just one dab here and another

[033-102]there,--gives such a tone of high life to the family? And seeing

[033-103]that the thing is so easily done, why do not more people attempt it?

[033-104]The tax on hair-powder is but thirteen shillings a year. It may,

[033-105]indeed, be that the slightest dab in the world justifies the wearer

[033-106]in demanding hot meat three times a day, and wine at any rate on

[033-107]Sundays. I think, however, that a bishop's wife may enjoy the

[033-108]privilege without such heavy attendant expense; otherwise the man who

[033-109]opened the bishop's door to Mr. Crawley would hardly have been so

[033-110]ornamented.

[033-111]The man asked for a card. "My name is Mr. Crawley," said our friend.

[033-112]"The bishop has desired me to come to him at this hour. Will you be

[033-113]pleased to tell him that I am here." The man again asked for a card.

[033-114]"I am not bound to carry with me my name printed on a ticket," said

[033-115]Mr. Crawley. "If you cannot remember it, give me pen and paper, and I

[033-116]will write it." The servant, somewhat awed by the stranger's manner,

[033-117]brought the pen and paper, and Mr. Crawley wrote his name:--

[033-118]THE REV. JOSIAH CRAWLEY, M.A.,

[033-119]_Perpetual Curate of Hogglestock._

[033-120]He was then ushered into a waiting-room, but, to his disappointment,

[033-121]was not kept there waiting long. Within three minutes he was ushered

[033-122]into the bishop's study, and into the presence of the two great

[033-123]luminaries of the diocese. He was at first somewhat disconcerted by

[033-124]finding Mrs. Proudie in the room. In the imaginary conversation with

[033-125]the bishop which he had been preparing on the road, he had conceived

[033-126]that the bishop would be attended by a chaplain, and he had suited

[033-127]his words to the joint discomfiture of the bishop and of the lower

[033-128]clergyman;--but now the line of his battle must be altered. This

[033-129]was no doubt an injury, but he trusted to his courage and readiness

[033-130]to enable him to surmount it. He had left his hat behind him in

[033-131]the waiting-room, but he kept his old short cloak still upon his

[033-132]shoulders; and when he entered the bishop's room his hands and arms

[033-133]were hid beneath it. There was something lowly in this constrained

[033-134]gait. It showed at least that he had no idea of being asked to

[033-135]shake hands with the August persons he might meet. And his head was

[033-136]somewhat bowed, though his great, bald, broad forehead showed itself

[033-137]so prominent, that neither the bishop nor Mrs. Proudie could drop it

[033-138]from their sight during the whole interview. He was a man who when

[033-139]seen could hardly be forgotten. The deep angry remonstrant eyes, the

[033-140]shaggy eyebrows, telling tales of frequent anger,--of anger frequent

[033-141]but generally silent,--the repressed indignation of the habitual

[033-142]frown, the long nose and large powerful mouth, the deep furrows

[033-143]on the cheek, and the general look of thought and suffering, all

[033-144]combined to make the appearance of the man remarkable, and to

[033-145]describe to the beholders at once his true character. No one ever on

[033-146]seeing Mr. Crawley took him to be a happy man, or a weak man, or an

[033-147]ignorant man, or a wise man.

[033-148]"You are very punctual, Mr. Crawley," said the bishop. Mr. Crawley

[033-149]simply bowed his head, still keeping his hands beneath his cloak.

[033-150]"Will you not take a chair nearer to the fire?" Mr. Crawley had not

[033-151]seated himself, but had placed himself in front of a chair at the

[033-152]extreme end of the room,--resolved that he would not use it unless he

[033-153]were duly asked.

[033-154]"Thank you, my lord," he said, "I am warm with walking, and, if you

[033-155]please, will avoid the fire."

[033-156]"You have not walked, Mr. Crawley?"

[033-157]"Yes, my lord. I have been walking."

[033-158]"Not from Hogglestock!"

[033-159]Now this was a matter which Mr. Crawley certainly did not mean to

[033-160]discuss with the bishop. It might be well for the bishop to demand

[033-161]his presence in the palace, but it could be no part of the bishop's

[033-162]duty to inquire how he got there. "That, my lord, is a matter of no

[033-163]moment," said he. "I am glad at any rate that I have been enabled to

[033-164]obey your lordship's order in coming hither on this morning."

[033-165]Hitherto Mrs. Proudie had not said a word. She stood back in the

[033-166]room, near the fire,--more backward a good deal than she was

[033-167]accustomed to do when clergymen made their ordinary visits. On

[033-168]such occasions she would come forward and shake hands with them

[033-169]graciously,--graciously even, if proudly; but she had felt that she

[033-170]must do nothing of that kind now; there must be no shaking hands with

[033-171]a man who had stolen a cheque for twenty pounds! It might probably be

[033-172]necessary to keep Mr. Crawley at a distance, and therefore she had

[033-173]remained in the background. But Mr. Crawley seemed to be disposed to

[033-174]keep himself in the background, and therefore she could speak. "I

[033-175]hope your wife and children are well, Mr. Crawley," she said.

[033-176]"Thank you, madam, my children are well, and Mrs. Crawley suffers no

[033-177]special ailment at present."

[033-178]"That is much to be thankful for, Mr. Crawley." Whether he were or

[033-179]were not thankful for such mercies as these was no business of the

[033-180]bishop or of the bishop's wife. That was between him and his God. So

[033-181]he would not even bow to this civility, but sat with his head erect,

[033-182]and with a great frown on his heavy brow.
