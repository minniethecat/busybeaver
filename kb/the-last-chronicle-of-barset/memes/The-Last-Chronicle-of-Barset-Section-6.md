[006-001]"But there is something in it, sir."

[006-002]"What is there in it? Do not keep me in suspense, Henry. What is it

[006-003]you mean?"

[006-004]"It is rather hard to be cross-questioned in this way on such a

[006-005]subject. When you express yourself as thankful that there is nothing

[006-006]in the rumour, I am forced to stop you, as otherwise it is possible

[006-007]that hereafter you may say that I have deceived you."

[006-008]"But you don't mean to marry her?"

[006-009]"I certainly do not mean to pledge myself not to do so."

[006-010]"Do you mean to tell me, Henry, that you are in love with Miss

[006-011]Crawley?" Then there was another pause, during which the archdeacon

[006-012]sat looking for an answer; but the major said never a word. "Am I to

[006-013]suppose that you intend to lower yourself by marrying a young woman

[006-014]who cannot possibly have enjoyed any of the advantages of a lady's

[006-015]education? I say nothing of the imprudence of the thing; nothing of

[006-016]her own want of fortune; nothing of your having to maintain a whole

[006-017]family steeped in poverty; nothing of the debts and character of the

[006-018]father, upon whom, as I understand, at this moment there rests a very

[006-019]grave suspicion of--of--of--what I'm afraid I must call downright

[006-020]theft."

[006-021]"Downright theft, certainly, if he were guilty."

[006-022]"I say nothing of all that; but looking at the young woman herself--"

[006-023]"She is simply the best educated girl whom it has ever been my lot to

[006-024]meet."

[006-025]"Henry, I have a right to expect that you will be honest with me."

[006-026]"I am honest with you."

[006-027]"Do you mean to ask this girl to marry you?"

[006-028]"I do not think that you have any right to ask me that question,

[006-029]sir."

[006-030]"I have a right at any rate to tell you this, that if you so far

[006-031]disgrace yourself and me, I shall consider myself bound to withdraw

[006-032]from you all the sanction which would be conveyed by my--my--my

[006-033]continued assistance."

[006-034]"Do you intend me to understand that you will stop my income?"

[006-035]"Certainly I should."

[006-036]"Then, sir, I think you would behave to me most cruelly. You advised

[006-037]me to give up my profession."

[006-038]"Not in order that you might marry Grace Crawley."

[006-039]"I claim the privilege of a man of my age to do as I please in

[006-040]such a matter as marriage. Miss Crawley is a lady. Her father is a

[006-041]clergyman, as is mine. Her father's oldest friend is my uncle. There

[006-042]is nothing on earth against her except her poverty. I do not think I

[006-043]ever heard of such cruelty on a father's part."

[006-044]"Very well, Henry."

[006-045]"I have endeavoured to do my duty by you, sir, always; and by my

[006-046]mother. You can treat me in this way, if you please, but it will not

[006-047]have any effect on my conduct. You can stop my allowance to-morrow,

[006-048]if you like it. I had not as yet made up my mind to make an offer to

[006-049]Miss Crawley, but I shall now do so to-morrow morning."

[006-050]This was very bad indeed, and the archdeacon was extremely unhappy.

[006-051]He was by no means at heart a cruel man. He loved his children

[006-052]dearly. If this disagreeable marriage were to take place, he would

[006-053]doubtless do exactly as his wife had predicted. He would not stop his

[006-054]son's income for a single quarter; and, though he went on telling

[006-055]himself that he would stop it, he knew in his own heart that any

[006-056]such severity was beyond his power. He was a generous man in money

[006-057]matters,--having a dislike for poverty which was not generous,--and

[006-058]for his own sake could not have endured to see a son of his in want.

[006-059]But he was terribly anxious to exercise the power which the use of

[006-060]the threat might give him. "Henry," he said, "you are treating me

[006-061]badly, very badly. My anxiety has always been for the welfare of

[006-062]my children. Do you think that Miss Crawley would be a fitting

[006-063]sister-in-law for that dear girl upstairs?"

[006-064]"Certainly I do, or for any other dear girl in the world; excepting

[006-065]that Griselda, who is not clever, would hardly be able to appreciate

[006-066]Miss Crawley, who is clever."

[006-067]"Griselda not clever! Good heavens!" Then there was another pause,

[006-068]and as the major said nothing, the father continued his entreaties.

[006-069]"Pray, pray think of what my wishes are, and your mother's. You are

[006-070]not committed as yet. Pray think of us while there is time. I would

[006-071]rather double your income if I saw you marry any one that we could

[006-072]name here."

[006-073]"I have enough as it is, if I may only be allowed to know that it

[006-074]will not be capriciously withdrawn." The archdeacon filled his glass

[006-075]unconsciously, and sipped his wine, while he thought what further

[006-076]he might say. Perhaps it might be better that he should say nothing

[006-077]further at the present moment. The major, however, was indiscreet,

[006-078]and pushed the question. "May I understand, sir, that your threat is

[006-079]withdrawn, and that my income is secure?"

[006-080]"What, if you marry this girl?"

[006-081]"Yes, sir; will my income be continued to me if I marry Miss

[006-082]Crawley?"

[006-083]"No, it will not." Then the father got up hastily, pushed the

[006-084]decanter back angrily from his hand, and without saying another word

[006-085]walked away into the drawing-room. That evening at the rectory was

[006-086]very gloomy. The archdeacon now and again said a word or two to his

[006-087]daughter, and his daughter answered him in monosyllables. The major

[006-088]sat apart moodily, and spoke to no one. Mrs. Grantly, understanding

[006-089]well what had passed, knew that nothing could be done at the present

[006-090]moment to restore family comfort; so she sat by the fire and knitted.

[006-091]Exactly at ten they all went to bed.

[006-092]"Dear Henry," said the mother to her son the next morning; "think

[006-093]much of yourself, and of your child, and of us, before you take any

[006-094]great step in life."

[006-095]"I will, mother," said he. Then he went out and put on his wrapper,

[006-096]and got into his dog-cart, and drove himself off to Silverbridge. He

[006-097]had not spoken to his father since they were in the dining-room on

[006-098]the previous evening. When he started, the marchioness had not yet

[006-099]come downstairs; but at eleven she breakfasted, and at twelve she

[006-100]also was taken away. Poor Mrs. Grantly had not had much comfort from

[006-101]her children's visits.

[006-102]CHAPTER IV.

[006-103]THE CLERGYMAN'S HOUSE AT HOGGLESTOCK.

[006-104][Illustration]

[006-105]Mrs. Crawley had walked from Hogglestock to Silverbridge on the

[006-106]occasion of her visit to Mr. Walker, the attorney, and had been

[006-107]kindly sent back by that gentleman in his wife's little open

[006-108]carriage. The tidings she brought home with her to her husband were

[006-109]very grievous. The magistrates would sit on the next Thursday,--it

[006-110]was then Friday,--and Mr. Crawley had better appear before them to

[006-111]answer the charge made by Mr. Soames. He would be served with a

[006-112]summons, which he could obey of his own accord. There had been many

[006-113]points very closely discussed between Walker and Mrs. Crawley, as to

[006-114]which there had been great difficulty in the choice of words which

[006-115]should be tender enough in regard to the feelings of the poor lady,

[006-116]and yet strong enough to convey to her the very facts as they stood.

[006-117]Would Mr. Crawley come, or must a policeman be sent to fetch him? The

[006-118]magistrates had already issued a warrant for his apprehension. Such

[006-119]in truth was the fact, but they had agreed with Mr. Walker, that as

[006-120]there was no reasonable ground for anticipating any attempt at escape

[006-121]on the part of the reverend gentleman, the lawyer might use what

[006-122]gentle means he could for ensuring the clergyman's attendance. Could

[006-123]Mrs. Crawley undertake to say that he would appear? Mrs. Crawley

[006-124]did undertake either that her husband should appear on the Thursday,

[006-125]or else that she would send over in the early part of the week and

[006-126]declare her inability to ensure his appearance. In that case it was

[006-127]understood the policeman must come. Then Mr. Walker had suggested

[006-128]that Mr. Crawley had better employ a lawyer. Upon this Mrs. Crawley

[006-129]had looked beseechingly up into Mr. Walker's face, and had asked

[006-130]him to undertake the duty. He was of course obliged to explain that

[006-131]he was already employed on the other side. Mr. Soames had secured

[006-132]his services, and though he was willing to do all in his power to

[006-133]mitigate the sufferings of the family, he could not abandon the duty

[006-134]he had undertaken. He named another attorney, however, and then sent

[006-135]the poor woman home in his wife's carriage. "I fear that unfortunate

[006-136]man is guilty. I fear he is," Mr. Walker had said to his wife within

[006-137]ten minutes of the departure of the visitor.

[006-138]Mrs. Crawley would not allow herself to be driven up to the garden

[006-139]gate before her own house, but had left the carriage some three

[006-140]hundred yards off down the road, and from thence she walked home.

[006-141]It was now quite dark. It was nearly six in the evening on a wet

[006-142]December night, and although cloaks and shawls had been supplied to

[006-143]her, she was wet and cold when she reached her home. But at such a

[006-144]moment, anxious as she was to prevent the additional evil which would

[006-145]come to them all from illness to herself, she could not pass through

[006-146]to her room till she had spoken to her husband. He was sitting in

[006-147]the one sitting-room on the left side of the passage as the house

[006-148]was entered, and with him was their daughter Jane, a girl now nearly

[006-149]sixteen years of age. There was no light in the room, and hardly

[006-150]more than a spark of fire showed itself in the grate. The father was

[006-151]sitting on one side of the hearth, in an old arm-chair, and there he

[006-152]had sat for the last hour without speaking. His daughter had been in

[006-153]and out of the room, and had endeavoured to gain his attention now

[006-154]and again by a word, but he had never answered her, and had not even

[006-155]noticed her presence. At the moment when Mrs. Crawley's step was

[006-156]heard upon the gravel which led to the door, Jane was kneeling before

[006-157]the fire with a hand upon her father's arm. She had tried to get her

[006-158]hand into his, but he had either been unaware of the attempt, or had

[006-159]rejected it.

[006-160]"Here is mamma, at last," said Jane, rising to her feet as her mother

[006-161]entered the house.

[006-162]"Are you all in the dark?" said Mrs. Crawley, striving to speak in a

[006-163]voice that should not be sorrowful.

[006-164]"Yes, mamma; we are in the dark. Papa is here. Oh, mamma, how wet you

[006-165]are!"

[006-166]"Yes, dear. It is raining. Get a light out of the kitchen, Jane, and

[006-167]I will go upstairs in two minutes." Then, when Jane was gone, the

[006-168]wife made her way in the dark over to her husband's side, and spoke a

[006-169]word to him. "Josiah," she said, "will you not speak to me?"

[006-170]"What should I speak about? Where have you been?"

[006-171]"I have been to Silverbridge. I have been to Mr. Walker. He, at any

[006-172]rate, is very kind."

[006-173]"I don't want his kindness. I want no man's kindness. Mr. Walker is

[006-174]the attorney, I believe. Kind, indeed!"

[006-175]"I mean considerate. Josiah, let us do the best we can in this

[006-176]trouble. We have had others as heavy before."

[006-177]"But none to crush me as this will crush me. Well; what am I to do?

[006-178]Am I to go to prison--to-night?" At this moment his daughter returned

[006-179]with a candle, and the mother could not make her answer at once. It

[006-180]was a wretched, poverty-stricken room. By degrees the carpet had

[006-181]disappeared, which had been laid down some nine or ten years since,

[006-182]when they had first come to Hogglestock, and which even then had not

[006-183]been new. Now nothing but a poor fragment of it remained in front of

[006-184]the fire-place. In the middle of the room there was a table which

[006-185]had once been large; but one flap of it was gone altogether, and the

[006-186]other flap sloped grievously towards the floor, the weakness of old

[006-187]age having fallen into its legs. There were two or three smaller

[006-188]tables about, but they stood propped against walls, thence obtaining

[006-189]a security which their own strength would not give them. At the

[006-190]further end of the room there was an ancient piece of furniture,

[006-191]which was always called "papa's secretary," at which Mr. Crawley

[006-192]customarily sat and wrote his sermons, and did all work that was done

[006-193]by him within his house. The man who had made it, some time in the

[006-194]last century, had intended it to be a locked guardian for domestic

[006-195]documents, and the receptacle for all that was most private in the

[006-196]house of some paterfamilias. But beneath the hands of Mr. Crawley it

[006-197]always stood open; and with the exception of the small space at which

[006-198]he wrote, was covered with dog's-eared books, from nearly all of

[006-199]which the covers had disappeared. There were there two odd volumes of

[006-200]Euripides, a Greek Testament, an Odyssey, a duodecimo Pindar, and a

[006-201]miniature Anacreon. There was half a Horace,--the two first books of

[006-202]the Odes at the beginning, and the De Arte Poetica at the end having

[006-203]disappeared. There was a little bit of a volume of Cicero, and there

[006-204]were Cæsar's Commentaries, in two volumes, so stoutly bound that they

[006-205]had defied the combined ill-usage of time and the Crawley family. All

[006-206]these were piled upon the secretary, with many others,--odd volumes

[006-207]of sermons and the like; but the Greek and Latin lay at the top, and

[006-208]showed signs of most frequent use. There was one arm-chair in the

[006-209]room,--a Windsor-chair, as such used to be called, made soft by an

[006-210]old cushion in the back, in which Mr. Crawley sat when both he and

[006-211]his wife were in the room, and Mrs. Crawley when he was absent.

[006-212]And there was an old horsehair sofa,--now almost denuded of its

[006-213]horsehair,--but that, like the tables, required the assistance of a

[006-214]friendly wall. Then there was half a dozen of other chairs,--all of

[006-215]different sorts,--and they completed the furniture of the room. It

[006-216]was not such a room as one would wish to see inhabited by a beneficed

[006-217]clergyman of the Church of England; but they who know what money will

[006-218]do and what it will not, will understand how easily a man with a

[006-219]family, and with a hundred and thirty pounds a year, may be brought

[006-220]to the need of inhabiting such a chamber. When it is remembered that

[006-221]three pounds of meat a day, at ninepence a pound, will cost over

[006-222]forty pounds a year, there need be no difficulty in understanding

[006-223]that it may be so. Bread for such a family must cost at least

[006-224]twenty-five pounds. Clothes for five persons, of whom one must at any

[006-225]rate wear the raiment of a gentleman, can hardly be found for less

[006-226]than ten pounds a year a head. Then there remains fifteen pounds for

[006-227]tea, sugar, beer, wages, education, amusements, and the like. In such

[006-228]circumstances a gentleman can hardly pay much for the renewal of his

[006-229]furniture!
