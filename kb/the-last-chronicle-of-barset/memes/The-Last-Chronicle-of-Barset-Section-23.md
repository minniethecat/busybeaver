[023-002]"And they do say if a poor man had done it, it'd been stealing, for

[023-003]sartain."

[023-004]"And I'm a poor man,--the poorest in all Hogglestock; and, therefore,

[023-005]of course, it is stealing. Of course I am a thief. Yes; of course I

[023-006]am a thief. When did not the world believe the worst of the poor?"

[023-007]Having so spoken, Mr. Crawley rose from his chair and hurried out of

[023-008]the cottage, waiting no further reply from Dan Morris or his wife.

[023-009]And as he made his way slowly home, not going there by the direct

[023-010]road, but by a long circuit, he told himself that there could be no

[023-011]sympathy for him anywhere. Even Dan Morris, the brickmaker, thought

[023-012]that he was a thief.

[023-013]"And am I a thief?" he said to himself, standing in the middle of the

[023-014]road, with his hands up to his forehead.

[023-015]CHAPTER XIII.

[023-016]THE BISHOP'S ANGEL.

[023-017]It was nearly nine before Mr. Crawley got back to his house, and

[023-018]found his wife and daughter waiting breakfast for him. "I should not

[023-019]wonder if Grace were over here to-day," said Mrs. Crawley. "She'd

[023-020]better remain where she is," said he. After this the meal passed

[023-021]almost without a word. When it was over, Jane, at a sign from her

[023-022]mother, went up to her father and asked him whether she should read

[023-023]with him. "Not now," he said, "not just now. I must rest my brain

[023-024]before it will be fit for any work." Then he got into the chair over

[023-025]the fire, and his wife began to fear that he would remain there all

[023-026]the day.

[023-027]But the morning was not far advanced, when there came a visitor who

[023-028]disturbed him, and by disturbing him did him real service. Just at

[023-029]ten there arrived at the little gate before the house a man on a

[023-030]pony, whom Jane espied, standing there by the pony's head and looking

[023-031]about for some one to relieve him from the charge of his steed.

[023-032]This was Mr. Thumble, who had ridden over to Hogglestock on a poor

[023-033]spavined brute belonging to the bishop's stable, and which had once

[023-034]been the bishop's cob. Now it was the vehicle by which Mrs. Proudie's

[023-035]episcopal messages were sent backwards and forwards through a

[023-036]twelve-miles ride round Barchester; and so many were the lady's

[023-037]requirements, that the poor animal by no means eat the hay of

[023-038]idleness. Mr. Thumble had suggested to Mrs. Proudie, after their

[023-039]interview with the bishop and the giving up of the letter to the

[023-040]clerical messenger's charge, that before hiring a gig from the

[023-041]"Dragon of Wantley," he should be glad to know,--looking as he always

[023-042]did to "Mary Anne and the children,"--whence the price of the gig was

[023-043]to be returned to him. Mrs. Proudie had frowned at him,--not with all

[023-044]the austerity of frowning which she could use when really angered,

[023-045]but simply with a frown which gave her some little time for thought,

[023-046]and would enable her to continue the rebuke if, after thinking,

[023-047]she should find that rebuke was needed. But mature consideration

[023-048]showed her that Mr. Thumble's caution was not without reason. Were

[023-049]the bishop energetic,--or even the bishop's managing chaplain as

[023-050]energetic as he should be, Mr. Crawley might, as Mrs. Proudie felt

[023-051]assured, be made in some way to pay for a conveyance for Mr. Thumble.

[023-052]But the energy was lacking, and the price of the gig, if the gig were

[023-053]ordered, would certainly fall ultimately upon the bishop's shoulders.

[023-054]This was very sad. Mrs. Proudie had often grieved over the necessary

[023-055]expenditure of episcopal surveillance, and had been heard to declare

[023-056]her opinion that a liberal allowance for secret service should

[023-057]be made in every diocese. What better could the Ecclesiastical

[023-058]Commissioners do with all those rich revenues which they had stolen

[023-059]from the bishops? But there was no such liberal allowance at present,

[023-060]and, therefore, Mrs. Proudie, after having frowned at Mr. Thumble for

[023-061]some seconds, desired him to take the grey cob. Now, Mr. Thumble had

[023-062]ridden the grey cob before, and would much have preferred a gig. But

[023-063]even the grey cob was better than a gig at his own cost.

[023-064]"Mamma, there's a man at the gate wanting to come in," said Jane. "I

[023-065]think he's a clergyman."

[023-066]Mr. Crawley immediately raised his head, though he did not at once

[023-067]leave his chair. Mrs. Crawley went to the window, and recognized the

[023-068]reverend visitor. "My dear, it is that Mr. Thumble, who is so much

[023-069]with the bishop."

[023-070]"What does Mr. Thumble want with me?"

[023-071]"Nay, my dear; he will tell you that himself." But Mrs. Crawley,

[023-072]though she answered him with a voice intended to be cheerful, greatly

[023-073]feared the coming of this messenger from the palace. She perceived

[023-074]at once that the bishop was about to interfere with her husband in

[023-075]consequence of that which the magistrates had done yesterday.

[023-076]"Mamma, he doesn't know what to do with his pony," said Jane.

[023-077]"Tell him to tie it to the rail," said Mr. Crawley. "If he has

[023-078]expected to find menials here, as he has them at the palace, he will

[023-079]be wrong. If he wants to come in here, let him tie the beast to the

[023-080]rail." So Jane went out and sent a message to Mr. Thumble by the

[023-081]girl, and Mr. Thumble did tie the pony to the rail, and followed the

[023-082]girl into the house. Jane in the meantime had retired out by the back

[023-083]door to the school, but Mrs. Crawley kept her ground. She kept her

[023-084]ground although she almost believed that her husband would prefer to

[023-085]have the field to himself. As Mr. Thumble did not at once enter the

[023-086]room, Mr. Crawley stalked to the door, and stood with it open in his

[023-087]hand. Though he knew Mr. Thumble's person, he was not acquainted

[023-088]with him, and therefore he simply bowed to the visitor, bowing

[023-089]more than once or twice with a cold courtesy, which did not put Mr.

[023-090]Thumble altogether at his ease. "My name is Mr. Thumble," said the

[023-091]visitor,--"The Reverend Caleb Thumble," and he held the bishop's

[023-092]letter in his hand. Mr. Crawley seemed to take no notice of the

[023-093]letter, but motioned Mr. Thumble with his hand into the room.

[023-094]"I suppose you have come over from Barchester this morning?" said

[023-095]Mrs. Crawley.

[023-096]"Yes, madam,--from the palace." Mr. Thumble, though a humble man in

[023-097]positions in which he felt that humility would become him,--a humble

[023-098]man to his betters, as he himself would have expressed it,--had still

[023-099]about him something of that pride which naturally belonged to those

[023-100]clergymen who were closely attached to the palace at Barchester. Had

[023-101]he been sent on a message to Plumstead,--could any such message from

[023-102]Barchester palace have been possible, he would have been properly

[023-103]humble in his demeanour to the archdeacon, or to Mrs. Grantly had he

[023-104]been admitted to the august presence of that lady; but he was aware

[023-105]that humility would not become him on his present mission; he had

[023-106]been expressly ordered to be firm by Mrs. Proudie, and firm he meant

[023-107]to be; and therefore, in communicating to Mrs. Crawley the fact that

[023-108]he had come from the palace, he did load the tone of his voice with

[023-109]something of dignity which Mr. Crawley might perhaps be excused for

[023-110]regarding as arrogance.

[023-111]"And what does the 'palace' want with me?" said Mr. Crawley. Mrs.

[023-112]Crawley knew at once that there was to be a battle. Nay, the battle

[023-113]had begun. Nor was she altogether sorry; for though she could not

[023-114]trust her husband to sit alone all day in his arm-chair over the

[023-115]fire, she could trust him to carry on a disputation with any other

[023-116]clergyman on any subject whatever. "What does the palace want with

[023-117]me?" And as Mr. Crawley asked the question he stood erect, and looked

[023-118]Mr. Thumble full in the face. Mr. Thumble called to mind the fact,

[023-119]that Mr. Crawley was a very poor man indeed,--so poor that he owed

[023-120]money all round the country to butchers and bakers, and the other

[023-121]fact, that he, Mr. Thumble himself, did not owe any money to any one,

[023-122]his wife luckily having a little income of her own; and, strengthened

[023-123]by these remembrances, he endeavoured to bear Mr. Crawley's attack

[023-124]with gallantry.

[023-125]"Of course, Mr. Crawley, you are aware that this unfortunate affair

[023-126]at Silverbridge--"

[023-127]"I am not prepared, sir, to discuss the unfortunate affair at

[023-128]Silverbridge with a stranger. If you are the bearer of any message to

[023-129]me from the Bishop of Barchester, perhaps you will deliver it."

[023-130]"I have brought a letter," said Mr. Thumble. Then Mr. Crawley

[023-131]stretched out his hand without a word, and taking the letter with him

[023-132]to the window, read it very slowly. When he had made himself master

[023-133]of its contents, he refolded the letter, placed it again in the

[023-134]envelope, and returned to the spot where Mr. Thumble was standing.

[023-135]"I will answer the bishop's letter," he said; "I will answer it of

[023-136]course, as it is fitting that I should do. Shall I ask you to wait

[023-137]for my reply, or shall I send it by course of post?"

[023-138]"I think, Mr. Crawley, as the bishop wishes me to undertake the

[023-139]duty--"

[023-140]"You will not undertake the duty, Mr. Thumble. You need not trouble

[023-141]yourself, for I shall not surrender my pulpit to you."

[023-142]"But the bishop--"

[023-143]"I care nothing for the bishop in this matter." So much he spoke in

[023-144]anger, and then he corrected himself. "I crave the bishop's pardon,

[023-145]and yours as his messenger, if in the heat occasioned by my strong

[023-146]feelings I have said aught which may savour of irreverence towards

[023-147]his lordship's office. I respect his lordship's high position as

[023-148]bishop of this diocese, and I bow to his commands in all things

[023-149]lawful. But I must not bow to him in things unlawful, nor must I

[023-150]abandon my duty before God at his bidding, unless his bidding be

[023-151]given in accordance with the canons of the Church and the laws of the

[023-152]land. It will be my duty, on the coming Sunday, to lead the prayers

[023-153]of my people in the church of my parish, and to preach to them from

[023-154]my pulpit; and that duty, with God's assistance, I will perform. Nor

[023-155]will I allow any clergyman to interfere with me in the performance

[023-156]of those sacred offices,--no, not though the bishop himself should

[023-157]be present with the object of enforcing his illegal command." Mr.

[023-158]Crawley spoke these words without hesitation, even with eloquence,

[023-159]standing upright, and with something of a noble anger gleaming over

[023-160]his poor wan face; and, I think, that while speaking them, he was

[023-161]happier than he had been for many a long day.

[023-162]Mr. Thumble listened to him patiently, standing with one foot a

[023-163]little in advance of the other, with one hand folded over the other,

[023-164]with his head rather on one side, and with his eyes fixed on the

[023-165]corner where the wall and ceiling joined each other. He had been

[023-166]told to be firm, and he was considering how he might best display

[023-167]firmness. He thought that he remembered some story of two parsons

[023-168]fighting for one pulpit, and he thought also that he should not

[023-169]himself like to incur the scandal of such a proceeding in the

[023-170]diocese. As to the law in the matter he knew nothing himself; but

[023-171]he presumed that a bishop would probably know the law better than a

[023-172]perpetual curate. That Mrs. Proudie was intemperate and imperious, he

[023-173]was aware. Had the message come from her alone, he might have felt

[023-174]that even for her sake he had better give way. But as the despotic

[023-175]arrogance of the lady had been in this case backed by the timid

[023-176]presence and hesitating words of her lord, Mr. Thumble thought

[023-177]that he must have the law on his side. "I think you will find, Mr.

[023-178]Crawley," said he, "that the bishop's inhibition is strictly legal."

[023-179]He had picked up the powerful word from Mrs. Proudie and flattered

[023-180]himself that it might be of use to him in carrying his purpose.
