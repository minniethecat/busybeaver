[054-001]Grace hardly knew what would be best. She only knew that Major

[054-002]Grantly was at Allington, and that he had come thither to see her.

[054-003]The idea of hurrying back after him was unpleasant to her, and yet

[054-004]she was so flurried that she felt thankful to Lily for taking her

[054-005]away from the cottage. The matter was compromised at last. They

[054-006]remained for half an hour, and ate some biscuits and pretended to

[054-007]drink a glass of wine, and then they started. John Eames, who in

[054-008]truth believed that Lily Dale was running away from him, was by

[054-009]no means well pleased, and when the girls were gone, did not make

[054-010]himself so agreeable to his old friend as he should have done. "What

[054-011]a fool I am to come here at all," he said, throwing himself into an

[054-012]arm-chair as soon as the front door was closed.

[054-013]"That's very civil to me, John!"

[054-014]"You know what I mean, Lady Julia. I am a fool to come near her,

[054-015]until I can do so without thinking more of her than I do of any other

[054-016]girl in the county."

[054-017]"I don't think you have anything to complain of as yet," said Lady

[054-018]Julia, who had in some sort perceived that Lily's retreat had been on

[054-019]Grace's account, and not on her own. "It seems to me that Lily was

[054-020]very glad to see you, and when I told her that you were coming to

[054-021]stay here, and would be near them for some days, she seemed to be

[054-022]quite pleased;--she did indeed."

[054-023]"Then why did she run away the moment I came in?" said Johnny.

[054-024]"I think it was something you said about that man who has gone to

[054-025]Allington."

[054-026]"What difference can the man make to her? The truth is, I despise

[054-027]myself;--I do indeed, Lady Julia. Only think of my meeting Crosbie at

[054-028]dinner the other day, and his having the impertinence to come up and

[054-029]shake hands with me."

[054-030]"I suppose he didn't say anything about what happened at the

[054-031]Paddington Station?"

[054-032]"No; he didn't speak about that. I wish I knew whether she cares for

[054-033]him still. If I thought she did, I would never speak another word to

[054-034]her,--I mean about myself. Of course I am not going to quarrel with

[054-035]them. I am not such a fool as that." Then Lady Julia tried to comfort

[054-036]him, and succeeded so far that he was induced to eat the mince veal

[054-037]that had been intended for the comfort and support of the two young

[054-038]ladies who had run away.

[054-039]"Do you think it is he?" were the first words which Grace said when

[054-040]they were fairly on their way back together.

[054-041]"I should think it must be. What other man can there be, of that

[054-042]sort, who would be likely to come to Allington to see you?"

[054-043]"His coming is not likely. I cannot understand that he should come.

[054-044]He let me leave Silverbridge without seeing me,--and I thought that

[054-045]he was quite right."

[054-046]"And I think he is quite right to come here. I am very glad he has

[054-047]come. It shows that he has really something like a heart inside him.

[054-048]Had he not come, or sent, or written, or taken some step before the

[054-049]trial comes on, to make you know that he was thinking of you, I

[054-050]should have said that he was as hard,--as hard as any other man that

[054-051]I ever heard of. Men are so hard! But I don't think he is, now. I

[054-052]am beginning to regard him as the one chevalier sans peur et sans

[054-053]reproche, and to fancy that you ought to go down on your knees before

[054-054]him, and kiss his highness's shoebuckle. In judging of men one's mind

[054-055]vacillates so quickly between the scorn which is due to a false man

[054-056]and the worship which is due to a true man." Then she was silent for

[054-057]a moment, but Grace said nothing, and Lily continued, "I tell you

[054-058]fairly, Grace, that I shall expect very much from you now."

[054-059]"Much in what way, Lily?"

[054-060]"In the way of worship. I shall not be content that you should merely

[054-061]love him. If he has come here, as he must have done, to say that the

[054-062]moment of the world's reproach is the moment he has chosen to ask you

[054-063]to be his wife, I think that you will owe him more than love."

[054-064]"I shall owe him more than love, and I will pay him more than love,"

[054-065]said Grace. There was something in the tone of her voice as she spoke

[054-066]which made Lily stop her and look up into her face. There was a smile

[054-067]there which Lily had never seen before, and which gave a beauty to

[054-068]her which was wonderful to Lily's eyes. Surely this lover of Grace's

[054-069]must have seen her smile like that, and therefore had loved her and

[054-070]was giving such wonderful proof of his love. "Yes," continued Grace,

[054-071]standing and looking at her friend, "you may stare at me, Lily, but

[054-072]you may be sure that I will do for Major Grantly all the good that I

[054-073]can do for him."

[054-074]"What do you mean, Grace?"

[054-075]"Never mind what I mean. You are very imperious in managing your own

[054-076]affairs, and you must let me be so equally in mine."

[054-077]"But I tell you everything."

[054-078]"Do you suppose that if--if--if in real truth it can possibly be the

[054-079]case that Major Grantly shall have come here to offer me his hand

[054-080]when we are all ground down into the dust, as we are, do you think

[054-081]that I will let him sacrifice himself? Would you?"

[054-082]"Certainly. Why not? There will be no sacrifice. He will be asking

[054-083]for that which he wishes to get; and you will be bound to give it to

[054-084]him."

[054-085]"If he wants it, where is his nobility? If it be as you say, he will

[054-086]have shown himself noble, and his nobility will have consisted in

[054-087]this, that he has been willing to take that which he does not want,

[054-088]in order that he may succour one whom he loves. I also will succour

[054-089]one whom I love, as best I know how." Then she walked on quickly

[054-090]before her friend, and Lily stood for a moment thinking before she

[054-091]followed her. They were now on a field-path, by which they were

[054-092]enabled to escape the road back to Allington for the greater part of

[054-093]the distance, and Grace had reached a stile, and had clambered over

[054-094]it before Lily had caught her.

[054-095]"You must not go away by yourself," said Lily.

[054-096]"I don't wish to go away by myself."

[054-097]"I want you to stop a moment and listen to me. I am sure you are

[054-098]wrong in this,--wrong for both your sakes. You believe that he loves

[054-099]you?"

[054-100]"I thought he did once; and if he has come here to see me, I suppose

[054-101]he does still."

[054-102]"If that be the case, and if you also love him--"

[054-103]"I do. I make no mystery about that to you. I do love him with all my

[054-104]heart. I love him to-day, now that I believe him to be here, and that

[054-105]I suppose I shall see him, perhaps this very afternoon. And I loved

[054-106]him yesterday, when I thought that I should never see him again. I

[054-107]do love him. I do. I love him so well that I will never do him an

[054-108]injury."

[054-109]"That being so, if he makes you an offer you are bound to accept it.

[054-110]I do not think that you have an alternative."

[054-111]"I have an alternative, and I shall use it. Why don't you take my

[054-112]cousin John?"

[054-113]"Because I like somebody else better. If you have got as good a

[054-114]reason I won't say another word to you."

[054-115]"And why don't you take that other person?"

[054-116]"Because I cannot trust his love; that is why. It is not very kind of

[054-117]you, opening my sores afresh, when I am trying to heal yours."

[054-118]"Oh, Lily, am I unkind,--unkind to you, who have been so generous to

[054-119]me?"

[054-120]"I'll forgive you all that and a deal more if you will only listen

[054-121]to me and try to take my advice. Because this major of yours does a

[054-122]generous thing, which is for the good of you both,--the infinite good

[054-123]of both of you,--you are to emulate his generosity by doing a thing

[054-124]which will be for the good of neither of you. That is about it. Yes,

[054-125]it is, Grace. You cannot doubt that he has been meaning this for some

[054-126]time past; and of course, if he looks upon you as his own,--and I

[054-127]daresay, if the whole truth is to be told, he does--"

[054-128]"But I am not his own."

[054-129]"Yes, you are, in one sense; you have just said so with a great deal

[054-130]of energy. And if it is so,--let me see, where was I?"

[054-131]"Oh, Lily, you need not mind where you were."

[054-132]"But I do mind, and I hate to be interrupted in my arguments. Yes,

[054-133]just that. If he saw his cow sick, he'd try to doctor the cow in her

[054-134]sickness. He sees that you are sick, and of course he comes to your

[054-135]relief."

[054-136]"I am not Major Grantly's cow."

[054-137]"Yes, you are."

[054-138]"Nor his dog, nor his ox, nor his ass, nor anything that is his,

[054-139]except--except, Lily, the dearest friend that he has on the face of

[054-140]the earth. He cannot have a friend that will go further for him than

[054-141]I will. He will never know how far I will go to serve him. You don't

[054-142]know his people. Nor do I know them. But I know what they are. His

[054-143]sister is married to a marquis."

[054-144]"What has that to do with it?" said Lily, sharply. "If she were

[054-145]married to an archduke, what difference would that make?"

[054-146]"And they are proud people--all of them--and rich; and they live with

[054-147]high persons in the world."

[054-148]"I didn't care though they lived with the royal family, and had the

[054-149]Prince of Wales for their bosom friend. It only shows how much better

[054-150]he is than they are."

[054-151]"But think what my family is,--how we are situated. When my father

[054-152]was simply poor I did not care about it, because he has been born and

[054-153]bred a gentleman. But now he is disgraced. Yes, Lily, he is. I am

[054-154]bound to say so, at any rate to myself, when I am thinking of Major

[054-155]Grantly; and I will not carry that disgrace into a family which would

[054-156]feel it so keenly as they would do." Lily, however, went on with her

[054-157]arguments, and was still arguing when they turned the corner of the

[054-158]lane, and came upon Lily's uncle and the major himself.

[054-159]CHAPTER XXX.

[054-160]SHOWING WHAT MAJOR GRANTLY DID AFTER HIS WALK.

[054-161]In going down from the church to the Small House Lily Dale had all

[054-162]the conversation to herself. During some portion of the way the path

[054-163]was only broad enough for two persons, and here Major Grantly walked

[054-164]by Lily's side, while Grace followed them. Then they found their way

[054-165]into the house, and Lily made her little speech to her mother about

[054-166]catching the major. "Yes, my dear, I have seen Major Grantly before,"

[054-167]said Mrs. Dale. "I suppose he has met you on the road. But I did not

[054-168]expect that any of you would have returned so soon." Some little

[054-169]explanation followed as to the squire, and as to Major Grantly's

[054-170]walk, and after that the great thing was to leave the two lovers

[054-171]alone. "You will dine here, of course, Major Grantly," Mrs. Dale

[054-172]said. But this he declined. He had learned, he said, that there was a

[054-173]night-train up to London, and he thought that he would return to town

[054-174]by that. He had intended, when he left London, to get back as soon as

[054-175]possible. Then Mrs. Dale, having hesitated for two or three seconds,

[054-176]got up and left the room, and Lily followed. "It seems very odd and

[054-177]abrupt," said Mrs. Dale to her daughter, "but I suppose it is best."

[054-178]"Of course it is best, mamma. Do as one would be done by,--that's the

[054-179]only rule. It will be much better for her that she should have it

[054-180]over."
