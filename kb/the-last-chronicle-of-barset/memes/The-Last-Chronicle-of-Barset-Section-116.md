[116-001]"Then why did he threaten me?"

[116-002]"Henry, you are obstinate."

[116-003]"I am not obstinate, mother."

[116-004]"Yes, you are. You remember nothing, and you forget nothing. You

[116-005]expect everything to be made smooth for you, and will do nothing

[116-006]towards making things smooth for anybody else. You ought to promise

[116-007]to give up the sale. If the worst came to the worst, your father

[116-008]would not let you suffer in pocket for yielding to him in so much."

[116-009]"If the worst comes to the worst, I wish to take nothing from my

[116-010]father."

[116-011]"You won't put off the sale, then?"

[116-012]The son paused a moment before he answered his mother, thinking over

[116-013]all the circumstances of his position. "I cannot do so as long as I

[116-014]am subject to my father's threat," he said at last. "What took place

[116-015]between my father and Miss Crawley can go for nothing with me. He has

[116-016]told me that his allowance to me is to be withdrawn. Let him tell me

[116-017]that he has reconsidered the matter."

[116-018]"But he has not withdrawn it. The last quarter was paid to your

[116-019]account only the other day. He does not mean to withdraw it."

[116-020]"Let him tell me so; let him tell me that my power of living at

[116-021]Cosby Lodge does not depend on my marriage,--that my income will be

[116-022]continued to me whether I marry or no, and I'll arrange matters with

[116-023]the auctioneer to-morrow. You can't suppose that I should prefer to

[116-024]live in France."

[116-025]"Henry, you are too hard on your father."

[116-026]"I think, mother, he has been too hard upon me."

[116-027]"It is you that are to blame now. I tell you plainly that that is my

[116-028]opinion. If evil comes of it, it will be your own fault."

[116-029]"If evil come of it I must bear it."

[116-030]"A son ought to give up something to his father;--especially to a

[116-031]father so indulgent as yours."

[116-032]But it was of no use. And Mrs. Grantly when she went to her bed could

[116-033]only lament in her own mind over what, in discussing the matter

[116-034]afterwards with her sister, she called the cross-grainedness of men.

[116-035]"They are as like each other as two peas," she said, "and though each

[116-036]of them wished to be generous, neither of them would condescend to be

[116-037]just." Early on the following morning there was, no doubt, much said

[116-038]on the subject between the archdeacon and his wife before they met

[116-039]their son at breakfast; but neither at breakfast nor afterwards was

[116-040]there a word said between the father and son that had the slightest

[116-041]reference to the subject in dispute between them. The archdeacon made

[116-042]no more speeches in favour of land, nor did he revert to the foxes.

[116-043]He was very civil to his son;--too civil by half, as Mrs. Grantly

[116-044]continued to say to herself. And then the major drove himself away

[116-045]in his cart, going through Barchester, so that he might see his

[116-046]grandfather. When he wished his father good-by, the archdeacon shook

[116-047]hands with him, and said something about the chance of rain. Had he

[116-048]not better take the big umbrella? The major thanked him courteously,

[116-049]and said that he did not think it would rain. Then he was gone. "Upon

[116-050]his own head be it," said the archdeacon when his son's step was

[116-051]heard in the passage leading to the back-yard. Then Mrs. Grantly got

[116-052]up quietly and followed her son. She found him settling himself in

[116-053]his dog-cart, while the servant who was to accompany him was still

[116-054]at the horse's head. She went up close to him, and, standing by the

[116-055]wheel of the gig, whispered a word or two into his ear. "If you love

[116-056]me, Henry, you will postpone the sale. Do it for my sake." There came

[116-057]across his face a look of great pain, but he answered her not a word.

[116-058]The archdeacon was walking about the room striking one hand open

[116-059]with the other closed, clearly in a tumult of anger, when his wife

[116-060]returned to him. "I have done all that I can," he said,--"all that I

[116-061]can; more, indeed, than was becoming for me. Upon his own head be it.

[116-062]Upon his own head be it!"

[116-063]"What is it that you fear?" she asked.

[116-064]"I fear nothing. But if he chooses to sell his things at Cosby Lodge

[116-065]he must abide the consequences. They shall not be replaced with my

[116-066]money."

[116-067]"What will it matter if he does sell them?"

[116-068]"Matter! Do you think there is a single person in the county who will

[116-069]not know that his doing so is a sign that he has quarrelled with me?"

[116-070]"But he has not quarrelled with you."

[116-071]"I can tell you then, that in that case I shall have quarrelled with

[116-072]him! I have not been a hard father, but there are some things which a

[116-073]man cannot bear. Of course you will take his part."

[116-074]"I am taking no part. I only want to see peace between you."

[116-075]"Peace!--yes; peace indeed. I am to yield in everything. I am to be

[116-076]nobody. Look here;--as sure as ever an auctioneer's hammer is raised

[116-077]at Cosby Lodge, I will alter the settlement of the property. Every

[116-078]acre shall belong to Charles. There is my word for it." The poor

[116-079]woman had nothing more to say;--nothing more to say at that moment.

[116-080]She thought that at the present conjuncture her husband was less in

[116-081]the wrong than her son, but she could not tell him so lest she should

[116-082]strengthen him in his wrath.

[116-083]Henry Grantly found his grandfather in bed, with Posy seated on the

[116-084]bed beside him. "My father told me that you were not quite well, and

[116-085]I thought that I would look in," said the major.

[116-086]"Thank you, my dear;--it is very good of you. There is not much the

[116-087]matter with me, but I am not quite so strong as I was once." And the

[116-088]old man smiled as he held his grandson's hand.

[116-089]"And how is cousin Posy?" said the major.

[116-090]"Posy is quite well;--isn't she, my darling?" said the old man.

[116-091]"Grandpa doesn't go to the cathedral now," said Posy; "so I come in

[116-092]to talk to him. Don't I, grandpa?"

[116-093]"And to play cat's-cradle;--only we have not had any cat's-cradle

[116-094]this morning,--have we, Posy?"

[116-095]"Mrs. Baxter told me not to play this morning, because it's cold for

[116-096]grandpa to sit up in bed," said Posy.

[116-097]When the major had been there about twenty minutes he was preparing

[116-098]to take his leave,--but Mr. Harding, bidding Posy to go out of the

[116-099]room, told his grandson that he had a word to say to him. "I don't

[116-100]like to interfere, Henry," he said, "but I am afraid that things are

[116-101]not quite smooth at Plumstead."

[116-102]"There is nothing wrong between me and my mother," said the major.

[116-103]"God forbid that there should be; but, my dear boy, don't let there

[116-104]be anything wrong between you and your father. He is a good man, and

[116-105]the time will come when you will be proud of his memory."

[116-106]"I am proud of him now."

[116-107]"Then be gentle with him,--and submit yourself. I am an old man

[116-108]now,--very fast going away from all those I love here. But I am happy

[116-109]in leaving my children because they have ever been gentle to me and

[116-110]kind. If I am permitted to remember them whither I am going, my

[116-111]thoughts of them will all be pleasant. Should it not be much to them

[116-112]that they have made my death-bed happy?"

[116-113]The major could not but tell himself that Mr. Harding had been a man

[116-114]easy to please, easy to satisfy, and, in that respect, very different

[116-115]from his father. But of course he said nothing of this. "I will do my

[116-116]best," he replied.

[116-117]"Do, my boy. Honour thy father,--that thy days may be long in the

[116-118]land."

[Illustration: "Honour thy Father,--that thy days may be long in the

[116-119]Land."]

[116-120]It seemed to the major as he drove away from Barchester that

[116-121]everybody was against him; and yet he was sure that he himself was

[116-122]right. He could not give up Grace Crawley; and unless he were to do

[116-123]so he could not live at Cosby Lodge.

[116-124]CHAPTER LIX.

[116-125]A LADY PRESENTS HER COMPLIMENTS TO MISS L. D.

[116-126]One morning, while Lily Dale was staying with Mrs. Thorne in London,

[116-127]there was brought up to her room, as she was dressing for dinner,

[116-128]a letter which the postman had just left for her. The address was

[116-129]written with a feminine hand, and Lily was at once aware that she did

[116-130]not know the writing. The angles were very acute, and the lines were

[116-131]very straight, and the vowels looked to be cruel and false, with

[116-132]their sharp points and their open eyes. Lily at once knew that it was

[116-133]the performance of a woman who had been taught to write at school,

[116-134]and not at home, and she became prejudiced against the writer before

[116-135]she opened the letter. When she had opened the letter and read it,

[116-136]her feelings towards the writer were not of a kindly nature. It was

[116-137]as follows:--

[116-138]"A lady presents her compliments to Miss L. D., and earnestly

[116-139]implores Miss L. D. to give her an answer to the following question.

[116-140]Is Miss L. D. engaged to marry Mr. J. E.? The lady in question

[116-141]pledges herself not to interfere with Miss L. D. in any way, should

[116-142]the answer be in the affirmative. The lady earnestly requests that a

[116-143]reply to this question may be sent to M. D., Post-office, 455 Edgware

[116-144]Road. In order that L. D. may not doubt that M. D. has an interest in

[116-145]J. E., M. D. encloses the last note she received from him before he

[116-146]started for the Continent." Then there was a scrap, which Lily well

[116-147]knew to be in the handwriting of John Eames, and the scrap was as

[116-148]follows:--"Dearest M.--Punctually at 8.30. Ever and always your

[116-149]unalterable J. E." Lily, as she read this, did not comprehend that

[116-150]John's note to M. D. had been in itself a joke.

[116-151]Lily Dale had heard of anonymous letters before, but had never

[116-152]received one, or even seen one. Now that she had one in her hand, it

[116-153]seemed to her that there could be nothing more abominable than the

[116-154]writing of such a letter. She let it drop from her, as though the

[116-155]receiving, and opening, and reading it had been a stain to her. As it

[116-156]lay on the ground at her feet, she trod upon it. Of what sort could a

[116-157]woman be who would write such a letter as that? Answer it! Of course

[116-158]she would not answer it. It never occurred to her for a moment that

[116-159]it could become her to answer it. Had she been at home or with her

[116-160]mother, she would have called her mother to her, and Mrs. Dale would

[116-161]have taken it from the ground, and have read it, and then destroyed

[116-162]it. As it was, she must pick it up herself. She did so, and declared

[116-163]to herself that there should be an end to it. It might be right that

[116-164]somebody should see it, and therefore she would show it to Emily

[116-165]Dunstable. After that it should be destroyed.

[116-166]Of course the letter could have no effect upon her. So she told

[116-167]herself. But it did have a very strong effect, and probably the exact

[116-168]effect which the writer had intended that it should have. J. E. was,

[116-169]of course, John Eames. There was no doubt about that. What a fool

[116-170]the writer must have been to talk of L. D. in the letter, when the

[116-171]outside cover was plainly addressed to Miss Lilian Dale! But there

[116-172]are some people for whom the pretended mystery of initial letters

[116-173]has a charm, and who love the darkness of anonymous letters. As Lily

[116-174]thought of this, she stamped on the letter again. Who was the M. D.

[116-175]to whom she was required to send an answer--with whom John Eames

[116-176]corresponded in the most affectionate terms? She had resolved that

[116-177]she would not even ask herself a question about M. D., and yet she

[116-178]could not divert her mind from the inquiry. It was, at any rate, a

[116-179]fact that there must be some woman designated by the letters,--some

[116-180]woman who had, at any rate, chosen to call herself M. D. And John

[116-181]Eames had called her M. There must, at any rate, be such a woman.

[116-182]This female, be she who she might, had thought it worth her while

[116-183]to make this inquiry about John Eames, and had manifestly learned

[116-184]something of Lily's own history. And the woman had pledged herself

[116-185]not to interfere with John Eames, if L. D. would only condescend to

[116-186]say that she was engaged to him! As Lily thought of the proposition,

[116-187]she trod upon the letter for the third time. Then she picked it up,

[116-188]and having no place of custody under lock and key ready to her hand,

[116-189]she put it in her pocket.
