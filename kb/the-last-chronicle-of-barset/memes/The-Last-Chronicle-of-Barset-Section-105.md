[105-001]He paused a moment before he replied, weighing, like a prudent man,

[105-002]the force of the words he was about to utter. "It is much better as

[105-003]it is," he said. "It is much better that you should be as strangers

[105-004]for the future."

[105-005]"I do not see that at all," said Crosbie. They were both leaning on

[105-006]the rails, and so they remained for the next twenty minutes. "I do

[105-007]not see that at all."

[105-008]"I feel sure of it. What could come of any renewed intercourse,--even

[105-009]if she would allow it?"

[105-010]"I might make her my wife."

[105-011]"And do you think that you would be happy with her, or she with you,

[105-012]after what has passed?"

[105-013]"I do think so."

[105-014]"I do not. It might be possible that she should bring herself

[105-015]to marry you. Women delight to forgive injuries. They like the

[105-016]excitement of generosity. But she could never forget that you had had

[105-017]a former wife, or the circumstances under which you were married. And

[105-018]as for yourself, you would regret it after the first month. How could

[105-019]you ever speak to her of your love without speaking also of your

[105-020]shame? If a man does marry he should at least be able to hold up his

[105-021]head before his wife."

[105-022]This was very severe, but Crosbie showed no anger. "I think I should

[105-023]do so," he said,--"after a while."

[105-024]"And then, about money? Of course you would have to tell her

[105-025]everything."

[105-026]"Everything--of course."

[105-027]"It is like enough that she might not regard that,--except that she

[105-028]would feel that if you could not afford to marry her when you were

[105-029]unembarrassed, you can hardly afford to do so when you are over head

[105-030]and ears in debt."

[105-031]"She has money now."

[105-032]"After all that has come and gone you would hardly seek Lily Dale

[105-033]because you want to marry a fortune."

[105-034]"You are too hard on me, Pratt. You know that my only reason for

[105-035]seeking her is that I love her."

[105-036]"I do not mean to be hard. But I have a very strong opinion that the

[105-037]quarrels of lovers, when they are of so very serious a nature, are

[105-038]a bad basis for the renewal of love. Come, let us go and dress for

[105-039]dinner. I am going to dine with Mrs. Thorne, the millionaire, who

[105-040]married a country doctor, and who used to be called Miss Dunstable."

[105-041]"I never dine out anywhere now," said Crosbie. And then they walked

[105-042]out of the Park together. Neither of them, of course, knew that Lily

[105-043]Dale was staying at the house at which Fowler Pratt was going to

[105-044]dine.

[105-045]Lily, as she rode home, did not speak a word. She would have given

[105-046]worlds to be able to talk, but she could not even make a beginning.

[105-047]She heard Bernard and Siph Dunn chatting behind her, and hoped that

[105-048]they would continue to do so till she was safe within the house. They

[105-049]all used her well, for no one tried to draw her into conversation.

[105-050]Once Emily said to her, "Shall we trot a little, Lily?" And then they

[105-051]had moved on quickly, and the misery was soon over. As soon as she

[105-052]was upstairs in the house, she got Emily by herself, and explained

[105-053]all the mystery in a word or two. "I fear I have made a fool of

[105-054]myself. That was the man to whom I was once engaged." "What, Mr.

[105-055]Crosbie?" said Emily, who had heard the whole story from Bernard.

[105-056]"Yes, Mr. Crosbie; pray, do not say a word of it to anybody,--not

[105-057]even to your aunt. I am better now, but I was such a fool. No, dear;

[105-058]I won't go into the drawing-room. I'll go upstairs, and come down

[105-059]ready for dinner."

[105-060]When she was alone she sat down in her habit, and declared to herself

[105-061]that she certainly would never become the wife of Mr. Crosbie. I do

[105-062]not know why she should make such a declaration. She had promised

[105-063]her mother and John Eames that she would not do so, and that promise

[105-064]would certainly have bound her without any further resolutions on

[105-065]her own part. But, to tell the truth, the vision of the man had

[105-066]disenchanted her. When last she had seen him he had been as it were a

[105-067]god to her; and though, since that day, his conduct to her had been

[105-068]as ungodlike as it well might be, still the memory of the outward

[105-069]signs of his divinity had remained with her. It is difficult to

[105-070]explain how it had come to pass that the glimpse which she had had

[105-071]of him should have altered so much within her mind;--why she should

[105-072]so suddenly have come to regard him in an altered light. It was not

[105-073]simply that he looked to be older, and because his face was careworn.

[105-074]It was not only that he had lost that look of an Apollo which Lily

[105-075]had once in her mirth attributed to him. I think it was chiefly that

[105-076]she herself was older, and could no longer see a god in such a man.

[105-077]She had never regarded John Eames as being gifted with divinity, and

[105-078]had therefore always been making comparisons to his discredit. Any

[105-079]such comparison now would tend quite the other way. Nevertheless

[105-080]she would adhere to the two letters in her book. Since she had seen

[105-081]Mr. Crosbie she was altogether out of love with the prospect of

[105-082]matrimony.

[105-083]She was in the room when Mr. Pratt was announced, and she at once

[105-084]recognized him as the man who had been with Crosbie. And when, some

[105-085]minutes afterwards, Siph Dunn came into the room, she could see that

[105-086]in their greeting allusion was made to the scene in the Park. But

[105-087]still it was probable that this man would not recognize her, and, if

[105-088]he did so, what would it matter? There were twenty people to sit down

[105-089]to dinner, and the chances were that she would not be called upon to

[105-090]exchange a word with Mr. Pratt. She had now recovered herself, and

[105-091]could speak freely to her friend Siph, and when Siph came and stood

[105-092]near her she thanked him graciously for his escort in the Park. "If

[105-093]it wasn't for you, Mr. Dunn, I really think I should not get any

[105-094]riding at all. Bernard and Miss Dunstable have only one thing to

[105-095]think about, and certainly I am not that one thing." She thought it

[105-096]probable that if she could keep Siph close to her, Mrs. Thorne, who

[105-097]always managed those things herself, might apportion her out to be

[105-098]led to dinner by her good-natured friend. But the fates were averse.

[105-099]The time had now come, and Lily was waiting her turn. "Mr. Fowler

[105-100]Pratt, let me introduce you to Miss Lily Dale," said Mrs. Thorne.

[105-101]Lily could perceive that Mr. Pratt was startled. The sign he gave was

[105-102]the least possible sign in the world; but still it sufficed for Lily

[105-103]to perceive it. She put her hand upon his arm, and walked down with

[105-104]him to the dining-room without giving him the slightest cause to

[105-105]suppose that she knew who he was.

[105-106]"I think I saw you in the Park riding?" he said.

[105-107]"Yes, I was there; we go nearly every day."

[105-108]"I never ride; I was walking."

[105-109]"It seems to me that the people don't go there to walk, but to stand

[105-110]still," said Lily. "I cannot understand how so many people can bear

[105-111]to loiter about in that way--leaning on the rails and doing nothing."

[105-112]"It is about as good as the riding, and costs less money. That is all

[105-113]that can be said for it. Do you live chiefly in town?"

[105-114]"O dear, no; I live altogether in the country. I'm only up here

[105-115]because a cousin is going to be married."

[105-116]"Captain Dale you mean--to Miss Dunstable?" said Fowler Pratt.

[105-117]"When they have been joined together in holy matrimony, I shall go

[105-118]down to the country, and never, I suppose, come up to London again."

[105-119]"You do not like London?"

[105-120]"Not as a residence, I think," said Lily. "But of course one's

[105-121]likings and dislikings on such a matter depend on circumstances. I

[105-122]live with my mother, and all my relatives live near us. Of course I

[105-123]like the country best, because they are there."

[105-124]"Young ladies so often have a different way of looking at this

[105-125]subject. I shouldn't wonder if Miss Dunstable's views about it were

[105-126]altogether of another sort. Young ladies generally expect to be taken

[105-127]away from their fathers and mothers, and uncles and aunts."

[105-128]"But you see I expect to be left with mine," said Lily. After that

[105-129]she turned as much away from Mr. Fowler Pratt as she could, having

[105-130]taken an aversion to him. What business had he to talk to her about

[105-131]being taken away from her uncles and aunts? She had seen him with Mr.

[105-132]Crosbie, and it might be possible that they were intimate friends.

[105-133]It might be that Mr. Pratt was asking questions in Mr. Crosbie's

[105-134]interest. Let that be as it might, she would answer no more questions

[105-135]from him further than ordinary good breeding should require of her.

[105-136]"She is a nice girl, certainly," said Fowler Pratt to himself, as

[105-137]he walked home, "and I have no doubt would make a good, ordinary,

[105-138]everyday wife. But she is not such a paragon that a man should

[105-139]condescend to grovel in the dirt for her."

[105-140]That night Lily told Emily Dunstable the whole of Mr. Crosbie's

[105-141]history as far as she knew it, and also explained her new aversion to

[105-142]Mr. Fowler Pratt. "They are very great friends," said Emily. "Bernard

[105-143]has told me so; and you may be sure that Mr. Pratt knew the whole

[105-144]history before he came here. I am so sorry that my aunt asked him."

[105-145]"It does not signify in the least," said Lily. "Even if I were to

[105-146]meet Mr. Crosbie I don't think I should make such a fool of myself

[105-147]again. As it is, I can only hope he did not see it."

[105-148]"I am sure he did not."

[105-149]Then there was a pause, during which Lily sat with her face resting

[105-150]on both her hands. "It is wonderful how much he is altered," she said

[105-151]at last.

[105-152]"Think how much he has suffered."

[105-153]"I suppose I am altered as much, only I do not see it in myself."

[105-154]"I don't know what you were, but I don't think you can have changed

[105-155]much. You no doubt have suffered too, but not as he has done."

[105-156]"Oh, as for that, I have done very well. I think I'll go to bed now.

[105-157]The riding makes me so sleepy."

[105-158]CHAPTER LIV.

[105-159]THE CLERICAL COMMISSION.

[105-160]It was at last arranged that the five clergymen selected should meet

[105-161]at Dr. Tempest's house in Silverbridge to make inquiry and report to

[105-162]the bishop whether the circumstances connected with the cheque for

[105-163]twenty pounds were of such a nature as to make it incumbent on him

[105-164]to institute proceedings against Mr. Crawley in the Court of Arches.

[105-165]Dr. Tempest had acted upon the letter which he had received from the

[105-166]bishop, exactly as though there had been no meeting at the palace, no

[105-167]quarrel to the death between him and Mrs. Proudie. He was a prudent

[105-168]man, gifted with the great power of holding his tongue, and had not

[105-169]spoken a word, even to his wife, of what had occurred. After such

[105-170]a victory our old friend the archdeacon would have blown his own

[105-171]trumpet loudly among his friends. Plumstead would have heard of it

[105-172]instantly, and the pæan would have been sung out in the neighbouring

[105-173]parishes of Eiderdown, Stogpingum, and St. Ewolds. The high-street of

[105-174]Barchester would have known of it, and the very bedesmen in Hiram's

[105-175]Hospital would have told among themselves the terrible discomfiture

[105-176]of the bishop and his lady. But Dr. Tempest spoke no word of it to

[105-177]anybody. He wrote letters to the two clergymen named by the bishop,

[105-178]and himself selected two others out of his own rural deanery, and

[105-179]suggested to them all a day at which a preliminary meeting should

[105-180]be held at his own house. The two who were invited by him were Mr.

[105-181]Oriel, the rector of Greshamsbury, and Mr. Robarts, the vicar of

[105-182]Framley. They all assented to the proposition, and on the day named

[105-183]assembled themselves at Silverbridge.
