## Event

The marriage of Grace Crawley to Major Henry Grantly is the emotional culmination of *The Last Chronicle of Barset*. Grace, the eldest daughter of the impoverished and disgraced [[josiah-crawley|Josiah Crawley]], is loved by Major Grantly, the widowed son of Archdeacon Grantly of Plumstead and a gentleman of means and standing. Their union, which at the novel's opening seems impossible, becomes the measure by which the novel judges the charity and flexibility of Barsetshire's social order.

## Context

The courtship unfolds under the shadow of the cheque accusation. Major Grantly's father, the Archdeacon, initially opposes the match on grounds of social propriety: "It would be an unfitting marriage" (004-065). Mrs. Grantly echoes: "Such a marriage for Henry would be very unseemly" (004-047). Yet Major Grantly persists, his constancy proving that his love for Grace is not conditional on her father's reputation. [[lady-lufton|Lady Lufton]], whose "Proposition" (001-017) constitutes an entire chapter, lends her support, recognising in Grace a young woman of character and in the match a means of healing the county's divisions. Grace, for her part, endures the humiliation of her father's disgrace with dignity, refusing to release Major Grantly from his commitment yet never pressing her claim.

## Aftermath

The marriage, when it finally takes place, represents more than a private happy ending. It is a public reconciliation: the Grantlys of Plumstead accept the Crawleys of Hogglestock as equals, and the social stigma of the trial is symbolically dissolved. Grace, who had been "driven over to Allington in a pony carriage" (017-167) as a charity case, enters Framley as a bride. The union validates the novel's central argument: that personal character, tested by adversity, matters more than social circumstance. It also provides the structural closure that the Barsetshire series requires, joining the two principal families of the novel in a bond that looks forward to the next generation.

## Significance

The Grace–Grantly marriage plot is Trollope's most carefully constructed illustration of his recurring theme: the tension between social propriety and personal merit. Grace Crawley has no fortune, no powerful connections, and a father accused of theft. Major Grantly has rank, wealth, and the expectations of his class. That the marriage succeeds against these odds is Trollope's affirmation that the Victorian social order, for all its rigidity, contains within it the resources for flexibility and forgiveness. The marriage also serves a structural function in the Barsetshire series: it reunites the clerical world of Barchester with the gentry world of Framley, consolidating the social fabric that the trial had threatened to tear apart.
