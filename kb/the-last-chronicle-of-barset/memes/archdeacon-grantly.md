## Character

Archdeacon Grantly is the formidable head of the Plumstead branch of the Grantly family and "the respected head" of his clerical circle (003-141). As archdeacon of the diocese, he wields considerable ecclesiastical authority, but it is his domestic authority — particularly over his son [[major-grantly|Major Henry Grantly]] — that fuels the novel's central conflict. When he learns that his widowed son, the heir to Plumstead, intends to marry Grace Crawley, the daughter of an accused cheque-forger, his opposition is immediate and absolute. Trollope presents him as a man of principle whose principles are indistinguishable from his prejudices: "Archdeacon Grantly was a gentleman, and never yet" forgot his own consequence (112-091).

## Biography

The Archdeacon has spent his long career at the centre of Barchester's ecclesiastical establishment. He is the son of a bishop and the father of a major — lineage matters intensely to him. His life at Plumstead, the family estate, represents the ideal of the country clergyman made prosperous: comfortable, respected, and absolutely certain of his place in the social order. The news of his son's attachment to Grace Crawley — "who had no money, and no" social standing — strikes at everything he values. His opposition unfolds through a series of confrontations, letters, and strategic silences. Yet the Archdeacon is not a villain; his eventual, grudging acceptance of Grace reflects Trollope's belief that even the most obstinate pride can yield to personal feeling.

## Significance

The Archdeacon embodies Trollope's critique of institutional pride. He is not wicked — he is "a gentleman" (112-091) — but his sense of social order is so rigid that it becomes indistinguishable from cruelty. His opposition to the Grantly-Crawley match mirrors the novel's larger concern with how institutions (the Church, the law, the family) respond to scandal. By forcing the Archdeacon to choose between his principles and his son, Trollope dramatises the cost of inflexibility. The Archdeacon's gradual softening — driven not by argument but by the steady pressure of personal relationships — is one of the novel's quiet triumphs.
