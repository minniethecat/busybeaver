[117-001]At night, before she went to bed, she showed the letter to Emily

[117-002]Dunstable. "Is it not surprising that any woman could bring herself

[117-003]to write such a letter?" said Lily.

[117-004]But Miss Dunstable hardly saw it in the same light. "If anybody were

[117-005]to write me such a letter about Bernard," said she, "I should show it

[117-006]to him as a good joke."

[117-007]"That would be very different. You and Bernard, of course, understand

[117-008]each other."

[117-009]"And so will you and Mr. Eames--some day, I hope."

[117-010]"Never more than we do now, dear. The thing that annoys me is that

[117-011]such a woman as that should have even heard my name at all."

[117-012]"As long as people have got ears and tongues, people will hear other

[117-013]people's names."

[117-014]Lily paused a moment, and then spoke again, asking another question.

[117-015]"I suppose this woman does know him? She must know him, because he

[117-016]has written to her."

[117-017]"She knows something about him, no doubt, and has some reason for

[117-018]wishing that you should quarrel with him. If I were you, I should

[117-019]take care not to gratify her. As for Mr. Eames's note, it is a joke."

[117-020]"It is nothing to me," said Lily.

[117-021]"I suppose," continued Emily, "that most gentlemen become acquainted

[117-022]with some people that they would not wish all their friends to know

[117-023]that they knew. They go about so much more than we do, and meet

[117-024]people of all sorts."

[117-025]"No gentleman should become intimately acquainted with a woman who

[117-026]could write such a letter as that," said Lily. And as she spoke she

[117-027]remembered a certain episode to John Eames's early life, which had

[117-028]reached her from a source which she had not doubted, and which had

[117-029]given her pain and offended her. She had believed that John Eames had

[117-030]in that case behaved cruelly to a young woman, and had thought that

[117-031]her offence had come simply from that feeling. "But of course it is

[117-032]nothing to me," she said. "Mr. Eames can choose his friends as he

[117-033]likes. I only wish that my name might not be mentioned to them."

[117-034]"It is not from him that she has heard it."

[117-035]"Perhaps not. As I said before, of course it does not signify; only

[117-036]there is something very disagreeable in the whole thing. The idea

[117-037]is so hateful! Of course this woman means me to understand that she

[117-038]considers herself to have a claim upon Mr. Eames, and that I stand in

[117-039]her way."

[117-040]"And why should you not stand in her way?"

[117-041]"I will stand in nobody's way. Mr. Eames has a right to give his hand

[117-042]to any one that he pleases. I, at any rate, can have no cause of

[117-043]offence against him. The only thing is that I do wish that my name

[117-044]could be left alone." Lily, when she was in her own room again, did

[117-045]destroy the letter; but before she did so she read it again, and it

[117-046]became so indelibly impressed on her memory that she could not forget

[117-047]even the words of it. The lady who wrote had pledged herself, under

[117-048]certain conditions, "not to interfere with Miss L. D." "Interfere

[117-049]with me!" Lily said to herself; "nobody can interfere with me; nobody

[117-050]has power to do so." As she turned it over in her mind, her heart

[117-051]became hard against John Eames. No woman would have troubled herself

[117-052]to write such a letter without some cause for the writing. That the

[117-053]writer was vulgar, false, and unfeminine, Lily thought that she could

[117-054]perceive from the letter itself; but no doubt the woman knew John

[117-055]Eames, had some interest in the question of his marriage, and was

[117-056]entitled to some answer to her question;--only was not entitled to

[117-057]such answer from Lily Dale.

[117-058]For some weeks past now, up to the hour at which this anonymous

[117-059]letter had reached her hands, Lily's heart had been growing soft

[117-060]and still softer towards John Eames; and now again it had become

[117-061]hardened. I think that the appearance of Adolphus Crosbie in the

[117-062]park, that momentary vision of the real man by which the divinity

[117-063]of the imaginary Apollo had been dashed to the ground, had done a

[117-064]service to the cause of the other lover; of the lover who had never

[117-065]been a god, but who of late years had at any rate grown into the full

[117-066]dimensions of a man. Unfortunately for the latter, he had commenced

[117-067]his love-making when he was but little more than a boy. Lily, as she

[117-068]had thought of the two together, in the days of her solitude, after

[117-069]she had been deserted by Crosbie, had ever pictured to herself the

[117-070]lover whom she had preferred as having something godlike in his

[117-071]favour, as being far the superior in wit, in manner, in acquirement,

[117-072]and in personal advantage. There had been good nature and true hearty

[117-073]love on the side of the other man; but circumstances had seemed to

[117-074]show that his good-nature was equal to all, and that he was able

[117-075]to share even his hearty love among two or three. A man of such a

[117-076]character, known by a girl from his boyhood as John Eames had been

[117-077]known by Lily Dale, was likely to find more favour as a friend than

[117-078]as a lover. So it had been between John Eames and Lily. While the

[117-079]untrue memory of what Crosbie was, or ever had been, was present to

[117-080]her, she could hardly bring herself to accept in her mind the idea

[117-081]of a lover who was less noble in his manhood than the false picture

[117-082]which that untrue memory was ever painting for her. Then had come

[117-083]before her eyes the actual man; and though he had been seen but for

[117-084]a moment, the false image had been broken into shivers. Lily had

[117-085]discovered that she had been deceived, and that her forgiveness had

[117-086]been asked, not by a god, but by an ordinary human being. As regarded

[117-087]the ungodlike man himself, this could make no difference. Having

[117-088]thought upon the matter deeply, she had resolved that she would not

[117-089]marry Mr. Crosbie, and had pledged herself to that effect to friends

[117-090]who never could have brought themselves to feel affection for him,

[117-091]even had she married him. But the shattering of the false image might

[117-092]have done John Eames a good turn. Lily knew that she had at any

[117-093]rate full permission from all her friends to throw in her lot with

[117-094]his,--if she could persuade herself to do so. Mother, uncle, sister,

[117-095]brother-in-law, cousin,--and now this new cousin's bride that was

[117-096]to be,--together with Lady Julia and a whole crowd of Allington

[117-097]and Guestwick friends, were in favour of such a marriage. There

[117-098]had been nothing against it but the fact that the other man had

[117-099]been dearer to her; and that other fact that poor Johnny lacked

[117-100]something,--something of earnestness, something of manliness,

[117-101]something of that Phoebus divinity with which Crosbie had contrived

[117-102]to invest his own image. But, as I have said above, John had

[117-103]gradually grown, if not into divinity, at least into manliness; and

[117-104]the shattering of the false image had done him yeoman's service. Now

[117-105]had come this accursed letter, and Lily, despite herself, despite her

[117-106]better judgment, could not sweep it away from her mind and make the

[117-107]letter as nothing to her. M. D. had promised not to interfere with

[117-108]her! There was no room for such interference, no possibility that

[117-109]such interference should take place. She hoped earnestly,--so she

[117-110]told herself,--that her old friend John Eames might have nothing to

[117-111]do with a woman so impudent and vulgar as must be this M. D.; but

[117-112]except as regarded old friendship, M. D. and John Eames, apart or

[117-113]together, could be as nothing to her. Therefore, I say that the

[117-114]letter had had the effect which the writer of it had desired.

[117-115]All London was new to Lily Dale, and Mrs. Thorne was very anxious

[117-116]to show her everything that could be seen. She was to return to

[117-117]Allington before the flowers of May would have come, and the crowd

[117-118]and the glare and the fashion and the art of the Academy's great

[117-119]exhibition must therefore remain unknown to her; but she was taken to

[117-120]see many pictures, and among others she was taken to see the pictures

[117-121]belonging to a certain nobleman who, with that munificence which is

[117-122]so amply enjoyed and so little recognized in England, keeps open

[117-123]house for the world to see the treasures which the wealth of his

[117-124]family has collected. The necessary order was procured, and on a

[117-125]certain brilliant April afternoon Mrs. Thorne and her party found

[117-126]themselves in this nobleman's drawing-room. Lily was with her, of

[117-127]course, and Emily Dunstable was there, and Bernard Dale, and Mrs.

[117-128]Thorne's dear friend Mrs. Harold Smith, and Mrs. Thorne's constant

[117-129]and useful attendant, Siph Dunn. They had nearly completed their

[117-130]delightful but wearying task of gazing at pictures, and Mrs. Harold

[117-131]Smith had declared that she would not look at another painting till

[117-132]the exhibition was open; three of the ladies were seated in the

[117-133]drawing-room, and Siph Dunn was standing before them, lecturing

[117-134]about art as though he had been brought up on the ancient masters;

[117-135]Emily and Bernard were lingering behind, and the others were simply

[117-136]delaying their departure till the truant lovers should have caught

[117-137]them. At this moment two gentlemen entered the room from the gallery,

[117-138]and the two gentlemen were Fowler Pratt and Adolphus Crosbie.

[117-139]All the party except Mrs. Thorne knew Crosbie personally, and all of

[117-140]them except Mrs. Harold Smith knew something of the story of what had

[117-141]occurred between Crosbie and Lily. Siph Dunn had learned it all since

[117-142]the meeting in the Park, having nearly learned it all from what he

[117-143]had seen there with his eyes. But Mrs. Thorne, who knew Lily's story,

[117-144]did not know Crosbie's appearance. But there was his friend Fowler

[117-145]Pratt, who, as will be remembered, had dined with her but the other

[117-146]day; and she, with that outspoken and somewhat loud impulse which was

[117-147]natural to her, addressed him at once across the room, calling him by

[117-148]name. Had she not done so, the two men might probably have escaped

[117-149]through the room, in which case they would have met Bernard Dale and

[117-150]Emily Dunstable in the doorway. Fowler Pratt would have endeavoured

[117-151]so to escape, and to carry Crosbie with him, as he was quite alive

[117-152]to the expedience of saving Lily from such a meeting. But, as things

[117-153]turned out, escape from Mrs. Thorne was impossible.

[117-154]"There's Fowler Pratt," she had said when they first entered, quite

[117-155]loud enough for Fowler Pratt to hear her. "Mr. Pratt, come here. How

[117-156]d'ye do? You dined with me last Tuesday, and you've never been to

[117-157]call."

[117-158]"I never recognize that obligation till after the middle of May,"

[117-159]said Mr. Pratt, shaking hands with Mrs. Thorne and Mrs. Smith, and

[117-160]bowing to Miss Dale.

[117-161]"I don't see the justice of that at all," said Mrs. Thorne. "It

[117-162]seems to me that a good dinner is as much entitled to a morsel of

[117-163]pasteboard in April as at any other time. You won't have another till

[117-164]you have called,--unless you're specially wanted."

[117-165]Crosbie would have gone on, but that in his attempt to do so he

[117-166]passed close by the chair on which Mrs. Harold Smith was sitting, and

[117-167]that he was accosted by her. "Mr. Crosbie," she said, "I haven't seen

[117-168]you for an age. Has it come to pass that you have buried yourself

[117-169]entirely?" He did not know how to extricate himself so as to move

[117-170]on at once. He paused, and hesitated, and then stopped, and made an

[117-171]attempt to talk to Mrs. Smith as though he were at his ease. The

[117-172]attempt was anything but successful; but having once stopped, he did

[117-173]not know how to put himself in motion again, so that he might escape.

[117-174]At this moment Bernard Dale and Emily Dunstable came up and joined

[117-175]the group; but neither of them had discovered who Crosbie was till

[117-176]they were close upon him.
