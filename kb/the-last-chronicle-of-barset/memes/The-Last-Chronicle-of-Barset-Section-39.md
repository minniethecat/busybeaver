[039-001]"What the world says of me I have learned to disregard very much, Mr.

[039-002]Robarts. But I hope that I shall never disobey the authority of the

[039-003]Church when properly and legally exercised."

[039-004]"I hope with all my heart you never will; nor I either. And the

[039-005]archdeacon, who knows, to the breadth of a hair, what a bishop ought

[039-006]to do and what he ought not, and what he may do and what he may not,

[039-007]will, I should say, be the last man in England to sin in that way."

[039-008]"Very probably. I am far from contradicting you there. Pray

[039-009]understand, Mr. Robarts, that I bring no accusation against the

[039-010]archdeacon. Why should I?"

[039-011]"I didn't mean to discuss him at all."

[039-012]"Nor did I, Mr. Robarts."

[039-013]"I only mentioned his name, because, as I said, he was over with us

[039-014]the other day at Framley, and we were all talking about your affair."

[039-015]"My affair!" said Mr. Crawley. And then came a frown upon his brow,

[039-016]and a gleam of fire into his eyes, which effectually banished that

[039-017]look of extreme humility which he had assumed. "And may I ask why the

[039-018]archdeacon was discussing--my affair?"

[039-019]"Simply from the kindness which he bears to you."

[039-020]"I am grateful for the archdeacon's kindness, as a man is bound to be

[039-021]for any kindness, whether displayed wisely or unwisely. But it seems

[039-022]to me that my affair, as you call it, Mr. Robarts, is of that nature

[039-023]that they who wish well to me will better further their wishes by

[039-024]silence than by any discussion."

[039-025]"Then I cannot agree with you." Mr. Crawley shrugged his shoulders,

[039-026]opened his hands a little and then closed them, and bowed his head.

[039-027]He could not have declared more clearly by any words that he differed

[039-028]altogether from Mr. Robarts, and that as the subject was one so

[039-029]peculiarly his own he had a right to expect that his opinion should

[039-030]be allowed to prevail against that of any other person. "If you come

[039-031]to that, you know, how is anybody's tongue to be stopped?"

[039-032]"That vain tongues cannot be stopped, I am well aware. I do not

[039-033]expect that people's tongues should be stopped. I am not saying what

[039-034]men will do, but what good wishes should dictate."

[039-035]"Well, perhaps you'll hear me out for a minute." Mr. Crawley again

[039-036]bowed his head. "Whether we were wise or unwise, we were discussing

[039-037]this affair."

[039-038]"Whether I stole Mr. Soames's money?"

[039-039]"No; nobody supposed for a moment you had stolen it."

[039-040]"I cannot understand how they should suppose anything else, knowing,

[039-041]as they do, that the magistrates have committed me for the theft.

[039-042]This took place at Framley, you say, and probably in Lord Lufton's

[039-043]presence."

[039-044]"Exactly."

[039-045]"And Lord Lufton was chairman at the sitting of the magistrates at

[039-046]which I was committed. How can it be that he should think otherwise?"

[039-047]"I am sure he has not an idea that you were guilty. Nor yet has Dr.

[039-048]Thorne, who was also one of the magistrates. I don't suppose one of

[039-049]them then thought so."

[039-050]"Then their action, to say the least of it, was very strange."

[039-051]"It was all because you had nobody to manage it for you. I thoroughly

[039-052]believe that if you had placed the matter in the hands of a good

[039-053]lawyer, you would never have heard a word more about it. That seems

[039-054]to be the opinion of everybody I speak to on the subject."

[039-055]"Then in this country a man is to be punished or not, according to

[039-056]his ability to fee a lawyer!"

[039-057]"I am not talking about punishment."

[039-058]"And presuming an innocent man to have the ability and not the will

[039-059]to do so, he is to be punished, to be ruined root and branch, self

[039-060]and family, character and pocket, simply because, knowing his own

[039-061]innocence, he does not choose to depend on the mercenary skill of a

[039-062]man whose trade he abhors for the establishment of that which should

[039-063]be clear as the sun at noon-day! You say I am innocent, and yet you

[039-064]tell me I am to be condemned as a guilty man, have my gown taken

[039-065]from me, be torn from my wife and children, be disgraced before the

[039-066]eyes of all men, and be made a byword and a thing horrible to be

[039-067]mentioned, because I will not fee an attorney to fee another man

[039-068]to come and lie on my behalf, to browbeat witnesses, to make false

[039-069]appeals, and perhaps shed false tears in defending me. You have come

[039-070]to me asking me to do this, if I understand you, telling me that the

[039-071]archdeacon would so advise me."

[039-072]"That is my object." Mr. Crawley, as he had spoken, had in his

[039-073]vehemence risen from his seat, and Mr. Robarts was also standing.

[039-074]"Then tell the archdeacon," said Mr. Crawley, "that I will have none

[039-075]of his advice. I will have no one there paid by me to obstruct the

[039-076]course of justice or to hoodwink a jury. I have been in courts of

[039-077]law, and know what is the work for which these gentlemen are hired.

[039-078]I will have none of it, and I will thank you to tell the archdeacon

[039-079]so, with my respectful acknowledgments of his consideration and

[039-080]condescension. I say nothing as to my own innocence, or my own guilt.

[039-081]But I do say that if I am dragged before that tribunal, an innocent

[039-082]man, and am falsely declared to be guilty, because I lack money to

[039-083]bribe a lawyer to speak for me, then the laws of this country deserve

[039-084]but little of that reverence which we are accustomed to pay to them.

[039-085]And if I be guilty--"

[039-086]"Nobody supposes you to be guilty."

[039-087]"And if I be guilty," continued Mr. Crawley, altogether ignoring the

[039-088]interruption, except by the repetition of his words, and a slight

[039-089]raising of his voice, "I will not add to my guilt by hiring any one

[039-090]to prove a falsehood or to disprove a truth."

[039-091]"I'm sorry that you should say so, Mr. Crawley."

[039-092]"I speak according to what light I have, Mr. Robarts; and if I have

[039-093]been over-warm with you,--and I am conscious that I have been in

[039-094]fault in that direction,--I must pray you to remember that I am

[039-095]somewhat hardly tried. My sorrows and troubles are so great that they

[039-096]rise against me and disturb me, and drive me on,--whither I would not

[039-097]be driven."

[039-098]"But, my friend, is not that just the reason why you should trust in

[039-099]this matter to some one who can be more calm than yourself?"

[039-100]"I cannot trust to any one,--in a matter of conscience. To do as you

[039-101]would have me is to me wrong. Shall I do wrong because I am unhappy?"

[039-102]"You should cease to think it wrong when so advised by persons you

[039-103]can trust."

[039-104]"I can trust no one with my own conscience;--not even the archdeacon,

[039-105]great as he is."

[039-106]"The archdeacon has meant only well to you."

[039-107]"I will presume so. I will believe so. I do think so. Tell the

[039-108]archdeacon from me that I humbly thank him;--that, in a matter of

[039-109]church question, I might probably submit my judgment to his; even

[039-110]though he might have no authority over me, knowing as I do that in

[039-111]such matters his experience has been great. Tell him also, that

[039-112]though I would fain that this unfortunate affair might burden the

[039-113]tongue of none among my neighbours,--at least till I shall have stood

[039-114]before the judge to receive the verdict of the jury, and, if needful,

[039-115]his lordship's sentence--still I am convinced that in what he has

[039-116]spoken, as also in what he has done, he has not yielded to the

[039-117]idleness of gossip, but has exercised his judgment with intended

[039-118]kindness."

[039-119]"He has certainly intended to do you a service; and as for its not

[039-120]being talked about, that is out of the question."

[039-121]"And for yourself, Mr. Robarts, whom I have ever regarded as a friend

[039-122]since circumstances brought me into your neighbourhood,--for you,

[039-123]whose sister I love tenderly in memory of past kindness, though now

[039-124]she is removed so far above my sphere, as to make it unfit that I

[039-125]should call her my friend--"

[039-126]"She does not think so at all."

[039-127]"For yourself, as I was saying, pray believe me that though from the

[039-128]roughness of my manner, being now unused to social intercourse, I

[039-129]seem to be ungracious and forbidding, I am grateful and mindful, and

[039-130]that in the tablets of my heart I have written you down as one in

[039-131]whom I could trust,--were it given to me to trust in men and women."

[039-132]Then he turned round with his face to the wall and his back to his

[039-133]visitor, and so remained till Mr. Robarts had left him. "At any rate

[039-134]I wish you well through your trouble," said Robarts; and as he spoke

[039-135]he found that his own words were nearly choked by a sob that was

[039-136]rising in his throat.

[039-137]He went away without another word, and got out to his gig without

[039-138]seeing Mrs. Crawley. During one period of the interview he had been

[039-139]very angry with the man,--so angry as to make him almost declare to

[039-140]himself that he would take no more trouble on his behalf. Then he

[039-141]had been brought to acknowledge that Mr. Walker was right, and that

[039-142]Crawley was certainly mad. He was so mad, so far removed from the

[039-143]dominion of sound sense, that no jury could say that he was guilty

[039-144]and that he ought to be punished for his guilt. And, as he so

[039-145]resolved, he could not but ask himself the question, whether the

[039-146]charge of the parish ought to be left in the hands of such a man? But

[039-147]at last, just before he went, these feelings and these convictions

[039-148]gave way to pity, and he remembered simply the troubles which seemed

[039-149]to have been heaped on the head of this poor victim to misfortune.

[039-150]As he drove home he resolved that there was nothing left for him to

[039-151]do, but to write to the dean. It was known to all who knew them both,

[039-152]that the dean and Mr. Crawley had lived together on the closest

[039-153]intimacy at college, and that that friendship had been maintained

[039-154]through life;--though, from the peculiarity of Mr. Crawley's

[039-155]character, the two had not been much together of late years. Seeing

[039-156]how things were going now, and hearing how pitiful was the plight in

[039-157]which Mr. Crawley was placed, the dean would, no doubt, feel it to be

[039-158]his duty to hasten his return to England. He was believed to be at

[039-159]this moment in Jerusalem, and it would be long before a letter could

[039-160]reach him; but there still wanted three months to the assizes, and

[039-161]his return might be probably effected before the end of February.

[039-162]"I never was so distressed in my life," Mark Robarts said to his

[039-163]wife.

[039-164]"And you think you have done no good?"

[039-165]"Only this, that I have convinced myself that the poor man is not

[039-166]responsible for what he does, and that for her sake as well as

[039-167]for his own, some person should be enabled to interfere for his

[039-168]protection." Then he told Mrs. Robarts what Mr. Walker had said; also

[039-169]the message which Mr. Crawley had sent to the archdeacon. But they

[039-170]both agreed that that message need not be sent on any further.

[039-171]CHAPTER XXII.

[039-172]MAJOR GRANTLY AT HOME.

[039-173]Mrs. Thorne had spoken very plainly in the advice which she had given

[039-174]to Major Grantly. "If I were you, I'd be at Allington before twelve

[039-175]o'clock to-morrow." That had been Mrs. Thorne's advice; and though

[039-176]Major Grantly had no idea of making the journey so rapidly as the

[039-177]lady had proposed, still he thought that he would make it before

[039-178]long, and follow the advice in spirit if not to the letter. Mrs.

[039-179]Thorne had asked him if it was fair that the girl should be punished

[039-180]because of the father's fault; and the idea had been sweet to him

[039-181]that the infliction or non-infliction of such punishment should be

[039-182]in his hands. "You go and ask her," Mrs. Thorne had said. Well;--he

[039-183]would go and ask her. If it should turn out at last that he had

[039-184]married the daughter of a thief, and that he was disinherited for

[039-185]doing so,--an arrangement of circumstances which he had to teach

[039-186]himself to regard as very probable,--he would not love Grace the less

[039-187]on that account, or allow himself for one moment to repent what he

[039-188]had done. As he thought of all this he became somewhat in love with a

[039-189]small income, and imagined to himself what honours would be done to

[039-190]him by the Mrs. Thornes of the county, when they should come to know

[039-191]in what way he had sacrificed himself to his love. Yes;--they would

[039-192]go and live at Pau. He thought Pau would do. He would have enough

[039-193]of income for that;--and Edith would get lessons cheaply, and would

[039-194]learn to talk French fluently. He certainly would do it. He would

[039-195]go down to Allington, and ask Grace to be his wife; and bid her

[039-196]understand that if she loved him she could not be justified in

[039-197]refusing him by the circumstances of her father's position.
