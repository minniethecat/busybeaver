[058-001]"Then I will go to the man, and will humble myself before him. Even

[058-002]that, hard as it is to me, may be a duty that I owe." Mr. Crawley as

[058-003]he said this was remembering the fact that he was a clergyman of the

[058-004]Church of England, and that he had a rank of his own in the country,

[058-005]which, did he ever do such a thing as go out to dinner in company,

[058-006]would establish for him a certain right of precedence; whereas this

[058-007]attorney, of whom he was speaking, was, so to say, nobody in the eyes

[058-008]of the world.

[058-009]"There need be no humbling, Josiah, other than that which is due from

[058-010]man to man in all circumstances. But never mind; we will not talk

[058-011]about that. If it seems good to you, go to Mr. Toogood. I think that

[058-012]it is good. May I write to him and say that you will go?"

[058-013]"I will write myself; it will be more seemly."

[058-014]Then the wife paused before she asked the next question,--paused for

[058-015]some minute or two, and then asked it with anxious doubt,--"And may

[058-016]I go with you, Josiah?"

[058-017]"Why should two go when one can do the work?" he answered sharply.

[058-018]"Have we money so much at command?"

[058-019]"Indeed, no."

[058-020]"You should go and do it all, for you are wiser in these things than

[058-021]I am, were it not that I may not dare to show--that I submit myself

[058-022]to my wife."

[058-023]"Nay, my dear!"

[058-024]"But it is ay, my dear. It is so. This is a thing such as men do; not

[058-025]such as women do, unless they be forlorn and unaided of men. I know

[058-026]that I am weak where you are strong; that I am crazed where you are

[058-027]clear-witted."

[058-028]"I meant not that, Josiah. It was of your health that I thought."

[058-029]"Nevertheless it is as I say; but, for all that, it may not be that

[058-030]you should do my work. There are those watching me who would say,

[058-031]'Lo! he confesses himself incapable.' And then some one would whisper

[058-032]something of a madhouse. Mary, I fear that worse than a prison."

[058-033]"May God in His mercy forbid such cruelty!"

[058-034]"But I must look to it, my dear. Do you think that that woman, who

[058-035]sits there at Barchester in high places, disgracing herself and that

[058-036]puny ecclesiastical lord who is her husband,--do you think that she

[058-037]would not immure me if she could? She is a she-wolf,--only less

[058-038]reasonable than the dumb brute as she sharpens her teeth in malice

[058-039]coming from anger, and not in malice coming from hunger as do the

[058-040]outer wolves of the forest. I tell you, Mary, that if she had a

[058-041]colourable ground for her action, she would swear to-morrow that I am

[058-042]mad."

[058-043]"You shall go alone to London."

[058-044]"Yes, I will go alone. They shall not say that I cannot yet do my own

[058-045]work as a man should do it. I stood up before him, the puny man who

[058-046]is called a bishop, and before her who makes herself great by his

[058-047]littleness, and I scorned them both to their faces. Though the shoes

[058-048]which I had on were all broken, as I myself could not but see when I

[058-049]stood, yet I was greater than they were with all their purple and

[058-050]fine linen."

[058-051]"But, Josiah, my cousin will not be harsh to you."

[058-052]"Well,--and if he be not?"

[058-053]"Ill-usage you can bear; and violent ill-usage, such as that which

[058-054]Mrs. Proudie allowed herself to exhibit, you can repay with interest;

[058-055]but kindness seems to be too heavy a burden for you."

[058-056]"I will struggle. I will endeavour. I will speak but little, and, if

[058-057]possible, I will listen much. Now, my dear, I will write to this man,

[058-058]and you shall give me the address that is proper for him." Then he

[058-059]wrote the letter, not accepting a word in the way of dictation from

[058-060]his wife, but "craving the great kindness of a short interview, for

[058-061]which he ventured to become a solicitor, urged thereto by his wife's

[058-062]assurance that one with whom he was connected by family ties would do

[058-063]as much as this for the possible preservation of the honour of the

[058-064]family." In answer to this, Mr. Toogood wrote back as follows:--"Dear

[058-065]Mr. Crawley, I will be at my office all Thursday morning next from

[058-066]ten to two, and will take care that you shan't be kept waiting for

[058-067]me above ten minutes. You parsons never like waiting. But hadn't you

[058-068]better come and breakfast with me and Maria at nine? then we'd have a

[058-069]talk as we walk to the office. Yours always, THOMAS TOOGOOD." And

[058-070]the letter was dated from the attorney's private house in Tavistock

[058-071]Square.

[058-072]"I am sure he means to be kind," said Mrs. Crawley.

[058-073]"Doubtless he means to be kind. But his kindness is rough;--I will

[058-074]not say unmannerly, as the word would be harsh. I have never even

[058-075]seen the lady whom he calls Maria."

[058-076]"She is his wife!"

[058-077]"So I would venture to suppose; but she is unknown to me. I will

[058-078]write again, and thank him, and say that I will be with him at ten to

[058-079]the moment."

[058-080]There were still many things to be settled before the journey could

[058-081]be made. Mr. Crawley, in his first plan, proposed that he should

[058-082]go up by night mail train, travelling in the third class, having

[058-083]walked over to Silverbridge to meet it; that he should then walk

[058-084]about London from 5 A.M. to 10 A.M., and afterwards come down by an

[058-085]afternoon train to which a third class was also attached. But at last

[058-086]his wife persuaded him that such a task as that, performed in the

[058-087]middle of the winter, would be enough to kill any man, and that,

[058-088]if attempted, it would certainly kill him; and he consented at

[058-089]last to sleep the night in town,--being specially moved thereto by

[058-090]discovering that he could, in conformity with this scheme, get in

[058-091]and out of the train at a station considerably nearer to him than

[058-092]Silverbridge, and that he could get a return-ticket at a third-class

[058-093]fare. The whole journey, he found, could be done for a pound,

[058-094]allowing him seven shillings for his night's expenses in London;

[058-095]and out of the resources of the family there were produced two

[058-096]sovereigns, so that in the event of accident he would not utterly be

[058-097]a castaway from want of funds.

[058-098]So he started on his journey after an early dinner, almost hopeful

[058-099]through the new excitement of a journey to London, and his wife

[058-100]walked with him nearly as far as the station. "Do not reject my

[058-101]cousin's kindness," were the last words she spoke.

[058-102]"For his professional kindness, if he will extend it to me, I will

[058-103]be most thankful," he replied. She did not dare to say more; nor had

[058-104]she dared to write privately to her cousin, asking for any special

[058-105]help, lest by doing so she should seem to impugn the sufficiency and

[058-106]stability of her husband's judgment. He got up to town late at night,

[058-107]and having made inquiry of one of the porters, he hired a bed for

[058-108]himself in the neighbourhood of the railway station. Here he had

[058-109]a cup of tea and a morsel of bread-and-butter, and in the morning

[058-110]he breakfasted again on the same fare. "No, I have no luggage,"

[058-111]he had said to the girl at the public-house, who had asked him as

[058-112]to his travelling gear. "If luggage be needed as a certificate of

[058-113]respectability, I will pass on elsewhere," said he. The girl stared,

[058-114]and assured him that she did not doubt his respectability. "I

[058-115]am a clergyman of the Church of England," he had said, "but my

[058-116]circumstances prevent me from seeking a more expensive lodging."

[058-117]They did their best to make him comfortable, and, I think, almost

[058-118]disappointed him in not heaping further misfortunes on his head.

[058-119]He was in Raymond's Buildings at half-past nine, and for half an

[058-120]hour walked up and down the umbrageous pavement,--it used to be

[058-121]umbrageous, but perhaps the trees have gone now,--before the doors

[058-122]of the various chambers. He could hear the clock strike from Gray's

[058-123]Inn; and the moment that it had struck he was turning in, but was

[058-124]encountered in the passage by Mr. Toogood, who was equally punctual

[058-125]with himself. Strange stories about Mr. Crawley had reached Mr.

[058-126]Toogood's household, and that Maria, the mention of whose Christian

[058-127]name had been so offensive to the clergyman, had begged her husband

[058-128]not to be a moment late. Poor Mr. Toogood, who on ordinary days

[058-129]did perhaps take a few minutes' grace, was thus hurried away almost

[058-130]with his breakfast in his throat, and, as we have seen, just

[058-131]saved himself. "Perhaps, sir, you are Mr. Crawley?" he said, in a

[058-132]good-humoured, cheery voice. He was a good-humoured, cheery-looking

[058-133]man, about fifty years of age, with grizzled hair and sunburnt face,

[058-134]and large whiskers. Nobody would have taken him to be a partner in

[058-135]any of those great houses of which we have read in history,--the

[058-136]Quirk, Gammon and Snaps of the profession, or the Dodson and Foggs,

[058-137]who are immortal.

[058-138]"That is my name, sir," said Mr. Crawley, taking off his hat and

[058-139]bowing low, "and I am here by appointment to meet Mr. Toogood, the

[058-140]solicitor, whose name I see affixed upon the door-post."

[058-141]"I am Mr. Toogood, the solicitor, and I hope I see you quite well,

[058-142]Mr. Crawley." Then the attorney shook hands with the clergyman and

[058-143]preceded him upstairs to the front room on the first floor. "Here we

[058-144]are, Mr. Crawley, and pray take a chair. I wish you could have made

[058-145]it convenient to come and see us at home. We are rather long, as my

[058-146]wife says,--long in family, she means, and therefore are not very

[058-147]well off for spare beds--"

[058-148]"Oh, sir."

[058-149]"I've twelve of 'em living, Mr. Crawley,--from eighteen years, the

[058-150]eldest,--a girl, down to eighteen months the youngest,--a boy, and

[058-151]they go in and out, boy and girl, boy and girl, like the cogs of a

[058-152]wheel. They ain't such far away distant cousins from your own young

[058-153]ones--only first, once, as we call it."

[058-154]"I am aware that there is a family tie, or I should not have ventured

[058-155]to trouble you."

[058-156]"Blood is thicker than water; isn't it? I often say that. I heard

[058-157]of one of your girls only yesterday. She is staying somewhere down

[058-158]in the country, not far from where my sister lives--Mrs. Eames, the

[058-159]widow of poor John Eames, who never did any good in this world. I

[058-160]daresay you've heard of her?"

[058-161]"The name is familiar to me, Mr. Toogood."

[058-162]"Of course it is. I've a nephew down there just now, and he saw

[058-163]your girl the other day;--very highly he spoke of her too. Let me

[058-164]see;--how many is it you have?"

[058-165]"Three living, Mr. Toogood."

[058-166]"I've just four times three;--that's the difference. But I comfort

[058-167]myself with the text about the quiver you know; and I tell them that

[058-168]when they've eat up all the butter, they'll have to take their bread

[058-169]dry."

[058-170]"I trust the young people take your teaching in a proper spirit."

[058-171]"I don't know much about spirit. There's spirit enough. My second

[058-172]girl, Lucy, told me that if I came home to-day without tickets for

[058-173]the pantomime I shouldn't have any dinner allowed me. That's the way

[058-174]they treat me. But we understand each other at home. We're all pretty

[058-175]good friends there, thank God. And there isn't a sick chick among the

[058-176]boiling."

[058-177]"You have many mercies for which you should indeed be thankful," said

[058-178]Mr. Crawley, gravely.

[058-179]"Yes, yes, yes; that's true. I think of that sometimes, though

[058-180]perhaps not so much as I ought to do. But the best way to be thankful

[058-181]is to use the goods the gods provide you. 'The lovely Thais sits

[058-182]beside you. Take the goods the gods provide you.' I often say that

[058-183]to my wife, till the children have got to calling her Thais. The

[058-184]children have it pretty much their own way with us, Mr. Crawley."
