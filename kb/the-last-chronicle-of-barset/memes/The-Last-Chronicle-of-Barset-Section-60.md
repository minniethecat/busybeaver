[060-001]"Go on;--go on. Let me hear it all, and I shall learn something."

[060-002]"I know now how vain, how vile I was. I always know afterwards how

[060-003]low the spirit has grovelled. I had gone to him then because I

[060-004]had resolved to humble myself, and, for my wife's sake, to ask my

[060-005]friend--for money. With words which were very awkward,--which no

[060-006]doubt were ungracious--I had asked him, and he had bid me follow

[060-007]him from his hall into his library. There he left me awhile, and on

[060-008]returning told me with a smile that he had sent for money,--and, if

[060-009]I can remember, the sum he named was fifty pounds."

[060-010]"But it has turned out, as you say, that you have paid fifty pounds

[060-011]with his money,--besides the cheque."

[060-012]"That is true;--that is quite true. There is no doubt of that. But

[060-013]as I was saying,--then he fell to talking about the books, and I was

[060-014]angered. I was very sore in my heart. From the moment in which the

[060-015]words of beggary had passed from my lips, I had repented. And he had

[060-016]laughed and had taken it gaily. I turned upon him and told him that I

[060-017]had changed my mind. I was grateful, but I would not have his money.

[060-018]And so I prepared to go. But he argued with me, and would not let me

[060-019]go,--telling me of my wife and of my children, and while he argued

[060-020]there came a knock at the door, and something was handed in, and I

[060-021]knew that it was the hand of his wife."

[060-022]"It was the money, I suppose?"

[060-023]"Yes, Mr. Toogood; it was the money. And I became the more uneasy,

[060-024]because she herself is rich. I liked it the less because it seemed to

[060-025]come from her hand. But I took it. What could I do when he reminded

[060-026]me that I could not keep my parish unless certain sums were paid?

[060-027]He gave me a little parcel in a cover, and I took it,--and left him

[060-028]sorrowing. I had never before come quite to that;--though, indeed, it

[060-029]had in fact been often so before. What was the difference whether the

[060-030]alms were given into my hands or into my wife's?"

[060-031]"You are too touchy about it all, Mr. Crawley."

[060-032]"Of course I am. Do you try it, and see whether you will be touchy.

[060-033]You have worked hard at your profession, I daresay."

[060-034]"Well, yes; pretty well. To tell the truth, I have worked hard. By

[060-035]George, yes! It's not so bad now as it used to be."

[060-036]"But you have always earned your bread; bread for yourself, and bread

[060-037]for your wife and little ones. You can buy tickets for the play."

[060-038]"I couldn't always buy tickets, mind you."

[060-039]"I have worked as hard, and yet I cannot get bread. I am older than

[060-040]you, and I cannot earn my bare bread. Look at my clothes. If you had

[060-041]to go and beg from Mr. Crump, would not you be touchy?"

[060-042]"As it happens, Crump isn't so well off as I am."

[060-043]"Never mind. But I took it, and went home, and for two days I did not

[060-044]look at it. And then there came an illness upon me, and I know not

[060-045]what passed. But two men who had been hard on me came to the house

[060-046]when I was out, and my wife was in a terrible state; and I gave her

[060-047]the money, and she went into Silverbridge and paid them."

[060-048]"And this cheque was with what you gave her?"

[060-049]"No; I gave her money in notes,--just fifty pounds. When I gave

[060-050]it her, I thought I gave it all; and yet afterwards I thought I

[060-051]remembered that in my illness I had found the cheque with the dean's

[060-052]money. But it was not so."

[060-053]"You are sure of that?"

[060-054]"He has said that he put five notes of £10 each into the cover, and

[060-055]such notes I certainly gave to my wife."

[060-056]"Where then did you get the cheque?" Mr. Crawley again paused

[060-057]before he answered. "Surely, if you will exert your mind, you will

[060-058]remember," said the lawyer. "Where did you get the cheque?"

[060-059]"I do not know."

[060-060]Mr. Toogood threw himself back in his chair, took his knee up into

[060-061]his lap to nurse it, and began to think of it. He sat thinking of it

[060-062]for some minutes without a word,--perhaps for five minutes, though

[060-063]the time seemed to be much longer to Mr. Crawley, who was, however,

[060-064]determined that he would not interrupt him. And Mr. Toogood's

[060-065]thoughts were at variance with Mr. Toogood's former words. Perhaps,

[060-066]after all, this scheme of Mr. Crawley's,--or rather the mode of

[060-067]defence on which he had resolved without any scheme,--might be the

[060-068]best of which the case admitted. It might be well that he should go

[060-069]into court without a lawyer. "He has convinced me of his innocence,"

[060-070]Mr. Toogood said to himself, "and why should he not convince a

[060-071]jury? He has convinced me, not because I am specially soft, or

[060-072]because I love the man,--for as to that I dislike him rather than

[060-073]otherwise;--but because there is either real truth in his words,

[060-074]or else so well-feigned a show of truth that no jury can tell the

[060-075]difference. I think it is true. By George, I think he did get the

[060-076]twenty pounds honestly, and that he does not this moment know where

[060-077]he got it. He may have put his finger into my eye; but, if so, why

[060-078]not also into the eyes of a jury?" Then he released his leg, and

[060-079]spoke something of his thoughts aloud. "It's a sad story," he said;

[060-080]"a very sad story."

[060-081]"Well, yes, it's sad enough. If you could see my house, you'd say

[060-082]so."

[060-083]"I haven't a doubt but what you're as innocent as I am." Mr. Toogood,

[060-084]as he said this, felt a little twinge of conscience. He did believe

[060-085]Mr. Crawley to be innocent, but he was not so sure of it as his words

[060-086]would seem to imply. Nevertheless he repeated the words again;--"as

[060-087]innocent as I am."

[060-088]"I don't know," said Mr. Crawley. "I don't know. I think I am; but I

[060-089]don't know."

[060-090]"I believe you are. But you see the case is a very distressing one.

[060-091]A jury has a right to say that the man in possession of a cheque

[060-092]for twenty pounds should account for his possession of it. If I

[060-093]understand the story aright, Mr. Soames will be able to prove that he

[060-094]brought the cheque into your house, and, as far as he knows, never

[060-095]took it out again."

[060-096]"I suppose so; all the same, if he brought it in, then did he also

[060-097]take it out again."

[060-098]"I am saying what he will prove,--or, in other words, what he will

[060-099]state upon oath. You can't contradict him. You can't get into the box

[060-100]to do it,--even if that would be of any avail; and I am glad that you

[060-101]cannot, as it would be of no avail. And you can put no one else into

[060-102]the box who can do so."

[060-103]"No; no."

[060-104]"That is to say, we think you cannot do so. People can do so many

[060-105]things that they don't think they can do; and can't do so many things

[060-106]that they think that they can do! When will the dean be home?"

[060-107]"I don't know."

[060-108]"Before the trial?"

[060-109]"I don't know. I have no idea."

[060-110]"It's almost a toss-up whether he'd do more harm or good if he were

[060-111]there."

[060-112]"I wish he might be there if he has anything to say, whether it might

[060-113]be for harm or good."

[060-114]"And Mrs. Arabin;--she is with him?"

[060-115]"They tell me she is not. She is in Europe. He is in Palestine."

[060-116]"In Palestine, is he?"

[060-117]"So they tell me. A dean can go where he likes. He has no cure of

[060-118]souls to stand in the way of his pleasures."

[060-119]"He hasn't,--hasn't he? I wish I were a dean; that is, if I were not

[060-120]a lawyer. Might I write a line to the dean,--and to Mrs. Dean, if

[060-121]it seemed fit? You wouldn't mind that? As you have come to see your

[060-122]cousin at last,--and very glad I am that you have,--you must leave

[060-123]him a little discretion. I won't say anything I oughtn't to say." Mr.

[060-124]Crawley opposed this scheme for some time, but at last consented to

[060-125]the proposition. "And I'll tell you what, Mr. Crawley; I am very fond

[060-126]of cathedrals, I am indeed; and I have long wanted to see Barchester.

[060-127]There's a very fine what-you-may-call-em; isn't there? Well; I'll

[060-128]just run down at the assizes. We have nothing to do in London when

[060-129]the judges are in the country,--of course." Mr. Toogood looked into

[060-130]Mr. Crawley's eyes as he said this, to see if his iniquity were

[060-131]detected, but the perpetual curate was altogether innocent in these

[060-132]matters. "Yes; I'll just run down for a mouthful of fresh air. Of

[060-133]course I shan't open my mouth in court. But I might say one word

[060-134]to the dean, if he's there;--and one word to Mr. Soames. Who is

[060-135]conducting the prosecution?" Mr. Crawley said that Mr. Walker was

[060-136]doing so. "Walker, Walker, Walker? oh,--yes; Walker and Winthrop,

[060-137]isn't it? A decent sort of man, I suppose?"

[060-138]"I have heard nothing to his discredit, Mr. Toogood."

[060-139]"And that's saying a great deal for a lawyer. Well, Mr. Crawley, if

[060-140]nothing else comes out between this and that,--nothing, that is, that

[060-141]shall clear your memory about that unfortunate bit of paper, you must

[060-142]simply tell your story to the jury as you've told it to me. I don't

[060-143]think any twelve men in England would convict you;--I don't indeed."

[060-144]"You think they would not?"

[060-145]"Of course I've only heard one side, Mr. Crawley."

[060-146]"No,--no,--no, that is true."

[060-147]"But judging as well as I can judge from one side, I don't think a

[060-148]jury can convict you. At any rate I'll see you at Barchester, and

[060-149]I'll write a line or two before the trial, just to find out anything

[060-150]that can be found out. And you're sure you won't come and take a bit

[060-151]of mutton with us in the Square? The girls would be delighted to see

[060-152]you, and so would Maria." Mr. Crawley said that he was quite sure

[060-153]he could not do that, and then having tendered reiterated thanks

[060-154]to his new friend in words which were touching in spite of their

[060-155]old-fashioned gravity, he took his leave, and walked back again to

[060-156]the public-house at Paddington.

[060-157]He returned home to Hogglestock on the same afternoon, reaching that

[060-158]place at nine in the evening. During the whole of the day after

[060-159]leaving Raymond's Buildings he was thinking of the lawyer, and of the

[060-160]words which the lawyer had spoken. Although he had been disposed to

[060-161]quarrel with Mr. Toogood on many points, although he had been more

[060-162]than once disgusted by the attorney's bad taste, shocked by his low

[060-163]morality, and almost insulted by his easy familiarity, still, when

[060-164]the interview was over, he liked the attorney. When first Mr. Toogood

[060-165]had begun to talk, he regretted very much that he had subjected

[060-166]himself to the necessity of discussing his private affairs with

[060-167]such a windbag of a man; but when he left the chamber he trusted Mr.

[060-168]Toogood altogether, and was very glad that he had sought his aid. He

[060-169]was tired and exhausted when he reached home, as he had eaten nothing

[060-170]but a biscuit or two since his breakfast; but his wife got him food

[060-171]and tea, and then asked him as to his success. "Was my cousin kind to

[060-172]you?"

[060-173]"Very kind,--more than kind,--perhaps somewhat too pressing in his

[060-174]kindness. But I find no fault. God forbid that I should. He is, I

[060-175]think, a good man, and certainly has been good to me."
