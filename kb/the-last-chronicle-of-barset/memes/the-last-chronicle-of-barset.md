## Work

*The Last Chronicle of Barset* is the sixth and concluding novel of Anthony Trollope's Chronicles of Barsetshire series. It was first published in monthly instalments between December 1866 and July 1867, and in two volumes later in 1867. The novel is widely regarded as Trollope's masterpiece and one of the greatest achievements of Victorian fiction — a complex, psychologically acute study of pride, poverty, and social justice set in the fictional county of Barsetshire.

## Overview

The novel's central plot concerns [[josiah-crawley|Josiah Crawley]], the impoverished perpetual curate of [[hogglestock]], who is accused of stealing a twenty-pound cheque. The charge threatens to destroy Crawley's family and brings into focus the novel's major themes: the dignity of poverty, the machinery of ecclesiastical justice, and the tension between personal honour and social reputation. Interwoven with the Crawley plot is the story of his daughter [[grace-crawley|Grace Crawley]] and her courtship by [[major-grantly|Major Henry Grantly]], a match opposed by the Grantly family on grounds of the Crawley scandal. A third strand follows the London solicitor [[toogood|Mr. Toogood]], who investigates the cheque's provenance and ultimately clears Crawley's name.

The novel brings together characters from across the Barsetshire series: [[lady-lufton|Lady Lufton]] of [[framley]], the formidable [[mrs-proudie|Mrs. Proudie]] of the bishop's palace, the Grantlys of Plumstead, and — in a celebrated late appearance — Mr. Harding, the former warden of Hiram's Hospital, whose deathbed scene is among the most moving passages in Victorian fiction.

## Context

*The Last Chronicle of Barset* is the culmination of a series that began with *The Warden* (1855) and continued through *Barchester Towers* (1857), *Doctor Thorne* (1858), *Framley Parsonage* (1861), and *The Small House at Allington* (1864). By 1867, Trollope had developed the fictional county of Barsetshire into one of the most fully realised imaginary worlds in English literature. This final novel was written at the height of Trollope's powers and draws together characters and themes from all five of its predecessors, providing a conclusion to the series that is at once narratively satisfying and morally serious.

The novel's treatment of clerical poverty drew on Trollope's knowledge of actual conditions in the Victorian church. The Ecclesiastical Commission, established in 1835, had begun the work of redistributing church revenues, but livings like Hogglestock remained scandalously inadequate. Crawley's plight was, for Trollope's first readers, a recognisable social reality as well as a fiction.

## Structure

The novel comprises 84 chapters, organised into what would have been 32 monthly instalments. Its narrative architecture is more complex than any previous Barsetshire novel, weaving three principal plot strands:

1. **The Crawley plot**: The accusation, trial, and exoneration of Josiah Crawley
2. **The marriage plot**: The courtship of Grace Crawley and Major Grantly
3. **The London plot**: Mr. Toogood's investigation and the novel's wider social canvas

A fourth strand — the story of Lily Dale and Johnny Eames, carried over from *The Small House at Allington* — provides a secondary romantic interest and a bridge to the earlier novels.
