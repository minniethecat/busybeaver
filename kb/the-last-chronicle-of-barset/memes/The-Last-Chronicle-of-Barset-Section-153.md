[153-001]"He puts me so much in mind of my father," the archdeacon said to his

[153-002]wife one day.

[153-003]"He is not so old as your father was when he died, by many years,"

[153-004]said Mrs. Grantly, "and I think one sees that difference."

[153-005]"Yes;--and therefore I say that he may still live for years. My

[153-006]father, when he took to his bed at last, was manifestly near his

[153-007]death. The wonder with him was that he continued to live so long.

[153-008]Do you not remember how the London doctor was put out because his

[153-009]prophecies were not fulfilled?"

[153-010]"I remember it well;--as if it were yesterday."

[153-011]"And in that way there is a great difference. My father, who was

[153-012]physically a much stronger man, did not succumb so easily. But the

[153-013]likeness is in their characters. There is the same mild sweetness,

[153-014]becoming milder and sweeter as they increased in age;--a sweetness

[153-015]that never could believe much evil, but that could believe less,

[153-016]and still less, as the weakness of age came on them. No amount of

[153-017]evidence would induce your father to think that Mr. Crawley stole

[153-018]that money." This was said of course before the telegram had come

[153-019]from Venice.

[153-020]"As far as that goes I agree with him," said Mrs. Grantly, who had

[153-021]her own reasons for choosing to believe Mr. Crawley to be innocent.

[153-022]"If your son, my dear, is to marry a man's daughter, it will be as

[153-023]well that you should at least be able to say that you do not believe

[153-024]that man to be a thief."

[153-025]"That is neither here nor there," said the archdeacon. "A jury must

[153-026]decide it."

[153-027]"No jury in Barsetshire shall decide it for me," said Mrs. Grantly.

[153-028]"I'm sick of Mr. Crawley, and I'm sorry I spoke of him," said the

[153-029]archdeacon. "But look at Mrs. Proudie. You'll agree that she was not

[153-030]the most charming woman in the world."

[153-031]"She certainly was not," said Mrs. Grantly, who was anxious to

[153-032]encourage her husband, if she could do so without admitting anything

[153-033]which might injure herself afterwards.

[153-034]"And she was at one time violently insolent to your father. And even

[153-035]the bishop thought to trample upon him. Do you remember the bishop's

[153-036]preaching against your father's chaunting? If I ever forget it!" And

[153-037]the archdeacon slapped his closed fist against his open hand.

[153-038]"Don't, dear; don't. What is the good of being violent now?"

[153-039]"Paltry little fool! It will be long enough before such a chaunt as

[153-040]that is heard in any English cathedral again." Then Mrs. Grantly got

[153-041]up and kissed her husband, but he, somewhat negligent of the kiss,

[153-042]went on with his speech. "But your father remembers nothing of it,

[153-043]and if there was a single human being who shed a tear in Barchester

[153-044]for that woman, I believe it was your father. And it was the same

[153-045]with mine. It came to that at last, that I could not bear to speak

[153-046]to him of any shortcoming as to one of his own clergymen. I might as

[153-047]well have pricked him with a penknife. And yet they say men become

[153-048]heartless and unfeeling as they grow old!"

[153-049]"Some do, I suppose."

[153-050]"Yes; the heartless and unfeeling do. As the bodily strength fails

[153-051]and the power of control becomes lessened, the natural aptitude of

[153-052]the man pronounces itself more clearly. I take it that that is it.

[153-053]Had Mrs. Proudie lived to be a hundred and fifty, she would have

[153-054]spoken spiteful lies on her deathbed." Then Mrs. Grantly told

[153-055]herself that her husband, should he live to be a hundred and fifty,

[153-056]would still be expressing his horror of Mrs. Proudie,--even on his

[153-057]deathbed.

[153-058]As soon as the letter from Mrs. Arabin had reached Plumstead, the

[153-059]archdeacon and his wife arranged that they would both go together to

[153-060]the deanery. There were the double tidings to be told,--those of Mr.

[153-061]Crawley's assured innocence, and those also of Mrs. Arabin's instant

[153-062]return. And as they went together various ideas were passing through

[153-063]their minds in reference to the marriage of their son with Grace

[153-064]Crawley. They were both now reconciled to it. Mrs. Grantly had long

[153-065]ceased to feel any opposition to it, even though she had not seen

[153-066]Grace; and the archdeacon was prepared to give way. Had he not

[153-067]promised that in a certain case he would give way, and had not that

[153-068]case now come to pass? He had no wish to go back from his word. But

[153-069]he had a difficulty in this,--that he liked to make all the affairs

[153-070]of his life matter for enjoyment, almost for triumph; but how was

[153-071]he to be triumphant over this marriage, or how even was he to enjoy

[153-072]it, seeing that he had opposed it so bitterly? Those posters, though

[153-073]they were now pulled down, had been up on all barn ends and walls,

[153-074]patent--alas, too patent--to all the world of Barsetshire! "What will

[153-075]Mr. Crawley do now, do you suppose?" said Mrs. Grantly.

[153-076]"What will he do?"

[153-077]"Yes; must he go on at Hogglestock?"

[153-078]"What else?" said the archdeacon.

[153-079]"It is a pity something could not be done for him after all he has

[153-080]undergone. How on earth can he be expected to live there with a

[153-081]wife and family, and no private means?" To this the archdeacon made

[153-082]no answer. Mrs. Grantly had spoken almost immediately upon their

[153-083]quitting Plumstead, and the silence was continued till the carriage

[153-084]had entered the suburbs of the city. Then Mrs. Grantly spoke again,

[153-085]asking a question, with some internal trepidation, which, however,

[153-086]she managed to hide from her husband. "When poor papa does go, what

[153-087]shall you do about St. Ewold's?" Now, St. Ewold's was a rural parish

[153-088]lying about two miles out of Barchester, the living of which was in

[153-089]the gift of the archdeacon, and to which the archdeacon had presented

[153-090]his father-in-law, under certain circumstances, which need not be

[153-091]repeated in this last chronicle of Barsetshire. Have they not been

[153-092]written in other chronicles? "When poor papa does go, what will you

[153-093]do about St. Ewold's?" said Mrs. Grantly, trembling inwardly. A word

[153-094]too much might, as she well knew, settle the question against Mr.

[153-095]Crawley for ever. But were she to postpone the word till too late,

[153-096]the question would be settled as fatally.

[153-097]"I haven't thought about it," he said sharply. "I don't like thinking

[153-098]of such things while the incumbent is still living." Oh, archdeacon,

[153-099]archdeacon! unless that other chronicle be a false chronicle, how

[153-100]hast thou forgotten thyself and thy past life! "Particularly not,

[153-101]when that incumbent is your father," said the archdeacon. Mrs.

[153-102]Grantly said nothing more about St. Ewold's. She would have said as

[153-103]much as she had intended to say if she had succeeded in making the

[153-104]archdeacon understand that St. Ewold's would be a very nice refuge

[153-105]for Mr. Crawley after all the miseries which he had endured at

[153-106]Hogglestock.

[153-107]They learned as they entered the deanery that Mrs. Baxter had already

[153-108]heard of Mrs. Arabin's return. "O yes, ma'am. Mr. Harding got a

[153-109]letter hisself, and I got another,--separate; both from Venice,

[153-110]ma'am. But when master is to come, nobody seems to know." Mrs. Baxter

[153-111]knew that the dean had gone to Jerusalem, and was inclined to think

[153-112]that from such distant bournes there was no return for any traveller.

[153-113]The east is always further than the west in the estimation of the

[153-114]Mrs. Baxters of the world. Had the dean gone to Canada, she would

[153-115]have thought that he might come back to-morrow. But still there was

[153-116]the news to be told of Mr. Crawley, and there was also joy to be

[153-117]expressed at the sudden coming back of the much-wished-for mistress

[153-118]of the deanery.

[153-119]"It's so good of you to come both together," said Mr. Harding.

[153-120]"We thought we should be too many for you," said the archdeacon.

[153-121]"Too many! O dear, no. I like to have people by me; and as for

[153-122]voices, and noise, and all that, the more the better. But I am weak.

[153-123]I'm weak in my legs. I don't think I shall ever stand again."

[153-124]"Yes, you will," said the archdeacon.

[153-125]"We have brought you good news," said Mrs. Grantly.

[153-126]"Is it not good news that Nelly will be home this week? You can't

[153-127]understand what a joy it is to me. I used to think sometimes, at

[153-128]night, that I should never see her again. That she would come back

[153-129]in time was all I have had to wish for." He was lying on his back,

[153-130]and as he spoke he pressed his withered hands together above the

[153-131]bedclothes. They could not begin immediately to tell him of Mr.

[153-132]Crawley, but as soon as his mind had turned itself away from the

[153-133]thoughts of his absent daughter, Mrs. Grantly again reverted to her

[153-134]news.

[153-135]"We have come to tell you about Mr. Crawley, papa."

[153-136]"What about him?"

[153-137]"He is quite innocent."

[153-138]"I knew it, my dear. I always said so. Did I not always say so,

[153-139]archdeacon?"

[153-140]"Indeed you did. I'll give you that credit."

[153-141]"And is it all found out?" asked Mr. Harding.

[153-142]"As far as he is concerned, everything is found out," said Mrs.

[153-143]Grantly. "Eleanor gave him the cheque herself."

[153-144]"Nelly gave it to him?"

[153-145]"Yes, papa. The dean meant her to give him fifty pounds. But it seems

[153-146]she got to be soft of heart and made it seventy. She had the cheque

[153-147]by her, and put it into the envelope with the notes."

[153-148]"Some of Stringer's people seem to have stolen the cheque from Mr.

[153-149]Soames," said the archdeacon.

[153-150]"O dear; I hope not."

[153-151]"Somebody must have stolen it, papa."

[153-152]"I had hoped not, Susan," said Mr. Harding. Both the archdeacon and

[153-153]Mrs. Grantly knew that it was useless to argue with him on such a

[153-154]point, and so they let that go.

[153-155]Then they came to discuss Mr. Crawley's present position, and Mr.

[153-156]Harding ventured to ask a question or two as to Grace's chance of

[153-157]marriage. He did not often interfere in the family arrangements of

[153-158]his son-in-law,--and never did so when those family arrangements

[153-159]were concerned with high matters. He had hardly opened his mouth

[153-160]in reference to the marriage of that August lady who was now the

[153-161]Marchioness of Hartletop. And of the Lady Anne, the wife of the Rev.

[153-162]Charles Grantly, who was always prodigiously civil to him, speaking

[153-163]to him very loud, as though he were deaf because he was old, and

[153-164]bringing him cheap presents from London of which he did not take much

[153-165]heed,--of her he rarely said a word, or of her children, to either

[153-166]of his daughters. But now his grandson, Henry Grantly, was going to

[153-167]marry a girl of whom he felt that he might speak without impropriety.

[153-168]"I suppose it will be a match; won't it, my dears?"

[153-169]"Not a doubt about it," said Mrs. Grantly. Mr. Harding looked at his

[153-170]son-in-law, but his son-in-law said nothing. The archdeacon did not

[153-171]even frown,--but only moved himself a little uneasily in his chair.

[153-172]"Dear, dear! What a comfort that must be," said the old man.

[153-173]"I have not seen her yet," said Mrs. Grantly; "but the archdeacon

[153-174]declares that she is all the graces rolled into one."

[153-175]"I never said anything half so absurd," replied the archdeacon.

[153-176]"But he really is quite in love with her, papa," said Mrs. Grantly.

[153-177]"He confessed to me that he gave her a kiss, and he only saw her once

[153-178]for five minutes."

[153-179]"I should like to give her a kiss," said Mr. Harding.

[153-180]"So you shall, papa, and I'll bring her here on purpose. As soon as

[153-181]ever the thing is settled, we mean to ask her to Plumstead."

[153-182]"Do you though? How nice! How happy Henry will be!"

[153-183]"And if she comes--and of course she will--I'll lose no time in

[153-184]bringing her over to you. Nelly must see her of course."
