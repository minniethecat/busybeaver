[041-001]"Curates indeed!" said the archdeacon. "It's too good for a bishop,

[041-002]unless one of the right sort."

[041-003]"Your father used to say those things, but with him the poorer the

[041-004]guest the better the cheer. When he had a few clergymen round him,

[041-005]how he loved to make them happy!"

[041-006]"Never talked shop to them,--did he?" said the archdeacon.

[041-007]"Not after dinner, at any rate. Goodness gracious, when one thinks of

[041-008]it! Do you remember how we used to play cards?"

[041-009]"Every night regularly;--threepenny points, and sixpence on the

[041-010]rubber," said the archdeacon.

[041-011]"Dear, dear! How things are changed! And I remember when the

[041-012]clergymen did more of the dancing in Barchester than all the other

[041-013]young men in the city put together."

[041-014]"And a good set they were;--gentlemen every one of them. It's well

[041-015]that some of them don't dance now;--that is, for the girls' sake."

[041-016]"I sometimes sit and wonder," said Mr. Harding, "whether your

[041-017]father's spirit ever comes back to the old house and sees the

[041-018]changes,--and if so whether he approves them."

[041-019]"Approves them!" said the archdeacon.

[041-020]"Well;--yes. I think he would, upon the whole. I'm sure of this: he

[041-021]would not disapprove, because the new ways are changed from his ways.

[041-022]He never thought himself infallible. And do you know, my dear, I am

[041-023]not sure that it isn't all for the best. I sometimes think that some

[041-024]of us were very idle when we were young. I was, I know."

[041-025]"I worked hard enough," said the archdeacon.

[041-026]"Ah, yes; you. But most of us took it very easily. Dear, dear! When

[041-027]I think of it, and see how hard they work now, and remember what

[041-028]pleasant times we used to have,--I don't feel sometimes quite sure."

[041-029]"I believe the work was done a great deal better than it is now,"

[041-030]said the archdeacon. "There wasn't so much fuss, but there was more

[041-031]reality. And men were men, and clergymen were gentlemen."

[041-032]"Yes;--they were gentlemen."

[041-033]"Such a creature as that old woman at the palace couldn't have held

[041-034]his head up among us. That's what has come from Reform. A reformed

[041-035]House of Commons makes Lord Brock Prime Minister, and then your Prime

[041-036]Minister makes Dr. Proudie a bishop! Well;--it will last my time, I

[041-037]suppose."

[041-038]"It has lasted mine,--like the wine," said Mr. Harding.

[041-039]"There's one glass more, and you shall have it, sir." Then Mr.

[041-040]Harding drank the last glass of the 1820 port, and they went into the

[041-041]drawing-room.

[041-042]On the next morning after breakfast the major went out for a walk

[041-043]by himself. His father had suggested to him that he should go over

[041-044]to shoot at Framley, and had offered him the use of everything

[041-045]the archdeaconry possessed in the way of horses, dogs, guns and

[041-046]carriages. But the major would have none of these things. He would go

[041-047]out and walk by himself. "He's not thinking of her; is he?" said the

[041-048]archdeacon to his wife, in a whisper. "I don't know. I think he is,"

[041-049]said Mrs. Grantly. "It will be so much the better for Charles, if

[041-050]he does," said the archdeacon grimly; and the look of his face as

[041-051]he spoke was by no means pleasant. "You will do nothing unjust,

[041-052]archdeacon," said his wife. "I will do as I like with my own," said

[041-053]he. And then he also went out and took a walk by himself.

[041-054]That evening after dinner, there was no 1820 port, and no

[041-055]recollections of old days. They were rather dull, the three of them,

[041-056]as they sat together,--and dulness is always more unendurable than

[041-057]sadness. Old Mr. Harding went to sleep and the archdeacon was cross.

[041-058]"Henry," he said, "you haven't a word to throw to a dog." "I've got

[041-059]rather a headache this evening, sir," said the major. The archdeacon

[041-060]drank two glasses of wine, one after another, quickly. Then he woke

[041-061]his father-in-law gently, and went off. "Is there anything the

[041-062]matter?" asked the old man. "Nothing particular. My father seems to

[041-063]be a little cross." "Ah! I've been to sleep and I oughtn't. It's my

[041-064]fault. We'll go in and smooth him down." But the archdeacon wouldn't

[041-065]be smoothed down on that occasion. He would let his son see the

[041-066]difference between a father pleased, and a father displeased,--or

[041-067]rather between a father pleasant, and a father unpleasant. "He hasn't

[041-068]said anything to you, has he?" said the archdeacon that night to his

[041-069]wife. "Not a word;--as yet." "If he does it without the courage to

[041-070]tell us, I shall think him a cur," said the archdeacon. "But he did

[041-071]tell you," said Mrs. Grantly, standing up for her favourite son;

[041-072]"and, for the matter of that, he has courage enough for anything. If

[041-073]he does it, I shall always say that he has been driven to it by your

[041-074]threats."

[041-075]"That's sheer nonsense," said the archdeacon.

[041-076]"It's not nonsense at all," said Mrs. Grantly.

[041-077]"Then I suppose I was to hold my tongue and say nothing?" said

[041-078]the archdeacon; and as he spoke he banged the door between his

[041-079]dressing-room and Mrs. Grantly's bedroom.

[041-080]On the first day of the new year Major Grantly spoke his mind to

[041-081]his mother. The archdeacon had gone into Barchester, having in vain

[041-082]attempted to induce his son to go with him. Mr. Harding was in the

[041-083]library reading a little and sleeping a little, and dreaming of

[041-084]old days and old friends, and perhaps, sometimes, of the old wine.

[041-085]Mrs. Grantly was alone in a small sitting-room which she frequented

[041-086]upstairs, when suddenly her son entered the room. "Mother," he said,

[041-087]"I think it better to tell you that I am going to Allington."

[041-088]"To Allington, Henry?" She knew very well who was at Allington, and

[041-089]what must be the business which would take him there.

[041-090]"Yes, mother. Miss Crawley is there, and there are circumstances

[041-091]which make it incumbent on me to see her without delay."

[041-092]"What circumstances, Henry?"

[041-093]"As I intend to ask her to be my wife, I think it best to do so now.

[041-094]I owe it to her and to myself that she should not think that I am

[041-095]deterred by her father's position."

[041-096]"But would it not be reasonable that you should be deterred by her

[041-097]father's position?"

[041-098]"No, I think not. I think it would be dishonest as well as

[041-099]ungenerous. I cannot bring myself to brook such delay. Of course I am

[041-100]alive to the misfortune which has fallen upon her,--upon her and me,

[041-101]too, should she ever become my wife. But it is one of those burdens

[041-102]which a man should have shoulders broad enough to bear."

[041-103]"Quite so, if she were your wife, or even if you were engaged to her.

[041-104]Then honour would require it of you, as well as affection. As it is,

[041-105]your honour does not require it, and I think you should hesitate, for

[041-106]all our sakes, and especially for Edith's."

[041-107]"It will do Edith no harm; and, mother, if you alone were concerned,

[041-108]I think you would feel that it would not hurt you."

[041-109]"I was not thinking of myself, Henry."

[041-110]"As for my father, the very threats which he has used make me

[041-111]conscious that I have only to measure the price. He has told me that

[041-112]he will stop my allowance."

[041-113]"But that may not be the worst. Think how you are situated. You are

[041-114]the younger son of a man who will be held to be justified in making

[041-115]an elder son, if he thinks fit to do so."

[041-116]"I can only hope that he will be fair to Edith. If you will tell him

[041-117]that from me, it is all that I will ask you to do."

[041-118]"But you will see him yourself?"

[041-119]"No, mother; not till I have been to Allington. Then I will see him

[041-120]again or not, just as he pleases. I shall stop at Guestwick, and

[041-121]will write to you a line from thence. If my father decides on doing

[041-122]anything, let me know at once, as it will be necessary that I should

[041-123]get rid of the lease of my house."

[041-124]"Oh, Henry!"

[041-125]"I have thought a great deal about it, mother, and I believe I am

[041-126]right. Whether I am right or wrong, I shall do it. I will not ask

[041-127]you now for any promise or pledge; but should Miss Crawley become my

[041-128]wife, I hope that you at least will not refuse to see her as your

[041-129]daughter." Having so spoken, he kissed his mother, and was about to

[041-130]leave the room; but she held him by his arm, and he saw that her

[041-131]eyes were full of tears. "Dearest mother, if I grieve you I am sorry

[041-132]indeed."

[041-133]"Not me, not me, not me," she said.

[041-134]"For my father, I cannot help it. Had he not threatened me I should

[041-135]have told him also. As he has done so, you must tell him. But give

[041-136]him my kindest love."

[041-137]"Oh, Henry; you will be ruined. You will, indeed. Can you not wait?

[041-138]Remember how headstrong your father is, and yet how good;--and how he

[041-139]loves you! Think of all that he has done for you. When did he refuse

[041-140]you anything?"

[041-141]"He has been good to me, but in this I cannot obey him. He should not

[041-142]ask me."

[041-143]"You are wrong. You are indeed. He has a right to expect that you

[041-144]will not bring disgrace upon the family."

[041-145]"Nor will I;--except such disgrace as may attend upon poverty.

[041-146]Good-by, mother. I wish you could have said one kind word to me."

[041-147]"Have I not said a kind word?"

[041-148]"Not as yet, mother."

[041-149]"I would not for worlds speak unkindly to you. If it were not for

[041-150]your father I would bid you bring whom you pleased home to me as your

[041-151]wife; and I would be as a mother to her. And if this girl should

[041-152]become your wife--"

[041-153]"It shall not be my fault if she does not."

[041-154]"I will try to love her--some day."

[041-155]Then the major went, leaving Edith at the rectory, as requested by

[041-156]his mother. His own dog-cart and his servant were at Plumstead, and

[041-157]he drove himself home to Cosby Lodge.

[041-158]When the archdeacon returned the news was told to him at once. "Henry

[041-159]has gone to Allington to propose to Miss Crawley," said Mrs. Grantly.

[041-160]"Gone,--without speaking to me!"

[041-161]"He left his love, and said that it was useless his remaining, as he

[041-162]knew he should only offend you."

[041-163]"He has made his bed, and he must lie upon it," said the archdeacon.

[041-164]And then there was not another word said about Grace Crawley on that

[041-165]occasion.

[041-166]CHAPTER XXIII.

[041-167]MISS LILY DALE'S RESOLUTION.

[041-168][Illustration]

[041-169]The ladies at the Small House at Allington breakfasted always at

[041-170]nine,--a liberal nine; and the postman whose duty it was to deliver

[041-171]letters in that village at half-past eight, being also liberal in

[041-172]his ideas as to time, always arrived punctually in the middle of

[041-173]breakfast, so that Mrs. Dale expected her letters, and Lily hers,

[041-174]just before their second cup of tea, as though the letters formed

[041-175]a part of the morning meal. Jane, the maid-servant, always brought

[041-176]them in, and handed them to Mrs. Dale,--for Lily had in these days

[041-177]come to preside at the breakfast-table; and then there would be an

[041-178]examination of the outsides before the envelopes were violated, and

[041-179]as each knew pretty well all the circumstances of the correspondence

[041-180]of the other, there would be some guessing as to what this or that

[041-181]epistle might contain; and after that a reading out loud of passages,

[041-182]and not unfrequently of the entire letter. But now, at the time

[041-183]of which I am speaking, Grace Crawley was at the Small House, and

[041-184]therefore the common practice was somewhat in abeyance.

[041-185]On one of the first days of the new year Jane brought in the letters

[041-186]as usual, and handed them to Mrs. Dale. Lily was at the time occupied

[041-187]with the teapot, but still she saw the letters, and had not her hands

[041-188]so full as to be debarred from the expression of her usual anxiety.

[041-189]"Mamma, I'm sure I see two there for me," she said. "Only one for

[041-190]you, Lily," said Mrs. Dale. Lily instantly knew from the tone of

[041-191]the voice that some letter had come, which by the very aspect of

[041-192]the handwriting had disturbed her mother. "There is one for you, my

[041-193]dear," said Mrs. Dale, throwing a letter across the table to Grace.

[041-194]"And one for you, Lily, from Bell. The others are for me." "And whom

[041-195]are yours from, mamma?" asked Lily. "One is from Mrs. Jones; the

[041-196]other, I think, is a letter on business." Then Lily said nothing

[041-197]further, but she observed that her mother only opened one of her

[041-198]letters at the breakfast-table. Lily was very patient;--not by

[041-199]nature, I think, but by exercise and practice. She had, once in

[041-200]her life, been too much in a hurry; and having then burned herself

[041-201]grievously, she now feared the fire. She did not therefore follow her

[041-202]mother after breakfast, but sat with Grace over the fire, hemming

[041-203]diligently at certain articles of clothing which were intended for

[041-204]use in the Hogglestock parsonage. The two girls were making a set of

[041-205]new shirts for Mr. Crawley. "But I know he will ask where they come

[041-206]from," said Grace; "and then mamma will be scolded." "But I hope

[041-207]he'll wear them," said Lily. "Sooner or later he will," said Grace;

[041-208]"because mamma manages generally to have her way at last." Then

[041-209]they went on for an hour or so, talking about the home affairs at

[041-210]Hogglestock. But during the whole time Lily's mind was intent upon

[041-211]her mother's letter.
