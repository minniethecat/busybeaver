[161-001]There he was certainly, tying his horse up to the railing. "Mamma,

[161-002]what am I to say to him?"

[161-003]"Nay, dear; he is your own friend,--of your own making. You must say

[161-004]what you think fit."

[161-005]"You are not going?"

[161-006]"I think we had better, dear." Then she went, and Jane with her, and

[161-007]Jane opened the door for Major Grantly. Mr. Crawley himself was away,

[161-008]at Hoggle End, and did not return till after Major Grantly had left

[161-009]the parsonage. Jane, as she greeted the grand gentleman, whom she

[161-010]had seen and no more than seen, hardly knew what to say to him.

[161-011]When, after a minute's hesitation, she told him that Grace was in

[161-012]there,--pointing to the sitting-room door, she felt that she had been

[161-013]very awkward. Henry Grantly, however, did not, I think, feel her

[161-014]awkwardness, being conscious of some small difficulties of his own.

[161-015]When, however, he found that Grace was alone, the task before him at

[161-016]once lost half its difficulties. "Grace," he said, "am I right to

[161-017]come to you now?"

[161-018]"I do not know," she said. "I cannot tell."

[161-019]"Dearest Grace, there is no reason on earth now why you should not be

[161-020]my wife."

[161-021]"Is there not?"

[161-022]"I know of none,--if you can love me. You saw my father?"

[161-023]"Yes, I saw him."

[161-024]"And you heard what he said?"

[161-025]"I hardly remember what he said;--but he kissed me, and I thought he

[161-026]was very kind."

[161-027]What little attempt Henry Grantly then made, thinking that he could

[161-028]not do better than follow closely the example of so excellent a

[161-029]father, need not be explained with minuteness. But I think that his

[161-030]first effort was not successful. Grace was embarrassed and retreated,

[161-031]and it was not till she had been compelled to give a direct answer

[161-032]to a direct question that she submitted to allow his arm round her

[161-033]waist. But when she had answered that question she was almost more

[161-034]humble than becomes a maiden who has just been wooed and won. A

[161-035]maiden who has been wooed and won, generally thinks that it is she

[161-036]who has conquered, and chooses to be triumphant accordingly. But

[161-037]Grace was even mean enough to thank her lover. "I do not know why you

[161-038]should be so good to me," she said.

[161-039]"Because I love you," said he, "better than all the world."

[161-040]"But why should you be so good to me as that? Why should you love me?

[161-041]I am such a poor thing for a man like you to love."

[161-042]"I have had the wit to see that you are not a poor thing, Grace;

[161-043]and it is thus that I have earned my treasure. Some girls are poor

[161-044]things, and some are rich treasures."

[161-045]"If love can make me a treasure, I will be your treasure. And if love

[161-046]can make me rich, I will be rich for you." After that I think he had

[161-047]no difficulty in following in his father's footsteps.

[161-048]After a while Mrs. Crawley came in, and there was much pleasant

[161-049]talking among them, while Henry Grantly sat happily with his love,

[161-050]as though waiting for Mr. Crawley's return. But though he was there

[161-051]nearly all the morning Mr. Crawley did not return. "I think he

[161-052]likes the brickmakers better than anybody in all the world, except

[161-053]ourselves," said Grace. "I don't know how he will manage to get on

[161-054]without his friends." Before Grace had said this, Major Grantly

[161-055]had told all his story, and had produced a letter from his father,

[161-056]addressed to Mr. Crawley, of which the reader shall have a copy,

[161-057]although at this time the letter had not been opened. The letter was

[161-058]as follows:--

[161-059]Plumstead Rectory, -- May, 186--.

[161-060]MY DEAR SIR,

[161-061]You will no doubt have heard that Mr. Harding, the vicar

[161-062]of St. Ewolds, who was the father of my wife and of Mrs.

[161-063]Arabin, has been taken from us. The loss to us of so

[161-064]excellent and so dear a man has been very great. I have

[161-065]conferred with my friend the Dean of Barchester as to a

[161-066]new nomination, and I venture to request your acceptance

[161-067]of the preferment, if it should suit you to move from

[161-068]Hogglestock to St. Ewolds. It may be as well that I should

[161-069]state plainly my reasons for making this offer to a

[161-070]gentleman with whom I am not personally acquainted. Mr.

[161-071]Harding, on his deathbed, himself suggested it, moved

[161-072]thereto by what he had heard of the cruel and undeserved

[161-073]persecution to which you have lately been subjected;

[161-074]as also,--on which point he was very urgent in what he

[161-075]said,--by the character which you bear in the diocese for

[161-076]zeal and piety. I may also add, that the close connection

[161-077]which, as I understand, is likely to take place between

[161-078]your family and mine has been an additional reason for

[161-079]my taking this step, and the long friendship which has

[161-080]existed between you and my wife's brother-in-law, the Dean

[161-081]of Barchester, is a third.

[161-082]St. Ewolds is worth £350 per annum, besides the house,

[161-083]which is sufficiently commodious for a moderate family.

[161-084]The population is about twelve hundred, of which more than

[161-085]a half consists of persons dwelling in an outskirt of the

[161-086]city,--for the parish runs almost into Barchester.

[161-087]I shall be glad to have your reply with as little delay

[161-088]as may suit your convenience, and in the event of your

[161-089]accepting the offer,--which I sincerely trust you may be

[161-090]enabled to do,--I shall hope to have an early opportunity

[161-091]of seeing you, with reference to your institution to the

[161-092]parish.

[161-093]Allow me also to say to you and to Mrs. Crawley that, if

[161-094]we have been correctly informed as to that other event

[161-095]to which I have alluded, we both hope that we may have

[161-096]an early opportunity of making ourselves personally

[161-097]acquainted with the parents of a young lady who is to

[161-098]be so dear to us. As I have met your daughter, I may

[161-099]perhaps be allowed to send her my kindest love. If, as my

[161-100]daughter-in-law, she comes up to the impression which she

[161-101]gave me at our first meeting, I, at any rate, shall be

[161-102]satisfied.

[161-103]I have the honour to be, my dear sir,

[161-104]Your most faithful servant,

[161-105]THEOPHILUS GRANTLY.

[161-106]This letter the archdeacon had shown to his wife, by whom it had not

[161-107]been very warmly approved. Nothing, Mrs. Grantly had said, could be

[161-108]prettier than what the archdeacon had said about Grace. Mrs. Crawley,

[161-109]no doubt, would be satisfied with that. But Mr. Crawley was such a

[161-110]strange man! "He will be stranger than I take him to be if he does

[161-111]not accept St. Ewolds," said the archdeacon. "But in offering it,"

[161-112]said Mrs. Grantly, "you have not said a word of your own high opinion

[161-113]of his merits." "I have not a very high opinion of them," said the

[161-114]archdeacon. "Your father had, and I have said so. And as I have the

[161-115]most profound respect for your father's opinion in such a matter, I

[161-116]have permitted that to overcome my own hesitation." This was pretty

[161-117]from the husband to the wife as it regarded her father, who had now

[161-118]gone from them; and, therefore, Mrs. Grantly accepted it without

[161-119]further argument. The reader may probably feel assured that the

[161-120]archdeacon had never, during their joint lives, acted in any church

[161-121]matter upon the advice given to him by Mr. Harding; and it was

[161-122]probably the case also that the living would have been offered to

[161-123]Mr. Crawley, if nothing had been said by Mr. Harding on the subject;

[161-124]but it did not become Mrs. Grantly even to think of all this. The

[161-125]archdeacon, having made his gracious speech about her father, was not

[161-126]again asked to alter his letter. "I suppose he will accept it," said

[161-127]Mrs. Grantly. "I should think that he probably may," said the

[161-128]archdeacon.

[161-129]So Grace, knowing what was the purport of the letter, sat with it

[161-130]between her fingers, while her lover sat beside her, full of various

[161-131]plans for the future. This was his first lover's present to her;--and

[161-132]what a present it was! Comfort, and happiness, and a pleasant home

[161-133]for all her family. "St. Ewolds isn't the best house in the world,"

[161-134]said the major, "because it is old, and what I call piecemeal; but

[161-135]it is very pretty, and certainly nice." "That is just the sort of

[161-136]parsonage that I dream about," said Jane. "And the garden is pleasant

[161-137]with old trees," said the major. "I always dream about old trees,"

[161-138]said Jane, "only I'm afraid I'm too old myself to be let to climb up

[161-139]them now." Mrs. Crawley said very little, but sat by with her eyes

[161-140]full of tears. Was it possible that, at last, before the world had

[161-141]closed upon her, she was to enjoy something again of the comforts

[161-142]which she had known in her early years, and to be again surrounded by

[161-143]those decencies of life which of late had been almost banished from

[161-144]her home by poverty!

[161-145]Their various plans for the future,--for the immediate future,--were

[161-146]very startling. Grace was to go over at once to Plumstead, whither

[161-147]Edith had been already transferred from Cosby Lodge. That was all

[161-148]very well; there was nothing very startling or impracticable in

[161-149]that. The Framley ladies, having none of those doubts as to what

[161-150]was coming which had for a while perplexed Grace herself, had taken

[161-151]little liberties with her wardrobe, which enabled such a visit to be

[161-152]made without overwhelming difficulties. But the major was equally

[161-153]eager,--or at any rate equally imperious,--in his requisition for

[161-154]a visit from Mr. and Mrs. Crawley themselves to Plumstead rectory.

[161-155]Mrs. Crawley did not dare to put forward the plain unadorned reasons

[161-156]against it, as Mr. Crawley had done when discussing the subject of

[161-157]a visit to the deanery. Nor could she quite venture to explain that

[161-158]she feared that the archdeacon and her husband would hardly mix well

[161-159]together in society. With whom, indeed, was it possible that her

[161-160]husband should mix well, after his long and hardly-tried seclusion?

[161-161]She could only plead that both her husband and herself were so little

[161-162]used to going out that she feared,--she feared,--she feared she

[161-163]knew not what. "We'll get over all that," said the major, almost

[161-164]contemptuously. "It is only the first plunge that is disagreeable."

[161-165]Perhaps the major did not know how very disagreeable a first plunge

[161-166]may be!

[161-167]At two o'clock Henry Grantly got up to go. "I should very much like

[161-168]to have seen him, but I fear I cannot wait longer. As it is, the

[161-169]patience of my horse has been surprising." Then Grace walked out with

[161-170]him to the gate, and put her hand upon his bridle as he mounted, and

[161-171]thought how wonderful was the power of Fortune, that the goddess

[161-172]should have sent so gallant a gentleman to be her lord and her lover.

[161-173]"I declare I don't quite believe it even yet," she said, in the

[161-174]letter which she wrote to Lily Dale that night.

[161-175]It was four before Mr. Crawley returned to his house, and then he was

[161-176]very weary. There were many sick in these days at Hoggle End, and he

[161-177]had gone from cottage to cottage through the day. Giles Hoggett was

[161-178]almost unable to work from rheumatism, but still was of opinion that

[161-179]doggedness might carry him on. "It's been a deal o' service to you,

[161-180]Muster Crawley," he said. "We hears about it all. If you hadn't a

[161-181]been dogged, where'd you a been now?" With Giles Hoggett and others

[161-182]he had remained all the day, and now he came home weary and beaten.

[161-183]"You'll tell him first," Grace had said, "and then I'll give him the

[161-184]letter." The wife was the first to tell him of the good fortune that

[161-185]was coming.

[161-186]He flung himself into the old chair as soon as he entered, and asked

[161-187]for some bread and tea. "Jane has already gone for it, dear," said

[161-188]his wife. "We have had a visitor here, Josiah."
