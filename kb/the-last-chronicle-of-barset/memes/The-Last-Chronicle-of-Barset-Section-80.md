[080-001]SEPTIMUS HARDING.

[080-002]After this he wrote another letter to his other daughter, Mrs.

[080-003]Grantly, telling her also of Mr. Toogood's visit; and then he spent

[080-004]the remainder of the day thinking over the gravity of the occurrence.

[080-005]How terrible would it be if a beneficed clergyman in the diocese

[080-006]should really be found guilty of theft by a jury from the city! And

[080-007]then he had always heard so high a character of this man from his

[080-008]son-in-law. No,--it was impossible to believe that Mr. Crawley had in

[080-009]truth stolen a cheque for twenty pounds!

[080-010]Mr. Toogood could get no other information in Barchester, and went on

[080-011]to Silverbridge early in the afternoon. He was half disposed to go

[080-012]by Hogglestock and look up his cousin, whom he had never seen, and

[080-013]his cousin's husband, upon whose business he was now intent; but on

[080-014]reflection he feared that he might do more harm than good. He had

[080-015]quite appreciated the fact that Mr. Crawley was not like other men.

[080-016]"The man's not above half-saved," he had said to his wife,--meaning

[080-017]thereby to insinuate that the poor clergyman was not in full

[080-018]possession of his wits. And, to tell the truth of Mr. Toogood, he

[080-019]was a little afraid of his relative. There was a something in Mr.

[080-020]Crawley's manner, in spite of his declared poverty, and in spite also

[080-021]of his extreme humility, which seemed to announce that he expected to

[080-022]be obeyed when he spoke on any point with authority. Mr. Toogood had

[080-023]not forgotten the tone in which Mr. Crawley had said to him, "Sir,

[080-024]this thing you cannot do." And he thought that, upon the whole, he

[080-025]had better not go to Hogglestock on this occasion.

[080-026]When at Silverbridge, he began at once to "rummage about." His chief

[080-027]rummaging was to be done at Mr. Walker's table; but before dinner he

[080-028]had time to call upon the magistrate's clerk, and ask a few questions

[080-029]as to the proceedings at the sitting from which Mr. Crawley was

[080-030]committed. He found a very taciturn old man, who was nearly as

[080-031]difficult to deal with in any rummaging process as a porcupine. But,

[080-032]nevertheless, at last he reached a state of conversation which was

[080-033]not absolutely hostile. Mr. Toogood pleaded that he was the poor

[080-034]man's cousin,--pleaded that, as the family lawyer, he was naturally

[080-035]the poor man's protector at such a time as the present,--pleaded also

[080-036]that as the poor man was so very poor, no one else could come forward

[080-037]on his behalf,--and in this way somewhat softened the hard sharpness

[080-038]of the old porcupine's quills. But after all this, there was very

[080-039]little to be learned from the old porcupine. "There was not a

[080-040]magistrate on the bench," he said, "who had any doubt that the

[080-041]evidence was sufficient to justify them in sending the case to the

[080-042]assizes. They had all regretted,"--the porcupine said in his softest

[080-043]moment,--"that the gentleman had come there without a legal adviser."

[080-044]"Ah, that's been the mischief of it all!" said Mr. Toogood, dashing

[080-045]his hand against the porcupine's mahogany table. "But the facts were

[080-046]so strong, Mr. Toogood!" "Nobody there to soften 'em down, you know,"

[080-047]said Mr. Toogood, shaking his head. Very little more than this was

[080-048]learned from the porcupine; and then Mr. Toogood went away, and

[080-049]prepared for Mr. Walker's dinner.

[080-050]Mr. Walker had invited Dr. Tempest and Miss Anne Prettyman and Major

[080-051]Grantly to meet Mr. Toogood, and had explained, in a manner intended

[080-052]to be half earnest and half jocose, that though Mr. Toogood was an

[080-053]attorney, like himself, and was at this moment engaged in a noble

[080-054]way on behalf of his cousin's husband, without any idea of receiving

[080-055]back even the money which he would be out of pocket; still he wasn't

[080-056]quite,--not quite, you know--"not quite so much of a gentleman as

[080-057]I am,"--Mr. Walker would have said, had he spoken out freely that

[080-058]which he insinuated. But he contented himself with the emphasis he

[080-059]put upon the "not quite," which expressed his meaning fully. And Mr.

[080-060]Walker was correct in his opinion of Mr. Toogood. As regards the two

[080-061]attorneys I will not venture to say that either of them was not a

[080-062]"perfect gentleman." A perfect gentleman is a thing which I cannot

[080-063]define. But undoubtedly Mr. Walker was a bigger man in his way than

[080-064]was Mr. Toogood in his, and did habitually consort in the county

[080-065]of Barsetshire with men of higher standing than those with whom Mr.

[080-066]Toogood associated in London.

[080-067]It seemed to be understood that Mr. Crawley was to be the general

[080-068]subject of conversation, and no one attempted to talk about anything

[080-069]else. Indeed, at this time, very little else was talked about in

[080-070]that part of the county;--not only because of the interest naturally

[080-071]attaching to the question of the suspected guilt of a parish

[080-072]clergyman, but because much had become lately known of Mr. Crawley's

[080-073]character, and because it was known also that an internecine feud

[080-074]had arisen between him and the bishop. It had undoubtedly become the

[080-075]general opinion that Mr. Crawley had picked up and used a cheque

[080-076]which was not his own;--that he had, in fact, stolen it; but there

[080-077]was, in spite of that belief, a general wish that he might be

[080-078]acquitted and left in his living. And when the tidings of Mr.

[080-079]Crawley's victory over the bishop at the palace had become bruited

[080-080]about, popular sympathy went with the victor. The theft was, as

[080-081]it were, condoned, and people made excuses which were not always

[080-082]rational, but which were founded on the instincts of true humanity.

[080-083]And now the tidings of another stage in the battle, as fought against

[080-084]Mr. Crawley by the bishop, had gone forth through the county, and men

[080-085]had heard that the rural dean was to be instructed to make inquiries

[080-086]which should be preliminary to proceedings against Mr. Crawley in

[080-087]an ecclesiastical court. Dr. Tempest, who was now about to meet Mr.

[080-088]Toogood at Mr. Walker's, was the rural dean to whom Mr. Crawley would

[080-089]have to submit himself in any such inquiry; but Dr. Tempest had not

[080-090]as yet received from the bishop any official order on the subject.

[080-091]"We are so delighted to think that you have taken up your cousin's

[080-092]case," said Mrs. Walker to Mr. Toogood, almost in a whisper.

[080-093]"He is not just my cousin, himself," said Mr. Toogood, "but of course

[080-094]it's all the same thing. And as to taking up his case, you see, my

[080-095]dear madam, he won't let me take it up."

[080-096]"I thought you had. I thought you were down here about it?"

[080-097]"Only on the sly, Mrs. Walker. He has such queer ideas that he will

[080-098]not allow a lawyer to be properly employed; and you can't conceive

[080-099]how hard that makes it. Do you know him, Mrs. Walker?"

[080-100]"We know his daughter Grace." And then Mrs. Walker whispered

[080-101]something further, which we may presume to have been an intimation

[080-102]that the gentleman opposite,--Major Grantly,--was supposed by some

[080-103]people to be very fond of Miss Grace Crawley.

[080-104]"Quite a child, isn't she?" said Toogood, whose own daughter, now

[080-105]about to be married, was three or four years older than Grace.

[080-106]"She's beyond being a child, I think. Of course she is young."

[080-107]"But I suppose this affair will knock all that on the head," said the

[080-108]lawyer.

[080-109]"I do not know how that may be; but they do say he is very much

[080-110]attached to her. The major is a man of family, and of course it would

[080-111]be very disagreeable if Mr. Crawley were found guilty."

[080-112]"Very disagreeable, indeed; but, upon my word, Mrs. Walker, I don't

[080-113]know what to say about it."

[080-114]"You think it will go against him, Mr. Toogood?" Mr. Toogood shook

[080-115]his head, and on seeing this, Mrs. Walker sighed deeply.

[080-116]"I can only say that I have heard nothing from the bishop as yet,"

[080-117]said Dr. Tempest, after the ladies had left the room. "Of course, if

[080-118]he thinks well to order it, the inquiry must be made."

[080-119]"But how long would it take?" asked Mr. Walker.

[080-120]"Three months, I should think,--or perhaps more. Of course Crawley

[080-121]would do all that he could to delay us, and I am not at all sure that

[080-122]we should be in any very great hurry ourselves."

[080-123]"Who are the 'we,' doctor?" said Mr. Walker.

[080-124]"I cannot make such an inquiry by myself, you know. I suppose the

[080-125]bishop would ask me to select two or four other clergymen to act with

[080-126]me. That's the usual way of doing it. But you may be quite sure of

[080-127]this, Walker; the assizes will be over, and the jury have found their

[080-128]verdict long before we have settled our preliminaries."

[080-129]"And what will be the good of your going on after that?"

[080-130]"Only this good:--if the unfortunate man be convicted--"

[080-131]"Which he won't," said Mr. Toogood, who thought it expedient to put

[080-132]on a bolder front in talking of the matter to the rural dean, than he

[080-133]had assumed in his whispered conversation with Mrs. Walker.

[080-134]"I hope not, with all my heart," said the doctor. "But, perhaps, for

[080-135]the sake of the argument, the supposition may be allowed to pass."

[080-136]"Certainly, sir," said Mr. Toogood. "For the sake of the argument, it

[080-137]may pass."

[080-138]"If he be convicted, then, I suppose, there will be an end of the

[080-139]question. He would be sentenced for not less, I should say, than

[080-140]twelve months; and after that--"

[080-141]"And would be as good a parson of Hogglestock when he came out of

[080-142]prison as when he went in," said Mr. Walker. "The conviction and

[080-143]judgment in a civil court would not touch his temporality."

[080-144]"Certainly not," said Mr. Toogood.

[080-145]"Of course not," said the doctor. "We all know that; and in the event

[080-146]of Mr. Crawley coming back to his parish it would be open to the

[080-147]bishop to raise the question as to his fitness for the duties."

[080-148]"Why shouldn't he be as fit as any one else?" said Mr. Toogood.

[080-149]"Simply because he would have been found to be a thief," said the

[080-150]doctor. "You must excuse me, Mr. Toogood, but it's only for the sake

[080-151]of the argument."

[080-152]"I don't see what that has to do with it," said Mr. Toogood. "He

[080-153]would have undergone his penalty."

[080-154]"It is preferable that a man who preaches from a pulpit should not

[080-155]have undergone such a penalty," said the doctor. "But in practice,

[080-156]under such circumstances,--which we none of us anticipate, Mr.

[080-157]Toogood,--the living should no doubt be vacated. Mr. Crawley would

[080-158]probably hardly wish to come back. The jury will do their work before

[080-159]we can do ours,--will do it on a much better base than any we can

[080-160]have; and, when they have done it, the thing ought to be finished. If

[080-161]the jury acquit him, the bishop cannot proceed any further. If he be

[080-162]found guilty I think that the resignation of the living must follow."

[080-163]"It is all spite, then, on the bishop's part?" said the major.

[080-164]"Not at all," said the doctor. "The poor man is weak; that is all. He

[080-165]is driven to persecute because he cannot escape persecution himself.

[080-166]But it may really be a question whether his present proceeding is not

[080-167]right. If I were bishop I should wait till the trial was over; that

[080-168]is all."

[080-169]From this and from much more that was said during the evening on the

[080-170]same subject Mr. Toogood gradually learned the position which Mr.

[080-171]Crawley and the question of Mr. Crawley's guilt really held in the

[080-172]county, and he returned to town resolved to go on with the case.

[080-173]"I'll have a barrister down express, and I'll defend him in his

[080-174]own teeth," he said to his wife. "There'll be a scene in court,

[080-175]I daresay, and the man will call upon his own counsel to hold his

[080-176]tongue and shut up his brief; and, as far as I can see, counsel

[080-177]in such a case would have no alternative. But there would come an

[080-178]explanation,--how Crawley was too honourable to employ a man whom

[080-179]he could not pay, and there would be a romance, and it would all go

[080-180]down with the jury. One wants sympathy in such a case as that--not

[080-181]evidence."
