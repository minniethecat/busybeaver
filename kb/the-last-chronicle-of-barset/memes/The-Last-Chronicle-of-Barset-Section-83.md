[083-001]What was he to do? This interruption had at least seemed to drive

[083-002]Lily from his mind, and to send his ideas back to the consideration

[083-003]of his pecuniary difficulties. He thought of his own bank, a West-End

[083-004]establishment at which he was personally known to many of the clerks,

[083-005]and where he had been heretofore treated with great consideration.

[083-006]But of late his balances had been very low, and more than once he had

[083-007]been reminded that he had overdrawn his account. He knew well that

[083-008]the distinguished firm of Bounce, Bounce, and Bounce would not cash a

[083-009]bill for him or lend him money without security. He did not even dare

[083-010]to ask them to do so.

[083-011]On a sudden he jumped into a cab, and was driven back to his office.

[083-012]A thought had come upon him. He would throw himself upon the kindness

[083-013]of a friend there. Hitherto he had contrived to hold his head so high

[083-014]above the clerks below him, so high before the Commissioners who were

[083-015]above him, that none there suspected him to be a man in difficulty.

[083-016]It not seldom happens that a man's character stands too high for his

[083-017]interest,--so high that it cannot be maintained, and so high that any

[083-018]fall will be dangerous. And so it was with Crosbie and his character

[083-019]at the General Committee Office. The man to whom he was now thinking

[083-020]of applying as his friend, was a certain Mr. Butterwell, who had been

[083-021]his predecessor in the secretary's chair, and who now filled the less

[083-022]onerous but more dignified position of a Commissioner. Mr. Crosbie

[083-023]had somewhat despised Mr. Butterwell, and had of late years not been

[083-024]averse to showing that he did so. He had snubbed Mr. Butterwell, and

[083-025]Mr. Butterwell, driven to his wits' ends, had tried a fall or two

[083-026]with him. In all these struggles Crosbie had had the best of it,

[083-027]and Butterwell had gone to the wall. Nevertheless, for the sake of

[083-028]official decency, and from certain wise remembrances of the sources

[083-029]of official comfort and official discomfort, Mr. Butterwell had

[083-030]always maintained a show of outward friendship with the secretary.

[083-031]They smiled and were gracious, called each other Butterwell

[083-032]and Crosbie, and abstained from all cat-and-dog absurdities.

[083-033]Nevertheless, it was the frequently expressed opinion of every clerk

[083-034]in the office that Mr. Butterwell hated Mr. Crosbie like poison. This

[083-035]was the man to whom Crosbie suddenly made up his mind that he would

[083-036]have recourse.

[083-037]As he was driven back to his office he resolved that he would make a

[083-038]plunge at once at the difficulty. He knew that Butterwell was fairly

[083-039]rich, and he knew also that he was good-natured,--with that sort of

[083-040]sleepy good-nature which is not active for philanthropic purposes,

[083-041]but which dislikes to incur the pain of refusing. And then Mr.

[083-042]Butterwell was nervous, and if the thing was managed well, he might

[083-043]be cheated out of an assent, before time had been given him in which

[083-044]to pluck up courage for refusing. But Crosbie doubted his own courage

[083-045]also,--fearing that if he gave himself time for hesitation he would

[083-046]hesitate, and that, hesitating, he would feel the terrible disgrace

[083-047]of the thing and not do it. So, without going to his own desk, or

[083-048]ridding himself of his hat, he went at once to Butterwell's room.

[083-049]When he opened the door, he found Mr. Butterwell alone, reading The

[083-050]Times. "Butterwell," said he, beginning to speak before he had even

[083-051]closed the door, "I have come to you in great distress. I wonder

[083-052]whether you can help me; I want you to lend me five hundred pounds?

[083-053]It must be for not less than three months."

[083-054]Mr. Butterwell dropped the paper from his hands, and stared at the

[083-055]secretary over his spectacles.

[083-056]CHAPTER XLIV.

[083-057]"I SUPPOSE I MUST LET YOU HAVE IT."

[083-058][Illustration]

[083-059]Crosbie had been preparing the exact words with which he assailed Mr.

[083-060]Butterwell for the last quarter of an hour, before they were uttered.

[083-061]There is always a difficulty in the choice, not only of the words

[083-062]with which money should be borrowed, but of the fashion after which

[083-063]they should be spoken. There is the slow deliberate manner, in using

[083-064]which the borrower attempts to carry the wished-for lender along with

[083-065]him by force of argument, and to prove that the desire to borrow

[083-066]shows no imprudence on his own part, and that a tendency to lend

[083-067]will show none on the part of the intended lender. It may be said

[083-068]that this mode fails oftener than any other. There is the piteous

[083-069]manner,--the plea for commiseration. "My dear fellow, unless you will

[083-070]see me through now, upon my word I shall be very badly off." And this

[083-071]manner may be divided again into two. There is the plea piteous with

[083-072]a lie, and the plea piteous with a truth. "You shall have it again

[083-073]in two months as sure as the sun rises." That is generally the plea

[083-074]piteous with a lie. Or it may be as follows: "It is only fair to

[083-075]say that I don't quite know when I can pay it back." This is the

[083-076]plea piteous with a truth, and upon the whole I think that this is

[083-077]generally the most successful mode of borrowing. And there is the

[083-078]assured demand,--which betokens a close intimacy. "Old fellow, can

[083-079]you let me have thirty pounds? No? Just put your name, then, on the

[083-080]back of this, and I'll get it done in the City." The worst of that

[083-081]manner is, that the bill so often does not get itself done in the

[083-082]City. Then there is the sudden attack,--that being the manner to

[083-083]which Crosbie had recourse in the present instance. That there are

[083-084]other modes of borrowing by means of which youth becomes indebted to

[083-085]age, and love to respect, and ignorance to experience, is a matter

[083-086]of course. It will be understood that I am here speaking only of

[083-087]borrowing and lending between the Butterwells and Crosbies of the

[083-088]world. "I have come to you in great distress," said Crosbie. "I

[083-089]wonder whether you can help me. I want you to lend me five hundred

[083-090]pounds." Mr. Butterwell, when he heard the words, dropped the paper

[083-091]which he was reading from his hand, and stared at Crosbie over his

[083-092]spectacles.

[083-093]"Five hundred pounds," he said. "Dear me, Crosbie; that's a large sum

[083-094]of money."

[083-095]"Yes, it is,--a very large sum. Half that is what I want at once; but

[083-096]I shall want the other half in a month."

[083-097]"I thought that you were always so much above the world in money

[083-098]matters. Gracious me;--nothing that I have heard for a long time has

[083-099]astonished me more. I don't know why, but I always thought that you

[083-100]had your things so very snug."

[083-101]Crosbie was aware that he had made one very great step towards

[083-102]success. The idea had been presented to Mr. Butterwell's mind, and

[083-103]had not been instantly rejected as a scandalously iniquitous idea, as

[083-104]an idea to which no reception could be given for a moment. Crosbie

[083-105]had not been treated as was the needy knife-grinder, and had ground

[083-106]to stand upon while he urged his request. "I have been so pressed

[083-107]since my marriage," he said, "that it has been impossible for me to

[083-108]keep things straight."

[083-109]"But Lady Alexandrina--"

[083-110]"Yes; of course; I know. I do not like to trouble you with my private

[083-111]affairs;--there is nothing, I think, so bad as washing one's dirty

[083-112]linen in public;--but the truth is, that I am only now free from the

[083-113]rapacity of the De Courcys. You would hardly believe me if I told you

[083-114]what I've had to pay. What do you think of two hundred and forty-five

[083-115]pounds for bringing her body over here, and burying it at De Courcy?"

[083-116]"I'd have left it where it was."

[083-117]"And so would I. You don't suppose I ordered it to be done. Poor dear

[083-118]thing. If it could do her any good, God knows I would not begrudge

[083-119]it. We had a bad time of it when we were together, but I would have

[083-120]spared nothing for her, alive or dead, that was reasonable. But to

[083-121]make me pay for bringing the body over here, when I never had a

[083-122]shilling with her! By George, it was too bad. And that oaf John De

[083-123]Courcy,--I had to pay his travelling bill too."

[083-124]"He didn't come to be buried;--did he?"

[083-125]"It's too disgusting to talk of, Butterwell; it is indeed. And when

[083-126]I asked for her money that was settled upon me,--it was only two

[083-127]thousand pounds,--they made me go to law, and it seems there was no

[083-128]two thousand pounds to settle. If I like, I can have another lawsuit

[083-129]with the sisters, when the mother is dead. Oh, Butterwell, I have

[083-130]made such a fool of myself. I have come to such shipwreck! Oh,

[083-131]Butterwell, if you could but know it all."

[083-132]"Are you free from the De Courcys now?"

[083-133]"I owe Gazebee, the man who married the other woman, over a thousand

[083-134]pounds. But I pay that off at two hundred a year, and he has a policy

[083-135]on my life."

[083-136]"What do you owe that for?"

[083-137]"Don't ask me. Not that I mind telling you;--furniture, and the lease

[083-138]of a house, and his bill for the marriage settlement,--d---- him."

[083-139]"God bless me. They seem to have been very hard upon you."

[083-140]"A man doesn't marry an earl's daughter for nothing, Butterwell. And

[083-141]then to think what I lost! It can't be helped now, you know. As a man

[083-142]makes his bed he must lie on it. I am sometimes so mad with myself

[083-143]when I think over it all,--that I should like to blow my brains out."

[083-144]"You must not talk in that way, Crosbie. I hate to hear a man talk

[083-145]like that."

[083-146]"I don't mean that I shall. I'm too much of a coward, I fancy." A

[083-147]man who desires to soften another man's heart, should always abuse

[083-148]himself. In softening a woman's heart, he should abuse her. "But life

[083-149]has been so bitter with me for the last three years! I haven't had an

[083-150]hour of comfort;--not an hour. I don't know why I should trouble you

[083-151]with all this, Butterwell. Oh,--about the money; yes; that's just

[083-152]how I stand. I owed Gazebee something over a thousand pounds, which

[083-153]is arranged as I have told you. Then there were debts, due by my

[083-154]wife,--at least some of them were, I suppose,--and that horrid,

[083-155]ghastly funeral,--and debts, I don't doubt, due by the cursed old

[083-156]countess. At any rate, to get myself clear I raised something over

[083-157]four hundred pounds, and now I owe five which must be paid, part

[083-158]to-morrow, and the remainder this day month."

[083-159]"And you've no security?"

[083-160]"Not a rag, not a shred, not a line, not an acre. There's my salary,

[083-161]and after paying Gazebee what comes due to him, I can manage to let

[083-162]you have the money within twelve months,--that is, if you can lend it

[083-163]me. I can just do that and live; and if you will assist me with the

[083-164]money, I will do so. That's what I've brought myself to by my own

[083-165]folly."

[083-166]"Five hundred pounds is such a large sum of money."

[083-167]"Indeed it is."

[083-168]"And without any security!"

[083-169]"I know, Butterwell, that I've no right to ask for it. I feel that.

[083-170]Of course I should pay you what interest you please."

[083-171]"Money's about seven now," said Butterwell.

[083-172]"I've not the slightest objection to seven per cent.," said Crosbie.

[083-173]"But that's on security," said Butterwell.

[083-174]"You can name your own terms," said Crosbie.

[083-175]Mr. Butterwell got out of his chair, and walked about the room with

[083-176]his hands in his pockets. He was thinking at that moment what Mrs.

[083-177]Butterwell would say to him. "Will an answer do to-morrow morning?"

[083-178]he said. "I would much rather have it to-day," said Crosbie. Then Mr.

[083-179]Butterwell took another turn about the room. "I suppose I must let

[083-180]you have it," he said.
