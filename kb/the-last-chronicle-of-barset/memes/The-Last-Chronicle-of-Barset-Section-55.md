[055-001]Grace was seated on a sofa, and Major Grantly got up from his chair,

[055-002]and came and stood opposite to her. "Grace," he said, "I hope you are

[055-003]not angry with me for coming down to see you here."

[055-004]"No, I am not angry," she said.

[055-005]"I have thought a great deal about it, and your friend, Miss

[055-006]Prettyman, knew that I was coming. She quite approves of my coming."

[055-007]"She has written to me, but did not tell me of it," said Grace, not

[055-008]knowing what other answer to make.

[055-009]"No,--she could not have done that. She had no authority. I only

[055-010]mention her name because it will have weight with you, and because

[055-011]I have not done that which, under other circumstances, perhaps, I

[055-012]should have been bound to do. I have not seen your father."

[055-013]"Poor papa," said Grace.

[055-014]"I have felt that at the present moment I could not do so with any

[055-015]success. It has not come of any want of respect either for him or

[055-016]for you. Of course, Grace, you know why I am here?" He paused, and

[055-017]then remembering that he had no right to expect an answer to such a

[055-018]question, he continued, "I have come here, dearest Grace, to ask you

[055-019]to be my wife, and to be a mother to Edith. I know that you love

[055-020]Edith."

[055-021]"I do indeed."

[055-022]"And I have hoped sometimes,--though I suppose I ought not to say

[055-023]so,--but I have hoped and almost thought sometimes, that you have

[055-024]been willing to--to love me, too. It is better to tell the truth

[055-025]simply, is it not?"

[055-026]"I suppose so," said Grace.

[055-027]"And therefore, and because I love you dearly myself, I have come to

[055-028]ask you to be my wife." Saying which he opened out his hand, and held

[055-029]it to her. But she did not take it. "There is my hand, Grace. If your

[055-030]heart is as I would have it you can give me yours, and I shall want

[055-031]nothing else to make me happy." But still she made no motion towards

[055-032]granting him his request. "If I have been too sudden," he said, "you

[055-033]must forgive me for that. I have been sudden and abrupt, but as

[055-034]things are, no other way has been open to me. Can you not bring

[055-035]yourself to give me some answer, Grace?" His hand had now fallen

[055-036]again to his side, but he was still standing before her.

[055-037]She had said no word to him as yet, except that one in which she had

[055-038]acknowledged her love for his child, and had expressed no surprise,

[055-039]even in her countenance, at his proposal. And yet the idea that he

[055-040]should do such a thing, since the idea that he certainly would do it

[055-041]had become clear to her, had filled her with a world of surprise. No

[055-042]girl ever lived with any beauty belonging to her who had a smaller

[055-043]knowledge of her own possession than Grace Crawley. Nor had she the

[055-044]slightest pride in her own acquirements. That she had been taught in

[055-045]many things more than had been taught to other girls, had come of her

[055-046]poverty and of the desolation of her home. She had learned to read

[055-047]Greek and Italian because there had been nothing else for her to do

[055-048]in that sad house. And, subsequently, accuracy of knowledge had been

[055-049]necessary for the earning of her bread. I think that Grace had at

[055-050]times been weak enough to envy the idleness and almost to envy the

[055-051]ignorance of other girls. Her figure was light, perfect in symmetry,

[055-052]full of grace at all points; but she had thought nothing of her

[055-053]figure, remembering only the poverty of her dress, but remembering

[055-054]also with a brave resolution that she would never be ashamed of it.

[055-055]And as her acquaintance with Major Grantly had begun and had grown,

[055-056]and as she had learned to feel unconsciously that his company was

[055-057]pleasanter to her than that of any other person she knew, she had

[055-058]still told herself that anything like love must be out of the

[055-059]question. But then words had been spoken, and there had been glances

[055-060]in his eye, and a tone in his voice, and a touch upon his fingers,

[055-061]of which she could not altogether refuse to accept the meaning. And

[055-062]others had spoken to her of it, the two Miss Prettymans and her

[055-063]friend Lily. Yet she would not admit to herself that it could be so,

[055-064]and she would not allow herself to confess to herself that she loved

[055-065]him. Then had come the last killing misery to which her father had

[055-066]been subjected. He had been accused of stealing money, and had been

[055-067]committed to be tried for the theft. From that moment, at any rate,

[055-068]any hope, if there had been a hope, must be crushed. But she swore

[055-069]to herself bravely that there had been no such hope. And she assured

[055-070]herself also that nothing had passed which had entitled her to

[055-071]expect anything beyond ordinary friendship from the man of whom she

[055-072]certainly had thought much. Even if those touches and those tones

[055-073]and those glances had meant anything, all such meaning must be

[055-074]annihilated by this disgrace which had come upon her. She might know

[055-075]that her father was innocent; she might be sure, at any rate, that

[055-076]he had been innocent in intention; but the world thought differently,

[055-077]and she, her brother and sister, and her mother and her poor father,

[055-078]must bend to the world's opinion. If those dangerous joys had meant

[055-079]anything, they must be taken as meaning nothing more.

[055-080]Thus she had argued with herself, and, fortified by such

[055-081]self-teachings, she had come down to Allington. Since she had been

[055-082]with her friends there had come upon her from day to day a clear

[055-083]conviction that her arguments had been undoubtedly true,--a clear

[055-084]conviction which had been very cold to her heart in spite of all her

[055-085]courage. She had expected nothing, hoped for nothing, and yet when

[055-086]nothing came she was sad. She thought of one special half-hour in

[055-087]which he had said almost all that he might have said,--more than he

[055-088]ought to have said;--of a moment during which her hand had remained

[055-089]in his; of a certain pressure with which he had put her shawl upon

[055-090]her shoulders. If he had only written to her one word to tell her

[055-091]that he believed her father was innocent! But no; she had no right to

[055-092]expect anything from him. And then Lily had ceased to talk of him,

[055-093]and she did expect nothing. Now he was there before her, asking her

[055-094]to come to him and be his wife. Yes; she would kiss his shoebuckles,

[055-095]only that the kissing of his shoebuckles would bring upon him that

[055-096]injury which he should never suffer from her hands! He had been

[055-097]generous, and her self-pride was satisfied. But her other pride was

[055-098]touched, and she also would be generous. "Can you not bring yourself

[055-099]to give me some answer?" he had said to her. Of course she must give

[055-100]him an answer, but how should she give it?

[055-101]"You are very kind," she said.

[055-102]"I would be more than kind."

[055-103]"So you are. Kind is a cold word when used to such a friend at such a

[055-104]time."

[055-105]"I would be everything on earth to you that a man can be to a woman."

[055-106]"I know I ought to thank you if I knew how. My heart is full of

[055-107]thanks; it is, indeed."

[055-108]"And is there no room for love there?"

[055-109]"There is no room for love in our house, Major Grantly. You have not

[055-110]seen papa."

[055-111]"No; but, if you wish it, I will do so at once."

[055-112]"It would do no good,--none. I only asked you because you can hardly

[055-113]know how sad is our state at home."

[055-114]"But I cannot see that that need deter you, if you can love me."

[055-115]"Can you not? If you saw him, and the house, and my mother, you would

[055-116]not say so. In the Bible it is said of some season that it is not a

[055-117]time for marrying, or for giving in marriage. And so it is with us."

[055-118]"I am not pressing you as to a day. I only ask you to say that you

[055-119]will be engaged to me,--so that I may tell my own people, and let it

[055-120]be known."

[055-121]"I understand all that. I know how good you are. But, Major Grantly,

[055-122]you must understand me also when I assure you that it cannot be so."

[055-123]"Do you mean that you refuse me altogether?"

[055-124]"Yes; altogether."

[055-125]"And why?"

[055-126]"Must I answer that question? Ought I to be made to answer it? But I

[055-127]will tell you fairly, without touching on anything else, that I feel

[055-128]that we are all disgraced, and that I will not take disgrace into

[055-129]another family."

[055-130]"Grace, do you love me?"

[055-131]"I love no one now,--that is, as you mean. I can love no one. I have

[055-132]no room for any feeling except for my father and mother, and for us

[055-133]all. I should not be here now but that I save my mother the bread

[055-134]that I should eat at home."

[055-135]"Is it as bad as that?"

[055-136]"Yes, it is as bad as that. It is much worse than that, if you knew

[055-137]it all. You cannot conceive how low we have fallen. And now they tell

[055-138]me that my father will be found guilty, and will be sent to prison.

[055-139]Putting ourselves out of the question, what would you think of a girl

[055-140]who could engage herself to any man under such circumstances? What

[055-141]would you think of a girl who would allow herself to be in love in

[055-142]such a position? Had I been ten times engaged to you I would have

[055-143]broken it off." Then she got up to leave him.

[055-144]But he stopped her, holding her by the arm. "What you have said will

[055-145]make me say what I certainly should never have said without it. I

[055-146]declare that we are engaged."

[055-147]"No, we are not," said Grace.

[055-148]"You have told me that you loved me."

[055-149]"I never told you so."

[055-150]"There are other ways of speaking than the voice; and I will boast to

[055-151]you, though to no one else, that you have told me so. I believe you

[055-152]love me. I shall hold myself as engaged to you, and I shall think

[055-153]you false if I hear that you listen to another man. Now, good-by,

[055-154]Grace;--my own Grace."

[055-155]"No, I am not your own," she said, through her tears.

[055-156]"You are my own, my very own. God bless you, dear, dear, dearest

[055-157]Grace. You shall hear from me in a day or two, and shall see me as

[055-158]soon as this horrid trial is over." Then he took her in his arms

[055-159]before she could escape from him, and kissed her forehead and her

[055-160]lips, while she struggled in his arms. After that he left the room

[055-161]and the house as quickly as he could, and was seen no more of the

[055-162]Dales upon that occasion.

[055-163]CHAPTER XXXI.

[055-164]SHOWING HOW MAJOR GRANTLY RETURNED TO GUESTWICK.

[055-165]Grace, when she was left alone, threw herself upon the sofa, and hid

[055-166]her face in her hands. She was weeping almost hysterically, and had

[055-167]been utterly dismayed and frightened by her lover's impetuosity.

[055-168]Things had gone after a fashion which her imagination had not painted

[055-169]to her as possible. Surely she had the power to refuse the man if she

[055-170]pleased. And yet she felt as she lay there weeping that she did in

[055-171]truth belong to him as part of his goods, and that her generosity had

[055-172]been foiled. She had especially resolved that she would not confess

[055-173]to any love for him. She had made no such confession. She had guarded

[055-174]herself against doing so with all the care which she knew how to

[055-175]use. But he had assumed the fact, and she had been unable to deny

[055-176]it. Could she have lied to him, and have sworn that she did not love

[055-177]him? Could she have so perjured herself, even in support of her

[055-178]generosity? Yes, she would have done so,--so she told herself,--if a

[055-179]moment had been given to her for thought. She ought to have done so,

[055-180]and she blamed herself for being so little prepared for the occasion.

[055-181]The lie would be useless now. Indeed, she would have no opportunity

[055-182]for telling it; for of course she would not answer,--would not even

[055-183]read his letter. Though he might know that she loved him, yet she

[055-184]would not be his wife. He had forced her secret from her, but he

[055-185]could not force her to marry him. She did love him, but he should

[055-186]never be disgraced by her love.
