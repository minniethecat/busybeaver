[086-001]"He is," said Lily, looking at her lover.

[086-002]"I'm going to have a trip for my own fun," said Johnny, "and I shall

[086-003]pick up evidence on the road, as I'm going;--that's all."

[086-004]Then Lily began to take an active part in the conversation, and a

[086-005]great deal was said about Mr. Crawley, and about Grace, and Lily

[086-006]declared that she would be very anxious to hear any news which John

[086-007]Eames might be able to send. "You know, John, how fond we are of your

[086-008]cousin Grace, at Allington? Are we not, uncle?"

[086-009]"Yes, indeed," said the squire. "I thought her a very nice girl."

[086-010]"If you should be able to learn anything that may be of use, John,

[086-011]how happy you will be."

[086-012]"Yes, I shall," said Johnny.

[086-013]"And I think it so good of you to go, John. But it is just like you.

[086-014]You were always generous." Soon after that he got up and went. It

[086-015]was very clear to him that he would have no moment in which to say

[086-016]a word alone to Lily; and if he could find such a moment, what good

[086-017]would such a word do him? It was as yet but a few weeks since she had

[086-018]positively refused him. And he too remembered very well those two

[086-019]words which she had told him that she would write in her book. As

[086-020]he had been coming to the house he had told himself that his coming

[086-021]would be,--could be of no use. And yet he was disappointed with the

[086-022]result of his visit, although she had spoken to him so sweetly.

[086-023]"I suppose you'll be gone when I come back?" he said.

[086-024]"We shall be here a month," said the squire.

[086-025]"I shall be back long before that, I hope," said Johnny. "Good-by,

[086-026]sir. Good-by, Dale. Good-by, Lily." And he put out his hand to her.

[086-027]"Good-by, John." And then she added, almost in a whisper, "I think

[086-028]you are very, very right to go." How could he fail after that to hope

[086-029]as he walked home that she might still relent. And she also thought

[086-030]much of him, but her thoughts of him made her cling more firmly than

[086-031]ever to the two words. She could not bring herself to marry him; but,

[086-032]at least, she would not break his heart by becoming the wife of any

[086-033]one else. Soon after this Bernard Dale went also. I am not sure that

[086-034]he had been well pleased at seeing John Eames become suddenly the

[086-035]hero of the hour. When a young man is going to perform so important

[086-036]an act as that of marriage, he is apt to think that he ought to be

[086-037]the hero of the hour himself--at any rate among his own family.

[086-038]Early on the next morning Lily was taken by her uncle to call upon

[086-039]Mrs. Thorne, and to see Emily Dunstable. Bernard was to meet them

[086-040]there, but it had been arranged that they should reach the house

[086-041]first. "There is nothing so absurd as these introductions," Bernard

[086-042]had said. "You go and look at her, and when you've had time to look

[086-043]at her, then I'll come!" So the squire and Lily went off to look at

[086-044]Emily Dunstable.

[086-045]"You don't mean to say that she lives in that house?" said Lily, when

[086-046]the cab was stopped before an enormous mansion in one of the most

[086-047]fashionable of the London squares.

[086-048]"I believe she does," said the squire.

[086-049]"I never shall be able to speak to anybody living in such a house as

[086-050]that," said Lily. "A duke couldn't have anything grander."

[086-051]"Mrs. Thorne is richer than half the dukes," said the squire. Then

[086-052]the door was opened by a porter, and Lily found herself within the

[086-053]hall. Everything was very great, and very magnificent, and, as she

[086-054]thought, very uncomfortable. Presently she heard a loud jovial voice

[086-055]on the stairs. "Mr. Dale, I'm delighted to see you. And this is your

[086-056]niece Lily. Come up, my dear. There is a young woman upstairs, dying

[086-057]to embrace you. Never mind the umbrella. Put it down anywhere. I want

[086-058]to have a look at you, because Bernard swears that you're so pretty."

[086-059]This was Mrs. Thorne, once Miss Dunstable, the richest woman in

[086-060]England, and the aunt of Bernard's bride. The reader may perhaps

[086-061]remember the advice which she once gave to Major Grantly, and her

[086-062]enthusiasm on that occasion. "There she is, Mr. Dale; what do you

[086-063]think of her?" said Mrs. Thorne, as she opened the door of a small

[086-064]sitting-room wedged in between two large saloons, in which Emily

[086-065]Dunstable was sitting.

[086-066]"Aunt Martha, how can you be so ridiculous?" said the young lady.

[086-067]"I suppose it is ridiculous to ask the question to which one really

[086-068]wants to have an answer," said Mrs. Thorne. "But Mr. Dale has, in

[086-069]truth, come to inspect you, and to form an opinion; and, in honest

[086-070]truth, I shall be very anxious to know what he thinks,--though, of

[086-071]course, he won't tell me."

[086-072]The old man took the girl in his arms, and kissed her on both cheeks.

[086-073]"I have no doubt you'll find out what I think," he said, "though I

[086-074]should never tell you."

[086-075]"I generally do find out what people think," she said. "And so you're

[086-076]Lily Dale?"

[086-077]"Yes, I'm Lily Dale."

[086-078]"I have so often heard of you, particularly of late; for you must

[086-079]know that a certain Major Grantly is a friend of mine. We must take

[086-080]care that that affair comes off all right, must we not?"

[086-081]"I hope it will." Then Lily turned to Emily Dunstable, and, taking

[086-082]her hand, went up and sat beside her, while Mrs. Thorne and the

[086-083]squire talked of the coming marriage. "How long have you been

[086-084]engaged?" said Lily.

[086-085]"Really engaged, about three weeks. I think it is not more than three

[086-086]weeks ago."

[086-087]"How very discreet Bernard has been. He never told us a word about it

[086-088]while it was going on."

[086-089]"Men never do tell, I suppose," said Emily Dunstable.

[086-090]"Of course you love him very dearly?" said Lily, not knowing what

[086-091]else to say.

[086-092]"Of course I do."

[086-093]"So do we. You know he's almost a brother to us; that is, to me and

[086-094]my sister. We never had a brother of our own." And so the morning was

[086-095]passed till Lily was told by her uncle to come away, and was told

[086-096]also by Mrs. Thorne that she was to dine with them in the square on

[086-097]that day. "You must not be surprised that my husband is not here,"

[086-098]she said. "He is a very odd sort of man, and he never comes to London

[086-099]if he can help it."

[086-100]CHAPTER XLVI.

[086-101]THE BAYSWATER ROMANCE.

[086-102]Eames had by no means done his work for that evening when he left Mr.

[086-103]Dale and Lily at their lodgings. He had other business on hand to

[086-104]which he had promised to give attention, and another person to see

[086-105]who would welcome his coming quite as warmly, though by no means as

[086-106]pleasantly, as Lily Dale. It was then just nine o'clock, and as he

[086-107]had told Miss Demolines,--Madalina we may as well call her now,--that

[086-108]he would be in Porchester Terrace by nine at the latest, it was

[086-109]incumbent on him to make haste. He got into a cab, and bid the cabman

[086-110]drive hard, and lighting a cigar, began to inquire of himself whether

[086-111]it was well for him to hurry away from the presence of Lily Dale

[086-112]to that of Madalina Demolines. He felt that he was half-ashamed of

[086-113]what he was doing. Though he declared to himself over and over again

[086-114]that he never had said a word, and never intended to say a word, to

[086-115]Madalina, which all the world might not hear, yet he knew that he was

[086-116]doing amiss. He was doing amiss, and half repented it, and yet he

[086-117]was half proud of it. He was most anxious to be able to give himself

[086-118]credit for his constancy to Lily Dale; to be able to feel that he

[086-119]was steadfast in his passion; and yet he liked the idea of amusing

[086-120]himself with his Bayswater romance, as he would call it, and was not

[086-121]without something of conceit as he thought of the progress he had

[086-122]made in it. "Love is one thing and amusement is another," he said to

[086-123]himself as he puffed the cigar-smoke out of his mouth; and in his

[086-124]heart he was proud of his own capacity for enjoyment. He thought it

[086-125]a fine thing, although at the same moment he knew it to be an evil

[086-126]thing--this hurrying away from the young lady whom he really loved

[086-127]to another as to whom he thought it very likely that he should be

[086-128]called upon to pretend to love her. And he sang a little song as he

[086-129]went, "If she be not fair for me, what care I how fair she be." That

[086-130]was intended to apply to Lily, and was used as an excuse for his

[086-131]fickleness in going to Miss Demolines. And he was, perhaps, too, a

[086-132]little conceited as to his mission to the Continent. Lily had told

[086-133]him that she was very glad that he was going; that she thought him

[086-134]very right to go. The words had been pleasant to his ears, and Lily

[086-135]had never looked prettier in his eyes than when she had spoken them.

[086-136]Johnny, therefore, was rather proud of himself as he sat in the cab

[086-137]smoking his cigar. He had, moreover, beaten his old enemy Sir Raffle

[086-138]Buffle in another contest, and he felt that the world was smiling on

[086-139]him;--that the world was smiling on him in spite of his cruel fate in

[086-140]the matter of his real lovesuit.

[086-141]There was a mystery about the Bayswater romance which was not without

[086-142]its allurement, and a portion of the mystery was connected with

[086-143]Madalina's mother. Lady Demolines was very rarely seen, and John

[086-144]Eames could not quite understand what was the manner of life of that

[086-145]unfortunate lady. Her daughter usually spoke of her with affectionate

[086-146]regret as being unable to appear on that particular occasion on

[086-147]account of some passing malady. She was suffering from a nervous

[086-148]headache, or was afflicted with bronchitis, or had been touched with

[086-149]rheumatism, so that she was seldom on the scene when Johnny was

[086-150]passing his time at Porchester Terrace. And yet he heard of her

[086-151]dining out, and going to plays and operas; and when he did chance

[086-152]to see her, he found that she was a sprightly old woman enough. I

[086-153]will not venture to say that he much regretted the absence of Lady

[086-154]Demolines, or that he was keenly alive to the impropriety of being

[086-155]left alone with the gentle Madalina; but the customary absence of

[086-156]the elder lady was an incident in the romance which did not fail to

[086-157]strike him.

[086-158]Madalina was alone when he was shown up into the drawing-room on the

[086-159]evening of which we are speaking.

[086-160]"Mr. Eames," she said, "will you kindly look at that watch which is

[086-161]lying on the table." She looked full at him with her great eyes wide

[086-162]open, and the tone of her voice was intended to show him that she was

[086-163]aggrieved.

[086-164]"Yes, I see it," said John, looking down on Miss Demolines' little

[086-165]gold Geneva watch, with which he had already made sufficient

[086-166]acquaintance to know that it was worth nothing. "Shall I give it

[086-167]you?"

[086-168]"No, Mr. Eames; let it remain there, that it may remind me, if it

[086-169]does not remind you, by how long a time you have broken your word."

[086-170]"Upon my word I couldn't help it;--upon my honour I couldn't."

[086-171]"Upon your honour, Mr. Eames!"

[086-172]"I was obliged to go and see a friend who has just come to town from

[086-173]my part of the country."
