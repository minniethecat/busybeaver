## Overview

Barchester is the cathedral city at the heart of the Barsetshire Chronicles. It serves as the seat of the bishopric and the centre of ecclesiastical authority for the county. The cathedral close, the bishop's palace, and the residences of the dean and canons form the social and political nexus around which much of the series' drama revolves. In *The Last Chronicle of Barset*, Barchester is the setting for key events including Crawley's summons before the ecclesiastical authorities and the Arabins' return from the Holy Land (001-006, 001-045).

## Significance

Barchester is more than a setting — it is a symbol of the Established Church itself. The grandeur of its cathedral contrasts sharply with the poverty of rural parishes like Hogglestock, embodying the institutional hierarchies that Trollope examines throughout the series. The city's social world — its garden parties, episcopal receptions, and clerical gatherings — provides the stage on which questions of preferment, reputation, and clerical duty are debated and decided. The return of the Arabins to Barchester in the novel's closing chapters signals the restoration of moral order after the turmoil of Crawley's trial (001-045).
