[076-001]"It would be a heavy expense, Mr. Toogood."

[076-002]"Yes," said Toogood, mournfully, thinking of the twelve children; "it

[076-003]would be a heavy expense. But I never like to stick at a thing when

[076-004]it ought to be done. I think I shall send a fellow after them."

[076-005]"I'll go," said Johnny.

[076-006]"How can you go?"

[076-007]"I'll make old Snuffle give me leave."

[076-008]"But will that lessen the expense?" said Mr. Walker.

[076-009]"Well, yes, I think it will," said John, modestly.

[076-010]"My nephew is a rich man, Mr. Walker," said Toogood.

[076-011]"That alters the case," said Mr. Walker. And thus, before they left

[076-012]the dining-room, it was settled that John Eames should be taught

[076-013]his lesson and should seek both Mrs. Arabin and Dr. Arabin on their

[076-014]travels.

[076-015]CHAPTER XLI.

[076-016]GRACE CRAWLEY AT HOME.

[076-017]On the morning after his return from London Mr. Crawley showed

[076-018]symptoms of great fatigue, and his wife implored him to remain in

[076-019]bed. But this he would not do. He would get up, and go out down to

[076-020]the brickfields. He had specially bound himself,--he said, to see

[076-021]that the duties of the parish did not suffer by being left in his

[076-022]hands. The bishop had endeavoured to place them in other hands, but

[076-023]he had persisted in retaining them. As he had done so he could allow

[076-024]no weariness of his own to interfere,--and especially no weariness

[076-025]induced by labours undertaken on his own behalf. The day in the week

[076-026]had come round on which it was his wont to visit the brickmakers, and

[076-027]he would visit them. So he dragged himself out of his bed and went

[076-028]forth amidst the cold storm of a harsh wet March morning. His wife

[076-029]well knew when she heard his first word on that morning that one of

[076-030]those terrible moods had come upon him which made her doubt whether

[076-031]she ought to allow him to go anywhere alone. Latterly there had been

[076-032]some improvement in his mental health. Since the day of his encounter

[076-033]with the bishop and Mrs. Proudie, though he had been as stubborn

[076-034]as ever, he had been less apparently unhappy, less depressed in

[076-035]spirits. And the journey to London had done him good. His wife had

[076-036]congratulated herself on finding him able to set about his work like

[076-037]another man, and he himself had experienced a renewal, if not of

[076-038]hope, at any rate, of courage, which had given him a comfort which he

[076-039]had recognized. His common-sense had not been very striking in his

[076-040]interview with Mr. Toogood, but yet he had talked more rationally

[076-041]then and had given a better account of the matter in hand than could

[076-042]have been expected from him for some weeks previously. But now that

[076-043]the labour was over, a reaction had come upon him, and he went away

[076-044]from his house having hardly spoken a word to his wife after the

[076-045]speech which he made about his duty to his parish.

[076-046]I think that at this time nobody saw clearly the working of his

[076-047]mind,--not even his wife, who studied it very closely, who gave him

[076-048]credit for all his high qualities, and who had gradually learned to

[076-049]acknowledge to herself that she must distrust his judgment in many

[076-050]things. She knew that he was good and yet weak, that he was afflicted

[076-051]by false pride and supported by true pride, that his intellect was

[076-052]still very bright, yet so dismally obscured on many sides as almost

[076-053]to justify people in saying that he was mad. She knew that he was

[076-054]almost a saint, and yet almost a castaway through vanity and hatred

[076-055]of those above him. But she did not know that he knew all this of

[076-056]himself also. She did not comprehend that he should be hourly telling

[076-057]himself that people were calling him mad and were so calling him with

[076-058]truth. It did not occur to her that he could see her insight into

[076-059]him. She doubted as to the way in which he had got the cheque,--never

[076-060]imagining, however, that he had wilfully stolen it;--thinking that

[076-061]his mind had been so much astray as to admit of his finding it and

[076-062]using it without wilful guilt,--thinking also, alas, that a man who

[076-063]could so act was hardly fit for such duties as those which were

[076-064]entrusted to him. But she did not dream that this was precisely his

[076-065]own idea of his own state and of his own position;--that he was

[076-066]always inquiring of himself whether he was not mad; whether, if mad,

[076-067]he was not bound to lay down his office; that he was ever taxing

[076-068]himself with improper hostility to the bishop,--never forgetting

[076-069]for a moment his wrath against the bishop and the bishop's wife,

[076-070]still comforting himself with his triumph over the bishop and the

[076-071]bishop's wife,--but, for all that, accusing himself of a heavy sin

[076-072]and proposing to himself to go to the palace and there humbly to

[076-073]relinquish his clerical authority. Such a course of action he was

[076-074]proposing to himself, but not with any realized idea that he would

[076-075]so act. He was as a man who walks along a river's bank thinking of

[076-076]suicide, calculating how best he might kill himself,--whether the

[076-077]river does not offer an opportunity too good to be neglected, telling

[076-078]himself that for many reasons he had better do so, suggesting to

[076-079]himself that the water is pleasant and cool, and that his ears would

[076-080]soon be deaf to the harsh noises of the world,--but yet knowing,

[076-081]or thinking that he knows, that he never will kill himself. So it

[076-082]was with Mr. Crawley. Though his imagination pictured to himself

[076-083]the whole scene,--how he would humble himself to the ground as he

[076-084]acknowledged his unfitness, how he would endure the small-voiced

[076-085]triumph of the little bishop, how, from the abjectness of his own

[076-086]humility, even from the ground on which he would be crouching, he

[076-087]would rebuke the loud-mouthed triumph of the bishop's wife; though

[076-088]there was no touch wanting to the picture which he thus drew,--he did

[076-089]not really propose to himself to commit this professional suicide.

[076-090]His wife, too, had considered whether it might be in truth becoming

[076-091]that he should give up his clerical duties, at any rate for a while;

[076-092]but she had never thought that the idea was present to his mind also.

[076-093]Mr. Toogood had told him that people would say that he was mad; and

[076-094]Mr. Toogood had looked at him, when he declared for the second time

[076-095]that he had no knowledge whence the cheque had come to him, as though

[076-096]his words were to be regarded as the words of some sick child. "Mad!"

[076-097]he said to himself, as he walked home from the station that night.

[076-098]"Well; yes; and what if I am mad? When I think of all that I have

[076-099]endured my wonder is that I should not have been mad sooner." And

[076-100]then he prayed,--yes, prayed, that in his madness the Devil might

[076-101]not be too strong for him, and that he might be preserved from some

[076-102]terrible sin of murder or violence. What, if the idea should come to

[076-103]him in his madness that it would be well for him to slay his wife and

[076-104]his children? Only that was wanting to make him of all men the most

[076-105]unfortunate.

[076-106]He went down among the brickmakers on the following morning, leaving

[076-107]the house almost without a morsel of food, and he remained at Hoggle

[076-108]End for the greater part of the day. There were sick persons there

[076-109]with whom he prayed, and then he sat talking with rough men while

[076-110]they ate their dinners, and he read passages from the Bible to women

[076-111]while they washed their husbands' clothes. And for a while he sat

[076-112]with a little girl in his lap teaching the child her alphabet. If it

[076-113]were possible for him he would do his duty. He would spare himself

[076-114]in nothing, though he might suffer even to fainting. And on this

[076-115]occasion he did suffer,--almost to fainting, for as he returned home

[076-116]in the afternoon he was forced to lean from time to time against the

[076-117]banks on the road-side, while the cold sweat of weakness trickled

[076-118]down his face, in order that he might recover strength to go on a few

[076-119]yards. But he would persevere. If God would but leave to him mind

[076-120]enough for his work, he would go on. No personal suffering should

[076-121]deter him. He told himself that there had been men in the world whose

[076-122]sufferings were sharper even than his own. Of what sort had been the

[076-123]life of the man who had stood for years on the top of a pillar? But

[076-124]then the man on the pillar had been honoured by all around him. And

[076-125]thus, though he had thought of the man on the pillar to encourage

[076-126]himself by remembering how lamentable had been that man's suffering,

[076-127]he came to reflect that after all his own sufferings were perhaps

[076-128]keener than those of the man on the pillar.

[076-129]When he reached home, he was very ill. There was no doubt about it

[076-130]then. He staggered to his arm-chair, and stared at his wife first,

[076-131]then smiled at her with a ghastly smile. He trembled all over, and

[076-132]when food was brought to him he could not eat it. Early on the next

[076-133]morning the doctor was by his bedside, and before that evening came

[076-134]he was delirious. He had been at intervals in this state for nearly

[076-135]two days, when Mrs. Crawley wrote to Grace, and though she had

[076-136]restrained herself from telling everything, she had written with

[076-137]sufficient strength to bring Grace at once to her father's bedside.

[076-138]He was not so ill when Grace arrived but that he knew her, and he

[076-139]seemed to receive some comfort from her coming. Before she had been

[076-140]in the house an hour she was reading Greek to him, and there was

[076-141]no wandering in his mind as to the due emphasis to be given to the

[076-142]plaints of the injured heroines, or as to the proper meaning of the

[076-143]choruses. And as he lay with his head half buried in the pillows, he

[076-144]shouted out long passages, lines from tragic plays by the score, and

[076-145]for a while seemed to have all the enjoyment of a dear old pleasure

[076-146]placed newly within his reach. But he tired of this after a while,

[076-147]and then, having looked round to see that his wife was not in the

[076-148]room, he began to talk of himself.

[076-149]"So you have been at Allington, my dear?"

[076-150]"Yes, papa."

[076-151]"Is it a pretty place?"

[076-152]"Yes, papa;--very pretty."

[076-153]"And they were good to you?"

[076-154]"Yes, papa;--very good."

[076-155]"Had they heard anything there about--me; of this trial that is to

[076-156]come on?"

[076-157]"Yes, papa; they had heard of it."

[076-158]"And what did they say? You need not think that you will shock me

[076-159]by telling me. They cannot say worse there than people have said

[076-160]here,--or think worse."

[076-161]"They don't think at all badly of you at Allington, papa."

[076-162]"But they must think badly of me if the magistrates were right?"

[076-163]"They suppose that there has been a mistake;--as we all think."

[076-164]"They do not try men at the assizes for mistakes."

[076-165]"That you have been mistaken, I mean;--and the magistrates mistaken."

[076-166]"Both cannot have been mistaken, Grace."

[076-167]"I don't know how to explain myself, papa; but we all know that it is

[076-168]very sad, and are quite sure that you have never meant for one moment

[076-169]to do anything that was wrong."

[076-170]"But people when they are,--you know what I mean, Grace; when they

[076-171]are not themselves,--do things that are wrong without meaning it."

[076-172]Then he paused, while she remained standing by him with her hand on

[076-173]the back of his. She was looking at his face, which had been turned

[076-174]towards her while they were reading together, but which now was so

[076-175]far moved that she knew that his eyes could not be fixed upon hers.

[076-176]"Of course if the bishop orders it, it shall be so," he said. "It is

[076-177]quite enough for me that he is the bishop."
