## Character

Conway Dalrymple is a fashionable young painter who moves in the artistic and social circles of London. He is a friend of John Eames and has achieved a measure of success in portraiture, having painted Mrs. Dobbs Broughton "as a Grace" — a picture that becomes the source of much trouble (044-070). Trollope presents him as a man of talent but uncertain moral compass, whose involvement with the married Mrs. Dobbs Broughton and his simultaneous pursuit of the heiress Clara Van Siever create a tangle of romantic and professional complications. "Conway Dalrymple had been the poorer man" before his success, and his rise in the world has not simplified his personal affairs (044-047).

## Biography

Dalrymple's artistic career has brought him into the orbit of wealthy London patrons. "Conway Dalrymple, whom the rich English" world has taken up, has become a sought-after portraitist in the last year or two (044-040). His most consequential commission is the portrait of Mrs. Dobbs Broughton, which requires long hours sitting in her drawing-room — an arrangement that her husband views with increasing suspicion. "Think of Conway Dalrymple passing so many hours in Maria's" company, Dobbs Broughton fumes (048-084). Meanwhile, Dalrymple becomes genuinely interested in Clara Van Siever, a painter herself, and must disentangle himself from Mrs. Dobbs Broughton's possessive attentions. The subplot culminates with Dalrymple's studio becoming the scene of confrontations over art, money, and fidelity.

## Significance

The Conway Dalrymple subplot provides *The Last Chronicle of Barset* with its principal comic relief from the gravity of the Crawley trial. Through Dalrymple's misadventures, Trollope satirizes the pretensions of the London art world and the romantic confusions of fashionable society. Dalrymple himself is neither hero nor villain — he is a talented man whose vanity and passivity lead him into situations he only half-controls. His eventual pairing with Clara Van Siever suggests that even in a novel dominated by the Crawley family's suffering, Trollope reserves space for a lighter resolution. The subplot also mirrors the novel's central concern with reputation and scandal: just as Josiah Crawley's name is blackened by the cheque accusation, Dalrymple's name risks being "blackened" by the appearance of impropriety with a married woman.
