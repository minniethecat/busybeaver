## Character

Mrs. Crawley is the wife of Josiah Crawley, the impoverished perpetual curate of Hogglestock, and mother of Grace Crawley. Before her marriage, she was of a higher social standing — "she had been the daughter of a clergyman, and had been brought up in comfort" — and Trollope notes that had her husband's career followed its early promise, "he might have been a bishop, and Mrs. Crawley, when she married him, had thought that she was to be the wife of a bishop" (002-062). Instead, the Crawleys have sunk into grinding poverty, and Mrs. Crawley bears the weight of household management with a quiet fortitude that contrasts sharply with her husband's proud, rigid character.

## Biography

Mrs. Crawley's life is defined by the long decline of her husband's clerical fortunes. "That had been Mrs. Crawley's luck in life," Trollope writes, to be the wife of a man whose brilliance and integrity have won him neither preferment nor income (002-066). In the novel's opening, she is already worn down by years of privation, yet she remains the practical anchor of the Crawley household. It is Mrs. Crawley who takes the fateful twenty-pound cheque from Lord Lufton to Mr. Crawley, who gave it to the butcher, "on the next day by Mrs. Crawley" (003-011). She walks from Hogglestock to Silverbridge to consult Mr. Walker, the lawyer, about her husband's impending trial (006-105). Throughout the crisis, she acts as intermediary between her husband's inflexible pride and the practical demands of survival, a role that exhausts her but which she never abandons.

## Significance

Mrs. Crawley represents the domestic cost of clerical poverty in Trollope's Barsetshire. Where her husband embodies the spiritual and intellectual tragedy of overlooked merit, Mrs. Crawley embodies its physical and emotional toll — the walked miles, the insufficient meals, the quiet humiliations of genteel poverty. Her loyalty to Mr. Crawley is never in question, even when his pride makes their situation worse. "Mrs. Crawley had many troubles during the" novel (007-148), yet she endures without complaint, making her one of Trollope's most sympathetic portraits of wifely devotion under extreme duress. She also serves as a foil to Mrs. Proudie: where the bishop's wife wields power aggressively, Mrs. Crawley exercises influence through quiet endurance.
