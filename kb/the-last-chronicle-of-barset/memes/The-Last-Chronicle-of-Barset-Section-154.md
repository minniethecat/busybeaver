[154-001]As they were leaving the room Mr. Harding called the archdeacon back,

[154-002]and taking him by the hand, spoke one word to him in a whisper. "I

[154-003]don't like to interfere," he said; "but might not Mr. Crawley have

[154-004]St. Ewold's?" The archdeacon took up the old man's hand and kissed

[154-005]it. Then he followed his wife out of the room, without making any

[154-006]answer to Mr. Harding's question.

[154-007]Three days after this Mrs. Arabin reached the deanery, and the joy at

[154-008]her return was very great. "My dear, I have been sick for you," said

[154-009]Mr. Harding.

[154-010]"Oh, papa, I ought not to have gone."

[154-011]"Nay, my dear; do not say that. Would it make me happy that you

[154-012]should be a prisoner here for ever? It was only when I seemed to get

[154-013]so weak that I thought about it. I felt that it must be near when

[154-014]they bade me not to go to the cathedral any more."

[154-015]"If I had been here, I could have gone with you, papa."

[154-016]"It is better as it is. I know now that I was not fit for it. When

[154-017]your sister came to me, I never thought of remonstrating. I knew then

[154-018]that I had seen it for the last time."

[154-019]"We need not say that yet, papa."

[154-020]"I did think that when you came home we might crawl there together

[154-021]some warm morning. I did think of that for a time. But it will never

[154-022]be so, dear. I shall never see anything now that I do not see from

[154-023]here,--and not that for long. Do not cry, Nelly. I have nothing to

[154-024]regret, nothing to make me unhappy. I know how poor and weak has been

[154-025]my life; but I know how rich and strong is that other life. Do not

[154-026]cry, Nelly,--not till I am gone; and then not beyond measure. Why

[154-027]should any one weep for those who go away full of years,--and full of

[154-028]hope?"

[154-029]On the day but one following the dean also reached his home. The

[154-030]final arrangements of his tour, as well as those of his wife, had

[154-031]been made to depend on Mr. Crawley's trial; for he also had been

[154-032]hurried back by John Eames's visit to Florence. "I should have come

[154-033]at once," he said to his wife, "when they wrote to ask me whether

[154-034]Crawley had taken the cheque from me, had anybody then told me that

[154-035]he was in actual trouble; but I had no idea then that they were

[154-036]charging him with theft."

[154-037]"As far as I can learn, they never really suspected him until after

[154-038]your answer had come. They had been quite sure that your answer would

[154-039]be in the affirmative."

[154-040]"What he must have endured it is impossible to conceive. I shall go

[154-041]out to him to-morrow."

[154-042]"Would he not come to us?" said Mrs. Arabin.

[154-043]"I doubt it. I will ask him, of course. I will ask them all here.

[154-044]This about Henry and the girl may make a difference. He has resigned

[154-045]the living, and some of the palace people are doing the duty."

[154-046]"But he can have it again?"

[154-047]"Oh, yes; he can have it again. For the matter of that, I need simply

[154-048]give him back his letter. Only he is so odd,--so unlike other people!

[154-049]And he has tried to live there, and has failed; and is now in debt. I

[154-050]wonder whether Grantly would give him St. Ewold's?"

[154-051]"I wish he would. But you must ask him. I should not dare."

[154-052]As to the matter of the cheque, the dean acknowledged to his wife at

[154-053]last that he had some recollection of her having told him that she

[154-054]had made the sum of money up to seventy pounds. "I don't feel certain

[154-055]of it now; but I think you may have done so." "I am quite sure I

[154-056]could not have done it without telling you," she replied. "At any

[154-057]rate you said nothing of the cheque," pleaded the dean. "I don't

[154-058]suppose I did," said Mrs. Arabin. "I thought that cheques were like

[154-059]any other money; but I shall know better for the future."

[154-060]On the following morning the dean rode over to Hogglestock, and as he

[154-061]drew near to the house of his old friend, his spirits flagged,--for

[154-062]to tell the truth, he dreaded the meeting. Since the day on which he

[154-063]had brought Mr. Crawley from a curacy in Cornwall into the diocese of

[154-064]Barchester, his friend had been a trouble to him rather than a joy.

[154-065]The trouble had been a trouble of spirit altogether,--not at all of

[154-066]pocket. He would willingly have picked the Crawleys out from the

[154-067]pecuniary mud into which they were ever falling, time after time,

[154-068]had it been possible. For, though the dean was hardly to be called a

[154-069]rich man, his lines had fallen to him not only in pleasant places,

[154-070]but in easy circumstances;--and Mr. Crawley's embarrassments, though

[154-071]overwhelming to him, were not so great as to have been heavy to

[154-072]the dean. But in striving to do this he had always failed, had

[154-073]always suffered, and had generally been rebuked. Crawley would

[154-074]attempt to argue with him as to the improper allotment of Church

[154-075]endowments,--declaring that he did not do so with any reference

[154-076]to his own circumstances, but simply because the subject was one

[154-077]naturally interesting to clergymen. And this he would do, as he was

[154-078]waving off with his hand offers of immediate assistance which were

[154-079]indispensable. Then there had been scenes between the dean and Mrs.

[154-080]Crawley,--terribly painful,--and which had taken place in direct

[154-081]disobedience to the husband's positive injunctions. "Sir," he had

[154-082]once said to the dean, "I request that nothing may pass from your

[154-083]hands to the hands of my wife." "Tush, tush," the dean had answered.

[154-084]"I will have no tushing or pshawing on such a matter. A man's wife is

[154-085]his very own, the breath of his nostril, the blood of his heart, the

[154-086]rib from his body. It is for me to rule my wife, and I tell you that

[154-087]I will not have it." After that the gifts had come from the hands of

[154-088]Mrs. Arabin;--and then again, after that, in the direst hour of his

[154-089]need, Crawley had himself come and taken money from the dean's hands!

[154-090]The interview had been so painful that Arabin would hardly have been

[154-091]able to count the money or to know of what it had consisted, had he

[154-092]taken the notes and cheque out of the envelope in which his wife had

[154-093]put them. Since that day the two had not met each other, and since

[154-094]that day these new troubles had come. Arabin as yet knew but little

[154-095]of the manner in which they had been borne, except that Crawley had

[154-096]felt himself compelled to resign the living of Hogglestock. He knew

[154-097]nothing of Mrs. Proudie's persecution, except what he gathered from

[154-098]the fact of the clerical commission of which he had been informed;

[154-099]but he could imagine that Mrs. Proudie would not lie easy on her

[154-100]bed while a clergyman was doing duty almost under her nose, who was

[154-101]guilty of the double offence of being accused of a theft, and of

[154-102]having been put into his living by the dean. The dean, therefore,

[154-103]as he rode on, pictured to himself his old friend in a terrible

[154-104]condition. And it might be that even now that condition would hardly

[154-105]have been improved. He was no longer suspected of being a thief; but

[154-106]he could have no money in his pocket; and it might well be that his

[154-107]sufferings would have made him almost mad.

[154-108]The dean also got down and left his horse at a farm-yard,--as Grantly

[154-109]had done with his carriage; and walked on first to the school. He

[154-110]heard voices inside, but could not distinguish from them whether Mr.

[154-111]Crawley was there or not. Slowly he opened the door, and looking

[154-112]round saw that Jane Crawley was in the ascendant. Jane did not know

[154-113]him at once, but told him when he had introduced himself that her

[154-114]father had gone down to Hoggle End. He had started two hours ago, but

[154-115]it was impossible to say when he might be back. "He sometimes stays

[154-116]all day long with the brickmakers," said Jane. Her mother was at

[154-117]home, and she would take the dean into the house. As she said this

[154-118]she told him that her father was sometimes better and sometimes

[154-119]worse. "But he has never been so very, very bad, since Henry Grantly

[154-120]and mamma's cousin came and told us about the cheque." That word

[154-121]Henry Grantly made the dean understand that there might yet be a ray

[154-122]of sunshine among the Crawleys.

[154-123]"There is papa," said Jane, as they got to the gate. Then they waited

[154-124]for a few minutes till Mr. Crawley came up, very hot, wiping the

[154-125]sweat from his forehead.

[154-126]"Crawley," said the dean, "I cannot tell you how glad I am to see

[154-127]you, and how rejoiced I am that this accusation has fallen off from

[154-128]you."

[154-129]"Verily the news came in time, Arabin," said the other; "but it was a

[154-130]narrow pinch--a narrow pinch. Will you not enter, and see my wife?"

[154-134]At this time Grace had returned home from Framley. As long as the

[154-135]terrible tragedy of the forthcoming trial was dragging itself on she

[154-136]had been content to stay away, at her mother's bidding. It has not

[154-137]been possible in these pages to tell of all the advice that had been

[154-138]given to the ladies of the Crawley family in their great difficulty,

[154-139]and of all the assistance that had been offered. The elder Lady

[154-140]Lufton and the younger, and Mrs. Robarts had continually been in

[154-141]consultation on the subject; Mrs. Grantly's opinion had been asked

[154-142]and given; and even the Miss Prettymans and Mrs. Walker had found

[154-143]means of expressing themselves. The communications to Mrs. Crawley

[154-144]had been very frequent,--though they had not of course been allowed

[154-145]to reach the ears of Mr. Crawley. What was to be done when the living

[154-146]should be gone and Mr. Crawley should be in prison? Some said that he

[154-147]might be there for six weeks, and some for two years. Old Lady Lufton

[154-148]made anxious inquiries about Judge Medlicote, before whom it was

[154-149]said that the trial would be taken. Judge Medlicote was a Dissenter,

[154-150]and old Lady Lufton was in despair. When she was assured by

[154-151]some liberally-disposed friend that this would certainly make no

[154-152]difference, she shook her head woefully. "I don't know why we are

[154-153]to have Dissenters at all," she said, "to try people who belong to

[154-154]the Established Church." When she heard that Judge Medlicote would

[154-155]certainly be the judge, she made up her mind that two years would be

[154-156]the least of it. She would not have minded it, she said, if he had

[154-157]been a Roman Catholic. And whether the punishment might be for six

[154-158]weeks or for two years, what should be done with the family? Where

[154-159]should they be housed? how should they be fed? What should be done

[154-160]with the poor man when he came out of prison? It was a case in which

[154-161]the generous, soft-hearted old Lady Lufton was almost beside herself.

[154-162]"As for Grace," said young Lady Lufton, "it will be a great deal

[154-163]better that we should keep her amongst us. Of course she will become

[154-164]Mrs. Grantly, and it will be nicer for her that it should be so." In

[154-165]those days the posters had been seen, and the flitting to Pau had

[154-166]been talked of, and the Framley opinion was that Grace had better

[154-167]remain at Framley till she should be carried off to Pau. There were

[154-168]schemes, too, about Jane. But what was to be done for the wife? And

[154-169]what was to be done for Mr. Crawley? Then came the news from Mrs.

[154-170]Arabin, and all interest in Judge Medlicote was at an end.
