[160-001]But even during the fortnight Mr. Crawley had not remained altogether

[160-002]neglected. Two days after Mr. Harding's death he had received a note

[160-003]from the dean in which he was advised not to resume the duties at

[160-004]Hogglestock for the present. "Of course you can understand that we

[160-005]have a sad house here at present," the dean had said. "But as soon as

[160-006]ever we are able to move in the matter we will arrange things for you

[160-007]as comfortably as we can. I will see the bishop myself." Mr. Crawley

[160-008]had no ambitious idea of any comfort which might accrue to him beyond

[160-009]that of an honourable return to his humble preferment at Hogglestock;

[160-010]but, nevertheless, he was in this case minded to do as the dean

[160-011]counselled him. He had submitted himself to the bishop, and he would

[160-012]wait till the bishop absolved him from his submission.

[160-013]On the day after the funeral, the bishop had sent his compliments to

[160-014]the dean with the expression of a wish that the dean would call upon

[160-015]him on any early day that might be convenient with reference to the

[160-016]position of Mr. Crawley of Hogglestock. The note was in the bishop's

[160-017]own handwriting and was as mild and civil as a bishop's note could

[160-018]be. Of course the dean named an early day for the interview; but it

[160-019]was necessary before he went to the bishop that he should discuss

[160-020]the matter with the archdeacon. If St. Ewolds might be given to Mr.

[160-021]Crawley, the Hogglestock difficulties would all be brought to an

[160-022]end. The archdeacon, after the funeral, had returned to Plumstead,

[160-023]and thither the dean went to him before he saw the bishop. He did

[160-024]succeed,--he and Mrs. Grantly between them,--but with very great

[160-025]difficulty, in obtaining a conditional promise. They had both thought

[160-026]that when the archdeacon became fully aware that Grace was to be his

[160-027]daughter-in-law, he would at once have been delighted to have an

[160-028]opportunity of extricating from his poverty a clergyman with whom it

[160-029]was his fate to be so closely connected. But he fought the matter

[160-030]on twenty different points. He declared at first that as it was his

[160-031]primary duty to give to the people of St. Ewolds the best clergyman

[160-032]he could select for them he could not give the preferment to Mr.

[160-033]Crawley, because Mr. Crawley, in spite of all his zeal and piety, was

[160-034]a man so quaint in his manners and so eccentric in his mode of speech

[160-035]as not to be the best clergyman whom he could select. "What is my

[160-036]old friend Thorne to do with a man in his parish who won't drink

[160-037]a glass of wine with him?" For Ullathorne, the seat of that Mr.

[160-038]Wilfred Thorne who had been so guilty in the matter of the foxes,

[160-039]was situated in the parish of St. Ewolds. When Mrs. Grantly proposed

[160-040]that Mr. Thorne's consent should be asked, the archdeacon became very

[160-041]angry. He had never heard so unecclesiastical a proposition in his

[160-042]life. It was his special duty to do the best he could for Mr. Thorne,

[160-043]but it was specially his duty to do so without consulting Mr. Thorne

[160-044]about it. As the archdeacon's objection had been argued simply on the

[160-045]point of the glass of wine, both the dean and Mrs. Grantly thought

[160-046]that he was unreasonable. But they had their point to gain, and

[160-047]therefore they only flattered him. They were sure that Mr. Thorne

[160-048]would like to have a clergyman in the parish who would himself be

[160-049]closely connected with the archdeacon. Then Dr. Grantly alleged that

[160-050]he might find himself in a trap. What if he conferred the living of

[160-051]St. Ewolds on Mr. Crawley and after all there should be no marriage

[160-052]between his son and Grace? "Of course they'll be married," said Mrs.

[160-053]Grantly. "It's all very well for you to say that, my dear; but the

[160-054]whole family are so queer that there is no knowing what the girl may

[160-055]do. She may take up some other fad now, and refuse him point blank."

[160-056]"She has never taken up any fad," said Mrs. Grantly, who now mounted

[160-057]almost to wrath in defence of her future daughter-in-law, "and you

[160-058]are wrong to say that she has. She has behaved beautifully;--as

[160-059]nobody knows better than you do." Then the archdeacon gave way so far

[160-060]as to promise that St. Ewolds should be offered to Mr. Crawley as

[160-061]soon as Grace Crawley was in truth engaged to Harry Grantly.

[160-062]After that, the dean went to the palace. There had never been any

[160-063]quarrelling between the bishop and the dean, either direct or

[160-064]indirect;--nor, indeed, had the dean ever quarrelled even with Mrs.

[160-065]Proudie. But he had belonged to the anti-Proudie faction. He had

[160-066]been brought into the diocese by the Grantly interest; and therefore,

[160-067]during Mrs. Proudie's life-time, he had always been accounted among

[160-068]the enemies. There had never been any real intimacy between the

[160-069]houses. Each house had been always asked to dine with the other

[160-070]house once a year; but it had been understood that such dinings were

[160-071]ecclesiastico-official, and not friendly. There had been the same

[160-072]outside diocesan civility between even the palace and Plumstead. But

[160-073]now, when the great chieftain of the palace was no more, and the

[160-074]strength of the palace faction was gone, peace, or perhaps something

[160-075]more than peace,--amity, perhaps, might be more easily arranged

[160-076]with the dean than with the archdeacon. In preparation for such

[160-077]arrangements the bishop had gone to Mr. Harding's funeral.

[160-078]And now the dean went to the palace at the bishop's behest. He

[160-079]found his lordship alone, and was received with almost reverential

[160-080]courtesy. He thought that the bishop was looking wonderfully aged

[160-081]since he last saw him, but did not perhaps take into account the

[160-082]absence of clerical sleekness which was incidental to the bishop's

[160-083]private life in his private room, and perhaps in a certain measure

[160-084]to his recent great affliction. The dean had been in the habit of

[160-085]regarding Dr. Proudie as a man almost young for his age,--having

[160-086]been in the habit of seeing him at his best, clothed in authority,

[160-087]redolent of the throne, conspicuous as regarded his apron and outward

[160-088]signs of episcopality. Much of all this was now absent. The bishop,

[160-089]as he rose to greet the dean, shuffled with his old slippers, and his

[160-090]hair was not brushed so becomingly as used to be the case when Mrs.

[160-091]Proudie was always near him.

[160-092]It was necessary that a word should be said by each as to the loss

[160-093]which the other had suffered. "Mr. Dean," said his lordship, "allow

[160-094]me to offer you my condolements in regard to the death of that very

[160-095]excellent clergyman and most worthy gentleman, your father-in-law."

[160-096]"Thank you, my lord. He was excellent and worthy. I do not suppose

[160-097]that I shall live to see any man who was more so. You also have a

[160-098]great,--a terrible loss."

[160-099]"O, Mr. Dean, yes; yes, indeed, Mr. Dean. That was a loss."

[160-100]"And hardly past the prime of life!"

[160-101]"Ah, yes;--just fifty-six,--and so strong! Was she not? At least

[160-102]everybody thought so. And yet she was gone in a minute;--gone in a

[160-103]minute. I haven't held up my head since, Mr. Dean."

[160-104]"It was a great loss, my lord; but you must struggle to bear it."

[160-105]"I do struggle. I am struggling. But it makes one feel so lonely in

[160-106]this great house. Ah, me! I often wish, Mr. Dean, that it had pleased

[160-107]Providence to have left me in some humble parsonage, where duty would

[160-108]have been easier than it is here. But I will not trouble you with all

[160-109]that. What are we to do, Mr. Dean, about this poor Mr. Crawley?"

[160-110]"Mr. Crawley is a very old friend of mine, and a very dear friend."

[160-111]"Is he? Ah! A very worthy man, I am sure, and one who has been much

[160-112]tried by undeserved adversities."

[160-113]"Most severely tried, my lord."

[160-114]"Sitting among the potsherds, like Job; has he not, Mr. Dean? Well;

[160-115]let us hope that all that is over. When this accusation about the

[160-116]robbery was brought against him, I found myself bound to interfere."

[160-117]"He has no complaint to make on that score."

[160-118]"I hope not. I have not wished to be harsh, but what could I do, Mr.

[160-119]Dean? They told me that the civil authorities found the evidence so

[160-120]strong against him that it could not be withstood."

[160-121]"It was very strong."

[160-122]"And we thought that he should at least be relieved, and we sent for

[160-123]Dr. Tempest, who is his rural dean." Then the bishop, remembering all

[160-124]the circumstances of that interview with Dr. Tempest,--as to which he

[160-125]had ever felt assured that one of the results of it was the death of

[160-126]his wife, whereby there was no longer any "we" left in the palace of

[160-127]Barchester,--sighed piteously, looking up at the dean with hopeless

[160-128]face.

[160-129]"Nobody doubts, my lord, that you acted for the best."

[160-130]"I hope we did. I think we did. And now what shall we do? He has

[160-131]resigned his living, both to you and to me, as I hear,--you being

[160-132]the patron. It will simply be necessary, I think, that he should ask

[160-133]to have the letters cancelled. Then, as I take it, there need be no

[160-134]reinstitution. You cannot think, Mr. Dean, how much I have thought

[160-135]about it all."

[160-136]Then the dean unfolded his budget, and explained to the bishop

[160-137]how he hoped that the living of St. Ewolds, which was, after some

[160-138]ecclesiastical fashion, attached to the rectory of Plumstead, and

[160-139]which was now vacant by the demise of Mr. Harding, might be conferred

[160-140]by the archdeacon upon Mr. Crawley. It was necessary to explain also

[160-141]that this could not be done quite immediately, and in doing this the

[160-142]dean encountered some little difficulty. The archdeacon, he said,

[160-143]wished to be allowed another week to think about it; and therefore

[160-144]perhaps provision for the duties at Hogglestock might yet be made for

[160-145]a few Sundays. The bishop, the dean said, might easily understand

[160-146]that, after what had occurred, Mr. Crawley would hardly wish to go

[160-147]again into that pulpit, unless he did so as resuming duties which

[160-148]would necessarily be permanent with him. To all this the bishop

[160-149]assented, but he was apparently struck with much wonder at the choice

[160-150]made by the archdeacon. "I should have thought, Mr. Dean," he said,

[160-151]"that Mr. Crawley was the last man to have suited the archdeacon's

[160-152]choice."

[160-153]"The archdeacon and I married sisters, my lord."

[160-154]"Oh, ah! yes. And he puts the nomination of St. Ewolds at your

[160-155]disposition. I am sure I shall be delighted to institute so worthy

[160-156]a gentleman as Mr. Crawley." Then the dean took his leave of the

[160-157]bishop,--as will we also. Poor dear bishop! I am inclined to think

[160-158]that he was right in his regrets as to the little parsonage. Not that

[160-159]his failure at Barchester, and his present consciousness of lonely

[160-160]incompetence, were mainly due to any positive inefficiency on his

[160-161]own part. He might have been a sufficiently good bishop, had it not

[160-162]been that Mrs. Proudie was so much more than a sufficiently good

[160-163]bishop's wife. We will now say farewell to him, with a hope that the

[160-164]lopped tree may yet become green again, and to some extent fruitful,

[160-165]although all its beautiful head and richness of waving foliage have

[160-166]been taken from it.

[160-167][[About]] a week after this Henry Grantly rode over from Cosby Lodge to

[160-168]Hogglestock. It has been just said that though the assizes had passed

[160-169]by and though all question of Mr. Crawley's guilt was now set aside,

[160-170]no visitor had of late made his way over to Hogglestock. I fancy

[160-171]that Grace Crawley forgot, in the fulness of her memory as to other

[160-172]things, that Mr. Harding, of whose death she heard, had been her

[160-173]lover's grandfather,--and that therefore there might possibly be some

[160-174]delay. Had there been much said between the mother and the daughter

[160-175]about the lover, no doubt all this would have been explained;

[160-176]but Grace was very reticent, and there were other matters in the

[160-177]Hogglestock household which in those days occupied Mrs. Crawley's

[160-178]mind. How were they again to begin life? for, in very truth, life

[160-179]as it had existed with them before, had been brought to an end. But

[160-180]Grace remembered well the sort of compact which existed between

[160-181]her and her lover;--the compact which had been made in very words

[160-182]between herself and her lover's father. Complete in her estimation

[160-183]as had been the heaven opened to her by Henry Grantly's offer, she

[160-184]had refused it all,--lest she should bring disgrace upon him. But the

[160-185]disgrace was not certain; and if her father should be made free from

[160-186]it, then,--then,--then Henry Grantly ought to come to her and be

[160-187]at her feet with all the expedition possible to him. That was her

[160-188]reading of the compact. She had once declared, when speaking of the

[160-189]possible disgrace which might attach itself to her family and to her

[160-190]name, that her poverty did not "signify a bit." She was not ashamed

[160-191]of her father,--only of the accusation against her father. Therefore

[160-192]she had hurried home when that accusation was withdrawn, desirous

[160-193]that her lover should tell her of his love,--if he chose to repeat

[160-194]such telling,--amidst all the poor things of Hogglestock, and not

[160-195]among the chairs and tables and good dinners of luxurious Framley.

[160-196]Mrs. Robarts had given a true interpretation to Lady Lufton of the

[160-197]haste which Grace had displayed. But she need not have been in so

[160-198]great a hurry. She had been at home already above a fortnight, and

[160-199]as yet he had made no sign. At last she said a word to her mother.

[160-200]"Might I not ask to go back to Miss Prettyman's now, mamma?" "I

[160-201]think, dear, you had better wait till things are a little settled.

[160-202]Papa is to hear again from the dean very soon. You see they are all

[160-203]in a great sorrow at Barchester about poor Mr. Harding's death."

[160-204]"Grace!" said Jane, rushing into the house almost speechless, at that

[160-205]moment, "here he is!--on horseback." I do not know why Jane should

[160-206]have talked about Major Grantly as simply "he." There had been no

[160-207]conversation among the sisters to justify her in such a mode of

[160-208]speech. Grace had not a moment to put two and two together, so that

[160-209]she might realize the meaning of what her mother had said; but

[160-210]nevertheless, she felt at the moment that the man, coming as he had

[160-211]done now, had come with all commendable speed. How foolish had she

[160-212]been with her wretched impatience!
