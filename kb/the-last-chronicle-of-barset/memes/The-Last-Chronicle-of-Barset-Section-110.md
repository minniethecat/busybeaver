[110-001]"My dear archdeacon, who ever expected to see you?" said old Lady

[110-002]Lufton. Then the two younger women greeted him. And they all smiled

[110-003]on him pleasantly, and seemed overjoyed to see him. He was, in truth,

[110-004]a great favourite at Framley, and each of the three was glad to

[110-005]welcome him. They believed in the archdeacon at Framley, and felt for

[110-006]him that sort of love which ladies in the country do feel for their

[110-007]elderly male friends. There was not one of the three who would not

[110-008]have taken much trouble to get anything for the archdeacon which

[110-009]they had thought the archdeacon would like. Even old Lady Lufton

[110-010]remembered what was his favourite soup, and always took care that he

[110-011]should have it when he dined at the Court. Young Lady Lufton would

[110-012]bring his tea to him as he sat in his chair. He was petted in the

[110-013]house, was allowed to poke the fire if he pleased, and called the

[110-014]servants by their names as though he were at home. He was compelled,

[110-015]therefore, to smile and to seem pleased; and it was not till after

[110-016]he had eaten his lunch, and had declared that he must return home

[110-017]to dinner, that the dowager gave him an opportunity of having the

[110-018]private conversation which he desired.

[110-019]"Can I have a few minutes' talk with you?" he said to her, whispering

[110-020]into her ear as they left the drawing-room together. So she led the

[110-021]way into her own sitting-room, telling him, as she asked him to

[110-022]be seated, that she had supposed that something special must have

[110-023]brought him over to Framley. "I should have asked you to come up

[110-024]here, even if you had not spoken," she said.

[110-025]"Then perhaps you know what has brought me over?" said the

[110-026]archdeacon.

[110-027]"Not in the least," said Lady Lufton. "I have not an idea. But I

[110-028]did not flatter myself that you would come so far on a morning

[110-029]call, merely to see us three ladies. I hope you did not want to see

[110-030]Ludovic, because he will not be back till to-morrow?"

[110-031]"I wanted to see you, Lady Lufton."

[110-032]"That is lucky, as here I am. You may be pretty sure to find me here

[110-033]any day in the year."

[110-034]After this there was a little pause. The archdeacon hardly knew how

[110-035]to begin his story. In the first place he was in doubt whether Lady

[110-036]Lufton had ever heard of the preposterous match which his son had

[110-037]proposed to himself to make. In his anger at Plumstead he had felt

[110-038]sure that she knew all about it, and that she was assisting his son.

[110-039]But this belief had dwindled as his anger had dwindled; and as the

[110-040]chaise had entered the parish of Framley he had told himself that

[110-041]it was quite impossible that she should know anything about it. Her

[110-042]manner had certainly been altogether in her favour since he had been

[110-043]in her house. There had been nothing of the consciousness of guilt in

[110-044]her demeanour. But, nevertheless, there was the coincidence! How had

[110-045]it come to pass that Grace Crawley and his son should be at Framley

[110-046]together? It might, indeed, be just possible that Flurry might have

[110-047]been wrong, and that his son had not been there at all.

[110-048]"I suppose Miss Crawley is at the parsonage?" he said at last.

[110-049]"Oh, yes; she is still there, and will remain there I should think

[110-050]for the next ten days."

[110-051]"Oh; I did not know," said the archdeacon very coldly.

[110-052]It seemed to Lady Lufton, who was as innocent as an unborn babe

[110-053]in the matter of the projected marriage, that her old friend the

[110-054]archdeacon was in a mind to persecute the Crawleys. He had on a

[110-055]former occasion taken upon himself to advise that Grace Crawley

[110-056]should not be entertained at Framley, and now it seemed that he had

[110-057]come all the way from Plumstead to say something further in the same

[110-058]strain. Lady Lufton, if he had anything further to say of that kind,

[110-059]would listen to him as a matter of course. She would listen to him

[110-060]and reply to him without temper. But she did not approve of it. She

[110-061]told herself silently that she could not approve of persecution or of

[110-062]interference. She therefore drew herself up, and pursed her mouth,

[110-063]and put on something of that look of severity which she could assume

[110-064]very visibly, if it so pleased her.

[110-065]"Yes; she is still there, and I think that her visit will do her a

[110-066]great deal of good," said Lady Lufton.

[110-067]"When we talk of doing good to people," said the archdeacon, "we

[110-068]often make terrible mistakes. It so often happens that we don't know

[110-069]when we are doing good and when we are doing harm."

[110-070]"That is true, of course, Dr. Grantly, and must be so necessarily,

[110-071]as our wisdom here below is so very limited. But I should think,--as

[110-072]far as I can see, that is,--that the kindness which my friend Mrs.

[110-073]Robarts is showing to this young lady must be beneficial. You know,

[110-074]archdeacon, I explained to you before that I could not quite agree

[110-075]with you in what you said as to leaving these people alone till after

[110-076]the trial. I thought that help was necessary to them at once."

[110-077]The archdeacon sighed deeply. He ought to have been somewhat

[110-078]renovated in spirit by the tone in which Lady Lufton spoke to him,

[110-079]as it conveyed to him almost an absolute conviction that his first

[110-080]suspicion was incorrect. But any comfort which might have come to him

[110-081]from this source was marred by the feeling that he must announce his

[110-082]own disgrace. At any rate he must do so, unless he were contented to

[110-083]go back to Plumstead without having learned anything by his journey.

[110-084]He changed the tone of his voice, however, and asked a question,--as

[110-085]it might be altogether on a different subject. "I heard yesterday,"

[110-086]he said, "that Henry was over here."

[110-087]"He was here yesterday. He came the evening before, and dined and

[110-088]slept here, and went home yesterday morning."

[110-089]"Was Miss Crawley with you that evening?"

[110-090]"Miss Crawley? No; she would not come. She thinks it best not to go

[110-091]out while her father is in his present unfortunate position; and she

[110-092]is right."

[110-093]"She is quite right in that," said the archdeacon; and then he paused

[110-094]again. He thought that it would be best for him to make a clean

[110-095]breast of it, and to trust to Lady Lufton's sympathy. "Did Henry go

[110-096]up to the parsonage?" he asked.

[110-097]But still Lady Lufton did not suspect the truth. "I think he did,"

[110-098]she replied, with an air of surprise. "I think I heard that he went

[110-099]up there to call on Mrs. Robarts after breakfast."

[110-100]"No, Lady Lufton, he did not go up there to call on Mrs. Robarts. He

[110-101]went up there because he is making a fool of himself about that Miss

[110-102]Crawley. That is the truth. Now you understand it all. I hope that

[110-103]Mrs. Robarts does not know it. I do hope for her own sake that Mrs.

[110-104]Robarts does not know it."

[110-105]The archdeacon certainly had no longer any doubt as to Lady Lufton's

[110-106]innocence when he looked at her face as she heard these tidings. She

[110-107]had predicted that Grace Crawley would "make havoc," and could not,

[110-108]therefore, be altogether surprised at the idea that some gentleman

[110-109]should have fallen in love with her; but she had never supposed

[110-110]that the havoc might be made so early in her days, or on so great a

[110-111]quarry. "You don't mean to tell me that Henry Grantly is in love with

[110-112]Grace Crawley?" she replied.

[110-113]"I mean to say that he says he is."

[110-114]"Dear, dear, dear! I'm sure, archdeacon, that you will believe me

[110-115]when I say that I knew nothing about it."

[110-116]"I am quite sure of that," said the archdeacon dolefully.

[110-117]"Or I certainly should not have been glad to see him here. But the

[110-118]house, you know, is not mine, Dr. Grantly. I could have done nothing

[110-119]if I had known it. But only to think--; well, to be sure. She has not

[110-120]lost time, at any rate."

[110-121]Now this was not at all the light in which the archdeacon wished that

[110-122]the matter should be regarded. He had been desirous that Lady Lufton

[110-123]should be horror-stricken by the tidings, but it seemed to him that

[110-124]she regarded the iniquity almost as a good joke. What did it matter

[110-125]how young or how old the girl might be? She came of poor people,--of

[110-126]people who had no friends,--of disgraced people; and Lady Lufton

[110-127]ought to feel that such a marriage would be a terrible misfortune and

[110-128]a terrible crime. "I need hardly tell you, Lady Lufton," said the

[110-129]archdeacon, "that I shall set my face against it as far as it is in

[110-130]my power to do so."

[110-131]"If they both be resolved I suppose you can hardly prevent it."

[110-132]"Of course I cannot prevent it. Of course I cannot prevent it. If he

[110-133]will break my heart and his mother's,--and his sister's,--of course

[110-134]I cannot prevent it. If he will ruin himself, he must have his own

[110-135]way."

[110-136]"Ruin himself, Dr. Grantly!"

[110-137]"They will have enough to live upon,--somewhere in Spain or France."

[110-138]The scorn expressed in the archdeacon's voice as he spoke of Pau as

[110-139]being "somewhere in Spain or France," should have been heard to be

[110-140]understood. "No doubt they will have enough to live upon."

[110-141]"Do you mean to say that it will make a difference as to your own

[110-142]property, Dr. Grantly?"

[110-143]"Certainly it will, Lady Lufton. I told Henry when I first heard

[110-144]of the thing,--before he had definitely made any offer to the

[110-145]girl,--that I should withdraw from him altogether the allowance that

[110-146]I now make him, if he married her. And I told him also, that if he

[110-147]persisted in his folly I should think it my duty to alter my will."

[110-148]"I am sorry for that, Dr. Grantly."

[110-149]"Sorry! And am not I sorry? Sorrow is no sufficient word. I am

[110-150]broken-hearted. Lady Lufton, it is killing me. It is indeed. I love

[110-151]him; I love him;--I love him as you have loved your son. But what is

[110-152]the use? What can he be to me when he shall have married the daughter

[110-153]of such a man as that?"

[110-154]Lady Lufton sat for a while silent, thinking of a certain episode

[110-155]in her own life. There had been a time when her son was desirous

[110-156]of making a marriage which she had thought would break her heart.

[110-157]She had for a time moved heaven and earth,--as far as she knew

[110-158]how to move them,--to prevent the marriage. But at last she had

[110-159]yielded,--not from lack of power, for the circumstances had been such

[110-160]that at the moment of yielding she had still the power in her hand of

[110-161]staying the marriage,--but she had yielded because she had perceived

[110-162]that her son was in earnest. She had yielded, and had kissed the

[110-163]dust; but from the moment in which her lips had so touched the

[110-164]ground, she had taken great joy in the new daughter whom her son

[110-165]had brought into the house. Since that she had learned to think

[110-166]that young people might perhaps be right, and that old people might

[110-167]perhaps be wrong. This trouble of her friend the archdeacon's was

[110-168]very like her own old trouble. "And he is engaged to her now?" she

[110-169]said, when those thoughts had passed through her mind.

[110-170]"Yes;--that is, no. I am not sure. I do not know how to make myself

[110-171]sure."

[110-172]"I am sure Major Grantly will tell you all the truth as it exists."

[110-173]"Yes; he'll tell me the truth,--as far as he knows it. I do not see

[110-174]that there is much anxiety to spare me in the matter. He is desirous

[110-175]rather of making me understand that I have no power of saving him

[110-176]from his own folly. Of course I have no power of saving him."
