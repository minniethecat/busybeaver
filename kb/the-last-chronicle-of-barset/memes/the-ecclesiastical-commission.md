## Organization

The Ecclesiastical Commission was a body established by the British Parliament in 1835 to reform the finances and administration of the Church of England. In Trollope's Barsetshire novels, the Commission appears as a distant but consequential force, intervening in the diocese's affairs to redistribute church revenues, suppress superfluous offices, and address the scandal of clerical poverty. In *The Last Chronicle of Barset*, the Commission is the subject of an entire chapter — "The Clerical Commission" (001-021) — and its work provides the institutional context for the novel's central concern with [[poverty-and-dignity|clerical poverty]].

## Role

The Commission's role in the novel is indirect but decisive. It is the body that has the power to determine the financial value of church livings, and its activities — "what the Commissioners do with all those rich revenues" (023-058) — are a matter of anxious speculation among Barsetshire's clergy. The Commission represents the intervention of the modern, reforming state into the ancient, customary world of the diocese. For clergymen like [[josiah-crawley|Josiah Crawley]], the Commission's work holds out the promise of relief: a redistribution of church wealth that might make a living like [[hogglestock]] adequate to support a family. But the Commission is also an object of suspicion, associated with the erosion of traditional clerical independence and the imposition of bureaucratic rationality on spiritual office.

The Commission appears in the novel at a crucial structural point. Chapter 54, "The Clerical Commission" (001-021), brings the reforming energies of metropolitan London into direct contact with the provincial world of Barsetshire, mirroring in institutional form the novel's broader concern with the relationship between centre and periphery, reform and tradition, justice and charity. Mr. Crosbie, a character carried over from the earlier Barsetshire novels, is said to have risen to "the more dignified position of a Commissioner" (084-042), illustrating the Commission's role as a ladder of professional advancement.

## Significance

The inclusion of the Ecclesiastical Commission in the novel's architecture is characteristic of Trollope's institutional realism. Rather than treat clerical poverty as a merely personal tragedy, Trollope embeds it in a specific institutional history. The Commission's existence reminds the reader that the conditions Crawley endures are not natural or inevitable but the product of specific legal and financial arrangements — arrangements that Parliament has already acknowledged as defective and begun to reform. This institutional framing gives the novel's moral argument a political edge: Crawley's suffering is not merely unfortunate but unjust, a failure of the church and the state to fulfil their obligations to those who serve them.
