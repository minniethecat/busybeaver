[071-001]Yours always,

[071-002]M. D. M.

[071-003]Thursday.

[071-004]Poor mamma isn't very well, so you had better ask for me.

[071-005]"Beautiful!" said Johnny, as he read the note. "There's nothing I

[071-006]like so much as a mystery,--especially if it's about nothing. I

[071-007]wonder why she is so desperately anxious that the picture should not

[071-008]be painted. I'd ask Dalrymple, only I should spoil the mystery." Then

[071-009]he sat himself down, and began to think of Lily. There could be no

[071-010]treason to Lily in his amusing himself with the freaks of such a

[071-011]woman as Miss Demolines.

[071-012]At eleven o'clock on the morning of the 1st of March,--the day

[071-013]following that on which Miss Demolines had written her note,--the

[071-014]easel was put up and the canvas was placed on it in Mrs. Broughton's

[071-015]room. Mrs. Broughton and Clara were both there, and when they had

[071-016]seen the outlines as far as it had been drawn, they proceeded to make

[071-017]arrangements for their future operations. The period of work was to

[071-018]begin always at eleven, and was to be continued for an hour and a

[071-019]half or for two hours on the days on which they met. I fear that

[071-020]there was a little improper scheming in this against the two persons

[071-021]whom the ladies were bound to obey. Mr. Dobbs Broughton invariably

[071-022]left his house soon after ten in the morning. It would sometimes

[071-023]happen, though not frequently, that he returned home early in the

[071-024]day,--at four perhaps, or even before that; and should he chance to

[071-025]do so while the picture was going on, he would catch them at their

[071-026]work if the work were postponed till after luncheon. And then again,

[071-027]Mrs. Van Siever would often go out in the morning, and when she did

[071-028]so, would always go without her daughter. On such occasions she went

[071-029]into the City, or to other resorts of business, at which, in some

[071-030]manner quite unintelligible to her daughter, she looked after her

[071-031]money. But when she did not go out in the morning, she did go out

[071-032]in the afternoon, and she would then require her daughter's company.

[071-033]There was some place to which she always went of a Friday morning,

[071-034]and at which she stayed for two or three hours. Friday therefore was

[071-035]a fitting day on which to begin the work at Mrs. Broughton's house.

[071-036]All this was explained between the three conspirators. Mrs. Dobbs

[071-037]Broughton declared that if she entertained the slightest idea that

[071-038]her husband would object to the painting of the picture in her room,

[071-039]nothing on earth would induce her to lend her countenance to it; but

[071-040]yet it might be well not to tell him just at first, perhaps not till

[071-041]the sittings were over,--perhaps not till the picture was finished;

[071-042]as, otherwise, tidings of the picture might get round to ears which

[071-043]were not intended to hear it. "Poor dear Dobbs is so careless with a

[071-044]secret." Miss Van Siever explained her motives in a very different

[071-045]way. "I know mamma would not let me do it if she knew it; and

[071-046]therefore I shall not tell her." "My dear Clara," said Mrs. Broughton

[071-047]with a smile, "you are so outspoken!" "And why not?" said Miss Van

[071-048]Siever. "I am old enough to judge for myself. If mamma does not want

[071-049]to be deceived, she ought not to treat me like a child. Of course

[071-050]she'll find it out sooner or later; but I don't care about that."

[071-051]Conway Dalrymple said nothing as the two ladies were thus excusing

[071-052]themselves. "How delightful it must be not to have a master," said

[071-053]Mrs. Broughton, addressing him. "But then a man has to work for his

[071-054]own bread," said he. "I suppose it comes about equal in the long

[071-055]run."

[071-056]Very little drawing or painting was done on that day. In the first

[071-057]place it was necessary that the question of costume should be

[071-058]settled, and both Mrs. Broughton and the artist had much to say on

[071-059]the subject. It was considered proper that Jael should be dressed as

[071-060]a Jewess, and there came to be much question how Jewesses dressed

[071-061]themselves in those very early days. Mrs. Broughton had prepared her

[071-062]jewels and raiment of many colours, but the painter declared that

[071-063]the wife of Heber the Kenite would have no jewels. But when Mrs.

[071-064]Broughton discovered from her Bible that Heber had been connected by

[071-065]family ties with Moses, she was more than ever sure that Heber's wife

[071-066]would have in her tent much of the spoilings of the Egyptians. And

[071-067]when Clara Van Siever suggested that at any rate she would not have

[071-068]worn them in a time of confusion when soldiers were loose, flying

[071-069]about the country, Mrs. Broughton was quite confident that she would

[071-070]have put them on before she invited the captain of the enemy's host

[071-071]into her tent. The artist at last took the matter into his own hand

[071-072]by declaring that Miss Van Siever would sit the subject much better

[071-073]without jewels, and therefore all Mrs. Broughton's gewgaws were put

[071-074]back into their boxes. And then on four different times the two

[071-075]ladies had to retire into Mrs. Broughton's room in order that Jael

[071-076]might be arrayed in various costumes,--and in each costume she had to

[071-077]kneel down, taking the hammer in her hand, and holding the pointed

[071-078]stick which had been prepared to do duty as the nail, upon the

[071-079]forehead of a dummy Sisera. At last it was decided that her raiment

[071-080]should be altogether white, and that she should wear, twisted round

[071-081]her head and falling over her shoulder, a Roman silk scarf of various

[071-082]colours. "Where Jael could have gotten it I don't know," said Clara.

[071-083]"You may be sure that there were lots of such things among the

[071-084]Egyptians," said Mrs. Broughton, "and that Moses brought away all the

[071-085]best for his own family."

[071-086]"And who is to be Sisera?" asked Mrs. Broughton in one of the pauses

[071-087]in their work.

[071-088]"I'm thinking of asking my friend John Eames to sit."

[071-089]"Of course we cannot sit together," said Miss Van Siever.

[071-090]"There's no reason why you should," said Dalrymple. "I can do the

[071-091]second figure in my own room." Then there was a bargain made that

[071-092]Sisera should not be a portrait. "It would never do," said Mrs.

[071-093]Broughton, shaking her head very gravely.

[071-094]Though there was really very little done to the picture on that day,

[071-095]the work was commenced; and Mrs. Broughton, who had at first objected

[071-096]strongly to the idea, and who had said twenty times that it was quite

[071-097]out of the question that it should be done in her house, became very

[071-098]eager in her delight about it. Nobody should know anything of the

[071-099]picture till it should be exhibited. That would be best. And it

[071-100]should be the picture of the year! She was a little heart-broken when

[071-101]Dalrymple assured her that it could not possibly be finished for

[071-102]exhibition in that May; but she came to again when he declared that

[071-103]he meant to put out all his strength upon it. "There will be five or

[071-104]six months' work in it," he said. "Will there, indeed? And how much

[071-105]work was there in 'The Graces'?" "The Graces," as will perhaps be

[071-106]remembered, was the triple portrait of Mrs. Dobbs Broughton herself.

[071-107]This question the artist did not answer with absolute accuracy, but

[071-108]contented himself with declaring that with such a model as Mrs.

[071-109]Broughton the picture had been comparatively easy.

[071-110]Mrs. Broughton, having no doubt that ultimate object of which she had

[071-111]spoken to her friend Conway steadily in view, took occasion before

[071-112]the sitting was over to leave the room, so that the artist might have

[071-113]an opportunity of speaking a word in private to his model,--if he had

[071-114]any such word to speak. And Mrs. Broughton, as she did this, felt

[071-115]that she was doing her duty as a wife, a friend, and a Christian. She

[071-116]was doing her duty as a wife, because she was giving the clearest

[071-117]proof in the world,--the clearest at any rate to herself,--that the

[071-118]intimacy between herself and her friend Conway had in it nothing that

[071-119]was improper. And she was doing her duty as a friend, because Clara

[071-120]Van Siever, with her large expectations, would be an eligible wife.

[071-121]And she was doing her duty as a Christian, because the whole thing

[071-122]was intended to be moral. Miss Demolines had declared that her

[071-123]friend Maria Clutterbuck,--as Miss Demolines delighted to call Mrs.

[071-124]Broughton, in memory of dear old innocent days,--had high principles;

[071-125]and the reader will see that she was justified in her declaration.

[071-126]"It will be better so," said Mrs. Broughton, as she sat upon her bed

[071-127]and wiped a tear from the corner of her eye. "Yes; it will be better

[071-128]so. There is a pang. Of course there's a pang. But it will be better

[071-129]so." Acting upon this high principle, she allowed Conway Dalrymple

[071-130]five minutes to say what he had to say to Clara Van Siever. Then she

[071-131]allowed herself to indulge in some very savage feelings in reference

[071-132]to her husband,--accusing her husband in her thoughts of great

[071-133]cruelty,--nay, of brutality, because of certain sharp words that he

[071-134]had said as to Conway Dalrymple. "But of course he can't understand,"

[071-135]said Mrs. Broughton to herself. "How is it to be expected that he

[071-136]should understand?"

[071-137]But she allowed her friend on this occasion only five minutes,

[071-138]thinking probably that so much time might suffice. A woman, when she

[071-139]is jealous, is apt to attribute to the other woman with whom her

[071-140]jealousy is concerned, both weakness and timidity, and to the man

[071-141]both audacity and strength. A woman who has herself taken perhaps

[071-142]twelve months in the winning, will think that another woman is to

[071-143]be won in five minutes. It is not to be supposed that Mrs. Dobbs

[071-144]Broughton had ever been won by any one except by Mr. Dobbs Broughton.

[071-145]At least, let it not be supposed that she had ever acknowledged a

[071-146]spark of love for Conway Dalrymple. But nevertheless there was enough

[071-147]of jealousy in her present mood to make her think poorly of Miss

[071-148]Van Siever's capacity for standing a siege against the artist's

[071-149]eloquence. Otherwise, having left the two together with the object

[071-150]which she had acknowledged to herself, she would hardly have returned

[071-151]to them after so very short an interval.

[071-152]"I hope you won't dislike the trouble of all this?" said Dalrymple to

[071-153]his model, as soon as Mrs. Broughton was gone.

[071-154]"I cannot say that I like it very much," said Miss Van Siever.

[071-155]"I'm afraid it will be a bore;--but I hope you'll go through with

[071-156]it."

[071-157]"I shall if I am not prevented," said Miss Van Siever. "When I've

[071-158]said that I'll do a thing, I like to do it."

[071-159]There was a pause in the conversation which took up a considerable

[071-160]portion of the five minutes. Miss Van Siever was not holding her nail

[071-161]during these moments, but was sitting in a commonplace way on her

[071-162]chair, while Dalrymple was scraping his palette. "I wonder what it

[071-163]was that first induced you to sit?" said he.

[071-164]"Oh, I don't know. I took a fancy for it."

[071-165]"I'm very glad you did take the fancy. You'll make an excellent

[071-166]model. If you won't mind posing again for a few minutes--I will not

[071-167]weary you to-day. Your right arm a little more forward."

[071-168]"But I should tumble down."

[071-169]"Not if you lean well on to the nail."

[071-170]"But that would have woken Sisera before she had struck a blow."

[071-171]"Never mind that. Let us try it." Then Mrs. Broughton returned, with

[071-172]that pleasant feeling in her bosom of having done her duty as a wife,

[071-173]a friend, and a Christian. "Mrs. Broughton," continued the painter,

[071-174]"just steady Miss Van Siever's shoulder with your hand; and now bring

[071-175]the arm and the elbow a little more forward."
