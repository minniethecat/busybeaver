[085-001]And Lily did go up to town with her uncle, two days only having been

[085-002]allowed to her for her preparations. There was very much for her to

[085-003]think of in such a journey. It was not only that she would see Emily

[085-004]Dunstable who was to be her cousin's wife, and that she would go to

[085-005]the play and visit the new conjuror's entertainment, but that she

[085-006]would be in the same city both with Adolphus Crosbie and with John

[085-007]Eames. Not having personal experience of the wideness of London, and

[085-008]of the wilderness which it is;--of the distance which is set there

[085-009]between persons who are not purposely brought together--it seemed

[085-010]to her fancy as though for this month of her absence from home she

[085-011]would be brought into close contiguity with both her lovers. She

[085-012]had hitherto felt herself to be at any rate safe in her fortress at

[085-013]Allington. When Crosbie had written to her mother, making a renewed

[085-014]offer which had been rejected, Lily had felt that she certainly need

[085-015]not see him unless it pleased her to do so. He could hardly force

[085-016]himself upon her at Allington. And as to John Eames, though he would,

[085-017]of course, be welcome at Allington as often as he pleased to show

[085-018]himself, still there was a security in the place. She was so much at

[085-019]home there that she could always be mistress of the occasion. She

[085-020]knew that she could talk to him at Allington as though from ground

[085-021]higher than that on which he stood himself; but she felt that this

[085-022]would hardly be the case if she should chance to meet him in London.

[085-023]Crosbie probably would not come in her way. Crosbie she thought,--and

[085-024]she blushed for the man she loved, as the idea came across her

[085-025]mind,--would be afraid of meeting her uncle. But John Eames would

[085-026]certainly find her; and she was led by the experience of latter days

[085-027]to imagine that John would never cross her path without renewing his

[085-028]attempts.

[085-029]But she said no word of all this, even to her mother. She was

[085-030]contented to confine her outspoken expectations to Emily Dunstable,

[085-031]and the play, and the conjuror. "The chances are ten to one against

[085-032]my liking her, mamma," she said.

[085-033]"I don't see that, my dear."

[085-034]"I feel to be too old to think that I shall ever like any more new

[085-035]people. Three years ago I should have been quite sure that I should

[085-036]love a new cousin. It would have been like having a new dress. But

[085-037]I've come to think that an old dress is the most comfortable, and an

[085-038]old cousin certainly the best."

[085-039]The squire had had taken for them a gloomy lodging in Sackville

[085-040]Street. Lodgings in London are always gloomy. Gloomy colours wear

[085-041]better than bright ones for curtains and carpets, and the keepers

[085-042]of lodgings in London seem to think that a certain dinginess of

[085-043]appearance is respectable. I never saw a London lodging in which

[085-044]any attempt at cheerfulness had been made, and I do not think that

[085-045]any such attempt, if made, would pay. The lodging-seeker would be

[085-046]frightened and dismayed, and would unconsciously be led to fancy

[085-047]that something was wrong. Ideas of burglars and improper persons

[085-048]would present themselves. This is so certainly the case that I doubt

[085-049]whether any well-conditioned lodging-house matron could be induced to

[085-050]show rooms that were prettily draped or pleasantly coloured. The big

[085-051]drawing-room and two large bedrooms which the squire took, were all

[085-052]that was proper, and were as brown, and as gloomy, and as ill-suited

[085-053]for the comforts of ordinary life as though they had been prepared

[085-054]for two prisoners. But Lily was not so ignorant as to expect cheerful

[085-055]lodgings in London, and was satisfied. "And what are we to do now?"

[085-056]said Lily, as soon as they found themselves settled. It was still

[085-057]March, and whatever may have been the nature of the weather at

[085-058]Allington, it was very cold in London. They reached Sackville

[085-059]Street about five in the evening, and an hour was taken up in

[085-060]unpacking their trunks and making themselves as comfortable as their

[085-061]circumstances allowed. "And now what are we to do?" said Lily.

[085-062]"I told them to have dinner for us at half-past six."

[085-063]"And what after that? Won't Bernard come to us to-night? I expected

[085-064]him to be standing on the door-steps waiting for us with his bride in

[085-065]his hand."

[085-066]"I don't suppose Bernard will be here to-night," said the squire. "He

[085-067]did not say that he would, and as for Miss Dunstable, I promised to

[085-068]take you to her aunt's house to-morrow."

[085-069]"But I wanted to see her to-night. Well;--of course bridesmaids

[085-070]must wait upon brides. And ladies with twenty thousand pounds can't

[085-071]be expected to run about like common people. As for Bernard,--but

[085-072]Bernard never was in a hurry." Then they dined, and when the squire

[085-073]had very nearly fallen asleep over a bottle of port wine which had

[085-074]been sent in for him from some neighbouring public-house, Lily began

[085-075]to feel that it was very dull. And she looked round the room, and

[085-076]she thought that it was very ugly. And she calculated that thirty

[085-077]evenings so spent would seem to be very long. And she reflected that

[085-078]the hours were probably going much more quickly with Emily Dunstable,

[085-079]who, no doubt, at this moment had Bernard Dale by her side. And then

[085-080]she told herself that the hours were not tedious with her at home,

[085-081]while sitting with her mother, with all her daily occupations within

[085-082]her reach. But in so telling herself she took herself to task,

[085-083]inquiring of herself whether such an assurance was altogether true.

[085-084]Were not the hours sometimes tedious even at home? And in this way

[085-085]her mind wandered off to thoughts upon life in general, and she

[085-086]repeated to herself over and over again the two words which she had

[085-087]told John Eames that she would write in her journal. The reader will

[085-088]remember those two words;--Old Maid. And she had written them in her

[085-089]book, making each letter a capital, and round them she had drawn a

[085-090]scroll, ornamented after her own fashion, and she had added the date

[085-091]in quaintly formed figures,--for in such matters Lily had some little

[085-092]skill and a dash of fun to direct it; and she had inscribed below it

[085-093]an Italian motto,--"Who goes softly, goes safely;" and above her work

[085-094]of art she had put a heading--"As arranged by Fate for L. D." Now she

[085-095]thought of all this, and reflected whether Emily Dunstable was in

[085-096]truth very happy. Presently the tears came into her eyes, and she got

[085-097]up and went to the window, as though she were afraid that her uncle

[085-098]might wake and see them. And as she looked out on the blank street,

[085-099]she muttered a word or two--"Dear mother! Dearest mother!" Then the

[085-100]door was opened, and her cousin Bernard announced himself. She had

[085-101]not heard his knock at the door as she had been thinking of the two

[085-102]words in her book.

[085-103]"What; Bernard!--ah, yes, of course," said the squire, rubbing his

[085-104]eyes as he strove to wake himself. "I wasn't sure you would come, but

[085-105]I'm delighted to see you. I wish you joy with all my heart,--with all

[085-106]my heart."

[085-107]"Of course, I should come," said Bernard. "Dear Lily, this is so good

[085-108]of you. Emily is so delighted." Then Lily spoke her congratulations

[085-109]warmly, and there was no trace of a tear in her eyes, and she was

[085-110]thoroughly happy as she sat by her cousin's side and listened to

[085-111]his raptures about Emily Dunstable. "And you will be so fond of her

[085-112]aunt," he said.

[085-113]"But is she not awfully rich?" said Lily.

[085-114]"Frightfully rich," said Bernard; "but really you would hardly find

[085-115]it out if nobody told you. Of course she lives in a big house, and

[085-116]has a heap of servants; but she can't help that."

[085-117]"I hate a heap of servants," said Lily.

[085-118]Then there came another knock at the door, and who should enter the

[085-119]room but John Eames. Lily for a moment was taken aback, but it was

[085-120]only for a moment. She had been thinking so much of him that his

[085-121]presence disturbed her for an instant. "He probably will not know

[085-122]that I am here," she had said to herself; but she had not yet been

[085-123]three hours in London, and he was already with her! At first he

[085-124]hardly spoke to her, addressing himself to the squire. "Lady Julia

[085-125]told me you were to be here, and as I start for the Continent early

[085-126]to-morrow morning, I thought you would let me come and see you before

[085-127]I went."

[085-128]"I'm always glad to see you, John," said the squire,--"very glad. And

[085-129]so you're going abroad, are you?"

[085-130]Then Johnny congratulated his old acquaintance, Bernard Dale, as to

[085-131]his coming marriage, and explained to them how Lady Julia in one

[085-132]of her letters had told him all about it, and had even given him

[085-133]the number in Sackville Street. "I suppose she learned it from you,

[085-134]Lily," said the squire. "Yes, uncle, she did." And then there came

[085-135]questions as to John's projected journey to the Continent, and

[085-136]he explained that he was going on law-business, on behalf of Mr.

[085-137]Crawley, to catch the dean and Mrs. Arabin, if it might be possible.

[085-138]"You see, sir, Mr. Toogood, who is Mr. Crawley's cousin, and also his

[085-139]lawyer, is my cousin, too; and that's why I'm going." And still there

[085-140]had been hardly a word spoken between him and Lily.

[085-141]"But you're not a lawyer, John; are you?" said the squire.

[085-142]"No. I'm not a lawyer myself."

[085-143]"Nor a lawyer's clerk?"

[085-144]"Certainly not a lawyer's clerk," said Johnny, laughing.

[085-145]"Then why should you go?" asked Bernard Dale.

[085-146]Then Johnny had to explain; and in doing so he became very eloquent

[085-147]as to the hardships of Mr. Crawley's case. "You see, sir, nobody can

[085-148]possibly believe that such a man as that stole twenty pounds."

[085-149]"I do not for one," said Lily.

[085-150]"God forbid that I should say he did," said the squire.

[085-151]"I'm quite sure he didn't," said Johnny, warming to his subject. "It

[085-152]couldn't be that such a man as that should become a thief all at

[085-153]once. It's not human nature, sir; is it?"

[085-154]"It is very hard to know what is human nature," said the squire.

[085-155]"It's the general opinion down in Barsetshire that he did steal it,"

[085-156]said Bernard. "Dr. Thorne was one of the magistrates who committed

[085-157]him, and I know he thinks so."

[085-158]"I don't blame the magistrates in the least," said Johnny.

[085-159]"That's kind of you," said the squire.

[085-160]"Of course you'll laugh at me, sir; but you'll see that we shall come

[085-161]out right. There's some mystery in it of which we haven't got at the

[085-162]bottom as yet; and if there is anybody that can help us it's the

[085-163]dean."

[085-164]"If the dean knows anything, why has he not written and told what he

[085-165]knows?" said the squire.

[085-166]"That's what I can't say. The dean has not had an opportunity of

[085-167]writing since he heard,--even if he has yet heard,--that Mr. Crawley

[085-168]is to be tried. And then he and Mrs. Arabin are not together. It's

[085-169]a long story, and I will not trouble you with it all; but at any

[085-170]rate I'm going off to-morrow. Lily, can I do anything for you in

[085-171]Florence?"

[085-172]"In Florence?" said Lily; "and are you really going to Florence? How

[085-173]I envy you."

[085-174]"And who pays your expenses?" said the squire.

[085-175]"Well;--as to my expenses, they are to be paid by a person who won't

[085-176]raise any unpleasant questions about the amount."

[085-177]"I don't know what you mean," said the squire.

[085-178]"He means himself," said Lily.

[085-179]"Is he going to do it out of his own pocket?"
