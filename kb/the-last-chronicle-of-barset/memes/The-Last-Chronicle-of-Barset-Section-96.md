[096-001]Posy was with him when Mrs. Grantly went up into his room, holding

[096-002]for him his hat and stick while he was engaged in brushing a

[096-003]suspicion of dust from his black gaiters. "Grandpapa, here is aunt

[096-004]Susan," said Posy. The old man looked up with something,--with some

[096-005]slightest sign of that habitual fear which was always aroused within

[096-006]his bosom by visitations from Plumstead. Had Mrs. Arabin thoroughly

[096-007]understood the difference in her father's feeling toward herself and

[096-008]toward her sister, I think she would hardly have gone forth upon

[096-009]any tour while he remained with her in the deanery. It is very hard

[096-010]sometimes to know how intensely we are loved, and of what value our

[096-011]presence is to those who love us! Mrs. Grantly saw the look,--did not

[096-012]analyse it, did not quite understand it,--but felt, as she had so

[096-013]often felt before, that it was not altogether laden with welcome. But

[096-014]all this had nothing to do with the duty on which she had come; nor

[096-015]did it, in the slightest degree, militate against her own affection.

[096-016]"Papa," she said, kissing him, "you are surprised to see me so

[096-017]early?"

[096-018]"Well, my dear, yes;--but very glad all the same. I hope everybody is

[096-019]well at Plumstead?"

[096-020]"Everybody, thank you, papa."

[096-021]"That is well. Posy and I are getting ready for church. Are we not,

[096-022]Posy?"

[096-023]"Grandpapa is getting ready. Mrs. Baxter won't let me go."

[096-024]"No, my dear, no;--not yet, Posy. When Posy is a great girl she can

[096-025]go to cathedral every day. Only then, perhaps, Posy won't want to

[096-026]go."

[096-027]"I thought that, perhaps, papa, you would sit with me a little while

[096-028]this morning, instead of going to morning prayers."

[096-029]"Certainly, my dear,--certainly. Only I do not like not going;--for

[096-030]who can say how often I may be able to go again? There is so little

[096-031]left, Susan,--so very little left."

[096-032]After that she had not the heart to ask him to stay, and therefore

[096-033]she went with him. As they passed down the stairs and out of the

[096-034]doors she was astonished to find how weak were his footsteps,--how

[096-035]powerless he was against the slightest misadventure. On this very

[096-036]day he would have tripped at the upward step at the cathedral door

[096-037]had she not been with him. "Oh, papa," she said, "indeed, indeed,

[096-038]you should not come here alone." Then he apologized for his little

[096-039]stumble with many words and much shame, assuring her that anybody

[096-040]might trip on an occasion. It was purely an accident; and though it

[096-041]was a comfort to him to have had her arm, he was sure that he should

[096-042]have recovered himself even had he been alone. He always, he said,

[096-043]kept quite close to the wall, so that there might be no mistake,--no

[096-044]possibility of an accident. All this he said volubly, but with

[096-045]confused words, in the covered stone passage leading into the

[096-046]transept. And, as he thus spoke, Mrs. Grantly made up her mind that

[096-047]her father should never again go to the cathedral alone. He never did

[096-048]go again to the cathedral,--alone.

[096-049]When they returned to the deanery, Mr. Harding was fluttered, weary,

[096-050]and unwell. When his daughter left him for a few minutes he told Mrs.

[096-051]Baxter, in confidence, the story of his accident, and his great grief

[096-052]that his daughter should have seen it. "Laws amercy, sir, it was

[096-053]a blessing she was with you," said Mrs. Baxter; "it was, indeed,

[096-054]Mr. Harding." Then Mr. Harding had been angry, and spoke almost

[096-055]crossly to Mrs. Baxter; but, before she left the room, he found an

[096-056]opportunity of begging her pardon,--not in a set speech to that

[096-057]effect, but by a little word of gentle kindness, which she had

[096-058]understood perfectly. "Papa," said Mrs. Grantly to him as soon as

[096-059]she had succeeded in getting both Posy and Mrs. Baxter out of the

[096-060]room,--against the doing of which, Mr. Harding had manoeuvred with

[096-061]all his little impotent skill,--"Papa, you must promise me that you

[096-062]will not go to the cathedral again alone, till Eleanor comes home."

[096-063]When he heard the sentence he looked at her with blank misery in his

[096-064]eyes. He made no attempt at remonstrance. He begged for no respite.

[096-065]The word had gone forth, and he knew that it must be obeyed. Though

[096-066]he would have hidden the signs of his weakness had he been able, he

[096-067]would not condescend to plead that he was strong. "If you think it

[096-068]wrong, my dear, I will not go alone," he said. "Papa, I do; indeed,

[096-069]I do. Dear papa, I would not hurt you by saying it if I did not know

[096-070]that I am right." He was sitting with his hand upon the table, and,

[096-071]as she spoke to him, she put her hand upon his, caressing it. "My

[096-072]dear," he said, "you are always right."

[096-073]She then left him again for awhile, having some business out in

[096-074]the city, and he was alone in his room for an hour. What was there

[096-075]left to him now in the world? Old as he was, and in some things

[096-076]almost childish, nevertheless, he thought of this keenly, and some

[096-077]half-realized remembrance of "the lean and slippered pantaloon"

[096-078]flitted across his mind, causing him a pang. What was there left to

[096-079]him now in the world? Posy and cat's-cradle! Then, in the midst of

[096-080]his regrets, as he sat with his back bent in his old easy-chair, with

[096-081]one arm over the shoulder of the chair, and the other hanging loose

[096-082]by his side, on a sudden there came across his face a smile as sweet

[096-083]as ever brightened the face of man or woman. He had been able to tell

[096-084]himself that he had no ground for complaint,--great ground rather for

[096-085]rejoicing and gratitude. Had not the world and all in it been good to

[096-086]him; had he not children who loved him, who had done him honour, who

[096-087]had been to him always a crown of glory, never a mark for reproach;

[096-088]had not his lines fallen to him in very pleasant places; was it not

[096-089]his happy fate to go and leave it all amidst the good words and kind

[096-090]loving cares of devoted friends? Whose latter days had ever been more

[096-091]blessed than his? And for the future--? It was as he thought of this

[096-092]that that smile came across his face,--as though it were already

[096-093]the face of an angel. And then he muttered to himself a word or

[096-094]two. "Lord, now lettest Thou Thy servant depart in peace. Lord, now

[096-095]lettest Thou Thy servant depart in peace."

[096-096]When Mrs. Grantly returned she found him in jocund spirits. And yet

[096-097]she perceived that he was so weak that when he left his chair he

[096-098]could barely get across the room without assistance. Mrs. Baxter,

[096-099]indeed, had not sent to her too soon, and it was well that the

[096-100]prohibition had come in time to prevent some terrible accident.

[096-101]"Papa," she said, "I think you had better go with me to Plumstead.

[096-102]The carriage is here, and I can take you home so comfortably." But he

[096-103]would not allow himself to be taken on this occasion to Plumstead. He

[096-104]smiled and thanked her, and put his hand into hers, and repeated his

[096-105]promise that he would not leave the house on any occasion without

[096-106]assistance, and declared himself specially thankful to her for

[096-107]coming to him on that special morning;--but he would not be taken to

[096-108]Plumstead. "When the summer comes," he said, "then, if you will have

[096-109]me for a few days!"

[096-110]He meant no deceit, and yet he had told himself within the last hour

[096-111]that he should never see another summer. He could not tell even his

[096-112]daughter that after such a life as this, after more than fifty years

[096-113]spent in the ministrations of his darling cathedral, it specially

[096-114]behoved him to die,--as he had lived,--at Barchester. He could not

[096-115]say this to his eldest daughter; but had his Eleanor been at home, he

[096-116]could have said it to her. He thought he might yet live to see his

[096-117]Eleanor once again. If this could be given to him he would ask for

[096-118]nothing more.

[096-119]On the afternoon of the next day, Mrs. Baxter wrote another letter,

[096-120]in which she told Mrs. Grantly that her father had declared, at his

[096-121]usual hour of rising that morning, that as he was not going to the

[096-122]cathedral he would, he thought, lie in bed a little longer. And then

[096-123]he had lain in bed the whole day. "And, perhaps, honoured madam,

[096-124]looking at all things, it's best as he should," said Mrs. Baxter.

[096-125]CHAPTER L.

[096-126]LADY LUFTON'S PROPOSITION.

[096-127]It was now known throughout Barchester that a commission was to be

[096-128]held by the bishop's orders, at which inquiry would be made,--that

[096-129]is, ecclesiastical inquiry,--as to the guilt imputed to Mr. Crawley

[096-130]in the matter of Mr. Soames's cheque. Sundry rumours had gone abroad

[096-131]as to quarrels which had taken place on the subject among certain

[096-132]clergymen high in office; but these were simply rumours, and nothing

[096-133]was in truth known. There was no more discreet clergyman in all the

[096-134]diocese than Dr. Tempest, and not a word had escaped from him as to

[096-135]the stormy nature of that meeting in the bishop's palace, at which he

[096-136]had attended with the bishop,--and at which Mrs. Proudie had attended

[096-137]also. When it is said that the fact of this coming commission was

[096-138]known to all Barsetshire, allusion is of course made to that portion

[096-139]of the inhabitants of Barsetshire to which clerical matters were

[096-140]dear;--and as such matters were specially dear to the inhabitants of

[096-141]the parish of Framley, the commission was discussed very eagerly in

[096-142]that parish, and was specially discussed by the Dowager Lady Lufton.

[096-143]And there was a double interest attached to the commission in the

[096-144]parish of Framley by the fact that Mr. Robarts, the vicar, had

[096-145]been invited by Dr. Tempest to be one of the clergymen who were to

[096-146]assist in making the inquiry. "I also propose to ask Mr. Oriel of

[096-147]Greshamsbury to join us," said Dr. Tempest. "The bishop wishes to

[096-148]appoint the other two, and has already named Mr. Thumble and Mr.

[096-149]Quiverful, who are both residents in the city. Perhaps his lordship

[096-150]may be right in thinking it better that the matter should not be left

[096-151]altogether in the hands of clergymen who hold livings in the diocese.

[096-152]You are no doubt aware that neither Mr. Thumble nor Mr. Quiverful do

[096-153]hold any benefice." Mr. Robarts felt,--as everybody else did feel

[096-154]who knew anything of the matter,--that Bishop Proudie was singularly

[096-155]ignorant in his knowledge of men, and that he showed his ignorance on

[096-156]this special occasion. "If he intended to name two such men he should

[096-157]at any rate have named three," said Dr. Thorne. "Mr. Thumble and Mr.

[096-158]Quiverful will simply be outvoted on the first day, and after that

[096-159]will give in their adhesion to the majority." "Mr. Thumble, indeed!"

[096-160]Lady Lufton had said, with much scorn in her voice. To her thinking,

[096-161]it was absurd in the highest degree that such men as Dr. Tempest and

[096-162]her Mr. Robarts should be asked to meet Mr. Thumble and Mr. Quiverful

[096-163]on a matter of ecclesiastical business. Outvoted! Of course they

[096-164]would be outvoted. Of course they would be so paralyzed by fear at

[096-165]finding themselves in the presence of real gentlemen, that they would

[096-166]hardly be able to vote at all. Old Lady Lufton did not in fact utter

[096-167]words so harsh as these; but thoughts as harsh passed through her

[096-168]mind. The reader therefore will understand that much interest was

[096-169]felt on the subject at Framley Court, where Lady Lufton lived with

[096-170]her son and her daughter-in-law.

[096-171]"They tell me," said Lady Lufton, "that both the archdeacon and Dr.

[096-172]Tempest think it right that a commission should be held. If so, I

[096-173]have no doubt that it is right."
