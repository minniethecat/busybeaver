[057-001]"Well; if you ask me, I think he has gone there to see your cousin,

[057-002]Grace Crawley."

[057-003]"He told me that he knew Grace," said John, looking as though he were

[057-004]conscious of his own ingenuity in putting two and two together very

[057-005]cleverly.

[057-006]"Your cousin Grace is a very pretty girl," said Lady Julia.

[057-007]"It's a long time since I've seen her," said Johnny.

[057-008]"Why, you saw her just this minute," said Lady Julia.

[057-009]"I didn't look at her," said Johnny. Therefore, when he again met

[057-010]Major Grantly, having continued to put two and two together with

[057-011]great ingenuity, he felt quite sure that the man had nothing to do

[057-012]with the arch-enemy, and he determined to be gracious. "Did you find

[057-013]them at home at Allington?" he said, raising his hat.

[057-014]"How do you do again?" said the major. "Yes, I found your friend Mrs.

[057-015]Dale at home."

[057-016]"But not her daughter, or my cousin? They were up there;--where I've

[057-017]come from. But, perhaps, they had got back before you left."

[057-018]"I saw them both. They found me on the road with Mr. Dale."

[057-019]"What,--the squire? Then you have seen everybody?"

[057-020]"Everybody I wished to see at Allington."

[057-021]"But you wouldn't stay at the 'Red Lion?'"

[057-022]"Well, no. I remembered that I wanted to get back to London; and as

[057-023]I had seen my friends, I thought I might as well hurry away."

[057-024]"You knew Mrs. Dale before, then?"

[057-025]"No, I didn't. I never saw her in my life before. But I knew the old

[057-026]squire when I was a boy. However, I should have said friend. I went

[057-027]to see one friend, and I saw her."

[057-028]John Eames perceived that his companion put a strong emphasis on the

[057-029]word "her," as though he were determined to declare boldly that he

[057-030]had gone to Allington solely to see Grace Crawley. He had not the

[057-031]slightest objection to recognizing in Major Grantly a suitor for his

[057-032]cousin's hand. He could only reflect what an unusually fortunate girl

[057-033]Grace must be if such a thing could be true. Of those poor Crawleys

[057-034]he had only heard from time to time that their misfortunes were as

[057-035]numerous as the sands on the sea-shore, and as unsusceptible of any

[057-036]fixed and permanent arrangement. But, as regarded Grace, here would

[057-037]be a very permanent arrangement. Tidings had reached him that Grace

[057-038]was a great scholar, but he had never heard much of her beauty. It

[057-039]must probably be the case that Major Grantly was fond of Greek. There

[057-040]was, he reminded himself, no accounting for tastes; but as nothing

[057-041]could be more respectable than such an alliance, he thought that it

[057-042]would become him to be civil to the major.

[057-043]"I hope you found her quite well. I had barely time to speak to her

[057-044]myself."

[057-045]"Yes, she was very well. This is a sad thing about her father."

[057-046]"Very sad," said Johnny. Perhaps the major had heard about the

[057-047]accusation for the first time to-day, and was going to find an escape

[057-048]on that plea. If such was the case, it would not be so well to be

[057-049]particularly civil.

[057-050]"I believe Mr. Crawley is a cousin of yours?" said the major.

[057-051]"His wife is my mother's first-cousin. Their mothers were sisters."

[057-052]"She is an excellent woman."

[057-053]"I believe so. I don't know much about them myself,--that is,

[057-054]personally. Of course I have heard of this charge that has been made

[057-055]against him. It seems to me to be a great shame."

[057-056]"Well, I can't exactly say that it is a shame. I do not know that

[057-057]there has been anything done with a feeling of persecution or of

[057-058]cruelty. It is a great mystery, and we must have it cleared up if we

[057-059]can."

[057-060]"I don't suppose he can have been guilty," said Johnny.

[057-061]"Certainly not in the ordinary sense of the word. I heard all the

[057-062]evidence against him."

[057-063]"Oh, you did?"

[057-064]"Yes," said the major. "I live near them in Barsetshire, and I am one

[057-065]of his bailsmen."

[057-066]"Then you are an old friend, I suppose?"

[057-067]"Not exactly that; but circumstances make me very much interested

[057-068]about them. I fancy that the cheque was left in his house by

[057-069]accident, and that it got into his hands he didn't know how, and that

[057-070]when he used it he thought it was his."

[057-071]"That's queer," said Johnny.

[057-072]"He is very odd, you know."

[057-073]"But it's a kind of oddity that they don't like at the assizes."

[057-074]"The great cruelty is," said the major, "that whatever may be the

[057-075]result, the punishment will fall so heavily upon his wife and

[057-076]daughters. I think the whole county ought to come forward and take

[057-077]them by the hand. Well, good-by. I'll drive on, as I'm a little in a

[057-078]hurry."

[057-079]"Good-by," said Johnny. "I'm very glad to have had the pleasure of

[057-080]meeting you." "He's a good sort of a fellow after all," he said to

[057-081]himself when the gig had passed on. "He wouldn't have talked in that

[057-082]way if he had meant to hang back."

[057-083]CHAPTER XXXII.

[057-084]MR. TOOGOOD.

[057-085][Illustration]

[057-086]Mr. Crawley had declared to Mr. Robarts, that he would summon no

[057-087]legal aid to his assistance at the coming trial. The reader may,

[057-088]perhaps, remember the impetuosity with which he rejected the advice

[057-089]on this subject which was conveyed to him by Mr. Robarts with all the

[057-090]authority of Archdeacon Grantly's name. "Tell the archdeacon," he had

[057-091]said, "that I will have none of his advice." And then Mr. Robarts had

[057-092]left him, fully convinced that any further interference on his part

[057-093]could be of no avail. Nevertheless, the words which had then been

[057-094]spoken were not without effect. This coming trial was ever present to

[057-095]Mr. Crawley's mind, and though, when driven to discuss the subject,

[057-096]he would speak of it with high spirit, as he had done both to the

[057-097]bishop and to Mr. Robarts, yet in his long hours of privacy, or when

[057-098]alone with his wife, his spirit was anything but high. "It will kill

[057-099]me," he would say to her. "I shall get salvation thus. Death will

[057-100]relieve me, and I shall never be called upon to stand before those

[057-101]cruel eager eyes." Then would she try to say words of comfort,

[057-102]sometimes soothing him as though he were a child, and at others

[057-103]bidding him be a man, and remember that as a man he should have

[057-104]sufficient endurance to bear the eyes of any crowd that might be

[057-105]there to look at him.

[057-106]"I think I will go up to London," he said to her one evening, very

[057-107]soon after the day of Mr. Robarts's visit.

[057-108]"Go up to London, Josiah!" Mr. Crawley had not been up to London

[057-109]once since they had been settled at Hogglestock, and this sudden

[057-110]resolution on his part frightened his wife. "Go up to London,

[057-111]dearest! and why?"

[057-112]"I will tell you why. They all say that I should speak to some man of

[057-113]the law whom I may trust about this coming trial. I trust no one in

[057-114]these parts. Not, mark you, that I say that they are untrustworthy.

[057-115]God forbid that I should so speak or even so think of men whom I

[057-116]know not. But the matter has become so common in men's mouths at

[057-117]Barchester and at Silverbridge, that I cannot endure to go among

[057-118]them and to talk of it. I will go up to London, and I will see your

[057-119]cousin, Mr. John Toogood, of Gray's Inn." Now in this scheme there

[057-120]was an amount of everyday prudence which startled Mrs. Crawley almost

[057-121]as much as did the prospect of the difficulties to be overcome if the

[057-122]journey were to be made. Her husband, in the first place, had never

[057-123]once seen Mr. John Toogood; and in days very long back, when he and

[057-124]she were making their first gallant struggle,--for in those days it

[057-125]had been gallant,--down in their Cornish curacy, he had reprobated

[057-126]certain Toogood civilities,--professional civilities,--which had been

[057-127]proffered, perhaps, with too plain an intimation that on the score

[057-128]of relationship the professional work should be done without payment.

[057-129]The Mr. Toogood of those days, who had been Mrs. Crawley's uncle,

[057-130]and the father of Mrs. Eames and grandfather of our friend Johnny

[057-131]Eames, had been much angered by some correspondence which had grown

[057-132]up between him and Mr. Crawley, and from that day there had been a

[057-133]cessation of all intercourse between the families. Since those days

[057-134]that Toogood had been gathered to the ancient Toogoods of old, and

[057-135]the son reigned on the family throne in Raymond's Buildings. The

[057-136]present Toogood was therefore first-cousin to Mrs. Crawley. But

[057-137]there had been no intimacy between them. Mrs. Crawley had not seen

[057-138]her cousin since her marriage,--as indeed she had seen none of her

[057-139]relations, having been estranged from them by the singular bearing of

[057-140]her husband. She knew that her cousin stood high in his profession,

[057-141]the firm of Toogood and Crump,--Crump and Toogood it should have been

[057-142]properly called in these days,--having always held its head up high

[057-143]above all dirty work; and she felt that her husband could look for

[057-144]advice from no better source. But how would such a one as he manage

[057-145]to tell his story to a stranger? Nay, how would he find his way alone

[057-146]into the lawyer's room, to tell his story at all,--so strange was he

[057-147]to the world? And then the expense! "If you do not wish me to apply

[057-148]to your cousin, say so, and there shall be an end of it," said Mr.

[057-149]Crawley in an angry tone.

[057-150]"Of course I would wish it. I believe him to be an excellent man, and

[057-151]a good lawyer."

[057-152]"Then why should I not go to his chambers? In formâ pauperis I must

[057-153]go to him, and must tell him so. I cannot pay him for the labour of

[057-154]his counsel, nor for such minutes of his time as I shall use."

[057-155]"Oh, Josiah, you need not speak of that."

[057-156]"But I must speak of it. Can I go to a professional man, who keeps

[057-157]as it were his shop open for those who may think fit to come, and

[057-158]purchase of him, and take of his goods, and afterwards, when the

[057-159]goods have been used, tell him that I have not the price in my hand?

[057-160]I will not do that, Mary. You think that I am mad, that I know not

[057-161]what I do. Yes,--I see it in your eyes; and you are sometimes partly

[057-162]right. But I am not so mad but that I know what is honest. I will

[057-163]tell your cousin that I am sore straitened, and brought down into

[057-164]the very dust by misfortune. And I will beseech him, for what of

[057-165]ancient feeling of family he may bear to you, to listen to me for a

[057-166]while. And I will be very short, and, if need be, will bide his time

[057-167]patiently, and perhaps he may say a word to me that may be of use."

[057-168]There was certainly very much in this to provoke Mrs. Crawley. It

[057-169]was not only that she knew well that her cousin would give ample

[057-170]and immediate attention, and lend himself thoroughly to the matter

[057-171]without any idea of payment,--but that she could not quite believe

[057-172]that her husband's humility was true humility. She strove to believe

[057-173]it, but knew that she failed. After all it was only a feeling on her

[057-174]part. There was no argument within herself about it. An unpleasant

[057-175]taste came across the palate of her mind, as such a savour will

[057-176]sometimes, from some unexpected source, come across the palate of the

[057-177]mouth. Well; she could only gulp at it, and swallow it and excuse it.

[057-178]Among the salad that comes from your garden a bitter leaf will now

[057-179]and then make its way into your salad-bowl. Alas, there were so many

[057-180]bitter leaves ever making their way into her bowl! "What I mean is,

[057-181]Josiah, that no long explanation will be needed. I think, from what I

[057-182]remember of him, that he would do for us anything that he could do."
