[009-001]"It should be an example to us, that we should look to our own steps,

[009-002]my dear," said the bishop.

[009-003]"That's all very well," said Mrs. Proudie, "but it has become your

[009-004]duty, and mine too, to look to the steps of other people; and that

[009-005]duty we must do."

[009-006]"Of course, my dear; of course." That was the tone in which the

[009-007]question of Mr. Crawley's alleged guilt was discussed at the palace.

[009-008]We have already heard what was said on the subject at the house of

[009-009]Archdeacon Grantly. As the days passed by, and as other tidings

[009-010]came in, confirmatory of those which had before reached him, the

[009-011]archdeacon felt himself unable not to believe in the man's guilt.

[009-012]And the fear which he entertained as to his son's intended marriage

[009-013]with Grace Crawley, tended to increase the strength of his belief.

[009-014]Dr. Grantly had been a very successful man in the world, and on all

[009-015]ordinary occasions had been able to show that bold front with which

[009-016]success endows a man. But he still had his moments of weakness, and

[009-017]feared greatly lest anything of misfortune should touch him, and mar

[009-018]the comely roundness of his prosperity. He was very wealthy. The

[009-019]wife of his bosom had been to him all that a wife should be. His

[009-020]reputation in the clerical world stood very high. He had lived all

[009-021]his life on terms of equality with the best of the gentry around

[009-022]him. His only daughter had made a splendid marriage. His two sons

[009-023]had hitherto done well in the world, not only as regarded their

[009-024]happiness, but as to marriage also, and as to social standing. But

[009-025]how great would be the fall if his son should at last marry the

[009-026]daughter of a convicted thief! How would the Proudies rejoice over

[009-027]him,--the Proudies who had been crushed to the ground by the success

[009-028]of the Hartletop alliance; and how would the low-church curates who

[009-029]swarmed in Barsetshire, gather together and scream in delight over

[009-030]his dismay! "But why should we say that he is guilty?" said Mrs.

[009-031]Grantly.

[009-032]"It hardly matters as far as we are concerned, whether they find him

[009-033]guilty or not," said the archdeacon; "if Henry marries that girl my

[009-034]heart will be broken."

[009-035]But perhaps to no one except to the Crawleys themselves had the

[009-036]matter caused so much terrible anxiety as to the archdeacon's son. He

[009-037]had told his father that he had made no offer of marriage to Grace

[009-038]Crawley, and he had told the truth. But there are perhaps few men who

[009-039]make such offers in direct terms without having already said and done

[009-040]that which make such offers simply necessary as the final closing of

[009-041]an accepted bargain. It was so at any rate between Major Grantly and

[009-042]Miss Crawley, and Major Grantly acknowledged to himself that it was

[009-043]so. He acknowledged also to himself that as regarded Grace herself

[009-044]he had no wish to go back from his implied intentions. Nothing that

[009-045]either his father or mother might say would shake him in that. But

[009-046]could it be his duty to bind himself to the family of a convicted

[009-047]thief? Could it be right that he should disgrace his father and his

[009-048]mother and his sister and his one child by such a connection? He

[009-049]had a man's heart, and the poverty of the Crawleys caused him no

[009-050]solicitude. But he shrank from the contamination of a prison.

[009-051]CHAPTER VI.

[009-052]GRACE CRAWLEY.

[009-053]It has already been said that Grace Crawley was at this time

[009-054]living with the two Miss Prettymans, who kept a girls' school at

[009-055]Silverbridge. Two more benignant ladies than the Miss Prettymans

[009-056]never presided over such an establishment. The younger was fat,

[009-057]and fresh, and fair, and seemed to be always running over with the

[009-058]milk of human kindness. The other was very thin and very small, and

[009-059]somewhat afflicted with bad health;--was weak, too, in the eyes, and

[009-060]subject to racking headaches, so that it was considered generally

[009-061]that she was unable to take much active part in the education of

[009-062]the pupils. But it was considered as generally that she did all the

[009-063]thinking, that she knew more than any other woman in Barsetshire, and

[009-064]that all the Prettyman schemes for education emanated from her mind.

[009-065]It was said, too, by those who knew them best, that her sister's

[009-066]good-nature was as nothing to hers; that she was the most charitable,

[009-067]the most loving, and the most conscientious of schoolmistresses.

[009-068]This was Miss Annabella Prettyman, the elder; and perhaps it may be

[009-069]inferred that some portion of her great character for virtue may have

[009-070]been due to the fact that nobody ever saw her out of her own house.

[009-071]She could not even go to church, because the open air brought on

[009-072]neuralgia. She was therefore perhaps taken to be magnificent, partly

[009-073]because she was unknown. Miss Anne Prettyman, the younger, went about

[009-074]frequently to tea-parties,--would go, indeed, to any party to which

[009-075]she might be invited; and was known to have a pleasant taste for

[009-076]pound-cake and sweet-meats. Being seen so much in the outer world,

[009-077]she became common, and her character did not stand so high as did

[009-078]that of her sister. Some people were ill-natured enough to say that

[009-079]she wanted to marry Mr. Winthrop; but of what maiden lady that goes

[009-080]out into the world are not such stories told? And all such stories in

[009-081]Silverbridge were told with special reference to Mr. Winthrop.

[009-082]Miss Crawley, at present, lived with the Miss Prettymans, and

[009-083]assisted them in the school. This arrangement had been going on for

[009-084]the last twelve months, since the time in which Grace would have left

[009-085]the school in the natural course of things. There had been no bargain

[009-086]made, and no intention that Grace should stay. She had been invited

[009-087]to fill the place of an absent superintendent, first for one month,

[009-088]then for another, and then for two more months; and when the

[009-089]assistant came back, the Miss Prettymans thought there were reasons

[009-090]why Grace should be asked to remain a little longer. But they took

[009-091]great care to let the fashionable world of Silverbridge know that

[009-092]Grace Crawley was a visitor with them, and not a teacher. "We pay

[009-093]her no salary, or anything of that kind," said Miss Anne Prettyman;

[009-094]a statement, however, which was by no means true, for during those

[009-095]four months the regular stipend had been paid to her; and twice since

[009-096]then, Miss Annabella Prettyman, who managed all the money matters,

[009-097]had called Grace into her little room, and had made a little speech,

[009-098]and had put a little bit of paper into her hand. "I know I ought

[009-099]not to take it," Grace had said to her friend Anne. "If I was not

[009-100]here, there would be no one in my place." "Nonsense, my dear," Anne

[009-101]Prettyman had said; "it is the greatest comfort to us in the world.

[009-102]And you should make yourself nice, you know, for his sake. All the

[009-103]gentlemen like it." Then Grace had been very angry, and had sworn

[009-104]that she would give the money back again. Nevertheless, I think she

[009-105]did make herself as nice as she knew how to do. And from all this it

[009-106]may be seen that the Miss Prettymans had hitherto quite approved of

[009-107]Major Grantly's attentions.

[009-108]But when this terrible affair came on about the cheque which had been

[009-109]lost and found and traced to Mr. Crawley's hands, Miss Anne Prettyman

[009-110]said nothing further to Grace Crawley about Major Grantly. It was not

[009-111]that she thought that Mr. Crawley was guilty, but she knew enough of

[009-112]the world to be aware that suspicion of such guilt might compel such

[009-113]a man as Major Grantly to change his mind. "If he had only popped,"

[009-114]Anne said to her sister, "it would have been all right. He would

[009-115]never have gone back from his word." "My dear," said Annabella, "I

[009-116]wish you would not talk about popping. It is a terrible word." "I

[009-117]shouldn't, to any one except you," said Anne.

[009-118]There had come to Silverbridge some few months since, on a visit to

[009-119]Mrs. Walker, a young lady from Allington, in the neighbouring county,

[009-120]between whom and Grace Crawley there had grown up from circumstances

[009-121]a warm friendship. Grace had a cousin in London,--a clerk high up and

[009-122]well-to-do in a public office, a nephew of her mother's,--and this

[009-123]cousin was, and for years had been, violently smitten in love for

[009-124]this young lady. But the young lady's tale had been sad, and though

[009-125]she acknowledged feelings of most affectionate friendship for the

[009-126]cousin, she could not bring herself to acknowledge more. Grace

[009-127]Crawley had met the young lady at Silverbridge, and words had been

[009-128]spoken about the cousin; and though the young lady from Allington was

[009-129]some years older than Grace, there had grown up to be a friendship,

[009-130]and, as is not uncommon between young ladies, there had been an

[009-131]agreement that they would correspond. The name of the lady was Miss

[009-132]Lily Dale, and the name of the well-to-do cousin in London was Mr.

[009-133]John Eames.

[009-134]At the present moment Miss Dale was at home with her mother at

[009-135]Allington, and Grace Crawley in her terrible sorrow wrote to her

[009-136]friend, pouring out her whole heart. As Grace's letter and Miss

[009-137]Dale's answer will assist us in our story, I will venture to give

[009-138]them both.

[009-139]Silverbridge, -- December, 186--.

[009-140]DEAREST LILY,

[009-141]I hardly know how to tell you what has happened, it is so

[009-142]very terrible. But perhaps you will have heard it already,

[009-143]as everybody is talking of it here. It has got into the

[009-144]newspapers, and therefore it cannot be kept secret. Not

[009-145]that I should keep anything from you; only this is so very

[009-146]dreadful that I hardly know how to write it. Somebody

[009-147]says,--a Mr. Soames, I believe it is,--that papa has taken

[009-148]some money that does not belong to him, and he is to be

[009-149]brought before the magistrates and tried. Of course, papa

[009-150]has done nothing wrong. I do think he would be the last

[009-151]man in the world to take a penny that did not belong to

[009-152]him. You know how poor he is; what a life he has had! But

[009-153]I think he would almost sooner see mamma starving;--I am

[009-154]sure he would rather be starved himself, than even borrow

[009-155]a shilling which he could not pay. To suppose that he

[009-156]would take money [she had tried to write the word "steal,"

[009-157]but she could not bring her pen to form the letters] is

[009-158]monstrous. But, somehow, the circumstances have been made

[009-159]to look bad against him, and they say that he must come

[009-160]over here to the magistrates. I often think that of all

[009-161]men in the world papa is the most unfortunate. Everything

[009-162]seems to go against him, and yet he is so good! Poor mamma

[009-163]has been over here, and she is distracted. I never saw

[009-164]her so wretched before. She had been to your friend, Mr.

[009-165]Walker, and came to me afterwards for a minute. Mr. Walker

[009-166]has got something to do with it, though mamma says she

[009-167]thinks he is quite friendly to papa. I wonder whether

[009-168]you could find out, through Mr. Walker, what he thinks

[009-169]about it. Of course, mamma knows that papa has done

[009-170]nothing wrong; but she says that the whole thing is most

[009-171]mysterious, and that she does not know how to account for

[009-172]the money. Papa, you know, is not like other people. He

[009-173]forgets things; and is always thinking, thinking, thinking

[009-174]of his great misfortunes. Poor papa! My heart bleeds so

[009-175]when I remember all his sorrows, that I hate myself for

[009-176]thinking about myself.

[009-177]When mamma left me,--and it was then I first knew that

[009-178]papa would really have to be tried,--I went to Miss

[009-179]Annabella, and told her that I would go home. She asked me

[009-180]why, and I said I would not disgrace her house by staying

[009-181]in it. She got up and took me in her arms, and there came

[009-182]a tear out of both her dear old eyes, and she said that if

[009-183]anything evil came to papa,--which she would not believe,

[009-184]as she knew him to be a good man,--there should be a

[009-185]home in her house not only for me, but for mamma and

[009-186]Jane. Isn't she a wonderful woman? When I think of her,

[009-187]I sometimes think that she must be an angel already.

[009-188]Then she became very serious,--for just before, through

[009-189]her tears, she had tried to smile,--and she told me to

[009-190]remember that all people could not be like her, who had

[009-191]nobody to look to but herself and her sister; and that at

[009-192]present I must task myself not to think of that which I

[009-193]had been thinking of before. She did not mention anybody's

[009-194]name, but of course I understood very well what she meant;

[009-195]and I suppose she is right. I said nothing in answer to

[009-196]her, for I could not speak. She was holding my hand, and I

[009-197]took hers up and kissed it, to show her, if I could, that

[009-198]I knew that she was right; but I could not have spoken

[009-199]about it for all the world. It was not ten days since

[009-200]that she herself, with all her prudence, told me that she

[009-201]thought I ought to make up my mind what answer I would

[009-202]give him. And then I did not say anything; but of course

[009-203]she knew. And after that Miss Anne spoke quite freely

[009-204]about it, so that I had to beg her to be silent even

[009-205]before the girls. You know how imprudent she is. But it

[009-206]is all over now. Of course Miss Annabella is right. He

[009-207]has got a great many people to think of; his father and

[009-208]mother, and his darling little Edith, whom he brought here

[009-209]twice, and left her with us once for two days, so that

[009-210]she got to know me quite well; and I took such a love for

[009-211]her, that I could not bear to part with her. But I think

[009-212]sometimes that all our family are born to be unfortunate,

[009-213]and then I tell myself that I will never hope for anything

[009-214]again.
