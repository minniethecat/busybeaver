[065-001]He went through the garden and over the lawn belonging to the Small

[065-002]House and saw no one. He forgot, I think, that ladies do not come

[065-003]out to pick roses when the ground is frozen, and that croquet is not

[065-004]often in progress with the hoar-frost on the grass. So he walked

[065-005]up to the little terrace before the drawing-room, and looking in

[065-006]saw Mrs. Dale, and Lily, and Grace at their morning work. Lily was

[065-007]drawing, and Mrs. Dale was writing, and Grace had her needle in her

[065-008]hand. As it happened, no one at first perceived him, and he had time

[065-009]to feel that after all he would have managed better if he had been

[065-010]announced in the usual way. As, however, it was now necessary that

[065-011]he should announce himself, he knocked at the window, and they all

[065-012]immediately looked up and saw him. "It's my cousin John," said Grace.

[065-013]"Oh, Johnny, how are you at last?" said Mrs. Dale. But it was Lily

[065-014]who, without speaking, opened the window for him, who was the first

[065-015]to give him her hand, and who led him through into the room.

[065-016]"It's a great shame my coming in this way," said John, "and letting

[065-017]all the cold air in upon you."

[065-018]"We shall survive it," said Mrs. Dale. "I suppose you have just come

[065-019]down from my brother-in-law?"

[065-020]"No; I have not seen the squire as yet. I will do so before I go

[065-021]back, of course. But it seemed such a commonplace sort of thing to go

[065-022]round by the village."

[065-023]"We are very glad to see you, by whatever way you come;--are we not,

[065-024]mamma?" said Lily.

[065-025]"I'm not so sure of that. We were only saying yesterday that as you

[065-026]had been in the country a fortnight without coming to us, we did not

[065-027]think we would be at home when you did come."

[065-028]"But I have caught you, you see," said Johnny.

[065-029]And so they went on, chatting of old times and of mutual friends very

[065-030]comfortably for full an hour. And there was some serious conversation

[065-031]about Grace's father and his affairs, and John declared his opinion

[065-032]that Mr. Crawley ought to go to his uncle, Thomas Toogood, not at all

[065-033]knowing at that time that Mr. Crawley himself had come to the same

[065-034]opinion. And John gave them an elaborate description of Sir Raffle

[065-035]Buffle, standing up with his back to the fire with his hat on his

[065-036]head, and speaking with a loud harsh voice, to show them the way in

[065-037]which he declared that that gentleman received his inferiors; and

[065-038]then bowing and scraping and rubbing his hands together and simpering

[065-039]with would-be softness,--declaring that after that fashion Sir Raffle

[065-040]received his superiors. And they were very merry,--so that no one

[065-041]would have thought that Johnny was a despondent lover, now bent on

[065-042]throwing the dice for his last stake; or that Lily was aware that she

[065-043]was in the presence of one lover, and that she was like to fall to

[065-044]the ground between two stools,--having two lovers, neither of whom

[065-045]could serve her turn.

[065-046]"How can you consent to serve him if he's such a man as that?" said

[065-047]Lily, speaking of Sir Raffle.

[065-048]"I do not serve him. I serve the Queen,--or rather the public. I

[065-049]don't take his wages, and he does not play his tricks with me. He

[065-050]knows that he can't. He has tried it, and has failed. And he only

[065-051]keeps me where I am because I've had some money left me. He thinks it

[065-052]fine to have a private secretary with a fortune. I know that he tells

[065-053]people all manner of lies about it, making it out to be five times

[065-054]as much as it is. Dear old Huffle Snuffle. He is such an ass; and

[065-055]yet he's had wit enough to get to the top of the tree, and to keep

[065-056]himself there. He began the world without a penny. Now he has got a

[065-057]handle to his name, and he'll live in clover all his life. It's very

[065-058]odd, isn't it, Mrs. Dale?"

[065-059]"I suppose he does his work?"

[065-060]"When men get so high as that, there's no knowing whether they work

[065-061]or whether they don't. There isn't much for them to do, as far as I

[065-062]can see. They have to look beautiful, and frighten the young ones."

[065-063]"And does Sir Raffle look beautiful?" Lily asked.

[065-064]"After a fashion, he does. There is something imposing about such a

[065-065]man till you're used to it, and can see through it. Of course it's

[065-066]all padding. There are men who work, no doubt. But among the bigwigs,

[065-067]and bishops and cabinet ministers, I fancy that the looking beautiful

[065-068]is the chief part of it. Dear me, you don't mean to say it's luncheon

[065-069]time?"

[065-070]But it was luncheon time, and not only had he not as yet said a word

[065-071]of all that which he had come to say, but had not as yet made any

[065-072]move towards getting it said. How was he to arrange that Lily

[065-073]should be left alone with him? Lady Julia had said that she should

[065-074]not expect him back till dinner-time, and he had answered her

[065-075]lackadaisically, "I don't suppose I shall be there above ten minutes.

[065-076]Ten minutes will say all I've got to say, and do all I've got to do.

[065-077]And then I suppose I shall go and cut names about upon bridges,--eh,

[065-078]Lady Julia?" Lady Julia understood his words; for once, upon a former

[065-079]occasion, she had found him cutting Lily's name on the rail of a

[065-080]wooden bridge in her brother's grounds. But he had now been a couple

[065-081]of hours at the Small House, and had not said a word of that which he

[065-082]had come to say.

[065-083]"Are you going to walk out with us after lunch?" said Lily.

[065-084]"He will have had walking enough," said Mrs. Dale.

[065-085]"We'll convoy him back part of the way," said Lily.

[065-086]"I'm not going yet," said Johnny, "unless you turn me out."

[065-087]"But we must have our walk before it is dark," said Lily.

[065-088]"You might go up with him to your uncle," said Mrs. Dale. "Indeed,

[065-089]I promised to go up myself, and so did you, Grace, to see the

[065-090]microscope. I heard Mr. Dale give orders that one of those

[065-091]long-legged reptiles should be caught on purpose for your

[065-092]inspection."

[065-093]Mrs. Dale's little scheme for bringing the two together was very

[065-094]transparent, but it was not the less wise on that account. Schemes

[065-095]will often be successful, let them be ever so transparent. Little

[065-096]intrigues become necessary, not to conquer unwilling people, but

[065-097]people who are willing enough, who, nevertheless, cannot give way

[065-098]except under the machinations of an intrigue.

[065-099]"I don't think I'll mind looking at the long-legged creature to-day,"

[065-100]said Johnny.

[065-101]"I must go, of course," said Grace.

[065-102]Lily said nothing at the moment, either about the long-legged

[065-103]creature or the walk. That which must be, must be. She knew well why

[065-104]John Eames had come there. She knew that the visits to his mother

[065-105]and to Lady Julia would never have been made, but that he might have

[065-106]this interview. And he had a right to demand, at any rate, as much as

[065-107]that. That which must be, must be. And therefore when both Mrs. Dale

[065-108]and Grace stoutly maintained their purpose of going up to the squire,

[065-109]Lily neither attempted to persuade John to accompany them, nor said

[065-110]that she would do so herself.

[065-111]"I will convoy you home myself," she said, "and Grace, when she has

[065-112]done with the beetle, shall come and meet me. Won't you, Grace?"

[065-113]"Certainly."

[065-114]"We are not helpless young ladies in these parts, nor yet timorous,"

[065-115]continued Lily. "We can walk about without being afraid of ghosts,

[065-116]robbers, wild bulls, young men, or gipsies. Come the field path,

[065-117]Grace. I will go as far as the big oak with him, and then I shall

[065-118]turn back, and I shall come in by the stile opposite the church gate,

[065-119]and through the garden. So you can't miss me."

[065-120]"I daresay he'll come back with you," said Grace.

[065-121]"No, he won't. He will do nothing of the kind. He'll have to go on

[065-122]and open Lady Julia's bottle of port wine for his own drinking."

[065-123]All this was very good on Lily's part, and very good also on the part

[065-124]of Mrs. Dale; and John was of course very much obliged to them. But

[065-125]there was a lack of romance in it all, which did not seem to him

[065-126]to argue well as to his success. He did not think much about it,

[065-127]but he felt that Lily would not have been so ready to arrange their

[065-128]walk had she intended to yield to his entreaty. No doubt in these

[065-129]latter days plain good sense had become the prevailing mark of

[065-130]her character,--perhaps, as Johnny thought, a little too strongly

[065-131]prevailing; but even with all her plain good sense and determination

[065-132]to dispense with the absurdities of romance in the affairs of her

[065-133]life, she would not have proposed herself as his companion for a

[065-134]walk across the fields merely that she might have an opportunity

[065-135]of accepting his hand. He did not say all this to himself, but he

[065-136]instinctively felt that it was so. And he felt also that it should

[065-137]have been his duty to arrange the walk, or the proper opportunity for

[065-138]the scene that was to come. She had done it instead,--she and her

[065-139]mother between them, thereby forcing upon him a painful conviction

[065-140]that he himself had not been equal to the occasion. "I always make a

[065-141]mull of it," he said to himself, when the girls went up to get their

[065-142]hats.

[065-143]They went down together through the garden, and parted where the

[065-144]paths led away, one to the great house and the other towards the

[065-145]church. "I'll certainly come and call upon the squire before I go

[065-146]back to London," said Johnny.

[065-147]"We'll tell him so," said Mrs. Dale. "He would be sure to hear that

[065-148]you had been with us, even if we said nothing about it."

[065-149]"Of course he would," said Lily; "Hopkins has seen him." Then they

[065-150]separated, and Lily and John Eames were together.

[065-151]Hardly a word was said, perhaps not a word, till they had crossed the

[065-152]road and got into the field opposite to the church. And in this first

[065-153]field there was more than one path, and the children of the village

[065-154]were often there, and it had about it something of a public nature.

[065-155]John Eames felt that it was by no means a fitting field to say that

[065-156]which he had to say. In crossing it, therefore, he merely remarked

[065-157]that the day was very fine for walking. Then he added one special

[065-158]word, "And it is so good of you, Lily, to come with me."

[065-159]"I am very glad to come with you. I would do more than that, John,

[065-160]to show how glad I am to see you." Then they had come to the second

[065-161]little gate, and beyond that the fields were really fields, and there

[065-162]were stiles instead of wicket-gates, and the business of the day must

[065-163]be begun.

[065-164]"Lily, whenever I come here you say you are glad to see me?"

[065-165]"And so I am,--very glad. Only you would take it as meaning what it

[065-166]does not mean, I would tell you, that of all my friends living away

[065-167]from the reach of my daily life, you are the one whose coming is ever

[065-168]the most pleasant to me."

[065-169]"Oh, Lily!"

[065-170]"It was, I think, only yesterday that I was telling Grace that you

[065-171]are more like a brother to me than any one else. I wish it might be

[065-172]so. I wish we might swear to be brother and sister. I'd do more for

[065-173]you then than walk across the fields with you to Guestwick Cottage.

[065-174]Your prosperity would then be the thing in the world for which I

[065-175]should be most anxious. And if you should marry--"
