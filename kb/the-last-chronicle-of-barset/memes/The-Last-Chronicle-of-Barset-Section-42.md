[042-001]Nothing was said about it at lunch, and nothing when they walked out

[042-002]after lunch, for Lily was very patient. But during the walk Mrs. Dale

[042-003]became aware that her daughter was uneasy. These two watched each

[042-004]other unconsciously with a closeness which hardly allowed a glance of

[042-005]the eye, certainly not a tone of the voice, to pass unobserved. To

[042-006]Mrs. Dale it was everything in the world that her daughter should be,

[042-007]if not happy at heart, at least tranquil; and to Lily, who knew that

[042-008]her mother was always thinking of her, and of her alone, her mother

[042-009]was the only human divinity now worthy of adoration. But nothing was

[042-010]said about the letter during the walk.

[042-011]When they came home it was nearly dusk, and it was their habit to

[042-012]sit up for a while without candles, talking, till the evening had in

[042-013]truth set in and the unmistakable and enforced idleness of remaining

[042-014]without candles was apparent. During this time, Lily, demanding

[042-015]patience of herself all the while, was thinking what she would do, or

[042-016]rather what she would say, about the letter. That nothing could be

[042-017]done or said in the presence of Grace Crawley was a matter of course,

[042-018]nor would she do or say anything to get rid of Grace. She would be

[042-019]very patient; but she would, at last, ask her mother about the

[042-020]letter.

[042-021]And then, as luck would have it, Grace Crawley got up and left the

[042-022]room. Lily still waited for a few minutes, and, in order that her

[042-023]patience might be thoroughly exercised, she said a word or two about

[042-024]her sister Bell; how the eldest child's whooping-cough was nearly

[042-025]well, and how the baby was doing wonderful things with its first

[042-026]tooth. But as Mrs. Dale had already seen Bell's letter, all this was

[042-027]not intensely interesting. At last Lily came to the point and asked

[042-028]her question. "Mamma, from whom was that other letter which you got

[042-029]this morning?"

[042-030]Our story will perhaps be best told by communicating the letter to

[042-031]the reader before it was discussed with Lily. The letter was as

[042-032]follows:--

[042-033]General Committee Office, -- January, 186--.

[042-034]I should have said that Mrs. Dale had not opened the letter till she

[042-035]had found herself in the solitude of her own bedroom; and that then,

[042-036]before doing so, she had examined the handwriting with anxious eyes.

[042-037]When she first received it she thought she knew the writer, but was

[042-038]not sure. Then she had glanced at the impression over the fastening,

[042-039]and had known at once from whom the letter had come. It was from Mr.

[042-040]Crosbie, the man who had brought so much trouble into her house, who

[042-041]had jilted her daughter; the only man in the world whom she had a

[042-042]right to regard as a positive enemy to herself. She had no doubt

[042-043]about it, as she tore the envelope open; and yet, when the address

[042-044]given made her quite sure, a new feeling of shivering came upon her,

[042-045]and she asked herself whether it might not be better that she should

[042-046]send his letter back to him without reading it. But she read it.

[042-047]"MADAM," the letter began,--

[042-048]You will be very much surprised to hear from me, and I

[042-049]am quite aware that I am not entitled to the ordinary

[042-050]courtesy of an acknowledgment from you, should you be

[042-051]pleased to throw my letter on one side as unworthy of your

[042-052]notice. But I cannot refrain from addressing you, and must

[042-053]leave it to you to reply to me or not, as you may think

[042-054]fit.

[042-055]I will only refer to that episode of my life with which

[042-056]you are acquainted, for the sake of acknowledging my great

[042-057]fault and of assuring you that I did not go unpunished. It

[042-058]would be useless for me now to attempt to explain to you

[042-059]the circumstances which led me into that difficulty which

[042-060]ended in so great a blunder; but I will ask you to believe

[042-061]that my folly was greater than my sin.

[042-062]But I will come to my point at once. You are, no doubt,

[042-063]aware that I married a daughter of Lord De Courcy, and

[042-064]that I was separated from my wife a few weeks after our

[042-065]unfortunate marriage. It is now something over twelve

[042-066]months since she died at Baden-Baden in her mother's

[042-067]house. I never saw her since the day we first parted. I

[042-068]have not a word to say against her. The fault was mine in

[042-069]marrying a woman whom I did not love and had never loved.

[042-070]When I married Lady Alexandrina I loved, not her, but your

[042-071]daughter.

[042-072]I believe I may venture to say to you that your daughter

[042-073]once loved me. From the day on which I last wrote to you

[042-074]that terrible letter which told you of my fate, I have

[042-075]never mentioned the name of Lily Dale to human ears. It

[042-076]has been too sacred for my mouth,--too sacred for the

[042-077]intercourse of any friendship with which I have been

[042-078]blessed. I now use it for the first time to you, in order

[042-079]that I may ask whether it be possible that her old love

[042-080]should ever live again. Mine has lived always,--has never

[042-081]faded for an hour, making me miserable during the years

[042-082]that have passed since I saw her, but capable of making me

[042-083]very happy, if I may be allowed to see her again.

[042-084]You will understand my purpose now as well as though I

[042-085]were to write pages. I have no scheme formed in my head

[042-086]for seeing your daughter again. How can I dare to form a

[042-087]scheme, when I am aware that the chance of success must be

[042-088]so strong against me? But if you will tell me that there

[042-089]can be a gleam of hope, I will obey any commands that

[042-090]you can put upon me in any way that you may point out. I

[042-091]am free again,--and she is free. I love her with all my

[042-092]heart, and seem to long for nothing in the world but that

[042-093]she should become my wife. Whether any of her old love may

[042-094]still abide with her, you will know. If it do, it may even

[042-095]yet prompt her to forgive one who, in spite of falseness

[042-096]of conduct, has yet been true to her in heart.

[042-097]I have the honour to be, Madam,

[042-098]Your most obedient servant,

[042-099]ADOLPHUS CROSBIE.

[042-100]This was the letter which Mrs. Dale had received, and as to which she

[042-101]had not as yet said a word to Lily, or even made up her mind whether

[042-102]she would say a word or not. Dearly as the mother and daughter loved

[042-103]each other, thorough as was the confidence between them, yet the

[042-104]name of Adolphus Crosbie had not been mentioned between them oftener,

[042-105]perhaps, than half-a-dozen times since the blow had been struck.

[042-106]Mrs. Dale knew that their feelings about the man were altogether

[042-107]different. She, herself, not only condemned him for what he had done,

[042-108]believing it to be impossible that any shadow of excuse could be

[042-109]urged for his offence, thinking that the fault had shown the man to

[042-110]be mean beyond redemption,--but she had allowed herself actually to

[042-111]hate him. He had in one sense murdered her daughter, and she believed

[042-112]that she could never forgive him. But Lily, as her mother well knew,

[042-113]had forgiven this man altogether, had made excuses for him which

[042-114]cleansed his sin of all its blackness in her own eyes, and was to

[042-115]this day anxious as ever for his welfare and his happiness. Mrs. Dale

[042-116]feared that Lily did in truth love him still. If it was so, was she

[042-117]not bound to show her this letter? Lily was old enough to judge for

[042-118]herself,--old enough, and wise enough too. Mrs. Dale told herself

[042-119]half-a-score of times that morning that she could not be justified in

[042-120]keeping the letter from her daughter.

[042-121]But yet she much wished that the letter had never been written,

[042-122]and would have given very much to be able to put it out of the way

[042-123]without injustice to Lily. To her thinking it would be impossible

[042-124]that Lily should be happy in marrying such a man. Such a marriage

[042-125]now would be, as Mrs. Dale thought, a degradation to her daughter.

[042-126]A terrible injury had been done to her; but such reparation as this

[042-127]would, in Mrs. Dale's eyes, only make the injury deeper. And yet Lily

[042-128]loved the man; and, loving him, how could she resist the temptation

[042-129]of his offer? "Mamma, from whom was that letter which you got this

[042-130]morning?" Lily asked. For a few moments Mrs. Dale remained silent.

[042-131]"Mamma," continued Lily, "I think I know whom it was from. If you

[042-132]tell me to ask nothing further, of course I will not."

[042-133]"No, Lily; I cannot tell you that."

[042-134]"Then, mamma, out with it at once. What is the use of shivering on

[042-135]the brink?"

[042-136]"It was from Mr. Crosbie."

[042-137]"I knew it. I cannot tell you why, but I knew it. And now, mamma;--am

[042-138]I to read it?"

[042-139]"You shall do as you please, Lily."

[042-140]"Then I please to read it."

[042-141]"Listen to me a moment first. For myself, I wish that the letter had

[042-142]never been written. It tells badly for the man, as I think of it. I

[042-143]cannot understand how any man could have brought himself to address

[042-144]either you or me, after having acted as he acted."

[042-145]"But, mamma, we differ about all that, you know."

[042-146]"Now he has written, and there is the letter,--if you choose to read

[042-147]it."

[042-148]Lily had it in her hand, but she still sat motionless, holding it.

[042-149]"You think, mamma, I ought not to read it?"

[042-150]"You must judge for yourself, dearest."

[042-151]"And if I do not read it, what shall you do, mamma?"

[042-152]"I shall do nothing;--or, perhaps, I should in such a case

[042-153]acknowledge it, and tell him that we have nothing more to say to

[042-154]him."

[042-155]"That would be very stern."

[042-156]"He has done that which makes some sternness necessary."

[042-157]Then Lily was again silent, and still she sat motionless, with the

[042-158]letter in her hand. "Mamma," she said, at last, "if you tell me not

[042-159]to read it, I will give it you back unread. If you bid me exercise my

[042-160]own judgment, I shall take it upstairs and read it."

[042-161]"You must exercise your own judgment," said Mrs. Dale. Then Lily got

[042-162]up from her chair and walked slowly out of the room, and went to her

[042-163]mother's chamber. The thoughts which passed through Mrs. Dale's mind

[042-164]while her daughter was reading the letter were very sad. She could

[042-165]find no comfort anywhere. Lily, she told herself, would surely give

[042-166]way to this man's renewed expressions of affection, and she, Mrs.

[042-167]Dale herself, would be called upon to give her child to a man whom

[042-168]she could neither love nor respect;--whom, for aught she knew, she

[042-169]could never cease to hate. And she could not bring herself to believe

[042-170]that Lily would be happy with such a man. As for her own life,

[042-171]desolate as it would be,--she cared little for that. Mothers know

[042-172]that their daughters will leave them. Even widowed mothers, mothers

[042-173]with but one child left,--such a one as was this mother,--are aware

[042-174]that they will be left alone, and they can bring themselves to

[042-175]welcome the sacrifice of themselves with something of satisfaction.

[042-176]Mrs. Dale and Lily had, indeed, of late become bound together

[042-177]especially, so that the mother had been justified in regarding the

[042-178]link which joined them as being firmer than that by which most

[042-179]daughters are bound to their mothers;--but in all that she would have

[042-180]found no regret. Even now, in these very days, she was hoping that

[042-181]Lily might yet be brought to give herself to John Eames. But she

[042-182]could not, after all that was come and gone, be happy in thinking

[042-183]that Lily should be given to Adolphus Crosbie.
