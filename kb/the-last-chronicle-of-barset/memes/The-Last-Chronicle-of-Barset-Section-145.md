[145-001]"His resignation means nothing," said Thumble.

[145-002]"It means a great deal," said Snapper; "and the duties must be

[145-003]provided for."

[145-004]"I won't provide for them," said Thumble; "and so you may tell the

[145-005]bishop." In these days Mr. Thumble was very angry with the bishop,

[145-006]for the bishop had not yet seen him since the death of Mrs. Proudie.

[145-007]Mr. Snapper had no alternative but to go to the bishop. The bishop

[145-008]in these days was very mild to those whom he saw, given but to few

[145-009]words, and a little astray,--as though he had had one of his limbs

[145-010]cut off,--as Mr. Snapper expressed it to Mrs. Snapper. "I shouldn't

[145-011]wonder if he felt as though all his limbs were cut off," said Mrs.

[145-012]Snapper; "you must give him time, and he'll come round by-and-by."

[145-013]I am inclined to think that Mrs. Snapper's opinion of the bishop's

[145-014]feelings and condition was correct. In his difficulty respecting

[145-015]Hogglestock and Mr. Thumble Mr. Snapper went to the bishop, and spoke

[145-016]perhaps a little harshly of Mr. Thumble.

[145-017]"I think, upon the whole, Snapper, that you had better go yourself,"

[145-018]said the bishop.

[145-019]"Do you think so, my lord?" said Snapper. "It will be inconvenient."

[145-020]"Everything is inconvenient; but you'd better go. And look here,

[145-021]Snapper, if I were you, I wouldn't say anything out at Hogglestock

[145-022]about the cheque. We don't know what it may come to yet." Mr.

[145-023]Snapper, with a heavy heart, left his patron, not at all liking the

[145-024]task that was before him. But his wife encouraged him to be obedient.

[145-025]He was the owner of a one-horse carriage, and the work was not,

[145-026]therefore, so hard to him as it would have been and had been to poor

[145-027]Mr. Thumble. And, moreover, his wife promised to go with him. Mr.

[145-028]Snapper and Mrs. Snapper did go over to Hogglestock, and the duty

[145-029]was done. Mrs. Snapper spoke a word or two to Mrs. Crawley, and Mr.

[145-030]Snapper spoke a word or two to Mr. Crawley; but not a word was said

[145-031]about the new news as to Mr. Soames's cheque, which were now almost

[145-032]current in Barchester. Indeed, no whisper about it had as yet reached

[145-033]Hogglestock.

[145-034]"One word with you, reverend sir," said Mr. Crawley to the chaplain,

[145-035]as the latter was coming out of the church, "as to the parish work,

[145-036]sir, during the week;--I should be glad if you would favour me with

[145-037]your opinion."

[145-038]"[[About]] what, Mr. Crawley?"

[145-039]"Whether you think that I may be allowed, without scandal, to visit

[145-040]the sick,--and to give instruction in the school."

[145-041]"Surely;--surely, Mr. Crawley. Why not?"

[145-042]"Mr. Thumble gave me to understand that the bishop was very

[145-043]urgent that I should interfere in no way in the ministrations

[145-044]of the parish. Twice did he enjoin on me that I should not

[145-045]interfere,--unnecessarily, as it seemed to me."

[145-046]"Quite unnecessary," said Mr. Snapper. "And the bishop will be

[145-047]obliged to you, Mr. Crawley, if you'll just see that the things go on

[145-048]all straight."

[145-049]"I wish it were possible to know with accuracy what his idea of

[145-050]straightness is," said Mr. Crawley to his wife. "It may be that

[145-051]things are straight to him when they are buried as it were out of

[145-052]sight, and put away without trouble. I hope it be not so with the

[145-053]bishop." When he went into his school and remembered,--as he did

[145-054]remember through every minute of his teaching--that he was to receive

[145-055]no portion of the poor stipend which was allotted for the clerical

[145-056]duties of the parish, he told himself that there was gross injustice

[145-057]in the way in which things were being made straight at Hogglestock.

[145-058]But we must go back to the major and to the archdeacon at

[145-059]Plumstead,--in which comfortable parish things were generally made

[145-060]straight more easily than at Hogglestock. Henry Grantly went over

[145-061]from Barchester to Plumstead in a gig from the "Dragon," and made

[145-062]his way at once into his father's study. The archdeacon was seated

[145-063]there with sundry manuscripts before him, and with one half-finished

[145-064]manuscript,--as was his wont on every Saturday morning. "Halloo,

[145-065]Harry," he said. "I didn't expect you in the least." It was barely an

[145-066]hour since he had told Mrs. Grantly that his complaint against his

[145-067]son was that he wouldn't come and make himself comfortable at the

[145-068]rectory.

[145-069]"Father," said he, giving the archdeacon his hand, "you have heard

[145-070]nothing yet about Mr. Crawley?"

[145-071]"No," said the archdeacon jumping up; "nothing new;--what is it?"

[145-072]Many ideas about Mr. Crawley at that moment flitted across the

[145-073]archdeacon's mind. Could it be that the unfortunate man had committed

[145-074]suicide, overcome by his troubles?

[145-075]"It has all come out. He got the cheque from my aunt."

[145-076]"From your aunt Eleanor?"

[145-077]"Yes; from my aunt Eleanor. She has telegraphed over from Venice

[145-078]to say that she gave the identical cheque to Crawley. That is all

[145-079]we know at present,--except that she has written an account of the

[145-080]matter to you, and that she will be here herself as quick as she can

[145-081]come."

[145-082]"Who got the message, Henry?"

[145-083]"Crawley's lawyer,--a fellow named Toogood, a cousin of his

[145-084]wife's;--a very decent fellow," added the major, remembering how

[145-085]necessary it was that he should reconcile his father to all the

[145-086]Crawley belongings. "He's to be over here on Monday, and then will

[145-087]arrange what is to be done."

[145-088]"Done in what way, Henry?"

[145-089]"There's a great deal to be done yet. Crawley does not know himself

[145-090]at this moment how the cheque got into his hands. He must be told,

[145-091]and something must be settled about the living. They've taken the

[145-092]living away from him among them. And then the indictment must be

[145-093]quashed, or something of that kind done. Toogood has got hold of the

[145-094]scoundrel at Barchester who really stole the cheque from Soames;--or

[145-095]thinks that he has. It's that Dan Stringer."

[145-096]"He's got hold of a regular scamp then. I never knew any good of Dan

[145-097]Stringer," said the archdeacon.

[145-098]Then Mrs. Grantly was told, and the whole story was repeated again,

[145-099]with many expressions of commiseration in reference to all the

[145-100]Crawleys. The archdeacon did not join in these at first, being rather

[145-101]shy on that head. It was very hard for him to have to speak to his

[145-102]son about the Crawleys as though they were people in all respects

[145-103]estimable and well-conducted, and satisfactory. Mrs. Grantly

[145-104]understood this so well, that every now and then she said some

[145-105]half-laughing word respecting Mr. Crawley's peculiarities, feeling

[145-106]that in this way she might ease her husband's difficulties. "He must

[145-107]be the oddest man that ever lived," said Mrs. Grantly, "not to have

[145-108]known where he got the cheque." The archdeacon shook his head, and

[145-109]rubbed his hands as he walked about the room. "I suppose too much

[145-110]learning has upset him," said the archdeacon. "They say he's not very

[145-111]good at talking English, but put him on in Greek and he never stops."

[145-112]The archdeacon was perfectly aware that he had to admit Mr. Crawley

[145-113]to his goodwill, and that as for Grace Crawley,--it was essentially

[145-114]necessary that she should be admitted to his heart of hearts. He had

[145-115]promised as much. It must be acknowledged that Archdeacon Grantly

[145-116]always kept his promises, and especially such promises as these. And

[145-117]indeed it was the nature of the man that when he had been very angry

[145-118]with those he loved, he should be unhappy until he had found some

[145-119]escape from his anger. He could not endure to have to own himself

[145-120]to have been in the wrong, but he could be content with a very

[145-121]incomplete recognition of his having been in the right. The posters

[145-122]had been pulled down and Mr. Crawley, as he was now told, had not

[145-123]stolen the cheque. That was sufficient. If his son would only drink

[145-124]a glass or two of wine with him comfortably, and talk dutifully

[145-125]about the Plumstead foxes, all should be held to be right, and

[145-126]Grace Crawley should be received with lavish paternal embraces. The

[145-127]archdeacon had kissed Grace once, and felt that he could do so again

[145-128]without an unpleasant strain upon his feelings.

[145-129]"Say something to your father about the property after dinner," said

[145-130]Mrs. Grantly to her son when they were alone together.

[145-131]"About what property?"

[145-132]"About this property, or any property; you know what I

[145-133]mean;--something to show that you are interested about his affairs.

[145-134]He is doing the best he can to make things right." After dinner, over

[145-135]the claret, Mr. Thorne's terrible sin in reference to the trapping

[145-136]of foxes was accordingly again brought up, and the archdeacon became

[145-137]beautifully irate, and expressed his animosity,--which he did not in

[145-138]the least feel,--against an old friend with an energy which would

[145-139]have delighted his wife, if she could have heard him. "I shall tell

[145-140]Thorne my mind, certainly. He and I are very old friends; we have

[145-141]known each other all our lives; but I cannot put up with this kind

[145-142]of thing,--and I will not. It's all because he's afraid of his own

[145-143]gamekeeper." And yet the archdeacon had never ridden after a fox in

[145-144]his life, and never meant to do so. Nor had he in truth been always

[145-145]so very anxious that foxes should be found in his covers. That fox

[145-146]which had been so fortunately trapped just outside the Plumstead

[145-147]property afforded a most pleasant escape for the steam of his anger.

[145-148]When he began to talk to his wife that evening about Mr. Thorne's

[145-149]wicked gamekeeper, she was so sure that all was right, that she said

[145-150]a word of her extreme desire to see Grace Crawley.

[145-151]"If he is to marry her, we might as well have her over here," said

[145-152]the archdeacon.

[145-153]"That's just what I was thinking," said Mrs. Grantly. And thus things

[145-154]at the rectory got themselves arranged.

[145-155]On the Sunday morning the expected letter from Venice came to

[145-156]hand, and was read on that morning very anxiously, not only by Mrs.

[145-157]Grantly and the major, but by the archdeacon also, in spite of the

[145-158]sanctity of the day. Indeed the archdeacon had been very stoutly

[145-159]anti-sabbatarial when the question of stopping the Sunday post to

[145-160]Plumstead had been mooted in the village, giving those who on that

[145-161]occasion were the special friends of the postman to understand that

[145-162]he considered them to be numskulls, and little better than idiots.

[145-163]The postman, finding the parson to be against him, had seen that

[145-164]there was no chance for him, and had allowed the matter to drop. Mrs.

[145-165]Arabin's letter was long and eager, and full of repetitions, but it

[145-166]did explain clearly to them the exact manner in which the cheque had

[145-167]found its way into Mr. Crawley's hand. "Francis came up to me," she

[145-168]said in her letter,--Francis being her husband, the dean,--"and asked

[145-169]me for the money, which I had promised to make up in a packet. The

[145-170]packet was not ready, and he would not wait, declaring that Mr.

[145-171]Crawley was in such a flurry that he did not like to leave him. I

[145-172]was therefore to bring it down to the door. I went to my desk, and

[145-173]thinking that I could spare the twenty pounds as well as the fifty, I

[145-174]put the cheque into the envelope, together with the notes, and handed

[145-175]the packet to Francis at the door. I think I told Francis afterwards

[145-176]that I put seventy pounds into the envelope, instead of fifty, but of

[145-177]this I will not be sure. _At any rate, Mr. Crawley got Mr. Soames's

[145-178]cheque from me._" These last words she underscored, and then went on

[145-179]to explain how the cheque had been paid to her a short time before by

[145-180]Dan Stringer.

[145-181]"Then Toogood has been right about the fellow," said the archdeacon.

[145-182]"I hope they'll hang him," said Mrs. Grantly. "He must have known all

[145-183]the time what dreadful misery he was bringing upon this unfortunate

[145-184]family."
