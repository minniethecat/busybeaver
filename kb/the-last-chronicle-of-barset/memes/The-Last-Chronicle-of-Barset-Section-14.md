[014-001]"Your bidding shall be done," she said,--"of course."

[014-002]"Tell them, then, that they must seek me here if they want me."

[014-003]"But, Josiah, think of the parish,--of the people who respect

[014-004]you,--for their sakes let it not be said that you were taken away by

[014-005]policemen."

[014-006]"Was St. Paul not bound in prison? Did he think of what the people

[014-007]might see?"

[014-008]"If it were necessary, I would encourage you to bear it without a

[014-009]murmur."

[014-010]"It is necessary, whether you murmur, or do not murmur. Murmur,

[014-011]indeed! Why does not your voice ascend to heaven with one loud wail

[014-012]against the cruelty of man?" Then he went forth from the room into an

[014-013]empty chamber on the other side of the passage; and his wife, when

[014-014]she followed him there after a few minutes, found him on his knees,

[014-015]with his forehead against the floor, and with his hands clutching at

[014-016]the scanty hairs of his head. Often before had she seen him so, on

[014-017]the same spot, half grovelling, half prostrate in prayer, reviling

[014-018]in his agony all things around him,--nay, nearly all things above

[014-019]him,--and yet striving to reconcile himself to his Creator by the

[014-020]humiliation of confession.

[014-021]It might be better with him now, if only he could bring himself to

[014-022]some softness of heart. Softly she closed the door, and placing the

[014-023]candle on the mantel-shelf, softly she knelt beside him, and softly

[014-024]touched his hand with hers. He did not stir nor utter a word, but

[014-025]seemed to clutch at his thin locks more violently than before. Then

[014-026]she kneeling there, aloud, but with low voice, with her thin hands

[014-027]clasped, uttered a prayer in which she asked her God to remove from

[014-028]her husband the bitterness of that hour. He listened till she had

[014-029]finished, and then he rose slowly to his feet. "It is in vain," said

[014-030]he. "It is all in vain. It is all in vain." Then he returned back to

[014-031]the parlour, and seating himself again in the arm-chair, remained

[014-032]there without speaking till past midnight. At last, when she told him

[014-033]that she herself was very cold, and reminded him that for the last

[014-034]hour there had been no fire, still speechless, he went up with her to

[014-035]their bed.

[014-036]Early on the following morning she contrived to let him know that she

[014-037]was about to send a neighbour's son over with a note to Mr. Walker,

[014-038]fearing to urge him further to change his mind; but hoping that he

[014-039]might express his purpose of doing so when he heard that the letter

[014-040]was to be sent; but he took no notice whatever of her words. At this

[014-041]moment he was reading Greek with his daughter, or rather rebuking her

[014-042]because she could not be induced to read Greek.

[014-043]"Oh, papa," the poor girl said, "don't scold me now. I am so unhappy

[014-044]because of all this."

[014-045]"And am not I unhappy?" he said, as he closed the book. "My God,

[014-046]what have I done against thee, that my lines should be cast in such

[014-047]terrible places?"

[014-048]The letter was sent to Mr. Walker. "He knows himself to be innocent,"

[014-049]said the poor wife, writing what best excuse she knew how to make,

[014-050]"and thinks that he should take no step himself in such a matter. He

[014-051]will not employ a lawyer, and he says that he should prefer that he

[014-052]should be sent for, if the law requires his presence at Silverbridge

[014-053]on Thursday." All this she wrote, as though she felt that she ought

[014-054]to employ a high tone in defending her husband's purpose; but she

[014-055]broke down altogether in the few words of the postscript. "Indeed,

[014-056]indeed I have done what I could!" Mr. Walker understood it all, both

[014-057]the high tone and the subsequent fall.

[014-058]On the Thursday morning, at about ten o'clock, a fly stopped at the

[014-059]gate of the Hogglestock Parsonage, and out of it there came two

[014-060]men. One was dressed in ordinary black clothes, and seemed from his

[014-061]bearing to be a respectable man of the middle class of life. He

[014-062]was, however, the superintendent of police for the Silverbridge

[014-063]district. The other man was a policeman, pure and simple, with the

[014-064]helmet-looking hat which has lately become common, and all the

[014-065]ordinary half-military and wholly disagreeable outward adjuncts of

[014-066]the profession. "Wilkins," said the superintendent, "likely enough I

[014-067]shall want you, for they tell me the gent is uncommon strange. But if

[014-068]I don't call you when I come out, just open the door like a servant,

[014-069]and mount up on the box when we're in. And don't speak nor say

[014-070]nothing." Then the senior policeman entered the house.

[014-071]He found Mrs. Crawley sitting in the parlour with her bonnet and

[014-072]shawl on, and Mr. Crawley in the arm-chair, leaning over the fire.

[014-073]"I suppose we had better go with you," said Mrs. Crawley directly the

[014-074]door was opened; for of course she had seen the arrival of the fly

[014-075]from the window.

[014-076]"The gentleman had better come with us if he'll be so kind," said

[014-077]Thompson. "I've brought a close carriage for him."

[014-078]"But I may go with him?" said the wife, with frightened voice. "I may

[014-079]accompany my husband. He is not well, sir, and wants assistance."

[014-080]Thompson thought about it for a moment before he spoke. There was

[014-081]room in the fly for only two, or if for three, still he knew his

[014-082]place better than to thrust himself inside together with his prisoner

[014-083]and his prisoner's wife. He had been specially asked by Mr. Walker

[014-084]to be very civil. Only one could sit on the box with the driver, and

[014-085]if the request was conceded the poor policeman must walk back. The

[014-086]walk, however, would not kill the policeman. "All right, ma'am," said

[014-087]Thompson;--"that is, if the gentleman will just pass his word not to

[014-088]get out till I ask him."

[014-089]"He will not! He will not!" said Mrs. Crawley.

[014-090]"I will pass my word for nothing," said Mr. Crawley.

[014-091]Upon hearing this, Thompson assumed a very long face, and shook his

[014-092]head as he turned his eyes first towards the husband and then towards

[014-093]the wife, and shrugged his shoulders, and compressing his lips, blew

[014-094]out his breath, as though in this way he might blow off some of the

[014-095]mingled sorrow and indignation with which the gentleman's words

[014-096]afflicted him.

[014-097]Mrs. Crawley rose and came close to him. "You may take my word for

[014-098]it, he will not stir. You may indeed. He thinks it incumbent on him

[014-099]not to give any undertaking himself, because he feels himself to be

[014-100]so harshly used."

[014-101]"I don't know about harshness," said Thompson, brindling up. "A close

[014-102]carriage brought, and--"

[014-103]"I will walk. If I am made to go, I will walk," shouted Mr. Crawley.

[014-104]"I did not allude to you,--or to Mr. Walker," said the poor wife.

[014-105]"I know you have been most kind. I meant the harshness of the

[014-106]circumstances. Of course he is innocent, and you must feel for him."

[014-107]"Yes, I feel for him, and for you too, ma'am."

[014-108]"That is all I meant. He knows his own innocence, and therefore he is

[014-109]unwilling to give way in anything."

[014-110]"Of course he knows hisself, that's certain. But he'd better come in

[014-111]the carriage, if only because of the dirt and slush."

[014-112]"He will go in the carriage; and I will go with him. There will be

[014-113]room there for you, sir."

[014-114]Thompson looked up at the rain, and told himself that it was very

[014-115]cold. Then he remembered Mr. Walker's injunction, and bethought

[014-116]himself that Mrs. Crawley, in spite of her poverty, was a lady. He

[014-117]conceived even unconsciously the idea that something was due to her

[014-118]because of her poverty. "I'll go with the driver," said he, "but

[014-119]he'll only give hisself a deal of trouble if he attempts to get out."

[014-120]"He won't; he won't," said Mrs. Crawley. "And I thank you with all my

[014-121]heart."

[014-122]"Come along, then," said Thompson.

[014-123]She went up to her husband, hat in hand, and looking round to see

[014-124]that she was not watched, put the hat on his head, and then lifted

[014-125]him as it were from his chair. He did not refuse to be led, and

[014-126]allowed her to throw round his shoulders the old cloak which was

[014-127]hanging in the passage, and then he passed out, and was the first

[014-128]to seat himself in the Silverbridge fly. His wife followed him, and

[014-129]did not hear the blandishments with which Thompson instructed his

[014-130]myrmidon to follow through the mud on foot. Slowly they made their

[014-131]way through the lanes, and it was nearly twelve when the fly was

[014-132]driven into the yard of the "George and Vulture" at Silverbridge.

[014-133]Silverbridge, though it was blessed with a mayor and corporation,

[014-134]and was blessed also with a Member of Parliament all to itself, was

[014-135]not blessed with any court-house. The magistrates were therefore

[014-136]compelled to sit in the big room at the "George and Vulture," in

[014-137]which the county balls were celebrated, and the meeting of the West

[014-138]Barsetshire freemasons was held. That part of the country was, no

[014-139]doubt, very much ashamed of its backwardness in this respect, but

[014-140]as yet nothing had been done to remedy the evil. Thompson and his

[014-141]fly were therefore driven into the yard of the Inn, and Mr. and

[014-142]Mrs. Crawley were ushered by him up into a little bed-chamber close

[014-143]adjoining to the big room in which the magistrates were already

[014-144]assembled. "There's a bit of fire here," said Thompson, "and you can

[014-145]make yourselves a little warm." He himself was shivering with the

[014-146]cold. "When the gents is ready in there, I'll just come and fetch

[014-147]you."

[014-148]"I may go in with him?" said Mrs. Crawley.

[014-149]"I'll have a chair for you at the end of the table, just nigh to

[014-150]him," said Thompson. "You can slip into it and say nothing to

[014-151]nobody." Then he left them and went away to the magistrates.

[014-152]Mr. Crawley had not spoken a word since he had entered the vehicle.

[014-153]Nor had she said much to him, but had sat with him holding his hand

[014-154]in hers. Now he spoke to her,--"Where is it that we are?" he asked.

[014-155]"At Silverbridge, dearest."

[014-156]"But what is this chamber? And why are we here?"

[014-157]"We are to wait here till the magistrates are ready. They are in the

[014-158]next room."

[014-159]"But this is the Inn?"

[014-160]"Yes, dear, it is the Inn."

[014-161]"And I see crowds of people about." There were crowds of people

[014-162]about. There had been men in the yard, and others standing about on

[014-163]the stairs, and the public room was full of men who were curious to

[014-164]see the clergyman who had stolen twenty pounds, and to hear what

[014-165]would be the result of the case before the magistrates. He must be

[014-166]committed; so, at least, said everybody; but then there would be the

[014-167]question of bail. Would the magistrates let him out on bail, and who

[014-168]would be the bailsmen? "Why are the people here?" said Mr. Crawley.

[014-169]"I suppose it is the custom when the magistrates are sitting," said

[014-170]his wife.

[014-171]"They have come to see the degradation of a clergyman," said

[014-172]he;--"and they will not be disappointed."

[014-173]"Nothing can degrade but guilt," said his wife.

[014-174]"Yes,--misfortune can degrade, and poverty. A man is degraded when

[014-175]the cares of the world press so heavily upon him that he cannot rouse

[014-176]himself. They have come to look at me as though I were a hunted

[014-177]beast."

[014-178]"It is but their custom always on such days."

[014-179]"They have not always a clergyman before them as a criminal." Then he

[014-180]was silent for a while, while she was chafing his cold hands. "Would

[014-181]that I were dead, before they had brought me to this! Would that I

[014-182]were dead!"
