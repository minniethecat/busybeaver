## Character

Dr. Tempest is the rector of Silverbridge and the rural dean of the district, a clergyman of considerable standing in the Barsetshire establishment. He is described as "a very old man" (064-039) and is known for his measured, unhurried approach to all matters. When Bishop Proudie attempts to rush the ecclesiastical investigation of Josiah Crawley, it is Dr. Tempest to whom the case is referred — and his deliberate pace becomes a source of intense frustration to the bishop and Mrs. Proudie. "Dr. Tempest would take a long time over" the investigation, the bishop hoped in vain (064-067), but Tempest's slowness proves to be a form of quiet resistance to episcopal pressure.

## Biography

Dr. Tempest first appears as one of the magistrates at the preliminary hearing of the Crawley case in Silverbridge, where he sits at "the left hand of the chairman" alongside Dr. Thorne (015-076). His role expands significantly when the bishop, at Mrs. Proudie's urging, decides to pursue ecclesiastical proceedings against Crawley and delegates the inquiry to Tempest as rural dean. "The letter was to explain to Dr. Tempest that the bishop" had determined to act (064-047), and the commission places Tempest in an uncomfortable position between episcopal authority and his fellow clergyman. Tempest's instinct is to delay, to consult, to avoid precipitate action — an approach that Mrs. Proudie finds maddening but that ultimately serves the cause of justice. "Dr. Tempest merely shook his head" when pushed for a quick decision (016-021), embodying the Barsetshire clergy's resistance to the Proudies' reformist zeal.

## Significance

Dr. Tempest represents the institutional counterweight to episcopal overreach in Trollope's Barsetshire. Where Bishop Proudie and his wife seek to impose their will through formal authority, Tempest embodies an older, slower model of church governance — one based on consultation, local knowledge, and a reluctance to judge hastily. His deliberate handling of the Crawley inquiry ensures that the matter proceeds at a pace that allows for the truth to emerge through the civil courts before any ecclesiastical penalty is imposed. In this, he is an ally to Crawley without ever explicitly taking his side, demonstrating how institutional inertia can sometimes serve justice better than zealous reform.
