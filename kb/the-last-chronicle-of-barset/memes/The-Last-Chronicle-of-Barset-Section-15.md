[015-001]"Is it not right, dear, that we should all bear what He sends us?"

[015-002]"Would that I were dead!" he repeated. "The load is too heavy for me

[015-003]to bear, and I would that I were dead!"

[015-004]The time seemed to be very long before Thompson returned and asked

[015-005]them to accompany him into the big room. When he did so, Mr. Crawley

[015-006]grasped hold of his chair as though he had resolved that he would not

[015-007]go. But his wife whispered a word to him, and he obeyed her. "He will

[015-008]follow me," she said to the policeman. And in that way they went from

[015-009]the small room into the large one. Thompson went first; Mrs. Crawley

[015-010]with her veil down came next; and the wretched man followed his wife,

[015-011]with his eyes fixed upon the ground and his hands clasped together

[015-012]upon his breast. He could at first have seen nothing, and could

[015-013]hardly have known where he was when they placed him in a chair. She,

[015-014]with a better courage, contrived to look round through her veil, and

[015-015]saw that there was a long board or table covered with green cloth,

[015-016]and that six or seven gentlemen were sitting at one end of it, while

[015-017]there seemed to be a crowd standing along the sides and about the

[015-018]room. Her husband was seated at the other end of the table, near

[015-019]the corner, and round the corner,--so that she might be close to

[015-020]him,--her chair had been placed. On the other side of him there was

[015-021]another chair, now empty, intended for any professional gentleman

[015-022]whom he might choose to employ.

[015-023]There were five magistrates sitting there. Lord Lufton, from Framley,

[015-024]was in the chair;--a handsome man, still young, who was very popular

[015-025]in the county. The cheque which had been cashed had borne his

[015-026]signature, and he had consequently expressed his intention of not

[015-027]sitting at the board; but Mr. Walker, desirous of having him there,

[015-028]had overruled him, showing him that the loss was not his loss. The

[015-029]cheque, if stolen, had not been stolen from him. He was not the

[015-030]prosecutor. "No, by Jove," said Lord Lufton, "if I could quash the

[015-031]whole thing, I'd do it at once!"

[015-032]"You can't do that, my lord, but you may help us at the board," said

[015-033]Mr. Walker.

[015-034]Then there was the Hon. George De Courcy, Lord De Courcy's brother,

[015-035]from Castle Courcy. Lord De Courcy did not live in the county, but

[015-036]his brother did so, and endeavoured to maintain the glory of the

[015-037]family by the discretion of his conduct. He was not, perhaps, among

[015-038]the wisest of men, but he did very well as a country magistrate,

[015-039]holding his tongue, keeping his eyes open, and, on such occasions as

[015-040]this, obeying Mr. Walker in all things. Dr. Tempest was also there,

[015-041]the rector of the parish, he being both magistrate and clergyman.

[015-042]There were many in Silverbridge who declared that Dr. Tempest would

[015-043]have done far better to stay away when a brother clergyman was

[015-044]thus to be brought before the bench; but it had been long since Dr.

[015-045]Tempest had cared what was said about him in Silverbridge. He had

[015-046]become so accustomed to the life he led as to like to be disliked,

[015-047]and to be enamoured of unpopularity. So when Mr. Walker had ventured

[015-048]to suggest to him that, perhaps, he might not choose to be there, he

[015-049]had laughed Mr. Walker to scorn. "Of course I shall be there," he

[015-050]said. "I am interested in the case,--very much interested. Of course

[015-051]I shall be there." And had not Lord Lufton been present he would have

[015-052]made himself more conspicuous by taking the chair. Mr. Fothergill was

[015-053]the fourth. Mr. Fothergill was man of business to the Duke of Omnium,

[015-054]who was the great owner of property in and about Silverbridge, and he

[015-055]was the most active magistrate in that part of the county. He was a

[015-056]sharp man, and not at all likely to have any predisposition in favour

[015-057]of a clergyman. The fifth was Dr. Thorne, of Chaldicotes, a gentleman

[015-058]whose name has been already mentioned in these pages. He had been

[015-059]for many years a medical man practising in a little village in the

[015-060]further end of the county; but it had come to be his fate, late in

[015-061]life, to marry a great heiress, with whose money the ancient house

[015-062]and domain of Chaldicotes had been purchased from the Sowerbys. Since

[015-063]then Dr. Thorne had done his duty well as a country gentleman,--not,

[015-064]however, without some little want of smoothness between him and the

[015-065]duke's people.

[015-066]Chaldicotes lay next to the duke's territory, and the duke had wished

[015-067]to buy Chaldicotes. When Chaldicotes slipped through the duke's

[015-068]fingers and went into the hands of Dr. Thorne,--or of Dr. Thorne's

[015-069]wife,--the duke had been very angry with Mr. Fothergill. Hence it had

[015-070]come to pass that there had not always been smoothness between the

[015-071]duke's people and the Chaldicotes people. It was now rumoured that

[015-072]Dr. Thorne intended to stand for the county on the next vacancy, and

[015-073]that did not tend to make things smoother. On the right hand of Lord

[015-074]Lufton sat Lord George and Mr. Fothergill, and beyond Mr. Fothergill

[015-075]sat Mr. Walker, and beyond Mr. Walker sat Mr. Walker's clerk. On the

[015-076]left hand of the chairman were Dr. Tempest and Dr. Thorne, and a

[015-077]little lower down was Mr. Zachary Winthrop, who held the situation of

[015-078]clerk to the magistrates. Many people in Silverbridge said that this

[015-079]was all wrong, as Mr. Winthrop was partner with Mr. Walker, who was

[015-080]always employed before the magistrates if there was any employment

[015-081]going for an attorney. For this, however, Mr. Walker cared very

[015-082]little. He had so much of his own way in Silverbridge, that he was

[015-083]supposed to care nothing for anybody.

[015-084]There were many other gentlemen in the room, and some who knew Mr.

[015-085]Crawley with more or less intimacy. He, however, took notice of no

[015-086]one, and when one friend, who had really known him well, came up

[015-087]behind and spoke to him gently leaning over his chair, the poor man

[015-088]hardly recognized his friend.

[015-089]"I'm sure your husband won't forget me," said Mr. Robarts, the

[015-090]clergyman of Framley, as he gave his hand to that lady across the

[015-091]back of Mr. Crawley's chair.

[015-092]"No, Mr. Robarts, he does not forget you. But you must excuse him if

[015-093]at this moment he is not quite himself. It is a trying situation for

[015-094]a clergyman."

[015-095]"I can understand all that; but I'll tell you why I have come. I

[015-096]suppose this inquiry will finish the whole affair, and clear up

[015-097]whatever may be the difficulty. But should it not do so, it may be

[015-098]just possible, Mrs. Crawley, that something may be said about bail. I

[015-099]don't understand much about it, and I daresay you do not either; but

[015-100]if there should be anything of that sort, let Mr. Crawley name me.

[015-101]A brother clergyman will be best, and I'll have some other gentleman

[015-102]with me." Then he left her, not waiting for any answer.

[015-103]At the same time there was a conversation going on between Mr. Walker

[015-104]and another attorney standing behind him, Mr. Mason. "I'll go to

[015-105]him," said Walker, "and try to arrange it." So Mr. Walker seated

[015-106]himself in the empty chair beside Mr. Crawley, and endeavoured to

[015-107]explain to the wretched man, that he would do well to allow Mr. Mason

[015-108]to assist him. Mr. Crawley seemed to listen to all that was said, and

[015-109]then turned upon the speaker sharply: "I will have no one to assist

[015-110]me," he said so loudly that every one in the room heard the words.

[015-111]"I am innocent. Why should I want assistance? Nor have I money to

[015-112]pay for it." Mr. Mason made a quick movement forward, intending to

[015-113]explain that that consideration need offer no impediment, but was

[015-114]stopped by further speech from Mr. Crawley. "I will have no one to

[015-115]help me," said he, standing upright, and for the first time removing

[015-116]his hat from his head. "Go on, and do what it is you have to do."

[015-117]After that he did not sit down till the proceedings were nearly over,

[015-118]though he was invited more than once by Lord Lufton to do so.

[015-119]We need not go through all the evidence that was brought to bear upon

[015-120]the question. It was proved that money for the cheque was paid to Mr.

[015-121]Crawley's messenger, and that this money was given to Mr. Crawley.

[015-122]When there occurred some little delay in the chain of evidence

[015-123]necessary to show that Mr. Crawley had signed and sent the cheque and

[015-124]got the money, he became impatient. "Why do you trouble the man?" he

[015-125]said. "I had the cheque, and I sent him; I got the money. Has any

[015-126]one denied it, that you should strive to drive a poor man like that

[015-127]beyond his wits?" Then Mr. Soames and the manager of the bank showed

[015-128]what inquiry had been made as soon as the cheque came back from the

[015-129]London bank; how at first they had both thought that Mr. Crawley

[015-130]could of course explain the matter, and how he had explained it by

[015-131]a statement which was manifestly untrue. Then there was evidence to

[015-132]prove that the cheque could not have been paid to him by Mr. Soames,

[015-133]and as this was given, Mr. Crawley shook his head and again became

[015-134]impatient. "I erred in that," he exclaimed. "Of course I erred. In my

[015-135]haste I thought it was so, and in my haste I said so. I am not good

[015-136]at reckoning money and remembering sums; but I saw that I had been

[015-137]wrong when my error was shown to me, and I acknowledged at once that

[015-138]I had been wrong."

[015-139]Up to this point he had behaved not only with so much spirit, but

[015-140]with so much reason, that his wife began to hope that the importance

[015-141]of the occasion had brought back the clearness of his mind, and that

[015-142]he would, even now, be able to place himself right as the inquiry

[015-143]went on. Then it was explained that Mr. Crawley had stated that the

[015-144]cheque had been given to him by Dean Arabin, as soon as it was shown

[015-145]that it could not have been given to him by Mr. Soames. In reference

[015-146]to this, Mr. Walker was obliged to explain that application had been

[015-147]made to the dean, who was abroad, and that the dean had stated that

[015-148]he had given fifty pounds to his friend. Mr. Walker explained also

[015-149]that the very notes of which this fifty pounds had consisted had been

[015-150]traced back to Mr. Crawley, and that they had had no connection with

[015-151]the cheque or with the money which had been given for the cheque at

[015-152]the bank.

[015-153]Mr. Soames stated that he had lost the cheque with a pocket-book;

[015-154]that he had certainly lost it on the day on which he had called on

[015-155]Mr. Crawley at Hogglestock; and that he missed his pocket-book on his

[015-156]journey back from Hogglestock to Barchester. At the moment of missing

[015-157]it he remembered that he had taken the book out from his pocket in

[015-158]Mr. Crawley's room, and, at that moment, he had not doubted but

[015-159]that he had left it in Mr. Crawley's house. He had written and

[015-160]sent to Mr. Crawley to inquire, but had been assured that nothing

[015-161]had been found. There had been no other property of value in the

[015-162]pocket-book,--nothing but a few visiting cards and a memorandum, and

[015-163]he had therefore stopped the cheque at the London bank, and thought

[015-164]no more about it.

[015-165]Mr. Crawley was then asked to explain in what way he came possessed

[015-166]of the cheque. The question was first put by Lord Lufton; but it soon

[015-167]fell into Mr. Walker's hands, who certainly asked it with all the

[015-168]kindness with which such an inquiry could be made. Could Mr. Crawley

[015-169]at all remember by what means that bit of paper had come into his

[015-170]possession, or how long he had had it? He answered the last question

[015-171]first. "It had been with him for months." And why had he kept it? He

[015-172]looked round the room sternly, almost savagely, before he answered,

[015-173]fixing his eyes for a moment upon almost every face around him as he

[015-174]did so. Then he spoke. "I was driven by shame to keep it,--and then

[015-175]by shame to use it." That this statement was true, no one in the room

[015-176]doubted.
