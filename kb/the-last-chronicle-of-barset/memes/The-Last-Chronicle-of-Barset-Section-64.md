[064-001]"I believe your lordship has attempted to silence him, and that he

[064-002]has refused to comply."

[064-003]"I thought it better for everybody's sake,--especially for his own,

[064-004]that he should for a while be relieved from his duties; but he is

[064-005]an obstinate man, a very obstinate man. I made the attempt with all

[064-006]consideration for his feelings."

[064-007]"He is hard put to it, my lord. I know the man and his pride. The

[064-008]dean has spoken of him to me more than once, and nobody knows him so

[064-009]well as the dean. If I might venture to offer an opinion--"

[064-010]"Good morning, Mr. Chadwick," said Mrs. Proudie, coming into the room

[064-011]and taking her accustomed seat. "No thank you, no; I will stay away

[064-012]from the fire, if you please. His lordship has spoken to you no doubt

[064-013]about this unfortunate, wretched man?"

[064-014]"We are speaking of him now, my dear."

[064-015]"Something must of course be done to put a stop to the crying

[064-016]disgrace of having such a man preaching from a pulpit in this

[064-017]diocese. When I think of the souls of the people in that poor

[064-018]village, my hair literally stands on end. And then he is

[064-019]disobedient!"

[064-020]"That is the worst of it," said the bishop. "It would have been

[064-021]so much better for himself if he would have allowed me to provide

[064-022]quietly for the services till the trial be over."

[064-023]"I could have told you, my lord, that he would not do that, from what

[064-024]I knew of him," said Mr. Chadwick.

[064-025]"But he must do it," said Mrs. Proudie. "He must be made to do it."

[064-026]"His lordship will find it difficult," said Mr. Chadwick.

[064-027]"I can issue a commission, you know, to the rural dean," said the

[064-028]bishop mildly.

[064-029]"Yes, you can do that. And Dr. Tempest in two months' time will have

[064-030]named his assessors--"

[064-031]"Dr. Tempest must not name them; the bishop must name them," said

[064-032]Mrs. Proudie.

[064-033]"It is customary to leave that to the rural dean," said Mr. Chadwick.

[064-034]"The bishop no doubt can object to any one named."

[064-035]"And can specially select any clergyman he pleases from the

[064-036]archdeaconry," said the bishop. "I have known it done."

[064-037]"The rural dean in such case has probably been an old man, and not

[064-038]active," said the lawyer.

[064-039]"And Dr. Tempest is a very old man," said Mrs. Proudie, "and in such

[064-040]a matter not at all trustworthy. He was one of the magistrates who

[064-041]took bail."

[064-042]"His lordship could hardly set him aside," said the lawyer. "At any

[064-043]rate I would not recommend him to try. I think you might suggest a

[064-044]commission of five, and propose two of the number yourself. I do not

[064-045]think that in such a case Dr. Tempest would raise any question."

[064-046]At last it was settled in this way. Mr. Chadwick was to prepare a

[064-047]letter to Dr. Tempest, for the bishop's signature, in which the

[064-048]doctor should be requested, as the rural dean to whom Mr. Crawley was

[064-049]subject, to hold a commission of five to inquire into Mr. Crawley's

[064-050]conduct. The letter was to explain to Dr. Tempest that the bishop,

[064-051]moved by his solicitude for the souls of the people of Hogglestock,

[064-052]had endeavoured, "in a friendly way," to induce Mr. Crawley to desist

[064-053]from his ministrations; but that having failed through Mr. Crawley's

[064-054]obstinacy, he had no alternative but to proceed in this way. "You had

[064-055]better say that his lordship, as bishop of the diocese, can take no

[064-056]heed of the coming trial," said Mrs. Proudie. "I think his lordship

[064-057]had better say nothing at all about the trial," said Mr. Chadwick. "I

[064-058]think that will be best," said the bishop.

[064-059]"But if they report against him," said Mr. Chadwick, "you can only

[064-060]then proceed in the ecclesiastical court,--at your own expense."

[064-061]"He'll hardly be so obstinate as that," said the bishop.

[064-062]"I'm afraid you don't know him, my lord," said the lawyer. The

[064-063]bishop, thinking of the scene which had taken place in that very room

[064-064]only yesterday, felt that he did know Mr. Crawley, and felt also that

[064-065]the hope which he had just expressed was one in which he himself put

[064-066]no trust. But something might turn up; and it was devoutly to be

[064-067]hoped that Dr. Tempest would take a long time over his inquiry. The

[064-068]assizes might come on as soon as it was terminated, or very shortly

[064-069]afterwards; and then everything might be well. "You won't find Dr.

[064-070]Tempest very ready at it," said Mr. Chadwick. The bishop in his heart

[064-071]was comforted by the words. "But he must be made to be ready to do

[064-072]his duty," said Mrs. Proudie, imperiously. Mr. Chadwick shrugged his

[064-073]shoulders, then got up, spoke his farewell little speeches, and left

[064-074]the palace.

[064-075]CHAPTER XXXV.

[064-076]LILY DALE WRITES TWO WORDS IN HER BOOK.

[064-077]John Eames saw nothing more of Lily Dale till he packed up his

[064-078]portmanteau, left his mother's house, and went to stay for a few days

[064-079]with his old friend Lady Julia; and this did not happen till he had

[064-080]been above a week at Guestwick. Mrs. Dale repeatedly said that it was

[064-081]odd that Johnny did not come to see them; and Grace, speaking of him

[064-082]to Lily, asked why he did not come. Lily, in her funny way, declared

[064-083]that he would come soon enough. But even while she was joking there

[064-084]was something of half-expressed consciousness in her words,--as

[064-085]though she felt it to be foolish to speak of his coming as she

[064-086]might of that of any other young man, before people who knew her

[064-087]whole story. "He'll come quick enough. He knows, and I know, that

[064-088]his coming will do no good. Of course I shall be glad to see him.

[064-089]Why shouldn't I be glad to see him? I've known him and liked

[064-090]him all my life. I liked him when there did not seem to be much

[064-091]about him to like, and now that he is clever, and agreeable, and

[064-092]good-looking,--which he never was as a lad,--why shouldn't I go on

[064-093]liking him? He's more like a brother to me than anybody else I've

[064-094]got. James,"--James was her brother-in-law, Dr. Crofts,--"thinks of

[064-095]nothing but his patients and his babies, and my cousin Bernard is

[064-096]much too grand a person for me to take the liberty of loving him. I

[064-097]shall be very glad to see Johnny Eames." From all which Mrs. Dale was

[064-098]led to believe that Johnny's case was still hopeless. And how should

[064-099]it not be hopeless? Had Lily not confessed within the last week or

[064-100]two that she still loved Adolphus Crosbie?

[064-101]Mrs. Eames also, and Mary, were surprised that John did not go over

[064-102]to Allington. "You haven't seen Mrs. Dale yet, or the squire?" said

[064-103]his mother.

[064-104]"I shall see them when I am at the cottage."

[064-105]"Yes;--no doubt. But it seems strange that you should be here so long

[064-106]without going to them."

[064-107]"There's time enough," said he. "I shall have nothing else to do

[064-108]when I'm at the cottage." Then, when Mary had spoken to him again in

[064-109]private, expressing a hope that there was "nothing wrong," he had

[064-110]been very angry with his sister. "What do you mean by wrong? What

[064-111]rubbish you girls talk! and you never have any delicacy of feeling to

[064-112]make you silent."

[064-113]"Oh, John, don't say such hard things as that of me!"

[064-114]"But I do say them. You'll make me swear among you some day that I

[064-115]will never see Lily Dale again. As it is, I wish I never had seen

[064-116]her,--simply because I am so dunned about it." In all of which I

[064-117]think that Johnny was manifestly wrong. When the humour was on him he

[064-118]was fond enough of talking about Lily Dale. Had he not taught her to

[064-119]do so, I doubt whether his sister would ever have mentioned Lily's

[064-120]name to him. "I did not mean to dun you, John," said Mary, meekly.

[064-121]But at last he went to Lady Julia's, and was no sooner there than he

[064-122]was ready to start for Allington. When Lady Julia spoke to him about

[064-123]Lily, he did not venture to snub her. Indeed, of all his friends,

[064-124]Lady Julia was the one with whom on this subject he allowed himself

[064-125]the most unrestricted confidence. He came over one day, just before

[064-126]dinner, and declared his intention of walking over to Allington

[064-127]immediately after breakfast on the following morning. "It's the last

[064-128]time, Lady Julia," he said.

[064-129]"So you say, Johnny."

[064-130]"And so I mean it! What's the good of a man frittering away his life?

[064-131]What's the good of wishing for what you can't get?"

[064-132]"Jacob was not in such a hurry when he wished for Rachel."

[064-133]"That was all very well for an old patriarch who had seven or eight

[064-134]hundred years to live."

[064-135]"My dear John, you forget your Bible. Jacob did not live half as long

[064-136]as that."

[064-137]"He lived long enough, and slowly enough, to be able to wait fourteen

[064-138]years;--and then he had something to comfort him in the meantime.

[064-139]And after all, Lady Julia, it's more than seven years since I first

[064-140]thought Lily was the prettiest girl I ever saw."

[064-141]"How old are you now?"

[064-142]"Twenty-seven,--and she's twenty-four."

[064-143]"You've time enough yet, if you'll only be patient."

[064-144]"I'll be patient for to-morrow, Lady Julia, but never again. Not

[064-145]that I mean to quarrel with her. I'm not such a fool as to quarrel

[064-146]with a girl because she can't like me. I know how it all is. If that

[064-147]scoundrel had not come across my path just when he did,--in that

[064-148]very nick of time, all might have been right betwixt her and me.

[064-149]I couldn't have offered to marry her before, when I hadn't as much

[064-150]income as would have found her in bread-and-butter. And then, just as

[064-151]better times came to me, he stepped in! I wonder whether it will be

[064-152]expected of me that I should forgive him?"

[064-153]"As far as that goes, you have no right to be angry with him."

[064-154]"But I am,--all the same."

[064-155]"And so was I,--but not for stepping in, as you call it."

[064-156]"You and I are different, Lady Julia. I was angry with him for

[064-157]stepping in; but I couldn't show it. Then he stepped out, and I did

[064-158]manage to show it. And now I shouldn't wonder if he doesn't step in

[064-159]again. After all, why should he have such a power? It was simply the

[064-160]nick of time which gave it to him." That John Eames should be able to

[064-161]find some consolation in this consideration is devoutly to be hoped

[064-162]by us all.

[064-163]There was nothing said about Lily Dale the next morning at breakfast.

[064-164]Lady Julia observed that John was dressed a little more neatly than

[064-165]usual;--though the change was not such as to have called for her

[064-166]special observation, had she not known the business on which he was

[064-167]intent.

[064-168]"You have nothing to send to the Dales?" he said, as he got up from

[064-169]the table.

[064-170]"Nothing but my love, Johnny."

[064-171]"No worsted or embroidery work,--or a pot of special jam for the

[064-172]squire?"

[064-173]"No, sir, nothing; though I should like to make you carry a pair of

[064-174]panniers, if I could."

[064-175]"They would become me well," said Johnny, "for I am going on an ass's

[064-176]errand." Then, without waiting for the word of affection which was on

[064-177]the old woman's lips, he got himself out of the room, and started on

[064-178]his journey.

[064-179]The walk was only three miles and the weather was dry and frosty, and

[064-180]he had come to the turn leading up to the church and the squire's

[064-181]house almost before he remembered that he was near Allington. Here

[064-182]he paused for a moment to think. If he continued his way down by the

[064-183]"Red Lion" and through Allington Street, he must knock at Mrs. Dale's

[064-184]door, and ask for admission by means of the servant,--as would be

[064-185]done by any ordinary visitor. But he could make his way on to the

[064-186]lawn by going up beyond the wall of the churchyard and through the

[064-187]squire's garden. He knew the path well,--very well; and he thought

[064-188]that he might take so much liberty as that, both with the squire and

[064-189]with Mrs. Dale, although his visits to Allington were not so frequent

[064-190]now as they used to be in the days of his boyhood. He did not wish

[064-191]to be admitted by the servant, and therefore he went through the

[064-192]gardens. Luckily he did not see the squire, who would have detained

[064-193]him, and he escaped from Hopkins, the old gardener, with little more

[064-194]than a word. "I'm going down to see the ladies, Hopkins; I suppose I

[064-195]shall find them?" And then, while Hopkins was arranging his spade so

[064-196]that he might lean upon it for a little chat, Johnny was gone and had

[064-197]made his way into the other garden. He had thought it possible that

[064-198]he might meet Lily out among the walks by herself, and such a meeting

[064-199]as this would have suited him better than any other. And as he

[064-200]crossed the little bridge which separated the gardens he thought of

[064-201]more than one such meeting,--of one especial occasion on which he

[064-202]had first ventured to tell her in plain words that he loved her. But

[064-203]before that day Crosbie had come there, and at the moment in which he

[064-204]was speaking of his love she regarded Crosbie as an angel of light

[064-205]upon the earth. What hope could there have been for him then? What

[064-206]use was there in his telling such a tale of love at that time? When

[064-207]he told it, he knew that Crosbie had been before him. He knew that

[064-208]Crosbie was at that moment the angel of light. But as he had never

[064-209]before been able to speak of his love, so was he then unable not to

[064-210]speak of it. He had spoken, and of course had been simply rebuked.

[064-211]Since that day Crosbie had ceased to be an angel of light, and he,

[064-212]John Eames, had spoken often. But he had spoken in vain, and now he

[064-213]would speak once again.
