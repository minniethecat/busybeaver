## Character

Mrs. Dobbs Broughton — Maria to her intimates — is a wealthy, restless London woman who presides over a fashionable drawing-room and harbours a dangerous romantic interest in the young painter Conway Dalrymple. She sits for her portrait "as a Grace" and uses the long hours of sitting to cultivate an intimacy that her husband views with growing jealousy (044-070). Trollope portrays her as a woman of vanity and impulse rather than genuine wickedness — her flirtation with Dalrymple is more about boredom and the desire for admiration than about any serious intention. The chapter title "Mrs. Dobbs Broughton Piles Her Fagots" (001-076) suggests her self-destructive accumulation of romantic complications. When her husband's financial speculation collapses, her social pretensions come crashing down with it.

## Biography

Mrs. Dobbs Broughton is the wife of a City man whose wealth, it eventually emerges, is built on precarious financial speculation. She maintains a fashionable establishment and moves in the London artistic circles that include Conway Dalrymple and John Eames. Her dinner party, noted in the chapter heading "Mrs. Dobbs Broughton's Dinner-party" (001-006), is one of the novel's set-piece social scenes. Her emotional entanglement with Dalrymple intensifies through the novel's middle chapters, culminating in the crisis precipitated by her husband's ruin. When Mr. Dobbs Broughton's financial house collapses — in part through the manipulations of Mr. Musselboro — she is forced to confront the consequences of the life she has led.

## Significance

Mrs. Dobbs Broughton serves as Trollope's portrait of a certain type of London woman — fashionable, idle, and emotionally under-occupied — whose flirtation with artistic bohemianism carries real social risks. Her subplot with Dalrymple provides the novel with its most sustained comedy of manners, balancing the gravity of the Crawley trial. Yet Trollope does not treat her purely as a figure of fun: her eventual fall, when her husband's bankruptcy strips away the material basis of her social position, carries a genuine pathos. "Mrs. Dobbs Broughton Piles Her Fagots" — the chapter title is a classical allusion to gathering fuel for one's own funeral pyre — and she is, in the end, the agent of her own social destruction.
