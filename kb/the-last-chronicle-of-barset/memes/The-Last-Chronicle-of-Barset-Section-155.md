[155-001]But even now, after this great escape, what was to be done? As to

[155-002]Grace, she had felt the absolute necessity of being obedient to her

[155-003]friends,--with the consent of course of her mother,--during the

[155-004]great tribulation of her family. Things were so bad that she had

[155-005]not the heart to make them worse by giving any unnecessary trouble

[155-006]as to herself. Having resolved,--and having made her mother so

[155-007]understand,--that on one point she would guide herself by her own

[155-008]feelings, she was contented to go hither and thither as she was

[155-009]told, and to do as she was bid. Her hope was that Miss Prettyman

[155-010]would allow her to go back to her teaching, but it had come to be

[155-011]understood among them all that nothing was to be said on that subject

[155-012]till the trial should be over. Till that time she would be passive.

[155-013]But then, as I have said, had come the news from Mrs. Arabin, and

[155-014]Grace, with all the others, understood that there would be no trial.

[155-015]When this was known and acknowledged, she declared her purpose of

[155-016]going back to Hogglestock. She would go back at once. When asked both

[155-017]by Lady Lufton and by Mrs. Robarts why she was in so great a haste,

[155-018]she merely said that it must be so. She was, as it were, absolved

[155-019]from her passive obedience to Framley authorities by the diminution

[155-020]of the family misfortunes.

[155-021]Mrs. Robarts understood the feeling by which Grace was hurried away.

[155-022]"Do you know why she is so obstinate?" Lady Lufton asked.

[155-023]"I think I do," said Mrs. Robarts.

[155-024]"And what is it?"

[155-025]"Should Major Grantly renew his offer to her she is under a pledge to

[155-026]accept him now."

[155-027]"Of course he will renew it, and of course she will accept him."

[155-028]"Just so. But she prefers that he should come for her to her own

[155-029]house,--because of its poverty. If he chooses to seek her there, I

[155-030]don't think she will make much difficulty." Lady Lufton demurred to

[155-031]this, not however with anger, and expressed a certain amount of mild

[155-032]displeasure. She did not quite see why Major Grantly should not be

[155-033]allowed to come and do his love-making comfortably, where there was

[155-034]a decent dinner for him to eat, and chairs and tables and sofas and

[155-035]carpets. She said that she thought that something was due to Major

[155-036]Grantly. She was in truth a little disappointed that she was not

[155-037]allowed to have her own way, and to arrange the marriage at Framley

[155-038]under her own eye. But, through it all, she appreciated Grace; and

[155-039]they who knew her well and heard what she said upon the occasion,

[155-040]understood that her favour was not to be withdrawn. All young women

[155-041]were divided by old Lady Lufton into sheep and goats,--very white

[155-042]sheep and very black goats;--and Grace was to be a sheep. Thus it

[155-043]came to pass that Grace Crawley was at home when the dean visited

[155-044]Hogglestock. "Mamma," she said, looking out of the window, "there is

[155-045]the dean with papa at the gate."

[155-046]"It was a narrow squeak--a very narrow squeak," Mr. Crawley had said

[155-047]when his friend congratulated him on his escape. The dean felt at

[155-048]the moment that not for many years had he heard the incumbent of

[155-049]Hogglestock speak either of himself or of anything else with so

[155-050]manifest an attempt at jocularity. Arabin had expected to find the

[155-051]man broken down by the weight of his sorrows, and lo! at the first

[155-052]moment of their first interview he himself began to ridicule them!

[155-053]Crawley having thus alluded to the narrow squeak had asked his

[155-054]visitor to enter the house and see his wife.

[155-055]"Of course I will," said Arabin, "but I will speak just a word to you

[155-056]first." Jane, who had accompanied the dean from the school, now left

[155-057]them, and went into the house to her mother. "My wife cannot forgive

[155-058]herself about the cheque," continued he.

[155-059]"There is nothing to be forgiven," said Mr. Crawley; "nothing."

[155-060]"She feels that what she did was awkward and foolish. She ought never

[155-061]to have paid a cheque away in such a manner. She knows that now."

[155-062]"It was given,--not paid," said Crawley; and as he spoke something

[155-063]of the black cloud came back upon his face. "And I am well aware

[155-064]how hard Mrs. Arabin strove to take away from the alms she bestowed

[155-065]the bitterness of the sting of eleemosynary aid. If you please,

[155-066]Arabin, we will not talk any more of that. I can never forget that

[155-067]I have been a beggar, but I need not make my beggary the matter of

[155-068]conversation. I hope the Holy Land has fulfilled your expectation?"

[155-069]"It has more than done so," said the dean, bewildered by the sudden

[155-070]change.

[155-071]"For myself, it is, of course, impossible that I should ever

[155-072]visit any scenes except those to which my immediate work may call

[155-073]me,--never in this world. The new Jerusalem is still within my

[155-074]reach,--if it be not forfeited by pride and obstinacy; but the old

[155-075]Jerusalem I can never behold. Methinks, because it is so, I would

[155-076]sooner stand with my foot on Mount Olivet, or drink a cup of water

[155-077]in the village of Bethany, than visit any other spot within the

[155-078]traveller's compass. The sources of the Nile, of which men now talk

[155-079]so much,--I see it in the papers and reviews which the ladies at

[155-080]Framley are so good as to send to my wife,--do not interest me much.

[155-081]I have no ambition to climb Mont Blanc or the Matterhorn; Rome makes

[155-082]my mouth water but little, nor even Athens much. I can realize

[155-083]without seeing all that Athens could show me, and can fancy that the

[155-084]existing truth would destroy more than it would build up. But to have

[155-085]stood on Calvary!"

[155-086]"We don't know where Calvary was," said the dean.

[155-087]"I fancy that I should know,--should know enough," said the illogical

[155-088]and unreasonable Mr. Crawley. "Is it true that you can look over

[155-089]from the spot on which He stood as He came across the brow of the

[155-090]hill, and see the huge stones of the Temple placed there by Solomon's

[155-091]men,--as He saw them;--right across the brook Cedron, is it not?"

[155-092]"It is all there, Crawley,--just as your knowledge of it tells you."

[155-093]"In the privilege of seeing those places I can almost envy a man

[155-094]his--money." The last word he uttered after a pause. He had been

[155-095]about to say that under such temptation he could almost envy a man

[155-096]his promotion; but he bethought himself that on such an occasion as

[155-097]this it would be better that he should spare the dean. "And now, if

[155-098]you wish it, we will go in. I fancy that I see my wife at the window,

[155-099]as though she were waiting for us." So saying, he strode on along the

[155-100]little path, and the dean was fain to follow him, even though he had

[155-101]said so little of all that he had intended to say.

[155-102]As soon as he was with Mrs. Crawley he repeated his apology about

[155-103]the cheque, and found himself better able to explain himself than he

[155-104]could do when alone with her husband. "Of course, it has been our

[155-105]fault," he said.

[155-106]"Oh, no," said Mrs. Crawley, "how can you have been in fault when

[155-107]your only object was to do us good?" But, nevertheless, the dean took

[155-108]the blame upon his own shoulders, or, rather upon those of his wife,

[155-109]and declared himself to be responsible for all the trouble about the

[155-110]cheque.

[155-111]"Let it go," said Crawley, after sitting for awhile in silence; "let

[155-112]it pass."

[155-113]"You cannot wonder, Crawley," said the dean, "that I should have felt

[155-114]myself obliged to speak of it."

[155-115]"For the future it will be well that it should be forgotten," said

[155-116]Crawley; "or, if not forgotten, treated as though forgotten. And now,

[155-117]dean, what must I do about the living?"

[155-118]"Just resume it, as though nothing had happened."

[155-119]"But that may hardly be done without the bishop's authority. I speak,

[155-120]of course, with deference to your higher and better information on

[155-121]such subjects. My experience in the taking up and laying down of

[155-122]livings has not been extended. But it seemeth to me that though it

[155-123]may certainly be in your power to nominate me again to the perpetual

[155-124]curacy of this parish,--presuming your patronage to be unlimited and

[155-125]not to reach you in rotation only,--yet the bishop may demand to

[155-126]institute again, and must so demand, unless he pleases to permit that

[155-127]my letter to him shall be revoked and cancelled."

[155-128]"Of course he will do anything of that kind. He must know the

[155-129]circumstances as well as you and I do."

[155-130]"At present they tell me that he is much afflicted by the death of

[155-131]his wife, and, therefore, can hardly be expected to take immediate

[155-132]action. There came here on the last Sunday one Mr. Snapper, his

[155-133]lordship's chaplain."

[155-134]"We all know Snapper," said the dean. "Snapper is not a bad little

[155-135]fellow."

[155-136]"I say nothing of his being bad, my friend, but merely mention the

[155-137]fact that on Sunday morning last he performed the service in our

[155-138]church. On the Sunday previous, one Mr. Thumble was here."

[155-139]"We all know Thumble, too," said the dean; "or, at least, know

[155-140]something about him."

[155-141]"He has been a thorn in our sides," said Mrs. Crawley, unable to

[155-142]restrain the expression of her dislike when Mr. Thumble's name was

[155-143]mentioned.

[155-144]"Nay, my dear, nay;--do not allow yourself the use of language so

[155-145]strong against a brother. Our flesh at that time was somewhat prone

[155-146]to fester, and little thorns made us very sore."

[155-147]"He is a horrible man," said Jane, almost in a whisper; but the words

[155-148]were distinctly audible by the dean.

[155-149]"They need not come any more," said Arabin.

[155-150]"That is where I fear we differ. I think they must come,--or some

[155-151]others in their place,--till the bishop shall have expressed his

[155-152]pleasure to the contrary. I have submitted myself to his lordship,

[155-153]and, having done so, feel that I cannot again go up into my pulpit

[155-154]till he shall have authorized me to do so. For a time, Arabin, I

[155-155]combated the bishop, believing,--then and now,--that he put forth his

[155-156]hand against me after a fashion which the law had not sanctioned. And

[155-157]I made bold to stand in his presence and to tell him that I would not

[155-158]obey him, except in things legal. But afterwards, when he proceeded

[155-159]formally, through the action of a commission, I submitted myself. And

[155-160]I regard myself still as being under submission."

[155-161]It was impossible to shake him. Arabin remained there for more than

[155-162]an hour, trying to pass on to another subject, but being constantly

[155-163]brought back by Mr. Crawley himself to the fact of his own dependent

[155-164]position. Nor would he condescend to supplicate the bishop. It was,

[155-165]he surmised, the duty of Dr. Tempest, together with the other four

[155-166]clergymen, to report to the bishop on the question of the alleged

[155-167]theft; and then doubtless the bishop, when he had duly considered

[155-168]the report, and,--as Mr. Crawley seemed to think was essentially

[155-169]necessary,--had sufficiently recovered from the grief at his wife's

[155-170]death, would, at his leisure, communicate his decision to Mr.

[155-171]Crawley. Nothing could be more complete than Mr. Crawley's humility

[155-172]in reference to the bishop; and he never seemed to be tired of

[155-173]declaring that he had submitted himself!

[155-174]And then the dean, finding it to be vain to expect to be left alone

[155-175]with Mr. Crawley for a moment,--in vain also to wait for a proper

[155-176]opening for that which he had to say,--rushed violently at his other

[155-177]subject. "And now, Mrs. Crawley," he said, "Mrs. Arabin wishes you

[155-178]all to come over to the deanery for a while and stay with us."
