[035-001]"Of course it will, sir. Your gown will be taken from you," said Mrs.

[035-002]Proudie. The bishop was looking with all his eyes up at the great

[035-003]forehead and great eyebrows of the man, and was so fascinated by the

[035-004]power that was exercised over him by the other man's strength that he

[035-005]hardly now noticed his wife.

[035-006]"It may well be so," continued Mr. Crawley. "The circumstances

[035-007]are strong against me; and, though your lordship has altogether

[035-008]misunderstood the nature of the duty performed by the magistrates in

[035-009]sending my case for trial,--although, as it seems to me, you have

[035-010]come to conclusions in this matter in ignorance of the very theory of

[035-011]our laws,--"

[035-012]"Sir!" said Mrs. Proudie.

[035-013]"Yet I can foresee the probability that a jury may discover me to

[035-014]have been guilty of theft."

[035-015]"Of course the jury will do so," said Mrs. Proudie.

[035-016]"Should such verdict be given, then, my lord, your interference will

[035-017]be legal, proper, and necessary. And you will find that, even if it

[035-018]be within my power to oppose obstacles to your lordship's authority,

[035-019]I will oppose no such obstacle. There is, I believe, no appeal in

[035-020]criminal cases."

[035-021]"None at all," said Mrs. Proudie. "There is no appeal against your

[035-022]bishop. You should have learned that before."

[035-023]"But till that time shall come, my lord, I shall hold my own at

[035-024]Hogglestock as you hold your own here at Barchester. Nor have you

[035-025]more power to turn me out of my pulpit by your mere voice, than I

[035-026]have to turn you out of your throne by mine. If you doubt me, my

[035-027]lord, your lordship's ecclesiastical court is open to you. Try it

[035-028]there."

[035-029]"You defy us, then?" said Mrs. Proudie.

[035-030]"My lord, I grant your authority as bishop to be great, but even a

[035-031]bishop can only act as the law allows him."

[035-032]"God forbid that I should do more," said the bishop.

[035-033]"Sir, you will find that your wicked threats will fall back upon your

[035-034]own head," said Mrs. Proudie.

[035-035]"Peace, woman," Mr. Crawley said, addressing her at last. The bishop

[035-036]jumped out of his chair at hearing the wife of his bosom called a

[035-037]woman. But he jumped rather in admiration than in anger. He had

[035-038]already begun to perceive that Mr. Crawley was a man who had better

[035-039]be left to take care of the souls at Hogglestock, at any rate till

[035-040]the trial should come on.

[035-041]"Woman!" said Mrs. Proudie, rising to her feet as though she really

[035-042]intended some personal encounter.

[035-043]"Madam," said Mr. Crawley, "you should not interfere in these

[035-044]matters. You simply debase your husband's high office. The distaff

[035-045]were more fitting for you. My lord, good morning." And before either

[035-046]of them could speak again, he was out of the room, and through the

[035-047]hall, and beyond the gate, and standing beneath the towers of the

[035-048]cathedral. Yes, he had, he thought, in truth crushed the bishop. He

[035-049]had succeeded in crumpling the bishop up within the clutch of his

[035-050]fist.

[035-051]He started in a spirit of triumph to walk back on his road towards

[035-052]Hogglestock. He did not think of the long distance before him for

[035-053]the first hour of his journey. He had had his victory, and the

[035-054]remembrance of that braced his nerves and gave elasticity to his

[035-055]sinews, and he went stalking along the road with rapid strides,

[035-056]muttering to himself from time to time as he went along some word

[035-057]about Mrs. Proudie and her distaff. Mr. Thumble would not, he

[035-058]thought, come to him again,--not, at any rate, till the assizes were

[035-059]drawing near. And he had resolved what he would do then. When the

[035-060]day of his trial was near, he would himself write to the bishop, and

[035-061]beg that provision might be made for his church, in the event of the

[035-062]verdict going against him. His friend, Dean Arabin, was to be home

[035-063]before that time, and the idea had occurred to him of asking the

[035-064]dean to see to this; but now the other would be the more independent

[035-065]course, and the better. And there was a matter as to which he was not

[035-066]altogether well pleased with the dean, although he was so conscious

[035-067]of his own peculiarities as to know that he could hardly trust

[035-068]himself for a judgment. But, at any rate, he would apply to the

[035-069]bishop,--to the bishop whom he had just left prostrate in his

[035-070]palace,--when the time of his trial should be close at hand.

[035-071]Full of such thoughts as these he went along almost gaily, nor felt

[035-072]the fatigue of the road till he had covered the first five miles out

[035-073]of Barchester. It was nearly four o'clock, and the thick gloom of

[035-074]the winter evening was making itself felt. And then he began to be

[035-075]fatigued. He had not as yet eaten since he had left his home in the

[035-076]morning, and he now pulled a crust out of his pocket and leaned

[035-077]against a gate as he crunched it. There were still ten miles before

[035-078]him, and he knew that such an addition to the work he had already

[035-079]done would task him very severely. Farmer Mangle had told him that he

[035-080]would not leave Framley Mill till five, and he had got time to reach

[035-081]Framley Mill by that time. But he had said that he would not return

[035-082]to Framley Mill, and he remembered his suspicion that his wife and

[035-083]farmer Mangle between them had cozened him. No; he would persevere

[035-084]and walk,--walk, though he should drop upon the road. He was now

[035-085]nearer fifty than forty years of age, and hardships as well as time

[035-086]had told upon him. He knew that though his strength was good for the

[035-087]commencement of a hard day's work, it would not hold out for him as

[035-088]it used to do. He knew that the last four miles in the dark night

[035-089]would be very sad with him. But still he persevered, endeavouring, as

[035-090]he went, to cherish himself with the remembrance of his triumph.

[035-091]He passed the turning going down to Framley with courage, but when

[035-092]he came to the further turning, by which the cart would return

[035-093]from Framley to the Hogglestock road, he looked wistfully down the

[035-094]road for farmer Mangle. But farmer Mangle was still at the mill,

[035-095]waiting in expectation that Mr. Crawley might come to him. But the

[035-096]poor traveller paused here barely for a minute, and then went on,

[035-097]stumbling through the mud, striking his ill-covered feet against the

[035-098]rough stones in the dark, sweating in his weakness, almost tottering

[035-099]at times, and calculating whether his remaining strength would serve

[035-100]to carry him home. He had almost forgotten the bishop and his wife

[035-101]before at last he grasped the wicket gate leading to his own door.

[035-102]"Oh, mamma, here is papa!"

[035-103]"But where is the cart? I did not hear the wheels," said Mrs.

[035-104]Crawley.

[035-105]"Oh, mamma, I think papa is ill." Then the wife took her drooping

[035-106]husband by both arms and strove to look him in the face. "He has

[035-107]walked all the way, and he is ill," said Jane.

[035-108]"No, my dear, I am very tired, but not ill. Let me sit down, and

[035-109]give me some bread and tea, and I shall recover myself." Then Mrs.

[035-110]Crawley, from some secret hoard, got him a small modicum of spirits,

[035-111]and gave him meat and tea, and he was docile; and, obeying her

[035-112]behests, allowed himself to be taken to his bed.

[035-113]"I do not think the bishop will send for me again," he said, as she

[035-114]tucked the clothes around him.

[035-115]CHAPTER XIX.

[035-116]WHERE DID IT COME FROM?

[035-117]When Christmas morning came no emissary from the bishop appeared at

[035-118]Hogglestock to interfere with the ordinary performance of the day's

[035-119]services. "I think we need fear no further disturbance," Mr. Crawley

[035-120]said to his wife,--and there was no further disturbance.

[035-121]On the day after his walk from Framley to Barchester, and from

[035-122]Barchester back to Hogglestock, Mr. Crawley had risen not much the

[035-123]worse for his labour, and had gradually given to his wife a full

[035-124]account of what had taken place. "A poor weak man," he said, speaking

[035-125]of the bishop. "A poor weak creature, and much to be pitied."

[035-126]"I have always heard that she is a violent woman."

[035-127]"Very violent, and very ignorant; and most intrusive withal."

[035-128]"And you did not answer her a word?"

[035-129]"At last my forbearance with her broke down, and I bade her mind her

[035-130]distaff."

[035-131]"What;--really? Did you say those words to her?"

[035-132]"Nay; as for my exact words I cannot remember them. I was thinking

[035-133]more of the words with which it might be fitting that I should answer

[035-134]the bishop. But I certainly told her that she had better mind her

[035-135]distaff."

[035-136]"And how did she behave then?"

[035-137]"I did not wait to see. The bishop had spoken, and I had replied; and

[035-138]why should I tarry to behold the woman's violence? I had told him

[035-139]that he was wrong in law, and that I at least would not submit to

[035-140]usurped authority. There was nothing to keep me longer, and so I went

[035-141]without much ceremony of leave-taking. There had been little ceremony

[035-142]of greeting on their part, and there was less in the making of adieux

[035-143]on mine. They had told me that I was a thief--"

[035-144]"No, Josiah,--surely not so? They did not use that very word?"

[035-145]"I say they did;--they did use the very word. But stop. I am wrong.

[035-146]I wrong his lordship, and I crave pardon for having done so. If my

[035-147]memory serve me, no expression so harsh escaped from the bishop's

[035-148]mouth. He gave me, indeed, to understand more than once that the

[035-149]action taken by the magistrates was tantamount to a conviction,

[035-150]and that I must be guilty because they had decided that there was

[035-151]evidence sufficient to justify a trial. But all that arose from my

[035-152]lord's ignorance of the administration of the laws of his country.

[035-153]He was very ignorant,--puzzle-pated, as you may call it,--led by the

[035-154]nose by his wife, weak as water, timid, and vacillating. But he did

[035-155]not wish, I think, to be insolent. It was Mrs. Proudie who told me to

[035-156]my face that I was a--thief."

[035-157]"May she be punished for the cruel word!" said Mrs. Crawley. "May the

[035-158]remembrance that she has spoken it come, some day, heavily upon her

[035-159]heart!"

[035-160]"'Vengeance is mine. I will repay,' saith the Lord," answered Mr.

[035-161]Crawley. "We may safely leave all that alone, and rid our minds of

[035-162]such wishes, if it be possible. It is well, I think, that violent

[035-163]offences, when committed, should be met by instant rebuke. To turn

[035-164]the other cheek instantly to the smiter can hardly be suitable in

[035-165]these days, when the hands of so many are raised to strike. But the

[035-166]return blow should be given only while the smart remains. She hurt

[035-167]me then; but what is it to me now, that she called me a thief to my

[035-168]face? Do I not know that, all the country round, men and women are

[035-169]calling me the same behind my back?"

[035-170]"No, Josiah, you do not know that. They say that the thing is very

[035-171]strange,--so strange that it requires a trial; but no one thinks you

[035-172]have taken that which was not your own."

[035-173]"I think I did. I myself think I took that which was not my own. My

[035-174]poor head suffers so;--so many grievous thoughts distract me, that

[035-175]I am like a child, and know not what I do." As he spoke thus he put

[035-176]both hands up to his head, leaning forward as though in anxious

[035-177]thought,--as though he were striving to bring his mind to bear with

[035-178]accuracy upon past events. "It could not have been mine, and yet--"

[035-179]Then he sat silent, and made no effort to continue his speech.
