[094-001]It will be remembered that the archdeacon had on a former occasion

[094-002]instructed his wife to write to their son and tell him of his

[094-003]father's determination. Mrs. Grantly had so manoeuvred that a little

[094-004]time had been gained, and that those instructions had not been

[094-005]insisted upon in all their bitterness. Since that time Major Grantly

[094-006]had renewed his assurance that he would marry Grace Crawley if Grace

[094-007]Crawley would accept him,--writing on this occasion direct to his

[094-008]father,--and had asked his father whether, in such case, he was to

[094-009]look forward to be disinherited. "It is essential that I should

[094-010]know," the major had said, "because in such case I must take

[094-011]immediate measures for leaving this place." His father had sent him

[094-012]back his letter, writing a few words at the bottom of it. "If you do

[094-013]as you propose above, you must expect nothing from me." The words

[094-014]were written in large round handwriting, very hurriedly, and the son

[094-015]when he received them perfectly understood the mood of his father's

[094-016]mind when he wrote them.

[094-017]Then there came tidings, addressed on this occasion to Mrs. Grantly,

[094-018]that Cosby Lodge was to be given up. Lady-day had come, and the

[094-019]notice, necessarily to be given at that period, was so given. "I know

[094-020]this will grieve you," Major Grantly had said, "but my father has

[094-021]driven me to it." This, in itself, was a cause of great sorrow, both

[094-022]to the archdeacon and to Mrs. Grantly, as there were circumstances

[094-023]connected with Cosby Lodge which made them think that it was a very

[094-024]desirable residence for their son. "I shall sell everything about the

[094-025]place and go abroad at once," he said in a subsequent letter. "My

[094-026]present idea is that I shall settle myself at Pau, as my income will

[094-027]suffice for me to live there, and education for Edith will be cheap.

[094-028]At any rate I will not continue in England. I could never be happy

[094-029]here in circumstances so altered. Of course I should not have left my

[094-030]profession, unless I had understood from my father that the income

[094-031]arising from it would not be necessary to me. I do not, however, mean

[094-032]to complain, but simply tell you that I shall go." There were many

[094-033]letters between the mother and son in those days. "I shall stay till

[094-034]after the trial," he said. "If she will then go with me, well and

[094-035]good; but whether she will or not, I shall not remain here." All this

[094-036]seemed to Mrs. Grantly to be peculiarly unfortunate, for, had he not

[094-037]resolved to go, things might even yet have righted themselves. From

[094-038]what she could now understand of the character of Miss Crawley, whom

[094-039]she did not know personally, she thought it probable that Grace,

[094-040]in the event of her father being found guilty by the jury, would

[094-041]absolutely and persistently refuse the offer made to her. She would

[094-042]be too good, as Mrs. Grantly put it to herself, to bring misery and

[094-043]disgrace into another family. But should Mr. Crawley be acquitted,

[094-044]and should the marriage then take place, the archdeacon himself might

[094-045]probably be got to forgive it. In either case there would be no

[094-046]necessity for breaking up the house at Cosby Lodge. But her dear son

[094-047]Henry, her best beloved, was obstinate and stiff-necked, and would

[094-048]take no advice. "He is even worse than his father," she said, in her

[094-049]short-lived anger, to her own father, to whom alone at this time she

[094-050]could unburden her griefs, seeking consolation and encouragement.

[094-051]It was her habit to go over to the deanery at any rate twice a week

[094-052]at this time, and on the occasion of one of the visits so made, she

[094-053]expressed very strongly her distress at the family quarrel which had

[094-054]come among them. The old man took his grandson's part through and

[094-055]through. "I do not at all see why he should not marry the young lady

[094-056]if he likes her. As for money, there ought to be enough without his

[094-057]having to look for a wife with a fortune."

[094-058]"It is not a question of money, papa."

[094-059]"And as to rank," continued Mr. Harding, "Henry will not at any rate

[094-060]be going lower than his father did when he married you;--not so low

[094-061]indeed, for at that time I was only a minor canon, and Mr. Crawley is

[094-062]in possession of a benefice."

[094-063]"Papa, all that is nonsense. It is, indeed."

[094-064]"Very likely, my dear."

[094-065]"It is not because Mr. Crawley is only perpetual curate of

[094-066]Hogglestock, that the archdeacon objects to the marriage. It has

[094-067]nothing to do with that at all. At the present moment he is in

[094-068]disgrace."

[094-069]"Under a cloud, my dear. Let us pray that it may be only a passing

[094-070]cloud."

[094-071]"All the world thinks that he was guilty. And then he is such a

[094-072]man:--so singular, so unlike anybody else! You know, papa, that I

[094-073]don't think very much of money, merely as money."

[094-074]"I hope not, my dear. Money is worth thinking of, but it is not worth

[094-075]very much thought."

[094-076]"But it does give advantages, and the absence of such advantages must

[094-077]be very much felt in the education of a girl. You would hardly wish

[094-078]Henry to marry a young woman who, from want of money, had not been

[094-079]brought up among ladies. It is not Miss Crawley's fault, but such has

[094-080]been her lot. We cannot ignore these deficiencies, papa."

[094-081]"Certainly not, my dear."

[094-082]"You would not, for instance, wish that Henry should marry a

[094-083]kitchen-maid."

[094-084]"But is Miss Crawley a kitchen-maid, Susan?"

[094-085]"I don't quite say that."

[094-086]"I am told that she has been educated infinitely better than most of

[094-087]the young ladies in the neighbourhood," said Mr. Harding.

[094-088]"I believe that her father has taught her Greek; and I suppose she

[094-089]has learned something of French at that school at Silverbridge."

[094-090]"Then the kitchen-maid theory is sufficiently disposed of," said Mr.

[094-091]Harding, with mild triumph.

[094-092]"You know what I mean, papa. But the fact is, that it is impossible

[094-093]to deal with men. They will never be reasonable. A marriage such as

[094-094]this would be injurious to Henry; but it will not be ruinous; and as

[094-095]to disinheriting him for it, that would be downright wicked."

[094-096]"I think so," said Mr. Harding.

[094-097]"But the archdeacon will look at it as though it would destroy Henry

[094-098]and Edith altogether, while you speak of it as though it were the

[094-099]best thing in the world."

[094-100]"If the young people love each other, I think it would be the best

[094-101]thing in the world," said Mr. Harding.

[094-102]"But, papa, you cannot but think that his father's wish should go for

[094-103]something," said Mrs. Grantly, who, desirous as she was on the one

[094-104]side to support her son, could not bear that her husband should, on

[094-105]the other side, be declared to be altogether in the wrong.

[094-106]"I do not know, my dear," said Mr. Harding; "but I do think, that if

[094-107]the two young people are fond of each other, and if there is anything

[094-108]for them to live upon, it cannot be right to keep them apart. You

[094-109]know, my dear, she is the daughter of a gentleman." Mrs. Grantly upon

[094-110]this left her father almost brusquely, without speaking another word

[094-111]on the subject; for, though she was opposed to the vehement anger of

[094-112]her husband, she could not endure the proposition now made by her

[094-113]father.

[094-114]Mr. Harding was at this time living all alone in the deanery. For

[094-115]some few years the deanery had been his home, and as his youngest

[094-116]daughter was the dean's wife, there could be no more comfortable

[094-117]resting-place for the evening of his life. During the last month or

[094-118]two the days had gone tediously with him; for he had had the large

[094-119]house all to himself, and he was a man who did not love solitude.

[094-120]It is hard to conceive that the old, whose thoughts have been all

[094-121]thought out, should ever love to live alone. Solitude is surely for

[094-122]the young, who have time before them for the execution of schemes,

[094-123]and who can, therefore, take delight in thinking. In these days the

[094-124]poor old man would wander about the rooms, shambling from one chamber

[094-125]to another, and would feel ashamed when the servants met him ever on

[094-126]the move. He would make little apologies for his uneasiness, which

[094-127]they would accept graciously, understanding, after a fashion, why

[094-128]it was that he was uneasy. "He ain't got nothing to do," said the

[094-129]housemaid to the cook, "and as for reading, they say that some of

[094-130]the young ones can read all day sometimes, and all night too; but,

[094-131]bless you, when you're nigh eighty, reading don't go for much." The

[094-132]housemaid was right as to Mr. Harding's reading. He was not one who

[094-133]had read so much in his earlier days as to enable him to make reading

[094-134]go far with him now that he was near eighty. So he wandered about the

[094-135]room, and sat here for a few minutes, and there for a few minutes,

[094-136]and though he did not sleep much, he made the hours of the night

[094-137]as many as was possible. Every morning he shambled across from the

[094-138]deanery to the cathedral, and attended the morning service, sitting

[094-139]in the stall which he had occupied for fifty years. The distance was

[094-140]very short, not exceeding, indeed, a hundred yards from a side-door

[094-141]in the deanery to another side-door into the cathedral; but short as

[094-142]it was there had come to be a question whether he should be allowed

[094-143]to go alone. It had been feared that he might fall on his passage and

[094-144]hurt himself; for there was a step here, and a step there, and the

[094-145]light was not very good in the purlieus of the old cathedral. A word

[094-146]or two had been said once, and the offer of an arm to help him had

[094-147]been made; but he had rejected the proffered assistance,--softly,

[094-148]indeed, but still firmly,--and every day he tottered off by himself,

[094-149]hardly lifting his feet as he went, and aiding himself on his journey

[094-150]by a hand upon the wall when he thought that nobody was looking at

[094-151]him. But many did see him, and they who knew him,--ladies generally

[094-152]of the city,--would offer him a hand. Nobody was milder in his

[094-153]dislikings than Mr. Harding; but there were ladies in Barchester upon

[094-154]whose arm he would always decline to lean, bowing courteously as he

[094-155]did so, and saying a word or two of constrained civility. There were

[094-156]others whom he would allow to accompany him home to the door of the

[094-157]deanery, with whom he delighted to linger and chat if the morning was

[094-158]warm, and to whom he would tell little stories of his own doings in

[094-159]the cathedral services in the old days, when Bishop Grantly had ruled

[094-160]in the diocese. Never a word did he say against Bishop Proudie, or

[094-161]against Bishop Proudie's wife; but the many words which he did say in

[094-162]praise of Bishop Grantly,--who, by his showing, was surely one of the

[094-163]best of churchmen who ever walked through this vale of sorrow,--were

[094-164]as eloquent in dispraise of the existing prelate as could have been

[094-165]any more clearly-pointed phrases. This daily visit to the cathedral,

[094-166]where he would say his prayers as he had said them for so many years,

[094-167]and listen to the organ, of which he knew all the power and every

[094-168]blemish as though he himself had made the stops and fixed the pipes,

[094-169]was the chief occupation of his life. It was a pity that it could not

[094-170]have been made to cover a larger portion of the day.

[094-171]It was sometimes sad enough to watch him as he sat alone. He would

[094-172]have a book near him, and for a while would keep it in his hands. It

[094-173]would generally be some volume of good old standard theology with

[094-174]which he had been, or supposed himself to have been, conversant from

[094-175]his youth. But the book would soon be laid aside, and gradually he

[094-176]would move himself away from it, and he would stand about in the

[094-177]room, looking now out of a window from which he would fancy that he

[094-178]could not be seen, or gazing up at some print which he had known for

[094-179]years; and then he would sit down for a while in one chair, and for

[094-180]a while in another, while his mind was wandering back into old days,

[094-181]thinking of old troubles and remembering his old joys. And he had a

[094-182]habit, when he was sure that he was not watched, of creeping up to

[094-183]a great black wooden case, which always stood in one corner of the

[094-184]sitting-room which he occupied in the deanery. Mr. Harding, when he

[094-185]was younger, had been a performer on the violoncello, and in this

[094-186]case there was still the instrument from which he had been wont to

[094-187]extract the sounds which he had so dearly loved. Now in these latter

[094-188]days he never made any attempt to play. Soon after he had come to

[094-189]the deanery there had fallen upon him an illness, and after that he

[094-190]had never again asked for his bow. They who were around him,--his

[094-191]daughter chiefly and her husband,--had given the matter much thought,

[094-192]arguing with themselves whether or no it would be better to invite

[094-193]him to resume the task he had so loved; for of all the works of his

[094-194]life this playing on the violoncello had been the sweetest to him;

[094-195]but even before that illness his hand had greatly failed him, and the

[094-196]dean and Mrs. Arabin had agreed that it would be better to let the

[094-197]matter pass without a word. He had never asked to be allowed to play.

[094-198]He had expressed no regrets. When he himself would propose that his

[094-199]daughter should "give them a little music,"--and he would make such a

[094-200]proposition on every evening that was suitable,--he would never say

[094-201]a word of those former performances at which he himself had taken a

[094-202]part. But it had become known to Mrs. Arabin, through the servants,

[094-203]that he had once dragged the instrument forth from its case when he

[094-204]had thought the house to be nearly deserted; and a wail of sounds had

[094-205]been heard, very low, very short-lived, recurring now and again at

[094-206]fitful intervals. He had at those times attempted to play, as though

[094-207]with a muffled bow,--so that none should know of his vanity and

[094-208]folly. Then there had been further consultations at the deanery, and

[094-209]it had been again agreed that it would be best to say nothing to him

[094-210]of his music.
