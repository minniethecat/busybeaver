[010-001]Pray write to me soon. I feel as though nothing on earth

[010-002]could comfort me, and yet I shall like to have your

[010-003]letter. Dear, dear Lily, I am not even yet so wretched but

[010-004]what I shall rejoice to be told good news of you. If it

[010-005]only could be as John wishes it! And why should it not?

[010-006]It seems to me that nobody has a right or a reason to be

[010-007]unhappy except us. Good-by, dearest Lily,

[010-008]Your affectionate friend,

[010-009]GRACE CRAWLEY.

[010-010]P.S.--I think I have made up my mind that I will go back

[010-011]to Hogglestock at once if the magistrates decide against

[010-012]papa. I think I should be doing the school harm if I were

[010-013]to stay here.

[010-014]The answer to this letter did not reach Miss Crawley till after the

[010-015]magistrates' meeting on the Thursday, but it will be better for our

[010-016]story that it should be given here than postponed until the result

[010-017]of that meeting shall have been told. Miss Dale's answer was as

[010-018]follows:--

[010-019]Allington, -- December, 186--.

[010-020]DEAR GRACE,

[010-021]Your letter has made me very unhappy. If it can at all

[010-022]comfort you to know that mamma and I sympathize with you

[010-023]altogether, in that you may at any rate be sure. But in

[010-024]such troubles nothing will give comfort. They must be

[010-025]borne, till the fire of misfortune burns itself out.

[010-026]I had heard about the affair a day or two before I got

[010-027]your note. Our clergyman, Mr. Boyce, told us of it. Of

[010-028]course we all know that the charge must be altogether

[010-029]unfounded, and mamma says that the truth will be sure to

[010-030]show itself at last. But that conviction does not cure the

[010-031]evil, and I can well understand that your father should

[010-032]suffer grievously; and I pity your mother quite as much as

[010-033]I do him.

[010-034]As for Major Grantly, if he be such a man as I took him to

[010-035]be from the little I saw of him, all this would make no

[010-036]difference to him. I am sure that it ought to make none.

[010-037]Whether it should not make a difference in you is another

[010-038]question. I think it should; and I think your answer to

[010-039]him should be that you could not even consider any such

[010-040]proposition while your father was in so great trouble. I

[010-041]am so much older than you, and seem to have had so much

[010-042]experience, that I do not scruple, as you will see, to

[010-043]come down upon you with all the weight of my wisdom.

[010-044][[About]] that other subject I had rather say nothing. I have

[010-045]known your cousin all my life, almost; and I regard no one

[010-046]more kindly than I do him. When I think of my friends, he

[010-047]is always one of the dearest. But when one thinks of going

[010-048]beyond friendship, even if one tries to do so, there are

[010-049]so many barriers!

[010-050]Your affectionate friend,

[010-051]LILY DALE.

[010-052]Mamma bids me say that she would be delighted to have you

[010-053]here whenever it might suit you to come; and I add to this

[010-054]message my entreaty that you will come at once. You say

[010-055]that you think you ought to leave Miss Prettyman's for a

[010-056]while. I can well understand your feeling; but as your

[010-057]sister is with your mother, surely you had better come

[010-058]to us,--I mean quite at once. I will not scruple to tell

[010-059]you what mamma says, because I know your good sense. She

[010-060]says that as the interest of the school may possibly be

[010-061]concerned, and as you have no regular engagement, she

[010-062]thinks you ought to leave Silverbridge; but she says that

[010-063]it will be better that you come to us than that you should

[010-064]go home. If you went home, people might say that you had

[010-065]left in some sort of disgrace. Come to us, and when all

[010-066]this has been put right, then you go back to Silverbridge;

[010-067]and then, if a certain person speaks again, you can make a

[010-068]different answer. Mamma quite understands that you are to

[010-069]come; so you have only got to ask your own mamma, and come

[010-070]at once.

[010-071]This letter, as the reader will understand, did not reach Grace

[010-072]Crawley till after the all-important Thursday; but before that day

[010-073]had come round, Grace had told Miss Prettyman,--had told both the

[010-074]Miss Prettymans--that she was resolved to leave them. She had done

[010-075]this without even consulting her mother, driven to it by various

[010-076]motives. She knew that her father's conduct was being discussed by

[010-077]the girls in the school, and that things were said of him which it

[010-078]could not but be for the disadvantage of Miss Prettyman that any one

[010-079]should say of a teacher in her establishment. She felt, too, that

[010-080]she could not hold up her head in Silverbridge in these days, as it

[010-081]would become her to do if she retained her position. She did struggle

[010-082]gallantly, and succeeded much more nearly than she was herself

[010-083]aware. She was all but able to carry herself as though no terrible

[010-084]accusation was being made against her father. Of the struggle,

[010-085]however, she was not herself the less conscious, and she told herself

[010-086]that on that account also she must go. And then she must go also

[010-087]because of Major Grantly. Whether he was minded to come and speak

[010-088]to her that one other needed word, or whether he was not so minded,

[010-089]it would be better that she should be away from Silverbridge. If he

[010-090]spoke it she could only answer him by a negative; and if he were

[010-091]minded not to speak it, would it not be better that she should leave

[010-092]herself the power of thinking that his silence had been caused by her

[010-093]absence, and not by his coldness or indifference?

[010-094]She asked, therefore, for an interview with Miss Prettyman, and was

[010-095]shown into the elder sister's room, at eleven o'clock on the Tuesday

[010-096]morning. The elder Miss Prettyman never came into the school herself

[010-097]till twelve, but was in the habit of having interviews with the young

[010-098]ladies,--which were sometimes very awful in their nature,--for the

[010-099]two previous hours. During these interviews an immense amount of

[010-100]business was done, and the fortunes in life of some girls were said

[010-101]to have been there made or marred; as when, for instance, Miss

[010-102]Crimpton had been advised to stay at home with her uncle in England,

[010-103]instead of going out with her sisters to India, both of which sisters

[010-104]were married within three months of their landing at Bombay. The way

[010-105]in which she gave her counsel on such occasions was very efficacious.

[010-106]No one knew better than Miss Prettyman that a cock can crow most

[010-107]effectively in his own farmyard, and therefore all crowing intended

[010-108]to be effective was done by her within the shrine of her own peculiar

[010-109]room.

[010-110]"Well, my dear, what is it?" she said to Grace. "Sit in the

[010-111]arm-chair, my dear, and we can then talk comfortably." The teachers,

[010-112]when they were closeted with Miss Prettyman, were always asked to sit

[010-113]in the arm-chair, whereas a small, straight-backed, uneasy chair was

[010-114]kept for the use of the young ladies. And there was, too, a stool of

[010-115]repentance, out against the wall, very uncomfortable indeed for young

[010-116]ladies who had not behaved themselves so prettily as young ladies

[010-117]generally do.

[010-118]Grace seated herself, and then began her speech very quickly. "Miss

[010-119]Prettyman," she said, "I have made up my mind that I will go home, if

[010-120]you please."

[010-121]"And why should you go home, Grace? Did I not tell you that you

[010-122]should have a home here?" Miss Prettyman had weak eyes, and was very

[010-123]small, and had never possessed any claim to be called good-looking.

[010-124]And she assumed nothing of majestical awe from any adornment or

[010-125]studied amplification of the outward woman by means of impressive

[010-126]trappings. The possessor of an unobservant eye might have called her

[010-127]a mean-looking, little old woman. And certainly there would have

[010-128]been nothing awful in her to any one who came across her otherwise

[010-129]than as a lady having authority in her own school. But within her

[010-130]own precincts, she did know how to surround herself with a dignity

[010-131]which all felt who approached her there. Grace Crawley, as she heard

[010-132]the simple question which Miss Prettyman had asked, unconsciously

[010-133]acknowledged the strength of the woman's manner. She already stood

[010-134]rebuked for having proposed a plan so ungracious, so unnecessary, and

[010-135]so unwise.

[010-136]"I think I ought to be with mamma at present," said Grace.

[010-137]"Your mother has your sister with her."

[010-138]"Yes, Miss Prettyman; Jane is there."

[010-139]"If there be no other reason, I cannot think that that can be held to

[010-140]be a reason now. Of course your mother would like to have you always;

[010-141]unless you should be married,--but then there are reasons why this

[010-142]should not be so."

[010-143]"Of course there are."

[010-144]"I do not think,--that is, if I know all that there is to be

[010-145]known,--I do not think, I say, that there can be any good ground for

[010-146]your leaving us now,--just now."

[010-147]Then Grace sat silent for a moment, gathering her courage, and

[010-148]collecting her words; and after that she spoke. "It is because of

[010-149]papa, and because of this charge--"

[010-150]"But, Grace--"

[010-151]"I know what you are going to say, Miss Prettyman;--that is, I think

[010-152]I know."

[010-153]"If you will hear me, you may be sure that you know."

[010-154]"But I want you to hear me for one moment first. I beg your pardon,

[010-155]Miss Prettyman; I do indeed, but I want to say this before you go on.

[010-156]I must go home, and I know I ought. We are all disgraced, and I won't

[010-157]stop here to disgrace the school. I know papa has done nothing wrong;

[010-158]but nevertheless we are disgraced. The police are to bring him in

[010-159]here on Thursday, and everybody in Silverbridge will know it. It

[010-160]cannot be right that I should be here teaching in the school, while

[010-161]it is all going on;--and I won't. And, Miss Prettyman, I couldn't do

[010-162]it,--indeed I couldn't. I can't bring myself to think of anything

[010-163]I am doing. Indeed I can't; and then, Miss Prettyman, there are

[010-164]other reasons." By the time that she had proceeded thus far, Grace

[010-165]Crawley's words were nearly choked by her tears.

[010-166]"And what are the other reasons, Grace?"

[010-167]"I don't know," said Grace, struggling to speak through her tears.

[010-168]"But I know," said Miss Prettyman. "I know them all. I know all your

[010-169]reasons, and I tell you that in my opinion you ought to remain where

[010-170]you are, and not go away. The very reasons which to you are reasons

[010-171]for your going, to me are reasons for your remaining here."

[010-172]"I can't remain. I am determined to go. I don't mind you and Miss

[010-173]Anne, but I can't bear to have the girls looking at me,--and the

[010-174]servants."

[010-175]Then Miss Prettyman paused awhile, thinking what words of wisdom

[010-176]would be most appropriate in the present conjuncture. But words of

[010-177]wisdom did not seem to come easily to her, having for the moment been

[010-178]banished by tenderness of heart. "Come here, my love," she said at

[010-179]last. "Come here, Grace." Slowly Grace got up from her seat and came

[010-180]round, and stood by Miss Prettyman's elbow. Miss Prettyman pushed

[010-181]her chair a little back, and pushed herself a little forward, and

[010-182]stretching out one hand, placed her arm round Grace's waist, and

[010-183]with the other took hold of Grace's hand, and thus drew her down and

[010-184]kissed the girl's forehead and lips. And then Grace found herself

[010-185]kneeling at her friend's feet. "Grace," she said, "do you not know

[010-186]that I love you? Do you not know that I love you dearly?" In answer

[010-187]to this, Grace kissed the withered hand she held in hers, while the

[010-188]warm tears trickled down upon Miss Prettyman's knuckles. "I love you

[010-189]as though you were my own," exclaimed the schoolmistress; "and will

[010-190]you not trust me, that I know what is best for you?"
