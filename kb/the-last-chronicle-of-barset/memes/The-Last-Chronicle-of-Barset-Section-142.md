[142-001]"I don't think it has been that," said Walker. "Had he known all

[142-002]through that it had come from Mrs. Arabin, he would never have said

[142-003]that Mr. Soames gave it to him, and then that the dean gave it him."

[142-004]"The truth has been that he has known nothing about it," said

[142-005]Toogood; "and we shall have to tell him."

[142-006]At that moment Mary Walker came into the room, and Mrs. Walker could

[142-007]not constrain herself. "Mary, Mr. Crawley is all right. He didn't

[142-008]steal the cheque. Mrs. Arabin gave it to him."

[142-009]"Who says so? How do you know? Oh, dear; I am so happy, if it's

[142-010]true." Then she saw Mr. Toogood, and curtseyed.

[142-011]"It is quite true, my dear," said Mr. Walker. "Mr. Toogood has had a

[142-012]message by the wires from Mrs. Arabin at Venice. She is coming home

[142-013]at once, and no doubt everything will be put right. In the meantime,

[142-014]it may be a question whether we should not hold our tongues. Mr.

[142-015]Crawley himself, I suppose, knows nothing of it yet?"

[142-016]"Not a word," said Toogood.

[142-017]"Papa, I must tell Miss Prettyman," said Mary.

[142-018]"I should think that probably all Silverbridge knows it by this

[142-019]time," said Mrs. Walker, "because Jane was in the room when the

[142-020]announcement was made. You may be sure that every servant in the

[142-021]house has been told." Mary Walker, not waiting for any further

[142-022]command from her father, hurried out of the room to convey the secret

[142-023]to her special circle of friends.

[142-024]It was known throughout Silverbridge that night, and indeed it made

[142-025]so much commotion that it kept many people for an hour out of their

[142-026]beds. Ladies who were not in the habit of going out late at night

[142-027]without the fly from the "George and Vulture," tied their heads up in

[142-028]their handkerchiefs, and hurried up and down the street to tell each

[142-029]other that the great secret had been discovered, and that in truth

[142-030]Mr. Crawley had not stolen the cheque. The solution of the mystery

[142-031]was not known to all,--was known on that night only to the very

[142-032]select portion of the aristocracy of Silverbridge to whom it was

[142-033]communicated by Mary Walker or Miss Anne Prettyman. For Mary Walker,

[142-034]when earnestly entreated by Jane, the parlour-maid, to tell her

[142-035]something more of the great news, had so far respected her father's

[142-036]caution as to say not a word about Mrs. Arabin. "Is it true, Miss

[142-037]Mary, that he didn't steal it?" Jane asked imploringly. "It is true.

[142-038]He did not steal it." "And who did, Miss Mary? Indeed I won't tell

[142-039]anybody." "Nobody. But don't ask any more questions, for I won't

[142-040]answer them. Get me my hat at once, for I want to go up to Miss

[142-041]Prettyman's." Then Jane got Miss Walker's hat, and immediately

[142-042]afterwards scampered into the kitchen with the news. "Oh, law, cook,

[142-043]it's all come out! Mr. Crawley's as innocent as the unborn babe. The

[142-044]gentleman upstairs what's just come, and was here once before,--for I

[142-045]know'd him immediate,--I heard him say so. And master said so too."

[142-046]"Did master say so his own self?" asked the cook.

[142-047]"Indeed he did; and Miss Mary told me the same this moment."

[142-048]"If master said so, then there ain't a doubt as they'll find him

[142-049]innocent. And who took'd it, Jane?"

[142-050]"Miss Mary says as nobody didn't steal it."

[142-051]"That's nonsense, Jane. It stands to reason as somebody had it as

[142-052]hadn't ought to have had it. But I'm as glad as anything as how that

[142-053]poor reverend gent 'll come off;--I am. They tells me it's weeks

[142-054]sometimes before a bit of butcher's meat finds its way into his

[142-055]house." Then the groom and the housemaid and the cook, one after

[142-056]another, took occasion to slip out of the back-door, and poor Jane,

[142-057]who had really been the owner of the news, was left alone to answer

[142-058]the bell.

[142-059]Miss Walker found the two Miss Prettymans sitting together over their

[142-060]accounts in the elder Miss Prettyman's private room. And she could

[142-061]see at once by signs which were not unfamiliar to her that Miss

[142-062]Anne Prettyman was being scolded. It often happened that Miss Anne

[142-063]Prettyman was scolded, especially when the accounts were brought out

[142-064]upon the table. "Sister, they are illegible," Mary Walker heard, as

[142-065]the servant opened the door for her.

[142-066]"I don't think it's quite so bad as that," said Miss Anne, unable to

[142-067]restrain her defence. Then, as Mary entered the room, Miss Prettyman

[142-068]the elder laid her hands down on certain books and papers as though

[142-069]to hide them from profane eyes.

[142-070]"I am glad to see you, Mary," said Miss Prettyman, gravely.

[142-071]"I've brought such a piece of news," said Mary. "I knew you'd be glad

[142-072]to hear it, so I ventured to disturb you."

[142-073]"Is it good news?" said Anne Prettyman.

[142-074]"Very good news. Mr. Crawley is innocent."

[142-075]Both the ladies sprung on to their legs. Even Miss Prettyman

[142-076]herself jumped up on to her legs. "No!" said Anne. "Your father has

[142-077]discovered it?" said Miss Prettyman.

[142-078]"Not exactly that. Mr. Toogood has come down from London to tell him.

[142-079]Mr. Toogood, you know, is Mr. Crawley's cousin; and he is a lawyer,

[142-080]like papa." It may be observed that ladies belonging to the families

[142-081]of solicitors always talk about lawyers, and never about attorneys or

[142-082]barristers.

[142-083]"And does Mr. Toogood say that Mr. Crawley is innocent?" asked Miss

[142-084]Prettyman.

[142-085]"He has heard it by a message from Mrs. Arabin. But you mustn't

[142-086]mention this. You won't, please, because papa has asked me not. I

[142-087]told him that I should tell you." Then, for the first time, the frown

[142-088]passed away entirely from Miss Prettyman's face, and the papers and

[142-089]account-books were pushed aside, as being of no moment. The news had

[142-090]been momentous enough to satisfy her. Mary continued her story almost

[142-091]in a whisper. "It was Mrs. Arabin who sent the cheque to Mr. Crawley.

[142-092]She says so herself. So that makes Mr. Crawley quite innocent. I am

[142-093]so glad."

[142-094]"But isn't it odd he didn't say so?" said Miss Prettyman.

[142-095]"Nevertheless, it's true," said Mary.

[142-096]"Perhaps he forgot," said Anne Prettyman.

[142-097]"Men don't forget such things as that," said the elder sister.

[142-098]"I really do think Mr. Crawley could forget anything," said the

[142-099]younger sister.

[142-100]"You may be sure it's true," said Mary Walker, "because papa said

[142-101]so."

[142-102]"If he said so, it must be true," said Miss Prettyman; "and I am

[142-103]rejoiced. I really am rejoiced. Poor man! Poor ill-used man! And

[142-104]nobody has ever believed that he has really been guilty, even though

[142-105]they may have thought that he spent the money without any proper

[142-106]right to it. And now he will get off. But dear me, Mary, Mr. Smithe

[142-107]told me yesterday that he had already given up his living, and that

[142-108]Mr. Spooner, the minor canon, was trying to get it from the dean. But

[142-109]that was because Mr. Spooner and Mrs. Proudie had quarrelled; and

[142-110]as Mrs. Proudie is gone, Mr. Spooner very likely won't want to move

[142-111]now."

[142-112]"They'll never go and put anybody into Hogglestock, Annabella, over

[142-113]Mr. Crawley's head," said Anne.

[142-114]"I didn't say that they would. Surely I may be allowed to repeat what

[142-115]I hear, like another person, without being snapped up."

[142-116]"I didn't mean to snap you up, Annabella."

[142-117]"You're always snapping me up. But if this is true, I cannot say

[142-118]how glad I am. My poor Grace! Now, I suppose, there will be no

[142-119]difficulty, and Grace will become a great lady." Then they discussed

[142-120]very minutely the chances of Grace Crawley's promotion.

[142-121]John Walker, Mr. Winthrop, and several others of the chosen spirits

[142-122]of Silverbridge, were playing whist at a provincial club, which had

[142-123]established itself in the town, when the news was brought to them.

[142-124]Though Mr. Winthrop was the partner of the great Walker, and though

[142-125]John Walker was the great man's son, I fear that the news reached

[142-126]their ears in but an underhand sort of way. As for the great man

[142-127]himself, he never went near the club, preferring his slippers and

[142-128]tea at home. The Walkerian groom, rushing up the street to the

[142-129]"George and Vulture," paused a moment to tell his tidings to the club

[142-130]porter; from the club porter it was whispered respectfully to the

[142-131]Silverbridge apothecary, who, by special grace, was a member of the

[142-132]club;--and was by him repeated with much cautious solemnity over the

[142-133]card-table. "Who told you that, Balsam?" said John Walker, throwing

[142-134]down his cards.

[142-135]"I've just heard it," said Balsam.

[142-136]"I don't believe it," said John.

[142-137]"I shouldn't wonder if it's true," said Winthrop. "I always said that

[142-138]something would turn up."

[142-139]"Will you bet three to one he is not found guilty?" said John Walker.

[142-140]"Done," said Winthrop; "in pounds." That morning the odds in the club

[142-141]against the event had been only two to one. But as the matter was

[142-142]discussed, the men in the club began to believe the tidings, and

[142-143]before he went home, John Walker would have been glad to hedge his

[142-144]bet on any terms. After he had spoken to his father, he gave his

[142-145]money up for lost.

[142-146]But Mr. Walker,--the great Walker,--had more to do that night before

[142-147]his son came home from the club. He and Mr. Toogood agreed that it

[142-148]would be right that they should see Dr. Tempest at once, and they

[142-149]went over together to the rectory. It was past ten at this time, and

[142-150]they found the doctor almost in the act of putting out the candles

[142-151]for the night. "I could not but come to you, doctor," said Mr.

[142-152]Walker, "with the news my friend has brought. Mrs. Arabin gave the

[142-153]cheque to Crawley. Here is a telegram from her saying so." And the

[142-154]telegram was handed to the doctor.

[142-155]He stood perfectly silent for a few minutes, reading it over and over

[142-156]again. "I see it all," he said, when he spoke at last. "I see it all

[142-157]now; and I must own I was never before so much puzzled in my life."

[142-158]"I own I can't see why she should have given him Mr. Soames's

[142-159]cheque," said Mr. Walker.

[142-160]"I can't say where she got it, and I own I don't much care," said Dr.

[142-161]Tempest. "But I don't doubt but what she gave it him without telling

[142-162]the dean, and that Crawley thought it came from the dean. I'm very

[142-163]glad. I am, indeed, very glad. I do not know that I ever pitied a man

[142-164]so much in my life as I have pitied Mr. Crawley."

[142-165]"It must have been a hard case when it has moved him," said Mr.

[142-166]Walker to Mr. Toogood as they left the clergyman's house; and then

[142-167]the Silverbridge attorney saw the attorney from London home to his

[142-168]inn.

[142-169]It was the general opinion at Silverbridge that the news from Venice

[142-170]ought to be communicated to the Crawleys by Major Grantly. Mary

[142-171]Walker had expressed this opinion very strongly, and her mother had

[142-172]agreed with her. Miss Prettyman also felt that poetical justice, or,

[142-173]at least, the romance of justice, demanded this; and, as she told her

[142-174]sister Anne after Mary Walker left her, she was of opinion that such

[142-175]an arrangement might tend to make things safe. "I do think he is an

[142-176]honest man and a fine fellow," said Miss Prettyman; "but, my dear,

[142-177]you know what the proverb says, 'There's many a slip 'twixt the cup

[142-178]and the lip.'" Miss Prettyman thought that anything which might be

[142-179]done to prevent a slip ought to be done. The idea that the pleasant

[142-180]task of taking the news out to Hogglestock ought to be confided to

[142-181]Major Grantly was very general; but then Mr. Walker was of opinion

[142-182]that the news ought not to be taken to Hogglestock at all till

[142-183]something more certain than the telegram had reached them. Early on

[142-184]the following morning the two lawyers again met, and it was arranged

[142-185]between them that the London lawyer should go over at once to

[142-186]Barchester, and that the Silverbridge lawyer should see Major

[142-187]Grantly. Mr. Toogood was still of opinion that with due diligence

[142-188]something might yet be learned as to the cheque, by inquiry among the

[142-189]denizens of "The Dragon of Wantly;" and his opinion to this effect

[142-190]was stronger than ever when he learned from Mr. Walker that "The

[142-191]Dragon of Wantly" belonged to Mrs. Arabin.
