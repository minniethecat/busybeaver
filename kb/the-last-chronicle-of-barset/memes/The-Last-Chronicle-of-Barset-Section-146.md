[146-001]"I don't suppose Dan Stringer cared much about that," said the major.

[146-002]"Not a straw," said the archdeacon, and then all hurried off to

[146-003]church; and the archdeacon preached the sermon in the fabrication of

[146-004]which he had been interrupted by his son, and which therefore barely

[146-005]enabled him to turn the quarter of an hour from the giving out of his

[146-006]text. It was his constant practice to preach for full twenty minutes.

[146-007]As Barchester lay on the direct road from Plumstead to Hogglestock,

[146-008]it was thought well that word should be sent to Mr. Toogood, desiring

[146-009]him not to come out to Plumstead on the Monday morning. Major Grantly

[146-010]proposed to call for him at "The Dragon," and to take him on from

[146-011]thence to Hogglestock. "You had better take your mother's horses all

[146-012]through," said the archdeacon. The distance was very nearly twenty

[146-013]miles, and it was felt both by the mother and the son, that the

[146-014]archdeacon must be in a good humour when he made such a proposition

[146-015]as that. It was not often that the rectory carriage-horses were

[146-016]allowed to make long journeys. A run into Barchester and back, which

[146-017]altogether was under ten miles, was generally the extent of their

[146-018]work. "I meant to have posted from Barchester," said the major. "You

[146-019]may as well take the horses through," said the archdeacon. "Your

[146-020]mother will not want them. And I suppose you might as well bring your

[146-021]friend Toogood back to dinner. We'll give him a bed."

[146-022]"He must be a good sort of man," said Mrs. Grantly; "for I suppose he

[146-023]has done all this for love?"

[146-024]"Yes; and spent a lot of money out of his own pocket too!" said the

[146-025]major enthusiastically. "And the joke of it is, that he has been

[146-026]defending Crawley in Crawley's teeth. Mr. Crawley had refused to

[146-027]employ counsel; but Toogood had made up his mind to have a barrister,

[146-028]on purpose that there might be a fuss about it in court. He thought

[146-029]that it would tell with the jury in Crawley's favour."

[146-030]"Bring him here, and we'll hear all about that from himself," said

[146-031]the archdeacon. The major, before he started, told his mother that he

[146-032]should call at Framley Parsonage on his way back; but he said nothing

[146-033]on this subject to his father.

[146-034]"I'll write to her in a day or two," said Mrs. Grantly, "and we'll

[146-035]have things settled pleasantly."

[146-036]CHAPTER LXXIV.

[146-037]THE CRAWLEYS ARE INFORMED.

[146-038]Major Grantly made an early start, knowing that he had a long day's

[146-039]work before him. He had written over-night to Mr. Toogood, naming the

[146-040]hour at which he would reach "The Dragon," and was there punctual

[146-041]to the moment. When the attorney came out and got into the open

[146-042]carriage, while the groom held the steps for him, it was plain

[146-043]to be seen that the respect in which he was held at "The Dragon"

[146-044]was greatly increased. It was already known that he was going to

[146-045]Plumstead that night, and it was partly understood that he was

[146-046]engaged with the Grantly and Arabin faction in defending Mr. Crawley

[146-047]the clergyman against the Proudie faction. Dan Stringer, who was

[146-048]still at the inn, as he saw his enemy get into the Plumstead

[146-049]carriage, felt himself to be one of the palace party, and felt that

[146-050]if Mrs. Proudie had only lived till after the assizes all this heavy

[146-051]trouble would not have befallen him. The waiter with the dirty napkin

[146-052]stood at the door and bowed, thinking perhaps that as the Proudie

[146-053]party was going down in Barchester, it might be as well to be civil

[146-054]to Mr. Toogood. The days of the Stringers were probably drawing to a

[146-055]close at "The Dragon of Wantly," and there was no knowing who might

[146-056]be the new landlord.

[146-057]Henry Grantly and the lawyer found very little to say to each other

[146-058]on their long way out to Hogglestock. They were thinking, probably,

[146-059]much of the coming interview, and hardly knew how to express

[146-060]their thoughts to each other. "I will not take the carriage up to

[146-061]the house," said the major, as they were entering the parish of

[146-062]Hogglestock; "particularly as the man must feed the horses." So they

[146-063]got out at a farmhouse about half a mile from the church, where the

[146-064]offence of the carriage and livery-servant would be well out of

[146-065]Mr. Crawley's sight, and from thence walked towards the parsonage.

[146-066]The church, and the school close to it, lay on their way, and as

[146-067]they passed by the school door they heard voices within. "I'll bet

[146-068]twopence he's there," said Toogood. "They tell me he's always either

[146-069]in one shop or the other. I'll slip in and bring him out." Mr.

[146-070]Toogood had assumed a comfortable air, as though the day's work was

[146-071]to be good pastime, and even made occasional attempts at drollery. He

[146-072]had had his jokes about Dan Stringer, and had attempted to describe

[146-073]the absurdities of Mr. Crawley's visit to Bedford Row. All this would

[146-074]have angered the major, had he not seen that it was assumed to cover

[146-075]something below of which Mr. Toogood was a little ashamed, but of

[146-076]which, as the major thought, Mr. Toogood had no cause to be ashamed.

[146-077]When, therefore, Toogood proposed to go into the school and bring

[146-078]Mr. Crawley out, as though the telling of their story would be the

[146-079]easiest thing in the world, the major did not stop him. Indeed he

[146-080]had no plan of his own ready. His mind was too intent on the tragedy

[146-081]which had occurred, and which was now to be brought to a close, to

[146-082]enable him to form any plan as to the best way of getting up the last

[146-083]scene. So Mr. Toogood, with quick and easy steps, entered the school,

[146-084]leaving the major still standing in the road. Mr. Crawley was in the

[146-085]school;--as was also Jane Crawley. "So here you are," said Toogood.

[146-086]"That's fortunate. I hope I find you pretty well?"

[146-087]"If I am not mistaken in the identity, my wife's relative, Mr.

[146-088]Toogood?" said Mr. Crawley, stepping down from his humble desk.

[146-089]"Just so, my friend," said Toogood, with his hand extended, "just so;

[146-090]and there's another gentleman outside who wants to have a word with

[146-091]you also. Perhaps you won't mind stepping out. These are the young

[146-092]Hogglestockians; are they?"

[Illustration: "These are the young Hogglestockians, are they?"]

[146-093]The young Hogglestockians stared at him, and so did Jane. Jane, who

[146-094]had before heard of him, did not like him at first sight, seeing

[146-095]that her father was clearly displeased by the tone of the visitor's

[146-096]address. Mr. Crawley was displeased. There was a familiarity about

[146-097]Mr. Toogood which made him sore, as having been exhibited before his

[146-098]pupils. "If you will be pleased to step out, sir, I will follow you,"

[146-099]he said, waving his hand towards the door. "Jane, my dear, if you

[146-100]will remain with the children, I will return to you presently. Bobby

[146-101]Studge has failed in saying his Belief. You had better set him on

[146-102]again from the beginning. Now, Mr. Toogood." And again he waved with

[146-103]his hand towards the door.

[146-104]"So that's my young cousin, is it?" said Toogood, stretching over and

[146-105]just managing to touch Jane's fingers,--of which act of touching Jane

[146-106]was very chary. Then he went forth, and Mr. Crawley followed him.

[146-107]There was the major standing in the road, and Toogood was anxious to

[146-108]be the first to communicate the good news. It was the only reward he

[146-109]had proposed to himself for the money he had expended and the time he

[146-110]had lost and the trouble he had taken. "It's all right, old fellow,"

[146-111]he said, clapping his hand on Crawley's shoulder. "We've got the

[146-112]right sow by the ear at last. We know all about it." Mr. Crawley

[146-113]could hardly remember the time when he had been called an old fellow

[146-114]last, and now he did not like it; nor, in the confusion of his mind,

[146-115]could he understand the allusion to the right sow. He supposed that

[146-116]Mr. Toogood had come to him about his trial, but it did not occur to

[146-117]him that the lawyer might be bringing him news which might make the

[146-118]trial altogether unnecessary. "If my eyes are not mistaken, there is

[146-119]my friend, Major Grantly," said Mr. Crawley.

[146-120]"There he is, as large as life," said Toogood. "But stop a moment

[146-121]before you go to him, and give me your hand. I must have the first

[146-122]shake of it." Hereupon Crawley extended his hand. "That's right. And

[146-123]now let me tell you we know all about the cheque,--Soames's cheque.

[146-124]We know where you got it. We know who stole it. We know how it came

[146-125]to the person who gave it to you. It's all very well talking, but

[146-126]when you're in trouble always go to a lawyer."

[146-127]By this time Mr. Crawley was looking full into Mr. Toogood's face,

[146-128]and seeing that his cousin's eyes were streaming with tears, began

[146-129]to get some insight into the man's character, and also some very

[146-130]dim insight into the facts which the man intended to communicate to

[146-131]himself. "I do not as yet fully understand you, sir," said he, "being

[146-132]perhaps in such matters somewhat dull of intellect, but it seemeth to

[146-133]me that you are a messenger of glad tidings, whose feet are beautiful

[146-134]upon the mountains."

[146-135]"Beautiful!" said Toogood. "By George, I should think they are

[146-136]beautiful! Don't you hear me tell you that we have found out all

[146-137]about the cheque, and that you're as right as a trivet?" They were

[146-138]still on the little causeway leading from the school up to the road,

[146-139]and Henry Grantly was waiting for them at the small wicket-gate. "Mr.

[146-140]Crawley," said the major, "I congratulate you with all my heart. I

[146-141]could not but accompany my friend, Mr. Toogood, when he brought you

[146-142]this good news."

[146-143]"I do not even yet altogether comprehend what has been told to me,"

[146-144]said Crawley, now standing out on the road between the other two men.

[146-145]"I am doubtless dull,--very dull. May I beg some clearer word of

[146-146]explanation before I ask you to go with me to my wife?"

[146-147]"The cheque was given to you by my aunt Eleanor."

[146-148]"Your aunt Eleanor!" said Crawley, now altogether in the clouds. Who

[146-149]was the major's aunt Eleanor? Though he had, no doubt, at different

[146-150]times heard all the circumstances of the connection, he had never

[146-151]realized the fact that his daughter's lover was the nephew of his old

[146-152]friend, Arabin.

[146-153]"Yes; by my aunt, Mrs. Arabin."

[146-154]"She put it into the envelope with the notes," said

[146-155]Toogood;--"slipped it in without saying a word to any one. I never

[146-156]heard of a woman doing such a mad thing in my life before. If she had

[146-157]died, or if we hadn't caught her, where should we all have been? Not

[146-158]but what I think I should have run Dan Stringer to ground too, and

[146-159]worked it out of him."

[146-160]"Then, after all, it was given to me by the dean?" said Crawley,

[146-161]drawing himself up.

[146-162]"It was in the envelope, but the dean did not know it," said the

[146-163]major.

[146-164]"Gentlemen," said Mr. Crawley, "I was sure of it. I knew it. Weak as

[146-165]my mind may be,--and at times it is very weak,--I was certain that

[146-166]I could not have erred in such a matter. The more I struggled with

[146-167]my memory, the more fixed with me became the fact,--which I had

[146-168]forgotten but for a moment,--that the document had formed a part of

[146-169]that small packet handed to me by the dean. But look you, sirs,--bear

[146-170]with me yet for a moment. I said that it was so, and the dean denied

[146-171]it."

[146-172]"The dean did not know it, man," said Toogood, almost in a passion.
