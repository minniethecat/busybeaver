[043-001]When Mrs. Dale went upstairs to her own room before dinner Lily was

[043-002]not there; nor were they alone together again that evening, except

[043-003]for a moment, when Lily, as was usual, went into her mother's room

[043-004]when she was undressing. But neither of them then said a word about

[043-005]the letter. Lily during dinner and throughout the evening had borne

[043-006]herself well, giving no sign of special emotion, keeping to herself

[043-007]entirely her own thoughts about the proposition made to her. And

[043-008]afterwards she had progressed diligently with the fabrication of Mr.

[043-009]Crawley's shirts, as though she had no such letter in her pocket. And

[043-010]yet there was not a moment in which she was not thinking of it. To

[043-011]Grace, just before she went to bed, she did say one word. "I wonder

[043-012]whether it can ever come to a person to be so placed that there can

[043-013]be no doing right, let what will be done;--that, do or not do, as you

[043-014]may, it must be wrong?"

[043-015]"I hope you are not in such a condition," said Grace.

[043-016]"I am something near it," said Lily, "but perhaps if I look long

[043-017]enough I shall see the light."

[043-018]"I hope it will be a happy light at last," said Grace, who thought

[043-019]that Lily was referring only to John Eames.

[043-020]At noon on the next day Lily had still said nothing to her mother

[043-021]about the letter; and then what she said was very little. "When must

[043-022]you answer Mr. Crosbie, mamma?"

[043-023]"When, my dear?"

[043-024]"I mean how long may you take? It need not be to-day."

[043-025]"No;--certainly not to-day."

[043-026]"Then I will talk over it with you to-morrow. It wants some

[043-027]thinking;--does it not, mamma?"

[043-028]"It would not want much with me, Lily."

[043-029]"But then, mamma, you are not I. Believing as I believe, feeling as

[043-030]I feel, it wants some thinking. That's what I mean."

[043-031]"I wish I could help you, my dear."

[043-032]"You shall help me,--to-morrow." The morrow came and Lily was still

[043-033]very patient; but she had prepared herself, and had prepared the time

[043-034]also, so that in the hour of the gloaming she was alone with her

[043-035]mother, and sure that she might remain alone with her for an hour or

[043-036]so. "Mamma, sit there," she said; "I will sit down here, and then I

[043-037]can lean against you and be comfortable. You can bear as much of me

[043-038]as that,--can't you, mamma?" Then Mrs. Dale put her arm over Lily's

[043-039]shoulder, and embraced her daughter. "And now, mamma, we will talk

[043-040]about this wonderful letter."

[043-041]"I do not know, dear, that I have anything to say about it."

[043-042]"But you must have something to say about it, mamma. You must bring

[043-043]yourself to have something to say,--to have a great deal to say."

[043-044]"You know what I think as well as though I talked for a week."

[043-045]"That won't do, mamma. Come, you must not be hard with me."

[043-046]"Hard, Lily!"

[043-047]"I don't mean that you will hurt me, or not give me any food,--or

[043-048]that you will not go on caring about me more than anything else in

[043-049]the whole world ten times over;--" And Lily as she spoke tightened

[043-050]the embrace of her mother's arm round her neck. "I'm not afraid

[043-051]you'll be hard in that way. But you must soften your heart so as to

[043-052]be able to mention his name and talk about him, and tell me what I

[043-053]ought to do. You must see with my eyes, and hear with my ears, and

[043-054]feel with my heart;--and then, when I know that you have done that,

[043-055]I must judge with your judgment."

[043-056]"I wish you to use your own."

[043-057]"Yes;--because you won't see with my eyes and hear with my ears.

[043-058]That's what I call being hard. Though you should feed me with blood

[043-059]from your breast, I should call you a hard pelican, unless you could

[043-060]give me also the sympathy which I demand from you. You see, mamma, we

[043-061]have never allowed ourselves to speak of this man."

[043-062]"What need has there been, dearest?"

[043-063]"Only because we have been thinking of him. Out of the full heart the

[043-064]mouth speaketh;--that is, the mouth does so when the full heart is

[043-065]allowed to have its own way comfortably."

[043-066]"There are things which should be forgotten."

[043-067]"Forgotten, mamma!"

[043-068]"The memory of which should not be fostered by much talking."

[043-069]"I have never blamed you, mamma; never, even in my heart. I have

[043-070]known how good and gracious and sweet you have been. But I have often

[043-071]accused myself of cowardice because I have not allowed his name to

[043-072]cross my lips either to you or to Bell. To talk of forgetting such an

[043-073]accident as that is a farce. And as for fostering the memory of it--!

[043-074]Do you think that I have ever spent a night from that time to this

[043-075]without thinking of him? Do you imagine that I have ever crossed

[043-076]our own lawn, or gone down through the garden-path there, without

[043-077]thinking of the times when he and I walked there together? There

[043-078]needs no fostering for such memories as those. They are weeds which

[043-079]will grow rank and strong though nothing be done to foster them.

[043-080]There is the earth and the rain, and that is enough for them. You

[043-081]cannot kill them if you would, and they certainly will not die

[043-082]because you are careful not to hoe and rake the ground."

[043-083]"Lily, you forget how short the time has been as yet."

[043-084]"I have thought it very long; but the truth is, mamma, that this

[043-085]non-fostering of memories, as you call it, has not been the real

[043-086]cause of our silence. We have not spoken of Mr. Crosbie because we

[043-087]have not thought alike about him. Had you spoken you would have

[043-088]spoken with anger, and I could not endure to hear him abused. That

[043-089]has been it."

[043-090]"Partly so, Lily."

[043-091]"Now you must talk of him, and you must not abuse him. We must talk

[043-092]of him, because something must be done about his letter. Even if it

[043-093]be left unanswered, it cannot be so left without discussion. And yet

[043-094]you must say no evil of him."

[043-095]"Am I to think that he behaved well?"

[043-096]"No, mamma; you are not to think that; but you are to look upon his

[043-097]fault as a fault that has been forgiven."

[043-098]"It cannot be forgotten, dear."

[043-099]"But, mamma, when you go to heaven--"

[043-100]"My dear!"

[043-101]"But you will go to heaven, mamma, and why should I not speak of it?

[043-102]You will go to heaven, and yet I suppose you have been very wicked,

[043-103]because we are all very wicked. But you won't be told of your

[043-104]wickedness there. You won't be hated there, because you were this or

[043-105]that when you were here."

[043-106]"I hope not, Lily; but isn't your argument almost profane?"

[043-107]"No; I don't think so. We ask to be forgiven just as we forgive. That

[043-108]is the way in which we hope to be forgiven, and therefore it is the

[043-109]way in which we ought to forgive. When you say that prayer at night,

[043-110]mamma, do you ever ask yourself whether you have forgiven him?"

[043-111]"I forgive him as far as humanity can forgive. I would do him no

[043-112]injury."

[043-113]"But if you and I are forgiven only after that fashion we shall never

[043-114]get to heaven." Lily paused for some further answer from her mother,

[043-115]but as Mrs. Dale was silent she allowed that portion of the subject

[043-116]to pass as completed. "And now, mamma, what answer do you think we

[043-117]ought to send to his letter?"

[043-118]"My dear, how am I to say? You know I have said already that if I

[043-119]could act on my own judgment, I would send none."

[043-120]"But that was said in the bitterness of gall."

[043-121]"Come, Lily, say what you think yourself. We shall get on better when

[043-122]you have brought yourself to speak. Do you think that you wish to see

[043-123]him again?"

[043-124]"I don't know, mamma. Upon the whole, I think not."

[043-125]"Then in heaven's name let me write and tell him so."

[043-126]"Stop a moment, mamma. There are two persons here to be

[043-127]considered,--or rather, three."

[043-128]"I would not have you think of me in such a question."

[043-129]"I know you would not; but never mind, and let me go on. The three of

[043-130]us are concerned, at any rate; you, and he, and I. I am thinking of

[043-131]him now. We have all suffered, but I do believe that hitherto he has

[043-132]had the worst of it."

[043-133]"And who has deserved the worst?"

[043-134]"Mamma, how can you go back in that way? We have agreed that that

[043-135]should be regarded as done and gone. He has been very unhappy, and

[043-136]now we see what remedy he proposes to himself for his misery. Do I

[043-137]flatter myself if I allow myself to look at it in that way?"

[043-138]"Perhaps he thinks he is offering a remedy for your misery."

[043-139]As this was said Lily turned round slowly and looked up into her

[043-140]mother's face. "Mamma," she said, "that is very cruel. I did not

[043-141]think you could be so cruel. How can you, who believe him to be so

[043-142]selfish, think that?"

[043-143]"It is very hard to judge of men's motives. I have never supposed him

[043-144]to be so black that he would not wish to make atonement for the evil

[043-145]he has done."

[043-146]"If I thought that there certainly could be but one answer."

[043-147]"Who can look into a man's heart and judge all the sources of his

[043-148]actions? There are mixed feelings there, no doubt. Remorse for what

[043-149]he has done; regret for what he has lost;--something, perhaps, of the

[043-150]purity of love."

[043-151]"Yes, something,--I hope something,--for his sake."

[043-152]"But when a horse kicks and bites, you know his nature and do not go

[043-153]near him. When a man has cheated you once, you think he will cheat

[043-154]you again, and you do not deal with him. You do not look to gather

[043-155]grapes from thistles, after you have found that they are thistles."

[043-156]"I still go for the roses though I have often torn my hand with

[043-157]thorns in looking for them."

[043-158]"But you do not pluck those that have become cankered in the

[043-159]blowing."

[043-160]"Because he was once at fault, will he be cankered always?"

[043-161]"I would not trust him."

[043-162]"Now, mamma, see how different we are; or, rather, how different it

[043-163]is when one judges for oneself or for another. If it were simply

[043-164]myself, and my own future fate in life, I would trust him with it all

[043-165]to-morrow, without a word. I should go to him as a gambler goes to

[043-166]the gambling-table, knowing that if I lost everything I could hardly

[043-167]be poorer than I was before. But I should have a better hope than the

[043-168]gambler is justified in having. That, however, is not my difficulty.

[043-169]And when I think of him I can see a prospect of success for the

[043-170]gambler. I think so well of myself that, loving him, as I do;--yes,

[043-171]mamma, do not be uneasy;--loving him, as I do, I believe I could be a

[043-172]comfort to him. I think that he might be better with me than without

[043-173]me. That is, he would be so, if he could teach himself to look back

[043-174]upon the past as I can do, and to judge of me as I can judge of him."

[043-175]"He has nothing, at least, for which to condemn you."

[043-176]"But he would have, were I to marry him now. He would condemn me

[043-177]because I had forgiven him. He would condemn me because I had borne

[043-178]what he had done to me, and had still loved him--loved him through it

[043-179]all. He would feel and know the weakness;--and there is weakness. I

[043-180]have been weak in not being able to rid myself of him altogether. He

[043-181]would recognize this after awhile, and would despise me for it. But

[043-182]he would not see what there is of devotion to him in my being able to

[043-183]bear the taunts of the world in going back to him, and your taunts,

[043-184]and my own taunts. I should have to bear his also,--not spoken aloud,

[043-185]but to be seen in his face and heard in his voice,--and that I could

[043-186]not endure. If he despised me, and he would, that would make us both

[043-187]unhappy. Therefore, mamma, tell him not to come; tell him that he can

[043-188]never come; but, if it be possible, tell him this tenderly." Then she

[043-189]got up and walked away, as though she were going out of the room; but

[043-190]her mother had caught her before the door was opened.
