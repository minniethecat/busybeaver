## Geography

Allington is a country estate situated near Guestwick, in a county neighbouring Barsetshire. It is the seat of the Dale family and serves as a pastoral retreat from the legal and social turmoil that engulfs the Crawley family in Barsetshire. When Grace Crawley's position at home becomes untenable during her father's disgrace, "Mr. Dale had asked her to go to Allington" (016-182), and "to Allington she went" (017-002). The journey from Hogglestock to Allington — "driven over to Allington in a pony carriage" (017-167) — marks a passage from the zone of scandal into a haven of gentry civility.

## The Dale Household

Allington is the home of Christopher Dale, the Squire of Allington, and his niece Lily Dale. The household represents the gentry values of hospitality and personal loyalty that Trollope consistently celebrates. When Grace arrives at Allington, she finds a household that, unlike the ecclesiastical circles of Barchester, judges her by her character rather than her father's alleged crime. The letter from Allington to Grace — "Allington, — December, 186—" (010-019) — sets in motion one of the novel's crucial movements.

## Cultural Significance

Allington in *The Last Chronicle of Barset* functions as a space outside the ecclesiastical jurisdiction of Barchester. It belongs to a neighbouring county and to a different order of social organisation — the gentry estate rather than the cathedral close. This geographical and social distance makes it the natural refuge for Grace Crawley, who can escape not only the material privations of Hogglestock but also the moral scrutiny of the Barchester establishment. For readers of Trollope's larger oeuvre, Allington is also the setting of *The Small House at Allington*, the fifth Barsetshire novel, and the home of Lily Dale, one of Trollope's most discussed heroines. Its appearance in *The Last Chronicle* thus serves as a bridge between the two novels and a reminder of the interconnected world Trollope built across the series.
