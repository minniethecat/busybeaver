[147-001]"Bear with me yet awhile. So far have I been from misdoubting the

[147-002]dean,--whom I have long known to be in all things a true and honest

[147-003]gentleman,--that I postponed the elaborated result of my own memory

[147-004]to his word. And I felt myself the more constrained to do this,

[147-005]because, in a moment of forgetfulness, in the wantonness of

[147-006]inconsiderate haste, with wicked thoughtlessness, I had allowed

[147-007]myself to make a false statement,--unwittingly false, indeed,

[147-008]nathless very false, unpardonably false. I had declared, without

[147-009]thinking, that the money had come to me from the hands of Mr. Soames,

[147-010]thereby seeming to cast a reflection upon that gentleman. When I had

[147-011]been guilty of so great a blunder, of so gross a violation of that

[147-012]ordinary care which should govern all words between man and man,

[147-013]especially when any question of money may be in doubt,--how could I

[147-014]expect that any one should accept my statement when contravened by

[147-015]that made by the dean? How, in such an embarrassment, could I believe

[147-016]my own memory? Gentlemen, I did not believe my own memory. Though all

[147-017]the little circumstances of that envelope, with its rich but perilous

[147-018]freightage, came back upon me from time to time with an exactness

[147-019]that has appeared to me to be almost marvellous, yet I have told

[147-020]myself that it was not so! Gentlemen, if you please, we will go

[147-021]into the house; my wife is there, and should no longer be left in

[147-022]suspense." They passed on in silence for a few steps, till Crawley

[147-023]spoke again. "Perhaps you will allow me the privilege to be alone

[147-024]with her for one minute,--but for a minute. Her thanks shall not be

[147-025]delayed, where thanks are so richly due."

[147-026]"Of course," said Toogood, wiping his eyes with a large red bandana

[147-027]handkerchief. "By all means. We'll take a little walk. Come along,

[147-028]major." The major had turned his face away, and he also was weeping.

[147-029]"By George! I never heard such a thing in all my life," said Toogood.

[147-030]"I wouldn't have believed it if I hadn't seen it. I wouldn't, indeed.

[147-031]If I were to tell that up in London, nobody would believe me."

[147-032]"I call that man a hero," said Grantly.

[147-033]"I don't know about being a hero. I never quite knew what makes a

[147-034]hero, if it isn't having three or four girls dying in love for you

[147-035]at once. But to find a man who was going to let everything in the

[147-036]world go against him, because he believed another fellow better than

[147-037]himself! There's many a chap thinks another man is wool-gathering;

[147-038]but this man has thought he was wool-gathering himself! It's not

[147-039]natural; and the world wouldn't go on if there were many like that.

[147-040]He's beckoning, and we had better go in."

[147-041]Mr. Toogood went first, and the major followed him. When they entered

[147-042]the front door they saw the skirt of a woman's dress flitting away

[147-043]through the door at the end of the passage, and on entering the room

[147-044]to the left they found Mr. Crawley alone. "She has fled, as though

[147-045]from an enemy," he said, with a little attempt at a laugh; "but I

[147-046]will pursue her, and bring her back."

[147-047]"No, Crawley, no," said the lawyer. "She's a little upset, and all

[147-048]that kind of thing. We know what women are. Let her alone."

[147-049]"Nay, Mr. Toogood; but then she would be angered with herself

[147-050]afterwards, and would lack the comfort of having spoken a word of

[147-051]gratitude. Pardon me, Major Grantly; but I would not have you leave

[147-052]us till she has seen you. It is as her cousin says. She is somewhat

[147-053]over-excited. But still it will be best that she should see you.

[147-054]Gentlemen, you will excuse me."

[147-055]Then he went out to fetch his wife, and while he was away not a word

[147-056]was spoken. The major looked out of one window and Mr. Toogood out

[147-057]of the other, and they waited patiently till they heard the coming

[147-058]steps of the husband and wife. When the door was opened, Mr. Crawley

[147-059]appeared, leading his wife by the hand. "My dear," he said, "you know

[147-060]Major Grantly. This is your cousin, Mr. Toogood. It is well that

[147-061]you know him too, and remember his great kindness to us." But Mrs.

[147-062]Crawley could not speak. She could only sink on the sofa, and hide

[147-063]her face, while she strove in vain to repress her sobs. She had been

[147-064]very strong through all her husband's troubles,--very strong in

[147-065]bearing for him what he could not bear for himself, and in fighting

[147-066]on his behalf battles in which he was altogether unable to couch

[147-067]a lance; but the endurance of so many troubles, and the great

[147-068]overwhelming sorrow at last, had so nearly overpowered her, that she

[147-069]could not sustain the shock of this turn in their fortunes. "She was

[147-070]never like this, sirs, when ill news came to us," said Mr. Crawley,

[147-071]standing somewhat apart from her.

[147-072]The major sat himself by her side, and put his hand upon hers, and

[147-073]whispered some word to her about her daughter. Upon this she threw

[147-074]her arms around him, and kissed his face, and then his hands, and

[147-075]then looked up into his face through her tears. She murmured some few

[147-076]words, or attempted to do so. I doubt whether the major understood

[147-077]their meaning, but he knew very well what was in her heart.

[147-078]"And now I think we might as well be moving," said Mr. Toogood. "I'll

[147-079]see about having the indictment quashed. I'll arrange all that with

[147-080]Walker. It may be necessary that you should go into Barchester the

[147-081]first day the judges sit; and if so, I'll come and fetch you. You may

[147-082]be sure I won't leave the place till it's all square."

[147-083]As they were going, Grantly,--speaking now altogether with

[147-084]indifference as to Toogood's presence,--asked Mr. Crawley's leave to

[147-085]be the bearer of these tidings to his daughter.

[147-086]"She can hear it in no tones that can be more grateful to her," said

[147-087]Mr. Crawley.

[147-088]"I shall ask her for nothing for myself now," said Grantly. "It would

[147-089]be ungenerous. But hereafter,--in a few days,--when she shall be more

[147-090]at ease, may I then use your permission--?"

[147-091]"Major Grantly," said Mr. Crawley, solemnly, "I respect you so

[147-092]highly, and esteem you so thoroughly, that I give willingly that

[147-093]which you ask. If my daughter can bring herself to regard you, as a

[147-094]woman should regard her husband, with the love that can worship and

[147-095]cling and be constant, she will, I think, have a fair promise of

[147-096]worldly happiness. And for you, sir, in giving to you my girl,--if

[147-097]so it be that she is given to you,--I shall bestow upon you a great

[147-098]treasure." Had Grace been a king's daughter, with a queen's dowry,

[147-099]the permission to address her could not have been imparted to her

[147-100]lover with a more thorough appreciation of the value of the privilege

[147-101]conferred.

[147-102]"He is a rum 'un," said Mr. Toogood, as they got into the carriage

[147-103]together; "but they say he's a very good 'un to go."

[147-104]After their departure Jane was sent for, that she might hear the

[147-105]family news; and when she expressed some feeling not altogether in

[147-106]favour of Mr. Toogood, Mr. Crawley thus strove to correct her views.

[147-107]"He is a man, my dear, who conceals a warm heart, and an active

[147-108]spirit, and healthy sympathies, under an affected jocularity of

[147-109]manner, and almost with a touch of assumed vulgarity. But when the

[147-110]jewel itself is good, any fault in the casket may be forgiven."

[147-111]"Then, papa, the next time I see him I'll like him,--if I can," said

[147-112]Jane.

[147-113]The village of Framley lies slightly off the road from Hogglestock

[147-114]to Barchester,--so much so as to add perhaps a mile to the journey

[147-115]if the traveller goes by the parsonage gate. On their route to

[147-116]Hogglestock our two travellers had passed Framley without visiting

[147-117]the village, but on the return journey the major asked Mr. Toogood's

[147-118]permission to make the deviation. "I'm not in a hurry," said Toogood.

[147-119]"I never was more comfortable in my life. I'll just light a cigar

[147-120]while you go in and see your friends." Toogood lit his cigar, and

[147-121]the major, getting down from the carriage, entered the parsonage. It

[147-122]was his fortune to find Grace alone. Robarts was in Barchester, and

[147-123]Mrs. Robarts was across the road, at Lufton Court. "Miss Crawley was

[147-124]certainly in," the servant told him, and he soon found himself in

[147-125]Miss Crawley's presence.

[147-126]"I have only called to tell you the news about your father," said he.

[147-127]"What news?"

[147-128]"We have just come from Hogglestock,--your cousin, Mr. Toogood, that

[147-129]is, and myself. They have found out all about the cheque. My aunt,

[147-130]Mrs. Arabin, the dean's wife, you know,--she gave it to your father."

[147-131]"Oh, Major Grantly!"

[147-132]"It seems so easily settled, does it not?"

[147-133]"And is it settled?"

[147-134]"Yes; everything. Everything about that." Now he had hold of her hand

[147-135]as if he were going. "Good-by. I told your father that I would just

[147-136]call and tell you."

[147-137]"It seems almost more than I can believe."

[147-138]"You may believe it; indeed you may." He still held her hand. "You

[147-139]will write to your mother I daresay to-night. Tell her I was here.

[147-140]Good-by now."

[147-141]"Good-by," she said. Her hand was still in his, as she looked up into

[147-142]his face.

[147-143]"Dear, dear, dearest Grace! My darling Grace!" Then he took her

[147-144]into his arms and kissed her, and went his way without another word,

[147-145]feeling that he had kept his word to her father like a gentleman.

[147-146]Grace, when she was left alone, thought that she was the happiest

[147-147]girl in Christendom. If she could only get to her mother, and tell

[147-148]everything, and be told everything! She had no idea of any promise

[147-149]that her lover might have made to her father, nor did she make

[147-150]inquiry of her own thoughts as to his reasons for staying with her so

[147-151]short a time; but looking back at it all she thought his conduct had

[147-152]been perfect.

[147-153]In the meantime the major, with Mr. Toogood, was driven home to

[147-154]dinner at Plumstead.

[147-155]CHAPTER LXXV.

[147-156]MADALINA'S HEART IS BLEEDING.

[147-157]John Eames, as soon as he had left Mrs. Arabin at the hotel and had

[147-158]taken his travelling-bag to his own lodgings, started off for his

[147-159]uncle Toogood's house. There he found Mrs. Toogood, not in the most

[147-160]serene state of mind as to her husband's absence. Mr. Toogood had

[147-161]now been at Barchester for the best part of a week,--spending a good

[147-162]deal of money at the inn. Mrs. Toogood was quite sure that he must

[147-163]be doing that. Indeed, how could he help himself? Johnny remarked

[147-164]that he did not see how in such circumstances his uncle was to help

[147-165]himself. And then Mr. Toogood had only written one short scrap of a

[147-166]letter,--just three words, and they were written in triumph. "Crawley

[147-167]is all right, and I think I've got the real Simon Pure by the heels."

[147-168]"It's all very well, John," Mrs. Toogood said; "and of course it

[147-169]would be a terrible thing to the family if anybody connected with

[147-170]it were made out to be a thief." "It would be quite dreadful," said

[147-171]Johnny. "Not that I ever looked upon the Crawleys as connections of

[147-172]ours. But, however, let that pass. I'm sure I'm very glad that your

[147-173]uncle should have been able to be of service to them. But there's

[147-174]reason in the roasting of eggs, and I can tell you that money is

[147-175]not so plenty in this house, that your uncle can afford to throw it

[147-176]into the Barchester gutters. Think what twelve children are, John.

[147-177]It might be all very well if Toogood were a bachelor, and if some

[147-178]lord had left him a fortune." John Eames did not stay very long in

[147-179]Tavistock Square. His cousins Polly and Lucy were gone to the play

[147-180]with Mr. Summerkin, and his aunt was not in one of her best humours.

[147-181]He took his uncle's part as well as he could, and then left Mrs.

[147-182]Toogood. The little allusion to Lord De Guest's generosity had not

[147-183]been pleasant to him. It seemed to rob him of all his own merit. He

[147-184]had been rather proud of his journey to Italy, having contrived to

[147-185]spend nearly forty pounds in ten days. He had done everything in the

[147-186]most expensive way, feeling that every napoleon wasted had been laid

[147-187]out on behalf of Mr. Crawley. But, as Mrs. Toogood had just told

[147-188]him, all this was nothing to what Toogood was doing. Toogood with

[147-189]twelve children was living at his own charges at Barchester, and

[147-190]was neglecting his business besides. "There's Mr. Crump," said Mrs.

[147-191]Toogood. "Of course he doesn't like it, and what can I say to him

[147-192]when he comes to me?" This was not quite fair on the part of Mrs.

[147-193]Toogood, as Mr. Crump had not troubled her even once as yet since her

[147-194]husband's departure.
