[138-001]"Of course I am anxious for my wife and children," said Crawley,

[138-002]repeating his words; "how anxious, I fancy no man can conceive who

[138-003]has not been near enough to absolute want to know how terrible is its

[138-004]approach when it threatens those who are weak and who are very dear!

[138-005]But, Mr. Robarts, you spoke just now of the chance of the thing,--the

[138-006]chance of your arranging on my behalf that I should for a while

[138-007]longer be left in the enjoyment of the freehold of my parish. It

[138-008]seemeth to me that there should be no chance on such a subject;

[138-009]that in the adjustment of so momentous a matter there should be a

[138-010]consideration of right and wrong, and no consideration of aught

[138-011]beside. I have been growing to feel, for some weeks past, that

[138-012]circumstances,--whether through my own fault or not is an outside

[138-013]question as to which I will not further delay you by offering even an

[138-014]opinion,--that unfortunate circumstances have made me unfit to remain

[138-015]here as guardian of the souls of the people of this parish. Then

[138-016]there came to me the letter from Dr. Tempest,--for which I am greatly

[138-017]beholden to him,--strengthening me altogether in this view. What

[138-018]could I do then, Mr. Robarts? Could I allow myself to think of my

[138-019]wife and my children when such a question as that was before me for

[138-020]self-discussion?"

[138-021]"I would,--certainly," said Robarts.

[138-022]"No, sir! Excuse the bluntness of my contradiction, but I feel

[138-023]assured that in such emergency you would look solely to duty,--as by

[138-024]God's help, I will endeavour to do. Mr. Robarts, there are many of us

[138-025]who in many things, are much worse than we believe ourselves to be.

[138-026]But in other matters, and perhaps of larger moment, we can rise to

[138-027]ideas of duty as the need for such ideas comes upon us. I say not

[138-028]this at all as praising myself. I speak of men as I believe that

[138-029]they will be found to be;--of yourself, of myself, and of others who

[138-030]strive to live with clean hands and a clear conscience. I do not for

[138-031]a moment think that you would retain your benefice at Framley if

[138-032]there had come upon you, after much thought, an assured conviction

[138-033]that you could not retain it without grievous injury to the souls of

[138-034]others and grievous sin to your own. Wife and children, dear as they

[138-035]are to you and to me,--as dear to me as to you,--fade from the sight

[138-036]when the time comes for judgment on such a matter as that!" They were

[138-037]standing quite still now, facing each other, and Crawley, as he spoke

[138-038]with a low voice, looked straight into his friend's eyes, and kept

[138-039]his hand firmly fixed on his friend's arm.

[138-040]"I cannot interfere further," said Robarts.

[138-041]"No,--you cannot interfere further." Robarts, when he told the story

[138-042]of the interview to his wife that evening, declared that he had never

[138-043]heard a voice so plaintively touching as was the voice of Mr. Crawley

[138-044]when he uttered those last words.

[138-045]They returned back to the servant and the house almost without a

[138-046]word, and Robarts mounted without offering to see Mrs. Crawley. Nor

[138-047]did Mr. Crawley ask him to do so. It was better now that Robarts

[138-048]should go. "May God send you through all your troubles," said Mr.

[138-049]Robarts.

[138-050]"Mr. Robarts, I thank you warmly, for your friendship," said Mr.

[138-051]Crawley. And then they parted. In about half an hour Mr. Crawley

[138-052]returned to the house. "Now for Pindar, Jane," he said, seating

[138-053]himself at his old desk.

[138-054]CHAPTER LXIX.

[138-055]MR. CRAWLEY'S LAST APPEARANCE IN HIS OWN PULPIT.

[138-056]No word or message from Mr. Crawley reached Barchester throughout

[138-057]the week, and on the Sunday morning Mr. Thumble was under a positive

[138-058]engagement to go out to Hogglestock, and perform the services of the

[138-059]church. Dr. Tempest had been quite right in saying that Mr. Thumble

[138-060]would be awed by the death of his patroness. Such was altogether

[138-061]the case, and he was very anxious to escape from the task he had

[138-062]undertaken at her instance, if it were possible. In the first place,

[138-063]he had never been a favourite with the bishop himself, and had now,

[138-064]therefore, nothing to expect in the diocese. The crusts from bits of

[138-065]loaves and the morsels of broken fishes which had come in his way

[138-066]had all come from the bounty of Mrs. Proudie. And then, as regarded

[138-067]this special Hogglestock job, how was he to get paid for it? Whence,

[138-068]indeed, was he to seek repayment for the actual money which he would

[138-069]be out of pocket in finding his way to Hogglestock and back again?

[138-070]But he could not get to speak to the bishop, nor could he induce any

[138-071]one who had access to his lordship to touch upon the subject. Mr.

[138-072]Snapper avoided him as much as possible; and Mr. Snapper, when he

[138-073]was caught and interrogated, declared that he regarded the matter as

[138-074]settled. Nothing could be in worse taste, Mr. Snapper thought, than

[138-075]to undo, immediately after the poor lady's death, work in the diocese

[138-076]which had been arranged and done by her. Mr. Snapper expressed his

[138-077]opinion that Mr. Thumble was bound to go out to Hogglestock; and,

[138-078]when Mr. Thumble declared petulantly that he would not stir a step

[138-079]out of Barchester, Mr. Snapper protested that Mr. Thumble would have

[138-080]to answer for it in this world and in the next if there were no

[138-081]services at Hogglestock on that Sunday. On the Saturday evening Mr.

[138-082]Thumble made a desperate attempt to see the bishop, but was told by

[138-083]Mrs. Draper that the bishop had positively declined to see him. The

[138-084]bishop himself probably felt unwilling to interfere with his wife's

[138-085]doings so soon after her death! So Mr. Thumble, with a heavy heart,

[138-086]went across to "The Dragon of Wantly," and ordered a gig, resolving

[138-087]that the bill should be sent in to the palace. He was not going to

[138-088]trust himself again upon the bishop's cob!

[138-089]Up to Saturday evening Mr. Crawley did the work of his parish, and

[138-090]on the Saturday evening he made an address to his parishioners from

[138-091]his pulpit. He had given notice among the brickmakers and labourers

[138-092]that he wished to say a few words to them in the school-room; but the

[138-093]farmers also heard of this and came with their wives and daughters,

[138-094]and all the brickmakers came, and most of the labourers were

[138-095]there, so that there was no room for them in the school-house. The

[138-096]congregation was much larger than was customary even in the church.

[138-097]"They will come," he said to his wife, "to hear a ruined man declare

[138-098]his own ruin, but they will not come to hear the word of God."

[138-099]When it was found that the persons assembled were too many for the

[138-100]school-room, the meeting was adjourned to the church, and Mr. Crawley

[138-101]was forced to get into his pulpit. He said a short prayer, and then

[138-102]he began his story.

[Illustration: "They will come to hear a ruined man

[138-103]declare his own ruin."]

[138-104]His story as he told it then shall not be repeated now, as the same

[138-105]story has been told too often already in these pages. Surely it was a

[138-106]singular story for a parish clergyman to tell of himself in so solemn

[138-107]a manner. That he had applied the cheque to his own purposes, and was

[138-108]unable to account for the possession of it, was certain. He did not

[138-109]know when or how he had got it. Speaking to them then in God's house

[138-110]he told them that. He was to be tried by a jury, and all he could

[138-111]do was to tell the jury the same. He would not expect the jury to

[138-112]believe him. The jury would, of course, believe only that which was

[138-113]proved to them. But he did expect his old friends at Hogglestock,

[138-114]who had known him so long, to take his word as true. That there was

[138-115]no sufficient excuse for his conduct, even in his own sight, this,

[138-116]his voluntary resignation of his parish, was, he said, sufficient

[138-117]evidence. Then he explained to them, as clearly as he was able, what

[138-118]the bishop had done, what the commission had done, and what he had

[138-119]done himself. That he spoke no word of Mrs. Proudie to that audience

[138-120]need hardly be mentioned here. "And now, dearest friends, I leave

[138-121]you," he said, with that weighty solemnity which was so peculiar to

[138-122]the man, and which he was able to make singularly impressive even on

[138-123]such a congregation as that of Hogglestock, "and I trust that the

[138-124]heavy but pleasing burden of the charge which I have had over you may

[138-125]fall into hands better fitted than mine have been for such work. I

[138-126]have always known my own unfitness, by reason of the worldly cares

[138-127]with which I have been laden. Poverty makes the spirit poor, and the

[138-128]hands weak, and the heart sore,--and too often makes the conscience

[138-129]dull. May the latter never be the case with any of you." Then he

[138-130]uttered another short prayer, and, stepping down from the pulpit,

[138-131]walked out of the church, with his weeping wife hanging on his arm,

[138-132]and his daughter following them, almost dissolved in tears. He never

[138-133]again entered that church as the pastor of the congregation.

[138-134]There was an old lame man from Hoggle End leaning on his stick near

[138-135]the door as Mr. Crawley went out, and with him was his old lame wife.

[138-136]"He'll pull through yet," said the old man to his wife; "you'll see

[138-137]else. He'll pull through because he's so dogged. It's dogged as does

[138-138]it."

[138-139]On that night the position of the members of Mr. Crawley's household

[138-140]seemed to have been changed. There was something almost of elation

[138-141]in his mode of speaking, and he said soft loving words, striving

[138-142]to comfort his wife. She, on the other hand, could say nothing to

[138-143]comfort him. She had been averse to the step he was taking, but

[138-144]had been unable to press her objection in opposition to his great

[138-145]argument as to duty. Since he had spoken to her in that strain which

[138-146]he had used with Robarts, she also had felt that she must be silent.

[138-147]But she could not even feign to feel the pride which comes from the

[138-148]performance of a duty. "What will he do when he comes out?" she said

[138-149]to her daughter. The coming out spoken of by her was the coming out

[138-150]of prison. It was natural enough that she should feel no elation.

[138-151]The breakfast on Sunday morning was to her, perhaps, the saddest

[138-152]scene of her life. They sat down, the three together, at the usual

[138-153]hour,--nine o'clock,--but the morning had not been passed as was

[138-154]customary on Sundays. It had been Mr. Crawley's practice to go into

[138-155]the school from eight to nine; but on this Sunday he felt, as he

[138-156]told his wife, that his presence would be an intrusion there. But

[138-157]he requested Jane to go and perform her usual task. "If Mr. Thumble

[138-158]should come," he said to her, "be submissive to him in all things."

[138-159]Then he stood at his door, watching to see at what hour Mr. Thumble

[138-160]would reach the school. But Mr. Thumble did not attend the school on

[138-161]that morning. "And yet he was very express to me in his desire that

[138-162]I would not myself meddle with the duties," said Mr. Crawley to his

[138-163]wife as he stood at the door,--"unnecessarily urgent, as I must say

[138-164]I thought at the time." If Mrs. Crawley could have spoken out her

[138-165]thoughts about Mr. Thumble at that moment, her words would, I think,

[138-166]have surprised her husband.

[138-167]At breakfast there was hardly a word spoken. Mr. Crawley took his

[138-168]crust and eat it mournfully,--almost ostentatiously. Jane tried and

[138-169]failed, and tried to hide her failure, failing in that also. Mrs.

[138-170]Crawley made no attempt. She sat behind her old teapot, with her

[138-171]hands clasped and her eyes fixed. It was as though some last day had

[138-172]come upon her,--this, the first Sunday of her husband's degradation.

[138-173]"Mary," he said to her, "why do you not eat?"
