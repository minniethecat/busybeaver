[133-001]And it was still the affair of Mr. Crawley which urged her on to

[133-002]further action. When the bishop received Mr. Crawley's letter he said

[133-003]nothing of it to her; but he handed it over to his chaplain. The

[133-004]chaplain, fearing to act upon it himself, handed it to Mr. Thumble,

[133-005]whom he knew to be one of the bishop's commission, and Mr. Thumble,

[133-006]equally fearing responsibility in the present state of affairs at the

[133-007]palace, found himself obliged to consult Mrs. Proudie. Mrs. Proudie

[133-008]had no doubt as to what should be done. The man had abdicated his

[133-009]living, and of course some provision must be made for the services.

[133-010]She would again make an attempt upon her husband, and therefore she

[133-011]went into his room holding Mr. Crawley's letter in her hand.

[133-012]"My dear," she said, "here is Mr. Crawley's letter. I suppose you

[133-013]have read it?"

[133-014]"Yes," said the bishop; "I have read it."

[133-015]"And what will you do about it? Something must be done."

[133-016]"I don't know," said he. He did not even look at her as he spoke. He

[133-017]had not turned his eyes upon her since she had entered the room.

[133-018]"But, bishop, it is a letter that requires to be acted upon at once.

[133-019]We cannot doubt that the man is doing right at last. He is submitting

[133-020]himself where his submission is due; but his submission will be of no

[133-021]avail unless you take some action upon his letter. Do you not think

[133-022]that Mr. Thumble had better go over?"

[133-023]"No, I don't. I think Mr. Thumble had better stay where he is," said

[133-024]the irritated bishop.

[133-025]"What, then, would you wish to have done?"

[133-026]"Never mind," said he.

[133-027]"But, bishop, that is nonsense," said Mrs. Proudie, adding something

[133-028]of severity to the tone of her voice.

[133-029]"No, it isn't nonsense," said he. Still he did not look at her, nor

[133-030]had he done so for a moment since she had entered the room. Mrs.

[133-031]Proudie could not bear this, and as her anger became strong within

[133-032]her breast, she told herself that she would be wrong to bear it. She

[133-033]had tried what gentleness would do, and she had failed. It was now

[133-034]imperatively necessary that she should resort to sterner measures.

[133-035]She must make him understand that he must give her authority to send

[133-036]Mr. Thumble to Hogglestock.

[133-037]"Why do you not turn round and speak to me properly?" she said.

[133-038]"I do not want to speak to you at all," the bishop answered.

[133-039]This was very bad;--almost anything would be better than this. He was

[133-040]sitting now over the fire, with his elbows on his knees, and his face

[133-041]buried in his hands. She had gone round the room so as to face him,

[133-042]and was now standing almost over him, but still she could not see his

[133-043]countenance. "This will not do at all," she said. "My dear, do you

[133-044]know that you are forgetting yourself altogether?"

[133-045]"I wish I could forget myself."

[133-046]"That might be all very well if you were in a position in which you

[133-047]owed no service to any one; or, rather, it would not be well then,

[133-048]but the evil would not be so manifest. You cannot do your duty in the

[133-049]diocese if you continue to sit there doing nothing, with your head

[133-050]upon your hands. Why do you not rally, and get to your work like a

[133-051]man?"

[133-052]"I wish you would go away and leave me," he said.

[133-053]"No, bishop, I will not go away and leave you. You have brought

[133-054]yourself to such a condition that it is my duty as your wife to stay

[133-055]by you; and if you neglect your duty, I will not neglect mine."

[133-056]"It was you that brought me to it."

[133-057]"No, sir, that is not true. I did not bring you to it."

[133-058]"It is the truth." And now he got up and looked at her. For a moment

[133-059]he stood upon his legs, and then again he sat down with his face

[133-060]turned towards her. "It is the truth. You have brought on me such

[133-061]disgrace that I cannot hold up my head. You have ruined me. I wish I

[133-062]were dead; and it is all through you that I am driven to wish it."

[133-063]Of all that she had suffered in her life this was the worst. She

[133-064]clasped both her hands to her side as she listened to him, and for

[133-065]a minute or two she made no reply. When he ceased from speaking he

[133-066]again put his elbows on his knees and again buried his face in his

[133-067]hands. What had she better do, or how was it expedient that she

[133-068]should treat him? At this crisis the whole thing was so important to

[133-069]her that she would have postponed her own ambition and would have

[133-070]curbed her temper had she thought that by doing so she might in any

[133-071]degree have benefited him. But it seemed to her that she could not

[133-072]rouse him by conciliation. Neither could she leave him as he was.

[133-073]Something must be done. "Bishop," she said, "the words that you speak

[133-074]are sinful, very sinful."

[133-075]"You have made them sinful," he replied.

[133-076]"I will not hear that from you. I will not indeed. I have endeavoured

[133-077]to do my duty by you, and I do not deserve it. I am endeavouring to

[133-078]do my duty now, and you must know that it would ill become me to

[133-079]remain quiescent while you are in such a state. The world around you

[133-080]is observing you, and knows that you are not doing your work. All I

[133-081]want of you is that you should arouse yourself, and go to your work."

[133-082]"I could do my work very well," he said, "if you were not here."

[133-083]"I suppose, then, you wish that I were dead?" said Mrs. Proudie.

[133-084]To this he made no reply, nor did he stir himself. How could flesh

[133-085]and blood bear this,--female flesh and blood,--Mrs. Proudie's flesh

[133-086]and blood? Now, at last, her temper once more got the better of her

[133-087]judgment, probably much to her immediate satisfaction, and she spoke

[133-088]out. "I'll tell you what it is, my lord; if you are imbecile, I

[133-089]must be active. It is very sad that I should have to assume your

[133-090]authority--"

[133-091]"I will not allow you to assume my authority."

[133-092]"I must do so, or must else obtain a medical certificate as to your

[133-093]incapacity, and beg that some neighbouring bishop may administer

[133-094]the diocese. Things shall not go on as they are now. I, at any rate,

[133-095]will do my duty. I shall tell Mr. Thumble that he must go over to

[133-096]Hogglestock, and arrange for the duties of the parish."

[133-097]"I desire that you will do no such thing," said the bishop, now again

[133-098]looking up at her.

[133-099]"You may be sure that I shall," said Mrs. Proudie, and then she left

[133-100]the room.

[133-101]He did not even yet suppose that she would go about this work at

[133-102]once. The condition of his mind was in truth bad, and was becoming

[133-103]worse, probably, from day to day; but still he did make his

[133-104]calculations about things, and now reflected that it would be

[133-105]sufficient if he spoke to his chaplain to-morrow about Mr. Crawley's

[133-106]letter. Since the terrible scene that Dr. Tempest had witnessed, he

[133-107]had never been able to make up his mind as to what great step he

[133-108]would take, but he had made up his mind that some great step was

[133-109]necessary. There were moments in which he thought that he would

[133-110]resign his bishopric. For such resignation, without acknowledged

[133-111]incompetence on the score of infirmity, the precedents were very few;

[133-112]but even if there were no precedents, it would be better to do that

[133-113]than to remain where he was. Of course there would be disgrace. But

[133-114]then it would be disgrace from which he could hide himself. Now there

[133-115]was equal disgrace; and he could not hide himself. And then such a

[133-116]measure as that would bring punishment where punishment was due. It

[133-117]would bring his wife to the ground,--her who had brought him to the

[133-118]ground. The suffering should not be all his own. When she found that

[133-119]her income, and her palace, and her position were all gone, then

[133-120]perhaps she might repent the evil that she had done him. Now, when he

[133-121]was left alone, his mind went back to this, and he did not think of

[133-122]taking immediate measures,--measures on that very day,--to prevent

[133-123]the action of Mr. Thumble.

[133-124]But Mrs. Proudie did take immediate steps. Mr. Thumble was at this

[133-125]moment in the palace waiting for instructions. It was he who had

[133-126]brought Mr. Crawley's letter to Mrs. Proudie, and she now returned

[133-127]to him with that letter in her hand. The reader will know what was

[133-128]the result. Mr. Thumble was sent off to Hogglestock at once on the

[133-129]bishop's old cob, and,--as will be remembered,--fell into trouble on

[133-130]the road. Late in the afternoon he entered the palace yard, having

[133-131]led the cob by the bridle the whole way home from Hogglestock.

[133-132]Some hour or two before Mr. Thumble's return Mrs. Proudie returned to

[133-133]her husband, thinking it better to let him know what she had done.

[133-134]She resolved to be very firm with him, but at the same time she

[133-135]determined not to use harsh language if it could be avoided. "My

[133-136]dear," she said, "I have arranged with Mr. Thumble." She found him

[133-137]on this occasion sitting at his desk with papers before him, with

[133-138]a pen in his hand; and she could see at a glance that nothing had

[133-139]been written on the paper. What would she have thought had she known

[133-140]that when he placed the sheet before him he was proposing to consult

[133-141]the archbishop as to the propriety of his resignation! He had not,

[133-142]however, progressed so far as to write even the date of his letter.

[133-143]"You have done what?" said he, throwing down the pen.

[133-144]"I have arranged with Mr. Thumble as to going out to Hogglestock,"

[133-145]said she firmly. "Indeed he has gone already." Then the bishop jumped

[133-146]up from his seat, and rang the bell with violence. "What are you

[133-147]going to do?" said Mrs. Proudie.

[133-148]"I am going to depart from here," said he. "I will not stay here

[133-149]to be the mark of scorn for all men's fingers. I will resign the

[133-150]diocese."

[133-151]"You cannot do that," said his wife.

[133-152]"I can try, at any rate," said he. Then the servant entered. "John,"

[133-153]said he, addressing the man, "let Mr. Thumble know the moment he

[133-154]returns to the palace that I wish to see him here. Perhaps he may not

[133-155]come to the palace. In that case let word be sent to his house."

[133-156]Mrs. Proudie allowed the man to go before she addressed her husband

[133-157]again. "What do you mean to say to Mr. Thumble when you see him?"

[133-158]"That is nothing to you."

[133-159]She came up to him and put her hand upon his shoulder, and spoke to

[133-160]him very gently. "Tom," she said, "is that the way in which you speak

[133-161]to your wife?"

[133-162]"Yes, it is. You have driven me to it. Why have you taken upon

[133-163]yourself to send that man to Hogglestock?"

[133-164]"Because it was right to do so. I came to you for instructions, and

[133-165]you would give none."

[133-166]"I should have given what instructions I pleased in proper time.

[133-167]Thumble shall not go to Hogglestock next Sunday."

[133-168]"Who shall go, then?"

[133-169]"Never mind. Nobody. It does not matter to you. If you will leave me

[133-170]now I shall be obliged to you. There will be an end of all this very

[133-171]soon,--very soon."

[133-172]Mrs. Proudie after this stood for a while thinking what she would

[133-173]say; but she left the room without uttering another word. As she

[133-174]looked at him a hundred different thoughts came into her mind. She

[133-175]had loved him dearly, and she loved him still; but she knew now,--at

[133-176]this moment felt absolutely sure,--that by him she was hated! In

[133-177]spite of all her roughness and temper, Mrs. Proudie was in this

[133-178]like other women,--that she would fain have been loved had it been

[133-179]possible. She had always meant to serve him. She was conscious

[133-180]of that; conscious also in a way that, although she had been

[133-181]industrious, although she had been faithful, although she was clever,

[133-182]yet she had failed. At the bottom of her heart she knew that she

[133-183]had been a bad wife. And yet she had meant to be a pattern wife!

[133-184]She had meant to be a good Christian; but she had so exercised her

[133-185]Christianity that not a soul in the world loved her, or would endure

[133-186]her presence if it could be avoided! She had sufficient insight to

[133-187]the minds and feelings of those around her to be aware of this.

[133-188]And now her husband had told her that her tyranny to him was so

[133-189]overbearing that he must throw up his great position, and retire to

[133-190]an obscurity that would be exceptionally disgraceful to them both,

[133-191]because he could no longer endure the public disgrace which her

[133-192]conduct brought upon him in his high place before the world! Her

[133-193]heart was too full for speech; and she left him, very quietly closing

[133-194]the door behind her.
