## Character

Lord Lufton, known familiarly as Ludovic, is the young peer of Framley Court and the son of Lady Lufton. He is a man of good intentions and generous impulses, but his casual handling of the twenty-pound cheque drawn on his bankers sets in motion the central crisis of the novel. "Lord Lufton held" the living at Hogglestock and had an established practice of sending money to the impoverished Crawley family (002-167). He is a close friend of Mark Robarts, the vicar of Framley, and moves easily between the worlds of the county gentry and the London social season. His character is that of a responsible landlord and a generous patron, but his very casualness about money — the easy confidence of the wealthy — creates the ambiguity that nearly destroys Josiah Crawley.

## Biography

Lord Lufton's connection to the Crawley affair begins with an act of charity: "the cheque given by Lord Lufton" for twenty pounds was sent to Mr. Crawley through the dean (003-018). Crawley, in his poverty and distraction, passed the cheque to the butcher without endorsing it — and when the cheque was traced, the question became whether Crawley had stolen it. "Lord Lufton had sent him a second cheque" when the first failed to reach Crawley (008-011), confirming both his generosity and his imperfect knowledge of the transactions conducted in his name. Lufton appears at Framley Hall in conference with Mark Robarts, where he expresses baffled sympathy for Crawley: "By Jove," said Lord Lufton, "I don't know" how the magistrates can convict him (008-106). He is willing to intervene on Crawley's behalf but is advised that his intervention might only complicate matters.

## Significance

Lord Lufton's role in the novel is structurally crucial but thematically ironic: his well-intentioned charity is the unwitting instrument of Crawley's ordeal. Trollope uses Lufton — a young man of wealth, good nature, and imperfect attention to detail — to illustrate how the machinery of class and money can crush even as it means to help. "Lord Lufton's cheque was traced back through" the banking system with an impersonal precision that Lufton himself never intended (003-002). The peer's awkward position — wanting to help Crawley but knowing that his own testimony might be used against the clergyman — mirrors the novel's broader concern with how systems of law and finance can produce injustice even when all the individual actors mean well.
