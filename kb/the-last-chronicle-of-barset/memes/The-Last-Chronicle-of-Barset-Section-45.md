[045-002]As he went downstairs Eames was still thinking of his meeting with

[045-003]Crosbie, and had as yet hardly said a word to his neighbour, and his

[045-004]neighbour had not said a word to him. Now Johnny understood dinners

[045-005]quite well enough to know that in a party of twelve, among whom six

[045-006]are ladies, everything depends on your next neighbour, and generally

[045-007]on the next neighbour who specially belongs to you; and as he took

[045-008]his seat he was a little alarmed as to his prospect for the next two

[045-009]hours. On his other hand sat Mrs. Ponsonby, the barrister's wife, and

[045-010]he did not much like the look of Mrs. Ponsonby. She was fat, heavy,

[045-011]and good-looking; with a broad space between her eyes, and light

[045-012]smooth hair;--a youthful British matron every inch of her, of whom

[045-013]any barrister with a young family of children might be proud. Now

[045-014]Miss Demolines, though she was hardly to be called beautiful, was at

[045-015]any rate remarkable. She had large, dark, well-shaped eyes, and very

[045-016]dark hair, which she wore tangled about in an extraordinary manner,

[045-017]and she had an expressive face,--a face made expressive by the

[045-018]owner's will. Such power of expression is often attained by dint of

[045-019]labour,--though it never reaches to the expression of anything in

[045-020]particular. She was almost sufficiently good-looking to be justified

[045-021]in considering herself to be a beauty.

[045-022]But Miss Demolines, though she had said nothing as yet, knew her game

[045-023]very well. A lady cannot begin conversation to any good purpose in

[045-024]the drawing-room, when she is seated and the man is standing;--nor

[045-025]can she know then how the table may subsequently arrange itself.

[045-026]Powder may be wasted, and often is wasted, and the spirit rebels

[045-027]against the necessity of commencing a second enterprise. But Miss

[045-028]Demolines, when she found herself seated, and perceived that on the

[045-029]other side of her was Mr. Ponsonby, a married man, commenced her

[045-030]enterprise at once, and our friend John Eames was immediately aware

[045-031]that he would have no difficulty as to conversation.

[045-032]"Don't you like winter dinner-parties?" began Miss Demolines. This

[045-033]was said just as Johnny was taking his seat, and he had time to

[045-034]declare that he liked dinner-parties at all periods of the year if

[045-035]the dinner was good and the people pleasant before the host had

[045-036]muttered something which was intended to be understood to be a grace.

[045-037]"But I mean especially in winter," continued Miss Demolines. "I don't

[045-038]think daylight should ever be admitted at a dinner-table; and though

[045-039]you may shut out the daylight, you can't shut out the heat. And then

[045-040]there are always so many other things to go to in May and June and

[045-041]July. Dinners should be stopped by Act of Parliament for those three

[045-042]months. I don't care what people do afterwards, because we always fly

[045-043]away on the first of August.

[045-044]"That is good-natured on your part."

[045-045]"I'm sure what I say would be for the good of society;--but at this

[045-046]time of the year a dinner is warm and comfortable."

[045-047]"Very comfortable, I think."

[045-048]"And people get to know each other;"--in saying which Miss Demolines

[045-049]looked very pleasantly up into Johnny's face.

[045-050]"There is a great deal in that," said he. "I wonder whether you and I

[045-051]will get to know each other?"

[045-052]"Of course we shall;--that is, if I'm worth knowing."

[045-053]"There can be no doubt about that, I should say."

[045-054]"Time alone can tell. But, Mr. Eames, I see that Mr. Crosbie is a

[045-055]friend of yours."

[045-056]"Hardly a friend."

[045-057]"I know very well that men are friends when they step up and shake

[045-058]hands with each other. It is the same as when women kiss."

[045-059]"When I see women kiss, I always think that there is deep hatred at

[045-060]the bottom of it."

[045-061]"And there may be deep hatred between you and Mr. Crosbie for

[045-062]anything I know to the contrary," said Miss Demolines.

[045-063]"The very deepest," said Johnny, pretending to look grave.

[045-064]"Ah; then I know he is your bosom friend, and that you will tell him

[045-065]anything I say. What a strange history that was of his marriage!"

[045-066]"So I have heard;--but he is not quite bosom friend enough with me to

[045-067]have told me all the particulars. I know that his wife is dead."

[045-068]"Dead; oh, yes; she has been dead these two years I should say."

[045-069]"Not so long as that, I should think."

[045-070]"Well,--perhaps not. But it's ever so long ago;--quite long enough

[045-071]for him to be married again. Did you know her?"

[045-072]"I never saw her in my life."

[045-073]"I knew her,--not well indeed; but I am intimate with her sister,

[045-074]Lady Amelia Gazebee, and I have met her there. None of that family

[045-075]have married what you may call well. And now, Mr. Eames, pray look at

[045-076]the menu and tell me what I am to eat. Arrange for me a little dinner

[045-077]of my own, out of the great bill of fare provided. I always expect

[045-078]some gentleman to do that for me. Mr. Crosbie, you know, only lived

[045-079]with his wife for one month."

[045-080]"So I've been told."

[045-081]"And a terrible month they had of it. I used to hear of it. He

[045-082]doesn't look that sort of man, does he?"

[045-083]"Well;--no. I don't think he does. But what sort of man do you mean?"

[045-084]"Why, such a regular Bluebeard! Of course you know how he treated

[045-085]another girl before he married Lady Alexandrina. She died of

[045-086]it,--with a broken heart; absolutely died; and there he is,

[045-087]indifferent as possible;--and would treat me in the same way

[045-088]to-morrow if I would let him."

[045-089]Johnny Eames, finding it impossible to talk to Miss Demolines about

[045-090]Lily Dale, took up the card of the dinner and went to work in

[045-091]earnest, recommending his neighbour what to eat and what to pass by.

[045-092]"But you've skipped the pâté," she said, with energy.

[045-093]"Allow me to ask you to choose mine for me instead. You are much more

[045-094]fit to do it." And she did choose his dinner for him.

[045-095]They were sitting at a round table, and in order that the ladies

[045-096]and gentlemen should alternate themselves properly, Mr. Musselboro

[045-097]was opposite to the host. Next to him on his right was old Mrs. Van

[045-098]Siever, the widow of a Dutch merchant, who was very rich. She was

[045-099]a ghastly thing to look at, as well from the quantity as from the

[045-100]nature of the wiggeries which she wore. She had not only a false

[045-101]front, but long false curls, as to which it cannot be conceived

[045-102]that she would suppose that any one would be ignorant as to their

[045-103]falseness. She was very thin, too, and very small, and putting aside

[045-104]her wiggeries, you would think her to be all eyes. She was a ghastly

[045-105]old woman to the sight, and not altogether pleasant in her mode of

[045-106]talking. She seemed to know Mr. Musselboro very well, for she called

[045-107]him by his name without any prefix. He had, indeed, begun life as a

[045-108]clerk in her husband's office.

[045-109]"Why doesn't What's-his-name have real silver forks?" she said to

[045-110]him. Now Mrs. What's-his-name,--Mrs. Dobbs Broughton we will call

[045-111]her,--was sitting on the other side of Mr. Musselboro, between him

[045-112]and Mr. Crosbie; and, so placed, Mr. Musselboro found it rather hard

[045-113]to answer the question, more especially as he was probably aware that

[045-114]other questions would follow.

[045-115]"What's the use?" said Mr. Musselboro. "Everybody has these plated

[045-116]things now. What's the use of a lot of capital lying dead?"

[045-117]"Everybody doesn't. I don't. You know as well as I do, Musselboro,

[045-118]that the appearance of the thing goes for a great deal. Capital isn't

[045-119]lying dead as long as people know that you've got it."

[045-120]Before answering this Mr. Musselboro was driven to reflect that Mrs.

[045-121]Dobbs Broughton would probably hear his reply. "You won't find that

[045-122]there is any doubt on that head in the City as to Broughton," he

[045-123]said.

[045-124]"I shan't ask in the City, and if I did, I should not believe what

[045-125]people told me. I think there are sillier folks in the City than

[045-126]anywhere else. What did he give for that picture upstairs which the

[045-127]young man painted?"

[045-128]"What, Mrs. Dobbs Broughton's portrait?"

[045-129]"You don't call that a portrait, do you? I mean the one with the

[045-130]three naked women?" Mr. Musselboro glanced round with one eye, and

[045-131]felt sure that Mrs. Dobbs Broughton had heard the question. But the

[045-132]old woman was determined to have an answer. "How much did he give for

[045-133]it, Musselboro?"

[045-134]"Six hundred pounds, I believe," said Mr. Musselboro, looking

[045-135]straight before him as he answered, and pretending to treat the

[045-136]subject with perfect indifference.

[045-137]"Did he indeed, now? Six hundred pounds! And yet he hasn't got silver

[045-138]spoons. How things are changed! Tell me, Musselboro, who was that

[045-139]young man who came in with the painter?"

[045-140]Mr. Musselboro turned round and asked Mrs. Broughton. "A Mr.

[045-141]John Eames, Mrs. Van Siever," said Mrs. Broughton, whispering

[045-142]across the front of Mr. Musselboro. "He is private secretary to

[045-143]Lord--Lord--Lord--I forget who. Some one of the Ministers, I know.

[045-144]And he had a great fortune left him the other day by Lord--Lord--Lord

[045-145]somebody else."

[045-146]"All among the lords, I see," said Mrs. Van Siever. Then Mrs. Dobbs

[045-147]Broughton drew herself back, remembering some little attack which had

[045-148]been made on her by Mrs. Van Siever when she herself had had the real

[045-149]lord to dine with her.

[045-150]There was a Miss Van Siever there also, sitting between Crosbie and

[045-151]Conway Dalrymple. Conway Dalrymple had been specially brought there

[045-152]to sit next to Miss Van Siever. "There's no knowing how much she'll

[045-153]have," said Mrs. Dobbs Broughton, in the warmth of her friendship.

[045-154]"But it's all real. It is, indeed. The mother is awfully rich."

[045-155]"But she's awful in another way, too," said Dalrymple.

[045-156]"Indeed she is, Conway." Mrs. Dobbs Broughton had got into a way of

[045-157]calling her young friend by his Christian name. "All the world calls

[045-158]him Conway," she had said to her husband once when her husband caught

[045-159]her doing so. "She is awful. Her husband made the business in the

[045-160]City, when things were very different from what they are now, and I

[045-161]can't help having her. She has transactions of business with Dobbs.

[045-162]But there's no mistake about the money."

[045-163]"She needn't leave it to her daughter, I suppose?"

[045-164]"But why shouldn't she? She has nobody else. You might offer to paint

[045-165]her, you know. She'd make an excellent picture. So much character.

[045-166]You come and see her."

[045-167]Conway Dalrymple had expressed his willingness to meet Miss Van

[045-168]Siever, saying something, however, as to his present position being

[045-169]one which did not admit of any matrimonial speculation. Then Mrs.

[045-170]Dobbs Broughton had told him, with much seriousness, that he was

[045-171]altogether wrong, and that were he to forget himself, or commit

[045-172]himself, or misbehave himself, there must be an end to their pleasant

[045-173]intimacy. In answer to which, Mr. Dalrymple had said that his Grace

[045-174]was surely of all Graces the least gracious. And now he had come to

[045-175]meet Miss Van Siever, and was seated next to her at table.

[045-176]Miss Van Siever, who at this time had perhaps reached her

[045-177]twenty-fifth year, was certainly a handsome young woman. She was fair

[045-178]and large, bearing no likeness whatever to her mother. Her features

[045-179]were regular, and her full, clear eyes had a brilliance of their

[045-180]own, looking at you always stedfastly and boldly, though very

[045-181]seldom pleasantly. Her mouth would have been beautiful had it not

[045-182]been too strong for feminine beauty. Her teeth were perfect,--too

[045-183]perfect,--looking like miniature walls of carved ivory. She knew

[045-184]the fault of this perfection, and shewed her teeth as little as she

[045-185]could. Her nose and chin were finely chiselled, and her head stood

[045-186]well upon her shoulders. But there was something hard about it all

[045-187]which repelled you. Dalrymple, when he saw her, recoiled from her,

[045-188]not outwardly, but inwardly. Yes, she was handsome, as may be a horse

[045-189]or a tiger; but there was about her nothing of feminine softness.

[045-190]He could not bring himself to think of taking Clara Van Siever as

[045-191]the model that was to sit before him for the rest of his life. He

[045-192]certainly could make a picture of her, as had been suggested by his

[045-193]friend, Mrs. Broughton, but it must be as Judith with the dissevered

[045-194]head, or as Jael using her hammer over the temple of Sisera. Yes,--he

[045-195]thought she would do as Jael; and if Mrs. Van Siever would throw

[045-196]him a sugar-plum,--for he would want the sugar-plum, seeing that

[045-197]any other result was out of the question,--the thing might be done.

[045-198]Such was the idea of Mr. Conway Dalrymple respecting Miss Van

[045-199]Siever,--before he led her down to dinner.
