[087-001]"That is the friend, I suppose, of whom I have heard from Maria."

[087-002]It is to be feared that Conway Dalrymple had not been so guarded as

[087-003]he should have been in some of his conversations with Mrs. Dobbs

[087-004]Broughton, and that a word or two had escaped from him as to the love

[087-005]of John Eames for Lily Dale.

[087-006]"I don't know what you may have heard," said Johnny, "but I was

[087-007]obliged to see these people before I left town. There is going to be

[087-008]a marriage and all that sort of thing."

[087-009]"Who is going to be married?"

[087-010]"One Captain Dale is going to be married to one Miss Dunstable."

[087-011]"Oh! And as to one Miss Lily Dale,--is she to be married to anybody?"

[087-012]"Not that I have heard of," said Johnny.

[087-013]"She is not going to become the wife of one Mr. John Eames?"

[087-014]He did not wish to talk to Miss Demolines about Lily Dale. He did not

[087-015]choose to disown the imputation, or to acknowledge its truth.

[087-016]"Silence gives consent," she said. "If it be so, I congratulate you.

[087-017]I have no doubt she is a most charming young woman. It is about seven

[087-018]years, I believe, since that little affair with Mr. Crosbie, and

[087-019]therefore that, I suppose, may be considered as forgotten."

[087-020]"It is only three years," said Johnny, angrily. "Besides, I don't

[087-021]know what that has to do with it."

[087-022]"You need not be ashamed," said Madalina. "I have heard how well you

[087-023]behaved on that occasion. You were quite the preux chevalier; and

[087-024]if any gentleman ever deserved well of a lady you deserved well of

[087-025]her. I wonder how Mr. Crosbie felt when he met you the other day at

[087-026]Maria's. I had not heard anything about it then, or I should have

[087-027]been much more interested in watching your meeting."

[087-028]"I really can't say how he felt."

[087-029]"I daresay not; but I saw him shake hands with you. And so Lily Dale

[087-030]has come to town?"

[087-031]"Yes,--Miss Dale is here with her uncle."

[087-032]"And you are going away to-morrow?"

[087-033]"Yes,--and I am going away to-morrow."

[087-034]After that there was a pause in the conversation. Eames was sick of

[087-035]it, and was very anxious to change the conversation. Miss Demolines

[087-036]was sitting in the shadow, away from the light, with her face half

[087-037]hidden by her hands. At last she jumped up, and came round and stood

[087-038]opposite to him. "I charge you to tell me truly, John Eames," she

[087-039]said, "whether Miss Lilian Dale is engaged to you as your future

[087-040]wife?" He looked up into her face, but made no immediate answer. Then

[087-041]she repeated her demand. "I ask you whether you are engaged to marry

[087-042]Miss Lilian Dale, and I expect a reply."

[087-043]"What makes you ask me such a question as that?"

[087-044]"What makes me ask you? Do you deny my right to feel so much interest

[087-045]in you as to desire to know whether you are about to be married? Of

[087-046]course you can decline to tell me if you choose."

[087-047]"And if I were to decline?"

[087-048]"I should know then that it was true, and I should think that you

[087-049]were a coward."

[087-050]"I don't see any cowardice in the matter. One does not talk about

[087-051]that kind of thing to everybody."

[087-052]"Upon my word, Mr. Eames, you are complimentary;--indeed you are. To

[087-053]everybody! I am everybody,--am I? That is your idea of--friendship!

[087-054]You may be sure that after that I shall ask no further questions."

[087-055]"I didn't mean it in the way you've taken it, Madalina."

[087-056]"In what way did you mean it, sir? Everybody! Mr. Eames, you must

[087-057]excuse me if I say that I am not well enough this evening to bear the

[087-058]company of--everybody. I think you had better leave me. I think that

[087-059]you had better go."

[087-060]"Are you angry with me?"

[087-061]"Yes, I am,--very angry. Because I have condescended to feel an

[087-062]interest in your welfare, and have asked you a question which I

[087-063]thought that our intimacy justified, you tell me that that is a kind

[087-064]of thing that you will not talk about to--everybody. I beg you to

[087-065]understand that I will not be your everybody. Mr. Eames, there is the

[087-066]door."

[087-067]Things had now become very serious. Hitherto Johnny had been seated

[087-068]comfortably in the corner of a sofa, and had not found himself bound

[087-069]to move, though Miss Demolines was standing before him. But now it

[087-070]was absolutely necessary that he should do something. He must either

[087-071]go, or else he must make entreaty to be allowed to remain. Would it

[087-072]not be expedient that he should take the lady at her word and escape?

[087-073]She was still pointing to the door, and the way was open to him. If

[087-074]he were to walk out now of course he would never return, and there

[087-075]would be the end of the Bayswater romance. If he remained it might

[087-076]be that the romance would become troublesome. He got up from his

[087-077]seat, and had almost resolved that he would go. Had she not somewhat

[087-078]relaxed the majesty of her anger as he rose, had the fire of her

[087-079]eye not been somewhat quenched and the lines of her mouth softened,

[087-080]I think that he would have gone. The romance would have been over,

[087-081]and he would have felt that it had come to an inglorious end; but it

[087-082]would have been well for him that he should have gone. Though the

[087-083]fire was somewhat quenched and the lines were somewhat softened, she

[087-084]was still pointing to the door. "Do you mean it?" he said.

[087-085]"I do mean it,--certainly."

[087-086]"And this is to be the end of everything?"

[087-087]"I do not know what you mean by everything. It is a very little

[087-088]everything to you, I should say. I do not quite understand your

[087-089]everything and your everybody."

[087-090]"I will go, if you wish me to go, of course."

[087-091]"I do wish it."

[087-092]"But before I go, you must permit me to excuse myself. I did not

[087-093]intend to offend you. I merely meant--"

[087-094]"You merely meant! Give me an honest answer to a downright question.

[087-095]Are you engaged to Miss Lilian Dale?"

[087-096]"No;--I am not."

[087-097]"Upon your honour?"

[087-098]"Do you think that I would tell you a falsehood about it? What I

[087-099]meant was that it is a kind of thing one doesn't like talking about,

[087-100]merely because stories are bandied about. People are so fond of

[087-101]saying that this man is engaged to that woman, and of making up

[087-102]tales; and it seems to be so foolish to contradict such things."

[087-103]"But you know that you used to be very fond of her?"

[087-104]He had taken up his hat when he had risen from the sofa, and was

[087-105]still standing with it ready in his hand. He was even now half-minded

[087-106]to escape; and the name of Lily Dale in Miss Demolines' mouth was so

[087-107]distasteful to him that he would have done so,--he would have gone

[087-108]in sheer disgust, had she not stood in his way, so that he could not

[087-109]escape without moving her, or going round behind the sofa. She did

[087-110]not stir to make way for him, and it may be that she understood that

[087-111]he was her prisoner, in spite of her late command to him to go. It

[087-112]may be, also, that she understood his vexation and the cause of it,

[087-113]and that she saw the expediency of leaving Lily Dale alone for the

[087-114]present. At any rate, she pressed him no more upon the matter. "Are

[087-115]we to be friends again?" she said.

[087-116]"I hope so," replied Johnny.

[087-117]"There is my hand, then." So Johnny took her hand and pressed it,

[087-118]and held it a little while,--just long enough to seem to give a

[087-119]meaning to the action. "You will get to understand me some day," she

[087-120]said, "and will learn that I do not like to be reckoned among the

[087-121]everybodies by those for whom I really--really--really have a regard.

[087-122]When I am angry, I am angry."

[087-123]"You were very angry just now, when you showed me the way to the

[087-124]door."

[087-125]"And I meant it too,--for the minute. Only think,--supposing you had

[087-126]gone! We should never have seen each other again;--never, never! What

[087-127]a change one word may make!"

[087-128]"One word often does make a change."

[087-129]"Does it not? Just a little 'yes,' or 'no.' A 'no' is said when a

[087-130]'yes' is meant, and then there comes no second chance, and what a

[087-131]change that may be from bright hopes to desolation! Or, worse again,

[087-132]a 'yes' is said when a 'no' should be said,--when the speaker knows

[087-133]that it should be 'no.' What a difference that 'no' makes! When one

[087-134]thinks of it, one wonders that a woman should ever say anything but

[087-135]'no.'"

[087-136]"They never did say anything else to me," said Johnny.

[087-137]"I don't believe it. I daresay the truth is, you never asked

[087-138]anybody."

[087-139]"Did anybody ever ask you?"

[087-140]"What would you give to know? But I will tell you frankly;--yes. And

[087-141]once,--once I thought that my answer would not have been a 'no.'"

[087-142]"But you changed your mind?"

[087-143]"When the moment came I could not bring myself to say the word that

[087-144]should rob me of my liberty for ever. I had said 'no' to him often

[087-145]enough before,--poor fellow; and on this occasion he told me that he

[087-146]asked for the last time. 'I shall not give myself another chance,'

[087-147]he said, 'for I shall be on board ship within a week.' I merely bade

[087-148]him good-by. It was the only answer I gave him. He understood me, and

[087-149]since that day his foot has never pressed his native soil."

[087-150]"And was it all because you are so fond of your liberty?" said

[087-151]Johnny.

[087-152]"Perhaps,--I did not--love him," said Miss Demolines, thoughtfully.

[087-153]She was now again seated in her chair, and John Eames had gone back

[087-154]to his corner of the sofa. "If I had really loved him I suppose it

[087-155]would have been otherwise. He was a gallant fellow, and had two

[087-156]thousand a year of his own, in India stock and other securities."

[087-157]"Dear me! And he has not married yet?"

[087-158]"He wrote me word to say that he would never marry till I was

[087-159]married,--but that on the day that he should hear of my wedding, he

[087-160]would go to the first single woman near him and propose. It was a

[087-161]droll thing to say; was it not?"

[087-162]"The single woman ought to feel herself flattered."

[087-163]"He would find plenty to accept him. Besides being so well off he was

[087-164]a very handsome fellow, and is connected with people of title. He had

[087-165]everything to recommend him."

[087-166]"And yet you refused him so often?"

[087-167]"Yes. You think I was foolish;--do you not?"

[087-168]"I don't think you were at all foolish if you didn't care for him."

[087-169]"It was my destiny, I suppose; I daresay I was wrong. Other girls

[087-170]marry without violent love, and do very well afterwards. Look at

[087-171]Maria Clutterbuck."

[087-172]The name of Maria Clutterbuck had become odious to John Eames. As

[087-173]long as Miss Demolines would continue to talk about herself he could

[087-174]listen with some amount of gratification. Conversation on that

[087-175]subject was the natural progress of the Bayswater romance. And if

[087-176]Madalina would only call her friend by her present name, he had

[087-177]no strong objection to an occasional mention of the lady; but the

[087-178]combined names of Maria Clutterbuck had come to be absolutely

[087-179]distasteful to him. He did not believe in the Maria Clutterbuck

[087-180]friendship,--either in its past or present existence, as described

[087-181]by Madalina. Indeed, he did not put strong faith in anything that

[087-182]Madalina said to him. In the handsome gentleman with two thousand

[087-183]a year, he did not believe at all. But the handsome gentleman had

[087-184]only been mentioned once in the course of his acquaintance with Miss

[087-185]Demolines, whereas Maria Clutterbuck had come up so often! "Upon my

[087-186]word I must wish you good-by," he said. "It is going on for eleven

[087-187]o'clock, and I have to start to-morrow at seven."
