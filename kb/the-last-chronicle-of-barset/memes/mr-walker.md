## Character

Mr. Walker is the principal attorney of Silverbridge and the lawyer to whom the Crawley family turns in their hour of crisis. He is a man of professional competence and genuine human sympathy — "the lawyer was very gentle" with Mrs. Crawley when she came to consult him (003-050) — and his role in the novel is that of the legal system's human face. Unlike the impersonal machinery of the assizes or the ecclesiastical commission, Walker deals directly with the Crawleys in their poverty and distress, and his advice shapes the course of their defence. His chapter, "What Mr. Walker Thought About It" (001-006), establishes him as one of the novel's key interpretive voices.

## Biography

Walker is the established solicitor of Silverbridge, with a practice that serves the town and its surrounding parishes. "The family of Mr. Walker, of Silverbridge" (002-013) is well-known locally, and his position is secure enough that he can afford to treat the penniless Crawleys with the same professional courtesy he would extend to paying clients. He first becomes involved in the case when Mrs. Crawley walks from Hogglestock to consult him — a journey whose physical hardship testifies to the family's desperation. "Mr. Walker and Mrs. Crawley" meet in his office, and he listens to her account of the cheque with the careful attention of a man who understands that the facts may not be as the prosecution presents them (003-050). Throughout the novel, he works in parallel with Mr. Toogood, the London solicitor, the two lawyers forming a professional alliance that ultimately exonerates Crawley.

## Significance

Mr. Walker represents the provincial legal professionalism that Trollope admired — competent, honest, and rooted in community knowledge. Where the London lawyers and the ecclesiastical authorities see the Crawley case in abstract legal or political terms, Walker sees it in human terms: a fellow parishioner, a clergyman he has known by reputation, a desperate wife who has walked miles to seek his help. His gentle treatment of Mrs. Crawley is one of the novel's quiet demonstrations that the legal system, for all its capacity to oppress, also contains within it the potential for decency. "What Mr. Walker Thought About It" — the chapter devoted to his perspective — signals Trollope's recognition that the provincial lawyer's opinion matters as much as the bishop's or the judge's.
