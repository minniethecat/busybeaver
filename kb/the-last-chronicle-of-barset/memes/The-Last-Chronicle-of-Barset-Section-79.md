[079-001]"I hope you will excuse my troubling you," said Mr. Toogood.

[079-002]"It is no trouble at all,--if I could be of any use. I don't know

[079-003]whether it is proper, but may I ask whether you call as,--as,--as a

[079-004]friend of Mr. Crawley's?"

[079-005]"Altogether as a friend, Mr. Harding."

[079-006]"I'm glad of that; though of course I am well aware that the

[079-007]gentlemen engaged on the prosecution must do their duty. Still,--I

[079-008]don't know,--somehow I would rather not hear them speak of this poor

[079-009]gentleman before the trial."

[079-010]"You know Mr. Crawley, then?"

[079-011]"Very slightly,--very slightly indeed. He is a gentleman not much

[079-012]given to social habits, and has been but seldom here. But he is an

[079-013]old friend whom my son-in-law loves dearly."

[079-014]"I'm glad to hear you say that, Mr. Harding. Perhaps before I go any

[079-015]further I ought to tell you that Mrs. Crawley and I are

[079-016]first-cousins."

[079-017]"Oh, indeed. Then you are a friend."

[079-018]"I never saw him in my life till a few days ago. He is very queer

[079-019]you know,--very queer indeed. I'm a lawyer, Mr. Harding, practising

[079-020]in London;--an attorney, that is." At each separate announcement

[079-021]Mr. Harding bowed, and when Toogood named his special branch of his

[079-022]profession Mr. Harding bowed lower than before, as though desirous of

[079-023]showing that he had great respect for attorneys. "And of course I'm

[079-024]anxious, if only out of respect for the family, that my wife's cousin

[079-025]should pull through this little difficulty, if possible."

[079-026]"And for the sake of the poor man himself too, and for his wife, and

[079-027]his children;--and for the sake of the cloth."

[079-028]"Exactly; taking it all together it's such a pity, you know. I think,

[079-029]Mr. Harding, he can hardly have intended to steal the money."

[079-030]"I'm sure he did not."

[079-031]"It's very hard to be sure of anybody, Mr. Harding;--very hard."

[079-032]"I feel quite sure that he did not. He has been a most pious,

[079-033]hard-working clergyman. I cannot bring myself to think that he is

[079-034]guilty. What does the Latin proverb say? 'No one of a sudden becomes

[079-035]most base.'"

[079-036]"But the temptation, Mr. Harding, was very strong. He was awfully

[079-037]badgered about his debts. That butcher in Silverbridge was playing

[079-038]the mischief with him."

[079-039]"All the butchers in Barsetshire could not make an honest man steal

[079-040]money, and I think that Mr. Crawley is an honest man. You'll excuse

[079-041]me for being a little hot about one of my own order."

[079-042]"Why; he's my cousin,--or rather, my wife's. But the fact is, Mr.

[079-043]Harding, we must get hold of the dean as soon as possible; and I'm

[079-044]going to send a gentleman after him."

[079-045]"To send a gentleman after him?" said Mr. Harding, almost in dismay.

[079-046]"Yes; I think that will be best."

[079-047]"I'm afraid he'll have to go a long way, Mr. Toogood."

[079-048]"The dean, I'm told, is in Jerusalem."

[079-049]"I'm afraid he is,--or on his journey there. He's to be there for the

[079-050]Easter week, and Sunday week will be Easter Sunday. But why should

[079-051]the gentleman want to go to Jerusalem after the dean?"

[079-052]Then Mr. Toogood explained as well as he was able that the dean might

[079-053]have something to say on the subject which would serve Mr. Crawley's

[079-054]defence. "We shouldn't leave any stone unturned," said Mr. Toogood.

[079-055]"As far as I can judge, Crawley still thinks,--or half thinks,--that

[079-056]he got the cheque from your son-in-law." Mr. Harding shook his

[079-057]head sorrowfully. "I'm not saying he did, you know," continued Mr.

[079-058]Toogood. "I can't see myself how it is possible;--but still, we ought

[079-059]not to leave any stone unturned. And Mrs. Arabin,--can you tell me at

[079-060]all where we shall find her?"

[079-061]"Has she anything to do with it, Mr. Toogood?"

[079-062]"I can't quite say that she has, but it's just possible. As I said

[079-063]before, Mr. Harding, we mustn't leave a stone unturned. They're not

[079-064]expected here till the end of April?"

[079-065]"[[About]] the 25th or 26th, I think."

[079-066]"And the assizes are the 28th. The judges come into the city on that

[079-067]day. It will be too late to wait till then. We must have our defence

[079-068]ready you know. Can you say where my friend will find Mrs. Arabin?"

[079-069]Mr. Harding began nursing his knee, patting it and being very tender

[079-070]to it, as he sat meditating with his head on one side,--meditating

[079-071]not so much as to the nature of his answer as to that of the

[079-072]question. Could it be necessary that any emissary from a lawyer's

[079-073]office should be sent after his daughter? He did not like the idea of

[079-074]his Eleanor being disturbed by questions as to a theft. Though she

[079-075]had been twice married and had a son who was now nearly a man, still

[079-076]she was his Eleanor. But if it was necessary on Mr. Crawley's behalf,

[079-077]of course it must be done. "Her last address was at Paris, sir; but

[079-078]I think she has gone on to Florence. She has friends there, and she

[079-079]purposes to meet the dean at Venice on his return." Then Mr. Harding

[079-080]turned the table and wrote on a card his daughter's address.

[079-081]"I suppose Mrs. Arabin must have heard of the affair?" said Mr.

[079-082]Toogood.

[079-083]"She had not done so when she last wrote. I mentioned it to her the

[079-084]other day, before I knew that she had left Paris. If my letters and

[079-085]her sister's letters have been sent on to her, she must know it now."

[079-086]Then Mr. Toogood got up to take his leave. "You will excuse me for

[079-087]troubling you, I hope, Mr. Harding."

[079-088]"Oh, sir, pray do not mention that. It is no trouble, if one could

[079-089]only be of any service."

[079-090]"One can always try to be of service. In these affairs so much is to

[079-091]be done by rummaging about, as I always call it. There have been many

[079-092]theatrical managers, you know, Mr. Harding, who have usually made up

[079-093]their pieces according to the dresses they have happened to have in

[079-094]their wardrobes."

[079-095]"Have there, indeed, now? I never should have thought of that."

[079-096]"And we lawyers have to do the same thing."

[079-097]"Not with your clothes, Mr. Toogood?"

[079-098]"Not exactly with our clothes;--but with our information."

[079-099]"I do not quite understand you, Mr. Toogood."

[079-100]"In preparing a defence we have to rummage about and get up what we

[079-101]can. If we can't find anything that suits us exactly, we are obliged

[079-102]to use what we do find as well as we can. I remember, when I was a

[079-103]young man, an ostler was to be tried for stealing some oats in the

[079-104]Borough; and he did steal them too, and sold them at a rag-shop

[079-105]regularly. The evidence against him was as plain as a pike-staff. All

[079-106]I could find out was that on a certain day a horse had trod on the

[079-107]fellow's foot. So we put it to the jury whether the man could walk as

[079-108]far as the rag-shop with a bag of oats when he was dead lame;--and we

[079-109]got him off."

[079-110]"Did you though?" said Mr. Harding.

[079-111]"Yes, we did."

[079-112]"And he was guilty?"

[079-113]"He had been at it regularly for months."

[079-114]"Dear, dear, dear! Wouldn't it have been better to have had

[079-115]him punished for the fault,--gently; so as to warn him of the

[079-116]consequences of such doings?"

[079-117]"Our business was to get him off,--and we got him off. It's my

[079-118]business to get my cousin's husband off, if I can, and we must do it,

[079-119]by hook or crook. It's a very difficult piece of work, because he

[079-120]won't let us employ a barrister. However, I shall have one in the

[079-121]court and say nothing to him about it at all. Good-by, Mr. Harding.

[079-122]As you say, it would be a thousand pities that a clergyman should be

[079-123]convicted of a theft;--and one so well connected too."

[079-124]Mr. Harding, when he was left alone, began to turn the matter over

[079-125]in his mind and to reflect whether the thousand pities of which Mr.

[079-126]Toogood had spoken appertained to the conviction of the criminal,

[079-127]or the doing of the crime. "If he did steal the money I suppose he

[079-128]ought to be punished, let him be ever so much a clergyman," said Mr.

[079-129]Harding to himself. But yet,--how terrible it would be! Of clergymen

[079-130]convicted of fraud in London he had often heard; but nothing of the

[079-131]kind had ever disgraced the diocese to which he belonged since he had

[079-132]known it. He could not teach himself to hope that Mr. Crawley should

[079-133]be acquitted if Mr. Crawley were guilty;--but he could teach himself

[079-134]to believe that Mr. Crawley was innocent. Something of a doubt had

[079-135]crept across his mind as he talked to the lawyer. Mr. Toogood, though

[079-136]Mrs. Crawley was his cousin, seemed to believe that the money had

[079-137]been stolen; and Mr. Toogood as a lawyer ought to understand such

[079-138]matters better than an old secluded clergyman in Barchester. But,

[079-139]nevertheless, Mr. Toogood might be wrong; and Mr. Harding succeeded

[079-140]in satisfying himself at last that he could not be doing harm in

[079-141]thinking that Mr. Toogood was wrong. When he had made up his mind

[079-142]on this matter he sat down and wrote the following letter, which he

[079-143]addressed to his daughter at the post-office in Florence:--

[079-144]Deanery, March --, 186--.

[079-145]DEAREST NELLY,--

[079-146]When I wrote on Tuesday I told you about poor Mr.

[079-147]Crawley, that he was the clergyman in Barsetshire of

[079-148]whose misfortune you read an account in Galignani's

[079-149]Messenger,--and I think Susan must have written about it

[079-150]also, because everybody here is talking of nothing else,

[079-151]and because, of course, we know how strong a regard the

[079-152]dean has for Mr. Crawley. But since that something has

[079-153]occurred which makes me write to you again,--at once. A

[079-154]gentleman has just been here, and has indeed only this

[079-155]moment left me, who tells me that he is an attorney in

[079-156]London, and that he is nearly related to Mrs. Crawley.

[079-157]He seems to be a very good-natured man, and I daresay he

[079-158]understands his business as a lawyer. His name is Toogood,

[079-159]and he has come down as he says to get evidence to help

[079-160]the poor gentleman on his trial. I cannot understand how

[079-161]this should be necessary, because it seems to me that the

[079-162]evidence should all be wanted on the other side. I cannot

[079-163]for a moment suppose that a clergyman and a gentleman such

[079-164]as Mr. Crawley should have stolen money, and if he is

[079-165]innocent I cannot understand why all this trouble should

[079-166]be necessary to prevent a jury finding him guilty.

[079-167]Mr. Toogood came here because he wanted to see the

[079-168]dean,--and you also. He did not explain, as far as I

[079-169]can remember, why he wanted to see you; but he said it

[079-170]would be necessary, and that he was going to send off a

[079-171]messenger to find you first, and the dean afterwards. It

[079-172]has something to do with the money which was given to

[079-173]Mr. Crawley last year, and which, if I remember right,

[079-174]was your present. But of course Mr. Toogood could not

[079-175]have known anything about that. However, I gave him the

[079-176]address,--poste restante, Florence,--and I daresay that

[079-177]somebody will make you out before long, if you are still

[079-178]stopping at Florence. I did not like letting him go

[079-179]without telling you about it, as I thought that a lawyer's

[079-180]coming to you would startle you.

[079-181]The bairns are quite well, as I told you in my other

[079-182]letter, and Miss Jones says that little Elly is as good

[079-183]as gold. They are with me every morning and evening, and

[079-184]behave like darling angels, as they are. Posy is my own

[079-185]little jewel always. You may be quite sure I do nothing to

[079-186]spoil them.

[079-187]God bless you, dearest Nelly,

[079-188]Your most affectionate father,
