[152-001]Upon hearing this she flashed round upon him almost angrily. "I never

[152-002]said that."

[152-003]"If you will ask me for any token of earnestness, I will give it

[152-004]you."

[152-005]"I want no token."

[152-006]"The best sign of earnestness a man can give generally in such a

[152-007]matter, is to show how ready he is to be married."

[152-008]"I never said anything about earnestness."

[152-009]"At the risk of making you angry I will go on, Lily. Of course when

[152-010]you tell me that you will have nothing to say to me, I try to amuse

[152-011]myself"--"Yes; by writing love-letters to M. D.," said Lily to

[152-012]herself.--"What is a poor fellow to do? I tell you fairly that when

[152-013]I leave you I swear to myself that I will make love to the first girl

[152-014]I can see who will listen to me--to twenty, if twenty will let me.

[152-015]I feel I have failed, and it is so I punish myself for my failure."

[152-016]There was something in this which softened her brow, though she did

[152-017]not intend that it should be so; and she turned away again, that he

[152-018]might not see that her brow was softened. "But, Lily, the hope ever

[152-019]comes back again, and then neither the one nor the twenty are of

[152-020]avail,--even to punish me. When I look forward and see what it might

[152-021]be if you were with me, how green it all looks and how lovely, in

[152-022]spite of all the vows I have made, I cannot help coming back again."

[152-023]She was now again near the window, and he had not followed her. As

[152-024]she neither turned towards him nor answered him, he moved from the

[152-025]table near which he was standing on to the rug before the fire, and

[152-026]leaned with both his elbows on the mantelpiece. He could still watch

[152-027]her in the mirror over the fireplace, and could see that she was

[152-028]still seeming to gaze out upon the street. And had he not moved her?

[152-029]I think he had so far moved her now, that she had ceased to think of

[152-030]the woman who had written to her,--that she had ceased to reject him

[152-031]in her heart on the score of such levities as that! If there were M.

[152-032]D.'s, like sunken rocks, in his course, whose fault was it? He was

[152-033]ready enough to steer his bark into the tranquil blue waters, if only

[152-034]she would aid him. I think that all his sins on that score were at

[152-035]this moment forgiven him. He had told her now what to him would be

[152-036]green and beautiful, and she did not find herself able to disbelieve

[152-037]him. She had banished M. D. out of her mind, but in doing so she

[152-038]admitted other reminiscences into it. And then,--was she in a moment

[152-039]to be talked out of the resolution of years; and was she to give

[152-040]up herself, not because she loved, but because the man who talked

[152-041]to her talked so well that he deserved a reward? Was she now to be

[152-042]as light, as foolish, as easy, as in those former days from which

[152-043]she had learned her wisdom? A picture of green lovely things could

[152-044]be delicious to her eyes as to his; but even for such a picture as

[152-045]that the price might be too dear! Of all living men,--of all men

[152-046]living in their present lives,--she loved best this man who was now

[152-047]waiting for some word of answer to his words, and she did love him

[152-048]dearly; she would have tended him if sick, have supplied him if in

[152-049]want, have mourned for him if dead, with the bitter grief of true

[152-050]affection;--but she could not say to herself that he should be her

[152-051]lord and master, the head of her house, the owner of herself, the

[152-052]ruler of her life. The shipwreck to which she had once come, and

[152-053]the fierce regrets which had thence arisen, had forced her to think

[152-054]too much of these things. "Lily," he said, still facing towards the

[152-055]mirror, "will you not come to me and speak to me?" She turned round,

[152-056]and stood a moment looking at him, and then, having again resolved

[152-057]that it could not be as he wished, she drew near to him. "Certainly I

[152-058]will speak to you, John. Here I am." And she came close to him.

[152-059]He took both her hands, and looked into her eyes. "Lily, will you be

[152-060]mine?"

[152-061]"No, dear; it cannot be so."

[152-062]"Why not, Lily?"

[152-063]"Because of that other man."

[152-064]"And is that to be a bar for ever?"

[152-065]"Yes; for ever."

[152-066]"Do you still love him?"

[152-067]"No; no, no!"

[152-068]"Then why should this be so?"

[152-069]"I cannot tell, dear. It is so. If you take a young tree and split

[152-070]it, it still lives, perhaps. But it isn't a tree. It is only a

[152-071]fragment."

[152-072]"Then be my fragment."

[152-073]"So I will, if it can serve you to give standing ground to such a

[152-074]fragment in some corner of your garden. But I will not have myself

[152-075]planted out in the middle, for people to look at. What there is left

[152-076]would die soon." He still held her hands, and she did not attempt to

[152-077]draw them away. "John," she said, "next to mamma, I love you better

[152-078]than all the world. Indeed I do. I can't be your wife, but you need

[152-079]never be afraid that I shall be more to another than I am to you."

[152-080]"That will not serve me," he said, grasping both her hands till he

[152-081]almost hurt them, but not knowing that he did so. "That is no good."

[152-082]"It is all the good that I can do you. Indeed I can do you,--can do

[152-083]no one any good. The trees that the storms have splintered are never

[152-084]of use."

[152-085]"And is this to be the end of all, Lily?"

[152-086]"Not of our loving friendship."

[152-087]"Friendship! I hate the word. I hear some one's step, and I had

[152-088]better leave you. Good-by."

[152-089]"Good-by, John. Be kinder than that to me as you are going." He

[152-090]turned back for a moment, took her hand, and held it tight against

[152-091]his heart, and then he left her. In the hall he met Mrs. Thorne, but,

[152-092]as she said afterwards, he had been too much knocked about to be able

[152-093]to throw a word to a dog.

[152-094]To Mrs. Thorne Lily said hardly a word about John Eames, and when her

[152-095]cousin Bernard questioned her about him she was dumb. And in these

[152-096]days she could assume a manner, and express herself with her eyes as

[152-097]well as with her voice, after a fashion, which was apt to silence

[152-098]unwelcome questioners, even though they were as intimate with her as

[152-099]was her cousin Bernard. She had described her feelings more plainly

[152-100]to her lover than she had ever done to any one,--even to her mother;

[152-101]and having done so she meant to be silent on that subject for

[152-102]evermore. But of her settled purpose she did say some word to Emily

[152-103]Dunstable that night. "I do feel," she said, "that I have got the

[152-104]thing settled at last."

[152-105]"And you have settled it, as you call it, in opposition to the wishes

[152-106]of all your friends?"

[152-107]"That is true; and yet I have settled it rightly, and I would not for

[152-108]worlds have it unsettled again. There are matters on which friends

[152-109]should not have wishes, or at any rate should not express them."

[152-110]"Is that meant to be severe to me?"

[152-111]"No; not to you. I was thinking about mamma, and Bell, and my uncle,

[152-112]and Bernard, who all seem to think that I am to be looked upon as a

[152-113]regular castaway because I am not likely to have a husband of my own.

[152-114]Of course you, in your position, must think a girl a castaway who

[152-115]isn't going to be married?"

[152-116]"I think that a girl who is going to be married has the best of it."

[152-117]"And I think a girl who isn't going to be married has the best of

[152-118]it;--that's all. But I feel that the thing is done now, and I am

[152-119]contented. For the last six or eight months there has come up, I

[152-120]know not how, a state of doubt which has made me so wretched that I

[152-121]have done literally nothing. I haven't been able to finish old Mrs.

[152-122]Heard's tippet, literally because people would talk to me about that

[152-123]dearest of all dear fellows, John Eames. And yet all along I have

[152-124]known how it would be,--as well as I do now."

[152-125]"I cannot understand you, Lily; I can't indeed."

[152-126]"I can understand myself. I love him so well,--with that intimate,

[152-127]close, familiar affection,--that I could wash his clothes for him

[152-128]to-morrow, out of pure personal regard, and think it no shame. He

[152-129]could not ask me to do a single thing for him,--except the one

[152-130]thing,--that I would refuse. And I'll go further. I would sooner

[152-131]marry him than any man in the world I ever saw, or, as I believe,

[152-132]that I ever shall see. And yet I am very glad that it is settled."

[152-133]On the next day Lily Dale went down to the Small House of Allington,

[152-134]and so she passes out of our sight. I can only ask the reader to

[152-135]believe that she was in earnest, and express my own opinion, in this

[152-136]last word that I shall ever write respecting her, that she will live

[152-137]and die as Lily Dale.

[152-138]CHAPTER LXXVII.

[152-139]THE ARABINS RETURN TO BARCHESTER.

[152-140]In these days Mr. Harding was keeping his bed at the deanery, and

[152-141]most of those who saw him declared that he would never again leave

[152-142]it. The archdeacon had been slow to believe so, because he had

[152-143]still found his father-in-law able to talk to him;--not indeed with

[152-144]energy, but then Mr. Harding had never been energetic on ordinary

[152-145]matters,--but with the same soft cordial interest in things which had

[152-146]ever been customary with him. He had latterly been much interested

[152-147]about Mr. Crawley, and would make both the archdeacon and Mrs.

[152-148]Grantly tell him all that they heard, and what they thought of the

[152-149]case. This of course had been before the all-important news had been

[152-150]received from Mrs. Arabin. Mr. Harding was very anxious, "Firstly,"

[152-151]as he said, "for the welfare of the poor man, of whom I cannot

[152-152]bring myself to think ill; and then for the honour of the cloth

[152-153]in Barchester." "We are as liable to have black sheep here as

[152-154]elsewhere," the archdeacon replied. "But, my dear, I do not think

[152-155]that the sheep is black; and we never have had black sheep in

[152-156]Barchester." "Haven't we though?" said the archdeacon, thinking,

[152-157]however, of sheep who were black with a different kind of blackness

[152-158]from this which was now attributed to poor Mr. Crawley,--of a

[152-159]blackness which was not absolute blackness to Mr. Harding's milder

[152-160]eyes. The archdeacon, when he heard his father-in-law talk after this

[152-161]fashion, expressed his opinion that he might live yet for years. He

[152-162]was just the man to linger on, living in bed,--as indeed he had

[152-163]lingered all his life out of bed. But the doctor who attended him

[152-164]thought otherwise, as did also Mrs. Grantly, and as did Mrs. Baxter,

[152-165]and as also did Posy. "Grandpa won't get up any more, will he?"

[152-166]Posy said to Mrs. Baxter. "I hope he will, my dear; and that very

[152-167]soon." "I don't think he will," said Posy, "because he said he would

[152-168]never see the big fiddle again." "That comes of his being a little

[152-169]melancholy like, my dear," said Mrs. Baxter.

[152-170]Mrs. Grantly at this time went into Barchester almost every day, and

[152-171]the archdeacon, who was very often in the city, never went there

[152-172]without passing half-an-hour with the old man. These two clergymen,

[152-173]essentially different in their characters and in every detail of

[152-174]conduct, had been so much thrown together by circumstances that

[152-175]the life of each had almost become a part of the life of the other.

[152-176]Although the fact of Mr. Harding's residence at the deanery had of

[152-177]late years thrown him oftener into the society of the dean than that

[152-178]of his other son-in-law, yet his intimacy with the archdeacon had

[152-179]been so much earlier, and his memories of the archdeacon were so much

[152-180]clearer, that he depended almost more upon the rector of Plumstead,

[152-181]who was absent, than he did upon the dean, whom he customarily saw

[152-182]every day. It was not so with his daughters. His Nelly, as he had

[152-183]used to call her, had ever been his favourite, and the circumstances

[152-184]of their joint lives had been such, that they had never been further

[152-185]separated than from one street of Barchester to another,--and that

[152-186]only for the very short period of the married life of Mrs. Arabin's

[152-187]first husband. For all that was soft and tender therefore,--which

[152-188]with Mr. Harding was all in the world that was charming to him,--he

[152-189]looked to his youngest daughter; but for authority and guidance and

[152-190]wisdom, and for information as to what was going on in the world, he

[152-191]had still turned to his son-in-law the archdeacon,--as he had done

[152-192]for nearly forty years. For so long had the archdeacon been potent as

[152-193]a clergyman in the diocese, and throughout the whole duration of such

[152-194]potency his word had been law to Mr. Harding in most of the affairs

[152-195]of life,--a law generally to be obeyed, and if sometimes to be

[152-196]broken, still a law. And now, when all was so nearly over, he would

[152-197]become unhappy if the archdeacon's visits were far between. Dr.

[152-198]Grantly, when he found that this was so, would not allow that they

[152-199]should be far between.
