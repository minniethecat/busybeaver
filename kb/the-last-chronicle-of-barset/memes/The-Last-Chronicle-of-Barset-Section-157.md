[157-001]"Very tired!"

[157-002]"Then perhaps I had better not keep you up."

[157-003]"Your remaining will make no difference in that respect. I don't

[157-004]suppose that I shall be in bed for the next four hours. But do as you

[157-005]like about going."

[157-006]"I am in no hurry," said Johnny. Then he sat down again, stretched

[157-007]out his legs and made himself comfortable.

[157-008]"I have been to see that woman," said Madalina after a pause.

[157-009]"What woman?"

[157-010]"Maria Clutterbuck,--as I must always call her; for I cannot bring

[157-011]myself to pronounce the name of that poor wretch who was done to

[157-012]death."

[157-013]"He blew his brains out in delirium tremens," said Johnny.

[157-014]"And what made him drink?" said Madalina with emphasis. "Never mind.

[157-015]I decline altogether to speak of it. Such a scene as I have had! I

[157-016]was driven at last to tell her what I thought of her. Anything so

[157-017]callous, so heartless, so selfish, so stone-cold, and so childish,

[157-018]I never saw before! That Maria was childish and selfish I always

[157-019]knew;--but I thought there was some heart,--a vestige of heart. I

[157-020]found to-day that there was none,--none. If you please we won't speak

[157-021]of her any more."

[157-022]"Certainly not," said Johnny.

[157-023]"You need not wonder that I am tired and feverish."

[157-024]"That sort of thing is fatiguing, I dare say. I don't know whether we

[157-025]do not lose more than we gain by those strong emotions."

[157-026]"I would rather die and go beneath the sod at once, than live without

[157-027]them," said Madalina.

[157-028]"It's a matter of taste," said Johnny.

[157-029]"It is there that that poor wretch is so deficient. She is thinking

[157-030]now, this moment, of nothing but her creature comforts. That tragedy

[157-031]has not even stirred her pulses."

[157-032]"If her pulses were stirred ever so, that would not make her happy."

[157-033]"Happy! Who is happy? Are you happy?"

[157-034]Johnny thought of Lily Dale and paused before he answered. No;

[157-035]certainly he was not happy. But he was not going to talk about his

[157-036]unhappiness to Miss Demolines! "Of course I am;--as jolly as a

[157-037]sandboy," he said.

[157-038]"Mr. Eames," said Madalina raising herself on her sofa, "if you can

[157-039]not express yourself in language more suitable to the occasion and to

[157-040]the scene than that, I think that you had better--"

[157-041]"Hold my tongue."

[157-042]"Just so;--though I should not have chosen myself to use words so

[157-043]abruptly discourteous."

[157-044]"What did I say;--jolly as a sandboy? There is nothing wrong in that.

[157-045]What I meant was, that I think that this world is a very good sort of

[157-046]world, and that a man can get along in it very well, if he minds his

[157-047]_p_'s and _q_'s."

[157-048]"But suppose it's a woman?"

[157-049]"Easier still."

[157-050]"And suppose she does not mind her _p_'s and _q_'s?"

[157-051]"Women always do."

[157-052]"Do they? Your knowledge of women goes as far as that, does it? Tell

[157-053]me fairly;--do you think you know anything about women?" Madalina

[157-054]as she asked the question, looked full into his face, and shook her

[157-055]locks and smiled. When she shook her locks and smiled, there was a

[157-056]certain attraction about her of which John Eames was fully sensible.

[157-057]She could throw a special brightness into her eyes, which, though it

[157-058]probably betokened nothing truly beyond ill-natured mischief, seemed

[157-059]to convey a promise of wit and intellect.

[157-060]"I don't mean to make any boast about it," said Johnny.

[157-061]"I doubt whether you know anything. The pretty simplicity of your

[157-062]excellent Lily Dale has sufficed for you."

[157-063]"Never mind about her," said Johnny impatiently.

[157-064]"I do not mind about her in the least. But an insight into that sort

[157-065]of simplicity will not teach you the character of a real woman. You

[157-066]cannot learn the flavour of wines by sipping sherry and water. For

[157-067]myself I do not think that I am simple. I own it fairly. If you must

[157-068]have simplicity, I cannot be to your taste."

[157-069]"Nobody likes partridge always," said Johnny laughing.

[157-070]"I understand you, sir. And though what you say is not complimentary,

[157-071]I am willing to forgive that fault for its truth. I don't consider

[157-072]myself to be always partridge, I can assure you. I am as changeable

[157-073]as the moon."

[157-074]"And as fickle?"

[157-075]"I say nothing about that, sir. I leave you to find that out. It is

[157-076]a man's business to discover that for himself. If you really do know

[157-077]aught of women--"

[157-078]"I did not say that I did."

[157-079]"But if you do, you will perhaps have discovered that a woman may be

[157-080]as changeable as the moon, and yet as true as the sun;--that she may

[157-081]flit from flower to flower, quite unheeding while no passion exists,

[157-082]but that a passion fixes her at once. Do you believe me?" Now she

[157-083]looked into his eyes again, but did not smile and did not shake her

[157-084]locks.

[157-085]"Oh yes;--that's true enough. And when they have a lot of children,

[157-086]then they become steady as milestones."

[157-087]"Children!" said Madalina, getting up and walking about the room.

[157-088]"They do have them you know," said Johnny.

[157-089]"Do you mean to say, sir, that I should be a milestone?"

[157-090]"A finger-post," said Johnny, "to show a fellow the way he ought to

[157-091]go."

[157-092]She walked twice across the room without speaking. Then she came and

[157-093]stood opposite to him, still without speaking,--and then she walked

[157-094]about again. "What could a woman better be, than a finger-post, as

[157-095]you call it, with such a purpose?"

[157-096]"Nothing better, of course;--though a milestone to tell a fellow his

[157-097]distances, is very good."

[157-098]"Psha!"

[157-099]"You don't like the idea of being a milestone."

[157-100]"No!"

[157-101]"Then you can make up your mind to be a finger-post."

[157-102]"John, shall I be a finger-post for you?" She stood and looked at him

[157-103]for a moment or two, with her eyes full of love, as though she were

[157-104]going to throw herself into his arms. And she would have done so, no

[157-105]doubt, instantly, had he risen to his legs. As it was, after having

[157-106]gazed at him for the moment with her love-laden eyes, she flung

[157-107]herself on the sofa, and hid her face among the cushions.

[157-108]He had felt that it was coming for the last quarter of an hour,--and

[157-109]he had felt, also, that he was quite unable to help himself. He

[157-110]did not believe that he should ever be reduced to marrying Miss

[157-111]Demolines, but he did see plainly enough that he was getting into

[157-112]trouble; and yet, for his life, he could not help himself. The moth

[157-113]who flutters round the light knows that he is being burned, and yet

[157-114]he cannot fly away from it. When Madalina had begun to talk to him

[157-115]about women in general, and then about herself, and had told him that

[157-116]such a woman as herself,--even one so liable to the disturbance of

[157-117]violent emotions,--might yet be as true and honest as the sun, he

[157-118]knew that he ought to get up and make his escape. He did not exactly

[157-119]know how the catastrophe would come, but he was quite sure that if he

[157-120]remained there he would be called upon in some way for a declaration

[157-121]of his sentiments,--and that the call would be one which all his wit

[157-122]would not enable him to answer with any comfort. It was very well

[157-123]jesting about milestones, but every jest brought him nearer to the

[157-124]precipice. He perceived that however ludicrous might be the image

[157-125]which his words produced, she was clever enough in some way to turn

[157-126]that image to her own purpose. He had called a woman a finger-post,

[157-127]and forthwith she had offered to come to him and be finger-post to

[157-128]him for life! What was he to say to her? It was clear that he must

[157-129]say something. As at this moment she was sobbing violently, he could

[157-130]not pass the offer by as a joke. Women will say that his answer

[157-131]should have been very simple, and his escape very easy. But men will

[157-132]understand that it is not easy to reject even a Miss Demolines when

[157-133]she offers herself for matrimony. And, moreover,--as Johnny bethought

[157-134]himself at this crisis of his fate,--Lady Demolines was no doubt at

[157-135]the other side of the drawing-room door, ready to stop him, should

[157-136]he attempt to run away. In the meantime the sobs on the sofa became

[157-137]violent, and still more violent. He had not even yet made up his

[157-138]mind what to do, when Madalina, springing to her feet, stood before

[157-139]him, with her curls wildly waving and her arms extended. "Let it be

[157-140]as though it were unsaid," she exclaimed. John Eames had not the

[157-141]slightest objection; but, nevertheless, there was a difficulty even

[157-142]in this. Were he simply to assent to this latter proposition, it

[157-143]could not be but that the feminine nature of Miss Demolines would be

[157-144]outraged by so uncomplimentary an acquiescence. He felt that he ought

[157-145]at least to hesitate a little,--to make some pretence at closing upon

[157-146]the rich offer that had been made to him; only that were he to show

[157-147]any such pretence the rich offer would, no doubt, be repeated. His

[157-148]Madalina had twitted him in the earlier part of their interview with

[157-149]knowing nothing of the nature of women. He did know enough to feel

[157-150]assured that any false step on his part now would lead him into very

[157-151]serious difficulties. "Let it be as though it were unsaid! Why, oh,

[157-152]why, have I betrayed myself?" exclaimed Madalina.

[157-153]John now had risen from his chair, and coming up to her took her by

[157-154]the arm and spoke a word. "Compose yourself," he said. He spoke in

[157-155]his most affectionate voice, and he stood very close to her.

[157-156]"How easy it is to bid me do that," said Madalina. "Tell the sea to

[157-157]compose itself when it rages!"

[157-158]"Madalina!" said he.

[157-159]"Well,--what of Madalina? Madalina has lost her own respect,--for

[157-160]ever."

[157-161]"Do not say that."

[157-162]"Oh, John,--why did you ever come here? Why? Why did we meet at

[157-163]that fatal woman's house? Or, meeting so, why did we not part as

[157-164]strangers? Sir, why have you come here to my mother's house day after

[157-165]day, evening after evening, if--. Oh, heavens, what am I saying? I

[157-166]wonder whether you will scorn me always?"

[157-167]"I will never scorn you."

[157-168]"And you will pardon me?"

[157-169]"Madalina, there is nothing to pardon."

[157-170]"And--you will love me?" Then, without waiting for any more

[157-171]encouraging reply,--unable, probably, to wait a moment longer, she

[157-172]sunk upon his bosom. He caught her, of course,--and at that moment

[157-173]the drawing-room door was opened, and Lady Demolines entered the

[157-174]chamber. John Eames detected at a glance the skirt of the old white

[157-175]dressing gown which he had seen whisking away on the occasion of his

[157-176]last visit at Porchester Terrace. But on the present occasion Lady

[157-177]Demolines wore over it a short red opera cloak, and the cap on her

[157-178]head was ornamented with coloured ribbons. "What is this," she said,

[157-179]"and why am I thus disturbed?" Madalina lay motionless in Johnny's

[157-180]arms, while the old woman glowered at him from under the coloured

[157-181]ribbons. "Mr. Eames, what is it that I behold?" she said.

[Illustration: "What is it that I behold?"]

[157-182]"Your daughter, madam, seems to be a little unwell," said Johnny.

[157-183]Madalina kept her feet firm upon the ground, but did not for a moment

[157-184]lose her purchase against Johnny's waistcoat. Her respirations came

[157-185]very strong, but they came a good deal stronger when he mentioned the

[157-186]fact that she was not so well as she might be.

[157-187]"Unwell!" said Lady Demolines. And John was stricken at the moment

[157-188]with a conviction that her ladyship must have passed the early years

[157-189]of her life upon the stage. "You would trifle with me, sir. Beware

[157-190]that you do not trifle with her,--with Madalina!"

[157-191]"My mother," said Madalina; but still she did not give up her

[157-192]purchase, and the voice seemed to come half from her and half from

[157-193]Johnny. "Come to me, my mother." Then Lady Demolines hastened to her

[157-194]daughter, and Madalina between them was gradually laid at her length

[157-195]upon the sofa. The work of laying her out, however, was left almost

[157-196]entirely to the stronger arm of Mr. John Eames. "Thanks, mother,"

[157-197]said Madalina; but she had not as yet opened her eyes, even for an

[157-198]instant. "Perhaps I had better go now," said Johnny. The old woman

[157-199]looked at him with eyes which asked him whether "he didn't wish he

[157-200]might get it" as plainly as though the words had been pronounced. "Of

[157-201]course I'll wait if I can be of any service," said Johnny.
