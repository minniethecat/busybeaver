[093-001]"I don't understand this at all," said Sir Raffle.

[093-002]"I was afraid that it was only too plain," said John Eames.

[093-003]"And you must go?"

[093-004]"Oh, yes;--that's certain. I have pledged myself to go."

[093-005]"Of course I don't know anything of this matter that is so important

[093-006]to your family."

[093-007]"No; you do not," said Johnny.

[093-008]"Can't you explain it to me, then? so that I may have some

[093-009]reason,--if there is any reason."

[093-010]Then John told the story of Mr. Crawley,--a considerable portion of

[093-011]the story; and in his telling of it, I think it probable that he

[093-012]put more weight upon the necessity of his mission to Italy than it

[093-013]could have fairly been made to bear. In the course of the narration

[093-014]Sir Raffle did once contrive to suggest that a lawyer by going to

[093-015]Florence might do the business at any rate as well as John Eames.

[093-016]But Johnny denied this. "No, Sir Raffle, it is impossible; quite

[093-017]impossible," he said. "If you saw the lawyer who is acting in the

[093-018]matter, Mr. Toogood, who is also my uncle, he would tell you the

[093-019]same." Sir Raffle had already heard something of the story of Mr.

[093-020]Crawley, and was now willing to accept the sad tragedy of that case

[093-021]as an excuse for his private secretary's somewhat insubordinate

[093-022]conduct. "Under the circumstances, Eames, I suppose you must go; but

[093-023]I think you should have told me all about it before."

[093-024]"I did not like to trouble you, Sir Raffle, with private business."

[093-025]"It is always best to tell the whole of a story," said Sir Raffle.

[093-026]Johnny being quite content with the upshot of the negotiations

[093-027]accepted this gentle rebuke in silence, and withdrew. On the next day

[093-028]he appeared again at the office in his ordinary costume, and an idea

[093-029]crossed Sir Raffle's brain that he had been partly "done" by the

[093-030]affectation of a costume. "I'll be even with him some day yet," said

[093-031]Sir Raffle to himself.

[093-032]"I've got my leave, boys," said Eames when he went out into the room

[093-033]in which his three friends sat.

[093-034]"No!" said Cradell.

[093-035]"But I have," said Johnny.

[093-036]"You don't mean that old Huffle Scuffle has given it out of his own

[093-037]head?" said Fisher.

[093-038]"Indeed he has," said Johnny; "and bade God bless me into the

[093-039]bargain."

[093-040]"And you didn't give him the oysters?" said FitzHoward. "Not a

[093-041]shell," said Johnny.

[093-042]"I'm blessed if you don't beat cock-fighting," said Cradell, lost in

[093-043]admiration at his friend's adroitness.

[093-044]We know how John passed his evening after that. He went first to see

[093-045]Lily Dale at her uncle's lodgings in Sackville Street, from thence

[093-046]he was taken to the presence of the charming Madalina in Porchester

[093-047]Terrace, and then wound up the night with his friend Conway

[093-048]Dalrymple. When he got to his bed he felt himself to have been

[093-049]triumphant, but in spite of his triumph he was ashamed of himself.

[093-050]Why had he left Lily to go to Madalina? As he thought of this he

[093-051]quoted to himself against himself Hamlet's often-quoted appeal to the

[093-052]two portraits. How could he not despise himself in that he could find

[093-053]any pleasure with Madalina, having a Lily Dale to fill his thoughts?

[093-054]"But she is not fair for me," he said to himself,--thinking thus to

[093-055]comfort himself. But he did not comfort himself.

[093-056]On the next morning early his uncle, Mr. Toogood, met him at the

[093-057]Dover Railway Station. "Upon my word, Johnny, you're a clever

[093-058]fellow," said he. "I never thought that you'd make it all right with

[093-059]Sir Raffle."

[093-060]"As right as a trivet, uncle. There are some people, if you can only

[093-061]get to learn the length of their feet, you can always fit them with

[093-062]shoes afterwards."

[Illustration: "As right as a trivet, Uncle."]

[093-063]"You'll go on direct to Florence, Johnny?"

[093-064]"Yes; I think so. From what we have heard, Mrs. Arabin must be either

[093-065]there or at Venice, and I don't suppose I could learn from any one at

[093-066]Paris at which town she is staying at this moment."

[093-067]"Her address is Florence;--poste restante, Florence. You will be sure

[093-068]to find out at any of the hotels where she is staying, or where she

[093-069]has been staying."

[093-070]"But when I have found her, I don't suppose she can tell me

[093-071]anything," said Johnny.

[093-072]"Who can tell? She may or she may not. My belief is that the money

[093-073]was her present altogether, and not his. It seems that they don't mix

[093-074]their moneys. He has always had some scruple about it because of her

[093-075]son by a former marriage, and they always have different accounts at

[093-076]their bankers'. I found that out when I was at Barchester."

[093-077]"But Crawley was his friend."

[093-078]"Yes, Crawley was his friend; but I don't know that fifty-pound notes

[093-079]have always been so very plentiful with him. Deans' incomes ain't

[093-080]what they were, you know."

[093-081]"I don't know anything about that," said Johnny.

[093-082]"Well; they are not. And he has nothing of his own, as far as I can

[093-083]learn. It would be just the thing for her to do,--to give the money

[093-084]to his friend. At any rate she will tell you whether it was so or

[093-085]not."

[093-086]"And then I will go on to Jerusalem, after him."

[093-087]"Should you find it necessary. He will probably be on his way back,

[093-088]and she will know where you can hit him on the road. You must make

[093-089]him understand that it is essential that he should be here some

[093-090]little time before the trial. You can understand, Johnny,"--and as he

[093-091]spoke Mr. Toogood lowered his voice to a whisper, though they were

[093-092]walking together on the platform of the railway station, and could

[093-093]not possibly have been overheard by any one. "You can understand that

[093-094]it may be necessary to prove that he is not exactly compos mentis,

[093-095]and if so it will be essential that he should have some influential

[093-096]friend near him. Otherwise that bishop will trample him into dust."

[093-097]If Mr. Toogood could have seen the bishop at this time and have read

[093-098]the troubles of the poor man's heart, he would hardly have spoken of

[093-099]him as being so terrible a tyrant.

[093-100]"I understand all that," said Johnny.

[093-101]"So that, in fact, I shall expect to see you both together," said

[093-102]Toogood.

[093-103]"I hope the dean is a good fellow."

[093-104]"They tell me he is a very good fellow."

[093-105]"I never did see much of bishops or deans as yet," said Johnny, "and

[093-106]I should feel rather awe-struck travelling with one."

[093-107]"I should fancy that a dean is very much like anybody else."

[093-108]"But the man's hat would cow me."

[093-109]"I daresay you'll find him walking about Jerusalem with a wide-awake

[093-110]on, and a big stick in his hand, probably smoking a cigar. Deans

[093-111]contrive to get out of their armour sometimes, as the knights of old

[093-112]used to do. Bishops, I fancy, find it more difficult. Well;--good-by,

[093-113]old fellow. I'm very much obliged to you for going,--I am, indeed. I

[093-114]don't doubt but what we shall pull through, somehow."

[093-115]Then Mr. Toogood went home to breakfast, and from his own house he

[093-116]proceeded to his office. When he had been there an hour or two, there

[093-117]came to him a messenger from the Income-tax Office, with an official

[093-118]note addressed to himself by Sir Raffle Buffle,--a note which looked

[093-119]to be very official. Sir Raffle Buffle presented his compliments to

[093-120]Mr. Toogood, and could Mr. Toogood favour Sir R. B. with the present

[093-121]address of Mr. John Eames. "Old fox," said Mr. Toogood;--"but then

[093-122]such a stupid old fox! As if it was likely that I should have peached

[093-123]on Johnny if anything was wrong." So Mr. Toogood sent his compliments

[093-124]to Sir Raffle Buffle, and begged to inform Sir R. B. that Mr. John

[093-125]Eames was away on very particular family business, which would take

[093-126]him in the first instance to Florence;--but that from Florence he

[093-127]would probably have to go on to Jerusalem without the loss of an

[093-128]hour. "Stupid old fool!" said Mr. Toogood, as he sent off his reply

[093-129]by the messenger.

[093-130]CHAPTER XLIX.

[093-131]NEAR THE CLOSE.

[093-132][Illustration]

[093-133]I wonder whether any one will read these pages who has never known

[093-134]anything of the bitterness of a family quarrel? If so, I shall have a

[093-135]reader very fortunate, or else very cold-blooded. It would be wrong

[093-136]to say that love produces quarrels; but love does produce those

[093-137]intimate relations of which quarrelling is too often one of the

[093-138]consequences,--one of the consequences which frequently seem to be so

[093-139]natural, and sometimes seem to be unavoidable. One brother rebukes

[093-140]the other,--and what brothers ever lived together between whom there

[093-141]was no such rebuking?--then some warm word is misunderstood and

[093-142]hotter words follow and there is a quarrel. The husband tyrannizes,

[093-143]knowing that it is his duty to direct, and the wife disobeys, or only

[093-144]partially obeys, thinking that a little independence will become

[093-145]her,--and so there is a quarrel. The father, anxious only for his

[093-146]son's good, looks into that son's future with other eyes than those

[093-147]of his son himself,--and so there is a quarrel. They come very

[093-148]easily, these quarrels, but the quittance from them is sometimes

[093-149]terribly difficult. Much of thought is necessary before the angry man

[093-150]can remember that he too in part may have been wrong; and any attempt

[093-151]at such thinking is almost beyond the power of him who is carefully

[093-152]nursing his wrath, lest it cool! But the nursing of such quarrelling

[093-153]kills all happiness. The very man who is nursing his wrath, lest it

[093-154]cool,--his wrath against one whom he loves perhaps the best of all

[093-155]whom it has been given him to love,--is himself wretched as long as

[093-156]it lasts. His anger poisons every pleasure of his life. He is sullen

[093-157]at his meals, and cannot understand his book as he turns its pages.

[093-158]His work, let it be what it may, is ill done. He is full of his

[093-159]quarrel,--nursing it. He is telling himself how much he has loved

[093-160]that wicked one, how many have been his sacrifices for that wicked

[093-161]one, and that now that wicked one is repaying him simply with

[093-162]wickedness! And yet the wicked one is at that very moment dearer to

[093-163]him than ever. If that wicked one could only be forgiven how sweet

[093-164]would the world be again! And yet he nurses his wrath.

[093-165]So it was in these days with Archdeacon Grantly. He was very angry

[093-166]with his son. It is hardly too much to say that in every moment of

[093-167]his life, whether waking or sleeping, he was thinking of the injury

[093-168]that his son was doing him. He had almost come to forget the fact

[093-169]that his anger had first been roused by the feeling that his son was

[093-170]about to do himself an injury,--to cut his own throat. Various other

[093-171]considerations had now added themselves to that, and filled not only

[093-172]his mind but his daily conversation with his wife. How terrible

[093-173]would be the disgrace to Lord Hartletop, how incurable the injury to

[093-174]Griselda, the marchioness, should the brother-in-law of the one, and

[093-175]the brother of the other, marry the daughter of a convicted thief!

[093-176]"Of himself he would say nothing." So he declared constantly, though

[093-177]of himself he did say a great deal. "Of himself he would say nothing,

[093-178]though of course such a marriage would ruin him in the county." "My

[093-179]dear," said his wife, "that is nonsense. That really is nonsense. I

[093-180]feel sure there is not a single person in the county who would think

[093-181]of the marriage in such a light." Then the archdeacon would have

[093-182]quarrelled with his wife too, had she not been too wise to admit

[093-183]such a quarrel. Mrs. Grantly was very wise and knew that it took two

[093-184]persons to make a quarrel. He told her over and over again that she

[093-185]was in league with her son,--that she was encouraging her son to

[093-186]marry Grace Crawley. "I believe that in your heart you wish it," he

[093-187]once said to her. "No, my dear, I do not wish it. I do not think it a

[093-188]becoming marriage. But if he does marry her, I should wish to receive

[093-189]his wife in my house, and certainly should not quarrel with him." "I

[093-190]will never receive her," the archdeacon had replied; "and as for him,

[093-191]I can only say that in such case I will make no provision for his

[093-192]family."
