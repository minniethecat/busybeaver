[044-003]"No, mamma, no. I have been looking for the light ever since I read

[044-004]his letter, and I think I see it. And now, mamma, I will make a clean

[044-005]breast of it. From the moment in which I heard that that poor woman

[044-006]was dead, I have been in a state of flutter. It has been weak of

[044-007]me, and silly, and contemptible. But I could not help it. I kept on

[044-008]asking myself whether he would ever think of me now. Well; he has

[044-009]answered the question; and has so done it that he has forced upon me

[044-010]the necessity of a resolution. I have resolved, and I believe that I

[044-011]shall be the better for it."

[044-012]The letter which Mrs. Dale wrote to Mr. Crosbie, was as follows:--

[044-013]"Mrs. Dale presents her compliments to Mr. Crosbie, and begs to

[044-014]assure him that it will not now be possible that he should renew the

[044-015]relations which were broken off three years ago, between him and Mrs.

[044-016]Dale's family." It was very short, certainly, and it did not by any

[044-017]means satisfy Mrs. Dale. But she did not know how to say more without

[044-018]saying too much. The object of her letter was to save him the trouble

[044-019]of a futile perseverance, and them from the annoyance of persecution;

[044-020]and this she wished to do without mentioning her daughter's name. And

[044-021]she was determined that no word should escape her in which there was

[044-022]any touch of severity, any hint of an accusation. So much she owed to

[044-023]Lily in return for all that Lily was prepared to abandon. "There is

[044-024]my note," she said at last, offering it to her daughter. "I did not

[044-025]mean to see it," said Lily, "and, mamma, I will not read it now. Let

[044-026]it go. I know you have been good and have not scolded him." "I have

[044-027]not scolded him, certainly," said Mrs. Dale. And then the letter was

[044-028]sent.

[044-029]CHAPTER XXIV.

[044-030]MRS. DOBBS BROUGHTON'S DINNER-PARTY.

[044-031]Mr. John Eames, of the Income-tax Office, had in these days risen

[044-032]so high in the world that people in the west-end of town, and very

[044-033]respectable people too,--people living in South Kensington, in

[044-034]neighbourhoods not far from Belgravia, and in very handsome houses

[044-035]round Bayswater,--were glad to ask him out to dinner. Money had been

[044-036]left to him by an earl, and rumour had of course magnified that

[044-037]money. He was a private secretary, which is in itself a great advance

[044-038]on being a mere clerk. And he had become the particularly intimate

[044-039]friend of an artist who had pushed himself into high fashion during

[044-040]the last year or two,--one Conway Dalrymple, whom the rich English

[044-041]world was beginning to pet and pelt with gilt sugar-plums, and who

[044-042]seemed to take very kindly to petting and gilt sugar-plums. I don't

[044-043]know whether the friendship of Conway Dalrymple had not done as much

[044-044]to secure John Eames his position at the Bayswater dinner-tables, as

[044-045]had either the private secretaryship, or the earl's money; and yet,

[044-046]when they had first known each other, now only two or three years

[044-047]ago, Conway Dalrymple had been the poorer man of the two. Some chance

[044-048]had brought them together, and they had lived in the same rooms for

[044-049]nearly two years. This arrangement had been broken up, and the Conway

[044-050]Dalrymple of these days had a studio of his own, somewhere near

[044-051]Kensington Palace, where he painted portraits of young countesses,

[044-052]and in which he had even painted a young duchess. It was the peculiar

[044-053]merit of his pictures,--so at least said the art-loving world,--that

[044-054]though the likeness was always good, the stiffness of the modern

[044-055]portrait was never there. There was also ever some story told in

[044-056]Dalrymple's pictures over and above the story of the portraiture.

[044-057]This countess was drawn as a fairy with wings, that countess as

[044-058]a goddess with a helmet. The thing took for a time, and Conway

[044-059]Dalrymple was picking up his gilt sugar-plums with considerable

[044-060]rapidity.

[044-061]On a certain day he and John Eames were to dine out together at a

[044-062]certain house in that Bayswater district. It was a large mansion,

[044-063]if not made of stone yet looking very stony, with thirty windows at

[044-064]least, all of them with cut-stone frames, requiring, let me say, at

[044-065]least four thousand a year for its maintenance. And its owner, Dobbs

[044-066]Broughton, a man very well known both in the City and over the grass

[044-067]in Northamptonshire, was supposed to have a good deal more than four

[044-068]thousand a year. Mrs. Dobbs Broughton, a very beautiful woman, who

[044-069]certainly was not yet thirty-five, let her worst enemies say what

[044-070]they might, had been painted by Conway Dalrymple as a Grace. There

[044-071]were, of course, three Graces in the picture, but each Grace was Mrs.

[044-072]Dobbs Broughton repeated. We all know how Graces stand sometimes;

[044-073]two Graces looking one way, and one the other. In this picture, Mrs.

[044-074]Dobbs Broughton as centre Grace looked you full in the face. The same

[044-075]lady looked away from you, displaying her left shoulder as one side

[044-076]Grace, and displaying her right shoulder as the other side Grace. For

[044-077]this pretty toy Mr. Conway Dalrymple had picked up a gilt sugar-plum

[044-078]to the tune of six hundred pounds, and had, moreover, won the heart

[044-079]both of Mr. and Mrs. Dobbs Broughton. "Upon my word, Johnny,"

[044-080]Dalrymple had said to his friend, "he's a deuced good fellow, has

[044-081]really a good glass of claret,--which is getting rarer and rarer

[044-082]every day,--and will mount you for a day, whenever you please,

[044-083]down at Market Harboro'. Come and dine with them." Johnny Eames

[044-084]condescended, and did go and dine with Mr. Dobbs Broughton. I wonder

[044-085]whether he remembered, when Conway Dalrymple was talking of the

[044-086]rarity of good claret, how much beer the young painter used to drink

[044-087]when they were out together in the country, as they used to be

[044-088]occasionally, three years ago; and how the painter had then been

[044-089]used to complain that bitter beer cost threepence a glass, instead

[044-090]of twopence, which had hitherto been the recognized price of the

[044-091]article. In those days the sugar-plums had not been gilt, and had

[044-092]been much rarer.

[044-093]Johnny Eames and his friend went together to the house of Mr. Dobbs

[044-094]Broughton. As Dalrymple lived close to the Broughtons, Eames picked

[044-095]him up in a cab. "Filthy things, these cabs are," said Dalrymple, as

[044-096]he got into the Hansom.

[044-097]"I don't know about that," said Johnny. "They're pretty good, I

[044-098]think."

[044-099]"Foul things," said Conway. "Don't you feel what a draught comes in

[044-100]here because the glass is cracked. I'd have one of my own, only I

[044-101]should never know what to do with it."

[044-102]"The greatest nuisance on earth, I should think," said Johnny.

[044-103]"If you could always have it standing ready round the corner," said

[044-104]the artist, "it would be delightful. But one would want half a dozen

[044-105]horses, and two or three men for that."

[044-106]"I think the stands are the best," said Johnny.

[044-107]They were a little late,--a little later than they should have been

[044-108]had they considered that Eames was to be introduced to his new

[044-109]acquaintances. But he had already lived long enough before the world

[044-110]to be quite at his ease in such circumstances, and he entered Mrs.

[044-111]Broughton's drawing-room with his pleasantest smile upon his face.

[044-112]But as he entered he saw a sight which made him look serious in spite

[044-113]of his efforts to the contrary. Mr. Adolphus Crosbie, secretary to

[044-114]the Board at the General Committee Office, was standing on the rug

[044-115]before the fire.

[044-116]"Who will be there?" Eames had asked of his friend, when the

[044-117]suggestion to go and dine with Dobbs Broughton had been made to him.

[044-118]"Impossible to say," Conway had replied. "A certain horrible fellow

[044-119]of the name of Musselboro, will almost certainly be there. He always

[044-120]is when they have anything of a swell dinner-party. He is a sort of

[044-121]partner of Broughton's in the City. He wears a lot of chains, and has

[044-122]elaborate whiskers, and an elaborate waistcoat, which is worse; and

[044-123]he doesn't wash his hands as often as he ought to do."

[044-124]"An objectionable party, rather, I should say," said Eames.

[044-125]"Well, yes; Musselboro is objectionable. He's very good-humoured you

[044-126]know, and good-looking in a sort of way, and goes everywhere; that

[044-127]is among people of this sort. Of course he's not hand-and-glove with

[044-128]Lord Derby; and I wish he could be made to wash his hands. They

[044-129]haven't any other standing dish, and you may meet anybody. They

[044-130]always have a Member of Parliament; they generally manage to catch

[044-131]a Baronet; and I have met a Peer there. On that august occasion

[044-132]Musselboro was absent."

[044-133]So instructed, Eames, on entering the room, looked round at once for

[044-134]Mr. Musselboro. "If I don't see the whiskers and chain," he had said,

[044-135]"I shall know there's a Peer." Mr. Musselboro was in the room, but

[044-136]Eames had descried Mr. Crosbie long before he had seen Mr.

[044-137]Musselboro.

[044-138]There was no reason for confusion on his part in meeting Crosbie.

[044-139]They had both loved Lily Dale. Crosbie might have been successful,

[044-140]but for his own fault. Eames had on one occasion been thrown into

[044-141]contact with him, and on that occasion had quarrelled with him and

[044-142]had beaten him, giving him a black eye, and in this way obtaining

[044-143]some mastery over him. There was no reason why he should be ashamed

[044-144]of meeting Crosbie; and yet, when he saw him, the blood mounted

[044-145]all over his face, and he forgot to make any further search for Mr.

[044-146]Musselboro.

[044-147]"I am so much obliged to Mr. Dalrymple for bringing you," said Mrs.

[044-148]Dobbs Broughton very sweetly, "only he ought to have come sooner.

[044-149]Naughty man! I know it was his fault. Will you take Miss Demolines

[044-150]down? Miss Demolines,--Mr. Eames."

[044-151]Mr. Dobbs Broughton was somewhat sulky and had not welcomed our hero

[044-152]very cordially. He was beginning to think that Conway Dalrymple gave

[044-153]himself airs and did not sufficiently understand that a man who had

[044-154]horses at Market Harboro' and '41 Lafitte was at any rate as good

[044-155]as a painter who was pelted with gilt sugar-plums for painting

[044-156]countesses. But he was a man whose ill-humour never lasted long, and

[044-157]he was soon pressing his wine on Johnny Eames as though he loved him

[044-158]dearly.

[044-159]But there was yet a few minutes before they went down to dinner, and

[044-160]Johnny Eames, as he endeavoured to find something to say to Miss

[044-161]Demolines,--which was difficult, as he did not in the least know Miss

[044-162]Demolines' line of conversation,--was aware that his efforts were

[044-163]impeded by thoughts of Mr. Crosbie. The man looked older than when he

[044-164]had last seen him,--so much older that Eames was astonished. He was

[044-165]bald, or becoming bald; and his whiskers were grey, or were becoming

[044-166]grey, and he was much fatter. Johnny Eames, who was always thinking

[044-167]of Lily Dale, could not now keep himself from thinking of Adolphus

[044-168]Crosbie. He saw at a glance that the man was in mourning, though

[044-169]there was nothing but his shirt-studs by which to tell it; and he

[044-170]knew that he was in mourning for his wife. "I wish she might have

[044-171]lived for ever," Johnny said to himself.

[044-172]He had not yet been definitely called upon by the entrance of the

[044-173]servant to offer his arm to Miss Demolines, when Crosbie walked

[044-174]across to him from the rug and addressed him.

[044-175]"Mr. Eames," said he, "it is some time since we met." And he offered

[044-176]his hand to Johnny.

[044-177]"Yes, it is," said Johnny, accepting the proffered salutation. "I

[044-178]don't know exactly how long, but ever so long."

[044-179]"I am very glad to have the opportunity of shaking hands with you,"

[044-180]said Crosbie; and then he retired, as it had become his duty to wait

[044-181]with his arm ready for Mrs. Dobbs Broughton. Having married an earl's

[044-182]daughter he was selected for that honour. There was a barrister in

[044-183]the room, and Mrs. Dobbs Broughton ought to have known better. As

[044-184]she professed to be guided in such matters by the rules laid down by

[044-185]the recognized authorities, she ought to have been aware that a man

[044-186]takes no rank from his wife. But she was entitled I think to merciful

[044-187]consideration for her error. A woman situated as was Mrs. Dobbs

[044-188]Broughton cannot altogether ignore these terrible rules. She cannot

[044-189]let her guests draw lots for precedence. She must select some one for

[044-190]the honour of her own arm. And amidst the intricacies of rank how

[044-191]is it possible for a woman to learn and to remember everything? If

[044-192]Providence would only send Mrs. Dobbs Broughton a Peer for every

[044-193]dinner-party, the thing would go more easily; but what woman will

[044-194]tell me, off-hand, which should go out of a room first: a C.B., an

[044-195]Admiral of the Blue, the Dean of Barchester, or the Dean of Arches?

[044-196]Who is to know who was everybody's father? How am I to remember that

[044-197]young Thompson's progenitor was made a baronet and not a knight when

[044-198]he was Lord Mayor? Perhaps Mrs. Dobbs Broughton ought to have known

[044-199]that Mr. Crosbie could have gained nothing by his wife's rank, and

[044-200]the barrister may be considered to have been not immoderately severe

[044-201]when he simply spoke of her afterwards as the silliest and most

[044-202]ignorant old woman he had ever met in his life. Eames with the lovely

[044-203]Miss Demolines on his arm was the last to move before the hostess.

[044-204]Mr. Dobbs Broughton had led the way energetically with old Lady

[044-205]Demolines. There was no doubt about Lady Demolines,--as his wife

[044-206]had told him, because her title marked her. Her husband had been

[044-207]a physician in Paris, and had been knighted in consequence of

[044-208]some benefit supposed to have been done to some French scion of

[044-209]royalty,--when such scions in France were royal and not imperial.

[044-210]Lady Demolines' rank was not much, certainly; but it served to mark

[044-211]her, and was beneficial.
