[Illustration: Lily wishes that they might swear to be

[066-001]Brother and Sister.]

[066-002]"It can never be like that between us," said Johnny.

[066-003]"Can it not? I think it can. Perhaps not this year, or next year;

[066-004]perhaps not in the next five years. But I make myself happy with

[066-005]thinking that it may be so some day. I shall wait for it patiently,

[066-006]very patiently, even though you should rebuff me again and again,--as

[066-007]you have done now."

[066-008]"I have not rebuffed you."

[066-009]"Not maliciously, or injuriously, or offensively. I will be very

[066-010]patient, and take little rebuffs without complaining. This is the

[066-011]worst stile of all. When Grace and I are here together we can never

[066-012]manage it without tearing ourselves all to pieces. It is much nicer

[066-013]to have you to help me."

[066-014]"Let me help you always," he said, keeping her hands in his after he

[066-015]had aided her to jump from the stile to the ground.

[066-016]"Yes, as my brother."

[066-017]"That is nonsense, Lily."

[066-018]"Is it nonsense? Nonsense is a hard word."

[066-019]"It is nonsense as coming from you to me. Lily, I sometimes think

[066-020]that I am persecuting you, writing to you, coming after you, as I

[066-021]am doing now,--telling the same whining story,--asking, asking, and

[066-022]asking for that which you say you will never give me. And then I feel

[066-023]ashamed of myself, and swear that I will do it no more."

[066-024]"Do not be ashamed of yourself; but yet do it no more."

[066-025]"And then," he continued, without minding her words, "at other times

[066-026]I feel that it must be my own fault; that if I only persevered with

[066-027]sufficient energy I must be successful. At such times I swear that I

[066-028]will never give it up."

[066-029]"Oh, John, if you could only know how little worthy of such pursuit

[066-030]it is."

[066-031]"Leave me to judge of that, dear. When a man has taken a month, or

[066-032]perhaps only a week, or perhaps not more than half an hour, to make

[066-033]up his mind, it may be very well to tell him that he doesn't know

[066-034]what he is about. I've been in the office now for over seven years,

[066-035]and the first day I went I put an oath into a book that I would come

[066-036]back and get you for my wife when I had got enough to live upon."

[066-037]"Did you, John?"

[066-038]"Yes. I can show it you. I used to come and hover about the place in

[066-039]the old days, before I went to London, when I was such a fool that

[066-040]I couldn't speak to you if I met you. I am speaking of a time long

[066-041]before,--before that man came down here."

[066-042]"Do not speak of him, Johnny."

[066-043]"I must speak of him. A man isn't to hold his tongue when everything

[066-044]he has in the world is at stake. I suppose he loved you after a

[066-045]fashion, once."

[066-046]"Pray, pray do not speak ill of him."

[066-047]"I am not going to abuse him. You can judge of him by his deeds. I

[066-048]cannot say anything worse of him than what they say. I suppose he

[066-049]loved you; but he certainly did not love you as I have done. I have

[066-050]at any rate been true to you. Yes, Lily, I have been true to you.

[066-051]I am true to you. He did not know what he was about. I do. I am

[066-052]justified in saying that I do. I want you to be my wife. It is no use

[066-053]your talking about it as though I only half wanted it."

[066-054]"I did not say that."

[066-055]"Is not a man to have any reward? Of course if you had married him

[066-056]there would have been an end of it. He had come in between me and my

[066-057]happiness, and I must have borne it, as other men bear such sorrows.

[066-058]But you have not married him; and, of course, I cannot but feel that

[066-059]I may yet have a chance. Lily, answer me this. Do you believe that I

[066-060]love you?" But she did not answer him. "You can at any rate tell me

[066-061]that. Do you think that I am in earnest?"

[066-062]"Yes, I think you are in earnest."

[066-063]"And do you believe that I love you with all my heart and all my

[066-064]strength and all my soul?"

[066-065]"Oh, John!"

[066-066]"But do you?"

[066-067]"I think you love me."

[066-068]"Think! what am I to say or to do to make you understand that my only

[066-069]idea of happiness is the idea that sooner or later I may get you to

[066-070]be my wife? Lily, will you say that it shall be so? Speak, Lily.

[066-071]There is no one that will not be glad. Your uncle will consent,--has

[066-072]consented. Your mother wishes it. Bell wishes it. My mother wishes

[066-073]it. Lady Julia wishes it. You would be doing what everybody about

[066-074]you wants you to do. And why should you not do it? It isn't that you

[066-075]dislike me. You wouldn't talk about being my sister, if you had not

[066-076]some sort of regard for me."

[066-077]"I have a regard for you."

[066-078]"Then why will you not be my wife? Oh, Lily, say the word now, here,

[066-079]at once. Say the word, and you'll make me the happiest fellow in all

[066-080]England." As he spoke he took her by both arms, and held her fast.

[066-081]She did not struggle to get away from him, but stood quite still,

[066-082]looking into his face, while the first sparkle of a salt tear formed

[066-083]itself in each eye. "Lily, one little word will do it,--half a word,

[066-084]a nod, a smile. Just touch my arm with your hand and I will take it

[066-085]for a yes." I think that she almost tried to touch him; that the word

[066-086]was in her throat, and that she almost strove to speak it. But there

[066-087]was no syllable spoken, and her fingers did not loose themselves to

[066-088]fall upon his sleeve. "Lily, Lily, what can I say to you?"

[066-089]"I wish I could," she whispered;--but the whisper was so hoarse that

[066-090]he hardly recognized the voice.

[066-091]"And why can you not? What is there to hinder you? There is nothing

[066-092]to hinder you, Lily."

[066-093]"Yes, John; there is that which must hinder me."

[066-094]"And what is it?"

[066-095]"I will tell you. You are so good and so true, and so

[066-096]excellent,--such a dear, dear, dear friend, that I will tell you

[066-097]everything, so that you may read my heart. I will tell you as I tell

[066-098]mamma,--you and her and no one else;--for you are the choice friend

[066-099]of my heart. I cannot be your wife because of the love I bear for

[066-100]another man."

[066-101]"And that man is he,--he who came here?"

[066-102]"Of course it is he. I think, Johnny, you and I are alike in this,

[066-103]that when we have loved we cannot bring ourselves to change. You will

[066-104]not change, though it would be so much better you should do so."

[066-105]"No; I will never change."

[066-106]"Nor can I. When I sleep I dream of him. When I am alone I cannot

[066-107]banish him from my thoughts. I cannot define what it is to love him.

[066-108]I want nothing from him,--nothing, nothing. But I move about through

[066-109]my little world thinking of him, and I shall do so to the end. I

[066-110]used to feel proud of my love, though it made me so wretched that I

[066-111]thought it would kill me. I am not proud of it any longer. It is a

[066-112]foolish poor-spirited weakness,--as though my heart had been only

[066-113]half formed in the making. Do you be stronger, John. A man should be

[066-114]stronger than a woman."

[066-115]"I have none of that sort of strength."

[066-116]"Nor have I. What can we do but pity each other, and swear that we

[066-117]will be friends,--dear friends. There is the oak-tree and I have got

[066-118]to turn back. We have said everything that we can say,--unless you

[066-119]will tell me that you will be my brother."

[066-120]"No; I will not tell you that."

[066-121]"Good-by, then, Johnny."

[066-122]He paused, holding her by the hand and thinking of another question

[066-123]which he longed to put to her,--considering whether he would ask her

[066-124]that question or not. He hardly knew whether he were entitled to ask

[066-125]it;--whether or no the asking of it would be ungenerous. She had said

[066-126]that she would tell him everything,--as she had told everything to

[066-127]her mother. "Of course," he said, "I have no right to expect to know

[066-128]anything of your future intentions?"

[066-129]"You may know them all,--as far as I know them myself. I have said

[066-130]that you should read my heart."

[066-131]"If this man, whose name I cannot bear to mention, should come

[066-132]again--"

[066-133]"If he were to come again he would come in vain, John." She did not

[066-134]say that he had come again. She could tell her own secret, but not

[066-135]that of another person.

[066-136]"You would not marry him, now that he is free?"

[066-137]She stood and thought a while before she answered him. "No, I should

[066-138]not marry him now. I think not." Then she paused again. "Nay, I am

[066-139]sure I would not. After what has passed I could not trust myself to

[066-140]do it. There is my hand on it. I will not."

[066-141]"No, Lily, I do not want that."

[066-142]"But I insist. I will not marry Mr. Crosbie. But you must not

[066-143]misunderstand me, John. There;--all that is over for me now. All

[066-144]those dreams about love, and marriage, and of a house of my own, and

[066-145]children,--and a cross husband, and a wedding-ring growing always

[066-146]tighter as I grow fatter and older. I have dreamed of such things

[066-147]as other girls do,--more perhaps than other girls, more than I

[066-148]should have done. And now I accept the thing as finished. You wrote

[066-149]something in your book, you dear John,--something that could not be

[066-150]made to come true. Dear John, I wish for your sake it was otherwise.

[066-151]I will go home and I will write in my book, this very day, Lilian

[066-152]Dale, Old Maid. If ever I make that false, do you come and ask me for

[066-153]the page."

[066-154]"Let it remain there till I am allowed to tear it out."

[066-155]"I will write it, and it shall never be torn out. You I cannot marry.

[066-156]Him I will not marry. You may believe me, Johnny, when I say there

[066-157]can never be a third."

[066-158]"And is that to be the end of it?"

[066-159]"Yes;--that is to be the end of it. Not the end of our friendship.

[066-160]Old maids have friends."

[066-161]"It shall not be the end of it. There shall be no end of it with me."

[066-162]"But, John--"

[066-163]"Do not suppose that I will trouble you again,--at any rate not for a

[066-164]while. In five years time perhaps,--"

[066-165]"Now, Johnny, you are laughing at me. And of course it is the best

[066-166]way. If there is not Grace, and she has caught me before I have

[066-167]turned back. Good-by, dear, dear John. God bless you. I think you

[066-168]the finest fellow there is in the world. I do, and so does mamma.

[066-169]Remember always that there is a temple at Allington in which your

[066-170]worship is never forgotten." Then she pressed his hand and turned

[066-171]away from him to meet Grace Crawley. John did not stop to speak a

[066-172]word to his cousin, but pursued his way alone.

[066-173]"That cousin of yours," said Lily, "is simply the dearest,

[066-174]warmest-hearted, finest creature that ever was seen in the shape of

[066-175]a man."

[066-176]"Have you told him that you think him so?" said Grace.

[066-177]"Indeed, I have," said Lily.

[066-178]"But have you told this finest, warmest, dearest creature that he

[066-179]shall be rewarded with the prize he covets?"

[066-180]"No, Grace. I have told him nothing of the kind. I think he

[066-181]understands it all now. If he does not, it is not for the want of my

[066-182]telling him. I don't suppose any lady was ever more open-spoken to a

[066-183]gentleman than I have been to him."
