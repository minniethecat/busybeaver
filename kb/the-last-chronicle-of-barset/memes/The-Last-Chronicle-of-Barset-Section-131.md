[131-001]"Oh, goodness! And what shall I say to her?"

[131-002]"You will have to say very little. She will speak to you."

[131-003]"I suppose everything belongs to her now," said Mrs. Broughton.

[131-004]"I know nothing about that. I never do know anything of mamma's money

[131-005]matters."

[131-006]"Of course she'll turn me out. I do not mind a bit about that,--only

[131-007]I hope she'll let me have some mourning." Then she made Clara promise

[131-008]that she would return as soon as possible, having in Clara's presence

[131-009]overcome all that feeling of dislike which she had expressed to

[131-010]Conway Dalrymple. Mrs. Broughton was generally affectionate to

[131-011]those who were near to her. Had Musselboro forced himself into her

[131-012]presence, she would have become quite confidential with him before he

[131-013]left her.

[131-014]"Mr. Musselboro will be here directly," said Mrs. Van Siever, as she

[131-015]was starting for Mrs. Broughton's house. "You had better tell him to

[131-016]come to me there; or, stop,--perhaps you had better keep him here

[131-017]till I come back. Tell him to be sure and wait for me."

[131-018]"Very well, mamma. I suppose he can wait below?"

[131-019]"Why should he wait below?" said Mrs. Van Siever, very angrily.

[131-020]Clara had made the uncourteous proposition to her mother with the

[131-021]express intention of making it understood that she would have nothing

[131-022]to say to him. "He can come upstairs if he likes it," said Clara;

[131-023]"and I will go up to my room."

[131-024]"If you fight shy of him, miss, you may remember this,--that you will

[131-025]fight shy of me at the same time."

[131-026]"I am sorry for that, mamma, for I shall certainly fight shy of Mr.

[131-027]Musselboro."

[131-028]"You can do as you please. I can't force you, and I shan't try. But

[131-029]I can make your life a burden to you,--and I will. What's the matter

[131-030]with the man that he isn't good enough for you? He's as good as any

[131-031]of your own people ever was. I hate your new-fangled airs,--with

[131-032]pictures painted on the sly, and all the rest of it. I hate such

[131-033]ways. See what they have brought that wretched man to, and the poor

[131-034]fool his wife. If you go and marry that painter, some of these days

[131-035]you'll be very much like what she is. Only I doubt whether he has got

[131-036]courage enough to blow his brains out." With these comfortable words,

[131-037]the old woman took herself off, leaving Clara to entertain her lover

[131-038]as best she might choose.

[131-039]Mr. Musselboro was not long in coming, and, in accordance with Mrs.

[131-040]Van Siever's implied directions to her daughter, was shown up into

[131-041]the drawing-room. Clara gave him her mother's message in a very few

[131-042]words. "I was expressly told, sir, to ask you to stop, if it is not

[131-043]inconvenient, as she very much wants to see you." Mr. Musselboro

[131-044]declared that of course he would stop. He was only too happy to have

[131-045]an opportunity of remaining in such delightful society. As Clara

[131-046]answered nothing to this, he went on to say that he hoped that the

[131-047]melancholy occasion of Mrs. Van Siever's visit to Mrs. Broughton

[131-048]might make a long absence necessary,--he did not, indeed, care how

[131-049]long it might be. He had recovered now from that paleness, and that

[131-050]want of gloves and jewellery which had befallen him on the previous

[131-051]day immediately after the sight he had seen in the City. Clara made

[131-052]no answer to the last speech, but, putting some things together in

[131-053]her work-basket, prepared to leave the room. "I hope you are not

[131-054]going to leave me?" he said, in a voice that was intended to convey

[131-055]much of love, and something of melancholy.

[131-056]"I am so shocked by what has happened, Mr. Musselboro, that I am

[131-057]altogether unfit for conversation. I was with poor Mrs. Broughton

[131-058]last night, and I shall return to her when mamma comes home."

[131-059]"It is sad, certainly; but what was there to be expected? If you'd

[131-060]only seen how he used to go on." To this Clara made no answer. "Don't

[131-061]go yet," said he; "there is something that I want to say to you.

[131-062]There is, indeed."

[131-063]Clara Van Siever was a young woman whose presence of mind rarely

[131-064]deserted her. It occurred to her now that she must undergo on some

[131-065]occasion the nuisance of a direct offer from this man, and that she

[131-066]could have no better opportunity of answering him after her own

[131-067]fashion than the present. Her mother was absent, and the field was

[131-068]her own. And, moreover, it was a point in her favour that the tragedy

[131-069]which had so lately occurred, and to which she had just now alluded,

[131-070]would give her a fair excuse for additional severity. At such a

[131-071]moment no man could, she told herself, be justified in making an

[131-072]offer of his love, and therefore she might rebuke him with the less

[131-073]remorse. I wonder whether the last words which Conway Dalrymple had

[131-074]spoken to her stung her conscience as she thought of this! She had

[131-075]now reached the door, and was standing close to it. As Mr. Musselboro

[131-076]did not at once begin, she encouraged him. "If you have anything

[131-077]special to tell me, of course I will hear you," she said.

[131-078]"Miss Clara," he began, rising from his chair, and coming into the

[131-079]middle of the room, "I think you know what my wishes are." Then he

[131-080]put his hand upon his heart. "And your respected mother is the same

[131-081]way of thinking. It's that that emboldens me to be so sudden. Not but

[131-082]what my heart has been yours and yours only all along, before the old

[131-083]lady so much as mentioned it." Clara would give him no assistance,

[131-084]not even the aid of a negative, but stood there quite passive, with

[131-085]her hand on the door. "Since I first had the pleasure of seeing you

[131-086]I have always said to myself, 'Augustus Musselboro, that is the woman

[131-087]for you, if you can only win her.' But then there was so much against

[131-088]me,--wasn't there?" She would not even take advantage of this by

[131-089]assuring him that there certainly always had been much against him,

[131-090]but allowed him to go on till he should run out all the length of

[131-091]his tether. "I mean, of course, in the way of money," he continued.

[131-092]"I hadn't much that I could call my own when your respected mamma

[131-093]first allowed me to become acquainted with you. But it's different

[131-094]now; and I think I may say that I'm all right in that respect. Poor

[131-095]Broughton's going in this way will make it a deal smoother to me; and

[131-096]I may say that I and your mamma will be all in all to each other now

[131-097]about money." Then he stopped.

[131-098]"I don't quite understand what you mean by all this," said Clara.

[131-099]"I mean that there isn't a more devoted fellow in all London than

[131-100]what I am to you." Then he was about to go down on one knee, but it

[131-101]occurred to him that it would not be convenient to kneel to a lady

[131-102]who would stand quite close to the door. "One and one, if they're put

[131-103]together well, will often make more than two, and so they shall with

[131-104]us," said Musselboro, who began to feel that it might be expedient to

[131-105]throw a little spirit into his words.

[131-106]"If you have done," said Clara, "you may as well hear me for a

[131-107]minute. And I hope you will have sense to understand that I really

[131-108]mean what I say."

[131-109]"I hope you will remember what are your mamma's wishes."

[131-110]"Mamma's wishes have no influence whatsoever with me in such matters

[131-111]as this. Mamma's arrangements with you are for her own convenience,

[131-112]and I am not a party to them. I do not know anything about mamma's

[131-113]money, and I do not want to know. But under no possible circumstances

[131-114]will I consent to become your wife. Nothing that mamma could say or

[131-115]do would induce me even to think of it. I hope you will be man enough

[131-116]to take this for an answer, and say nothing more about it."

[131-117]"But, Miss Clara--"

[131-118]"It's no good your Miss Claraing me, sir. What I have said you may be

[131-119]sure I mean. Good-morning, sir." Then she opened the door, and left

[131-120]him.

[131-121]"By Jove, she is a Tartar," said Musselboro to himself, when he was

[131-122]alone. "They're both Tartars, but the younger is the worse." Then he

[131-123]began to speculate whether Fortune was not doing the best for him in

[131-124]so arranging that he might have the use of the Tartar-mother's money

[131-125]without binding himself to endure for life the Tartar qualities of

[131-126]the daughter.

[131-127]It had been understood that Clara was to wait at home till her mother

[131-128]should return before she again went across to Mrs. Broughton. At

[131-129]about eleven Mrs. Van Siever came in, and her daughter intercepted

[131-130]her at the dining-room door before she had made her way upstairs to

[131-131]Mr. Musselboro. "How is she, mamma?" said Clara with something of

[131-132]hypocrisy in her assumed interest for Mrs. Broughton.

[131-133]"She is an idiot," said Mrs. Van Siever.

[131-134]"She has had a terrible misfortune!"

[131-135]"That is no reason why she should be an idiot; and she is heartless

[131-136]too. She never cared a bit for him;--not a bit."

[131-137]"He was a man whom it was impossible to care for much. I will go to

[131-138]her now, mamma."

[131-139]"Where is Musselboro?"

[131-140]"He is upstairs."

[131-141]"Well?"

[131-142]"Mamma, that is quite out of the question. Quite. I would not marry

[131-143]him to save myself from starving."

[131-144]"You do not know what starving is yet, my dear. Tell me the truth at

[131-145]once. Are you engaged to that painter?" Clara paused a moment before

[131-146]she answered, not hesitating as to the expediency of telling her

[131-147]mother any truth on the matter in question, but doubting what the

[131-148]truth might really be. Could she say that she was engaged to Mr.

[131-149]Dalrymple, or could she say that she was not? "If you tell me a lie,

[131-150]miss, I'll have you put out of the house."

[Illustration: "You do not know what starving is, my dear."]

[131-151]"I certainly shall not tell you a lie. Mr. Dalrymple has asked me to

[131-152]be his wife, and I have made him no answer. If he asks me again I

[131-153]shall accept him."

[131-154]"Then I order you not to leave this house," said Mrs. Van Siever.

[131-155]"Surely I may go to Mrs. Broughton?"

[131-156]"I order you not to leave this house," said Mrs. Van Siever

[131-157]again,--and thereupon she stalked out of the dining-room and went

[131-158]upstairs. Clara had been standing with her bonnet on, ready dressed

[131-159]to go out, and the mother made no attempt to send the daughter up to

[131-160]her room. That she did not expect to be obeyed in her order may be

[131-161]inferred from the first words which she spoke to Mr. Musselboro. "She

[131-162]has gone off to that man now. You are no good, Musselboro, at this

[131-163]kind of work."

[131-164]"You see, Mrs. Van, he had the start of me so much. And then being at

[131-165]the West End, and all that, gives a man such a standing with a girl!"

[131-166]"Bother!" said Mrs. Van Siever, as her quick ear caught the sound of

[131-167]the closing hall-door. Clara had stood a minute or two to consider,

[131-168]and then had resolved that she would disobey her mother. She tried to

[131-169]excuse her own conduct to her own satisfaction as she went. "There

[131-170]are some things," she said, "which even a daughter cannot hear from

[131-171]her mother. If she chooses to close the door against me, she must do

[131-172]so."

[131-173]She found Mrs. Broughton still in bed, and could not but agree with

[131-174]her mother that the woman was both silly and heartless.

[131-175]"Your mother says that everything must be sold up," said Mrs.

[131-176]Broughton.
