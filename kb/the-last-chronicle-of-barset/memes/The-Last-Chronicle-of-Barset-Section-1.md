[001-092]CHAPTER I.

[001-093]HOW DID HE GET IT?

[001-094][Illustration]

[001-095]"I can never bring myself to believe it, John," said Mary Walker,

[001-096]the pretty daughter of Mr. George Walker, attorney of Silverbridge.

[001-097]Walker and Winthrop was the name of the firm, and they were

[001-098]respectable people, who did all the solicitors' business that had

[001-099]to be done in that part of Barsetshire on behalf of the Crown, were

[001-100]employed on the local business of the Duke of Omnium who is great in

[001-101]those parts, and altogether held their heads up high, as provincial

[001-102]lawyers often do. They,--the Walkers,--lived in a great brick

[001-103]house in the middle of the town, gave dinners, to which the county

[001-104]gentlemen not unfrequently condescended to come, and in a mild way

[001-105]led the fashion in Silverbridge. "I can never bring myself to believe

[001-106]it, John," said Miss Walker.

[001-107]"You'll have to bring yourself to believe it," said John, without

[001-108]taking his eyes from his book.

[001-109]"A clergyman,--and such a clergyman too!"

[001-110]"I don't see that that has anything to do with it." And as he now

[001-111]spoke, John did take his eyes off his book. "Why should not a

[001-112]clergyman turn thief as well as anybody else? You girls always seem

[001-113]to forget that clergymen are only men after all."

[001-114]"Their conduct is likely to be better than that of other men, I

[001-115]think."

[001-116]"I deny it utterly," said John Walker. "I'll undertake to say that

[001-117]at this moment there are more clergymen in debt in Barsetshire than

[001-118]there are either lawyers or doctors. This man has always been in

[001-119]debt. Since he has been in the county I don't think he has ever been

[001-120]able to show his face in the High Street of Silverbridge."

[001-121]"John, that is saying more than you have a right to say," said Mrs.

[001-122]Walker.

[001-123]"Why, mother, this very cheque was given to a butcher who had

[001-124]threatened a few days before to post bills all about the county,

[001-125]giving an account of the debt that was due to him, if the money was

[001-126]not paid at once."

[001-127]"More shame for Mr. Fletcher," said Mary. "He has made a fortune as

[001-128]butcher in Silverbridge."

[001-129]"What has that to do with it? Of course a man likes to have his

[001-130]money. He had written three times to the bishop, and he had sent

[001-131]a man over to Hogglestock to get his little bill settled six days

[001-132]running. You see he got it at last. Of course, a tradesman must look

[001-133]for his money."

[001-134]"Mamma, do you think that Mr. Crawley stole the cheque?" Mary, as she

[001-135]asked the question, came and stood over her mother, looking at her

[001-136]with anxious eyes.

[001-137]"I would rather give no opinion, my dear."

[001-138]"But you must think something when everybody is talking about it,

[001-139]mamma."

[001-140]"Of course my mother thinks he did," said John, going back to his

[001-141]book. "It is impossible that she should think otherwise."

[001-142]"That is not fair, John," said Mrs. Walker; "and I won't have you

[001-143]fabricate thoughts for me, or put the expression of them into my

[001-144]mouth. The whole affair is very painful, and as your father is

[001-145]engaged in the inquiry, I think that the less said about the matter

[001-146]in this house the better. I am sure that that would be your father's

[001-147]feeling."

[001-148]"Of course I should say nothing about it before him," said Mary. "I

[001-149]know that papa does not wish to have it talked about. But how is one

[001-150]to help thinking about such a thing? It would be so terrible for all

[001-151]of us who belong to the Church."

[001-152]"I do not see that at all," said John. "Mr. Crawley is not more than

[001-153]any other man just because he's a clergyman. I hate all that kind of

[001-154]clap-trap. There are a lot of people here in Silverbridge who think

[001-155]the matter shouldn't be followed up, just because the man is in a

[001-156]position which makes the crime more criminal in him than it would be

[001-157]in another."

[001-158]"But I feel sure that Mr. Crawley has committed no crime at all,"

[001-159]said Mary.

[001-160]"My dear," said Mrs. Walker, "I have just said that I would rather

[001-161]you would not talk about it. Papa will be in directly."

[001-162]"I won't, mamma;--only--"

[001-163]"Only! yes; just only!" said John. "She'd go on till dinner if any

[001-164]one would stay to hear her."

[001-165]"You've said twice as much as I have, John." But John had left the

[001-166]room before his sister's last words could reach him.

[001-167]"You know, mamma, it is quite impossible not to help thinking of it,"

[001-168]said Mary.

[001-169]"I dare say it is, my dear."

[001-170]"And when one knows the people it does make it so dreadful."

[001-171]"But do you know them? I never spoke to Mr. Crawley in my life, and

[001-172]I do not think I ever saw her."

[001-173]"I knew Grace very well,--when she used to come first to Miss

[001-174]Prettyman's school."

[001-175]"Poor girl. I pity her."

[001-176]"Pity her! Pity is no word for it, mamma. My heart bleeds for them.

[001-177]And yet I do not believe for a moment that he stole the cheque. How

[001-178]can it be possible? For though he may have been in debt because they

[001-179]have been so very, very poor; yet we all know that he has been an

[001-180]excellent clergyman. When the Robartses were dining here last, I

[001-181]heard Mrs. Robarts say that for piety and devotion to his duties she

[001-182]had hardly ever seen any one equal to him. And the Robartses know

[001-183]more of them than anybody."

[001-184]"They say that the dean is his great friend."

[001-185]"What a pity it is that the Arabins should be away just now when he

[001-186]is in such trouble." And in this way the mother and daughter went

[001-187]on discussing the question of the clergyman's guilt in spite of Mrs.

[001-188]Walker's previously expressed desire that nothing more might be said

[001-189]about it. But Mrs. Walker, like many other mothers, was apt to be

[001-190]more free in converse with her daughter than she was with her son.

[001-191]While they were thus talking the father came in from his office, and

[001-192]then the subject was dropped. He was a man between fifty and sixty

[001-193]years of age, with grey hair, rather short, and somewhat corpulent,

[001-194]but still gifted with that amount of personal comeliness which

[001-195]comfortable position and the respect of others will generally seem to

[001-196]give. A man rarely carries himself meanly, whom the world holds high

[001-197]in esteem.

[001-198]"I am very tired, my dear," said Mr. Walker.

[001-199]"You look tired. Come and sit down for a few minutes before you

[001-200]dress. Mary, get your father's slippers." Mary instantly ran to the

[001-201]door.

[001-202]"Thanks, my darling," said the father. And then he whispered to his

[001-203]wife, as soon as Mary was out of hearing, "I fear that unfortunate

[001-204]man is guilty. I fear he is! I fear he is!"

[001-205]"Oh, heavens! what will become of them?"

[001-206]"What indeed? She has been with me to-day."

[001-207]"Has she? And what could you say to her?"

[001-208]"I told her at first that I could not see her, and begged her not to

[001-209]speak to me about it. I tried to make her understand that she should

[001-210]go to some one else. But it was of no use."
