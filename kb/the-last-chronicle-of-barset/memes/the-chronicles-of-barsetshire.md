## Work

The Chronicles of Barsetshire is a series of six novels by Anthony Trollope, published between 1855 and 1867, set primarily in the fictional cathedral county of Barsetshire. The series is among the most celebrated achievements of Victorian fiction: a sustained, multi-volume portrait of provincial English life that examines the workings of the Church of England, the gentry, and the social order they jointly sustain. The six novels — *The Warden*, *Barchester Towers*, *Doctor Thorne*, *Framley Parsonage*, *The Small House at Allington*, and *The Last Chronicle of Barset* — are linked by shared characters, recurring locations, and a consistent moral vision.

## Overview

The series traces the ecclesiastical and social life of [[barchester]] and its surrounding parishes, estates, and villages across two decades. The novels are not a continuous narrative but a series of interconnected stories, each centred on a particular crisis or set of characters while drawing on the established world of its predecessors. Major recurring characters include Archdeacon Grantly, Mr. Harding, [[lady-lufton|Lady Lufton]], and [[mrs-proudie|Mrs. Proudie]], while locations such as [[framley]], [[hogglestock]], [[allington]], and [[silverbridge]] appear across multiple volumes.

The series moves from the intimate scale of *The Warden* — a study of one elderly clergyman's conscience — to the panoramic social canvas of *The Last Chronicle of Barset*, which brings together characters from across the county for a narrative that encompasses law, poverty, marriage, and death. This expansion of scope across the series mirrors Trollope's own development as a novelist and his growing ambition to portray English society in its institutional and moral complexity.

## Context

Trollope conceived the Barsetshire novels at a time when the serial novel was the dominant form of literary production. Each Barsetshire novel was first published in monthly instalments (except *The Warden*, which appeared as a single volume), and the demands of serial publication — cliffhangers, recurring characters, narrative momentum — shaped the novels' structure. The series established Trollope's reputation and remains, along with the Palliser novels, his most enduring contribution to English literature.

The Barsetshire series is notable for its creation of a coherent fictional geography. The county of Barsetshire, with its cathedral city, market towns, country estates, and impoverished parishes, constitutes one of the most fully realised imaginary worlds in fiction before Tolkien. The series' unity of place allows Trollope to explore how the same institutions — the church, the law, the family — function differently at different levels of the social scale.
