[102-001]Lily Dale had not liked the idea of being fitted out thus

[102-002]expensively. A box at the opera was all very well, as it was not

[102-003]procured especially for her. And tickets for other theatres did not

[102-004]seem to come unnaturally for a night or two. But her spirit had

[102-005]militated against the hat and the habit and the horse. The whip was a

[102-006]little present from Emily Dunstable, and that of course was accepted

[102-007]with a good grace. Then there came the horse,--as though from the

[102-008]heavens; there seemed to be ten horses, twenty horses, if anybody

[102-009]needed them. All these things seemed to flow naturally into Mrs.

[102-010]Thorne's establishment, like air through the windows. It was very

[102-011]pleasant, but Lily hesitated when she was told that a habit was to

[102-012]be given to her. "My dear old aunt insists," said Emily Dunstable.

[102-013]"Nobody ever thinks of refusing anything from her. If you only knew

[102-014]what some people will take, and some people will even ask, who have

[102-015]nothing to do with her at all!" "But I have nothing to do with

[102-016]her,--in that way I mean," said Lily. "Oh, yes, you have," said

[102-017]Emily. "You and Bernard are as good as brother and sister, and

[102-018]Bernard and I are as good as man and wife, and my aunt and I are as

[102-019]good as mother and daughter. So you see, in a sort of a way you are

[102-020]a child of the house." So Lily accepted the habit; but made a stand

[102-021]at the hat, and paid for that out of her own pocket. When the squire

[102-022]had seen Lily on horseback he asked her questions about it. "It

[102-023]was a hired horse, I suppose?" he said. "I think it came direct

[102-024]from heaven," said Lily. "What do you mean, Lily?" said the squire,

[102-025]angrily. "I mean that when people are so rich and good-natured as

[102-026]Mrs. Thorne it is no good inquiring where things come from. All that

[102-027]I know is that the horses come out of Potts' livery-stable. They

[102-028]talk of Potts as if he were a good-natured man who provides horses

[102-029]for the world without troubling anybody." Then the squire spoke to

[102-030]Bernard about it, saying that he should insist on defraying his

[102-031]niece's expenses. But Bernard swore that he could give his uncle no

[102-032]assistance. "I would not speak to her about such a thing for all the

[102-033]world," said Bernard. "Then I shall," said the squire.

[102-034]In those days Lily thought much of Johnny Eames,--gave to him perhaps

[102-035]more of that thought which leads to love than she had ever given

[102-036]him before. She still heard the Crawley question discussed every

[102-037]day. Mrs. Thorne, as we all know, was at this time a Barsetshire

[102-038]personage, and was of course interested in Barsetshire subjects; and

[102-039]she was specially anxious in the matter, having strong hopes with

[102-040]reference to the marriage of Major Grantly and Grace, and strong

[102-041]hopes also that Grace's father might escape the fangs of justice. The

[102-042]Crawley case was constantly in Lily's ears, and as constantly she

[102-043]heard high praise awarded to Johnny for his kindness in going after

[102-044]the Arabins. "He must be a fine young fellow," said Mrs. Thorne, "and

[102-045]we'll have him down at Chaldicotes some day. Old Lord De Guest found

[102-046]him out and made a friend of him, and old Lord De Guest was no fool."

[102-047]Lily was not altogether free from a suspicion that Mrs. Thorne knew

[102-048]the story of Johnny's love and was trying to serve Johnny,--as other

[102-049]people had tried to do, very ineffectually. When this suspicion came

[102-050]upon her she would shut her heart against her lover's praises, and

[102-051]swear that she would stand by those two letters which she had written

[102-052]in her book at home. But the suspicion would not be always there, and

[102-053]there did come upon her a conviction that her lover was more esteemed

[102-054]among men and women than she had been accustomed to believe. Her

[102-055]cousin, Bernard Dale, who certainly was regarded in the world as

[102-056]somebody, spoke of him as his equal; whereas in former days Bernard

[102-057]had always regarded Johnny Eames as standing low in the world's

[102-058]regard. Then Lily, when alone, would remember a certain comparison

[102-059]which she once made between Adolphus Crosbie and John Eames, when

[102-060]neither of the men had as yet pleaded his cause to her, and which had

[102-061]been very much in favour of the former. She had then declared that

[102-062]Johnny was a "mere clerk." She had a higher opinion of him now,--a

[102-063]much higher opinion, even though he could never be more to her than a

[102-064]friend.

[102-065]In these days Lily's new ally, Emily Dunstable, seemed to Lily to be

[102-066]so happy! There was in Emily a complete realization of that idea of

[102-067]ante-nuptial blessedness of which Lily had often thought so much.

[102-068]Whatever Emily did she did for Bernard; and, to give Captain Dale his

[102-069]due, he received all the sweets which were showered upon him with

[102-070]becoming signs of gratitude. I suppose it is always the case at such

[102-071]times that the girl has the best of it, and on this occasion Emily

[102-072]Dunstable certainly made the most of her happiness. "I do envy you,"

[102-073]Lily said one day. The acknowledgment seemed to have been extorted

[102-074]from her involuntarily. She did not laugh as she spoke, or follow up

[102-075]what she had said with other words intended to take away the joke

[102-076]of what she had uttered,--had it been a joke; but she sat silent,

[102-077]looking at the girl who was re-arranging flowers which Bernard had

[102-078]brought to her.

[102-079]"I can't give him up to you, you know," said Emily.

[102-080]"I don't envy you him, but 'it,'" said Lily.

[102-081]"Then go and get an 'it' for yourself. Why don't you have an 'it' for

[102-082]yourself? You can have an 'it' to-morrow, if you like,--or two or

[102-083]three, if all that I hear is true."

[102-084]"No, I can't," said Lily. "Things have gone wrong with me. Don't ask

[102-085]me anything more about it. Pray don't. I shan't speak of it if you

[102-086]do."

[102-087]"Of course I will not if you tell me I must not."

[102-088]"I do tell you so. I have been a fool to say anything about it.

[102-089]However, I have got over my envy now, and am ready to go out with

[102-090]your aunt. Here she is."

[102-091]"Things have gone wrong with me." She repeated the same words to

[102-092]herself over and over again. With all the efforts which she had made

[102-093]she could not quite reconcile herself to the two letters which she

[102-094]had written in the book. This coming up to London, and riding in the

[102-095]Park, and going to the theatres, seemed to unsettle her. At home she

[102-096]had schooled herself down into quiescence, and made herself think

[102-097]that she believed that she was satisfied with the prospects of her

[102-098]life. But now she was all astray again, doubting about herself,

[102-099]hankering after something over and beyond that which seemed to be

[102-100]allotted to her,--but, nevertheless, assuring herself that she never

[102-101]would accept of anything else.

[102-102]I must not, if I can help it, let the reader suppose that she was

[102-103]softening her heart to John Eames because John Eames was spoken well

[102-104]of in the world. But with all of us, in the opinion which we form

[102-105]of those around us, we take unconsciously the opinion of others. A

[102-106]woman is handsome because the world says so. Music is charming to

[102-107]us because it charms others. We drink our wines with other men's

[102-108]palates, and look at our pictures with other men's eyes. When Lily

[102-109]heard John Eames praised by all around her, it could not be but that

[102-110]she should praise him too,--not out loud, as others did, but in the

[102-111]silence of her heart. And then his constancy to her had been so

[102-112]perfect! If that other one had never come! If it could be that she

[102-113]might begin again, and that she might be spared that episode in her

[102-114]life which had brought him and her together!

[102-115]"When is Mr. Eames going to be back?" Mrs. Thorne said at dinner one

[102-116]day. On this occasion the squire was dining at Mrs. Thorne's house;

[102-117]and there were three or four others there,--among them a Mr. Harold

[102-118]Smith, who was in Parliament, and his wife, and John Eames's especial

[102-119]friend, Sir Raffle Buffle. The question was addressed to the squire,

[102-120]but the squire was slow to answer, and it was taken up by Sir Raffle

[102-121]Buffle.

[102-122]"He'll be back on the 15th," said the knight, "unless he means to

[102-123]play truant. I hope he won't do that, as his absence has been a

[102-124]terrible inconvenience to me." Then Sir Raffle explained that John

[102-125]Eames was his private secretary, and that Johnny's journey to the

[102-126]Continent had been made with, and could not have been made without,

[102-127]his sanction. "When I came to hear the story, of course I told him

[102-128]that he must go. 'Eames,' I said, 'take the advice of a man who knows

[102-129]the world. Circumstanced as you are, you are bound to go.' And he

[102-130]went."

[102-131]"Upon my word that was very good-natured of you," said Mrs. Thorne.

[102-132]"I never keep a fellow to his desk who has really got important

[102-133]business elsewhere," said Sir Raffle. "The country, I say, can afford

[102-134]to do as much as that for her servants. But then I like to know that

[102-135]the business is business. One doesn't choose to be humbugged."

[102-136]"I daresay you are humbugged, as you call it, very often," said

[102-137]Harold Smith.

[102-138]"Perhaps so; perhaps I am; perhaps that is the opinion which they

[102-139]have of me at the Treasury. But you were hardly long enough there,

[102-140]Smith, to have learned much about it, I should say."

[102-141]"I don't suppose I should have known much about it, as you call it,

[102-142]if I had stayed till Doomsday."

[102-143]"I daresay not; I daresay not. Men who begin as late as you did never

[102-144]know what official life really means. Now I've been at it all my

[102-145]life, and I think I do understand it."

[102-146]"It's not a profession I should like unless where it's joined with

[102-147]politics," said Harold Smith.

[102-148]"But then it's apt to be so short," said Sir Raffle Buffle. Now it

[102-149]had happened once in the life of Mr. Harold Smith that he had been

[102-150]in a Ministry, but, unfortunately, that Ministry had gone out almost

[102-151]within a week of the time of Mr. Smith's adhesion. Sir Raffle and Mr.

[102-152]Smith had known each other for many years, and were accustomed to

[102-153]make civil little speeches to each other in society.

[102-154]"I'd sooner be a horse in a mill than have to go to an office every

[102-155]day," said Mrs. Smith, coming to her husband's assistance. "You, Sir

[102-156]Raffle, have kept yourself fresh and pleasant through it all; but who

[102-157]besides you ever did?"

[102-158]"I hope I am fresh," said Sir Raffle; "and as for pleasantness, I

[102-159]will leave that for you to determine."

[102-160]"There can be but one opinion," said Mrs. Thorne.

[102-161]The conversation had strayed away from John Eames, and Lily was

[102-162]disappointed. It was a pleasure to her when people talked of him

[102-163]in her hearing, and as a question or two had been asked about him,

[102-164]making him the hero of the moment, it seemed to her that he was

[102-165]being robbed of his due when the little amenities between Mr. and

[102-166]Mrs. Harold Smith and Sir Raffle banished his name from the circle.

[102-167]Nothing more, however, was said of him at dinner, and I fear that he

[102-168]would have been altogether forgotten throughout the evening, had not

[102-169]Lily herself referred,--not to him, which she could not possibly have

[102-170]been induced to do,--but to the subject of his journey. "I wonder

[102-171]whether poor Mr. Crawley will be found guilty?" she said to Sir

[102-172]Raffle up in the drawing-room.
