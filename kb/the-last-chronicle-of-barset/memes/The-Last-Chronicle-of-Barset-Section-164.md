[164-001]"I'm not so sure of that," said Conway, who had heard something of

[164-002]Mr. Peter Bangles. "There are men who have strong wills of their own,

[164-003]and strong hands of their own."

[164-004]"Poor Madalina!" said Johnny. "If he does beat her, I hope he will

[164-005]do it tenderly. It may be that a little of it will suit her fevered

[164-006]temperament."

[164-007]Before the summer was over Conway Dalrymple had been married to

[164-008]Clara Van Siever, and by a singular arrangement of circumstances

[164-009]had married her with the full approval of old Mrs. Van. Mr.

[164-010]Musselboro,--whose name I hope has not been altogether forgotten,

[164-011]though the part played by him has been subordinate,--had opposed

[164-012]Dalrymple in the efforts made by the artist to get something out of

[164-013]Broughton's estate for the benefit of the widow. From circumstances

[164-014]of which Dalrymple learned the particulars with the aid of an

[164-015]attorney, it seemed to him that certain facts were wilfully kept in

[164-016]the dark by Musselboro, and he went with his complaint to Mrs. Van

[164-017]Siever, declaring that he would bring the whole affair into court,

[164-018]unless all the workings of the firm were made clear to him. Mrs. Van

[164-019]was very insolent to him,--and even turned him out of the house. But,

[164-020]nevertheless, she did not allow Mr. Musselboro to escape. Whoever was

[164-021]to be left in the dark she did not wish to be there herself;--and

[164-022]it began to dawn upon her that her dear Musselboro was deceiving

[164-023]her. Then she sent for Dalrymple, and without a word of apology for

[164-024]her former conduct, put him upon the right track. As he was pushing

[164-025]his inquiries, and working heaven and earth for the unfortunate

[164-026]widow,--as to whom he swore daily that when this matter was settled

[164-027]he would never see her again, so terrible was she to him with her

[164-028]mock affection and pretended hysterics, and false moralities,--he

[164-029]was told one day that she had gone off with Mr. Musselboro! Mr.

[164-030]Musselboro, finding that this was the surest plan of obtaining for

[164-031]himself the little business in Hook Court, married the widow of his

[164-032]late partner, and is at this moment probably carrying on a law-suit

[164-033]with Mrs. Van. For the law-suit Conway Dalrymple cared nothing. When

[164-034]the quarrel had become hot between Mrs. Van and her late myrmidon,

[164-035]Clara fell into Conway's hands without opposition; and, let the

[164-036]law-suit go as it may, there will be enough left of Mrs. Van's money

[164-037]to make the house of Mr. and Mrs. Conway Dalrymple very comfortable.

[164-038]The picture of Jael and Sisera was stitched up without any

[164-039]difficulty, and I daresay most of my readers will remember it hanging

[164-040]on the walls of the exhibition.

[164-041]Before I take my leave of the diocese of Barchester for ever, which

[164-042]I purpose to do in the succeeding paragraph, I desire to be allowed

[164-043]to say one word of apology for myself, in answer to those who

[164-044]have accused me,--always without bitterness, and generally with

[164-045]tenderness,--of having forgotten, in writing of clergymen, the first

[164-046]and most prominent characteristic of the ordinary English clergyman's

[164-047]life. I have described many clergymen, they say, but have spoken of

[164-048]them all as though their professional duties, their high calling,

[164-049]their daily workings for the good of those around them, were matters

[164-050]of no moment, either to me, or, in my opinion, to themselves. I would

[164-051]plead, in answer to this, that my object has been to paint the social

[164-052]and not the professional lives of clergymen; and that I have been led

[164-053]to do so, firstly, by a feeling that as no men affect more strongly,

[164-054]by their own character, the society of those around than do country

[164-055]clergymen, so, therefore, their social habits have been worth the

[164-056]labour necessary for painting them; and secondly, by a feeling

[164-057]that though I, as a novelist, may feel myself entitled to write of

[164-058]clergymen out of their pulpits, as I may also write of lawyers and

[164-059]doctors, I have no such liberty to write of them in their pulpits.

[164-060]When I have done so, if I have done so, I have so far transgressed.

[164-061]There are those who have told me that I have made all my clergymen

[164-062]bad, and none good. I must venture to hint to such judges that

[164-063]they have taught their eyes to love a colouring higher than nature

[164-064]justifies. We are, most of us, apt to love Raphael's madonnas

[164-065]better than Rembrandt's matrons. But, though we do so, we know that

[164-066]Rembrandt's matrons existed; but we have a strong belief that no

[164-067]such woman as Raphael painted ever did exist. In that he painted,

[164-068]as he may be surmised to have done, for pious purposes,--at least

[164-069]for Church purposes,--Raphael was justified; but had he painted so

[164-070]for family portraiture he would have been false. Had I written an

[164-071]epic about clergymen, I would have taken St. Paul for my model; but

[164-072]describing, as I have endeavoured to do, such clergymen as I see

[164-073]around me, I could not venture to be transcendental. For myself I can

[164-074]only say that I shall always be happy to sit, when allowed to do so,

[164-075]at the table of Archdeacon Grantly, to walk through the High Street

[164-076]of Barchester arm in arm with Mr. Robarts of Framley, and to stand

[164-077]alone and shed a tear beneath the modest black stone in the north

[164-078]transept of the cathedral on which is inscribed the name of Septimus

[164-079]Harding.

[164-080]And now, if the reader will allow me to seize him affectionately by

[164-081]the arm, we will together take our last farewell of Barset and of the

[164-082]towers of Barchester. I may not venture to say to him that, in this

[164-083]country, he and I together have wandered often through the country

[164-084]lanes, and have ridden together over the too-well wooded fields, or

[164-085]have stood together in the cathedral nave listening to the peals

[164-086]of the organ, or have together sat at good men's tables, or have

[164-087]confronted together the angry pride of men who were not good. I may

[164-088]not boast that any beside myself have so realized the place, and

[164-089]the people, and the facts, as to make such reminiscences possible

[164-090]as those which I should attempt to evoke by an appeal to perfect

[164-091]fellowship. But to me Barset has been a real county, and its city a

[164-092]real city, and the spires and towers have been before my eyes, and

[164-093]the voices of the people are known to my ears, and the pavement of

[164-094]the city ways are familiar to my footsteps. To them all I now say

[164-095]farewell. That I have been induced to wander among them too long by

[164-096]my love of old friendships, and by the sweetness of old faces, is

[164-097]a fault for which I may perhaps be more readily forgiven, when I

[164-098]repeat, with some solemnity of assurance, the promise made in my

[164-099]title, that this shall be the last chronicle of Barset.
