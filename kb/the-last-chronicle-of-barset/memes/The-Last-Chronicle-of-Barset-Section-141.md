[141-001]"He will be very well paid, I think," said John, "when he hears the

[141-002]truth. If you could see inside his mind at this moment, I'm sure

[141-003]you'd find that he thinks he stole the cheque."

[141-004]"He cannot think that, Mr. Eames. Besides, at this moment I hope he

[141-005]has heard the truth."

[141-006]"That may be, but he did think so. I do believe that he had not the

[141-007]slightest notion where he got it; and, which is more, not a single

[141-008]person in the whole county had a notion. People thought that he had

[141-009]picked it up, and used it in his despair. And the bishop has been so

[141-010]hard upon him."

[141-011]"Oh, Mr. Eames, that is the worst of all."

[141-012]"So I am told. The bishop has a wife, I believe."

[141-013]"Yes, he has a wife, certainly," said Mrs. Arabin.

[141-014]"And people say that she is not very good-natured."

[141-015]"There are some of us at Barchester who do not love her very dearly.

[141-016]I cannot say that she is one of my own especial friends."

[141-017]"I believe she has been hard to Mr. Crawley," said John Eames.

[141-018]"I should not be in the least surprised," said Mrs. Arabin.

[141-019]Then they reached Turin, and there, taking up "Galignani's Messenger"

[141-020]in the reading-room of Trompetta's Hotel, John Eames saw that Mrs.

[141-021]Proudie was dead. "Look at that," said he, taking the paragraph

[141-022]to Mrs. Arabin; "Mrs. Proudie is dead!" "Mrs. Proudie dead!" she

[141-023]exclaimed. "Poor woman! Then there will be peace at Barchester!"

[141-024]"I never knew her very intimately," she afterwards said to her

[141-025]companion, "and I do not know that I have a right to say that she

[141-026]ever did me an injury. But I remember well her first coming into

[141-027]Barchester. My sister's father-in-law, the late bishop, was just

[141-028]dead. He was a mild, kind, dear old man, whom my father loved beyond

[141-029]all the world, except his own children. You may suppose we were all

[141-030]a little sad. I was not specially connected with the cathedral then,

[141-031]except through my father,"--and Mrs. Arabin, as she told all this,

[141-032]remembered that in the days of which she was speaking she was a

[141-033]young mourning widow,--"but I think I can never forget the sort of

[141-034]harsh-toned pæan of low-church trumpets with which that poor woman

[141-035]made her entry into the city. She might have been more lenient, as

[141-036]we had never sinned by being very high. She might, at any rate, have

[141-037]been more gentle with us at first. I think we had never attempted

[141-038]much beyond decency, good-will and comfort. Our comfort she utterly

[141-039]destroyed. Good-will was not to her taste. And as for decency, when I

[141-040]remember some things, I must say that when the comfort and good-will

[141-041]went, the decency went along with them. And now she is dead! I wonder

[141-042]how the bishop will get on without her."

[141-043]"Like a house on fire, I should think," said Johnny.

[141-044]"Fie, Mr. Eames; you shouldn't speak in such a way on such a

[141-045]subject."

[141-046]Mrs. Arabin and Johnny became fast friends as they journeyed home.

[141-047]There was a sweetness in his character which endeared him readily

[141-048]to women; though, as we have seen, there was a want of something to

[141-049]make one woman cling to him. He could be soft and pleasant-mannered.

[141-050]He was fond of making himself useful, and was a perfect master of

[141-051]all those little caressing modes of behaviour in which the caress

[141-052]is quite impalpable, and of which most women know the value and

[141-053]appreciate the comfort. By the time that they had reached Paris John

[141-054]had told Mrs. Arabin the whole story of Lily Dale and Crosbie, and

[141-055]Mrs. Arabin had promised to assist him, if any assistance might be in

[141-056]her power.

[141-057]"Of course I have heard of Miss Dale," she said, "because we know the

[141-058]De Courcys." Then she turned away her face, almost blushing, as she

[141-059]remembered the first time that she had seen that Lady Alexandrina

[141-060]De Courcy whom Mr. Crosbie had married. It had been at Mr. Thorne's

[141-061]house at Ullathorne, and on that day she had done a thing which she

[141-062]had never since remembered without blushing. But it was an old story

[141-063]now, and a story of which her companion knew nothing,--of which he

[141-064]never could know anything. That day at Ullathorne Mrs. Arabin, the

[141-065]wife of the Dean of Barchester, than whom there was no more discreet

[141-066]clerical matron in the diocese, had--boxed a clergyman's ears!

[141-067]"Yes," said John, speaking of Crosbie, "he was a wise fellow; he knew

[141-068]what he was about; he married an earl's daughter."

[141-069]"And now I remember hearing that somebody gave him a terrible

[141-070]beating. Perhaps it was you?"

[141-071]"It wasn't terrible at all," said Johnny.

[141-072]"Then it was you?"

[141-073]"Oh, yes; it was I."

[141-074]"Then it was you who saved poor old Lord De Guest from the bull?"

[141-075]"Go on, Mrs. Arabin. There is no end of the grand things I've done."

[141-076]"You're quite a hero of romance."

[141-077]He bit his lip as he told himself that he was not enough of a hero.

[141-078]"I don't know about that," said Johnny. "I think what a man ought to

[141-079]do in these days is to seem not to care what he eats and drinks, and

[141-080]to have his linen very well got up. Then he'll be a hero." But that

[141-081]was hard upon Lily.

[141-082]"Is that what Miss Dale requires?" said Mrs. Arabin.

[141-083]"I was not thinking about her particularly," said Johnny, lying.

[141-084]They slept a night in Paris, as they had done also at Turin,--Mrs.

[141-085]Arabin not finding herself able to accomplish such marvels in the

[141-086]way of travelling as her companion had achieved--and then arrived

[141-087]in London in the evening. She was taken to a certain quiet clerical

[141-088]hotel at the top of Suffolk Street, much patronized by bishops and

[141-089]deans of the better sort, expecting to find a message there from

[141-090]her husband. And there was the message--just arrived. The dean had

[141-091]reached Florence three days after her departure; and as he would do

[141-092]the journey home in twenty-four hours less than she had taken, he

[141-093]would be there, at the hotel, on the day after to-morrow. "I suppose

[141-094]I may wait for him, Mr. Eames?" said Mrs. Arabin.

[141-095]"I will see Mr. Toogood to-night, and I will call here to-morrow,

[141-096]whether I see him or not. At what hour will you be in?"

[141-097]"Don't trouble yourself to do that. You must take care of Sir Raffle

[141-098]Buffle, you know."

[141-099]"I shan't go near Sir Raffle Buffle to-morrow, nor yet the next day.

[141-100]You mustn't suppose that I am afraid of Sir Raffle Buffle."

[141-101]"You are only afraid of Lily Dale." From all which it may be seen

[141-102]that Mrs. Arabin and John Eames had become very intimate on their way

[141-103]home.

[141-104]It was then arranged that he should call on Mr. Toogood that same

[141-105]night or early the next morning, and that he should come to the hotel

[141-106]at twelve o'clock on the next day. Going along one of the passages

[141-107]he passed two gentlemen in shovel-hats, with very black new coats,

[141-108]and knee-breeches; and Johnny could not but hear a few words which

[141-109]one clerical gentleman said to the other. "She was a woman of great

[141-110]energy, of wonderful spirit, but a firebrand, my lord,--a complete

[141-111]firebrand!" Then Johnny knew that the Dean of A. was talking to the

[141-112]Bishop of B. about the late Mrs. Proudie.

[141-113]CHAPTER LXXI.

[141-114]MR. TOOGOOD AT SILVERBRIDGE.

[141-115]We will now go back to Mr. Toogood as he started for Silverbridge, on

[141-116]the receipt of Mrs. Arabin's telegram from Venice. "I gave cheque to

[141-117]Mr. Crawley. It was part of a sum of money. Will write to Archdeacon

[141-118]Grantly to-day, and return home at once." That was the telegram

[141-119]which Mr. Toogood received at his office, and on receiving which he

[141-120]resolved that he must start to Barchester immediately. "It isn't

[141-121]certainly what you may call a paying business," he said to his

[141-122]partner, who continued to grumble; "but it must be done all the same.

[141-123]If it don't get into the ledger in one way it will in another." So

[141-124]Mr. Toogood started for Silverbridge, having sent to his house in

[141-125]Tavistock Square for a small bag, a clean shirt, and a toothbrush.

[141-126]And as he went down in the railway-carriage, before he went to sleep,

[141-127]he turned it all over in his mind. "Poor devil! I wonder whether any

[141-128]man ever suffered so much before. And as for that woman,--it's ten

[141-129]thousand pities that she should have died before she heard it. Talk

[141-130]of heart-complaint; she'd have had a touch of heart-complaint if she

[141-131]had known this!" Then, as he was speculating how Mrs. Arabin could

[141-132]have become possessed of the cheque, he went to sleep.

[141-133]He made up his mind that the first person to be seen was Mr. Walker,

[141-134]and after that he would, if possible, go to Archdeacon Grantly. He

[141-135]was at first minded to go at once out to Hogglestock; but when he

[141-136]remembered how very strange Mr. Crawley was in all his ways, and

[141-137]told himself professionally that telegrams were but bad sources of

[141-138]evidence on which to depend for details, he thought that it would be

[141-139]safer if he were first to see Mr. Walker. There would be very little

[141-140]delay. In a day or two the archdeacon would receive his letter, and

[141-141]in a day or two after that Mrs. Arabin would probably be at home.

[141-142]It was late in the evening before Mr. Toogood reached the house of

[141-143]the Silverbridge solicitor, having the telegram carefully folded in

[141-144]his pocket; and he was shown into the dining-room while the servant

[141-145]took his name up to Mr. Walker. The clerks were gone, and the office

[141-146]was closed; and persons coming on business at such times,--as they

[141-147]often did come to that house,--were always shown into the parlour. "I

[141-148]don't know whether master can see you to-night," said the girl; "but

[141-149]if he can, he'll come down."

[141-150]When the card was brought up to Mr. Walker he was sitting alone with

[141-151]his wife. "It's Toogood," said he; "poor Crawley's cousin."

[141-152]"I wonder whether he has found anything out," said Mrs. Walker.

[141-153]"May he not come up here?" Then Mr. Toogood was summoned into the

[141-154]drawing-room, to the maid's astonishment; for Mr. Toogood had made no

[141-155]toilet sacrifices to the goddess or grace who presides over evening

[141-156]society in provincial towns,--and presented himself with the telegram

[141-157]in his hand. "We have found out all about poor Crawley's cheque," he

[141-158]said, before the maid-servant had closed the door. "Look at that,"

[141-159]and he handed the telegram to Mr. Walker. The poor girl was obliged

[141-160]to go, though she would have given one of her ears to know the exact

[141-161]contents of that bit of paper.

[141-162]"Walker, what is it?" said his wife, before Walker had had time to

[141-163]make the contents of the document his own.

[141-164]"He got it from Mrs. Arabin," said Toogood.

[141-165]"No!" said Mrs. Walker. "I thought that was it all along."

[141-166]"It's a pity you didn't say so before," said Mr. Walker.

[141-167]"So I did; but a lawyer thinks that nobody can ever see anything but

[141-168]himself;--begging your pardon, Mr. Toogood, but I forgot you were one

[141-169]of us. But, Walker, do read it." Then the telegram was read. "I gave

[141-170]cheque to Mr. Crawley. It was part of a sum of money,"--with the rest

[141-171]of it. "I knew it would come out," said Mrs. Walker. "I was quite

[141-172]sure of it."

[141-173]"But why the mischief didn't he say so?" said Walker.

[141-174]"He did say that he got it from the dean," said Toogood.

[141-175]"But he didn't get it from the dean; and the dean clearly knew

[141-176]nothing about it."

[141-177]"I'll tell you what it is," said Mrs. Walker; "it has been some

[141-178]private transaction between Mr. Crawley and Mrs. Arabin, which the

[141-179]dean was to know nothing about; and so he wouldn't tell. I must say

[141-180]I honour him."
