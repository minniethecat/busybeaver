[026-001]"I was not quite well, Mrs. Thorne."

[026-002]"Fiddlestick. Don't tell me of not being well. There was Emily

[026-003]breaking her heart about you."

[026-004]"I'm sure Miss Dunstable--"

[026-005]"To tell you the truth, I think she'll get over it. It won't be

[026-006]mortal with her. But do tell me, Major Grantly, what are we to think

[026-007]about this poor Mr. Crawley? It was so good of you to be one of his

[026-008]bailsmen."

[026-009]"He would have found twenty in Silverbridge, if he had wanted them."

[026-010]"And do you hear that he has defied the bishop? I do so like him for

[026-011]that. Not but what poor Mrs. Proudie is the dearest friend I have in

[026-012]the world, and I'm always fighting a battle with old Lady Lufton on

[026-013]her behalf. But one likes to see one's friends worsted sometimes, you

[026-014]know."

[026-015]"I don't quite understand what did happen at Hogglestock on Sunday,"

[026-016]said the major.

[026-017]"Some say he had the bishop's chaplain put under the pump. I don't

[026-018]believe that; but there is no doubt that when the poor fellow tried

[026-019]to get into the pulpit, they took him and carried him neck and heels

[026-020]out of the church. But, tell me, Major Grantly, what is to become of

[026-021]the family?"

[026-022]"Heaven knows!"

[026-023]"Is it not sad? And that eldest girl is so nice! They tell me

[026-024]that she is perfect,--not only in beauty, but in manners and

[026-025]accomplishments. Everybody says that she talks Greek just as well as

[026-026]she does English, and that she understands philosophy from the top to

[026-027]the bottom."

[026-028]"At any rate, she is so good and so lovely that one cannot but pity

[026-029]her now," said the major.

[026-030]"You know her, then, Major Grantly? By-the-by, of course you do, as

[026-031]you were staying with her at Framley."

[026-032]"Yes, I know her."

[026-033]"What is to become of her? I'm going your way. You might as well

[026-034]get into the carriage, and I'll drive you home. If he is sent to

[026-035]prison,--and they say he must be sent to prison,--what is to become

[026-036]of them?" Then Major Grantly did get into the carriage, and, before

[026-037]he got out again, he had told Mrs. Thorne the whole story of his

[026-038]love.

[026-039]She listened to him with the closest attention; only interrupting

[026-040]him now and then with little words, intended to signify her approval.

[026-041]He, as he told his tale, did not look her in the face, but sat with

[026-042]his eyes fixed upon her muff. "And now," he said, glancing up at her

[026-043]almost for the first time as he finished his speech, "and now, Mrs.

[026-044]Thorne, what am I to do?"

[026-045]"Marry her, of course," said she, raising her hand aloft and bringing

[026-046]it down heavily upon his knee as she gave her decisive reply.

[026-047]"H--sh--h," he exclaimed, looking back in dismay towards the

[026-048]servants.

[026-049]"Oh, they never hear anything up there. They're thinking about the

[026-050]last pot of porter they had, or the next they're to get. Deary me,

[026-051]I am so glad! Of course you'll marry her."

[026-052]"You forget my father."

[026-053]"No, I don't. What has a father to do with it? You're old enough to

[026-054]please yourself without asking your father. Besides, Lord bless me,

[026-055]the archdeacon isn't the man to bear malice. He'll storm and threaten

[026-056]and stop the supplies for a month or so. Then he'll double them, and

[026-057]take your wife to his bosom, and kiss her and bless her, and all that

[026-058]kind of thing. We all know what parental wrath means in such cases as

[026-059]that."

[026-060]"But my sister--"

[026-061]"As for your sister, don't talk to me about her. I don't care two

[026-062]straws about your sister. You must excuse me, Major Grantly, but Lady

[026-063]Hartletop is really too big for my powers of vision."

[026-064]"And Edith,--of course, Mrs. Thorne, I can't be blind to the fact

[026-065]that in many ways such a marriage would be injurious to her. No man

[026-066]wishes to be connected with a convicted thief."

[026-067]"No, Major Grantly; but a man does wish to marry the girl that he

[026-068]loves. At least, I suppose so. And what man ever was able to give a

[026-069]more touching proof of his affection than you can do now? If I were

[026-070]you, I'd be at Allington before twelve o'clock to-morrow,--I would

[026-071]indeed. What does it matter about the trumpery cheque? Everybody

[026-072]knows it was a mistake, if he did take it. And surely you would not

[026-073]punish her for that."

[026-074]"No,--no; but I don't suppose she'd think it a punishment."

[026-075]"You go and ask her, then. And I'll tell you what. If she hasn't

[026-076]a house of her own to be married from, she shall be married from

[026-077]Chaldicotes. We'll have such a breakfast! And I'll make as much

[026-078]of her as if she were the daughter of my old friend, the bishop

[026-079]himself,--I will indeed."

[026-080]This was Mrs. Thorne's advice. Before it was completed, Major Grantly

[026-081]had been carried half-way to Chaldicotes. When he left his impetuous

[026-082]friend he was too prudent to make any promise, but he declared that

[026-083]what she had said should have much weight with him.

[026-084]"You won't mention it to anybody?" said the major.

[026-085]"Certainly not, without your leave," said Mrs. Thorne. "Don't you

[026-086]know that I'm the soul of honour?"

[026-087]CHAPTER XV.

[026-088]UP IN LONDON.

[026-089][Illustration]

[026-090]Some kind and attentive reader may perhaps remember that Miss

[026-091]Grace Crawley, in a letter written by her to her friend Miss Lily

[026-092]Dale, said a word or two of a certain John. "If it can only be as

[026-093]John wishes it!" And the same reader, if there be one so kind and

[026-094]attentive, may also remember that Miss Lily Dale had declared,

[026-095]in reply, that "about that other subject she would rather say

[026-096]nothing,"--and then she had added, "When one thinks of going

[026-097]beyond friendship,--even if one tries to do so,--there are so many

[026-098]barriers!" From which words the kind and attentive reader, if such

[026-099]reader be in such matters intelligent as well as kind and attentive,

[026-100]may have learned a great deal with reference to Miss Lily Dale.

[026-101]We will now pay a visit to the John in question,--a certain Mr. John

[026-102]Eames, living in London, a bachelor, as the intelligent reader will

[026-103]certainly have discovered, and cousin to Miss Grace Crawley. Mr. John

[026-104]Eames at the time of our story was a young man, some seven or eight

[026-105]and twenty years of age, living in London, where he was supposed by

[026-106]his friends in the country to have made his mark, and to be something

[026-107]a little out of the common way. But I do not know that he was very

[026-108]much out of the common way, except in the fact that he had had some

[026-109]few thousand pounds left him by an old nobleman, who had been in no

[026-110]way related to him, but who had regarded him with great affection,

[026-111]and who had died some two years since. Before this, John Eames had

[026-112]not been a very poor man, as he filled the comfortable official

[026-113]position of private secretary to the Chief Commissioner of the

[026-114]Income-tax Board, and drew a salary of three hundred and fifty pounds

[026-115]a year from the resources of his country; but when, in addition to

[026-116]this source of official wealth, he became known as the undoubted

[026-117]possessor of a hundred and twenty-eight shares in one of the most

[026-118]prosperous joint-stock banks in the metropolis, which property had

[026-119]been left to him free of legacy duty by the lamented nobleman above

[026-120]named, then Mr. John Eames rose very high indeed as a young man

[026-121]in the estimation of those who knew him, and was supposed to be

[026-122]something a good deal out of the common way. His mother, who lived

[026-123]in the country, was obedient to his slightest word, never venturing

[026-124]to impose upon him any sign of parental authority; and to his

[026-125]sister, Mary Eames, who lived with her mother, he was almost a god

[026-126]upon earth. To sisters who have nothing of their own,--not even

[026-127]some special god for their own individual worship,--generous,

[026-128]affectionate, unmarried brothers, with sufficient incomes, are gods

[026-129]upon earth.

[026-130]And even up in London Mr. John Eames was somebody. He was so

[026-131]especially at his office; although, indeed, it was remembered by many

[026-132]a man how raw a lad he had been when he first came there, not so

[026-133]very many years ago; and how they had laughed at him and played

[026-134]him tricks; and how he had customarily been known to be without a

[026-135]shilling for the last week before pay-day, during which period he

[026-136]would borrow sixpence here and a shilling there with great energy,

[026-137]from men who now felt themselves to be honoured when he smiled upon

[026-138]them. Little stories of his former days would often be told of him

[026-139]behind his back; but they were not told with ill-nature, because

[026-140]he was very constant in referring to the same matters himself.

[026-141]And it was acknowledged by every one at the office, that neither

[026-142]the friendship of the nobleman, nor the fact of the private

[026-143]secretaryship, nor the acquisition of his wealth, had made him proud

[026-144]to his old companions or forgetful of old friendships. To the young

[026-145]men, lads who had lately been appointed, he was perhaps a little

[026-146]cold; but then it was only reasonable to conceive that such a one

[026-147]as Mr. John Eames was now could not be expected to make an intimate

[026-148]acquaintance with every new clerk that might be brought into the

[026-149]office. Since competitive examinations had come into vogue, there was

[026-150]no knowing who might be introduced; and it was understood generally

[026-151]through the establishment,--and I may almost say by the civil service

[026-152]at large, so wide was his fame,--that Mr. Eames was very averse

[026-153]to the whole theory of competition. The "Devil take the hindmost"

[026-154]scheme, he called it; and would then go on to explain that hindmost

[026-155]candidates were often the best gentlemen, and that, in this way, the

[026-156]Devil got the pick of the flock. And he was respected the more for

[026-157]this opinion, because it was known that on this subject he had fought

[026-158]some hard battles with the chief commissioner. The chief commissioner

[026-159]was a great believer in competition, wrote papers about it, which he

[026-160]read aloud to various bodies of the civil service,--not at all to

[026-161]their delight,--which he got to be printed here and there, and which

[026-162]he sent by post all over the kingdom. More than once this chief

[026-163]commissioner had told his private secretary that they must part

[026-164]company, unless the private secretary could see fit to alter his

[026-165]view, or could, at least, keep his views to himself. But the private

[026-166]secretary would do neither; and, nevertheless, there he was, still

[026-167]private secretary. "It's because Johnny has got money," said one of

[026-168]the young clerks, who was discussing this singular state of things

[026-169]with his brethren at the office. "When a chap has got money, he may

[026-170]do what he likes. Johnny has got lots of money, you know." The young

[026-171]clerk in question was by no means on intimate terms with Mr. Eames,

[026-172]but there had grown up in the office a way of calling him Johnny

[026-173]behind his back, which had probably come down from the early days of

[026-174]his scrapes and his poverty.

[026-175]Now the entire life of Mr. John Eames was pervaded by a great secret;

[026-176]and although he never, in those days, alluded to the subject in

[026-177]conversation with any man belonging to the office, yet the secret was

[026-178]known to them all. It had been historical for the last four or five

[026-179]years, and was now regarded as a thing of course. Mr. John Eames was

[026-180]in love, and his love was not happy. He was in love, and had long

[026-181]been in love, and the lady of his love was not kind to him. The

[026-182]little history had grown to be very touching and pathetic, having

[026-183]received, no doubt, some embellishments from the imaginations of the

[026-184]gentlemen of the Income-tax Office. It was said of him that he had

[026-185]been in love from his early boyhood, that at sixteen he had been

[026-186]engaged, under the sanction of the nobleman now deceased and of the

[026-187]young lady's parents, that contracts of betrothals had been drawn up,

[026-188]and things done very unusual in private families in these days, and

[026-189]that then there had come a stranger into the neighbourhood just as

[026-190]the young lady was beginning to reflect whether she had a heart of

[026-191]her own or not, and that she had thrown her parents, and the noble

[026-192]lord, and the contract, and poor Johnny Eames to the winds, and had--

[026-193]Here the story took different directions, as told by different men.

[026-194]Some said the lady had gone off with the stranger, and that there had

[026-195]been a clandestine marriage, which afterwards turned out to be no

[026-196]marriage at all; others, that the stranger suddenly took himself off,

[026-197]and was no more seen by the young lady; others that he owned at last

[026-198]to having another wife,--and so on. The stranger was very well known

[026-199]to be one Mr. Crosbie, belonging to another public office; and there

[026-200]were circumstances in his life, only half known, which gave rise to

[026-201]these various rumours. But there was one thing certain, one point

[026-202]as to which no clerk in the Income-tax Office had a doubt, one fact

[026-203]which had conduced much to the high position which Mr. John Eames now

[026-204]held in the estimation of his brother clerks,--he had given this Mr.

[026-205]Crosbie such a thrashing that no man had ever received such treatment

[026-206]before and had lived through it. Wonderful stories were told

[026-207]about that thrashing, so that it was believed, even by the least

[026-208]enthusiastic in such matters, that the poor victim had only dragged

[026-209]on a crippled existence since the encounter. "For nine weeks he never

[026-210]said a word or eat a mouthful," said one young clerk to a younger

[026-211]clerk who was just entering the office; "and even now he can't speak

[026-212]above a whisper, and has to take all his food in pap." It will be

[026-213]seen, therefore, that Mr. John Eames had about him much of the

[026-214]heroic.
