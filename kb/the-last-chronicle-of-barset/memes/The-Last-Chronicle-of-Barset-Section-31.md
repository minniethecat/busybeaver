[031-103]CHAPTER XVII.

[031-104]MR. CRAWLEY IS SUMMONED TO BARCHESTER.

[031-105][Illustration]

[031-106]The scene which occurred in Hogglestock church on the Sunday after

[031-107]Mr. Thumble's first visit to that parish had not been described with

[031-108]absolute accuracy either by the archdeacon in his letter to his son,

[031-109]or by Mrs. Thorne. There had been no footman from the palace in

[031-110]attendance on Mr. Thumble, nor had there been a battle with the

[031-111]brickmakers; neither had Mr. Thumble been put under the pump. But Mr.

[031-112]Thumble had gone over, taking his gown and surplice with him, on the

[031-113]Sunday morning, and had intimated to Mr. Crawley his intention of

[031-114]performing the service. Mr. Crawley, in answer to this, had assured

[031-115]Mr. Thumble that he would not be allowed to open his mouth in the

[031-116]church; and Mr. Thumble, not seeing his way to any further successful

[031-117]action, had contented himself with attending the services in his

[031-118]surplice, making thereby a silent protest that he, and not Mr.

[031-119]Crawley, ought to have been in the reading-desk and the pulpit.

[031-120]When Mr. Thumble reported himself and his failure at the palace,

[031-121]he strove hard to avoid seeing Mrs. Proudie, but not successfully.

[031-122]He knew something of the palace habits, and did manage to reach

[031-123]the bishop alone on the Sunday evening, justifying himself to his

[031-124]lordship for such an interview by the remarkable circumstances of

[031-125]the case and the importance of his late mission. Mrs. Proudie always

[031-126]went to church on Sunday evenings, making a point of hearing three

[031-127]services and three sermons every Sunday of her life. On week-days

[031-128]she seldom heard any, having an idea that week-day services were an

[031-129]invention of the High Church enemy, and that they should therefore be

[031-130]vehemently discouraged. Services on saints' days she regarded as rank

[031-131]papacy, and had been known to accuse a clergyman's wife, to her face,

[031-132]of idolatry, because the poor lady had dated a letter, St. John's

[031-133]Eve. Mr. Thumble, on this Sunday evening, was successful in finding

[031-134]the bishop at home, and alone, but he was not lucky enough to get

[031-135]away before Mrs. Proudie returned. The bishop, perhaps, thought that

[031-136]the story of the failure had better reach his wife's ears from Mr.

[031-137]Thumble's lips than from his own.

[031-138]"Well, Mr. Thumble?" said Mrs. Proudie, walking into the study, armed

[031-139]in her full Sunday-evening winter panoply, in which she had just

[031-140]descended from her carriage. The church which Mrs. Proudie attended

[031-141]in the evening was nearly half a mile from the palace, and the

[031-142]coachman and groom never got a holiday on Sunday night. She was

[031-143]gorgeous in a dark brown silk dress of awful stiffness and terrible

[031-144]dimensions; and on her shoulders she wore a short cloak of velvet and

[031-145]fur, very handsome withal, but so swelling in its proportions on all

[031-146]sides as necessarily to create more of dismay than of admiration in

[031-147]the mind of any ordinary man. And her bonnet was a monstrous helmet

[031-148]with the beaver up, displaying the awful face of the warrior, always

[031-149]ready for combat, and careless to guard itself from attack. The large

[031-150]contorted bows which she bore were as a grisly crest upon her casque,

[031-151]beautiful, doubtless, but majestic and fear-compelling. In her hand

[031-152]she carried her armour all complete, a prayer-book, a bible, and a

[031-153]book of hymns. These the footman had brought for her to the study

[031-154]door, but she had thought fit to enter her husband's room with them

[031-155]in her own custody.

[031-156]"Well, Mr. Thumble!" she said.

[031-157]Mr. Thumble did not answer at once, thinking, probably, that the

[031-158]bishop might choose to explain the circumstances. But, neither did

[031-159]the bishop say any thing.

[031-160]"Well, Mr. Thumble?" she said again; and then she stood looking at

[031-161]the man who had failed so disastrously.

[031-162]"I have explained to the bishop," said he. "Mr. Crawley has been

[031-163]contumacious,--very contumacious indeed."

[031-164]"But you preached at Hogglestock?"

[031-165]"No, indeed, Mrs. Proudie. Nor would it have been possible, unless

[031-166]I had had the police to assist me."

[031-167]"Then you should have had the police. I never heard of anything so

[031-168]mismanaged in all my life,--never in all my life." And she put her

[031-169]books down on the study table, and turned herself round from Mr.

[031-170]Thumble towards the bishop. "If things go on like this, my lord," she

[031-171]said, "your authority in the diocese will very soon be worth nothing

[031-172]at all." It was not often that Mrs. Proudie called her husband my

[031-173]lord, but when she did do so, it was a sign that terrible times had

[031-174]come;--times so terrible that the bishop would know that he must

[031-175]either fight or fly. He would almost endure anything rather than

[031-176]descend into the arena for the purpose of doing battle with his wife,

[031-177]but occasions would come now and again when even the alternative of

[031-178]flight was hardly left to him.

[031-179]"But, my dear,--" began the bishop.

[031-180]"Am I to understand that this man has professed himself to be

[031-181]altogether indifferent to the bishop's prohibition?" said Mrs.

[031-182]Proudie, interrupting her husband and addressing Mr. Thumble.

[031-183]"Quite so. He seemed to think that the bishop had no lawful power in

[031-184]the matter at all," said Mr. Thumble.

[031-185]"Do you hear that, my lord?" said Mrs. Proudie.

[031-186]"Nor have I any," said the bishop, almost weeping as he spoke.
