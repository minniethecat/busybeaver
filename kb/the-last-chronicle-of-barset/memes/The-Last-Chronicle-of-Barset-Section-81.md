[081-008]CHAPTER XLIII.

[081-009]MR. CROSBIE GOES INTO THE CITY.

[081-010]"I've known the City now for more than ten years, Mr. Crosbie, and

[081-011]I never knew money to be so tight as it is at this moment. The best

[081-012]commercial bills going can't be done under nine, and any other

[081-013]kind of paper can't so much as get itself looked at." Thus spoke

[081-014]Mr. Musselboro. He was seated in Dobbs Broughton's arm-chair in

[081-015]Dobbs Broughton's room in Hook Court, on the hind legs of which he

[081-016]was balancing himself comfortably; and he was communicating his

[081-017]experience in City matters to our old friend, Adolphus Crosbie,--of

[081-018]whom we may surmise that he would not have been there, at that

[081-019]moment, in Hook Court, if things had been going well with him. It was

[081-020]now past eleven o'clock, and he should have been at his office at

[081-021]the West End. His position in his office was no doubt high enough

[081-022]to place him beyond the reach of any special inquiry as to such

[081-023]absences; but it is generally felt that when the Crosbies of the West

[081-024]End have calls into the City about noon, things in the world are not

[081-025]going well with them. The man who goes into the City to look for

[081-026]money is generally one who does not know where to get money when he

[081-027]wants it. Mr. Musselboro on this occasion kept his hat on his head,

[081-028]and there was something in the way in which he balanced his chair

[081-029]which was in itself an offence to Mr. Crosbie's personal dignity. It

[081-030]was hardly as yet two months since Mr. Dobbs Broughton had assured

[081-031]him in that very room that there need not be the slightest anxiety

[081-032]about his bill. Of course it could be renewed,--the commission being

[081-033]duly paid. As Mr. Dobbs Broughton explained on that occasion, that

[081-034]was his business. There was nothing he liked so much as renewing

[081-035]bills for such customers as Mr. Crosbie; and he was very candid at

[081-036]that meeting, explaining how he did this branch of his business,

[081-037]raising money on his own credit at four or five per cent., and

[081-038]lending it on his own judgment at eight or nine. Mr. Crosbie did not

[081-039]feel himself then called upon to exclaim that what he was called upon

[081-040]to pay was about twelve, perfectly understanding the comfort and

[081-041]grace of euphony; but he had turned it over in his mind, considering

[081-042]whether twelve per cent. was not more than he ought to be mulcted

[081-043]for the accommodation he wanted. Now, at the moment, he would have

[081-044]been glad to get it from Mr. Musselboro, without further words, for

[081-045]twenty.

[081-046]Things had much changed with Adolphus Crosbie when he was driven to

[081-047]make morning visits to such a one as Mr. Musselboro with the view of

[081-048]having a bill renewed for two hundred and fifty pounds. In his early

[081-049]life he had always had the merit of being a careful man as to money.

[081-050]In some other respects he had gone astray very foolishly,--as has

[081-051]been partly explained in our earlier chapters; but up to the date

[081-052]of his marriage with Lady Alexandrina De Courcy he had never had

[081-053]dealings in Hook Court or in any such locality. Money troubles

[081-054]had then come upon him. Lady Alexandrina, being the daughter of a

[081-055]countess, had high ideas; and when, very shortly after his marriage,

[081-056]he had submitted to a separation from his noble wife, he had found

[081-057]himself and his income to be tied up inextricably in the hands of

[081-058]one Mr. Mortimer Gazebee, a lawyer who had married one of his wife's

[081-059]sisters. It was not that Mr. Gazebee was dishonest; nor did Crosbie

[081-060]suspect him of dishonesty; but the lawyer was so wedded to the

[081-061]interest of the noble family with which he was connected, that he

[081-062]worked for them all as an inferior spider might be supposed to

[081-063]work, which, from the infirmity of its nature, was compelled by

[081-064]its instincts to be catching flies always for superior spiders. Mr.

[081-065]Mortimer Gazebee had in this way entangled Mr. Crosbie in his web on

[081-066]behalf of those noble spiders, the De Courcys, and our poor friend,

[081-067]in his endeavour to fight his way through the web, had fallen into

[081-068]the hands of the Hook Court firm of Mrs. Van Siever, Dobbs Broughton,

[081-069]and Musselboro.

[081-070]"Mr. Broughton told me when I was last here," said Crosbie, "that

[081-071]there would be no difficulty about it."

[081-072]"And it was renewed then; wasn't it?"

[081-073]"Of course it was,--for two months. But he was speaking of a

[081-074]continuation of renewal."

[081-075]"I'm afraid we can't do it, Mr. Crosbie. I'm afraid we can't, indeed.

[081-076]Money is so awful tight."

[081-077]"Of course I must pay what you choose to charge me."

[081-078]"It isn't that, Mr. Crosbie. The bill is out for collection, and must

[081-079]be collected. In times like these we must draw ourselves in a little,

[081-080]you know. Two hundred and fifty pounds isn't a great deal of money,

[081-081]you will say; but every little helps, you know; and, besides, of

[081-082]course we go upon a system. Business is business, and must not be

[081-083]made pleasure of. I should have had a great deal of pleasure in doing

[081-084]this for you, but it can't be done in the way of business."

[081-085]"When will Broughton be here?"

[081-086]"He may be in at any time;--I can't say when. I suppose he's down at

[081-087]the court now."

[081-088]"What court?"

[081-089]"Capel Court."

[081-090]"I suppose I can see him there?" said Crosbie.

[081-091]"If you catch him you can see him, of course. But what good will

[081-092]that do you, Mr. Crosbie? I tell you that we can't do it for you.

[081-093]If Broughton was here this moment it couldn't make the slightest

[081-094]difference."

[081-095]Now Mr. Crosbie had an idea that Mr. Musselboro, though he sat in

[081-096]Dobbs Broughton's seat and kept on his hat, and balanced his chair on

[081-097]two legs, was in truth nothing more than a clerk. He did not quite

[081-098]understand the manner in which the affairs of the establishment were

[081-099]worked, though he had been informed that Mrs. Van Siever was one of

[081-100]the partners. That Dobbs Broughton was the managing man, who really

[081-101]did the business, he was convinced; and he did not therefore like to

[081-102]be answered peremptorily by such a one as Musselboro. "I should wish

[081-103]to see Mr. Broughton," he said.

[081-104]"You can call again,--or you can go down to the court if you like

[081-105]it. But you may take this as an answer from me that the bill can't

[081-106]be renewed by us." At this moment the door of the room was opened,

[081-107]and Dobbs Broughton himself came into it. His face was not at all

[081-108]pleasant, and any one might have seen with half an eye that the

[081-109]money-market was a great deal tighter than he liked it to be. "Here

[081-110]is Mr. Crosbie here,--about that bill," said Musselboro.

[081-111]"Mr. Crosbie must take up his bill; that's all," said Dobbs

[081-112]Broughton.

[081-113]"But it doesn't suit me to take it up," said Crosbie.

[081-114]"Then you must take it up without suiting you," said Dobbs Broughton.

[081-115]It might have been seen, I said, with half an eye, that Mr. Broughton

[081-116]did not like the state of the money-market; and it might also be

[081-117]seen with the other half that he had been endeavouring to mitigate

[081-118]the bitterness of his dislike by alcoholic aid. Musselboro at once

[081-119]perceived that his patron and partner was half drunk, and Crosbie

[081-120]was aware that he had been drinking. But, nevertheless, it was

[081-121]necessary that something more should be said. The bill would be due

[081-122]to-morrow,--was payable at Crosbie's bankers; and, as Mr. Crosbie too

[081-123]well knew, there were no funds there for the purpose. And there were

[081-124]other purposes, very needful, for which Mr. Crosbie's funds were at

[081-125]the present moment unfortunately by no means sufficient. He stood

[081-126]for a few moments thinking what he would do;--whether he would leave

[081-127]the drunken man and his office and let the bill take its chance, or

[081-128]whether he would make one more effort for an arrangement. He did

[081-129]not for a moment believe that Broughton himself was subject to any

[081-130]pecuniary difficulty. Broughton lived in a big house, as rich men

[081-131]live, and had a name for commercial success. It never occurred to

[081-132]Crosbie that it was a matter of great moment to Dobbs Broughton

[081-133]himself that the bill should be taken up. Crosbie still thought that

[081-134]Musselboro was his special enemy, and that Broughton had joined

[081-135]Musselboro in his hostility simply because he was too drunk to know

[081-136]better. "You might, at any rate, answer me civilly, Mr. Broughton,"

[081-137]he said.

[081-138]"I know nothing about civility with things as they are at present,"

[081-139]said Broughton. "Civil by ----! There's nothing so civil as paying

[081-140]money when you owe it. Musselboro, reach me down the decanter and

[081-141]some glasses. Perhaps Mr. Crosbie will wet his whistle."

[081-142]"He don't want any wine,--nor you either," said Musselboro.

[081-143]"What's up now?" said Broughton, staggering across the room towards

[081-144]a cupboard, in which it was his custom to keep a provision of that

[081-145]comfort which he needed at the present moment. "I suppose I may stand

[081-146]a glass of wine to a fellow in my own room, if I like it."

[081-147]"I will take no wine, thank you," said Crosbie.

[081-148]"Then you can do the other thing. When I ask a gentleman to take a

[081-149]glass of wine, there is no compulsion. But about the bill there is

[081-150]compulsion. Do you understand that? You may drink, or let it alone;

[081-151]but pay you must. Why, Mussy, what d'ye think?--there's Carter,

[081-152]Ricketts and Carter;--I'm blessed if Carter just now didn't beg for

[081-153]two months, as though two months would be all the world to him, and

[081-154]that for a trumpery five hundred pounds. I never saw money like it

[081-155]is now; never." To this appeal, Musselboro made no reply, not caring,

[081-156]perhaps, at the present moment to sustain his partner. He still

[081-157]balanced himself in his chair, and still kept his hat on his head.

[081-158]Even Mr. Crosbie began to perceive that Mr. Musselboro's genius was

[081-159]in the ascendant in Hook Court.

[081-160]"I can hardly believe," said Crosbie, "that things can be so bad that

[081-161]I cannot have a bill for two hundred and fifty pounds renewed when I

[081-162]am willing to pay for the accommodation. I have not done much in the

[081-163]way of bills, but I never had one dishonoured yet."

[081-164]"Don't let this be the first," said Dobbs Broughton.

[081-165]"Not if I can prevent it," said Crosbie. "But, to tell you the

[081-166]truth, Mr. Broughton, my bill will be dishonoured unless I can have

[081-167]it renewed. If it does not suit you to do it, I suppose you can

[081-168]recommend me to some one who can make it convenient."

[081-169]"Why don't you go to your bankers?" said Musselboro.

[081-170]"I never did ask my bankers for anything of the kind."

[081-171]"Then you should try what your credit with them is worth," said

[081-172]Broughton. "It isn't worth much here, as you can perceive. Ha, ha,

[081-173]ha!"

[081-174]Crosbie, when he heard this, became very angry; and Musselboro,

[081-175]perceiving this, got out of his chair, so that he might be in

[081-176]readiness to prevent any violence, if violence were attempted.

[081-177]"It really is no good your staying here," he said. "You see that

[081-178]Broughton has been drinking. There's no knowing what he may say or

[081-179]do."

[081-180]"You be blowed," said Broughton, who had taken the arm-chair as soon

[081-181]as Musselboro had left it.
