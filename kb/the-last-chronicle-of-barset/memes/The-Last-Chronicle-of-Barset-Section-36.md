[036-001]"And yet?"--said his wife, encouraging him to proceed. If she could

[036-002]only learn the real truth, she thought that she might perhaps yet

[036-003]save him, with assistance from their friends.

[036-004]"When I said that I had gotten it from that man I must have been

[036-005]mad."

[036-006]"From which man, love?"

[036-007]"From the man Soames,--he who accuses me. And yet, as the Lord hears

[036-008]me, I thought so then. The truth is, that there are times when I

[036-009]am not--sane. I am not a thief,--not before God; but I am--mad at

[036-010]times." These last words he spoke very slowly, in a whisper,--without

[036-011]any excitement,--indeed with a composure which was horrible to

[036-012]witness. And what he said was the more terrible because she was so

[036-013]well convinced of the truth of his words. Of course he was no thief.

[036-014]She wanted no one to tell her that. As he himself had expressed it,

[036-015]he was no thief before God, however the money might have come into

[036-016]his possession. That there were times when his reason, once so

[036-017]fine and clear, could not act, could not be trusted to guide him

[036-018]right, she had gradually come to know with fear and trembling. But

[036-019]he himself had never before hinted his own consciousness of this

[036-020]calamity. Indeed he had been so unwilling to speak of himself and of

[036-021]his own state, that she had been unable even to ask him a question

[036-022]about the money,--lest he should suspect that she suspected him. Now

[036-023]he was speaking,--but speaking with such heartrending sadness that

[036-024]she could hardly urge him to go on.

[036-025]"You have sometimes been ill, Josiah, as any of us may be," she said,

[036-026]"and that has been the cause."

[036-027]"There are different kinds of sickness. There is sickness of the

[036-028]body, and sickness of the heart, and sickness of the spirit;--and

[036-029]then there is sickness of the mind, the worst of all."

[036-030]"With you, Josiah, it has chiefly been the first."

[036-031]"With me, Mary, it has been all of them,--every one! My spirit is

[036-032]broken, and my mind has not been able to keep its even tenour amidst

[036-033]the ruins. But I will strive. I will strive. I will strive still. And

[036-034]if God helps me, I will prevail." Then he took up his hat and cloak,

[036-035]and went forth among the lanes; and on this occasion his wife was

[036-036]glad that he should go alone.

[036-037]This occurred a day or two before Christmas, and Mrs. Crawley during

[036-038]those days said nothing more to her husband on the subject which he

[036-039]had so unexpectedly discussed. She asked him no questions about the

[036-040]money, or as to the possibility of his exercising his memory, nor did

[036-041]she counsel him to plead that the false excuses given by him for his

[036-042]possession of the cheque had been occasioned by the sad slip to which

[036-043]sorrow had in those days subjected his memory and his intellect. But

[036-044]the matter had always been on her mind. Might it not be her paramount

[036-045]duty to do something of this at the present moment? Might it not be

[036-046]that his acquittal or conviction would depend on what she might now

[036-047]learn from him? It was clear to her that he was brighter in spirit

[036-048]since his encounter with the Proudies than he had ever been since the

[036-049]accusation had been first made against him. And she knew well that

[036-050]his present mood would not be of long continuance. He would fall

[036-051]again into his moody silent ways, and then the chance of learning

[036-052]aught from him would be past, and perhaps, for ever.

[036-053]He performed the Christmas services with nothing of special

[036-054]despondency in his tone or manner, and his wife thought that she

[036-055]had never heard him give the sacrament with more impressive dignity.

[036-056]After the service he stood awhile at the churchyard gate, and

[036-057]exchanged a word of courtesy as to the season with such of the

[036-058]families of the farmers as had stayed for the Lord's supper.

[036-059]"I waited at Framley for your reverence till arter six,--so I did,"

[036-060]said farmer Mangle.

[036-061]"I kept the road, and walked the whole way," said Mr. Crawley. "I

[036-062]think I told you that I should not return to the mill. But I am not

[036-063]the less obliged by your great kindness."

[036-064]"Say nowt o' that," said the farmer. "No doubt I had business at the

[036-065]mill,--lots to do at the mill." Nor did he think that the fib he was

[036-066]telling was at all incompatible with the Holy Sacrament in which he

[036-067]had just taken a part.

[036-068]The Christmas dinner at the parsonage was not a repast that did much

[036-069]honour to the season, but it was a better dinner than the inhabitants

[036-070]of that house usually saw on the board before them. There was roast

[036-071]pork and mince-pies, and a bottle of wine. As Mrs. Crawley with her

[036-072]own hand put the meat upon the table, and then, as was her custom in

[036-073]their house, proceeded to cut it up, she looked at her husband's face

[036-074]to see whether he was scrutinizing the food with painful eye. It was

[036-075]better that she should tell the truth at once than that she should

[036-076]be made to tell it, in answer to a question. Everything on the table,

[036-077]except the bread and potatoes, had come in a basket from Framley

[036-078]Court. Pork had been sent instead of beef, because people in the

[036-079]country, when they kill their pigs, do sometimes give each other

[036-080]pork,--but do not exchange joints of beef, when they slay their oxen.

[036-081]All this was understood by Mrs. Crawley, but she almost wished that

[036-082]beef had been sent, because beef would have attracted less attention.

[036-083]He said, however, nothing to the meat; but when his wife proposed to

[036-084]him that he should eat a mince-pie he resented it. "The bare food,"

[036-085]said he, "is bitter enough, coming as it does; but that would choke

[036-086]me." She did not press it, but eat one herself, as otherwise her girl

[036-087]would have been forced also to refuse the dainty.

[036-088]That evening, as soon as Jane was in bed, she resolved to ask him

[036-089]some further questions. "You will have a lawyer, Josiah,--will you

[036-090]not?" she said.

[036-091]"Why should I have a lawyer?"

[036-092]"Because he will know what questions to ask, and how questions on the

[036-093]other side should be answered."

[036-094]"I have no questions to ask, and there is only one way in which

[036-095]questions should be answered. I have no money to pay a lawyer."

[036-096]"But, Josiah, in such a case as this, where your honour, and our very

[036-097]life depend upon it--"

[036-098]"Depend on what?"

[036-099]"On your acquittal."

[036-100]"I shall not be acquitted. It is as well to look it in the face at

[036-101]once. Lawyer, or no lawyer, they will say that I took the money. Were

[036-102]I upon the jury, trying the case myself, knowing all that I know

[036-103]now,"--and as he said this he struck forth with his hands into the

[036-104]air,--"I think that I should say so myself. A lawyer will do no good.

[036-105]It is here. It is here." And again he put his hands up to his head.

[036-106]So far she had been successful. At this moment it had in truth been

[036-107]her object to induce him to speak of his own memory, and not of the

[036-108]aid that a lawyer might give. The proposition of the lawyer had been

[036-109]brought in to introduce the subject.

[036-110]"But, Josiah,--"

[036-111]"Well?"

[036-112]It was very hard for her to speak. She could not bear to torment him

[036-113]by any allusion to his own deficiencies. She could not endure to make

[036-114]him think that she suspected him of any frailty either in intellect

[036-115]or thought. Wifelike, she desired to worship him, and that he should

[036-116]know that she worshipped him. But if a word might save him! "Josiah,

[036-117]where did it come from?"

[036-118]"Yes," said he; "yes; that is the question. Where did it come

[036-119]from?"--and he turned sharp upon her, looking at her with all the

[036-120]power of his eyes. "It is because I cannot tell you where it came

[036-121]from that I ought to be,--either in Bedlam, as a madman, or in the

[036-122]county gaol as a thief." The words were so dreadful to her that she

[036-123]could not utter at the moment another syllable. "How is a man--to

[036-124]think himself--fit--for a man's work, when he cannot answer his wife

[036-125]such a plain question as that?" Then he paused again. "They should

[036-126]take me to Bedlam at once,--at once,--at once. That would not

[036-127]disgrace the children as the gaol will do."

[036-128]Mrs. Crawley could ask no further questions on that evening.

[036-129]CHAPTER XX.

[036-130]WHAT MR. WALKER THOUGHT ABOUT IT.

[036-131][Illustration]

[036-132]It had been suggested to Mr. Robarts, the parson of Framley, that

[036-133]he should endeavour to induce his old acquaintance, Mr. Crawley, to

[036-134]employ a lawyer to defend him at his trial, and Mr. Robarts had not

[036-135]forgotten the commission which he had undertaken. But there were

[036-136]difficulties in the matter of which he was well aware. In the first

[036-137]place Mr. Crawley was a man whom it had not at any time been easy

[036-138]to advise on matters private to himself; and, in the next place,

[036-139]this was a matter on which it was very hard to speak to the man

[036-140]implicated, let him be who he would. Mr. Robarts had come round to

[036-141]the generally accepted idea that Mr. Crawley had obtained possession

[036-142]of the cheque illegally,--acquitting his friend in his own mind

[036-143]of theft, simply by supposing that he was wool-gathering when the

[036-144]cheque came in his way. But in speaking to Mr. Crawley, it would be

[036-145]necessary,--so he thought,--to pretend a conviction that Mr. Crawley

[036-146]was as innocent in fact as in intention.

[036-147]He had almost made up his mind to dash at the subject when he met Mr.

[036-148]Crawley walking through Framley to Barchester, but he had abstained,

[036-149]chiefly because Mr. Crawley had been too quick for him, and had got

[036-150]away. After that he resolved that it would be almost useless for

[036-151]him to go to work unless he should be provided with a lawyer ready

[036-152]and willing to undertake the task; and as he was not so provided

[036-153]at present, he made up his mind that he would go into Silverbridge,

[036-154]and see Mr. Walker, the attorney there. Mr. Walker always advised

[036-155]everybody in those parts about everything, and would be sure to

[036-156]know what would be the proper thing to be done in this case. So

[036-157]Mr. Robarts got into his gig, and drove himself into Silverbridge,

[036-158]passing very close to Mr. Crawley's house on his road. He drove at

[036-159]once to Mr. Walker's office, and on arriving there found that the

[036-160]attorney was not at that moment within. But Mr. Winthrop was within.

[036-161]Would Mr. Robarts see Mr. Winthrop? Now, seeing Mr. Winthrop was

[036-162]a very different thing from seeing Mr. Walker, although the two

[036-163]gentlemen were partners. But still Mr. Robarts said that he would see

[036-164]Mr. Winthrop. Perhaps Mr. Walker might return while he was there.

[036-165]"Is there anything I can do for you, Mr. Robarts?" asked Mr.

[036-166]Winthrop. Mr. Robarts said that he had wished to see Mr. Walker

[036-167]about that poor fellow Crawley. "Ah, yes; very sad case! So much

[036-168]sadder being a clergyman, Mr. Robarts. We are really quite sorry for

[036-169]him;--we are indeed. We wouldn't have touched the case ourselves

[036-170]if we could have helped ourselves. We wouldn't indeed. But we are

[036-171]obliged to take all that business here. At any rate he'll get nothing

[036-172]but fair usage from us."

[036-173]"I am sure of that. You don't know whether he has employed any lawyer

[036-174]as yet to defend him?"

[036-175]"I can't say. We don't know, you know. I should say he had,--probably

[036-176]some Barchester attorney. Borleys and Bonstock in Barchester are very

[036-177]good people,--very good people indeed;--for that sort of business I

[036-178]mean, Mr. Robarts. I don't suppose they have much county property in

[036-179]their hands."
