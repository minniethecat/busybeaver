[018-001]"I'm afraid I shall be a great trouble to Mrs. Dale."

[018-002]"A trouble to mamma! Indeed you will not. You shall be a trouble to

[018-003]no one but me. I will have all the trouble myself, and the labour I

[018-004]delight in shall physic my pain."

[018-005]Grace Crawley could not during the journey be at home and at ease

[018-006]even with her friend Lily. She was going to a strange house under

[018-007]strange circumstances. Her father had not indeed been tried and found

[018-008]guilty of theft, but the charge of theft had been made against him,

[018-009]and the magistrates before whom it had been made had thought that the

[018-010]charge was true. Grace knew that all the local newspapers had told

[018-011]the story, and was of course aware that Mrs. Dale would have heard

[018-012]it. Her own mind was full of it, and though she dreaded to speak of

[018-013]it, yet she could not be silent. Miss Dale, who understood much of

[018-014]this, endeavoured to talk her friend into easiness; but she feared to

[018-015]begin upon the one subject, and before the drive was over they were,

[018-016]both of them, too cold for much conversation. "There's mamma," said

[018-017]Miss Dale as they drove up, turning out of the street of the village

[018-018]to the door of Mrs. Dale's house. "She always knows, by instinct,

[018-019]when I am coming. You must understand now that you are among us,

[018-020]that mamma and I are not mother and daughter, but two loving old

[018-021]ladies, living together in peace and harmony. We do have our

[018-022]quarrels,--whether the chicken shall be roast or boiled, but never

[018-023]anything beyond that. Mamma, here is Grace, starved to death; and she

[018-024]says if you don't give her some tea she will go back at once."

[018-025]"I will give her some tea," said Mrs. Dale.

[018-026]"And I am worse than she is, because I've been driving. It's all up

[018-027]with Bernard and Mr. Green for the next week at least. It is freezing

[018-028]as hard as it can freeze, and they might as well try to hunt in

[018-029]Lapland as here."

[018-030]"They'll console themselves with skating," said Mrs. Dale.

[018-031]"Have you ever observed, Grace," said Miss Dale, "how much amusement

[018-032]gentlemen require, and how imperative it is that some other game

[018-033]should be provided when one game fails?"

[018-034]"Not particularly," said Grace.

[018-035]"Oh, but it is so. Now, with women, it is supposed that they can

[018-036]amuse themselves or live without amusement. Once or twice in a year,

[018-037]perhaps something is done for them. There is an arrow-shooting

[018-038]party, or a ball, or a picnic. But the catering for men's sport is

[018-039]never-ending, and is always paramount to everything else. And yet the

[018-040]pet game of the day never goes off properly. In partridge time, the

[018-041]partridges are wild, and won't come to be killed. In hunting time

[018-042]the foxes won't run straight,--the wretches. They show no spirit,

[018-043]and will take to ground to save their brushes. Then comes a nipping

[018-044]frost, and skating is proclaimed; but the ice is always rough, and

[018-045]the woodcocks have deserted the country. And as for salmon,--when the

[018-046]summer comes round I do really believe that they suffer a great deal

[018-047]about the salmon. I'm sure they never catch any. So they go back to

[018-048]their clubs and their cards, and their billiards, and abuse their

[018-049]cooks and blackball their friends. That's about it, mamma; is it

[018-050]not?"

[018-051]"You know more about it than I do, my dear."

[018-052]"Because I have to listen to Bernard, as you never will do. We've got

[018-053]such a Mr. Green down here, Grace. He's such a duck of a man,--such

[018-054]top-boots and all the rest of it. And yet they whisper to me that

[018-055]he doesn't ride always to hounds. And to see him play billiards

[018-056]is beautiful, only he never can make a stroke. I hope you play

[018-057]billiards, Grace, because uncle Christopher has just had a new table

[018-058]put up."

[018-059]"I never saw a billiard-table yet," said Grace.

[018-060]"Then Mr. Green shall teach you. He'll do anything that you ask him.

[018-061]If you don't approve the colour of the ball, he'll go to London to

[018-062]get you another one. Only you must be very careful about saying that

[018-063]you like anything before him, as he'll be sure to have it for you

[018-064]the next day. Mamma happened to say that she wanted a four-penny

[018-065]postage-stamp, and he walked off to Guestwick to get it for her

[018-066]instantly, although it was lunch-time."

[018-067]"He did nothing of the kind, Lily," said her mother. "He was going

[018-068]to Guestwick, and was very good-natured, and brought me back a

[018-069]postage-stamp that I wanted."

[018-070]"Of course he's good-natured, I know that. And there's my cousin

[018-071]Bernard. He's Captain Dale, you know. But he prefers to be called Mr.

[018-072]Dale, because he has left the army, and has set up as junior squire

[018-073]of the parish. Uncle Christopher is the real squire; only Bernard

[018-074]does all the work. And now you know all about us. I'm afraid you'll

[018-075]find us dull enough,--unless you can take a fancy to Mr. Green."

[018-076]"Does Mr. Green live here?" asked Grace.

[018-077]"No; he does not live here. I never heard of his living anywhere. He

[018-078]was something once, but I don't know what; and I don't think he's

[018-079]anything now in particular. But he's Bernard's friend, and like most

[018-080]men, as one sees them, he never has much to do. Does Major Grantly

[018-081]ever go forth to fight his country's battles?" This last question she

[018-082]asked in a low whisper, so that the words did not reach her mother.

[018-083]Grace blushed up to her eyes, however, as she answered,--

[018-084]"I think that Major Grantly has left the army."

[018-085]"We shall get her round in a day or two, mamma," said Lily Dale to

[018-086]her mother that night. "I'm sure it will be the best thing to force

[018-087]her to talk of her troubles."

[018-088]"I would not use too much force, my dear."

[018-089]"Things are better when they're talked about. I'm sure they are. And

[018-090]it will be good to make her accustomed to speak of Major Grantly.

[018-091]From what Mary Walker tells me, he certainly means it. And if so, she

[018-092]should be ready for it when it comes."

[018-093]"Do not make her ready for what may never come."

[018-094]"No, mamma; but she is at present such a child that she knows nothing

[018-095]of her own powers. She should be made to understand that it is

[018-096]possible that even a Major Grantly may think himself fortunate in

[018-097]being allowed to love her."

[018-098]"I should leave all that to Nature, if I were you," said Mrs. Dale.

[018-099]CHAPTER X.

[018-100]DINNER AT FRAMLEY COURT.

[018-101]Lord Lufton, as he drove home to Framley after the meeting of

[018-102]the magistrates at Silverbridge, discussed the matter with his

[018-103]brother-in-law, Mark Robarts, the clergyman. Lord Lufton was driving

[018-104]a dog-cart, and went along the road at the rate of twelve miles

[018-105]an hour. "I'll tell you what it is, Mark," he said, "that man is

[018-106]innocent; but if he won't employ lawyers at his trial, the jury will

[018-107]find him guilty."

[018-108]"I don't know what to think about it," said the clergyman.

[018-109]"Were you in the room when he protested so vehemently that he didn't

[018-110]know where he got the money?"

[018-111]"I was in the room all the time."

[018-112]"And did you not believe him when he said that?"

[018-113]"Yes,--I think I did."

[018-114]"Anybody must have believed him,--except old Tempest, who never

[018-115]believes anybody, and Fothergill, who always suspects everybody. The

[018-116]truth is, that he had found the cheque and put it by, and did not

[018-117]remember anything about it."

[018-118]"But, Lufton, surely that would amount to stealing it."

[018-119]"Yes, if it wasn't that he is such a poor, cracked, crazy creature,

[018-120]with his mind all abroad. I think Soames did drop his book in

[018-121]his house. I'm sure Soames would not say so unless he was quite

[018-122]confident. Somebody has picked it up, and in some way the cheque has

[018-123]got into Crawley's hand. Then he has locked it up and has forgotten

[018-124]all about it; and when that butcher threatened him, he has put his

[018-125]hand upon it, and he has thought, or believed, that it had come from

[018-126]Soames or from the dean, or from heaven, if you will. When a man is

[018-127]so crazy as that, you can't judge of him as you do of others."

[018-128]"But a jury must judge of him as it would of others."

[018-129]"And therefore there should be a lawyer to tell the jury what to do.

[018-130]They should have somebody up out of the parish to show that he is

[018-131]beside himself half his time. His wife would be the best person, only

[018-132]it would be hard lines on her."

[018-133]"Very hard. And after all he would only escape by being shown to be

[018-134]mad."

[018-135]"And he is mad."

[018-136]"Mrs. Proudie would come upon him in such a case as that, and

[018-137]sequester his living."

[018-138]"And what will Mrs. Proudie do when he's a convicted thief? Simply

[018-139]unfrock him, and take away his living altogether. Nothing on earth

[018-140]should induce me to find him guilty if I were on a jury."

[018-141]"But you have committed him."

[018-142]"Yes,--I've been one, at least, in doing so. I simply did that which

[018-143]Walker told us we must do. A magistrate is not left to himself as a

[018-144]juryman is. I'd eat the biggest pair of boots in Barchester before I

[018-145]found him guilty. I say, Mark, you must talk it over with the women,

[018-146]and see what can be done for them. Lucy tells me that they're so

[018-147]poor, that if they have bread to eat, it's as much as they have."

[018-148]On this evening Archdeacon Grantly and his wife dined and slept at

[018-149]Framley Court, there having been a very long family friendship

[018-150]between old Lady Lufton and the Grantlys, and Dr. Thorne with his

[018-151]wife, from Chaldicotes, also dined at Framley. There was also there

[018-152]another clergyman from Barchester, Mr. Champion, one of the prebends

[018-153]of the cathedral. There were only three now who had houses in the

[018-154]city since the retrenchments of the ecclesiastical commission had

[018-155]come into full force. And this Mr. Champion was dear to the Dowager

[018-156]Lady Lufton, because he carried on worthily the clerical war against

[018-157]the bishop which had raged in Barsetshire ever since Dr. Proudie had

[018-158]come there,--which war old Lady Lufton, good and pious and charitable

[018-159]as she was, considered that she was bound to keep up, even to the

[018-160]knife, till Dr. Proudie and all his satellites should have been

[018-161]banished into outer darkness. As the light of the Proudies still

[018-162]shone brightly, it was probable that poor old Lady Lufton might die

[018-163]before her battle was accomplished. She often said that it would be

[018-164]so, but when so saying, always expressed a wish that the fight might

[018-165]be carried on after her death. "I shall never, never rest in my

[018-166]grave," she had once said to the archdeacon, "while that woman sits

[018-167]in your father's palace." For the archdeacon's father had been Bishop

[018-168]of Barchester before Dr. Proudie. What mode of getting rid of the

[018-169]bishop or his wife Lady Lufton proposed to herself, I am unable to

[018-170]say; but I think she lived in hopes that in some way it might be

[018-171]done. If only the bishop could have been found to have stolen a

[018-172]cheque for twenty pounds instead of poor Mr. Crawley, Lady Lufton

[018-173]would, I think, have been satisfied.

[018-174]In the course of these battles Framley Court would sometimes assume a

[018-175]clerical aspect,--have a prevailing hue, as it were, of black coats,

[018-176]which was not altogether to the taste of Lord Lufton, and as to which

[018-177]he would make complaint to his wife, and to Mark Robarts, himself a

[018-178]clergyman. "There's more of this than I can stand," he'd say to the

[018-179]latter. "There's a deuced deal more of it than you like yourself, I

[018-180]know."
