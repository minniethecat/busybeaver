[069-001]CHAPTER XXXVII.

[069-002]HOOK COURT.

[069-003]Mr. Dobbs Broughton and Mr. Musselboro were sitting together on a

[069-004]certain morning at their office in the City, discussing the affairs

[069-005]of their joint business. The City office was a very poor place

[069-006]indeed, in comparison with the fine house which Mr. Dobbs occupied at

[069-007]the West End; but then City offices are poor places, and there are

[069-008]certain City occupations which seem to enjoy the greater credit the

[069-009]poorer are the material circumstances by which they are surrounded.

[069-010]Turning out of a lane which turns out of Lombard Street, there is a

[069-011]desolate, forlorn-looking, dark alley, which is called Hook Court.

[069-012]The entrance to this alley is beneath the first-floor of one of the

[069-013]houses in the lane, and in passing under this covered way the visitor

[069-014]to the place finds himself in a small paved square court, at the two

[069-015]further corners of which there are two open doors; for in Hook Court

[069-016]there are only two houses. There is No. 1, Hook Court, and No. 2,

[069-017]Hook Court. The entire premises indicated by No. 1, are occupied by a

[069-018]firm of wine and spirit merchants, in connexion with whose trade one

[069-019]side and two angles of the court are always lumbered with crates,

[069-020]hampers, and wooden cases. And nearly in the middle of the court,

[069-021]though somewhat more to the wine-merchants' side than to the other,

[069-022]there is always gaping open a trap-door, leading down to vaults

[069-023]below; and over the trap there is a great board with a bright

[069-024]advertisement in very large letters:--

[069-025]BURTON AND BANGLES.

[069-026]HIMALAYA WINES,

[069-027]22_s._ 6_d._ per dozen.

[069-028]And this notice is so bright and so large, and the trap-door is

[069-029]so conspicuous in the court, that no visitor, even to No. 2, ever

[069-030]afterwards can quite divest his memory of those names, Burton and

[069-031]Bangles, Himalaya wines. It may therefore be acknowledged that Burton

[069-032]and Bangles have achieved their object in putting up the notice.

[069-033]The house No. 2, small as it seems to be, standing in the jamb of a

[069-034]corner, is divided among different occupiers, whose names are painted

[069-035]in small letters upon the very dirty posts of the doorway. Nothing

[069-036]can be more remarkable than the contrast between Burton and Bangles

[069-037]and these other City gentlemen in the method taken by them in

[069-038]declaring their presence to visitors in the court. The names of Dobbs

[069-039]Broughton and of A. Musselboro,--the Christian name of Mr. Musselboro

[069-040]was Augustus,--were on one of those dirty posts, not joined together

[069-041]by any visible "and," so as to declare boldly that they were

[069-042]partners; but in close vicinity,--showing at least that the two

[069-043]gentlemen would be found in apartments very near to each other. And

[069-044]on the first-floor of this house Dobbs Broughton and his friend did

[069-045]occupy three rooms,--or rather two rooms and a closet--between them.

[069-046]The larger and front room was tenanted by an old clerk, who sat

[069-047]within a rail in one corner of it. And there was a broad, short

[069-048]counter which jutted out from the wall into the middle of the room,

[069-049]intended for the use of such of the public as might come to transact

[069-050]miscellaneous business with Dobbs Broughton or Augustus Musselboro.

[069-051]But any one accustomed to the look of offices might have seen

[069-052]with half an eye that very little business was ever done on that

[069-053]counter. Behind this large room was a smaller one, belonging to Dobbs

[069-054]Broughton, in the furnishing and arrangement of which some regard had

[069-055]been paid to comfort. The room was carpeted, and there was a sofa

[069-056]in it, though a very old one, and two arm-chairs and a mahogany

[069-057]office-table, and a cellaret, which was generally well supplied

[069-058]with wine which Dobbs Broughton did not get out of the vaults of

[069-059]his neighbours, Burton and Bangles. Behind this again, but with a

[069-060]separate entrance from the passage, was the closet; and this closet

[069-061]was specially devoted to the use of Mr. Musselboro. Closet as it

[069-062]was,--or cupboard as it might almost have been called,--it contained

[069-063]a table and two chairs; and it had a window of its own, which opened

[069-064]out upon a blank wall which was distant from it not above four feet.

[069-065]As the house to which this wall belonged was four stories high, it

[069-066]would sometimes happen that Mr. Musselboro's cupboard was rather

[069-067]dark. But this mattered the less as in these days Mr. Musselboro

[069-068]seldom used it. Mr. Musselboro, who was very constant at his place of

[069-069]business,--much more constant than his friend, Dobbs Broughton,--was

[069-070]generally to be found in his friend's room. Only on some special

[069-071]occasions, on which it was thought expedient that the commercial

[069-072]world should be made to understand that Mr. Augustus Musselboro had

[069-073]an individual existence of his own, did that gentleman really seat

[069-074]himself in the dark closet. Mr. Dobbs Broughton, had he been asked

[069-075]what was his trade, would have said that he was a stockbroker; and he

[069-076]would have answered truly, for he was a stockbroker. A man may be a

[069-077]stockbroker though he never sells any stock; as he may be a barrister

[069-078]though he has no practice at the bar. I do not say that Mr. Broughton

[069-079]never sold any stock; but the buying and selling of stock for other

[069-080]people was certainly not his chief business. And had Mr. Musselboro

[069-081]been asked what was his trade, he would have probably given an

[069-082]evasive answer. At any rate in the City, and among people who

[069-083]understood City matters, he would not have said that he was a

[069-084]stockbroker. Both Mr. Broughton and Mr. Musselboro bought and sold

[069-085]a good deal, but it was chiefly on account. The shares which were

[069-086]bought and sold very generally did not pass from hand to hand;

[069-087]but the difference in the price of the shares did do so. And then

[069-088]they had another little business between them. They lent money on

[069-089]interest. And in this business there was a third partner, whose name

[069-090]did not appear on the dirty door-post. That third partner was Mrs.

[069-091]Van Siever, the mother of Clara Van Siever whom Mr. Conway Dalrymple

[069-092]intended to portray as Jael driving a nail into Sisera's head.

[069-093]On a certain morning Mr. Broughton and Mr. Musselboro were sitting

[069-094]together in the office which has been described. They were in Mr.

[069-095]Broughton's room, and occupied each an arm-chair on the different

[069-096]sides of the fire. Mr. Musselboro was sitting close to the table, on

[069-097]which a ledger was open before him, and he had a pen and ink before

[069-098]him, as though he had been at work. Dobbs Broughton had a small

[069-099]betting-book in his hand, and was seated with his feet up against the

[069-100]side of the fireplace. Both men wore their hats, and the aspect of

[069-101]the room was not the aspect of a place of business. They had been

[069-102]silent for some minutes when Broughton took his cigar-case out of his

[069-103]pocket, and nibbled off the end of a cigar, preparatory to lighting

[069-104]it.

[069-105]"You had better not smoke here this morning, Dobbs," said Musselboro.

[069-106]"Why shouldn't I smoke in my own room?"

[069-107]"Because she'll be here just now."

[069-108]"What do I care? If you think I'm going to be afraid of Mother Van,

[069-109]you're mistaken. Let come what may, I'm not going to live under her

[069-110]thumb." So he lighted his cigar.

[069-111]"All right," said Musselboro, and he took up his pen and went to work

[069-112]at his book.

[069-113]"What is she coming here for this morning?" asked Broughton.

[069-114]"To look after her money. What should she come for?"

[069-115]"She gets her interest. I don't suppose there's better paid money in

[069-116]the City."

[069-117]"She hasn't got what was coming to her at Christmas yet."

[069-118]"And this is February. What would she have? She had better put her

[069-119]dirty money into the three per cents., if she is frightened at having

[069-120]to wait a week or two."

[069-121]"Can she have it to-day?"

[069-122]"What, the whole of it? Of course she can't. You know that as well as

[069-123]I do. She can have four hundred pounds, if she wants it. But seeing

[069-124]all she gets out of the concern, she has no right to press for it in

[069-125]that way. She is the ---- old usurer I ever came across in my life."

[069-126]"Of course she likes her money."

[069-127]"Likes her money! By George she does; her own and anybody else's that

[069-128]she can get hold of. For a downright leech, recommend me always to a

[069-129]woman. When a woman does go in for it, she is much more thorough than

[069-130]any man." Then Broughton turned over the little pages of his book,

[069-131]and Musselboro pondered over the big pages of his book, and there was

[069-132]silence for a quarter of an hour.

[069-133]"There's something about nine hundred and fifteen pounds due to her,"

[069-134]said Musselboro.

[069-135]"I daresay there is."

[069-136]"It would be a very good thing to let her have it if you've got it.

[069-137]The whole of it this morning, I mean."

[069-138]"If! yes, if!" said Broughton.

[069-139]"I know there's more than that at the bank."

[069-140]"And I'm to draw out every shilling that there is! I'll see Mother

[069-141]Van--further first. She can have £500 if she likes it,--and the

[069-142]rest in a fortnight. Or she can have my note-of-hand for it all at

[069-143]fourteen days."

[069-144]"She won't like that at all," said Musselboro.

[069-145]"Then she must lump it. I'm not going to bother myself about her.

[069-146]I've pretty nearly as much money in it as she has, and we're in a

[069-147]boat together. If she comes here bothering, you'd better tell her

[069-148]so."

[069-149]"You'll see her yourself?"

[069-150]"Not unless she comes within the next ten minutes. I must go down to

[069-151]the court. I said I'd be there by twelve. I've got somebody I want to

[069-152]see."

[069-153]"I'd stay if I were you."

[069-154]"Why should I stay for her? If she thinks that I'm going to make

[069-155]myself her clerk, she's mistaken. It may be all very well for you,

[069-156]Mussy, but it won't do for me. I'm not dependent on her, and I don't

[069-157]want to marry her daughter."

[069-158]"It will simply end in her demanding to have her money back again."

[069-159]"And how will she get it?" said Dobbs Broughton. "I haven't a doubt

[069-160]in life but she'd take it to-morrow if she could put her hands upon

[069-161]it. And then, after a bit, when she began to find that she didn't

[069-162]like four per cent., she'd bring it back again. But nobody can do

[069-163]business after such a fashion as that. For the last three years she's

[069-164]drawn close upon two thousand a year for less than eighteen thousand

[069-165]pounds. When a woman wants to do that, she can't have her money in

[069-166]her pocket every Monday morning."

[069-167]"But you've done better than that yourself, Dobbs."

[069-168]"Of course I have. And who has made the connexion; and who has done

[069-169]the work? I suppose she doesn't think that I'm to have all the sweat

[069-170]and that she is to have all the profit."

[069-171]"If you talk of work, Dobbs, it is I that have done the most of it."

[069-172]This Mr. Musselboro said in a very serious voice, and with a look of

[069-173]much reproach.

[069-174]"And you've been paid for what you've done. Come, Mussy, you'd better

[069-175]not turn against me. You'll never get your change out of that. Even

[069-176]if you marry the daughter, that won't give you the mother's money.

[069-177]She'll stick to every shilling of it till she dies; and she'd take

[069-178]it with her then, if she knew how." Having said this, he got up from

[069-179]his chair, put his little book into his pocket, and walked out of

[069-180]the office. He pushed his way across the court, which was more than

[069-181]ordinarily crowded with the implements of Burton and Bangles' trade,

[069-182]and as he passed under the covered way he encountered at the entrance

[069-183]an old woman getting out of a cab. The old woman was, of course,

[069-184]Mother Van, as her partner, Mr. Dobbs Broughton, irreverently called

[069-185]her. "Mrs. Van Siever, how d'ye do? Let me give you a hand. Fare from

[069-186]South Kensington? I always give the fellows three shillings."
