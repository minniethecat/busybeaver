[136-001]The news went out to Plumstead Episcopi by the postman, and happened

[136-002]to reach the archdeacon as he was talking to his sexton at the little

[136-003]gate leading into the churchyard. "Mrs. Proudie dead!" he almost

[136-004]shouted, as the postman notified the fact to him. "Impossible!"

[136-005]"It be so for zartain, yer reverence," said the postman, who was

[136-006]proud of his news.

[136-007]"Heavens!" ejaculated the archdeacon, and then hurried in to his

[136-008]wife. "My dear," he said--and as he spoke he could hardly deliver

[136-009]himself of his words, so eager was he to speak them--"who do you

[136-010]think is dead? Gracious heavens! Mrs. Proudie is dead!" Mrs. Grantly

[136-011]dropped from her hand the teaspoonful of tea that was just going into

[136-012]the pot, and repeated her husband's words. "Mrs. Proudie dead?" There

[136-013]was a pause, during which they looked into each other's faces. "My

[136-014]dear, I don't believe it," said Mrs. Grantly.

[136-015]But she did believe it very shortly. There were no prayers at

[136-016]Plumstead rectory that morning. The archdeacon immediately went out

[136-017]into the village, and soon obtained sufficient evidence of the truth

[136-018]of that which the postman had told him. Then he rushed back to his

[136-019]wife. "It's true," he said. "It's quite true. She's dead. There's no

[136-020]doubt about that. She's dead. It was last night about seven. That was

[136-021]when they found her, at least, and she may have died about an hour

[136-022]before. Filgrave says not more than an hour."

[136-023]"And how did she die?"

[136-024]"Heart-complaint. She was standing up, taking hold of the bedstead,

[136-025]and so they found her." Then there was a pause, during which the

[136-026]archdeacon sat down to his breakfast. "I wonder how he felt when he

[136-027]heard it?"

[136-028]"Of course he was terribly shocked."

[136-029]"I've no doubt he was shocked. Any man would be shocked. But when you

[136-030]come to think of it, what a relief!"

[136-031]"How can you speak of it in that way?" said Mrs. Grantly.

[136-032]"How am I to speak of it in any other way?" said the archdeacon. "Of

[136-033]course I shouldn't go and say it out in the street."

[136-034]"I don't think you ought to say it anywhere," said Mrs. Grantly. "The

[136-035]poor man no doubt feels about his wife in the same way that anybody

[136-036]else would."

[136-037]"And if any other poor man has got such a wife as she was, you may be

[136-038]quite sure that he would be glad to be rid of her. I don't say that

[136-039]he wished her to die, or that he would have done anything to contrive

[136-040]her death--"

[136-041]"Gracious, archdeacon; do, pray, hold your tongue."

[136-042]"But it stands to reason that her going will be a great relief to

[136-043]him. What has she done for him? She has made him contemptible to

[136-044]everybody in the diocese by her interference, and his life has been a

[136-045]burden to him through her violence."

[136-046]"Is that the way you carry out your proverb of De mortuis?" said Mrs.

[136-047]Grantly.

[136-048]"The proverb of De mortuis is founded on humbug. Humbug out of doors

[136-049]is necessary. It would not do for you and me to go into the High

[136-050]Street just now and say what we think about Mrs. Proudie; but I don't

[136-051]suppose that kind of thing need be kept up in here, between you and

[136-052]me. She was an uncomfortable woman,--so uncomfortable that I cannot

[136-053]believe that any one will regret her. Dear me! Only to think that she

[136-054]has gone! You may as well give me my tea."

[136-055]I do not think that Mrs. Grantly's opinion differed much from that

[136-056]expressed by her husband, or that she was, in truth, the least

[136-057]offended by the archdeacon's plain speech. But it must be remembered

[136-058]that there was probably no house in the diocese in which Mrs. Proudie

[136-059]had been so thoroughly hated as she had been at the Plumstead

[136-060]rectory. There had been hatred at the deanery; but the hatred at the

[136-061]deanery had been mild in comparison with the hatred at Plumstead. The

[136-062]archdeacon was a sound friend; but he was also a sound enemy. From

[136-063]the very first arrival of the Proudies at Barchester, Mrs. Proudie

[136-064]had thrown down her gauntlet to him, and he had not been slow in

[136-065]picking it up. The war had been internecine, and each had given the

[136-066]other terrible wounds. It had been understood that there should be

[136-067]no quarter, and there had been none. His enemy was now dead, and

[136-068]the archdeacon could not bring himself to adopt before his wife the

[136-069]namby-pamby every-day decency of speaking well of one of whom he

[136-070]had ever thought ill, or of expressing regret when no regret could

[136-071]be felt. "May all her sins be forgiven her," said Mrs. Grantly.

[136-072]"Amen," said the archdeacon. There was something in the tone of

[136-073]his Amen which thoroughly implied that it was uttered only on the

[136-074]understanding that her departure from the existing world was to be

[136-075]regarded as an unmitigated good, and that she should, at any rate,

[136-076]never come back again to Barchester.

[136-077]When Lady Lufton heard the tidings, she was not so bold in speaking

[136-078]of it as was her friend the archdeacon. "Mrs. Proudie dead!" she

[136-079]said to her daughter-in-law. This was some hours after the news had

[136-080]reached the house, and when the fact of the poor lady's death had

[136-081]been fully recognized. "What will he do without her?"

[136-082]"The same as other men do," said young Lady Lufton.

[136-083]"But, my dear, he is not the same as other men. He is not at all like

[136-084]other men. He is so weak that he cannot walk without a stick to lean

[136-085]upon. No doubt she was a virago, a woman who could not control her

[136-086]temper for a moment! No doubt she had led him a terrible life! I have

[136-087]often pitied him with all my heart. But, nevertheless, she was useful

[136-088]to him. I suppose she was useful to him. I can hardly believe that

[136-089]Mrs. Proudie is dead. Had he gone, it would have seemed so much more

[136-090]natural. Poor woman. I daresay she had her good points." The reader

[136-091]will be pleased to remember that the Luftons had ever been strong

[136-092]partisans on the side of the Grantlys.

[136-093]The news made its way even to Hogglestock on the same day. Mrs.

[136-094]Crawley, when she heard it, went out after her husband, who was in

[136-095]the school. "Dead!" said he, in answer to her whisper. "Do you tell

[136-096]me that the woman is dead?" Then Mrs. Crawley explained that the

[136-097]tidings were credible. "May God forgive her all her sins," said Mr.

[136-098]Crawley. "She was a violent woman, certainly, and I think that she

[136-099]misunderstood her duties; but I do not say that she was a bad woman.

[136-100]I am inclined to think that she was earnest in her endeavours to do

[136-101]good." It never occurred to Mr. Crawley that he and his affair had,

[136-102]in truth, been the cause of her death.

[136-103]It was thus that she was spoken of for a few days; and then men and

[136-104]women ceased to speak much of her, and began to talk of the bishop

[136-105]instead. A month had not passed before it was surmised that a man so

[136-106]long accustomed to the comforts of married life would marry again;

[136-107]and even then one lady connected with low-church clergymen in and

[136-108]around the city was named as a probable successor to the great lady

[136-109]who was gone. For myself, I am inclined to think that the bishop will

[136-110]for the future be content to lean upon his chaplain.

[136-111]The monument that was put up to our old friend's memory in one of the

[136-112]side aisles of the choir of the cathedral was supposed to be designed

[136-113]and executed in good taste. There was a broken column, and on the

[136-114]column simply the words, "My beloved wife!" Then there was a slab by

[136-115]the column, bearing Mrs. Proudie's name, with the date of her life

[136-116]and death. Beneath this was the common inscription,--

[136-117]"_Requiescat in pace._"

[136-118]CHAPTER LXVIII.

[136-119]THE OBSTINACY OF MR. CRAWLEY.

[136-120]Dr. Tempest, when he heard the news, sent immediately to Mr. Robarts,

[136-121]begging him to come over to Silverbridge. But this message was

[136-122]not occasioned solely by the death of Mrs. Proudie. Dr. Tempest

[136-123]had also heard that Mr. Crawley had submitted himself to the

[136-124]bishop, that instant advantage,--and as Dr. Tempest thought, unfair

[136-125]advantage,--had been taken of Mr. Crawley's submission, and that the

[136-126]pernicious Thumble had been at once sent over to Hogglestock. Had

[136-127]these palace doings with reference to Mr. Crawley been unaccompanied

[136-128]by the catastrophe which had happened, the doctor, much as he might

[136-129]have regretted them, would probably have felt that there was nothing

[136-130]to be done. He could not in such case have prevented Thumble's

[136-131]journey to Hogglestock on the next Sunday, and certainly he could

[136-132]not have softened the heart of the presiding genius at the palace.

[136-133]But things were very different now. The presiding genius was gone.

[136-134]Everybody at the palace would for a while be weak and vacillating.

[136-135]Thumble would be then thoroughly cowed; and it might at any rate be

[136-136]possible to make some movement in Mr. Crawley's favour. Dr. Tempest,

[136-137]therefore, sent for Mr. Robarts.

[136-138]"I'm giving you a great deal of trouble, Robarts," said the doctor;

[136-139]"but then you are so much younger than I am, and I've an idea

[136-140]that you would do more for this poor man than any one else in the

[136-141]diocese." Mr. Robarts of course declared that he did not begrudge his

[136-142]trouble, and that he would do anything in his power for the poor man.

[136-143]"I think that you should see him again, and that you should then see

[136-144]Thumble also. I don't know whether you can condescend to be civil to

[136-145]Thumble. I could not."

[136-146]"I am not quite sure that incivility would not be more efficacious,"

[136-147]said Mr. Robarts.

[136-148]"Very likely. There are men who are deaf as adders to courtesy, but

[136-149]who are compelled to obedience at once by ill-usage. Very likely

[136-150]Thumble is one of them; but of that you will be the best judge

[136-151]yourself. I would see Crawley first, and get his consent."

[136-152]"That's the difficulty."

[136-153]"Then I should go on without his consent, and I would see Thumble and

[136-154]the bishop's chaplain, Snapper. I think you might manage just at this

[136-155]moment, when they will all be a little abashed and perplexed by this

[136-156]woman's death, to arrange that simply nothing shall be done. The

[136-157]great thing will be that Crawley should go on with the duty till the

[136-158]assizes. If it should then happen that he goes into Barchester, is

[136-159]acquitted, and comes back again, the whole thing will be over, and

[136-160]there will be no further interference in the parish. If I were you,

[136-161]I think I would try it." Mr. Robarts said that he would try it. "I

[136-162]daresay Mr. Crawley will be a little stiff-necked with you."

[136-163]"He will be very stiff-necked with me," said Mr. Robarts.

[136-164]"But I can hardly think that he will throw away the only means he has

[136-165]of supporting his wife and children, when he finds that there can be

[136-166]no occasion for his doing so. I do not suppose that any person wishes

[136-167]him to throw up his work now that that poor woman has gone."

[136-168]Mr. Crawley had been almost in good spirits since the last visit

[136-169]which Mr. Thumble had made to him. It seemed as though the loss of

[136-170]everything in the world was in some way satisfactory to him. He had

[136-171]now given up his living by his own doing, and had after a fashion

[136-172]acknowledged his guilt by this act. He had proclaimed to all around

[136-173]him that he did not think himself to be any longer fit to perform the

[136-174]sacred functions of his office. He spoke of his trial as though a

[136-175]verdict against him must be the result. He knew that in going into

[136-176]prison he would leave his wife and children dependent on the charity

[136-177]of their friends,--on charity which they must condescend to accept,

[136-178]though he could not condescend to ask it. And yet he was able to

[136-179]carry himself now with a greater show of fortitude than had been

[136-180]within his power when the extent of his calamity was more doubtful.

[136-181]I must not ask the reader to suppose that he was cheerful. To have

[136-182]been cheerful under such circumstances would have been inhuman.

[136-183]But he carried his head on high, and walked firmly, and gave his

[136-184]orders at home with a clear voice. His wife, who was necessarily more

[136-185]despondent than ever, wondered at him,--but wondered in silence. It

[136-186]certainly seemed as though the very extremity of ill-fortune was

[136-187]good for him. And he was very diligent with his school, passing the

[136-188]greater part of the morning with the children. Mr. Thumble had told

[136-189]him that he would come on Sunday, and that he would then take charge

[136-190]of the parish. Up to the coming of Mr. Thumble he would do everything

[136-191]in the parish that could be done by a clergyman with a clear spirit

[136-192]and a free heart. Mr. Thumble should not find that spiritual weeds

[136-193]had grown rank in the parish because of his misfortunes.
