[012-001]Therefore he started off on the Monday morning after breakfast and

[012-002]walked to Silverbridge, and as he walked he built various castles in

[012-003]the air. Why should he not marry Grace,--if she would have him,--and

[012-004]take her away beyond the reach of her father's calamity? Why should

[012-005]he not throw over his own people altogether, money, position,

[012-006]society, and all, and give himself up to love? Were he to do so, men

[012-007]might say that he was foolish, but no one could hint that he was

[012-008]dishonourable. His spirit was high enough to teach him to think that

[012-009]such conduct on his part would have in it something of magnificence;

[012-010]but, yet, such was not his purpose. In going to Miss Prettyman it was

[012-011]his intention to apologize for not doing this magnificent thing. His

[012-012]mind was quite made up. Nevertheless he built those castles in the

[012-013]air.

[012-014]It so happened that he encountered the younger Miss Prettyman in

[012-015]the hall. It would not at all have suited him to reveal to her the

[012-016]purport of his visit, or ask her either to assist his suit or to

[012-017]receive his apologies. Miss Anne Prettyman was too common a personage

[012-018]in the Silverbridge world to be fit for such employment. Miss Anne

[012-019]Prettyman was, indeed, herself submissive to him, and treated him

[012-020]with the courtesy which is due to a superior being. He therefore

[012-021]simply asked her whether he could be allowed to see her sister.

[012-022]"Surely, Major Grantly;--that is, I think so. It is a little early,

[012-023]but I think she can receive you."

[012-024]"It is early, I know; but as I want to say a word or two on

[012-025]business--"

[012-026]"Oh, on business. I am sure she will see you on business; she will

[012-027]only be too proud. If you will be kind enough to step in here for two

[012-028]minutes." Then Miss Anne, having deposited the major in the little

[012-029]parlour, ran upstairs with her message to her sister. "Of course it's

[012-030]about Grace Crawley," she said to herself as she went. "It can't be

[012-031]about anything else. I wonder what it is he's going to say. If he's

[012-032]going to pop, and the father in all this trouble, he's the finest

[012-033]fellow that ever trod." Such were her thoughts as she tapped at the

[012-034]door and announced in the presence of Grace that there was somebody

[012-035]in the hall.

[012-036]"It's Major Grantly," whispered Anne, as soon as Grace had shut the

[012-037]door behind her.

[012-038]"So I supposed by your telling her not to go into the hall. What has

[012-039]he come to say?"

[012-040]"How on earth can I tell you that, Annabella? But I suppose he can

[012-041]have only one thing to say after all that has come and gone. He can

[012-042]only have come with one object."

[012-043]"He wouldn't have come to me for that. He would have asked to see

[012-044]herself."

[012-045]"But she never goes out now, and he can't see her."

[012-046]"Or he would have gone to them over at Hogglestock," said Miss

[012-047]Prettyman. "But of course he must come up now he is here. Would you

[012-048]mind telling him? or shall I ring the bell?"

[012-049]"I'll tell him. We need not make more fuss than necessary, with the

[012-050]servants, you know. I suppose I'd better not come back with him?"

[012-051]There was a tone of supplication in the younger sister's voice as she

[012-052]made the last suggestion, which ought to have melted the heart of the

[012-053]elder; but it was unavailing. "As he has asked to see me, I think you

[012-054]had better not," said Annabella. Miss Anne Prettyman bore her cross

[012-055]meekly, offered no argument on the subject, and returning to the

[012-056]little parlour where she had left the major, brought him upstairs and

[012-057]ushered him into her sister's room without even entering it again,

[012-058]herself.

[012-059]Major Grantly was as intimately acquainted with Miss Anne Prettyman

[012-060]as a man under thirty may well be with a lady nearer fifty than

[012-061]forty, who is not specially connected with him by any family tie; but

[012-062]of Miss Prettyman he knew personally very much less. Miss Prettyman,

[012-063]as has before been said, did not go out, and was therefore not common

[012-064]to the eyes of the Silverbridgians. She did occasionally see her

[012-065]friends in her own house, and Grace Crawley's lover, as the major had

[012-066]come to be called, had been there on more than one occasion; but of

[012-067]real personal intimacy between them there had hitherto existed none.

[012-068]He might have spoken, perhaps, a dozen words to her in his life. He

[012-069]had now more than a dozen to speak to her, but he hardly knew how to

[012-070]commence them.

[012-071]She had got up and curtseyed, and had then taken his hand and asked

[012-072]him to sit down. "My sister tells me that you want to see me," she

[012-073]said, in her softest, mildest voice.

[012-074]"I do, Miss Prettyman. I want to speak to you about a matter that

[012-075]troubles me very much,--very much indeed."

[012-076]"Anything that I can do, Major Grantly--"

[012-077]"Thank you, yes. I know that you are very good, or I should not have

[012-078]ventured to come to you. Indeed I shouldn't trouble you now, of

[012-079]course, if it was only about myself. I know very well what a great

[012-080]friend you are to Miss Crawley."

[012-081]"Yes, I am. We love Grace dearly here."

[012-082]"So do I," said the major, bluntly; "I love her dearly, too." Then he

[012-083]paused, as though he thought that Miss Prettyman ought to take up the

[012-084]speech. But Miss Prettyman seemed to think differently, and he was

[012-085]obliged to go on. "I don't know whether you have ever heard about it,

[012-086]or noticed it, or--or--or--" He felt that he was very awkward, and he

[012-087]blushed. Major as he was, he blushed as he sat before the old woman,

[012-088]trying to tell his story, but not knowing how to tell it. "The truth

[012-089]is, Miss Prettyman, I have done all but ask her to be my wife, and

[012-090]now has come this terrible affair about her father."

[012-091]"It is a terrible affair, Major Grantly; very terrible."

[012-092]"By Jove, you may say that!"

[012-093]"Of course Mr. Crawley is as innocent in the matter as you or I are."

[012-094]"You think so, Miss Prettyman?"

[012-095]"Think so! I feel quite sure of it. What; a clergyman of the Church

[012-096]of England, a pious, hard-working country clergyman, whom we have

[012-097]known among us by his good works for years, suddenly turn thief,

[012-098]and pilfer a few pounds! It is not possible, Major Grantly. And the

[012-099]father of such a daughter, too! It is not possible. It may do for

[012-100]men of business to think so, lawyers and such like, who are obliged

[012-101]to think in accordance with the evidence, as they call it; but to

[012-102]my mind the idea is monstrous. I don't know how he got it, and I

[012-103]don't care; but I'm quite sure he did not steal it. Whoever heard of

[012-104]anybody becoming so base as that all at once?"

[012-105]The major was startled by her eloquence, and by the indignant tone of

[012-106]voice in which it was expressed. It seemed to tell him that she would

[012-107]give him no sympathy in that which he had come to say to her, and to

[012-108]upbraid him already in that he was not prepared to do the magnificent

[012-109]thing of which he had thought when he had been building his castles

[012-110]in the air. Why should he not do the magnificent thing? Miss

[012-111]Prettyman's eloquence was so strong that it half convinced him that

[012-112]the Barchester Club and Mr. Walker had come to a wrong conclusion

[012-113]after all.

[012-114]"And how does Miss Crawley bear it?" he asked, desirous of postponing

[012-115]for a while any declaration of his own purpose.

[012-116]"She is very unhappy, of course. Not that she thinks evil of her

[012-117]father."

[012-118]"Of course she does not think him guilty."

[012-119]"Nobody thinks him so in this house, Major Grantly," said the little

[012-120]woman, very imperiously. "But Grace is, naturally enough, very

[012-121]sad;--very sad indeed. I do not think I can ask you to see her

[012-122]to-day."

[012-123]"I was not thinking of it," said the major.

[012-124]"Poor, dear girl! it is a great trial for her. Do you wish me to give

[012-125]her any message, Major Grantly?"

[012-126]The moment had now come in which he must say that which he had come

[012-127]to say. The little woman waited for an answer, and as he was there,

[012-128]within her power as it were, he must speak. I fear that what he said

[012-129]will not be approved by any strong-minded reader. I fear that our

[012-130]lover will henceforth be considered by such a one as being but a

[012-131]weak, wishy-washy man, who had hardly any mind of his own to speak

[012-132]of;--that he was a man of no account, as the poor people say. "Miss

[012-133]Prettyman, what message ought I to send to her?" he said.

[012-134]"Nay, Major Grantly, how can I tell you that? How can I put words

[012-135]into your mouth?"

[012-136]"It isn't the words," he said; "but the feelings."

[012-137]"And how can I tell the feelings of your heart?"

[012-138]"Oh, as for that, I know what my feelings are. I do love her with all

[012-139]my heart;--I do, indeed. A fortnight ago I was only thinking whether

[012-140]she would accept me when I asked her,--wondering whether I was too

[012-141]old for her, and whether she would mind having Edith to take care

[012-142]of."

[012-143]"She is very fond of Edith,--very fond indeed."

[012-144]"Is she?" said the major, more distracted than ever. Why should he

[012-145]not do the magnificent thing after all? "But it is a great charge for

[012-146]a young girl when she marries."

[012-147]"It is a great charge;--a very great charge. It is for you to think

[012-148]whether you should entrust so great a charge to one so young."

[012-149]"I have no fear about that at all."

[012-150]"Nor should I have any,--as you ask me. We have known Grace well,

[012-151]thoroughly, and are quite sure that she will do her duty in that

[012-152]state of life to which it may please God to call her."

[012-153]The major was aware when this was said to him that he had not come

[012-154]to Miss Prettyman for a character of the girl he loved; and yet

[012-155]he was not angry at receiving it. He was neither angry, nor even

[012-156]indifferent. He accepted the character almost gratefully, though

[012-157]he felt that he was being led away from his purpose. He consoled

[012-158]himself for this, however, by remembering that the path by which Miss

[012-159]Prettyman was now leading him, led to the magnificent, and to those

[012-160]pleasant castles in the air which he had been building as he walked

[012-161]into Silverbridge. "I am quite sure that she is all that you say," he

[012-162]replied. "Indeed I had made up my mind about that long ago."

[012-163]"And what can I do for you, Major Grantly?"

[012-164]"You think I ought not to see her?"

[012-165]"I will ask herself, if you please. I have such trust in her judgment

[012-166]that I should leave her altogether to her own discretion."

[012-167]The magnificent thing must be done, and the major made up his mind

[012-168]accordingly. Something of regret came over his spirit as he thought

[012-169]of a father-in-law disgraced and degraded, and of his own father

[012-170]broken-hearted. But now there was hardly an alternative left to him.

[012-171]And was it not the manly thing for him to do? He had loved the girl

[012-172]before this trouble had come upon her, and was he not bound to accept

[012-173]the burden which his love had brought with it? "I will see her," he

[012-174]said, "at once, if you will let me, and ask her to be my wife. But I

[012-175]must see her alone."

[012-176]Then Miss Prettyman paused. Hitherto she had undoubtedly been playing

[012-177]her fish cautiously, or rather her young friend's fish,--perhaps I

[012-178]may say cunningly. She had descended to artifice on behalf of the

[012-179]girl whom she loved, admired, and pitied. She had seen some way into

[012-180]the man's mind, and had been partly aware of his purpose,--of his

[012-181]infirmity of purpose, of his double purpose. She had perceived that a

[012-182]word from her might help Grace's chance, and had led the man on till

[012-183]he had committed himself, at any rate to her. In doing this she had

[012-184]been actuated by friendship rather than by abstract principle. But

[012-185]now, when the moment had come in which she must decide upon some

[012-186]action, she paused. Was it right, for the sake of either of them,

[012-187]that an offer of marriage should be made at such a moment as this?

[012-188]It might be very well, in regard to some future time, that the major

[012-189]should have so committed himself. She saw something of the man's

[012-190]spirit, and believed that, having gone so far,--having so far told

[012-191]his love, he would return to his love hereafter, let the result of

[012-192]the Crawley trial be what it might. But,--but, this could be no

[012-193]proper time for love-making. Though Grace loved the man, as Miss

[012-194]Prettyman knew well,--though Grace loved the child, having allowed

[012-195]herself to long to call it her own, though such a marriage would be

[012-196]the making of Grace's fortune as those who loved her could hardly

[012-197]have hoped that it should ever have been made, she would certainly

[012-198]refuse the man, if he were to propose to her now. She would refuse

[012-199]him, and then the man would be free;--free to change his mind if he

[012-200]thought fit. Considering all these things, craftily in the exercise

[012-201]of her friendship, too cunningly, I fear, to satisfy the claims of a

[012-202]high morality, she resolved that the major had better not see Miss

[012-203]Crawley at the present moment. Miss Prettyman paused before she

[012-204]replied, and, when she did speak, Major Grantly had risen from his

[012-205]chair and was standing with his back to the fire. "Major Grantly,"

[012-206]she said, "you shall see her if you please, and if she pleases; but I

[012-207]doubt whether her answer at such a moment as this would be that which

[012-208]you would wish to receive."
