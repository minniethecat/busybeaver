[126-001]Since that time I have continued in my parish,--working,

[126-002]not without zeal, though in truth, almost without

[126-003]hope,--and learning even from day to day that the opinions

[126-004]of men around me have declared me to be guilty of the

[126-005]crime imputed to me. And now the bishop has issued a

[126-006]commission as preparatory to proceeding against me under

[126-007]the Act for the punishment of clerical offences. In doing

[126-008]this, I cannot say that the bishop has been ill-advised,

[126-009]even though the advice may have come from that

[126-010]evil-tongued lady, his wife. And I hold that a woman may

[126-011]be called on for advice, with most salutary effect, in

[126-012]affairs as to which any show of female authority would be

[126-013]equally false and pernicious. With me it has ever been so,

[126-014]and I have had a counsellor by me as wise as she has been

[126-015]devoted.

[126-016]It must be noticed that in the draught copy of his letter which Mr.

[126-017]Crawley gave to his wife to read this last sentence was not inserted.

[126-018]Intending that she should read his letter, he omitted it till he made

[126-019]the fair copy.

[126-020]Over this commission his lordship has appointed Dr.

[126-021]Tempest of Silverbridge to preside, and with him I have

[126-022]been in communication. I trust that the labours of the

[126-023]gentlemen of whom it is composed may be brought to a

[126-024]speedy close; and, having regard to their trouble, which

[126-025]in such a matter is, I fear, left without remuneration, I

[126-026]have informed Dr. Tempest that I should write this letter

[126-027]to you with the intent and assured purpose of resigning

[126-028]the perpetual curacy of Hogglestock into your hands.

[126-029]You will be good enough, therefore, to understand that

[126-030]I do so resign the living, and that I shall continue to

[126-031]administer the services of the church only till some

[126-032]clergyman, certified to me as coming from you or from

[126-033]the bishop, may present himself in the parish, and shall

[126-034]declare himself prepared to undertake the cure. Should it

[126-035]be so that Mr. Thumble be sent hither again, I will sit

[126-036]under him, endeavouring to catch improvement from his

[126-037]teaching, and striving to overcome the contempt which I

[126-038]felt for him when he before visited this parish. I annex

[126-039]beneath my signature a copy of the letter which I have

[126-040]written to the bishop on this subject.

[126-041]And now it behoves me, as the guardianship of the souls of

[126-042]those around me was placed in my hands by you, to explain

[126-043]to you as shortly as may be possible the reasons which

[126-044]have induced me to abandon my work. One or two whose

[126-045]judgment I do not discredit,--and I am allowed to name

[126-046]Dr. Tempest of Silverbridge as one,--have suggested to me

[126-047]that I should take no step myself till after my trial.

[126-048]They think that I should have regard to the chance of the

[126-049]verdict, so that the preferment may still be mine should

[126-050]I be acquitted; and they say, that should I be acquitted,

[126-051]the bishop's action against me must of necessity cease.

[126-052]That they are right in these facts I do not doubt; but

[126-053]in giving such advice they look only to facts, having no

[126-054]regard to the conscience. I do not blame them. I should

[126-055]give such advice myself, knowing that a friend may give

[126-056]counsel as to outer things, but that a man must satisfy

[126-057]his inner conscience by his own perceptions of what is

[126-058]right and what is wrong.

[126-059]I find myself to be ill-spoken of, to be regarded with

[126-060]hard eyes by those around me, my people thinking that I

[126-061]have stolen this money. Two farmers in this parish have,

[126-062]as I am aware, expressed opinions that no jury could

[126-063]acquit me honestly, and neither of these men have appeared

[126-064]in my church since the expression of that opinion. I doubt

[126-065]whether they have gone to other churches; and if not they

[126-066]have been deterred from all public worship by my presence.

[126-067]If this be so, how can I with a clear conscience remain

[126-068]among these men? Shall I take from their hands wages for

[126-069]those administrations, which their deliberately formed

[126-070]opinions will not allow them to accept from my hands?

[126-071]And yet, though he thus pleaded against himself, he knew that the two

[126-072]men of whom he was speaking were thick-headed dolts who were always

[126-073]tipsy on Saturday nights, and who came to church perhaps once in

[126-074]three weeks.

[126-075]Your kind heart will doubtless prompt you to tell me

[126-076]that no clergyman could be safe in his parish if he were

[126-077]to allow the opinion of chance parishioners to prevail

[126-078]against him; and you would probably lay down for my

[126-079]guidance that grand old doctrine, "Nil conscire sibi,

[126-080]nullâ pallescere culpâ." Presuming that you may do so, I

[126-081]will acknowledge such guidance to be good. If my mind were

[126-082]clear in this matter, I would not budge an inch for any

[126-083]farmer,--no, nor for any bishop, further than he might by

[126-084]law compel me! But my mind is not clear. I do grow pale,

[126-085]and my hair stands on end with horror, as I confess to

[126-086]myself that I do not know whether I stole this money or

[126-087]no! Such is the fact. In all sincerity I tell you that I

[126-088]know not whether I be guilty or innocent. It may be that

[126-089]I picked up the cheque from the floor of my room, and

[126-090]afterwards took it out and used it, not knowing whence it

[126-091]had come to me. If it be so, I stole it, and am guilty

[126-092]before the laws of my country. If it be so, I am not fit

[126-093]to administer the Lord's sacraments to these people. When

[126-094]the cup was last in my hand and I was blessing them,

[126-095]I felt that I was not fit, and I almost dropped the

[126-096]chalice. That God will know my weakness and pardon me

[126-097]the perplexity of my mind,--that is between Him and His

[126-098]creature.

[126-099]As I read my letter over to myself I feel how weak are my

[126-100]words, and how inefficient to explain to you the exact

[126-101]position in which I stand; but they will suffice to

[126-102]convince you that I am assuredly purposed to resign this

[126-103]parish of Hogglestock, and that it is therefore incumbent

[126-104]on you, as patron of the living, to nominate my successor

[126-105]to the benefice. I have only further to ask your pardon

[126-106]for this long letter, and to thank you again for the many

[126-107]and great marks of friendship which you have conferred on

[126-108]me. Alas, could you have foreseen in those old days how

[126-109]barren of all good would have been the life of him you

[126-110]then esteemed, you might perhaps have escaped the disgrace

[126-111]of being called the friend of one whom no one now regards

[126-112]with esteem.

[126-113]Nevertheless, I may still say that I am,

[126-114]With all affection, yours truly,

[126-115]JOSIAH CRAWLEY.

[126-116]The last paragraph of the letter was also added since his wife had

[126-117]read it. When he had first composed his letter, he had been somewhat

[126-118]proud of his words, thinking that he had clearly told his story. But

[126-119]when, sitting alone at his desk, he read it again, filling his mind

[126-120]as he went on with ideas which he would fain have expressed to his

[126-121]old friend, were it not that he feared to indulge himself with too

[126-122]many words, he began to tell himself that his story was anything but

[126-123]well told. There was no expression there of the Hoggethan doctrine.

[126-124]In answer to such a letter as that the dean might well say, "Think

[126-125]again of it. Try yet to save yourself. Never mind the two farmers, or

[126-126]Mr. Thumble, or the bishop. Stick to the ship while there is a plank

[126-127]above the water." Whereas it had been his desire to use words that

[126-128]should make the dean clearly understand that the thing was decided.

[126-129]He had failed,--as he had failed in everything throughout his life;

[126-130]but nevertheless the letter must go. Were he to begin again he would

[126-131]not do it better. So he added to what he had written a copy of his

[126-132]note to the bishop, and the letter was fastened and sent.

[126-133]Mrs. Crawley might probably have been more instant in her efforts

[126-134]to stop the letter, had she not felt that it would not decide

[126-135]everything. In the first place it was not improbable that the letter

[126-136]might not reach the dean till after his return home,--and Mrs.

[126-137]Crawley had long since made up her mind that she would see the dean

[126-138]as soon as possible after his return. She had heard from Lady Lufton

[126-139]that it was not doubted in Barchester that he would be back at any

[126-140]rate before the judges came into the city. And then, in the next

[126-141]place, was it probable that the dean would act upon such a letter by

[126-142]filling up the vacancy, even if he did get it? She trusted in the

[126-143]dean, and knew that he would help them, if any help were possible.

[126-144]Should the verdict go against her husband, then indeed it might be

[126-145]that no help would be possible. In such case she thought that the

[126-146]bishop with his commission might prevail. But she still believed

[126-147]that the verdict would be favourable,--if not with an assured belief,

[126-148]still with a hope that was sufficient to stand in lieu of a belief.

[126-149]No single man, let alone no twelve men, could think that her husband

[126-150]had intended to appropriate that money dishonestly. That he had

[126-151]taken it improperly,--without real possession,--she herself believed;

[126-152]but he had not taken it as a thief, and could not merit a thief's

[126-153]punishment.

[126-154]After two days he got a reply from the bishop's chaplain, in which

[126-155]the chaplain expressed the bishop's commendation of Mr. Crawley's

[126-156]present conduct. "Mr. Thumble shall proceed from hence to Hogglestock

[126-157]on next Sunday," said the chaplain, "and shall relieve you for the

[126-158]present from the burden of your duties. As to the future status of

[126-159]the parish, it will perhaps be best that nothing shall be done till

[126-160]the dean returns,--or perhaps till the assizes shall be over. This is

[126-161]the bishop's opinion." It need hardly be explained that the promised

[126-162]visit of Mr. Thumble to Hogglestock was gall and wormwood to Mr.

[126-163]Crawley. He had told the dean that should Mr. Thumble come, he would

[126-164]endeavour to learn something even from him. But it may be doubted

[126-165]whether Mr. Crawley in his present mood could learn anything useful

[126-166]from Mr. Thumble. Giles Hoggett was a much more effective teacher.

[126-167]"I will endure even that," he said to his wife, as she handed to him

[126-168]back the letter from the bishop's chaplain.

[126-169]CHAPTER LXIII.

[126-170]TWO VISITORS TO HOGGLESTOCK.

[126-171]The cross-grainedness of men is so great that things will often

[126-172]be forced to go wrong, even when they have the strongest possible

[126-173]natural tendency of their own to go right. It was so now in these

[126-174]affairs between the archdeacon and his son. The original difficulty

[126-175]was solved by the good feeling of the young lady,--by that and by

[126-176]the real kindness of the archdeacon's nature. They had come to terms

[126-177]which were satisfactory to both of them, and those terms admitted of

[126-178]perfect reconciliation between the father and his son. Whether the

[126-179]major did marry the lady or whether he did not, his allowance was to

[126-180]be continued to him, the archdeacon being perfectly willing to trust

[126-181]himself in the matter to the pledge which he had received from Miss

[126-182]Crawley. All that he required from his son was simply this,--that he

[126-183]should pull down the bills advertising the sale of his effects. Was

[126-184]any desire ever more rational? The sale had been advertised for a day

[126-185]just one week in advance of the assizes, and the time must have been

[126-186]selected,--so thought the archdeacon,--with a malicious intention.

[126-187]Why, at any rate, should the things be sold before any one knew

[126-188]whether the father of the young lady was or was not to be regarded

[126-189]as a thief? And why should the things be sold at all, when the

[126-190]archdeacon had tacitly withdrawn his threats,--when he had given his

[126-191]son to understand that the allowance would still be paid quarterly

[126-192]with the customary archidiaconal regularity, and that no alteration

[126-193]was intended in those settlements under which the Plumstead foxes

[126-194]would, in the ripeness of time, become the property of the major

[126-195]himself. It was thus that the archdeacon looked at it, and as he did

[126-196]so, he thought that his son was the most cross-grained of men.
