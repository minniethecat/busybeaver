[135-001]Yes, he was a widower, and he might do as he pleased. The tyrant

[135-002]was gone, and he was free. The tyrant was gone, and the tyranny had

[135-003]doubtless been very oppressive. Who had suffered as he had done? But

[135-004]in thus being left without his tyrant he was wretchedly desolate.

[135-005]Might it not be that the tyranny had been good for him?--that the

[135-006]Lord had known best what wife was fit for him? Then he thought of a

[135-007]story which he had read,--and had well marked as he was reading,--of

[135-008]some man who had been terribly afflicted by his wife, whose wife had

[135-009]starved him and beaten him and reviled him; and yet this man had been

[135-010]able to thank his God for having thus mortified him in the flesh.

[135-011]Might it not be that the mortification which he himself had doubtless

[135-012]suffered in his flesh had been intended for his welfare, and had

[135-013]been very good for him? But if this were so, it might be that the

[135-014]mortification was now removed because the Lord knew that his servant

[135-015]had been sufficiently mortified. He had not been starved or beaten,

[135-016]but the mortification had been certainly severe. Then there came

[135-017]words--into his mind, not into his mouth--"The Lord sent the thorn,

[135-018]and the Lord has taken it away. Blessed be the name of the Lord."

[135-019]After that he was very angry with himself, and tried to pray that he

[135-020]might be forgiven. While he was so striving there came a low knock at

[135-021]the door, and Mrs. Draper again entered the room.

[135-022]"Dr. Filgrave, my lord, was not at home," said Mrs. Draper; "but he

[135-023]will be sent the very moment he arrives."

[135-024]"Very well, Mrs. Draper."

[135-025]"But, my lord, will you not come to your dinner? A little soup, or

[135-026]a morsel of something to eat, and a glass of wine, will enable your

[135-027]lordship to bear it better." He allowed Mrs. Draper to persuade him,

[135-028]and followed her into the dining-room. "Do not go, Mrs. Draper," he

[135-029]said; "I would rather that you should stay with me." So Mrs. Draper

[135-030]stayed with him, and administered to his wants. He was desirous of

[135-031]being seen by as few eyes as possible in these the first moments of

[135-032]his freedom.

[135-033]He saw Dr. Filgrave twice, both before and after the doctor had been

[135-034]upstairs. There was no doubt, Dr. Filgrave said, that it was as Mrs.

[135-035]Draper had surmised. The poor lady was suffering, and had for years

[135-036]been suffering, from heart-complaint. To her husband she had never

[135-037]said a word on the subject. To Mrs. Draper a word had been said now

[135-038]and again,--a word when some moment of fear would come, when some

[135-039]sharp stroke of agony would tell of danger. But Mrs. Draper had kept

[135-040]the secret of her mistress, and none of the family had known that

[135-041]there was aught to be feared. Dr. Filgrave, indeed, did tell the

[135-042]bishop that he had dreaded all along exactly that which had happened.

[135-043]He had said the same to Mr. Rerechild, the surgeon, when they two

[135-044]had had a consultation together at the palace on the occasion of

[135-045]a somewhat alarming birth of a grandchild. But he mixed up this

[135-046]information with so much medical Latin, and was so pompous over it,

[135-047]and the bishop was so anxious to be rid of him, that his words did

[135-048]not have much effect. What did it all matter? The thorn was gone, and

[135-049]the wife was dead, and the widower must balance his gain and loss as

[135-050]best he might.

[135-051]He slept well, but when he woke in the morning the dreariness of his

[135-052]loneliness was very strong on him. He must do something, and must see

[135-053]somebody, but he felt that he did not know how to bear himself in his

[135-054]new position. He must send of course for his chaplain, and tell his

[135-055]chaplain to open all letters and to answer them for a week. Then he

[135-056]remembered how many of his letters in days of yore had been opened

[135-057]and been answered by the helpmate who had just gone from him. Since

[135-058]Dr. Tempest's visit he had insisted that the palace letter-bag should

[135-059]always be brought in the first instance to him;--and this had been

[135-060]done, greatly to the annoyance of his wife. In order that it might

[135-061]be done the bishop had been up every morning an hour before his

[135-062]usual time; and everybody in the household had known why it was

[135-063]so. He thought of this now as the bag was brought to him on the

[135-064]first morning of his freedom. He could have it where he pleased

[135-065]now;--either in his bedroom or left for him untouched on the

[135-066]breakfast-table till he should go to it. "Blessed be the name of

[135-067]the Lord," he said as he thought of all this; but he did not stop

[135-068]to analyse what he was saying. On this morning he would not enjoy

[135-069]his liberty, but desired that the letter-bag might be taken to Mr.

[135-070]Snapper, the chaplain.

[135-071]The news of Mrs. Proudie's death had spread all over Barchester

[135-072]on the evening of its occurrence, and had been received with that

[135-073]feeling of distant awe which is always accompanied by some degree of

[135-074]pleasurable sensation. There was no one in Barchester to lament a

[135-075]mother, or a sister, or a friend who was really loved. There were

[135-076]those, doubtless, who regretted the woman's death,--and even some

[135-077]who regretted it without any feeling of personal damage done to

[135-078]themselves. There had come to be around Mrs. Proudie a party who

[135-079]thought as she thought on church matters, and such people had lost

[135-080]their head, and thereby their strength. And she had been staunch to

[135-081]her own party, preferring bad tea from a low-church grocer, to good

[135-082]tea from a grocer who went to the ritualistic church or to no church

[135-083]at all. And it is due to her to say that she did not forget those who

[135-084]were true to her,--looking after them mindfully where looking after

[135-085]might be profitable, and fighting their battles where fighting might

[135-086]be more serviceable. I do not think that the appetite for breakfast

[135-087]of any man or woman in Barchester was disturbed by the news of Mrs.

[135-088]Proudie's death, but there were some who felt that a trouble had

[135-089]fallen on them.

[135-090]Tidings of the catastrophe reached Hiram's Hospital on the evening of

[135-091]its occurrence,--Hiram's Hospital, where dwelt Mr. and Mrs. Quiverful

[135-092]with all their children. Now Mrs. Quiverful owed a debt of gratitude

[135-093]to Mrs. Proudie, having been placed in her present comfortable home

[135-094]by that lady's patronage. Mrs. Quiverful perhaps understood the

[135-095]character of the deceased woman, and expressed her opinion respecting

[135-096]it, as graphically as did any one in Barchester. There was the

[135-097]natural surprise felt at the Warden's lodge in the Hospital when the

[135-098]tidings were first received there, and the Quiverful family was at

[135-099]first too full of dismay, regrets and surmises, to be able to give

[135-100]themselves impartially to criticism. But on the following morning,

[135-101]conversation at the breakfast-table naturally referring to the great

[135-102]loss which the bishop had sustained, Mrs. Quiverful thus pronounced

[135-103]her opinion of her friend's character: "You'll find that he'll feel

[135-104]it, Q.," she said to her husband, in answer to some sarcastic remark

[135-105]made by him as to the removal of the thorn. "He'll feel it, though

[135-106]she was almost too many for him while she was alive."

[135-107]"I daresay he'll feel it at first," said Quiverful; "but I think

[135-108]he'll be more comfortable than he has been."

[135-109]"Of course he'll feel it, and go on feeling it till he dies, if he's

[135-110]the man I take him to be. You're not to think that there has been no

[135-111]love because there used to be some words, that he'll find himself the

[135-112]happier because he can do things more as he pleases. She was a great

[135-113]help to him, and he must have known that she was, in spite of the

[135-114]sharpness of her tongue. No doubt she was sharp. No doubt she was

[135-115]upsetting. And she could make herself a fool too in her struggles to

[135-116]have everything her own way. But, Q., there were worse women than

[135-117]Mrs. Proudie. She was never one of your idle ones, and I'm quite sure

[135-118]that no man or woman ever heard her say a word against her husband

[135-119]behind his back."

[135-120]"All the same, she gave him a terribly bad life of it, if all is true

[135-121]that we hear."

[135-122]"There are men who must have what you call a terribly bad life of

[135-123]it, whatever way it goes with them. The bishop is weak, and he wants

[135-124]somebody near to him to be strong. She was strong,--perhaps too

[135-125]strong; but he had his advantage out of it. After all I don't know

[135-126]that his life has been so terribly bad. I daresay he's had everything

[135-127]very comfortable about him. And a man ought to be grateful for that,

[135-128]though very few men ever are."

[135-129]Mr. Quiverful's predecessor at the Hospital, old Mr. Harding, whose

[135-130]halcyon days in Barchester had been passed before the coming of the

[135-131]Proudies, was in bed playing cat's-cradle with Posy seated on the

[135-132]counterpane, when the tidings of Mrs. Proudie's death were brought to

[135-133]him by Mrs. Baxter. "Oh, sir," said Mrs. Baxter, seating herself on

[135-134]a chair by the bed-side. Mr. Harding liked Mrs. Baxter to sit down,

[135-135]because he was almost sure on such occasions to have the advantage of

[135-136]a prolonged conversation.

[135-137]"What is it, Mrs. Baxter?"

[135-138]"Oh, sir!"

[135-139]"Is anything the matter?" And the old man attempted to raise himself

[135-140]in his bed.

[135-141]"You mustn't frighten grandpa," said Posy.

[135-142]"No, my dear; and there isn't nothing to frighten him. There isn't

[135-143]indeed, Mr. Harding. They're all well at Plumstead, and when I heard

[135-144]from the missus at Venice, everything was going on well."

[135-145]"But what is it, Mrs. Baxter?"

[135-146]"God forgive her all her sins--Mrs. Proudie ain't no more." Now there

[135-147]had been a terrible feud between the palace and the deanery for

[135-148]years, in carrying on which the persons of the opposed households

[135-149]were wont to express themselves with eager animosity. Mrs. Baxter and

[135-150]Mrs. Draper never spoke to each other. The two coachmen each longed

[135-151]for an opportunity to take the other before a magistrate for some

[135-152]breach of the law of the road in driving. The footmen abused each

[135-153]other, and the grooms occasionally fought. The masters and mistresses

[135-154]contented themselves with simple hatred. Therefore it was not

[135-155]surprising that Mrs. Baxter, in speaking of the death of Mrs.

[135-156]Proudie, should remember first her sins.

[135-157]"Mrs. Proudie dead!" said the old man.

[135-158]"Indeed she is, Mr. Harding," said Mrs. Baxter, putting both her

[135-159]hands together piously. "We're just grass, ain't we, sir! and dust

[135-160]and clay and flowers of the field?" Whether Mrs. Proudie had most

[135-161]partaken of the clayey nature or of the flowery nature, Mrs. Baxter

[135-162]did not stop to consider.

[135-163]"Mrs. Proudie dead!" said Posy, with a solemnity that was all her

[135-164]own. "Then she won't scold the poor bishop any more."

[135-165]"No, my dear; she won't scold anybody any more; and it will be a

[135-166]blessing for some, I must say. Everybody is always so considerate in

[135-167]this house, Miss Posy, that we none of us know nothing about what

[135-168]that is."

[135-169]"Dead!" said Mr. Harding again. "I think, if you please, Mrs. Baxter,

[135-170]you shall leave me for a little time, and take Miss Posy with you."

[135-171]He had been in the city of Barchester some fifty years, and here was

[135-172]one who might have been his daughter, who had come there scarcely ten

[135-173]years since, and who now had gone before him! He had never loved Mrs.

[135-174]Proudie. Perhaps he had gone as near to disliking Mrs. Proudie as he

[135-175]had ever gone to disliking any person. Mrs. Proudie had wounded him

[135-176]in every part that was most sensitive. It would be long to tell,

[135-177]nor need it be told now, how she had ridiculed his cathedral work,

[135-178]how she had made nothing of him, how she had despised him, always

[135-179]manifesting her contempt plainly. He had been even driven to rebuke

[135-180]her, and it had perhaps been the only personal rebuke which he had

[135-181]ever uttered in Barchester. But now she was gone; and he thought of

[135-182]her simply as an active pious woman, who had been taken away from her

[135-183]work before her time. And for the bishop, no idea ever entered Mr.

[135-184]Harding's mind as to the removal of a thorn. The man had lost his

[135-185]life's companion at that time of life when such a companion is most

[135-186]needed; and Mr. Harding grieved for him with sincerity.
