[120-001]On the following morning he found Miss Van Siever already seated in

[120-002]Mrs. Broughton's room when he reached it. And at the moment Mrs.

[120-003]Broughton was not there. As he took Clara's hand, he could not

[120-004]prevent himself from asking her whether she had heard anything?

[120-005]"Heard what?" said Clara. "Then you have not," said he. "Never mind

[120-006]now, as Mrs. Broughton is here." Then Mrs. Broughton had entered

[120-007]the room. She seemed to be quite cheerful, but Dalrymple perfectly

[120-008]understood, from a special glance which she gave to him, that he was

[120-009]to perceive that her cheerfulness was assumed for Clara's benefit.

[120-010]Mrs. Broughton was showing how great a heroine she could be on behalf

[120-011]of her friends. "Now, my dear," she said, "do remember that this is

[120-012]the last day. It may be all very well, Conway, and, of course, you

[120-013]know best; but as far as I can see, you have not made half as much

[120-014]progress as you ought to have done." "We shall do excellently well,"

[120-015]said Dalrymple. "So much the better," said Mrs. Broughton; "and now,

[120-016]Clara, I'll place you." And so Clara was placed on her knees, with

[120-017]the turban on her head.

[120-018]Dalrymple began his work assiduously, knowing that Mrs. Broughton

[120-019]would not leave the room for some minutes. It was certain that she

[120-020]would remain for a quarter of an hour, and it might be as well that

[120-021]he should really use that time on his picture. The peculiar position

[120-022]in which he was placed probably made his work difficult to him. There

[120-023]was something perplexing in the necessity which bound him to look

[120-024]upon the young lady before him both as Jael and as the future Mrs.

[120-025]Conway Dalrymple, knowing as he did that she was at present simply

[120-026]Clara Van Siever. A double personification was not difficult to him.

[120-027]He had encountered it with every model that had sat to him, and with

[120-028]every young lady he had attempted to win,--if he had ever made such

[120-029]an attempt with one before. But the triple character, joined to the

[120-030]necessity of the double work, was distressing to him. "The hand a

[120-031]little further back, if you don't mind," he said, "and the wrist

[120-032]more turned towards me. That is just it. Lean a little more over

[120-033]him. There--that will do exactly." If Mrs. Broughton did not go very

[120-034]quickly, he must begin to address his model on a totally different

[120-035]subject, even while she was in the act of slaying Sisera.

[120-036]"Have you made up your mind who is to be Sisera?" asked Mrs.

[120-037]Broughton.

[120-038]"I think I shall put in my own face," said Dalrymple; "if Miss Van

[120-039]Siever does not object."

[120-040]"Not in the least," said Clara, speaking without moving her

[120-041]face--almost without moving her lips.

[120-042]"That will be excellent," said Mrs. Broughton. She was still quite

[120-043]cheerful, and really laughed as she spoke. "Shall you like the idea,

[120-044]Clara, of striking the nail right through his head?"

[120-045]"Oh, yes; as well his head as another's. I shall seem to be having my

[120-046]revenge for all the trouble he has given me."

[120-047]There was a slight pause, and then Dalrymple spoke. "You have had

[120-048]that already, in striking me right through the heart."

[120-049]"What a very pretty speech! Was it not, my dear?" said Mrs.

[120-050]Broughton. And then Mrs. Broughton laughed. There was something

[120-051]slightly hysterical in her laugh which grated on Dalrymple's

[120-052]ears,--something which seemed to tell him that at the present moment

[120-053]his dear friend was not going to assist him honestly in his effort.

[120-054]"Only that I should put him out, I would get up and make a curtsey,"

[120-055]said Clara. No young lady could ever talk of making a curtsey for

[120-056]such a speech if she supposed it to have been made in earnestness.

[120-057]And Clara, no doubt, understood that a man might make a hundred such

[120-058]speeches in the presence of a third person without any danger that

[120-059]they would be taken as meaning anything. All this Dalrymple knew, and

[120-060]began to think that he had better put down his palette and brush,

[120-061]and do the work which he had before him in the most prosaic language

[120-062]that he could use. He could, at any rate, succeed in making Clara

[120-063]acknowledge his intention in this way. He waited still for a minute

[120-064]or two, and it seemed to him that Mrs. Broughton had no intention

[120-065]of piling her fagots on the present occasion. It might be that the

[120-066]remembrance of her husband's ruin prevented her from sacrificing

[120-067]herself in the other direction also.

[120-068]"I am not very good at pretty speeches, but I am good at telling the

[120-069]truth," said Dalrymple.

[120-070]"Ha, ha, ha!" laughed Mrs. Broughton, still with a touch of

[120-071]hysterical action in her throat. "Upon my word, Conway, you know how

[120-072]to praise yourself."

[120-073]"He dispraises himself most unnecessarily in denying the prettiness

[120-074]of his language," said Clara. As she spoke she hardly moved her lips,

[120-075]and Dalrymple went on painting from the model. It was clear that Miss

[120-076]Van Siever understood that the painting, and not the pretty speeches,

[120-077]was the important business on hand.

[120-078]Mrs. Broughton had now tucked her feet up on the sofa, and was gazing

[120-079]at the artist as he stood at his work. Dalrymple, remembering how he

[120-080]had offered her his purse,--an offer which, in the existing crisis of

[120-081]her affairs, might mean a great deal,--felt that she was ill-natured.

[120-082]Had she intended to do him a good turn, she would have gone now;

[120-083]but there she lay, with her feet tucked up, clearly purposing to

[120-084]be present through the whole of that morning's sitting. His anger

[120-085]against her added something to his spirit, and made him determine

[120-086]that he would carry out his purpose. Suddenly, therefore, he prepared

[120-087]himself for action.

[120-088]He was in the habit of working with a Turkish cap on his head, and

[120-089]with a short apron tied round him. There was something picturesque

[120-090]about the cap, which might not have been incongruous with

[120-091]love-making. It is easy to suppose that Juan wore a Turkish cap when

[120-092]he sat with Haidee in Lambro's island. But we may be quite sure that

[120-093]he did not wear an apron. Now Dalrymple had thought of all this,

[120-094]and had made up his mind to work to-day without his apron; but when

[120-095]arranging his easel and his brushes, he had put it on from force of

[120-096]habit, and was now disgusted with himself as he remembered it. He put

[120-097]down his brush, divested his thumb of his palette, then took off his

[120-098]cap, and after that untied the apron.

[120-099]"Conway, what are you going to do?" said Mrs. Broughton.

[120-100]"I am going to ask Clara Van Siever to be my wife," said Dalrymple.

[120-101]At that moment the door was opened, and Mrs. Van Siever entered the

[120-102]room.

[120-103]Clara had not risen from her kneeling posture when Dalrymple began to

[120-104]put off his trappings. She had not seen what he was doing as plainly

[120-105]as Mrs. Broughton had done, having her attention naturally drawn

[120-106]towards her Sisera; and, besides this, she understood that she was

[120-107]to remain as she was placed till orders to move were given to her.

[120-108]Dalrymple would occasionally step aside from his easel to look at

[120-109]her in some altered light, and on such occasions she would simply

[120-110]hold her hammer somewhat more tightly than before. When, therefore,

[120-111]Mrs. Van Siever entered the room Clara was still slaying Sisera, in

[120-112]spite of the artist's speech. The speech, indeed, and her mother both

[120-113]seemed to come to her at the same time. The old woman stood for a

[120-114]moment holding the open door in her hand. "You fool!" she said, "what

[120-115]are you doing there, dressed up in that way like a guy?" Then Clara

[120-116]got up from her feet and stood before her mother in Jael's dress and

[120-117]Jael's turban. Dalrymple thought that the dress and turban did not

[120-118]become her badly. Mrs. Van Siever apparently thought otherwise. "Will

[120-119]you have the goodness to tell me, miss, why you are dressed up after

[120-120]that Mad Bess of Bedlam fashion?"

[120-121]The reader will no doubt bear in mind that Clara had other words

[120-122]of which to think besides those which were addressed to her by her

[120-123]mother. Dalrymple had asked her to be his wife in the plainest

[120-124]possible language, and she thought that the very plainness of the

[120-125]language became him well. The very taking off of his apron, almost

[120-126]as he said the words, though to himself the action had been so

[120-127]distressing as almost to overcome his purpose, had in it something

[120-128]to her of direct simple determination which pleased her. When he had

[120-129]spoken of having had a nail driven by her right through his heart,

[120-130]she had not been in the least gratified; but the taking off of the

[120-131]apron, and the putting down of the palette, and the downright way in

[120-132]which he had called her Clara Van Siever,--attempting to be neither

[120-133]sentimental with Clara, nor polite with Miss Van Siever,--did please

[120-134]her. She had often said to herself that she would never give a plain

[120-135]answer to a man who did not ask her a plain question;--to a man who,

[120-136]in asking this question, did not say plainly to her, "Clara Van

[120-137]Siever, will you become Mrs. Jones?"--or Mrs. Smith, or Mrs. Tomkins,

[120-138]as the case might be. Now Conway Dalrymple had asked her to become

[120-139]Mrs. Dalrymple very much after this fashion. In spite of the

[120-140]apparition of her mother, all this had passed through her mind. Not

[120-141]the less, however, was she obliged to answer her mother, before she

[120-142]could give any reply to the other questioner. In the meantime Mrs.

[120-143]Dobbs Broughton had untucked her feet.

[120-144]"Mamma," said Clara, "who ever expected to see you here?"

[120-145]"I daresay nobody did," said Mrs. Van Siever; "but here I am,

[120-146]nevertheless."

[120-147]"Madam," said Mrs. Dobbs Broughton, "you might at any rate have gone

[120-148]through the ceremony of having yourself announced by the servant."

[120-149]"Madam," said the old woman, attempting to mimic the tone of the

[120-150]other, "I thought that on such a very particular occasion as this I

[120-151]might be allowed to announce myself. You tomfool, you, why don't you

[120-152]take that turban off?" Then Clara, with slow and graceful motion,

[120-153]unwound the turban. If Dalrymple really meant what he had said, and

[120-154]would stick to it, she need not mind being called a tomfool by her

[120-155]mother.

[120-156]"Conway, I am afraid that our last sitting is disturbed," said Mrs.

[120-157]Broughton, with her little laugh.

[120-158]"Conway's last sitting certainly is disturbed," said Mrs. Van Siever,

[120-159]and then she mimicked the laugh. "And you'll all be disturbed,--I can

[120-160]tell you that. What an ass you must be to go on with this kind of

[120-161]thing, after what I said to you yesterday! Do you know that he got

[120-162]beastly drunk in the City last night, and that he is drunk now, while

[120-163]you are going on with your tomfooleries?" Upon hearing this, Mrs.

[120-164]Dobbs Broughton fainted into Dalrymple's arms.

[120-165]Hitherto the artist had not said a word, and had hardly known what

[120-166]part it would best become him now to play. If he intended to marry

[120-167]Clara,--and he certainly did intend to marry her if she would have

[120-168]him,--it might be as well not to quarrel with Mrs. Van Siever. At any

[120-169]rate there was nothing in Mrs. Van Siever's intrusion, disagreeable

[120-170]as it was, which need make him take up his sword to do battle with

[120-171]her. But now, as he held Mrs. Broughton in his arms, and as the

[120-172]horrid words which the old woman had spoken rung in his ears, he

[120-173]could not refrain himself from uttering reproach. "You ought not to

[120-174]have told her in this way, before other people, even if it be true,"

[120-175]said Conway.

[120-176]"Leave me to be my own judge of what I ought to do, if you please,

[120-177]sir. If she had any feeling at all, what I told her yesterday would

[120-178]have kept her from all this. But some people have no feeling, and

[120-179]will go on being tomfools though the house is on fire." As these

[120-180]words were spoken, Mrs. Broughton fainted more persistently than

[120-181]ever,--so that Dalrymple was convinced that whether she felt or not,

[120-182]at any rate she heard. He had now dragged her across the room, and

[120-183]laid her upon the sofa, and Clara had come to her assistance. "I

[120-184]daresay you think me very hard because I speak plainly, but there are

[120-185]things much harder than plain speaking. How much do you expect to be

[120-186]paid, sir, for this picture of my girl?"
