[124-001]"My decision has nothing to do with the jury's verdict. My

[124-002]decision--"

[124-003]"Stop a moment, Mr. Crawley. It is possible that you might say that

[124-004]which should not be said."

[124-005]"There is nothing to be said,--nothing which I could say, which

[124-006]I would not say at the town cross if it were possible. As to this

[124-007]money, I do not know whether I stole it or whether I did not."

[124-008]"That is just what I have thought."

[124-009]"It is so."

[124-010]"Then you did not steal it. There can be no doubt about that."

[124-011]"Thank you, Dr. Tempest. I thank you heartily for saying so much.

[124-012]But, sir, you are not the jury. Nor, if you were, could you whitewash

[124-013]me from the infamy which has been cast on me. Against the opinion

[124-014]expressed at the beginning of these proceedings by the bishop of

[124-015]the diocese,--or rather against that expressed by his wife,--I did

[124-016]venture to make a stand. Neither the opinion which came from the

[124-017]palace, nor the vehicle by which it was expressed, commanded my

[124-018]respect. Since that, others have spoken to whom I feel myself bound

[124-019]to yield;--yourself not the least among them, Dr. Tempest;--and to

[124-020]them I shall yield. You may tell the Bishop of Barchester that I

[124-021]shall at once resign the perpetual curacy of Hogglestock into the

[124-022]hands of the Dean of Barchester, by whom I was appointed."

[124-023]"No, Mr. Crawley; I shall not do that. I cannot control you, but

[124-024]thinking you to be wrong, I shall not make that communication to the

[124-025]bishop."

[124-026]"Then I shall do so myself."

[124-027]"And your wife, Mr. Crawley, and your children?"

[124-028]At that moment Mr. Crawley called to mind the advice of his friend

[124-029]Giles Hoggett. "It's dogged as does it." He certainly wanted

[124-030]something very strong to sustain him in his difficulty. He found that

[124-031]this reference to his wife and children required him to be dogged in

[124-032]a very marked manner. "I can only trust that the wind may be tempered

[124-033]to them," he said. "They will, indeed, be shorn lambs."

[124-034]Dr. Tempest got up from his chair, and took a couple of turns about

[124-035]the room before he spoke again. "Man," he said, addressing Mr.

[124-036]Crawley with all his energy, "if you do this thing, you will then

[124-037]at least be very wicked. If the jury find a verdict in your favour

[124-038]you are safe, and the chances are that the verdict will be in your

[124-039]favour."

[124-040]"I care nothing now for the verdict," said Mr. Crawley.

[124-041]"And you will turn your wife into the poorhouse for an idea!"

[124-042]"It's dogged as does it," said Mr. Crawley to himself. "I have

[124-043]thought of that," he said aloud. "That my wife is dear to me, and

[124-044]that my children are dear, I will not deny. She was softly nurtured,

[124-045]Dr. Tempest, and came from a house in which want was never known.

[124-046]Since she has shared my board she has had some experience of that

[124-047]nature. That I should have brought her to all this is very terrible

[124-048]to me,--so terrible, that I often wonder how it is that I live. But,

[124-049]sir, you will agree with me, that my duty as a clergyman is above

[124-050]everything. I do not dare, even for their sake, to remain in the

[124-051]parish. Good morning, Dr. Tempest." Dr. Tempest, finding that he

[124-052]could not prevail with him, bade him adieu, feeling that any service

[124-053]to the Crawleys within his power might be best done by intercession

[124-054]with the bishop and with the dean.

[124-055]Then Mr. Crawley walked back to Hogglestock, repeating to himself

[124-056]Giles Hoggett's words, "It's dogged as does it."

[124-057]CHAPTER LXII.

[124-058]MR. CRAWLEY'S LETTER TO THE DEAN.

[124-059][Illustration]

[124-060]Mr. Crawley, when he got home after his walk to Silverbridge, denied

[124-061]that he was at all tired. "The man at Silverbridge, whom I went to

[124-062]see administered refreshment to me;--nay, he administered it with

[124-063]salutary violence," he said, affecting even to laugh. "And I am

[124-064]bound to speak well of him on behalf of mercies over and beyond that

[124-065]exhibited by the persistent tender of some wine. That I should find

[124-066]him judicious I had expected. What little I have known of him taught

[124-067]me so to think of him. But I found with him also a softness of heart

[124-068]for which I had not looked."

[124-069]"And you will not give up the living, Josiah?"

[124-070]"Most certainly I will. A duty, when it is clear before a man, should

[124-071]never be made less so by any tenderness in others." He was still

[124-072]thinking of Giles Hoggett. "It's dogged as does it." The poor woman

[124-073]could not answer him. She knew well that it was vain to argue with

[124-074]him. She could only hope that in the event of his being acquitted

[124-075]at the trial, the dean, whose friendship she did not doubt, might

[124-076]re-endow him with the small benefice which was their only source of

[124-077]bread.

[124-078]On the following morning there came by post a short note from Dr.

[124-079]Tempest. "My dear Mr. Crawley," the note ran,

[124-080]I implore you, if there be yet time, to do nothing rashly.

[124-081]And even although you should have written to the bishop or

[124-082]to the dean, your letters need have no effect, if you will

[124-083]allow me to make them inoperative. Permit me to say that

[124-084]I am a man much older than you, and one who has mixed much

[124-085]both with clergymen and with the world at large. I tell

[124-086]you with absolute confidence, that it is not your duty in

[124-087]your present position to give up your living. Should your

[124-088]conduct ever be called in question on this matter you will

[124-089]be at perfect liberty to say that you were guided by my

[124-090]advice. You should take no step till after the trial.

[124-091]Then, if the verdict be against you, you should submit to

[124-092]the bishop's judgment. If the verdict be in your favour,

[124-093]the bishop's interference will be over.

[124-094]And you must remember that if it is not your duty as a

[124-095]clergyman to give up your living, you can have no right,

[124-096]seeing that you have a wife and family, to throw it away

[124-097]as an indulgence to your pride. Consult any other friend

[124-098]you please;--Mr. Robarts, or the dean himself. I am

[124-099]quite sure that any friend who knows as many of the

[124-100]circumstances as I know will advise you to hold the

[124-101]living, at any rate till after the trial. You can refer

[124-102]any such friend to me.

[124-103]Believe me to be, yours very truly,

[124-104]MORTIMER TEMPEST.

[124-105]Mr. Crawley walked about again with this letter in his pocket, but

[124-106]on this occasion he did not go in the direction of Hoggle End. From

[124-107]Hoggle End he could hardly hope to pick up further lessons of wisdom.

[124-108]What could any Giles Hoggett say to him beyond what he had said to

[124-109]him already? If he were to read the doctor's letter to Hoggett, and

[124-110]to succeed in making Hoggett understand it all, Hoggett could only

[124-111]caution him to be dogged. But it seemed to him that Hoggett and his

[124-112]new friend at Silverbridge did not agree in their doctrines, and it

[124-113]might be well that he should endeavour to find out which of them had

[124-114]most of justice on his side. He was quite sure that Hoggett would

[124-115]advise him to adhere to his project of giving up the living,--if only

[124-116]Hoggett could be made to understand the circumstances.

[124-117]He had written, but had not as yet sent away his letter to the dean.

[124-118]His letter to the bishop would be but a note, and he had postponed

[124-119]the writing of that till the other should be copied and made

[124-120]complete.

[124-121]He had sat up late into the night composing and altering his letter

[124-122]to his old friend, and now that the composition was finished he was

[124-123]loth to throw it away. Early in this morning, before the postman had

[124-124]brought to him Dr. Tempest's urgent remonstrance, he had shown to his

[124-125]wife the draught of his letter to the dean. "I cannot say that it is

[124-126]not true," she had said.

[124-127]"It is certainly true."

[124-128]"But I wish, dear, you would not send it. Why should you take any

[124-129]step till the trial be over?"

[124-130]"I shall assuredly send it," he had replied. "If you will peruse it

[124-131]again, you will see that the epistle would be futile were it kept

[124-132]till I shall have been proved to be a thief."

[124-133]"Oh, Josiah, such words kill me."

[124-134]"They are not pleasant, but it will be well that you should become

[124-135]used to them. As for the letter, I have taken some trouble to express

[124-136]myself with perspicuity, and I trust that I may have succeeded." At

[124-137]that time Hoggett was altogether in the ascendant; but now, as he

[124-138]started on his walk, his mind was somewhat perturbed by the contrary

[124-139]advice of one, who after all, might be as wise as Hoggett. There

[124-140]would be nothing dogged in the conduct recommended to him by Dr.

[124-141]Tempest. Were he to follow the doctor's advice, he would be trimming

[124-142]his sails, so as to catch any slant of a breeze that might be

[124-143]favourable to him. There could be no doggedness in a character that

[124-144]would submit to such trimming.

[124-145]The postman came to Hogglestock but once in a day, so that he could

[124-146]not despatch his letter till the next morning,--unless, indeed, he

[124-147]chose to send it a distance of four miles to the nearest post-office.

[124-148]As there was nothing to justify this, there was another night for

[124-149]the copying of his letter,--should he at last determine to send

[124-150]it. He had declared to Dr. Tempest that he would send it. He had

[124-151]sworn to his wife that it should go. He had taken much trouble with

[124-152]it. He believed in Hoggett. But, nevertheless, this incumbency of

[124-153]Hogglestock was his all in the world. It might be that he could still

[124-154]hold it, and have bread at least for his wife to eat. Dr. Tempest had

[124-155]told him that he would be probably acquitted. Dr. Tempest knew as

[124-156]much of all the circumstances as did he himself, and had told him

[124-157]that he was not guilty. After all Dr. Tempest knew more about it than

[124-158]Hoggett knew.

[124-159]If he resigned the living, what would become of him,--of him,--of him

[124-160]and of his wife? Whither would they first go when they turned their

[124-161]back upon the door inside which there had at any rate been shelter

[124-162]for them for many years? He calculated everything that he had, and

[124-163]found that at the end of April, even when he should have received his

[124-164]rent-charge, there would not be five pounds in hand among them. As

[124-165]for his furniture, he still owed enough to make it impossible that he

[124-166]should get anything out of that. And these thoughts all had reference

[124-167]to his position if he should be acquitted. What would become of his

[124-168]wife if he should be convicted? And as for himself, whither should he

[124-169]go when he came out of prison?

[124-170]He had completely realized the idea that Hoggett's counsel was

[124-171]opposed to that given to him by Dr. Tempest; but then it might

[124-172]certainly be the case that Hoggett had not known all the facts. A man

[124-173]should, no doubt, be dogged when the evils of life are insuperable;

[124-174]but need he be so when the evils can be overcome? Would not Hoggett

[124-175]himself undergo any treatment which he believed to be specific for

[124-176]rheumatism? Yes; Hoggett would undergo any treatment that was not in

[124-177]itself opposed to his duty. The best treatment for rheumatism might

[124-178]be to stay away from the brick-field on a rainy day; but if so,

[124-179]there would be no money to keep the pot boiling, and Hoggett would

[124-180]certainly go to the brick-field, rheumatism and all, as long as his

[124-181]limbs would carry him there. Yes; he would send his letter. It was

[124-182]his duty, and he would do it. Men looked askance at him, and pointed

[124-183]at him as a thief. He would send the letter, in spite of Dr. Tempest.

[124-184]Let justice be done, though the heaven may fall.
