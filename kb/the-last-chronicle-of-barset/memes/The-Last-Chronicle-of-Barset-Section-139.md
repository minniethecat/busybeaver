[139-001]"I cannot," she replied, speaking not in a whisper, but in words

[139-002]which would hardly get themselves articulated. "I cannot. Do not ask

[139-003]me."

[139-004]"For the honour of the Lord you will want the strength which bread

[139-005]alone can give you," he said, intimating to her that he wished her to

[139-006]attend the service.

[139-007]"Do not ask me to be there, Josiah. I cannot. It is too much for me."

[139-008]"Nay; I will not press it," he said. "I can go alone." He uttered no

[139-009]word expressive of a wish that his daughter should attend the church;

[139-010]but when the moment came, Jane accompanied him. "What shall I do,

[139-011]mamma," she said, "if I find I cannot bear it?" "Try to bear it," the

[139-012]mother said. "Try, for his sake. You are stronger now than I am."

[139-013]The tinkle of the church bell was heard at the usual time, and Mr.

[139-014]Crawley, hat in hand, stood ready to go forth. He had heard nothing

[139-015]of Mr. Thumble, but had made up his mind that Mr. Thumble would not

[139-016]trouble him. He had taken the precaution to request his churchwarden

[139-017]to be early at the church, so that Mr. Thumble might encounter no

[139-018]difficulty. The church was very near to the house, and any vehicle

[139-019]arriving might have been seen had Mr. Crawley watched closely. But no

[139-020]one had cared to watch Mr. Thumble's arrival at the church. He did

[139-021]not doubt that Mr. Thumble would be at the church. With reference to

[139-022]the school, he had had some doubt.

[139-023]But just as he was about to start he heard the clatter of a gig. Up

[139-024]came Mr. Thumble to the door of the parsonage, and having come down

[139-025]from his gig was about to enter the house as though it were his own.

[139-026]Mr. Crawley greeted him in the pathway, raising his hat from his

[139-027]head, and expressing a wish that Mr. Thumble might not feel himself

[139-028]fatigued with his drive. "I will not ask you into my poor house," he

[139-029]said, standing in the middle of the pathway; "for that my wife is

[139-030]ill."

[139-031]"Nothing catching, I hope?" said Mr. Thumble.

[139-032]"Her malady is of the spirit rather than of the flesh," said Mr.

[139-033]Crawley. "Shall we go on to the church?"

[139-034]"Certainly,--by all means. How about the surplice?"

[139-035]"You will find, I trust, that the churchwarden has everything in

[139-036]readiness. I have notified to him expressly your coming, with the

[139-037]purport that it may be so."

[139-038]"You'll take a part in the service, I suppose?" said Mr. Thumble.

[139-039]"No part,--no part whatever," said Mr. Crawley, standing still for a

[139-040]moment as he spoke, and showing plainly by the tone of his voice how

[139-041]dismayed he was, how indignant he had been made, by so indecent a

[139-042]proposition. Was he giving up his pulpit to a stranger for any reason

[139-043]less cogent than one which made it absolutely imperative on him to be

[139-044]silent in that church which had so long been his own?

[139-045]"Just as you please," said Mr. Thumble. "Only it's rather hard lines

[139-046]to have to do it all myself after coming all the way from Barchester

[139-047]this morning." To this Mr. Crawley condescended to make no reply

[139-048]whatever.

[139-049]In the porch of the church, which was the only entrance, Mr. Crawley

[139-050]introduced Mr. Thumble to the churchwarden, simply by a wave of the

[139-051]hand, and then passed on with his daughter to a seat which opened

[139-052]upon the aisle. Jane was going on to that which she had hitherto

[139-053]always occupied with her mother in the little chancel; but Mr.

[139-054]Crawley would not allow this. Neither to him nor to any of his family

[139-055]was there attached any longer the privilege of using the chancel of

[139-056]the church of Hogglestock.

[139-057]Mr. Thumble scrambled into the reading-desk some ten minutes after

[139-058]the proper time, and went through the morning service under, what

[139-059]must be admitted to be, serious difficulties. There were the eyes

[139-060]of Mr. Crawley fixed upon him throughout the work, and a feeling

[139-061]pervaded him that everybody there regarded him as an intruder. At

[139-062]first this was so strong upon him that Mr. Crawley pitied him, and

[139-063]would have encouraged him had it been possible. But as the work

[139-064]progressed, and as custom and the sound of his own voice emboldened

[139-065]him, there came to the man some touches of the arrogance which so

[139-066]generally accompanies cowardice, and Mr. Crawley's acute ear detected

[139-067]the moment when it was so. An observer might have seen that the

[139-068]motion of his hands was altered as they were lifted in prayer. Though

[139-069]he was praying, even in prayer he could not forget the man who was

[139-070]occupying his desk.

[139-071]Then came the sermon, preached very often before, lasting exactly

[139-072]half-an-hour, and then Mr. Thumble's work was done. Itinerant

[139-073]clergymen, who preach now here and now there, as it had been the lot

[139-074]of Mr. Thumble to do, have at any rate this relief,--that they can

[139-075]preach their sermons often. From the communion-table Mr. Thumble had

[139-076]stated that, in the present peculiar circumstances of the parish,

[139-077]there would be no second service at Hogglestock for the present; and

[139-078]this was all he said or did peculiar to the occasion. The moment

[139-079]the service was over he got into his gig, and was driven back to

[139-080]Barchester.

[139-081]"Mamma," said Jane, as they sat at their dinner, "such a sermon I am

[139-082]sure was never heard in Hogglestock before. Indeed, you can hardly

[139-083]call it a sermon. It was downright nonsense."

[139-084]"My dear," said Mr. Crawley, energetically, "keep your criticisms for

[139-085]matters that are profane; then, though they be childish and silly,

[139-086]they may at least be innocent. Be critical on Euripides, if you must

[139-087]be critical." But when Jane kissed her father after dinner, she,

[139-088]knowing his humour well, felt assured that her remarks had not been

[139-089]taken altogether in ill part.

[139-090]Mr. Thumble was neither seen nor heard of again in the parish during

[139-091]the entire week.

[139-092]CHAPTER LXX.

[139-093]MRS. ARABIN IS CAUGHT.

[139-094][Illustration]

[139-095]One morning about the middle of April Mr. Toogood received a telegram

[139-096]from Venice which caused him instantly to leave his business in

[139-097]Bedford Row and take the first train for Silverbridge. "It seems

[139-098]to me that this job will be a deal of time and very little money,"

[139-099]said his partner to him, when Toogood on the spur of the moment was

[139-100]making arrangements for his sudden departure and uncertain period

[139-101]of absence. "That's about it," said Toogood. "A deal of time, some

[139-102]expense, and no returns. It's not the kind of business a man can live

[139-103]upon; is it?" The partner growled, and Toogood went. But as we must

[139-104]go with Mr. Toogood down to Silverbridge, and as we cannot make the

[139-105]journey in this chapter, we will just indicate his departure and then

[139-106]go back to John Eames, who, as will be remembered, was just starting

[139-107]for Florence when we last saw him.

[139-108]Our dear old friend Johnny had been rather proud of himself as he

[139-109]started from London. He had gotten an absolute victory over Sir

[139-110]Raffle Buffle, and that alone was gratifying to his feelings. He

[139-111]liked the excitement of a journey, and especially of a journey

[139-112]to Italy; and the importance of the cause of his journey was

[139-113]satisfactory to him. But above all things he was delighted at having

[139-114]found that Lily Dale was pleased at his going. He had seen clearly

[139-115]that she was much pleased, and that she made something of a hero

[139-116]of him because of his alacrity in the cause of his cousin. He had

[139-117]partially understood,--had understood in a dim sort of way,--that

[139-118]his want of favour in Lily's eyes had come from some deficiency of

[139-119]his own in this respect. She had not found him to be a hero. She had

[139-120]known him first as a boy, with boyish belongings around him, and she

[139-121]had seen him from time to time as he became a man, almost with too

[139-122]much intimacy for the creation of that love with which he wished to

[139-123]fill her heart. His rival had come before her eyes for the first time

[139-124]with all the glories of Pall Mall heroism about him, and Lily in her

[139-125]weakness had been conquered by them. Since that she had learned how

[139-126]weak she had been,--how silly, how childish, she would say to herself

[139-127]when she allowed her memory to go back to the details of her own

[139-128]story; but not the less on that account did she feel the want of

[139-129]something heroic in a man before she could teach herself to look

[139-130]upon him as more worthy of her regard than other men. She had still

[139-131]unconsciously hoped in regard to Crosbie, but now that hope had been

[139-132]dispelled as unconsciously, simply by his appearance. There had

[139-133]been moments in which John Eames had almost risen to the necessary

[139-134]point,--had almost made good his footing on the top of some moderate,

[139-135]but still sufficient mountain. But there had still been a succession

[139-136]of little tumbles,--unfortunate slips for which he himself should

[139-137]not always have been held responsible; and he had never quite stood

[139-138]upright on his pinnacle, visible to Lily's eyes as being really

[139-139]excelsior. Of all this John Eames himself had an inkling which had

[139-140]often made him very uncomfortable. What the mischief was it she

[139-141]wanted of him; and what was he to do? The days for plucking glory

[139-142]from the nettle danger were clean gone by. He was well dressed. He

[139-143]knew a good many of the right sort of people. He was not in debt. He

[139-144]had saved an old nobleman's life once upon a time, and had been a

[139-145]good deal talked about on that score. He had even thrashed the man

[139-146]who had ill-treated her. His constancy had been as the constancy of

[139-147]a Jacob! What was it that she wanted of him? But in a certain way

[139-148]he did know what was wanted; and now, as he started for Florence,

[139-149]intending to stop nowhere till he reached that city, he hoped that

[139-150]by this chivalrous journey he might even yet achieve the thing

[139-151]necessary.

[139-152]But on reaching Paris he heard tidings of Mrs. Arabin which induced

[139-153]him to change his plans and make for Venice instead of for Florence.

[139-154]A banker at Paris, to whom he brought a letter, told him that Mrs.

[139-155]Arabin would now be found at Venice. This did not perplex him at

[139-156]all. It would have been delightful to see Florence,--but was more

[139-157]delightful still to see Venice. His journey was the same as far as

[139-158]Turin; but from Turin he proceeded through Milan to Venice, instead

[139-159]of going by Bologna to Florence. He had fortunately come armed with

[139-160]an Austrian passport,--as was necessary in those bygone days of

[139-161]Venetia's thraldom. He was almost proud of himself, as though he

[139-162]had done something great, when he tumbled in to his inn at Venice,

[139-163]without having been in a bed since he left London.

[139-164]But he was barely allowed to swim in a gondola, for on reaching

[139-165]Venice he found that Mrs. Arabin had gone back to Florence. He had

[139-166]been directed to the hotel which Mrs. Arabin had used, and was there

[139-167]told that she had started the day before. She had received some

[139-168]letter, from her husband as the landlord thought, and had done so.

[139-169]That was all the landlord knew. Johnny was vexed, but became a little

[139-170]prouder than before as he felt it to be his duty to go on to Florence

[139-171]before he went to bed. There would be another night in a railway

[139-172]carriage, but he would live through it. There was just time to have

[139-173]a tub and a breakfast, to swim in a gondola, to look at the outside

[139-174]of the Doge's palace, and to walk up and down the piazza before he

[139-175]started again. It was hard work, but I think he would have been

[139-176]pleased had he heard that Mrs. Arabin had retreated from Florence to

[139-177]Rome. Had such been the case, he would have folded his cloak around

[139-178]him, and have gone on,--regardless of brigands,--thinking of Lily,

[139-179]and wondering whether anybody else had ever done so much before

[139-180]without going to bed. As it was, he found that Mrs. Arabin was at

[139-181]the hotel in Florence,--still in bed, as he had arrived early in the

[139-182]morning. So he had another tub, another breakfast, and sent up his

[139-183]card. "Mr. John Eames,"--and across the top of it he wrote, "has come

[139-184]from England about Mr. Crawley." Then he threw himself on to a sofa

[139-185]in the hotel reading-room, and went fast to sleep.
