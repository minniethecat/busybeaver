[050-001]Croquet is a pretty game out of doors, and chess is delightful in a

[050-002]drawing-room. Battledoor and shuttlecock and hunt-the-slipper have

[050-003]also their attractions. Proverbs are good, and cross questions with

[050-004]crooked answers may be made very amusing. But none of these games are

[050-005]equal to the game of love-making,--providing that the players can

[050-006]be quite sure that there shall be no heart in the matter. Any touch

[050-007]of heart not only destroys the pleasure of the game, but makes the

[050-008]player awkward and incapable and robs him of his skill. And thus it

[050-009]is that there are many people who cannot play the game at all. A

[050-010]deficiency of some needed internal physical strength prevents the

[050-011]owners of the heart from keeping a proper control over its valves,

[050-012]and thus emotion sets in, and the pulses are accelerated, and feeling

[050-013]supervenes. For such a one to attempt a game of love-making, is as

[050-014]though your friend with the gout should insist on playing croquet. A

[050-015]sense of the ridiculous, if nothing else, should in either case deter

[050-016]the afflicted one from the attempt. There was no such absurdity with

[050-017]our friend Mrs. Dobbs Broughton and Conway Dalrymple. Their valves

[050-018]and pulses were all right. They could play the game without the

[050-019]slightest danger of any inconvenient result;--of any inconvenient

[050-020]result, that is, as regarded their own feelings. Blind people cannot

[050-021]see and stupid people cannot understand,--and it might be that Mr.

[050-022]Dobbs Broughton, being both blind and stupid in such matters, might

[050-023]perceive something of the playing of the game and not know that it

[050-024]was only a game of skill.

[050-025]When I say that as regarded these two lovers there was nothing of

[050-026]love between them, and that the game was therefore so far innocent, I

[050-027]would not be understood as asserting that these people had no hearts

[050-028]within their bosoms. Mrs. Dobbs Broughton probably loved her husband

[050-029]in a sensible, humdrum way, feeling him to be a bore, knowing him to

[050-030]be vulgar, aware that he often took a good deal more wine than was

[050-031]good for him, and that he was almost as uneducated as a hog. Yet she

[050-032]loved him, and showed her love by taking care that he should have

[050-033]things for dinner which he liked to eat. But in this alone there were

[050-034]to be found none of the charms of a fevered existence, and therefore

[050-035]Mrs. Dobbs Broughton, requiring those charms for her comfort, played

[050-036]her little game with Conway Dalrymple. And as regarded the artist

[050-037]himself, let no reader presume him to have been heartless because he

[050-038]flirted with Mrs. Dobbs Broughton. Doubtless he will marry some day,

[050-039]will have a large family for which he will work hard, and will make a

[050-040]good husband to some stout lady who will be careful in looking after

[050-041]his linen. But on the present occasion he fell into some slight

[050-042]trouble in spite of the innocence of his game. As he quitted his

[050-043]friend's room he heard the hall-door slammed heavily; then there was

[050-044]a quick step on the stairs, and on the landing-place above the first

[050-045]flight he met the master of the house, somewhat flurried, as it

[050-046]seemed, and not looking comfortable, either as regarded his person or

[050-047]his temper. "By George, he's been drinking!" Conway said to himself,

[050-048]after the first glance. Now it certainly was the case that poor Dobbs

[050-049]Broughton would sometimes drink at improper hours.

[050-050]"What the devil are you doing here?" said Dobbs Broughton to his

[050-051]friend the artist. "You're always here. You're here a doosed sight

[050-052]more than I like." Husbands when they have been drinking are very apt

[050-053]to make mistakes as to the purport of the game.

[050-054]"Why, Dobbs," said the painter, "there's something wrong with you."

[050-055]"No, there ain't. There's nothing wrong; and if there was, what's

[050-056]that to you? I shan't ask you to pay anything for me, I suppose."

[050-057]"Well;--I hope not."

[050-058]"I won't have you here, and let that be an end of it. It's all very

[050-059]well when I choose to have a few friends to dinner, but my wife can

[050-060]do very well without your fal-lalling here all day. Will you remember

[050-061]that, if you please?"

[050-062]Conway Dalrymple, knowing that he had better not argue any question

[050-063]with a drunken man, took himself out of the house, shrugging his

[050-064]shoulders as he thought of the misery which his poor dear playfellow

[050-065]would now be called upon to endure.

[050-066]CHAPTER XXVII.

[050-067]A HERO AT HOME.

[050-068]On the morning after his visit to Miss Demolines John Eames found

[050-069]himself at the Paddington Station asking for a ticket for Guestwick,

[050-070]and as he picked up his change another gentleman also demanded

[050-071]a ticket for the same place. Had Guestwick been as Liverpool or

[050-072]Manchester, Eames would have thought nothing about it. It is a matter

[050-073]of course that men should always be going from London to Liverpool

[050-074]and Manchester; but it seemed odd to him that two men should want

[050-075]first-class tickets for so small a place as Guestwick at the same

[050-076]moment. And when, afterwards, he was placed by the guard in the

[050-077]same carriage with this other traveller, he could not but feel

[050-078]some little curiosity. The man was four or five years Johnny's

[050-079]senior, a good-looking fellow, with a pleasant face, and the outward

[050-080]appurtenances of a gentleman. The intelligent reader will no doubt

[050-081]be aware that the stranger was Major Grantly; but the intelligent

[050-082]reader has in this respect had much advantage over John Eames, who

[050-083]up to this time had never even heard of his cousin Grace Crawley's

[050-084]lover. "I think you were asking for a ticket for Guestwick," said

[050-085]Johnny;--whereupon the major owned that such was the case. "I lived

[050-086]at Guestwick the greater part of my life," said Johnny, "and it's

[050-087]the dullest, dearest little town in all England." "I never was

[050-088]there before," said the major, "and indeed I can hardly say I am

[050-089]going there now. I shall only pass through it." Then he got out his

[050-090]newspaper, and Johnny also got out his, and for a time there was no

[050-091]conversation between them. John remembered how holy was the errand

[050-092]upon which he was intent, and gathered his thoughts together,

[050-093]resolving that having so great a matter on his mind he would think

[050-094]about nothing else and speak about nothing at all. He was going

[050-095]down to Allington to ask Lily Dale for the last time whether she

[050-096]would be his wife; to ascertain whether he was to be successful or

[050-097]unsuccessful in the one great wish of his life; and, as such was

[050-098]the case with him,--as he had in hand a thing so vital, it could

[050-099]be nothing to him whether the chance companion of his voyage was

[050-100]an agreeable or a disagreeable person. He himself, in any of the

[050-101]ordinary circumstances of life, was prone enough to talk with any

[050-102]one he might meet. He could have travelled for twelve hours together

[050-103]with an old lady, and could listen to her or make her listen to him

[050-104]without half an hour's interruption. But this journey was made on no

[050-105]ordinary occasion, and it behoved him to think of Lily. Therefore,

[050-106]after the first little almost necessary effort at civility, he fell

[050-107]back into gloomy silence. He was going to do his best to win Lily

[050-108]Dale, and this doing of his best would require all his thought and

[050-109]all his energy.

[050-110]And probably Major Grantly's mind was bent in the same direction. He,

[050-111]too, had this work before him, and could not look upon his work as a

[050-112]thing that was altogether pleasant. He might probably get that which

[050-113]he was intent upon obtaining. He knew,--he almost knew,--that he had

[050-114]won the heart of the girl whom he was seeking. There had been that

[050-115]between him and her which justified him in supposing that he was dear

[050-116]to her, although no expression of affection had ever passed from her

[050-117]lips to his ears. Men may know all that they require to know on that

[050-118]subject without any plainly spoken words. Grace Crawley had spoken

[050-119]no word, and yet he had known,--at any rate had not doubted, that

[050-120]he could have the place in her heart of which he desired to be the

[050-121]master. She would never surrender herself altogether till she had

[050-122]taught herself to be sure of him to whom she gave herself. But she

[050-123]had listened to him with silence that had not rebuked him, and he had

[050-124]told himself that he might venture, without fear of that rebuke as

[050-125]to which the minds of some men are sensitive to a degree which other

[050-126]men cannot even understand. But for all this Major Grantly could not

[050-127]be altogether happy as to his mission. He would ask Grace Crawley

[050-128]to be his wife; but he would be ruined by his own success. And the

[050-129]remembrance that he would be severed from all his own family by the

[050-130]thing that he was doing, was very bitter to him. In generosity he

[050-131]might be silent about this to Grace, but who can endure to be silent

[050-132]on such a subject to the woman who is to be his wife? And then it

[050-133]would not be possible for him to abstain from explanation. He was now

[050-134]following her down to Allington, a step which he certainly would not

[050-135]have taken but for the misfortune which had befallen her father, and

[050-136]he must explain to her in some sort why he did so. He must say to

[050-137]her,--if not in so many words, still almost as plainly as words could

[050-138]speak,--I am here now to ask you to be my wife, because you specially

[050-139]require the protection and countenance of the man who loves you, in

[050-140]the present circumstances of your father's affairs. He knew that he

[050-141]was doing right;--perhaps had some idea that he was doing nobly; but

[050-142]this very appreciation of his own good qualities made the task before

[050-143]him the more difficult.

[050-144]Major Grantly had The Times, and John Eames had the Daily News, and

[050-145]they exchanged papers. One had the last Saturday, and the other the

[050-146]last Spectator, and they exchanged those also. Both had the Pall Mall

[050-147]Gazette, of which enterprising periodical they gradually came to

[050-148]discuss the merits and demerits, thus falling into conversation at

[050-149]last, in spite of the weight of the mission on which each of them was

[050-150]intent. Then, at last, when they were within half-an-hour of the end

[050-151]of their journey, Major Grantly asked his companion what was the best

[050-152]inn at Guestwick. He had at first been minded to go on to Allington

[050-153]at once,--to go on to Allington and get his work done, and then

[050-154]return home or remain there, or find the nearest inn with a decent

[050-155]bed, as circumstances might direct him. But on reconsideration, as he

[050-156]drew nearer to the scene of his future operations, he thought that

[050-157]it might be well for him to remain that night at Guestwick. He did

[050-158]not quite know how far Allington was from Guestwick, but he did know

[050-159]that it was still mid-winter, and that the days were very short. "The

[050-160]Magpie" was the best inn, Johnny said. Having lived at Guestwick all

[050-161]his life, and having a mother living there now, he had never himself

[050-162]put up at "The Magpie," but he believed it to be a good country inn.

[050-163]They kept post-horses there, he knew. He did not tell the stranger

[050-164]that his late old friend, Lord De Guest, and his present old friend,

[050-165]Lady Julia, always hired post-horses from "The Magpie," but he

[050-166]grounded his ready assertion on the remembrance of that fact. "I

[050-167]think I shall stay there to-night," said the major. "You'll find it

[050-168]pretty comfortable, I don't doubt," said Johnny. "Though, indeed, it

[050-169]always seems to me that a man alone at an inn has a very bad time of

[050-170]it. Reading is all very well, but one gets tired of it at last. And

[050-171]then I hate horse-hair chairs." "It isn't very delightful," said the

[050-172]major, "but beggars mustn't be choosers." Then there was a pause,

[050-173]after which the major spoke again. "You don't happen to know which

[050-174]way Allington lies?"
