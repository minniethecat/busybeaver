[101-001]"You have obtained it."

[101-002]"I am in the way to do so. Perhaps one out of ten struggling artists

[101-003]is successful, and for him the profession is very charming. It is

[101-004]certainly a sad feeling that there is so much of chance in the

[101-005]distribution of the prizes. It is a lottery. But one cannot complain

[101-006]of that when one has drawn the prize." Dalrymple was not a man

[101-007]without self-possession, nor was he readily abashed, but he found it

[101-008]easier to talk of his possession than to make his offer. The turban

[101-009]was his difficulty. He had told himself over and over again within

[101-010]the last five minutes, that he would have long since said what he

[101-011]had to say had it not been for the turban. He had been painting all

[101-012]his life from living models,--from women dressed up in this or that

[101-013]costume, to suit the necessities of his picture,--but he had never

[101-014]made love to any of them. They had been simply models to him, and now

[101-015]he found that there was a difficulty. "Of that prize," he said, "I

[101-016]have made myself tolerably sure; but as to the other prize, I do not

[101-017]know. I wonder whether I am to have that." Of course Miss Van Siever

[101-018]understood well what was the prize of which he was speaking; and as

[101-019]she was a young woman with a will and purpose of her own, no doubt

[101-020]she was already prepared with an answer. But it was necessary that

[101-021]the question should be put to her in properly distinct terms. Conway

[101-022]Dalrymple certainly had not put his question in properly distinct

[101-023]terms at present. She did not choose to make any answer to his last

[101-024]words; and therefore simply suggested that as time was pressing he

[101-025]had better go on with his work. "I am quite ready now," said she.

[101-026]"Stop half a moment. How much more you are thinking of the picture

[101-027]than I am! I do not care twopence for the picture. I will slit the

[101-028]canvas from top to bottom without a groan,--without a single inner

[101-029]groan,--if you will let me."

[101-030]"For heaven's sake do nothing of the kind! Why should you?"

[101-031]"Just to show you that it is not for the sake of the picture that

[101-032]I come here. Clara--" Then the door was opened, and Isaac appeared,

[101-033]very weary, having been piling fagots with assiduity, till human

[101-034]nature could pile no more. Conway Dalrymple, who had made his way

[101-035]almost up to Clara's seat, turned round sharply towards his easel, in

[101-036]anger at having been disturbed. He should have been more grateful for

[101-037]all that his Isaac had done for him, and have recognized the fact

[101-038]that the fault had been with himself. Mrs. Broughton had been twelve

[101-039]minutes out of the room. She had counted them to be fifteen,--having

[101-040]no doubt made a mistake as to three,--and had told herself that

[101-041]with such a one as Conway Dalrymple, with so much of the work ready

[101-042]done to his hand for him, fifteen minutes should have been amply

[101-043]sufficient. When we reflect what her own thoughts must have been

[101-044]during the interval,--what it is to have to pile up such fagots as

[101-045]those, how she was, as it were, giving away a fresh morsel of her own

[101-046]heart during each minute that she allowed Clara and Conway Dalrymple

[101-047]to remain together, it cannot surprise us that her eyes should have

[101-048]become dizzy, and that she should not have counted the minutes with

[101-049]accurate correctness. Dalrymple turned to his picture angrily, but

[101-050]Miss Van Siever kept her seat and did not show the slightest emotion.

[101-051]"My friends," said Mrs. Broughton, "this will not do. This is not

[101-052]working; this is not sitting."

[101-053]"Mr. Dalrymple has been explaining to me the precarious nature of an

[101-054]artist's profession," said Clara.

[101-055]"It is not precarious with him," said Mrs. Dobbs Broughton,

[101-056]sententiously.

[101-057]"Not in a general way, perhaps; but to prove the truth of his words

[101-058]he was going to treat Jael worse than Jael treats Sisera."

[101-059]"I was going to slit the picture from the top to the bottom."

[101-060]"And why?" said Mrs. Broughton, putting up her hands to heaven in

[101-061]tragic horror.

[101-062]"Just to show Miss Van Siever how little I care about it."

[101-063]"And how little you care about her, too," said Mrs. Broughton.

[101-064]"She might take that as she liked." After this there was another

[101-065]genuine sitting, and the real work went on as though there had been

[101-066]no episode. Jael fixed her face, and held her hammer as though her

[101-067]mind and heart were solely bent on seeming to be slaying Sisera.

[101-068]Dalrymple turned his eyes from the canvas to the model, and from the

[101-069]model to the canvas, working with his hand all the while, as though

[101-070]that last pathetic "Clara" had never been uttered; and Mrs. Dobbs

[101-071]Broughton reclined on a sofa, looking at them and thinking of her own

[101-072]singularly romantic position, till her mind was filled with a poetic

[101-073]frenzy. In one moment she resolved that she would hate Clara as

[101-074]woman was never hated by woman; and then there were daggers, and

[101-075]poison-cups, and strangling cords in her eye. In the next she was as

[101-076]firmly determined that she would love Mrs. Conway Dalrymple as woman

[101-077]never was loved by woman; and then she saw herself kneeling by a

[101-078]cradle, and tenderly nursing a baby, of which Conway was to be the

[101-079]father and Clara the mother. And so she went to sleep.

[101-080]For some time Dalrymple did not observe this; but at last there was

[101-081]a little sound,--even the ill-nature of Miss Demolines could hardly

[101-082]have called it a snore,--and he became aware that for practical

[101-083]purposes he and Miss Van Siever were again alone together. "Clara,"

[101-084]he said, in a whisper. Mrs. Broughton instantly aroused herself from

[101-085]her slumbers, and rubbed her eyes. "Dear, dear, dear," she said, "I

[101-086]declare it's past one. I'm afraid I must turn you both out. One more

[101-087]sitting, I suppose, will finish it, Conway?"

[101-088]"Yes, one more," said he. It was always understood that he and Clara

[101-089]should not leave the house together, and therefore he remained

[101-090]painting when she left the room. "And now, Conway," said Mrs.

[101-091]Broughton, "I suppose that all is over?"

[101-092]"I don't know what you mean by all being over."

[101-093]"No,--of course not. You look at it in another light, no doubt.

[101-094]Everything is beginning for you. But you must pardon me, for my heart

[101-095]is distracted,--distracted,--distracted!" Then she sat down upon the

[101-096]floor, and burst into tears. What was he to do? He thought that the

[101-097]woman should either give him up altogether, or not give him up. All

[101-098]this fuss about it was irrational! He would not have made love to

[101-099]Clara Van Siever in her room if she had not told him to do so!

[101-100]"Maria," he said, in a very grave voice, "any sacrifice that is

[101-101]required on my part on your behalf I am ready to make."

[101-102]"No, sir; the sacrifices shall all be made by me. It is the part of

[101-103]a woman to be ever sacrificial!" Poor Mrs. Dobbs Broughton! "You

[101-104]shall give up nothing. The world is at your feet, and you shall

[101-105]have everything,--youth, beauty, wealth, station, love,--love; and

[101-106]friendship also, if you will accept it from one so poor, so broken,

[101-107]so secluded as I shall be." At each of the last words there had been

[101-108]a desperate sob; and as she was still crouching in the middle of the

[101-109]room, looking up into Dalrymple's face while he stood over her, the

[101-110]scene was one which had much in it that transcended the doings of

[101-111]everyday life, much that would be ever memorable, and much, I have

[101-112]no doubt, that was thoroughly enjoyed by the principal actor. As for

[101-113]Conway Dalrymple, he was so second-rate a personage in the whole

[101-114]thing, that it mattered little whether he enjoyed it or not. I don't

[101-115]think he did enjoy it. "And now, Conway," she said, "I will give

[101-116]you some advice. And when in after-days you shall remember this

[101-117]interview, and reflect how that advice was given you,--with what

[101-118]solemnity,"--here she clasped both her hands together,--"I think that

[101-119]you will follow it. Clara Van Siever will now become your wife."

[101-120]"I do not know that at all," said Dalrymple.

[101-121]"Clara Van Siever will now become your wife," repeated Mrs. Broughton

[101-122]in a louder voice, impatient of opposition. "Love her. Cleave to her.

[101-123]Make her flesh of your flesh and bone of your bone. But rule her!

[101-124]Yes, rule her! Let her be your second self, but not your first self.

[101-125]Rule her. Love her. Cleave to her. Do not leave her alone, to feed on

[101-126]her own thoughts as I have done,--as I have been forced to do. Now

[101-127]go. No, Conway, not a word; I will not hear a word. You must go, or

[101-128]I must." Then she rose quickly from her lowly attitude, and prepared

[101-129]herself for a dart at the door. It was better by far that he should

[101-130]go, and so he went.

[101-131]An American when he has spent a pleasant day will tell you that he

[101-132]has had "a good time." I think that Mrs. Dobbs Broughton, if she

[101-133]had ever spoken the truth of that day's employment, would have

[101-134]acknowledged that she had had "a good time." I think that she enjoyed

[101-135]her morning's work. But as for Conway Dalrymple, I doubt whether he

[101-136]did enjoy his morning's work. "A man may have too much of this sort

[101-137]of thing, and then he becomes very sick of his cake." Such was the

[101-138]nature of his thoughts as he returned to his own abode.

[101-139]CHAPTER LII.

[101-140]WHY DON'T YOU HAVE AN "IT" FOR YOURSELF?

[101-141]Of course it came to pass that Lily Dale and Emily Dunstable were

[101-142]soon very intimate, and that they saw each other every day. Indeed,

[101-143]before long they would have been living together in the same house

[101-144]had it not been that the squire had felt reluctant to abandon the

[101-145]independence of his own lodgings. When Mrs. Thorne had pressed her

[101-146]invitation for the second, and then for the third time, asking them

[101-147]both to come to her large house, he had begged his niece to go and

[101-148]leave him alone. "You need not regard me," he had said, speaking

[101-149]not with the whining voice of complaint, but with that thin tinge

[101-150]of melancholy which was usual to him. "I am so much alone down at

[101-151]Allington, that you need not mind leaving me." But Lily would not

[101-152]go on those terms, and therefore they still lived together in the

[101-153]lodgings. Nevertheless Lily was every day at Mrs. Thorne's house, and

[101-154]thus a great intimacy grew up between the girls. Emily Dunstable had

[101-155]neither brother nor sister, and Lily's nearest male relative in her

[101-156]own degree was now Miss Dunstable's betrothed husband. It was natural

[101-157]therefore that they should at any rate try to like each other. It

[101-158]afterwards came to pass that Lily did go to Mrs. Thorne's house, and

[101-159]she stayed there for awhile; but when that occurred the squire had

[101-160]gone back to Allington.

[101-161]Among other generous kindnesses Mrs. Thorne insisted that Bernard

[101-162]should hire a horse for his cousin Lily. Emily Dunstable rode daily,

[101-163]and of course Captain Dale rode with her;--and now Lily joined the

[101-164]party. Almost before she knew what was being done she found herself

[101-165]provided with hat and habit and horse and whip. It was a way with

[101-166]Mrs. Thorne that they who came within the influence of her immediate

[101-167]sphere should be made to feel that the comforts and luxuries arising

[101-168]from her wealth belonged to a common stock, and were the joint

[101-169]property of them all. Things were not offered and taken and talked

[101-170]about, but they made their appearance, and were used as a matter of

[101-171]course. If you go to stay at a gentleman's house you understand that,

[101-172]as a matter of course, you will be provided with meat and drink. Some

[101-173]hosts furnish you also with cigars. A small number give you stabling

[101-174]and forage for your horse; and a very select few mount you on

[101-175]hunting days, and send you out with a groom and a second horse.

[101-176]Mrs. Thorne went beyond all others in this open-handed hospitality.

[101-177]She had enormous wealth at her command, and had but few of those

[101-178]all-absorbing drains upon wealth which in this country make so many

[101-179]rich men poor. She had no family property,--no place to keep up in

[101-180]which she did not live. She had no retainers to be maintained because

[101-181]they were retainers. She had neither sons nor daughters. Consequently

[101-182]she was able to be lavish in her generosity; and as her heart was

[101-183]very lavish, she would have given her friends gold to eat had

[101-184]gold been good for eating. Indeed there was no measure in her

[101-185]giving,--unless when the idea came upon her that the recipient of

[101-186]her favours was trading on them. Then she could hold her hand very

[101-187]stoutly.
