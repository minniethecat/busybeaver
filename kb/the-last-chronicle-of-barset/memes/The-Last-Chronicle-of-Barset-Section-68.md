[068-001]Most affectionately your own,

[068-002]HENRY GRANTLY.

[068-003]When the letter was given to Grace across the breakfast-table, both

[068-004]Mrs. Dale and Lily suspected that it came from Major Grantly, but not

[068-005]a word was spoken about it. When Grace with hesitating hand broke the

[068-006]envelope, neither of her friends looked at her. Lily had a letter

[068-007]of her own, and Mrs. Dale opened the newspaper. But still it was

[068-008]impossible not to perceive that her face became red with blushes,

[068-009]and then they knew that the letter must be from Major Grantly. Grace

[068-010]herself could not read it, though her eye ran down over the two pages

[068-011]catching a word here and a word there. She had looked at the name at

[068-012]once, and had seen the manner of his signature. "Most affectionately

[068-013]your own!" What was she to say to him? Twice, thrice, as she sat at

[068-014]the breakfast-table she turned the page of the letter, and at each

[068-015]turning she read the signature. And she read the beginning, "Dearest

[068-016]Grace." More than that she did not really read till she had got the

[068-017]letter away with her into the seclusion of her own room.

[Illustration: She read the beginning--"Dearest Grace."]

[068-018]Not a word was said about the letter at breakfast. Poor Grace went on

[068-019]eating or pretending to eat, but could not bring herself to utter a

[068-020]word. Mrs. Dale and Lily spoke of various matters, which were quite

[068-021]indifferent to them; but even with them the conversation was so

[068-022]difficult that Grace felt it to be forced, and was conscious that

[068-023]they were thinking about her and her lover. As soon as she could make

[068-024]an excuse she left the room, and hurrying upstairs took the letter

[068-025]from her pocket and read it in earnest.

[068-026]"That was from Major Grantly, mamma," said Lily.

[068-027]"I daresay it was, my dear."

[068-028]"And what had we better do; or what had we better say?"

[068-029]"Nothing,--I should say. Let him fight his own battle. If we

[068-030]interfere, we may probably only make her more stubborn in clinging to

[068-031]her old idea."

[068-032]"I think she will cling to it."

[068-033]"For a time she will, I daresay. And it will be best that she should.

[068-034]He himself will respect her for it afterwards." Thus it was agreed

[068-035]between them that they should say nothing to Grace about the letter

[068-036]unless Grace should first speak to them.

[068-037]Grace read her letter over and over again. It was the first

[068-038]love-letter she had ever had;--the first letter she had ever received

[068-039]from any man except her father and brother,--the first, almost, that

[068-040]had ever been written to her by any other than her own old special

[068-041]friends. The words of it were very strange to her ear. He had told

[068-042]her when he left her that he would write to her, and therefore she

[068-043]had looked forward to the event which had now come; but she had

[068-044]thought that it would be much more distant,--and she had tried to

[068-045]make herself believe that when it did come it would be very different

[068-046]from this letter which she now possessed. "He will tell me that he

[068-047]has altered his mind. He ought to do so. It is not proper that he

[068-048]should still think of me when we are in such disgrace." But now the

[068-049]letter had come, and she acknowledged the truth of his saying that

[068-050]written words were clearer in their expression than those simply

[068-051]spoken. "Not that I could ever forget a syllable that he said." Yet,

[068-052]as she held the letter in her hand she felt that it was a possession.

[068-053]It was a thing at which she could look in coming years, when he and

[068-054]she might be far apart,--a thing at which she could look with pride

[068-055]in remembering that he had thought her worthy of it.

[068-056]Neither on that day nor on the next did she think of her answer, nor

[068-057]on the third or the fourth with any steady thinking. She knew that an

[068-058]answer would have to be written, and she felt that the sooner it was

[068-059]written the easier might be the writing; but she felt also that it

[068-060]should not be written too quickly. A week should first elapse, she

[068-061]thought, and therefore a week was allowed to elapse, and then the day

[068-062]for writing her answer came. She had spoken no word about it either

[068-063]to Mrs. Dale or to Lily. She had longed to do so, but had feared.

[068-064]Even though she should speak to Lily she could not be led by Lily's

[068-065]advice. Her letter, whatever it might be, must be her own letter. She

[068-066]would admit of no dictation. She must say her own say, let her say it

[068-067]ever so badly. As to the manner of saying it, Lily's aid would have

[068-068]been invaluable; but she feared that she could not secure that aid

[068-069]without compromising her own power of action,--her own individuality;

[068-070]and therefore she said no word about the letter either to Lily or to

[068-071]Lily's mother.

[068-072]On a certain morning she fixed herself at her desk to write her

[068-073]letter. She had known that the task would be difficult, but she had

[068-074]little known how difficult it would be. On that day of her first

[068-075]attempt she did not get it written at all. How was she to begin? He

[068-076]had called her "Dearest Grace;" and this mode of beginning seemed as

[068-077]easy as it was sweet. "It is very easy for a gentleman," she said to

[068-078]herself, "because he may say just what he pleases." She wrote the

[068-079]words, "Dearest Henry," on a scrap of paper, and immediately tore it

[068-080]into fragments as though she were ashamed of having written them. She

[068-081]knew that she would not dare to send away a letter beginning with

[068-082]such words. She would not even have dared to let such words in her

[068-083]own handwriting remain within the recesses of her own little desk.

[068-084]"Dear Major Grantly," she began at length. It seemed to her to

[068-085]be very ugly, but after much consideration she believed it to be

[068-086]correct. On the second day the letter was written as follows:--

[068-087]Allington, Thursday.

[068-088]MY DEAR MAJOR GRANTLY,--

[068-089]I do not know how I ought to answer your kind letter, but

[068-090]I must tell you that I am very much flattered by your

[068-091]great goodness to me. I cannot understand why you should

[068-092]think so much of me, but I suppose it is because you have

[068-093]felt for all our misfortunes. I will not say anything

[068-094]about what might have happened, if it had not been for

[068-095]papa's sorrow and disgrace; and as far as I can help it,

[068-096]I will not think of it; but I am sure that I ought not to

[068-097]think about loving any one, that is, in the way you mean,

[068-098]while we are in such trouble at home. I should not dare to

[068-099]meet any of your great friends, knowing that I had brought

[068-100]nothing with me but disgrace. And I should feel that I was

[068-101]doing an injury to _dear_ Edith, which would be worse to

[068-102]me than anything.

[068-103]Pray believe that I am quite in earnest about this. I know

[068-104]that a gentleman ought not to marry any girl to do himself

[068-105]and his family an injury by it; and I know that if I

[068-106]were to make such a marriage I should be unhappy ever

[068-107]afterwards, even though I loved the man ever so dearly,

[068-108]with all my heart.

[068-109]These last words she had underscored at first, but the doing so had

[068-110]been the unconscious expression of her own affection, and had been

[068-111]done with no desire on her part to convey that expression to him. But

[068-112]on reading the words she discovered their latent meaning, and wrote

[068-113]it all again.

[068-114]Therefore I know that it will be best that I should wish

[068-115]you good-by, and I do so, thanking you again and again for

[068-116]your goodness to me.

[068-117]Believe me to be,

[068-118]Yours very sincerely,

[068-119]GRACE CRAWLEY.

[068-120]The letter when it was written was hateful to her; but she had

[068-121]tried her hand at it again and again, and had found that she could

[068-122]do nothing better. There was much in his letter that she had not

[068-123]attempted to answer. He had implored her to tell him whether or no

[068-124]she did in truth love him. Of course she loved him. He knew that well

[068-125]enough. Why should she answer any such question? There was a way of

[068-126]answering it indeed which might serve her turn,--or rather serve his,

[068-127]of which she was thinking more than of her own. She might say that

[068-128]she did not love him. It would be a lie, and he would know that

[068-129]it would be a lie. But still it might serve the turn. She did not

[068-130]like the idea of writing such a lie as that, but nevertheless she

[068-131]considered the matter. It would be very wicked; but still, if it

[068-132]would serve the turn, might it not be well to write it? But at last

[068-133]she reflected that, after all, the doing of the thing was in her

[068-134]own hands. She could refuse to marry this man without burdening her

[068-135]conscience with any lie about it. It only required that she should

[068-136]be firm. She abstained, therefore, from the falsehood, and left her

[068-137]lover's question unanswered. So she put up her letter and directed

[068-138]it, and carried it herself to the village post-office.

[068-139]On the day after this she got the second letter, and that she showed

[068-140]immediately to Mrs. Dale. It was from her mother, and was written to

[068-141]tell her that her father was seriously ill. "He went up to London to

[068-142]see a lawyer about this weary work of the trial," said Mrs. Crawley.

[068-143]"The fatigue was very great, and on the next day he was so weak that

[068-144]he could not leave his bed. Dr. Turner, who has been very kind, says

[068-145]that we need not frighten ourselves, but he thinks it must be some

[068-146]time before he can leave the house. He has a low fever on him, and

[068-147]wants nourishment. His mind has wandered once or twice, and he has

[068-148]asked for you, and I think it will be best, love, that you should

[068-149]come home. I know you will not mind it when I say that I think he

[068-150]would like to have you here. Dr. Turner says that the illness is

[068-151]chiefly owing to his not having proper food."

[068-152]Of course she would go at once. "Dear Mrs. Dale," she said, "I must

[068-153]go home. Can you send me to the station?" Then Mrs. Dale read the

[068-154]letter. Of course they would send her. Would she go on that day, or

[068-155]on the next? Might it not be better to write first, and say that she

[068-156]was going? But Grace would go at once. "I know it will be a comfort

[068-157]to mamma; and I know that he is worse than mamma says." Of course

[068-158]there was no more to be said, and she was despatched to the station.

[068-159]Before she went Mrs. Dale asked after her purse. "If there is any

[068-160]trouble about money,--for your journey, or anything, you will not

[068-161]scruple to come to me as to an old friend." But Grace assured her

[068-162]that there was no trouble about money--for her journey. Then Lily

[068-163]took her aside and produced two clean new five-pound notes. "Grace,

[068-164]dear, you won't be ill-natured. You know I have a little fortune of

[068-165]my own. You know I can give them without missing them." Grace threw

[068-166]herself into her friend's arms and wept, but would have none of her

[068-167]money. "Buy a present from me for your mother,--whom I love though

[068-168]I do not know her." "I will give her your love," Grace said, "but

[068-169]nothing else." And then she went.
