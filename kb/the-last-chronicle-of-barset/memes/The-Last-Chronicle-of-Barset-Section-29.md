[029-001]"She is older, I suppose."

[029-002]"She's a little older, and a deal idler. How idle people do get!

[029-003]Look at him. Since he has had a curate he hardly ever stirs round

[029-004]the parish. And he is getting so fat that-- H--sh! Here she is

[029-005]herself,--come to give her judgment upon us." Then a stout lady, the

[029-006]wife of the vicar, walked slowly up the aisle. "Well, girls," she

[029-007]said, "you have worked hard, and I am sure Mr. Boyce will be very

[029-008]much obliged to you."

[029-009]"Mr. Boyce, indeed!" said Lily Dale. "We shall expect the whole

[029-010]parish to rise from their seats and thank us. Why didn't Jane and

[029-011]Bessy come and help us?"

[029-012]"They were so tired when they came in from the coal club. Besides,

[029-013]they don't care for this kind of thing,--not as you do."

[029-014]"Jane is utilitarian to the backbone, I know," said Lily, "and Bessy

[029-015]doesn't like getting up ladders."

[029-016]"As for ladders," said Mrs. Boyce, defending her daughter, "I am not

[029-017]quite sure that Bessy isn't right. You don't mean to say that you did

[029-018]all those in the capitals yourself?"

[029-019]"Every twig, with Hopkins to hold the ladder and cut the sticks; and

[029-020]as Hopkins is just a hundred and one years old, we could have done it

[029-021]pretty nearly as well alone."

[029-022]"I do not think that," said Grace.

[029-023]"He has been grumbling all the time," said Lily, "and swears he never

[029-024]will have the laurels so robbed again. Five or six years ago he used

[029-025]to declare that death would certainly save him from the pain of such

[029-026]another desecration before the next Christmas; but he has given up

[029-027]that foolish notion now, and talks as though he meant to protect the

[029-028]Allington shrubs at any rate to the end of this century."

[029-029]"I am sure we gave our share from the parsonage," said Mrs. Boyce,

[029-030]who never understood a joke.

[029-031]"All the best came from the parsonage, as of course they ought," said

[029-032]Lily. "But Hopkins had to make up the deficiency. And as my uncle

[029-033]told him to take the haycart for them instead of the hand-barrow, he

[029-034]is broken-hearted."

[029-035]"I am sure he was very good-natured," said Grace.

[029-036]"Nevertheless he is broken-hearted; and I am very good-natured too,

[029-037]and I am broken-backed. Who is going to preach to-morrow morning,

[029-038]Mrs. Boyce?"

[029-039]"Mr. Swanton will preach in the morning."

[029-040]"Tell him not to be long, because of the children's pudding. Tell Mr.

[029-041]Boyce if he is long, we won't any of us come next Sunday."

[029-042]"My dear, how can you say such wicked things! I shall not tell him

[029-043]anything of the kind."

[029-044]"That's not wicked, Mrs. Boyce. If I were to say I had eaten so much

[029-045]lunch that I didn't want any dinner, you'd understand that. If Mr.

[029-046]Swanton will preach for three-quarters of an hour--"

[029-047]"He only preached for three-quarters of an hour once, Lily."

[029-048]"He has been over the half-hour every Sunday since he has been here.

[029-049]His average is over forty minutes, and I say it's a shame."

[029-050]"It is not a shame at all, Lily," said Mrs. Boyce, becoming very

[029-051]serious.

[029-052]"Look at my uncle; he doesn't like to go to sleep, and he has to

[029-053]suffer a purgatory in keeping himself awake."

[029-054]"If your uncle is heavy, how can Mr. Swanton help it? If Mr. Dale's

[029-055]mind were on the subject he would not sleep."

[029-056]"Come, Mrs. Boyce; there's somebody else sleeps sometimes besides my

[029-057]uncle. When Mr. Boyce puts up his finger and just touches his nose,

[029-058]I know as well as possible why he does it."

[029-059]"Lily Dale, you have no business to say so. It is not true. I don't

[029-060]know how you can bring yourself to talk in that way of your own

[029-061]clergyman. If I were to tell your mamma she would be shocked."

[029-062]"You won't be so ill-natured, Mrs. Boyce,--after all that I've done

[029-063]for the church."

[029-064]"If you'd think more about the clergyman, Lily, and less about the

[029-065]church," said Mrs. Boyce very sententiously, "more about the matter

[029-066]and less about the manner, more of the reality and less of the form,

[029-067]I think you'd find that your religion would go further with you. Miss

[029-068]Crawley is the daughter of a clergyman, and I'm sure she'll agree

[029-069]with me."

[029-070]"If she agrees with anybody in scolding me I'll quarrel with her."

[029-071]"I didn't mean to scold you, Lily."

[029-072]"I don't mind it from you, Mrs. Boyce. Indeed, I rather like it. It

[029-073]is a sort of pastoral visitation; and as Mr. Boyce never scolds me

[029-074]himself, of course I take it as coming from him by attorney." Then

[029-075]there was silence for a minute or two, during which Mrs. Boyce was

[029-076]endeavouring to discover whether Miss Dale was laughing at her or

[029-077]not. As she was not quite certain, she thought at last that she would

[029-078]let the suspected fault pass unobserved. "Don't wait for us, Mrs.

[029-079]Boyce," said Lily. "We must remain till Hopkins has sent Gregory to

[029-080]sweep the church out and take away the rubbish. We'll see that the

[029-081]key is left at Mrs. Giles's."

[029-082]"Thank you, my dear. Then I may as well go. I thought I'd come in

[029-083]and see that it was all right. I'm sure Mr. Boyce will be very much

[029-084]obliged to you and Miss Crawley. Good-night, my dear."

[029-085]"Good-night, Mrs. Boyce; and be sure you don't let Mr. Swanton be

[029-086]long to-morrow." To this parting shot Mrs. Boyce made no rejoinder;

[029-087]but she hurried out of the church somewhat the quicker for it, and

[029-088]closed the door after her with something of a slam.

[029-089]Of all persons clergymen are the most irreverent in the handling of

[029-090]things supposed to be sacred, and next to them clergymen's wives, and

[029-091]after them those other ladies, old or young, who take upon themselves

[029-092]semi-clerical duties. And it is natural that it should be so; for is

[029-093]it not said that familiarity does breed contempt? When a parson takes

[029-094]his lay friend over his church on a week day, how much less of the

[029-095]spirit of genuflexion and head-uncovering the clergyman will display

[029-096]than the layman! The parson pulls about the woodwork and knocks about

[029-097]the stonework, as though it were mere wood and stone; and talks

[029-098]aloud in the aisle, and treats even the reading-desk as a common

[029-099]thing; whereas the visitor whispers gently, and carries himself as

[029-100]though even in looking at a church he was bound to regard himself as

[029-101]performing some service that was half divine. Now Lily Dale and Grace

[029-102]Crawley were both accustomed to churches, and had been so long at

[029-103]work in this church for the last two days, that the building had lost

[029-104]to them much of its sacredness, and they were almost as irreverent as

[029-105]though they were two curates.

[029-106]"I am so glad she has gone," said Lily. "We shall have to stop here

[029-107]for the next hour, as Gregory won't know what to take away and what

[029-108]to leave. I was so afraid she was going to stop and see us off the

[029-109]premises."

[029-110]"I don't know why you should dislike her."

[029-111]"I don't dislike her. I like her very well," said Lily Dale. "But

[029-112]don't you feel that there are people whom one knows very intimately,

[029-113]who are really friends,--for whom if they were dying one would

[029-114]grieve, whom if they were in misfortune one would go far to help, but

[029-115]with whom for all that one can have no sympathy. And yet they are

[029-116]so near to one that they know all the events of one's life, and are

[029-117]justified by unquestioned friendship in talking about things which

[029-118]should never be mentioned except where sympathy exists."

[029-119]"Yes; I understand that."

[029-120]"Everybody understands it who has been unhappy. That woman sometimes

[029-121]says things to me that make me wish,--wish that they'd make him

[029-122]bishop of Patagonia. And yet she does it all in friendship, and mamma

[029-123]says that she is quite right."

[029-124]"I liked her for standing up for her husband."

[029-125]"But he does go to sleep,--and then he scratches his nose to show

[029-126]that he's awake. I shouldn't have said it, only she is always hinting

[029-127]at uncle Christopher. Uncle Christopher certainly does go to sleep

[029-128]when Mr. Boyce preaches, and he hasn't studied any scientific little

[029-129]movements during his slumbers to make the people believe that he's

[029-130]all alive. I gave him a hint one day, and he got so angry with me!"

[029-131]"I shouldn't have thought he could have been angry with you. It seems

[029-132]to me from what you say that you may do whatever you please with

[029-133]him."

[029-134]"He is very good to me. If you knew it all,--if you could understand

[029-135]how good he has been! I'll try and tell you some day. It is not what

[029-136]he has done that makes me love him so,--but what he has thoroughly

[029-137]understood, and what, so understanding, he has not done, and what he

[029-138]has not said. It is a case of sympathy. If ever there was a gentleman

[029-139]uncle Christopher is one. And I used to dislike him so, at one time!"

[029-140]"And why?"

[029-141]"Chiefly because he would make me wear brown frocks when I wanted to

[029-142]have them pink or green. And he kept me for six months from having

[029-143]them long, and up to this day he scolds me if there is half an inch

[029-144]on the ground for him to tread upon."

[029-145]"I shouldn't mind that if I were you."

[029-146]"I don't,--not now. But it used to be serious when I was a young

[029-147]girl. And we thought, Bell and I, that he was cross to mamma. He and

[029-148]mamma didn't agree at first, you know, as they do now. It is quite

[029-149]true that he did dislike mamma when we first came here."

[029-150]"I can't think how anybody could ever dislike Mrs. Dale."

[029-151]"But he did. And then he wanted to make up a marriage between Bell

[029-152]and my cousin Bernard. But neither of them cared a bit for the other,

[029-153]and then he used to scold them,--and then,--and then,--and then--Oh,

[029-154]he was so good to me! Here's Gregory at last. Gregory, we've been

[029-155]waiting this hour and a half."

[029-156]"It ain't ten minutes since Hopkins let me come with the barrows,

[029-157]miss."

[029-158]"Then Hopkins is a traitor. Never mind. You'd better begin now,--up

[029-159]there at the steps. It'll be quite dark in a few minutes. Here's Mrs.

[029-160]Giles with her broom. Come, Mrs. Giles; we shall have to pass the

[029-161]night here if you don't make haste. Are you cold, Grace?"

[029-162]"No; I'm not cold. I'm thinking what they are doing now in the church

[029-163]at Hogglestock."

[029-164]"The Hogglestock church is not pretty;--like this?"

[029-165]"Oh, no. It is a very plain brick building, with something like a

[029-166]pigeon-house for a belfry. And the pulpit is over the reading-desk,

[029-167]and the reading-desk over the clerk, so that papa, when he preaches,

[029-168]is nearly up to the ceiling. And the whole place is divided into

[029-169]pews, in which the farmers hide themselves when they come to church."

[029-170]"So that nobody can see whether they go to sleep or no. Oh, Mrs.

[029-171]Giles, you mustn't pull that down. That's what we have been putting

[029-172]up all day."

[029-173]"But it be in the way, miss; so that the minister can't budge in or

[029-174]out o' the door."

[029-175]"Never mind. Then he must stay one side or the other. That would be

[029-176]too much after all our trouble!" And Miss Dale hurried across the

[029-177]chancel to save some prettily arching boughs, which, in the judgment

[029-178]of Mrs. Giles, encroached too much on the vestry door. "As if it

[029-179]signified which side he was," she said in a whisper to Grace.

[029-180]"I don't suppose they'll have anything in the church at home," said

[029-181]Grace.

[029-182]"Somebody will stick up a wreath or two, I daresay."
