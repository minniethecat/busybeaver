[075-001]"Don't be a fool, Lucy."

[075-002]"Of course I know that you are in love. I hope you are not going to

[075-003]give over being in love, Johnny, because it is such fun."

[075-004]"Wait till you're caught yourself, my girl."

[075-005]"I don't mean to be caught till some great swell comes this way.

[075-006]And as great swells never do come into Tavistock Square I shan't

[075-007]have a chance. I'll tell you what I would like; I'd like to have a

[075-008]Corsair,--or else a Giaour;--I think a Giaour would be nicest. Only

[075-009]a Giaour wouldn't be a Giaour here, you know. Fancy a lover 'Who

[075-010]thundering comes on blackest steed, With slackened bit and hoof of

[075-011]speed.' Were not those the days to live in! But all that is over now,

[075-012]you know, and young people take houses in Woburn Place, instead of

[075-013]being locked up, or drowned, or married to a hideous monster behind a

[075-014]veil. I suppose it's better as it is, for some reasons."

[075-015]"I think it must be more jolly, as you call it, Lucy."

[075-016]"I'm not quite sure. I know I'd go back and be Medora, if I could.

[075-017]Mamma is always telling Polly that she must be careful about

[075-018]William's dinner. But Conrad didn't care for his dinner. 'Light toil!

[075-019]to cull and dress thy frugal fare! See, I have plucked the fruit that

[075-020]promised best.'"

[075-021]"And how often do you think Conrad got drunk?"

[075-022]"I don't think he got drunk at all. There is no reason why he should,

[075-023]any more than William. Come along, and take me down to dinner. After

[075-024]all, papa's leg of mutton is better than Medora's apples, when one is

[075-025]as hungry as I am."

[075-026]The leg of mutton on this occasion consisted of soup, fish, and a

[075-027]bit of roast beef, and a couple of boiled fowls. "If I had only two

[075-028]children instead of twelve, Mr. Walker," said the host, "I'd give you

[075-029]a dinner à la Russe."

[075-030]"I don't begrudge Mrs. Toogood a single arrow in her quiver on that

[075-031]score," said Mr. Walker.

[075-032]"People are getting to be so luxurious that one can't live up to them

[075-033]at all," said Mrs. Toogood. "We dined out here with some new comers

[075-034]in the square only last week. We had asked them before, and they came

[075-035]quite in a quiet way,--just like this; and when we got there we found

[075-036]they'd four kinds of ices after dinner!"

[075-037]"And not a morsel of food on the table fit to eat," said Toogood.

[075-038]"I never was so poisoned in my life. As for soup,--it was just the

[075-039]washings of the pastrycook's kettle next door."

[075-040]"And how is one to live with such people, Mr. Walker?" continued Mrs.

[075-041]Toogood. "Of course we can't ask them back again. We can't give them

[075-042]four kinds of ices."

[075-043]"But would that be necessary? Perhaps they haven't got twelve

[075-044]children."

[075-045]"They haven't got any," said Toogood, triumphing; "not a chick

[075-046]belonging to them. But you see one must do as other people do. I

[075-047]hate anything grand. I wouldn't want more than this for myself, if

[075-048]bank-notes were as plenty as curl-papers."

[075-049]"Nobody has any curl-papers now, papa," said Lucy.

[075-050]"But I can't bear to be outdone," said Mr. Toogood. "I think it's

[075-051]very unpleasant,--people living in that sort of way. It's all very

[075-052]well telling me that I needn't live so too;--and of course I don't.

[075-053]I can't afford to have four men in from the confectioner's, dressed a

[075-054]sight better than myself, at ten shillings a head. I can't afford it,

[075-055]and I don't do it. But the worst of it is that I suffer because other

[075-056]people do it. It stands to reason that I must either be driven along

[075-057]with the crowd, or else be left behind. Now, I don't like either.

[075-058]And what's the end of it? Why, I'm half carried away and half left

[075-059]behind."

[075-060]"Upon my word, papa, I don't think you're carried away at all," said

[075-061]Lucy.

[075-062]"Yes, I am; and I'm ashamed of myself. Mr. Walker, I don't dare to

[075-063]ask you to drink a glass of wine with me in my own house,--that's

[075-064]what I don't,--because it's the proper thing for you to wait till

[075-065]somebody brings it you, and then to drink it by yourself. There is no

[075-066]knowing whether I mightn't offend you." And Mr. Toogood as he spoke

[075-067]grasped the decanter at his elbow. Mr. Walker grasped another at his

[075-068]elbow, and the two attorneys took their glass of wine together.

[075-069]"A very queer case this is of my cousin Crawley's," said Toogood to

[075-070]Walker, when the ladies had left the dining-room.

[075-071]"A most distressing case. I never knew anything so much talked of in

[075-072]our part of the country."

[075-073]"He can't have been a popular man, I should say?"

[075-074]"No; not popular,--not in the ordinary way;--anything but that.

[075-075]Nobody knew him personally before this matter came up."

[075-076]"But a good clergyman, probably? I'm interested in the case, of

[075-077]course, as his wife is my first-cousin. You will understand, however,

[075-078]that I know nothing of him. My father tried to be civil to him once,

[075-079]but Crawley wouldn't have it at all. We all thought he was mad then.

[075-080]I suppose he has done his duty in his parish?"

[075-081]"He has quarrelled with the bishop, you know,--out and out."

[075-082]"Has he, indeed? But I'm not sure that I think so very much about

[075-083]bishops, Mr. Walker."

[075-084]"That depends very much on the particular bishop. Some people say

[075-085]ours isn't all that a bishop ought to be, while others are very fond

[075-086]of him."

[075-087]"And Mr. Crawley belongs to the former set; that's all?" said Mr.

[075-088]Toogood.

[075-089]"No, Mr. Toogood; that isn't all. The worst of your cousin is that

[075-090]he has an aptitude to quarrel with everybody. He is one of those men

[075-091]who always think themselves to be ill-used. Now our dean, Dr. Arabin,

[075-092]has been his very old friend,--and as far as I can learn, a very good

[075-093]friend; but it seems that Mr. Crawley has done his best to quarrel

[075-094]with him too."

[075-095]"He spoke of the dean in the highest terms to me."

[075-096]"He may do that,--and yet quarrel with him. He'd quarrel with his own

[075-097]right hand, if he had nothing else to quarrel with. That makes the

[075-098]difficulty, you see. He'll take nobody's advice. He thinks that we're

[075-099]all against him."

[075-100]"I suppose the world has been heavy on him, Mr. Walker?"

[075-101]"The world has been very heavy on him," said John Eames, who had now

[075-102]been left free to join the conversation, Mr. Summerkin having gone

[075-103]away to his lady-love. "You must not judge him as you do other men."

[075-104]"That is just it," said Mr. Walker. "And to what result will that

[075-105]bring us?"

[075-106]"That we ought to stretch a point in his favour," said Toogood.

[075-107]"But why?" asked the attorney from Silverbridge. "What do we mean

[075-108]when we say that one man isn't to be trusted as another? We simply

[075-109]imply that he is not what we call responsible."

[075-110]"And I don't think Mr. Crawley is responsible," said Johnny.

[075-111]"Then how can he be fit to have charge of a parish?" said Mr. Walker.

[075-112]"You see where the difficulty is. How it embarrasses one all round.

[075-113]The amount of evidence as to the cheque is, I think, sufficient to

[075-114]get a verdict in an ordinary case, and the Crown has no alternative

[075-115]but so to treat it. Then his friends come forward,--and from sympathy

[075-116]with his sufferings, I desire to be ranked among the number,--and

[075-117]say, 'Ah, but you should spare this man, because he is not

[075-118]responsible.' Were he one who filled no position requiring special

[075-119]responsibility, that might be very well. His friends might undertake

[075-120]to look after him, and the prosecution might perhaps be smothered.

[075-121]But Mr. Crawley holds a living, and if he escape he will be

[075-122]triumphant,--especially triumphant over the bishop. Now, if he has

[075-123]really taken this money, and if his only excuse be that he did not

[075-124]know when he took it whether he was stealing or whether he was

[075-125]not,--for the sake of justice that ought not to be allowed." So spoke

[075-126]Mr. Walker.

[075-127]"You think he certainly did steal the money?" said Johnny.

[075-128]"You have heard the evidence, no doubt?" said Mr. Walker.

[075-129]"I don't feel quite sure about it, yet," said Mr. Toogood.

[075-130]"Quite sure of what?" said Mr. Walker.

[075-131]"That the cheque was dropped in his house."

[075-132]"It was at any rate traced to his hands."

[075-133]"I have no doubt about that," said Toogood.

[075-134]"And he can't account for it," said Walker.

[075-135]"A man isn't bound to show where he got his money," said Johnny.

[075-136]"Suppose that sovereign is marked," and Johnny produced a coin from

[075-137]his pocket, "and I don't know but what it is; and suppose it is

[075-138]proved to have belonged to some one who lost it, and then to be

[075-139]traced to my hands,--how am I to say where I got it? If I were asked,

[075-140]I should simply decline to answer."

[075-141]"But a cheque is not a sovereign, Mr. Eames," said Walker. "It is

[075-142]presumed that a man can account for the possession of a cheque. It

[075-143]may be that a man should have a cheque in his possession and not be

[075-144]able to account for it, and should yet be open to no grave suspicion.

[075-145]In such a case a jury has to judge. Here is the fact: that Mr.

[075-146]Crawley has the cheque, and brings it into use some considerable time

[075-147]after it is drawn; and the additional fact that the drawer of the

[075-148]cheque had lost it, as he thought, in Mr. Crawley's house, and had

[075-149]looked for it there, soon after it was drawn, and long before it was

[075-150]paid. A jury must judge; but, as a lawyer, I should say that the

[075-151]burden of disproof lies with Mr. Crawley."

[075-152]"Did you find out anything, Mr. Walker," said Toogood, "about the man

[075-153]who drove Mr. Soames that day?"

[075-154]"No,--nothing."

[075-155]"The trap was from 'The Dragon' at Barchester, I think?"

[075-156]"Yes,--from 'The Dragon of Wantly.'"

[075-157]"A respectable sort of house?"

[075-158]"Pretty well for that, I believe. I've heard that the people are

[075-159]poor," said Mr. Walker.

[075-160]"Somebody told me that they'd had a queer lot about the house, and

[075-161]that three or four of them left just then. I think I heard that two

[075-162]or three men from the place went to New Zealand together. It just

[075-163]came out in conversation while I was in the inn-yard."

[075-164]"I have never heard anything of it," said Mr. Walker.

[075-165]"I don't say that it can help us."

[075-166]"I don't see that it can," said Mr. Walker.

[075-167]After that there was a pause, and Mr. Toogood pushed about the old

[075-168]port, and made some very stinging remarks as to the claret-drinking

[075-169]propensities of the age. "Gladstone claret the most of it is, I

[075-170]fancy," said Mr. Toogood. "I find that port wine which my father

[075-171]bought in the wood five-and-twenty years ago is good enough for me."

[075-172]Mr. Walker said that it was quite good enough for him, almost too

[075-173]good, and that he thought that he had had enough of it. The host

[075-174]threatened another bottle, and was up to draw the cork,--rather to

[075-175]the satisfaction of John Eames, who liked his uncle's port,--but Mr.

[075-176]Walker stopped him. "Not a drop more for me," he said. "You are quite

[075-177]sure?" "Quite sure." And Mr. Walker moved towards the door.

[075-178]"It's a great pity, Mr. Walker," said Toogood, going back to the old

[075-179]subject, "that this dean and his wife should be away."

[075-180]"I understand that they will both be home before the trial," said Mr.

[075-181]Walker.

[075-182]"Yes,--but you know how very important it is to learn beforehand

[075-183]exactly what your witnesses can prove and what they can't prove. And

[075-184]moreover, though neither the dean nor his wife might perhaps be able

[075-185]to tell us anything themselves, they might help to put us on the

[075-186]proper scent. I think I'll send somebody after them. I think I will."
