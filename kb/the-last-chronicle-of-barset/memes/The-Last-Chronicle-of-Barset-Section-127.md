[127-001]But the major had his own way of looking at the matter. He had, he

[127-002]flattered himself, dealt very fairly with his father. When he had

[127-003]first made up his mind to make Miss Crawley his wife, he had told

[127-004]his father of his intention. The archdeacon had declared that, if he

[127-005]did so, such and such results would follow,--results which, as was

[127-006]apparent to every one, would make it indispensable that the major

[127-007]should leave Cosby Lodge. The major had never complained. So he

[127-008]told himself. He had simply said to his father,--"I shall do as I

[127-009]have said. You can do as you have said. Therefore, I shall prepare

[127-010]to leave Cosby Lodge." He had so prepared; and as a part of that

[127-011]preparation, the auctioneer's bills had been stuck up on the posts

[127-012]and walls. Then the archdeacon had gone to work surreptitiously with

[127-013]the lady,--the reader will understand that we are still following

[127-014]the workings of the major's mind,--and having succeeded in obtaining

[127-015]a pledge which he had been wrong to demand, came forward very

[127-016]graciously to withdraw his threats. He withdrew his threats because

[127-017]he had succeeded in his object by other means. The major knew nothing

[127-018]of the kiss that had been given, of the two tears that had trickled

[127-019]down his father's nose, of the generous epithets which the archdeacon

[127-020]had applied to Grace. He did not guess how nearly his father had

[127-021]yielded altogether beneath the pressure of Grace's charms,--how

[127-022]willing he was to yield altogether at the first decent opportunity.

[127-023]His father had obtained a pledge from Grace that she would not marry

[127-024]in certain circumstances,--as to which circumstances the major was

[127-025]strongly resolved that they should form no bar to his marriage,--and

[127-026]then came forward with his eager demand that the sale should be

[127-027]stopped! The major could not submit to so much indignity. He had

[127-028]resolved that his father should have nothing to do with his marriage

[127-029]one way or the other. He would not accept anything from his father

[127-030]on the understanding that his father had any such right. His father

[127-031]had asserted such right with threats, and he, the major, taking such

[127-032]threats as meaning something, had seen that he must leave Cosby

[127-033]Lodge. Let his father come forward, and say that they meant nothing,

[127-034]that he abandoned all right to any interference as to his son's

[127-035]marriage, and then the son--would dutifully consent to accept his

[127-036]father's bounty! They were both cross-grained, as Mrs. Grantly

[127-037]declared; but I think that the major was the most cross-grained of

[127-038]the two.

[127-039]Something of the truth made its way into Henry Grantly's mind as he

[127-040]drove himself home from Barchester after seeing his grandfather. It

[127-041]was not that he began to think that his father was right, but that

[127-042]he almost perceived that it might be becoming in him to forgive some

[127-043]fault in his father. He had been implored to honour his father, and

[127-044]he was willing to do so, understanding that such honour must, to a

[127-045]certain degree, imply obedience,--if it could be done at no more than

[127-046]a moderate expense to his feelings. The threatened auctioneer was the

[127-047]cause of offence to his father, and he might see whether it would not

[127-048]be possible to have the sale postponed. There would, of course, be a

[127-049]pecuniary loss, and that in his diminished circumstances,--he would

[127-050]still talk to himself of his diminished circumstances,--might be

[127-051]inconvenient. But so much he thought himself bound to endure on his

[127-052]father's behalf. At any rate, he would consult the auctioneer at

[127-053]Silverbridge.

[127-054]But he would not make any pause in the measures which he had proposed

[127-055]to himself as likely to be conducive to his marriage. As for Grace's

[127-056]pledge, such pledges from young ladies never went for anything. It

[127-057]was out of the question that she should be sacrificed, even though

[127-058]her father had taken the money. And, moreover, the very gist of the

[127-059]major's generosity was to consist in his marrying her whether the

[127-060]father were guilty or innocent. He understood that perfectly, and

[127-061]understood also that it was his duty to make his purpose in this

[127-062]respect known to Grace's family. He determined, therefore, that he

[127-063]would go over to Hogglestock, and see Mr. Crawley before he saw the

[127-064]auctioneer.

[127-065]Hitherto Major Grantly had never even spoken to Mr. Crawley. It may

[127-066]be remembered that the major was at the present moment one of the

[127-067]bailsmen for the due appearance of Mr. Crawley before the judge,

[127-068]and that he had been present when the magistrates sat at the inn

[127-069]in Silverbridge. He therefore knew the man's presence, but except

[127-070]on that occasion he had never even seen his intended future

[127-071]father-in-law. From the moment when he had first allowed himself

[127-072]to think of Grace, he had desired, yet almost feared, to make

[127-073]acquaintance with the father; but had been debarred from doing so by

[127-074]the peculiar position in which Mr. Crawley was placed. He had felt

[127-075]that it would be impossible to speak to the father of his affection

[127-076]for the daughter without any allusion to the coming trial; and he did

[127-077]not know how such allusion could be made. Thinking of this, he had at

[127-078]different times almost resolved not to call at Hogglestock till the

[127-079]trial should be over. Then he would go there, let the result of the

[127-080]trial have been what it might. But it had now become necessary for

[127-081]him to go on at once. His father had precipitated matters by his

[127-082]appeal to Grace. He would appeal to Grace's father, and reach Grace

[127-083]through his influence.

[127-084]He drove over to Hogglestock, feeling himself to be anything but

[127-085]comfortable as he came near to the house. And when he did reach the

[127-086]spot he was somewhat disconcerted to find that another visitor was

[127-087]in the house before him. He presumed this to be the case, because

[127-088]there stood a little pony horse,--an animal which did not strongly

[127-089]recommend itself to his instructed eye,--attached by its rein to

[127-090]the palings. It was a poor humble-looking beast, whose knees had

[127-091]very lately become acquainted with the hard and sharp stones of a

[127-092]newly-mended highway. The blood was even now red upon the wounds.

[127-093]"He'll never be much good again," said the major to his servant.

[127-094]"That he won't, sir," said the man. "But I don't think he's been very

[127-095]much good for some time back."

[127-096]"I shouldn't like to have to ride him into Silverbridge," said the

[127-097]major, descending from the gig, and instructing his servant to move

[127-098]the horse and gig about as long as he might remain within the house.

[127-099]Then he walked across the little garden and knocked at the door.

[127-100]The door was immediately opened, and in the passage he found Mr.

[127-101]Crawley, and another clergyman whom the reader will recognize as Mr.

[127-102]Thumble. Mr. Thumble had come over to make arrangements as to the

[127-103]Sunday services and the parochial work, and had been very urgent in

[127-104]impressing on Mr. Crawley that the duties were to be left entirely

[127-105]to himself. Hence had come some bitter words, in which Mr. Crawley,

[127-106]though no doubt he said the sharper things of the two, had not been

[127-107]able to vanquish his enemy so completely as he had done on former

[127-108]occasions.

[127-109]"There must be no interference, my dear sir,--none whatever, if you

[127-110]please," Mr. Thumble had said.

[127-111]"There shall be none of which the bishop shall have reason to

[127-112]complain," Mr. Crawley had replied.

[127-113]"There must be none at all, Mr. Crawley, if you please. It is only

[127-114]on that understanding that I have consented to take the parish

[127-115]temporarily into my hands. Mrs. Crawley, I hope that there may be

[127-116]no mistake about the schools. It must be exactly as though I were

[127-117]residing on the spot."

[127-118]"Sir," said Mr. Crawley, very irate at this appeal to his wife, and

[127-119]speaking in a loud voice, "do you misdoubt my word; or do you think

[127-120]that if I were minded to be false to you, that I should be corrected

[127-121]in my falsehood by the firmer faith of my wife?"

[127-122]"I meant nothing about falsehood, Mr. Crawley."

[127-123]"Having resigned this benefice for certain reasons of my own, with

[127-124]which I shall not trouble you, and acknowledging as I do,--and have

[127-125]done in writing under my hand to the bishop,--the propriety of his

[127-126]lordship's interference in providing for the services of the parish

[127-127]till my successor shall have been instituted, I shall, with what

[127-128]feelings of regret I need not say, leave you to the performance of

[127-129]your temporary duties."

[127-130]"That is all that I require, Mr. Crawley."

[127-131]"But it is wholly unnecessary that you should instruct me in mine."

[127-132]"The bishop especially desires--" began Mr. Thumble. But Mr. Crawley

[127-133]interrupted him instantly.--

[127-134]"If the bishop has directed you to give me such instruction, the

[127-135]bishop has been much in error. I will submit to receive none from him

[127-136]through you, sir. If you please, sir, let there be an end of it;" and

[127-137]Mr. Crawley waved his hand. I hope that the reader will conceive the

[127-138]tone of Mr. Crawley's voice, and will appreciate the aspect of his

[127-139]face, and will see the motion of his hand, as he spoke these latter

[127-140]words. Mr. Thumble felt the power of the man so sensibly that he was

[127-141]unable to carry on the contest. Though Mr. Crawley was now but a

[127-142]broken reed, and was beneath his feet, yet Mr. Thumble acknowledged

[127-143]to himself that he could not hold his own in debate with this broken

[127-144]reed. But the words had been spoken, and the tone of the voice had

[127-145]died away, and the fire in the eyes had burned itself out before the

[127-146]moment of the major's arrival. Mr. Thumble was now returning to his

[127-147]horse, and having enjoyed,--if he did enjoy,--his little triumph

[127-148]about the parish, was becoming unhappy at the future dangers

[127-149]that awaited him. Perhaps he was the more unhappy because it had

[127-150]been proposed to him by authorities at the palace that he should

[127-151]repeatedly ride on the same animal from Barchester to Hogglestock

[127-152]and back. Mr. Crawley was in the act of replying to lamentations on

[127-153]this subject, with his hand on the latch, when the major arrived--"I

[127-154]regret to say, sir, that I cannot assist you by supplying any other

[127-155]steed." Then the major had knocked, and Mr. Crawley had at once

[127-156]opened the door.

[127-157]"You probably do not remember me, Mr. Crawley?" said the major. "I am

[127-158]Major Grantly." Mrs. Crawley, who heard these words inside the room,

[127-159]sprang up from her chair, and could hardly resist the temptation to

[127-160]rush into the passage. She too had barely seen Major Grantly; and

[127-161]now the only bright gleam which appeared on her horizon depended on

[127-162]his constancy under circumstances which would have justified his

[127-163]inconstancy. But had he meant to be inconstant, surely he would never

[127-164]have come to Hogglestock!

[127-165]"I remember you well, sir," said Mr. Crawley. "I am under no common

[127-166]obligation to you. You are at present one of my bailsmen."

[127-167]"There's nothing in that," said the major.

[127-168]Mr. Thumble, who had caught the name of Grantly, took off his hat,

[127-169]which he had put on his head. He had not been particular in keeping

[127-170]off his hat before Mr. Crawley. But he knew very well that Archdeacon

[127-171]Grantly was a big man in the diocese; and though the Grantlys and

[127-172]the Proudies were opposed to each other, still it might be well to

[127-173]take off his hat before any one who had to do with the big ones of

[127-174]the diocese. "I hope your respected father is well, sir?" said Mr.

[127-175]Thumble.

[127-176]"Pretty well, I thank you." The major stood close up against the wall

[127-177]of the passage, so as to allow room for Mr. Thumble to pass out. His

[127-178]business was one on which he could hardly begin to speak until the

[127-179]other visitor should have gone. Mr. Crawley was standing with the

[127-180]door wide open in his hand. He also was anxious to be rid of Mr.

[127-181]Thumble,--and was perhaps not so solicitous as a brother clergyman

[127-182]should have been touching the future fate of Mr. Thumble in the

[127-183]matter of the bishop's old cob.
