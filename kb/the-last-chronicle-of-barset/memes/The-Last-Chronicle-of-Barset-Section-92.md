[092-001]During dinner she struggled to say a word or two to her husband, as

[092-002]though there had been no quarrel between them. With him the matter

[092-003]had gone so deep that he could not answer her in the same spirit.

[092-004]There were sundry members of the family present,--daughters, and a

[092-005]son-in-law, and a daughter's friend who was staying with them; but

[092-006]even in the hope of appearing to be serene before them he could not

[092-007]struggle through his deep despondence. He was very silent, and to his

[092-008]wife's words he answered hardly anything. He was courteous and gentle

[092-009]with them all, but he spoke as little as was possible, and during

[092-010]the evening he sat alone, with his head leaning on his hand,--not

[092-011]pretending even to read. He was aware that it was too late to make

[092-012]even an attempt to conceal his misery and his disgrace from his own

[092-013]family.

[092-014]His wife came to him that night in his dressing-room in a spirit of

[092-015]feminine softness that was very unusual with her. "My dear," said

[092-016]she, "let us forget what occurred this morning. If there has been any

[092-017]anger we are bound as Christians to forget it." She stood over him as

[092-018]she spoke, and put her hand upon his shoulder almost caressingly.

[092-019]"When a man's heart is broken, he cannot forget it," was his reply.

[092-020]She still stood by him, and still kept her hand upon him; but she

[092-021]could think of no other words of comfort to say. "I will go to bed,"

[092-022]he said. "It is the best place for me." Then she left him, and he

[092-023]went to bed.

[092-024]CHAPTER XLVIII.

[092-025]THE SOFTNESS OF SIR RAFFLE BUFFLE.

[092-026]We have seen that John Eames was prepared to start on his journey in

[092-027]search of the Arabins, and have seen him after he had taken farewell

[092-028]of his office and of his master there, previous to his departure; but

[092-029]that matter of his departure had not been arranged altogether with

[092-030]comfort as far as his official interests were concerned. He had been

[092-031]perhaps a little abrupt in his mode of informing Sir Raffle Buffle

[092-032]that there was a pressing cause for his official absence, and Sir

[092-033]Raffle had replied to him that no private pressure could be allowed

[092-034]to interfere with his public duties. "I must go, Sir Raffle, at any

[092-035]rate," Johnny had said; "it is a matter affecting my family, and must

[092-036]not be neglected." "If you intend to go without leave," said Sir

[092-037]Raffle, "I presume you will first put your resignation into the hands

[092-038]of Mr. Kissing." Now, Mr. Kissing was the secretary to the Board.

[092-039]This had been serious undoubtedly. John Eames was not specially

[092-040]anxious to keep his present position as private secretary to Sir

[092-041]Raffle, but he certainly had no desire to give up his profession

[092-042]altogether. He said nothing more to the great man on that occasion,

[092-043]but before he left the office he wrote a private note to the chairman

[092-044]expressing the extreme importance of his business, and begging that

[092-045]he might have leave of absence. On the next morning he received it

[092-046]back with a very few words written across it. "It can't be done,"

[092-047]were the very few words which Sir Raffle Buffle had written across

[092-048]the note from his private secretary. Here was a difficulty which

[092-049]Johnny had not anticipated, and which seemed to be insuperable. Sir

[092-050]Raffle would not have answered him in that strain if he had not been

[092-051]very much in earnest.

[092-052]"I should send him a medical certificate," said Cradell, his friend

[092-053]of old.

[092-054]"Nonsense," said Eames.

[092-055]"I don't see that it's nonsense at all. They can't get over a medical

[092-056]certificate from a respectable man; and everybody has got something

[092-057]the matter with him of some kind."

[092-058]"I should go and let him do his worst," said Fisher, who was another

[092-059]clerk. "It wouldn't be more than putting you down a place or two.

[092-060]As to losing your present berth you don't mind that, and they would

[092-061]never think of dismissing you."

[092-062]"But I do mind being put down a place or two," said Johnny, who could

[092-063]not forget that were he so put down his friend Fisher would gain the

[092-064]step which he would lose.

[092-065]"I should give him a barrel of oysters, and talk to him about the

[092-066]Chancellor of the Exchequer," said FitzHoward, who had been private

[092-067]secretary to Sir Raffle before Eames, and might therefore be supposed

[092-068]to know the man.

[092-069]"That might have done very well if I had not asked him and been

[092-070]refused first," said John Eames. "I'll tell you what I'll do, I'll

[092-071]write a long letter on a sheet of foolscap paper, with a regular

[092-072]margin, so that it must come before the Board, and perhaps that will

[092-073]frighten him."

[092-074]When he mentioned his difficulty on that evening to Mr. Toogood, the

[092-075]lawyer begged him to give up the journey. "It will only be sending a

[092-076]clerk, and it won't cost so very much after all," said Toogood. But

[092-077]Johnny's pride could not allow him to give way. "I'm not going to be

[092-078]done about it," said he. "I'm not going to resign, but I will go even

[092-079]though they may dismiss me. I don't think it will come to that, but

[092-080]if it does it must." His uncle begged of him not to think of such

[092-081]an alternative; but this discussion took place after dinner, and

[092-082]away from the office, and Eames would not submit to bow his neck to

[092-083]authority. "If it comes to that," said he, "a fellow might as well

[092-084]be a slave at once. And what is the use of a fellow having a little

[092-085]money if it does not make him independent? You may be sure of one

[092-086]thing, I shall go; and that on the day fixed."

[092-087]On the next morning John Eames was very silent when he went into Sir

[092-088]Raffle's room at the office. There was now only this day and another

[092-089]before that fixed for his departure, and it was of course very

[092-090]necessary that matters should be arranged. But he said nothing to Sir

[092-091]Raffle during the morning. The great man himself was condescending

[092-092]and endeavoured to be kind. He knew that his stern refusal had

[092-093]greatly irritated his private secretary, and was anxious to show

[092-094]that, though in the cause of public duty he was obliged to be stern,

[092-095]he was quite willing to forget his sternness when the necessity for

[092-096]it had passed away. On this morning, therefore, he was very cheery.

[092-097]But to all his cheery good-humour John Eames would make no response.

[092-098]Late in the afternoon, when most of the men had left the office,

[092-099]Johnny appeared before the chairman for the last time that day with a

[092-100]very long face. He was dressed in black, and had changed his ordinary

[092-101]morning coat for a frock, which gave him an appearance altogether

[092-102]unlike that which was customary to him. And he spoke almost in a

[092-103]whisper, very slowly; and when Sir Raffle joked,--and Sir Raffle

[092-104]often would joke,--he not only did not laugh, but he absolutely

[092-105]sighed. "Is there anything the matter with you, Eames?" asked Sir

[092-106]Raffle.

[092-107]"I am in great trouble," said John Eames.

[092-108]"And what is your trouble?"

[092-109]"It is essential for the honour of one of my family that I should be

[092-110]at Florence by this day week. I cannot make up my mind what I ought

[092-111]to do. I do not wish to lose my position in the public service, to

[092-112]which, as you know, I am warmly attached; but I cannot submit to see

[092-113]the honour of my family sacrificed!"

[092-114]"Eames," said Sir Raffle, "that must be nonsense;--that must be

[092-115]nonsense. There can be no reason why you should always expect to have

[092-116]your own way in everything."

[092-117]"Of course if I go without leave I shall be dismissed."

[092-118]"Of course you will. It is out of the question that a young man

[092-119]should take the bit between his teeth in that way."

[092-120]"As for taking the bit between his teeth, Sir Raffle, I do not

[092-121]think that any man was ever more obedient, perhaps I should say

[092-122]more submissive, than I have been. But there must be a limit to

[092-123]everything."

[092-124]"What do you mean by that, Mr. Eames?" said Sir Raffle, turning in

[092-125]anger upon his private secretary. But Johnny disregarded his anger.

[092-126]Johnny, indeed, had made up his mind that Sir Raffle should be very

[092-127]angry. "What do you mean, Mr. Eames, by saying that there must be

[092-128]a limit? I know nothing about limits. One would suppose that you

[092-129]intended to make an accusation against me."

[092-130]"So I do. I think, Sir Raffle, that you are treating me with great

[092-131]cruelty. I have explained to you that family circumstances--"

[092-132]"You have explained nothing, Mr. Eames."

[092-133]"Yes, I have, Sir Raffle. I have explained to you that matters

[092-134]relating to my family, which materially affect the honour of a

[092-135]certain one of its members, demand that I should go at once to

[092-136]Florence. You tell me that if I go I shall be dismissed."

[092-137]"Of course you must not go without leave. I never heard of such a

[092-138]thing in all my life." And Sir Raffle lifted up his hands towards

[092-139]heaven, almost in dismay.

[092-140]"So I have drawn up a short statement of the circumstances, which I

[092-141]hope may be read at the Board when the question of my dismissal comes

[092-142]before it."

[092-143]"You mean to go, then?"

[092-144]"Yes, Sir Raffle; I must go. The honour of a certain branch of my

[092-145]family demands that I should do so. As I have for some time been so

[092-146]especially under you, I thought it would be proper to show you what

[092-147]I have said before I send my letter in, and therefore I have brought

[092-148]it with me. Here it is." And Johnny handed to Sir Raffle an official

[092-149]document of large dimensions.

[092-150]Sir Raffle began to be uncomfortable. He had acquired a character

[092-151]for tyranny in the public service of which he was aware, though

[092-152]he thought that he knew well that he had never deserved it. Some

[092-153]official big-wig,--perhaps that Chancellor of the Exchequer of whom

[092-154]he was so fond,--had on one occasion hinted to him that a little

[092-155]softness of usage would be compatible with the prejudices of the

[092-156]age. Softness was impossible to Sir Raffle; but his temper was

[092-157]sufficiently under his control to enable him to encounter the rebuke,

[092-158]and to pull himself up from time to time when he found himself

[092-159]tempted to speak loud and to take things with a high hand. He knew

[092-160]that a clerk should not be dismissed for leaving his office, who

[092-161]could show that his absence had been caused by some matter really

[092-162]affecting the interest of his family; and that were he to drive Eames

[092-163]to go on this occasion without leave, Eames would be simply called

[092-164]in to state what was this matter of moment which had taken him away.

[092-165]Probably he had stated that matter of moment in this very document

[092-166]which Sir Raffle was holding in his hand. But Sir Raffle was not

[092-167]willing to be conquered by the document. If it was necessary that he

[092-168]should give way, he would much prefer to give way,--out of his own

[092-169]good-nature, let us say,--without looking at the document at all.

[092-170]"I must, under the circumstances, decline to read this," said he,

[092-171]"unless it should come before me officially," and he handed back the

[092-172]paper.

[092-173]"I thought it best to let you see it if you pleased," said John

[092-174]Eames. Then he turned round as though he were going to leave the

[092-175]room; but suddenly he turned back again. "I don't like to leave you,

[092-176]Sir Raffle, without saying good-by. I do not suppose we shall meet

[092-177]again. Of course you must do your duty, and I do not wish you to

[092-178]think that I have any personal ill-will against you." So saying, he

[092-179]put out his hand to Sir Raffle as though to take a final farewell.

[092-180]Sir Raffle looked at him in amazement. He was dressed, as has been

[092-181]said, in black, and did not look like the John Eames of every day to

[092-182]whom Sir Raffle was accustomed.
