[104-001]Bernard and Emily were not insatiable lovers, but, nevertheless,

[104-002]Mrs. Thorne had thought it proper to provide a fourth in the

[104-003]riding-parties, and had put Mr. Dunn upon this duty. "Don't bother

[104-004]yourself about it, Siph," she had said; "only if those lovers should

[104-005]go off philandering out of sight, our little country lassie might

[104-006]find herself to be nowhere in the Park." Siph had promised to make

[104-007]himself useful, and had done so. There had generally been so large a

[104-008]number in their party that the work imposed on Mr. Dunn had been very

[104-009]light. Lily had never found out that he had been especially consigned

[104-010]to her as her own cavalier, but had seen quite enough of him to be

[104-011]aware that he was a pleasant companion. To her, thinking, as she ever

[104-012]was thinking, about Johnny Eames, Siph was much more agreeable than

[104-013]might have been a younger man who would have endeavoured to make her

[104-014]think about himself.

[104-015]Thus when she found herself riding alone in Rotten Row with Siph

[104-016]Dunn, she was neither disconcerted nor displeased. He had been

[104-017]talking to her about Lord De Guest, whom he had known,--for Siph knew

[104-018]everybody,--and Lily had begun to wonder whether he knew John Eames.

[104-019]She would have liked to hear the opinion of such a man about John

[104-020]Eames. She was making up her mind that she would say something about

[104-021]the Crawley matter,--not intending of course to mention John Eames's

[104-022]name,--when suddenly her tongue was paralyzed and she could not

[104-023]speak. At that moment they were standing near a corner, where a

[104-024]turning path made an angle in the iron rails, Mr. Dunn having

[104-025]proposed that they should wait there for a few minutes before they

[104-026]returned home, as it was probable that Bernard and Miss Dunstable

[104-027]might come up. They had been there for some five or ten minutes, and

[104-028]Lily had asked her first question about the Crawleys,--inquiring of

[104-029]Mr. Dunn whether he had heard of a terrible accusation which had

[104-030]been made against a clergyman in Barsetshire,--when on a sudden her

[104-031]tongue was paralyzed. As they were standing, Lily's horse was turned

[104-032]towards the diverging path, whereas Mr. Dunn was looking the other

[104-033]way, towards Achilles and Apsley house. Mr. Dunn was nearer to the

[104-034]railings, but though they were thus looking different ways they

[104-035]were so placed that each could see the face of the other. Then, on

[104-036]a sudden, coming slowly towards her along the diverging path and

[104-037]leaning on the arm of another man, she saw,--Adolphus Crosbie.

[104-038]She had never seen him since a day on which she had parted from him

[104-039]with many kisses,--with warm, pressing, eager kisses,--of which

[104-040]she had been nowhat ashamed. He had then been to her almost as her

[104-041]husband. She had trusted him entirely, and had thrown herself into

[104-042]his arms with a full reliance. There is often much of reticence on

[104-043]the part of a woman towards a man to whom she is engaged, something

[104-044]also of shamefacedness occasionally. There exists a shadow of doubt,

[104-045]at least of that hesitation which shows that in spite of vows the

[104-046]woman knows that a change may come, and that provision for such

[104-047]possible steps backward should always be within her reach. But Lily

[104-048]had cast all such caution to the winds. She had given herself to

[104-049]the man entirely, and had determined that she would sink or swim,

[104-050]stand or fall, live or die, by him and by his truth. He had been as

[104-051]false as hell. She had been in his arms, clinging to him, kissing

[104-052]him, swearing that her only pleasure in the world was to be with

[104-053]him,--with him her treasure, her promised husband; and within a

[104-054]month, a week, he had been false to her. There had come upon her

[104-055]crushing tidings, and she had for days wondered at herself that they

[104-056]had not killed her. But she had lived, and had forgiven him. She had

[104-057]still loved him, and had received new offers from him, which had been

[104-058]answered as the reader knows. But she had never seen him since the

[104-059]day on which she had parted from him at Allington, without a doubt as

[104-060]to his faith. Now he was before her, walking on the footpath, almost

[104-061]within reach of her whip.

[104-062]He did not recognize her, but as he passed on he did recognize Mr.

[104-063]Onesiphorus Dunn, and stopped to speak to him. Or it might have

[104-064]been that Crosbie's friend Fowler Pratt stopped with this special

[104-065]object,--for Siph Dunn was an intimate friend of Fowler Pratt's.

[104-066]Crosbie and Siph were also acquainted, but in those days Crosbie did

[104-067]not care much for stopping his friends in the Park or elsewhere. He

[104-068]had become moody and discontented, and was generally seen going about

[104-069]the world alone. On this special occasion he was having a little

[104-070]special conversation about money with his very old friend Fowler

[104-071]Pratt.

[104-072]"What, Siph, is this you? You're always on horseback now," said

[104-073]Fowler Pratt.

[104-074]"Well, yes; I have gone in a good deal for cavalry work this last

[104-075]month. I've been lucky enough to have a young lady to ride with me."

[104-076]This he said in a whisper, which the distance of Lily justified. "How

[104-077]d'ye do, Crosbie? One doesn't often see you on horseback, or on foot

[104-078]either."

[104-079]"I've something to do besides going to look or to be looked at,"

[104-080]said Crosbie. Then he raised his eyes and saw Lily's side-face, and

[104-081]recognized her. Had he seen her before he had been stopped on his way

[104-082]I think he would have passed on, endeavouring to escape observation.

[104-083]But as it was, his feet had been arrested before he knew of her close

[104-084]vicinity, and now it would seem that he was afraid of her, and was

[104-085]flying from her, were he at once to walk off, leaving his friend

[104-086]behind him. And he knew that she had seen him, and had recognized

[104-087]him, and was now suffering from his presence. He could not but

[104-088]perceive that it was so from the fixedness of her face, and from the

[104-089]constrained manner in which she gazed before her. His friend Fowler

[104-090]Pratt had never seen Miss Dale, though he knew very much of her

[104-091]history. Siph Dunn knew nothing of the history of Crosbie and his

[104-092]love, and was unaware that he and Lily had ever seen each other.

[104-093]There was thus no help near her to extricate her from her difficulty.

[104-094]"When a man has any work to do in the world," said Siph, "he always

[104-095]boasts of it to his acquaintance, and curses his luck to himself. I

[104-096]have nothing to do and can go about to see and to be seen;--and I

[104-097]must own that I like it."

[104-098]"Especially the being seen,--eh, Siph?" said Fowler Pratt. "I also

[104-099]have nothing on earth to do, and I come here every day because it is

[104-100]as easy to do that as to go anywhere else."

[104-101]Crosbie was still looking at Lily. He could not help himself. He

[104-102]could not take his eyes from off her. He could see that she was as

[104-103]pretty as ever, that she was but very little altered. She was, in

[104-104]truth, somewhat stouter than in the old days, but of that he took no

[104-105]special notice. Should he speak to her? Should he try to catch her

[104-106]eye, and then raise his hat? Should he go up to her horse's head

[104-107]boldly, and ask her to let bygones be bygones? He had an idea that of

[104-108]all courses which he could pursue that was the one which she would

[104-109]approve the best,--which would be most efficacious for him, if with

[104-110]her anything from him might have any efficacy. But he could not do

[104-111]it. He did not know what words he might best use. Would it become him

[104-112]humbly to sue to her for pardon? Or should he strive to express his

[104-113]unaltered love by some tone of his voice? Or should he simply ask

[104-114]her after her health? He made one step towards her, and he saw that

[104-115]the face became more rigid and more fixed than before, and then he

[104-116]desisted. He told himself that he was simply hateful to her. He

[104-117]thought that he could perceive that there was no tenderness mixed

[104-118]with her unabated anger.

[104-119]At this moment Bernard Dale and Emily came close upon him, and

[104-120]Bernard saw him at once. It was through Bernard that Lily and Crosbie

[104-121]had come to know each other. He and Bernard Dale had been fast

[104-122]friends in old times, and had, of course, been bitter enemies since

[104-123]the day of Crosbie's treachery. They had never spoken since, though

[104-124]they had often seen each other, and Dale was not at all disposed to

[104-125]speak to him now. The moment that he recognized Crosbie he looked

[104-126]across to his cousin. For an instant, an idea had flashed across

[104-127]him that he was there by her permission,--with her assent; but it

[104-128]required no second glance to show him that this was not the case.

[104-129]"Dunn," he said, "I think we will ride on," and he put his horse into

[104-130]a trot. Siph, whose ear was very accurate, and who knew at once that

[104-131]something was wrong, trotted on with him, and Lily, of course, was

[104-132]not left behind. "Is there anything the matter?" said Emily to her

[104-133]lover.

[104-134]"Nothing specially the matter," he replied; "but you were standing in

[104-135]company with the greatest blackguard that ever lived, and I thought

[104-136]we had better change our ground."

[104-137]"Bernard!" said Lily, flashing on him with all the fire which her

[104-138]eyes could command. Then she remembered that she could not reprimand

[104-139]him for the offence of such abuse in such a company; so she reined in

[104-140]her horse and fell a-weeping.

[104-141]Siph Dunn, with his wicked cleverness, knew the whole story at once,

[104-142]remembering that he had once heard something of Crosbie having

[104-143]behaved very ill to some one before he married Lady Alexandrina De

[104-144]Courcy. He stopped his horse also, falling a little behind Lily,

[104-145]so that he might not be supposed to have seen her tears, and began

[104-146]to hum a tune. Emily also, though not wickedly clever, understood

[104-147]something of it. "If Bernard says anything to make you angry, I will

[104-148]scold him," she said. Then the two girls rode on together in front,

[104-149]while Bernard fell back with Siph Dunn.

[104-150]"Pratt," said Crosbie, putting his hand on his friend's shoulder as

[104-151]soon as the party had ridden out of hearing, "do you see that girl

[104-152]there in the dark blue habit?"

[104-153]"What, the one nearest to the path?"

[104-154]"Yes; the one nearest to the path. That is Lily Dale."

[104-155]"Lily Dale!" said Fowler Pratt.

[104-156]"Yes; that is Lily Dale."

[104-157]"Did you speak to her?" Pratt asked.

[104-158]"No; she gave me no chance. She was there but a moment. But it was

[104-159]herself. It seems so odd to me that I should have been thus so near

[104-160]her again." If there was any man to whom Crosbie could have spoken

[104-161]freely about Lily Dale it was this man, Fowler Pratt. Pratt was the

[104-162]oldest friend he had in the world, and it had happened that when he

[104-163]first woke to the misery that he had prepared for himself in throwing

[104-164]over Lily and betrothing himself to his late wife, Pratt had been the

[104-165]first person to whom he had communicated his sorrow. Not that he had

[104-166]ever been really open in his communications. It is not given to such

[104-167]men as Crosbie to speak openly of themselves to their friends. Nor,

[104-168]indeed, was Fowler Pratt one who was fond of listening to such tales.

[104-169]He had no such tales to tell of himself, and he thought that men and

[104-170]women should go through the world quietly, not subjecting themselves

[104-171]or their acquaintances to anxieties and emotions from peculiar

[104-172]conduct. But he was conscientious, and courageous also as well as

[104-173]prudent, and he had dared to tell Crosbie that he was behaving very

[104-174]badly. He had spoken his mind plainly, and had then given all the

[104-175]assistance in his power.
