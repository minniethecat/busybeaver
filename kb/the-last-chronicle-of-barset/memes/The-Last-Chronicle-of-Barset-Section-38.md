[038-001]"Footed it every inch of the way, blowed if he didn't," the ostler

[038-002]was saying to a gentleman's groom, whom Mr. Robarts recognized to be

[038-003]the servant of his friend, Major Grantly; and Mr. Robarts knew that

[038-004]they also were talking about Mr. Crawley. Everybody in the county was

[038-005]talking about Mr. Crawley. At home, at Framley, there was no other

[038-006]subject of discourse. Lady Lufton, the dowager, was full of it, being

[038-007]firmly convinced that Mr. Crawley was innocent, because the bishop

[038-008]was supposed to regard him as guilty. There had been a family

[038-009]conclave held at Framley Court over that basket of provisions which

[038-010]had been sent for the Christmas cheer of the Hogglestock parsonage,

[038-011]each of the three ladies, the two Lady Luftons and Mrs. Robarts,

[038-012]having special views of their own. How the pork had been substituted

[038-013]for the beef by old Lady Lufton, young Lady Lufton thinking that

[038-014]after all the beef would be less dangerous, and how a small turkey

[038-015]had been rashly suggested by Mrs. Robarts, and how certain small

[038-016]articles had been inserted in the bottom of the basket which Mrs.

[038-017]Crawley had never shewn to her husband, need not here be told at

[038-018]length. But Mr. Robarts, as he heard the two grooms talking about

[038-019]Mr. Crawley, began to feel that Mr. Crawley had achieved at least

[038-020]celebrity.

[038-021]The groom touched his hat as Mr. Robarts walked up. "Has the major

[038-022]returned home yet?" Mr. Robarts asked. The groom said that his master

[038-023]was still at Plumstead, and that he was to go over to Plumstead to

[038-024]fetch the major and Miss Edith in a day or two. Then Mr. Robarts got

[038-025]into his gig, and as he drove out of the yard he heard the words of

[038-026]the men as they returned to the same subject. "Footed it all the

[038-027]way," said one. "And yet he's a gen'leman, too," said the other.

[038-028]Mr. Robarts thought of this as he drove on, intending to call at

[038-029]Hogglestock on that very day on his way home. It was undoubtedly the

[038-030]fact that Mr. Crawley was recognized to be a gentleman by all who

[038-031]knew him, high or low, rich or poor, by those who thought well of

[038-032]him and by those who thought ill. These grooms, who had been telling

[038-033]each other that this parson, who was to be tried as a thief, had been

[038-034]constrained to walk from Hogglestock to Barchester and back, because

[038-035]he could not afford to travel in any other way, and that his boots

[038-036]were cracked and his clothes ragged, had still known him to be a

[038-037]gentleman! Nobody doubted it; not even they who thought he had stolen

[038-038]the money. Mr. Robarts himself was certain of it, and told himself

[038-039]that he knew it by evidences which his own education made clear to

[038-040]him. But how was it that the grooms knew it? For my part I think that

[038-041]there are no better judges of the article than the grooms.

[038-042]Thinking still of all which he had heard, Mr. Robarts found himself

[038-043]at Mr. Crawley's gate at Hogglestock.

[038-044]CHAPTER XXI.

[038-045]MR. ROBARTS ON HIS EMBASSY.

[038-046]Mr. Robarts was not altogether easy in his mind as he approached Mr.

[038-047]Crawley's house. He was aware that the task before him was a very

[038-048]difficult one, and he had not confidence in himself,--that he was

[038-049]exactly the man fitted for the performance of such a task. He was a

[038-050]little afraid of Mr. Crawley, acknowledging tacitly to himself that

[038-051]the man had a power of ascendancy with which he would hardly be able

[038-052]to cope successfully. In old days he had once been rebuked by Mr.

[038-053]Crawley, and had been cowed by the rebuke; and though there was no

[038-054]touch of rancour in his heart on this account, no slightest remaining

[038-055]venom,--but rather increased respect and friendship,--still he

[038-056]was unable to overcome the remembrance of the scene in which the

[038-057]perpetual curate of Hogglestock had undoubtedly had the mastery

[038-058]of him. So, when two dogs have fought and one has conquered, the

[038-059]conquered dog will always show an unconscious submission to the

[038-060]conqueror.

[038-061]He hailed a boy on the road as he drew near to the house, knowing

[038-062]that he would find no one at the parsonage to hold his horse for him,

[038-063]and was thus able without delay to walk through the garden and knock

[038-064]at the door. "Papa was not at home," Jane said. "Papa was at the

[038-065]school. But papa could certainly be summoned. She herself would run

[038-066]across to the school if Mr. Robarts would come in." So Mr. Robarts

[038-067]entered, and found Mrs. Crawley in the sitting-room. Mr. Crawley

[038-068]would be in directly, she said. And then, hurrying on to the subject

[038-069]with confused haste, in order that a word or two might be spoken

[038-070]before her husband came back, she expressed her thanks and his for

[038-071]the good things which had been sent to them at Christmas-tide.

[038-072]"It's old Lady Lufton's doings," said Mr. Robarts, trying to laugh

[038-073]the matter over.

[038-074]"I knew that it came from Framley, Mr. Robarts, and I know how good

[038-075]you all are there. I have not written to thank Lady Lufton. I thought

[038-076]it better not to write. Your sister will understand why, if no one

[038-077]else does. But you will tell them from me, I am sure, that it was, as

[038-078]they intended, a comfort to us. Your sister knows too much of us for

[038-079]me to suppose that our great poverty can be secret from her. And, as

[038-080]far as I am concerned, I do not now much care who knows it."

[038-081]"There is no disgrace in not being rich," said Mr. Robarts.

[038-082]"No; and the feeling of disgrace which does attach itself to being so

[038-083]poor as we are is deadened by the actual suffering which such poverty

[038-084]brings with it. At least it has become so with me. I am not ashamed

[038-085]to say that I am very grateful for what you all have done for us at

[038-086]Framley. But you must not say anything to him about that."

[038-087]"Of course I will not, Mrs. Crawley."

[038-088]"His spirit is higher than mine, I think, and he suffers more from

[038-089]the natural disinclination which we all have to receiving alms. Are

[038-090]you going to speak to him about this affair of the--cheque, Mr.

[038-091]Robarts?"

[038-092]"I am going to ask him to put his case into some lawyer's hands."

[038-093]"Oh! I wish he would!"

[038-094]"And will he not?"

[038-095]"It is very kind of you, your coming to ask him, but--"

[038-096]"Has he so strong an objection?"

[038-097]"He will tell you that he has no money to pay a lawyer."

[038-098]"But, surely, if he were convinced that it was absolutely necessary

[038-099]for the vindication of his innocence, he would submit to charge

[038-100]himself with an expense so necessary, not only for himself, but for

[038-101]his family?"

[038-102]"He will say it ought not to be necessary. You know, Mr. Robarts,

[038-103]that in some respects he is not like other men. You will not let what

[038-104]I say of him set you against him?"

[038-105]"Indeed, no."

[038-106]"It is most kind of you to make the attempt. He will be here

[038-107]directly, and when he comes I will leave you together."

[038-108]While she was yet speaking his step was heard along the gravel-path,

[038-109]and he hurried into the room with quick steps. "I crave your pardon,

[038-110]Mr. Robarts," he said, "that I should keep you waiting." Now Mr.

[038-111]Robarts had not been there ten minutes, and any such asking of

[038-112]pardon was hardly necessary. And, even in his own house, Mr.

[038-113]Crawley affected a mock humility, as though, either through

[038-114]his own debasement, or because of the superior station of the

[038-115]other clergyman, he were not entitled to put himself on an equal

[038-116]footing with his visitor. He would not have shaken hands with Mr.

[038-117]Robarts,--intending to indicate that he did not presume to do so

[038-118]while the present accusation was hanging over him,--had not the

[038-119]action been forced upon him. And then there was something of a

[038-120]protest in his manner, as though remonstrating against a thing that

[038-121]was unbecoming to him. Mr. Robarts, without analysing it, understood

[038-122]it all, and knew that behind the humility there was a crushing

[038-123]pride,--a pride which, in all probability, would rise up and crush

[038-124]him before he could get himself out of the room again. It was,

[038-125]perhaps, after all, a question whether the man was not served rightly

[038-126]by the extremities to which he was reduced. There was something

[038-127]radically wrong within him, which had put him into antagonism with

[038-128]all the world, and which produced these never-dying grievances. There

[038-129]were many clergymen in the country with incomes as small as that

[038-130]which had fallen to the lot of Mr. Crawley, but they managed to

[038-131]get on without displaying their sores as Mr. Crawley displayed his.

[038-132]They did not wear their old rusty cloaks with all that ostentatious

[038-133]bitterness of poverty which seemed to belong to that garment when

[038-134]displayed on Mr. Crawley's shoulders. Such, for a moment, were Mr.

[038-135]Robarts' thoughts, and he almost repented himself of his present

[038-136]mission. But then he thought of Mrs. Crawley, and remembering that

[038-137]her sufferings were at any rate undeserved, determined that he would

[038-138]persevere.

[038-139]Mrs. Crawley disappeared almost as soon as her husband appeared,

[038-140]and Mr. Robarts found himself standing in front of his friend, who

[038-141]remained fixed on the spot, with his hands folded over each other

[038-142]and his neck slightly bent forward, in token also of humility. "I

[038-143]regret," he said, "that your horse should be left there, exposed to

[038-144]the inclemency of the weather; but--"

[038-145]"The horse won't mind it a bit," said Mr. Robarts. "A parson's horse

[038-146]is like a butcher's, and knows that he mustn't be particular about

[038-147]waiting in the cold."

[038-148]"I never have had one myself," said Mr. Crawley. Now Mr. Robarts had

[038-149]had more horses than one before now, and had been thought by some

[038-150]to have incurred greater expense than was befitting in his stable

[038-151]comforts. The subject, therefore, was a sore one, and he was worried

[038-152]a little. "I just wanted to say a few words to you, Crawley," he

[038-153]said, "and if I am not occupying too much of your time--"

[038-154]"My time is altogether at your disposal. Will you be seated?"

[038-155]Then Mr. Robarts sat down, and, swinging his hat between his

[038-156]legs, bethought himself how he should begin his work. "We had the

[038-157]archdeacon over at Framley the other day," he said. "Of course you

[038-158]know the archdeacon?"

[038-159]"I never had the advantage of any acquaintance with Dr. Grantly. Of

[038-160]course I know him well by name, and also personally,--that is, by

[038-161]sight."

[038-162]"And by character?"

[038-163]"Nay; I can hardly say so much as that. But I am aware that his name

[038-164]stands high with many of his order."

[038-165]"Exactly; that is what I mean. You know that his judgment is thought

[038-166]more of in clerical matters than that of any other clergyman in the

[038-167]county."

[038-168]"By a certain party, Mr. Robarts."

[038-169]"Well, yes. They don't think much of him, I suppose, at the palace.

[038-170]But that won't lower him in your estimation."

[038-171]"I by no means wish to derogate from Dr. Grantly's high position

[038-172]in his own archdeaconry,--to which, as you are aware, I am not

[038-173]attached,--nor to criticize his conduct in any respect. It would be

[038-174]unbecoming in me to do so. But I cannot accept it as a point in a

[038-175]clergyman's favour, that he should be opposed to his bishop."

[038-176]Now this was too much for Mr. Robarts. After all that he had heard of

[038-177]the visit paid by Mr. Crawley to the palace,--of the venom displayed

[038-178]by Mrs. Proudie on that occasion, and of the absolute want of

[038-179]subordination to episcopal authority which Mr. Crawley himself was

[038-180]supposed to have shown,--Mr. Robarts did feel it hard that his friend

[038-181]the archdeacon should be snubbed in this way because he was deficient

[038-182]in reverence for his bishop! "I thought, Crawley," he said, "that

[038-183]you yourself were inclined to dispute orders coming to you from the

[038-184]palace. The world at least says as much concerning you."
