[112-002]from his chair. "If I am to do it, I had better do it at once."

[112-003]"Be gentle with her, my friend." The archdeacon paused again. He

[112-004]certainly had entertained the idea of encountering Miss Crawley with

[112-005]severity rather than gentleness. Lady Lufton rose from her seat, and

[112-006]coming up to him, took one of his hands between her own two. "Be

[112-007]gentle to her," she said. "You have owned that she has done nothing

[112-008]wrong." The archdeacon bowed his head in token of assent and left the

[112-009]room.

[112-010]Poor Grace Crawley!

[112-011]CHAPTER LVII.

[112-012]A DOUBLE PLEDGE.

[112-013]The archdeacon, as he walked across from the Court to the parsonage,

[112-014]was very thoughtful and his steps were very slow. This idea of seeing

[112-015]Miss Crawley herself had been suggested to him suddenly, and he had

[112-016]to determine how he would bear himself towards her, and what he would

[112-017]say to her. Lady Lufton had beseeched him to be gentle with her. Was

[112-018]the mission one in which gentleness would be possible? Must it not be

[112-019]his object to make this young lady understand that she could not be

[112-020]right in desiring to come into his family and share in all his good

[112-021]things when she had got no good things of her own,--nothing but evil

[112-022]things to bring with her? And how could this be properly explained to

[112-023]the young lady in gentle terms? Must he not be round with her, and

[112-024]give her to understand in plain words,--the plainest which he could

[112-025]use,--that she would not get his good things, though she would most

[112-026]certainly impose the burden of all her evil things on the man whom

[112-027]she was proposing to herself as a husband. He remembered very well as

[112-028]he went, that he had been told that Miss Crawley had herself refused

[112-029]the offer, feeling herself to be unfit for the honour tendered to

[112-030]her; but he suspected the sincerity of such a refusal. Calculating

[112-031]in his own mind the unreasonably great advantages which would be

[112-032]conferred on such a young lady as Miss Crawley by a marriage with

[112-033]his son, he declared to himself that any girl must be very wicked

[112-034]indeed who should expect, or even accept, so much more than was her

[112-035]due;--but nevertheless he could not bring himself to believe that

[112-036]any girl, when so tempted, would, in sincerity, decline to commit

[112-037]this great wickedness. If he was to do any good by seeing Miss

[112-038]Crawley, must it not consist in a proper explanation to her of the

[112-039]selfishness, abomination, and altogether damnable blackness of such

[112-040]wickedness as this on the part of a young woman in her circumstances?

[112-041]"Heaven and earth!" he must say, "here are you, without a penny in

[112-042]your pocket, with hardly decent raiment on your back, with a thief

[112-043]for your father, and you think that you are to come and share in

[112-044]all the wealth that the Grantlys have amassed, that you are to have

[112-045]a husband with broad acres, a big house, and game preserves, and

[112-046]become one of a family whose name has never been touched by a single

[112-047]accusation,--no, not by a suspicion? No;--injustice such as that

[112-048]shall never be done betwixt you and me. You may wring my heart, and

[112-049]you may ruin my son; but the broad acres and the big house, and the

[112-050]game preserves, and the rest of it, shall never be your reward for

[112-051]doing so." How was all that to be told effectively to a young woman

[112-052]in gentle words? And then how was a man in the archdeacon's position

[112-053]to be desirous of gentle words,--gentle words which would not be

[112-054]efficient,--when he knew well in his heart of hearts that he had

[112-055]nothing but his threats on which to depend. He had no more power of

[112-056]disinheriting his own son for such an offence as that contemplated

[112-057]than he had of blowing out his own brains, and he knew that it was

[112-058]so. He was a man incapable of such persistency of wrath against one

[112-059]whom he loved. He was neither cruel enough nor strong enough to do

[112-060]such a thing. He could only threaten to do it, and make what best use

[112-061]he might of threats, whilst threats might be of avail. In spite of

[112-062]all that he had said to his wife, to Lady Lufton, and to himself, he

[112-063]knew very well that if his son did sin in this way he, the father,

[112-064]would forgive the sin of the son.

[112-065]In going across from the front gate of the Court to the parsonage

[112-066]there was a place where three roads met, and on this spot there stood

[112-067]a finger-post. Round this finger-post there was now pasted a placard,

[112-068]which at once arrested the archdeacon's eye:--"Cosby Lodge--Sale

[112-069]of furniture--Growing crops to be sold on the grounds. Three

[112-070]hunters. A brown gelding warranted for saddle or harness!"--The

[112-071]archdeacon himself had given the brown gelding to his son, as a great

[112-072]treasure.--"Three Alderney cows, two cow-calves, a low phaeton, a

[112-073]gig, two ricks of hay." In this fashion were proclaimed in odious

[112-074]details all those comfortable additions to a gentleman's house in the

[112-075]country, with which the archdeacon was so well acquainted. Only last

[112-076]November he had recommended his son to buy a certain new-invented

[112-077]clod-crusher, and the clod-crusher had of course been bought. The

[112-078]bright blue paint upon it had not as yet given way to the stains of

[112-079]the ordinary farmyard muck and mire;--and here was the clod-crusher

[112-080]advertised for sale! The archdeacon did not want his son to leave

[112-081]Cosby Lodge. He knew well enough that his son need not leave Cosby

[112-082]Lodge. Why had the foolish fellow been in such a hurry with his

[112-083]hideous ill-conditioned advertisements? Gentle! How was he in such

[112-084]circumstances to be gentle? He raised his umbrella and poked angrily

[112-085]at the disgusting notice. The iron ferule caught the paper at a chink

[112-086]in the post, and tore it from the top to the bottom. But what was the

[112-087]use? A horrid ugly bill lying torn in such a spot would attract only

[112-088]more attention than one fixed to a post. He could not condescend,

[112-089]however, to give to it further attention, but passed on up to the

[112-090]parsonage. Gentle, indeed!

[112-091]Nevertheless Archdeacon Grantly was a gentleman, and never yet had

[112-092]dealt more harshly with any woman than we have sometimes seen him do

[112-093]with his wife,--when he would say to her an angry word or two with

[112-094]a good deal of marital authority. His wife, who knew well what his

[112-095]angry words were worth, never even suggested to herself that she had

[112-096]cause for complaint on that head. Had she known that the archdeacon

[112-097]was about to undertake such a mission as this which he had now in

[112-098]hand, she would not have warned him to be gentle. She, indeed, would

[112-099]have strongly advised him not to undertake the mission, cautioning

[112-100]him that the young lady would probably get the better of him.

[112-101]"Grace, my dear," said Mrs. Robarts, coming up into the nursery in

[112-102]which Miss Crawley was sitting with the children, "come out here a

[112-103]moment, will you?" Then Grace left the children and went out into the

[112-104]passage. "My dear, there is a gentleman in the drawing-room who asks

[112-105]to see you."

[112-106]"A gentleman, Mrs. Robarts! What gentleman?" But Grace, though she

[112-107]asked the question, conceived that the gentleman must be Henry

[112-108]Grantly. Her mind did not suggest to her the possibility of any other

[112-109]gentleman coming to see her.

[112-110]"You must not be surprised, or allow yourself to be frightened."

[112-111]"Oh, Mrs. Robarts, who is it?"

[112-112]"It is Major Grantly's father."

[112-113]"The archdeacon?"

[112-114]"Yes, dear; Archdeacon Grantly. He is in the drawing-room."

[112-115]"Must I see him, Mrs. Robarts?"

[112-116]"Well, Grace,--I think you must. I hardly know how you can refuse. He

[112-117]is an intimate friend of everybody here at Framley."

[112-118]"What will he say to me?"

[112-119]"Nay; that I cannot tell. I suppose you know--"

[112-120]"He has come, no doubt, to bid me have nothing to say to his son. He

[112-121]need not have troubled himself. But he may say what he likes. I am

[112-122]not a coward, and I will go to him."

[112-123]"Stop a moment, Grace. Come into my room for an instant. The children

[112-124]have pulled your hair about." But Grace, though she followed Mrs.

[112-125]Robarts into the bedroom, would have nothing done to her hair. She

[112-126]was too proud for that,--and we may say, also, too little confident

[112-127]in any good which such resources might effect on her behalf. "Never

[112-128]mind about that," she said. "What am I to say to him?" Mrs. Robarts

[112-129]paused before she replied, feeling that the matter was one which

[112-130]required some deliberation. "Tell me what I must say to him?" said

[112-131]Grace, repeating her question.

[112-132]"I hardly know what your own feelings are, my dear."

[112-133]"Yes, you do. You do know. If I had all the world to give, I would

[112-134]give it all to Major Grantly."

[112-135]"Tell him that, then."

[112-136]"No, I will not tell him that. Never mind about my frock, Mrs.

[112-137]Robarts. I do not care for that. I will tell him that I love his

[112-138]son and his granddaughter too well to injure them. I will tell him

[112-139]nothing else. I might as well go now." Mrs. Robarts, as she looked at

[112-140]Grace, was astonished at the serenity of her face. And yet when her

[112-141]hand was on the drawing-room door Grace hesitated, looked back, and

[112-142]trembled. Mrs. Robarts blew a kiss to her from the stairs; and then

[112-143]the door was opened, and the girl found herself in the presence of

[112-144]the archdeacon. He was standing on the rug, with his back to the

[112-145]fire, and his heavy ecclesiastical hat was placed on the middle of

[112-146]the round table. The hat caught Grace's eye at the moment of her

[112-147]entrance, and she felt that all the thunders of the Church were

[112-148]contained within it. And then the archdeacon himself was so big and

[112-149]so clerical, and so imposing! Her father's aspect was severe, but the

[112-150]severity of her father's face was essentially different from that

[112-151]expressed by the archdeacon. Whatever impression came from her father

[112-152]came from the man himself. There was no outward adornment there;

[112-153]there was, so to say, no wig about Mr. Crawley. Now the archdeacon

[112-154]was not exactly adorned; but he was so thoroughly imbued with high

[112-155]clerical belongings and sacerdotal fitnesses as to appear always as

[112-156]a walking, sitting, or standing impersonation of parsondom. To poor

[112-157]Grace, as she entered the room, he appeared to be an impersonation of

[112-158]parsondom in its severest aspect.

[112-159]"Miss Crawley, I believe?" said he.

[112-160]"Yes, sir," said she, curtseying ever so slightly, as she stood

[112-161]before him at some considerable distance.

[112-162]His first idea was that his son must be indeed a fool if he was going

[112-163]to give up Cosby Lodge and all Barsetshire, and retire to Pau, for so

[112-164]slight and unattractive a creature as he now saw before him. But this

[112-165]idea stayed with him only for a moment. As he continued to gaze at

[112-166]her during the interview he came to perceive that there was very

[112-167]much more than he had perceived at the first glance, and that his

[112-168]son, after all, had had eyes to see, though perhaps not a heart to

[112-169]understand.

[112-170]"Will you not take a chair?" he said. Then Grace sat down, still at

[112-171]a distance from the archdeacon, and he kept his place upon the rug.

[112-172]He felt that there would be a difficulty in making her feel the

[112-173]full force of his eloquence all across the room; and yet he did

[112-174]not know how to bring himself nearer to her. She became suddenly

[112-175]very important in his eyes, and he was to some extent afraid of

[112-176]her. She was so slight, so meek, so young; and yet there was about

[112-177]her something so beautifully feminine,--and, withal, so like a

[112-178]lady,--that he felt instinctively that he could not attack her with

[112-179]harsh words. Had her lips been full, and her colour high, and had

[112-180]her eyes rolled, had she put forth against him any of that ordinary

[112-181]artillery with which youthful feminine batteries are charged, he

[112-182]would have been ready to rush to the combat. But this girl, about

[112-183]whom his son had gone mad, sat there as passively as though she

[112-184]were conscious of the possession of no artillery. There was not a

[112-185]single gun fired from beneath her eyelids. He knew not why, but he

[112-186]respected his son now more than he had respected him for the last two

[112-187]months;--more, perhaps, than he had ever respected him before. He was

[112-188]as eager as ever against the marriage;--but in thinking of his son in

[112-189]what he said and did after these few first moments of the interview,

[112-190]he ceased to think of him with contempt. The creature before him was

[112-191]a woman who grew in his opinion till he began to feel that she was in

[112-192]truth fit to be the wife of his son--if only she were not a pauper,

[112-193]and the daughter of a mad curate, and, alas! too probably, of a

[112-194]thief. Though his feeling towards the girl was changed, his duty to

[112-195]himself, his family, and his son, was the same as ever, and therefore

[112-196]he began his task.
