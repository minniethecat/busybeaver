## Character

Bishop Proudie is the bishop of the diocese of Barchester — a position of immense ecclesiastical authority, yet held by a man singularly incapable of exercising it. Trollope introduces him through the letters he sends in the Crawley affair, letters that cause "vexation" to a man of Crawley's temperament (002-137). The Bishop is not a bad man; he is a weak one, a man who knows what is right but lacks the force of character to act upon it. His tragedy — and his comedy — is that his wife, [[mrs-proudie|Mrs. Proudie]], wields the authority that properly belongs to his office. "Bishop Proudie sat alone in his study" (091-085): the image of the solitary bishop, master of his diocese but not of his own household, is one of Trollope's sharpest character sketches.

## Biography

Bishop Proudie came to the see of Barchester through the workings of ecclesiastical politics rather than through any particular distinction of learning or character. He "knew that Mr. Crawley was not" the sort of clergyman who would bend to his authority, which both troubled and relieved him (020-100). In the Crawley affair, the Bishop finds himself caught between his pastoral duty to a distressed clergyman and his wife's determination to crush what she perceives as clerical insubordination. He consults, hesitates, and ultimately defers — the pattern of his entire episcopate. His relationship with Mrs. Proudie is the defining fact of his life: "He knew Mrs. Proudie also" (063-009), Trollope writes with devastating understatement, for to know Mrs. Proudie is to be governed by her.

## Significance

Bishop Proudie is Trollope's most sustained portrait of institutional weakness. In a novel concerned with authority — who has it, who deserves it, and how it should be exercised — the Bishop stands as a cautionary figure. He possesses all the formal attributes of power (the palace, the diocese, the title) but none of the personal qualities required to wield it. His wife's dominance over him is both comic and pathetic, a domestic inversion that mirrors the novel's larger anxieties about rightful authority. Trollope never condemns the Bishop outright; instead, he invites the reader to pity a man whose office exceeds his character. The Bishop's silence in the face of his wife's machinations is itself a form of moral failure, one that contributes to the suffering of the Crawley family.
