[106-001]It was now April, and the judges were to come into Barchester

[106-002]before the end of the month. What then could be the use of this

[106-003]ecclesiastical inquiry exactly at the same time? Men and women

[106-004]declared that it was a double prosecution, and that a double

[106-005]prosecution for the same offence was a course of action opposed to

[106-006]the feelings and traditions of the country. Miss Anne Prettyman

[106-007]went so far as to say that it was unconstitutional, and Mary Walker

[106-008]declared that no human being except Mrs. Proudie would ever have been

[106-009]guilty of such cruelty. "Don't tell me about the bishop, John," she

[106-010]said; "the bishop is a cypher." "You may be sure Dr. Tempest would

[106-011]not have a hand in it if it were not right," said John Walker. "My

[106-012]dear Mr. John," said Miss Anne Prettyman, "Dr. Tempest is as hard as

[106-013]a bar of iron, and always was. But I am surprised that Mr. Robarts

[106-014]should take a part in it."

[106-015]In the meantime, at the palace, Mrs. Proudie had been reduced to

[106-016]learn what was going on from Mr. Thumble. The bishop had never spoken

[106-017]a word to her respecting Mr. Crawley since that terrible day on which

[106-018]Dr. Tempest had witnessed his imbecility,--having absolutely declined

[106-019]to answer when his wife had mentioned the subject. "You won't speak

[106-020]to me about it, my dear?" she had said to him, when he had thus

[106-021]declined, remonstrating more in sorrow than in anger. "No; I won't,"

[106-022]the bishop had replied; "there has been a great deal too much talking

[106-023]about it. It has broken my heart already, I know." These were very

[106-024]bad days in the palace. Mrs. Proudie affected to be satisfied with

[106-025]what was being done. She talked to Mr. Thumble about Mr. Crawley

[106-026]and the cheque, as though everything were arranged quite to her

[106-027]satisfaction,--as though everything, indeed, had been arranged by

[106-028]herself. But everybody about the house could see that the manner of

[106-029]the woman was altogether altered. She was milder than usual with the

[106-030]servants and was almost too gentle in her usage of her husband. It

[106-031]seemed as though something had happened to frighten her and break

[106-032]her spirit, and it was whispered about through the palace that she

[106-033]was afraid that the bishop was dying. As for him, he hardly left his

[106-034]own sitting-room in these days, except when he joined the family at

[106-035]breakfast and at dinner. And in his study he did little or nothing.

[106-036]He would smile when his chaplain went to him, and give some trifling

[106-037]verbal directions; but for days he scarcely ever took a pen in his

[106-038]hands, and though he took up many books he read hardly a page. How

[106-039]often he told his wife in those days that he was broken-hearted, no

[106-040]one but his wife ever knew.

[106-041]"What has happened that you should speak like that?" she said to him

[106-042]once. "What has broken your heart?"

[106-043]"You," he replied. "You; you have done it."

[106-044]"Oh, Tom," she said, going back into the memory of very far distant

[106-045]days in her nomenclature, "how can you speak to me so cruelly as

[106-046]that! That it should come to that between you and me, after all!"

[106-047]"Why did you not go away and leave me that day when I told you?"

[106-048]"Did you ever know a woman who liked to be turned out of a room in

[106-049]her own house?" said Mrs. Proudie. When Mrs. Proudie had condescended

[106-050]so far as this, it must be admitted that in those days there was

[106-051]great trouble in the palace.

[106-052]Mr. Thumble, on the day before he went to Silverbridge, asked for an

[106-053]audience with the bishop in order that he might receive instructions.

[106-054]He had been strictly desired to do this by Mrs. Proudie, and had not

[106-055]dared to disobey her injunctions,--thinking, however, himself, that

[106-056]his doing so was inexpedient. "I have got nothing to say to you about

[106-057]it; not a word," said the bishop crossly. "I thought that perhaps

[106-058]you might like to see me before I started," pleaded Mr. Thumble very

[106-059]humbly. "I don't want to see you at all," said the bishop; "you are

[106-060]going there to exercise your own judgment,--if you have got any;

[106-061]and you ought not to come to me." After that Mr. Thumble began to

[106-062]think that Mrs. Proudie was right, and that the bishop was near his

[106-063]dissolution.

[106-064]Mr. Thumble and Mr. Quiverful went over to Silverbridge together in

[106-065]a gig, hired from the "Dragon of Wantly"--as to the cost of which

[106-066]there arose among them a not unnatural apprehension which amounted

[106-067]at last almost to dismay. "I don't mind it so much for once," said

[106-068]Mr. Quiverful, "but if many such meetings are necessary, I for one

[106-069]can't afford it, and I won't do it. A man with my family can't allow

[106-070]himself to be money out of pocket in that way." "It is hard," said

[106-071]Mr. Thumble. "She ought to pay it herself, out of her own pocket,"

[106-072]said Mr. Quiverful. He had had concerns with the palace when Mrs.

[106-073]Proudie was in the full swing of her dominion, and had not as yet

[106-074]begun to suspect that there might possibly be a change.

[106-075]Mr. Oriel and Mr. Robarts were already sitting with Dr. Tempest when

[106-076]the other two clergymen were shown into the room. When the first

[106-077]greetings were over luncheon was announced, and while they were

[106-078]eating not a word was said about Mr. Crawley. The ladies of the

[106-079]family were not present, and the five clergymen sat round the table

[106-080]alone. It would have been difficult to have got together five

[106-081]gentlemen less likely to act with one mind and one spirit;--and

[106-082]perhaps it was all the better for Mr. Crawley that it should be so.

[106-083]Dr. Tempest himself was a man peculiarly capable of exercising the

[106-084]functions of a judge in such a matter, had he sat alone as a judge;

[106-085]but he was one who would be almost sure to differ from others who sat

[106-086]as equal assessors with him. Mr. Oriel was a gentleman at all points;

[106-087]but he was very shy, very reticent, and altogether uninstructed in

[106-088]the ordinary daily intercourse of man with man. Any one knowing him

[106-089]might have predicted of him that he would be sure on such an occasion

[106-090]as this to be found floundering in a sea of doubts. Mr. Quiverful was

[106-091]the father of a large family, whose whole life had been devoted to

[106-092]fighting a cruel world on behalf of his wife and children. That fight

[106-093]he had fought bravely; but it had left him no energy for any other

[106-094]business. Mr. Thumble was a poor creature,--so poor a creature that,

[106-095]in spite of a small restless ambition to be doing something, he was

[106-096]almost cowed by the hard lines of Dr. Tempest's brow. The Rev. Mark

[106-097]Robarts was a man of the world, and a clever fellow, and did not

[106-098]stand in awe of anybody,--unless it might be, in a very moderate

[106-099]degree, of his patrons the Luftons, whom he was bound to respect;

[106-100]but his cleverness was not the cleverness needed by a judge. He was

[106-101]essentially a partisan, and would be sure to vote against the bishop

[106-102]in such a matter as this now before him. There was a palace faction

[106-103]in the diocese, and an anti-palace faction. Mr. Thumble and Mr.

[106-104]Quiverful belonged to one, and Mr. Oriel and Mr. Robarts to the

[106-105]other. Mr. Thumble was too weak to stick to his faction against the

[106-106]strength of such a man as Dr. Tempest. Mr. Quiverful would be too

[106-107]indifferent to do so,--unless his interest were concerned. Mr. Oriel

[106-108]would be too conscientious to regard his own side on such an occasion

[106-109]as this. But Mark Robarts would be sure to support his friends and

[106-110]oppose his enemies, let the case be what it might. "Now, gentlemen,

[106-111]if you please, we will go into the other room," said Dr. Tempest.

[106-112]They went into the other room, and there they found five chairs

[106-113]arranged for them round the table. Not a word had as yet been said

[106-114]about Mr. Crawley, and no one of the four strangers knew whether Mr.

[106-115]Crawley was to appear before them on that day or not.

[106-116]"Gentlemen," said Dr. Tempest, seating himself at once in an

[106-117]arm-chair placed at the middle of the table, "I think it will be

[106-118]well to explain to you at first what, as I regard the matter, is the

[106-119]extent of the work which we are called upon to perform. It is of its

[106-120]nature very disagreeable. It cannot but be so, let it be ever so

[106-121]limited. Here is a brother clergyman and a gentleman, living among

[106-122]us, and doing his duty, as we are told, in a most exemplary manner;

[106-123]and suddenly we hear that he is accused of a theft. The matter is

[106-124]brought before the magistrates, of whom I myself was one, and he was

[106-125]committed for trial. There is therefore primâ facie evidence of his

[106-126]guilt. But I do not think that we need go into the question of his

[106-127]guilt at all." When he said this, the other four all looked up at

[106-128]him in astonishment. "I thought that we had been summoned here for

[106-129]that purpose," said Mr. Robarts. "Not at all, as I take it," said

[106-130]the doctor. "Were we to commence any such inquiry, the jury would

[106-131]have given their verdict before we could come to any conclusion; and

[106-132]it would be impossible for us to oppose that verdict, whether it

[106-133]declares this unfortunate gentleman to be innocent or to be guilty.

[106-134]If the jury shall say that he is innocent, there is an end of the

[106-135]matter altogether. He would go back to his parish amidst the sympathy

[106-136]and congratulations of his friends. That is what we should all wish."

[106-137]"Of course it is," said Mr. Robarts. They all declared that was their

[106-138]desire, as a matter of course; and Mr. Thumble said it louder than

[106-139]any one else.

[106-140]"But if he be found guilty, then will come that difficulty to the

[106-141]bishop, in which we are bound to give him any assistance within our

[106-142]power."

[106-143]"Of course we are," said Mr. Thumble, who, having heard his own voice

[106-144]once, and having liked the sound, thought that he might creep into a

[106-145]little importance by using it on any occasion that opened itself for

[106-146]him.

[106-147]"If you will allow me, sir, I will venture to state my views as

[106-148]shortly as I can," said Dr. Tempest. "That may perhaps be the most

[106-149]expeditious course for us all in the end."

[106-150]"Oh, certainly," said Mr. Thumble. "I didn't mean to interrupt."

[106-151]"In the case of his being found guilty," continued the doctor, "there

[106-152]will arise the question whether the punishment awarded to him by

[106-153]the judge should suffice for ecclesiastical purposes. Suppose, for

[106-154]instance, that he should be imprisoned for two months, should he be

[106-155]allowed to return to his living at the expiration of that term?"

[106-156]"I think he ought," said Mr. Robarts;--"considering all things."

[106-157]"I don't see why he shouldn't," said Mr. Quiverful.

[106-158]Mr. Oriel sat listening patiently, and Mr. Thumble looked up to the

[106-159]doctor, expecting to hear some opinion expressed by him with which he

[106-160]might coincide.

[106-161]"There certainly are reasons why he should not," said Dr. Tempest;

[106-162]"though I by no means say that those reasons are conclusive in the

[106-163]present case. In the first place, a man who has stolen money can

[106-164]hardly be a fitting person to teach others not to steal."

[106-165]"You must look to the circumstances," said Robarts.

[106-166]"Yes, that is true; but just bear with me a moment. It cannot, at any

[106-167]rate, be thought that a clergyman should come out of prison and go

[106-168]to his living without any notice from his bishop, simply because he

[106-169]has already been punished under the common law. If this were so, a

[106-170]clergyman might be fined ten days running for being drunk in the

[106-171]street,--five shillings each time,--and at the end of that time might

[106-172]set his bishop at defiance. When a clergyman has shown himself to

[106-173]be utterly unfit for clerical duties, he must not be held to be

[106-174]protected from ecclesiastical censure or from deprivation by the

[106-175]action of the common law."
