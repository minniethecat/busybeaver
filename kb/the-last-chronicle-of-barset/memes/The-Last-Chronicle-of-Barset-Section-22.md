[022-001]When she had been alone for a few minutes, Mrs. Crawley got up from

[022-002]her chair, and going into the kitchen, lighted the fire there, and

[022-003]put the kettle over it, and began to prepare such breakfast for

[022-004]her husband as the means in the house afforded. Then she called

[022-005]the sleeping servant-girl, who was little more than a child, and

[022-006]went into her own girl's room, and then she got into bed with her

[022-007]daughter.

[022-008]"I have been up with your papa, dear, and I am cold."

[022-009]"Oh, mamma, poor mamma! Why is papa up so early?"

[022-010]"He has gone out to visit some of the brickmakers before they go to

[022-011]their work. It is better for him to be employed."

[022-012]"But, mamma, it is pitch dark."

[022-013]"Yes, dear, it is still dark. Sleep again for a while, and I will

[022-014]sleep too. I think Grace will be here to-night, and then there will

[022-015]be no room for me here."

[022-016]Mr. Crawley went forth and made his way with rapid steps to a portion

[022-017]of his parish nearly two miles distant from his house, through which

[022-018]was carried a canal, affording water communication in some intricate

[022-019]way both to London and Bristol. And on the brink of this canal there

[022-020]had sprung up a colony of brickmakers, the nature of the earth in

[022-021]those parts combining with the canal to make brickmaking a suitable

[022-022]trade. The workmen there assembled were not, for the most part,

[022-023]native-born Hogglestockians, or folk descended from Hogglestockian

[022-024]parents. They had come thither from unknown regions, as labourers of

[022-025]that class do come when they are needed. Some young men from that and

[022-026]neighbouring parishes had joined themselves to the colony, allured by

[022-027]wages, and disregarding the menaces of the neighbouring farmers; but

[022-028]they were all in appearance and manners nearer akin to the race of

[022-029]navvies than to ordinary rural labourers. They had a bad name in the

[022-030]country; but it may be that their name was worse than their deserts.

[022-031]The farmers hated them, and consequently they hated the farmers.

[022-032]They had a beershop, and a grocer's shop, and a huxter's shop for

[022-033]their own accommodation, and were consequently vilified by the small

[022-034]old-established tradesmen around them. They got drunk occasionally,

[022-035]but I doubt whether they drank more than did the farmers themselves

[022-036]on market-day. They fought among themselves sometimes, but they

[022-037]forgave each other freely, and seemed to have no objection to black

[022-038]eyes. I fear that they were not always good to their wives, nor

[022-039]were their wives always good to them; but it should be remembered

[022-040]that among the poor, especially when they live in clusters, such

[022-041]misfortunes cannot be hidden as they may be amidst the decent

[022-042]belongings of more wealthy people. That they worked very hard was

[022-043]certain; and it was certain also that very few of their number ever

[022-044]came upon the poor rates. What became of the old brickmakers no one

[022-045]knew. Who ever sees a worn-out aged navvie?

[022-046]Mr. Crawley, ever since his first coming into Hogglestock, had been

[022-047]very busy among these brickmakers, and by no means without success.

[022-048]Indeed the farmers had quarrelled with him because the brickmakers

[022-049]had so crowded the narrow parish church, as to leave but scant room

[022-050]for decent people. "Doo they folk pay tithes? That's what I want

[022-051]'un to tell me?" argued one farmer,--not altogether unnaturally,

[022-052]believing as he did that Mr. Crawley was paid by tithes out of his

[022-053]own pocket. But Mr. Crawley had done his best to make the brickmakers

[022-054]welcome at the church, scandalizing the farmers by causing them

[022-055]to sit or stand in any portion of the church which was hitherto

[022-056]unappropriated. He had been constant in his personal visits to them,

[022-057]and had felt himself to be more a St. Paul with them than with any

[022-058]other of his neighbours around him.

[022-059]It was a cold morning, but the rain of the preceding evening had

[022-060]given way to frost, and the air, though sharp, was dry. The ground

[022-061]under the feet was crisp, having felt the wind and frost, and was no

[022-062]longer clogged with mud. In his present state of mind the walk was

[022-063]good for our poor pastor, and exhilarated him; but still, as he went,

[022-064]he thought always of his injuries. His own wife believed that he was

[022-065]about to commit suicide, and for so believing he was very angry with

[022-066]her; and yet, as he well knew, the idea of making away with himself

[022-067]had flitted through his own mind a dozen times. Not from his own wife

[022-068]could he get real sympathy. He would see what he could do with a

[022-069]certain brickmaker of his acquaintance.

[022-070]"Are you here, Dan?" he said, knocking at the door of a cottage which

[022-071]stood alone, close to the towing-path of the canal, and close also to

[022-072]a forlorn corner of the muddy, watery, ugly, disordered brickfield.

[022-073]It was now just past six o'clock, and the men would be rising, as

[022-074]in midwinter they commenced their work at seven. The cottage was an

[022-075]unalluring, straight brick-built tenement, seeming as though intended

[022-076]to be one of a row which had never progressed beyond Number One. A

[022-077]voice answered from the interior, inquiring who was the visitor, to

[022-078]which Mr. Crawley replied by giving his name. Then the key was turned

[022-079]in the lock, and Dan Morris, the brickmaker, appeared with a candle

[022-080]in his hand. He had been engaged in lighting the fire, with a view to

[022-081]his own breakfast. "Where is your wife, Dan?" asked Mr. Crawley. The

[022-082]man answered by pointing with a short poker, which he held in his

[022-083]hand, to the bed, which was half screened from the room by a ragged

[022-084]curtain, which hung from the ceiling half-way down to the floor. "And

[022-085]are the Darvels here?" asked Mr. Crawley. Then Morris, again using

[022-086]the poker, pointed upwards, showing that the Darvels were still in

[022-087]their own allotted abode upstairs.

[022-088]"You're early out, Muster Crawley," said Morris, and then he went on

[022-089]with his fire. "Drat the sticks, if they bean't as wet as the old

[022-090]'un hisself. Get up, old woman, and do you do it, for I can't. They

[022-091]wun't kindle for me, nohow." But the old woman, having well noted the

[022-092]presence of Mr. Crawley, thought it better to remain where she was.

[022-093]Mr. Crawley sat himself down by the obstinate fire, and began to

[022-094]arrange the sticks. "Dan, Dan," said a voice from the bed, "sure you

[022-095]wouldn't let his reverence trouble himself with the fire."

[022-096]"How be I to keep him from it, if he chooses? I didn't ax him." Then

[022-097]Morris stood by and watched, and after a while Mr. Crawley succeeded

[022-098]in his attempt.

[022-099]"How could it burn when you had not given the small spark a current

[022-100]of air to help it?" said Mr. Crawley.

[022-101]"In course not," said the woman, "but he be such a stupid."

[022-102]The husband said no word in acknowledgment of this compliment, nor

[022-103]did he thank Mr. Crawley for what he had done, nor appear as though

[022-104]he intended to take any notice of him. He was going on with his work

[022-105]when Mr. Crawley again interrupted him.

[022-106]"How did you get back from Silverbridge yesterday, Dan?"

[022-107]"Footed it,--all the blessed way."

[022-108]"It's only eight miles."

[022-109]"And I footed it there, and that's sixteen. And I paid

[022-110]one-and-sixpence for beer and grub;--s'help me, I did."

[022-111]"Dan!" said the voice from the bed, rebuking him for the impropriety

[022-112]of his language.

[022-113]"Well; I beg pardon, but I did. And they guv' me two bob;--just two

[022-114]plain shillings, by ----"

[022-115]"Dan!"

[022-116]"And I'd 've arned three-and-six here at brickmaking easy; that's

[022-117]what I would. How's a poor man to live that way? They'll not cotch me

[022-118]at Barchester 'Sizes at that price; they may be sure of that. Look

[022-119]there,--that's what I've got for my day." And he put his hand into

[022-120]his breeches'-pocket and fetched out a sixpence. "How's a man to fill

[022-121]his belly out of that? Damnation!"

[022-122]"Dan!"

[022-123]"Well, what did I say? Hold your jaw, will you, and not be halloaing

[022-124]at me that way? I know what I'm a saying of, and what I'm a doing

[022-125]of."

[022-126]"I wish they'd given you something more with all my heart," said

[022-127]Crawley.

[022-128]"We knows that," said the woman from the bed. "We is sure of that,

[022-129]your reverence."

[022-130]"Sixpence!" said the man, scornfully. "If they'd have guv me nothing

[022-131]at all but the run of my teeth at the public-house, I'd 've taken it

[022-132]better. But sixpence!"

[022-133]Then there was a pause. "And what have they given to me?" said Mr.

[022-134]Crawley, when the man's ill-humour about his sixpence had so far

[022-135]subsided as to allow of his busying himself again about the premises.

[022-136]"Yes, indeed;--yes, indeed," said the woman. "Yes, yes, we feel that;

[022-137]we do indeed, Mr. Crawley."

[022-138]"I tell you what, sir; for another sixpence I'd 've sworn you'd

[022-139]never guv' me the paper at all; and so I will now, if it bean't too

[022-140]late;--sixpence or no sixpence. What do I care? d---- them."

[022-141]"Dan!"

[022-142]"And why shouldn't I? They hain't got brains enough among them to

[022-143]winny the truth from the lies,--not among the lot of 'em. I'll swear

[022-144]afore the judge that you didn't give it me at all, if that'll do any

[022-145]good."

[022-146]"Man, do you think I would have you perjure yourself, even if that

[022-147]would do me a service? And do you think that any man was ever served

[022-148]by a lie?"

[022-149]"Faix, among them chaps it don't do to tell them too much of the

[022-150]truth. Look at that!" And he brought out the sixpence again from his

[022-151]breeches'-pocket. "And look at your reverence. Only that they've let

[022-152]you out for a while, they've been nigh as hard on you as though you

[022-153]were one of us."

[022-154]"If they think that I stole it, they have been right," said Mr.

[022-155]Crawley.

[022-156]"It's been along of that chap, Soames," said the woman. "The lord

[022-157]would 've paid the money out of his own pocket and never said not a

[022-158]word."

[022-159]"If they think that I've been a thief, they've done right," repeated

[022-160]Mr. Crawley. "But how can they think so? How can they think so? Have

[022-161]I lived like a thief among them?"

[022-162]"For the matter o' that, if a man ain't paid for his work by them as

[022-163]is his employers, he must pay hisself. Them's my notions. Look at

[022-164]that!" Whereupon he again pulled out the sixpence, and held it forth

[022-165]in the palm of his hand.

[022-166]"You believe, then," said Mr. Crawley, speaking very slowly, "that I

[022-167]did steal the money. Speak out, Dan; I shall not be angry. As you go

[022-168]you are honest men, and I want to know what such of you think about

[022-169]it."

[022-170]"He don't think nothing of the kind," said the woman, almost getting

[022-171]out of bed in her energy. "If he'd athought the like o' that in his

[022-172]head, I'd read 'un such a lesson he'd never think again the longest

[022-173]day he had to live."

[022-174]"Speak out, Dan," said the clergyman, not attending to the woman.

[022-175]"You can understand that no good can come of a lie." Dan Morris

[022-176]scratched his head. "Speak out, man, when I tell you," said Crawley.

[Illustration: "Speak out, Dan."]

[022-177]"Drat it all," said Dan, "where's the use of so much jaw about it?"

[022-178]"Say you know his reverence is as innocent as the babe as isn't

[022-179]born," said the woman.

[022-180]"No; I won't,--say nothing of the kind," said Dan.

[022-181]"Speak out the truth," said Crawley.

[022-182]"They do say, among 'em," said Dan, "that you picked it up, and then

[022-183]got a woolgathering in your head till you didn't rightly know where

[022-184]it come from." Then he paused. "And after a bit you guv' it me to get

[022-185]the money. Didn't you, now?"
