[067-001]"And why have you sent him away disappointed? You know you love him."

[067-002]"You see, my dear," said Lily, "you allow yourself, for the sake of

[067-003]your argument, to use a word in a double sense, and you attempt to

[067-004]confound me by doing so. But I am a great deal too clever for you,

[067-005]and have thought too much about it, to be taken in in that way. I

[067-006]certainly love your cousin John; and so I do love Mr. Boyce, the

[067-007]vicar."

[067-008]"You love Johnny much better than you do Mr. Boyce."

[067-009]"True; very much better; but it is the same sort of love. However, it

[067-010]is a great deal too deep for you to understand. You're too young, and

[067-011]I shan't try to explain it. But the long and the short of it is,--I

[067-012]am not going to marry your cousin."

[067-013]"I wish you were," said Grace, "with all my heart."

[067-014]John Eames as he returned to the cottage was by no means able to

[067-015]fall back upon those resolutions as to his future life, which he had

[067-016]formed for himself and communicated to his friend Dalrymple, and

[067-017]which he had intended to bring at once into force in the event of

[067-018]his being again rejected by Lily Dale. "I will cleanse my mind of it

[067-019]altogether," he had said, "and though I may not forget her, I will

[067-020]live as though she were forgotten. If she declines my proposal again,

[067-021]I will accept her word as final. I will not go about the world any

[067-022]longer as a stricken deer,--to be pitied or else bullied by the rest

[067-023]of the herd." On his way down to Guestwick he had sworn twenty times

[067-024]that it should be so. He would make one more effort, and then he

[067-025]would give it up. But now, after his interview with Lily, he was as

[067-026]little disposed to give it up as ever.

[067-027]He sat upon a gate in a paddock through which there was a back

[067-028]entrance into Lady Julia's garden, and there swore a thousand oaths

[067-029]that he would never give her up. He was, at any rate, sure that she

[067-030]would never become the wife of any one else. He was equally sure that

[067-031]he would never become the husband of any other wife. He could trust

[067-032]her. Yes; he was sure of that. But could he trust himself? Communing

[067-033]with himself, he told himself that after all he was but a poor

[067-034]creature. Circumstances had been very good to him, but he had done

[067-035]nothing for himself. He was vain, and foolish, and unsteady. So he

[067-036]told himself while sitting upon the gate. But he had, at any rate,

[067-037]been constant to Lily, and constant he would remain.

[067-038]He would never more mention her name to any one,--unless it were to

[067-039]Lady Julia to-night. To Dalrymple he would not open his mouth about

[067-040]her, but would plainly ask his friend to be silent on that subject if

[067-041]her name should be mentioned by him. But morning and evening he would

[067-042]pray for her, and in his prayers he would always think of her as

[067-043]his wife. He would never speak to another girl without remembering

[067-044]that he was bound to Lily. He would go nowhere into society without

[067-045]recalling to mind the fact that he was bound by the chains of a

[067-046]solemn engagement. If he knew himself he would be constant to Lily.

[067-047]And then he considered in what manner it would be best and most

[067-048]becoming that he should still prosecute his endeavour and repeat his

[067-049]offer. He thought that he would write to her every year, on the same

[067-050]day of the year, year after year, it might be for the next twenty

[067-051]years. And his letters should be very simple. Sitting there on the

[067-052]gate he planned the wording of his letters;--of his first letter, and

[067-053]of his second, and of his third. They should be very like to each

[067-054]other,--should hardly be more than a repetition of the same words.

[067-055]"If now you are ready for me, then, Lily, am I, as ever, still ready

[067-056]for you." And then "if now" again, and again "if now;--and still

[067-057]if now." When his hair should be grey, and the wrinkles on his

[067-058]cheeks,--ay, though they should be on hers, he would still continue

[067-059]to tell her from year to year that he was ready to take her. Surely

[067-060]some day that "if now" would prevail. And should it never prevail,

[067-061]the merit of his constancy should be its own reward.

[067-062]Such letters as those she would surely keep. Then he looked forward,

[067-063]down into the valley of coming years, and fancied her as she might

[067-064]sit reading them in the twilight of some long evening,--letters which

[067-065]had been written all in vain. He thought that he could look forward

[067-066]with some satisfaction towards the close of his own career, in having

[067-067]been the hero of such a love-story. At any rate, if such a story were

[067-068]to be his story, the melancholy attached to it should arise from no

[067-069]fault of his own. He would still press her to be his wife. And then

[067-070]as he remembered that he was only twenty-seven and that she was

[067-071]twenty-four, he began to marvel at the feeling of grey old age which

[067-072]had come upon him, and tried to make himself believe that he would

[067-073]have her yet before the bloom was off her cheek.

[067-074]He went into the cottage and made his way at once into the room in

[067-075]which Lady Julia was sitting. She did not speak at first, but looked

[067-076]anxiously into his face. And he did not speak, but turned to a table

[067-077]near the window and took up a book,--though the room was too dark for

[067-078]him to see to read the words. "John," at last said Lady Julia.

[067-079]"Well, my lady?"

[067-080]"Have you nothing to tell me, John?"

[067-081]"Nothing on earth,--except the same old story, which has now become a

[067-082]matter of course."

[067-083]"But, John, will you not tell me what she has said?"

[067-084]"Lady Julia, she has said no; simply no. It is a very easy word to

[067-085]say, and she has said it so often that it seems to come from her

[067-086]quite naturally." Then he got a candle and sat down over the fire

[067-087]with a volume of a novel. It was not yet past five, and Lady Julia

[067-088]did not go upstairs to dress till six, and therefore there was an

[067-089]hour during which they were together. John had at first been rather

[067-090]grand to his old friend, and very uncommunicative. But before the

[067-091]dressing bell had rung he had been coaxed into a confidential strain

[067-092]and had told everything. "I suppose it is wrong and selfish," he

[067-093]said. "I suppose I am a dog in a manger. But I do own that there is a

[067-094]consolation to me in the assurance that she will never be the wife of

[067-095]that scoundrel."

[067-096]"I could never forgive her if she were to marry him now," said Lady

[067-097]Julia.

[067-098]"I could never forgive him. But she has said that she will not, and I

[067-099]know that she will not forswear herself. I shall go on with it, Lady

[067-100]Julia. I have made up my mind to that. I suppose it will never come

[067-101]to anything, but I shall stick to it. I can live an old bachelor as

[067-102]well as another man. At any rate I shall stick to it." Then the good

[067-103]silly old woman comforted him and applauded him as though he were a

[067-104]hero among men, and did reward him, as Lily had predicted, by one of

[067-105]those now rare bottles of superexcellent port which had come to her

[067-106]from her brother's cellar.

[067-107]John Eames stayed out his time at the cottage, and went over more

[067-108]than once again to Allington, and called on the squire, on one

[067-109]occasion dining with him and meeting the three ladies from the Small

[067-110]House; and he walked with the girls, comporting himself like any

[067-111]ordinary man. But he was not again alone with Lily Dale, nor did he

[067-112]learn whether she had in truth written those two words in her book.

[067-113]But the reader may know that she did write them there on the evening

[067-114]of the day on which the promise was made. "Lilian Dale,--Old Maid."

[067-115]And when John's holiday was over, he returned to his duties at the

[067-116]elbow of Sir Raffle Buffle.

[067-117]CHAPTER XXXVI.

[067-118]GRACE CRAWLEY RETURNS HOME.

[067-119][Illustration]

[067-120][[About]] this time Grace Crawley received two letters, the first of

[067-121]them reaching her while John Eames was still at the cottage, and

[067-122]the other immediately after his return to London. They both help to

[067-123]tell our story, and our reader shall, therefore, read them if he

[067-124]so please,--or, rather, he shall read the first and as much of the

[067-125]second as is necessary for him. Grace's answer to the first letter he

[067-126]shall see also. Her answer to the second will be told in a very few

[067-127]words. The first was from Major Grantly, and the task of answering

[067-128]that was by no means easy to Grace.

[067-129]Cosby Lodge, -- February, 186--.

[067-130]DEAREST GRACE,

[067-131]I told you when I parted from you, that I should write

[067-132]to you, and I think it best to do so at once, in order

[067-133]that you may fully understand me. Spoken words are soon

[067-134]forgotten,--

[067-135]"I shall never forget his words," Grace said to herself as she read

[067-136]this;--

[067-137]and are not always as plain as they might be. Dear Grace,

[067-138]I suppose I ought not to say so, but I fancied when I

[067-139]parted from you at Allington, that I had succeeded in

[067-140]making myself dear to you. I believe you to be so true in

[067-141]spirit, that you were unable to conceal from me the fact

[067-142]that you love me. I shall believe that this is so, till I

[067-143]am deliberately and solemnly assured by yourself that it

[067-144]is not so;--and I conjure you to think what is due both to

[067-145]yourself and to myself, before you allow yourself to think

[067-146]of making such an assurance unless it be strictly true.

[067-147]I have already told my own friends that I have asked you

[067-148]to be my wife. I tell you this, in order that you may

[067-149]know how little effect your answer to me has had towards

[067-150]inducing me to give you up. What you said about your

[067-151]father and your family has no weight with me, and ought

[067-152]ultimately to have none with you. This business of your

[067-153]father's is a great misfortune,--so great that, probably,

[067-154]had we not known each other before it happened, it might

[067-155]have prevented our becoming intimate when we chanced to

[067-156]meet. But we had met before it happened, and before it

[067-157]happened I had determined to ask you to be my wife. What

[067-158]should I have to think of myself if I allowed my heart to

[067-159]be altered by such a cause as that?

[067-160]I have only further to say that I love you better than any

[067-161]one in the world, and that it is my best hope that you

[067-162]will be my wife. I will not press you till this affair of

[067-163]your father's has been settled; but when that is over I

[067-164]shall look for my reward without reference to its result.

[067-165]Not that I doubt the result if there be anything like

[067-166]justice in England; but that your debt to me, if you owe

[067-167]me any debt, will be altogether irrespective of that. If,

[067-168]as I suppose, you will remain at Allington for some time

[067-169]longer, I shall not see you till after the trial is over.

[067-170]As soon as that is done, I will come to you wherever you

[067-171]are. In the meantime I shall look for an answer to this;

[067-172]and if it be true that you love me, dear, dear Grace, pray

[067-173]have the courage to tell me so.
