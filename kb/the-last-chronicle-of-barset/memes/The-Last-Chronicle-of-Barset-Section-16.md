[016-001]And then the other question was pressed upon him; and he lifted up

[016-002]his hands, and raised his voice, and swore by the Saviour in whom he

[016-003]trusted, that he knew not from whence the money had come to him. Why

[016-004]then had he said that it had come from the dean? He had thought so.

[016-005]The dean had given him money, covered up, in an enclosure, "so that

[016-006]the touch of the coin might not add to my disgrace in taking his

[016-007]alms," said the wretched man, thus speaking openly and freely in his

[016-008]agony of the shame which he had striven so persistently to hide. He

[016-009]had not seen the dean's monies as they had been given, and he had

[016-010]thought that the cheque had been with them. Beyond that he could tell

[016-011]them nothing.

[016-012]Then there was a conference between the magistrates and Mr. Walker,

[016-013]in which Mr. Walker submitted that the magistrates had no alternative

[016-014]but to commit the gentleman. To this Lord Lufton demurred, and with

[016-015]him Dr. Thorne.

[016-016]"I believe, as I am sitting here," said Lord Lufton, "that he has

[016-017]told the truth, and that he does not know any more than I do from

[016-018]whence the cheque came."

[016-019]"I am quite sure he does not," said Dr. Thorne.

[016-020]Lord George remarked that it was the "queerest go he had ever come

[016-021]across." Dr. Tempest merely shook his head. Mr. Fothergill pointed

[016-022]out that even supposing the gentleman's statement to be true, it by

[016-023]no means went towards establishing the gentleman's innocence. The

[016-024]cheque had been traced to the gentleman's hands, and the gentleman

[016-025]was bound to show how it had come into his possession. Even supposing

[016-026]that the gentleman had found the cheque in his house, which was

[016-027]likely enough, he was not thereby justified in changing it, and

[016-028]applying the proceeds to his own purposes. Mr. Walker told them that

[016-029]Mr. Fothergill was right, and that the only excuse to be made for Mr.

[016-030]Crawley was that he was out of his senses.

[016-031]"I don't see it," said Lord Lufton. "I might have a lot of paper

[016-032]money by me, and not know from Adam where I got it."

[016-033]"But you would have to show where you got it, my lord, when inquiry

[016-034]was made," said Mr. Fothergill.

[016-035]Lord Lufton, who was not particularly fond of Mr. Fothergill, and

[016-036]was very unwilling to be instructed by him in any of the duties of a

[016-037]magistrate, turned his back at once upon the duke's agent; but within

[016-038]three minutes afterwards he had submitted to the same instructions

[016-039]from Mr. Walker.

[016-040]Mr. Crawley had again seated himself, and during this period of the

[016-041]affair was leaning over the table with his face buried on his arms.

[016-042]Mrs. Crawley sat by his side, utterly impotent as to any assistance,

[016-043]just touching him with her hand, and waiting behind her veil till

[016-044]she should be made to understand what was the decision of the

[016-045]magistrates. This was at last communicated to her,--and to him,--in

[016-046]a whisper by Mr. Walker. Mr. Crawley must understand that he was

[016-047]committed to take his trial at Barchester, at the next assizes, which

[016-048]would be held in April, but that bail would be taken;--his own bail

[016-049]in five hundred pounds, and that of two others in two hundred and

[016-050]fifty pounds each. And Mr. Walker explained further that he and the

[016-051]bailmen were ready, and that the bail-bond was prepared. The bailmen

[016-052]were to be the Rev. Mr. Robarts, and Major Grantly. In five minutes

[016-053]the bond was signed and Mr. Crawley was at liberty to go away, a free

[016-054]man,--till the Barchester Assizes should come round in April.

[016-055]Of all that was going on at this time Mr. Crawley knew little or

[016-056]nothing, and Mrs. Crawley did not know much. She did say a word of

[016-057]thanks to Mr. Robarts, and begged that the same might be said to--the

[016-058]other gentleman. If she had heard the major's name she did not

[016-059]remember it. Then they were led out back into the bed-room, where

[016-060]Mrs. Walker was found, anxious to do something, if she only knew

[016-061]what, to comfort the wretched husband and the wretched wife. But what

[016-062]comfort or consolation could there be within their reach? There was

[016-063]tea made ready for them, and sandwiches cut from the Inn larder. And

[016-064]there was sherry in the Inn decanter. But no such comfort as that was

[016-065]possible for either of them.

[016-066]They were taken home again in the fly, returning without the escort

[016-067]of Mr. Thompson, and as they went some few words were spoken by Mrs.

[016-068]Crawley. "Josiah," she said, "there will be a way out of this, even

[016-069]yet, if you will only hold up your head and trust."

[016-070]"There is a way out of it," he said. "There is a way. There is but

[016-071]one way." When he had so spoken she said no more, but resolved that

[016-072]her eye should never be off him, no,--not for a moment. Then, when

[016-073]she had gotten him once more into that front parlour, she threw her

[016-074]arms round him and kissed him.

[016-075]CHAPTER IX.

[016-076]GRACE CRAWLEY GOES TO ALLINGTON.

[016-077][Illustration]

[016-078]The tidings of what had been done by the magistrates at their petty

[016-079]sessions was communicated the same night to Grace Crawley by Miss

[016-080]Prettyman. Miss Anne Prettyman had heard the news within five minutes

[016-081]of the execution of the bail-bond, and had rushed to her sister with

[016-082]information as to the event. "They have found him guilty; they have,

[016-083]indeed. They have convicted him,--or whatever it is, because he

[016-084]couldn't say where he got it." "You do not mean that they have sent

[016-085]him to prison?" "No;--not to prison; not as yet, that is. I don't

[016-086]understand it altogether; but he's to be tried again at the assizes.

[016-087]In the meantime he's to be out on bail. Major Grantly is to be the

[016-088]bail,--he and Mr. Robarts. That, I think, was very nice of him." It

[016-089]was undoubtedly the fact that Miss Anne Prettyman had received an

[016-090]accession of pleasurable emotion when she learned that Mr. Crawley

[016-091]had not been sent away scathless, but had been condemned, as it

[016-092]were, to a public trial at the assizes. And yet she would have

[016-093]done anything in her power to save Grace Crawley, or even to save

[016-094]her father. And it must be explained that Miss Anne Prettyman was

[016-095]supposed to be specially efficient in teaching Roman history to her

[016-096]pupils, although she was so manifestly ignorant of the course of

[016-097]law in the country in which she lived. "Committed him," said Miss

[016-098]Prettyman, correcting her sister with scorn. "They have not convicted

[016-099]him. Had they convicted him, there could be no question of bail."

[016-100]"I don't know how all that is, Annabella, but at any rate Major

[016-101]Grantly is to be the bailsman, and there is to be another trial at

[016-102]Barchester." "There cannot be more than one trial in a criminal

[016-103]case," said Miss Prettyman, "unless the jury should disagree, or

[016-104]something of that kind. I suppose he has been committed, and that the

[016-105]trial will take place at the assizes." "Exactly,--that's just it."

[016-106]Had Lord Lufton appeared as lictor, and had Thompson carried the

[016-107]fasces, Miss Anne would have known more about it.

[016-108]The sad tidings were not told to Grace till the evening. Mrs.

[016-109]Crawley, when the inquiry was over before the magistrates, would fain

[016-110]have had herself driven to the Miss Prettymans' school, that she

[016-111]might see her daughter; but she felt that to be impossible while

[016-112]her husband was in her charge. The father would of course have gone

[016-113]to his child, had the visit been suggested to him; but that would

[016-114]have caused another terrible scene; and the mother, considering it

[016-115]all in her mind, thought it better to abstain. Miss Prettyman did

[016-116]her best to make poor Grace think that the affair had gone so far

[016-117]favourably,--did her best, that is, without saying anything which her

[016-118]conscience told her to be false. "It is to be settled at the assizes

[016-119]in April," she said.

[016-120]"And in the meantime what will become of papa?"

[016-121]"Your papa will be at home, just as usual. He must have some one to

[016-122]advise him. I dare say it would have been all over now if he would

[016-123]have employed an attorney."

[016-124]"But it seems so hard that an attorney should be wanted."

[016-125]"My dear Grace, things in this world are hard."

[016-126]"But they are always harder for papa and mamma than for anybody

[016-127]else." In answer to this, Miss Prettyman made some remarks intended

[016-128]to be wise and kind at the same time. Grace, whose eyes were laden

[016-129]with tears, made no immediate reply to this, but reverted to her

[016-130]former statement, that she must go home. "I cannot remain, Miss

[016-131]Prettyman; I am so unhappy."

[016-132]"Will you be more happy at home?"

[016-133]"I can bear it better there."

[016-134]The poor girl soon learned from the intended consolations of those

[016-135]around her, from the ill-considered kindnesses of the pupils, and

[016-136]from words which fell from the servants, that her father had in

[016-137]fact been judged to be guilty, as far as judgment had as yet gone.

[016-138]"They do say, miss, it's only because he hadn't a lawyer," said the

[016-139]housekeeper. And if men so kind as Lord Lufton and Mr. Walker had

[016-140]made him out to be guilty, what could be expected from a stern judge

[016-141]down from London, who would know nothing about her poor father and

[016-142]his peculiarities, and from twelve jurymen who would be shopkeepers

[016-143]out of Barchester. It would kill her father, and then it would kill

[016-144]her mother; and after that it would kill her also. And there was no

[016-145]money in the house at home. She knew it well. She had been paid three

[016-146]pounds a month for her services at the school, and the money for the

[016-147]last two months had been sent to her mother. Yet, badly as she wanted

[016-148]anything that she might be able to earn, she knew that she could

[016-149]not go on teaching. It had come to be acknowledged by both the

[016-150]Miss Prettymans that any teaching on her part for the present was

[016-151]impossible. She would go home and perish with the rest of them. There

[016-152]was no room left for hope to her, or to any of her family. They had

[016-153]accused her father of being a common thief,--her father whom she knew

[016-154]to be so nobly honest, her father whom she believed to be among the

[016-155]most devoted of God's servants. He was accused of a paltry theft, and

[016-156]the magistrates and lawyers and policemen among them had decided that

[016-157]the accusation was true! How could she look the girls in the face

[016-158]after that, or attempt to hold her own among the teachers!

[016-159]On the next morning there came the letter from Miss Lily Dale, and

[016-160]with that in her hand she again went to Miss Prettyman. She must

[016-161]go home, she said. She must at any rate see her mother. Could Miss

[016-162]Prettyman be kind enough to send her home. "I haven't sixpence to pay

[016-163]for anything," she said, bursting out into tears; "and I haven't a

[016-164]right to ask for it." Then the statements which Miss Prettyman made

[016-165]in her eagerness to cover this latter misfortune were decidedly

[016-166]false. There was so much money owing to Grace, she said; money for

[016-167]this, money for that, money for anything or nothing! Ten pounds would

[016-168]hardly clear the account. "Nobody owes me anything; but if you'll

[016-169]lend me five shillings!" said Grace, in her agony. Miss Prettyman, as

[016-170]she made her way through this difficulty, thought of Major Grantly

[016-171]and his love. It would have been of no use, she knew. Had she brought

[016-172]them together on that Monday, Grace would have said nothing to him.

[016-173]Indeed such a meeting at such a time would have been improper.

[016-174]But, regarding Major Grantly, as she did, in the light of a

[016-175]millionaire,--for the wealth of the archdeacon was notorious,--she

[016-176]could not but think it a pity that poor Grace should be begging for

[016-177]five shillings. "You need not at any rate trouble yourself about

[016-178]money, Grace," said Miss Prettyman. "What is a pound or two more or

[016-179]less between you and me? It is almost unkind of you to think about

[016-180]it. Is that letter in your hand anything for me to see, my dear?"

[016-181]Then Grace explained that she did not wish to show Miss Dale's

[016-182]letter, but that Miss Dale had asked her to go to Allington. "And you

[016-183]will go," said Miss Prettyman. "It will be the best thing for you,

[016-184]and the best thing for your mother."
