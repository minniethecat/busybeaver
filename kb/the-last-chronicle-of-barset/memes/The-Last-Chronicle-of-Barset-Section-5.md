[005-001]Major Grantly had been a successful man in life,--with the one

[005-002]exception of having lost the mother of his child within a twelvemonth

[005-003]of his marriage and within a few hours of that child's birth. He had

[005-004]served in India as a very young man, and had been decorated with the

[005-005]Victoria Cross. Then he had married a lady with some money, and had

[005-006]left the active service of the army, with the concurring advice of

[005-007]his own family and that of his wife. He had taken a small place in

[005-008]his father's county, but the wife for whose comfort he had taken it

[005-009]had died before she was permitted to see it. Nevertheless he had gone

[005-010]to reside there, hunting a good deal and farming a little, making

[005-011]himself popular in the district, and keeping up the good name of

[005-012]Grantly in a successful way, till--alas,--it had seemed good to him

[005-013]to throw those favouring eyes on poor Grace Crawley. His wife had

[005-014]now been dead just two years, and as he was still under thirty,

[005-015]no one could deny it would be right that he should marry again.

[005-016]No one did deny it. His father had hinted that he ought to do so,

[005-017]and had generously whispered that if some little increase to the

[005-018]major's present income were needed, he might possibly be able to do

[005-019]something. "What is the good of keeping it?" the archdeacon had said

[005-020]in liberal after-dinner warmth; "I only want it for your brother and

[005-021]yourself." The brother was a clergyman.

[005-022]And the major's mother had strongly advised him to marry again

[005-023]without loss of time. "My dear Henry," she had said, "you'll never be

[005-024]younger, and youth does go for something. As for dear little Edith,

[005-025]being a girl, she is almost no impediment. Do you know those two

[005-026]girls at Chaldicotes?"

[005-027]"What, Mrs. Thorne's nieces?"

[005-028]"No; they are not her nieces but her cousins. Emily Dunstable is very

[005-029]handsome;--and as for money--!"

[005-030]"But what about birth, mother?"

[005-031]"One can't have everything, my dear."

[005-032]"As far as I am concerned, I should like to have everything or

[005-033]nothing," the major had said laughing. Now for him to think of Grace

[005-034]Crawley after that,--of Grace Crawley who had no money, and no

[005-035]particular birth, and not even beauty itself,--so at least Mrs.

[005-036]Grantly said,--who had not even enjoyed the ordinary education of

[005-037]a lady, was too bad. Nothing had been wanting to Emily Dunstable's

[005-038]education, and it was calculated that she would have at least twenty

[005-039]thousand pounds on the day of her marriage.

[005-040]The disappointment to the mother would be the more sore because

[005-041]she had gone to work upon her little scheme with reference to Miss

[005-042]Emily Dunstable, and had at first, as she thought, seen her way to

[005-043]success,--to success in spite of the disparaging words which her

[005-044]son had spoken to her. Mrs. Thorne's house at Chaldicotes,--or Dr.

[005-045]Thorne's house as it should, perhaps, be more properly called, for

[005-046]Dr. Thorne was the husband of Mrs. Thorne,--was in these days the

[005-047]pleasantest house in Barsetshire. No one saw so much company as the

[005-048]Thornes, or spent so much money in so pleasant a way. The great

[005-049]county families, the Pallisers and the De Courcys, the Luftons

[005-050]and the Greshams, were no doubt grander, and some of them were

[005-051]perhaps richer than the Chaldicote Thornes,--as they were called to

[005-052]distinguish them from the Thornes of Ullathorne; but none of these

[005-053]people were so pleasant in their ways, so free in their hospitality,

[005-054]or so easy in their modes of living, as the doctor and his wife.

[005-055]When first Chaldicotes, a very old country seat, had by the chances

[005-056]of war fallen into their hands and been newly furnished, and newly

[005-057]decorated, and newly gardened, and newly greenhoused and hot-watered

[005-058]by them, many of the county people had turned up their noses at

[005-059]them. Dear old Lady Lufton had done so, and had been greatly

[005-060]grieved,--saying nothing, however, of her grief, when her son and

[005-061]daughter-in-law had broken away from her, and submitted themselves to

[005-062]the blandishments of the doctor's wife. And the Grantlys had stood

[005-063]aloof, partly influenced, no doubt, by their dear and intimate old

[005-064]friend Miss Monica Thorne of Ullathorne, a lady of the very old

[005-065]school, who, though good as gold and kind as charity, could not

[005-066]endure that an interloping Mrs. Thorne, who never had a grandfather,

[005-067]should come to honour and glory in the county, simply because of her

[005-068]riches. Miss Monica Thorne stood out, but Mrs. Grantly gave way, and

[005-069]having once given way found that Dr. Thorne, and Mrs. Thorne, and

[005-070]Emily Dunstable, and Chaldicote House together, were very charming.

[005-071]And the major had been once there with her, and had made himself

[005-072]very pleasant, and there had certainly been some little passage

[005-073]of incipient love between him and Miss Dunstable, as to which Mrs.

[005-074]Thorne, who managed everything, seemed to be well pleased. This had

[005-075]been after the first mention made by Mrs. Grantly to her son of Emily

[005-076]Dunstable's name, but before she had heard any faintest whispers of

[005-077]his fancy for Grace Crawley; and she had therefore been justified

[005-078]in hoping,--almost in expecting, that Emily Dunstable would be her

[005-079]daughter-in-law, and was therefore the more aggrieved when this

[005-080]terrible Crawley peril first opened itself before her eyes.

[005-081]CHAPTER III.

[005-082]THE ARCHDEACON'S THREAT.

[005-083]The dinner-party at the rectory comprised none but the Grantly

[005-084]family. The marchioness had written to say that she preferred to have

[005-085]it so. The father had suggested that the Thornes of Ullathorne, very

[005-086]old friends, might be asked, and the Greshams from Boxall Hill, and

[005-087]had even promised to endeavour to get old Lady Lufton over to the

[005-088]rectory, Lady Lufton having in former years been Griselda's warm

[005-089]friend. But Lady Hartletop had preferred to see her dear father

[005-090]and mother in privacy. Her brother Henry she would be glad to meet,

[005-091]and hoped to make some arrangement with him for a short visit to

[005-092]Hartlebury, her husband's place in Shropshire,--as to which latter

[005-093]hint, it may, however, be at once said, that nothing further was

[005-094]spoken after the Crawley alliance had been suggested. And there had

[005-095]been a very sore point mooted by the daughter in a request made

[005-096]by her to her father that she might not be called upon to meet

[005-097]her grandfather, her mother's father, Mr. Harding, a clergyman of

[005-098]Barchester, who was now stricken in years.--"Papa would not have

[005-099]come," said Mrs. Grantly, "but I think,--I do think--" Then she

[005-100]stopped herself.

[005-101]"Your father has odd ways sometimes, my dear. You know how fond I am

[005-102]of having him here myself."

[005-103]"It does not signify," said Mrs. Grantly. "Do not let us say anything

[005-104]more about it. Of course we cannot have everything. I am told the

[005-105]child does her duty in her sphere of life, and I suppose we ought to

[005-106]be contented." Then Mrs. Grantly went up to her own room, and there

[005-107]she cried. Nothing was said to the major on the unpleasant subject

[005-108]of the Crawleys before dinner. He met his sister in the drawing-room,

[005-109]and was allowed to kiss her noble cheek. "I hope Edith is well,

[005-110]Henry," said the sister. "Quite well; and little Dumbello is the

[005-111]same, I hope?" "Thank you, yes; quite well." Then there seemed to

[005-112]be nothing more to be said between the two. The major never made

[005-113]inquiries after the august family, or would allow it to appear that

[005-114]he was conscious of being shone upon by the wife of a marquis. Any

[005-115]adulation which Griselda received of that kind came from her father,

[005-116]and, therefore, unconsciously she had learned to think that her

[005-117]father was better bred than the other members of her family, and more

[005-118]fitted by nature to move in that sacred circle to which she herself

[005-119]had been exalted. We need not dwell upon the dinner, which was but a

[005-120]dull affair. Mrs. Grantly strove to carry on the family party exactly

[005-121]as it would have been carried on had her daughter married the son

[005-122]of some neighbouring squire; but she herself was conscious of the

[005-123]struggle, and the fact of there being a struggle produced failure.

[005-124]The rector's servants treated the daughter of the house with special

[005-125]awe, and the marchioness herself moved, and spoke, and ate, and drank

[005-126]with a cold magnificence, which I think had become a second nature

[005-127]with her, but which was not on that account the less oppressive.

[005-128]Even the archdeacon, who enjoyed something in that which was so

[005-129]disagreeable to his wife, felt a relief when he was left alone after

[005-130]dinner with his son. He felt relieved as his son got up to open the

[005-131]door for his mother and sister, but was aware at the same time that

[005-132]he had before him a most difficult and possibly a most disastrous

[005-133]task. His dear son Henry was not a man to be talked smoothly out of,

[005-134]or into, any propriety. He had a will of his own, and having hitherto

[005-135]been a successful man, who in youth had fallen into few youthful

[005-136]troubles,--who had never justified his father in using stern parental

[005-137]authority,--was not now inclined to bend his neck. "Henry," said the

[005-138]archdeacon, "what are you drinking? That's '34 port, but it's not

[005-139]just what it should be. Shall I send for another bottle?"

[005-140]"It will do for me, sir. I shall only take a glass."

[005-141]"I shall drink two or three glasses of claret. But you young fellows

[005-142]have become so desperately temperate."

[005-143]"We take our wine at dinner, sir."

[005-144]"By-the-by, how well Griselda is looking."

[005-145]"Yes, she is. It's always easy for women to look well when they're

[005-146]rich." How would Grace Crawley look, then, who was poor as poverty

[005-147]itself, and who should remain poor, if his son was fool enough to

[005-148]marry her? That was the train of thought which ran through the

[005-149]archdeacon's mind. "I do not think much of riches," said he, "but

[005-150]it is always well that a gentleman's wife or a gentleman's daughter

[005-151]should have a sufficiency to maintain her position in life."

[005-152]"You may say the same, sir, of everybody's wife and everybody's

[005-153]daughter."

[005-154]"You know what I mean, Henry."

[005-155]"I am not quite sure that I do, sir."

[005-156]"Perhaps I had better speak out at once. A rumour has reached your

[005-157]mother and me, which we don't believe for a moment, but which,

[005-158]nevertheless, makes us unhappy even as a report. They say that there

[005-159]is a young woman living in Silverbridge to whom you are becoming

[005-160]attached."

[005-161]"Is there any reason why I should not become attached to a young

[005-162]woman in Silverbridge?--though I hope any young woman to whom I may

[005-163]become attached will be worthy at any rate of being called a young

[005-164]lady."

[005-165]"I hope so, Henry; I hope so. I do hope so."

[005-166]"So much I will promise, sir; but I will promise nothing more."

[005-167]The archdeacon looked across into his son's face, and his heart sank

[005-168]within him. His son's voice and his son's eyes seemed to tell him two

[005-169]things. They seemed to tell him, firstly, that the rumour about Grace

[005-170]Crawley was true; and, secondly, that the major was resolved not to

[005-171]be talked out of his folly. "But you are not engaged to any one, are

[005-172]you?" said the archdeacon. The son did not at first make any answer,

[005-173]and then the father repeated the question. "Considering our mutual

[005-174]positions, Henry, I think you ought to tell me if you are engaged."

[005-175]"I am not engaged. Had I become so, I should have taken the first

[005-176]opportunity of telling either you or my mother."

[005-177]"Thank God. Now, my dear boy, I can speak out more plainly. The young

[005-178]woman whose name I have heard is daughter to that Mr. Crawley who is

[005-179]perpetual curate at Hogglestock. I knew that there could be nothing

[005-180]in it."
