[007-001]Mrs. Crawley could not answer her husband's question before her

[007-002]daughter, and was therefore obliged to make another excuse for again

[007-003]sending her out of the room. "Jane, dear," she said, "bring my things

[007-004]down to the kitchen and I will change them by the fire. I will be

[007-005]there in two minutes, when I have had a word with your papa." The

[007-006]girl went immediately and then Mrs. Crawley answered her husband's

[007-007]question. "No, my dear; there is no question of your going to

[007-008]prison."

[007-009]"But there will be."

[007-010]"I have undertaken that you shall attend before the magistrates at

[007-011]Silverbridge on Thursday next, at twelve o'clock. You will do that?"

[007-012]"Do it! You mean, I suppose, to say that I must go there. Is anybody

[007-013]to come and fetch me?"

[007-014]"Nobody will come. Only you must promise that you will be there. I

[007-015]have promised for you. You will go; will you not?" She stood leaning

[007-016]over him, half embracing him, waiting for an answer; but for a

[007-017]while he gave none. "You will tell me that you will do what I have

[007-018]undertaken for you, Josiah?"

[007-019]"I think I would rather that they fetched me. I think that I will not

[007-020]go myself."

[007-021]"And have policemen come for you into the parish! Mr. Walker has

[007-022]promised that he will send over his phaeton. He sent me home in it

[007-023]to-day."

[007-024]"I want nobody's phaeton. If I go I will walk. If it were ten times

[007-025]the distance, and though I had not a shoe left to my feet I would

[007-026]walk. If I go there at all, of my own accord, I will walk there."

[007-027]"But you will go?"

[007-028]"What do I care for the parish? What matters it who sees me now? I

[007-029]cannot be degraded worse than I am. Everybody knows it."

[007-030]"There is no disgrace without guilt," said his wife.

[007-031]"Everybody thinks me guilty. I see it in their eyes. The children

[007-032]know of it, and I hear their whispers in the school, 'Mr. Crawley has

[007-033]taken some money.' I heard the girl say it myself."

[007-034]"What matters what the girl says?"

[007-035]"And yet you would have me go in a fine carriage to Silverbridge, as

[007-036]though to a wedding. If I am wanted there let them take me as they

[007-037]would another. I shall be here for them,--unless I am dead."

[007-038]At this moment Jane reappeared, pressing her mother to take off her

[007-039]wet clothes, and Mrs. Crawley went with her daughter to the kitchen.

[007-040]The one red-armed young girl who was their only servant was sent

[007-041]away, and then the mother and child discussed how best they might

[007-042]prevail with the head of the family. "But, mamma, it must come right;

[007-043]must it not?"

[007-044]"I trust it will. I think it will. But I cannot see my way as yet."

[007-045]"Papa cannot have done anything wrong."

[007-046]"No, my dear; he has done nothing wrong. He has made great

[007-047]mistakes, and it is hard to make people understand that he has not

[007-048]intentionally spoken untruths. He is ever thinking of other things,

[007-049]about the school, and his sermons, and he does not remember."

[007-050]"And about how poor we are, mamma."

[007-051]"He has much to occupy his mind, and he forgets things which dwell in

[007-052]the memory with other people. He said that he had got this money from

[007-053]Mr. Soames, and of course he thought that it was so."

[007-054]"And where did he get it, mamma?"

[007-055]"Ah,--I wish I knew. I should have said that I had seen every

[007-056]shilling that came into the house; but I know nothing of this

[007-057]cheque,--whence it came."

[007-058]"But will not papa tell you?"

[007-059]"He would tell me if he knew. He thinks it came from the dean."

[007-060]"And are you sure it did not?"

[007-061]"Yes; quite sure; as sure as I can be of anything. The dean told me

[007-062]he would give him fifty pounds, and the fifty pounds came. I had them

[007-063]in my own hands. And he has written to say that it was so."

[007-064]"But couldn't this be part of the fifty pounds?"

[007-065]"No, dear, no."

[007-066]"Then where did papa get it? Perhaps he picked it up, and has

[007-067]forgotten?"

[007-068]To this Mrs. Crawley made no reply. The idea that the cheque had been

[007-069]found by her husband,--had been picked up as Jane had said,--had

[007-070]occurred also to Jane's mother. Mr. Soames was confident that he had

[007-071]dropped the pocket-book at the parsonage. Mrs. Crawley had always

[007-072]disliked Mr. Soames, thinking him to be hard, cruel, and vulgar. She

[007-073]would not have hesitated to believe him guilty of a falsehood, or

[007-074]even of direct dishonesty, if by so believing she could in her own

[007-075]mind have found the means of reconciling her husband's possession of

[007-076]the cheque with absolute truth on his part. But she could not do so.

[007-077]Even though Soames had, with devilish premeditated malice, slipped

[007-078]the cheque into her husband's pocket, his having done so would not

[007-079]account for her husband's having used the cheque when he found it

[007-080]there. She was driven to make excuses for him which, valid as they

[007-081]might be with herself, could not be valid with others. He had said

[007-082]that Mr. Soames had paid the cheque to him. That was clearly a

[007-083]mistake. He had said that the cheque had been given to him by the

[007-084]dean. That was clearly another mistake. She knew, or thought she

[007-085]knew, that he, being such as he was, might make such blunders as

[007-086]these, and yet be true. She believed that such statements might be

[007-087]blunders and not falsehoods,--so convinced was she that her husband's

[007-088]mind would not act at all times as do the minds of other men. But

[007-089]having such a conviction she was driven to believe also that almost

[007-090]anything might be possible. Soames may have been right, or he might

[007-091]have dropped, not the book, but the cheque. She had no difficulty

[007-092]in presuming Soames to be wrong in any detail, if by so supposing

[007-093]she could make the exculpation of her husband easier to herself. If

[007-094]villany on the part of Soames was needful to her theory, Soames would

[007-095]become to her a villain at once,--of the blackest dye. Might it not

[007-096]be possible that the cheque having thus fallen into her husband's

[007-097]hands, he had come, after a while, to think that it had been sent to

[007-098]him by his friend, the dean? And if it were so, would it be possible

[007-099]to make others so believe? That there was some mistake which would

[007-100]be easily explained were her husband's mind lucid at all points, but

[007-101]which she could not explain because of the darkness of his mind, she

[007-102]was thoroughly convinced. But were she herself to put forward such

[007-103]a defence on her husband's part, she would in doing so be driven to

[007-104]say that he was a lunatic,--that he was incapable of managing the

[007-105]affairs of himself or his family. It seemed to her that she would be

[007-106]compelled to have him proved to be either a thief or a madman. And

[007-107]yet she knew that he was neither. That he was not a thief was as

[007-108]clear to her as the sun at noonday. Could she have lain on the man's

[007-109]bosom for twenty years, and not yet have learned the secrets of the

[007-110]heart beneath? The whole mind of the man was, as she told herself,

[007-111]within her grasp. He might have taken the twenty pounds; he might

[007-112]have taken it and spent it, though it was not his own; but yet he

[007-113]was no thief. Nor was he a madman. No man more sane in preaching

[007-114]the gospel of his Lord, in making intelligible to the ignorant the

[007-115]promises of his Saviour, ever got into a parish pulpit, or taught in

[007-116]a parish school. The intellect of the man was as clear as running

[007-117]water in all things not appertaining to his daily life and its

[007-118]difficulties. He could be logical with a vengeance,--so logical as to

[007-119]cause infinite trouble to his wife, who, with all her good sense, was

[007-120]not logical. And he had Greek at his fingers' ends,--as his daughter

[007-121]knew very well. And even to this day he would sometimes recite to

[007-122]them English poetry, lines after lines, stanzas upon stanzas, in a

[007-123]sweet low melancholy voice, on long winter evenings when occasionally

[007-124]the burden of his troubles would be lighter to him than was usual.

[007-125]Books in Latin and in French he read with as much ease as in English,

[007-126]and took delight in such as came to him, when he would condescend

[007-127]to accept such loans from the deanery. And there was at times a

[007-128]lightness of heart about the man. In the course of the last winter he

[007-129]had translated into Greek irregular verse the very noble ballad of

[007-130]Lord Bateman, maintaining the rhythm and the rhyme, and had repeated

[007-131]it with uncouth glee till his daughter knew it all by heart. And when

[007-132]there had come to him a five-pound note from some admiring magazine

[007-133]editor as the price of the same,--still through the dean's hands,--he

[007-134]had brightened up his heart and had thought for an hour or two that

[007-135]even yet the world would smile upon him. His wife knew well that he

[007-136]was not mad; but yet she knew that there were dark moments with him,

[007-137]in which his mind was so much astray that he could not justly be

[007-138]called to account as to what he might remember and what he might

[007-139]forget. How would it be possible to explain all this to a judge and

[007-140]jury, so that they might neither say that he was dishonest, nor yet

[007-141]that he was mad? "Perhaps he picked it up, and had forgotten," her

[007-142]daughter said to her. Perhaps it was so, but she might not as yet

[007-143]admit as much even to her child.

[007-144]"It is a mystery, dear, as yet, which, with God's aid, will be

[007-145]unravelled. Of one thing we at least may be sure; that your papa has

[007-146]not wilfully done anything wrong."

[007-147]"Of course we are sure of that, mamma."

[007-148]Mrs. Crawley had many troubles during the next four or five days, of

[007-149]which the worst, perhaps, had reference to the services of the Sunday

[007-150]which intervened between the day of her visit to Silverbridge, and

[007-151]the sitting of the magistrates. On the Saturday it was necessary

[007-152]that he should prepare his sermons, of which he preached two on

[007-153]every Sunday, though his congregation consisted only of farmers,

[007-154]brickmakers, and agricultural labourers, who would willingly have

[007-155]dispensed with the second. Mrs. Crawley proposed to send over to

[007-156]Mr. Robarts, a neighbouring clergyman, for the loan of a curate. Mr.

[007-157]Robarts was a warm friend to the Crawleys, and in such an emergency

[007-158]would probably have come himself; but Mr. Crawley would not hear of

[007-159]it. The discussion took place early on the Saturday morning, before

[007-160]it was as yet daylight, for the poor woman was thinking day and

[007-161]night of her husband's troubles, and it had this good effect, that

[007-162]immediately after breakfast he seated himself at his desk, and worked

[007-163]at his task as though he had forgotten all else in the world.

[007-164]And on the Sunday morning he went into his school before the hour of

[007-165]the church service, as had been his wont, and taught there as though

[007-166]everything with him was as usual. Some of the children were absent,

[007-167]having heard of their teacher's tribulation, and having been told

[007-168]probably that he would remit his work; and for these absent ones he

[007-169]sent in great anger. The poor bairns came creeping in, for he was a

[007-170]man who by his manners had been able to secure their obedience in

[007-171]spite of his poverty. And he preached to the people of his parish on

[007-172]that Sunday, as he had always preached; eagerly, clearly, with an

[007-173]eloquence fitted for the hearts of such an audience. No one would

[007-174]have guessed from his tones and gestures and appearance on that

[007-175]occasion, that there was aught wrong with him,--unless there had been

[007-176]there some observer keen enough to perceive that the greater care

[007-177]which he used, and the special eagerness of his words, denoted a

[007-178]special frame of mind.
