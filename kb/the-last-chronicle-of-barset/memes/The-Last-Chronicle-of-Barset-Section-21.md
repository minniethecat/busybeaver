[021-001]The Palace, Barchester, -- December, 186--.

[021-002]REVEREND SIR,--[he left out the dear, because he knew

[021-003]that if he inserted it he would be compelled to write the

[021-004]letter over again]

[021-005]I have heard to-day with the greatest trouble of spirit,

[021-006]that you have been taken before a bench of magistrates

[021-007]assembled at Silverbridge, having been previously arrested

[021-008]by the police in your parsonage house at Hogglestock, and

[021-009]that the magistrates of Silverbridge have committed you to

[021-010]take your trial at the next assizes at Barchester, on a

[021-011]charge of theft.

[021-012]Far be it from me to prejudge the case. You will

[021-013]understand, reverend sir, that I express no opinion

[021-014]whatever as to your guilt or innocence in this matter.

[021-015]If you have been guilty, may the Lord give you grace to

[021-016]repent of your great sin and to make such amends as may

[021-017]come from immediate acknowledgment and confession. If you

[021-018]are innocent, may He protect you, and make your innocence

[021-019]to shine before all men. In either case may the Lord be

[021-020]with you and keep your feet from further stumbling.

[021-021]But I write to you now as your bishop, to explain to you

[021-022]that circumstanced as you are, you cannot with decency

[021-023]perform the church services of your parish. I have that

[021-024]confidence in you that I doubt not you will agree with

[021-025]me in this, and will be grateful to me for relieving you

[021-026]so far from the immediate perplexities of your position.

[021-027]I have, therefore, appointed the Rev. Caleb Thumble to

[021-028]perform the duties of incumbent of Hogglestock till such

[021-029]time as a jury shall have decided upon your case at

[021-030]Barchester; and in order that you may at once become

[021-031]acquainted with Mr. Thumble, as will be most convenient

[021-032]that you should do, I will commission him to deliver this

[021-033]letter into your hand personally to-morrow, trusting that

[021-034]you will receive him with that brotherly spirit in which

[021-035]he is sent upon this painful mission.

[021-036]Touching the remuneration to which Mr. Thumble will become

[021-037]entitled for his temporary ministrations in the parish

[021-038]of Hogglestock, I do not at present lay down any strict

[021-039]injunction. He must, at any rate, be paid at a rate not

[021-040]less than that ordinarily afforded for a curate.

[021-041]I will once again express my fervent hope that the Lord

[021-042]may bring you to see the true state of your own soul, and

[021-043]that He may fill you with the grace of repentance, so that

[021-044]the bitter waters of the present hour may not pass over

[021-045]your head and destroy you.

[021-046]I have the honour to be,

[021-047]Reverend Sir,

[021-048]Your faithful servant in Christ,

[021-049]T. BARNUM.*

[021-050]*Baronum Castrum having been the old Roman name from which

[021-051]the modern Barchester is derived, the bishops of the

[021-052]diocese have always signed themselves Barnum.

[021-053]The bishop had hardly finished his letter when Mrs. Proudie returned

[021-054]to the study, followed by the Rev. Caleb Thumble. Mr. Thumble was a

[021-055]little man, about forty years of age, who had a wife and children

[021-056]living in Barchester, and who existed on such chance clerical crumbs

[021-057]as might fall from the table of the bishop's patronage. People in

[021-058]Barchester said that Mrs. Thumble was a cousin of Mrs. Proudie's; but

[021-059]as Mrs. Proudie stoutly denied the connection, it may be supposed

[021-060]that the people of Barchester were wrong. And, had Mr. Thumble's wife

[021-061]in truth been a cousin, Mrs. Proudie would surely have provided for

[021-062]him during the many years in which the diocese had been in her hands.

[021-063]No such provision had been made, and Mr. Thumble, who had now been

[021-064]living in the diocese for three years, had received nothing else from

[021-065]the bishop than such chance employment as this which he was now to

[021-066]undertake at Hogglestock. He was a humble, mild-voiced man, when

[021-067]within the palace precincts, and had so far succeeded in making

[021-068]his way among his brethren in the cathedral city as to be employed

[021-069]not unfrequently for absent minor canons in chanting the week-day

[021-070]services, being remunerated for his work at the rate of about two

[021-071]shillings and sixpence a service.

[021-072]The bishop handed his letter to his wife, observing in an off-hand

[021-073]kind of way that she might as well see what he said. "Of course I

[021-074]shall read it," said Mrs. Proudie. And the bishop winced visibly,

[021-075]because Mr. Thumble was present. "Quite right," said Mrs. Proudie,

[021-076]"quite right to let him know that you knew that he had been

[021-077]arrested,--actually arrested by the police."

[021-078]"I thought it proper to mention that, because of the scandal," said

[021-079]the bishop.

[021-080]"Oh, it has been terrible in the city," said Mr. Thumble.

[021-081]"Never mind, Mr. Thumble," said Mrs. Proudie. "Never mind that at

[021-082]present." Then she continued to read the letter. "What's this?

[021-083]Confession! That must come out, bishop. It will never do that you

[021-084]should recommend confession to anybody, under any circumstances."

[021-085]"But, my dear--"

[021-086]"It must come out, bishop."

[021-087]"My lord has not meant auricular confession," suggested Mr. Thumble.

[021-088]Then Mrs. Proudie turned round and looked at Mr. Thumble, and Mr.

[021-089]Thumble nearly sank amidst the tables and chairs. "I beg your pardon,

[021-090]Mrs. Proudie," he said. "I didn't mean to intrude."

[021-091]"The word must come out, bishop," repeated Mrs. Proudie. "There

[021-092]should be no stumbling-blocks prepared for feet that are only too

[021-093]ready to fall." And the word did come out.

[021-094]"Now, Mr. Thumble," said the lady, as she gave the letter to her

[021-095]satellite, "the bishop and I wish you to be at Hogglestock early

[021-096]to-morrow. You should be there not later than ten, certainly." Then

[021-097]she paused until Mr. Thumble had given the required promise. "And we

[021-098]request that you will be very firm in the mission which is confided

[021-099]to you, a mission which, as of course you see, is of a very delicate

[021-100]and important nature. You must be firm."

[021-101]"I will endeavour," said Mr. Thumble.

[021-102]"The bishop and I both feel that this most unfortunate man must not

[021-103]under any circumstances be allowed to perform the services of the

[021-104]Church while this charge is hanging over him,--a charge as to the

[021-105]truth of which no sane man can entertain a doubt."

[021-106]"I'm afraid not, Mrs. Proudie," said Mr. Thumble.

[021-107]"The bishop and I therefore are most anxious that you should make Mr.

[021-108]Crawley understand at once,--at once," and the lady, as she spoke,

[021-109]lifted up her left hand with an eloquent violence which had its

[021-110]effect upon Mr. Thumble, "that he is inhibited,"--the bishop shook

[021-111]in his shoes,--"inhibited from the performance of any of his sacred

[021-112]duties." Thereupon, Mr. Thumble promised obedience and went his way.

[021-113]CHAPTER XII.

[021-114]MR. CRAWLEY SEEKS FOR SYMPATHY.

[021-115][Illustration]

[021-116]Matters went very badly indeed in the parsonage house at Hogglestock.

[021-117]On the Friday morning, the morning of the day after his committal,

[021-118]Mr. Crawley got up very early, long before the daylight, and dressing

[021-119]himself in the dark, groped his way downstairs. His wife having

[021-120]vainly striven to persuade him to remain where he was, followed

[021-121]him into the cold room below with a lighted candle. She found him

[021-122]standing with his hat on and with his old cloak, as though he were

[021-123]prepared to go out. "Why do you do this?" she said. "You will make

[021-124]yourself ill with the cold and the night air; and then you, and I

[021-125]too, will be worse than we now are."

[021-126]"We cannot be worse. You cannot be worse, and for me it does not

[021-127]signify. Let me pass."

[021-128]"I will not let you pass, Josiah. Be a man and bear it. Ask God for

[021-129]strength, instead of seeking it in an over-indulgence of your own

[021-130]sorrow."

[021-131]"Indulgence!"

[021-132]"Yes, love;--indulgence. It is indulgence. You will allow your mind

[021-133]to dwell on nothing for a moment but your own wrongs."

[021-134]"What else have I that I can think of? Is not all the world against

[021-135]me?"

[021-136]"Am I against you?"

[021-137]"Sometimes I think you are. When you accuse me of self-indulgence you

[021-138]are against me,--me, who for myself have desired nothing but to be

[021-139]allowed to do my duty, and to have bread enough to keep me alive, and

[021-140]clothes enough to make me decent."

[021-141]"Is it not self-indulgence, this giving way to grief? Who would know

[021-142]so well as you how to teach the lesson of endurance to others? Come,

[021-143]love. Lay down your hat. It cannot be fitting that you should go out

[021-144]into the wet and cold of the raw morning."

[021-145]For a moment he hesitated, but as she raised her hand to take his

[021-146]cloak from him he drew back from her, and would not permit it. "I

[021-147]shall find those up whom I want to see," he said. "I must visit my

[021-148]flock, and I dare not go through the parish by daylight lest they

[021-149]hoot after me as a thief."

[021-150]"Not one in Hogglestock would say a word to insult you."

[021-151]"Would they not? The very children in the school whisper at me. Let

[021-152]me pass, I say. It has not as yet come to that, that I should be

[021-153]stopped in my egress and ingress. They have--bailed me; and while

[021-154]their bail lasts, I may go where I will."

[021-155]"Oh, Josiah, what words to me! Have I ever stopped your liberty?

[021-156]Would I not give my life to secure it?"

[021-157]"Let me go, then, now. I tell you that I have business in hand."

[021-158]"But I will go with you? I will be ready in an instant."

[021-159]"You go! Why should you go? Are there not the children for you to

[021-160]mind?"

[021-161]"There is only Jane."

[021-162]"Stay with her, then. Why should you go about the parish?" She

[021-163]still held him by the cloak, and looked anxiously up into his face.

[021-164]"Woman," he said, raising his voice, "what is it that you dread? I

[021-165]command you to tell me what is it that you fear?" He had now taken

[021-166]hold of her by the shoulder, slightly thrusting her from him, so that

[021-167]he might see her face by the dim light of the single candle. "Speak,

[021-168]I say. What is that you think that I shall do?"

[021-169]"Dearest, I know that you will be better at home, better with me,

[021-170]than you can be on such a morning as this out in the cold damp air."

[021-171]"And is that all?" He looked hard at her, while she returned his gaze

[021-172]with beseeching loving eyes. "Is there nothing behind, that you will

[021-173]not tell me?"

[021-174]She paused for a moment before she replied. She had never lied to

[021-175]him. She could not lie to him. "I wish you knew my heart towards

[021-176]you," she said, "with all and everything in it."

[021-177]"I know your heart well, but I want to know your mind. Why would you

[021-178]persuade me not to go out among my poor?"

[021-179]"Because it will be bad for you to be out alone in the dark lanes, in

[021-180]the mud and wet, thinking of your sorrow. You will brood over it till

[021-181]you will lose your senses through the intensity of your grief. You

[021-182]will stand out in the cold air, forgetful of everything around you,

[021-183]till your limbs will be numbed, and your blood chilled,--"

[021-184]"And then--?"

[021-185]"Oh, Josiah, do not hold me like that, and look at me so angrily."

[021-186]"And even then I will bear my burden till the Lord in His mercy shall

[021-187]see fit to relieve me. Even then I will endure, though a bare bodkin

[021-188]or a leaf of hemlock would put an end to it. Let me pass on; you need

[021-189]fear nothing."

[021-190]She did let him pass without another word, and he went out of the

[021-191]house, shutting the door after him noiselessly, and closing the

[021-192]wicket-gate of the garden. For a while she sat herself down on the

[021-193]nearest chair, and tried to make up her mind how she might best treat

[021-194]him in his present state of mind. As regarded the present morning

[021-195]her heart was at ease. She knew that he would do now nothing of that

[021-196]which she had apprehended. She could trust him not to be false in

[021-197]his word to her, though she could not before have trusted him not

[021-198]to commit so much heavier a sin. If he would really employ himself

[021-199]from morning till night among the poor, he would be better so,--his

[021-200]trouble would be easier of endurance,--than with any other employment

[021-201]which he could adopt. What she most dreaded was that he should sit

[021-202]idle over the fire and do nothing. When he was so seated she could

[021-203]read his mind, as though it was open to her as a book. She had been

[021-204]quite right when she had accused him of over-indulgence in his grief.

[021-205]He did give way to it till it became a luxury to him,--a luxury which

[021-206]she would not have had the heart to deny him, had she not felt it

[021-207]to be of all luxuries the most pernicious. During these long hours,

[021-208]in which he would sit speechless, doing nothing, he was telling

[021-209]himself from minute to minute that of all God's creatures he was

[021-210]the most heavily afflicted, and was revelling in the sense of the

[021-211]injustice done to him. He was recalling all the facts of his life,

[021-212]his education, which had been costly, and, as regarded knowledge,

[021-213]successful; his vocation to the church, when in his youth he

[021-214]had determined to devote himself to the service of his Saviour,

[021-215]disregarding promotion or the favour of men; the short, sweet days

[021-216]of his early love, in which he had devoted himself again,--thinking

[021-217]nothing of self, but everything of her; his diligent working, in

[021-218]which he had ever done his very utmost for the parish in which he was

[021-219]placed, and always his best for the poorest; the success of other

[021-220]men who had been his compeers, and, as he too often told himself,

[021-221]intellectually his inferiors; then of his children, who had been

[021-222]carried off from his love to the churchyard,--over whose graves he

[021-223]himself had stood, reading out the pathetic words of the funeral

[021-224]service with unswerving voice and a bleeding heart; and then of his

[021-225]children still living, who loved their mother so much better than

[021-226]they loved him. And he would recall all the circumstances of his

[021-227]poverty,--how he had been driven to accept alms, to fly from

[021-228]creditors, to hide himself, to see his chairs and tables seized

[021-229]before the eyes of those over whom he had been set as their spiritual

[021-230]pastor. And in it all, I think, there was nothing so bitter to the

[021-231]man as the derogation from the spiritual grandeur of his position

[021-232]as priest among men, which came as one necessary result from his

[021-233]poverty. St. Paul could go forth without money in his purse or shoes

[021-234]to his feet or two suits to his back, and his poverty never stood in

[021-235]the way of his preaching, or hindered the veneration of the faithful.

[021-236]St. Paul, indeed, was called upon to bear stripes, was flung into

[021-237]prison, encountered terrible dangers. But Mr. Crawley,--so he told

[021-238]himself,--could have encountered all that without flinching. The

[021-239]stripes and scorn of the unfaithful would have been nothing to him,

[021-240]if only the faithful would have believed in him, poor as he was, as

[021-241]they would have believed in him had he been rich! Even they whom he

[021-242]had most loved treated him almost with derision, because he was now

[021-243]different from them. Dean Arabin had laughed at him because he had

[021-244]persisted in walking ten miles through the mud instead of being

[021-245]conveyed in the dean's carriage; and yet, after that, he had been

[021-246]driven to accept the dean's charity! No one respected him. No one!

[021-247]His very wife thought that he was a lunatic. And now he had been

[021-248]publicly branded as a thief; and in all likelihood would end his

[021-249]days in a gaol! Such were always his thoughts as he sat idle, silent,

[021-250]moody, over the fire; and his wife well knew their currents. It would

[021-251]certainly be better that he should drive himself to some employment,

[021-252]if any employment could be found possible to him.
