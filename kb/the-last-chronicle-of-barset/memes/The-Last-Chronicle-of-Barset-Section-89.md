[089-001]Once at this period Archdeacon Grantly and Dr. Tempest met each other

[089-002]and discussed the question of Mr. Crawley's guilt. Both these men

[089-003]were inimical to the present bishop of the diocese, and both had

[089-004]perhaps respected the old bishop beyond all other men. But they

[089-005]were different in this, that the archdeacon hated Dr. Proudie as

[089-006]a partisan,--whereas Dr. Tempest opposed the bishop on certain

[089-007]principles which he endeavoured to make clear, at any rate to

[089-008]himself. "Wrong!" said the archdeacon, speaking of the bishop's

[089-009]intention of issuing a commission--"of course he is wrong. How could

[089-010]anything right come from him or from her? I should be sorry to have

[089-011]to do his bidding."

[089-012]"I think you are a little hard upon Bishop Proudie," said Dr.

[089-013]Tempest.

[089-014]"One cannot be hard upon him," said the archdeacon. "He is so

[089-015]scandalously weak, and she is so radically vicious, that they cannot

[089-016]but be wrong together. The very fact that such a man should be a

[089-017]bishop among us is to me terribly strong evidence of evil days

[089-018]coming."

[089-019]"You are more impulsive than I am," said Dr. Tempest. "In this case I

[089-020]am sorry for the poor man, who is, I am sure, honest in the main. But

[089-021]I believe that in such a case your father would have done just what

[089-022]the present bishop is doing;--that he could have done nothing else;

[089-023]and as I think that Dr. Proudie is right I shall do all that I can to

[089-024]assist him in the commission."

[089-025]The bishop's secretary had written to Dr. Tempest, telling him of

[089-026]the bishop's purpose; and now, in one of the last days of March, the

[089-027]bishop himself wrote to Dr. Tempest, asking him to come over to the

[089-028]palace. The letter was worded most courteously, and expressed very

[089-029]feelingly the great regret which the writer felt at being obliged to

[089-030]take these proceedings against a clergyman in his diocese. Bishop

[089-031]Proudie knew how to write such a letter. By the writing of such

[089-032]letters, and by the making of speeches in the same strain, he had

[089-033]become Bishop of Barchester. Now, in this letter, he begged Dr.

[089-034]Tempest to come over to him, saying how delighted Mrs. Proudie would

[089-035]be to see him at the palace. Then he went on to explain the great

[089-036]difficulty which he felt, and great sorrow also, in dealing with

[089-037]this matter of Mr. Crawley. He looked, therefore, confidently for Dr.

[089-038]Tempest's assistance. Thinking to do the best for Mr. Crawley, and

[089-039]anxious to enable Mr. Crawley to remain in quiet retirement till the

[089-040]trial should be over, he had sent a clergyman over to Hogglestock,

[089-041]who would have relieved Mr. Crawley from the burden of the

[089-042]church-services;--but Mr. Crawley would have none of this relief.

[089-043]Mr. Crawley had been obstinate and overbearing, and had persisted

[089-044]in claiming his right to his own pulpit. Therefore was the bishop

[089-045]obliged to interfere legally, and therefore was he under the

[089-046]necessity of asking Dr. Tempest to assist him. Would Dr. Tempest come

[089-047]over on the Monday, and stay till the Wednesday?

[089-048]The letter was a very good letter, and Dr. Tempest was obliged to do

[089-049]as he was asked. He so far modified the bishop's proposition that he

[089-050]reduced the sojourn at the palace by one night. He wrote to say that

[089-051]he would have the pleasure of dining with the bishop and Mrs. Proudie

[089-052]on the Monday, but would return home on the Tuesday, as soon as the

[089-053]business in hand would permit him. "I shall get on very well with

[089-054]him," he said to his wife before he started; "but I am afraid of the

[089-055]woman. If she interferes, there will be a row." "Then, my dear,"

[089-056]said his wife, "there will be a row, for I am told that she always

[089-057]interferes." On reaching the palace about half-an-hour before

[089-058]dinner-time, Dr. Tempest found that other guests were expected, and

[089-059]on descending to the great yellow drawing-room, which was used only

[089-060]on state occasions, he encountered Mrs. Proudie and two of her

[089-061]daughters arrayed in a full panoply of female armour. She received

[089-062]him with her sweetest smiles, and if there had been any former enmity

[089-063]between Silverbridge and the palace, it was now all forgotten.

[089-064]She regretted greatly that Mrs. Tempest had not accompanied the

[089-065]doctor;--for Mrs. Tempest also had been invited. But Mrs. Tempest was

[089-066]not quite as well as she might have been, the doctor had said, and

[089-067]very rarely slept away from home. And then the bishop came in and

[089-068]greeted his guest with his pleasantest good-humour. It was quite

[089-069]a sorrow to him that Silverbridge was so distant, and that he saw

[089-070]so little of Dr. Tempest; but he hoped that that might be somewhat

[089-071]mended now, and that leisure might be found for social delights;--to

[089-072]all which Dr. Tempest said but little, bowing to the bishop at each

[089-073]separate expression of his lordship's kindness.

[089-074]There were guests there that evening who did not often sit at the

[089-075]bishop's table. The archdeacon and Mrs. Grantly had been summoned

[089-076]from Plumstead, and had obeyed the summons. Great as was the enmity

[089-077]between the bishop and the archdeacon, it had never quite taken the

[089-078]form of open palpable hostility. Each, therefore, asked the other to

[089-079]dinner perhaps once every year; and each went to the other, perhaps,

[089-080]once in two years. And Dr. Thorne from Chaldicotes was there, but

[089-081]without his wife, who in these days was up in London. Mrs. Proudie

[089-082]always expressed a warm friendship for Mrs. Thorne, and on this

[089-083]occasion loudly regretted her absence. "You must tell her, Dr.

[089-084]Thorne, how exceedingly much we miss her." Dr. Thorne, who was

[089-085]accustomed to hear his wife speak of her dear friend Mrs. Proudie

[089-086]with almost unmeasured ridicule, promised that he would do so.

[089-087]"We are so sorry the Luftons couldn't come to us," said Mrs.

[089-088]Proudie,--not alluding to the dowager, of whom it was well known that

[089-089]no earthly inducement would have sufficed to make her put her foot

[089-090]within Mrs. Proudie's room;--"but one of the children is ill, and she

[089-091]could not leave him." But the Greshams were there from Boxall Hill,

[089-092]and the Thornes from Ullathorne, and, with the exception of a single

[089-093]chaplain, who pretended to carve, Dr. Tempest and the archdeacon

[089-094]were the only clerical guests at the table. From all which Dr.

[089-095]Tempest knew that the bishop was anxious to treat him with special

[089-096]consideration on the present occasion.

[089-097]The dinner was rather long and ponderous, and occasionally almost

[089-098]dull. The archdeacon talked a good deal, but a bystander with an

[089-099]acute ear might have understood from the tone of his voice that he

[089-100]was not talking as he would have talked among friends. Mrs. Proudie

[089-101]felt this, and understood it, and was angry. She could never find

[089-102]herself in the presence of the archdeacon without becoming angry.

[089-103]Her accurate ear would always appreciate the defiance of episcopal

[089-104]authority, as now existing in Barchester, which was concealed, or

[089-105]only half concealed, by all the archdeacon's words. But the bishop

[089-106]was not so keen, nor so easily roused to wrath; and though the

[089-107]presence of his enemy did to a certain degree cow him, he strove to

[089-108]fight against the feeling with renewed good-humour.

[089-109]"You have improved so upon the old days," said the archdeacon,

[089-110]speaking of some small matter with reference to the cathedral, "that

[089-111]one hardly knows the old place."

[089-112]"I hope we have not fallen off," said the bishop, with a smile.

[089-113]"We have improved, Dr. Grantly," said Mrs. Proudie, with great

[089-114]emphasis on her words. "What you say is true. We have improved."

[089-115]"Not a doubt about that," said the archdeacon. Then Mrs. Grantly

[089-116]interposed, strove to change the subject, and threw oil upon the

[089-117]waters.

[089-118]"Talking of improvements," said Mrs. Grantly, "what an excellent row

[089-119]of houses they have built at the bottom of High Street. I wonder who

[089-120]is to live in them?"

[089-121]"I remember when that was the very worst part of the town," said Dr.

[089-122]Thorne.

[089-123]"And now they're asking seventy pounds apiece for houses which

[089-124]did not cost above six hundred each to build," said Mr. Thorne of

[089-125]Ullathorne, with that seeming dislike of modern success which is

[089-126]evinced by most of the elders of the world.

[089-127]"And who is to live in them?" asked Mrs. Grantly.

[089-128]"Two of them have been already taken by clergymen," said the bishop,

[089-129]in a tone of triumph.

[089-130]"Yes," said the archdeacon, "and the houses in the Close which used

[089-131]to be the residences of the prebendaries have been leased out to

[089-132]tallow-chandlers and retired brewers. That comes of the working of

[089-133]the Ecclesiastical Commission."

[089-134]"And why not?" demanded Mrs. Proudie.

[089-135]"Why not, indeed, if you like to have tallow-chandlers next door to

[089-136]you?" said the archdeacon. "In the old days, we would sooner have had

[089-137]our brethren near to us."

[089-138]"There is nothing, Dr. Grantly, so objectionable in a cathedral town

[089-139]as a lot of idle clergymen," said Mrs. Proudie.

[089-140]"It is beginning to be a question to me," said the archdeacon,

[089-141]"whether there is any use in clergymen at all for the present

[089-142]generation."

[089-143]"Dr. Grantly, those cannot be your real sentiments," said Mrs.

[089-144]Proudie. Then Mrs. Grantly, working hard in her vocation as a

[089-145]peacemaker, changed the conversation again, and began to talk of the

[089-146]American war. But even that was made matter of discord on church

[089-147]matters,--the archdeacon professing an opinion that the Southerners

[089-148]were Christian gentlemen, and the Northerners infidel snobs; whereas

[089-149]Mrs. Proudie had an idea that the Gospel was preached with genuine

[089-150]zeal in the Northern States. And at each such outbreak the poor

[089-151]bishop would laugh uneasily, and say a word or two to which no

[089-152]one paid much attention. And so the dinner went on, not always in

[089-153]the most pleasant manner for those who preferred continued social

[089-154]good-humour to the occasional excitement of a half-suppressed battle.

[089-155]Not a word was said about Mr. Crawley. When Mrs. Proudie and the

[089-156]ladies had left the dining-room, the bishop strove to get up a little

[089-157]lay conversation. He spoke to Mr. Thorne about his game, and to Dr.

[089-158]Thorne about his timber, and even to Mr. Gresham about his hounds.

[089-159]"It is not so very many years, Mr. Gresham," said he, "since the

[089-160]Bishop of Barchester was expected to keep hounds himself," and the

[089-161]bishop laughed at his own joke.

[089-162]"Your lordship shall have them back at the palace next season," said

[089-163]young Frank Gresham, "if you will promise to do the county justice."

[089-164]"Ha, ha, ha!" laughed the bishop. "What do you say, Mr. Tozer?" Mr.

[089-165]Tozer was the chaplain on duty.

[089-166]"I have not the least objection in the world, my lord," said Mr.

[089-167]Tozer, "to act as second whip."

[089-168]"I'm afraid you'll find them an expensive adjunct to the episcopate,"

[089-169]said the archdeacon. And then the joke was over; for there had been

[089-170]a rumour, now for some years prevalent in Barchester, that Bishop

[089-171]Proudie was not liberal in his expenditure. As Mr. Thorne said

[089-172]afterwards to his cousin the doctor, the archdeacon might have spared

[089-173]that sneer. "The archdeacon will never spare the man who sits in his

[089-174]father's seat," said the doctor. "The pity of it is that men who

[089-175]are so thoroughly different in all their sympathies should ever be

[089-176]brought into contact." "Dear, dear," said the archdeacon, as he stood

[089-177]afterwards on the rug before the drawing-room fire, "how many rubbers

[089-178]of whist I have seen played in this room." "I sincerely hope that you

[089-179]will never see another played here," said Mrs. Proudie. "I'm quite

[089-180]sure that I shall not," said the archdeacon. For this last sally his

[089-181]wife scolded him bitterly on their way home. "You know very well,"

[089-182]she said, "that the times are changed, and that if you were Bishop of

[089-183]Barchester yourself you would not have whist played in the palace."

[089-184]"I only know," said he, "that when we had the whist we had some true

[089-185]religion along with it, and some good sense and good feeling also."

[089-186]"You cannot be right to sneer at others for doing what you would do

[089-187]yourself," said his wife. Then the archdeacon threw himself sulkily

[089-188]into the corner of his carriage, and nothing more was said between

[089-189]him and his wife about the bishop's dinner-party.
