[091-001]"Why should I be dismissed from your room without a reason?" said

[091-002]Mrs. Proudie. "Cannot Dr. Tempest understand that a wife may share

[091-003]her husband's counsels,--as she must share his troubles? If he

[091-004]cannot, I pity him very much as to his own household."

[091-005]"Dr. Tempest," said the bishop, "Mrs. Proudie takes the greatest

[091-006]possible interest in everything concerning the diocese."

[091-007]"I am sure, my lord," said the doctor, "that you will see how

[091-008]unseemly it would be that I should interfere in any way between you

[091-009]and Mrs. Proudie. I certainly will not do so. I can only say again

[091-010]that if you will communicate to me your wishes in writing, I will

[091-011]attend to them,--if it be possible."

[091-012]"You mean to be stubborn," said Mrs. Proudie, whose prudence was

[091-013]beginning to give way under the great provocation to which her temper

[091-014]was being subjected.

[091-015]"Yes, madam; if it is to be called stubbornness, I must be

[091-016]stubborn. My lord, Mrs. Proudie spoke to me on this subject in the

[091-017]breakfast-room after you had left it, and I then ventured to explain

[091-018]to her that in accordance with such light as I have on the matter, I

[091-019]could not discuss it in her presence. I greatly grieve that I failed

[091-020]to make myself understood by her,--as, otherwise, this unpleasantness

[091-021]might have been spared."

[091-022]"I understood you very well, Dr. Tempest, and I think you to be a

[091-023]most unreasonable man. Indeed, I might use a much harsher word."

[091-024]"You may use any word you please, Mrs. Proudie," said the doctor.

[091-025]"My dear, I really think you had better leave us for a few minutes,"

[091-026]said the bishop.

[091-027]"No, my lord,--no," said Mrs. Proudie, turning round upon her

[091-028]husband. "Not so. It would be most unbecoming that I should be turned

[091-029]out of a room in this palace by an uncourteous word from a parish

[091-030]clergyman. It would be unseemly. If Dr. Tempest forgets his duty,

[091-031]I will not forget mine. There are other clergymen in the diocese

[091-032]besides Dr. Tempest who can undertake the very easy task of this

[091-033]commission. As for his having been appointed rural dean I don't know

[091-034]how many years ago, it is a matter of no consequence whatever. In

[091-035]such a preliminary inquiry any three clergymen will suffice. It need

[091-036]not be done by the rural dean at all."

[091-037]"My dear!"

[091-038]"I will not be turned out of this room by Dr. Tempest;--and that is

[091-039]enough."

[091-040]"My lord," said the doctor, "you had better write to me as I proposed

[091-041]to you just now."

[091-042]"His lordship will not write. His lordship will do nothing of the

[091-043]kind," said Mrs. Proudie.

[091-044]"My dear!" said the bishop, driven in his perplexity beyond all

[091-045]carefulness of reticence. "My dear, I do wish you wouldn't,--I do

[091-046]indeed. If you would only go away!"

[091-047]"I will not go away, my lord," said Mrs. Proudie.

[091-048]"But I will," said Dr. Tempest, feeling true compassion for the

[091-049]unfortunate man whom he saw writhing in agony before him. "It will

[091-050]manifestly be for the best that I should retire. My lord, I wish you

[091-051]good morning. Mrs. Proudie, good morning." And so he left the room.

[091-052]"A most stubborn and a most ungentlemanlike man," said Mrs. Proudie,

[091-053]as soon as the door was closed behind the retreating rural dean.

[091-054]"I do not think that in the whole course of my life I ever met with

[091-055]any one so insubordinate and so ill-mannered. He is worse than the

[091-056]archdeacon." As she uttered these words she paced about the room. The

[091-057]bishop said nothing; and when she herself had been silent for a few

[091-058]minutes she turned upon him. "Bishop," she said, "I hope that you

[091-059]agree with me. I expect that you will agree with me in a matter that

[091-060]is of so much moment to my comfort, and I may say to my position

[091-061]generally in the diocese. Bishop, why do you not speak?"

[091-062]"You have behaved in such a way that I do not know that I shall ever

[091-063]speak again," said the bishop.

[091-064]"What is this that you say?"

[091-065]"I say that I do not know how I shall ever speak again. You have

[091-066]disgraced me."

[091-067]"Disgraced you! I disgrace you! It is you that disgrace yourself by

[091-068]saying such words."

[091-069]"Very well. Let it be so. Perhaps you will go away now and leave me

[091-070]to myself. I have got a bad headache, and I can't talk any more. Oh

[091-071]dear, oh dear, what will he think of it!"

[091-072]"And you mean to tell me that I have been wrong!"

[091-073]"Yes, you have been wrong,--very wrong. Why didn't you go away when

[091-074]I asked you? You are always being wrong. I wish I had never come to

[091-075]Barchester. In any other position I should not have felt it so much.

[091-076]As it is I do not know how I can ever show my face again."

[091-077]"Not have felt what so much, Mr. Proudie?" said the wife, going back

[091-078]in the excitement of her anger to the nomenclature of old days. "And

[091-079]this is to be my return for all my care in your behalf! Allow me to

[091-080]tell you, sir, that in any position in which you may be placed I know

[091-081]what is due to you, and that your dignity will never lose anything

[091-082]in my hands. I wish that you were as well able to take care of it

[091-083]yourself." Then she stalked out of the room, and left the poor man

[091-084]alone.

[091-085]Bishop Proudie sat alone in his study throughout the whole day. Once

[091-086]or twice in the course of the morning his chaplain came to him on

[091-087]some matter of business, and was answered with a smile,--the peculiar

[091-088]softness of which the chaplain did not fail to attribute to the right

[091-089]cause. For it was soon known throughout the household that there had

[091-090]been a quarrel. Could he quite have made up his mind to do so,--could

[091-091]he have resolved that it would be altogether better to quarrel with

[091-092]his wife,--the bishop would have appealed to the chaplain, and have

[091-093]asked at any rate for sympathy. But even yet he could not bring

[091-094]himself to confess his misery, and to own himself to another to be

[091-095]the wretch that he was. Then during the long hours of the day he sat

[091-096]thinking of it all. How happy could he be if it were only possible

[091-097]for him to go away, and become even a curate in a parish, without his

[091-098]wife! Would there ever come to him a time of freedom? Would she ever

[091-099]die? He was older than she, and of course he would die first. Would

[091-100]it not be a fine thing if he could die at once, and thus escape from

[091-101]his misery?

[091-102]What could he do, even supposing himself strong enough to fight the

[091-103]battle? He could not lock her up. He could not even very well lock

[091-104]her out of his room. She was his wife, and must have the run of

[091-105]his house. He could not altogether debar her from the society of

[091-106]the diocesan clergymen. He had, on this very morning, taken strong

[091-107]measures with her. More than once or twice he had desired her to

[091-108]leave the room. What was there to be done with a woman who would not

[091-109]obey her husband,--who would not even leave him to the performance of

[091-110]his own work? What a blessed thing it would be if a bishop could go

[091-111]away from his home to his work every day like a clerk in a public

[091-112]office,--as a stone-mason does! But there was no such escape for him.

[091-113]He could not go away. And how was he to meet her again on this very

[091-114]day?

[091-115]And then for hours he thought of Dr. Tempest and Mr. Crawley,

[091-116]considering what he had better do to repair the shipwreck of the

[091-117]morning. At last he resolved that he would write to the doctor; and

[091-118]before he had again seen his wife, he did write his letter, and

[091-119]he sent it off. In this letter he made no direct allusion to the

[091-120]occurrence of the morning, but wrote as though there had not been any

[091-121]fixed intention of a personal discussion between them. "I think it

[091-122]will be better that there should be a commission," he said, "and I

[091-123]would suggest that you should have four other clergymen with you.

[091-124]Perhaps you will select two yourself out of your rural deanery; and,

[091-125]if you do not object, I will name as the other two Mr. Thumble and

[091-126]Mr. Quiverful, who are both resident in the city." As he wrote these

[091-127]two names he felt ashamed of himself, knowing that he had chosen

[091-128]the two men as being special friends of his wife, and feeling that

[091-129]he should have been brave enough to throw aside all considerations

[091-130]of his wife's favour,--especially at this moment, in which he was

[091-131]putting on his armour to do battle against her. "It is not probable,"

[091-132]he continued to say in his letter, "that you will be able to make

[091-133]your report until after the trial of this unfortunate gentleman shall

[091-134]have taken place, and a verdict shall have been given. Should he be

[091-135]acquitted, that, I imagine, should end the matter. There can be no

[091-136]reason why we should attempt to go beyond the verdict of a jury.

[091-137]But should he be found guilty, I think we ought to be ready with

[091-138]such steps as it will be becoming for us to take at the expiration

[091-139]of any sentence which may be pronounced. It will be, at any rate,

[091-140]expedient that in such case the matter should be brought before an

[091-141]ecclesiastical court." He knew well as he wrote this, that he was

[091-142]proposing something much milder than the course intended by his wife

[091-143]when she had instigated him to take proceedings in the matter; but

[091-144]he did not much regard that now. Though he had been weak enough to

[091-145]name certain clergymen as assessors with the rural dean, because he

[091-146]thought that by doing so he would to a certain degree conciliate his

[091-147]wife,--though he had been so far a coward, yet he was resolved that

[091-148]he would not sacrifice to her his own judgment and his own conscience

[091-149]in his manner of proceeding. He kept no copy of his letter, so that

[091-150]he might be unable to show her his very words when she should ask

[091-151]to see them. Of course he would tell her what he had done; but in

[091-152]telling her he would keep to himself what he had said as to the

[091-153]result of an acquittal in a civil court. She need not yet be told

[091-154]that he had promised to take such a verdict as sufficing also for an

[091-155]ecclesiastical acquittal. In this spirit his letter was written and

[091-156]sent off before he again saw his wife.

[091-157]He did not meet her till they came together in the drawing-room

[091-158]before dinner. In explaining the whole truth as to circumstances as

[091-159]they existed at the palace at that moment, it must be acknowledged

[091-160]that Mrs. Proudie herself, great as was her courage, and wide as

[091-161]were the resources which she possessed within herself, was somewhat

[091-162]appalled by the position of affairs. I fear that it may now be too

[091-163]late for me to excite much sympathy in the mind of any reader on

[091-164]behalf of Mrs. Proudie. I shall never be able to make her virtues

[091-165]popular. But she had virtues, and their existence now made her

[091-166]unhappy. She did regard the dignity of her husband, and she felt at

[091-167]the present moment that she had almost compromised it. She did also

[091-168]regard the welfare of the clergymen around her, thinking of course

[091-169]in a general way that certain of them who agreed with her were the

[091-170]clergymen whose welfare should be studied, and that certain of them

[091-171]who disagreed with her were the clergymen whose welfare should be

[091-172]postponed. But now an idea made its way into her bosom that she was

[091-173]not perhaps doing the best for the welfare of the diocese generally.

[091-174]What if it should come to pass that all the clergymen of the diocese

[091-175]should refuse to open their mouths in her presence on ecclesiastical

[091-176]subjects, as Dr. Tempest had done? This special day was not one on

[091-177]which she was well contented with herself, though by no means on that

[091-178]account was her anger mitigated against the offending rural dean.
